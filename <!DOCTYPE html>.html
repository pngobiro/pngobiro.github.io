Physical Environment: The Countries of Eastern Africa

Introduction

Eastern Africa is a vibrant region located in the eastern part of the African continent. It encompasses eleven independent countries: Sudan, Ethiopia, Somalia, South Sudan, Kenya, Uganda, Eritrea, Burundi, Rwanda, Djibouti, and Tanzania. In this lesson, we'll delve into the unique physical environment of this region, exploring its diverse landscapes, countries, and physical features.

Position and Size of the Countries

Eastern Africa is situated between latitudes 22°N and 12°S, and longitudes 22°E and 51°E/52°E. Sudan stands as the largest country in the region, while Djibouti is the smallest.

Landlocked Countries

Several countries in Eastern Africa lack a coastline and are referred to as landlocked countries. These include:

  • Burundi
  • Uganda
  • Rwanda
  • Ethiopia
  • South Sudan

Latitudes and Longitudes

Understanding latitudes and longitudes is crucial for locating places on Earth. Let's explore their characteristics:

Latitudes

  • Imaginary lines running from west to east, also known as parallels.
  • The equator (0°) is the main line of latitude, dividing Earth into the Northern and Southern Hemispheres.
  • Key latitudes include the Tropic of Cancer (23.5°N), the Arctic Circle (66.5°N), the Tropic of Capricorn (23.5°S), and the Antarctic Circle (66.5°S).

Longitudes

  • Imaginary lines running from the North Pole to the South Pole, also called meridians.
  • The Greenwich Meridian (0°) or Prime Meridian is the main line of longitude, passing through Greenwich, London.
  • Longitudes determine time zones.

Map Interpretation

Maps are powerful tools for representing and understanding our world. To effectively interpret a map, we need to consider its key elements:

  • Frame: The border of the map's coverage area.
  • Title: The name or subject of the map.
  • Key: Explains the symbols used on the map.
  • Scale: Shows the relationship between map distances and real-world distances.
  • Compass directions: Indicate directions on the map.

Interpreting Human Activities

Maps often depict human activities like farming, mining, trade, and tourism through symbols. By understanding these symbols, we can gain insights into the economic activities of a region.

For example:

Economic Activity Evidence on a Map
Dairy farming Creameries, dairy farms, cattle dips
Beef farming Ranches, slaughterhouses
Cash crop farming Plantation symbols for tea, coffee, etc.
Mining Quarry symbols, mine shafts
Trade Market symbols, shops, towns

Interpreting Drainage Features

Drainage features like rivers, lakes, and swamps are typically represented in blue on maps. Continuous blue lines indicate permanent rivers, while broken blue lines represent seasonal rivers.

Interpreting Settlement Patterns

Settlement patterns describe how people are distributed in an area. Common patterns include:

  • Linear settlement: Along roads, rivers, or coastlines.
  • Nucleated/clustered settlement: People concentrated in a central location.

Interpreting Administration

Administrative features on a map include boundaries for countries, regions, and districts, as well as symbols for government offices and administrative centers.
