AGRICULTURE NOTES

TOPIC 1. AGRICULTURE AND ENVIROMENT

Definition of terms

Agriculture. is the science, art, or practice of cultivating the soil, producing crops, and raising livestock.

Environment sum total of all the living and non-living elements and their effects which influence human life.

1.1 Over view of Agriculture

a) importance of Agriculture in Kenya

Agriculture plays a vital role in Kenya's economy, contributing significantly to both its GDP and the livelihoods of the population. Here are some key aspects of its importance:

  1. Economic Contribution: Agriculture accounts for approximately 33% of Kenya's GDP and about 65% of total export earnings. It is a critical driver of the country's economy.
  2. Employment: The sector provides employment to about $75 \%$ of the rural population and over $40 \%$ of the total population. It is the main source of income for many Kenyans, particularly in rural areas.
  3. Food Security: Agriculture is crucial for ensuring food security in Kenya. The production of staple crops like maize, tea, coffee, horticultural products, and various fruits and vegetables is vital for feeding the population.
  4. Export Earnings: Kenya's agricultural exports, especially tea, coffee, cut flowers, and fresh produce, are a significant source of foreign exchange. The country is one of the world's leading exporters of tea and flowers.
  5. Poverty Reduction: As a major source of livelihood for the majority of rural households, agriculture plays a central role in reducing poverty. By improving productivity, farmers can increase their income and improve living standards.
  6. Industrial Development: Agriculture is closely linked to Kenya's industrial sector. The agricultural products are used in various industries, such as food processing, textile manufacturing, and beverage production, providing raw materials for these sectors.
  7. Sustainability and Rural Development: Agricultural activities are essential for the development of rural areas, providing infrastructure, improving access to markets, and supporting community services. Furthermore, with sustainable farming practices, agriculture can help in environmental conservation and climate adaptation.
  8. Cultural and Social Significance: Agriculture is embedded in the Kenyan culture and social life. Traditional farming practices, communal labor, and agriculture-related festivals and rituals form an integral part of rural Kenyan communities.

b) Relate Key Natural Resources to Agricultural production in Kenya

In Kenya, key natural resources are closely linked to agricultural production, influencing the country's ability to produce a wide variety of crops and livestock. Here are the key natural resources and their relationship to agriculture:

1. Land

Role in Agriculture:

Land is the primary resource for agricultural activities in Kenya. It is used for the cultivation of food crops, cash crops, and livestock farming. The availability of fertile land determines the types and quantity of crops that can be grown in different regions.

Impact:

Regions with fertile volcanic soils (such as parts of the Rift Valley) are highly productive, growing crops like maize, wheat, tea, and coffee. In contrast, arid and semi-arid areas (like northeastern Kenya) face challenges in agricultural production, limiting the types of crops that can be grown and leading to reliance on drought-resistant crops and livestock.

2. Water Resources

Role in Agriculture:

Water is essential for irrigation, especially in dry areas, and for maintaining livestock. Kenya's agricultural production relies on both rain-fed farming and irrigation schemes.

Impact:

The availability of water affects crop yields and livestock productivity. Major rivers, such as the Tana River, as well as irrigation systems in areas like Mwea and the use of water from dams, play a crucial role in ensuring food security and supporting large-scale farming of crops like rice, sugarcane, and horticultural products. However, irregular rainfall and water scarcity in certain regions affect agricultural productivity.

3. Soil

Role in Agriculture:

Soil fertility is critical for crop production. Fertile soils are necessary for growing high-yield crops, while poor soils may require more input in terms of fertilizers and soil management.

Impact:

The country's diverse soils support different agricultural activities. Volcanic soils in the Rift Valley, for example, are ideal for growing crops such as maize, potatoes, and flowers. In contrast, areas with less fertile soils may require soil conservation practices to enhance productivity.

4. Climate

Role in Agriculture:

Kenya's climate varies from tropical on the coast to temperate in the highlands and arid in the northeastern parts. The climate determines the types of crops that can be grown, the timing of planting and harvesting, and the overall agricultural productivity.

Impact:

Kenya's highland areas, with moderate temperatures and regular rainfall, support crops like tea, coffee, and horticultural products. On the other hand, the semi-arid and arid regions face challenges like water scarcity and frequent droughts, affecting the viability of crop farming and leading to a reliance on livestock.

5. Biodiversity

Role in Agriculture:

Kenya's diverse ecosystems contribute to agricultural production in various ways, including pollination, pest control, and the availability of a wide range of plant species for food and medicine.

Impact:

For instance, the rich biodiversity in the highland areas provides a favorable environment for coffee and tea plantations, which are highly sensitive to pests and diseases. The health of natural ecosystems, such as forests and wetlands, is essential for maintaining agricultural systems by offering critical ecosystem services like water filtration, climate regulation, and soil conservation.

6. Mineral Resources

Role in Agriculture:

Kenya is rich in mineral resources like limestone, phosphate, and gypsum, which are used in the production of fertilizers and soil amendments that enhance agricultural productivity.

Impact:

Access to minerals supports the availability of essential inputs like fertilizers, which are critical for improving soil fertility and boosting crop yields. Fertilizer use, in particular, is crucial for boosting maize and other crop production in areas with poor or degraded soils.

7. Forests

Role in Agriculture:

Forests provide important resources for agriculture, including wood for construction and fuel, as well as contributing to the overall environmental balance through water retention and soil conservation.

Impact:

Forests in regions like Mount Kenya, the Aberdares, and Mau Complex also provide critical environmental services, such as regulating rainfall patterns, controlling erosion, and supporting biodiversity, all of which directly impact agricultural productivity.
