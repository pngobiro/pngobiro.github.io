Module 3: Methods of Examination of Tissues

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Describe the dissociation method for examining tissues.
  • Explain the smear technique and its applications.
  • Differentiate between thick and thin sections and their uses.
  • Understand the principles of vital staining.

Dissociation and Smear Technique

1. Dissociation

The tissue is teased/dissected with mounted pins. Dissection is carried out while the specimen is immersed in an isotonic solution, e.g., normal saline, in a Petri dish or watch glass.

It is then transferred to a microscope slide, a cover glass laid on the preparation and examined by phase-contrast microscope.

Advantage

The method shows the structures of the cell without staining while the cells are still alive.

Disadvantage

The anatomical relationship of cells is destroyed.

2. Smear Technique

Material to be examined is either pressed against a slide (impression smear) and spread with a platinum wire loop or crushed and spread with another slide.

The specimen can be fixed wet, stained to demonstrate specific structures and after clearing, mounted on a media with a high refractive index.

The technique permits a much more detailed examination than a wet prep but suffers from similar limitations as above.

Thick and Thin Sections

3. Thick Sections

Are employed in case of emergency where a very thin slice of fixed or unfixed material is cut with a sharp knife or razor.

Staining is restricted to the surface of the tissue for urgently required diagnosis. Requires great experience.

4. Vital Staining

This is the staining of cells or tissues in the living state. It is divided into:

  • Intra-vital staining
  • Supra-vital staining

Intra-vital Staining

  • Staining takes place in the living body (in-vivo). Usually, by injection of a dye solution.
  • It demonstrates phagocytic cells (histiocytes) and reticulo-endothelial cells found in the bone marrow.
  • The stain is injected into a lab animal which is killed after an interval of time. The tissues are then fixed, processed and sectioned.
  • These sections are stained by a routine method and examined microscopically.
  • The phagocytic cells engulf microscopic coloured particles which are visible inside these cells.
  • This is used to demonstrate elements of the RE system.
  • Dyes used for intra-vital staining:
    • Trypan blue
    • Indian ink
    • Congo red
    • Vital new red

Supra-vital Staining

It is the staining of slide preparation of living detached cells outside the body (in-vitro).

Dyes used are:

  • Ehrlich's methylene blue
  • Neutral red
  • Janus green

Limitations of Vital Staining

  • Only a few specific elements can be demonstrated.
  • The nuclear membrane cannot be stained (impermeable).
  • The strength of the dye employed is very critical. If too weak, there is little or no staining taking place. If too strong, the cells are killed.

5. Thin Sections

It is the most common method of preparation of tissues for physiological examination, whether routine or research purposes. A thin section (3-5um) in thickness is taken is processed for examination under the microscope.

Paraffin wax is the commonly used embedding medium. Others include celloidin, ester wax, gelatin and synthetic resins

Advantages

  • Cells can be studied in their relationship to one another in the tissue.
  • The preparation is permanent.

6. Paraffin Wax Sections

The tissue is first fixed, dehydrated and then placed in a clearing agent. It is then blocked in paraffin wax which permits easier sectioning using a microtome.

7. Micro-incineration

This method is used to locate the presence and position of mineral elements in tissues.

It is based on the production of parallel sections, one being incinerated and the other stained by a routine method as a control. The ash residue of the incinerated section is examined alongside the stained preparation and the presence and location of any mineral elements noted.

Useful in the demo of silica in lung silicosis. Uses a polarizing microscope to examine the ash residue.

8. Auto-radiography

Is a technique used to determine the presence and distribution (site & amount) of radioactive isotopes in a tissue.

A photographic emulsion is exposed to a section of tissue for a sufficient amount of time to allow the radioactivity to reduce the silver in the emulsion. Black granules after development of the emulsion by a standard method indicates the presence of radioactivity.

💡 Pro Tip

The choice of examination method depends on the specific requirements of the study and the nature of the tissue being examined.

✍️ Practice Exercise

1. Explain the difference between intra-vital and supra-vital staining.

2. What are the advantages of using thin sections for tissue examination?

View Solution

1. Intra-vital staining involves staining cells within a living organism, while supra-vital staining involves staining living cells that have been removed from the organism.

2. Thin sections allow for the study of cell relationships and provide permanent preparations.

📚 Key Takeaways

  • Dissociation allows for the examination of living cells but destroys their anatomical relationships.
  • The smear technique provides a more detailed examination than wet preps but has similar limitations.
  • Thick sections are used for urgent diagnoses, while thin sections are used for routine and research purposes.
  • Vital staining involves staining cells in the living state and can be either intra-vital or supra-vital.
  • Micro-incineration and auto-radiography are used to locate mineral elements and radioactive isotopes in tissues respectively.
