Module 9: Fixation of Gross Specimen and Simple Fixatives (Formaldehyde)

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Describe the procedures for fixing entire organs, including the CNS, lungs, heart, liver, kidney, spleen, and intestines.
  • Explain the properties and uses of formaldehyde as a fixative.
  • Describe the composition of 4% buffered and 10% buffered formalin.
  • Understand the mode of action of formaldehyde in preserving tissue.
  • Recognize the safety precautions when using formaldehyde.

Fixation of Gross Specimen

Specimens can be fixed as an entire organ and this is done by 10% formal saline.

The technique of fixation varies with the organ to be preserved.

1. C.N.S

The tissues of the CNS contain the brain and spinal cord.

If the whole brain is to be preserved it should be suspended in 10% neutral formal saline by means of a cord passed under the basilar artery.

If the spinal cord is to be preserved whole it should be laid flat on an arrow slip of wood and incised along the entire length and pinned onto the board with plastic pins. Note metal pins shouldn't be used since they rust and leave holes in the tissue.

Fixation is accomplished by floating the pinned specimen board upper most in 10% neutral formal saline.

2. Lungs

10% formal saline is run into each of the major bronchi from an aspirator placed 1.2 m higher than specimen.

The fluid is pumped in until the contours of the lungs appear sharply outlined.

The bronchi should then be plunged with absorbent cotton wool and specimen immersed in a large volume of the fixative.

3. Heart

It should be packed with small balls of absorbent cotton wool saturated with 10% formal saline.

The specimen should then be immersed in a large volume of 10% formal saline.

4. Liver, Kidney and Spleen

They are best fixed by injection.

Formal saline 10% is injected into the blood vessel of the organ by means of Robert bronchogram syringe, then the specimen immersed in a large volume of the fixative.

5. Intestines

The method depends on the pathology to be demonstrated.

If the natural shape is to be preserved e.g. to demonstrate certain diseases e.g. Crohn's disease the specimen should be packed with absorbent cotton wool and soaked in 10% formal saline.

If it is for the demonstration of parasite inside the gut, it is opened up and pinned out in a similar manner as the spinal cord.

Simple Fixatives (Formaldehyde)

Formaldehyde (Formalin)

  • It is the most widely employed universal fixative particularly for routine paraffin embedded sections.
  • It is a gas produced by oxidation of methyl alcohol and is soluble in water to the extent of 37- 40 % by weight.
  • It is used as 4% buffered or 10% buffered formalin.
  • It has a very pungent odor, sold under the name of formaldehyde (40%) or formalin (a colorless liquid).
  • It is also obtainable in a stable solid form composed of high molecular weight polymers known as paraformaldehyde.
  • 4% formaldehyde or 10% buffered formalin is commonly prepared by 100ml of 40% formaldehyde to 900ml distilled water with 4 g sodium phosphatase, monobasic and 6.5g sodium phosphate, dibasic (anhydrous). To be effective, the specimen should be completely submerged in 5 to 20 times its volume of fixative.
  • Formalin does not precipitate proteins and only slightly precipitates other components of the cell. It does not harden or render albumin insoluble but subsequent hardening by alcohols is prevented.
  • Formalin neither preserves nor destroys adipose tissue and is a good fixative for complex lipids but has no effect on neutral fats.
  • Formaldehyde solution is nearly always acid. It becomes acid on storage as formalin oxidizes to formic acid, reducing its preservation capabilities. This necessitates neutralization of the solution by addition of magnesium carbonate or sodium hydroxide crystals.( in a wide mouthed container to avoid explosion due to release of CO2 gas).
  • Formaldehyde solution produces acid formalin haematin pigment which can be seen in sites containing blood.
  • Phosphate buffers such as sodium phosphate monobasic and sodium phosphate dibasic are effective for the neutralization of formalin and the pH of the solution produced is stable for many months.
  • Formalin should not be used with chromates because it readily oxidizes to formic acid.
  • Formaldehyde is an immediate irritant to the eyes, upper respiratory tract and the skin, and safety precautions should include proper ventilation and exhaust, limited or restricted exposure periods and thorough washing if spilt on tissue surfaces such as skin.

Mode of Action

Aldehydes form crosslinks between proteins, creating a gel, thus retaining cellular constituents in their in vivo relationships to each other. Soluble proteins are fixed to structural proteins and rendered insoluble, giving some mechanical strength to the entire structure which enables it to withstand subsequent processing.

💡 Pro Tip

Formaldehyde is a versatile and widely used fixative, but proper handling and safety precautions are essential due to its potential hazards.

1. Suspend the brain in 10% neutral formal saline by means of a cord passed under the basilar artery.

2. Mix 100ml of 40% formaldehyde with 900ml distilled water, 4g sodium phosphatase monobasic, and 6.5g sodium phosphate dibasic.

3. Formaldehyde forms crosslinks between proteins, creating a gel that retains cellular constituents in their in vivo relationships.

📚 Key Takeaways

  • Fixation of gross specimens varies depending on the organ and the pathology to be demonstrated.
  • Formaldehyde is a widely used fixative that is effective for routine paraffin-embedded sections.
  • Buffered formalin solutions are commonly used to maintain a stable pH and prevent the formation of formalin pigment.
  • Formaldehyde preserves tissue by forming crosslinks between proteins.
  • Proper safety precautions must be followed when using formaldehyde due to its irritant properties.
