Module 6: Post Mortem Changes and Functions of Fixatives

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Define post-mortem changes and describe the processes of autolysis and putrefaction.
  • Identify the signs of autolysis in the nucleus, cytoplasm, epithelium, and glycogen.
  • Explain how temperature affects the action of fixatives and post-mortem changes.
  • Describe the functions of fixatives in preserving tissue structure and constituents.
  • List the criteria/qualities of a good fixative.

Post Mortem Changes

These are the changes that take place on tissue cells soon after death or removal of cells/organ from the body.

Types

  1. Autolysis
  2. Putrefaction

Autolysis

  • This is the lysing or dissolving of the cells due to the action of the enzymes.
  • The normal behavior of enzymes is altered after death of the cell when lysosomes rupture to release enzymes that dissolve surrounding cells.
  • This occurs in the kidney and GIT.
  • The main groups of enzymes causing this are:
    • cathepsins (which react on proteins to peptides),
    • Carboxypeptidase, aminopeptidase (which break down peptides into individual amino acids).

Signs of Autolysis

The cell components undergo different changes. These are:

  1. Nucleus
    • condenses (pyknosis)
    • Fragments (karryohexis)
    • Disappears (karyolysis)
  2. Cytoplasm: swells and granulates and becomes a homogenous mass with loss of normal staining reaction.
  3. Epithelium: desquamates (broken arrangement) and splits away from basement membrane.
  4. Glycogen: diffuses and diminishes out of the cell leaving an empty membrane.

Functions and Qualities of a Good Fixative

Putrefaction

  • It is caused by bacterial action on tissues soon after death or removal of tissue from the body.
  • It results in the formation of gas.
  • The bacteria originate from the alimentary canal and surrounding organs. They could be normal flora during life or pathogenic during life affecting tissue, e.g., N. Meningitis, which multiply after the death of tissue.

NB: the action of fixatives is affected by temperature (it is increased at temp above 37°C. Most fixatives however produce maximum effect at room temperature.

Autolysis is reduced at lower temp but increased at temp above 37°C. When fixation is not immediate, it is good to keep the specimen at lower temperatures i.e. below R.T. (2-4°C).

Fixatives

A fixative is described as a substance which will preserve after death, the shape, structure, relationship and chemical constituents of tissues and cells.

The preservation should be such that the fixed tissue resembles as closely as possible the form it had during life.

In addition, it must render the tissue and cells insensitive to any subsequent treatments which may be necessary for the production of the final slide

Functions of Fixatives

  1. Help maintain a proper relationship between cells and extracellular substances:
    • connective tissue fibers (collagen, reticulin, elastin)
    • amorphous ground substances
  2. Brings out differences in R.I. and increases visibility or contrast between different tissue elements.
  3. Render cell constituents insoluble, with tissue proteins serving as the primary target for stabilization.

Criteria/Qualities of a Good Fixative

  1. Must cause sudden death to tissues cells without shrinking, swelling or any other distortion.
  2. Must be antibacterial to prevent putrefaction.
  3. Must inactivate enzymes to prevent autolysis.
  4. Must penetrate tissues quickly and evenly.
  5. Must render insoluble substances of the cell.
  6. Must preserve the cell in as life like manner as possible.
  7. Must harden tissues and enable easy manipulation of naturally soft tissues e.g. brain and liver
  8. Must alter to varying degrees the refractive index of different cell structures so that they are more visible due to differentiation/give good optical differentiation.
  9. Must fortify the tissue against the harsh effects of the solutions used during tissue processing.
  10. Must allow application of numerous staining procedures.
  11. Should be simple to prepare and economical to use.
  12. It should allow the tissue to be stored for long period of time.
  13. It should permit the restoration of natural color for photography and mounting as museum specimens

💡 Pro Tip

Understanding post-mortem changes is crucial for selecting the appropriate fixative and ensuring proper tissue preservation.

✍️ Practice Exercise

1. Differentiate between autolysis and putrefaction.

2. List five qualities of a good fixative.

View Solution

1. Autolysis is cell self-destruction by enzymes, while putrefaction is bacterial decomposition. Autolysis occurs in the kidney and GIT, while putrefaction originates from the alimentary canal.

2. Must cause sudden death to tissues cells without shrinking, swelling or any other distortion. Must be antibacterial to prevent putrefaction. Must inactivate enzymes to prevent autolysis. Must penetrate tissues quickly and evenly. Must render insoluble substances of the cell.

📚 Key Takeaways

  • Post-mortem changes, including autolysis and putrefaction, can significantly alter tissue structure and composition.
  • Temperature plays a crucial role in both the action of fixatives and the rate of post-mortem changes.
  • Fixatives are essential for preserving tissue structure, maintaining cell relationships, and rendering constituents insoluble.
  • A good fixative should possess several qualities, including rapid penetration, prevention of autolysis and putrefaction, and minimal tissue distortion.
