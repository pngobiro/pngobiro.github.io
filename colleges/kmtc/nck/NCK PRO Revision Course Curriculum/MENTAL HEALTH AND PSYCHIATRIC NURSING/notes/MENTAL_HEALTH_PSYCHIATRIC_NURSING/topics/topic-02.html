üß¨ Theories of Personality Development

Overview

There are two primary theories discussed in this unit:

  • Psychoanalytic theory by Sigmund Freud
  • Social learning theory by Erik Erickson

Psychoanalytic Theory (Sigmund Freud)

The theory posits that all human behaviour is determined by earlier life events and that human behaviour is driven by energy known as libido. It is also argued that people function at three levels of awareness:

Levels of Awareness

  • Conscious level where immediate events and perceptions are stored
  • Pre-conscious level where thoughts and feelings are not accessible with ease
  • Unconscious level where material is not accessible most of the time

Developmental Stages

According to the proponents of psychoanalysis, people develop through five stages:

  1. Oral phase (Birth to 1 year): Sexual satisfaction is obtained through the mouth by actions of sucking or biting.
  2. Anal phase (1 year to 3 years): Sexual satisfaction is obtained through the anus.
  3. Phallic phase (3 years to 6 years): During which interest in the genitalia is developed and the individual acquires gender roles.
  4. Latent phase (7 years to 11 years): Where there is no sexual interest, this divides boys and girls into different groups.
  5. Genital phase (12 years and onwards): When the capacity for objective and mature sex is developed.

Personality Structures

According to this theory, the human personality is made up of three hypothetical structures:

  • Id
    The id is the most primitive and is driven by impulses. It demands immediate gratification of the needs.
  • Ego
    The ego, which unlike the id, is in contact with reality. It is able to delay the satisfaction of a need until an appropriate time, place, or object is available. It mediates between the id and the super ego.
  • Super Ego
    The super ego, whose main function is to oppose the id. It contains values, legal, moral regulations, and social expectations.

Socio-learning Theory (Erik Erickson)

Erikson postulated eight human developmental stages which one has to go through in normal life. He called them psychosocial stages. Each stage is characterised by tasks or ego qualities, which one has to develop in normal life.

  • Trust versus Mistrust (0 - 18 months)
    This is attained during infancy. Trust is developed when the mother breastfeeds the infant as well as meeting other needs of the infant such as changing wet diapers.
  • Autonomy versus Shame and Doubt (18 months - 3 years)
    During this period the child wants some independence from the mother. This period is referred to as toddlerhood.
  • Initiative versus Guilt (3 - 6 years)
    Otherwise known as the pre-school years. The child is able to initiate action on its own in an effort to manipulate the environment.
  • Industry versus Inferiority (6 - 12 years)
    This takes place during school years. The child is able to acquire skills in sports, calculate in figures and language skills are well developed.
  • Identity versus Role Confusion (12 - 20 years)
    Referred to as adolescence. During this time an individual acquires an identity as a male or female corresponding to specific roles in the society.
  • Intimacy versus Isolation (20 - 40 years)
    Here, one has productive work and has satisfactory sexual relations with an intimate member of the opposite sex.
  • Generativity versus Stagnation (45 - 65 years)
    During this period, one is involved in establishing and guiding the next generation. Productivity and creativity is at its peak.
  • Integrity versus Despair (65 - death)
    During this time one develops new and different love for one‚Äôs parents. One also develops emotional integration and is able to defend the dignity of one‚Äôs own life-style against threat. The individual is capable of fellowship and will often take leadership responsibilities in the community.

ü©∫ Symptomatology of Mental Illness

Symptoms Affecting Consciousness

  • Confusion: disturbance of orientation to place, person and time
  • Stupor: lack of reaction to surroundings. Lack of awareness of the surroundings. No response to stimuli. Not aware of what is going on in the environment.
  • Delirium: bewildered, restless. Dream like change in consciousness. Impaired reality testing. Anxiety, confusion, disorientation, restless. Hallucinations experience.

Symptoms Affecting Self and Environment

  • Depersonalization: disturbances in the way one experiences the self
  • Derealization: disturbance in the way one experiences one‚Äôs physical environment

These two are common in one experiencing psychological stress due to bereavement, severe illness, loss of income or livelihood.

Symptoms Affecting Mood

Mood is the sustained and pervasive inward feeling. Mood is influenced by patient's experiences and expectations as well as presence of the disease.

Affect is the somatic and behavioral changes (outward expression of the emotion).

Emotion is the feeling or response to sensory input from the external enviroment or mental images.

  • Depression: a pathological feeing of sadness. Disease process of the mind. An illness or a symptom common in depressive illness. One perceives loss in life, person, property or status.
  • Elation: Air of confidence and enjoyment. Associated with increased motor activity. One is cheerful and has happy mood.
  • Inappropriate (incongruous): mood is not in harmony with patient ideas.
  • Flat (blunt): does not show any feelings.
  • Apathy: blunted emotion associated with loss of energy and drive.

Symptoms Affecting Motor Activity

Externally observable behaviors (Impulses, Motivations, Drive instincts, Wishes).

  • Hyperactivity: restless, aggressive and active. Common in maniacs and substance abuse.
  • Waxy flexibility: patient maintains body position in a particular posture for sometime. Common in catatonic schizophrenia.
  • Negativism: frequent opposition to suggestions and instructions.
  • Mannerism: habitual involuntary movement and attitudes.
  • Echopraxia: imitation of one person‚Äôs movement by another.
  • Compulsion: uncontrollable urge to perform and act repeatedly. Common in obsessive-compulsive neurosis.

Symptoms Affecting Thinking Process

  • Flight of ideas: rapid thought manifested by verbalization. Shifting of ideas which tend to be connected to one another. Common in maniacs.
  • Neologisms: new words created by a patient or a normal word used to mean a different thing. Common in schizophrenia.
  • Word salad: several words put together with no clear meaning.
  • Thought block: sudden interruption in the stream of thought before an idea or thought is completed.
  • Incoherence: thinking that does not flow logically.
  • Echolalia: psychopathological repetition of words or phrases said by another person.

Symptoms Affecting Thought Content

  • Thought broadcasting: patient feels that he/she shares hi/her ideas with other people. One may hear his/her ideas loudly from a radio, telephone. Common in Schizophrenia.
  • Delusion: false belief firmly held by patient and cannot be removed by argument or logic. It is out of keeping in educational or cultural background.
    • Grandeur: exaggerated self importance, ability, wealth, association with important people.
    • Persecution: one feels he/she is being persecuted by some people.
    • Worthlessness: feeling unworthy, remorse, one can easily commit suicide. Common in depressive illness.
    • Nihilistic: patient believes that he does not exist, extreme pessimism.
    • Hypochondriac: belief in ones illness contrary to all medical evidence.
    • Religious: special relation with God.
    • Jealousy: doubt about the fidelity of the sexual partner.

Symptoms Affecting Sensory Perception

  • Hallucinations: false sensory perception not associated with real external stimuli. Affects five common senses: Visual, tactile, Olfactory, Gustatory, Auditory.
  • Illusions: false sensory perception with external stimuli. Misinterpretation of objects (e.g., one sees a snake instead of a stick). Common in organic psychosis.

üìã Assessment of a Psychiatric Patient

History Taking in Psychiatry

  1. Personal data/demographic data
  2. Chief complains
  3. History of presenting illness
  4. Past medical and psychiatric history
  5. Family history
  6. Personal development history: delivery, milestones, infancy and early childhood
  7. Social history
  8. Sexual history

Mental Status Examination (MSE)

General Appearance: Dress, grooming, hygiene, cosmetics, apparent age, posture, facial expression.

Behaviour/Activity: Hyperactivity or hypoactivity, rigid, relaxed, restless, or agitated motor movements, gait and coordination, facial grimacing, gestures, mannerisms, passive, combative, bizarre.

Attitude: Interactions with interviewer: - Cooperative, resistive, friendly, hostile.

Speech:

  • Quantity: - poverty of speech, poverty of content, volume.
  • Quality: - articulate, congruent, monotonous, talkative, repetitious, spontaneous, circumstantial, confabulation, tangential and pressured
  • Rate: - slowed, rapid

Mood and Affect:

  • Mood (Intensity depth duration): - sad, fearful, depressed, angry, anxious, ambivalent, happy, ecstatic, grandiose.
  • Affect (Intensity depth duration): - appropriate, apathetic, constricted, blunted, flat, labile, euphoric.

Perception: Hallucination, illusions, depersonalization, derealization, distortions.

Thoughts: Form and content - logical vs. illogical, loose associations, flight of ideas, autistic, blocking, broadcasting, neologisms, word salad, obsessions, ruminations, delusions, abstract vs. concrete.

Sensorium and Cognition: Level of consciousness, orientation, attention span, recent and remote memory, concentration, ability to comprehend and process information, intelligence.

Judgment: Ability to assess and evaluate situations makes rational decisions, understand consequence of behaviour, and take responsibly for actions.

Insight: Ability to perceive and understand the cause and nature of own and other‚Äôs situation.

Reliability: Interviewer‚Äôs impression that individual reported information accurately and completely.

Psychosocial Criteria:

  • Internal: Psychiatric or medical illness, perceived loss such as loss of self concept, /self-esteem
  • External: Actual loss, e.g. death of loved ones, diverse, lack of support systems, job or financial loss, retirement of dysfunctional family system

Coping Skills: Adaptation to internal and external stressors, use of functional, adaptive coping mechanisms, and techniques, management of activities of daily living.

Relationships: Attainment and maintenance of satisfying, interpersonal relationships congruent with developmental stages, including sexual relationship as appropriate for age and status.

Cultural: Ability to adapt and conform to present norms, rules, ethics.

Spiritual (Value-belief): Presence of self-satisfying value-belief system that the individual regards as right, desirable, worthwhile, and comforting.

Occupational: Engagement is useful, rewarding activity, congruent with developmental stages and societal standards (work, school and recreation).
