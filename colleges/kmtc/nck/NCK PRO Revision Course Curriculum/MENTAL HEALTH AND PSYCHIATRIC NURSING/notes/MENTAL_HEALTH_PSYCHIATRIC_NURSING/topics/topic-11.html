Definition

Are disorders caused by physical illnesses affecting the brain.

It represents a group of mental disorders that present with a variety of symptoms, especially a disturbance in cognition.

Includes:

  • Delirium
  • Dementia
  • Amnesia

üë¥ Dementia

Definition and Characteristics

This is a slow decline in cognitive functioning, causing multiple changes. Affects advanced ages above 65 years.

  • It is characterized by gradual loss of memory & depletion of higher intellectual functioning.
  • It is a syndrome where there is disturbance in memory, intellect, personality change, cognitive impairment (amnesia), apraxia (difficulty in complex task).

Clinical Features

  • Impairment of intellectual functions.
  • Impairment in memory/recent memory.
  • Deterioration in personality with lack of personal care.
  • Impairment in judgement & impulse control.
  • Impairment in abstract thinking
  • Emotional lability.
  • Catastrophic reaction when confronted with an assignment which is beyond the intellectual capacity.
  • Thought abnormalities- delusions
  • Urinary/ fecal incontinence may develop.
  • Disorientation in time, place, and person
  • Altered sleep- sleep day time & awake at night.

Presenting Complaints

  • Forgetfulness & decline in mental functioning.
  • Families seek help coz of failing memory, change in personality or behaviour and disorientation.
  • Being apathetic/ disinterested.
  • Decline in every day functioning-dressing, washing, cooking.
  • Decline in memory, thinking, judgement, orientation and language.

Causes/Aetiological Factors

  • Alhzeimers disease
  • Infections of the CNS- tertiary syphylis, T.B brain, fungal meningitis, HIV.
  • Encephalopathy.
  • Vascular dementia/multi-infarct.-caused by poor blood supply to the brain.
  • Toxic dementias ‚Äì drugs, carbon monoxide, benzodiazepines, anticonvulsants.
  • Deficiency dementias-pernicious anaemia, folic acid deficiency, thiamine & pellagra.
  • Neoplatic dementias- neoplasms & other space occupying lesions.
  • Endocrine causes- thyroid, pituitary, adrenal dysfunction.
  • Metabolic dementia-chronic hepatic or uremic encephalopathy.
  • Traumatic dementias- head injury, chronic subdural haematoma.

Investigations

  • Physical exam & history taking- decline in memory/forgetfulness & disorientation, impaired ADLs.
  • Total blood count
  • VDRL
  • Thyroid function test
  • CT scan/MRI brain- check lesions
  • Brain biopsy
  • Lumbar puncture

Nursing Diagnosis

  • Impaired verbal communication rltd to cerebral impairment as evidenced by altered memory, judgment.
  • Bathing / hygiene self care deficit rltd to cognitive impairment as evidenced by inability to complete ADLs.
  • Risk for injury rltd to cognitive impairment & wandering behaviour.
  • Impaired social interaction rltd to cognitive impairment & difficulty recalling people.
  • Risk for violence (self directed or directed to others ) rltd to suspicion & inability to recognize people or places.

Management

  • Preventive measures especially in vascular dementia eg. Diet, exercise.
  • Treat the cause ‚ÄìDM, HTN, syphyllis, deficiencies like B12.
  • Administer antipsychotics in very low doses to manage aggression & restlessness & delusions.
  • Aspirin is given in low doses in vascular dementia inorder to slow deterioration.
  • Give sedatives for abnormal sleep patterns in low doses.
    NB: Avoid benzodiazepines in elderly as they cause increased confusion.
  • Monitor pts vital signs.
  • Individual & group psychotherapy with focus on anxiety mgt & changes in lifestyle.
  • Give educational & supportive psychotherapy & explain nature & course of the disease.
  • Assist client to accept the extent of their disability.
  • Give calendars for orientation, give note books for taking notes for memory problems.

Health Education

  • Reduction of stress & introduction of healthy habits in daily living.
  • Acceptance of a spouse as they deteriorate.
  • Developing ways of combating disorientation - appointment calendars, clocks, stable routines, setting places for important items.

üòµ Delirium

Definition and Epidemiology

Delirium is a state of mental confusion that develops quickly and usually flactuates in intensity. It is a syndrome or group of symptoms caused by a disturbance in the normal functioning of the brain.

Epidemiology: It affects atleast 1 in 10 hospitalized patients, and is a common part of many terminal illnesses. Its common in the elderly than in the general population.

A delirious patient has a reduced awareness of and responsiveness to the environment, which may be manifested as disorientation, incoherence and memory disturbance. Delirium is often marked by hallucinations, delusions and a dream like state.

Clinical Features

Diagnostic features include an acute onset, usually over hours or days, of confusion where the patient is disoriented and struggles to understand surroundings. This is evidenced by clouded thinking or awareness.

This condition is often accompanied by:

  • Poor memory.
  • Agitation.
  • Emotional upset.
  • Loss of orientation/disorientation.
  • Wandering/altered attention.
  • Auditory hallucinations such as hearing voices.
  • Visual hallucinations.
  • Withdrawal from others.
  • Illusions.
  • Disturbed sleep (reversal of sleep patterns).
  • Autonomic features, for example, sweating, tachycardia.

Risk Factors

  • Predisposing factors include: Older age, male sex, presence of dementia, depression, immobility, severity of physical illness and metabolic abnormalities.
  • Precipitating factors include: narcotics, severe acute illness, hypernatremia, hypoxemia, shock, pain, a high number of hospital admissions.

Causes (Acronym ‚Äì I WATCH DEATH)

  • I - Infections like malaria, typhoid.
  • W - Withdrawal(alcohol, sedatives).
  • A - Acute metabolic conditions(hypoglycaemia)
  • T - Trauma (head injury)
  • C - CNS pathology( intracranial space occupying lesions, tumours)
  • H - Hypoxia- 0f lungs and heart.
  • D - Deficiency- Thiamine, Vit B 12 deficiency.
  • E - Endocrine disorders( hyper/hypothyroidism)
  • A - Acute vascular conditions like Acute ischaemic attacks.
  • T - Toxins (Carbon monoxide poisoning, anticholinergics poisoning)
  • H - Heavy metals:lead, mercury.

Pathology

Underlying cause is lack of oxygen, glucose, amino acids, vitamin deficiency, synthesis and blockage of neurotransmitters, accumulation of toxins, and impaired cerebral blood flow.

Investigations

  • Electro-encephalogram
  • Neurotransmitter study.
  • Brain imaging/CT scan of brain.

Management

Medical Management

  • Identify the cause and treat the underlying cause. Example- 50ml of 50% dextrose IV for hypoglycemia, Oxygen for hypoxia, IV thiamine 100mg for thiamine deficiency.
  • Symptomatic measures. Since many pts are agitated, emergency psychiatric treatment may be needed.
  • Antipsychotics in low doses to control agitation, psychotic symptoms or aggression & should be continued for 3-5 days after the delirium has subsided.
  • Insomnia use small doses of benzodiazepines (diazepam, lorazepam) incase of alcohol delirium or antipsychotics (haloperidol) in cases not secondary to alcohol, till recovery occurs to cause sedation.
  • If delirium is due to severe pain, give analgesics(opioids for both analgesics and sedation).

Nursing Diagnosis and Interventions

  • Risk for trauma related to impairment in cognitive & psychomotor function.
    Need to assist pt with ambulation, keep a dim light on at night and arrange furniture appropriately.
  • Disturbed thought processes rltd to cerebral degeneration as evidenced by disorientation, confusion, memory deficits.
    Intervention- frequent orientation of client, placing clocks & calendars strategically and monitor side effects of drugs.
  • Self care deficit rltd to disorientation, confusion & memory deficits as evidenced by inability to fulfill activities of daily living.
    Intervention- Provide assistance in self care activities like bathing & involve family members in care of patient.

General Nursing Care

  • Nurse pt in a quiet room and well lit room to reduce disorientation.
  • Monitor the progress and report adequately on: Delirium rating scale, Delirium assessing scale.
  • Provide physical, sensory and environmental support. Physical support prevents accidents in the delirious patient.
  • Provide adequate sensory stimulation. Too high or low stimulation disorientates them. Provide a friend or relative in their room, give familiar pictures, a clock, calendar.
  • Regular orientation to person, place and time make the patient comfortable.

üìâ Amnesia

Characteristics and Causes

Affects memory mainly. Marked by memory impairment & forgetfulness.

Causes

  • Head injury
  • Alcohol blackout
  • ECT effects
  • Severe hypoxia
  • Herpes encephalopathy
  • Substance abuse

Types

  • Anterograde amnesia - impairment in the ability to learn new information.
  • Retrograde amnesia - inability to recall previously remembered knowledge.

Management

  • Depends on the cause.
  • Alcoholic blackout treatment with psychotherapy.
  • Supportive prompts about date, time, patient location, reduced anxiety & disorientation.
  • Explain problem to the patient.
