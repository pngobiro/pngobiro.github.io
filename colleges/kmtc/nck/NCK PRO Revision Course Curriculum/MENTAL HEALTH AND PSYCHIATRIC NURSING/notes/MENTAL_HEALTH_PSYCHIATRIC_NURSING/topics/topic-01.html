Definitions of Mental Health

Mental health is defined as the simultaneous success at working, loving, and creating with the capacity for mature and flexible resolution of conflicts between instincts, conscience, other people and reality (Evelyn and Wasili, 1986).

According to the World Health Organisation (WHO), mental health is a state of emotional well-being which enables one to function comfortably within society and to be satisfied with one‚Äôs own achievements.

Mental health also refers to the ability of the individual to carry out their social role and to be able to adapt to their environment (Johnson, 1997).

A state of emotional and psychological well being in which an individual is able to use his or her cognitive and emotional capabilities, function in the society and meet the ordinary demands of everyday life.

Definition of Psychiatry

Psychiatry- the branch of medicine that deals with the diagnosis, treatment and prevention of mental and emotional disorders.

It was first coined by Johann Christian Reil in 1808. It comes from the greek word ‚Äúpsyche‚Äù meaning soul and ‚Äúiatriyos‚Äù meaning to heal.

A branch of medicine that deals with the achievement and maintance of psychological wellbeing.

Definitions of Mental Illness

A mental illness is defined as a disorder with psychological or behavioural manifestations and/or impairment of functioning due to a social, psychological, genetic, physical, chemical or biological disturbance (Evelyn and Wasili, 1986).

Any condition that affects a persons mood, behaviour or thoughts in a way that is distressing or impairs functioning.

Characteristics of Mental Illness

A mentally ill person may have at least one of the following characteristics:

  • Being dissatisfied with one‚Äôs abilities and accomplishments
  • Having ineffective or unsatisfying interpersonal relationships
  • Dissatisfaction with one‚Äôs place in the world
  • Having ineffective coping/adaptation mechanisms and lacking personal growth

üß¨ Factors Contributing to Mental Health and Illness

Factors Contributing to Mental Health

  • Good nutrition - balanced diet and foods rich in omega 3,6 and 9. Fish, nuts, proteins.
  • Parental upbringing - good parenting results to better mental health during childhood and adulthood.
  • Ethical and moral upbringing - whats taught at school level and community as people grow up.
  • Physical health - physically fit individuals are likely to have sober minds
  • Exercise - helps in relaxation and proper blood circulation to the brain. Relieves stresses and calms individuals.
  • Personal efforts to practice mental hygiene - keeping off mind from unnecessary life stressors. Good thinking and watching right things.
  • Healthy environment - calm and unpolluted environment. free from toxic fumes and substances, free from noise.

Factors that Contribute to Mental Illness (Causes of MI)

A complex interplay of many factors cause mental illness. Contributing factors include:-

  • Biological factors
  • Psychological factors
  • Environmental factors
  • Nutritional factors
  • Socio-economic factors

Biological Factors

  • Some mental illness have been linked to an abnormal balance of neurotransmitters. When these chemicals are out of balance or not working properly, messages may not be transmitted properly through the brain, leading to symptoms of mental illness.
  • In addition defects in or injury to certain areas of the brain have been linked to certain conditions.
  • Genetics/Heredity - many mental illnesses run in families. Susceptibility is passed on in families through genes.
  • Infections - certain infections are linked to brain damage, hence developing mental illness. streptococcus bacteria has been linked to the development of obsessive complulsive disorder and other mental illnesses in children.
  • Brain defects or injury - some injury occurs due to birth asphyxia when brain is deprived oxygen resulting to death of cells. This can result to autism or cerebral palsies later in life.

Psychological Factors

This includes:-

  • Severe psychological trauma suffered as a child, such as emotional, physical or sexual abuse.
  • An important early loss e.g loss of a parent
  • Neglect of a child especially in early years.
  • Poor ability to relate with others.

Environmental Factors

  • Toxic environment or environment with fumes can trigger mental illness.
  • Very noisy environments can contribute to stress and altered sleep patterns which after a long time could lead to depression.
  • Fumes from marijuana can affect the non-passive smokers and can trigger psychotic episodes.

Socio-economic Factors

  • Violence within families and peer pressure influence(substance use) can predispose one to mental illness.
  • Sudden economic/finacial shifts can trigger depressive episodes.
  • Social expectations like ‚Äúthinness for beauty‚Äù can subject people to eating disorders.

Nutritional Factors

  • Poor nutrition can cause some eating disorders like-anorexia nervosa and bulimia nervosa.

Classification of Causes

The causes of mental illness can be classified into three categories:

  • Predisposing Factors
    These factors determine the likelihood of one getting a mental illness. Usually they are adverse experiences one undergoes in early life. They include physical, psychological and social factors in infancy and early childhood.
  • Precipitating Factors
    These are events that take place shortly before the onset of a disorder. Physical precipitants include cerebral tumours, malaria or drug abuse. Social and psychological precipitants include misfortunes such as loss of a job, losing a loved person, or sudden change in routine activities.
  • Perpetuating Factors
    Once the disorder has been triggered, these factors do prolong the course of the disease. They are secondary in nature since they may appear long after the original predisposing factors have been treated. Examples include secondary demoralisation and withdrawal from social activities.

Factors that Influence Attitudes towards Mental Health and Mental Illness

  • Culture: The way people think, behave or feel is shaped by their culture. Culture also determines the features of insanity, for example, who is labelled as insane and under what circumstances. What is considered insane in one culture may be considered perfectly normal in another. Culture also gives guidelines on the nature of treatment and the identity of the helper.
  • Education: The level of education also influences attitudes towards mental health and mental illness. An educated person has a better understanding of health and mental illness, thus making their attitude more positive.
  • Health Beliefs: These will determine whether the individual‚Äôs attitude is positive or negative. It will depend on how the patient explains the illness to themselves, that is, whether they believe in germ theory, evil spirits or an imbalance of some kind.
  • Religion: A patient‚Äôs reaction to mental illness will often depend on whether or not the patient believes in God or a particular religion, for example, some religions believe that ill health is caused by evil spirits. Usually, religion encourages the followers to be empathetic to sick people.

Global History

Demonological Period

  • King Nebuchadnezzar ate grace behaved like an ox
  • Ajax impaled himself, believed he was tormented by demons
  • Treatment was harsh, beating, locking up in dark room, throwing into rivers, ponds and forests

Political Period

  • Associated with king Edward 11 of England
  • Law passed in parliament to protect the mentally ill.
  • In 1403, sister of the order of Saint Mary managed to start a facility to care for mentally sick at Bedlam
  • A facility that accommodated six.
  • Thought of metal illness as demonic continued
  • Treatment remained as in demonological period.
  • Facility were dark, humid and infested with lice
  • 18th century 1st qualified nurse was appointed to look after mentally ill by Edward Tyson
  • Qualified nurses were harsh to patients
  • Both men and women were housed together
  • Overcrowded, disease outbreaks were common
  • Members of public visited the facilities to view patients as a form of entertainment.
  • At St. Lukes hospital this form of entertainment was banned
  • Members of public were issued with a ticket
  • They were to see patients in the presence of an attendant

Humanitarian Period

  • Reforms of system began in France, followed by Britain and finally America
  • 1793 at Bicetre Hospital in Paris Dr. Philippe Pinepil unchained a group of patients who were in chains for 30 years.
  • Dr. Philippe advocated kindness for mentally ill persons
  • 1796 William Tuke started reforms in Britain
  • He advocated for human treatment of the mentally sick.
  • Introduced Occupational Therapy
  • Men were involved in gardening and women in sewing.
  • Men and women assisted attendants in their daily activities
  • Members of the staff were not specifically trained to deal with mental health issues
  • 1808 a bill was passed to regulate treatment of mental patients
  • Dorothea Lynda Dix introduced reforms of mentally ill care in America
  • Visited Britain earlier before reforms and witnessed great improvement of patients in Britain
  • 1841 she passed a bill to regulate treatment of mentally sick in America
  • 1853 Dr. W.A.F Browne started informal mental health lectures to nurses
  • 1882, formal training on mental health began in America at Mclean Hospital, Boston
  • 1884 formal training in mental health started in Germany
  • Other countries followed to train.

Scientific Period

  • Modern treatments based on scientific findings were developed.
  • Many forms of treatments were discovered. hydrotherapy, insulin therapy and leucotomy were abandoned later
  • Surgical cutting of white nerve fibres
  • Within the brain, especially prefrontal lobotomy
  • Physical treatments-chemotherapy and electroconvulsive therapy developed
  • Psychotherapy, behavior therapy, occupational therapy and rehabilitation were also developed.
  • Integration of mental health services with other health services
  • Trained manpower was developed to care for mentally sick
  • Men and women were managed separately
  • Children managed separately
  • With available resources different health conditions were managed separately in different units.

Development of Psychiatry and Mental Health Services in Kenya

  • 1910: Mathare hospital was started as a lunatic asylum. Before it was a smallpox isolation centre.
  • 1924: Asylum was named Mathare. Custodial care in dark gloomy and damp atmosphere. Europeans Asians and Africans were managed separately. Care determined by one‚Äôs racial background.
  • 1961: Training of enrolled psychiatric nurses began. Later it was turned into post-enrolled psychiatric nursing course.
  • 1962: Decentralization of mental health care was started. Nakuru 22 bed psychiatric unit was built.
  • 1963: Machakos, Nyeri and Muranga were started. 1st two registered psychiatric nurses were trained.
  • 1965: Kakamega and Port Reitz were started.
  • 1972-1982: Most psychiatrists received overseas training at institute of psychiatry in England.
  • 1979: Post-basic diploma in psychiatric nursing started.
  • 1982: Training of psychiatrists started in Nairobi university.
  • 1983: In Nairobi psychiatric services started.

Currently all provincial hospitals have operational psychiatric units. Some district hospitals have units. Mathare hospital is being redeveloped. Future is to have community psychiatric services all over the country.

Mental Health Legislation

Mental Health Act of the laws of Kenya CAP 248 1989 (Revised in 1991)

Guiding Principles

Regulates mental health care environment in the best interest of the Patient/client/customer/user.

  • Safeguards the human rights of the patient as guaranteed
  • Under international human right instruments
  • Regional charters as well as national constitutions and legislations
  • Sets obligations of mental health care providers

Scope of Regulation

  • Access to mental health care
  • Provision of mental health care
  • Treatment for voluntary and involuntary patients
  • Statutory treatment through criminal justice systems
  • Regulates the manner in which the property of mentally sick is dealt with by the courts of law.

Objectives of Mental Health Act

  • Demystify mental illness
  • Destigmatise mental illness
  • Descriminate mental illness
  • Decentralize mental health services
  • Make mental illness a community issue

Part V - Voluntary Patients (Voluntary Admission)

In such cases forms MOH 637 in duplicate should be filled and signed by the guardian or the parent.

Part VI - Involuntary Patients (Involuntary Admission)

  • Involuntary patients are those who are incapable of expressing themselves as willing or unwilling to receive treatment.
  • They require the forms MOH 614 to be filled in duplicate by the husband, wife or relative of the patient, indicating the reasons why they are applying for admission.
  • The patient is admitted for a period of not more than six months. The ‚Äòperson in charge‚Äô can prolong this period by six more months provided the total period does not exceed twelve months.
  • An MOH 615 form should be filled by the doctor indicating why he thinks that the patient can benefit from the treatment.
  • Both the MOH 614 and MOH 615 forms must reach the hospital within 14 days of the date they were signed, otherwise they become invalid.

Part VII ‚Äì Emergency Admissions

  • A police officer, chief or assistant chief can arrest any person(s) who is found to be dangerous to him/herself or others, and take them to a mental hospital for treatment within 24 hours or a reasonable time.
  • The patient should be reviewed after 72 hours and can be discharged if found to be of sound mind.
  • If found to be of unsound mind, the patient may be admitted for treatment as an involuntary patient.
  • For the purposes of admission, the form MOH 638 must be filled in by the police officer or an administrative officer.

Part VIII - Admission and Discharge of Members of the Armed Forces

  • Any member of the armed forces may be admitted into a mental hospital for observation, if a medical officer of the armed forces, by letter addressed to the ‚Äòperson in charge‚Äô, and certifies that:
    • The member of the armed forces has been examined within a period of 48 hours before issuing the letter
    • For reasons recorded in the letter, the member of the armed forces is a proper person to be admitted to a mental hospital for observation and treatment
  • A member of the armed forces may be admitted for an initial period of 28 days from the date of admission, that period may be extended if, at or before the end of 28 days.
  • Two medical practitioners, one of whom shall be a psychiatrist, may recommend the extension after re-examining the patient.
  • The said patient can be discharged if two medical practitioners, one of whom is a psychiatrist, by a letter addressed to the ‚Äòperson in charge‚Äô, certifying that they have examined the member of the armed forces within a period of 72 hours before issuing the letter.

Part IX - Admission of a Patient from Foreign Countries

According to this section of the act:

  1. No person suffering from mental disorder shall be admitted into a mental hospital in Kenya from any state outside Kenya except under Part IX of M.H.A.
  2. On the admission of a person into a mental hospital under this section, the ‚Äòperson in charge‚Äô shall within 72 hours or such longer period as the board may approve:

  2. Within that period forward to the Board a report on the findings, together with the warrant or other document from the foreign country concerned accompanying the patient/person.

A person shall not be detained in a mental hospital under this section for a period longer than two months from the date of admission to the mental hospital unless the board, on application in the prescribed forms by the ‚Äòperson in charge‚Äô, approves.

Kenya Board of Mental Health

The act also provides for the establishment of a board, that is, the Kenya board of mental health for the purposes of administering this act.

Members

  • Chairman, who can be the Director of Medical Services (DMS) or the Deputy Director of Medical Services and is appointed by the cabinet secretary
  • Psychiatrist (medical practitioner) appointed by the cabinet secretary
  • One clinical officer with training and experience in mental health care appointed by the cabinet secretary.
  • One psychiatric nurse with experience in mental health care, appointed by the cabinet secretary.
  • The commissioner of social services or their nominee appointed by the cabinet secretary.
  • Director of education or their nominee appointed by the cabinet secretary.
  • A representative from each province in Kenya, being resident in the provinces, appointed by the cabinet secretary.

The board may co-opt any person whose skills, knowledge or experience may be useful. It may also establish a committee for better function and regulate its own procedure.

Functions of Kenya Board of Mental Health

  • To coordinate mental health activities in Kenya.
  • To advise the government on the state of mental health and mental health care facilities in Kenya.
  • To inspect mental health care hospitals to ensure that they meet the prescribed standards.
  • To approve the establishment of mental health care hospitals.
  • To assist when necessary in the administration of mental health hospitals.
  • To receive and investigate any matters referred to it by a patient or relative of a patient concerning the treatment of the patient at a mental health hospital and, where necessary, to take or recommend to the minister any remedial action.
  • To advise the government on the care of the persons suffering from mental sub-normality without mental disorder.
  • To initiate and organise community or family based programmes for the care of persons suffering from mental disorder, and to perform such other functions as may be placed upon it by this act or under the law.
