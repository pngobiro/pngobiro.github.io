üß† Introduction to Neuroses

Definitions

Neuroses are psychiatric disturbances, characterized by excessive anxiety that is expressed directly or altered through defense mechanisms.

It appears as a symptom, such as an obsession, a compulsion, a phobia, or a sexual dysfunction.

Common Characteristics

The following are common characteristics of people with neuroses:

  • They are aware that they are experiencing distress.
  • They are aware that their behaviors are maladaptive.
  • They are unaware of any possible psychological causes of the distress.
  • They feel helpless to change their situation.
  • They experience no loss of contact with reality.

Types of Neuroses

The following disorders are examples of psychoneurotic responses to severe anxiety as they appear in the DSM-IV-TR:

  • Anxiety disorders: Disorders in which the characteristic features are symptoms of anxiety and avoidance behavior
  • Somatoform disorders: Disorders in which the characteristic features are physical symptoms for which there is no demonstrable organic pathology.
  • Dissociative disorders: Disorders in which the characteristic feature is a disruption in the usually integrated functions of consciousness, memory, identity, or perception of the environment

Definition of Anxiety

  • Feeling of apprehension accompanied by sympathetic nervous system arousal e.g. sweating, ‚ÜëHR, ‚ÜëRR
  • Abnormal fear out of proportion to any external stimulus
  • A Psychophysiologic response to a perception of internal or external danger.
  • May be spontaneous

Types of Anxiety Disorders (DSM IV-TR)

1. Panic Attack

Abrupt & discrete period of intense fear or discomfort characterized by:

  • Palpitations
  • Sweating
  • Trembling
  • Shortness of breath
  • Feeling of choking
  • Chest discomfort
  • Paresthesias
  • Nausea/abdominal Distress
  • Feeling dizzy
  • Derealization
  • Depersonalization
  • Fear of going crazy
  • Fear of dying
  • Chills or hot flushes

2. Panic Disorder

  • Recurrent unexpected Panic Attacks
  • Persistent concern about having additional attacks
  • Worry about the implications of the attack or its consequences
  • Significant change in behaviour related to the attacks
  • Presence // Absence of Agoraphobia

3. Agoraphobia

Anxiety about being in places or situations from which escape might be difficult (or embarrassing) or which help may not be available in the event of having an unexpected or situationally predisposed Panic Attack or panic like symptoms.

Fears involve situations like:

  • Being outside the home
  • Being on a bridge
  • Traveling in a bus
  • Being in a crowd / standing in a line

The situations are avoided or else endured with marked distress or anxiety about having a panic attack, or require a companion.

4. General Anxiety Disorder (GAD)

  • Uncontrollable Anxiety++ / worry+ about event(s)/activities
  • Characterized by Restlessness, fatigue, irritability, concentration difficulties, muscle tension, insomnia
  • The signs must have lasted for more than 6 months
  • S+S causes significant Distress/functioning
  • No drug/medical condition

5. Specific Phobia

Marked & persistent fear that is excessive or unreasonable, cued by the presence or anticipation of a specific object or situation. Exposure to the phobic stimulus almost invariably invokes an immediate anxiety response, which may take the form of a situationally bound/predisposed Panic Attack (Children = crying, freezing, tantrums, or clinging).

Adults recognizes that the fear is excessive/unreasonable. The phobic situation is avoided or else is endured with intense anxiety or distress. Distresses / Interferes significantly with functioning.

Types of Specific Phobia:

  • Animal: Arachnophobia (spiders), Zoophobia (animals), Ailurophobia (cats)
  • Natural Environment: Acrophobia (heights), Aquaphobia/Hydrophobia (water), Nyctophobia (darkness), Astraphobia (lightning/thunder), Mysophobia (germs/dirt)
  • Blood-Injection-injury: Haematophobia (blood), Algophobia (pain)
  • Situational: Claustrophobia (enclosed spaces), Monophobia (being alone), Ocholophobia (crowds), Public transport fear
  • Others: Thanatophobia (death), HIV/AIDS, Venophobia (venereal disease), Xenophobia (strangers), triskaidekaphobia (number 13)

6. Social Phobia

  • A marked & persistent fear of social or performance situation(s) in which the person is exposed to unfamiliar people or to possible scrutiny by others.
  • Fears that will act in a way that will be humiliating/embarrassing.
  • Exposure to the feared social situation almost invariably provokes anxiety, which may take the form of a Panic Attack.
  • Person recognizes that the fear is excessive / unreasonable.
  • E.g. Initiating /maintaining conversations; Participating in small groups; Dating; Speaking to authority figures; attending parties.

7. Other Types (AD NOS)

  • Situational Anxiety
  • Anxiety about Death
  • Anxiety about Mutilation, Loss of Prowess, or Loss of Attractiveness
  • Anxiety about Loss of Self-esteem
  • Separation Anxiety
  • Stranger Anxiety
  • Anxiety about Loss of Control
  • Anxiety about Dependency
  • Anxiety about Intimacy
  • Anxiety about being Punished
  • Signal Anxiety

üîÑ Obsessive-Compulsive & Stress Disorders

Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD)

Obsessions

  • Recurrent & persistent thoughts, impulses, or images that are experienced, at some time during the disturbance, as intrusive & inappropriate & that cause marked anxiety / distress
  • The thoughts are not simply excessive worries about real life problems
  • Person attempts to ignore/suppress/neutralize them with other thought/action
  • Person recognizes that the thoughts are a product of own mind (not imposed from without)

Compulsions

  • Repetitive behaviours or mental acts that the person feels driven to perform in response to an obsession, or according to the rules that must be applied rigidly
  • The acts are aimed at preventing or reducing distress or preventing some dreaded event or situation, but the acts either are not concerned in a realistic way with what they are designed to neutralize / prevent / are clearly excessive

Note: Person recognizes that the obsessions and compulsions are excessive / unreasonable (but some may not have the insight). Significantly interferes with functioning.

Examples: Washers, Checkers.

Post Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)

Exposed to traumatic event in which one experienced, witnessed, or was confronted with an event(s) that involved actual/threatened death/serious injury, or a threat to the physical integrity of self/others. One‚Äôs response involved intense fear, helplessness or horror.

Duration: Comes 1 month + after the event.

Symptoms

  • Re-experiencing: Recurrent & intrusive distressing recollections, distressing dreams, acting/feeling as if event recurring, intense psychological distress at exposure to cues, physiological reactivity.
  • Avoidance & Numbing: Efforts to avoid thoughts/feelings/conversations/activities/places related to trauma, inability to recall important aspects, markedly diminished interest, feeling of detachment, restricted range of affect, sense of foreshortened future.
  • Increased Arousal: Insomnia, irritability, hypervigilance, difficulty concentrating, exaggerated startle response.

Acute Stress Disorder

Symptoms:

  • Re-experiencing: Recurrent images/thoughts/dreams/illusions/flashbacks.
  • Avoidance of stimuli that arouse recollections.
  • Anxiety / arousal.

üíä Management of Neuroses

Psychotherapy

  • Individual psychotherapy - clients discusses his/her difficulties or fears with a therapist
  • Supportive therapy - Support adaptive defenses, Reality Testing, Advice, Adaptive Behaviour
  • Cognitive therapy - Try to change ones perception of difficulty/fear. Achieved using Psychodynamic Therapy (Interpretation, confrontation, clarification). Clients become aware of fears. Done in sessions.
  • Group/Family therapy - Good in PTSD. Clients share experiences with other clients or relatives. They come to know they are not alone.

Behaviour Therapy

  • Systematic desensitization ‚Äì gradual exposure of client to phobic stimuli either in real or imagined situation. Relaxation is required.
  • Implosion therapy (flooding) - Client imagines situation or participates in real life situations that s/he finds extremely frightening for a prolonged period of time. Relaxation is not required.
  • Adjunctive techniques - e.g. Relaxation, Hypnosis, Biofeedback.

ü©∫ Somatoform Disorders

Definition

A group of disorders that include medical symptoms and complaints FOR WHICH AN ADEQUATE MEDICAL EXPLANATION CANNOT BE FOUND. Not intentionally produced. Onset, severity and duration of symptoms are strongly linked to psychological factors.

Somatization is the expression of psychological stress through physical symptoms.

Most Common Presenting Symptoms

  • Abdominal pain
  • Chest pain
  • Dyspnea
  • Headache
  • Fatigue
  • Cough
  • Back pain
  • Nervousness
  • Dizziness

Types of Somatoform Disorders

1. Somatization Disorder

‚ÄúHysteria‚Äù, Briquet‚Äôs Syndrome. Multiplicity of somatic complaints involving multiple organ systems. Female predominance. Before age 30. Chronic. Excessive medical help-seeking behavior.

Diagnostic Criteria:

  • Four pain symptoms
  • One sexual symptom
  • One pseudoneurological symptom
  • Two Gastrointestinal symptoms

Cannot be fully explained by any known mechanism or substance use. Physical complaints or impairment are in excess of what could be expected. Significant impairment in functioning.

2. Conversion Disorder

Marked by the presence of deficits in voluntary motor and sensory functions. Monosymptomatic (one or more neurological symptoms).

  • Symptoms: Impaired coordination/balance, paralysis, weakness, aphonia, difficulty swallowing, lump in throat, urinary retention, loss of touch/pain, double vision, blindness, deafness, seizures.
  • Symptoms do not conform to known anatomical pathways and physiological mechanisms.
  • Dramatic or histrionic; suggestible.
  • Symptoms are self-limited and do not lead to physical changes/disability.

3. Hypochondriasis

  • Preoccupation with the fear of contracting, or the belief of having, a serious disease
  • Misinterpretation of physical symptoms and sensations
  • Request for admission to the ‚Äúsick role‚Äù, which offers an escape
  • ‚ÄúDoctor shopping‚Äù
  • Medical history is presented in great detail and length

4. Body Dysmorphic Disorder

  • Preoccupation with an imagined defect or an exaggerated distortion of a minimal or minor defect in physical appearance (dysmorphophobia)
  • Marked distress over supposed deformity
  • Frequent mirror checking and checking in other reflecting surfaces
  • Excessive grooming behavior; avoidance of usual activities

5. Somatoform Pain Disorder

  • Presence of pain that is the ‚Äúpredominant focus of clinical attention‚Äù
  • Not fully accounted by a nonpsychiatric medical or neurological condition
  • The symbolic meaning of body disturbances relate to atonement for perceived sin, to expiation of guilt, or to suppressed aggression

Factitious Disorders

  • Intentional production or feigning of physical signs and symptoms.
  • Can be psychological symptoms or physical symptoms
  • Munchausen‚Äôs syndrome: patients go from one primary care provider or hospital to another, seeking attention. Usually co-morbid with psychiatric conditions.
ALERT Signs:
  • Numerous surgical scars, usually in the abdominal area
  • Patient is truculent and evasive
  • Personal and medical history were fraught with acute and harrowing adventures
  • History of many hospitalizations, malpractice claims, insurance claims
  • Involved in the healthcare profession

Malingering

Intentional fabrication of symptoms to achieve a secondary gain, usually material benefits.

Principles of Management

  • Emphasize explanation
  • Arrange for regular follow-up
  • Treat mood/anxiety disorder
  • Minimize polypharmacy and multiple diagnostic tests
  • Provide specific treatment when indicated

üß© Dissociative Disorders

Definition

Disturbance in the normally well integrated continuum of consciousness, memory, identity and perception. Dissociation is an unconscious defence mechanism that protects the individual against overwhelming anxiety.

Types (DSM-IV-TR)

  • Depersonalization Disorder
    A persistent or recurrent alteration in perception of the self while reality testing remains intact (they are not delusional or hallucinating). The person may feel mechanical, dreamy and detached from the body. May experience a sense of deadness of the body or seeing oneself from a distance. Does not respond to medication or any therapy.
  • Dissociative Amnesia
    Marked by the inability to recall important personal information, often of a traumatic or stressful nature. This lack of memory is too pervasive to be explained by ordinary forgetfulness.
    • General amnesia: unable to recall information about entire lifetime.
    • Localized amnesia: unable to remember all events in a certain period.
    • Selective amnesia: able to recall some but not all events in a certain period.
  • Dissociative Fugue
    Characterized by sudden, unexpected travel away from the customary locale and inability to recall one‚Äôs identity and information about some or all of the past.
  • Dissociative Identity Disorder
    Is the presence of two or more distinct personality states that recurrently take control of behavior.
