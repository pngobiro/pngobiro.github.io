üìä Affective (Mood) Disorders Overview

A group of mental disorders that present mainly with symptoms of mood disturbance with associated changes in thinking and behaviour.

Classification

  • Manic episode
  • Depressive episode
  • Bipolar mood disorder
02

‚ö° Mania

Definition and Characteristics

A state of abnormally elevated or irritable mood, arousal or energy levels. It is usually the opposite of depression. A manic episode is not a disorder in and of itself, but a type of bipolar disorder.

A manic episode is characterized by a period of time where an elevated, expansive or notably irritable mood is present, lasting for at least 1 week. The feelings must be sufficiently severe to cause difficulty or impairment in occupational, social, educational or other important functioning.

Stages of Mania

  • Stage 1: Euphoria - Mild elevation of mood. Usually seen in hypomania.
  • Stage 2: Elation - Moderate elevation of mood. Feeling of confidence and enjoyment.
  • Stage 3: Exaltation - Severe elevation of mood. Delusions of grandeur.
  • Stage 4: Ecstasy - Very severe elevation of mood. Seen in delirious & stupors mania.

Diagnostic Symptoms

3 or more of the following symptoms must be present:

  • Inflated self esteem/ grandiosity.
  • Decreased need for sleep.
  • More talkative than usual/ pressure of speech.
  • Flight of ideas.
  • Attention is easily drawn to irrelevant items.
  • Increase in goal directed activity (either socially, at work, school or sexually).
  • Excessive involvement in pleasurable activities that have a high potential for painful consequences.

Other Symptoms

  • Appearance - often reflects the prevailing mood. Brightly colored clothes.
  • Increased appetite - Food may be eaten greedily.
  • Irritability - when stopped from doing what they want.
  • Psychomotor activity - overactive. Often start activities but leave them unfinished & start new ones.
  • Speech - rapid & copious.
  • Grandiose delusions.
  • Hallucination - can occur in severe cases.

Management of Mania

Pharmacotherapy

  • Anti-psychotics: which helps to treat hallucinations & delusions.
    • Haloperidol 1.5-4mg upto three times a day.
    • Chlorpromazine 100-200mg IM stat then 12 hourly OR Haloperidol 5-10mg 12 hrly.
    • Olanzepine 15mg OD.
  • Mood stabilizers:
    • Lithium to control acute attacks and stabilize mood. Dose 0.4-1.2g daily as a single dose or in two divided doses.
    • Anticonvulsants can also be used like Na-valproate in pts unresponsive to lithium. 750mg daily in 2-3 divided doses.
    • Tegretol 200mg BD.
  • Benzodiazepines: diazepam is helpful in the initial stages of RX until lithium achieves full effect. Shouldnt be used for a long time to avoid dependence.

Other Therapies

  • Psychotherapy: Self help coping strategies & healthy lifestyle choices.
  • ECT: severe mania when pt does not tolerate or respond to lithium or other drugs.

Nursing Management

  • Identify and manage the cause.
  • Do various investigations (HB, blood sugars, BS for MPs, UECs, Head CT & MRI).
  • Monitor Bp before each dose & withhold if systolic BP is 90 or below.
  • Sleep state should be monitored to ensure 6-7 hrs of sleep.
  • Incase of extra pyramidal side effects like dystonia occurs one hour-48 hrs after starting treatment, give antiparkinsonian drugs-artane (0.5-6 mg daily in 2 to 4 divided doses).
  • Close monitoring of pt to avoid danger to self or others.

üåßÔ∏è Depression

Definition

Is a medical illness that causes persistent feelings of sadness & loss of interest.

Types/Levels of Depression

  • Transient depression
    Characterized by sadness, downhearted, crying, tiredness.
  • Mild depression
    Triggered by an external event and the experience follows normal grief reaction. Lasts less than 2 weeks.
  • Moderate depression
    Persists over time. The person experiences a sense of change and often seeks help.
  • Severe/Major depression
    Symptoms include: sadness, apathy, feelings of worthlessness, self blame, thoughts of suicide, weight loss, lessened interest in sex, sleeplessness, reduction in activity.

Causes

  • Loss: e.g. relationship, loved one, major body part or function.
  • Major life events: related to disruption of life patterns/ conflict.
  • Role changes: e.g. marriage, divorce, parenting or employment status.
  • Disruption of coping skills/stress.
  • Physical changes: due to medical illness or debilitating condition or drug induced.
  • Disturbance in neurotransmitter system.
  • Genetic factors.
  • Physical, sexual and emotional abuse.
  • Hormonal changes: especially during menopause.

Signs and Symptoms

Emotional

Feelings of sadness & unhappiness, Irritability & frustration, Angry outbursts, Feelings of worthlessness.

Physical

Fatigue, tiredness & loss of energy, Changes in appetite, Insomnia or excessive sleeping, Slowed speaking/body movements, Unexplained physical problems (backpain, headache).

Cognitive/Behavioral

Loss of interest or pleasure, Reduced sex drive, Indecisiveness, distractibility, Frequent thoughts of death or suicide, Crying spells.

Management

Pharmacotherapy

SSRI‚Äôs, TCA‚Äôs, Monoamine oxidase inhibitors.

Antidepressants-tricyclics: (imipramine, start at dose of 25-50mg each night and build to 100-150 mg) for 7-10days or until patient is stable.

Other Treatments

  • Electroconvulsive therapy (ECT): to relieve the underlying symptoms of psychosis due to depression.
  • Psychotherapy: psychological counselling is essential with focus on causes of depression. It helps clients to cope better with stressful life events.
    • Encourage client to discuss losses and changes in life situation.
    • Assist in developing short term goals.
  • Cognitive behavioral therapy
  • Family therapy and social support
  • Vagus nerve stimulation: Rx using electrical impulses with a surgically implanted pulse generator to affect the mood of the brain. It helps in chronic treatment of resistant depression.
  • Trans cranial magnetic stimulation: The use of powerful magnetic field to alter the brain activity. A large electromagnetic coil is held against your scalp near your forehead to produce an electric current in the brain. Its an option for pple not responding to antidepressants.

Nursing Interventions

  • Encourage daily exercise.
  • Engage client in group activities.
  • Promote adequate nutrition: weigh patient weekly to check for weight /gain.
  • Ensure adequate sleep and rest.
  • Encourage patient to stay out of bed during the day.
  • Behavior therapy: Rewarding acceptable behavior and providing social skills.
  • Maintenance of social support by family members.
  • Physical care: encourage and supervise personal hygiene.
  • Prevention of suicide.
  • Promoting home and community based care.

üé¢ Bipolar Disorders

Definition

It is a disorder in which there are alternating periods of depression and mania.

Classification

  • Bipolar 1: xterized by episodes of severe mania and severe depression.
  • Bipolar 11: xterized by episodes of hypomania and severe depression.

Symptoms

  • Manic episodes: symptoms include hyperactivity, speech pressure, hyper sexuality, delusions, abusive and sleeplessness.
  • Depressive episodes: symptoms are the same as those of major depression.

The current episode in bipolar mood disorder is specified as one of the following:

  • Hypomanic
  • Manic without psychotic symptoms
  • Manic with psychotic symptoms
  • Mild or moderate depression
  • Severe depression, without psychotic symptoms
  • Severe depression with psychotic symptoms
  • Mixed
  • In remission

üö´ Suicide

It is the commonest cause of death in psychiatric patients. Suicide means deliberate self harm. Suicide is among the top 13 causes of death according to WHO.

Terminology

  • Attempted suicide: unsuccessful suicidal act with non-fatal outcomes.
  • Parasuicide: Is a self injury that appears to have clear intent to cause bodily harm or death. It is a risk factor to suicide.
  • Suicidal gesture: An attempted suicide where the person never intends to die by suicide but sometimes some of those persons may accidentally die during the act.

Causes

  • Genetic: suicidal behaviour tends to run in families.
  • Psychiatric disorders: are the major causes of suicide - depression, alcoholism, drug dependence, schizophrenia.
  • Physical disorder: pts with incurable or painful physical disorders like cancer, HIV/AIDS.
  • Psychosocial factors: marital difficulties, loss of loved ones by death.

Risk Factors

  • Age ‚Äì greater than 40 years increases suicidal tendencies.
  • Male sex - common in males than in females.
  • Staying single.
  • Previous suicidal attempts.
  • Depression increases risk by 25 %.
  • Family history of suicide.
  • History of childhood abuse.
  • Alcohol & drug dependence.
  • Recent serious loss or major stressful life event-death or other loss.
  • Untreatable physical illness.
  • Unemployment.
  • Low frustration tolerance.

Management of Suicidal Patient

Assessment (SAD PERSONS Scale)

  • S ‚Äì Sex
  • A ‚Äì Age
  • D ‚Äì Depression
  • P ‚Äì Previous attempts
  • E ‚Äì Ethanol use
  • R ‚Äì Rational thinking loss
  • S ‚Äì Social support lacking
  • O ‚Äì Organized plan
  • N ‚Äì No spouse
  • S ‚Äì Sickness
Assess the lethality of the suicide plan: Evaluation of the suicide plan is important in determining the degree of suicide risk.

3 Elements of Lethality Assessment

  1. Is there a specific plan with details?
    • Jumping off a high place
    • Using a gun
    • Hanging
    • Poisoning with Carbon monoxide
    • Slashing ones waist
  2. How lethal is the proposed method?
  3. Is there access to the planned method?

Nursing Diagnosis

  • Risk for suicide related to various emotional states & situational circumstances (feelings of hopelessness, anger, frustration, abandonment, rejection), secondary to loss of job as evidenced by suicide attempt.
  • Self care deficit related to frustration evidenced by poor grooming.
  • Social isolation related to feelings of fear & shame as evidenced by avoidance of family members.
  • Anxiety
  • Altered nutrition
  • Sleep pattern disturbance

Interventions

  • Pharmacological: Antidepressants for pts with depressive disorders. Lithium incase pts have bipolar disorder (decreases suicide attempts). Antipsychotics (clozapine). Antianxiety (diazepam) to RX panic & insomnia.
  • ECT: decreases acute suicidal ideation.
  • Support: Encourage pt to join survivor support groups/networks.
  • Education: Teach pt on stress management & coping skills, communication skills after a suicide attempt.
  • Focus: A focus on personal strengths & positive thoughts & emotions is essential.
  • Counseling: should involve a NO-SUICIDE CONTRACT (No harm contract). This is a written contract in which the patient agrees not to harm himself, but to take an alternative action if feeling suicidal. E.g talk with staff.
  • Spiritual care: Have pastor / chaplain instill hope concerning gift of life to the patient.
