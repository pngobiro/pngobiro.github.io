üß© Autistic Disorder

Definition and Characteristics

Autism is a state of psychotic disorder with its onset in early infancy. Its characterized by a withdrawal of the child into self and into a fantasy world of his or her own creation.

Causes

  • Unsatisfactory mother child relationship.
  • Genetic.
  • Neurological complications like abnormality in brain structure.

Presenting Symptoms

Impairment in communication and imaginative activity:

  • Language delay or absence of language.
  • Abnormal speech patterns & content. E.g echolalia, perseveration.
  • Stereotypical or repetitive use of language.
  • Lack of spontaneous imaginative play.
  • Failure to imitate other‚Äôs activities /words.

Impairment in social interactions:

  • Lack of interest in social activities.
  • Limited eye-eye contact & facial expressions.
  • Absent social smile.
  • Indifference to affection & physical contact.
  • Not able to share enjoyment, interest, or achievement with others.
  • Failure to develop friendships or imaginative plays with peers.

Markedly restricted, stereotypical patterns of behaviour, interest and activities:

  • Rigid adherence to routines & catastrophic reactions to changes in environment E.g moving furniture.
  • Repetitive motor mannerisms E.g head banging, hand biting.
  • Pre-occupation with repetitive activities like pouring water, twirling string.
  • Persistent pre-occupation with parts of objects.

Other features:

  • Mental retardation- most autistic children have an IQ of less than 70. Present with mild to profound mental retardation.
  • Autistic children enjoy music.
  • Epilepsy is common in children with IQ of less than 50.
  • Lack of attachment to parents & absence of separation anxiety.

Nursing Diagnosis

  • Risk for self mutilation related to neurological alterations.
  • Impaired social interaction related to inability to trust; neurological alterations.
  • Impaired verbal communication related to withdrawal into the self; inadequate sensory stimulation; neurological alterations.
  • Disturbed personal identity related to inadequate sensory stimulation; neurological alterations.

Management

Behavioral Therapy

  • Development of a regular routine with as few changes as possible.
  • Structuring the classroom materials and maintainance of acquired learning.
  • Positive reinforcements to teach self care skills.
  • Speech therapy & sign language teaching.
  • Behavioral techniques to encourage interpersonal relations.

Psychotherapy

  • Parental counselling and supporting psychotherapy are very useful in allaying parental anxiety & guilt, & ensuring their active involvement in therapy.

Pharmacotherapy

  • Used to treat autism & associated epilepsy.
  • Haloperidol decreases the dopamine levels in the brain, hence decrease hyperactivity & behavioral symptoms.
  • Risperidone also antipsychotic can be used.
  • Other drugs include : SSRIs, chorpromazine, imipramine, multivitamins.
  • Antiepileptic medication is used for the treatment of generalized or other seizures.

Nursing Management

  • Observation to ensure safety & to assist in understanding the individual child.
  • Establishment of one-on‚Äìone relationships for support, & coordination of services.
  • Teach the family about the therapeutic effects & side effects of drugs.
  • Special parental support, teaching & reassurance of parents to avoid self blame.
  • The nurse should focus on the mother‚Äôs significance to her child, enhancing her self esteem & discussing specific plans to deal with child.

‚ö° Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD)

Definition and Prevalence

This problem is referred to as hyperactivity and is characterized by impulsivity (trait of acting without reflection & without thought to the consequences of the behaviour), excessive motor activity & difficulties in sustaining attention.

It is the commonest psychological referral to mental health & pediatric facilities. Its common in boys than girls. Occurs before the age of 8 years.

Clinical Features

  • Excessive or exaggerated muscular activity like harphazard running or fidgeting.
  • Difficulty in sustaining attention.
  • Being highly distractible.
  • Failure to follow instructions or respond to demands made on them.
  • Low tolerance to frustration.
  • Tend to be of low intelligence due to hyperactivity. IQ 7-15 which is below average.
  • They are socially intrusive and immature.
  • Have high rate of driving offences when they attain adolescence.
  • They don‚Äôt get along with their parents since they don‚Äôt obey them.
  • Their negative behaviour tends to make their peers to view them negatively.
  • They perform poorly in school with specific learning disabilities such as difficulties in reading or learning basic subjects in schools.

Causes

  • Genetics - hyperactive parents get children with ADHD.
  • Abnormal levels/deficits of neurotransmitters is associated with symptoms of inattention, hyperactivity, impulsivity, mood & aggression - dopamine, norepinephrine, serotonin.
  • Alterations in specific areas of the brain like ‚Äì frontal lobes, basal ganglia, caudate nucleus, cerebellum.
  • Intra-uterine exposure to toxic substances like alcohol. Causes fetal alcohol syndrome.
  • Perinatal influences like - prematurity, low birth weight, signs of fetal distress, prolonged labour, asphyxia.
  • Environmental exposure to lead during childhood.
  • Diet factors - food dyes/colourings, preservatives, artificial flavourings.

Management

Nursing Diagnosis

  • Risk for injury related to impulsive and accident-prone behaviour & inability to perceive self harm.
  • Impaired social interaction related to intrusive & immature behaviour.
  • Low self esteem related to dysfunctional family system & negative feedback.
  • Non compliance to task expectations related to low frustration tolerance & short attention span.

Medical Management

  • CNS stimulants - dextroamphetamine are given to increase the attention span, control hyperactive behaviour & improve learning ability.
    To reduce anorexia side effect of most drugs, the medication may be administered immediately after meals.
    To prevent insomnia, the last dose should be administered atleast 6 hrs before bedtime.
  • Atomoxetine ‚Äì Selective norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor.
    Signs of liver damage should be observed while pt is on atomoxetine - itching, dark urine, right upper quardrant pain, yellow skin & eyes, sorethroat, fever, malaise.
  • Bupropion - antidepressant

Nursing Management

  • Ensure the client has a safe environment. Remove objects from immediate area on which client could injure self as a result of random, hyperactive movements.
  • Identify deliberate behaviours that put the child at risk for injury. Institute consequences for repetition of this behaviour.
  • Develop a trusting relationship with the child.
  • Discuss with the client those behaviours that are and that are not acceptable.
  • Give immediate positive feedback for acceptable behaviour-positive reinforcement.
  • Provide an environment for task efforts that is free of distractions as possible. Client is highly distractible hence unable to perform in presence of even minimal stimulation.
  • The client should be weighed regularly at least weekly while on therapy with CNS stimulants becoz of potential for anorexia, weight loss & temporary interruption of growth & development.
  • Nurse should be careful to monitor cardiovascular function while pt is on CNS stimulants to avoid sudden death incase of cardiovascular disease.

Other Management

  • Behavioral therapy- positive reinforcement is encouraged.
  • Family therapy

üß† Intellectual Disability (Mental Retardation)

Definition

Previously known as mental retardation in DSM IV. Mental retardation is defined as 'significantly sub-average general intellectual functioning, that is accompanied by significant limitations in adaptive functioning' in such areas like self-care, work and safety.

For one to be diagnosed as having mental retardation, the problem should have started before the age of seventeen years. If it occurs thereafter, it is considered as dementia.

Mental retardation occurs among children throughout the world. It tends to increase in severity with age starting at five to six and reaches the peak at aged 15.

Levels of Mental Retardation

Level IQ Range Characteristics
Borderline 71-84 With early diagnosis, parental assistance and educational programs, they can adjust socially, master simple academic and occupational skills, and become self-reliant.
Mild 50-55 to 70 They form the majority. Do not show any brain pathology and are capable of schooling. Require supervision since they are unable to foresee the consequences of their actions. Can adjust socially and become self-reliant.
Moderate 35-40 to 50-55 Considered trainable (e.g. cooking). Low learning ability, not able to conceptualise. Physically appear clumsy with poor motor coordination. May turn out to be hostile and aggressive. Can acquire partial independence. Rare occasions of gifted specialised skills (e.g. music).
Severe 20-25 to 35-40 'Dependent retarded'. Severely retarded speech and motor development. Sensory and motor handicaps common. Able to develop limited personal hygiene/self-care skills. Can perform simple occupational tasks under supervision.
Profound Below 20-25 'Life support retarded'. Deficient in adaptive behaviour, unable to master simplest tasks. Physical deformities, CNS pathology, retarded growth common. Convulsive seizures, mutism, deafness, low resistance to disease. Short life expectancy. Custodial care throughout life.

Causes

  • Genetic factors, often due to chromosomal influence and it runs in families, for example, Down's syndrome.
  • Infections and toxic agents, especially during foetal development.
  • Prematurity and physical trauma. Premature babies, especially those with low birth weight, develop neurological disorders and often mental retardation.
  • Ionising radiation. The radiation may act directly on the fertilised ovum or may produce mutations in the sex cells of either or both parents, which in turn may lead to defective offspring.
  • Malnutrition and other biological factors also contribute to the condition.

Management

Nursing Diagnosis

  • Risk for injury related to altered physical mobility or aggressive behaviour.
  • Self care deficit related to altered physical mobility or lack of maturity.
  • Impaired verbal communication related to developmental alteration.
  • Impaired social interaction related to speech deficiencies or difficulty conforming to convectional social behaviour.
  • Delayed growth & development related to isolation from significant others; hereditary factors.
  • Anxiety (moderate to severe) related to hospitalization & absence of familiar surroundings.
  • Ineffective coping related to inadequate coping skills secondary to developmental delay.

General Management

Currently there is no cure, though with appropriate support & teaching most individuals can learn to do many things. There are various homes for the mentally retarded people & schools.

Various medical complications are managed. E.g autistic children are given antipsychotics or mood stabilizers to help with their behaviour.

  • Behavioral therapy.
  • Family therapy-counselling the family on ways of assisting the individual in self care & other activities.
  • Healthy lifestyle is encouraged & avoidance of high risk behaviours.

üò° Conduct Disorder

Definition

It is a serious behavioral and emotional disorder that can occur in children and teens. A child with this disorder may display a pattern of disruptive and violent behavior and have problems following rules.

It is not uncommon for children and teens to have behavior-related problems at some time during their development. However, the behavior is considered to be a conduct disorder when it is long-lasting and when it violates the rights of others, goes against accepted norms of behavior and disrupts the child's or family's everyday life.

Symptoms

  • Aggressive behavior: These are behaviors that threaten or cause physical harm and may include fighting, bullying, being cruel to others or animals, using weapons, and forcing another into sexual activity.
  • Destructive behavior: This involves intentional destruction of property such as arson (deliberate fire-setting) and vandalism (harming another person's property).
  • Deceitful behavior: This may include repeated lying, shoplifting, or breaking into homes or cars in order to steal.
  • Violation of rules: This involves going against accepted rules of society or engaging in behavior that is not appropriate for the person's age. These behaviors may include running away, skipping school, playing pranks, or being sexually active at a very young age.

In addition, many children with conduct disorder are irritable, have low self-esteem, and tend to throw frequent temper tantrums. Some may abuse drugs and alcohol. Children with conduct disorder often are unable to appreciate how their behavior can hurt others and generally have little guilt or remorse about hurting others.

Causes

  • Biological: Some studies suggest that defects or injuries to certain areas of the brain can lead to behavior disorders. Conduct disorder has been linked to particular brain regions involved in regulating behavior, impulse control, and emotion.
  • Genetics: Many children and teens with conduct disorder have close family members with mental illnesses, including mood disorders, anxiety disorders, substance use disorders and personality disorders.
  • Environmental: Factors such as a dysfunctional family life, childhood abuse, traumatic experiences, a family history of substance abuse, and inconsistent discipline by parents may contribute to the development of conduct disorder.
  • Psychological: Some experts believe that conduct disorders can reflect problems with moral awareness (notably, lack of guilt and remorse) and deficits in cognitive processing.
  • Social: Low socioeconomic status and not being accepted by their peers appear to be risk factors for the development of conduct disorder.

Diagnosis and Treatment

Diagnosis: Medical and psychiatric history, physical exam, laboratory tests (blood tests), neuroimaging studies.

Treatment

  • Individual Psychotherapy (a type of counseling) is aimed at helping the child learn to express and control anger in more appropriate ways.
  • Cognitive-behavioral therapy aims to reshape the child's thinking (cognition) to improve problem solving skills, anger management, moral reasoning skills, and impulse control.
  • Family therapy may be used to help improve family interactions and communication among family members.
  • A specialized therapy technique called parent management training (PMT) teaches parents ways to positively alter their child's behavior in the home.

üíß Enuresis (Bedwetting)

Causes

  • Poor toilet training
  • Delay in ability to hold urine
  • Small bladders
  • Poor sleep habits
  • Problems in hormones that help regulate urination
  • Family history of bedwetting

Management

  • Positive reinforcement eg. Use of sticker charts for dry nights
  • Night time alarm clocks
  • Bladder training to help increase bladder size and childs ability to know when they have to urinate
  • Decrease fluids and caffeine at night

üçΩÔ∏è Eating Disorders

For many, eating symbolizes parental nurturing ‚Äì the love and care that are the prototype of and a basis for all future intimate relationships. For some, however, eating creates anxiety because of its association with unsatisfactory and unpleasant parent-child interactions. Clearly, food and eating have greater individual and cultural meaning and importance than merely an activity undertaken to sustain life.

Anorexia Nervosa

This is a life-threatening eating disorder characterized by:

  • the client‚Äôs refusal or, inability to maintain a minimally normal body weight (85% less than expected for their age and height)
  • intense fear of gaining weight or becoming fat (Main Sign)
  • significantly disturbed perception of the shape or size of the body
  • steadfast inability or refusal to acknowledge the seriousness of the problem or even that one exists
  • Experienced amenorrhea for at least three consecutive cycles
  • A preoccupation with food and food-related activities

Signs and Symptoms

  • Body image disturbance
  • Amenorrhea
  • Depressive symptoms such as depressed mood, social withdrawal, irritability, and insomnia
  • Feelings of ineffectiveness
  • Inflexible thinking
  • Strong need to control the environment
  • Limited spontaneity and overly restrained emotional expression
  • Complaints of constipation and abdominal pain
  • Cold intolerance
  • Lethargy
  • Emaciation
  • Hypotension, hypothermia and bradycardia
  • Hypertrophy of salivary glands
  • Elevated BUN
  • Electrolyte imbalances
  • Leukopenia and mild anemia
  • Elevated liver function studies

Bulimia Nervosa

Is an eating disorder characterized by:

  • Recurrent episodes (at least twice a week for 3 months) of binge eating (consuming a large amount of food, far greater than most people eat at a time, in a discrete period of usually 2 hours or less)
  • Binge eating followed by inappropriate compensatory behaviors to avoid weight gain such as: Purging (self-induced vomiting, misuse of laxatives, enemas, diuretics), Fasting, Excessively exercising.

Signs and Symptoms

  • Self-evaluation overly influenced by body shape and weight
  • Usually within normal weight range, possible underweight or overweight
  • Restriction of total calorie consumption between binges, selecting low-calorie foods while avoiding foods perceived to be fattening are likely to trigger a binge
  • Depressive and anxiety symptoms
  • Possible substance use involving alcohol or stimulants
  • Loss of dental enamel
  • Chipped, ragged, or moth eaten appearance of the teeth
  • Increased dental caries
  • Menstrual irregularities
  • Dependence on laxatives
  • Esophageal tears
  • Fluid and electrolyte abnormalities: Metabolic alkalosis (from vomiting) or metabolic acidosis (from diarrhea)
  • Mildly elevated serum amylase levels

Risk Factors for Eating Disorders

Disorder Biologic Developmental Family Socio-Cultural
Anorexia Nervosa Obesity; dieting at an early age Issues of developing autonomy and having control over self and environment; developing a unique identity; dissatisfaction with body image Family lacks emotional support; parental maltreatment; cannot deal with conflict Cultural ideal of being thin; media focus on beauty, thinness, fitness, preoccupation with achieving the ideal body
Bulimia Nervosa Obesity; early dieting; possible serotonin and norepinephrine disturbances; chromosome 1 susceptibility Self-perceptions of being overweight, fat, unattractive, and undesirable; dissatisfaction with body image Chaotic family with loose boundaries; parental maltreatment including possible physical or sexual abuse Same with above, weight-related teasing

Nursing Management for Eating Disorders

Common Nursing Diagnoses: Body image disturbance, Self-esteem disturbance, Ineffective individual coping.

Interventions for Anorexia Nervosa

  • Promote improved nutrition ‚Äì assume a calm, matter-of-fact attitude and positive expectation of the client, meeting minimal nutritional goals is non-negotiable.
    • Tube or IV feedings
    • Weigh daily, record intake and output, observe client during meals and bathroom activities
    • Avoid discussing food, recipes, restaurants and eating
    • Provide a pleasant meal time environment and adopt realistic expectations of how much the client will eat
    • Frequent, small meals are more acceptable
    • Set time limit of about one-half hour to forestall mealtime ‚Äúmarathon‚Äù
    • Collaborate with a dietitian
    • Acknowledge and recognize efforts of clients who meet weight gain goals but avoid praise or flattery
    • Behavior modification therapy can help
  • Promote effective individual coping
    • The best way is to involve the clients in their own treatment planning
    • Give clients the opportunity to practice problem solving.
    • Set firm, clear limits to provide the secure environment needed to learn more effective coping behaviors
    • Explore client‚Äôs feelings about their families, their roles in the family and their autonomy within the family system

Interventions for Bulimia Nervosa

  • Promoting effective coping with anxiety ‚Äì help them recognize events that create anxiety and to avoid binging and purging in response to anxiety
  • Promoting improved fluid volume
  • Promoting effective individual coping
    • It is important for clients to identify situations or patterns of events that precede episodes of binging and purging.
    • They need to learn effective ways of expressing feelings and assertive techniques to diminish guilt interactions in the future
  • Promoting effective family coping
