Introduction

Cognition is the brain's ability to process, retain, and use information. A cognitive disorder is a disruption or impairment in these higher level functions of the brain. The DSM-5 reconceptualized these disorders as neurocognitive disorders (NCDs), which includes delirium, major NCD, and mild NCD. This chapter focuses on delirium and dementia, emphasizing the care of clients and the needs of their caregivers.

Delirium

Delirium is a syndrome that involves a disturbance of consciousness accompanied by a change in cognition. It usually develops over a short period (hours to days) and fluctuates throughout the course of the day. Clients have difficulty paying attention, are easily distracted, and may have sensory disturbances such as illusions, misinterpretations, or hallucinations.

Elderly patients are the group most frequently diagnosed with delirium. It is common in postsurgical patients and terminally ill patients. Causes often include multiple stressors such as trauma, drug toxicity, or metabolic disturbances.

BOX 24.1 Most Common Causes of Delirium

  • Physiological/Metabolic: Hypoxemia, electrolyte disturbances, renal/hepatic failure, hypoglycemia, dehydration, sleep deprivation, vitamin deficiency, brain tumor, head injury.
  • Infection: Sepsis, UTI, pneumonia, meningitis, encephalitis, HIV, syphilis.
  • Drug Related: Intoxication (anticholinergics, lithium, alcohol, sedatives), Withdrawal (alcohol, sedatives), Reactions to anesthesia or prescription drugs.

Treatment and Prognosis

The primary treatment is to identify and treat the underlying medical condition. Delirium is usually transient. Psychopharmacology may include antipsychotics like haloperidol (Haldol) for agitation. Benzodiazepines are generally avoided unless the delirium is caused by alcohol withdrawal, as they may worsen delirium in the elderly.

Nursing Process: Delirium

Assessment: History of medical illness, alcohol/drug use. General appearance may be restless/hyperactive or sluggish/lethargic. Mood is labile. Thought process is disorganized. Sensorium is impaired (disoriented to time/place, impaired memory). Judgment is poor.

NURSING CARE PLAN: Delirium

Diagnosis: Acute Confusion

Outcomes:

  • Free of injury.
  • Engage in trust relationship within 24 hours.
  • Increase reality contact within 24-48 hours.

Interventions:

  • Safety: Teach to request assistance. Close supervision. Restraints only as last resort.
  • Managing Confusion: Speak calmly and clearly. Allow time for response. Provide orienting cues (calendar, clock). Use supportive touch if tolerated. Reduce environmental stimulation (sensory overload).
  • Physical Needs: Monitor sleep, nutrition, and elimination.

Dementia (Major Neurocognitive Disorder)

Dementia is a disease process marked by progressive cognitive impairment with no change in the level of consciousness. It involves multiple cognitive deficits:

  • Aphasia: Deterioration of language function.
  • Apraxia: Impaired ability to execute motor functions despite intact motor abilities.
  • Agnosia: Inability to recognize or name objects despite intact sensory abilities.
  • Executive functioning disturbance: Inability to think abstractly, plan, initiate, sequence, monitor, and stop complex behavior.

Types of Dementia

  • Alzheimer disease: Progressive disorder with atrophy of cerebral neurons and senile plaque deposits.
  • Lewy body dementia: Progressive cognitive impairment with extensive neuropsychiatric symptoms and motor symptoms.
  • Vascular dementia: Abrupt onset, often following stroke, with a stepwise progression.
  • Frontotemporal lobar degeneration (Pick disease): Affects frontal/temporal lobes; personality changes occur early.
  • Prion diseases: E.g., Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease. Rapid progression.
  • Parkinson disease: Dementia affects 25-75% of patients.
  • Huntington disease: Inherited; involves cerebral atrophy and choreiform movements.

Treatment

Pharmacology: Cholinesterase inhibitors (Donepezil, Rivastigmine, Galantamine) slow progression temporarily. Memantine (Namenda) blocks NMDA receptors to slow progression in moderate/severe stages. Antipsychotics are used for psychotic symptoms but carry a black box warning for increased mortality in elderly patients.

Nursing Process: Dementia

Assessment: Confabulation (making up answers to fill memory gaps) may occur. Loss of ability to perform familiar tasks (apraxia). Labile mood, catastrophic emotional reactions. Poor judgment and insight.

NURSING CARE PLAN: Dementia

Diagnosis: Impaired Memory

Interventions:

  • Provide opportunities for reminiscence.
  • Use written cues (calendars, lists).
  • Minimize environmental changes; establish routine.
  • Provide single-step instructions.
  • Integrate reminders of previous events.

Interventions for Dementia

Therapeutic Interaction:

  • Reminiscence therapy: Thinking about or relating personally significant past experiences.
  • Distraction: Shifting attention to a neutral topic when the client is agitated.
  • Time away: Leaving the client for a short period and returning to reengage.
  • Going along: Providing emotional reassurance without correcting misperception (used for delusions in dementia when reality orientation causes distress).
  • Reframing: Offering alternative explanations for events to decrease fear.

Role of the Caregiver

Most caregivers are women (often adult daughters). Caregiving is exhausting and stressful, leading to role strain. Caregivers need education, outlets for feelings (support groups), and respite care to maintain their own well-being. The STAR Program is a best practice training for caregivers to decrease depression and burden.

Self-Awareness Issues

Nurses may feel frustrated by the lack of improvement in dementia clients. It is important to find satisfaction in providing dignity and emotional support, even if the client cannot express gratitude. Discussing feelings with colleagues helps prevent burnout.

Key Points

  • ‚óè

    Cognitive disorders (NCDs) involve disruption in higher brain functions. Delirium is acute and reversible; dementia is chronic and progressive.

  • ‚óè

    Delirium is usually caused by an underlying medical condition (infection, toxicity, metabolic imbalance). Safety is the priority.

  • ‚óè

    Dementia involves memory loss, aphasia, apraxia, agnosia, and executive functioning disturbance. Alzheimer disease is the most common type.

  • ‚óè

    Nursing care for dementia focuses on safety, structure, physiological needs, and preserving dignity. Psychosocial interventions (reminiscence, distraction) are key.

  • ‚óè

    Caregivers require ongoing education and support to prevent role strain and burnout.

CLINICAL EXAMPLE

Martha Smith, 79, with Alzheimer disease, is admitted to a nursing home. She has poor judgment, no short-term memory, and is suspicious. She wanders at night and eats little.

  1. Additional Assessments: Food preferences, usual hygiene routine, favorite activities, value personal items.
  2. Nursing Diagnoses: Chronic Confusion, Impaired Social Interaction, Disturbed Sleep Pattern, Self-Care Deficit, Risk for Imbalanced Nutrition.
  3. Interventions:
    • Confusion: Use orienting cues, do not force decisions.
    • Social Interaction: One-on-one activities initially.
    • Nutrition: Finger foods, eat in quiet area.
    • Sleep: Soothing night routine, discourage day naps.
    • Hygiene: Imitate home routine, structured schedule.
