Introduction

Personality can be defined as an ingrained, enduring pattern of behaving and relating to the self, others, and the environment; it includes perceptions, attitudes, and emotions. Personality disorders are diagnosed when there is impairment of personality functioning and personality traits that are maladaptive. Individuals have identity problems such as egocentrism, or being self-centered, and their sense of self-esteem comes from gaining power or pleasure that is often at the expense of others.

Maladaptive or dysfunctional personality traits exhibited by individuals with a personality disorder may include:

  • Negative behaviors toward others, such as being manipulative, dishonest, deceitful, or lying
  • Anger and/or hostility
  • Irritable, labile moods
  • Lack of guilt or remorse, emotionally cold and uncaring
  • Impulsivity, distractibility, poor judgment
  • Irresponsible, not accountable for own actions
  • Risk-taking, thrill-seeking behaviors
  • Mistrust
  • Exhibitionism
  • Entitlement
  • Dependency, insecurity
  • Eccentric perceptions

Categories of Personality Disorders

Personality disorder diagnoses are organized according to clusters around a predominant type of behavioral pattern.

Cluster A: Odd or Eccentric Behaviors

  • Paranoid personality disorder: Characterized by pervasive mistrust and suspiciousness of others.
  • Schizoid personality disorder: Characterized by detachment from social relationships and a restricted range of emotional expression.
  • Schizotypal personality disorder: Characterized by acute discomfort in relationships, cognitive or perceptual distortions, and eccentricities of behavior.

Cluster B: Erratic or Dramatic Behaviors

  • Antisocial personality disorder: Characterized by disregard for and violation of the rights of others.
  • Borderline personality disorder (BPD): Characterized by instability of interpersonal relationships, self-image, and affects, and marked impulsivity.
  • Histrionic personality disorder: Characterized by excessive emotionality and attention seeking.
  • Narcissistic personality disorder: Characterized by grandiosity, need for admiration, and lack of empathy.

Cluster C: Anxious or Fearful Behaviors

  • Avoidant personality disorder: Characterized by social inhibition, feelings of inadequacy, and hypersensitivity to negative evaluation.
  • Dependent personality disorder: Characterized by submissive and clinging behavior related to an excessive need to be taken care of.
  • Obsessive-compulsive personality disorder: Characterized by preoccupation with orderliness, perfectionism, and control.

Etiology

Biologic Theories

Personality develops through the interaction of hereditary dispositions and environmental influences. Temperament refers to the biologic processes of sensation, association, and motivation that underlie the integration of skills and habits based on emotion. The four temperament traits are:

  1. Harm avoidance: Fear of uncertainty, social inhibition, shyness.
  2. Novelty seeking: Quick-tempered, curious, easily bored, impulsive.
  3. Reward dependence: Tenderhearted, sensitive, sociable, dependent on approval.
  4. Persistence: Hardworking, ambitious, overachiever.

Psychodynamic Theories

Character consists of concepts about the self and the external world. It develops over time as a person comes into contact with people and situations. Three major character traits have been distinguished:

  • Self-directedness: Responsible, reliable, resourceful, goal-oriented.
  • Cooperativeness: Empathic, tolerant, compassionate, supportive.
  • Self-transcendence: Spiritual, unpretentious, humble, fulfilled.

Treatment

Personality disorders are often difficult to treat because personality characteristics are deeply ingrained. Combinations of medication and group and individual therapies are more likely to be effective than any single treatment.

Psychopharmacology

Pharmacologic treatment focuses on the client's symptoms rather than the particular subtype. Four symptom categories underlie personality disorders:

  • Cognitive-perceptual distortions: Magical thinking, odd beliefs, illusions, suspiciousness, ideas of reference, and low-grade psychotic symptoms. Usually respond to low-dose antipsychotic medications.
  • Affective symptoms and mood dysregulation: Emotional instability, detachment, depression, and dysphoria. Treated with lithium, carbamazepine, valproate, or low-dose neuroleptics. Emotional detachment often responds to SSRIs or atypical antipsychotics.
  • Aggression and behavioral dysfunction: Impulsivity, predatory aggression. Treated with lithium, anticonvulsant mood stabilizers, and benzodiazepines.
  • Anxiety: Chronic cognitive or somatic anxiety. Treated with SSRIs, MAOIs, or low-dose antipsychotics.

Psychotherapy

Individual and group psychotherapy goals focus on building trust, teaching basic living skills, providing support, decreasing distressing symptoms, and improving interpersonal relationships. Cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT) helps clients recognize negative thoughts and feelings and replace them with positive patterns. Dialectical behavior therapy (DBT) was designed specifically for clients with BPD to focus on distorted thinking and behavior based on the assumption that poorly regulated emotions are the underlying problem.

Cluster A: Odd or Eccentric Behaviors

Paranoid Personality Disorder

Clinical Picture: Pervasive mistrust and suspiciousness of others. Clients interpret others' actions as potentially harmful. They appear aloof, withdrawn, guarded, and hypervigilant. They use the defense mechanism of projection.

Nursing Interventions: Approach in a formal, business-like manner. Be on time, keep commitments, and be straightforward. Involve the client in formulating the care plan. Help clients validate ideas before taking action.

Schizoid Personality Disorder

Clinical Picture: Pervasive pattern of detachment from social relationships and restricted emotional expression. Clients appear aloof, indifferent, and emotionally cold. They have a rich fantasy life but lack the desire for involvement with others.

Nursing Interventions: Focus on improved functioning in the community. Help find suitable housing that accommodates the need for solitude. Establish a working relationship with a case manager.

Schizotypal Personality Disorder

Clinical Picture: Pervasive pattern of social and interpersonal deficits, acute discomfort with relationships, and cognitive or perceptual distortions (ideas of reference, magical thinking). Appearance may be odd or unkempt.

Nursing Interventions: Develop self-care and social skills. Encourage a daily routine for hygiene. Role-play social interactions. Focus on functioning in the community.

BEST PRACTICE: Treatment of Schizotypal Personality Disorder

Second-generation antipsychotics (mainly risperidone) are most often used. Early identification is crucial as this disorder can lead to schizophrenia.

Cluster B: Erratic or Dramatic Behaviors

Antisocial Personality Disorder

Clinical Picture: Pervasive pattern of disregard for and violation of the rights of others, deceit, and manipulation. History of conduct disorder in childhood. Clients appear charming but lack empathy, remorse, and conscience. They view the world as hostile and use others for personal gain.

CLINICAL VIGNETTE: Antisocial Personality Disorder

Steve found himself in the local jail again after being arrested for burglary. He had told the police it wasn't breaking and entering; he had his friend's permission to use his parents' home, but they'd just forgotten to leave the key. Steve has a long juvenile record... He blames his trouble on "having the wrong friends." This is his third arrest. Steve's wife left him... He wishes he could win the lottery... He's tired of people demanding that he grow up, get a job, and settle down.

Nursing Process: Antisocial Personality Disorder

Assessment: Low frustration tolerance, impulsive behavior, poor judgment, conflict with authority, lack of remorse, manipulative behavior.

Interventions:

  • Forming a Therapeutic Relationship: Limit setting (state limit, consequence, expected behavior) and confrontation (point out problematic behavior neutrally).
  • Problem-Solving: Teach step-by-step problem-solving skills. Encourage time-outs to manage emotions.
  • Enhancing Role Performance: Identify barriers to success in work or family roles. Assess substance use.

NURSING CARE PLAN: Antisocial Personality Disorder

Diagnosis: Ineffective Coping

Interventions:

  • Identify unacceptable behaviors and consequences.
  • Be consistent and firm with the care plan.
  • Avoid arguing or trying to coax the client.
  • Provide immediate positive feedback for acceptable behavior.

Borderline Personality Disorder (BPD)

Clinical Picture: Pervasive pattern of unstable interpersonal relationships, self-image, and affect, and marked impulsivity. Clients experience dysphoric mood, fear of abandonment, and "splitting" (viewing people as all good or all bad). Recurrent self-mutilation and suicidal behavior are common.

CLINICAL VIGNETTE: Borderline Personality Disorder

Sally had been calling her therapist all day... She was sure her therapist was angry at her and was probably going to drop her as a client. Then she'd have no one... Sally was upset and crying as she began to run the razor blade across her arm. As the blood trickled out, she began to calm down.

Nursing Process: Borderline Personality Disorder

Assessment: History of disturbed early relationships, use of transitional objects, labile mood, dysphoria, splitting, impulsivity, self-harm.

Interventions:

  • Promoting Safety: Assess for suicidal ideation and self-harm. Use no-self-harm contracts (though controversial, can promote dialogue). Treat injuries matter-of-factly.
  • Therapeutic Relationship: Provide structure and limit setting. Establish clear boundaries.
  • Cognitive Restructuring: Thought stopping, positive self-talk, decatastrophizing.
  • Structuring Time: Written schedules to minimize unstructured time and boredom.

NURSING CARE PLAN: Borderline Personality Disorder

Diagnosis: Risk for Self-Mutilation

Interventions:

  • Assess history of self-harm.
  • Observe client closely.
  • Secure dangerous objects.
  • Encourage identification of feelings triggers self-harm.
  • Teach positive coping strategies.

Histrionic Personality Disorder

Clinical Picture: Pervasive pattern of excessive emotionality and attention seeking. Clients exaggerate emotions, obsess over appearance, and may be flirtatious or seductive. Emotions are shallow and changeable.

Nursing Interventions: Give feedback about social interactions. Teach social skills. Encourage assertive communication.

Narcissistic Personality Disorder

Clinical Picture: Pervasive pattern of grandiosity, need for admiration, and lack of empathy. Clients are arrogant, entitled, and hypersensitive to criticism. They exploit relationships for status.

Nursing Interventions: Use self-awareness to avoid anger. Set limits on rude behavior. Teach self-care skills matter-of-factly.

Cluster C: Anxious or Fearful Behaviors

Avoidant Personality Disorder

Clinical Picture: Pervasive pattern of social inhibition, feelings of inadequacy, and hypersensitivity to negative evaluation. Clients desire relationships but fear rejection.

Nursing Interventions: Provide support and reassurance. Help explore positive self-aspects. Practice social skills in a nonthreatening environment.

Dependent Personality Disorder

Clinical Picture: Pervasive and excessive need to be taken care of, leading to submissive and clinging behavior. Clients fear separation and have difficulty making decisions.

Nursing Interventions: Foster autonomy and self-reliance. Help clients identify strengths. Teach problem-solving and decision-making.

Obsessive-Compulsive Personality Disorder

Clinical Picture: Preoccupation with orderliness, perfectionism, and mental and interpersonal control. Different from OCD (an anxiety disorder). Clients are rigid, formal, and lack spontaneity. They have difficulty completing tasks due to perfectionism.

Nursing Interventions: Help clients view decision-making differently (e.g., meeting deadlines vs. perfection). Encourage negotiation and taking risks.

Self-Awareness Issues

Nurses working with clients with personality disorders may feel frustrated or angry due to the slow progress and manipulative behaviors. It is crucial to maintain professional boundaries and not take the client's behaviors personally. Team communication is essential to prevent "splitting" behaviors where the client pits staff members against each other.

Points to Consider

  • Talk to colleagues about feelings of frustration.
  • Clear, frequent communication helps diminish manipulation.
  • Set realistic goals; change is slow.
