Learning Objectives

After reading this chapter, you should be able to:

  1. Explain what is meant by "psychosomatic illness."
  2. Describe somatic symptom illnesses and identify their three central features.
  3. Distinguish somatic symptom illnesses from factitious disorders and malingering.
  4. Discuss the etiologic theories related to somatic symptom illnesses.
  5. Apply the nursing process to the care of clients with somatic symptom illnesses.

Introduction

Psychosomatic refers to the connection between the mind (psyche) and the body (soma). The mind can cause the body to create physical symptoms or worsen physical illnesses. Somatization is the transference of mental experiences and states into bodily symptoms.

Overview of Somatic Symptom Illnesses

Central features:

  • Physical complaints suggest major medical illness but have no demonstrable organic basis.
  • Psychological factors/conflicts are important in initiating, exacerbating, and maintaining symptoms.
  • Symptoms are not under the client's conscious control.

Types

  • Somatic Symptom Disorder: One or more physical symptoms with no organic basis. Excessive thoughts/energy focused on health concerns.
  • Conversion Disorder: Unexplained, sudden deficits in sensory or motor function (e.g., blindness, paralysis) suggesting a neurologic disorder. La belle indiff√©rence refers to a seeming lack of concern or distress about the functional loss.
  • Pain Disorder: Primary physical symptom is pain, unrelieved by analgesics, affected by psychological factors.
  • Illness Anxiety Disorder (Hypochondriasis): Preoccupation with the fear that one has a serious disease (disease conviction) or will get one (disease phobia).

Onset and Clinical Course

Usually chronic or recurrent. Clients often go from one provider to another ("doctor shopping") seeking a physical diagnosis. They tend to be pessimistic about the medical establishment.

CLINICAL VIGNETTE: Conversion Disorder

Matthew, 13, has sudden onset blindness. Extensive tests show no organic pathology. He displays la belle indiff√©rence, speaking calmly about his blindness. The nurse discovers Matthew witnessed his father's violent rage toward his mother 2 days prior.

Related Disorders (Intentional)

Distinguished from somatic symptom illnesses because symptoms are under conscious control.

  • Malingering: Intentional production of false/exaggerated symptoms for external incentives (avoiding work, evading prosecution, obtaining drugs).
  • Factitious Disorder (Munchausen Syndrome): Intentional production of symptoms solely to gain attention (sick role).
  • Factitious Disorder Imposed on Another (Munchausen by Proxy): Inflicting illness/injury on someone else to gain attention as a "hero" or caregiver.

Etiology

Psychosocial: Internalization of stress/anxiety expressed through physical symptoms. Primary gain: Direct internal benefit (relief of anxiety). Secondary gain: External benefits (attention, excused from responsibilities).

Biologic: Differences in regulation/interpretation of stimuli. Amplified sensory awareness.

Application of the Nursing Process

Assessment

Investigate physical health status first. Clients provide detailed accounts of physical problems. Mood is often labile. Thought content focuses on physical concerns. Little insight.

NURSING CARE PLAN: Conversion Disorder

Diagnosis: Ineffective Denial

Interventions:

  • After medical evaluation, withdraw attention from physical symptom.
  • Expect participation in activities.
  • Do not argue with client.
  • Focus interactions on feelings/relationships.
  • Teach coping strategies (problem-solving, stress management).
  • Praise client for discussing emotional issues.

Interventions

Health Teaching: Establish daily routine. Promote nutrition/sleep. Do not strip client of somatizing defenses until new coping skills are learned.

Expressing Emotions: Keep a journal connecting physical symptoms to stress. Limit time focused on complaints (e.g., 5 mins/hour).

Coping Strategies: Emotion-focused (relaxation) and problem-focused (problem-solving) strategies.

Self-Awareness Issues

Nurses may feel frustrated by clients' refusal to accept psychological basis for symptoms. Progress is slow. Must remain nonjudgmental and accept the client's complaints as real to them.

Points to Consider

  • Carefully assess physical complaints; do not assume they are psychological.
  • Validate the client's feelings ("I know you aren't feeling well...").
  • Remember symptoms are not under voluntary control.
