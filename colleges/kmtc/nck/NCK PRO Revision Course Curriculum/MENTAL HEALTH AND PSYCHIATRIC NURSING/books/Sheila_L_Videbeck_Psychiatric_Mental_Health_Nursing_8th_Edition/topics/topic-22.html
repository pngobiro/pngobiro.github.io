  1. Discuss the characteristics, risk factors, and family dynamics of autism spectrum disorder (ASD) and attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder (ADHD).
  2. Apply the nursing process to the care of children and adolescents with ASD and ADHD and their families.

  5. Evaluate your feelings, beliefs, and attitudes about clients with ASD and ADHD and their parents and caregivers.

Introduction

Psychiatric disorders are not diagnosed as easily in children as they are in adults. Children usually lack the abstract cognitive abilities and verbal skills to describe what is happening. This chapter focuses on neurodevelopmental disorders, usually diagnosed in infancy or childhood and sometimes adolescence; many of these disorders persist into adulthood.

Intellectual disability involves below-average intellectual functioning (IQ <70) accompanied by significant limitations in areas of adaptive functioning such as communication skills, self-care, home living, social or interpersonal skills, and health and safety. The degree of disability is based on IQ and cognitive functioning (mild, moderate, severe, or profound).

Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD)

Autism spectrum disorder (ASD) is characterized by pervasive and usually severe impairment of reciprocal social interaction skills, communication deviance, and restricted stereotypical behavioral patterns. Previous disorders such as Rett disorder, childhood disintegrative disorder, and Asperger disorder are now viewed on the continuum of the autism spectrum.

ASD is almost five times more prevalent in boys than in girls and is usually identified by 18 months to 3 years of age. Children with ASD may display little eye contact, few facial expressions, limited gestures, and lack of spontaneous enjoyment. They may engage in stereotyped motor behaviors such as hand flapping, body twisting, or head banging.

BOX 22.1 Behaviors Common with ASD

  • Not responding to own name by 1 year (e.g., appears not to hear)
  • Doesn't show interest by pointing to objects or people by 14 months of age
  • Doesn't play pretend games by 18 months of age
  • Avoids eye contact and prefers to be alone
  • Delayed speech and language skills
  • Obsessive interests (e.g., gets stuck on an idea)
  • Upset by minor changes in routine
  • Repeats words or phrases over and over
  • Flaps hands, rocks body, or spins in a circle
  • Unusual reactions to sounds, smells, or other sensory experiences

Treatment

The goals of treatment are to reduce behavioral symptoms and to promote learning and development, particularly the acquisition of language skills. Comprehensive and individualized treatment, including special education and language therapy, as well as cognitive behavioral therapy for anxiety and agitation, is associated with more favorable outcomes. Pharmacologic treatment with antipsychotics, such as haloperidol (Haldol), risperidone (Risperdal), or aripiprazole (Abilify), may be effective for specific target symptoms such as temper tantrums, aggressiveness, self-injury, hyperactivity, and stereotyped behaviors.

BEST PRACTICE: Early Intensive Behavioral Intervention (EIBI)

EIBI is based on principles of applied behavioral analysis and consists of 20 to 40 hours/week of intensive therapy for several months to years. Tasks are broken down into tiny steps that are taught and rewarded. Evidence shows improvement in expressive and receptive language and daily communication skills.

Related Disorders

Tic Disorders

A tic is a sudden, rapid, recurrent, nonrhythmic, stereotyped motor movement or vocalization. Tourette disorder involves multiple motor tics and one or more vocal tics, which occur many times a day for more than 1 year. Tic disorders are usually treated with risperidone (Risperdal) or olanzapine (Zyprexa).

Elimination Disorders

  • Encopresis: The repeated passage of feces into inappropriate places by a child who is at least 4 years of age. Often involuntary and associated with constipation.
  • Enuresis: The repeated voiding of urine during the day or at night into clothing or bed by a child at least 5 years of age. Treated with imipramine (Tofranil) or behavioral approaches (e.g., pad with warning bell).

Other Disorders

  • Specific Learning Disorder: Achievement in reading, mathematics, or written expression below that expected for age.
  • Developmental Coordination Disorder: Impaired coordination severe enough to interfere with academic achievement or ADLs.
  • Stereotypic Movement Disorder: Rhythmic, repetitive behaviors (hand waving, rocking, head banging) that appear to have no purpose.
  • Communication Disorders: Deficits in language, speech, and communication (e.g., stuttering, social communication disorder).
  • Sluggish Cognitive Tempo (SCT): A syndrome including daydreaming, trouble focusing, mental fogginess, staring, sleepiness, and slowness, but without hyperactivity or impulsivity.

Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD)

ADHD is characterized by inattentiveness, overactivity, and impulsiveness. It affects 5% to 8% of school-aged children, with many continuing to be symptomatic into adulthood. It is more common in boys.

Clinical Course

ADHD is usually identified when the child begins preschool or school. Symptoms include fidgeting, inability to sit still, excessive noise, distractibility, inability to listen to directions, and interrupting others. Socially, peers may ostracize the child. Adolescents with ADHD may engage in risky behaviors such as cutting class, speeding, or substance use.

Treatment

The most effective treatment combines pharmacotherapy with behavioral, psychosocial, and educational interventions.

Psychopharmacology

Medications decrease hyperactivity and impulsiveness and improve attention.

TABLE 22.1 Drugs Used to Treat ADHD
Drug Class Generic (Trade) Name Nursing Considerations
Stimulants Methylphenidate (Ritalin, Concerta, Daytrana) Monitor for appetite suppression or growth delays. Give after meals. Patch worn for 9 hours.
Dextroamphetamine (Dexedrine) Monitor for insomnia. Give last dose in early afternoon.
Amphetamine (Adderall) Monitor for insomnia and appetite suppression.
Lisdexamfetamine (Vyvanse) Capsule can be swallowed or dissolved in water.
Antidepressant (SNRI) Atomoxetine (Strattera) Give with food. Monitor for appetite suppression and elevated liver function.
Antihypertensives Clonidine (Kapvay), Guanfacine (Intuniv) Monitor for hypotension, dizziness, syncope, somnolence.

Strategies for Home and School

Therapeutic play techniques are used to understand the child's thoughts and feelings and to promote communication. Creative play techniques can help children express themselves. Behavioral strategies include consistent rewards and consequences, time-outs, and daily report cards.

Application of the Nursing Process: ADHD

CLINICAL VIGNETTE: ADHD

Scott, 8 years old, struggles with his morning routine. Despite rules, he plays with toys instead of dressing. He is easily distracted, jumping from one activity to another (dressing, playing with cars, stacking blocks). He constantly interrupts his mother and forgets instructions (like putting on socks). His mother is weary and has to constantly redirect him.

Assessment

History: Fussy as infant, "always on the go" toddler. Difficulty in all major life areas.
Appearance/Behavior: Cannot sit still, squirms, darts around. Speech is unimpaired but cannot carry conversation (interrupts, blurts out).
Mood/Affect: Labile mood, anxiety, frustration, agitation.
Intellectual Process: Alert and oriented but attention span is markedly impaired (2-3 seconds to minutes). Distractible.
Judgment/Insight: Poor judgment, impulsive. Unaware behavior is different from others.

NURSING CARE PLAN: ADHD

Diagnosis: Ineffective Role Performance

Outcomes:

  • Complete tasks with assistance (Immediate).
  • Demonstrate acceptable social skills (Immediate).
  • Participate successfully in educational setting (Stabilization).
  • Verbalize positive statements about self (Community).

Interventions:

  • Ensuring Safety: Stop unsafe behavior immediately. Provide close supervision. Give clear directions.
  • Improving Role Performance: Give positive feedback. Manage environment (reduce distractions).
  • Simplifying Instructions: Gain full attention before speaking. Break complex tasks into small steps. Allow breaks.
  • Routine: Promote a structured daily routine.
  • Client/Family Education: Include parents in planning. Teach about medication. Support groups.

Mental Health Promotion

Early detection and successful intervention are key. The SNAP-IV Teacher and Parent Rating Scale (Box 22.3) is an assessment tool used for initial evaluation of ADHD, ODD, and conduct disorders. Early identification helps children reach their maximum potential.

Self-Awareness Issues

Working with children and adolescents can be rewarding but difficult. Nurses must avoid being overly critical of parents, understanding that caring for a child with these disorders 24/7 is exhausting. The nurse should focus on the strengths and assets of both the child and the parents, support their efforts to remain hopeful, and ask how the parents are doing.
