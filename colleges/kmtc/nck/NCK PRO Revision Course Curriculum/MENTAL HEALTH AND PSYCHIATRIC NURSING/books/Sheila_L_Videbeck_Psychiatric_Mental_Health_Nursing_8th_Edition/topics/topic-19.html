Introduction

Substance use disorders and related disorders are a national health problem. More than 16.6 million adult Americans and 697,000 adolescents have an alcohol use disorder. Alcohol-related death is the third leading preventable cause of death in the United States.

The "opioid crisis" is a major concern, with an estimated 130 deaths occurring daily in the United States from opioid overdose.

Types of Substance Abuse

Categories of drugs include alcohol, sedatives, hypnotics, and anxiolytics, stimulants, cannabis, opioids, hallucinogens, and inhalants.

Definitions

  • Intoxication: Use of a substance that results in maladaptive behavior.
  • Withdrawal syndrome: Negative psychological and physical reactions that occur when use of a substance ceases or dramatically decreases.
  • Detoxification: The process of safely withdrawing from a substance.
  • Substance abuse/dependence: Using a drug in a way that is inconsistent with medical or social norms and despite negative consequences.

Onset and Clinical Course

Alcohol: The first episode of intoxication often occurs between ages 12 and 14. A pattern of more severe difficulties emerges in the mid-20s to mid-30s, often characterized by the first blackout. Tolerance develops, followed by a tolerance break where very small amounts intoxicate the person. Spontaneous remission or "natural recovery" can occur, but poor outcomes are associated with earlier onset and longer periods of use.

Specific Substances

Alcohol

Intoxication/Overdose: CNS depressant. Overdose can result in vomiting, unconsciousness, and respiratory depression. Treatment involves gastric lavage or dialysis.

Withdrawal: Symptoms begin 4 to 12 hours after cessation. Includes tremors, sweating, elevated pulse/BP, insomnia, anxiety. Severe withdrawal can progress to delirium tremens (hallucinations, seizures). Withdrawal is usually managed with benzodiazepines (e.g., lorazepam, chlordiazepoxide) using a fixed schedule or symptom-triggered dosing (using CIWA-AR scale).

Clinical Institute Withdrawal Assessment (CIWA-AR)

Assesses nausea, tremor, paroxysmal sweats, anxiety, agitation, tactile disturbances, auditory disturbances, visual disturbances, headache, and orientation. A score less than 10 usually indicates no need for additional medication.

Sedatives, Hypnotics, and Anxiolytics

Includes benzodiazepines and barbiturates. Intoxication symptoms are similar to alcohol. Benzodiazepine withdrawal can be managed by tapering the dose.

Stimulants

Includes amphetamines, cocaine, methamphetamine. Intoxication includes euphoria, hyperactivity, hypervigilance, anxiety, and hallucinations. Withdrawal causes marked dysphoria ("crashing") and fatigue but is not life-threatening.

Cannabis

Most widely used illegal drug (though legal status is changing). Intoxication includes lowered inhibitions, relaxation, euphoria, and increased appetite. No clinically significant withdrawal syndrome is identified.

Opioids

Includes morphine, heroin, codeine, fentanyl, methadone. Intoxication involves apathy, lethargy, constricted pupils, and drowsiness. Overdose can cause coma, respiratory depression, and death; treated with naloxone (Narcan). Withdrawal includes anxiety, restlessness, aching back/legs, nausea, vomiting, dysphoria, and sweating.

Hallucinogens

Includes LSD, PCP, ecstasy. Distorts perception of reality. Intoxication includes anxiety, depression, paranoid ideation. PCP toxicity can include seizures and hypertension. No withdrawal syndrome, but flashbacks can occur.

Inhalants

Includes gasoline, glue, paint thinner. Intoxication involves dizziness, nystagmus, uncoordination, and slurred speech. Acute toxicity causes anoxia and respiratory depression. Can cause significant brain damage.

Treatment

12-Step Program (AA): Based on the philosophy that total abstinence is essential and alcoholics need the help and support of others. Uses a spiritual (non-religious specific) approach.

Dual Diagnosis: Clients with both substance abuse and another psychiatric illness. Successful treatment often requires integrated programs that address both issues simultaneously.

CLINICAL VIGNETTE: Dual Diagnosis

Michael, 33, has schizophrenia and alcohol dependence. He drinks to quiet the "voices." Traditional treatment failed; he is now homeless. He requires an integrated approach addressing housing, social support, and treatment for both conditions.

Application of the Nursing Process

Assessment

Screening tools like the SSI-AOD or CAGE questionnaire help identify problems. Denial is a common defense mechanism.

NURSING CARE PLAN: Alcohol Withdrawal

Diagnosis: Risk for Injury (withdrawal)

Interventions:

  • Monitor vital signs and CIWA score.
  • Administer benzodiazepines as ordered.
  • Provide safe, quiet environment.
  • Reorient client as needed.

Interventions

Health Teaching: Dispel myths (e.g., "I can stop anytime"). Teach about relapse triggers.

Addressing Family Issues: Codependence is a maladaptive coping pattern in family members. Enabling behaviors seem helpful but perpetuate substance use. Refer families to Al-Anon or AlaTeen.

Promoting Coping Skills: Focus on the here-and-now. Set realistic goals (e.g., "stay sober today").

Substance Abuse in Health Professionals

Physicians and nurses have higher rates of dependence on controlled substances than other professionals. Warning signs include incorrect drug counts, wasting of drugs, client reports of ineffective pain relief, and unexplained absences. Nurses have an ethical and legal responsibility to report suspected substance abuse in colleagues to a supervisor.
