Learning Objectives

After reading this chapter, you should be able to:

  1. Compare and contrast the symptoms of anorexia nervosa and bulimia nervosa.
  2. Discuss various etiologic theories of eating disorders.
  3. Identify effective treatment for clients with eating disorders.
  4. Apply the nursing process to the care of clients with eating disorders.
  5. Provide teaching to clients, families, and community members.

Introduction

Eating disorders can be viewed on a continuum, with anorexia (eating too little) on one end and obesity (eating too much) on the other. Bulimia involves chaotic eating. More than 90% of cases occur in women. The prevalence is estimated to be 2% to 4% of the general population.

Categories of Eating Disorders

Anorexia Nervosa

A life-threatening disorder characterized by:

  • Restriction of nutritional intake necessary to maintain minimally normal body weight.
  • Intense fear of gaining weight or becoming fat.
  • Significantly disturbed perception of body shape or size (body image disturbance).
  • Inability to acknowledge the seriousness of the problem.

Subtypes:

  1. Restricting subtype: Weight loss through dieting, fasting, or excessive exercise.
  2. Binge eating and purging subtype: Binge eating followed by purging (vomiting, laxatives, enemas, diuretics).

BOX 20.1 Physical Problems of Anorexia Nervosa

  • Amenorrhea
  • Constipation
  • Overly sensitive to cold, lanugo hair
  • Hair loss, dry skin
  • Bradycardia, arrhythmias, orthostasis
  • Electrolyte imbalance (hyponatremia, hypokalemia)

Bulimia Nervosa

Characterized by recurrent episodes of binge eating followed by inappropriate compensatory behaviors (purging, fasting, excessive exercise) to avoid weight gain. Weight is usually in the normal range. Recurrent vomiting destroys tooth enamel and causes dental caries.

Related Disorders

  • Binge eating disorder: Recurrent binge eating without compensatory behaviors.
  • Night eating syndrome: Morning anorexia, evening hyperphagia, nighttime awakenings to eat.
  • Pica: Persistent ingestion of nonfood substances.
  • Rumination: Repeated regurgitation of food that is rechewed, reswallowed, or spit out.
  • Orthorexia nervosa: Obsession with "proper" or healthful eating.

Etiology

Biologic: Genetic vulnerability, dysfunction of the hypothalamus (lateral vs. ventromedial), neurochemical changes (low norepinephrine in starvation, low serotonin in bulimia).

Developmental: Struggle for autonomy, identity. Body image disturbance.

Family: Family discord, lack of emotional support, childhood adversity.

Sociocultural: Media focus on thinness as beauty, pressure from others.

Clinical Course & Treatment

Anorexia Nervosa

Onset between 14 and 18 years. Treatment focuses on weight restoration, nutritional rehabilitation, and rehydration. Family therapy is effective for clients younger than 18. Individual therapy (CBT) is also used.

Pharmacology: Amitriptyline and cyproheptadine can promote weight gain. Olanzapine affects body image distortions. Fluoxetine prevents relapse.

CLINICAL VIGNETTE: Anorexia Nervosa

Maggie, 15, is 5'7" and weighs 92 lb. She lost 20 lb in 4 months. She wears layers of clothes to hide weight loss and stay warm. She exercises excessively. She denies having a problem, saying she is too fat.

Bulimia Nervosa

Onset late adolescence/early adulthood (18-19). Often treated as outpatient. CBT is most effective. Self-monitoring (diary of food, feelings, behaviors) is a key technique.

Pharmacology: Antidepressants (SSRIs like fluoxetine) reduce binge eating and improve mood.

CLINICAL VIGNETTE: Bulimia Nervosa

Susan eats from grocery bags while driving home to hide her eating. She consumes cookies, chips, and ham rapidly. She vomits immediately upon getting home, feeling guilty and ashamed. She promises herself to stop, but the cycle continues.

Application of the Nursing Process

Assessment

Use tools like the Eating Attitudes Test.

History: Perfectionists, eager to please (anorexia); impulsive, history of shoplifting/substance abuse (bulimia).

Appearance: Slow, lethargic, emaciated (anorexia); normal weight (bulimia).

Mood/Affect: Labile, sad, anxious. Anorexia: somber, serious. Bulimia: initially pleasant/cheerful, but ashamed of behaviors.

Thought Process: Preoccupied with food/dieting. Body image distortion can be delusional.

Judgment/Insight: Limited insight (anorexia); shame/guilt but feel out of control (bulimia).

Self-Concept: Low self-esteem; judge self-worth by weight.

Interventions

  • Nutritional Eating Patterns: Sit with client during meals. Monitor for 1-2 hours after meals to prevent purging. Weigh daily in uniform clothing.
  • Coping Strategies: Help identify emotions (alexithymia). Use self-monitoring. Relaxation and distraction.
  • Body Image: Help accept a more normal body weight. Identify non-food related strengths.
  • Education: Basic nutrition, harmful effects of purging.

NURSING CARE PLAN: Bulimia

Diagnosis: Ineffective Coping

Interventions:

  • Set limits on eating habits (eat at table, designated times).
  • Encourage expression of feelings (anxiety, guilt).
  • Use a diary to track food and feelings.
  • Teach problem-solving process.
