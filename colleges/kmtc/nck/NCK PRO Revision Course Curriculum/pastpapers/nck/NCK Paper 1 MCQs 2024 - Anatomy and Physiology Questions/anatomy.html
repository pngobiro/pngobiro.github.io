1.
A patient presents with muscle weakness and fatigue. Upon further examination, it is revealed that the patient has decreased acetylcholine receptors at the neuromuscular junction. Which of the following conditions is most likely responsible for this presentation?
A) Myasthenia gravis
B) Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS)
C) Guillain-Barre syndrome
D) Muscular dystrophy
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Myasthenia gravis

Explanation: Myasthenia gravis is an autoimmune disorder characterized by the production of antibodies that block or destroy acetylcholine receptors at the neuromuscular junction. This prevents proper muscle contraction, leading to weakness and fatigue that worsens with activity.

2.
Following a severe traumatic brain injury, a patient is experiencing alterations in consciousness characterized by periods of wakefulness but with no meaningful interaction with the environment. Which term best describes this state?
A) Coma
B) Vegetative state
C) Minimally conscious state
D) Locked-in syndrome
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Vegetative state

Explanation: A vegetative state is defined as wakefulness without awareness. The patient may have sleep-wake cycles and open their eyes (wakefulness), but lacks cognitive function or meaningful interaction with the environment (awareness).

3.
In response to chronic hypoxia, which physiological adaptation occurs in the body to increase oxygen delivery to tissues?
A) Decreased erythropoietin production
B) Decreased red blood cell production
C) Increased cardiac output
D) Decreased ventilation rate
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Increased cardiac output

Explanation: The body compensates for hypoxia by attempting to deliver more blood to tissues per minute. While the primary long-term adaptation is increased red blood cell production (Polycythemia) driven by increased erythropoietin, options A and B state "Decreased", making them incorrect. Therefore, increased cardiac output is the correct physiological response among the choices.

4.
A patient with a history of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) presents with increased dyspnea and cough productive of purulent sputum. On auscultation, coarse crackles are heard bilaterally. Which complication of COPD is the patient most likely experiencing?
A) Acute exacerbation
B) Pneumothorax
C) Pulmonary embolism
D) Pleural effusion
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Acute exacerbation

Explanation: An Acute Exacerbation of COPD (AECOPD) is characterized by a worsening of respiratory symptoms, specifically increased dyspnea, increased sputum volume, and sputum purulence (The Winnipeg Criteria). The presence of purulent sputum suggests an infectious trigger.

5.
A patient with a spinal cord injury at the level of T6 presents with hypotension, bradycardia, and diaphoresis following a sudden change in position from supine to sitting. Which condition is the patient most likely experiencing?
A) Autonomic dysreflexia
B) Neurogenic shock
C) Spinal shock
D) Orthostatic hypotension
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) Orthostatic hypotension

Explanation: The key trigger here is the "sudden change in position." Patients with spinal cord injuries often lack the sympathetic tone required to vasoconstrict peripheral vessels when standing up, leading to a drop in blood pressure. Note: Autonomic dysreflexia usually presents with hypertension, not hypotension.

6.
A patient with chronic kidney disease presents with metabolic acidosis. Which compensatory mechanism is the body most likely to employ to normalize pH?
A) Hyperventilation
B) Increased bicarbonate reabsorption
C) Increased renal excretion of hydrogen ions
D) Decreased renal excretion of bicarbonate ions
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Hyperventilation

Explanation: In metabolic acidosis, the lungs compensate by increasing the respiratory rate (hyperventilation/Kussmaul breathing) to blow off CO2 (an acid). The kidneys are the *cause* of the problem in CKD, so they cannot effectively compensate by excreting hydrogen or reabsorbing bicarbonate (options B and C).

7.
A patient presents with symptoms of polyuria, polydipsia, and polyphagia. Laboratory tests reveal hyperglycemia and glycosuria. Which type of diabetes mellitus is the patient most likely experiencing?
A) Type 1 diabetes mellitus
B) Type 2 diabetes mellitus
C) Gestational diabetes mellitus
D) Latent autoimmune diabetes in adults (LADA)
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Type 1 diabetes mellitus

Explanation: While the "3 Ps" (Polyuria, Polydipsia, Polyphagia) occur in both types, they are the classic abrupt presentation of Type 1 Diabetes (along with weight loss). Type 2 is often asymptomatic or gradual. Without age/weight context, Type 1 is the textbook association for this specific triad of symptoms appearing significantly.

8.
A patient with a history of chronic liver disease presents with jaundice, ascites, and spider angiomas. Laboratory tests reveal elevated serum bilirubin levels, prolonged prothrombin time, and hypoalbuminemia. Which condition is the patient most likely experiencing?
A) Hepatic encephalopathy
B) Hepatorenal syndrome
C) Cirrhosis
D) Acute liver failure
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Cirrhosis

Explanation: The constellation of symptoms (spider angiomas, ascites, jaundice) combined with lab findings (low albumin, high bilirubin, prolonged PT) indicates decompensated chronic liver disease, which defines Cirrhosis. Hepatic encephalopathy (A) would specifically mention confusion/asterixis.

9.
A patient with chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) is prescribed albuterol (salbutamol) inhaler as a rescue medication. Which of the following best describes the pharmacological action of albuterol?
A) Anticholinergic
B) Beta-adrenergic agonist
C) Corticosteroid
D) Methylxanthine
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Beta-adrenergic agonist

Explanation: Albuterol is a Short-Acting Beta Agonist (SABA). It stimulates beta-2 receptors in the smooth muscle of the airways, causing bronchodilation.

10.
A patient with a history of congestive heart failure is prescribed furosemide. Which electrolyte imbalance is the patient at risk for developing with furosemide therapy?
A) Hyperkalemia
B) Hypokalemia
C) Hyponatremia
D) Hypernatremia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Hypokalemia

Explanation: Furosemide is a loop diuretic that inhibits the reabsorption of sodium and chloride in the loop of Henle. This action significantly increases the excretion of potassium, placing the patient at high risk for hypokalemia.

11.
A patient is admitted with a suspected myocardial infarction. Which cardiac biomarker is most specific for myocardial injury?
A) Troponin
B) Creatine kinase-MB (CK-MB)
C) Myoglobin
D) Brain natriuretic peptide (BNP)
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Troponin

Explanation: Cardiac Troponins (I and T) are the most sensitive and specific biomarkers for myocardial necrosis. CK-MB is found in other muscles, and Myoglobin is non-specific. BNP is a marker for Heart Failure, not specifically MI injury.

12.
A patient with chronic kidney disease is prescribed erythropoietin-stimulating agents (ESAs) to manage anemia. Which laboratory parameter should be monitored closely during ESA therapy?
A) Serum potassium
B) Serum creatinine
C) Hemoglobin
D) Platelet count
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Hemoglobin

Explanation: ESAs stimulate red blood cell production. They must be monitored closely to ensure Hemoglobin does not rise too high (usually >11-12 g/dL), as this increases the risk of stroke, thrombosis, and hypertension.

13.
A patient with hypertension is prescribed an angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitor. Which adverse effect is most commonly associated with ACE inhibitors?
A) Hyperkalemia
B) Hypokalemia
C) Dry cough
D) Bradycardia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Dry cough

Explanation: A persistent dry cough is a classic side effect of ACE inhibitors, caused by the accumulation of bradykinin in the lungs. While Hyperkalemia (A) is a serious risk, the cough is the most *common* adverse effect leading to discontinuation.

14.
A patient with a history of rheumatoid arthritis is prescribed methotrexate. Which laboratory parameter should be monitored closely during methotrexate therapy?
A) Serum sodium
B) Serum creatinine
C) Liver function tests
D) White blood cell count
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Liver function tests

Explanation: Methotrexate is hepatotoxic. While it can also suppress bone marrow (affecting WBCs, option D), liver toxicity is a primary concern requiring regular monitoring of LFTs.

15.
A patient with a history of coronary artery disease is prescribed aspirin for secondary prevention. Which adverse effect is a potential concern with long-term aspirin therapy?
A) Thrombocytopenia
B) Gastrointestinal bleeding
C) Hypercholesterolemia
D) Hypertension
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Gastrointestinal bleeding

Explanation: Aspirin inhibits prostaglandins that protect the gastric lining, increasing the risk of ulcers and gastrointestinal bleeding.

16.
A patient with a history of heart failure is prescribed spironolactone. Which electrolyte imbalance is the patient at risk for developing with spironolactone therapy?
A) Hypernatremia
B) Hyperkalemia
C) Hypokalemia
D) Hyponatremia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Hyperkalemia

Explanation: Spironolactone is a potassium-sparing diuretic (Aldosterone antagonist). By blocking aldosterone, it promotes sodium excretion but retains potassium, leading to a risk of Hyperkalemia.

17.
A patient with chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) is prescribed oxygen therapy to maintain adequate oxygenation. Which assessment finding indicates the need for adjustment of oxygen therapy?
A) Decreased respiratory rate
B) Oxygen saturation (SpO2) of 92%
C) Pink, warm, and dry skin
D) Decreased dyspnea and fatigue
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Decreased respiratory rate

Explanation: In COPD patients, the respiratory drive may be driven by low oxygen levels (hypoxic drive) rather than high CO2. If a patient receives too much oxygen, their drive to breathe may be suppressed, leading to a significantly decreased respiratory rate and CO2 narcosis. Options B, C, and D are signs of *successful* therapy.

18.
A patient with a history of deep vein thrombosis (DVT) is prescribed warfarin therapy. Which laboratory test is used to monitor the therapeutic effect of warfarin?
A) Activated partial thromboplastin time (aPTT)
B) International normalized ratio (INR)
C) Platelet count
D) D-dimer
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) International normalized ratio (INR)

Explanation: Warfarin affects the extrinsic pathway of coagulation, which is measured by PT/INR. Heparin is monitored using aPTT (Option A).

19.
A patient with a history of type 2 diabetes mellitus is prescribed metformin therapy. Which adverse effect is associated with metformin use and requires immediate medical attention?
A) Hypoglycemia
B) Lactic acidosis
C) Hyperkalemia
D) Peripheral neuropathy
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Lactic acidosis

Explanation: Lactic acidosis is a rare but serious (life-threatening) complication of Metformin, particularly in patients with renal impairment. Metformin does *not* typically cause hypoglycemia (Option A) on its own.

20.
A patient with a history of asthma is prescribed an inhaled corticosteroid. Which adverse effect is associated with long-term use of inhaled corticosteroids?
A) Oral thrush
B) Hypertension
C) Hyperglycemia
D) Osteoporosis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Oral thrush

Explanation: Oral candidiasis (thrush) is a common local side effect of inhaled corticosteroids due to local immunosuppression in the mouth. Patients are advised to rinse their mouth after use. Note: Osteoporosis (D) is possible with *high dose/systemic* use, but thrush is the most direct common side effect of *inhaled* use.

21.
A patient with heart failure is prescribed digoxin. Which electrolyte imbalance increases the risk of digoxin toxicity?
A) Hyperkalemia
B) Hypernatremia
C) Hypokalemia
D) Hyponatremia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Hypokalemia

Explanation: Low potassium (Hypokalemia) allows more Digoxin to bind to the ATPase pump in the heart, increasing the drug's effect and the risk of toxicity.

22.
A patient with chronic kidney disease is prescribed calcium carbonate as a phosphate binder. Which instruction should the nurse provide to the patient regarding calcium carbonate administration?
A) Take with meals
B) Take on an empty stomach
C) Take with a full glass of water
D) Crush tablets before administration
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Take with meals

Explanation: Phosphate binders like calcium carbonate must be taken *with* food to bind the phosphorus in the food within the GI tract, preventing its absorption.

23.
A patient with a history of angina pectoris is prescribed nitroglycerin sublingual tablets for chest pain relief. Which instruction should the nurse provide to the patient regarding nitroglycerin administration?
A) Swallow the tablet whole with water.
B) Chew the tablet before swallowing.
C) Place the tablet under the tongue until it dissolves.
D) Apply the tablet to the skin over the chest area.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Place the tablet under the tongue until it dissolves.

Explanation: Sublingual administration allows for rapid absorption into the bloodstream through the mucous membranes, bypassing the liver (first-pass metabolism) for quick relief of angina.

24.
A patient with a history of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) is prescribed ipratropium bromide inhaler. Which instruction should the nurse provide to the patient regarding ipratropium inhaler use?
A) Shake the inhaler before each use.
B) Inhale deeply and rapidly through the mouthpiece.
C) Hold breath for 10 seconds after inhalation.
D) Rinse mouth after each use.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Hold breath for 10 seconds after inhalation.

Explanation: Holding the breath allows the medication to settle in the airways and increases absorption. Note: While rinsing the mouth is critical for steroids (to prevent thrush), holding the breath is a universal step for effective MDI use to ensure the drug reaches the lungs.

25.
A patient with a history of heart failure is prescribed carvedilol. Which parameter should the nurse monitor closely during carvedilol therapy?
A) Blood glucose levels
B) Serum potassium levels
C) Blood pressure
D) Liver function tests
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Blood pressure

Explanation: Carvedilol is a non-selective beta-blocker and alpha-1 blocker used for heart failure. It causes vasodilation and decreased heart rate, so monitoring for Hypotension and Bradycardia (Blood Pressure and Pulse) is essential.
