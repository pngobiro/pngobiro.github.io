201.
A patient presents with sudden-onset, painless vision loss in the right eye. Fundoscopic examination reveals a cherry-red spot at the macula and attenuated retinal vessels. Which of the following conditions is most likely responsible for this presentation?
A) Central retinal artery occlusion
B) Central retinal vein occlusion
C) Retinal detachment
D) Acute angle-closure glaucoma
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Central retinal artery occlusion

Explanation: The "cherry-red spot" is the classic sign of Central Retinal Artery Occlusion (CRAO). The retina becomes pale due to ischemia (edema), but the macula remains red because it receives blood supply from the choroid (via the ciliary arteries) which is separate from the retinal artery. It is a painless, sudden event.

202.
A patient presents with gradual, painless visual field loss in both eyes, particularly in the peripheral vision. Tonometry reveals elevated intraocular pressure (IOP). Which of the following conditions is most likely responsible for this presentation?
A) Open-angle glaucoma
B) Closed-angle glaucoma
C) Age-related macular degeneration
D) Diabetic retinopathy
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Open-angle glaucoma

Explanation: Primary Open-Angle Glaucoma (POAG) is characterized by a *gradual*, *painless* increase in IOP leading to optic nerve damage and peripheral visual field loss ("tunnel vision"). Closed-angle (B) is painful and acute.

203.
A patient presents with redness, pain, and photophobia in the left eye. Slit-lamp examination reveals ciliary injection, corneal edema, and a mid-dilated pupil with fixed reaction to light. Which of the following conditions is most likely responsible for this presentation?
A) Anterior uveitis
B) Acute angle-closure glaucoma
C) Endophthalmitis
D) Corneal abrasion
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Acute angle-closure glaucoma

Explanation: While redness and photophobia are common in uveitis (A), the specific finding of a "mid-dilated pupil with fixed reaction" alongside corneal edema is diagnostic for Acute Angle-Closure Glaucoma. Uveitis typically presents with a *constricted* (miotic) pupil.

204.
A patient presents with sudden-onset, severe eye pain, headache, nausea, and vomiting. On examination, the affected eye appears red, with a steamy cornea and mid-dilated, non-reactive pupil. Which of the following conditions is most likely responsible for this presentation?
A) Acute angle-closure glaucoma
B) Central retinal artery occlusion
C) Optic neuritis
D) Corneal ulcer
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Acute angle-closure glaucoma

Explanation: This is a classic medical emergency presentation. The "steamy cornea" is due to corneal edema from high pressure. The systemic symptoms (nausea/vomiting) are triggered by the intense pain and vagal stimulation.

205.
A patient presents with a yellowish-white spot on the cornea, surrounded by a ring of inflammation. Slit-lamp examination reveals branching, filamentous opacities extending from the corneal lesion. Which of the following conditions is most likely responsible for this presentation?
A) Herpes simplex keratitis
B) Bacterial keratitis
C) Fungal keratitis
D) Acanthamoeba keratitis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Herpes simplex keratitis

Explanation: The "branching, filamentous opacities" describe a dendritic ulcer, which is the pathognomonic sign of Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV) epithelial keratitis. Note: Corticosteroids are contraindicated in this condition as they can worsen the ulcer.
