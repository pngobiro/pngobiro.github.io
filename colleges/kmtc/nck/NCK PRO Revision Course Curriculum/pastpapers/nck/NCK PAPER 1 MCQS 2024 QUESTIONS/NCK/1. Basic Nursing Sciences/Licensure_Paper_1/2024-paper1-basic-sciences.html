Part I: Anatomy and Physiology

Q1 - Q25
1.
A patient presents with muscle weakness and fatigue. Upon further examination, it is revealed that the patient has decreased acetylcholine receptors at the neuromuscular junction. Which of the following conditions is most likely responsible for this presentation?
A) Myasthenia gravis
B) Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS)
C) Guillain-Barre syndrome
D) Muscular dystrophy
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Myasthenia gravis

Explanation: Myasthenia gravis is an autoimmune disorder characterized by the production of antibodies against acetylcholine receptors at the neuromuscular junction. This leads to a decrease in the number of available receptors, resulting in muscle weakness and fatigue that typically worsens with activity.

Why other options are incorrect:

  • B) ALS: Involves degeneration of upper and lower motor neurons, not receptor destruction.
  • C) Guillain-Barre: Demyelination of peripheral nerves (ascending paralysis).
  • D) Muscular dystrophy: Genetic defects in muscle proteins (dystrophin), not neuromuscular junction receptors.
2.
Following a severe traumatic brain injury, a patient is experiencing alterations in consciousness characterized by periods of wakefulness but with no meaningful interaction with the environment. Which term best describes this state?
A) Coma
B) Vegetative state
C) Minimally conscious state
D) Locked-in syndrome
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Vegetative state

Explanation: A vegetative state is characterized by the return of sleep-wake cycles (wakefulness) but a complete lack of cognitive function or awareness of the environment. The brainstem functions are preserved, allowing for autonomic survival.

Key Differences:

  • Coma: No wakefulness (eyes closed) and no awareness.
  • Minimally conscious state: Minimal but definite behavioral evidence of self or environmental awareness.
  • Locked-in syndrome: Full consciousness but complete paralysis (except usually vertical eye movements).
3.
In response to chronic hypoxia, which physiological adaptation occurs in the body to increase oxygen delivery to tissues?
A) Decreased erythropoietin production
B) Decreased red blood cell production
C) Increased cardiac output
D) Decreased ventilation rate
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Increased cardiac output (Note: The most classic response is Increased Erythropoietin/RBCs, but given the options, C is a valid acute/chronic adaptation, though usually secondary to Polycythemia. However, looking at the distractors: A and B are opposites of the truth, and D is incorrect. Therefore C is the only plausible physiological increase).

Detailed Analysis: While the primary response to chronic hypoxia is increased erythropoietin leading to polycythemia (increased RBCs), options A and B state "Decreased". Chronic hypoxia causes pulmonary vasoconstriction and often increased cardiac output initially to maintain O2 delivery, though chronic strain leads to cor pulmonale. Among the choices provided, the body attempts to increase perfusion (CO) or ventilation (rate increases, not decreases).

Exam Logic: If an option was "Increased red blood cell production" (Polycythemia), that would be the best answer for chronic adaptation. With the given choices, reject A, B, and D immediately.

4.
A patient with a history of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) presents with increased dyspnea and cough productive of purulent sputum. On auscultation, coarse crackles are heard bilaterally. Which complication of COPD is the patient most likely experiencing?
A) Acute exacerbation
B) Pneumothorax
C) Pulmonary embolism
D) Pleural effusion
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Acute exacerbation

Explanation: An acute exacerbation of COPD (AECOPD) is defined by an acute worsening of respiratory symptoms, specifically the "cardinal symptoms": increased dyspnea, increased sputum volume, and increased sputum purulence. The presence of purulent sputum strongly suggests a bacterial infection triggering the exacerbation.

5.
A patient with a spinal cord injury at the level of T6 presents with hypotension, bradycardia, and diaphoresis following a sudden change in position from supine to sitting. Which condition is the patient most likely experiencing?
A) Autonomic dysreflexia
B) Neurogenic shock
C) Spinal shock
D) Orthostatic hypotension
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) Orthostatic hypotension

Explanation: The key is the trigger: "sudden change in position from supine to sitting" leading to hypotension. Patients with SCI lose vasomotor tone and the ability to reflexively vasoconstrict when upright, leading to pooling of blood and a drop in BP.

Why not Autonomic Dysreflexia? AD causes severe hypertension, not hypotension, usually triggered by a noxious stimulus (full bladder), not just position change.

Why not Neurogenic Shock? While neurogenic shock causes hypotension and bradycardia, it is a distributive shock state occurring shortly after injury, not specifically triggered by position changes (though orthostasis is a component of the autonomic dysfunction).

6.
A patient with chronic kidney disease presents with metabolic acidosis. Which compensatory mechanism is the body most likely to employ to normalize pH?
A) Hyperventilation
B) Increased bicarbonate reabsorption
C) Increased renal excretion of hydrogen ions
D) Decreased renal excretion of bicarbonate ions
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Hyperventilation

Explanation: In metabolic acidosis, the primary defect is low pH and low HCO3. The respiratory system compensates by increasing the rate and depth of breathing (hyperventilation/Kussmaul breathing) to blow off CO2 (an acid). The kidneys are the diseased organ in CKD, so they cannot effectively perform options B, C, or D to compensate.

7.
A patient presents with symptoms of polyuria, polydipsia, and polyphagia. Laboratory tests reveal hyperglycemia and glycosuria. Which type of diabetes mellitus is the patient most likely experiencing?
A) Type 1 diabetes mellitus
B) Type 2 diabetes mellitus
C) Gestational diabetes mellitus
D) Latent autoimmune diabetes in adults (LADA)
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Type 1 diabetes mellitus

Explanation: The classic triad of "3 Ps" (Polyuria, Polydipsia, Polyphagia) with weight loss and acute onset is most characteristic of Type 1 DM, where there is absolute insulin deficiency. While Type 2 can present with these, it is often insidious. Without age context, Type 1 is the textbook presentation for the "3 Ps".

8.
A patient with a history of chronic liver disease presents with jaundice, ascites, and spider angiomas. Laboratory tests reveal elevated serum bilirubin levels, prolonged prothrombin time, and hypoalbuminemia. Which condition is the patient most likely experiencing?
A) Hepatic encephalopathy
B) Hepatorenal syndrome
C) Cirrhosis
D) Acute liver failure
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Cirrhosis

Explanation: The clinical picture (chronic history, stigmata like spider angiomas, ascites) and lab profile (synthetic dysfunction: low albumin, high PT) describe decompensated cirrhosis. Encephalopathy and hepatorenal syndrome are complications of cirrhosis, but the overall presentation describes the state of Cirrhosis.

9.
A patient with chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) is prescribed albuterol (salbutamol) inhaler as a rescue medication. Which of the following best describes the pharmacological action of albuterol?
A) Anticholinergic
B) Beta-adrenergic agonist
C) Corticosteroid
D) Methylxanthine
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Beta-adrenergic agonist

Explanation: Albuterol is a Short-Acting Beta Agonist (SABA). It stimulates beta-2 receptors in the smooth muscle of the bronchioles, causing bronchodilation.

10.
A patient with a history of congestive heart failure is prescribed furosemide. Which electrolyte imbalance is the patient at risk for developing with furosemide therapy?
A) Hyperkalemia
B) Hypokalemia
C) Hyponatremia
D) Hypernatremia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Hypokalemia

Explanation: Furosemide is a loop diuretic that blocks the Na+/K+/2Cl- cotransporter in the Loop of Henle. This leads to significant excretion of Potassium (K+), Magnesium, Calcium, and Sodium. Hypokalemia is a major concern, especially for cardiac patients (risk of arrhythmias).

11.
A patient is admitted with a suspected myocardial infarction. Which cardiac biomarker is most specific for myocardial injury?
A) Troponin
B) Creatine kinase-MB (CK-MB)
C) Myoglobin
D) Brain natriuretic peptide (BNP)
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Troponin

Explanation: Cardiac Troponins (I and T) are highly sensitive and specific markers of myocardial necrosis. CK-MB is less specific (found in some skeletal muscle). Myoglobin is early but non-specific. BNP is a marker for Heart Failure stretch, not infarction specificity.

12.
A patient with chronic kidney disease is prescribed erythropoietin-stimulating agents (ESAs) to manage anemia. Which laboratory parameter should be monitored closely during ESA therapy?
A) Serum potassium
B) Serum creatinine
C) Hemoglobin
D) Platelet count
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Hemoglobin

Explanation: The goal of ESA is to raise Hb, but raising it too high (>11-12 g/dL) or too quickly increases the risk of thromboembolic events, stroke, and hypertension. Regular Hb monitoring is mandatory.

13.
A patient with hypertension is prescribed an angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitor. Which adverse effect is most commonly associated with ACE inhibitors?
A) Hyperkalemia
B) Hypokalemia
C) Dry cough
D) Bradycardia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Dry cough

Explanation: ACE inhibitors prevent the breakdown of Bradykinin. The accumulation of bradykinin in the lungs causes a persistent, dry, irritating cough in about 10-20% of patients.

14.
A patient with a history of rheumatoid arthritis is prescribed methotrexate. Which laboratory parameter should be monitored closely during methotrexate therapy?
A) Serum sodium
B) Serum creatinine
C) Liver function tests
D) White blood cell count
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Liver function tests

Explanation: Methotrexate is hepatotoxic. While it can also cause bone marrow suppression (D), liver fibrosis and toxicity are major concerns requiring regular LFT monitoring.

15.
A patient with a history of coronary artery disease is prescribed aspirin for secondary prevention. Which adverse effect is a potential concern with long-term aspirin therapy?
A) Thrombocytopenia
B) Gastrointestinal bleeding
C) Hypercholesterolemia
D) Hypertension
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Gastrointestinal bleeding

Explanation: Aspirin inhibits COX-1, which protects the gastric mucosa. Long-term use increases the risk of gastric ulcers and GI bleeding.

16.
A patient with a history of heart failure is prescribed spironolactone. Which electrolyte imbalance is the patient at risk for developing with spironolactone therapy?
A) Hypernatremia
B) Hyperkalemia
C) Hypokalemia
D) Hyponatremia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Hyperkalemia

Explanation: Spironolactone is a Potassium-Sparing diuretic (Aldosterone antagonist). By blocking aldosterone, it promotes sodium excretion but retains potassium, leading to a risk of hyperkalemia.

17.
A patient with chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) is prescribed oxygen therapy to maintain adequate oxygenation. Which assessment finding indicates the need for adjustment of oxygen therapy?
A) Decreased respiratory rate
B) Oxygen saturation (SpO2) of 92%
C) Pink, warm, and dry skin
D) Decreased dyspnea and fatigue
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Decreased respiratory rate

Explanation: In some COPD patients (retainers), the drive to breathe is driven by hypoxemia (hypoxic drive). If too much oxygen is given, this drive is removed, leading to hypoventilation (decreased RR), CO2 narcosis, and potentially respiratory arrest. SpO2 of 92% is the ideal target range (88-92%) for COPD.

18.
A patient with a history of deep vein thrombosis (DVT) is prescribed warfarin therapy. Which laboratory test is used to monitor the therapeutic effect of warfarin?
A) Activated partial thromboplastin time (aPTT)
B) International normalized ratio (INR)
C) Platelet count
D) D-dimer
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) International normalized ratio (INR)

Explanation: Warfarin is monitored using PT/INR. The target INR is usually 2.0-3.0. aPTT is used for Heparin.

19.
A patient with a history of type 2 diabetes mellitus is prescribed metformin therapy. Which adverse effect is associated with metformin use and requires immediate medical attention?
A) Hypoglycemia
B) Lactic acidosis
C) Hyperkalemia
D) Peripheral neuropathy
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Lactic acidosis

Explanation: While rare, Lactic Acidosis is a serious, potentially fatal complication of Metformin, especially in patients with renal impairment. Metformin does not typically cause hypoglycemia on its own.

20.
A patient with a history of asthma is prescribed an inhaled corticosteroid. Which adverse effect is associated with long-term use of inhaled corticosteroids?
A) Oral thrush
B) Hypertension
C) Hyperglycemia
D) Osteoporosis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Oral thrush

Explanation: Local deposition of steroids in the mouth can cause Oropharyngeal Candidiasis (thrush). Patients are taught to rinse their mouth after use. Note: High dose long-term use *can* cause osteoporosis (D), but Oral Thrush is the most common local adverse effect specifically mentioned in standard nursing pharmacology for inhaled routes.

21.
A patient with heart failure is prescribed digoxin. Which electrolyte imbalance increases the risk of digoxin toxicity?
A) Hyperkalemia
B) Hypernatremia
C) Hypokalemia
D) Hyponatremia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Hypokalemia

Explanation: Digoxin competes with potassium for binding sites on the Na+/K+ ATPase pump. If potassium is low (hypokalemia), more digoxin binds, leading to increased effect and toxicity.

22.
A patient with chronic kidney disease is prescribed calcium carbonate as a phosphate binder. Which instruction should the nurse provide to the patient regarding calcium carbonate administration?
A) Take with meals
B) Take on an empty stomach
C) Take with a full glass of water
D) Crush tablets before administration
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Take with meals

Explanation: Phosphate binders work by binding to dietary phosphate in the GI tract, preventing its absorption. Therefore, they must be in the gut at the same time as the food (i.e., taken with meals).

23.
A patient with a history of angina pectoris is prescribed nitroglycerin sublingual tablets for chest pain relief. Which instruction should the nurse provide to the patient regarding nitroglycerin administration?
A) Swallow the tablet whole with water.
B) Chew the tablet before swallowing.
C) Place the tablet under the tongue until it dissolves.
D) Apply the tablet to the skin over the chest area.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Place the tablet under the tongue until it dissolves.

Explanation: Sublingual administration allows for rapid absorption into the systemic circulation, bypassing the first-pass metabolism of the liver, which is crucial for acute angina relief.

24.
A patient with a history of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) is prescribed ipratropium bromide inhaler. Which instruction should the nurse provide to the patient regarding ipratropium inhaler use?
A) Shake the inhaler before each use.
B) Inhale deeply and rapidly through the mouthpiece.
C) Hold breath for 10 seconds after inhalation.
D) Rinse mouth after each use.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Hold breath for 10 seconds after inhalation.

Explanation: Holding the breath allows the medication to settle in the lungs (sedimentation) rather than being exhaled immediately. Option A is for MDIs generally, but C is a specific breathing technique instruction. Note: Ipratropium is anticholinergic; rinsing mouth (D) is vital for *steroids* to prevent thrush, though often recommended for inhalers to prevent dry mouth, it's not the critical "action" instruction. Holding breath is critical for efficacy.

25.
A patient with a history of heart failure is prescribed carvedilol. Which parameter should the nurse monitor closely during carvedilol therapy?
A) Blood glucose levels
B) Serum potassium levels
C) Blood pressure
D) Liver function tests
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Blood pressure

Explanation: Carvedilol is a non-selective beta-blocker and alpha-1 blocker. It causes vasodilation and reduced heart rate, leading to potential hypotension and bradycardia. Monitoring BP and Heart Rate is essential.

Part II: Pharmacology

Q51 - Q75
51.
A patient is prescribed a beta-blocker for the management of hypertension. Which adverse effect should the nurse monitor closely in the patient?
A) Bradycardia
B) Hyperglycemia
C) Hypertension
D) Tachycardia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Bradycardia

Explanation: Beta-blockers reduce heart rate and myocardial contractility. Bradycardia is a direct extension of the therapeutic effect and a common adverse effect.

52.
A patient is prescribed an angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitor for heart failure. Which laboratory parameter should the nurse monitor closely during ACE inhibitor therapy?
A) Serum potassium levels
B) Serum sodium levels
C) Blood glucose levels
D) Serum creatinine levels
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Serum potassium levels

Explanation: ACE inhibitors decrease aldosterone secretion, which can lead to potassium retention (Hyperkalemia).

53.
A patient is prescribed an opioid analgesic for postoperative pain management. Which nursing intervention is essential for preventing respiratory depression in the patient?
A) Administering naloxone prophylactically
B) Monitoring oxygen saturation with pulse oximetry
C) Encouraging deep breathing exercises
D) Administering bronchodilators as needed
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Monitoring oxygen saturation with pulse oximetry

Explanation: Continuous monitoring of respiratory status (Rate and Saturation) is crucial to detect early signs of opioid-induced respiratory depression.

54.
A patient is prescribed a loop diuretic for the management of heart failure. Which electrolyte imbalance is most commonly associated with loop diuretic therapy?
A) Hyponatremia
B) Hypokalemia
C) Hyperkalemia
D) Hypocalcemia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Hypokalemia

Explanation: Loop diuretics cause significant loss of potassium in the urine.

55.
A patient is prescribed a proton pump inhibitor (PPI) for the treatment of gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD). Which instruction should the nurse include in patient education about PPI therapy?
A) "Take the medication with antacids for enhanced effectiveness."
B) "Avoid consuming citrus fruits while on PPI therapy."
C) "Take the medication 30 minutes before meals."
D) "Report any signs of gastrointestinal bleeding promptly."
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) "Take the medication 30 minutes before meals."

Explanation: PPIs are most effective when taken 30-60 minutes before the first meal of the day to inhibit the proton pumps when they are activated by food.

56.
A patient is prescribed a calcium channel blocker for the management of hypertension. Which adverse effect should the nurse monitor closely in the patient?
A) Hypoglycemia
B) Peripheral edema
C) Hypokalemia
D) Tachycardia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Peripheral edema

Explanation: Calcium channel blockers (especially dihydropyridines like Amlodipine) cause precapillary vasodilation, leading to fluid extravasation and peripheral edema.

57.
A patient is prescribed a selective serotonin reuptake inhibitor (SSRI) for the treatment of depression. Which adverse effect should the nurse monitor closely in the patient?
A) Bradycardia
B) Hypotension
C) Hypernatremia
D) Suicidal ideation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) Suicidal ideation

Explanation: There is an increased risk of suicidal ideation/behavior during the initiation of antidepressant therapy, particularly in children, adolescents, and young adults (Black Box Warning).

58.
A patient is prescribed a statin medication for lipid-lowering therapy. Which adverse effect should the nurse monitor closely in the patient?
A) Muscle pain or weakness
B) Dry cough
C) Hypertension
D) Hyperkalemia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Muscle pain or weakness

Explanation: Statins can cause myopathy and rhabdomyolysis. Muscle pain (myalgia) is the most common symptom indicating potential toxicity.

59.
A patient is prescribed a benzodiazepine for the management of anxiety. Which instruction should the nurse include in patient education about benzodiazepine therapy?
A) "Take the medication with grapefruit juice for enhanced absorption."
B) "Avoid consuming alcohol while on benzodiazepine therapy."
C) "Take the medication on an empty stomach for better effectiveness."
D) "Discontinue the medication abruptly if you experience side effects."
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) "Avoid consuming alcohol while on benzodiazepine therapy."

Explanation: Both are CNS depressants. Combining them can lead to profound respiratory depression, sedation, and death.

60.
A patient is prescribed a nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drug (NSAID) for the management of pain. Which adverse effect should the nurse monitor closely in the patient?
A) Hypotension
B) Hyperkalemia
C) Gastrointestinal bleeding
D) Hyperglycemia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Gastrointestinal bleeding

Explanation: NSAIDs inhibit prostaglandins that protect the stomach lining, increasing the risk of ulcers and GI bleeds.

61.
A patient is prescribed a loop diuretic for the management of edema. Which electrolyte imbalance is most commonly associated with loop diuretic therapy?
A) Hyponatremia
B) Hypomagnesemia
C) Hyperkalemia
D) Hypocalcemia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Hypomagnesemia

Explanation: Note: Loop diuretics cause loss of K+, Na+, Ca2+, and Mg2+. Hypomagnesemia is a common effect alongside hypokalemia.

62.
A patient is prescribed an anticoagulant for the prevention of venous thromboembolism (VTE). Which laboratory parameter should the nurse monitor closely during anticoagulant therapy?
A) Platelet count
B) International normalized ratio (INR)
C) Serum sodium levels
D) Blood urea nitrogen (BUN)
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) International normalized ratio (INR)

Explanation: Assuming Warfarin is the prescribed oral anticoagulant for VTE prophylaxis in this context. INR is the specific monitoring test.

63.
A patient is prescribed a beta-adrenergic agonist for the management of asthma. Which adverse effect should the nurse monitor closely in the patient?
A) Hypotension
B) Hyperkalemia
C) Tachycardia
D) Hypoglycemia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Tachycardia

Explanation: Beta-2 agonists (like albuterol) can have some crossover effect on Beta-1 receptors in the heart, causing tachycardia and palpitations.

64.
A patient is prescribed a proton pump inhibitor (PPI) for the treatment of peptic ulcer disease. Which adverse effect should the nurse monitor closely in the patient?
A) Constipation
B) Hypokalemia
C) Diarrhea
D) Clostridium difficile infection

Correct Answer: D) Clostridium difficile infection

Explanation: Acid suppression alters the gut flora and increases the risk of C. difficile infection.

65.
A patient is prescribed an antipsychotic medication for the management of schizophrenia. Which adverse effect should the nurse monitor closely in the patient?

B) Hypoglycemia
C) Extrapyramidal symptoms
D) Hypercalcemia

Correct Answer: C) Extrapyramidal symptoms

Explanation: EPS (dystonia, akathisia, parkinsonism, tardive dyskinesia) are common side effects of antipsychotics, especially first-generation ones.

66.

D) Hyperkalemia

67.

68.

A) Hypoglycemia

Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Gastrointestinal bleeding

69.
A patient is prescribed a benzodiazepine for the management of insomnia. Which adverse effect should the nurse monitor closely in the patient?

B) Hyperglycemia
C) Daytime drowsiness
D) Bradycardia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Daytime drowsiness

70.
A patient is prescribed a beta-blocker for the management of migraine headaches. Which adverse effect should the nurse monitor closely in the patient?
A) Hypertension
B) Bradycardia
C) Hyperglycemia
D) Constipation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Bradycardia

Explanation: Regardless of the indication (migraine vs HTN), the pharmacologic action remains beta-blockade, slowing the heart rate.

71.
A patient is prescribed a proton pump inhibitor (PPI) for the treatment of gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD). Which adverse effect should the nurse monitor closely in the patient?

72.
A patient is prescribed an antipsychotic medication for the management of bipolar disorder. Which adverse effect should the nurse monitor closely in the patient?
A) Hypertension
B) Hyperglycemia
C) Hypotension
D) Hypernatremia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Hyperglycemia

Explanation: Second-generation (atypical) antipsychotics are strongly associated with metabolic syndrome, including hyperglycemia, weight gain, and dyslipidemia.

73.
A patient is prescribed an opioid analgesic for the management of acute pain. Which adverse effect should the nurse monitor closely in the patient?
A) Hypertension
B) Hypoglycemia
C) Respiratory depression
D) Hyperkalemia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Respiratory depression

Explanation: The most serious life-threatening side effect of opioids.

74.
A patient is prescribed a loop diuretic for the management of heart failure. Which electrolyte imbalance is most commonly associated with loop diuretic therapy?
A) Hypercalcemia
B) Hypokalemia
C) Hypernatremia
D) Hypomagnesemia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Hypokalemia

Explanation: Hypokalemia is the most common and clinically significant electrolyte imbalance requiring monitoring and often supplementation. (Note: Option D Hypomagnesemia is also true, but K+ is the primary concern).

75.
A patient is prescribed an angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitor for the management of hypertension. Which adverse effect should the nurse monitor closely in the patient?
A) Hyperkalemia
B) Hypoglycemia
C) Hypercalcemia
D) Hypotension
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Hyperkalemia

Explanation: ACE inhibitors cause potassium retention. (Hypotension is a therapeutic effect/excessive effect, but Hyperkalemia is a specific metabolic adverse reaction).

Part III: Immunology

Q301 - Q325
301.
A patient is exposed to the hepatitis B virus (HBV) and develops immunity against future infections. Which type of immunity is primarily responsible for this protection?
A) Passive immunity
B) Innate immunity
C) Humoral immunity
D) Cell-mediated immunity
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Humoral immunity

Explanation: Humoral immunity involves B cells producing specific antibodies (Anti-HBs) that circulate in the blood and neutralize the virus upon re-exposure.

302.
Which of the following cell types is primarily responsible for presenting antigens to T cells during the immune response?
A) B cells
B) Natural killer (NK) cells
C) Macrophages
D) Eosinophils
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Macrophages

Explanation: Macrophages are professional Antigen-Presenting Cells (APCs), along with Dendritic cells and B cells. They phagocytose pathogens and present peptide fragments on MHC molecules to T cells.

303.
A patient with a severe bacterial infection has elevated levels of C-reactive protein (CRP) in their blood. Which of the following functions does CRP primarily serve during the acute phase response?
A) Opsonization of pathogens
B) Activation of complement system
C) Phagocytosis of pathogens
D) Neutralization of toxins
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Opsonization of pathogens

Explanation: CRP binds to phosphocholine on the surface of dead or dying cells (and some bacteria) to activate the complement system and promote phagocytosis by macrophages (Opsonization).

304.
Which of the following types of T cells is primarily involved in the regulation of the immune response by suppressing excessive inflammation and preventing autoimmunity?
A) Helper T cells (Th1)
B) Cytotoxic T cells (CTLs)
C) Regulatory T cells (Tregs)
D) Memory T cells
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Regulatory T cells (Tregs)

Explanation: Tregs (CD4+CD25+FoxP3+) suppress immune responses of other cells to maintain self-tolerance and prevent autoimmune disease.

305.
Which of the following mechanisms is responsible for the phenomenon of immunological memory, wherein the immune system mounts a faster and more robust response upon re-exposure to a previously encountered pathogen?
A) Clonal expansion of memory B cells
B) Differentiation of nave T cells into effector T cells
C) Production of antibodies by plasma cells
D) Activation of the complement system
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Clonal expansion of memory B cells

Explanation: Upon re-exposure, memory B cells recognize the antigen and rapidly proliferate (clonal expansion) and differentiate into plasma cells, producing high-affinity IgG antibodies much faster than the primary response.

306.
A patient with a severe fungal infection demonstrates impaired neutrophil function. Which of the following cellular processes is most likely to be affected in this patient?
A) Phagocytosis
B) Antibody production
C) Cytokine secretion
D) Mast cell degranulation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Phagocytosis

Explanation: Neutrophils are the primary phagocytes of the innate immune system. Their main function is to engulf and destroy pathogens (Phagocytosis).

307.
Which of the following statements accurately describes the role of natural killer (NK) cells in the immune response?
A) NK cells directly kill virus-infected cells and tumor cells.
B) NK cells produce antibodies against pathogens.
C) NK cells present antigens to T cells to initiate adaptive immunity.
D) NK cells release cytokines to recruit other immune cells to the site of infection.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) NK cells directly kill virus-infected cells and tumor cells.

Explanation: NK cells are innate immune cells that detect and induce apoptosis in cells that lack "self" markers (MHC Class I), such as virally infected or cancerous cells.

308.
Which of the following cytokines is primarily responsible for promoting differentiation of nave T cells into Th1 cells and enhancing cell-mediated immune responses?
A) Interleukin-2 (IL-2)

309.
A patient presents with symptoms of severe allergies, including difficulty breathing, hives, and swelling. Which of the following cells is primarily responsible for mediating these allergic responses?
A) Eosinophils
B) Basophils
C) Mast cells
D) Neutrophils
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Mast cells

Explanation: Mast cells reside in tissues and contain granules of histamine. When IgE binds to mast cells during an allergic reaction, they degranulate, releasing histamine which causes vasodilation, bronchoconstriction, and edema.

C) IgE

Correct Answer: A) IgA

Explanation: Secretory IgA is found in high concentrations in colostrum and breast milk, protecting the infant's GI tract. IgG crosses the placenta, providing passive immunity *before* birth.

314.
Which of the following statements accurately describes the role of dendritic cells in the immune response?

B) Dendritic cells produce antibodies against pathogens.
C) Dendritic cells activate nave T cells and initiate adaptive immune responses.
D) Dendritic cells release histamine and other inflammatory mediators.

Correct Answer: C) Dendritic cells activate nave T cells and initiate adaptive immune responses.

Explanation: Dendritic cells are the most potent Antigen-Presenting Cells. They capture antigens in tissues, migrate to lymph nodes, and present them to nave T cells, bridging innate and adaptive immunity.

319.
Which of the following statements accurately describes the role of major histocompatibility complex (MHC) molecules in antigen presentation?
A) MHC class I molecules present exogenous antigens to CD4+ T cells.
B) MHC class I molecules present endogenous antigens to CD8+ T cells.
C) MHC class II molecules present endogenous antigens to CD4+ T cells.
D) MHC class II molecules present exogenous antigens to CD8+ T cells.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) MHC class I molecules present endogenous antigens to CD8+ T cells.

Explanation:

  • MHC I: Found on all nucleated cells, presents endogenous (intracellular) antigens to CD8+ Cytotoxic T cells.
  • MHC II: Found on APCs, presents exogenous (extracellular) antigens to CD4+ Helper T cells.

320.
Which of the following statements accurately describes the process of opsonization during the immune response?
A) Opsonins facilitate the lysis of target cells by forming membrane attack complexes.
B) Opsonins promote the phagocytosis of pathogens by coating them with proteins.
C) Opsonins neutralize toxins produced by pathogens.
D) Opsonins stimulate the release of histamine from mast cells.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Opsonins promote the phagocytosis of pathogens by coating them with proteins.

Explanation: Opsonization is the process of "buttering the bread" - coating pathogens with antibodies (IgG) or complement proteins (C3b) to make them easier for phagocytes to bind to and ingest.

321.
A patient is diagnosed with selective IgA deficiency, a primary immunodeficiency disorder. Which of the following complications is most commonly associated with this condition?
A) Recurrent bacterial infections
B) Severe combined immunodeficiency (SCID)
C) Chronic granulomatous disease (CGD)
D) Autoimmune hemolytic anemia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Recurrent bacterial infections

Explanation: IgA protects mucosal surfaces (respiratory, GI). Deficiency leads to recurrent sinopulmonary and GI infections. Note: Patients are also at risk for anaphylaxis if they receive blood products containing IgA.
