HIV/AIDS Management

NCK, KRCHN, PAPER 2 - 1100 MCQS Answers and Rationales

Instructions

  • This paper consists of 50 Multiple Choice Questions focusing on HIV/AIDS pathophysiology, diagnosis, and management.
  • Each question has four options; select the most appropriate one.
  • Detailed rationales are provided for each answer to facilitate effective revision.
  • Total Marks: 50 | Time Allowed: 60 Minutes.

Section 1: Pathophysiology and Diagnosis

25 Marks
1.
What type of virus causes HIV?
A) DNA virus
B) Retrovirus
C) Adenovirus
D) Herpesvirus
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Retrovirus

Rationale: HIV is a retrovirus, which means it uses reverse transcriptase to convert its RNA genome into DNA after infecting host cells, a key characteristic distinguishing it from DNA viruses.

2.
Which cells are primarily targeted by HIV?
A) Red blood cells
B) Neutrophils
C) CD4+ T lymphocytes
D) Platelets
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C) CD4+ T lymphocytes

Rationale: HIV specifically infects CD4+ T cells, weakening the immune system by depleting these helper T cells critical for immune response coordination.

3.
Which of the following is the most common mode of HIV transmission globally?
A) Blood transfusion
B) Sexual contact
C) Vertical transmission
D) Sharing utensils
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Sexual contact

Rationale: Sexual contact is the primary mode of HIV transmission worldwide, especially unprotected heterosexual and homosexual intercourse.

4.
What is the window period in HIV infection?
A) Time from infection to AIDS diagnosis
B) Time from infection to seroconversion
C) Time from treatment initiation to viral suppression
D) Time from exposure to symptom onset
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Time from infection to seroconversion

Rationale: The window period is the time between initial HIV infection and when antibodies are detectable by standard tests.

5.
Which of the following tests detects antibodies to HIV?
A) PCR
B) ELISA
C) Western blot
D) CD4 count
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) ELISA

Rationale: ELISA is commonly used as a screening test to detect antibodies produced against HIV.

6.
What is the main role of reverse transcriptase in HIV replication?
A) Replicating viral RNA
B) Integrating viral DNA into host genome
C) Converting viral RNA into DNA
D) Assembling new virions
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C) Converting viral RNA into DNA

Rationale: Reverse transcriptase converts HIV’s RNA genome into DNA, allowing it to integrate into the host genome.

7.
Which antiretroviral drug class inhibits reverse transcriptase?
A) Protease inhibitors
B) Nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitors (NRTIs)
C) Integrase inhibitors
D) Fusion inhibitors
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitors (NRTIs)

Rationale: NRTIs block the reverse transcriptase enzyme, preventing the conversion of viral RNA to DNA.

8.
What is the significance of a CD4 count below 200 cells/mm³ in an HIV patient?
A) Normal immune function
B) Indicates acute infection
C) AIDS diagnosis threshold
D) Viral suppression achieved
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C) AIDS diagnosis threshold

Rationale: A CD4 count below 200 cells/mm³ defines AIDS, indicating severe immune compromise and high risk for opportunistic infections.

9.
Which opportunistic infection is most commonly associated with AIDS?
A) Tuberculosis
B) Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia
C) Influenza
D) Malaria
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia

Rationale: Pneumocystis pneumonia is a hallmark opportunistic infection in AIDS due to the weakened immune system.

10.
What is the primary goal of antiretroviral therapy (ART)?
A) Cure HIV infection
B) Suppress viral replication
C) Increase antibody production
D) Eliminate HIV reservoirs
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Suppress viral replication

Rationale: ART aims to reduce viral load to undetectable levels, preserving immune function and preventing progression.

11.
Which HIV test directly detects viral RNA?
A) ELISA
B) Western blot
C) PCR
D) Rapid antibody test
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C) PCR

Rationale: PCR detects HIV RNA directly and can diagnose infection during the window period before antibodies develop.

12.
Which of the following is NOT a common side effect of protease inhibitors?
A) Lipodystrophy
B) Hyperglycemia
C) Nephrotoxicity
D) Hyperlipidemia
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C) Nephrotoxicity

Rationale: Nephrotoxicity is not typical of protease inhibitors but is more associated with drugs like tenofovir.

13.
What is the significance of a viral load test in HIV management?
A) Measures CD4 count
B) Detects antibody presence
C) Quantifies HIV RNA in blood
D) Determines drug resistance
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C) Quantifies HIV RNA in blood

Rationale: Viral load tests monitor the amount of HIV RNA, indicating treatment effectiveness.

14.
Which opportunistic infection is prevented by prophylactic TMP-SMX in HIV patients?
A) Tuberculosis
B) Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia
C) Cryptococcal meningitis
D) Toxoplasmosis
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia

Rationale: TMP-SMX is used prophylactically to prevent Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia in patients with low CD4 counts.

15.
What is the main transmission risk for healthcare workers regarding HIV?
A) Skin contact
B) Mucous membrane exposure
C) Needlestick injury
D) Aerosol inhalation
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C) Needlestick injury

Rationale: Needlestick injuries pose the highest occupational risk of HIV transmission among healthcare workers.

16.
Which HIV subtype is most prevalent globally?
A) HIV-1 Group M
B) HIV-2
C) HIV-1 Group O
D) HIV-1 Group N
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: A) HIV-1 Group M

Rationale: HIV-1 Group M is the predominant global strain responsible for the majority of infections.

17.
Which of the following is a common clinical symptom during acute HIV infection?
A) Chronic diarrhea
B) Fever and rash
C) Weight gain
D) Neuropathy
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Fever and rash

Rationale: Acute HIV infection often presents with flu-like symptoms, including fever, rash, and lymphadenopathy.

18.
What does the acronym PrEP stand for in HIV prevention?
A) Pre-exposure prophylaxis
B) Post-exposure prevention
C) Primary eradication protocol
D) Progressive elimination procedure
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: A) Pre-exposure prophylaxis

Rationale: PrEP refers to medication taken by HIV-negative individuals to reduce infection risk before exposure.

19.
Which bodily fluid has the highest concentration of HIV?
A) Saliva
B) Blood
C) Tears
D) Sweat
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Blood

Rationale: Blood contains the highest concentration of HIV, making transfusions a high-risk transmission route.

20.
Which antiretroviral drug class blocks the fusion of HIV with host cells?
A) Integrase inhibitors
B) Fusion inhibitors
C) Protease inhibitors
D) NRTIs
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Fusion inhibitors

Rationale: Fusion inhibitors prevent HIV from entering host cells by blocking the fusion process.

21.
Which laboratory test confirms an HIV diagnosis after a positive screening test?
A) ELISA
B) Western blot
C) PCR
D) CD4 count
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Western blot

Rationale: Western blot is a confirmatory test detecting specific HIV proteins after a positive ELISA.

22.
Which of the following can be a long-term complication of HIV infection despite treatment?
A) Cardiovascular disease
B) Diabetes insipidus
C) Osteoarthritis
D) Thyroid dysfunction
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: A) Cardiovascular disease

Rationale: Chronic HIV infection and some ART drugs increase risk of cardiovascular disease.

23.
How does HIV avoid detection by the immune system?
A) Rapid mutation of surface proteins
B) Destroying B cells directly
C) Hiding in blood plasma
D) Causing antibody overproduction
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: A) Rapid mutation of surface proteins

Rationale: HIV frequently mutates its envelope proteins, allowing evasion of immune responses.

24.
Which HIV drug class inhibits integration of viral DNA into the host genome?
A) Protease inhibitors
B) Integrase inhibitors
C) NRTIs
D) Fusion inhibitors
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Integrase inhibitors

Rationale: Integrase inhibitors block the viral enzyme that inserts viral DNA into the host cell genome.

25.
Which stage of HIV infection is characterized by severe immunodeficiency and opportunistic infections?
A) Acute HIV infection
B) Chronic HIV infection
C) AIDS
D) Seroconversion
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C) AIDS

Rationale: AIDS is the advanced stage marked by CD4 depletion and increased opportunistic infections.

Section 2: Clinical Management & Prevention

25 Marks
26.
What is the primary mechanism of action of protease inhibitors?
A) Prevent viral entry
B) Block viral assembly and maturation
C) Inhibit reverse transcriptase
D) Increase CD4 count
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Block viral assembly and maturation

Rationale: Protease inhibitors block the HIV protease enzyme, preventing cleavage of viral proteins needed for maturation.

27.
Which of the following is NOT a typical symptom of HIV infection?
A) Lymphadenopathy
B) Night sweats
C) Bradycardia
D) Weight loss
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C) Bradycardia

Rationale: Bradycardia (slow heart rate) is not commonly associated with HIV infection symptoms.

28.
Which HIV prevention method involves treating infected individuals to reduce transmission risk?
A) PrEP
B) Post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP)
C) Treatment as prevention (TasP)
D) Barrier methods
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C) Treatment as prevention (TasP)

Rationale: TasP involves effective ART to lower viral load, reducing transmission risk.

29.
Which vaccine is recommended for HIV-infected individuals to prevent pneumococcal disease?
A) BCG vaccine
B) Pneumococcal conjugate vaccine
C) Influenza vaccine
D) Hepatitis B vaccine
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Pneumococcal conjugate vaccine

Rationale: HIV patients are at increased risk for pneumococcal infections; vaccination is essential.

30.
What does the term “seroconversion” mean in HIV?
A) Loss of all HIV antibodies
B) Development of detectable HIV antibodies after infection
C) Transition from HIV to AIDS
D) Viral suppression after ART
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Development of detectable HIV antibodies after infection

Rationale: Seroconversion is the point when HIV antibodies become detectable in blood tests.

31.
Which of the following best describes the HIV genome?
A) Double-stranded DNA
B) Single-stranded RNA
C) Double-stranded RNA
D) Single-stranded DNA
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Single-stranded RNA

Rationale: HIV’s genome is composed of single-stranded RNA, which is reverse transcribed into DNA in the host.

32.
Which of these is a common neurological complication of AIDS?
A) Peripheral neuropathy
B) Stroke
C) Multiple sclerosis
D) Guillain-Barré syndrome
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: A) Peripheral neuropathy

Rationale: HIV/AIDS patients commonly develop peripheral neuropathy due to viral damage or ART side effects.

33.
Which test is recommended for newborns to detect HIV infection?
A) ELISA
B) Western blot
C) PCR
D) CD4 count
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C) PCR

Rationale: PCR detects viral RNA/DNA and is used in newborns, who carry maternal antibodies that interfere with antibody tests.

34.
Which factor does NOT increase risk of HIV transmission?
A) Presence of other sexually transmitted infections
B) Use of barrier contraception
C) High viral load in the infected partner
D) Multiple sexual partners
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Use of barrier contraception

Rationale: Barrier contraception (e.g., condoms) decreases risk of HIV transmission.

35.
Which organ is the main site of HIV replication?
A) Liver
B) Spleen
C) Lymph nodes
D) Kidney
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C) Lymph nodes

Rationale: HIV primarily replicates in lymphoid tissues, especially lymph nodes.

36.
What is the first step in HIV entry into a host cell?
A) Fusion of viral and cell membranes
B) Binding of gp120 to CD4 receptor
C) Reverse transcription
D) Viral budding
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Binding of gp120 to CD4 receptor

Rationale: HIV entry starts with the viral gp120 protein binding to the CD4 receptor on host T cells.

37.
Which population group has a naturally lower risk of HIV infection due to genetic mutation?
A) Individuals with CCR5 delta-32 mutation
B) Individuals with sickle cell trait
C) Individuals with hemophilia
D) Individuals with blood type O
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: A) Individuals with CCR5 delta-32 mutation

Rationale: CCR5 delta-32 mutation leads to defective CCR5 receptors, reducing HIV’s ability to enter cells.

38.
What is the recommended duration for post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) after potential HIV exposure?
A) 3 days
B) 1 week
C) 28 days
D) 6 months
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C) 28 days

Rationale: PEP involves a 28-day course of antiretrovirals to prevent infection after exposure.

39.
Which of the following is a non-nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitor (NNRTI)?
A) Zidovudine
B) Efavirenz
C) Lopinavir
D) Raltegravir
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Efavirenz

Rationale: Efavirenz is an NNRTI that binds directly to reverse transcriptase, inhibiting its function.

40.
Which opportunistic infection can cause meningitis in AIDS patients?
A) Cryptococcus neoformans
B) Candida albicans
C) Herpes simplex virus
D) Mycobacterium tuberculosis
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: A) Cryptococcus neoformans

Rationale: Cryptococcal meningitis is common in AIDS due to immune suppression.

41.
Which of the following is NOT an effect of HIV infection on the immune system?
A) Decreased CD4 T cell count
B) Increased antibody diversity
C) Impaired cell-mediated immunity
D) Increased susceptibility to infections
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Increased antibody diversity

Rationale: HIV decreases immune function and antibody response, not increases antibody diversity.

42.
What is the role of the HIV protease enzyme?
A) Reverse transcription of RNA to DNA
B) Cleavage of viral polyproteins into functional proteins
C) Integration of viral DNA into host genome
D) Binding of virus to host cells
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Cleavage of viral polyproteins into functional proteins

Rationale: HIV protease processes viral polyproteins essential for assembling mature infectious virus particles.

43.
Which of the following is NOT a recommended HIV prevention strategy?
A) Condom use
B) Needle exchange programs
C) Sharing razors
D) Male circumcision
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C) Sharing razors

Rationale: Sharing razors is a risk factor for HIV transmission, not a prevention strategy.

44.
Which symptom is most suggestive of HIV infection progression to AIDS?
A) Persistent fever and weight loss
B) Mild cough
C) Transient rash
D) Occasional headaches
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: A) Persistent fever and weight loss

Rationale: Persistent systemic symptoms such as fever and weight loss indicate disease progression.

45.
Which is the best measure to assess immune function in HIV?
A) Viral load
B) CD4+ T cell count
C) Antibody titers
D) Complete blood count
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) CD4+ T cell count

Rationale: CD4 count reflects immune system strength and guides treatment decisions.

46.
Which type of hypersensitivity reaction is commonly seen in immune reconstitution inflammatory syndrome (IRIS)?
A) Type I
B) Type II
C) Type III
D) Type IV
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: D) Type IV

Rationale: IRIS is a delayed-type (Type IV) hypersensitivity reaction due to restored immune response.

47.
What is the main reason for drug resistance development in HIV therapy?
A) Poor adherence to medication
B) Viral mutation rate is low
C) Excessive dosing
D) Use of combination therapy
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: A) Poor adherence to medication

Rationale: Irregular ART allows viral replication and mutation, leading to resistance.

48.
Which vaccine is routinely recommended for all HIV-positive patients?
A) MMR vaccine
B) Hepatitis B vaccine
C) Varicella vaccine
D) Yellow fever vaccine
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Hepatitis B vaccine

Rationale: HIV patients have increased risk for Hepatitis B; vaccination is recommended unless already immune.

49.
What is the primary function of the gp41 protein of HIV?
A) Attachment to CD4 receptor
B) Fusion of viral and host membranes
C) Reverse transcription
D) Protease activity
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Fusion of viral and host membranes

Rationale: gp41 mediates fusion of viral envelope with the host cell membrane, facilitating entry.

50.
Which of the following is TRUE regarding HIV-2 compared to HIV-1?
A) HIV-2 is more virulent
B) HIV-2 has higher transmission rates
C) HIV-2 has slower disease progression
D) HIV-2 is the predominant global strain
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C) HIV-2 has slower disease progression

Rationale: HIV-2 generally causes slower progression to AIDS and is less transmissible compared to HIV-1.
