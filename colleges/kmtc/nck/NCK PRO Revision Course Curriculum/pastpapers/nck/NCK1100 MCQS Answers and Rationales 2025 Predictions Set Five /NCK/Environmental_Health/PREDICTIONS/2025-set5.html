Section 1: Water, Sanitation & Hygiene (WASH)

25 Marks
1.
A rural community reports increased cases of diarrhea following a flood. Which intervention should take priority to prevent further cases?
A. Distribution of oral rehydration salts (ORS)
B. Health education on personal hygiene
C. Restoration of safe water supply
D. Chlorination of household water containers
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: While ORS and hygiene education are essential for treatment and prevention, restoring access to safe water is the most immediate and sustainable intervention. Without safe water, residents will continue to be exposed to pathogens, perpetuating the cycle of disease. This addresses the source, not just the symptoms, making it the most critical action.

2.
During a sanitation inspection, a latrine is found built less than 15 meters from a shallow well. What is the most likely risk associated with this setup?
A. Bad smell affecting water taste
B. Risk of fly infestation
C. Contamination of the water source
D. Difficulty accessing the latrine
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Building a latrine too close to a water source increases the risk of fecal matter leaching into the well, especially if the water table is high. This leads to serious contamination with pathogens like E. coli or cholera, which can cause widespread waterborne illnesses. Proper siting is crucial for environmental health.

3.
Which vector control method best illustrates an integration of environmental and chemical approaches?
A. Fogging alone during outbreaks
B. Draining stagnant water and using larvicides
C. Spraying insecticides inside houses
D. Distributing insecticide-treated nets
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Integrated vector management involves combining environmental measures (like eliminating breeding sites) with chemical control (larvicides). This dual strategy is more sustainable and effective, reducing reliance on chemicals alone and minimizing resistance. It addresses both prevention and immediate control, aligning with public health best practices.

4.
Which method is best suited for community-level disposal of organic solid waste?
A. Open dumping
B. Sanitary landfill
C. Composting
D. Incineration
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Composting is a sustainable, low-cost method ideal for communities with organic waste. It reduces waste volume and produces useful compost for agriculture. Unlike dumping or incineration, composting is environmentally friendly and encourages community involvement, promoting sustainable waste management practices and reducing pollution risks.

5.
A food vendor lacks a proper waste disposal system. What is the most probable consequence of this on community health?
A. Increased rodent infestation
B. Reduction in food demand
C. Decreased microbial content in food
D. Higher customer turnover
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: A

Rationale: Improper disposal of food waste attracts rodents and other pests that carry diseases like leptospirosis and plague. Rodents contaminate food and surfaces, increasing the risk of zoonotic transmission. Ensuring proper waste disposal is key to maintaining hygiene and protecting public health in food environments.

6.
Which of the following indicates an effective community-based water treatment intervention?
A. Government-funded piped water schemes
B. Households consistently using biosand filters
C. Periodic testing by health inspectors
D. Distribution of bottled water during drought
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: While centralized systems are important, household-level biosand filters empower communities to take control of their water safety. They are low-cost, sustainable, and effective in removing pathogens when used consistently. Regular use indicates proper uptake and success of community water treatment interventions.

7.
During a cholera outbreak, a public health officer prioritizes door-to-door chlorine distribution. Which justification best supports this strategy?
A. Chlorine kills all microorganisms
B. Ensures political visibility of the intervention
C. Provides immediate and accessible protection
D. Reduces the need for health education
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Chlorine is a fast-acting disinfectant that can be used immediately by households to treat contaminated water. Its availability at the point of use provides an essential barrier during outbreaks, especially in areas lacking centralized water treatment. It’s a practical, rapid-response strategy for epidemic control.

8.
What is the most environmentally sustainable method for managing non-infectious hospital waste?
A. Incineration
B. Landfilling
C. Autoclaving
D. Segregation and recycling
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: D

Rationale: Recycling non-infectious hospital waste reduces environmental impact, conserves resources, and decreases the cost of disposal. Segregation ensures that only hazardous materials are treated with costly or energy-intensive methods like incineration, promoting a circular economy and environmental stewardship within healthcare systems.

9.
A health worker observes schoolchildren frequently absent due to respiratory issues. The school is located near a dumpsite. What is the most appropriate environmental health intervention?
A. Administer cough syrups
B. Provide face masks to children
C. Advocate for relocation or closure of the dumpsite
D. Increase awareness on respiratory hygiene
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Chronic exposure to pollutants from dumpsites can cause or worsen respiratory diseases. While masks and awareness help, removing the environmental hazard altogether is the most effective long-term solution. Public health advocacy addresses root causes, not just symptoms, aligning with environmental justice principles.

10.
A newly built housing project lacks proper drainage. What long-term health risk is most likely if no intervention occurs?
A. Vector-borne diseases
B. Malnutrition
C. Tetanus
D. Water fluorosis
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: A

Rationale: Poor drainage creates breeding grounds for mosquitoes and other vectors, leading to diseases such as malaria and dengue. Standing water and waste accumulation contribute significantly to vector proliferation. Addressing drainage is a fundamental step in environmental sanitation and disease prevention.

11.
What is the most reliable method for determining whether a water source is microbiologically safe for drinking?
A. Checking for color and turbidity
B. Measuring pH and temperature
C. Conducting a fecal coliform test
D. Assessing the water's taste and smell
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: A fecal coliform test directly detects bacteria from human or animal waste, which are indicators of microbial contamination. While color, smell, and pH may suggest potential problems, they don’t confirm biological contamination. Fecal coliform presence signifies a risk of diseases like typhoid and dysentery, making it the most dependable method.

12.
A community uses pit latrines but frequently experiences latrine overflow during the rainy season. What long-term solution is most appropriate?
A. Add more superstructure to the latrines
B. Raise latrines above flood level and improve drainage
C. Use chemical disinfectants frequently
D. Limit usage to prevent overflow
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Overflowing latrines during rains contaminate surface water and soil, posing serious public health risks. Constructing latrines above the flood level and improving drainage ensures waste remains contained. Chemical disinfectants and usage control are short-term solutions and do not address structural inadequacies or floodwater management.

13.
Which housing design feature most effectively prevents vector infestation in tropical climates?
A. Open windows for ventilation
B. Concrete flooring with sealed edges
C. Thatched roofs to absorb heat
D. Curtain partitions inside the house
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Concrete floors reduce breeding sites for insects like fleas and mosquitoes, which thrive in dirt floors or cracks. Sealing gaps prevents pests from entering and nesting. While ventilation is important, open windows without screens invite vectors. Structural prevention is key in vector control strategies.

14.
A household stores water in open containers. Which health promotion message best addresses this practice?
A. "Boil your drinking water regularly"
B. "Keep your containers near sunlight"
C. "Cover containers to prevent contamination"
D. "Empty containers every two days"
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Open containers invite vectors like flies and mosquitoes and allow for fecal-oral contamination through dust or hands. Covering containers is a simple, effective behavior change that prevents disease. While boiling is helpful, contamination may occur after boiling if storage is unhygienic, making covers a first line of defense.

15.
In a cholera-prone area, which action provides the greatest long-term reduction in disease burden?
A. Distributing cholera vaccines during outbreaks
B. Conducting weekly community cleanups
C. Establishing piped, treated water supply systems
D. Providing hand sanitizers to schools
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Cholera thrives where water and sanitation infrastructure is lacking. Vaccines offer temporary protection, and cleanup activities reduce immediate risk, but long-term prevention requires reliable access to treated water. Infrastructure improvements interrupt the fecal-oral transmission route and promote continuous, community-wide disease control.

16.
The most sustainable way to reduce indoor air pollution in a rural home using firewood is:
A. Open windows while cooking
B. Use of improved cookstoves with chimneys
C. Cooking during off-peak hours
D. Burning firewood in smaller quantities
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Improved cookstoves significantly reduce smoke emission by improving combustion efficiency and venting smoke outside. This reduces the risk of respiratory diseases associated with indoor air pollution. While open windows help, they don’t eliminate pollutants. Cookstove innovation is both sustainable and health-promoting.

17.
A community health nurse observes that a borehole frequently breaks down. What is the most effective strategy to ensure consistent access to clean water?
A. Provide bottled water as backup
B. Train local artisans in borehole maintenance
C. Request municipal water connection
D. Shift to river water with filtration
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Empowering local communities with technical skills ensures timely repairs, reducing dependence on external support. It promotes ownership, sustainability, and resilience. External water sources may be inconsistent or unsafe, and shifting to untreated river water increases disease risk. Community capacity building is the ideal long-term approach.

18.
Which of the following contributes most to the transmission of trachoma in poor sanitation areas?
A. Overcrowding in homes
B. Use of shared latrines
C. Improper waste disposal
D. Flies breeding on human waste
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: D

Rationale: Trachoma is spread by eye-seeking flies that breed on exposed human feces. Proper sanitation disrupts this vector’s lifecycle. While other factors play roles, addressing open defecation and waste disposal directly impacts transmission rates. Environmental interventions are key in the WHO’s SAFE strategy for trachoma control.

19.
A key component of school-based environmental health education should include:
A. Emphasis on medical treatment options
B. Awareness of health rights
C. Hands-on hygiene practices like handwashing
D. Legal aspects of sanitation
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Practical hygiene behaviors like handwashing have a direct impact on reducing disease transmission among schoolchildren. They are age-appropriate, easily teachable, and reinforce personal responsibility. While understanding health rights is important, practical skills bring immediate health benefits and are retained better through experiential learning.

20.
The best indicator for effective household waste management at community level is:
A. Regular municipal garbage collection
B. Visible absence of litter and illegal dumpsites
C. High fines for illegal dumping
D. Household ownership of garbage bins
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: A clean environment reflects behavior change and system effectiveness. Garbage bins and fines are helpful, but without community buy-in and functional collection systems, waste accumulates. Environmental cleanliness is a direct observable outcome of good waste management, making it a reliable indicator of success.

21.
During a community diagnosis, it is found that waste segregation is poorly practiced. What is the most effective public health response?
A. Develop a waste management policy
B. Initiate enforcement through local police
C. Train households on practical segregation methods
D. Hire more waste collection personnel
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Behavior change through education is more effective than enforcement or policy alone. Teaching people how and why to separate waste at the source empowers them to adopt sustainable practices. Training leads to long-term improvements, which enforcement without awareness cannot sustain.

22.
What is the most effective measure for preventing lead poisoning from informal battery recycling in urban slums?
A. Use of protective gloves and masks
B. Relocating activities away from homes
C. Conducting regular blood lead level testing
D. Banning the activity completely
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Informal battery recycling releases toxic lead particles that affect children and adults. The most protective step is removing the source from residential zones. While PPE reduces exposure, relocation limits environmental contamination entirely. Long-term health effects are best mitigated by minimizing exposure at its source.

23.
A community facing recurrent water shortages is advised to construct rainwater harvesting systems. What factor should be prioritized in design?
A. Size of storage tank
B. Color of the roof
C. Cost of materials
D. Type of pipe used
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: A

Rationale: The storage tank’s capacity determines how much rainwater can be saved for use during dry periods. Oversized tanks may not be cost-effective, while undersized tanks result in water loss. Design should match rainfall patterns, roof size, and household needs to maximize the system's utility and sustainability.

24.
Which practice best demonstrates community-level adaptation to climate change in environmental health?
A. Distribution of mosquito nets
B. Water rationing during drought
C. Planting drought-resistant vegetation
D. Spraying pesticides more frequently
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Climate adaptation focuses on long-term solutions to environmental changes. Drought-resistant plants conserve water and reduce soil erosion, contributing to food security and sustainable land use. Other options are reactive rather than adaptive. Vegetative adaptation builds resilience and aligns with sustainable environmental management goals.

25.
A peri-urban area experiences increased cases of respiratory illness. Which environmental determinant is most likely responsible?
A. Excessive rainfall
B. Lack of green spaces
C. Unregulated industrial emissions
D. Seasonal dust storms
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Industrial emissions release pollutants like sulfur dioxide and particulates, which irritate airways and cause chronic respiratory diseases. In peri-urban areas, factories often lack proper regulation, directly affecting nearby populations. While dust and rainfall may play roles, sustained pollution exposure is a stronger and more controllable risk.

Section 2: Waste & Pollution Management

25 Marks
26.
The most appropriate method for disposing of medical sharps in a rural health center is:
A. Burning in an open pit
B. Dumping in a community landfill
C. Encapsulation and burial
D. Soaking in disinfectant before disposal
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Encapsulation involves placing sharps in a sealed, puncture-proof container and burying it in a safe pit. It prevents injury and environmental contamination. Open burning releases toxins, and dumping in landfills risks scavenger exposure. Disinfectants are insufficient for managing physical hazards posed by sharps.

27.
What is the primary health concern associated with improper e-waste disposal?
A. Increased greenhouse gas emissions
B. Contamination with heavy metals like lead and mercury
C. Overcrowding of landfill sites
D. Generation of non-biodegradable waste
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: E-waste contains toxic substances such as lead, cadmium, and mercury. When improperly disposed of, these substances leach into soil and groundwater, posing serious health risks like neurological damage and kidney disease. The main health concern is direct exposure to or ingestion of these heavy metals.

28.
Which of the following is the best example of a point-source pollutant?
A. Pesticide runoff from farms
B. Car emissions across a city
C. Smoke from household fires
D. Discharge from a sewage pipe
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: D

Rationale: Point-source pollution originates from a single, identifiable source, such as a pipe or factory discharge. This makes it easier to monitor and regulate. In contrast, the others represent non-point sources, which are diffuse and more difficult to manage due to their widespread nature.

29.
In urban planning, which feature is most important for reducing the urban heat island effect?
A. Expanding road networks
B. Increasing green cover and trees
C. Building more high-rise apartments
D. Using dark roofing materials
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Vegetation helps cool the environment through shade and evapotranspiration, reducing the heat island effect common in cities. Green spaces also improve air quality and promote mental health. High-rise buildings and dark roofs absorb heat, worsening the urban heat issue.

30.
Which of the following best defines environmental sanitation?
A. Collection and disposal of medical waste only
B. Control of environmental factors that may harm health
C. Disinfection of public hospitals
D. Immunization of children in urban areas
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Environmental sanitation involves managing water, air, soil, waste, and housing to prevent disease and promote health. It’s broader than waste collection or disinfection alone. The goal is to eliminate conditions conducive to disease transmission and ensure a healthy living environment.

31.
What is the most effective method of reducing transmission of soil-transmitted helminths?
A. Spraying insecticides on open soil
B. Providing footwear and improving sanitation
C. Boiling drinking water
D. Disposing of food waste properly
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Soil-transmitted helminths (like hookworms) enter the body through skin contact with contaminated soil or ingestion of eggs. Wearing shoes prevents skin penetration, while sanitation reduces environmental contamination with feces. Boiling water helps but does not address the primary transmission pathway for these parasites.

32.
What is the major environmental risk associated with unlined landfills?
A. Spread of mosquitoes
B. Contamination of groundwater
C. Noise pollution
D. Deforestation
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Unlined landfills allow leachate—a toxic mix of waste fluids—to seep into the soil and contaminate groundwater. This endangers drinking water supplies and ecosystems. Lined landfills prevent this by creating a barrier between the waste and the environment.

33.
Which of the following diseases is most commonly associated with poor solid waste management?
A. Tuberculosis
B. Schistosomiasis
C. Leptospirosis
D. Cholera
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: D

Rationale: Inadequate waste management can contaminate water sources, facilitating the spread of waterborne diseases like cholera. Flies and rodents feeding on waste further spread pathogens. While leptospirosis is linked to rodents, cholera is more directly tied to contaminated water from unmanaged waste.

34.
Which of the following practices is most effective in controlling houseflies at the household level?
A. Spraying insecticide every week
B. Burning waste in the compound
C. Covering food and disposing of garbage properly
D. Leaving windows open for airflow
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Houseflies breed on exposed waste and carry pathogens. Proper food covering and waste disposal eliminate breeding grounds and reduce attraction to the house. Insecticides can help, but behavioral practices are more sustainable and cost-effective for long-term control.

35.
Which of the following is a renewable energy source suitable for rural health facilities?
A. Diesel generators
B. Charcoal
C. Solar power
D. Firewood
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Solar power is clean, sustainable, and suitable for off-grid areas, making it ideal for rural health facilities. It reduces dependency on fossil fuels and offers consistent energy for lighting, refrigeration, and medical equipment, especially in areas lacking electrical infrastructure.

36.
A child presents with blue lines on the gums and symptoms of anemia. Which environmental health hazard is most likely involved?
A. Excess fluoride in water
B. Lead poisoning
C. Mercury exposure
D. Iron deficiency
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Lead poisoning can cause a bluish line along the gums (Burton’s line), anemia, abdominal pain, and cognitive deficits. It’s commonly linked to exposure from paint, pipes, or contaminated soil. Recognizing these signs is crucial for early intervention and removal of the exposure source.

37.
What is the most appropriate method for managing biodegradable solid waste at community level?
A. Incineration
B. Deep burial
C. Composting
D. Exporting to other towns
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Composting is a sustainable, low-cost solution for managing organic waste like food scraps and plant material. It converts waste into nutrient-rich manure, reduces landfill burden, and supports agriculture. Incineration is unnecessary and polluting for biodegradable materials.

38.
What is a major public health risk of using untreated wastewater for irrigation?
A. Salinization of soil
B. Destruction of crop quality
C. Transmission of intestinal parasites
D. Increased food prices
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Untreated wastewater contains pathogens like Ascaris and Giardia. Using it for irrigation contaminates vegetables and fruits, posing health risks, especially when consumed raw. The WHO recommends treating wastewater to a safe standard before agricultural use to prevent foodborne diseases.

39.
Which of the following is most effective in preventing malaria in a high-burden community?
A. Wearing long-sleeved clothing at night
B. Mass spraying of insecticides in households
C. Use of insecticide-treated mosquito nets
D. Taking antimalarial drugs monthly
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: ITNs provide a physical and chemical barrier against mosquitoes, reducing malaria transmission effectively and affordably. Spraying is costly and less sustainable. Drug prophylaxis is not practical for entire populations. Nets remain the cornerstone of community-level malaria prevention.

40.
Which of the following is the least environmentally sustainable energy source?
A. Hydroelectric
B. Solar
C. Biomass (wood)
D. Wind
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Though renewable, using wood for energy contributes to deforestation, indoor air pollution, and carbon emissions if not managed sustainably. In contrast, hydro, solar, and wind have minimal environmental footprints when properly implemented. Biomass overuse is common in low-resource settings.

41.
Which environmental factor most significantly contributes to non-communicable diseases?
A. Noise pollution
B. Industrial air pollution
C. Open defecation
D. Unsafe drinking water
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Chronic exposure to air pollutants from industry increases the risk of asthma, heart disease, and lung cancer—major non-communicable diseases. While waterborne and sanitation issues contribute to infectious diseases, air pollution is a critical and growing factor in the NCD burden worldwide.

42.
Which practice increases the risk of zoonotic disease transmission in urban settings?
A. Keeping pets indoors
B. Feeding animals with commercial feeds
C. Keeping livestock in residential areas
D. Regular veterinary checkups
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Housing animals in human dwellings increases exposure to zoonotic diseases like leptospirosis and brucellosis. Close contact facilitates transmission via feces, urine, or direct bites. Urban zoning should discourage such practices to protect public health.

43.
Which group is most vulnerable to environmental hazards in low-income settings?
A. Adolescents
B. Working adults
C. Elderly and young children
D. Middle-aged individuals
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: These groups have weaker immune systems and lower physiological resilience, making them more susceptible to pollutants, infections, and heat stress. Environmental interventions should prioritize their protection due to their high vulnerability and limited coping mechanisms.

44.
Which of the following best reduces the incidence of vector-borne diseases in stagnant water areas?
A. Adding chlorine
B. Regular desilting and draining
C. Installing water filters
D. Building stone embankments
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Stagnant water serves as breeding ground for mosquitoes. Desilting and draining eliminate these habitats, disrupting vector life cycles. Chlorine treats drinking water but does not affect mosquito larvae. Preventing accumulation of stagnant water is a key vector control strategy.

45.
Which of the following would most likely reduce the incidence of fluorosis in a community?
A. Promoting milk consumption
B. Switching to alternative water sources
C. Boiling water before use
D. Using plastic water storage tanks
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Fluorosis results from excessive fluoride in drinking water. Boiling does not remove fluoride, and dietary changes may not prevent accumulation. Identifying and switching to low-fluoride water sources is the most effective intervention to prevent the condition.

46.
Which of the following is the most common source of indoor air pollution in low-income households?
A. Refrigerators
B. Kerosene lamps
C. Tobacco smoke
D. Cooking with biomass fuels
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: D

Rationale: Burning wood, charcoal, or dung indoors without ventilation produces harmful smoke containing particulates and toxic gases. This leads to respiratory infections, especially in women and children. Clean cooking solutions like LPG or improved stoves are crucial in reducing this burden.

47.
Which of the following waste types is most hazardous to public health if not disposed of properly?
A. Paper waste
B. Plastic wrappers
C. Used syringes
D. Food leftovers
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Syringes are sharp medical waste that can transmit bloodborne infections like HIV and hepatitis if reused or handled improperly. They require specialized disposal through incineration or encapsulation. Public health interventions must prioritize medical waste segregation and safe disposal.

48.
What is the most effective long-term strategy for vector control in urban slums?
A. Weekly insecticide spraying
B. Community-led environmental sanitation
C. Building mosquito-proof houses
D. Mass drug administration
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Empowering communities to clean up waste, drain stagnant water, and maintain hygiene disrupts breeding environments for disease vectors. It’s cost-effective, sustainable, and fosters ownership. Spraying is temporary, while structural changes are not feasible without community participation.

49.
Which of the following interventions best supports environmental health during floods?
A. Mass chlorination of food
B. Isolation of infected individuals
C. Provision of clean drinking water
D. Distribution of antibiotics to all residents
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Floods often contaminate water sources, leading to outbreaks of cholera, typhoid, and hepatitis A. Ensuring access to clean drinking water is the first line of defense. Antibiotics and isolation are only useful after infection, while prevention requires water safety.

50.
Which of the following is a key environmental risk factor for skin cancer?
A. High humidity
B. Indoor air pollution
C. Ultraviolet radiation from sunlight
D. Poor sanitation
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Prolonged exposure to UV radiation damages skin cells and increases the risk of skin cancer. Protective behaviors like using sunscreen and wearing hats are essential, especially in regions with high sun intensity. Other options are unrelated to skin cancer risk.
