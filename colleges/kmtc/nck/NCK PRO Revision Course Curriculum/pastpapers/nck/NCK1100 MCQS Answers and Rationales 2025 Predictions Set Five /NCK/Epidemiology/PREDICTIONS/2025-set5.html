Epidemiology

NCK, KRCHN, PAPER 2 - 1100 MCQS Answers and Rationales

Instructions

  • This paper consists of 50 Multiple Choice Questions focusing on Epidemiology.
  • Each question has four options; select the most appropriate one.
  • Detailed rationales are provided for each answer to facilitate effective revision.
  • Total Marks: 50 | Time Allowed: 60 Minutes.

Section 1: Epidemiological Principles

25 Marks
1.
What is the main purpose of epidemiology?
A) To treat individual patients
B) To study disease distribution and determinants in populations
C) To develop new medications
D) To perform surgical interventions
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) To study disease distribution and determinants in populations

Rationale: Epidemiology focuses on understanding how diseases are distributed in populations and what factors determine or influence this distribution, which is essential for public health planning and disease prevention.

2.
Which measure best describes the frequency of a disease at a specific point in time?
A) Incidence rate
B) Prevalence rate
C) Mortality rate
D) Case fatality rate
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Prevalence rate

Rationale: Prevalence refers to the proportion of individuals who have a disease at a specific point or period, indicating disease burden in a population.

3.
Incidence rate is best defined as:
A) Number of new cases in a population over a period of time
B) Total cases existing at a point in time
C) Number of deaths due to a disease
D) Proportion of cases that result in death
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: A) Number of new cases in a population over a period of time

Rationale: Incidence measures new cases occurring in a population during a specified time, which helps understand disease risk and dynamics.

4.
Which of the following is NOT a characteristic of a cohort study?
A) Observational design
B) Follows subjects over time to observe outcomes
C) Random allocation of exposure
D) Measures incidence of disease
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C) Random allocation of exposure

Rationale: Cohort studies are observational and do not involve random allocation; this is a feature of experimental studies like randomized controlled trials.

5.
What does a case-control study compare?
A) Exposure status between diseased and non-diseased individuals
B) Disease incidence between two groups
C) Prevalence of disease over time
D) Mortality rates in exposed populations
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: A) Exposure status between diseased and non-diseased individuals

Rationale: Case-control studies compare past exposure history in individuals with disease (cases) and without disease (controls) to identify risk factors.

6.
Which measure is most appropriate to estimate risk in a cohort study?
A) Odds ratio
B) Relative risk
C) Attributable risk
D) Case fatality rate
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Relative risk

Rationale: Relative risk compares the risk of disease in exposed versus unexposed groups, commonly used in cohort studies.

7.
In epidemiology, the term "endemic" means:
A) A disease occurring at low rates in a population
B) A disease constantly present in a population
C) A sudden increase in disease cases
D) Global spread of a disease
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) A disease constantly present in a population

Rationale: Endemic diseases maintain a steady presence within a specific geographic area or population.

8.
Which of the following best defines a "pandemic"?
A) Disease outbreak confined to a single location
B) A disease affecting many countries or continents
C) Sporadic cases of a disease
D) Disease limited to animals
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) A disease affecting many countries or continents

Rationale: A pandemic refers to an epidemic that has spread across countries or continents, affecting a large number of people globally.

9.
The term "mortality rate" refers to:
A) Number of new cases of a disease
B) Number of deaths in a population over a time period
C) Proportion of people cured from a disease
D) Number of people exposed to a risk factor
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Number of deaths in a population over a time period

Rationale: Mortality rate quantifies deaths in a specified population and time frame.

10.
Which of the following describes "confounding"?
A) A factor that distorts the association between exposure and outcome
B) A causal factor in disease
C) An error in data collection
D) Random variation in study results
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: A) A factor that distorts the association between exposure and outcome

Rationale: Confounding occurs when a third variable influences both the exposure and outcome, leading to a misleading association.

11.
The "attack rate" is best described as:
A) The rate of new cases in a population over a long period
B) The proportion of people who become ill in a defined group during an outbreak
C) The death rate from a disease
D) The rate of recovery from an infection
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) The proportion of people who become ill in a defined group during an outbreak

Rationale: Attack rate is the proportion of exposed individuals who develop disease during a specific outbreak or exposure period.

12.
The "case fatality rate" measures:
A) Risk of dying among all exposed individuals
B) The proportion of deaths among diagnosed cases of a disease
C) Mortality rate in the general population
D) The incidence of new cases
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) The proportion of deaths among diagnosed cases of a disease

Rationale: Case fatality rate is the percentage of cases that result in death, reflecting disease severity.

13.
Which of the following is NOT a measure of association?
A) Odds ratio
B) Relative risk
C) Incidence rate
D) Attributable risk
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C) Incidence rate

Rationale: Incidence rate measures frequency, not the strength of association between exposure and outcome.

14.
Bias in epidemiological studies refers to:
A) Random errors in data collection
B) Systematic error that distorts study results
C) Normal variation in populations
D) Adjustment for confounding variables
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Systematic error that distorts study results

Rationale: Bias is a systematic error affecting the validity of study findings.

15.
A "cross-sectional study" measures:
A) Incidence over time
B) Exposure and disease status at a single point in time
C) Risk factors prospectively
D) Deaths following disease diagnosis
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Exposure and disease status at a single point in time

Rationale: Cross-sectional studies assess exposure and outcome simultaneously in a population.

16.
In an epidemic curve, the x-axis usually represents:
A) Number of cases
B) Time of disease onset
C) Population size
D) Type of exposure
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Time of disease onset

Rationale: Epidemic curves plot the number of cases against time to visualize outbreak progression.

17.
Which of these is a characteristic of a randomized controlled trial (RCT)?
A) Observational only
B) Random assignment of exposure or intervention
C) Uses retrospective data only
D) Does not measure outcomes
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Random assignment of exposure or intervention

Rationale: RCTs involve random allocation to interventions to minimize bias and establish causality.

18.
What is "herd immunity"?
A) Immunity acquired by an individual
B) Immunity in a population that reduces disease spread when many are immune
C) Immunity developed after infection only
D) Immunity from passive antibody transfer
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Immunity in a population that reduces disease spread when many are immune

Rationale: Herd immunity protects populations when a critical mass is immune, reducing disease transmission.

19.
Which factor is an example of a "host factor" in disease epidemiology?
A) Bacterial virulence
B) Air pollution
C) Age and immune status
D) Water contamination
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C) Age and immune status

Rationale: Host factors relate to the characteristics of the person that affect disease susceptibility.

20.
"Incubation period" is defined as:
A) Time between exposure and symptom onset
B) Duration of symptoms
C) Time from disease diagnosis to recovery
D) Interval between vaccination doses
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: A) Time between exposure and symptom onset

Rationale: Incubation period is the latency time before clinical signs appear after exposure.

21.
Which of the following study designs is best suited to study rare diseases?
A) Cohort study
B) Case-control study
C) Cross-sectional study
D) Randomized controlled trial
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Case-control study

Rationale: Case-control studies efficiently investigate rare diseases by selecting cases and controls.

22.
The term "surveillance" in epidemiology refers to:
A) Investigating individual cases only
B) Continuous systematic collection, analysis, and interpretation of health data
C) Treating patients during outbreaks
D) Random sampling of the population
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Continuous systematic collection, analysis, and interpretation of health data

Rationale: Surveillance monitors disease trends to guide public health interventions.

23.
Which of the following is an example of primary prevention?
A) Screening for cancer
B) Vaccination against infectious diseases
C) Rehabilitation after stroke
D) Treating tuberculosis
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Vaccination against infectious diseases

Rationale: Primary prevention aims to prevent disease occurrence, with vaccination being a key example.

24.
The "population at risk" is best described as:
A) People currently ill
B) People exposed and susceptible to the disease
C) People already immune
D) People who cannot be infected
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) People exposed and susceptible to the disease

Rationale: Population at risk includes individuals who can potentially develop the disease.

25.
A "confounding variable" must:
A) Be related only to the exposure
B) Be related only to the outcome
C) Be associated with both exposure and outcome but not be a causal factor
D) Cause the outcome directly
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C) Be associated with both exposure and outcome but not be a causal factor

Rationale: Confounders distort the true exposure-disease relationship because they influence both.

Section 2: Data Analysis & Study Design

25 Marks
26.
Which measure is best suited for assessing the risk of developing a disease in a population over a specified period?
A) Prevalence
B) Incidence rate
C) Mortality rate
D) Case fatality rate
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Incidence rate

Rationale: Incidence rate measures the number of new cases occurring in a population during a specific time period and is best suited for assessing the risk of developing a disease. Prevalence includes both new and existing cases, while mortality and case fatality rates relate to death outcomes rather than risk of developing disease.

27.
Which of the following study designs is most appropriate to investigate the cause of a rare disease?
A) Cohort study
B) Case-control study
C) Cross-sectional study
D) Randomized controlled trial
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Case-control study

Rationale: Case-control studies are efficient for rare diseases because they start with cases (those with disease) and compare them to controls. Cohort studies require following a large population over time, which can be inefficient for rare diseases. Cross-sectional studies cannot establish causality, and randomized trials are often unethical or impractical for rare diseases.

28.
What does the term "confounding" refer to in epidemiology?
A) Random error in data collection
B) A variable associated with both exposure and outcome, distorting the true association
C) A bias introduced by selective participation
D) Loss of participants during follow-up
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) A variable associated with both exposure and outcome, distorting the true association

Rationale: Confounding occurs when an extraneous factor is related to both the exposure and outcome, potentially misleading the apparent association. This can be controlled through study design or statistical methods.

29.
Which measure provides the proportion of deaths among diagnosed cases of a disease?
A) Incidence rate
B) Prevalence rate
C) Mortality rate
D) Case fatality rate
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: D) Case fatality rate

Rationale: Case fatality rate measures the proportion of deaths within a defined group of diagnosed cases, reflecting disease severity. Mortality rate measures deaths in a general population, incidence is about new cases, and prevalence is about total cases.

30.
In a cohort study, what is the main advantage over a case-control study?
A) Less time consuming
B) More efficient for rare diseases
C) Can calculate incidence and relative risk directly
D) Requires fewer resources
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C) Can calculate incidence and relative risk directly

Rationale: Cohort studies follow exposed and unexposed groups over time, allowing direct calculation of incidence rates and relative risks. Case-control studies cannot calculate incidence directly and estimate odds ratios instead.

31.
Which term describes the proportion of a population with a disease at a specific point in time?
A) Point prevalence
B) Period prevalence
C) Incidence rate
D) Attack rate
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: A) Point prevalence

Rationale: Point prevalence measures the proportion of people with a disease at a particular moment, while period prevalence covers a time span. Incidence rate measures new cases, and attack rate is a form of incidence used in outbreaks.

32.
What type of bias occurs when participants are lost to follow-up in a longitudinal study?
A) Recall bias
B) Selection bias
C) Attrition bias
D) Reporting bias
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C) Attrition bias

Rationale: Attrition bias arises when participants drop out during a study, potentially leading to systematic differences between those who remain and those lost, which may bias results.

33.
Which epidemiological term refers to the strength of association between an exposure and an outcome?
A) Prevalence
B) Relative risk
C) Sensitivity
D) Specificity
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Relative risk

Rationale: Relative risk measures the strength of association by comparing incidence in exposed vs unexposed groups. Sensitivity and specificity relate to test performance, and prevalence describes disease frequency.

34.
What is the main purpose of randomization in a clinical trial?
A) To increase sample size
B) To reduce confounding by equal distribution of known and unknown factors
C) To ensure blinding of participants
D) To speed up data collection
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) To reduce confounding by equal distribution of known and unknown factors

Rationale: Randomization aims to evenly distribute confounders across treatment groups, minimizing bias and allowing causal inference. Blinding is a separate process, and randomization does not directly affect sample size or speed.

35.
Which of the following is a non-modifiable risk factor?
A) Smoking
B) Hypertension
C) Age
D) Physical inactivity
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C) Age

Rationale: Age is non-modifiable, meaning it cannot be changed. Smoking, hypertension, and inactivity are modifiable risk factors that can be targeted in prevention efforts.

36.
Which type of study provides the highest level of evidence for causality?
A) Case-control study
B) Cross-sectional study
C) Randomized controlled trial
D) Ecological study
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C) Randomized controlled trial

Rationale: RCTs minimize bias through randomization and control conditions, providing the strongest evidence for causality compared to observational studies.

37.
What does "incidence density" take into account that simple incidence proportion does not?
A) Number of existing cases
B) Total population size
C) Person-time at risk
D) Case severity
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C) Person-time at risk

Rationale: Incidence density measures new cases per unit of person-time, accounting for varying follow-up times, unlike incidence proportion which ignores time dimension.

38.
Which bias occurs when participants remember past exposures differently depending on their disease status?
A) Selection bias
B) Recall bias
C) Observer bias
D) Confounding
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Recall bias

Rationale: Recall bias happens in retrospective studies when cases may recall exposures more accurately or differently than controls, affecting validity.

39.
Which of the following is an example of primary prevention?
A) Screening for breast cancer
B) Vaccination against measles
C) Rehabilitation after stroke
D) Chemotherapy for cancer
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Vaccination against measles

Rationale: Primary prevention aims to prevent disease occurrence (e.g., vaccination). Screening is secondary prevention (early detection), rehabilitation is tertiary (manage established disease), and chemotherapy is treatment.

40.
In epidemiology, "specificity" of a diagnostic test refers to:
A) Ability to identify those with the disease
B) Ability to identify those without the disease
C) Proportion of true positives among positives
D) Proportion of false negatives
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Ability to identify those without the disease

Rationale: Specificity is the tests ability to correctly identify those who do NOT have the disease (true negatives), reducing false positives.

41.
The "ecological fallacy" refers to:
A) Incorrectly assuming group-level associations apply at individual level
B) Bias introduced by ecological factors
C) Confounding due to environmental exposures
D) Error due to missing data
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: A) Incorrectly assuming group-level associations apply at individual level

Rationale: Ecological fallacy occurs when conclusions about individuals are drawn solely from group-level data, which can be misleading.

42.
The "attack rate" is most commonly used in:
A) Chronic disease studies
B) Outbreak investigations
C) Longitudinal cohort studies
D) Randomized trials
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Outbreak investigations

Rationale: Attack rate measures new cases in a short period during outbreaks, especially for infectious diseases.

43.
Which of the following is a potential limitation of cross-sectional studies?
A) High cost
B) Cannot establish temporality
C) Large sample size required
D) Difficult to conduct
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Cannot establish temporality

Rationale: Cross-sectional studies assess exposure and outcome at one time, limiting ability to determine which came first (temporality).

44.
Which statistical test is most appropriate for comparing the incidence of disease between two groups?
A) Chi-square test
B) T-test
C) Correlation coefficient
D) ANOVA
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: A) Chi-square test

Rationale: Chi-square tests compare proportions such as incidence rates between categorical groups, while t-tests and ANOVA compare means.

45.
In which type of study are participants matched on confounders to control bias?
A) Cohort study
B) Case-control study
C) Cross-sectional study
D) Ecological study
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Case-control study

Rationale: Matching is commonly used in case-control studies to control confounding by selecting controls similar to cases on certain factors.

46.
What is "lead-time bias"?
A) Overestimation of survival time due to earlier detection by screening
B) Delay in disease diagnosis
C) Bias due to loss to follow-up
D) Confounding by indication
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: A) Overestimation of survival time due to earlier detection by screening

Rationale: Lead-time bias occurs when screening advances diagnosis time without affecting survival, making survival seem longer.

47.
Which of the following best describes a "surveillance system" in epidemiology?
A) A method for randomizing participants
B) A system for ongoing data collection and analysis of disease occurrence
C) A treatment protocol for infectious diseases
D) A case-control study design
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) A system for ongoing data collection and analysis of disease occurrence

Rationale: Surveillance systems monitor disease trends to guide public health actions.

48.
Which of the following terms describes the proportion of people exposed to a risk factor in a population?
A) Prevalence of exposure
B) Incidence of disease
C) Attributable risk
D) Odds ratio
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: A) Prevalence of exposure

Rationale: Prevalence of exposure measures how common a risk factor is in the population.

49.
Which of the following describes a "double-blind" study?
A) Only participants are unaware of group assignment
B) Only investigators are unaware of group assignment
C) Both participants and investigators are unaware of group assignment
D) Neither group assignment nor outcomes are recorded
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C) Both participants and investigators are unaware of group assignment

Rationale: Double-blinding prevents bias by keeping both parties unaware, reducing placebo and observer biases.

50.
The term "herd immunity" refers to:
A) Immunity of individual after vaccination
B) Resistance of a population to disease spread when a high proportion are immune
C) Immunity conferred only by natural infection
D) Immunity that wanes over time
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B) Resistance of a population to disease spread when a high proportion are immune

Rationale: Herd immunity occurs when enough people are immune, reducing disease spread and protecting non-immune individuals.
