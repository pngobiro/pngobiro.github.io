Communicable & Vector Borne Diseases

NCK, KRCHN, PAPER 2 - 1100 MCQS Answers and Rationales

Instructions

  • This paper consists of 50 Multiple Choice Questions focusing on Communicable and Vector Borne Diseases.
  • Each question has four options; select the most appropriate one.
  • Detailed rationales are provided for each answer to facilitate effective revision.
  • Total Marks: 50 | Time Allowed: 60 Minutes.

Section 1: Disease Transmission & Prevention

25 Marks
1.
Which of the following is the most effective strategy to interrupt the transmission cycle of malaria?
A. Prompt treatment of infected individuals
B. Routine mass drug administration
C. Use of insecticide-treated mosquito nets
D. Vaccination of all household contacts
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Insecticide-treated nets (ITNs) reduce mosquito bites, which directly interrupts malaria transmission. Prompt treatment helps reduce parasite load but doesn’t prevent mosquito exposure. Mass drug administration has logistical and ethical challenges. Vaccination isn't yet widely effective or available for all.

2.
What is the primary mode of transmission for tuberculosis (TB)?
A. Feco-oral route
B. Vector bite
C. Droplet infection
D. Skin contact
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: TB is transmitted via inhalation of airborne droplets expelled by an infected person through coughing or sneezing. It is not spread by skin contact, ingestion, or vectors. Understanding this helps implement control measures like ventilation, masks, and isolation.

3.
In leprosy, which of the following indicates multibacillary disease?
A. One skin lesion and no nerve involvement
B. Two skin lesions with sensory loss
C. Five or more skin lesions with nerve involvement
D. No skin lesion but positive nasal smear
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Multibacillary leprosy is diagnosed when there are five or more skin lesions, especially with nerve involvement or positive slit skin smears. Fewer lesions suggest paucibacillary leprosy. The number and distribution of lesions help determine treatment duration.

4.
Which is the most effective community-based strategy to reduce the incidence of cholera outbreaks?
A. Boiling drinking water
B. Distributing antibiotics
C. Providing ORS to all households
D. Improving sanitation and waste disposal
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: D

Rationale: Cholera transmission is primarily through contaminated water and food. Improving sanitation addresses the root cause by preventing fecal contamination of water sources. Boiling helps temporarily, and antibiotics are used in severe cases, not for prevention.

5.
What is the causative agent of visceral leishmaniasis (kala-azar)?
A. Plasmodium falciparum
B. Leishmania donovani
C. Trypanosoma cruzi
D. Wuchereria bancrofti
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Leishmania donovani is the protozoan responsible for visceral leishmaniasis, transmitted by the sandfly. Plasmodium causes malaria, Trypanosoma causes Chagas disease, and Wuchereria causes lymphatic filariasis. Correct identification guides accurate diagnosis and treatment.

6.
Which sign is most characteristic of measles in the early stage?
A. Koplik spots
B. Maculopapular rash
C. Lymphadenopathy
D. Productive cough
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: A

Rationale: Koplik spots—small white lesions inside the cheeks—are a pathognomonic early sign of measles, appearing before the skin rash. Cough, rash, and lymphadenopathy are common but not specific. Early recognition helps with isolation and control.

7.
What is the recommended isolation period for a child with chickenpox?
A. Until all lesions crust
B. 3 days after rash appears
C. While fever persists
D. Until rash fades completely
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: A

Rationale: Chickenpox is contagious until all lesions have crusted over, which typically occurs 5–7 days after onset. Fever or fading of the rash is not reliable for ending isolation. Crusting ensures viral shedding has stopped.

8.
Which vector is responsible for transmitting dengue fever?
A. Culex mosquito
B. Anopheles mosquito
C. Aedes mosquito
D. Tsetse fly
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Aedes aegypti is the main vector for dengue fever, known for daytime biting. Anopheles transmits malaria, Culex transmits filariasis, and tsetse fly transmits sleeping sickness. Vector identification is essential for targeted control measures.

9.
Which of the following best describes the incubation period of hepatitis A?
A. 2–6 days
B. 5–10 days
C. 15–50 days
D. 60–120 days
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Hepatitis A has an incubation period of 15–50 days, typically around 28 days. This long period allows for silent spread before symptoms appear. Accurate knowledge is important for contact tracing and post-exposure prophylaxis.

10.
Which lab finding supports a diagnosis of typhoid fever in the first week of illness?
A. Positive Widal test
B. Blood culture positivity
C. Elevated ALT
D. Stool culture positivity
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Blood culture is most sensitive in the first week of typhoid fever. The Widal test may give false positives, and stool cultures are better in later stages. Early diagnosis through blood culture enables timely treatment and outbreak control.

11.
A patient with persistent fever and night sweats tests positive for HIV. Which co-infection is most likely?
A. Tuberculosis
B. Malaria
C. Hepatitis B
D. Dengue
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: A

Rationale: HIV patients are particularly susceptible to TB due to weakened immunity. Night sweats and persistent fever are classic TB symptoms. Malaria and dengue present acutely, and hepatitis B is not typically associated with those symptoms.

12.
Which of the following vaccines is most effective in preventing cervical cancer?
A. Hepatitis B vaccine
B. BCG vaccine
C. HPV vaccine
D. MMR vaccine
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: The human papillomavirus (HPV) vaccine protects against strains that cause cervical cancer. It is ideally administered before sexual debut. BCG is for TB, Hepatitis B for liver infections, and MMR for childhood viral illnesses.

13.
Which type of immunity is achieved through administration of immunoglobulins?
A. Active artificial
B. Passive natural
C. Passive artificial
D. Active natural
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Passive artificial immunity is provided by injecting antibodies, offering immediate but short-lived protection. It’s useful in post-exposure prophylaxis (e.g., rabies). Active immunity requires antigen exposure and is longer-lasting.

14.
Which of the following is the reservoir for rabies virus?
A. Mosquito
B. Dog
C. Bat
D. Rodent
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: In many regions, domestic dogs are the primary reservoirs for rabies. Bats can also be reservoirs in some areas, but dogs are most significant in Africa and Asia. Mosquitoes and rodents are not rabies vectors.

15.
What is the first-line treatment for uncomplicated falciparum malaria in endemic areas?
A. Quinine sulfate
B. Artesunate monotherapy
C. Artemisinin-based combination therapy (ACT)
D. Chloroquine
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: ACT is recommended by WHO as first-line treatment for Plasmodium falciparum malaria due to rising resistance to older drugs. Monotherapy increases risk of resistance. Chloroquine is ineffective in most regions due to resistance.

16.
What is the major route of HIV transmission in children under five years of age?
A. Sexual abuse
B. Blood transfusion
C. Mother-to-child transmission
D. Shared toys
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Vertical transmission—from mother to child during pregnancy, delivery, or breastfeeding—is the most common mode in young children. Prevention includes antiretrovirals during pregnancy and safe feeding practices.

17.
A nurse suspects meningococcal meningitis in a teenager. What is the priority action?
A. Begin oral antibiotics
B. Isolate and report to public health authorities
C. Schedule a lumbar puncture
D. Check for neck stiffness
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Meningococcal meningitis is highly contagious and notifiable. Immediate isolation and notification enable rapid outbreak response. Clinical confirmation and treatment follow, but public health action takes priority.

18.
Which of the following diseases can be prevented by the pentavalent vaccine?
A. Pneumococcal pneumonia
B. Pertussis
C. Measles
D. Mumps
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: The pentavalent vaccine protects against diphtheria, pertussis (whooping cough), tetanus, hepatitis B, and Haemophilus influenzae type B. Measles and mumps are prevented by MMR, and pneumococcus by a separate vaccine.

19.
Which characteristic lesion is associated with cutaneous leishmaniasis?
A. Painful vesicles
B. Dry, scaly rash
C. Painless ulcer with raised edges
D. Crusting pustule
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Cutaneous leishmaniasis presents with a painless ulcer that has raised, indurated edges and may heal slowly. Proper identification avoids misdiagnosis and incorrect antibiotic use for presumed bacterial infections.

20.
The common sign of helminthic infestation in children is:
A. Productive cough
B. Perianal itching
C. Vomiting
D. Macular rash
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Perianal itching is a classic symptom of pinworm (Enterobius) infection, especially in children. Other helminths may present with anemia, abdominal pain, or malnutrition, but itching is most specific and common.

21.
Which of the following best describes herd immunity?
A. Immunity achieved through vaccination of high-risk individuals
B. Protection of an individual through lifelong immunity
C. Resistance of a group when a critical portion is immune
D. Immunity obtained through previous infection only
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Herd immunity occurs when a large portion of a community becomes immune to an infectious disease, reducing its spread. This protects those who cannot be vaccinated. It’s achieved through vaccination or prior infection and is a crucial public health strategy in controlling outbreaks.

22.
Which condition must be met for a disease to be declared eradicated globally?
A. No local cases reported in five years
B. Elimination from the human population with no reservoir
C. Disease no longer causing public health concern
D. Vaccine developed and distributed globally
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Global eradication means the disease is completely removed from all human populations and has no animal or environmental reservoir. Smallpox is an example. Simply having no local cases or having a vaccine does not equate to eradication.

23.
What is the primary purpose of contact tracing during a communicable disease outbreak?
A. To punish carriers of disease
B. To isolate confirmed positive cases only
C. To identify, test, and manage people at risk of infection
D. To educate the public on hygiene practices
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Contact tracing involves identifying individuals exposed to a contagious disease to test, treat, or isolate them, thereby interrupting disease transmission. It is a critical epidemiological tool, especially during outbreaks like Ebola or COVID-19.

24.
Which symptom most differentiates yellow fever from other febrile illnesses in its toxic phase?
A. High fever
B. Jaundice and bleeding
C. Body aches
D. Headache
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: In the toxic phase, yellow fever presents with jaundice and bleeding due to liver failure and coagulopathy, distinguishing it from other febrile illnesses. Fever and headache are nonspecific and common to many infections.

25.
Which of the following is the most sensitive test for early detection of HIV infection?
A. Rapid antibody test
B. CD4 count
C. HIV p24 antigen test
D. ELISA for IgG
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: The HIV p24 antigen test can detect the virus before antibodies are formed, making it highly sensitive in early infection. Antibody-based tests may yield false negatives during the window period. CD4 count assesses disease progression, not diagnosis.

Section 2: Disease Control & Management

25 Marks
26.
What is the most effective way to prevent neonatal tetanus in endemic regions?
A. Neonatal immunization
B. Antibiotic prophylaxis
C. Clean delivery practices and maternal TT immunization
D. Early umbilical cord clamping
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Neonatal tetanus is best prevented through clean delivery practices and immunizing pregnant women with tetanus toxoid (TT). Neonatal immunization is ineffective due to immature immune systems. Hygiene and maternal antibodies provide effective protection.

27.
Which vector-borne disease presents with a "bull's eye" rash in early stages?
A. Leptospirosis
B. Lyme disease
C. Leishmaniasis
D. Dengue
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Lyme disease, transmitted by ticks, often presents with an erythema migrans rash that resembles a bull’s eye. Recognizing this rash early is key to initiating treatment. Other diseases listed do not have this hallmark feature.

28.
Which of the following diseases is transmitted by the blackfly (Simulium species)?
A. Dengue
B. Onchocerciasis
C. Malaria
D. Yellow fever
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Onchocerciasis (river blindness) is caused by Onchocerca volvulus, transmitted by blackflies. Dengue and yellow fever are transmitted by Aedes mosquitoes, while malaria is spread by Anopheles mosquitoes. Recognizing vectors aids targeted interventions.

29.
Which is the most appropriate intervention during a measles outbreak in a community with low vaccination coverage?
A. Quarantine all unvaccinated children
B. Mass immunization campaign
C. Treat only symptomatic cases
D. Close schools indefinitely
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: A mass immunization campaign raises herd immunity rapidly, curbing outbreak progression. Quarantine alone isn’t sufficient if many remain susceptible. Treatment doesn’t prevent further spread, and prolonged school closure has wide socio-economic impacts.

30.
Which environmental factor most promotes the breeding of Culex mosquitoes?
A. Clean stagnant water
B. Flowing river water
C. Polluted water with organic matter
D. Dry sand and soil
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Culex mosquitoes breed in stagnant, polluted water like sewage and drains. In contrast, Anopheles prefers clean water, and Aedes often breeds in clean water in artificial containers. Identifying breeding sites is crucial for vector control.

31.
What is the best indicator to assess the effectiveness of a tuberculosis control program?
A. Number of BCG vaccines administered
B. Decline in smear-positive TB cases
C. Increase in latent TB detection
D. TB knowledge in the community
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: A reduction in smear-positive TB cases indicates effective detection and treatment of the most infectious patients, reflecting program success. Vaccination numbers and knowledge levels support prevention but do not directly reflect control of transmission.

32.
Which of the following is an early clinical sign of leptospirosis?
A. Hematuria
B. Conjunctival suffusion
C. Hepatomegaly
D. Skin ulceration
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Conjunctival suffusion (redness without exudate) is a distinctive early feature of leptospirosis. It helps differentiate from other febrile illnesses. Hematuria and hepatomegaly occur later, while skin ulceration is not a feature.

33.
Which arboviral disease can lead to hemorrhagic fever and shock, particularly in secondary infections?
A. Japanese encephalitis
B. Chikungunya
C. Dengue
D. Yellow fever
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Dengue virus, especially during a secondary infection with a different serotype, can trigger Dengue Hemorrhagic Fever (DHF) and Dengue Shock Syndrome (DSS). Early recognition and fluid management are lifesaving. Other arboviruses rarely cause such complications.

34.
In the context of communicable disease control, a "case definition" helps to:
A. Restrict public panic
B. Enable laboratory confirmation only
C. Identify and classify disease cases consistently
D. Guide health education efforts only
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: A case definition ensures uniform criteria for diagnosing and reporting cases during an outbreak. It includes clinical, epidemiological, and sometimes laboratory components. It is crucial for surveillance and outbreak management.

35.
Which statement best explains why lymphatic filariasis causes elephantiasis?
A. Parasites destroy capillaries
B. Chronic inflammation blocks lymphatic drainage
C. Immune response causes hyperplasia
D. Allergic reaction to parasite eggs
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Adult worms live in lymphatic vessels, causing chronic inflammation and fibrosis, which leads to lymphatic obstruction and severe swelling (elephantiasis). This is a progressive process requiring long-term management and vector control.

36.
In plague transmission, which of the following is the primary vector?
A. Flea
B. Tick
C. Louse
D. Mosquito
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: A

Rationale: Plague is primarily transmitted by fleas, especially those on rodents. Ticks and lice transmit other diseases like typhus or relapsing fever. Correct identification supports rodent and flea control strategies in affected areas.

37.
Why is cholera often described as a "disease of poverty"?
A. It affects only undernourished children
B. It thrives in poor sanitation and limited clean water
C. It spreads only in war zones
D. It is genetic and affects certain populations only
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Cholera outbreaks are most common in areas lacking clean water and sanitation infrastructure, making it closely linked with poverty. The bacteria spread through fecal contamination, often during emergencies or in informal settlements.

38.
Which factor contributes most to antimicrobial resistance in tuberculosis?
A. High bacterial load
B. Delayed onset of symptoms
C. Poor drug adherence and inappropriate prescribing
D. HIV co-infection
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Multidrug-resistant TB (MDR-TB) commonly arises from poor adherence, incorrect drug regimens, or interrupted treatment. HIV increases susceptibility, but resistance results primarily from mismanagement of antibiotics.

39.
A patient with high fever, lymphadenopathy, and an eschar at the site of a mite bite likely has:
A. Scrub typhus
B. Leptospirosis
C. Malaria
D. Filariasis
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: A

Rationale: Scrub typhus, caused by Orientia tsutsugamushi, presents with fever, enlarged lymph nodes, and a characteristic black eschar at the bite site. It is transmitted by chigger mites. Diagnosis is clinical and epidemiological in endemic areas.

40.
Which of the following statements best explains the difficulty in controlling zoonotic diseases?
A. They are all untreatable in animals
B. They do not cause symptoms in humans
C. They have animal reservoirs that are hard to monitor
D. They are all transmitted by vectors
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Zoonotic diseases persist in wild or domestic animal populations, making surveillance and control challenging. Reservoir species may be asymptomatic and widespread. Control requires a One Health approach involving veterinary and environmental sectors.

41.
In community surveillance, which data source is most reliable for identifying an emerging communicable disease outbreak?
A. Media reports
B. Hospital emergency logs
C. Traditional healers’ reports
D. Weekly immunization reports
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Hospital emergency logs provide timely and structured data on unusual illness patterns. Early warning signs can be identified from these trends, enabling faster outbreak detection than routine or anecdotal sources.

42.
Which test confirms active hepatitis B infection?
A. Anti-HBs
B. Anti-HBc
C. HBsAg
D. HBeAb
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: The presence of Hepatitis B surface antigen (HBsAg) indicates active infection. Anti-HBs suggests immunity, anti-HBc indicates past or ongoing exposure, and HBeAb shows lower infectivity. Proper interpretation guides public health response.

43.
What is the best approach to prevent malaria re-introduction in an area declared malaria-free?
A. Discontinue spraying programs
B. Restrict entry of people with fever
C. Maintain vector surveillance and prompt treatment of imported cases
D. Stop malaria education programs
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Sustained surveillance and quick response to imported cases prevent malaria resurgence. Vector control and health education should also continue. Complacency can reverse elimination gains, as imported cases may re-establish transmission.

44.
In polio surveillance, the presence of which condition is most closely monitored?
A. Febrile rash illness
B. Acute flaccid paralysis
C. Chronic cough
D. Neck stiffness
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Acute flaccid paralysis (AFP) in children under 15 is a key indicator for polio surveillance. Even one case warrants investigation. AFP monitoring helps detect silent circulation of wild or vaccine-derived poliovirus.

45.
Why is syphilis considered a "great imitator"?
A. It mimics many other diseases in its clinical manifestations
B. It causes painless ulcers
C. It affects only immunocompromised individuals
D. It is confined to skin manifestations
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: A

Rationale: Syphilis can mimic various conditions at different stages—rashes, neurologic symptoms, cardiovascular disease—making it a diagnostic challenge. Recognition requires high clinical suspicion, especially in high-risk populations.

46.
Which of the following indicates a successful malaria vector control program?
A. Fewer confirmed dengue cases
B. Reduced indoor mosquito density and parasite prevalence
C. Increased use of anti-malarials
D. More cases of asymptomatic malaria
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Effective vector control leads to reduced mosquito populations and fewer people carrying malaria parasites. Increased drug use or asymptomatic cases may indicate ongoing transmission. Surveillance confirms progress.

47.
Which strategy best supports integrated vector management?
A. Exclusive use of insecticide sprays
B. Community-led waste management and environmental control
C. Centralized fogging by health authorities
D. Frequent larvicide application alone
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Integrated vector management combines biological, environmental, and chemical tools, with community participation at the core. Sole reliance on insecticides can breed resistance and is not sustainable.

48.
Which intervention is crucial during the incubation period of a communicable disease?
A. Case treatment
B. Chemoprophylaxis for contacts
C. Case isolation
D. Vaccination of already sick individuals
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Chemoprophylaxis during incubation (e.g., meningitis, malaria) helps prevent disease development in exposed individuals. Treating cases or isolating after symptoms appear doesn’t affect incubation. Vaccinating sick individuals offers little immediate benefit.

49.
Which of the following diseases is most associated with periodic fevers and chills every 48 hours?
A. Dengue
B. Malaria (P. vivax)
C. Typhoid
D. Filariasis
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Plasmodium vivax causes tertian malaria, with fevers every 48 hours due to synchronized red cell rupture. Typhoid and dengue have continuous fevers. Recognizing periodicity aids accurate diagnosis.

50.
What is the purpose of the DOTS strategy in TB control?
A. Prevent TB in exposed individuals
B. Provide short-course treatment in hospitals
C. Ensure adherence through supervised treatment
D. Treat latent TB infection only
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Directly Observed Treatment, Short-course (DOTS) ensures TB patients complete their therapy under supervision, reducing default rates and resistance. It's a cornerstone of global TB control, especially in low-resource settings.
