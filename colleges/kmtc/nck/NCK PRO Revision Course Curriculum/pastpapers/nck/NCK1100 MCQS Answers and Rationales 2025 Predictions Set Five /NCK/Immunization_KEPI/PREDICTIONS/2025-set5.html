KEPI & Immunization

Kenya Expanded Programme on Immunization - Paper 2 Revision

Instructions

  • This paper consists of 50 Multiple Choice Questions focusing on KEPI schedules, vaccine storage, and administration.
  • Each question has four options; select the most appropriate one.
  • Detailed rationales are provided for each answer to facilitate effective revision.
  • Total Marks: 50 | Time Allowed: 60 Minutes.

Section 1: Vaccine Schedules and Administration

25 Marks
1.
What is the primary goal of KEPI?
A) To provide nutrition supplements to children
B) To ensure universal access to vaccines for preventable diseases
C) To treat common childhood infections
D) To provide antibiotics for infectious diseases
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: KEPI aims to reduce morbidity and mortality from vaccine-preventable diseases by providing universal immunization coverage, especially among children.

2.
Which vaccine is given at birth as part of the KEPI schedule?
A) Oral Polio Vaccine (OPV)
B) BCG vaccine
C) Measles vaccine
D) DPT vaccine
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: The Bacillus Calmette-Guérin (BCG) vaccine is administered at birth to protect against tuberculosis, especially severe forms in children.

3.
How many doses of Oral Polio Vaccine (OPV) are given during the KEPI schedule?
A) One
B) Two
C) Three
D) Four
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: D

Rationale: OPV is administered at birth, 6 weeks, 10 weeks, and 14 weeks, totaling four doses as per the KEPI schedule.

4.
Which of the following vaccines is administered at 9 months of age in KEPI?
A) Measles vaccine
B) DPT vaccine
C) BCG vaccine
D) Hepatitis B vaccine
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: A

Rationale: The measles vaccine is given at 9 months to provide immunity before the child reaches an age of high exposure risk.

5.
Which vaccine protects against diphtheria, pertussis, and tetanus?
A) BCG
B) DPT
C) OPV
D) Hepatitis B
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: The DPT vaccine targets diphtheria, pertussis (whooping cough), and tetanus, three serious bacterial infections.

6.
What is the route of administration for the BCG vaccine?
A) Oral
B) Intramuscular
C) Subcutaneous
D) Intradermal
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: D

Rationale: BCG vaccine is given intradermally, which means into the skin’s superficial layer to stimulate an immune response.

7.
Which vaccine is NOT part of the traditional KEPI schedule?
A) Measles
B) BCG
C) Rotavirus
D) DPT
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: While Rotavirus vaccine has been introduced in some immunization programs, it is not part of the original traditional KEPI schedule.

8.
At what age is the first dose of DPT vaccine given in the KEPI schedule?
A) At birth
B) 6 weeks
C) 9 months
D) 12 months
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: The first dose of DPT is given at 6 weeks, followed by doses at 10 and 14 weeks.

9.
What is herd immunity?
A) Immunity achieved only by vaccinated individuals
B) Protection of the entire community when a high proportion is immune
C) Immunity transferred from mother to child
D) Immunity that lasts a lifetime
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Herd immunity occurs when enough people in a population are immune to a disease, reducing its spread and protecting those who are not immune.

10.
Which vaccine is given orally in KEPI?
A) BCG
B) Measles
C) OPV
D) DPT
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: The Oral Polio Vaccine (OPV) is administered by mouth to induce immunity against poliovirus.

11.
Which disease does the BCG vaccine primarily protect against?
A) Measles
B) Tuberculosis
C) Tetanus
D) Poliomyelitis
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: BCG vaccine protects mainly against tuberculosis, particularly severe forms in children.

12.
Why is the measles vaccine given at 9 months rather than earlier?
A) Because maternal antibodies interfere before 9 months
B) To coincide with DPT vaccine
C) Due to availability of the vaccine
D) Because it requires oral administration
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: A

Rationale: Maternal antibodies present in infants under 9 months can neutralize the measles vaccine, so it is given at 9 months when immunity develops better.

13.
What type of vaccine is the DPT vaccine?
A) Live attenuated
B) Inactivated (killed)
C) Toxoid
D) Subunit
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: The DPT vaccine contains toxoids for diphtheria and tetanus and killed whole cells for pertussis; the diphtheria and tetanus components are toxoids (inactivated toxins).

14.
Which of the following is NOT a vaccine-preventable disease covered under KEPI?
A) Measles
B) Polio
C) Malaria
D) Tuberculosis
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Malaria is not vaccine-preventable by the vaccines in KEPI; prevention involves other measures like insecticide-treated nets.

15.
How many doses of the measles vaccine are recommended in the expanded schedule?
A) One
B) Two
C) Three
D) Four
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Two doses of measles vaccine are recommended to ensure long-lasting immunity, usually at 9 months and at 15 months.

16.
Which of these vaccines is typically given intramuscularly?
A) OPV
B) BCG
C) DPT
D) Measles
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: The DPT vaccine is administered intramuscularly, commonly in the thigh or deltoid muscle.

17.
What is a cold chain in immunization programs?
A) A chain of vaccination centers
B) A system to keep vaccines at recommended temperatures during storage and transport
C) The process of freezing vaccines
D) The order of vaccine administration
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: The cold chain is critical to maintain vaccine potency by ensuring they remain within recommended temperature ranges from manufacture to administration.

18.
Which vaccine in KEPI protects against hepatitis B?
A) BCG
B) Hepatitis B vaccine
C) OPV
D) Measles vaccine
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: The hepatitis B vaccine prevents hepatitis B virus infection and its complications like liver cirrhosis and cancer.

19.
What is the recommended spacing between the three doses of DPT vaccine?
A) 1 week
B) 4 weeks
C) 3 months
D) 6 months
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: The three doses of DPT vaccine are typically spaced 4 weeks apart to ensure effective immunity development.

20.
Which of the following is a contraindication to administering live vaccines?
A) Mild fever
B) Severe immunosuppression
C) Common cold
D) Allergic rhinitis
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Live vaccines are contraindicated in severely immunosuppressed individuals as they may cause infection due to weakened immunity.

21.
What is the primary method to monitor the success of immunization programs like KEPI?
A) Disease incidence rates
B) Number of vaccines delivered
C) Parental satisfaction
D) Hospital bed occupancy
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: A

Rationale: Monitoring disease incidence rates allows assessment of vaccine effectiveness and overall program success.

22.
Which of the following is a common side effect of vaccination?
A) Severe anaphylaxis in all recipients
B) Mild fever and swelling at injection site
C) Development of the disease being vaccinated against
D) No side effects at all
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Mild fever and localized swelling or pain are common side effects; severe reactions are rare.

23.
What is the importance of the “drop-out rate” in immunization programs?
A) Percentage of children who receive the first vaccine dose but not the subsequent doses
B) Number of children who miss school
C) Percentage of vaccines wasted
D) Number of healthcare workers quitting
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: A

Rationale: The drop-out rate indicates children starting but not completing vaccination, a key factor in program effectiveness.

24.
Which of these is a benefit of immunization?
A) Increased disease outbreaks
B) Protection against vaccine-preventable diseases
C) Increased antibiotic resistance
D) Decreased life expectancy
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Immunization protects individuals and communities against infectious diseases, reducing illness and mortality.

25.
What does “vaccine efficacy” refer to?
A) How easy the vaccine is to administer
B) The percentage reduction of disease in vaccinated individuals compared to unvaccinated
C) Cost-effectiveness of vaccine programs
D) Number of doses required
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Vaccine efficacy measures how well a vaccine prevents disease under controlled clinical trial conditions.

Section 2: Clinical Practice & Storage

25 Marks
26.
Which vaccine prevents tetanus?
A) OPV
B) Hepatitis B
C) DPT
D) BCG
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: The DPT vaccine includes tetanus toxoid to prevent tetanus infection.

27.
Which of these is a strategy to improve immunization coverage in Kenya?
A) Reducing the number of vaccines
B) Outreach and mobile clinics in remote areas
C) Increasing vaccine cost
D) Limiting vaccination to hospitals only
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Outreach and mobile clinics help reach underserved populations, increasing vaccine coverage.

28.
Which of the following is NOT a characteristic of live attenuated vaccines?
A) They contain weakened forms of the pathogen
B) They usually provide long-lasting immunity
C) They require multiple doses every year
D) They may be contraindicated in immunocompromised patients
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Live attenuated vaccines generally provide long-lasting immunity after one or two doses, not requiring yearly boosters like some inactivated vaccines.

29.
Which vaccine is given intradermally in KEPI?
A) Measles
B) BCG
C) DPT
D) Hepatitis B
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: BCG is administered intradermally.

30.
How can vaccine-preventable diseases be eradicated?
A) By vaccinating a majority of the population and maintaining high coverage
B) By treating all cases with antibiotics
C) By reducing vaccination frequency
D) By focusing only on symptomatic patients
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: A

Rationale: Eradication requires high vaccine coverage to interrupt disease transmission.

31.
What does “cold chain break” mean?
A) When vaccines are administered incorrectly
B) When vaccines are exposed to temperatures outside recommended ranges
C) When the immunization schedule is missed
D) When healthcare workers refuse vaccination
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Cold chain breaks compromise vaccine potency due to improper temperature control.

32.
Which vaccine is recommended for pregnant women to protect newborns against neonatal tetanus?
A) BCG
B) Tetanus toxoid
C) Measles
D) OPV
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Tetanus toxoid vaccination in pregnancy prevents neonatal tetanus by transferring maternal antibodies.

33.
What is the purpose of vaccine adjuvants?
A) To weaken the pathogen
B) To enhance immune response to the vaccine
C) To preserve the vaccine
D) To reduce side effects
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Adjuvants boost the body's immune response to the antigen in vaccines.

34.
Which of these vaccines requires booster doses in later childhood?
A) Measles
B) OPV
C) DPT
D) BCG
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: The DPT vaccine requires booster doses after the primary series to maintain immunity.

35.
What is a “vaccine schedule”?
A) List of vaccines administered during outbreaks
B) Timetable specifying timing and sequence of vaccine doses
C) List of side effects of vaccines
D) A report of vaccine coverage
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: A vaccine schedule guides timing and intervals of vaccine administration for optimal immunity.

36.
Which vaccine is contraindicated in infants with severe allergic reactions to eggs?
A) Measles
B) OPV
C) Influenza (not part of KEPI but relevant)
D) BCG
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Influenza vaccines grown in eggs can trigger allergic reactions in egg-sensitive individuals.

37.
How is immunization status verified in children?
A) Parental recall only
B) Immunization card or health booklet
C) Doctor’s memory
D) School attendance record
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Immunization cards provide a reliable record of vaccines received.

38.
What is the typical cause of vaccine failure?
A) Incorrect vaccine storage
B) Incomplete immunization schedule
C) Poor vaccine administration technique
D) All of the above
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: D

Rationale: Vaccine failure can result from multiple factors including storage, schedule adherence, and administration.

39.
What disease does OPV protect against?
A) Measles
B) Polio
C) Tuberculosis
D) Hepatitis B
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: OPV prevents poliomyelitis by inducing immunity to poliovirus.

40.
What is the common site of injection for infant DPT vaccine?
A) Deltoid muscle
B) Gluteal muscle
C) Anterolateral thigh
D) Subcutaneous tissue
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: The anterolateral thigh is the preferred site for intramuscular injections in infants.

41.
Which of the following vaccines is known to cause a "scar" at the injection site?
A) Measles
B) BCG
C) DPT
D) OPV
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: BCG vaccination typically leaves a small scar, considered a sign of successful vaccination.

42.
What is the major reason for incomplete immunization in children?
A) Vaccine shortage
B) Parental refusal or lack of awareness
C) Lack of healthcare workers
D) Disease outbreak
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Parental refusal, lack of knowledge, or access issues are major barriers to full immunization.

43.
How should vaccines be stored during transport in KEPI?
A) At room temperature
B) In insulated containers with ice packs maintaining 2-8°C
C) Frozen
D) Direct sunlight
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Vaccines must be kept cold but not frozen, typically between 2-8°C during transport.

44.
Which vaccine prevents Haemophilus influenzae type b (Hib) disease?
A) OPV
B) Hib vaccine
C) DPT
D) Measles
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: The Hib vaccine protects against infections caused by Haemophilus influenzae type b bacteria.

45.
Why is immunization important in controlling infectious diseases?
A) It eradicates all diseases immediately
B) It reduces disease transmission and severity
C) It increases antibiotic use
D) It replaces hygiene practices
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: Immunization reduces transmission and severity of infections, contributing to disease control.

46.
When is the third dose of the hepatitis B vaccine given in KEPI?
A) At birth
B) 6 weeks
C) 10 weeks
D) 14 weeks
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: D

Rationale: The third dose is given at 14 weeks to complete the hepatitis B vaccination series.

47.
Which statement about vaccine side effects is true?
A) Severe side effects occur in most vaccinated children
B) Side effects are always life-threatening
C) Most side effects are mild and temporary
D) No side effects ever occur
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: Most vaccine side effects are mild (e.g., fever, soreness) and temporary, serious effects are rare.

48.
Which group is most prioritized in KEPI?
A) Elderly people
B) School-age children
C) Infants and young children
D) Pregnant women only
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: KEPI primarily targets infants and young children due to their vulnerability to vaccine-preventable diseases.

49.
Which of the following vaccines is given at birth to prevent perinatal infection?
A) Measles
B) OPV
C) Hepatitis B vaccine
D) DPT
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: C

Rationale: The hepatitis B vaccine given at birth prevents transmission from mother to child during birth.

50.
What is the purpose of the second dose of the measles vaccine?
A) To prevent side effects from the first dose
B) To boost immunity in case of primary vaccine failure
C) To protect against mumps
D) To replace the BCG vaccine
View Answer & Rationale

Correct Answer: B

Rationale: The second dose ensures protection in children who did not respond to the first dose or whose immunity waned.
