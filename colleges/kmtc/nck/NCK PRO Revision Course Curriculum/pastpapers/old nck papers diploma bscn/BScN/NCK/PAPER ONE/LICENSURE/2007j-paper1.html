Part I: Multiple Choice Questions

20 Marks
1.
The main function of the thymus gland is the:
a) Maturation and differentiation of T-cells
b) Production and proliferation of B-cells
c) Stimulation and dissemination of red blood cells
d) Stimulation and production of thyroid hormone
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Maturation and differentiation of T-cells

Explanation: The thymus gland is a primary lymphoid organ where T-lymphocytes (T-cells) mature and differentiate. B-cells mature in the bone marrow.

2.
Corneal infections are difficult to treat due to the fact that:
a) The surface area of the cornea is small
b) Causative organisms cannot be recovered
c) Corneal infections are not easily diagnosed
d) The cornea is an avascular organ
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) The cornea is an avascular organ

Explanation: The cornea lacks blood vessels (avascular). This makes it difficult for the body's immune defenses (white blood cells) and systemic antibiotics to reach the site of infection effectively.

3.
When monitoring for infection in an elderly client, a nurse would expect:
a) More severe signs of infection
b) Shaking, chills but no fever
c) Changes in mental status
d) Fever shivering and restlessness
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Changes in mental status

Explanation: In the elderly, classic signs of infection like fever and leukocytosis may be absent or blunted due to an aging immune system. Acute confusion (delirium) or changes in mental status are often the first and only sign of infection (e.g., UTI or pneumonia).

4.
Toxoplasmosis in a newborn is mostly acquired through:
a) Contact with maternal genito-urinary secretions
b) Nosocomial transfer from the environment at birth
c) The placenta before birth
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) The placenta before birth

Explanation: Congenital toxoplasmosis is acquired transplacentally (vertical transmission) when the mother acquires the infection during pregnancy.

5.
When caring for a client in pain, it is important for the nurse to keep in mind that:
a) Many patients experience exaggerated pain
b) Pain is a subjective, personal experience
c) Pain can be eliminated with the use of drugs only
d) Pain always indicates current or impending tissue damage
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Pain is a subjective, personal experience

Explanation: The golden rule of pain management is that pain is whatever the patient says it is. It is a subjective experience and cannot be objectively measured like vital signs.

6.
The first appropriate nursing intervention to a newly admitted client with urine specific gravity of 1.040 is to:
a) Restrict oral fluids
b) Encourage liquid diet
c) Administer IV fluids
d) Continue with normal prescribed care
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Administer IV fluids

Explanation: Normal urine specific gravity is 1.005 to 1.030. A value of 1.040 indicates highly concentrated urine, suggesting significant dehydration. For a newly admitted client with this level of concentration, prompt rehydration is needed. Depending on the patient's ability to swallow, oral fluids or IV fluids would be indicated. In an admission context with high SG, administering IV fluids is often a primary medical/nursing intervention to restore volume quickly.

7.
The appropriate nursing intervention for a client with a diagnosis of ineffective airway clearance is:
a) Withholding pain medication
b) Administering cough suppressants every 4-6 hours
c) Administering antibiotics as prescribed
d) Maintaining adequate hydration and providing increased humidity
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Maintaining adequate hydration and providing increased humidity

Explanation: Adequate hydration helps thin mucous secretions, making them easier to expectorate. Humidity also loosens secretions. Cough suppressants would be contraindicated as they prevent the clearing of the airway.

8.
Narcotic analgesics are administered continuously (cautiously) to individuals following brain surgery because:
a) Brain surgery does not result in much pain and discomfort
b) Sedation may result in paradoxical increase in intra-cranial pressure
c) Papillary responses may be impaired and respirations depressed
d) Psychological dependence may occur more quickly following surgery
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Papillary responses may be impaired and respirations depressed

Explanation: Narcotic analgesics (opioids) cause pupillary constriction (miosis) and respiratory depression (which increases CO2 and thus ICP). These effects can mask the neurological signs (pupil changes, LOC changes) used to monitor patients after brain surgery. Therefore, they are used with extreme caution or specific short-acting agents are chosen.

9.
A client's symptom indicative of a complication associated with acute otitis media is:
a) Mild fever
b) Abrupt relief of pain
c) Tinnitus
d) Feeling of fullness in the ear
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Abrupt relief of pain

Explanation: In acute otitis media, pressure builds up behind the tympanic membrane causing severe pain. Abrupt relief of this pain typically indicates spontaneous rupture (perforation) of the tympanic membrane, which is a complication.

10.
In screening for HIV, positive Elisa test result is followed by:
a) CD4 cell count
b) Blood culture of HIV
c) Western blot
d) A polymerase chain reaction (CPR)
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Western blot

Explanation: Historically, the ELISA was the screening test, and a positive result was confirmed with a more specific test, the Western Blot. (Note: Modern algorithms may use differentiation assays, but in the context of this 2007 paper, Western Blot is the standard confirmatory test).

11.
In herpes Zoster:
a) There is a high risk of secondary bacterial infection
b) Vaccination is the most appropriate preventive approach
c) The clients are contagious to individuals with chicken pox
d) Contact isolation is appropriate for immuno-compromised clients
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) The clients are contagious to individuals with chicken pox (likely meant "susceptible to chicken pox")

Explanation: Herpes Zoster (Shingles) is caused by the reactivation of the Varicella-Zoster virus. A person with active shingles can transmit the virus to a person who has never had chickenpox (or the vaccine), causing them to develop chickenpox (not shingles directly).

12.
Pre-operative care of a client with ruptured appendix includes:
a) A diet of clear fluids
b) An enema to prepare bowel for surgery
c) Administering intravenous antibiotics
d) Applying a warm bath on the abdomen
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Administering intravenous antibiotics

Explanation: A ruptured appendix causes peritonitis. Immediate management involves making the patient NPO (no diet), starting IV fluids and IV antibiotics to fight infection. Enemas (b) and heat application (d) are contraindicated as they can increase peristalsis/circulation and spread infection.

13.
The immunological reaction to chemicals in cosmetic or poison Ivy is categorized as:
a) Allergic/anaphylactic reaction
b) Cytotoxic reaction
c) Immuno complex mediated reaction
d) Delayed type hypersensitivity
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Delayed type hypersensitivity

Explanation: Contact dermatitis (like from Poison Ivy) is a classic Type IV (Delayed) Hypersensitivity reaction, mediated by T-cells.

14.
Witnessing an informed consent form attests to the fact that the nurse:
a) Saw the correct person sign the form and agrees that the patient was alert and aware of what was being signed
b) Verifies that all the patient's questions were answered
c) Believes that the patient's participation in decision making was voluntary
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Believes that the patient's participation in decision making was voluntary (and authentic)

Explanation: The nurse's signature primarily witnesses that the client signed the form voluntarily, the signature is authentic, and the client appears competent. While (a) is also largely correct, the core legal witnessing role is establishing authenticity and volition.

15.
The finding that is characteristic of a client with Guillain-Barre syndrome is:
a) Symmetric paralysis
b) Tachycardia
c) Increased peristalsis
d) Amnesia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Symmetric paralysis

Explanation: Guillain-Barre is characterized by acute, ascending, symmetric weakness or paralysis. It typically starts in the legs and moves upward.

16.
The increased advocacy for non-antibiotic management of infantile diarrhea is a result of scientific evidence that:
a) Most infections are related to gestational irritation
b) Viral agents are a leading cause of those infections
c) Teething problems are associated the diarrhea
d) Contamination of food in preparation and handling results in the infection
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Viral agents are a leading cause of those infections

Explanation: Most cases of acute diarrhea in children are caused by viruses (like Rotavirus), against which antibiotics are ineffective. Overuse of antibiotics leads to resistance.

17.
The emergence of Multidrug Resistance Tuberculosis (MDR-TB) cases is a result of:
a) Unscrupulous use and high cost of acquiring anti TB drugs
b) Limited knowledge on proper nutrition by the patients
c) Non adherence to stipulated treatment by the clients
d) Mutation of the tubercle bacilli
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Non adherence to stipulated treatment by the clients

Explanation: Poor adherence to the long course of TB treatment (interruption, incomplete doses) is the primary driver for the development of drug-resistant strains.

18.
The most common and treatable type of tissue rejection occurs:
a) Between 2-3 days after transplant
b) Between 4 days and 3 months after transplant
c) Between 4 months and 1 year
d) 5 years after transplant operation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Between 4 days and 3 months after transplant

Explanation: Acute rejection typically occurs from the first week up to 3 months post-transplant. It is mediated by T-cells and is the most common type, but it is also the most treatable with immunosuppressive therapy.

19.
Deficiency of folic acid in the first trimester of pregnancy is associated with the development of:
a) Phocomelia
b) Spina bifida
c) Cleft lip and palate
d) Hydrocephalus
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Spina bifida

Explanation: Folic acid is essential for neural tube closure, which happens in the first 28 days of pregnancy. Deficiency leads to Neural Tube Defects (NTDs) like Spina Bifida and Anencephaly.

20.
The care of a patient with a pressure ulcer includes:
a) Providing a cardiac bed
b) Massaging reddened areas of patientâ€™s skin
c) Use of a pull sheet when moving client up in bed
d) Having the patient sit on a donnut type of device
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Use of a pull sheet when moving client up in bed

Explanation: Using a pull sheet (draw sheet) prevents shearing forces and friction that damage the skin when moving a patient. Massaging reddened areas (b) is contraindicated as it damages capillaries. Donut devices (d) are contraindicated as they create venous congestion and ischemia.

Part II: Short Answer Questions

1.
Explain two predisposing factors to nosocomial infection (4 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Immunocompromised Status: Patients with weakened immune systems due to disease (HIV, diabetes, cancer) or treatment (chemotherapy, steroids) are more susceptible to infections.
  • Invasive Procedures: Use of invasive devices such as urinary catheters, IV cannulas, central lines, and ventilators breach natural barriers, providing a direct route for pathogens to enter the body.
  • Antibiotic Misuse: Overuse or improper use of antibiotics leads to the development of resistant bacteria (e.g., MRSA), which are harder to treat.
  • Poor Infection Control Practices: Lack of proper hand hygiene by healthcare workers facilitates cross-contamination between patients.
2.
Explain three factors that influence an individual's health status (6 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Biological/Genetic Factors: Age, sex, and inherited genetic traits predispose individuals to certain conditions (e.g., sickle cell, breast cancer).
  2. Environmental Factors: Physical environment (clean water, air quality, housing) and social environment (stress, community support) directly impact health.
  3. Lifestyle/Behavioral Factors: Personal choices such as diet, physical activity, smoking, and alcohol consumption are major determinants of health.
  4. Socioeconomic Factors: Income, education, and occupation affect access to resources, nutrition, and healthcare services.

4.
Explain three factors that influence glomerular filtration (6 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Renal Blood Flow: Adequate blood flow to the kidneys is essential. A drop in systemic blood pressure (hypotension) reduces renal perfusion and thus decreases GFR.
  2. Glomerular Capillary Hydrostatic Pressure: This is the main driving force for filtration. It is maintained by the relative constriction of the efferent arteriole versus the afferent arteriole.
  3. Colloid Osmotic Pressure: Plasma proteins in the blood oppose filtration. If plasma protein levels are low (hypoalbuminemia), GFR may increase; if high, it decreases.
  4. Surface Area of Filtration Membrane: Disease processes that destroy glomeruli reduce the available surface area, lowering GFR.
5.
Outline the importance of nutrition in HIV/AIDS management (4 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Supports Immune Function: Adequate protein and micronutrients are needed to produce immune cells (CD4) to fight opportunistic infections.
  • Prevents Wasting (Cachexia): HIV increases metabolic demand. Proper nutrition prevents the loss of lean muscle mass and weight loss associated with disease progression.
  • Improves Drug Efficacy/Tolerance: Good nutrition helps the body process ARVs effectively and manage side effects like nausea or diarrhea.
  • Tissue Repair: Nutrients are essential for repairing tissues damaged by infections.
6.
Explain three respiratory complications of mechanical ventilation (6 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Ventilator-Associated Pneumonia (VAP): Bacterial infection of the lungs develops because the endotracheal tube bypasses natural upper airway defenses and allows aspiration of secretions.
  2. Barotrauma (Pneumothorax): High inflation pressures can rupture alveoli, causing air to leak into the pleural space (pneumothorax) or mediastinum.
  3. Volutrauma: Overdistension of alveoli from high tidal volumes causes lung injury and inflammation.
  4. Tracheal Injury: Prolonged pressure from the endotracheal tube cuff can cause tracheal stenosis, erosion, or fistula formation.
7.
Outline the management of inhalation burns (8 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Secure Airway (Immediate): Assess for signs of obstruction (stridor, soot, burns). Early intubation is crucial if there is evidence of impending airway swelling/edema.
  2. Oxygenation: Administer 100% humidified oxygen via non-rebreather mask or ET tube. This is vital to displace carbon monoxide (CO) from hemoglobin.
  3. Respiratory Support: Mechanical ventilation may be needed for respiratory failure or ARDS. Use lung-protective strategies.
  4. Bronchodilators: Administer nebulized bronchodilators to treat bronchospasm.
  5. Pulmonary Hygiene: Frequent suctioning and chest physiotherapy to clear soot and secretions.
  6. Fluid Resuscitation: Initiate Parkland formula, but monitor closely as excessive fluid can worsen pulmonary edema.
  7. Monitoring: Continuous pulse oximetry (though unreliable for CO), ABGs (for Carboxyhemoglobin levels), and chest x-rays.

Part III: Essay Questions

1.
A six year old boy X, is admitted to the hospital with a diagnosis of severe malaria.
a) State five features of severe malaria (5 marks)
b) Describe the management of Master X within the first 72 hours (15 marks)
Show Answer

a) Features of Severe Malaria:

  1. Cerebral Malaria: Unarousable coma or convulsions (seizures).
  2. Severe Anemia: Hb < 5 g/dL.
  3. Respiratory Distress: Acidotic breathing (deep/labored), pulmonary edema.
  4. Hypoglycemia: Blood glucose < 2.2 mmol/L.
  5. Renal Failure: Oliguria or high creatinine.
  6. Severe Jaundice: Evidence of hemolysis and liver dysfunction.

b) Management (First 72 Hours):

1. Immediate Assessment & Resuscitation (ABCs): Ensure patent airway, breathing (give oxygen if distress/hypoxia), and circulation (IV access).

2. Antimalarial Therapy:
- Administer IV Artesunate (2.4mg/kg) at 0, 12, and 24 hours, then daily. This is the drug of choice.
- Alternatively, IV Quinine in dextrose (monitor for hypoglycemia and hypotension).

3. Correct Hypoglycemia: Check random blood sugar immediately. If low, give IV Dextrose 10% bolus (2-5ml/kg).

4. Manage Fever: Tepid sponging and antipyretics (Paracetamol) to prevent febrile convulsions.

5. Manage Convulsions: If seizing, administer Rectal Diazepam or IV Diazepam. Maintain safety.

6. Fluid Management: Rehydrate carefully. Avoid fluid overload (risk of pulmonary edema). Monitor urine output.

7. Manage Anemia: Transfuse packed red cells if Hb is critically low (< 5g/dl) or if there is respiratory distress with anemia.

8. Nursing Care:
- Observation: Monitor GCS, Vital signs (TPR, BP) q4h.
- Nutrition: If unconscious, NG tube feeding; if conscious, encourage oral intake.
- Hygiene: Mouth care, turn q2h to prevent sores.

2.
Mrs. Y has been diagnosed with bleeding peptic ulcers that require surgical intervention.
a) Explain the immediate pre-operative care necessary for Mrs. Y (6 marks)
b) Outline the information contained in intra-operative nurses' notes (6 marks)
c) Describe the post-operative management of Mrs. Y within the first 24 hours (8 marks)
Show Answer

a) Immediate Pre-operative Care:

  1. Stabilization: Resuscitate with IV fluids (crystalloids/colloids) and blood products if Hgb is low to correct hypovolemia from bleeding.
  2. NPO Status: Keep nil per oral to prevent aspiration during anesthesia.
  3. Gastric Decompression: Insert a Nasogastric (NG) tube to lavage the stomach (remove blood clots) and monitor ongoing bleeding.
  4. Consent: Ensure valid informed consent is signed for the specific surgery.
  5. Laboratory: Ensure Type & Cross-match is done, coagulation profile, and CBC are checked.
  6. Pre-medication: Administer prescribed antibiotics or PPIs/H2 blockers.

b) Intra-operative Nurses' Notes:

  • Patient identification and time of arrival in theater.
  • Type of anesthesia used and time started.
  • Positioning of the patient.
  • Skin preparation (antiseptic used).
  • Incision time and type.
  • Details of procedure performed.
  • Sponge, Needle, and Instrument counts: Verification of counts before and after closure.
  • Fluids/Blood products administered and Urine output.
  • Vital signs trends.
  • Drains/Packs inserted.

c) Post-operative Management (First 24 Hours):

  1. Airway & Breathing: Maintain patent airway. Administer Oxygen. Encourage deep breathing/coughing (splinting incision) to prevent atelectasis.
  2. Circulation (Hemorrhage Check): Monitor Vitals q15min then q30min/1h. Watch for tachycardia/hypotension (signs of re-bleeding). Check dressing for soakage.
  3. NG Tube Care: Connect to suction or gravity. Monitor drainage amount and color (expect bloody initially, then turning bilious). Do not manipulate tube if gastric surgery done.
  4. Fluid Balance: Maintain IV fluids until oral intake is permitted. Monitor urine output (>30ml/hr).
  5. Pain Management: Administer analgesics (e.g., Morphine/Pethidine) as prescribed to facilitate movement and breathing.
  6. Wound Care: Check surgical site for bleeding. Reinforce dressing if needed.
  7. DVT Prophylaxis: Early leg exercises and mobilization as tolerated.
