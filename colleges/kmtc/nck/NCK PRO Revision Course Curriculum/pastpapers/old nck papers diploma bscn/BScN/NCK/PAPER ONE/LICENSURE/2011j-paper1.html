NCK Paper One

Licensure Examination - General Nursing

Part I: Multiple Choice Questions

20 Marks
1.
Nursing management of contusion of the eye includes:
a) Encouraging the client to lie supine quietly
b) Applying a warm compressor immediately after injury
c) Patching the eye closed until it can be examined
d) Flushing the eye with copious amounts of water
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Patching the eye closed until it can be examined

Explanation: For a contusion (blunt trauma) to the eye, protection is a priority. Patching reduces eye movement and protects from light and further injury. Ice (cold) compresses are indicated for the first 24 hours to reduce swelling; warm compresses (option b) would increase bleeding and swelling and are contraindicated immediately after injury. Flushing (option d) is for chemical burns. While rest is important, usually the head is elevated (semi-Fowler's) rather than supine (option a) to reduce intraocular pressure and edema.

2.
The preferred position for a client following craniotomy is:
a) High Fowlers position
b) Turned to the operative side
c) Turned to the non-operative side
d) Low Fowlers position
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Turned to the non-operative side

Explanation: After a supratentorial craniotomy, the patient is usually positioned on the non-operative side to prevent pressure on the surgical flap and site. The head of the bed is typically elevated 30 degrees (Low Fowler's/Semi-Fowler's) to promote venous drainage and reduce intracranial pressure (ICP). While Low Fowler's (d) is also correct for head elevation, turning to the non-operative side is a specific precaution for the surgical site.

3.
Laboratory findings that are consistent with hyperthyroidism include:
a) Decreased serum triiodothyronine (T3) and thyroxine (T4) levels
b) Elevated serum thyrotropin releasing hormone (TRH) levels
c) Decreased radioactive iodine uptake
d) Increased free triiodothyronine (T3) and thyroxine (T4) levels
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Increased free triiodothyronine (T3) and thyroxine (T4) levels

Explanation: Hyperthyroidism is characterized by the overproduction of thyroid hormones, leading to elevated serum T3 and T4 levels. TSH levels are typically suppressed (decreased) due to negative feedback. Radioactive iodine uptake is usually increased.

4.
Blood supply to gastrointestinal tract:
a) Originates from the aorta and branches to many arteries
b) Accounts for 10% of the cardiac output
c) Carries absorbed nutrients away from the lumen via the hepatic vein
d) Circulates through the liver by means of the portal vein
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Originates from the aorta and branches to many arteries

Explanation: The arterial blood supply to the GI tract originates from the abdominal aorta via three major branches: the celiac trunk, the superior mesenteric artery, and the inferior mesenteric artery. It accounts for about 20-25% of cardiac output (not 10%). The portal vein carries venous blood (with nutrients) to the liver, not the arterial supply.

5.
Breast tumours that respond best to adjuvant therapy are those that are:
a) Estrogen-receptor positive
b) Estrogen-receptor negative
c) Poorly differentiated
d) Locally infiltrating
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Estrogen-receptor positive

Explanation: Breast tumors that express estrogen receptors (ER+) rely on estrogen for growth. These tumors respond well to hormonal adjuvant therapy (like Tamoxifen or aromatase inhibitors) which blocks estrogen effects. ER-negative tumors do not respond to this therapy.

6.
A patient newly diagnosed with HIV/AIDS who laments, "I can't believe I got this virus. I am going to die and everyone will know it was due to my behaviour. How could i be so stupid?" is in the stage of:
a) Depression
b) Denial
c) Acceptance
d) Bargaining
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Denial

Explanation: The statement "I can't believe I got this" is characteristic of the **Denial** stage of the Kubler-Ross grief cycle ("No, not me, it can't be true"). The self-blame ("How could I be so stupid") also indicates the transition into Anger ("Why me?"), but the initial disbelief is the hallmark of the immediate reaction (Denial).

7.
Substances that create colloid osmotic pressure in the capillaries include:
a) Albumin, globulin
b) Plasma, potassium
c) Globulin, sodium
d) Albumin, potassium
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Albumin, globulin

Explanation: Colloid osmotic pressure (oncotic pressure) is exerted by large molecular weight proteins that cannot pass through the capillary membrane. The primary plasma proteins responsible for this are **Albumin** (which contributes the most) and **Globulins**. Potassium and Sodium are crystalloids and do not create colloid osmotic pressure.

8.
Discharge instructions for a client with chronic respiratory acidosis should include:
a) Discussing how to plan for periods of increased activity
b) Teaching about a low protein, low carbohydrate diet
c) Demonstrating exercises to increase vital capacity
d) Encouraging participation in activities such as jogging
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Demonstrating exercises to increase vital capacity

Explanation: Chronic respiratory acidosis is often caused by COPD where CO2 retention occurs due to poor ventilation. Breathing exercises (like pursed-lip breathing and diaphragmatic breathing) help improve ventilation, increase vital capacity, and facilitate CO2 elimination. Jogging is too strenuous, and a low carbohydrate diet is beneficial (to reduce CO2 production) but usually high fat/adequate protein is recommended, not low protein.

9.
Inhalation anaesthetics are:
a) Muscle-relaxants used to facilitate surgery by paralyzing skeletal muscles
b) Non-opioid agent that produce anaesthesia or sedation
c) Opioid agents that produce unconsciousness during surgery
d) Organic liquids used for induction and or maintenance of general anaesthesia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Organic liquids used for induction and or maintenance of general anaesthesia

Explanation: Volatile inhalation anesthetics (like Sevoflurane, Isoflurane, Halothane) are organic liquids that are vaporized and inhaled to induce or maintain general anesthesia. Muscle relaxants (a) and opioids (c) are distinct classes of drugs.

10.
The predilection of HIV for certain cells in the immune system results in:
a) More T4 lymphocytes and general lymphocytopenia
b) Fewer activated B-cells and hypogammaglobulinemia
c) Increased response in cutaneous hypersensitivity
d) Susceptibility to opportunistic infection and neoplasm
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Susceptibility to opportunistic infection and neoplasm

Explanation: HIV specifically targets and destroys CD4+ T lymphocytes (T4 cells). The depletion of these cells compromises cell-mediated immunity, resulting in susceptibility to opportunistic infections (like PCP, TB) and neoplasms (like Kaposi's sarcoma). It causes *less* T4 cells (not more as in option a).

11.
A client with leukemia who develops tumor lysis syndrome would most likely have:
a) An elevated serum acid phosphate level
b) A decreased serum alkaline phosphate level
c) An increased serum uric acid level
d) A decreased serum calcium level
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) An increased serum uric acid level

Explanation: Tumor Lysis Syndrome involves the rapid breakdown of malignant cells, releasing intracellular contents. Key findings are Hyperuricemia (high uric acid from DNA breakdown), Hyperkalemia, Hyperphosphatemia, and secondary Hypocalcemia (calcium binds to excess phosphate). While decreased calcium (d) is a feature, increased uric acid (c) is a hallmark metabolic disturbance that causes renal failure.

12.
Correct endotracheal (ET) placement is conclusively verified when:
a) Chest excursion is asymmetrical
b) Air emerges from the endotracheal tube
c) Breath sounds are bilaterally equal
d) Breath sounds are auscultated over the epigastrium
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Breath sounds are bilaterally equal

Explanation: Bilateral equal breath sounds indicate that the tube is in the trachea and ventilating both lungs, rather than being in the esophagus (where you'd hear sounds over epigastrium) or right mainstem bronchus (where you'd hear asymmetrical sounds). *Note: Capnography is the gold standard, but among clinical signs listed, bilateral breath sounds is the verification method.*

13.
The most common preventable cause of valvular heart disease is:
a) Congenital disease acquired intrauterine
b) Calcium deposits and thrombus formation
c) Marfans syndrome
d) Beta-hemolytic streptococcal infection
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Beta-hemolytic streptococcal infection

Explanation: Rheumatic Heart Disease, caused by an untreated Group A Beta-hemolytic streptococcal infection (strep throat), is the leading preventable cause of valvular heart disease (especially mitral stenosis) worldwide.

14.
Initial treatment for a client with warm autoimmune hemolytic anaemia includes:
a) Intensive immunosuppressive therapy, radiotherapy
b) High dose corticosteroid therapy, administration of immunoglobulins
c) Transfusion with cryoprecipitate, chemotherapy
d) Radiation, transfusion with whole blood
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) High dose corticosteroid therapy, administration of immunoglobulins

Explanation: The first-line treatment for warm autoimmune hemolytic anemia (WAIHA) is corticosteroids (like prednisone) to suppress the immune response. IVIG (immunoglobulins) may be used in severe cases. Transfusion is avoided if possible due to difficulty cross-matching.

15.
Tympanometry is a:
a) Test to help determine inner ear problems such as otosclerosis
b) Subjective procedure to asses eardrum mobility
c) Procedure to diagnose serous otitis
d) Method used to assess Eustachian tube malfunction
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Procedure to diagnose serous otitis

Explanation: Tympanometry is an objective test that measures the compliance (mobility) of the tympanic membrane and middle ear pressure. It is diagnostic for middle ear fluid (serous otitis media - Type B curve) and Eustachian tube dysfunction (Type C curve). Option B is incorrect because it is an *objective* test, not subjective.

16.
The respiratory problems that accompany peritonitis are as a result of:
a) Associated pain interfering with ventilation
b) Decreased pressure against the diaphragm
c) Increased oxygen demands from skeletal tissue
d) Fluid shifts to the thoracic cavity
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Associated pain interfering with ventilation

Explanation: Peritonitis causes severe abdominal pain. Patients engage in shallow breathing (splinting) to avoid moving the diaphragm and abdominal muscles, which leads to hypoventilation and potential atelectasis.

17.
An anorexic client states that her stomach is "fat" and that all the food she is being forced to eat is making her obese. The nurse's best reply is:
a) "Your body is starting to work normally again and is getting stronger and healthier"
b) "You have a bloated stomach because there is too much salt in your food"
c) "No, it's not, you look much better now than when you were first admitted"
d) "May be we have been feeding you too much and you're gaining weight too fast"
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) "You have a bloated stomach because there is too much salt in your food" (Partial/Best of options)

Explanation: This is a difficult question with imperfect options. However, addressing the physical sensation the client feels (bloating is common during refeeding) with a physiological explanation is often better than arguing with the delusion (Option C: "No it's not") or validating the fear of obesity (Option D). Option A ("Your body is getting stronger") is a positive reframe but dismisses the specific complaint. In NCK context, providing a physiological reason for the sensation (bloating) helps reduce anxiety without challenging the body dysmorphia directly.

18.
Skin changes in the elderly people leave them more susceptible to:
a) Heat stroke and heat exhaustion
b) Melasma and comedone formation
c) Hirsutism and heat retention
d) Seborrhea and scalp scaliness
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Heat stroke and heat exhaustion

Explanation: Aging skin has fewer sweat glands, decreased vascularity, and thinned subcutaneous fat. This impairs thermoregulation (the ability to cool the body via sweating and vasodilation), making the elderly highly susceptible to heat stroke and heat exhaustion.

19.
Match the objective client data in column A with the musculo-skeletal disorder they are primarily associated with in column B:
A: Unsteady gait, Flushed warm skin
a) Unsteady gait -> Osteomalacia (2); Flushed skin -> Pagets disease (3)
b) Unsteady gait -> Osteomyelitis (4); Flushed skin -> Osteosarcoma (5)
c) Unsteady gait -> Osteoporosis (1); Flushed skin -> Osteomyelitis (4)
d) Unsteady gait -> Osteomalacia (2); Flushed skin -> Osteoporosis (1)
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Unsteady gait -> Osteomalacia (2); Flushed skin -> Pagets disease (3)

Explanation:
1. Paget's Disease (3): Characterized by highly vascular bone remodeling, leading to "flushed warm skin" over the affected bones due to increased blood flow.
2. Osteomalacia (2): Softening of bones often leads to skeletal deformities and a characteristic "waddling" or unsteady gait.

20.
When teaching about proper use of antacids, the nurse should instruct the client to:
a) Stagger the administration of the antacids with other medications
b) Take the antacids with all other medications
c) Take other medications in the daytime and antacids at night
d) Drink a glass of water immediately after taking the antacids
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Stagger the administration of the antacids with other medications

Explanation: Antacids can alter the absorption of many other medications by changing gastric pH or binding to the drug. It is standard practice to separate (stagger) antacid administration by at least 1-2 hours from other medications.

Part II: Long Answer Questions

1.
"Compartment syndrome is a medical emergency." Explain. (4 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

Compartment syndrome is a medical emergency because:

  1. It involves increased pressure within a confined anatomical space (fascial compartment) that restricts the volume.
  2. This pressure compromises circulation (blood flow) to the tissues within that space.
  3. The reduced blood flow leads to tissue ischemia (lack of oxygen).
  4. If not relieved promptly (via fasciotomy), it causes irreversible tissue necrosis, nerve damage, and loss of limb function within 4-6 hours.
2.
State four nursing diagnoses for a client with "urolithiasis" (kidney stones). (4 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Acute Pain related to inflammation, obstruction, and abrasion of the urinary tract by stones.
  2. Impaired Urinary Elimination related to mechanical obstruction by stones or inflammation.
  3. Risk for Infection related to urinary stasis or trauma to tissues.
  4. Deficient Knowledge related to dietary modifications and prevention of recurrence.
3.
Describe the pathophysiology of osteomyelitis. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Inoculation: Pathogenic organisms (usually Staphylococcus aureus) enter the bone via direct extension (open fracture) or hematogenous spread (bloodstream).
  2. Inflammation: The bacteria proliferate in the medullary canal, initiating an inflammatory response with edema and pus formation.
  3. Increased Pressure: The rigid bone cannot expand, so the pressure within the medullary cavity increases rapidly.
  4. Ischemia: The high pressure compresses blood vessels, compromising blood supply and leading to bone ischemia and necrosis.
  5. Sequestrum Formation: The area of dead bone separates from the healthy bone, forming a sequestrum.
  6. Involucrum Formation: The periosteum lifts and new bone forms around the dead tissue, creating a sheath called an involucrum.
