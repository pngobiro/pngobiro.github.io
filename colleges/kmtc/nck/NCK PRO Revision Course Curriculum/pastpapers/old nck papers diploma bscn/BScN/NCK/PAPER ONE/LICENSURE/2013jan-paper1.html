Part I: Multiple Choice Questions

20 Marks
1.
When the nurse commits her/himself to actively promote patients well being, she/he is practicing the principle of:
a) Fidelity
b) Justice
c) Beneficence
d) Non-maleficence
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Beneficence

Explanation: Beneficence is the ethical principle of acting in the best interest of the patient and actively promoting their well-being. It goes beyond just "doing no harm" (non-maleficence) to actually doing good.

  • a) Fidelity: Refers to faithfulness, keeping promises, and loyalty to the patient.
  • b) Justice: Refers to fairness and equality in the distribution of resources and care.
  • d) Non-maleficence: The obligation to do no harm or minimize harm.
2.
The features that suggest fracture base of the skull include:
a) Rhinorrhea, loss of gag reflex
b) Otorrhea, sub-conjuctival bleeding
c) Cervical spine 1 fracture, dilated pupils
d) Glasgow coma scale of 5, constricted pupils
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Otorrhea, sub-conjuctival bleeding

Explanation: Basal skull fractures are characterized by specific signs including CSF leakage from the nose (rhinorrhea) or ears (otorrhea), and hemorrhagic signs like periorbital ecchymosis (Raccoon eyes) and mastoid ecchymosis (Battle's sign). Sub-conjunctival bleeding can also occur due to tracking of blood. While loss of gag reflex indicates nerve damage, otorrhea is a cardinal sign of basal skull fracture.

Clinical Signs: Remember "Battle's Sign" (bruising behind ear) and "Raccoon Eyes" (bruising around eyes) as classic indicators.

3.
The risk factors for Guillain-Barre Syndrome are:
a) HIV/AIDS, typhoid immunization
b) Hodgkins disease, syphilis
c) Surgery, infection with Campylobacter
d) Influenza immunization, infection with Neisseria meningitides
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Surgery, infection with Campylobacter

Explanation: Guillain-Barré Syndrome (GBS) is often preceded by an infection. Campylobacter jejuni enteritis is the most common antecedent infection. Surgery and trauma are also known triggers. While some immunizations have been historically linked, Campylobacter is a definitive and major risk factor.

4.
The causes of acute interstitial nephritis include:
a) Allergy to penicillin, acute bacterial pyelonephritis
b) Hydro-nephrosis, vesico-ureteric reflex
c) Leptospirosis, sickle-cell nephropathy
d) Myeloma, renal dysplasia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Allergy to penicillin, acute bacterial pyelonephritis

Explanation: Acute Interstitial Nephritis (AIN) is most commonly caused by hypersensitivity reactions to drugs (like penicillins, NSAIDs, PPIs) and infections (like pyelonephritis). Drug-induced allergic reaction is the hallmark cause.

5.
Structures in the middle ear include:
a) Auditory canal, ossicles
b) Round window, Eustachian tube
c) Ossicles, cochlear
d) Oval window, semi-circular canals
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Round window, Eustachian tube

Explanation: The middle ear cavity contains the ossicles (malleus, incus, stapes). It communicates with the nasopharynx via the Eustachian tube. The round window and oval window separate the middle ear from the inner ear. The auditory canal is external, and the cochlea/semicircular canals are inner ear structures.

6.
The clinical manifestations of exfoliative dermatitis include:
a) Erythematous plaques and acne
b) Widespread purpura and wheals
c) Erythema and palpable nodules
d) Generalized redness and scaling
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Generalized redness and scaling

Explanation: Exfoliative dermatitis (erythroderma) involves widespread erythema (redness) and scaling (exfoliation) of the skin, affecting more than 90% of the body surface area.

7.
The limitations of ultrasound in gastro-enterology include:
a) Has low sensitivity for small lesions, obesity obscures the images
b) Exposes one to high radiation dose, cannot be used on a patient who has metallic prosthesis
c) Is expensive, has little functional information
d) It may under stage some tumors. Its role in gastro-intestinal diseases has not been fully investigated
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Has low sensitivity for small lesions, obesity obscures the images

Explanation: Ultrasound waves are attenuated by subcutaneous fat (making obesity a limitation) and are reflected by gas (making bowel gas a barrier). It provides poor visualization of structures deep to gas or bone.

8.
High risk category of patients should be considered for specific antithrombotic prophylaxis, which include those undergoing:
a) Hip surgery, pelvic surgery for removal of malignancy
b) Treatment for major medical conditions such as pneumonia and HIV/AIDS
c) Knee surgery, treatment for heart failure
d) Antiretroviral therapy, inflammatory bowel syndrome management
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Hip surgery, pelvic surgery for removal of malignancy

Explanation: Major orthopedic surgery (hip/knee replacement, hip fracture surgery) and major cancer surgery (especially pelvic/abdominal) carry the highest risk for Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT) and Pulmonary Embolism (PE), requiring aggressive prophylaxis.

9.
The most appropriate drugs for the management of nausea and vomiting in palliative care are:
a) Sodium docusate, scopolamine
b) Metoclopramide, chlorpheniramine
c) Chlorpheniramine, sodium docusate
d) Scopolamine, metoclopramide
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Scopolamine, metoclopramide

Explanation: In palliative care:
Metoclopramide is effective for gastric stasis and visceral causes.
Scopolamine (Hyoscine) is effective for motion sickness, bowel obstruction, and drying secretions ("death rattle").
Note: Sodium docusate is a stool softener, not an anti-emetic.

10.
The clinical features of open angle glaucoma include:
a) Slow loss of sight, loss of visual field, cupping of the optic disc
b) Blurred vision, corneal oedema, red eye
c) Rapid loss of vision, dilated pupil, severe eye pain
d) Constricted pupil, very painful eye, blurred vision
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Slow loss of sight, loss of visual field, cupping of the optic disc

Explanation: Primary Open Angle Glaucoma (POAG) is typically asymptomatic in early stages, causing gradual, painless loss of peripheral vision ("tunnel vision") and pathological cupping of the optic disc. Option (c) describes Acute Closed Angle Glaucoma.

11.
To identify the bowel sounds the nurse will place a warmed diaphragm of the stethoscope on:
a) The supra-pubic region
b) Right hypochondrium
c) All the quadrants of the abdomen towards the umbilicus
d) All the nine regions of the abdomen
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) All the quadrants of the abdomen towards the umbilicus

Explanation: While bowel sounds can often be heard in the RLQ (ileocecal valve area), standard assessment involves listening in all four quadrants to ensure motility is present throughout. The stethoscope is typically placed lightly near the umbilicus in each quadrant.

12.
The technique used to determine the mobility of abdominal organs is:
a) Auscultation
b) Palpation
c) Percussion
d) Inspection
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Palpation

Explanation: Palpation (specifically deep palpation or ballottement) is used to assess organs for size, shape, consistency, and mobility. It can detect if a mass or organ moves with respiration or manual pressure.

13.
The dosage of rectal diazepam for a 4 year old child weighing 15kgs is:
a) 1.5 mgs in 0.3 millilitres
b) 10 mgs in 5 mililitres
c) 5 mgs in 2.5 mililitres
d) 7.5 mgs in 1.5 mililitres
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) 7.5 mgs in 1.5 mililitres

Explanation: The standard dose for rectal diazepam in children is 0.5 mg/kg.
Calculation: $15 \text{ kg} \times 0.5 \text{ mg/kg} = 7.5 \text{ mg}$.
Standard rectal solution is often 5 mg/mL.
Volume: $7.5 \text{ mg} \div 5 \text{ mg/mL} = 1.5 \text{ mL}$.

14.
Manifestations of hypothyroidism in reproductive system include:
a) Increased libido, oligospermia
b) Impotence, metrorrhagia
c) Anovulation, high incidence of spontaneous abortion
d) Irregular menstrual cycles, increased libido
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Anovulation, high incidence of spontaneous abortion

Explanation: Hypothyroidism disrupts the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal axis, often leading to anovulatory cycles, infertility, menorrhagia (heavy periods), and an increased risk of miscarriage (spontaneous abortion) if pregnancy occurs.

15.
The causes of exertional dyspnea include:
a) Pneumothorax, lobar collapse
b) Bronchial carcinoma, pneumoconiosis
c) Pulmonary embolism, pneumonia
d) Atelectasis, pulmonary oedema
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Bronchial carcinoma, pneumoconiosis

Explanation: Exertional dyspnea (breathlessness on effort) typically indicates chronic or progressive lung/heart disease. Bronchial carcinoma and pneumoconiosis are chronic conditions that progressively compromise lung function. Options a, c, and d list acute conditions (pneumothorax, PE, pulmonary edema) which usually cause acute/resting dyspnea.

16.
Sinus bradycardia contributes to:
a) Decreased filling times, increased mean pressure
b) Increased oxygen demand, decreased preload
c) Decreased mean arterial pressure, increased preload
d) Increased filling times, increased mean arterial pressure
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Increased filling times, increased mean arterial pressure

Explanation: Bradycardia (slow heart rate) increases the duration of diastole, which increases ventricular filling time and thus increases preload. According to the Frank-Starling law, this increases stroke volume, which can help maintain or slightly increase Mean Arterial Pressure (MAP) if the rate isn't too low.

Note: If bradycardia is severe, MAP drops, but the primary physiological effect of slow rate is increased filling time.

17.
The function of niacin in the body is:
a) Inhibition of production of cholesterol, is necessary for cell respiration
b) Formation of collagen is necessary for red blood cell maturation
c) Is necessary for DNA synthesis, is antioxidant
d) Is necessary for carbohydrate metabolism
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Is necessary for carbohydrate metabolism

Explanation: Niacin (Vitamin B3) is a precursor for NAD and NADP, coenzymes essential for redox reactions in the metabolism of carbohydrates, fats, and proteins (cellular respiration).

18.
As regards the process of inflammation, release of histamine is triggered when:
a) Cells degranulate and antibody titre is low
b) Platelets are activated and mast cells degranulate
c) Mast cells degranulate and basophils depressed
d) Binding of antibody to mast cells and basophils occur
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Platelets are activated and mast cells degranulate

Explanation: Histamine is stored in mast cell granules. Release (degranulation) occurs when an antigen binds to IgE antibodies attached to the mast cell surface. The question is phrased slightly ambiguously, but the event of "mast cells degranulating" is the direct cause of histamine release.

19.
The child has foreign body aspiration and probably not laryngo-tracheobronchitis (croup) if there is:
a) Hoarse voice, stridor of gradual onset
b) Prolonged inspirational crepitations on auscultation
c) Sudden onset of stridor, upper respiratory tract infection
d) Focal reduced air entry, sudden onset of respiratory distress
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Focal reduced air entry, sudden onset of respiratory distress

Explanation: Foreign body aspiration typically presents with a sudden onset of choking/coughing/stridor in a previously well child, often with focal findings (like reduced air entry on one side). Croup typically has a gradual onset, is preceded by URI symptoms, and presents with fever and a barking cough.

20.
The risks associated with knee-chest position when the patient is on the operation table for a long period of time includes:
a) Venous pooling as blood shifts to the lower extremities
b) Damage to arterial plexus derived from internal iliac arteries
c) Deep venous thrombosis in the lower extremities
d) Back strain
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Deep venous thrombosis in the lower extremities

Explanation: Prolonged positioning in knee-chest (genucubital) position can cause venous stasis in the lower extremities due to compression and gravity, increasing the risk of Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT). Hypotension is also a risk.

Part II: Long Answer Questions

35 Marks
1.
Explain four body surface defenses against infection. (8 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Intact Skin (Physical Barrier): The multiple layers of the epidermis, especially the keratinized stratum corneum, provide a tough, water-resistant barrier that prevents pathogen entry. Periodic shedding removes attached microbes.
  2. Mucous Membranes: Line body cavities (respiratory, GI, genitourinary tracts). They secrete mucus which traps pathogens and particles.
  3. Ciliary Action: In the respiratory tract, cilia beat in a coordinated wave (mucociliary escalator) to move mucus-trapped pathogens up and out of the lungs to be swallowed or coughed out.
  4. Chemical Defenses (pH and Enzymes):
    • Acid Mantle: Skin has a low pH (3-5) which inhibits bacterial growth.
    • Lysozyme: Enzyme found in tears, saliva, and sweat that breaks down bacterial cell walls.
    • Gastric Acid: Low pH in the stomach kills most ingested pathogens.
2.
Outline the management of atopic dermatitis. (3 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Skin Hydration (Emollients): Regular and liberal application of moisturizers/emollients to restore the skin barrier and reduce dryness.
  2. Avoidance of Triggers: Identifying and avoiding irritants (harsh soaps, wool) and allergens that exacerbate the condition.
  3. Pharmacotherapy:
    • Topical Corticosteroids: For acute flare-ups to reduce inflammation.
    • Antihistamines: To control pruritus (itching), especially at night.
3.
Describe the pathophysiology of rheumatoid arthritis. (7 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Autoimmune Trigger: An unknown trigger (genetic/environmental) causes the immune system to attack synovial tissue.
  2. Synovitis: Inflammation of the synovium (joint lining) occurs, with infiltration of T-cells, B-cells, and macrophages.
  3. Pannus Formation: The inflamed synovium proliferates and thickens into a granulation tissue called pannus.
  4. Joint Destruction: The pannus invades and erodes the articular cartilage and underlying bone. Enzymes causing this destruction are released by neutrophils and macrophages.
  5. Deformity: Destruction of cartilage, bone, and supporting ligaments/tendons leads to joint instability, subluxation, and characteristic deformities (e.g., ulnar deviation).
  6. Fibrosis and Ankylosis: Fibrous tissue may replace the pannus, leading to stiffness (fibrous ankylosis) and eventually calcification (bony ankylosis).
4.
A three year old child has been under home management of asthma for the last 2 weeks. State seven factors that may necessitate the child’s admission to hospital for further management. (7 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Failure of outpatient therapy: No improvement despite adequate use of bronchodilators and steroids.
  2. Severe respiratory distress: Marked by severe retractions, nasal flaring, or difficulty speaking/feeding.
  3. Low Oxygen Saturation: SpO2 < 92% on room air.
  4. "Silent Chest": Absence of wheezing on auscultation (indicating minimal air movement), which is a sign of impending respiratory failure.
  5. Altered Level of Consciousness: Drowsiness, confusion, or agitation due to hypoxia/hypercapnia.
  6. Dehydration: Inability to take fluids orally due to respiratory distress.
  7. Social Factors: Inability of parents to manage care at home or distance from medical facility.
  8. Peak Expiratory Flow Rate (PEFR): < 50% of predicted/personal best (though difficult to measure in a 3-year-old).
5.
Explain the pathophysiology for Helicobacter pylori infection. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Colonization: H. pylori (a gram-negative spiral bacterium) enters the stomach and uses its flagella to burrow through the protective mucus layer to the gastric epithelium.
  2. Acid Neutralization: It produces large amounts of the enzyme urease, which converts urea into ammonia and carbon dioxide. The ammonia neutralizes gastric acid in the immediate vicinity, creating a protective cloud that allows the bacteria to survive.
  3. Mucosal Injury: The ammonia and other cytotoxins (like VacA and CagA) produced by the bacteria damage the epithelial cells and degrade the protective mucus layer.
  4. Inflammation: The infection triggers an inflammatory response (gastritis), attracting neutrophils and lymphocytes. This chronic inflammation further damages the mucosa.
  5. Ulcer Formation: The compromised mucosal barrier allows gastric acid to digest the stomach wall, leading to peptic ulcer formation.
6.
State five human developmental (from conception to old age) factors that may affect drug action in the body. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Age (Liver/Kidney Maturity): Infants have immature liver enzymes (reduced metabolism) and renal function (reduced excretion), leading to drug toxicity. The elderly have declining organ function.
  2. Body Composition (Water/Fat Content): Infants have higher total body water (affecting distribution of water-soluble drugs). The elderly have higher body fat (increasing half-life of lipid-soluble drugs).
  3. Plasma Protein Levels: Infants and the elderly may have lower albumin levels, leading to higher fractions of free (active) drug.
  4. Gastric pH: Gastric pH varies with age (less acidic in neonates and elderly), affecting absorption of oral medications.
  5. Blood-Brain Barrier: Is immature in neonates, allowing drugs to enter the CNS more easily.
  6. Pregnancy: Altered hemodynamics, plasma volume, and the presence of the placental barrier affect drug distribution and fetal exposure.
