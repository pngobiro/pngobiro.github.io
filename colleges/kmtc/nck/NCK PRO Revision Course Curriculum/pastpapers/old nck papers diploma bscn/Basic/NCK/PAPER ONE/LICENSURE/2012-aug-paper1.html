Part I: Multiple Choice Questions

20 Marks
1.
Use of hyperthermia as a treatment modality for cancer may cause:-
a) Hypotension, skin burn, tissue damage.
b) Nausea, vomiting, delayed wound healing.
c) Delayed wound healing, hypertension, nausea.
d) Skin burn, immunosuppression, infections.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Hypotension, skin burn, tissue damage.

Explanation: Hyperthermia treatment involves raising body tissue temperatures to 40–45°C. Systemic hyperthermia causes widespread vasodilation, leading to hypotension (low blood pressure). Direct heat application can inherently cause skin burns and tissue damage (blisters/necrosis) if not strictly monitored.

Study Tip: Remember "Heat expands" -> Vasodilation -> Hypotension.

2.
Late complications of cataract surgery include:-
a) Retinal detachment, hyphaema, flat anterior chamber.
b) Posterior capsule opacification, iris prolapse, hyphaema.
c) Flat anterior chamber, iris prolapse, posterior capsule opacification.
d) Posterior capsule opacification, retinal detachment, secondary cataract.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Posterior capsule opacification, retinal detachment, secondary cataract.

Explanation:

  • Posterior Capsule Opacification (PCO): Also known as "secondary cataract," this is the most common late complication.
  • Retinal Detachment: A serious late complication that can occur months or years later.
Hyphaema and Iris prolapse (Options A, B, C) are typically early complications occurring within hours or days of surgery.

3.
The intracranial hemorrhage with the worst prognosis is:-
a) Cephalohematoma.
b) Epidural hemorrhage.
c) Intracerebral hemorrhage.
d) Subarachnoid hemorrhage.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Intracerebral hemorrhage.

Explanation: Intracerebral hemorrhage (ICH) typically carries the highest morbidity and mortality rates because the bleeding occurs directly within the brain tissue (parenchyma), causing direct neuronal destruction and increased intracranial pressure. While Subarachnoid is also very dangerous, ICH statistics for survival and functional recovery are often poorer.

4.
The most common benign bone tumor is:-
a) Osteochondroma.
b) Osteoid osteoma.
c) Enchondroma.
d) Giant cell tumor.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Osteochondroma.

Explanation: An Osteochondroma (an overgrowth of cartilage and bone at the end of the bone near the growth plate) is statistically the most common non-cancerous (benign) bone tumor.

5.
Fat embolism as a complication of fractures:-
a) Is the leading cause of death in fracture patients.
b) Results in symptoms of decreased mentation.
c) May compromise patient's respiratory status necessitating ventilatory support.
d) Is more common in the elderly than younger patients.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) May compromise patient's respiratory status necessitating ventilatory support.

Explanation: Fat Embolism Syndrome (FES) primarily affects the lungs (hypoxemia, dyspnea). Severe cases lead to Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome (ARDS) requiring mechanical ventilation. While altered mentation (b) occurs, the respiratory compromise is the hallmark physiologic threat.

6.
A statement indicating that a nurse understands pneumothorax as a pleural injury is:-
a) Closed pneumothorax may occur following a tension pneumothorax.
b) Open pneumothorax is characterized by a shift of mediastinum to the ipsilateral side.
c) Pneumothorax leads to decreased intrathoracic pressure in both open and closed pneumothorax.
d) Open pneumothorax allows atmospheric air to enter pleural space during inspiration.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Open pneumothorax allows atmospheric air to enter pleural space during inspiration.

Explanation: In an open pneumothorax (sucking chest wound), the chest wall defect allows air to be sucked directly into the pleural space from the outside environment during inspiration, collapsing the lung.

Why others are wrong: Mediastinal shift occurs to the contralateral (opposite) side (b), and pneumothorax leads to increased (less negative) intrathoracic pressure (c).

7.
Signs of hypothyroidism include:-
a) Muscle weakness, depression.
b) Tachycardia, irritability.
c) Decreased appetite, tachycardia.
d) Hypertension, somnolence.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Muscle weakness, depression.

Explanation: Hypothyroidism implies a slow metabolism. Symptoms include lethargy, depression, muscle weakness, weight gain (despite decreased appetite), bradycardia, and cold intolerance. Options B and C describe hyperthyroidism (high metabolism signs like tachycardia).

8.
A classification of ringworms that infect the finger nails is:-
a) Tinea corporis.
b) Tinea unguium.
c) Tinea cruris.
d) Tinea pedis.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Tinea unguium.

Explanation:

  • Tinea unguium (Onychomycosis): Infection of the nails.
  • Tinea corporis: Ringworm of the body.
  • Tinea cruris: Jock itch (groin).
  • Tinea pedis: Athlete's foot.

9.
Following gastrectomy, dumping syndrome occurs as a result of:-
a) Reduced gastric motility resulting from vagus nerve stimulation.
b) Removal of acid producing cells thus resulting in reduced absorption of vit B12.
c) Rapid gastric emptying of hyperosmolar fluid and substances into the duodenum.
d) Damage of vagus nerve and cardiac sphincter.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Rapid gastric emptying of hyperosmolar fluid and substances into the duodenum.

Explanation: Dumping syndrome is caused by the "dumping" of a large bolus of hypertonic food (especially sugars) into the small intestine rapidly. This draws fluid into the bowel lumen, causing cramps, diarrhea, and hypovolemia.

10.
Priority nursing intervention for a patient with a peritoneal abscess is to:-
a) Administer analgesics for relief of pain.
b) Administer intravenous fluids to maintain fluid volume balance.
c) Reassure the patient to relieve anxiety and co-operate during pre-operative preparation.
d) Administer parenteral nutrition to obtain optimum weight gain while the gut is rested.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Administer intravenous fluids to maintain fluid volume balance.

Explanation: Peritonitis and abscesses cause significant fluid shifts ("third spacing") into the peritoneal cavity, leading to hypovolemia and potential shock. Maintaining circulatory volume via IV fluids is the physiological priority (ABC principle - Circulation). Pain relief (A) is important but secondary to hemodynamic stability.

11.
A nephrostomy tube is inserted:-
a) In the anterior bladder wall and joined to the abdominal wall.
b) In the posterior bladder wall and joined to the abdominal wall.
c) To a portion of the ileum then brought to the surface of the abdomen.
d) Through the skin into the kidney.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Through the skin into the kidney.

Explanation: A nephrostomy is an artificial opening created between the kidney and the skin which allows for the urinary diversion directly from the upper part of the urinary system (renal pelvis).

12.
The first step in urinary catheterization procedure is:-
a) Provide adequate lighting.
b) Prepare fluid to inflate the balloon.
c) Selection of catheter size.
d) Ask patient to empty bladder.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Provide adequate lighting.

Explanation: While gathering equipment is early, ensuring the environment is appropriate (lighting, privacy) is a foundational step before setting up the sterile field. Good lighting is essential to locate the urethra and maintain sterility. (Note: You do not ask a patient to empty their bladder if you are catheterizing them to drain it).

13.
When estimating the size of burns, the palm of the patient's hand represents total body surface area of:-
a) 3%.
b) 1%.
c) 4.5%.
d) 9%.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) 1%.

Explanation: The "Rule of Palms" states that the surface area of the patient's palm (including fingers) is approximately equal to 1% of their total body surface area (TBSA). This is useful for estimating small or scattered burns.

14.
A patient with potassium levels in excess of 5.0 mEq/L is considered to be in a state of:-
a) Hypernatremia.
b) Hypercalcemia.
c) Hypervolemia.
d) Hyperkalemia.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Hyperkalemia.

Explanation: The normal range for serum Potassium (K+) is typically 3.5 to 5.0 mEq/L. Levels above 5.0 indicate Hyperkalemia. (Na = Sodium, Ca = Calcium).

15.
The health message given to a patient following tympanoplasty is:-
a) Drink fluids using a straw.
b) Avoid blowing the nose and sneeze through the mouth.
c) Administer saline water into the ear to aid healing.
d) Avoid noisy places upto two weeks post operatively.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Avoid blowing the nose and sneeze through the mouth.

Explanation: After ear surgery (tympanoplasty), increased pressure in the eustachian tube can dislodge the graft. Patients are taught to sneeze with their mouth open and avoid blowing their nose to prevent pressure buildup.

16.
The correct steps in giving first aid to a child with a knife cut on the index finger is:-
a) Apply pressure, apply surgical spirit, bandage.
b) Apply surgical spirit, wash finger under running water, bandage.
c) Wash finger under running water, apply gentian violet, bandage.
d) Apply gentian violet, wash finger under surgical spirit, bandage.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: *Note: Best practice is typically Pressure -> Wash -> Bandage. Between options provided, 'Apply pressure' is the priority for a cut.

However, option structure suggests a sequence. Option (a) starts with "Apply pressure" which is the absolute priority to stop bleeding. Surgical spirit is generally discouraged directly on open wounds now (pain/tissue damage), but historically or in this exam context, stopping bleeding (pressure) is the first action.

17.
The process of immersing used dressing instruments in dilute hibitane solution is known as:-
a) Chemical sterilization.
b) Disinfection.
c) Decontamination.
d) Cold sterilization.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Decontamination.

Explanation: The initial soaking of used instruments in a disinfectant solution (like Hibitane/Chlorhexidine or Jik) immediately after use to kill vegetative microbes and make items safe to handle before cleaning is termed decontamination. "Disinfection" is the general process, but the specific step of immersing used items is usually taught as decontamination.

18.
Specific defence mechanisms are achieved by:-
a) Phagocytosis.
b) Chemotaxis.
c) Inflammatory responses.
d) Cell-mediated immunity.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Cell-mediated immunity.

Explanation: The immune system has non-specific (innate) defenses like phagocytosis and inflammation (options a, b, c). Specific (adaptive) immunity involves lymphocytes (T-cells and B-cells) targeting specific antigens, which includes Cell-mediated immunity (T-cells) and Humoral immunity (B-cells).

20.
Indicate whether the statements are true or false.
a) The uterus develops by fusion of the lower end of the Wolffian ducts.
b) Total abdominal hysterectomy is the definitive treatment of endometriosis.
Show Answer

Answers:

  • a) False. The uterus develops from the fusion of the Mullerian (paramesonephric) ducts. Wolffian ducts form male reproductive structures.
  • b) True. While conservative treatment is tried first, TAH with Bilateral Salpingo-Oophorectomy (TAH-BSO) is considered the definitive cure as it removes the hormonal stimulation and the organs involved.

Part II: Short Answer Questions

40 Marks
1.
Draw and label a diagram illustrating the thyroid gland and its associated structures. (6 marks)
Show Answer

Diagram Key Components:

Your diagram should clearly depict the butterfly shape of the gland located in the anterior neck.

  1. Thyroid Cartilage: Located superior to the gland (Adam's apple).
  2. Cricoid Cartilage: Located just below the thyroid cartilage.
  3. Right and Left Lobes: The two main wings of the gland.
  4. Isthmus: The bridge of tissue connecting the two lobes across the trachea.
  5. Trachea: The windpipe, with tracheal rings visible below the isthmus.
  6. Pyramidal Lobe: (Optional but accurate) A small lobe extending upward from the isthmus.

Drawing Tip: Ensure the isthmus is drawn overlying the 2nd to 4th tracheal rings.

2.
Outline two effects of Aldosterone. (2 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Sodium Retention: Increases reabsorption of sodium ions from the renal tubules back into the blood.
  2. Potassium Excretion: Increases secretion of potassium ions into the urine.
  3. Water Retention: Secondary to sodium retention, water follows by osmosis, increasing blood volume and pressure.
3.
Outline the components of assessment in the nursing process. (3 marks)
Show Answer
  1. History Taking (Subjective Data): Interviewing the patient to gather information about symptoms, past medical history, and complaints.
  2. Physical Examination (Objective Data): Inspection, Palpation, Percussion, and Auscultation to gather observable data.
  3. Diagnostic Investigations: Laboratory tests, radiological studies, and other procedures to confirm findings.
4.
State six (6) foot care instructions for a patient with diabetes mellitus. (6 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Inspect feet daily for cuts, blisters, or redness using a mirror if necessary.
  2. Wash feet daily with lukewarm water and dry thoroughly, especially between the toes.
  3. Apply moisturizer to dry skin but avoid applying it between the toes to prevent fungal infections.
  4. Do not walk barefoot; always wear protective footwear (shoes/slippers).
  5. Cut toenails straight across to prevent ingrown toenails; file sharp edges.
  6. Wear well-fitting shoes that are not too tight, and check inside shoes for stones/objects before wearing.
  7. Avoid using hot water bottles or heating pads on feet due to reduced sensation (neuropathy).
5.
State five (5) specific nursing assessments done for a patient with a tracheostomy. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Respiratory Status: Assess rate, depth, effort of breathing, and oxygen saturation.
  2. Secretions: Assess color, consistency, amount, and odor of secretions from the tube.
  3. Stoma Site: Inspect the skin around the stoma for redness, infection, breakdown, or bleeding.
  4. Tube Patency: Ensure the inner cannula is clean and the tube is not blocked.
  5. Cuff Pressure: Check cuff inflation pressure (if cuffed tube) to prevent aspiration or tracheal necrosis.
  6. Securement: Check that ties/tapes are secure (one finger breadth tightness) to prevent dislodgement.
6.
a) Outline four (4) specific nursing interventions for a patient with pelvic inflammatory disease. (4 marks)
b) State four (4) factors that cause infertility in males. (4 marks)
Show Answer

a) Nursing Interventions for PID:

  1. Positioning: Place patient in Semi-Fowler’s position to promote drainage of pelvic abscesses and prevent upward spread of infection.
  2. Medication Administration: Administer prescribed broad-spectrum antibiotics and analgesics strictly.
  3. Infection Control: Use standard precautions and properly dispose of perineal pads (biohazard).
  4. Rest: Encourage bed rest to decrease metabolic rate and mobilize defenses against infection.
  5. Perineal Hygiene: Perform or assist with frequent perineal care to maintain cleanliness.

b) Factors causing Male Infertility:

  1. Low Sperm Count (Oligospermia): Due to infections (mumps), hormonal imbalance, or heat.
  2. Structural Blockage: Obstruction of the vas deferens or epididymis (e.g., from STI scarring).
  3. Erectile Dysfunction/Impotence: Inability to achieve erection or ejaculation.
  4. Abnormal Sperm Morphology: High percentage of malformed sperm unable to penetrate the ovum.
  5. Cryptorchidism: Undescended testes (too warm for sperm production).
7.
State four (4) purposes for nasogastric tube insertion. (4 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Decompression: To remove gas and fluids from the stomach (e.g., in intestinal obstruction).
  2. Feeding (Gavage): To administer nutrition and fluids to patients unable to swallow.
  3. Lavage: To wash out the stomach contents (e.g., in poisoning or overdose).
  4. Diagnosis: To aspirate gastric contents for laboratory analysis (e.g., pH, blood).
  5. Administration of Medication: For patients who cannot swallow oral pills.
8.
a) List four (4) indications of Fluconazole. (2 marks)
b) Outline four (4) nursing considerations for a patient on corticosteroid therapy. (4 marks)
Show Answer

a) Indications of Fluconazole:

  1. Vaginal Candidiasis (Yeast infection).
  2. Oropharyngeal Candidiasis (Oral Thrush).
  3. Cryptococcal Meningitis.
  4. Prophylaxis in immunocompromised patients (e.g., HIV/AIDS, chemotherapy).

b) Nursing Considerations for Corticosteroids:

  1. Monitor Blood Glucose: Steroids cause hyperglycemia; monitor levels closely, especially in diabetics.
  2. Infection Prevention: Steroids suppress the immune system; protect patient from exposure to infections.
  3. Gastric Protection: Administer with food or milk to prevent gastric irritation/ulcers (or give prescribed PPIs).
  4. Avoid Abrupt Withdrawal: Educate patient not to stop suddenly to prevent adrenal crisis; tapering is required.
  5. Monitor Fluid/Electrolytes: Check for edema (sodium retention) and hypokalemia.

a) Definition:

Pneumonia is an acute inflammation of the lung parenchyma (alveoli and bronchioles) caused by various microorganisms (bacteria, viruses, fungi) leading to consolidation and impaired gas exchange.

2.

Mrs. Ke, a 43 year old patient has been admitted to the burns unit. She sustained burns to her face, upper arms and chest following a kerosene stove explosion. Preliminary investigations confirm inhalation injury.

a) Explain the impact of burn injury on the respiratory system. (6 marks)

b) Describe the specific nursing management of Mrs. Ke in the first 24 hours. (14 marks)

Show Answer

a) Impact of Burn Injury on Respiratory System:

  1. Upper Airway Edema: Direct thermal injury to the face/throat causes rapid swelling of the mucosa, potentially leading to total airway obstruction (laryngeal edema).
  2. Inhalation of Toxic Fumes (Smoke Inhalation): Chemicals cause irritation, bronchospasm, and damage to the cilia, impairing clearance of secretions.
  3. Carbon Monoxide Poisoning: CO binds to hemoglobin with higher affinity than oxygen, leading to severe systemic hypoxia despite normal PaO2.
  4. Lower Airway Injury: Chemical pneumonitis from soot/particles leads to mucosal sloughing, pulmonary edema, and eventual ARDS (Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome).
  5. Restricted Chest Expansion: Full-thickness circumferential burns to the chest (eschar) act as a tourniquet, physically preventing chest expansion during breathing.

b) Specific Nursing Management (First 24 Hours - Emergent Phase):

  • 1. Airway Management (Priority):
    • Assess patency immediately (stridor, hoarseness, soot in sputum).
    • Maintain high Fowler's position.
    • Prepare for early prophylactic intubation due to facial burns and risk of laryngeal edema.
    • Administer 100% humidified oxygen.
  • 2. Fluid Resuscitation (Circulation):
    • Establish 2 large-bore IV lines.
    • Calculate fluid needs using Parkland Formula (4ml x kg x %TBSA).
    • Administer 50% of calculated Ringer's Lactate in the first 8 hours.
    • Monitor Urine Output hourly (Aim for 0.5-1ml/kg/hr) as the best indicator of fluid adequacy.
  • 3. Pain Management:
    • Administer IV opioids (Morphine/Fentanyl) as prescribed. Avoid IM/SC injections due to poor absorption in shock.
    • Assess pain scores frequently.
  • 4. Wound Care & Infection Control:
    • Remove constrictive clothing/jewelry.
    • Cover burns with clean/sterile sheets to prevent contamination.
    • Administer Tetanus Toxoid prophylaxis.
    • Maintain strict aseptic technique.
  • 5. Monitoring:
    • Monitor vital signs hourly (BP, Pulse, RR, Temp).
    • Monitor for circumferential chest burn effects (need for escharotomy).
