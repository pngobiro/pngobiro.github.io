Examination for Admission to the Register of Community Health Nurses (Basic)

Part I: Multiple Choice Questions

20 Marks
1.
Community diagnosis is carried out:-
a) With active participation of members of the community.
b) With active participation of the leaders in the community.
c) With active participation of health workers only.
d) By strictly looking at medical problems.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) With active participation of members of the community.

Explanation: Community diagnosis is a fundamental principle of Primary Health Care (PHC). It requires the full involvement of the community in identifying their own health problems, setting priorities, and planning solutions. It goes beyond just medical problems to include social, economic, and environmental factors.

2.
Incidence rate:-
a) Is used to record protracted diseases.
b) Shows the magnitude/burden of disease in the community.
c) Is useful for planning and administration of health care services.
d) Shows the rate of development of disease in the community.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Shows the rate of development of disease in the community.

Explanation: Incidence measures the number of new cases of a disease occurring in a population during a specified period. It effectively measures the risk or rate at which healthy people develop the disease. Options B and C describe Prevalence (total burden).

3.
The common side effect a nurse should counsel a mother for after giving her child pneumococcal vaccine is:-
a) Unusual crying.
b) Loss of appetite.
c) Diarrhoea and vomiting.
d) Blood clot at the site of injection.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Loss of appetite.

Explanation: Common side effects of the Pneumococcal Conjugate Vaccine (PCV) include injection site reactions (pain, redness, swelling), fever, irritability, drowsiness, and loss of appetite. Unusual crying is more specific to DPT, though irritability occurs in PCV.

4.
A special consideration for immunocompromised person regarding immunization is that:-
a) Asymptomatic HIV infected children should not receive BCG vaccine.
b) Live vaccines can safely be given to persons on corticosteroid therapy.
c) Asymptomatic children infected with HIV should receive all standard KEPI vaccines.
d) Pentavalent vaccine can be administered to a child with neurologic disease so long as they are monitored closely.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Asymptomatic children infected with HIV should receive all standard KEPI vaccines.

Explanation:
• Asymptomatic HIV+ children can and should receive all standard vaccines, including BCG (unless they have clinical symptoms of AIDS).
• Symptomatic HIV+ children should NOT receive BCG or Yellow Fever (live vaccines).
• Persons on high-dose corticosteroids are immunocompromised and should generally avoid live vaccines.
• Note on Option A: Standard guidelines state asymptomatic HIV children CAN receive BCG at birth. It is only contraindicated in symptomatic HIV.

5.
When constructing a septic tank for 15 users, the recommended dimensions are:-
a) Length: 300cm, Breadth: 60cm, Width/Depth: 150cm
b) Length: 200cm, Breadth: 60cm, Width/Depth: 100cm
c) Length: 360cm, Breadth: 60cm, Width/Depth: 160cm
d) Length: 450cm, Breadth: 60cm, Width/Depth: 150cm
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Length: 300cm, Breadth: 60cm, Width/Depth: 150cm

Explanation:
• 15 users generate approximately 1500-2000 liters of wastewater per day.
• A retention time of 24+ hours is needed.
• Volume Calculation for (a): $3.0m \times 0.6m \times 1.5m = 2.7 m^3$ (2,700 Liters). This provides adequate capacity for 15 users.
• Option (b) is too small (1.2 $m^3$). Options (c) and (d) are unnecessarily large for 15 users.
• Note: While 60cm breadth is narrow (standard often >75cm), mathematically Option A is the most appropriate volume.

6.
A typical case of severe cholera presents with:-
a) Profuse watery stool preceding vomiting.
b) High fever with severe abdominal cramps.
c) Vomiting preceding profuse watery stool.
d) Decreased urine production preceding profuse watery stool.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Profuse watery stool preceding vomiting.

Explanation: The classic presentation of cholera is the sudden onset of painless, profuse "rice-water" diarrhea. Vomiting typically follows the diarrhea. Fever is usually absent or low-grade. Abdominal cramps may occur from electrolyte imbalance but are not the primary feature compared to the purging diarrhea.

7.
Amoebic dysentery:-
a) Is spread by infected persons who pass infective trophozoites in feaces.
b) Presents with very severe tenesmus.
c) May present with bottle shaped ulcers on the intestinal wall.
d) Has an epidemic occurrence.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) May present with bottle shaped ulcers on the intestinal wall.

Explanation:
• Entamoeba histolytica invades the colonic mucosa creating characteristic "flask-shaped" or bottle-shaped ulcers.
• Option A is incorrect because the infective stage is the cyst, not the trophozoite (trophozoites die quickly outside the body).
• Tenesmus is present but usually less severe than in Bacillary dysentery.
• It is usually endemic/sporadic rather than epidemic (unlike Bacillary dysentery/Shigellosis).

8.
A child who presents with widening of the wrists, recurrent pneumonia and has prominent costo-chrondral joints is likely to have:-
a) Vitamin A deficiency.
b) Vitamin B deficiency.
c) Vitamin C deficiency.
d) Vitamin D deficiency.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Vitamin D deficiency.

Explanation: These are classic signs of Rickets:
• Widening of wrists (epiphyseal enlargement).
• Prominent costo-chondral joints ("Rachitic Rosary").
• Susceptibility to respiratory infections (recurrent pneumonia) due to weak chest muscles and thoracic deformity.

9.
Measurement of the mid upper arm circumference is performed :-
a) When the child's age is under one year.
b) With or without a weighing scale.
c) As a screening method for in-patients.
d) During field surveys.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) During field surveys.

Explanation: Mid-Upper Arm Circumference (MUAC) is a rapid screening tool for malnutrition primarily used in community field surveys and emergency settings for children aged 6 months to 5 years. It is independent of age (in this range) and requires only a tape measure, making it ideal for the field.

10.
Drugs that are known to reduce the efficacy of hormonal contreptives are:-
a) Artemether Lumefantrine, Rifampicin.
b) Pyrazinamide, Phenytoin.
c) Phenytoin, Rifampicin.
d) Ethambutol, co-trimoxazole.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Phenytoin, Rifampicin.

Explanation: Both Rifampicin (an anti-TB drug) and Phenytoin (an anticonvulsant) are potent liver enzyme inducers (cytochrome P450). They increase the metabolism of hormonal contraceptives, significantly reducing their effectiveness and increasing the risk of unintended pregnancy.

11.
The WHO recommended treatment of uncomplicated neisseria gonorrhoea is:-
a) Benzyl penicillin 3g intramuscularly as a single dose.
b) Ofloxacin 400 mg orally as a single dose.
c) Ceftriaxone 250 mg intramuscularly as a single dose.
d) Azithromycin 1g orally as a single dose.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Ceftriaxone 250 mg intramuscularly as a single dose.

Explanation: Due to widespread resistance to penicillins and quinolones (Ofloxacin), Ceftriaxone (a 3rd generation cephalosporin) is the recommended first-line treatment for gonorrhoea. It is often co-administered with Azithromycin or Doxycycline to treat potential Chlamydia co-infection.

12.
When managing accidental exposure to body fluids and possible HIV infection after a needle prick, the action that should be taken in correct sequence is:-
a) Wash the wound under running water with soap, take emergency ARVs, undergo HIV test.
b) Take emergency ARVs, wash the wound under running water with soap, undergo HIV test.
c) Wash the wound under running water with soap, undergo HIV test, take emergency ARVs.
d) Undergo HIV test, wash the wound under running water with soap, take emergency ARVs.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Wash the wound under running water with soap, undergo HIV test, take emergency ARVs.

Explanation:
1. First Aid: Immediately wash the site with soap and running water (do not scrub).
2. Assessment/Testing: Establish baseline HIV status of the exposed person (and source if possible).
3. PEP: Initiate Post-Exposure Prophylaxis (ARVs) if indicated, ideally within 2 hours (max 72 hours).

13.
Controlling function in management involves:-
a) Performance appraisal, quality management.
b) Determining philosophy and goals, managing action.
c) Recruiting, interviewing and orientating staff.
d) Establishing structures, grouping activities.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Performance appraisal, quality management.

Explanation: Controlling is the process of monitoring performance against goals and taking corrective action.
• Option B is Planning.
• Option C is Staffing.
• Option D is Organizing.

14.
After Alma Ata Declaration, three (3) elements of primary health that were added in Kenya are:-
a) Mental health, immunization, dental health.
b) Mental health, HIV and AIDs, Dental health.
c) Mental health, provision of essential drugs, HIV and AIDs.
d) Mental health, provision of essential drugs, immunization.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Mental health, HIV and AIDs, Dental health.

Explanation: The original Alma Ata declaration had 8 elements (Education, Food/Nutrition, Water/Sanitation, MCH/FP, Immunization, Endemic diseases, Common ailments treatment, Essential drugs). Kenya expanded this list to include Mental Health, Dental Health, and HIV/AIDS control (and sometimes Community Based Rehabilitation/Eye care) as additional pillars relevant to the local context.

15.
Community participation in organization of health services in ascending order includes:-
a) Information, discussion, consultation, full control.
b) Consultation, information, discussion, full control.
c) Consultation, discussion, information, full control.
d) Information, consultation, discussion, full control.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Information, consultation, discussion, full control.

Explanation: This follows the ladder of participation (passive to active):
1. Information: Telling people what is planned (Passive).
2. Consultation: Asking for feedback but keeping decision power.
3. Discussion/Partnership: Shared decision making.
4. Control: Community has full power over resources and decisions.

16.
Specific functions of school health include:-
a) Conducting special screening programs, treatment of cases by teachers and school nurse.
b) Determining the health status of each child, continuously appraising changes in health status of a child.
c) Continuously appraising changes in each child, case management of a sick child.
d) Determining the health status of each child, case management of a sick child.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Continuously appraising changes in each child, case management of a sick child.

Explanation: School health services aim to monitor growth and development (continuous appraisal) and intervene when illness occurs (case management/referral). It is comprehensive, covering prevention, promotion, and curative aspects.

17.
Primary prevention of occupational health care hazards includes:-
a) Emphasis on first aid to minimize complications.
b) Dealing with substances inhaled or contracted because of employee's poor attitudes.
c) General screening of non occupational illness before a person is hired.
d) Screening of the working environment to check that recommended preventive measures are put in place.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Screening of the working environment to check that recommended preventive measures are put in place.

Explanation: Primary prevention aims to prevent the injury/illness from occurring. Controlling the hazard at the source (environmental screening and engineering controls) is the most effective primary prevention strategy. First aid is secondary/tertiary.

18.
A child aged 9 – 12 months is able to:-
a) Stand without assistance and throw balls.
b) Walk without support and grasp small objects with thumb and fingers.
c) Enjoy imitation and take objects out of a container.
d) Walk and can say some sentences.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Enjoy imitation and take objects out of a container.

Explanation:
• 9-12 months: Pincer grasp, putting things in/out of containers, imitating sounds/gestures (wave bye-bye), cruising or walking with support.
• Walking alone typically occurs around 12-15 months.
• Sentences occur around 18-24 months.

19.
Indicate whether the statements are True or False.
a) Naturalistic trend assumes that illness is believed to be cured by a super natural being.
b) In the personalistic trend, treatment is normally related to the cause of misfortune resulting in illness.
Show Answer

a) False. The Naturalistic system explains illness in impersonal, systemic terms (e.g., imbalance of hot/cold, yin/yang). The Personalistic system believes illness is caused by supernatural beings or humans (witches, spirits).

b) True. In Personalistic systems, because the illness is caused by an active agent (spirit/person), treatment involves rituals or counter-magic to appease or defeat that agent.

20.
Match the conditions in column A with the correct response in column B.
COLUMN A COLUMN B
a) Measles. 1. Fever is absent.
b) Rubella. 2. Koplik's spots are not present.
3. Cough may be present.
4. Odynophagia is usually present.
5. Patient has no sub-occipital lymph nodes.
Show Answer

Matches:

  • a) Measles matches with 3: "Cough may be present." (Part of the 3 C's: Cough, Coryza, Conjunctivitis).
  • b) Rubella matches with 2: "Koplik's spots are not present." (This distinguishes it from Measles, which DOES have Koplik's spots. Rubella is also known for sub-occipital lymphadenopathy, so option 5 is incorrect for Rubella).

Part II: Short Answer Questions

40 Marks
1.
Outline six (6) universal precautions for HIV prevention among health workers. (6 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Hand Hygiene: Washing hands with soap and water or using alcohol rub before and after patient contact.
  2. Personal Protective Equipment (PPE): Using gloves, gowns, masks, and goggles when exposure to blood/fluids is anticipated.
  3. Safe Handling of Sharps: Do not recap needles; dispose of sharps immediately in puncture-proof safety boxes.
  4. Safe Waste Disposal: Proper segregation and incineration of infectious waste.
  5. Safe Injection Practices: Use of single-use, auto-disable syringes.
  6. Management of Spills: Disinfecting blood/fluid spills with 0.5% chlorine solution.
  7. Sterilization/Disinfection: Proper processing of reusable instruments.
2.
State six (6) characteristics of culture. (6 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Learned: It is acquired through socialization, not inherited biologically.
  2. Shared: It is common to a group of people.
  3. Dynamic/Changing: It evolves over time.
  4. Symbolic: Based on symbols (language, art) that carry meaning.
  5. Integrated: Different aspects (religion, economy, family) are interconnected.
  6. Adaptive: It helps people survive in their environment.
  7. Transmissible: Passed from generation to generation.
3.
List eight (8) essential components of palliative care nursing. (4 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Pain management/relief.
  2. Symptom management (nausea, vomiting, fatigue).
  3. Psychological/Emotional support.
  4. Spiritual support.
  5. Social support for patient and family.
  6. Communication and counseling.
  7. Bereavement support.
  8. End-of-life care (terminal care).
  9. Multidisciplinary team approach.
4.
Outline five (5) phases of disaster management cycle. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Mitigation/Prevention: Activities to reduce the risk or impact of a disaster.
  2. Preparedness: Planning and training before a disaster occurs.
  3. Response: Immediate actions taken during or immediately after impact (Search and Rescue).
  4. Recovery: Restoring basic services and normalcy (Rehabilitation).
  5. Reconstruction: Long-term rebuilding and improvement (building back better).
5.
State four (4) socio-economic effects of malaria to a country. (4 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Loss of Productivity/Man-hours: Workers absent due to illness reduce economic output.
  2. Strain on Health Budget: High cost of treatment and hospital bed occupancy.
  3. School Absenteeism: Affects children's education and future potential.
  4. Financial Burden on Families: Spending income on treatment and funerals instead of development.
  5. Loss of Tourism: Fear of malaria discourages visitors.
  6. Premature Death: Loss of human capital.
6.
Outline five (5) ways by which transmission of a communicable disease can be interrupted. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Treatment of Cases: Reduces the reservoir of infection.
  2. Isolation/Quarantine: Separates infected/exposed persons from the healthy.
  3. Vector Control: Destroying carriers (e.g., mosquitoes) via spraying or draining water.
  4. Immunization: Increasing host resistance (Herd immunity).
  5. Environmental Sanitation: Safe water, waste disposal (interrupts fecal-oral route).
  6. Personal Hygiene: Hand washing, covering coughs.
7.
Draw and label a diagram illustrating the life cycle of Wuchereria Bancrofti. (6 marks)
Show Answer

Description of Life Cycle (for diagram):

  1. Mosquito Bite: Infected mosquito takes a blood meal, depositing L3 larvae on skin.
  2. Entry: Larvae enter through the puncture wound.
  3. Migration: Larvae migrate to lymphatics and lymph nodes.
  4. Maturation: Develop into adult worms (takes months).
  5. Microfilariae Release: Adults produce sheathed microfilariae that circulate in blood (nocturnal periodicity).
  6. Mosquito Uptake: Mosquito ingests microfilariae during blood meal.
  7. Development in Mosquito: Microfilariae shed sheath, penetrate midgut → thoracic muscles → L1 → L2 → L3 (infective larvae).
  8. Migration to Proboscis: L3 larvae move to mosquito's head/proboscis ready to infect new host.
8.
State four (4) obstacles encountered in implementation of inter-sectoral collaboration. (4 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Conflicting Priorities: Different sectors (e.g., Agriculture vs Health) have different goals.
  2. Budgetary Constraints: Lack of joint funding mechanisms; "silo" budgeting.
  3. Poor Communication: Lack of dialogue or clear channels between departments.
  4. Lack of Political Will/Leadership: No overarching authority to enforce collaboration.
  5. Professional Jealousy/Turf Wars: Sectors protecting their own domains.
  6. Differing Policies/Procedures: Bureaucratic hurdles.

Part III: Essay/Long Answer Questions

40 Marks
1.
An outbreak of poliomyelitis has been reported to you as a nursing officer in-charge of a health centre.

a) State three (3) stages of poliomyelitis. (3 marks)
b) Describe the steps you will follow in investigating this outbreak. (12 marks)
c) Outline five (5) preventive strategies you would put in place to curb this condition. (5 marks)
Show Answer

a) Stages of Poliomyelitis:

  1. Prodromal Stage (Minor Illness): Flu-like symptoms (fever, sore throat, malaise).
  2. Pre-paralytic Stage (Major Illness): Meningeal irritation, stiffness of neck/back, muscle pain (spasms), high fever.
  3. Paralytic Stage: Flaccid paralysis (asymmetrical), loss of reflexes.

b) Steps in Investigating the Outbreak:

  1. Verify the Diagnosis: Visit the reported cases. Take history (Acute Flaccid Paralysis - AFP). Collect 2 stool samples 24-48 hours apart within 14 days of onset for lab confirmation.
  2. Confirm Existence of Epidemic: Compare observed cases with expected numbers (even one case of Polio is an outbreak).
  3. Notify Authorities: Immediately inform the District Disease Surveillance Coordinator (DDSC) and Ministry of Health.
  4. Define a Case: Use standard definition (e.g., any child <15 years with AFP).
  5. Active Case Search: Conduct house-to-house and facility-based searches to find missed cases.
  6. Data Collection & Analysis: Record details (Time, Place, Person). Map the cases to identify clusters.
  7. Identify Population at Risk: Assess immunization coverage in the area (verify cards).
  8. Formulate Hypothesis: Determine the source and mode of spread.
  9. Implement Control Measures: Start mop-up campaigns immediately (don't wait for lab results if clinical suspicion is high).
  10. Write Report: Document findings and actions taken.

c) Preventive Strategies:

  1. Routine Immunization: Ensure all children receive 4 doses of OPV (Birth, 6, 10, 14 weeks) and IPV.
  2. Supplementary Immunization Activities (SIAs): Conduct National Immunization Days (NIDs) or Mop-up campaigns in high-risk areas.
  3. AFP Surveillance: Maintain sensitive surveillance to detect and isolate cases early.
  4. Environmental Sanitation: Improve human waste disposal (latrines) to stop fecal-oral transmission.
  5. Health Education: Educate community on hygiene, importance of vaccines, and reporting paralysis.
2.
As a middle level manager at your facility, you have been tasked with organizing for patient care.

a) Explain five (5) models of nursing care delivery. (10 marks)
b) Describe how you will use the principles of management to organize nursing care delivery. (10 marks)
Show Answer

a) Models of Nursing Care Delivery:

  1. Functional Nursing: Task-oriented. Nurse A does meds, Nurse B does dressings, Nurse C does vitals. Efficient but fragmented care.
  2. Team Nursing: A team leader (RN) coordinates a group of staff (LPNs, aides) to care for a group of patients. Democratic, but relies heavily on communication.
  3. Primary Nursing: One nurse has 24-hour responsibility for a patient's care plan. Associates cover when primary is off. Holistic and high continuity, but expensive.
  4. Case Method (Total Patient Care): One nurse provides all care for one or more patients during a shift. Oldest method (ICU style). High quality, low continuity between shifts.
  5. Modular Nursing: A modification of team nursing where the unit is divided into modules/districts, and a team is assigned geographically.

b) Applying Principles of Management to Organize Care:

  1. Division of Work: Assign tasks based on qualification and competence (e.g., RNs for complex care, aides for hygiene) to increase efficiency.
  2. Unity of Command: Ensure each nurse reports to only one supervisor (e.g., the Shift In-charge) to avoid confusion.
  3. Span of Control: Ensure the team leader supervises a manageable number of staff (e.g., 4-6) for effective oversight.
  4. Delegation: Grant authority to staff to perform tasks while retaining accountability. Delegate according to the scope of practice.
  5. Scalar Chain: Establish clear lines of communication from the In-charge down to the lowest cadre for reporting problems.
  6. Unity of Direction: All nursing activities should be directed towards one goal (quality patient recovery) through a common nursing care plan.
  7. Discipline: Ensure adherence to protocols, SOPs, and shift timings for smooth operation.
  8. Order: Ensure materials (drugs, equipment) and people (staff) are in the right place at the right time (Duty Rota).
