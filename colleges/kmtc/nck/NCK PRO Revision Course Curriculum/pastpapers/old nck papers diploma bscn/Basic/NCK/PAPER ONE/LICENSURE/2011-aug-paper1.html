Part I: Multiple Choice Questions

20 Marks
1.
The cycle in which bile salts and other substances excreted by the liver are absorbed by the intestinal mucosa and returned to the liver via the portal circulation is:-
a) Extra corporeal circulation.
b) Enterohepatic circulation.
c) Collateral circulation.
d) Mesenteric circulation.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Enterohepatic circulation.

Explanation: This describes the recycling process where bile acids are excreted into the duodenum, absorbed in the terminal ileum, and returned to the liver via the portal vein for re-secretion.

2.
Gram negative bacilli include:-
a) Escherichia coli, salmonella, shigella.
b) Salmonella, staphylococcus, streptococcus.
c) Shigella, neisseria gonorrhoea, streptococci.
d) Clostridia, escherichia coli, staphylococcus.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Escherichia coli, salmonella, shigella.

Explanation:

  • Group A (Correct): All three (E. coli, Salmonella, Shigella) are gram-negative rods (bacilli).
  • Group B: Staphylococcus and Streptococcus are gram-positive cocci.
  • Group C: Neisseria is a gram-negative diplococcus; Streptococci are gram-positive.
  • Group D: Clostridia are gram-positive rods; Staph are gram-positive cocci.

3.
Complications of asthma are:-
a) Haemothorax, emphysema.
b) Chronic bronchitis, emphysema.
c) Left sided heart failure, ruptured bleb.
d) Pulmonary hypotension, acute bronchitis.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Chronic bronchitis, emphysema. (or potentially C regarding blebs/pneumothorax).

Explanation: Long-standing, poorly controlled asthma can lead to airway remodeling and chronic obstructive changes similar to COPD (Chronic Bronchitis/Emphysema). Additionally, severe acute asthma attacks can cause a ruptured bleb leading to pneumothorax. However, left-sided heart failure is not a direct complication (asthma causes cor pulmonale/right-sided failure). Between options, chronic lung changes (B) are common sequelae.

4.
When performing postural drainage for the lower lobes of the lung, the nurse positions the patient in:-
a) Sitting up/Fowler's position.
b) Reverse trendelenburg position.
c) Dorsal position with a pillow supporting the waist.
d) Lateral position with the head elevated at 30 to 45 degrees.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: Note: The options provided contain inaccuracies based on standard practice.

Explanation: To drain the lower lobes, gravity must assist drainage from the bottom of the lungs towards the trachea. This requires the Trendelenburg position (head down, feet up).

  • Option A drains apical (upper) segments.
  • Option B (Reverse Trendelenburg) puts the head up, which traps secretions in lower lobes.
  • Option D (Head elevated) also drains upper lobes.
In the absence of a correct "Trendelenburg" option, the question likely intended to ask for Upper Lobes, or meant "Prone/Supine with hips elevated" (Trendelenburg). If forced to choose in a flawed question, one might look for positions that don't sit the patient fully upright, but none listed are correct for lower lobes.

5.
The aura stage of a tonic clonic seizure is characterized by:-
a) Visual disturbance, tingling sensations, change in mood.
b) Auditory sensation, change in mood, loss of consciousness.
c) Tonic contractions, temporary cessation of breathing, staring into the space.
d) Audible cry, tonic contractions, lasts for 3 to 4 seconds.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Visual disturbance, tingling sensations, change in mood.

Explanation: An aura is a perceptual disturbance experienced by some with epilepsy before a seizure. It serves as a warning and can involve visual changes (flashes), sensory changes (smells, tingling), or mood changes.
Why others are wrong: (b) Loss of consciousness occurs after the aura. (c) Describes the seizure itself (absence or tonic phase). (d) Describes the onset of the tonic phase (epileptic cry).

6.
Clinical manifestations of hypoparathyroidism include:-
a) Hyperphosphatemia, hypophostemia.
b) Tetany, hypophosphatemia.
c) Laryngeal spasm, bronchospasm.
d) Hypercalcuria, photophobia.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Laryngeal spasm, bronchospasm.

Explanation: Hypoparathyroidism leads to low serum calcium (Hypocalcemia). Signs of hypocalcemia include neuromuscular irritability, tetany, and potentially life-threatening laryngeal spasm or bronchospasm.
Analysis of other options: (a) Contradictory terms. (b) Hypoparathyroidism causes Hyperphosphatemia (high phosphate), not hypo. (d) Hypercalciuria is typically associated with hyperparathyroidism.

7.
Management of impetigo involves:-
a) Use of systemic antibiotics.
b) Administering systemic corticosteroids.
c) Use of topical antifungal creams.
d) Treatment with opioid analgesics.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Use of systemic antibiotics. (Or topical antibiotics for mild cases).

Explanation: Impetigo is a bacterial infection (Staph/Strep). Treatment involves antibiotics (Topical Mupirocin for mild, Systemic/Oral for widespread).
Why others are wrong: (b) Steroids suppress immunity and worsen infection. (c) Antifungals treat fungal infections, not bacterial. (d) Opioids are too strong; impetigo is itchy but usually not painful enough for opioids.

8.
When caring for a patient with perineal wound, the nurse should:-
a) Remove the packing and drains within 24 hours.
b) Care for the patient in a dorsal recumbent position for the 1st 3 days.
c) Administer analgesic before irrigating the wound.
d) Remove the urinary catheter second day post-operatively.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Administer analgesic before irrigating the wound.

Explanation: Perineal wounds are extremely painful. Pre-procedural analgesia is the priority nursing intervention to ensure patient comfort and compliance during wound care/irrigation. Timing of drain/catheter removal is usually a surgeon's specific order.

9.
The main purpose of jejunojejunostomy in gastric resection is to:-
a) Prevent reflux of bile and pancreatic fluids into the oesophagus.
b) Enhance peristaltic movement.
c) Improve absorption of nutrients from the small intestines.
d) Prevent constipation.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Prevent reflux of bile and pancreatic fluids into the oesophagus.

Explanation: A jejunojejunostomy (often part of a Roux-en-Y procedure) creates a Y-connection that diverts bile and pancreatic juices further down the intestine, preventing them from flowing back up into the stomach remnant and esophagus (Alkaline Reflux Gastritis).

10.
A nephrostomy drainage is usually inserted in the:-
a) Urinary bladder.
b) Ureters of both kidneys.
c) Nephrones.
d) Renal pelvis.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Renal pelvis.

Explanation: A nephrostomy tube is a catheter inserted through the flank skin directly into the renal pelvis of the kidney to drain urine, usually when the ureter is obstructed.

11.
Transurethral incision of the prostate is indicated in management of:-
a) Cancer of the prostate.
b) Initial stages of benign prostatic hypertrophy.
c) Painful benign prostatic hypertrophy.
d) Benign prostatic hypertrophy causing complete urinary obstruction.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Initial stages of benign prostatic hypertrophy. (Usually for smaller prostates).

Explanation: Transurethral Incision of the Prostate (TUIP) is typically indicated for men with moderate symptoms and a small to moderately enlarged prostate (typically < 30g). It involves making cuts in the prostate neck to improve flow, rather than removing tissue like in TURP. It is not sufficient for massive obstruction or cancer.

12.
Risk factors of osteoporosis include:-
a) Lack of vitamin D in diet, high estrogen levels in women.
b) Too much exposure to the sun, low testosterone levels in men.
c) Increase in age, late menopause.
d) Excessive alcohol consumption, early menopause.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Excessive alcohol consumption, early menopause.

Explanation:

  • Alcohol: Inhibits bone formation.
  • Early Menopause: Results in early loss of the protective effect of estrogen on bone density.
Why others are wrong: High estrogen (a) is protective. Sun exposure (b) provides Vit D (protective). Late menopause (c) extends estrogen protection.

13.
In rheumatoid arthritis, inflammation of the joints begins in the:-
a) Hip, ankle, elbow.
b) Feet, wrist, toes.
c) Shoulder, fingers, wrist.
d) Clavicle, hands, fingers.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Feet, wrist, toes. (Small joints).

Explanation: Rheumatoid Arthritis typically presents symmetrically in the small joints of the hands (MCP, PIP joints, wrists) and feet/toes. Large joint involvement (Hips/Shoulders) usually occurs later.

14.
The main aim of escharotomy in treatment of full thickness burns is to:-
a) Improve oxygenation of the burnt tissues.
b) Remove the dead tissues.
c) Allow underlying viable tissues to expand.
d) Reduce pain.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Allow underlying viable tissues to expand.

Explanation: In full-thickness burns, the skin becomes leathery and rigid (eschar). If this occurs circumferentially around a limb or chest, edema builds up underneath, and the rigid eschar acts like a tourniquet. An escharotomy (cutting the eschar) relieves this pressure, allowing tissues to expand and restoring circulation/ventilation.

15.
Characteristics of 1st degree burns include:-
a) Erythema and pain.
b) Chilling and dry wounds.
c) Healing by scaling, painless wounds.
d) Black appearance, painless wounds.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Erythema and pain.

Explanation: A 1st-degree burn (Superficial burn), like a mild sunburn, affects only the epidermis. It is characterized by redness (erythema), blanching on pressure, mild swelling, and pain. It does not blister.

16.
In simple mastectomy:-
a) Pectoralis major muscle and the entire breast is excised.
b) Axillary lymph nodes and pectoralis major muscles are left intact.
c) Underlying chest muscles and chain of internal mammary lymph nodes are removed.
d) Entire breast and axillary lymph nodes are removed.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Axillary lymph nodes and pectoralis major muscles are left intact.

Explanation: A Simple (Total) Mastectomy involves removal of the entire breast tissue, nipple, and areola. However, unlike a Radical Mastectomy, the chest muscles (Pectoralis) and axillary lymph nodes are not removed (though a sentinel node biopsy might be done).

17.
When performing gastric lavage, the nurse should:-
a) Place the patient in the left lateral position.
b) Use a 50cc syringe to push the lavage solution into the stomach.
c) Elevate patient in bed so as to drain the lower part of the stomach.
d) Allow lavage solution to drain from the stomach by gravity.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Place the patient in the left lateral position. (Also D is part of the process, but A is the safety position).

Explanation: The left lateral decubitus position (often with head down) is critical to prevent aspiration of stomach contents into the lungs if the patient vomits during the procedure. It keeps the pylorus up, slowing gastric emptying into the intestine.

18.
Reed-sternberg cell is a pathologic hallmark essential in diagnosis of:-
a) Hodgkin's disease.
b) Acute myeloid leukemia.
c) Chronic lymphocytic leukemia.
d) Non Hodgkin's disease.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Hodgkin's disease.

Explanation: The presence of giant, multinucleated Reed-Sternberg cells in a lymph node biopsy is the definitive diagnostic marker for Hodgkin's Lymphoma. Their absence usually indicates Non-Hodgkin's Lymphoma.

20.
Indicate whether the statements are True or False.
a) Intracapsular extraction of the lens is commonly used in management of cataracts.
b) Linear extraction is performed in the elderly people in management of cataracts.
Show Answer

Answers:

  • a) False. (Historically used, but nowadays Extracapsular extraction [ECCE] or Phacoemulsification is standard to preserve the posterior capsule for lens implantation).
  • b) False. Linear extraction (or curette evacuation) is typically reserved for soft cataracts in young people/children, not the elderly who have hard nuclei.

Part II: Short Answer Questions

40 Marks
1.
Outline four (4) specific nursing roles for a patient on opioid analgesics. (4 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Monitor Respiratory Status: Check respiratory rate before and after administration. Withhold if RR < 12/min.
  2. Safety Precautions: Raise bed rails and assist with ambulation due to risk of sedation and dizziness/falls.
  3. Monitor Elimination: Opioids cause constipation; encourage fluids/fiber and monitor bowel movements. Monitor for urinary retention.
  4. Pain Assessment: Re-assess pain level 30 mins after administration to evaluate efficacy.
2.
State five (5) ways by which a nurse establishes rapport with a client. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Active Listening: Giving full attention, making eye contact, and validating the client's feelings.
  2. Proper Introduction: Introducing oneself by name and role clearly.
  3. Non-Judgmental Attitude: Showing acceptance and respect regardless of the client's background or condition.
  4. Empathy: Showing understanding of the client's situation from their perspective.
  5. Consistency and Reliability: Keeping promises and being available when needed.
3.
List six (6) clinical manifestations of cardiac tamponade. (3 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Hypotension: Low blood pressure (part of Beck's Triad).
  2. Muffled Heart Sounds: Distant sounds due to fluid (part of Beck's Triad).
  3. Distended Neck Veins (JVD): Due to high venous pressure (part of Beck's Triad).
  4. Pulsus Paradoxus: Drop in SBP >10mmHg during inspiration.
  5. Tachycardia: Compensatory mechanism.
  6. Dyspnea/Tachypnea: Shortness of breath.

5.
State six (6) nursing interventions for a patient with pelvic inflammatory disease. (6 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Positioning: Place in Semi-Fowler's position to promote pelvic drainage and localize infection.
  2. Administer Antibiotics: Ensure timely administration of broad-spectrum antibiotics as prescribed.
  3. Pain Management: Administer analgesics and apply warm compresses to the abdomen if indicated.
  4. Rest: Strict bed rest to reduce metabolic demand and limit spread of infection.
  5. Perineal Hygiene: Frequent care to remove infectious discharge.
  6. Fluid Management: Encourage oral fluids or maintain IV fluids to correct dehydration from fever.
6.
Formulate five (5) nursing diagnoses for a patient with a compound fracture of the femur. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Acute Pain related to tissue trauma and muscle spasms.
  2. Risk for Infection related to broken skin integrity and exposure of bone (compound fracture).
  3. Impaired Physical Mobility related to loss of bone integrity and traction/splinting.
  4. Risk for Fluid Volume Deficit related to hemorrhage from the fracture site.
  5. Risk for Peripheral Neurovascular Dysfunction related to tissue edema and nerve compression.
7.
Describe the physiology of respiration. (7 marks)
Show Answer

The physiology of respiration involves three main processes:

  1. Ventilation (Breathing):
    • Inspiration: Diaphragm and intercostal muscles contract, chest cavity expands, intrathoracic pressure drops, and air rushes in.
    • Expiration: Muscles relax, chest recoils, pressure increases, and air is pushed out.
  2. External Respiration (Diffusion):
    • Exchange of gases between alveoli and pulmonary capillaries. Oxygen diffuses into blood, Carbon Dioxide diffuses into alveoli based on partial pressure gradients.
  3. Internal Respiration (Tissue level):
    • Exchange of gases between systemic capillaries and body tissues. Oxygen moves into cells, CO2 moves into blood.

Control: Regulated by the Medulla Oblongata and Pons based on chemical stimuli (CO2/pH levels).

Part III: Essay/Long Answer Questions

40 Marks
1.

Mrs. M. a 45 year old patient is admitted in the surgical ward with a diagnosis of cancer of the colon and is scheduled for surgery and colostomy creation.

a) State three (3) clinical manifestations that Mrs. M. may present with. (3 marks)

b) Describe the specific nursing management of Mrs. M. post-operatively till discharge. (14 marks)

c) Outline three (3) health messages you will share with Mrs. K (Mrs. M) on discharge. (3 marks)

Show Answer

a) Clinical Manifestations:

  1. Change in Bowel Habits: Alternating constipation and diarrhea.
  2. Rectal Bleeding: Passage of bright red blood or melaena.
  3. Abdominal Pain: Cramping or gas pains.
  4. Weight Loss: Unexplained loss of weight.

b) Post-operative Management:

  1. Stoma Care:
    • Inspect stoma color regularly (Should be pink/red and moist). A dusky/black stoma indicates necrosis (Emergency).
    • Monitor skin around stoma for irritation. Apply barrier creams.
    • Measure stoma size for correct appliance fit (it will shrink over weeks).
  2. Gastrointestinal Function:
    • Monitor for return of bowel sounds and passage of flatus.
    • Note characteristics of effluent (initially mucous/serosanguinous, then liquid stool).
    • Maintain NPO until bowel function returns, then graduate diet.
  3. Pain Management:
    • Administer analgesics to facilitate deep breathing and early ambulation.
    • Splint incision during coughing.
  4. Psychological Support (Body Image):
    • Encourage patient to look at the stoma.
    • Listen to concerns about altered body image and sexuality.
    • Involve family in care.
  5. Education:
    • Teach patient/family how to empty and change the colostomy bag.

c) Health Messages on Discharge:

  1. Diet: Eat a balanced diet. Avoid gas-forming foods (cabbage, beans) initially. Chew food thoroughly.
  2. Hydration: Drink plenty of fluids (2-3L/day) to prevent constipation/dehydration.
  3. Stoma Monitoring: Report any changes in stoma color, severe skin irritation, or absence of stool for >24 hours.
2.

Master Ko 10 years old is admitted in a paediatric ward with a diagnosis of acute glomerulonephritis.

a) Define glomerulonephritis. (1 mark)

b) Describe the pathophysiology of acute glomerulonephritis. (4 marks)

c) State three (3) clinical manifestations that Master Ko may present with. (3 marks)

d) Describe the specific management of Master Ko till discharge. (12 marks)

Show Answer

a) Definition:

Acute Glomerulonephritis (AGN) is an inflammation of the glomerular capillaries, typically following a streptococcal infection of the throat or skin, characterized by hematuria, edema, and hypertension.

b) Pathophysiology:

  1. Antigen-Antibody Reaction: Following a Strep infection (Group A Beta-hemolytic), antigen-antibody complexes form in the blood.
  2. Deposition: These complexes become trapped in the glomerular basement membrane.
  3. Inflammation: This triggers an inflammatory response causing proliferation of endothelial cells and infiltration of leukocytes.
  4. Reduced Filtration: The glomeruli become swollen and blocked, reducing the Glomerular Filtration Rate (GFR).
  5. Result: Retention of sodium and water (Edema/HTN) and leakage of RBCs/Protein (Hematuria/Proteinuria).

c) Clinical Manifestations:

  1. Periorbital Edema: Puffiness around eyes, especially in the morning.
  2. Hematuria: Tea-colored or cola-colored urine.
  3. Hypertension: Elevated blood pressure.
  4. Oliguria: Decreased urine output.

d) Specific Management:

  • 1. Rest: Strict bed rest during the acute phase (until hematuria/HTN subsides) to conserve energy and reduce metabolic waste.
  • 2. Fluid Management:
    • Fluid restriction may be necessary if edema/oliguria is severe.
    • Strict Input/Output monitoring. daily weights.
  • 3. Dietary Management:
    • Low Sodium Diet: To control edema and hypertension.
    • Low Protein Diet: If azotemia (high BUN) is severe, to reduce nitrogenous waste.
    • Potassium Restriction: If oliguria is present (risk of hyperkalemia).
  • 4. Medication Administration:
    • Antihypertensives: (e.g., Hydralazine) to control BP.
    • Diuretics: (e.g., Furosemide) to reduce fluid overload.
    • Antibiotics: (Penicillin) if persistent strep infection is present.
  • 5. Monitoring:
    • Monitor BP q 4h (Risk of hypertensive encephalopathy).
    • Monitor urine for protein and blood.
