Part I: Multiple Choice Questions

20 Marks
1.
The trophoblastic layer responsible for nourishing the growing organism during embedment process is:-
a) Mesoderm.
b) Syncytiotrophoblast.
c) Cytotrophoblast.
d) Ectoderm.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Syncytiotrophoblast.

Explanation: The trophoblast differentiates into two layers: the inner cytotrophoblast and the outer syncytiotrophoblast. The syncytiotrophoblast invades the endometrial wall, eroding maternal blood vessels to establish the uteroplacental circulation, thus providing nourishment to the embryo during implantation.

2.
Client Mi’s weight is 50 kgs at 20 weeks gestation. At 36 weeks she will weigh approximately:-
a) 58 kgs.
b) 60 kgs.
c) 62 kgs.
d) 64 kgs.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) 58 kgs.

Explanation: In the second half of pregnancy, the average weight gain is approximately 0.5 kg (1 lb) per week. From 20 weeks to 36 weeks is a duration of 16 weeks.
Calculation: 16 weeks × 0.5 kg/week = 8 kg.
Final weight = 50 kg + 8 kg = 58 kg.

3.
Mrs Ra, para 0+0 gravida 1 visits antenatal clinic with complaints of increased vaginal discharge. The specific intervention would include:-
a) Advice her to bring her partner for investigations, perform urinalysis.
b) Exclude possibility of infection, advice on hygiene.
c) Advice her to start douching, commence antifungal treatment.
d) Advice her to avoid tight inner wears, commence antibacterial therapy.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Exclude possibility of infection, advice on hygiene.

Explanation: Increased vaginal discharge (leukorrhea) is physiological in pregnancy due to high estrogen levels. However, it is crucial to first exclude pathological causes (candidiasis, trichomoniasis, BV) before attributing it to pregnancy. Hygiene advice is appropriate. Blind treatment (c/d) is incorrect without diagnosis.

4.
The immediate action that should be taken when a mother develops respiratory distress after administration of magnesium sulphate is:-
a) Summon for help, give atropine.
b) Give oxygen 4 litres/min, prop up the patient.
c) Summon for help, administer 10 mls of 10% calcium gluconate i.v. slowly.
d) Prop up the patient, summon for help.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Summon for help, administer 10 mls of 10% calcium gluconate i.v. slowly.

Explanation: Respiratory depression is a sign of Magnesium Sulphate toxicity. The specific antidote is Calcium Gluconate, which antagonizes the effects of magnesium. It must be given slowly IV. Help is required immediately.

5.
The daily carbohydrate requirements for a Type 2 diabetes mellitus mother should be increased after delivery by:-
a) 10 – 20 gms.
b) 20 – 30 gms.
c) 30 – 40 gms.
d) 40 – 50 gms.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) 30 – 40 gms.

Explanation: Lactating mothers generally require an additional 500 kcal per day. The carbohydrate allowance specifically is often increased by about 30-50g to support milk production and prevent hypoglycemia, especially if on insulin, though strict control is still needed for diabetics.

6.
The appropriate interventions to minimize mother to child transmission of HIV during vaginal delivery include:-
a) Avoid perineal tears, wipe the baby immediately.
b) Avoid artificial rupture of membranes, rule out urinary tract infection.
c) Shorten time during which membranes are ruptured to less than 4 hours, use chlorhexidine for vulval and vaginal cleaning before vaginal examination.
d) Minimize number of vaginal examinations, rule out urinary tract infection.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Shorten time during which membranes are ruptured to less than 4 hours, use chlorhexidine for vulval and vaginal cleaning before vaginal examination.

Explanation: Prolonged rupture of membranes (>4 hours) significantly increases the risk of vertical transmission. Chlorhexidine vaginal cleansing is a recommended intervention in some protocols (specifically previous NCK guidelines) to reduce viral load in the canal. Avoiding ARM and minimizing VEs are also correct strategies included in broader PMTCT guidelines.

7.
The importance of assessing the state of the cervix during labour is:-
a) To determine adequacy of the pelvis.
b) For accurate measurement of the cervix.
c) To determine fetal presentation.
d) To determine if the woman meets criteria for implementation of partogram.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) To determine if the woman meets criteria for implementation of partogram.

Explanation: The partogram is commenced only when the woman is in the Active Phase of labor. The definition of active phase is cervical dilation of 4cm or more. Therefore, assessing the cervix determines if active labor has begun and the partogram should be started.

8.
On diagnosis of fetal distress, the fluid of choice to administer is:-
a) 1 litre of 5% dextrose for 30 minutes, then evaluate foetal well being.
b) 500 mls of 10% dextrose for one hour, followed by 500 mls of normal saline.
c) 1 litre of normal saline for 30 minutes, then evaluate foetal heart rate.
d) 50 mls of 50% dextrose as bolus then 5% dextrose for two hours.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) 1 litre of normal saline for 30 minutes, then evaluate foetal heart rate.

Explanation: Maternal hydration (IV loading) helps improve uterine perfusion and oxygen delivery to the fetus. Normal Saline or Ringer's Lactate is preferred over Dextrose, as large amounts of Dextrose can cause maternal hyperglycemia and fetal rebound hypoglycemia/acidosis.

9.
Following administration of epidural anaesthesia during labour, the blood pressure should be recorded every:-
a) 5 minutes for the initial 20 minutes, then at 15 minutes interval.
b) 10 minutes for the first 2 hours, then half hourly.
c) 15 minutes until the anaesthesia wears off.
d) 10 minutes until the blood pressure reading is satisfactory.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) 5 minutes for the initial 20 minutes, then at 15 minutes interval.

Explanation: Hypotension is a very common and immediate side effect of epidural anesthesia due to sympathetic blockade. Intensive monitoring (every 5 mins) is required immediately after the bolus to detect and treat drops in BP quickly.

10.
Mrs. Wi a previous caesarean section mother para 2+0 gravida 3 comes to your health facility at 36 weeks gestation with complaints of intermittent right abdominal pain for three days. The probable diagnosis would be:-
a) Ruptured abdominal ectopic pregnancy.
b) Impending rupture of uterus.
c) Silent rupture of the uterus.
d) Acute abdomen.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Impending rupture of uterus. (Or possibly 'Silent' scar dehiscence, but pain suggests impending issue).

Explanation: In a woman with a previous scar, abdominal pain (especially localized over the scar area) is a classic sign of scar dehiscence or impending rupture. "Intermittent" may relate to contractions or movement stressing the scar. Ectopic (a) is impossible at 36 weeks. Acute abdomen (d) is too vague. The history of C/S makes rupture the primary differential.

11.
Indicate whether the statements are true or false.
a) Immediately after delivery of the 1st twin, an abdominal examination is done and if the presentation is cephalic, the mother is encouraged to push.
b) After delivery of the 2nd twin, the midwife should observe for signs of placenta separation before giving uterotonic agents.
Show Answer

Answers:

  • a) False: After the first twin, abdominal exam is done to rule out transverse lie, but one should usually wait for contractions to resume before pushing, unless fetal distress is present. Pushing without contractions is ineffective. However, active management often involves rupturing membranes if cephalic and contractions are present. The key is confirming lie and presentation first.
  • b) True: Uterotonic agents (Oxytocin/Ergometrine) should generally be withheld until after the delivery of the last fetus to prevent entrapment of the remaining twin. Once the second twin is born, active management (oxytocin) is given.
12.
The physical examination done on a newborn baby during a home visit on 3rd day of life includes:-
a) Temperature, respiration, skin turgor and jaundice.
b) Breast engorgement, weight gain, range of movement.
c) Pus swab for culture and sensitivity, feeding pattern, wake and sleep pattern.
d) Ecchymatic mark, colour and odour of stool, weight gain.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Temperature, respiration, skin turgor and jaundice.

Explanation: Day 3 is a critical time for:
1. Jaundice: Physiological jaundice peaks around day 3-4.
2. Dehydration/Feeding: Skin turgor checks hydration status.
3. Sepsis: Temp and Respiration check for infection.
Weight loss is common on day 3, not gain (b/d). Pus swabs (c) are only if infection is present.

13.
The apgar score for the baby born at term with heart rate 110 bpm, gasping respiration, blue extremities, some flexion of limbs and minimal grimace at 1 minute is:-
a) 10
b) 8
c) 6
d) 4
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) 6

Calculation:
- Heart Rate (110 bpm): >100 = 2
- Respiration (Gasping): Slow/Irregular = 1
- Color (Blue extremities): Acrocyanosis = 1
- Muscle Tone (Some flexion): 1
- Reflex Irritability (Minimal grimace): 1
Total = 6

14.
The amount of 3 hourly feeds appropriate for a baby whose weight is 1600 grams on day two is:-
a) 12 mls.
b) 14 mls.
c) 16 mls.
d) 18 mls.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) 16 mls.

Explanation:
- Weight: 1.6 kg.
- Fluid requirement for Day 2 (VLBW): ~80 ml/kg/day.
- Total daily volume: 1.6 kg × 80 ml = 128 ml.
- Feeding frequency: 3 hourly = 24 / 3 = 8 feeds/day.
- Amount per feed: 128 ml ÷ 8 feeds = 16 ml.

16.
The common complications of a light for dates baby include:-
a) Polycythaemia, congenital abnormalities, fetal alcohol syndrome.
b) Infection, meconium aspiration, hypoglycaemia.
c) Twin to twin transfusion, asphyxia, poor temperature control.
d) Polycythemia, meconium aspiration, twin to twin transfusion.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Infection, meconium aspiration, hypoglycaemia.

Explanation: Light for dates (SGA/IUGR) babies are prone to:
- Hypoglycemia: Low glycogen stores.
- Meconium Aspiration: Due to intrauterine stress/hypoxia.
- Hypothermia: Lack of fat (poor temp control).
- Polycythemia: Response to chronic hypoxia.
Option B captures three major complications (Infection is also a risk due to immune status). Option D has TTTS which is a cause, not a general complication of all SGA.

17.
The immediate interventions following development of urine retention after delivery include:-
a) Running tap water, sitting in a bath tab.
b) Catheterization, administering analgesics.
c) Applying warm compresses on perineum, catheterization.
d) Sitz bath, applying warm compresses on perineum.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Running tap water, sitting in a bath tab.

Explanation: Initial management should always be non-invasive to stimulate the voiding reflex (sensory stimulation like running water, privacy, warm bath). Catheterization (b/c) is a last resort due to infection risk.

18.
The role of the midwife in relieving after pain during peurperium includes:-
a) Administering appropriate analgesics, restricting ambulation.
b) Encouraging bed rest, advising the mother not to breastfeed until pains reduce.
c) Administering an enema, restricting ambulation.
d) Encouraging frequent urination, administering appropriate analgesics.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Encouraging frequent urination, administering appropriate analgesics.

Explanation:
- Empty Bladder: A full bladder displaces the uterus and interferes with contraction, increasing pain.
- Analgesics: Paracetamol/NSAIDs are standard.
- Ambulation is good (prevents DVT). Breastfeeding causes pain (oxytocin) but should NOT be discouraged.

19.
Still births and deaths that occur in the first week of life are classified as:-
a) Neonatal deaths.

2.
State six (6) probable signs of pregnancy. (6 marks)
Show Answer

Probable Signs:

  1. Hegar’s Sign: Softening of the lower uterine segment.
  2. Goodell’s Sign: Softening of the cervix.
  3. Chadwick’s Sign (Jacquemier's): Bluish discoloration of the vagina/cervix.
  4. Ballottement: Passive movement of the unengaged fetus.
  5. Positive Pregnancy Test: Detection of HCG in urine/blood (can be caused by tumors).
  6. Abdominal Enlargement: Progressive growth of the uterus.
  7. Braxton Hicks Contractions: Painless uterine contractions.
3.
Outline six (6) specific interventions recommended for a rhesus negative mother during the antenatal period. (6 marks)
Show Answer

Interventions:

  1. Blood Grouping & Screening: Confirm Rh status and screen partner's blood group.
  2. Antibody Screening (Indirect Coombs Test): Check for sensitization (presence of antibodies) at booking and 28 weeks.
  3. Prophylactic Anti-D: Administer anti-D immunoglobulin at 28 and 34 weeks gestation if non-sensitized.
  4. Manage Sensitizing Events: Administer Anti-D within 72 hours of any event causing fetomaternal hemorrhage (e.g., amniocentesis, APH, abdominal trauma).
  5. Education: Educate mother on the importance of reporting bleeding or trauma immediately.
  6. Planning Delivery: Plan for hospital delivery where blood transfusion and neonatal care are available.
4.
State five (5) pillars of safe motherhood. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Pillars of Safe Motherhood:

  1. Family Planning: To ensure individuals and couples have the information and services to plan the timing, number, and spacing of pregnancies.
  2. Antenatal Care: To monitor pregnancy and detect complications early.
  3. Clean/Safe Delivery: Care by a skilled birth attendant.
  4. Essential Obstetric Care (EOC): Availability of emergency care for complications.
  5. Postnatal Care: Care for mother and baby after birth.
  6. (Bonus: Equity for Women / Primary Health Care).
5.
Outline six (6) causes of acute uterine inversion. (6 marks)
Show Answer

Causes/Risk Factors:

  1. Mismanagement of Third Stage: Pulling on the umbilical cord (Cord traction) when the uterus is relaxed/atonic.
  2. Fundal Pressure: Applying pressure on the fundus while the uterus is relaxed.
  3. Short Umbilical Cord: Absolute or relative shortness pulling the fundus down during descent.
  4. Adherent Placenta (Accreta): Placenta fails to separate and pulls the wall down.
  5. Uterine Atony: A relaxed, thin uterus is easier to invert.
  6. Sudden Emptying: Rapid delivery of a large baby or polyhydramnios.
  7. Manual Removal of Placenta: Improper technique withdrawing the hand.

  3. Breathing Techniques: Deep breathing, panting.

Show Answer

Key Interventions:

  1. Monitor Vital Signs: BP, Pulse, Temperature to detect hemorrhage or infection.
  2. Check Involution: Palpate uterine fundus to ensure regression.
  3. Monitor Lochia: Check amount, color, and odor.
  4. Support Breastfeeding: Ensure proper latching and prevent breast complications.
  5. Perineal/Wound Care: Inspect episiotomy/CS wound for healing.

  8. Health Education: Nutrition, hygiene, danger signs.
  9. Vitamin A Supplementation: Administer 200,000 IU.

Part III: Essay/Long Answer Questions

1.
Mrs. May para 4+0 is brought to your health facility seven (7) days after home delivery with complaints of per vaginal bleeding.

a) Define post partum haemorrhage. (2 marks)

Show Answer

Post Partum Haemorrhage (PPH):

PPH is defined as blood loss from the genital tract of 500ml or more following a vaginal delivery (or 1000ml following a Caesarean section), or any amount of blood loss that causes hemodynamic instability (deterioration in maternal condition) within the puerperium (up to 6 weeks).

(Note: Mrs. May has Secondary PPH occurring after 24 hours).

b) Explain the physiological changes of lochia. (6 marks)

Show Answer

Lochia Changes:

  1. Lochia Rubra (Red):
    • Duration: Days 1-4.
    • Composition: Blood, shreds of decidua, chorion, amniotic fluid, lanugo.
    • Appearance: Red color (like menstruation).
  2. Lochia Serosa (Pink/Brown):
    • Duration: Days 5-9.
    • Composition: Fewer red blood cells, more leucocytes, wound exudate, mucus, microorganisms.
    • Appearance: Pinkish or brownish color.
  3. Lochia Alba (White/Yellow):
    • Duration: Days 10-14 (up to 3-6 weeks).
    • Composition: Leucocytes, decidual cells, mucus, bacteria, epithelial cells.
    • Appearance: Creamy white or yellowish.

c) Describe the specific management of Mrs. May till bleeding is controlled. (12 marks)

Show Answer

Management of Secondary PPH:

  1. Admission & Resuscitation: Admit immediately. Assess ABCs. If in shock, elevate legs, give oxygen.
  2. IV Access & Fluids: Insert large-bore cannula (16G). Start IV fluids (Crystalloids/Blood) to restore volume. Take blood for grouping, cross-match, and Hb.
  3. Rub up Contraction: If the uterus is palpable and boggy, massage it to expel clots and stimulate contraction.
  4. Administer Uterotonics:
    • Oxytocin IV/IM or Ergometrine (if no hypertension) to contract the uterus.
    • Misoprostol can be used.
  5. Antibiotics: Secondary PPH is often caused by infection (Endometritis). Start broad-spectrum antibiotics (e.g., Ampicillin, Gentamicin, Metronidazole) IV.
  6. Empty Bladder: Catheterize to ensure the bladder doesn't inhibit uterine contraction.
  7. Exploration/Evacuation: If bleeding persists and retained products are suspected (common after home delivery), arrange for Manual Vacuum Aspiration (MVA) or Examination Under Anesthesia (EUA) to remove retained placental tissue.
  8. Monitoring: Monitor BP, Pulse, bleeding, and urine output strictly.
2.
Mrs. Hii para 1+0 gravida 2, comes to labour ward in active phase of labour and progresses well giving birth to a live full term male infant.

a) Describe the physiological changes that take place in the circulatory system of an infant immediately the cord is clamped. (6 marks)

Show Answer

Circulatory Changes at Birth:

  1. Closure of Umbilical Arteries and Vein: Clamping stops placental flow, increasing systemic vascular resistance.
  2. Closure of Ductus Venosus: With cessation of umbilical flow, the shunt through the liver closes, forcing blood through the liver sinusoids.
  3. Closure of Foramen Ovale: First breath expands lungs -> Pulmonary vascular resistance drops -> Increased pulmonary blood flow -> Increased return to Left Atrium -> Left Atrial pressure exceeds Right Atrial pressure -> Functional closure of the flap valve.
  4. Closure of Ductus Arteriosus: Increased oxygen concentration in blood and drop in prostaglandins cause constriction of the muscular wall of the ductus arteriosus, diverting blood to the lungs.

b) Describe how you would assess the state of the neuromuscular system of Baby Hii. (12 marks)

Show Answer

Neuromuscular Assessment (Ballard/Dubowitz Score):

  1. Posture: Observe the baby at rest. A term baby should have full flexion of arms and legs.
  2. Square Window (Wrist): Flex the hand on the forearm. In a term baby, the palm lies flat against the forearm (0 degree angle).
  3. Arm Recoil: Flex arms for 5 seconds, then extend and release. Term baby's arms recoil briskly to flexion.
  4. Popliteal Angle: Flex thigh to abdomen, extend leg at knee. Term baby has resistance and angle is small (<90 degrees).
  5. Scarf Sign: Pull arm across chest. In term baby, elbow does not cross the midline.
  6. Heel to Ear: Attempt to bring foot to ear. In term baby, strong resistance prevents this.
  7. Reflexes: Check primitive reflexes: Moro, Grasp (Palmar/Plantar), Sucking, Rooting. All should be present and strong.

c) List four (4) abnormalities that can be found on the genitalia of a male infant during first examination. (2 marks)

Show Answer

Abnormalities:

  1. Undescended Testes (Cryptorchidism): Testes not palpable in scrotum.
  2. Hypospadias: Urethral opening on the underside of the penis.
  3. Epispadias: Urethral opening on the upper aspect.
  4. Hydrocele: Fluid collection in the scrotum.
  5. Inguinal Hernia.
  6. Ambiguous Genitalia.
