PAPER THREE

Examination for Admission to the Register of Community Health Nurses (Basic)

Instructions

  • All questions are compulsory.
  • For Part I (M.C.Q's): Write your answers in capital letters on the space provided.
  • For Part II (Short Answer): Answers should follow each other on the space provided.
  • For Part III (Essay): Answers must be written on the specified pages.
  • Each M.C.Q. is 1 mark.

Part I: Multiple Choice Questions

20 Marks
1.
An example of a disease that does not pass from human to human is:-
a) Tuberculosis.
b) Diphtheria.
c) Tetanus.
d) Malaria.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Tetanus.

Explanation: Tetanus is caused by Clostridium tetani spores found in the environment (soil, dust, manure). It is non-communicable, meaning it cannot be transmitted from person to person. Tuberculosis and Diphtheria are directly communicable. Malaria involves a human reservoir but requires a mosquito vector; however, Tetanus is the classic example of an infectious, non-communicable disease.

2.
In the treatment of tuberculosis "DOT" means:-
a) Patient getting TB treatment as close to home as possible.
b) Patient adheres to treatment during the intensive phase only.
c) Directly observed treatment with weekly monitoring.
d) Patient takes every drug dosage under supervision during the intensive phase.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Patient takes every drug dosage under supervision during the intensive phase.

Explanation: DOTS (Directly Observed Treatment, Short-course) involves a health worker or designated community member watching the patient swallow their medication to ensure adherence, particularly during the critical intensive phase of treatment.

3.
All health care services and activities within the district are monitored and supervised by the district:-
a) Health management team.
b) The Nursing Officers.
c) Medical Officer of Health.
d) Clinical Officer.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Health management team.

Explanation: The District Health Management Team (DHMT), now typically the Sub-County Health Management Team (SCHMT) in the devolved system, is responsible for the overall planning, monitoring, and supervision of health services in the area.

4.
Triaging may best be defined as the process of:-
a) Assessing all incoming casualties, of a disaster.
b) Initiating diagnostic measures for casualties of a disaster.
c) Determining the urgency of need of care for casualties of a disaster.
d) Setting priorities and assigning urgency ratings for casualties of a disaster.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Setting priorities and assigning urgency ratings for casualties of a disaster.

Explanation: Triage is derived from the French word "trier" (to sort). It is the process of sorting patients based on the severity of their condition to maximize the number of survivors with limited resources.

5.
Non-organic growth failure (failure to thrive) in a child may be attributed to:-
a) A poor mother-child bond of attachment.
b) Altered physiology due to improper feeding.
c) Congenital disorders of metabolism.
d) High incidence of child abuse.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) A poor mother-child bond of attachment.

Explanation: Non-organic failure to thrive is caused by psychosocial or environmental factors rather than a medical condition. A poor attachment or emotional deprivation can lead to poor feeding and growth issues.

6.
The acid-generating bacterium in our mouth that is the most common cause of tooth decay (dental caries) is the:-
a) Streptococcus viridans.
b) Neisseria catarrhalis.
c) Streptococcus mutans.
d) Staphylococcus aureus.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Streptococcus mutans.

Explanation: Streptococcus mutans metabolizes sugars to produce acid, which demineralizes tooth enamel, leading to dental caries.

7.
The term "role" may be defined as:-
a) Community's expectation of a trained person.
b) Legal and moral binding of a professional.
c) Capacity to run health care activities.
d) Activities one is expected to perform as a professional.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Activities one is expected to perform as a professional.

Explanation: A role is the set of behaviors, rights, obligations, beliefs, and norms as conceptualized by people in a social situation. In a professional context, it refers to the activities and responsibilities expected of that position.

8.
Currently in Kenya the problems associated with old age are made worse by the:-
a) Early retirement and meager pension.
b) Inability to acquire new marketable skills.
c) Poor health, poverty and dependency.
d) Disintegration of traditional social support systems.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Disintegration of traditional social support systems.

Explanation: While all options contribute, the collapse of the extended family structure (traditional support system) due to urbanization and socio-economic changes has significantly exacerbated the vulnerability of the elderly in Kenya.

9.
A major cause of "missed opportunity" in the immunization programme is:-
a) Failure of the cold chain.
b) Use of vaccines that have lost potency.
c) High drop-out of immunizable target group.
d) Vaccine stock out.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Vaccine stock out.

Explanation: A missed opportunity occurs when a child or woman eligible for vaccination visits a health facility but does not receive the vaccine. Vaccine stock-outs are a primary logistical cause. Note: Provider factors (like refusing to open a vial for one child) are also common causes.

10.
The process in community diagnosis in which data is separated and categorized in order to help one understand its meaning is known as data:-
a) Handling.
b) Analysis.
c) Cleaning.
d) Presentation.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Analysis.

Explanation: Data analysis involves inspecting, cleansing, transforming, and modeling data to discover useful information. The specific act of separating and categorizing to find meaning is the core of analysis.

11.
Vitamins found in the watery parts of the foods we eat:-
a) Vit K, Niacin, Riboflavin, panthonic acid.
b) Pantothenic acid, Thiamine, Pyridoxine, Vit E.
c) Vit K, Pantothenic acid, Thiamine, Pyridoxine.
d) Folate, Cyanocobalamine, Thiamine, Riboflavin.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Folate, Cyanocobalamine, Thiamine, Riboflavin.

Explanation: This question asks for water-soluble vitamins.
• Vitamin C and the B-complex vitamins are water-soluble.
• Vitamins A, D, E, and K are fat-soluble.
Option D contains Folate (B9), Cyanocobalamin (B12), Thiamine (B1), and Riboflavin (B2), which are all water-soluble.

12.
Essential amino acids can be defined as amino acids that:-
a) Cannot be made by the body.
b) Body can make if nitrogen is available.
c) Are unique in their primary structure.
d) Are the basic building blocks of proteins.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Cannot be made by the body.

Explanation: Essential amino acids cannot be synthesized by the organism at a speed commensurate with its demand, and thus must be supplied in the diet.

13.
The dosage of drugs in primary level ARV prophylaxis for prevention of mother-to-child transmission of the HIV is:-
a) Nevirapine (NVP) 200mg to mother at onset of labour, infant NVP 2mg/kg as soon as possible within 72 hours of delivery.
b) Nevirapine (NVP) 200mg to mother during labour, infant NVP 2mg/kg immediately after birth plus 4 weeks of AZT 4mg/kg B.D.
c) AZT 300mg BD plus NVP 200mg to mother during labour, infant 2mg/kg after birth plus AZT 4mg/kg BD for one week.
d) AZT 600mg to mother at onset of labour, infant NVP 2mg/kg immediately after birth.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Nevirapine (NVP) 200mg to mother at onset of labour, infant NVP 2mg/kg as soon as possible within 72 hours of delivery.

Note: This reflects the older "Single Dose NVP" regimen. Protocols have since evolved (e.g., Option B+), but in the context of this 2014 paper and "primary level" basic prophylaxis history, Option A describes the classic single-dose intervention.

14.
Water-related diseases include:-
a) Typhoid, malaria, cholera.
b) Malaria, cholera, schistosomiasis.
c) Cholera, schistosomiasis, diarrhoea.
d) Malaria, schistosomiasis, onchocerciasis.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Malaria, cholera, schistosomiasis.

Explanation: This list covers the spectrum of water-related classifications:
• Cholera: Water-borne (ingested).
• Schistosomiasis: Water-based (snails in water).
• Malaria: Water-related insect vector (mosquitoes breed in water).

15.
When health care is geographically, financially and culturally within easy reach to the entire community the service is said to be:-
a) Available to the community.
b) Acceptable to the community.
c) Accessible to the community.
d) Affordable to the community.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Accessible to the community.

Explanation: Accessibility encompasses three dimensions: Physical (geographical), Economic (financial/affordability), and Socio-cultural (acceptability).

16.
The purpose of a screening test is to:-
a) Diagnose persons with the disease.
b) Detect persons with risk factors of sub-clinical disease.
c) Check whether a population is at risk of a disease.
d) Collect the data for a mass campaign.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Detect persons with risk factors of sub-clinical disease.

Explanation: Screening is not diagnostic. Its purpose is to identify unrecognized disease or defects in an apparently healthy population (sub-clinical phase) so they can be referred for diagnosis and treatment.

17.
Herd immunity may be said to exist in a community when:-
a) Both dead and live antigens are used to immunize.
b) Vaccination is stopped in a community that has been well vaccinated.
c) The incidence of the disease decrease to a very low level.
d) A high percentage of people are immune to an infection.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) A high percentage of people are immune to an infection.

Explanation: Herd immunity occurs when a significant portion of a population becomes immune to an infectious disease, thereby providing indirect protection to those who are not immune.

18.
Emergency hormonal contraception is usually effective following unprotected sex when taken within a period of:-
a) 120 hours.
b) 72 hours.
c) 24 hours.
d) 12 hours.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) 72 hours.

Explanation: Standard Levonorgestrel-based ECPs (like Postinor-2) are most effective when taken within 72 hours (3 days) of unprotected intercourse. Ulipristal acetate can be effective up to 120 hours, but 72 hours is the standard answer for common ECPs.

19.
Indicate whether the statements are True or False.
a) Ascribed status is apposition in the community that one gains on the basis of merit or achievement.
b) Gender equity is whereby men and women are treated in the same way by both law and culture.
Show Answer

a) False. Ascribed status is assigned at birth or involuntarily later in life (e.g., race, sex, age), not gained by merit. Merit-based status is "Achieved status".

b) False. This definition describes Gender Equality. Gender Equity refers to fairness of treatment for women and men, according to their respective needs. This may include equal treatment or treatment that is different but considered equivalent in terms of rights/benefits.

Part II: Short Answer Questions

40 Marks
1.
Write short notes on:
i) Probability sampling method. (3 marks)
ii) Non-probability sampling method. (3 marks)
Show Answer

i) Probability Sampling Method:

  • A sampling technique where every member of the population has a known, non-zero chance of being selected.
  • It relies on random selection to ensure representativeness.
  • Examples include Simple Random Sampling, Stratified Random Sampling, and Cluster Sampling.
  • Allows for statistical generalization to the larger population.

ii) Non-probability Sampling Method:

  • A sampling technique where samples are selected based on the subjective judgment of the researcher rather than random selection.
  • Not all members of the population have a chance of being selected.
  • Examples include Convenience Sampling, Purposive Sampling, and Quota Sampling.
  • Used for exploratory research, qualitative studies, or when a random sample is not feasible.
2.
List:
a) The scientific name of the commonest organisms that cause:- (2 marks)
i) Visceral leishmaniasis.
ii) Malaria.
iii) Typhoid fever.
iv) Plague.
b) Four (4) types of bacterial food poisoning. (2 marks)
Show Answer

a) Scientific Names:

  1. Visceral leishmaniasis: Leishmania donovani
  2. Malaria: Plasmodium falciparum (commonest/most severe)
  3. Typhoid fever: Salmonella typhi
  4. Plague: Yersinia pestis

b) Types of Bacterial Food Poisoning (Causative Agents):

  • Salmonella food poisoning (Salmonellosis)
  • Staphylococcal food poisoning (Staphylococcus aureus)
  • Botulism (Clostridium botulinum)
  • Clostridium perfringens food poisoning
  • Bacillus cereus food poisoning
3.
Outline the six (6) steps of problem solving process in (a sequential order). (6 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Identify and Define the Problem: Clearly state what the issue is.
  2. Analyze the Problem: Gather data and determine the root causes.
  3. Develop Potential Solutions/Alternatives: Brainstorm possible ways to address the problem.
  4. Select the Best Solution: Evaluate alternatives and choose the most feasible/effective one.
  5. Implement the Solution: Put the chosen plan into action.
  6. Evaluate the Results: Monitor outcomes to see if the problem has been resolved.
4.
Briefly discuss the clinical benefits of anti-retroviral treatment (ARV) to the HIV-infected person. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  • Viral Load Suppression: Reduces the amount of HIV in the body to undetectable levels.
  • Immune System Restoration: Increases CD4 cell count, restoring the body's ability to fight infections.
  • Reduction in Opportunistic Infections: Decreases the incidence of OIs like TB, PCP, and candidiasis.
  • Improved Quality of Life: Reduces HIV-related symptoms, allowing the patient to live a normal, productive life.
  • Prolonged Survival: Significantly increases life expectancy.
  • Prevention of Transmission: Reduces the risk of transmitting the virus to sexual partners and from mother to child (Treatment as Prevention).
5.
As the community health nurse in-charge of a health centre briefly discuss the process of hospital waste management. (5 marks)
Show Answer

The process involves a chain of events from generation to final disposal:

  1. Segregation: Separating waste at the point of generation into categories (e.g., infectious/red bin, sharps/safety box, general/black bin) to prevent cross-contamination.
  2. Collection and Storage: Using appropriate, color-coded containers that are leak-proof and not overfilled. Storing in a designated, secure area before disposal.
  3. Transportation: Moving waste from storage to the disposal site using dedicated trolleys or vehicles, ensuring no spillage.
  4. Treatment: Making the waste safe before final disposal. Methods include incineration (burning) for infectious waste, autoclaving (sterilizing), or chemical disinfection.
  5. Final Disposal: Safe burial of treated waste/ash in an ash pit or sanitary landfill, and placenta pit for organic waste.
6.
Outline five (5) activities that are carried out during school health service. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Health Assessment/Screening: Routine physical exams, vision/hearing tests, and dental checks to detect defects early.
  2. Health Education: Teaching students about hygiene, nutrition, sexual health, and disease prevention.
  3. Control of Communicable Diseases: Immunization programs and deworming exercises.
  4. First Aid and Emergency Care: Treating minor injuries and sudden illnesses occurring at school.
  5. Environmental Sanitation Inspection: Ensuring safe water supply, proper latrines, and general cleanliness of the school compound.
  6. Nutrition Monitoring: Assessing nutritional status and supervising school feeding programs.
7.
State four (4) functions for the occupational health nurse. (4 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Health Surveillance: Monitoring the health status of workers through pre-employment and periodic medical examinations.
  2. Risk Assessment: Identifying and evaluating hazards in the workplace (chemical, physical, ergonomic).
  3. Treatment of Injuries/Illnesses: Providing first aid and emergency care for work-related injuries.
  4. Health Promotion: Counseling workers on lifestyle, safety practices, and use of Personal Protective Equipment (PPE).
  5. Record Keeping: Maintaining accurate records of worker health and accidents.
8.
Draw and label a diagram to illustrate the life cycle of hookworm. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Lifecycle Description (for diagram):

  1. Eggs in Soil: Eggs are passed in feces of infected person.
  2. Rhabditiform Larvae: Eggs hatch in soil (1-2 days) releasing larvae.
  3. Filariform Larvae: Larvae develop into infective form (5-10 days).
  4. Penetration: Filariform larvae penetrate the skin (usually feet) of a human host.
  5. Migration: Larvae enter bloodstream → Heart → Lungs.
  6. Ascent: Larvae penetrate alveoli, ascend bronchial tree to trachea, and are swallowed.
  7. Maturation: Larvae reach small intestine, attach to wall, and mature into adults.
  8. Egg Production: Adult females lay eggs which are passed in feces.

(In an exam, draw the cycle with arrows connecting these stages).

Part III: Essay/Long Answer Questions

40 Marks
1.
You have been requested by the nurse incharge of a health centre to prepare for an immunization session at the CWC- MCH clinic for a month.

a) Explain the process you will use to determine the number of immunization sessions you will hold. (4 marks)
b) Describe the preparation of supplies and equipments needed for a session. (9 marks)
c) Explain how you will arrange the following for an immunization session.
i) Immunization area. (5 marks)
ii) Immunization equipment. (2 marks)
Show Answer

a) Determining Number of Sessions:

  1. Calculate Target Population: Estimate the number of infants/children eligible for immunization in the catchment area for the year (usually 3-4% of total population).
  2. Determine Monthly Target: Divide the annual target by 12 to get the number of children to be vaccinated per month.
  3. Assess Workload Capacity: Determine how many children can be effectively handled in one session by the available staff (e.g., 30-50 per session).
  4. Calculate Frequency: Divide the monthly target by the session capacity. E.g., if 200 children need vaccination and capacity is 50/session, you need 4 sessions per month (once a week).
  5. Consider Accessibility: Adjust based on community distance and logistics (outreach vs static).

b) Preparation of Supplies and Equipment:

  • Vaccines: Estimate required doses based on expected turnout + wastage factor. Remove from fridge only when ready, check expiry dates, and ensure VVM (Vaccine Vial Monitor) status is valid. Load into vaccine carrier with conditioned ice packs.
  • Cold Chain Equipment: Ensure vaccine carrier is clean and ice packs are fully frozen then "conditioned" (sweating) to prevent freezing sensitive vaccines.
  • Injection Equipment: Prepare sufficient Auto-Disable (AD) syringes (0.05ml for BCG, 0.5ml for others) and mixing syringes (2ml/5ml).
  • Safety Boxes: Assemble safety boxes for immediate disposal of used sharps.
  • Stationery: Ready the MCH registers (CWC register), tally sheets, Mother-Child booklets, and pens.
  • Emergency Kit: Ensure Adrenaline (Epinephrine) and hydrocortisone are available for anaphylaxis management.
  • Hygiene Supplies: Cotton wool, clean water, soap, and a receptacle for non-sharp waste.

c) Arrangement:

i) Immunization Area (Flow):

  • Waiting Area: Well-ventilated, sheltered space for mothers to sit.
  • Registration/Screening: First station to check child's card, weigh, and determine eligible vaccines.
  • Administration Station: Where vaccines are given. Should be clean, well-lit, and out of direct sun.
  • Tallying/Recording: Where immunization is recorded in the register and tally sheet.
  • Health Education/Observation: Final area where advice is given (side effects, next visit) and child is observed for immediate reactions (AEFI) for 15-30 mins.
  • The arrangement should ensure a unidirectional flow to prevent congestion.

ii) Immunization Equipment (On the table):

  • Vaccine carrier with vaccines kept in the shade (not direct sun).
  • Foam pad (in the carrier lid) to hold open vials.
  • Safety box within arm's reach of the vaccinator.
  • Tray with cotton wool and water.
  • Syringes kept in their sterile packets until use.
2.
You are the district public health nurse, you receive report from one of the sub-district hospitals that a number of patients have been admitted there with clinical features of Acute Meningococcal Meningitis (Epidemic Bacterial Meningitis).

a) List the causative organism and its main mode of spread. (1 mark)
b) List eight (8) clinical features of acute meningococcal meningitis. (4 marks)
c) Describe the management of the infected persons in the ward. (8 marks)
d) Describe the prevention and control measures of the epidemic. (7 marks)
Show Answer

a) Organism and Spread:

  • Causative Organism: Neisseria meningitidis (Meningococcus).
  • Mode of Spread: Direct contact with respiratory droplets or secretions from the nose and throat of infected persons or carriers.

b) Clinical Features (8):

  1. High fever (sudden onset).
  2. Severe headache.
  3. Stiffness of the neck (Nuchal rigidity).
  4. Photophobia (sensitivity to light).
  5. Vomiting (often projectile).
  6. Altered mental status/confusion.
  7. Petechial or purpuric rash (non-blanching spots).
  8. Positive Kernig's sign.
  9. Positive Brudzinski's sign.
  10. Convulsions/Seizures (especially in children).

c) Management in Ward:

  • Isolation: Isolate patient (droplet precautions) for at least 24 hours after starting antibiotics.
  • Antibiotic Therapy: Administer high-dose IV antibiotics immediately (e.g., Ceftriaxone or Benzylpenicillin) as per protocol.
  • Airway/Breathing: Maintain patent airway, administer oxygen if needed.
  • Fluids: Establish IV access and manage hydration/shock with IV fluids.
  • Symptomatic Relief: Antipyretics (Paracetamol) for fever, analgesics for headache, and anticonvulsants (Diazepam) if seizures occur.
  • Monitoring: Vital signs (neuro-observations) and intake/output chart.
  • Nursing Care: Minimize noise/light (photophobia), mouth care, and turn patient to prevent pressure sores if unconscious.

d) Prevention and Control of Epidemic:

  • Notification: Immediately notify higher health authorities of the outbreak.
  • Case Management: Early diagnosis and treatment to reduce reservoir.
  • Contact Tracing: Identify close contacts of cases.
  • Chemoprophylaxis: Administer prophylactic antibiotics (e.g., Ciprofloxacin single dose or Rifampicin) to close contacts.
  • Vaccination: Mass vaccination campaign in the affected area with appropriate meningococcal vaccine.
  • Health Education: Educate community on signs/symptoms, early referral, and reducing overcrowding/ventilation.
  • Surveillance: Enhanced surveillance to detect new cases.
  • Crowd Control: discourage large gatherings during the active epidemic phase.
