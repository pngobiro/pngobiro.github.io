Part I: Multiple Choice Questions

20 Marks
1.
A patient with increased intracranial pressure will present with:-
a) Tachycardia, bradycardia, hypotension.
b) Reflex vomiting, hypertension, tachycardia.
c) Projectile vomiting, tachycardia, hypothermia.
d) Hypertension, tachycardia, bradypnea.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Projectile vomiting, tachycardia, hypothermia. (See explanation below)

Explanation: This question contains inconsistent options regarding vitals. The classic sign of Increased Intracranial Pressure (ICP) is Cushing's Triad: Hypertension (widening pulse pressure), Bradycardia (not tachycardia), and Irregular Respirations (Bradypnea).

However, Projectile Vomiting (without nausea) is a specific and hallmark sign of increased ICP. Among the choices provided, projectile vomiting is the most distinct neurological sign listed in option C, although the vital signs listed in all options are confusing/atypical for classic Cushing's. Option D lists Hypertension and Bradypnea (2/3 of Cushing's) but includes Tachycardia (incorrect). In exam contexts, look for the "hallmark" symptom like Projectile Vomiting.

2.
The microbes associated with food poisoning include:-
a) Staphylococcus aureus, campylobacter jejuni, vibrio cholerae.
b) Streptococcus pyogenes, Klebsiella pneumoniae, staphylococcus epidermidis.
c) Clostridium Welchi, salmonella pseudomonas aeginosa.
d) Staphylococcus aureus, salmonella Typhimurium, campylobacter, jejuni.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Staphylococcus aureus, salmonella Typhimurium, campylobacter, jejuni.

Explanation: These are the classic enteric pathogens associated with food poisoning.

  • Staph aureus: Produces toxins in food (fast acting).
  • Salmonella: Common in poultry/eggs.
  • Campylobacter: Common in undercooked poultry.
Option A includes Vibrio cholerae which causes cholera (water-borne), not typically classified under general "food poisoning" in the same category, though transmitted via food/water.

3.
Indicate whether the following statements are true or false.
a) The left kidney is slightly lower than the right kidney.
b) The nasal part of the pharynx has stratified squamous epithelium.
Show Answer

Answers:

  • a) False. The Right kidney is slightly lower than the left because of the large space occupied by the liver on the right side.
  • b) False. The nasopharynx is lined with pseudostratified ciliated columnar epithelium (respiratory epithelium) to filter air. The oropharynx and laryngopharynx are lined with stratified squamous epithelium to protect against abrasive food.
4.
Cushing's syndrome commonly presents with:-
a) Deepening of voice, gynaecomastia, gigantism.
b) Facial hair, acne, enlargement of the clitoris.
c) Increased libido, Menorrhagia, muscle atrophy.
d) Acne, hirsutism, uterine muscles hypertrophy.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Facial hair, acne, enlargement of the clitoris.

Explanation: Cushing's syndrome involves excess cortisol and often excess androgens. Clinical features include moon face, buffalo hump, central obesity, purple striae, and signs of virilization in women such as hirsutism (facial hair), acne, and clitoral enlargement due to androgen excess. Option A describes signs more aligned with acromegaly or specific hormonal tumors.

5.
Seborrheic dermatoses commonly affects the following parts of the body:-
a) Lower limbs, the back and axillae.
b) The neck, groins and calf region.
c) Under the breasts, ears, eyelids.
d) Gluteal crease, the back and abdomen.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Under the breasts, ears, eyelids.

Explanation: Seborrheic dermatitis affects areas rich in sebaceous (oil) glands and skin folds (intertriginous areas). Common sites include the scalp, eyebrows, eyelids, nasolabial folds, behind the ears, and skin folds like under the breasts (inframammary) and axillae.

6.
A correct nursing diagnosis for a patient with chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) is:-
a) "Pulmonary potential for respiratory failure related to lungs degeneration".
b) "Activity intolerance due to loneliness".
c) "Ineffective individual coping related to lower activity level".
d) "Delayed recovery due to poor tolerance of activity and medication".
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) "Ineffective individual coping related to lower activity level". (Note: This is the best constructed NANDA-style format among choices, though physiological diagnoses like 'Ineffective Breathing Pattern' are more common).

Critique of options:

  • a: "Pulmonary potential..." is not a standard NANDA label.
  • b: "Due to loneliness" is not a correct etiology for activity intolerance in COPD (it's due to hypoxia/dyspnea).
  • d: Not a standard diagnosis structure.
Option C reflects the psychosocial impact of the chronic disease limiting lifestyle.

7.
The common features associated with pathophysiology of asthma includes:-
a) Alveoli hyperinflation, mediastinal shift and swelling of membranes lining the bronchi.
b) Bronchial muscle enlargement, filling of the bronchi with thick mucus, negative pressure in the pleural space.
c) Contraction of bronchial muscles, atrophy of bronchial mucus membranes and air trapping in the alveoli.
d) Air trapping in the alveoli, contraction of bronchial muscles, production of tenacious sputum.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Air trapping in the alveoli, contraction of bronchial muscles, production of tenacious sputum.

Explanation: The "Asthma Triad" of pathophysiology consists of: 1. Bronchospasm: Contraction of bronchial smooth muscles. 2. Edema/Inflammation: Swelling of the mucosa. 3. Mucus Hypersecretion: Production of thick (tenacious) sputum. This leads to airway narrowing and subsequent air trapping in the alveoli (hyperinflation).

8.
Following hemorrhoidectomy the nurse should:-
a) Ensure the patient sits on a soft cushion for the first three days.
b) Advise the patient to only request for a bland diet in his menu till bowel action normalizes.
c) Ensure the patient has a sitz bath after every bowel action.
d) Remove the anal pack 8 hours after the surgery to allow for passing of flatus.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Ensure the patient has a sitz bath after every bowel action.

Explanation: Sitz baths are a standard of care after hemorrhoidectomy. They promote cleanliness, reduce pain, and increase blood flow to aid healing. They should be taken 3-4 times a day and especially after each bowel movement. Packs are usually removed day 1 post-op, not 8 hours, depending on surgeon protocol, but sitz baths are the key nursing intervention for comfort.

9.
Pernicious anaemia is associated with:-
a) Gastrectomy, autoimmune destruction of gastric parietal cells.
b) Vit B6 deficiency excess folic acid.
c) Deficiency of folic acid, excess intake of niacin.
d) Partial gastrectomy, vitamin B2 and B6 deficiency.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Gastrectomy, autoimmune destruction of gastric parietal cells.

Explanation: Pernicious anemia is caused by a lack of Intrinsic Factor, which is needed to absorb Vitamin B12. Intrinsic factor is produced by gastric parietal cells. Causes include autoimmune destruction of these cells or surgical removal of the stomach part containing them (gastrectomy).

10.
A late manifestation of compartment syndrome is:-
a) Pallor.
b) Paresthesia.
c) Pulselessness.
d) Pain.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Pulselessness.

Explanation: The 5 Ps of compartment syndrome are Pain, Pallor, Paresthesia, Paralysis, and Pulselessness.

  • Pain: The earliest and most reliable sign.
  • Paresthesia: Early/Mid sign of nerve ischemia.
  • Pulselessness: A very late and ominous sign, indicating that compartment pressure has exceeded arterial pressure. Irreversible damage has often occurred by this point.

11.
Indicators for inhalational burns are:-
a) History of the burn occurring in an enclosed area, singed nasal hair, anaemia.
b) Voice hoarseness, bloody sputum, seizures.
c) Singed nasal hair, the burn occurring in an enclosed area, sooty sputum.
d) Bloody sputum, hypoventilation, paradoxical breathing.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Singed nasal hair, the burn occurring in an enclosed area, sooty sputum.

Explanation: The classic triad of signs pointing to inhalation injury includes: 1. History: Burn in an enclosed space (trapped smoke). 2. Facial Signs: Singed nasal hairs/eyebrows, facial burns. 3. Sputum: Carbonaceous (sooty/black) sputum.

12.
A patient verbalizing one of the following post total mastectomy of the left breast, will be an indication of effective patient teaching:-
a) "I will ensure my left arm is always at rest."
b) "On discharge from hospital, I shall buy heavy duty gloves to wear when gardening."
c) "I should have a prosthesis implant strictly after 1 year."
d) "There is no harm carrying my handbags on either shoulder."
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) "On discharge from hospital, I shall buy heavy duty gloves to wear when gardening."

Explanation: This statement shows understanding of Lymphedema precautions. The patient must protect the arm on the affected side (left) from any injury (cuts, scratches, burns) which could lead to infection and severe swelling because lymph nodes have been removed. Heavy gloves provide protection.
Why others are wrong: (a) Immobilization (always at rest) can lead to frozen shoulder; exercises are encouraged. (d) Handbags should NOT be carried on the affected shoulder (constriction).

13.
The ratio of replacing crystalloids blood loss in haemorrhagic shock management is:-
a) 1:1.
b) 2:1.
c) 3:1.
d) 4:1.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) 3:1.

Explanation: The standard guideline for fluid resuscitation in hemorrhagic shock is to replace every 1 mL of blood loss with 3 mL of crystalloid solution (Normal Saline or Ringer's Lactate). This 3:1 ratio accounts for the fluid shift from the intravascular space into the interstitial space.

14.
Following functional endoscopic sinus surgery (FESS), the nurse should:-
a) Assist the patient to brush teeth 8 hours after surgery.
b) Nurse the patient in Trendelenburg position.
c) Remove the nasal pack 12 hours post surgery to present a nasal voice.
d) Encourage the patient to breath through the mouth.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Encourage the patient to breath through the mouth.

Explanation: After FESS, the nose is usually packed with gauze or balloons to control bleeding. The patient cannot breathe through the nose and must be encouraged and reassured to breathe through the mouth.
Contraindications: Trendelenburg increases bleeding (head should be elevated). Brushing teeth might be restricted or done gently to avoid pressure.

15.
Predisposing factors to retinal detachment include:-
a) Scleritis, hypermetropia, uveitis.
b) Myopia, ocular trauma, cataract extraction.
c) Trachoma, secondary glaucoma, conjunctivitis.
d) Conjunctivitis, episcleritis, retinitis.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Myopia, ocular trauma, cataract extraction.

Explanation: Major risk factors include: 1. Myopia (Nearsightedness): The elongated eye shape thins the retina. 2. Trauma: Direct injury can tear the retina. 3. Cataract Surgery: Aphakia or pseudophakia increases risk.

16.
Cancer that gets cured through chemotherapy is:-
a) Breast carcinoma.
b) Colorectal carcinoma.
c) Burkitt's lymphoma.
d) Gastric carcinoma.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Burkitt's lymphoma.

Explanation: Burkitt's lymphoma is a highly aggressive B-cell non-Hodgkin lymphoma but is also one of the most chemosensitive malignancies. It has a very high cure rate with combination chemotherapy alone. Other solid tumors usually require surgery/radiation in addition to chemo.

17.
Causes of functional intestinal obstruction includes:-
a) Diverticular disease Hirschsprung's disease.
b) Intussusception, hyperkalemia.
c) Hyperkalemia, peritonitis.
d) Ascites, intestinal worms.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Hyperkalemia, peritonitis. (Correction: Should be Hypokalemia usually, but Peritonitis causes Paralytic Ileus).

Explanation: Functional obstruction (Paralytic Ileus) involves a failure of peristalsis without physical blockage. Causes include: 1. Peritonitis: Inflammation paralyzes the bowel. 2. Electrolyte imbalances: Specifically Hypokalemia (low potassium), not Hyperkalemia, causes muscle weakness/ileus. However, in exam context, this option pairs peritonitis (a major cause) with an electrolyte issue. Note: Intussusception, Worms, Diverticulitis are mechanical causes.

18.
The clinical features for kidney rejection post transplantation includes:-
a) Anaemia, anuria, hypotension.
b) Hypertension, weight gain, oliguria.
c) Tenderness on the area over the transplanted kidney, weight gain, Hypokalemia.
d) Hyperuricemia, hyperactivity and polyuria.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Hypertension, weight gain, oliguria.

Explanation: Signs of graft rejection mimic renal failure: 1. Oliguria: Decreased urine output (kidney stops working). 2. Weight Gain/Edema: Fluid retention. 3. Hypertension: Fluid overload and RAAS activation. 4. Graft Tenderness: Pain over the site (mentioned in C, but C has hypokalemia; rejection usually causes hyperkalemia).

19.
Warning signs for a patient with a fracture on the base of the skull are:-
a) Otorrhea, nasal congestion, tachypnoea.
b) Raccoon eye, Battle's sign, otorrhea.
c) Nasal congestion, Rhinorrhea and vomiting.
d) Vomiting, excessive salivation, excessive swallowing.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Raccoon eye, Battle's sign, otorrhea.

Explanation: Classic signs of Basilar Skull Fracture: 1. Raccoon Eyes: Periorbital ecchymosis. 2. Battle's Sign: Bruising behind the ear (mastoid). 3. CSF Otorrhea/Rhinorrhea: Leakage of Cerebrospinal fluid from ear/nose.

20.
The properties of general anesthesia are:-
a) Amnesia, euphoria, hypnosis.
b) Hypnosis, analgesia, muscle relaxation.
c) Loss of consciousness, amnesia and analgesia.
d) Partial loss of consciousness, muscle relaxation and amnesia.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Hypnosis, analgesia, muscle relaxation. (Also C is very close).

Explanation: The "Triad of General Anesthesia" consists of: 1. Unconsciousness (Hypnosis): Patient is asleep. 2. Analgesia: Pain relief. 3. Muscle Relaxation: Paralysis for surgery. Option B covers these three pillars best in technical terms. Amnesia is also a property, but muscle relaxation is key for major surgery.

Part II: Short Answer Questions

40 Marks
1.
State four (4) common errors in formulation of a nursing diagnosis. (4 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Using a medical diagnosis: e.g., writing "Pneumonia" instead of "Ineffective Airway Clearance".
  2. Stating the problem as an intervention: e.g., "Catheterize patient" instead of "Urinary Retention".
  3. Stating the problem as a patient need: e.g., "Needs fluids" instead of "Deficient Fluid Volume".
  4. Using value-laden or judgmental language: e.g., "Uncooperative patient".
  5. Reversing the problem and etiology: e.g., "Pain related to tissue damage" vs "Tissue damage related to pain" (Cause and effect mix-up).
2.
Write short notes on "Humulin" with reference to the following sub-headings:
  1. Classification.
  2. Onset of action.
  3. Peak of action.
  4. Duration of action.
  5. Four (4) specific nursing responsibilities. (6 marks)
Show Answer

Note: "Humulin" usually refers to Humulin R (Regular) or Humulin N (NPH). Assuming Humulin R (Short-acting) as standard reference unless specified.

  • a) Classification: Short-acting Insulin (Soluble/Regular Insulin).
  • b) Onset of action: 30 minutes to 1 hour.
  • c) Peak of action: 2 to 4 hours.
  • d) Duration of action: 5 to 8 hours.
  • e) Nursing Responsibilities:
    1. Monitor Blood Glucose: Check levels before administration to prevent hypoglycemia.
    2. Meal Timing: Ensure food is available and patient eats within 30 mins of injection.
    3. Site Rotation: Rotate injection sites (abdomen, arm, thigh) to prevent lipodystrophy.
    4. Double Check: Verify dosage with a second nurse before administration (High Alert Med).
3.
a) Draw and label a diagram illustrating the thyroid gland and associated structures. (5 marks)
b) State three (3) functions of thyroid hormones. (3 marks)
Show Answer

a) Diagram: (See NCK 2012 Paper Q1 for details). Draw Butterfly shape, Thyroid Cartilage (superior), Cricoid Cartilage, Isthmus, Trachea, Left/Right Lobes.

b) Functions of Thyroid Hormones (T3/T4):

  1. Metabolism Regulation: Increases Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR) and heat production.
  2. Growth and Development: Essential for normal physical growth and mental development (brain maturation) in children.
  3. Cardiovascular Function: Increases heart rate and strength of contraction (permissive effect on catecholamines).
  4. Nutrient Metabolism: Affects metabolism of proteins, fats, and carbohydrates.
4.
a) Define parenteral feeding. (1 mark)
b) State five (5) indications for parenteral feeding. (5 marks)
Show Answer

a) Definition: Parenteral feeding (TPN/PPN) is the administration of nutrients (amino acids, lipids, dextrose, vitamins) directly into the bloodstream (intravenously), bypassing the gastrointestinal tract.

b) Indications:

  1. Severe Malabsorption: Short bowel syndrome.
  2. Intestinal Obstruction: Where enteral feeding is contraindicated.
  3. Severe Pancreatitis: To rest the gut.
  4. Prolonged Post-operative Ileus.
  5. Severe Burns/Trauma: High caloric needs where patient cannot eat enough.
  6. Intestinal Fistulas.
5.
Describe the management of an adult with foreign body airway obstruction (FBAU) and has a weak cough. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Note: A weak/ineffective cough indicates severe obstruction. The patient cannot clear the object themselves.

  1. Assess Severity: Confirm the cough is ineffective (unable to speak, breathe, or cry out).
  2. Call for Help: Shout for assistance or alert emergency response.
  3. Back Blows (5): Stand behind patient, support chest with one hand, lean them forward, and give up to 5 sharp blows between shoulder blades with heel of hand. Check after each blow.
  4. Abdominal Thrusts (Heimlich Maneuver) (5): If back blows fail, stand behind, wrap arms around waist, place fist above navel, grasp with other hand, and pull sharply inwards and upwards.
  5. Repeat: Continue alternating 5 back blows and 5 abdominal thrusts until object is expelled or patient becomes unconscious.
  6. If Unconscious: Lower to floor, start CPR (Chest compressions).
6.
State five (5) health messages a nurse should share with a patient on discharge post myocardial infarction. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Medication Adherence: Take prescribed drugs (Aspirin, Beta-blockers, Statins) exactly as directed; do not stop abruptly.
  2. Dietary Modification: Adopt a heart-healthy diet low in saturated fats, cholesterol, and salt; increase fruits and vegetables.
  3. Activity/Exercise: Resume activity gradually (Cardiac Rehab); avoid heavy lifting; stop if chest pain occurs.
  4. Smoking Cessation: Stop smoking completely to prevent vasoconstriction and further damage.
  5. Symptom Recognition: Report chest pain, shortness of breath, or excessive fatigue immediately; keep sublingual nitroglycerin handy.
7.
a) List four (4) late clinical manifestations of cancer of the cervix. (2 marks)
b) Outline four (4) specific nursing interventions for patient with cancer of the cervix. (4 marks)
Show Answer

a) Late Manifestations:

  1. Severe back pain or leg pain (nerve involvement).
  2. Weight loss and cachexia (wasting).
  3. Foul-smelling vaginal discharge.
  4. Hematuria or rectal bleeding (invasion of bladder/rectum).
  5. Leg edema (lymphatic obstruction).

b) Nursing Interventions:

  1. Pain Management: Administer prescribed analgesics and provide comfort measures for pelvic/back pain.
  2. Hygiene: Perform frequent perineal care/douches (if prescribed) to manage discharge and odor; sitz baths.
  3. Psychological Support: Provide counseling regarding body image, sexual function, and anxiety about diagnosis.
  4. Elimination Support: Monitor for urinary retention or constipation; catheterize if needed (post-radical hysterectomy).
  5. Nutrition: High protein, high vitamin diet to correct anemia and promote healing.

Part III: Essay/Long Answer Questions

40 Marks
1.

Mr. T 40 years is admitted to the ward suffering from acute pancreatitis.

a) Describe the pathophysiology of pancreatitis. (5 marks)

b) Describe the nursing management for Mr. T from admission till discharge. (12 marks)

c) State three (3) complications of acute pancreatitis. (3 marks)

Show Answer

a) Pathophysiology (Autodigestion Theory):

  • Trigger: Etiological factors (Alcohol, Gallstones) cause injury to pancreatic cells or obstruction of the duct.
  • Activation: Pancreatic enzymes (Trypsinogen, Lipase, Amylase) which are normally inactive in the pancreas, become prematurely activated inside the gland.
  • Autodigestion: Activated Trypsin digests the pancreatic tissue itself.
    • Elastase causes hemorrhage (dissolves blood vessel elastic fibers).
    • Phospholipase A causes fat necrosis.
  • Inflammation: This tissue damage triggers a massive inflammatory response, vasodilation, and fluid shift (third spacing), leading to edema, necrosis, and potentially hypovolemic shock.

b) Nursing Management:

  1. Pain Relief (Priority):
    • Administer analgesics (e.g., Pethidine/Meperidine). Morphine is sometimes avoided as it may spasm the Sphincter of Oddi (controversial but traditional teaching).
    • Positioning: Fetal position or leaning forward often reduces pain.
  2. Fluid & Electrolyte Balance:
    • Establish IV access and administer aggressive fluid resuscitation (Crystalloids) to prevent shock from third-spacing.
    • Monitor Calcium levels (Hypocalcemia is a risk due to fat necrosis binding calcium). Treat tetany with IV Calcium Gluconate.
  3. Rest the Pancreas (NPO):
    • Strict Nil Per Os (NPO) to stop enzyme secretion.
    • Insert Nasogastric (NG) tube for suction to relieve nausea/vomiting and reduce gastric acid stimulus.
    • Initiate TPN if NPO status is prolonged.
  4. Respiratory Care:
    • Monitor for pleural effusion/ARDS. Encourage deep breathing.
  5. Monitoring:
    • Monitor blood glucose (Beta cell damage causes hyperglycemia). Administer insulin if needed.
    • Monitor Turner's sign (flank bruising) or Cullen's sign (umbilical bruising) indicating hemorrhage.
  6. Dietary Re-introduction:
    • Start clear liquids when pain subsides and bowel sounds return.
    • Progress to low-fat, high-carbohydrate diet. Avoid heavy meals.
  7. Education:
    • Strict alcohol abstinence.

c) Complications:

  1. Pancreatic Pseudocyst: Fluid-filled sac outside the pancreas.
  2. Hypovolemic Shock: Due to fluid shift.
  3. ARDS (Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome).
  4. Diabetes Mellitus: Destruction of islet cells.
  5. Hypocalcemia (Tetany).
2.

Mr. S. is admitted in a ward with a diagnosis of chronic osteoarthritis and is scheduled for total hip joint replacement.

a) State three (3) differences between rheumatoid arthritis and osteoarthritis. (3 marks)

b) Describe the pre and post operative care for Mr. S. (14 marks)

c) State three (3) contra-indications for hip replacement. (3 marks)

Show Answer

a) Differences:

Feature Osteoarthritis (OA) Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA)
Cause Degenerative ("Wear and Tear"). Autoimmune inflammatory disease.
Symmetry Asymmetrical (one side often worse). Symmetrical (both sides affected).
Systemic Signs Local joint pain only; no systemic fever. Systemic: Fever, fatigue, malaise.
Morning Stiffness Short duration (< 30 mins). Prolonged (> 1 hour).

b) Pre and Post-Operative Care:

Pre-operative:

  1. Education: Teach use of trapeze bar, incentive spirometer, and post-op limitations (no bending >90).
  2. Infection Control: Treat any existing infections (UTI, dental) to prevent hematogenous spread to the new joint. Skin prep with antiseptic.
  3. Assessment: Baseline neurovascular status of the affected limb.
  4. Blood: Group and cross-match blood; consider autologous donation.
  5. Consent: Ensure informed consent is signed.

Post-operative:

  1. Positioning (Crucial):
    • Maintain abduction of the affected leg using an abduction pillow to prevent dislocation.
    • Avoid flexion >90 degrees (do not sit low, use raised toilet seat).
    • Do not cross legs.
  2. Neurovascular Checks: Monitor color, warmth, sensation, movement, and pulses (pedal) of the affected leg frequently to detect nerve damage or ischemia.
  3. DVT Prophylaxis: Administer anticoagulants (Heparin/Enoxaparin), apply anti-embolism stockings, and encourage foot exercises.
  4. Wound Care: Monitor dressing for bleeding/drainage (Hemovac drain care). strict asepsis.
  5. Pain Management: PCA or regular analgesics to facilitate early mobilization.
  6. Mobilization: Early ambulation (often day 1) with walker/crutches as per protocol. Weight bearing status as prescribed (cemented vs uncemented).

c) Contra-indications for THR:

  1. Active Infection: Local or systemic infection (sepsis, osteomyelitis) puts the implant at high risk.
  2. Severe Osteoporosis: Poor bone stock may not support the implant.
  3. Rapidly Progressive Neuro-muscular disease: e.g., Charcot joint (neuropathic arthropathy).
  4. Severe Cardiac/Respiratory Disease: Patient cannot withstand anesthesia or rehabilitation.
