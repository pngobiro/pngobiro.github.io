üëÅÔ∏è Overview

Health assessment is essential and fundamental knowledge for registered nurses. Health assessment involves the systematic collection of subjective and objective data from the patient and analysis of these data to make clinical judgements about the patient's health status.

The process of collecting data for health assessment is often described as invisible and continual. Nurses are continually observing their patients to collect data in order to make the necessary clinical judgements regardless of where they are‚Äîhospital, clinic, community, home or residential long-term care. Assessment is the first step of the nursing process, and learning to effectively collect information systematically and comprehensively from patients about their health for assessment and clinical judgements is crucial to making accurate clinical decisions about patient treatment and care.

As the only healthcare professionals with patients 24 hours a day, seven days a week, nurses are usually the first to detect and respond to changes in a patient's condition at the bedside. Nurses are reliant on their health assessment knowledge and skills in order to identify life-threatening complications for the patient, as well as to detect subtle and overt changes that represent deterioration in the patient's condition. Registered nurses save lives and improve the health outcomes of their patients through early recognition and nursing actions as a result of continuing assessment on a daily basis. Hence it is important that students learn to develop, practise and consolidate skills to conduct systematic and comprehensive health assessments, through the collection of a health history and physical examination of the patient. It is vital that nurses listen to their patients and inspect and examine them in a systematic and comprehensive way.

This chapter provides students with the opportunity to develop the knowledge, skills and techniques for conducting health assessments using a systems framework approach. The skills addressed include assessment of the abdomen and gastrointestinal tract (27-1), assessment of the musculoskeletal system (27-2), assessment of the cardiovascular and peripheral vascular systems (27-3), assessment of the respiratory system (27-4), mental state assessment (27-5) and assessment of central nervous system and level of consciousness (27-6).

üå≠ Skill 27-1: Assessment of the Abdomen and Gastrointestinal Tract

DELEGATION CONSIDERATIONS

Assessment of the abdomen and gastrointestinal (GI) tract may be undertaken by enrolled nurses under the guidance of the registered nurse.

  • Ensure review of the anatomy and physiology of the GI system.
  • Ensure that hand hygiene is performed and personal protective equipment is worn when appropriate.

EQUIPMENT

  • Stethoscope
  • Small pillow for under the knees
  • Tape measure or ruler
  • Marking pen
  • Pen
  • Documentation

PROCEDURE

STEPS RATIONALE
1. Determine need to assess GI status of patient. Abdominal pain is one of the most common symptoms reported when seeking healthcare.
2. Focused health history
a. Determine chief complaint‚Äîask about the key characteristics of pain and each sign/symptom using the PQRST approach (see Chapter 6).
b. Ask the patient to describe their elimination pattern, and specifically the presence of:
  • abdominal pain
  • nausea and/or vomiting
  • anorexia
  • dysphagia
  • diarrhoea
  • constipation
  • abdominal distension
  • increased eructation
  • increased flatulence
  • dysuria and/or nocturia.
c. Ascertain past history of abdominal-specific pathology, including surgery and communicable diseases.
d. Ask about alcohol and/or drug use.
e. Ask about travel history. 		The focused abdominal health history provides insight into the link between a patient's lifestyle and abdominal information and pathology.
3. Explain procedure to patient and clarify any concerns. Promotes patient's cooperation and comfort during the assessment.
4. Ensure patient comfort and privacy.
5. Ensure appropriate position for examination‚Äîsupine with small pillow beneath knees. Examiner stands to the right of the patient.
6. Abdominal landmarks
a. Identify abdominal landmarks
  1. Xiphoid process.
  2. Costal margins.
  3. Abdominal midline.
  4. Umbilicus.
  5. Rectus abdominis muscles.
  6. Anterior superior iliac spine.
  7. Inguinal ligament.
  8. Symphysis pubis.
b. Anatomical mapping
(1) Four quadrants:
  1. Right upper quadrant (RUQ)
  2. Right lower quadrant (RLQ)
  3. Left upper quadrant (LUQ)
  4. Left lower quadrant (LLQ)
 The abdomen is the largest cavity of the body. It is located between the diaphragm and symphysis pubis. It is ovoid in shape and contains several vital organs.
Anatomical maps provide a framework for examination of the abdomen.
The abdominal cavity can be divided into four quadrants (see Figure 27-13A):
  • Imaginary vertical line from xiphoid process to symphysis pubis
  • Imaginary horizontal line level with umbilicus.
(2) Nine regions:
  1. Epigastric region
  2. Umbilical region
  3. Hypogastric region
  4. Right hypochondriac region
  5. Left hypochondriac region
  6. Right lumbar region
  7. Left lumbar region
  8. Right iliac region
  9. Left iliac region
 The abdominal cavity can be subdivided into a further nine anatomical regions:
  • Imaginary vertical lines linking left and right mid-clavicles to left and right inguinal ligaments
  • Horizontal line level with lower border of costal margins
  • Horizontal line level with anterior superior iliac spines.
7. Examination of the abdomen
A. Inspect the abdomen in each of the 4 quadrants for:
  • (1) Contour, symmetry, pigmentation and colour, viewed from the costal margin to the symphysis pubis.
  • (2) Separation of the rectus abdominis muscles‚Äîinstruct the patient to raise the head and shoulders.
  • (3) Scars and striae.
  • (4) Obvious signs of trauma.
  • (5) Respiratory movement.
  • (6) Masses or nodules.
  • (7) Visible peristalsis‚Äîperistaltic movement slowly traverses the abdomen in a slanting downward direction.
  • (8) Umbilicus in relation to the abdominal surface.
 Normally the abdominal contour is flat.
The abdomen should be symmetrical bilaterally.
The abdomen should be uniform in colour and pigmentation.
Normally the symmetry of the abdomen remains uniform, and no ridge is observed parallel to the umbilicus or between the rectus abdominis muscles.
Normally respiratory movement should rise with inspiration and fall with expiration. There should be no respiratory retractions.
Ripple of peristalsis may be observed in thin patients.
The umbilicus is normally depressed and beneath the abdominal surface.
B. Auscultate the abdomen in each of the 4 quadrants for bowel sounds, noting frequency, pitch, quality. Place the diaphragm of the stethoscope lightly on the abdominal wall, beginning in the RLQ. Listen to the frequency of bowel sounds. Bowel sounds are heard as intermittent gurgling sounds throughout the abdominal quadrants. They are usually high-pitched and occur between 5 and 30 times a minute. Bowel sounds result from the movement of air and fluid through the GI tract. Normally no audible bruits are auscultated.
C. Percuss the abdomen in each of the 4 quadrants:
  • (1) Abdominal surface (distribution of percussion sounds).
  • (2) Intensity, pitch, duration, quality.
  • (3) Beginning in the RLQ, moving upward and crossing over to the LUQ and down to the LLQ.
  • (4) Bladder‚Äîpercuss upward from the symphysis pubis to the umbilicus.
 Tympany is normally the predominant sound heard because of the presence of air in the stomach and intestine. It is a high-pitched sound of lengthy duration.
Dullness is normally heard over the liver or a distended bladder. Dull sounds are high-pitched and of moderate duration.
Normally the liver span is between 6 and 12 cm in the right midclavicular line. There is a direct correlation between body size and liver size.
Dullness is also heard over solid organs or masses. Note where the sound changes from dullness to tympany.
A urine-filled bladder is dull to percussion. A recently emptied bladder should not be percussable above the symphysis pubis.
D. Palpate the abdomen in each of the 4 quadrants:
  • (1) Warm your hands.
  • (2) First identify location of abdominal tenderness.
  • (3) Watch person‚Äôs face during palpation.
  • (4) Light palpation (2‚Äì3 cm depth)‚Äîuse the pads of fingers to depress the abdominal wall.
  • (5) Deep palpation (4‚Äì5 cm depth)‚Äîuse the palmar surface of the extended fingers to depress the abdominal wall.
 Normally the abdomen should feel soft, non-tender and without masses. Light palpation reveals changes in skin temperature, tenderness or guarding or large masses.
Normally no organ enlargement should be palpable. Nor should there be any abnormal masses, bulges or swelling.
Normally only the aorta and the edge of the liver are palpable in thin patients. When the large colon or bladder are full, palpation is possible but atypical.
RECORDING AND REPORTING HOME CARE CONSIDERATIONS
  • Findings from inspection, auscultation, percussion and palpation assessment of the abdomen are documented.
  • Report any abnormal findings.
  • Explanation of factors that promote bowel and bladder elimination.
  • Caution against the excessive use of laxatives.
  • Recommendations and evaluation of management of acute or chronic pain.

Reference: Estes MEZ 2010 Health assessment and physical examination, ed 4. New York, Cengage Learning.

ü¶¥ Skill 27-2: Assessment of the Musculoskeletal System

DELEGATION CONSIDERATIONS

Assessment of the musculoskeletal system may be undertaken by enrolled nurses under the guidance of the registered nurse.

  • Ensure review of the anatomy and physiology of the musculoskeletal system for risk factors, specifically the major muscle groups and joints.
  • Ensure hand hygiene is performed and personal protective equipment worn when appropriate.

EQUIPMENT

  • Tape measure
  • Marking pen
  • Pen
  • Documentation
STEPS RATIONALE
1. Determine need to assess musculoskeletal status of patient. Alterations in the musculoskeletal system will affect the person's ability to complete activities of daily living, occupation and recreation. The assessment focuses on determining range of joint motion, muscle strength and tone and joint and muscle condition.
2. Focused health history.
a. Determine chief complaint‚Äîcharacteristics of pain and each sign/symptom.
b. Determine presence of:
  • Pain‚Äîlocation and quantity, associated manifestations, aggravating and alleviating factors, timing
  • Weakness‚Äîlocation and quantity
  • Limited movement‚Äîlocation and quantity, inflammation, stiffness, muscle atrophy, deformity, crepitus, joint effusion
  • Aggravating factors‚Äîoverexertion, immobility, excessive weight gain, non-compliance with medication, physiotherapy (PT) and occupational therapy (OT) regimens
  • Alleviating factors‚ÄîPT and OT, anti-inflammatory medications, analgesics, ice, heat, rest
c. Ask about past history of musculoskeletal specific conditions and surgery, and non-musculoskeletal-specific conditions.
d. Ask about social history‚Äîalcohol use, work environment, recreation and leisure. 		The focused musculoskeletal health history provides insight into the link between a patient's lifestyle and musculoskeletal information and pathology.
3. Explain procedure to patient and clarify any concerns. Promotes patient's cooperation and comfort during the assessment.
4. Ensure patient comfort and privacy.
5. Ensure appropriate position for examination. The examiner stands in front of the patient for anterior assessment and behind the patient for posterior assessment.
6. Physical examination
A. Overall appearance
(1) Height and weight.
(2) Ability to tolerate weightbearing on lower limbs.
(3) Observe for structural abnormalities.
(4) Note indications of discomfort. 		Body height and weight should be appropriate for age and gender. The patient should be able to enter the assessment area by ambulating independently.
B. Posture
(1) Instruct the patient to stand with feet together. Observe the structural and spatial relationship of the head, torso, pelvis and limbs.
(2) Assess for symmetry of the shoulders, scapulae and iliac crest.
(3) Instruct the patient to sit; observe posture. 		Normally, in the standing position the head and torso are upright. The shoulders and hips are level, with symmetry of the scapulae and iliac crests. The arms hang freely from the shoulders. The feet are aligned and toes point forward. The extremities are proportional to the overall body size and shape; the limbs are symmetrical with each other.
In the sitting position, both feet should be placed firmly on the surface of the floor, with toes pointing forward.
C. Gait and mobility
(1) Instruct the patient to walk normally across the room.
(2) Observe the patient during transfer between standing and sitting positions.
(3) Observe patient's balance during above movements. 		Normally, walking is initiated in a smooth and rhythmic motion. The foot is lifted and propelled forwards in a straight line; as the heel strikes the floor, bodyweight is shifted to the ball of the foot. The patient remains erect and balanced during all stages of gait. Step height and length are symmetrical for each foot. Arms swing freely at the side of the torso and in the opposite direction to the movement of the legs. The head and neck face the intended direction. The patient should be able to transfer with ease between varying positions.
D. Inspection
(1) Muscle size and shape‚Äîoverall appearance of muscle mass; observe for muscle contraction.
(2) Joint contour‚Äîobserve the shape of the joint in its neutral anatomical position. Observe the skin and subcutaneous tissue surrounding the joint for colour, inflammation, bruising, deformities, masses, nodules and skin integrity. 		Normally muscle contraction will be affected by the exercise and activity patterns of the individual. During contraction, tautness in the muscle should be observed. Muscle shape may be accentuated in certain body areas but should be symmetrical.
Normally joint contour is flat in extension and smooth and rounded in flexion.
There should be no observable abnormalities to the skin and subcutaneous tissue surrounding the joint.
Bilateral joints should be symmetrical and similar in appearance.
E. Palpation
(1) Muscle tone‚Äîpalpate each muscle group by applying light pressure with the pads of the fingers of the dominant hand. Note the change in muscle shape between the 'belly' of the muscle (wide central area) and where it tapers off into the tendon.
(2) Joints‚Äîpalpate each of the major skeletal joints, with the joint in the neutral anatomical position. Apply light pressure with the pads of the fingers of the dominant hand. Palpate from the periphery inwards to the centre of the joint. Note any pain, swelling, crepitus, warmth or nodules.
(3) Range of motion (ROM): examine each of the major skeletal joints: temporomandibular joint, cervical spine, shoulders, elbows, wrists and hands, hips, knees, ankles and feet, spine (see Table 27-14).
a. Instruct the patient to move each of the major skeletal joints through each of its various ranges of movement. Notice the angle, pain, tenderness or crepitus.
b. If the patient is unable to move a joint through active ROM, passive joint movement is required; gently support and move the joints of the extremities through their ROM (see Figure 27-16). Always stop at complaints of pain.
(4) Muscle strength‚Äîeach muscle group is assessed for strength using the same movements as those performed through ROM (see Table 27-16 and Figure 27-17). Note strength and equality. Compare the right and left of paired muscle groups. Note involuntary movements. 		Normally, on palpation the muscle should feel smooth and firm.
Normally when the skeletal joints are palpated in their neutral anatomical position, the external joint contour feels smooth, strong and firm. The area surrounding the joint should be free from abnormalities.
Normally as the joint is moved through its ROM, it should be able to articulate in proper alignment without any visible or palpable deformity.
Normal muscle strength will allow for complete voluntary ROM of joints against both gravity and moderate to full resistance.
RECORDING AND REPORTING HOME CARE CONSIDERATIONS
  • Findings from inspection and palpation in the assessment of the muscles, joints and ROM are documented.
  • Report any abnormal findings.
  • Education for the identification of risk factors affecting mobility and the interruption of the activities of daily living cycle.

Reference: Estes MEZ 2010 Health assessment and physical examination, ed 4. New York, Cengage Learning.

‚ù§Ô∏è Skill 27-3: Assessment of Cardiovascular System

DELEGATION CONSIDERATIONS

Assessment of the cardiovascular system (CVS) and peripheral vascular system (PVS) may be undertaken by enrolled nurses under the guidance of the registered nurse.

  • Ensure review of the anatomy and physiology of the CVS and PVS for risk factors.
  • Ensure hand hygiene is performed and personal protective equipment is worn when appropriate.

EQUIPMENT

  • Stethoscope
  • Sphygmomanometer
  • Watch with second hand
  • Tape measure
STEPS RATIONALE
1. Determine need to assess CVS and PVS status of patient. A patient with the signs and symptoms of heart (cardiac) problems (e.g. chest pain or irregular pulse) may be suffering a life-threatening condition requiring immediate attention.
2. Focused health history
a. Determine chief complaint‚Äîcharacteristics of chest pain and each sign/symptom.
b. Determine presence of chest pain‚Äîdescription of onset, location/radiation, duration, character/quality, aggravating factors, relieving factors, timing and severity (0‚Äì10 scale).
c. Ask about associated signs and symptoms:
  • syncope
  • palpitations
  • nausea and vomiting
  • peripheral oedema
  • extremity pain
d. Ask about past history:
  • cardiac-specific, e.g. angina, heart failure, coronary artery disease (CAD), hypertension, rheumatic fever, valvular disease
  • non-cardiac-specific, e.g. bleeding or blood disorders, diabetes, stroke
  • cardiac or vascular surgery
e. Ask about smoking and alcohol and/or drug use.
f. Ask about travel history.
g. Determine family history‚Äîheart and vascular diseases that are familial include aneurysm, stroke, CAD, hypertension, myocardial infarction, stroke. 		The focused cardiac and peripheral vascular health history provides insight into the link between a patient‚Äôs lifestyle and cardiac and peripheral vasculature information and pathology.
3. Explain procedure to patient and clarify any concerns. Promotes patient‚Äôs cooperation and comfort during the assessment
4. Ensure patient comfort and privacy.
5. Ensure appropriate position (supine/semi-Fowler‚Äôs) for examination‚Äîperformed through the anterior thorax with the examiner on the right side of the patient. Patients with heart or respiratory disease often suffer shortness of breath when lying flat.
6. Assessment of cardiovascular system
A. Identify praecordial (cardiac) landmarks:
  • A. Aortic area: 2nd intercostal space at right sternal border
  • P. Pulmonic area: 2nd intercostal space at left sternal border
  • T. Tricuspid area: 4th intercostal space at left sternal border
  • M. Mitral/apical area/impulse/point of maximal impulse (PMI): 4th to 5th intercostal space left of midclavicular line
B. Assessment of the praecordium: inspection and palpation are performed simultaneously and methodically, looking for visible pulsations, source of vibrations or thrills and the apical impulse.
  • (1) Inspect and palpate the angle of Louis (between the sternal body and manubrium) with the index finger.
  • (2) Inspect and palpate the aortic area.
  • (3) Inspect and palpate the pulmonic area.
  • (4) Inspect and palpate the tricuspid area.
  • (5) Inspect and palpate the mitral area.
C. Auscultation of heart sounds is commenced at the aortic cardiac landmark and a systematic approach is followed across each of the cardiac landmarks.
  • (1) Place the diaphragm of the stethoscope on the chest wall over the aortic landmark and listen for S‚ÇÇ.
  • (2) Place the diaphragm of the stethoscope on the chest wall over the pulmonic landmark and listen for S‚ÇÇ.
  • (3) Place the diaphragm of the stethoscope on the chest wall over the tricuspid landmark and listen for S‚ÇÅ.
  • (4) Place the diaphragm of the stethoscope on the chest wall over the mitral landmark and listen for S‚ÇÅ.
  • (5) Auscultate for rate and rhythm when both sounds are heard clearly.
 Inspection, palpation and auscultation should be performed using a systematic approach, over the identified praecordial landmarks. The use of percussion is limited because X-rays and other diagnostic tests provide the same information more accurately.
No pulsations or vibrations or thrills should be visible or felt in the aortic, pulmonic or tricuspid areas. If pulsations or vibrations are palpated, their occurrence is timed in relation to systole or diastole by auscultating heart sounds simultaneously.
Auscultation of the heart detects normal heart sounds, extra heart sounds and murmurs. Normal sounds are created by the closing of the valves.
S‚ÇÇ is produced by the closure of the semilunar valves, and is louder than S‚ÇÅ at the aortic and pulmonic landmarks.
S‚ÇÅ is produced by the closure of the tricuspid and mitral valves. S‚ÇÅ at the tricuspid area is softer than at the mitral area.
S‚ÇÅ is heard loudest in the mitral area.
8. Assessment of peripheral vascular system
A. Upper limbs
  • (1) Inspection: lift both hands and inspect both sides, noting colour of skin, nail bed, temperature, texture and turgor and the presence of lesions, oedema or clubbing.
  • (2) Palpation:
  • a. Capillary return: depress the nail bed and release; note the time for colour return (usually 1‚Äì2 seconds).
  • b. Radial pulse: palpate both radial pulses, located laterally to the radius and apply pressure. Note the rate, rhythm, elasticity of the vessel wall and relative strength of the pulses (should be equal).
  • c. Brachial pulse: palpate the brachial pulse bilaterally; located medially in the cubital fossa. Note the rate, rhythm, elasticity of the vessel wall and relative strength of the pulses (should be equal).
 Flattening of angle and clubbing occur with congenital cyanotic heart disease and cor pulmonale.
Never use your thumb to take a pulse. The thumb contains a ‚Äòfalse‚Äô pulse that beats at a different rate to the heart. Never palpate both carotid arteries simultaneously‚Äîthis occludes the blood supply to the brain and surrounding structures.
It is not usually required to palpate the ulnar pulse; however, this is performed during the Allen test to identify collateral circulation and is commonly performed prior to arterial blood gas sampling.
B. Lower limbs
  • (1) Inspection: inspect both legs together, noting skin colour, hair distribution, venous patterns, size and any skin lesions, scars or ulcers. Both legs should be symmetrical without swelling or atrophy. Measure the limbs' circumference at an equal distance from each patella. Record your findings in centimetres.
  • (2) Palpation:
  • a. Palpate for temperature along both legs down to the feet. The skin should be warm and equal bilaterally.
  • b. Palpate the peripheral arteries in both legs. Note the rate, rhythm, elasticity of the vessel wall and relative strength of the pulses (should be equal).
  • c. Femoral pulse: located inferior to the inguinal ligament halfway between the pubis and anterior superior iliac spines.
  • d. Popliteal pulse: more diffuse and difficult to locate. Often it is lateral to the medial tendon.
  • e. Posterior tibial pulse: curve your fingers around the medial malleolus. You will feel tapping behind the groove between the malleolus and the Achilles tendon. If you cannot, try dorsiflexing the foot.
  • f. Dorsalis pedis: requires a very light touch. Normally it is just lateral to and parallel with the extensor tendon of the big toe.
 Abnormal:
‚Ä¢ Acute, unilateral painful calf swelling and asymmetry of calves of 1 cm or more is abnormal and could signify evidence of a deep-vein thrombosis
‚Ä¢ Asymmetry bilaterally 1‚Äì3 cm could indicate mild lymphoedema; 3‚Äì5 cm moderate lymphoedema; and >5 cm severe lymphoedema.
Never use your thumb to take a pulse. The thumb contains a 'false' pulse that beats at a different rate to the heart.
RECORDING AND REPORTING HOME CARE CONSIDERATIONS
  • The cardiovascular system plays a role in the function of many health systems, and therefore it is important to document any abnormal findings.
  • Documentation includes
    • Rate (beats per minute): normally 60‚Äì100 (adult), 70‚Äì130 (child, depending on age)
    • Rhythm: normally strong and regular
    • Character: unequal pulses; congenital abnormality in radial pulse‚Äîaortic arch aneurysm; lower limb pulses‚Äîatherosclerosis, arterial embolism
  • Compare all pulses bilaterally, taking note of the rate, rhythm and character of each pulse and document on the vascular observation chart.
  • All chest pain should be considered to be cardiac in nature until proven otherwise‚Äîurgent medical assistance is required.
  • Any abnormal finding could indicate impairment in the function of the cardiovascular system and should be considered a potential emergency and medical attention is required.

Reference: Estes MEZ 2010 Health assessment and physical examination, ed 4. New York, Cengage Learning.

ü´Å Skill 27-4: Assessment of the Respiratory System

DELEGATION CONSIDERATIONS

Assessment of the respiratory system may be undertaken by enrolled nurses under the guidance of the registered nurse.

  • Ensure review of the anatomy and physiology of the respiratory system for risk factors.
  • Ensure review of the anatomical landmarks of the chest wall.
  • Ensure hand hygiene is performed and personal protective equipment worn when appropriate.

EQUIPMENT

  • Stethoscope
  • Pen
  • Documentation
  • Watch (with a second hand)
  • Portable pulse oximeter
STEPS RATIONALE
1. Determine need to assess the respiratory function of the patient. The primary role of the respiratory system is to transfer oxygen into the body via the lungs and to remove carbon dioxide to the external environment. Respiration is a basic human need for survival. Nurses need to be able to recognise and understand the signs of impaired respiration or respiratory distress and provide urgent interventions.
2. Focused health history
a. Determine chief complaint‚Äîcharacteristics of pain and each sign/symptom.
b. Ascertain main respiratory symptoms:
  • dyspnoea
  • cough and sputum
  • haemoptysis
  • chest pain
  • wheeze (note: severe asthma may not have a wheeze)
c. Ascertain past medical history‚Äîany history of similar symptom(s) with any treatment and outcome:
  • childhood asthma, wheezing or 'bronchitis'
  • malignant disease
  • infections including pneumonia, tuberculosis and whooping cough
  • chest trauma and operations
  • thromboembolic disease, specifically deep vein thrombosis and pulmonary embolus
d. Ask about drug history:
  • use of inhalers
  • use of steroids
  • other relevant drugs that may affect respiratory function, e.g. morphine (respiratory depression), angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitors (cough).
e. Ask about allergies:
  • food
  • inhaled allergens
  • drugs
f. Ask about occupational and social history:
  • occupational history may be very important in respiratory disease
  • hobbies and pets may also be responsible for respiratory disease
g. Ask about lifestyle, including alcohol and illicit drug use.
h. Smoking history should detail, for example, the type and number of cigarettes smoked currently and in the past. 		The focused respiratory health history provides insight into the link between a patient's lifestyle and respiratory information and pathology.
3. Physical examination
A. Inspection
(1) Thorax:
  • Size
  • Shape
  • Symmetry
  • Contour
(2) Respiration:
  • Rate
  • Pattern
  • Inspiration/expiration ratio
  • Audible adventitious sounds
  • Use of accessory muscles
(3) Skin:
  • Condition
  • Colour
  • Superficial venous patterns
(4) Peripheries:
  • Lips
  • Fingers
  • Toes
 An accurate measurement of the respiratory system requires inspection, palpation and percussion of the chest wall. It will also include listening to the lung sounds heard during inspiration and expiration.
The depth of inspiration is assessed by the movement of the chest wall. Deep inspiration involves the complete expansion of the lungs with complete expiration.
B. Palpation
(1) Divide the thorax up into four sections: posterior chest, anterior chest, right and left lateral chest, and apices.
(2) General palpation.
a. Palpate the skin by gently placing the end of your fingers on the body; note temperature, skin condition.
b. Palpate thoracic muscles for abnormalities.
c. Palpate thoracic skeleton for abnormalities. If the patient identifies an area of abnormality or pain, inform them that you will palpate this last.
(3) Assess thoracic expansion.
a. Anterior hand position: hands are placed on the anterolateral chest with thumbs extended along the costal margin aimed at the xiphoid process.
b. Posterior hand position: thumbs are placed along the 10th rib with the hand placed gently on the posterolateral chest. Note: thumbs should be 3‚Äì5 cm apart.
(4) Assess tactile fremitus. While systematically palpating the thorax, have the patient repeat the words ‚Äòone, two, three‚Äô or, more commonly, ‚Äòninety-nine‚Äô. 		The tissue contains fine pockets of air that can be felt on palpation. During palpation a ‚Äòcrackling‚Äô can be felt‚Äîcrepitation, e.g. subcutaneous emphysema.
Thoracic expansion refers to the degree of expansion of the thorax that can be palpated during inspiration. Nurses need to note the rise and fall of the chest, symmetry and that the thorax expands.
Fremitus refers to a vibration that can be felt during palpation. Tactile fremitus is palpable against the thoracic wall when the patient is speaking (phonation). The strength of the vibration decreases as distance between the palpating hand and the sound transmission increases. Observed in e.g. pneumothorax or pleural effusion.
C. Percussion
Percussion is the ‚Äòtapping‚Äô of the body surface to identify underlying structures.		 In the context of a respiratory assessment percussion is used to identify the relative amount of air, liquid or solid matter in the lung and to identify the position and boundaries of organs.
D. Auscultation
a. The diaphragm of the stethoscope is commonly used to auscultate the chest (see Figure 27-43).		 Auscultation is a useful tool that can identify how the respiratory system is functioning. It allows you to be able to identify obstruction in the airway.
4. Monitor patient for signs of light-headedness or signs of hyperventilation.
RECORDING AND REPORTING HOME CARE CONSIDERATIONS
  • The respiratory system plays a role in the function of many health systems, and therefore it is important to document any abnormal findings.
  • Documentation includes:
    • rate (breaths per minute): normally 12‚Äì20 (adult), 20‚Äì30 (child)
    • rhythm: normally regular and deep
    • sound: normal breath sounds are called vesicular
  • Abnormal sounds could be evidence of an obstruction requiring urgent intervention and thus need to be reported immediately, including:
    • wheeze: narrowing of the airways
    • crackles: can represent the opening of alveoli and is evident in pulmonary congestion
    • bronchial breathing: harsh sounds and poor quality (gaps can be heard during the inspiratory and expiratory phases)
    • pleural rub: stiffening of the pleural membrane, commonly heard in pleurisy
    • stridor: harsh inspiratory sound evident of a partial obstruction of the airway. In children, stridor is often accompanied by a ‚Äòseal-like‚Äô barking cough in the case of croup.
  • Any abnormal finding could indicate an impairment in the function of the respiratory system and should be considered a potential emergency and medical attention is required.

üß† Skill 27-5: Mental State Assessment

Mental state assessment is a key nursing skill and provides a structured set of observations about the patient's cognitive and emotional state at a specific point in time.

DELEGATION CONSIDERATIONS

The mental state assessment can be conducted without supervision. Most aspects of the mental state assessment can be carried out as part of routine nursing care, i.e. they can be integrated into other nursing procedures.

Before proceeding:

  • review the categories of mental state assessment, and communication skills
  • revise the structured format for mental state assessment (BATOMI) (see Box 44-4)

EQUIPMENT

  • Mental health assessment uses communication skills and therapeutic use of self
  • Consider privacy and appropriateness of setting
STEPS RATIONALE
1. Determine need to assess mental state of patient. Mental state is changeable, and is influenced by a range of biological, psychological and emotional factors.
2. Focused mental health history
a. Consider the patient's usual mental state, and how mental state might be affected by illness.
b. Consider the presence of factors that may affect mental state:
  • pain/discomfort
  • sedative or other effects of medication
  • tiredness
  • anxiety about health/hospitalisation
  • sensory loss or impairment
  • nausea or other symptoms
  • any history of mental illness
  • alcohol or drug use
c. Procedure:
  • (1) Initiate communication by introductions, clarification of the patient's immediate needs and problems.
  • (2) Consider positioning of nurse and patient to assist communication, i.e. by enabling eye contact.
  • (3) Ideally sit at the same level as the patient.
  • (4) Continue with assessment using guideline below to assess mental state.
 The focused mental health history provides insight into the links between a patient's general health and their cognitive and emotional state.
Promotes rapport and psychological comfort during assessment.
3. Assess mental state
A. Assessment of appearance‚Äîincludes both expected and unusual aspects of appearance. Observe for:
  • (1) Gross appearance (stature, state of dress, appropriateness of dress for current situation).
  • (2) Patient's apparent age compared with recorded age.
  • (3) Position during assessment (sitting, lying).
  • (4) Grooming and evidence of self-care or neglect.
  • (5) Jewellery, makeup.
  • (6) Distinguishing features (complexion, marks on skin, any observable disabilities).
  • (7) Facial expressions.
 Appearance provides an initial set of clues to the patient's mental state.
Expected and unusual aspects of appearance are equally important.
B. Assessment of behaviour‚Äîincludes all observable behaviour. Observe for:
  • (1) Physical signs of anxiety or distress (handwringing, nervous mannerisms, plucking at clothes/hair, clutching objects such as purse or personal items).
  • (2) Changes in behaviour in response to assessment.
  • (3) Any tremor or involuntary movements.
  • (4) Gestures with hands, eyes, head.
  • (5) Crying, laughing or other behavioural indications of mood.
  • (6) Eye contact with interviewer (observe for constant, intermittent, intense or absent eye contact).
  • (7) Motor activity (settled/relaxed, changes of position, restlessness).
 Many changes in mental state are apparent from observations of behaviour.
Changes in behaviour can provide an indication of concerns and anxieties.
C. Assessment of affect and mood‚Äîincludes direct and indirect observations of emotional state.
  • (1) Emotional responsiveness (warm, engaging, distant, aloof).
  • (2) Responses to topics of discussion.
  • (3) Crying, smiling, laughing and other behavioural indicators of emotional state.
  • (4) Conversational triggers to emotional responses.
  • (5) Congruence between emotional response and content of conversation.
  • (6) Irritability/anger.
  • (7) Indications of anxiety.
  • (8) Patient‚Äôs self-rating of mood (1 = lowest ever; 10 = best ever).
 Assessment of mood includes both the nurse‚Äôs assessment and the patient‚Äôs statements about their mood.
Affect may change throughout an interview or procedure; changes can be as important as overall mood.
D. Assessment of thought and speech‚Äîincludes directly observed speech and underlying thought processes. Make allowances for age, culture, language. Observe for:
  • (1) Rate of speech (slowed, normal, accelerated, pressured).
  • (2) Themes of thought and speech (preoccupations, guilt, depressive themes, dominant concerns).
  • (3) Reduced/absent speech.
  • (4) Organisation of speech (coherent, logical, complete sentences, goal oriented).
  • (5) Evidence of high ideas that are out of context (i.e. paranoid ideas, delusions).
  • (6) Evidence of unexplained perceptions (voices, visual hallucinations).
 Patterns of thought are assessed by attending to speech.
Patients can also be asked to describe their thoughts.
E. Assessment of orientation
(1) Aspects of orientation are:
  • Time
  • Place
  • Person.
(2) Orientation is assessed by asking specific questions or interpreting conversational cues to orientation (e.g. names, time, place of assessment). 		Orientation provides important clues to organic or functional confusional states as well as additional information about problems of mood, thinking and perception.
F. Assessment of memory‚Äîincludes all retention of information, whether short or long term.
(1) Observe for:
  • Attention/concentration/registration
  • Short-term memory
  • Long-term memory.
(2) Ask specific questions if you need to test different aspects of memory, i.e. can the patient remember your name:
  • immediately on being introduced (registration)
  • five minutes after introduction (short-term memory)
  • the day after an introduction (long-term memory)
 Memory can be disrupted in many illnesses and when emotional and cognitive functioning is impaired.
General assessment conversation can provide important information about memory.
G. Assessment of insight and judgment‚Äîassesses and records the patient's understanding of their current health problems. Observe for:
  • (1) Realistic perception of current health problems.
  • (2) Realistic perception of any need for treatment.
  • (3) Awareness of consequences of own choices.
 Insight is important in order to engage patient in a plan of care.
Impaired cognitive and emotional functioning can lead to reduced insight. Assessment of judgment can be an important indicator of safety.
RECORDING AND REPORTING HOME CARE CONSIDERATIONS
  • Mental state assessment is recorded in a narrative style using a structured format with headings for each component of assessment (see Box 45-5). Issues of concern such as low mood or anxiety should be discussed with a senior colleague or supervisor.
  • Each mental state assessment should be compared with the patient's baseline assessment.
  • Mental state assessment can be undertaken in the home as part of routine nursing care.
  • The assessment may need to be abridged if there is not sufficient privacy.

ü©∫ Skill 27-6: CNS and Level of Consciousness Assessment

DELEGATION CONSIDERATIONS

Assessment of the central nervous system (CNS) may be undertaken by enrolled nurses under the guidance of the registered nurse.

  • Ensure review of the anatomy and physiology of the CNS for risk factors
  • Ensure review of the Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS)
  • Ensure hand hygiene is performed and personal protective equipment is worn when appropriate.

EQUIPMENT

  • Penlight torch
  • Neurological observation chart
  • Pen
STEPS RATIONALE
1. Determine need to assess neurological status of patient.
2. Focused health history
a. Determine chief complaint‚Äîcharacteristics of pain and each sign/symptom using the PQRST approach (see Chapter 6).
b. Ascertain presence of:
  • headache
  • seizure‚Äîlocation (i.e. body parts involved), quantity in time, quality (general or localised)
  • associated manifestations‚Äîincontinence, injury, memory loss and cyanosis
  • aggravating factors‚Äîbright/flashing lights, TV, sleep deprivation, stress, fever in children, alcohol.
c. Ask about setting‚Äîwarning aura, sequence of events, e.g. headache
d. Ask about timing‚Äîfirst onset.
e. Assess syncope‚Äîquality, i.e. total vs partial loss of consciousness; and quantity in time.
f. Assess pain‚Äîlocation and quality
g. Assess paraesthesia and associated manifestations, e.g. pain, stiffness, gait changes, poor peripheral pulses, injury
h. Assess disturbances in gait: quality‚Äîataxic, spastic, hemiplegic
i. Assess visual changes: quality‚Äîblindness, in particular fields of vision, blurred vision, bright/flashing lights
j. Ask about vertigo.
k. Assess for memory disorders: quality‚Äîrecent or remote memory loss
l. Assess for dysphasia or dysphagia.
m. Determine past history of neurological-specific conditions and surgery, and non-neurological-specific conditions.
n. Ask about social history‚Äîalcohol use, work environment, recreation and leisure, sexual practice, stress. 		Certain conditions place patients at risk of neurological alterations (stroke, seizure, paralysis). Physical signs and symptoms such as confusion, drowsiness and limb weakness may indicate alterations in neurological function.
As cognitive function deteriorates, the patient becomes disorientated to time, place and person and is unable to process and demonstrate higher order functioning.
The focused neurological health history provides insight into the link between a patient‚Äôs lifestyle and neurological information and pathology.
3. Explain procedure to patient and clarify any concerns. Promotes patient's cooperation and comfort during the assessment.
4. Ensure patient comfort and privacy.
5. Neurological assessment commences with a Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) score to measure level of consciousness (see Table 27-25).
A. Eye opening
(1) Observe the patient's eyes when entering the room. Patient assessed in this level of consciousness should be recorded as 'spontaneous' (score 4).
(2) If the patient is not awake, give the patient a command such as: 'Open your eyes.' Positive patient response should be recorded as eye opening 'to speech' (score 3).
(3) If the patient does not respond to verbal stimuli, assess their response to a painful stimulus (see motor response below); progress from light to deep stimulus. Positive patient response should be recorded as eye opening 'to pain' (score 2).
(4) There may be no response to speech or painful stimuli. Patient assessed in this level of consciousness should be recorded as 'no response' (score 1). 		The GCS assesses the patient's level of consciousness. It is able to evaluate the brain function as a whole, rather than assessing particular lobes of the brain.
The level of consciousness exists along a continuum from full waking, alertness and cooperation through to any form of external stimuli. A fully conscious patient responds to questions spontaneously. As consciousness lowers, a patient may show irritability, a shortened attention span or an unwillingness to cooperate.
The patient's eyes typically should be open (depending on time of day or presentation).
B. Best verbal response
(1) Assess orientation by seeking the best verbal response from the patient to simple questions: 'What is your name?' 'Where are you at the moment?' 'What day is it today?' 'Do you know what month it is?' 'Do you know what year it is?' If answered correctly, patient response should be recorded as 'orientated' (score 5).
(2) Patients should be able to hold a conversation and answer the above questions correctly. If answered incorrectly, patient response should be recorded as 'confused' (score 4).
(3) Patient may respond with single words and answer does not match the question asked. Patient response should be recorded as 'inappropriate words' (score 3).
(4) Patient may respond with incomprehensible sounds only (such as moans and groans). Patient response should be recorded as 'incomprehensible sounds' (score 2).
(5) There may be no response to any stimuli. Patient response should be recorded as 'no response' (score 1).
C. Best motor response
(1) Assess the patient's ability to follow two simple commands such as: 'Stick out your tongue.' 'Lift up your arms.' 'Squeeze and let go of my hands.' Positive patient response should be recorded as 'obeys commands' (score 6).
(2) If patient is unable to perform the above activities, apply a central painful stimulus (e.g. trapezium squeeze) to determine the best arm response.
a. The patient may demonstrate purposeful movement to remove the painful stimulus, bringing the hand up to at least chin level. Patient response should be recorded as 'localises to pain' (score 5).
b. The patient may bend the arm at the elbows but fails to locate the pain. Patient response should be recorded as 'flexion withdrawal' (score 4).
c. The patient may demonstrate abnormal flexion (decorticate posturing): flexion of the elbow, wrist and fingers, with adduction of the limb. Lower limbs may also be in extension, internal rotation and plantar flexion. Patient response should be recorded as 'abnormal flexion' (score 3).
d. The patient may demonstrate abnormal extension (decerebrate posturing): elbow extension, adduction and internal rotation with palm pronated. Lower limbs may also be in extension and plantar flexion. Patient response should be recorded as ‚Äòabnormal extension‚Äô (score 2).
e. There may be no response after the application of a central painful stimulus. Patient response should be recorded as ‚Äòno response‚Äô (score 1). 		As neurological function deteriorates motor responses are reduced. A patient with serious neurological impairment exhibits abnormal posturing in response to pain.
6. Total GCS scores out of 15 and document on neurological observation chart. Overall score or change in score determines need to report to medical officer.
7. Assess pupil reaction and gauge.
a. Check pupillary response for shape, size (mm) and reaction to light.
b. Use a pen torch to elicit a response from the pupils‚Äîshine the light from the peripheries of the eye, to the centre.
c. Note pupillary response for both eyes, which should be brisk constriction to light.
d. Measure pupil size, which should be equal.
8. Assess limb power.
A. Bilateral upper limb muscle strength
(1) Ask the patient to take your hands and squeeze.
(2) Ask the patient to pull your hands towards them against resistance.
(3) Ask the patient to push your hands away against resistance.
B. Bilateral lower limb muscle strength
(1) Ask the patient to push down with their foot onto your hands against resistance.
(2) Ask the patient to lift up their foot against your hand with resistance.
(3) Apply gently pressure to the shins and ask the patient to extend and flex their legs against resistance. 		Response is recorded on a continuum from normal through mild weakness, severe weakness, flexion, extension to no response.
9. Continue to perform neurological assessment as required by presentation and hospital protocol. The GCS allows the evaluation of a patient‚Äôs status over time. The higher the score, the more improved or normal the level of functioning.
RECORDING AND REPORTING HOME CARE CONSIDERATIONS
  • Record GCS scores with date and time of assessment. Report decrease in GCS by two or more points to medical officer immediately.
  • Pupils‚Äô response: document size of pupil (mm) and response to light.
  • Limb movements‚Äô response: document power of movements of arms and legs.
  • Act FAST:
    • Face: check their face‚Äîhas their mouth drooped?
    • Arms: can they lift both arms?
    • Speech: is their speech slurred? Do they understand you?
    • Time: is critical. If you see any of these signs, call for emergency medical assistance (National Stroke Foundation, 2012).
