Urine and Stool Sample Collection

01

üîç Definition & Types

Definition: The procedure of obtaining samples of body waste (urine or feces) for laboratory analysis to aid in diagnosis and treatment.

  • Mid-Stream Urine (MSU): Clean catch for culture and sensitivity.
  • 24-Hour Urine Collection: For quantitative analysis of substances (e.g., proteins, hormones).
  • Catheter Specimen of Urine (CSU): Sterile collection from a catheter port (never the bag).
  • Stool for Ova & Cysts: To detect parasites.
  • Stool for Occult Blood: To detect hidden bleeding.
02

üíß Mid-Stream Urine (MSU)

Goal: To collect urine from the bladder that is not contaminated by bacteria from the urethra or external genitalia.

Concept Diagram 

    [ 1. Start Voiding ] --> [ 2. Collect ] --> [ 3. Finish Voiding ]
          (Flush)             (Sample)             (Flush)
             |                   |                    |
       [   Toilet   ]       [ Sterile Jar ]      [   Toilet   ]

Implementation:

  1. Wash hands and clean the genital area (front to back for females).
  2. Instruct patient to begin voiding into the toilet/bedpan.
  3. Interrupt the stream and pass the sterile container into the stream.
  4. Collect 30-60ml of urine.
  5. Remove container and allow patient to finish voiding.
  6. Label specimen immediately.
03

üí© Stool Sample Collection

Step Rationale
1. Provide a clean bedpan/commode. Sample must not be mixed with urine or toilet water.
2. Instruct patient not to urinate into the pan. Urine can destroy parasites or alter results.
3. Use a spatula to transfer stool to the specimen jar. Aseptic technique.
4. Collect from different parts of the stool (blood/mucus areas). Increases likelihood of detecting pathogens.
5. Seal and label. Prevents contamination and ensures identification.
04

üì¢ Important Guidelines

‚ö†Ô∏è Timeliness

Send specimens to the laboratory immediately. Urine can change pH and bacteria can multiply if left at room temperature. Stool parasites may die if the sample cools.

  • Labeling: Patient Name, ID, Date, Time, Test Required.
  • Safety: Always wear gloves. Treat all specimens as potentially biohazardous.
  • Storage: If delay is unavoidable, refrigerate urine (if protocol allows).
