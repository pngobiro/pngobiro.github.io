üîç Overview of Vital Signs

Measuring vital signs is the process of assessing temperature, pulse, respiration, blood pressure, and pain to detect physiological deviations from normal.

  • Temperature: Measuring the balance between heat production and loss.
  • Pulse: Measurement of pressure pulsation created by heart contractions.
  • Respirations: Assessment of breathing pattern and rate.
  • Blood Pressure: Force of blood against arterial walls.
  • Pain: Subjective evaluation of reported discomfort.
02

üìã Clinical Considerations

  • Check for hot/cold food intake in the last 15-30 mins (affects temperature).
  • Assess activities in the last 15-30 mins (affects pulse/BP).
  • Ensure patient has rested for at least 5 minutes before measurement.
  • Choose appropriate sites (axillary, groin, rectal, radial, apical).
03

‚öôÔ∏è Implementation Highlights

Temperature

Axillary: Dry the site first. Place thermometer bulb in the skin fold and hold firmly until the alarm sounds (or 4-7 mins for non-digital).

Pulse

Radial: Use fingertips of index and middle fingers (never the thumb). Count for a full minute to note rate, rhythm, and volume.

Apical: Auscultate over the fifth intercostal space, left midclavicular line.

Respirations

Observe the complete cycle without drawing the patient's attention to the activity to avoid alterations in breathing patterns.

Blood Pressure

Support forearm at heart level. Inflate cuff to 30mmHg above the point where the pulse disappears. Deflate at 2mmHg/sec while listening for Korotkoff sounds.

04

üìù Documentation

Always record: Date, time, findings on appropriate charts, any actions taken for abnormalities, and the patient's response.
