üì° Communicating Research Findings

The significance of conducting research is to communicate and share the research findings. If you do not disseminate the findings of your study, then nobody will know what the outcome of the study was. There are various ways of communicating the research findings. They include:

  • A written report for academic purposes, for example, a dissertation or a thesis, which are a requirement in the obtaining of a certain academic level.
  • A written report prepared for managers and policy implementers (some of whom have no knowledge of research methodology). In such reports you need to have a very clear and succinct report without the academic jargons.
  • A written report sent as an article for publication in refereed journals.
  • Presentations of the research findings in workshops, seminars and conferences.

üé§ Presenting at Professional Conferences

Because publication in journals which is referred takes long (up to 3 years), presentation of research findings is more rapid in distribution of study results. Presentation should be in nursing conferences as well as interdisciplinary conferences.

Research results can be presented at professional conferences in two ways:

  • Oral presentation
  • Poster presentation‚Äî has become more popular

Potential participants are contacted through "call for abstracts". The call for abstracts is a request for a summary for a study that the researcher wishes to present at a conference. These requests are published in professional journals and distributed to educational institutions, health care agencies and potential participants from professional organizations notices of research.

Conferences are generally distributed 6-12 months before the event. Each conference or seminar provide guidelines for presenters and for submission of abstracts. Abstract length varies from 50-1000 words, but many have 200-300 word limitations. It contains the purpose, research question(s), hypothesis, design, methodology, major findings and conclusions.

Generally, those selected to participate receive no pay and are required to cover their own travel expenses. In some cases conference registration fee is waived or reduced for participants.

Presenting a Research Paper (Oral)

An oral presentation of a research report is usually referred to as paper presentation. Paper because it has been written on paper and is read by the investigator. If principal investigator is unable to attend the conference, a co-investigator or another person familiar with the study presents the paper.

Advantages

  • The researcher presents findings that are current/recent unlike publication that lags behind for up to 3 years.
  • The researcher has an opportunity to interact with those people who are interested in the study and will be able to locate other researchers who are studying the same or similar phenomena.

The presenter is usually given 10-15 minutes. Sometimes additional time is allocated for questions or during break, other questions are dealt with. Format for oral presentation is the same as that of the journal but condensation of material is done to fit the time given.

Presenting a Research Poster

Has become popular. This is a visual method of presentation. During a poster session, the presenters usually remain with their posters and interact with viewers. Posters sessions generally are held during research conferences where oral presentations are also given. 1-2 hours is devoted to posters. Some conferences may have only poster presentation.

Poster presentation sessions are an excellent way for beginning research to 'get their feet wet' is not scary as standing up in front of an audience and orally discussing a study.

  • Size: Sizes of posters vary according to different research conference requirements. Common size is 4 feet by 8 feet.
  • Design: Posters should be carefully constructed, colour combinations should be used to be attractive. Do not put too much material on a poster, diagrams, graphs, and tables are effective means of presenting certain aspects of the study such as findings.
  • Content: Posters should contain the research problem, hypothesis, research question, description of the sample, methods, findings, major conclusions.

üì∞ Publication of a Journal Article

The growth of the nursing profession depends on the ability of its members to share a body of knowledge. Nursing research is the method of building the knowledge and publications are the major medium for sharing this knowledge. Research should be conducted with the idea of publication in mind.

  • A few journals pay authors a per-page fee. Generally authors do not receive any compensation.
  • Authors may pay for any revisions they may wish to be made on the galleys received.
  • Galley proofs are sheets of paper showing how the article will appear in type set format.
  • Some journals provide an author with several complimentary copies of the issue in which the article appears.
  • When an author receives an acceptance letter from a journal, an appropriate date or month of publication is usually included. (1 month-3 years lag).

Preparing the Article

Length of journal varies but most editions prefer articles of 10-15 typed page. The format and sections vary according to the journal parts including introduction, literature review, methods, findings and discussion.

Selecting a Journal

Selecting an appropriate journal is an important decision. Check local and international journal (online) that can publish the research findings. They could be nursing only or nursing and allied journals. The nurse author should not forget magazines designed for the general public.

Types of Journals

  • Referred journal: is one in which subject experts, chosen by journals editorial staff, evaluate manuscripts.
  • Non referred journal: is one that uses editorial members of staff or consultants to evaluate manuscripts.

Sending Query Letters

Before submitting an article at a journal, it may be wise for the author to first determine the journals interest in the manuscript. This is done by a letter of inquiry called query letter. A query letter contains an outline of a manuscript and important information about the manuscript that an author sends to an editor to determine the editor's interest in publishing the material.

It should be addressed to the editor by name. On receiving the query, the editor may;

  • Request for submission of manuscript or
  • Suggestions for revision in the manuscript then submission or
  • Discourage submission of the manuscript

Many journals require a signed statement that the manuscript is not being considered by any other journal.

Reasons for Rejection of the Manuscript

  • Poorly written manuscript
  • Poorly developed idea, not consistent with purpose of journal
  • Methodology problems
  • Content undocumented, content inaccurate e.t.c.

‚öôÔ∏è Utilization of Research Findings

The use research findings in nursing practice is called research utilization. In the past, many actions of nurses have been based on tradition or authority. This is no longer acceptable in this day of evidence-based practice. Nurses should be able to justify the discussions they make and the care they give.

Barriers to the utilization of nursing research have been identified with means to bridge them. These barriers include:

i. Nurses lack of knowledge
Many nurses are unaware of many research findings. Role models in research for less experienced stuff, create nursing research department, creation of formal nursing committee and presentation of research grand round are just but few suggested ways to improve utilization.
ii. Nurses negative attitudes
Negative attitudes towards research has contributed to underutilization of research findings. Can be addressed by having role models in clinical area who value research, have research courses at all levels of education and also having faulty with positive attitudes who help students early in their careers.
iii. Inadequate dissemination of research findings
Most nursing research studies are never published or presented at research meetings or workshops. Also those that are published or presented are not written or verbally presented at a level where the practicing nurse can understand the findings. Many practicing nurses complain about their ability to understand articles in the research journals, language is technical and length for researchers rather than for clinicians. Researchers in academic settings are sometimes rewarded for publication in practice journals. Nurses need to publish in popular journals, make research findings understandable. Also consider publication and presentation for the lay media to counter this problem.
iv. Lack of institutional support
Nurses frequently perceive that there is little institutional support for nursing research. To remove this barrier, nurses should get time off to attend nursing conferences; institutions should make available current journals available in the institution‚Äôs library and allocating time for nurses to read research reports during the workday.
v. Findings are not ready for use in practice
Study findings is not something that can be crammed over night and ingested in the morning. No study findings should be implemented if the study has not been replicated in several clinical settings, with similar results being found. For research findings to be ready for use in practice, the findings must have been replicated at least once and demonstrated to be true with "red" patients as well with "normal" subjects.

üë©‚Äç‚öïÔ∏è The Role of Nurses in Research

There are many roles that nurses can assume in association with research projects. These include:

1. Principal Investigator

Nurses can serve as principal investigators in scientific investigations. However one needs special research preparations nurses can also conduct small-scale survey study.

2. Member of a Research Team

Nurses can serve as members of research team. They may act as data collectors or administer the experimental intervention of the study. As nurses participate in research interest in research increases and the enthusiasm to conduct their own research grow.

3. Identifier of Researchable Problems

Nurses of all levels, have responsibility of trying to identify areas of needed research. Nurses at the bedside are particularly well situated to identify patient related researchable problem.

4. Evaluator of Research Findings

Every nurse should be involved in the evaluation of research findings. As research consumers, nurses have the obligation to become familiar with research findings and determine the usefulness of these findings in the practice area. Beginning researches should antique research articles with experienced researchers and eventually on their own. Evaluation of research is not an easy task and one needs to acquire some of the skills needed to antique research articles and reports.

5. User of Research Findings

After evaluating research findings, nurses should use relevant findings in their practice. The primary goal of nursing research is improved care to patients/clients.

6. Patient/Client Advocate

All nurses have the responsibility to act as patient/client advocates when their patient/clients are involved in research. The advocacy involves making sure that the ethical aspects of research are upheld. Nurses might help in explaining a study to potential participants before study begins.

7. Subject in Studies

Nurses can act as subjects in research. Issues about nurses welfare or work place issues such as motivation among others can be done nurses being subjects of study.
