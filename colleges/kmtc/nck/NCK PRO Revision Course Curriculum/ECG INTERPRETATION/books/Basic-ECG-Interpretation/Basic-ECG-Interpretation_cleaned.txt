<p>Electrocardiography is a fundamental part of cardiovascular assessment. It is an essential tool for investigating cardiac arrhythmias and is also useful in diagnosing cardiac disorders such as myocardial infarction. Familiarity with the wide range of patterns seen in the electrocardiograms of normal subjects and an understanding of the effects of non-cardiac disorders on the trace are prerequisites to accurate interpretation.</p>

<p>The six chest leads (V1 to V6) "view" the heart in the horizontal plane. The information from the limb electrodes is combined to produce the six limb leads (I, II, III, aVR, aVL, and aVF), which view the heart in the vertical plane. The information from these 12 leads is combined to form a standard electrocardiogram.</p>
<p><img src="assets/images/image-20260119-0e3cdf3e.jpeg" alt="img-6.jpeg">
Vertical and horizontal perspective of the leads. The limb leads "view" the heart in the vertical plane and the chest leads in the horizontal plane</p>
<ul>
<li>leads II, III, and aVF view the inferior surface of the heart;</li>
<li>leads V1 to V4 view the anterior surface;</li>
<li>leads I, aVL, V5, and V6 view the lateral surface; and</li>
<li>leads V1 and aVR look through the right atrium directly into the cavity of the left ventricle.</li>
</ul>

<p>The amplitude of the waveform recorded in any lead may be influenced by;</p>
<p>the myocardial mass,
the net vector of depolarisation,
the thickness and properties of the intervening tissues, and
the distance between the electrode and the myocardium.</p>
<p>Patients with ventricular hypertrophy have a relatively large myocardial mass and are therefore likely to have high amplitude waveforms. In the presence of pericardial fluid, pulmonary emphysema, or obesity, there is increased resistance to current flow, and thus waveform amplitude is reduced.</p>

<p>The direction of the deflection on the electrocardiogram depends on whether the electrical impulse is travelling towards or away from a detecting electrode.</p>
<ul>
<li>By convention, an electrical impulse travelling directly towards the electrode produces an upright ("positive") deflection relative to the isoelectric baseline whereas</li>
<li>an impulse moving directly away from an electrode produces a downward ("negative") deflection relative to the baseline.</li>
<li>When the wave of depolarisation is at right angles to the lead, an equiphasic deflection is produced.</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="cardiac-axis">Cardiac axis<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#cardiac-axis" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>The cardiac axis refers to the mean direction of the wave of ventricular depolarisation in the vertical plane, measured from a zero-reference point. The zero-reference point looks at the heart from the same viewpoint as lead I. An axis lying above this line is given a negative number, and an axis lying below the line is given a positive number. Theoretically, the cardiac axis may lie anywhere between 180 and - 180°. The normal range for the cardiac axis is between - 30° and 90°. An axis lying beyond - 30° is termed left axis deviation, whereas an axis &gt; 90° is termed right axis deviation.</p>
<p>Conditions for which determination of the axis is helpful in diagnosis;</p>
<ul>
<li>Conduction defects—for example, left anterior hemiblock</li>
<li>Ventricular enlargement—for example, right ventricular hypertrophy</li>
<li>Broad complex tachycardia—for example, bizarre axis suggestive of ventricular origin</li>
<li>Congenital heart disease—for example, atrial septal defects</li>
<li>Pre-excited conduction—for example, Wolff-Parkinson-White syndrome</li>
<li>Pulmonary embolus</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="p-wave">P Wave<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#p-wave" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Characteristics of the P wave</p>
<p>a) Positive in leads I and II
b) Best seen in leads II and V1
c) Commonly biphasic in lead V1
d) &lt; 3 small squares in duration
e) &lt; 2.5 small squares in amplitude</p>
<p>The wave of depolarisation is directed inferiorly and towards the left, and thus the P wave tends to be upright in leads I and II and inverted in lead aVR. A negative P wave in lead I may be due to;</p>
<ul>
<li>incorrect recording of the electrocardiogram (that is, with transposition of the left and right arm electrodes),</li>
<li>dextrocardia, or</li>
<li>abnormal atrial rhythms.</li>
</ul>
<p>The P wave in V1 is often biphasic. Early right atrial forces are directed anteriorly, giving rise to an initial positive deflection; these are followed by left atrial forces travelling posteriorly, producing a later negative deflection. A large negative deflection (area of &gt; one small square) suggests left atrial enlargement.</p>
<p>Normal P waves may have a slight notch, particularly in the precordial (chest) leads. Bifid P waves result from slight asynchrony between right and left atrial depolarisation. A pronounced notch with a peak-to-peak interval of &gt; 1 mm (0.04 s) is usually pathological (delayed conduction) and is seen in association with a left atrial abnormality—for example, in mitral stenosis.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="pr-interval">PR Interval<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#pr-interval" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>

The PR interval is the time between the onset of atrial depolarisation and the onset of ventricular depolarisation. Abnormalities of the conducting system may lead to transmission delays, prolonging the PR interval.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="qrs-complex">QRS complex<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#qrs-complex" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>The wave of depolarisation reaches the endocardium at the apex of the ventricles, and then travels to the epicardium, spreading outwards in all directions. Depolarisation of the right and left ventricles produces opposing electrical vectors, but the left ventricle has the larger muscle mass and its depolarisation dominates the electrocardiogram.</p>
<p>Nomenclature in QRS complexes</p>
<ul>
<li>Q wave: Any initial negative deflection</li>
<li>R wave: Any positive deflection</li>
<li>S wave: Any negative deflection after an R wave</li>
</ul>
<p>The duration of the QRS complex is measured in the lead with the widest complex and should not exceed 0.10 s (2½ small squares). Delays in ventricular depolarisation—for example, bundle branch block—give rise to abnormally wide QRS complexes (≥ 0.12 s).</p>
<p>The left side of the septum depolarises first, and the impulse then spreads towards the right. Lead V1 lies immediately to the right of the septum and thus registers an initial small positive deflection (R wave) as the depolarisation wave travels towards this lead.</p>
<p>When the wave of septal depolarisation travels away from the recording electrode, the first deflection inscribed is negative. Thus small “septal” Q waves are often present in the lateral leads, usually leads I, aVL, V5, and V6. These non-pathological Q waves are;
a. &lt;1 small square wide,
b. &lt; 2 small squares deep
c. &lt; ¼ of the amplitude of the corresponding R wave.</p>
<p>In the precordial leads, QRS morphology changes depending on whether the depolarisation forces are moving towards or away from a lead. The forces generated by the free wall of the left ventricle predominate, and therefore in lead V1 a small R wave is followed by a large negative deflection (S wave).</p>
<p>Differential Diagnosis for a positive R wave in V1;</p>
<ul>
<li>Normal in children and young adults</li>
<li>Right Ventricular Hypertrophy</li>
<li>Pulmonary Embolus</li>
<li>Persistence of infantile pattern</li>
<li>Left to right shunt</li>
<li>Right Bundle Branch Block</li>
<li>Posterior Myocardial Infarction (ST elevation in Leads V7, V8, V9)</li>
<li>WPW type A (Wolff-Parkinson-White)</li>
<li>Incorrect lead placement (e.g. V1 and V3 reversed)</li>
<li>Dextrocardia</li>
<li>Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy</li>
<li>Dystrophy (Myotonic dystrophy, Duchenne Muscular dystrophy)</li>

<p>The R wave in the precordial leads steadily increases in amplitude from lead V1 to V6, with a corresponding decrease in S wave depth, culminating in a predominantly positive complex in V6. Thus, the QRS complex gradually changes from being predominantly negative in lead V1 to being predominantly positive in lead V6. The lead with an equiphasic QRS complex is located over the transition zone; this lies between leads V3 and V4 but shifts towards the left with age.</p>
<p><img src="assets/images/image-20260119-9f61b7a7.jpeg" alt="img-12.jpeg"></p>
<p>The height of the R wave is variable and increases progressively across the precordial leads; it is usually &lt; 27 mm in leads V5 and V6. The R wave in lead V6, however, is often smaller than the R wave in V5, since the V6 electrode is further from the left ventricle. A tall R wave is defined as an R wave which is the same size as an S wave.</p>
<p>The S wave is deepest in the right precordial leads; it decreases in amplitude across the precordium and is often absent in leads V5 and V6. The depth of the S wave should not exceed 30 mm in a normal individual, although S waves and R waves &gt; 30 mm are occasionally recorded in normal young male adults.</p>
<p>Low voltage is generally defined as QRS amplitudes of &lt; 5 mm in all the limb leads or QRS amplitudes of &lt; 10 mm in all the precordial leads. The differential diagnosis for low voltage includes;</p>

<li>Myxoedema</li>
<li>Large pericardial effusion</li>
<li>Large pleural effusion</li>
<li>End-stage cardiomyopathy</li>
<li>Severe chronic obstructive pulmonary disease</li>
<li>Severe obesity</li>
<li>Infiltrative myocardial diseases</li>
