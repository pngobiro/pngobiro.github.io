<p>This unit gives an introduction to management and is divided into five sections. The first section explains the concept of management and developments in management theory. Sections two and three cover the principles and functions of management respectively. The final two sections cover the utilisation of various management concepts, theories, principles and functions in the management of nursing care services.</p>
<p>On completion of this unit, you should be able to effectively manage the nursing care services in your area of work.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="section-1-the-concept-of-management">SECTION 1: THE CONCEPT OF MANAGEMENT<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#section-1-the-concept-of-management" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Introduction</p>
<p>During the course of your practice as a nurse and in your day to day life, you have come across some aspects of management even though you may not have been aware of it. This section will introduce you to the concept of management and briefly describe the developments in some of the management theories which have influenced health and nursing management practice.</p>

<li>Describe the meaning of management.</li>
<li>Describe different approaches to the definition of management.</li>
<li>Describe the importance of studying management.</li>
<li>Describe the main approaches to the study of organisation's structure and management.</li>
<li>Describe the historical development of management.</li>
<li>Describe how management theories apply to nursing practice.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="definitions-of-management">Definitions of Management<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#definitions-of-management" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="different-approaches-to-the-definition-of-management">Different Approaches to the Definition of Management<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#different-approaches-to-the-definition-of-management" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>Management may be viewed as:</p>
<ul>
<li>A social position</li>
<li>A function</li>
<li>The people who discharge it</li>
<li>An authority</li>
<li>A discipline</li>
<li>A field of study</li>
</ul>
<p>Different writers have defined management in various ways. The common definitions are listed below.</p>
<p>Management is:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>The people charged with the responsibility of running an organisation, for example, the management of Kenyatta National Hospital (KNH).</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The process by which resources are mobilised, combined and coordinated effectively to achieve organisational objectives.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Getting things done.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Getting work done through the efforts of other people. That is:</p>
</li>
</ul>
<ol>
<li>People are the most important means or resource for getting things done.</li>
<li>People must work, performing certain activities and tasks to reach certain ends or objectives.</li>
<li>Managers are judged not just on their performance but on the results achieved by their subordinates.</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>The efficient use of resources.</li>
<li>Getting people to work harmoniously together, that is, careful balancing of resources and where everyone knows what they are supposed to do.</li>
<li>Making a decision, that is, making a choice between two or more courses of action, around an act (what), quantity, quality or authority needed.</li>
<li>The process of achieving organisational effectiveness within a changing environment by balancing efficiency, effectiveness and equity thereby obtaining the most from limited resources and working with and through other people.</li>
</ul>
<p>The definition of management is based on two main principles:</p>
<ul>
<li>The first principle lays emphasis on people, who are the most important resource in any organisation.</li>
<li>The second principle emphasises a commitment to the achievement of organisational goals. This is the primary task of management.</li>
</ul>
<p>Management is active and involves changing behaviour and making things happen. It is also an everyday activity involving interactions between people that are not unrelated or entirely dissimilar to other spheres of life.</p>
<p>It can be regarded as:</p>
<ul>
<li>Taking place within a structured organisational setting and with prescribed roles</li>
<li>Directed towards the attainment of aims and objectives</li>
<li>Achieved through the efforts of other people</li>
<li>Using systems and procedures</li>
</ul>
<p>Why Study Management?</p>
<ul>
<li>To learn what leading writers say about management.</li>
<li>To understand the interrelationship between development of theory and management practice.</li>
<li>To understand the principles underlying the process of management.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="management-theories">Management Theories<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#management-theories" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Management theories describe how managers conduct activities, and keep institutions operating in an effective way in order to meet their objectives. The different approaches used by managers have gone through an evolutionary process. In practice, no single approach will be sufficient. Most managers use a combination of approaches to create effectiveness within the organisation. The four main classifications are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Classical Theories (Scientific Management)</li>
<li>Human Relations or Behavioural Approach Theories</li>
<li>Systems Theories</li>
<li>Contingency Theories</li>
</ul>
<p>The theories outlined above are covered in the pages that follow.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="classical-theories">Classical Theories<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#classical-theories" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Classical theories, also known as the scientific approach to management started in the later part of the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. The approach relied on systematic information collection, analysis and identification of causes and effects, followed by effective organisation of management structure. The objective was to develop basic principles that could guide the design, creation and maintenance of organisations. The emphasis was on efficiency and effectiveness. Some of the prominent figures that may be identified with the classical school of thought are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Frederic W. Taylor</li>
<li>Henri Fayol</li>
<li>Max Weber</li>
</ul>

<p>According to Maslow's theory, every human being has basic needs. People are motivated by the desire to satisfy these needs. Once a need is satisfied it ceases to be a motivator. As one need is satisfied, another appears and takes its place, and the individual is then motivated to satisfy the new need. The theory also assumes that a person can satisfy one need at a time. Maslow, a psychologist, developed the hierarchy of needs with the physiological needs at the bottom, which the individuals must satisfy before realising higher level needs. Maslow visualised needs as arranged in hierarchical order starting with basic needs, progressing to higher needs depending on the individual. There are more people at the base of the triangle still trying to satisfy the basic needs and fewer people were on the higher subsequent levels. Only a small percentage of people can reach the top of the hierarchy.</p>
<p>The various needs can be further defined as follows:</p>
<ul>
<li>Physiological needs are those needs that are required for survival. These include food, oxygen and sleep, sex and rest.</li>
<li>Safety needs refer to the need for job security, shelter and a stable environment.</li>
</ul>

Bishop's Computers</p>
<ul>
<li>Love and belonging indicate a need for affectionate relations with others as well as the need to belong to a group.</li>
<li>Self esteem refers to the need for self respect and recognition. At this stage people strive for power, achievement and status.</li>
<li>Self actualisation is a need for self fulfilment and is the culmination of all other needs.</li>

<p>McGregor's theory 'X' and 'Y' are a set of management assumptions about the behaviour of subordinates. He noted that most managers make these assumptions about their employees. The first sets of assumption are known as Theory 'X' they include:</p>
<ul>
<li>An average human being has an inherent dislike for work and will avoid it if possible.</li>
<li>As a result of the assumption above, most people must be coerced, controlled, directed and threatened with punishment in order to produce.</li>
<li>The average human being has to be closely directed, wishes to avoid responsibility and only wants security.</li>
</ul>
<p>The second set of assumptions known as Theory 'Y' regard people in a more favourable way. They state that:</p>
<ul>
<li>Employees like work, which is as natural as rest or play.</li>
<li>Human beings do not have to be controlled or coerced as long as commitment to the organisation is present.</li>
<li>Under proper conditions, they will not only accept but also seek responsibility.</li>
</ul>
<p>Theory 'X' and Theory 'Y' concern the attitudes management has towards the employee. The manager holding Theory X assumptions tends to be tough, authoritarian and supports tight controls with punishments. As a result, they tend to supervise workers very closely.</p>
<p>On the other hand, the manager using Theory Y believes in self control, is democratic, and consults staff. They encourage participation in decision making by subordinates.</p>
<p>Generally a blend between 'Theory X' and 'Theory Y' is more likely to provide effective management although this also depends on the prevailing environmental conditions.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="introduction">Introduction<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#introduction" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>As a practising nurse, you will spend most of your working life in health care institutions at all levels, from staff nurse to the highest positions of responsibility. Whatever your role, it is important to understand that all nurses lead and manage to some degree. In this section, you will look at leadership and the qualities of leadership in the twenty first century.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="leadership">Leadership<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#leadership" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The word leadership is an intriguing one and brings up images of all kinds of people. Some names that come to mind include former leaders like President Julius Nyerere, President Jomo Kenyatta and Prime Minister Margaret Thatcher. In nursing, Florence Nightingale may be considered an example of a leader in the profession.</p>
<p>You may also think of a relative or a nurse who has had notable leadership skills.</p>
<p>A leader is anyone who uses interpersonal skills to influence others to accomplish a specific goal. A leader can also be defined as a person who exerts influence by using certain personal behaviours and strategies or a person given authority by the statutes of an organisation to lead a group of people.</p>
<p>Leadership can be defined as 'the use of one's skills to influence others to perform to the best of their ability towards the achievement of goals' (Douglas, 1996). It is the art of getting others to want to do what one deems as important.</p>
<p>Leadership is not a fixed position. It is a process which changes on a situational basis. A manager leads by personally and actively working with subordinates for two main reasons:</p>
<ul>
<li>To guide and motivate their behaviour to fit the plans that have been established</li>
<li>To understand the feelings of employees and the problems they face as they translate plan into action</li>
</ul>
<p>Managers and leaders are different in the following ways:</p>
<ul>
<li>Managers think incrementally while leaders think radically</li>
<li>Managers do things right while leaders do the right things</li>
<li>Leaders stand out by being different</li>
<li>Leaders question assumptions and are suspicious of tradition</li>
</ul>
<p>'Manager' is derived from the Latin term 'managere', that is, 'someone in control'. A manager uses authority, the legitimate right to govern</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="types-of-leadership">Types of Leadership<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#types-of-leadership" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>There are two types of leadership. These are:</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="formal">Formal<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#formal" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>This is when an appointed leader is chosen by the administration and given official or legitimate authority. This form of leadership has the greatest impact when followers accept the leader.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="informal">Informal<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#informal" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>An informal leader does not have official authority to direct activities of others. They are usually chosen from within a specific group, for example, social group, church organisation or work group. An individual may become an informal leader as a result of a variety of factors including age, seniority, special competencies or personality.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="theories-of-leadership">Theories of Leadership<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#theories-of-leadership" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>Various writers have attempted to explain the theoretical basis for leaders and leadership. You will look at the two following theories in detail:</p>
<ul>
<li>Trait Theory of Leadership</li>
<li>Situational or Contingency Theory of Leadership</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="trait-theory-of-leadership">Trait Theory of Leadership<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#trait-theory-of-leadership" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>This was the first theory to emerge and was studied from approximately 1900 to 1950. This theory states that leaders possess a set of physical and emotional characteristics that are important for inspiring others towards a common goal.</p>
<p>Some theorists who subscribe to this theory believe that leaders are born with certain qualities that determine leadership ability and</p>
<p>success.</p>
<p>The leader is seen as gifted or develops certain characteristics including:</p>
<ul>
<li>Physique, that is, weight, height.</li>
<li>Intellect, that is, knowledge, judgement.</li>
<li>Personality, that is, aggressiveness, dominance and authoritarianism.</li>
</ul>
<p>According to this theory, the leader behaves according to the role expectations of the group.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="situational-or-contingency-theory-of-leadership">Situational or Contingency Theory of Leadership<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#situational-or-contingency-theory-of-leadership" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This theory states that the effectiveness of leadership depends on the relationship among the leaders and the task at hand, interpersonal skills and the favourability of the work situation.</p>
<p>However, there are critical factors that must be considered in the above relationships, these include:</p>
<ul>
<li>The degree of trust and respect between the leader and follower</li>
<li>The clarity of goals to be accomplished</li>
<li>The ability of the leader to reward followers and exert influence</li>
</ul>
<p>As a result of all the above, leaders were viewed as able to adapt their style according to the situation. For example, if one is faced with a specific situation, they consider the challenges and encourage an adaptive leadership style to understand the issue being faced. The implication here is that you, as a nurse manager, must assess each situation and determine appropriate action, based on the people involved.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="leadership-styles">Leadership Styles<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#leadership-styles" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The styles of leadership used by managers vary from organisation to organisation and are tailored to fit their needs and individual behaviour. There are too many kinds of leaders, personnel, tasks, organisations and environments for any one leadership style to apply.</p>
<p>There are three main styles of leadership, namely:</p>
<ul>
<li>Authoritarian or Autocratic</li>
<li>Democratic or Participative</li>
<li>Laissez faire or Permissive</li>
</ul>
<p>You shall now examine each of the leadership styles individually.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="authoritarian-or-autocratic">Authoritarian or Autocratic<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#authoritarian-or-autocratic" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>In this leadership style, the leader assumes full responsibility for all decisions and actions. The characteristics of an autocratic leader include:</p>
<p>This style of leadership may be counter productive. It may cause employees to lose interest and initiative and stop thinking for themselves because there is no need for independent thought. It may also lead to loss of motivation, especially where employees find it necessary to adopt an attitude of obedience to an autocratic leader. Due to lack of motivation, the employee may perform dismally. As a result, less than optimal goals are achieved because the resources utilised are the manager's only.</p>
<p>Some forms of authoritarian leadership are applicable to nursing. It is particularly suitable in crisis situations, when clear directions are of highest priority. It may also apply in situations where the people being led are inexperienced.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="democratic-or-participative-leadership">Democratic or Participative Leadership<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#democratic-or-participative-leadership" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Democratic leadership refers to a situation where people are free to express themselves. In contrast to the autocratic style of leadership, the manager here is 'people oriented' and focuses attention on human aspects as well as on building effective work groups.</p>
<p>A collaborative spirit or joint effort exists which allows for group participation in decision making. The leader assumes that employees are eager to perform their jobs and are capable of doing so. The group is seen as having responsibility for goal determination and achievement. Democratic leadership encourages enthusiasm, high morale and increased satisfaction.</p>
<p>Research has shown that this style of leadership leads to high productivity and is the most desirable form in a wide variety of work situations.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="laissez-faire-or-permissive-leadership">Laissez-Faire or Permissive Leadership<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#laissez-faire-or-permissive-leadership" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Laissez-faire leadership is at the opposite end of the continuum from authoritarian leadership. In this style, the leader lets people do what they want. The leader plays down their role in the group's activity and exercises minimum direction or control. The leader wants everyone to feel good about what they are doing and often avoids responsibility by relinquishing power to followers and permitting them to engage in managerial activities, for example, decision making, planning, setting goals, structuring and controlling the organisation.</p>

<p>This style is often evident when a manager is too weak or feels too threatened to exercise the function of leadership. It is also visible where the manager has a great need for approval and does not want to offend anybody. In this leadership style, the following can be observed:</p>
<ul>
<li>Little or no visible leadership</li>
<li>The manager's tend to be preoccupied with their own work</li>
<li>There is no communication and direction to the employee</li>
<li>There is very little teamwork and employees do as they wish</li>
</ul>
<p>It should be noted, however, that this style could be highly effective in motivated professional groups, for example, in research projects in which independent thinking is rewarded.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="qualities-of-effective-leadership">Qualities of Effective Leadership<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#qualities-of-effective-leadership" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>For leaders to be effective in influencing others to perform, they should possess the following qualities:</p>
<ul>
<li>Empathy, that is, an ability to look at things from another person's point of view.</li>
<li>Respect, that is, an effective leader should respect others as unique individuals.</li>
<li>Objectivity, that is, no bias or prejudice.</li>
<li>Self awareness, that is, self knowledge, being aware of the impact you have on others and being aware of your ability to make decisions or involve yourself with specific problems.</li>
</ul>
<p>A nurse manager uses all the leadership styles separately or together depending on their flexibility and the circumstances inherent in each situation. There are many similarities and differences in the behaviour, attitude and conditions present in the various leadership styles. Not all of the behaviours are evident at any one time.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="levels-of-leadership">Levels of Leadership<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#levels-of-leadership" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Your success as a leader may also depend on your level of leadership. John C. Maxwell lists the five levels of leadership as:</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="position">Position<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#position" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>This is the lowest level of leadership and is based solely on title and position.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="permission">Permission<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#permission" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>As relationships are developed with others, they give permission to the leader to lead beyond the limits of their job description.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="production">Production<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#production" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>As the group becomes more productive together, leadership is advanced.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="people-development">People Development<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#people-development" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This level of leadership involves developing people and assisting them to reach their potential.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="division-of-labour">Division of Labour<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#division-of-labour" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This is one of the basic principles applied in nursing practice to a large extent. It is a way of determining who is responsible for what. In an organisation, no single person can</p>

<p>do everything. In order to achieve organisational goals, people must be assigned tasks according to their skills. The objective of division of work is to produce more and better work with the same effort. The tasks that individuals perform must be related to each other and integrated. This calls for an adequate definition of duties to be duly communicated to all the people concerned.</p>
<p>The idea of division of labour in an organisation makes it possible for work to proceed quickly and smoothly. For example, in a hospital, there is a chief nurse, senior nursing officers and nursing officers. Each of these people have a specific job to perform in order to ensure the success of the organisation. Each person has the responsibility to perform their task in coordination with other people's duties.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="responsibility-accountability-and-authority">Responsibility, Accountability and Authority<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#responsibility-accountability-and-authority" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Once the employees have been given different tasks to perform, the leader is responsible for the work to be done. Similarly, employees are responsible for the completion of their specific tasks. <div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="responsibility">Responsibility<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#responsibility" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This is a duty or assignment related to a job. It also refers to each person's obligation to perform at an acceptable level. For example, one responsibility common to a nurse in charge is establishing the patient care assignment for a specific area of work. Nurse managers should always be assigned responsibilities with accompanying authority because, if the latter is not given, role confusion occurs for all individuals involved. For example, a nurse manager may have the responsibility for maintaining high professional standards, but if they are not given the authority to discipline employees as needed, this responsibility may be difficult or impossible to implement.</p>

<p>This means that someone must be able to explain actions and results or accept ownership for the results or lack thereof. For example, a</p>
<p>qualified registered nurse is accountable for nursing care given to a patient or client.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="authority">Authority<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#authority" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Authority has its base in responsibility. Dictionaries define authority as 'legal or rightful power, a right to command or act, domination, jurisdiction'. Authority defines which position will be superior and which will be subordinate.</p>
<p>The various levels of divisional activities in health organisations are a direct result of the size and complexity of modern institutions. For example, in a hospital, at the top level is the operating authority, that is, the chief nurse who takes the place of employer in the superior versus subordinate relationship. Policy and decisions are made at this level. They delegate responsibility to a group of nurse managers who exercise the delegated authority in supervising the work of others. This is formal authority. On the other hand, responsibility can be delegated for performance of specific functions. This functional authority is based on technical knowledge and personal skills. Both forms of authority are necessary for achieving goals and satisfying individual needs.</p>

<p>In many areas of your work, you are required to work with others in a smooth and efficient manner in order to achieve your objectives. Teamwork has been defined as 'coordinated action by a group of individuals in regular contact wherein members contribute toward task achievement' (Sikula and McKenna 1994). A health care team includes nurses, doctors, allied health professionals and support staff who have to work together in order to deliver quality services to the patients.</p>
<p>A team usually has a leader. Leadership is an important part of efficient teams. <div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="discipline">Discipline<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#discipline" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>The last principle of management to be covered is discipline. You should be familiar with the term because you may have been involved in disciplining someone, for example, your child or a sibling. On the other hand, you may have been</p>
<p>Bishop's Computers</p>
<p>Bishop's Computers</p>
<p>disciplined or punished after violating rules and regulations either at home or in the work place. The word 'discipline' originates from the latin term 'disciplina', which means teaching or learning. As it was put forward by Fayol, discipline is 'respect for agreements which are directed at achieving obedience, application, energy and the outward marks of respect'. The primary emphasis in discipline is in assisting employees to behave in a manner that allows them to be self directed in meeting organisational goals. Punishment may be applied for improper behaviour in constructive discipline. This should be carried out in a supportive, corrective manner.</p>
<p>Management usually disciplines an employee for:</p>
<ul>
<li>Not meeting laid down standards of performance.</li>
<li>Violating the rules and policies.</li>
<li>Insubordination, that is, lack of respect for authority.</li>
</ul>
<p>The highest level and most effective form of discipline is self discipline because the roles are internalised and become part of the individual's personality (Curtin, 1996). The manager plays a vital role in this. There are several factors that must be present to foster a climate of self discipline. You will cover the factors cited by Health Care Education Associates (1987). They include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Employee awareness and understanding of rules and regulations that govern behaviour. These must be clearly written and communicated to subordinates.</li>
<li>An atmosphere of mutual trust should exist. The manager must believe that employees are capable of actively seeking self discipline. Conversely, the employees must perceive the manager as honest and trustworthy.</li>
<li>Employees should identify with the goals of the organisation. When this happens, they are more likely to accept the standards of conduct deemed acceptable by the organisation.</li>
</ul>
<p>Discipline, therefore, is important for the smooth running of the organisation. It should be used as a means of helping the employees grow but not as a punitive measure.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="disciplinary-process">Disciplinary Process<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#disciplinary-process" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The following is a guideline on the steps in the disciplinary process that can be applied to an employee who has violated rules or regulations.</p>
<ol>
<li>Preliminary investigation and caution to the employee about the problem.</li>
<li>A cordial discussion with the offender and a brief warning as to why further violations will not be tolerated.</li>
<li>A stronger verbal warning after a further violation of regulation.</li>
<li>A formal written warning.</li>
<li>A written warning accompanied by suspension from the job for a prescribed number of days.</li>
<li>Suspension from the job for a longer period of time.</li>
<li>Discharge with opportunity to appeal.</li>
</ol>
<p>This process provides an opportunity to make amends for violations of the rules and regulations.</p>
<p>Administering discipline is one of the most difficult and resented tasks, but as a manager, this is your final responsibility. Your main concern should be to provide staff that can give safe care to patients and to help employees grow as individual human beings.</p>

<p>This is the first function of management. In module one, you learnt about the nursing process and the various stages involved. Planning is also a continuous process, it involves going through similar stages. You live in an era of change and therefore, you need to plan your activities.</p>
<p>Planning is a never ending process. It is the beginning of management. Planning is simply defined as deciding in advance what will and will not be done in the next minute, hour, day, month or year. Planning is advance thinking as a basis for action. It involves what needs to be done, how it will be done and mechanisms of evaluating work done. Planning, therefore, is having a specific objective or purpose and mapping out a method before hand. When planning, you should consider the seen and unseen factors, and keep in mind that all factors influence one another.</p>

<p>The next stage of the planning process is establishing goals to be achieved. These goals determine the direction of activities and serve as a guide for action. <div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="organisational-communication">Organisational Communication<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#organisational-communication" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>In units one and two of module one, you covered the various principles of communication. Please refer to your notes on this topic, as they will be useful for the following topic. In this section, you will specifically focus on organisational communication. There is no organisation that can exist without communication.</p>
<p>Communication is referred to as the lifeblood of any organisation. Communication refers to the need to create common understanding. It is, therefore, the process of transmitting ideas and information from one person, the sender, to another or group of people, receivers.</p>
<p>Organisational communication is the exchange of information within the organisation. While employee communication is the responsibility of the manager, the manager's work may be supplemented through some already established means of communication within an organisation. The manager is charged with the responsibility of passing information through various channels.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="formal-and-informal-communication">Formal and Informal Communication<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#formal-and-informal-communication" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The formal communication system is mainly used. It is based on a chain of command from the top of the organisation to the bottom. This is used for all official messages including directives, procedures, policies, job instructions and so on. An informal communication system can also be used. This is usually oral and not crucial to the functioning of the organisation. It originates from informal groupings, for example, associations or welfare groups. In most cases, messages are transmitted through the 'grapevine' (also known as rumours) and sometimes the communication is distorted or groundless. Such types of communication can cause fear and anxiety among staff and the manager should be aware of its existence and reinforce stability using more formal methods of communication.</p>
<p>You will now look at some of the different types of formal organisational communication.</p>

Bishop's Computers</p>
<p>Vertical Flow</p>
<p>The manager is responsible for passing information through vertical channels of communication, which includes both a downward and an upward flow of communication.</p>
<p>A two way exchange of information is essential in organisations and managers must ensure that there is feedback at all levels.</p>
<p>Downward Communication</p>
<p>Most information within organisations moves from the top downward, that is, from the top levels of management to employees. This may be through face to face communication or written materials, for example, memos or circulars.</p>
<p>The purpose of this form of communication is mainly to inform employees of their responsibilities, commitment to the objectives of the organisation and any other relevant information. Barriers to this form of communication may include:</p>
<ul>
<li>The manager may withhold information</li>
<li>The employee may fail to understand the message</li>
<li>The employee may get information but this may not be relevant to their needs</li>
</ul>
<p>Upward Communication</p>
<p>This is the flow of information from the bottom up or from the subordinates to the supervisor. Ideally information should pass freely up the chain of command so that management gets the feedback needed to evaluate results and initiate improvements. Barriers to upward communication may include:</p>
<ul>
<li>If employees feel management is not interested in their ideas, they will not offer them.</li>
<li>At times, bad news gets blocked and is not communicated to the top management.</li>
<li>Delays in relaying messages.</li>
</ul>
<p>Delegating</p>
<p>Once you are responsible and accountable for patient care activities, you will find that there are times when a job gets too big and part of it must be entrusted to someone else.</p>
<p>Delegation is the process through which responsibility and authority for performing a task, function, activity or decision is transferred to another individual who accepts that authority and responsibility (Sullivan and Decker 1992: 216).</p>
<p>Although the delegator remains accountable for the task, the delegate is also accountable to the delegator for responsibilities assumed. Delegation is a dynamic process, which involves three factors:</p>
<ul>
<li>Responsibility for work delegated, that is, willingness to do the assigned work or an obligation to accomplish a task.</li>
<li>Accountability or the obligation to carry out the responsibility or authority or act of accepting ownership for the results or lack thereof.</li>
<li>Authority, that is, the right to act or empower.</li>
</ul>
<p>You can delegate only those tasks for which you are responsible.</p>
<p>In delegation, responsibility and authority are transferred, while accountability is shared.</p>
<p>To clearly understand who is responsible for certain tasks, it is important to look at:</p>
<ul>
<li>Practice acts, that is, the scope of nursing practice.</li>
<li>Standards of care.</li>
<li>Job descriptions for various positions.</li>
<li>Policy statements regarding the quality of care.</li>
</ul>
<p>For delegation to be effective, the following must be considered:</p>
<ul>
<li>The manager and immediate subordinate must have a good interpersonal relationship.</li>
<li>The manager must have good administrative skills to be able to delegate.</li>
<li>The assigned duties should be well understood, well defined and well written to both of them.</li>
<li>The person to whom work is to be delegated must be assisted to become self sufficient.</li>
</ul>
<p>Every activity you perform has some benefits to the people involved as well as the organisation. The delegator benefits as they are able to devote more time to those tasks that cannot be delegated and with more time available can develop new skills and abilities.</p>
<p>Meanwhile, the delegate benefits because they gain new skills and abilities that can facilitate upward mobility and build self esteem and</p>
<p>Bishop's Computers
Bishop's Computers</p>
<p>confidence resulting in job motivation and satisfaction. They also have the freedom to exercise initiative and learn how to make decisions independently.</p>
<p>The organisation benefits in that teamwork improves and the organisation achieves its goals more efficiently. Finally, the patient benefits as the quality of care improves leading to increased patient satisfaction.</p>

<p>The process of delegation involves the following steps:</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="defining-the-task">Defining the Task<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#defining-the-task" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="determining-who-should-perform-the-task">Determining Who Should Perform the Task<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#determining-who-should-perform-the-task" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="providing-clear-communication-about-expectations-regarding-the-task">Providing Clear Communication About Expectations Regarding the Task<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#providing-clear-communication-about-expectations-regarding-the-task" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="monitoring-performance-and-providing-feedback">Monitoring Performance and Providing Feedback<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#monitoring-performance-and-providing-feedback" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Analyse performance according to established goals. Monitoring provides a mechanism for feedback and control and ensures that delegated tasks are carried out as agreed.</p>
<p>Delegation is good because it enables the manager to concentrate on the most important tasks. However, there are some problems which may affect both the delegator and delegate.</p>
<p>On the part of the manager they may:</p>
<ul>
<li>Be reluctant to delegate adequately to their subordinates. In some cases, the manager might delegate responsibility and not authority.</li>
<li>Fail to delegate because they can do a better job.</li>
<li>Lack the ability to communicate to people what is to be done.</li>
</ul>
<p>As a general guideline, only routine activities may be delegated. Sensitive issues, for example, disciplinary and staff performance appraisal should not be delegated.</p>

<li>Lack the necessary information and resources which creates an attitude that might make a person reject further assignments (non supportive environment).</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="controlling">Controlling<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#controlling" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>Controlling is the managerial function concerned with making sure plans succeed. It means measuring and correcting the performance of employees to ensure that the planned objectives of an organisation are achieved. Controlling involves the regulation of activities so that some targeted element of performance remains within acceptable limits. It also guides activities and assures certain minimum standards.</p>
<p>The nurse manager needs to take into account both external and internal environments</p>

Bishop's Computers</p>
<p>because they will influence the way they exercise control.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="external-environment">External Environment<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#external-environment" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This includes the laws and acts that regulate institutions, for example, the Nursing Practice Act, which regulates nursing practice in terms of registration and licensure, in order to maintain standards of nursing services.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="internal-environment">Internal Environment<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#internal-environment" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This includes self regulation within the organisation or nursing department, for example, quality control relating to care of patients, staffing and budget reports.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="importance-of-control">Importance of Control<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#importance-of-control" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Control is important for several reasons including the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>To ensure work is done according to the objectives set and activities are carried out as planned, within the allocated time and with the resources provided.</li>
<li>To enable supervisors to recognise gaps in the knowledge and understanding of the staff, and arrange for appropriate training.</li>
<li>To enable management to ensure that the resources provided for work are adequate and are being properly used.</li>
<li>To enable management to identify the cause of work deficiencies.</li>
<li>To facilitate the recognition and reward for good work done and recognise suitable staff.</li>
<li>To identify mistakes before they become critical, bearing in mind that prevention is better than cure.</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="establishing-the-standards">Establishing the Standards<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#establishing-the-standards" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The first step involves formulating standards. Standards are yardsticks against which nurse managers devise controls. They are a way to measure and evaluate quality and quantity of performance. They are closely related to organisational goals but are more specific. People must understand the results desired to avoid confusion. Standards may be classified as:</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="tangible-and-objective">Tangible and Objective<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#tangible-and-objective" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Tangible standards are physical standards, which pertain to actual operation of the department. They can be quantitative or qualitative, for example, the number of nurses to cover the duty per shift and quality of care expected, for instance, every patient will have a nursing care plan twelve hours after admission.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="intangible-and-subjective">Intangible and Subjective<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#intangible-and-subjective" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Intangible standards may include the reputation of the hospital based on good or bad publicity. They can also refer to the morale of employees, attitudes and relationships between employees and complaints. These are difficult to measure but can be assessed with a degree of confidence.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="actual-performance">Actual Performance<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#actual-performance" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>After setting standards, they must be measured to identify their achievement. This can be done by direct observation and checking on employees. Observations for control should attempt to identify:</p>
<ul>
<li>Inadequate output or improperly performed jobs.</li>
<li>Deviations from expected standards. Assuming a questioning rather than confrontational attitude.</li>
<li>Comparisons between actual performances with the laid down standards.</li>
</ul>
<p>It is important to correctly interpret reasons for deviations from standards.</p>
<p>Some deviations may be due to temporary circumstances, for example, patients may not have had bed baths due to fewer nurses on duty rather than due to poor performance.</p>

<p>If performance falls short of standards, then analysis indicates that corrective action is required. Supervisors must decide what remedial action is necessary to get improved results in the near future. These corrections may require:</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="human-resource-management">Human Resource Management<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#human-resource-management" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>The shift in terminology from 'personnel' to 'human resources' is symbolic of a true change in the field. The term human resource reflects the position that people are a resource that needs to be managed strategically in support of the organisation's mission.</p>
<p>The effective functioning of an organisation depends upon various resources. Human resources or manpower is one of the most vital resources for the labour intensive health institution.</p>
<p>Human resource management can be said to be an integral part of the role of any person who is responsible for the work of others. In this module, human resource management will refer to a group of specialists concerned with increasing the effectiveness of staff performance in an organisation.</p>
<p>According to Basawanthappa (2002), the main objectives of human resource management are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Effective utilisation of human resources to enable the achievement of organisational goals.</li>
<li>Establishment and maintenance of an adequate organisational structure and desirable working relationship among staff.</li>
<li>Securing integration of the individual and informal groups with the organisation and thereby ensuring their commitment, involvement and loyalty.</li>
<li>Recognition and satisfaction of individual needs and group goals.</li>
<li>Provision of maximum opportunities for individual development and advancement.</li>
<li>Maintenance of high morals in organisations.</li>
</ul>
<p>Bearing in mind the objectives covered previously, you will now look at the main aspects of human resource management. These include:</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="recruitment-of-staff">Recruitment of Staff<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#recruitment-of-staff" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This involves preparing job descriptions and specifications, drafting job advertisements, interviewing candidates and assessing appropriate salary levels for new employees. A job description states the principal duties, responsibilities and the scope of authority. <div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="staff-training-and-development">Staff Training and Development<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#staff-training-and-development" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

The human resources department has the responsibility of assessing the training needs, designing training methodology and evaluating training activities to determine their effectiveness.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="health-and-safety">Health and Safety<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#health-and-safety" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<p>Welfare services are provided for matters concerning employees which are not immediately connected with their jobs although they may be connected generally with their place of work. These include counselling, problems of health or sickness, social and sporting activities, restaurants, child care facilities and special services for retired employees.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="consultation-and-negotiation">Consultation and Negotiation<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#consultation-and-negotiation" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Opportunities should exist for collective bargaining and liaising with trade unions. Trade unions should be utilised by management as an avenue for consultation and joint planning with employees. Trade unions should not be viewed as antagonists but a partner in planning and decision making. If employees through their trade unions felt appreciated, they would be motivated to perform better and improve the image of the organisation.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="labour-management-relations">Labour Management Relations<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#labour-management-relations" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Labour management relations has been defined as 'the effort to develop harmonious working relations between employees and management in order for a firm to increase productivity, improve quality of work life and remain competitive'.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="objectives-of-labour-management-relations">Objectives of Labour Management Relations<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#objectives-of-labour-management-relations" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>To enhance a healthy relationship between employers and employees, that is, a sound, harmonious and mutually beneficial relationship.</li>
<li>To safeguard the interests of employees and management by securing mutual understanding and goodwill between them.</li>
<li>To avoid industrial and workplace conflict that could affect production and productivity.</li>
<li>To preventing or reduce labour turnover and frequent absenteeism.</li>
<li>To nurture and sustain workplace democracy in order to enhance employee participation in decision making and problem solving.</li>
<li>To eliminate strikes, lockouts and go slows.</li>
<li>To allow the government some control over private means of production,</li>
</ul>

Bishop's Computers</p>
<p>distribution and service provision in the public interest.</p>
<p>The management should never be caught up in a surprise strike if they have been in good relations and communication with the employees. Those in Management positions should not be members of trade unions.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="financial-resource-management">Financial Resource Management<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#financial-resource-management" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>In the previous section, you covered the management of human resources. In this part, you are going to examine financial resource management. Managing finances is one of the most important roles of a nurse manager. Arguments have been put forward that this function should be left to accountants who are specialised in this area. However, if nurses do not have the basic skills to enable them manage finances, they will not succeed in providing nursing services. Most of the resources you will be managing are acquired with money.</p>
<p>Financial planning requires that the manager identifies objectives, policies and procedures. The budget is one of the main tools of financial management.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="budgeting">Budgeting<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#budgeting" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>In simple terms, budgeting involves managing money and how it is used to maintain other resources.</p>
<p>A budget is a means of control, which reflects the plan against which actual performance is measured. It can also be defined as a means of checking the progress made in keeping the expenses and costs in compliance with an organisation's financial plans. Budgeting is the process of planning and controlling future operations by comparing actual results with planned budgetary expectations.</p>
<p>The following are some of the purposes of a budget:</p>
<ul>
<li>To avoid spending more than an organisation can afford</li>
<li>To aid in planning and controlling</li>
<li>To assist in assessing the financial requirements of the institution</li>
<li>To indicate the areas in which money raised or received will be spent</li>
<li>To facilitate comparison of actual performance with budgeted targets</li>
</ul>
<p>In order to draw up a meaningful budget, the hospital should have a clearly defined organisational structure with responsibilities defined and assigned. Personnel at all levels of management should participate in budget development. The personnel involved should have an understanding of the ideas and financial goals of the hospital.</p>
<p>There must be an adequate system, which provides reliable financial and statistical information to the responsible people. The budget should allow enough freedom to accomplish departmental objectives and must be flexible enough to allow for unpredictable expenditures.</p>
<p>There are two main types of budgets, which the nurse may be involved in preparing:</p>
<ul>
<li>Operational or Annual Budget</li>
<li>Capital Budget</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="operational-or-annual-budget">Operational or Annual Budget<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#operational-or-annual-budget" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This is the organisation's statement of expected revenue or income and expenses for the coming year. It coincides with the fiscal year. The fiscal year is a specified twelve month period during which the operational and financial performance of the organisation is measured. It usually coincides with the calendar year, that is, it runs from January to December or may follow another time frame, for example, the government calendar, which runs from July to June.</p>
<p>The operational budget includes the accumulated estimates of operating revenues and expenses. In nursing management, the revenue is derived from patient fees. Actual payment is generated by a given service or procedure.</p>
<p>The expenses consist of salary and non salary items, for example, personnel emoluments and education, including in service training, on the job training, educational leave and travel scholarships. They may also include personnel uniforms, books, periodical subscriptions, laundry, medical equipment and maintenance, drugs and pharmaceuticals, non pharmaceutical supplies, legal and professional fees and stationery.</p>
<p>The expenses should be comprehensive and thorough. Budgets are prepared by the heads of each ward or department in consultation with staff. Thereafter, the matron, chief nurse or director of nursing services compiles the nursing division budget. In some cases, the director of</p>
<p>Bishop's Computers
Bishop's Computers</p>
<p>nursing services prepares a budget for the whole of the nursing division.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="capital-budget">Capital Budget<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#capital-budget" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This is the second type of budget. It outlines the need for major equipment or physical changes in the organisation requiring large sums of money, for example, physical renovations, new construction and new or replacement equipment. Organisations define capital items based on certain criteria, for instance, the item must have an expected performance of a minimum of one year or more and exceed a certain shilling value, for example, fifty thousand. The criteria vary from one hospital to another.</p>
<p>Requests for capital items are usually made on a special form or letter, accompanied by a written justification for the item. If there are a large number of items requested, a priority list should be set.</p>
<p>Where possible, the period within which items are needed should be specified. Capital costs should consider long term goals and must complement the organisational objectives.</p>
<p>All nurse managers are involved in the budgeting process.</p>
<p>Preparation begins several months before the end of the fiscal year to allow time for careful preparation. In a decentralised system, budget preparation has several steps:</p>
<ul>
<li>Review of policies, standards and objectives.</li>
<li>Top level management projections for the future and preparation of guidelines.</li>
<li>Middle level nurse managers prepare the annual budget.</li>
<li>The administrator of nursing services, finance director or manager reviews the budget.</li>
<li>Budget is accepted or modified.</li>
<li>Budget is implemented and regularly evaluated.</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="managing-materials">Managing Materials<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#managing-materials" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>So far you have managed human resources and money. The other resource that a manager is concerned with in the organisation is</p>
<p>Bishop's Computers
Bishop's Computers</p>
<p>the materials. Materials refer to drugs, supplies and equipment needed by the nurses and other health personnel to deliver services. For quality and efficient services, the materials must be provided at the right place, at the right time and in the right quantity.</p>
<p>Materials are essential if the health care institution is to achieve its objectives. The nurse manager should know the policies and procedures that relate to materials management. You should be familiar with the following activities:</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="demand-estimation">Demand Estimation<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#demand-estimation" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Since a large quantity of materials is used in hospitals, you need to identify your requirements or needs beforehand.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="procurement">Procurement<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#procurement" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Having developed your list of requirements, the next step is procurement or ordering. Some institutions have laid out specific rules and regulations regarding procurement. This is aimed at reducing wastage and maximising the value of money.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="receipt-and-inspection">Receipt and Inspection<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#receipt-and-inspection" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The materials received should be subjected to either physical or chemical inspection. This ensures that you received the right quality and quantity of materials as requested by the organisation.</p>

<p>Adequate quantities of materials should be available whenever required. Close supervision of movement of materials or consumption rate is a good tool for proper control.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="storage">Storage<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#storage" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The materials should be placed in a store within or near the institution. The store should be of adequate size to accommodate all the different materials required, for example, fridges, shelves and cupboards. The materials should also be stored in appropriate conditions, for example, temperature, light as specified by the manufacturer.</p>
<p>It is, therefore, important that the nurse manager adopts the proper procedures for materials management.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="managing-time">Managing Time<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#managing-time" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Time is an important resource although in many cases, it is not thought of as such. It is sometimes referred to as one of the M's of management and stands for moments. The other M's being money, manpower and materials. For example, you may recall times when you have wished that a day had more than twenty four hours or that a week had more than seven days so that you can accomplish the tasks you had set out to do. This often happens when you are not managing time well.</p>
<p>The demands on time as a manager and a health care provider fall into three categories (Nduba 1999):</p>
<ul>
<li>Tasks that have to be performed. These relate to key responsibilities, for example, clinical or administrative duties.</li>
<li>Tasks that other people pressurise you into doing. Some of these are important and must be done while others you do because you do not want to say no.</li>
<li>Tasks that you perform because you want to. These are usually out of choice and include tasks that someone else could do just as well, for example, attending a nurses' association meeting.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="time-waster">Time Waster<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#time-waster" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This is something that prevents a person from accomplishing a job or achieving a goal. Some of the common time wasters include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Interruptions such as telephone calls and drop in visitors.</li>
<li>Lack of clear cut goals, objectives and priorities.</li>
<li>Meetings both scheduled and unscheduled.</li>
<li>Lack of daily or weekly plans.</li>
<li>Lack of self discipline.</li>
<li>Failure to delegate.</li>
<li>Ineffective communication.</li>

<p>A Health information system is an integrated system used in health care settings to manage patient information. It is primarily a centralised patient record of demographics, health and financial information (Yoderwise 1999). The information is entered, stored, retrieved, displayed and communicated. A major function is to communicate and integrate patient care data and information, and provide management support.</p>
<p>Health data is entered into the system from computer stations located in the various departments in the organisation and stored in a central computer to be accessed by all patient care services. The systems contain a central database that is written to and accessed by all departments, for example, nursing, laboratory, pharmacy, radiology, physiotherapy and so on. The programs that link the individual departments must be able to 'talk' to one another in order to exchange patient data. For example, the wards need access to laboratory results or the pharmacy may need access to the ward for information like patient height, weight or allergies as entered by the nurse in the patient's clinical chart.</p>
<p>The nurse managers and staff must understand the patient care process from a data information knowledge perspective. They must be aware of the data collected, reasons why it is collected and decisions made based on the data.</p>
<p>The type of information that can be tracked in this system includes:</p>
<ul>
<li>Appointments, admissions, discharges and transfers.</li>
<li>Medication profiles, for instance, prescriptions and allergy reports.</li>
<li>Care planning.</li>
<li>Patient acuities.</li>
<li>Human resources, including staff pay roll, performance, incident reports and staff scheduling.</li>
<li>Plans, for example, budgeting information.</li>
</ul>
<p>An information system must provide the following (Yoder-Wise, 1999):</p>
<ul>
<li>Information confidentiality and security</li>
<li>Uniform data definitions</li>
<li>Standard format for transmitting data</li>

<li>Linking of clinical and financial information</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="nursing-care-delivery-systems">Nursing Care Delivery Systems<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#nursing-care-delivery-systems" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The practice of nursing has emerged as an aggregate of complicated duties and responsibilities. There is, therefore, a need to develop certain systems, methods or modalities to ensure delivery of quality care. There are four methods of assignment of patient care in a hospital. These are:</p>

<li>Functional Nursing</li>
<li>Team Nursing</li>
<li>Primary Nursing</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="case-assignment-method">Case Assignment Method<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#case-assignment-method" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This was the first method to be identified in nursing care delivery. It was popular in the 1920's. Each patient is assigned to a nurse for total patient care while the nurse is on duty. If they go off duty, the work is handed over promptly to another nurse. This means that the patient has a different nurse</p>

<p>each shift. Assignment is made according to the severity of illness and tends to be mostly for acutely ill patients, for example, in the intensive care unit (ICU).</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="functional-nursing">Functional Nursing<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#functional-nursing" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The second method is known as the functional nursing method. It was the first major deviation from case method. It began in the early 1950's when there were few registered nurses and more practical nurses available. Nurse aides provided most of the patient care.</p>
<p>This method emphasises the division of labour according to specific tasks. It is 'task or thing oriented' and is determined by the technical aspects of the job to be done. Each nurse has a clearly defined set of tasks determined by complexity, including skills, knowledge and experience in certain nursing techniques, for example, drug administration, wound dressing, bed baths and so on.</p>
<p>In this approach, the nurse in charge must have experience and exceptional knowledge in nursing. The nurse must know the skill level of their workers in order to make accurate assignments. The nurse in charge establishes rigid routines, structures and time schedules.</p>
<p>This method has several advantages:</p>
<ul>
<li>It emphasises the efficient delivery of care</li>
<li>There is little likelihood of confusion over who will do what</li>
<li>Minimal time is spent coordinating activities</li>
<li>It is economical in that it allows for the use of less skilled personnel</li>
<li>Each member can become highly skilled if they do the same tasks repetitively</li>
</ul>
<p>It proves particularly useful when workers have limited knowledge and experience, facilities and equipment and there is a shortage of staff.</p>
<p>However, there are some disadvantages. These include:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>The nurse tends to lose close contact with patients.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Fragmentation of care rather than total care. This means that the patient's needs may be overlooked because of failure to fit in compartments or task categories, for example, there may be a nurse skilled in administering medication but none skilled in dealing with anxiety.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Continuity of care is difficult if not impossible since no single staff member has a complete picture of the client's needs and responses to nursing or medical interventions.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>There is little avenue for development.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="team-nursing">Team Nursing<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#team-nursing" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Team nursing was introduced in the late 1950's to improve nursing services by utilising the knowledge and skills of professional nurses and to supervise the increasing number of auxiliary staff. The philosophy supports achieving goals through group action or team spirit.</p>
<p>Team nursing can be organised in the following manner:</p>
<ul>
<li>A group or team of nurses with different levels of skills are assigned to a group of patients. The size and composition of the team is dependent upon the setting.</li>
<li>The team works together to accomplish a goal.</li>
<li>They focus on patient centred as opposed to task oriented assignments. These are based on patient needs and the knowledge, skills and experiences of team members.</li>
<li>A member of the work group is assigned as a leader. In the original concept, the team was led by a registered nurse.</li>
<li>The leadership role may be permanent or rotated.</li>
<li>The team works together with each member performing the tasks for which they are best prepared.</li>
<li>Team members report to the team leader who reports to the head nurse.</li>
<li>Success is dependent upon effective communication.</li>
</ul>
<p>This approach has several strengths. These include:</p>
<ul>
<li>It allows individual members to make personal and useful suggestions.</li>
<li>It combines the best thinking of all team members about patients' problems and improves the quality of decision making.</li>
<li>It cultivates team spirit, which affects the climate and continuity of care.</li>
<li>There is fragmentation of care and better utilisation of personnel in the performance of quality care.</li>
<li>There is also the potential for leadership development and it encourages greater staff satisfaction due to increased</li>
</ul>
<p>Bishop's Computers
Bishop's Computers</p>
<p>guidance and better matching of assignments to skills.</p>
<p>As an individualised care approach, it is often more satisfying to patients. The patients are cared for by a limited number of nurses who know them better. This provides an opportunity for more therapeutic and enhanced nurse patient relationships.</p>
<p>However, there are also weaknesses associated with this approach. These include:</p>
<ul>
<li>It may be difficult to find the time needed for team planning and conferences. This would mean that at times, care plans are not comprehensive enough.</li>
<li>The time spent in coordinating delegated work and supervision can prove expensive.</li>
<li>Logistics may hamper team nursing, for example, four or five nurses converging at a medicine cupboard designed to support one nurse</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="primary-nursing">Primary Nursing<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#primary-nursing" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This is a newer approach to the delivery of nursing services.</p>
<p>The concept was developed in the early 1970s and is used by many institutions in the developed world. People are becoming more aware of it in this country and some institutions have begun to incorporate it.</p>
<p>The following is the basis for assignment:</p>
<ul>
<li>A professional nurse, usually a registered nurse, is assigned to a patient for their total hospital stay or to a small group of patients, not more than four or five.</li>
<li>This nurse assumes responsibility for twenty four hours a day for the duration of the patient's stay in hospital.</li>
<li>The primary nurse assesses plans and executes the plan or may delegate to a secondary or associate nurse to execute the plan during her absence.</li>
<li>Every nurse serves as the primary nurse for a few patients and as an associate nurse for other patients on other shifts.</li>
<li>The primary nurse communicates with the physician and coordinates care with other health workers.</li>
<li>The chief nurse functions as a coordinator of the unit and is a resource person for the primary nurses.</li>
</ul>
<p>This system has the following advantages:</p>
<ul>
<li>It encourages a one to one relationship and in so doing promotes total patient care by virtue of the quality of interaction.</li>
<li>The nurse coordinates all aspects of care, including the physical, social and psychological, which ensures the continuity of care.</li>
<li>This method promotes increased autonomy and responsibility leading to job satisfaction due to involvement.</li>
<li>Clients are satisfied as a result of their increased interaction with one nurse who is knowledgeable.</li>
</ul>
<p>However, there are also several disadvantages associated with this method. These include:</p>
<ul>
<li>It limits professional mobility. An associate nurse may find it difficult to follow plans made by another nurse.</li>
<li>It requires high levels of expertise and commitment from all nurses, that is, patient care may suffer if the nurse is not competent enough to perform all the tasks assigned.</li>
<li>Primary nursing requires competent practitioners who can function independently when utilising the nursing process. However, not all nurses are comfortable in accepting this responsibility.</li>
<li>It may be less economical than the other methods since it may require a larger percentage of registered nurses.</li>
</ul>

<p>The legal responsibility refers to the obligation upon nurses to obey the law in professional activities. The professional nurse should understand their legal responsibility when taking care of patients and be aware of their limitations. Patients have a right to expect professionals to live up to certain safety standards and to demonstrate responsibility and accountability.</p>
<p>The scope of nursing practice is defined and guided by the nursing practice act and the common law. The nursing practice act provides legal regulation for licensure, while common law is derived from legal doctrine and consists of both broad and comprehensive principles based on justice, reason and common sense. The two are responsible for educational and examination requirements, providing licensing for those who meet professional requirements and defining the functions of each category, for example, registered nurse or enrolled nurse.</p>
<p>Nursing practice should not leave room for negligence or malpractice because this can lead to litigation. Litigation is being taken to a court of law by a patient who makes a claim for damages or compensation.</p>
