<p>As you learnt in the introduction to reproductive health unit, the Safe Motherhood Initiative (SMI) is designed to reduce the high numbers of deaths (maternal mortality) and illnesses (maternal morbidity) resulting from complications of pregnancy and childbirth. It is, therefore, important to study and understand the whole process of reproduction, that is, the anatomy and physiology of the human reproductive system.</p>
<p>A lot of the information you will find in this unit is not new to you as you studied it during your previous nursing training. However, it is important to go through everything once again as you will build on this knowledge in the subsequent sections of this unit.</p>

<p>The non-pregnant uterus is a hollow pear-shaped muscular organ situated in the pelvic cavity. It is 7.5 centimetres long, 5 centimetres wide,</p>
<p>2.5 centimetres deep and 1.25 centimetres thick. It lies behind the bladder and in front of the rectum. It leans forward over the bladder, which is known as anteversion and bends forward the cervix at the level of the internal os, which is known as anteflexion. When a woman stands, the position becomes almost horizontal with the fundus, resting on the bladder.</p>
<p>The uterus performs the following functions among others:</p>
<ul>
<li>Receives the fertilised ovum</li>
<li>Provides shelter for the foetus during pregnancy</li>
<li>Expels the uterine contents during childbirth</li>
</ul>
<p>Anterior to the uterus lie the utero-vesical pouch and the bladder, while posteriorly lie the recto-uterine pouch of Douglas and the rectum. Laterally, on either side, are the broad ligaments, the uterine tubes and the ovaries.</p>

<li>Ligaments</li>
<li>The muscles of the pelvic floor which is mainly the levator ani</li>
<li>The pubovesico-cervical support consisting mainly of fascia</li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li>The transverse cervical ligaments</li>
<li>The uterosacral ligaments</li>
<li>The pubocervical ligaments</li>
<li>The broad ligaments</li>

<p>The transverse cervical (also called cardinal ligaments) are the most important uterine support. <div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="the-uterine-tubes-fallopian-tubes">The Uterine Tubes (Fallopian Tubes)<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-uterine-tubes-fallopian-tubes" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>The fallopian tubes are passages that link the ovaries to the uterus. Each tube is between 10 - 12 centimetres long and is divided into four main parts, namely:</p>
<ul>
<li>The interstitial portion, which is the medial part that opens into the uterus. Its whole length lies within the walls of the uterus. It is the shortest and narrowest part of the tube with a length of 1.25 centimetres and its lumen is 1 millimetre wide.</li>
<li>The isthmus, which is the narrow portion immediately adjoining the uterus.</li>
<li>The ampulla, which is the widest and longest (5 centimetres long) part of the fallopian tube and it is here that fertilisation occurs.</li>
<li>The infundibulum, which is the fimbriated end of the fallopian tube. It is in close proximity to the ovary and its finger-like projections are known as fimbriae. One of the fimbriae, being longer than the others, is in contact with the ovary and picks up the ovum from the surface of the ovary at ovulation.</li>
</ul>
<p>The uterine tube has three coats or layers. These are the serous, the muscular, and the mucous layers. The inner epithelium is lined with a mucous membrane of ciliated cubical epithelium, which is arranged in folds. These folds slow the ovum down on its way to the uterus. In the lining are cells that produce a secretion containing glycogen to nourish the ovum. Beneath the lining is a layer of vascular connective tissue.</p>
<p>The middle layer of thin muscle coat consists of two layers, a longitudinal outer layer and a circular inner layer, both of smooth muscle. The tube is covered with peritoneum.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="the-ovaries">The Ovaries<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-ovaries" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>These are two glandular structures lying on the posterior surface of the broad ligament, supported by the ovarian and the infundibulopelvic ligaments. They are oval shaped and are composed of a medulla and a cortex, which is covered with germinal epithelium.</p>

<p>From puberty some of the Graafian follicles enlarge under the influence of the follicle-stimulating hormone. One Graafian follicle matures each month and ruptures to release an ovum. The ovaries produce ova and the hormones oestrogen and progesterone.</p>

<ul>
<li>The Follicle-Stimulating Hormone (FSH)</li>
<li>The Luteinizing Hormone (LH)</li>
<li>Oestrogen</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="what-are-the-functions-of-fsh-and-lh">What are the functions of FSH and LH?<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#what-are-the-functions-of-fsh-and-lh" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="follicle-stimulating-hormone-fsh">Follicle-Stimulating Hormone (FSH)<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#follicle-stimulating-hormone-fsh" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The FSH causes the Graafian follicle in the ovarian cortex to develop, mature and rupture to release the ovum into the fimbriated end of the fallopian tube. This is known as ovulation.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="the-menstrual-cycle">The Menstrual Cycle<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-menstrual-cycle" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The average menstrual cycle is taken to be 28 days, although it may vary in length from 22 to 35 days. The hormones you have just looked at control the menstrual cycle. A normal menstrual cycle is divided into three phases, which will now be covered individually.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="phase-1-the-menstrual-phase">Phase 1: The Menstrual Phase<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#phase-1-the-menstrual-phase" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This is the phase during which vaginal bleeding occurs. It lasts three to five days. The endometrium is shed down to the basal layer and discharged together with blood from the capillaries and the unfertilised ovum.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="phase-2-the-proliferative-follicular-phase">Phase 2: The Proliferative/Follicular Phase<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#phase-2-the-proliferative-follicular-phase" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This phase follows the menstrual phase and lasts until ovulation, approximately 14 days from the first day of menstruation in a 28-day cycle. It is the phase of regrowth and thickening under the influence of oestrogen.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="phase-3-the-secretory-luteal-phase">Phase 3: The Secretory/Luteal Phase<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#phase-3-the-secretory-luteal-phase" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This phase follows immediately after ovulation under the influence of progesterone and oestrogen from the corpus luteum. The endometrium thickens and becomes spongy, and there is an increase in secretions from the endometrial glands. If the fertilisation does not occur, the ovum dies and degenerates 36 to 48 hours after its release. The corpus luteum also degenerates about 10 days later. Menstruation then takes place 14 days after ovulation. If, on the other hand, fertilisation occurs, the corpus luteum continues to grow and produces hormones that support the pregnancy. The fertilised ovum gets implanted on the endometrium.</p>

<p>The posterior outlet is bounded anteriorly by the pubic arch, laterally by the ischial tuberosities, posteriorly by the tip of the sacrum or coccyx and the ligament joining the ischial tuberosities to the sacrum, which are known as sacrotuberous ligaments.</p>

<ul>
<li>The anatomical outlet, which is formed by the lower borders of each of the bones and the sacrotuberous ligaments.</li>
<li>The obstetrical outlet, which is of greater significance because it includes the narrow pelvic strait through which the foetus must pass. It is the space between the narrow pelvic strait and the anatomical outlet. The outlet is diamond-shaped and has three diameters.</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="the-pelvic-floor">The Pelvic Floor<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-pelvic-floor" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>The pelvic floor is formed by two muscle layers and layers of pelvic fascia. The most important of these is the strong diaphragm of muscles slung like a hammock from the walls of the pelvis. This pelvic diaphragm is made up of muscles and fascia, which unite in the midline. The muscles are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Levator ani, which are flat muscles that form a sling which supports the pelvic organs</li>
<li>Coccygeus, which are triangular sheets of muscle and tendinous fibres situated behind the levator ani</li>

<p>The pelvic floor supports the weight of the abdominal and pelvic organs. Its muscles are responsible for the voluntary control of micturition and defecation. They also play an important part in sexual intercourse. During childbirth, the pelvic floor influences the passive movement of the foetus through the birth canal and relaxes to allow its exit from the pelvis. In prolonged labour, it is strained and overstretched. This may lead to genital prolapse later.</p>
<p>Every pregnant woman needs to have an assessment of the pelvic capacity to determine pelvic adequacy for the passage of the child she is carrying.</p>
<p>It is important for you to understand the obstetric anatomy of the female pelvis. You will need this knowledge in order to understand and appreciate the mechanisms of labour, which will be covered later in unit three.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="the-foetal-skull">The Foetal Skull<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-foetal-skull" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>The foetal skull contains a delicate brain, which may be subjected to great pressures as the head passes through the birth canal. You need to understand the landmarks and measurements of the foetal skull, so that you are able to recognise normal presentation and positions, and thus facilitate delivery with the least possible trauma to mother and child.</p>
<p>The landmarks of the skull are described as:</p>
<ul>
<li>The vault, which lies between the orbital ridges and the nape of the neck. Here the bones are thin and pliable so as to allow for alterations of the skull at birth. It is composed of the occipital bone, two parietal bones and two frontal bones.</li>
<li>The base, which is comprised of firmly united bones that protect the vital centres in the medulla.</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="fertilisation-and-development-of-the-fertilised-ovum">Fertilisation and Development of the Fertilised Ovum<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#fertilisation-and-development-of-the-fertilised-ovum" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Fertilisation takes place when the sperm meets the ovum in the fallopian tube. After ovulation, the ovum passes into the fallopian tube and is moved along by the cilia and the peristaltic muscular contraction of the tube. At this time the</p>
<p>Bishop's Computers</p>

<p>Bishop's Computers</p>
<p>cervix produces alkaline mucus that attracts the spermatozoa.</p>
<p>When intercourse takes place many spermatozoa (about 300 million) are deposited in the upper vagina. Those that reach the light cervical mucus survive and propel themselves towards the fallopian tubes while the rest are destroyed by the acid medium of the vagina. Many sperm will reach the fallopian tube where they meet the ovum. During this journey the sperm mature and are capable of producing an enzyme known as hyaluronidase, which will help penetration into the ovum. Only one sperm will enter the ovum. After the sperm goes into the ovum, the membrane is sealed to prevent other sperm from entering.</p>
<p><img src="assets/images/image-20260111-29fcd297.jpeg" alt="img-27.jpeg"></p>
<p>In order to understand the process of fertilisation you will need to remember some of the basic facts. These are:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Each nucleus of the human body cell contains 46 chromosomes. These chromosomes are responsible for the transmission of all inheritable qualities.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The ovum and the spermatozoon each contain only 23 chromosomes, hence when fertilisation occurs, the total 46 chromosomes are established. Thus, some inherited qualities are from the father and some from the mother.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>An ovum always contains one X chromosome.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>A spermatozoon contains either an X chromosome or Y chromosome.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>When a sperm with an X chromosome fertilises an ovum, the combination will be XX. This will give rise to a female. If a sperm with a Y chromosome fertilises an ovum, the combination will be XY. This will give rise to a male.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The unfertilised ovum stays alive for about 24 hours and spermatozoa for up to 48 hours in the genital tract.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>As mentioned earlier, the sperm and the ovum will each contribute 23 chromosomes to make a total of 46 chromosomes. The fertilised ovum is now known as a zygote.</p>
<p>After fertilisation has taken place in the ampulla of the fallopian tube, the zygote goes through the tube and continues until it reaches the uterus three to four days later. During this time, cell division takes place and the fertilised ovum or zygote divides into two cells, then into four and so on until a cluster of cells known as morula is formed. Next, a fluid filled cavity (blastocele) appears in the morula, which now becomes known as a blastocyst. A single layer of cells called the trophoblast, which will later form the placenta and chorion, surrounds the blastocyst. The mass of inner cells forms the foetus and amnion.</p>
<p>Pregnancy begins immediately after fertilisation. This process is then followed by implantation of the developing embryo into the deciduas. Thereafter, there is growth and differentiation of the various organs in the foetus in the first three months of pregnancy.</p>
<p>Development of the placenta and membranes is almost complete by the tenth week of pregnancy.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="formation-of-the-decidua">Formation of the Decidua<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#formation-of-the-decidua" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The decidua is the name given to the endometrium during pregnancy. From conception, increased secretion of oestrogen causes the endometrium to grow four times more than its non-pregnant size. Three layers can now be differentiated: the basal layer, the spongy layer and the compact layer.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="the-spongy-layer">The Spongy Layer<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-spongy-layer" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="the-compact-layer">The Compact Layer<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-compact-layer" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="implantation">Implantation<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#implantation" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>This is a process by which the blastocyst burrows into the thickened endometrium known as decidua. This process takes place on or about the sixth day after fertilisation. When the blastocyst enters the uterus, the trophoblast that lies over the inner cells mass becomes sticky and adheres to the endometrium.</p>
<p>The trophoblast secretes an enzyme, which digests the stroma cells and causes a depression in which the blastocyst lies. This digestion continues until the blastocyst is buried in the spongy layer and the site of entry is sealed. The endometrium closes over it completely.</p>
<p>The blastocyst is now referred to as the embryo. The decidua underneath the blastocyst is called the basal decidua, that which covers it is the capsular decidua and the remainder is called the true decidua. Eventually, as the blastocyst grows and fills the uterine cavity, the capsular decidua meets and fuses with the true decidua.</p>
<p>You will now see what happens to the trophoblast and the inner cell mass, which was mentioned earlier.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="the-trophoblast">The Trophoblast<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-trophoblast" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Small projections called chorionic villi appear all over the surface of the trophoblast at the point of contact with the basal decidua. These grow rapidly and obtain nourishment for the growing embryo.</p>
<p>The villi that proliferate are known as Chorion frondosum, which by the twelfth week are fully developed and form the placenta. Those that surround the decidua capsularis degenerate and become the membrane called the chorion.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="the-inner-cell-mass">The Inner Cell Mass<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-inner-cell-mass" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>While the trophoblast is developing into the placenta, which will nourish the foetus, the inner cell mass is forming the foetus. The cells differentiate into three layers, each of which forms particular parts as follows:</p>
<ul>
<li>The ectoderm, which forms the skin and the nervous system.</li>
<li>The mesoderm, which forms the bones, muscles, the heart and blood vessels.</li>
<li>The endoderm, which forms the mucous membranes and glands.</li>
<li>Two cavities, which appear in the inner cell mass. The amniotic cavity on the side of the ectoderm and the yolk sac on the side of the endoderm.</li>
<li>The amniotic cavity, which is filled with fluid, enlarges and folds round the embryo to enclose it. The amnion forms the lining. It swells and eventually comes into contact with the chorion.</li>
<li>The yolk sac nourishes the embryo until the placenta is sufficiently developed to take over.</li>
<li>Embryo is the name given to the blastocyst for the period from implantation up to eight weeks. During this period all the organs and systems of the body are laid down and thereafter they only grow. From the eighth week through to delivery, the embryo is known as a foetus.</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="the-placenta">The Placenta<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-placenta" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>The fully formed placenta is a spongy disc, circular in shape, about 20 centimetres in diameter, and 2.5 centimetres thick. It weighs about 500 grams and is dark red in colour. It is composed of mainly chorionic villi, which are also known as chorion frondosum. The placenta begins to develop around the fourteenth day after fertilisation, and is fully formed by the sixteenth week of pregnancy.</p>
<p>The placenta has a foetal and a maternal surface. The maternal surface is the one attached to the uterine wall. The foetal surface lies adjacent to the foetus and is covered with amnion, which gives it a shiny appearance.</p>
<p>The placenta is divided into 15 to 20 centimetre lobes by deep grooves. It is made up of 15 to 20 cotyledons and an umbilical cord, which is usually attached near its centre. The umbilical cord has two arteries and one vein.</p>
<p>The developing chorion villi are of two kinds: those that enter deeply into the basal deciduas, anchoring the placenta and shorter ones, which hang loosely in the maternal blood and absorb food, oxygen and excrete waste, known as the nutritive villi. The former are less in number. Mature chorionic villi are tree-like and contain the foetal capillaries network arising from one stem. Each villum is covered with few layers of tissue, which make it impossible for the foetal and maternal blood to mix.</p>
<p>It was mentioned earlier that the placenta is fully formed by the sixteenth week after fertilisation.</p>
<p>Foetal blood is pumped by the foetal heart along the umbilical arteries and through their branches to the capillaries in the chorionic villi in the placenta. Here the blood absorbs oxygen and nutrients and is returned via the umbilical vein to the foetus.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="functions-of-the-placenta">Functions of the Placenta<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#functions-of-the-placenta" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>The placenta has several functions as indicated below:</p>
<ul>
<li>Respiratory, that is, oxygen from the mother's haemoglobin passes to the foetus and the foetus gives off carbon dioxide by simple diffusion.</li>
<li>Nutritive, whereby the foetus obtains nutrients from the mother's diet through the placenta.</li>
<li>Excretory, that is, waste products of metabolism and carbon dioxide are excreted into maternal blood by diffusion.</li>
<li>Protective, where the placenta acts as a barrier against transfer of infection from the mother to the foetus. However, a few organisms such as the rubella virus and the spirochaete of syphilis are able to pass through.</li>
<li>Storage, that is, the placenta stores glycogen and re-converts it for use as required and also stores iron and fat soluble vitamins.</li>
<li>Endocrine, where the placenta acts as an endocrine gland and produces a number of hormones as follows, which include Human Chorionic Gonadotrophin (HCG), oestrogens, progesterone and Human Placental Lactogen (HCL).</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="the-umbilical-cord-or-funis">The Umbilical Cord or Funis<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-umbilical-cord-or-funis" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>The umbilical cord extends from the foetus to the placenta. It consists of the umbilical blood vessels, two arteries and one vein. The blood vessels are enclosed and protected by Wharton's jelly and a layer of amnion covers the whole cord.</p>
<p>The length is about 50 centimetres. If it measures 40 centimetres or less, then it is considered to be short. A very long cord may become wrapped around the neck or body of the foetus or become knotted and cause occlusion of blood vessels, especially during labour.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="the-foetal-sac">The Foetal Sac<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-foetal-sac" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="the-amniotic-fluid-liquor-amnii">The Amniotic Fluid (Liquor Amnii)<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-amniotic-fluid-liquor-amnii" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The amniotic fluid is a clear, pale, straw-coloured fluid consisting of 99% water. The amniotic fluid fills the space enclosed by the amnion which is the innermost membrane enclosing the foetus. It is constantly in circulation, being excreted by the placenta and the amnion itself. Later in pregnancy, the foetus swallows the amniotic fluid and passes it out as urine.</p>
<p>The amniotic fluid is contained in the foetal sac, which consists of a double membrane. The outer membrane is the chorion and the inner is the amnion. The chorion is a thick, opaque and fragile membrane while the amnion is a smooth, tough translucent membrane. It is derived from the inner cell mass. The foetal sac contains the amniotic fluid and the foetus.</p>
<p>The functions of the amniotic fluid include the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>It allows for the growth and free movement of the foetus.</li>
<li>It guards the foetus against mechanical shocks, that is, it acts like a shock absorber.</li>
<li>It equalises the pressure exerted by the uterine contractions.</li>
<li>In the early months of pregnancy, it allows plenty of room for free movement.</li>
<li>In labour, the bag of fore waters assists the dilatation of the cervix.</li>
<li>When membranes rupture during labour, the liquor flushes the birth canal from above with fluid that is aseptic and bactericidal.</li>
</ul>
<p>The source of amniotic fluid is thought to be both foetal and maternal. It is secreted by the amnion, especially that which covers the placenta and the umbilical cord. Some fluid is excluded from the maternal vessels in the deciduas and some from the foetal vessels in</p>

<p>The foetus does not breathe in the utero. If it did, it would drown in liqour amnii. Since the foetus does not breathe, the blood does not go to the lungs for oxygen. <div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="the-hypogastric-arteries">The Hypogastric Arteries<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-hypogastric-arteries" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>These two vessels branch off from the internal iliac arteries. They are known as umbilical arteries when they enter the umbilical cord.</p>
<p>All foetal blood passes through the placenta to get oxygen and nutrients and to eliminate waste. The course taken by the blood through the foetal circulation is as shown opposite.</p>
<p>The blood takes about half a minute to circulate. Oxygenated blood (50% saturated with oxygen) and nutrients flow through the placenta to the umbilical vein.</p>
<p>The umbilical vein takes the purified blood from the placenta and enters the baby's body at the umbilicus, then runs beneath the anterior abdominal wall to the under-surface of the liver (the liver gets the most oxygenated blood in the foetus). Here it gives off two branches to the left lobe. A small branch is also given off to join the portal vein and takes blood to the right lobe.</p>
<p><img src="assets/images/image-20260111-5ef47281.jpeg" alt="img-30.jpeg"></p>
<p>The vein now becomes the ductus venosus and carries the blood to the inferior vena cava where it mixes with blood from the lower parts of the body. The blood then enters the right atrium and most of it passes through the voramen ovale into the left atrium. In this way, it bypasses the lungs. The blood then passes through the mitral valve into the left ventricle and out through the aorta. The aorta gives out branches to the head, neck and arms. These, therefore, receive purer blood than any part of the foetal body except the liver.</p>
<p>Deoxygenated blood from the head, neck and arms passes through the superior vena cava to the right auricle and is directed through the tricuspid valve to the right ventricle. From the right ventricle the blood enters the pulmonary artery. A small branch takes a small amount of blood to the functionless lungs. The remaining blood passes through the ductus arteriosus to the aorta to supply the remaining body organs and legs.</p>
<p>The internal iliac arteries lead into the hypo gastric arteries, which return blood to the placenta via the umbilical arteries. The external iliac artery supplies blood to the lower extremities and returns to the inferior vena cava. The umbilical arteries then pass into the abdominal wall to the umbilical cord at the navel and so reach the placenta where the blood passes through the chronic villi for respiratory and metabolic exchange, ready to circulate through the foetus again.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="adaptation-at-birth">Adaptation at Birth<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#adaptation-at-birth" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>At birth, the baby cries and the lungs expand and their vascular field increases. This draws blood to the lungs through the pulmonary arteries, which is then collected and returned via the pulmonary veins to the left atrium. Placental circulation ceases soon after birth reducing blood flow to the right side of the heart, while increasing blood flow to the left side. This causes the foramen ovale to close.</p>
<p>With the establishment of pulmonary respiration, the ductus arteriosus closes and the cessation of placental circulation results in the collapse and drying of the umbilical vein, the ductus venosus and the hypogastric arteries.</p>
<p>The umbilical vein becomes the ligamentum teres, the ductus venosus the ligamentum venosus and the foremen ovale becomes the fossa ovalis. The hypogastric arteries remain open as the superior vesical arteries.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="the-breasts">The Breasts<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-breasts" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>The breasts are secreting glands composed mainly of glandular tissue arranged in lobes (about 20 in number). Each lobe is divided into lobules that consist of alveoli and ducts. The alveoli contain acini cells, which produce milk. The acini cells are surrounded by myoepithelia cells, which contract and relax to propel milk out. Small lactiferous ducts, carrying milk from the alveoli, unite to form larger ducts. One large duct leaves the lobe and widens to form an ampulla (lactiferous sinus) which acts as a temporally milk reservoir. A lactiferous tubule emerges from</p>

<p>each ampulla to the surface of the nipple. Each breast functions independently of the other.</p>
<p><img src="assets/images/image-20260111-d63e7fb4.jpeg" alt="img-31.jpeg"></p>
<p>The nipple is composed of erectile tissue, which is covered with epithelium and plain muscle fibres. These muscles have a sphincter-like action which controls the flow of milk. Around the nipple is an area of pigmented skin called the areola, which contains the Montgomery's glands. The glands produce sebum, which lubricates the breasts during pregnancy and throughout breastfeeding.</p>
<p>Blood supply is from the internal and external mammary arteries and branches from the intercostal arteries. During lactation blood supply is increased. Blood, from the breasts, drains into the mammary and axillary veins. The lymphatic drainage systems of the two breasts communicate freely. This accounts for the rapid spread of malignant growth from one breast to another. The breasts have poor nerve supply and their function is controlled by hormones.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="physiology-of-lactation">Physiology of Lactation<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#physiology-of-lactation" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>During pregnancy, oestrogen and progesterone hormones stimulate the growth and secretion of colostrum. Production of milk is inhibited by oestrogen. At the end of the third stage of labour, hormone production is reduced by the delivery of the placenta. This stimulates the anterior pituitary gland to release Prolactin, which initiates milk production. The sucking reflex by the infant stimulates the production of oxytocin by the posterior pituitary gland. The oxytocin is carried in the blood stream to the</p>
<p>breasts where it causes the myoepithelia cells to contract and propel milk along the ducts.</p>
<p>You will learn more about breast-feeding in unit three.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="physiological-changes-in-pregnancy">Physiological Changes in Pregnancy<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#physiological-changes-in-pregnancy" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<li>The respiratory system</li>
<li>The renal system</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="changes-in-the-uterus">Changes in the Uterus<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#changes-in-the-uterus" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>The uterus provides a nutritive and protective environment in which the foetus will develop and grow. The uterine muscle fibre increases in size (hypertrophy) and in number (hyperplasia). The uterus continues to grow this way for the first 20 weeks, thereafter, it stretches to accommodate its contents. It increases in size from 60 grams - 900 grams.</p>
<p>By the eighth week of gestation it begins to generate small waves of contractions known as Braxton Hicks contractions, which are painless and continue throughout pregnancy. The blood supply to the uterus increases to keep pace with its growth and also to meet the needs of the placenta.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="changes-in-the-vagina">Changes in the Vagina<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#changes-in-the-vagina" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Oestrogen causes the vagina to become more elastic. These changes will allow dilatation during the second stage of labour to receive the descending foetal head.</p>
<p>There is an increased amount of the normal white vaginal discharge called leucorrhoea. The vaginal ph becomes more acidic to provide protection to some micro-organisms but also increases susceptibility to others such as candida albicans.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="changes-in-the-cardiovascular-system">Changes in the Cardiovascular System<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#changes-in-the-cardiovascular-system" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The cardiac output increases from 5 litres to 7 litres per minute by late pregnancy. This is caused by an increase in resting heart rate of about 15 beats per minute by the end of pregnancy and an increase in blood volume.</p>
<p>The red cell mass increases by about 18% by the end of the pregnancy. The plasma volume increases from the tenth week of pregnancy and reaches its maximum level of 50% above non-pregnant values by the 32nd to 34th week, and maintains this until term.</p>
<p>As the plasma increase is greater than that of the red cell mass, there is haemodilution effect. This results in lowered haemoglobin level. This effect is referred to as physiological anaemia. The mean acceptable HB level in pregnancy is 10 - 12 g/dl of blood.</p>
<p>Despite these changes the blood pressure remains normal. The increased cardiac output does not affect the blood pressure. It may drop slightly by mid-trimester and towards term it returns to the level of the first trimester.</p>

<p>There is increased salivation. Women often experience changes in their sense of taste,</p>
<p>leading to dietary changes and food cravings. Craving for substances such as bricks/soil is known as pica.</p>
