üëã Introduction & Objectives

This section covers both infertility and climacteric crisis and the menopause in women. The Kenyan government, in its policies and guidelines on reproductive health, includes infertility and climacteric problems as components of reproductive health in essential obstetric care.

Objectives

By the end of this section you will be able to:

  • Describe infertility and climacteric crisis
  • Specify causative factors of infertility
  • Describe the management of infertile couples
  • Specify climacteric symptoms
  • Describe the management of a woman who presents with menopause
02

üë™ Infertility

Most normal couples achieve a pregnancy within a few months of trying. However, failure to do so after one year may be arbitrarily defined as infertility, provided that normal intercourse is taking place not less than twice a week. But what is the difference between infertility and sterility?

‚è≥

Infertility

The inability to achieve a pregnancy or to carry a pregnancy to term after one year of unprotected intercourse.

üö´

Sterility

The inability to produce offspring, i.e. the inability to conceive (female sterility) or to induce conception (male sterility).

There are two types of infertility; primary and secondary. In primary infertility, no conception has taken place at all. In secondary infertility, there may have been some conception even if it ended in a spontaneous abortion.

There are several causes of infertility, which will be covered here under three broad categories - general factors, female factors and male factors.

General Factors Affecting Fertility

üéÇ

Age

Fertility in women is at its height in the late teens and early twenties. It declines slowly after 30 years. Male spermatogenesis continues throughout life but ageing reduces fertility.

ü•ó

Health and Nutrition

Anorexia nervosa leads to amenorrhea. Obesity is connected to less frequent ovulation. Chronic alcoholism or drugs (e.g., morphine) depress ovarian activity.

üß†

Psychological Factors

Anxiety and tension seem to be responsible for infertility in some individuals through changes in neuroendocrine control.

Female Factors Affecting Fertility

  • Congenital: Mullerian agenesis (no uterus or ovaries), vaginal atresia (narrowing/stenosis).
  • Infections: Leading to tubal blockage (e.g., gonococcal, chlamydia, tuberculosis, salmonella, enterobias vermicularis, postabortal/puerperal sepsis).
  • Endocrine: Pituitary adenoma (increased prolactin/galactorrhoea), Hormonal imbalance (endometrial hyperplasia), Hypothyroidism, Diabetes mellitus.
  • Uterine Fibromyoma: Distorting uterine cavity or blocking tubes.
  • Cervical Hostility: Cervical mucus unreceptive to spermatozoa (infection or antibodies).
  • Cervical Incompetence: Cause of mid-trimester abortion/secondary infertility.
  • Endometriosis: Menorrhagia may lead to infertility.

Male Factors Affecting Fertility

  • Congenital: Hypospadias (ventral external urethra meatus malformation).
  • Undescended Testes: Temperature of scrotal contents is ~1¬∞C below body temperature. Raised temperature impairs spermatogenesis (undescended testes, hot baths, tight clothes, high altitude).
  • Infections: Bacterial (gonococcal chlamydia blocking vas deferens), Viral (mumps causing orchitis).
  • Auto-immune: Cervix hostile to sperm/antibodies.
  • Impotence: No erection.
  • Coitus Issues: Ignorance or excessive coitus.
  • Sperm Quality: Oligospermia (low count) or Azoospermia (no sperm).
  • Endocrine Disorders: Pituitary failure, adrenal hyperplasia, thyroid deficiency.

Remember

Examine every newborn male at birth to detect undescended testes in order to prevent the problem of infertility later.

üìã Management of Infertility

Infertility is a very stressful condition with a continuous cycle of hope and disappointment. Life centres around trying to conceive a baby and nothing else seems important. Partners blame each other and become frustrated and guilty. Patients should be managed by a gynaecologist.

The couple should be investigated based on their general reproductive history. Clinical examination of each partner is carried out separately and any worries should be elicited and confidential information obtained from each partner.

Investigation Plan

01
Male Partner Evaluation

History of sexual function, past infections (orchitis, STDs). Clinical exam of testes, epididymis, varicocele, hernia, prostate.

02
Semen Analysis

First step in investigation. Abstain for 3-5 days. Check colour, viscosity, bacteriology, motility, morphology (ideal 50%), and count (40-370 million/cc). In Kenya, 20 million is acceptable.

03
Female Partner Evaluation

History of peritonitis (TB), menstrual history, pregnancy outcomes, marital history. Routine pelvic exam.

04
Female Diagnostic Tests

Hysterosalpingography (HSG): For tubal patency.
Laparoscopy + Dye: Assess patency, fimbriae health, tubal movement.
Ovulation Confirmation: Temp method, cervical mucous, Progesterone level (Day 21 > 20 mmol/L), Ultrasound, Endometrial biopsy.

Treatment of Infertility

Not all couples need investigation/treatment. For young couples with short periods of infertility, reassurance may suffice. If no abnormality is found, pregnancy is always possible.

Female Treatment

  • Ovulatory failure: See section two details.
  • Salpingolysis: Dividing peritubal adhesions (most successful tubal operation).
  • Salpingotomy: Opening distal end of hydrosalpinx.
  • Tubal anastomosis: Excising blocked isthmic portion and joining ends.
  • In Vitro Fertilisation (IVF): Oocytes retrieved, fertilised, embryo replaced.

Male Treatment

  • Azoospermia: Treatable with HMG if due to lack of gonadotrophic stimulation. No treatment for congenital/chromosomal causes.
  • Synthetic Androgens: Mesterolone (proviron) 50mg daily for 3 months for spermatogenesis.
  • Oligospermia: Improve general health, exercise, diet. Avoid alcohol, tobacco, caffeine. Excision of varicocele.
  • Artificial Insemination (AIH): Using partner's semen. Useful for impotence or anatomical abnormalities (hypospadias).
üí°

General Advice

Counsel on general measures: refrain from excessive coitus, adequate sleep, weight loss for obese patients, avoid excessive heat to scrotum (hot baths, tight underwear).

Social Impact

Problems faced by infertile couples include poor social standing, economic unproductiveness stress, sexual disinterest, domestic disharmony, separation/divorce, and potential promiscuity increasing STI/HIV risk.

üçÇ Climacteric Crisis

In women climacteric crisis is the period of menopause (while in males it is known as andropause).

Menopause is a period in a woman's life when menstruation ceases naturally. There is progressive ovarian failure, which is preceded by complete absence of menstruation. Menopause is declared after one year of no menstruation.

To some, menopause is a threatening period that declares the end of their femininity, while to others it is a time when many former diseases or ailments recur. A few will see this process as witchcraft or untreatable disease. You should note that sexual desire is present in most women for many years after menstruation ceases.

Climacteric Symptoms

The climacteric symptoms occur as a syndrome (Menopausal syndrome) related to hormonal imbalances.

üå°Ô∏è

Hot Flushes

Flushing of face/neck with sweating. Caused by rise in peripheral blood flow and pulse. 80-85% of women experience this. In Kenya often mistaken for malaria or typhoid.

‚öñÔ∏è

Physical Changes

Weight gain (low activity), Osteoporosis (oestrogen deficiency/calcium excretion), Atrophic vulva changes (dyspareunia).

üòü

Psychological

Anxiety, irritability, insomnia, depression. Aches, pains, headaches, urinary urge/incontinence as physical expressions of anxiety.

‚ö†Ô∏è

Remember

Although excessive and irregular blood loss is not uncommon at about the time of menopause, it must never be accepted as normal or 'just the change'.

Management of Menopause

The majority of women soon adjust to their new situation, especially with family support. For severe problems:

  • Hormone Replacement Therapy (HRT): Oestrogen to suppress hot flushes, sleeplessness, depression. (e.g., Premarin 0.62mgs daily, Progynoval 1mg daily). Long-term therapy (10 years) prevents osteoporosis but may induce endometrial/breast carcinoma (progesterone may prevent this).
  • Local Treatment: Dienestrol cream for atrophic genital changes.
  • Diet: Increase calcium intake. Balanced diet with plant foods (fresh fruits, vegetables) and soya beans (natural oestrogen).
  • Psychological Counselling: Valuable to help patient understand physiological changes.
