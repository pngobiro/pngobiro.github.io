üëã Introduction & Objectives

In this section you are going to focus on menstrual disorders. Menstruation is a normal body event in every woman, even though for some it may be an uncomfortable experience. On a light note, however, there is an old adage that says 'the menstrual flow is the tears of a disappointed uterus'.

Objectives

By the end of this section, you will be able to:

  • Describe factors that influence menstruation
  • Describe the various menstrual disorders and their management

Factors Influencing Normal Menstruation

The events occurring in the following organs influence the mechanism of normal menstruation:

Hypothalamus

Influences the anterior pituitary gland to produce follicle stimulating hormone.

Anterior Pituitary

Produces follicle stimulating hormone (matures Graafian follicle) and luteinising hormone (develops corpus luteum to produce oestrogen/progesterone).

Ovaries

Develop the Graafian follicle.

üè®

Uterine Endometrium

Thickens under the influence of oestrogen and progesterone, in preparation to receive the ovum.

02

üö´ Amenorrhoea

üìñ

Amenorrhoea

A symptom, not a disease. Derived from the Greek word amenrein (A... without, Men... month, Rein... to flow). It means 'absence or cessation of menstruation'.

Physiological (Normal) Amenorrhoea

The absence of menstrual periods can be physiologically normal during these periods in a woman's life:

  • Before puberty, when the hormones concerned have not started functioning.
  • During pregnancy, when the hormones concerned are diverted to the growth of the fertilised ovum.
  • During lactation (after delivery), which results in lactation amenorrhoea due to the presence of prolactin.
  • At menopause, when the hormones diminish and cease to be produced.

Primary Amenorrhoea

Primary amenorrhoea means that menstruation has never occurred. This is seen in a young woman who is over 17 years of age and who has not yet begun to menstruate but exhibits signs of sexual maturation. Pathological primary amenorrhoea is when the patient has never menstruated and has not developed secondary sexual characteristics.

Causes

üß™

Hormonal Factors

Due to the malfunctioning of the pituitary gland. As a result, the hormones responsible for sex maturation are affected, which in turn affects the beginning of menstruation. In Cushing's syndrome, the excessive production of cortisols may hinder menstruation from starting.

üß¨

Developmental Anomalies

During the development of the foetus, the vagina, uterus or ovaries may fail to develop.

‚ö†Ô∏è Imperforate Hymen: Blood accumulates behind the hymen (cryptomenorrhoea). If untreated, leads to haematometra. Patient presents with abdominal pain and absence of menstruation. Cured by incision of the hymen.

Other causes include male pseudohermaphroditism and Turner's syndrome.

Secondary Amenorrhoea

Secondary amenorrhoea simply means that the periods, which were once present, have stopped. It occurs after a normal menarche, which then ceases for more than six months.

Causes

  • Hormonal Disturbances: Hypopituitarism (e.g., Sheehan's disease after severe postpartum haemorrhage), adrenal gland, thyroid gland, or ovarian disturbances.
  • Debilitating Systemic Disorders: Chronic diseases like genital tuberculosis or severe anaemia.
  • Nervous Disorders: Stress acting on the hypothalamus (stress or hypothalamic amenorrhoea). Causes include emotional upsets, psychiatric disorders, exercise amenorrhoea (marathon runners), and brain tumours.
  • Drugs: Post contraceptive amenorrhoea (may take 3-6 months to return). Phenothiazines and certain hypotensive agents (stimulate prolactin).
  • Dietary Amenorrhoea: Weight loss due to prolonged fasting or nutritional deficiency.
  • Ovarian Cysts: Follicular and corpus luteum cysts cause amenorrhoea but tend to regress.

Oligomenorrhoea

This is a type of amenorrhoea where there is infrequent menstruation, which may occur months before menopause and, at times, due to emotional upset.

Management of Amenorrhoea

First, establish the cause. Take a detailed history and carry out a clinical examination to rule out pregnancy. Consider general health, psychological, and environmental factors.

‚ö†Ô∏è

Remember

The most common cause of secondary amenorrhoea is almost always pregnancy, therefore, an obstetric and gynaecological history is important to making a diagnosis.

01
History Taking

Distinguish between primary and secondary amenorrhoea. Note if patient experiences physical disturbances of menstruation without bleeding (possible genital tract cause).

02
Pelvic Examination

Exclude pregnancy or uterine hypoplasia. Bi-manual examination can reveal gross abnormalities such as cryptomenorrhoea.

03
Radiological Examination

X-ray of the chest and straight skull x-ray to detect enlargement of the sella turcica (pituitary fossa).

04
Endocrine Tests

Urine samples (24h) and blood hormone levels (FSH, LH, Prolactin). Refer to endocrinologist if hyper-prolactinaemia is found.

05
Other Procedures

Laparoscopy (for developmental anomalies/biopsy), Dilatation and Curettage (exclude TB), Ultrasound/CT Scan.

Medical Treatment

Treatment depends on the cause:

  • Clomiphene Citrate (Clomid): Induces ovulation. Dosage: 50mg daily for 5 days. If no response, gradually increase up to 200mg. Side effects: Hyper-stimulation, multiple gestation, abortion, teratology, bloating/nausea.
  • HMG and HCG: Indicated when there is failure to ovulate after clomid (6-12 months). Dosage: HMG 375 units daily, increasing up to 1500 units. Requires hormonal monitoring.
  • Bromocriptine: For hyperprolactinaemia. Dosage: 2.5mg up to four weeks.
  • Other Agents: Glucocorticosteroids (dexamethasone 0.5mg nocte, prednisone 0.75mg) and oestradiol.
  • Psychotherapy: For emotional stress.
  • Surgery: Excision of pituitary tumours.

ü§ï Dysmenorrhoea

Dysmenorrhoea means painful menstruation.

Primary (Spasmodic) Dysmenorrhoea

Starts soon after puberty. Pain is 'cramp-like', starts at beginning of period, lasts few hours to 2 days. May radiate to legs, accompanied by nausea/vomiting/fainting.

Theories: Ischaemia due to prolonged uterine contraction, increased Prostaglandins (PGF2), psychological factors, cervical stenosis.

Management

  • Take history, perform physical exam to exclude tumours.
  • Explain normal cycle, advise on exercise and avoiding unnecessary restriction.
  • Drugs: Analgesics/Antipyretics (Aspirin, Paracetamol 2 tabs tid), Mefenamic acid (Ponstan) 500mg tid, Flufenamic acid, Indomethacin. Do not give addictive drugs like morphine/pethidine.
  • Contraceptive Pill: High-progesterone, low-oestrogen combined pill (e.g., minovular) for 4-6 months to inhibit ovulation.
  • Surgery: Pre-sacral neurectomy (last resort).

Secondary (Congestive) Dysmenorrhoea

Caused by pathology in the pelvis. Dull aching pain in lower abdomen, begins 3-4 days (up to 10) prior to menstruation. Made worse by exercise.

‚ö†Ô∏è

Important

Dysmenorrhoea which starts after the age of 30 years should always be investigated since it could be due to some pathology in the reproductive system.

Causes: Chronic PID (most common), Endometriosis, Uterine fibroids, Adhesions, Salpingitis. Management involves treating the cause.

Pre Menstrual Tension Syndrome (PMT)

Symptoms appearing regularly about 12 days before menstruation.

üíß

Water Retention

Weight gain, painful breasts, abdominal distension, bloatedness.

üò©

Pain & Mood

Backache, headache, tension, irritability, depression, loss of concentration.

üòµ

Autonomic

Dizziness, cold sweats, nausea, hot flushes.

Management: Diuretics (Chlorothiazide), Progesterones (Norethisterone 20mg daily day 15-25), Tranquillisers, Psychotherapy, low salt diet, avoid alcohol/caffeine, exercise.

ü©∏ Abnormal Uterine Bleeding

Dysfunctional Uterine Bleeding (DUB)

Diagnosed by exclusion of infection, ectopic pregnancy, trauma, fibroids, cancer, and hormonal treatment. Cause is usually hormonal imbalance.

Management:

  • Refer to hospital. Resuscitate if sick.
  • Teenagers: Combined Oestrogen/Progesterone pill for 3-6 cycles.
  • Reproductive age: Diagnostic curettage needed (rule out malignancy/pregnancy complications).
  • Over 40: Accurate diagnosis and curettage essential.

Menorrhagia

Normal cycle with excessive blood loss (>70ml). Not a disease but a symptom.

Causes: Fibroids, Chronic PID, Polyps, Clotting abnormalities, Hormonal state, Emotional factors, IUCD.

Management: History/Exam, Blood tests (Hb, Grouping), D&C (may be curative), Hysterectomy for older women.

Metrorrhagia

Menstrual bleeding lasting too long. Caused by irregular shedding of endometrium.

Causes: Cancer possibility, Polyps, Incomplete abortion, Hydatidiform mole, Chorion carcinoma, Breakthrough bleeding (contraceptives).

Management: Detailed history, Exam (digital/speculum), Pap smear, Refer to gynaecologist. Never dismiss lightly.

Other Disorders

Epimenorrhoea

Manage by referring for investigation. Treatment may involve hormonal contraceptive pill, iron, diagnostic curettage, or hysterectomy.

Hypomenorrhoea

Considered normal, but may indicate other problems discussed later.

üï∏Ô∏è Endometriosis

Endometrial tissue deposited in any organ (e.g., ovary, uterosacral ligaments). Internal endometriosis (invading myometrium) is called Adenomyosis.

Occurs in tissues adjacent to Mullerian systems. Disseminated by lymphatic/vascular systems. Common in social groups delaying childbearing. Rare before 20 years or after menopause.

Physical Signs

  • 'Chocolate cysts': Tender, fixed, bilateral masses.
  • Uterine lesions: Nodular/uniform enlargement.
  • Recto-vaginal lesions: Tender, hard fixed (simulate rectal carcinoma), cause dyspareunia.
  • Dysmenorrhoea, Menorrhagia, Infertility (30% of patients).

Management

Refer to gynaecologist. Laparotomy indicated if cystic swelling felt.

Under 40 years: Diathermy destruction of areas. Hormonal therapy (progesterone steroids cyclically/continuously for 3-12 months). Pregnancy (suppressive/curative).

Over 40 years: Hysterectomy (may include removal of affected ovary).
