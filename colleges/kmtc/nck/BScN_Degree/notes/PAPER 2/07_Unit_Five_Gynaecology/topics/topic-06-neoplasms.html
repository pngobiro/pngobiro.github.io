üëã Introduction & Objectives

This section looks at neoplastic disorders, (more commonly known as tumours). You may find that here these two terms will be used interchangeably. You need to have an idea of the underlying pathology of these tumours so that you can identify them early and decide whether referral to hospital is urgently needed or not. You will also look at neoplasms of the genital tract and the breast (the breast is an accessory organ of the reproductive system).

This unit covers the types of neoplasms, aetiological factors, and prevention in general and then go on to a more detailed discussion of how each organ is affected.

Objectives

By the end of this section you will be able to:

  • List two types of tumours
  • State causative factors
  • Describe the investigations carried out to rule out tumours
  • Describe the management of various neoplasms of the reproductive organs

üìä General Overview of Tumours

Tumours

There are two types of tumours:

  • Benign: Which are confined to a particular tissue. They include warts or multiple papillomata, fibroma and lipoma and endometriosis.
  • Malignant: Which tend to spread to other organs near and far, for example, carcinomas that affect the epithelial cells and sarcoma affecting the connective tissue.

Causes of Neoplasms

The causes of neoplasms are not well known but they are associated with the following:

  • Drugs: For example, thalidomide, a tranquiliser once widely used, causes deformities in new born babies as well as tumours.
  • Radiation: May lead to leukaemia (cancer of the blood).
  • Toxins: For example, aflatoxin found in cereals and groundnuts may lead to carcinoma of the liver.
  • Alcohol: May lead to cirrhosis of the liver.
  • Smoking: Is associated with cancer of the lungs.
  • High Parity: Women are reportedly prone to cancer of the cervix.
  • Sexual Activity: Especially at an early age may also lead to cancer of the cervix.

Diagnosis and Management

The diagnosis is generally based on clinical suspicion, symptoms, signs and examination and a biopsy for histology.

The management of neoplasms follows the same general measures that you have been looking at throughout this unit. Can you remember them?

The first step is to take a detailed history. Your investigations should include routine laboratory data like haemoglobin, urea, blood group, urinalysis and so on. You should also conduct an Examination Under Anesthesia (EUA) where a biopsy is taken to detect the type of cancer and staging.

Treatment Modalities

  • Surgery: May be palliative in the late stages and in cases where the tumour mass is reduced. Radical surgery is performed in the early stages and involves the whole organ/system being removed. This procedure is sometimes curative.
  • Chemotherapy: Involves the administration of anti-cancer drugs.
  • Radiotherapy: Is usually recommended in the advanced stage.

More often, a combination of all the aforementioned treatments is used.

Prevention

Can this condition be prevented? There are measures that women can take which would help to curb tumours in their early stages and control them. These include the following:

  • A pelvic examination once a year.
  • Breast examination (women can also be taught self-examination and are encouraged to perform this frequently).
  • Pap smear tests for all women of reproductive age once every year. For a detailed explanation of the findings of the Pap smear test, refer to unit four. Remember that Class IV refers to obvious malignancy.

Other measures include:

  • Sonography, whereby a scan of the pelvic area attempts to identify ovarian tumours and uterine fibroids.
  • Endometrial biopsy in post-menopausal bleeding after dilatation and curettage to detect cancer of endometrium.

ü©π Tumours of the Vulva, Urethra & Vagina

Tumours of the Vulva

Genital cancers in women account for 5% of the reproductive organ cancers. The benign lesions (tumours) of the vulva include:

  • Bartholin's cyst/abscess which is the most common.
  • Granuloma lesions which arise as a reaction to germs due to condylomata lata in third stage tissue reaction, tuberculosis, schistosomiasis and/or condylomata acuminata due to viral warts.
  • Very rarely carcinoma may arise in Bartholin's gland.

Another rare tumour of the vulva is basal cell carcinoma (rodent ulcer), which grows slowly and does not metastasise although it infiltrates locally.

Symptoms and Signs

  • Ulcer or swelling, soreness or irritations are early signs.
  • Slight bleeding may occur.
  • Later, there is a purulent discharge, which becomes very offensive.
  • Enlarged inguinal glands may breakdown and ulcerate and occasionally severe haemorrhage may occur from erosion of femoral vessels.

Diagnosis is made by biopsy taken for histologic examination.

Treatment

For cancer of the vulva, treatment will include examination under anaesthesia after which a biopsy is taken, and staging done. Surgery may involve the radical excision of the vulva and of the inguinal and femoral glands on both sides, especially if it is undertaken in the late stage.

Alternatively, an excision and biopsy may be done. Radiotherapy is performed on tumours at the fourchette because of involvement of the anal canal. This may be curative in the early stages.

Tumours of the Urethra

A urethra carbuncle is a small, bright red swelling at the urethral orifice, usually occurring in menopausal women. Some carbuncles cause no symptoms but most are very tender, and may cause dyspareunia or pain on micturition. They are not neoplastic but probably result from chronic infection of the paraurethral glands in the floor of the urethra.

Treatment is by excision or destruction by diathermy. Carcinoma of the urethra is a rare tumour but if it occurs, it is usually a squamous cell carcinoma arising near the meatus. It may sometimes be an adenocarcinoma arising in the paraurethral glands.

Tumours of the Vagina

The benign tumours here include condylomata acuminata, which are also known as venereal genital warts. This is caused by the human papilloma virus and is transmitted through genital contact.

The originally small papillary growths tend to coalesce and form large cauliflower-like masses, which proliferate profusely during pregnancy. The growths may aggregate into a large mass that may even block the introitus during late delivery. Secondary infection is common. These venereal warts usually regress markedly during puerperium and may even disappear. The virus does not affect the foetus.

Management

The most effective drug is fluorouracil, recommended for use only during non-pregnant state because it is noxious to the foetus. The gravida with this condition should be treated by cryotherapy or excision of the lesions. Improved vaginal hygiene is also beneficial.

Other Tumours

Fibromyoma is another benign tumour which may occasionally arise in the vaginal wall. It is hard and smooth and can easily be enucleated. Another possible tumour is endometriosis.

Carcinoma of the vagina is rare and, if it occurs, it is usually of the squamous type. Cancer of the cervix very commonly spreads to the vaginal vault. In the case of endometrial carcinoma, isolated vaginal metastases may occur. Patients complain of bleeding, especially after coitus and later, an offensive watery discharge. The growth may ulcerate forming a fistula into the rectum or vagina.

Treatment is often very unsatisfactory. Most cases are treated by local application of radium or caesium and external irradiation of the lymphatic glands of the pelvis. The only alternative treatment is extensive operation to remove the whole vagina, uterus and pelvic lymphatic glands. If the rectum is involved, it is also removed and colostomy established.

üéóÔ∏è Tumours of the Cervix

The benign tumours here are Polyps, Fibroids, and Nabothian follicles (which are often a result of infection).

The carcinomas include adenocarcinoma, which is common, and squamous, which is less common.

Carcinoma of the cervix is a common genital malignancy. It begins in the cervix at the margin of the external os where it stays for sometime before it begins to penetrate the neighbouring tissues. It extends outwards towards the pelvic wall, downwards into the vagina, backwards to the rectum and forwards to the bladder.

Signs and Symptoms

The affected patient is likely to be between 30 to 40 years of age and the signs and symptoms may present with:

  • Post-coital bleeding because the penis rubs on the ulcerated cervix.
  • Post-menopausal bleeding. Any woman who resumes bleeding per vagina after menstruation had ceased should suspect the possibility of cancer of the cervix.
  • Offensive purulent discharge per vagina will indicate infection of the necrotic surfaces of the tumour.
  • Pain in the lower abdomen or back (this is a very late symptom).

Risk Factors

‚ö†Ô∏è

Behavioural & Physical

  • Early age (usually below 18 years) of first coitus.
  • Multiple sexual partners.
  • Viral infections like Herpes Simplex Type II.
  • High parity, that is, above five children.
  • Poor hygiene, whereby dirt collects in the vagina or in the foreskin of uncircumcised men.

Prevention and Management

Prevention includes educating women of a reproductive age to be aware of the signs and symptoms and to take the Papanicolaou test every year.

Initial Diagnosis Steps:

  1. Take the patient's history
  2. Physical examination
  3. Perform a biopsy
  4. In the early stage, a Pap smear test will help discover whether the condition is curable
  5. Examination Under Anaesthesia (EUA) in order to conduct the biopsy and do staging

After a diagnosis has been made, treatment should be given. Surgery involves radical hysterectomy in the early stages or may be necessary in combination with radiotherapy. Radiotherapy is either external (pelvis) or intracavity (caecium in the uterine cavity).

The prognosis is best in the early stages of the disease. Survival from the five year time frame and beyond is very poor. Complications of radiotherapy include relapses, ovarian failure, RVF and VVF.

ü•ö Tumours of the Ovaries

The ovary is the only organ in the body that is capable of producing many varieties of tumours. The majority of them are cystic and benign. Some are highly malignant and some produce oestrogen with symptoms of menorrhagia.

Cysts

  • Follicular cysts: Symptoms vary from no symptoms to pain and/or haemorrhage. May reach the size of a tennis ball.
  • Lutein and theca lutein cysts: Lined by cells of the corpus luteum type. Look yellowish. Most common type is the corpus luteum cyst.
  • Blood cysts: Bleeding into the follicular cyst, corpus luteum or neoplasm. If blood shed for some time, it is thicker and darker ('chocolate' or 'tarry' cyst).

Benign Ovarian Neoplasms

  • Mucinous cystadenoma: Most common. Can reach enormous size. Rare before puberty.
  • Serous cystadenoma: Common. Smooth lining.
  • Dermoid cyst (Benign teratoma): Contains tissues from multiple germ layers (endoderm, mesoderm, ectoderm). Most common between 20-30 years.

Malignant Tumours

Third most common tumours affecting the genital tract. Tend to spread quietly around peritoneum and alimentary canal. Prognosis is usually poor due to silent nature.

Complications

Torsion

Twisting on pedicle. Sudden severe pain, vomiting. Tender firm lump separated from uterus.

Rupture

Serious event. Immediate laparotomy needed if impending rupture.

ü©∏

Haemorrhage

Small haemorrhages common. Massive haemorrhage causes pain similar to torsion.

Infection

Not common. May be involved in appendicitis or diverticulitis.

Management

Possibilities include cystectomy (shelling out benign tumour), removal of one or both ovaries/tubes/uterus, or biopsy of inoperable mass. Torsion and rupture are acute surgical emergencies requiring ovary and tube removal.

Uterine Fibroids (Fibromyoma)

The benign tumour that is the most common growth in the uterus of women is the fibroid. Arises in the muscular wall. Varies in size from seedling to enormous masses.

Symptoms and Signs

  • Abdominal tumour: Often first sign. Rarely painful but causes discomfort/weight.
  • Menorrhagia: Common reason for seeking advice. Increased amount/duration, clots, regular cycles.
  • Pain: Rare unless complications (torsion/degeneration) or associated endometriosis.
  • Pressure: Frequency/retention of urine.
  • Child bearing: Causes abortion, obstructs labour.
  • Physical signs: Asymmetrical enlargement, harder than pregnancy, not tender (unless red degeneration), uterine souffl√©, displaced cervix.

Management

For small benign tumours causing no symptoms: No treatment, regular re-examination (6-12 months). Usually cease growth after menopause.

Surgical treatment indicated for: Heavy/prolonged bleeding, large tumours (especially in young women), possible malignant change (growth after menopause), retention of urine, obstruction of labour, torsion.

  • Myomectomy: Removal of fibroid only. Uterus retained. For women wishing to keep uterus/infertility.
  • Hysterectomy: Removal of uterus with fibroid. For patients 40+ years.

Prognosis: Surgical therapy is curative. Pregnancy possible after myomectomy.

Carcinoma of the Uterus

Common malignancies affecting the female are those of the breast and uterus. Sarcomas are rather uncommon uterine malignancies accounting for less than 2% of total malignancies. Occurs most frequently in postmenopausal women.

üéÄ Carcinoma of the Breast

The breast is the most common site of carcinoma in women aged 40 to 44 years of age and the leading cause of death.

üîç Click to zoom

Fig. 6.2: Breast cancer risk factors and anatomy.

Risk Factors

  • Heredity (mothers/sisters with breast cancer: 2-3x risk).
  • Marital status/Parity (higher in single/nulliparous; lower in 3+ children).
  • Mammary dysplasia or cystic disease.
  • Cancer in opposite breast.
  • Cancer of uterus/ovary (doubled risk).
  • Abnormal hormonal environment.
  • Oral contraceptives and menopausal oestrogen.

Clinical Evaluation

80% present with painless lump. 90% discovered by patient.

Examination: Non-tender firm/hard lump, poorly delimited. Nipple/skin retraction. Erosion of nipple. Discharge (watery/serous/bloody).

Diagnosis

  • Blood tests (Sedimentation rate, Alkaline phosphatase, Calcium).
  • Imaging: X-ray (lungs), CT scan (bones/brain), Mammogram.
  • Biopsy (Mandatory): Needle biopsy or Open biopsy (general anaesthesia). Cytology of discharge.

Management

  • Radical Mastectomy: Removal of breast, pectoral muscles, axillary nodes. Contra-indicated if spread to sub-clavicular nodes.
  • Extended Radical Mastectomy: Adds internal mammary nodes.
  • Modified Radical Mastectomy: Preserves pectoralis major muscles (cosmetic/functional).
  • Simple Mastectomy: Malignancy confined to breast.
  • Local Excision/Lumpectomy: For early breast cancer (Stage I).
  • Radiotherapy: For locally advanced cases or refusal of mastectomy. Can be pre/post-operative.
  • Chemotherapy: Especially for positive axillary nodes or distant metastases (Stage IV).

Complications & Prognosis

Complications include local recurrence and oedema of the arm (lymphatic obstruction). Chronic oedema managed by elevation and elastic sleeve.

Prognosis: 5-year cure rate for confined cancer is 75-90%. With axial nodes, drops to 40-60%. More malignant in young women.

Benign Breast Conditions

Mammary Dysplasia (Chronic Cystic Mastitis)

Common in 30-50 years. Related to ovarian activity (oestrogen). Symptoms: asymptomatic lump, nipple discharge, pre-menstrual discomfort/cyst increase. Fluctuation in size is common.

Management: Biopsy to rule out cancer. Aspiration of cyst. Cytology. Re-examination. Support brassiere for pain. Risk of cancer is ~2x general population.

Fibro Adenoma

Common in young women (within 20 years of puberty). Round, firm, discrete, movable, non-tender mass. Treatment: Excision and pathologic examination.

Hydatidiform Mole

Abnormal pregnancy with proliferation of chorionic villi covering and dissolution of vascular stroma. Results in mass of cysts resembling bunch of grapes. Can be Complete (no foetus, higher malignancy risk) or Partial (associated with foetus).

Aetiology: Maternal age (<20 or >45), High parity, Malnutrition.

Clinical Features

  • Bleeding (common, sometimes with vesicles).
  • Hyperemesis (due to high HCG).
  • Pallor and dyspnoea (anaemia).
  • Anxiety/Tremors (HCG weak thyroid stimulating properties).
  • High HCG after 12 weeks.
  • Uterus larger than expected. 'Doughy' feel. No foetal heart/parts.
  • Pre-eclampsia signs at 16 weeks. Proteinuria.

Management

Investigation: Urinary HCG, Ultrasound (highly diagnostic).

Action: Evacuation by suction (usually feasible up to 14 weeks). Risks: haemorrhage, perforation, dissemination. Oxytocin for severe bleeding. Prostaglandin/Oxytocin if >16 weeks. Hysterectomy for older women.

üìÖ

Follow-up

  • Family planning for one year.
  • Monthly pregnancy test for one year. If titres increase -> suspect Choriocarcinoma.
  • Chemotherapy if high/detectable HCG after 6 months.
  • Chest x-rays monthly until negative titres, then every 2 months.

Prognosis: Almost 100% cure rate with close follow-up and early chemotherapy.

Choriocarcinoma

Cancer of the placenta. Common in East Asia. 50% occur after hydatidiform mole, 25% after abortion, 25% after pregnancy. Poor prognosis if untreated.

Management: Chemotherapy is immediately indicated. Methotrexate is the principle drug. Combined therapy used if ineffective. Advise delaying conception for a year after chemotherapy.
