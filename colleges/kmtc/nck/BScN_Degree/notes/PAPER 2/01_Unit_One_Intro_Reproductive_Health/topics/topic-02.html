Adolescent and Youth Health

üå± Introduction & Objectives

Although adolescents and the youth form the majority of our population, their issues have, for a long time, been ignored. Yet young people have special needs and requirements. At the fourth World Conference on Women in Beijing in 1995, it was concluded that adolescents are a particularly vulnerable group due to the fact that they are known to have:

  • Inadequate level of knowledge about human sexuality
  • Inadequate or poor information on quality reproductive health services
  • High risk sexual behaviours
  • Discriminatory social practices

In response to these insights, the GoK decided to address adolescent and youth issues by developing new strategic approaches to communication programmes to enhance positive behaviour development and adoption by young people.

üìñ Defining Adolescence

Adolescence is defined as the transition period between childhood and adulthood. It is a period of rapid growth and maturation as the person reaches puberty. The most colourful years of life are the teenage years. This is a period when the youth want to be appreciated for who they are and seek affection and attention.

‚ÑπÔ∏è

Age Ranges

The onset of adolescence varies from one individual to the other, and between the two sexes:

  • Girls: May begin as early as 9 years
  • Boys: May begin at 12 years
  • General Range: 13 - 18 years
  • Youth: Extends up to 21 years

However, according to the laws of Kenya, a person is considered to be an adult and answerable for their own actions from the age of 18.

üß¨ Characteristics of Adolescence

Physical Characteristics

Adolescence is marked by the development of secondary characteristics under the influence of the gonadotrophic hormone from the anterior pituitary gland.

Girls (Influence of Oestrogen) Boys (Influence of Androgens)
Breasts increase in size and are spherical in shape due to enlarged glandular tissue Enlarged testis and penis
Typical female shape and contour of the body develop (broad hips, narrow chest/shoulders) Body shape takes on typical adult characteristics (broad shoulders, rapid growth in height)
Hair develops in the armpits and pubic region Development of armpit, pubic and facial hairs
Internal organs of reproduction mature (vagina, uterus, ovaries) and menstruation (menarche) begins First ejaculation (spermache) and nocturnal emissions (wet dreams) occur
Face may become smooth or facial pimples (acne) may develop Skin problems such as acne develop and face looks rough; voice deepens

Emotional & Psychological Changes

While the physiological changes are taking place and can be observed, great emotional and psychological changes are taking place that may not be as noticeable.

  • Changing relationships with parents: May involve the adolescent pulling away and becoming more independent.
  • Changing relationships with friends: Peers are an important influence but they care more about what their friends think of them.
  • Relationship with the opposite sex: Increases as they learn how to cope with romantic and sexual feelings.
  • Personal feelings: Need to accept themselves as an independent individual.
  • Values and behaviours: May attempt to behave more as adults, resolving problems in a responsible manner.
  • Recreational activities: Increased interest in everyday recreational activities.
  • Mood swings: Increase as they seek attention and want to belong and be appreciated.

Reproductive Health Needs

Young people have special needs in all circumstances. Each age group within this population (9 - 24 years) has different problems and requirements. As a health worker, you need to address the reproductive health needs of adolescents/youths diligently in order to assist them to make informed choices.

Common reproductive health problems include early childbearing (70% prevalence), STIs/HIV/AIDS (45% prevalence), and unsafe abortion.

Identified Needs Include:

  • Information on sexuality and reproductive health
  • Access to family planning services and provision of effective methods
  • Prenatal and post abortion care, irrespective of age or marital status
  • Safe delivery, preferably in a hospital
  • Diagnosis and treatment of sexually transmitted diseases
  • Protection from sexual abuse
  • Culturally appropriate guidance and counselling
  • Education in negotiating skills

Principles for Working with Young People

  • Understand cultural sensitivities
  • Identify and encourage peer leadership
  • Establish links between health and community services
  • Ensure Privacy and Confidentiality
  • Consider the gender of the service provider (refer to same sex where possible)

Services to Meet Youth Needs

In order to help the adolescent/youth enjoy good reproductive health, there is a need to do the following:

  • Recognise the importance of health education and give services to meet the needs of adolescents both in and out of school. Integrated sex education and services for young people should include family planning information and counselling on gender relations, sexually transmitted diseases and HIV/AIDS, sexual abuse and reproductive health rights
  • Ensure that health care programmes and providers attitudes allow adolescents access to RH services and information
  • Support efforts to eradicate female genital mutilation and other harmful practices, including sexual abuse, trafficking of adolescents for forced labour, marriage and commercial sex
  • Develop a comprehensive reproductive health package to address youth/adolescent issues through the provision of counselling and user friendly clinics
  • Promote responsible and healthy RH and sexual behaviour of the youth through counselling and guidance
  • Sensitise leaders, programme managers, service providers, teachers, the community, adolescents and youths themselves, on the reproductive health needs and rights of adolescents and youths

Once the youths know their rights and have appropriate information, they will be able to make informed choices. Consequently, their reproductive health will improve tremendously. It is important to remember that young people are flexible, resourceful and energetic. They can help each other through peer counselling and peer education. You should, therefore, try to tap these strengths in order to be able to provide them with the necessary support.

üß≠ Life Planning Skills

Life planning skills refer to the information that you impart to young people to help them cope with the life challenges they meet as they grow up. Life planning skills are very handy when counselling adolescents who are facing a dilemma and trying to make an informed choice or decision. These are divided into two components:

  1. Values and values verification
  2. Decision making abilities (communication, assertiveness, negotiation)

Values and Harmful Practices

Values dictate the behaviour of individuals. Unfortunately, some communities value practices that pose significant danger to reproductive health.

‚ñ∂ Wife Inheritance

This practice risks the spread of STIs/HIV/AIDS and all their consequences. It is also one of the main causes of disharmony in the family unit.

‚ñ∂ Wife Beating

A number of women have been maimed. Often they feel stigmatised and unable to seek medical attention. Some suffer miscarriages and premature deliveries due to violence.

‚ñ∂ Female Genital Mutilation (FGM)

Brings risk of infection and bleeding. Long term leads to prolonged second stage of labour due to scarring, lacerations, and injury to pelvic floor.

‚ñ∂ Early Girl Child Marriage

Early pregnancy presents complications like prolonged/obstructed labour and ruptured uterus due to immature pelvis. Leads to high school dropout rates and illiteracy.

‚úÖ

Positive Values to Reinforce

  • Education of the Girl Child: Enables knowledge of health issues, economic empowerment, and productivity.
  • Abstinence: Encouraging delaying sex until marriage reduces HIV/AIDS spread and unwanted pregnancies.
  • Virginity: Both girls and boys should be encouraged to remain virgins and abstain from premarital sex until they get married. This will help in reducing the spread of HIV/AIDS, which is a national disaster as the majority of the youths are infected/affected. It will also reduce the risk of unwanted pregnancy and the accompanying health consequences.

Decision Making & Assertiveness

Decision making is the art of making a choice out of several options. Key skills include:

  • Communication: The art of passing information. When the youth are equipped with accurate information about themselves, they will be able to account for decisions they make. They must have good communication skills.
  • Assertiveness: Being confident and able to make a stand (e.g., saying NO to drugs).

The Five Rights of Assertiveness:

  1. Right to Expression
  2. Right to Set one's priorities
  3. Right to Refuse requests without guilt
  4. Right to Judge one's behaviour
  5. Right to Seek help

Young people need accurate information regarding reproductive health, especially related to HIV/AIDS and substance abuse. Once they know the facts and the outcomes of their behaviour, they will be able to communicate effectively, make informed choices and defend them against their peers. They must know the options available based on the consequences. Negotiation and assertiveness skills play an important role in communication as the youth make decisions, which affect their reproductive health.

The last and most important step of decision making is seeking help. Our youths need to feel free and look for information and assistance when not sure of the decision to be made. They should know where to go for assistance when they need help. This could be to their parents, teachers, religious leaders or health professionals.

These life planning skills are important to the youths. So learn them well to enable you to impart them to the young people.

Consequences of Poor Decisions

  • Drug Abuse: Impairs ability to make sound decisions.
  • Unprotected Sex: Risks STIs/HIV/AIDS, unwanted pregnancy, poverty.
  • Crime: Robbery/stealing often found among those with low negotiation and assertive skills. In order to enhance the health and well being of adolescents and youths, remedial activities need to be undertaken.

üõ†Ô∏è Activities to Improve Health

As a health care provider you should advocate active involvement of adolescents/youth in projects aimed at promoting responsible sexuality, preventing unwanted pregnancy, unsafe abortion and spread of STIs/HIV/AIDS. The advocacy should focus on several issues. Most importantly, there should be attempts to integrate gender issues into youth programmes so that they reinforce appropriate values. Additionally, you should incorporate Information, Education and Communication (IEC) in the reproductive health programs.

Key Activities

  • Advocating the recognition of socioeconomic and reproductive health needs and rights of adolescents and the youth
  • Establishing an understanding with youth advisory councils at all levels to coordinate and advise on youth and adolescent health
  • Developing specific messages for different target groups (parents, religious and other leaders, youths and adolescents)
  • Implementing youth friendly and accessible RH services to enable the youth to seek services and receive counselling without barriers. Health providers should create a conducive environment when counselling and avoid biases that discourage youths from coming to the facility
  • Educating youths on their rights in order to help them attain the highest degree of health and self esteem. They should be sensitised to advocate against marriage at an early age, female circumcision, child abuse and so on. They should also advocate for the respect of good cultural values, access to RH services and the need for informed choice and privacy
  • Conducting basic/applied research on youth and adolescent issues on regular basis and implementing the findings

Important Note

Young people are flexible, resourceful and energetic. They can help each other through peer counselling and peer education. Health workers should tap into these strengths to provide necessary support.
