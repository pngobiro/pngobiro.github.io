üíâ Obstetric Anaesthesia

Anaesthesia means absence of sensation and freedom from pain. General anaesthesia is the induction of unconsciousness, which may also involve the giving of some analgesia.

Remember: General anaesthesia for a pregnant woman in her second and third trimesters, or for a mother who has just delivered, is dangerous due to the effect of progesterone.

General Anaesthesia

It is essential that only trained anaesthesia personnel administer general anaesthesia to patients. Moreover, it is essential to place an endotracheal tube in all pregnant patients undergoing general anaesthesia, since the risk of aspiration pneumonitis, secondary to the inhalation of gastric content, is a significant one.

Until the baby is born, the mother should receive 100% oxygen and a low concentration of an inhalation agent such as isoflurane 0.5%. After delivery anaesthesia can be deepened by supplementing the anaesthetic with higher concentrations of potent volatile inhalation agents.

ü§¢

Mendelson's Syndrome

It is believed that in pregnancy and especially in advanced labour there is a delay in gastric emptying time, due to the effect of progesterone on the gastro-intestinal tract. In cases where the acid stomach contents are aspirated into the lungs, a condition known as Mendelson's Syndrome result. The alveoli are damaged which causes the impairment of gaseous exchange. It is impossible to oxygenate the mother and in severe cases death may ensue.

Prevention: The main method of prevention is the administration of antacid therapy while in labour to high risk patients. Rapid sequence induction is used for unprepared patients. The method always includes endo-tracheal intubation with the use of cricoid pressure.

ü´Ä

Aortocaval Occlusion

The cause of aortocaval occlusion is the weight of the gravid uterus, which partially blocks the inferior vena cava. The venous return is then reduced, which in turn leads to a fall of cardiac output. This occurs when a pregnant woman lies supine in late pregnancy for a long period in labour.

Prevention: Encourage the mother in labour to remain in an upright position for as long as possible. During labour, when the woman needs to lie flat, the midwife should ensure she is tilted to the left at an angle of 15¬∞.

Maternal Awareness

The woman retains a high level of consciousness and she is able to recall events that occurred during the operation. However, she is unable to give any indication of sensation since she is paralysed. This can be a terrifying experience.

Regional Anaesthesia

The operational region is made free from pain by infiltrating an anaesthetic drug into the nerves supplying the region.

1Ô∏è‚É£

Epidural Block

Achieved by infiltrating anaesthetic into the epidural space. Can be Lumbar (most common) or Caudal (uncommon).

2Ô∏è‚É£

Spinal Anaesthesia

Local anaesthestic solution is injected into the subarachnoid space. Suitable for caesarean section provided the operation is not prolonged.

3Ô∏è‚É£

Pudendal Block

Used to anaesthetise areas that are supplied by pudendal nerve, namely lower vargina, perineum and vulva. This method is very unreliable.

4Ô∏è‚É£

Para Cervical Block

The para cervical plexus is blocked. The technique is not favourable since the uterine artery passes close to the nerve plexus.

Complications of Regional Anaesthesia

  • Hypotension: Result of vasodilatation. Prevented by rapid intravenous infusion (preload).
  • Dural Tap: Accidental puncture of the durameter. Causes severe headache.
  • Total Spinal Block: Accidental extensive block leading to collapse and cardiac arrest.
  • Bloody Tap: Puncture of epidural veins. Risk of toxicity.

Inhalation Anaesthesia

This is a process by which you administer volatile agents using a facemask in pain relief. It is indicated in the late first stage and during the second stage of labour.

There are two types of inhalation anaesthesia:

  • Tritene: Up to 0.5% of tritene in air. Accumulates in maternal blood stream causing sleep/disorientation.
  • Entonox: Composed of 50% nitrous oxide and 50% oxygen. When used in high concentration it is suitable for anaesthesia. It has no side effects.

Episiotomy

Episiotomy is invasion through the perineal tissues, which is designed to enlarge the vulva outlet during delivery. Give an episiotomy only when indicated.

Procedure

01
Timing & Preparation

Best timed when the presenting part is directly applied to the perineum. Prepare trolley with suture pack, lignocaine, chromic catgut, needle holder, scissors, and artery forceps.

02
Infiltration

When the head reaches the pelvic floor, two fingers of the left hand are inserted between the perineum and the foetal head. Lignocaine, 0.5%, is infiltrated into the area.

03
Incision

Using the right hand, the midwife places the tip of the opened scissors and makes an incision at the height of a contraction.

04
Repair

The episiotomy should be repaired as soon as possible (immediately after the third stage). The repair begins at the apex of the vaginal wound.

Vacuum Extraction (Ventouse)

Indications for vacuum delivery: Mild foetal distress, Delay in second stage, Malposition, Maternal exhaustion.

Fig. 5.5: Vacuum Extraction

Procedure Highlights: The cup is placed on the scalp as near as possible to the posterior fontanelle. The nurse uses the hand pump to create vacuum gradually. Increase negative pressure by 0.2kg/cm at one minute interval until 0.8kg/cm is attained. Intermittent traction is applied with uterine contraction.

Remember: Never use the cup actively to rotate the baby's head during the procedure. Do not continue this procedure for more than 30 minutes.

Forceps Delivery

This procedure is performed by a forceps which is an instrument that has two parts that cross each other like scissors and lock at the intersection. Types include: Low Forceps, Mid Forceps, and High Forceps (rarely used).

01
Application

The left blade is applied first. The blade is passed between the head and the palm or surface of the right fingers.

02
Positioning

The shanks are pressed backwards against the perineum and the handle should lock and lie in a horizontal position.

03
Delivery

Traction is applied during contractions. The direction of pull is altered as the head descends to follow the curve of birth canal.

Caesarean Section

Caesarian section is the delivery of the foetus through an incision made on the abdominal wall and uterus.

1Ô∏è‚É£

Lower Segment

The operation of choice. Blood loss is minimal, incision is easy to repair, and risk of rupture during subsequent labour is lessened.

2Ô∏è‚É£

Classical

Incision made directly into the wall of the body of the uterus. Rarely performed now (e.g., gestation <32 weeks).

3Ô∏è‚É£

Extra-Peritoneal

Access to lower uterine segment secured by dissecting tissues around bladder to by-pass peritoneal cavity. Outdated.

4Ô∏è‚É£

Hysterectomy

Porro's Operation. Removal of uterus follows C-section (e.g., placenta accreta, rupture).

Vasa Praevia

Occurs when there is a velamentous insertion and the blood vessel from the cord lies over the os. Rupture of vessels should be suspected when there is fresh bleeding after the rupture of the membranes following foetal distress.

Cord Prolapse

Cord prolapse is a term used when the umbilical cord lies in front of the presenting part and the membranes are ruptured.

Fig. 5.6: Cord Presentation and Prolapse

Management of Pulsating Cord (First Stage)

01
Immediate Action

Push up the presenting part so as not to compress the cord. If the cord is outside the vagina, gently replace it in the vagina to prevent spasm and keep warm.

02
Positioning

Keep the mother in knee chest position without removing your fingers. Continue elevating the presenting part until the patient is in theatre.

03
Prepare for Surgery

Give oxygen, draw blood for grouping/cross matching, start IV line. Rush to theatre for emergency C-section.

Fig. 5.7: Management of Cord Prolapse Flowchart

Post Partum Haemorrhage (PPH)

Defined as excessive bleeding of more than 500mls of blood from the genital tract after the birth of a baby.

1Ô∏è‚É£

Primary PPH

Occurs within 24 hours. Sources: Placental site, Lacerations, Blood coagulation disorders.

2Ô∏è‚É£

Secondary PPH

Occurs after 24 hours up to 6 weeks. Causes: Retained placenta/products, infection.

Management Principles: Arrest bleeding (rub uterus, syntometrine, bimanual compression), Restore blood volume, Treat circulatory failure.

Ruptured Uterus

‚ö° Late Signs: Mother is dehydrated, rapid pulse, pyrexia, uterus moulded round foetus (Bandl's ring), presenting part high/wedged, mother feels "something has given way" followed by shock.

Management: Combat shock (IV fluids), Prepare for emergency C-section/Hysterectomy/Repair.

Shoulder Dystocia

Failure of the shoulder to rotate spontaneously after delivery of the head.

  • McRoberts Manoeuvre: Mother lies flat, knees brought up to chest.
  • Supra Pubic Pressure: Apply pressure to dislodge anterior shoulder.
  • Rubin's Manoeuvre: Push posterior shoulder towards foetal chest.
  • Zavanelli Manoeuvre: Reinsert head and perform C-section (last resort).

Shock

Collapse of the circulation system.

01
Initial Stage

Reduced venous return, stroke volume, and cardiac output.

02
Compensatory Stage

Sympathetic nervous system constricts vessels. Increased heart rate. Adrenaline/aldosterone released.

03
Hypovolaemic Stage

Further fall in cardiac output/BP. Vital organs lack perfusion. Renal failure begins.

04
Irreversible Stage

Multisystem failure and death.
