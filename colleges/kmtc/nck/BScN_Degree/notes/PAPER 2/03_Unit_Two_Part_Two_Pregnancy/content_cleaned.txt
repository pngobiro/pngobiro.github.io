<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="introduction">Introduction<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#introduction" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

Remember that a successful pregnancy and delivery depend to a great extent on the care given right from conception to delivery. The knowledge, skills and attitudes acquired will enable you to manage mothers competently.</p>
<p>As you progress, reflect upon the topics that were covered in part one of this unit, that is, fertilisation, development of the foetus, physiological changes in pregnancy, the breast and lactation. The knowledge you gained will be applied as you go through this section.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="diagnosing-pregnancy">Diagnosing Pregnancy<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#diagnosing-pregnancy" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>You will start by looking at the diagnosis of pregnancy and defining some of the terms commonly associated with pregnancy.</p>
<ul>
<li>Trimester refers to a three month calendar period.</li>
<li>Gravidity is the number of times a woman has been pregnant regardless of the outcome of the pregnancies.</li>
<li>Parity refers to the delivery of a child that grew beyond 28 weeks (the age of viability) irrespective of whether the baby was born alive or dead.</li>
<li>A Primigravida is a woman who is pregnant for the first time.</li>
<li>A Primipara is a woman who has had only one delivery.</li>
<li>A Multigravida is a woman who has had two or more pregnancies.</li>
<li>Multipara refers to a woman who has had two or more deliveries.</li>
<li>Nullipara is a term that refers to a woman who has never delivered.</li>
<li>A Grand multipara is a woman who has had six or more deliveries.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="remembe">Remembe<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#remembe" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>If a woman keeps on aborting before the 28th week, she becomes a multigravida but will remain nullipara.</p>
<p>The average duration of pregnancy is 266 days. This is counting from the time the pregnancy started, which is after ovulation and approximately two weeks after the first day of the Last Normal Menstrual Period (LNMP). If the counting is made from the first day of the LNMP, then the duration is 280 days or forty weeks.</p>
<p>The duration of pregnancy can also be expressed in months. These can be calendar months or lunar months. A lunar month is the time from one new moon to the next. It is equal to four weeks or exactly 28 days. A calendar month is longer, usually 30 or 31 days. Therefore, on average, pregnancy lasts 40</p>
<p>weeks, that is, ten lunar months or approximately nine calendar months.</p>
<p>The diagnosis of pregnancy is made on the basis of symptoms, signs and investigations of pregnancy. These can be conveniently divided into those of the first, second and third trimester.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="signs-and-symptoms-of-pregnancy">Signs and Symptoms of Pregnancy<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#signs-and-symptoms-of-pregnancy" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The main symptoms and signs during the first trimester include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Amenorrhoea</li>
<li>Enlargement and tenderness of the breasts</li>
<li>Nausea</li>
<li>Excessive salivation (ptyalism)</li>
<li>Frequency of micturition due to the pressure of the gravid uterus on the urinary bladder</li>
<li>Constitutional symptoms (that is, tiredness, weakness and sometimes depression)</li>
</ul>
<p>As previously mentioned, a pregnant woman often experiences tiredness, general weakness and sometimes depression. The real cause of these symptoms is not established but may be hormonal. These symptoms usually improve by the 16th week after the last menstruation.</p>
<p>As the pregnancy progresses more signs and symptoms are observed. You will cover each of these in a little more detail.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="enlargement-of-the-breasts">Enlargement of the Breasts<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#enlargement-of-the-breasts" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The nipple and the areola increase in size. Small nodules, known as Montgomery's tubercles, develop around the nipple. The areola darkens.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="the-cervix">The Cervix<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-cervix" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The cervix becomes bluish in colour, which is referred to as Jacquemier's sign, because of the increased vascularity and congestion with blood. It also becomes softer. When you perform a bimanual vaginal examination with two fingers inserted in the anterior fornix of the vagina, and the other hand placed behind the uterus abdominally, the fingers of both hands almost meet because of the softness of the isthmus. This is known as Hegar's sign. It is most marked between the 6th and 12th week of pregnancy. There is also an increased pulsation felt in the lateral fornices, which is known as Osiander's sign. The uterus is slightly enlarged.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="quickening">Quickening<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#quickening" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This is when a mother feels the first movements. Primigravidae recognise these movements at about the 20th week, and the multipara at about the 16th week.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="uterus-enlargement">Uterus Enlargement<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#uterus-enlargement" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This occurs around the 12th week. You should be able to feel the enlarged uterus abdominally just above the symphysis pubis. The fundus reaches the level of the umbilicus at about the 22nd week, and the xiphisternum by the 36th week.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="foetal-identification">Foetal Identification<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#foetal-identification" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>You should be able to feel the foetal parts, for example, the head, from the 24th week onwards. You might be able to feel foetal movements while palpating the fundus. Foetal heart sounds can be heard around the 24th week. The rate varies from 120 to 160 beats per minute.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="investigations">Investigations<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#investigations" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Now that you are aware of the symptoms and signs of pregnancy, you will look at the investigations that may be carried out to confirm pregnancy.</p>
<p>When diagnosing pregnancy, it is not always necessary that you carry out investigations. However, it is important for you to know the kind of investigations that may be done.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="pregnancy-test">Pregnancy Test<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#pregnancy-test" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This depends on the Human Chorionic Gonadotrophins (HCG) secreted in the mother's urine. It is more likely to be certain when the urine is concentrated, that is, a fresh, clean, early morning specimen. With a sensitive test, HCG can be detected in maternal plasma or urine by eight to nine days after ovulation.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="the-ultrasound">The Ultrasound<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-ultrasound" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This is a scanner, which enables you to see the foetus and its cardiac activity, particularly if performed by the 6th week of pregnancy.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="x-ray-method">X-ray Method<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#x-ray-method" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>By the 16th week, if an x-ray is performed, it will show foetal bones. This is an undesirable method of diagnosing pregnancy because in early or mid-pregnancy, the foetus is very susceptible to the adverse effects of radiation.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="routine-management-of-the-pregnant-woman">Routine Management of the Pregnant Woman<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#routine-management-of-the-pregnant-woman" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>In unit one of this module you were introduced to the Safe Motherhood Initiative (SMI) and child survival. You learnt that one of the pillars of safe motherhood and child survival was antenatal care. In this section you are going to study antenatal care in detail.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="the-aims-of-antenatal-care">The Aims of Antenatal Care<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-aims-of-antenatal-care" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="did-you-think-of-this">Did you think of this?<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#did-you-think-of-this" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>Antenatal care is the care you give to a pregnant woman to ensure that she reaches the end of her pregnancy both physically and psychologically healthy and that she delivers a healthy baby.</p>
<p>Good quality antenatal care always includes:</p>
<ul>
<li>Diagnosing the pregnancy before 24 weeks</li>
<li>Regular blood pressure checks, testing for oedema, and urine examination</li>
<li>Regular abdominal examination</li>
<li>The recognition of high risk cases and their referral for special care</li>
<li>Immunisation against tetanus to both the mother and unborn baby</li>
<li>Treating of minor complications</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="remember">Remember:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#remember" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Good antenatal care must include the development of a relationship of trust between the health worker, the pregnant woman and, if possible, her family.</p>
<p>In order for you to provide good quality antenatal care, you must achieve the following aims:</p>
<ul>
<li>Make the mother aware of the benefits offered by your clinic both during pregnancy, at the time of delivery, and afterwards.</li>
<li>Advise the mother on how to look after herself and her baby. You should also advise on a balanced diet, and activities likely to improve and maintain her health and that of her baby.</li>
<li>Prepare her psychologically and physically for delivery. Do this by allaying her fears and counselling on harmful beliefs and practices.</li>
<li>Give prophylactic treatment against anaemia and vaccination against neonatal tetanus.</li>
<li>Identify those 'at risk', that is, women who might have problems during pregnancy or labour, and correct the abnormalities or refer to hospital for management of the pregnancy or delivery.</li>
<li>Help a mother to make an Individual Birth Plan (IBP).</li>
<li>Help the mother prepare for, and learn how to take good care of the child physically, psychologically and socially.</li>
</ul>
<p>You will now look at factors that influence the maternal and perinatal mortality rates.</p>
<p>Maternal mortality refers to the death of the mother during pregnancy or within 42 days after the termination of pregnancy. The cause may be related to or aggravated by pregnancy, labour or puerperium. Data from the 2003 Kenya Demographic Health Survey (KDHS) estimated maternal mortality ratio at 414 maternal deaths per 100,000 live births.</p>
<p>Perinatal mortality includes stillbirths, which is the death of the foetus after 28 weeks and neonatal deaths, that is, death during the first week of life. According to the 2003 KDHS, the</p>
<p>results indicate that perinatal mortality rate is 40 deaths per 1,000 pregnancies.</p>
<p>A successful outcome of the pregnancy depends on the following two general factors:</p>
<ul>
<li>The reproductive efficiency of the mother</li>
<li>The standard of obstetric care</li>
</ul>
<p>The reproductive efficiency of the woman, in turn, depends on the following factors:</p>
<ul>
<li>The age of the mother</li>
<li>Her parity</li>
<li>Her physique and stature</li>
<li>Her general state of health</li>
</ul>
<p>You will now cover each factor separately, starting with the age of the mother.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="age-of-mother">Age of Mother<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#age-of-mother" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The optimum age for child bearing is between the age of 18 and 35 years. Pregnancy occurring below 18 years of age leads to a high incidence of prematurity and perinatal mortality rate. Since adolescent girls have not yet grown to their full stature, obstructed labour with all its disastrous results is very common.</p>
<p>After the age of 35, babies are usually considerably large, and the pelvic joints stiffer, thus they do not give room easily. Uterine dysfunction with slow dilatation of the cervix is common. Childbearing towards the end of the woman's reproductive life carries additional risk. Hypertension, other vascular diseases, and renal degenerative conditions begin to appear, leading to placental insufficiency.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="parity">Parity<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#parity" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The risks in pregnancy for the mother and child are relatively high in the first pregnancy. They then drop sharply in the second and third and then slowly rise in subsequent pregnancies. Therefore, by the sixth pregnancy the risks to this multipara exceed those of the primigravida and thereafter rise more steeply with each pregnancy.</p>
<p>A grand multipara is likely to get complications in pregnancy and labour.</p>
<p>Iron deficiency anaemia, which occurs because pregnancy and lactation deplete the woman's iron stores.</p>
<p>Malpresentation because of over relaxed abdominal muscles, that is, muscles that are not firm.</p>
<p>Uterine rupture due to obstructed labour and malpresentation.</p>
<p>Postpartum haemorrhage, which occurs because the uterus has fibrous tissues and is overstretched due to repeated pregnancies. This causes uterine atony. This occurs when the uterine muscle has lost the tone.</p>
<p>Cephalo-pelvic disproportion, that is, babies tend to be larger with each succeeding pregnancy. Disproportion may, therefore, occur after many subsequent pregnancies.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="remember">Remember:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#remember" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>As a health worker you should not unduly rely on the security of a good past obstetric history. If you do, you may easily overlook signs of trouble in pregnancy or labour in a multipara.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="physique-and-stature">Physique and Stature<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#physique-and-stature" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The maternal height is a very valuable measure of reproductive efficiency. In any community, the shortest height group has the highest perinatal mortality and the highest prematurity rate. Women below 150cms by height are at risk of cephalo-pelvic disproportion. On the other hand, women who are obese are more at risk of getting pre-eclampsia.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="maternal-health">Maternal Health<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#maternal-health" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The reproductive efficiency of the woman also depends on the woman's general state of health. If she starts off her pregnancy in poor health, she is likely to deteriorate throughout her pregnancy.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="factors-that-make-a-woman-more-vulnerable-to-a-difficult-pregnancy">Factors that make a woman more vulnerable to a difficult pregnancy<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#factors-that-make-a-woman-more-vulnerable-to-a-difficult-pregnancy" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Low social status, which means the woman, may not be able to afford good nutrition or medical care. She, at the same time, is likely to live in a poor environment, conducive to infections.</li>
<li>A large family to take care of, which is likely to increase stress and predispose her to other diseases such as high blood pressure.</li>
<li>Poor nutrition because of frequent and closely spaced pregnancies, when a woman's body is depleted of nutrients. This is known as maternal depletion syndrome. Also, because of certain beliefs and taboos, some women regard some foods such as eggs as harmful to them during pregnancy.</li>
</ul>
<p>Bishop's Computers

Bishop's Computers</p>
<ul>
<li>Infectious diseases, because immunity to infection is weakened during pregnancy. Infections such as AIDS, malaria, amoebic dysentery, and infective hepatitis, are particularly severe during pregnancy.</li>
<li>Anaemia, which is caused by a number of factors such as hookworm infestation, is common in rural areas, especially where there are no latrines. You will learn more about anaemia in pregnancy in section two of this unit.</li>
<li>Many women still deliver without the care of a skilled attendant. People who are unskilled and untrained still deliver many women in unhygienic conditions. There is a high risk of rupture of uterus and other complications.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="antenatal-care">Antenatal Care<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#antenatal-care" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="activities-at-the-first-antenatal-visit">Activities at the First Antenatal Visit<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#activities-at-the-first-antenatal-visit" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>The first antenatal visit should take place as early as possible, preferably in the first trimester. It is an opportunity to collect baseline data about the mother, for example, weight, blood pressure and so on. This baseline data is important because it forms a basis for comparison during later visits. Also, during the first visit, any other abnormalities can be detected and treated before they have a detrimental effect.</p>
<p>The activities of the first visit include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Registration</li>
<li>Height and weight measurement</li>
<li>History taking</li>
<li>Physical examination</li>
<li>Laboratory examination</li>
<li>Management of complaints</li>
<li>Immunisation</li>
<li>Health Education</li>
</ul>
<p>You will now review each of these activities in a bit more detail.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="registration">Registration<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#registration" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>Every pregnant woman that comes to the antenatal clinic should be registered and given an antenatal card.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="weight-and-height-measurement">Weight and Height Measurement<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#weight-and-height-measurement" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>Measure height and weight and record them on the card. A woman with a height below 150cms is likely to have cephalopelvic disproportion. Excessive weight gain, that is, more than 1kg per week indicates accumulation of fluid in the tissues (oedema). A lack of increase in weight or weight loss may mean malnutrition.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="history-taking">History Taking<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#history-taking" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>When you take the history you should include the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>Particulars of the mother, which include the name, age, marital status, religion, and address.</li>
<li>History of the present pregnancy, which includes the date of the first day of the last normal menstrual period, the parity and gravidity. From this, calculate and record the Expected Date of Delivery (EDD) by adding seven days to the first day of the last normal menstrual period and subtracting three months from that month. For example, if her last period started on 18th June, 18 plus 7 gives you 25. Then June minus three months, (6-3) will give March. So the EDD is March 25th of the following year.</li>
<li>Gynaecological history, which will include the menstrual history, any past operations for gynaecological complaints and Sexually Transmitted Infections (STIs).</li>
<li>Past obstetric history, for which you should ask the mother about the outcomes or complications of each of the previous pregnancies. Inquire into whether she delivered at home or at a hospital. Ask for the birth weights of the children, whether they were breastfed and whether they are alive or not.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="general-medical-history">General Medical History<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#general-medical-history" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Ask about any serious medical diseases likely to affect pregnancy and labour such as heart diseases, diabetes mellitus, renal diseases, tuberculosis and sickle cell disease.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="surgical-history">Surgical History<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#surgical-history" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Find out about injuries to the pelvis and spine, operations on the abdomen and blood transfusion.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="physical-examination">Physical Examination<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#physical-examination" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>A thorough examination is made at the first visit. This is divided into a general examination and an obstetric examination. The general examination should include:</p>
<ul>
<li>General appearance, namely the nutritional status, whether weak or sick</li>
</ul>

<p>looking. Note the gait and if she walks with a limp.</p>

<li>Height, noting that any woman below 150cms in height is likely to have cephalopelvic disproportion.</li>
<li>Weight, that is, a pregnant woman on average gains 2kg every month. The total weight gain by the end of pregnancy is about 10-20kg. Excessive weight gain indicates accumulation of fluid in the tissues (oedema). Lack of increase in weight or loss may mean malnutrition. <div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="breast-examination">Breast Examination<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#breast-examination" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Palpate gently with the flat of the hand to feel for any lumps. Check if the nipple is protractile. Educate the woman on how to examine the breasts by herself.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="obstetric-examination-abdominal">Obstetric Examination (Abdominal)<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#obstetric-examination-abdominal" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>An obstetric examination aims at:</p>
<ul>
<li>Observing for signs of pregnancy</li>
<li>Assessing foetal size and growth</li>
<li>Assessing foetal health</li>
<li>Diagnosing the location of foetal parts</li>
<li>Detecting any deviation from normal</li>
</ul>
<p>The client is asked to empty her bladder and then to lie on her back on a couch with the arms by her sides. Inspect the abdomen for the shape, size, scars and foetal movements. Any abnormality detected is referred for further investigations.</p>
<p>Estimate the height of the fundus and compare it with the weeks of amenorrhoea. This helps you to assess foetal growth and detect any deviations from the normal. Check for varicosities at the back of the leg. Ask the woman to remove any tight clothing at the leg. At this time it is also important to check for phlebitis by looking for any reddened areas.</p>
<p>Palpate the sides of the abdomen to locate the foetal back in order to determine the position. You do this by facing the patient and then supporting the uterus with your right hand. Keep pushing the foetus with your left hand towards the right. Reverse the order to feel the left side of the uterus.</p>
<p>Palpate the lower pole of the uterus, just above the pelvic brim. Ask the mother to bend her knees slightly in order to relax the abdominal muscles and ask her to breathe steadily with her mouth open. Facing the foot of the bed, mark the brim of the pelvis with your hands and cup what presents between them. If the head is presenting, a smooth surface will be felt. If you do not find the head in the pelvic brim or at the fundus, suspect a transverse lie. This will be significant only after the 36th week of gestation.</p>
<p>The foetal heart sounds are listened to last, so as to assess the foetal wellbeing. The Pinard's foetal stethoscope is commonly used to hear the foetal heart.</p>
<p>As you perform the physical examination, talk to the mother and check her facial reaction as you palpate the abdomen. When you complete the procedure reassure the mother.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="findings-of-abdominal-examinations">Findings of Abdominal Examinations<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#findings-of-abdominal-examinations" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>All these are recorded upon completion of the exercise and they include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Gestational age, which is estimated as per the size of the fundus.</li>
<li>Lie, which refers to the relationship between the long axis of the foetus and that of the mother and can either be longitudinal, transverse or oblique.</li>
<li>Attitude, which refers to the relationship of the foetal head and limbs to its trunk. It is most commonly flexion.</li>
<li>Presentation, which means the foetal part that is lying at the pelvic brim or in the lower pole of the uterus and can either be vertex, breach, face, shoulder or brow. The most common is the vertex. Note that vertex/brow/face all refer to head presentation.</li>
<li>Denominator is the name given to the presenting part for example, occiput for vertex, sacrum for breech and mentum for face.</li>
<li>Position, which refers to the relationship between the denominator of the presentation and the landmarks of the pelvic brim.</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="breastfeeding">Breastfeeding<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#breastfeeding" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="the-advantages-of-breastfeeding">The Advantages of Breastfeeding<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-advantages-of-breastfeeding" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Breast milk is clean, while powdered milk usually gets contaminated by the water used to mix it or by being put into a dirty bottle. This may lead to diarrhoea and death.</li>
<li>Breast milk is a balanced food for the baby, while powdered milk often results in fat or thin babies.</li>
<li>Breast milk is always available while powdered milk is imported and expensive.</li>
</ul>
<p>Thus, breast milk is cheaper.</p>
<ul>
<li>Breast milk contains protective antibodies while powdered milk, if badly handled, will contain bacteria.</li>
<li>Breastfeeding brings close body contact between mother and baby. There is psychological satisfaction and the mother and baby develop a strong relationship, which leads to good social adjustment later in life. Tell the mother to put the baby to the breast immediately after birth.</li>
<li>Breast milk is at the right temperature all the time.</li>
<li>Breast milk contains a substance that promotes absorption of iron from the baby's gut.</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Follow-up Antenatal Visits</strong>
Can you think of the importance of follow-up visits?</p>
<p>The main aim of follow-up visits is to check the general health of the mother and to ascertain the rate of growth and welfare of the foetus. You would also check the lie and presentation of the foetus so as to determine the expected mode of delivery. It is also an opportunity to allay any anxiety or fears in the mother about the pregnancy or labour.</p>
<p><strong>Remember:</strong>
The wellbeing of the foetus is indicated by an increasing maternal weight that is proportional to the increasing uterine size and is compatible with gestational age, regular foetal movements that follow a regular pattern, and foeta heart rate of between 110-150 beats per minute.</p>
<p>During follow-up visits, your activities should include the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>A short history should be taken in which you ask how she has been, ask about her appetite, sleeping habits, vaginal discharge or bleeding, headaches which are persistent and swelling of the feet.</li>
<li>Estimate the fundal height; determine the foetal lie, position and presentation. Auscultate the foetal heart rate and note its rhythm.</li>
<li>Treat any minor ailments and refer major ones to hospital. Give ferrous sulphate and folic acid as prophylaxis for anaemia.</li>
<li>Give health information at every visit. Always give the woman information about her present condition and a return date for the next visit.</li>
<li>Give malaria prevention treatment, that is, sulphur based drugs, with the first dose during the second trimester and second dose during the third trimester.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Remember:</strong>
As you carry out the activities previously covered, you will also need to be able to identify women who fall in the high-risk and low-risk groups in your health facility.</p>
<p>You will now look at the low risk ailments or minor disorders of pregnancy. Major disorders of pregnancy will be covered in the next section.</p>
<p><strong>Low Risk Ailments (Minor Orders of Pregnancy)</strong></p>
<p>These remain minor as long as they do not pose much risk to the life of the mother and the baby. They are mainly caused by hormonal, accommodation, metabolic and/or postural changes. They include backache, constipation, abdominal cramps, heartburn and morning sickness.</p>
<p><strong>Remember:</strong>
Minor disorders may escalate and become serious complications of pregnancy. Therefore, as a health provider you have to be alert to the development of any complications and refer appropriately.</p>
<p>You will now cover the management of each minor disorder.</p>
<p><strong>Nausea and Vomiting (Morning Sickness)</strong></p>
<p>As mentioned in the previous section, this is mainly caused by a rise in hormonal levels. It occurs between four and sixteen weeks, usually in the morning hours, but it can occur at any time of the day. When vomiting becomes severe, it is called hyperemesis gravidarum. This condition will be discussed in the section on risk factors in pregnancy.</p>
<p>Advise the woman to take carbohydrate snacks at bedtime and in the morning before rising. At this point, it is important to rule out other causes of vomiting.</p>
<p><strong>Heartburn</strong></p>
<p>This refers to a burning sensation that is felt at the medistinal region due to reflux of gastric contents into the oesophagus through the cardiac sphincter that is relaxed by the increased levels of progesterone. It is prevalent between 30 to 40 weeks.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="obstetric-pharmacology">Obstetric Pharmacology<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#obstetric-pharmacology" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This is the study of the actions and effects of drugs on the foetus and the gravid uterus. Although the placenta forms a barrier between the foetal and maternal circulations, the drugs can still cross it. Drugs that are lipid, that is, fat soluble, can only cross if present in high concentrations. Water soluble drugs on the other hand, cross the placental barrier easily.</p>
<p>Once a drug has crossed to the foetus, it may persist there for a long time. This is due to the poor development of the mechanisms necessary to excrete the drugs.</p>
<p>The possible bad effects of drugs on the growing foetus are expressed by the term 'teratogenic', which means the making of monsters or abnormal children. Drugs with such properties are particularly dangerous soon after implantation when the majority of the foetal organs are being formed. This is the stage of organ formation or organogenesis. However, even later, the use of drugs carries risks as the central nervous system develops throughout pregnancy.</p>
<p>The high risk period, therefore, is the first 12 weeks of pregnancy, especially three to eight weeks from the first day of the last normal menstrual period.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="drugs-during-labour">Drugs During Labour<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#drugs-during-labour" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Any drug which acts to depress the respiratory system, such as those given to the mother to relieve pain, will make the newborn have difficulty in starting respiration. Barbiturates and anaesthetic agents may depress the newborn's respiration.</p>

<p>Drugs acting on the uterus are classified into two groups, namely uterine relaxants and uterine stimulants.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="progesteron">Progesteron<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#progesteron" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>These act in early pregnancy and are believed to make the uterus less irritable. They are sometimes used in the management of habitual or threatened abortion. As some of these agents cause masculinisation in female foetuses, they should be used with great caution. Never give these drugs yourself. Refer the patient to hospital.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="adrenoreceptor-stimulants">Adrenoreceptor Stimulants<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#adrenoreceptor-stimulants" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>These include isoxsuprine, salbutamol (ventolin) and orciprenaline. They are used in late pregnancy to prevent premature labour as they have a direct relaxant effect on the uterine muscle. However, they also have strong cardiovascular effects and their use on a large scale can be dangerous.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="uterine-stimulants">Uterine Stimulants<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#uterine-stimulants" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>These include prostaglandins, oxytocin and ergometrine.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="prostaglandins">Prostaglandins<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#prostaglandins" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>These are a group of naturally occurring agents, which, depending on the circumstances, can either contract or relax smooth muscle. They are produced by the seminal vesicles. Other sites of origin are the adrenals, kidney, liver, gut and nervous tissue.</p>
<p>Certain types of prostaglandins cause vigorous uterine contractions at any stage of pregnancy. They can be used to induce an abortion or to induce labour. They can be administered intravenously, orally, as vaginal pellets, or as an intrauterine injection. At present they are very expensive</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="oxytocin">Oxytocin<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#oxytocin" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This is produced by the posterior lobe of the pituitary gland. Syntocinon (pitocin) is a synthetic drug similar to oxytocin. Its dosage is expressed in units and it is usually given by an intravenous infusion so that its effects can be quickly discontinued if necessary. It is occasionally administered as a tablet (buccal pitocin). Syntocinon produces rhythmic contractions of the uterus with a relaxation phase in between, thus mimicking normal uterine activity in labour. Its uses include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Induction of labour</li>
<li>Sometimes treatment of uterine inertia</li>
<li>Management of some cases of postpartum haemorrhage</li>
<li>Management of inevitable abortions</li>
<li>Evacuation of molar pregnancy</li>
</ul>
<p>Dangers of oxytocin include foetal hypoxia and perinatal death as well as rupture of uterus.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="ergometrine">Ergometrine<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#ergometrine" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This is one of the ergot alkaloids, a naturally occurring group of substances prepared from a fungus. These substances have the ability to cause strong contractions of smooth muscle, including the uterine muscle. This will be covered further on the next page.</p>
<p>Unlike other ergot alkaloids, Ergometrine acts almost solely on the uterus. It produces a sustained tonic contraction of the uterus and its effects wear off after two to four hours. It is therefore unsuitable for inducing labour where slow, regular contractions with a good relaxation phase, like those produced by oxytocin are needed.</p>
<p>Ergometrine is the most important drug used to treat or prevent post partum haemorrhage. This is because of its ability to produce a sustained contraction, which shortens the muscle fibres so</p>
<p>Bishop's Computers
Page 70 of 317
Bishop's Computers</p>
<p>as to prevent bleeding from the placental site. When given intravenously it acts in 45 seconds, but when given intramuscularly, it has the disadvantage of being slow to act (seven minutes).</p>
<p>However, as pitocin acts more rapidly when given by intramuscular route (two minutes), the two drugs are usually given as a combination known as syntometrine which is five units of syntocin combined with 0.5mg of ergometrine. Syntometrine acts within two to three minutes when given intramuscularly.</p>
<p>Ergometrine can also cause peripheral vasoconstriction, hence the patient's blood pressure can increase after an injection of ergometrine, particularly after an intravenous injection.</p>
<p>To avoid toxic effects, 1.5mg is the maximum total dose of ergometrine maleate that can be given safely in 24 hours.</p>

<p>Ergometrine should be avoided in cases of moderate or severe hypertension UNLESS there is postpartum haemorrhage.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="introduction">Introduction<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#introduction" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>In the previous section you looked at the management of normal pregnancy and the management of minor disorders caused by pregnancy. In this section you will look at the high risk factors in pregnancy including the medical conditions that affect pregnancy. The types of women in this group are those who are at higher risk of having complications either during pregnancy or labour. This may be because in their past deliveries they had problems like postpartum haemorrhage, which are likely to recur, or they have chronic diseases such as diabetes which are likely to get worse with pregnancy.</p>
<p>There are also those who are at risk in this particular pregnancy because of a problem such as severe anaemia, which has just developed. Note that these are the major causes of both maternal and foetal morbidity and mortality. Thus, early diagnosis and proper management is essential for a successful pregnancy and delivery.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="risk-factors-conditions-in-pregnancy">Risk Factors/Conditions in Pregnancy<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#risk-factors-conditions-in-pregnancy" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>You will start with some of the more common conditions during pregnancy that may cause risk to the pregnant woman and/or the foetus.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="hyperemesis-gravidarum">Hyperemesis Gravidarum<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#hyperemesis-gravidarum" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>This is a condition where vomiting is severe and continuous throughout the day. The woman vomits everything she has eaten. This usually leads to severe dehydration and ketoacidosis. She becomes malnourished. If treatment is not started quickly, liver and kidney damage may result. Anaemia may develop as a result of lack of vitamin B, folic acid and iron.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="can-you-think-of-three-conditions-that-are-associated-with-hyperemesis-gravidarum">Can you think of three conditions that are associated with hyperemesis gravidarum?<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#can-you-think-of-three-conditions-that-are-associated-with-hyperemesis-gravidarum" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="did-you-think-of-these">Did you think of these?<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#did-you-think-of-these" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>Hyperemesis gravidarum occurs in very few women. It is usually associated with multiple pregnancies, hydatidiform mole and/or a history of habitual abortions.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="management-of-hyperemesis-gravidarum">Management of Hyperemesis Gravidarum<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#management-of-hyperemesis-gravidarum" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>If in a health centre or dispensary, the patient should be referred to a hospital as soon as the diagnosis is made. In the hospital, the following should be done:</p>
<ul>
<li>Intravenous infusion of five percent dextrose alternating with normal saline will be given to correct the dehydration.</li>
<li>Anti emetics like promethazine hydrochloride (phenergan) or metoclopramide hydrochloride (plasil) are given usually parenterally to control the vomiting.</li>
<li>Multivitamin supplements are given.</li>
<li>The patient is reassured and her visitors restricted.</li>
<li>Routine nursing care and observations of vital signs are maintained twice daily or as necessary.</li>
</ul>

Bishop's Computers</p>
<p>The patient should be discharged at least two to three days after vomiting has ceased. The case should be followed up in the antenatal clinic.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="polyhydramnios">Polyhydramnios<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#polyhydramnios" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This is a condition in which the quantity of amniotic fluid exceeds 1500mls. It may not become apparent until it reaches 3000mls. It is a fairly rare condition.</p>
<p>Polyhydramnios is associated with the following conditions:</p>
<ul>
<li>Oesophageal atresia</li>
<li>Open neural tube defect</li>
<li>Multiple pregnancy, especially in monozygotic twins</li>
<li>Maternal diabetes mellitus</li>
<li>Rarely in rhesus isoimmunisation</li>
<li>Severe foetal abnormalities</li>
</ul>
<p>There are two types of polyhydramnios: chronic and acute.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="chronic-polyhydramnios">Chronic Polyhydramnios<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#chronic-polyhydramnios" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This occurs gradually, usually from about the 30th week of pregnancy.</p>
<p>It is the most common type.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="acute-polyhydramnios">Acute Polyhydramnios<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#acute-polyhydramnios" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This is a rare type, which occurs at about 20 weeks and comes on very suddenly. The uterus reaches the xiphisternum in about three to four days. It is associated with monozygotic twins or severe foetal abnormality.</p>
<p>Polyhydramnios can be recognised in the following ways:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>The mother may complain of breathlessness and discomfort.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>If the condition is acute in onset, she may complain of severe abdominal pain.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The condition may aggravate other symptoms associated with pregnancy such as indigestion, heartburn, constipation, oedema, varicose veins of the vulva and lower limbs.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>On abdominal inspection, the uterus is larger than expected for the period of gestation and is globular in shape. The abdominal skin appears stretched and tight with marked striae gravidurum and marked superficial blood vessels.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>On palpation, the uterus is tense and it is difficult to feel foetal parts.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The abdominal girth is much more than expected for the period of gestation.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Auscultation of the foetal heart is difficult because of the free movement of the foetus.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Where possible an ultrasonic scan should be done to confirm the diagnosis. It may also reveal multiple pregnancy or foetal abnormality if these are present</p>
</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="management-of-polyhydramnios">Management of Polyhydramnios<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#management-of-polyhydramnios" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The mother is admitted to hospital and, where possible, the cause of the condition is determined. The subsequent care will be determined by the condition of the mother, the cause and the period of gestation.</p>
<p>If there is foetal abnormality, the method and timing of delivery will depend on the severity. If there is gross abnormality, induction should be started. The nursing care should include rest in bed in sitting position to relieve dyspnoea. Assist the patient with personal hygiene and routine prenatal observations.</p>
<p>If abdominal discomfort is severe, abdominal amniocentesis may be considered. If it is done, infection prevention measures must be observed and only 500ml should be withdrawn at a time. Labour may be induced in the case of late pregnancy. Before the membranes are ruptured, the lie must be determined and the membranes ruptured cautiously allowing the fluid to flow slowly. This is to avoid cord prolapse, alteration of the lie and abruptio placenta which may occur after sudden reduction of uterine size.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="complications-of-polyhydramnios">Complications of Polyhydramnios<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#complications-of-polyhydramnios" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>There are several complications associated with polyhydramnios. These include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Increased foetal mobility leading to unstable lie and malpresentation</li>
<li>Cord presentation and cord prolapse</li>
<li>Premature rupture of the membranes</li>
<li>Placenta abruptio when the membranes rupture</li>
<li>Premature labour</li>
<li>Postpartum haemorrhage</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="oligohydramnios">Oligohydramnios<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#oligohydramnios" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>In this condition there is an abnormally small amount of amniotic fluid. It may be 300 to 500ml at term but amounts vary and it may be much less. It is associated with absence of kidneys or</p>
<p>Bishop's Computers
Bishop's Computers
Page 72 of 317</p>
<p>Potter's syndrome in which the foetus has pulmonary hypoplasia.</p>
<p>The lack of amniotic fluid reduces intrauterine space and causes deformities of the foetus due to compression. The baby's skin is dry and leathery in appearance and the nose may be flat. It may have talipes and a squashed-looking face.</p>
<p>The following characteristics will help you recognise the presence of oligohydramnios:</p>
<ul>
<li>The uterus is smaller than expected for the period of gestation</li>
<li>The mother notices reduced foetal movements if she has had a previous normal pregnancy</li>
<li>On palpation the foetal parts are easily felt and the uterus is small and compact</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="management">Management<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#management" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The woman should be admitted for investigations, usually in the form of an ultrasound scan. If there are no foetal abnormalities, the pregnancy will be allowed to continue. Labour may be induced early to avoid placental insufficiency.</p>
<p>Analgesics are given during labour because the contractions are usually very painful. However, be aware that impaired circulation may cause foetal hypoxia. After delivery the baby is examined carefully for abnormalities.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="bleeding-in-late-pregnancy-antepartum-haemorrhage">Bleeding in Late Pregnancy (Antepartum Haemorrhage)<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#bleeding-in-late-pregnancy-antepartum-haemorrhage" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Bleeding in late pregnancy refers to any bleeding from the genital tract from the 28th week of gestation and before the birth of the baby. It is usually known as antepartum haemorrhage.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="remember">Remember:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#remember" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Never perform a vaginal examination on a woman with antepartum haemorrhage. This may lead to severe bleeding which can be fatal.</p>
<p>The two most important causes of bleeding in late pregnancy are placenta praevia and abruptio placentae. You will now look at each of these separately.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="placenta-praevia">Placenta Praevia<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#placenta-praevia" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This is bleeding from a partially separated placenta, which is wholly or partially situated in the lower uterine segment. It might be covering either part or the entire internal os.</p>
<p>It is more likely to occur with increasing maternal age. It is more common in women aged 35 and above. It is also associated with increasing parity, and is twice as common in multigravida as in primigravida.</p>
<p>Placenta praevia is divided into four types or degrees.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="type-1">Type 1<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#type-1" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The placenta lies in the upper segment and only the lower margin dips into the lower uterine segment.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="type-ii">Type II<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#type-ii" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The placenta is partially situated in the lower uterine segment with the lower margin of the placenta reaching the edge of the internal os but does not cover it. It is known as marginal placenta praevia.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="type-iii">Type III<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#type-iii" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The placenta covers the internal os when closed up to three to four centimetres dilatation. This is known as partial or incomplete placenta praevia.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="type-iv">Type IV<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#type-iv" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The placenta lies centrally over the internal os and covers the os even when the cervix is fully dilated.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="signs-and-symptoms-of-placenta-praevia">Signs and Symptoms of Placenta Praevia<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#signs-and-symptoms-of-placenta-praevia" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>The signs and symptoms of placenta praevia include painless vaginal bleeding which starts when at rest or sleeping. It starts suddenly, usually from the 32nd week of gestation because of Braxton Hicks contractions.</p>
<p>Additionally, because the placenta occupies the lower uterine segment, the foetal head remains high, which results in malpresentation and unstable lie. If bleeding is severe, the blood pressure is low, the pulse and respirations are high, and there is shock corresponding with the amount of bleeding.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="the-management-of-placenta-praevia">The Management of Placenta Praevia<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-management-of-placenta-praevia" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>If in a health centre or dispensary, refer all pregnant women with vaginal bleeding to hospital. Ensure there is a running intravenous drip of saline or dextrose before transferring the patient to the hospital. A nurse should always accompany the patient to the hospital.</p>
<p>In the hospital the type of management will depend on the amount of blood loss, the condition of the mother and foetus, the location of the placenta and the gestation period.</p>
<p>The aim of management is to control haemorrhage and to try to conserve the pregnancy up to 38 weeks gestation when the foetus is mature.</p>
<p>Where there is slight vaginal bleeding, conservative treatment is started if the pregnancy has not reached 38 weeks of gestation. The patient is admitted for complete bed rest and total care.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="measures-to-be-taken-in-the-case-of-placenta-praevia">Measures to be Taken in the Case of Placenta Praevia<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#measures-to-be-taken-in-the-case-of-placenta-praevia" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Blood is taken for HB, grouping and cross matching</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>She is put on mild sedation like phenobarbitone</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>No abdominal palpation is done as it may trigger severe bleeding</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Save all pads to assess blood loss</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Give high protein diet</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Take two hourly vital signs</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>On the third day speculum examination is done to exclude incidental haemorrhage</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>At 34 weeks scanning is carried out to assess progress and to confirm diagnosis</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The patient should be retained in hospital until the 37th week when Examination Under Anaesthesia (EUA) is done in theatre ready for caesarean section in case of severe bleeding</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>In placenta praevia type one and two and if placenta is anterior, the membranes are ruptured and spontaneous delivery awaited. Labour is induced with oxytocin drug</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>In placenta praevia type two with placenta posteriorly situated and in type three and four, caesarean section is performed</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>In the case of moderate to severe vaginal bleeding, you should set up intravenous infusion and prepare for immediate caesarean section. Blood for HB grouping and cross matching should be taken, and physical and psychological preparation of the mother is done.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="complications-of-placenta-praevia">Complications of Placenta Praevia<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#complications-of-placenta-praevia" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>The following are some complications of placenta praevia:</p>
<ul>
<li>Post partum haemorrhage</li>
<li>Foetal hypoxia</li>
<li>Puerperal sepsis</li>
<li>Anaemia</li>
<li>Maternal and foetal death</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="abruptio-placentae-accidental-haemorrhage">Abruptio Placentae (Accidental Haemorrhage)<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#abruptio-placentae-accidental-haemorrhage" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>This is bleeding from premature separation of a normally situated placenta occurring after the 28th week of gestation. It is associated with the following conditions:</p>
<ul>
<li>Hypertensive conditions and pre-eclampsia</li>
<li>High parity</li>
<li>Trauma</li>
<li>Sudden release of polyhydramnious</li>
<li>High fever</li>
<li>Traction of abnormally short umbilical cord during labour</li>
<li>External cephalic version</li>
<li>Fright or sudden shock, for example, bad news</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="types-of-abruptio-placentae">Types of Abruptio Placentae<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#types-of-abruptio-placentae" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>There are three clinical presentations.</p>
<p>Mixed or combined, where bleeding is partly revealed and partly concealed.</p>
<p>Concealed, where the blood is trapped between the placenta, membranes and the uterine wall. There is no visible bleeding.</p>
<p>External or revealed, which is where there is free (visible) vaginal haemorrhage.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="signs-and-symptoms-of-abruptio-placentae">Signs and Symptoms of Abruptio Placentae<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#signs-and-symptoms-of-abruptio-placentae" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>In the revealed type, there is slight to severe vaginal bleeding. On abdominal palpation there may or may not be pain and tenderness. The pulse is raised and there is low blood pressure or hypertension.</p>
<p>In the concealed type, there is severe abdominal pain and the patient is in shock. There is no vaginal bleeding and the uterus is very tender and is board like. The foetal parts cannot be palpated and there are no foetal heart sounds. The pulse is raised and there may be oliguria and proteinuria.</p>
<p>In the combined type, the patient has both features of revealed and concealed bleeding. The degree of shock is higher than the visible blood loss. The uterus is tender and rigid and pain is constant.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="management-of-abruptio-placentae">Management of Abruptio Placentae<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#management-of-abruptio-placentae" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Refer the patient to hospital if in a health centre or dispensary. She should be started on intravenous drip. Inform the hospital if possible. Take a specimen of blood for grouping cross matching before starting the drip and take it with the patient when she is transferred. In the hospital, admit the patient and call the doctor immediately. Give emotional support and physical care. Relieve pain with IM morphine 15mg or pethidine 100mg. If there is severe bleeding treat for shock and prepare for caesarean section.</p>
<p>Take vital signs, such as blood pressure and pulse, quarter to half hourly and temperature four hourly. Raise the foot of the bed to prevent vena cava occlusion by gravid uterus. Maintain a urine output chart and test urine for protein. You should also test blood for coagulation defects and take clotting time at intervals for monitoring. Prepare for reception and resuscitation of the baby. Usually, the baby is still born. A blood transfusion should be given where necessary.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="conservative-management">Conservative Management<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#conservative-management" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>This occurs in cases of mild separation of the placenta. When the mother and baby are in good condition, intra-uterine scanning is done to assess the degree of haemorrhage and continuous foetal monitoring is done to assess foetal condition.</p>
<p>If both mother and baby are well and gestation is under 37 weeks, she may be discharged and seen weekly at the antenatal clinic.</p>
<p>At 37 weeks gestation you should readmit the mother for induction. The membranes should be ruptured and she should be started on oxytocin drip and monitored half hourly for onset of labour. In case of foetal distress, Caesarean section should be performed.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="complications-of-abruptio-placentae">Complications of Abruptio Placentae<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#complications-of-abruptio-placentae" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>These can be very serious and include the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>Failure of blood clotting mechanisms, leading to excessive haemorrhage in concealed bleeding</li>
<li>Renal failure or hypovoleamia</li>
<li>Puerperal sepsis</li>
<li>Anaemia</li>
<li>Maternal death</li>
<li>Foetal death</li>
<li>Anterior pituitary gland necrosis. Thrombosis of pituitary gland may occur in severe bleeding and if the mother stays in shock for long</li>
</ul>
<p>Other causes of antepartum haemorrhage include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Rupture of small vessels at the edge of the placenta</li>
<li>Cervical erosion</li>
<li>Cancer of cervix</li>
<li>Severe cervicitis</li>
<li>Infected cervical polyp</li>
</ul>
<p>For any of these conditions, refer the patient to the hospital for management. You will learn their specific management in unit three.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="pre-eclampsia">Pre-eclampsia<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#pre-eclampsia" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="can-you-think-of-five-conditions-that-may-predispose-a-mother-to-pre-eclampsia">Can you think of five conditions that may predispose a mother to pre-eclampsia?<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#can-you-think-of-five-conditions-that-may-predispose-a-mother-to-pre-eclampsia" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>Pre-eclampsia is a condition peculiar to pregnancy and occurring usually after the 28th week of gestation. It is characterised by the presence of hypertension, oedema and proteinuria or any two of the three. It is more common in the following conditions:</p>
<ul>
<li>Primigravida, especially the too young or over 35 years</li>
<li>Multiple pregnancy</li>
<li>Diabetes mellitus</li>
<li>Hydatidiform mole</li>
<li>Essential hypertension</li>
<li>Polyhydramnios</li>
<li>Mothers with past history of pre-eclampsia</li>
<li>Obese mothers</li>
</ul>
<p>As you can see, it is important to take a good history as it will enable you to detect this condition early.</p>
<p>The cause of pre-eclampsia is not known, but there are various theories relating to its possible causes, which are endocrine in nature, metabolic or immunological.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="diagnosis-of-pre-eclampsia">Diagnosis of Pre-eclampsia<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#diagnosis-of-pre-eclampsia" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The diagnosis of pre-eclampsia is not easy since the mother has no obvious complaints. There are three cardinal signs.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="hypertension">Hypertension<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#hypertension" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>There is a rise in diastolic pressure of 15 to 20mm/hg above the mother's normal diastolic pressure, or an increase above 80 to 90mm/hg on two occasions. A marked increase in systolic pressure above that expected for the mother's age is important to note, for example, 140 to 170 where the mother's normal pressure is between 90/60 and 120/70mm/hg.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="proteinuria">Proteinuria<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#proteinuria" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This is important in the absence of urinary tract infection. It may be detected in testing a midstream specimen of urine, which should be followed by laboratory investigation. The amount of protein in the urine indicates the severity of pre-eclampsia.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="oedema">Oedema<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#oedema" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Oedema of ankles is common in late pregnancy but it disappears overnight. This is known as physiological oedema. Any generalised oedema is significant and occult oedema is suspected in cases of excess weight gain above what is expected for the gestation. Clinical oedema may be mild or severe. The oedema puts on pressure and is found in the following areas:</p>
<p>Feet, ankles, and pretibial region; lower abdomen; vulva, which is uncomfortable and distressing to the mother; sacral area in a mother confined to bed; facial puffiness of the face and eye lids, fingers.</p>
<p>Pre-eclampsia can be classified as mild, moderate or severe.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="mild-pre-eclampsia">Mild Pre-eclampsia<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#mild-pre-eclampsia" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>This is detected when, after resting, the diastolic pressure is 15 to 20mm/hg above the basal blood pressure recorded in early pregnancy or a diastolic above 80 to 90mm/hg, for example, BP 130/80 to 140/90. Oedema of feet, ankles and pretibial region may also be present.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="moderate-pre-eclampsia">Moderate Pre-eclampsia<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#moderate-pre-eclampsia" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>This is diagnosed when there is marked rise in both systolic and diastolic pressure - 140/100 to 160/100mm/hg, proteinuria of 0.5gm/litre with no evidence of urinary tract infection and generalised oedema.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="severe-pre-eclampsia">Severe Pre-eclampsia<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#severe-pre-eclampsia" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Symptoms include the blood pressure exceeding 160/110mm/hg and an increased proteinuria over 1gm/litre. There may be marked generalised oedema, frontal headache and visual disturbances.</p>
<p>The effects of severe pre-eclampsia on the mother include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Abruptio placenta</li>
<li>Condition may worsen, leading to eclampsia</li>
<li>The kidneys, lungs, heart and liver may be seriously damaged due to haematological disturbance</li>
<li>The capillaries within the fundus of the eye may be irreparably damaged causing blindness</li>
</ul>
<p>The effects on the foetus are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Low birth weight due to reduced placental function</li>
<li>Increased incidence of hypoxia during prenatal and intranatal periods</li>
<li>Placenta abruptio leading to hypoxia and later death</li>
<li>Prematurity if the baby is delivered early delivery due to placenta abruptio or worsening of the condition</li>
</ul>
<p>The midwife plays an important role in detecting pre-eclampsia. There should always be vigilant antenatal care to enable early detection and management.</p>
<p>You should also ensure a thorough history taking to detect the mothers at risk early in pregnancy.</p>
<p>Follow up should include monitoring of weight and blood pressure and urine testing on every subsequent visit.</p>
<p>Factors that may predispose the patient to pre-eclampsia, such as multiple pregnancies and obesity should be noted early.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="management-of-pre-eclampsia">Management of Pre-eclampsia<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#management-of-pre-eclampsia" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>The method of management will depend on the severity of the condition. The main principles of care are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Provide adequate rest and monitoring of observations to avoid eclampsia</li>
<li>Prolong the pregnancy until the baby is mature enough to survive</li>
<li>Safeguard the life of the mother</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="mild-pre-eclampsia-management">Mild Pre-eclampsia Management<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#mild-pre-eclampsia-management" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>The mother should be advised on bed rest at home and she is seen weekly to assess her condition. She should be given anti-hypertensive drugs such as aldomet 500mg. Sedatives such as valium or phenobarbitone should also be administered to help her rest and she should be advised to report to the hospital in case of any problem.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="moderate-pre-eclampsia-management">Moderate Pre-eclampsia Management<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#moderate-pre-eclampsia-management" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>The patient should be admitted to hospital for bed rest. She should only be allowed to go to the toilet. She should be nursed in sitting position or lying on the side to encourage uterine blood flow. Bed rest will reduce oedema by improving the renal circulation, facilitating kidney filtration and producing diuresis. It also lowers the blood pressure.</p>
<p>The patient's diet should be rich in protein, fibre and vitamins and low in carbohydrates and salt. Her weight should be recorded twice a week. Observe for oedema daily. Urine should be tested for protein and Ketones. Esbach setting is done daily to assess level of protein loss. Twenty four hour urine collection should be done to estimate oestriol level as an indication of placental function.</p>
<p>Fluid intake and output should be maintained strictly to monitor renal function.</p>
<p>Sedatives, such as phenobarbitone, may be given to ensure rest and sleep. Give antihypertensive drugs like aldomet to lower blood pressure. Take vital signs: blood pressure, temperature, pulse and respirations four hourly.</p>
<p>The foetal heart rate should be taken four hourly or two times daily, depending on the condition. A kick chart to monitor foetal movement should also be kept.</p>
<p>When the mother's condition improves, she can be discharged to attend clinic weekly until she goes into spontaneous labour. Otherwise, she</p>
<p>should be admitted at 38 weeks for induction of labour. If, in spite of the above care, the condition does not improve, caesarean section should be performed.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="management-of-labour">Management of Labour<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#management-of-labour" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Remain with the mother throughout labour. Maintain close vigilant observations of:</p>
<ul>
<li>Presence of oedema</li>
<li>Urinary output</li>
<li>Urinalysis results</li>
<li>Blood pressure</li>
</ul>
<p>Report any deviations to the doctor immediately. Take vital signs as follows: blood pressure and pulse rate half hourly, temperature four hourly, unless otherwise indicated.</p>
<p>Perform abdominal examination and observe for contractions and foetal heart rate half hourly. At the same time, observe for signs of second stage of labour and immediately alert both the obstetrician and paediatrician.</p>
<p>After delivery, monitor blood pressure four hourly for 24 hours. Urinalysis should be done twice a day. You should maintain a urinary output chart and continue with antihypertensive drugs and normal postnatal or caesarean section care.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="active-management-of-severe-pre-eclampsia">Active Management of Severe Pre-eclampsia<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#active-management-of-severe-pre-eclampsia" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Admit the mother in a quiet, dimly lit room on complete bedrest. In the room there should be an emergency tray and an epileptic tray in case of a fit. The aim of care is to prevent convulsions and control hypertension to prevent death of the mother and foetus.</p>
<p>Place the mother on lateral position to improve foetal circulation and to prevent vena cavae compression by the uterus. Remain with the mother and maintain vigilant observations. On admission you should take all observations and note them on a chart and continue half hourly or as prescribed by the doctor.</p>
<p>The doctor will prescribe antihypertensive drugs like hydrolysing 5-10mg which is administered slowly and blood pressure monitored every five minutes until it stabilises. Diazepam is also given 10mg stat followed by 40mg in 5% dextrose. Lasix 20 to 80mg may be given as a diuretic. Antibiotics may be prescribed if necessary. Strict monitoring of blood pressure should be done.</p>
<p>Maintain a strict intake and output chart and test all urine that is passed. In some cases an indwelling catheter may be passed. Fluid intake is restricted to one to two litres in 24 hours.</p>
<p>Esbach should be set daily. The weight should also be measured daily or on alternative days.</p>
<p>Administer medication as prescribed. Observe for signs of onset of labour and signs of impending eclampsia. If protenuria and high blood pressure persist, the doctor should induce labour by artificial rupture of membranes followed by syntocinon drip. Unless there is some obstetric contra-indication a caesarean section will be done.</p>
<p>You have seen that mothers with severe pre-eclampsia can proceed to eclampsia. The following are warning signs of impending eclampsia:</p>
<ul>
<li>A sharp rise in blood pressure</li>
<li>Diminished urinary output</li>
<li>Increased proteinuria</li>
<li>Severe persistent frontal occipital headache</li>
<li>Drowsiness or confusion (due to cerebral oedema)</li>
<li>Blurring of vision or flashing lights (due to retinal oedema)</li>
<li>Nausea and vomiting</li>
<li>Epigastric pain which the mother may interpret as indigestion (due to oedema of the liver)</li>
</ul>
<p>Should a mother present to your clinic with these signs, give her an anticonvulsant and refer to hospital immediately for further management. In the hospital, the midwife should summon the doctor immediately.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="care-during-labour">Care During Labour<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#care-during-labour" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Treatment for severe pre-eclampsia should be continued. Perform a vaginal examination to assess the progress of labour. During the second stage, episiotomy should be performed to shorten the phase and the doctor will use vacuum extractor to prevent the mother from pushing. Ergometrine is avoided because of its vaso-constrictive effect and instead syntocinon 5 IV in a drip or intramuscularly is given. A Caesarean section may be performed if the condition does not improve or there is obstetric contra-indication for vaginal delivery.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="post-delivery-care-for-pre-eclampsia-cases">Post Delivery Care for Pre-eclampsia Cases<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#post-delivery-care-for-pre-eclampsia-cases" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Sedate the mother and continue observations of vital signs. Continue and adjust drugs as necessary.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="eclampsia">Eclampsia<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#eclampsia" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Eclampsia is an acute condition characterised by convulsions and coma. The incidence of</p>

<p>eclampsia is 0.2 to 0.5% of all pregnancies. It can occur in the antenatal period at the rate of about 20%; during the intrapartum period at the rate of about 25% and during the postnatal period within the first few hours after delivery (35%).</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="signs-and-symptoms-of-eclampsia">Signs and Symptoms of Eclampsia<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#signs-and-symptoms-of-eclampsia" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The prodromal signs of eclampsia are those we have described as serious signs of preeclampsia. The more immediate precursors of eclampsia are vomiting, intense headache and epigastric pain.</p>
<p>There are four stages of an eclampsia fit.</p>
<p><strong>Premonitory stage</strong>, which lasts 10 to 20 seconds. The mother is restless and rapid eye movements can be noted. The head may be drawn to one side, twitching of the facial muscles may occur, and the mother is not aware of what is happening.</p>
<p>The <strong>tonic stage</strong> lasts 10 to 20 seconds. The muscles of the mother's body go into spasms and become rigid. The back may become arched and her teeth become tightly clenched. The eyes appear like they are staring and her diaphragm goes into spasm. Respirations cease and cyanosis occurs.</p>
<p>The <strong>chronic stage</strong> lasts 60 to 90 seconds. There is violent contraction and intermitted relaxation of the mother's muscles causing convulsive movements. There is increased salivation and foaming at the mouth.</p>
<p>The mother's face becomes congested and bloated while her features become distorted. The mother becomes unconscious and breathing is stertorous while the pulse full and bounding. The convulsions subside gradually. In the stage of <strong>coma</strong> stertorous breathing continues and the coma may persist for minutes or hours. Further convulsions may occur before the mother regains consciousness.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="cardiac-disease-in-pregnancy">Cardiac Disease in Pregnancy<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#cardiac-disease-in-pregnancy" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>There are changes that occur in the cardiac system during pregnancy due to the increased demand in the foeto-placental unit. These changes increase the workload of the heart. The major changes are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Blood volume increases by 35%</li>
<li>Cardiac output increases by 40%, that is from 4.5 to 6l/min</li>
</ul>
<p>The extra work that the heart has to do is reduced by the decreased blood viscosity and lowered peripheral resistance. The pulse rate rises slightly in order to pump out the extra blood around the body. Oxygen consumption is raised. The heart is displaced upwards during the last trimester by the gravid uterus. During the third stage of labour 300 to 400ml of blood is added to the circulating volume by the contracting uterus.</p>
<p>These changes commence in early pregnancy and gradually reach their maximum at the 30th week and are maintained until term.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="risk-factors-for-heart-disease">Risk Factors for Heart Disease<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#risk-factors-for-heart-disease" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The following factors predispose patients to heart disease:</p>
<ul>
<li>Anaemia, which should be avoided and if present, vigorously treated.</li>
<li>Infections, the most common of which are upper respiratory infections. These should be treated with antibiotics.</li>
<li>Obesity should be avoided. Controlled weight gain should be encouraged to avoid extra strain on the heart.</li>
<li>Hypertension and pre-eclampsia should be admitted and controlled.</li>
<li>Smoking mothers should be advised to control their habits.</li>
<li>Multiple pregnancies should be well monitored.</li>
<li>Strain of any form should be avoided and mothers should be encouraged to have enough rest and adequate sleep.</li>
<li>Exercises that induce breathlessness should be discouraged.</li>
<li>Fatigue of any kind should be avoided.</li>
</ul>
<p>Cardiac disease in pregnancy has been classified in four grades. These are:</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="cardiac-grade-i">Cardiac Grade I<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#cardiac-grade-i" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<p>There are symptoms even at rest. There are signs of cardiac disease and heart failure.</p>
<p>Effects of cardiac disease in pregnancy</p>
<ul>
<li>The increase in blood volume and body weight causes strain on the already impaired heart.</li>
<li>The increased cardiac output reaches maximum at 30 weeks when the output is 25% above normal and, therefore, there is greater need for rest.</li>
<li>The normal venous dilation, which accompanies pregnancy, slows the venous return to the heart and, therefore, increases the difficulty in maintaining adequate output.</li>
</ul>
<p>This results in an increased risk of thromboemboli and bacterial endocarditis and raised maternal mortality when blood flow is impaired. There also also risks to the foetus and these include intrauterine growth retardation, raised incidence of congenital heart disease, and raised risk of foetal loss.</p>

<p>When managing a patient with severe cardiac disease, the following steps should be taken in the prenatal stage.</p>
<p>The patient should be nursed as a cardiac failure patient. She should be admitted on first contact for complete bed rest. <p>A very sick mother should be nursed in the propped up position and preferably in a cardiac bed. You should monitor foetal heart and foetal placental blood flow. <p>In terms of psychological care, it is very important to reassure the patient about her condition. Attend to her emotional needs and give counselling on reproductive health.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="second-stage">Second stage<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#second-stage" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Usually short and easy</li>
<li>Sit up or lie in the most comfortable position</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="third-stage">Third Stage<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#third-stage" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Patient may collapse when uterus contracts returning more blood into circulation thus overloading the heart. To avoid this, the right hand is placed on the abdomen firmly above the umbilicus to decrease abdominal pressure.</p>
<p>Discourage mother from over breathing because it draws more blood to the heart. If syntocinon is given, it should be continuous infusion with a syringe pump (10 to 20 units). Lasix should be given half hour before commencing the drip. This also applies if blood is to be transfused.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="puerperium">Puerperium<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#puerperium" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Heart failure may occur suddenly during puerperium, especially if the patient has incompetence of the aortic valve.</p>
<p>The patient should be nursed on complete bed rest. Ensure adequate breathing and leg exercises to prevent embolism. Ambulate on the fourth to fifth day. You should continue antibiotics for two weeks</p>
<p>Breastfeeding is encouraged unless there is actual heart failure.</p>
<p>The following family planning methods are advised:</p>
<ul>
<li>Natural family planning</li>
<li>Barrier methods with spermicides</li>
<li>Progesterone only pill</li>

<p>The mother will require adequate health information messages concerning contraceptives and her condition in order to make an informed choice.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="anaemia-in-pregnancy">Anaemia in Pregnancy<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#anaemia-in-pregnancy" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Anaemia is a deficiency in the quality or quantity of red blood cells with the result that the oxygen carrying capacity of the blood is reduced. The normal haemoglobin level in a female is 12 to 14gm per deciliter. Anaemia is diagnosed in pregnant women when the haemoglobin level is below 10gm per deciliter.</p>
<p>The following are some of the signs and symptoms of anaemia:</p>
<ul>
<li>Pallor of mucous membranes</li>
<li>Breathlessness</li>
<li>Dizziness</li>
<li>Fatigue and lethargy</li>
<li>Fainting attacks</li>
<li>Headaches due to lack of sufficient oxygen to brain cells</li>
<li>Anorexia and vomiting</li>
</ul>
<p>Anaemia affects the patient in several ways. With regard to the mother, anaemia has the following effects:</p>
<ul>
<li>It reduces enjoyment of pregnancy due to fatigue</li>
<li>It reduces resistance to infection caused by impaired cell mediated immunity</li>
<li>Predisposition to postpartum haemorrhage</li>
<li>Potential threat to life</li>
<li>Problems caused by treatment and side effects like constipation</li>
</ul>
<p>Anaemia also affects the foetus in the following ways:</p>
<ul>
<li>High perinatal mortality if maternal haemoglobin level is below 8gm/decilitre</li>
<li>Increased risk of intra uterine hypoxia and growth retardation and severe asphyxia in severe anaemia</li>
<li>Increased sudden infant death when maternal haemoglobin is below 10gm/decilitre</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="degrees-of-anaemia">Degrees of Anaemia<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#degrees-of-anaemia" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>These are classified according to the severity in pregnancy:</p>
<ul>
<li>Mild anaemia is when haemoglobin level is between 8.1gm/dl to 9.9gm/dl</li>
<li>Moderate anaemia is when the haemoglobin level is between 5.1gm/dl to 8gm/dl</li>
<li>Severe anaemia is when the haemoglobin is less than 5gm/dl</li>
</ul>
<p>In severe anaemia there is:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Renal hypoxia resulting in retention of sodium and electrolytes</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Myocardial hypoxia leading to heart failure</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Mental confusion</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Cough, especially with congestion in lungs</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>You will now look at the types of anaemia commonly seen in pregnancy.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="physiological-anaemia">Physiological Anaemia<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#physiological-anaemia" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>During pregnancy the blood plasma volume increases by 15% by the 10th week of gestation and 50% by the 32nd to the 35th week of pregnancy.</p>
<p>The red cells mass increases by 30%. These result in increased cardiac output from five to seven litres per minute.</p>
<p>These changes result in apparent anaemia but as this represents the normal pregnancy state, they should not be regarded as pathological.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="iron-deficiency-anaemia">Iron Deficiency Anaemia<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#iron-deficiency-anaemia" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>During pregnancy approximately 1400gm of iron is needed during the entire period. Please note that this is given in small doses of about 200gm three times a day. This is necessary for:</p>
<ul>
<li>The increased number of red blood cells</li>
<li>The foetus and the placenta</li>
<li>Replacement of blood lost during delivery</li>
<li>Lactation</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="remember">Remember:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#remember" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Absorption of iron is usually hindered by tea or coffee consumption, thus ascorbic acid is given to hasten iron absorption if one cannot stop taking tea or coffee.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="folic-acid-deficiency-anaemia">Folic Acid Deficiency Anaemia<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#folic-acid-deficiency-anaemia" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Folic acid is required for the increased cell growth of both the mother and the foetus. The main causes of folic acid deficiency anaemia are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Low dietary intake</li>
<li>Reduced absorption</li>
<li>Interference with utilisation like in substance abuse, anti-convulsant drugs and sulphonamides which are folate antagonists</li>
<li>Excessive demand and loss like in haemolytic anaemia</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="management-of-anaemia-in-pregnancy">Management of Anaemia in Pregnancy<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#management-of-anaemia-in-pregnancy" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The management of a woman with anaemia depends on the type and severity of anaemia, and the duration of pregnancy.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="mild-anaemia">Mild Anaemia<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#mild-anaemia" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This is characterised by haemoglobin between 8.1 to 9.9gm/decilitre. At a gestation of 20 to 29 weeks, the woman is given haematinics and a diet rich in protein and iron.</p>
<p>At 30 to 36 weeks, the haemoglobin levels are checked, diet is emphasised and haematinics continued. These include oral iron, for example, ferrous sulphate 200mg three times daily.</p>
<p>Investigations are carried out to establish the cause of the anaemia, for example, malarial parasites, hookworms, sickle cell disease. The mother is given health messages on nutrition, rest and taking drugs as prescribed.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="moderate-anaemia">Moderate Anaemia<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#moderate-anaemia" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This is characterised by haemoglobin levels of between 5.1 to 8gm per decilitre. At gestation of 29 to 30 weeks investigations are carried out to establish the cause and institute treatment.</p>
<p>Haematinics are given and a total dose of parenteral inferon 50 mgs/mililitre is given in a slow intravenous infusion of normal saline after a test dose to rule out sensitivity.</p>
<p>Intramuscular iron in the form of sorbital 50mg/ml is also administered. The dose is 1.5mg/kg body weight weekly. Haemoglobin levels are monitored regularly starting on the third day after commencement of treatment and then monthly. The injection should not be given in conjunction with oral iron as this enhances toxic effects.</p>
<p>At 30 to 36 weeks of gestation the woman is given total dose inferon and transfused with no more than 500ml whole blood. The blood is given slowly under close supervision. After transfusion, the woman will be put on folic acid. At 37 weeks blood transfusion is given again as above.</p>
<p>Parenteral iron is contraindicated for women who have liver or renal conditions.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="severe-anaemia">Severe Anaemia<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#severe-anaemia" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This is characterised by haemoglobin below 5gm per decilitre. This is an emergency where the mother is admitted and put on complete bed rest to reduce cardiac workload as she could go into cardiac failure.</p>
<p>Investigations are carried out to establish the cause. Meanwhile, she is nursed in left lateral position to prevent compression of the vena</p>

<p>cava by the gravid uterus. Vital observations are taken quarter hourly and the foetal heart rate is monitored.</p>
<p>Transfuse three units of packed cells slowly. Monitoring is continued quarter hourly. Administration of haematinics is continued.</p>
<p>In case of malaria, hookworm or sickle cell disease, the root cause of the anaemia is treated.</p>
<p>Health messages are shared on diet and general prevention.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="management-during-labour">Management During Labour<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#management-during-labour" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Blood is cross-matched and the patient is started on transfusion of packed cells only to avoid cardiac overload. Emergency drugs are kept ready. In the second stage of labour, oxygen is given and a vacuum extraction is carried out. Intravenous lasix is given. Syntocinon 40 to 60 units in half litre of 5% dextrose is given by pump.</p>
<p><strong>Remember:</strong></p>
<p>Ergometrine is contra-indicated because it causes vaso-constriction.</p>
<p>Blood loss should be minimised by rubbing the uterus to contract it. Controlled cord traction is used to deliver the placenta. The mother should avoid any exertion.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="post-natal-care">Post Natal Care<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#post-natal-care" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The mother is given antibiotics to prevent infection, and put on haematinics for three months. The haemoglobin is checked on the third and sixth week.</p>
<p>Family planning and good nutrition are encouraged.</p>
<p>If a pregnant woman has folic acid deficiency, you should give folic acid supplements and oral Iron. If she has vitamin B12 deficiency she should be given a weekly dose of 100mg of vitamin B12 injections until the condition is reversed.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="prevention-of-anaemia-in-pregnancy">Prevention of Anaemia in Pregnancy<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#prevention-of-anaemia-in-pregnancy" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The prevention of anaemia in pregnancy involves taking the following steps.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="health-education">Health Education<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#health-education" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>You should advise mothers in the antenatal clinic about a balanced diet. Green vegetables should not be overcooked as this destroys the folic acid. Teach them about proper disposal of</p>
<p>faeces to avoid hookworm infestation. Encourage the practice of child spacing to avoid frequent pregnancies so as to give the woman's body time to replenish her body stores. In addition encourage her to continue coming to antenatal clinic.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="prophylactic-medication">Prophylactic Medication<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#prophylactic-medication" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Give the following supplements to the women throughout pregnancy:</p>
<ul>
<li>Ferrous sulphate 200mg three times a day</li>
<li>Folic acid 5mg daily</li>
<li>Prophylactic anti malarial medication</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="urinary-tract-infections">Urinary Tract Infections<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#urinary-tract-infections" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Although Urinary Tract Infections (UTI) also occur to women who are not pregnant, it is a common problem among pregnant women. In a pregnant woman, this infection presents in different forms, some of which are serious, and others of mild consequence. The common conditions of urinary tract infection in pregnancy are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Asymptomatic bacteriuria</li>
<li>Acute cystitis</li>
<li>Acute pyelonephritis</li>
</ul>
<p>Asymptomatic bacteriuria is more common in pregnant women than in non-pregnant women. The condition is also twice as common in pregnant women with sickle cell trait and three times in those with diabetes as compared to normal pregnant women.</p>
<p>A woman with asymptomatic UTI may feel nothing except a slight pain when passing urine. She may also have offensive smelling urine. If the condition remains untreated during pregnancy, about 25 to 35% of these women will develop acute pyelonephritis.</p>
<p>Acute pyelonephritis occurs in two percent of all pregnant women and its effect may be fatal to the mother and/or her foetus. This occurs in those women with previous asymptomatic bacteriuria.</p>
<p>Acute cystitis is less common in pregnancy than asymptomatic bacteriuria. However, it causes more concern because of its symptoms.</p>
<p>As stated earlier, urinary tract infection occurs more frequently in pregnancy. This is because:</p>
<ul>
<li>The pregnant uterus causes pressure on the ureters and the bladder which delays emptying.</li>
<li>The action of hormones on the smooth muscles of the ureters and bladder also causes them to relax and dilate easily. This causes urine to move more slowly down the dilated tubes and infection lodges in them easily.</li>
<li>Normally the urinary tract mucosa is highly sensitive to invading organisms and the ureters go into spasmodic contractions to get rid of such invaders.</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="effects-of-pregnancy-on-hiv">Effects of Pregnancy on HIV<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#effects-of-pregnancy-on-hiv" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>Pregnancy is a very important and emotional period for a woman. There are many issues and concerns that HIV/AIDS presents to pregnant mothers, their partners, their families and health care workers during this period.</p>
<p>In the early asymptomatic phase of HIV disease, pregnancy does not seem to have any significant effect on the progress of HIV. However, pregnancy will greatly affect women whose defence mechanism has already been destroyed. Pregnancy in such women will make the disease progress rapidly to full blown AIDS.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="effects-of-hiv-on-pregnancy">Effects of HIV on Pregnancy<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#effects-of-hiv-on-pregnancy" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>HIV infection on the other hand, does not usually appear to seriously affect the pregnancy. However, HIV infection may cause an increased likelihood of intra uterine growth retardation, prematurity, still births and congenital infection.</p>
<p>An HIV positive woman has about 30% chance of transmitting the HIV virus to her infant. This may occur during pregnancy, at childbirth, or during breastfeeding.</p>

<p>HIV screening during pregnancy needs careful sensitive consideration. The decision to screen a woman for HIV infection is a joint consideration between the health worker and the woman, but the woman herself should make the final decision.</p>
<p>Any HIV testing must be accompanied by careful and adequate pre test counselling with proper post test counselling and support. Confidentiality of the results is important. There are many advantages of knowing whether a woman is HIV positive during her pregnancy.</p>

<p>If the HIV infection is newly diagnosed, the woman is under a lot of stress and will need a lot of support and counselling.</p>
<p>The health care services and the health workers should make a great effort to establish good support and care structures to manage women, their partners and their newborns. Support and care will need to be considered for the family.</p>
<p><strong>Remember:</strong>
Women with HIV need extra care during pregnancy. They should be seen more frequently than usual.</p>
<p>It is very important to be on the look out for the development of any HIV related conditions, especially for infections such as vaginal and oral thrush and other opportunistic infections such as herpes. Other infections which should be treated are respiratory infections, diarrhoea, skin infections, sexually transmitted diseases and Kaposis sarcoma.</p>
<p>It is important to provide counselling for encouragement and support to the HIV positive mother and her partner throughout the pregnancy. It is also important to start preparing them for possible problems that may occur after the pregnancy, that is, whether to breast or bottle feed the baby, possibility of HIV diagnosis</p>
