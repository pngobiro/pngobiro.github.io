Effective communication in a health facility is crucial. Doctors and nurses collaborate with each other to ensure the patients get the care they deserve. No single medical personnel can provide effective quality care without involving others.

üó£Ô∏è Forms of Communication

Communication in a health facility may occur in four main forms:

‚ñ∂ 1. Intrapersonal Communication

Occurs when you communicate to yourself without verbalising. It is an imaginary conversation reflecting the individual's thoughts and feelings. It helps in critical thinking and problem solving (e.g., planning patient care).

‚ñ∂ 2. Interpersonal Communication

Face-to-face communication between two or more persons. Used to share health messages, change attitudes, and persuade patients (e.g., regarding harmful cultural practices). Allows for immediate feedback and clarification.

‚ñ∂ 3. Mass Communication

Used to reach many people at the same time through media (TV, radio, newspapers). Often used in health for vaccination campaigns, natural disasters, or disease outbreaks.

‚ñ∂ 4. Organisational Communication

Occurs between management and employees to achieve goals. It harmonizes activities and explains expected conduct through circulars and documents.

üìù Presentation of Reports

Nursing reports are essential for the continuity of care. They inform authorities or other staff about events in a unit or institution. Examples include shift reports (Cardex), monthly health centre reports, and quarterly reports.

Principles of Writing a Good Report

  • Know the purpose and subject matter (factual).
  • Omit opinions from factual sections.
  • Keep it accurate, relevant, and concise.
  • Organise points logically with clear headings.
  • Start with the most pressing problem requiring action.
  • Emphasize ideas/facts providing solutions.

Content of a Health Centre Report

Standard Sections

  • 1
    Staff Statistics

    Total numbers, new members, resignations, transfers, retirees, leave.

  • 2
    Cadres of Staff

    Clinical officers, Nurses, Technicians, Support staff, etc.

  • 3
    Cases Seen

    New cases, re-attendances, referrals, deaths, deliveries, drug supply.

  • 4
    Problems Experienced

    Staff discipline, mechanical breakdowns, equipment repairs, disease outbreaks.

‚ö†Ô∏è Negative Incidence & Appraisal Reports

Negative Incidence/Accident Report

A formal statement on any unusual event in a ward with risk of possible legal action. It informs the employer and serves as evidence.

üö®

Incidents Requiring Reports

  • Suicide attempts
  • Absconding patients
  • Falls, burns, or injuries in the ward
  • Medication errors
  • Physical altercations
  • Wrong identification/treatment

Principles for the Statement

  1. Complete in triplicate (Authority, Ward, Patient File).
  2. Be concise, clear, unambiguous, and non-emotional (factual).
  3. Quantify observable damage (e.g., burn size in cm).
  4. Avoid medical jargon/abbreviations where possible.
  5. State only what you saw (witnesses included).
  6. Include accurate date, time, and immediate actions taken (First aid, Doctor notified, etc.).

Appraisal Reports

Written annually by immediate supervisors to summarize an employee's performance. It should be objective, accurate, and balanced (strengths and weaknesses).

Contents: Qualifications, experience, ability, character, relationships, areas for improvement, and recommendations for training/promotion.

‚úâÔ∏è Writing an Official Letter

Official letters are used for transfers, study leave, promotions, or informing employers of important occurrences.

Structure of an Official Letter

  • References: 'Our Ref' (links to file), 'Your Ref' (replying to).
  • Date: Full format (e.g., 4th December 2001).
  • Salutation: 'Dear Sir' (Organization), 'Dear Sir/Madam' (Unknown gender), 'Dear Mr/Ms [Name]' (Known person).
  • Body: Subject heading, Introduction/Acknowledgement, Facts, Closing with suggested action.
  • Close: 'Yours faithfully' (if Dear Sir/Madam), 'Yours sincerely' (if Dear Name).

ü§ù Conducting Meetings

Meetings are held to pass messages, review progress, or hear ideas. Preparation is key: notify participants, select a convenient date/venue, and prepare an agenda.

The Agenda

An agenda ensures objectives are met and items are discussed in sequence. It typically includes:

  1. Welcoming participants
  2. Confirmation of previous minutes
  3. Matters arising
  4. New matters
  5. A.O.B. (Any Other Business)
  6. Closure and next meeting date

Roles

Chairman Secretary
Calls meeting to order and maintains order Circulates attendance list
Ensures participation of every member Takes accurate minutes of decisions
Keeps discussion focused on agenda Circulates minutes in advance
Summarizes opinions and closes meeting Makes preparations for next meeting

Minutes

Minutes are a summary of proceedings and decisions. They must include dates, attendees, apologies, decisions made, responsibilities assigned, and signatures of the chairman and secretary upon approval.
