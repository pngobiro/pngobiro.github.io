üå± Human Growth and Development

Studying human growth and development provides practical guidance for healthcare providers to support healthy growth and understand individuals with special needs.

  • Growth: The progressive development of a living thing, especially the process by which the body reaches complete physical development.
  • Development: The process of growth and differentiation.

Psychoanalytic Theory (Freud)

Sigmund Freud's psychoanalytic theory emphasizes personality development and early childhood experiences. Freud proposed that early experiences shape one's personality for an entire lifetime.

Freud's Psychosexual Stages

Stage Age Focus or Interests
Oral 0 - 1 Focus on mouth, teeth and gums (sensual pleasure through feeding)
Anal 1 - 3 Focus on control and self-control (toilet training)
Phallic 3 - 6 Focus on genital stimulation and gender differentiation
Latency 7 - 11 Energy focused on non-sensual activity (schoolwork)
Genital 12+ Development of mature sexual interests

Note: Erik Erikson proposed a related series of psychosocial stages (e.g., trust vs. mistrust in infancy) that emphasize social influences rather than sexual drives.

üß© Personality Structure

Freud described three components of personality that distinguish individuals from one another.

‚ñ∂ 1. The Id (The Instincts)

The reservoir of instinctual drives (biological urges, wishes). It is dominated by the pleasure principle, seeking immediate gratification. It is the unconscious component.

‚ñ∂ 2. The Ego (The Reality)

The central part of personality that deals with reality. It acts as a mediator between the unconscious impulses of the Id and the standards of the Super Ego. It is influenced by social forces.

‚ñ∂ 3. The Super Ego (The Morality)

The moral component that modifies and inhibits the instinctual impulses of the Id. It develops as the child adopts values and standards from parents and society. It includes the "Ego ideal".

Anxiety

Anxiety is a state of tension that motivates a person to do something. It develops as a result of a conflict between the Id, Ego, and Super Ego.

Ego Defence Mechanisms

To defend itself against unacceptable impulses from the Id, the Ego develops specific psychic means known as defence mechanisms.

Repression
The exclusion of impulses from conscious awareness.
Projection
The process of ascribing to others one's own unacknowledged desires.
Reaction Formation
The establishment of a pattern of behaviour directly opposed to a strong unconscious need.
Compensation
The process of masking weaknesses or developing certain positive traits to make up for limitations (e.g., intellectually inferior person building up body).
Sublimation
Diverting sexual energy to some other socially acceptable and sometimes admirable activities.

‚ö° Stress & Coping

Stress is an unpleasant state of emotional and physiological arousal experienced in situations perceived as dangerous or threatening. The circumstances that cause stress are called stressors.

Sources of Stress

  • Catastrophes: Sudden, life-threatening disasters (earthquakes, wars, accidents).
  • Major Life Changes: Death of a spouse, divorce, job loss, imprisonment.
  • Daily Hassles: Commuting, noisy neighborhoods, workplace conflicts. Cumulatively, these can predict illness.

The Stress Response

The body activates the sympathetic nervous system (fight-or-flight). Adrenaline and cortisol are released, heart rate increases, and digestion slows.

Coping Strategies

Problem-Focused Coping Emotion-Focused Coping
Taking action to modify or avoid the situation. Trying to moderate or eliminate unpleasant emotions.
Best when opportunities exist to change the situation. Useful as a short-term strategy or when the situation cannot be changed.
Example: Organizing a study group. Example: Relaxation, denial, wishful thinking.

Management Techniques

  • Social Support: Support from friends/family buffers stress.
  • Progressive Muscular Relaxation: Tensing and relaxing muscle groups.
  • Meditation: Focusing attention (e.g., Transcendental Meditation).
  • Aerobic Exercise: Increases endurance and lowers baseline heart rate.
