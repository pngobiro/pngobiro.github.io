üëã Introduction & Objectives

Despite your best attempts to communicate with your patients or colleagues, you may fail because of certain barriers or factors that hinder effective communication. It is, therefore, important for you to know some of these barriers and how you can overcome them.

üéØ

Learning Objectives

By the end of this section you will be able to:

  • Explain what is meant by a barrier to effective communication
  • Describe barriers that may affect the six elements of the communication process
02

üß± Defining Barriers

Barriers are the factors that prevent effective communication. After being placed in a channel, the message may be interfered with by some disturbance which increases the difficulty in perception or prevents some elements of the message from reaching the receiver.

Barriers to effective communication may be due to any breakdown in the six elements of the communication process:

üì¢ Barriers Resulting from the Source

The person who initiates the communication process is called the originating communicator or source of the message. The source should have qualities that facilitate effective communication.

  • Understanding the Audience: The source should have a sound understanding of their audience and a good knowledge of their subject. The audience determines the level of language and vocabulary to be used.
  • Socio-cultural Factors: When addressing illiterate peasants, one has to put oneself in their situation to be able to appreciate their culture, values, perceptions, worries and hopes. Failure to recognise the importance of these factors can lead to a breakdown in communication.
  • Physical Barriers: These may be attributed to the climate, for example, very cold or very hot weather, wind, noise and so on.
  • System Overload: This may occur where an individual sends or receives too much information at the same time. For example, a nurse taking over an evening shift urgently called to attend to a telephone call from the matron.
  • Privacy and Confidentiality: Where privacy and confidentiality are not observed, the readiness of the patient to talk freely is severely restricted which can lead to a communication breakdown.
04

‚úâÔ∏è Barriers Affecting the Message

The message is what the sender wishes to convey to the audience. In order for the message to be well-received and effective, the sender must plan it well with the needs of the audience in mind.

‚ö†Ô∏è

Planning Considerations

  • What does the sender want the receiver to know?
  • How do they expect the receiver to feel?
  • Do they want to change the behaviour of the receiver?

Techniques for Creating Effective Messages

In order to avoid failure in communication, you must address the following factors:

  • The message must be expressed in simple language. It must be short and to the point.
  • The message must be problem-centred. It must address a health concern or issue affecting the individual or the community.
  • The message should be culturally relevant and not offensive to the values and beliefs of the community or individuals. Religious beliefs are particularly important.
  • The message must fall within the socio-economic abilities of the audience. Do not propose solutions that are beyond the reach of your audience.
  • The message must demonstrate that it is much more important and beneficial to do what is proposed in the message than what the message opposes.
05

üëÇ Barriers Resulting from the Receiver

Inadequate knowledge or understanding of the socio-economic and cultural factors surrounding the audience can bring about communication breakdown. Understanding the audience is key to successful communication.

Factors in a Communication Breakdown at the Receiver

  • Inadequate Preparation: If the receiver is inadequately prepared physically, socially and psychologically about the venue and time, they may arrive late.
  • Lack of Interest: If the receiver does not know the benefits to gain from the message, they may have little interest to listen.
  • Language Barriers: If the message is expressed in complicated jargon.
  • Poor Delivery: If the sender stammers or speaks inaudibly.
  • Environment: If the message is received in a noisy environment.
  • Emotional State: If the receiver is emotionally disturbed, there will be a lack of concentration.
  • Religious Beliefs: If the message is against religious beliefs (e.g., messages on artificial family planning to Catholics).
  • Information Overload: If the message contains too much information, the receiver will be overwhelmed and confused.
  • Timing: If the message takes a long time to reach the receiver, the audience will change their priorities.
06

üèôÔ∏è Barriers Resulting from the Social Setting

The social setting is the environment in which the message is transmitted. The social setting creates 'noise' which interferes with the message. This 'noise' can be visual, olfactory, tactile, electronic, cultural, racial or age related.

  • Distractions: The selected venue has posters irrelevant to the health message given, acting as detractors.
  • Social Status: Failure to include respected persons (chiefs, councillors) when organising a health meeting.
  • Age and Marital Status: Married people may not feel free to discuss certain issues in the presence of single people. Intergenerational communication is not easy.
  • Cultural Beliefs: In some communities, men and women do not mix easily; addressing them together causes a barrier.
07

üì° Barriers Affecting the Communication Channel

Communication channels are the means by which a message travels from the source to the receiver.

  • Verbal Communication: The sender speaking in a low voice without an amplifier to a large crowd.
  • Mass Media Access: Using radio, TV, or newspapers without considering the socio-economic status of the receiver (access to media).
  • Age Appropriateness: Selecting a verbal channel without considering if the content/delivery suits youth or older persons.
üí°

Tip for Success

Effective communication is often facilitated by using more than one channel. For example, a nurse might use a poster (visual) to show family planning methods while using their spoken voice (audio) to explain how each method works.

08

üìä Barriers Affecting Effects & Feedback

Effects

Effects are the outcomes resulting from the communication of a message (new knowledge, change in attitudes, or change in behaviour). Barriers include:

  • Failure to use appropriate language or prepare the audience.
  • The receiver being emotionally disturbed.
  • Disregarding cultural beliefs (e.g., a male midwife sharing health messages with women who believe only female midwives should conduct deliveries).

Feedback

Feedback is used to determine if the intended message is understood by the receiver. A breakdown occurs when the sender receives a negative response indicating that the message is not understood.
