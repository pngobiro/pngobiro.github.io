üëã Introduction & Objectives

Many of the factors that cause breakdown in everyday communication also present barriers to counselling. Barriers to effective counselling interfere with the counselling process by making the patient unable to make informed decisions or disclose their feelings and concerns fully.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this section you will be able to:

  • Describe different types of barriers to effective counselling
  • List the rights of a patient
  • Explain ethical issues to be considered when counselling

üöß Types of Barriers

Barriers can stem from the physical environment, the counsellor, or the patient.

‚ñ∂ Physical Barriers
  • Interview Room: Must be private, quiet, well-ventilated, and clean. Lack of privacy prevents disclosure.
  • Distractions: Noise, poor lighting, or distracting equipment.
  • Appearance: Unpleasant appearance may prevent the patient from listening or taking the counsellor seriously.
‚ñ∂ Social & Cultural Differences
  • Age and Sex: Significant age differences (e.g., counsellor young enough to be patient's child) or gender differences may cause discomfort.
  • Background: Differences in nationality, race, or ethnicity may lead to misunderstandings of beliefs and taboos.
‚ñ∂ Non-Verbal Communication

Negative gestures can cause failure:

  • Frowning or showing disgust
  • Signs of boredom or amusement
  • Displaying disapproval

Tip: Use positive gestures like smiling and nodding to demonstrate interest.

03

üë• Patient & Counsellor Barriers

Barriers Caused by the Patient

  • Lack of Interest: Makes a patient inattentive and creates prejudice.
  • Emotional Disturbance: If emotions are too high, the session may need to be postponed.

Counsellor-Related Barriers

Specific behaviors by the counsellor can derail the process:

Failure to Listen Not receiving the intended message.
Failure to Probe Not asking relevant questions to get adequate responses.
Being Judgemental Approving or disapproving of the patient's statements.
Rejection Refusing to discuss topics, implying judgement.
Parroting Repeating the same phrases even when the question changes.
Defending Attempting to protect oneself or others from negative feedback.
Giving Advice Telling the patient what to do instead of helping them explore options.
Changing Topics Directing interaction to self-interest rather than the patient's lead.
04

‚öñÔ∏è Rights of a Patient

The patient has specific rights which the counsellor must observe and respect.

Respect
The patient is a human being with their own culture and views. They have a right to appropriate care without prejudice.
Privacy and Confidentiality
Discussions must be in private. Information must strictly remain between counsellor and patient unless consent is given.
Consent
No decisions should be imposed. The patient must agree to all plans.
Right of Refusal
The patient has the right to refuse counselling services offered by an incompetent counsellor.
Involvement
The patient should be involved in planning the course of action for their benefit.
Informed
The patient must be told the whole truth about their health status and have queries answered.
Right of Referral
The counsellor should refer the patient if unable to meet their needs.

üìú Ethical Issues & Code of Practice

The primary role of a counsellor is to serve the patient's interests at all times. The counsellor should be seen as a helper rather than an adviser.

Code of Practice

  • ‚úì

    Ensure the patient suffers no physical or psychological harm.

  • ‚úì

    Set and monitor boundaries between counselling and other relationships.

  • ‚úì

    Do not exploit patients financially, sexually, or emotionally.

  • ‚úì

    Respect the patient's ability to make decisions aligned with their own beliefs.

Terms of Counselling

Terms should be communicated in the initial contact, including:

  • Availability, time, and location.
  • Fees (if applicable) and cancellation policy.
  • Number of sessions.
  • Record keeping and confidentiality policies.

Counsellor Competence

Counsellors must work within their known limits of competence. They should not offer services if their ability is impaired by personal difficulties, illness, or substance use. Referral is mandatory when the patient's needs exceed the counsellor's competence.
