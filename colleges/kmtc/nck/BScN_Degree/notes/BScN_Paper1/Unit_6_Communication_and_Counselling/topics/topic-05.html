üëã Introduction & Definition

Counselling is one of the most important tasks of a nurse. You are often counselling patients, children, relatives, and colleagues without necessarily being aware of the process.

üí°

Definition

Counselling is the act of working with a patient to help them clarify personal goals and find ways of overcoming their problems with the aim of assisting the individual change behaviours that are interfering with attainment of basic needs.

üéØ

Learning Objectives

By the end of this section you will be able to:

  • Define counselling
  • Describe types of counselling
  • Describe the characteristics of an effective counsellor
02

üóÇÔ∏è Types of Counselling

The type of counselling is determined by the nature of problems presented by the patients.

‚ñ∂ Individual Counselling

Occurs when a counsellor is working with only one person at any given time (e.g., a nurse dealing with a single patient).

‚ñ∂ Group Counselling

Occurs when a counsellor works with more than one person. Recommended for people facing the same problem who need mutual support (e.g., Alcoholics Anonymous).

‚ñ∂ Marital Counselling

Conducted by a trained therapist with both partners present. Assists the couple to understand their problems and find solutions.

‚ñ∂ Family Counselling

Focuses on family issues and is conducted when all relevant family members are present to ensure effective resolution.

‚ñ∂ Special Group Counselling

Targeted groups requiring specific adjustments, including:

  • Drug and substance abusers
  • Rape victims and rapists
  • HIV/AIDS infected and affected people
  • Terminally ill people
  • Retirees and Retrenchees

‚≠ê Qualities of a Good Counsellor

Patience
Do not hurry the patient or show boredom. Wait until the patient has understood clearly before moving on.
Warmth
Show warmth without being possessive. Smile and show concern and acceptance.
Confidentiality
Keep all information secret. Counselling should be done in a private room. Disclosure is unprofessional except to involved professionals.
Honesty
Always tell the truth. If unsure, admit it and seek information. Encourages the patient to also be honest.
Empathy
Understanding the patient's feelings and communicating this understanding. It involves sharing while retaining objectivity.
Observance
Observe facial expressions and mood swings to interpret non-verbal communication and detect inappropriate behavior.
Accepting
Accept the patient as they are. Be non-judgmental and avoid criticism.

üîÑ The Counselling Process

The role of a Health Care Worker (HCW) is to support and assist the patient by listening, understanding choices, and helping explore options. The HCW is not responsible for making the final decisions.

The GATHER Approach

There are six basic steps applied in the initial counselling process, remembered by the acronym GATHER.

Stages of Counselling

  • 1
    Exploration Stage

    Establishing rapport and clarifying problems. Patient asks: "What are my problems?"

  • 2
    Understanding Stage

    Insight and new perspectives. Patient asks: "What do I need or want?"

  • 3
    Action Stage

    Developing strategies and plans. Patient asks: "How do I get what I need?"

üõ†Ô∏è Communication Skills

Attending Skills (SOLER)

Effective attending tells the patient that you are listening.

S Sitting Squarely facing the person (or angled if less threatening).
O Open Posture (uncrossed legs/arms) to signify availability.
L Leaning Forward at times to show involvement.
E Eye Contact maintained without staring/glaring.
R Relaxed and natural demeanor.

Verbal Skills

  • Paraphrasing: Repeating statements using different words to confirm understanding.
  • Reflecting Feelings: Understanding emotional responses and communicating them back (e.g., "You are feeling anxious about...").
  • Questioning: Use open-ended questions (How, What, When) to encourage discussion.
  • Clarifying: Preventing misunderstanding by focusing on specific points.
  • Summarising: Pulling threads together to see the whole picture.

üîö Termination of Counselling

Termination means ending the counselling relationship when the patient shows improvement and self-reliance. Separation can be challenging, and patients may exhibit reactions similar to the stages of grief:

‚ñ∂ 1. Denial

Shock, disbelief, and refusal to accept termination. Action: Convince them of their progress and need for independence.

‚ñ∂ 2. Anger

Feeling abandoned or asking "why me?". Action: Encourage expression of feelings.

‚ñ∂ 3. Bargaining

Trying to change the counsellor's mind or setting unrealistic goals to prolong contact. Action: Emphasize achievements and necessity of independence.

‚ñ∂ 4. Depression

Sadness, hopelessness, withdrawal, anorexia, insomnia upon realizing termination is real.

‚ñ∂ 5. Acceptance

Accepting reality and planning ways to cope independently. Confidence is gained.
