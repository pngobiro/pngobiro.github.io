ü¶∂ Congenital Abnormalities: Talipes

Talipes (Clubfoot)

Talipes describes a group of foot deformities involving the ankle (talus) and foot (pes). It is one of the most common congenital orthopaedic deformities (1 in 700-1000 live births), occurring more frequently in boys.

Types of Talipes

‚ñ∂ 1. Talipes Equinovarus (Most Common - 95%)

The foot is fixed in plantar flexion (toes down) and deviates medially (inwards). The heel is elevated off the ground. If untreated, the child walks on the toes and outer border of the foot.

‚ñ∂ 2. Talipes Calcaneovalgus

The foot is dorsiflexed (toes up) and deviates laterally (outwards). The child walks on an outwardly turned heel and the inner border of the foot.

‚ñ∂ 3. Other Types
  • Talipes Cavovarus: Heel turned inwards, weight on outer sole.
  • Talipes Equinovalgus: Heel elevated and turned outwards.
  • Talipes Calcaneovarus: Heel turned inwards, anterior foot elevated.

Management

Treatment should commence soon after birth (36-48 hours). Delay makes correction difficult as bones/muscles develop abnormally.

  • Conservative: Manual manipulation, strapping, splints (e.g., Denis Browne Splint), or Plaster of Paris (POP).
  • Surgical: Tenotomy (cutting/realigning Achilles tendon) or division of soft tissues if conservative methods fail.

üöë Fractures, Dislocations & Sprains

Fractures

A break in the continuity of a bone. In young children, greenstick fractures (incomplete break) are common.

Clinical Features

  • Pain on palpation
  • Shortness of limb
  • Swelling and redness
  • Loss of function
  • Deformity

Management Principles

  1. Reduction:
    • Closed: Manipulation/Traction (e.g., Bryant's traction for young children).
    • Open: Surgical incision and internal fixation (plates, screws, pins).
  2. Immobilisation: Splints or Plaster of Paris (POP). Check circulation and sensation regularly.
  3. Rehabilitation: Physiotherapy to restore function.

Dislocations & Sprains

Condition Definition Management
Dislocation Displacement of bones at a joint. Closed/Open reduction, immobilisation until pain/swelling resolve.
Sprain Stretching of ligaments at a joint (commonly ankle). Cold/Heat application, firm bandage/splint, rest, mild analgesics.

ü¶¥ Joint Disorders

Juvenile Rheumatoid Arthritis (JRA)

A chronic inflammatory disease affecting joints in children. It presents in three main forms:

‚ñ∂ 1. Polyarticular Disease

Involves many joints (especially small hand joints). More common in girls. Symmetrical involvement (knees, ankles, wrists). Can cause growth retardation.

‚ñ∂ 2. Pauciarticular Disease

Affects few joints (knees, ankles, elbows). ~33% of cases. Risk of eye inflammation (iris/ciliary body).

‚ñ∂ 3. Rheumatoid Arthritis (Systemic)

Unknown cause. Features intermittent pyrexia, rash, leucocytosis, anaemia, and lymphadenopathy.

üí°

Nursing Care for JRA

  • Complete bed rest during acute phase.
  • Assistance with feeding/bathing.
  • Physiotherapy to prevent stiffness/contractures.
  • Psychological support for child and family (chronic condition).
  • Splinting may be required.
