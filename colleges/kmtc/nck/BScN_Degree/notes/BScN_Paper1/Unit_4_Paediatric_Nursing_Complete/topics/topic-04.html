üëã Introduction

Children are more prone to infections than adults because they have low body resistance. They are also susceptible as a result of their mobility, especially as a toddler, of coming into contact with dirt and other harmful substances.

üß† Meningococcal Meningitis

Meningitis is the inflammation of the meninges (membranes covering the brain and spinal cord). The causative organism is Neisseria Meningitis (Meningococci). In many parts of Africa, sero group A is the main cause of epidemics.

Fig 4.1: Meningitis Pathophysiology

Clinical Features

  • Acute and sudden onset, severe headache spreading down the neck.
  • Pyrexia, tachycardia, rigors.
  • Neck Stiffness and Kernig's sign positive (pain/inability to extend knee when hip is flexed).
  • In babies: Bulging anterior fontanelle, high-pitched cry.
  • Photophobia, convulsions, and potentially petechial haemorrhage.
üöë

Nursing Care & Treatment

  • Isolation: Admit to isolated cubicle, barrier nursing for 48 hours.
  • Environment: Quiet, darkened room (photophobia).
  • Drug Therapy: Crystalline penicillin (300mg/kg/day) or Chloramphenicol (100mg/kg/day). Phenobarbitone for convulsions.
  • Monitoring: Assess for increased intracranial pressure (ICP), fluid balance chart.

ü´Å Respiratory Infections

Whooping Cough (Pertussis)

An infectious respiratory disease caused by Bordetella pertussis. Characterised by thick, stringy mucus and violent coughing ending in an inspiratory whoop.

Clinical Stages

  1. Catarrhal Stage (14 days): Resembles common cold (slow onset cough, fever).
  2. Paroxysmal Stage: Spasms of coughing (expiration) followed by "whoop" (inspiration). Vomiting often follows coughing.

Management: Complete bed rest, humid environment, small frequent feeds (re-feed after vomiting). Antibiotics: Erythromycin.

Tuberculosis

Infectious disease caused by Tubercle bacillus. Can be Pulmonary (lungs) or Extra-pulmonary (other organs, e.g., via infected milk).

ü¶† Other Major Infections

Poliomyelitis

Acute infectious viral disease of the anterior horn cells of the spinal cord. Transmitted via the Five F's: Fingers, Flies, Faeces, Fluids, and Food.

‚ö†Ô∏è

Management Precaution

No injections are to be administered during the acute stage as they may precipitate paralysis.

Hepatitis

  • Hepatitis A: Faecal-oral route. Jaundice, clay-colored stools, dark urine. Self-limiting.
  • Hepatitis B: Serum hepatitis. Transmitted via blood, vertical (mother-to-child). Can lead to chronic liver disease.

Malaria

Caused by Plasmodium parasites (P. falciparum is most dangerous). Transmitted by female Anopheles mosquito.

Clinical Features: Fever (hot/cold stages), vomiting, headache, anaemia, splenomegaly. Complications include cerebral malaria and severe anaemia.

Treatment: Quinine sulphate or Artemisinin-based combination therapies.

üö® Paediatric Emergencies

Cardio-Pulmonary Resuscitation (CPR)

If a child collapses, start resuscitation using the 'ABC' approach:

  • A Airway: Position head, clear mucus/vomit.
  • B Breathing: Look, listen, feel. Provide ventilation if not breathing.
  • C Circulation: Cardiac massage. Ratio of 1:5 (lung inflation : chest compression).

Acute Convulsions

Spasmodic contraction of muscles. Common causes include febrile convulsions (malaria), meningitis, epilepsy, hypoglycemia.

Management: Ensure airway, semi-prone position, protect from injury (do not restrain), administer anticonvulsants (Rectal Diazepam/Phenobarbitone) if prolonged.
