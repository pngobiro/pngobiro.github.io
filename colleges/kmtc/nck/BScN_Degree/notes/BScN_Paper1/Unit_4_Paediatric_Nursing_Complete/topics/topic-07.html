üíß Hydrocephalus

This is an abnormal condition of fluid around the brain or inside the ventricles. It is usually a result of an interference with the circulation or absorption of cerebral spinal fluid (CSF).

Types of Hydrocephalus

‚ñ∂ 1. Communicating (Extra Ventricular)

Obstruction is outside the ventricular system (blockage of subarachnoid cisterns). The fluid compresses the brain and distends the cranial cavity.

Causes: Subarachnoid haemorrhage, Bacterial meningitis (TB), Head injury.

‚ñ∂ 2. Non-Communicating

Obstruction is within the ventricular systems, interfering with flow to the subarachnoid space.

Causes: Congenital defects (aqueduct stenosis, Arnold Chiari malformation) or Acquired defects (tumours, cysts).

Management

Medical: Acetazolamide (diamox) to reduce CSF production. Repeated lumbar punctures.

Surgical: Removal of obstructions (tumours/cysts) or Ventriculostomy (Shunting). A catheter with a valve (e.g., Spitz Holter) is inserted to shunt CSF from the ventricle to another area like the abdominal cavity.

Nursing Care

  • Pre-op: Monitor intracranial pressure, measure head circumference, palpate fontanelles, turn position two hourly (support neck).
  • Post-op: Monitor vital signs hourly initially. Ensure child lies on the good side to prevent pressure on the shunt valve. Monitor for infection or vomiting.

üß¨ Other Neural Defects

Microcephaly

A congenital condition where there is a defect in the growth of the brain. The size of the brain becomes three times smaller than normal. Causes include foetal radiation, maternal phenylketonuria, or congenital infections (rubella, syphilis). There is no treatment.

Spina Bifida

A congenital abnormality resulting from a defect in the formation of the skeletal arch enclosing the spinal cord, most common in the lumbar region.

Management of Spina Bifida Cystica (Meningomyelocele)

Surgery is indicated at the earliest opportunity to prevent neurological damage.

Preoperative Care: Prevent infection and injury to the sac. Apply aseptic technique dressing using warm normal saline changed four hourly.

‚ö° Epilepsy

A neurological condition characterised by recurrent seizures. A seizure is a sudden attack of altered cerebral function caused by abnormal electrical discharges.

Classifications

Type Description
Petit Mal "Small sickness". Sudden momentary loss of consciousness with minor colonic jerks. Blank facial expression.
Jacksonian Unilateral chronic movements starting in one group of muscles and spreading systematically.
Grand Mal "Major epilepsy". Loss of consciousness. Stages: Aura (warning) ‚Üí Tonic (rigid) ‚Üí Clonic (violent spasms) ‚Üí Comatose (deep sleep).
üöë

Emergency Management: The 'ABC' Rule

An epileptic seizure is a medical emergency.

  1. Airway: Ensure a clear airway.
  2. Breathing: Enable patient to breathe.
  3. Circulation: Loosen clothing.

Do NOT: Restrain the patient (may cause injury).
Do: Protect the head, place in lateral position after seizure to drain secretions.

Status Epilepticus: Repeated seizures without recovering consciousness between attacks. Medical emergency treated with anti-convulsants (Phenobarbitone, Phenytoin).

ü©∏ Diabetes Mellitus

A clinical symptom characterised by hyperglycaemia due to relative deficiencies of insulin action. Rare in children under two years.

Pathophysiology

Insulin deficiency leads to:

  1. 1Reduced glucose uptake by cells (Hyperglycaemia).
  2. 2Glycosuria (glucose in urine) exceeding renal threshold.
  3. 3Polyuria leading to electrolyte depletion and dehydration.
  4. 4Fat breakdown causing ketosis (acidosis).

Clinical Features in Children

  • Sudden onset (often triggered by stress/infection).
  • Excessive thirst (Polydipsia) and Polyuria.
  • New onset bed wetting.
  • Rapid weight loss despite hunger.
  • Ketosis (vomiting, abdominal pain, sweet breath).

Management: Insulin administration, diet control, hydration, and education on preventing coma (Hypoglycaemic vs Hyperglycaemic).

üê¢ Hypothyroidism (Cretinism)

Congenital condition where the thyroid gland under-secretes thyroxine. Occurs in 1 in 5,000 births.

Clinical Features

  • Delayed physiological jaundice.
  • Cold, coarse, dry skin and brittle hair.
  • Broad, flat nose; thick lips; protruding tongue.
  • Large abdomen, umbilical hernia, constipation.
  • Subnormal temperature and slow pulse.
  • Mental retardation if not treated early.

Management: Life-long treatment with Thyroxine. Dosage adjusted based on response (pulse rate, bowel movements, alertness).
