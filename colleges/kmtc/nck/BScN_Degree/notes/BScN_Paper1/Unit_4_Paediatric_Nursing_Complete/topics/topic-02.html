üè• Admission Procedure

You have seen that the care of children is unique and special. Child care is based on certain principles, which you should adhere to in order to provide quality nursing care to children in any clinical situations.

üéØ

Objectives

  • Describe the admission procedure for sick children and how to record their personal histories
  • Explain how to perform a complete physical examination
  • Describe how to prepare for and carry out the common investigations
  • Define and explain the importance of IMCI strategy

The Admission Process

When working with children, realise that routine procedures may cause anxiety and distress to children and parents. A concerned, kind, and understanding approach is essential. Avoid criticising parents who are misinformed; instead, reassure them to alleviate fears.

  • Parental Presence: Parents/guardians should accompany children to the assigned bed and receive a pleasant reception in a clean, quiet environment.
  • Orientation: Introduce older children and parents to others in the ward. Orient them to facilities (toilets, playroom) and explain rules (visiting hours, food).
  • Personal Details: Find out the child's likes, dislikes, and commonly used name. Obtain informed consent before parents leave.

History Taking

History is usually taken from parents/guardians. Important details include:

  • Medical History: Previous illnesses, surgeries.
  • Family/Social History: Socio-economic status, obstetric history of mother.
  • Observation: Establish rapport, observe non-verbal clues, and clarify questions to avoid offense.

ü©∫ Physical Examination

Perform an objective physical examination from head to toe to make a nursing diagnosis. Exercise patience, make friends with the child, and be flexible‚Äîleave upsetting procedures until last.

Technique Description
Observation Visual and clinical scrutiny. Check temperature, pulse, and respiration.
Inspection Critical scrutiny to look for signs and symptoms of disease or abnormality.
Palpation Examining with a finger/hand over the chest or abdomen.
Percussion Placing left middle fingers over an area and tapping with the right finger.
Auscultation Listening for sound by use of the ears and stethoscope.

Positioning: Seek help from parents to prevent unnecessary movement. Talk in a low, persuasive voice. Examination of young children is often best performed on the parent's lap.

üî¨ Investigations

Investigations are crucial for accurate diagnosis. The nurse is responsible for carrying them out or assigning them to relevant personnel (doctor, lab technologist).

‚ö†Ô∏è

Important

Ensure all investigations are carried out accurately, punctually, and aseptically. Correct labelling of forms is vital to avoid mix-ups. Prepare parents and patients physically and psychologically.

Common Investigations:

  • Body fluids, excretions, secretions, tissues for culture and sensitivity.
  • Blood: WBC counts, differentials, Haemoglobin, Malarial parasites, Grouping/Cross-matching.
  • Urine: Microscopic, albumen, sugar, acetone.
  • Stool: Ova, cysts, occult blood.
  • Radiological (X-rays) and Endoscopic investigations.
  • Sputum or gastric aspirate (via naso-gastric tube) for microscopic culture/sensitivity.

üåê IMCI Strategy

IMCI (Integrated Management of Childhood Illness) is a strategy combining improved management of childhood illnesses with nutrition, immunisation, and maternal health. It aims to reduce infant mortality, severity of illness, and disability.

Rationale

Introduced by WHO/UNICEF because 1.2 million children in developing countries die before their fifth birthday. 70% of deaths are due to five specific diseases: Acute Respiratory Infection (pneumonia), Measles, Malnutrition, Malaria, and Diarrhoea.

Symptom Overlap

Most sick children present with clinical features related to more than one condition, making single diagnosis difficult. IMCI uses an integrated approach.

Presenting Symptoms with Overlapping Diagnosis
Presenting Complaints Possible Cause or Associated Condition
Cough and/or fast breathing Pneumonia, severe anaemia, P. falciparum malaria
Lethargy / Unconsciousness Cerebral malaria, meningitis, severe dehydration, severe pneumonia
Measles rash Pneumonia, diarrhoea, ear infection
Very sick young child with fever Pneumonia, sepsis, meningitis

Interventions

Promotion & Prevention Curative Care (Response to Sickness)
Nutrition improvement, Insecticide bed nets Early recognition, early case management, compliance
Vaccination Case management of ARI, diarrhoea, measles, malaria, malnutrition
Micronutrient supplementation Complementary feeding counselling, Iron/antihelminthic treatment

Benefits of IMCI

  • Accurate identification of illnesses in outpatient settings.
  • Appropriate combined treatment of major illnesses.
  • Strengthened counselling for relatives.
  • Accelerated referral of severely ill children.
  • Promotion of appropriate care-seeking behaviours at home.
