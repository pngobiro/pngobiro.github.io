You are already conversant with the various types of foods used in Kenyan society and their nutritive value. However different they may be, the common denominator is that they provide us with all the nutrients we need for our bodily functions. Nutrition plays a vital role in growth, development, and general health maintenance.

Food Nutrients and Their Functions

‚ñ∂ 1. Proteins

Supply amino acids for growth and repair of body tissue. Essential for immunity, blood clotting, transport, and regulation of osmotic pressure.

  • Animal: Meat, Chicken, Fish, Milk, Eggs, Cheese
  • Plant: Peas, Groundnuts, Beans, Fruits, Lentils, Vegetables
‚ñ∂ 2. Carbohydrates

Main energy source (Glucose). Important for brain and nerve function. Includes sugars, starch, and polysaccharides.

Sources: Potatoes, rice, ugali, fruits, milk, cereals, vegetables.

‚ñ∂ 3. Fats

Body's largest energy store. Provides support/protection for organs (kidneys), forms cholesterol/steroid hormones, and acts as vehicle for fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K).

Sources: Fatty meat, butter, milk, vegetable oils.

‚ñ∂ 4. Vitamins
  • Water Soluble: B complex, C.
  • Fat Soluble: A, D, E, K. (Stored in liver/adipose tissue).
‚ñ∂ 5. Mineral Salts & Water

Minerals: Iron (prevents anaemia), Zinc (growth/healing), Iodine (prevents goitre), Calcium, Sodium, Potassium.

Water: Major component of the body. Solvent for cellular changes, transport medium, temperature regulation.

Clinical Forms of PEM

Feature Kwashiorkor Marasmus
Deficiency Protein (relative to calories) Calories/Total Food (Starvation)
Age Onset 1 - 3 years (often after weaning) Any age (common in infants)
Appearance Oedema (face, feet, abdomen), "Moon face" Wasted, wrinkled, "Old man" face
Skin/Hair Hair thin/brownish, Skin changes (flaky paint) Loose, flaccid skin due to fat loss
Appetite Poor, child is miserable/apathetic Good (ravenous), child is anxious
Weight Masked by oedema < 60% of standard weight

Marasmic Kwashiorkor: A mixed condition where a child has muscle wasting (< 60% expected weight) and oedema.

üöë

Management of PEM

Immediate Requirement: Energy.

  • Oedematous (Kwashiorkor): 100 calories/kg/day.
  • Marasmic (Wasted): 150-200 calories/kg/day.

Nursing Interventions:

  1. Resuscitation: Keep warm, treat shock (IV glucose/fluids), treat infections.
  2. Feeding: Naso-gastric if necessary. Start with diluted milk products + sugar + vitamins. Gradually introduce solids.
  3. Monitoring: Fluid balance chart, urine output, daily weight.

üíä Vitamin Deficiencies

Vitamin A Deficiency

Sources: Breast milk, dark leafy vegetables, carrots, eggs, fortified margarine.

Clinical Features: Night blindness, dry conjunctiva/cornea (Xerophthalmia), Bitot's spots, Keratomalacia (irreversible corneal damage/blindness).

Treatment: 200,000 i.u. orally (Day 1, Day 2, then 1-4 weeks later). Halve dose for infants < 12 months.

Vitamin B Deficiency (B Complex)

  • B1 (Thiamine): Beriberi (muscle weakness, cardiac failure).
  • B2 (Riboflavin): Cracked lips, glossitis, stomatitis.
  • Nicotinic Acid: Pellagra.

Vitamin C Deficiency (Scurvy)

Clinical Features: Haemorrhages in skin, gums, and under periosteum of long bones (painful). Occurs 6-16 months of age.

Vitamin D Deficiency (Rickets)

Failure in mineralisation of growing bone due to lack of Vitamin D, Calcium, or Phosphorus. Osteomalacia is the adult equivalent.

Clinical Features of Rickets:

  • Delayed closure of anterior fontanelle ("Soft cranium")
  • Delayed dentition
  • Pigeon chest (sternal projection)
  • Bow legs or Knock knees
  • Widening of wrists (distal radius/ulna)

Management: Vitamin D supplements (500-50,000 I.U daily), sunlight exposure, calcium-rich diet.

üîÑ Other Metabolic Disorders

Obesity

Ingestion of more food than required. Complications include:

  • Orthopaedic: Blount's disease (tibia vara), slipped femoral epiphysis.
  • Respiratory: Sleep apnoea, hypoventilation (Pickwickian syndrome).
  • Cardiovascular: Hypertension, heart failure.
  • Metabolic: Type 2 Diabetes, gall bladder disease.

Renal Osteodystrophy

Skeletal changes found in chronic renal failure due to calcium/phosphate metabolism alteration. Low calcium stimulates parathyroid hormone, causing bone re-absorption (demineralisation).
