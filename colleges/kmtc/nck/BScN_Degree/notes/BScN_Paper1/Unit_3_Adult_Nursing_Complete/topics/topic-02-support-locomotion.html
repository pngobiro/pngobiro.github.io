The Bony Framework

Bone is a type of connective tissue made up of water (20%), organic material (30-40%), and inorganic materials (40-50%). The main inorganic components are calcium and phosphorus salts.

Bone Development & Cells

  • Osteoblasts: Cells involved in bone building. Present at centres of ossification.
  • Osteoclasts: Responsible for bone resorption (reshaping and maintaining).

The Human Skeleton

The skeleton is divided into two main parts:

  1. 1 Axial Skeleton: Skull, vertebral column, ribs, and sternum.
  2. 2 Appendicular Skeleton: Shoulder girdle, upper limbs, pelvic girdle, and lower limbs.

Joints and Movements

Joints are points where bones attach to each other. There are three main types:

Fibrous Joints
Fixed and immovable joints (e.g., sutures between skull bones).
Cartilaginous Joints
Slightly movable, enabling bending (e.g., joints between vertebrae).
Synovial Joints
Freely movable joints (diarthrosis).

Types of Synovial Joints

Ball and Socket Joint

Pivot Joint

Hinge Joint

Joint Movements

Flexion Bending forward and backward (e.g., knee joint).
Extension Straightening or bending backwards.
Abduction Movement away from the midline.
Adduction Movement towards the middle of the body.
Rotation Movement around the long axis.
Circumduction Combination of adduction, flexion, and extension.

Disorders of Bones

Spinal Deformities

Disorders occurring during growth and development or due to disease:

  • Scoliosis: Lateral deviation of the spinal column. Manifests with uneven shoulders, prominent scapula, and asymmetry of the thoracic cage.
  • Kyphosis: Exaggeration in the curvature of the thoracic spine (hunchback).
  • Lumbar Lordosis: Exaggeration in the curvature of the lumbar spine (swayback).

Metabolic Bone Disorders

‚ñ∂ Osteoporosis

Rate of bone resorption exceeds formation, leading to brittle, fragile bones. Common in elderly and post-menopausal women. Manifests with pain, spontaneous fractures, and loss of height.

Management: Calcium/Vitamin D supplements, oestrogen replacement, weight-bearing exercises, calcitonin treatment.

‚ñ∂ Osteomalacia and Rickets

Vitamin D deficiency leading to soft, deformed bones. Called Rickets in children and Osteomalacia in adults. Causes include malabsorption and lack of sunlight.

Bone Infections (Osteomyelitis)

Infection of bone tissue, usually by Staphylococcus. Can be acute or chronic.

Management Goals:

  • Pain and fever control.
  • Preventing spread of infection (vigorous antibiotic therapy).
  • Immobilisation of the affected part.
  • Surgical removal of dead bone (sequestrectomy) in chronic cases.

Principle Management of Fractures

Management falls along three main lines:

  1. 1 Reduction: Replacing bone fragments to near normal anatomical position (Open or Closed).
  2. 2 Immobilisation: Preventing movement of injured parts using splints, casts, or traction.
  3. 3 Rehabilitation: Restoration of function.

Traction

Traction involves a steady pull on a body part to reduce fracture, maintain alignment, and overcome muscle spasm. It can be skin traction or skeletal traction. Weights must hang freely.

Complications of Fractures

  • Fat embolism
  • Deep vein thrombosis (DVT)
  • Impaired healing (malunion, non-union)
  • Infection (especially in open fractures)

Disorders of Joints

Arthritis

‚ñ∂ Rheumatoid Arthritis

Systemic inflammatory disease affecting synovial joints. Characterised by morning stiffness, pain at rest, and symmetry.

‚ñ∂ Osteoarthritis (Degenerative Joint Disease)

Non-inflammatory condition characterised by degeneration of joint cartilage and formation of bone spurs (osteophytes). Pain increases with activity and weather changes.

‚ñ∂ Gout

Accumulation of uric acid crystals in joints. Manifests with sudden, severe pain, often in the big toe. Aggravated by purine-rich foods (roast meat, liver).

Amputation

Amputation is necessary for progressive vascular disease, severe trauma, or malignant tumours. Phantom pain (pain in the missing limb) is a common complication.

Post-operative Care:

  • Monitor for haemorrhage.
  • Wound drainage and dressing.
  • Stump care and shaping for prosthesis.
  • Psychological support for body image changes.

Low Back Pain

A major musculoskeletal disorder caused by lumbosacral strain, unstable ligaments, or disc problems.

Management:

  • Rest on a firm mattress (modified supine position).
  • Heat and analgesics for pain relief.
  • Pelvic traction if necessary.
  • Proper body mechanics and posture education.
