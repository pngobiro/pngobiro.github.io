Principles of Palliative Care

Palliative care is the active total care of patients whose disease is not responsive to curative treatment. It aims to achieve the best possible quality of life for patients and their families.

‚ÑπÔ∏è

WHO Definition

"The active total care of patients whose disease is not responsive to curative treatment. Control of pain, of psychological, social and spiritual problems is paramount."

Core Principles

  • Pain Relief: Ensuring death free of avoidable pain and suffering.
  • Affirming Life: Regarding dying as a normal process; neither hastening nor postponing death.
  • Holistic Care: Integrating psychological and spiritual aspects.
  • Support System: Helping the family cope with illness and bereavement.
  • Teamwork: Involving nurses, physicians, social workers, and pastoral care.
02

Stages of Dying (Kubler-Ross)

Patients react differently to terminal illness, but Dr. Elisabeth Kubler-Ross identified five common stages. Note that these do not necessarily appear in this specific order.

‚ñ∂ 1. Denial

The person acts as though nothing has happened and may refuse to believe or understand the impending loss. "No, not me."

‚ñ∂ 2. Anger

The individual resists the loss and may express anger at God, family, or healthcare providers. "Why me?"

‚ñ∂ 3. Bargaining

Attempts to postpone awareness of the loss. "God, if you give me life I will serve you better."

‚ñ∂ 4. Depression

Realization of the full impact of the loss. The person feels lonely, may withdraw, and refuse to talk.

‚ñ∂ 5. Acceptance

The loss is accepted, and the person begins to look to the future or finds peace.

03

Nursing Management

Assessment

Assessment is ongoing and includes the client, family, and significant others. Key questions:

  • "Tell me how this diagnosis makes you feel?"
  • "How has this change affected you today?"
  • "What are your feelings about your family?"

Planning & Implementation

The focus is to support the client physically, emotionally, and developmentally while maintaining dignity.

  • Therapeutic Communication: Establish a caring relationship.
  • Promoting Hope: Even if full recovery is not expected, hope for comfort and peace remains.
  • Facilitating Mourning: Allow expression of grief.
  • Symptom Management: Address specific conditions (HIV/AIDS, Cancer, Organ Failure).
04

Pain Management

Before treatment, evaluate: Location, Severity, Radiation, Type, Aggravating/Alleviating factors.

The WHO Analgesic Ladder

WHO recommends drugs be given: By Mouth (preferred route), By Clock (regular intervals), and By Ladder (step-wise approach).

ü™ú

The Ladder Steps

  • 1
    Step 1: Non-Opioids

    Paracetamol, NSAIDs (e.g., Ibuprofen, Aspirin). Used for mild pain.

  • 2
    Step 2: Mild Opioids

    Codeine, often combined with Paracetamol. Used for moderate pain.

  • 3
    Step 3: Strong Opioids

    Morphine (Oramorph, MST), Diamorphine. Used for severe pain.

üí°

Adjuvant Drugs

Adjuvant drugs (e.g., antidepressants, anticonvulsants, corticosteroids) should be given at any step to enhance analgesic efficacy or treat concurrent symptoms.
