Anatomy and Physiology

Fig 4.1: The Digestive System

The digestive system consists of the gastrointestinal (GI) tract and accessory organs. The tract is a long tube extending from the mouth to the anus.

‚ñ∂ Upper GI Tract
  • Mouth: Chewing (mastication) and mixing food with saliva (contains amylase).
  • Pharynx: Passageway connecting mouth to oesophagus; prevents aspiration via epiglottis.
  • Oesophagus: Muscular tube (~25cm) propelling food to the stomach via peristalsis.
  • Stomach: Temporary reservoir. Secretes gastric juice containing enzymes, HCL, and intrinsic factor (essential for Vit B12 absorption).
‚ñ∂ Lower GI Tract
  • Small Intestine: Comprises duodenum, jejunum, and ileum. Site of digestion and absorption via villi.
  • Large Intestine: Comprises caecum, colon (ascending, transverse, descending, sigmoid), rectum, and anus. Functions include water/electrolyte absorption, microbial activity (Vit K synthesis), and waste elimination.

Accessory Organs

The Liver

Fig 4.2: Structure of the Liver

The heaviest internal organ. It performs critical metabolic functions:

  • Carbohydrate Metabolism: Regulates blood glucose (glycogenesis/glycogenolysis).
  • Protein Metabolism: Deamination of amino acids, formation of urea, synthesis of plasma proteins and clotting factors.
  • Fat Metabolism: Desaturation of fats for energy.
  • Detoxification: Drugs and noxious substances.
  • Bile Secretion: Essential for fat digestion.

Gall Bladder

Stores and concentrates bile produced by the liver.

Pancreas

Exocrine function (enzymes) and Endocrine function (Insulin/Glucagon).

Upper GI Disorders

Oesophageal Disorders

Oesophagitis
Caused by chemical injury, infection, or reflux. Manifests with heartburn and retro-sternal pain.
Hiatus Hernia
Stomach herniates into the thoracic cavity causing reflux. Managed by small meals, upright positioning after eating, and weight reduction.

Peptic Ulcer Disease (PUD)

A break in the mucosal lining of the lower oesophagus, stomach, or duodenum exposed to acid-pepsin secretions. Associated with Helicobacter pylori infection and NSAID use.

Feature Gastric Ulcer Duodenal Ulcer
Pain Timing 1-2 hours after meals 2-4 hours after meals (often at night)
Food Effect Pain aggravated by food Pain relieved by food
Age Group Older patients Younger patients
Acid Secretion Normal or hyposecretion Hypersecretion

Lower GI Disorders

Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)

‚ñ∂ Crohn's Disease

Chronic inflammation affecting any segment of the GI tract. Transmural (affects all layers). Manifests with intermittent diarrhoea, pain, weight loss, and fistula formation.

‚ñ∂ Ulcerative Colitis

Inflammatory disease affecting the colon and rectum (mucosal layer only). Manifests with bloody diarrhoea, pus in stool, and abdominal pain.

Acute Abdomen

Group of abdominal conditions requiring prompt surgical treatment (e.g., Peritonitis, Appendicitis, Obstruction).

Management Principles:

  • NPO (Nil Per Oral)
  • Nasogastric (NG) suction
  • IV fluids and electrolyte correction
  • Antibiotics and Analgesics
  • Surgical intervention (Laparotomy)

Colostomy Care

A surgical operation in which a piece of the colon is diverted to an artificial opening in the abdominal wall so as to bypass a damaged part of the colon.

Hepatic and Biliary Disorders

Hepatitis

Inflammation of the liver caused by viruses, toxins, or alcohol.

Type Transmission Characteristics
Hepatitis A Fecal-oral route Infectious hepatitis. Incubation 15-40 days.
Hepatitis B Blood/Body fluids Serum hepatitis. Severe illness. Can lead to carcinoma/cirrhosis.
Hepatitis C Blood/Body fluids Common in IV drug users. Often becomes chronic.

Liver Cirrhosis

Chronic disease characterized by fibrosis and abnormal nodules. Causes include alcoholism and chronic hepatitis.

Complications:

  • Portal Hypertension: Increased pressure in portal vein causing oesophageal varices (bleeding risk) and caput medusae.
  • Ascites: Accumulation of fluid in the peritoneal cavity.
  • Hepatic Encephalopathy: Neuropsychiatric syndrome due to accumulation of toxins (ammonia).

Fig 4.9: Cholelithiasis (Gallstones)

Cholelithiasis
Formation of gallstones from cholesterol or bile pigments. Risk factors: Female, Obesity, Forty (Age), Fertile ("Four Fs").
Cholecystitis
Inflammation usually associated with stones. Manifests with intolerance to fatty foods, RUQ pain, and vomiting.
