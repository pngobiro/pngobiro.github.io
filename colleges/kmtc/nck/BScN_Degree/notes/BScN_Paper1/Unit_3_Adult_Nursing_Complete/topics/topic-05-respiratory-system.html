Anatomy and Physiology

üîç Click to zoom

Fig 5.1: The Respiratory System

The respiratory system is divided into the upper and lower airways. Its primary function is to facilitate the intake of air, gaseous exchange, and excretion of waste gases (carbon dioxide).

‚ñ∂ Upper Airway

Filters, moistens, and warms entering air.

  • Nose: Respiration and olfaction (smell). Includes sinuses (maxillary, frontal, sphenoidal, ethmoidal).
  • Pharynx: Passage for air and food; houses tonsils (lymphoid tissue).
  • Larynx: The voice box; protects lower airway.

Fig 5.2: The Upper Airway

‚ñ∂ Lower Airway

Enables gaseous exchange.

  • Trachea: The windpipe with C-shaped cartilages.
  • Bronchi & Bronchioles: Conducting airways leading to lobes.
  • Alveoli: Sac-like structures where gas exchange occurs (produce surfactant).
  • Lungs: Elastic organs contained within the thoracic cage, enclosed by pleura.

Lung Volumes and Capacities

üîç Click to zoom

Fig 5.3: Lung Volumes and Capacities

Tidal Volume (TV)
Amount of air that comes into and out of the lungs with each cycle of respiration (~500ml).
Expiratory Reserve Volume (ERV)
Largest additional volume of air that can be forcibly expired after a normal expiration (~1000-1200ml).
Inspiratory Reserve Volume (IRV)
Largest additional volume of air that can be forcibly inspired after a normal inspiration (~3000-3300ml).
Residual Volume (RV)
Air that cannot be forcibly expired from the lungs (~1200ml).
Vital Capacity (VC)
TV + IRV + ERV. The amount of air that can be forcibly expired after forcible inspiration.

Clinical Assessment

Common clinical features in respiratory conditions include:

Cough & Sputum

Reflex from irritation. Sputum is examined for consistency and colour (e.g., blood-stained, purulent).

Hemoptysis

Blood in sputum. Associated with TB, bronchitis, carcinoma, or embolism.

Dyspnoea

Laboured breathing caused by increased lung rigidity and airway resistance.

Cyanosis

Blue discoloration when circulating haemoglobin carries less than 2/3 of expected oxygen.

Wheeze

Associated with bronchospasms and airway constriction (e.g., Asthma).

Clubbing

Deformity of fingers resulting from hypoxic lung disease like malignancies or bronchiectasis.

Upper Respiratory Disorders

Epistaxis (Nose Bleed)

Caused by trauma, infection, tumours, or hypertension.

ü©π

First Aid Management

  1. Keep the patient quiet.
  2. Place in sitting position, leaning forward.
  3. Apply pressure by pinching the lower portion of the nose for 10-15 minutes.
  4. Apply ice if available.
  5. If bleeding persists, packing with vasoconstrictive agents or cauterisation may be needed.

Infections

Condition Description Management
Rhinitis Inflammation of nasal mucosa (allergic or infective). Antihistamines, decongestants, avoid allergens.
Sinusitis Blockage of sinus ostia causing infection/inflammation. Antibiotics (e.g., Amoxil), steam inhalation, analgesics, drainage.
Pharyngitis Inflammation of pharynx. Sore throat. Fluids, saline gargles, antibiotics if bacterial (Strep).
Laryngitis Inflammation of larynx. Hoarseness/loss of voice. Voice rest, steam inhalation, fluids, avoid irritants (smoking).

Airway Obstruction

Blockage of air passage. Causes include oedema, foreign body, or tumour.

Lower Respiratory Disorders

Bronchial Asthma

Intermittent, reversible, obstructive airway disease characterized by bronchospasm, mucosal oedema, and increased secretions.

Types

  • Extrinsic: Allergic history (pollen, food). Common in children.
  • Intrinsic: Not allergen-related. Triggered by cold, exercise, stress.
  • Mixed: Most common form.

Status Asthmaticus

Severe asthma unresponsive to conventional therapy lasting >24 hours. A medical emergency leading to respiratory acidosis.

Management: IV fluids, humidified oxygen, IV Aminophylline, Corticosteroids, Nebulisation.

Nursing Management

  • Position: Sitting up (High Fowler's) to facilitate breathing.
  • Oxygen: Low humidified oxygen to correct hypoxia.
  • Medication: Bronchodilators, mucolytics, antibiotics (if infection).
  • Education: Identify triggers, proper inhaler use, breathing exercises.

Pneumonia

Acute respiratory illness causing inflammation and consolidation of lung tissue. Can be bacterial (Streptococcus pneumoniae) or viral.

Pulmonary Tuberculosis (TB)

Caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis (acid-fast bacilli). Airborne transmission.

Clinical Features: Persistent cough (>4 weeks), unexplained weight loss, night sweats, haemoptysis, chest pain.

Management Goals:

  • Compliance with therapeutic regimen (DOTS).
  • Prevention of spread (isolation precautions initially).
  • Nutritional support.

Other Conditions

Emphysema
Irreversible distention of air spaces with destruction of alveolar walls. Causes: Smoking, chronic bronchitis. Management: Bronchodilators, breathing retraining (pursed-lip), cessation of smoking.
Empyema
Collection of pus in the pleural cavity. Managed by drainage (needle aspiration or chest tube), antibiotics, and breathing exercises.
Pleural Effusion
Accumulation of fluid (blood, serous, pus) in pleural space. Compression of lung causes dyspnoea and dull percussion note.

Respiratory Procedures

Underwater Seal Drainage (Chest Tube)

Used to drain fluid or air from the pleural space (Pneumothorax, Haemothorax) while preventing backflow.

Nursing Care Checklist

  • ‚úì Position: Keep drainage bottle below patient level.
  • ‚úì Patency: Check for oscillation/fluctuation in the tube. Bubbling indicates air drainage.
  • ‚úì Tubing: Keep straight, avoid kinks. Ensure connections are tight.
  • ‚úì Safety: Have clamps ready. Do not clamp routinely unless changing bottle or checking for leak.
  • ‚úì Monitoring: Mark drainage level. Observe respiratory status.

Tracheostomy Care

Surgical opening into the trachea to bypass obstruction, remove secretions, or permit long-term ventilation.

Care Priorities:

  • Prevent dislodgment (secure ties).
  • Suctioning (aseptic technique) to maintain patency.
  • Humidification of air.
  • Stoma care to prevent infection.
  • Communication methods for the patient.
