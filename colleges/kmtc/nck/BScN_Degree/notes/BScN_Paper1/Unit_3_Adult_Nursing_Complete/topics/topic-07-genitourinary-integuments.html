Anatomy of the Urinary System

Fig 7.1: The Urinary System Overview

The Kidney

Bean-shaped organs located behind the peritoneum (retroperitoneal). Structural parts include the Cortex (outer), Medulla (inner, containing pyramids), and Pelvis (funnel-shaped collection area).

Fig 7.2: Gross Structure of the Kidney

The Nephron

The functional unit of the kidney (>1 million per kidney). Consists of:

  • Glomerulus: Capillary network for filtration.
  • Bowman's Capsule: Surrounds the glomerulus.
  • Tubules: Proximal Convoluted Tubule, Loop of Henle, Distal Convoluted Tubule, Collecting Duct.

Fig 7.3: Microscopic Structure of the Nephron

Urine Formation

Three phases process plasma into urine:

  1. 1 Simple Filtration: Occurs in the glomerulus. Blood pressure forces water and small solutes into Bowman's capsule. GFR is approx. 125ml/min.
  2. 2 Selective Reabsorption: Occurs in tubules. Essential substances (water, glucose, amino acids, electrolytes) are reabsorbed back into blood.
  3. 3 Secretion: Substances (drugs, excess ions) are moved from blood into tubules for excretion.

Urine Composition

Water (96%), Urea (2%), Uric acid, Creatinine, Electrolytes (Na, K, Cl). Normal output: 1000-1500ml/day.

Lower Urinary Tract

Ureters
Propel urine via peristalsis to the bladder. Entry at an oblique angle prevents reflux.
Bladder
Reservoir. Made of smooth detrusor muscle. Capacity varies (600-1000ml).
Urethra
Passage for urine. In males, also passage for semen. Controlled by internal (involuntary) and external (voluntary) sphincters.

Renal Disorders

Urinary Tract Obstruction

Blockage leading to urine stasis, infection, and potential kidney damage. Causes include calculi, tumours, BPH, and strictures.

  • Hydronephrosis: Dilation of renal pelvis.
  • Hydroureter: Dilation of ureter.

Renal Calculi (Stones)

Formed from calcium, magnesium, uric acid, or cysteine. Caused by dehydration, infection, immobility, or high purine diet.

Management: Hydration (>3L/day), pain relief, dietary changes, lithotripsy, or surgery.

Urinary Tract Infections (UTI)

Condition Site Features
Cystitis Bladder Frequency, urgency, dysuria, suprapubic pain. Common in females.
Pyelonephritis Kidney Fever, chills, flank pain, malaise. Can lead to renal damage.
Urethritis Urethra Discharge, itching, dysuria. Can be sexually transmitted.

Acute Renal Failure (ARF)

Rapid decline in renal function with azotemia (high BUN/Creatinine) and oliguria (<400ml/day).

Causes Classification

  • Pre-renal: Reduced blood flow (Hypovolaemia, Shock, Heart Failure).
  • Intra-renal: Direct damage (Nephrotoxins, Glomerulonephritis).
  • Post-renal: Obstruction (Calculi, BPH, Tumours).

Phases

  1. 1 Oliguric Phase: Urine <400ml/day. Danger of hyperkalaemia and fluid overload.
  2. 2 Diuretic Phase: Urine output increases (1-3L/day). Danger of dehydration and electrolyte loss.
  3. 3 Recovery Phase: GFR rises, BUN stabilizes. Can take 3-12 months.

Chronic Renal Failure (CRF)

Progressive, irreversible destruction of nephrons. Symptoms appear when >70% function is lost.

Management Principles

  • Diet: Restrict protein, sodium, potassium, and phosphate. High calorie.
  • Fluid: Restricted based on output.
  • Drugs: Antihypertensives, Diuretics, Erythropoietin/Iron for anaemia. Avoid nephrotoxic drugs (NSAIDs).
  • Dialysis: Haemodialysis or Peritoneal dialysis when conservative management fails.
03

Bladder & Reproductive Associated Disorders

Bladder Cancer

Most common urinary system tumour. Risk factors: Smoking, industrial chemicals (rubber, dye), chronic schistosomiasis.

Clinical Feature: Painless haematuria is the hallmark sign.

Management: Transurethral resection (TURBT), Cystectomy (partial/radical), Chemotherapy, Immunotherapy (BCG).

Benign Prostatic Hypertrophy (BPH)

Non-malignant enlargement of the prostate gland compressing the urethra. Common in men >40 years.

Symptoms: Frequency, urgency, nocturia, weak stream, hesitance.

Fig 7.7: Normal vs. Enlarged Prostate

Management: Prostatectomy (TURP). Post-op care involves continuous bladder irrigation to prevent clot retention.

Prostate Cancer

Most common cancer in men. Asymptomatic early on; late signs include urinary obstruction, bone pain (metastasis), weight loss.

Screening: Digital Rectal Exam (DRE), PSA (Prostate Specific Antigen).

Testicular Cancer

Common in young men (20-35 yrs). Associated with cryptorchidism (undescended testes). Manifests as a painless hard lump.

The Integumentary System

Skin Structure

Fig 7.8: Anatomy of the Skin

  • Epidermis: Outer layer, stratified epithelium. Avascular.
  • Dermis: Middle layer. Connective tissue, blood vessels, nerves, glands, hair follicles.
  • Subcutaneous: Adipose tissue for insulation.

Functions: Protection, Vitamin D synthesis, Temperature regulation, Excretion, Sensation.

Common Skin Disorders

Eczema (Dermatitis)

  • Contact Dermatitis: Reaction to irritants (chemicals) or allergens (nickel, poison ivy).
  • Atopic Dermatitis: Chronic inflammatory condition linked to asthma/hay fever. Intense pruritus.

Psoriasis

Chronic autoimmune disease causing rapid skin cell turnover. Manifests as silvery scales on red plaques (knees, elbows, scalp).

Tumours

  • Benign: Keloids, Cysts.
  • Malignant: Basal Cell Carcinoma, Melanoma, Kaposi's Sarcoma (associated with HIV).

Management of Burns

Types of Burns

  • Thermal: Heat, scalds, flame.
  • Chemical: Acids, alkalis. (Flush immediately with water).
  • Electrical: Current passes through body. Internal damage often exceeds external. Monitor heart (arrhythmias).
  • Inhalation: Smoke/heat damage to airway. Emergency intubation may be needed.

Fluid Resuscitation (Parkland Formula)

Administration Rule:

  • Give 50% of total in the first 8 hours.
  • Give remaining 50% over the next 16 hours.
  • Fluid of choice: Ringer's Lactate.

Phases of Burn Care

  1. 1 Emergency Phase: Stop burning process, Airway/Breathing/Circulation, Fluid resuscitation.
  2. 2 Acute Phase: Wound care (debridement, grafting), Infection control, Nutrition (high calorie/protein), Pain management.
  3. 3 Rehabilitation Phase: Physical therapy, prevention of contractures, psychosocial support.
