Composition and Function of Blood

Blood is a connective tissue comprising plasma (90% water with dissolved proteins, salts, nutrients) and cellular components.

Cell Type Name Function
Red Blood Cells Erythrocytes Transport gases (Oxygen/CO2). Contain Haemoglobin. Produced via erythropoiesis (controlled by erythropoietin from kidneys). Lifespan ~120 days.
White Blood Cells Leucocytes Granulocytes (Neutrophils, Eosinophils, Basophils): Phagocytosis.
Lymphocytes (T & B): Immune response/Memory.
Monocytes: Phagocytosis.
Platelets Thrombocytes Initiate blood clotting and plug capillaries.

Blood Clotting & Grouping

Clotting involves the activation of Prothrombin to Thrombin, which acts on Fibrinogen to form Fibrin.

ABO System

  • Group A: A antigens, Anti-B antibodies.
  • Group B: B antigens, Anti-A antibodies.
  • Group AB: A & B antigens, No antibodies (Universal Recipient).
  • Group O: No antigens, Anti-A & Anti-B antibodies (Universal Donor).

Rhesus System

Presence of Rhesus antigen = Rh+. Absence = Rh-. Rh- individuals form antibodies if exposed to Rh+ blood.

The Cardiovascular System

Anatomy of the Heart

Fig 6.1: Structure of the Heart

The heart is a hollow muscular organ located in the thoracic cavity. It has three layers:

  1. 1 Endocardium: Innermost layer of epithelium.
  2. 2 Myocardium: Middle layer of specialised cardiac muscle.
  3. 3 Pericardium: Outer sac (Fibrous outer, Serous inner).

Circulation Path: Vena Cavae ‚Üí Right Atrium ‚Üí Tricuspid Valve ‚Üí Right Ventricle ‚Üí Pulmonary Valve ‚Üí Pulmonary Artery ‚Üí Lungs ‚Üí Pulmonary Veins ‚Üí Left Atrium ‚Üí Mitral Valve ‚Üí Left Ventricle ‚Üí Aortic Valve ‚Üí Aorta ‚Üí Body.

Conducting System & Cardiac Cycle

Fig 6.2: Conducting System of the Heart

  • SA Node: Pacemaker, initiates impulse.
  • AV Node: Delays impulse to allow atrial contraction.
  • Bundle of His: Transmits to ventricles.
  • Purkinje Fibres: Stimulates ventricular contraction.

Heart Sounds

Lub: Closure of Atrioventricular (AV) valves (systole).
Dub: Closure of Semilunar valves (diastole).

Blood Pressure and Pulse

Pulse: Palpable wave of distension. Normal rate: 60-100 bpm.

Blood Pressure: Force of blood against vessel walls.

Circulation & Lymphatic System

Structure of Circulation

Fig 6.3: The Aorta

Fig 6.4: Circle of Willis (Brain Supply)

Arteries: Carry blood away from heart (elastic).
Veins: Carry blood to heart (have valves).
Capillaries: Site of exchange.

Lymphatic System

Transports proteins/fats from GI tract and returns excess interstitial fluid to blood. Components:

  • Lymph Fluid: Pale yellow fluid.
  • Lymph Nodes: Filter bacteria/foreign particles.
  • Spleen: Immunity, phagocytosis, blood storage, erythropoiesis.
  • Thymus: T-lymphocyte maturation.

Cardiac Disorders

Inflammatory Heart Disease

‚ñ∂ Pericarditis

Inflammation of the pericardium. Causes: Infection, trauma, autoimmune. Symptoms: Chest pain, friction rub. Complication: Cardiac Tamponade (compression of heart by fluid).

‚ñ∂ Endocarditis

Inflammation of the inner layer (valves). Infective (bacterial/fungal) or Non-infective (Rheumatic fever). Symptoms: Fever, murmurs.

‚ñ∂ Rheumatic Heart Disease

Autoimmune reaction to Streptococcus pyogenes throat infection. Damages heart valves. Management: Benzathine penicillin prophylaxis.

Coronary Heart Disease (CHD)

Impaired blood flow to myocardium leading to ischemia. Risk factors: Hypertension, Smoking, Obesity, Diabetes, High Cholesterol.

Myocardial Infarction (Heart Attack)

Destruction of myocardial tissue due to occlusion.

Clinical Features & Management

Signs: Severe crushing chest pain radiating to arm/jaw, anxiety, sweating, nausea, weak pulse.

Management (MONA): Morphine (pain/anxiety), Oxygen, Nitroglycerin (vasodilation), Aspirin (anti-platelet). Also thrombolytics, beta-blockers, bed rest.

Heart Failure

Inability of the heart to pump enough blood.

Type Pathophysiology Signs/Symptoms
Right-Sided Congestion in systemic circulation/viscera. Oedema (pitting), Hepatomegaly, Ascites, Distended Jugular Veins.
Left-Sided Congestion in pulmonary circulation. Pulmonary Oedema, Dyspnoea, Orthopnoea, Cyanosis, Cough.

Management: Improve oxygenation (sit up, O2), Digoxin (increase force), Diuretics (reduce fluid), Vasodilators.

Arrhythmias & Shock

  • Atrial Fibrillation: Uncoordinated atrial quivers. Risk of clots.
  • Ventricular Fibrillation: Fatal rapid uncoordinated twitching. No cardiac output. Requires Defibrillation.

Types of Shock

  • Hypovolaemic: Volume loss (bleeding, dehydration).
  • Cardiogenic: Pump failure (MI).
  • Neurogenic: Vasodilation (spinal injury).
  • Septic: Infection toxins (vasodilation).

Vascular and Blood Disorders

Vascular Disorders

Atheroma
Accumulation of cholesterol in vessel walls. Precursor to thrombosis and aneurysms.
Arteriosclerosis
Loss of elasticity and hardening of vessel walls. Leads to hypertension.
Embolism
Blockage by clot, fat, or air moving through bloodstream. Pulmonary Embolism is life-threatening.
Aneurysm
Out-pouching of artery (usually Aorta). Risk of rupture and massive haemorrhage.
Raynaud's Disease
Severe vasoconstriction of fingers/toes due to cold/stress.

Blood Disorders

Anaemia

Low RBC/Haemoglobin count.

  • Sickle Cell: Genetic. HbS causes rigid sickle shape, blocking vessels (crisis) and haemolysis.
  • Iron Deficiency: Nutritional/Blood loss.
  • Megaloblastic: Vitamin B12/Folic acid deficiency.
  • Aplastic: Bone marrow failure.

Coagulation Disorders

  • DIC (Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation): Widespread clotting depleting factors, leading to massive bleeding.
  • Haemophilia: Genetic deficiency of Factor VIII (A) or IX (B).

Transfusion Reaction Protocol

  1. STOP the transfusion immediately.
  2. Maintain IV line with saline.
  3. Notify physician and blood bank.
  4. Re-check tags and patient ID.
  5. Monitor vital signs and urine output.
  6. Save blood bag for analysis.
