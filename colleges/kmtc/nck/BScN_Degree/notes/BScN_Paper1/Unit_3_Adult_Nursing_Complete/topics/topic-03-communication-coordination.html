Introduction

Body systems do not work in isolation‚Äîtheir functions are interrelated and integrated. The coordination of all these functions is carried out by the nervous and endocrine systems.

This section covers:

  • Structure and function of the nervous system.
  • Disorders of the nervous system (e.g., Stroke, Epilepsy).
  • Special senses (Eyes and Ears) and their disorders.
  • Endocrine glands, hormones, and disorders (e.g., Diabetes Mellitus).

The Nervous System

Structure

The nervous system is divided into:

  • Central Nervous System (CNS): Brain and spinal cord.
  • Peripheral Nervous System (PNS): Cranial nerves, spinal nerves, and autonomic nervous system.
üîç Click to zoom

Fig 3.1: Structure of a Neuron

The functional unit is the neuron. Neurons transmit information via nerve impulses. They connect at a synapse using neurotransmitters like Acetylcholine and Noradrenaline.

Protection of the CNS

The brain and spinal cord are covered by membranes called meninges:

  1. 1 Dura Mater: Outermost layer.
  2. 2 Arachnoid Mater: Middle layer.
  3. 3 Pia Mater: Innermost layer.

Cerebrospinal Fluid (CSF) flows in the subarachnoid space, acting as a shock absorber and facilitating nutrient exchange.

Fig 3.2: Flow of Cerebrospinal Fluid

Parts of the Brain

Part Function
Cerebrum Mental activity (memory, intelligence), sensory perception, voluntary movement control. Largest part.
Cerebellum Coordination of voluntary muscle movement, posture, and balance.
Medulla Oblongata Vital centres: Respiratory, cardiac, vasomotor, and reflex centres (vomiting, coughing).
Hypothalamus Temperature regulation, control of the pituitary gland.
Brain Stem Connects brain to spinal cord; contains reticular formation (arousal, consciousness).

Reflex Action

The spinal cord contains connector neurons that transmit impulses directly from sensory to motor nerves, creating a reflex arc (e.g., withdrawing hand from heat).

Disorders of the Nervous System

Increased Intracranial Pressure (ICP)

Caused by cerebral oedema, hydrocephalus, or space-occupying lesions (tumours, haematomas). It is life-threatening.

Clinical Manifestations of Increased ICP

  • Severe headache
  • Projectile vomiting
  • Papilloedema (swelling of optic disc)
  • Decreased level of consciousness
  • Cushing's Triad: Hypertension, bradycardia, irregular respirations

Stroke (Cerebrovascular Accident - CVA)

A sudden loss of brain function due to disruption of blood supply. Can be ischemic (thrombotic/embolic) or haemorrhagic.

Clinical Features

  • Hemiplegia: Paralysis on the side of the body opposite to the brain lesion.
  • Aphasia: Defects in speech (expressive or receptive).
  • Visual disturbances: e.g., temporary blindness.
  • Confusion and loss of consciousness.

Epilepsy

Recurrent, unprovoked seizures. Can be generalized (Grand Mal, Petit Mal) or partial (Focal).

‚ñ∂ Grand Mal Seizures (Tonic-Clonic)
  1. Aura: Warning sign.
  2. Tonic Phase: Generalised muscle contraction/stiffness.
  3. Clonic Phase: Jerky movements.
  4. Post-ictal Phase: Relaxation, sleep, or confusion.
‚ñ∂ Status Epilepticus

Medical Emergency: Continuous seizures without recovery of consciousness. Can lead to hypoxia and brain damage.

Management: IV Diazepam, airway maintenance, oxygen, IV fluids.

Other Disorders

Meningitis
Inflammation of the meninges. Signs: Fever, severe headache, neck stiffness (nuchal rigidity), photophobia. Diagnosed via Lumbar Puncture.
Parkinson's Disease
Degeneration of basal ganglia due to dopamine deficiency. Features: Tremors ("pill-rolling"), rigidity, bradykinesia (slow movement), shuffling gait. Treated with Levodopa.

The Eye and Ear

The Eye

Common Eye Disorders

‚ñ∂ Cataract

Opacity of the lens causing gradual painless blurring of vision and glare. Treated surgically by lens extraction.

‚ñ∂ Glaucoma

Increased intraocular pressure (IOP) damaging the optic nerve. Causes tunnel vision and blindness if untreated. Acute angle-closure glaucoma is a medical emergency with severe pain and halos around lights.

‚ñ∂ Conjunctivitis

Inflammation of the conjunctiva ("Pink Eye"). Redness, discharge, itching. Can be bacterial, viral, or allergic.

The Ear

Organ of hearing and balance. Divided into External, Middle, and Inner Ear.

Disorders

  • Otitis Media: Infection of the middle ear. Common in children. Causes pain, fever, and bulging tympanic membrane. Complications: Mastoiditis, perforation.
  • Meniere's Disease: Inner ear disorder causing the triad of vertigo, tinnitus, and sensorineural hearing loss.

The Endocrine System

The endocrine system consists of ductless glands that secrete hormones directly into the bloodstream to regulate body functions.

Fig 3.5: Major Endocrine Glands

Key Glands and Hormones

Gland Hormones Main Functions
Pituitary (Anterior) GH, TSH, ACTH, FSH, LH, Prolactin Growth, metabolism, reproduction ("Master Gland").
Pituitary (Posterior) ADH, Oxytocin Water balance, uterine contraction.
Thyroid Thyroxine (T4), T3, Calcitonin Metabolic rate, calcium regulation.
Parathyroid Parathormone (PTH) Increases blood calcium levels.
Adrenal Cortex Cortisol, Aldosterone Stress response, sodium/potassium balance.
Pancreas Insulin, Glucagon Blood glucose regulation.

Diabetes Mellitus

A group of metabolic diseases characterised by hyperglycemia resulting from defects in insulin secretion, insulin action, or both.

Type I (IDDM)

Autoimmune destruction of pancreatic beta cells. Absolute lack of insulin. Requires lifelong insulin therapy.

Type II (NIDDM)

Insulin resistance or relative insulin deficiency. Often associated with obesity. Managed with diet, exercise, and oral hypoglycaemics.

Management Goals

  • Maintenance of near-normal blood glucose levels.
  • Prevention of acute complications (hypoglycaemia, DKA).
  • Prevention of long-term complications (neuropathy, retinopathy, nephropathy).

Other Endocrine Disorders

Hyperthyroidism (Thyrotoxicosis)
Excess thyroid hormone. Symptoms: Weight loss, heat intolerance, tachycardia, anxiety, exophthalmos (bulging eyes). Common cause: Graves' disease.
Hypothyroidism (Myxoedema)
Deficiency of thyroid hormone. Symptoms: Weight gain, cold intolerance, fatigue, dry skin. Cretinism in children.
Cushing's Syndrome
Excess corticosteroids. Symptoms: "Moon face", "Buffalo hump", truncal obesity, thin skin, hypertension.
Addison's Disease
Hypofunction of adrenal cortex. Symptoms: Weakness, hyperpigmentation, hypotension, hyperkalaemia.
