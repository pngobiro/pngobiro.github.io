Introduction to Adult Nursing

Unit three part one of the general nursing module, describes the conditions and diseases of the adult that affect a number of the bodies systems.

Nursing of the adult requires an in depth knowledge of each condition. This units' role is to assist you in understanding and describing each condition of the systems, following the format of:

  • Anatomy and physiology
  • Definition
  • Causes (where applicable)
  • Pathophysiology
  • Clinical features
  • Diagnosis and investigations
  • Management (using the nursing process)
  • Complications

Recommended Reading

Although there is no single book covering all materials in this unit, consider reading Anatomy and Physiology in Health by Wilson Kathleen and Anne Waugh.

Adult nursing is the care of those aged above 12 years and suffering from general medical and surgical conditions. In order to function better as an adult nurse, you need to understand how the human body works.

The IMAI Approach

There is a very important concept, which is recommended for you to apply to the management of your patients: the Integrated Management of Adolescent and Adult Illness (IMAI).

IMAI is a health care strategy that addresses the overall health care of the patient. It focuses on the main clinical conditions that account for most deaths among adolescents and adults. It integrates the prevention of illnesses and the care into a single health care package.

02

Structure and Function of Cells

The human body is made up of cells, which form the basic units of tissues, organs and systems. All cells in the body have a cell membrane, which forms the boundary of the cell. The cell is made of protoplasm, which can be divided into two:

  1. 1 Cytoplasm: A gel-like substance in which many processes take place.
  2. 2 Nucleus: Responsible for cell coordination and cell division. It is made up of protein granules and is surrounded by a membrane.

Cell Components

Component Function
Nucleic Acids RNA: Found in the nucleus and cytoplasm.
DNA: Found exclusively in the chromosomes in the nucleus.
Mitochondria Rod-shaped structures that are the factories for production of energy.
Golgibody Folded tubes involved in protein and carbohydrate processing and transfer.
Endoplasmic Reticulum Network of tubes responsible for the manufacture of enzymes.
Lysosomes Structures that form the digestive system of the cell.

Cell Transport Mechanisms

The normal transfer of a substance from cell to cell occurs across the cell membrane through the following processes:

Diffusion
The process in which dissolved substances move across cells following a concentration gradient so that they balance on both sides of that gradient.
Osmosis
The process which involves the movement of solute materials from a region of higher concentration to that of lower concentration via a semi-permeable membrane.
Active Transport
A process where energy is utilised to move substances in any direction.
03

Tissues and Organs

Tissues are made up of large numbers of cells. There are four key types of tissues in the human body:

‚ñ∂ Epithelial Tissues

Epithelial tissues cover and protect body parts. They also produce secretions. The epithelium covers such areas as the stomach, nose, throat and many other areas. Types include squamous, columnar, cuboidal, ciliated, and transitional epithelium.

‚ñ∂ Connective Tissue

This is the tissue that supports body structures and is found in every part of the body. It may be loosely structured, densely structured or fatty. Cells include fibroblasts, macrophages, fat cells and mast cells. Bone and cartilage are densely structured connective tissues.

‚ñ∂ Muscle Tissue

Makes up the red part of flesh. It is capable of stretching and contracting. Types include:

  • Skeletal: Voluntary or involuntary.
  • Cardiac: Found exclusively in the heart.
  • Visceral: Makes up internal organs and walls of blood vessels.
‚ñ∂ Nerve Tissues

This is a type of tissue that is excitable and conducts impulses. It makes up the nerves.

04

Body Fluids and Electrolytes

Water makes up about 60% of the weight of an individual. It protects cells, regulates body temperature, maintains intracellular pressure, and acts as a medium of excretion.

Fluid in the body is divided into:

  • Intracellular fluid (ICF): Inside the cell.
  • Extracellular fluid (ECF): Outside the cell.

Important Electrolytes

Electrolyte Function Food Sources
Sodium (Na+) Nerve conduction, muscle contraction, acid-base balance (most abundant in ECF). Processed foods, snacks, smoked foods, table salt.
Potassium (K+) Nerve conduction, muscle strength (most abundant in ICF). Ripe bananas, avocados, oranges, potatoes, dates.
Calcium (Ca++) Bone/teeth structure, nerve/muscle function, blood clotting. Grains, legumes, leafy vegetables.
Magnesium (Mg++) Bone constituent, energy metabolism. Cocoa, seafood, dried beans, peas.

Fluid and Electrolyte Imbalance

Imbalances occur in most major illnesses. They can be deficits or excesses.

‚ñ∂ Fluid Volume Deficit

Occurs when fluid loss exceeds intake. Patients present with dehydration.

  • Inadequate Intake: Inability to perceive thirst, oral trauma, withholding fluids.
  • Excessive Loss: Vomiting, diarrhoea, suction, diuretic drugs, burns, fever.
  • Symptoms: Acute weight loss, increased thirst, decreased urine output, tachycardia, hypotension, sunken eyes, dry skin.

Temperature & Dehydration

Patients with dehydration suffer from high temperature because one of the functions of water is to act as a coolant. When fluid is lost, the cooling mechanism is affected.

‚ñ∂ Fluid Volume Excess

Caused by high sodium/water intake or inadequate renal losses (renal disease, heart failure). Symptoms include acute weight gain, pitting oedema, and pulmonary oedema.

05

Acid-Base Balance

The balance between acids and bases must be maintained for body processes. An acid produces hydrogen ions; a base reacts with acid to form salt and water.

Condition Description Common Causes Signs/Symptoms
Respiratory Acidosis Excess carbonic acid (pH < 7.5). Inadequate ventilation, asthma, respiratory obstruction. Cyanosis, tachycardia, disorientation.
Metabolic Acidosis Deficit of bicarbonate ion. Starvation (increased ketones), diarrhoea. Headache, weakness, deep laboured breathing.
Respiratory Alkalosis Carbonic acid deficit (pH > 7.5). Hyperventilation (anxiety, pain, hysteria). Deep rapid breathing, tingling, tinnitus.
Metabolic Alkalosis Excess bicarbonate ion. Vomiting, gastric lavage, excess sodium bicarbonate. Tetany, tingling, tremors, dizziness.
06

Infections and Immune Response

The body must constantly fight against aggressors (micro-organisms). Those that are harmful are called pathogens.

Types of Infectious Agents

  • Viruses: Small intracellular organisms containing DNA or RNA (e.g., HIV, Herpes).
  • Bacteria: Unicellular organisms. Classified by shape (cocci, bacilli) and staining (gram positive/negative).
  • Spirochaetes: Move in corkscrew fashion (e.g., Treponema pallidum causing syphilis).
  • Fungi: Yeasts and moulds (e.g., Candidiasis).
  • Parasites: Protozoa (Malaria), helminths, arthropods.

Malaria

Caused by Plasmodium (commonly P. falciparum in Kenya). Transmitted by female Anopheles mosquito.

HIV and AIDS Management

The management of a HIV-infected patient focuses on:

  1. 1 Monitoring disease progression and immune function.
  2. 2 Initiating and monitoring antiretroviral therapy (ARVs like Nevirapine, AZT).
  3. 3 Detecting and treating opportunistic infections (Pneumonia, Candidiasis, TB).
  4. 4 Patient education on transmission and prevention (ABCD approach).
