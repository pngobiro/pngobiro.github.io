ü©∫ Overview & Needs

In the management of critically ill patients, your emphasis will be on those actions that aim at sustaining life through assisting the failing body systems/organs to regain their functions. This includes physical and psychological comfort, as well as social aspects of care.

General Needs

  • Physical Needs: Leading to psychological needs.
  • Family Needs: Individual needs create family needs, leading to community needs.
  • Cultural Framework: Care must be within the accepted cultural and legal framework of the society.
02

üí§ Care of the Unconscious Patient

The unconscious patient is highly dependent. Management focuses on supporting body functions until consciousness is regained.

‚ñ∂ Management Pathway Steps
  • Airway Care: Recovery position, suctioning, oxygen, intubation if needed.
  • Respiration Monitoring: Detect complications like increased rate or abnormal sounds.
  • Circulation: Maintain blood volume, monitor BP/Pulse/ECG, physiotherapy.
  • Nutrition: Nasal gastric tube or parenteral feeds; fluid/electrolyte balance.
  • Skin Integrity: Daily bathing, 2-hourly turning, clean dry linen.
  • Elimination: Catheterisation if needed, monitoring output.
  • Psychosocial: Address patient by name, inform of actions, involve relatives.
03

üî• Management of Burns

Accurate evaluation includes age, extent/depth, inhalation injury, associated illness, and time elapsed.

üö®

Priority Actions

  • Ensure secure airway (especially for face/neck burns).
  • Wash off chemicals with cold water immediately.
  • Remove clothing (unless adherent).
  • Cover with wet sterile pads.
  • Administer IV fluids for burns >10% (children) or >15% (adults).

Wallace's Rule of Nines

A quick guide to estimate the extent of a burn where body areas are multiples of nine.

Fluid Resuscitation (First 48 Hours)

The Parkland Formula is the standard guideline:

$$ \text{Total Fluid (24hrs)} = 4\text{ml} \times \% \text{Burn} \times \text{Body Weight (kg)} $$

Administration Rate: Give half of this total in the first 8 hours, and the remaining half over the next 16 hours.

Flow Rate (drops/min):

$$ \frac{\text{Total Fluid} \times \text{Drop Factor (e.g. 15)}}{\text{Time in Minutes (24 } \times \text{ 60)}} $$

ü´Å Respiratory & Cardiac Failure

Respiratory Failure

Defined as the inability to maintain acceptable arterial oxygen, CO2, and pH values.

Management:

  • Open airway (Head-tilt, Chin-lift, Jaw thrust).
  • Artificial airways: Oro-pharyngeal (unconscious only) or Naso-pharyngeal (semi-conscious).
  • Advanced control: Endo-tracheal intubation.

Cardiac Arrest

Diagnostic signs: Breathing stops, No palpable pulse, No heartbeat, Pupils fixed and dilated.

‚ö°

CPR Guidelines

  • Adults: Ratio 15:2 (compressions to ventilations). Rate ~80/min.
  • Children/Neonates: Ratio 5:1.
  • ACLS: Early assessment, Early CPR, Early defibrillation, Early access to advanced care.
06

üíß Renal Failure

Acute Renal Failure (ARF): Abrupt cessation of function. Reversible. Signs include oliguria (<400ml/day) and rising creatinine.

Chronic Renal Failure (CRF): Irreversible reduction in function.

Management Principles

  • Volume Control: Restore blood volume for ARF. Avoid fluid overload in CRF.
  • Electrolytes: Manage hyperkalemia and acidosis.
  • Dialysis: Indicated for life-threatening complications or uremic symptoms.
  • Drugs: Avoid nephrotoxins. Adjust doses for renal clearance. Treat hypertension.
