üí° Introduction & Definitions

In your practice, you might have experienced a situation where the life of a patient was lost but could have been saved had the situation been handled with a different approach. It is, therefore, important for you to learn the approaches that would increase patient survival rates by improving the care you give to support a failing biological system.

üìò

Definition of Critical Care Nursing

The Oxford Pocket Dictionary defines the word critical as 'at a crisis'. The word 'crisis' is defined in the Dorland's Pocket Medical Dictionary as: 'The turning point of a disease for better or worse, especially a sudden change'.

It follows, therefore, that critical care nursing can be defined as the nursing care given to a patient whose health is in danger or in a crisis, so as to save their life or prevent complications.

The main purpose of critical care nursing is to maintain accurate continuous observations of the patient's vital functions and to treat or support a failing or failed biological system. It focuses on the whole body system so as to maintain health.

02

ü§ï Types of Critically Ill Patients

During your practice as a nurse you will have come across a patient whose condition you would have classified as critical. Before you proceed, reflect on why you classified that patient as critical.

It is possible you may have based your judgment on the following observations:

  • The magnitude or extent of anatomical structural damage, for example, second degree burns of more than 25%.
  • Severe injuries to the head or chest.
  • The effect of the disease/condition on circulation, breathing, and electrolyte balance.
  • The organs affected by the disease, for example, cardiac arrest, respiratory failure, pulmonary distress, and renal failure.

Going by this approach, a critically ill patient is one whose physical condition, physiological and psychological state poses an immediate threat to their life.

By now you must have realised that critically ill patients need total and specialised nursing care. This care is usually provided in special facilities. However, one fact that you as a health worker must bear in mind, is that their survival rate will highly depend on your quick and accurate intervention.

üöë

ABC Principles of First Aid Care

Intervention should be prioritised on the ABC principles:

  1. Airway must be established and maintained
  2. Breathing established
  3. Circulation must be promoted and maintained

üè® Critical Care Facilities: Acute Room

Have you come across or heard about the acute room? Is it referred to as an 'acute room' because of its location, equipment, or condition of patients nursed there?

Actually, all of these factors are important. For example, the location must allow easy access to continuous monitoring of patients by the health team. Patients who are nursed here require life support equipment and continuous monitoring or observation. Hence this room must be located closer to the nursing station than others.

‚ñ∂ Essential Acute Room Equipment
  • Suction equipment: Should include suction machines and a tray containing sterile suction catheters to help clear the patient's airways.
  • Oxygen administration equipment: Fully assembled, ready for use. Includes full oxygen cylinder, gauge and mask.
  • Intravenous administration apparatus: Includes drip stands, intravenous administration sets and a stock of intravenous fluids.
  • Adequate stocks of linen: Necessary as patients nursed in this room often require frequent changing of bed linen.
  • Observation equipment: Thermometers, stethoscope, blood pressure machine, torch, diagnostic sets, etc.
‚ÑπÔ∏è

Staffing & Setup

It is recommended that a nurse should always be in the acute room, preferably at a ratio of one nurse to two patients at any given time.

An acute room can be set up in a ward where one cubicle/corner can be identified and set aside for patients needing critical care in hospitals without established intensive care units (e.g., post-operative patients first 48 hours, unconscious patients, patients under special procedures).

04

ü©∫ Intensive Care Unit (ICU)

This can be defined as a room/unit in which a critically ill patient is being actively treated as well as monitored. The purpose of intensive care is to maintain life until the precipitating causes of body failure can be identified and successfully treated to allow the system to regain self-control.

Feature Requirement
Nurse-Patient Ratio 1:1 at any given time
Bed Capacity Sizing One bed for every 50 beds in the hospital

Space Allocation Not less than two bed spaces in a general ward (for equipment)

Infrastructure Requirements

  • Location: Easily accessible to casualty, labour ward, and operating theatre.
  • Infection Control: Cubicles preferred. Measures include changing clothing, visitor control, high standard cleaning, and frequent decontamination.
  • Utilities: Ventilation/heating, piped gases (oxygen, nitrous oxide), piped vacuum, adequate lighting with emergency generator backup.
  • Support Services: 24-hour laboratory service (blood gas analysis), 24-hour technical assistance for electronics.
  • Equipment: Monitoring oscilloscopes per bed, resuscitation trolley, defibrillator, pacemaker, adjustable beds with ripple mattresses.
05

üè• Types of Intensive Care Units

General ICU

One unit admits all types of patients (adults, neonates, paediatrics, cardiac, burns). Most common in Kenya.

Coronary Care Unit

Admits only those suffering from coronary and heart related emergencies (e.g., myocardial infarction, heart surgeries).

Paediatric ICU

Admits general paediatric emergencies.

Neonatal ICU

Neonates requiring critical care are admitted and managed here.

High Dependant Unit (HDU)

Functions as a step down to ICU. Patients are nursed here after discharge from ICU before getting to general wards.

06

üî• Specialized Units: Burns & Renal

Burns Unit

Special burns centres are ideal but often located in general wards due to constraints. The unit is designed primarily to reduce the risk of infection, as burns patients have lost the skin's mechanical barrier.

‚ö†Ô∏è

Burns Unit Requirements

  • High level of cleanliness.
  • Infection control measures: visitor management, changing garments/shoes.
  • Good ventilation.
  • Staff with upper respiratory infections should avoid the unit.
  • Ideally meets all requirements of an ICU.

Renal Dialysis Unit

Designed to ensure maintenance and sustenance of life during dialysis. Features include:

  • Piped with specially treated water system (acceptable electrolyte concentration).
  • Dialysis equipment.
  • Resuscitation and monitoring equipment similar to ICU.

Note: This care can be given in a general ICU if a dialysis machine is available.
