üìã Introduction & Assessment

This section will focus on the assessment, admission and ethical/legal issues in critical care. The emphasis will be on the application of the nursing process in critical care and the need to consider the legal implications of admitting the patient to a critical care unit.

Assessment of the Critically Ill Patient

It is important to remember the need to assess the patient using a holistic approach. Margaret Ann Berry (1973), and Hudak (1982) explain that assessing and dealing only with the patient's biological, physiological and sociological needs would be inadequate. You need to look at each patient as an individual with unique needs.

The Nursing Process

In step one of the nursing process, the main objective is to assess and interpret presenting clinical signs and assign remedial and nursing interventions. For example, if a patient has respiratory distress, the remedial action could include:

  • Administration of oxygen by mask
  • Clearing the airways by suction
  • Propping up patients to ease breathing
  • Putting the patient in a recovery position to facilitate drainage of secretion if unconscious

History Taking

Attention should be focused on:

  • The onset of the problem (when and how it started).
  • Main presenting signs and aggravating factors.
  • Current medication.
  • Previous episodes relevant to the current problem.
  • Previous medical/surgical treatments.

Physical Examination

Quick appraisal of the patient's general condition is important to help you to decide where there is need for immediate life saving intervention. Each body system should be checked for impaired function.

Critical Signs to Note

  • Low blood pressure
  • Weak peripheral pulse
  • Cold extremities & peripheral cyanosis
  • Cerebral function alteration (coma)
  • Reduced urinary output
  • Dyspnoea
  • High temperature
  • Unexplained fatigue
  • Chest pain
  • Tachycardia or palpitation

üõ£Ô∏è Critical Pathways

As a nurse, you must master the fine art of selecting the appropriate critical pathway. Once the critically ill patient is identified they must be categorised into the appropriate pathophysiological state and admitted to a critical pathway, such as:

  • Trauma
  • Sepsis
  • Coma
  • Cardiac abnormalities
  • Over-dosage (poisoning)

Each critical pathway has protocols and guidelines for the care of individuals. Studies indicate that where protocols are adhered to, the mortality rate for patients tends to be low.

üè• Admission Procedure & Criteria

In critical care nursing, life-saving techniques should be administered first. Other admission protocols follow. The general procedure is to deal with the biological problem of the patient first, so as to save and sustain life, before considering any other protocols such as finances.

Admission Criteria

The patient has to meet any of the following categories to be admitted to ICU:

Categories for ICU Admission

  • 1

    Continuous Monitoring: Patients who look stable but may have life threatening complications (e.g., post myocardial infarction, post cardiac catheterisation).

  • 2

    Extensive Specialised Nursing Care: Patients needing strict fluid input administration like DKA and burns.

  • 3

    Constant Physician Care: Where doctors and nurses remain at the bedside to attend to any changes and institute therapy (e.g., after open heart surgery).

Informed Consent

Informed consent implies the right of a patient or a relative to make a choice based on clear understanding of the risks and benefits of the different alternatives available. A broader definition includes the right to refuse to have any treatment at all.

‚ñ∂ Case Studies: Futility vs. Rights

Case One: Baby K (1992)

Baby K was born with anencephaly. The mother insisted on life support contrary to medical custom. The US courts upheld the mother's right to demand treatment although the diagnosis and prognosis were not in dispute.

Case Two: Catherine Gilgum

A unilateral "do-not-resuscitate" order was written by a physician and ethics committee representative without family mediation or following the patient's previously stated wishes. This highlighted the ethical failure of unilateral decisions without bilateral consensus.

ICU Treatment Entitlement Index

Engelhardt et al (1986) suggested a formula to guide choices when resources are scarce:

The ICU Treatment Entitlement Index (ICU E.I.)

$$ \text{ICU E.I.} = \frac{P \times Q \times L}{C} $$

Where:
P = Potential benefit of treatment
Q = Quality of life expected
L = Remaining length of life
C = Cost

Conclusions on Ethics

  • Physicians/clinicians would be ethically failing if they take unilateral decisions without informing patients/relatives about treatment goals.
  • Patients and relatives must be candidly informed to ensure scarce resources are used on positive goals rather than futile ones.
  • Integration is necessary while providing feedback to avoid confusing prognostic information.
