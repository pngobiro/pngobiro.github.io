üõå Lifting and Moving Patients

Proper techniques prevent injury to both the patient and the nurse. Always plan exactly what will be done before attempting to move a patient.

‚ö†Ô∏è

Safety Guidelines

  • Know the patient's capabilities and restrictions.
  • Lock wheels of beds and wheelchairs.
  • Elevate the bed to a safe working level.
  • Observe body mechanics: Keep back straight, bend knees, use leg muscles.
  • Reduce friction: Roll or push rather than pull.

Transfer Techniques

‚ñ∂ Bed to Stretcher

The most convenient way is to place a sheet underneath the patient, then carefully pull on the sheet to slide the patient to the stretcher. For lifting, a three-carrier lift is recommended.

‚ñ∂ Bed to Chair
  1. Place chair parallel to the head of the bed.
  2. Assist patient to sitting position on edge of bed. (Wait a few seconds for dizziness to pass).
  3. Face patient, spread feet for base support, and block patient's feet with yours.
  4. Hold patient in axillary area (not chest wall).
  5. Pivot with patient and lower gently into chair.
03

üß™ Collection of Specimens

Always apply infection prevention knowledge (gloves, decontamination) when collecting fluids.

Types of Specimens

‚ñ∂ Blood Collection

Procedure for Venipuncture:

  1. Apply tourniquet and identify vein.
  2. Decontaminate skin.
  3. Insert needle, withdraw blood, release tourniquet.
  4. Apply pressure to site.
  5. Transfer blood to correct container.

Malaria Slide: Clean middle finger, prick once, wipe first drop, collect subsequent drops on slide.

‚ñ∂ Urine Collection
  • Routine: Direct voiding into clean container.
  • Midstream (C&S): Clean genital area. Pass first flow into toilet, collect middle flow in sterile bottle, finish in toilet.
  • 24-Hour: Discard first voiding (start time). Collect ALL subsequent urine for 24 hours.
‚ñ∂ Swabs & Sputum

Throat Swab: Depress tongue, say "Ah", sweep inflamed area without touching tongue.

Wound Swab: Clean wound if necessary, sweep over inflamed area/pus.

Sputum: Early morning specimen preferred. Deep cough required (not saliva).

üß† Neurological Assessment

Used to monitor progress in head injury, poisoning, or unconsciousness. Includes checking level of consciousness, pupillary reaction, and motor function.

Key Assessments

  • Consciousness: Call name ‚Üí Command (Open eyes) ‚Üí Pain Stimulus (Prick/Pinch). Record as Deeply Unconscious, Semi-conscious, or Alert.
  • Pupils: Check size, shape, equality, and reaction to light (Brisk, Sluggish, Fixed).
  • Motor Function: Hand grip strength, ability to push feet against resistance.

üíß Intravenous Fluids

Types of Fluids

  • Isotonic: 0.9% Saline, 5% Dextrose. (Equal to plasma).
  • Hypotonic: 0.45% Saline. (Dilute).
  • Hypertonic: 10% Dextrose, 3% Saline. (Concentrated).

Calculating Infusion Rates

üßÆ

Formulas

1. Calculate Volume per Hour:

$$ \text{ml per hour} = \frac{\text{Total Volume (ml)}}{\text{Total Time (hours)}} $$

2. Calculate Drops per Minute:

$$ \text{Drops per min} = \frac{\text{ml per hour} \times \text{Drop Factor}}{60 \text{ minutes}} $$

Example: 3000ml over 24 hours with drop factor 20:

$$ \frac{3000}{24} = 125 \text{ ml/hr} $$
$$ \frac{125 \times 20}{60} = 41.6 \approx 42 \text{ drops/min} $$

Complications

Infiltration: Fluid running into tissues. Signs: Swelling, coldness, pain. Action: Remove needle.

Phlebitis: Inflammation of vein. Signs: Red, warm, swollen, painful. Action: Remove needle, warm compress.

üïäÔ∏è Care for the Terminally Ill

Kubler-Ross Stages of Dying

Stage Response Typical Comment
1. Denial Disbelief "No, not me."
2. Anger Hostility "Why me?"
3. Bargaining Promises "Yes me, but... I need more time."
4. Depression Sadness/Grief "Yes me." (Quiet crying)
5. Acceptance Peace "I am ready."

Physical Care Needs

  • Nourishment: Sips of water, ice chips if swallowing reflex present.
  • Hygiene: Keep clean and dry. Oral care is crucial.
  • Positioning: Semi-sitting for dyspnea; side-lying for noisy breathing (death rattle).
  • Pain Control: Keep free from pain without dulling consciousness if possible.

Signs of Approaching Death

Loss of motion/sensation starting in legs; cold/clammy skin; cyanosis; Cheyne-Stokes respiration; irregular weak pulse; dilated pupils.
