This section covers the essential skills required to obtain a comprehensive history, perform a physical examination, interpret findings, and formulate a clinical diagnosis.

Learning Objectives

  • Obtain a comprehensive history from a patient.
  • Perform a physical examination on a patient.
  • Interpret the findings from history taking and physical examination.
  • Formulate a clinical diagnosis.

üó£Ô∏è The Health History

Ethical Considerations

‚öñÔ∏è

Patient Rights

The patient has the right to know why information is being collected and how it will be used. You must:

  • Identify yourself and your role.
  • Explain the purpose of the history.
  • Ensure confidentiality.
  • Provide a private setting for the interview.

Interviewing Techniques

Make the patient feel at ease. Use open-ended questions (e.g., "Tell me about it") to encourage spontaneous information flow. Listen actively and observe non-verbal communication. Adjust your language to the patient's level of understanding.

Content of Health History

‚ñ∂ Biographical & Informant Data

Bio Data: Name, age, sex, marital status, occupation, ethnicity, address.

Informant: Identify who is giving the history (patient, parent, relative). Assess reliability.

‚ñ∂ Chief Complaint & HPI

Chief Complaint: The main reason for seeking help ("What brings you to the clinic?").

History of Present Illness (HPI): A complete story of the problem including onset, setting, manifestations, course, and associated symptoms.

‚ñ∂ Past Medical & Family History

Past History: Childhood illnesses, injuries, hospitalizations, operations, allergies, immunizations.

Family History: Hereditary diseases (diabetes, hypertension), communicable diseases (TB), causes of death in immediate family.

ü©∫ Physical Examination

Perform assessments in a well-lit, warm area. Use the four fundamental processes: Inspection, Palpation, Percussion, and Auscultation.

1. General Assessment

Observe general state of health, vital signs (BP, Pulse, Resp, Temp), height, and weight. Check for pallor (anemia), jaundice (yellow), or cyanosis (blue).

2. Head, Neck, and Chest

  • Head: Palpate cranium for lumps.
  • Eyes: Check pupils, conjunctiva (pallor), sclera (jaundice). Test visual acuity.
  • Ears: Check canal for discharge/wax. Test hearing (whisper test).
  • Neck: Palpate lymph nodes and thyroid. Check JVP.

Percussion Notes

  • Resonance: Normal air-filled lung.
  • Hyper-resonance: Emphysema (over-inflated).
  • Dullness: Solid organ (liver) or fluid/consolidation.
  • Tympany: Air-filled stomach (drum-like).

3. Cardiovascular System

Inspect for apex beat. Auscultate heart sounds ("lub-dub"). Check for murmurs. Palpate peripheral pulses.

üåê Integrated Management (IMAI)

Concept

Integrated Management of Adolescent and Adult Illness (IMAI) focuses on the main clinical conditions causing death in adults/adolescents. It integrates prevention and care into a single package.

Benefits

  • Addresses major problems including chronic diseases (HIV/AIDS).
  • Balances prevention and care.
  • Promotes cost savings and equity.
  • Decentralizes care to the community level.
üí°

Preventive Interventions

  • Voluntary HIV counseling and testing (VCT).
  • STI counseling and safer sex education.
  • Lifestyle messages (alcohol, smoking, diet).
  • Specific interventions: Bed nets, Tetanus toxoid.
