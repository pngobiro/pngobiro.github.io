ü¶† Concept of Infectious Diseases

Over the past few decades, Kenya has experienced a drastic change in the pattern of infectious diseases. Eradicated diseases are re-emerging, and new ones are being identified (e.g., Hepatitis B and C). The situation is exacerbated by antibiotic resistance and high HIV/AIDS prevalence.

Nosocomial Infections: Hospital-acquired infections causing suffering and death worldwide. Common types include surgical wound infections, lower respiratory tract infections, and urinary tract infections.

Risk Groups

‚ö†Ô∏è

Who is at Risk?

  • Service Providers: High risk due to constant exposure to infections and invasive procedures.
  • Patients: Risk of cross-infection from other patients or health workers.
  • Community: Risk from inappropriate disposal of medical waste (contaminated sharps, dressings, etc.).

The Disease Transmission Cycle

Infectious agents survive in a reservoir, leave through a place of exit via a mode of transmission, and enter a susceptible host through a place of entry.

Micro-organisms vs. Pathogens

  • Normal Flora: Micro-organisms normally present on skin, respiratory, intestinal, and genital tracts.
  • Pathogens: Micro-organisms associated with disease not normally found on/in the body.

Breaking the Cycle: The mode of transmission is the easiest point to break the cycle via hand washing, aseptic technique, and proper instrument processing.

üõ°Ô∏è Standard Precautions

Standard precautions are clinical practice recommendations designed to minimize the risk of exposure to infectious materials. They must be applied to every patient regardless of their presumed infection status.

Hand Washing

The single most effective method of preventing transfer of micro-organisms. Essential elements: Soap, Running Water, Friction.

Type Description Use Case
Routine Plain soap and running water. Removes soil and transient micro-organisms. Before/after patient contact, routine tasks.
Antiseptic Antimicrobial soap + water. Kills/inhibits resident micro-organisms. High risk areas, before invasive procedures, immune-compromised patients.
Alcohol Rub 60-90% alcohol. Kills transient/resident organisms. When running water unavailable and hands NOT visibly soiled.
Surgical Scrub Antiseptic scrub. Prevents growth for a period of time. Before surgical procedures.
üí°

Critical Times for Hand Washing

  • Immediately after arriving at work
  • Before and after examining each patient
  • After touching mucous membranes or accidental contact with fluids
  • After removing gloves
  • After using the toilet
  • Before leaving work

Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)

‚ñ∂ Gloves

Sterile Surgical Gloves: For surgical procedures, implants, pelvic exams in labor.

Single Use Examination Gloves: For contact with mucous membranes (IUCD, drawing blood, pelvic exams).

Utility Gloves: Heavy duty for cleaning, waste disposal, and instrument processing.

‚ñ∂ Other PPE
  • Gowns/Aprons: Waterproof material. Use when splashing of fluids is anticipated (delivery, surgery).
  • Masks/Eye Shields: Protection against airborne pathogens and splashes (dental surgery, delivery).
  • Headgear/Boots: For operative procedures. Caps cover hair; boots should be waterproof.

‚öôÔ∏è Instrument Processing

Processing involves steps to reduce infection risk: Decontamination, Cleaning, and either Sterilization or High Level Disinfection (HLD).

üîç Click to zoom

Fig 1.2: The cycle of instrument processing.

1. Decontamination

Goal: Kills viruses (HIV, Hep B) and makes items safer to handle.
Method: Soak immediately after use in 0.5% Chlorine solution for 10 minutes.

üß™

Preparing Chlorine Solution

Formula for Liquid Bleach (JIK):

$$ \frac{\% \text{ Chlorine in bleach}}{\% \text{ Chlorine desired}} - 1 = \text{Parts of water per part of bleach} $$

Example: To make 0.5% from 3.5% bleach: $\frac{3.5}{0.5} - 1 = 6$ parts water to 1 part bleach.

Formula for Bleach Powder:

$$ \frac{\% \text{ Chlorine desired}}{\% \text{ Chlorine in powder}} \times 1000 = \text{Grams of powder per litre of water} $$

2. Cleaning

Mechanical removal of soil and organic material using a soft brush, detergent, and water. Crucial step: if not done, sterilization/HLD may fail as organic matter protects micro-organisms.

3. Sterilization vs. High Level Disinfection (HLD)

Feature Sterilization High Level Disinfection (HLD)
Definition Eliminates ALL micro-organisms including bacterial endospores. Eliminates all micro-organisms but NOT reliably all bacterial endospores.
Methods
  • Steam (Autoclave): 121¬∞C for 20-30 mins.
  • Dry Heat: 170¬∞C for 1 hour.
  • Chemical: Glutaraldehyde soak (10 hours).
  • Boiling: Rolling boil for 20 mins.
  • Chemical: Chlorine or Glutaraldehyde soak (20 mins).
Indication Items contacting bloodstream or tissue (scalpels, needles). Items contacting mucous membranes or broken skin (speculums).

Antiseptics vs. Disinfectants

Antiseptics: Used on skin/living tissue (e.g., Alcohol, Chlorhexidine, Iodine).

Disinfectants: Used on inanimate objects (High level: Chlorine, Glutaraldehyde; Low level: Phenols for floors/walls).

‚ñ∂ Common Antiseptics
Agent Advantages Disadvantages
Alcohol (60-90%) Rapid kill, evaporates. Drying, not for mucous membranes.
Chlorhexidine (Hibitane) Persistent effect (6 hrs). Rare irritation.
Iodophors (Betadine) Less irritating than iodine. Best for genital area. Effectiveness reduced by blood.

üóëÔ∏è Waste Disposal

Types of Waste

  • General Waste: Non-hazardous (paper, boxes).
  • Medical Waste: Infectious (blood, tissue, sharps). Use colored containers (e.g., Red).
  • Hazardous Chemical Waste: Toxic chemicals, radioactive waste.

Sharps Safety

üíâ

Handling Sharps

  • Do NOT recap needles (use "scoop" method if necessary).
  • Do NOT bend or break needles.
  • Dispose immediately in puncture-resistant containers.
  • Dispose of containers when 3/4 full.

Disposal Methods

Burning/Incineration: Preferred for medical waste to destroy micro-organisms. Open burning is not recommended but if done, must be supervised.

Burial: In a deep pit surrounded by a fence to prevent scavenging.
