<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="the-concept-of-community-diagnosis">The Concept of Community Diagnosis<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-concept-of-community-diagnosis" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Community diagnosis is a process through which health workers together with members of the community identify the community's priority health problems, and together make plans of action and implement them. It points out where the health services should put their main efforts and resources. You learnt in unit three on Primary Health Care (PHC), a community's full participation depends on the four concepts of PHC or the 4As.</p>
<p><img src="assets/images/image-20260117-52dbf936.jpeg" alt="img-0.jpeg"></p>
<p>The four A's are acceptability, accessibility, affordability and availability using appropriate and local technologies. In the past, professionals including health workers always considered themselves experts on various issues. Health workers would visit a community and without consultation, impose certain sanctions or treatments without the community's consent. Also, some communities have received assistance particularly when there is a disaster</p>

<p>and then been abandoned without being helped to address the causative factors. This has often resulted in dependency, lack of self-initiative to improve themselves or even a belief that they are helpless.</p>
<p>Luckily, this notion has changed. It is now realised that people have the ability to help themselves if they are given some facilitation or guidance. People want to be independent and self-reliant. It is also true that people tend to cherish and care for what they have acquired through a struggle. Such involvement empowers individuals and makes them resourceful and self-reliant.</p>
<p>The community diagnosis concept therefore stresses that the community must identify its problems, prioritise them and draw a plan of action to address the identified problems. The community then implements this plan to resolve the problems. It emphasises total community involvement. This is because the community knows its problems and priorities better than the health worker. When they actively participate in solving these issues they become bound by the decisions they make and feel motivated to see the plans through.</p>
<p>Sometimes, one of the problems you experience as health workers is that communities may be more concerned about water or access to markets than they are about medical problems. In such situations, you need to look at all aspects of community development, that is, adopt an intersectoral approach, so that the real needs are tackled.</p>
<p>There is no need to rush into a programme before there is understanding and commitment. This will just lead to failure. Sometimes you may even have to show your willingness and interest in what the community wants before you can concentrate on the main health problems.</p>
<p>As health workers, you talk all the time about patient diagnosis. Is this the same as community diagnosis? Move on to look at the difference between patient diagnosis and community diagnosis.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="patient-diagnosis-versus-community-diagnosis">Patient Diagnosis versus Community Diagnosis<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#patient-diagnosis-versus-community-diagnosis" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>In your basic training and in unit one of module one you learned how to diagnose a disease in a patient.</p>
<p>On a notepad write down the four basic steps you would follow when making a patient diagnosis?</p>
<p>Your answer should have included the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>Collecting basic information or history taking</li>
<li>Planning a programme or diagnosis</li>
<li>Implementation or treatment</li>
<li>Follow up and evaluation</li>
</ul>
<p>You can now look at the patient diagnosis steps in more detail:</p>
<ol>
<li>greet and welcome the patient and offer them a seat</li>
<li>Ask for the patient's name, age, sex, marital, status and patient's residential address.</li>
<li>Take history including details of the patient's progress so far.</li>
<li>Perform a physical examination.</li>
<li>Carry out or request special investigations. Make a differential diagnosis, followed by a specific diagnosis once results of investigations are confirmed. You may even state the expected outcome.</li>
<li>Prescribe the most appropriate treatment.</li>
<li>Give the patient a date to return for review. A patient with an acute condition should be hospitalised for monitoring and review.</li>
</ol>
<p>Depending on the presenting signs and symptoms, each time they are reviewed their diagnosis may change.</p>
<ol start="8">
<li>As the patient improves discharge them.</li>
</ol>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="remember">Remember:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#remember" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>People are individuals, no two people are alike. Some may have started treating themselves before coming for your help.</p>
<p>In community diagnosis, you follow the same basic steps as the ones you have seen in patient diagnosis. The only difference is that the amount of data is much greater and requires more lengthy analysis and processing.</p>
<p>In community diagnosis you start by collecting basic information. You collect information about the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>Local people and their environment</li>
<li>The number of people and their distribution</li>
<li>The diseases the local people suffer from</li>
<li>The organisation of local health services</li>
</ul>
<p>Bishop's Computers
Bishop's Computers</p>
<p>You then make a community diagnosis by identifying the main health problems and the reasons for them. Identify priority health problems and plan a community health programme or treatment to solve these problems.</p>
<p><strong>Remember:</strong>
<strong>It is important to only select priority health problems.</strong></p>
<p>This is because health centres often have limited resources and many demands on those resources. There are simply not enough resources to solve all the health problems in the community. Therefore, the health care worker together with the community must select priorities for health action. Remember, it is important to choose only those problems that the community can do something practical about with the help of the local health team (health centre or dispensary staff).</p>
<p>The final step is follow up to evaluate the programme and see if it has made the community healthier. You can evaluate an activity by counting or measuring things or simply by using your judgement. For example, to evaluate whether women are attending your antenatal clinic, you can check how many of the total number of pregnant women in your catchment area attend the clinic. You can also assess whether they are satisfied with the service by talking to women who attend the clinic and also to some who don't.</p>
<p>The tools you use in patient diagnosis are, for example:</p>
<ul>
<li>Sphygmomanometer (BP machine)</li>
<li>Stethoscope</li>
<li>Weighing scales</li>
<li>Thermometer</li>
<li>Chairs</li>
<li>Record books</li>
</ul>
<p>In community diagnosis, you use survey tools for example:</p>
<ul>
<li>Maps</li>
<li>Weighing scale</li>
<li>Specimen bottles</li>
<li>Questionnaires</li>
</ul>
<p>Now you have looked at the difference between patient diagnosis and community diagnosis. You can now consider the purpose of conducting a community diagnosis.</p>
<p><strong>Purpose of Community Diagnosis</strong></p>
<p>The main purpose of carrying out a community diagnosis is to collect information on the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>Demographic data plus all the vital health statistics</li>
<li>Utilisation of health services especially of maternal and child health clinics</li>
<li>The causes of morbidity and mortality (by age and sex)</li>
<li>State of nutrition, diet, weaning patterns and the growth of preschool and school-going children</li>
<li>Patterns of leadership and communication within the community</li>
<li>State of mental health and common causes of stress</li>
<li>State of the environment including water, housing and disease vectors</li>
<li>The community's knowledge, attitudes and practices (KAP) in relation to health-related activities</li>
<li>Epidemiological details of endemic diseases</li>
<li>Available resources and services for overall development of health-related activities for example, education, agriculture, veterinary and social services</li>
<li>Socio-cultural and socio-economic class divisions within the community (social stratification)</li>
</ul>
<p>Although a community diagnosis can yield a lot of data, it is not possible to collect it all in detail. Therefore, you need to tailor your survey to suit the specific information you want to collect.</p>
<p>You will now look at some of the terminologies used in community diagnosis.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="infant-mortality-rate">Infant Mortality Rate<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#infant-mortality-rate" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This is the probability of dying between birth and exactly one year of age expressed per 1,000 live births. It is calculated by dividing the number of infant deaths during calendar year by the number of live births in the same year.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="crude-birth-rate">Crude Birth Rate<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#crude-birth-rate" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This is the number of births per 1,000 population. It is calculated by dividing the total number of births in a year by the mid-year population.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="crude-death-rate">Crude Death Rate<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#crude-death-rate" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This is the number of deaths in one year per 1,000 population. It is also called Crude Mortality Rate. It is calculated by dividing the total number of deaths (D) by mid-year population (P) and expressed per a thousand population.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="rate-of-natural-increase">Rate of Natural Increase<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#rate-of-natural-increase" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>RNI in a country can be calculated by finding the difference between birth rate and death rate per 1,000 then expressing such a difference as a percentage. It is the percentage annual rate of population growth without regard for migration.</p>
<p>A negative number means the death rate is greater than the birth rate and so the population is decreasing.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="dependency-ratio">Dependency Ratio<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#dependency-ratio" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This is the ratio of (potentially) economically active population to the retired population and children under 18 years of age, giving a rough estimate of the number of dependants per worker. It is calculated by dividing under 18's and over 64's by 18's to 64's.</p>
<p>In addition to the terminologies listed, there are two other terminologies used in community diagnosis, namely, indicators and variables.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="indicators">Indicators<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#indicators" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Events or facts that can be measured to reflect the health status of an individual or community.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="variable">Variable<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#variable" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Characteristic within the study sample whose value changes among the study subjects. It is an observation made on the study subjects. Often two or more observations are made on a number of subjects. These observations either increase or decrease simultaneously or at varying levels. The two or more observations are called variables and their relationship is called correlation. For example, in a study of youths you may decide to observe their age and weight. In this case, the weight and age are the variables. There are two types of variables, namely, independent and dependent variables. In the example just given, age is an independent variable while weight is a dependent variable. Independent variables are plotted on the horizontal axis of a graph while a dependant variable is plotted on the vertical axis. Variables can also be described as qualitative or quantitative. Quantitative variables are expressed in numerical terms. For example, age, height, area, weight and so on. Qualitative variables are adjectives that describe the subject of study, for example, farmer, teacher, nurse, male, female, green, yellow, white, sadness, happiness, satisfaction, religion.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="ethical-considerations-in-community-diagnosis">Ethical Considerations in Community Diagnosis<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#ethical-considerations-in-community-diagnosis" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>When conducting community diagnosis, it is very important to avoid taking any action that may be considered offensive by the community. You need to make sure that the tools you use to collect information are not in any way offensive to the community. They should not cause any physical, emotional, spiritual or cultural harm to that community.</p>
<p>You should consider some of the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>Obtaining permission to enter into the community boundaries</li>
<li>Obtaining informed consent before interviewing patients, families or groups</li>
<li>Establishing rapport before exploring sensitive areas</li>
<li>Ensuring confidentiality of the data collected</li>
<li>Selecting good interviewers</li>
<li>Training interviewers</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="the-process-of-community-diagnosis">The Process of Community Diagnosis<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-process-of-community-diagnosis" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The process of community diagnosis requires careful planning right from the beginning. It involves initial exploration and interaction with the community, planning of the survey, developing and pre-testing the survey tools and methods, and executing the survey and analysing the results. Once all the information has been gathered it must be documented and its conclusions disseminated to the community. The process of community diagnosis is made up of the following steps:</p>
<ol>
<li>Exploration</li>
<li>Planning of the survey</li>
<li>Developing and pre-testing survey tools</li>
<li>Execution of the survey and data analysis</li>
<li>Report writing, dissemination and community action</li>
</ol>
<p>In this section you will only learn the first two steps, that is, exploration and how to plan a survey. The rest will be considered in subsequent sections of this unit.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="exploring-the-community-community-inventory">Exploring the Community (Community Inventory)<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#exploring-the-community-community-inventory" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Exploration simply means mapping out of a community in order to learn or discover about it. It is also known as community inventory. Ideally, you should only carry out a community diagnosis after a request by the community or the people involved in providing health care. The exploration phase is made up of three main activities:</p>
<ul>
<li>Seeking permission and informing the various leaders</li>
<li>Seeking reactions of members of the community</li>
<li>Gathering background data about the community</li>
</ul>
<p>Although these activities are listed separately, they actually can take place at the same time.</p>
<p>You will now look at each activity in turn.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="seeking-permission-and-informing-authorities-in-the-community">Seeking Permission and Informing Authorities in the Community<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#seeking-permission-and-informing-authorities-in-the-community" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>For the survey to succeed you must seek permission from community leaders.</p>
<p>Start by channelling your request through the official hierarchy of administrative leaders in that community:</p>
<ul>
<li>Health personnel such as Medical Officer in Charge</li>
<li>Governmental officials such as Chiefs or District Officers</li>
<li>Community leaders through, for example, the village health committee</li>
</ul>
<p>You should approach all these people, introduce yourself and clearly state the objectives of the survey and your plan of action. Remember, for them to give you permission to proceed they need to understand what you intend to do and how it will benefit the community.</p>
<p>So be well prepared. The community leaders are extremely important to the success of the survey as they clearly understand what, how, when, and why things happen. They also influence other members of the community more directly than administrators who do not live in the community. You should therefore seek their assistance in implementing the survey. They will only cooperate if they perceive some beneficial results from their cooperation. So you should always plan a survey with the intention of carrying out an appropriate action programme for the community.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="seeking-reactions-of-members-of-the-community">Seeking Reactions of Members of the Community<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#seeking-reactions-of-members-of-the-community" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>During this period of exploration, you also sound out the reactions of members of the community. This can be done by talking to people informally in market places and eating places. This way, you will easily find their opinions or problems in the community and their likely solutions. Also by talking to them you can gather information regarding possible resistance to the survey and how to approach different members of the community.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="gathering-background-data">Gathering Background Data<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#gathering-background-data" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The period of exploration also presents you with the opportunity to gather background data about the community. For instance, the Medical Officer of Health in charge of the district will give you an overview of the health profile of the district. The District Commissioner will give you boundaries,</p>
<p>Bishop's Computers
Bishop's Computers</p>
<p>the population and maps of the area. The District Education Officer will give information about educational activities and literacy levels of the community members. Other district heads will give information related to their areas of jurisdiction. Although some of the records might not contain the most recent data, you can make projections by estimating the current population figures in the community.</p>
<p>Similarly by looking at other government reports you can gather information regarding the climate and weather conditions, water sources and the road network. The older people in the community are a little used resource and yet they can provide you with a lot of information on the community's history.</p>
<p>Gather information as you move around the community by:</p>
<ul>
<li>Questioning</li>
<li>Observing</li>
<li>Smelling</li>
<li>Listening</li>
</ul>
<p>You should also look at findings of previous surveys so that you can adequately address any new problems.</p>
<p>Once you explore the community and gather all the information you need you are now ready to plan your survey.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="planning-the-survey">Planning the Survey<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#planning-the-survey" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This is a very important part of the process of community diagnosis. There is a popular adage which says 'If you fail to plan you plan to fail'. So if you fail to plan the survey carefully and properly, your study will be unsuccessful or will give you unreliable results.</p>
<p>During the planning phase, you should attempt to answer the following questions.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="why-is-the-survey-being-done">Why is the Survey Being Done?<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#why-is-the-survey-being-done" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>A survey is not carried out just to obtain interesting information. There must be good reasons and the reasons must be useful to the community. This question must therefore be answered to the satisfaction of all the leaders who are concerned with the community.</p>
<p>If the community has given you permission to carry out the survey, it will expect and have a right to expect some beneficial results from its cooperation. You should therefore plan the survey with the intention of carrying out a proper action programme for the community. This information would most likely have been communicated during the exploratory phase.</p>
<p>Once the community leaders understand the reasons and are ready to cooperate with you, a meeting of the members of the community should be called to explain why there is a survey, why they have been chosen, what will be involved, when it will be done and what will happen to the results. During this meeting you should invite government, health and community leaders so that the community can see who is supporting your work and who will be moving around their community and homes.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="where-will-it-take-place">Where Will it Take Place?<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#where-will-it-take-place" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="how-do-we-select-and-train-the-official-interviewers">How do we select and train the official interviewers?<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#how-do-we-select-and-train-the-official-interviewers" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="remember">Remember:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#remember" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<p>The people you select for training as interviewers should have the following qualities:</p>
<ul>
<li>Be literate and well known to the community</li>
<li>Have the ability to display the right attitudes and opinions</li>

<li>Be able to use the tools presented in your package</li>
<li>Be able to establish good rapport with individuals, families or groups they will meet</li>
<li>Be good listeners and sensitive towards other people's feelings</li>
<li>Be able to relate well to the community members</li>
</ul>

Bishop's Computers</p>
<p>community. If one of your tools addresses a specific group like the youths, you should select an interviewer of the same age or sex who can identify with the group. <p>The people who are selected as interviewers have different educational backgrounds from yours and may interpret questions and answers differently. So you must train them on how to administer the survey tools. When training interviewers you need to explain the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>The purpose of the survey</li>

If each interviewer asks questions in their own manner the answers will be unreliable because they may refer to different things.</p>
<p>During the training you should hold mock interviews with the interviewers so that you can ensure that each one of them can handle the assignment. Use this opportunity to correct them and also to clarify issues about the questionnaire such as wrong translations and questions. Once you are confident that your interviewers can handle the job, you should carry out a trial test or pilot test on a section of the community who have similar characteristics as the study group. This gives them a feel of the real situation and helps you to assess them further.</p>
<p>Remember that the pilot group should not be included in the study group.</p>

<ul>
<li>
<p>Establish rapport by greeting the respondents and introducing themselves</p>

<li>
<p>Explain carefully why they have come and what is the purpose of the survey</p>

<li>

<li>

<p>Having planned your survey and trained your interviewers, you now need to identify a representative sample which will answer your questions and provide you with the information and results you need. You will now look at sampling.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="sampling-for-a-survey">Sampling for a Survey<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#sampling-for-a-survey" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Sampling is the process of selecting a number of individuals or units of the study population in such a way that the individuals selected represent the larger groups from which they are selected. You will agree that it is neither practical nor economical to survey a whole population. That is why the part of the population studied is called a sample. The aim is to get the the same information from that sample that you would have got if the whole population had been surveyed. For this reason, when you are selecting a sample for a survey, you must make sure that it is representative of the whole population.</p>
<p>Also, in the sampling process you have to give an equal chance for each person in the population to be included in the sample. Otherwise you can come to wrong conclusions.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="a-study-population">A Study Population<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#a-study-population" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>A study population is the entire group of individuals, events or objects that have common observable characteristics. For example:</p>
<ul>
<li>All first years in nursing</li>
<li>All under fives in a given community</li>
<li>All qualified nurses with mental health qualifications</li>
</ul>
<p>This must be clearly defined for example, according to age, sex, or residence. A study population may also be selected for example, according to villages, institutions, records. Each</p>
<p>Bishop's Computers
Bishop's Computers</p>
<p>population is made up of study units identified by the type of problem that you want to study.</p>
<p>A study population can be divided into two main groups, namely, accessible population and the representative sample.</p>
<p>The accessible population is a group of individuals, objects and events with characteristics comparable to the target population and relevant to the study.</p>
<p>The representative sample is a group from the study population, which has all the important/relevant characteristics of the total population.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="sampling-methods">Sampling Methods<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#sampling-methods" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Before you sample you need to develop a sampling frame. A sampling frame is a list of all units that make up the study population. It enables you to sample the study units in such a way that the probability or the different units to be selected in the sample are known. Sampling techniques fall under two main groups, namely, probability sampling and non probability sampling.</p>
<p>You will now consider each group starting with probability sampling.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="probability-sampling">Probability Sampling<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#probability-sampling" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Probability sampling looks at the entire group of individuals, events or objects that have common observable characteristics. It has been found to give accurate results when one is studying groups that are too large to study in their entity. It also provides you with an efficient system of capturing; in a small group the variations or similarities that exist in the target population.</p>
<p>The following are the most commonly used methods in probability sampling:</p>
<ul>
<li>Simple random sampling</li>
<li>Systematic sampling</li>
<li>Stratified sampling</li>
<li>Cluster sampling</li>
<li>Multi-stage sampling</li>
</ul>
<p>You will now look at each in turn.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="sampling-methods">sampling methods<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#sampling-methods" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="simple-random-sampling">Simple Random Sampling<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#simple-random-sampling" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This is the simplest form of probability sampling. It means that every sampling unit in the population has an equal chance of being included in the sample. You can draw a simple random sample using the following steps:</p>
<ul>
<li>Make a list of all the units in the population to be studied</li>
<li>Decide on the sample size</li>
<li>Select the required number of units using ballot or lottery method or random numbers</li>
</ul>
<p>For example to draw a random sample of five patients from a list of 250 using the ballot method, you follow this method:</p>
<ul>
<li>Give each client a number (1 - 250)</li>
<li>Write them on a small piece of paper</li>
<li>Fold them individually and put them in a box</li>
<li>Shake the box vigorously to mix them</li>
<li>Pick five pieces one by one and note the numbers and record</li>
</ul>
<p>Each patient is a unit and the names of the patients on these numbered papers form the sample or study population.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="systematic-sampling">Systematic Sampling<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#systematic-sampling" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Here you first decide the sample size you want and then proceed to select the individuals or units using a systematic method.</p>
<p>For example, let us assume that the population size you want to study consists of 1000 women. Out of this population, you only want to pick a sample of ten women for your study. This gives you a ratio of 1:100 or a sample fraction of 1/10. Now, with this ratio you can proceed to pick the sample population as follows:</p>
<ul>
<li>Select the first file randomly. Let us assume that you have selected file number 25.</li>
<li>Starting with file no. 25, proceed to pick every 100 file, that is, file 125, 225, 325, 525, 625, 725, 825, and 925. You now have your random sample of ten files.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="stratified-sampling">Stratified Sampling<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#stratified-sampling" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This is dividing the sample frame into smaller sub samples in order to enable you to capture the variable aspects of each subgroup. This method is used when the study population is very variable, for example, different ethnic groups, different ecological areas, or age groups. It allows you to subdivide the population into sub populations which are more</p>
<p>Bishop's Computers</p>
<p>Bishop's Computers</p>
<p>Bishop's Computers</p>
<p>homogeneous. You then apply simple random sampling to each subgroup or stratum.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="cluster-sampling">Cluster Sampling<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#cluster-sampling" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>In this method, you randomly select groups or clusters and not the individuals or cases. This method is used when it is not possible to obtain a sampling frame because the population is either too large or scattered over a large geographical area. In cluster sampling you select an intact group and include all the members of that group in the sample. For example, say you want to study patients suffering from malaria in your district. It would be expensive and time consuming to compile a list of all malaria patients who have been hospitalised in your district.</p>
<p>So the logical thing to do would be to list all health facilities in your district and then randomly select them according to your sample size. Once you select them, you would then include all the malaria patients in those health facilities in your sample.</p>
<p>The methods of sampling you have just covered are known as probability sampling. You will now look at another group of sampling methods known as biased sampling or non-probability sampling methods.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="non-probability-sampling-methods">Non Probability Sampling Methods<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#non-probability-sampling-methods" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Non probability sampling methods are used when a researcher is not interested in selecting a sample that is representative of the population. They are mainly used in qualitative studies where the focus is on in-depth information rather than making generalisations. Some examples of non-probability sampling methods are convenient sampling, quota sampling and purposive sampling.</p>
<p>You will now look briefly at each method.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="convenience-sampling">Convenience Sampling<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#convenience-sampling" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>In this method, you select cases or units of observation as they become available.</p>
<p>For example, a health worker wanting to study attitudes of villagers towards family planning may decide to interview all adults visiting Maternal Child Health or Family Planning (MCH/FP) clinic on that day. Such a sample is useful for giving a first impression of a situation. However, it is not representative of the community. This sample is considered unrepresentative because some units can easily be missed out or under selected.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="quota-sampling">Quota Sampling<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#quota-sampling" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>In this method, the researcher simply selects subjects to fit in identified quotas, say for example, a certain religion or social class. Quota sampling ensures that various groups or quotas of the population are included in the study according to some criteria. The selection is not random as the individuals are just picked as they fit into the identified quotas.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="purposive-sampling">Purposive Sampling<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#purposive-sampling" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Here the researcher simply picks individuals or cases that have the information or characteristics which they requires. It is sometimes used in one of the stages in the sampling procedure, for instance, to get the location or district in which the units of observation have the required characteristics. Once the units are selected, the researcher may then apply random sampling to obtain the actual sample of cases.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="bias-and-sampling-errors">Bias and Sampling Errors<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#bias-and-sampling-errors" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>As you noted in probability sampling, selecting a sample requires special techniques which ensure that each person in a population has an equal chance of being selected into the sample. If a sample is not randomly selected or if the interviewers do not follow a similar and consistent method, there will be errors in the data gathered. These types of errors are called bias. Further, even when the sampling techniques which reduce bias are correctly applied during the selection of a sample, the results of the study may be subject to another error known as sampling error. This is because within the small group selected to represent a larger one, there may be people whose characteristics are very different from anyone else's in the same group. Very small samples tend to have higher sampling errors than large samples.</p>

<p>A questionnaire is a set of standardised questions designed to collect information about a specific aspect or issue in the community. It is therefore a tool for collecting information. Information from a questionnaire helps you to make plans for your health services and to evaluate them.</p>
<p>Before you design a questionnaire it is important for you to know what information you need to collect and how it will be used. It would help you to make a list of what you want to know. In other words, what do you really want to find out or achieve with the questionnaire?</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="qualities-of-a-good-questionnaire">Qualities of a Good Questionnaire<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#qualities-of-a-good-questionnaire" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>A good questionnaire has the following qualities:</p>
<ul>
<li>Has simple and specific questions. Avoids wording that is above the vocabulary or reading skills of the respondents.</li>
<li>Has short and precise questions. The number of questions should not be too many or else they will put off the person being interviewed. In other words, keep it short and simple (KISS).</li>
<li>Avoids use of abbreviations or jargon.</li>
<li>Avoids questions that are too demanding and time consuming.</li>
<li>Avoids bias in questions. Biased questions influence people to answer in a way that does not accurately reflect their position. For example, a question like 'Do you agree with the majority of the people that health standards are falling?' implies that the respondent should agree.</li>
<li>Avoids making assumptions. Questions such as 'How many children do you have?' assume that the respondent has children. You should only ask this question after establishing the situation with the question 'Do you have children?'</li>
<li>Avoids double questions. For example, 'Did the MCH talk help to identify ways to improve the sanitation and nutrition of your children?' It is better to ask about sanitation and nutrition separately.</li>
<li>Has clear wording. Words such as majority, older people, regularly, might mean different things to different people and so should be avoided.</li>
<li>Questions ask about simple common happenings.</li>
<li>Questions range from known to unknown and from simple to complex.</li>
<li>All the questions should relate to the purpose of study. Eliminate 'nice' to</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="types-of-information">Types of Information<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#types-of-information" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="remember">Remember:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#remember" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The response or information you obtain is only as good as the question. To get correct information you must ask the right question.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="types-of-question">Types of Question<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#types-of-question" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>A questionnaire should be laid out in such a way that it provides easy flow from one topic to another. It should have both open- and closed-ended questions. They should be arranged in such a way that they allow natural flow of discussion.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="open-ended-question">Open-ended Question<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#open-ended-question" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>An open-ended question is a type of question that allows the respondent to provide their own answer. It encourages the respondent to think and describe a situation in their own words. The respondent is not given any answers to select from. The answer given is best recorded in the respondent's own words. Although it is the easiest way to ask for information the responses are not easy to analyse. The answers are bound to be varied and so you need to categorise and summarise them.</p>
<p>Open-ended questions are useful because they give more information on:</p>
<ul>
<li>Facts and details which the researcher may not be familiar with</li>
<li>Opinions, attitudes and suggestions</li>
<li>Sensitive issues</li>
</ul>
<p>The following are examples of open-ended questions:</p>
<ol>
<li>What did the traditional birth attendants do when your labour started?</li>
<li>What do you think are the reasons for the high dropout rate of health committee members?</li>
<li>What would you do if you noticed that your daughter (a schoolgirl) has a relationship with her teacher?</li>
</ol>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="focus-group-discussions-fgds">Focus Group Discussions (FGDs)<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#focus-group-discussions-fgds" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This is a group discussion that gathers together people from similar backgrounds or experiences to discuss a specific topic of interest to the researcher. The group of participants are guided by a moderator (or group facilitator), who introduces topics for discussion and helps the group to participate in a lively and natural discussion amongst themselves.</p>
<p>A focus group is not a group interview where a moderator asks the group questions and participants individually provide answers. The focus group relies on group discussion and is especially successful where the participants are able to talk to each other about the topic of interest. This is important as it allows the participants the opportunity to disagree or agree with each other. It can provide insight into how a group thinks about an issue, about the range of opinions and ideas, and the inconsistencies and variation that exist in a particular community in terms of beliefs and their experiences and practices.</p>
<p>The discussion is usually 'focused' on a particular area of interest. It does not usually cover a large range of issues, but allows you to explore one or two topics in greater detail.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="focus-groups-in-a-community-diagnosis-survey">Focus Groups in a Community Diagnosis Survey<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#focus-groups-in-a-community-diagnosis-survey" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Focus groups can be used in the following ways:</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="exploratory-studies">Exploratory Studies<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#exploratory-studies" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Focus groups are a valuable method to explore a topic about which little is known, or little has been written in the past. For example, in order to set up a successful health education programme you need to understand people's traditional health beliefs. Focus groups can begin this process by providing the first in-depth descriptions of how the community sees the cause and treatment of certain illnesses. Focus groups can also be used to discover local terms used for signs and symptoms of illness, types of illness, and other concepts relating to health.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="solving-specific-programme-problems">Solving Specific Programme Problems<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#solving-specific-programme-problems" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="conducting-a-focus-group-discussion">Conducting a Focus Group Discussion<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#conducting-a-focus-group-discussion" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>In order to conduct a successful focus group discussion you should adopt the following four steps:</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="step-one-preparation">Step One: Preparation<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#step-one-preparation" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>First you recruit participants. Focus groups are 'focused' because the participants usually share a common characteristic. This may be age, sex, educational background, religion or something directly related to the topic. This encourages the group to speak more freely about the subject without fear of being judged by others who are thought to be superior. For example, young women may not be as forthcoming in their ideas and opinions in the presence of their mothers or mothers-in-law. The participants should have prior knowledge so that they can come prepared.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="step-two-physical-arrangement">Step Two: Physical Arrangement<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#step-two-physical-arrangement" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>It is good to make sitting arrangements that allow participants to see each other. Circular seating is the best as everybody can see each other. Avoid the traditional classroom type of sitting. Ensure the room is well lit and ventilated and has minimum or no disturbance so that the participants can concentrate on the discussion. The environment should promote talking and sharing.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="step-three-preparing-a-discussion-guide">Step Three: Preparing a Discussion Guide<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#step-three-preparing-a-discussion-guide" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>You should prepare a set of questions that will help you to guide the discussion. These questions should also allow free flow from one aspect of the topic to the next in a relevant fashion. This helps the participants to think logically and build on the topic you are investigating. Have a mixture of general and more specific questions. If your questions are all general, you may not elicit detailed responses from the participants. On the other hand, if your questions are all specific, you may neglect to address and receive information on the 'bigger picture.'</p>
<p>Make sure you have a variety of follow up 'probes' for each of your questions in the event that you need to clarify questions or have participants elaborate on their responses.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="step-four-the-discussion">Step Four: The Discussion<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#step-four-the-discussion" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Before you proceed you should identify among your team one facilitator and one recorder and introduce them to the group.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="functions-of-the-facilitator">Functions of the Facilitator<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#functions-of-the-facilitator" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The functions of the facilitator are to stimulate and support the discussion by:</p>
<ul>
<li>Introducing the topic and all the other participants. Self introduction is better because it already sets in motion the tone of sharing for everybody.</li>
<li>Reassuring them and explaining the purpose of the discussion and the type of information required. The participant also needs to know how and where the information will be used.</li>
<li>Encouraging discussion by being enthusiastic, lively, humorous and showing interest in the group's ideas. The facilitator formulates and asks questions following the prepared guide. The facilitator should involve all the members but must remain neutral to all responses so that the participants can freely express their feelings, opinions and views.</li>
<li>Encouraging involvement of all the members of the group.</li>
</ul>
<p>To encourage involvement of all members of the group the facilitator should ask open-ended</p>
<p>Bishop's Computers
Bishop's Computers</p>
<p>questions. The facilitator should identify and manage individuals who dominate the discussion and deny others a chance to respond. One way of managing domineering members is to ignore them and instead give attention to the other members when they want to give an opinion. Maintain eye contact with the shy ones and prompt them to talk by calling them by name and posing questions directly to them. Build rapport and empathise as necessary. You should also watch their expressions, mannerism and non-verbal communication. Try to understand what they are saying, communicating or insinuating. If something is not clear ask for elaboration. Avoid being</p>
<p>an expert.</p>
<p>The members may ask for your opinion. Redirect the question back to them by asking for their opinion instead or what action they would take in respect of the question.</p>
<p>After the session is over, you could share with them the information they were asking for. Control the discussion without blocking their freedom of expression and keep within the time allocated for this. At the end of the session you should thank them for sharing their time and ideas with you.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="what-the-recorder-records">What the Recorder Records<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#what-the-recorder-records" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The recorder records all key issues raised in the session and other factors that may influence the interpretation of information in as much detail as possible. This involves noting down the responses from the group and observing and documenting any non-verbal messages that could indicate how a group is feeling about the topic under discussion.</p>
<p>The following must also be recorded:</p>
<ul>
<li>Date, time, place</li>
<li>Names of participants</li>
<li>Description of the group level of participation including any dominant participant</li>
<li>Details of opinions of participants as much as possible using their own words especially for key statements</li>
<li>Details of emotional aspects and the vocabulary used. This will be particularly useful for developing questionnaires or health learning materials. If possible, a tape recorder should be used as well</li>
</ul>
<p>The recorder may also help the moderator if necessary. She or he may point out questions that are not well explored; questions missed, or suggest areas that could be investigated. The</p>
<p>recorder should not be especially obvious to the group but should be able to communicate with the facilitator if required and help them resolve conflicting issues.</p>
<p>At the end of the discussion, the facilitator should sit with the recorder and review the discussion and complete the notes and evaluate how the discussion went. They should then prepare a full report of the discussion using the participant's own words. It is necessary to list the key statements, ideas and attitudes expressed during each topic. These statements are usually coded and written on the left-hand margin while the comments are written on the right-hand side. It may be necessary to formulate additional questions at this stage for those issues that were not yet clear or controversial.</p>
<p>Next, you will look at how you can pre-test your instruments in order to ensure that they are capable of collecting the data you need.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="pre-testing-the-instruments">Pre-testing the Instruments<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#pre-testing-the-instruments" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>It is very important to pre-test all the instruments you intend to use before they are finally administered. It enables the interviewing team to discern, alter or delete questions which are being misinterpreted or are too sensitive to be asked without offending people. It also gives you the opportunity to discover if the various parts of the questionnaire flow in a logical order.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="points-to-look-for-when-pre-testing-a-questionnaire">Points to Look for When Pre-testing a Questionnaire<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#points-to-look-for-when-pre-testing-a-questionnaire" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>According to Salant and Dillman (1994), any pre-test aims to answer the following questions:</p>
<ul>
<li>Does each question measure what it is intended to measure?</li>
<li>Do respondents understand all the words?</li>
<li>Are questions interpreted similarly by all respondents?</li>
<li>Does each closed ended question have an answer that applies to each respondent?</li>
<li>Does the questionnaire create a positive impression, one that motivates people to answer it?</li>
<li>Are the answers which respondents can choose from correct? Are some responses missing? Do some questions elicit uninterpretable answers?</li>
</ul>
<p>Bishop's Computers

Bishop's Computers</p>
<ul>
<li>Does any part of the questionnaire suggest bias on the part of the researcher?</li>
<li>Is the questionnaire too long?</li>
</ul>

<p>By the time you get to this stage, you have already developed the survey instruments, trained the interviewers on how to use these instruments and even pre-tested them. You do this in order to ensure that the correct standards are adhered to during the actual survey. Once the real survey begins, you should continue to work closely with the interviewers.</p>

<p>Your presence reassures them and they also get the opportunity to clarify issues that may arise. On your part, you get assured that the interviewers are continuing to follow the techniques you taught them.</p>
<p>When the interviewers approach a respondent, they should:</p>
<ul>
<li>Introduce themselves by name</li>
<li>Show their identity cards for the activity</li>
<li>Show their letter of permission to carry out the exercise</li>
<li>Explain why they have come and the purpose of the survey</li>
<li>Establish rapport with the respondent/s so that they can feel at ease with each other</li>
<li>Ask if it is convenient to interview the person at that time</li>
<li>Should the person refuse to cooperate, the interviewers should do their best to persuade such a person to agree</li>

<p>If it is convenient to interview the person at the time you have requested, give the client/family time to be comfortable and proceed. If not, ask for a more convenient time when this will be possible.</p>
<p>This must be within the prescribed period of the exercise as indicated in the permit. In which case the interviewer must return punctually as agreed upon with the family or individual.</p>
<p>If the person refuses to cooperate and remains adamant, the interviewers should politely thank the person for their time spent and proceed to the next interview. Sometimes people may refuse to respond to the question raised.</p>
<p><strong>Remember the rights of the individual. The respondent has the right to refuse to participate in an interview or experiment or medication.</strong></p>
<p>There are various reasons why people do not answer questions in a survey. These include:</p>
<ul>
<li>If the people were not informed of the survey, its objectives and when it would be performed</li>
<li>If for some reason the person to be interviewed is temporarily away from home</li>
<li>Lack of interest in cooperating or active opposition to the survey</li>
<li>In order to reduce the incidence of such opposition from your sample group, you should always make sure that the community is well informed about the study. If the target respondent is temporarily away from home, the interviewer should make arrangements to return when he or she is in. People who lack interest need a clearer explanation and persuasion so that they can see how they will benefit from the exercise. Activists can be difficult; if they remain adamant just politely thank them and let them free. They are just exercising their rights. If you find that a large number of people in your group are non-respondents, you will need to do a random selection from that group and re-approach them.</li>
</ul>
<p>The results you get from the new sample will be representative of the entire non-respondent group. Compare the results with the original sample of all respondents and calculate the difference.</p>

<p>Interviewers should be advised to use a pencil when filling out forms so that it is easier to make corrections. They should not erase a wrong response. If a mistake is made, the incorrect response should be crossed out and the correct response marked above it. Incorrect responses should not be erased because it is possible that if the erasure is incomplete, the response might not be legible or might be confused with a different response. The interviewer should fill in the responses at the time they are given. No response should be filled in afterwards because the interviewer may remember the response incorrectly.</p>
<p>Ensure that every interviewer has all the tools they need to collect data such as tools for anthropometric measurements and laboratory specimen containers.</p>
<p>You should avail a convenient carrier for these tools and check the packs daily to ensure that any specimens collected were handled correctly. It is good to remind the interviewers to recheck their measurements before they leave the client to avoid mistakes. They should also check the forms before they leave the respondent in order to be sure that all relevant information is satisfactory filled in the appropriate space provided.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="data-analysis">Data Analysis<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#data-analysis" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The data you obtain from the field is known as 'raw data'. In this state, it does not give much information and is therefore difficult to interpret. That is why it needs further work known as data analysis.</p>
<p>Data analysis is the separation and categorisation of numerical data into groups in order to understand its meaning. Statistical methods are used to do this because they:</p>
<ul>
<li>Summarise the data.</li>
<li>Make inferences about the data. This means that data which has been gathered on a sample can be used to indicate what is probably happening to the entire population so as to make judgement about them.</li>
</ul>
<p>The process of data analysis involves the following steps:</p>
<ul>
<li>Data cleaning</li>
<li>Sorting or tallying</li>
<li>Coding and entering data</li>
<li>Analysis of results</li>
</ul>
<p>These steps are covered in detail in module four unit three on Research in Nursing. You will now look briefly at them.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="sorting-and-tallying-data">Sorting and Tallying Data<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#sorting-and-tallying-data" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Once you have collected data from the field, you need to organise it in a systematic manner that facilitates analysis. You do this by sorting and tallying the data.</p>
<p>Sorting is arranging raw data in groups or in a particular order. You should select a system of sorting which facilitates data analysis. For example, if you are collecting data on users of family planning, you may decide to sort your data into two groups, that is, users and non-users of family planning. If your questionnaire is made up of closed-ended questions, such as the yes and no type, then you can assign numbers to these questions. For example, 1 to Yes and 0 to No, and sort them accordingly. On the other hand, if your questionnaire has open-ended questions, then you need to categorise all the responses given and assign numbers to them.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="individual-results">Individual Results<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#individual-results" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The individuals who formed a part of your survey sample as well as those who provided you with specimens and tests deserve to be given feedback on your findings. You should tell them at the time of examination that they will be contacted later if anything abnormal is found. Those who you took specimens from or on whom you conducted tests should be told if anything abnormal was found. When giving such feedback you should be cautious not to arouse anxiety about harmless conditions.</p>
<p>You should only give feedback on those conditions that require treatment. If you diagnose a condition that requires treatment, you should start it at once or refer the person to the nearest health facility for follow-up care. If during the survey you find specific notifiable diseases you should report to the relevant authorities, such as the medical officer in charge.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="preliminary-report">Preliminary Report<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#preliminary-report" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This goes to the community in which the diagnosis was conducted in order to thank them for their cooperation. It is given soon after the field work is completed by arranging meetings for the people and their leaders. This report covers your general impressions of the community's health status as well as preliminary survey findings. Do not give specific information on this day, since analysis will not be ready yet. Give them information on obvious points like disease outbreak, hygiene, sanitation, attitudes, nutrition, practices and utilisation of health services.</p>
<p>You should promise them a more detailed report later. By giving them the findings of the report, you increase their awareness of their real health needs and how these can be solved.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="non-medical-reports">Non-medical Reports<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#non-medical-reports" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This report is less scientific and is usually produced for non-medical people. It comes out after full analysis has been done and</p>
<p>Bishop's Computers</p>
<p>Bishop's Computers</p>
<p>conclusions and recommendations have been formulated. It is an important report because its message can influence social and political leaders to start doing something about improving the health of the population.</p>
<p>According to Bennett F.J (1979), the contents of this report should cover the following aspects of the survey:</p>
<ul>
<li>The reasons for doing the community diagnosis</li>
<li>The findings which help to define the situation. Here you select only those characteristics which describe the real characteristics of the health situation in this population. Often it is sufficient to describe findings in words.</li>
</ul>
<p>However if you feel that tables would be indispensable then they should be short and easy to understand. You can also use the graphical forms which were described earlier. They have been found to be very helpful in conveying survey findings at a glance</p>
<ul>
<li>The conclusions and recommendations you make should be of practical significance. You should leave out those that are of scientific or academic interest to be documented in the medical report. This report is a social or political tool to shed light on the improvements that need to be done in the community's health services. So make your recommendations practical.</li>
</ul>
<p>Communities are made up of people with varying educational levels. Some are illiterate or are of low educational standards. Such people would benefit more from personal contact or a discussion type of feedback. That is why in our communities Barazas are commonly used to communicate matters to people. However, if you are dealing with a literate population you can disseminate the report through the mail or even the mass media that is newspapers, radio and television. Newspapers are a good medium because they have a wide coverage and people from elsewhere might also benefit from the report findings.</p>
<p>Lastly, you will look at the community diagnosis report also known as the medical report.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="the-medical-or-community-diagnosis-report">The Medical or Community Diagnosis Report<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-medical-or-community-diagnosis-report" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This is a detailed scientific report which provides an account of the planning and execution of the survey as well as the results. It should present</p>
<p>the data you collected fully and adequately and give accurate interpretations of the analyses. Its dissemination can be done in a workshop setting and tailored to meet the needs of the various levels of health personnel who are invited.</p>
<p>A well written community diagnosis report is made up of distinct sections or components which fall under the following headings:</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="title">Title<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#title" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Use a title that is short and simple and yet informative. The title of your survey report is important because at a glance it gives the reader information about what your survey was about and it also helps the librarian to index the book properly.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="table-of-contents">Table of contents<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#table-of-contents" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This is like a map which helps the readers to locating various sections of the survey report quickly and easily. It often contains chapter headings, main headings and sub headings with their corresponding page numbers.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="lists-of-tables-and-figures">Lists of tables and figures<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#lists-of-tables-and-figures" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This follows the same format as the table of contents and lists the titles of the tables, diagrams, graphs, charts used in the report and their corresponding page numbers.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="list-of-abbreviations-and-acronyms">List of abbreviations and acronyms<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#list-of-abbreviations-and-acronyms" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>An abbreviation is a short form of a word, for example, Tb for tuberculosis. An acronym is a contraction formed by taking the first letter of several words, for example, HIV which stands for Human Immunodeficiency Virus. In this section of a report you give a list of all the abbreviations and acronyms you have used in the document with a full explanation of what they stand for. <div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="introduction">Introduction<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#introduction" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This section gives the background to your study. It seeks to explain why the survey was undertaken and which questions it was designed to answer. It is written in the form of broad and specific objectives which also reflect on the purpose of the study. Usually before embarking on a survey one reviews the relevant literature. If you consulted any literature you should make reference to this fact in this section as it lends support to the arguments you put forward in the introduction</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="aims-and-objectives-of-the-study">Aims and objectives of the study<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#aims-and-objectives-of-the-study" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="materials-and-methods">Materials and methods<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#materials-and-methods" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Here you describe your survey design, techniques and the instruments or tools you used to collect the data. In particular, you include information about:</p>
<ul>
<li>The sources of the data collected that is the type of records, where they were found, and how complete they were. If the source of data was people, you should describe them and their characteristics, for example, were they from the same village, location.</li>

<p>This section calls for honesty and openness in admitting the difficulties you may have encountered. It helps other researchers not to make the same mistakes you made. This is the essence of learning and maturity. You are actually taking a step back to ask yourself the question 'If I was to do this survey all over again, what would I do differently?'</p>

<ul>
<li>Sampling methods</li>
<li>Standardisation of tests and measurements</li>
<li>Observation variation</li>
<li>Incomplete records due to poor supervision</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="results-findings">Results/ findings<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#results-findings" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This section deals with presentation of results in any one of the formats you covered earlier, that is, figures, tabular and graphical formats. It does not matter which format you choose as long as it brings out clearly those characteristics which you think are important. If you want to show the trend in certain age groups or time periods, then you may consider using a graphical form such as a frequency polygon. A list of figures or a table may be indicated if you want to describe in detail a distribution or size of characteristics. However, when presenting figures you should take note of the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>Do not use figures with several decimal places unless the precision of the measurement justifies this</li>
<li>It is misleading to present percentages with one or more decimals if the sample size is small</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="discussions">Discussions<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#discussions" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This is mainly your interpretation of data, a process in which logical thinking, judgement and common sense all play a major role. You will be asking questions such as how reliable and valid</p>
<p>Bishop's Computers
Bishop's Computers</p>
<p>are the observations, whether the figures are high or low, and if there are associations between variables which may indicate a causal relationship.</p>
<p>When interpreting your results it is often necessary to compare them with and refer to other studies on the same topic. In surveys which cover a variety of independent health problems this section may be combined with that of results.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="conclusions-and-recommendations">Conclusions and recommendations<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#conclusions-and-recommendations" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This is your brief summary of the essential findings and careful consideration of how the community health problem you have diagnosed can be reduced and/or controlled. You should also explain the causes of the health problems and how they can be prevented.</p>
<p>You need to describe what the community should and can do to control diseases for all concerned.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="references">References<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#references" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>It is almost impossible to conduct a survey without consulting published and unpublished documents in the community. These could be hospital records, maps and others. You must give credit to authors of any work you quote from or refer to by listing them.</p>
<p>Generally a reference gives the name of the author, the year of publication of the document, the title of the book or paper and the publisher.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="appendices">Appendices<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#appendices" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<p>Once you have produced your reports and given feedback to all the concerned parties, the next and last step in the process of community diagnosis is the action phase. A community survey should lead to community action.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="community-health-action">Community Health Action<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#community-health-action" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>A community survey identifies a host of health problems that need to be addressed. It may have revealed a need for greater emphasis on MCH services or environmental sanitation. Therefore, you need to sit down with the community to prioritise and plan what you going to do about the identified health problems. In short, you need to mobilise them to take action.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="mobilise-community-action">Mobilise Community Action<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#mobilise-community-action" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>You can mobilise the community through a number of interventions, namely:</p>
<ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="health-education">Health Education<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#health-education" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Health education is not just about sending out posters and pamphlets to the community. It is about listening and finding out why people do things the way they do. It is about stimulating their interest in their health problems through discussion and sometimes by example. It is also about giving people information and helping them to set priorities and improve their own health.</p>
<p>Health education cannot be prescribed in doses! You will therefore need to target individuals, families and the community at large with health messages using the media that is available and affordable.</p>
<p><strong>Remember:</strong></p>
<p>Health education cannot be handed out to the community; it must be shared with them. Immunisation</p>
<p>If the results of your survey indicated low immunisation coverage as one of the reasons behind the high morbidity and mortality rates among children, then you need to plan immunisation action. You will need to identify all non-immunised and inadequately immunised persons, and ensure that they receive immunisation. You will also need to ensure that you have a good supply of vaccines and that they are well maintained in order to preserve their viability and potency. Remember for your campaign to succeed you must plan it with community leaders.</p>

<p>Most of the health problems found in our communities indicate the need for environmental improvement. Most surveys reveal need for housing improvement, construction of more latrines, protection of springs, and improvement of food storage. These require major education and motivation campaigns involving village health committees in order to get the necessary improvements in place.</p>
<p>The community must take action and the initiative to bring about the necessary changes. There is also need for intersectoral collaboration in order to bring in useful expertise from other sectors, such as agriculture.</p>
