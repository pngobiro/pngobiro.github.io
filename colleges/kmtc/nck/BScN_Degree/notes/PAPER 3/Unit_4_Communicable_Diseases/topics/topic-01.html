Introduction & Objectives

A disease that is passed from one person to another person is called a communicable or transmissible disease. Examples include measles, HIV infection, tuberculosis, chickenpox, gonorrhoea, scabies, malaria, cholera, and roundworms.

Communicable diseases are important because many are common, serious (causing death/disability), can cause epidemics, and are often preventable.

02

Patterns in the Community

Disease occurrence depends on the balance between the Agent (organism), the Host (person), and the Environment.

Endemic vs. Epidemic

  • Endemic Disease: Occurs in a population at a constant rate over several years. The balance between agent, host, and environment is fairly constant.
  • Epidemic Disease: Occurs in a population at a higher rate than normal over a given time interval. This happens when the balance shifts in favor of the agent (e.g., many non-immune people).

03

The Host and Infection

An infection occurs when a micro-organism invades a host and begins to reproduce.

Stages of Infection

  • Incubation Period: Time between infection and appearance of symptoms. The host is unaware of the infection.
  • Sub-clinical Infection: No clear signs/symptoms. Host immune system fights the agent. Host may be a "carrier" and infect others (e.g., HIV).
  • Clinical Infection: Detectable symptoms and signs appear. Agent has multiplied and affected body function.

Parts of the Cycle

  • 1

    The Source (Reservoir): Where the organism spreads from (infected person, animal, soil). Humans are the main reservoir for most human communicable diseases.

  • 2

    Transmission Route: How it leaves the source and reaches the new host (Direct contact, Vectors, Faecal-oral, Airborne, Transplacental, Blood contact).

  • 3

    Susceptible Host: An individual with low resistance/immunity (due to no prior contact, immunosuppression like AIDS, malnutrition).
