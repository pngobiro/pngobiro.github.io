01

Overview

Airborne diseases enter the body via the respiratory tract. Pathogens are discharged in fluid droplets when an infected person talks, coughs, or sneezes. Small droplets remain airborne; larger ones fall and mix with dust.

üí°

Prevention Focus

Good ventilation, avoiding overcrowding, and respiratory etiquette (covering mouth) are critical.

02

Common Viral Infections

1. Measles

Highly contagious viral infection. Major cause of child mortality.

  • Signs: 3Cs (Cough, Coryza, Conjunctivitis), Koplik's spots (white spots in mouth), and a characteristic rash that "matches" down the body.
  • Management: Vitamin A (200,000 IU), fluids, paracetamol. Admit if complicated (pneumonia, dehydration).
  • Prevention: BCG at birth (partial protection), Measles vaccine at 9 months.

2. Whooping Cough (Pertussis)

Bacterial infection causing sticky mucus and paroxysmal cough.

  • Catarrhal Stage: Troublesome night cough.
  • Paroxysmal Stage: Severe cough with "whoop", vomiting, exhaustion. (Babies <3 months may not whoop!).
  • Management: Broad-spectrum antibiotics, support. No sedatives.

3. Mumps & Chickenpox

  • Mumps: Swelling of salivary glands. Risk of Orchitis (testes inflammation) in post-pubertal males.
  • Chickenpox: Itchy vesicles (fluid-filled rash) appearing in crops. Caused by VZV. Self-limiting.
03

Meningitis

Cause: Neisseria meningitidis. Acute and dangerous.

Signs: Severe headache, neck rigidity, irritability, confusion, positive Kernig's/Brudzinki's signs.

Diagnosis: Lumbar puncture (CSF examination).

Management: Immediate IM Penicillin or Chloramphenicol. Notification is mandatory.

04

Tuberculosis (TB)

Cause: Mycobacterium tuberculosis. Resurgent due to HIV/AIDS.

Clinical Picture

  • Early: Productive cough (3+ weeks), night sweats, weight loss, loss of appetite.
  • Late: Haemoptysis (bloody sputum), difficulty breathing, extreme weight loss.

Diagnosis: Sputum smear (AFB test) - 3 early morning specimens.

üíä

DOTS Strategy

Directly Observed Treatment short course. Ensures patients take their medication correctly to prevent drug resistance.

05

Leprosy (Hansen's Disease)

Cause: Mycobacterium leprae. Disabling and deforming but not a "killer" disease.

Types:

  • Pauci-Bacillary (PBL): Skin patches (1-5cm), nerve damage. Negative smear.
  • Multi-Bacillary (MBL): Numerous patches, positive smear, later nerve damage.

Nerve Involvement: Sensory loss leads to painless burns and injuries. Eyelid paralysis (lagophthalmos) leads to blindness.

Management: Multi-Drug Therapy (Rifampicin, Dapsone, Clofazimine) for 6 months.
