<p>This unit gives an introduction to the concept of community health and is divided into five sections. The first section looks at the concept of community health nursing and how people organise themselves within the community. The second section looks at the principles of community health nursing and the roles and functions of a community health nurse. The third section looks at vital statistics and epidemiology in the community. The fourth and fifth sections describe the organisation of health care services in the community and how you can apply Information, Education and Communication (IEC) to promote health.</p>

<p>In this section you will start by defining the community before moving on to explore the concept of community health. <div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h4 level="4" id="what-is-a-community">What is a Community?<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#what-is-a-community" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h4></div>
<p>A community is a group of people (a large or small group) living in a certain geographical area and working together for a common goal. They share the same resources such as water, climatic and geographic conditions, health services, administration and leadership, as well as disadvantages such as shortages, risks and dangers.</p>

<p>You will now look at the components that make up a community starting with the individual.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="individual">Individual<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#individual" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Each individual you know is different and unique. Each has a mixture of characteristics, some of which they share with others and some of which are part of a particular culture. Culture is all those things which people learn, share and pass on to later generations. One of the most important of these things is language, for it is the means by which people communicate with one another.</p>
<p>As a health worker you must be able to communicate with individuals in the community where you work and acquire some knowledge of the language they speak. It is important to be able to show respect and understanding for other people and their culture as well as knowing their local practices like greetings.</p>
<p>Individuals learn beliefs and customs about right and wrong behaviour as they grow up. For example, in some areas, young people are taught that dead ancestors can influence the lives of living people. If the ancestors become angry they can cause disease and misfortune. Therefore, when you teach such individuals about the effects of germs on their health status, they may not be convinced and may opt to follow their traditional beliefs.</p>
<p>Sometimes, people follow both sets of ideas – traditional and modern, whereby they consult the dispensary for treatment of the symptoms and return to the traditional healer to get rid of what they think is the 'real' cause of the illness. As a community health worker you need to understand human behaviour and the factors that influence their behaviour in order to be effective in your community health activities.</p>
<p>An individual can only attain full health if they have wholesome growth. The place where this wholesome growth can best be cultivated is the family.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="the-family">The Family<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-family" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The family is the smallest recognised group of individuals in a community. It begins with a marriage union in which husbands and wives have certain rights and obligations. It is one of the oldest institutions that mankind has known. It defies time, boundaries, cultures and human understanding.</p>
<p>It is therefore not surprising that scholars from all over the world who have studied the family of mankind do not agree on one single definition of the family. Rather than look at every available definition of the family, you will consider one which tends to agree on many aspects of the family.</p>
<p>The family is a group of two or more persons, who share emotional bonds and material things, usually live in the same household, are related by blood, marriage or adoption, and sexual relationship is socially approved for the parents.</p>
<p>The family is a very important social group in community health.</p>
<p>The family provides love, security and a sense of belonging for individuals from the time they are born. Many of a person's characteristics are influenced by their family. For example, a child learns from their family what behaviour is acceptable in the community and the language its parents speak. Families have a strong influence on what each member does.</p>
<p>Often there is an important figure in the family who makes the decisions or whose opinions are highly valued. The opinion of people in the family may be more important to a person than the opinion of a health worker. This has important effects on health behaviour and the use of health services. For example, in some communities women may not be free to go to or take their children to a health centre, even if they want to, because their husbands do not allow them. These are important things to remember when you advise individuals about a health problem.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="functions-of-the-family">Functions of the Family<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#functions-of-the-family" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The family has many important functions, these include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Bringing about a sense of togetherness and a balance between individual and shared (mutual) action by each family member; nurturance and trust; stability and integrity of the group; interdependence and the ability to meet demands for survival and development</li>

<p>Community Sub-systems</p>
<p>A community is made up of various sub-systems, all of which have a bearing on how people live and behave. For a community to function smoothly the various sub-systems must work in harmony. In the next sub-section you will look at the eight sub-systems found in a community and how they influence the health of its people.</p>
<p>A community has eight essential sub-systems, which interact and interrelate continuously. Take a look at each of these sub-systems in detail to understand how they influence the health of people living within a community.</p>
<p>Socio-cultural System</p>
<p>This system is made up of all the customs and beliefs, family and kinships, leadership and power structures in society. This sub-system exerts a powerful influence on the lifestyles of the community members, their priorities and their attitudes and values towards health and illness.</p>
<p>For example some cultural factors promote either acceptance or stigma towards a certain illness. High-risk behaviour may be a result of cultural traditions.</p>
<p>Political System</p>
<p>This sub-system is made up of the government and its development policies as well as political organisations.</p>
<p>If there is political support towards improving health care delivery, the government provides the mechanism and structure for the planning, implementation and evaluation of the health care delivery system. The constitution of Kenya contains a declaration for the elimination of poverty, ignorance and disease; hence the establishment of the Ministry of Health and several other ministries</p>
<p>Economic System</p>
<p>The government's ability to provide health and other services to its citizens depends on the state of the economy. The poorer the economy of the country, the more disadvantaged its people will be. Low economic status is highly associated with malnutrition and communicable diseases.</p>
<p>Education System</p>
<p>Education is the main tool of changing behaviour and improving individual and community health. Low educational status perpetuates underdevelopment, harmful traditions and superstitions.</p>
<p>The educational system can be effectively used to pass health related information and messages that could significantly transform the perception of the communities on healthy living and prevention of illnesses.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="principles-and-process-of-family-health-care">Principles and Process of Family Health Care<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#principles-and-process-of-family-health-care" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>In order for you to work successfully with a family and achieve your goals of promoting health and preventing disease, you must observe the following principles:</p>
<ul>
<li>Establish a good working relationship with the family</li>
<li>Plan relevant health education and sharing of clear health messages, which will guide them on how to take care of themselves</li>
<li>Gather relevant information about the family which will enable them to identify health problems and set priorities</li>
<li>Provide need-based support and services to the family regardless of sex, age, income, and religion, in order to improve their health status</li>
<li>Work in collaboration with other health service agencies to avoid duplicating family health care</li>
<li>To succeed in your family health care activities, following these principles alone is not enough. You also need to employ the nursing process approach in the care of families.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="step-1-assessment">Step 1: Assessment<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#step-1-assessment" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="step-3-implementation">Step 3: Implementation<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#step-3-implementation" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="step-4-evaluation">Step 4: Evaluation<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#step-4-evaluation" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="home-visiting">Home Visiting<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#home-visiting" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Assessment is the first step in the process approach to family health care, but when do you carry out this assessment? You could assess family members when they visit your health facility. However, in order to get a comprehensive picture of a family's health, you need to visit them at home. Home visits are an important part of your work as a community health nurse as they allow you to see families and their needs in their own homes.</p>
<p>Home visiting is one of the essential community health services that you should provide. It has two main purposes:</p>
<ul>
<li>It allows you to follow up individual families at home to find out why some health problems persist in the community despite efforts to prevent or control them, for example malnutrition, communicable diseases, or repeated failure to attend clinics, especially if the family is at risk</li>
<li>It keeps you aware of what is going on in your catchment area</li>
</ul>
<p>In order for you to conduct home visiting successfully, you need to have the following skills:</p>
<ul>
<li>Good technical skills and knowledge of preventive and therapeutic measures</li>
<li>Good communication skills and teaching ability</li>
<li>Good leadership skills and rational thinking to make sound judgments</li>
<li>Good counselling skills and an understanding of human relations</li>
</ul>
<p>During home visits you act on your own, making decisions on the spot and carrying them out. You need to be prepared. When planning and implementing home visits, you should be guided by some basic principles in order to make a success of it.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="principles-of-home-visiting">Principles of Home Visiting<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#principles-of-home-visiting" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Home visits should be:</p>
<ul>
<li>Planned and of benefit to the patient</li>
<li>Purposeful, clear and meet the patient's needs</li>
<li>Regular and flexible according to the needs of the patient</li>
<li>Educative to the patient. Home visits provide an excellent opportunity for health education</li>
<li>Used to demonstrate principles of health</li>
<li>Convenient and acceptable to the patient</li>
<li>Respectful of the patient's right to refuse care</li>
<li>Recorded in the appropriate case file</li>
</ul>
<p>If you follow these basic principles when planning your home visits, you will find your home visits fun and productive.</p>

<p>The community health nurse shares information with the patient on the reason and purposes for home visits. This interaction may occur in a hospital ward or at a clinic</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="pre-visit-activities">Pre-visit Activities<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#pre-visit-activities" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

Bishop's Computers</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="termination-phase-of-visit">Termination Phase of Visit<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#termination-phase-of-visit" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<ul>
<li>
<p>Home visiting gives a more accurate assessment of the family structure and behaviour in their natural environment.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Home visits provide an opportunity to observe the physical environment of the home and identify barriers to, and resources for achieving family health.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>At home, the nurse works with the patient first hand to implement health action using realistic resources.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>By meeting the family on its home ground the nurse will be enhancing the family's sense of control and active participation in meeting its health needs.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>It provides an excellent opportunity to implement planned health care.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>It provides an opportunity to learn about the home and family situation.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>It provides an opportunity to render health care services to the family members in their own surroundings.</p>
</li>
<li>

</li>
<li>

</li>
<li>
<p>It provides an opportunity to observe and appreciate family practices and progress of care given by the nurse and others.</p>
</li>

<p>The disadvantages of home visiting include the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>Home visits consume a lot the nurse's time and energy as well as transport fuel (petrol or diesel) or bus fare.</li>
<li>Unforeseen events may occur during home visits, which will interfere with planned activities.</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="principles-of-community-health-alma-ata-declaration-who-1978">Principles of Community Health (Alma Ata Declaration - WHO 1978)<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#principles-of-community-health-alma-ata-declaration-who-1978" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Availability of health care for all people and at a cost they can afford</li>
<li>Promotive and preventive aspects of health care</li>
<li>Integration of curative and preventive services</li>
<li>Active participation of individuals and communities in the planning and provision of care</li>
<li>Development of maximum potential for self-care</li>
<li>Utilisation of all levels and types of community manpower</li>
<li>Inter-sectoral approach</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="principles-of-community-health-hentsch-1985">Principles of Community Health (Hentsch - 1985)<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#principles-of-community-health-hentsch-1985" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Health care should be shaped around the life patterns of the population. It should meet the needs of the community.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Primary health care should be an integral part of the national health system.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Health care activities should be fully integrated with the activities of the other sectors involved in community development such as agriculture, education, public works, housing and communication.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The local population should be actively involved in the formulation and implementation of health care activities, so that health care can be brought into line with local needs and priorities.</p>
</li>
<li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="principles-of-community-health-nursing">Principles of Community Health Nursing<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#principles-of-community-health-nursing" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>So far you have looked at the principles of community health.</p>
<p>Now look at the principles of community health nursing.</p>
<ul>
<li>Community health nursing services should be available to all, according to their health needs regardless of sex, age, culture, religion, social or economic status, race, political affiliation, ethnicity or nationality.</li>
<li>A community health nursing programme must have clearly defined objectives and purposes for its services.</li>
<li>Community health nursing should not be a vertical programme. A community health nurse must work with other stakeholders in the development, implementation, monitoring and evaluation of the community health programme.</li>
<li>Community health nursing should involve the community right through the planning implementation and evaluation of the programme.</li>
<li>The community health service should build the capacity of the community to run their own health programme for the purpose of sustainability. These include training of the Communities Own Resource Persons (CORPs).</li>
<li>Health education and counselling for the individual, family and community are integral parts of community health nursing.</li>
<li>Community health nursing services should be based on the identified needs of the patient and there should be continuity of services to the patient.</li>
<li>Community health nursing should work within the community's culture and norms without compromising professionalism.</li>
<li>Community health nursing is a service and there should therefore be no room</li>
</ul>
<p>to demand favours, gifts or bribes from clients.</p>
<ul>
<li>Community health nursing is dynamic and the nurses should therefore actively participate in continuing professional development so as to keep abreast with new developments.</li>
<li>Community health nursing services should develop proper guidelines and maintain proper records and reports.</li>
</ul>
<p>Remember: The community health nurse must maintain ethics as well as a professional relationship with all the individuals and groups in the community, at all times.</p>
<p>The Roles and Functions of a Community Health Nurse</p>
<p>As a nurse, you have achieved the right to perform your duties through merit (formal training). You are trained to play a certain part as a member of a health team which is bound by the legal and moral expectations of your profession. This is what you call your role. In your role as a nurse, you have developed ideas about what is appropriate to say or do within this role. The role of the community health nurse is to prevent illness and maximise the health of individuals in the community.</p>
<p>Your function, on the other hand, refers to the job that you are expected to perform as a nurse. That is, the broad areas of responsibility which you assume as a nurse. Your functions as a nurse may vary according to your training, experience, and designation. The functions of a community health nurse are many and emanate from your role.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="statistics">Statistics<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#statistics" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Statistics is the science that deals with the mass of numerical data emanating from various activities and the environment, and which seeks to mould such data into forms from which you can draw useful conclusions. Onyango &amp; Plews (1987).</p>
<p>You can see from this definition that a statistician is primarily concerned with numbers. There are two types of statistics. The first is concerned with clarifying the hidden (but significant) relations that exist between the figures. This is known as descriptive or summary statistics. Descriptive statistics are used to describe values obtained from measurement of variables.</p>
<p>The other type of statistics is known as inferential statistics. It uses various mathematical devices to draw useful conclusions from summarised data. In community health nursing, you mainly use descriptive statistics also called summary statistics. In the next subsection you will consider the various types of descriptive statistics and their uses.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="descriptive-statistics">Descriptive Statistics<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#descriptive-statistics" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Descriptive statistics are used to describe frequency distribution, measures of dispersion (or variability), standardised scores and measures of central tendency.</p>
<p><strong>Remember:</strong></p>
<p><strong>Descriptive statistics summarise, describe and organise data.</strong></p>

<ul>
<li>Frequency distribution</li>
<li>Proportions, rates and ratios</li>

<li>Measures of central tendency:</li>
<li>Mode</li>
<li>Median</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="frequency-distribution">Frequency Distribution<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#frequency-distribution" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This is a method used to organise data in tabular form according to the frequency of their occurrence. There are two types of frequency distribution: Ungrouped and Grouped.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="proportion">Proportion<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#proportion" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="ratio">Ratio<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#ratio" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>A ratio is a type of descriptive statistic in which the numerator is not connected to denominator. It is mainly used for comparison of various populations in relation to a given variable or disease.</p>
<p>An example is when you say that the ratio of women to men suffering from malaria is three to one (3:1).</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="rate">Rate<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#rate" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>A rate is the most commonly used measure for descriptive statistics. It is widely used since it includes the events, the population affected and the time specifications within which the event has occurred. The morbidity rate (prevalence) of malaria in a community can be expressed as 20 per 1000 cases in the last three months.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="percentage-distribution">Percentage Distribution<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#percentage-distribution" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This is another type of descriptive statistic used to indicate the percentage of the samples whose scores fall into a specific group, as well as the number of scores in that group. The numerator is described in relation to the denominator per a hundred. Percentage distribution is mainly useful for comparing the present data with findings from other studies that have differing sample sizes.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="measures-of-central-tendency">Measures of Central Tendency<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#measures-of-central-tendency" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>These are generally referred to as 'average' measures. The three measures of central tendency commonly used are the mode, median, and mean. They describe how closely related the data is.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="mode">Mode<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#mode" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Mode is the numerical value or score that occurs most times - it is the most frequently occurring item or value. The mode is the most suitable measure of central tendency for nominal data (naming category type of data).</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="median">Median<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#median" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Median is the score at the exact centre of a distribution; it is also called the 50th percentile. The median is the most central value when raw data is arranged on a scale from the highest to the lowest - it is obtained by rank ordering of the scores.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="mean">Mean<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#mean" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Mean is the measure most commonly used. It is the total sum of scores divided by the number of scores being summed. The mean is the most suitable measure of central tendency for interval and ratio level data.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="measures-of-dispersion">Measures of Dispersion<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#measures-of-dispersion" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The measures of dispersion are used to measure the individual differences of scores in a sample. Measures of dispersion give an indication of how scores in a sample are dispersed around the mean. They show how different the scores are or the extents to which individual scores deviate from one another. If the individual scores are similar, the measure of variability is small and the sample is relatively similar or homogeneous in terms of those scores. A wide variation in scores may indicate a heterogeneous sample. The most common measures of variability used are range, variance and standard deviation.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="range">Range<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#range" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The range is a simple measure of dispersion that is obtained by subtracting the lowest score from the highest score. The range is the difference between the highest and lowest score. It is not a very significant statistical measure.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="variance">Variance<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#variance" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The variance is a measure of how individual scores in a set of data vary in their distribution from one to the other. To understand the calculation of variance, it is recommended that you read further on this topic from any textbook on statistics or research methodology</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="standard-deviation">Standard Deviation<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#standard-deviation" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The Standard Deviation (SD) is the other measure of dispersion. Standard deviation is calculated by finding the square root of variance - that means you have to calculate the variance first.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="looking-for-information-and-measuring-disease-in-a-community">Looking for Information and Measuring Disease in a Community<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#looking-for-information-and-measuring-disease-in-a-community" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="the-catchment-area">The Catchment Area<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-catchment-area" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>In order to look for information in your community and measure disease, you need to be familiar with the area served by your health facility. Indeed, you will agree that an effective health care provider is one who is familiar with the area from where their patient comes from and their common health problems. This area is known as the catchment area. There are two ways you can find out the catchment area of your health facility:</p>
<ul>
<li>You can outline the administrative boundary around the area.</li>
<li>You can draw a circle with a radius of 5, 10 or 20km from the health facility, depending on the distances at which people live.</li>
</ul>
<p>It is important for the community health nurse to establish their catchment area and mark it out on a map or draw the map.</p>
<p><strong>The area from where most of the patients in your health facility come from is called the Catchment Area.</strong></p>
<p>Once you have established your catchment area, you still need to know how many people are there in the area and whether they are scattered all around or concentrated at certain areas/sections.</p>
<p>Information about the catchment area's population distribution can be obtained from recent local or national census estimates. You would then have to adjust this information according to the birth and death rates in the catchment area. Another method would be to conduct a simple survey of the catchment area. This would provide you with information such as the location of houses, the density of houses, and the number of people in each house. You will learn the skills of conducting a health survey in module four.</p>
<p>One of the most important sources of information on health and disease are the records you keep in your routine work at health centres, clinics and hospitals. Outpatient cards, inpatient notes, daily registers, monthly and annual reports, clinic records and health visiting books are examples of such records. If information is carelessly or incompletely entered into these records, they do not provide accurate or reliable information for measuring disease. It is the responsibility of each health worker to endeavour to keep accurate or good records.</p>
<p>You now know how to locate your catchment area and where to look for information. Next, you will examine the types of information you need to look for.</p>
<p>In order to work effectively, you have to plan. To plan, you need information. To plan for people, you need information about people. This kind of information is called demographic information. In the next sub-section, you will consider the types of demographic information that you will use in health care planning, how to obtain this information and how to use it for planning activities at your health facility.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="demographic-information">Demographic Information<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#demographic-information" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Demography is the study of population dynamics in relation to births, deaths, migration and related factors such as age, sex and geographical distribution among others.</p>
<p>Demographic information is information about people — how many people there are, where they live, how many are born, how many die.</p>
<p>Demographic information is very useful in health care planning. Some of the common methods used to collect demographic information include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Population census</li>
<li>Civil registration for example identity card issuing, birth and death registration</li>
<li>Special community surveys</li>
<li>Hospital and health centre records</li>
<li>Epidemiological studies</li>
</ul>
<p>You will now explore each of these methods over the next few pages.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="population-census">Population Census<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#population-census" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>A census is the procedure of counting people. In many countries including Kenya, a census is conducted every ten years (decennially). In Kenya, the last census was carried out in 1998.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="population-census">Population Census<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#population-census" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Some of the information collected about each person included the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>Sex</li>
<li>Age</li>
<li>Level of education</li>
<li>Marital status</li>
<li>Profession, number of children etc</li>
<li>Migration status (where the person came from)</li>
</ul>
<p>The government uses the results of census for formulating long-term developmental plans including provision of health care services. A census enables the government to estimate the annual population growth rate. Currently the population growth rate of Kenya is estimated at 2.56% per year (2005 estimates). At the community level, it helps you to carry out specific measurements that allow you to plan for your health services more effectively.</p>
<p>A census is carried out only once every ten years so it can not tell you about what happens to people in between one census and the next. To find out more about people, you need to employ other demographic methods, such as civil registration.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="civil-registration">Civil Registration<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#civil-registration" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>In Kenya, births and deaths are recorded by the Registrar of Births and Deaths. Births and deaths are important events in a community. The information recorded in the birth and death certificates can be useful for finding out more about the people in different areas. The number of births and deaths have an important effect on the total population, that is, the population gets smaller if there are fewer births than deaths; it remains the same if the numbers remain the same; and gets larger if there are more births than deaths.</p>
<p>You should now appreciate the importance of accurately recording information on births and deaths in your community. Next, you will look at special surveys.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="special-surveys">Special Surveys<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#special-surveys" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Surveys are studies that are carried out over a short period of time in a given population to generate baseline data that is then used for describing specific situations and planning intervention measures.</p>
<p>Often, when you want to carry out a specific task in a specific area, the information from the census or civil registration is not enough. To obtain more information, you have to make more inquiries using a survey. A good example is the Kenya Demographic and Health Survey (KDHS) carried out every five years. In practice surveys require money, skilled people and extra equipment and supplies. For this reason, surveys are carried out for specific circumstances.</p>
<p>In unit five, you will look at a common type of survey method used in community health known as Community Diagnosis.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="population-distribution-and-density">Population Distribution and Density<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#population-distribution-and-density" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>In order to plan effectively, you need to know how the population in your area is distributed. One effective method of presenting this type of information is called a population pyramid. You construct a population pyramid by making a horizontal bar for each age group, according to sex.</p>
<p>As you can see from the graphic opposite, the length of each bar in a population pyramid represents the number of people of that age and sex. The longer the bar, the more people there are in that group. The shorter the bar, the fewer people there are in that group. The population pyramid for Kenya is broader at the base because there are many young children. This in turn is because there is a high birth rate. However, the death rate among very young children is also high; so not all the children at the base of the pyramid survive to reach a higher age group. That is the reason that the bars get shorter as you go towards the top of the pyramid.</p>
<p><img src="assets/images/image-20260113-34ee330b.jpeg" alt="img-0.jpeg"></p>
<p>The second factor you need to consider when looking at the population is the population density. This means how many people live in a given area of land.</p>
<p>To calculate the population density, you divide the number of people by the available land area (in square kilometres). In some towns, where there are a lot of people occupying little land, the population density is high. In some villages, where there are fewer people and a lot of land, population density is low.</p>
<p>The population of a community does not always stay the same. This is because there are always people moving in or out. One way in which the population of a place can change is by migration, that is, people moving from one place to another. When people move out of a place,</p>
<p>the population of that place gets smaller. The place is said to be losing population due to migration (emigration). When people move into a place, the population of that place gets bigger. That place is said to be gaining population by migration (immigration).</p>
<p>The tendency in many developing countries such as ours is towards urban migration, that is, for people to migrate from villages to towns and big cities.</p>
<p>As you saw earlier, the number of people who are born and the number who die have an important effect on the total population. In order to study what will happen to the population in the future (say in the next 5-15 years) you need information on the birth rate and the death rate. In the next sub-section you will learn how to measure the crude birth rate and the crude death rate.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="growth-rate">Growth Rate<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#growth-rate" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Once you know the crude birth rate and the crude death rate, you can calculate the population Growth Rate (GR). The GR is the percentage increase in population. It tells you by how much the population got bigger or smaller. To calculate GR, you subtract the death rate (in percent) from the birth rate (in percent).</p>
<p>Growth Rate = Crude Birth Rate (%) - Crude Death Rate (%)</p>
<p>In this example, the crude birth rate was 5% and the crude death rate was 1.5%, so the growth rate would be: 5% - 1.5% = 3.5%.</p>
<p>According to the 1999 census estimates, Kenya's population growth rate has fallen from 3.8% in 1978 to 2.5% in 1998.</p>
<p>Besides births and deaths, there are other things to consider while studying the growth of a population. These things include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Rate of migration in and out of the country</li>
<li>The number of women who can bear children (women of reproductive age from 15-49 years)</li>
<li>The occurrence of birth and death events</li>
<li>The rate of family planning acceptance and practice</li>
<li>The occurrence of major natural or man-made disasters such as war, epidemics, floods, famine, volcanic eruptions etc</li>
</ul>
<p>As you can see, the demographic information you collect can assist you a great deal in planning your health services effectively. It can tell you, for example, if the majority of your community members are made up of children under ten years; if this number will get even larger because of the high birth rates; and whether there are more deaths in this age group, therefore pointing to the critical need for medical services for this group.</p>
<p>Besides numbers, you also need to look at what health problems are most important in an area. You will now explore how to measure disease in the community.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="measuring-diseases-in-a-population">Measuring Diseases in a Population<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#measuring-diseases-in-a-population" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Measuring diseases in a population helps you to determine what health problems are important in your community so that you can plan accordingly. It also helps you to determine what resources and how much you need to promote health, prevent illnesses and treat diseases.</p>
<p>As stated earlier, one of the most important sources of information on health and disease are the records you keep in the routine work at health centres, clinics and hospitals. Outpatient cards, inpatient notes, daily registers, monthly and annual reports, clinic records and health visiting books are examples of such records.</p>
<p>If records are carelessly or incompletely entered they will not provide you with accurate or reliable information for measuring disease. It is therefore your responsibility to endeavour to keep accurate records.</p>
<p>When you measure disease, you should observe the following three important aspects:</p>
<ul>
<li>Measuring disease by incidence and prevalence</li>
<li>Expressing diseases by rates (with specification of time and population)</li>
<li>Accuracy in measurements</li>
</ul>
<p>Consider each of these aspects in turn over the next few pages.</p>
<p><strong>Remember:</strong></p>
<p>You must keep accurate records at all times.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="incidence-and-prevalence-of-disease">Incidence and Prevalence of Disease<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#incidence-and-prevalence-of-disease" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="what-do-you-understand-by-the-terms-incidence-and-prevalence">What do you understand by the terms incidence and prevalence?<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#what-do-you-understand-by-the-terms-incidence-and-prevalence" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="incidence">Incidence<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#incidence" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>Incidence measures new cases during a period of time among those at risk of acquiring the disease at the beginning of the duration in a given population.</p>
<p>For instance, if there were 12 new cases of malaria in your area which has 1,200 people in January, then the incidence of malaria in</p>
<p>January would be (12/1200 = 0.01) 1 case per 100 people in the population.</p>
<p>Incidence measures new cases during a period of time among those at risk of accruing the disease at the beginning of the duration in a given population.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="prevalence">Prevalence<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#prevalence" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Prevalence gives information about the total number of cases of a disease or condition at a particular time - whether new or old cases. Prevalence helps you to know how big a problem is. For example, if on the 1 January you did a survey of your area, containing 1,000 people and found that the total number of cases of malaria was 41, you would say that the prevalence of malaria on 1 January was 41 cases per 1,000 people in that area. This knowledge would help you to plan your drugs, surgical and equipments supplies among other things.</p>
<p>Prevalence measures all cases during a period of time in a specified population.</p>

<p>Finally, there are two more rates that are useful to know about. These are: Infant Mortality Rate (IMR) and the Child Mortality Rate (CMR). <p>Most errors are made by the people taking the measurements and not by the instruments used or by the patients. This type of inaccuracy is called observer error. There may also be problems with the instruments such as weighing scales, particularly if the zero reading is not checked regularly. Other errors may occur when writing down the figures on the record card. In measuring the effects of a disease or treatment, you need to take your measurements and record them accurately.</p>
<p>To reduce inaccuracies, there are three important things that medical staff can do:</p>
<ul>
<li>All staff should follow agreed standard methods; for example, how long the thermometer should be left in the mouth; and how to ask the questions in the questionnaire.</li>
<li>All staff should undergo thorough training and be supportively supervised to see that they are following agreed procedures.</li>
<li>Staff should initial case histories, physical examinations or laboratory tests which they perform so that it is clear who did them. This also helps when checking records for missing information.</li>

<p>Having learnt how to measure diseases, you now need to be able to predict which people are at high risk of getting them and work to prevent that from happening. You will learn that in the next</p>
<p>sub-section which covers epidemiology.</p>
<p>Epidemiology is the branch of medicine that studies the patterns of disease occurrence in human population and the factors that influence these patterns.</p>
<p>It studies the disease distribution and determinants in the populations.</p>
<p>Before you go further, consider the following definition of disease.</p>
<p>Disease is the inability of the individual to function, physically, mentally, socially at a level that is both individually satisfying and appropriate to the stage of growth and development of the individual - Hardley (1974).</p>
<p>Simply put, you can say that any condition that causes health to decline is called a disease. The detailed study of diseases (distribution and determinants) and their effects on the health of the people is known as epidemiology.</p>
<p>In epidemiology you look at three components of a disease.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="disease-frequency">Disease Frequency<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#disease-frequency" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Disease frequency is the measurement of how often a disease occurs and the disability or death caused. This information is summarised in the form of rates and ratios, that is, prevalence rate, incidence rate, death rate etc.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="distribution-of-disease">Distribution of Disease<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#distribution-of-disease" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Distribution of disease is concerned with describing how widespread a disease is in terms of person, place and time. In measuring the distribution of a disease you ask the following questions:</p>
<ul>
<li>Who are the people affected?</li>
<li>Where do they come from?</li>
<li>When are they affected (during dry season or rainy season)?</li>
<li>Determinants of Disease</li>
<li>Determination of disease involves interpretation of the distribution of disease in terms of its possible causal factors. Disease determinant factors are the agent, the host and the environment.</li>
</ul>
<p>In epidemiology you use data on the frequency and distribution of disease in order to help you understand whether the disease is an epidemic, endemic</p>
<p>or sporadic.</p>
<p>An epidemic disease is one that occurs unexpectedly and may give rise to many new cases in a short period of time. An endemic disease is one that is present all the time in a community.</p>
<p>A sporadic disease is one that occurs only occasionally and without a regular pattern.</p>
<p>An endemic disease is that which occurs at a constant rate over a long duration of time (several years or decades) in a given population.</p>
<p>In some parts of Kenya, malaria is an endemic disease as it is present in the population all the time, as indicated by the pink areas on the map on the right.</p>
<p>An epidemic disease is that which occurs in a higher rate than it normally does in a given population over a given duration of time (weeks or months).</p>
<p>It is also referred to as an outbreak.</p>

<p>A good example of an epidemic is cholera.
When you say that a disease is epidemic, you use the information about both frequency and distribution.

<p>Epidemiological studies aim to:</p>
<ul>
<li>Describe the distribution and extent of a disease problem in human population.</li>
<li>Identify aetiological factors in the pathogenesis of disease.</li>
<li>Provide the data essential to the planning, implementation and evaluation of services for the prevention, control and treatment of disease and to set up priorities among these services.</li>
</ul>
<p>The community health nurse has an important role to play in the prevention and control of communicable diseases. This includes participating in the early diagnosis and treatment, sending notification of notifiable diseases, tracing the contacts and keeping them under surveillance, identifying sources of disease and educating the members of the community.</p>
<p>So far you have looked at the definition of epidemiology, its components and aims.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="epidemiological-methods-and-tools">Epidemiological Methods and Tools<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#epidemiological-methods-and-tools" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Epidemiological studies are classified into two groups according to their purpose.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="descriptive-studies">Descriptive Studies<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#descriptive-studies" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

The studies involve determining the incidence, prevalence and mortality rate for disease in large population groups, according to characteristics such as sex, age, race and geographical area. These are studies of factors associated with distribution of health and disease in human population</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="analytical-studies">Analytical Studies<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#analytical-studies" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>These types of epidemiological studies focus on disease determinants, that is agents of disease causation, host related factors, vectors (where applicable) and environmental factors.</p>
<p>Analytical epidemiology studies the underlying causes of health problems. It seeks to uncover the source and mode of spread of disease; looking at multiple factors that bring about the disease in different population groups. Analytical epidemiology looks for reasons behind the relatively high or low frequency of disease in specific human groups (cause-effect relationships).</p>
<p>There are three main types of descriptive studies</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="case-series">Case Series<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#case-series" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>In case series, clinicians describe the experience of a single patient or group of patients with similar diagnosis. This is common among clinicians whereby they use the unusual features of a unique disease among patients to make a diagnosis. It is through case series studies that clinicians are able to diagnose new or emerging diseases, such as ebola and chloroquine resistant malaria.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="cross-sectional-studies">Cross-sectional Studies<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#cross-sectional-studies" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Cross-sectional studies provide information about the frequency and characteristics of a disease by studying the disease status in a population at a specific time period in a given year. Data is gathered over a large geographical area from a large sample over a short duration of time for, example a few months.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="correlation-studies">Correlation Studies<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#correlation-studies" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Correlation studies use data of the entire population to compare disease frequencies between different groups of people during the same period of time. These types of studies are concerned with how a certain independent variable is related with one or several dependent variable(s).</p>
<p>Analytical studies are divided into two types; observational and experimental studies. The bulk of analytical studies are observational. This is because they are less invasive and less likely to raise ethical issues.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="observational-studies">Observational Studies<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#observational-studies" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>In observational studies you observe events in the community as they occur. They may occur in seasons or be triggered by certain events such as floods that may lead to waterborne diseases or an outbreak of malaria. Another example is the increased incidence of fractures among children during the fruit picking season. As an epidemiologist, you need to put measures in place to prevent these problems. There are two main categories under these type of studies, that is cohort studies and case control studies.</p>
<p><strong>Cohort studies</strong> look at certain exposures over a period of time (prospectively) and how they are related to certain outcomes. Subjects are followed up over a period of time.</p>
<p><strong>Case control studies</strong> look at certain outcomes and try to establish what might have caused them in the past. For example people suffering from a skin disease may be asked to recall where they have worked or lived previously so as to understand what they had been exposed to and that might have caused the disease. They are also referred to as retrospective studies.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="experimental-studies">Experimental Studies<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#experimental-studies" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>In experimental studies, you set up experiments with controls in order to see the cause and effect. For example, you can decide to give malaria prophylaxis or mosquito nets to one community during the rainy season and deny the same to another similar community.</p>
<p>You then observe the two to see if they have the same incidence of malaria or if they have different incidence of malaria.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="the-epidemiological-triad">The Epidemiological Triad<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-epidemiological-triad" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>The triad describes the relationship between various factors that cause disease and how they interact to determine disease occurrence. The factors, namely agent and host, interact in the environment to cause disease in humans. The natural progression of a disease is influenced by the following factors:</p>
<ul>
<li>The causative agent(s)</li>
<li>The susceptible host</li>
<li>The environment</li>
<li>The vector(s) in some diseases</li>
</ul>

<p>The agent is the harmful aetiological factor that causes health problems. The aetiological factor (agent) may cause disease either by its presence or by its absence. The causative agent may be a substance, living or non-living, excessively present or lacking, tangible or non-tangible.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="physical-agents">Physical Agents<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#physical-agents" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>These include mechanical forces such as friction, extreme heat, cold, humidity, pressure, sound, radiation, electricity, etc.</p>

<p>Endocrine disorders, for example diabetes mellitus (lack of insulin), thyrotoxicosis (excess thyroid hormone).</p>
<p>Immunodeficiency (HIV infection, some drugs). The human or animal that comes into contact with and is affected by the agent is the host. Some factors within the host determine its interaction with the agent.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="social-and-economic-status">Social and Economic Status<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#social-and-economic-status" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>A person's social and economic status has an effect on both their exposure to disease agents and also their access to appropriate health care. The environment includes all the external conditions and influences that affect the life and development of an individual and their community. The environment may enhance or inhibit the interaction between the host and the agent.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="physical">Physical<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#physical" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>

Changes in the environment due to various factors may, for example, lead to pollution of water, air, soil and noise. This in turn cause disease in the members of the community.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="biological">Biological<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#biological" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="social-and-economic">Social and Economic<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#social-and-economic" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>This is the human society in which every human being lives. Men and women live among others in groups in which there are shared values, customs, habits, beliefs, attitude and culture. Conflicts among human beings lead to tension which may cause mental and behavioural disorders.</p>
<p>Examples of social economic factors include; Housing, water, lighting, sanitation, area of residence, food, education, occupation, working environment and health care among others.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="levels-of-disease-prevention">Levels of Disease Prevention<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#levels-of-disease-prevention" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Diseases evolve over time and the pathological changes become less reversible as the disease process continues. There are three stages in the development of a disease, namely, healthy, sub-clinical or pre-symptomatic, and clinical. It is obvious that the best sort of prevention is that applied before the person becomes ill.</p>
<p>A three-level model for intervention, based on the stages of the disease, was developed by Leavel and Clark (1965). The three levels of the model are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Primary prevention of disease</li>
<li>Secondary prevention of disease</li>
<li>Tertiary prevention of disease</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="primary-prevention">Primary Prevention<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#primary-prevention" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>This is the actual prevention of a disease. Primary prevention is carried out before the disease or dysfunction has occurred in the body. It is directed at decreasing the risks of acquiring the disease.</p>
<p>Primary prevention methods applied to people and the environment include: health education; environmental sanitation; supply of clean safe water; adequate nutrition; rest; sleep; recreation; personal hygiene; good working conditions; good housing; regular medical checkups; screening for disease; genetic screening and counselling; immunisation against specific disease; avoidance of home, traffic, and industrial accidents.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="remember">Remember:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#remember" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Prevention is better than cure.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="secondary-prevention">Secondary Prevention<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#secondary-prevention" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Secondary prevention aims at diagnosing a disease early and treating it even before the symptoms have appeared. This is the second stage of disease which is called sub-clinical or pre-symptomatic. The main goal is to cure the disease completely in its early stages or slow down its progression, in order to prevent the development of complication and disability. This kind of prevention often requires screening, to find the people who have the illness but don't know they have it.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="activities-of-secondary-prevention">Activities of Secondary Prevention<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#activities-of-secondary-prevention" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Screening tests to detect early the pre-symptomatic physiological and anatomical indicators of disease, for example pap smear, random blood sugar test, etc.</li>
<li>Case finding and case-tracing.</li>
<li>Screening surveys and examinations.</li>
<li>Mass treatment and campaigns.</li>
<li>Adequate treatment of disease.</li>
<li>Follow-up of treated patients at special clinics and at their homes.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="remember">Remember:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#remember" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Secondary prevention detects disease early by screening and starts treatment promptly</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="tertiary-prevention">Tertiary Prevention<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#tertiary-prevention" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>This means diagnosing and treating people who are already sick with a disease, in order to reduce suffering, cure the disease, and prevent disability.</p>
<p>If a permanent disability such as blindness or</p>
<p>paralysis remains, then special rehabilitation services may be necessary. Most of the curative work that goes on in the outpatient and inpatient departments occurs at this stage of disease.</p>
<p><strong>Remember:</strong>
Tertiary prevention diagnoses, treats and rehabilitates.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="organisational-structure-of-the-ministry-of-health">Organisational Structure of the Ministry of Health<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#organisational-structure-of-the-ministry-of-health" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The development of a country or nation is done by its people.</p>
<p>People can only contribute and participate in the development of their country if they are healthy. Thus one of the important activities of the government is to look after the health of the people. The government carries out this activity through the Ministry of Health.</p>
<p>The Ministry of Health is the main provider of health services to all the citizens of this country.</p>

<p>The Ministry of Health operates at four main levels, which are based on our country's administrative setup. The four levels are:</p>
<ul>
<li>National (Central)</li>
<li>Provincial</li>
<li>District</li>
<li>Community (Peripheral)</li>
</ul>
<p>As you are well aware, the country is divided into administrative units called provinces. Each province is further divided into smaller units called districts, which are in turn divided into locations and sub-locations.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="how-many-provinces-are-there-in-this-country">How many provinces are there in this country?<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#how-many-provinces-are-there-in-this-country" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="national-central-level">National (Central Level)<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#national-central-level" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The national (central) level is the headquarters where political, professional and administrative matters are coordinated and policy decisions made. It is headed by a minister, assistant minister and a permanent secretary, in that order of seniority. These leaders are politically appointed and need not be health professionals. Next in this hierarchy comes the technical leader of health services, that is, the Director of Medical Services (DMS). The Director of Medical Services supervises all matters pertaining to preventive, promotive and curative health services. They are assisted by Deputy Directors, who are responsible for the various divisions which deal with the different responsibilities, such as mental health, communicable diseases and health planning, among others.</p>

<p>The Ministry of Health is represented at the provincial level by the Provincial Medical Officer, who is a senior medical officer in charge of organisation and administration of health services within the province.</p>
<p>The Provincial Medical Officer is answerable to the Director of Medical Services at the Ministry of Health headquarters. At the provincial level, they are assisted by other health officers responsible for various provincial health care departments, for example, provincial matron. At the provincial level, there is usually a provincial hospital.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="district-level">District Level<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#district-level" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>The district is the basic organisational unit of the government health services. It is a key level in the health sector administrative setup because the government decentralised almost all of its activities and made the district the focus for rural development. The government delivers health care to the district population through:</p>
<ul>
<li>A district or sub-district hospital</li>
<li>Health centres</li>
<li>Dispensaries</li>
<li>Mobile (outreach) units</li>
</ul>
<p>The district health service is headed by a District Medical Officer of Health (DMOH) also referred to as Medical Officer of Health (MOH). The administrative headquarters of the district health services are usually at the district or sub-district hospital, where the DMOH is in most cases also the medical superintendent of the hospital.</p>
<p>The district hospital provides limited specialised medical services and also logistic and technical support to the health centres and dispensaries in the periphery. It is a crucial link in the administrative support and referral chain of health services being provided to the population in the communities.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="what-duties-does-the-moh-carry-out-in-your-district">What duties does the MOH carry out in your district?<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#what-duties-does-the-moh-carry-out-in-your-district" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>The duties of the MOH include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Administration of the district health services</li>
<li>Hospital work and other clinical duties</li>
<li>Training of staff in the district</li>
<li>Planning and coordinating all health activities in the district</li>
<li>Supervision of health care delivery in the district</li>
</ul>
<p>The MOH does not work in isolation. They head a team of health professionals who form the District Health Management Team (DHMT).</p>
<p>The DHMT is charged with the responsibility of monitoring and supervising all health care services in the district. Most of the members of the DHMT are found at the district hospital. The other key members of the DHMT are found at the district administrative headquarters</p>
<p>The members of the DHMT include:</p>
<ul>
<li>The District Medical Officer of Health (Chairman)</li>
<li>The District Public Health Nurse</li>
<li>The District Hospital Matron</li>
<li>The District Public Health Officer</li>
<li>The District Public Health Education Officer</li>
<li>The District Health Administrative Officer</li>
<li>The District Health Information Officer</li>
<li>The District Pharmacist</li>
</ul>
<p>The DHMT has other co-opted members who include:</p>
<ul>
<li>District HIV/AIDS/STD Coordinator</li>
<li>District Physiotherapist</li>
<li>District Clinical Officer</li>
<li>District Nutritionist</li>
<li>District Laboratory Technologist</li>
<li>District Orthopaedician</li>
</ul>
<p>Important functions of the DHMT include:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Formulating relevant health objectives for the district in keeping with the provincial and national health policies.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Identifying health problems and needs in the district.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Training and deployment of staff to health facilities.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Planning and coordinating health activities for optimal utilisation of district resources.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Supervising all health care activities and services within the district.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Collecting and analysing data on community health needs and assessing health coverage.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Monitoring and supporting the rural health staff and community health workers.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Licensing health facilities/clinics.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Remember:
All health services and activities within the district are monitored and supervised by the DHMT.</p>
<p>Since all community health nurses are supervised by the district public health nurse, it is important that you understand their duties and responsibilities.</p>
<p>The District Public Health Nurse</p>
<p>The District Public Health Nurse (DPHN), also known as the District Community Health Nurse, is an important member of the DHMT and is responsible to the DMOH (is supervised by the DMOH).</p>
<p>The main duties and responsibilities of the DPHN are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Planning, organising and supervising all community health activities in the district.</li>
<li>Deploying nursing staff to community/rural health facilities.</li>
<li>Conducting staff update courses.</li>
<li>Collecting health information and compiling reports about community health services.</li>
<li>Planning and coordinating health campaigns.</li>
<li>Procurement, storage and distribution of EPI vaccines.</li>
<li>Implementing health development projects for the district development committee.</li>
</ul>
<p>Community/Peripheral Level</p>
<p>The last level in this hierarchy is the community/peripheral level. This is really at the community level where there are divisions, locations and sub-locations. Here health centres, dispensaries and in some places, community based health workers provide basic curative, promotive and preventive services. They may be augmented by the activities of special programmes, such as KEPI, or various mobile services. These health services together form the backbone of rural health service where about 80% of the population live.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="health-sector-reforms">Health Sector Reforms<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#health-sector-reforms" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Many Kenyans have for a long time lacked access to basic health care, safe drinking water, proper sanitation and adequate nutrition. Kenyans suffer from preventable or easily treatable diseases.</p>
<p>Old diseases which had earlier been contained, such as tuberculosis, have come back, while new diseases and viruses like HIV/AIDS have emerged.</p>
<p>The deteriorating situation in health caused the government of Kenya to develop a Health Policy Framework Paper in 1999 outlining the strategies for the development and management of the health services in the country. To implement this paper, a Health Sector Reform Secretariat was established to spearhead the reform process.</p>
<p>The effects and institutional arrangements put in place to implement the Kenya's Health Policy Framework Paper (KHPFP) were slow. The downward trend in the poor health of Kenyans continued to raise concern to both the Ministry of Health and other key stakeholders in the health sector.</p>
<p>Many consultative meetings and workshops were held under the guidance of the Ministry of Health to explore ways in which the KHPFP policy objectives could be translated into actions to reverse the deteriorating health situation in the country.</p>
<p>From these meetings, the National Health Sector Strategic Plan 1999-2004 (NHSSP) was established to address the constraints in the health sector. This was a five year strategic plan which started in 1999. It had the following objectives:</p>
<ul>
<li>To ensure the equitable allocation of government resources to reduce disparities in health resources distribution.</li>
<li>To increase the cost effectiveness and the cost efficiency of resource allocation and use.</li>
<li>To continue to manage population growth.</li>
<li>To enhance the regulatory role of the government in all aspects of the health care provision.</li>
<li>To create an enabling environment for increased private sector and community involvement in health service provision and financing.</li>
<li>To increase and diversify per capita financial flows in the health sector.</li>
</ul>
<p>The achievement of these objectives required a lot of effort and resources at a time when the government was faced with donor embargos and other problems. The government of Kenya, through the Ministry of Health, came up with a strategy called the National Priority Health Package. This package ranks the health problems in Kenya according to the associated morbidity and mortality.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="high-priority-health-packages">High Priority Health Packages<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#high-priority-health-packages" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Malaria prevention and treatment package.</li>
<li>Reproductive health package.</li>
<li>HIV/AIDS/TB prevention and management package.</li>
<li>Integrated Management of Childhood Illnesses (IMCI).</li>
<li>Expanded program on immunisation.</li>
<li>Control and prevention of major environmental health related communicable diseases such as cholera, typhoid, dysentery and food safety.</li>
</ul>

<li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="concepts-and-principles-of-iec">Concepts and Principles of IEC<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#concepts-and-principles-of-iec" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h4 level="4" id="what-is-iec">What is IEC?<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#what-is-iec" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h4></div>
<p>IEC is a systematic attempt to influence positively the attitudes, practices and behaviour of individuals, families and the community, to adopt healthy living.</p>
<p>Information, Education and Communication is a process that empowers individuals, family and communities to make decisions concerning their health. It encourages people to change their behaviours and social conditions.</p>
<p>IEC combines strategies, approaches and methods that enable communities, groups and organisations to play active roles in preventing diseases, thereby promoting and sustaining good health.</p>
<p>With IEC, activities are developed based upon the assessed needs, sound planned educational principles and continuous monitoring and reviewing of the objectives and planned activities.</p>
<p><img src="assets/images/image-20260113-8f2ac74d.jpeg" alt="img-5.jpeg"></p>
<p>In order to increase access to knowledge by different people on various issues, products and behaviour, health information must be communicated through various channels. These include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Face-to-face or interpersonal communication such as individual discussions, counselling sessions, group discussions and community meetings or events</li>
<li>Mass media such as newspapers, radio and television</li>
</ul>
<p>The main reason of using a variety of media is because it has been found that people are influenced differently by different media. Take the youth for example, they get more information through music and so radio is a powerful tool for them. Adult learners on the other hand prefer discussions and interpersonal communication.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h4 level="4" id="what-is-the-purpose-of-iec">What is the purpose of IEC?<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#what-is-the-purpose-of-iec" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h4></div>
<p>Some of the main purposes of IEC are to:</p>
<ul>
<li>Inform the people about health and health related issues relevant to them</li>
<li>Dispel rumours, fears and misconceptions about certain health issues and to reassure them</li>
<li>Motivate the patients and members of communities to seek health care when sick and promote healthful living by keeping good habits and behaviour</li>
<li>Empower the individuals, families and the community to participate in the implementation of programmes that promote healthful living</li>
</ul>
<p>IEC therefore is not only health education but it is a comprehensive approach. It builds the capacity of communities, families and individuals</p>
<p>Bishop's Computers
Bishop's Computers</p>
<p>by giving them relevant information communicated in a way that they can understand, internalise and take appropriate action. The information may not necessarily be directly health information as long as it will impact on people's life. For example you can give people information on the cash crops they should grow even if it means getting the Agriculture Officer to show them. Cash crops can help in alleviating poverty which as you know has a negative impact on people's health. Now move on to briefly look at factors that influence adult learning. You will learn adult education in more detail when you come to the unit on Teaching Methodology in module four.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="factors-that-influence-adult-learning">Factors that Influence Adult Learning<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#factors-that-influence-adult-learning" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>From your own experience, you may have realised that various conditions and reasons influence adult learning. You are now going to look at these conditions and reasons that promote learning in the adults.</p>
<p>What are some of the factors that influence adult learning?</p>
<p>As you may have indicated adults learn faster and better when:</p>
<ul>
<li>They make the decision to learn on their own</li>
<li>The information or skills acquired are of immediate use to them</li>
<li>Information or skills given/shared relate to their immediate or future activity</li>
<li>They know what to expect or what is expected of them</li>
<li>Learning takes place in an atmosphere of trust, acceptance and encouragement</li>
<li>There are no distractions during the learning session</li>
<li>The information to be learnt is clear and relevant to their needs</li>
<li>They are involved in what is being taught and are actively involved in the learning activities</li>
<li>The material being learnt is based or related to what they already know</li>
<li>They receive immediate feedback from the teacher about their performance</li>
</ul>
<p>Most of the teaching that you are going to carry out as a community health nurse will be focused on adults. You are going to be talking to the youth, parents, family planning patients, household heads, community members and community leaders.</p>
<p>Since learning is a process of acquiring knowledge, skills and attitudes, which results in modification or change in thinking and behaviour, you should always bear in mind that the people you are teaching have some knowledge no matter how inaccurate it may be. You should therefore start with what they know and what they would like to hear. Once you have their confidence, it is easy to then carry them with you. Always avoid offending people even when tackling the harmful cultural habits, you have to be very tactful and make them diagnose the problem themselves. Use problem-based learning which makes the learners discuss their problems more openly and search for solutions. You will learn about this in greater detail in the Teaching Methodology unit in module four.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="mobilising-iec-materials">Mobilising IEC Materials<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#mobilising-iec-materials" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>As mentioned earlier, when you are sharing information you should not assume what the learner's level of knowledge is. It is therefore very important for you to prepare yourself and put together all the necessary resources you will need. This is what is known as mobilising the IEC materials.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="what-are-some-of-the-materials-you-may-require-during-iec">What are some of the materials you may require during IEC?<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#what-are-some-of-the-materials-you-may-require-during-iec" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The materials you need to mobilise depend on the message you want to communicate. For example, if you are talking to a group of mothers about a weaning diet, it may be necessary that you put together nutritious foods which are locally available and culturally acceptable to give an infant. If you are talking about family planning, you will need to have all the contraceptives methods as you may even have to demonstrate, for example wearing a condom. In some health talks, you might not get a chance to have the actual items on display but a poster showing the same will do. A good example is the poster shown above. Before you use a poster, you should ensure that it is developed and pre-tested on the target audience in order to ensure that the community understands the message you want to communicate.</p>
<p>Mobilising materials may also mean, checking the channels/media of communication so that the individuals, families and indeed entire communities can get the message as and when planned. The media may be print, radio, television or face-to-face. Some of the materials</p>

Bishop's Computers</p>
<p>may be the actual items such as food, while others may be in form of handouts or posters. There are many posters, charts, calendars and even pamphlets that have been developed for different messages which you may have seen in your health facility. These are some of the resources that you may have to assemble before you start your talk.</p>
<p>Organising resources does not only include the media that you want to use but also may mean organising the space you are going to use or even seeking permission to organise such a meeting.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="what-is-meant-by-organising-space-or-seeking-permission">What is meant by organising space or seeking permission?<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#what-is-meant-by-organising-space-or-seeking-permission" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Whether you are talking to an individual, family or community, you need a place where you can all assemble. You should check the place to ensure that it has a conducive environment for a talk. For example, you do not want to be in a noisy place or in an open space when it is either raining or very sunny. Once you select the place you will need to check on the seating arrangements if you want people to be seated and make the necessary arrangements.</p>
<p>As for seeking permission to hold a meeting, it is important to operate within the law as this could affect your programme. If you need permission to hold a Baraza, it is important for you to seek it from the relevant authorities. In fact it may be helpful to involve the authorities concerned, such as the chiefs and other respected members of that community. This means you would have to brief them well in advance and give them detailed plans.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="organising-health-education-talks">Organising Health Education Talks<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#organising-health-education-talks" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This is a method of teaching/learning in which relevant health messages are shared with individuals or groups to enable them to meet identified health needs and make informed decisions about their lives.</p>
<p>Before organising a health talk, it is very important, you understand the health problems and needs of the individual, family or the particular group that you are going to talk to.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="why-is-it-important-for-you-to-understand-the-health-problems-of-your-target-group">Why is it important for you to understand the health problems of your target group?<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#why-is-it-important-for-you-to-understand-the-health-problems-of-your-target-group" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Knowing the problems affecting the target audience of your talk and what they need to solve the particular problem is important because it:</p>
<ul>
<li>Enables you to determine the desires, concerns and interest of the client/community and identify what information will be presented</li>
<li>Helps you to ensure that the health talk is aimed at the current needs of the client/community and what materials and resources are needed</li>
<li>Enables you to set specific objectives thereby assisting the learners to know what is covered in the discussion</li>
<li>Enables you to evaluate the outcome of learning</li>
</ul>
<p>Whether the aim of your health talk is to educate, dispel rumours/misconceptions about health problems or to provide instructions and/or demonstrations on health actions and practices, you must organise it well so as to effect any change.</p>
<p>You should bear in mind:</p>
<ul>
<li>The individuals or community's health needs and problems</li>
<li>The need for clear, simple culturally relevant health messages</li>
<li>The use of appropriate teaching/learning materials (posters, charts or models)</li>
<li>The need to respect and work with Community's Own Resource Persons (CORPS)</li>
<li>The need to empower and work with others (volunteers, field health workers, staff, community based health workers etc.) in promoting IEC</li>
<li>The need to use counselling skills in health talks</li>
</ul>
<p>During health talks, you should give the client/audience the right information through direct or face-to-face communication. The process of communication in teaching is covered in detail in the Teaching Methodology unit in module four.</p>
