üëã Introduction

Welcome to Section One of the Environmental Health unit. Since the environment comprises all things that make up your surroundings, environmental health describes the aspects of health related to or emanating from your interaction with the environment.

üéØ Objectives
  • Define the environment
  • Explain the components of the environment
  • Describe the effects of environmental factors on health
02

üß© Environmental Components

The following factors can have an effect on your health:

  • Biological Environment

    Living things including plants, people, and animals.

  • Physical Environment

    Geographical (climate, soil, altitude) and man-made components.

  • Socio-cultural Environment

    Customs, beliefs, food habits, and practices.

  • Economic and Political Environment

    Work, money, government policies, and leadership.

03

üå± Biological Environment

The biological component of the environment is made up of living things: plants, people, and animals.

Plants

Vegetation prevents soil erosion, protects water sources, and acts as windbreakers. They provide food (vegetables, fruits) and medicinal value (e.g., Neem tree for malaria, Aloe vera for wounds). However, some plants can cause allergies (hay fever) or be poisonous.

People

Human activities and overcrowding can promote disease transmission (e.g., in slums). Activities like quarrying produce dust causing respiratory issues. Deforestation and riverbank cultivation degrade the environment.

Animals

Domestic animals provide food (meat, milk) and manure but can transmit zoonotic diseases (e.g., rabies from dogs). Wild animals attract tourism but snakebites and insect vectors (mosquitoes, houseflies) pose health risks.

04

üèîÔ∏è Physical Environment

Divided into geographical and man-made components.

  • Climate & Altitude: Determine crops (tea in cool areas, oranges in hot). Cold climates encourage respiratory diseases; hot climates favor vectors like mosquitoes.
  • Pollution: Industrial waste, smoke, pesticides, and fertilizers spoil natural resources (air, water, soil).
‚ö†Ô∏è Key Reminder

Most micro-organisms that cause disease are transmitted through air, water, and food. Dampness in houses favors airborne diseases.

05

üë• Socio-Cultural & Economic Factors

Socio-Cultural

Includes food habits, taboos, and practices.

  • Food Habits: Vary by community (e.g., Githeri vs. Ugali).
  • Taboos: Restrictions on pregnant women (e.g., avoiding certain meats).
  • Harmful Practices: Wife inheritance (HIV/AIDS risk), FGM (delivery complications), unsafe circumcision.

Economic & Political

Economy: Determines quality of environmental health. Poverty may lead to stress and alcoholism; affluence to lifestyle diseases (obesity).

Development: Projects like irrigation improve income but creates breeding sites for vectors (mosquitoes, snails).

Politics: Government policies enforce environmental health. Political instability disrupts health services.
