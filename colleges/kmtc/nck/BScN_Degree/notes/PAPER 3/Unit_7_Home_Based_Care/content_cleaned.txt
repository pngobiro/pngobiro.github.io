<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="introduction">Introduction<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#introduction" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>In the last two decades, there have been dramatic changes in the health needs of our</p>
<p>populations due to the rise in non-communicable diseases, terminal illnesses, injuries leading to disability and HIV/AIDS. These changes have led to an increase in the need for long-term care and the need for care to manage everyday living.</p>
<p>To meet this challenge, the Ministry of Health (MoH) has had to adopt a different approach to health sector policy and health care services. It has adopted the home-based care approach. In this section, you shall look at the concepts and principles of home-based care.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="the-concept-of-home-based-care">The Concept of Home-based Care<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-concept-of-home-based-care" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>You will start by trying to understand the meaning of home-based care.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="how-would-you-define-home-based-care">How would you define home-based care?<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#how-would-you-define-home-based-care" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Home-based care is the care of persons with chronic or terminal illnesses extended from the hospital or health facility to the patients' homes through family participation and community involvement within available resources and in collaboration with health care workers.</p>
<p>It is a holistic and comprehensive care which relies on collaboration between the hospital, the family of the patient, and the community, in order to enhance the quality of life of the patients and their families. In home-based care, the care of the patient is extended from the</p>
<p>Bishop's Computers</p>
<p>Bishop's Computers</p>
<p>hospital or health facility where they are initially seen to their homes. This therefore implies that these patients require certain services. These services form the components of home-based care.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="components-of-home-based-care">Components of Home-based Care<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#components-of-home-based-care" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>There are four components of comprehensive home-based care.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="clinical-care">Clinical Care<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#clinical-care" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Clinical care comprises early diagnosis, rational and targeted treatment and planning for the care of persons suffering from chronic or terminal and debilitating illnesses. The patients who are assessed and referred for home-based care need the continuum of care extended rationally. If a patient has not been well diagnosed and treated, don't you think the purpose and spirit of home-based care would be defeated?</p>
<p>Can you think of reasons why a patient may not receive good quality home-based care? One reason is that complications may arise from the disease that the patient is suffering from. When a patient is well diagnosed and rationally cared for at a health facility, it is easier to anticipate and plan for complications and how to manage them.</p>
<p>There are four components of comprehensive home-based care.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="nursing-care">Nursing Care<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#nursing-care" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Patients for HBC need nursing care to promote and maintain good health, hygiene and nutrition. As a community health nurse, it is your responsibility to provide this care and extend it to the home. By training family and community members, it is possible to extend the continuum of care to the home. Do you think this is possible? What do you think happens to all those patients who are discharged from hospital with residual effects of diseases and complications? Somehow, their families, friends and community provide some form of nursing care. In home-based care, care is extended by contributing skills together with other professionals and also training family and community members to give care to those that require it.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="counselling-and-psychospiritual-care">Counselling and Psychospiritual Care<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#counselling-and-psychospiritual-care" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The main aim of providing care to people with chronic and terminal illnesses and injuries is to prolong their life and make it bearable. This cannot happen unless there is positive living and</p>
<p>decisions are made on the basis of informed choice. Counselling and psychospiritual care reduces stress and anxiety for both the patients and their families. It also helps individuals to make informed decisions on say HIV testing, planning for the future, making behavioural changes and involving sexual partner(s) in such decisions.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="social-support">Social Support<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#social-support" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>On many occasions when patients are discharged from health facilities, you fail to realise the network of social and support services that they can benefit from. Patients with chronic and debilitating conditions need information and referral to support groups such as church organisations, youth groups and other social organisations. They also need to be referred to welfare services provided by social workers, children's department and other services, which may be provided by various governmental and non-governmental organisations. These individuals also need to be accepted by society and get involved in community activities depending on their capabilities.</p>
<p>These individuals and their families may also require legal advice and material assistance. You should never forget that these services compliment the care that is given in your health facilities.</p>
<p>These components will be revisited in further detail in another section that will focus entirely on them. Having briefly learnt about the components of HBC, move on to look at a summary which lists the areas in which HBC services are provided.</p>
<p>These areas are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Nursing care (personal and general hygiene)</li>
<li>Nutrition</li>
<li>Advice and promotion of positive living habits and behaviour</li>
<li>Support through counselling and links with other services</li>
<li>Treatment of the specific disease-related conditions and complications</li>
<li>Physical therapy, vocational and occupational therapy</li>
</ul>

<p>The concept of HBC, as you may have realised, does not just address any disease condition but is intended for debilitating diseases that make patients unable to care for themselves. In HIV/AIDS for instance, you do not provide HBC to those who are HIV positive but to those with advanced AIDS illness. HBC concerns those who are sick but still able to care for themselves as well as those who are bedridden and unable to care for themselves.</p>
<p>So far you have looked at the definition and components of HBC. Before you go further, first ask yourself the following questions. Why HBC? Why can't patients for HBC stay in the health facility and be cared for by trained health care practitioners? What these questions are really seeking is the rationale behind HBC</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="rationale-for-home-based-care">Rationale for Home-based Care<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#rationale-for-home-based-care" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>You are probably well aware of the big problem presented by HIV/AIDS to the health care services in this country. Demand for health services has increased due to the growing numbers of individuals who have become ill as a result of HIV infection. This has resulted in an increased workload and congestion of health facilities.</p>
<p>Hospital bed occupancy rates have increased with over 55% of beds occupied by People Living With HIV/AIDS (PLWHA). Apart from HIV/AIDS, other chronic diseases such as cancer have made people require long-term care.</p>
<p>In addition there are other reasons why the home-based concept has been adopted. It has been noted that:</p>
<ul>
<li>People with AIDS and other debilitating illnesses are discharged from health institutions where there are trained professionals and sent home to be cared for by untrained relatives with no professional back-up support. HBC will train HBC providers to offer continued quality services and also coordinate services between the community and health facility after discharge.</li>
<li>HBC helps reduce the stigma attached to some chronic diseases as the providers are knowledgeable of the diseases, which assists them to change their attitudes toward the disease and the patient.</li>
<li>People with chronic debilitating illnesses, for example HIV/AIDS, need continuity of care to prolong their lives and reduce their suffering.</li>
<li>Health institutions have many limitations such as shortage of health workers, few hospital beds and a shortage of other resources.</li>
<li>HBC offers support that will enable the patient to extend their productive lives for many years.</li>
</ul>
<p>This has meant that many patients may have failed to get the care that they deserve.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="objectives-and-principles-of-home-based-care">Objectives and Principles of Home-based Care<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#objectives-and-principles-of-home-based-care" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The main objectives of the Home-based Care program are:</p>
<ul>
<li>To facilitate the continuity of the patient's care from the health facility to the home and community.</li>
<li>To empower the patients, the family and the community with the knowledge needed to ensure disease prevention, care and support-related to the chronic illness.</li>
<li>To enhance the functioning of critical system and process to enable accessible, effective and sustainable HBC.</li>
<li>To streamline an appropriate structure for the patient referral from the institutions into the community and from the community to appropriate health and social facilities.</li>
</ul>
<p>Bishop's Computers
Page 228 of 267
Bishop's Computers</p>
<ul>
<li>To establish appropriate structures and systems for management, coordination, networking and resource mobilisation.</li>
<li>To articulate policy and advocacy positions that yield clearly defined roles and responsibilities, build strong leadership support and help reduce stigma.</li>
<li>To establish systems to facilitate provision of a comprehensive continuum of care that meets priority needs of infected and affected people.</li>
</ul>
<p>To ensure that the foregoing benefits are realised, HBC should be regarded as a holistic system of care with provisions for the following principles.</p>
<p>The principles of home-based care include the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Ensuring appropriate, cost-effective access to quality health care and support to enable persons living with chronic illnesses to retain their self-sufficiency and maintain quality of life.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Encouraging the active participation and involvement of the patient and their family.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Fostering the active participation and involvement of those most able to provide support to the community at all levels.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Targeting social assistance to all affected families especially children.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Caring for caregivers, in order to minimise the physical and spiritual exhaustion that can come with the prolonged care of the terminally ill.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Ensuring respect for the basic human rights.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Developing the vital role of home-based care as the link between prevention and care.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Taking a multi-sector approach to care and support.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Addressing the reproductive health needs of persons living with chronic illnesses.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Instituting measures to ensure the economic sustainability of home care support.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Building and supporting referral networks/linkages and collaboration among participating entities.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Building capacity at the household, community and institutional levels.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Addressing the differential gender impact of the HIV/AIDS epidemic and other chronic illnesses and care for the patients.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="advantages-and-disadvantages-of-home-based-care">Advantages and Disadvantages of Home-based Care<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#advantages-and-disadvantages-of-home-based-care" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Many authors agree that HBC has a positive impact on the social, economic, psychological and physical wellbeing of the patient, family, community and the general health care system.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="the-patient">The Patient<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-patient" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The following are the advantages of HBC to the patient:</p>
<ul>
<li>The patient is cared for in a familiar environment. Such a patient usually suffers less stress and anxiety compared to the one in hospital, clinic or nursing home. When people are in a familiar environment their illness is more tolerable.</li>
<li>When the patients are in their homes, they continue to participate in family matters. Those who are heads of their families continue doing so and can be consulted on various family issues. It is quite difficult when one is in hospital or a clinic to make a decision about, for example, which goat to sell in order to pay for school fees or which part of the farm should be tilled.</li>
<li>When the patient is at home close to family members, friends and relatives, there is a sense of belonging. This is not the case if one is in a hospital setting where the caregivers are strangers who keep changing with every shift.</li>
<li>When the patient is in close contact with familiar people they are likely to accept their conditions and illnesses. The acceptance contributes to quicker recovery or, in the case of HIV/AIDS, it may assist in better management of the syndrome.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="the-family">The Family<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-family" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Care given in the home can be less expensive than that in the hospital. You are aware that patients will pay for bed charges, food and other items, which will normally be available and shared at home.</li>
</ul>
<p>Bishop's Computers

<ul>
<li>Caring for sick people at home prevents separation and holds family members together. You will no doubt have heard of patients being divorced or separated because of illness. Others get into adulterous relationships because their spouses are not at home. This can be prevented through home-based care.</li>
<li>When family members are given education and information on diseases, it helps them to understand these diseases better and accept the patients.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="the-community">The Community<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-community" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Training in home-based care helps community members to be aware of the various illnesses affecting members of their communities. As always there are myths, misconception and beliefs especially in relation to chronic illnesses, for example, HIV/AIDS and epilepsy. An informed community counteracts these myths and beliefs and is therefore able to actively participate in prevention efforts.</li>
<li>It's cheap. The cost is shared as members contribute for upkeep and the costs of going to visit a person in hospital are reduced.</li>
<li>Community cohesiveness is maintained. This ensures that the community is able to respond to other members' needs.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="the-health-care-system">The Health Care System<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-health-care-system" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<li>HBC reduces the pressure on hospital services and hence the health system. Therefore hospitals have fewer patients to attend to and thus are able to provide quality services to those patients who require short-term care.</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="disadvantages-of-hbc">Disadvantages of HBC<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#disadvantages-of-hbc" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="role-of-the-patient">Role of the Patient<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#role-of-the-patient" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The patient is one of the main players in home-based care. When the patients are not very sick, they may provide their own care. However, in some cases they are too sick and require somebody else to care for them. Their role in HBC is to:</p>
<ul>
<li>Identify the primary or alternative caregiver.</li>
<li>Participate in the care process, but not passively, especially in making decisions on own welfare.</li>
<li>If possible, give consent on caregivers and where the care will be provided, for example, home or hospital especially during the terminal phase of the disease.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="role-of-family-members-and-caregivers">Role of Family Members and Caregivers<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#role-of-family-members-and-caregivers" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The sick person's family members, relatives, friends and other caregivers play an important role in the provision of home-based care. Their role is to:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Learn to accept and adjust to the situation, including that of the terminally ill with AIDS.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Collaborate with other care providers, for example, religious institutions, support groups, health and social institutions.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Be able to volunteer or agree on other possible caregivers that could be involved in providing the services in the family. This becomes shared responsibility on issues of referral and networking.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Learn to consult with the patients on matters concerning them.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Involve the patient in all care activities and any other family activities without discrimination.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Emphasise the need to prepare for death as inevitable and sensitise the patient about the importance of ensuring the continuing care of family members who are left behind.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Encourage and help the patient to write a will.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Remember that being present is a major support.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>As a community health nurse you need to provide education on home nursing skills, counselling, as well as information on psychosocial and material support, patient caregiver interaction and communication.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="role-of-home-care-team">Role of Home Care Team<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#role-of-home-care-team" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Home care teams are supervised by a medical or social work professional, and may be associated with a local health centre or community organisation. They are organised to provide a variety of services to patients and their families. The community health worker is a key member of this team.</p>
<p>The home care teams should be able to:</p>
<ul>
<li>Manage patient's disease-related conditions.</li>
<li>Provide home nursing care.</li>
<li>Arrange voluntary counselling and testing services for HIV.</li>
<li>Provide supportive counselling.</li>
<li>Refer the patients for further specialised care such as treatment, radiotherapy, counselling, and emotional/spiritual support.</li>
<li>Educate patient/family/community on related diseases.</li>
<li>Arrange spiritual/pastoral care.</li>
<li>Mobilise resources for support of the programme.</li>
<li>Train the caregiver on all HBC services.</li>
<li>Provide facilitative supervision to the caregiver.</li>
<li>Train the patients on how to care for themselves.</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="overview-of-disease-transmission">Overview of Disease Transmission<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#overview-of-disease-transmission" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>As you already know, micro-organisms are found everywhere in the environment - on people, animals, plants, soil, air, water and so on.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="what-do-you-call-the-micro-organisms-which-are-present-on-peoples-skin-respiratory-intestinal-and-genital-tracts">What do you call the micro-organisms which are present on peoples' skin, respiratory, intestinal and genital tracts?<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#what-do-you-call-the-micro-organisms-which-are-present-on-peoples-skin-respiratory-intestinal-and-genital-tracts" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>They are called normal flora</p>
<p>Micro-organisms which are associated with disease are called pathogens. All micro-organisms can cause infection or disease. They do this when:</p>
<ul>
<li>Normal flora is introduced into an area of the body in which they are not normally found</li>
<li>Pathogens are introduced into the body</li>
<li>Micro-organisms are introduced into the body of a person who is immuno-compromised and thus susceptible to infections</li>
</ul>
<p>In the case of HIV/AIDS, cancer and other terminal illnesses you can see that all the three situations are critical during HBC.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="infection-transmission-in-the-home-based-care-setting">Infection Transmission in the Home-based Care Setting<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#infection-transmission-in-the-home-based-care-setting" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Infection transmission in the HBC setting can take various forms. A patient can transmit an infection to a caregiver, a caregiver can also spread infection from one patient to another. Caregivers can cross-infect their family members and the community and, if the caregiver has an infection can also transmit an infection to the patient. Finally there can be self-infection.</p>
<p>You will now look at how each of these modes of transmission work.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="transmission-from-patients-to-caregiver">Transmission from Patients to Caregiver<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#transmission-from-patients-to-caregiver" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This occurs when the caregiver gets exposed to the patient's infected body fluids (blood, semen or saliva) or contaminated air through:</p>
<ul>
<li>Not using gloves or other available plastic waterproof material while handling soiled linen, blood and other body fluids.</li>
<li>Attending to a patient while having open uncovered cuts, wounds or abrasions.</li>
<li>Inhaling contaminated air, which may cause infections, for example, acquiring chest infections such as tuberculosis while caring for a patient.</li>
<li>Splashing infected body fluids, for example, blood on mucous membranes like on eyes or nose while attending childbirth by an HIV-positive mother.</li>
</ul>
<p>HIV/AIDS and hepatitis B are two diseases that are commonly transmitted by this method.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="transmission-from-patient-to-sexual-partner-s">Transmission from Patient to Sexual Partner(s)<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#transmission-from-patient-to-sexual-partner-s" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Being HIV-positive does not mean the person is no longer capable of having a sexual relationship or in need of sexual satisfaction. In the early stages of HIV infection, the person has minimal or no signs of the disease, so is still capable of passing the infection sexually to any sexual partner(s). This form of infection can be prevented by:</p>
<ul>
<li>Educating the infected person on the infection including mode of spread and all known preventive measures.</li>
<li>Stressing total abstinence (primary or secondary) as the primary preventive measure.</li>
<li>Urging People Living with Aids (PLWHA) (who are not able to abstain) to have only one sexual partner, with whom they must use condoms consistently and correctly to prevent re-infection and spread of the disease.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="transmission-by-caregiver-from-one-patient-to-another">Transmission by Caregiver from One Patient to Another<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#transmission-by-caregiver-from-one-patient-to-another" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This occurs when the caregiver does not wash their hands before and after caring for different patients. It can also occur through the use of surgical instruments, needles, syringes and other equipment that has not been well processed, that is decontamination, cleaning and/or sterilisation.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="transmission-from-caregiver-to-own-families-and-the-communities">Transmission from Caregiver to Own Families and the Communities<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#transmission-from-caregiver-to-own-families-and-the-communities" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Caregivers can cross-infect their family and the community in several ways:</p>
<ul>
<li>When caregivers do not wash their hands after leaving the health facility or patient's home.</li>
<li>When medical waste such as bandages and drug administration sets, are not well disposed.</li>
<li>When caregivers wear contaminated clothes.</li>
<li>When caregivers acquire an infection from a patient and spread it to their families who in turn spread it to others in the community.</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="transmission-from-caregiver-to-patients">Transmission from Caregiver to Patients<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#transmission-from-caregiver-to-patients" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<p>In order to break the cycle of disease transmission, appropriate infection prevention practices should be followed in HBC.</p>
<p>Proper use of these practices:</p>
<ul>
<li>Prevents post-procedure infections in patients</li>
<li>Provides safe and high quality services</li>

<p>Having practiced as a nurse, you should know by now that hand washing is the most important way of reducing the transmission of micro-organisms.</p>
<p>Scientific evidence shows that hand washing reduces the risk of micro-organisms and also reduces mortality and morbidity associated with these micro-organisms.</p>

<p>List six appropriate times for hand washing.</p>
<ul>
<li>Immediately after arriving at the workstation or home</li>
<li>After coming into contact with equipments used for patient care or contact with the patient's body fluids and/or mucous membranes</li>

<p>List four types of hand washing.</p>

<p>In the home care environment hand washing with plain soap and running water for 10-15 seconds is enough. Antiseptics are not necessary. If there is no tap or running water, the caregiver can use a 'leaky' tin or ask somebody to pour water for them.</p>
<p>Remember to teach your patients, their families and community members proper hand washing procedures. Also, teach them to use small pieces of bar soap, how to keep the bar soap dry (not moist) and how to avoid wet hands during procedures. If water is not available, then alcohol hand rub can be used. You can prepare the following hand rub solution at the health facility and supply your patients:</p>
<p>2ml of glycerine or propylene glycol or sorbitol and 100ml of 60-90% alcohol.</p>

<p>Plain water removes 50% of transient micro-organisms, while soap removes 80-90% of micro-organisms.</p>
<p>Gloves provide a strong barrier against micro-organisms. They can also protect us against hazardous chemicals. In the home care setting, caregivers should always wear gloves to prevent direct contact with body fluid. If gloves are not available, they can use plastic bags.</p>
<p>They should also protect their feet when cleaning body fluids spilled on the floor. Remember, the use of two pairs of gloves at the same time (double gloves) is not recommended because one may slip off. It is also not cost effective</p>

<p>Can you remember how antiseptics and disinfectants are defined?</p>
<p>As a reminder, it was said that antiseptics are agents used on the skin and mucous membranes to remove or kill micro-organisms without causing damage and irritation to the skin or mucous membranes.</p>
<p>Disinfectants are chemical agents used to kill micro-organisms on inanimate objects, for example, instruments.</p>
<p>List six antiseptics commonly used in your practice and at home.</p>
<p>Your answer should include the following antiseptics:</p>
<ul>
<li>Alcohol (60-90% ethyl isopropyl)</li>
<li>Chlorhexidine (hibitane, hibiclens, hibiscrub savlon)</li>
<li>Hexachlorophene</li>
<li>Iodine (including tincture of iodine)</li>
<li>Iodophors (solutions that contain iodine in a complex form, for example, betadine)</li>
<li>Para-chloro-meta-xylenol (dettol)</li>
</ul>
<p>Antiseptics should not be used on inanimate objects such as instruments and surfaces.</p>
<p>Antiseptics should only be used for the following purposes:</p>
<ul>
<li>Surgical scrub.</li>
<li>Skin, cervical and vaginal preparation before clinical procedures.</li>
<li>Hand washing in high-risk situations especially before invasive procedures.</li>
</ul>
<p>There are two types of disinfectant:</p>
<ul>
<li>Low-level disinfectants which kill most bacteria and some viruses.</li>
<li>High-level disinfectants which kill bacteria, viruses, fungi and some but not all bacterial endophores. These can be used to process instruments.</li>
</ul>
<p>Some common high-level disinfectants are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Chlorine</li>
<li>Glutaraldehyde (cidex)</li>
</ul>
<p>Common low-level disinfectants are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Phenols (for example, carbolic acid (lysol))</li>
<li>Quaternary ammonium compounds (for example, benzylkonium chloride)</li>
</ul>
<p>Before you use any of these disinfectants and antiseptics always read the label. You should also assist your patients to choose cheap but effective chemicals for use in the home environment. They should not be contaminated and they should be well labelled to avoid accidental poisoning.</p>
<p>The advantages and disadvantages of the various chemicals that have been covered are explained in detail in module one, unit two. Review them again so that you can support other caregivers effectively.</p>
<p>Preventing Injuries with Sharp Instruments</p>
<p>List six ways of preventing injuries from sharp instruments.</p>
<p>Your list should include the following ways of preventing injuries with sharp instruments:</p>
<ul>
<li>Handle hypodermic needles, syringes and other sharps minimally after use.</li>

<li>Dispose the puncture resistant container when Â¾ full by incineration or burning.</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="what-is-hiv-aids">What is HIV/AIDS?<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#what-is-hiv-aids" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>HIV stands for Human Immunodeficiency Virus, while AIDS stands for Acquired Immune Deficiency Syndrome. The HIV virus causes AIDS by reducing the ability of the body to fight infection.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="how-is-it-transmitted">How is it transmitted?<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#how-is-it-transmitted" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>There are three modes of transmission of the HIV:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Sexual - unprotected sexual intercourse (vaginal, oral and anal) with an infected person. This is the most common mode of transmission in this country.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Contact with infected blood or other body fluids. Transfusion of blood products from an infected person/donor. Use of contaminated instruments such as needles, syringes, knives or blades. These instruments may have been used for circumcision, skin piercing, scarification, drug abuse and other traditional practices like female genital cutting.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>From an infected mother to a child in the uterus, during labour and delivery or during breastfeeding.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="what-are-the-three-phases-of-hiv-aids">What are the three phases of HIV/AIDS?<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#what-are-the-three-phases-of-hiv-aids" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>HIV/AIDS progresses through three main phases</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="phase-1">Phase 1<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#phase-1" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>Phase 1 is when HIV is present in the body but laboratory tests can not detect it. This phase lasts for up to six months and includes the entry</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="phase-2">Phase 2<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#phase-2" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>Phase 2 is the detection stage, when laboratory tests can detect the presence of HIV. However, no signs or symptoms are present at this stage. It can last from two months to several years. It is also referred to as the HIV positive phase. It comprises the seroconversion stage and the asymptomatic seropositive stage.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="phase-3">Phase 3<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#phase-3" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>Phase 3 is the phase when the person shows signs and symptoms of AIDS and develops full blown AIDS. It is also called the AIDS phase.</p>
<p>AIDS, as you know is not transmitted by casual contact, (holding hands, hugging, kissing, sharing food or drink.) You should therefore show affection to your patients and their families.</p>
<p>You are probably aware that in this country there is Voluntary Counselling and Testing centres (VCTs). You should encourage your patients, friends and family members to visit the VCT and find out their HIV status.</p>
<p>Counselling and testing has three steps:</p>
<ul>
<li>The test decision or pre-test counselling</li>
<li>The test which is done by a finger prick to collect a few drops of blood</li>
<li>Post-test or results counselling</li>
</ul>
<p>Even when caring for people living with HIV/AIDS you should maintain utmost confidentiality of your patients' conditions unless</p>
<p>they desire that others know. You should however recommend testing for partners of HBC patients.</p>
<p>People living with HIV/AIDS need to receive education on how to protect themselves, and their partners, through either abstinence or using condoms consistently and correctly and having only one sexual partner.</p>

<p>The presence of at least two major and two minor signs is enough to diagnose AIDS in a child, in the absence of other known causes of immunosuppression. In the adult, the presence of at least two major signs and one minor sign are enough to diagnose AIDS.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="major-and-minor-signs-of-aids-in-adults">Major and Minor Signs of AIDS in Adults<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#major-and-minor-signs-of-aids-in-adults" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Major: Unexplained 10% weight loss in less than one month
Persistent diarrhoea for over one month
Fever for over one month</p>
<p>Minor: Cough for over one month
General pruritic dermatitis
Recurrent herpes zoster
Oral thrush: candidiasis
Generalised enlarged lymph nodes
Disseminated progressive herpes simplex</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="major-and-minor-signs-of-aids-in-children">Major and Minor Signs of AIDS in Children<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#major-and-minor-signs-of-aids-in-children" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Major: Weight loss or slow growth
Chronic diarrhoea for over one month
Fever for over one month</p>
<p>Minor: Recurrent common infections, for example, tonsillitis, otitis media, laryngitis
Generalised lymph node enlargement
Generalise dermatitis
Oral thrush
Cough for over one month
Confirmed maternal HIV infection</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="aids-related-conditions">AIDS-related Conditions<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#aids-related-conditions" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Home caregivers need to recognise early and manage common illnesses related to HIV/AIDS in the home environment. You will now look at some of the most common illnesses in PLWHAs starting with skin conditions.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="skin-problems">Skin Problems<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#skin-problems" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>About 90% of people with HIV infection will develop at least one skin problem in the course of the disease. These skin problems can be due to:</p>
<ul>
<li>Fungal infections: ringworm, cryptococcus.</li>
<li>Bacterial infections: impetigo, folliculitis, abscesses.</li>
<li>Viral infections: herpes zoster/simplex, warts, molluscum contagiosum</li>
<li>Parasites: scabies, body lice.</li>
<li>A typical presentation of dermatoses including psoriasis, pruritic papular eruptions and seborrheic dermatitis.</li>
<li>Allergies to drugs, the sun, x-rays and eczema.</li>
<li>Tumours such as Kaposi's sarcoma or lymphomas.</li>
<li>Trauma as a result of injury or scratching with dirty nails.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="skin-problems">Skin Problems<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#skin-problems" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>In order to minimise skin problems and treat them properly, you need to teach the caregiver the following simple measures:</p>
<ul>
<li>Keeping skin clean - washing with soap and water.</li>
<li>Keeping nails short to avoid damaging the skin during scratching.</li>
<li>Using flat part of hand to rub if itchy, not the nails.</li>
<li>Cooling the skin with water or fan in cases of severe itching.</li>
<li>Applying lotions for example, calamine lotion to skin rashes, painful and weeping lesions.</li>
<li>Using any local herbal remedies that are effective.</li>
<li>Applying petroleum jelly (for example, vaseline) or glycerine if the skin is dry.</li>
<li>If it is a child or a mentally confused adult, put gloves or socks on their hands to prevent skin damage due to scratching.</li>
<li>Washing open sores with clean water and soap, keeping them dry and applying gentian violet solution.</li>
<li>Rubbing pressure areas to improve circulation.</li>
<li>Keeping pressure areas dry.</li>
<li>Frequent turning of the patient.</li>
<li>In the case of patients with incontinence, cleaning the skin in the area with warm water and dry it to avoid skin irritation.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="tiredness-and-weakness">Tiredness and Weakness<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#tiredness-and-weakness" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Tiredness and weakness are common problems for patients with HIV/AIDS. Some of the causes of tiredness and weakness include poor nutrition, depression, anaemia, respiratory disease and other AIDS-related illnesses.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="what-advice-would-you-give-to-a-patient-who-is-suffering-from-tiredness-and-weakness">What advice would you give to a patient who is suffering from tiredness and weakness?<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#what-advice-would-you-give-to-a-patient-who-is-suffering-from-tiredness-and-weakness" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Your answer should include the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>To rest</li>
<li>To reduce activities that make the patient tired or to make it easier, for example, instead of washing while standing, the patients could sit</li>
<li>To perform simple exercises</li>
<li>To take a well-balanced and adequate diet</li>
</ul>
<p>Bishop's Computers
Bishop's Computers</p>
<ul>
<li>Use of support, for example, a cane or crutches</li>
<li>Use of energy drinks and food</li>
</ul>
<p>You should teach the family how to help the patient in some of these activities. For example, they can support the patient while they are getting in and out of bed, while going to the toilet and in many other activities.</p>
<p>They can also be taught to give the patient simple exercise and massage. They should seek medical help when these simple measures do not work.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="sore-mouth-and-throat">Sore Mouth and Throat<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#sore-mouth-and-throat" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Candidiasis is a very common problem among PLWHAs. It can lead to patches in the mouth and difficulty in swallowing. Other problems which interfere with eating and swallowing are: herpes simplex in the mouth, malnutrition which causes cracks and sores in the oral cavity, Kaposi's sarcoma and dental problems.</p>
<p>You should teach the patient, family and caregiver the following simple measures on how to manage this condition:</p>
<ul>
<li>The patient should rinse the mouth with warm, salty water</li>
<li>The patient can suck a lemon and apply gentian violet in the mouth</li>
<li>The patient should eat soft foods as tolerated</li>
<li>Use of straws for liquids and soups</li>
<li>Eating of cold foods, drinks or ice to numb the mouth</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="pain">Pain<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#pain" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Pain can be caused by many factors in a patient with HIV/AIDS. It can present in the form of joint, chest, muscle and headaches, or just generalised pain. It is important to understand the main cause of the pain, whether it is due to an infection or ectopic pregnancy, and treat it immediately.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="how-do-we-manage-pain-in-plwhas">How do we manage pain in PLWHAs?<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#how-do-we-manage-pain-in-plwhas" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>There are a number of things you can do to manage pain in PLWHAs:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>The patient can take an analgesics like aspirin or paracetamol.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Caregivers can gently massage the area while talking to the patient to distract them.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>They can assist the patient to change position in bed and keep the patient's environment quiet and calm.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>In the case of chest pain, you can apply a warm compress to the area where the discomfort seems to be centred.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>If there is pain following a cough, you can hold a pillow or hand tightly over the area that hurts when coughing.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Use distractors like TV, radio, interesting stories.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="fever">Fever<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#fever" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Fever, as you have learnt from previous units, is a symptom of various illnesses. It makes the patient very uncomfortable and can be dangerous, especially in small children. Fever may cause loss of body fluids and it may also lead to delirium and convulsions, which can cause brain damage.</p>
<p>Fever may be associated with any of the following conditions:</p>
<ul>
<li>AIDS-related opportunistic infections such as pneumonia and tuberculosis.</li>
<li>Diseases like malaria, measles and meningitis.</li>
<li>Infective diarrhoea.</li>
<li>The HIV infection itself.</li>
</ul>
<p>In the home setting you can manage fever by:</p>
<ul>
<li>Checking whether the patient has a fever by either using a thermometer if available, or placing the back of your hand on the patient's forehead and the back of the other hand on your own forehead to compare the two.</li>
<li>Lowering the fever by removing the patient's clothing and exposing them to a fresh air breeze.</li>
<li>Cooling the skin by bathing the patient with lukewarm water or wiping the skin with wet cloths.</li>
<li>Give antipyretics, such as aspirin or paracetamol.</li>
<li>Giving plenty of fluids such as water, juice and soup to replace the fluid that is lost through sweating, in order to prevent dehydration.</li>
<li>If the person is weak they should be turned, cleaned and observed for dehydration.</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="chronic-diarrhoea">Chronic Diarrhoea<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#chronic-diarrhoea" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>

<li>Their stools are frequent (three or more in a day)</li>
<li>Change in character, amount and consistency of the stool</li>

<li>

<li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="coughing-and-difficulty-in-breathing">Coughing and Difficulty in Breathing<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#coughing-and-difficulty-in-breathing" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>These are caused by TB, pneumonia, bronchitis, heart problems, asthma and common cold among others. It is important for patients to be investigated because some of the diseases are contagious. <div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="confusion-fear-anxiety-and-depression">Confusion, Fear, Anxiety and Depression<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#confusion-fear-anxiety-and-depression" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>HIV infection can cause psychological effects, especially if the person is not properly counselled to cope with the situation. It can also cause mental confusion or dementia and peripheral nerve damage.</p>
<p>Mental confusion can be due to the direct effect of the virus on the brain, head injury, or severe depression. Depending on the degree, this can be a serious disability. Patients can present with inability to concentrate, loss of memory, slow thinking, poor short-term memory and personality change. In late stages, about half develop motor dysfunctions like weakness of one part of the body, tremors, inability to walk and incontinence of stool and urine. In anxiety and depression, patients have feelings of nervousness, fear, sadness and hopelessness.</p>
<p>Some of the physical and mental symptoms of anxiety are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Lack of appetite.</li>
<li>Feeling short of breath.</li>
<li>Shaking, sweating and feeling faint.</li>
<li>Palpitations, tingling sensations.</li>
<li>Insomnia, difficulty to concentrate.</li>
<li>Feeling out of control, worried and irritable.</li>
</ul>
<p>Signs and symptoms of depression are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Feelings of hopelessness and helplessness.</li>
<li>Sleeping too much or too little.</li>
<li>Eating too much or loss of appetite.</li>
<li>Withdrawal from normal activities.</li>
<li>Comments about wanting to commit suicide.</li>
</ul>
<p>The home-care for patients who have fear, anxiety, depression and confusion comprises:</p>
<ul>
<li>Keeping loose and dangerous objects out of the reach of the patient.</li>
<li>Helping the person to stand and walk about.</li>
<li>Ensuring the person is always accompanied.</li>
<li>Keeping medicines out of the way.</li>
<li>Ensuring that the patient rests, eats properly and is well groomed.</li>
<li>Spiritual guidance from friends, counsellors and religious persons.</li>
<li>Encouraging peer contact and identifying community persons and groups dealing with PLWHAs.</li>
<li>Discouraging use of recreational drugs.</li>
<li>Letting the patient know that their feelings are normal.</li>
<li>Communicating unconditional love by the family and friends.</li>
<li>Assisting patients to plan their daily, weekly and monthly activities.</li>
<li>Assisting patients to relax and teaching them that feelings of depression and anxiety are normal.</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="cancer-prevention">Cancer Prevention<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#cancer-prevention" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Cancer prevention aims at ensuring that there are fewer cases of cancer. Cancer prevention measures are divided into three: primary, secondary and tertiary.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="primary-prevention">Primary Prevention<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#primary-prevention" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This involves asking people to stop smoking, drinking excessive alcohol, decrease their fat intake, limit exposure to the sun in fair skinned people, and to limit exposure to carcinogens among people who work with chemicals and in industries. In addition, it involves protecting people from:</p>
<ul>
<li>Exposure to ionising radiation which can lead to cancer of the thyroid.</li>
<li>Developing atrophic gastritis and pernicious anaemia which can lead to cancer of stomach.</li>
<li>Early sexual activity and multiple sexual partners which can cause cancer of the cervix.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="secondary-prevention">Secondary Prevention<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#secondary-prevention" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Secondary prevention is done by identifying individuals with cancer before signs and symptoms develop. This takes the form of early screening for breast cancer, pap smears for cancer of the cervix, and bimanual/digital examination for prostatic cancer. Pelvic exams and endometrial tissue sampling are also important.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="tertiary-prevention">Tertiary Prevention<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#tertiary-prevention" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Tertiary prevention involves early and quick treatment before the effects of the cancer become widespread and unmanageable.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="home-based-care-for-cancer-patients">Home-based Care for Cancer Patients<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#home-based-care-for-cancer-patients" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The overall aim of cancer management is to control the malignancy and extend the life of the patient or cure the cancer.</p>
<p>Once cancer is diagnosed, several options are available which include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Surgery</li>
<li>Radiation therapy</li>
<li>Chemotherapy</li>
</ul>
<p>These options may be used individually or in combination. You will probably have nursed patients undergoing some of these treatment options. You have also covered the actual management of various types of cancers in the previous module.</p>
<p>At this point you must be wondering whether all cancer patients need home-based care. Some patients may require long term treatment while others may present too late for a complete cure to occur. It is this latter group that requires HBC. As mentioned on the previous page, once cancer is diagnosed, several options are available.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="surgery">Surgery<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#surgery" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Surgery can be a form of prophylaxis. For example, patients with a history of breast cancer may choose to undergo mastectomy instead of waiting to suffer the effects of cancer. It may also be done for palliation, that is, to control difficult symptoms that could arise later. Patients who have undergone surgery may take time to heal and instead of staying at the hospital for long periods, the justification and rationale for HBC to nurse them at home can be used.</p>
<p>Patients who have undergone surgery may suffer from pain at the surgical incision, altered nutrition and a decrease in self-image due to removal of an organ or structure from their body such as a breast. Other more acute problems, such as infections and bleeding, can be managed at the health facility in the immediate post-operative period.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="radiation-therapy-radiotherapy">Radiation Therapy (Radiotherapy)<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#radiation-therapy-radiotherapy" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This is the use of high energy ionising radiation to kill tumour cells. It can be used singly or in combination with other treatment options. Radiotherapy may be given over a period of time with varying side effects. These effects can be managed while the patient is at home.</p>
<p>Patients who are on radiotherapy may have bone marrow depression, frequent infections, skin changes such as drying, allopecia, nausea and vomiting, diarrhoea, anaemia and taste alterations, among others.</p>

Bishop's Computers</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="chemotherapy">Chemotherapy<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#chemotherapy" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Various drugs and drug combinations are used in the treatment of cancers. You will have come across some of these drugs. They include vincristine, bleomycin, doxorubicin, cytarabine, methotrexate and mitomycin. These drugs have many side effects. These include: bone marrow depression, nausea and vomiting, stomatitis, alopecia and problems affecting fertility, kidneys, lungs and nervous system.</p>
<p>The management of these effects require HBC services.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="what-hbc-measures-can-be-instituted-to-help-cancer-patients">What HBC measures can be instituted to help cancer patients?<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#what-hbc-measures-can-be-instituted-to-help-cancer-patients" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Home care measures that should be instituted include:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Pain management. This was covered in the section on palliative care in module one unit three. Review it again to better understand pain management in cancer patients.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Close monitoring of the patient by relatives, friends and caregivers, in order to detect complications of surgery, radiation and medication.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>A nutritious and appetising diet. Certain foods may be removed from the diet such as spicy, dry food, alcohol and hot liquids. The patient should take small frequent meals which are high in calories and protein.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Good personal and general hygiene need to be practiced in order to reduce the risk of infection.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Patients with radiation implants should be cared for in separate rooms to protect other family members from radiation. Body secretions should be handled carefully. Precautions should be taken in patients with isotope administration. The caregivers should observe the following radiation precautions:</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Principle of time, which says that exposure to radiation is proportional to the time of exposure.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Principle of distance, that is the nearer to the emitting source the higher the dose.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Principle of shielding, whereby one uses materials which decrease transmission of radiation.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Pregnant mothers and children under 18 years should not visit those with radiation implants.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Careful observation, good disposal of waste and labelling should be undertaken.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>During and after chemotherapy, minimise stimuli from noise, light and odours (especially food).</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Teach the patient relaxation techniques.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Provide counselling, spiritual care, emotional support and close observation.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Patients with terminal cancer may present with depression, fear, anxiety, fever, skin problems, diarrhoea and vomiting. You should be able to use the same management protocols that were mentioned while learning about management of AIDS-related conditions in the home environment.</p>

<p>This means that all measures to provide comfort should be instituted in the home. These include positioning using assistive devices for example crutches, and walking canes providing rest and other measures to relieve pain which have already been covered.</p>

Some patients may require to be taught how to inject themselves with insulin and other drugs.</p>

<p>The spiritual and psychological care of the patient is another important area.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="home-based-care-needs">Home-based Care Needs<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#home-based-care-needs" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>HBC needs can be identified as those specific to the patient, to the family and to the community within which the patient lives. These needs may be physical, spiritual/pastoral, social or psychological and may vary from person to person, and from one community to another.</p>
<p>These needs should be identified when a patient is being enrolled into a HBC programme, for example, while still in hospital, so as to ensure proper planning and integration of activities.</p>
<p>Early identification also ensures adequate resource mobilisation and the sustainability of activities initiated.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="spiritual-pastoral-needs">Spiritual/Pastoral Needs<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#spiritual-pastoral-needs" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Strengthening existing faith and helping the patient in spiritual growth boosts the spiritual aspect of life.</p>
<p>This plays a great part in encouraging the person to have a positive view of life and to forgive others and themselves for any misconceptions and liabilities.</p>

<ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="social-needs">Social Needs<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#social-needs" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The patient and especially PLWHAs need company and association without stigma or discrimination. Family and community members should facilitate recreation and exercise at clubs/groups of their choice. People living with chronic illnesses need to be considered as people of value and having rights to be respected. The social needs of patients living with chronic illnesses include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Respect and dignity</li>
<li>Love and acceptance from others</li>
<li>Company of those around them</li>
<li>Source of income/income-generating activity</li>
<li>Right to own, inherit and bequeath property</li>
<li>Confidentiality regarding their condition by all who know about it</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="psychological-needs">Psychological Needs<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#psychological-needs" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Love, encouragement, warmth, appreciation, reassurance and help in coping with the disease are the most important psychological needs.</p>
<p>Religious groups, volunteer groups and other related support groups can all play a part in meeting these psychological and counselling needs.</p>
<p>They can:</p>
<ul>
<li>Instil hope so that the patient can continue with their daily activities as long as possible</li>
<li>Maintain confidentiality and unconditional acceptance and love</li>
<li>Provide supportive counselling to live positively</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="care-of-pressure-areas-and-pressure-sores">Care of Pressure Areas and Pressure Sores<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#care-of-pressure-areas-and-pressure-sores" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Some of the patients who require HBC are bedridden and therefore at risk of developing bed sores. The objective here is to prevent the development of pressure sores around the protruding bony parts of the body in a patient who is unable to move out of bed, as well as minimise the risk of infection and promote healing.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="list-the-steps-you-would-take-to-prevent-pressure-sores-in-a-bedridden-patient">List the steps you would take to prevent pressure sores in a bedridden patient.<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#list-the-steps-you-would-take-to-prevent-pressure-sores-in-a-bedridden-patient" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Measures of preventing pressure sores are:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Getting the patient out of bed as much as possible</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Turning the patient every two to four hours</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Attending to pressure areas by massaging areas of prominent bones with soap and water</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Using soft bed sheets and changing the bedding whenever wet</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Straightening the bedding often</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Putting cushions under the body to keep the bony parts from rubbing together</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Holding a bedridden child on someone's lap as often as possible</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>You will also require the following resources for pressure area care:</p>
<ul>
<li>Gloves or plastic/polythene bags to use as gloves if patient is soiled or has wounds</li>
<li>Stool or chair</li>
<li>Container of warm water</li>
<li>Basin or similar container</li>
<li>Plastic or polythene paper</li>
<li>Soap</li>
<li>Patient's own towel or a clean cloth</li>
<li>Body powder (if possible)</li>
<li>Vaseline or lotion</li>
<li>Toilet paper and a receiver</li>
<li>Clean linen</li>
<li>Container for soiled linen</li>
<li>Material for treatment of sores</li>
</ul>
<p>Once you have assembled all your resources, you should explain the procedure to the patient and examine the areas to check if pressure sores are forming.</p>
<p>Place the plastic paper or towel under the part to be treated first. Then with soapy hands you should gently massage each area in a circular movement and for long enough to stimulate good circulation of blood (count to ten as you massage each part) then rinse and pat dry. Apply powder and/or vaseline and ask the patient how they feel.</p>
<p>Remember to clear the materials used and to clean the equipment. Also change or straighten the underlying bed cloth as necessary and remake the bed so that you leave the patient comfortable. Lastly wash and dry your hands.</p>
<p>If the patient has pressure sores, in addition to the previous steps, you should dress the pressure sore by:</p>
<ul>
<li>Dipping a small piece of cloth into the soapy water and clean the sore.</li>
<li>Wash around the edge of the sore first, then wash from the centre out to the edges until it looks clean. If possible use separate pieces of cloth for each wiping.</li>
<li>If the wound has pus or blood in it, cover with a clean piece of cloth or a strip of cloth (torn as a bandage) after cleaning; leave it loose and change it everyday. If the sore is dry, leave it exposed.</li>
<li>Raise the area with the sore as often as possible to relieve it from pressure of the bedding (this can be done by changing the patient's position in bed).</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="general-care-of-wounds-and-sores">General Care of Wounds and Sores<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#general-care-of-wounds-and-sores" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>General wound care is provided to promote healing.</p>
<p>The requirements and procedures are the same as those for pressure sore care. However, the caregiver may utilise dressing solutions prescribed and obtained from hospitals/health institutions.</p>
<p>More importantly, you must observe infection prevention procedures by:</p>
<ul>
<li>Protecting hands with gloves or plastic bags</li>
<li>Rinsing soiled items in cold water, and then pouring this water into the toilet/latrine</li>
<li>Soaking soiled items in jik 1:6 concentration for ten minutes</li>
<li>Putting soiled clothing into a container with soap and pounding them vigorously with a stick</li>
<li>Boiling the soaked linen for ten minutes</li>
<li>Rinse and then dry in the sun</li>
</ul>
<p>While handling sores, wounds, abscesses and boils, you should be careful with the handling of body fluids. The fluids include blood, pus, urine, vomitus, stool, sputum, saliva, vaginal fluid and semen. These fluids can be found in beddings, bandages, clothing, sanitary pads or cotton wool.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="other-tasks">Other Tasks<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#other-tasks" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Always use gloves and other protective materials. If the items are reusable, make sure you decontaminate, clean them and sterilise them. You should teach all caregivers how to prepare decontamination solutions such as jik. If the items are not to be reused they should be burned, buried or disposed of in a pit latrine.</p>
<p>Other chores which you need to undertake for HBC patients include:</p>
<ul>
<li>General house cleaning and clearing of the compound.</li>
<li>Ensuring a constant supply of clean and safe drinking water as well as water for other purposes in the home.</li>
<li>Good food storage and food security.</li>
<li>Proper waste and garbage disposal and the control of vectors to diseases such as malaria, typhoid, shigellosis, amoebiasis and even plague.</li>
</ul>
<p>One of your biggest roles in the provision of basic nursing care for HBC patients is training of the family members, volunteers and community based health workers on how to carry out the different activities.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="components-of-a-balanced-diet">Components of a Balanced Diet<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#components-of-a-balanced-diet" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Foods can be divided into three basic categories, with each playing its important role in nutrition.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="body-building">Body Building<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#body-building" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>These are foods that support the function and formation of general structures of all tissues, including muscles, bones, teeth, skin and nails.</p>
<p>They include animal products like meat, fish, milk, chicken, eggs and plant proteins like beans, peas, groundnuts, green grams, lentils, sim-sim (sesame).</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="energy-giving">Energy Giving<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#energy-giving" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>These are foods which provide the body with energy.</p>
<p>They include starchy foods like potatoes, yams, cassava, bananas, sugar, wheat, rice, maize meal, bread, chapati, pasta, as well as fats and vegetable oils.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="protective">Protective<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#protective" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>They provide vitamins and minerals that are key in strengthening the immune system.</p>
<p>They include fruits like oranges, pineapples, pawpaw, mangoes and greens/vegetables, like carrots, tomatoes, all local green leafy vegetables, cauliflower, cucumber, eggplant (biringanya) and cabbage.</p>
<p>In addition, water and minerals like iron and calcium are essential to good nutrition. Water is necessary for bodily functions and to prevent dehydration. Minerals are necessary elements of the blood, bones, teeth, and body processes.</p>
<p>Examples of foods that are rich in iron are fish, meat, and dark green leafy vegetables. Calcium is derived from milk, groundnuts, and eggs. Other important minerals like potassium, selenium, zinc, and magnesium are vital for survival.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="why-is-a-well-balanced-diet-important">Why is a well balanced diet important?<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#why-is-a-well-balanced-diet-important" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>A well balanced diet is important for:</p>
<ul>
<li>Repairing worn out body cells</li>
<li>Providing energy</li>
<li>Protecting the body against infection</li>
<li>Improving the wellbeing of the patient</li>

<p>People living with HIV/AIDS and chronic diseases like cancer and diabetes have many questions about their diet.</p>
<p>Loss of appetite or difficulty in eating can be very distressing for the sick person, making them feel helpless and ineffective. The loss of weight can cause much fear.</p>
<p>All the foods the family is familiar with can be combined to meet the nutritional requirements of the patient and hence allay the fears and answer their many questions.</p>

<p>Though it is difficult to overcome some of the economical and environmental barriers, you can take the following actions to improve the nutritional status of the patients:</p>
<ul>
<li>Assess the nutritional status of all patients.</li>
<li>Identify patients who require more extensive nutrition management.</li>
<li>Identify 'high risk' signs and symptoms to patient's nutrition.</li>
<li>Work with the patient to develop an individualised nutrition care plan and implement it.</li>

<p>You should train all caregivers to look out for the following high risk signs and symptoms and take the appropriate action immediately.</p>

<ul>
<li>Appetite loss (for a long period of time, for example, several weeks)</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="common-nutrition-problems">Common Nutrition Problems<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#common-nutrition-problems" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The most common problems associated with poor nutrition are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Severe weight loss as a result of poor appetite leading to failure to meet dietary requirements. This can be overcome by encouraging small, frequent feeds.</li>
<li>Anaemia due to poor dietary intake or lack of iron in the diet. It may be as a result of infections such as malaria, hookworm or other parasite infestations that destroy red blood cells.</li>
<li>Skin conditions due to lack of vitamins in the diet, for example, scurvy and pellagra.</li>
<li>Failure to thrive or maintain a reasonably good level of health.</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="fat-malabsorption">Fat Malabsorption<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#fat-malabsorption" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Eliminate oils, butter and margarine and foods that contain or are prepared with them</li>
<li>Eat only the leanest available meat</li>
<li>Eat fruits, vegetables and other low fat foods</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="fatigue-and-lethargy">Fatigue and Lethargy<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#fatigue-and-lethargy" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>

<li>Ask for assistance from a family member, friend or neighbour to assist</li>
<li>Set time each day for eating</li>
<li>Eat slowly</li>
<li>Eat fresh fruits that don't require preparation</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="severe-diarrhoea">Severe Diarrhoea<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#severe-diarrhoea" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="nutritional-advice-for-diabetic-patients">Nutritional Advice for Diabetic Patients<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#nutritional-advice-for-diabetic-patients" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Studies have shown that many complications of diabetes can be prevented or delayed through effective management. This includes lifestyle measures such as a healthy diet, physical activity, the avoidance of being overweight and obesity and not smoking.</p>
<p>Diabetes therapy is not only about lowering blood glucose levels, but also about the overall reduction in the risk factors for diabetic complications. This includes the control of blood pressure and blood lipids. Thus diabetic patients require lifelong care and management.</p>
<p>Diabetes education plays a key role in empowering people with the knowledge and skills to manage their own condition effectively. In order to prevent or delay complications, people with diabetes may have to modify their lifestyle.</p>
<p>People with type 2 diabetes often require oral drugs, and sometimes insulin to control their blood glucose levels. People with type 1 diabetes require insulin to survive. Although insulin has been designated an essential drug by WHO, it is not yet universally accessible to all those who need it in many developing countries. In some of these countries people with diabetes die because they cannot get the insulin they need to survive.</p>
<p>Several approaches have been tried to prevent type 1 diabetes but none of them have been known to work. The prevention of type 1 diabetes remains an objective for the future.</p>
<p>However, simple lifestyle measures have been shown to be effective in preventing or delaying the onset of type 2 diabetes.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="increased-physical-activity">Increased Physical Activity<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#increased-physical-activity" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>It is estimated that currently 60% of the world's population do not do enough physical activity, with adults in developed countries most likely to be inactive. Studies have shown that just 30 minutes of moderate exercise a day, five days a week, is enough to promote good health and reduce the chances of developing type 2 diabetes.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="a-healthy-diet">A Healthy Diet<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#a-healthy-diet" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Eating between three and five servings of fruit and vegetables a day and eating less sugar and saturated fats has been shown to be important in maintaining appropriate weight, and therefore lowering the risk of type 2 diabetes.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="weight-loss">Weight Loss<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#weight-loss" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>More than one billion adults worldwide are overweight, at least 300 million of whom are considered obese. It is estimated that well over half of all cases of type 2 diabetes could be avoided if excessive weight gain in adults could be prevented.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="non-smoking">Non-Smoking<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#non-smoking" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>People with diabetes are at greater risk of dying from coronary heart disease, stroke and peripheral vascular disease than people without the condition. Smoking increases the risk even further.</p>
<p>For both types of diabetes, nutritional advice is necessary.</p>
<p>The main aim is to keep the glucose levels under control.</p>
<p>The patient with diabetes needs to be given the same advice mentioned above for preventing type 2 diabetes. It is important to understand that people with diabetes have the same nutritional needs as anyone else.</p>
<p>In addition to exercise and medication (insulin and oral diabetes pill), nutrition is important for good diabetes control. By eating well-balanced meals in the correct amounts, a person with diabetes can keep the blood glucose level as close to normal (non-diabetes level) as possible.</p>

<p>Clinical care in the context of HBC is the continuation of medical care in the home.</p>
<p>The idea is to ensure the continuity of the care and treatment the patient was receiving from the health facility. This is referred to as the continuum of care. It is collaborative care provision by the health care workers, the family members and the community.</p>
<p>The objectives of clinical care are as follows:</p>
<ul>
<li>Ensuring early detection, treatment of opportunistic infections and other complications that occur as a result of chronic illnesses.</li>
<li>Reducing the suffering from conditions associated with the chronic illness like HIV/AIDS infection, cancer, diabetic, hypertension and other chronic illness.</li>
<li>Protecting the patient against further infections especially during a long hospital stay.</li>
<li>Preventing transmission of infections from the patient to the health workers, relatives and friends and vise versa.</li>
<li>Ensuring that drugs prescribed to the patient by the clinician are administered at home according to the regimen of intake.</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="counselling-and-psychospiritual-care">Counselling and Psychospiritual Care<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#counselling-and-psychospiritual-care" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Counselling helps people to understand and deal with their problems and communicate better with those around them.</p>
<p>You have already learnt about communication and counselling in module one on general nursing.</p>
<p>You should utilise the knowledge, skills and attitudes you acquired in that unit to care for HBC patients.</p>
<p>Psychospiritual support is also an effective means of helping patients to cope with their feelings. Spiritual concerns about impending death may give rise to an interest in spiritual matters and a search for religious support.</p>
<p>Spiritual care may take various forms. These may include praying together, reading from the scriptures of the Koran or bible, etc.</p>
<p>In the context of HBC there are several types of counselling:</p>
<ul>
<li>Pre and post-test HIV counselling (Voluntary Counselling and Testing)</li>
<li>Behaviour change counselling</li>
<li>Group counselling</li>
<li>Family counselling</li>
<li>Supportive counselling</li>
<li>Crisis counselling</li>
<li>Psychological trauma counselling</li>
<li>Spiritual/pastoral counselling</li>
<li>Death and bereavement counselling</li>
</ul>
<p>The objectives of counselling and psychospiritual care in HBC are to:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Control the spread of HIV/AIDS through information dissemination, promotion of safer sex, advocacy for behaviour change and encouragement of better health seeking behaviour.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Help the patient to come to terms with the illness and to adopt a positive living attitude.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Help the patient make well informed decisions, for example, about sex and sexuality.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Offer psychological and spiritual support to patients and their families.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Help patients to assess and talk about what their life has meant to them through their belief systems, whatever they may be.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Help patients accept the need to talk to family members about their condition and future plans.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>A good counsellor must have the following tools (basic counselling skills) to be effective:</p>
<ul>
<li>Self-awareness</li>
<li>Communication (verbal and non-verbal)</li>
<li>Active listening</li>
<li>Attending skills</li>
<li>Paraphrasing</li>
<li>Reflecting feelings</li>
<li>Questioning</li>
<li>Clarifying</li>
<li>Summarising</li>
</ul>
<p>In counselling, the focus is the person, not the disease. You should be able to listen actively and respond with empathy. Because of the feelings that your patients may have, you should be able to influence your patients to be religious. This gives peace to the mind. Forgiveness and reconciliation replace anger and guilt. You can invite spiritual persons to come and talk to the sick person.</p>
<p>Many illnesses cause emotional, physical, and psychosocial pain and stress. You have already covered some of the stages of stress.</p>
<p>These include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Shock, fear and denial.</li>
<li>Accepting, withdrawal, depression, suicide.</li>
<li>Accepting help, making plans about self and family.</li>
<li>Becoming ill and weak.</li>
<li>Anger, despair and/or sadness.</li>
</ul>
<p>Counsel your patients and provide them with the basic physical needs which you have already covered. If possible provide material support or invite those who are able to provide the support. While communicating, you should be aware that this is a two way process. It should involve a response from the patient.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="how-can-you-show-your-patients-that-you-have-accepted-them">How can you show your patients that you have accepted them?<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#how-can-you-show-your-patients-that-you-have-accepted-them" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Patients should be included in day to day activities.</p>
<p>They should eat with the family, eat out in restaurants, go to social events, and celebrate events. Let the patients belong to clubs, groupings and other social structures. <p>When patients have a terminal illness they should be assisted to prepare for their deaths with good psychospiritual care and support. Many patients can live fulfilling lives and die peacefully. Patients should be given the opportunity to write their own will.</p>

<ul>

<li>Provide a sense of belonging</li>
<li>Reduce anxiety</li>
<li>Improve on relationships</li>
<li>Ensure a high quality of care</li>

<p>This ultimately contributes to a patient's quality of life.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="what-is-community-mobilisation">What is Community Mobilisation?<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#what-is-community-mobilisation" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>It is the process of getting the community incorporated to fully participate in the programmes for the purpose of ownership and sustainability.</p>

<p>HBC has the following advantages:</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="community-sensitisation-and-motivation">Community Sensitisation and Motivation<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#community-sensitisation-and-motivation" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The first and second steps in the process of community mobilisation are to organise yourself to enter the community and to mobilise people.</p>
<p>You cannot succeed unless you get people to understand what HBC is. These are the people who will act as advocates of HBC. Community members must clearly understand both your role as well as theirs, otherwise they will view you with suspicion.</p>
<p>The members must also know the importance of their actions.</p>
<p>They need to understand what they stand to gain from the process in the short and long term. If people do not understand their role and why they should participate they may withdraw, causing the initiative to die out immediately when you leave.</p>
<p>Community based health activities, for example, HBC can die out if there is no motivation.</p>
<p>You can work around this by:</p>
<ul>
<li>Identifying traditional beliefs, which are interfering with HBC</li>
<li>Explaining the disadvantages of not participating</li>
<li>Countering negative attitudes by some people by involving their friends and relatives</li>
<li>Seminars and workshops for the leaders</li>
<li>Helping plan for trips for staff from the health facility</li>
</ul>
<p>Failure of activities can also occur due to problems of:</p>
<ul>
<li>Transport</li>
<li>Punctuality and poor management</li>
<li>Inaccurate orders for supplies and inadequate equipment</li>
<li>Long distances between facilities and communities resulting in less contact and communication</li>
<li>Communication barriers</li>
<li>Natural and man-made calamities, for example, bad weather and famine making some communities shift from one area or shift their attention</li>
<li>Lack of teamwork</li>
<li>Poor referral system</li>
</ul>
<p>You can work round these problems by:</p>
<ul>
<li>Planning good orders and requisitions for supplies for HBC.</li>
<li>Being punctual when required.</li>
<li>Having mobile clinics and using local leaders to pass on instructions and other correspondence.</li>
<li>Having frequent meetings, discussions and involvement among health facility staff, community members and other stakeholders.</li>
<li>Establish a well structured referral system.</li>
</ul>
<p>Besides this, when sensitising the community, you can give out handouts; attend barazas, go to schools and churches, use film and other media and make public announcements through radio programmes about the diseases to ensure adequate knowledge and motivation.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="money">Money<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#money" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>Money is an important resource in the provision of HBC. Diseases like AIDS and cancer are long, expensive and debilitating illnesses. They eventually render the affected and infected incapable of participating in gainful employment. Yet they need money to pay for services or to buy goods such as food, clothing, drugs and other materials. They may also need to pay for health, legal and other services.</p>
<p>In the HBC system, money can be provided by the family, the community, the government or through insurance. It is unfortunate that many insurance organisations discriminate against patients with terminal illnesses. However you should work hard at sensitising the community about the needs of the patients and how they can be met.</p>
<p>In Kenya, raising money through Harambee is common and community members can come together to raise money to pay a hospital bill, buy a wheelchair or crutches. Whatever the source, your role is to sensitise members and patients on the need for the funds, and the likely sources of the funds.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="materials">Materials<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#materials" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Many illnesses that require HBC tend to render the affected persons incapable of meeting even the most basic material needs of everyday life. For instance a PLWHA may become too weak to fetch water or firewood, or run errands and do shopping.</p>
<p>Food production may be affected due to frequent sickness from opportunistic infections. Thus, the material resources required to assist can be in the form of food, cooking fuel (for example, firewood), water, or money for drugs and other purposes.</p>
<p>These materials may or may not be readily available.</p>
<p>Within communities, the materials can be bought by individuals, communities or families. They can also be donated by organisations. Some non-governmental organisations may be willing to donate the materials or money to procure them.</p>

<p>Right now there is a cost sharing policy in Kenya. You therefore need to explain to your patients how they can obtain these materials to avoid disappointment.</p>

<p>SECTION 7: REFERRAL AND NETWORKING FOR HOME-BASED CARE</p>
<p>In this section you will look at referral and networking for HBC.</p>
<p>When you work in a community you cannot work alone. The work is too much and you may not have all the resources necessary to accomplish your goals. In order to be effective you must network and refer your patients. Referral and networking are essential to ensure continuity of quality care for the patient at all times</p>
<p>Objectives</p>
<p>By the end of this section you will be able to:</p>
<ul>
<li>Describe the community networking for HBC</li>
<li>Explain the importance of networking for HBC</li>
<li>Describe the referral system in HBC</li>
<li>Explain an effective referral channel for HBC patients</li>
<li>State some of the constraints in referral and networking</li>
</ul>
<p>Networking for Home-based Care</p>
<p>What is a network?</p>
<p>A network is a group of individuals or organisations that work together, undertake joint activities, or exchange information in order to strengthen and extend their individual capacities.</p>
<p>Networking has the following advantages:</p>
<ul>
<li>It promotes unity, harmony and understanding among the groups or individuals.</li>
<li>It provides a learning experience, people and groups can learn from each other.</li>
<li>It can assist individuals and groups to address complex problems by involving others.</li>
<li>It promotes peer support.</li>
<li>It reduces duplication of work.</li>
<li>It reduces the isolation of individuals or groups working alone and provides a forum for consultation.</li>
</ul>
<p>In HBC, several networks exist. There are networks for individuals working with PLWHA, cancer patients, and so on. Several networks may also exist for people infected and affected by, for example, HIV/AIDS.</p>
<p>As a community health nurse, you can facilitate networking in the community where you work by doing the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>Establishing networking at different levels, for example, district, location and village. Involving all institutions and groups working with the disease, such as HIV/AIDS.</li>
<li>Facilitating the exchange of information between one group and another. This would prevent repetition and duplication of efforts.</li>
<li>Making sure referral channels exist, for example, from one centre to another. Letting each organisation or individual be aware of the existence of the others.</li>
<li>Ensuring that the basic essentials are available for the betterment of the patient who requires HBC.</li>
</ul>
<p>You should never forget to establish the correct links between one group and another. This is where your community mobilisation skills matter. Also remember to share your experiences and information as often as possible.</p>
<p>Referral</p>
<p>Referral is an effective and efficient two way process of linking a patient from one caring service to another.</p>
<p>As mentioned earlier, you may not be able to do all things by yourself or indeed at the same place. There may be a time when you need to send your patients or community members to other institutions or people for further care.</p>
<p>Before you refer a patient, you should have recognised the signs and symptoms or the need for referral.</p>
<p>Why are patients referred?</p>
<p>Patients are referred for the following reasons:</p>
<ul>
<li>When services or resources within reach are not able to meet the patients' immediate needs.</li>
<li>In cases where the acute phase of the disease has been dealt with, and it is considered safe to transfer care to other caring services/organisations within the community.</li>
<li>When the caregiver experiences burnout and has no access to counselling services for personal growth.</li>
</ul>
<p>Bishop's Computers

Bishop's Computers</p>
<ul>
<li>When the caregiver has limitations in meeting certain needs of the patient, for example, based on religious beliefs.</li>
<li>For better, more competent management in the next stage of referral.</li>
<li>For specialised care in a hospital setting, especially if the patient is deteriorating.</li>
<li>For continuity of care from the health facility downwards, or from family level back to the health facility.</li>
</ul>
<p>Referral to and from the family/community should be well arranged to avoid unnecessary burdens to the patient and family.</p>
<p>The patient and the family should be prepared for the discharge and told what equipment and supplies they require for HBC.</p>
<p>The HBC team should also inform the family that in case of a change of condition of the patient, the patient will be referred to a health facility including what the family requires for the referral.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="constraints-limitations-in-referral-and-networking">Constraints/Limitations in Referral and Networking<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#constraints-limitations-in-referral-and-networking" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>There exists a number of constraints that can hinder effective referral and networking.</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Competition among various organisations, so that they do not disclose what they are doing and which services are offered. They prefer to work in isolation.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Lack of evenly distributed community HBC programmes, with the result that some areas lack services and some are overcrowded.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Lack of resources needed for patients to travel from one point to another.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Lack of referral and networking guidelines as well as standardised referral procedures.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Ignorance among family members about HBC due to lack of awareness and proper guidance.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Fear of breach of confidentiality.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Stigma and discrimination associated with some chronic illnesses like HIV/AIDS, which makes PLWHA reluctant to accept referral to certain facilities.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Poor mobilisation and sensitisation of partners.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Lack of confidence in the institution/service where referral is made.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Lack of updated and proper directory of referral and networking.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Lack of knowledge by people referring on how and when to refer or network.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Cultural, social, religious and economic factors.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Poor management of referral system.</p>
</li>
</ul>

<p>As a health worker, you can address the constraints just mentioned by taking the following steps:</p>
<ul>
<li>Holding collaborative meetings among various referral and networking partners</li>
<li>Giving correct/proper information on referral to the relevant organisations and a proper patient history to the referral point</li>
<li>Ensuring confidentiality</li>
<li>Lobbying and advocating for the rights of the patient</li>
</ul>
<p>Appropriate referrals expand capacity and improve care.</p>
