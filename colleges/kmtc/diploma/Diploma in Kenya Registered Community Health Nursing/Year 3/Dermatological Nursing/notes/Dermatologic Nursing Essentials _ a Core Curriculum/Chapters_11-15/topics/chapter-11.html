Key Points

  • Infection compromises the barrier function of the skin.
  • Immunocompromised patients are at higher risk for dissemination of cutaneous infections.
  • Cutaneous infections can be caused by both extrinsic and intrinsic factors.
  • Appropriate treatment depends upon accurate recognition and diagnosis.

Infections in the skin may range from superficial to deep and may be caused by bacteria, fungi, or viruses. These infections may occur in otherwise healthy individuals.

üß´ Bacterial Infections

I. Impetigo

Definition: A common, contagious, superficial skin infection caused by streptococci, staphylococci, or both.

1. Bullous Impetigo

üîç Click to zoom

Figure 11-1: Bullous impetigo. (Courtesy of Charles E. Lewis, MD.)

  • Etiology: Caused by group II Staphylococcus aureus. Usually not secondarily contaminated by streptococci.
  • Pathophysiology: Begins as small vesicles evolving into sharply demarcated bullae without an erythematous halo. Ruptures to form shallow erosions. Nikolsky sign is not present. Characteristic honey-colored crust.

2. Nonbullous Impetigo

üîç Click to zoom

Figure 11-2: Nonbullous impetigo. (Courtesy of Charles E. Lewis, MD.)

  • Etiology: Caused by beta-hemolytic streptococci. Enters via minor trauma.
  • Pathophysiology: Small vesicle/pustule ruptures to expose moist, erythematous base with honey-colored crust.

3. Ecthyma

üîç Click to zoom

Figure 11-3: Ecthyma. (Courtesy of John K. Randall, MD.)

Ulcerated form of nonbullous impetigo extending into the dermis. Caused by Streptococcus pyogenes. Lesions have a purulent, necrotic base.

Patient Education: Impetigo

  • Infected children should be isolated briefly until treatment is underway.
  • Spread via fingers, towels, and clothing.
  • Bleach baths: ¬º to ¬Ω cup household bleach in tub, soak 5-15 min, 2x weekly.
  • Mupirocin nasal ointment bid x 5 days to reduce colonization.
  • Monitor for poststreptococcal glomerulonephritis (hematuria/edema) 1-3 weeks post-infection.

II. Cellulitis

üîç Click to zoom

Figure 11-4: Cellulitis.

Definition: Diffuse, acute bacterial infection of skin and subcutaneous tissue.

Etiology: Group A beta-hemolytic streptococci (erysipelas) or staphylococci. H. influenzae, Pseudomonas in specific cases.

Assessment: Pain, erythema, increased warmth, edema, fever, lymphadenopathy.

Treatment: Dicloxacillin, Cephalexin. Mark margins to monitor spread.

III. Furuncle / Carbuncle

üîç Click to zoom

Figure 11-5: Furunculosis/furuncle.

  • Furuncle: A boil (abscess).
  • Carbuncle: Aggregation of interconnected furuncles draining through multiple openings.
  • Etiology: Staphylococcus.
  • Treatment: Warm compresses, I&D, antibiotics.

IV. Erythrasma

Superficial bacterial infection in intertriginous areas (groin, axilla, toes) caused by Corynebacterium minutissimum.

Diagnosis: Wood's lamp fluorescence bright coral red.

Treatment: Erythromycin or Clindamycin topical, Aluminum chloride.

V. Pitted Keratolysis

üîç Click to zoom

Figure 11-6: Pitted keratolysis.

Noninflammatory infection on soles/palms caused by Micrococcus sedentarius or Corynebacterium. Presents as crater-like pits (1-7mm). Associated with hyperhidrosis and malodor.

VI. Folliculitis

üîç Click to zoom

Figure 11-7: Folliculitis.

Inflammation of the hair follicle. Can be bacterial (Staph), fungal, or mechanical (Pseudofolliculitis barbae from shaving).

VII. Infections with Potential Life-Threatening Complications

A. Meningococcemia

üîç Click to zoom

Figure 11-8: Meningococcemia.

Caused by Neisseria meningitides. Sudden fever, headache, stiff neck. Rash: pink macules -> purpuric lesions with central gunmetal gray discoloration. Emergency treatment required.

B. Staphylococcal Scalded Skin Syndrome (SSSS)

üîç Click to zoom

Figure 11-9: SSSS.

Epidermolytic toxin causes separation of epidermis. Nikolsky sign present. Sandpaper-like erythema followed by peeling.

C. Necrotizing Fasciitis

üîç Click to zoom

Figure 11-10: Necrotizing fasciitis.

Life-threatening soft tissue infection. Critical sign: Severe pain out of proportion to clinical signs. Requires prompt surgical debridement and broad-spectrum antibiotics.

I & II. Warts (Verruca)

Caused by Human Papillomavirus (HPV).

Common Warts (Verruca Vulgaris)

üîç Click to zoom

Figure 11-11: Verruca vulgaris.

Hyperkeratotic, irregular surface. Black dots (thrombosed capillaries) visible.

Flat Warts (Verruca Plana)

üîç Click to zoom

Figure 11-12: Verruca plana.

Slightly raised, smooth, flat surface. Common on face and dorsum of hands.

III. Molluscum Contagiosum

üîç Click to zoom

Figure 11-13: Molluscum contagiosum (pubic).

Caused by Poxvirus. Discrete, 2-5mm, flesh-colored papules with central umbilication. Core contains white "molluscum body".

IV. Condyloma Acuminatum (Genital Warts)

üîç Click to zoom

Figure 11-14: Condyloma acuminatum.

HPV types 6 and 11 (most common). Cauliflower-like masses on genitalia. Types 16 and 18 associated with dysplasia/cancer. Treatment includes Imiquimod, Podophyllin, Cryotherapy.

V. Human Herpesvirus (HHV)

üîç Click to zoom

Figure 11-16: HHV (lips).

HSV-1 (oral) and HSV-2 (genital). Grouped vesicles on erythematous base. Primary, latent, and recurrent phases. Tzanck smear shows multinucleated giant cells.

Other Forms:

  • Herpes Whitlow: Infection of fingertip (Fig 11-18).
  • Herpes Gladiatorum: Athletes (wrestlers).
  • Eczema Herpeticum: Widespread infection in atopic dermatitis (Fig 11-19).

VI. Varicella-Zoster Virus (Shingles)

üîç Click to zoom

Figure 11-20: Varicella-zoster virus.

Reactivation of dormant varicella virus in nerve root. Dermatomal distribution (rarely crosses midline). Prodromal pain precedes eruption. Hutchinson sign (tip of nose) implies ocular involvement.
