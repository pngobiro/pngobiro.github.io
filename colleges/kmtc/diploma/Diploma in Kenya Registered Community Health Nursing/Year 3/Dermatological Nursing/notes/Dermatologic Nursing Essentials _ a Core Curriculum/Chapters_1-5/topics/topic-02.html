01

Overview

An initial assessment for patients with skin disorders should be approached as you would any patient. A comprehensive assessment of a dermatologic condition includes the history given by the patient (subjective data) and the findings of the physical examination of the integumentary system (objective data). The patient history and physical examination should also occur in an appropriately private area to ensure privacy and dignity, preferably with natural lighting.

Box 2-1. Sample Questions for Initial Assessment or Triage

Key questions with initial history of skin problem:

  • Onset and Evolution: How long has the lesion been present? Has it gotten better or worse?
  • Symptoms: Does it itch? Or how does this bother you?
  • Treatment to Date: Tell me all the things that you have used to try to treat this?

Additional questions:

  • What did it first look like?
  • Has it changed, grown, bled, itched, or failed to heal?
  • Have you been out of the country lately?
  • Does it come and go?
  • Have you recently started any changes in your daily medicines?
  • Have you recently started any new medicines?
  • Any history of similar symptoms for you in the past?
  • Do any family members have the same or similar symptoms?
  • Have you used or done anything that seems to make it better?

Lesions can be defined as primary or secondary. Primary lesions are structural changes in the skin that have specific, visual characteristics and develop without any preceding skin changes. Secondary lesion is one that has changed due to natural progression or due to physical factors such as rubbing or scratching. Special or "other" lesions are those that occur in the skin only and in the skin most often or can be perceived most easily on the skin.

02

Patient History (Subjective Data)

A preliminary history helps to establish rapport and engages the patient in the process. Then, moving to physical examination allows appropriately chosen selective questions to be asked subsequently.

Key History Components

  • Family History: Conditions such as psoriasis, eczema, skin cancer, or even keratosis pilaris have a genetic tendency.
  • Medical History: Chronic illness, surgical procedures, and recent infections (e.g., strep infection leading to guttate psoriasis).
  • Medication History: Current prescription therapy, over-the-counter preparations (herbs, vitamins), and medication allergies.
  • Occupational History: Important for contact dermatitis or symptoms that improve away from work.
  • Social History: Hobbies, sun exposure, pet exposure, alcohol consumption, recreational drugs, smoking history, travel, and sexual orientation.
  • Social Factors: Insurance coverage and ability to pay for treatments are crucial in dermatology where topical prescriptions can be expensive.
  • Psychological Factors: Patient belief systems and the psychological impact of visible skin disease.
03

Visual or Physical Examination (Objective Data)

Skin provides an opportunity for visual inspection. This makes inspection the most important part of the physical examination of the skin. A good source of light is the best way to effectively examine the skin (and mouth). Natural lighting in the exam room improves visualization of most skin changes. Side lighting helps detect elevation changes.

Evaluation of Specific Lesions: Primary Lesions

Primary lesions are the physical changes caused directly by the disease process. They have specific, visual characteristics and develop without any preceding skin changes.

Macule

A circumscribed, flat discoloration, which varies widely in size, color, and shape, which cannot be palpated.

Evaluation of Specific Lesions: Secondary Lesions

Secondary lesions are physical changes due to natural evolution over time or environmental factors (rubbing, scratching, infection).

Scale

Accumulated compact desquamated layers of stratum corneum, which can appear dry or greasy.

Common Configurations

  • Annular: Ring shaped with active margins (e.g., tinea).
  • Discoid: Disk shaped (e.g., lupus).
  • Linear: In a line (e.g., poison ivy/Rhus).
  • Dermatomal/Zosteriform: Following nerve root (e.g., shingles).
  • Guttate: Resembling a drop.
  • Iris/Target: "Bull's-eye" lesion.
  • Serpiginous: Snake-like.
07

Diagnostic Indicators

Hair and Nails

Changes in hair and nails can indicate systemic disease.

  • Hypertrichosis/Hypotrichosis: Excessive or too little hair.
  • Alopecia: Generalized thinning or lost patches.
  • Nails: Pale beds (circulatory), pitting (psoriasis), inflamed cuticles (infection).

Pruritus (Itching)

Primary symptom in many dermatologic disorders. The itch threshold is lower at night.

  • Contributing Factors: Psychologic stress, fear, boredom, dry skin.
  • Conditions: Scabies, atopic dermatitis, lichen planus, urticaria, systemic disease (Hodgkin disease).
