Key Points

  • Benign neoplasms are noncancerous and have no malignant potential.
  • Treatment is often sought for cosmetic purposes or comfort/irritation.
  • Common types include skin tags, cysts, keloids, and seborrheic keratoses.
01

Acrochordons (Skin Tags)

üîç

Fig 14-1A: Pedunculated skin tag (axilla).

üîç

Fig 14-1B: Eyelid acrochordon.

Definition: Soft, pedunculated, flesh-colored/pigmented growths. Common in skin folds (neck, axillae, groin). Asymptomatic unless irritated by friction/jewelry.

Incidence: 25% of population. Associated with obesity, diabetes, and insulin resistance.

Treatment

  • Snip Excision: Scissor excision at base.
  • Electrodesiccation: Light burning.
  • Cryosurgery: Liquid nitrogen.

Calluses & Corns

Fig 14-2: Corn over metatarsal head.

Fig 14-3: Corns and calluses.

Feature Callus (Tyloma) Corn (Clavus)
Appearance Diffusely thickened, no distinct border. Circumscribed, conical core of keratin.
Sensation Nontender (dull pain with pressure). Marked tenderness (sharp pain) due to core pressing on nerves.
Location Weight-bearing surfaces/palms. Bony prominences (toes). "Soft corns" between toes.

Treatment: 40% Salicylic acid plaster, paring/debridement, proper footwear (extra wide), orthopedic padding.

04

Dermatofibroma

Fig 14-5: "Dimple Sign"

Definition: Benign fibrous nodule, often reaction to insect bite or trauma.

Assessment: Firm, button-like nodule on legs. "Dimple Sign" (Retraction sign): Dimples when pinched laterally.

Treatment: None usually required. Excision creates a scar (trade-off).

06

Lipoma

üîç

Figure 14-8: Lipomas (rubbery, mobile).

Definition: Benign tumor of subcutaneous fat. Most common soft tissue neoplasm.

Signs: Soft, rounded, mobile, "slippage sign" (slips from under fingers). Asymptomatic.

Treatment: None/Excision.

07

Sebaceous Hyperplasia

Figure 14-9: Yellowish papules with central dell.

Definition: Hypertrophy of sebaceous glands. Common on forehead/cheeks in adults >30.

Appearance: Yellowish, soft papules with central umbilication (dell). Often has telangiectasia.

Differential: Must distinguish from Basal Cell Carcinoma (BCC usually pearly, not yellow).

08

Seborrheic Keratosis (SK)

Figure 14-10: Seborrheic Keratoses (various appearances).

Definition: Most common benign cutaneous neoplasm. Hereditary (Autosomal dominant).

Appearance: "Stuck-on", warty, greasy, hyperkeratotic. Tan to black. "Barnacles of aging".

Treatment: Cryosurgery, Curettage, Shave. (Cosmetic).

09

Cherry Angioma

Figure 14-11: Cherry angioma.

Definition: Benign vascular proliferation. Bright red, dome-shaped papules (1-3mm). Increase with age.

Education: Will bleed profusely if picked (vascular). New lesions likely to develop.

General Patient Education

  • Monitoring: Watch for changes in size, color, bleeding, or pain in any lesion.
  • Wound Care: Keep excision sites clean/moist (antibiotic ointment) and covered for 24-48 hrs.
  • Keloids: Avoid elective piercings/tattoos if prone to scarring.
  • Infection Signs: Redness, heat, swelling, pus, fever >101¬∞F.
