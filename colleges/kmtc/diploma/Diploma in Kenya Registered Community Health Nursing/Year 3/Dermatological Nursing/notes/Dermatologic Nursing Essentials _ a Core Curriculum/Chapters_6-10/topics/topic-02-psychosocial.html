01

Psychosocial Functions of Skin

The skin is the largest and most visible organ of the body, serving as the barrier between the individual and the external environment, including the interpersonal and social environment.

üîÑ

The Mind-Skin Connection

The skin affects the mind: It acts as a sense organ and interface with the world. Imperfections can detrimentally affect well-being and body image ("look good‚Äìfeel good" factor).

The mind affects the skin: It acts as a facade and organ of emotional expression (e.g., blushing). Stress, anxiety, and depression can precipitate or worsen skin conditions.

Psychosocial Impacts

  • Rejection by others frequently occurs.
  • Unsightly skin typically provokes negative feelings in the observer.
  • Interpretation of outside stimuli and expression of enjoyment are adversely affected.
02

Classification of Disorders

Primary Psychiatric Diseases Presenting in Dermatology

‚ñ∂ Delusional & OCD
  • Delusional disorders: Olfactory syndromes, Dysmorphic syndromes.
  • Obsessive-compulsive disorders (OCD): Body dysmorphic disorders, Skin picking, Trichotillomania (hair pulling).
‚ñ∂ Anxiety & Pruritus
  • Anxiety disorders: General anxiety, Specific phobias (Syphilis, Cancer).
  • Chronic pruritus: Psychogenic pruritus, Nodular prurigo.
  • Dysesthesias: Burning mouth syndrome, vulvodynia.
‚ñ∂ Factitious Disorders
  • Dermatitis artefacta (patient-created lesions).
  • Factitious/induced illness.
  • Malingering.

Psychosocial Comorbidities

Patients may experience: Anger, Anxiety, Avoidance, Bullying, Depression, Embarrassment, Guilt, Isolation, Low self-esteem, Stigmatization, Suicidal thoughts.

Any of these can hinder self-management, leading to a downward spiral of worsening skin condition and quality of life.

03

Nursing Consultation

Accurate, holistic assessment is essential. The consultation should take place in a quiet, comfortable environment.

The Four-Stage Therapeutic Consultation

  1. 1 Clinical Observation: How does the patient behave? Nonverbal cues? Interaction with others?
  2. 2 Clinician's Thoughts: Is the impression congruent with what they say? Is there a sense of reality?
  3. 3 Questions: Discuss sleep, appetite, stress management, quality of life.
  4. 4 Validated Tools: Use questionnaires for accurate assessment.
üí¨

Communication Skills

Reflective Listening: "It sounds like you are feeling..."

Open-ended Questions: "Help me understand how you feel about your skin?"

Core Conditions: Congruence (genuineness), Empathy, Unconditional positive regard.

05

Specialist Referral

üö®

Indicators for Referral

  • High scores in quality-of-life indices (not in isolation).
  • Anxiety and profound preoccupation with a delusional belief (e.g., infestation).
  • Difficult-to-engage patients recounting minute medical history details.
  • Signs of personality disorder (infantile, manipulative, poor impulse control).
  • Unusual skin appearance suggestive of self-infliction (symmetric, within reach).
  • Sense of unreality in patient's narrative.
  • Serious deterioration in condition/self-care related to psychosocial state.
06

Evidence-Based Interventions

‚ñ∂ Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy (CBT)

Aims to change unhelpful thinking patterns to improve emotional state and behavior.

PhaseActivity
1. AssessmentIdentify impairment, appraise meaning of events.
2. ReconceptualizationReframe experiences, challenge negative thoughts (e.g., "I am a failure because...").
3. Skills AcquisitionSquare breathing, progressive relaxation, challenging automatic thoughts.
4. Skills ConsolidationHomework, activity scheduling, mindfulness.
5. MaintenanceDiary, support groups.
6. Follow-upEvaluate integration into daily life.
‚ñ∂ Habit Reversal (HR)

Used to manage the itch-scratch cycle. Replace an old habit with a new one.

  • 1Awareness Training: Keep scratch diary, recognize early urges.
  • 2Competing Response: Do an action incompatible with scratching (e.g., clenching fist) for 1 min.
  • 3Symbolic Rehearsal: Visualize the trigger and the new response.
‚ñ∂ Social Cognitive Theory (Self-Efficacy)

Building belief in one's ability to succeed in specific situations.

  • Mastery Experience: Break tasks down, ensure early success (e.g., learn emollient use first).
  • Vicarious Experience: Demonstrate application, group learning.
  • Social Persuasion: Set achievable goals, emphasize success.
  • Emotional Regulation: Teach stress management, relaxation.
