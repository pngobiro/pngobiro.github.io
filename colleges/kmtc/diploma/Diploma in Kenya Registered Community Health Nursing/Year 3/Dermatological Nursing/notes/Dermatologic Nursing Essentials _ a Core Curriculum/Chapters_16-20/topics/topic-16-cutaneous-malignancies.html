‚òÄÔ∏è Actinic Keratosis

Overview

Actinic keratosis (AK), also called solar keratosis, is a premalignant neoplasm of the epidermis. AK presents as solitary or multiple lesions on sun-exposed areas, and greater than 80% occur on the head, neck, forearms, and dorsal hands. History of AK correlates with increased risk for developing invasive squamous cell carcinomas (SCCs).

Definition
AK is a premalignant, atypical proliferation of mutated keratinocytes arising from the basal layer and remaining in the epidermis.

Etiology & Pathogenesis

  • Etiology: Chronic UV radiation, genetic predisposition (fair skin), genetic disorders (xeroderma pigmentosum), advancing age, and immunosuppression.
  • Pathogenesis: Chronic UV radiation (mostly UVB 290-320 nm) induces p53 tumor suppressor gene mutation in keratinocytes. This leads to prolonged cell life span, delayed apoptosis, and clonal expansion of atypical keratinocytes.

Clinical Variants

  • Common AK: Few millimeters to 2cm, pink or erythematous, keratotic papule or plaque.
  • Hypertrophic AK (HAK): Tan, white, or gray, thick or exophytic scaly papule or plaque, with erythematous base.
  • Atrophic AK: Smooth pink/erythematous macule/patch, absent keratotic scale.
  • Pigmented AK: Keratotic scale over hyperpigmented macule.
  • Cutaneous horn: Keratotic projection resembling a cone/spicule.
  • Actinic cheilitis: Rough keratotic papule/plaque on lip; may fissure or ulcerate.

Treatment Modalities

  1. Cryotherapy: Most common, first-line treatment (98.8% cure rate).
  2. Electrodessication and curettage: Ideal for hyperkeratotic AKs.
  3. Topical 5-fluorouracil (5-FU): Interferes with DNA/RNA synthesis of mutated keratinocytes.
  4. Imiquimod: Immune response modifier triggering cytokines.

Prognosis

Approximately 65% of all SCC and 36% of all BCC are transformed from preexisting AKs.

ü©π Basal Cell Carcinoma (BCC)

BCC is the most common nonmelanoma skin cancer (NMSC) in the US. It is a malignant epithelial neoplasm arising from basal cells. It often remains local with low metastatic potential and is highly curable.

Pathogenesis

Over 70% of BCC development is caused by alteration in the PTCH1 gene on chromosome 9q (tumor suppressor gene). Mutations in P53 are also involved early in carcinogenesis.

Clinical Variants

Treatment

Mohs Micrographic Surgery: Treatment of choice for BCC on cosmetic sensitive areas (face, ears), morpheaform BCC, or recurrent lesions. Offers highest cure rate (99% for primary).

Other options include cryosurgery, electrodessication/curettage (ED&C), surgical excision, and radiation.

03

üß¨ Squamous Cell Carcinoma (SCC)

SCC is a malignant epithelial neoplasm arising from mutated keratinocytes. Unlike BCC, SCC has potential to metastasize.

Etiology & Risk Factors

  • Cumulative UV radiation (p53 mutation).
  • Chronic inflammation (Marjolin ulcer), burns, lichen sclerosus.
  • HPV infection (types 16, 18, 31, 5, 8).
  • Chemical carcinogens (arsenic, tar).

Clinical Variants

  • Invasive SCC: Firm, indurated papulonodule/plaque with hyperkeratotic scale. Poorly differentiated types may be soft, granulomatous, or ulcerated.
  • SCC in situ (Bowen disease): Erythematous, crusty, rough, scaly patch (Fig 16-10).
  • Keratoacanthoma: Fast-growing dome-shaped nodule with central keratotic plug (Fig 16-13).

üåë Malignant Melanoma

Melanoma is the most dangerous form of skin cancer, accounting for ~75% of skin cancer deaths. It arises from melanocytes and can metastasize rapidly.

Clinical Assessment (ABCDE Rule)

  • Asymmetry: Mismatching halves
  • Border: Irregular, poorly defined
  • Color: Variegated shades (black, blue, red, white)
  • Diameter: > 6mm (usually)
  • Evolution: Changing in size, shape, color

Clinical Variants

üîç Click to zoom

FIGURE 16-18. Superficial spreading melanoma.

  • Superficial Spreading Melanoma (SSM): Most common (70%). Asymmetrical, well-demarcated plaque with color variation.
  • Nodular Melanoma (NM): Second most common (10-15%). Rapid vertical growth. Dark brown/black/blue nodule. Can be amelanotic (Fig 16-20).
  • Acral Lentiginous Melanoma (ALM): Palms, soles, subungual. More common in dark-skinned individuals.
  • Lentigo Maligna Melanoma (LMM): Sun-exposed skin of elderly (face). Gradual growth.

ü©∫ Cutaneous T-Cell Lymphoma (CTCL)

CTCL is a malignancy of helper T cells (CD4+). Mycosis Fungoides (MF) and S√©zary Syndrome (SS) are the most common types.

Clinical Stages of MF

  1. Patch Phase: Dusky red, scaly macules/patches in non-sun-exposed areas (buttocks, groin). Persistent pruritus.
  2. Plaque Phase: Well-defined, dusky red, elevated plaques. May have wart-like infiltrates.
  3. Tumor Phase: Nodular lesions, reddish-brown/purplish. May ulcerate.

06

ü¶† Kaposi Sarcoma (KS)

KS is an angioproliferative disorder caused by HHV-8. It is not a true sarcoma but a vascular hyperplasia.

Clinical Variants

  • Classic (Mediterranean): Elderly men. Chronic, red/purple nodules on lower limbs.
  • African/Endemic: Young adults and children in equatorial Africa. Can be florid/aggressive.
  • Iatrogenic (Transplant): Immunosuppressed patients. May regress if immunosuppression is reduced.
  • AIDS-Associated: Aggressive. Oral cavity, face, trunk involved. Red/purple macules progressing to nodules/plaques.
