Papulosquamous diseases are varied. They have some shared morphologic features, which can lead to challenges in diagnosis. Interestingly though, their etiologies are different. Some of the common diseases in this group are psoriasis, pityriasis rosea (PR), lichen planus (LP), and lichen simplex chronicus (LSC).

01

Psoriasis

Psoriasis is a chronic skin condition that presents in a variety of ways. It is a genetic condition triggered by environmental factors such as infection, medications, and trauma. It is characterized by exaggerated and disordered epidermal cell proliferation and keratinization.

Etiology & Triggers

  • Genetics: Multifactorial inheritance. Correlation with HLA-Cw6 antigen.
  • Infections: Streptococcal infection (specifically guttate psoriasis), HIV.
  • Stress: Significant trigger for exacerbation.
  • Drugs: Beta-blockers, lithium, antimalarials, interferon, NSAIDs, tetracyclines, steroid withdrawal.
  • Trauma: Koebner phenomenon (lesions forming at injury sites).

Clinical Manifestations (Phenotypes)

90% of cases. Well-defined red or salmon-colored, scaly plaques. Common on scalp, elbows, knees, sacrum.

‚ñ∂ Guttate Psoriasis

Widespread small erythematous papules with scale. Often preceded by streptococcal infection. Best treated with phototherapy.

‚ñ∂ Inverse Psoriasis

Plaques in intertriginous areas (axillae, groin, gluteal cleft). Red, thinner, without scale due to moisture/friction.

‚ñ∂ Erythrodermic Psoriasis

Severe, rare. Widespread deep erythema and scaling affecting entire body surface. Can cause fluid/nutrient loss and infection.

Palmoplantar: Chronic, localized to palms/soles. Strongly associated with smoking.
Generalized: Rare, life-threatening. Widespread sterile pustules, fever, malaise.

Pitting, onycholysis, "oil spots". Occurs in 50% of patients.

Systemic Associations

  • Cardiovascular: Increased risk of atherosclerosis, MI, stroke.
  • Metabolic Syndrome: Obesity, hypertension, diabetes, fatty liver disease.
  • Psoriatic Arthritis: Seronegative inflammatory arthritis. Sausage fingers (dactylitis). Destructive.
  • Psychosocial: Depression, anxiety, suicidal ideation.

Lichen Planus

An acute or chronic inflammatory disease affecting skin, mucous membranes, hair, and nails. Often idiopathic or triggered by Hepatitis C, drugs, or contact allergens.

The 5 P's of Lichen Planus

  1. 1Purple
  2. 2Polygonal
  3. 3Pruritic (often intense)
  4. 4Planar (flat-topped)
  5. 5Papules

Look for Wickham striae: tiny white lacy lines on the surface.

Clinical Variants

  • Cutaneous: Flexor surfaces of wrists, forearms, legs. Linear LP (Koebner).
  • Oral LP: White, lacy patterns on buccal mucosa. Can be erosive/painful.
  • Genital LP: Painful erosions, impact on QOL.
  • Lichen Planopilaris: Scalp variant causing scarring alopecia.

03

Pityriasis Rosea

Mild, acute, self-limiting exanthematous eruption. Suspected viral etiology (HHV-7/HHV-6).

Classic Presentation

  1. 1 Herald Patch: Solitary, oval, salmon-colored patch on trunk with collarette of scale. Precedes eruption by ~2 weeks.
  2. 2 Secondary Eruption: Widespread smaller oval papules/macules.
  3. 3 Distribution: "Christmas Tree" pattern along skin cleavage lines on trunk.

Management

Self-limiting: Resolves spontaneously in 8-12 weeks.

Treatment: Generally unnecessary. Education and reassurance. Treat pruritus if present.

04

Keratosis Pilaris

Very common genetic condition (50-80% of adolescents). Disorder of keratinization causing coarse follicular plugs ("chicken skin").

  • Location: Lateral upper arms, anterior thighs, cheeks.
  • Treatment: Difficult. Emollients, urea/lactic acid creams, retinoids (use sparingly). Do not scrub.

05

Lichen Simplex Chronicus (LSC)

Also known as Neurodermatitis. Characterized by skin lichenification (thickening, exaggerated skin markings) secondary to excessive scratching and rubbing.

üîÑ

The Itch-Scratch Cycle

Pruritus leads to scratching -> Scratching causes lichenification -> Lichenified skin itches more -> More scratching.

Key to Treatment: Breaking this cycle is the single most important intervention.

Management

  • Topical Steroids: High potency, often under occlusion.
  • Sedating Antihistamines: Hydroxyzine or Doxepin at night to stop nocturnal scratching.
  • Behavioral: Patient education on pathomechanism is crucial.
