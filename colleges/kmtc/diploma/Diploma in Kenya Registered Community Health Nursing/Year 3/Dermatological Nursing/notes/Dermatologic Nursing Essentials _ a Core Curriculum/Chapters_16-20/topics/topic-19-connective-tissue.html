Overview

Connective tissue disorders (collagen vascular disease) are defined as a group of acquired disorders characterized by immunologic and inflammatory changes in small blood vessels and connective tissue. Common features include arthritis, skin lesions, iritis, pericarditis, pleuritis, subcutaneous nodules, myocarditis, vasculitis, and nephritis.

ü¶ã Lupus Erythematosus

Lupus is a chronic autoimmune disease resulting in inflammation and tissue damage. It strikes women 10 to 15 times more frequently than men.

Types of Lupus

‚ñº Chronic Cutaneous LE (CCLE)

Most common form is Discoid Lupus Erythematosus (DLE).

  • Lesions: Erythematous, scaly, thickened, scarring, or discoloration.
  • Localized DLE: Limited to head, ears, neck (Fig 19-1, 19-2).
  • Generalized DLE: Anywhere on skin.
  • Lupus Profundus: DLE lesions with panniculitis (firm fatty lumps) (Fig 19-3).

Subacute Cutaneous LE (SCLE)
  • Papulosquamous (psoriasis-like) or Annular lesions.
  • Photosensitive. Usually non-scarring.
Acute Cutaneous LE (ACLE)
  • Malar Rash: "Butterfly" rash across cheeks and nose.
  • Generalized maculopapular eruption resembling sunburn.
  • Strongly associated with active SLE.

‚ñ∂ Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE)

Attacks multiple body systems (skin, joints, kidneys, heart, brain). Can have significant morbidity.

üìã

Criteria for Diagnosis (ACR/SLICC)

Patient must meet 4 criteria (at least 1 clinical + 1 immunologic) OR biopsy-proven nephritis + ANA/Anti-dsDNA.

Clinical:
  • Malar rash / Oral lesions
  • Nonscarring alopecia
  • Synovitis / Serositis
  • Renal / Neurologic
  • Hemolytic anemia / Low platelets
Immunologic:
  • ANA (Antinuclear antibody)
  • Anti-dsDNA (Specific for SLE)
  • Anti-Smith
  • Antiphospholipid antibodies
  • Low complement

Drug-Induced Lupus

Develops after taking certain medications. Resolves after discontinuation.

Treatment

  • Prevention: Sun avoidance, sunscreen, stress reduction.
  • Antimalarials: Hydroxychloroquine (Plaquenil). Requires eye exams every 6 months (retinal toxicity).
  • Steroids: Topical or systemic corticosteroids.
  • Immunosuppressants: Methotrexate, Azathioprine, Mycophenolate for severe disease.

Chronic autoimmune disease causing thickening and tightening of the skin via excessive collagen production. "Scleroderma" literally means "hard skin".

Treatment

No cure. Treatment targets symptoms:

  • Raynaud's: Calcium channel blockers, warmth.
  • Renal: ACE inhibitors for hypertension.
  • GI: PPIs for reflux, antibiotics for bacterial overgrowth.

üí™ Inflammatory Myopathies

Autoimmune disorders characterized by inflammation of connective tissue and muscle fibers.

Dermatomyositis (DM)

Distinctive rash + proximal muscle weakness.

  • Heliotrope Rash: Dusky red/lilac rash on eyelids with periorbital edema (Fig 19-7A).
  • Gottron Papules: Pathognomonic flat, reddish/purplish papules over knuckles/joints (Fig 19-7B).
  • Associations: Malignancy (older adults), Calcinosis (juvenile form).

Other Myopathies

  • Polymyositis (PM): Symmetric proximal muscle weakness without rash.
  • Inclusion Body Myositis (IBM): Older adults (>50), affects distal and proximal muscles, often asymmetric. Resistant to therapy.

Treatment

High dose steroids (Prednisone), Immunosuppressants (Methotrexate), IVIg, Physical Therapy.
