Overview

Phototherapy is the exposure of nonionizing radiation for therapeutic benefit; ultraviolet light (UVL) is not visible and is classified by wavelength‚ÄîUVC, UVB, and UVA; UVB and UVA are used in treating dermatologic diseases. Phototherapy is the use of UVL to clear skin diseases. This is also referred to as photomedicine. Phototherapy is prescribed by dermatologists and other physicians, nurse practitioners, and/or physician assistants.

  2. Full-body cabinets‚Äîfirst used in the 1970s.

  5. Ultraviolet A1 (UA1), the long-wavelength band of UVA‚Äîfirst used in the 2000s (340 to 400 nm).

Phototherapy Terms

  • Wavelength: The distance measured along the wave from any given point to the next similar point, as from crest to crest (peaks).
  • Nanometer (nm): The unit of measurement used to describe the distance between wavelengths. One billion nanometers equals 1 m or 39.37 inches.
  • Photons: The energy emitted from the UVL wavelength.
  • Joules (J): The dosing unit for UVA. One joule equals 1,000 millijoules.
  • Millijoules (mJ): The dosing unit for UVB.

Facts about UVL

  1. Longer wavelengths produce fewer photons, resulting in less energy.
  2. Shorter wavelengths produce more photons, resulting in more energy.
  3. UVL can do one of three things. It can be reflected, transmitted, or absorbed. When UVL is absorbed, photons activate specific cells in the dermis and epidermis called chromophores, resulting in a biological response.
  4. Most natural sunlight exposure occurs in the first 18 to 20 years of life, and phototherapy adds to total lifetime accumulation. Overexposure to UVL increases the risk of skin damage. Therefore, risks versus benefits of therapy must be considered.
  5. UVL's therapeutic physiological effects are immune function manipulation, decreased DNA synthesis, and selective cytotoxicity.

Common Diseases Treated with Phototherapy

The most common dermatological diseases treated with phototherapy are psoriasis, vitiligo, various types of dermatitis, and cutaneous T-cell lymphoma (CTCL). The goals of phototherapy are different depending on the disease being treated.

  1. Psoriasis: goal is to depress the immune system and slow down T-cell activity, which will then result in thinning of plaques, decreased pruritus, decreased scaling, and induction of remission.
  2. Vitiligo: goal is repigmentation, which will occur in 50% to 80% of patients.
  3. Dermatitis: goal is to depress the immune system resulting in elimination or reduction of pruritus. Improvement of the rash is secondary. This group includes atopic dermatitis, eczema, folliculitis, IRBD or papular dermatitis, and lichen planus.
  4. CTCL: goal is to slow the systemic progression of the disease as evidenced by flattening of plaques and normalization of skin pigmentation.

Phototherapy Types

UVL waves found in sunlight have a therapeutic effect. The types of UVL used to treat skin diseases are UVB and UVA.

A. Ultraviolet B (UVB)

UVB is referred to as the sunburn ray. It is 1,000 times more capable of producing erythema than UVA and is dosed in millijoules (mJ). It has shallow penetration into the epidermis, takes a short time to develop erythema (4 to 6 hours), and the erythema resolves quickly (18 to 24 hours). Skin protection against UVB is referred to as hyperplasia, thickening of the top layer of skin.

‚ñ∂ Broadband UVB (BB-UVB)

Wavelength: 290 to 320 nm

  • Must wear goggles, as corneal burns can occur.
  • Sunburning spectrum of light, therefore potentially the most damaging.
  • Potential carcinogenesis following long-term exposure.
  • No medications needed as epidermis has sufficient chromophores to absorb UVB. When phototherapy truly indicated in pregnant women, UVB is frequently the type chosen because no additional medications are used with this treatment.

‚ñ∂ Excimer Laser UVB

Wavelength: 308 nm

  • Treatment twice a week.
  • Emits light that is monochromatic and coherent.
  • Handheld device with a fiber-optic arm allows for targeted application of light while avoiding unaffected skin.
  • Uses a spot diameter of 14 to 30 mm.
‚ñ∂ Narrowband UVB (NB-UVB)

Wavelength: 311 to 313 nm

  • Clears lesions faster than BB-UVB.
  • Fewer treatments necessary.
  • Treatment Schedule:
    • Clearing phase: three to five treatments per week until disease clears.
    • Maintenance phase: every 9 to 11 days to maintain clearance.

Clinical Considerations for UVB

Contraindications

Absolute: Xeroderma pigmentosum, Albinism, Porphyria.

Relative: Photodermatoses exacerbation, previous nonmelanoma skin cancers/melanoma history, inability to stand, noncompliance, unstable/debilitated/intoxicated patients.

Dosing Protocols

Intensity and dosage length are increased as tolerated per a specific protocol designated by the prescribing provider.

B. Ultraviolet A (UVA)

UVA is referred to as the suntanning ray. It is 1,000 times less effective in producing erythema than UVB and is dosed in joules (J). UVA penetrates deep into the dermis, takes a longer time for erythema to develop (12 to 36 hours) and can take days to resolve. Skin protection against UVA is referred to as melanogenesis, which leads to hyperpigmentation (tanning).

1. UVA (320-400 nm)

To be effective, must be given with UVB or a photosensitizing medication. May enhance photobiologic effects of UVB but can cause retinal/lens damage.

2. UVA-1 (340-400 nm)

  • Penetrates deep into reticular dermis.
  • Induces T-cell apoptosis and activates fibroblasts (collagen breakdown).
  • Immediate hyperpigmentation, lower burn risk.
  • Longer treatment times (10-40 min), 10-40 treatments per course.

3. Psoralen/UVA (PUVA)

Interaction of psoralen (methoxsalen) and UVA. Methoxsalen is a photosensitizing drug administered topically or systemically.

‚ö†Ô∏è

PUVA Risks & Precautions

PUVA can be more effective than UVB but carries higher risks:

  • Increased risk of malignant melanoma, squamous cell carcinoma, skin aging.
  • Topical Methoxsalen: High risk of burning. Wash site after treatment; protect for 8 hours.
  • Systemic Methoxsalen: Ingested 1.5-2 hours before treatment.

UVL Lamps

New lamps contain maximum energy. Power is gradually lost with use (no expiration date for unused). Lamps should be changed when burnt out or damaged in an even pattern to avoid "hot spots".

  • BB-UVB: Red lettering.
  • NB-UVB: Gold lettering.
  • UVA: Black lettering.
  • UVA-1: Metal halide high-pressure lamp (requires heat removal).

Metering

Metering determines lamp energy output as it declines. There are no FDA standards.

  • Integrated Dosimetry: Built-in sensors automatically adjust time.
  • Nondosimetry (External): Portable meter used weekly. Cold lamps emit less energy, so warm up 5-10 mins before metering.

Phototherapy Pearls

Phototherapy can be the last resort for some skin diseases. Two primary goals are safe treatment and patient education.

üéì

Patient Education Objectives (PUVA)

The patient will:

  1. Verbalize understanding of PUVA therapy.
  2. Verbalize understanding of action/side effects of oral methoxsalen.
  3. Demonstrate adherence to regimen (take meds 1.5-2 hrs prior, protect skin/eyes for 24 hrs).
  4. Demonstrate physical signs of therapeutic response (light pink erythema 24-48 hrs post-treatment).

Important Reminders

  • The number one fear in phototherapy is overtreatment.
  • The number one error in phototherapy is undertreatment.
  • Follow the "five rights": Right patient, Right chart, Right UVL/dose, Right unit, Right shielding.
