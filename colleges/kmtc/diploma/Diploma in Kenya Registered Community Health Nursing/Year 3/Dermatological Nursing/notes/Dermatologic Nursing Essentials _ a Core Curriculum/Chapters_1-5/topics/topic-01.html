01

Overview and Functions of the Integumentary System

The integumentary system is an extraordinary body system, which includes the largest and most visible organ, the skin. The skin forms the integumentary system when combined with the accessory structures of hair, nails, and glands. As the only organ of the body, which is readily available to be inspected and judged by all, the skin's very visible characteristics play a unique role in every individual's well-being.

A conceptual framework for recognizing and understanding diseases of the skin relies on principles of diagnosis common to medicine and nursing, namely, historical and social factors, physical examination, and laboratory techniques. To relate these principles of diagnosis and treatment to clinicopathologic events in dermatology, it is important to start with an overview of the skin. The skin is composed of three layers: epidermis, dermis, and subcutaneous fat accounting for 15% to 20% of the body's weight (Figure 1-1). Disease may localize exclusively in one or all of these layers. There is considerable regional variation in the relative thickness of these layers.

Key Functions

  1. Protection
    • An intact stratum corneum provides a physical barrier against foreign substances and bacteria.
    • Mechanical strength is provided by intercellular bonding in the epidermis and collagen, elastin, and ground substance in the dermis.
    • Subcutaneous tissue acts as a shock absorber.
    • Melanin screens and absorbs ultraviolet radiation.
  2. Homeostasis
    • The skin prevents dehydration through loss of internal fluids and electrolytes.
    • The skin limits absorption of external fluids and gases.
  3. Excretion
    • The glands of the skin excrete waste products, in addition to sweat, which is mostly water and electrolytes.
    • The waste products excreted can include urea, lactic acid, bile, ammonia, and even alcohol.
  4. Thermoregulation
    • Body temperature is controlled by:
      • Conduction of heat from the skin to the air or other objects
      • Radiation of heat from the body surface
      • Convection of heat by air currents
      • Evaporation of perspiration
    • The cutaneous vasculature plays an important role in body temperature regulation. Blood vessels:
      • Dilate when the external environment is warm to promote heat loss
      • Constrict in a cold environment to conserve heat
  5. Vitamin D production
    • Ultraviolet light converts 7-dehydrocholesterol to vitamin D3 in the epidermis.
    • Vitamin D3 is converted in the kidneys into the active form of vitamin D.
  6. Sensory perception
    • The system contains mechanoreceptors and unmyelinated nerve fibers.
    • Touch, pressure, temperature, pain, and itch are transmitted.
  7. Psychosocial
    • The skin, nails, and hair influence sexual attraction, general well-being, and self-image. Outward expressions of anxiety, fear, and anger are visible through sweating, pallor, and flushing.
    • Different societies and culture greatly influence how one accepts or rejects outward appearance of this body system.
  8. Wound healing
    • The skin can repair and regenerate itself.
    • These important wound healing abilities are influenced by multiple internal and external factors.

Structure of the Skin

FIGURE 1-2. Layers of the epidermis.

A. Epidermis

The epidermis is the outermost structure of the skin. It:

  • Contains multiple layers of cells (stratified)
  • Has considerable regional variation, depending on the site:
    • Thickest on palms and soles, approximately 1.5 mm
    • Very thin on eyelid, less than 0.4 mm
  • Is without lymphatic and vascular channels, and connective tissue; therefore, it derives its nutritional support from the underlying dermis.

Keratinization

The epidermis rejuvenates itself through the process of keratinization. Epidermal keratinization is the process of morphological and biochemical differentiation of the keratinocyte, beginning in the basal cell layer and ending in the stratum corneum as a horn or cornified cell.

Keratin

Keratin, the major product of the cornified cell, is a highly resistant, insoluble, fibrous protein. It represents the end product of a differentiated epidermal keratinocyte. Keratinization also involves synthesis of several other proteins and additional substances such as keratohyalin granules, which act as glue.

Layers of the Epidermis

  1. Germinating or basal cell layer (stratum basale, also called stratum germinativum)

    The basal cell layer is the innermost layer of the epidermis. It consists of a single layer of elongated cells. Each cell divides (mitosis) into two daughter cells. One remains as "basal cell," the other migrates upward through the remainder of the epidermis.

  2. Prickle cell or spiny layer (stratum spinosum)

    The prickle cell layer consists of many rows of flattened polygonal cells that are held together by "prickles" or "spines." These prickles are desmosomes, which are small thickenings in an intracellular bridge.

  3. Granular cell layer (stratum granulosum)

    The granular cell layer is most prominent on the palms and soles. It consists of one to three layers of flattened, irregularly shaped cells that have large numbers of keratohyalin granules. Keratohyalin granules comprise particulate materials that have a high sulfur-protein content. Keratinocytes lose their nucleus in this layer, thereby becoming nonviable.

  4. Glassy layer (stratum lucidum)

    The stratum lucidum is made up of one or more rows of distended irregular cells. It is an even, colorless, translucent, or shiny band. The stratum lucidum is almost nonexistent except on thicker skin areas such as palms and soles.

  5. Horny cell layer (stratum corneum)

    The horny cell layer consists of anucleated, cornified cells. They are also known as horn cells. The nucleus and other cytoplasmic organelles have been totally degraded. The remaining material is predominantly keratin. Other substances include water, water-insoluble proteins, amino acids, sugars, urea, minerals, and lipids. These act as buffers and lubricants.

    • Cells of the epidermis are continuously being shed or desquamated from the stratum corneum.
    • It takes approximately 14 days for the keratinocyte to travel from the basal cell layer to the stratum corneum. Once in the stratum corneum, it takes another 14 days before it is shed.
    • New cells are formed in the basal cell layer at the same rate cells are shed in the stratum corneum.

Functions of the Horny Cell Layer

  • Functions as the body's major physical barrier by being relatively impermeable to water and electrolytes.
  • Resists damaging chemicals, provides physical toughness, impedes passage of electrical currents, and retards the proliferation of microorganisms through its relatively dry surface.
  • Functions as a reservoir for topical medications.

B. Cells in the Epidermis

1. Keratinocytes

Account for at least 80% of the cellular components of the epidermis. They have the specialized function of producing keratin. During keratinization, the keratinocytes change shape (flatten), lose organelles, form fibrous protein (keratin), become dehydrated, and thicken their cell membrane.

2. Melanocyte cells

  • Embryonic development: Derived from the neural crest; by the 8th week, enter the epidermis. Failure to migrate results in entities such as blue nevus and mongolian spots.
  • Regional variation: Present on all parts of the body. More on the face than abdomen. Ratios of melanocytes to keratinocytes vary from 1:4 to 1:10.
  • Melanosome/melanin: Specialized organelles called melanosomes store melanin.
    • Eumelanins: Brown and black colors.
    • Pheomelanins: Yellow to reddish brown colors.
  • Epidermal melanin unit: One melanocyte interacts with many keratinocytes via dendrites. Melanosomes are transferred to keratinocytes by apocopation.
  • Melanin production: Controlled by genetics, hormones, and environment. The number of melanocytes is the same regardless of race or sex; skin color is determined by the amount of melanin, size of melanosomes, and degradation rate.
  • Hormonal influence: MSH, estrogen, and progesterone influence pigmentation (e.g., melasma in pregnancy).
  • Pigment variation:
    • Vitiligo: Destruction of melanocytes.
    • Albinism: Defects in production/distribution (tyrosinase).
    • Freckles: Localized increased production.
    • Nevi/Melanoma: Benign/malignant proliferations.
  • Ultraviolet light: Melanin shields from UV rays. Exposure expedites melanosome transfer and production.

3. Langerhans cells

Dendritic cells found in granular, spinous, and basal layers. Derived from bone marrow precursor cells. Account for approximately 4% of epidermal cell population.

  • Function: Immunocompetent cells involved in uptake, processing, and presentation of antigen to lymphocytes. First line of immunologic defense. Involved in allergic contact hypersensitivity.
  • Functionally impaired by UV radiation.

4. Merkel cells

Found in basal cell layer, collected in tactile discs. Present in nonhairy skin (digits, lips, oral cavity).

  • Function: Slowly adapting mechanoreceptors. Transmit stimulus to neurite via chemical synapse. Involved in sensing touch and pressure.

C. Basement Membrane Zone

Found at the junction of the epidermis and dermis. Includes:

  • Hemidesmosomes: Attachment plates between basal keratinocyte and lamina densa.
  • Lamina densa: Collagen providing structure, flexibility, and barrier/filter functions.
  • Lamina lucida: Clear zone.
  • Anchoring fibrils: Fibrous component.

Diseases: Genetic diseases involving separation of epidermis and dermis result in blistering (e.g., epidermolysis bullosa, bullous pemphigoid).

D. Dermis

Principal mass of skin (1-4 mm thick). Provides structural integrity, encloses appendages, supports nerve/vascular network.

  • Composition:
    • Collagen: Major structural protein, provides tensile strength. Degraded by collagenase.
    • Elastic fibers: 5-9% of fibers. Provide resiliency and elasticity. Loss leads to striae.
    • Reticular fibers: Young collagen fibers, anchoring fibrils.
    • Ground substance: Viscoelastic gel, binds water.
  • Layers:
    • Papillary dermis: Uppermost, molds to epidermal rete ridges. Thin collagen, abundant ground substance.
    • Reticular dermis: Bulk of dermis. Thick collagen bundles parallel to surface. Carries physical stress.

Cells in the Dermis

  1. Fibroblast: Principal cell. Produces collagen, elastin, ground substance.
  2. Mast cell: Secretory cells (histamine, heparin). Respond to physical/chemical stimuli causing vasodilation, edema, and itch (immediate hypersensitivity).
  3. Macrophages: Phagocytosis, immune response (antigen presentation).
  4. Histiocytes: Scavengers (engulf debris).

E. Dermal Vasculature & Lymphatics

Two interconnected horizontal plexuses:

  • Superficial plexus: Junction of papillary/reticular dermis.
  • Deep plexus: Base of reticular dermis.

Functions: Thermoregulation, blood pressure regulation, nutrition, inflammation.

Lymphatics: Parallel vascular plexuses, filter and transport lymph.

F. Nerves

  • Autonomic motor:
    • Adrenergic: Vasoconstriction, apocrine gland, arrector pili.
    • Cholinergic: Eccrine sweat secretion.
  • Somatic sensory:
    • Corpuscular receptors: Meissner (touch - papillary dermis), Pacini (pressure - deep dermis).
    • Free nerve endings: Temperature, pain, itch.
    • Dermatome: Cutaneous area supplied by single spinal nerve.

G. Subcutaneous Tissue

Third layer (Hypodermis). Consists of lipocytes (fat cells). Insulates, shock absorbs, energy storehouse. Absent in eyelids, penis, scrotum, nipple.

Appendages: Glands

A. Sebaceous Glands

  • Location: All areas except palms/soles. Numerous on face, scalp, chest, back.
  • Unit: Associated with hair follicles (pilosebaceous unit). Independent in eyelids (Meibomian), lips (Fordyce spots), etc.
  • Development: Hormone dependent (androgens). Small at birth, enlarge at puberty.
  • Function: Produce sebum (lipids/debris). Waterproofing, lubrication, bacteriostatic.

B. Apocrine Glands

  • Location: Axilla, anogenital, areolae, ear canal (ceruminous), eyelid (Moll).
  • Function: Unique scent (pheromone).
  • Secretion: Milky, odorless, sterile. Odor produced by bacterial action on surface.
  • Stimulation: Emotional stress (adrenergic).

C. Eccrine Sweat Glands

  • Function: Thermoregulation (cooling by evaporation).
  • Location: Universal (except ear canal, lips, nail beds). Concentrated on palms, soles, forehead.
  • Anatomy: Coiled secretory portion (dermis) and duct (opens to surface).
  • Stimulation:
    • Thermal: Hypothalamus response (trunk/face).
    • Mental: Emotional stress (palms/soles/axilla).
    • Gustatory: Spicy foods.
  • Sweat Composition: Hypotonic. Water, electrolytes (Na, Cl, K), urea, lactate.

Immunologic Responses

A. Humoral Immunity

Regulated by B lymphocytes (produce antibodies/immunoglobulins).

  • IgG, IgM: Fix complement.
  • IgA: Mucosal surfaces.
  • IgE: Immediate hypersensitivity (mast cells).

B. Cellular Immunity

Involves T lymphocytes.

  • T-helper (CD4): Activate B cells, secrete cytokines.
  • T-cytotoxic (CD8): Kill target cells (lysis).
  • T-memory: Allergy, contact hypersensitivity.

C. Complement System

Proteins that lyse cells, aid phagocytosis, and trigger histamine release. Activated by antibodies (classic) or microbes (alternative).
