01

Diagnostic Aids

As adjuncts to what is seen with the eyes or palpated with the hands, numerous diagnostic tools offer key information in making a diagnosis. Nursing staff rarely perform all steps independently in diagnostic procedures used to establish skin disorders. Correct visualization and sample selection are critically important in obtaining the proper diagnostic specimen.

  • Alcohol Wipe: Swept across a lesion to elucidate small, delicate telangiectasia and translucence (e.g., basal cell carcinoma).
  • Diascopy: Gentle pressure utilizing a translucent object (glass slide) to observe effect on erythema.
    • Reduction in erythema/pigment = Blood vessel dilation.
    • No change = Extravasation of blood or increased pigment.
  • Mineral Oil: Applied to glass slide to help visualize specimens (e.g., scabies mite).
  • Dark-field Examination: Background is darkened to visualize the causative spirochete for syphilis. Specimens must be examined immediately.
02

Diagnostic Tests

KOH (Potassium Hydroxide) Preparation

Diagnostic method used to confirm presence or absence of hyphae, spores, and pseudospores. A 10% to 20% KOH solution digests keratin but leaves fungal forms intact.

üîç Click to zoom

FIGURE 3-1. KOH solution and slide.

  1. Use no. 15 blade to scrape skin/scale from edge of scaling lesion onto glass slide. Avoid extremely thick scale. (For Candida, use pustule).
  2. Sweep scale into small pile in center of slide.
  3. Place one or two drops of 10\% KOH on slide.
  4. Apply glass coverslip. Blot excess KOH with paper towel.
  5. Gently heat to dissolve keratin (or let sit 30 mins). Examine under microscope (low then high power).
  6. Repeat if negative but suspicion remains high.

Other Diagnostic Methods

  • Fungal Cultures: Used when KOH is negative or exact species is needed. Scrape dry patch, intact pustule, or nail onto dermatophyte culture medium. Incubate 7-10 days.
  • Wood's Fluorescence Lamp: UV bulbs (365 nm) cause microorganisms to fluoresce.
    • Fungus/Ringworm: Blue-gray or yellow-green.
    • Tinea Versicolor: Gold.
    • Urine (porphyrins): Orange.
    • Note: T. tonsurans (common tinea capitis) does not fluoresce.
  • Tzanck Smear: Specimen from base of new vesicle stained (Giemsa, Wright, methylene blue) to look for giant cells (Herpes virus). Does not distinguish HSV from Zoster.
  • Viral Culture: Swab serum from opened vesicle/crust into viral transport media. Identifies HSV, VZV, etc. PCR also used.
  • Bacterial Culture: Swab/aspirate intact pustules, bullae, abscesses to determine organism and abundance.
  • Scabies Scraping: Vigorously scrape small papules/burrows with oil-moistened no. 15 blade. Examine microscopically.
  • Direct Immunofluorescence: Diagnose immunologic abnormalities (bullous diseases, connective tissue disorders). Visualizes immunoglobulins, complement, fibrin.
  • Gram Stain: Identifies Gram-negative vs Gram-positive organisms.
  • Hair Pull (Pluck): Evaluates anagen/telogen ratio. Pluck ~50 hairs quickly. Anagen stains with citrulline; telogen does not.

03

Skin Biopsies

üîç Click to zoom

FIGURE 3-3. Straight blade (Shave) and punch tools.

A. Shave Biopsy/Excision

Tangential specimen resulting in minimal scarring. Removing portion of epidermis and underlying papillary dermis.

  • Indications: Dome-shaped intradermal nevi, fibromas, warts, seborrheic/actinic keratoses.
  • Technique: Use single-edged blade. Hemostasis via aluminum chloride or electrosurgery.
  • Risks: Hypopigmentation, localized infection.

B. Punch Biopsy

Circular/elliptical razor-sharp tool (2.0 to 8.0 mm). Obtains full thickness (stratum corneum to fat).

  • Indications: Diagnosing eruptions (early lesion/intact vesicle), discoid lupus (older lesion), small nevi, BCC/SCC, granuloma annulare.
  • Technique: Local anesthesia (lidocaine 1% w/ epi). Stretch skin perpendicular to lines of tension. Twist punch. May require suture.
  • Risks: Infection, scarring, nerve damage.

C. Elliptical Excision/Wedge

Used when normal and lesional skin junctions are needed. Greater depth.

  • Indications: BCC, SCC, nevi.
  • Technique: Incision length 2.5-3x width. Layered closure often necessary.
  • Mohs Technique: Serial excisions systematically mapped and microscopically examined to define cancer extent and ensure clear margins (BCC, SCC).

Cryosurgery

Destruction of tissue through freezing (-195.6^{\circ}\mathrm{C} liquid nitrogen). Causes ice formation, thermal shock, vascular stasis, necrosis. No pathology specimen obtained.

üîç Click to zoom

FIGURE 3-4. Liquid nitrogen as used for cryosurgery.

  • Indications: Actinic keratoses, thin seborrheic keratosis, leukoplakia, molluscum, warts, superficial BCC/SCC in situ (rarely).
  • Application: Spray or cotton-tipped applicator. Intense stinging peaks at 2 mins.
  • Nursing Considerations: Good cosmetic results. Painful on nose, lips, ears, eyelids. Blisters form in 3-6 hours, flatten 2-3 days, peel 2-3 weeks.

05

Hemostasis

  • Direct Pressure: Easiest, least traumatizing.
  • Hemostatic Agents: Aluminum chloride, ferric subsulfate (after shave/curettage).
  • Electrocautery/Electrodesiccation: High-frequency radio current.
  • Electrodesiccation & Curettage (ED&C): For small BCC/SCC. Remove soft tissue with curette, destroy rest with electricity. Heals by secondary intention (2-3 weeks).
06

Patch Testing Overview

Essential diagnostic tool for Allergic Contact Dermatitis (Type IV hypersensitivity). Connects sensitizing substance to reaction.

Methods

  • Finn Chamber/Scanpor Tape: Aluminum cups on tape. Allergens added manually (liquid or ointment).
  • T.R.U.E. TEST: Standardized, ready-to-use. 35 common allergens + control in hydrophilic gels on polyester backing.

Patient Selection

  • Conditions: Dermatitis/eczema, erythroderma, urticaria, photosensitivity.
  • Presentations: Suggestive history, specific suspected antigen, flares (weekend/vacation improvement), occupational relation, chronic hand/foot dermatitis.
07

Patch Testing Application Techniques

General Considerations

  • Apply to clean skin, upper back (avoid midline, hairy areas).
  • Contraindications: Recent oral steroids/IM steroids, high-potency topical steroids on test area, intense sun exposure/phototherapy.
  • NEVER test unknown substances.
  • Readings:
    • 1st Reading: 48 hours (at removal).
    • 2nd Reading: 72-96 hours (delayed reactions).
  • Keep dry. Remove if unbearable burning/itching occurs.

Box 3-1: Patient Do's and Don'ts

  • DO NOT use oral/IM steroids recently.
  • DO NOT apply creams/oils to back 1-2 days before.
  • DO keep area dry (sponge bath only).
  • DO NOT engage in strenuous activity (sweating loosens tape).
  • DO wear loose clothing (tight t-shirt to sleep).
  • DO commit to all 3 appointments (Application, 48h read, 72-96h read).
