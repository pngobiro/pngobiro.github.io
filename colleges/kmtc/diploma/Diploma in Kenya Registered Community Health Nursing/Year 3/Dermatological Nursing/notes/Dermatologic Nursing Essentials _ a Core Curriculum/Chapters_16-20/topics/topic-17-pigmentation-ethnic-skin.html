üî¨ The Melanocyte & Pigmentation

Overview

The melanocyte comprises approximately 5%-10% of the cellular component of the interfollicular epidermis. They reside in the basal layer of epidermis, forming epidermal melanin units (1 melanocyte to 30-40 keratinocytes).

Melanin Functions

  • Absorb ultraviolet light
  • Protect genome of dividing basal keratinocytes and melanocytes
  • Scavenge free oxygen radicals

Vitiligo

Acquired loss of pigmentation characterized histologically by complete absence of melanocytes in association with a total loss of epidermal pigmentation.

Clinical Manifestations

  • Distribution: Face, joints, hands, and legs are most commonly affected. Often symmetrical.
  • Patterns:
    • Localized: Focal, Segmental, Mucosal, "Lip-tip"
    • Generalized: Acrofacial, Vulgaris, Mixed
    • Universal: Covers almost entire body

Therapeutic Modalities

Medical: Topical steroids (class 3), Topical immunomodulators, Phototherapy (PUVA, UVB narrowband, Excimer laser).
Depigmentation: Monobenzone (MBEH) used when >50% involvement.
Surgical: Punch grafts, Suction blisters, Autologous cultures.

Piebaldism

Congenital absence of melanocytes in affected areas of skin and hair (white forelock is characteristic). Autosomal dominant mutation of c-kit or SNA12 gene.

Tuberous Sclerosis

Hypopigmentation is one of the earliest clinical signs. It is a rare, multisystem, genetic disease.

üü§ Disorders of Hyperpigmentation

Melasma (Chloasma faciei)

Acquired, brown hypermelanosis of the face. Often linked to hormonal factors (pregnancy, oral contraceptives).

Clinical Manifestations
Symmetrical, irregular light to dark-brown hyperpigmentation. Most common presentation is Centrofacial (cheeks, forehead, upper lip, nose, chin).

Treatment

Hydroquinone (2-4%), Retinoids, Broad-spectrum sunscreen, Azelaic acid, Chemical peels.

Melanocytic Nevi

Benign tumors composed of nevus cells derived from melanocytes.

üåç Ethnic Skin Considerations

Normal Variants in Darker Skin

  • Pigmentary Demarcation Lines (PDLs): Distinct lines between darkly and lightly pigmented skin (Futcher/Voigt lines). Common on upper arms.
  • Longitudinal Melanonychia: Linear dark bands in nail plates. 50% incidence in African descent. Must distinguish from subungual melanoma.
  • Palmar/Plantar Pigmentation: Macules or diffuse pigmentation in flexural creases.

Disease Presentation Variances

Inflammatory diseases appear differently in darker skin types. Erythema may be less evident, appearing purple, gray, or black. Follicular prominence is common. Postinflammatory hyper/hypopigmentation is a frequent sequela.

ü©∫ Common Disorders & Triads

Follicular Occlusion Triad

A group of three diseases that often occur together:

  1. 1
    Hidradenitis Suppurativa: Apocrine gland disease (axilla, groin).
  2. 2
    Acne Conglobata: Severe cystic acne with sinus tracts.
  3. 3
    Dissecting Cellulitis of the Scalp: Inflammatory nodules and sinus tracts on scalp.

Keloids

Exaggerated fibroblastic response to injury resulting in collagen deposition extending beyond the original wound boundary.

Hair Loss Disorders

Traction Alopecia: Caused by prolonged tension on hair (braids, weaves). Affects frontal/temporal hairline.

Central Centrifugal Cicatricial Alopecia (CCCA): Scarring alopecia starting at vertex. Associated with hot combs, oils, and chemical relaxers.

üß¨ Specific Ethnic Disorders

Pseudofolliculitis Barbae

Inflammatory disorder caused by tightly coiled hair re-entering the skin after shaving ("ingrown hairs"). Common in African American men.

üîç Click to zoom

FIGURE 17-15. Pseudofolliculitis barbae.

Treatment: Discontinue shaving, modify shaving technique, laser hair removal.

Dermatosis Papulosa Nigra (DPN)

Benign, stuck-on appearing hyperpigmented papules on face/neck. Variant of seborrheic keratosis. Common in African descent.

Melanocytic Lesions in Asian Patients

  • Nevus of Ota: Unilateral blue-black/brown pigmentation on face (V1/V2 trigeminal distribution), sclera, and oral mucosa. (Fig 17-17)
  • Nevus of Ito: Similar to Ota but located on shoulder/neck (posterior supraclavicular/lateral brachial nerve distribution).
  • Congenital Dermal Melanocytosis (Mongolian Spot): Blue-gray patch on sacrum/buttocks of neonates. Fades with time. (Fig 17-18)
