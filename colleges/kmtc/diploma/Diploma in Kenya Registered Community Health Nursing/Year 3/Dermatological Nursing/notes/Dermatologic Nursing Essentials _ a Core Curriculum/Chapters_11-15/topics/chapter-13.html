üçº Transient Cutaneous Conditions

Many skin conditions in newborns are transient and self-limiting, related to birth events, immaturity of physiologic systems, or maternal hormones.

1. Acne Neonatorum

üîç Click to zoom

Figure 13-1: Neonatal acne (benign cephalic pustulosis).

Inflammatory papules/pustules on cheeks. Present in 20% of infants at 2-4 weeks. Caused by transient androgen increase or Pityrosporum reaction.

2. Acropustulosis of Infancy

üîç Click to zoom

Figure 13-2: Acropustulosis of infancy.

Recurrent crops of pruritic pustules/vesicles on palms and soles. Mistaken for scabies. Recurs every 2-4 weeks.

3. Erythema Toxicum Neonatorum

üîç Click to zoom

Figure 13-3: Erythema toxicum neonatorum.

Central yellowish papule with irregular macular flare. Occurs at 24-72 hours. Spares palms/soles.

4. Milia

üîç Click to zoom

Figure 13-4: Milia.

Small retention cysts (pearly white papules) on nose, cheeks, chin. Epstein pearls are milia on the oral palate.

5. Cutis Marmorata

üîç Click to zoom

Figure 13-5: Cutis marmorata.

Physiologic reaction to cold causing reticulated mottling. Disappears on rewarming.

Other Conditions

  • Harlequin Color Change: Dependent redness/upper blanching when on side.
  • Subcutaneous Fat Necrosis: Indurated red/purple nodules. Associated with hypercalcemia. (Fig 13-6)
  • Sucking Blister: Bulla on hand/wrist from in-utero sucking.
  • Transient Pustular Melanosis: Vesicles/pustules that leave collarette of scale and hyperpigmentation. (Fig 13-8)
02

üå°Ô∏è Miliaria (Sweat Retention)

Inflammation following obstruction of eccrine sweat glands. Related to heat and humidity.

Figure 13-9: Miliaria in newborn.

Types

  • Miliaria Crystallina: Clear, superficial vesicles (1-2mm). Asymptomatic. (Fig 13-10)
  • Miliaria Rubra (Prickly Heat): Small erythematous papules. Intense prickling sensation. (Fig 13-11)
  • Miliaria Profunda: Firm, flesh-colored papules. Can lead to heat exhaustion.
üí°

Management

Control heat/humidity, remove occlusive clothing, fever management. Use cool compresses or calamine lotion.

03

üëÖ Oral Candidiasis (Thrush)

üîç Click to zoom

Figure 13-12: Oral thrush (thick white patches).

Definition: Infection of oropharyngeal cavity with Candida albicans. White, adherent, curd-like plaques that cannot be scraped off (or leave inflamed base if scraped).

Treatment: Nystatin suspension or Fluconazole. Boil bottle nipples/pacifiers.

04

üêü Ichthyosis

Definition: Excessive scaling of the skin ("fish scale").

1. Ichthyosis Vulgaris

üîç Click to zoom

Figure 13-13: Ichthyosis vulgaris (spares popliteal fossa).

Autosomal dominant. Fine scales, hyperlinear palms. Associated with atopic dermatitis.

2. X-Linked Ichthyosis

üîç Click to zoom

Figure 13-14: X-linked ichthyosis (large discrete scales).

Males only. Steroid sulfatase deficiency. Dirty yellow/brown scales. Corneal opacities.

3. Lamellar Ichthyosis & CNIE

üîç Click to zoom

Figure 13-15: Lamellar ichthyosis.

Autosomal recessive. Collodion membrane at birth. Plate-like scales, ectropion (eversion of eyelids).

4. Epidermolytic Ichthyosis

üîç Click to zoom

Figure 13-16: Epidermolytic ichthyosis ("corrugated cardboard").

Blistering in neonatal period. Thick, warty, malodorous scales.

üß¥

Treatment

Hydration, emollients, keratolytics (Alpha hydroxy acids), topical retinoids. Prevent overheating (sweat duct obstruction).

üî¥ Hemangiomas

Benign vascular tumor of endothelial cells. 5-7% of newborns.

Types

Growth Cycle

Rapid Growth (1-6 mos) → Stabilization → Regression (years).

Figure 13-21C: Regression phase (age 7 years).

Complications & Syndromes

  • Functional Impairment: Vision (Fig 13-22), airway (beard distribution, Fig 13-26).
  • Ulceration: Pain, bleeding, infection. (Fig 13-23).
  • PHACE Syndrome: Large facial hemangiomas associated with brain/heart/eye defects. (Fig 13-27).
  • Hemangiomatosis: >5 lesions, risk of liver hemangiomas (Fig 13-25).
üíä

Treatment

  • Propranolol: Systemic therapy of choice. Monitor HR/BP/Glucose.
  • Timolol: Topical gel for superficial lesions.
  • Laser: For ulceration or residual erythema.
