Immune responses that result in tissue injury are called hypersensitivity reactions. There are four types, classified by mechanism and time of onset.

Type I (Immediate/Anaphylactic) IgE mediated; mast cell histamine release 15‚Äì30 min Wheal and flare Anaphylaxis, asthma, urticaria, angioedema

üî¥ Urticaria, Angioedema, and Anaphylaxis

Urticaria (Hives)

Urticaria involves the superficial dermis, marked by transient, edematous, pruritic wheals. Lesions typically last less than 24 hours. Chronic urticaria is defined as episodes lasting >6 weeks.

Angioedema

Involves deeper subcutaneous tissue and mucosa (lips, eyes, throat). Swelling is painful or burning rather than pruritic. Can be life-threatening if airway is involved.

Treatment Modalities

  • First-line: Second-generation H1 antagonists (Cetirizine, Loratadine, Fexofenadine).
  • Second-line: First-generation H1 antagonists (Diphenhydramine, Hydroxyzine) or H2 blockers (Ranitidine).
  • Anaphylaxis: Epinephrine IM (1:1,000) is treatment of choice.

ü©∏ Vasculitis

Cutaneous Small-Vessel Vasculitis (CSVV)

Also known as leukocytoclastic vasculitis (LCV). Characterized by inflammation of small blood vessels (arterioles, capillaries, venules).

Clinical Features

Palpable Purpura: Non-blanchable, elevated hemorrhagic lesions, usually on lower extremities. May be painful or pruritic.

Erythema Multiforme (EM)

Acute, self-limiting skin disease characterized by target lesions. Most commonly associated with Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV) infection.

Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS) & Toxic Epidermal Necrolysis (TEN)

Severe mucocutaneous reactions, usually drug-induced (sulfa, anticonvulsants, allopurinol, NSAIDs). Characterized by extensive necrosis and detachment of the epidermis.

üï≥Ô∏è Pyoderma Gangrenosum

A chronic, recurrent, neutrophilic dermatosis. Lesions are painful ulcers with a characteristic undermined, violaceous border. It is NOT infectious or gangrenous.

Associations (50% of cases)

  • Inflammatory Bowel Disease (Ulcerative Colitis, Crohn's)
  • Rheumatoid Arthritis
  • Hematologic malignancies (Leukemia, Myeloma)
‚ö°

Pathergy

Enlargement or new lesion formation following trauma (including surgery or debridement). Surgical intervention should be avoided if possible.

ü¶µ Erythema Nodosum

The most common form of panniculitis (inflammation of subcutaneous fat). Presents as tender, erythematous nodules usually on the anterior shins.

Etiology

  • Infections: Streptococcal (most common in children), TB, Coccidioidomycosis.
  • Drugs: Oral contraceptives, sulfonamides.
  • Systemic Disease: Sarcoidosis (most common cause in adults), IBD.
  • Idiopathic: ~50% of cases.
