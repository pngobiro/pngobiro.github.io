üîÑ The 7 Steps of Community Diagnosis

The process of community diagnosis involves a systematic approach consisting of seven key steps:

  1. Exploration
  2. Planning of the survey
  3. Developing and Pre-testing survey tools
  4. Execution of the survey
  5. Data analysis
  6. Report writing, dissemination
  7. Community action
02

üß≠ Step 1: Exploring the Community

Exploration is essentially mapping out a community in order to learn or discover about it. It is also known as community inventory.

This phase comprises three main activities:

1. Seeking Permission & Informing Leaders

Permission is sought from:

  • Government leaders: Chiefs or subchiefs.
  • Community leaders: Village health leaders, nyumba kumi leaders. These are extremely important in determining the success of the survey since they influence the community more.
  • Health personnel: Medical superintendent or nursing officer in charge of a facility.

2. Seeking Reactions of Community Members

  • This can be done informally in market places or eateries.
  • Helps find their opinions or problems and likely solutions.
  • Helps establish any possible resistance and how to approach different members.

3. Gathering Background Data

Information collected includes:

  • Local people and the environment
  • Number of people and their distribution
  • Diseases that the locals commonly suffer from
  • The organization of health services

üìÖ Step 2: Planning the Survey

‚ùì

Key Questions to Answer

  1. Why is the Survey Being Done? (OBJECTIVES)
  2. Where will it take place? (STUDY AREA)
  3. Who will be interviewed? (SAMPLING & INTERVIEWERS)
  4. When will the survey take place? (TIMING)
  5. What will be covered in the survey? (TOOLS, RESOURCES)
  6. How are the research assistants selected and trained? (INTERVIEWERS)

Selection & Training of Interviewers

Qualities of Interviewers

  • Be literate and well known to the community
  • Ability to display the right attitudes and opinions
  • Able to explain the questionnaire effectively
  • Able to use the tools
  • Able to establish good rapport with individuals/families
  • Good listeners and sensitive towards other people's feeling

Tips on Training

  • Training should be done in groups rather than individuals.
  • Explain the survey purpose, guidelines, and procedures.
  • Go through interview schedules question by question (including probing).
  • Prepare specifications for handling difficult situations.
  • Demonstrate how to carry out interviews properly.
  • Pair off interviewers to practice on each other.
  • Pretest interviews under similar conditions.

üìä Sampling Fundamentals

Definitions

Sampling
The process of selecting a number of individuals or units of the study population in such a way that the individuals selected represent the larger groups from which they are selected for the purpose of determining parameters or characteristics of the whole population.
Sample
A part of a statistical population whose properties are studied to gain information about the whole.
Study Population
The entire group of individuals, events or objects that have common observable characteristics (e.g., All first years in nursing).
Representative Sample
A group from the study population which has all the important/relevant characteristics of the total population.

Probability Sampling

Utilizes some form of random selection such that different units in your population have equal probabilities of being chosen. Used for large groups to give accurate results.

1. Simple Random Sampling

Every sampling unit in the population has an equal chance of being selected. Methods include balloting, table of random numbers, or computer generators.

2. Systematic Sampling

Obtained by selecting one unit on a random basis and choosing additional elementary units at evenly spaced intervals.

Example:

Target Population (N) = 200 ANC clients. Desired Sample (n) = 50.

  1. Divide 200 (N) by 50 (n) to get 4 (k).
  2. Randomly select a number between 1 and 4 (e.g., 2).
  3. Client 002 is the starting point.
  4. Select every \( 4^{\text{th}}(K^{\text{th}}) \) client until 50 clients are selected.

3. Stratified Sampling

Dividing the sample frame into smaller sub-samples (strata) to capture variable aspects (e.g., different ethnic groups, age groups). Simple random sampling is then applied to each stratum.

4. Cluster Sampling

Randomly selecting groups or clusters (not individuals). Used when population is large or scattered. Example: Randomly selecting health facilities and including all patients in those facilities.

5. Multistage Sampling

Combines two or more probability sampling techniques (e.g., Cluster + Stratified + Simple Random).

Non-Probability Sampling

Used when representativeness is not the primary goal, often in qualitative studies.

Method Description
Convenience Sampling Selecting cases as they become available (e.g., interviewing everyone visiting a clinic today). Useful for first impressions but not representative.
Quota Sampling Selecting subjects to fit identified quotas (e.g., specific religion or social class). Selection is not random.
Purposive Sampling Picking individuals who have specific information or characteristics required.
Snowballing Initial subjects identify others with required characteristics, who then identify others.

Bias & Error

  • Sampling Bias: The tendency to favor the selection of units that have particular characteristics.
  • Sampling Error: The differences between the sample and the population due to the particular units that happen to have been selected.
