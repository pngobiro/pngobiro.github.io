üè∑Ô∏è Types of Community Diagnosis

There are two primary approaches to conducting a community diagnosis, each serving a distinct purpose:

1. Comprehensive Community Diagnosis

Goal: Aims to obtain general information about the community.

This approach covers a broad range of variables to create a complete profile of the community's health status, resources, and demographics.

2. Problem-Oriented Community Diagnosis

Goal: Responds to a particular need.

This approach focuses on a specific health issue or problem identified within the community, allowing for targeted intervention.

02

üë• 1. Demographic Variables

The first element of a comprehensive diagnosis involves understanding the population structure and dynamics.

  • Population Size & Distribution: Total population, geographical distribution including Urban-Rural index & Population Density.
  • Composition: Age & Sex composition.
  • Vital Indicators: Selected indicators e.g. Growth rate, CBR (Crude Birth Rate), CDR (Crude Death Rate) & Life expectancy rate.
  • Movement: Patterns of migration.
  • Future Trends: Population projection.

3. Health & Illness Patterns

This element focuses on the morbidity and mortality profile of the community:

Indicator Description
Mortality Leading causes of death in the general population.
Morbidity Leading causes of illness and disease.
Infant Mortality Leading causes of death among infants (under 1 year).
Maternal Mortality Leading causes of death related to pregnancy and childbirth.
Hospital Admission Leading causes of hospital admission.

4. Health Resources

Assessment of available capacity to address health needs:

  • Manpower Resources: Availability of healthcare professionals (doctors, nurses, CHWs).
  • Material Resources: Availability of facilities, equipment, drugs, and funding.
