üìù Questionnaires

A questionnaire is a set of standardized questions designed to collect information about a specific aspect or issue. It helps make plans for health services and evaluate them.

‚ñ∂ Qualities of a Good Questionnaire
  • Simple & Specific: Questions should be short, precise, and avoid jargon or abbreviations.
  • Unbiased: Avoid leading questions or making assumptions.
  • Logical Flow: Questions should range from known to unknown, simple to complex, and flow easily from one topic to another.
  • Relevant: All questions should relate to the purpose of the study.
  • Acceptable: View questions through the respondent's eye. Will it be seen as reasonable? Will it infringe on privacy?
  • Actionable: Do not screen for diseases if no effective treatment can be offered.
‚ñ∂ Presentation Tips
  • Make it attractive using good quality paper.
  • Organize layout for easy completion.
  • Number all pages.
  • Include brief but clear instructions.
  • Keep it short.

Types of Questions

1. Open-ended vs. Closed-ended

Feature Open-Ended / Unstructured Closed-Ended / Structured
Definition Allows respondent to provide their own answer in their own words. Offers a list of possible answers to choose from.
Use Case Exploratory, sensitive issues, opinions, facts researcher is unfamiliar with. Specific aspects, uniform data collection.
Advantages Greater depth, simple to formulate, reveals hidden motivations/feelings. Easy to analyze, economical (time/money), easier to administer.
Disadvantages Difficult to analyze/categorize, time-consuming, responses may be irrelevant. Difficult to construct, limited responses, compels choice.

3. Matrix Questions

Questions sharing the same set of response categories (e.g., Likert scale).

Example: How satisfied are you with your:

  • Financial status: 1 2 3 4 5
  • Quality of life: 1 2 3 4 5

Key: 1=Extremely dissatisfied ... 5=Extremely satisfied

Pros: Efficient use of space, easier to complete, easy comparison.
Cons: "Pattern answering" (respondents may not be keen).

üó£Ô∏è Focus Group Discussions (FGDs)

An FGD gathers people from similar backgrounds/experiences to discuss a specific topic. It relies on interaction‚Äîparticipants talk to each other, not just the moderator.

Uses of FGDs

  • Exploratory studies: Exploring topics where little is known.
  • Testing ideas: Getting feedback on new programs/plans.
  • Problem solving: Identifying barriers in existing programs.

Key Steps

  1. Preparation: Recruit participants with common characteristics.
  2. Physical Arrangement: Circular seating, well-lit, minimal disturbance.
  3. Discussion Guide: Prepare logical questions with probes.
  4. The Discussion: Facilitated by a team.

Roles in an FGD

Role Responsibilities
Facilitator
  • Introduce topic and participants.
  • Encourage involvement and control dominant speakers.
  • Watch for non-verbal cues.
  • Keep discussion on time.
Recorder
  • Record key issues and opinions (verbatim if possible).
  • Document non-verbal messages/emotions.
  • Help resolve conflicting issues.

üéôÔ∏è Interviews

An interview is an oral administration of a questionnaire or schedule. It must be a face-to-face encounter.

Advantages

  • Provides in-depth data.
  • Flexible; interviewer can clarify questions.
  • Can extract sensitive info through rapport.
  • Higher response rate.
  • Can observe non-verbal cues.

Disadvantages

  • Expensive (travel costs).
  • Time-consuming.
  • Requires high skill level.
  • Potential for interviewer bias.
  • Lack of anonymity may influence responses.

Rules for Interviewers

  • Be pleasant, relaxed, and friendly.
  • Interact as an equal; do not appear like a spy.
  • Be familiar with the guide.
  • Ensure confidentiality.
  • Do not ask leading questions.
  • Remain neutral.

Types of Interview Schedules

Structured

Questions with specific responses to check.

Unstructured

General plan/guide. Questions are open, relying on probing.

Semi-Structured

Includes both structured and open-ended questions.

üîç Observation & Pre-testing

Observation Checklists

Forms used to record what the observer sees during data collection. Sometimes rating scales are used to quantify observations (e.g., cleanliness levels).

Pre-testing Instruments

It is vital to pre-test tools before the actual survey to identify misinterpreted or sensitive questions and ensure logical flow.

Pre-testing Procedure:

  1. Peer review (colleagues review questions).
  2. Test on people similar to the target group.
  3. Simulate actual data collection.
  4. Obtain feedback on form and content.
  5. Check if questions produce needed info.
  6. Try out tabulation/analysis.
  7. Revise accordingly.
