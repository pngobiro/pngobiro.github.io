Kenya Medical Training College
Community Health Nursing - Infection Prevention and Control

Instructions to Candidates:

Answer all questions. Use clear and concise language.

Question 1

(a) Define the concept of Infection Prevention and Control. (2 marks)

Sample Answer:

Infection Prevention and Control refers to the policies, procedures, and practices implemented in healthcare settings to minimize the risk of transmitting infections to patients, healthcare providers, support staff, and the community.

Question 2

(a) State the main goal of infection prevention. (2 marks)

Sample Answer:

The main goal of infection prevention is to minimize post-procedure infections in patients and prevent the transmission of life-threatening infections to patients, service providers, auxiliary staff, and members of the community.

Question 3

(a) List five objectives of studying Infection Prevention and Control. (5 marks)

Sample Answer:

  1. Describe the disease transmission cycle and those at risk of infection.
  2. State an overview of infectious diseases.
  3. List some standard precautions.
  4. List common antiseptics, disinfectants, and antiseptic techniques.
  5. Describe the use and disposal of needles and other sharps.

Question 4

(a) Describe the recent changes in the pattern of infectious diseases in Kenya. (4 marks)

Sample Answer:

Over the past few decades, Kenya has experienced a drastic change in the pattern of infectious diseases. Some eradicated diseases are re-emerging (e.g., COVID-19), and new ones are being identified. There have been sporadic outbreaks of diseases like cholera, measles, plague, and yellow fever. New viruses like Hepatitis B and C have become common. Drug resistance and high HIV/AIDS prevalence further complicate the situation.

Question 5

(a) Explain the term "nosocomial infections." (2 marks)

Sample Answer:

Nosocomial infections are hospital-acquired infections that patients contract during their stay in a healthcare facility. These infections can range from trivial to life-threatening conditions like septicemia.

Question 6

(a) Identify the most frequent types of nosocomial infections globally. (3 marks)

Sample Answer:

Globally, the most frequent nosocomial infections are surgical wound infections, lower respiratory tract infections, and urinary tract infections.

Question 7

(a) Explain why healthcare facilities are ideal settings for the transmission of infections. (3 marks)

Sample Answer:

Healthcare facilities are ideal settings for the transmission of infections because the invasive procedures routinely performed on patients can introduce micro-organisms into the body. Additionally, the presence of sick individuals and the use of shared equipment contribute to the risk.

Question 8

(a) Identify the groups of people who are at risk of infection in the context of healthcare. (3 marks)

Sample Answer:

The groups at risk of infection include service providers in healthcare facilities (due to constant exposure), their families (due to poor infection prevention practices), and the community (through contact with medical waste).

Question 9

(a) Differentiate between infection and colonization. (4 marks)

Sample Answer:

Infection is the invasion and multiplication of microorganisms in a host, with an associated host response (e.g., fever, pain). Colonization, on the other hand, is when microorganisms are present in the host but do not invade or cause a response. Infection may require treatment, while colonization typically does not.

Question 10

(a) Describe the disease transmission cycle. (5 marks)

Sample Answer:

The disease transmission cycle involves the following steps:

  1. Infectious agents survive, grow, or multiply in a reservoir.
  2. They leave the reservoir through a place of exit.
  3. They are transmitted via a mode of transmission.
  4. They enter a susceptible host through a place of entry.
  5. The cycle repeats.

Question 11

(a) Differentiate between normal flora and pathogens. (2 marks)

Sample Answer:

Normal flora are micro-organisms normally present on people's skin, respiratory, intestinal, and genital tracts, and they often help prevent pathogenic colonization. Pathogens are micro-organisms not normally found in the body and are associated with disease.

Question 12

(a) List and briefly describe five types of microbial agents. (5 marks)

Sample Answer:

  1. Commensal bacteria: Found as normal flora, preventing pathogenic colonization.
  2. Pathogenic bacteria: Have great virulence and cause infection.
  3. Viruses: Not mentioned in the context
  4. Parasites: Not mentioned in the context
  5. Fungi: Not mentioned in the context

Question 13

(a) Explain the circumstances under which micro-organisms can cause infection or disease. (3 marks)

Sample Answer:

Micro-organisms can cause infection or disease when:

  • Normal flora are introduced into an area of the body where they are not normally found.
  • Pathogens are introduced into the body.
  • Micro-organisms are introduced into the body of an immuno-compromised person.

Question 14

(a) Explain how infections can be transmitted in a healthcare setting. (4 marks)

Sample Answer:

Infections can be transmitted in a healthcare setting when:

  • A healthcare worker's skin is pierced by contaminated sharps.
  • A healthcare worker's broken skin comes into contact with a patient's blood or body fluids.
  • A patient's blood or body fluids splash onto a healthcare worker's mucous membranes.

Question 15

(a) List five WHO guidelines for preventing the spread of infections in healthcare settings. (5 marks)

Sample Answer:

  1. Observe and maintain good personal hygiene and wear appropriate attire.
  2. Be vaccinated against vaccine-preventable conditions.
  3. If working in vulnerable areas (e.g., handling cooked food), be periodically screened for certain diseases.
  4. If suffering from infectious conditions and working with susceptible patients, be re-deployed until cleared.
  5. Treat all patients as potentially infectious.

Question 16

(a) Describe five ways a healthcare worker can spread infections from one patient to another or to their family and community. (5 marks)

Sample Answer:

  1. Not washing hands before and after contact with patients or before leaving the health facility.
  2. Using surgical instruments that have not been processed properly.
  3. Improper disposal of medical waste.
  4. Contaminating clothing or hair while rendering care to patients.
  5. Contracting infections and spreading them to family members.

Question 17

(a) Define the term "Standard Precautions." (2 marks)

Sample Answer:

Standard precautions are a set of clinical practice recommendations designed to help minimize the risk of exposure to infectious materials, such as blood and other body fluids, by both patients and staff.

Question 18

(a) Explain why standard precautions should be followed routinely at all times. (2 marks)

Sample Answer:

Standard precautions should be followed routinely because it is often not possible to tell who is infected with viruses like HIV or hepatitis, even the infected persons themselves may not know. Therefore, these precautions should apply to every patient.

Question 19

(a) Explain why handwashing is considered the single most effective method of preventing the transfer of micro-organisms. (2 marks)

Sample Answer:

Handwashing is considered the single most effective method because it removes soil, organic material, and transient micro-organisms from the skin, thus breaking the chain of infection transmission.

Question 20

(a) List five appropriate times for washing hands while at work in a healthcare setting. (5 marks)

Sample Answer:

  1. Immediately after arriving at work.
  2. Before examining each patient.
  3. After examining each patient.
  4. After touching contaminated instruments or objects.
  5. After touching mucous membranes.

Question 21

(a) List the three essential elements for effective handwashing. (3 marks)

Sample Answer:

  1. Soap
  2. Running water
  3. Friction

Question 22

(a) Describe the four types of handwashing. (8 marks)

Sample Answer:

  1. Routine handwashing: Using plain soap and running water to remove transient micro-organisms and soil.
  2. Antiseptic handwashing: Using antiseptic soap and running water to kill or inhibit the growth of resident micro-organisms.
  3. Alcohol hand rub: Using alcohol-based hand rub to kill or inhibit the growth of micro-organisms when hands are not visibly soiled.
  4. Surgical hand scrub: Scrubbing with antiseptic before surgical procedures to prevent the growth of micro-organisms.

Question 23

(a) State the policy statement regarding handwashing in a healthcare facility according to the document. (5 marks)

Sample Answer:

  1. Patients and family members shall be instructed in proper handwashing.
  2. The patient's hands shall be washed before eating, after toileting, and when soiled.
  3. The healthcare facility-approved soap shall be used for routine handwashing.
  4. Repeat handwashing shall not be done in the same container of water.
  5. Hands shall be washed under running water.

Question 24

(a) Explain the purpose of using personal protective equipment (PPE) in a healthcare setting. (2 marks)

Sample Answer:

Personal protective equipment (PPE) are physical barriers used to prevent the transmission of infection to the patient or from the patient to the persons attending them.

Question 25

(a) Describe the purpose of wearing gloves in a healthcare setting. (2 marks)

Sample Answer:

Gloves provide a barrier against potentially infectious micro-organisms in blood, other body fluids, and medical waste, thus lowering the risk of transmitting infections to both healthcare workers and patients. They also protect against hazardous chemical waste.

Question 26

(a) List three situations when a healthcare worker should wear gloves. (3 marks)

Sample Answer:

  1. Providing clinical services.
  2. Handling or cleaning used instruments and other items.
  3. Performing housekeeping activities.

Question 27

(a) Differentiate between sterile surgical gloves and single-use examination gloves and provide examples of when each should be used. (6 marks)

Sample Answer:

Sterile surgical gloves or high-level disinfected surgical gloves should be worn during procedures where the main aim is to avoid the introduction of pathogens into the patient, such as surgical procedures or insertion of Norplant implants. Single-use examination gloves should be worn for procedures in which you will be in contact with intact mucous membranes, such as IUCD insertion or pelvic examination.

Question 28

(a) Explain when gowns and aprons should be used in a healthcare setting and provide examples of such situations. (4 marks)

Sample Answer:

Gowns and aprons should be made of waterproof material and worn when splashing of blood or other body fluids or any other potentially infectious materials is anticipated. Examples include surgical operations, conducting deliveries, and during intubations and sanctioning procedures.

Question 29

(a) Describe the purpose of masks and eye shields and when they should be used. (4 marks)

Sample Answer:

Masks and eye shields provide some protection against airborne pathogens and shield against splashes. They should be worn during dental surgery, when conducting deliveries, endoscopies, or in any other situations where splashing or spattering is anticipated.

Question 30

(a) Explain the use of headgear and boots in a healthcare setting. (2 marks)

Sample Answer:

Caps are worn to cover the head. Boots and shoe covers should be waterproof and cleaned frequently. They should be used in operative procedures.

Question 31

(a) Define the terms "antiseptic" and "disinfectant" and explain the difference between them. (4 marks)

Sample Answer:

An antiseptic is a chemical agent used on the skin and mucous membranes to remove or kill micro-organisms without causing damage. A disinfectant is a chemical agent used to kill micro-organisms on inanimate objects. Antiseptics are used on living tissue, while disinfectants are used on non-living surfaces.

Question 32

(a) Differentiate between high-level and low-level disinfectants. (4 marks)

Sample Answer:

High-level disinfectants kill bacteria, viruses, fungi, and some bacterial endospores. Low-level disinfectants kill most bacteria and some viruses and fungi but do not kill tuberculosis-causing micro-organisms and bacterial endospores.

Question 33

(a) Explain why instruments should not be soaked or stored in a disinfectant solution for a very long time. (2 marks)

Sample Answer:

Instruments should not be soaked or stored in a disinfectant solution for a very long time because micro-organisms can live and multiply in disinfectant solutions, which can contaminate the instruments and lead to infections.

Question 34

(a) For each of the following antiseptics/disinfectants, list the antimicrobial spectrum, advantages, disadvantages, and comments/recommendations: (20 marks)

i. Alcohol (60-90% ethyl or isopropyl)

ii. Chlorhexidine gluconate (Hibitane, Hibiclens, Hibiscrub)/Chlorhexidine gluconate with cetrimide (Savlon)

iii. Hexachlorophene (Phisohex)

iv. Iodine, including tincture of iodine (iodine and alcohol)

v. Iodophors (solutions such as Povidone Iodine e. g. Betadine)

vi. Para-Chloro-Meta-Xylenol (PCMX, Chloroxylenol, e.g. Dettol)

Sample Answer:

i. Alcohol (60-90% ethyl or isopropyl)

  • Antimicrobial Spectrum: Kills micro-organisms most rapidly. Effective against a broad range of micro-organisms.
  • Advantages: Most effective in reducing micro-organisms. Effectiveness is only moderately reduced by blood or other organic material.
  • Disadvantages: Has a drying effect on the skin. Cannot be used when the skin is dirty. Cannot be used on mucous membranes.
  • Comments: Wash the area before applying. Must dry skin completely to be effective. The 60-70% strength is most effective.

ii. Chlorhexidine gluconate (Hibitane, Hibiclens, Hibiscrub)/Chlorhexidine gluconate with cetrimide (Savlon)

  • Antimicrobial Spectrum: Effective against a broad range of micro-organisms, remains effective for at least 6 hours after being applied, but has a minimal effect on tuberculosis and fungi.
  • Advantages: Has a good persistent effect. Effectiveness is not reduced by blood or other organic material.
  • Disadvantages: On rare occasions, products containing chlorhexidine have been reported to cause irritation, especially when used in the genital area. Effectiveness can be reduced by hard water, hand creams, and natural soaps.
  • Comments: Recommended antiseptic for surgical hand scrub and skin preparation. Preparations without cetrimide are preferable.

iii. Hexachlorophene (Phisohex)

  • Antimicrobial Spectrum: Poor effectiveness against most micro-organisms.
  • Advantages: Has a good, persistent effect with repeated use, which remains effective for at least six hours after being applied.
  • Disadvantages: Potentially toxic to the nervous system. Occasional use is not effective in reducing the number of micro-organisms on the hands. If use is discontinued after long-term use, a rebound increase of bacterial growth will occur.
  • Comments: Contraindicated for routine use on irritated or broken skin or mucous membranes. Not recommended for use in surgical hand scrub or skin preparation.

iv. Iodine, including tincture of iodine (iodine and alcohol)

  • Antimicrobial Spectrum: Effective against a broad range of micro-organisms.
  • Advantages: Fast-acting.
  • Disadvantages: Can cause irritation. Effectiveness is markedly reduced by blood or other organic material.
  • Comments: Too irritating for routine use in surgical hand scrub or for use on mucous membranes. When used for preparation, iodine must be allowed to dry and then removed from the skin with alcohol.

v. Iodophors (solutions such as Povidone Iodine e. g. Betadine)

  • Antimicrobial Spectrum: Effective against a broad range of micro-organisms.
  • Advantages: Less irritating to the skin than iodine. Can be used on mucous membranes.
  • Disadvantages: Effectiveness is moderately reduced by blood or other organic material.
  • Comments: Recommended antiseptic for surgical hand scrub and client preparation. Best antiseptic for use in the genitalia area, vagina, and cervix. Effective 1-2 minutes after application. Most preparations should be used at full strength.

vi. Para-Chloro-Meta-Xylenol (PCMX, Chloroxylenol, e.g. Dettol)

  • Antimicrobial Spectrum: Fairly effective against most micro-organisms.
  • Advantages: Has a persistent effect over several hours. Activity is only minimally reduced by blood or other organic material.
  • Disadvantages: Less effective than chlorhexidine and iodophors.
  • Comments: Not recommended for routine use. PCMX is available in both antiseptic and disinfectant preparations. Preparations containing alcohol should not be used on mucous membranes. Disinfectant preparations containing PCMX should not be used as antiseptics.

Question 35

(a) For each of the following high-level disinfectants, list the antimicrobial spectrum, advantages, disadvantages, and comments/recommendations: (10 marks)

i. Chlorine

ii. Glutaraldehyde (Cidex)

Sample Answer:

i. Chlorine

  • Antimicrobial Spectrum: Effective against a broad range of micro-organisms, including tubercle bacilli. Does not kill all bacterial endospores.
  • Advantages: Fast-acting. Least expensive disinfectant.
  • Disadvantages: Can be corrosive to metals when in prolonged contact (more than 20 minutes). Can be irritating to the skin, eyes, and respiratory tract. Leaves a residue.
  • Comments: Available in liquid (sodium hypochlorite), powder (calcium hypochlorite), and tablet (sodium dichloride socyanurate) forms. Can be used for decontamination (by soaking for ten minutes) or HLD (by soaking for 20 minutes).

ii. Glutaraldehyde (Cidex)

  • Antimicrobial Spectrum: Effective against a broad range of micro-organisms, including tubercle bacilli. Does not kill all bacterial endospores.
  • Advantages: Fast-acting. Least expensive disinfectant.
  • Disadvantages: Can be corrosive to metals when in prolonged contact (more than 20 minutes). Can be irritating to the skin, eyes, and respiratory tract. Leaves a residue.
  • Comments: Used most commonly to process medical equipment such as laparoscopes that cannot be heat sterilized. Can be used for HLD (by soaking for 20 minutes) and sterilization (by soaking for ten hours) of instruments and other items.

Question 36

(a) Provide examples of common low-level disinfectants and explain where they are commonly used. (4 marks)

Sample Answer:

Examples of low-level disinfectants (LLDs) are phenols (carbolic acid such as phenol, lysol) and quaternary ammonium compounds (berzal conium chloride – zephiron). They are commonly used to disinfect walls, floors, and furnishings.

Question 37

(a) Define "aseptic technique" and explain its importance in nursing practice. (4 marks)

Sample Answer:

Aseptic technique is a method of carrying out sterile procedures so that there is the minimum risk of introducing infection. It is important in nursing practice to prevent the spread of infections during procedures such as wound dressing, administration of injections, and surgical procedures.

Question 38

(a) List five sterile procedures that a nurse might perform where aseptic technique is crucial. (5 marks)

Sample Answer:

  1. Wound dressing.
  2. Administration of an injection.
  3. Removing stitches, staples, and clips.
  4. Shortening or removing drains.
  5. Urinary catheterization.

Question 39

(a) State the basic rules of surgical asepsis as described by Brunner and Sudarth (1988). (4 marks)

Sample Answer:

According to Brunner and Sudarth (1988), only sterile surfaces or articles may touch other sterile surfaces or articles and remain sterile. Un-sterile contact at any point renders a sterile area contaminated. If there is any doubt about the sterility of an article or area, it is considered un-sterile.

Question 40

(a) Explain how to handle unused sterile supplies. (2 marks)

Sample Answer:

Unused sterile supplies must be discarded or re-sterilized if they are to be used again.

Question 41

(a) Describe how to pour sterile fluids to prevent contamination. (2 marks)

Sample Answer:

Pour sterile fluids from a point high enough to prevent accidental touching of the receptacle, but not so high as to produce splashing, which could cause the fluid to touch an unsterile surface and then flow back into the receptacle, causing contamination.

Question 42

(a) List the practices performed just before or during a clinical procedure to maintain aseptic technique. (5 marks)

Sample Answer:

  1. Hand washing.
  2. Surgical hand scrub.
  3. Using barriers such as gloves and surgical attire.
  4. Proper preparation of a patient for clinical procedures.
  5. Maintaining a sterile field.

Question 43

(a) Explain the purpose of using an antiseptic solution on the patient's skin, vagina, or cervix before a procedure. (2 marks)

Sample Answer:

The purpose is to destroy or prevent the growth of micro-organisms and reduce the risk of surgical site infections.

Question 44

(a) Describe the steps for preparing the skin before a surgical procedure. (5 marks)

Sample Answer:

  1. Clean the site with soap and water.
  2. Wipe the skin in a circular motion, beginning in the center and moving outwards.
  3. Use sterile cotton balls or cotton wool sponges held on sponge forceps.
  4. Apply an appropriate antiseptic (e.g., iodophor, 4% chlorhexidine, 1-3% iodine followed by alcohol, or chlorhexidine with cetrimide).
  5. Allow to dry or wipe off excess as needed.

Question 45

(a) Explain which antiseptic should be used for preparing the vagina and cervix before procedures like IUD insertion and why. (4 marks)

Sample Answer:

An appropriate antiseptic, such as an iodophor, should be applied to the vagina and cervix before passing instruments into the uterus. Alcohol-based antiseptics should not be used because they are damaging to these tissues.

Question 46

(a) Describe the measures for maintaining a sterile field during a surgical or procedure site. (5 marks)

Sample Answer:

Measures for maintaining a sterile field include:

  • Placing only sterile items within the sterile field.
  • Opening, dispensing, or transferring sterile items without contaminating them.
  • Considering items below the level of the draped client to be unsterile.
  • Not allowing sterile personnel to reach across unsterile areas.
  • Not allowing unsterile personnel to reach across the sterile field.

Question 47

(a) Describe the measures for maintaining a safe environment in the surgical/procedure area. (5 marks)

Sample Answer:

Measures for maintaining a safe environment include:

  • Limiting the entry of unauthorized individuals.
  • Closing doors and curtains during procedures.
  • Requiring personnel to wear clean clothes, a mask, a cap, and sturdy footwear.
  • Enclosing the area to minimize dust and eliminate insects.
  • Disinfecting and cleaning surfaces contaminated with blood or body fluids.

Question 48

(a) Explain how careless disposal of contaminated sharps can cause infections. (2 marks)

Sample Answer:

Careless disposal of contaminated sharps can cause infections in the healthcare facility and the community if individuals are pricked or injured by improperly discarded sharps.

Question 49

(a) Describe how to make hypodermic needles and other sharps unusable. (2 marks)

Sample Answer:

Hypodermic needles and other sharps can be made unusable by incinerating them.

Question 50

(a) Explain how to reduce the risk of infections from sharps before disposal. (2 marks)

Sample Answer:

The risk of infections from sharps can be reduced by decontaminating them before disposal and burying them in a pit to prevent scavenging.

Question 51

(a) Describe the process of decontamination for instruments and other items. (4 marks)

Sample Answer:

Decontamination is performed by soaking instruments and other items immediately after use in a 0.5% chlorine solution for ten minutes. This process kills viruses and many other micro-organisms, making the items easier to clean.

Question 52

(a) Explain how to dispose of sharps safely. (5 marks)

Sample Answer:

Sharps should be handled with extreme caution. Used needles should not be recapped by hand. If necessary, use the single-hand "scoop" method. Do not remove the needle from the syringe by hand. Dispose of the needle and attached syringe in a puncture-resistant sharps disposal container.

Question 53

(a) List five rules to obey when handling sharps disposal containers. (5 marks)

Sample Answer:

  1. Dispose of sharps disposal containers when they are three-quarters full.
  2. Dispose of used sharps immediately after use in designated puncture-resistant containers.
  3. Containers should not be located in areas open to the public.
  4. Discard used syringes and needles as a unit in the designated puncture-resistant container.
  5. Hold reusable syringes, needles, or sharps in a puncture-resistant leak-proof container labeled with a biohazard sign for transport to the re-processing area.

Question 54

(a) Describe the steps for decontaminating reusable hypodermic needles and syringes. (6 marks)

Sample Answer:

  1. Drop the needle and syringe into a container of 0.5% chlorine solution and let them soak for ten minutes.
  2. Remove the needle and syringe from the solution using cheatle forceps or by hand if hands are protected by utility gloves.
  3. Rinse by flushing with clean water or clean immediately.

Question 55

(a) Describe the management of injuries from needles and other sharps. (3 marks)

Sample Answer:

Management of injuries from needles and other sharps includes:

  • Wash needle stick cuts with soap and water.
  • Flush the nose, mouth, or skin with water if splashed with blood or body fluid.
  • Irrigate the eyes with water or saline if splashed with body fluid.

Question 56

(a) Explain what post-exposure prophylaxis is and list the factors to be considered following exposure to blood or other body fluids. (5 marks)

Sample Answer:

Post-exposure prophylaxis is the administration of drugs to reduce the risk of transmission of bloodborne pathogens after potential exposure. Factors to consider include the infection status of the source patient, the type of exposure, whether the exposed person has been vaccinated against Hepatitis B, the time elapsed since exposure, and the availability of required drugs.

Question 57

(a) Describe the prophylaxis for Hepatitis B and HIV after potential exposure. (4 marks)

Sample Answer:

For Hepatitis B, prophylaxis includes Hepatitis B immune globulin and Hepatitis B vaccine. For HIV, several antiretroviral drugs are used, either alone or in combination, such as Zidovudine (AZT), Lamivudine (3TC), and Nelfinavir (Viracept).

Question 58

(a) List the guidelines to follow when giving injections to reduce the risk of transmitting infections between patients. (3 marks)

Sample Answer:

  1. Every time you give an injection, use a new or correctly processed hypodermic needle and syringe.
  2. Do not change the needle without changing the syringe between patients.
  3. Reusing the same syringe to give injections to many clients is not a safe practice.

Question 59

(a) Describe the guidelines to follow when administering intravenous fluids to avoid the transmission of infections. (3 marks)

Sample Answer:

  1. Unhook the needle or catheter from the IV line and dispose of it in a sharps disposal container.
  2. Throw away any remaining fluid.
  3. Do not use the same IV line and fluid bag/bottle with multiple clients.

Question 60

(a) Describe the guidelines for using multi-dose vials safely. (5 marks)

Sample Answer:

  1. Check the vial to be sure there are no leaks or cracks.
  2. Check the solution to be sure it is not cloudy and that there is no particulate matter in the vial.
  3. Wipe the top of the vial with a fresh cotton swab soaked with 60-70% alcohol.
  4. Allow to dry.
  5. Always use a sterile needle and syringe every time medication is withdrawn from a multiple-dose vial.

Question 61

(a) Explain why healthcare workers are at high risk of infection when processing instruments and other items. (2 marks)

Sample Answer:

Healthcare workers are at high risk of infection when processing instruments and other items because they are at risk of exposure to blood or other body fluids.

Question 62

(a) List four situations that increase the risk of exposure to infections during the processing of instruments. (4 marks)

Sample Answer:

  1. Open cuts on the hands or forearms.
  2. Chapped or cracked hands.
  3. Injuries from needle sticks or other sharp instruments.
  4. Splashing of blood or other body fluids onto mucous membranes.

Question 63

(a) Describe the focus of measures to protect oneself when handling instruments and other items. (2 marks)

Sample Answer:

The measures focus on destroying as many micro-organisms as possible early in the process and preventing blood and other body fluids from coming into contact with the skin or membranes.

Question 64

(a) List the steps involved in processing instruments and other items. (3 marks)

Sample Answer:

  1. Decontamination.
  2. Cleaning.
  3. Either sterilization or high-level disinfection.

Question 65

(a) Explain the purpose of decontamination in the processing of instruments. (2 marks)

Sample Answer:

Decontamination kills viruses and many micro-organisms, making the instruments and other items safe to handle by the staff who clean them. It also makes them easier to clean by preventing blood, other body fluids, and tissue from drying on them.

Question 66

(a) Describe how to prepare a 0.5% chlorine solution from household liquid bleach (sodium hypochlorite) that is 3.5% chlorine. (4 marks)

Sample Answer:

To make a 0.5% chlorine solution from 3.5% bleach, add one part bleach to six parts water (3.5% / 0.5% - 1 = 6).

Question 67

(a) Explain how to prepare a 0.5% chlorine solution from calcium hypochlorite powder containing 35% active chlorine. (4 marks)

Sample Answer:

To make a 0.5% chlorine solution from calcium hypochlorite powder containing 35% active chlorine, dissolve 14.3 grams of the powder in each liter of water (0.5% / 35% * 1000 = 14.3).

Question 68

(a) Explain why chlorine is widely used for preventing infections in healthcare settings. (2 marks)

Sample Answer:

Chlorine is widely used because it is a proven and powerful killer of micro-organisms and it deodorizes.

Question 69

(a) Describe the process of cleaning instruments and other items. (5 marks)

Sample Answer:

Cleaning involves scrubbing with a brush, detergent, and water to remove blood, other body fluids, organic material, tissue, and dirt. Instruments should be held under the surface of the water while scrubbing. Disassemble instruments with multiple parts and brush in the grooves, teeth, and joints. Rinse thoroughly with clean running water.

Question 70

(a) Explain why cleaning is a crucial step in processing instruments. (2 marks)

Sample Answer:

Cleaning is crucial because if it has not been done, sterilization and high-level disinfection (HLD) may not be effective. Micro-organisms trapped in organic material may be protected and survive, and organic material can make chemicals used in sterilization and HLD less effective.

Question 71

(a) Explain why detergents are important for effective cleaning. (2 marks)

Sample Answer:

Detergents are important because water alone does not remove proteins, oils, or grease. Detergents break and suspend oil and grease, making them easy to remove.

Question 72

(a) Explain why hand soap should not be used for cleaning instruments. (2 marks)

Sample Answer:

Hand soap should not be used because fatty acids in the soap will react with minerals in hard water, leaving a residue that is difficult to remove.

Question 73

(a) Explain why steel wool or abrasive cleaners should be avoided when cleaning instruments. (2 marks)

Sample Answer:

Steel wool or abrasive cleaners should be avoided because they scratch or pit metal, creating grooves that can collect micro-organisms and increase corrosion.

Question 74

(a) Define "sterilization" and explain its purpose. (4 marks)

Sample Answer:

Sterilization is the process that eliminates all micro-organisms, including bacterial endospores. Its purpose is to ensure that instruments and other items that come into contact with the bloodstream or tissues under the skin are free from all living organisms.

Question 75

(a) List three methods of sterilization. (3 marks)

Sample Answer:

  1. Steam under pressure (autoclaving).
  2. Dry heat.
  3. Chemicals.

Question 76

(a) Explain why boiling and flaming are not effective sterilization techniques. (2 marks)

Sample Answer:

Boiling and flaming are not effective sterilization techniques because they do not effectively kill all micro-organisms, especially bacterial endospores.

Question 77

(a) List three factors that affect the effectiveness of sterilization. (3 marks)

Sample Answer:

  1. The amount and type of micro-organisms.
  2. Absence of organic material or other matter such as dirt.
  3. The potential the item has to hide or conceal micro-organisms.

Question 78

(a) Explain why sterilization is preferable to high-level disinfection for instruments that contact the bloodstream and tissues. (2 marks)

Sample Answer:

Sterilization is preferable because it kills all micro-organisms, including bacterial endospores, whereas high-level disinfection may not reliably kill all bacterial endospores.

Question 79

(a) Describe the steps involved in steam sterilization or autoclaving. (11 marks)

Sample Answer:

  1. Decontaminate, clean, and dry all instruments.
  2. Open or unlock all joined instruments.
  3. Disassemble scissors with sliding or multiple parts.
  4. Wrap instruments in two layers of paper, cotton, or muslin fabric.
  5. Arrange all packs, drums, or unwrapped items in the chamber to allow steam circulation.
  6. Follow the manufacturer's instructions, but generally sterilize wrapped items for 30 minutes and unwrapped items for 20 minutes at 121°C (250°F) and 106 Kpa (15 Ib/in2) pressure.
  7. If automatic, the heat will shut off, and the pressure will fall. If not, turn off the heat or remove the autoclave from the heat source.
  8. Wait until the pressure gauge reads "zero" to open the autoclave.
  9. Open the lid or door to allow steam to escape. Leave instrument packs or items in the autoclave until they dry completely.
  10. Remove the packs, drums, or unwrapped items using sterile pickups.
  11. Place packs or drums on a padded surface until they are cool to prevent condensation.

Question 80

(a) Explain what "shelf life" is in the context of wrapped sterile items and list the factors that affect it. (6 marks)

Sample Answer:

Shelf life is the length of time a wrapped sterile item is considered sterile. Factors affecting shelf life include the type of packaging material used, cleanliness of the store, humidity in the store, temperature of the storage area, whether packs are stored on open or closed shelves, and the use of dust-proof covers.

Question 81

(a) Describe how to store unwrapped sterilized items. (2 marks)

Sample Answer:

Unwrapped items should be used immediately after removal from the autoclave. They can also be kept in a covered, sterile container for up to one week.

Question 82

(a) Describe the steps involved in dry heat sterilization using a hot-air oven. (7 marks)

Sample Answer:

  1. Decontamination: Clean and dry all instruments.
  2. Wrap the instruments in foil or double-layered cotton or muslin fabric.
  3. Put unwrapped instruments on a metal tray or shelf.
  4. Place instruments in the oven and heat to the designated temperature.
  5. Ensure the designated temperature is reached (e.g., 170°C for 1 hour, 160°C for 2 hours).
  6. Leave instruments in the oven to cool before removing.
  7. When cool, remove instruments using sterile pickups.

Question 83

(a) Explain when chemical sterilization is used and describe the process. (4 marks)

Sample Answer:

Chemical sterilization is used for instruments and other items that are heat-sensitive or when heat sterilization is unavailable. The process involves soaking items in a chemical solution, such as glutaraldehyde, followed by rinsing in sterile water.

Question 84

(a) Define "high-level disinfection" (HLD) and explain when it is suitable. (4 marks)

Sample Answer:

High-level disinfection (HLD) is the process that eliminates all micro-organisms, including bacteria, viruses, fungi, and parasites, but does not reliably kill all bacterial endospores. It is suitable for instruments and other items that will come into contact with broken skin or intact mucous membranes.

Question 85

(a) Describe the steps involved in high-level disinfection by boiling. (6 marks)

Sample Answer:

  1. Decontaminate and clean all instruments and other items.
  2. Completely submerge all instruments and other items in the water.
  3. Cover the pot or close the lid of the boiler and bring the water to a gentle, rolling boil.
  4. When the water comes to a rolling boil, start timing for 20 minutes.
  5. Lower the heat to keep the water at a gentle rolling boil.
  6. After 20 minutes, remove instruments using dry, high-level disinfected cheatle forceps.

Question 86

(a) Describe the process of high-level disinfection using chemicals. (4 marks)

Sample Answer:

For high-level disinfection using chemicals:

  • Instruments and other items must be completely submerged in chemical solution.
  • All hinged instruments must be disassembled.
  • Soak for 20 minutes from the time the instruments are added.
  • Do not add or remove anything once the timing begins.
  • Rinse all instruments thoroughly with sterile water before use.

Question 87

(a) Define "housekeeping" in the context of a healthcare facility. (2 marks)

Sample Answer:

Housekeeping refers to the general cleaning of baths, sinks, wash basins, beds, tables, floors, walls, and other service areas in a healthcare facility.

Question 88

(a) Explain the role of a healthcare worker in ensuring a clean environment. (2 marks)

Sample Answer:

A healthcare worker is responsible for ensuring that a clean environment is achieved by removing visible dust and dirt routinely with water and detergent, and supervising those charged with cleaning responsibilities.

Question 89

(a) Describe the importance of cleaning and maintenance in microbiological control of the healthcare facility environment. (4 marks)

Sample Answer:

Cleaning and maintenance prevent the buildup of soil, dust, or other foreign material that can harbor pathogens and support their growth. Cleaning reduces or eliminates the reservoirs of potential pathogenic micro-organisms.

Question 90

(a) Differentiate between the cleaning routine for low-risk and high-risk areas in a healthcare facility. (6 marks)

Sample Answer:

Low-risk areas (e.g., walls, ceilings, floors) should be cleaned with water and detergent using a damp cloth. High-risk areas (e.g., sinks, toilets, latrines) require a disinfectant cleaning solution. Toilets and latrines should be cleaned daily or as required with a separate cloth or brush.

Question 91

(a) Describe the types of cleaning solutions used in a health facility and their applications. (6 marks)

Sample Answer:

Types of cleaning solutions include:

  • Plain detergent and water: Used for low-risk areas and general cleaning.
  • 0.5% sodium hypochlorite solution: Used to kill or inactivate micro-organisms during cleaning and to decontaminate areas.
  • Disinfectant/detergent cleaning solution: Contains a disinfectant, detergent, and water; used for cleaning areas contaminated with infectious materials.

Question 92

(a) Define the three types of waste found in healthcare facilities. (6 marks)

Sample Answer:

The three types of waste are:

  • General waste: Non-hazardous waste that poses no risk of injury or infection.
  • Medical waste: Material generated in the diagnosis, treatment, or immunization of clients, including blood, body fluids, organic waste, and sharps.
  • Hazardous chemical waste: Chemical waste that is potentially toxic or poisonous.

Question 93

(a) Explain the importance of proper waste disposal in healthcare settings. (5 marks)

Sample Answer:

Proper waste disposal is important to minimize the spread of infections, reduce the risk of accidental injury, help provide an aesthetically pleasing atmosphere, reduce odors, reduce the chances of attracting insects and animals, and reduce the likelihood of contaminating the soil or groundwater.

Question 94

(a) Describe the four aspects of medical waste management. (8 marks)

Sample Answer:

The four aspects of medical waste management are:

  • Sorting: Separating waste at the point of generation.
  • Handling: Minimizing contact with waste before disposal.
  • Interim storage: Storing waste safely before final disposal.
  • Disposal: Using appropriate methods to eliminate waste.

Question 95

(a) Explain the process of "waste segregation" and its importance. (4 marks)

Sample Answer:

Waste segregation is the process of sorting waste at the point at which it is generated. It is important because it can greatly reduce the amount that needs special handling, thus reducing risks. Ensure that separate containers are used for disposing of general and medical waste. Use coloured plastic containers/bags, painted drums or labels that can be easily read to help distinguish between general and medical containers. For example, use red containers/plastic bags for medical waste.

Question 96

(a) Describe the guidelines for handling medical waste. (5 marks)

Sample Answer:

Guidelines for handling medical waste include:

  • Handle medical waste as little as possible.
  • Empty waste containers when they become three-quarters full.
  • Do not collect medical waste by emptying it into open carts or wheelbarrows.
  • Always wear heavy utility gloves and shoes.
  • Always wash your hands after handling waste and removing gloves.

Question 97

(a) Describe the guidelines for interim storage of medical waste. (4 marks)

Sample Answer:

Guidelines for interim storage include:

  • Place waste in an area that is minimally accessible.
  • Make sure all containers have lids.
  • Cover the containers to prevent insects, rodents, and other animals from getting into them.
  • Plan for only short-term storage (usually for several hours, but no more than one or two days).

Question 98

(a) Describe the preferred method for disposing of medical waste and explain why. (4 marks)

Sample Answer:

The preferred method is burning, preferably in an incinerator or oil drum, because high temperatures destroy micro-organisms and reduce the volume of waste.

Question 99

(a) Describe the guidelines for the disposal of sharps. (5 marks)

Sample Answer:

Sharps should be disposed of in puncture-resistant containers. Close the containers when they are three-quarters full. Sharps can be rendered harmless by placing them in a metal container, adding fuel, igniting it, and letting it burn until the fire goes out. The resulting solid block can then be buried.

Question 100

(a) Describe the guidelines for the disposal of liquid medical waste. (5 marks)

Sample Answer:

Carefully pour liquid waste down a sink, drain, flushable toilet, or latrine. Rinse the area thoroughly with water. Decontaminate the container that held the liquid waste by filling it with a 0.5% chlorine solution and soaking it for 10 minutes before washing. Always wear gloves and shoes when handling liquid medical waste.

Question 101

(a) Describe the guidelines for the disposal of hazardous chemical waste. (4 marks)

Sample Answer:

Cleaning solutions and disinfectants should be handled as described for liquid medical waste. Rinse containers thoroughly with water. Do not reuse plastic containers. Always wear gloves and shoes when handling hazardous chemical waste.

Question 102

(a) Explain the purpose of transmission-based precautions and list three types. (5 marks)

Sample Answer:

Transmission-based precautions are used in addition to standard precautions for patients who are known or suspected to be infected or colonized with highly transmissible or epidemiologically important pathogens. The three types are contact precautions, droplet precautions, and airborne precautions.

Question 103

(a) Describe the guidelines for contact precautions. (4 marks)

Sample Answer:

Guidelines for contact precautions include:

  • Use non-sterile, clean, disposable gloves, gown, or apron.
  • Use disposable or dedicated reusable equipment.
  • Limit patient contact with non-infected persons.
  • Place the patient in a single room or cohort with similar patients.

Question 104

(a) Describe the guidelines for droplet precautions. (4 marks)

Sample Answer:

Guidelines for droplet precautions include:

  • Use a surgical/medical mask.
  • Maintain a distance of at least 1 meter between the infectious patient and others.
  • Place the patient in a single room or cohort with similar patients.
  • Limit patient movement.

Question 105

(a) Describe the guidelines for airborne precautions. (4 marks)

Sample Answer:

Guidelines for airborne precautions include:

  • Use a particulate respirator/N95 mask.
  • Place the patient in an adequately ventilated room.
  • Limit patient movement.
  • Use airborne precautions during aerosol-generating procedures.

Question 106

(a) Define "barrier nursing" and explain its purpose. (4 marks)

Sample Answer:

Barrier nursing is an intervention in nursing practice that aims at preventing cross-infection of communicable diseases and the spread of an infectious agent from an infected or colonized patient to susceptible persons. The purpose is to erect a barrier to the passage of infectious pathogenic organisms between the contagious patient and other patients and staff in the hospital.

Question 107

(a) List five diseases that would warrant barrier nursing. (5 marks)

Sample Answer:

  1. Open pulmonary tuberculosis.
  2. Typhoid.
  3. Amoebic and bacillary dysentery.
  4. Gastrointestinal anthrax.
  5. Hepatitis A.

Question 108

(a) List five considerations that must be taken into account before deciding to apply barrier nursing to a patient. (5 marks)

Sample Answer:

  1. Patient's clinical condition (e.g., mental health status).
  2. Mode of transmission of the infection.
  3. Availability of supplies.
  4. Susceptibility of others to the infection.
  5. Evidence-based practice, e.g., laboratory report.

Question 109

(a) Explain the importance of involving the patient and significant others in decision-making regarding barrier nursing. (2 marks)

Sample Answer:

Sample Answer:

Involving the patient and significant others in decision-making ensures their cooperation and support, making the process smoother and more effective.

Question 110

(a) Describe the standards of care in barrier nursing related to handwashing. (4 marks)

Sample Answer:

Hands should be washed thoroughly with soap and running water or alcohol hand rub before and after contact with the patient to prevent the transfer of micro-organisms. Handwashing should also be done after contact with body fluids.

Question 111

(a) Describe the standards of care in barrier nursing related to patient direct care. (5 marks)

Sample Answer:

Standards of care related to patient direct care include assessing the risk of transmitting infection, limiting the patient's movement, using personal toiletries (or individual bedpans with proper disinfection), emphasizing handwashing after using the toilet and before meals, and using disposable gloves, goggles, and aprons when in contact with blood, body fluids, and secretions.

Question 112

(a) Describe the standards of care in barrier nursing related to protective clothing. (3 marks)

Sample Answer:

Protective clothing such as gloves, masks, goggles, aprons, boots, and caps should be worn by all healthcare workers who provide care to patients undergoing barrier nursing. Supportive staff and laboratory staff handling contaminated materials should also wear protective clothing.

Question 113

(a) Describe the standards of care in barrier nursing related to equipment and linen. (3 marks)

Sample Answer:

Equipment should be decontaminated and cleaned after use. Used linen should be disinfected before laundering. Protective gloves should be used when handling used equipment and linen.

Question 114

(a) Describe the standards of care in barrier nursing related to the safe handling of used sharps. (5 marks)

Sample Answer:

Standards of care related to sharps include:

  • Do not disassemble needles from syringes or other devices; discard as a single unit.
  • Do not recap needles.
  • Do not carry used sharps by hand or pass them to another person.
  • Discard sharps immediately after use into a sharps container.
  • Ensure sharps containers are placed at the point of use and securely closed when three-quarters full.

Question 115

(a) Describe the standards of care in barrier nursing related to the environment. (2 marks)

Sample Answer:

The environment should be kept clean and free from dust. Spills of body fluids should be disinfected with a recommended disinfectant before cleaning. Color-coded, leak-proof waste containers and liners should be used to segregate generated wastes.

Question 116

(a) Describe the standards of care in barrier nursing related to food and visitors. (3 marks)

Sample Answer:

The patient should be provided with appropriate and properly cooked food. The number of visitors should be limited to the most significant persons. Visitors should use protective clothing and practice handwashing before and after visiting.

Question 117

(a) Explain the importance of health education for staff, patients, and family members in the context of barrier nursing. (4 marks)

Sample Answer:

Health education is crucial to ensure that everyone understands the procedures, standards, and reasons for barrier nursing. This helps reduce anxiety and depression in patients, ensures cooperation, and promotes a safer environment.

Question 118

(a) Describe the standard layout of a barrier area or room. (4 marks)

Sample Answer:

A barrier area should have a demarcated area with curtains or screens or a cubicle with a notice indicating "KEEP OFF" or "BARRIER NURSING AREA." There should be reasonable space, color-coded bins and liners for waste segregation, and running tap water nearby (or improvised running water for handwashing).

Question 119

(a) List the items required for barrier nursing for one patient. (7 marks)

Sample Answer:

Items required include:

  • Gowns (2)
  • Apron (2)
  • Caps (2)
  • Masks (disposable)
  • Boot or over-boots (2 pairs)
  • Examination gloves (2 pairs)
  • Protective eyewear (2)

Question 120

(a) Describe the specifications for gowns used in barrier nursing. (4 marks)

Sample Answer:

Gowns should be made from cotton cloth, cotton blend, or disposable fabric, open at the back with ties at the neck, waist, and middle of the back, have ribbed or elasticized cuffs (or thumb hooks), and be long enough to reach the knees.
