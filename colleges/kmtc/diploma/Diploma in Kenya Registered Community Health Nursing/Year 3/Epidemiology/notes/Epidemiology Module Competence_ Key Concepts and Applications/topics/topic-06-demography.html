üåç Definitions & Concepts

Demography is the scientific study of human populations.

üìå

Formal Demography

The scientific study of human populations with respect to their:

  • Size: Number of persons.
  • Distribution: Arrangement in space (geographic, residential).
  • Structure: Distribution by sex and age.
  • Change: Growth or decline (births, deaths, migration).

Key Demographic Terms

Net Migration
The combined effect of immigration (in-coming) and emigration (out-going).
Population Pyramid
A graphical illustration that shows the distribution of various age groups in a population.
Replacement Level Fertility
The level of fertility at which a population exactly replaces itself from one generation to the next.
Urbanization
The increase in the urban population and growth of cities.

‚ûó Population Change

There are four ways in which the number of people in an area can change. This relationship is captured in the Balancing Equation:

P_{t+1} = P_t + (B - D) + (I - E)

Where:

  • Pt = Population at initial time
  • B = Births (Natural Increase component)
  • D = Deaths (Natural Increase component)
  • I = Immigration (Net Migration component)
  • E = Emigration (Net Migration component)
üí°

Growth Components

Natural Increase = Births - Deaths

Net Migration = Immigration - Emigration

Total Growth = Natural Increase + Net Migration

üîÑ The Demographic Cycle

World history suggests that every nation passes through a demographic cycle consisting of 5 stages.

Stage Characteristics Balance Type
1. High Stationary High Birth Rate + High Death Rate. Population remains low/stable. Old Balance
2. Early Expanding Death rate begins to decline while Birth rate remains high. Population starts to grow. Imbalance
3. Late Expanding Death rate declines further; Birth rate tends to fall. Population grows but slows down. Imbalance
4. Low Stationary Low Birth Rate + Low Death Rate. Population stabilizes. New Balance
5. Declining Birth rate falls lower than Death rate. Population starts declining. Negative Growth

üìä Common Rates & Ratios

‚ñ∂ Fertility Measures
  • Crude Birth Rate: Annual number of live births per 1,000 people.
  • General Fertility Rate: Annual number of live births per 1,000 women of childbearing age (15-49).
  • Total Fertility Rate: Number of live births per woman completing her reproductive life.
‚ñ∂ Mortality Measures
  • Crude Death Rate: Annual number of deaths per 1,000 people.
  • Infant Mortality Rate: Annual number of deaths of children < 1 year per 1,000 live births.
  • Life Expectancy: Number of years an individual at a given age could expect to live.
