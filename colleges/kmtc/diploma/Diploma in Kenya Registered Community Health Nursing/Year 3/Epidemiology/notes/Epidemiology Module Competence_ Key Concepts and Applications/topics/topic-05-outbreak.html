üîé Overview

One of the first actions that results from a surveillance case report or report of a cluster is investigation. This may be as limited as a phone call or as extensive as a coordinated field investigation.

‚ö†Ô∏è

Key Definitions

  • Epidemic/Outbreak: The occurrence of more cases of disease than expected in a given area or among a specific group over a particular period.
  • Cluster: An aggregation of cases in a given area over a particular period without regard to whether the number is more than expected.

Purposes of Investigation

  • 1
    Prevent Transmission: Stop the spread from cases to contacts.
  • 2
    Identify Source: Find the reservoir (environment, food, person).
  • 3
    Identify Other Cases: Uncover unreported cases to gauge the full scope.
  • 4
    Conduct Surveillance: Collect data to identify trends and formulate policy.
02

ü¶∂ Steps in an Outbreak Investigation

A systematic approach is essential for a successful investigation. The following 10 steps are generally recognized:

  1. 1
    Prepare for Field Work: Gather supplies, review literature, consult experts.
  2. 2
    Establish Existence of Outbreak: Compare observed cases to expected numbers. Rule out "false" epidemics (e.g., reporting changes).
  3. 3
    Verify Diagnosis: Review clinical findings and laboratory results.
  4. 4
    Define and Identify Cases: Create a standard case definition (clinical criteria, time, place, person) and find cases.
  5. 5
    Perform Descriptive Epidemiology: Characterize the outbreak by Time, Place, and Person.
  6. 6
    Develop Hypotheses: Formulate ideas about the source, mode of transmission, and exposures.
  7. 7
    Evaluate Hypotheses: Compare hypotheses with established facts. Use analytic studies (Cohort or Case-Control).
  8. 8
    Refine Hypotheses: If necessary, conduct additional studies.
  9. 9
    Implement Control Measures: The primary goal! Destroy contaminated food, isolate carriers, vaccinate, etc.
  10. 10
    Communicate Findings: Write a report, brief health officials, and recommend future prevention strategies.

üìà The Epidemic Curve

The "epi curve" serves as a map of an epidemic. It provides a simple visual display of the outbreak's magnitude and time trend.

üîç Click to zoom

Fig. 5.1: The Epidemic Curve

What It Tells You

  • Time Course: Where you are in the epidemic (beginning, peak, end).
  • Outbreak Type: Helps distinguish between:
    • Common Source (Point source, Continuous, Intermittent)
    • Propagated (Person-to-person)
  • Probable Exposure Period: Can be deduced if the incubation period is known.

üè• Case & Contact Management

Case Management

‚ñ∂ 1. Verify Diagnosis

Establish the etiologic agent. Use laboratory tests or make the best guess if data is unavailable.

‚ñ∂ 2. Determine Infectivity

Is the case still infectious? Use estimation (onset dates + known infectious period) or verification (lab tests).

‚ñ∂ 3. Minimize Infectivity

Use antimicrobials/drugs to shorten the infectious period. Work with physicians to ensure appropriate treatment.

‚ñ∂ 4. Prevention Practices

Disinfect materials, use isolation, encourage hygiene (hand washing, covering coughs), restrict activities (e.g., food handlers).

Contact Management

  • ‚óè

    Determine Risk: Assess frequency, duration, and dosage of exposure. Is the contact susceptible?

  • ‚óè

    Check for Illness: Is the contact already ill? If so, manage as a case.

  • ‚óè

    Prevent Illness: Use vaccines (if timely), immune globulins, or chemoprophylaxis (antimicrobials).

  • ‚óè

    Surveillance: Monitor contacts for symptoms (active or passive surveillance) or quarantine if necessary.
