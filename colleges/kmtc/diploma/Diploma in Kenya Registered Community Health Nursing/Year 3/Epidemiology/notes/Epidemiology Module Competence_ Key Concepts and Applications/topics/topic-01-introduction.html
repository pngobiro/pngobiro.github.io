üìñ Introduction & Definitions

Epidemiology, literally meaning "the study of what is upon the people", is derived from Greek epi, meaning "upon, among", demos, meaning "people, district", and logos, meaning "study".

üìå

Formal Definition

Epidemiology is defined as the study of distribution and determinants of health related states in populations and use of this study to address health related problems.

The term is now widely applied to cover the description and causation of not only epidemic diseases, but of diseases in general, and even many non-disease health-related conditions, such as high blood pressure and obesity.

Key Components of the Definition

  • Study: Epidemiology is a scientific discipline with sound methods of scientific inquiry at its foundation. It is data driven and relies on a systematic and unbiased approach to collection, analysis, and interpretation of data.
  • Distribution: Epidemiology is concerned with the frequency and pattern of health events in a population.
  • Determinants: Any factor, whether event, characteristic, or other definable entity, that brings about a change in a health condition.
  • Specified Populations: The epidemiologist is concerned about the collective health of the community or population, unlike the clinician who is concerned with the individual.

üìê The Epidemiologic Triad

The term 'epidemiologic triad' is used to describe the intersection (common ground) of Host, Agent, and Environment in analyzing an outbreak.

üîç Click to zoom

Fig. 1.1: The Epidemiologic Triad (Agent, Host, Environment)

Components of the Triad

Component Description
Host The organism (human or animal) that harbors the disease. Factors include genetics, age, sex, immune status, and behavior.
Agent The cause of the disease. Can be biological (bacteria, virus), chemical (poison, alcohol), or physical (trauma, radiation).
Environment External factors that allow transmission. Includes physical (climate), biological (vectors), and social (sanitation) surroundings.

üî§ Key Terms in Epidemiology

Infection

The invasion of a living organism, the host, by another living organism ‚Äì the agent.

Infectivity

A measure of the ability of a disease agent to establish itself in the host. Quantified by ID (Infective Dose).

Virulence

A measure of the severity of a disease caused by a specified agent. Often quantified by LD_{50} (Lethal Dose for 50% of the population).

Epidemic

New cases of a certain disease, in a given human population, and during a given period, substantially exceed what is expected based on recent experience.

Endemic

Diseases that "reside within" a population (constant presence).

Pandemic

An epidemic occurring on a scale which crosses international boundaries, usually affecting a large number of people.

üìú Historical Evolution

The Greek physician Hippocrates, known as the father of medicine, is the first person known to have examined the relationships between the occurrence of disease and environmental influences. He coined the terms endemic and epidemic.

Timeline of Epidemiology

  • ‚óè

    Mid-1800s: Epidemiological methods began to be applied in the investigation of disease occurrence (e.g., John Snow and Cholera).

  • ‚óè

    1930s-1940s: Epidemiologists extended methods to non-infectious diseases.

  • ‚óè

    Post-WWII: Explosion in development of research methods. Studies like Doll and Hill (lung cancer & smoking) and the Framingham Heart Study.

  • ‚óè

    1960s-1970s: Eradication of smallpox worldwide.

  • ‚óè

    1980s: Extended to injuries and violence.

  • ‚óè

    1990s: Molecular and genetic epidemiology took root.

‚öôÔ∏è Core Epidemiologic Functions

In the mid-1980s, five major tasks of epidemiology in public health practice were identified. A sixth task was recently added.

  1. 1
    Public Health Surveillance: The ongoing systematic collection, analysis, interpretation, and dissemination of health data to help guide public health decision making.
  2. 2
    Field Investigation: One of the first actions that results from a surveillance case report. Can range from a phone call to a massive coordinated effort (e.g., locating sexual contacts, identifying a food source).
  3. 3
    Analytic Studies: Used when surveillance and field investigations are not sufficient. Employ rigorous methods (Design, Conduct, Analysis, Interpretation) to evaluate hypotheses.
  4. 4
    Evaluation: Determining the relevance, effectiveness, efficiency, and impact of activities with respect to established goals.
  5. 5
    Linkages: Field epidemiology is a "team sport". Epidemiologists participate as members of multidisciplinary teams (laboratorians, sanitarians, nurses, etc.).
  6. 6
    Policy Development: Epidemiologists provide input, testimony, and recommendations regarding disease control strategies, regulations, and health policy.

üî¨ Epidemiological Approaches

Epidemiologists employ a range of study designs generally categorized as:

üí°

The Basic Approach

In very simple terms, an epidemiologist:

  1. Counts: Cases or health events.
  2. Divides: The number of cases by appropriate denominator to calculate rates.
  3. Compares: These rates over time or for different groups of people.

Types of Studies

  • Descriptive Epidemiology: Pertains to the "who, what, where and when". Characterizes health events by time, place, and person.
  • Analytic Epidemiology: Deals more with the "how" and "why". Uses comparison groups to test hypotheses about causal relationships.
  • Experimental Epidemiology: The epidemiologist controls the factors (e.g., Randomized Control Trials, Community Trials).
