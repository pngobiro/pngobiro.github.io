üìä Overview of Measures

Epidemiology relies on specific measures to quantify the frequency and distribution of diseases. The two primary measures are Incidence and Prevalence.

üí°

Key Distinction

Incidence counts NEW cases (events).

Prevalence counts EXISTING cases (status).

02

üìà Incidence

Incidence quantifies the development of disease. It is a measure of the risk of developing some new condition within a specified period of time.

1. Incidence Proportion (Cumulative Incidence)

The number of new cases within a specified period divided by the size of the population initially at risk.

\text{Incidence Proportion} = \frac{\text{New cases within time period} \times 1000}{\text{Population initially at risk}}
üìù

Example

If a population initially contains 1,000 non-diseased persons and 28 develop a condition over two years:

\frac{28}{1000} \times 100 = 2.8\%

2. Incidence Rate (Incidence Density)

The number of new cases per population at risk in a given time period. Often used when the denominator is the sum of person-time.

Principles of Incidence

  • Incidence is a fundamental tool for studying the etiologic factors (causes) of both acute and chronic illness.
  • It provides a direct measure of the rate at which individuals become ill, thus providing a basis for statements about risk.
  • The denominator must only include the "at risk" population (excluding those who already have the disease or are immune).
03

üì∑ Prevalence

Prevalence is the total number of cases of a disease in the population at any given time. It is like a "snapshot" of the disease in the population.

Relationship to Incidence and Duration

Prevalence is directly related to the duration of the disease.

\text{Prevalence} \approx \text{Incidence} \times \text{Average Duration}

Types of Prevalence

‚ñ∂ Point Prevalence

The prevalence of a condition at a specific point in time (e.g., "Do you have the flu right now?").

\text{Point Prevalence} = \frac{\text{Number of existing cases at a point in time} \times 1000}{\text{Total study population}}
‚ñ∂ Period Prevalence

The total number of cases known to have existed at some time during a specified period (e.g., "Have you had the flu in the last 12 months?").

It includes cases that existed at the start + new cases during the period.

\text{Period Prevalence} = \frac{\text{Existing cases within a period} \times 1000}{\text{Average study population}}
‚ñ∂ Lifetime Prevalence

The total number of individuals that have had a condition at any point in their life.

Factors Affecting Prevalence

Prolongation of life (without cure) High case-fatality (death)

04

üìâ Attack Rates

Attack rates are cumulative incidence rates used in epidemics (e.g., food poisoning). They refer to the number of persons ill from a specific disease in a limited time period in a specific group.

1. General Attack Rate

\text{Attack Rate} = \frac{\text{Number of cases in exposed population} \times 100}{\text{Total population exposed}}

2. Food Specific Attack Rate

Used to pinpoint the source of food poisoning.

\text{Food Specific AR} = \frac{\text{Number who ate food and became ill} \times 100}{\text{Total number who ate the food}}

3. Secondary Attack Rate

Measures the spread of infection within a family or household following exposure to a primary case.

\text{Secondary AR} = \frac{\text{New cases among contacts} \times 100}{\text{Total number of susceptible contacts}}
05

‚úÖ Accuracy & Validity

Screening tests sort out apparently well persons who probably have the disease from those who probably do not. A screening test is not diagnostic.

Validity has two components: Sensitivity and Specificity.

2x2 Table: True Disease Status vs. Test Result

Screening Result Diseased (True Positive) Not Diseased (True Negative)
Positive Test True Positive (TP) False Positive (FP)
Negative Test False Negative (FN) True Negative (TN)

Formulas

\text{Sensitivity} = \frac{TP}{TP + FN} \times 100

The ability of a test to correctly identify those who HAVE the disease.

\text{Specificity} = \frac{TN}{TN + FP} \times 100

The ability of a test to correctly identify those who DO NOT have the disease.
