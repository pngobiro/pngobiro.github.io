3.0 Process of Infection

Six requirements for the successful invasion of the host by an infectious agent include:

  1. Condition in the environment must be favourable to the agent or the agent must be able to adapt in the environment
  2. Suitable reservoirs must be present
  3. A susceptible host must be present
  4. A satisfactory portal of entry into the host
  5. Accessible portal of exit from the host
  6. Appropriate means of dissemination and transmission to a new host

Determinants of Disease

Factors or events that are capable of bringing about change in health (PBSCB) i.e physical, biological, social, cultural and behavioural factors that influence health.

Examples:

  • Specific biologic agents (e.g., bacteria, viruses, protozoa).
  • Chemical agents that may act as carcinogens
  • Stress or adverse lifestyle patterns (lack of exercise, or tobacco consumption or diet high in saturated fat)

Determinants of disease can be intrinsic or extrinsic.

‚ñ∂ Intrinsic Determinants

What are the intrinsic determinants of disease? (e.g., genetics, age, sex, physiological state)

‚ñ∂ Extrinsic Determinants
  1. Physical: mechanical injuries/trauma, thermal, radiation, noise, psychological stress, light, etc.
  2. Chemical: organic/inorganic forms, gases
  3. Nutritional: metabolic, primary/secondary, nutritional deficiencies, hormonal imbalances, etc.
  4. Biological: all living organisms that cause disease or infection - bacteria, virus, rickettsia, protozoa, fungi, etc. Genetic defects are included here

Dynamics of Disease Transmission

  • Disease results from the interaction of the host (a person), the agent and the environment
  • Many underlying principles govern transmission of diseases
  • Human susceptibility is determined by genetic, nutritional and immunological characteristics

Chain of Transmission

It is a process that begins when (1) an infectious agent or pathogen (2) leaves its reservoir, source, or host through (3) a portal of exit, (4) is conveyed by some mode of transmission, (5) enters the host through an appropriate portal of entry, and (6) infects a susceptible host. The now-infected susceptible host becomes a new reservoir and the whole process starts over.

The concept of a chain of infection is essential to our understanding of why we do what we do to prevent infection. If any link of the chain of infection can be broken, the spread of infection can be prevented.

Concept of Immunity

Herd Immunity

  • Is the resistance of a group to an attack by a disease to which the large proportion of the members of the group are immune
  • Is the protective effect that arises from a high proportion of the population have had a disease (active natural immunization) or have been immunized (active artificial immunization)
  • Occurs when spread of disease in a community reaches a certain proportion of people who are immune in that the likelihood of encountering a susceptible individual to whom the disease can be transmitted is minimal
üìã

Conditions for Herd Immunity

  1. Disease agent must be restricted to a single host species within which transmission occurs
  2. Transmission must be relatively direct from one member of the host species to another
  3. Infections must induce solid immunity
  4. Probability of an infected person encountering every other individual in the population remains the same (constant random mixing of the population)

Host-Parasite Relationships

Many microorganisms may benefit from mankind by developing a range of host-organism inter-relationship including:

  • Symbiosis: Cooperative association between two dissimilar organisms beneficial to both
  • Commensalism: Association between two dissimilar organisms living together benefiting one without harming the other
  • Parasitism: Relation between two dissimilar organisms living together benefiting the parasite but harming the host
  • Saprophytism: Relation where organisms live on dead tissues.

Factors Determining Host-Microorganisms Relationships

‚ñ∂ 1. Infectious Agent Factors
  • Mode of entry: Ingestion (external route), Inoculation (parenteral), Inhalation (respiratory) and Direct contact (contagious infection)
  • Spread of infection: through phagocytic cells, blood vessels, lymphatics
  • Production of toxin: exotoxins and endotoxins
  • Virulence of organisms: adhesions, penetration and colonization, toxins, antimicrobial resistance and host factors
  • Products of organisms: e.g. enzymes
‚ñ∂ 2. Host Factors
  • Physical barrier e.g. skin
  • Chemical barrier ‚Äì mucous secretions, gastric acid
  • Effective drainage in the respiratory tract, gastrointestinal tract, urinary and genital system
  • Immune defence mechanisms (immunity) - non-specific (innate) and specific or adaptive immune mechanisms (humoral ‚Äì antibodies; cellular T and B cells)

Natural History of Disease

Every disease in a host follows a potentially predictable life cycle from onset to final outcome (natural history). Understanding this is vital to clinicians for treatment and public health professionals for prevention strategies.

The 4 Stages

  • 1

    Stage of Susceptibility: Disease has not yet developed, but risk factors are present.

  • 2

    Stage of Pre-symptomatic Disease: Disease process has begun, but no overt signs or symptoms (e.g., incubation period).

  • 3

    Stage of Clinical Disease: Signs and symptoms are evident; pathological alteration in structure/function.

  • 4

    Stage of Diminished Capacity: Recovery, disability, death, or chronicity.

Epidemiological Triad

Amount of disease in a population results from interaction of the 3 components (Host, Agent, Environment). In a state of equilibrium, they are balanced.

üîç Click to zoom

Diagram 2.3: Epidemiologic Triad

‚ñ∂ The Host
  • The individual human being whose state is determined by the interaction of genetics and environmental factors
  • Disease only occurs in a host who is susceptible (lack of susceptibility is due to immunity or inherent factors)
  • Examples: age, sex, ethnic groups, nutritional status, socio-economic, personal behaviours, etc.
‚ñ∂ The Agent
  • This is the factor whose presence or absence causes a disease
  • Examples - biological agents, chemical agents, nutritive element and physical agents
  • Variations: Phenotypic differences, Genotypic differences, Drug resistance (e.g., HIV subtypes)
‚ñ∂ Environment
  • Physical: weather, climate, geology
  • Biological: sources of food, water and air, presence of vectors, flora and fauna
  • Socioeconomic and cultural: density, crowding, adequate housing, war, sanitation, availability of health care, social norms

Levels of Disease Prevention

Disease prevention applies to measures taken to prevent diseases before they occur as well as measures taken to prevent disease progression.

Classification of Diseases

  • 1) Communicable Diseases: Transmissible from one person, or animal, to another. Illness arises from transmission of an infectious agent or its toxic product.
  • 2) Non-Communicable Diseases: Chronic medical conditions or diseases which are non-infectious (NCDs). Currently responsible for over 60% of global deaths.
12.0

Disease Outbreak

A disease outbreak is the occurrence of a number of cases of a disease that is larger than expected for a given time and place.

10 Steps for Investigation of Disease Outbreak

  1. 1. Prepare to investigate: Identify team, review literature, notify entities, determine immediate measures.
  2. 2. Verify the diagnosis and confirm outbreak: Laboratory confirmation, collect specimens.
  3. 3. Case definition: Establish standard criteria (confirm, probable, suspect). Dynamic.
  4. 4. Case finding: Conduct systematic search, create line list.
  5. 5. Perform descriptive epidemiology: Tabulate/orient data by person, place, time. Epidemic Curve.
  6. 6. Hypothesis generation: The how and the why. Compare with known sources.
  7. 7. Evaluate hypothesis: Statistics, Cohort/Case-control studies.
  8. 8. Additional environmental studies: Collect food/water samples.
  9. 9. Implement control/prevention measure: Coordinate stakeholders, develop strategies.
  10. 10. Communicate findings: Disseminate report, educate community.
