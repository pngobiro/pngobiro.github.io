1.0

Introduction

Sampling is the process or technique of selecting a sample of appropriate characteristics and adequate size.

Definitions

  • Population: Defined by its nature (an individual, housing, a firm etc.), its intrinsic characteristics (gender, housing type, industries), and its localisation (city, neighbourhood etc.). Essentially: who, where and when.
  • Sampling unit: The basic unit around which a sampling procedure is planned e.g., a person, group (household, school, district, etc.) or a component (e.g. eye, physiological response).
  • Sampling frame: List of all of the sampling units in a population.
  • Sample: Collection of sampling units from the eligible population.

Sampling Methods

Sampling methods are broadly categorized into two types:

1. Random (Probability) Sampling 2. Non-random Sampling
a) Simple random sample a) Convenience sample
b) Stratified random sample b) Systematic sample (can be both)
c) Cluster sample c) Consecutive sample
d) Adaptive cluster sample d) Quota sample
e) Multistage sample e) Volunteer sample
f) Capture-recapture
2.1

Probability Sampling Methods

1) Simple Random Sampling

A simple random sample is one in which each of the possible samples of elements taken from a population of elements has the same probability of selection.

In a simple random sample without replacement, any element selected in a sample cannot be selected again for the same sample.

Advantages Disadvantages
Simple process and easy to understand Not most efficient method (not most precise estimate for the cost)
Easy calculation of means and variance Requires knowledge of the complete sampling frame
Sampling error easily measured Cannot always be certain that there is an equal chance of selection
Non respondents or refusals

2) Systematic Sampling

Sampling units are spaced regularly throughout the sampling frame (e.g., every 3rd unit). Not a probability sample unless the starting point is randomly selected.

üî¢

Procedure

  • 1

    Arrange the units in some kind of sequence

  • 2

    Divide total sampling population by the designated sample size (e.g. 1200/60 = 20)

  • 3

    Choose a random starting point (e.g., random number between 1 and 20)

  • 4

    Select units at regular intervals (e.g., every 20th unit: 4th, 24th, 44th...)

Advantages: Sampling frame does not need to be defined in advance; Easier to implement in the field; Ensures coverage of the spectrum of units.

Disadvantages: Variance cannot be estimated unless assumptions are made.

3) Stratified Random Sampling

The sampling frame comprises groups, or strata, with certain characteristics (e.g., age-group, urban/rural). A sample of units is selected from each group or stratum. Used for a population with distinct subgroups.

‚ñ∂ Pros & Cons: Stratified Sampling
Advantages Disadvantages
Assures that certain subgroups are represented Loss of precision if small number of units is sampled from strata
Allows estimation of parameters in different strata Sampling error is difficult to measure
More precise estimates (strata are homogeneous) Different strata can be difficult to identify
Strata of interest can be sampled most intensively Administrative complexity

4) Cluster Sampling

Clusters of sampling units (e.g., neighbourhoods, schools) are first selected randomly. Individual sampling units are then selected from within each cluster. A type of multi-stage sampling where all units at the lower level are included in the sample.

Advantages: Entire sampling frame need not be enumerated in advance; More economical.

Disadvantages: Loss of precision (wider variance), but can be accounted for with larger number of clusters.

üí°

Example: Dental Caries in School Children

  1. Among schools, list all classrooms.
  2. Take a simple random sample of classrooms (clusters).
  3. Examine all children in the selected classrooms.

5) Multistage Sampling

Similar to cluster sampling except that there are two sampling events. Primary units are randomly selected, then individual units within primary units are randomly selected.

Example: Select classrooms (primary units), then select a random sample of children within those classrooms (secondary units).

2.2

Non-Probability Sampling Methods

1) Convenience (Accidental, Haphazard) Sampling

A non-random collection of sampling units from an undefined sampling frame. Investigator decides who is enrolled.

  • Advantages: Convenient, easy to perform.
  • Disadvantages: Not statistical justification; Biased.
  • Examples: Case series at a hospital, students walking down a hall.

2) Consecutive Sampling

A case series of ALL patients with the condition of interest within a hospital or clinic, not just those the investigators happen to know about.

  • Advantages: Removes investigator bias in selection; Straightforward enrollment.
  • Disadvantage: Non-random.

3) Snowball Sampling

The researcher asks the respondents to give referrals to other possible respondents. Used for hard-to-reach populations (e.g., drug addicts, homeless people).

4) Purposive Sampling (Judgemental/Selective)

Sample with a purpose in mind. Focus on particular characteristics of a population that are of interest.

Types: Maximum variation, homogeneous, typical case, extreme (deviant) case, critical case, expert, total population.

5) Quota Sampling

Researcher selects respondents according to some fixed quota (representative group).
