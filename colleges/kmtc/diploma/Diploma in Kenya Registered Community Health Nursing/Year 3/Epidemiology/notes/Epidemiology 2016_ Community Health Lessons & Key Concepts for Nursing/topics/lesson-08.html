1.0

Introduction

Disease surveillance is an information-based activity involving the collection, analysis and interpretation of large volumes of data originating from a variety of sources.

Definitions

  • WHO: The ongoing, systematic collection of data related to health events; their verification, analysis, interpretation, and the dissemination of information to those who need to know in order to reduce morbidity and mortality & to improve health.
  • CDC: Ongoing systematic collection, analysis, and interpretation of health data essential to the planning, implementation, & evaluation of public health practice, closely integrated with the timely dissemination of the data to those who need to know.

Epidemiology in Emergencies

In emergencies, epidemiology has three elements:

‚ñ∂ i) Descriptive Epidemiology

Determines the distribution of a disease among displaced populations. Describes the health problem, its frequency, who, where, and when.

Examples: Monitoring health status to detect cholera cases; Conducting nutritional surveys.

‚ñ∂ ii) Analytical Epidemiology

Compares those who are ill with those who are not in order to identify the risk of disease or protective factors. Examines causes (e.g. environmental and behavioural factors).

Example: Investigating an outbreak of an unknown disease in a displaced population settlement.

‚ñ∂ iii) Evaluation Epidemiology

Examines the relevance, effectiveness and impact of different programme activities in relation to the health of the affected populations.

Example: Evaluating a malaria control programme.

2.0

Purpose & Sources

Purpose of Surveillance

  1. Morbidity and mortality reporting
  2. Monitoring disease trends
  3. Describing natural history of diseases
  4. Documenting of distribution and spread of disease
  5. Establish long term trends in disease occurrence
  6. Detect epidemics, outbreaks or new syndromes
  7. Identify high risk groups or areas
  8. Facilitating planning of control and disease prevention
  9. Evaluation of intervention measures
  10. Resource allocation and planning

Sources of Information

  • Health facilities
  • Death and birth registers
  • Laboratories
  • The community self
  • Special search e.g. Chickenpox
  • Investigation of Outbreaks
  • Surveys
4.0

Type of Information

The Person

Demographic characteristics (gender, age, race/ethnicity, occupation, SES, etc.) reveal disease trends.

Place

Best to characterize cases by place of exposure rather than by place of report. Data by geographic location can be presented in a table or map.

Time

Compare number of cases reported in time period of interest (weeks, months, years) to historical periods. Preferable to use date of onset rather than date of report to avoid delays.

5.0

Process

  1. Detect priority diseases
  2. Plan for surveillance
  3. Collect and compile information (tally, maps, investigate)
  4. Analyse and interpret data
  5. Supervise and give feedback
  6. Monitoring and Evaluation
6.0

Surveillance Systems

6.1 Criteria of Evaluating Surveillance Systems

Evaluated according to attributes such as: simplicity, flexibility, acceptability, sensitivity, positive predictive value, representativeness, timeliness, and resources.

6.2 Components of Surveillance Systems

1. Defining Target Events/Diseases

Prioritized based on burden, fatality, control potential, etc.

Objectives: Control (reduce morbidity/mortality), Elimination (prevent outbreaks), Eradication (subtract agent from earth).

Immediate: Polio, measles, cholera, food poisoning.

6.4 Types of Surveillance

‚ñ∂ 1. Passive Surveillance
  • Collection of data from existing unsolicited reports.
  • Health authorities do not stimulate reporting.
  • Common, less costly, but data often incomplete.
  • Good for conditions with clear symptomatology (e.g., measles).
‚ñ∂ 2. Active Surveillance
  • Active finding of cases (calling facilities, visiting hospitals).
  • More time/resource intensive but more complete.
  • Used for outbreaks or keeping close track of cases.
‚ñ∂ 3. Sentinel Surveillance
  • Selects a small group of health workers/sites to gather data.
  • Provides detailed, accurate data from selected sites.
  • Quicker and less costly than universal active surveillance.
‚ñ∂ 4. Syndromic Surveillance
  • Analysis of non-specific data (syndromes) to detect trends.
  • Sources: ER visits, pharmacy sales, school absenteeism.
‚ñ∂ 5. Outbreak Surveillance
  • Establish source/cause for quick control.
  • Provides info on vaccine efficacy, attack rates.

6.5 Epidemiological Indicators

  • Quantitative: Calculated from numbers (Incidence, Prevalence, Mortality rate).
  • Qualitative: Measure attitudes, knowledge, awareness (e.g., awareness of immunization value).

Programme Outcome Indicators: Access, Coverage, Quality, Availability.

7.0+

Data, Uses & Challenges

7.0 Data Presentation

Line graphs (time), Maps (place), Histograms/Charts (distributions), Spot maps, Tables.

8.0 Uses of Surveillance Data

  • Priority setting and planning
  • Prediction and early detection of epidemics
  • Monitoring and evaluation of interventions
  • Identify high risk groups
  • Trigger disease control effort

9.0 Challenges & 10.0 Problems

  • Failure to report on time / Incomplete reporting
  • Inadequate data analysis
  • Poor feedback to health workers
  • Underutilization of information
  • Lack of training / Understaffing
  • Complexity of forms
