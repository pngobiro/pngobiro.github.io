Objectives

At the end of the lesson the learner will be able to:

  1. Describe different types of epidemiological studies/designs
  2. State advantages and disadvantages of each study design
1.0

Introduction

Epidemiological information is collected by conducting a study in the community. Since it is difficult to collect information from all members of the community, there is a need to sample the population.

Qualitative Designs

Are complementary to quantitative designs and important in the study of social determinants of health problems. Methods include focus group discussions (FGDs), interviews, surveys, self-reports, observations, and document analysis. Sampling is mainly purposive.

Quantitative Designs

Goal is to understand the frequency and causes of health-related phenomena by seeking causes through describing associations between exposures (causes) and outcomes. Methods include observational, experimental, and mixed.

2.0

Choosing a Design

Factors to consider when choosing a study design:

  1. Purpose of the study: Hypothesis-testing versus hypothesis generating; Finding signal versus quantifying the signal.
  2. Available resources
  3. Need for data collection
  4. Choice of outcome
  5. Ability to draw valid causal inference
3.0

Study Design Classification

Epidemiological studies are broadly grouped as experimental and non-experimental (observational).

  • Observational studies are further sub-classified as descriptive and analytical.
  • Analytical designs can be case-controlled or cohort.
  • Descriptive designs can be cross-sectional, correlational, and case series.
  • Experimental studies can be clinical or community trials (randomized or non-randomized).

4.0

Observational (Non-Experimental) Designs

4.1 Descriptive Studies

Provide information on patterns of disease occurrence. Descriptive statistics generated can be correlated with clinical observations or laboratory studies to generate hypotheses. Three critical dimensions are person, place, and time.

Advantages Disadvantages
Uses routinely collected data; less expensive Usually cannot test epidemiologic hypotheses
Good for assessing patterns/prevalence Lacks comparison group
Useful for formulation of hypotheses Cannot discern temporal relationships; not useful for rare events

4.1.1 Correlational Studies

Measure of association: correlation coefficient (r), ranging from -1 to 1. Uses include suggesting disease causation, describing social attributes, and surveillance.

Example: Death rates from coronary artery disease correlate with per capita cigarette sales.

  • Pros: Quick, inexpensive, uses available data.
  • Cons: Ecological fallacy (aggregate data doesn't represent individuals), no individual risk factor info.

4.1.2 Case Reports and Case Series

A case report discusses a patient's diagnosis/outcome. A case series analyses a series of patients with common characteristics. Useful for describing new interventions or unusual diagnoses.

  • Pros: Paint national perspective, useful for hypothesis formulation, recognize new diseases (e.g. initial AIDS reports).
  • Cons: No comparison group, cannot test valid statistical association, presence of risk factor may be coincidental.

4.1.3 Cross-Sectional Designs (Surveys)

Observational study of a defined population at a single point in time. Determines prevalence (existing cases) rather than incidence (new cases).

  • Pros: Convenient, quick, inexpensive, can generate hypotheses, useful for planning.
  • Cons: Cannot establish causality (temporal relationship unclear), identifies prevalent not incident cases, survivor bias, not suitable for rare conditions.

4.2 Analytic Designs

Focus on searching for underlying causes to uncover the source and mode of spread. Designed to test hypotheses generated from descriptive studies.

4.2.1 Longitudinal Cohort Studies

Observational. Goal is to observe outcomes as they occur. Identifies a group initially free of outcome, but varying in exposure. Followed over time.

Types:

  • Retrospective (Historical): Identified from past records, followed to present. Quick but relies on records.
  • Prospective: Assembled in present, followed into future. Accurate but expensive/lengthy.

Bias in Cohort Studies

  • Selection Bias: Participants selected based on characteristics affecting outcome.
  • Information Bias: Different quality of info from exposed vs non-exposed.
  • Misclassification Bias: Error in classifying exposure or disease status.

4.2.2 Case-Controlled Studies

Identify participants based on disease/outcome status (Cases) and compare with those without (Controls) to look for past exposure. Typically retrospective.

5.0

Experimental Designs

A population is selected for a planned trial of a regimen. Distinguished by manipulation of exposure and randomization. Provides stronger evidence of effect (outcome) compared to observational designs.

5.1 Community Trials

Experiments involving communities as a whole (e.g., fluoridation of water). Two similar communities selected; one receives intervention, one serves as control.

Steps in conducting community trials:

  1. Development of a protocol
  2. Community selection and recruitment
  3. Establishment of baseline and surveillance
  4. Intervention selection and assignment
  5. Oversight and data monitoring
  6. Evaluation

5.2 Clinical Trials

Evaluates effects of therapeutic interventions in groups of human subjects. Individual within a group is studied.

Randomized Clinical Trials (RCTs)

Gold standard for inferring causality. Subjects assigned by formal chance mechanism (randomization) to intervention or control.

Key Features:

  • Randomization: Ensures comparable groups, controls confounders.
  • Blinding/Masking: Prevents bias (Single or Double-blind).
  • Analysis: Intention-to-treat.

Non-Randomized Studies

Participants are not assigned by chance; they may choose their group or be assigned by researchers. Quasi-experimental.
