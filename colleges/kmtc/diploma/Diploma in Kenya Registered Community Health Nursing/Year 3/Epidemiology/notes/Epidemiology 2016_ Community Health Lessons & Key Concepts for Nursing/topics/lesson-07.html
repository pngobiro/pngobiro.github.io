1.0

Introduction

There are different kinds of testing in medicine:

  1. Diagnostic: Specifically looking for a suspected condition which is tested for and confirmed or excluded.
  2. Case-finding: Usually in an investigation of exposed people, to sort the exposed and ill from the exposed and well (E.g., test people who were in contact with a case of tuberculosis).
  3. Screening: Usually no specific exposure or indication that the individual has disease (E.g. routine PSA testing in middle-aged males).
2.0

Screening

Defined as a relatively quick means of detecting potential diseases before it has become manifest. Screening does not establish a diagnosis but helps to identify individuals who appear to have a given disease.

  • Valuable where early identification leads to more effective treatment and better prognosis.
  • Can take place at different stages in the natural history of disease.
  • Represents primary or secondary prevention.
üí°

Examples

Screening for breast cancer (mammography), cervical cancer (Pap smear), hypertension, diabetes, tuberculosis, HIV infection, etc.

3.0

Reasons for Screening

  1. Identify risk factors for disease
  2. Reduce morbidity and mortality
  3. Improved quality of life
4.0

Considerations for Screening

‚ñ∂ 1. Nature of the Disease
  • Significant public health problem
  • Detectable before symptoms develop
  • Important enough to the public
  • High prevalence in pre-symptomatic stage
‚ñ∂ 2. Nature of the Screening Test
  • Accurate (valid and precise)
  • Quick and simple
  • Safe and acceptable
  • Relatively inexpensive
‚ñ∂ 3. Nature of Follow-up & Treatment
  • Follow-up tests must be available to confirm diagnosis.
  • Treatment must be available, accessible, and more effective if initiated early.
5.0

Types of Screening

  1. Mass Screening: Aimed at large population groups (e.g., visual impairment in schools).
  2. Selective (Targeted) Screening: Applied only to high-risk groups (e.g., TB in prisons, lead in inner-city children).
  3. Multiphase Screening: Employs multiple tests at once to detect multiple conditions.
  4. Case Finding (Opportunistic): Occurs in clinical settings when patients visit for unrelated problems.
6.0

Principles for Screening Programs

  1. Condition should be an important health problem
  2. There should be a recognizable early or latent stage
  3. There should be an accepted treatment
  4. The screening test is valid, reliable, with acceptable yield
  5. The test should be acceptable to the population
  6. Cost should be economically balanced
7.0

Characteristics of a Good Screening Test

7.1 Validity (Accuracy)

Defined as the ability to distinguish between who has a disease and who does not.

Sensitivity

Ability to correctly identify those who have the disease (True Positives).

$$ \text{Sensitivity} = \frac{\text{TP}}{\text{TP} + \text{FN}} = \frac{a}{a+c} $$

Specificity

Ability to correctly identify those who do not have the disease (True Negatives).

$$ \text{Specificity} = \frac{\text{TN}}{\text{TN} + \text{FP}} = \frac{d}{b+d} $$
‚öñÔ∏è

Trade-off

Increasing sensitivity often decreases specificity (more false positives). Increasing specificity often decreases sensitivity (more false negatives).

Predictive Value

Depends on the prevalence of disease.

Positive Predictive Value (PPV): Probability that a person with a positive test actually has the disease.

$$ \text{PPV} = \frac{\text{TP}}{\text{TP} + \text{FP}} $$

Negative Predictive Value (NPV): Probability that a person with a negative test truly does not have the disease.

$$ \text{NPV} = \frac{\text{TN}}{\text{TN} + \text{FN}} $$
üßÆ

Example: Mammography

Data: TP=132, FP=985, FN=47, TN=62,295

  • Sensitivity = 132 / (132 + 47) = 73.7%
  • Specificity = 62,295 / (985 + 62,295) = 98.4%
  • PPV = 132 / (132 + 985) = 11.8%
  • NPV = 62,295 / (47 + 62,295) = 99.9%

7.2 Reliability (Precision)

Ability of a test to give consistent results when performed more than once on the same individual. Can be improved by standardizing methods and training observers.

7.3 Yield

The amount of previously unrecognized disease diagnosed. Depends on sensitivity, prevalence, frequency, and participation.

7.4 Efficacy

Benefits of screening among those who use it compared to those who don't.

8.0

Bias in Screening

  • Referral/Volunteer Bias: Volunteers are often more health-conscious and have better prognosis.
  • Length Bias: Screening selectively identifies slow-progressing cases (better prognosis), missing rapid cases.
  • Lead Time Bias: Survival looks better because disease is found earlier, not because death is delayed.
  • Over-diagnosis Bias: Detecting pseudo-disease or early cases that would never have caused symptoms.
