üïØÔ∏è Understanding Grief & Loss

Grief is a subjective state of emotional, physical, and social response to a valued entity. Mourning is the period of grief characterized by sadness, guilt, anger, helplessness, and despair.

Types of Loss

  • Real Loss: Loss of a loved one, property, or relationship shared by others.
  • Perceived Loss: Felt by the individual alone (e.g., loss of femininity following mastectomy).

Types of Grief

Anticipatory Grief
Begins before the actual loss (e.g., diagnosis of terminal illness). It can increase in intensity as loss becomes imminent.

Anniversary Reactions
Occurs following reminders (holidays, special days). Can last days or weeks; not necessarily a setback.

Complicated/Traumatic Grief
Chronic, disabling, and intense. May progress to major depression or PTSD symptoms.

üìâ Stages of Grief

General Stages

  1. Shock & Denial: Numbed disbelief; emotional protection.
  2. Pain & Guilt: Excruciating pain, remorse, chaotic feelings.
  3. Anger & Bargaining: Lashing out ("Why me?"), bargaining with higher powers.
  4. Depression & Reflection: Sad reflection, isolation, realizing true magnitude of loss.
  5. The Upward Turn: Physical symptoms lessen; life becomes calmer.
  6. Reconstruction: Seeking realistic solutions, working on practical problems.
  7. Acceptance & Hope: Dealing with reality; planning for the future.

Kubler-Ross Model (DABDA)

‚ñ∂ The 5 Stages
  1. Denial: "This can't be happening." Conscious or unconscious refusal to accept facts.
  2. Anger: "Why me? It's not fair!" Misplaced rage and envy.
  3. Bargaining: "I'll do anything for a few more years." Attempting to postpone death/loss.
  4. Depression: "What's the point?" Preparatory grief; disconnecting from love objects.
  5. Acceptance: "It's going to be okay." Coming to terms with mortality.

Maladaptive Responses

Type Characteristics
Delayed/Inhibited Absence of grief when expected. Fixed in denial.
Prolonged Normal activities not resumed after 4-6 weeks (acute phase). Associated with self-blame.
Exaggerated Overwhelming behavior; fixed in anger/depression. Somatic complaints.

üõå Care of the Dying

Rights of the Dying Person

‚ñ∂ View Bill of Rights
  • Right to be treated as a living human being until death.
  • Right to maintain a sense of hopefulness.
  • Right to express feelings and emotions about approaching death.
  • Right to participate in decisions concerning care.
  • Right not to die alone.
  • Right to be free from pain.
  • Right to have questions answered honestly (not deceived).
  • Right to die in peace and dignity.
  • Right to retain individuality and not be judged.
  • Right to discuss religious/spiritual experiences.
  • Right to expect sanctity of the human body after death.

Nursing Interventions

  • Examine Own Feelings: Nurses must examine their own feelings about death to give maximum help.
  • Dignity: Treat patient with respect regardless of condition.
  • Presence: Nurses are often the most important link with life; provide physical comfort and emotional support.
  • Support: Nurses also need support systems to manage the emotional stress of caring for the dying.

üéóÔ∏è Palliative & Hospice Care

Palliative Care
Comprehensive person and family-centered care when disease is not responsive to treatment.

Hospice Care
Care concerned with end of life, focusing on realistic social, emotional, spiritual, and financial preparation for death. (Usually last 6 months).

Principles of Hospice Care

  • ‚úì Death must be accepted.
  • ‚úì Interdisciplinary team management.
  • ‚úì Pain and symptom management is priority.
  • ‚úì Patient and family viewed as a single unit of care.
  • ‚úì Home care is necessary/encouraged.
  • ‚úì Bereavement care provided to family.

Symptom Management

1. Pain

Poor management affects all aspects of well-being. Patients on analgesics should continue them; silence does not mean absence of pain.

Use alternative routes (rectal, sublingual) if swallowing is difficult. Manage constipation (opioid side effect).

2. Dyspnoea

Assess intensity, lung sounds, edema, and sputum. Treat underlying cause if possible.

3. Nutrition (Anorexia & Cachexia)

Tips for promotion:

  • Offer small portions of favorite foods.
  • Don't stress about "balanced" diet.
  • Cool foods may be better tolerated.
  • Avoid arguments at meal times.
  • Allow patient to refuse food/fluids.
