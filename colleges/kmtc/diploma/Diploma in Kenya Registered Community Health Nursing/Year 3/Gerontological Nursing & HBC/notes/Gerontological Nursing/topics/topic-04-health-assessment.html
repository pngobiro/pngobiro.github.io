Integumentary Conditions

ü¶† Basal Cell Carcinoma

Common in older persons who have spent a lot of time in the sun. Note any pigmented area that is enlarging and elevated. It bleeds easily when touched.

  • Decubitus Ulcers: Caused by shrinkage of subcutaneous tissue that normally cushions skin over bony prominences. High risk for bed-ridden or wheelchair-bound elderly.
  • Hypothermia: Occurs due to decreased subcutaneous tissue/fat. Extra care needed to avoid exposure to cold extremes.

Systemic Conditions

‚ñ∂ Musculoskeletal & Respiratory

Musculoskeletal: Osteoporosis, Degenerative joint disease, Rheumatoid arthritis, Bursitis.

Respiratory: COPD (emphysema, asthma, bronchitis), Influenza, Pneumonia (including aspiration), Tuberculosis, Lung cancer.

‚ñ∂ Cardiovascular & Hematopoietic

CVS: Coronary artery/valve disease, Heart block, CCF, Peripheral vascular disease, Occlusive disorders (thrombosis, phlebitis), Aneurysm, Hypertension.

Hematopoietic: Increased plasma viscosity (vascular occlusion risk), Decreased RBC production (anemia risk).

‚ñ∂ Sensory & Endocrine

Endocrine: Diabetes Mellitus, Hypoglycemia, Hypothyroidism.

üìã History Taking

Guidelines for Interviewing

üí° Environment Preparation
  • Ensure comfort (empty bladder, lenses/dentures in situ).
  • Room should be well lit with no background noise.
  • Ensure privacy and ask client preference regarding presence of relatives.

Components of History

  1. 1
    Reasons for contact.
  2. 2
    Demographic data.
  3. 3
    Source and reliability of the history (verify with significant others if mentally impaired).
  4. 4
    Present and past health status (medical, obstetric, psychiatric).
  5. 5
    Family and Social history.

Height Loss: Shortening due to intervertebral space narrowing.

Weight: Males lose weight at 55; Women gain at 50, then lose in mid-70s. Fat redistributes from extremities to trunk.

System-Specific Assessment

Abdomen
Easier to palpate organs due to muscle wasting. Liver/kidneys decrease in size. Check for masses or distended bladder. Rectal exam confirms findings.

Genito-Urinary
Female: Thin vulva, atrophy. Check for irritation, ulceration, prolapse (uterine/bladder), and cervical cancer.
Male: Sparse gray pubic hair. Decreased penis/testis size. Prostate palpated per rectum (check for hypertrophy/cancer).

Neurological
Motor: Observe bulk, tone, strength, gait, balance.
Reflexes: Diminished, sluggish, or absent.
Sensory: Diminished tactile, deep pain, and temperature sensation.

Key Investigations

  • Hematological: RBCs, WBCs, serum chemistry (compare with young adult norms).
  • Fluids: Urine, Sputum, Stool tests.
  • Mental Status: Cognitive (memory, reasoning) and Emotional adaptation.
  • Functional: Capacity to perform ADLs.
