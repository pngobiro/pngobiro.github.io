üî¨ Biological Theories of Aging

Biological theories attempt to explain the physical changes of aging. No single biological theory completely explains aging; rather, a combination of factors is likely responsible.

A. Genetic Theory (Programmed Theory)

Proposed by Hayflick (1960). Key concepts include:

  • Predetermined Lifespan: The lifespan of animals is pre-determined by a genetic programme.
  • Biologic Clock: Humans have a maximum lifespan of approximately 110 years.
  • Hayflick Limit: Normal human cells divide about 50 times, after which they stop dividing, shrink, and die.

B. Mutation Theory

States that aging is a result of the mutation of somatic cells or alteration of the DNA repair mechanism.

In effect, aging is caused by an assortment of genetic diseases, each of which has adverse symptoms only at advanced ages.

C. Wear and Tear Theories

This theory posits that organs have a limited or fixed amount of energy available and will wear out on a scheduled basis. The body is likened to a machine that works well for a specified period but eventually fails.

Aggravating Factors:

This process is accelerated by harmful stress factors such as:

  • Smoking
  • Poor diet
  • Muscular strain
  • Large intake of alcohol

Evidence: Microscopic signs of wear and tear can be observed in striated skeletal muscle, heart muscles, and nerve cells.

D. Immunity Theory

Based on the knowledge that the immune system, particularly the thymus and immuno-competent cells of the bone marrow, is affected by the aging process.

  • Decreased Defense: Older people have fewer defense mechanisms against foreign organisms, making them more susceptible to diseases (e.g., cancers, infections).
  • Auto-immune Response: The body reacts against itself, producing antibodies against its own cells, food, or foreign bodies.

This may explain the development of autoimmune diseases like lupus, diabetes, and rheumatoid arthritis.

E. Cross-linkage Theory (Glycosylation Theory)

Attributes aging to chemical changes where proteins, structural molecules, and DNA develop detrimental chemical bonds (cross-links) to each other.

As cross-linked proteins accumulate, they damage cells and tissues, resulting in increased tissue stiffness and slowing down bodily processes.

F. Free Radical Theory

Proposed by Harman (1956). It suggests that aging is caused by increased levels of highly unstable molecules called free radicals.

‚ñ∂ Mechanism of Action
  • Free radicals are produced by metabolism, radiation, and pollutants.
  • They disrupt or damage normal cells and genetic material.
  • Damage to cell membranes leads to accumulated cell damage and eventual organ failure.
  • Causes buildup of fatty pigment granules called lipofuscin (age spots).
üõ°Ô∏è Protection

Vitamins E, C, and serotonin (anti-oxidants) inhibit the production of free radicals and may delay aging.

G. Other Biological Theories

These propose that hormonal changes, neurotransmitter levels, or viruses are responsible for aging changes.

üß† Psychosocial Theories of Aging

These theories do not explain why aging occurs but attempt to explain why older adults have different responses to the aging process.

A. Disengagement Theory

Explains why aging persons separate from the mainstream of society.

  • Proposes that older people are systematically separated from society because they are perceived as not benefiting society.
  • Suggests that elderly persons desire to withdraw, making the disengagement mutually beneficial.

B. Activity Theory

Proposes that activity is necessary for successful aging.

  • Active participation in physical and mental activity helps maintain functioning.
  • Purposeful activities and interactions lead to improved self-esteem and life satisfaction.

C. Continuity Theory

States that an individual's characteristic coping strategies are in place long before old age.

The best way to predict how a person will adjust to old age is to examine how they have adjusted to other changes throughout their life.

D. Sub-culture Theory

Proposed by Rose (1962‚Äì1965). States that old people form a sub-culture with their own norms, expectations, beliefs, and habits.

  • They become less integrated into general society and more integrated among themselves.
  • This improves the self-image of older people and reduces the negative cultural view of aging.

E. Age Stratification Theory

Based on the following beliefs:

  • Individuals pass through society in cohorts of age (social, biological, psychological).
  • New cohorts are continuously being born with a unique sense of history.
  • Society itself is continuously changing, as are the people and roles in each strata.
  • There is a dynamic interplay between individual aging and change.

F. Personal Environment Fit Theory

Proposed by Lawton (1985). States that aging is a result of the interrelationship between personal competence and the environment.

Personal competence includes:

  • Ego strength
  • Motor skills
  • Biological health
  • Cognitive capacity
  • Sensory perception
