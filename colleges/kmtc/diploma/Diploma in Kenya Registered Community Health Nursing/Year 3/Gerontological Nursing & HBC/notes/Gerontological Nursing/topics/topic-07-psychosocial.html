Retirement represents a major adjustment in aging, often involving the loss of occupational identity, status, income, and daily structure.

Phases of Retirement

‚ñ∂ 1. Remote & Near Phases
  • Remote Phase: Early anticipation of future retirement; little rational preparation.
  • Near Phase: Reality sets in; preparation for leaving job begins; fantasies regarding the retirement role develop.
‚ñ∂ 2. Honeymoon & Disenchantment
  • Honeymoon Phase: Euphoric period testing fantasies; attempting to do everything at once.
  • Disenchantment Phase: A letdown or depression as life stabilizes and unrealistic fantasies are not met.
‚ñ∂ 3. Re-orientation & Stability
  • Re-orientation Phase: Realistic choices and alternative sources of satisfaction are considered.
  • Stability Phase: An understanding of the retirement role is achieved.
  • Termination Phase: Role is lost due to resumption of work or dependency/illness.

Nurse's Role in Retirement

  • ‚úì
    Counseling: Assist in preparation and facing realities.
  • ‚úì
    Support: Help find new identity sources and facilitate re-orientation.
  • ‚úì
    Health Promotion: Encourage good habits (diet, avoiding alcohol/tobacco, regular exams) to maximize potential in old age.

üß† Cognition & Intelligence

Cognition deals with gaining, interpreting, and using information. Perception involves collecting and interpreting stimuli.

Type Description Aging Impact
Fluid Intelligence Ability to perform tasks or make judgments based on unfamiliar stimuli ("thinking on your feet"). Biologically mediated. Declines from adolescence onwards.
Crystallized Intelligence Ability based on knowledge and experience acquired over a lifetime (wisdom). Shows slight decline or remains stable with age.
‚ÑπÔ∏è Memory & Learning

Learning does not necessarily decline, but speed of processing may. Short-term memory generally wanes, while past memory is often preserved. The elderly may compensate for memory gaps using lifetime experience.

üó£Ô∏è Counseling & Support

Counseling Goals

Counseling helps the elderly prepare for life changes such as decreased morale, introversion, and memory loss. Strategies include:

  • Life Review: Reminiscing about past achievements.
  • Legacy Planning: Planning for the future.
  • Relationship Repair: Overcoming barriers like bitterness or anger.

Support Networks

Social networks provide a sense of belonging. There are four basic styles:

  1. 1
    Kinship Style: Network primarily made of relatives.
  2. 2
    Friendship Style: Network made of friends rather than relatives.
  3. 3
    Associate Style: Network related to groups (work, church).
  4. 4
    Restricted Style: Limited or non-existent network (needs encouragement to expand).

üõë Elder Abuse

‚ö†Ô∏è Definition

Elder abuse can involve physical violence, personal neglect, financial exploitation, violation of rights, denial of healthcare, or self-inflicted abuse.

Types of Abuse

  • Physical: Battering, hitting, shoving, denying food/medication.
  • Psychological: Threats, name-calling, isolation.
  • Exploitation: Misuse of property, forcing changes to wills/deeds.
  • Violation of Rights: Denying privacy, abandonment.

Risk Factors

Vulnerability Factors

  • Inability to meet physiological needs or manage finances.
  • Mental impairment (dementia) or depression.
  • Social isolation ("Invisibility").

Abuser Characteristics

  • Financial difficulties.
  • History of violence or substance abuse.
  • Caregiver stress or unemployment.
  • Intra-family conflicts.
