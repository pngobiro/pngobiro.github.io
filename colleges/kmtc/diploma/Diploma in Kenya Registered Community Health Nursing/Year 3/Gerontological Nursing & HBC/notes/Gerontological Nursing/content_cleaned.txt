<ul>
<li>Geriatrics: is the study of medical aspect of old age and application of the knowledge related to biological, biomedical, behavioral and social aspects of aging to the prevention and care of the elderly person.</li>
<li>Aging: total time related changes experienced by an individual which are biological, psychological, sociological and spiritual.</li>
<li>Ageism: a process of systematic stereotyping and discrimination against old people because they are old.</li>
<li>Gerontophobia: unreasonable fear and irrational hatred of older people</li>
<li>Age: the length of time that has passed since birth.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>Life expectancy: statistically determined number of years that a person at a particular age may expect to live.</li>
<li>Lifespan: longest period of life of an organism known from the study of it.</li>
<li>Biological clock: each cell is believed to be programmed through the DNA to self-destruct i.e. contain cells that are known to multiply and divide a fixed number of times and then self-destruct.</li>
<li>Aging pacemaker: pacemaker in the brain initiates a neurohormonal response possibly through serotonin, dopamine neurotransmitter or tryptophan that results in aging.</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="aging-theories">Aging theories<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#aging-theories" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="biological-theories">Biological theories<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#biological-theories" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>a) Genetic theory: Programmed theory of aging by Hayflic 1960. He says that:-</p>
<ul>
<li>Lifespan of animals is pre-determined by a genetic programme. <em>Biologic clock</em> of humans has a maximum of 110 years.</li>
<li>Normal human cells divide about 50 times after which they stop dividing and shrink and die.</li>
</ul>
<p>b) Mutation theory: states that aging is as a result of <em>mutation of somatic cells or alteration of DNA repair mechanism</em>. In effect, aging is caused by an assortment of genetic diseases, each of which has adverse symptoms only at advanced ages.</p>
<p>c) Wear and tear theories: says that organs have limited or fixed amount of energy available and <em>will wear out on a scheduled basis</em>. When enough cells wear out, the body does not function well. This process is made worse by:-</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Harmful stress factors e.g.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>smoking,</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Poor diet</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Muscular strain</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Large intake of alcohol</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Theory is supported by-:</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Microscopic signs of wear and tear in Striated skeletal muscle, heart muscles and nerve cells</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The body is likened to a machine that works well in a specified period but will eventually fail due to wear and tear.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>d) Immunity theory: it is based on knowledge that immune system particularly the thymus and the immuno-component cells of the bone marrow are affected by the aging process.</li>
<li>Older people have fewer defense mechanisms against foreign organisms. They are more susceptible to diseases e.g. cancers, infections.</li>
<li>Their body also has increased auto-immune responses, i.e. the body reacts against itself producing antibodies against its cells. It also develops antibodies against food, foreign bodies, etc.</li>
<li>This may explain development of autoimmune disease like lupus, diabetes, rheumatoid arthritis e.t.c</li>
</ul>
<p><img src="assets/images/image-20251226-cb5587a7.jpeg" alt="img-8.jpeg"></p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="e-cross-linkage-theory">e) Cross-linkage theory:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#e-cross-linkage-theory" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Also known as the glycosylation theory of aging.</li>
<li>This theory attributes aging to chemical changes that happen in the body.</li>
<li>These changes happen gradually as proteins, structural molecules, and DNA develops detrimental chemical bonds (also known as cross-links) to each other. When these cross-links between molecules occur, they lose basic levels of functionality.</li>
<li>As cross-linked proteins accumulate over time they will damage cells and tissues, resulting in increased tissue stiffness and slowing down processes within the body.</li>
</ul>
<p>f) Free radical theory by Harman 1956: protective mechanisms to free radicals decline with age. Due to force on poly-unsaturated fats in the cell, unstable free radicals are released and peri-oxidised resulting in destruction of cell structures. Vitamins E, C and serotonin (anti-oxidants) inhibit the production of free radicals and thus delay aging. With age when the free radical molecules attack other cells they damage the cell membranes leading to aging due to accumulated cell damage. Eventually this leads to organ failure or</p>
<ul>
<li>Free radical theory: it proposes that aging is caused by increased levels of highly unstable molecules called free radicals.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>These particles are produced by normal metabolism, exposure to radiation, pollutants or to other free radicals. Free radicals disrupt or damage normal cells and their genetic materials. Eventually this leads to aging and the death of cells. Free radicals cause building up of fatty pigment granules called lipofuscin resulting in age spots.</li>
</ul>
<p>g) Other biological theories: proposes that hormonal changes, neurotransmitters levels or viruses are responsible for the changes in aging. No biological theory completely explains aging</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="psychosocial-theories-of-aging">Psychosocial theories of aging<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#psychosocial-theories-of-aging" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>These don’t explain why aging occurs but rather attempt to explain why older adults have different responses to the aging process.</li>
</ul>
<p>a) Disengagement theory: it explains why aging persons separate from mainstream of society. It proposes that older people are systematically separated or disengaged from society because they do not benefit society as a whole. It further says that elderly persons desire to withdraw from society as they age, so that the disengagement is of mutual benefit.</p>
<p>b) Activity theory: it proposes that activity is necessary for successful aging. Active participation in both physical and mental activity helps in maintaining functioning into old age. Purposeful activities and interactions leads to improved self esteem and overall satisfaction with life even at an extended age.</p>
<p><img src="assets/images/image-20251226-cb5587a7.jpeg" alt="img-9.jpeg"></p>
<p>c) Continuity theory: it states that an individuals characteristic coping strategies are in place long before old age but personality features are also dynamic. Using this theory the best way to predict how a person will adjust to old age is to examine how the person has adjusted to other changes in life.</p>
<p>20</p>
<p>d) Sub-culture theory by Rose 1962 – 1965: it states that old people as a group have their own norms, expectation, beliefs and habits therefore they form a sub-culture. They are less integrated to the society and more integrated among themselves. This serves in improving self image of older people and reduces cultural negative view of aging.</p>
<p><img src="assets/images/image-20251226-cb5587a7.jpeg" alt="img-10.jpeg"></p>
<p>e) Age stratification theory: its based on beliefs that:-</p>
<ul>
<li>Individuals pass through the society in cohort of age, social, biological and psychological.</li>
<li>New cohorts are continuously being born with unique sense of history.</li>
<li>Society itself is continuously changing as are the people and their roles in each strata.</li>
<li>Society can be divided into various strata according to roles and age.</li>
<li>There is a dynamic interplay between individual aging and change.</li>
</ul>
<p>22</p>
<p>f) Personal environment fit theory by Lawton 1985: it states that aging is as a result of interrelationship between personal competence and environment which is a potential for eliciting behavior from individuals towards and includes:-</p>
<ul>
<li>Ego strength</li>
<li>Motor skills</li>
<li>Biological health</li>
<li>Cognitive capacity</li>
<li>Sensory perception</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="normal-physical-changes-that-occur-in-old-age">Normal physical changes that occur in old age<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#normal-physical-changes-that-occur-in-old-age" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="cardiovascular-changes">Cardiovascular changes<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#cardiovascular-changes" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>The heart: the heart muscles become rigid i.e. they lose elasticity and contract poorly. This affects the output of the heart especially when the person performs activities e.g. walking or any other exercise.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>There is a misplacement of the apex of the heart caused by the back of the old person being bent (kyphoscoliosis). If you place a stethoscope on the normal area you will miss the apex beat.</li>
<li>Murmurs: most of the aged persons have a diastolic heart murmur heard at the base of the heart. It is due to the hardening of the aortic valve.</li>
<li>Heart rate: does not change with age.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>Arterial circulation: Arteries become rigid increasing resistance to the blood flow. This causes a rise in blood pressure in the aged.</li>
<li>Venous circulation: this does not change with age unless the person is sick.</li>
<li>Blood pressure: significant increase in systolic, slight increase in diastolic, increase in peripheral resistance and pulse pressure.</li>
<li>Peripheral blood vessels: they are easy to palpate because of increased arterial wall narrowing and loss of connective tissues. They feel more tortuous and rigid. This causes the extremities of the old people to be cold especially at night and they also get discolored.</li>
<li>Other conditions associated with the heart and blood vessels in old age are; hypertension, congestive cardiac failure, myocardial infarction, arteriosclerosis, thrombo-phlebitis.</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="respiratory-system-changes">Respiratory system changes<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#respiratory-system-changes" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Pulmonary flow and diffusion: There is decreased blood flow leading to poor gaseous exchange in the lungs, thus decreased diffusion.</li>
<li>There is increased in residual lung volume</li>
<li>Reduced vital capacity and decreased cough reflex.</li>
<li>Anatomic structure: increased anterior-posterior diameter</li>
<li>Respiratory accessory muscles: degeneration and decrease strength; increased rigidity of the chest wall</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="integumentary-changes">Integumentary changes<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#integumentary-changes" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Texture: skin looses elasticity, wrinkles and becomes drier. It is cooler to touch and there is reduced sweating.</li>
<li>Fat distribution: there is less on the extremities but more on the trunk.</li>
<li>Hair color: white or grey in color. Hair distribution is thin on the scalp. Women may develop chin and lip hair</li>
<li>Nails: there is decreased growth rate</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="genito-urinary-system">Genito-urinary system<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#genito-urinary-system" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Kidney function: there is reduced glomerular filtration rate due to decreased cardiac output. There is loss of protein due to renal inefficiency.</li>
<li>Renal blood flow: due to decreased cardiac output, reduction in the filtration rate and renal efficiency. Subsequent loss of proteins from the kidneys may occur.</li>
</ul>
<p>29</p>
<ul>
<li>Micturition: men- the frequency may increase due to prostatic enlargement. Women: urgency and stress incontinence due to increased perineal muscle tone. Nocturia increases for both men and women. With advanced age incontinence increases.</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="reproductive-system">Reproductive system<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#reproductive-system" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Female: there is reduction in estrogen causing atrophy of the breasts, ovary, uterus, and epithelial lining of the vaginal wall. The vaginal canal narrows and shortens. vaginal secretions reduce and become more alkaline leading to increased rate of vaginitis.</li>
<li>Men: there is a decrease in the production of testosterone leading to decreased sexual drive and lowered fertility. Testes decrease in size, sperm count decreases; and viscosity of seminal fluid diminishes.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="musculoskeletal-changes">Musculoskeletal changes<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#musculoskeletal-changes" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Muscles: there is decreased muscle mass all over the body leading to reduced physical strength.</li>
<li>Bones: there is reduction in calcium and phosphorous causing the bones to be porous and weak. There is shortening in height due to intervertebral space narrowing.</li>
<li>Joints: the joints become less flexible and less mobile and there is reduced range of movements.</li>
</ul>
<p>33</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="digestive-system">Digestive system<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#digestive-system" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>

<li>Swallowing is more difficult as salivary secretions diminish and therefore old people prefer soft diet.</li>

<li>Sluggish peristalsis due to reduced gastrointestinal motility leading to constipation.</li>
<li>Esophagus: there is decreased esophageal peristalsis. Increased incidence of hiatus hernia with accompanying gases distention</li>

<ul>
<li>There is general decrease in the ability to respond to stimuli.</li>
<li>Reflexes are slowed.</li>
<li>There is decline in ability to learn new skills and experiences.</li>
<li>Older people forget easily. Preservation of memory of past experiences is good. Older people have difficulties making decisions. They have difficulties in remembering immediate or recent events.</li>

<li>There is decreased lacrimation leading to frequent infection of the eyes</li>
<li>There is inability to adapt to darkness resulting from presbyopia.</li>

<li>Sunken appearance of the eye due to loss of orbital fat.</li>

<p>Smell: there is some decline in sensitivity to usual smells.</p>

<p>Taste: there may be decrease in the number of taste buds on the tongue causing an increased complaints about food.</p>

<li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="abnormal-conditions-common-in-aging">Abnormal conditions common in aging<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#abnormal-conditions-common-in-aging" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="integumentary-conditions">Integumentary conditions<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#integumentary-conditions" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Basal cell carcinoma: common in older persons who have spent a lot of time in the sun. Note any pigmented area that is enlarging and is elevated from the skin. It bleeds easily when touched.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Decubitus ulcers: caused by shrinkage of subcutaneous tissue that normally cushions the skin over the bony prominences. The skin of the aged person has a greater risk of breakdown. Extra care should be taken of elderly people who are bed-ridden or confined to wheelchairs.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="distribution">Distribution<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#distribution" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>

<li>Aging causes</li>
<li>A decrease in total body mass</li>
<li>A decrease in total lean body mass</li>
<li>Decrease in body water</li>
<li>Increase in total body fat</li>
</ul>

<li>Water soluble drug e.g. gentamycin, remains in the blood stream longer with high concentration especially with dehydration.</li>

<li>There is reduced H.b and plasma protein in elderly person leading to decreased available sites for protein bound drugs such as warfarin.</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="personal-space">Personal space<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#personal-space" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Older persons need space of their own where they can get privacy and comfort. This can be a house, room or part of a room. In this space the elder person can keep treasures acquired in a lifetime.</li>
<li>If she/he is moved another space should be provided and the person should be allowed to hold, touch and enjoy his treasures.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="nutritional-needs">Nutritional needs<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#nutritional-needs" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Nutritional needs must be met to prevent the body from deteriorating further.</li>
<li>1 person needs at least 1 gm of protein per kg body weight added if the person is sick.</li>
<li>Old people may not experience hunger due to poor taste perception and slowed activity. Nutrients are absorbed slowly and since most people live alone they may fail to cook.</li>
<li>They should be encouraged to cook food rich in protein, carbohydrates, minerals, vitamins to meet body requirements and they should take adequate fluids.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>Food should be soft so that the old person can use his denture to chew. It should also be soft since sometimes they may have problem with swallowing.</li>
</ul>
<p>Elimination needs</p>
<ul>
<li>Old people are pre-occupied with their bowel functions and they require reassurance that daily bowel movements may not be necessary.</li>
</ul>
<p>Constipation</p>
<ul>
<li>Many old people require stimulation to keep their bowels functioning. Some may require laxative or stool softener but care should be taken that an old person does not use them daily and think that he/she cannot do without them.</li>
<li>Clients should be encouraged to exercise regularly, eat fruits and vegetables and drink plenty of water.</li>
</ul>
<p>Bladder and bowel incontinence</p>
<ul>
<li>The older person may have difficulty in controlling bladder and bowel function. To overcome this, the older person should be retrained to be following regular schedules.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>Retraining is usually welcome since it saves the person from embarrassment.</li>
<li>To start with trips to the bathroom should be short interval which should be gradually lengthened. Catheters should be avoided as they also introduce infection to the urinary tract.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="difficult-in-voiding">Difficult in voiding<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#difficult-in-voiding" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Retention of urine is common in the older people (men) due to enlargement of the prostate gland. Corrective surgery should be organized. Urine output also decreases due to reduction in the glomerular filtration rate.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="physical-hygiene-needs">Physical hygiene needs<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#physical-hygiene-needs" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Skin care: Skin is more delicate and requires better care to avoid infection or breakdown due to pressure. If the older person is incontinent of either urine, stool or both, care should be taken to keep him clean and dry.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Skin oil or lotion should be used to keep the skin soft and promote peripheral circulation.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The older person sweat and oil glands are less active so the person can take a bath less often and should use bath oil or mild soap.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Oral hygiene: Older person should be encouraged to take care of the mouth and take care of the dentures.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Hair: should be kept clean and tidy</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Nails: finger nails are hard and brittle, so should be kept soft by being rubbed with lotion or being soaked in warm water. Should be cut and kept short.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Shaving the male: assist the older person to shave their beard regularly and ensure their safety.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Safety: loss of Proprioception (sensation of body space). Restrains e.g. side rails in beds, adaptive devices for bath tabs and toilets.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Communications: older people experience problems communicating due to their failing sensory system. The is need to be encouraged to communicate and where possible they should not be kept alone in a room.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Hearing loss (presbycusis): starts at 40 and progresses with age. Older people who are hard of hearing should be assessed and mechanical aid should be provided.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Vision impairment; presbyopia: impaired vision due to the aging process. Aids for visually impaired should be provided e.g. large prints in telephone numbers, magnifying glass, spectacles, more night light to help in night blindness.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Physical activities and exercise: activity and exercise are very important to the older person to maintain circulation, muscle tone, general health and to prevent deformities that may occur in disease.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Emotional support and mental stimulation: the elderly person should be encouraged to remain self-sufficient and mentally active. This will prevent the person from boredom and depression. The older should be allowed to make decisions affecting his care.</p>
</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="drugs-and-the-elderly">Drugs and the elderly<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#drugs-and-the-elderly" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Elderly people use more drugs than any other age group in the community. This leads to more adverse reaction occurring in the elderly people since they are normally failing e.g. eyesight cause the elderly to make mistakes in their medication.</li>
<li>Physiological consideration of their failing functioning should be put into consideration while prescribing drugs.</li>
<li>The liver and the kidney may not be able to synthesize and excrete drugs. Age related changes in the function of CVS, gastrointestinal systems slows the metabolism and affects circulation, absorption and metabolism of drugs.</li>
<li>Drugs can also affect nutritional status of the elderly person due to some of the side effects e.g. loss of appetite, nausea, irritable stomach, decreased absorption of nutrients.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="possible-side-effects-of-drugs">Possible side effects of drugs<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#possible-side-effects-of-drugs" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Sedatives and hypnotics: confusion, delusion, hallucinations, falls, agitation, and altered behavior. They should be given in low doses</li>
<li>Analgesics: e.g. salicylates can cause electrolyte imbalance, bleeding, and nephrotoxicity.</li>
<li>Tranquilizers: hypotension, cerebral depression</li>
<li>Central nervous system stimulants: can cause confusion, paranoia</li>
<li>Cardiovascular drugs: e.g. digitalis; can cause dysarrthymias and gastrointestinal and mental symptoms.</li>
</ul>
<p>NB: when an elderly person is given drugs to self-medicate, the following should be explained.</p>
<p>i. Explain the action, side effects and dosage of each medication
ii. Write out the drugs schedule
iii. Encourage use of standard containers rather than safety lids.
iv. Destroy old unused medication
v. Periodically review the medication schedule.
vi. Discourage the use of over the counter drugs without consultation.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="bereavement">Bereavement<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#bereavement" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="stages-of-bereavement">Stages of bereavement<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#stages-of-bereavement" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="1-cognition">1. cognition<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#1-cognition" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Def: Cognition is a group of mental processes that includes attention, memory, producing and understanding language, solving problems, and making decisions.</p>
<ul>
<li>Learning does not necessarily decline with age and is facilitated when individual set their own pace. Short term memory generally wanes with age.</li>
<li>Crystallized intelligence which is based on past experiences show slight decline with age.</li>
<li>Fluid intelligence which is biologically mediated declines from adolescence on.</li>
<li>Information overload increases (neural noise)</li>
<li>Need for activity with meaningful outcome increases.</li>
<li>Cognitive decline may be related to nearness to death (terminal drop)</li>
</ul>
<ol start="2">
<li>
<p>Self concept/ self esteem: ability to accept age-related changes becomes important. Need to find meaningful substitutes for multiple losses increase.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Drug therapy: most dynamic effect are frequently accomplished not through administration of drugs but through their withdrawal. Age related changes may greatly affect the rate of absorption, distribution, metabolism and excretion. Vulnerability to adverse effects increases with age. Problems related to intravenous fluid therapy increases.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Elimination: disturbances are usually due too diet and or fluid intake change. Constipation is usually due to decrease of bulk in diet and decreases in fluid intake.</p>
</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>Diarrhea is usually due to laxative abuse ( higher in women than men).</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>Urinary changes include the following</li>
<li>Sensitivity of kidneys to sudden acid balance changes increase.</li>
<li>Nocturia increase</li>
<li>Significance of adequate fluid intake increases.</li>
<li>Lack of urinary control increases social isolation.</li>
<li>Prostatic enlargement increases frequently.</li>
</ul>
<ol start="5">
<li>Nutritional state</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>Low income has adverse effect on nutrition</li>
<li>Loneliness may be related to nutritional problems</li>
<li>Degree of physical activities is a major consideration.</li>
<li>Condition of the mouth and dentures are important factors.</li>
<li>Importance of food rituals surrounding food increases in old age.</li>
</ul>
<ol start="6">
<li>
<p>Pain: sensitivity changes to pain may occur. Tolerance to deep pain decreases while tolerance to subcutaneous pain increases in some instances death is welcome.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Skin: skin grows thin, appears dry, brittle and fragile. It increases in wrinkles due to loss of subcutaneous fat. Ability to remain properly hydrated is lost. It decreases in turgor with collagen content being increased. Ability to maintain body temperature decreases. Sexual and cultural differences are manifested. Sweat glands function decreases. Pigmentation spots (liver spots) increases. Number of capillary loops and blood vessels decreases.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Nails: grow slowly. Impairment of peripheral circulation results in their thickening and brittleness.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Hair: loss occurs but differ between sexes. Loss of pigmentation causes graying. Decrease in oil makes hair appearance dull and lifeless. Axillary hair often disappears. Body and pubic hair decreases.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Mouth: there is need for denture due to loss of teeth. Periodontal disease increases. Secretion reduces. Decline in number of taste buds results in decreased capacity to taste.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Physical exercise and mobility: muscle size diminishes and muscle tone and strength decreases. Maximum peak of strength is between 20 to 30 years of age. Loss may be secondary to decreased activity. Grip strength is one of the earliest indication of change. Potassium content declines. Tendency towards spinal kyphosis (dowagers hump) develops. Involuntary and painful muscle cramps results from decreased circulation to the extremities.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Bone mass: wide spread decrease occurs. Fractures occur more frequently in weight bearing areas. Reduction in height may occur due to intra-vertebral disc changes.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Safety: ambiguous environment increases confusion. Need for environmental clues increases e.g. hand rails in rooms, large size room numbers etc. need for appropriate designed equipment and furnishing increases. Uneven walking surfaces can cause falls.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Sleep: general pattern frequently changes and period of sleep becomes shorter. Proportion of REM and non-REM sleep remain fairly constant until about 60 years of age. Reduction in slow wave sleep occurs. Deep sleep(stage 4) is often absent. Concern and anxiety about pattern changes increases.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Time perception: tendency to underestimate time. Need for structure and adherence to time schedules increases.</p>
</li>
</ol>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="retirement">RETIREMENT<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#retirement" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Def: Retirement is the point where a person stops employment completely. It is one of the major adjustment to be made as an individual ages. When one looses a work role, for many people it is one experience of the impact of aging.</p>
<ul>
<li>Retirement is difficult especially in a society where worth is commonly judged by an individual’s productivity. Work is viewed as a requirement in a productive society.</li>
</ul>
<p><img src="assets/images/image-20251226-f5efd12b.jpeg" alt="img-16.jpeg"></p>
<ul>
<li>Unemployment for whatever reason is viewed negatively. Occupational identity is responsible for social positions and social roles.</li>
<li>Certain characteristics are associated with occupational position e.g.</li>
<li>Tough construction worker</li>
<li>Wild exotic dancer</li>
<li>The fair judge</li>
<li>The righteous clergy man</li>
<li>The learned lawyer</li>
<li>The eccentric artist</li>
</ul>
<p>78</p>
<ul>
<li>People are usually discussed according to their profession e.g. the nurse who lives down the road.</li>
<li>All these leads to an individual feeling of loss of identity with retirement.</li>
<li>Throughout childhood, adolescence, adulthood, an individual is guided towards independence and responsible adult role. Nobody is prepared for old age and how to live.</li>
</ul>
<p><img src="assets/images/image-20251226-f5efd12b.jpeg" alt="img-17.jpeg"></p>
<ul>
<li>Health workers should assist individuals in the preparation of retirement by:
Prevention and intervention and maximizing the potential for health and wellbeing in old age.
Encouraging aged individuals to establish and practice good health habits e.g.:</li>
<li>Eating proper diets</li>
<li>Avoiding excessive alcohol</li>
<li>Avoiding drug abuse</li>
<li>Avoiding tobacco abuse</li>
<li>Having regular physical examination</li>
</ul>
<p>80</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Since work is one’s primary interest, activity and social contact, retirement leaves a void in a person’s life therefore an aging individual should be encouraged to develop other interest unrelated to work.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Retirement should be facilitated by learning how to use-appreciated and gain satisfaction from leisure time and use it as a therapeutic stress outlet of life stress as one ages.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="phases-of-retirement">Phases of retirement<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#phases-of-retirement" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ol>
<li>
<p>Remote phase: early in occupational carrier, future retirement is anticipated but rational preparation is seldom done.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Near phase: when reality of retirement is evident, preparation for leaving one’s job begins as does fantasy regarding the retirement role.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Honeymoon phase: following the retirement event a somewhat euphoric period begins in which fantasizes from the pre-retirement period are tested. Retirees attempt to do everything they never had time for simultaneously. A variety of factors interferes e.g., health; limits this leading to the development of a staby lifestyle.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Disenchantment phase: as life begins to stabilize a letdown, sometimes a depression is experienced. The more unrealistic the pre-retirement fantasy, the greater the degree of disenchantment.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Re-orientation phase: realistic choices and alternative sources of satisfaction are considered. The disenchantment with the new retirement routine can be replaced by developing a lifestyle that provides some satisfaction.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Stability phase: an understanding of the retirement role is achieved and provides a framework for concern; involvement and action in the elderly person's life. Some enter this phase directly after honeymoon phase and some never reach it at all.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Termination phase: the retirement role is lost as a result of either the resumption of work role or dependency due to illness or disability</p>
</li>
</ol>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="cognition-and-perception-in-old-age">COGNITION AND PERCEPTION IN OLD AGE<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#cognition-and-perception-in-old-age" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Cognition: deals with the way people gain information from the environment and the way they interpret and use information.</li>
<li>Perception: includes collection, interpretation and recognition of stimuli including pain.</li>
<li>Cognition: intelligence, memory, language and decision making</li>
<li>Cognition and perception is connected in the CNS with special senses of vision, hearing, touch, smell and taste.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="types-of-intelligence">Types of intelligence<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#types-of-intelligence" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ol>
<li>Fluid intelligence (young people): ability to perform tasks or make judgment based on unfamiliar stimuli (known as thinking on your feet).</li>
<li>Crystallized intelligence (old people): ability to perform tasks or make judgment based on knowledge and experience acquired through life time (wisdom).</li>
</ol>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="perception-and-cognition-and-aging">Perception and cognition and aging<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#perception-and-cognition-and-aging" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Aged person experiences sensory changes interfering with collection of information. Many aged persons are considered confused or are suffering from altered sensory perception.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Elderly people who do not see or hear well may walk out into traffic or make mistakes about direction; not due to failure of proper sensory information.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Intelligence does not automatically decrease with aging nor does the ability to learn.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Elderly people may appear to be less intelligent due to tendency to be slower and more cautious in their response.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Lack of formal education may also cause the elderly person to appear less intelligent. They may lack polish in speech or have less vocabulary.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The speed at which information is processed and recalled does change with age. Forgetfulness or memory loss is common with aging; at 75 years as much as 30% of memory is lost.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Most elderly can compensate for gaps in memory by using experiences gathered over a lifetime.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Ability to acquire new information also appears to decrease with age. This could be more of a lack of desire to learn than inability to acquire and retain information. Once information is learned the alert elderly person is able to recall it as a younger person.</p>
</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="grief">GRIEF<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#grief" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Def: is a subjective state of emotional, physical and social response to a valued entity.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="types">Types<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#types" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ol>
<li>Anticipatory grief: Grief that begins before (in anticipation of) the loss, such as the initiation of divorce proceedings or when a loved one is diagnosed with a terminal illness.</li>
<li>Anniversary reactions: Grief responses that occur following reminders of the loss, such as on anniversaries, holidays or other special days throughout the year. These can last for days or weeks, and are not necessarily a setback in the grieving process.</li>
<li>Complicated grief (also called Traumatic Grief): Occurs when grief becomes chronic, disabling and more intense. This is often seen as a progression of grief into major depression, with some features of post-traumatic stress disorder, such as nightmares and flashbacks.</li>
</ol>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="types-of-loss">Types of loss<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#types-of-loss" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Real loss e.g. of a loved one, property or relationship which can be shared by others.</li>
<li>Perceived loss: that is felt by the individual alone and not shared by others e.g. loss of femininity following a major operation e.g. mastectomy or hysterectomy</li>
</ul>
<p>NB: Any situation which cause change can be identified as loss. Failure either real or perceived can be viewed as loss. Loss or anticipated loss can trigger a grief response. The period of grief is known as mourning which is a normal response characterized by sadness, guilt, anger, helplessness and despair.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="stages-of-grief">STAGES OF GRIEF<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#stages-of-grief" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ol>
<li>
<p>SHOCK &amp; DENIAL-
The person reacts to learning of the loss with numbed disbelief. He deny the reality of the loss at some level, in order to avoid the pain. Shock provides emotional protection from being overwhelmed all at once. This may last for weeks.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>PAIN &amp; GUILT-
As the shock wears off, it is replaced with the suffering of unbelievable pain. Although excruciating and almost unbearable, it is important that you experience the pain fully, and not hide it, avoid it or escape from it with alcohol or drugs. He/she may have guilty feelings or remorse over things he/she did or didn't do with your loved one. Life feels chaotic and scary during this phase.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>ANGER &amp; BARGAINING-
Frustration gives way to anger, and one may lash out and lay unwarranted blame for the death on someone else. This is a time for the release of bottled up emotion.</p>
</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>One may rail against fate, questioning "Why me?" one may also try to bargain in vain with the powers that be for a way out of his/her despair ("I will never drink again if you just bring him back").</li>
</ul>
<ol start="4">
<li>"DEPRESSION", REFLECTION, LONELINESS-
Just when one’s friends may think he/she should be getting on with life, a long period of sad reflection will likely overtake him/her. This is a normal stage of grief and one should not be "talked out of it" by well-meaning outsiders. Encouragement from others is not helpful during this stage of grieving.</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>During this time, one finally realizes the true magnitude of his/her loss, and it depresses him/her. One may isolate self on purpose, reflect on things he/she did with his/her lost one, and focus on memories of the past. One may sense feelings of emptiness or despair.</li>
</ul>
<ol start="5">
<li>
<p>THE UPWARD TURN-
As one starts to adjust to life without his/her dear one, his/her life becomes a little calmer and more organized. One's physical symptoms lessen, &amp; "depression" begins to lift slightly.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>RECONSTRUCTION &amp; WORKING THROUGH-
As one becomes more functional, his/her mind starts working again, and you will find yourself seeking realistic solutions to problems posed by life without your loved one. You will start to work on practical and financial problems and reconstructing yourself and your life without him or her.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>ACCEPTANCE &amp; HOPE-
One learns to accept and deal with the reality of his/her situation. Acceptance does not necessarily mean instant happiness. Given the pain and turmoil one has experienced, one can never return to the carefree, untroubled self that existed before this tragedy.</p>
</li>
</ol>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="kubler-ross-model">Kubler Ross model<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#kubler-ross-model" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>The stages, popularly known by the acronym DABDA, include:</li>
</ul>
<ol>
<li>
<p>Denial — "I feel fine."; "This can't be happening, not to me." Denial is usually only a temporary defense for the individual. This feeling is generally replaced with heightened awareness of possessions and individuals that will be left behind after death. Denial can be conscious or unconscious refusal to accept facts, information, or the reality of the situation.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Anger — "Why me? It's not fair!"; "How can this happen to me?"; "Who is to blame?"</p>
</li>
</ol>
<p>Once in the second stage, the individual recognizes that denial cannot continue. Because of anger, the person is very difficult to care for due to misplaced feelings of rage and envy. Anger can manifest itself in different ways. People can be angry with themselves, or with others, and especially those who are close to them. It is important to remain detached and nonjudgmental when dealing with a person experiencing anger from grief.</p>
<ol start="3">
<li>Bargaining — "I'll do anything for a few more years."; "I will give my life savings if..."</li>
</ol>
<p>The third stage involves the hope that the individual can somehow postpone or delay death. Usually, the negotiation for an extended life is made with a higher power in exchange for a reformed lifestyle. Psychologically, the individual is saying, "I understand I will die, but if I could just do something to buy more time..." People facing less serious trauma can bargain or seek to negotiate a compromise. For example "Can we still be friends?.." when facing a break-up. Bargaining rarely provides a sustainable solution, especially if it's a matter of life or death.</p>
<ol start="4">
<li>Depression — "I'm so sad, why bother with anything?"; "I'm going to die soon so what's the point?"; "I miss my loved one, why go on?" During the fourth stage, the dying person begins to understand the certainty of death. Because of this, the individual may become silent, refuse visitors and spend much of the time crying and grieving.</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>This process allows the dying person to disconnect from things of love and affection. Don't attempt to cheer up an individual who is in this stage. It is an important time for grieving that must be processed. Depression could be referred to as the dress rehearsal for the 'aftermath'. It is a kind of acceptance with emotional attachment. It's natural to feel sadness, regret, fear, and uncertainty when going through this stage. Feeling those emotions shows that the person has begun to accept the situation.</li>
</ul>
<ol start="5">
<li><strong>Acceptance</strong> — "It's going to be okay."; "I can't fight it, I may as well prepare for it."</li>
</ol>
<p>In this last stage, individuals begin to come to terms with their mortality, or that of a loved one, or other tragic event. This stage varies according to the person's situation. People dying can enter this stage a long time before the people they leave behind, who must pass through their own individual stages of dealing with the grief.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="length-of-the-grief-process">Length of the grief process<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#length-of-the-grief-process" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Stages of grief allow the bereaved person or elderly approach resolution of mourning.</li>
<li>Each stage of grief present tasks to be overcome through painful experimental processes.</li>
<li>Grief is complete when a person can remember comfortably and realistically both the pleasures and disappointment of the lost person or object.</li>
<li>Length of grief is individualistic and can last a number of years without being maladaptive.</li>
<li>Acute phase lasts 6 to 8 weeks but its longer in older adults where it takes 6 months to 1 year.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="factors-that-influence-outcomes-of-grief">Factors that influence outcomes of grief<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#factors-that-influence-outcomes-of-grief" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>

<li>The importance of the lost object or person as a source of support</li>
<li>The degree of dependency on relation with lost object or person.</li>
<li>The number and nature of other meaningful relationship the mourner has.</li>
<li>The degree of ambivalence felt towards the lost person or object.</li>
<li>The number and nature of previous grief experiences e.g. grief which can be cumulative especially when the previous grief has not been resolved.</li>
<li>The age of the lost person e.g. child, adult or elderly person.</li>
<li>Health of the mourner at the time of loss. Status of physical, psychological condition and coping capacity for stress.</li>
<li>The degree of preparation for the loss</li>
</ol>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="1-anticipatory-grief">1. Anticipatory grief<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#1-anticipatory-grief" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>

<li>Stages of grieving are similar to those of normal loss with a difference that conventional grief tends to reduce in intensity as time passes.</li>
<li>Anticipatory grief increases in intensity as expected loss becomes more imminent.</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="disadvantages-of-anticipatory-grief">Disadvantages of anticipatory grief<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#disadvantages-of-anticipatory-grief" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ol>
<li>If family members complete the process of grieving then attachment from the dying person occurs prematurely.</li>
<li>The person experiences loneliness, isolation as well as psychological pain of imminent death without family support.</li>
<li>There may be problem of re-establishing prior relationship after grieving if death does not occur.</li>
</ol>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="maladaptive-response-to-loss">Maladaptive response to loss<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#maladaptive-response-to-loss" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Delayed or inhibited grief: absence of grief when it ordinarily would be expected. It is caused by:-</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Cultural influences</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Danger; the person is fixed in denial stage</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Grief may be triggered later by another unrelated event or another loss resulting in over-reaction. It occurs due to :-</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Ambivalent feelings</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Outside pressure to resume normal function</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Perceived lack of internal and external sources to cope with profound loss.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="2-prolonged-grief">2. Prolonged grief<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#2-prolonged-grief" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Grief is considered prolonged if activities of normal living are not resumed after a period of 4 to 6 weeks.</li>
<li>If grief is associated with self-blame; difficulties accepting loss, sudden untimely loss, history of dependence on the dead person.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="signs-and-symptoms">Signs and symptoms<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#signs-and-symptoms" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<ol>
<li>Development of physical symptoms experienced by the dead person</li>
<li>Progressive social withdrawal</li>
<li>Poor interpersonal relationships with friends and relatives</li>
<li>Participation in activities detrimental to one’s social or economic existence.</li>
</ol>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="3-exaggerated-grief">3. Exaggerated grief<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#3-exaggerated-grief" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Signs and symptoms of normal grieving process are exaggerated.</li>
<li>Feeling of sadness, helplessness, hopelessness, anger and guilt are accompanied by other somatic complaints.</li>
<li>This leads to overwhelming behavior without progression toward completion of the grieving process.</li>
<li>Individual is fixed in anger stage and blames others or self for the loss which leads to depression.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="stages-of-death-by-kubler-ross-1969">Stages of death by Kubler Ross 1969<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#stages-of-death-by-kubler-ross-1969" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ol>
<li>Denial and isolation: it is very difficult for any individual to face the fact that death is to be faced soon. The most common reaction is to isolate oneself until defences are achieved.</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>Denial permits hope to exist but most patients are ready to accept the fact that they are dying but families continue to express denial.</li>
<li>Denial delay, communication of concerns with the patient stopping denial and isolation by thinking about unfinished business e.g. personal affairs, finances, arrangement for spouse, children and others.</li>
</ul>
<ol start="2">
<li>
<p>Anger: the person experiences anger with the person asking the question; why me? The patient is difficult to nurse as nothing seems to please him or her. The person wants to express their outrage and helplessness. After expressing their anger they move on.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Bargaining: third phase of dying when the person attempts to negotiate and trade. It usually involves a deal with God; the physician or the nurse. E.g. If I can live long enough to attend my son's wedding I will be ready to die. If possible patients should be granted their request.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Depression: the patient is now aware that death is inevitable. Defense mechanism are no longer effective. Sadness and anguish are felt and expressed.</p>
</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>The patient may organize to gain support from loved one's and nurses.</li>
<li>The resolution leads to final stage.</li>
</ul>
<ol start="5">
<li>Acceptance: it's a time of relative peace. The patient wants to review the past and think about the unknown future. Patient may not talk a lot about but he/she wants other people nearby. With pain relieve the person accepts death and wants to be comforted by having significant others nearby.</li>
</ol>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="nursing-intervention">Nursing intervention<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#nursing-intervention" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ol>
<li>To give maximum help to the dying by examining the nurse's own feeling about death.</li>
<li>Patient is an individual and should be treated with respect and dignity regardless of background or condition.</li>
<li>Social values may affect reaction to the dying person e.g. age, attractiveness, socio-economic status, former accomplishment. These may affect whether the person is cared for or abandoned while dying.</li>
<li>Nurses usually become the most important link with life for the dying person.</li>
<li>The nurse provides physical comfort and emotional support. It is an emotional stress to the nurse assigned to people who are dying and these need to share their feelings and reactions with others to obtain support.</li>
</ol>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="bill-of-rights-for-a-dying-person">Bill of rights for a dying person<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#bill-of-rights-for-a-dying-person" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ol>
<li>
<p>I have a right to be treated as a living human being until I die</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>I have a right to maintain a sense of hopefulness, however changing its focus may be.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>I have the right to be cared for by those who can maintain a sense of hopefulness, however changing this may be.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>I have a right to express my feelings and emotions and my approaching death in my own way.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>I have a right to participate in decision concerning my care.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>I have a right to expect continuing medical and nursing attention even though "cure" goals must be changed to comfort goals.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>I have a right not to die alone.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>I have a right to be free from pain.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>I have a right to have my questions answered honestly.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>I have a right not to be deceived</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>I have a right to help from and for my family in accepting my death.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>I have the right to die in peace and with dignity</p>
</li>

</li>
<li>

</li>
<li>
<p>I have the right to expect that the sanctity of the human body will be respected after death.</p>
</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="palliative-care">PALLIATIVE CARE<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#palliative-care" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Def: comprehensive person and family centered care when disease is not responsive to treatment.</p>
<ul>
<li>Hospice care: care concerned with end of life focussing on realistic social, emotional, spiritual and financial preparation for death.</li>
<li>It addresses the whole person including physical, social and spiritual aspect in life threatening illness.</li>
<li>Hospice care can be defined as provided to terminally ill person’s and their families in the last 6 months of the patient's life. Most patients are referred for hospice care when its too late in their illnesses.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="reasons-for-late-referral">Reasons for late referral<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#reasons-for-late-referral" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ol>
<li>
<p>Values and attitudes of health care providers</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Inadequate dissemination of existing knowledge about pain and symptom management.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Health care providers difficulties in effectively communicating with the terminally ill person.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Insufficient attention to palliative care concept in health care provider’s education and training.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Confusion about eligibility criteria for hospice services.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Palliative care in hospital setting:</p>
</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>50% of all deaths occur in the hospital set-up. In hospital set up:-</li>
<li>Many patients received unwanted care at the end of life.</li>
<li>Clinicians are not aware of the patient’s preference for life sustaining treatment even when preference were documented in the clinical records.</li>
<li>Pain is often poorly controlled at the end of life.</li>
<li>Efforts to enhance communication are ineffective</li>
</ul>
<ol start="2">
<li>Palliative care in long-term care facilities:</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>These are homes for the aged where most of the residents die. Palliative care can be offered there.</li>
</ul>
<ol start="3">
<li>Hospice care:</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Hospice care is not a home but a concept of care in which the end of life is viewed as a developmental care. Hospice is derived from the word “hospes”...meaning “hosts”.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Historically hospice is referred to as shelter or way station for weary travels on a pilgrimage. Hospice care has become an alternative to depersonalize death in institutions.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Hospice care began as a response in gaps in care e.g.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Between treating the disease and treating the person</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Between technological research and psychological support</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Between the general denial of the facts of death in the society and acceptance of death by those who face it.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="principles-of-hospice-care">Principles of hospice care<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#principles-of-hospice-care" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ol>
<li>Death must be accepted</li>
<li>The patient’s total care is best managed by an interdisciplinary team whose members communicate regularly with each other.</li>
<li>Pain and other symptoms of terminal illness must be managed.</li>
<li>The patient and family should be viewed as a single unit of care.</li>
<li>Home care of the dying is necessary</li>
<li>Bereavement care must be provided to family.</li>
<li>Research and education should be ongoing.</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>Hospice movement is based on a belief that meaningful living is achievable during terminal illness. Its best supported in the home free from technology intervention to prolong physiologic dying.</li>
<li>In hospice set up the patient and his family make up a unit of care. The goals is to enable the patient to remain at home surrounded by the people and objects that have been important to him/her throughout life.</li>
<li>Hospice does not seek to hasten death or encourage the prolongation of life through artificial means.</li>
<li>The care is based on competent patients full or open awareness of dying embracing realism about death.</li>
<li>It helps the patient’s family to understand the dying process so that they can live each moment as fully as possible.</li>
<li>Although most care is given in patient’s home, some programmes have developed in-patient facilities or residences where terminally ill person’s have no support.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="eligibility-criteria">Eligibility criteria<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#eligibility-criteria" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>a) Patient must have progressive irreversible illness
b) Limited life expectancy
c) Client must have opted for palliative care
d) It does not only serve cancer patients but any patient with life-limiting illness.
e) Presence of a family member or other caregiver continuously in the home when the patient is no longer able to safely care for him/herself.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="types-of-care-offered">Types of care offered<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#types-of-care-offered" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="nursing-care-of-terminally-ill-patients">Nursing care of terminally ill patients<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#nursing-care-of-terminally-ill-patients" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="1-psychosocial-issue">1. Psychosocial issue<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#1-psychosocial-issue" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Education about illnesses and patient’s life with the illness</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Value clarification</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Treatment decision making</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>End of life closure</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Nurses should try and understand the illness from the patient's point of view.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>An honest discussion about death should be held giving the patient information on illness, end of life choices, open discussion about death.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>At the same time the nurses should be culturally aware and sensitive in their approaches to communication about death to patient and relatives and how much information they can handle.</p>

</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="2-communication">2. Communication<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#2-communication" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="general-guidelines">General guidelines<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#general-guidelines" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<ul>
<li>Deliver and interpret the technical information necessary for making decisions without hiding behind medical terminology</li>

<ul>
<li>Pain is usually many of the end of life illnesses e.g. cancer.</li>
<li>Poor management of pain affects psychological, emotional, social and financial well being of the patient.</li>
<li>Patients on analgesic for pain should continue receiving those medications and inability to complain should not be mistaken for absence of pain. If patient is unable to swallow oral analgesic, drugs can be administered per rectal or sublingually.</li>
