üîÑ Pharmacokinetics: Absorption

Pharmacokinetics refers to how the body processes drugs. In the elderly, physiological changes significantly alter absorption, distribution, metabolism, and excretion.

Oral Medication Absorption

  • Gastric pH: Reduced gastric acid and enzymes lead to increased pH. Since most medications are designed to dissolve in an acid medium, a neutral or alkaline environment results in decreased absorption.
  • Motility: Decreased gastric motility causes slower emptying. While medication stays in contact with the stomach lining longer (potentially increasing absorption), it can also cause wearing of the stomach lining.
  • Peristalsis: Decreased peristalsis delays drug transportation and action.
02

‚öñÔ∏è Distribution

Aging causes significant body composition changes that interfere with drug distribution:

Body Composition Change Pharmacological Impact
Decrease in total body water Water-soluble drugs (e.g., Gentamicin) remain in the bloodstream in higher concentrations, especially with dehydration.
Increase in total body fat Fat-soluble drugs get trapped in body fat, reducing levels in the bloodstream initially but potentially prolonging effects.
Reduced Plasma Protein (Albumin) Decreased binding sites for protein-bound drugs (e.g., Warfarin), leading to higher levels of free, active drug.
03

üè≠ Metabolism

The liver's ability to metabolize drugs declines with age due to:

  • Decreased enzyme production.
  • Reduced hepatic blood flow (due to circulatory changes).
  • Reduced liver effectiveness.
‚ö†Ô∏è Risk Alert

The reduced speed of metabolism increases the risk of drug toxicity in the elderly.

04

üöΩ Excretion

Kidney function decreases with age, affecting drug elimination:

  • Reduced blood flow to the kidneys.
  • Diminished renal function causes drug retention and increased blood levels.
  • Specific drugs like Digoxin, Lithium, and Cimetidine can further diminish renal function.
üíä Polypharmacy

The use of excessive, often unnecessary medications (prescription and over-the-counter) is common. It increases the risk of adverse reactions, cognitive impairment, and behavioral changes.

üìã Management & Side Effects

General Considerations

Elderly people use more drugs than any other age group. Adverse reactions are common due to failing functions (e.g., poor eyesight leading to medication errors).

Drugs can affect nutritional status by causing:

  • Loss of appetite
  • Nausea
  • Irritable stomach
  • Decreased nutrient absorption

Common Side Effects

Drug Class Potential Side Effects in Elderly
Sedatives / Hypnotics Confusion, delusion, hallucinations, falls, agitation.
Analgesics (e.g., Salicylates) Electrolyte imbalance, bleeding, nephrotoxicity.
Tranquilizers Hypotension, cerebral depression.
CNS Stimulants Confusion, paranoia.
Cardiovascular (e.g., Digitalis) Dysrhythmias, gastrointestinal symptoms, mental changes.
