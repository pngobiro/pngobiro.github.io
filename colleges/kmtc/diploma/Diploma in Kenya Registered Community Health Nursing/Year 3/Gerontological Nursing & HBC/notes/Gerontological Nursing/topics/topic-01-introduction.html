üìñ Definition of Terms

Understanding the specialized terminology is essential for gerontological nursing practice.

Geriatrics
The study of the medical aspect of old age and the application of knowledge related to biological, biomedical, behavioral, and social aspects of aging to the prevention and care of the elderly person.

Aging
Total time-related changes experienced by an individual which are biological, psychological, sociological, and spiritual.

Ageism
A process of systematic stereotyping and discrimination against old people simply because they are old.

Gerontophobia
Unreasonable fear and irrational hatred of older people.

Age
The length of time that has passed since birth.

Biological & Statistical Concepts

  • Life expectancy: The statistically determined number of years that a person at a particular age may expect to live.
  • Lifespan: The longest period of life of an organism known from the study of it.
  • Biological clock: The theory that each cell is programmed through DNA to self-destruct; cells multiply and divide a fixed number of times before self-destructing.
  • Aging pacemaker: A pacemaker in the brain that initiates a neurohormonal response (possibly through serotonin, dopamine, or tryptophan) resulting in aging.

Common Challenges

Elderly individuals often face significant life transitions and stressors:

  • Retirement: Loss of occupational identity and income changes.
  • Widowhood: Loss of spouse and companionship.
  • Chronic Illness: Managing long-term health conditions.
  • Relocation: Moving to different environments, such as rural areas or care facilities.

ü©∫ Scope of Gerontological Nursing

Gerontological nursing (also referred to as care of older adults) entails specific focus areas:

  • Focuses on the care of patients 65 years or older.
  • Requires special attention due to the unique physiological and psychological changes occurring in this population.
‚ö†Ô∏è Important Distinction

Gerontological nursing is NOT hospice care. While it may involve end-of-life care, its primary focus is on promoting health and managing conditions in the aging population, distinct from purely palliative care for the terminally ill.

üîÑ The Aging Process

Definition

Aging is defined as the total time-related changes experienced by an individual. It is a multidimensional process involving:

Dimensions of Aging: Biological ‚Ä¢ Psychological ‚Ä¢ Sociological ‚Ä¢ Spiritual

Types of Aging

‚ñ∂ Biological Aging

Refers to changes in the structure and functions of the body that occur over a lifespan. It can be gradual and is affected by:

  • Genetic inheritance
  • External environmental factors
  • Internal environmental factors
‚ñ∂ Psychological Aging

Involves behavioral changes in self-perception and reactions to biological changes. It is influenced by:

  • Body conditions (e.g., cardiovascular or renal system health)
  • Brain changes affecting memory, learning, motivation, and emotions
‚ñ∂ Functional Aging

Refers to the capacity of the individual to function in society compared to others in the same age group. It measures ability rather than chronological age.

‚ñ∂ Sociological Aging

Changes in roles and social habits of individuals in society. It includes:

  • Changes in social expectations
  • Changes in social status
