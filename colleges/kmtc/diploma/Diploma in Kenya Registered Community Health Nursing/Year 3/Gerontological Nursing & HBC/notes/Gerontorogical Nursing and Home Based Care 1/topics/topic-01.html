Myths vs. Facts of Aging

‚ùå

Myths

  • All older people are alike.
  • Older people cannot learn.
  • Older people are senile.
  • All older people are in nursing homes.
  • All older people are sick and helpless.
  • Older people become more religious as they age.
‚úÖ

Facts

  • Older people are widely different from each other and their experiences cannot be generalized.
  • Aging is an individual event that is heavily influenced by societal attitude and support.

Definitions

Gerontology
The study of the aged or it is the study of the physical, mental, and social processes of growing old. It is coined from the word geron (aging) and logos (knowledge).
Geriatrics
Branch in gerontology which involves the medical care of the aged.
Aging
Refers to the normal process of time-related change in human or it refers to the deterioration of physiological functions necessary for survival; it begins with birth and continues through life. People age differently and within an individual, systems and organs age at different rates.

Factors Influencing Aging

Aging can be viewed as a balance between negative and positive factors:

  • Negative factors: Stress, cardiovascular diseases, diabetes, sensory loss, obesity.
  • Positive factors: Exercises, good nutrition, social support, stress management, coping resources.
  • Other Influencers: Heredity, Occupation, Social status, Level of activity, Health maintenance, Stress coping, Health status.

üìä Classification & Theories of Aging

Classification of Aging

  • Chronological aging: Number of years lived.
  • Bureaucratic: Eligibility for social programmes (e.g., most civil servants retire at 60 years).
  • Functioning: IADL, ADL activities.
  • Social: Roles and relationships in the social structure as people age.
  • Biological: Physical changes to organs and cells.
  • Psychological: Mental state, physical performance.
  • Demographic: Trends in the overall and aging population.

Classification of Aging (WHO)

  • Young-old: 65-75 years (transition period to retirement)
  • Advanced-old: 75-85 years
  • Very advanced-old: >85 years

Ageism

Coined by Robert Butler. It is prejudgement based on age. It causes us to assume things about persons based solely on their age and includes discrimination based on their age.

Theories of Aging

A theory is a statement that explains a phenomena or a statement that establishes an answer to a problem. Theories help us to interpret aging.

‚ñ∂ 1. Disengagement Theory (Elaine, 1961)

States that over time elderly people withdraw from the social roles and relationships that were central to their life in adulthood. They become inactive and withdrawn. Transfer of power and responsibility to younger persons is done to replace the old from work place. The theory claims that it is natural and acceptable for older adult to withdraw from societal roles and the society eases away from them.

‚ñ∂ 2. Activity Theory

Developed as a reaction to disengagement theory. It says that persons who are active will be more satisfied and better adjusted than those who have disengaged. Optimal ageing is achieved when individuals engage in activities.

‚ñ∂ 3. Continuity Theory (Atchley, 1989)

States that older adults will usually hold to the same activities, behaviours, personalities as they were in middle age as it aid in making adaptive choices in future.

‚ñ∂ 4. Psychological Model Theory

Based on the theory of human development (Erik Erickson theory of psychosocial development). It states that later adulthood is a life stage of its own, unique problems, tasks; it is here we have integrity verses despair.

‚ñ∂ 5. Role Theory

People hold a variety of roles throughout life. Roles determine self-concept and self-image. Aging alters roles, norms, standards and expectations (e.g., a 65 years old man who is a breadwinner will have difficulty to adjust to forced retirement, death of a spouse).

‚ñ∂ 6. Immune Theory

As one grows older, ability of the body to gain protection against infections declines. As the immune system weakens, one is susceptible to diseases thus aggravating the aging process.

‚ñ∂ 7. Neuroendocrine Theory

Aging results to decline in both neuronal and endocrine output. Deficiency results to dysfunction in various endocrine glands and their target organs (e.g., withdrawal of oestrogen postmenopausal predisposes a woman to osteoporosis, deficiency of insulin results to diabetes).

‚ñ∂ 8. Run-Up Programme Theory

Genetic materials in the cell keep on deteriorating as one grows old and no more replacement.

‚ñ∂ 9. Wear and Tear Theory

Our bodies are like a machine; as one grows old several tissues are damaged leading to loss of function.

üìâ Degenerative Changes & Management

1. Integumentary System

Changes: Oil and sweat glands becomes less active. Skin losses its elasticity and becomes dry. Skin folds and wrinkles forms. Reduced blood flow to the skin which can interfere with healing upon injury. Nails becomes thick, tough and brittle. Increased sensitivity to temperature. Balding becomes common in men. Hair losses color, hair loss is common.

ü©π

Management

  • Encourage daily exposure to sunlight.
  • Care for injuries immediately.
  • Shampoo as often as needed.
  • Oil the skin.
  • Shower routinely.
  • Wear socks, sweaters, heavy jackets to keep body warm.

2. Circulatory System

Changes: Stiffening of the arterial system occurs. There is increased afterload on the left ventricle resulting to systolic blood pressure. Congestive heart failure can ensue.

‚ù§Ô∏è

Management

  • Avoid strenuous exercises.
  • Rest following an activity.
  • Exercise as per individual tolerance.
  • Prevent formation of thrombus e.g., use supportive stockings, if confined to bed, ROM activities.
  • In case of high blood pressure; take low sodium diet, decrease fat intake.
  • Adhere to medication and keep clinic appointments.

3. Respiratory System

Changes: Alveoli becomes thinner and less elastic. Respiratory muscles become weaker. Ribcage becomes more rigid. Difficult coughing up secretions. Changes in larynx leads to high pitched and weak voice.

ü´Å

Management

  • Encourage client to sleep in semi-fowlers position; use 2-3 pillows.
  • Breath deeply and cough frequently.
  • Avoid polluted environment.

4. Digestive System

Changes: There is loss of teeth. Digestive juice and enzymes are less. Muscle actions becomes slower and peristalsis decreases. Liver functions decreases. Decreased sensation of taste result to poor appetite and reduced intake. Slower digestion leads to constipation. Increase in thirst threshold.

üçΩÔ∏è

Management

  • Good oral hygiene.
  • Repair lost teeth.
  • Relaxed eating atmosphere.
  • High fiber diet, protein rich.
  • Offer a variety of food; seasoning.
  • Encourage fluid intake.

5. Genitourinary System

Changes: Kidney decrease in size and become less efficient. Bladder decrease in size and holds less urine. Blood flow to kidneys reduces. Number of functioning nephrones reduces. Urine incontinence may occur. 75% of men experience hypertrophy of the prostate.

üöª

Management

  • Regular trips to bathroom.
  • Wear easy to remove clothes.
  • Increase fluid intake but reduce during bed time.
  • Teach clients on pelvic floor exercises.

6. Musculoskeletal System

Changes: There is loss of bone mass due to loss of calcium. Risks of fractures increases. Cartilages deteriorates resulting to narrowing of joints. Discs between the vertebrae of the spine becomes less dense resulting to back pains. Arthritis is common. Loss of muscle function is common (sarcopenia).

ü¶¥

Management

  • Exercises.
  • Eat balanced diet, more calcium, vitamin D.
  • Quit smoking.
  • Drink adequate water.

7. Reproductive System

Changes (Female): Oestrogen and progesterone levels goes down resulting to: Thinning of the vaginal wall, Decrease of vaginal secretions, Cessation of menses, Uterus may sag (uterine prolapse), Breasts sag.

Changes (Male): Decrease in testosterone results to: Slow production of sperms, Response to sexual stimuli slows, Testes becomes small, less firm.

General: Erectile dysfunction is common with advancing age. Sexual desire may or may not decrease in both sexes. NB: Sex in elderly improves muscle tone and circulation thus encourage couples to stay together.

8. Nervous System

Changes: Blood flow to the brain decreases. There is progressive loss of brain cells. Sensory perception diminishes (taste, vision, hearing). Nerve endings become less sensitive reducing ability to respond to pain. Alzheimer's, dementia, myopia, presbyopia are common.

üß†

Management

  • Reality orientation to time, place, person (e.g., regular clothing during the day and night, close curtain at night, open curtain during the day).
  • Encourage conversation about familiar things.
  • Surgery to improve sight (e.g., in cataract silicon prosthesis is replaced).
  • Provision of Hearing aid in partial hearing loss.
  • Alternative means of communication should be taught to patient, family, friends, community (e.g., pen and paper, facial expressions, lip reading).

üìã Principles & Needs of the Elderly

Principles of Gerontological Nursing

  • Psychosocial approach: Needs arise from living alone, social isolation, functional dependence, changes in sexual relationship, abuse and neglect. Understand patient life history and preference of care.
  • Multidisciplinary team: Key in geriatric care. Provides support to patient, families, and help them adapt to illness.
  • Importance of chronic illness: Older persons suffer from chronic conditions and acute illnesses which makes management complex.
  • Aging is not a disease: It is a normal process; it occurs at different rates.
  • Reversible conditions: Treatable conditions are often under-diagnosed or mistaken for old age (e.g., malnutrition).
  • Functional ability: Quality of life is a critical outcome.
  • Cognitive/Affective disorders: Prevalent and often undiagnosed early.
  • Setting: Care ranges from home to long-term institutions.
  • Ethics: Ethical issues and end of life care are critical.

Needs of Elderly Persons

  1. Health care: Risk of co-morbidities (diabetes, heart disease, cancers, arthritis, etc.).
  2. Nutritional needs: Poor appetite due to physical illness, sensory loss, constipation, loss of teeth.
  3. Financial security: Need finances for hospital bills, drugs, and personal needs (often poorer than rest of population).
  4. Mobility: Activity limitation due to chronic illness, difficulty in ADLs.
  5. Social security: Loneliness as children move out.
  6. Personal security: Risk of abuse, rape, or neglect.
  7. Proper housing: Accessibility, spacing, lighting, ventilation.
  8. Educational training: How to use technology, media involvement.
  9. Values and beliefs: Respect for worship, food preferences, dressing.

Role of the Nurse

  • Observing and interpreting signs of normal aging vs pathological changes.
  • Promoting physical safety (removal of hazards, nightlights).
  • Providing for socialization and intimacy needs.
  • Assist in obtaining mechanical devices (e.g., walkers).
  • Resolving personal attitudes about aging and death.
  • Link families to community resources (CBOs).
  • Educate patient on safety and disease prevention.
  • Prevent multisystem complications (skin care, ROM).
  • Provision of referral and linkages.

ü©∫ Assessment of an Elderly Person

Interviewing Skills

  • Speak to the patient not caregiver.
  • Speak facing the patient such that he can see your face.
  • Avoid ageist remarks; don't agree with them if patient uses them.
  • Be observant of their dressing (they expect you to be too).
  • Attend to their comfort.
  • Warm your hands before touch.
  • Be patient during interview (slow response does not indicate dementia).

History Taking

  1. Chief complaints, history of present complaints, medications, duration.
  2. Past medical/surgical history.
  3. Family social history.
  4. Head to toe examination.

Screening Tools During Assessment

Category Key Assessment Areas
A) Medication Kind of medication? Interactions? Inappropriate drugs (benzodiazepines, etc.)? Average number including OTC/herbal?
B) Mobility Get-up and go test, Functional reach, One leg balance.
C) ADLs Assess for Activities of Daily Living and Instrumental ADLs.
D) Social Support Who helps if sick? Where do children live?
E) Incontinence Stress or urge? "Rush to toilet?", "Urine with cough/sneeze?"
F) Nutrition Weight loss? Medication? Depression? Finances?
G) Health Condition Cancer, hypertension, etc.
H) Depression "How do you see your future?", "Are you often sad?"

üèôÔ∏è Social Services for Elderly Persons

Kenya has about 1.4 million people aged 65 years and above (projected 2.5 million by 2030). Homes for the aged have emerged due to changing family values, cost of living, and migration.

Available Social Services

  • A) Home Care: Patient lives with family; family trained on resources and when to seek hospital care.
  • B) Nursing Homes & Hospice:
    • Hospice: Palliative care (e.g., Nairobi Hospice, Pinnacle Home Services).
    • Respite Care: Short term relief for primary caregivers.
    • Day Care: Coordinating services in community (e.g., Kibera Day care).
    • Nursing Homes in Kenya: Nyumba ya wazee (Nairobi/Mombasa), Missionaries of Charity.
  • C) Stipends: Monthly stipends and NHIF cover for needy elderly (>70 years).
  • D) Government Partnership: Teaming up with homes for the poor.
  • E) Health: Access to HIV/AIDS services, sanitation, and incorporation of geriatrics in learning institutions.
  • F) Community Organizations: CBOs, peer groups, CHWs.
