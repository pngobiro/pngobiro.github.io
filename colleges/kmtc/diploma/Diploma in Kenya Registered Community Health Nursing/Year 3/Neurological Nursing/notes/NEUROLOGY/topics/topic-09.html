üò´ Trigeminal Neuralgia (Tic Douloureux)

A condition of the 5th Cranial Nerve (Trigeminal) characterized by paroxysms of pain in the areas innervated by the nerve branches (most commonly 2nd and 3rd branches).

‚ö°

Pain Characteristics

Pain ends as abruptly as it starts. Described as unilateral shooting and stabbing sensation. Involuntary contraction of facial muscles can cause twitching ("tic").

Triggers

Paroxysms can occur with stimulation of nerve terminals by:

  • Washing the face, shaving, or brushing teeth.
  • Eating at extreme temperatures.
  • Drafts of cold air.

Management

  • Pharmacologic: Antiseizure agents like Carbamazepine (Tegretol) reduce transmission of impulses. Taken with meals. Gabapentin/Baclofen are alternatives.
  • Surgical: Microvascular Decompression or Percutaneous Radiofrequency Trigeminal Gangliolysis.
  • Nursing: Teach patient to avoid triggers (room temperature water/food, protect from cold wind).

üëÅÔ∏è Bell's Palsy

Caused by unilateral inflammation of the 7th Cranial Nerve (Facial), resulting in weakness or paralysis of facial muscles on the affected side. Possible causes include vascular ischemia, viral disease (HSV, Herpes Zoster), or autoimmune disease.

Clinical Manifestations

  • Facial Distortion: Paralysis of muscles, flattening of nasolabial fold.
  • Eye: Inability to close eye, increased lacrimation (tearing).
  • Pain: Face, behind ear, and eye.
  • Speech/Eating: Difficulty speaking and eating on affected side.

Management

  • Reassurance: Spontaneous recovery usually occurs within 3 to 5 weeks. It is NOT a stroke.
  • Medication: Corticosteroids (Prednisone) to reduce inflammation/edema. Analgesics for pain.
  • Eye Care: Protect the eye from injury (patch, sunglasses, artificial tears) since the eyelid cannot close.

üìâ Myasthenia Gravis (MG)

An autoimmune disorder affecting the myoneural junction characterized by varying degrees of weakness of voluntary muscles.

‚ÑπÔ∏è

Pathophysiology

Autoantibodies attack acetylcholine receptor sites at the neuromuscular junction, impairing transmission of impulses. Fewer receptors are available for stimulation, resulting in weakness that escalates with continued activity.

Clinical Manifestations

  • Ocular (Initial): Diplopia (double vision) and Ptosis (drooping eyelids).
  • Bulbar Symptoms: Weakness of face and throat muscles. Bland facial expression.
  • Laryngeal: Dysphonia (voice impairment), risk of choking/aspiration.
  • General: Weakness of extremities and intercostal muscles.
  • Note: Purely motor disorder; no sensory loss.

Diagnostics

Tensilon Test (Edrophonium Chloride):

  • Acetylcholinesterase inhibitor injected IV.
  • Positive Result: Facial muscle weakness and ptosis resolve for about 5 minutes.
  • Antidote: Atropine (must be available for side effects like bradycardia).

Medical Management

Treatment Action
Anticholinesterase Agents Pyridostigmine (Mestinon), Neostigmine. Increase available acetylcholine.
Immunosuppressives Corticosteroids, Azathioprine. Reduce antibody production.
Plasmapheresis Plasma exchange to remove circulating antibodies (used in exacerbations).
Thymectomy Removal of thymus gland (site of antibody production).

Myasthenic Crisis

An exacerbation characterized by severe generalized muscle weakness and respiratory/bulbar weakness that may result in respiratory failure.

Precipitators & Management

Causes: Infection (most common), medication change, surgery, pregnancy.

Management: Respiratory support (ventilation), suctioning, nutritional support, ABG monitoring.
