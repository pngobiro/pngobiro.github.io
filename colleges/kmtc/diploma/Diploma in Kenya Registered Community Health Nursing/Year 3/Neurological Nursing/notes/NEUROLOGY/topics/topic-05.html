ü§ï Head Injuries

Head injury is a broad classification including injury to the scalp, skull, or brain. It is the most common cause of death from trauma.

‚ÑπÔ∏è

Classification of Damage

Primary Injury: Initial damage from the traumatic event (contusions, lacerations, torn blood vessels).

Secondary Injury: Evolves over hours/days due to brain swelling or bleeding.

Pathophysiology Cycle

  1. Brain suffers traumatic injury.
  2. Brain swelling or bleeding increases intracranial volume.
  3. Rigid cranium prevents expansion ‚Üí ICP Increases.
  4. Pressure on blood vessels slows cerebral blood flow.
  5. Cerebral hypoxia and ischemia occur.
  6. ICP continues to rise ‚Üí Potential Brain Herniation ‚Üí Cessation of blood flow ‚Üí Brain Death.

Types of Brain Injuries

‚ñ∂ Concussion (Minor)

Temporary loss of neurologic function with no apparent structural damage.

  • Symptoms: Dizziness, "seeing stars", or brief loss of consciousness (seconds).
‚ñ∂ Contusion (Severe)

Brain is bruised with possible surface hemorrhage. Unconsciousness lasts more than a few seconds/minutes.

  • Signs: Motionless, faint pulse, shallow respirations, cool/pale skin, involuntary bowel/bladder evacuation.
‚ñ∂ Intracranial Hemorrhage (Hematomas)

Most serious injuries. Collections of blood within the cranial vault.

  • Epidural: Above the dura.
  • Subdural: Below the dura.
  • Intracerebral: Within the brain.

Note: Symptoms often delayed until hematoma is large enough to distort brain/increase ICP.

ü©∫ Management of Head Injuries

Clinical Manifestations

  • Altered LOC, confusion, sudden neurologic deficits.
  • Pupillary abnormalities, absent gag/corneal reflexes.
  • Vital Sign Changes: Altered respiratory pattern, hypertension, bradycardia/tachycardia, hypothermia/hyperthermia.
  • Sensory dysfunction (vision/hearing), spasticity, seizures.
  • Headache, vertigo, movement disorders.
üö®

Critical Safety Alert

Any individual with a head injury is presumed to have a cervical spine injury until proven otherwise. The patient must be transported on a board with head and neck maintained in alignment.

Medical Management

  • Manage ICP: Treat brain swelling and bleeding.
  • Surgery: Evacuate blood clots, debridement/elevation of fractures, suture lacerations.
  • Supportive Care: Ventilatory support (intubation for comatose patients), seizure prevention (antiseizure drugs), fluid/electrolyte maintenance, nutritional support.
  • GI: NG tube (unless contraindicated) to manage reduced motility/reverse peristalsis.

Nursing Diagnoses

  • Ineffective airway clearance / Impaired gas exchange
  • Ineffective cerebral tissue perfusion (increased ICP)
  • Deficient fluid volume
  • Imbalanced nutrition
  • Risk for injury (seizures/disorientation)

ü¶¥ Spinal Cord Injury (SCI)

Major health problem, occurring 4x more often in males. Predominant risk factors: Age, Gender, Alcohol/Drug use.

Most Frequently Involved Vertebrae

These areas are most susceptible due to greater range of mobility:

  • Cervical: C5, C6, C7
  • Thoracic: T12
  • Lumbar: L1

Clinical Manifestations

Type Description
Complete Lesion Total loss of sensory and motor function below injury. Results in Paraplegia (lower body) or Quadriplegia (all four extremities).
Incomplete Lesion Some motor/sensory function preserved below neurologic level.
Respiratory Dysfunction related to level. High cervical injury = Acute respiratory failure (leading cause of death).

Emergency Management

Goal: Prevent an incomplete injury from becoming complete.

  • Immobilization on spinal (back) board.
  • Head and neck in neutral position.
  • Avoid flexing/extending neck during intubation.

Medical Management

  • Resuscitation: Maintain oxygenation and cardiovascular stability.
  • Medication: High-dose corticosteroids (Methylprednisolone) to improve outcomes.
  • Stabilization: Reduction of dislocations, skeletal traction (tongs/calipers).

Surgical Indications

  • Cord compression evident.
  • Fragmented/unstable vertebral body.
  • Penetrating wound to the cord.
  • Bony fragments in spinal canal.
  • Deteriorating neurologic status.

Potential Complications

  • Neurogenic shock
  • Deep Venous Thrombosis (DVT)
  • Respiratory failure / Pneumonia
  • Autonomic Dysreflexia

üìã Nursing Process: SCI

Assessment

  • Breathing: Observe pattern, cough strength, auscultate lungs (paralysis of abdominal/respiratory muscles diminishes cough).
  • Neurologic: Monitor closely for changes in motor/sensory function or progressive damage.

Nursing Diagnoses

  • Ineffective breathing patterns related to weakness/paralysis of muscles.
  • Ineffective airway clearance related to weak cough.
  • Impaired physical mobility related to motor/sensory impairment.
  • Disturbed sensory perception related to impairment.
  • Risk for impaired skin integrity related to immobility/sensory loss.
