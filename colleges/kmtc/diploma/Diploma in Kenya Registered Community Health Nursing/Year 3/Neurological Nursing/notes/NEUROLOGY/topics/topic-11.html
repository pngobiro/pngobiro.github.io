üß† Classification & Pathophysiology

A brain tumor is a localized intracranial lesion that occupies space within the skull. They can grow as a spherical mass or diffusely infiltrate tissue.

Primary vs. Secondary

Primary Tumors: Originate from cells and structures within the brain.

Secondary (Metastatic) Tumors: Develop from structures outside the brain (e.g., lung, breast). Occur in 20% to 40% of all cancer patients.

Types of Primary Tumors

  • Gliomas: Originate within brain tissue. Infiltrate any portion of the brain; most common type.
  • Meningiomas: Arise from the coverings of the brain (dural meningioma).
  • Acoustic Neuromas: Develop on cranial nerves (CN VIII).
  • Angiomas: Masses composed largely of abnormal blood vessels found on the surface or within the brain.
  • Pituitary/Pineal Tumors: Arise from the respective glands.

Pathophysiologic Events

The effects of neoplasms occur from compression and infiltration of tissue, leading to:

  • Increased Intracranial Pressure (ICP) and cerebral edema.
  • Seizure activity and focal neurologic signs.
  • Hydrocephalus.
  • Altered pituitary function.

ü©∫ Clinical Manifestations

Symptoms can be generalized (reflecting increased ICP) or focal (reflecting the specific brain region affected).

Symptom Category Specific Manifestations
Generalized (ICP) Headache (worse in morning), vomiting (projectile), papilledema, personality changes.
Localized/Focal
  • Motor: Hemiparesis, seizures (focal or generalized).
  • Sensory: Abnormalities in sensation, visual alterations.
  • Cognitive: Mental status changes, language disturbances (aphasia).

Assessment & Diagnostics

  • History: Key component. Time frame and evolution of symptoms.
  • Neurologic Exam: Indicates areas of CNS involvement.
  • CT Scan: Specifics on number, size, and density of lesions.
  • MRI: Highly useful for diagnosis.
  • Cerebral Angiography: Visualizes blood vessels; localizes tumors.
  • EEG: Detects abnormal brain waves (e.g., temporal lobe seizures).

üíä Medical & Surgical Management

Medical Management

  • Chemotherapy & Radiation: Used alone or with surgery. Radiation decreases recurrence of incompletely resected tumors.
  • Corticosteroids: (e.g., Dexamethasone) Reduce cerebral edema/inflammation before and after treatment.
  • Osmotic Diuretics: (e.g., Mannitol) Decrease brain fluid content.
  • Antiseizure Agents: (e.g., Phenytoin) Prevent and treat seizures.

Surgical Management

Objective: Remove or destroy the entire tumor without increasing neurologic deficit, or relieve symptoms by partial removal (decompression).

Craniotomy

Surgical opening of the skull. Approaches include supratentorial, infratentorial, and transsphenoidal.

Nursing Management

Preoperative

  • Assess gag reflex and ability to swallow (risk of aspiration due to cranial nerve dysfunction).
  • Teach patient to direct food to unaffected side, sit upright, use semisoft diet.
  • Suctioning equipment available at bedside.

Postoperative

  • Monitoring: Vital signs, Neuro checks (LOC, motor, sensory, pupils), ICP signs.
  • Safety: Reorient frequently (person, time, place). Protect from injury if cognitive changes present.
  • Positioning: Elevate head of bed (unless contraindicated) to promote venous drainage.
04

üîÑ Cerebral Metastases

Secondary tumors originating from elsewhere in the body (Lung, Breast, Kidney, etc.).

Neurologic Deficits

  • Headache, gait disturbances.
  • Visual impairment, personality changes.
  • Altered mentation (memory loss, confusion).
  • Focal weakness, paralysis, aphasia, seizures.

Palliative Management

Goal is to eliminate or reduce serious symptoms.

  • Radiation Therapy: Cornerstone of treatment.
  • Surgery: For single metastasis.
  • Pharmacologic: Corticosteroids (relieve headache/edema), Antiseizure agents, Analgesics (morphine for severe pain).

Complications

Venous Thromboembolism (VTE): DVT and PE occur in about 15% of patients causing significant morbidity.
