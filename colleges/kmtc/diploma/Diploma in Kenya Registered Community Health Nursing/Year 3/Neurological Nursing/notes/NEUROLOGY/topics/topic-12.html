üß† Overview & Classification

Seizures are episodes of abnormal motor, sensory, autonomic, or psychic activity resulting from sudden excessive discharge from cerebral neurons.

Epilepsy is a group of syndromes characterized by recurring seizures.

Classification

Partial Seizures: Begin in one part of the brain. Can be Simple (no loss of consciousness) or Complex (impaired consciousness/automatisms).

Generalized Seizures: Involve the whole brain (both hemispheres). Includes Tonic-Clonic (Grand Mal), Tonic, Clonic, Absence (Petit Mal), and Myoclonic.

Causes (Etiology)

  • Idiopathic: Genetic, developmental defects.
  • Acquired: Hypoxemia, fever (childhood), head injury, hypertension, CNS infections, metabolic/toxic conditions (drug withdrawal), brain tumors.
02

üìâ Stages of Generalized (Grand Mal) Seizures

These involve both hemispheres, causing bilateral body reaction.

  1. Prodrome Stage: Days or hours before. Characterized by irritability or hyperactivity.
  2. Aura (Warning): Unusual feelings (peculiar sensation, funny smell, nausea, flashing light).
  3. Tonic Stage (10-20 sec): Muscle rigidity, eyes look up, respiration stops (cyanosis). Tongue biting may occur.
  4. Clonic Stage (~30 sec): Muscle relaxation interrupts tonic contraction. Brief violent muscle spasms, frothing at mouth, incontinence.
  5. Ictal Phase: Deep coma for minutes or hours.
  6. Post-ictal Stage: Recovery. Patient is confused, hard to arouse, may sleep for hours. Complains of headache, sore muscles, fatigue.
03

üíä Medical Management

Goal: Achieve seizure control with minimal side effects. Therapy controls rather than cures.

Common Antiseizure Drugs

Carbamazepine (Tegretol) Clonazepam (Klonopin)
Phenytoin (Dilantin) Phenobarbital (Luminal)
Valproate (Depakote) Levetiracetam (Keppra)
Gabapentin (Neurontin) Lamotrigine (Lamictal)
Topiramate (Topamax) Diazepam/Lorazepam (Emergency)

Side Effects Categories:

  • Idiosyncratic/Allergic (skin reactions).
  • Acute Toxicity (at initial prescription).
  • Chronic Toxicity (late in therapy).

Surgical Management

Indicated for tumors, abscesses, cysts, or intractable epilepsy not responding to medication (focal resection).

Assessment Data

  • History of events (precipitating factors).
  • Presence of Aura.
  • Type of movements and areas involved.
  • Pupil size, eye deviation.
  • Automatisms (lip smacking, swallowing).
  • Incontinence.
  • Duration of each phase.
  • Post-seizure status (confusion, weakness, ability to speak).

Care During a Seizure

üö®

Safety First

  • Provide Privacy.
  • Ease to floor if possible; protect head with pad.
  • Loosen constrictive clothing.
  • Push aside furniture.
  • Do NOT restrain the patient.
  • Do NOT pry open clenched jaws.
  • Place on side with head flexed forward to prevent aspiration.

Care After Seizure (Post-ictal)

  • Keep patient on side (patent airway).
  • Reorient to environment on awakening.
  • If agitated, use calm persuasion.

‚ö†Ô∏è Status Epilepticus

Definition: Acute prolonged seizure activity (series of generalized seizures) without full recovery of consciousness between attacks.

Medical Emergency: Vigorous muscle contractions impose heavy metabolic demand and interfere with respiration, leading to cerebral hypoxia and potential irreversible brain damage.

Precipitators: Withdrawal of antiseizure meds, fever, infection.

Emergency Management

  • Airway: Establish airway and oxygenation. Intubation if unconscious.
  • Meds (Immediate): IV Diazepam (Valium) or Lorazepam to halt seizures.
  • Meds (Maintenance): Phenytoin or Phenobarbital later.
  • Other: IV Dextrose if hypoglycemia is cause. General anesthesia if unsuccessful.

Nursing Interventions

  • Continuous monitoring of respiratory and cardiac function (risk of depression from sedatives).
  • Side positioning for drainage.
  • Suction equipment available.
  • Protect IV line during seizures.
