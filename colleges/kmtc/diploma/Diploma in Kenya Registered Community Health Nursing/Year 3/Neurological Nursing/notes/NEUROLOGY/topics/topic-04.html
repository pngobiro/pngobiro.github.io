üö´ Ischemic Stroke

Pathophysiology: The Ischemic Cascade

Begins when cerebral blood flow falls to less than 25 mL/100 g/min.

  1. Neurons cannot maintain aerobic respiration.
  2. Mitochondria switch to anaerobic respiration.
  3. Generation of large amounts of lactic acid (pH change).
  4. Insufficient energy production leading to cell death (infarction).

Types of Ischemic Stroke

  • Large Artery Thrombotic: Atherosclerotic plaques.
  • Small Penetrating Artery: Most common type.
  • Cardiogenic Embolic: Associated with cardiac dysrhythmias (usually atrial fibrillation).
  • Cryptogenic: No known cause.

Clinical Manifestations

‚ñ∂ Visual Deficits
  • Hemianopsia: Loss of half of the visual field.
  • Loss of peripheral vision.
  • Diplopia.
‚ñ∂ Motor Deficits
  • Hemiparesis: Weakness on one side.
  • Hemiplegia: Paralysis on one side.
  • Ataxia (unsteady gait).
  • Dysphagia (difficulty swallowing).
‚ñ∂ Verbal Deficits
  • Expressive Aphasia: Difficulty speaking.
  • Receptive Aphasia: Difficulty understanding.
‚ñ∂ Sensory & Cognitive

Sensory: Paresthesia (occurs on side opposite the lesion).

Cognitive: Memory loss, decreased attention span, poor abstract reasoning, altered judgment.

Emotional: Loss of self-control, depression, withdrawal.

Medical Management

Thrombolytic Therapy (t-PA)

Recombinant tissue plasminogen activator (t-PA) binds to fibrin and converts plasminogen to plasmin, stimulating fibrinolysis.

  • Dosage: 0.9 mg/kg (Maximum dose 90 mg).
  • Monitoring: Vital signs every 15 min for 2 hrs, then every 30 min for 6 hrs, then hourly for 16 hrs.

Non-t-PA Management

  • Anticoagulants (IV heparin or LMWH).
  • Reduce ICP (Osmotic diuretics like Mannitol).
  • Maintain PaCO2 within normal range.
  • Elevate head of bed.
  • Hemodynamic monitoring (Systolic < 180 mmHg, Diastolic < 100 mmHg).

üí• Hemorrhagic Stroke & Aneurysm

Caused by bleeding into brain tissue, ventricles, or subarachnoid space.

  • Primary: Spontaneous rupture of small vessels (80% due to uncontrolled hypertension).
  • Secondary: Associated with AVMs, aneurysms, or medications.

Intracranial (Cerebral) Aneurysm

Dilation of the walls of a cerebral artery due to weakness in the arterial wall.

Symptoms of Rupture

  • Sudden, unusually severe headache.
  • Loss of consciousness.
  • Nuchal rigidity (pain/rigidity of back of neck/spine).
  • Visual disturbances (if adjacent to oculomotor nerve).

Medical Management

  • Goals: Recover from initial insult, prevent rebleeding, prevent complications.
  • Bed rest with sedation.
  • Management of vasospasm.
  • Analgesics (Codeine, Acetaminophen).
  • Compression stockings (DVT prevention).
üõë

Aneurysm Precautions

  1. Immediate and absolute bed rest in a quiet, non-stressful environment.
  2. Elevate head of bed 15 to 30 degrees.
  3. Restrict visitors (family only).
  4. Nurse administers all personal care (feeding, bathing) to prevent exertion.
  5. Dim lighting; no TV or radio.

üìù Nursing Process

Assessment

  • Change in LOC or responsiveness.
  • Presence of voluntary/involuntary movements.
  • Eye opening, pupil size and reaction.
  • Stiffness or flaccidity of the neck.

Diagnoses

  • Ineffective cerebral tissue perfusion related to bleeding/edema.
  • Impaired physical mobility related to hemiparesis.
  • Acute pain (shoulder) related to hemiplegia.
  • Self-care deficits related to stroke sequelae.
  • Disturbed sensory perception related to altered integration.

Potential Complications

  • Inadequate oxygen delivery to the brain.
  • Pneumonia.
  • Vasospasm.
  • Rebleeding.
  • Hydrocephalus.
