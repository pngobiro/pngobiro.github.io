üåê Nervous System Overview

The nervous system controls all motor, sensory, autonomic, cognitive, and behavioral activities. It consists of two main divisions:

  • Central Nervous System (CNS): Consisting of the brain and spinal cord.
  • Peripheral Nervous System (PNS): Consisting of the cranial and spinal nerves.

The Peripheral Nervous System is further divided into:

  • Somatic (Voluntary): Conveys sensory information to the CNS and transmits motor messages to the body (31 spinal nerves, 12 cranial nerves).
  • Autonomic (Involuntary): Regulates automatic behaviors (heart rate, BP, respiration, digestion).

Autonomic Nervous System Divisions

System Sympathetic Parasympathetic
Function Prepares organs for rigorous activity ("Fight or Flight"). Facilitates vegetative, non-emergency responses ("Rest and Digest").
Effect Increases heart rate, BP, respiration. Decreases functions increased by sympathetic system.
Neurotransmitter Mainly Norepinephrine. Mainly Acetylcholine.

‚ö° Neurons & Neurotransmitters

The basic functional unit of the brain is the neuron. It is composed of:

  • Cell Body (Soma): Contains the nucleus and metabolic machinery.
  • Dendrites: Branch-type structures that receive electrochemical messages.
  • Axon: Long projection carrying impulses away from the cell body.

Key Terms

Ganglia/Nuclei: Clusters of nerve cell bodies.

Center: A cluster of cell bodies with the same function (e.g., respiratory center).

Neuroglia: Supportive cells that protect and nourish neurons (90% of brain cells).

Neurotransmitters

Chemicals that communicate messages across the synaptic cleft. They can be excitatory or inhibitory.

Neurotransmitter Function/Association
Acetylcholine Major transmitter of the parasympathetic nervous system.
Serotonin Mood, sleep, appetite. Low levels associated with epilepsy.
Dopamine Movement, reward. Decreased in Parkinson's disease.
Norepinephrine Major transmitter of the sympathetic nervous system.
GABA Gamma-aminobutyric acid. Major inhibitory neurotransmitter. Lack associated with Huntington's disease.

üß† Anatomy of the Brain

The brain is divided into three major areas: the Cerebrum, Brain Stem, and Cerebellum.

üîç Click to zoom

Fig 1.3: Major divisions of the brain

1. Cerebrum

Composed of two hemispheres separated by the longitudinal fissure and connected by the corpus callosum. The surface is folded into gyri (convolutions) and sulci (grooves) to increase surface area.

‚ñ∂ Lobes of the Cerebrum
  • Frontal Lobe: Concentration, abstract thought, memory, motor function. Contains Broca's area (motor speech).
  • Parietal Lobe: Sensory analysis and interpretation. Awareness of body in space.
  • Temporal Lobe: Auditory reception. Contains interpretive area for integration.
  • Occipital Lobe: Visual interpretation.
üîç Click to zoom

Fig 1.4: Regions of the Human Brain

2. Diencephalon Structures

  • Thalamus: Relay station for all sensation except smell.
  • Hypothalamus: Regulates autonomic nervous system, endocrine system (pituitary), hunger, thirst, temperature, and sleep-wake cycles.
  • Basal Ganglia: Control of fine motor movements.

3. Brain Stem

  • Midbrain: Center for auditory and visual reflexes. Origins of CN III & IV.
  • Pons: Bridge between cerebellum halves and medulla/cerebrum. Controls respiration. Origins of CN V-VIII.
  • Medulla Oblongata: Decussation (crossing) of motor fibers. Centers for respiration, heart rate, swallowing, vomiting. Origins of CN IX-XII.

4. Cerebellum

Responsible for coordination of movement, balance, fine movement, and position sense (proprioception).

üõ°Ô∏è Protection of the CNS

Meninges

Fibrous connective tissues covering the brain and spinal cord:

  1. Dura Mater: Outermost, tough, fibrous layer.
  2. Arachnoid Mater: Middle, web-like layer. Contains villi for CSF absorption.
  3. Pia Mater: Innermost, delicate layer closely adhering to the brain.

Blood-Brain Barrier (BBB)

A semipermeable barrier formed by tight junctions of capillaries and astrocytes. It prevents proteins and pathogens from entering the brain while limiting exchange between blood and brain ECF.

ü¶¥ Spinal Cord & Vertebral Column

The spinal cord extends from the foramen magnum to L1/L2, terminating at the conus medullaris. It is protected by the vertebral column.

Vertebral Column Segments

  • Cervical: 7 vertebrae
  • Thoracic: 12 vertebrae
  • Lumbar: 5 vertebrae
  • Sacrum: 5 fused vertebrae
  • Coccyx: 4 fused vertebrae

Nerve Roots

Dorsal Root: Sensory (Afferent) - transmits sensation from body to CNS.

Ventral Root: Motor (Efferent) - transmits impulses from CNS to muscles/glands.
