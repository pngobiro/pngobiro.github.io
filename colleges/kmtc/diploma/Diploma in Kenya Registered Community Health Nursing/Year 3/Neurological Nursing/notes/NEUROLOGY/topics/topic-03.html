üí§ Altered Level of Consciousness (LOC)

An altered level of consciousness (LOC) refers to a state in which the patient is not oriented, does not follow commands, or needs persistent stimuli to achieve a state of alertness. It is gauged on a continuum from alertness to coma.

üìñ

Key Definitions

  • Coma: Clinical state of unconsciousness where the patient is unaware of self or environment for prolonged periods (days to months/years).
  • Akinetic Mutism: State of unresponsiveness to the environment; patient makes no movement or sound but sometimes opens eyes.
  • Persistent Vegetative State: Condition where patient is wakeful but devoid of conscious content, without cognitive or affective mental function.

Pathophysiology

Altered LOC is not a disorder itself but a symptom of multiple pathophysiologic phenomena. Causes include:

  • Neurologic: Head injury, stroke.
  • Toxicologic: Drug overdose, alcohol intoxication.
  • Metabolic: Hepatic/renal failure, diabetic ketoacidosis.

Disruptions can occur in neurons, neurotransmitters, or brain anatomy (hemispheres, lobes, brain stem).

Medical Management

First Priority: Obtain and maintain a patent airway.

  • Intubation/Tracheostomy/Mechanical ventilation for oxygenation.
  • Circulatory status monitoring (BP, HR) to ensure perfusion.
  • IV access for fluids and medications.
  • Nutritional support (feeding tube/gastrostomy).

Comprehensive nursing interventions are required to maintain physiologic stability and prevent complications.

‚ñ∂ Maintaining the Airway
  • Elevate head of bed to 30 degrees.
  • Position in lateral or semiprone position to allow jaw/tongue to fall forward and promote drainage.
  • Suctioning and oral hygiene.
  • Chest physiotherapy and postural drainage (unless contraindicated).
  • Auscultate chest every 8 hours.
‚ñ∂ Protecting the Patient
  • Padded side rails raised at all times.
  • Prevent injury from invasive lines/equipment.
  • Protect patient dignity (privacy, speaking to patient).
‚ñ∂ Fluid Balance & Nutrition
  • Assess hydration (turgor, mucous membranes, I&O, labs).
  • Administer IV fluids slowly (rapid administration may increase ICP).
  • Tube feedings as prescribed.
‚ñ∂ Skin, Mouth, & Eye Care

Mouth: Cleanse and rinse carefully; petrolatum on lips. Move ET tube daily to prevent ulceration.

Skin: Regular turning schedule to prevent breakdown/necrosis.

Eyes: Cleanse with cotton balls/saline. Artificial tears every 2 hours if corneal reflex is absent.

‚ñ∂ Thermoregulation

High fever increases metabolic demand. Strategies:

  • Remove bedding.
  • Administer acetaminophen.
  • Cool sponge bath / electric fan.
  • Hypothermia blanket.

üìà Increased Intracranial Pressure (ICP)

Normal ICP: 10 to 20 mm Hg.

Monro-Kellie Hypothesis: The cranial vault contains brain tissue (1,400 g), blood (75 mL), and CSF (75 mL). Because space is limited, an increase in one component causes a change in the volume of the others.

Clinical Manifestations

Early Signs Later Signs
  • Disorientation, restlessness, confusion
  • Increasing drowsiness
  • Pupillary changes (sluggish)
  • Headache (constant, increasing intensity)
  • Weakness in one extremity
  • Comatose state
  • Cushing's Triad: Increased systolic BP, Decreased pulse, Irregular respirations
  • Altered respiratory patterns (Cheyne-Stokes)
  • Projectile vomiting
  • Loss of brain stem reflexes
  • Hemiplegia

Management of Increased ICP

Goals: Decrease cerebral edema, lower CSF volume, or decrease cerebral blood volume.

  • Osmotic Diuretics: Mannitol to dehydrate brain tissue.
  • Corticosteroids: Dexamethasone to reduce edema.
  • Fluid Restriction: To minimize cerebral edema.
  • CSF Drainage: Via ventriculostomy.
  • Control Fever: To reduce metabolic demand.
  • Oxygenation: Maintain systemic BP and oxygenation.

Monitoring ICP

Invasive monitoring identifies pressure early. Devices include intraventricular catheters (ventriculostomy), subarachnoid bolts, or fiberoptic transducer-tipped catheters. Ventriculostomy allows for both monitoring and CSF drainage.

üî™ Intracranial Surgery

Craniotomy: Opening the skull surgically to remove tumors, relieve ICP, evacuate clots, or control hemorrhage.

Approaches

  • Supratentorial: Above the tentorium.
  • Infratentorial: Below the tentorium.
  • Transsphenoidal: Through mouth/nasal sinuses (for pituitary gland).
  • Burr Holes: Circular openings for exploration, diagnosis, or evacuation of hematomas.

Nursing Management

Preoperative

  • Assess LOC and neurologic deficits.
  • Administer antiseizure meds (Phenytoin), corticosteroids (Dexamethasone), diuretics (Furosemide), and antibiotics.
  • Shave surgical site immediately before surgery.
  • Insert indwelling catheter and central/arterial lines.

Postoperative

  • Monitor ICP, vital signs, and arterial blood gases.
  • Detect/reduce cerebral edema (Mannitol, Dexamethasone).
  • Relieve pain (Acetaminophen for fever; Codeine/Morphine for headache).
  • Prevent seizures (Phenytoin).
  • Assess respiratory function (essential).
  • Inspect dressing for bleeding or CSF drainage.
‚ùó

Positioning

Elevate head of bed 30 cm (12 in) after patient is conscious to aid venous drainage. Place in semiprone position to facilitate gas exchange until consciousness returns (unless contraindicated).
