üìã Health History & Manifestations

An important aspect of the neurologic assessment is the history of the present illness. This includes observing overall appearance, mental status, posture, movement, and affect.

Key areas of inquiry include:

  • Details of symptoms: onset, character, severity, location, duration, frequency.
  • Precipitating, aggravating, and relieving factors.
  • Family history of genetic diseases.
  • History of trauma or falls involving the head or spinal cord.
  • Use of alcohol, medications, and recreational drugs.

Common Clinical Manifestations

‚ñ∂ Pain & Sensation

Assess for pain and abnormal sensations such as numbness or loss of sensation.

‚ñ∂ Seizures

Result from abnormal paroxysmal discharges in the cerebral cortex, manifesting as alterations in sensation, behavior, movement, perception, or consciousness.

‚ñ∂ Dizziness & Vertigo

Dizziness: Abnormal sensation of imbalance or movement.

Vertigo: Sensation of rotation or spinning, usually a manifestation of vestibular dysfunction.

‚ñ∂ Visual Disturbances

Lesions of the eye, pathway, or cortex interfere with visual acuity. Abnormal eye movements (e.g., nystagmus) can cause diplopia (double vision).

‚ñ∂ Weakness

Muscle weakness is a common manifestation of neurologic disease.

üß† Assessing Cerebral Function

The assessment is divided into five components: Cerebral function, Cranial nerves, Motor system, Sensory system, and Reflexes.

Mental Status & Intellectual Function

Begins by observing appearance, behavior, dress, grooming, and personal hygiene.

  • Orientation: Time, place, and person.
  • Intellectual Function: A person with average IQ can subtract seven digits without faltering from 100 and recite five digits backward.
  • Thought Content: Are thoughts spontaneous, natural, clear, relevant, and coherent?
  • Emotional Status: Assess affect (external manifestation of mood).

Perception & Language

üëÅÔ∏è Cranial Nerve Examination

  • I (Olfactory): With eyes closed, identify familiar odors (coffee, tobacco).
  • II (Optic): Visual acuity (Snellen chart), visual fields, ophthalmoscopic exam.
  • III, IV, VI (Oculomotor, Trochlear, Abducens): Tested as a unit. Eyelid movement (check for ptosis), eyeball movement.
  • V (Trigeminal):
    Sensory: Touch/pain on face (forehead, cheek, jaw). Corneal reflex.
    Motor: Clench teeth, open mouth (symmetry), jaw jerk.
  • VII (Facial): Symmetry of facial movements (smile, whistle, frown, close eyes tightly). Taste (anterior 2/3 tongue).
  • VIII (Acoustic/Vestibulocochlear): Hearing (whisper test, Weber/Rinne tests) and equilibrium.
  • IX & X (Glossopharyngeal & Vagus): Gag reflex, swallowing, "Ah" sound (uvula rise), taste (posterior 1/3).
  • XI (Spinal Accessory): Shrug shoulders (trapezius), turn head against resistance (sternocleidomastoid).
  • XII (Hypoglossal): Protrude tongue (check deviation/tremors), tongue strength.

Balance and Coordination

Assesses cerebellar function. Tests include rapid alternating movements and point-to-point testing.

Romberg Test

Screening test for balance. Patient stands with feet together, arms at side, eyes open then closed for 20-30 seconds. Positive Romberg: Loss of balance (slight swaying is normal).

Sensory Examination

Largely subjective. Performed with patient's eyes closed.

  • Tactile Sensation: Cotton wisp.
  • Superficial Pain: Sharp vs. dull object.
  • Vibration: Tuning fork on bony prominence.
  • Proprioception (Position Sense): Moving toe up/down.

üî¨ Diagnostic Tests

  • Computed Tomography (CT): Uses x-rays for cross-sectional views. Shows tissue density variations (tumors, infarction, hemorrhage, atrophy).
  • Positron Emission Tomography (PET): Measures metabolic activity using radioactive substances. Useful for Alzheimer's, locating lesions, and blood flow measurement.
  • Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI): Uses magnetic fields (no radiation). Provides clearer images than CT. Excellent for MS, tumors, and chemical changes.
  • Cerebral Angiography: X-ray study of cerebral circulation with contrast dye. Investigates vascular disease, aneurysms, AVMs.
  • Myelography: X-ray of spinal subarachnoid space with contrast. Shows spinal cord distortion, herniated discs, tumors.
  • Electroencephalography (EEG): Records electrical activity of the brain using scalp electrodes. Assessment for seizures and coma.

Lumbar Puncture (Spinal Tap)

Insertion of a needle into the lumbar subarachnoid space (between L3-L4 or L4-L5) to withdraw CSF.

‚ö†Ô∏è

Contraindications

Contraindicated in patients with increased intracranial pressure (ICP) as it may cause brain herniation through the foramen magnum.

Indications

  • Obtain CSF for examination (cell count, culture, glucose, protein).
  • Measure/reduce CSF pressure.
  • Detect blood in CSF.
  • Administer antibiotics intrathecally.

Nursing Care

  • Pre-procedure: Obtain consent, explain procedure, instruct patient to void.
  • Procedure: Position patient on side with knees flexed (fetal position) to open vertebral spaces. Maintain immobility.
  • Post-procedure: Lie prone for 2-3 hours to reduce leakage. Encourage fluids. Monitor for complications.

Complications

Post-lumbar puncture headache: Most common (15-30%). Throbbing, worsens when sitting/standing. Managed by bed rest, hydration, analgesics, or epidural blood patch.
