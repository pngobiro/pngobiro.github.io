üíß Hydrocephalus

An abnormal condition involving fluid accumulation around the brain or inside the ventricles, usually resulting from interference with CSF circulation or absorption.

Type Pathophysiology Common Causes
Communicating
(Extra-ventricular)		 Obstruction is outside the ventricular system. Blockage of subarachnoid cisterns preventing absorption. Subarachnoid hemorrhage, meningitis, toxoplasmosis, head injury.
Non-Communicating Obstruction is within the ventricular system, blocking flow to the subarachnoid space. Congenital defects (Arnold-Chiari, aqueduct stenosis), tumors, cysts.

Management

  • Medical: Acetazolamide (Diamox) to reduce CSF production (mild cases). Repeated lumbar punctures.
  • Surgical: Removal of obstructions (tumors/cysts) or Ventriculostomy (Shunting).

Shunting Procedures

A radio-opaque ventricular catheter with a valve is inserted to shunt CSF from the ventricle to another area (usually the abdominal cavity/peritoneum). Common systems: Spitz-Holter, Hakim, Heyer-Schulte Pudenz.

Nursing Care

‚ñ∂ Preoperative Care
  • Monitor signs of increased ICP.
  • Frequently measure head circumference (OFC).
  • Palpate fontanelles for tension/bulging.
  • Change position every 2 hours to prevent sores.
  • Support the neck when moving the child.
‚ñ∂ Postoperative Care
  • Monitor vital signs and ICP signs hourly.
  • Positioning: Lie on the unoperated side to prevent pressure on the shunt valve.
  • Check dressing for leakage.
  • Monitor for complications: Meningitis, vomiting, shunt infection/blockage.

üß¨ Congenital Defects

Microcephaly

A defect in brain growth where the brain size is significantly smaller (3x) than normal.

  • Predisposing Factors: Fetal radiation, Maternal phenylketonuria, Congenital infections (Rubella, Zika, Syphilis), Anoxia.
  • Features: Small head, sloping forehead, relatively large ears.
  • Management: No cure available. Supportive care for parents.

Spina Bifida

A defect in the formation of the skeletal arch enclosing the spinal cord (posterior lamina fails to fuse). Common in the lumbar region.

Nursing Care (Pre- & Post-Op)

  • Prevent Infection: Use aseptic technique. Dress sac with warm sterile saline; change every 4 hours.
  • Prevent Injury: Protect the sac from pressure or trauma.
  • Assessment: Monitor vital signs, neurological status, and musculoskeletal function.

‚ôø Cerebral Palsy (CP)

A group of disorders affecting movement, muscle tone, or posture caused by damage to the immature, developing brain (usually before birth).

Clinical Manifestations

‚ñ∂ Movement & Coordination
  • Spasticity: Stiff muscles, exaggerated reflexes.
  • Rigidity: Stiff muscles with normal reflexes.
  • Ataxia: Lack of balance/coordination.
  • Gait: Scissoring, walking on toes, crouched gait.
  • Tremors or writhing movements.
‚ñ∂ Associated Problems
  • Speech/swallowing difficulties (drooling).
  • Developmental delays.
  • Seizures (Epilepsy).
  • Sensory impairments (vision/hearing).

Management

No cure; treatment focuses on improving function.

  • Medications: Muscle relaxants (Baclofen, Diazepam), Botox injections for spasticity.
  • Therapies: Physical (strength/balance), Occupational (adaptive equipment), Speech therapy.
  • Surgery: Orthopedic surgery for contractures; Selective dorsal rhizotomy (cutting nerve fibers) for severe spasticity.

üí™ Neuromuscular Disorders

Poliomyelitis (Polio)

A viral disease caused by poliovirus affecting the spinal cord and brain stem.

Types

  • Non-paralytic: Flu-like symptoms, neck stiffness, meningitis signs.
  • Paralytic: Virus attacks motor neurons. Spinal (limb paralysis), Bulbar (breathing/swallowing/speech).
  • Post-polio Syndrome: Symptoms return years after recovery (fatigue, muscle weakness/atrophy).

Management: Supportive (pain relief, physical therapy, mechanical ventilation if breathing affected).

Muscular Dystrophy

Group of genetic diseases causing progressive muscle weakness and degeneration.

Type Characteristics
Duchenne (DMD) Most common. Affects boys. Onset early childhood. Frequent falls, waddling gait, large calves (pseudohypertrophy), Gower's sign (difficulty rising).
Becker (BMD) Similar to Duchenne but milder and progresses slower. Onset teens to adulthood.

Management: Physical therapy, corticosteroids (Prednisone) to slow progression, respiratory support.

ü¶† Meningococcal Meningitis

Caused by Neisseria meningitidis. Infectious and transmissible.

Pediatric Clinical Features

  • Onset: Acute and sudden. Pyrexia, tachycardia, rigors.
  • Infants: Bulging anterior fontanelle, high-pitched cry, irritability, drowsiness.
  • Older Children: Severe headache, neck stiffness, positive Kernig's/Brudzinski's signs.
  • Specific Signs: Photophobia, Petechial hemorrhage (bleeding spots/rash).

Nursing Management

  • Isolation: Admit to isolated cubicle. Barrier nursing for 48 hours.
  • Environment: Quiet, darkened room (photophobia).
  • Drug Therapy: Crystalline Penicillin (IV/IM), Chloramphenicol. Phenobarbitone for convulsions.
