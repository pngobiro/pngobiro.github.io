üß£ Multiple Sclerosis (MS)

An immune-mediated progressive demyelinating disease of the CNS. Demyelination refers to the destruction of myelin, impairing transmission of nerve impulses.

  • Onset: Typically young adults ages 20 to 40; affects women more than men.
  • Causes: Genetic susceptibility + nongenetic trigger (viral infection, low Vitamin D).

Clinical Manifestations

‚ñ∂ Common Symptoms
  • Fatigue, weakness, numbness, coordination difficulties.
  • Visual Disturbances: Blurring, diplopia, scotoma (patchy blindness), total blindness (lesions in optic nerves).
  • Spasticity, loss of abdominal reflexes.
  • Heat intolerance.
  • Autonomic dysfunction: Bladder/bowel/sexual dysfunction.

Management

Goal: Delay progression, manage chronic symptoms, treat exacerbations.

Treatment Type Agents/Intervention Purpose
Pharmacologic Interferons (beta-1a, beta-1b) Reduce relapse frequency.
Exacerbations Corticosteroids (Methylprednisolone) Limit severity/duration of inflammation.
Symptom Mgmt Baclofen (spasticity), Amantadine (fatigue) Control specific symptoms.

üö∂ Parkinson's Disease

A slowly progressing neurologic movement disorder leading to disability. Associated with decreased levels of dopamine.

‚ÑπÔ∏è

Cardinal Signs

  1. Tremor: Often resting, "pill-rolling".
  2. Rigidity: Resistance to passive movement.
  3. Bradykinesia: Abnormally slow movements.

Also: Postural instability, hypokinesia, mask-like face.

Medical Management

  • Levodopa (with Carbidopa): Mainstay of treatment. Converted to dopamine in the basal ganglia.
  • Anticholinergics: Control tremor and rigidity (e.g., Benztropine).
  • Dopamine Agonists: Bromocriptine.
  • Surgical: Deep brain stimulation or stereotactic surgery for refractory cases.

üß¨ Huntington's Disease

A chronic, progressive, hereditary disease resulting in involuntary choreiform movement and dementia.

Pathology: Premature death of cells in the striatum (basal ganglia) and cortex.

Clinical Features

  • Chorea: Repetitive, rapid, jerky, involuntary movements.
  • Intellectual Decline: Dementia, memory loss, poor judgment.
  • Emotional Disturbance: Depression, apathy, irritability, social withdrawal.

Treatment: No cure. Management is palliative. Medications used for depression and involuntary movements (chorea).

üß© Dementia & Alzheimer's Disease

Dementia: A group of symptoms affecting memory, thinking, and social abilities severely enough to interfere with daily life.

Alzheimer's Disease: The most common cause of progressive dementia. Characterized by plaques (beta-amyloid) and tangles (tau protein) in the brain.

Types of Progressive Dementia

Type Key Characteristics
Alzheimer's Memory loss, plaques/tangles, brain atrophy.
Vascular Caused by blood vessel damage (strokes). Problems with focus/organization.
Lewy Body Abnormal protein clumps. Visual hallucinations, parkinsonism, sleep acting-out.
Frontotemporal Personality, behavior, and language changes.

Alzheimer's Disease Symptoms

‚ñ∂ Cognitive Changes
  • Memory loss affecting daily function (repeating statements, getting lost).
  • Difficulty finding words.
  • Disorientation to time/place.
  • Poor judgment and decision making.
‚ñ∂ Personality/Behavior Changes
  • Depression, apathy, social withdrawal.
  • Mood swings, irritability, aggressiveness.
  • Wandering.
  • Delusions.

Pharmacologic Therapy

  • Cholinesterase Inhibitors: Donepezil (Aricept), Rivastigmine. Boost cell communication.
  • Memantine (Namenda): Regulates glutamate. For moderate to severe cases.

‚ö†Ô∏è Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis (ALS)

Also known as motor neuron disease. A progressive degeneration of motor neurons in the brain and spinal cord controlling voluntary muscles.

üö®

Prognosis

Most patients die from respiratory failure within 3 to 5 years of symptom onset. The disease does not typically affect mental faculties (reasoning, memory).

Symptoms

  • Early: Muscle twitches, cramps, stiffness, weakness in arm/leg.
  • Bulbar Onset: Slurred speech (dysarthria), difficulty swallowing (dysphagia).
  • Late: Paralysis of voluntary muscles, respiratory compromise requiring ventilation.

Management

  • Riluzole (Rilutek): Only drug shown to prolong survival (by a few months).
  • Supportive Care:
    • Respiratory support (BiPAP, Ventilation).
    • Nutritional support (PEG tube) for dysphagia.
    • Communication devices.
