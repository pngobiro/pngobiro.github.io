üîò Spinal Disc Problems

Disorders affecting intervertebral discs include disc herniation (prolapsed intervertebral disc), degenerative disc disease, and infection (discitis).

Spinal Anatomy Review

  • The spinal cord ends between L1 and L2 in adults.
  • Below the cord termination, nerve roots form the cauda equina ("horse's tail").
  • Intervertebral Discs: Composed of a peripheral fibrocartilaginous part (annulus fibrosus) and a central gelatinous part (nucleus pulposus).

Prolapsed Intervertebral Disc (Herniation)

Occurs when the nucleus pulposus herniates through the annulus fibrosus, irritating or compressing adjacent nerve roots. Most common in those under 40 years old.

Lumbosacral Disc Herniation (Sciatica)

Most commonly occurs at L5/S1. Symptoms include:

  • Unilateral leg pain radiating below the knee (more severe than back pain).
  • Numbness/paresthesia in dermatomal distribution.
  • Positive Straight Leg Raising Test.
  • Pain relieved by lying down; exacerbated by sitting/walking.

Thoracic Disc Herniation

Less common. Can lead to nerve root irritation or cord compression (paraplegia, clonus, +Babinski reflex).

Degenerative Disc Disease

Symptoms of back/neck pain caused by wear-and-tear. Causes include aging, genetics, and environment. Pathologic changes include annular tears, disc space narrowing, and osteophyte formation.

ü¶† Discitis

Inflammation of the vertebral disc space, usually associated with infection (commonly Staphylococcus aureus).

Risk Factors Immunosuppression (diabetes), IV drug use, post-surgery.
Presentation Insidious onset of neck/back pain, localized tenderness, restricted mobility, fever, weight loss.
Investigations Raised ESR/CRP. MRI is most sensitive. Biopsy for confirmation.
Management Antibiotics (6-8 weeks), Immobilization (brace), Analgesia. Surgery if neurologic deficit occurs.

Classification

  • Mechanical: Varies with posture, exacerbated by movement.
  • Nonspecific: Cannot be attributed to specific cause.
  • Inflammatory: Pain/stiffness on waking, improved by movement (e.g., Ankylosing Spondylitis).

RED FLAGS: When to Refer

Cauda Equina Syndrome (Emergency)

  • Severe progressive bilateral leg weakness.
  • Recent onset urinary retention/incontinence.
  • Fecal incontinence.
  • Saddle anesthesia (perianal sensory loss).
  • Laxity of anal sphincter.

Spinal Fracture

  • Sudden severe central pain relieved by lying down.
  • History of trauma (or lifting in osteoporosis).
  • Structural deformity (step-off).

Cancer or Infection

  • Onset age >50 or <20.
  • Pain remains when supine; night pain disturbing sleep.
  • History of cancer, IV drug use, HIV, fever, weight loss.

Management

Principles: Keep patient active and provide analgesia.

  • Activity: Bed rest considered only in exceptional cases (max 48 hrs). Encourage swimming/exercise.
  • Analgesia: Simple analgesics (Paracetamol/NSAIDs) first line. Weak opioids if necessary. Muscle relaxants for spasm.
  • Surgery: Discectomy or spinal fusion for severe nerve compression or intractable pain unresponsive to conservative treatment.
