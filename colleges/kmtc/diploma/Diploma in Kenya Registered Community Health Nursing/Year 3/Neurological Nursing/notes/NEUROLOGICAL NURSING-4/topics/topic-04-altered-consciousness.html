üìñ Introduction & Definitions

An altered level of consciousness (LOC) refers to a state in which the patient is not oriented, does not follow commands, or needs persistent stimuli to achieve a state of alertness. LOC is gauged on a continuum with a normal state of alertness and full cognition (consciousness) on one end and coma on the other end.

Key Terminologies

Coma
A clinical state of unconsciousness in which the patient is unaware of self or the environment for prolonged periods (days to months or even years).
Akinetic Mutism
A state of unresponsiveness to the environment in which the patient makes no movement or sound but sometimes opens the eyes.
Persistent Vegetative State
A condition in which the patient is described as wakeful but devoid of conscious content, without cognitive or affective mental function.
02

üß¨ Pathophysiology

Altered LOC is not a disorder itself; rather, it is a function and symptom of multiple pathophysiologic phenomena. Causes of coma are generally classified into three categories:

  • Neurologic: Head injury, stroke
  • Toxicologic: Drug overdose, alcohol intoxication
  • Metabolic: Hepatic or renal failure, diabetic ketoacidosis

These causes result in neurologic disruption in the cells of the nervous system, neurotransmitters, or brain anatomy.

  • A disruption in the basic functional units (neurons) or neurotransmitters results in faulty impulse transmission.
  • Intact anatomic structures (corpus callosum, lobes) are needed for proper communication.
  • The brain stem controls heart, respiration, and blood pressure. Disruptions here or in cerebral structures due to trauma, edema, or pressure from tumors lead to altered LOC.
03

ü©∫ Clinical Manifestations

As the patient's state of alertness decreases, changes occur in pupillary response, eye opening, verbal response, and motor response.

  • Initial Signs: Subtle behavioral changes such as restlessness or increased anxiety.
  • Pupillary Changes: Pupils become sluggish (slower response to light). As the patient becomes comatose, pupils become fixed (no response).
  • Coma: The patient does not open eyes, respond verbally, or move extremities in response to requests.

üìä Assessment & Glasgow Coma Scale

A complete assessment focuses on the neurologic system, including mental status, cranial nerve function, cerebellar function, reflexes, and motor/sensory function.

Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS)

LOC is a sensitive indicator of neurologic function. The GCS assesses three parameters on a scale from 3 to 15.

Interpretation

  • Comatose with localized signs (abnormal pupillary/motor): Suspect neurologic disease.
  • Comatose with preserved pupillary reflexes: Suspect toxic or metabolic disorder.

üî¨ Diagnostic Findings

Imaging Procedures

  • Computed Tomography (CT)
  • Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)
  • Positron Emission Tomography (PET)
  • Electroencephalography (EEG)

Laboratory Tests

Used to identify toxic or metabolic causes:

  • Blood glucose
  • Electrolytes
  • Serum ammonia and BUN (Blood Urea Nitrogen)
  • Calcium levels
  • PT/PTT (Clotting times)
  • Serum ketones, alcohol/drug levels, Arterial Blood Gases (ABGs)

üè• Medical Management

  1. 1
    Airway Management (First Priority): Obtain and maintain patent airway. Intubation or tracheostomy may be needed with mechanical ventilation for adequate oxygenation.
  2. 2
    Circulatory Status: Monitor BP and heart rate to ensure perfusion to body and brain. Establish IV access.
  3. 3
    Nutritional Support: Initiate feeding tube or gastrostomy as soon as possible.
  4. 4
    Pharmacologic Management: Treat underlying cause and prevent complications.

Nursing Diagnoses

  • Ineffective airway clearance related to altered LOC
  • Risk of injury related to decreased LOC
  • Deficient fluid volume related to inability to take fluids by mouth
  • Risk for impaired skin integrity related to immobility
  • Ineffective thermoregulation related to hypothalamic damage
  • Impaired urinary elimination / Bowel incontinence

Nursing Interventions

Maintaining the Airway

  • Elevate head of bed to 30 degrees.
  • Position in lateral or semiprone position to allow jaw/tongue to fall forward and promote drainage.
  • Suctioning and oral hygiene.
  • Chest physiotherapy and postural drainage. Auscultate chest every 8 hours.

Protecting the Patient

  • Raise padded side rails.
  • Prevent injury from invasive lines/equipment.
  • Protect patient dignity (provide privacy, speak to patient).

Fluid Balance & Nutrition

  • Assess hydration (turgor, mucous membranes, I&O, labs).
  • Administer IV fluids slowly to prevent increased ICP.

Mouth Care

  • Inspect for dryness/crusting. Cleanse and rinse carefully.
  • Apply petroleum to lips.
  • Move endotracheal tube to opposite side daily to prevent ulceration.

Skin & Joint Integrity

  • Regular turning schedule to prevent pressure ulcers.
  • Passive range of motion exercises.

Corneal Integrity

  • Cleanse eyes with sterile normal saline.
  • Instill artificial tears every 2 hours if prescribed.
  • Protect eyes if corneal reflex is absent.

Thermoregulation

High fever may indicate infection or hypothalamic damage.

  • Remove bedding.
  • Administer acetaminophen.
  • Cool sponge baths / fans / hypothermia blanket.
  • Monitor temperature frequently to prevent shivering (which increases metabolic demand).

Urinary & Bowel Function

  • Palpate/scan bladder for retention.
  • Indwelling catheter for acute phase; monitor output.
  • Monitor bowel movements for constipation/impaction.
  • Stool softeners, suppositories, or enemas as needed.

Complications to Monitor

Potential Complications

  • Respiratory Failure / Pneumonia: Monitor vital signs and respiratory function closely.
  • Aspiration
  • Pressure Ulcers
  • Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT): Monitor for signs; administer prophylaxis (heparin, compression stockings).
  • Contractures
