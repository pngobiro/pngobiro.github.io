üìã Health History & Manifestations

An important aspect of the neurologic assessment is the history of the present illness. This involves observing overall appearance, mental status, posture, movement, and affect.

Key History Components

  • Symptom Details: Onset, character, severity, location, duration, frequency, precipitating/aggravating/relieving factors.
  • Family History: Presence of genetic diseases.
  • Trauma History: Any falls or injuries involving the head or spinal cord.
  • Substance Use: Alcohol, medications, and recreational drugs.

Common Clinical Manifestations

Key Symptoms

  • Pain
  • Seizures: Result of abnormal paroxysmal discharges in the cerebral cortex.
  • Dizziness/Vertigo: Abnormal sensation of imbalance or movement (vestibular dysfunction).
  • Visual Disturbances: Loss of acuity, diplopia (double vision).
  • Weakness: Specifically muscle weakness.
  • Abnormal Sensation: Numbness or paresthesia.

üß† Assessing Cerebral Function

Mental Status Examination

Assessment begins by observing appearance, behavior, dress, grooming, and personal hygiene. Key areas include:

  • Level of Consciousness (LOC): Is the patient alert, drowsy, or stuporous?
  • Orientation: To time, place, and person.
  • Intellectual Function: Average IQ capability (e.g., subtract 7 from 100 consecutively).
  • Thought Content: Are thoughts spontaneous, clear, coherent? Presence of fixed ideas or illusions.
  • Emotional Status: Affect (mood manifestation), irritability, anxiety, or mood swings.

Perception & Agnosia

Agnosia is the inability to interpret or recognize objects seen through the special senses.

Type of Agnosia Affected Cerebral Area
Visual Occipital lobe
Auditory Temporal lobe
Tactile Parietal lobe
Body parts/relationships Parietal lobe

Language Ability & Aphasia

Aphasia is a deficiency in language function.

Type of Aphasia Brain Area Involved
Auditory-receptive Temporal lobe
Visual-receptive Parietal-occipital area
Expressive speaking Frontal areas
Expressive writing Posterior frontal area

üëÅÔ∏è Examining the Cranial Nerves

Nerve Assessment Technique
I (Olfactory) With eyes closed, identify familiar odors (coffee, tobacco). Test each nostril separately.
II (Optic) Snellen eye chart, visual fields, ophthalmoscopic examination.
III, IV, VI
(Oculomotor, Trochlear, Abducens)		 Test ocular rotations, conjugate movements, nystagmus, pupillary reflexes, and inspect for ptosis.
V (Trigeminal) Sensory: Touch cotton to forehead, cheeks, jaw. Test sharp/dull pain discrimination.
Reflex: Corneal reflex (blink response).
Motor: Clench/move jaw.
VII (Facial) Observe symmetry in facial movements (smile, whistle, frown, close eyes tight). Check for flaccid paralysis. Taste test (sugar/salt).
VIII (Acoustic) Whisper test, Weber test (lateralization), Rinne test (air vs. bone conduction).
IX (Glossopharyngeal) Assess swallowing and taste discrimination on posterior third of tongue.
X (Vagus) Gag reflex, hoarseness check. Observe symmetric rise of uvula when saying "ah".
XI (Spinal Accessory) Palpate strength of trapezius (shrug shoulders) and sternocleidomastoid (turn head) against resistance.
XII (Hypoglossal) Protrude tongue (check for deviation/tremors). Move tongue against resistance.
05

üí™ Examining the Motor System

Muscle Tone & Strength

Assessment includes muscle size, tone (spasticity, rigidity, flaccidity), and strength. Inspect for atrophy or involuntary movements (tremors, tics).

Muscle Strength Grading Scale

Grade Description
5 Full power against gravity and resistance (Normal)
4 Fair but not full strength against gravity and moderate resistance (Slight weakness)
3 Just sufficient strength to overcome gravity (Moderate weakness)
2 Ability to move but not overcome gravity (Severe weakness)
1 Minimal contractile power (Very severe weakness)
0 No movement (Complete paralysis)

Balance and Coordination

Tests cerebellar function. Ataxia is incoordination of voluntary muscle action.

The Romberg Test

A screening test for balance. The patient stands with feet together and arms at the side, first with eyes open, then closed for 20-30 seconds.

Positive Romberg: Loss of balance (slight swaying is normal).

‚ö° Reflexes & Sensory System

Reflexes

Deep Tendon Reflexes (DTRs): Biceps, brachioradialis, triceps, patellar, ankle.

Reflex Grading Scale

Grade Response
0 No response
1+ Diminished (hypoactive)
2+ Normal
3+ Increased (may be normal)
4+ Hyperactive (hyperreflexia)

Superficial Reflexes: Corneal, gag, abdominal, plantar, perianal. Graded as present (+) or absent (-).

Sensory Examination

Largely subjective; requires patient cooperation. Performed with patient's eyes closed.

  • Tactile Sensation: Light touch with cotton wisp.
  • Superficial Pain: Sharp vs. dull discrimination (using broken tongue blade).
  • Vibration: Low-frequency tuning fork on bony prominences.
  • Proprioception: Position sense (moving toe up/down).
