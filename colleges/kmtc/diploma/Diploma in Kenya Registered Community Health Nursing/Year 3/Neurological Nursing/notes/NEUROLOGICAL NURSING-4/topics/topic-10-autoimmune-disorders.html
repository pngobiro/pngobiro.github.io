üß† Multiple Sclerosis (MS)

Multiple Sclerosis (MS) is an immune-mediated progressive demyelinating disease of the CNS. Demyelination refers to the destruction of myelin, the fatty and protein material that surrounds certain nerve fibers in the brain and spinal cord, resulting in impaired transmission of nerve impulses.

Typically presents in young adults (ages 20 to 40) and affects women more frequently than men.

Clinical Manifestations

Signs and symptoms vary based on lesion location:

  • Fatigue, depression, weakness, numbness.
  • Difficulty in coordination, loss of balance (ataxia), pain.
  • Visual Disturbances: Blurring of vision, diplopia, patchy blindness (scotoma), total blindness (optic nerve lesions).
  • Spasticity: Muscle hypertonicity of extremities and loss of abdominal reflexes.

Assessment & Diagnosis

  • MRI: Primary tool for visualizing plaques, documenting activity, and evaluating treatment.
  • CSF Electrophoresis: Identifies presence of antibodies (oligoclonal banding).

Management

üíä

Pharmacologic Therapy

  • Interferons (beta-1a and beta-1b): Reduce relapse frequency by 30%.
  • Corticosteroids: Limit severity and duration of exacerbations.
  • Symptom Management: Antidepressants, opiates, or antiseizure medications for pain.

Nursing Diagnoses

  • Impaired physical mobility (weakness, spasticity).
  • Risk for injury (sensory/visual impairment).
  • Impaired urinary/bowel elimination.
  • Impaired speech and swallowing.
  • Disturbed thought processes (cognitive changes).
  • Ineffective individual coping.

üí™ Myasthenia Gravis (MG)

An autoimmune disorder affecting the myoneural junction, characterized by varying degrees of weakness of the voluntary muscles.

Pathophysiology

Autoantibodies directed at acetylcholine receptor sites impair transmission of impulses. Fewer receptors are available for stimulation, resulting in voluntary muscle weakness that escalates with continued activity.

Note: 80% of patients have thymic hyperplasia or a thymic tumor.

Clinical Manifestations

  • Ocular: Diplopia (double vision) and Ptosis (drooping eyelids) - often initial symptoms.
  • Bulbar: Weakness of face and throat muscles; bland facial expression, dysphonia (voice impairment), choking/aspiration risk.
  • Generalized: Extremity weakness.

NB: Myasthenia gravis is purely a motor disorder with no effect on sensation or coordination.

Diagnostics: The Tensilon Test

Edrophonium chloride (Tensilon), an anticholinesterase agent, is injected IV. Facial muscle weakness and ptosis should resolve for about 5 minutes.

Safety: Atropine should be available to control side effects (bradycardia, sweating, cramping).

Management

  • Pharmacologic: Anticholinesterase agents (Pyridostigmine, Neostigmine) to increase acetylcholine. Immunosuppressants (Azathioprine, Cyclosporine).
  • Plasmapheresis/IVIG: Used to treat exacerbations.
  • Surgical: Thymectomy (removal of thymus gland).

Myasthenic Crisis

An exacerbation characterized by severe generalized muscle weakness and respiratory/bulbar weakness resulting in respiratory failure. Common precipitator is infection.

Management: Respiratory support (ventilation), Neostigmine, Plasmapheresis/IVIG.

‚ö° Guillain-Barr√© Syndrome (GBS)

An autoimmune attack on the peripheral nerve myelin. Results in acute, rapid segmental demyelination producing ascending weakness, dyskinesia, hyporeflexia, and paresthesias.

Antecedent Event: Often a viral respiratory or GI infection occurs ~2 weeks prior.

Pathophysiology

Schwann cells (which produce myelin in PNS) are spared, allowing for remyelination and recovery in the post-acute phase.

Clinical Manifestations

  • Ascending Weakness: Usually begins in legs and progresses upward. Can lead to quadriplegia.
  • Respiratory Failure: Demyelination of diaphragm/intercostal nerves (neuromuscular respiratory failure).
  • Cranial Nerve Involvement: Blindness (optic), bulbar weakness (glossopharyngeal/vagus) causing swallowing difficulty.
  • Autonomic Dysfunction: Cardiac dysrhythmias, BP fluctuations.

Medical Management

GBS is a medical emergency requiring ICU management due to rapid progression and respiratory risk.

  • Respiratory Support: Mechanical ventilation if necessary.
  • Immobility Management: Anticoagulants, compression stockings to prevent DVT/PE.
  • Therapy: Plasmapheresis and IVIG to reduce antibody load.

Nursing Diagnoses

  • Ineffective breathing pattern / Impaired gas exchange.
  • Impaired physical mobility.
  • Imbalanced nutrition (less than body requirements).
  • Impaired verbal communication.
  • Fear and anxiety (related to paralysis/loss of control).
