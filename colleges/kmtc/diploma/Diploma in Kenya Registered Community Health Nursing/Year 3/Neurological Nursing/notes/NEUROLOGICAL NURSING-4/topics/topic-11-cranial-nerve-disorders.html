The most common disorders of the cranial nerves are:

  • Trigeminal Neuralgia: A condition affecting the fifth cranial nerve (CN V).
  • Bell's Palsy: Caused by involvement of the seventh cranial nerve (CN VII).

‚ö° Trigeminal Neuralgia

Also known as Tic Douloureux, this is a condition of the fifth cranial nerve characterized by paroxysms of pain in the area innervated by any of the three branches (most commonly the second and third).

üî•

Pain Characteristics

The pain ends as abruptly as it starts and is described as a unilateral shooting and stabbing sensation. Involuntary contraction of facial muscles can cause sudden closing of the eye or a twitch of the mouth.

Etiology

  • Chronic compression of the nerve root.
  • Vascular pressure from structural abnormalities (e.g., loop of an artery).
  • Degenerative changes.

Triggers

Paroxysms can occur with stimulation of nerve terminals by:

  • Washing the face
  • Shaving
  • Brushing teeth
  • Eating/drinking (extreme temperatures)
  • Draft of cold air

Medical Management

Pharmacologic Therapy

  • Antiseizure Agents: Carbamazepine (Tegretol) relieves pain by reducing transmission of impulses. Taken with meals.
  • Alternatives: Gabapentin (Neurontin) and Baclofen.

Surgical Management

  • Microvascular Decompression: Relieves compression of the nerve.
  • Percutaneous Radiofrequency Trigeminal Gangliolysis.

Nursing Management

  • Prevention of Pain: Teach patient to avoid aggravating factors (room temperature water, soft foods, shield face from cold).
  • Post-operative Assessment: Evaluate for facial motor and sensory deficits in the three branches of the nerve.
03

üé≠ Bell's Palsy

Bell's palsy (facial paralysis) is due to unilateral inflammation of the seventh cranial nerve, resulting in weakness or paralysis of the facial muscles on the affected side.

Pathophysiology

The inflamed, edematous nerve becomes compressed to the point of damage, or its nutrient vessel is occluded, producing ischemic necrosis. Possible causes include vascular ischemia, viral disease (herpes simplex/zoster), or autoimmune disease.

Clinical Manifestations

  • Distortion of the face from paralysis.
  • Increased lacrimation (tearing).
  • Painful sensations in the face, behind the ear, and in the eye.
  • Speech difficulties.
  • Difficulty eating on the affected side.

Prognosis

Patients should be reassured that no stroke has occurred. Spontaneous recovery usually occurs within 3 to 5 weeks.

  • Corticosteroids (Prednisone): Reduced inflammation and edema to restore circulation to the nerve.
  • Analgesics: Control facial pain.
  • Eye Care: Protect the eye if the eyelid does not close (patch, artificial tears, sunglasses).
  • Physical Therapy: Facial exercises to maintain muscle tone and prevent denervation.
