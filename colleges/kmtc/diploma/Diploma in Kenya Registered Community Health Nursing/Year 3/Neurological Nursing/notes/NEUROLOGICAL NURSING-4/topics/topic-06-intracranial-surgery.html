üß† Definition & Surgical Approaches

Craniotomy involves opening the skull surgically to gain access to intracranial structures.

Indications

  • To remove a tumor
  • To relieve elevated ICP
  • To evacuate a blood clot
  • To control hemorrhage

Approaches to Craniotomy

‚ÑπÔ∏è

Anatomical Landmark

The Tentorium Cerebelli is an extension of the dura mater that separates the cerebellum from the inferior portion of the occipital lobes.

Approach Description
Supratentorial Above the tentorium into the supratentorial compartment.
Infratentorial Below the tentorium.
Transsphenoidal Through the mouth and nasal sinuses to gain access to the pituitary gland.
Burr Holes Circular openings made in the skull by a hand drill or automatic craniotome. Used for exploration, diagnosis, evacuating hematomas/abscesses, or accessing ventricles.

Other Cranial Procedures

  • Craniectomy: Excision of a portion of the skull.
  • Cranioplasty: Repair of a cranial defect using a plastic or metal plate.
03

üè• Preoperative Management

Medical Management

Diagnostic procedures include CT scanning, MRI, and cerebral angiography (to study tumor blood supply).

Pharmacologic Prep

  • Anti-seizure Medication: e.g., Phenytoin to reduce risk of post-op seizures.
  • Corticosteroids: e.g., Dexamethasone to reduce cerebral edema.
  • Diuretics: e.g., Furosemide or Osmotic diuretics.
  • Antibiotics: To reduce risk of infection.

Note: Fluids are restricted.

Nursing Management

  • Assessment: Evaluate LOC, responsiveness, and identify neurologic deficits (paralysis, visual dysfunction, speech issues).
  • Education: Provide information to patient and family about what to expect.
  • Preparation:
    • Surgical site is shaved immediately before surgery.
    • Indwelling urinary catheter inserted.
    • Central and arterial lines placed for monitoring.
04

üõå Postoperative Management

Ongoing management focuses on detecting and reducing cerebral edema, relieving pain, preventing seizures, and monitoring ICP.

‚ö°

Critical Monitoring

Arterial and central venous pressure lines are used to monitor hemodynamic status. The patient may be intubated and receiving supplemental oxygen.

Specific Interventions

Reducing Cerebral Edema

  • Medications: Osmotic diuretics and Corticosteroids (Dexamethasone IV every 6 hours for 24-72 hours, then tapered to oral).
  • Fluid Control: Monitor intake; avoid overhydration.
  • Temperature: Maintain normal temperature (fever increases metabolic demand).

Relieving Pain & Preventing Seizures

  • Acetaminophen: For fevers.
  • Codeine/Morphine: Parenteral administration for headache relief.
  • Phenytoin: Anti-seizure prophylaxis.

üìù Nursing Process & Care Plan

Assessment

Frequency of monitoring is based on clinical status. Key assessments include:

  • Respiratory function (ABGs).
  • Vital signs fluctuations (indicate increased ICP).
  • Neurologic checks (LOC, pupils, motor function).
  • Surgical dressing inspection (bleeding, CSF drainage).

Nursing Diagnoses

  • Ineffective cerebral tissue perfusion related to cerebral edema.
  • Potential for ineffective thermoregulation (hypothalamic damage/infection).
  • Risk for ineffective breathing pattern related to edema.
  • Disturbed sensory perception (periorbital edema, dressings).
  • Body image disturbance.

Nursing Interventions

Respiratory Function

  • Place patient in semiprone position (unless contraindicated) to facilitate gas exchange until conscious.
  • Suction cautiously to remove secretions (can raise ICP).
  • Monitor ABGs and ventilator settings.

Cerebral Perfusion & Edema

  • Elevate head of bed 30.5 cm (12 in) to aid venous drainage.
  • Administer corticosteroids/diuretics as prescribed.
  • Assess LOC frequently.

Sensory Deprivation & Safety

  • Lubricate eyelids and apply cold compresses for periocular edema.
  • Administer analgesics (e.g., Codeine) that do not mask responsiveness.
  • Pad hands and bed rails (seizure precautions).
  • Orient patient frequently to time, place, and person.

Wound Care

Reinforce blood-stained dressings with sterile dressing. Blood-soaked dressings act as a culture medium for bacteria.

Potential Complications

üö®

Alert

  • Cerebral edema
  • Intracranial hemorrhage and hypovolemic shock
  • Infections (Meningitis)
  • Seizures
