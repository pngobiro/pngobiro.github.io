üß¨ Introduction to the Nervous System

The nervous system controls all motor, sensory, autonomic, cognitive, and behavioral activities. It maintains homeostasis and integrates all body activities.

‚ÑπÔ∏è

System Composition

The nervous system has approximately 10 million sensory neurons that send information about the internal and external environment to the brain and 500,000 motor neurons that control the muscles and glands.

Divisions of the Nervous System

  • Central Nervous System (CNS): Includes the brain and spinal cord.
  • Peripheral Nervous System (PNS): Made up of the cranial and spinal nerves. It is further divided into:
    • Somatic (voluntary) nervous system
    • Autonomic (involuntary) nervous system

‚ö° Neurons & Neurotransmitters

The Neuron

The basic functional unit of the brain is the neuron. It is composed of:

  • Cell body
  • Dendrite
  • Axon

Nerve cell bodies occurring in clusters are called ganglia or nuclei. A cluster of cell bodies with the same function is called a center (e.g., the respiratory center). Neuroglial cells support, protect, and nourish neurons.

Neurotransmitters

Neurotransmitters communicate messages from one neuron to another or from a neuron to a specific target tissue. They are manufactured and stored in synaptic vesicles and enable conduction of impulses across the synaptic cleft.

üí°

Clinical Relevance

Many neurologic disorders are due to an imbalance in neurotransmitters:

  • Huntington's disease: Lack of GABA and acetylcholine
  • Epilepsy: Low serotonin levels
  • Parkinson's disease: Decrease in dopamine
Neurotransmitter Function/Association
Acetylcholine Major transmitter of the parasympathetic nervous system
Norepinephrine Major transmitter of the sympathetic nervous system
Serotonin Regulates mood, sleep, and appetite
Dopamine Motor control and reward pathways
GABA Gamma-aminobutyric acid; inhibitory neurotransmitter

üß† Anatomy of the Brain

The brain is divided into three major areas:

  1. Cerebrum (composed of two hemispheres, thalamus, hypothalamus, and basal ganglia)
  2. Brain Stem
  3. Cerebellum

1. The Cerebrum

The cerebrum is the largest part of the brain, responsible for reasoning, thought, memory, speech, and sensation. It consists of two hemispheres separated by the great longitudinal fissure and joined by the corpus callosum (thick collection of nerve fibers).

The surface has folded layers called gyri (convolutions) which increase surface area. The hemispheres are divided into four lobes:

  • Frontal Lobe: Largest lobe. Responsible for concentration, abstract thought, memory, motor function, and speech (Broca's area).
  • Parietal Lobe: Predominantly sensory. Contains the primary sensory cortex.
  • Temporal Lobe: Contains auditory receptive areas and interpretive area for integration.
  • Occipital Lobe: Responsible for visual interpretation.

Deep Structures

Basal Ganglia: Masses of nuclei deep in the hemispheres responsible for control of fine motor movements.

Thalamus: Relay station for all sensation except smell. Memory, sensation, and pain impulses pass through here.

Hypothalamus: Located anterior and inferior to the thalamus. Regulates pituitary secretion, fluid balance, temperature (vasoconstriction/vasodilation), and appetite (hunger center).

2. The Brain Stem

Consists of:

  • Midbrain: Connects pons and cerebellum with cerebral hemispheres. Contains sensory/motor pathways and centers for auditory/visual reflexes. Origin of CN III and IV.
  • Pons: Bridge between cerebellum halves and medulla/cerebrum. Controls heart, respiration, and BP. Connection for CN V through VIII.
  • Medulla Oblongata: Contains motor fibers from brain to cord and sensory fibers from cord to brain. Connection for CN IX through XII.

3. The Cerebellum

Separated from cerebral hemispheres by the tentorium cerebelli. Responsible for:

  • Coordination of movement
  • Fine movement
  • Balance
  • Position sense (proprioception)
  • Integration of sensory input

Structures Protecting the Brain

The brain is protected by the rigid skull and the meninges. The layers of the meninges are:

  1. Dura mater (outermost)
  2. Arachnoid mater (middle)
  3. Pia mater (innermost)

Cerebrospinal Fluid (CSF)

Clear, colorless fluid (specific gravity 1.007) produced in the choroid plexuses of the ventricles. It circulates through the ventricular system and around the brain/spinal cord.

Cerebral Circulation

The brain receives ~15% of cardiac output (750 mL/min). It does not store nutrients and has high metabolic demand.

Arterial Supply: Two internal carotid arteries (anterior circulation) and two vertebral arteries.

ü¶¥ Spinal Cord & Peripheral Nervous System

Anatomy of the Spinal Cord

Extends from the foramen magnum to the lower border of the first lumbar vertebra (tapers to conus medullaris). It is continuous with the medulla oblongata.

Protected by the vertebral column:

  • 7 Cervical
  • 12 Thoracic
  • 5 Lumbar
  • 5 Sacral (fused)
  • Coccyx

Peripheral Nervous System

Includes cranial nerves, spinal nerves, and the autonomic nervous system.

Cranial Nerves

12 pairs. Three are sensory (I, II, VIII), five are motor (III, IV, VI, XI, XII), and four are mixed (V, VII, IX, X).

Spinal Nerves

31 pairs: 8 cervical, 12 thoracic, 5 lumbar, 5 sacral, 1 coccygeal.

  • Dorsal roots: Sensory. Transmit impulses from dermatomes (somatic or visceral).
  • Ventral roots: Motor. Transmit impulses from cord to body (somatic or visceral).
