üìñ Concept & Pathophysiology

Cranial Vault Components

The rigid cranial vault contains three volume components:

  • Brain tissue: 1,400 g (80%)
  • Blood: 75 mL (10%)
  • Cerebrospinal Fluid (CSF): 75 mL (10%)
üìä

Normal ICP

The volume and pressure of these three components are usually in a state of equilibrium and produce the ICP. Normal ICP is 10 to 20 mmHg (measured in the lateral ventricles).

Monro-Kellie Hypothesis

This hypothesis states that because of the limited space for expansion within the skull, an increase in any one of the components causes a change in the volume of the others.

Compensation is typically accomplished by:

  • Displacing or shifting CSF (most common)
  • Increasing the absorption of CSF
  • Decreasing cerebral blood volume

Pathophysiology of Increased ICP

Increased ICP is a syndrome that affects many patients with acute neurologic conditions. It is commonly caused by:

  • Head injury
  • Brain tumors
  • Subarachnoid hemorrhage
  • Toxic and viral encephalopathies

Increased ICP decreases cerebral perfusion, stimulates further swelling (edema), and shifts brain tissue through openings in the rigid dura, resulting in herniation‚Äîa dire, frequently fatal event.

Decreased Cerebral Blood Flow (CBF)

Increased ICP may significantly reduce cerebral blood flow, resulting in ischemia and cell death. In the early stages of cerebral ischemia, the vasomotor centers are stimulated and the systemic pressure rises to maintain cerebral blood flow. This is usually accompanied by a slow bounding pulse and respiratory irregularities.

Cerebral Edema

Defined as an abnormal accumulation of water or fluid in the intracellular space, extracellular space, or both, associated with an increase in brain tissue volume.

Early Clinical Manifestations

Any sudden change in patient's behavior:

  • Disorientation, restlessness, confusion
  • Increasing drowsiness
  • Increased respiratory effort
  • Pupillary changes
  • Constant headache, increasing in intensity, aggravated by movement/straining
  • Weakness in one extremity or one side

Later Signs of Increasing ICP

Critical Signs (Cushing's Triad)

  • Blood pressure increases (widening pulse pressure)
  • Pulse rate decreases (Bradycardia)
  • Respiratory rate decreases / Irregular pattern
  • Decreasing level of consciousness until comatose
  • Temperature increases
  • Altered respiratory patterns including Cheyne-Stokes breathing
  • Projectile vomiting
  • Loss of brain stem reflexes (pupillary, gag, swallowing)
  • Hemiplegia / Posturing (Decorticate/Decerebrate)

üî¨ Assessment & Monitoring

Diagnostic Findings

The patient may undergo:

  • Computed Tomography (CT)
  • Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)
  • Cerebral Angiography
  • Positron Emission Tomography (PET)
  • Trans-cranial Doppler studies (info about cerebral blood flow)

Monitoring ICP

Invasive monitoring is important to identify increased pressure early, quantify the degree of elevation, initiate treatment, provide access to CSF for drainage, and evaluate effectiveness of treatment.

Methods of Monitoring

Method Description
Ventriculostomy (Intraventricular Catheter) Fine-bore catheter inserted into a lateral ventricle (usually non-dominant hemisphere). Connected to a fluid-filled system and transducer. Gold standard for monitoring and allows CSF drainage.
Subarachnoid Bolt Screw or bolt placed through skull into subarachnoid space.
Epidural/Subdural Catheter Placed in the respective spaces.
Fiberoptic Transducer Sensor-tipped catheter placed in ventricle or subdural space.

üè• Medical & Nursing Management

Immediate Management Goals

  1. Decrease cerebral edema
  2. Lower volume of CSF
  3. Decrease cerebral blood volume
  4. Maintain cerebral perfusion

Interventions

  • Osmotic Diuretics: Mannitol to dehydrate brain tissue and reduce edema.
  • Corticosteroids: Dexamethasone to reduce inflammation/edema (especially with tumors).
  • Fluid Restriction: Limit overall fluid intake.
  • CSF Drainage: Via ventriculostomy.
  • Fever Control: Hyperthermia increases metabolic demand.
  • Positioning: Elevate head of bed (Semi-Fowlers) to promote venous drainage.

Nursing Process

Assessment: History, GCS, pupil response, motor/sensory function, vital signs (Cushing's reflex).

Nursing Diagnoses:

  • Ineffective airway clearance related to diminished protective reflexes.
  • Ineffective breathing patterns related to neurologic dysfunction.
  • Ineffective cerebral tissue perfusion related to increased ICP.
  • Deficient fluid volume related to fluid restriction.
  • Risk for infection related to ICP monitoring.

Potential Complications

  • Brain Stem Herniation: Displacement of brain tissue, often fatal.
  • Diabetes Insipidus: Decreased ADH secretion leading to excessive urine output.
  • SIADH: Increased ADH secretion leading to fluid retention.
