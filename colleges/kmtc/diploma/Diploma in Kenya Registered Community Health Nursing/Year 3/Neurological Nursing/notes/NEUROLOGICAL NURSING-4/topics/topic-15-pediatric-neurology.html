üíß Hydrocephalus

An abnormal condition of fluid accumulation around the brain or inside the ventricles, usually resulting from interference with circulation or absorption of cerebrospinal fluid (CSF).

Classification

Type Mechanism Common Causes
Communicating
(Extra-ventricular)		 Obstruction outside the ventricular system (blockage of subarachnoid cisterns). Fluid is not absorbed. Subarachnoid hemorrhage, Bacterial meningitis, Toxoplasmosis, Head injury.
Non-Communicating Obstruction within the ventricular systems (interferes with flow to subarachnoid space). Congenital defects (Arnold-Chiari, Aqueduct stenosis), Tumors, Cysts, Hematomas.

Management

  • Medical: Acetazolamide (Diamox) to reduce CSF production (mild cases). Repeated lumbar punctures.
  • Surgical: Removal of obstructions (tumors/cysts) or shunting.

Ventriculostomy (Shunting)

Insertion of a radio-opaque ventricular catheter to shunt CSF from the ventricle to an area outside the CNS (e.g., abdominal cavity). Valves (Spitz Holter, Hakim) prevent backflow.

Nursing Care

‚ö°

Critical Nursing Interventions

  • Assessment: Measure head circumference regularly. Palpate fontanelles for tension/bulging.
  • Positioning: Support the neck when moving the child. Turn every 2 hours. Post-op: Lie on the unaffected side to prevent pressure on the shunt valve.
  • Monitoring: Vital signs and signs of increased ICP.

üß¨ Congenital Defects

Microcephaly

A defect in brain growth where the brain is significantly smaller than normal. The head appears small, forehead slopes backward, and ears appear relatively large.

  • Predisposing Factors: Fetal radiation, Maternal phenylketonuria, Congenital infections (Rubella, Zika, Herpes), Intrauterine anoxia.
  • Management: No cure; supportive care for parents.

Spina Bifida

A defect in the formation of the skeletal arch enclosing the spinal cord (posterior lamina fails to fuse). Most common in lumbar/lumbosacral region.

Nursing Care

  • Infection Prevention: Aseptic technique for dressing changes (warm normal saline) every 4 hours until surgery.
  • Protection: Prevent injury to the sac.
  • Observation: Monitor vital signs and musculoskeletal condition.
04

‚ö° Pediatric Epilepsy

Recurrent seizures caused by abnormal, excessive electrical discharges of cerebral neurons.

Types

  • Petit Mal: Brief loss of awareness.
  • Grand Mal: Tonic-clonic convulsions.
  • Jacksonian: Unilateral clonic movements spreading systematically through muscle groups.
üöë

Emergency Management (ABC Rule)

  • Airway: Ensure clear airway.
  • Breathe: Enable patient to breathe.
  • Circulation: Loosen clothing.

Pharmacology

  • Phenobarbitone (Luminal): 3-6 mg/kg.
  • Phenytoin Sodium (Epanutin): 10-20 mg/kg.
05

ü¶† Meningococcal Meningitis

Inflammation of the meninges caused by Neisseria meningitidis (intracellular gram-negative diplococcus). Highly infectious.

Clinical Features in Children

Older Children Infants/Young Babies
Severe headache, stiff neck Bulging anterior fontanelle (instead of stiff neck)
Positive Kernig's sign High-pitched cry, irritability
Pyrexia, tachycardia, rigors Drowsiness, comatose state
Photophobia, vomiting Petechial hemorrhage (bleeding spots)

Diagnostic Tests

Lumbar Puncture: Analysis of CSF shows increased polymorph cells, turbid (cloudy) appearance, raised pressure, low glucose, and high protein.

Management

  • Isolation: Single cubicle, barrier nursing for at least 48 hours. Quiet, darkened room.
  • Drug Therapy:
    • Crystalline Penicillin: 300mg/kg/day (high dose).
    • Chloramphenicol: 100mg/kg/day IM, then oral syrup.
    • Phenobarbitone: For convulsions.
