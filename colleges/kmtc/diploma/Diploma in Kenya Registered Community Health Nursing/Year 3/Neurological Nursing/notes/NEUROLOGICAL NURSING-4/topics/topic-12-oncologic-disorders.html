üß† Primary Brain Tumors

A brain tumor is a localized intracranial lesion that occupies space within the skull. Tumors usually grow as a spherical mass, but they can grow diffusely and infiltrate tissue.

‚ö°

Physiologic Changes

The effects of neoplasms occur from the compression and infiltration of tissue, leading to:

  • Increased intracranial pressure (ICP) and cerebral edema
  • Seizure activity and focal neurologic signs
  • Hydrocephalus
  • Altered pituitary function

Classification

  • Primary Brain Tumors: Originate from cells and structures within the brain.
  • Secondary (Metastatic) Tumors: Develop from structures outside the brain and occur in 20% to 40% of all patients with cancer.

Types of Primary Tumors

  • Dural Meningioma: Arising from the coverings of the brain.
  • Acoustic Neuroma: Developing in or on the cranial nerves.
  • Gliomas: Originating within brain tissue. Infiltrate any portion of the brain; most common type of brain tumor.
  • Angiomas: Masses composed largely of abnormal blood vessels found either in or on the surface of the brain.
  • Other: Tumors of the pituitary and pineal glands.
03

ü©∫ Clinical Manifestations

Brain tumors can produce either focal or generalized neurologic signs and symptoms.

Generalized Symptoms

Reflect increased ICP (e.g., headache, vomiting, papilledema).

Focal (Localized) Symptoms

Result from tumors interfering with functions in specific brain regions:

  • Hemiparesis
  • Seizures
  • Mental status changes
  • Sensory and motor abnormalities
  • Visual alterations
  • Alterations in cognition and language disturbances (aphasia)
04

üî¨ Assessment & Diagnostics

  • History: Evolution of symptoms is key.
  • Neurologic Examination: Indicates areas of CNS involvement.
  • CT Scan: Specific info on number, size, and density of lesions.
  • MRI: Diagnostic imaging tool.
  • Cerebral Angiography: Visualizes blood vessels; localizes tumors.
  • Electroencephalogram (EEG): Detects abnormal brain waves (seizures).
05

üè• Management

Medical Management

  • Radiation Therapy: Cornerstone of treatment; decreases recurrence of incompletely resected tumors.
  • Chemotherapy: Used alone or with radiation/surgery.
  • Corticosteroids: Used before and after treatment to reduce cerebral edema and promote recovery.

Surgical Management

Objective: Remove or destroy the entire tumor without increasing neurologic deficit (paralysis, blindness) or relieve symptoms by partial removal (decompression).

Nursing Management

üö®

Aspiration Risk

Patients with diminished gag response due to cranial nerve dysfunction are at high risk. Teach patient to direct food to the unaffected side, sit upright, use semisoft diet, and have suction available.

Key Interventions

  • Perform neurologic checks and monitor vital signs.
  • Space nursing interventions to prevent rapid increases in ICP.
  • Reorient patient frequently (person, time, place).
  • Monitor motor function and sensory disturbances.
  • Evaluate speech, eye movement, and pupil reaction.
06

üîÑ Cerebral Metastases

Significant number of cancer patients experience deficits due to metastasis to the brain.

Symptoms: Headache, gait disturbances, visual impairment, personality changes, altered mentation, focal weakness, paralysis, aphasia, seizures.

Medical Management

Treatment is palliative (eliminating/reducing serious symptoms). Approaches include radiation, surgery (for single metastasis), and chemotherapy.

Pharmacologic Therapy

Medication Class Purpose
Corticosteroids (Dexamethasone) Relieve headache/altered LOC by reducing inflammation and edema around deposits.
Osmotic Agents (Mannitol) Decrease fluid content of the brain.
Antiseizure Agents (Phenytoin) Prevent and treat seizures.
Anticoagulants Prevent DVT/PE (occur in ~15% of patients).
Pain Management Morphine (epidural/subarachnoid) for severe pain.
