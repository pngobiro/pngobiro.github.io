üß† Head Injuries: Overview

Head injury is a broad classification that includes injury to the scalp, skull, or brain. It is the most common cause of death from trauma.

Classification of Damage

Primary Injury: The initial damage to the brain resulting from the traumatic event (contusions, lacerations, torn blood vessels from impact/acceleration/deceleration).

Secondary Injury: Evolves over the ensuing hours and days after the initial injury, primarily due to brain swelling or ongoing bleeding.

Pathophysiology

The sequence of events following traumatic injury often leads to a cycle of increasing pressure and ischemia:

ü§ï Types of Brain Injuries

1. Concussion

A minor head injury characterized by temporary loss of neurologic function with no apparent structural damage. Symptoms range from dizziness/spots before eyes ("seeing stars") to complete loss of consciousness for a few seconds.

2. Contusion

A more severe injury in which the brain is bruised, with possible surface hemorrhage. The patient is unconscious for more than a few seconds or minutes.

  • Signs: Faint pulse, shallow respirations, cool/pale skin.
  • Often involuntary evacuation of bowels/bladder.

3. Intracranial Hemorrhage

Hematomas (collections of blood) within the cranial vault are the most serious brain injuries. Delayed symptoms are frequent until the hematoma distorts the brain and increases ICP.

  • Epidural: Above the dura.
  • Subdural: Below the dura.
  • Intracerebral: Within the brain tissue.

Clinical Manifestations

  • Altered level of consciousness, confusion.
  • Pupillary abnormalities (shape, size, response).
  • Altered/absent gag reflex or corneal reflex.
  • Sudden onset of neurologic deficits.
  • Vital Sign Changes: Altered respiratory pattern, hypertension, bradycardia/tachycardia, hypo/hyperthermia.
  • Sensory dysfunction (vision, hearing, smell).
  • Spasticity, movement disorders, seizures.
  • Headache, vertigo.

üè• Management of Head Injuries

Assessment & Diagnostics

  • History: Time of injury, cause (missile, fall, object), direction/force of blow.
  • Examination: Complete neurologic exam, GCS assessment.
  • Imaging: CT, MRI, PET scan.
üö®

Critical Precaution

Any individual with a head injury is presumed to have a cervical spine injury until proven otherwise. Transport on a board with head and neck maintained in alignment with the body axis.

Medical Management

  • ICP Management: Treat rising ICP (See Topic 05).
  • Surgery: Evacuation of blood clots, debridement/elevation of depressed fractures, suture of lacerations.
  • Supportive Care:
    • Ventilatory support (intubation for comatose patients).
    • Seizure prevention (antiseizure drugs).
    • Fluid/electrolyte maintenance.
    • Nutritional support.
    • Pain and anxiety management.
    • NG tube (for reduced gastric motility/aspiration risk).

Nursing Diagnoses

  • Ineffective airway clearance/impaired gas exchange.
  • Ineffective cerebral tissue perfusion related to increased ICP.
  • Deficient fluid volume related to decreased LOC/hormonal dysfunction.
  • Imbalanced nutrition (less than body requirements).
  • Risk for injury (seizures, disorientation).
  • Potential for impaired skin integrity.
  • Disturbed thought processes.

Potential Complications

  • Cerebral edema and herniation
  • Impaired oxygenation and ventilation
  • Post-traumatic seizures

ü¶¥ Spinal Cord Injury (SCI)

A major health problem occurring almost four times more often in males. Predominant risk factors: Age, gender, alcohol/drug use.

Most Frequent Sites: C5, C6, C7, T12, L1 (areas with greater range of mobility).

Clinical Manifestations

  • Complete Lesion: No motor/sensory function preserved below level.
    • Paraplegia: Paralysis of lower body.
    • Quadriplegia: Paralysis of all four extremities.
  • Incomplete Lesion: Some function preserved.
  • Respiratory Dysfunction: Related to level of injury (abdominal/intercostal muscles T1-T11, diaphragm). High cervical injury = acute respiratory failure (leading cause of death).

Emergency Management

Critical to prevent incomplete injury from becoming complete.

Immobilization

Immobilize on a spinal (back) board with head and neck in a neutral position at the scene.

Medical & Surgical Management

  • Resuscitation: Maintain oxygenation and cardiovascular stability.
  • Corticosteroids: High-dose methylprednisolone to improve outcomes.
  • Intubation: Extreme care to avoid flexing/extending neck.
  • Stabilization: Reduction of dislocations, skeletal traction (tongs/calipers).
  • Surgery Indications: Cord compression, fragmented/unstable vertebral body, penetrating wound, bone fragments in canal, deteriorating neurologic status.

Complications

  • Neurogenic Shock
  • Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)
  • Respiratory Failure / Pneumonia
  • Autonomic Dysreflexia

Nursing Process

Assessment: Observe breathing pattern, cough strength, auscultate lungs (paralysis affects clearance). Monitor for motor/sensory changes.

Diagnoses:

  • Ineffective breathing patterns/airway clearance (muscle weakness).
  • Impaired physical mobility.
  • Disturbed sensory perception.
  • Risk for impaired skin integrity.
