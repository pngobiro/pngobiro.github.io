üìñ Definitions & Pathophysiology

Definitions

  • Seizures: Episodes of abnormal motor, sensory, autonomic, or psychic activity resulting from sudden excessive discharge from cerebral neurons.
  • Epilepsy: A group of syndromes characterized by recurring seizures. Can be primary (idiopathic) or secondary (symptom of underlying condition).

Pathophysiology

The underlying cause is an electrical disturbance in nerve cells in one section of the brain, causing them to emit abnormal, recurring, uncontrolled electrical discharges. The seizure is a manifestation of this discharge.

Causes

Category Description
Idiopathic Genetic, developmental defects.
Acquired Hypoxemia, vascular insufficiency, fever (childhood), head injury, hypertension, CNS infections, metabolic/toxic conditions (kidney injury, hyponatremia, hypoglycemia), brain tumor, drug/alcohol withdrawal.
03

üóÇÔ∏è Classification of Seizures

Seizures are divided into two main categories based on origin:

1. Partial Seizures

Begin in one part of the brain.

  • Simple Partial: Consciousness remains intact. Symptoms may be motor (hand shaking), sensory, or autonomic.
  • Complex Partial: Impairment of consciousness. Automatism (lip smacking, picking at clothes), excessive emotions.

2. Generalized Seizures

Involve electrical discharges in the whole brain (both hemispheres).

  • Tonic-Clonic (Grand Mal)
  • Tonic: Stiffening of muscles.
  • Clonic: Jerking movements.
  • Absence (Petit Mal): Brief loss of awareness (staring).
  • Myoclonic: Sudden muscle jerks.

ü©∫ Clinical Manifestations

Stages of a Grand Mal Seizure

1
Prodrome Stage: Days or hours before. Irritability, hyperactivity, mood changes.
2
Aura (Warning) Stage: Unusual feelings immediately preceding seizure (peculiar sensation, funny smell, nausea, flashing lights).
3
Tonic Stage (10-20 sec): Muscles become rigid, eyes roll up, respiration stops (cyanosis), jaw clenches (tongue biting possible).
4
Clonic Stage (~30 sec): Rhythmic muscle relaxation and contraction. Violent spasms, frothing at mouth, incontinence of urine/feces.
5
Ictal Phase: The seizure episode itself.
6
Post-ictal Stage: Recovery period. Patient is confused, hard to arouse, may sleep for hours. Complains of headache, sore muscles, fatigue.

üè• Assessment & Medical Management

Diagnostic Findings

  • History: Developmental history, precipitating factors, aura presence.
  • MRI: Detect lesions or focal abnormalities.
  • EEG: Classifies seizure type and localizes focus.
  • SPECT: Helpful in localizing seizure focus.

Pharmacologic Therapy

Goal: Seizure control with minimal side effects. Drugs control rather than cure.

Common Antiseizure Medications

  • Carbamazepine (Tegretol)
  • Clonazepam (Klonopin)
  • Gabapentin (Neurontin)
  • Lamotrigine (Lamictal)
  • Levetiracetam (Keppra)
  • Phenobarbital (Luminal)
  • Phenytoin (Dilantin)
  • Valproate (Depakote)

Side Effects

  • Idiosyncratic/Allergic: Skin reactions.
  • Acute Toxicity: When initially prescribed.
  • Chronic Toxicity: Late in therapy (e.g., gingival hyperplasia with Phenytoin).

Surgical Management

Indicated for intractable seizures not responsive to medication (e.g., focal atrophic process, tumors, vascular anomalies). Removal of the epileptogenic focus may produce long-term control.

Assessment During a Seizure

Observe and record:

  • Circumstances before seizure (stimuli).
  • Occurrence of aura.
  • First movement/body part involved.
  • Incontinence.
  • Duration of each phase.
  • Paralysis or weakness afterwards.

Care During a Seizure

üö®

Critical Safety Measures

  • Do NOT restrain the patient.
  • Do NOT pry open jaws if clenched.
  • Protect the head with a pad.
  • Provide privacy.
  • Ease patient to floor if possible.
  • Loosen constrictive clothing.
  • Push aside furniture.
  • If in bed: remove pillows, raise side rails.
  • Place on side with head flexed forward to facilitate drainage.
  • Suction if needed and available.

Care After a Seizure (Post-ictal)

  • Keep patient on side to prevent aspiration.
  • Ensure patent airway.
  • Reorient to environment upon awakening.
  • Use calm persuasion if agitated.

Nursing Diagnoses

  • Risk for injury related to seizure activity.
  • Fear related to possibility of seizures.
  • Ineffective coping.
  • Deficient knowledge.

‚ö†Ô∏è Status Epilepticus

Definition: Acute prolonged seizure activity; a series of generalized seizures that occur without full recovery of consciousness between attacks.

üöë

Medical Emergency

Vigorous muscular contractions impose heavy metabolic demand and interfere with respiration. Repeated cerebral anoxia and edema can lead to irreversible and fatal brain damage.

Precipitating Factors

Withdrawal of antiseizure medication, fever, concurrent infection.

Management

  1. 1
    Airway & Oxygenation: Establish airway. Intubate if unconscious/unresponsive.
  2. 2
    Stop Seizures: IV Diazepam (Valium) or Lorazepam given slowly.
  3. 3
    Maintenance: Phenytoin or Phenobarbital to maintain seizure-free state.
  4. 4
    Metabolic Support: IV dextrose if due to hypoglycemia.
  5. 5
    General Anesthesia: With short-acting barbiturate if initial treatment fails.
