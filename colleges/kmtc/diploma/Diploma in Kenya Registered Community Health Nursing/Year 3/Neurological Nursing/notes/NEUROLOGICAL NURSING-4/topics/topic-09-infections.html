üß† Meningitis

Meningitis is an inflammation of the meninges, the protective membranes that surround the brain and spinal cord.

Classification

  • Aseptic Meningitis: Bacteria are not the cause; viral or secondary to lymphoma, leukemia, or brain abscess.
  • Septic Meningitis: Caused by bacteria. Common agents include Neisseria meningitidis, Haemophilus influenzae, and Streptococcus pneumoniae.

Predisposing Factors

  • Overcrowding
  • Tobacco use
  • Viral upper respiratory infections
  • Otitis media and mastoiditis
  • Immunosuppression

Pathophysiology

Meningeal infections generally originate in one of two ways:

  1. Through the bloodstream as a consequence of other infections.
  2. By direct extension (traumatic injury to facial bones, invasive procedures).

N. meningitidis concentrates in the nasopharynx and is transmitted by secretion or aerosol contamination. Once the organism crosses the blood-brain barrier, it causes an inflammatory reaction in the meninges. Inflammation of the subarachnoid space and pia mater occurs, potentially causing increased intracranial pressure (ICP). Inflammatory cellular material accumulates in the CSF.

Clinical Manifestations

Headache and fever are frequently the initial symptoms. Fever tends to remain high.

Key Signs

  • Nuchal Rigidity: Stiff neck; painful/difficult flexion due to muscle spasms.
  • Positive Kernig's Sign: When lying with thigh flexed on abdomen, the leg cannot be completely extended.
  • Positive Brudzinski's Sign: Neck flexion causes flexion of knees and hips.
  • Photophobia: Extreme sensitivity to light.

Other Symptoms:

  • Rash (striking feature of N. meningitidis infection).
  • Disorientation and memory impairment.
  • Seizures and increased ICP.

Management

Diagnosis: Lumbar puncture to obtain CSF for culture and Gram staining.

Medical Management

  • Antibiotics: Early administration that crosses the blood-brain barrier (Penicillin G, Cephalosporins like Ceftriaxone, Vancomycin/Rifampin for resistant strains).
  • Corticosteroids: Dexamethasone as adjunct therapy.
  • Fluid Volume Expanders: For dehydration and shock.
  • Antiseizure Medications: Phenytoin (Dilantin) for seizure control.

Nursing Management

  • Assessment: Neurologic status, vital signs, pulse oximetry, ABGs (respiratory support may be needed).
  • Precautions: Droplet precautions until 24 hours after antibiotic initiation.
  • Supportive Care: IV fluid replacement (careful not to increase ICP), antipyretics for fever, seizure precautions, prevention of immobility complications (pressure ulcers, pneumonia).
  • Family Support: Assist family in coping with the crisis.

üíä Brain Abscess

A collection of infectious material within the tissue of the brain. It may occur by direct invasion (trauma/surgery) or spread from nearby sites (sinuses, ears, teeth).

üõ°Ô∏è

Prevention

Otitis media, mastoiditis, sinusitis, dental infections, and systemic infections should be treated promptly to prevent brain abscess.

Clinical Manifestations

  • Headache: Usually worse in the morning; most prevailing symptom.
  • Vomiting.
  • Focal neurologic signs (weakness, decreasing vision, seizures).
  • Mental status changes (lethargy, confusion, irritability).
  • Fever may or may not be present.

Management

  • Diagnosis: CT scan or MRI to locate the site.
  • Medical: Antimicrobial therapy (Penicillin G, Chloramphenicol), Corticosteroids (reduce inflammatory edema), Antiseizure medications.
  • Surgical: Incision or aspiration.
  • Nursing: Ongoing neurologic assessment (ICP changes), patient safety (falls/seizures), family support.

ü§í Encephalitis

Acute inflammatory process of the brain tissue. Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV) is the most common cause of acute encephalitis. Other causes include fungal and arthropod-borne vectors.

Clinical Features

Initial Symptoms: Fever, headache, confusion, behavioral abnormalities.

Focal Signs: Reflect areas of inflammation/necrosis; behavioral changes, focal seizures, dysphasia, hemiparesis, altered LOC.

Management

  • Diagnosis: Neuroimaging, EEG, CSF examination.
  • Treatment: Acyclovir (antiviral agent) is the medication of choice for HSV encephalitis.
