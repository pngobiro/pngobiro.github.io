Veins and Lymphatics

Venous Disorders

Venous disorders are common conditions affecting the venous system. Unlike arteries, veins have thinner walls and are subject to different pathological processes.

Varicose Veins

Definition: Abnormally dilated, tortuous veins produced by prolonged, increased intraluminal pressure and loss of support of the superficial venous system.

Etiology and Pathogenesis:

  • Increased venous pressure (most important factor)
  • Incompetent valves in perforating veins
  • Weakness of venous walls
  • Hereditary factors
  • Pregnancy and obesity
  • Prolonged standing

Morphology:

  • Dilated, tortuous, elongated veins
  • Most commonly affects superficial veins of legs
  • Valve cusps may be thickened and shortened
  • Venous wall shows fibrosis and thickening

Clinical Features:

  • Aching, heavy sensation in legs
  • Muscle cramps and fatigue
  • Visible dilated veins
  • Edema of ankles and feet
  • Skin changes including pigmentation
  • Risk of thrombophlebitis

Thrombophlebitis and Phlebothrombosis

Thrombophlebitis

Primary venous inflammation with secondary thrombosis.

  • More common in superficial veins
  • Usually associated with varicose veins
  • Thrombus is firmly attached to vessel wall
  • Lower risk of embolism

Phlebothrombosis

Primary venous thrombosis with minimal or absent inflammation.

  • More common in deep veins
  • Associated with stasis and hypercoagulability
  • Thrombus is loosely attached
  • Higher risk of pulmonary embolism

Risk Factors (Virchow's Triad)

  1. Venous stasis: Prolonged bed rest, immobilization
  2. Hypercoagulability: Pregnancy, oral contraceptives, malignancy
  3. Vessel wall injury: Trauma, surgery, intravenous catheters

Lymphatic Disorders

Lymphangitis

Acute inflammation of lymphatic vessels, usually bacterial in origin.

  • Most commonly caused by Œ≤-hemolytic streptococci
  • Spreads from focus of infection (wound, cellulitis)
  • Characterized by red streaking of skin
  • May progress to lymphadenitis

Lymphedema

Swelling caused by lymphatic obstruction or abnormality.

Primary Lymphedema:

  • Congenital malformation of lymphatic system
  • May be hereditary (Milroy's disease)
  • Usually affects lower extremities

Secondary Lymphedema:

  • Acquired obstruction of lymphatics
  • Causes include:
    • Malignant tumors
    • Surgical removal of lymph nodes
    • Radiation therapy
    • Filariasis (in tropical countries)
    • Chronic inflammation

Clinical Features:

  • Non-pitting edema
  • Usually unilateral
  • Skin thickening and fibrosis
  • Increased susceptibility to infection
  • Progressive and usually irreversible

Superior and Inferior Vena Cava Syndromes

Superior Vena Cava Syndrome

Obstruction of superior vena cava causing impaired venous return from head, neck, and upper extremities.

Causes:

  • Malignant tumors (most common):
    • Lung cancer (especially small cell)
    • Lymphomas
    • Mediastinal masses
  • Benign causes:
    • Aortic aneurysm
    • Thrombosis
    • Fibrosis

Clinical Features:

  • Facial and neck swelling
  • Dilated veins over chest and neck
  • Cyanosis of face and upper extremities
  • Headache and dizziness
  • Shortness of breath

Inferior Vena Cava Syndrome

Less common than superior vena cava syndrome.

Causes:

  • Hepatic tumors
  • Renal tumors
  • Thrombosis
  • Pregnancy (compression)

Clinical Features:

  • Lower extremity edema
  • Distended abdominal veins
  • Ascites
  • Renal dysfunction
