Special Topics in Nursing

Loss, Grieving, and Death

Terminologies

  • Loss: An actual or potential situation in which something that is valued is changed, no longer available or gone.
  • Grief: The total response to the emotional experience related to loss.
  • Bereavement: The subjective response experienced by the surviving loved ones.
  • Mourning: The behavioural process through which grief is eventually resolved or altered.

Types of Losses

Loss occurs when a valued object is changed or is no longer available. There are many types of loss, including:

  • Actual loss: Death of a loved one, theft of one's property.
  • Perceived loss: Occurs when a sense of loss is felt by an individual but is not tangible to others.
  • Physical loss: Loss of an extremity in an accident, scarring from burns, permanent injury.
  • Psychological loss: Such as a woman feeling inadequate after menopause and resultant infertility.

Grief

Grief is a universal, normal response to loss. There are different types of grief including uncomplicated ("normal"), complicated, dysfunctional, and anticipatory.

Stages of Grieving (Kübler-Ross, 1969)

Stage Behavioural Response Nursing Implication
Denial Refuses to believe that loss is happening. "This can't be happening, not to me." Verbally support but do not reinforce denial.
Anger May direct anger at nurse about matters that normally would not bother them. "Why me? It's not fair!" Help understand that anger is a normal response.
Bargaining Seeks to bargain to avoid loss. "I'll do anything for a few more years." Listen attentively and allow client to talk. Offer spiritual support.
Depression Grieves over what has happened and what cannot be. May talk freely or withdraw. "I'm so sad, why bother?" Allow client to express sadness. Communicate nonverbally by sitting quietly. Use touch.
Acceptance Comes to terms with loss. Decreased interest in surroundings. May begin making plans. "It's going to be okay." Help family understand client's decreased need to socialize. Encourage participation in treatment.

Dying and Death

Death is defined as the cessation of all vital functions of the body including the heartbeat, brain activity (including the brain stem), and breathing.

Signs of Death

  • Cessation of breathing
  • Cardiac arrest (No pulse)
  • Pallor mortis (paleness)
  • Livor mortis (settling of blood)
  • Algor mortis (reduction in body temperature)
  • Rigor mortis (stiffening of limbs)
  • Decomposition

Last Offices

Last Offices means 'caring for the body after death'. It is the final expression of caring a nurse can undertake for their client. It is essential to maintaining a safe environment and ensuring safe passage of the body. Cultural and religious practices must be respected.

Hospice Care

Hospice, a type of care for the terminally ill, is founded on the concept of allowing individuals to die with dignity and be surrounded by those who love them.

Oxygen Administration

Oxygen therapy is the administration of supplemental oxygen to a patient to prevent or treat hypoxia. Oxygen is a drug and therefore requires prescribing in all but emergency situations.

Indications for Oxygen Therapy

  • Respiratory compromise: Cyanosis, tachypnoea, hypoxemia.
  • Cardiac compromise: Chest pain, shock, tachycardia.
  • Neurological deficits: Cerebrovascular accident, spinal injuries, coma.

Methods of Oxygen Delivery

  • Nasal Cannulae: For low concentrations (25-30%) at 1-4 L/min.
  • Simple Face Mask: For medium concentrations (up to 60%) at 6 L/min.
  • Venturi Masks: For delivering controlled oxygen concentrations, useful for CO₂ retainers.
  • Non-rebreather Mask: Delivers high concentration of O₂ (up to 90%) at 10-15 L/min. Reservoir bag must be filled before applying.
  • Humidified Circuits / High Flow Systems
  • Transtracheal oxygen

Hazards of Oxygen Therapy

  • Carbon Dioxide Retention
  • Oxygen Toxicity
  • Atelectasis
  • Retrolental Fibroplasia (in premature infants)

Suctioning

Used to clear the airway of blood, mucous, and vomitus. Rigid catheters (Yankauer) are used for the mouth and oropharynx; soft catheters for the nose and nasopharynx. Suction for no longer than 15 seconds at a time.

Intravenous (IV) Fluid Administration

The goal of intravenous fluid (IV) administration is to carefully achieve and maintain a euvolemic and isotonic environment within the body, as well as to provide for a variety of nutritional and pharmacologic interventions.

Types of IV Fluids

  • Crystalloids: The primary fluid used. Classified by tonicity.
    • Isotonic: Tonicity equal to body plasma (e.g., Lactated Ringer's, Normal Saline 0.9%, D5W).
    • Hypertonic: Higher tonicity, shifts water into bloodstream (e.g., 3% NaCl, D50W).
    • Hypotonic: Lower tonicity, shifts water out of bloodstream (e.g., 0.45% NaCl).
  • Colloids: Contain large proteins or molecules to maintain blood volume (e.g., albumin, dextran).
  • Blood and blood products: Most desirable for replacement as hemoglobin carries oxygen.

Adverse Effects of IV Therapy

  • Infection: Any break in the skin carries a risk.
  • Phlebitis: Inflammation of a vein.
  • Infiltration / Extravasation: IV fluid leaks into surrounding tissue.
  • Fluid overload: Fluids given at a higher rate than the system can absorb.
  • Hypothermia: Infusion of large amounts of cold fluids.
  • Electrolyte imbalance.
  • Embolism: A blood clot, air bubble, or solid mass blocking a vessel.

Blood Transfusion

The transfusion process is complex and involves many interlinking chains of events. The principle aims are to improve oxygen carrying capacity, provide symptomatic improvement, and reduce hypovolaemia.

Blood Products Available

  • Whole blood: For acute haemorrhage (>1500 ml loss).
  • Packed Red Blood Cells (PRBC): To increase oxygen-carrying capacity.
  • Fresh Frozen Plasma (FFP): Expands blood volume and provides clotting factors.
  • Platelets: Replaces platelets in thrombocytopenia.
  • Cryoprecipitate: For clotting factor deficiencies.

Administration and Pre-transfusion Checks

  • Confirm physician's order and signed consent.
  • Two nurses must confirm client name, ID, blood type, Rh type, product unit number, and expiration date at the bedside.
  • Start transfusion within 30 minutes of arrival at bedside.
  • Use a blood giving set with an integral mesh filter.
  • Take baseline vital signs immediately before starting.
  • Closely observe the patient during the first 15 minutes as a reaction is most likely to occur then.

Transfusion Reactions

If a reaction is suspected (e.g., fever, chills, rash, back pain, change in BP/pulse), STOP THE TRANSFUSION IMMEDIATELY.

How to Act

  1. Stop the transfusion.
  2. Maintain venous access (keep the line open with normal saline).
  3. Check ABCs and examine the patient.
  4. Re-check all identifying information (patient ID band, blood bag, forms).
  5. Call for senior and specialist help.
  6. Retain the unfinished blood bag and giving set to be sent back to the laboratory.
  7. Monitor vital signs closely and document everything.

First Aid

First Aid is the immediate and temporary care given to a victim of an accident or sudden illness before the services of a physician can be obtained.

Priority of Treatment (ABC)

  1. AIRWAY: Ensure the airway is open and clear.
  2. BREATHING: Check for breathing and provide rescue breaths if necessary.
  3. CIRCULATION: Check for a pulse and control severe bleeding.

Choking (Airway Obstruction)

For a conscious victim with poor or no air exchange, perform the Heimlich Maneuver (abdominal thrusts).

CPR and Recovery Position

If a victim has no pulse, begin Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation (CPR). If the person is breathing and does not have a suspected spinal injury, place them in the recovery position.

Fractures and Splinting

Keep the victim still and splint the limb before moving, immobilizing the joint above and below the suspected fracture. Do not try to straighten a deformed limb.

Burns

  • First-degree (redness, mild swelling): Apply cool, wet cloths or immerse in water.
  • Second-degree (blisters, considerable pain): Immerse in cool water, apply dry sterile dressings. Elevate limbs.
  • Third-degree (deep tissue destruction, white/charred): Call for help immediately. Cover with dry sterile dressing. Treat for shock.

Caution

DO NOT clean severe burns, break blisters, remove stuck clothing, or apply grease/ointment.

Bleeding

  1. Wear gloves. Have the victim lie down.
  2. Elevate the injured limb higher than the heart unless a fracture is suspected.
  3. Apply direct pressure on the wound with a sterile pad or clean cloth.
  4. If bleeding is controlled, bandage firmly.
  5. If direct pressure fails, a tourniquet is a last resort. Call for help immediately.
