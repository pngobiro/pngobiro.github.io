Advanced Concepts in Nursing: Assessment, Theories, and Critical Thinking

Health Assessment

Accurate diagnosis rests firmly upon the foundation of a thoughtful and inclusive history and a competently performed physical examination.

History Taking

Good history taking is an important first step in treating the patient. It is a key component of patient assessment, enabling the delivery of high-quality care.

Components of a Comprehensive Patient History

  • Chief Complaint (CC): Why the patient is seeking care.
  • History of Present Illness (HPI): Using mnemonics like OPQRST (Onset, Provocation, Quality, Region/Radiation, Severity, Timing) or SOCRATES (Site, Onset, Character, Radiation, Associations, Timing, Exacerbating/Relieving factors, Severity).
  • Past Medical History: Previous illnesses, surgeries, injuries, hospitalizations, immunizations, allergies.
  • Family History: Health status of parents, siblings, children; history of diseases in the family.
  • Drug History: Current medications (prescription and over-the-counter), allergies.
  • Social History (Lifestyle): Alcohol, smoking, recreational drugs, occupation, home situation.
  • Review of Systems: A series of questions about each body system to uncover other symptoms.

Physical Examination

Physical assessment findings, or objective data, are obtained through the use of four specific diagnostic techniques: inspection, palpation, percussion, and auscultation.

Assessment Techniques

  1. Inspection: Critical observation using all senses. Look at color, shape, symmetry, position, and note any odors.
  2. Palpation: Light and deep touch to assess skin temperature, texture, moisture, tenderness, and organ characteristics.
  3. Percussion: Striking the body surface to produce sounds that indicate if tissue is air-filled, fluid-filled, or solid.
  4. Auscultation: Listening to sounds produced by the body, usually with a stethoscope (e.g., heart sounds, breath sounds, bowel sounds).

Head-to-Toe Examination Sequence (Abbreviated)

  • General Survey: Physical appearance, body structure, mobility, behavior.
  • Head and Face: Inspect scalp, hair, skull, face. Palpate sinuses.
  • Eyes: Inspect external structures, test PERRLA.
  • Ears, Nose, Mouth, Throat: Inspect and palpate.
  • Neck: Inspect for symmetry, palpate lymph nodes and carotid pulse.
  • Posterior & Anterior Thorax: Inspect, palpate for expansion and fremitus, auscultate lung fields.
  • Upper Extremities: Inspect symmetry, test muscle strength, check capillary refill, palpate radial pulse.
  • Heart: Palpate apical impulse, auscultate 4 cardiac areas.
  • Abdomen: Inspect, auscultate bowel sounds, light palpation.
  • Lower Extremities: Inspect symmetry, test ROM, test muscle strength, palpate dorsalis pedis pulse.
  • Neurologic: Test sensory and motor function.
  • Breast Exam: Inspect and palpate.

Gordon's Typology of 11 Functional Health Patterns

Marjorie Gordon (1987) proposed functional health patterns as a guide for establishing a comprehensive nursing data base. These 11 categories enable a systematic and standardized approach to data collection.

  1. Health Perception/Health Management: Client's perceived health and well-being and how health is managed.
  2. Nutritional/Metabolic: Pattern of food and fluid consumption.
  3. Elimination: Patterns of excretory function (bowel, bladder, skin).
  4. Activity/Exercise: Pattern of exercise, activity, leisure.
  5. Sleep-Rest: Patterns of sleep, rest, and relaxation.
  6. Cognitive/Perceptual: Sensory-perceptual and cognitive patterns.
  7. Self-Perception/Self-Concept: Client's self-concept and perceptions of self.
  8. Role/Relationship: Pattern of role participation and relationships.
  9. Sexuality/Reproductive: Patterns of satisfaction with sexuality; reproductive patterns.
  10. Coping/Stress Tolerance: General coping pattern and its effectiveness.
  11. Value/Belief: Patterns of values, beliefs, and goals that guide choices.

Critical Thinking

Definition: Is the intellectually disciplined process of actively and skillfully conceptualizing, applying, analyzing, synthesizing, and/or evaluating information gathered from observation, experience, reflection, reasoning, or communication as a guide to belief and action.

Importance of Critical Thinking in Nursing

  • Nurses are expected to accept more responsibilities, collaborate, and make independent decisions.
  • It is the key to preventing and resolving complex patient problems.
  • Critical thinking, problem solving, and decision making are interrelated processes essential to safe, competent, skillful nursing practice.
  • Nurses deal with constant change in stressful environments.

Skills in Critical Thinking

  1. Critical Analysis: Application of questions to a situation to determine essential information.
  2. Inductive Reasoning: Making generalizations from a set of facts or observations (specific to general).
  3. Deductive Reasoning: Reasoning from the general to the specific.

Attitudes that Foster Critical Thinking

  • Independence of thought
  • Fair-mindedness
  • Insight into egocentricity
  • Intellectual humility
  • Intellectual courage
  • Integrity
  • Perseverance
  • Confidence in reasoning
  • Curiosity

The Nursing Process

The Nursing Process is a systematic, rational method of planning and providing individualized care. It is a framework for critical thinking and is specific to the nursing profession. Its purpose is to "diagnose and treat human responses to actual or potential health problems".

Phases of the Nursing Process

  1. Assessing: Collect, organize, validate, and document data. This includes subjective data (what the client says) and objective data (what the nurse observes).
  2. Diagnosing: Analyze data, identify problems, risks, and strengths, and formulate diagnostic statements (e.g., using NANDA).
  3. Planning: Prioritize problems/diagnoses, formulate goals/desired outcomes, select nursing interventions, and write nursing orders. Goals should be SMART (Specific, Measurable, Attainable, Relevant, Time-bound).
  4. Implementing: Reassess the client, determine the need for assistance, implement nursing interventions, supervise delegated care, and document activities. This is the "doing" phase.
  5. Evaluating: Collect data related to outcomes, compare data with outcomes, relate nursing actions to client goals, draw conclusions about problem status, and continue, modify, or terminate the client's care plan.

Nursing Theories and Models

A theory is a supposition or system of ideas that is proposed to explain a given phenomenon. A nursing theory is the body of knowledge that is used to support nursing practice, organizing knowledge and explaining phenomena in nursing.

Metaparadigms in Nursing

Four concepts are generally considered central to the discipline of nursing:

  • Person: The recipient of care.
  • Environment: The internal and external surroundings that affect the client.
  • Health: The degree of wellness or wellbeing that the client experiences.
  • Nursing: The attributes, characteristics, and actions of the nurse providing care.

Major Theorists and Their Models

  • Florence Nightingale: Environmental Theory. Linked health with five environmental factors: pure air, pure water, efficient drainage, cleanliness, and light.
  • Virginia Henderson: Definition of Nursing. Conceptualized the nurse's role as assisting individuals to gain independence in meeting 14 fundamental needs.
  • Dorothea Orem: Self-Care Deficit Theory. Focuses on the concepts of self-care, self-care deficit, and nursing systems (wholly compensatory, partly compensatory, supportive-educative).
  • Betty Neuman: Systems Model. Views a client as an open system's relationship to stress, reaction to it, and reconstitution factors. Interventions are based on primary, secondary, and tertiary prevention.
  • Hildegard Peplau: Interpersonal Relations Model. Focuses on the therapeutic relationship between the nurse and client, which evolves in four phases: orientation, identification, exploitation, and resolution.
  • Martha Rogers: Science of Unitary Human Beings. Views humans as irreducible, indivisible, pan-dimensional energy fields integral with the universe.
  • Imogene King: Theory of Goal Attainment. Describes a dynamic, interpersonal relationship in which a person grows and develops to attain certain life goals, set mutually by the nurse and client.
