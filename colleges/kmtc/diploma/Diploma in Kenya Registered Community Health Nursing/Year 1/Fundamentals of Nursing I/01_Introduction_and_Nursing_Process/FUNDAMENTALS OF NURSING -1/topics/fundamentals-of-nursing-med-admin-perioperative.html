Medication Administration and Perioperative Nursing

Drug Administration

The administration of medications is controlled by three Acts of Parliament - the Medicines Act (1968), the Misuse of Drugs Act (1971) and the Poisons Act (1972) - and a Statutory Instrument - the Misuse of Drugs Regulations (1985). These provide the framework within which medicines are stored, transported, prescribed, recorded, dispensed and administered.

The nurse should always assess a client's health status and obtain a medication history prior to giving any medication. The extent of assessment depends on the client's illness or current condition, the intended drug and route of administration.

During drug administration, a nurse should have:

  • knowledge of the drug
  • know its effects and potential side effects
  • know the patient's condition
  • assess the suitability for that medication at that given time.

Practice Guidelines

  • Nurses who administer medications are responsible for their own actions.
  • Be knowledgeable.
  • Keep narcotics and barbiturates in a locked place.
  • Use only medications that are in a clearly labeled container.
  • Calculate drug dosages accurately.
  • Administer only medications that are personally prepared.
  • Identify the client correctly before drug administration.
  • Do not leave medications on the bedside with certain exceptions e.g cough syrup.
  • Take special precautions when administering certain medications e.g insulin.
  • In case of a medication error, report immediately.

Rights of Medication Administration

  1. Right patient: Check the name on the order and the patient. Use 2 identifiers. Ask patient to identify himself/herself.
  2. Right medication: Check the medication label. Check the order.
  3. Right dose: Check the order. Confirm appropriateness of the dose using a current drug reference. If necessary, calculate the dose and have another nurse calculate the dose as well.
  4. Right route: Again, check the order and appropriateness of the route ordered. Confirm that the patient can take or receive the medication by the ordered route.
  5. Right time: Check the frequency of the ordered medication. Double-check that you are giving the ordered dose at the correct time. Confirm when the last dose was given.
  6. Right documentation: Document administration AFTER giving the ordered medication. Chart the time, route, and any other specific information as necessary.
  7. Right reason: Confirm the rationale for the ordered medication. What is the patient's history? Why is he/she taking this medication?
  8. Right response: Make sure that the drug led to the desired effect. If an antihypertensive was given, has his/her blood pressure improved? Document your monitoring.

Calculating Drug Doses

Example: a prescription requires 30 mg of a drug that is dispensed as 60 mg in 5 ml

$$ \text{What you want}: 30 \text{ mg} \times \frac{\text{total volume}}{\text{What you've got}: 60 \text{ mg}} \times 5 \text{ ml} = \frac{30}{60} \times 5 = 2.5 \text{ ml required} $$

Routes of Drug Administration

A route of drug administration is the path by which a drug or other substance is brought into contact with the body. When administering a drug, the nurse should ensure that the pharmaceutical preparation is appropriate for the route specified.

Parenteral Routes

The term parenteral administration implies the routes through which the drug directly reaches the body fluids, by passing the preliminary process of transport through the intestinal wall or pulmonary alveoli.

Common Parenteral Routes:
  • a) Subcutaneous (S/C)
  • b) Intramuscular (I/M)
  • c) Intravenous (I/V)
  • d) Intradermal

Intramuscular (IM) Injections

The intramuscular route delivers injections directly into muscles which have an efficient blood supply and can absorb from 1 ml to 5 ml of medication, depending on the site.

Subcutaneous (SC) Injections

Small amounts of medication (0.2-2 ml) are given into the subcutaneous tissue to allow a slow, sustained absorption. It is an ideal route for insulin and heparin.

Intradermal Injections

Used for tests like TB screening. The needle is held at a 15-degree angle to the skin, and a small bleb or bubble is formed just below the epidermis.

Other Routes of Administration

  • Rectal: Bypasses the upper GI tract, works quickly. Used for suppositories or enemas for local or systemic effects.
  • Inhalation: Used for gaseous and volatile agents and aerosols for rapid onset of action.
  • Ophthalmic: Usually applied topically as eye drops or ointment for diagnostic or treatment purposes.

Perioperative Nursing

Perioperative nursing encompasses three phases:

  • Preoperative: begins when the decision to have surgery is made and ends when the client is transferred to the operating table.
  • Intraoperative: begins when the client is transferred to the operating table and ends when admitted to the recovery room.
  • Postoperative: begins with admission to the recovery room and ends when healing is complete.

Purposes of Surgery

  • Diagnostic: To determine or confirm a diagnosis.
  • Palliative: To relieve symptoms of a disease, not to cure.
  • Ablative: To remove a diseased body part.
  • Constructive: To restore function lost or reduced as a result of congenital anomalies.
  • Transplant: To replace a malfunctioning organ or structure.

Pre-operative Phase

The goal is to ensure that the client is mentally and physically prepared for surgery.

Assessment

  • Pre-operative informed consent.
  • Nursing history to plan care.
  • Physical assessment.
  • Screening tests.

Pre-operative Checklist

  • Patient's name confirmed.
  • Consent form signed.
  • Patient starved (NPO).
  • Jewellery/dentures/other prostheses removed.
  • Bladder emptied.
  • Baseline observations taken.
  • Allergies noted.
  • Available lab work/x-rays/blood for transfusion.
  • Theatre gown worn.
  • Shaving (prn).

Implementation

  • Preoperative teaching: information, skills training, psychosocial support.
  • Physical preparation: nutrition and fluids, elimination, hygiene, medications, rest and sleep, valuables, prostheses, skin preparation, vital signs.

Intraoperative Phase

The overall goals are to maintain the client's safety and to maintain homeostasis.

Implementation

  • Surgical skin preparation: cleaning surgical site, shaving if necessary, applying antimicrobial agent.
  • Positioning: provides optimal visualization of and access to the surgical site, and protects the client from harm.

Postoperative Phase

This phase is important for the client's recovery.

Immediate Postoperative Assessment (Recovery Room)

  • Level of consciousness.
  • Vital signs.
  • Skin colour and temperature.
  • Comfort and pain level.
  • Fluid balance (IV fluids, drains, urine output).
  • Dressing and bedclothes for drainage.

Implementation

  • Pain management.
  • Positioning.
  • Deep breathing, coughing, and leg exercises.
  • Moving and ambulation.
  • Hydration and Diet.
  • Urinary elimination.
  • Suction if needed.
  • Home care teaching and discharge planning.
