Common Clinical Procedures

Vital Signs

Vital or cardinal signs are temperature, pulse, respiration and blood pressure. Monitoring a client's vital signs should not be an automatic or a routine procedure; it should be a thoughtful, scientific assessment. When and how often to assess a specific client's vital signs are chiefly nursing judgments, depending on the client's health status.

Vital signs are objective measurements of the patient's most basic body function. A change in vital signs can be a strong indicator of a decline in the patient's health status.

When to Assess Vital Signs

  • On admission to provide a baseline for comparison.
  • When a client has a change in health status or reports symptoms such as chest pain.
  • Before and after surgery or an invasive procedure.
  • Before and/or after administration of medication that could affect vital signs.
  • Before and after any nursing intervention that could affect the vital signs (e.g., ambulating a client who has been on bedrest).

Temperature

The system that regulates body temperature has three main parts: sensors in the skin, an integrator in the hypothalamus, and an effector system that adjusts the production and loss of heat.

Factors Affecting Body Temperature

  • Age: As people age they become more sensitive to extremely hot or cold environments.
  • Diurnal variations (circadian rhythms): Temperature fluctuates throughout the day.
  • Exercise: Increases heat production, raising body temperature.
  • Hormones: Women's temperatures can be raised during ovulation.
  • Stress
  • Environment: Extreme temperatures may result in heat stroke or hypothermia.
  • Hot and cold food and drink may affect oral temperature.

Methods of Taking Temperature

  • Axillary (under the armpit): Provides the least accurate results.
  • Oral (under the tongue): Never used with infants or very young children.
  • Rectal (inserted into the rectum): Provides the most accurate recording. Most often used for infants.
  • Tympanic (in the ear canal): Used mostly for infants.
  • Temporal (across the forehead): Noninvasive and quick.

Normal and Abnormal Temperatures

  • A normal body temperature taken orally is 98.6°F (37°C), with a range of 97.8–99.1°F (36.5–37.2°C).
  • A fever is a temperature of 101°F (38.3°C) or higher in an infant younger than three months or above 102°F (38.9°C) for older children and adults.
  • Hypothermia is recognized as a temperature below 96°F (35.5°C).

Pulse

A pulse is a wave of blood created by contraction of the left ventricle of the heart. The pulse wave represents the stroke volume output.

Pulse Assessment

  • Rate: Tachycardia (>100 beats/min), Bradycardia (<60 beats/min).
  • Rhythm: Regular or irregular (Dysrhythmia).
  • Strength: weak (1+), strong (2+), bounding (3+), thready (barely felt), absent.

Respirations

Respiration is the act of breathing. Its purpose is to exchange O₂ and CO₂ in the lungs/tissues. We count respirations (ventilations) per minute.

Respiration Assessment

  • Rate: Normal is 16-20/min for adults. Tachypnea (>20/min), Bradypnea (<16/min).
  • Rhythm: Regular or irregular.
  • Depth: Shallow, normal, or deep.
  • Effort: Unlabored or labored (Dyspnea). Apnea is the absence of breathing.

Blood Pressure

Blood Pressure (BP) is the lateral force on the walls of an artery by the pulsing blood under pressure from the heart. Systolic pressure is the peak pressure during contraction, and diastolic pressure is the minimum pressure during relaxation.

Korotkoff's Sounds

Measurement of blood pressure by auscultation is based on the sounds produced as a result of changes in blood flow, termed Korotkoff's sounds:

  1. Phase I: The pressure level at which the first faint, clear tapping sounds are heard (systolic BP).
  2. Phase II: During cuff deflation when a murmur or swishing sounds are heard.
  3. Phase III: The period during which sounds are crisper and increase in intensity.
  4. Phase IV: When a distinct, abrupt, muffling of sound is heard.
  5. Phase V: The pressure level when the last sound is heard (diastolic BP).

Specimen Collection

Specimen collection refers to collecting various samples, such as stool, urine, blood and other body fluids or tissues, from the patient for diagnostic or therapeutic purposes.

General Considerations

  • Wear gloves to protect self from contact with body fluids.
  • Get the request and identify the patient and specimen type.
  • Explain the purpose and method to the patient.
  • Assemble necessary materials.
  • Use an appropriate, clearly labeled container with a tight cover.
  • Apply strict aseptic techniques.
  • Wash hands before and after collection.
  • Ensure the specimen is representative and adequate in quantity.
  • Arrange for immediate transportation to the laboratory.

Criteria for Rejection of Specimen

  • Missing or inadequate identification.
  • Insufficient quantity.
  • Specimen collected in an inappropriate container.
  • Contamination suspected.
  • Inappropriate transport or storage.
  • Unknown time delay.
  • Haemolysed blood sample.

Blood Sample

Whole blood is required for bacteriological examination, and serum is used for serological techniques. Skin antisepsis is extremely important.

  • Collect blood during the early stages of disease.
  • Collect blood during paroxysm of fever.
  • Less quantity of blood is needed from small children.

Cerebrospinal Fluid (CSF)

Examination of CSF is essential in diagnosing meningeal irritation or cerebral issues. About 3-10 ml of CSF is collected.

Precautions for CSF Collection:

  • Collect CSF before antimicrobial therapy is started.
  • Use a screw-capped sterile container.
  • Do not delay transport and laboratory investigations.
  • Store at 37°C if delay is inevitable.

Sputum Specimen

Before collection, teach the patient to cough deeply to raise sputum, not saliva. Provide oral care without toothpaste. Collect about 15-30 ml in a sterile container, ensuring the outside is not contaminated. Label and send to the lab promptly.

Urine Specimen

The clinical information obtained from a urine specimen is influenced by the collection method, timing and handling.

Types of Urine Collection

  • Random Specimen: Easiest to obtain, used for routine analysis.
  • First Morning Specimen: Specimen of choice for urinalysis, more concentrated.
  • Midstream Clean Catch Specimen: Preferred for culture and sensitivity testing.
  • Timed Collection Specimen: Collected over 8 or 24 hours to measure substance concentrations.
  • Catheter Collection Specimen: For bedridden patients or those who cannot urinate independently.
  • Suprapubic Aspiration Specimen: A sterile specimen collected by needle aspiration through the abdominal wall.
  • Pediatric Specimen: A special collection bag is used for infants.

Stool Specimen

This procedure is used to test for infectious organisms, mucus, fat, parasites, or blood in the stool.

Guidelines for Stool Collection

  • The specimen should not be contaminated with urine.
  • Collect 1 to 2 gm of fresh stool.
  • Submit more than one specimen on different days if possible.
  • The specimen must be received within 1-2 hours of passage.

Wound Dressing and Asepsis

Introduction to Wounds

The skin is the largest organ of the body. Wounds can be intentional (e.g., venipuncture) or unintentional (e.g., fracture). They can be open (skin is broken) or closed (tissues traumatized without a skin break).

Classification of Wounds

  • Clean wounds: Minimal inflammation, closed.
  • Clean-contaminated wounds: Surgical wounds.
  • Contaminated wounds: Open, fresh, accidental wounds; show inflammation.
  • Dirty/infected wounds: Contain dead tissue with clinical infection (e.g., purulent drainage).

Dressing Wounds

Purposes of Dressings

  • Protect the wound from mechanical injury and microbial contamination.
  • Provide/maintain high humidity and thermal insulation.
  • Absorb drainage or debride a wound.
  • Prevent haemorrhage.
  • Splint or immobilize the wound site to facilitate healing.

Types of Dressings

  • Wet-to-Dry: Used for dirty or infected wounds to debride them. A moist dressing is placed on the wound and allowed to dry; removal takes exudate and debris with it.
  • Wet-to-Wet: Used on clean, granulating wounds. The dressing remains wet so that it does not debride the healing tissue upon removal.
  • Antibiotic Ointment: An alternative for clean, healing wounds. A small amount is coated on the wound and may be covered with dry gauze.

Asepsis

Asepsis is the state of being free from disease-causing contaminants. Aseptic technique involves practices that minimize the introduction of microorganisms to patients during care.

Basic Principles of Asepsis

  • Use of sterile equipment.
  • Avoidance of direct contact with the susceptible site ('non-touch' technique).
  • Effective hand hygiene.
  • Effectively cleaned equipment (e.g., dressing trolleys).
  • Appropriate use of Personal Protective Equipment (PPE).
  • Safe disposal of used equipment.

The Chain of Infection

Breaking any link in the chain will assist in preventing the spread of microorganisms.

Aseptic Dressing Technique

Aseptic dressing technique involves maintaining asepsis, exposing the wound for the minimum time, and employing an efficient procedure.

Wound Closure

Stitches, Clips, or Staple Removal

Normally removed within approximately 10 days of the surgical procedure unless otherwise advised.

Bandages

Bandages hold a dressing in place, create pressure to control bleeding, help keep wound edges closed, secure splints, and provide support.
