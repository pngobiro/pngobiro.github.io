Introduction to Histology

What is Histology?

Histology, also known as microscopic anatomy or microanatomy, is the branch of biology which studies the microscopic anatomy of biological tissues. It is the microscopic counterpart to gross anatomy, which looks at larger structures visible without a microscope.

In medicine, histology is an essential tool for understanding health and disease. By examining a thin slice of tissue under a microscope, pathologists can identify abnormalities, such as inflammation, cancer, or other pathological conditions. It bridges the gap between anatomy and physiology by showing how the structure of cells and tissues relates to their function.

Etymology

The word "histology" comes from the Greek words histos, meaning "tissue," and -logia, meaning "study."

The Hierarchy of Biological Organization

Histology focuses on the tissue level of organization, which is a crucial step in the hierarchy that builds a complete organism.

  1. Cells: The basic structural and functional unit of all known organisms.
  2. Tissues: Groups of similar cells and their extracellular matrix, from the same origin, that together carry out a specific function.
  3. Organs: A collection of different tissues structurally joined to serve a common function (e.g., the heart, liver, skin).
  4. Organ Systems: A group of organs that work together to perform one or more functions (e.g., the digestive system, nervous system).
  5. Organism: An individual living being.

The Four Basic Tissue Types

All the various tissues and organs in the body are composed of just four basic tissue types. Understanding these four types is fundamental to the study of histology.

  • • Epithelial Tissue: Forms coverings and linings on body surfaces, and constitutes glands. Functions include protection, secretion, absorption, and sensation.
  • • Connective Tissue: Supports, connects, and separates different types of tissues and organs. It includes bone, cartilage, fat, blood, and lymphatic tissue.
  • • Muscle Tissue: Specialized for contraction to produce movement. There are three types: skeletal, smooth, and cardiac muscle.
  • • Nervous Tissue: The main component of the nervous system (brain, spinal cord, and nerves), which regulates and controls body functions through nerve impulses.

Basic Histological Technique: Preparing a Slide

To view tissues under a microscope, they must be specially prepared in a multi-step process. The goal is to preserve the tissue's structure and produce a very thin slice that light can pass through.

Steps in Tissue Preparation
StepPurpose
1. FixationTo preserve the tissue structure and prevent decomposition. Formalin is a common fixative.
2. DehydrationTo remove water from the tissue, usually by using increasing concentrations of alcohol.
3. ClearingTo replace the alcohol with a substance (like xylene) that is miscible with the embedding medium.
4. Infiltration & EmbeddingThe tissue is placed in melted paraffin wax, which infiltrates the cells. The wax then hardens into a solid block.
5. SectioningThe paraffin block is sliced into extremely thin sections (typically 5-10 micrometers) using a microtome.
6. StainingThe tissue sections are mounted on a glass slide and stained with dyes to make different components visible.

The most common staining combination is Hematoxylin and Eosin (H&E). Hematoxylin stains acidic structures (like the cell nucleus) a purplish-blue, while Eosin stains basic structures (like the cytoplasm) pink.
