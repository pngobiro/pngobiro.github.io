📚 Centrally acting agents: Methyldopa

Centrally acting agents are antihypertensive drugs that work within the central nervous system (CNS) to lower blood pressure. Methyldopa is a key example of a centrally acting antihypertensive.

Methyldopa: Aldomin

  • Trade name: Aldomin.
  • Classification: Antihypertensive, centrally acting antiadrenergic agent.
  • Action: Methyldopa lowers blood pressure by:
    • Stimulating Central Inhibitory Alpha-Adrenergic Receptors: The active metabolite of methyldopa, alpha-methylnorepinephrine, lowers blood pressure (BP) by stimulating central inhibitory alpha-adrenergic receptors in the brain.
    • Reduced Sympathetic Outflow: This central action reduces sympathetic outflow from the brainstem to the peripheral cardiovascular system, leading to decreased peripheral vascular resistance and reduced blood pressure.
  • Uses: Primarily used for:
    • Hypertension: Effective in managing essential hypertension, particularly in pregnant women due to its established safety profile in pregnancy.
    • Hypertensive crises: Can be used parenterally (IV) to manage hypertensive emergencies, although less common now with newer agents available.
  • Contraindications:
    • Hypersensitivity to methyldopa.
    • Active hepatic diseases: Liver diseases like hepatitis or cirrhosis, as methyldopa can cause hepatotoxicity.
    • Pheochromocytoma: Tumor of the adrenal gland, as methyldopa can paradoxically increase blood pressure in these patients.
  • Side effects: Common side effects include:
    • Central Nervous System: Headache, dizziness, general weakness, marked drowsiness, depression, and sedation, especially at the start of therapy.
    • Cardiovascular: Bradycardia (slow heart rate), orthostatic hypotension (drop in blood pressure upon standing).
    • Gastrointestinal: Dry mouth, nausea, vomiting, sore or "black" tongue.
    • Hepatic: Jaundice and liver disorders, including hepatitis (rare but serious). Regular liver function tests are recommended.
    • Hematologic: Hemolytic anemia (rare), leukopenia (decreased white blood cells).
    • Other: Male impotence.
  • Dosage:
    • Tablets: Initially 250 mg twice or three times daily (bid or tid).

Nursing considerations:-

  • Activities to avoid: Avoid activities that require mental alertness such as driving, operating machinery, especially when starting therapy or increasing dosage, due to drowsiness and dizziness.
  • Jaundice monitoring: Note any evidence of jaundice, and monitor liver function tests at regular intervals, especially during long-term therapy.
  • Orthostatic Hypotension: Advise patients to rise slowly from sitting or lying positions to minimize orthostatic hypotension and dizziness.
  • Reportable Symptoms: Instruct patients to report any bothersome or persistent side effects, especially signs of liver dysfunction (jaundice, abdominal pain, dark urine) or CNS depression.
  • Urine Discoloration: Explain to patients that urine may rarely turn dark or blue in color, which is a harmless effect of the medication.
  • Medical ID: Advise clients to carry a card detailing their current medication regimen, especially noting methyldopa use, to inform healthcare providers in emergency situations.

📚 5. Agents that act directly on vascular smooth muscles:- Hydralazine hydrochloride

Hydralazine hydrochloride (Apresoline) is a direct-acting vasodilator that lowers blood pressure by directly relaxing arteriolar smooth muscle, reducing peripheral resistance.

Hydralazine hydrochloride: Apresoline

  • Trade name: Apresoline.
  • Classification: Antihypertensive, direct-acting vasodilator.
  • Action: Hydralazine directly affects vascular smooth muscles, causing vasodilation, particularly in arterioles. This action leads to:
    • Reduced Peripheral Resistance: Directly dilates arterioles, reducing peripheral vascular resistance, which lowers blood pressure.
    • Increased Cardiac Output: By reducing afterload (resistance against which the heart pumps), hydralazine can increase cardiac output and improve blood flow to organs like the brain and kidneys.
  • Uses: Primarily used for:
    • Hypertension: Often used in combination with other antihypertensive agents, such as diuretics and beta-blockers, to manage moderate to severe hypertension.
    • Hypertensive emergencies: Can be administered parenterally (IV or IM) in hypertensive emergencies to rapidly lower blood pressure.
  • Contraindications:
    • Angina pectoris: May exacerbate angina by causing reflex tachycardia and increased myocardial oxygen demand.
    • Rheumatic heart disease: Use with caution.
    • Chronic glomerulonephritis: Use with caution in renal disease.
    • Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE): Hydralazine can induce a lupus-like syndrome.
  • Side effects: Common side effects include:
    • Cardiovascular: Orthostatic hypotension, reflex tachycardia (increased heart rate in response to lowered blood pressure), palpitations.
    • Neurological: Headache, dizziness.
    • Gastrointestinal: Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea.
    • Other: Constipation, male impotence, nasal congestion.
  • Dosage:
    • Tablets: Initially 10 mg four times daily (qid) for 2-4 days, then increased to 25 mg twice daily (bid). Dosage is adjusted based on blood pressure response and tolerance.
    • I.V. or I.M.: 50 mg, administered IV slowly or IM, repeated as necessary in hypertensive emergencies. May decrease blood pressure within 5 minutes of IV administration.

Comparison of Methyldopa and Hydralazine Hydrochloride:

  • Mechanisms of Action:
    • Methyldopa (Centrally Acting Agent): Lowers blood pressure by stimulating alpha-adrenergic receptors in the brain, which reduces sympathetic outflow, leading to decreased peripheral vascular resistance and heart rate. It acts centrally within the CNS.
    • Hydralazine Hydrochloride (Direct Vasodilator): Lowers blood pressure by directly relaxing vascular smooth muscle, primarily in arterioles, leading to decreased peripheral vascular resistance and afterload. It acts directly on blood vessels.
  • Primary Uses in Hypertension Management:
    • Methyldopa:
      • Gestational Hypertension: Particularly favored for managing hypertension during pregnancy due to its long-established safety record for both mother and fetus.
      • Essential Hypertension: Can be used for chronic hypertension, often as a second- or third-line agent due to its side effect profile.
      • Hypertensive Crises: Can be used intravenously in hypertensive emergencies, although less common now compared to newer agents.
    • Hydralazine Hydrochloride:
      • Moderate to Severe Hypertension: Used in combination therapy for managing hypertension, especially when multiple drugs are needed.
      • Hypertensive Emergencies: Effective for rapid blood pressure reduction in hypertensive emergencies, particularly via parenteral administration.
      • Heart Failure: In heart failure, particularly in African American patients, combined with isosorbide dinitrate to reduce afterload and improve cardiac output.
  • Major Side Effects:
    • Methyldopa:
      • CNS Depression: Marked drowsiness, sedation, dizziness, depression, and impaired mental alertness are common, limiting its use in patients needing to be alert.
      • Orthostatic Hypotension: Significant postural hypotension.
      • Hepatic Issues: Risk of jaundice and liver disorders.
      • Other: Dry mouth, nausea, and potential hematologic side effects.
    • Hydralazine Hydrochloride:
      • Reflex Tachycardia and Palpitations: Common due to rapid vasodilation, which can increase heart rate and cause palpitations.
      • Orthostatic Hypotension: Significant postural hypotension.
      • Headache and Dizziness: Vasodilation-induced headache and dizziness.
      • Lupus-like Syndrome: Risk of drug-induced lupus erythematosus with long-term use.

    Conditions Favoring Use of Each Drug:

    • Methyldopa Favored When:
      • Pregnancy-Induced Hypertension: Preferred for hypertension management during pregnancy due to its safety profile.
      • Patients Needing Central Nervous System Effects: In specific cases where central sympatholytic action is desired, although CNS depression side effects are a significant consideration.
    • Hydralazine Hydrochloride Favored When:
      • Rapid Blood Pressure Reduction Needed: In hypertensive emergencies or situations requiring quick-acting vasodilation, particularly when given IV.
      • Adjunctive Therapy in Heart Failure: Especially in combination with nitrates for heart failure management to reduce afterload.
      • Patients Tolerating Reflex Tachycardia: In patients who can tolerate the reflex tachycardia and other vasodilator-related side effects.

📚 Key Takeaways

  • Centrally acting agents like Methyldopa lower BP by reducing sympathetic outflow from the brainstem; Methyldopa is favored in pregnancy hypertension.
  • Methyldopa side effects include drowsiness, hypotension, and liver issues; nursing care involves monitoring mental alertness and liver function.
  • Hydralazine hydrochloride, a direct vasodilator, acts directly on arterioles to reduce peripheral resistance, used in hypertension and emergencies.
  • Hydralazine side effects include reflex tachycardia and orthostatic hypotension; contraindicated in angina pectoris and certain heart conditions.
