Oral Contraceptives Side Effects and Considerations

üéØ Learning Objectives

  • Identify the contraindications for using combination oral contraceptives.
  • Describe the common and serious side effects associated with oral contraceptive use.
  • Explain the drug interactions that can affect the efficacy or increase the risks of oral contraceptives.
  • Detail the nursing considerations for women using oral contraceptives, emphasizing patient education and monitoring.

üìö Contraindications of Oral Contraceptives

While oral contraceptives (OCs) are generally safe and effective, certain conditions and factors contraindicate their use due to increased health risks. Major contraindications include:

  1. History of cerebrovascular diseases (stroke or transient ischemic attack - TIA): Estrogen-containing contraceptives can increase the risk of thromboembolic events, including stroke.
  2. History of or current thromboembolic conditions: Deep vein thrombosis (DVT) or pulmonary embolism (PE), as OCs increase clotting risk.
  3. Coronary artery disease or other ischemic heart disease: Increased risk of cardiovascular events.
  4. Cancer breast or history of estrogen-dependent tumors: Estrogens can stimulate the growth of hormone-sensitive cancers.
  5. Impaired hepatic function or active liver disease: Liver is crucial for hormone metabolism; impaired function can lead to drug accumulation and increased risks.
  6. Renal or cardiac diseases: Conditions that may be exacerbated by fluid retention and hormonal effects of OCs.
  7. Undiagnosed abnormal genital bleeding: Until evaluated to rule out serious underlying conditions.
  8. Pregnancy: Known or suspected pregnancy (Category X drugs).
  9. Smokers over age 35: Significantly increased risk of cardiovascular side effects, especially thromboembolism and myocardial infarction, in women over 35 who smoke and use estrogen-containing contraceptives.

üìö Side effects of Oral Contraceptives

Oral contraceptives can cause a range of side effects, varying in severity and frequency among users. Common and potential side effects include:

  1. Cardiovascular:
    • Hypertension: Can cause or exacerbate hypertension due to estrogen-induced sodium and water retention and effects on the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system.
    • Thromboembolic disorders: Increased risk of venous thromboembolism (DVT, PE), myocardial infarction (MI), and stroke, especially in smokers, women over 35, and those with predisposing conditions. Risk is higher with estrogen-containing COCs than progestin-only pills.
  2. Metabolic:
    • Weight gain: Fluid retention and anabolic effects of hormones can lead to weight gain.
    • Altered lipid profiles: Can cause changes in cholesterol and triglyceride levels.
    • Impaired glucose tolerance: May affect glucose metabolism, particularly concerning for women with pre-diabetes or diabetes.
  3. Dermatologic:
    • Oily skin and acne: Hormonal changes can affect skin oil production.
    • Hirsutism: Increased hair growth in a male-like pattern in women due to progestin component with androgenic activity.
    • Melasma (chloasma or "mask of pregnancy"): Darkening of skin on the face, exacerbated by sun exposure.
  4. Neurological:
    • Headache and migraine: Changes in hormone levels can trigger or worsen headaches and migraines.
    • Mood changes: Mood swings, depression, anxiety.
  5. Breast Changes:
    • Breast tenderness and enlargement: Hormonal effects can cause breast discomfort and increased size.
  6. Menstrual Changes:
    • Breakthrough bleeding and spotting: Common, especially in the first few months of use, as the body adjusts to hormonal changes.
    • Changes in menstrual flow: Can alter menstrual flow, often leading to lighter and more regular periods.
    • Amenorrhea: In some cases, periods may become very light or cease altogether (amenorrhea), especially with prolonged use or certain formulations.
    • Anxiety & decrease in menstrual flow.
  7. Other:
    • Fluid retention and edema: Due to estrogen and progestin effects on sodium and water balance.
    • Decreased quantity & quality of breast milk: Estrogen-containing contraceptives can reduce breast milk production and alter milk composition, which is a concern for breastfeeding mothers.

üìö Nursing considerations

Nursing considerations:

  • 1- Tablets should be taken approximately at the same time each day to maintain consistent hormone levels, enhancing contraceptive efficacy. Consistency is key for effectiveness. Take with meal or at bed time to reduce nausea and improve absorption.
  • 2- Spotting bleeding may occur 1-2 first days of the cycle, which is common, especially during the first few months of use as the body adjusts to the hormones. If spotting or breakthrough bleeding continues beyond the first few cycles or becomes heavy, instruct patient to notify the physician to rule out other causes and consider alternative formulations.
  • 3- For the 21 day regimen ,tablet is taken daily beginning on day 5 of the cycle (No tablets are taken for 7 days) to mimic a typical menstrual cycle and allow for withdrawal bleeding during the hormone-free interval.
  • 4- For the 28 day regimen, tablets are taken for the first 21days following by 7 days of iron containing tablets (or placebo) to maintain a daily pill-taking habit and often include iron supplements to address potential iron loss during menstruation, although the primary purpose of the 7-day break is to allow for withdrawal bleeding.
  • 5- If a woman fails to take one or more tablets, the following recommendations should be followed to maintain contraception and manage missed doses:
    • If 1 tablet is missed: It should be taken as soon as it is remembered, even if it means taking two pills in one day. Contraceptive protection is usually maintained if only one pill is missed. Alternatively 2 tablets can be taken the following day to catch up.
    • If 2 tablets are missed (in a row, especially at the beginning of the cycle): Take two tablets as soon as remembered and two tablets the next day. Use backup contraception (non-hormonal methods like condoms) for the next 7 days to prevent pregnancy, as contraceptive protection may be reduced. alternatively 2 tablets can be taken on the day the missed tablets are remembered with the second missed tablet being discarded.
    • If 3 tablets are missed (during the first two weeks of the cycle): Discontinue the current pack and start a new pack 7 days after the last tablet was taken & additional contraceptive method should be used until the start of the next menstrual period. A new medication cycle should be initiated to re-establish hormonal control.
  • 6- Advise the client if she develops pain in the legs or chest, dizziness to discontinue the therapy & notify the physician, as these may be signs of thromboembolic disorders or cardiovascular complications, requiring immediate medical evaluation.
  • 7- Advise the client prior to initiate therapy that there is a high risk for cancer of breast, especially in women with a family history of breast cancer or genetic predispositions. Discuss risk vs. benefits.
  • 8- Instruct client to avoid smoking, especially if over 35 years old, as smoking significantly increases the risk of cardiovascular side effects, including blood clots, heart attack, and stroke, in women taking estrogen-containing oral contraceptives.
  • 9- If a woman is breasted, instruct her to find other form of contraception because estrogen-containing OCs can decrease the quantity and quality of breast milk. Progestin-only pills or non-hormonal methods might be more suitable for breastfeeding mothers.

Question: A 30-year-old woman who smokes and has a family history of breast cancer is seeking advice on contraception. Discuss the contraindications and risks associated with combination oral contraceptives for her, and what alternative contraceptive methods might be more suitable.

View Solution

Contraindications and Risks of COCs for a 30-year-old Smoker with Family History of Breast Cancer:

  • Smoking Over Age 35: Being a smoker, especially over the age of 35, is a significant contraindication for combination oral contraceptives (COCs). Smoking greatly increases the risk of serious cardiovascular side effects, such as:
    • Thromboembolism: Increased risk of blood clots, including deep vein thrombosis (DVT) and pulmonary embolism (PE).
    • Myocardial Infarction (Heart Attack) and Stroke: Smoking combined with estrogen-containing contraceptives sharply elevates the risk of heart attack and stroke due to increased clotting tendency and vascular effects of smoking and estrogen.
  • Family History of Breast Cancer: A family history of breast cancer, while not an absolute contraindication, raises concerns about using estrogen-containing contraceptives.
    • Potential Increased Breast Cancer Risk: Estrogen can stimulate the growth of hormone-sensitive breast cancer cells. Although the overall evidence is complex and somewhat debated, there is a theoretical and some observed increased risk of breast cancer with long-term estrogen exposure, especially in women with a family history, suggesting caution.

Risks Outweigh Benefits: For a 30-year-old woman who smokes and has a family history of breast cancer, the risks associated with combination oral contraceptives generally outweigh the benefits of contraception in most cases. The combination of smoking and estrogen significantly elevates cardiovascular risks, and family history adds to the concern regarding breast cancer risk.

Alternative Contraceptive Methods: More suitable contraceptive methods with lower risks for this patient include:

  1. Progestin-Only Pills (POPs or Mini-Pills):
    • Lower Thromboembolic Risk: Progestin-only pills do not contain estrogen and therefore have a significantly lower risk of thromboembolic events, making them safer for smokers and women at higher risk of cardiovascular issues.
    • Suitable for Breastfeeding: POPs are also safe for breastfeeding mothers as they do not significantly affect breast milk production.
    • Slightly Less Effective: Slightly less effective than COCs in preventing pregnancy and require very strict timing for consistent efficacy.
  2. Non-Hormonal Methods:
    • Barrier Methods: Condoms (male and female), diaphragms, and cervical caps. These methods do not involve hormones and have no cardiovascular or cancer risk implications. They also offer protection against sexually transmitted infections (STIs).
    • Copper Intrauterine Device (IUD): A highly effective, long-acting reversible contraceptive (LARC) method that is hormone-free and does not carry the same risks as estrogen-containing contraceptives. Insertion and removal require a healthcare provider.
    • Sterilization: For women who have completed childbearing, permanent sterilization options like tubal ligation are highly effective and eliminate contraceptive risks.

Recommendation: For this patient, non-hormonal methods like copper IUD or barrier methods, or progestin-only methods, would be safer and more appropriate options than combination oral contraceptives. A thorough discussion of risks and benefits of all contraceptive options should be conducted to help the patient make an informed decision based on her health profile and preferences.

üìö Key Takeaways

  • Oral contraceptives (OCs) are contraindicated in women over 35 who smoke, with thromboembolic history, breast cancer, or liver disease due to increased risks.
  • Side effects of OCs include hypertension, thromboembolism, weight gain, mood changes; ACHES warning signs (Abdominal pain, Chest pain, Headaches, Eye problems, Severe leg pain) indicate serious complications.
  • Nursing considerations: ensure consistent daily intake, manage missed doses with specific guidelines, educate patients on ACHES signs, smoking risks, and need for regular check-ups.
