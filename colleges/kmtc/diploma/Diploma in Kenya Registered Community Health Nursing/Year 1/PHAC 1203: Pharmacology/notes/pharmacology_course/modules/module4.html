Drug Excretion and Bioavailability

üéØ Learning Objectives

  • Define drug excretion and its significance in pharmacokinetics.
  • Identify the major routes of drug elimination from the body.
  • Explain the concept of onset of action and factors that influence it.
  • Describe peak of activity and duration of drug action.
  • Define bioavailability and factors affecting it.

üìö Excretion: Eliminating Drugs from the Body

Excretion is the process by which drugs and their pharmacologically active or inactive metabolites are eliminated from the body. This process is crucial for decreasing drug levels in the body and terminating their effects. The main routes of drug elimination include:

  1. Kidney: The primary route of excretion for most drugs and their metabolites is through the kidneys into the urine.
  2. Lung: Volatile liquids and gases, such as general anesthetics, are excreted via the lungs in expired air.
  3. Intestine: Some agents are eliminated through the intestine in feces, primarily via biliary excretion (excretion in bile).
  4. Sweat and salivary glands: Minor routes of excretion include sweat and salivary glands, which can sometimes cause skin rashes as a side effect.
  5. Mammary glands: Drugs can be transferred from mother to baby through mammary glands during breastfeeding.

üí° Pro Tip

Renal disease significantly increases the risk of drug accumulation and toxicity because the kidney is the major organ for drug excretion. Dosage adjustments are often necessary in patients with impaired renal function, and some drugs may be contraindicated.

üìö Onset of Action

Onset of action refers to the time interval between drug administration and the first observable therapeutic effect. It depends on several factors:

  1. Route of administration: IV administration generally has the fastest onset, followed by IM, subcutaneous, and oral routes.
  2. Characteristics of the drug: Factors such as solubility and ionization affect how quickly a drug can be absorbed and reach its target site.
  3. Drug‚Äôs rate of absorption through various membranes: The ease with which a drug crosses biological membranes influences its absorption rate and onset of action.
  4. Formulation of the dose: Liquid formulations are generally absorbed faster than solid forms, affecting onset time.

üí° Pro Tip

Onset of action is particularly variable after oral administration due to factors like food presence in the stomach, gastrointestinal (GI) motility, and other individual physiological factors.

üìö Peak of Activity and Duration of Action

  1. Peak of activity: This is when the drug reaches its maximum effect, often coinciding with the peak serum concentration. Some drugs may cause the serum concentration to surpass the optimal therapeutic level, potentially leading to side effects. The concentration then falls due to biotransformation and excretion. This peak effect is often shorter with short-acting drugs, especially when administered initially or intermittently.
  2. Duration of action: This is the period between drug administration and the complete excretion of the drug (or its metabolites) from the body. It determines how long the drug's therapeutic effect lasts.

üí° Pro Tip

Example: In diabetes treatment, insulin formulations with varying durations of action are mixed to maintain therapeutic insulin levels throughout the day and night.

üìö Bioavailability

Bioavailability measures the fraction of the administered dosage of a drug that reaches the systemic circulation in a chemically unchanged form and becomes available at the site of drug action or in body fluids (like serum) to exert a therapeutic effect. It is a function of:

  1. The drug itself: Drug properties such as solubility and first-pass metabolism affect bioavailability.
  2. The metabolism of the patient: Individual differences in metabolism can alter bioavailability.
  3. The rate at which the drug is liberated from its dosage form or from storage in the body: How quickly the drug is released from its formulation or storage sites (like fat or muscle) influences its bioavailability.

üí° Pro Tip

Example: Many drugs bind to serum proteins (like albumin), from which they are gradually released. Others may be stored in specific sites, such as bone (e.g., tetracycline). Drugs are considered not bioavailable if they are:

  • Bound to protein or another substance, rendering them permanently or temporarily inactive.
  • Not released from their dosage form or storage site.
  • Partially or totally degraded before reaching systemic circulation.

‚úçÔ∏è Practice Exercise

Question: Compare and contrast the onset of action and duration of action for intravenously (IV) versus orally administered drugs. Explain how bioavailability differs between these routes.

View Solution

Onset of action: IV drugs typically have a much faster onset of action compared to orally administered drugs. IV administration places the drug directly into the bloodstream, resulting in immediate therapeutic effects. Oral drugs, on the other hand, must undergo absorption from the gastrointestinal tract, leading to a delayed onset.

Duration of action: The duration of action can vary depending on the drug's properties and metabolism, but the route of administration also plays a role. Initially, IV drugs might have a shorter duration of peak effect if rapidly metabolized and excreted. However, the overall duration is more dependent on the drug's pharmacokinetic properties than the route itself, assuming similar doses.

Bioavailability: IV drugs have 100% bioavailability because they are directly introduced into the systemic circulation, bypassing absorption barriers. Orally administered drugs typically have lower bioavailability due to incomplete absorption and first-pass metabolism in the liver, where a portion of the drug is metabolized before reaching systemic circulation.

üìö Key Takeaways

  • Drug excretion is the process of eliminating drugs and metabolites from the body, primarily through the kidneys.
  • Onset of action is the time to the first therapeutic effect, influenced by administration route, drug characteristics, absorption rate, and formulation.
  • Peak of activity is when the drug effect is maximal, often correlating with peak serum concentration, while duration of action is the length of time the drug remains effective.
  • Bioavailability refers to the fraction of the administered drug that reaches systemic circulation unchanged, influenced by drug properties, patient metabolism, and dosage form liberation.
