üìö Chronic toxicity of narcotic analgesics

Chronic toxicity from narcotic analgesics primarily involves the development of dependence and tolerance, which are significant concerns with long-term opioid use. Key aspects of chronic toxicity include:

  • Chronic Dependence: Long-term use of narcotics, even when medically prescribed, can lead to chronic dependence. This is a well-recognized problem not only among illicit drug users but also among patients using narcotics for chronic pain management. Easy access to narcotics for healthcare professionals (physicians, nurses, pharmacists) increases the risk of dependence.
  • Non-Therapeutic Use: Narcotic analgesics are sometimes misused for non-therapeutic purposes, driven by their euphoric effects and addictive potential.

üìö Signs & symptoms of chronic narcotic analgesics use and withdrawal

Signs & symptoms of chronic use:

  • Constricted pupils (miosis).
  • Constipation due to reduced bowel motility.
  • Skin infections and needle scar abscesses in intravenous drug users.
  • Itching on the anterior surface of the body, a common dermatologic complaint.

Withdrawal symptoms:

  • Onset: Withdrawal symptoms appear when drugs is withheld for 4-12 hrs. & characterized by intense craving for the drug, insomnia, yawning, sneezing, vomiting, diarrhea, tremors, sweating, mental depression, muscular aches, pain, chills & anxiety.

    • üí° Pro Tip

    N.B.: While highly uncomfortable and distressing, opioid withdrawal syndrome is typically not life-threatening in otherwise healthy adults, although medical supervision and supportive care are important.

üìö Uses, Contraindications and Side effects of narcotic analgesics

Uses:

  1. Sever pain: Management of severe acute or chronic pain, such as postoperative pain, cancer pain, and pain from trauma.
  2. Hepatic and renal colic: Relief of severe pain associated with hepatic and renal colic.
  3. Preanesthetic medication: To provide analgesia and sedation before surgery.
  4. Postsurgical pain: Management of pain following surgical procedures.
  5. Diarrhea & dysentry: To decrease intestinal motility and relieve severe diarrhea or dysentery (though less common now due to other antidiarrheals).
  6. Pain from MI, carcinoma: Relief of severe pain associated with MI and carcinoma.
  7. Postpartum pain & burns: Management of severe pain after childbirth or burns.
  8. Antitussive: Codeine and hydrocodone, in lower doses, are used as antitussives to suppress cough.

Contraindications:

  1. Asthmatic conditions
  2. Emphysema
  3. Sever obesity
  4. Convulsions
  5. Diabetic acidosis
  6. Myxedema
  7. Addisson's disease
  8. Hepatic cirrhosis

Question: Describe the chronic toxicity associated with narcotic analgesics. What are the main signs and symptoms of chronic opioid use and opioid withdrawal syndrome?

View Solution

Chronic Toxicity Associated with Narcotic Analgesics: Chronic toxicity primarily manifests as dependence and tolerance, which are significant concerns with long-term use.

Chronic Dependence:

  • Psychological Dependence (Addiction): A psychological drive to take opioids for their euphoric effects or to relieve distress, characterized by compulsive drug-seeking behavior.
  • Physical Dependence: Physiological adaptation to chronic opioid exposure, where the body requires the drug to function normally. Abrupt cessation leads to withdrawal symptoms.
  • Tolerance: Reduced responsiveness to a drug with prolonged use, requiring higher doses to achieve the same effect. This necessitates dose escalation over time to maintain pain relief, further increasing dependence risk.

Signs and Symptoms of Chronic Opioid Use and Opioid Withdrawal Syndrome:

  • Signs and Symptoms of Chronic Opioid Use:
    • Constricted Pupils (Miosis): Persistent pinpoint pupils due to opioid receptor activation.
    • Constipation: Chronic constipation due to reduced bowel motility.
    • Skin and Injection Site Issues: In intravenous drug users, signs include skin infections, cellulitis, and needle scar abscesses at injection sites.
    • Pruritus: Chronic itching, particularly on the anterior surface of the body, due to histamine release.
  • Opioid Withdrawal Syndrome: Occurs upon abrupt cessation or significant dose reduction in opioid-dependent individuals. Symptoms include:
    • Early Symptoms (4-12 hours after last dose): Yawning, lacrimation (tearing), rhinorrhea (runny nose), diaphoresis (sweating), anxiety, restlessness, insomnia, increased craving for opioids.
    • Late Symptoms (peak within 72 hours): More intense and severe symptoms include:
      • Gastrointestinal: Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal cramps.
      • Musculoskeletal: Muscle aches and pains, tremors, goosebumps (piloerection), kicking movements (restless legs).
      • Cardiovascular: Tachycardia, hypertension.
      • Psychological: Marked anxiety, irritability, depression, panic attacks, intense drug craving.
      • General: Chills, fever, dehydration, significant discomfort and distress.

üìö Key Takeaways

  • Chronic use of narcotic analgesics leads to tolerance and dependence; withdrawal symptoms include intense craving, GI distress, and CNS excitability.
  • Narcotic analgesics act by binding to specific receptor in the CNS resulting in analgesia- action 121 - Action exactly is unknown but may be by decreasing cell membrane permeability to sodium transmission of pain impulses.
  • Uses include severe pain management (acute, chronic, cancer, post-operative), hepatic and renal colic, and as antitussives.
  • Contraindications include respiratory conditions, obesity, convulsions, and hepatic cirrhosis. Side effects: respiratory depression, constipation, nausea, sedation.
