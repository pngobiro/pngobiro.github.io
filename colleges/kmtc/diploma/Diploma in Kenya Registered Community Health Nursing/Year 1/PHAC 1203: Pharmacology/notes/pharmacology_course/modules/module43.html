üìö Dosage and Nursing Considerations for Nitrates/nitrites

Nitrates and nitrites are available in several dosage forms, each suited for different clinical scenarios:

  1. Dosage Forms:
    • Sublingual Tablets: For immediate relief of acute angina attacks. Example: Cordil 5 mg PRN (as needed).
    • Oral Tablets and Capsules: For regular, longer-term prophylaxis against angina. Example: Isotard 20 ‚Äì 40 mg twice a day.
    • Topical Ointments and Patches: For sustained release and prolonged prophylaxis. Topical: available as patches or ointment.
    • Parenteral (IV Infusion): For acute, severe angina or unstable angina requiring immediate and continuous nitrate administration. Parental (IV infusion).
  2. Nursing Considerations:
    • Administration:
      • Empty Stomach: Administer medications, especially oral forms, on an empty stomach to enhance absorption, unless GI upset occurs, in which case they can be taken with a light snack.
      • Sublingual Tablets Handling: Advise patients to carry sublingual tablets in their original glass container, tightly capped, to prevent degradation from light and moisture.
      • Sublingual Administration Technique: Instruct patients on the proper technique for taking sublingual tablets:
        • At the onset of anginal pain, place one tablet under the tongue and let it dissolve completely. Avoid swallowing until fully dissolved to ensure sublingual absorption.
        • If pain is not relieved within 5 minutes after the first tablet, take a second tablet. If pain persists after a total of 3 tablets taken at 5-minute intervals, seek immediate medical attention in an emergency room. Do not exceed three tablets in 15 minutes without medical supervision.
        • Take sublingual tablets 5-15 minutes prior to activities likely to induce angina, such as exercise or emotional stress, for prophylactic effect.
        • Advise taking sublingual tablets while sitting or lying down to avoid postural hypotension and prevent falls due to dizziness.
      • Management of Headache: Headache is a common side effect due to vasodilation. Reassure patients that it is usually transient and often diminishes with continued use. Analgesics like acetaminophen may be used to manage headache if needed.
      • Isosorbide Mononitrate for Liver Impairment: Isosorbide mononitrate may be preferred in patients with liver impairments as it undergoes less hepatic metabolism compared to other nitrates.

üìö Isosorbide dinitrate: Detailed Look

Isosorbide dinitrate is another nitrate commonly used for angina management, available in various forms for both acute and chronic treatment. Key features include:

  • Dosage Forms: Available in multiple formulations:
    • Capsules (chewable and sustained-release)
    • Sublingual tablets
    • Tablets (immediate and extended-release)
  • Trade Names: Cordil, Isotard, Sorbitrate, Isordil.
  • Class: Coronary vasodilator, nitrate.
  • Dosage Forms & Strengths:
    • Capsules: 20-40 mg (sustained release)
    • Tablets: 5-40 mg (immediate release and chewable), 20-40 mg (sublingual)
  • Uses:
    • Tablets (immediate release): Primarily for prophylaxis of anginal pain‚Äîtaken regularly to prevent angina attacks.
    • Chewable and Sublingual Tablets: To terminate acute angina attacks and relieve acute pain rapidly. Sublingual route allows for quick absorption and fast relief.
    • Esophageal Spasm: May be used to relieve esophageal spasm due to its smooth muscle relaxant properties.
  • Side Effects: Similar to other nitrates, common side effects include:
    • Headache
    • Hypotension, including postural hypotension

Patient Education on Using Sublingual Nitroglycerin and Oral Isosorbide Dinitrate:

  • Sublingual Nitroglycerin (for Acute Angina):
    • Purpose: To relieve chest pain during an acute angina attack quickly. Keep these tablets readily accessible at all times.
    • Administration:
      1. At the first sign of chest pain, stop activity and sit or lie down.
      2. Place one tablet under your tongue and let it dissolve completely. Do not swallow or chew it; allow for complete sublingual absorption.
      3. If pain persists after 5 minutes, take another tablet, and repeat up to three tablets, 5 minutes apart. If pain is not relieved after three tablets in 15 minutes, call emergency services immediately.
    • Storage: Store tablets in their original dark glass container, tightly closed, and away from heat, moisture, and light. Replace your prescription every six months, as they degrade once opened.
    • Side Effects: Be aware of potential side effects like headache, dizziness, and lightheadedness, which are common. Sit down when taking nitroglycerin to avoid falls from dizziness.
  • Oral Isosorbide Dinitrate (for Angina Prophylaxis):
    • Purpose: To prevent angina attacks from occurring. Take this medication regularly as prescribed, even when feeling well.
    • Administration:
      1. Take oral tablets or capsules as prescribed, usually once or twice daily. Follow the specific instructions for your formulation (immediate release, extended release, or chewable).
      2. Take on an empty stomach with a full glass of water for better absorption, unless food is recommended to reduce stomach upset.
      3. Do not crush or chew extended-release tablets or capsules; swallow them whole. Chewable tablets should be chewed thoroughly before swallowing.
    • Side Effects: Similar to nitroglycerin, watch for headache, dizziness, and hypotension. Rise slowly from sitting or lying positions to minimize dizziness.
  • When to Use Which Medication:
    • Use Sublingual Nitroglycerin: For sudden onset of chest pain (acute angina attack).
    • Use Oral Isosorbide Dinitrate: Daily to prevent angina attacks from happening (prophylaxis). It is not meant to provide immediate relief during an acute attack.
  • General Advice:
    • Keep a record of angina attacks, noting what triggers them and how medications help.
    • Always carry sublingual nitroglycerin with you.
    • Inform all healthcare providers about both medications.
    • Avoid alcohol as it can enhance the hypotensive effects of both drugs.

üìö Key Takeaways

  • Nitrates/nitrites come in sublingual (fast-acting), oral (prophylaxis), topical, and IV forms, each for specific angina management needs.
  • Sublingual Nitroglycerin is for acute angina relief; instruct patients on proper use, storage, and when to seek emergency help.
  • Isosorbide dinitrate is used for angina prophylaxis; available in various oral forms, with headache and hypotension as common side effects.
  • Nursing education is crucial for patients using both short-acting (Nitroglycerin) and long-acting (Isosorbide) nitrates to understand their distinct roles and proper administration techniques.
