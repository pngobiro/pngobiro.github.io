Tetracyclines

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Describe the mechanism of action of tetracycline antibiotics.
  • Identify the therapeutic uses of tetracyclines, including specific infections and conditions.
  • Discuss the contraindications and side effects associated with tetracycline use.
  • Detail the nursing considerations for patients receiving tetracycline therapy, emphasizing administration guidelines and patient education.

📚 Tetracyclines: Broad-Spectrum Antibiotics

Tetracyclines are a class of broad-spectrum antibiotics effective against a wide range of bacteria, including gram-positive and gram-negative bacteria, as well as other microorganisms like mycoplasmas, rickettsiae, and chlamydiae. However, due to increasing resistance, their use has become more selective. Key aspects of tetracyclines include:

  • Spectrum of Activity: Broad-spectrum antibiotics, effective against many bacterial types, but resistance is a growing concern limiting their widespread use for common infections.
  • Mechanism of Action: Tetracyclines inhibit bacterial protein synthesis, which is essential for bacterial growth and function.

📚 Action and Uses of Tetracyclines

Action:

  • Inhibition of Protein Synthesis: Tetracyclines inhibit bacterial protein synthesis by binding to the 30S ribosomal subunit of bacteria. This binding prevents the attachment of aminoacyl-tRNA to the ribosome, thus blocking the addition of amino acids to the growing peptide chain and halting protein synthesis.
  • Bacteriostatic Effect: The drugs are primarily bacteriostatic, meaning they inhibit bacterial growth and reproduction, but do not typically kill bacteria directly. The host's immune system then clears the infection. They are bactericidal at high concentrations against certain organisms.

Uses:

Due to resistance, tetracyclines are now selectively used for specific infections and conditions:

  • Rickettsial Infections: Drugs of choice for rickettsial infections such as:
    • Rocky Mountain spotted fever
    • Endemic typhus
  • Mycoplasma Infections: Effective against infections caused by Mycoplasma pneumoniae, such as atypical pneumonia and urethritis due to mycoplasma.
  • Plague Prophylaxis: Used for prophylaxis of plague after exposure.
  • Trachoma and Amebiasis: Adjunct treatment for trachoma (chronic infectious eye disease) and acute intestinal amebiasis.
  • Gram-negative Bacteria Infections: Drug of choice for infections caused by certain gram-negative bacteria, including:
    • Cholera
    • Brucellosis
  • Gonorrhea Alternative: Alternative treatment to penicillin for uncomplicated gonorrhea in penicillin-allergic patients (though resistance is increasing).
  • Other Uses:
    • Vaginitis
    • Severe acne vulgaris
    • Topical applications for skin granulomas and certain dermatological conditions.

📚 Contraindications and Side effects

Contraindications:

  • Hypersensitivity: Allergy to tetracyclines.
  • Pregnancy and Lactation: Contraindicated during pregnancy (last trimester) and breastfeeding due to potential harm to the fetus and infant.
  • Children Under 8 Years: Contraindicated in children under 8 years old because tetracyclines can cause permanent teeth discoloration and affect bone growth. Should be avoided during tooth development stage.

Side effects:

Tetracyclines are associated with various side effects, some common and others more serious:

  • Common Side Effects:
    • Gastrointestinal: Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal discomfort, flatulence, sore throat, anorexia (loss of appetite). GI irritation is common and can limit tolerability.
    • Dermatologic: Skin rashes, photosensitivity (increased sensitivity to sunlight, leading to sunburn easily).
    • Discoloration: Discoloration of nails and teeth (permanent teeth staining in infants and children if given during tooth development).
    • Black hairy tongue: A benign condition characterized by a dark, furry appearance of the tongue.
  • Serious Side Effects:
    • Bone Growth and Teeth Effects: Delayed bone growth in children and permanent teeth discoloration if used during tooth development (pregnancy, infancy, childhood up to 8 years).
    • Hepatotoxicity: Liver damage, especially with high doses or pre-existing liver disease.
    • Nephrotoxicity: Kidney damage, particularly with outdated tetracyclines or in patients with renal impairment.
    • Thrombophlebitis: IV administration may cause thrombophlebitis (vein inflammation and clot formation) at the injection site.
    • Fanconi-like syndrome: Administration of deteriorated tetracyclines (outdated or improperly stored) can lead to Fanconi-like syndrome, a serious kidney disorder characterized by nausea, vomiting, acidosis, proteinuria, hypokalemia, polyuria, and polydipsia. Always avoid using outdated tetracyclines.
    • Superinfection: Risk of superinfection, including candidiasis (yeast infection) and Clostridium difficile-associated diarrhea, due to broad-spectrum antibiotic effect altering normal flora.

✍️ Practice Exercise

Question: Explain why tetracyclines are contraindicated in children under 8 years old and during pregnancy. What specific side effects are of concern in these populations, and what alternative antibiotics might be considered?

View Solution

Contraindication of Tetracyclines in Children Under 8 Years Old and During Pregnancy: Tetracyclines are contraindicated in children under 8 years of age and during pregnancy (especially the last trimester) due to the risk of specific and significant developmental toxicities related to bone and teeth development.

Specific Side Effects of Concern:

  • Children Under 8 Years Old:
    • Permanent Teeth Discoloration: Tetracyclines have a high affinity for calcium and can bind to calcium in developing teeth, leading to permanent discoloration of teeth. This manifests as a yellow, gray, or brown staining of the teeth, which is cosmetically undesirable and permanent.
    • Delayed Bone Growth: Tetracyclines can also deposit in bone tissue and interfere with bone growth. This can result in temporary or permanent stunting of bone growth in young children, affecting their skeletal development.
  • During Pregnancy:
    • Fetal Teeth and Bone Development: If taken during pregnancy, especially in the second and third trimesters when fetal tooth and bone development is most active, tetracyclines can cross the placenta and deposit in fetal teeth and bones, causing teeth staining and potential bone growth issues in the developing fetus.
    • Hepatotoxicity Risk: Although less common, there is a risk of hepatotoxicity in pregnant women with tetracycline use, which poses additional risks to both mother and fetus.

Alternative Antibiotics: Given these risks, alternative antibiotics are generally preferred in children under 8 and pregnant women, depending on the infection being treated:

  • For Children:
    • Amoxicillin: Often used for respiratory and ear infections.
    • Cephalosporins: Like cefalexin or cefuroxime, are broad-spectrum antibiotics generally safe for children.
    • Macrolides: Like erythromycin or azithromycin, are alternatives for respiratory and skin infections, especially if penicillin allergy is a concern.
  • For Pregnant Women:
    • Penicillins and Cephalosporins: Generally considered safe and often used for various infections during pregnancy.
    • Macrolides: Erythromycin and azithromycin can be used for certain infections, depending on the specific pathogen and clinical situation.
    • Nitrofurantoin or Fosfomycin: For urinary tract infections, often considered safe in pregnancy, especially in the first and second trimesters, but should be used cautiously and avoided near term in some guidelines due to potential neonatal hemolytic anemia risk.

The choice of alternative antibiotic depends on the type of infection, susceptibility patterns, patient allergies, and other clinical factors. It's crucial to select antibiotics that are both safe and effective for these vulnerable populations, avoiding tetracyclines to prevent developmental harm.

📚 Key Takeaways

  • Tetracyclines are broad-spectrum antibiotics inhibiting bacterial protein synthesis, used for rickettsial, mycoplasma infections, cholera, and acne.
  • Contraindicated in pregnancy (teeth/bone effects) and children under 8 (teeth discoloration, growth inhibition). Side effects: GI upset, photosensitivity, teeth staining, and risk of liver/kidney damage.
  • Nursing care involves avoiding use in contraindicated groups, advising against outdated drugs, administering on empty stomach (unless GI upset occurs), and educating patients about photosensitivity and interactions with antacids and dairy.
