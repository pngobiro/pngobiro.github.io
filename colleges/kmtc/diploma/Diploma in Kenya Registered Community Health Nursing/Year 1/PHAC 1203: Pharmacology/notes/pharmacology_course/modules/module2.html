üìö Factors Affecting Drug Absorption (Continued)

Continuing our discussion on factors affecting drug absorption, let's delve into:

  1. Nature of the absorbing surface (cell membrane): The characteristics of the cell membrane at the absorption site significantly affect drug passage.
    • The drug molecule can pass through a single layer of cells (e.g., intestinal epithelium) more quickly than through multiple layers (e.g., skin).
    • The size of the absorbing surface area also matters; a larger surface area allows for greater and more rapid absorption.

    üí° Pro Tip

    A larger absorbing surface area generally leads to greater and faster drug absorption, enhancing the drug's effect.

  2. Blood flow to the site of administration: Blood flow rate to the absorption site is crucial.
    • Rich blood supply, like in the sublingual area, enhances absorption.
    • Poorly vascularized sites, such as subcutaneous tissue, delay absorption.
    • In conditions like shock, poor peripheral circulation can significantly impair drug absorption from intramuscular (IM) sites.

    üí° Pro Tip

    Intravenous (IV) injections bypass the absorption process entirely, placing drugs directly into the circulatory system for immediate effect.

  3. Solubility of the drug: For a drug to be absorbed, it must be in solution.
    • More soluble drugs are absorbed more rapidly.
    • Chemicals or minerals forming insoluble precipitates in the gastrointestinal tract (GIT) or drugs insoluble in water or lipids are poorly absorbed.
    • Parenterally administered drugs in an oily vehicle are absorbed more slowly than those dissolved in water or isotonic sodium chloride.
  4. Influence of pH: The pH of the environment affects drug ionization and absorption.
    • Acidic drugs (e.g., Aspirin) are less ionized in an acidic environment like the stomach, facilitating diffusion across membranes into circulation.
    • Basic drugs tend to ionize in the stomach's acidic environment, hindering absorption through the gastric mucosa. Conversely, they are better absorbed in alkaline media.

    üí° Pro Tip

    Un-ionized drugs are generally more lipid-soluble and diffuse readily across cell membranes, while ionized drugs are less lipid-soluble and less diffusible.

üìö Drug Concentration and Dosage Form

  1. Drug concentration: Higher drug concentrations tend to be absorbed more rapidly than lower concentrations. In some cases, a large initial dose (loading dose) may be given to quickly achieve therapeutic levels, followed by smaller maintenance doses.

    üí° Pro Tip

    The initial dose is called the Priming or Loading dose, while the subsequent smaller doses are Maintenance doses.

  2. Dosage form: The formulation of a drug can significantly impact its absorption. For example, "Enteric coating" on drugs is used to:
    1. Prevent decomposition of chemically sensitive drugs from gastric secretions.
    2. Prevent dilution of the drug before it reaches the intestine.
    3. Prevent nausea and vomiting.
    4. Provide a delayed action of the drug.

    üí° Pro Tip

    Capsule forms are generally absorbed more rapidly than tablets because the powder inside capsules provides a larger surface area for dissolution compared to compressed tablets.

üìö Distribution Barriers: Specialized Membranes

Distribution barriers are specialized biological membranes that selectively restrict drug passage to certain body sites:

  1. Blood-Brain Barrier (BBB): This unique anatomical arrangement selectively distributes lipid-soluble drugs into the brain and cerebrospinal fluid (CSF).
    • Ionized drugs and poorly fat-soluble drugs are restricted from entering the brain and CSF.
    • Antibiotics that do not readily cross the BBB are ineffective for CNS infections like meningitis and encephalitis unless administered intrathecally.
    • Intrathecal administration bypasses the BBB, allowing direct drug action within the CNS.
  2. Placental Barrier (PB): This membrane separates the maternal and fetal blood vessels.
    • Tissue enzymes in the placenta can metabolize certain agents, but generally, the PB is less selective than the BBB.
    • Unlike the BBB, many drugs can cross the placenta, potentially affecting the fetus.
    • Two major types of drug effects on the fetus include:
      1. First trimester effects: Drugs can induce aberrant development of organs and systems, leading to teratogenic effects (physical defects in the developing embryo).
      2. Second and third trimester effects: Drugs can affect fetal function and may cause respiratory depression in the newborn due to the fetus's limited ability to biotransform and excrete drugs.

Question: Explain why the blood-brain barrier (BBB) is clinically significant in treating central nervous system (CNS) infections. How does the solubility of a drug affect its ability to cross the BBB?

View Solution

The BBB is clinically significant because it restricts many drugs, especially those not lipid-soluble or ionized, from entering the CNS. This poses a challenge in treating CNS infections like meningitis because many antibiotics cannot effectively cross the BBB to reach therapeutic concentrations in the brain and CSF.

Drug solubility greatly affects BBB penetration. Lipid-soluble drugs can readily diffuse across the BBB, whereas water-soluble or ionized drugs are significantly restricted. Therefore, to treat CNS infections effectively, drugs must be lipid-soluble or be administered in a way that bypasses the BBB, such as intrathecal injection.

üìö Key Takeaways

  • Drug absorption is affected by the nature of the absorbing surface, blood flow, drug solubility, pH, and drug concentration.
  • The Blood-Brain Barrier (BBB) selectively limits drug entry into the brain, primarily allowing lipid-soluble drugs to pass.
  • The Placental Barrier (PB) is less selective than the BBB, allowing many drugs to cross and potentially affect the fetus.
  • Understanding distribution barriers is critical for drug development and therapeutic strategies, especially for CNS-related conditions and pregnancy.
