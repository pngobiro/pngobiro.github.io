Let's explore specific examples of antineoplastic drugs, categorized by their mechanisms of action, to understand their clinical applications and nursing implications:

  1. A) Alkylating Agents: These agents work by adding alkyl groups to DNA, which disrupts DNA replication and cell function.
    • 1. Carboplatin:
      • Trade name: Paraplatin.
      • Class: Antineoplastic, alkylating agent.
      • Uses: Primarily for ovarian cancer.
      • Additional Side effects: Neurotoxicity and nephrotoxicity, in addition to common chemotherapy side effects.
    • 2. Cyclophosphamide (CYC) :
      • Trade name: Cytoxan.

      • Additional Side effects: Alopecia, bone marrow depression, hemorrhagic cystitis (bladder inflammation with bleeding), darkening of skin and fingernails.
    • Ifosfamide:
      • Trade name: Ifex.
      • Class: Antineoplastic, alkylating agent.
      • Uses: Used to treat acute leukemia, testicular cancer, and malignant lymphomas.
      • Additional Side effects: Hemorrhagic cystitis, similar to cyclophosphamide, but often more pronounced.
  2. B) Antimetabolites: These drugs interfere with DNA replication by mimicking essential metabolites, disrupting DNA and RNA synthesis.

Question: Compare and contrast the side effect profiles and primary uses of Carboplatin and Methotrexate. Which type of cancer is each primarily used to treat?

View Solution

Comparison of Carboplatin and Methotrexate:

  • Mechanism of Action:
    • Carboplatin: Alkylating agent; damages DNA by forming crosslinks, leading to cell death.
    • Methotrexate: Antimetabolite; inhibits folic acid metabolism, disrupting DNA and RNA synthesis.
  • Primary Cancer Uses:
    • Carboplatin: Primarily used in the treatment of ovarian cancer. Also effective in lung, bladder, and testicular cancers.
    • Methotrexate: Used for uterine choriocarcinoma, vesicular mole, and leukemia. Also used in lower doses for non-cancerous conditions like rheumatoid arthritis.
  • Side Effect Profiles:
    • Carboplatin:
      • Common: Bone marrow depression (leading to anemia, leukopenia, thrombocytopenia), nausea and vomiting, alopecia.
      • Specific: Neurotoxicity (peripheral neuropathy, ototoxicity) and nephrotoxicity. Nausea and vomiting can be severe but are generally manageable with antiemetics.
    • Methotrexate:
      • Common: Severe bone marrow depression, hepatotoxicity, mucositis (stomatitis), nausea and vomiting, alopecia.
      • Specific: Hemorrhagic enteritis (intestinal bleeding and inflammation), transient paresis (muscle weakness), and seizures. Hepatotoxicity and bone marrow suppression are major dose-limiting toxicities.

    Summary of Differences:

    • Cancer Types: Carboplatin is a first-line treatment for ovarian cancer and other solid tumors. Methotrexate is key for choriocarcinoma, leukemia, and certain autoimmune diseases.
    • Side Effects: Both cause significant bone marrow suppression and GI issues. Carboplatin is particularly noted for neuro- and nephrotoxicity, while Methotrexate is more associated with hepatotoxicity and severe mucositis.
    • Nursing Implications: For both drugs, monitoring blood counts, renal and liver function, and managing nausea and vomiting are critical. For Methotrexate, monitoring for mucositis and ensuring leucovorin rescue (to mitigate bone marrow suppression) are also important. Hydration and monitoring for neurotoxicity are key for Carboplatin.

üìö Key Takeaways

  • Carboplatin is an alkylating agent mainly for ovarian cancer, with neuro- and nephrotoxicity as key side effects.
  • Cyclophosphamide, another alkylating agent, treats lymphomas and myeloma, notable for hemorrhagic cystitis and alopecia.
  • Ifosfamide, similar to cyclophosphamide, treats leukemia and lymphomas, also with a risk of hemorrhagic cystitis.
  • Cytarabine, an antimetabolite, treats acute leukemia and lymphoma, causing cytarabine syndrome with flu-like symptoms.
  • Methotrexate, another antimetabolite, treats choriocarcinoma and leukemia, with severe bone marrow depression and hepatotoxicity risks.
  • Cisplatin, a miscellaneous agent, is known for causing severe nausea and vomiting; hydration is crucial to prevent nephrotoxicity.
