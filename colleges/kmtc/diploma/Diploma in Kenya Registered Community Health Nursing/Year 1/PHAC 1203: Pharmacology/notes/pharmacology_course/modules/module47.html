üìö Beta-adrenargic blocking agents Examples

Let's examine specific examples of beta-adrenergic blocking agents, focusing on Atenolol and Propranolol, to highlight their differences and clinical applications:

  1. Atenolol: Cardioselective Beta-Blocker
    • Trade name: Normatin.
    • Classification: Beta-adrenergic blocking agent.
    • Selectivity: Beta 1 -adrenoreceptor blocking drug, which is cardioselective, meaning it primarily blocks Œ≤1 receptors in the heart.
    • Uses:
      • Hypertension: Effective in managing hypertension, particularly mild to moderate cases.
      • Angina Pectoris: Used to reduce the frequency and severity of angina attacks.
    • Dosage: Available in Tablets: 50 mg and 100 mg.
      • Initial dose: Typically 50 mg daily.
      • Dosage Adjustment: If there is no adequate response at 50 mg, the dose may be increased to 100 mg daily. Further increases are generally not recommended.
      • IV Use in Acute MI: In cases of acute myocardial infarction (MI), IV administration may be used initially. Give 5 mg IV over 5 minutes, and if there is no response or adverse effects, another 5 mg dose can be given after 10 minutes.
    • Specific nursing considerations:
      • IV Dilution: For IV use, Atenolol may be diluted in sodium chloride, dextrose, or dextrose saline solutions to facilitate slower administration and reduce potential side effects.
  2. Propranolol hydrochloride: Nonselective Beta-Blocker
    • Trade name: Inderal, Deralin.
    • Classification: Beta-adrenergic blocking agent, antiarrhythmic.
    • Selectivity: Non-selective beta-blocker, meaning it blocks both beta-1 (Œ≤1) and beta-2 (Œ≤2) adrenergic receptors.
    • Action: Manifests both beta1 and beta 2 adrenergic blocking activity, affecting heart rate, contractility, bronchial smooth muscle, and peripheral vasculature.
    • Indication: Broad range of uses due to its non-selectivity:
      • Angina pectoris: Effective in managing stable and unstable angina.
      • Hypertension: Used to treat essential hypertension, often in combination with other antihypertensives.
      • Cardiac arrhythmias: Management of various arrhythmias, particularly supraventricular and ventricular tachycardias.
      • Prophylaxis of migraine: Prevention of migraine headaches.
      • Prophylaxis of Myocardial Infarction (MI): Post-MI to reduce mortality and prevent re-infarction.
      • Pheochromocytoma: Management of hypertension associated with pheochromocytoma (a tumor of the adrenal gland).
    • Additional side effects: Psoriasis-like eruptions have been reported with propranolol use.
    • Dosage: Tablets are available, and dosage is highly individualized.
      • Initial dose: Typically starts at 40 mg twice daily (bid).
      • Maintenance dose: Usual maintenance dose ranges from 120-240 mg daily, divided into 2-3 doses, adjusted based on therapeutic response and tolerance.
    • Contraindications: Asthma and other bronchospastic conditions due to Œ≤2 blockade causing bronchoconstriction.

Question: Compare and contrast Atenolol and Propranolol regarding their cardioselectivity, clinical uses, and potential risks for patients with asthma or diabetes.

View Solution

Comparison of Atenolol and Propranolol:

  • Cardioselectivity:
    • Atenolol: Cardioselective, primarily blocks Œ≤1 receptors in the heart. Has less effect on Œ≤2 receptors, especially at lower doses.
    • Propranolol: Nonselective, blocks both Œ≤1 and Œ≤2 receptors. Affects heart, bronchioles, peripheral vasculature, and other Œ≤2 receptor locations.
  • Clinical Uses:
    • Atenolol:
      • Hypertension: Effective for managing high blood pressure, especially in patients needing a cardioselective agent.
      • Angina: Useful in treating chronic stable angina.
      • Favored in patients where Œ≤2 blockade is undesirable, such as those with mild asthma or peripheral vascular disease, although caution is still needed.
    • Propranolol:
      • Hypertension: Effective for hypertension but less preferred as a first-line agent due to non-selectivity and potential for broader side effects.
      • Angina: Manages various types of angina.
      • Arrhythmias: Treats supraventricular and ventricular arrhythmias.
      • Migraine Prophylaxis: Effective in preventing migraines.
      • Essential Tremor: Used to manage essential tremor.
      • Anxiety: Can be used off-label for anxiety symptoms due to its autonomic nervous system effects.
  • Potential Risks for Patients with Asthma or Diabetes:
    • Patients with Asthma:
      • Atenolol: Safer than nonselective beta-blockers but still should be used cautiously as cardioselectivity is not absolute and bronchospasm is still a potential risk, especially at higher doses.
      • Propranolol: Significantly contraindicated in asthma due to Œ≤2 blockade, which can cause bronchoconstriction and exacerbate asthma symptoms, potentially leading to severe respiratory distress.
    • Patients with Diabetes:
      • Atenolol and Propranolol: Both can mask symptoms of hypoglycemia, such as tachycardia, which is an important warning sign for patients and clinicians. Nonselective beta-blockers like propranolol may be more problematic as they can also inhibit glycogenolysis (glucose release from liver stores) mediated by Œ≤2 receptors, potentially prolonging hypoglycemia and making recovery from hypoglycemic episodes more difficult. Cardioselective agents may be slightly safer but still require caution and patient education about monitoring blood glucose and recognizing other non-adrenergic signs of hypoglycemia (sweating, hunger, confusion).

üìö Key Takeaways

  • Atenolol (cardioselective Œ≤1-blocker) is used mainly for hypertension and angina, safer for respiratory conditions than nonselective agents.
  • Propranolol (nonselective Œ≤1 and Œ≤2 blocker) treats hypertension, angina, arrhythmias, migraine, and essential tremor; contraindicated in asthma due to bronchoconstriction risk.
  • Dosages for both drugs are individualized; Atenolol starts at 50 mg daily, Propranolol starts at 40 mg BID, with adjustments based on response.
  • Nursing considerations include monitoring heart rate and blood pressure, caution in patients with asthma and diabetes, and educating patients on gradual dose changes and potential side effects.
