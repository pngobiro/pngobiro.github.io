Antihistamines

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Define antihistamines and their classification as H1 blockers.
  • Explain the role of histamine in allergic reactions and inflammatory processes.
  • Describe the mechanism of action of H1 antihistamines in blocking histamine effects.
  • Identify the therapeutic uses of antihistamines for allergic conditions and other indications.

📚 Antihistamines ―H1 Blockers

Antihistamines, specifically H1 blockers, are a class of drugs used to counteract the effects of histamine, a compound released by the body during allergic reactions and certain inflammatory processes. Histamine plays a key role in immediate hypersensitivity reactions and causes various symptoms by acting on different receptors in the body.

Histamine and Its Role:

  • Storage: Histamine is stored in mast cells and basophils, found in almost every tissue in the body.
  • Release Triggers: Various stimuli can trigger histamine release, including:
    • Tissue injury
    • Antigen-antibody reactions (allergic reactions)
    • Extreme cold
    • Certain drugs and toxins
  • Histamine Release Pathway: These stimuli cause mast cells and basophils to degranulate, releasing histamine from storage granules into the vascular system and surrounding tissues.

Effects of Histamine Release:

Once released, histamine interacts with histamine receptors (H1, H2, H3, H4), leading to various physiological responses. H1 receptor activation is primarily responsible for allergic symptoms:

  1. Vascular Effects:
    • Dilation of small arterioles and increased permeability of capillaries: Histamine causes dilation of small blood vessels (arterioles) and increases the permeability of capillaries and venules.
    • Increased Vascular Permeability: Results in fluid leakage from capillaries into tissues, leading to:
      • Hypotension (low blood pressure) due to reduced blood volume in circulation.
      • Edema (swelling) due to fluid accumulation in tissues.
      • Nasal and laryngeal edema: Swelling in nasal passages and larynx, contributing to nasal congestion and breathing difficulties in allergic reactions.
    • Smooth Muscle Contraction:
      • Contraction of some smooth muscles: Histamine causes contraction of certain smooth muscles, notably in the bronchioles, leading to:
        • Bronchoconstriction: Narrowing of airways, contributing to breathing difficulties in allergic asthma.
        • Uterine contraction: Can cause uterine contraction.
      • Stimulation of Acid Secretion:
        • Acid secretion in the stomach: Histamine stimulates gastric acid secretion by parietal cells in the stomach. This effect is mediated by H2 receptors (not H1) and is relevant to gastric acid regulation but not typically targeted by H1 antihistamines.
        • Salivary, bronchial & intestinal secretions: Also stimulates secretions in salivary, bronchial, and intestinal glands, contributing to increased mucus and fluid production in these areas.
      • Sensory Nerve Endings Stimulation:
        • Pain and itching: Histamine stimulates sensory nerve endings, causing pain and itching, particularly in allergic skin reactions and insect bites.

📚 Action of antihistamines

Action of H1 Antihistamines: H1 antihistamines, the primary type used to treat allergies, work by blocking the H1 receptors, thus preventing histamine from binding and exerting its effects. Their mechanism involves:

  • H1 Receptor Blockade: Antihistamines competitively block histamine from binding to H1 receptors on cells in various tissues, including smooth muscle cells, endothelial cells, and nerve endings.
  • Reversal of Histamine Effects: By blocking H1 receptors, antihistamines counteract or reverse the effects of histamine, including:
    • Reduced Vasodilation and Permeability: Decreasing vasodilation and capillary permeability, which helps reduce edema, nasal congestion, and hypotension.
    • Reduced Smooth Muscle Contraction: Preventing histamine-induced contraction of bronchial smooth muscle, which aids in bronchodilation and relief of asthma symptoms (though less effective in acute asthma attacks than β2-agonists).
    • Reduced Sensory Nerve Stimulation: Diminishing histamine's stimulation of sensory nerve endings, reducing itching and pain associated with allergic reactions.
  • No Effect on Histamine Release: Importantly, antihistamines do not prevent the release of histamine from mast cells and basophils; they only block the receptors, preventing histamine from acting on target tissues.

    • 💡 Pro Tip

    The effect of histamines may be reversed either by drugs that block histamine receptors (antihistamine) or by drugs that have effects opposite to those of histamine e.g. epinephrine.

📚 Uses and Contraindications of antihistamines

Uses:

H1 antihistamines are used to treat a variety of allergic conditions and symptoms:

  • Treatment of seasonal allergic rhinitis (hay fever) and perennial allergic rhinitis (year-round allergies): To relieve symptoms like sneezing, runny nose, itchy eyes, and throat.
  • Allergic conjunctivitis: To relieve itching and redness of the eyes due to allergies.
  • Treatment of urticarial transfusion reactions: To manage hives and itching from allergic reactions to blood transfusions.
  • Topical dermatitis: To relieve itching and inflammation associated with allergic skin conditions like eczema and contact dermatitis.
  • Insect bites and stings: To reduce itching and swelling from insect bites.
  • Sneezing & rhinorrhea due to common cold: Although they do not treat the viral infection, antihistamines can help alleviate symptoms like runny nose and sneezing associated with the common cold.
  • Prophylaxis & treatment of motion sickness: Certain antihistamines with anticholinergic properties (like dimenhydrinate and promethazine) are effective in preventing and treating motion sickness-induced nausea and vomiting.
  • Night – time sleep aid: Some first-generation antihistamines, due to their sedative properties, are used as over-the-counter sleep aids for short-term insomnia.

Contraindications:

  • Hypersensitivity: Known allergy to the specific antihistamine or class of antihistamines.
  • Pregnancy: Use during pregnancy should be謹慎 and typically avoided in the first trimester unless benefits outweigh risks.
  • Angle-closure glaucoma: Anticholinergic effects can exacerbate narrow-angle glaucoma.
  • Prostatic hypertrophy: Anticholinergic effects can worsen urinary retention in patients with prostatic hypertrophy.
  • CNS depression: Use cautiously with other CNS depressants (e.g., alcohol, benzodiazepines, opioids) due to additive sedative effects. Phenothiazine-type antihistamines (like promethazine) have more pronounced CNS depressant effects.
  • Bone marrow depression: May exacerbate bone marrow suppression.
  • Comatose patients: Contraindicated in comatose patients.

✍️ Practice Exercise

Question: Explain the mechanism of action of H1 antihistamines in alleviating allergy symptoms. Why are antihistamines more effective in preventing allergic reactions than in reversing them once they have fully developed?

View Solution

Mechanism of Action of H1 Antihistamines in Alleviating Allergy Symptoms: H1 antihistamines alleviate allergy symptoms by competitively blocking histamine at H1 receptors. This action prevents histamine, released during an allergic reaction, from binding to its receptors and triggering allergic responses.

Key Aspects of Antihistamine Action:

  • Competitive H1 Receptor Blockade: H1 antihistamines bind to H1 receptors, located on smooth muscle cells, endothelial cells, and sensory nerves, thereby preventing histamine from binding to these receptors.
  • Reduced Allergic Symptoms: By blocking H1 receptors, antihistamines counteract the effects of histamine, leading to a reduction in typical allergy symptoms:
    • Reduced Edema and Nasal Congestion: By blocking histamine-induced vasodilation and increased capillary permeability, antihistamines decrease fluid leakage into tissues, reducing edema and nasal congestion.
    • Decreased Itching and Pain: Block histamine stimulation of sensory nerve endings, alleviating itching and pain associated with allergic reactions.
    • Bronchodilation (Limited): While antihistamines can help prevent bronchoconstriction, their effect is minor compared to potent bronchodilators like beta-2 agonists. They are not primary bronchodilators in asthma but can help reduce histamine-mediated bronchial constriction in allergic asthma.

Why Antihistamines are Better for Prevention than Reversal: Antihistamines are more effective when taken prophylactically or at the onset of allergic symptoms rather than after the reaction is fully established because:

  • Preventive Action: Antihistamines are best at preventing histamine from binding to receptors in the first place. When taken before exposure to an allergen, they can block histamine receptors preemptively, reducing or preventing allergic symptoms from developing.
  • Limited Reversal of Established Reactions: Once an allergic reaction is in full swing, histamine has already bound to many receptors and triggered a cascade of inflammatory events. Antihistamines can still help reduce ongoing symptoms but are less effective at reversing already established effects like significant edema, bronchoconstriction, or severe itching. In such cases, other medications like epinephrine (for anaphylaxis) or corticosteroids (for inflammation) may be needed to more effectively reverse the reaction.
  • Symptom Management, Not Cure: Antihistamines primarily manage the symptoms of allergic reactions; they do not cure the allergy or prevent histamine release itself. Therefore, preventing receptor activation from the start is more effective than trying to reverse a full-blown allergic response.

📚 Key Takeaways

  • Antihistamines (H1 blockers) counteract histamine effects by blocking H1 receptors, reducing allergic symptoms like edema, itching, and nasal congestion.
  • Antihistamines are used for allergic rhinitis, conjunctivitis, urticaria, insect bites, motion sickness, and as sleep aids.
  • Contraindications include hypersensitivity, glaucoma, prostatic hypertrophy; side effects: sedation, dizziness, anticholinergic effects.
  • Antihistamines are more effective preventatively; nursing care involves managing sedation, anticholinergic effects, and advising caution with CNS depressants.
