üìö Progestins

Progestins are synthetic forms of progesterone, a naturally occurring steroid hormone produced by the ovaries. Progesterone and progestins play crucial roles in the female reproductive system, particularly in regulating the menstrual cycle and maintaining pregnancy. Key aspects of progestins include:

  • Primary Hormone: Progesterone is the primary natural progestin, essential for preparing the uterus for implantation of a fertilized egg and maintaining pregnancy.
  • Synthetic Progestins: Synthetic progestins are used in various medications for contraception, hormone replacement therapy, and treatment of gynecological disorders.

üìö Medroxyprogesterone Acetate: Depo-Provera, Provera

Medroxyprogesterone Acetate: Depo-Provera, Provera

  • Generic name: medroxyprogesterone acetate.
  • Trade names: Depo-Provera, Provera.
  • Classification: Hormone, progestin.
  • Pregnancy risk category X: Contraindicated in pregnancy due to potential fetal harm.
  • Available forms:
    • Tablets: Available in 2.5 mg, 5 mg, and 10 mg tablets (Provera).
    • Injection (suspension): Available as a depot injection in 150 mg/ml and 400 mg/ml concentrations (Depo-Provera).
  • Indications & dosages: Dosage varies widely depending on the condition:
    • Abnormal Uterine Bleeding and Secondary Amenorrhea: For abnormal uterine bleeding caused by hormonal imbalance and secondary amenorrhea (absence of menstruation), typically 5-10 mg orally per day.
    • Endometrial or Renal Cancer (advanced): Higher doses, 40 to 1,000 mg I.M. weekly, used in palliative treatment of endometrial or renal cancer.
  • Action: Medroxyprogesterone acetate, a synthetic progestin, exerts its effects by:
    • Suppressing Ovulation: Primarily suppresses ovulation, likely by inhibiting pituitary gonadotropin secretion (LH and FSH), which prevents follicular maturation and egg release.
    • Endometrial Effects: Transforms proliferative endometrium into secretory endometrium, creating conditions less favorable for implantation, although this is not the primary contraceptive mechanism.

üìö Adverse reactions and Contraindications & cautions

Adverse reactions

  • Central Nervous System (CNS): Depression, cerebrovascular accident (CVA), pain, headache, dizziness.
  • Cardiovascular (CV): Thrombophlebitis, pulmonary embolism, edema, thromboembolism, hypertension.
  • Gastrointestinal (GI): Abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, constipation.
  • Genitourinary (GU): Bleeding irregularities (breakthrough bleeding, spotting, amenorrhea), dysmenorrhea, cervical erosion, abnormal secretions.
  • Hepatic: Jaundice (rare).
  • Metabolic: Weight changes (usually weight gain).
  • Dermatologic: Skin rash, sterile abscesses (at injection site), acne, pruritus (itching), alopecia (hair loss), hirsutism (increased hair growth in women).
  • Other: Breast tenderness, enlargement, or secretion.

Contraindications & cautions

  • Contraindicated in patients hypersensitive to drug and in those with active thromboembolic disorders or history of thromboembolic disorders, cerebrovascular disease, breast cancer, missed abortion, or hepatic dysfunction. Tablets are contraindicated in patients with liver dysfunction or known or suspected malignant disease of genital organs.
  • Use cautiously in patients with diabetes mellitus, seizures, migraine, cardiac or renal disease, asthma, and depression, as these conditions may be exacerbated by hormonal therapy.

Question: Explain the mechanism of action of Medroxyprogesterone Acetate as a contraceptive. Describe the key differences between the tablet and injectable (Depo-Provera) formulations in terms of their uses and duration of action.

View Solution

Mechanism of Action of Medroxyprogesterone Acetate as a Contraceptive: Medroxyprogesterone acetate primarily prevents pregnancy by suppressing ovulation. It achieves this through the following hormonal actions:

  • Suppression of Ovulation: Medroxyprogesterone acetate inhibits the secretion of gonadotropins (LH and FSH) from the pituitary gland. These hormones are essential for the development of ovarian follicles and the LH surge that triggers ovulation. By suppressing LH and FSH, particularly LH, medroxyprogesterone acetate prevents the ovary from releasing an egg.
  • Thickening of Cervical Mucus: Progestins like medroxyprogesterone acetate cause the cervical mucus to thicken, becoming more viscous and less penetrable to sperm. This thickened mucus acts as a barrier, hindering sperm transport into the uterus and reducing the likelihood of fertilization.
  • Endometrial Changes: Progestins alter the endometrium, making it less favorable for implantation. The endometrium becomes thinner and less developed, reducing its receptivity to a fertilized egg. This effect is a secondary contraceptive mechanism, ensuring that even if ovulation and fertilization occur, implantation is less likely.

Key Differences Between Tablet and Injectable (Depo-Provera) Formulations:

  • Formulation and Route of Administration:
    • Tablets (Provera): Oral tablets are designed for daily administration. They provide a consistent daily dose of medroxyprogesterone acetate.
    • Injectable Suspension (Depo-Provera): An intramuscular depot injection that releases medroxyprogesterone acetate slowly over a long period (typically 3 months for contraceptive use, or longer for higher doses in cancer treatment).
  • Duration of Action:
    • Tablets (Provera): Short-acting, requiring daily administration to maintain contraceptive effect or hormonal balance. The effects wear off within 24 hours of a missed dose, necessitating strict daily adherence for contraception.
    • Injectable Suspension (Depo-Provera): Long-acting, providing contraception for 3 months (12 weeks) per injection. A single injection releases the drug slowly and continuously, providing prolonged hormonal effects, eliminating the need for daily pill-taking.
  • Primary Uses:
    • Tablets (Provera): Primarily used for managing gynecological conditions requiring cyclical progestin therapy, such as abnormal uterine bleeding due to hormonal imbalance and secondary amenorrhea. Also used in hormone replacement therapy.
    • Injectable Suspension (Depo-Provera): Primarily used for long-term contraception due to its convenience of infrequent dosing. Also used at higher doses in palliative treatment of endometrial and renal cancers due to its potent progestational effects.
  • Convenience and Compliance:
    • Tablets (Provera): Require daily adherence, which can be challenging for some women, potentially leading to missed doses and reduced contraceptive efficacy.
    • Injectable Suspension (Depo-Provera): Offers high convenience and improved compliance due to infrequent administration (once every 3 months). Eliminates the need for daily pill-taking, beneficial for women who prefer not to take daily medication or have difficulty with adherence.

üìö Key Takeaways

  • Progestins like Medroxyprogesterone Acetate (MPA) mimic progesterone, used for contraception and gynecological conditions. MPA suppresses ovulation and alters cervical mucus/endometrium.
  • MPA tablets (Provera) treat abnormal uterine bleeding and amenorrhea; injectable Depo-Provera provides long-term contraception and treats endometrial/renal cancers at high doses.
  • Side effects of progestins include bleeding irregularities, weight changes, mood disturbances; contraindicated in thromboembolic disorders, pregnancy, and certain cancers.
  • Nursing considerations: IM injection can be painful, rotate sites; monitor for thromboembolic signs, advise on consistent use for contraception, and report menstrual irregularities or severe side effects.
