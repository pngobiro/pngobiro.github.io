Specific Iron Preparation Examples

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Describe the characteristics, advantages, and dosages of Ferrous Sulfate.
  • Explain the uses, side effects, and nursing considerations specific to Iron Dextran injection.
  • Compare and contrast Ferrous Sulfate and Iron Dextran in terms of route of administration, efficacy, and safety profiles.
  • Understand the nursing actions to minimize side effects and enhance the therapeutic effect of iron preparations.

📚 Specific Iron Preparations

Let's delve into specific examples of iron preparations, focusing on oral and parenteral forms, and their nursing considerations:

  1. 1) Ferrous Sulfate: Oral Iron Supplement
    • Trade names: Ferrograd, Eryfer.
    • Class: Antianemic, iron supplement.
    • Advantages:
      • Less Expensive: One of the most cost-effective oral iron supplements.
      • Effective Oral Form: Considered the most effective oral iron preparation in terms of iron absorption and bioavailability.
      • Stable in Air: More stable in air compared to some other iron salts, which simplifies storage and handling.
    • Dosage Forms: Available in various oral formulations:
      • Elixir (liquid solution)
      • Oral solution
      • Tablets
      • Enteric-coated tablets (designed to dissolve in the intestine to reduce stomach irritation)
    • Dosage:
      • Prophylaxis: 300 mg daily, typically given to prevent iron deficiency in at-risk populations like pregnant women.
      • Treatment: 300 mg twice daily (bid) to treat existing iron deficiency anemia. Dosage may be adjusted based on severity and patient response.
  2. 2) Iron Dextran Injection: Parenteral Iron
    • Trade name: Imferon.
    • Class.: Iron preparation, parenteral form.
    • Note: Iron dextran is a complex of ferric hydroxide and dextran, specifically formulated for parenteral use when oral iron is not suitable or effective. It is the primary iron preparation used parenterally.
    • Uses: Administered via IM or IV routes for treating iron deficiency anemia in specific cases:
      • When oral iron administration is impossible (e.g., patient unable to swallow or absorb oral medications).
      • For patients with severe iron deficiency or ongoing blood loss where rapid iron repletion is needed.
    • Side effects: Carries a higher risk of adverse reactions compared to oral iron:
      • Anaphylaxis: Risk of severe allergic reactions, including anaphylaxis, which can be life-threatening.
      • Rashes and Urticaria: Skin reactions such as rashes, hives, and itching.
      • Gastrointestinal Issues: Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea.
      • Cardiovascular: Hypotension, tachycardia (rapid heart rate), and in severe cases, shock.
      • Injection Site Reactions:
        • IM: Can cause brown skin discoloration and abscess formation at the injection site. IM route is particularly painful.
        • IV: May lead to phlebitis (vein inflammation) at the infusion site.

📚 Nursing Considerations for Iron Therapy

Nursing care for patients receiving iron preparations, whether oral or parenteral, includes several important considerations:

General Nursing Considerations:

  • Dietary Advice:
    • Take Iron with Meals: Advise clients to take oral iron supplements with meals to reduce gastric irritation, although food may decrease absorption.
    • Enhance Absorption with Vitamin C: Encourage intake of citrus juices or Vitamin C-rich foods when taking oral iron to enhance iron absorption.
    • Avoid Certain Foods and Drinks: Advise clients to avoid taking iron with milk, tea, coffee, or antacids, as these can significantly decrease iron absorption.
    • Balanced Diet: Encourage clients to maintain a well-balanced diet rich in iron-containing foods to support overall iron repletion.
  • Medication Administration:
    • Outdated Drugs: Do not use outdated or deteriorated drugs. Discard unused medications appropriately.
    • IM Technique: Administer IM iron dextran deeply into a large muscle mass using the Z-track technique to minimize pain and staining.
    • Empty Stomach for Oral Iron: Administer oral iron on an empty stomach (1 hour before or 2 hours after meals) for optimal absorption, if tolerated; if not tolerated due to GI upset, it can be taken with meals.
    • Pregnancy Status: For female clients, especially if pregnant, determine the trimester to adjust iron supplementation accordingly and monitor for specific pregnancy-related needs.
    • Report Side Effects: Instruct patients to report any side effects to their healthcare provider, especially severe gastrointestinal issues or signs of allergic reactions.
    • Sunlight Avoidance: Advise patients to avoid prolonged direct sunlight exposure, as iron can increase photosensitivity and risk of sunburn-like reactions.
  • Child Safety:
    • Keep Out of Reach: Emphasize the importance of keeping iron supplements out of reach of children, as overdose can be extremely toxic and potentially fatal in pediatric populations.
    • Liquid Iron Dilution: When administering liquid iron to children, dilute it with water or fruit juice and use a straw to minimize teeth staining.
  • Patient Counseling:
    • Discuss Stool Changes: Warn clients about the possibility of indigestion, changes in stool color (black stools are normal), and constipation, which are common but harmless side effects.

Specific Nursing Considerations for Iron Dextran Injection:

  • Avoid Mixing: Never mix iron dextran with other medications or add it to parenteral nutrition solutions due to compatibility issues.
  • Monitor Vital Signs: Obtain baseline vital signs before administering iron dextran and monitor closely during and after administration to detect any adverse reactions early.
  • Slow Administration: Administer the drug IM deeply and IV very slowly to minimize pain and reduce the risk of systemic reactions.
  • Prevent Staining: Use a separate needle for withdrawal from vial and injection to prevent staining of subcutaneous tissue with IM injections. Use Z-track technique for IM injections.
  • Emergency Preparedness: Be prepared to assist with treatment of iron intoxication and anaphylaxis, having emergency equipment and medications readily available.

✍️ Practice Exercise

Question: A patient is prescribed oral ferrous sulfate for iron deficiency anemia. What key instructions should you provide to the patient to maximize absorption and minimize common side effects?

View Solution

Key Instructions for Patients Prescribed Oral Ferrous Sulfate:

  • 1. Timing of Administration:
    • Take on an Empty Stomach: For best absorption, take ferrous sulfate on an empty stomach, at least 1 hour before or 2 hours after meals. However, if gastrointestinal upset occurs, it can be taken with food.
    • Consistent Schedule: Take the medication at the same time(s) each day to maintain a consistent blood level and improve adherence.
  • 2. Enhancing Absorption:
    • Take with Vitamin C: Take ferrous sulfate with citrus juices or Vitamin C supplements. Vitamin C significantly enhances iron absorption from the gut.
  • 3. Minimizing Absorption Interference:
    • Avoid Certain Foods/Drinks: Do not take iron supplements with milk, tea, coffee, antacids, or calcium supplements. These can significantly decrease iron absorption. If necessary, take these at least 2 hours apart from iron supplements.
  • 4. Managing Side Effects:
    • Expect Stool Changes: Inform the patient that their stools will likely become dark or black, which is a normal and harmless side effect of iron supplementation.
    • Manage Constipation: Increase dietary fiber intake, drink plenty of fluids, and engage in light exercise to help manage constipation, a common side effect. If constipation is severe, discuss stool softeners with their healthcare provider.
    • Take with Food if Needed: If nausea or stomach upset occurs, it’s okay to take ferrous sulfate with a small amount of food to improve tolerability, even though it may slightly reduce absorption.
  • 5. Safety and Storage:
    • Keep Out of Reach of Children: Store iron supplements safely, out of reach of children, as overdose can be dangerous, especially for young children.
  • 6. Follow-up:
    • Regular Check-ups: Emphasize the importance of regular follow-up appointments and blood tests to monitor iron levels and adjust dosage as needed.

📚 Key Takeaways

  • Ferrous Sulfate is an effective, low-cost oral iron supplement; take with Vitamin C to enhance absorption, avoid milk/tea/antacids.
  • Iron Dextran injection is for parenteral use when oral iron is unsuitable, offering rapid iron repletion but with higher risk of anaphylaxis and injection site reactions.
  • Nursing care involves advising on diet, proper oral iron administration, managing GI side effects, and ensuring safe handling, especially for parenteral iron to prevent severe reactions.
  • Monitor for side effects of both oral and parenteral iron, educate patients on expected stool color changes and importance of follow-up.
