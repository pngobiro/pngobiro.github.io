📚 Antiulcer Drugs: Managing Gastric Acidity

Antiulcer drugs are medications used to treat peptic ulcer disease, a condition characterized by sores in the lining of the stomach or duodenum (first part of the small intestine). These drugs aim to reduce gastric acidity, protect the mucosal lining, and promote ulcer healing. Key aspects of antiulcer drugs include:

  • Therapeutic Goal: To relieve pain, heal ulcers, prevent recurrence, and manage complications of peptic ulcer disease.
  • Major Classes: Antiulcer drugs are broadly classified into several categories based on their mechanisms of action:
    1. Antacids
    2. Histamine H2-receptor antagonists (H2 blockers)
    3. Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs)
    4. Mucosal protectants (e.g., sucralfate)
    5. Prostaglandin analogs (e.g., misoprostol)
    6. Antibiotics (to eradicate Helicobacter pylori)

📚 1. Antacids: Neutralizing Gastric Acid

Antacids are over-the-counter (OTC) medications that neutralize gastric acid, providing quick relief from symptoms of heartburn and acid indigestion. They are basic compounds that work directly in the stomach to reduce acidity. Key features of antacids include:

Action:

  • Neutralization of Gastric Acid: Antacids act by chemically neutralizing hydrochloric acid (HCl) in the stomach. They are alkaline compounds that react with stomach acid to form salt and water, reducing the overall acidity in the stomach.
  • Increased Gastric pH: By neutralizing acid, antacids increase the gastric pH (make it less acidic). Increasing the pH above 3.5 reduces pepsin activity (pepsin is most active in a highly acidic environment), and increasing it to 4 or 5 can significantly inhibit pepsin's peptic activity.
  • Pepsin Inhibition: At a pH of 4, the activity of pepsin, a proteolytic enzyme in gastric juice that can damage the stomach lining, is inhibited. At a pH of 5, pepsin activity is almost completely abolished, reducing its contribution to mucosal damage.

Uses:

  • Treatment of hyperacidity-related conditions: Primarily used for symptomatic relief of conditions associated with excess gastric acid, such as:
    • Heartburn (pyrosis)
    • Acid indigestion
    • Gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD) - for occasional symptom relief.
    • Peptic ulcer disease - as adjunctive therapy to relieve symptoms, but not primary treatment for ulcer healing.
    • Duodenal ulcer - to alleviate acid-related discomfort.

Contraindications:

  • Sodium-containing antacids: Contraindicated or used with caution in patients with:
    • Congestive Heart Failure (CHF)
    • Hypertension
    • Other conditions requiring low sodium diets, as sodium intake can exacerbate fluid retention and cardiovascular issues.
  • Pregnancy: Use during pregnancy should be limited and under medical advice due to potential electrolyte imbalances.
  • Children less than 6 years of age: Not recommended for young children without medical supervision.

Nursing considerations:

  • Administration Timing:
    • 3 Hours After Meals: Recommend that most antacids be taken about 3 hours after meals and at bedtime to neutralize acid when it is typically most problematic and to prolong the duration of effect.
    • Chewable Tablets: Tablets should be thoroughly chewed before swallowing to increase surface area for acid neutralization and enhance effectiveness.
    • Liquid Forms: Liquid suspensions should be thoroughly shaken before pouring to ensure uniform dose and efficacy.
    • Follow with Water or Milk: Administer tablets followed by a glass of milk or water to aid in swallowing and enhance drug dissolution and action in the stomach.
  • Fluid Intake: Encourage adequate fluid intake:
    • 2500-3000 cc fluids daily: Advise clients taking aluminum or calcium-containing antacids to maintain adequate fluid intake (2500-3000 cc daily) to prevent constipation, a common side effect, especially with aluminum-based antacids.
  • Monitor Bowel Function: Advise clients to report persistent diarrhea or constipation to their physician, as these may require adjustment in antacid type or additional management.
    • Switch Antacid Type: If constipation occurs with aluminum or calcium-containing antacids, switching to a magnesium-containing antacid (or alternating) may help, as magnesium can have a laxative effect. Conversely, for diarrhea, aluminum or calcium-containing antacids may be preferable due to their constipating effects.

✍️ Practice Exercise

Question: Explain the mechanism of action of antacids in relieving symptoms of acid reflux and peptic ulcers. What instructions would you give to a patient regarding the timing and method of taking antacids to maximize their effectiveness and minimize side effects like constipation?

View Solution

Mechanism of Action of Antacids in Relieving Acid Reflux and Peptic Ulcers: Antacids relieve symptoms of acid reflux and peptic ulcers by directly neutralizing stomach acid. This neutralization process reduces the acidity in the stomach and duodenum, which helps alleviate symptoms and promote healing. Key aspects of their mechanism include:

  • Chemical Neutralization: Antacids are basic (alkaline) compounds containing ingredients like aluminum hydroxide, magnesium hydroxide, calcium carbonate, or sodium bicarbonate. When ingested, these bases chemically react with hydrochloric acid (HCl) in the stomach.
  • Reduced Gastric Acidity: The chemical reaction neutralizes the HCl, converting it into water and salt. This process raises the pH of the stomach contents, making it less acidic. For example, aluminum hydroxide reacts with HCl to form aluminum chloride and water.
  • Pepsin Inactivation: By raising the gastric pH, antacids also reduce the activity of pepsin, an enzyme that digests proteins and can damage the stomach lining in acidic conditions. Pepsin is most active at a low pH (acidic environment); increasing the pH to 4 or 5 significantly diminishes its activity.
  • Symptom Relief: The reduction in gastric acidity and pepsin activity helps to relieve symptoms such as heartburn, acid indigestion, and pain associated with peptic ulcers by reducing acid-induced irritation and damage to the esophageal and gastric mucosa.

Patient Instructions for Maximizing Effectiveness and Minimizing Constipation: To maximize the effectiveness of antacids and minimize side effects like constipation, instruct patients to:

  1. Timing of Administration:
    • Take 3 Hours After Meals and at Bedtime: For optimal effect, take antacids about 3 hours after meals and again at bedtime. This timing helps to neutralize acid when it is most likely to cause symptoms, especially after meals and during nighttime acid reflux. Taking antacids at bedtime can also help reduce nocturnal acid production.
  2. Method of Administration:
    • Chew Tablets Thoroughly: If using chewable tablets, chew them completely before swallowing. This increases the surface area of the antacid, enhancing its acid-neutralizing capacity and effectiveness.
    • Follow with Liquid: After chewing tablets or taking liquid antacids, follow with a full glass of water or milk. This aids in swallowing and helps distribute the antacid throughout the stomach, improving its action.
    • Shake Liquid Suspensions: If using liquid antacid suspensions, shake the bottle well before each dose to ensure that the medication is evenly distributed and to get a consistent dose.
  3. Fluid Intake and Diet:
    • Increase Fluid Intake: Drink 2500-3000 cc of fluids per day, especially if using aluminum or calcium-containing antacids, to help prevent constipation.
    • Dietary Fiber: Maintain a diet with adequate fiber to help counteract the constipating effects of some antacids, particularly those containing aluminum or calcium.
  4. Report Bowel Changes: Advise patients to report persistent diarrhea or constipation to their healthcare provider. These symptoms may require a change in the type of antacid or additional medical advice.

📚 Key Takeaways

  • Antiulcer drugs treat peptic ulcers by reducing gastric acidity; Antacids neutralize stomach acid, increasing pH and reducing pepsin activity.
  • Antacids are used for heartburn, acid indigestion, GERD, and peptic ulcer symptom relief; take 3 hours post-meals and at bedtime.
  • Contraindications for antacids include sodium-containing types in CHF or hypertension; chronic use of aluminum antacids may link to Alzheimer's. Side effects include constipation (aluminum/calcium) or diarrhea (magnesium).
  • Nursing advice: chew tablets well, follow with water, maintain high fluid intake (2.5-3L/day), and report persistent bowel changes.
