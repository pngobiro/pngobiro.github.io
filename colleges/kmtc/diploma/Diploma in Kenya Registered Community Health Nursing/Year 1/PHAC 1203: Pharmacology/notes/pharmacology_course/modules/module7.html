Channels of Administration: Subcutaneous to Rectal Routes

  • Describe subcutaneous, intramuscular, and intravenous routes of injection, including their specific advantages and disadvantages.
  • Explain intradermal and intrathecal routes of administration and their specialized uses.
  • Discuss the rectal route of drug administration, including its uses, advantages, and disadvantages.
  • Identify factors that affect drug absorption and action for each route of administration discussed.

üìö Parenteral Routes of Administration (Continued)

There are several techniques for administering drugs by injection, each targeting a specific tissue area. These include:

  1. A. Subcutaneous Injection (SC): SC injection involves administering a drug into the subcutaneous tissues, just beneath the skin.

    Advantages of Subcutaneous Injection:

    • Slow Drug Absorption: Provides slow and sustained drug absorption, which can be advantageous for certain medications.

    Disadvantages of Subcutaneous Injection:

    • Painful Route: Generally considered the most painful injection route due to nerve endings in the subcutaneous tissue.
    • Slow Absorption Rate: Absorption is slower compared to intramuscular or intravenous routes, making it less suitable for emergencies.
    • Not Effective in Emergencies: Due to poor blood supply in subcutaneous tissue, it is not effective when rapid drug action is required.

    üí° Pro Tip

    Drug absorption from SC injection can be modulated:

    • Reduced by adding a vasoconstrictor (e.g., epinephrine) or applying cold packs, both causing vasoconstriction and decreased blood flow.
    • Increased by applying heat, which causes vasodilation and increased blood supply, leading to quicker drug absorption.
    • These manipulations can be used to manage toxicity or enhance drug effect duration.
  2. B. Intramuscular Route (IM): IM injection delivers drugs directly into the muscle tissue.

    Characteristics of Intramuscular Injection:

    • Widely Used: Second most common route for injectable drugs.
    • Suitable for Solutions and Suspensions: Can be used for both aqueous solutions and depot preparations (suspensions).
    • Oil-Based Drugs: Drugs that are very water-insoluble can be administered IM in an oil base, allowing for slow release over an extended period.
    • Vascular Network: Muscle tissue has a greater vascular network than subcutaneous tissue, leading to quicker drug absorption compared to SC route.
    • Less Painful than SC: Generally less painful than subcutaneous injections due to fewer nerve endings in muscle tissue.
  3. C. Intravenous Injection (IV): IV injection involves administering medication directly into a vein.

    Methods of Intravenous Injection:

    • Bolus Injection: Administering the entire drug quantity at once.
    • Continuous Infusion (IV drip): Infusing the drug slowly and continuously over time.

    Advantages of Intravenous Injection:

    • Rapid and Accurate Blood Levels: Achieves highly accurate and immediate blood drug levels.
    • Route for Irritant Drugs: Provides a route for administering drugs that may be irritant via other routes, as rapid dilution in blood reduces local irritation (e.g., chemotherapy).
    • Less Painful: Less painful during drug administration compared to SC and IM routes.

    Disadvantages of Intravenous Injection:

    • Risk of Overdose: Accidental overdose is a significant risk, and once injected, there is no way to retrieve the drug from circulation.
    • Infiltration: Risk of drug infiltration into surrounding tissues if the needle slips out of the vein, causing local reactions.
    • Thrombophlebitis: Repeated IV injections can lead to thrombophlebitis (vein inflammation). Using continuous IV drip can minimize this risk.
    • Inadvertent Arterial Injection: Accidental injection into an artery instead of a vein is a severe complication.

    üí° Pro Tip

    IV solutions must be completely homogenous and free from particulate matter to prevent emboli (EMBLOI). Never inject solutions with visible particles intravenously.

  4. D. Intradermal (ID) and Intrathecal Administration:
    • Intradermal (ID): Involves injecting the drug just below the epidermis (superficial skin layer). Primarily used for allergy testing and sensitivity tests (e.g., TB test, penicillin sensitivity test).
    • Intrathecal: Involves injecting drugs into the cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) by inserting a needle between vertebrae into the spinal fluid. Used for spinal anesthesia, chemotherapy for CNS metastases, and bypassing the blood-brain barrier.

üìö E. Rectal, Urethral, and Vaginal Suppositories

Suppositories are drug mixtures with a firm base, molded into shapes for insertion into body cavities or orifices (rectum, urethra, vagina). The rectal route is particularly useful when:

  • Oral route is not feasible (e.g., patient is vomiting or unconscious).
  • Physician prefers to avoid injectable routes.
  • Self-medication is desired over oral routes in some cases.

Advantages of Suppositories:

  • Local or Systemic Effects: Can produce both local effects (e.g., hemorrhoid treatment) and systemic effects as the drug is absorbed into the bloodstream.

Disadvantages of Suppositories:

  • Erratic Absorption: Absorption from the rectal area can be unpredictable, leading to inaccurate blood drug level predictions and requiring dosage adjustments.
  • Ineffective with Diarrhea: Rectal route is not effective if the patient has diarrhea as the drug may be expelled before absorption.

Examples of Suppositories:

  • Rectal suppositories: Used to treat hemorrhoids.
  • Vaginal suppositories: Used to treat vaginitis.
  • Urethral suppositories: Used to treat bladder conditions.

üí° Pro Tip

Suppositories should be kept cool to maintain their shape and efficacy.

Question: A patient requires immediate and precise drug blood levels. Which parenteral route is most appropriate, and why? Conversely, if a patient is experiencing nausea and vomiting, which route of administration might be preferred if medication is needed?

View Solution

Most appropriate route for immediate and precise drug blood levels: Intravenous (IV) injection is the most appropriate route because it directly introduces the drug into the bloodstream, achieving immediate and predictable drug concentrations.

Preferred route for a patient with nausea and vomiting: For a patient experiencing nausea and vomiting, the rectal route via suppositories might be preferred. This route bypasses the oral route, which may be compromised due to vomiting, and avoids the discomfort and potential complications of injections, while still allowing for drug absorption and systemic effect.

üìö Key Takeaways

  • Subcutaneous (SC) injection offers slow, sustained absorption, while intramuscular (IM) provides quicker absorption due to muscle vascularity.
  • Intravenous (IV) injection achieves immediate and complete bioavailability but carries risks of overdose and complications.
  • Intradermal (ID) and intrathecal routes are specialized for local skin reactions and CNS drug delivery, respectively.
  • Rectal suppositories offer an alternative when oral or injectable routes are not suitable, providing local or systemic effects with variable absorption.
