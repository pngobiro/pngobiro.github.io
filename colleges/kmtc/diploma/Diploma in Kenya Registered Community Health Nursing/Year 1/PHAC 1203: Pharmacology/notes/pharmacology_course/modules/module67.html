üìö Adrenergic blocking (sympatholytic) Agents

Adrenergic blocking agents, also known as sympatholytic agents or adrenergic antagonists, are drugs that block the effects of adrenergic neurotransmitters (epinephrine and norepinephrine) and adrenergic drugs. They act by binding to adrenergic receptors but, unlike agonists, do not activate them. Instead, they prevent receptor activation by endogenous catecholamines and sympathomimetic drugs, thus reducing or blocking sympathetic nervous system effects.

Beta-Blockers as Adrenergic Blocking Agents:

Beta-blockers, a significant class of adrenergic blocking agents, have been previously discussed in detail (Module 47). As a reminder, their primary action is to block beta-adrenergic receptors, leading to:

  • Cardiovascular Effects: Reduced heart rate, decreased myocardial contractility, and lowered blood pressure by blocking Œ≤1 receptors in the heart.
  • Bronchial Effects: Potential for bronchoconstriction by blocking Œ≤2 receptors in the lungs (more pronounced with nonselective beta-blockers).
  • Clinical Uses: Management of hypertension, angina pectoris, certain arrhythmias, and post-myocardial infarction therapy.

üìö Cholinergic Blocking (Parasympatholytic) Drugs

Cholinergic blocking drugs, also known as parasympatholytic agents or anticholinergics, are medications that block the action of acetylcholine (ACh), the primary neurotransmitter of the parasympathetic nervous system (PNS). By blocking acetylcholine, these drugs reduce or inhibit parasympathetic nerve impulses, leading to a variety of physiological effects.

Action of Cholinergic Blocking Drugs:

Cholinergic blocking agents exert their effects by preventing acetylcholine from binding to its receptors. Key aspects of their action include:

  • Acetylcholine Receptor Blockade: These agents competitively block muscarinic and nicotinic cholinergic receptors, preventing acetylcholine from binding and exerting its normal effects.
  • Antagonizing Parasympathetic Effects: By blocking acetylcholine, they reduce or eliminate parasympathetic nervous system influences on various organs and tissues.

View Solution

Adrenergic Blocking Agents (Sympatholytics) vs. Cholinergic Blocking Drugs (Parasympatholytics):

  • General Mechanisms of Action:
    • Adrenergic Blocking Agents (Sympatholytics): Block adrenergic receptors (alpha and beta), preventing the binding and action of adrenergic neurotransmitters like norepinephrine and epinephrine. They essentially dampen or block the "fight or flight" responses mediated by the sympathetic nervous system.
    • Cholinergic Blocking Drugs (Parasympatholytics): Block cholinergic receptors, specifically muscarinic and nicotinic receptors, preventing acetylcholine from binding and exerting its effects. They inhibit or reduce the "rest and digest" activities of the parasympathetic nervous system.
  • Nervous System Primarily Antagonized:
    • Adrenergic Blocking Agents: Primarily antagonize the Sympathetic Nervous System (SNS).
    • Cholinergic Blocking Drugs: Primarily antagonize the Parasympathetic Nervous System (PNS).
  • Example of Clinical Effect for Each Class:
    • Adrenergic Blocking Agents (Beta-Blockers): Reduced Heart Rate in Hypertension. Beta-blockers like Atenolol block Œ≤1-adrenergic receptors in the heart, reducing heart rate and contractility. This effect is therapeutically used to lower blood pressure and manage hypertension by decreasing cardiac output and sympathetic drive to the heart.
    • Cholinergic Blocking Drugs (Anticholinergics): Bronchodilation in Asthma. Atropine, a cholinergic blocking drug, can cause bronchodilation by blocking muscarinic receptors in bronchial smooth muscle. Although not a primary treatment for asthma, anticholinergics can be used to reduce bronchospasm in certain respiratory conditions by diminishing parasympathetic-induced bronchial constriction. However, Œ≤2-agonists are the mainstay for bronchodilation in asthma due to their more direct and selective action on bronchial smooth muscle.

üìö Key Takeaways

  • Adrenergic blocking agents (sympatholytics) block adrenergic receptors, antagonizing sympathetic nervous system effects; Beta-blockers are a key example.
  • Cholinergic blocking drugs (parasympatholytics) block cholinergic receptors, antagonizing parasympathetic nervous system effects.
  • Adrenergic blockers reduce heart rate and blood pressure; cholinergic blockers can cause bronchodilation and reduce secretions.
