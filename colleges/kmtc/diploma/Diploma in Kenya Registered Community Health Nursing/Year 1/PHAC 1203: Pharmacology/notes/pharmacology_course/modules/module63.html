üìö Examples of NSAIDs

Let's examine specific examples of Nonsteroidal Anti-inflammatory Drugs (NSAIDs) to understand their individual characteristics, uses, and nursing considerations:

  1. 1. Diclofenac Sodium: Voltaren, Rufenal
    • Trade name: Voltaren, Rufenal.
    • Class.: Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory analgesic.
    • Dosage Forms: Available in various formulations:
      • Suppositories: For rectal administration, useful when oral route is compromised or for localized rectal conditions.
      • Tablets: Immediate-release and enteric-coated tablets for oral administration.
      • Injection: For intramuscular (IM) use, typically for acute pain or when oral administration is not feasible.
    • Dose: Varies depending on the formulation and condition treated, typically ranging from 100-200 mg daily in 2-4 divided doses.
    • Nursing considerations:
      • Administer with Food: Give diclofenac on a full stomach to minimize gastrointestinal irritation. Food can help buffer the drug's effect on the stomach lining.
      • Deep IM Injection: When administering intramuscularly, ensure deep injection into a large muscle mass to reduce local irritation and pain, as diclofenac can be irritating to tissues.
  2. 2. Indomethacin: Indocid
    • Trade name: Indocid.
    • Class. : Anti-inflammatory, analgesic, antipyretic, NSAID.
    • Dosage Forms: Available in:
      • Suppositories: For rectal administration, useful for nocturnal pain relief and when oral route is not preferred.
      • Capsules: For oral administration, typically immediate-release.
    • Dose: Typically 25mg ‚Äì 50 mg two to three times daily (bid-tid), adjusted based on patient response and tolerance.
  3. 3. Ibuprofen: Brufen, artofen
    • Trade names: Brufen, artofen, Motrin, Advil.
    • Class.: Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory analgesic, NSAID.
    • Dosage: Available in various strengths; typical adult dose is 300 mg orally, two to three times daily (bid-tid), adjusted based on pain and inflammation severity.

üìö Drug Interactions and General Nursing Considerations for NSAIDs

Drug Interactions:

NSAIDs can interact with several other classes of drugs, potentially altering their effects or increasing the risk of adverse reactions:

  • Anticoagulants (e.g., Warfarin, Heparin): Increased risk of bleeding due to additive antiplatelet effects. Concurrent use should be avoided if possible; if necessary, monitor closely for signs of bleeding.
  • Steroids (Corticosteroids): Increased risk of gastrointestinal bleeding and ulceration when NSAIDs are used with corticosteroids. Combination should be avoided unless necessary and patients are closely monitored for GI adverse effects.
  • Alcohol: Increased risk of GI bleeding and irritation. Patients should be advised to limit or avoid alcohol intake while taking NSAIDs.
  • Other NSAIDs: Concurrent use of multiple NSAIDs increases the risk of side effects, particularly GI bleeding and renal toxicity, without significant additional benefit. Combination therapy should generally be avoided.

General Nursing Considerations for NSAIDs:

  1. Assess Patient History: Obtain thorough patient history, including allergies (especially to aspirin or other NSAIDs), gastrointestinal ulcer history, bleeding disorders, cardiovascular conditions, and renal function, to identify contraindications and risk factors.
  2. Administer with Food: Administer oral NSAIDs with food or milk to reduce gastrointestinal irritation and discomfort.
  3. Monitor for GI Side Effects: Educate patients about the signs of GI irritation and bleeding (e.g., stomach pain, black stools, coffee-ground emesis) and instruct them to report these symptoms immediately.
  4. Monitor for Bleeding: Observe patients for signs of bleeding, especially if they are also taking anticoagulants or have a history of bleeding disorders. Monitor for bruising, petechiae, bleeding gums, and prolonged bleeding from cuts.
  5. Renal Function Monitoring: For patients at risk of renal impairment or those on long-term NSAID therapy, monitor renal function (BUN, creatinine) periodically, as NSAIDs can impair renal blood flow and function.
  6. Cardiovascular Risk Awareness: Be aware of the cardiovascular risks associated with NSAIDs, especially non-selective NSAIDs and COX-2 inhibitors. Assess patients with cardiovascular disease for increased risk of thrombotic events and heart failure exacerbation.
  7. Patient Education: Educate patients on:
    • Proper use of NSAIDs, including dosage and administration instructions.
    • Potential side effects and signs of adverse reactions to report.
    • Importance of avoiding concurrent use of other NSAIDs or aspirin without consulting a healthcare provider.
    • The need to inform healthcare providers about NSAID use before surgery or dental procedures due to increased bleeding risk.

Comparison of Diclofenac and Ibuprofen:

  • Clinical Uses:
    • Diclofenac:
      • Musculoskeletal Conditions: Widely used for rheumatoid arthritis, osteoarthritis, ankylosing spondylitis, and acute musculoskeletal injuries.
      • Post-operative Pain: Effective for managing moderate to severe postoperative pain and inflammatory conditions.
      • Specific Formulations: Suppository form useful for nocturnal pain and when oral administration is not feasible. Injection form for acute, severe pain.
    • Ibuprofen:
      • Mild to Moderate Pain: Effective for mild to moderate pain relief, such as headache, dental pain, menstrual cramps, and muscle aches.
      • Fever Reduction: Commonly used as an antipyretic to reduce fever in adults and children.
      • Inflammatory Conditions: Used for osteoarthritis, rheumatoid arthritis, and other inflammatory conditions, though often at higher doses than for simple analgesia.
      • OTC Availability: Available over-the-counter in lower strengths, making it easily accessible for self-management of minor pain and fever.
  • Side Effect Profiles:
    • Diclofenac:
      • Gastrointestinal: Moderate risk of GI side effects, including ulceration and bleeding, though generally considered to have a slightly lower GI risk compared to indomethacin but higher than ibuprofen.
      • Cardiovascular: Some studies suggest a higher cardiovascular risk compared to ibuprofen and naproxen, particularly for thrombotic events. Use with caution in patients with cardiovascular disease.
      • Hepatic: Potential for liver enzyme elevation; liver function monitoring may be warranted with long-term use.
      • Injection Site Reactions: IM injection can be painful and cause local irritation; suppositories can cause rectal irritation.
    • Ibuprofen:
      • Gastrointestinal: Generally considered to have a lower risk of GI side effects compared to aspirin and some other non-selective NSAIDs like diclofenac and indomethacin, especially at lower OTC doses. However, GI risks still exist, particularly at higher prescription doses or with long-term use.
      • Cardiovascular: Cardiovascular risk is present, similar to other NSAIDs, especially at higher doses and in patients with pre-existing heart conditions, but generally considered lower than some COX-2 selective inhibitors and possibly diclofenac.
      • Renal: Renal side effects are typical of NSAIDs, including risk of acute kidney injury, especially in dehydrated or elderly patients.

    Clinical Scenarios Favoring Diclofenac vs. Ibuprofen:

    • Diclofenac Preferred When:
      • Moderate to Severe Inflammatory Conditions: Conditions requiring potent anti-inflammatory action, such as ankylosing spondylitis or acute gout flares, where its stronger COX-2 inhibition may be beneficial.
      • Post-operative Pain: Management of moderate to severe pain after surgery, particularly when IM or suppository forms are convenient or necessary.
    • Ibuprofen Preferred When:
      • Mild to Moderate Pain and Fever: For routine pain relief of headaches, menstrual cramps, muscle aches, and fever reduction, especially in OTC settings.
      • Patients at Lower GI Risk: In patients at higher risk of GI side effects where a lower GI risk NSAID is desired, especially at lower doses and for short-term use.
      • Pediatric Use: Often favored in pediatric populations for fever and pain relief due to extensive pediatric dosing experience and availability of child-friendly formulations.

üìö Key Takeaways

  • Diclofenac (Voltaren, Rufenal) is used for musculoskeletal conditions and post-op pain; administer with food, deep IM to reduce irritation.
  • Indomethacin (Indocid) is potent for inflammatory conditions, available in suppositories and capsules, monitor for GI and CNS side effects.
  • Ibuprofen (Brufen, Artofen) is common OTC NSAID for mild pain and fever; lower GI risk but still use caution in sensitive patients.
  • General NSAID considerations: assess allergy history, administer with food, monitor for GI and bleeding risks, and educate patients on side effects and interactions.
