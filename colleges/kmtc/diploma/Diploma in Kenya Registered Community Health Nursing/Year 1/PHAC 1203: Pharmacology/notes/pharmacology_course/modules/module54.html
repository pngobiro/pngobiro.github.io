üìö Centrally acting skeletal muscle relaxants* Anticonvulsants

Centrally acting skeletal muscle relaxants are drugs that act on the central nervous system to reduce muscle spasm and hypertonia. Some of these drugs, like Phenytoin, also have significant anticonvulsant properties, making them versatile in treating neurological conditions. Phenytoin is a prime example of a drug with dual classification as both a centrally acting skeletal muscle relaxant and an anticonvulsant.

üìö Phenytoin: Dilantin

Phenytoin (Dilantin) is a widely used anticonvulsant medication, effective in managing various seizure disorders. It is also classified as a centrally acting skeletal muscle relaxant due to its CNS depressant effects. Key features include:

  • Trade name: Dilantin.
  • Class: Anticonvulsant , antiarrhythmic.
  • Action: Phenytoin acts in the motor cortex of the brain to reduce the spread of electrical discharges from the rapidly firing epileptic foci in this area. Also affects activity of centers in the brain stem responsible for the tonic phase of grand mal seizures.
  • Uses:
    • Chronic epilepsy.
    • Premature ventricular contractions.
    • Tachycardia.
  • Contraindications:
    • Hypersensitivity.
  • Side effects:
    • Drowsiness, ataxia, dizziness, measles-like rash, gingival hyperplasia, Hirsutism (excessive hair growth) , hypoglycemia.
    • N.B: Rapid I.V. administration Hypotension & arrhythmia.
  • Dosage for arrhythmias:
    • Tabs 200-400 mg daily.
    • I.V. 100 mg q 5 minutes up to a maximum of 1g.
  • Nursing considerations:
    • I.V. phenytoin may form a precipitate, so flush tubing by saline (not dextrose) before & after administration.
    • Assess for hypersensitivity.
    • If a pregnant woman takes this drug, tell her not to breast-feed her baby.
    • Obtain liver & kidney function studies.
    • Monitor serum drug levels on a routine basis.
    • During I.V. therapy, monitor B.P. for signs of hypotension.
    • Take food to minimize GI upset.
    • If the patient is diabetic, monitor for signs of hypoglycemia.
    • Oral hygiene to minimize bleeding from the gum.
    • Report any excessive growth of hair.

Question: Explain the mechanism of action of Phenytoin as an anticonvulsant. Why is slow intravenous administration of Phenytoin crucial, and what are the potential cardiovascular risks associated with rapid IV infusion?

View Solution

Mechanism of Action of Phenytoin as an Anticonvulsant: Phenytoin's anticonvulsant action is primarily due to its modulation of sodium channels in neurons, which stabilizes nerve cell membranes and reduces the hyperexcitability that leads to seizures. Specifically:

  • Sodium Channel Blockade: Phenytoin prolongs the inactivated state of voltage-gated sodium channels. These channels are essential for the generation and propagation of action potentials in neurons. By prolonging their inactivation, phenytoin reduces the ability of neurons to fire at high frequencies.
  • Stabilization of Neuronal Membranes: This action stabilizes neuronal membranes, making them less excitable and less likely to depolarize excessively and generate repetitive action potentials that characterize seizure activity.
  • Limiting Seizure Spread: By reducing neuronal excitability and stabilizing membranes, phenytoin helps to limit the spread of electrical discharges from seizure foci in the brain, thus preventing or controlling seizures.

Cruciality of Slow Intravenous Administration of Phenytoin and Cardiovascular Risks: Slow intravenous (IV) administration of phenytoin is crucial due to the significant cardiovascular risks associated with rapid infusion. Rapid IV infusion can lead to:

  • Hypotension: Phenytoin can cause vasodilation and depress cardiac function, leading to a sudden drop in blood pressure if administered too quickly. Rapid infusion overwhelms the body's compensatory mechanisms, resulting in significant hypotension.
  • Cardiac Arrhythmias: Phenytoin can affect cardiac electrical activity, and rapid IV administration can disrupt normal cardiac rhythms, potentially causing bradycardia, heart block, ventricular fibrillation, or asystole. These arrhythmias can be life-threatening.
  • "Purple Glove Syndrome": Although not directly cardiovascular, rapid IV infusion, especially into small veins or in elderly patients, can increase the risk of "purple glove syndrome," a painful distal extremity syndrome involving discoloration, swelling, and pain, thought to be related to extravasation and local tissue irritation.

Nursing Implications for Safe IV Phenytoin Administration:

  • Slow Infusion Rate: Adhere strictly to recommended infusion rates (typically not exceeding 50 mg/minute in adults) to minimize cardiovascular risks. Use an infusion pump to control the rate accurately.
  • Cardiac Monitoring: Continuously monitor ECG and blood pressure during IV phenytoin administration, especially during loading doses.
  • Dilution: Dilute phenytoin in appropriate solutions (typically normal saline) as recommended by manufacturer guidelines. Avoid dextrose solutions as phenytoin may precipitate.
  • Avoid Hand Veins: Use a large vein, preferably in the antecubital fossa or a central line if available, to reduce the risk of local reactions and ensure adequate dilution by blood flow. Avoid veins in the hand or wrist if possible.
  • Monitor for Hypotension and Arrhythmias: Watch closely for signs of hypotension (dizziness, lightheadedness, decreased blood pressure) and cardiac arrhythmias (bradycardia, irregular pulse). Have emergency medications (vasopressors, antiarrhythmics) and resuscitation equipment readily available.

üìö Key Takeaways

  • Phenytoin, an anticonvulsant, stabilizes neuronal membranes by modulating sodium channels, used for chronic epilepsy and certain arrhythmias.
  • Phenytoin side effects include drowsiness, ataxia, gingival hyperplasia, and hirsutism; rapid IV infusion risks hypotension and arrhythmias. Contraindicated in hypersensitivity.
  • Nursing considerations involve slow IV administration, cardiac monitoring during IV use, monitoring serum levels, ensuring good oral hygiene, and educating patients about side effects and adherence.
