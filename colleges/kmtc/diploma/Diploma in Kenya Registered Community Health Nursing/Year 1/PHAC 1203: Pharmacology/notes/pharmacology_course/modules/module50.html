üìö Anti-anxiety Agents

Anti-anxiety agents, also known as anxiolytics or minor tranquilizers, are medications used to reduce anxiety, tension, and nervousness. Benzodiazepines are the most commonly prescribed class of anti-anxiety agents due to their effectiveness and relatively favorable safety profile compared to older agents like barbiturates. Key aspects of anti-anxiety agents include:

  • Therapeutic Goal: To relieve symptoms of anxiety disorders, stress-related conditions, and to provide sedation or muscle relaxation in certain medical procedures.
  • Classes: Primary class is benzodiazepines; other categories include antihistamines (like hydroxyzine) and certain antidepressants (like SSRIs and SNRIs), though benzodiazepines are typically the first-line for rapid anxiety relief.
  • Benzodiazepines: Widely favored due to their efficacy, rapid onset of action, and better safety margin compared to barbiturates. However, they also carry risks, including dependence and withdrawal symptoms.

üìö Diazepam: A Benzodiazepine Example (Valium)

Diazepam (Valium, Assival) is a long-acting benzodiazepine, widely used for its anxiolytic, sedative, muscle relaxant, and anticonvulsant properties. Key features include:

  • Trade name: Valium, assival.
  • Class: Antianxiety agent, benzodiazepine.
  • Action: Benzodiazepines, including diazepam, enhance the effects of GABA (gamma-aminobutyric acid), an inhibitory neurotransmitter in the brain. They:
    • Enhance GABA Action: Increase the efficiency of GABA binding to GABA-A receptors, which enhances chloride ion influx into neurons.
    • CNS Neurotransmitter Inhibition: This increased chloride influx leads to hyperpolarization of neuronal membranes, reducing neuronal excitability and causing CNS depression, which mediates the anxiolytic, sedative, and muscle relaxant effects.
  • Metabolism and Excretion: Diazepam is metabolized in the liver and excreted through the urine. Liver impairment can significantly affect its metabolism and necessitate dosage adjustments.
  • Indications: Diazepam has a broad range of clinical uses:
    • Symptomatic relief of anxiety and tension associated with anxiety disorders, situational stress, and panic disorder.
    • Alcohol withdrawal: Management of acute alcohol withdrawal symptoms, including agitation, tremors, and delirium tremens.
    • Muscle relaxant: To relieve muscle spasm and spasticity associated with various conditions.
    • Anticonvulsant: Used as an adjunct in treating certain types of seizures, particularly status epilepticus.
    • Preoperatively: To provide sedation and reduce anxiety before surgery or medical procedures.
    • Pre- endoscopy: Used before gastroscopy or esophagoscopy to reduce anxiety and muscle tension, facilitating the procedure.
    • Treatment of status epilepticus: As an emergency treatment to control prolonged seizures.
    • Relief of facial muscle spasm: Management of muscle spasms in the face and jaw, such as temporomandibular joint (TMJ) disorders.
  • Contraindications:
    • Hypersensitivity to benzodiazepines.
    • Acute narrow-angle glauccma.
    • Pregnancy.
    • Shock, coma.
    • Alcoholic intoxication (to avoid respiratory of depression).
  • Side effects:
    • Drowsiness, fatigue, ataxia, hypotension, visual disturbances, headache, phlebitis at injection site.
  • Dosage:
    • Ampules of 2 ml containing 10 mg.
    • Tablets 2 mg, 5 mg or 10 mg.
    • I.V. or I. M. 2-20mg depending on the indication.
    • Tablets 2-10 mg 2-4 times daily.

Question: Explain the mechanism by which benzodiazepines like Diazepam reduce anxiety. What are the key nursing considerations for patients who are prescribed benzodiazepines for long-term anxiety management?

View Solution

Receptor Theory Explained: Imagine receptors as locks on the surface of cells, and drugs as keys. According to the receptor theory, drugs work by fitting into these "locks" (receptors). When a drug "key" fits into a receptor "lock," it can either activate the receptor to produce a cell response (like turning a key to open a door - agonist effect) or block the receptor to prevent a natural substance from activating it (like inserting a wrong key that blocks the lock - antagonist effect).

Example of an Agonist: Albuterol, used for asthma, acts as an agonist at beta-2 adrenergic receptors in the lungs. It "turns on" these receptors to relax bronchial muscles, opening up airways and easing breathing, similar to how adrenaline (a natural agonist) would.

Example of an Antagonist: Propranolol, a beta-blocker, acts as an antagonist at beta-1 adrenergic receptors in the heart. It "blocks" these receptors, preventing adrenaline and noradrenaline from increasing heart rate and force. This helps to slow down the heart rate and lower blood pressure, which is useful in conditions like hypertension or angina.

üìö Key Takeaways

  • Anti-anxiety agents (benzodiazepines) enhance GABA's inhibitory effects in the CNS, reducing anxiety, tension, and inducing sedation.
  • Diazepam (Valium) is a long-acting benzodiazepine used for anxiety, alcohol withdrawal, muscle spasm, seizures, and preoperative sedation. Side effects include drowsiness and hypotension.
  • Contraindications for benzodiazepines include glaucoma, pregnancy, and acute alcohol intoxication. Use short-term and at lowest effective dose to minimize dependence.
  • Nursing considerations include monitoring for CNS depression, hypotension, dependence, and educating patients on gradual withdrawal and avoiding alcohol.
