üìö Coronary vasodilators: Antianginal drugs

Antianginal drugs are used to treat angina pectoris, a condition characterized by chest pain due to myocardial ischemia (inadequate blood supply to the heart muscle). These drugs aim to reduce the frequency, severity, and duration of anginal attacks.

Angina Pectoris:

  • Definition: Angina pectoris is a clinical syndrome characterized by paroxysmal (sudden, periodic) chest pain, typically in the anterior chest. This pain is caused by myocardial ischemia‚Äîan imbalance between myocardial oxygen supply and demand.
  • Causes: The imbalance in oxygen supply and demand is usually due to:
    • Atherosclerosis: Narrowing of coronary arteries due to plaque buildup, reducing blood flow.
    • Vasospasm: Spasm of coronary arteries, temporarily reducing blood flow to the myocardium.

Drug Groups for Angina Treatment:

There are three main groups of drugs used to treat angina:

  1. Nitrates/nitrites
  2. Beta-adrenergic blocking agents (Beta-blockers)
  3. Calcium channel blocking agents (Calcium antagonists)

üìö Nitrates/nitrites: Mechanism of Action

Nitrates and nitrites are vasodilators that are a cornerstone in the treatment of angina pectoris. Nitroglycerin is the most commonly used nitrate. Their mechanism of action involves:

  • Direct Vasodilation: Nitrates directly relax vascular smooth muscle throughout the body, affecting both arteries and veins. This vasodilation helps to:
    • Reduce myocardial oxygen requirements by decreasing cardiac preload and afterload.
    • Increase myocardial oxygen supply by dilating coronary arteries and improving collateral blood flow to ischemic areas of the myocardium.
  • Effects on Vascular Beds:
    • Venodilation: Predominantly dilate veins, leading to reduced venous return to the heart (reduced preload). This decreases ventricular filling pressure and myocardial wall tension, lowering oxygen demand.
    • Arterial Dilation: Also dilate arteries, particularly coronary arteries, which increases coronary blood flow, improving oxygen supply to the myocardium. Dilation of systemic arterioles reduces systemic vascular resistance and arterial blood pressure (reduced afterload), further decreasing myocardial workload.

üìö Indications and Contraindications of Nitrates/nitrites

Indications:

  1. Prophylaxis and treatment of acute angina pectoris: To prevent angina attacks (prophylaxis) and to relieve pain during an acute anginal episode (treatment).
  2. Treatment of chronic angina pectoris: For long-term management to reduce the frequency and severity of anginal symptoms.
  3. Treatment of hypertension associated with myocardial infarction (MI) or congestive heart failure (CHF): To reduce cardiac workload and improve hemodynamics in these conditions.
  4. Nitroglycerin ointment is also specifically indicated for the treatment of Raynaud‚Äôs disease, a condition causing vasospasm in peripheral blood vessels.

Contraindications:

  1. Sensitivity to nitrates or nitrites: Allergic reactions to these drugs.
  2. Hypotension: Already low blood pressure; nitrates can further lower BP, causing severe hypotension.
  3. Severe anemia: Can exacerbate hypotension and reduce oxygen delivery.
  4. Head trauma or increased intracranial pressure: Vasodilation could increase intracranial pressure.
  5. Cerebral hemorrhage: Risk of worsening bleeding in cerebral hemorrhage.

üìö Side Effects and Drug Interactions of Nitrates/nitrites

Side Effects:

  1. Headache: Common due to vasodilation of cerebral vessels; often transient and may diminish with continued use.
  2. Syncope and dizziness: Resulting from postural hypotension, especially upon standing quickly.
  3. Postural hypotension: Due to venous pooling and reduced venous return.
  4. Transient flushing and palpitation: Vasodilation can cause skin flushing and increased awareness of heartbeat.
  5. Topical application may lead to dermatitis (skin irritation or rash).

Drug interaction:

  • Antihypertensive agents, Beta-adrenergic blocking agents, and calcium-channel blocking agents: Concurrent use with nitrates can lead to additive hypotension, increasing the risk of dizziness and fainting. Monitor blood pressure closely when these drugs are used together.

Question: Explain how nitrates/nitrites alleviate chest pain in angina pectoris. Describe the effects of nitrates on both veins and arteries and how these effects contribute to angina relief.

View Solution

Mechanism of Action of Nitrates/Nitrites in Alleviating Angina Pectoris: Nitrates and nitrites, such as nitroglycerin, relieve chest pain in angina pectoris by reducing myocardial oxygen demand and increasing myocardial oxygen supply. This is achieved through vasodilation, affecting both veins and arteries.

Effects on Veins:

  • Venodilation: Nitrates primarily dilate systemic veins. This dilation increases venous capacity and reduces the amount of blood returning to the heart (reduced venous return or preload).
  • Reduced Preload: By decreasing preload, nitrates reduce the ventricular filling pressure and the stretch on the heart muscle fibers (myocardial wall tension). According to the Frank-Starling law, less stretch means less forceful contraction and, consequently, reduced myocardial oxygen demand.

Effects on Arteries:

  • Arterial Dilation: Nitrates also dilate arteries, particularly the large coronary arteries. This dilation increases coronary blood flow, improving oxygen supply to the ischemic (oxygen-deprived) areas of the myocardium.
  • Reduced Afterload: Dilation of systemic arterioles reduces peripheral vascular resistance, which in turn decreases arterial blood pressure and afterload (the resistance against which the heart must pump). Reduced afterload means the heart does not have to work as hard to eject blood, thus lowering myocardial oxygen demand.

Combined Effect:

By causing both venodilation and arterial dilation, nitrates effectively:

  • Reduce Myocardial Oxygen Demand: Decreased preload and afterload lower the workload on the heart, reducing its oxygen requirement.
  • Increase Myocardial Oxygen Supply: Dilation of coronary arteries improves blood flow to the heart muscle, enhancing oxygen delivery.

This dual action helps to restore the balance between oxygen supply and demand in the myocardium, alleviating the chest pain associated with angina pectoris.

üìö Key Takeaways

  • Angina pectoris is chest pain due to myocardial ischemia, often caused by atherosclerosis or vasospasm. Antianginal drugs aim to restore oxygen balance.
  • Nitrates/nitrites dilate blood vessels, reducing preload and afterload, and increasing coronary blood flow to relieve angina.
  • Nitrates are used for both acute angina treatment and prophylaxis, and to manage hypertension with MI or CHF; Nitroglycerin ointment treats Raynaud‚Äôs disease.
  • Side effects include headache, hypotension, dizziness; contraindications include hypotension, anemia, and head trauma. Drug interactions with antihypertensives increase hypotension risk.
