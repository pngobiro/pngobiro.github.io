📚 Tricyclic antidepressants

Tricyclic antidepressants (TCAs) are a class of antidepressants that were among the first medications used to treat depression. While newer antidepressants like SSRIs and SNRIs are now more commonly prescribed due to fewer side effects, TCAs remain effective for certain conditions. Key aspects of tricyclic antidepressants include:

  • Mechanism of Action: TCAs primarily work by:
    • Blocking Neurotransmitter Reuptake: Preventing the presynaptic re-uptake of neurotransmitters norepinephrine and serotonin in the brain. This action increases the concentration of these neurotransmitters in the synaptic cleft, enhancing neurotransmission.
    • Mood Elevation: By increasing the availability of norepinephrine and serotonin, TCAs help elevate mood and alleviate symptoms of depression.
  • Clinical Use: Effective in treating various depressive disorders, and also used for other conditions like chronic pain and nocturnal enuresis.

📚 1. Clomipramine Hydrochloride: A Tricyclic Antidepressant (Anafranil)

Clomipramine Hydrochloride (Anafranil) is a tricyclic antidepressant particularly noted for its efficacy in treating obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD). Key features include:

  • Trade name: Anafranil.
  • Class.: Antidepressant, tricyclic antidepressant (TCA).
  • Action: As a tricyclic antidepressant, Clomipramine:
    • Inhibits Reuptake of Serotonin and Norepinephrine: Prevents the presynaptic re-uptake of serotonin and norepinephrine, increasing their levels in the synaptic cleft and enhancing neurotransmission.
  • Uses: Primarily used for:
    • Treatment of obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD): Considered a first-line tricyclic antidepressant specifically for OCD, often more effective than other TCAs and SSRIs for this condition.
    • Panic disorders: Management of panic disorder symptoms.
    • Phobic disorders: Treatment of phobic and anxiety disorders.
  • Contraindications:
    • Hypersensitivity to clomipramine or other tricyclic antidepressants.
    • Use within 14 days of monoamine oxidase inhibitors (MAOIs) due to risk of serotonin syndrome.
    • Pregnancy and lactation: Generally contraindicated due to potential fetal and infant risks.
    • Shock: Contraindicated in patients experiencing shock.
    • Bone marrow depression: Patients with pre-existing bone marrow suppression.
    • Acute recovery phase after myocardial infarction: Use is generally avoided immediately post-MI.
  • Side effects: Significant side effects are common with clomipramine, including:
    • Anticholinergic Effects: Dry mouth, blurred vision, constipation, urinary retention.
    • Cardiovascular Effects: Orthostatic hypotension (dizziness upon standing), tachycardia, arrhythmias.
    • Central Nervous System Effects: Drowsiness, dizziness, seizures (lowers seizure threshold), tremors, anxiety, restlessness.
    • Other: Weight gain, sweating, sexual dysfunction.
  • Dosage: Dosage is individualized and started low, then gradually increased:
    • Tablets: Initial dose typically 25-50 mg daily.
    • Maintenance Dose: Dosage is gradually increased to 75-150 mg per day, given in 1-3 divided doses, depending on response and tolerance. Higher doses may be used for OCD treatment.

📚 2. Imipramine Hydrochloride: Another Tricyclic Antidepressant (Tofranil)

Imipramine Hydrochloride (Tofranil) is another classic tricyclic antidepressant, effective in treating depression and certain childhood conditions. Key features include:

  • Trade name: Tofranil.
  • Class.: Antidepressant, tricyclic antidepressant (TCA).
  • Action: Similar to clomipramine, imipramine:
    • Inhibits Reuptake of Serotonin and Norepinephrine: Prevents reuptake of serotonin and norepinephrine in the CNS, enhancing their neurotransmitter effects.
  • Uses:
    • Relief symptoms of depression: Effective in treating major depressive disorder, improving mood, sleep, and appetite.
    • Enuressis in children: Used to treat nocturnal enuresis (bed-wetting) in children aged 6 years and older. Its mechanism in enuresis is thought to be related to anticholinergic effects, bladder muscle relaxation, and possible effects on sleep cycles.
  • Contraindications: Similar to other TCAs, including hypersensitivity and recent MAOI use.
  • Side effects: Side effect profile is typical of TCAs:
    • Anticholinergic effects, cardiovascular effects (orthostatic hypotension, arrhythmias), CNS effects (drowsiness), and others similar to clomipramine.
  • Dosage: Varies by indication:
    • For treatment of depression in adults: Oral dosage typically starts at 50 mg twice or three times daily (bid or tid), gradually increased as needed up to 200-300 mg daily.
    • For treatment of children enureses (6 years or older): Lower doses are used, typically 25 mg daily, 1 hr before bedtime. Dosage may be adjusted based on response and age.

✍️ Practice Exercise

Question: Compare and contrast Clomipramine and Imipramine in terms of their primary therapeutic uses, noting any specific conditions for which one might be preferred over the other. Also, discuss the common side effects associated with both drugs.

View Solution

Comparison of Clomipramine and Imipramine:

  • Primary Therapeutic Uses:
    • Clomipramine:
      • Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD): Considered a first-line tricyclic antidepressant specifically for OCD, often more effective than other TCAs and SSRIs for this condition.
      • Panic and Phobic Disorders: Also used for panic disorder and various phobic disorders.
    • Imipramine:
      • Major Depressive Disorder: Primarily used for treating symptoms of depression, improving mood, sleep, and appetite in adults.
      • Nocturnal Enuresis in Children: Specifically indicated for managing bed-wetting in children aged 6 and older, leveraging its anticholinergic effects and impact on sleep cycles.
  • Preference in Specific Conditions:
    • Clomipramine is preferred for OCD due to its relatively greater serotonergic activity compared to other TCAs, making it more effective in managing obsessive and compulsive behaviors.
    • Imipramine is preferred for childhood enuresis due to established efficacy and clinical experience in treating bed-wetting, as well as for general depression management, though less specialized than Clomipramine for OCD.
  • Common Side Effects:
    • Shared Tricyclic Antidepressant Side Effects: Both Clomipramine and Imipramine share typical side effects associated with tricyclic antidepressants, including:
      • Anticholinergic Effects: Dry mouth, constipation, blurred vision, urinary retention.
      • Cardiovascular Effects: Orthostatic hypotension, dizziness, tachycardia, potential for arrhythmias.
      • Central Nervous System Effects: Drowsiness, sedation, dizziness, cognitive impairment.
      • Other: Weight gain, sexual dysfunction.
    • Intensity and Specificity: While both share similar categories of side effects, the intensity and specific manifestation can vary between individuals and may be somewhat drug-dependent. For example, Clomipramine might have a slightly higher propensity for serotonergic side effects due to its greater serotonin reuptake inhibition.

📚 Key Takeaways

  • Tricyclic antidepressants (TCAs) like Clomipramine and Imipramine increase norepinephrine and serotonin levels by inhibiting their reuptake.
  • Clomipramine is particularly effective for OCD, while Imipramine is used for depression and childhood enuresis.
  • Side effects of TCAs include anticholinergic effects, cardiovascular issues, and CNS depression. Contraindications include MAOI use and hypersensitivity.
  • Nursing considerations involve monitoring for side effects, especially cardiovascular and anticholinergic effects, and educating patients about gradual dose changes and potential risks.
