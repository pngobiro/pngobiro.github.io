üìö 6) Penicillin G Sodium ‚Äúfor injection‚Äù : [Benzylpeniollin Na]

Penicillin G Sodium is a crystalline form of penicillin G intended for injection, known for its rapid onset of action. Key features include:

  • Trade names: Crystapen, crystalline penicillin.
  • Note: Differentiated from Penicillin G potassium ‚Äúoral‚Äù + Injection [Benzylpenicillin k], which has different formulations and uses.
  • Trade name for potassium form: Megacillin.
  • Class: Antibiotic, penicillin group.
  • N.B.: Penicillin G, due to its low cost, remains a first-line choice for treating many infections when susceptible organisms are involved. Its rapid onset of action makes it particularly suitable for fulminating (sudden and severe) infections. However, it is destroyed by acid and penicillinase, limiting its oral use and effectiveness against penicillinase-producing bacteria.

üìö Advantages, Disadvantages, and Uses

Advantages:

  • Low cost, making it accessible for many treatments.
  • Rapid onset of action, crucial for severe infections.

Disadvantages:

  • Destroyed by acid, limiting oral administration efficacy.
  • Susceptible to penicillinase, ineffective against penicillinase-producing bacteria.

Uses:

  • Effective for infections caused by penicillin-sensitive organisms.

üìö Side Effects, Contraindications, and Dosage Forms

Side Effects:

  • Rapid IV administration can cause hyperkalemia and cardiac arrhythmias, especially in patients with renal impairment due to rapid potassium influx.
  • Thrombophlebitis is a risk with IV administration, requiring careful monitoring of the injection site.

Contraindications:

  • Hypersensitivity to penicillin or any penicillin formulation.

Dosage Forms:

  • Penicillin G potassium (injection) vials: 1 MU (million units).
  • Penicillin G sodium vials: 1 MU, 5 MU, 10 MU.

Dosage:

  • I.M. or continuous I.V. infusion: 300,000 ‚Äì 30 million units per day, depending on the infection severity and patient condition.
  • Pediatric: 100,000 ‚Äì 250,000 units/kg daily in divided doses for injection.
  • Oral solutions/Tablets (Penicillin V, not G): Adult oral dosage is 200,000 ‚Äì 500,000 units every 6-8 hours. Pediatric oral dosage is 25,000 ‚Äì 90,000 units/kg daily in divided doses.

    Note: 250 mg of oral penicillin solution is roughly equivalent to 400,000 units of injection solution. Oral formulations are typically Penicillin V (phenoxymethylpenicillin), which is more acid-stable and better absorbed orally than Penicillin G.

üìö Nursing Considerations for Penicillin G Sodium

Administering Penicillin G Sodium and Potassium safely and effectively requires specific nursing considerations:

  • Administration:
    • Preferred Route: Intramuscular (IM) injection is generally preferred to minimize discomfort. When administering IM, use solutions with concentrations up to 100,000 units/mL to reduce pain at the injection site.
    • Slow IV Infusion: For IV administration, infuse slowly, especially with high doses, to prevent rapid electrolyte shifts and cardiac arrhythmias. Continuous IV infusion is often used for high-dose therapy.
    • Dilution for IV: For IV use, dilute Penicillin G sodium in compatible solutions like sodium chloride or dextrose. Avoid using dextrose saline as diluent unless compatibility is confirmed.
  • Monitoring:
    • Monitor Intake and Output (I&O): Essential to assess renal function and fluid balance, especially in high-dose therapy.
    • Monitor for Hyperkalemia: Rapid IV administration can lead to hyperkalemia, especially with Penicillin G potassium. Monitor serum potassium levels, particularly in patients with renal impairment.
    • Assess for Thrombophlebitis: Regularly assess the IV site for signs of thrombophlebitis (pain, redness, swelling) during and after IV infusion.
    • Monitor for Cardiac Arrhythmias: Especially during rapid IV infusion, monitor cardiac rhythm for arrhythmias.
    • Observe for Allergic Reactions: Observe closely for any signs of allergic reactions (rash, urticaria, wheezing, anaphylaxis) after administration.
  • Storage and Preparation:
    • Storage: Reconstituted solutions can be stored at room temperature for 24 hours or in the refrigerator for up to 1 week, depending on the formulation and manufacturer guidelines.
    • Dilution: Use 1% - 2% lidocaine (without epinephrine) as a diluent for IM injections to decrease pain at the injection site, if not contraindicated.
    • Mixing Precautions: Note that Penicillin G should not be mixed with aminoglycosides in the same IV solution as they are physically and chemically incompatible.

    Penicillin G potassium and Penicillin G sodium contain significant amounts of potassium and sodium, respectively. Consider the electrolyte load, especially in patients with electrolyte imbalances or renal/cardiac conditions. Penicillin G potassium is often avoided in patients with hyperkalemia, and Penicillin G sodium is used cautiously in patients who need to restrict sodium intake.

Question: Compare and contrast Penicillin G Sodium and Penicillin G Potassium, focusing on their clinical uses and specific nursing considerations. Why might one be preferred over the other in certain patients?

View Solution

Comparison of Penicillin G Sodium and Penicillin G Potassium:

  • Clinical Uses: Both Penicillin G Sodium and Penicillin G Potassium are used for treating infections caused by penicillin-sensitive organisms. Their effectiveness is similar against susceptible bacteria. The choice between them is often dictated by patient-specific electrolyte considerations rather than differences in antibacterial activity.
  • Electrolyte Content:
    • Penicillin G Sodium: Contains a significant amount of sodium. This can be a concern for patients who need to restrict sodium intake, such as those with heart failure, hypertension, or renal disease.
    • Penicillin G Potassium: Contains a significant amount of potassium. This can be problematic for patients with hyperkalemia or renal impairment who are at risk of potassium overload, especially with rapid IV administration or high doses.
  • Nursing Considerations:
    • Administration: Both can be given IM or IV. IV administration should be slow for both to minimize risks.
    • Monitoring: For both, monitor for allergic reactions, thrombophlebitis at the IV site, and electrolyte imbalances (sodium with Penicillin G Sodium, potassium with Penicillin G Potassium).
    • Specific Use Case:
      • Penicillin G Potassium Preference: May be preferred in patients where sodium load is a concern and potassium levels are normal.
      • Penicillin G Sodium Preference: May be considered an alternative to Penicillin G potassium in patients with hyperkalemia or conditions exacerbated by potassium, provided sodium intake is not a primary concern.

üìö Key Takeaways

  • Penicillin G Sodium and Potassium are used for penicillin-sensitive infections, with Penicillin G Sodium being preferred for injection due to rapid action.
  • Rapid IV administration of Penicillin G can cause hyperkalemia and arrhythmias; thrombophlebitis is a risk with IV use.
  • Contraindications include penicillin hypersensitivity; use Penicillin G potassium cautiously in hyperkalemia and Penicillin G sodium cautiously in sodium restriction.
  • Nursing care involves monitoring electrolytes, cardiac rhythm, infusion sites, and observing for allergic reactions. IM route is preferred for less discomfort.
