Biotransformation, also known as metabolism, is the process by which the body chemically alters drugs. This process typically inactivates drugs and converts them into more soluble compounds or metabolites, facilitating their excretion from the body. Key aspects of drug metabolism include:

  • Purpose: To detoxify and prepare drugs for elimination, usually by making them more water-soluble.
  • Primary Site: The liver is the primary site of drug metabolism due to its high concentration of metabolic enzymes.
  • Other Sites: Other tissues, including plasma, kidneys, lungs, and the intestinal mucosa, also contribute to drug metabolism, although to a lesser extent than the liver.
  • Microsomal Enzymes: Chemical alterations are primarily produced by the microsomal enzyme system, which is located largely in the liver. These enzymes, particularly cytochrome P450 enzymes, are crucial for many drug metabolism reactions.
  • Outcome: Metabolism typically converts drugs into more polar and water-soluble forms, which are easier to excrete via the kidneys and bile.

üìö Processes of Drug Metabolism

Drug metabolism occurs through several types of reactions:

  1. Conjugation reaction: This involves the union of a polar group of a drug with another substance in the body. The conjugated molecule becomes more polar and water-soluble, making it more easily excretable.
  2. Oxidation-reduction reaction: These reactions involve the addition or removal of oxygen or electrons, often altering the drug's pharmacological activity.
  3. Hydrolysis: This process involves breaking down a drug molecule by adding water, which can also lead to inactivation or altered activity.

üí° Pro Tip

These metabolic responses generally result in a loss of pharmacological activity and are sometimes referred to as DETOXIFICATION reactions.

üìö Factors Affecting Drug Metabolism

Prolonged drug metabolism may be expected in certain conditions:

  • Liver disease: Impaired liver function reduces the capacity for drug metabolism, leading to prolonged drug effects and potential toxicity.
  • Renal problems: Kidney dysfunction can affect the excretion of drug metabolites, indirectly impacting metabolism as the body attempts to compensate.
  • Severe cardiovascular dysfunction: Reduced blood flow to metabolic organs, like the liver, can slow down drug metabolism.
  • Infants with immature metabolizing systems: Neonates and infants have underdeveloped liver enzyme systems, resulting in slower drug metabolism.
  • Aged individuals with degenerative enzyme function: Elderly patients often have reduced liver function and enzyme activity, which can prolong drug metabolism.

üí° Pro Tip

Cumulative drug effects may occur when drug metabolism is delayed, leading to excessive or prolonged responses even to ordinary doses of a drug.

Question: Explain why liver disease can significantly affect drug metabolism. What are the potential consequences for drug therapy in patients with liver impairment?

View Solution

Liver disease significantly impairs drug metabolism because the liver is the primary site for this process, housing the microsomal enzyme system responsible for biotransformation. When liver function is compromised, the liver's capacity to metabolize drugs is reduced.

Consequences for drug therapy in patients with liver impairment include:

  • Prolonged drug half-life: Drugs may remain in the body longer, leading to extended pharmacological effects.
  • Increased drug levels: Reduced metabolism can cause drug concentrations to build up in the body, increasing the risk of toxicity.
  • Exaggerated drug response: Even standard doses may produce stronger-than-expected effects.
  • Adverse drug reactions: The likelihood of side effects and toxic reactions is heightened due to prolonged exposure and elevated drug levels.

Therefore, dosage adjustments and careful monitoring are crucial when administering drugs to patients with liver disease.

üìö Key Takeaways

  • Drug metabolism (biotransformation) is the process of chemically inactivating drugs, primarily in the liver.
  • Microsomal enzymes in the liver are key to metabolism, converting drugs into more water-soluble metabolites for excretion.
  • Main metabolic reactions include conjugation, oxidation-reduction, and hydrolysis.
  • Factors like liver disease, age, cardiovascular function, and renal problems can significantly affect drug metabolism, potentially leading to drug accumulation and toxicity.
