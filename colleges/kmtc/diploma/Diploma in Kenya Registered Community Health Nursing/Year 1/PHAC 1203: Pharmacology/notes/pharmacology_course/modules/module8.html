Topical Route, Drug Names, and Mechanisms

üéØ Learning Objectives

  • Describe the topical route of drug administration and its intended use.
  • Differentiate between ointments and creams in topical preparations.
  • Explain the difference between generic and trade names of drugs.
  • Introduce the major mechanisms by which drugs produce their effects.

üìö E. Topical Route

The topical route involves applying drugs directly to the body surface, such as skin or mucous membranes, for local effects. These preparations are intended for use at the site of administration. However, some exceptions exist, such as nitroglycerin ointment, which is applied to the skin but achieves a systemic effect.

Topical Preparations: Ointments vs. Creams

Two common types of topical preparations are often confused:

  1. Ointments:
    • Usually made with a petrolatum (petroleum jelly) base, which is oily and occlusive.
    • Quite greasy, designed for prolonged contact with the skin to provide a barrier and enhance drug penetration.
    • Do not disappear or penetrate deeply into the skin; they remain on the surface, providing a protective layer.
  2. Creams:
    • Made of viscous water-soluble chemicals, making them lighter and less greasy than ointments.
    • Typically disappear after being applied to the skin as they are absorbed.
    • Preferred by many patients over ointments because creams do not discolor clothing and are less greasy.

Examples of Topical Applications:

  • Lotions: Liquid suspensions or dispersions used for external applications (e.g., calamine lotion for soothing skin irritations).
  • Creams: Aqueous and oily emulsions to soothe skin conditions and deliver medication.
  • Ointments: Semisolid preparations with a petrolatum or lanolin base, used for their soothing or bacteriostatic effects. Ophthalmic ointments are sterile and used for eye conditions.
  • Pastes: Ointment-like preparations suited only for external application (e.g., zinc oxide paste for skin protection).
  • Foams and aerosols: Powders or solutions for spraying on the skin, used as topical anesthetics, for soothing, protection, or inhalation for bronchodilation.
  • Drops: Aqueous solutions used to anesthetize, soothe, or medicate eyes, ears, or nose.
  • Installations: Aqueous solutions instilled into body cavities or wounds, allowed to remain in contact with tissue to medicate or cleanse.

üìö Naming of Drugs: Generic vs. Trade Names

Drugs are identified by two types of names:

  1. Generic name (chemical name):
    • This is the official, nonproprietary name of a drug, often derived from its chemical structure.
    • Defines the chemical structure of the drug and is universally recognized, regardless of the manufacturer.
    • Example: Acetylsalicylic acid is the generic name for aspirin.
  2. Trade name (brand name):
    • This is the proprietary name given by a specific pharmaceutical company that manufactures and markets the drug.
    • It is usually simpler and easier to remember than the generic name.
    • A drug can have multiple trade names depending on the companies marketing it, but it only has one generic name.
    • Example: Aspirin is a common trade name for acetylsalicylic acid, but other trade names exist (e.g., Bayer Aspirin).

üìö How Do Drugs Work? Basic Mechanisms

Despite extensive research, the exact mechanisms by which some drugs produce their effects are not fully understood. However, several major theories and mechanisms describe how drugs interact with the body to elicit responses:

  1. The Receptor Theory:
    • Cells in certain tissues contain receptors, which are specific protein structures.
    • These receptors normally interact with endogenous substances (e.g., hormones, neurotransmitters) produced naturally by the body.
    • Drugs can mimic or block the action of these endogenous substances by binding to these receptors.
    • Drugs can act as agonists, stimulating receptors to produce a response similar to the body's natural substances, or as antagonists (blockers), occupying receptors and preventing natural substances from eliciting a response.

      • üí° Pro Tip

      Example: Acetylcholine contracts muscle fibers by interacting with its receptors. Atropine blocks acetylcholine receptors, preventing muscle contraction (antagonist effect).

  2. Antimetabolites:
    • These drugs closely resemble substances used by cells for nutrition (e.g., vitamins, amino acids).
    • When absorbed, cells mistakenly use antimetabolites instead of nutrients, disrupting normal cellular functions, such as multiplication.
    • Example: Sulfonamides are antimetabolites that bacteria mistakenly use, which then inhibit bacterial multiplication.
  3. Enzyme Inhibitors:
    • Enzymes are biological catalysts that speed up chemical reactions in the body.
    • Some drugs work by inhibiting specific enzymes, thereby altering enzyme-regulated processes.
    • Example: Diuretics inhibit enzymes necessary for salt and water reabsorption in the kidneys, leading to increased urine output.
  4. Action on Cell Membranes:
    • Cell membrane function, especially in nerve and muscle cells, depends on the movement of ions (e.g., sodium, potassium, calcium) across the membrane.
    • Certain drugs can interfere with ion movement across cell membranes, altering nerve and muscle cell activity.
    • Example: Local anesthetics block sodium channels in nerve membranes, preventing nerve impulse transmission and thus providing anesthesia.
  5. Cytotoxic Effect:
    • Cytotoxic drugs are used to kill bacteria or malignant cells without causing undue harm to the patient's normal cells.
    • These drugs target essential cellular processes more critical to rapidly dividing cells (like bacteria or cancer cells) than to normal cells, leading to selective toxicity.

‚úçÔ∏è Practice Exercise

Question: Explain the receptor theory of drug action in simple terms. Give an example of a drug that acts as an agonist and another that acts as an antagonist.

View Solution

Receptor Theory Explained: Imagine receptors as locks on the surface of cells, and drugs as keys. According to the receptor theory, drugs work by fitting into these "locks" (receptors). When a drug "key" fits into a receptor "lock," it can either activate the receptor to produce a cell response (like turning a key to open a door - agonist effect) or block the receptor to prevent a natural substance from activating it (like inserting a wrong key that blocks the lock - antagonist effect).

Example of an Agonist: Albuterol, used for asthma, acts as an agonist at beta-2 adrenergic receptors in the lungs. It "turns on" these receptors to relax bronchial muscles, opening up airways and easing breathing, similar to how adrenaline (a natural agonist) would.

Example of an Antagonist: Propranolol, a beta-blocker, acts as an antagonist at beta-1 adrenergic receptors in the heart. It "blocks" these receptors, preventing adrenaline and noradrenaline from increasing heart rate and force. This helps to slow down the heart rate and lower blood pressure, which is useful in conditions like hypertension or angina.

üìö Key Takeaways

  • Topical drug administration is for local effects on the body surface, with ointments being greasy and protective, and creams being lighter and absorbable.
  • Drugs have generic names (chemical names) and trade names (brand names given by manufacturers).
  • Drugs work through various mechanisms, including receptor interaction (agonism/antagonism), acting as antimetabolites, inhibiting enzymes, affecting cell membranes, and exerting cytotoxic effects.
  • The receptor theory explains drug action as a "key-lock" mechanism, where drugs bind to receptors to stimulate or block cellular responses.
