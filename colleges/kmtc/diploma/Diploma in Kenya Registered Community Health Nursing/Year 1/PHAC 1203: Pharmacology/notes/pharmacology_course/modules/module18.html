üìö 1) Ampicillin

Ampicillin is a broad-spectrum penicillin antibiotic, effective against a range of bacteria. Key features of ampicillin include:

  • Trade name: Penbritin.
  • Class: Antibiotic, penicillin group.
  • Notes:
    • Susceptible to breakdown by penicillinase (beta-lactamase) enzymes produced by some bacteria.
    • Absorption: 30-60% absorbed from the gastrointestinal tract (GIT) after oral administration.
    • Acid Stability: Stable in acidic conditions, allowing for oral administration.
    • Spectrum: Broad-spectrum antibiotic, effective against a wider range of bacteria than narrow-spectrum penicillins.
  • Uses: Particularly useful for:
    • Respiratory tract infections
    • Urinary tract infections (UTIs)
    • Gastrointestinal (GI) tract infections
    • Other infections caused by ampicillin-sensitive organisms.
  • Dosage forms: Available in various forms:
    • Capsules: 250 mg
    • Suspension: 125 mg/5ml
    • Vials for injection: 250 mg, 500 mg, 1000 mg
    • Ampecilin forte capsules: 500 mg
    • Ampecilin forte suspension: 250 mg/5ml
  • Dosage:
    • Adults: 250 mg ‚Äì 500 mg every 6 hours via P.O., I.V., or I.M. routes.
    • Children: 50 mg/kg per day, divided into 4 doses.
    • For bacterial meningitis:
      • Adults: 12 gm daily in divided doses (3 gm every 6 hours).
      • Children: Up to 400 mg/kg daily, divided into doses every 4 hours.
  • Specific nursing considerations:
    • Reconstitution: After reconstitution for I.M. or I.V. use, the solution should be used within one hour to maintain stability.
    • I.V. Administration: Should be administered slowly over 3-5 minutes or via infusion to minimize irritation and adverse effects.

üìö 2) Amoxycillin

Amoxycillin is another broad-spectrum penicillin, closely related to ampicillin, known for its good oral absorption. Key features include:

  • Trade names: moxypen, Moxifarm, amoxitid.
  • Class: Antibiotic, penicillin group.
  • Notes:
    • Inactivated by penicillinase, similar to ampicillin.
    • Acid Stable: Stable in gastric acid, allowing for reliable oral administration.
    • Spectrum: Broad-spectrum antibiotic, effective against a range of gram-positive and gram-negative bacteria.
    • Absorption: Well absorbed from the gastrointestinal tract, achieving higher serum levels compared to ampicillin when given orally.
  • Uses: Effective for treating:
    • Urinary tract infections (UTIs)
    • Respiratory infections
    • Skin and soft tissue infections
    • Gonorrhea
  • Dosage Forms:
    • Capsules: 250 mg
    • Suspension: 125 mg/5 ml
    • Moyxpen forte capsules: 500 mg
    • Moxypen forte suspension: 250 mg/5ml
  • Dosage: 250-500 mg every 8 hours, orally.
  • Specific nursing consideration:
    • Reconstituted suspension is stable for 1 week at room temperature and for 2 weeks when refrigerated at 2-8¬∞C.

üìö 3) Amoxycillin & Potassium Clavulanate

Amoxycillin & Potassium Clavulanate is a combination drug that combines amoxycillin with clavulanic acid, a beta-lactamase inhibitor. This combination enhances amoxycillin's effectiveness against penicillinase-producing bacteria. Key points include:

  • Trade names: Augmentin, Augmin.
  • Class: Antibiotic, penicillin combination.
  • Note: Potassium clavulanate inactivates beta-lactamase enzymes, which are a common mechanism of bacterial resistance to penicillins.
  • Dose: Tablets or suspension, typically 250-500 mg every 8 hours, depending on severity and indication.

üìö 4) Cloxacillin

Cloxacillin is a penicillinase-resistant penicillin, primarily used for infections caused by penicillin-resistant staphylococci. Key features include:

  • Trade name: Orbenin.
  • Class: Antibiotic, penicillin group.
  • N.B.: More resistant to penicillinase (beta-lactamase) than penicillin G and many other penicillins.
  • Uses: Effective against infections caused by penicillinase-producing staphylococci, streptococci, and pneumococci, such as:
    • Osteomyelitis (bone infection)
    • Infected wounds and burns
    • Septic arthritis (joint infection)
    • Pneumonia
  • Dosage forms:
    • Vials for injection: 250 mg, 1g
    • Capsules: 250 mg, 500 mg
    • Syrup: 125 mg/5 ml, 250 mg/5ml
  • Dose:
    • Adults: 250 ‚Äì 500 mg every 6 hours.
    • Children: 50 mg/kg per day, divided into 4 doses.
  • Nursing considerations:
    • Administer on an empty stomach to enhance absorption.

‚úçÔ∏è Practice Exercise

Question: Compare and contrast Ampicillin and Cloxacillin in terms of their spectrum of activity and resistance to penicillinase. In which clinical scenario would Cloxacillin be preferred over Ampicillin?

View Solution

Comparison of Ampicillin and Cloxacillin:

  • Spectrum of Activity:
    • Ampicillin: Broad-spectrum penicillin, effective against a wide range of gram-positive and gram-negative bacteria.
    • Cloxacillin: Narrower spectrum, primarily effective against gram-positive bacteria, particularly penicillinase-producing staphylococci, streptococci, and pneumococci.
  • Penicillinase Resistance:
    • Ampicillin: Susceptible to inactivation by penicillinase (beta-lactamase), limiting its effectiveness against bacteria that produce this enzyme.
    • Cloxacillin: Penicillinase-resistant, making it effective against bacteria that produce penicillinase and are resistant to penicillins like penicillin G and ampicillin.

Clinical Scenario Favoring Cloxacillin over Ampicillin: Cloxacillin would be preferred over ampicillin in cases of suspected or confirmed infections caused by penicillinase-producing staphylococci. For example, in a patient with a skin infection suspected to be caused by Staphylococcus aureus (a common penicillinase producer), cloxacillin would be a better choice than ampicillin because it is more likely to be effective against these resistant bacteria.

üìö Key Takeaways

  • Ampicillin is a broad-spectrum penicillin susceptible to penicillinase, used for respiratory, urinary, and GI infections.
  • Amoxycillin is also broad-spectrum, well-absorbed orally, and used for similar infections as ampicillin, plus gonorrhea.
  • Amoxycillin & Potassium Clavulanate combines amoxycillin with a beta-lactamase inhibitor to overcome bacterial resistance.
  • Cloxacillin is penicillinase-resistant, ideal for treating infections caused by penicillin-resistant staphylococci, including skin, bone, and joint infections.
