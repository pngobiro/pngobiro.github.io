üìö Antihistamine Examples

Let's examine specific examples of H1 antihistamines, Brompheniramine Maleate and Promethazine Hcl, to understand their individual characteristics, uses, and nursing implications:

  1. 1- Brompheniramine Maleate: Ahiston
    • Trade name: Ahiston.
    • Class. : Antihistamine, first-generation H1 blocker.
    • Action: Brompheniramine is an H1 receptor antagonist that competes with histamine for H1-receptor sites on effector cells. It is known for:
      • Antihistaminic Action: Effectively blocks H1 receptors, reducing histamine-mediated symptoms.
      • Mild Sedative Effect: Compared to other first-generation antihistamines, it has a relatively lower incidence of sedation, although drowsiness can still occur.
    • Dose: Available in tablets, typically containing 2 mg per tablet.
      • Adults and children over 12 years: Usual dose is 4 mg every 4-6 hours, or as directed by a physician, not to exceed 24 mg in 24 hours.
      • Children 6-12 years: 2 mg every 4-6 hours, not to exceed 12 mg in 24 hours.
      • Children 2-6 years: Use is generally not recommended without medical advice; if used, dosage is lower and carefully determined by a healthcare provider.
    • Nursing considerations:
      • Sedative Effects: Warn patients about potential drowsiness and advise caution when performing activities requiring alertness, such as driving or operating machinery.
  2. 2- Promethazine Hcl: Phenergan, Prothiazine
    • Trade name: Phenergan, Prothiazine.
    • Class:. Potent antihistamine, first-generation H1 blocker, phenothiazine derivative.
    • Action: Promethazine is a potent H1 receptor antagonist with multiple pharmacological actions:
      • Antihistaminic Action: Effectively blocks H1 receptors, providing relief from allergy symptoms.
      • Antiemetic Effect: Strong antiemetic properties, useful in preventing and treating nausea and vomiting, likely due to its anticholinergic and CNS depressant effects, particularly on the chemoreceptor trigger zone (CTZ).
      • Sedative Action: Significant sedative properties, often used to promote sleep and reduce anxiety. It is more sedating than many other first-generation antihistamines.
    • Uses: Promethazine is versatile, used for:
      • Allergic conditions: Relief of symptoms of perennial and seasonal allergic rhinitis, allergic conjunctivitis, and urticaria.
      • Motion sickness: Effective in preventing and treating motion sickness due to its antihistaminic and anticholinergic properties.
      • Nausea & vomiting due to anesthesia, postoperative nausea and vomiting, and drug-induced nausea.
      • Sedation: As a sedative for preoperative and postoperative sedation, and for nighttime sedation.
    • Forms: Available in various formulations:
      • Syrup: Oral syrup form, often used in children, available in concentrations like 5ml containing 5 mg or 25 mg of promethazine hydrochloride.
      • Tablets: Tablets for oral administration, available in different strengths.
      • Injectable: Formulations for intramuscular or intravenous injection, used in hospital settings for rapid effect.
      • Rectal Suppositories: Useful for patients who cannot take oral medication due to nausea or vomiting.
    • Side effects: Due to its potent anticholinergic and CNS depressant effects, promethazine has several potential side effects:
      • Marked Sedation: Significant drowsiness and sedation are very common, limiting daytime use for many patients.
      • Anticholinergic Effects: Dry mouth, blurred vision, urinary retention, constipation, due to its anticholinergic properties.
      • Cardiovascular: Orthostatic hypotension (dizziness upon standing), tachycardia (less common but possible).
      • Neurological: Dizziness, confusion, especially in elderly patients.
      • Other: Photosensitivity, thickening of bronchial secretions (due to anticholinergic drying effect).

Question: Compare and contrast Brompheniramine Maleate and Promethazine Hcl in terms of their sedative effects and primary clinical uses. Which situations might favor the use of Brompheniramine over Promethazine, and vice versa?

View Solution

Comparison of Brompheniramine Maleate and Promethazine Hcl:

  • Sedative Effects:
    • Brompheniramine Maleate: Has a relatively mild sedative effect compared to other first-generation antihistamines. While it can cause drowsiness, it is generally less sedating than Promethazine.
    • Promethazine Hcl: Known for its potent sedative properties. It is significantly more sedating than Brompheniramine and is often used specifically for its sedative effects, in addition to its antihistaminic and antiemetic actions.
  • Primary Clinical Uses:
    • Brompheniramine Maleate:
      • Allergic Rhinitis and Conjunctivitis: Primarily used for symptomatic relief of seasonal and perennial allergies, hay fever, and allergic conjunctivitis.
      • Mild Allergic Reactions: Suitable for milder allergic reactions where significant sedation is not desired.
      • Daytime Use: Preferred when daytime sedation needs to be minimized, allowing patients to manage allergy symptoms with less drowsiness.
    • Promethazine Hcl:
      • Nausea and Vomiting: Highly effective as an antiemetic, especially for motion sickness, postoperative nausea and vomiting, and drug-induced nausea.
      • Sedation: Used for preoperative and postoperative sedation, and nighttime sedation due to its strong sedative properties.
      • Severe Allergic Reactions: Can be used for allergic conditions, especially when combined sedative and antihistaminic effects are beneficial, although other antihistamines might be preferred for routine allergy symptoms due to less sedation.
  • Situations Favoring Brompheniramine over Promethazine:
    • Daytime Allergy Relief: When treating allergy symptoms during the day where minimizing sedation is important, Brompheniramine is favored due to its milder sedative profile.
    • Patients Needing to Remain Alert: For individuals who need to remain alert and functional, such as during work or school, Brompheniramine is a better choice due to less drowsiness.
  • Situations Favoring Promethazine over Brompheniramine:
    • Nausea and Vomiting: When antiemetic effect is a primary goal, Promethazine is preferred due to its strong antiemetic properties, effective for motion sickness and chemotherapy-induced nausea.
    • Sedation is Desired: When sedation is a therapeutic goal, such as preoperative sedation or nighttime sleep aid, Promethazine is more effective due to its potent sedative action.
    • Severe Allergic Reactions: In severe allergic reactions where combined antihistaminic and sedative effects are beneficial, and when rapid onset and parenteral administration are needed, Promethazine injection can be used.

üìö Key Takeaways

  • Brompheniramine Maleate (Ahiston) is a first-generation antihistamine with mild sedation, used for allergic rhinitis and conjunctivitis, suitable for daytime use.
  • Promethazine Hcl (Phenergan, Prothiazine) is a potent antihistamine with strong sedative and antiemetic effects, used for severe allergies, motion sickness, and sedation.
  • Brompheniramine is preferred when less sedation is desired; Promethazine is favored when sedation and antiemetic effects are beneficial.
  • Nursing considerations for both include advising caution due to drowsiness, especially with Promethazine, and managing anticholinergic side effects.
