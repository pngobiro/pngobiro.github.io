ü´Å COPD Overview

Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD) is a disease characterized by airflow limitation that is not fully reversible. The airflow limitation is usually progressive and associated with an abnormal inflammatory response of the lung to noxious particles or gases, resulting in narrowing of airways, hypersecretion of mucus, and changes in the pulmonary vasculature.

Risk Factors

Cigarette smoking, air pollution, and occupational exposure (coal, cotton, grain) are important risk factors that contribute to COPD development, which may occur over a 20- to 30-year span.

Clinical Manifestations

  • Chronic cough
  • Sputum production
  • Dyspnea on exertion (often worsens over time)
  • Weight loss is common

Complications: Respiratory insufficiency and failure (major complications), pneumonia, atelectasis, and pneumothorax.

Definition

Chronic bronchitis, a disease of the airways, is defined as the presence of cough and sputum production for at least 3 months in each of two consecutive years.

Pathophysiology

  • Smoke or other environmental pollutants irritate the airways, resulting in inflammation and hypersecretion of mucus.
  • Constant irritation causes the mucus-secreting glands and goblet cells to increase in number, leading to increased mucus production.
  • Mucus plugging reduces ciliary function.
  • Bronchial walls become thickened, narrowing the bronchial lumen.
  • Alveoli adjacent to bronchioles may become damaged and fibrosed, altering alveolar macrophage function (increasing susceptibility to infection).

üéà Pulmonary Emphysema

Emphysema describes an abnormal distention of the air spaces beyond the terminal bronchioles and destruction of the walls of the alveoli. This causes an increase in dead space (lung area where no gas exchange can occur) and impaired oxygen diffusion, which leads to hypoxemia.

Pathophysiology

  • Gas Exchange: CO‚ÇÇ elimination is impaired (hypercapnia), leading to respiratory acidosis.
  • Circulation: Pulmonary capillary bed is reduced, increasing resistance to pulmonary blood flow.
  • Cardiac Impact: Right ventricle must maintain higher blood pressure in the pulmonary artery, leading to Cor Pulmonale (right-sided heart failure).

Types of Emphysema

Type Characteristics
Panlobular (Panacinar) Destruction of respiratory bronchiole, alveolar duct, and alveolus. Little inflammatory disease. Hyperinflated chest, marked dyspnea on exertion, weight loss. Expiration becomes active requiring muscular effort.

üè• Management

Medical Management

  • Smoking cessation.
  • Pharmacologic therapy: Bronchodilators, corticosteroids, alpha1-antitrypsin augmentation therapy, antibiotics, mucolytics, antitussives, vasodilators, narcotics.
  • Oxygen therapy (including nighttime).
  • Vaccines.
  • Surgery: Bullectomy, lung volume reduction, lung transplantation.

Nursing Management

‚ñ∂ 1. Achieving Airway Clearance
  • Monitor for dyspnea and hypoxemia.
  • Administer bronchodilators/corticosteroids and monitor for side effects.
  • Eliminate pulmonary irritants (smoking).
  • Instruct in directed/controlled coughing.
  • Chest physiotherapy with postural drainage, increased fluid intake, bland aerosol mists.
‚ñ∂ 2. Improving Breathing Patterns
  • Inspiratory muscle training: Breathing retraining.
  • Diaphragmatic breathing: Reduces respiratory rate, increases alveolar ventilation, helps expel air.
  • Pursed-lip breathing: Slows expiration, prevents small airway collapse, controls rate/depth, promotes relaxation.
‚ñ∂ 3. Improving Activity Tolerance
  • Evaluate activity tolerance and use teaching strategies for ADLs.
  • Exercise training to strengthen muscles and improve endurance.
  • Use of walking aids if appropriate.
  • Consult rehabilitation/occupational/physical therapy.
‚ñ∂ 4. Monitoring & Managing Complications
  • Assess for respiratory insufficiency, failure, infection, atelectasis.
  • Monitor cognitive changes, increasing dyspnea, tachypnea, tachycardia.
  • Monitor pulse oximetry and administer oxygen.
  • Encourage immunization against influenza and Streptococcus pneumoniae.
  • Avoid high pollen, air pollution, and extreme temperatures.
  • Pneumothorax Alert: If rapid onset of shortness of breath occurs, evaluate for pneumothorax (symmetry, breath sounds, pulse ox).
