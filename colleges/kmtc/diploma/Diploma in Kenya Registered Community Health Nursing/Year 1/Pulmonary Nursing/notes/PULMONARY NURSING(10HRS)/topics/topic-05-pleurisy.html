üîç Definition & Etiology

Pleurisy refers to inflammation of both the visceral and parietal pleurae. When inflamed, pleural membranes rub together, the result is severe, sharp, knifelike pain with breathing that is intensified on inspiration.

Common Causes

Pleurisy may develop in conjunction with:

  • Pneumonia or upper respiratory tract infection (URI)
  • Tuberculosis (TB)
  • Collagen diseases
  • Chest trauma
  • Pulmonary infarction or Pulmonary Embolism (PE)
  • Primary or metastatic cancer
  • Thoracotomy

Progression

As pleural fluid develops and accumulates, the pain often lessens, and the friction rub disappears because the fluid separates the inflamed pleural surfaces.

üè• Assessment & Management

Assessment and Diagnostic Methods

  • Auscultation for pleural friction rub
  • Chest x-rays
  • Sputum culture
  • Thoracentesis for pleural fluid examination, pleural biopsy (less common)

Medical Management

Objectives are to discover the underlying condition causing the pleurisy and to relieve the pain.

  • Monitoring: Patient is monitored for signs and symptoms of pleural effusion (shortness of breath, pain, assumption of a position that decreases pain, and decreased chest wall excursion).
  • Analgesics: Prescribed analgesics, such as NSAIDs, are given to relieve pain and allow effective coughing.
  • Comfort Measures: Applications of heat or cold are provided for symptomatic relief.
  • Interventions: An intercostal nerve block is done for severe pain.
