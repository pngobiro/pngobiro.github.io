üîç Overview & Triggers

Asthma is a chronic inflammatory disease of the airways characterized by hyperresponsiveness, mucosal edema, and mucus production. This inflammation ultimately leads to recurrent episodes of asthma symptoms: cough, chest tightness, wheezing, and dyspnea.

Patients may experience symptom-free periods alternating with acute exacerbations. It is the most common chronic disease of childhood but can begin at any age.

Risk Factors & Triggers

  • Allergy: The strongest predisposing factor.
  • Airway Irritants: Pollutants, cold, heat, strong odors, smoke, perfumes.
  • Physical/Emotional: Exercise, stress, emotional upset.
  • Medical Conditions: Rhinosinusitis with postnasal drip, viral respiratory tract infections, Gastroesophageal Reflux (GERD).

Additional Characteristics:

  • Attacks frequently occur at night or early morning.
  • Expiration requires effort and becomes prolonged.
  • As exacerbation progresses, central cyanosis secondary to severe hypoxia may occur.
  • Diaphoresis, tachycardia, and widened pulse pressure.
  • Exercise-induced asthma: Maximal symptoms during exercise, absence of nocturnal symptoms, "choking" sensation.

üíä Management

Assessment & Diagnostics

  • Family, environmental, and occupational history.
  • Pulmonary Function Tests (PFTs): Forced Expiratory Volume (FEV) and Forced Vital Capacity (FVC) are decreased.
  • ABGs: Hypocapnia and respiratory alkalosis are common findings initially.

Pharmacologic Therapy

Medications are categorized into long-acting control and quick-relief medications.

Class Examples
Short-acting Beta2-agonists Salbutamol
Anticholinergics Ipratropium (Atrovent), Atropine
Corticosteroids Prednisone, Hydrocortisone (often MDI)
Others Leukotriene modifiers, Methylxanthines

üë©‚Äç‚öïÔ∏è Nursing Management

The immediate nursing care depends on the severity of symptoms. A calm approach is essential as patients are often frightened.

‚ñ∂ Key Interventions
  • Assessment: Monitor severity of symptoms, breath sounds, peak flow, pulse oximetry, and vital signs.
  • Medication Safety: Obtain allergy history. Identify current meds. Administer prescribed meds and monitor response.
  • Hydration: Administer fluids if dehydrated to loosen secretions.
  • Education: Teach about triggers, inhaler technique, and peak-flow monitoring.

üö® Status Asthmaticus

Medical Emergency

Status Asthmaticus is severe and persistent asthma that does not respond to conventional therapy. It can progress rapidly to asphyxiation. Factors include infection, anxiety, nebulizer abuse, dehydration, and aspirin hypersensitivity.

Pathology & Manifestations

  • Pathology: Decrease in bronchial diameter and Ventilation-Perfusion (V/Q) abnormality.
  • Signs: Severe asthma symptoms. Note: With greater obstruction, wheezing may disappear, signaling impending respiratory failure ("Silent Chest").
  • Diagnostics: Respiratory Alkalosis is the most common ABG finding.

Emergency Management

  • Oxygen: High-flow supplemental oxygen via non-rebreather mask (keep O2 sat >95%).
  • Pharmacology: Beta2-agonists, corticosteroids, Magnesium sulfate (calcium antagonist for smooth muscle relaxation).
  • Hydration: IV fluids (3-4 L/day) to combat dehydration and loosen secretions.
  • Contraindication: Sedatives are contraindicated.
