ü´Å Bronchiectasis

Bronchiectasis is a condition where the bronchial tubes of the lungs are permanently damaged, widened, and thickened. These damaged air passages allow bacteria and mucus to build up and pool in the lungs, resulting in frequent infections and airway blockage.

Causes & Risk Factors

  • Airway Obstruction: Tumor, foreign body.
  • Infection: Complications of long-term pulmonary infections (TB, influenza, measles).
  • Genetic Disorders: Cystic Fibrosis.
  • Host Defense: Abnormal host defense (e.g., ciliary dyskinesia, immunodeficiency).

Clinical Manifestations

  • Chronic cough with copious purulent sputum.
  • Hemoptysis.
  • Clubbing of the fingers.
  • Repeated episodes of pulmonary infection.

Diagnosis

A definite diagnostic clue is a prolonged history of productive cough, with sputum consistently negative for tubercle bacilli. Diagnosis is established via CT scan.

Medical Management

Objectives: Promote bronchial drainage and prevent/control infection.

  • Clearance: Chest physiotherapy (percussion), postural drainage, expectorants, bronchoscopy.
  • Infection Control: Antimicrobial therapy (guided by sputum sensitivity), vaccines (flu/pneumococcal).
  • Pharmacology: Bronchodilators.
  • Lifestyle: Smoking cessation.
  • Surgery: Segmental resection (infrequent).

üë∂ Bronchiolitis

Bronchiolitis is a common lung infection in young children and infants causing inflammation and congestion in the small airways (bronchioles). It is almost always viral and peaks during winter months.

ü¶†

Respiratory Syncytial Virus (RSV)

Most cases are caused by RSV. The infection causes bronchioles to swell and fill with mucus, obstructing airflow.

Clinical Presentation

Initial symptoms (first few days):

  • Runny/stuffy nose
  • Cough
  • Slight fever

Progression:

  • Wheezing (whistling noise on exhalation)
  • Difficulty breathing
  • Possible otitis media (ear infection)

Diagnosis & Treatment

Diagnosis Usually based on observation and auscultation. Viral testing (nasal swab) or Chest X-ray may be used for severe cases.
Treatment Supportive care at home is standard. Antibiotics are ineffective (viral cause). Steroids and chest physiotherapy are not recommended.
Hospitalization Required for severe cases to provide humidified oxygen and IV fluids for dehydration.
Prevention Maintain a smoke-free environment. Smoke aggravates symptoms.
