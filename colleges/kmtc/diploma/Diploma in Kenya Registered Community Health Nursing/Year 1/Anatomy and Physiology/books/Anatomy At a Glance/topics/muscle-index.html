The abdomen

All the muscles of the anterior abdominal wall serve to protect the viscera by their contraction, to produce movement and to increase the intra-abdominal pressure, as in defecation, coughing, parturition, etc. All these muscles are supplied by the lower six thoracic and the first lumbar nerves.

External oblique

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the outer surfaces of the lower eight ribs to the iliac crest as far forward as the anterior superior spine.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: The free lower border forms the inguinal ligament between the anterior superior iliac spine and the pubic tubercle. The muscle becomes aponeurotic and reaches the midline where it interdigitates with the opposite side to form the linea alba. The superficial inguinal ring is a gap in the aponeurosis above and medial to the pubic tubercle. The aponeurosis contributes to the anterior rectus sheath.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Flexion of the spine, side flexion and rotation of the trunk-the right external oblique produces rotation to the left.

Internal oblique

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the thoracolumbar fascia, the iliac crest and the lateral half of the inguinal ligament.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: Into an aponeurosis which is attached to the costal margin and to the linea aspera, after splitting to enclose the rectus abdominis, thus contributing to the rectus sheath. The lower fibres from the inguinal ligament contribute to the conjoint tendon which is attached to the pubic crest and the pectineal line.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Flexion of the spine, side flexion and rotation of the trunk-the right internal oblique produces rotation to the right.

Transversus abdominis

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the thoracolumbar fascia, the iliac crest and the lateral one third of the inguinal ligament. Also from the inner surfaces of the lower six ribs, interdigitating with the diaphragm.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: The aponeurosis passes to the linea aspera, contributing to the rectus sheath. The lower fibres help to form the conjoint tendon.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Most of the fibres are transverse and thus pull in and flatten the abdominal wall.

Rectus abdominis

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the anterior surfaces of the 5th, 6th and 7th costal cartilages.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: The pubic crest and tubercle and the front of the symphysis. There are three tendinous intersections in the upper part of the muscle which are adherent to the anterior rectus sheath.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: The muscle is enclosed in the rectus sheath. A strong flexor of the trunk, it can also tilt the pelvis backwards. In a person lying prone, rectus contracts when the head is lifted from the pillow or when the leg is raised from the bed.

For further important details of the muscles of the anterior abdominal wall, see the inguinal canal and the rectus sheath (p. 30).

The diaphragm

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the inner surfaces of the lower six ribs, from the back of the xiphisternum, from the right and left crura which are attached, respectively, to the upper three and the upper two lumbar vertebrae, and from the medial and lateral arcuate ligaments which bridge over the psoas major and quadratus lumborum.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: The fibres (striated muscle) are inserted into the central tendon.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: The diaphragm is involved in respiration. When the muscle fibres contract, the diaphragm is lowered, thus increasing the vertical dimension of the thorax. In the later stages of contraction, using the liver as a fulcrum it raises the lower ribs, thus increasing the width of the lower thorax. At the same time, it increases the intra-abdominal pressure and is thus used in expulsive efforts-defecation, micturition, parturition, etc.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Phrenic nerve (C3, 4 and 5).

Quadratus lumborum

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the posterior part of the iliac crest.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the 12th rib.
  • ‚Ä¢ Action: Side flexion of the trunk.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Adjacent lumbar nerves.
  • ‚Ä¢ Psoas major: see lower limb (p. 165).

The upper limb

Latissimus dorsi

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the spines of the lower six thoracic vertebrae, the lumbar spines via the thoracolumbar fascia and the medial part of the iliac crest.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the floor of the intertubercular sulcus, curving round teres major.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Adduction and medial rotation of the arm. Can hold up the lower limb girdle as in crutch walking.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Thoracodorsal nerve.

Serratus anterior

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the lateral surfaces of the upper eight ribs.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: Into the medial border of the scapula.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Protraction of the scapula and rotation so that the glenoid points upwards, thus helping in abduction of the upper limb. Helps to keep the scapula in contact with the chest wall.

Nerve supply: Long thoracic nerve.

Levator scapulae

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the transverse processes of the upper cervical vertebrae.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the medial border of the scapula above the spine.
  • ‚Ä¢ Action: Elevates the scapula.

Nerve supply: C3 and 4.

The rhomboids

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From spines of thoracic vertebrae.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the medial border of the scapula.
  • ‚Ä¢ Action: Bracing back the scapula.

Nerve supply: Dorsal scapular nerve.

  • ‚Ä¢ Trapezius: See p. 167.

Pectoralis major

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the sternum and the upper six costal cartilages and from the medial half of the clavicle.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the lateral lip of the intertubercular sulcus.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Adduction, flexion and medial rotation of the arm.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Medial and lateral pectoral nerves.

Pectoralis minor

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the 3rd, 4th and 5th ribs.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the coracoid process.
  • ‚Ä¢ Action: Depresses the tip of the shoulder.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Medial and lateral pectoral nerves.

Deltoid

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the lateral third of the clavicle, the acromion and the spine of the scapula.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the deltoid tubercle on the lateral surface of the humerus.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Abduction of the upper limb (assisted by supraspinatus and serratus anterior), flexion (anterior fibres) and extension (posterior fibres) of the arm.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Axillary nerve.

Teres major

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the lower angle of the scapula.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the medial lip of the intertubercular sulcus, curving round teres major.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Adduction and medial rotation of the arm.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Lower subscapular nerve.

The rotator cuff

Consists of subscapularis, supraspinatus, infraspinatus and teres minor. Acting together, these muscles maintain the stability of the shoulder joint as well as having their own individual actions, as follows:

Subscapularis

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the subscapular fossa.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: Passes in front of the shoulder joint to the lesser tuberosity of the humerus.
  • ‚Ä¢ Action: Medial rotation of the arm.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Subscapular nerves.

Supraspinatus

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the supraspinous fossa.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the top of the greater tuberosity of the humerus.
  • ‚Ä¢ Action: Initiates abduction of the arm.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Suprascapular nerve.

Infraspinatus

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the infraspinous fossa.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the back of the greater tuberosity.
  • ‚Ä¢ Action: Lateral rotation of the arm.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Suprascapular nerve.

Teres minor

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the lateral border of the scapula.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the humerus below infraspinatus.
  • ‚Ä¢ Action: Lateral rotation of the arm.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Axillary nerve.

Coracobrachialis

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the coracoid process along with the short head of biceps.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the medial side of the humerus.
  • ‚Ä¢ Action: Moves the arm upwards and medially.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Musculocutaneous nerve.

Biceps brachii

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: Long head from the supraglenoid tubercle and short head from the coracoid process (with coracobrachialis).
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the radial tuberosity and, via the bicipital aponeurosis, into the deep fascia of the forearm.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Flexion and supination of the forearm.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Musculocutaneous nerve.

Brachialis

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the front of the lower part of the humerus.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the tubercle on the ulna just below the coronoid process.
  • ‚Ä¢ Action: Flexion of the elbow.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Musculocutaneous nerve and radial nerve.

Triceps

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: Three heads: long from the infraglenoid tubercle, lateral from the humerus above the spiral line, and medial from the back of the lower part of the humerus.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: Into the olecranon.
  • ‚Ä¢ Action: Extensor of the elbow.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Radial nerve.

Pronator teres

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the common flexor origin on the medial epicondyle of the humerus.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the lateral surface of the shaft of the radius.
  • ‚Ä¢ Action: Pronation of the forearm.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Median nerve.

Flexor carpi radialis

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the common flexor origin.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the base of metacarpals 2 and 3.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Flexion and abduction of wrist.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Median nerve.

Palmaris longus

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the common flexor origin.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the flexor retinaculum and the palmar aponeurosis.
  • ‚Ä¢ Action: Flexor of the wrist. (Often absent.)
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Median nerve.

Flexor carpi ulnaris

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the common flexor origin and the posterior border of the ulna.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the pisiform and thence by the pisometacarpal ligament to the 5th metacarpal.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Flexion and adduction of the wrist.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Ulnar nerve.

Flexor digitorum superficialis

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the common flexor origin and the shaft of the radius.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the sides of the middle phalanges of the four fingers. The tendons are perforated by the tendons of flexor digitorum profundus.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Flexion of the proximal two phalanges and of the wrist.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Median nerve.

Flexor pollicis longus

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the front of the shaft of the radius.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the distal phalanx of the thumb.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Flexion of all the joints of the thumb.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Median nerve.

Flexor digitorum profundus

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the front of the shaft of the ulna and its posterior border.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the distal phalanges of the four fingers, the tendons passing through those of flexor digitorum superficialis.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Flexion of the fingers and the wrist.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Half by the median nerve and half by the ulnar nerve.

Pronator quadratus

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the lower end of the front of the radius.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the lower end of the ulna.
  • ‚Ä¢ Action: Pronator of the forearm.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Median nerve.

Brachioradialis

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the lateral supracondylar ridge of the humerus.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the lower end of the radius.
  • ‚Ä¢ Action: Flexion of the elbow.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Radial nerve.

Extensor carpi radialis longus and brevis

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the lateral supracondylar ridge of the humerus.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the bases of the 2nd and 3rd metacarpals.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Extension and abduction of the wrist.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Radial nerve.

Extensor digitorum

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the common extensor origin on the lateral epicondyle of the humerus.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the bases of the middle and distal phalanges of the four fingers via the extensor expansion.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Extension of the fingers and of the wrist (but see also the lumbricals and interossei).
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Radial (posterior interosseous) nerve.

Extensor digiti minimi

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the common extensor origin.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the extensor expansion of the little finger.
  • ‚Ä¢ Action: Extension of the little finger.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Radial (posterior interosseous) nerve.

Extensor carpi ulnaris

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the common extensor origin and from the posterior border of the ulna.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the base of the 5th metacarpal.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Extension and adduction of the wrist.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Radial (posterior interosseous) nerve.

Supinator

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the lateral side of the humerus and the ulna.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: It wraps around the radius from behind to be inserted into the upper part of its shaft.
  • ‚Ä¢ Action: Supination.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Radial (posterior interosseous) nerve.

Abductor pollicis longus

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the posterior surfaces of the radius and ulna.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the base of the 1st metacarpal.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Abduction and extension of the thumb.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Radial (posterior interosseous) nerve.

Extensor pollicis brevis

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the back of the radius.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the base of the proximal phalanx of the thumb.
  • ‚Ä¢ Action: Extension of the proximal phalanx of the thumb.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Radial (posterior interosseous) nerve.

Extensor pollicis longus

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the back of the ulna.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: Into the base of the distal phalanx of the thumb.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Extension of all the joints of the thumb.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Radial (posterior interosseous) nerve.

Extensor indicis

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the back of the ulna.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the side of the extensor digitorum tendon to the index finger.
  • ‚Ä¢ Action: Helps to extend the index finger.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Radial (posterior interosseous) nerve.

Abductor pollicis brevis

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the flexor retinaculum and adjacent carpal bones.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the base of the proximal phalanx of the thumb.
  • ‚Ä¢ Action: Abduction of the thumb.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Median nerve.

Flexor pollicis brevis

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the flexor retinaculum and adjacent carpal bones.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the base of the proximal phalanx of the thumb. Its tendon contains a sesamoid bone.
  • ‚Ä¢ Action: Flexion of the proximal phalanx of the thumb.

    • Nerve supply: Median nerve.

Opponens pollicis

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the flexor retinaculum and adjacent carpal bones.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the shaft of the 1st metacarpal.
  • ‚Ä¢ Action: Produces opposition of the thumb.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Median nerve.

Adductor pollicis

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: Oblique head from the bases of metacarpals; transverse head from the shaft of the 3rd metacarpal.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the base of the medial side of the proximal phalanx of the thumb. The tendon contains a sesamoid bone.
  • ‚Ä¢ Action: Adduction of the thumb.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Deep branch of the ulnar nerve.

Abductor digiti minimi

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the pisiform bone.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the base of the proximal phalanx of the little finger.
  • ‚Ä¢ Action: Abduction of the little finger.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Ulnar nerve.

Flexor digiti minimi

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the flexor retinaculum and adjacent carpal bone.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the base of the proximal phalanx of the little finger.
  • ‚Ä¢ Action: Flexes the proximal phalanx of the little finger.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Ulnar nerve.

Opponens digiti minimi

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the flexor retinaculum and adjacent carpal bones.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the shaft of the fifth metacarpal.
  • ‚Ä¢ Action: Opposition of the little finger.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Ulnar nerve.

The lumbrical muscles

  • ‚Ä¢ Origins: The four muscles arise from the sides of the tendons of the flexor digitorum profundus.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertions: To the lateral sides of the dorsal extensor expansions of the extensor digitorum tendon.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Flexion of the metacarpophalangeal joints and extension of the interphalangeal joints.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Medial two muscles by the ulnar nerve and lateral two by the median nerve.

The interossei

  • ‚Ä¢ Origins: Dorsal from adjacent sides of four metacarpals and palmar from one side of each metacarpal.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertions: Into the dorsal extensor expansions and the sides of the proximal phalanges in such a way that the dorsal interossei abduct the fingers and the palmar adduct them (Fig. 39.2).
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Adduction and abduction as above. Both sets of muscles produce flexion of the metacarpophalangeal joints and extension of the interphalangeal joints, as in the 'precision grip'.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: All by the deep branch of the ulnar nerve.

The lower limb

Gluteus maximus

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the posterior part of the gluteal surface of the ilium, the back of the sacrum and its associated ligaments.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the gluteal tuberosity of the femur (25%) and the iliotibial tract (75%).
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Extension and lateral rotation of the thigh.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Inferior gluteal nerve.

Gluteus medius

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the gluteal surface of the ilium.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the greater trochanter.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Abduction and medial rotation of the thigh.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Superior gluteal nerve.

Gluteus minimus

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the gluteal surface of the ilium below gluteus medius.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the greater trochanter.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Abduction and medial rotation of the thigh. The most important action of medius and minimus is to prevent the pelvis tilting to the unsupported side when taking the weight on one leg, as in walking.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Superior gluteal nerve.

Tensor fasciae latae

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the anterior part of the crest of the ilium.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the lateral condyle of the tibia via the iliotibial tract.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Extension of the knee joint. Helps the gluteal muscles to prevent tilting of the pelvis.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Superior gluteal nerve.

Piriformis

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the front of the sacrum.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: Into the greater trochanter via the greater sciatic notch.
  • ‚Ä¢ Action: Lateral rotation of the thigh.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: From the sacral plexus.

Obturator internus

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the inner surface of the hip bone and the obturator membrane.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the greater trochanter via the lesser sciatic notch.
  • ‚Ä¢ Action: Lateral rotation of the thigh.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: From the sacral plexus.

Quadratus femoris

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the outer surface of the ischial tuberosity.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the quadrate tubercle on the intertrochanteric crest of the femur.
  • ‚Ä¢ Action: Lateral rotation of the thigh.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: From the sacral plexus.

Obturator externus

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the obturator membrane and the surrounding bone.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the trochanteric fossa of the femur.
  • ‚Ä¢ Action: Lateral rotation of the thigh.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Obturator nerve.

Iliacus

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the concave inner surface of the ilium.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: Passes under the inguinal ligament to the lesser trochanter, in company with psoas major.
  • ‚Ä¢ Action: Flexion of the thigh.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Femoral nerve.

Psoas major

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the transverse processes and the sides of the bodies and intervertebral discs of the lumbar vertebrae.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: Passes under the inguinal ligament to the lesser trochanter in company with iliacus. (Joint muscle often called iliopsoas.)
  • ‚Ä¢ Action: Flexion of the thigh.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Femoral nerve.

Sartorius

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the anterior superior iliac spine.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the medial side of the upper end of the tibia just in front of gracilis and semitendinosus.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Flexion and abduction of the thigh and flexion of the knee (the 'tailor's position').
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Femoral nerve.

Quadriceps femoris:

Rectus femoris

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the anterior inferior iliac spine (straight head) and the upper lip of the acetabulum (reflected head).

Vastus medialis

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the medial lip of the linea aspera.

Vastus lateralis Origin: From the lateral lip of the linea aspera.

Vastus intermedius

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the lateral and anterior surfaces of the femur.

Insertion of quadriceps: The four parts of quadriceps are inserted into the patella and, from here, to the tubercle of the tibia. The patella is thus a sesamoid bone in the tendon of quadriceps.

  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Extension and stabilization of the knee. Rectus is also a weak flexor of the thigh.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Femoral nerve.

Pectineus

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the superior ramus of the pubis.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the back of the femur between the lesser trochanter and the linea aspera.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Adduction and flexion of the thigh.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Femoral and obturator nerves.

Adductor longus

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the front of the pubis just below the pubic tubercle.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the middle third of the linea aspera.
  • ‚Ä¢ Action: Adduction of the thigh.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Obturator nerve.

Adductor brevis

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the inferior ramus of the pubis.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the upper part of the linea aspera.
  • ‚Ä¢ Action: Adduction of the thigh.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Obturator nerve.

Adductor magnus

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the inferior ramus of the pubis and the ramus of the ischium, back as far as the ischial tuberosity.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the whole length of the linea aspera and to the adductor tubercle of the femur.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Adduction and extension of the thigh (the latter action is carried out by the 'hamstring' part of the muscle which arises from the ischial tuberosity).
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Adductor part by the femoral nerve and hamstring part by the sciatic nerve.

Gracilis

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the inferior ramus of the pubis and the ramus of the ischium.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the medial side of the tibia between sartorius and semitendinosus.
  • ‚Ä¢ Action: Adduction of the thigh.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Obturator nerve.

Biceps femoris

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: Long head from the ischial tuberosity and short head from the linea aspera.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: By a thick tendon into the head of the fibula.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Extension of the hip and flexion of the knee.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Sciatic nerve (both components).

Semitendinosus

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the ischial tuberosity.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the medial side of the front of the tibia, behind sartorius and gracilis.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Extension of the hip and flexion of the knee joint.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Sciatic nerve (tibial component).

Semimembranosus

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the ischial tuberosity.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To a groove on the tibial medial condyle.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Extension of the hip and flexion of the knee.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Sciatic nerve (tibial component).

The lower limb

Tibialis anterior

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the lateral surface of the tibia.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the base of the 1st metatarsal and the medial cuneiform.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Dorsiflexion and inversion of the foot.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Deep peroneal nerve.

Extensor hallucis longus

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the middle third of the shaft of the fibula.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the base of the distal phalanx of the big toe.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Extension of the big toe and dorsiflexion of the foot.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Deep peroneal nerve.

Extensor digitorum longus

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the shaft of the fibula.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the bases of the middle and distal phalanges of the four lateral toes via the dorsal extensor expansions.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Extension of the toes and dorsiflexion of the foot.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Deep peroneal nerve.

Peroneus tertius

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: Formed by the lower part of extensor digitorum longus.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: Into the base of the 5th metatarsal.
  • ‚Ä¢ Action: Dorsiflexion of the foot.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Deep peroneal nerve.

Extensor digitorum brevis

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the upper surface of the calcaneus.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the proximal phalanx of the big toe and to the extensor digitorum longus tendons of the next three toes.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Dorsiflexion of the foot and extension of the toes.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Deep peroneal nerve.

Peroneus longus

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the upper two-thirds of the lateral surface of the shaft of the fibula.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the base of the 1st metatarsal and the medial cuneiform, via the groove on the cuboid.
  • ‚Ä¢ Action: Eversion of the foot.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Superficial peroneal nerve.

Peroneus brevis

The head and neck

The muscles of mastication

Temporalis

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the lateral side of the skull below the temporal line.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the coronoid process of the mandible, extending down the anterior border as far as the third molar tooth. It passes deep to the zygomatic arch.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Closes the mouth and clenches the teeth. The posterior fibres are horizontal and help to retract the mandible when closing the mouth.

Masseter

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the lower border of the zygomatic arch.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the lateral side of the mandible in the region of the angle.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Closes the mouth and clenches the teeth.

Lateral pterygoid

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the lateral pterygoid plate.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the neck of the mandible and the intra-articular disc of the temporomandibular joint.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: It protrudes the mandible and moves the head of the mandible onto the articular eminence when the mouth is opened.

Medial pterygoid

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the lateral pterygoid plate.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the medial surface of the mandible near the angle.
  • ‚Ä¢ Action: Helps to close the mouth.

Nerve supply of the muscles of mastication: The muscles of mastication are all supplied by the mandibular division of the trigeminal nerve.

The muscles of facial expression

The principal muscles of facial expression are shown in Fig. 67.1, from which their actions can be deduced. Only a few of them will be described here.

Occipitofrontalis

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: The frontal belly extends backwards from the forehead region and the occipital belly extends forwards from the occipital bone.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: Both parts are inserted into the galea aponeurotica (epicranial aponeurosis).
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: The frontal belly lifts the eyebrows when looking upwards and both bellies can move the whole scalp on the underlying loose fascia.

Orbicularis oculi

In two parts. The orbital part surrounds the whole eye, blending with the frontal belly of occipitofrontalis. It closes the eye tightly. The palpebral part is in the eyelid and closes the eye gently as in sleep.

Orbicularis oris

Surrounds the whole mouth and blends in with the surrounding muscles that are shown in Fig. 67.1. Closes the mouth and protrudes the lips.

Buccinator

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the pterygomandibular ligament, where it is continuous with the superior constrictor of the pharynx.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: Blends in with the orbicularis oris. Tightens the cheeks and keeps them in contact with the gums, thus preventing food collecting in the vestibule of the mouth.

Platysma

Origin: From the skin over the upper part of the chest, crossing the mandible to blend in with the orbicularis oris. Pulls down the corners of the mouth and has an antisphincteric action on the neck as in loosening a tight collar.

Nerve supply of the muscles of facial expression: All the muscles of facial expression are supplied by the facial nerve.

Trapezius

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the superior nuchal line, the ligamentum nuchae and the spines of all the thoracic vertebrae.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: Into the spine of the scapula, the lateral edge of the acromion and the lateral third of the clavicle.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Extends the head on looking upwards, raises the tips of the shoulders, braces the shoulders back, and helps serratus anterior to rotate the scapula during abduction of the arm.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Spinal accessory nerve.

Sternocleidomastoid

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the front of the manubrium (by a narrow rounded tendon) and the medial third of the clavicle.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the mastoid process and the lateral part of the superior nuchal line.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Rotates the head to the opposite side and flexes the cervical spine to the same side, thus bringing the ear nearer to the shoulder of the same side. Both muscles acting together can flex the cervical spine against resistance. An accessory muscle of respiration.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Spinal accessory nerve.

Scalenus anterior

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From anterior tubercles of the transverse processes of several cervical vertebrae.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: To the scalene tubercle on the medial border of the 1st rib, by means of a narrow pointed tendon.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Flexes the cervical spine, produces lateral flexion to the same side and rotation to the opposite side. An accessory muscle of respiration.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Cervical spinal nerves.

Scalenus medius

  • ‚Ä¢ Origin: From the posterior tubercles of the transverse processes of most of the cervical vertebrae.
  • ‚Ä¢ Insertion: Into a large area on the 1st rib behind scalenus anterior.
  • ‚Ä¢ Actions: Similar to those of scalenus anterior.
  • ‚Ä¢ Nerve supply: Cervical spinal nerves.
