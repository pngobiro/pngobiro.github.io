THE PRIMITIVE STREAK

  • ‚Ä¢It develops at the beginning of $\mathbf{3}^{\text{rd}}$ week.
  • ‚Ä¢Proliferation and migration of cells of the epiblast to the median plane of the embryonic disc.
  • ‚Ä¢Cells leave the deep surface and form a loose network of embryonic connective tissue called MESENCYME OR MESOBLAST OR MESODERM.

origine SOURCES OF MESENCHYME

  • ‚Ä¢Definitions:
  • ‚Ä¢Mesoderm refers to cells derived from the epiblast and extraembryonic tissues.
  • ‚Ä¢Mesenchyme refers to loosely organized embryonic unspecialized cells that are set in a gelatinous ground substance.
  • ‚Ä¢Sources:
  • ‚Ä¢- Mesoderm
  • ‚Ä¢- Primitive streak
  • ‚Ä¢- Somites.
  • ‚Ä¢- The somites differentiate to scleretome, myotome and dermatome.
  • ‚Ä¢- In early stages of differentiation, the somite cells show epithelial characteristics.
  • ‚Ä¢- Later, they loose this characteristic to form a loose jelly-like tissue - MESENCHYME.
  • ‚Ä¢- Mesodermal lateral plates.
  • ‚Ä¢- Neural crest cells especially in the head region

  • ‚Ä¢Mesenchyme consists of:
  • ‚Ä¢- cells

  • ‚Ä¢Ability to differentiate in many different ways

Marfan Syndrome

  • ‚Ä¢An inherited disorder caused by a defective gene for the glycoprotein fibrillin resulting in abnormal development of elastic fibers.
  • ‚Ä¢This causes tissues that contain many elastic fibers to be malformed or weak (including the covering of bone, ligament that suspends the lens of the eye, and the walls of large arteries.
  • ‚Ä¢People with Marfan syndrome are often tall, have long arms, legs, fingers and toes, blurred vision, and weakened aortic walls that may burst.

HISTOGENESIS OF CARTILAGE

  • ‚Ä¢Cartilage develops from mesenchyme.
  • ‚Ä¢First appears in embryos during the $\mathbf{5}^{\text{th}}$ week.
  • ‚Ä¢In areas where cartilage is to develop, the mesenchyme condenses to form chondrification centers.
  • ‚Ä¢The mesenchymal cells differentiate into chondroblasts that secrete collagenous fibrils and the ground substance.
  • ‚Ä¢Collagenous and/or elastic fibers are deposited in the intercellular substance or matrix.
  • ‚Ä¢Three types of cartilage are distinguished according to the type of matrix that is formed:
  • ‚Ä¢- Hyaline cartilage, the most widely distributed type (e.g., in joints)
  • ‚Ä¢- Fibrocartilage (e.g., in intervertebral discs)
  • ‚Ä¢- Elastic cartilage (e.g., in auricle of ear)

HISTOGENESIS OF BONE

  • ‚Ä¢Bone primarily develops in two types of connective tissue, mesenchyme and cartilage, but can also develop in other connective tissues.
  • ‚Ä¢Like cartilage, bone consists of cells and an organic intercellular substance-the bone matrixthat comprises collagen fibrils embedded in an amorphous component.
  • ‚Ä¢Osteogenesis and chondrogenesis are programmed early in development and are independent events under the influence of vascular events.

skeleton BONE FORMATION

  • ‚Ä¢Bone formation is termed osteogenesis or ossification.
  • ‚Ä¢It is a process of transforming mesenchyme to bone.
  • ‚Ä¢Bone first appearance - condensations of mesenchymal cells.
  • ‚Ä¢Condensation marks the beginning of selective gene activity, which precedes cell differentiation.
  • ‚Ä¢Two types of ossification occur.
  • ‚Ä¢- Intramembranous ossification is the formation of bone directly from or within fibrous connective tissue membranes.
  • ‚Ä¢- Endochondrial ossification is the formation of bone from hyaline cartilage models.
  • ‚Ä¢- Intramembranous ossification - no bone model.
  • ‚Ä¢- Endochondrial ossification - first form hyaline cartilage bone model.
  • ‚Ä¢Bone morphogenetic proteins (BMP-5 and BMP-7), the growth factor Gdf5, members of the transforming growth factor $\beta$ (TGF-$\beta$) superfamily, and other signaling molecules have been implicated as endogenous regulators of chondrogenesis and skeletal development.

skeleton INTRAMEMBRANOUS OSSIFICATION

  • ‚Ä¢Mesenchyme forms a membranous sheath, hence, the name intramembranous ossification.
  • ‚Ä¢Also called dermal ossification because it normally occurs in the deeper layers of connective tissue of the dermis of the skin.
  • ‚Ä¢The mesenchyme condenses and becomes highly vascular.
  • ‚Ä¢Some cells differentiate into osteoblasts (bone-forming cells) and begin to deposit unmineralized matrix osteoid.
  • ‚Ä¢Calcium phosphate is then deposited in the osteoid tissue as it is organized into bone.
  • ‚Ä¢Osteoblasts are trapped in the matrix and become osteocytes.
  • ‚Ä¢At first, new bone has no organized pattern.
  • ‚Ä¢Spicules of bone soon become organized and coalesce into lamellae (layers).
  • ‚Ä¢Concentric lamellae develop around blood vessels, forming osteons (haversian systems).
  • ‚Ä¢Some osteoblasts remain at the periphery of the developing bone and continue to lay down lamellae, forming plates of compact bone on the surfaces.
  • ‚Ä¢Between the surface plates, the intervening bone remains spiculated or spongy.
  • ‚Ä¢Osteoclasts are multinucleated cells with a hematopoietic origin.
  • ‚Ä¢In the interstices of spongy bone, the mesenchyme differentiates into bone marrow.
  • ‚Ä¢During fetal and postnatal life, there is continuous remodeling of bone by the coordinated action of osteoclasts and osteoblasts.

skeleton ENDOCHONDRAL OSSIFICATION

  • ‚Ä¢Endochondral ossification is formation of bone from preexisting cartilaginous models.
  • ‚Ä¢In a long bone, for example, the primary center of ossification appears in the diaphysis - the part of a long bone between its ends - that forms the shaft of the bone.
  • ‚Ä¢At the center of ossification, chondrocytes increase in size (hypertrophy), the matrix becomes calcified, and the cells die.
  • ‚Ä¢Concurrently, a thin layer of bone is deposited under the perichondrium surrounding the diaphysis; thus, the perichondrium becomes the periosteum.
  • ‚Ä¢Invasion by vascular connective tissue from blood vessels surrounding the periosteum also breaks up the cartilage.
  • ‚Ä¢Some invading cells differentiate into hemopoietic cells, blood cells, of the bone marrow.
  • ‚Ä¢Transformation of cartilage model to bone proceeds towards the epiphyses (ends of the bone).
  • ‚Ä¢The spicules of bone are remodeled by the action of osteoclasts and osteoblasts.

Growth at epiphyseal plates

growth GROWTH IN THICKNESS

  • ‚Ä¢Bone grow in thickness or diameter by appositional growth.
  • ‚Ä¢The steps in these process are:
  • ‚Ä¢- Periosteal cells differentiate into osteoblasts which secrete collagen fibers and organic molecules to form the matrix.
  • ‚Ä¢- Ridges fuse and the periosteum becomes the endosteum.
  • ‚Ä¢- New concentric lamellae are formed.
  • ‚Ä¢- Osetoblasts under the periosteum form new circumferential lamellae.

Bone Growth in Width

  • ‚Ä¢By appositional growth at the bone's surface
  • ‚Ä¢Periosteal cells differentiate into osteoblasts and form bony ridges and then a tunnel around periosteal blood vessel.
  • ‚Ä¢Concentric lamellae fill in the tunnel to form an osteon.

growth Long bone lengthening

  • ‚Ä¢Lengthening of long bones occurs at the diaphysial-epiphysial junction.
  • ‚Ä¢Cartilage cells in the diaphysial-epiphyseal region proliferate by mitosis.
  • ‚Ä¢Toward the diaphysis, the cartilage cells hypertrophy and the matrix becomes calcified.
  • ‚Ä¢Spicules are isolated from each other by vascular invasion from the medullary (marrow) cavity.
  • ‚Ä¢Bone is deposited on these spicules by osteoblasts; resorption of this bone keeps the spongy bone masses relatively constant in length and enlarges the medullary cavity.
  • ‚Ä¢Ossification of limb bones begins at the end of the embryonic period and thereafter makes demands on the maternal supply of calcium and phosphorus.
  • ‚Ä¢Pregnant women are therefore advised to maintain an adequate intake of these elements to preserve healthy bones and teeth.
  • ‚Ä¢At birth, the diaphyses are largely ossified, but most of the epiphyses are still cartilaginous.
  • ‚Ä¢Secondary ossification centers appear in the epiphyses in most bones during the first few years after birth.
  • ‚Ä¢Ossification spreads radially, and only the articular cartilage and a transverse plate of cartilage, the epiphysial cartilage plate, remain cartilaginous.
  • ‚Ä¢Upon completion of growth, this plate is replaced by spongy bone; the epiphyses and diaphysis are united, and no further elongation of the bone occurs.
  • ‚Ä¢In most bones, the epiphyses have fused with the diaphysis by the age of 20 years.
  • ‚Ä¢The rate of deposition and resorption is balanced to regulate the thickness of the compact bone and the size of the medullary cavity.
  • ‚Ä¢The internal reorganization of bone continues throughout life.
  • ‚Ä¢In addition to membranous and endochondral ossification, chondroid tissue, which also differentiates from mesenchyme, is now recognized as an important factor for skeletal growth.

RICKETS

  • ‚Ä¢Rickets is a disease that occurs in children who have a vitamin D deficiency.
  • ‚Ä¢This vitamin is required for calcium absorption by the intestine.
  • ‚Ä¢The resulting calcium deficiency causes disturbances of ossification of the epiphysial cartilage plates (i.e., they are not adequately mineralized), and there is disorientation of cells at the metaphysis.
  • ‚Ä¢The limbs are shortened and deformed, with severe bowing of the limb bones.

joint DEVELOPMENT OF JOINTS

  • ‚Ä¢Joints begin to develop with the appearance of the interzonal mesenchyme during the $\mathbf{6}^{\text{th}}$ week, and by the end of the $\mathbf{8}^{\text{th}}$ week, they resemble adult joints.
  • ‚Ä¢Joints are classified as fibrous joints, cartilaginous joints, and synovial joints.
  • ‚Ä¢Joints with little or no movement are classified according to the type of material holding the bones together, for example, the bones involved in fibrous joints are joined by fibrous tissue.

FIBROUS JOINTS

  • ‚Ä¢During the development of fibrous joints, the interzonal mesenchyme between the developing bones differentiates into dense fibrous tissue, for example, the sutures of the cranium are fibrous joints.

CARTILAGINOUS JOINTS

  • ‚Ä¢During the development of cartilaginous joints, the interzonal mesenchyme between the developing bones differentiates into hyaline cartilage (e.g., the costochondral joints) or fibrocartilage (e.g., the pubic symphysis).

SYNOVIAL JOINTS

  • ‚Ä¢During the development of synovial joints (e.g., the knee joint), the interzonal mesenchyme between the developing bones differentiates as follows:
  • ‚Ä¢- Peripherally it forms the capsular and other ligaments.
  • ‚Ä¢- Centrally it disappears, and the resulting space becomes the joint cavity or synovial cavity.
  • ‚Ä¢- Where it lines the joint capsule and articular surfaces, it forms the synovial membrane (which secretes synovial fluid), a part of the joint capsule (fibrous capsule lined with synovial membrane)
  • ‚Ä¢An abnormal intrauterine environment restricting embryonic and fetal movements may interfere with limb development and cause joint fixation.
