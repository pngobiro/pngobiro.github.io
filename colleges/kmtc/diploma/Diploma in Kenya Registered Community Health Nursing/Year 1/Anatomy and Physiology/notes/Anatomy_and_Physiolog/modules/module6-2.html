Module 6: Sensory Functions, Nervous Tissue, Neuroglia

üéØ Learning Objectives

  • Describe the sensory functions of the nervous system, including somatic and visceral sensory divisions.
  • Describe the structure and function of neurons.
  • Classify neurons based on their structure and function.
  • Describe the structure and function of neuroglia.
  • Explain myelination and the regeneration of nervous tissue.
  • Differentiate between local potentials and action potentials.

üìö Sensory Functions

Sensory input gathered by the sensory (afferent) division of the PNS is further divided into the somatic and visceral sensory divisions.

Somatic sensory division: Consists of neurons that carry signals from skeletal muscles, bones, joints, and skin. It also includes special sensory signals from the organs of vision, hearing, taste, smell, and balance (sometimes called the special sensory division).

Visceral sensory division: Consists of neurons that transmit signals from viscera (organs) such as the heart, lungs, stomach, kidneys, and urinary bladder.

Sensory input from both divisions is carried from sensory receptors to the spinal cord and/or brain by spinal and cranial nerves.

Integrative functions: The nervous system analyzes and interprets incoming sensory information and determines an appropriate response.

Motor functions: Actions performed in response to the integrative functions. Motor output is performed by the motor (efferent) division of the PNS, which is further subdivided into the somatic and autonomic divisions.

  • Voluntary/Skeletal Muscle
  • Involuntary (Reflexes)

Organs that carry out the effects of the nervous system are called effectors.

üìù Summary

  • The somatic sensory division carries signals from the body's periphery and special senses.
  • The visceral sensory division carries signals from internal organs.
  • The nervous system integrates sensory information and produces a motor response.
  • Effectors carry out the response.

üìö Nervous Tissue

Neurons are excitable cells responsible for sending and receiving signals in the form of action potentials.

Cell body (soma): The most metabolically active region of the neuron. It manufactures all the proteins needed for the neuron and contains many organelles.

Dendrites: Short, branched processes that receive input from other neurons, which they transmit to the cell body in the form of electrical impulses.

Axon: Each neuron has only one axon (nerve fiber) that can generate and conduct action potentials. The region where the axon originates from the cell body is the axon hillock. The axon may split into multiple branches called telodendria, which end in axon terminals. Axon terminals contain synaptic bulbs.

The neuron has three main functional regions: receptive (dendrites and cell body), conducting (axon), and secretory (axon terminal).

Neurons can be classified based on their structure:

  • Multipolar: Single axon, multiple dendrites (99% of neurons in the brain and spinal cord).
  • Bipolar: One axon, one dendrite, with a cell body between them (found in the eye, ear, and olfactory epithelium).
  • Pseudounipolar: One fused axon that extends from the cell body and divides into two processes. These are sensory neurons that carry information about pain, touch, and pressure.

Neurons can also be classified based on their function:

  • Sensory (afferent) neurons: Carry information into the CNS.
  • Interneurons: Relay information within the CNS between sensory and motor neurons.
  • Motor (efferent) neurons: Carry information away from the CNS to muscles and glands.

üìù Summary

  • Neurons are excitable cells that transmit signals.
  • The cell body (soma) is the metabolic center.
  • Dendrites receive input.
  • The axon conducts action potentials.
  • Neurons can be multipolar, bipolar, or pseudounipolar.
  • Sensory neurons carry information to the CNS.
  • Interneurons relay information within the CNS.
  • Motor neurons carry information away from the CNS.

üìö Neuroglia

Neuroglia provide structural support and protection for neurons. They also maintain the environment around neurons. Astrocytes control calcium (important for learning and memory). Oligodendrocytes, microglia, and ependymal cells reside within the CNS, while Schwann cells and satellite cells reside in the PNS.

Myelination: Occurs when neuroglial cells wrap multiple layers of membrane around an axon. The gaps in the myelin sheath are called nodes of Ranvier. Myelination speeds up action potential conduction (15-20x faster).

White matter: Composed of myelinated axons. It acts as a highway for action potentials.

Gray matter: Composed of unmyelinated dendrites and axons. It is involved in reflexes and information processing (memory).

Regeneration of nervous tissue: Regeneration (replacement of damaged tissue) is nearly nonexistent in the CNS and is limited in the PNS. Neural tissue can regenerate only if the cell body remains intact.

üìù Summary

  • Neuroglia support and protect neurons.
  • Myelination speeds up action potential conduction.
  • White matter is myelinated, and gray matter is unmyelinated.
  • Nervous tissue regeneration is limited.

üìö Electrophysiology of Neurons

All neurons are excitable, or responsive, in the presence of various stimuli: chemical signals, local electrical signals, and mechanical deformation. Stimuli generate electrical changes across the neuron plasma membrane that are rapidly conducted (conductivity) along the entire length of the membrane.

Local potentials: Small, local changes in the membrane potential of a neuron. They are useful for short-distance signaling and are often generated by chemically gated ion channels (neurotransmitters).

Action potentials: Uniform, rapid changes in the membrane potential that are propagated along the entire length of the axon. They are generated by voltage-gated ion channels.

üìù Summary

  • Neurons are excitable cells.
  • Local potentials are small, graded changes in membrane potential.
  • Action potentials are rapid, all-or-none changes in membrane potential.

Question 2: What is the function of myelin?

  1. A) To slow down action potential conduction.
  2. B) To speed up action potential conduction.
  3. C) To generate action potentials.
  4. D) To receive input from other neurons.

B) To speed up action potential conduction.

Question 3: What is the difference between a local potential and an action potential?

  1. A) Local potentials are all-or-none, while action potentials are graded.
  2. B) Local potentials are long-distance signals, while action potentials are short-distance signals.
  3. C) Local potentials are generated by chemically gated ion channels, while action potentials are generated by voltage-gated ion channels.
  4. D) There is no difference.

C) Local potentials are generated by chemically gated ion channels, while action potentials are generated by voltage-gated ion channels.
