SKELETAL MUSCLE

  • About 43% of the total body weight.

FUNCTIONS:

  1. Movement
  2. Shape
  3. Storage (proteins)

SKELETAL MUSCLE General organization

  • Consists largely of elongated cells: the MUSCLE FIBRES.
  • The length of a muscle fibre ranges from 1-300 mm.
  • The SARCOLEMMA is the cell membrane of the muscle fibre (cell) & the protoplasm is called the SARCOPLASM.

SKELETAL MUSCLE General organization

  • □Muscle fibres are subdivided into RED & WHITE
  • □The red fibres have myoglobin

SKELETAL MUSCLE General organization

  • Within the sarcoplasm are fibrils (MYOFIBRILS).
  • Also in abundance within the sarcoplasm are mitochondria & inclusions such as glycogen.

SKELETAL MUSCLE General organization

  • □Myofibrils are not homogenous, but exhibit light & dark bands.
  • □Under EM, a fibril consists of alternating FILAMENTS of proteins called MYOSIN & ACTIN.

SKELETAL MUSCLE General organization

  • □The functional unit of skeletal muscle is the SARCOMERE

SKELETAL MUSCLE coverings

  • □Each fibre is surrounded by fine connective tissue of collagen & elastic fibres, the ENDOMYSIUM
  • □Fibres are aggregated into bundles by the PERIMYSIUM
  • □The entire muscle is surrounded by the EPIMYSIUM

SKELETAL MUSCLE Fibre arrangement

FIBRE ORIENTATION

  • Parallel (strap)
  • Oblique (Pennate)
  • Radial, Triangular or fan shaped.

SKELETAL MUSCLE Fibre arrangement

STRAP

  • Fibres run almost whole length of muscle & parallel to its long axis
  • E.g. SARTORIUS

SKELETAL MUSCLE Fibre arrangement

PENNATE

  • Fibres run obliquely to the long axis of the muscle & attached to a tendon/aponeurosis that is parallel to long axis.
  • TYPES: UNIPENNATE, BIPENNATE & MULTIPENNATE

SKELETAL MUSCLE Fibre arrangement

UNIPENNATE

  • Fibres attached on tendon on one side only
  • E.g. FLEXOR POLLICIS LONGUS

SKELETAL MUSCLE Fibre arrangement

BIPENNATE

  • Fibres attached to both sides of the tendon
  • E.g. RECTUS FEMORIS

SKELETAL MUSCLE Fibre arrangement

MULTIPENNATE

  • Several pennate groups lie side by side

CONTRACTING MUSCLE Functional aspects

  • The greater the cross-section of a muscle, the greater is the force it can produce.
  • The longer the fibres, the greater is the range of shortening.

CONTRACTING MUSCLE Functional aspects

  • The total force of contraction is a sum of individual fibre effort.
  • Pennate muscles have more fibres.
  • When the whole distance between the points of attachment is occupied by muscle tissue, then shortening is greater.

CONTRACTING MUSCLE Adaptation to shortening

  • □Parallelism of fibres
  • □Great length

CONTRACTING MUSCLE Adaptation to power

  • □Structure (MULTIPENNATE e.g. deltoid)
  • □Large mass (e.g. Gluteus maximus)

CONTRACTING MUSCLE Effectiveness in joint movement
MOMENT about the joint

  • Greater when the pull of the muscle is far removed from the joint it controls.
  • Less where the muscle passes close to the joint.

CONTRACTING MUSCLE Effectiveness in joint movement

JOINT CROSSING

  • UNI-ARTICULAR muscles cross/act on one joint only
  • BI-ARTICULAR muscles cross two joints & are capable of producing movement at one or both joints.
  • MULTI-ARTICULAR cross more than two joints

CONTRACTING MUSCLE Effectiveness in joint movement
ACTIVE INSUFFICIENCY

  • Inability of a muscle to shorten sufficiently.

PASSIVE INSUFFICIENCY

  • Inability to lengthen sufficiently.

SOME MUSCLES DO NOT CROSS JOINTS

  1. Cutaneous muscles (face)
  2. Articular muscles (articularis genu)
  3. Sphincteric muscles (anal sphincters)
  4. Tubular (pharyngeal)

CONTRACTING MUSCLE Group action of muscles

Muscles which produce a movement are called PRIME MOVERS

ANTAGONISTS have to be lengthened or shortened to allow movement

CONTRACTING MUSCLE Group action of muscles

ECCENTRIC ACTION

  • □Control of movement through relaxation of an antagonist due to effect of gravity.

CONCENTRIC ACTION

  • □Muscle shortens when it acts as a prime mover

CONTRACTING MUSCLE Group action of muscles

  • When a muscle is capable of moving more than one joint, then at one time, it may be necessary to prevent one of the actions.
  • A muscle which does this is called a SYNERGIST or fixator.

SKELETAL MUSCLE Attachment to bone

  • Collagen fibres in the connective around fibres extend into the bone as SHARPEY'S FIBRES
  • The proximal attachment of a limb muscle is called the ORIGIN and the distal attachment the INSERTION.

SKELETAL MUSCLE Attachment to bone

  • When the fibrous end is rounded, it is called a TENDON.
  • When flattened or membranous, it is called an APONEUROSIS.

SKELETAL MUSCLE Nomenclature

  1. SHAPE
    • Quadratus (4-sided)
    • Gracilis (slender)
  2. GENERAL FORM
    • Brevis (short)
    • Teres (smooth)
    • Serratus (serrated)

SKELETAL MUSCLE Nomenclature

  1. No. OF HEADS
    • Triceps (three- e.g. triceps brachii)
    • Biceps (two-e.g. biceps femoris & biceps brachii)
    • Quadriceps (four- e.g. quadriceps femoris)
  2. LOCATION
    • Anterior/Posterior/Lateral/Medial
    • Brachialis (arm)
    • Tibialis (next to tibia-e.g. Tibialis anterior)

SKELETAL MUSCLE Nomenclature

  1. ACTION
    • Flexion e.g. Flexor carpi ulnaris
    • Abduction e.g. abductor hallucis
  2. DIRECTION
    • Transverse e.g. transversus abdominis
    • Straight e.g. Rectus femoris

SKELETAL MUSCLE Nomenclature

  • SIZE (Magnus, Maximus, Major)
    • e.g. Gluteus maximus
    • Adductor magnus
    • Teres major

SKELETAL MUSCLE Innervation

  • The nerve to a muscle is called a MOTOR nerve.
  • A motor nerve has a mixture of motor (efferent) fibres, sensory fibres and sympathetic fibres.

NEUROMUSCULAR JUNCTION
Point where the motor nerve joins the muscle
