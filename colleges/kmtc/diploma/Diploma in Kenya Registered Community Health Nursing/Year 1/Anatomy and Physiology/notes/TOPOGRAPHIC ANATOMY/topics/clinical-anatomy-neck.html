üß¥ Skin of the Neck

üò© Paralysis of the Platysma

  • ‚Ä¢ Anatomical basis
  • - results from injury to the cervical branch of the facial nerve
  • - causes the skin to fall away from the neck in slack folds.
  • ‚Ä¢ Prevention
  • - preserve the cervical branch of the facial nerve.
  • - careful suturing of the skin and edges of the platysma.
  • ‚Ä¢ Clinical significance
  • - the skin wound is distracted (pulled in different directions) by the contracting platysma muscle fibers,
  • - a broad ugly scar may develop.

üë∂ Congenital Torticollis

  • ‚Ä¢ results from a fibrous tissue tumor (L. fibromatosis colli) of the SCM before or shortly after birth.
  • ‚Ä¢ causes the head to tilt toward, and the face to turn away from, the affected side
  • ‚Ä¢ Clinical significance
  • - the position of the infant's head necessitates a breech delivery.
  • - Injury to SCM when an infant's head is pulled too much during a difficult birth, tearing its fibers (muscular torticollis)
  • ‚Ä¢ Anatomical Basis
  • - Formation of a hematoma with entrapment of a branch of the spinal accessory nerve (CN XI) which denervates part of the SCM.
  • - The stiffness and twisting of the neck results from fibrosis and shortening of the SCM.
  • ‚Ä¢ Solution
  • - Surgical release of the SCM from its inferior attachments to the manubrium and clavicle inferior to the level of CN XI

üò´ Spasmodic Torticollis

  • ‚Ä¢ Clinical significance
  • - cervical dystonia (abnormal tonicity of the cervical muscles) usually begins in adulthood.
  • ‚Ä¢ Anatomical basis
  • - It may involve any bilateral combination of lateral neck muscles, especially the SCM and trapezius.
  • ‚Ä¢ Characteristics
  • - sustained turning, tilting, flexing, or extending of the neck
  • - Shifting the head laterally or anteriorly can occur involuntarily
  • - The shoulder is usually elevated and displaced anteriorly on the side to which the chin turns.

üíâ Subclavian Vein Puncture - Infraclavicular Approach
- Landmarks

  • ‚Ä¢ middle part of the clavicle
  • ‚Ä¢ jugular notch in the manubrium
  • ‚Ä¢ sternoclavicular joint
  • ‚Ä¢ Anatomical basis
  • - The needle puncture is inferior to middle of the clavicle and is advanced medially toward the jugular notch until the right venous angle
  • - Here the internal jugular and subclavian veins merge to form the brachiocephalic vein.
  • ‚Ä¢ Clinical Significance
  • - puncture the pleura and lung, resulting in pneumothorax.
  • - If inserted too far posteriorly, it may enter the subclavian artery.

ü©∏ Prominence of the External Jugular Vein

  • ‚Ä¢ Purpose
  • - May serve as an internal barometer.
  • ‚Ä¢ Anatomical basis
  • - Normal EJV pressure usually visible above the clavicle for only a short distance.
  • - venous pressure rises (e.g. in heart failure), prominent vein throughout its course along the side of the neck.
  • ‚Ä¢ Clinical Significance
  • - Diagnostic signs of heart failure, SVC obstruction, enlarged supraclavicular lymph nodes, increased intrathoracic pressure.

‚ö†Ô∏è Severance of the External Jugular Vein

  • ‚Ä¢ Surface Anatomy
  • - along the posterior border of the SCM where it pierces the roof of the lateral cervical region (e.g., by a knife slash)
  • ‚Ä¢ Clinical significance
  • - Negative intrathoracic pressure sucks air into the vein resulting in a venous air embolism
  • ‚Ä¢ Anatomical basis
  • - Lumen is held open by the tough investing layer of deep cervical fascia

üß† Lesions of the Spinal Accessory Nerve

  • ‚Ä¢ Causes
  • - Penetrating trauma, such as a stab or bullet wound.
  • - Surgical procedures in the lateral cervical region.
  • - Tumors at the cranial base
  • - cancerous cervical lymph nodes.
  • - Fractures of the jugular foramen where CN XI leaves the cranium.
  • ‚Ä¢ Clinical picture
  • - a unilateral lesion usually does not produce an abnormal position of the head.
  • - weakness in turning the head to the opposite side against resistance.
  • - weakness and atrophy of the trapezius, impairing neck movements.
  • - inability to elevate and retract the shoulder
  • - difficulty in elevating the upper limb superior to the horizontal level.
  • - Reduced normal prominence in the neck produced by the trapezius
  • - Drooping of the shoulder

üß† Phrenic Nerve Injury

  • ‚Ä¢ Results in paralysis of the corresponding half of the diaphragm
  • ‚Ä¢ A phrenic nerve block produces a short period of paralysis of the diaphragm on one side (e.g., for a lung operation).
  • ‚Ä¢ A surgical phrenic nerve crush (e.g., compressing the nerve injuriously with forceps) produces a longer period of paralysis (sometimes for weeks after surgical repair of a diaphragmatic hernia).
  • ‚Ä¢ accessory phrenic nerve must also be crushed

üß† Injury to the Suprascapular Nerve

  • ‚Ä¢ Anatomical basis
  • - fractures of the middle third of the clavicle.
  • - loss of lateral rotation of the humerus at the glenohumeral joint.
  • ‚Ä¢ Clinical picture
  • - the relaxed limb rotates medially into the waiter's tip position
  • - The ability to initiate abduction of the limb is also affected.

ü©∏ Ligation of the External Carotid Artery

  • ‚Ä¢ Purpose
  • - to control bleeding from one of its relatively inaccessible branches.
  • ‚Ä¢ Clinical significance
  • - decreases blood flow through the artery and its branches but does not eliminate it.
  • ‚Ä¢ Anatomical basis
  • - Blood flows in a retrograde direction from the other side through communications between its branches and across the midline.
  • - the descending branch of the occipital artery provides the main collateral circulation
  • - anastomosing with the vertebral and deep cervical arteries.

üî™ Surgical Dissection of the Carotid Triangle

  • ‚Ä¢ Purpose
  • - surgical approach to the carotid system of arteries.
  • ‚Ä¢ Anatomical basis
  • - provides access to the IJV, the vagus and hypoglossal nerves, and the cervical sympathetic trunk.
  • ‚Ä¢ Clinical significance
  • - Damage or compression of the vagus and/or recurrent laryngeal nerves may produce an alteration in the voice because these nerves supply laryngeal muscles.

üíì Carotid Pulse

  • ‚Ä¢ Surface anatomy
  • - in a groove between the trachea and the infrahyoid muscles.
  • - Usually easily palpated just deep to the anterior border of the SCM at the level of the superior border of the thyroid cartilage.
  • - Is routinely checked during cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR).
  • ‚Ä¢ Clinical Significance
  • - Absence of a carotid pulse indicates cardiac arrest.

‚ö†Ô∏è Carotid Sinus Hypersensitivity

  • ‚Ä¢ Is exceptional responsiveness of the carotid sinuses in various types of vascular disease
  • ‚Ä¢ external pressure on the carotid artery may cause
  • - slowing of the heart rate,
  • - a fall in blood pressure,
  • - cardiac ischemia
  • - fainting (syncope) from sudden and critical decrease in cerebral perfusion
  • ‚Ä¢ Clinical Significance
  • - Checking carotid pulse is not recommended for people with cardiac or vascular disease.
  • - Alternate sites, such as the radial artery at the wrist, should be used

üíì Internal Jugular Pulse

  • ‚Ä¢ Surface Anatomy
  • - pulsations are transmitted through the surrounding tissue and may be observed beneath the SCM superior to the medial end of the clavicle.
  • ‚Ä¢ Anatomical basis
  • - Because there are no valves in the brachiocephalic vein or the superior vena cava, a wave of contraction passes up these vessels to the inferior bulb of the IJV.
  • - The pulsations are especially visible when the person's head is inferior to the lower limbs (the Trendelenburg position).
  • ‚Ä¢ Clinical Significance
  • - The internal jugular pulse increases considerably in tricuspid valve disease because of increase in pressure in the pulmonary circulation and the right side of the heart.
  • - Use the right IJV because it runs a straighter, more direct course to the right atrium than does the left

üíâ Internal Jugular Vein Puncture

  • ‚Ä¢ Surface Anatomy
  • - palpate the common carotid artery and insert the needle into the IJV just lateral to it at a $30^{\circ}$ angle
  • - aim at the apex of the triangle between the sternal and the clavicular heads of the SCM (lesser supraclavicular fossa)
  • - directed inferolaterally
  • ‚Ä¢ Clinical Significance
  • - Catheterization for central lines

üß¨ Ectopic Thyroid Gland

  • ‚Ä¢ Anatomical basis
  • - Failure to descend from the embryonic origin in the tongue,
  • ‚Ä¢ Result
  • - Lingual thyroid gland
  • - High location in the neck at or just inferior to the hyoid.
  • ‚Ä¢ Clinical significance
  • - If in the median plane of the neck it is the only thyroid tissue present.
  • - May be associated with a thyroglossal duct cyst

üß¨ Accessory Thyroid Glandular Tissue

  • ‚Ä¢ Anatomical basis
  • - portions of the thyroglossal duct persist to form thyroid tissue.
  • ‚Ä¢ Sites
  • - anywhere along the embryonic course of the thyroglossal duct
  • ‚Ä¢ Clinical significance
  • - may be functional but is often of insufficient size to maintain normal function if the thyroid gland is removed.

üß¨ Pyramidal Lobe of the Thyroid Gland

  • ‚Ä¢ Anatomical basis
  • - remnants of the epithelium and connective tissue of the thyroglossal duct.
  • ‚Ä¢ Clinical significance
  • - In approximately $50 \%$
  • - The isthmus may be incomplete
  • - May have a band of connective tissue containing accessory thyroid tissue continue from the apex of the pyramidal lobe to the hyoid.

ü©∫ Enlargement of the Thyroid Gland

  • ‚Ä¢ Clinical significance
  • - swelling in the neck
  • - compress the trachea, esophagus, and recurrent laryngeal nerves
  • ‚Ä¢ Anatomical basis
  • - Enlargement is anteriorly, posteriorly, inferiorly, or laterally.
  • - Substernal extension of a goiter is also common.

‚ö†Ô∏è Inadvertent Removal of the Parathyroid Glands

  • ‚Ä¢ Anatomical basis
  • - variable position puts them in danger of being damaged or removed during surgical procedures in the neck
  • ‚Ä¢ Clinical significance
  • - results in tetany, a severe neurologic syndrome characterized by muscle twitches and cramps.
  • - decreased serum calcium levels.
  • - laryngeal and respiratory muscles are involved, failure to respond immediately with appropriate therapy can result in death.
  • ‚Ä¢ Solution
  • - Preserve the posterior part of the lobes of the thyroid gland
  • - Carefully isolate them with their blood vessels intact before removal of the thyroid gland.
  • - may also be transplanted

üî™ Thyroidectomy

‚ö†Ô∏è Injury to the Recurrent Laryngeal Nerves

  • ‚Ä¢ Anatomic position
  • - Near the inferior pole of the thyroid gland
  • ‚Ä¢ Anatomic basis
  • - the right recurrent laryngeal nerve is intimately related to the inferior thyroid artery and its branches
  • - may cross anterior or posterior to branches of the artery, or it may pass between them.
  • ‚Ä¢ Surgical significance
  • - Because of this close relationship, the inferior thyroid artery is ligated some distance lateral to the thyroid gland, where it is not close to the nerve.
  • ‚Ä¢ Clinical significance
  • - Hoarseness is the usual sign of unilateral recurrent nerve injury
  • - temporary aphonia or disturbance of phonation (voice production)
  • - laryngeal spasm may occur

ü¶¥ Fracture of the Hyoid

  • ‚Ä¢ Cause
  • - manual strangulation by compression of the throat
  • ‚Ä¢ Result
  • - Depression of the body of the bone onto the thyroid cartilage.
  • ‚Ä¢ Clinical significance
  • - Difficulty swallowing because of inability to elevate the hyoid and move it anteriorly beneath the tongue
  • - maintenance of the separation of the alimentary and respiratory tracts difficult
  • - ? aspiration pneumonia.
