üìñ Introduction

  • ‚Ä¢ largest & most complex joint of the body

  • ‚Ä¢ Stability depends almost entirely on its associated ligaments and muscles

ü¶µ Knee Anatomy

  • ‚Ä¢ The Knee Joint is the largest & complex joint in the body .
  • ‚Ä¢ It consists of 3 Joints:
  • 1) Medial Condylar Joint : Between the medial condyle "of the femur" & the medial condyle "of the tibia" .
  • 2) Latral Condylar Joint : Between the lateral condyle "of the femur" & the lateral condyle "of the tibia" .
  • 3) Patellofemoral Joint : Between the patella & the patellar surface of the femur.
  • ‚Ä¢ The fibula is NOT directly involved in the joint .

C Properties of menisci

  • ‚Ä¢ Composed of fibrocartilage and covers the sup. surface of the tibial condyles.
  • ‚Ä¢ Act as swabs to lubricate the joint cavity
  • ‚Ä¢ Adapt or increase the contact surface area (congruent) between the round femoral condyles and the relatively flat tibial plateau on the medial side, and the convex tibial plateau on the lateral side

C Properties of menisci

  • ‚Ä¢ play an important role in load transmission
  • ‚Ä¢ shock absorbers for the knee by spreading compression forces from the femur over a wider area on the tibia
  • ‚Ä¢ Additional sensory function for better stability of the knee joint
  • ‚Ä¢ Flexion/ extension occur in the upper compartment
  • ‚Ä¢ Rotations in the lower compartment (fruit bat has no menisci nor popliteus muscle)

  • ‚Ä¢ Each covers peripheral two-thirds of the articular surface of the tibia.

üö∂ Movements

  • ‚Ä¢ the axis around which motion takes place is not a fixed one, but shifts forward during extension and backward during flexion;
  • ‚Ä¢ the commencement of flexion and the end of extension are accompanied by rotatory movements associated with the fixation of the limb in a position of great stability

üö∂ Movements

Extension -Quadriceps.F Flexion by the Biceps femoris, Semitendinosus, and Semimembranosus, assisted by the Gracilis, Sartorius, Gastrocnemius, Popliteus, and Plantaris.

üîÑ Rotation

Rotation outward is by the Biceps femoris, and rotation inward by the Popliteus, Semitendinosus and, to a slight extent, the Semimembranosus, the Sartorius, and the Gracilis.

üîÑ Rotation

  • ‚Ä¢ The Popliteus at the commencement of the movement of flexion; by its contraction the leg is rotated inward in a fixed femur,
  • ‚Ä¢ or, if the tibia be fixed, the thigh is rotated outward, and the knee-joint is unlocked

üîí Locking- principles

  • ‚Ä¢ Permits standing upright with little expenditure of energy in the form of muscle contraction
  • ‚Ä¢ Articular surface of the medial femoral condyle is longer than the surface on the lateral femoral condyle

üîí Extension:- Pre-locking

  • ‚Ä¢ Moving into extension from a flexed position
  • ‚Ä¢ Articular surface of lateral femoral condyle stops
  • ‚Ä¢ Surface for articulation still exposed on medial condyle

üîí Locking

  • ‚Ä¢ Medial Rotation accompanies final stages of extension
  • ‚Ä¢ Accommodates remainder of exposed articular surface on medial femoral condyle
  • ‚Ä¢ Promotes greater contact between articular surfaces of femoral and tibial condyles
  • ‚Ä¢ Tightens collateral and cruciate ligaments

üîí Locking

  • ‚Ä¢ Locked position is brought about by medial rotation of the femur on the tibia during final stages of extension
  • ‚Ä¢ Promotes maximum contact between articular surfaces of femoral and tibial condyles
  • ‚Ä¢ Renders ligaments taut
  • ‚Ä¢ Ligaments maintain the joint in the stable (extended) position

ü¶¥ Tibia

  • ‚Ä¢ Significant part is subcutaneous
  • ‚Ä¢ poor muscle cover (surrounded by tendons).
  • ‚Ä¢ A rather proximally placed nutrient foramenposterior tibial artery.
  • ‚Ä¢ Commonly fractured bone with poor healing properties

ü¶¥ Fibula

  • ‚Ä¢ Strut, key in formation & stability of AJ
  • ‚Ä¢ Good muscle cover - peroneal artery.
  • ‚Ä¢ Little contribution in weight bearing in the presence of an intact normal tibia & therefore may be sacrificed for transplant.
  • ‚Ä¢ It may also be used to bear weight in absence of the tibia (tibiarization of the fibula)

ü©∏ Compartment syndrome

  • ‚Ä¢ fracture of the tibia or fibula with muscle swelling or blood clot.
  • ‚Ä¢ Increase in the intracompartmental pressures results in compression of arteries/veins & nerves.
  • ‚Ä¢ pain, pallor, paralysis, paresthesiae & pulselessness (5Ps)
  • ‚Ä¢ If unattended results in limb death and amputation
  • ‚Ä¢ The common peroneal nerve is commonly in fractures of the neck of the fibula e.g police baton injury(Policeman's nerve)
  • ‚Ä¢ It may also be entrappment in the fibres of peroneus longus muscle in this area.
  • ‚Ä¢ The ankle dorsiflexors are denervated.

ü©π Varicose veins

  • ‚Ä¢ Elongated, dilated & tortuous superficial veins.
  • ‚Ä¢ Causes
  • ‚Ä¢ Incompetence of the valves at either the sapheno-femoral or sapheno-popliteal junctions or both.
  • ‚Ä¢ Incompetent valves in perforators
  • ‚Ä¢ Blocked deep veins
  • ‚Ä¢ Their association with pregnancy is due to pressure of the gravid uterus on big veins in the Pelvis.

ü©∏ Vessels

  • ‚Ä¢ Before its termination, the popliteal artery gives off sural branches to each head of gastrocnemius muscle. These are end arteries.
