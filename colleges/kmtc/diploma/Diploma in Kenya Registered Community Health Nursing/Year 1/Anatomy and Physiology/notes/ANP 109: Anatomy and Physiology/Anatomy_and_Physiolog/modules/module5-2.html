Module 5: Transport Across the Plasma Membrane, Active Transport

üéØ Learning Objectives

  • Explain how substances cross the plasma membrane.
  • Differentiate between passive and active transport.
  • Describe the different types of passive transport, including simple diffusion, facilitated diffusion, and osmosis.
  • Define tonicity and explain its importance in osmosis.
  • Describe the different types of active transport, including primary active transport, secondary active transport, and vesicular transport.

üìö Transport Across the Plasma Membrane

The phospholipid bilayer is selectively permeable. It allows specific things to pass through, but not others. It is critical for the survival of the cell, and it prohibits the passage of other molecules.

Substances cross the plasma membrane in several ways. Some do not require cell energy (passive transport), while others do (active transport). The substance, size, and concentration of ICF and ECF vary in the energy required.

üìù Summary

  • The plasma membrane is selectively permeable.
  • Passive transport does not require cell energy.
  • Active transport requires cell energy.

üìö Passive Transport

Diffusion: The movement of solute molecules from an area of high concentration to an area of low concentration (down the concentration gradient) until equilibrium is reached.

  • Simple diffusion: Involves mostly nonpolar solutes (e.g., oxygen, carbon dioxide, lipids, hydrocarbons) that are able to pass straight through the phospholipid bilayer without the need for membrane proteins.
  • Facilitated diffusion: Involves charged or polar solutes (e.g., ions, glucose) that cross the phospholipid bilayer with the help of a membrane protein (either a carrier or a channel).

Osmosis: Involves the movement of solvent (water) across a membrane from an area of lower solute concentration (higher water concentration) to an area of higher solute concentration (lower water concentration). Water moves across the plasma membrane either through water channels (aquaporins) or directly through the membrane itself in small amounts.

Osmotic pressure is the driving force exerted by solute molecules that causes water to move from an area of low solute concentration to an area of high solute concentration.

Hydrostatic pressure is the force that fluids exert, pushing on a surface.

Tonicity: A way to compare the osmotic pressure gradient between two solutions (e.g., the cytosol and the ECF).

  • Isotonic: The ECF has the same solute concentration as the cytosol. There is no net movement of water.
  • Hypertonic: The ECF has a higher solute concentration than the cytosol. Water moves out of the cell, causing the cell to shrink (crenate).
  • Hypotonic: The ECF has a lower solute concentration than the cytosol. Water moves into the cell, causing the cell to swell and possibly burst (lyse).

üìù Summary

  • Diffusion is the movement of solutes from high to low concentration.
  • Simple diffusion involves nonpolar solutes moving directly through the membrane.
  • Facilitated diffusion involves polar or charged solutes using membrane proteins.
  • Osmosis is the movement of water across a membrane.
  • Tonicity compares solute concentrations between solutions.
  • Isotonic solutions have equal concentrations.
  • Hypertonic solutions have a higher solute concentration.
  • Hypotonic solutions have a lower solute concentration.

üìö Active Transport

Active transport requires energy in the form of ATP to move solutes against their concentration gradients (from low to high concentration). Both primary and secondary active transport use plasma membrane carrier proteins called pumps.

Primary active transport: A pump in the plasma membrane binds and transports a solute against its concentration gradient using the energy from the hydrolysis of ATP. An example is the sodium-potassium pump (Na+/K+ pump), which is important for maintaining Na+ and K+ concentration gradients (antiport - potassium in, sodium out).

Secondary active transport: Uses ATP indirectly. ATP is used to create and maintain a concentration gradient of one substance. Then, the potential energy of that gradient is used to power the transport of another substance against its gradient.

üìù Summary

  • Active transport moves solutes against their concentration gradient.
  • Primary active transport uses ATP directly.
  • The sodium-potassium pump is an example of primary active transport.
  • Secondary active transport uses ATP indirectly.

üìö Active Transport via Vesicles

Vesicles are small sacs filled with large molecules too big to transport by other means.

Endocytosis: Transports ECF, large molecules, and whole cells into the cell. Types of endocytosis include:

  • Phagocytosis: "Cell eating," where cells ingest large particles such as bacteria or dead cells.
  • Pinocytosis: "Cell drinking," where cells engulf fluid droplets.
  • Receptor-mediated endocytosis: A specific type of endocytosis where receptors fill a vesicle with specific molecules (e.g., cholesterol).

Exocytosis: Where large molecules exit the cell (secretion).

üìù Summary

  • Vesicles transport large molecules.
  • Endocytosis brings substances into the cell.
  • Phagocytosis is cell eating.
  • Pinocytosis is cell drinking.
  • Receptor-mediated endocytosis is specific.
  • Exocytosis releases substances from the cell.
