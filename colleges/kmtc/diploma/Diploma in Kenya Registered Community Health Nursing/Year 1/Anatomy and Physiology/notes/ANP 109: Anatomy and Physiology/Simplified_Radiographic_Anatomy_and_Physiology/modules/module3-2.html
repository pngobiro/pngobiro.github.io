Radius and Ulna, Carpal Bones, Metacarpal Bones, and Phalanges

üìö Radius and Ulna

Radius and ulna are the bones of the forearm. They extend from the elbow proximally to the wrist distally.

The Radius:

It is the lateral of the two bones of the forearm; it extends from the lateral side of the elbow to the thumb side of the wrist and runs parallel to the ulna. The proximal end of the radius is a disc-shaped structure known as the head of the radius. The superior surface of the head of the radius articulates with the capitulum of the humerus as part of the elbow joint, and the lateral margin of the head of the radius articulates with a shallow depression on the medial side of the proximal ulna known as the radial notch of the ulna at the proximal radioulnar joint. A narrow region lies immediately distal to the radial head known as the neck of the radius. Inferiorly there an oval-shaped, bony protuberance acts as a muscular attachment point located on the medial side of the radius known as the radial tuberosity. On the medial side of the shaft of the radius there is small ridge forms the interosseous border of the radius, it is for the attachment of interosseous membrane. The interosseous membrane is a sheet of dense connective tissue extends between the radius and ulna to unite these two bones. The distal end of the radius expands to form a rectangular end; it has two smooth facets, for articulation with the scaphoid and lunate carpal bones at the radiocarpal joint or wrist joint. On the medial surface of the distal end of the radius, there is shallow concavity known as the ulnar notch of the radius, it articulates with the head of the ulna, to form the distal radioulnar joint. The lateral side of the distal end of the radius has a projection known as the styloid process of the radius; it provides attachment for ligaments that support the lateral side of the wrist joint.

The Ulna:

It is the medial bone of the forearm; it extends, parallel to the radius, from the medial side of the elbow to the medial side of the wrist. At the proximal end of the ulna there is wrench shaped area known as the trochlear notch, it articulates with the trochlea of the humerus at the medial side of the elbow joint. The lower margin of the trochlear notch is formed by a boney prominence known as the coronoid process, and the upper margin of the trochlear notch is formed by a boney tip extends from the olecranon process which form the posterior and superior portions of the proximal ulna.

Inferior to the trochlear notch there is a roughened area known as the ulnar tuberosity, it is the attachment area for brachialis muscle. Inferolateral to the trochlear notch, is the location of radial notch of the ulna, it is the articulation site between the proximal radius and ulna, to form the proximal radioulnar joint.

üìö The Carpal Bones

The carpal bones are series of eight small bones located at the wrist and base of the hand. They are arranged in two rows, a proximal row and a distal row. Each row is composed of four carpal bones attached to each other by ligaments to form one unit (Figure 9.6).

The bones in the proximal row are:

  1. Scaphoid
  2. Lunate
  3. Triquetrum
  4. Pisiform

The bones in the distal row are:

  1. Trapezium
  2. Trapezoid
  3. Capitate
  4. Hamate

The Scaphoid: is the boat-shaped carpal bone (Figure 9.8), it is the first and largest carpal bone on the radial side at the proximal row. It articulates with the radius proximally, trapezoid and trapezium laterally, lunate and capitate medially (Figures 9.6 &9.7).The scaphoid is the most frequently fractured carpal bone, because of its connections with the two rows of carpal bones.

The Lunate: The word lunate is derived from the Latin word luna which means ("moon). Lunate is a crescent-shaped carpal bone with a convex proximal articular facet and a concave distal articular facet (Figure 9.9). It is situated in the center of the proximal row of the carpal bones, and articulates with radius proximally, the capitate distally, scaphoid laterally and Triquetrum medially (Figures 9.6 & 9.7). The lunate is the most frequently dislocated carpal bone.

Triquetrum: the name triquetrum means three-cornered, it is pyramidal shaped carpal bone with three corners (Figure 9.10), it is located between the lunate and the pisiform bones on the ulnar side of the proximal row of the carpal bones, it articulates with the lunate laterally, the pisiform anteriorly and the hamate distally and the triangular articular disk proximally which separates the triquetrum from the distal end of the ulna (Figure 9.6). Triquetrum is the 3rd most commonly fractured carpal bone.

Pisiform: as the Latin word (pisum) means "pea", the name pisiform means the pea shaped bone (Figure 9.11). It is the smallest carpal bone and the last carpal bone to ossify, it is a sesamoid bone located on the anteromedial side of the proximal row of carpal bones, in a plane anterior and superficially to all other carpal bones (Figure 9.6). Pisiform has only one side that articulates with the triquetrum.

Trapezium: as the Greek word (trapezion) means ‚Äúa little table‚Äù, the name trapezium mean the bone that looks like a table, it is a four-sided carpal bone with multiple articulation surfaces (Figure 9.12). The trapezium articulates with the scaphoid proximally, the trapezoid and second metacarpal medially, and first metacarpal distally. Trapezium articulation with the first metacarpal is saddle-shaped and is responsible for thumb opposition and movement (Figure 9.6).

Trapezoid: as have been mentioned previously (trapezion) is a Greek word means ‚Äúa little table‚Äù, the name trapezoid mean the bone that resembles a table (Figure 9.13), trapezoid is the smallest bone in the distal row of carpal bones, it is wedge-shaped with four articular surfaces to articulate with the scaphoid proximally, the trapezium laterally, the capitate medially, and the second metacarpal distally (Figures 9.6 & 9.7). Trapezoid is the least common of all carpal fractures, due to its protected position.

Hamate: as the Latin word (hamulus) means ‚Äúa little hook‚Äù Hamate means hooked bone. Hamate is a wedge-shaped bone with a hook-like process known as the hook of hamate (Figure 9.14), Hamate is situated on the medial side of the distal row of carpal bones. It articulates with lunate, triquetrum, capitate beside the fourth and fifth metacarpals (Figure 9.6).

Capitate: the name Capitate is derived from the Latin word caput, which means ‚Äúhead‚Äù (Figure 9.15). Capitate is a head-shaped bone situated in the center of the distal row of carpal bones. It is the most central and largest carpal bone, capitate articulates with scaphoid and lunate proximally, hamate medially, trapezoid laterally, second and third carpals distally (Figure 9.6).

üìö Metacarpal Bones

Metacarpal bones lie between the carpal bones of the wrist and the bones of the fingers and thumb, each metacarpal bone composed of a base, shaft and a head, they articulate proximally with the distal row of carpal bones and distally with the proximal phalanges. Metacarpal bones are numbered in a lateral to medial direction and correspond to the fingers whose phalanges they articulate with as follows:

  1. First Metacarpal proximal to the Thumb
  2. Second Metacarpal proximal to the Index Finger
  3. Third Metacarpal proximal to the Middle Finger
  4. Fourth Metacarpal proximal to the Ring Finger
  5. Fifth Metacarpal proximal to the Little Finger (Figures 9.16 & 9.17)

üìö Upper limb Phalanges

Upper limb phalanges are the bones of fingers and thumb, they are 14 bones, the thumb has two phalanges (proximal and distal), while the other digits have three phalanges (proximal, middle, and distal). The phalanges articulate with each other through interphalangeal joints, while the proximal phalanges articulate with the metatarsal bones through metatarsophalangeal joints. The bone of each phalanx consists of head at the distal end, shaft and base at the proximal end (Figure. 9.18).

üìù Summary

  • The radius is the lateral bone of the forearm, articulating with the humerus and ulna proximally, and with the carpal bones distally. Key features include the head, neck, radial tuberosity, and styloid process.
  • The ulna is the medial bone of the forearm, articulating with the humerus and radius proximally. Key features include the olecranon, trochlear notch, coronoid process, and ulnar tuberosity.
  • The carpal bones are the eight small bones of the wrist, arranged in two rows (proximal and distal).
  • The metacarpals are the five bones of the palm, numbered I-V from lateral (thumb) to medial (little finger).
  • The phalanges are the bones of the fingers. Each finger has three phalanges (proximal, middle, distal), except the thumb, which has two.

Upper Limb Joints

The Shoulder Joint

Shoulder joint, also known as Glenohumeral joint, it is the multiaxial ball-and-socket synovial joint formed by the articulation of the head of the humerus with the glenoid cavity of the scapula, which is deepened and slightly enlarged by the glenoid labrum, a fibrocartilage ring attaching to the outside margin of the glenoid fossa (Figure 10.1).

The capsule of the shoulder joint:

As a synovial joint, the shoulder joint has a capsule surrounds the joint cavity and encloses synovial fluid that lubricates the articular surfaces of the joint, it is attached medially to the outer margin of the glenoid fossa and laterally to the anatomical neck of the humerus, just distal to the head of the humerus. This capsule is loose inferiorly, and lies in folds during full adduction of the arm, these folds allow for abduction of the arm, during which they become taught (Figure 10.2).

Bursae of the shoulder joint:

The bursa is a fluid filled sac provides cushioning between joint structures to protect the joint capsule from wear. The glenohumeral joint has a number of bursae that protect the joint capsule from excessive wear from overlying structures. The most common of which is the subacromial bursa which lies between the acromion process of the scapula and the supraspinatus muscle. Subscapular bursa protects the joint capsule and the muscle tendon of the supraspinatus muscle from wear from the acromion process during arm abduction. Subscapular bursa lies between the tendon of subscapularis muscle and the scapula. It reduces wear and tear on the subscapularis tendon during shoulder joint movement (Figure 10.3).

üìù Summary

  • The shoulder joint (glenohumeral joint) is a ball-and-socket joint formed by the articulation of the head of the humerus and the glenoid cavity of the scapula.
  • It is a highly mobile joint, allowing for a wide range of movements.
  • The joint capsule encloses the joint, and bursae provide cushioning and reduce friction.

üí° Pro Tip

The shoulder joint's high mobility comes at the cost of stability. It is one of the most commonly dislocated joints in the body. Understanding the structures that contribute to its stability (ligaments, muscles, labrum) is crucial.

    • Joint capsule: A fibrous sac that encloses the joint.

Elbow joint

Elbow joint

The elbow is the joint connecting the upper arm to the forearm. It is classed as a hinge-type synovial joint. It is the articulation point of the humerus and the radius and the ulna. The elbow joint consists of two separate articulations:

  1. Humeroulnar joint between the trochlea on the medial aspect of the distal end of the humerus and the trochlear notch on the proximal ulna.
  2. Humeroradial joint between the capitulum on the lateral aspect of the distal end of the humerus with the head of the radius.

These two joints give the elbow its characteristic hinge like properties. The rounded surfaces of the capitulum of the humerus rotate against the concave surfaces of the head of the radius, and the wrench shape of the trochlear notch of the ulna moves around the trochlea of the humerus (Figure 10.6).

The capsule of the elbow joint:

The elbow joint has a strong and fibrous capsule encloses and strengthens the joint. This capsule is thickened medially and laterally to form collateral ligaments, which stabilize the flexing and extending motion of the arm (Figure 10.7).

The bursa of the elbow joint:

There are many bursae in the elbow, but only a few have clinical importance:

  1. Intratendinous bursa: leis within the tendon of the triceps brachii muscle.
  2. Subtendinous bursa: reduces friction between the olecranon and the tendon of the triceps brachii muscle during extension and flexion of the arm.
  3. Olecranon bursa: lies between the olecranon and the overlying connective tissue.

üìù Summary

  • The elbow joint is a hinge joint formed by the articulation of the humerus, radius, and ulna.
  • It consists of two articulations: the humeroulnar joint (trochlea of humerus and trochlear notch of ulna) and the humeroradial joint (capitulum of humerus and head of radius).
  • The joint capsule and collateral ligaments provide stability, while bursae reduce friction.

üí° Pro Tip

Remember that the elbow joint primarily allows for flexion and extension. While there is some rotation possible at the radioulnar joints, the elbow itself is not designed for significant rotation.

Proximal and Distal Radioulnar Joints, Wrist Joint

Proximal Radioulnar Joint

The proximal radioulnar joint is pivot-joint formed by an articulation between the head of the radius and the radial notch of the ulna; it lies immediately distal to the elbow joint, and is enclosed with in the same articular capsule.

The radial head is held in place by the Annular Radial Ligament, which forms a ‚Äòcollar‚Äô around the joint. The annular radial ligament is lined with a synovial membrane, reducing friction during movement. Movement is produced by the head of the radius rotating within the annular ligament. There are two movements possible at this joint; pronation and supination (Figure 10.10).

Distal Radioulnar Joint

The distal radioulnar joint is pivot-joint formed by an articulation between the head of the ulna and the ulnar notch on the distal end of the radius, just proximally to the wrist joint. The distal radioulnar joint is strengthened by anterior and posterior ligaments and a fibrocartilaginous ligament known as the articular disk, they hold the radius and ulna together during joint movement and separate the distal radioulnar joint from the wrist joint (Figure 10.10).

Wrist Joint

The wrist is the joint connecting the forearm and the hand, it is a synovial joint formed by articulation between the distal end of the radius and the articular disk proximally and scaphoid, lunate and triquetrum distally the ulna is not part of the wrist joint).

The radius articulates with the scaphoid laterally and the lunate medially (Figure 10.11) on the medial side of the wrist joint there is a cartilaginous structure known as the triangular fibrocartilage complex (TFCC), it extends from the ulna to insert into the lunate and triquetrum via the ulnolunate and ulnotriquetral ligaments respectively (Figure 10.12). The triangular fibrocartilage complex (TFCC) is a major contributor to the stability of the wrist joint.

Metacarp ophalangeal joint

Metacarpophalangeal joints are condyloid type synovial joints between the head of metacarpal and the base of proximal phalanx in each of the 5 digits. Each joint is enclosed by a fibrous capsule which attaches to the margins of the articular surfaces and is lined by a synovial membrane. There are two collateral ligaments and one palmar ligament associated with each metacarpophalangeal joint.

Interphalangeal joints

Interphalangeal joints are hinge joints between the phalanges, there are two Interphalangeal joints in each digit except the thumb which has only one interphalangeal joint. The two joints are: Proximal Interphalangeal Joints between the proximal phalanx and intermediate phalanx in each of the 2nd to 5th digits and Distal Interphalangeal Joints between the intermediate phalanx and distal phalanx in each of the 2nd to 5th digits (Figure 10.13).

üìù Summary

  • The proximal and distal radioulnar joints allow for pronation and supination of the forearm.
  • The wrist joint (radiocarpal joint) is formed by the articulation of the radius and the articular disc with the scaphoid, lunate, and triquetrum. The ulna is not directly involved in the wrist joint.
  • Metacarpophalangeal (MCP) joints are condyloid joints connecting the metacarpals to the proximal phalanges, allowing for flexion, extension, abduction, adduction, and circumduction.
  • Interphalangeal (IP) joints are hinge joints between the phalanges, allowing for flexion and extension. Each finger has a proximal IP (PIP) and a distal IP (DIP) joint, while the thumb has only one IP joint.

The Pelvic Girdle

The Pelvic Girdle

The pelvic girdle is formed by the two hip bones; it connects the bones of the lower limb to the axial skeleton. The bones are firmly attached to the axial skeleton via the sacrum of the vertebral column; they also converge anteriorly to attach to each other. The pelvic girdle is part of the bony pelvis which is composed of the two hip bones, the sacrum and the coccyx (Figure 11.1). The bones of the pelvis are strongly attached to each other to form a strong immobile, weight-bearing structure providing a stable foundation for the upper body as the pelvis transfers the body weight from the vertebral column, through the pelvic girdle into the lower limbs.

üìö The Hip Bone

The right and left hip bones (also known as the innominate bones) are two irregularly shaped bones that form the main component of the pelvic girdle, each hip bone is comprised of the three parts;

  1. The ilium.
  2. The pubis.
  3. The ischium.

There is a cup shaped socket located at the middle region between ilium, pubis and ischium known as the acetabulum (a Latin ward means ‚Äúvinegar cup‚Äù), where the head of the femur articulates to form the hip joint, hip bone has two more articulations beside the hip joint; the right and left hip bones articulate with each other anteriorly to form the symphysis pubis, the two hip bones articulate with the sacrum posteriorly to form the sacroiliac joint (Figures 11.2 & 11.3).

The Ilium:

The ilium is the widest, largest and the superior of the three parts of the hip bone. The body of the ilium forms the superior part of the acetabulum (acetabular roof). Immediately above the acetabulum, the ilium expands to form the wing.

The ilium has two surfaces provide muscular attachment. The inner surface has a concave shape produces the iliac fossa. The external surface has a convex shape.

The ilium has a superior thickened margin known as the iliac crest; it extends anteriorly from a bony prominence known as the anterior superior iliac spine (ASIS) to a posterior bony prominence known as the posterior superior iliac spine (PSIS). There is an indentation on the posteroinferior aspect of the ilium known as the greater sciatic notch; it lies between the posterior inferior iliac spine (above), and the ischial spine (below).

The Pubis:

The Pubis: is the most anterior portion of the hip bone. It consists of:

  1. A body.
  2. A superior ramus.
  3. An inferior ramus (ramus means branch.)

The Pubic body: is located medially, it articulates with the opposite pubic body at the pubic symphysis. Its superior aspect is marked by a rounded thickening known as the pubic crest, which extends laterally as the pubic tubercle.

The Superior pubic ramus: extends laterally from the body to form part of the acetabulum.

The Inferior pubic ramus: projects towards the ischium. Together, the superior and inferior rami enclose part of the Obturator foramen, through which the obturator nerve, artery and vein pass through to reach the lower limb.

The Ischium:

The Ischium forms the posteroinferior part of the hip bone; it is composed of

  1. Ischial body.
  2. Ischial ramus.
  3. Ischial tuberosity.
  4. Ischial spine.

The ischial ramus combines with the inferior pubic ramus to enclose part of the obturator foramen.

The ischial tuberosity is located on the posteroinferior aspect of the ischium, when sitting; the body weight falls on right and left ischial tuberosities.

The ischial spine is a posteromedial projection located near the junction of the superior ramus and body.

üìù Summary

  • The pelvic girdle is formed by the two hip bones, which connect the lower limbs to the axial skeleton (sacrum).
  • Each hip bone is composed of three fused bones: the ilium, ischium, and pubis.
  • The acetabulum is a cup-shaped socket on the lateral surface of the hip bone, where the head of the femur articulates to form the hip joint.
  • The ilium is the largest and most superior part of the hip bone, with features like the iliac crest, iliac spines, and iliac fossa.
  • The ischium forms the posteroinferior part of the hip bone, with features like the ischial tuberosity (the "sitting bone") and ischial spine.
  • The pubis forms the anterior part of the hip bone, and the two pubic bones articulate at the pubic symphysis.

üí° Pro Tip

When studying the hip bone, it can be helpful to think of the ilium as the "wing," the ischium as the "sitting bone," and the pubis as the "front" bone. This can help you remember their relative positions and key features.

  • The shoulder joint is a highly mobile ball-and-socket joint, stabilized by ligaments, muscles, and the glenoid labrum.
  • The elbow joint is a hinge joint, allowing for flexion and extension of the forearm.
  • The radioulnar joints (proximal and distal) allow for pronation and supination of the forearm.
  • The wrist joint is a complex joint involving the radius, ulna, and carpal bones.
  • The pelvic girdle connects the lower limbs to the axial skeleton and provides stability for weight-bearing.
  • The hip bone is composed of the fused ilium, ischium, and pubis, forming the acetabulum for articulation with the femur.
