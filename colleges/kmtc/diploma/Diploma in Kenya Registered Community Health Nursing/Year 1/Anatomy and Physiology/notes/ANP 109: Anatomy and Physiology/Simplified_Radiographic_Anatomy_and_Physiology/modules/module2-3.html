The Thoracic Cage

Thoracic Cage is the thoracic wall skeleton. It consists of Sternum anteriorly, 12 thoracic vertebrae posteriorly and 12 pairs of ribs anchored to the vertebrae (Figure 7.1).

Function of thoracic cage: The main functions of the thoracic cage are as follows:

  1. It supports the thorax and to protect the vital thoracic and abdominal organs,
  2. It provides attachment for upper limbs and supports their weight.
  3. It provides attachment for the muscles that move upper limb and stabilize their position.
  4. It resists the negative pressure generated by the elastic recoil of the lungs during respiration.

üìö Sternum

Sternum: is a flat elongated bone located in the anterior part of the thoracic cage. It is anchored to the rib via costal cartilage. The sternum consists of three parts (Figure 7.2):

  1. The manubrium
  2. The body
  3. The xiphoid process

The manubrium: it is the broad superior part of the sternum, it is quadrangular in shape. It has a superior shallow, U-shaped border called the jugular notch located between the medial ends of the clavicles. The inferior border of the sternum is oval in shape and covered with a thin layer of cartilage for articulation with the body of the sternum at the sternal angle. The lateral borders of the sternum are marked by depression for the first costal cartilages.

The Body of the Sternum: it is the long flat central part of the sternum, directed upward and forward, its superior border joins the manubrium at the sternal angle where the cartilage of the second rib is attached to the sternum. At the lateral border of the body of the sternum there are four angular depressions for the cartilages of the third, fourth, fifth, and sixth ribs. The inferior border of the body of the sternum is narrow, and articulates with the xiphoid process, at this point there is a notch for the cartilage of the seventh rib.

The xiphoid process: it is a small cartilaginous process located at the inferior end of the sternum, which is usually ossified in the adult human.

The sternal angle: also clinically known as the Angle of Louis, it is the point where the body of the sternum joins the manubrium; it is about 160 degrees, it can be felt at the anterior wall of the chest at the farthest point of sternal projects, there are two lateral depressions at sternal angle for the attachment of the second pair of costal cartilages, that is why it can be used as a land mark for the second rib. The sternal angle is located at the level of the inferior border of T4 (Figures 7.3& 7.4).

Ribs and Costal cartilages

ribs are long bones, flattened and curved. There are twelve pairs of ribs in the thoracic cage; they connect the sternum anteriorly to the upper ten thoracic vertebrae posteriorly.

Function of ribs: As part of the thoracic, the ribs protect the vital thoracic and part of the abdominal organs. They also play a role in breathing, as they move to permit lung inflation during thoracic expansion.

Rib Classifications: there are two types of rib classification:

  1. According to their anatomical features ribs are classified into: Typical and Atypical ribs.
  2. According to their articulation, location and their costal cartilage ribs are classified into: True, Floating and False ribs.

Typical Ribs include from 3rd up to 9th ribs: A typical rib is mainly composed of: head, neck and body. The head of a typical rib is wedge shaped with two articular facets separated by crest, the inferior facet articulates with the numerically corresponding vertebrae and the superior facet articulates with the vertebrae above. The neck of a typical rib connects the head with the body at a roughed tubercle, with a facet for articulation with the transverse process of the corresponding vertebrae. The body of a typical rib is thin, flat and curved, most markedly at the costal angle where the rib turns anterolaterally; there is a groove in internal surface of the body of a typical rib for the protection of the neurovascular supply of the thorax (Figure 7.5).

üìù Summary

  • The thoracic cage is composed of the sternum, 12 pairs of ribs, and 12 thoracic vertebrae. It protects thoracic organs and plays a role in breathing.
  • The sternum, or breastbone, consists of the manubrium, body, and xiphoid process. The sternal angle is a landmark for the second rib.
  • Ribs are classified as typical or atypical based on their anatomy, and as true, false, or floating based on their anterior attachments.

Atypical Ribs and Costal Cartilages

Atypical Ribs are the 1st, 2nd, 10th and 12th ribs: Atypical are characterized with the following features:

1st Rib: is the shortest rib, it is flat and wider than other ribs. It has a small rounded head with one facet for articulation with its corresponding vertebrae. The superior and lateral surfaces are indented by two grooves for the subclavian vessels (Figure 7.6).

2nd Rib: the second rib is longer than the first rib and thinner, it has two articular facets and a tubercle on its superior surface for the attachment of Serratus anterior muscle (Figure 7.7).

10th, 11th and 12th Ribs: are directly attached to their numerically corresponding vertebrae with single facet for articulation (Figure 7.8).

The costal cartilages: There are 12 pairs of costal cartilage form part of the anterior wall of the thoracic cage. They connect the ribs to the sternum and help in extending the forward motion of the ribs. They also contribute to thoracic elasticity within by allowing chest expand during respiration. The upper seven pairs of costal cartilage are directly connected to the sternum, while the next lower three pairs are connected with the next upper ribs, and the last two pairs of costal cartilage are pointed, ending in the abdominal wall.

üìù Summary

  • Atypical ribs (1st, 2nd, 10th, 11th, and 12th) have unique features that distinguish them from typical ribs.
  • The 1st rib is the shortest, widest, and most curved, with a single articular facet on its head.
  • The 2nd rib is longer and thinner than the 1st, with two articular facets and a tuberosity for the serratus anterior muscle.
  • The 10th, 11th, and 12th ribs each have a single articular facet on their heads.
  • Costal cartilages are bars of hyaline cartilage that connect the ribs to the sternum (true ribs) or to each other (false ribs), or end in the abdominal wall (floating ribs). They contribute to the elasticity and mobility of the thoracic cage.

üí° Pro Tip

Pay close attention to the differences between typical and atypical ribs. Knowing these variations can help you identify individual ribs on radiographs and understand their unique biomechanical roles.

üìö Key Takeaways

  • The thoracic cage is a bony and cartilaginous structure that protects the thoracic organs and plays a crucial role in breathing.
  • The sternum consists of the manubrium, body, and xiphoid process. The sternal angle is an important clinical landmark.
