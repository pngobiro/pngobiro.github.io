Module 2: Regional Terms, Body Cavities and Serous Membranes

üéØ Learning Objectives

  • Define regional terms and describe the major regions of the body.
  • Identify and describe the three primary planes of section.
  • Describe the major body cavities and their subdivisions.
  • Explain the function of serous membranes.
  • Identify the layers of serous membranes and the spaces they form.

üìö Regional Terms

The body can be divided into two regions: axial (head, neck, and trunk) and appendicular (upper and lower limbs or appendages).

üìù Summary

  • The axial region includes the head, neck, and trunk.
  • The appendicular region includes the upper and lower limbs.

üìö Three Primary Planes of Section

Planes of section are a means of studying the form and function of a body region by dividing the body or a body part for examination:

Sagittal plane: Divides the body or body part into right and left sections.

  • Midsagittal plane (median plane): Divides the body or body part into equal left and right regions.
  • Parasagittal plane: Divides the body or body part into unequal right and left sections.

Frontal plane (coronal plane): Divides the body or body part into anterior and posterior sections.

Transverse plane (horizontal plane): Divides the body or body part into superior and inferior sections or proximal and distal sections for the appendicular region.

üìù Summary

  • Sagittal plane divides into right and left.
  • Midsagittal divides into equal right and left.
  • Parasagittal divides into unequal right and left.
  • Frontal (coronal) plane divides into anterior and posterior.
  • Transverse (horizontal) plane divides into superior and inferior.

üìö Body Cavities and Serous Membranes

A cavity is any fluid-filled space within the body. The axial region of the body is divided into several cavities. These cavities protect internal organs and allow them to move and expand as necessary to function.

Two major cavities: Posterior and anterior with their subdivisions.

Dorsal Body Cavity: Largely located on the posterior side of the body, it is subdivided into two parts: the cranial cavity (within the skull, protects the brain) and the vertebral (spinal) cavity (within the vertebral column, protects the spinal cord). These two cavities are continuous and filled with cerebrospinal fluid (CSF).

Ventral Body Cavity: Separated into two divisions by the diaphragm:

  • Thoracic Cavity: Divided into three smaller cavities:
    • Pleural cavities: Each surrounds either the left or right lung.
    • Mediastinum: Found between pleural cavities, houses the heart, great vessels, and the trachea/esophagus; it is not located within a serous membrane.
    • Pericardial cavity: Found within the mediastinum, is a serous membrane that surrounds the heart.
  • Abdominopelvic Cavity: Subdivided into the superior abdominal cavity (spans from the diaphragm to the bony pelvis) and the pelvic cavity (within the bony pelvis). The abdominal cavity contains organs from several systems, including the digestive, lymphatic, reproductive, and urinary. The peritoneal cavity is an abdominal subcavity found within a serous membrane. The abdominopelvic cavity can be divided into segments or quadrants using imaginary lines that cross the umbilicus, which divides the cavity into right upper, right lower, left upper, and left lower quadrants. It can also be divided into nine segments using two parasagittal and two transverse imaginary lines: right and left hypochondriac, epigastric, right and left lumbar, umbilical, right and left iliac (inguinal), and hypogastric regions.

Serous Membranes: Formed by thin sheets of tissue that fold over themselves to create a continuous double-layer structure with space between their layers. These membranes form certain cavities found in the ventral cavity and surround the heart, lungs, and many abdominal organs. Serous fluid is found within the cavity between the two membrane layers; it is an extremely thin layer of fluid that prevents friction from the movement of organs.

Visceral layer: The layer of the serous membrane that is in contact with the underlying organ. The outermost layer is called the parietal layer.

Pleural membranes: Consist of the outer parietal pleura (follows the contours of the thoracic wall) and the inner visceral pleura (runs along the surface of the lungs). The thin space enclosed by the pleural membranes forms the pleural cavities.

Pericardial membranes: Consist of the outer parietal pericardium (separates the heart from the mediastinum) and the inner visceral pericardium (lies directly on the heart muscle). The space created by the pericardial membrane forms the pericardial cavity.

Peritoneal membranes: Surrounding some of the abdominal organs, consists of the outer parietal peritoneum and the inner visceral peritoneum. The space between these layers forms the peritoneal cavity. The peritoneal cavity is extensive but does not cover every organ. The kidneys lie outside of and behind the peritoneal peritoneum and are therefore called retroperitoneal organs.

üìù Summary

  • Body cavities are fluid-filled spaces that protect and allow movement of organs.
  • The dorsal cavity includes the cranial and vertebral cavities.
  • The ventral cavity includes the thoracic and abdominopelvic cavities.
  • The thoracic cavity contains the pleural cavities (lungs) and the pericardial cavity (heart).
  • The abdominopelvic cavity contains the abdominal and pelvic cavities.
  • Serous membranes are double-layered membranes that line body cavities and surround organs.
  • The visceral layer is next to the organ, and the parietal layer is the outer layer.
  • Serous fluid between the layers reduces friction.
