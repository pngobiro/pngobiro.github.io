Module 4: Isotopes, Radioactivity, Mixtures, Chemical Bonds

üéØ Learning Objectives

  • Define isotope and mass number.
  • Explain radioactivity and radioisotopes.
  • Define mixture and describe the three types of mixtures.
  • Differentiate between a molecule and a compound.
  • Explain how chemical bonds are formed.
  • Differentiate between ionic and covalent bonds.

üìö Isotopes and Radioactivity

The mass number of an atom is equal to the sum of all the protons and neutrons found in the atomic nucleus.

An isotope is an atom with the same atomic number (same number of protons) but a different mass number (different number of neutrons).

Radioisotopes are unstable isotopes with high energy or radiation that can be released by radioactive decay. This allows the isotope to assume a more stable form.

üìù Summary

  • Mass number = protons + neutrons.
  • Isotopes have the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons.
  • Radioisotopes are unstable isotopes that undergo radioactive decay.

üìö Mixtures

A mixture is formed when atoms of two or more elements are physically intermixed without changing the chemical nature of the atoms. There are three types of mixtures:

Suspensions: A mixture containing two or more components. The components are usually large and unevenly distributed. They will settle out when left undisturbed.

Colloids: A mixture containing two or more components. The components are small, evenly distributed, and will not settle out.

Solutions: A mixture containing two or more components. The components are extremely small, evenly distributed, and will not settle out. A solution consists of a solute (the substance that is dissolved) and a solvent (the substance that dissolves the solute).

üìù Summary

  • Mixtures involve physically intermixed atoms or molecules.
  • Suspensions have large, unevenly distributed components that settle out.
  • Colloids have small, evenly distributed components that do not settle out.
  • Solutions have very small, evenly distributed components (solute and solvent) that do not settle out.

üìö Chemical Bonds

Matter can be combined chemically to form a molecule, which is when atoms of two or more elements are combined by forming chemical bonds. A chemical bond is not a physical structure but rather an energy relationship.

A molecule is formed by the chemical bonding between two or more atoms of the same element. A compound is formed when two or more atoms from different elements are combined by chemical bonding.

Very large molecules composed of many atoms are called macromolecules.

Molecular formulas are a way to represent molecules symbolically with letters and numbers to show the kinds and numbers of atoms in a molecule.

Chemical bonds are formed when valence electrons in the valence shell (the outermost electron shell) of atoms interact.

Valence electrons determine how an atom interacts with other atoms and whether it will form bonds with a specific atom.

Octet rule: An atom is most stable when there are 8 electrons in its valence shell.

Duet rule: An atom is most stable when its first electron shell holds 2 electrons.

üìù Summary

  • Molecules are formed when atoms are chemically bonded.
  • A molecule can be made of atoms of the same element.
  • A compound is made of atoms of different elements.
  • Macromolecules are very large molecules.
  • Molecular formulas show the types and numbers of atoms in a molecule.
  • Chemical bonds involve interactions between valence electrons.
  • The octet rule states that atoms are stable with 8 electrons in the valence shell (except for the first shell, which is stable with 2 - duet rule).

üìö Ionic and Covalent Bonds

Ionic bonds are formed when electrons are transferred from a metal atom to a nonmetal atom. This results in the formation of ions: cations (positively charged ions that have lost one or more electrons) and anions (negatively charged ions that have gained one or more electrons). The attraction between opposite charges holds or bonds the atoms to one another, forming a compound called a salt.

Covalent bonds, the strongest type of chemical bond, are formed when two or more nonmetals share electrons between themselves. Two atoms can share one (single bond), two (double bond), or three (triple bond) electron pairs.

üìù Summary

  • Ionic bonds involve the transfer of electrons between a metal and a nonmetal.
  • Ionic bonds form cations (+) and anions (-).
  • The attraction between opposite charges forms the ionic bond.
  • Covalent bonds involve the sharing of electrons between nonmetals.
  • Covalent bonds can be single, double, or triple.
