Module 3: Homeostasis and Feedback loops

üéØ Learning Objectives

  • Define homeostasis.
  • Explain the role of feedback loops in maintaining homeostasis.
  • Differentiate between negative and positive feedback loops.
  • Provide examples of negative and positive feedback loops in the human body.
  • Define gradient and provide examples of different types of gradients.

üìö Homeostasis

Homeostasis: The maintenance of the body's stable internal environment. It is a dynamic process, meaning it is constantly adjusting. It involves feedback loops, the relationship between structure and function, gradients, and cell-to-cell communication.

üìù Summary

  • Homeostasis is the maintenance of a stable internal environment.
  • It is a dynamic process that involves constant adjustments.

üìö Negative Feedback Loops

Negative feedback loops: One of the primary mechanisms for the maintenance of homeostasis. They oppose the initial change in a regulated variable and reduce the output. When a regulated variable is outside of its set point, information (stimulus) is detected by receptors in the control center. The control center then sends signals to cells and organs (effectors) to cause physiological responses that return the variable to the set point.

üìù Summary

  • Negative feedback loops oppose the initial change and reduce output.
  • They involve a stimulus, receptor, control center, effector, and response.
  • They help maintain homeostasis by returning variables to their set point.

üìö Positive Feedback Loops

Positive feedback loops: Less common than negative feedback loops. They contribute to the maintenance of homeostasis. Effector activity increases and reinforces the initial stimulus and shuts off when conditions return to the normal range.

üìù Summary

  • Positive feedback loops reinforce the initial stimulus.
  • They are less common than negative feedback loops.
  • They shut off when conditions return to normal.

üìö Gradients

A gradient is present any time more of something exists in one area than another where the two areas are connected. Gradients drive many physiological processes.

Examples:

  • Temperature gradient: Exists when there is a temperature difference between two connected regions.
  • Concentration gradient: Exists where there is a concentration difference between two connected regions.
  • Pressure gradient: Exists when there is a pressure difference between two connected regions.

üìù Summary

  • A gradient is a difference in the amount of something between two connected areas.
  • Gradients drive many physiological processes.
  • Examples include temperature, concentration, and pressure gradients.

  2. B) Negative Feedback Loop

Question 2: What is an example of a gradient in the human body?

  1. A) Temperature difference between the body core and the skin.
  2. B) Concentration difference of sodium ions inside and outside of a cell.
  3. C) Pressure difference between the inside of the lungs and the atmosphere.
  4. D) All of the above
View Answer

D) All of the above

Question 3: What is the role of an effector in a feedback loop?

  1. A) To detect the stimulus.
  2. B) To process information and determine the response.
  3. C) To carry out the response that brings the variable back to the set point.
  4. D) To set the normal range for the variable.

C) To carry out the response that brings the variable back to the set point.
