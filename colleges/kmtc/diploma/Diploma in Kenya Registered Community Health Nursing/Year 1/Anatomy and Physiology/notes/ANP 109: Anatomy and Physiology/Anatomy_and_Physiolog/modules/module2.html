Module 2: Types of Anatomy and Physiology, Anatomical Position and Directional Terms

üéØ Learning Objectives

  • Differentiate between systemic and regional anatomy.
  • Differentiate between gross and microscopic anatomy.
  • Define neurophysiology and cardiovascular physiology.
  • Describe the anatomical position.
  • Use directional terms to describe the location of body structures.

üìö Types of Anatomy and Physiology

The study of the human body can be approached in the following ways:

Systemic Anatomy: (Used in textbooks) Examines the human body by individual organ systems.

Regional Anatomy: Divides the body into regions of study (such as the head and neck).

Surface Anatomy: Studies surface markings of the body.

Gross Anatomy: Examines structures that can be seen with the unaided eye.

Microscopic Anatomy: Studies structures that can only be seen with the aid of a microscope. This includes histology (the study of tissues) and cytology (the study of cells).

Physiology: Subfields are classified by organ or organ system being studied:

  • Neurophysiology: Studies the brain and nerves.
  • Cardiovascular Physiology: Studies the heart and blood vessels.

üìù Summary

  • Systemic anatomy studies individual organ systems.
  • Regional anatomy divides the body into regions.
  • Surface anatomy studies surface markings.
  • Gross anatomy examines structures visible to the naked eye.
  • Microscopic anatomy studies structures requiring a microscope.
  • Neurophysiology studies the brain and nerves.
  • Cardiovascular physiology studies the heart and blood vessels.

üìö Anatomical Position and Directional Terms

Anatomical Position: A common frame of reference from which all body parts and regions are described. In anatomical position, the body is standing upright, feet are shoulder-width apart, with upper limbs at the sides of the trunk, and the head and palms are facing forward.

The body is always referred to as if it were in anatomical position, even if it is in another position.

"Right" and "left" always refer to the sides of the body being described, not our own.

Directional Terms: Help ensure accurate communication and describe the relative location of body parts and markings. Common paired terms include:

  • Anterior (ventral): The front of the human body or body part. Posterior (dorsal) refers to the back.
  • Superior (cranial): Toward the head. Inferior (caudal) means towards the tail. These terms are used to refer to positions on the head, neck, and trunk only.
  • Proximal: Closer to the point of origin. Distal means more distant (further away) from the same point of origin.
  • The body's midline is an imaginary line that runs down the middle of the body. Medial means closer to the midline, while lateral means further away.
  • Superficial: Structures closer to the surface of the body. Deep means further away from the surface.

üìù Summary

  • Anatomical position is a standardized way of describing body position.
  • Directional terms describe the location of body parts relative to each other.
  • Anterior/posterior refer to front/back.
  • Superior/inferior refer to head/tail.
  • Proximal/distal refer to closeness/distance from the point of origin.
  • Medial/lateral refer to closeness/distance from the midline.
  • Superficial/deep refer to closeness/distance from the body surface.
