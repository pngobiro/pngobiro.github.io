Anatomy and Physiology
Multiple Choice Questions

Question 1

Which of the following is NOT a characteristic of living organisms?

Reveal Answer

Answer: c) Inability to respond to stimuli

Reason: Living organisms are characterized by their ability to respond to stimuli in their environment. This is a fundamental characteristic of life, allowing organisms to adapt and survive. Options a), b), and d) are all essential characteristics of living organisms.

Question 2

What level of structural organization consists of groups of similar cells that work together to perform a specific function?

Reveal Answer

Answer: c) Tissue level

Reason: Tissues are groups of similar cells that work together to perform a specific function. For example, muscle tissue is made up of muscle cells that contract to produce movement. The chemical level involves atoms and molecules, the cellular level involves individual cells, and the organ level involves different tissues working together.

Question 3

Which directional term describes a structure that is located closer to the point of attachment of a limb to the trunk?

Reveal Answer

Answer: b) Proximal

Reason: Proximal means closer to the point of origin or attachment. In the context of limbs, it refers to a structure being closer to where the limb attaches to the trunk. Distal is the opposite, meaning farther away from the point of attachment. Lateral means away from the midline, and medial means toward the midline.

Question 4

The serous membrane that directly covers the lungs is called the:

Reveal Answer

Answer: b) Visceral pleura

Reason: The visceral pleura is the serous membrane that directly covers the lungs. The parietal pleura lines the thoracic cavity wall. The pericardium is the serous membrane that surrounds the heart.

Question 5

Which type of feedback mechanism amplifies the initial stimulus, moving the system further away from the set point?

Reveal Answer

Answer: b) Positive feedback

Reason: Positive feedback mechanisms amplify the initial stimulus, driving the system further away from the set point until a specific event occurs to stop the process. Negative feedback, on the other hand, opposes the initial change and returns the system to the set point. Feedforward is a type of control where the body anticipates a change and prepares for it. Homeostatic regulation is a general term for the processes that maintain stability in the body.

Question 6

What is the primary function of the mitochondria within a cell?

Reveal Answer

Answer: b) Energy production (ATP synthesis)

Reason: Mitochondria are often referred to as the "powerhouses" of the cell because they are the primary site of ATP (adenosine triphosphate) synthesis. ATP is the cell's main energy currency. While other organelles are involved in protein synthesis (ribosomes), lipid synthesis (endoplasmic reticulum), and waste removal (lysosomes), mitochondria are specialized for energy production.

Question 7

Which type of chemical bond involves the sharing of electrons between atoms?

Reveal Answer

Answer: b) Covalent bond

Reason: Covalent bonds are formed when atoms share one or more pairs of electrons. This type of bond is typically strong and is common in organic molecules. Ionic bonds involve the transfer of electrons and the attraction between oppositely charged ions. Hydrogen bonds are weaker attractions between polar molecules. Van der Waals forces are very weak attractions between molecules due to temporary fluctuations in electron distribution.

Question 8

Which of the following is an example of a polysaccharide?

Reveal Answer

Answer: c) Glycogen

Reason: Glycogen is a polysaccharide, which is a large carbohydrate molecule made up of many monosaccharide units (glucose in this case). It serves as the primary storage form of glucose in animals. Glucose and fructose are monosaccharides (simple sugars), and sucrose is a disaccharide (composed of two monosaccharides).

Question 9

What is the function of a buffer in a biological system?

Reveal Answer

Answer: c) To maintain a stable pH

Reason: Buffers are substances that help resist changes in pH when an acid or base is added to a solution. They are crucial in biological systems for maintaining the pH within a narrow range that is optimal for enzyme activity and other cellular processes.

Question 10

Which type of lipid is a major component of cell membranes and has a hydrophilic head and hydrophobic tails?

Reveal Answer

Answer: b) Phospholipids

Reason: Phospholipids are amphipathic molecules, meaning they have both a hydrophilic (water-loving) head and hydrophobic (water-fearing) tails. This property allows them to form the lipid bilayer of cell membranes, with the hydrophilic heads facing the aqueous environment and the hydrophobic tails facing each other.

Question 11

Which of the following is NOT a function of proteins in the human body?

Reveal Answer

Answer: c) Storing genetic information

Reason: Proteins have diverse functions in the body, including acting as enzymes, transporters, and structural components. However, the primary molecule for storing genetic information is DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid), not protein.

Question 12

What is the process by which water moves across a selectively permeable membrane from a region of higher water concentration to a region of lower water concentration?

Reveal Answer

Answer: b) Osmosis

Reason: Osmosis is the specific term for the movement of water across a selectively permeable membrane from a region of higher water concentration (lower solute concentration) to a region of lower water concentration (higher solute concentration). It is a passive process driven by the difference in water potential.

Question 13

What type of solution would cause a red blood cell to swell and potentially burst?

Reveal Answer

Answer: c) Hypotonic solution

Reason: A hypotonic solution has a lower solute concentration than the inside of the cell. This causes water to move into the cell, leading to swelling and potentially lysis (bursting). An isotonic solution has the same solute concentration as the cell, so there is no net movement of water. A hypertonic solution has a higher solute concentration, causing water to move out of the cell and the cell to shrink.

Question 14

Which transport process requires energy in the form of ATP to move substances against their concentration gradient?

Reveal Answer

Answer: d) Active transport

Reason: Active transport requires the input of energy, usually in the form of ATP, to move substances against their concentration gradient (from low to high concentration). Simple diffusion, facilitated diffusion, and osmosis are all passive processes that do not require cellular energy.

Question 15

What is the function of the sodium-potassium pump in animal cells?

Reveal Answer

Answer: b) To actively transport sodium ions out of the cell and potassium ions into the cell

Reason: The sodium-potassium pump is an example of primary active transport. It uses ATP to move sodium ions out of the cell and potassium ions into the cell, both against their concentration gradients. This creates and maintains the electrochemical gradients that are essential for nerve impulse transmission and other cellular functions.

Question 16

Which division of the nervous system is responsible for the "fight-or-flight" response?

Reveal Answer

Answer: c) Sympathetic nervous system

Reason: The sympathetic nervous system is a division of the autonomic nervous system that prepares the body for "fight-or-flight" responses in stressful situations. It increases heart rate, breathing rate, and blood flow to muscles, among other effects. The parasympathetic nervous system is responsible for "rest-and-digest" functions. The somatic nervous system controls voluntary movements, and the central nervous system is the brain and spinal cord.

Question 17

Which part of a neuron receives signals from other neurons?

Reveal Answer

Answer: b) Dendrite

Reason: Dendrites are branched extensions of a neuron that receive signals from other neurons or sensory receptors. The axon transmits signals away from the cell body, the cell body (soma) is the main part of the neuron containing the nucleus, and the myelin sheath is an insulating layer around some axons.

Question 18

What is the function of myelin in the nervous system?

Reveal Answer

Answer: b) To increase the speed of action potential conduction

Reason: Myelin is a fatty substance that forms an insulating sheath around some axons. This insulation allows action potentials to "jump" between the nodes of Ranvier (gaps in the myelin sheath), significantly increasing the speed of conduction. This is called saltatory conduction.

Question 19

What is the resting membrane potential of a neuron primarily determined by?

Reveal Answer

Answer: b) The concentration gradient of potassium ions

Reason: The resting membrane potential is primarily determined by the equilibrium potential for potassium ions (K+), which is influenced by the concentration gradient of K+ across the membrane and the membrane's relatively high permeability to K+ at rest. The sodium-potassium pump helps maintain these gradients, but the K+ gradient has the most significant influence on the resting potential.

Question 20

What happens during the depolarization phase of an action potential?

Reveal Answer

Answer: b) Sodium ions move into the cell

Reason: During depolarization, voltage-gated sodium channels open, allowing sodium ions (Na+) to rapidly enter the cell. This influx of positive charge makes the inside of the membrane more positive, causing the membrane potential to move towards zero and even become positive.

Question 21

Which neurotransmitter is primarily involved in muscle contraction?

Reveal Answer

Answer: c) Acetylcholine

Reason: Acetylcholine (ACh) is the primary neurotransmitter at the neuromuscular junction, where it is released by motor neurons to stimulate muscle contraction. Dopamine is involved in reward and motivation, serotonin in mood regulation, and GABA is an inhibitory neurotransmitter.

Question 22

What is the primary function of the cerebellum?

Reveal Answer

Answer: b) Coordination of movements, balance, and posture

Reason: The cerebellum plays a crucial role in coordinating voluntary movements, maintaining balance and posture, and motor learning. It receives input from sensory systems, the cerebral cortex, and other brain areas to fine-tune movements and make them smooth and accurate.

Question 23

Which lobe of the cerebral cortex is primarily responsible for processing visual information?

Reveal Answer

Answer: d) Occipital lobe

Reason: The occipital lobe, located at the back of the brain, is the primary visual processing center. It receives and interprets information from the eyes, allowing us to see and recognize objects.

Question 24

What is the function of the hypothalamus?

Reveal Answer

Answer: d) Maintain homeostasis by regulating body temperature, hunger, thirst, and sleep-wake cycles

Reason: The hypothalamus is a small but crucial region of the brain that plays a vital role in maintaining homeostasis. It acts as a control center for many autonomic functions, including body temperature, hunger, thirst, sleep-wake cycles, and the release of hormones from the pituitary gland. While it influences basic drives and emotions, its primary function is homeostatic regulation.

Question 25

What is the main function of the hormone insulin?

Reveal Answer

Answer: b) Decrease blood glucose levels

Reason: Insulin is a hormone produced by the pancreas that lowers blood glucose levels. It promotes the uptake of glucose by cells, particularly muscle and fat cells, and stimulates the storage of glucose as glycogen in the liver. Glucagon has the opposite effect, increasing blood glucose levels.

Question 26

Which of the following is NOT a function of the skeletal system?

Reveal Answer

Answer: d) Generate heat through shivering

Reason: While muscle contraction, including shivering, generates heat, this is a function of the muscular system, not the skeletal system. The skeletal system provides support and protection, produces blood cells in the bone marrow, and stores minerals.

Question 27

What is the functional unit of a skeletal muscle fiber?

Reveal Answer

Answer: b) Sarcomere

Reason: The sarcomere is the basic contractile unit of a skeletal muscle fiber. It is the repeating unit between two Z lines and contains the myofilaments (actin and myosin) that slide past each other during muscle contraction. Myofibrils are bundles of myofilaments, and sarcoplasm is the cytoplasm of a muscle cell.

Question 28

What is the role of calcium ions in muscle contraction?

Reveal Answer

Answer: b) To bind to troponin, causing a conformational change that exposes the myosin-binding sites on actin

Reason: Calcium ions released from the sarcoplasmic reticulum bind to troponin, a protein associated with tropomyosin on the actin filament. This binding causes a conformational change in troponin, which moves tropomyosin away from the myosin-binding sites on actin, allowing the myosin heads to bind and initiate contraction.

Question 29

Which type of muscle tissue is under voluntary control?

Reveal Answer

Answer: a) Skeletal muscle

Reason: Skeletal muscle is the only type of muscle tissue that is under conscious, voluntary control. It is responsible for movements of the skeleton. Smooth muscle and cardiac muscle are under involuntary control, regulated by the autonomic nervous system and hormones.

Question 30

What is the primary function of the respiratory system?

Reveal Answer

Answer: c) To exchange oxygen and carbon dioxide between the air and the blood

Reason: The primary function of the respiratory system is gas exchange – taking in oxygen from the air and releasing carbon dioxide from the blood. This occurs in the alveoli of the lungs. While the circulatory system transports gases and nutrients, and the urinary system removes waste products, these are not the primary functions of the respiratory system.

Question 31

Which of the following is NOT a component of the upper respiratory tract?

Reveal Answer

Answer: d) Trachea

Reason: The trachea (windpipe) is part of the lower respiratory tract. The upper respiratory tract consists of the nose, pharynx (throat), and larynx (voice box).

Question 32

What is the function of the diaphragm in breathing?

Reveal Answer

Answer: c) To contract and relax, changing the volume of the thoracic cavity and causing inhalation and exhalation

Reason: The diaphragm is the primary muscle of respiration. When it contracts, it flattens and increases the volume of the thoracic cavity, causing inhalation. When it relaxes, it returns to its dome shape, decreasing the volume of the thoracic cavity and causing exhalation.

Question 33

Where does gas exchange primarily occur in the lungs?

Reveal Answer

Answer: c) Alveoli

Reason: The alveoli are tiny air sacs in the lungs where the exchange of oxygen and carbon dioxide between the air and the blood takes place. They have a very large surface area and thin walls, which facilitate diffusion.

Question 34

What is the main function of the cardiovascular system?

Reveal Answer

Answer: b) To transport blood, which carries oxygen, nutrients, hormones, and other substances, to and from the tissues

Reason: The primary function of the cardiovascular system is to circulate blood throughout the body. Blood acts as a transport medium, delivering oxygen, nutrients, hormones, and other essential substances to the tissues and removing waste products like carbon dioxide.

Question 35

Which chamber of the heart receives oxygenated blood from the lungs?

Reveal Answer

Answer: c) Left atrium

Reason: Oxygenated blood returning from the lungs enters the left atrium through the pulmonary veins. The right atrium receives deoxygenated blood from the body. The ventricles pump blood out of the heart.

Question 36

What is the function of the heart valves?

Reveal Answer

Answer: b) To prevent the backflow of blood

Reason: Heart valves ensure that blood flows in one direction through the heart. They open and close in response to pressure changes, preventing blood from flowing back into the previous chamber.

Question 37

Which type of blood vessel carries blood away from the heart?

Reveal Answer

Answer: a) Arteries

Reason: Arteries carry blood away from the heart, typically oxygenated blood to the body tissues (except for the pulmonary arteries, which carry deoxygenated blood to the lungs). Veins carry blood toward the heart. Capillaries are the smallest blood vessels and are the site of nutrient and waste exchange. Venules are small veins that collect blood from capillaries.

Question 38

What is the primary function of red blood cells?

Reveal Answer

Answer: b) To transport oxygen

Reason: Red blood cells, also known as erythrocytes, contain hemoglobin, a protein that binds to oxygen and allows for its transport from the lungs to the body tissues. White blood cells fight infection, platelets are involved in blood clotting, and antibodies are produced by certain white blood cells.

Question 39

What is the function of the lymphatic system?

Reveal Answer

Answer: b) To filter and return interstitial fluid to the bloodstream, and to play a role in immunity

Reason: The lymphatic system is a network of vessels and nodes that collects excess interstitial fluid (lymph), filters it through lymph nodes, and returns it to the bloodstream. It also plays a crucial role in the immune system by housing and transporting lymphocytes (white blood cells).

Question 40

Which of the following is NOT a function of the digestive system?

Reveal Answer

Answer: d) Production of hormones that regulate blood glucose levels

Reason: While the pancreas, an accessory organ of the digestive system, produces hormones like insulin and glucagon that regulate blood glucose, this is considered an endocrine function, not a primary digestive function. The main functions of the digestive system are ingestion, digestion, absorption, and elimination of waste.

Question 41

Where does the majority of nutrient absorption occur in the digestive tract?

Reveal Answer

Answer: b) Small intestine

Reason: The small intestine is the primary site of nutrient absorption. It has a large surface area due to the presence of villi and microvilli, which greatly increase the absorptive capacity.

Question 42

What is the function of bile in digestion?

Reveal Answer

Answer: b) To emulsify fats

Reason: Bile, produced by the liver and stored in the gallbladder, emulsifies fats. This means it breaks down large fat globules into smaller droplets, increasing their surface area for digestion by lipases (fat-digesting enzymes).

Question 43

Which organ produces urine?

Reveal Answer

Answer: b) Kidneys

Reason: The kidneys are the organs responsible for filtering blood and producing urine. They remove waste products, regulate blood volume and pressure, and maintain electrolyte balance.

Question 44

What is the functional unit of the kidney?

Reveal Answer

Answer: a) Nephron

Reason: The nephron is the functional unit of the kidney, responsible for filtering blood and producing urine. Each kidney contains about a million nephrons. The glomerulus is a part of the nephron, the renal pelvis is where urine collects, and the ureter carries urine from the kidney to the bladder.

Question 45

Which hormone regulates water reabsorption in the kidneys?

Reveal Answer

Answer: c) Antidiuretic hormone (ADH)

Reason: Antidiuretic hormone (ADH), also known as vasopressin, regulates water reabsorption in the collecting ducts of the nephrons. It increases the permeability of the collecting ducts to water, allowing more water to be reabsorbed back into the blood, thus concentrating the urine.

Question 46

Which of the following is NOT a function of the endocrine system?

Reveal Answer

Answer: c) Produce rapid, short-lived responses to stimuli

Reason: The endocrine system typically produces slower, longer-lasting responses to stimuli compared to the nervous system. It uses hormones, chemical messengers that travel through the bloodstream, to regulate various bodily functions. Rapid, short-lived responses are more characteristic of the nervous system.

Question 47

Which gland is often called the "master gland" of the endocrine system because it controls the activity of many other endocrine glands?

Reveal Answer

Answer: c) Pituitary gland

Reason: The pituitary gland, located at the base of the brain, is often referred to as the "master gland" because it produces hormones that regulate the activity of many other endocrine glands in the body. It is, however, itself under the control of the hypothalamus.

Question 48

What is the primary function of the hormone epinephrine (adrenaline)?

Reveal Answer

Answer: b) To prepare the body for "fight-or-flight" responses

Reason: Epinephrine (adrenaline), released from the adrenal medulla, is a key hormone in the "fight-or-flight" response. It increases heart rate, blood pressure, blood glucose levels, and redirects blood flow to muscles, preparing the body for action.

Question 49

Which of the following is NOT a function of the male reproductive system?

Reveal Answer

Answer: c) Nourish and protect a developing fetus

Reason: Nourishing and protecting a developing fetus is a function of the female reproductive system (specifically, the uterus). The male reproductive system is responsible for producing sperm (in the testes) and the male sex hormone testosterone, and for delivering sperm to the female reproductive tract.

Question 50

Where does fertilization of the egg by sperm typically occur in the female reproductive system?

Reveal Answer

Answer: b) Fallopian tube (uterine tube)

Reason: Fertilization, the union of sperm and egg, typically occurs in the fallopian tube (also called the uterine tube or oviduct). The egg is released from the ovary and travels through the fallopian tube, where it may encounter sperm. If fertilization occurs, the resulting zygote then travels to the uterus for implantation.
