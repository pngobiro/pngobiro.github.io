FUNDAMENTALS OF ENVIRONMENTAL HEALTH

📚 Terminologies Used in Fundamentals of E.H

  • 📌 Environment- It is a collective term used to describe all the living and nonliving things that make up our surroundings.
  • 📌 Health- A complete state of physical, mental and social wellbeing of an individual and not just merely the absence of disease or infirmity.
  • 📌 Agent- These are living organisms that transmit diseases. Microorganisms that release toxins to environment that causes diseases or death.
  • 📌 Host- A living organism where a parasite lives.
  • 📌 Health promotion- It is an active process for better health, encompassing educational components, behavior change initiatives and environmental organizational and legislative interventions strategies.
  • 📌 Hygiene- The science of health and the study of ways of preserving it, particularly by promoting cleanliness.
  • 📌 Demography- The study of human population in terms of age and sex structure, its distribution and growth.
  • 📌 Public Health- The science and art of preventing disease, prolonging life and promoting health through organized efforts by society, communities and individuals.
  • 📌 Sanitation- This is the means of collecting and disposing of excreta and liquid waste hygienically.
  • 📌 Epidemiology- Is the study of the distribution of diseases, risk factors and determinant of disease in population.
  • 📌 Community diagnosis- Is the process of determining the nature of a disorder(disease) in the society by considering the community signs and symptoms, background, lab-tests or x-ray examination.
  • 📌 Incubation period- It is the interval between exposure to an infection and the appearance of the first signs and symptoms.
  • 📌 Sign- It is an indication of a particular disorder that's detected by a medical expert during examination.
  • 📌 Symptom- An indication of a disease/disorder noticed by the patient.

🏛️ Historical Background of E.H.S

The department of EHS is one of the oldest departments. The first PHO, the then called Sanitary inspector was employed in 1907. The title has undergone several changes to the current PHO and PHT. Currently the department has 4185 certificate holders(PHT's) and diploma(PHO's).

PHT's are deployed to be in charge of public health activities at locations and sublocations while PHO's take divisions and districts.

In some ways public health is a modern concept of human development in science, although it has roots and entiquity from the beginning of human civilization.

Early religions attempted to regulate the behavior that was specifically related to health from the type of food eaten to regulating certain indulgent behaviours such as drinking alcohol or sexual relations.

By Roman times, it was well understood that proper diversion of human waste was a necessary tenet of public health in urban areas. The Chinese developed the practice of variolation following a smallpox epidemic around 1000 BC. An individual without the disease could gain some measures of immunity against it by inhaling the dried crusts that formed around lesions of infected individuals.

💡

Key Historical Milestones

The science of epidemiology was founded by John Snow's identification of a polluted public water well as the source of an 1854 cholera outbreak in London. Dr Snow believed in the Germ theory of disease as opposed to the prevailing Miasma Theory.

The modern era of public health didn't begin until the 1880's with Louis Pasteur's Germ theory and production of artificial vaccines.

Other public health interventions included latrinization, the building of sewers, the regular collection of garbage followed by incineration or disposal in a land field, providing clean water and draining stagnant water to prevent the breeding of mosquitoes.

This contribution was made by Edwin Chadwick in 1843 who published a report on the sanitation of the working class population in Great Britain at the time. So begun the inspection of the modern public health.

🇰🇪 Public Health in Kenya

The first missionary society in Mombasa in 1888 appears to have been the first to undertake medical work in Kenya. Their main concern was the spread of Christianity which they did simultaneously with medical services. Consequently they laid foundation of curative and preventive medicine.

Medical services both in Kenya and Uganda were amalgamated but in 1903 the services were separately developed in the two countries. Active environment work started in Kenya between 1913 and 1920 when the first sanitary inspectors were employed.

In 1920, the first director of Medical Services was appointed to advice the government on medical requirement for the whole country and to plan, execute preventive measures against communicable diseases.

📜 Public Health Ordinance (1921)

In 1921 the first public health ordinance was passed and its basic aims were:

  • ✅Suppression of infectious diseases
  • ✅Abatement of nuisances
  • ✅Sanitation improvement
  • ✅Housing improvement

Further expansion took place between 1940 and 1954, including the establishment of the Medical Training Centre, health centers, and a health education unit.

🏢 Department of Environmental Health and Sanitation (DEHS)

🎯

Goal and Mission

Goal: To improve the status of environment in order to reduce health risks for All Kenyans.

Mission: To provide comprehensive and effective environmental health services for the people of Kenya, ensuring that through appropriate interventions, monitoring, regulation, and enforcement, all can enjoy services which are so far as possible safe and without risk to health or welfare.

Core Functions

Public Health Officers are appointed under the Public Health Act, Cap 242. The department plays a leading role in the prevention and control of preventable diseases through several core functions:

  • ➡️Sanitation and Hygiene Promotion: Coordinating activities, formulating policies, and promoting positive behavior change.
  • ➡️Food Safety and Quality Control: Risk assessment, surveillance of food-borne diseases, and enforcement of safety laws.
  • ➡️Port Health Services: Implementation of International Health Regulations, disease surveillance at points of entry, and screening of passengers.
  • ➡️Occupational Health and Safety: Implementing WHO action plans and providing technical advice to industries.
  • ➡️Water Safety and Quality control: Ensuring public health safety of water sources and promoting water treatment.
  • ➡️Vector and Vermin Control: Coordination of Integrated Vector Management activities and IRS.
  • ➡️Pollution Control and Housing: Management of solid, liquid, and hazardous waste.

🛡️ Levels of Disease Prevention

🔄 The Three Stages of Prevention

  1. Primary Prevention

    Actions taken before a person gets sick. This includes methods aimed at people (e.g., immunization, personal hygiene) and the environment (e.g., safe water supply, vector control).

  2. Secondary Prevention

    Actions taken after a disease has started but before symptoms appear (sub-clinical stage). The process of finding these cases is called screening (e.g., weighing children, contact tracing).

  3. Tertiary Prevention

    Actions taken when symptoms are present. This involves diagnosis, treatment, management, rehabilitation, and disability limitation to arrest the disease process and prevent complications.
