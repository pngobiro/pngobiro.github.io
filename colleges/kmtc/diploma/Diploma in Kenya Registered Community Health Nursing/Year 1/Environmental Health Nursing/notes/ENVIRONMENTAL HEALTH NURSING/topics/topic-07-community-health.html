üëë Leadership and Power

Power
A person's ability to control activities of other individuals.
Leadership
The ability to inspire people to follow instructions voluntarily and manage the completion of a project without exercising force.

Types of Leadership

‚ñ∂ Core Styles
  • 1
    Democratic: Decisions made based on team opinion/feedback. Employees have a voice.
  • 2
    Autocratic: Opposite of democratic. Opinions of employees are not considered; leaders expect adherence to their decisions.
  • 3
    Laissez-faire: "Let them do". Least intrusive; authority lies with employees. Can empower but may limit development.
‚ñ∂ Modern & Organizational Styles
  • 4
    Strategic: Bridge between senior team and employees. Balances executive interests with working conditions.
  • 5
    Transformational: Aims at improving functions/capabilities. Pushes boundaries. Common in growth-minded companies.
  • 6
    Transactional: Based on action-and-reward (e.g., bonuses for targets).
  • 7
    Coach-Style: Focuses on individual strengths and talent within larger growth.
  • 8
    Bureaucratic: Goes by the books. Rejects opinions if they go against company ethos/policy.

‚ö° Sources of Power

Based on French and Raven (1959), power is categorized into Positional and Personal sources.

Positional Power Sources

Type Description Limitations
Legitimate Formal right to make demands (e.g., CEO, Monarch). Unpredictable/unstable; lost if position is lost.
Reward Ability to compensate for compliance (promotions, raises). Weakens if rewards are used up or lack perceived value.
Coercive Ability to punish (fire, demote). Can cause resentment and dissatisfaction.
Informational Control over information others need. Derives from access/manipulation of information.

Personal Power Sources

  • ‚òÖ
    Expert Power: Based on high levels of skill, knowledge, and judgment. People trust and respect the subject matter expert.
  • ‚ô•
    Referent Power: Result of perceived attractiveness, worthiness, and right to respect (e.g., celebrities, likeable leaders). Can be abused if character is lacking.

üè° Rural vs. Urban Communities

Rural Communities

Features include:

  • ‚úì
    Smaller size, sparsely populated.
  • ‚úì
    Homogeneity of language, culture, customs.
  • ‚úì
    Main occupation is agriculture.
  • ‚úì
    Close contact with nature; slower means of communication.

Urban Communities

Regions surrounding cities where inhabitants have non-agricultural jobs. Classification hierarchy:

Classification Description Example
Town Larger than a village; distinct functions like manufacturing/retail. -
City Leading town with complex associative life and commercial functions. -
Million City Population exceeds one million. London (1800), Paris (1850).
Conurbation Large area formed by merging separate towns/cities. Greater London, Tokyo.
Megalopolis "Great city" - union of conurbations. Boston to Washington (USA).
Mega City Population > 10 million. New York (1950).

üöß Problems of Urban Settlements

‚ñ∂ Developing Countries Challenges
  • 1
    Overcrowding & Slums: Unsustainable density, unauthorized settlements (squatter settlements). In Asia Pacific, ~60% of urban population lives in squatter settlements.
  • 2
    Economic Problems: Huge migrant populations create a pool of unskilled workforce; jobs do not keep pace with population growth.
  • 3
    Socio-Cultural Problems: Inadequate social infrastructure (health, education). High crime rates. Skewed sex ratios due to male-selective migration.
  • 4
    Environmental Problems: Waste disposal issues, lack of drinking water, improper sewerage, air pollution from traditional fuels, heat islands from concrete structures.
