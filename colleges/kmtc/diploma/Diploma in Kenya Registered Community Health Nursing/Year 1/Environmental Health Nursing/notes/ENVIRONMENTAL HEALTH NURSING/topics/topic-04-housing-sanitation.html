Health Risks of Poor Housing

Poor housing is associated with a wide range of health conditions including:

  • Respiratory diseases (e.g., asthma, influenza, tuberculosis)
  • Cardiovascular diseases
  • Injuries
  • Mental health issues
  • Infectious diseases (including diarrhea)

5 Principles of a Healthy Home

‚ñ∂ View Principles
  1. 1
    Continuous Fresh Air: Using balanced air management systems to expel damp, stale air and distribute fresh filtered air.
  2. 2
    Proper Sealing & Insulation: Eliminating air leaks through floors/walls/basements to keep pests and bad air out. Proper insulation eliminates cold areas where dampness accumulates.
  3. 3
    Non-Toxic Materials: Reducing exposure to chemicals (VOCs, formaldehyde) by using low-toxic paints, flooring, and avoiding fragranced products.
  4. 4
    Cleanable Surfaces: Maximizing smooth surfaces (replacing carpet) to reduce dust mites and allergens.
  5. 5
    Healthy Home Habits: Using bath fans, kitchen hoods, HEPA vacuums, taking shoes off at the door, and changing filters.

üï≥Ô∏è Conservancy Systems

Conservancy systems are non-water systems where excreta are returned directly to earth or temporarily stored. Common in rural areas without piped water.

‚ñ∂ 1. Pit Latrine

The most common system. Comprised of three components:

  • The Pit: Minimum depth of 5m. Circular pits (1.2m diameter) are stronger than rectangular ones. Should be 30m+ from water sources and 10m+ from dwellings.
  • The Slab: Usually reinforced concrete (mix 1:3:6). Forms the floor with a squat hole/aperture. Should slope towards the aperture for cleaning.
  • Superstructure: Provides privacy and protection from weather. Must be fly-proofed and have sufficient ventilation/lighting.

‚ñ∂ 2. Ventilated Improved Pit (VIP) Latrine

Addresses bad odour and flies found in traditional pit latrines.

Key Feature - The Vent Pipe:

  • Acts as a chimney for warm pit gases to escape.
  • Causes air current: in through squat hole, out through pipe.
  • Must extend >150mm above the roof.
  • Ideally painted black to absorb heat and increase air movement.

Key Feature - The Fly Screen:

  • Fixed at the top of the vent pipe.
  • Prevents flies from entering/escaping.
  • Flies attracted to the light at the top of the pipe are trapped and die.

Types: Single pit, Double pit (alternating use), Multiple pits (schools/markets).

‚ñ∂ 3. Trench Latrine

Description: Shallow trench (600mm deep, 400mm wide). Soil heaped on side for users to cover excreta after use.

Use Case: Temporary settlements or camps (military, construction) for short duration (<1 year).

Disadvantages: Gross pollution of ground/water, attracts flies/vermin, bad odour, unsightly.

‚ñ∂ 4. Bucket Latrine

System: Excreta deposited into a bucket (night soil) which is emptied daily (usually at night).

Design: Galvanized iron bucket placed in a collection chamber with rear access. Superstructure similar to pit latrine.

Disadvantages: Expensive to operate, social stigma for laborers ("night soil operators"), health risk to operators, fly breeding, difficult disposal.

‚ñ∂ 5. Chemical Closet

Mechanism: Excreta deposited into a tank containing water and chemicals (e.g., caustic soda) that liquefy and disinfect the waste.

Use Case: Aircraft, ships, buses, caravans, camps.

Pros/Cons: Hygienic if maintained, but expensive and cannot withstand abuse (only absorbent tissue allowed).

üöΩ Water-Based & Modern Systems

Pour Flush Latrine

üîç Click to zoom

Fig. 4.4: Pour Flush System

Uses a small amount of water (1-3 liters) poured by hand to flush excreta through a water seal trap into a pit.

  • Water Seal: A trap (20mm seal depth) prevents foul gases and insects from entering the latrine.
  • Design: Can be located inside the house. Often uses two pits (alternating use) connected by a discharge pipe.
  • Pros: No odour/flies, low cost, upgradable.
  • Cons: Requires constant water supply, prone to blockage with bulky anal cleaning materials.

Ecological Sanitation (ECOSAN)

Focuses on the reuse of nutrients (resource recovery). Excreta is treated as a resource rather than waste.

Urine Diverting Dry Toilets (UDDT)

Separates urine and feces. Urine is collected/stored and used as fertilizer. Feces are dried/composted and used as soil conditioner or for biogas production.
