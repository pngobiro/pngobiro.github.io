Definitions

Occupational Health
An area of work in public health to promote and maintain the highest degree of physical, mental and social well-being of workers in all occupations.
Occupational Safety (OSH)
A multidisciplinary field concerned with the safety, health, and welfare of people at work. Focuses on primary prevention of hazards.
üìú

Legal Framework

The Occupational Safety and Health Act, 2007 (Revised 2012 CAP 514): An Act of Parliament to provide for the safety, health, and welfare of workers and all persons lawfully present at workplaces.

Core Objectives

  • 1
    Maintenance and promotion of workers' health and working capacity.
  • 2
    Improvement of working conditions to become conducive to safety and health.
  • 3
    Development of work cultures that support health, safety, and positive social climate.

‚ö†Ô∏è The 7 Common Workplace Hazards

Occupational hazards are risks associated with working in specific occupations. They are categorized into seven main types:

‚ñ∂ 1. Safety Hazards

The most common workplace risks affecting employees who work directly with machinery or construction.

  • Spills, trips (cords, ice).
  • Falls from heights (ladders, scaffolds, roofs).
  • Unguarded moving machinery parts.
  • Electrical hazards (frayed cords, improper wiring).
  • Confined spaces.
‚ñ∂ 2. Biological Hazards (Biohazards)

Exposure to harm or disease from working with animals, people, or infectious plant materials.

  • Blood and body fluids.
  • Fungi/mold, Bacteria, Viruses.
  • Insect bites, Animal/bird droppings.
‚ñ∂ 3. Physical Hazards

Factors within the environment that can harm the body without necessarily touching it.

  • Radiation (ionizing, EMFs, microwaves).
  • High exposure to sunlight/UV rays.
  • Temperature extremes (hot/cold).
  • Constant loud noise.
‚ñ∂ 4. Ergonomic Hazards

Occur when the type of work, body positions, and working conditions put a strain on the body. Hardest to spot.

  • Improperly adjusted workstations.
  • Frequent lifting or Poor posture.
  • Repetitive awkward movements.
  • Excessive vibration.
‚ñ∂ 5. Chemical Hazards

Exposure to chemical preparations (solid, liquid, gas).

  • Cleaning products, paints, acids, solvents.
  • Vapors and fumes (welding).
  • Gases (acetylene, propane, carbon monoxide).
  • Pesticides.
‚ñ∂ 6. Work Organization Hazards

Stressors causing stress (short-term) and strain (long-term).

  • Workload demands, high intensity/pace.
  • Workplace violence, sexual harassment.
  • Lack of respect or control/flexibility.
‚ñ∂ 7. Environmental Hazards

Unpredictable factors often related to weather and climate.

  • Extreme temperatures and precipitation.
  • Unstable infrastructure.
  • Pollution (air/chemical).
  • Dangerous animals/violent public members.

‚úÖ HACCP System

Hazard Analysis Critical Control Point (HACCP) is a systematic approach to the identification, evaluation, and control of food safety hazards.

The 7 Basic Principles

  1. 1
    Conduct a Hazard Analysis: Identify hazards (biological, chemical, physical) reasonably likely to cause injury.
  2. 2
    Determine Critical Control Points (CCPs): Steps where control can be applied to prevent/eliminate hazards.
  3. 3
    Establish Critical Limits: Max/min values (temp, time, pH) to which a parameter must be controlled.
  4. 4
    Establish Monitoring Procedures: Planned sequence of observations to assess if a CCP is under control.
  5. 5
    Establish Corrective Actions: Procedures to follow when a deviation occurs.
  6. 6
    Establish Verification Procedures: Activities (other than monitoring) to determine validity of the plan.
  7. 7
    Establish Record-Keeping Procedures: Documentation of the plan, monitoring, and corrective actions.

Key Definitions

Control Point Any step at which biological, chemical, or physical factors can be controlled.
Critical Control Point (CCP) A step essential to prevent or eliminate a food safety hazard.
Critical Limit A criterion that separates acceptability from unacceptability (e.g., Min Temp 155¬∞F).
Deviation Failure to meet a critical limit.
HACCP Plan The written document based on HACCP principles.

Preliminary Tasks

Before applying the principles, the team must:

  • 1
    Assemble the HACCP Team.
  • 2
    Describe the food and its distribution.
  • 3
    Describe the intended use and consumers.
  • 4
    Develop a flow diagram describing the process.
  • 5
    Verify the flow diagram on-site.
