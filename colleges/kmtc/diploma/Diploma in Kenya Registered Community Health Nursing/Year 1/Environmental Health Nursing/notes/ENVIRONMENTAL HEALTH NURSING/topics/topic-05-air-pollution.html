üå¨Ô∏è Indoor Air Quality & Ventilation

The quality of indoor air can be affected by a variety of factors resulting in health-related symptoms or general discomfort. Common contaminants include:

  • Tobacco smoke: Carbon monoxide, arsenic, particulates
  • Building materials: Formaldehyde, fiberglass, volatile organic compounds (VOCs)
  • Cleaning compounds: Ammonia, chlorine
  • Equipment emissions: Ozone, ammonia
  • Biological: Radon, molds, pollens
ü§í

Sick Building Syndrome Effects

Health effects may include irritation of the eyes, nose, and throat, headaches, dizziness, and fatigue. These effects are often short-term and treatable but can mimic colds or viral diseases.

Types of Ventilation Systems

Ventilation is generally categorized into five types:

‚ñ∂ 1. Natural Ventilation

Depends on natural forces like wind and thermal buoyancy. Factors include climate, human behavior, and building design.

  • Buoyancy-Driven (Stack Ventilation): Based on temperature differences. Warm indoor air rises and exits high openings; cool outdoor air enters low openings.
  • Wind-Driven: Based on wind interaction with chimneys and windows (cross ventilation).
‚ñ∂ 2. Mechanical Ventilation

Uses mechanical fans installed in windows, walls, or ducts.

  • a
    Exhaust-Only: Reduces indoor pressure; fresh air enters through leaks. Economical but suited for colder climates.
  • b
    Supply-Only: Pressurizes the room to force air in. Better control over incoming air pollutants.
  • c
    Balanced: Equal rates of supply and exhaust. Often uses Heat Recovery Ventilators (HRV).
  • d
    Energy-Recovery: Transfers heat from exhaust to supply air to minimize energy waste.
‚ñ∂ 3. Hybrid Ventilation (Mixed Mode)

Depends on natural driving forces but uses mechanical fans to increase flow rates when natural forces are insufficient. Commonly used in patient rooms with airborne infections.

‚ñ∂ 4. Spot Ventilation

Auxiliary system using local exhaust fans (e.g., bathrooms, kitchens) to remove moisture and pollution at the source.

‚ñ∂ 5. Task Ambient Conditioning (TAC)

Regulates airflow in specific spaces to meet individual comfort preferences, allowing for energy savings by keeping the central temperature higher/lower than the strict comfort zone.

üè≠ Pollution Overview

Pollution is the introduction of harmful materials (pollutants) into the environment. It makes land, water, air, or other parts of the environment unsafe or unsuitable to use.

Toxic pollution affects more than 200 million people worldwide. In the worst areas, life expectancy may be as low as 45 years.

üóëÔ∏è Land Pollution

Land becomes polluted by household garbage and industrial waste. Waste is categorized as:

Solid Waste
Organic material, paper, food, plastics (13%), rubber, metals, wood, glass.
Hazardous Waste
Industrial: mining, petroleum refining, pesticide manufacturing.
Household: paints, solvents, motor oil, fluorescent lights.

üíß Water Pollution

Occurs when chemicals or dangerous foreign substances are introduced to water.

Type Cause Impact
Chemical/Biological Sewage, pesticides, heavy metals (lead, mercury). Pathogen growth, toxicity to marine life and humans.
Thermal Pollution Discharge of hot water from factories/power plants. Reduces oxygen in water, kills fish/wildlife.
Nutrient Pollution (Eutrophication) Excess nitrogen/nutrients (fertilizers). Excessive algae growth blocks light; decomposition consumes oxygen, killing aquatic animals.

üå´Ô∏è Air Pollution

Air pollution occurs when things not normally there are added to the air (99% of which is N‚ÇÇ, O‚ÇÇ, water vapor, inert gases).

Criteria Air Pollutants

  • 1
    Particulate Matter (PM): Tiny solid/liquid particles (soot) from burning fuels. Can penetrate lungs and bloodstream.
  • 2
    Dangerous Gases: Sulfur dioxide (SO_2), Carbon monoxide (CO), Nitrogen oxides (NO_x). Create acid rain and smog.
  • 3
    Greenhouse Gases: Carbon dioxide (CO_2) and others that trap heat, causing global warming.
  • 4
    Ozone (O_3): Beneficial in upper atmosphere, but toxic at ground level (smog ingredient).
  • 5
    Lead: Damages nervous system, kidneys, and brain.

Health Impact

Air pollution kills more than 2 million people each year. It causes respiratory illnesses, cardiovascular disease, and increases in asthma. The very young, old, and immune-compromised are most at risk.

Noise Pollution

Sound coming from planes, industry, or other sources reaching harmful levels. Links to stress-related illnesses, high blood pressure, speech interference, and hearing loss. Underwater noise pollution disrupts whales' navigation systems.

Light Pollution
