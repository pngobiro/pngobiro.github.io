üî¨ Introduction to Vectors

Vectors are insects, ticks, rats, mice, birds, and other animals that transmit disease-producing organisms to humans.

Public Health Role

To prevent human disease and discomfort caused by these vectors, health departments provide technical information on monitoring and control methods. Activities often include identifying specimens, conducting surveys for mosquitoes/flies/ticks, and testing wild birds for viruses (e.g., West Nile virus).

Common Disease Vectors

‚ñ∂ Mosquitoes

Mosquitoes are responsible for transmitting numerous diseases, including:

  • Arboviral Encephalitis
  • West Nile Virus
  • Dengue Fever
  • Malaria

Key species include the Asian Tiger Mosquito.

‚ñ∂ Ticks

Ticks transmit various tick-borne diseases such as:

  • Lyme Disease
  • Ehrlichiosis
  • Babesiosis

üå± Environmental Management

Environmental management seeks to change the environment to prevent or minimize vector propagation and human contact with the vector-pathogen. This is the mainstay of vector control (e.g., for Dengue).

Three Types of Management

1
Environmental Modification
Long-lasting physical transformations to reduce vector larval habitats, such as installation of a reliable piped water supply to communities.
2
Environmental Manipulation
Temporary changes involving the management of "essential" containers. Examples include: frequent emptying/cleaning of water-storage vessels, cleaning gutters, recycling discarded tires.
3
Changes to Human Habitation/Behaviour
Actions to reduce contact, such as installing mosquito screening on windows/doors and using mosquito nets.

Key Strategies

‚ñ∂ Water Supply & Storage

Improvement: Piped water is preferable to wells or communal standpipes. Reliable supply reduces the need for storage containers that serve as breeding grounds.

Mosquito-proofing: Containers should have tight lids or mesh screens. Expanded polystyrene beads can be used on the water surface to inhibit egg-laying (oviposition).

‚ñ∂ Solid Waste Management

"Reduce, Reuse, Recycle" principles apply heavily to vector control.

  • Tires: Used tires are highly productive larval habitats. They should be recycled, incinerated, or buried properly.
  • Street Cleansing: Removes discarded water-bearing containers and cleans drains to prevent stagnation.
  • Collection Frequency: Twice per week is recommended in warm climates to control houseflies and rodents.

üß™ Chemical Control

Chemical control should be considered complementary to environmental management.

Larvicides (Targeting Larvae)

Used for containers that cannot be eliminated. Must be safe for potable water if used in drinking supplies.

Perifocal treatment: Spraying insecticide on larval habitats and peripheral surfaces (internal/external walls of containers) to destroy larvae and adult mosquitoes frequenting the site.

Adulticides (Targeting Adults)

Space Spraying: Recommended primarily for emergency situations to suppress ongoing epidemics.

  • Objective: Massive, rapid destruction of the adult vector population.
  • Timing: Must correspond to the peak flight activity of the target species (e.g., early morning or late afternoon for Aedes).
  • Methods: Thermal fogs (10‚Äì50 l/ha) or Ultra-low-volume (ULV) cold aerosols (0.5‚Äì2.0 l/ha). Can be portable, vehicle-mounted, or aerial.

Safety Precautions

  • Follow label instructions carefully.
  • Operators must wear protective clothing, gloves, goggles, and masks.
  • Monitor blood cholinesterase levels if using organophosphates.
  • Householders and pets must remain outside during indoor space spraying.

üêü Biological & Integrated Approaches

Biological Control

Introduction of organisms that prey upon or parasitize the target species.

  • üêü
    Larvivorous Fish: Species like Poecilia reticulata (Guppy) used in large water containers or wells. Only native species should be used to avoid disrupting ecosystems.
  • ü¶ê
    Predatory Copepods: Small freshwater crustaceans effective in water-storage tanks (e.g., successful use in Vietnam).

Integrated Vector Management (IVM)

Combines multiple strategies and sectors for effective control.

1. Advocacy & Legislation

  • Legislative: Laws to address tire disposal, sanitary landfills, and building codes.
  • Regulatory: Enforcement of sanitary laws (e.g., fines for construction sites harbouring mosquitoes).

2. Social Mobilization (COMBI)

Communication-for-Behavioural-Impact (COMBI): Moving beyond just "knowledge" to promoting specific behavioural changes. Requires engaging decision-makers, community leaders, and individuals.

3. New Tools

  • Insecticide-Treated Materials (ITMs): Curtains and covers for water containers.
  • Lethal Ovitraps: Traps modified to kill immature or adult mosquitoes (e.g., using insecticide strips or sticky surfaces).
