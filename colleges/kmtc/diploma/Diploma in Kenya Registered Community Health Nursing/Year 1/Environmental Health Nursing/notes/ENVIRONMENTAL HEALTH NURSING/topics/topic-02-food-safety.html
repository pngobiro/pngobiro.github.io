Hygiene

Impact of Poor Hygiene

Poor food hygiene practice will lead to those consuming the food to become severely ill, most likely with food poisoning.

Types of Food Safety and Hygiene

This includes:

  • Personal hygiene
  • Safe handling of food
  • Preventing cross-contamination
  • Cleaning procedures
  • Allergen control
  • Safe storage of food
  • Cooking temperatures

ü•´ Methods of Food Preservation

Among the oldest methods of preservation are drying, refrigeration, and fermentation. Modern methods include canning, pasteurization, freezing, irradiation, and the addition of chemicals.

‚ñ∂ 1. Drying

Drying or "dehydrating" food is a method of food preservation that removes enough moisture from the food so bacteria, yeast and molds cannot grow.

Advantages Disadvantages
Increases its longevity in a very simple way Dried food does not taste the same as fresh food does
Inhibits the growth of bacteria, mold, and yeast Moisture left in the food or allowed in during storage can cause mold
Can be preserved for up to several years with proper storage Overly dried foods can be exceptionally hard, often to the point where they do not soften
‚ñ∂ 2. Refrigeration & Freezing

Refrigeration

Refrigeration (cold temperature) slows down the bad bacterial growth greatly, so food lasts 10 or more times longer to spoil. Label raw meat etc. so they are only stored for a day or two in the refrigerator prior to being cooked and consumed.

Freezing

Freeze foods at 0 degrees Fahrenheit or lower. To freeze foods rapidly, set the temperature control at -10 degrees Fahrenheit or lower 24 hours in advance. Keep a thermometer in the freezer to assure proper freezing temperatures are maintained. Freeze foods immediately after they are packaged and sealed.

‚ñ∂ 3. Fermentation

This is the breakdown of carbs like starch and sugar by bacteria and yeast and an ancient technique of preserving food.

Types of Fermentation
  • a
    Lactic acid fermentation: Yeast strains and bacteria convert starches or sugars into lactic acid, requiring no heat. Used for sauerkraut, pickles, kimchi, yogurt, and sourdough bread.
  • b
    Ethanol/Alcohol fermentation: Yeasts break pyruvate molecules in starches or sugars down into alcohol and carbon dioxide molecules. Produces wine and beer.
  • c
    Acetic acid fermentation: Starches and sugars from grains and fruit ferment into sour tasting vinegar and condiments. Examples include apple cider vinegar, wine vinegar, and kombucha.
Stages of Fermentation
  1. 1
    Primary fermentation: Microbes begin rapidly working on raw ingredients. Yeasts or other microbes convert carbohydrates (sugars) into other substances such as alcohols and acids.
  2. 2
    Secondary fermentation: Lasts several days or even weeks. Alcohol levels rise and yeasts/microbes die off. Once alcohol is between 12‚Äì15% and it kills the yeast, distillation is needed to remove water.
‚ö†Ô∏è

Disadvantages/Risks

  • Bloating: Result of excess gas being produced after probiotics kill harmful gut bacteria.
  • Headaches and migraines: Caused by biogenic amines (histamine and tyramine) produced during fermentation.
  • Histamine intolerance: Can cause itching, headaches, runny nose, and digestive symptoms.
  • Food-borne illness: Contamination with bacteria (Salmonella, E. coli, Staphylococcus aureus) is possible if not managed correctly.
  • Infection from probiotics: In rare cases, especially in people with compromised immune systems.
  • Antibiotic resistance: Probiotic bacteria can carry genes that confer resistance to antibiotics.
‚ñ∂ 4. Pasteurization

Pasteurization is a heat-treatment process that destroys pathogenic microorganisms in certain foods and beverages.

Types of Thermal Processing Methods
  • Thermization: Heat milk to 57¬∞C‚Äì68¬∞C for 15 minutes. Targets pathogens while leaving good bacteria.
  • Batch pasteurization (LTLT): Heat milk to 63¬∞C for 30 minutes. Alters protein structure.
  • Flash pasteurization (HTST): Heat milk to 72¬∞C‚Äì74¬∞C for 15‚Äì20 seconds.
  • Ultra-high temperature (UHT): Heat milk to 135¬∞C‚Äì140¬∞C for 2‚Äì4 seconds. Targets Coxiella burnetii.
  • Canned sterilization: Wet treatment in autoclave at 115¬∞C‚Äì121¬∞C for 10‚Äì20 minutes.
Steps of Pasteurization Process
  1. 1
    Milk chilling: Reducing temperatures to 2¬∞C‚Äì5¬∞C arrests bacterial growth.
  2. 2
    Pre-heating (regeneration) and Standardization: Heated to about 40¬∞C. Standardization ensures consistent butter-fat content.
  3. 3
    Clarification: Removal of foreign matter using centrifugal clarifier or filters.
  4. 4
    Homogenization: Breaking down milk fat globules into tiny droplets (100‚Äì170 bars) to prevent cream separation.
  5. 5
    Heating section: Raises temperatures to required 72¬∞C (for HTST) to kill Clostridium botulinum spores.
  6. 6
    Holding section: Ensures milk stays at required temperature for the specific time (e.g., 16 seconds).
  7. 7
    Cooling/chilling section: Rapidly cooled to 4¬∞C for packaging.
‚ñ∂ Other Methods (Canning, Irradiation, Chemical)

Canning: Process of placing foods in jars or cans and heating properly to a specified temperature. The high heat destroys microorganisms and inactivates enzymes.

Irradiation: Application of ionizing radiation to food to improve safety and extend shelf life by reducing/eliminating microorganisms and insects. It does not make foods radioactive.

Chemical: Use of additives such as sodium benzoate, benzoic acid, nitrites, sulfites, sodium sorbate and potassium sorbate to inhibit spoilage organisms.

‚öñÔ∏è Food Control Systems

Food Control means a mandatory, regulatory activity of enforcement by the competent health authority to provide consumer protection and ensure that all food during production, handling, storage, processing and distribution is safe, wholesome and fit for human consumption.

Principal Objectives

  • Protecting public health by reducing the risk of foodborne illness.
  • Protecting consumers from unsanitary, unwholesome, mislabelled or adulterated food.
  • Contributing to economic development by maintaining consumer confidence.

Elements of Food Control

a) Food Law and Regulations
Essential component providing legal powers to ensure food safety. Modern laws should be preventive rather than reactive.
b) Food Control Management
Requires policy and operational coordination at the national level to develop and implement integrated national food control strategies.
c) Inspection Services
Administration and implementation of food laws require qualified, trained, efficient and honest food inspection services. Inspectors inspect premises, evaluate HACCP plans, sample food, and recognize evidence of breaches.
d) Laboratory Services
Essential for physical, microbiological and chemical analyses. Analytical results are often used as evidence in a court of law.
e) Information, Education, Communication
Delivery of information and advice to stakeholders across the farm-to-table continuum.

ü§¢ Food Poisoning

Food poisoning (foodborne illness) is illness caused by eating contaminated food. Infectious organisms including bacteria, viruses and parasites or their toxins are the most common causes.

‚ñ∂ Salmonella

Cause: Salmonella enterica bacterium.

Transmission: Contact with infected food handler, fecal matter, touching rodents/reptiles/birds. Beef, dairy, eggs, and poultry are high risk.

Symptoms: Fever, stomach cramps, diarrhea. Usually appear 12-72 hours after infection and last 4-7 days.

Treatment: Rest and hydration. Antibiotics in some cases.

‚ñ∂ Listeria

Cause: Listeria monocytogenes. High risk for pregnant women and immunocompromised.

Sources: Raw/cooked seafood, deli meats, premixed raw vegetables, unpasteurized milk, soft cheeses.

Symptoms: Tiredness, fever, headaches, aches. Can lead to septicaemia or meningitis. In pregnant women, can cause miscarriage.

‚ñ∂ Staphylococcus (Golden Staph)

Cause: Staphylococcus aureus. Common on skin, mouth, nose.

Symptoms: Soreness, redness, heat, swelling. Severe cases: high fever, skin rashes, blisters, impetigo.

Treatment: Antibiotics (penicillin). Good food safety hygiene is crucial.

‚ñ∂ Trichinosis

Cause: Roundworm infection from raw/undercooked meats (especially game).

Symptoms: Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain. If muscles affected: fever, swelling, muscle pain, sensitivity to light.

Prevention: Avoid undercooked meat. Freezing some meats for 3 weeks can kill trichinosis.

‚ñ∂ E. coli

Cause: Escherichia coli bacteria found in digestive systems. Contact with feces.

Sources: Contaminated water, meat, poultry, dairy, fruits, vegetables.

Symptoms: Severe abdominal cramps, nausea, fever, bloody diarrhea. Can cause kidney failure/anemia.

‚ñ∂ Campylobacter

Cause: Bacteria often found on raw/undercooked poultry.

Symptoms: Stomach cramps, bloody diarrhea, fever, nausea.

Treatment: Rest and rehydration.

‚ñ∂ Clostridium (C. diff)

Cause: Bacterium living in digestive tract. Overgrowth releases toxins attacking intestines.

Symptoms: Abdominal pain, watery diarrhea. Severe: weight loss, blood/pus in stool.

Risk: Patients in hospitals/hospices, those on chemotherapy or with weakened immune systems.

üè≠ Food Production

Food production refers to the process of conversion of raw materials into finished food products. It involves three major steps:

  1. 1
    Input: Different raw materials required.
  2. 2
    Processing: Converting raw ingredients into edible form.
  3. 3
    Output: The final product produced.

The Food System Stages

The food system comprises seven stages:

  • Food Production (Agriculture/Farming)
  • Distribution and Aggregation
  • Food Processing
  • Marketing
  • Purchasing
  • Consumption
  • Waste Recovery

üîç Inspection of Food Premises

The purpose of food hygiene inspections is to find out if food is being handled and produced hygienically, is safe to eat, and to identify factors which have the potential to cause food poisoning or injury.

‚ÑπÔ∏è

Codex Alimentarius & HACCP

International standards apply (Codex Alimentarius - "general principles for good food hygiene"). It is also recommended to get premises certified to follow the HACCP system (Hazard Analysis and Critical Control Point).

‚ñ∂ 1. Primary Production

Primary production should be managed in a way that ensures food is safe and suitable for its intended use.

  • Environmental Hygiene: Avoid areas where the environment poses a threat to food safety.
  • Hygienic Production: Control contamination from air, soil, water, feedstuffs, fertilizers, pesticides, veterinary drugs.
  • Handling, Storage & Transport: Protect food from pests and chemical/physical contaminants. Segregate unfit material.
  • Cleaning & Personnel: Ensure effective cleaning and maintenance and appropriate personal hygiene.
‚ñ∂ 2. Establishment: Design and Facilities

Premises should be located, designed and constructed to ensure contamination is minimized and maintenance/cleaning is facilitated.

  • Location: Away from polluted areas, flood-prone areas, and pest infestations.
  • Internal Structure: Surfaces should be smooth, impervious, non-toxic, and easy to clean. Floors should allow drainage.
  • Equipment: Designed for adequate maintenance, cleaning, and functioning. Food control equipment should monitor temperature/humidity.
  • Facilities:
    • Water Supply: Adequate potable water. Non-potable water systems must be separate and identified.
    • Drainage: Designed to avoid contamination.
    • Cleaning: Adequate facilities for cleaning food, utensils and equipment (hot/cold water).
    • Personal Hygiene: Wash basins, lavatories, changing facilities.
    • Temperature Control: Facilities for heating, cooling, cooking, refrigerating and freezing.
    • Air Quality/Ventilation: Minimize airborne contamination, control odours and humidity.
    • Lighting: Adequate natural or artificial lighting.
    • Storage: Adequate facilities for food ingredients and non-food chemicals.
‚ñ∂ 3. Control of Operation

Objective: To produce food which is safe and suitable for human consumption.

  • HACCP: Identify critical steps, implement control procedures, monitor, and review.
  • Time & Temperature Control: Critical for safety (cooking, cooling, processing, storage).
  • Cross-contamination: Separate raw/unprocessed food from ready-to-eat foods. Restrict access to processing areas.
  • Incoming Materials: Inspect and sort raw materials. Do not accept if known to contain parasites/toxins.
  • Packaging: Must provide protection and be non-toxic.
  • Water/Ice/Steam: Only potable water for food contact. Ice/steam made from potable water.
  • Documentation: Records of processing, production and distribution retained for period exceeding shelf-life.
  • Recall Procedures: Effective procedures to deal with food safety hazards and recall implicated lots.
‚ñ∂ 4. Maintenance and Sanitation

Establishments and equipment should be kept in appropriate state of repair.

  • Cleaning: Remove food residues and dirt. Use physical (heat, scrubbing) or chemical (detergents) methods.
  • Pest Control: Prevent access (seal holes, drains, screens). Deny harbourage (store food in pest-proof containers). Monitor and eradicate.
  • Waste Management: Suitable provision for removal and storage of waste.
‚ñ∂ 5. Establishment: Personal Hygiene
  • Health Status: People suspected of suffering from disease transmitted through food should not enter food handling areas.
  • Illness to Report: Jaundice, diarrhea, vomiting, fever, sore throat with fever, infected skin lesions, discharges from ear/eye/nose.
  • Personal Cleanliness: Wear protective clothing. Wash hands at start, after toilet, and after handling raw food.
  • Behaviour: Refrain from smoking, spitting, chewing/eating, sneezing/coughing over food. No jewellery.
‚ñ∂ 6. Transportation

Measures should be taken to protect food from contamination, damage, and growth of pathogens during transport.

Conveyances/containers should be designed to be effectively cleaned, disinfected, and maintain necessary temperature/humidity.

‚ñ∂ 7. Product Information & Consumer Awareness

Lot Identification: Essential for product recall and stock rotation.

Product Information: Adequate information to enable safe handling, display, storage and preparation.

Labelling: Prepackaged foods should be labelled with clear instructions.

Consumer Education: Health education programmes covering general food hygiene.

‚ñ∂ 8. Personnel Training

Training is fundamentally important. All personnel should be aware of their role and responsibility in protecting food from contamination.

Training programmes should be routinely reviewed and updated where necessary.
