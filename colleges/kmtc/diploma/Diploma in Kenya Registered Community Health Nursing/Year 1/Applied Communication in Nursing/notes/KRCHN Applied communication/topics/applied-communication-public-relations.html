Public Relations

üìñ Definition of Terms

Public Relations (PR)

  1. i)Public relations is "the management of communication between an organization and its publics" (Grunig and Hunt, 1984, p. 6).
  2. ii)Public Relations is "the deliberate, planned, and sustained effort to establish and maintain mutual understanding between an organization and its publics." (Institute of Public Relations, UK)
  3. iii)Public Relations is "the planned effort to influence opinion through good character and responsible performance, based upon mutually satisfactory two-way communications."

üí° Note

PR creates an image and impression of the company, products, and services.

Communication

Not only sending a message to a receiver, but also understanding the messages of others through listening and dialogue.

Organization

A group organized with a common purpose. In most cases, it is a business, a corporation, a government agency, or a non-profit group.

Public

  1. i)Any groups of people held together by a common interest. They differ from audiences in that they often self-organize and do not have to attune to messages. Publics differ from stakeholders in that they do not necessarily have a financial stake in the organization.
  2. ii)A group of human individuals or a segment of persons having the same interest/problems/circumstances.

Target Audiences

Are publics/population who receive a specific tailored message that is aimed at their interests.

Propaganda

Propaganda is the manipulation of symbols. It is drawing an imitation, accepted attitudes, and skills. It describes the skillful application of publicity and advertising. Also, it aims at the end of helping an idea gain acceptance. It is not objective or unbiased.

Goodwill

A concept referring to the ability of an individual/business to exert influence within a community or market.

Campaigns

Campaigns (e.g., political campaigns) embrace various public relations through a variety of media. Campaigns aim at a number of objectives, but focused on specific objectives. A campaign objective may be the election of a candidate, the promotion of a political cause or issue, the recalling of a product, or the raising of a quota of goods.

Lobbying

It enables the exertion of influence (smooth and measured pressure) on another exertion of persuasion, often pressure. In essence, it means a group putting its points of view forward in an attempt to win the other group's support.

Publicity

Disseminating planned messages through selected media in an organization's interest.

Publicity enhances the reputation of your organization.

Thomas Jefferson used the phrase Public Relations in a draft of his seventh address to the US Congress.

In India, Great Indian Peninsular Railway Company Limited (GIPR) always carried on publicity in Public Relations campaign in England to promote tourism in India through mass media and pamphlets.

During the time of First World War, a central publicity board was set up at Bombay, now Mumbai to disseminate war news to the public and press.

After Second World War, the Public Relations activity gained importance both privately as well as governmentally. Public Relations campaigns.

üéØ Purpose of Public Relations

  • To identify, build, and sustain mutually beneficial relationships.
  • Need for Public Relations.
  • Effective Public Relations campaign is needed for attracting and motivating the public concerning product, service, or towards the purpose of the program.
  • It not only encourages the involvement of the public, but also results in better image.
  • An effective Public Relations can create and build up the image of an individual or an organization or a nation.
  • At the time of adverse publicity or when the organization is under crisis, an effective Public Relations can remove the misunderstanding and can create mutual understanding between the organization and the public.

Public relations is establishing a relationship among two groups, e.g., organization and public.

  • Art and science of developing rapport, understanding, and goodwill.
  • It analyzes the public perception and attitudes, identifies the organization's policy with public in terms of public relation and executes programs for communication with the public. Outcomes of Public Relations:
  • Planned effort/management functions.
  • The relationship between an organization and its publics.
  • Evaluation of public attitudes and opinions.
  • An organization's policies, procedures, and actions as they relate to said organization's publics.

üóÇÔ∏è Some Public Relations Functions

Public Relations is a broad discipline that can be subdivided into many types of functions. There are primary areas of functional responsibility or different arenas in which we can categorize the profession of public relations, and these are:

  • Corporate public relations.
  • Agency public relations.
  • Government public affairs.
  • Non-profit/NGO activity public relations.

The function of Public Relations:

Public Relations helps our company to operate smoothly, to reach decisions, and function more effectively by contributing to mutual understanding among groups and institutions. It serves a wide variety of institutions in society, such as businesses, trade unions, government agencies, voluntary associations, foundations, hospitals, schools, colleges, and religious institutions. To achieve their goals, these institutions must develop effective relationships with many different audiences or publics such as employees, members, customers, local communities, shareholders, and other institutions and with society at large. (Public Relations Society of America, A).

The unique management function of public relations is critical to the success of any organization that engages people in its operation, whether they are shareholders, employees, or customers.

Public relations is even difficult to define. Is it an art or science? Either way, the public relations function is prevalent and growing. The fragmentation of media and growth of multiple message sources means that public relations is on the ascendant, while traditional forms of mass communication, such as newspapers, are on the decline. You can find public relations in virtually every individual, government, and non-profit organization. Its broad scope makes it impossible to understand without some attention to the anatomy of this diverse and dynamic profession.

Public Relations Process

The first phase of Public Relations process is identifying and gathering information or messages to be communicated.

The second phase of Public Relations is to obtain an understanding of the existing image or awareness level about the issue in the target group or common public.

The third phase of Public Relations is development of communication objectives and priorities.

The fourth phase of Public Relations is developing the message and choosing the media to transmit it.

The fifth phase of Public Relations is the implementation of the message and media coordination or the dissemination of messages.

The sixth phase of Public Relations is communication process to check whether messages reached properly and the expected action or behavior or knowledge has happened.

The seventh phase of Public Relations, in case the message did not reach properly, identify the reason for the ineffectiveness and do the rectification of the same and disseminate the revised message.
