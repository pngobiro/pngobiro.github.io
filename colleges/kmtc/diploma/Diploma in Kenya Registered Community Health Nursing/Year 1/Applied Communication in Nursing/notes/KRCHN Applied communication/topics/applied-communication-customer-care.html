üìñ Definition of Terms

Customer

This is someone who obtains goods or services, i.e., a recipient of your product or service. This is the person who needs your assistance. These persons may be internal or external. Never consider a customer as an interruption of your work, but always remember they are the reason why you have work.

Internal Customers

A person/department within an organization that is dependent on another department.

External Customers

A person/organization outside the organization taking/obtaining goods or services.

New Customers

These are the people who will provide new income and profit and become part of a customer base which the marketing department will want to retain and develop.

Old Customers

These are the already existing customers. The organization must make sure that these customers will come back and buy again and buy more. This will lead to increased evaluation and profits.

Customer Satisfaction

This is a "feel good" factor which affects both customer and staff, and which encourages the customer to come back again.

Customer Retention

This refers to the ability of a company/organization to retain its customers over some specified period. High customer retention means customers of the product or business tend to return to continue to buy services or products.

Why are loyal customers important than satisfied customers?

If customer satisfaction is a short-term win, customer loyalty is the long-term goal. Loyal customers will spend more, stay with your company longer, and will recommend you to family and friends. They will stick with you because they want to, because they have a bond.

Why is customer loyalty more important than customer satisfaction?

Customer loyalty measures something more than satisfaction, it measures whether someone is willing to put their name on the line and recommend your product or service to others, or if they are willing to stick with your product/service in spite of an occasional poor experience.

What is the difference between customer loyalty and customer satisfaction?

Customer satisfaction is a measurement of a customer's attitude towards a product, a service or a brand. Customer loyalty is a set of behaviors and attitudes that a customer exhibits that demonstrate loyalty to a product, service, or brand such as repeat purchases or choosing the brand over competitors.

There is only one boss, and whoever that person is who shies away from running or hides their business/corporation into the world, the boss remains the same. It's the customer. The customer is the person who pays everyone's salary and who decides whether a business is going to succeed or fail.

Literally, everything we do, every concept, every technological advance, and associated employee is directed with this one objective clearly in mind: "Making the customer happy."

Customer Service is the Core of Your Business

Ask any CEO of a company, president of a bank, manager of an office, or staff person, and they will likely tell you how important the customer is to their operations and success. In meeting after meeting, heads of industry, the service industry, and government try to convince the audience how much they believe in customer service, and they use phrases like "It's our mission, it's our number one priority, it's our goal, it's why we are in business," which often prove to be mere empty forms of words written in memory of a person who has died (e.g., on a grave side). Customer service and personal interaction with a client mean that the customer will be heard and his/her problems will not go unanswered or ignored. The Phone Etiquette

The phone is one of the most important and commonly used tools in business. Many types of businesses, companies, and departments use phones in their work every day. However, most of us don't think of the phone as a tool and as a result, misuse it. The phone is a link between us and the world outside our business or department. Unfortunately, sometimes we don't pay attention or make a conscious effort to monitor what kind of message we are sending out to customers and the outside world.

Some basic rules of phone etiquette are:

  • Speak directly into the mouthpiece of the phone or headset when talking.
  • Do NOT eat or chew gum when talking on the phone.
  • Do NOT cover the phone with your hand or cup your hand to avoid the customer hearing you. Chances are, they will still be able to comprehend what you are saying.
  • If you are interrupted or must talk to somebody else in your workplace while you are on the phone, simply ask the customer if they can hold and press the HOLD button.
  • Do NOT put the headset on the desk without putting the customer on HOLD. The customer will hear everything being discussed in your office.
  • Always be courteous.
  • The phone etiquette, unlike many verbal communication, can be uniform and is not culturally biased.

The phone is often the first interaction a customer comes into contact with an organization or company. Being phone friendly is one of the most expressive and cost effective ways to deal with customer service.

Answering the Phone:

How a company answers the phone can tell a whole story of how they treat customers and employees. The correct phrase said in the right order in a positive tone makes a good impression and starts the customer-client relationship off on the right foot.

  • Always try to answer your own phone whenever possible.
  • Always practice answering your phone within 3 rings. More than 3 rings signals chaos in your office or ineffectiveness on the part of your company or organization.
  • Greet the caller, e.g., "Hello," "Good morning," "Good afternoon." Good manners shows you respect the caller.
  • Give your name, e.g., "Hi, my name is..." This is a courtesy that serves to personalize the customer service experience as well as allowing the customer to know how you are accountable for your level of service. He/she now has a point of reference and someone to contact when he/she calls back.
  • Ask the customer how you can help. Asking how to help conveys to the customer you are there to serve his/her needs and to solve his/her problems. This also makes the customer with a positive impression.

For example:

"Good morning, thanks for calling X Hospital. My name is T. How may I help you?"

The greeting is key to the entire interaction.

üí° Note

Some things which may upset a customer are simple and avoidable.

Examples of how to handle these situations:

  • Putting a customer on hold: Ask the customer if you can put them on hold, wait for them to say "yes" or "no," and then explain it will only be for a short period of time. Thank customers for holding.
  • Transferring a call: Ask the customer if they mind being transferred, wait for them to say "yes" or "no," and then explain why they are being transferred and to whom. If you are not sure to whom a call should be transferred, take the caller's name and phone number and find out where the call needs to be transferred to. Also, give the caller your name and phone number as a reference in case the appropriate party does not contact them. Or, verify that it is okay with the caller for you to transfer them.
  • Call the department or person where you are transferring a call and make sure that they can take the call. If they are able to take the call, give them the person's name, request, and any other relevant information. Return to your caller and give them the name of the person they are being transferred to, the department, and the phone number, if possible.
  • Taking a message: Explain to your co-worker (if he/she is in a meeting, conference, or training) that you are taking a message. Do not say, e.g., he/she is gravely ill, having a hangover, or come to work drunk. You can be found that you do not know where he/she is or that he/she "was just there." Give a reasonable estimate of when the co-worker will return. Offer to take a message or transfer to another staff member. If a co-worker is on holiday and will not return to the office for some time, it is permissible to say that he/she is on holiday. However, avoid details such as "Mr. X is at the beach and I am sure he is having a great time!" While such details may seem harmless, innocent, and even humorous, they give the wrong impression of those seeking service.
  • Screening calls: There is a lot of controversy over whether calls should be screened. It is not recommended to screen calls if you want to enhance or encourage good public relations. You should always aim towards not screening calls, if at all possible. "Yes, he's in. May I feel him who is calling?" is an example of an inappropriate response when you are not screening calls. If someone is unavailable ONLY to certain individuals, and you must screen the call, then do so towards using responses like, "She is away for now. May I take your name and number?" Or, "May I ask who is calling? Thank you. Let me check and see if he is in." If you are required to ask who is calling or what the nature of the call is, be aware of your tone of voice. Screening calls is always a delicate situation, so it is critical not to offend or upset your caller with your tone of voice.
  • Ending the call: This is the final step in phone etiquette. A good customer service representative ends the call on a positive note, repeating actions agreed to be taken, and what is going to be done to help or serve the customer.

A good phone message includes:

  • Name of person/for whom the message was left.
  • Caller's name, company/department, and number.
  • Date and time.
  • Message.
  • Action to be taken, e.g., "Please call back," or "URGENT."

It is critical to deliver the message to the person it is intended for as soon as possible and to maintain confidentiality with all messages. When delivering a written message, either throw the message away or burn it so as not to allow them to be easily read by other staff members or visitors.

When returning a phone call:

Returning phone calls can be frustrating, so to avoid returning phone calls to a person who is unavailable, try calling them back asking, "When is the best time for me to call back?" or "When is the best time for them to call me back?"

üí° Note

When calling for another individual (e.g., a colleague), return calls during specified times. Example: "I expect him to return by 2 pm. You can reach him between 2 and 5 pm." When placing outbound calls from customers, be sure you have the right phone number before you place an outbound call. Keeping a "frequently called numbers" list within reach can prove to be very helpful.

Try utilizing a few of these suggestions:

  • Prepare yourself. Visualize your caller as a friendly and positive person.
  • Plan the objectives you want to accomplish by putting them down ahead of time.
  • State your concerns up front to identify the information you need to obtain from the conversation. Anticipate what questions or objections you may encounter prior to placing the call and develop answers to them to avoid making additional damages.
  • Take notes during the outbound call.
  • Specify any follow up action to the caller, such as when you will get back to him.

When you are leaving a message:

When you read an answered device, such as a voicemail or an answering machine, be sure to leave the following information:

  • Your name.
  • Your department and phone number.
  • The date and time you called.
  • The message.
  • When you are available to take calls.

To conclude the conversation...

Many people dread bringing a telephone conversation to an end. Here are some tips to avoid awkwardness and to make your conversation with professional ease:

  • Talk into the phone.
  • Try using a "closing phrase" such as "I'm really glad you called" or "I'm glad we resolved this issue."
  • State the action you will take.
  • Affirm any follow up actions such as times, dates or deadlines.
  • Thank them for calling and say "Good bye." Rephrase for using phrases like "bye bye," "cheerio," or "Alright, see you."

‚úâÔ∏è Proper Phone Message

Although we express a lot to our customers through the sound and the one of our voice, what words and phrases we use in a conversation can also convey a significant message. Sometimes people feel frustrated and end up demeaning a negative message to the customer. Pay attention to what type of language you are using. For example, instead of beginning a sentence with phrases like "You have to," "You need to," or "Why didn't you?", try starting with words like "We will," or "Would you please."

Never refer to a matter or inquiry as "Your problem," or "Your complaint." Instead, try using softer phrasing and identifying it as something along the lines of "Your question," "Your concern," or "This situation." When you do not have the knowledge or expertise to handle a customer's situation, never reply with remarks like "I can't do that," or "That's not my job." Instead, try a more helpful approach by outlining what actions and steps you are capable of taking to aid their situation.

Example:

"While I'm unable to help you on this matter, I will speak to my manager about your concern."

Avoid brushing off as unprofessional by all means, and refrain from using expressions such as:

  • "Hang on"
  • "Hold on"
  • "Who's calling?"
  • "I can't hear you, speak up."
  • "I can't tell you. You'll have to speak to someone else."

The following terms would be more appropriate:

  • "May I place you on hold?"
  • "May I know who is calling, please?"
  • "I am having a difficult time hearing you."

Showing enthusiasm, i.e., positive attitude or approach to the caller, when talking a call to demonstrate the business is happy to welcome the caller and to do business with them. Being friendly is important for anyone who takes the phone calls to understand the need to be friendly in all matters. This friendliness that is shown on the phone has been referred to as "need to please." Go to please in your service when talking on the phone. üìû Summary: Responding to Incoming Phone Calls

When responding to incoming phone calls:

  • Answer calls as soon as possible, using the business approved greeting.
  • Display a friendly and helpful demeanor to demonstrate your willingness to be of service.
  • Quickly identify the caller's reason for calling and provide the required assistance promptly, or redirect the call to someone else who can deal with the call.
  • Be prepared to describe caller details, booking details, or the reason for the call.
  • Make sure all messages are recorded accurately and comprehensively, and forwarded to the appropriate person.
  • Follow up on any phone messages taken to ensure they have been received and/or acted upon.
  • Treat abusive or threatening phone calls seriously and report them immediately to your Supervisor.
  • Always use language (tone and volume) appropriate to the nature of the call to demonstrate respect for the other person and to show their call is valued. Rude and aggressive calls.

Dealing with a caller who is rude or aggressive does not give you the right to reply in kind and be rude or aggressive in return. This means in these situations you:

  • Must both wear black when someone swore at you.
  • Must both use the same tone of voice as used by the caller when they are obviously annoyed.
  • Must both use the same type of language.
  • Your responses and the language you use must always remain professional and always reflect the company's image.
  • Must both be rude in return.
  • Whatever work there is, there must be any notification for being rude to any caller, regardless of the circumstances.

Key to dealing with rude and angry people on the phone are:

  • To remain calm and in control. This may be achieved by:
    • Taking a deep breath mentally.
    • Recognizing the caller (what it is). It is an unavoidable call, most calls are not like this.
    • Do not allow this call to spoil your day or ruin your reputation.
    • Seeing the caller as a challenge rather than a problem or issue and doing your best to meet the challenge of the caller.
  • To respond with fairness regardless of how much you may feel like it is inappropriate or being provoked. You must respond calmly in order to try to calm the other person down.
  • To remain polite in your responses, using words like "Sir," "Madam," "Please," and "Thank you." The key is, the more polite you are, the more you encourage a similar response from the caller.
  • To both believe the caller. Try to remember the caller is not upset with you personally, instead they are angry about something else. You might have happened to be the one who picked up the phone, so never assume their anger or rudeness (sarcasm or tone of voice) is directed at you.
  • To get to the reason for the call as quickly as possible by redirecting their statements, criticisms, negativity back to the real point of the call, i.e., what they want to discuss. You need to cut through their anger as quickly as possible and start addressing their real problem.

For example:

"Yes, Madam, I can tell you are upset. I apologize. Will you allow me to transfer you to Mr. X, our manager, so he can help you? He has the authority to deal with this kind of issue." Where a phone call is obviously rude, including calls that are obscene, you should simply hang up straight away and report the call to your Supervisor.

The Basic Requirements:

The points to note when taking or making a call include:

  • Always say "Please" when asking for something.
  • Always say "Thank you" when provided with information or assistance that was required.
  • Make mention of your appreciation of any special effort the other person has made on your behalf (e.g., locating a certain person, finding required information, making a sensible suggestion) that may have both solved your problem, but also affirmed you in some way.
  • Always remain professional, even when the other party may be rude, unhelpful, or extremely unprofessional themselves.
  • Never run down a third party to the person you are speaking to. For instance, do not mention to the person on the phone on the switchboard was slow or unhelpful.
  • Ask when a person who is unavailable is likely to be available or ask them when is the best time to call.
  • Use the person's name, if known, or use Sir or Madam, according to business protocol when talking to a Madam or Sir.
  • Speak at a normal volume. Do not whisper and never shout.
  • Talk at a normal pace.
  • Do not cover the phone.
  • Use correct language.
  • Do not use slang expressions, do not take any complaints (have arisen as a result of staff member making what they thought was a funny comment but which the other person did not find at all humorous), do not make racist, sexist, or ageist comments.
  • Avoid using industry terms and/or business-specific terminology when speaking to another industry professional. This can confuse people outside the organization and make them feel they are being excluded from the conversation.
  • Eliminate any background noise as far as possible.
  • Turn off radios or music and close doors and windows to reduce noise from other areas.
  • Talk directly into the phone or headset mouthpiece.
  • Pronounce your words correctly. Slurring words or terms you can scarcely expect the other party will be unimpressed with.
  • Be careful how you put the phone down when you are talking to the customer.
  • Put down gently to avoid noise to the other party.
  • Never talk to a third party while you are on the phone to someone else.
  • Ask customers if you can help them in any other way before hanging up.
  • Thank customers for their call, where appropriate.
  • Let the customer hang up before you do.
  • Calls are dynamic.

Types of calls are often divided into outbound and inbound.

  • Inbound calls are calls that are made by the customer to obtain information, reporting a problem, or asking for help. These calls are substantially different from outbound calls, where employees make calls to potential customers, mostly with intentions of helping or serving the individual.
  • Call center staff are often organized into a multi-tier support system for a more efficient handling of calls. The first tier in such a model consists of operators who direct inquiries to the appropriate department and provide general directory information.
  • If a caller requires more assistance, the call is forwarded to the second tier, where more specific issues can be resolved. In some cases, there may be three or more tiers of support staff.
  • If a caller requires more assistance, the call is forwarded to the third tier of support, typically the third tier of support is formed by product engineers, developers, or highly skilled clinical support staff of the product.

Management of Call Centers:

Management of call centers involves balancing the requirements of cost-effectiveness and service. Call centers do not want to wait in exorbitant long queues until they can be helped, and so management must provide sufficient staff in inbound capacity to ensure that the quality of service is maintained. However, staff costs generally form more than half the cost of running a call center, and so management must minimize the number of agents required on duty at any one time.

To perform this balancing act, call center managers make use of demand estimation. Call volumes, forecasting, and demand estimation techniques are used to determine the level of calls required at any time.

Forecasting methods rely on data acquired from various sources, including historical data, queued data, and so on.

Forecasting methods must predict the traffic expected within the call center in quarter hour increments, and these results must be compared to staffing rosters. Special attention must be paid to the busy hour.

Forecasting methods must be used to preemptive action to prevent a situation where equipment needs to be upgraded as capacity of the call center is exceeded.

üìä RATER Model of Customer Service

The RATER model is derived from SERVQUAL (Service Quality), a service quality model developed by Parasuraman, Zeithaml, and Berry.

The RATER model was published in their book, "Delivering Quality Service: Balancing Customer Perceptions and Expectations."

The RATER model is used to measure customer expectations and the 5 dimensions that customers consider when using a service. It is a condensed version of the original SERVQUAL instrument, which had 10 dimensions, namely: tangibles, reliability, responsiveness, competence, courtesy, credibility, security, access, communication, and understanding the customer.

According to the RATER framework, your customers evaluate your business based on 5 dimensions (metrics), either conscious or unconscious. These include:

Reliability

This is the ability to provide dependable services accurately, regularly, and on time. Reliability is the most important service dimension in the RATER model.

For instance:

  • When you don't offer services to your customers as they expect, your company won't be reliable to them.
  • When you can't deliver products on time, when your product doesn't offer what it claims, etc., this is when your customers will clearly say that your company/organization is not reliable.

The service that your organization delivers should be communicated clearly. You need to create customer expectations based on what service you will offer them.

How to improve service reliability:

  • Communicate with your customers. The more you tell them, the better.
  • Be clear and transparent.
  • Take customer feedback and see what they think of your business.
  • Ensure marketing efforts do not create too much hype.
  • Keep expectations low and deliver more. Do not raise the bar too high in terms of building customer expectations.

Assurance

This is the dimension that deals with trust and credibility. That is competence of the staff, and their expertise, confidence, and trust.

Meeting customer expectations begins when you can show them that you are trustworthy and know what you are doing.

This includes answering customer inquiries and concerns effectively and professionally.

How to make your customers trust you:

  • Answer customer queries and concerns promptly and accurately.
  • Train your staff customer support staff.
  • Educate your customers by addressing their common issues.
  • Be honest and transparent.
  • Use trustworthy symbols (e.g., visual, logical, and indicators) on your website.

Tangibles

It refers to the physical aspect of your service, such as the website of the staff, equipment, etc. E.g., Does the physical environment of your business make a good impression?

Tangibles do not always mean something that has a physical appearance. Rather, it refers to the image of your business. For example, if you have an e-commerce store, the tangibles will include your website (online store), product photos, website layout, product descriptions, payment methods that you allow, customer support, etc. If you own a laundromat, tangibles will include your office vehicles, staff uniform, etc.

üí° Note

A poorly designed website or a mismanaged office will turn potential customers away. Customers to go elsewhere. If they will go elsewhere.

How to improve tangibles:

  • Have a well-designed website.
  • Ensure staff needs to be well-dressed, groomed.
  • Office and equipment should be clean, tidy, and well-arranged.
  • All customer touch points should demonstrate a superb customer experience.

Empathy

This refers to your employees' relationship with the customers. Does your customer support staff empathize with customers? Your staff should do so. Empathy can make your customers experience this feeling.

The first step of empathizing is listening to the problem, apologizing, offering assistance, and making the customer know what you are working on the solution.

How to improve empathy:

  • Listen to your customers.
  • Apologize.
  • Train your customer support agents well.
  • Update customers on the progress of their complaints.
  • A short call back can do wonders.

Responsiveness

This refers to the ability and willingness to offer timely and quality services to the customers. I.e., how effectively you respond to your customers, and how fast you do it.

How to be responsive:

  • Be available across all channels/media where your customers are.
  • Train your staff.
  • Use software and tools to boost responsiveness.
  • Try responding customer queries within hours.
  • Set up automated responses.
  • Be active on social media. Respond to customer queries.
