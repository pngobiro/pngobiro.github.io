RHEUMATIC FEVER

Lecturer: Dr Karari

outline

  • Introduction
  • Pathophysiology
  • Clinical features
  • Investigations
  • Diagnosis
  • Management

INTRODUCTION

  • A multisystemic inflammatory disorder that typically develops after an episode of streptococcal pharyngitis. Does not follow infection by strep at other sites
  • Presents with variety of symptoms affecting Heart, CNS, Skin and Joints
  • Symptoms occur 2-3 wks after initial attack of strep pharyngitis.
  • Usually affects children, young adults peak 5-16yrs

Introd - cont

  • Recurrence is high unless prevention is instituted
  • Main associated risk factor is crowding, hence a disease of low social economic population groups. Rare in the west, common in developing countries
  • 3% of untreated strep pharyngitis will develop RF.
  • Determined by serotypes of the group A strep present in the population

PATHOGENESIS

  • Exact mechanism unclear.
  • Previously thought to be due to the direct action of the bacteria, or toxic effects of extra cellular products
  • Now widely known to be an abnormal, or dysfunctional immune response to an extra cellular antigen produced by bacteria

Path - Cont

  • Toxicity due to antigenic mimicry
  • Similarity between carbohydrate of bacterial cell wall & glycoprotein of the tissues affected, or other molecular similarity between the bacteria and the tissue cell membrane
  • The streptococcal bacterial cell wall has 'M' proteins, that are highly antigenic

Path - cont

  • Antibodies produced cross-react with cardiac myofibre protein myosin, smooth muscle cells of arteries, inducing cytokine release that causes tissue destruction.
  • Inflammation occurs through direct attachment of complement and fc receptor mediated recruitment of Macrophages and Neutophills

Path -Cont

  • Profound morbidity results from affliction of the heart due to repeated inflammation alternating with fibrinous resolution
  • Cardinal anatomical changes of the Heart valves include thickening, fusion, shortening of the tendinous cords - RHD

CLINICAL FEATURES

Presents with variety of symptoms that may include:

  • Fever,
  • anorexia,
  • lethargy and
  • joint pains,
  • skin rashes, subcutaneous nodules
  • CVS symptoms
  • neurological manifestations

(1) CVS

  • Pancarditis - endocarditis, myocarditis, pericarditis
  • Key and frequent manifestation of RF
  • May present as Chest pain, palpitations, Breathlessness, tachycardia, Mitral/aortic regurgitation, cardiomegaly, CCF
  • Other hazards include embolization, Infective endocarditis

(2) CNS manifestations

  • Occurs in 10% of the patients
  • Usually a late manifestation. Latent period varies. May occur 3mths after episode of RF, or even several decades later.
  • Chorea, speech abnormalities
  • Patients should be given prophylaxis to prevent further attacks

(3) Migratory Polyarthritis

  • Early feature
  • Associated with high titres of strep antibodies
  • Present in 75% of patients
  • Characterized by acute, painful, assymetric and migratory inflammation of large joints (knees, elbow, wrist, ankle)
  • Does not affect small joints of the hands, feet
  • Joints are affected in quick succession

(4) Subcutaneous nodules

  • Rare, occurs in 10% of cases
  • Nodules found on extensor surfaces of joints, over bone tendons
  • Firm, painless
  • Seen in patients with longstanding RHD

(5) Erythema marginatum

  • An uncommon manifestation
  • Characterized by macular eruptions with rounded borders, usually concentrated on the trunk

INVESTIGATIONS

  • Lab : - Full Haemogram, ESR, CRP
  • Throat swab culture for group A B-hemolytic Strep
  • ASO titres, levels >200u(adults), >300 Children
  • CXR: cardiomegaly, pulmonary congestion
  • ECG: prolonged QRS interval, heart block
  • Echocardiography: Cardiac dilatation, valve abnormalities

DIAGNOSIS

  • Involves clinical assessment and Lab Investigations
  • Clinical diagnosis - utilizes the modified Jones criteria (after Duckett Jones, pub 1944)
  • Makes use of major and minor criteria, plus evidence of a recent strep infection (i.e. recovery of the organism on culture and antistreptolysin O antibodies)

Cont - Major criteria

  • Carditis
  • Migratory Polyarthritis
  • Chorea
  • Subcutaneous nodules
  • Erythema maginatum

Diagnosis Cont - Minor criteria

  • Fever
  • Arthralgia
  • Lab
    • Acute phase reactants, raised ESR, leucocytosis,
  • Prolonged PR interval

Diagnosis - cont

Diagnosis made based on

  • 2 or one major criteria, plus
  • 2 minor criteria
  • plus evidence of previous strep infection

MANAGEMENT

Approach

Treatment geared towards

  1. Reduction of inflammation,
  2. Treatment of +ve throat infection,
  3. Prophylaxis with continued use of low dose antibiotics
  4. Management of the resulting complications

Management - Cont

  • Infection during acute attack
    • Use of Benzyl Penicillin 1.2 mu i.m q6h for 10days, Erythromycin
    • For individuals with +ve throat culture.
  • Antibiotics will not alter the course of established disease
  • Prevention is by eradication of acute infection, and prophylaxis continued for 10yrs. Screening School Children

Management-Cont

  • Prophylaxis Benzyl Penicillin 1.2mu i.m monthly,
  • Bedrest, supportive therapy, duration guided by symptoms & markers of inflammation

Management - Cont

  • Inflammation
    • Use of NSAIDS. ASA relieves symptoms rapidly. 60-100mg/kg/day in divided doses
    • Severe symptoms of carditis and arthritis may require Corticosteroids, give Predisolone 1-2mg/kg/day in divided doses..

Management - Cont

  • Heart Failure
    • Digoxin, Diuretics, Corticosteroids (response is usually satisfactory)
    • Valve replacement if does not respond to medical treatment
