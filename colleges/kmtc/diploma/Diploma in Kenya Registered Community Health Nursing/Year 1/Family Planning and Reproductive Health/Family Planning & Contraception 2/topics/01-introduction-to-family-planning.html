Introduction to Family Planning & Reproductive Health

Definitions and Core Concepts

Family planning is a program to regulate the timing, number, and spacing of children in a family through the practice of contraception or other methods of birth control. It allows individuals and couples to anticipate and attain their desired number of children and the spacing and timing of their births. It is achieved through the use of contraceptive methods and the treatment of involuntary infertility.

This component of reproductive health enables couples and women to choose whether, when, or how often to reproduce, allowing them to delay a first or subsequent child and space them as desired. A woman's ability to space and limit her pregnancies has a direct impact on her health and well-being as well as on the outcome of each pregnancy.

Key Terminology

  • •

    Birth Control: Voluntary limitation or control/regulation of the number of children conceived, especially by the planned use of contraceptive techniques.

  • •

    Reproductive Health: A state of complete physical, mental and social well-being and not merely the absence of disease or infirmity, in all matters relating to the reproductive system, its functions and processes.

  • •

    Contraceptive Prevalence Rate (CPR): The percentage of married women of reproductive age (15-49) who are using any method of family planning, whether modern or traditional, to space or limit births.

  • •

    Total Fertility Rate (TFR): The number of children that would be born alive to a woman during her lifetime, taken as an average of a given group of women of reproductive age (15-49 years).

  • •

    Unmet Need for FP: A term used when a married woman of reproductive age wants to either space or limit births and is not using any method of family planning, despite being sexually active.

Note on Abortion

The National Policy for Population and Sustainable Development makes it clear that abortion will not be used as a method of family planning in Kenya and every attempt will be made to eliminate the need for abortion through reliable information, counselling and services.

The Importance and Rationale of Family Planning

Family planning is a critical component of reproductive health programs and a strategy to reduce high maternal, infant, and child mortality and morbidity. The importance of family planning lies in its contraceptive, non-contraceptive, and health benefits.

Benefits for the Mother and Family

  • ✓

    Improved Health: Mothers are protected against unwanted pregnancies, reducing deaths related to high-risk pregnancies and unsafe abortions. It allows pregnancies to be carried within the optimal childbearing age bracket of 19 to 35 years.

  • ✓

    Non-Contraceptive Health Benefits: Some methods, like hormonal contraceptives, can reduce anaemia and the risk of certain cancers of the genital tract. Barrier methods minimize the transmission of STIs and HIV/AIDS.

  • ✓

    Social and Economic Stability: Limiting family size allows for more resources for each family member. Socially healthy families are more productive and economically stable, participating more in community development.

  • ✓

    Empowerment: Women learn to make informed choices about their bodies and lives, and it allows for more relaxed sexual relations within a partnership.

Benefits for Children

  • ✓

    Healthier Children: Child spacing gives the mother time to nurse and care for each child, reducing deaths related to malnutrition and infection in children under five.

  • ✓

    Better Opportunities: FP contributes to ensuring children have access to adequate food, clothing, housing, and educational opportunities.

Benefits for the Nation

  • ✓

    Population Management: Helps regulate population growth to match economic growth, which can prevent poverty, crime, and environmental degradation.

  • ✓

    Economic Planning: A controlled birth rate allows the country to budget more effectively for its citizens.

  • ✓

    Reduced Health Burden: Reduced fertility and mortality rates lessen the overall burden on the nation's health system.

The Critical Role of Birth Spacing

A woman's ability to space and limit her pregnancies has a direct impact on her health and the outcome of each pregnancy. Unintended pregnancies are often associated with short between-birth intervals, which can have deadly consequences for infants and children.

WHO Recommendation

The World Health Organization (WHO) recommends waiting at least two to three years after giving birth before getting pregnant again. After a miscarriage or induced abortion, the recommended minimum interval to the next pregnancy is at least six months.

Risks of Spacing Pregnancies Too Close (less than 12-18 months)

  • !

    Increased risk of placental abruption and placenta previa.

  • !

    Increased risk of uterine rupture in women attempting vaginal birth after cesarean (VBAC).

  • !

    Increased risk of low birth weight, preterm birth, and small size for gestational age.

  • !

    Associated with an increased risk of autism in second-born children.

Risks of Spacing Pregnancies Too Far Apart (5 years or more)

  • !

    Increased risk of pre-eclampsia.

  • !

    Increased risk of preterm birth, low birth weight, and small size for gestational age.

Family Planning in Kenya: A Snapshot

Kenya was the first country in sub-Saharan Africa to establish a National Family Planning programme in 1967. The practice has increased steadily since the early 1980s, witnessing a historic decline in fertility. However, challenges remain.

Key Statistics (based on 2008-2010 data)

  • •

    Total Fertility Rate (TFR): Declined from 8.1 births per woman in 1978 to 4.6 in 2008.

  • •

    Contraceptive Prevalence Rate (CPR): Reached 46% in 2008, with modern methods rising from 32% to 39% between 2003 and 2008.

  • •

    Unmet Need for FP: Estimated at around 24-25%, indicating a significant gap between the desire to use FP and actual use.

  • •

    Birth Intervals: Half of all pregnancies occur less than 24 months after the preceding birth, with 15% occurring within less than 12 months.

  • •

    Maternal Mortality Rate (MMR): Stood at a high 488 per 100,000 live births, showing limited improvement.

Factors such as social and cultural beliefs, gender dynamics, lack of male involvement, and weak health management systems continue to impede the demand for and utilization of reproductive health care in the country.
