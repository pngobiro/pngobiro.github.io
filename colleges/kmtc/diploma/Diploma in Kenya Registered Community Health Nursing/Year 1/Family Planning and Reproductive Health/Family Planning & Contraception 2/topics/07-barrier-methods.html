Barrier Methods of Contraception

Introduction to Barrier Methods

Barrier methods work by preventing pregnancy by blocking the entry of sperm into the upper genital tract. They are generally categorized into two types:

  • 1

    Physical Barrier Methods: These devices, such as condoms, diaphragms, and cervical caps, physically block sperm from reaching the cervix.

  • 2

    Chemical Barrier Methods (Spermicides): These agents kill or inactivate sperm on contact. They are often used in combination with physical barriers to increase effectiveness.

These methods are easily available, reversible, and have fewer systemic side effects than hormonal methods. Their effectiveness is highly dependent on consistent and correct use.

The Male Condom

The male condom is a thin sheath, usually made of latex, placed over the erect penis before any vaginal insertion. It prevents pregnancy by acting as a barrier to the passage of semen into the vagina.

Effectiveness

  • Perfect Use Failure Rate: ~3% per year.
  • Typical Use Failure Rate: ~14% per year.

The difference is due to common errors like inconsistent use, incorrect placement, or using oil-based lubricants which can damage latex.

Dual Protection

Among all barrier methods, the male condom provides the most effective protection of the genital tract from Sexually Transmitted Infections (STIs), including HIV/AIDS. This is a critical non-contraceptive benefit.

AdvantagesDisadvantages
  • Provides dual protection against pregnancy and STIs/HIV.
  • Readily available and inexpensive.
  • Involves the male partner in contraception.
  • May help prevent premature ejaculation.
  • No systemic side effects.
  • User-dependent; requires correct use with every act of intercourse.
  • May decrease sexual sensation for some users.
  • Risk of breakage or slippage.
  • Some users may have a latex allergy.
  • Cannot be used with oil-based lubricants (e.g., petroleum jelly, baby oil).

Client Education: How to Use a Male Condom

  • Place the condom on the erect penis before any genital contact, leaving a small empty space at the tip to collect semen.
  • After ejaculation, withdraw the penis while still erect, holding the rim of the condom to the base of the penis to prevent spillage.
  • Use a new condom for every act of intercourse. Do not reuse condoms.
  • Store condoms in a cool, dry place, away from direct sunlight and sharp objects (not in a wallet for long periods).

The Female Condom

The female condom (e.g., Reality) is a polyurethane sheath with two flexible rings. The inner ring at the closed end is inserted into the vagina to anchor the device, while the outer ring remains outside the vaginal opening. It can be inserted up to 8 hours before intercourse.

Effectiveness

  • Perfect Use Failure Rate: ~5% per year.
  • Typical Use Failure Rate: ~21% per year.
AdvantagesDisadvantages
  • Provides protection against STIs/HIV.
  • Controlled by the woman.
  • Can be inserted up to 8 hours before intercourse.
  • Does not deteriorate with oil-based lubricants.
  • Made of polyurethane, so it is safe for those with latex allergies.
  • Higher failure rate than the male condom.
  • Can be difficult to place correctly.
  • Considered cumbersome or bulky by some users.
  • More expensive than male condoms.
  • Simultaneous use with a male condom is not recommended as they may adhere and slip.

Diaphragm and Cervical Cap

Both the diaphragm and cervical cap are latex or silicone devices inserted into the vagina before intercourse to cover the cervix. They must be used with spermicide to be effective.

The Diaphragm

A shallow, dome-shaped cup with a flexible spring rim. It is placed behind the pubic bone and into the posterior fornix, covering the cervix. It must be left in place for at least 6 hours after intercourse but removed within 24 hours.

  • Effectiveness (Typical Use): ~20% failure rate.

The Cervical Cap

A smaller, cup-shaped device that fits snugly over the base of the cervix with suction. It can be left in place for up to 48 hours.

  • Effectiveness (Typical Use): Failure rate is ~20% for nulliparous women and ~40% for parous women.

Important Considerations

  • Professional Fitting Required: Both devices must be fitted by a trained healthcare provider. Refitting is needed after childbirth or significant weight change (e.g., >20 lbs / ~9 kg).
  • Risk of Toxic Shock Syndrome (TSS): Do not leave the diaphragm in for more than 24 hours or the cervical cap for more than 48 hours.
  • Urinary Tract Infections (UTIs): Diaphragm use may increase the risk of UTIs.

Spermicidal Agents

Vaginal spermicides consist of a chemical agent (like nonoxynol-9) in a base such as foam, jelly, cream, or suppository. The agent is a surfactant that destroys the sperm cell membrane. They must be inserted into the vagina before each act of coitus.

Effectiveness

  • Perfect Use Failure Rate: ~6% per year.
  • Typical Use Failure Rate: ~26% per year.

Due to the high failure rate when used alone, spermicides are best used in combination with another barrier method like the condom or diaphragm to augment contraceptive efficacy.

Advantages and Disadvantages

  • Advantages: Easy to apply, available over-the-counter, provides lubrication.
  • Disadvantages: Low effectiveness when used alone, provides minimal protection from STIs, may cause vaginal irritation or allergic reactions in either partner. Frequent use may increase risk of HIV transmission due to irritation of the vaginal lining.
