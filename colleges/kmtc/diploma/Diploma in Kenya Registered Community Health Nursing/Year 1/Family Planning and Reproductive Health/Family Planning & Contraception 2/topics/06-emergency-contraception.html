Emergency Contraception (EC)

What is Emergency Contraception?

Emergency Contraception (EC), sometimes called post-coital contraception or the "morning-after pill," refers to methods of contraception that can be used to prevent pregnancy after unprotected sexual intercourse. It is intended for occasional, emergency use and not as a regular method of family planning.

Key Facts about EC

  • Time Window: Hormonal EC pills must be taken within 120 hours (5 days) of unprotected intercourse. The sooner they are taken, the more effective they are.
  • Effectiveness: EC reduces the risk of pregnancy from a single act of unprotected intercourse from about 8% to 2%, providing about 75-95% protection.
  • Not for Regular Use: EC is less effective than other regular contraceptive methods and should only be used in emergency situations.

When to Use Emergency Contraception

EC may be necessary in any situation where unprotected vaginal sex has occurred and the woman wishes to avoid pregnancy. This includes:

  • •

    Condom Failure: The condom broke, slipped off, or was used incorrectly.

  • •

    Missed Pills: Forgetting to take regular birth control pills (3 or more COCs, or a POP more than 3 hours late).

  • •

    Late Injection: Being more than 4 weeks late for a DMPA injection.

  • •

    Dislodged Barrier: A diaphragm, cervical cap, or IUD slipped out of place.

  • •

    Failed Withdrawal: Coitus interruptus was not performed in time.

  • •

    No Contraception Used: Sex without any birth control method.

  • •

    Forced Sex: In cases of coerced sexual intercourse or rape.

Mechanism of Action

EC pills work primarily by preventing or delaying ovulation. Depending on when in the menstrual cycle they are taken, they may also:

  • Inhibit or slow down the transportation of the egg and sperm in the fallopian tube, thus preventing fertilization.
  • Prevent implantation by altering the endometrium, making it unsuitable for a fertilized egg.

Important Clarification

Emergency contraceptives do not disrupt an existing (established) pregnancy. They are not effective once implantation has occurred and will not cause an abortion.

Types of Emergency Contraception and Dosage

Several methods can be used for emergency contraception, with Emergency Contraceptive Pills (ECPs) being the most common.

1. Progestin-Only ECPs (Levonorgestrel)

This is the most common and more effective regimen, with fewer side effects. Brands available in Kenya include Postinor-2, Pregnon, Smart Lady, and ECee2.

  • Standard Dose: Take one tablet of 750 mcg levonorgestrel, and repeat the dose 12 hours later. A total of two pills are required.
  • Single Dose (Preferred): Alternatively, take two 750 mcg levonorgestrel pills (total 1.5 mg) as a single dose. This is often preferred for better compliance.

2. Combined Oral Contraceptives (Yuzpe Method)

This method uses regular combined oral contraceptive pills, but it is less effective and causes more nausea than the progestin-only method.

  • Low-dose pills (e.g., Microgynon): Take 4 tablets stat, and repeat with 4 tablets after 12 hours. (Total of 8 pills).
  • High-dose pills (e.g., Eugynon): Take 2 tablets stat, and repeat with 2 tablets after 12 hours. (Total of 4 pills).

3. Copper T380 Intrauterine Device (IUD)

The Copper T380 IUD is the most effective form of emergency contraception. It can be inserted up to 5-7 days after unprotected intercourse.

  • Effectiveness: Reduces the risk of pregnancy by more than 99%.
  • Benefit: Can be left in place to provide highly effective, long-term contraception.

Side Effects and Management

Side effects are usually mild and temporary.

Side EffectManagement / Counseling Point
Nausea and Vomiting More common with the combined (Yuzpe) method. An anti-emetic can be taken beforehand. If vomiting occurs within 2 hours of taking the pills, the dose should be repeated.
Slight Irregular Bleeding / Spotting Reassure the client that this is common and will stop without treatment.
Change in Menstrual Cycle The next period may come a few days earlier or later than expected. If it is more than one week late, the client should be assessed for pregnancy.
Other effects Breast tenderness, headache, abdominal pain, and dizziness may also occur.

Counseling Note: It should be emphasized that EC does not protect against STIs or HIV. Recurrent demand for EC is an indication that the woman requires further counseling on other, more reliable contraceptive options.
