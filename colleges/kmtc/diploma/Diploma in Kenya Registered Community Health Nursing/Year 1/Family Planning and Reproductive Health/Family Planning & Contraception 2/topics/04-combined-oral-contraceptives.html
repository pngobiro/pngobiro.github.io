Combined Oral Contraceptives (COCs)

Introduction and Mechanism of Action

Combined Oral Contraceptives (COCs), often called "the pill," are tablets containing a combination of two synthetic hormones: estrogen (usually ethinyl estradiol) and progestin. They are one of the most widely used and effective reversible methods of contraception.

Mechanism of Action

The combination of estrogen and progestin creates a synergistic effect that prevents pregnancy primarily through the following mechanisms:

  • 1

    Suppression of Ovulation: This is the dominant mechanism. The hormones inhibit the production of Follicle-Stimulating Hormone (FSH) and Luteinizing Hormone (LH) from the pituitary gland. This prevents the ovarian follicles from maturing and releasing an egg (ovulation).

  • 2

    Thickening of Cervical Mucus: The progestin component makes the cervical mucus thick, viscous, and scanty, which creates a barrier that prevents sperm from penetrating the cervix and reaching the egg.

  • 3

    Alteration of the Endometrium: The hormones change the endometrial lining of the uterus, making it thin and unsuitable for the implantation of a fertilized egg.

COCs have no effect on an already existing pregnancy.

Types of COCs

COCs come in various formulations, typically in 21-day or 28-day packs. The 28-day packs contain 21 active (hormone) pills and 7 placebo (inactive, often iron) pills to help the user maintain a daily routine. They are classified based on their hormone dosage throughout the cycle.

  • •

    Monophasic: Each active pill contains the same constant dose of both estrogen and progestin. Examples include Microgynon, Lo-Femenal, Nordette, and Eugynon.

  • •

    Biphasic: The active pills in the packet contain two different dose combinations of estrogen and progestin.

  • •

    Triphasic: The active pills contain three different dose combinations of estrogen and progestin throughout the cycle. Examples include Logynon and Trinordial.

Benefits of COCs

Contraceptive Benefits

  • ✓

    Highly effective (failure rates of 0.1% with perfect use to 5% with typical use) and safe for the majority of women.

  • ✓

    Effective immediately when started within the first five days of the menstrual cycle.

  • ✓

    Easy to use and allows for spontaneous sexual relations.

  • ✓

    Fertility returns promptly after discontinuation.

Non-Contraceptive Health Benefits

COCs provide several significant health benefits beyond pregnancy prevention:

  • •

    Menstrual Cycle Regulation: Reduces menstrual flow (lighter, shorter periods), decreases menstrual cramps (dysmenorrhea), and can eliminate mid-cycle pain (mittelschmerz).

  • •

    Cancer Risk Reduction: Protects against ovarian and endometrial cancer. This protection increases with duration of use and persists for at least 15 years after stopping.

  • •

    Disease Prevention: Decreases the incidence of benign breast disease, symptomatic Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID), and functional ovarian cysts.

  • •

    Anemia Prevention: Reduces menstrual blood loss, which helps prevent and improve iron-deficiency anemia.

  • •

    Ectopic Pregnancy Prevention: Prevents ectopic pregnancies by suppressing ovulation.

  • •

    Other Benefits: Can be used for the treatment of acne and hirsutism (excess hair growth).

Eligibility and Contraindications (MEC)

While safe for most women, COCs are not suitable for everyone. The WHO Medical Eligibility Criteria guide their use.

Who Can Generally Use COCs (MEC Categories 1 & 2)

  • ✓

    Women of any parity, including those who have never given birth.

  • ✓

    Post-abortion women (can start within 7 days).

  • ✓

    Women with conditions like anemia, uterine fibroids, endometriosis, and a family history of breast cancer.

  • Women over 39 years, or who have uncomplicated diabetes or undiagnosed breast mass may use with extra care (Category 2).

Who Should Not Use COCs (MEC Categories 3 & 4 - Absolute Contraindications)

  • ✗

    Pregnancy: Known or suspected pregnancy.

  • ✗

    Breastfeeding: Breastfeeding mothers less than 6 weeks postpartum (Category 4) or between 6 weeks and 6 months postpartum (Category 3).

  • ✗

    Smoking: Women over 35 who smoke.

  • ✗

    Cardiovascular Disease: History of or current deep vein thrombosis (DVT), pulmonary embolism (PE), stroke, ischemic heart disease, or complicated valvular heart disease.

  • ✗

    Hypertension: Untreated or severe hypertension (BP >160/100 mmHg).

  • ✗

    Cancer: Current or past history of breast cancer; estrogen-dependent neoplasia.

  • ✗

    Liver Disease: Active or severe liver disease (acute hepatitis, severe cirrhosis, liver tumors).

  • ✗

    Migraines: Migraine headaches with aura.

  • ✗

    Undiagnosed Bleeding: Undiagnosed abnormal vaginal bleeding.

  • ✗

    Drug Interactions: Use of certain medications like rifampicin (for TB) or some anticonvulsants.

Usage Instructions

Correct and consistent use is crucial for the effectiveness of COCs.

  • Starting the Pill: Take the first pill on any day from the 1st to the 7th day of the menstrual cycle for immediate effectiveness. If started after day 7, a backup method (like condoms) is needed for the first 7 days.
  • Daily Routine: Take one pill each day at the same preferred time.
  • Packet Schedule:
    • For 21-day packs, take one pill daily for 21 days, then have a 7-day break (no pills). Start the next pack on the 8th day.
    • For 28-day packs, take one pill daily for 28 days without any break. The last 7 pills are placebos. Start a new pack immediately after finishing the old one.

Managing Missed Pills

SituationAction Required
1 pill missed Take the missed pill as soon as you remember, even if it means taking two pills in one day. Continue the rest of the pack as usual. No backup contraception is needed.
2 or more consecutive pills missed Take the most recently missed pill as soon as you remember. Discard any other missed pills. Continue taking the rest of the pack as usual. Use a backup method (e.g., condoms) or abstain from sex for the next 7 days. If pills were missed in the last week of active pills, skip the hormone-free week and start a new pack immediately. Consider emergency contraception if unprotected sex occurred.

Side Effects and Warning Signs

Minor Side Effects

These are common, especially in the first 3 months of use, and usually resolve on their own. They include:

  • Nausea and vomiting (taking the pill with food or at bedtime can help)
  • Spotting or bleeding between periods
  • Mild headaches
  • Breast fullness and tenderness
  • Slight weight gain or changes in mood
  • Amenorrhea (no menstrual bleeding)

Warning Signs (ACHES)

Serious major complications are rare but possible. Advise the client to stop taking COCs and return to the clinic immediately if she experiences any of the following warning signs, which can be remembered by the acronym ACHES:

  • A

    Abdominal pain (severe): Could indicate a liver tumor or gallbladder problem.

  • C

    Chest pain (severe), shortness of breath, or coughing up blood: Could indicate a blood clot in the lungs (pulmonary embolism) or heart attack.

  • H

    Headaches (severe): Could indicate a stroke or high blood pressure.

  • E

    Eye problems (blurred vision, flashing lights, or blindness): Could indicate a blood clot in the eye or a stroke.

  • S

    Severe leg pain (in the calf or thigh): Could indicate a blood clot in the leg (deep vein thrombosis).
