Client Assessment, Counseling, and Rights

Client Rights and Provider Needs

A rights-based approach is fundamental to providing quality family planning services. All clients have certain inalienable rights that must be respected by service providers.

The Rights of a Family Planning Client

  • ✓

    Right to Information: Clients should receive adequate, accurate, and complete information regarding the services provided to enable them to make an informed decision.

  • ✓

    Right to Access Services: All clients, including adolescents and persons with disabilities (PwDs), have the right to FP services at all levels of care.

  • ✓

    Right to Informed Choice: Clients should be counseled on the full range of available contraceptive options and be free to choose the method they prefer.

  • ✓

    Right to Safe Services: Service providers must adhere to infection-prevention practices and provide clear instructions for the effective and safe use of the chosen method.

  • ✓

    Right to Privacy and Confidentiality: Care should be individualized and discrete. Client information must be protected from access by anyone not directly involved in their care.

  • ✓

    Right to Dignity and Comfort: Clients should be treated with dignity, friendliness, and respect. Precautions should be taken to ensure minimal discomfort.

  • ✓

    Right to Continuity of Care: Client records and follow-ups should be accurately documented to ensure appropriate management and clinical safety.

Provider Staff Needs

To deliver quality care, providers also have needs that must be met by the healthcare system:

  • Supportive Supervision and Management: A work environment that emphasizes mentoring and joint problem-solving.
  • Information, Training, and Development: Ongoing opportunities for training to update knowledge and maintain a high level of performance.
  • Supplies, Equipment, and Infrastructure: Sufficient and appropriate supplies and logistics to ensure uninterrupted FP services.

The Counseling Process in Family Planning

Counseling is a person-to-person interaction where the service provider gives adequate information to enable a client to make an informed choice. It is a vital part of reproductive healthcare, helping clients select a contraceptive method they are satisfied with and use it safely and effectively.

The GATHER Approach to Counseling

The GATHER framework provides six essential steps for effective family planning counseling.

  1. G

    Greet: Greet the client, welcome them, offer a seat, and introduce yourself. Establish a good rapport and ensure privacy and confidentiality.

  2. A

    Ask: Ask the client about themselves, their needs, concerns, and any previous FP experiences. Explore their reproductive goals, STI risk, and what they already know.

  3. T

    Tell: Tell the client about the available methods of family planning and reproductive choices. Explain how they work, their advantages, disadvantages, and side effects. Correct any misinformation politely.

  4. H

    Help: Help the client make an informed choice. Based on their history and needs, help them find a suitable method. Avoid biases and ask what their sexual partner prefers.

  5. E

    Explain: Explain in detail the method the client has chosen. Provide detailed instructions on how to use it, what side effects to expect, and what to do if they occur. Ensure they understand warning signs that require a clinic visit.

  6. R

    Return/Refer: Remind the client about the return visit schedule. When they return, ask about their experience, address any problems, and answer questions. Arrange for referrals as needed.

Informed Consent with BRAIDED

When counseling, use the BRAIDED acronym to ensure all components of informed consent are covered and documented:

  • B - Benefits/Advantages
  • R - Risks/Disadvantages
  • A - Alternatives/Other methods available
  • I - Inquiries/Allow time for questions
  • D - Decisions/Opportunity to decide or change mind
  • E - Explanation/About the method and how to use it
  • D - Documentation/Record everything taught

Barriers to Effective Counseling

Several factors can hinder the counseling process:

  • Lack of privacy or confidentiality
  • Differences in age, gender, language, or level of education
  • Religious beliefs or personal biases of the provider
  • Inappropriate non-verbal communication or a judgmental attitude
  • Information overload or an unconducive environment

Client Assessment and Physical Examination

The main objective of client assessment is to ensure it is safe for the client to use a chosen contraceptive method and to identify any conditions that may require treatment, referral, or special follow-up.

How to be Reasonably Sure a Client is Not Pregnant

You can be reasonably sure a client is not pregnant if she has no clinical symptoms of pregnancy and meets any one of the following criteria:

  • •

    She is within the first 7 days after the start of her normal menses.

  • •

    She has not had sexual intercourse since her last menses.

  • •

    She has been correctly and consistently using another reliable contraceptive method.

  • •

    She is within 4 weeks postpartum (for non-breastfeeding women).

  • •

    She is fully or nearly fully breastfeeding, is less than 6 months postpartum, and her menses have not returned (LAM criteria).

  • •

    She is within the first 7 days post-abortion.

If the client answers "no" to all of the above, pregnancy should be suspected. A pregnancy test may be necessary if available.

History Taking and Physical Examination

The purpose of the history and physical exam is to obtain baseline data, screen the client for contraindications, and detect any abnormalities that may require treatment or referral.

Components of Client Assessment
ComponentDetails
History Taking Includes personal data, social history, medical history, menstrual history (LMP, cycle length), obstetric history (pregnancies, deliveries, complications), and sexual history (number of partners, STIs).
General Examination A systematic head-to-toe examination, noting general health, nutritional status, mental state, and vital signs (weight, blood pressure).
Pelvic Examination (Required for IUCDs, Diaphragm, Sterilization). Includes speculum examination to inspect the vagina and cervix, and a bimanual examination to assess the size and position of the uterus and check for any masses or tenderness. A Pap smear may be indicated.

Factors Influencing FP Access and Acceptance

Various factors can either facilitate or hinder a client's ability to access and continue using family planning services.

Factors that Influence Acceptance and Continuation

  • ✓

    Information, Education, and Counseling (IEC): Awareness created through effective counseling.

  • ✓

    Health Reasons: Desire to protect maternal and child health.

  • ✓

    Economic Reasons: Ability to adequately support the family financially.

  • ✓

    Couple's Choice & Peer Influence: Decisions made jointly by partners and influence from peers.

  • ✓

    Social Factors: Employment, desire to fit into a certain social class.

Barriers to Accessing FP Services

  • ✗

    Logistical Barriers: Distance to clinics, cost of services and supplies.

  • ✗

    Social and Cultural Barriers: Religious objections, cultural norms, and lack of partner support.

  • ✗

    Provider Bias: Judgmental attitudes from healthcare providers, especially towards adolescents or unmarried individuals.

  • ✗

    Systemic Obstacles: Poor organization of services, legal and medical regulations that restrict access.
