Intrauterine Contraceptive Devices (IUCDs)

Introduction to IUCDs

An Intrauterine Contraceptive Device (IUCD), also known as a coil or loop, is a small, flexible device that is inserted into the uterine cavity by a trained service provider. It is a safe, highly effective, and long-acting reversible contraceptive method. There are two main categories of IUCDs: copper-releasing and hormone-releasing.

Mechanism of Action

Copper-Releasing IUCDs

Copper IUCDs primarily work by preventing fertilization. They do not typically affect ovulation. The copper causes a sterile inflammatory reaction in the uterine cavity, which is toxic to sperm and ova.

The key mechanisms can be remembered by the acronym SOFIE:

  • S

    Sperm Immobilization: The copper ions and products of the inflammatory process damage sperm and prevent them from moving towards the ovum.

  • O

    Ova Transportation: The device may alter the speed of ovum transport through the fallopian tube.

  • F

    Fertilization Interference: The primary effect is to prevent sperm from fertilizing the egg.

  • I

    Implantation Prevention: The inflammatory reaction makes the endometrium hostile and unsuitable for implantation, should fertilization occur.

  • E

    Endometrial Thinning: Changes in the endometrium contribute to the hostile environment.

Hormone-Releasing IUCDs (Intrauterine Systems - IUS)

These devices (e.g., Mirena) release a small amount of progestin (levonorgestrel) directly into the uterus. Their mechanisms include:

  • Thickening the cervical mucus to obstruct the passage of sperm.
  • Suppressing the endometrium, making it thin and unsuitable for implantation.
  • Inhibiting sperm migration and function within the uterus and fallopian tubes.

Advantages and Limitations

AdvantagesDisadvantages / Limitations
  • Highly effective (failure rate <1-2%) and very safe.
  • Long-acting, continuous protection.
  • Immediately reversible with a quick return to fertility upon removal.
  • Does not interfere with sexual intercourse.
  • No systemic hormonal side effects (for copper IUCDs).
  • Can be used by breastfeeding women.
  • Reduces overall risk of ectopic pregnancy compared to no method.
  • Hormonal IUCDs (Mirena) significantly decrease menstrual blood loss and dysmenorrhea.
  • Requires a trained provider for insertion and removal.
  • Does not protect against STIs or HIV/AIDS.
  • Copper IUCDs: May increase menstrual bleeding (menorrhagia) and cramping (dysmenorrhea), especially in the first few months.
  • Hormonal IUCDs: May cause irregular bleeding or spotting initially.
  • Rare complications include uterine perforation during insertion, expulsion of the device, and an increased risk of Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID) in the first 20 days after insertion if an underlying infection is present.

Addressing Myths about the IUCD

  • ✗

    IUCDs are not abortifacients; they primarily work by preventing fertilization.

  • ✗

    IUCDs do not cause infertility.

  • ✗

    IUCDs do not travel to distant parts of the body like the heart or brain.

  • ✗

    They rarely cause discomfort to the male partner.

Contraindications and Warning Signs

Absolute Contraindications (MEC Category 4)

An IUCD should NOT be used if a woman has:

  • Known or suspected pregnancy.
  • Current Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID), purulent cervicitis, or pelvic tuberculosis.
  • Post-septic abortion.
  • Unexplained abnormal vaginal bleeding suspicious for a serious underlying condition.
  • Uterine or cervical cancer.
  • Congenital uterine abnormalities that significantly distort the uterine cavity.
  • High individual risk of STIs (e.g., multiple sexual partners).

Warning Signs for Clients (PAINS)

Clients should be instructed to return to the clinic immediately if they experience any of the following signs, which can be remembered by the acronym PAINS:

  • P

    Period late, abnormal spotting or bleeding.

  • A

    Abdominal pain, pain with intercourse.

  • I

    Infection exposure (abnormal vaginal discharge), chills or fever.

  • N

    Not feeling well, fever, chills.

  • S

    Strings missing, shorter, or longer.

Client Education

Counsel the client to check for the strings regularly, especially after her period, to ensure the IUCD is still in place. Reassure her that initial cramping and heavier bleeding are common with the copper IUCD but should improve. Provide a follow-up appointment schedule (e.g., at 6 weeks, then yearly).
