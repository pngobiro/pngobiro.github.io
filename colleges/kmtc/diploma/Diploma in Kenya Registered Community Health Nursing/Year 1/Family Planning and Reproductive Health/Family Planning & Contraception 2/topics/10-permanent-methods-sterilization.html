Permanent Methods: Voluntary Surgical Contraception (VSC)

Introduction to Sterilization

Voluntary Surgical Contraception (VSC), or sterilization, includes safe and simple surgical procedures for both males and females that are intended to provide permanent contraception. These methods are highly effective and are generally chosen by individuals or couples who are certain they do not want any more children.

Who Can Use Sterilization?

This method is suitable for men, women, and couples who:

  • •

    Are certain they have completed their desired family size.

  • •

    Want a highly effective, permanent form of contraception.

  • •

    Have difficulty with temporary methods that require compliance or re-supply.

  • •

    Have a medical condition for whom a future pregnancy would pose a serious health risk.

There are no medical restrictions based on age or parity alone.

Essential Counseling for VSC

Thorough and careful counseling is absolutely necessary before a sterilization procedure to ensure the client's decision is voluntary and fully informed, helping to avoid future regret.

Key Counseling Points

  • Informed Choice: The client must be informed about all other available contraceptive options, including long-acting reversible methods like IUCDs and implants.
  • Permanence: It must be clearly understood that the procedure is permanent and generally irreversible. While reversal surgery exists, its success cannot be guaranteed and it is often expensive and difficult.
  • Voluntary Consent: The client must sign a standard consent form voluntarily, without any coercion or incentives. They are free to change their mind at any time before the procedure. Spousal consent is not mandatory but counseling both partners is ideal.
  • Procedure Details: Explain the procedure, its effectiveness, and possible complications or side effects.
  • No STI Protection: Sterilization does not protect against Sexually Transmitted Infections (STIs), including HIV/AIDS. If at risk, condom use is still necessary.

Female Sterilization (Bilateral Tubal Ligation - BTL)

Female sterilization prevents fertilization by mechanically blocking the fallopian tubes, preventing the sperm from reaching the oocyte. It is a highly effective and immediate form of contraception.

Effectiveness: Very effective, with a cumulative 10-year failure rate of approximately 1.85% (varying by method from 0.8% to 3.7%).

Methods of Tubal Ligation

  • Minilaparotomy: A small abdominal incision, common in postpartum or post-abortion settings.
  • Laparoscopy: A minimally invasive procedure using a laparoscope, often performed as an interval procedure.
  • Methods include using clips, rings, or bands for occlusion; segmental destruction with electrocoagulation; or suture ligation with partial salpingectomy.
AdvantagesDisadvantages / Risks
  • Permanent and highly effective.
  • Effective immediately.
  • Does not change sexual function or interfere with intercourse.
  • No significant long-term side effects; does not affect breastfeeding.
  • Decreased risk of ovarian cancer.
  • Involves a surgical procedure with risks of anesthesia, infection, bleeding, or injury to other organs.
  • Considered irreversible; possibility of future regret.
  • If pregnancy does occur (rarely), it is more likely to be ectopic.
  • More expensive and complex than vasectomy.

Male Sterilization (Vasectomy)

Vasectomy involves the division and occlusion of the vas deferens on each side to prevent sperm from mixing with seminal fluid during ejaculation. It is a simpler, safer, and less expensive procedure than female sterilization, usually performed under local anesthesia in an outpatient setting.

Effectiveness: Highly effective, with a failure rate of approximately 0.1%.

Delayed Effectiveness

A vasectomy is not effective immediately. Remnant sperm remain in the ejaculatory ducts. The man is not considered sterile until he has produced sperm-free ejaculates, which typically requires 15-20 ejaculations or about 12 weeks. A backup contraceptive method must be used during this time.

AdvantagesDisadvantages / Risks
  • Permanent and highly effective.
  • Safe, quick outpatient procedure.
  • No hormones involved.
  • No significant long-term side effects.
  • More cost-effective than tubal ligation.
  • Reversal is difficult, expensive, and often unsuccessful.
  • Not effective immediately; requires backup contraception.
  • Does not protect against STIs.
  • Minor short-term discomfort, and risks of complications like hematoma, infection, or sperm granulomas.
  • Possibility of patient regret.

Correcting Myths and Misconceptions

Counseling should address common myths associated with sterilization.

Myths About Female Sterilization (Tubal Ligation)

  • ✗

    Myth: It causes a loss of sexual desire or makes a woman "less of a woman."
    Fact: It does not affect hormones, libido, or sexual function.

  • ✗

    Myth: Eggs build up inside the body.
    Fact: The body safely reabsorbs the unfertilized eggs.

  • ✗

    Myth: It stops menstruation.
    Fact: Menstruation continues as the hormonal cycle is unaffected.

Myths About Male Sterilization (Vasectomy)

  • ✗

    Myth: Vasectomy is the same as castration.
    Fact: Vasectomy does not involve removal of the testicles and does not affect testosterone production, male physical traits, or strength.

  • ✗

    Myth: The quantity of ejaculate is reduced.
    Fact: Sperm make up a very small portion of semen volume, so the change is unnoticeable.

  • ✗

    Myth: It causes a loss of sexual desire or ability.
    Fact: It does not affect libido, erections, or sexual pleasure.

  • ✗

    Myth: Sperm build up in the body.
    Fact: The body safely reabsorbs unused sperm cells.

Medical Eligibility Criteria for VSC

The WHO criteria for VSC are adapted to guide providers on when it is safe to perform the procedure.

VSC Eligibility Categories
CategoryDescription
Accept (A) No medical reason prevents performing the procedure in a routine setting.
Caution (C) The procedure can be performed in a routine setting, but with extra preparation and precautions.
Delay (D) Delay the procedure until a specific medical condition is treated or resolved. Provide a temporary method in the meantime.
Special-Refer (S) The procedure requires a special setting with an experienced surgeon, specific equipment, and/or specialist medical support. Refer the client and provide a temporary method.
