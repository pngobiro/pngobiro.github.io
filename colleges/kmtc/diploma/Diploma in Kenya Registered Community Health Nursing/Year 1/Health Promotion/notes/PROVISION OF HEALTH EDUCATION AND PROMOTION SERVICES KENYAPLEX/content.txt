<p>Relationship to Occupational Standards
This unit addresses the Unit of Competency: Provide Health Education and Promotion Services</p>
<p>Duration of Unit: 50 hours</p>
<p>Unit Description
This unit specifies the competencies required to Provide Health Education and Promotion. It involves identifying and assessing health education and promotion needs, developing objectives, planning, implementing, evaluating and documenting health promotion and education activities. Its main objective is to enlighten a person or a community on healthcare.</p>

<li>Plan health education and promotion</li>
<li>Implement action plan for health education and promotion</li>
<li>Monitor and evaluate health education and promotion</li>
<li>Document health education and promotion</li>
</ol>

<p>Basics community health assessment techniques</p>
<p>Needs Assessment:</p>
<p>A Framework for Identifying Community Needs</p>
<p>FIVE NEEDS ASSESSMENT TECHNIQUES</p>
<p>Five commonly utilized approaches for gathering new information on the needs of community residents. They are:</p>
<ol>
<li>The key informant approach.</li>
<li>The public forum approach.</li>
<li>The nominal group process technique.</li>
<li>The Delphi technique.</li>
<li>The survey approach.</li>
</ol>
<p>KEY INFORMANT APPROACH</p>
<p>Purpose</p>
<p>To collect information from those community residents who, because of their professional training and/or affiliation with particular organizations, agencies, or associations, are in a prime position to know what the needs facing the community are likely to be.</p>
<p>Approach</p>
<p>A brief interview or survey is conducted by one or more sponsoring organizations, agencies, or associations, and administered to community residents identified as "key informants."</p>
<p>The data derived from these procedures can be used by the sponsoring group to obtain a more comprehensive viewpoint of what the needs facing the public are.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="types-of-key-informants">Types of Key Informants<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#types-of-key-informants" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Elected officials (e.g., mayors, commissioners, etc.)</p>
<p>Key persons in institutional areas of the community (e.g., religious leaders, bankers, public safety officials, school administrators, hospital administrators, etc.)</p>
<p>Agency administrators (e.g., social service department)</p>
<p>Leaders of public service organizations (e.g., Chamber of Commerce, Kiwanis Club)</p>
<p>Professionals in specific service areas (e.g., physicians, lawyers, school faculty, etc.)</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="how-to-implement-the-key-informant-approach">How to Implement the Key Informant Approach<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#how-to-implement-the-key-informant-approach" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ol>
<li>Compile a list of "key informants" by name.</li>
<li>Decide how you want to collect information from these key informants—via questionnaires, interviews, or meetings (perhaps all).</li>
<li>Construct a brief questionnaire and/or interview form which can be used to obtain the information you need.</li>
<li>Gather data.</li>
<li>Organize data.</li>
<li>Interpret data.</li>
<li>Schedule a meeting with key informants. Present the findings of your study to them. Discuss your interpretations and their interpretations of the data</li>
</ol>
<p>The instrument administered to key informants should contain questions that will successfully elicit the type of information needed to identify community needs.</p>
<p>This might include the following types of questions:</p>
<p>The key informant's perceptions (or attitudes) of general community needs, or needs that might exist within specific areas of the community (e.g., the local economy, public education, or health services);</p>
<p>(2) his/her perceptions (or attitudes) concerning what is currently being done about meeting those needs;
(3) his/her ideas as to what should be done about resolving needs that remain unmet.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="advantages">Advantages<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#advantages" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ol>
<li>
<p>One of the easiest and least expensive ways to systematically assess needs.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Opportunity to establish rapport and trust and thus obtain the insiders' view.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Depth of information concerning causes or reasons.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Permits continual clarification of ideas and information.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Can be combined effectively with other techniques.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Permits input from many individuals with different perspectives on the needs of the community.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Does not involve the high cost of printing and data analysis.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>May help initiate (or strengthen) the lines of communication on among service organizations, agencies, and associations.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Discussion of the findings with the key informants promotes insights for all concerned.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The data collection instruments are usually easier to construct than those associated with the Survey Approach.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Can be implemented by community volunteers, thereby building citizen involvement and awareness</p>
</li>
</ol>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="disadvantages">Disadvantages<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#disadvantages" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ol>
<li>The information derived from this technique may represent a "biased perspective": information is typically elicited from "providers of services" (as opposed to the "consumers" of services).</li>
<li>The information derived from key informants often represents the perspectives (and biases) of the organization, agencies, and associations with which these informants are associated.</li>
<li>A group meeting held to "feedback" the findings of the study to the key informants may only work to rigidify a "provider" bias in terms of clarifying what the real needs are</li>
<li>Personal relationships between researchers and informants may influence type of data obtained.</li>
<li>Jealousies and resentment on the part of other community members whose opinions are not solicited may develop.</li>
<li>Should be combined with other methods, because representativeness of total community is difficult to achieve.</li>
<li>Few people can sense all the needs and concerns of all people in a community—the perspectives of those who are less visible may be overlooked.</li>
</ol>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="the-public-forum-approach">THE PUBLIC-FORUM APPROACH<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-public-forum-approach" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="purpose">Purpose<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#purpose" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>To elicit information from a wide range of community residents concerning issues and community needs via group discussion taking place at a series of public meetings</p>
<p>Who Should Attend Forums?</p>
<p>Open invitation (encourage all members of the community to attend). Special invitation to "key informants," such as those types previously considered under the Key Informant Approach.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="how-to-implement-the-public-forum-approach">How to Implement the Public Forum Approach<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#how-to-implement-the-public-forum-approach" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<ol>
<li>Develop a list of discussion questions that will serve as the basis for group discussion.</li>
</ol>
<p>Such questions as:</p>
<p>What are the most important needs facing our community?</p>
<p>Why are these important needs?</p>
<p>What have we done to help meet these needs in the past?</p>
<p>Where have we failed in the past in our attempt to meet these needs?</p>
<ol start="2">
<li>Select a strategically located place for the initial meeting.</li>
</ol>
<p>Try to select a meeting place that you feel will be conducive to the open interchange of ideas.</p>
<p>Also, select a site that is geographically and socially acceptable to all segments of the population.</p>
<ol start="3">
<li>
<p>Publicize the purpose, data, and place at which the forum will be held. Use the media as much as possible</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The group sponsoring the initial forum should take the initiative in conducting the first meeting.</p>
</li>
</ol>
<p>A person representing the group should be responsible for communication of the purpose of the forum to those present and what the meeting hopes to accomplish.</p>
<p>Another person representing the sponsoring group should be responsible for recording ideas and suggestions presented at the meeting</p>
<ol start="5">
<li>after stating the purpose, objective, and "ground rules" for the initial forum, the discussion leader should pose the questions prepared in advance to the audience.</li>
</ol>
<p>Encourage the open discussion and interchange of ideas</p>
<ol start="6">
<li>
<p>If the participants are on the right track, you'll find the recommendations for topics to consider and/or directions to consider for possible next meetings will "come from the floor."</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Make sure the recorder gets the names of all the participants so they may be personally contacted prior to the next forum.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Recognize that unlike the other needs-assessment approaches discussed thus far, you'll probably need to "play it by ear" more with the Public Forum Approach.</p>
</li>
</ol>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="advantages">Advantages<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#advantages" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ol>
<li>
<p>Offers a good way to elicit opinions from a wide range of the citizenry.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Provides an opportunity for citizens to actively participate in the needs assessment process.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Participants in the forums may offer able assistance to decision makers after the needs assessment process is completed</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Often contributes to enhancing the lines of communication between the "providers" and "consumers" of services and programs.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Perhaps the least expensive of all the systematic needs assessment approaches. It is also one of the easiest to implement.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Can provide a quick, intensive picture of community concerns.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Gives community issues broad visibility.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Useful to identify problems, assess needs, or to suggest questions requiring further study.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Design is flexible—a variety of techniques can be incorporated.</p>
</li>
</ol>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="disadvantages">Disadvantages<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#disadvantages" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ol>
<li>The burden will be squarely on the sponsoring organizations, agencies, or associations to encourage participation.</li>
<li>Require good leadership and advance organization.</li>
<li>Opinions obtained are limited to those who attend—all viewpoints may not be heard.</li>
<li>Participants in the forums may actually represent a variety of "vested interest" groups.</li>
<li>Poor advance planning and advertising may result in limited participation</li>
<li>Participants in forums may use the sessions as a vehicle to publicize their grievances ("gripes") about local organizations or agencies.</li>
<li>If not well-facilitated, only the vocal minorities will be heard.</li>
<li>A large turnout may prevent everyone from speaking and may limit time allowed for each speaker.</li>
<li>May generate more questions than answers.</li>
<li>The forums may bring about unrealistic expectations in the minds of the participants in terms of what "providers" can do to help meet needs.</li>
</ol>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="the-nominal-group-process-approach">THE NOMINAL GROUP PROCESS APPROACH<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-nominal-group-process-approach" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="purpose">Purpose<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#purpose" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>An idea generating strategy to gather individual’s ideas in a face-to-face non-threatening situations.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="approach">Approach<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#approach" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The nominal group process is intended to maximize creative participation of group members. Input from all participants is required. The process takes advantage of each person’s knowledge and experience.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="how-to-implement-the-nominal-group-process-approach">How to Implement the Nominal Group Process Approach<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#how-to-implement-the-nominal-group-process-approach" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The following steps outline one general approach to using the process:</p>
<ol>
<li>If a large number of participants are involved, divide participants into small groups of 6 to 20 persons.</li>
<li>Members of the group write their individuals ideas on paper.</li>
<li>Each person discusses his/her ideas and all concerns are listed on a chart or board.</li>
<li>Each idea is discussed, clarified, and evaluated by the group.</li>
<li>Each person assigns priorities by silent ballot.</li>
<li>Group priorities are tallied.</li>
<li>Discussion of final group priorities.</li>
</ol>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="advantages">Advantages<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#advantages" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ol>
<li>
<p>If well-organized in advance, a heterogeneous group can move toward definite conclusions.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Can be used to expand the data obtained from surveys or existing documents, or can be used to generate a more specific survey.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Motivates all participants to get involved because they sense they are personally affected.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Generates many ideas in a short period of time; allows for a full range of individuals' thoughts and concerns.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>A good way to obtain input from people of different backgrounds and experiences.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Gives all participants an equal opportunity to express opinions and ideas in a non-threatening setting.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Stimulates creative thinking and effective dialogue. 8. Allow for clarification of ideas</p>
</li>
</ol>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="disadvantages">Disadvantages<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#disadvantages" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ol>
<li>
<p>May be extremely difficult to implement with large audiences unless advance preparation has taken place to train group facilitators and divide participants into groups of 6 to 10 members</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Process may appear rigid if group leader does not show flexibility—encourage agenda building, and show respect for all ideas and concerns.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>May be some overlap of ideas due to unclear wording or inadequate group discussion.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>"Knowledgeable" individuals selected to participate may not represent all community subgroups.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>(Confident and forceful) personalities may dominate unless leadership skills are exercised.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>not be a sufficient source of data in itself; may require follow-up survey, observations or documentary analysis</p>
</li>
</ol>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="the-delphi-technique">THE DELPHI TECHNIQUE<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-delphi-technique" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="purpose">Purpose<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#purpose" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>An idea-generating strategy that does not require face-to-face interaction, although it also can be used in small groups or workshop setting.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="approach">Approach<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#approach" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The Delphi technique is more structured than the nominal group process and uses a series of questionnaires and summarized feedback reports from preceding responses.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="how-to-implement-the-delphi-technique">How to Implement the Delphi Technique<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#how-to-implement-the-delphi-technique" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The following steps outline a general approach for using the Delphi technique:</p>
<ol>
<li>
<p>Develop a questionnaire focusing on identified issues: problems, causes, solutions, actions. The intent is for each respondent to list ideas regarding the specified issue.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Distribute the questionnaire to an appropriate group of respondents.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Each respondent independently generates ideas in answering the questions and returns the questionnaires.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Summarize the questionnaires into a feedback report and develop a second questionnaire for the same respondent group.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The second questionnaire should ask respondents to prioritized or rank input from the first round.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Distribute feedback summary and second questionnaire.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Respondents review feedback report, independently rate priority ideas in second questionnaire, and return response</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>This process is repeated until general agreement is reached on problems, causes, solutions, and actions.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>A final summary and feedback report is prepared and distributed to respondents.</p>
</li>
</ol>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="advantages">Advantages<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#advantages" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ol>
<li>Allows participants to remain anonymous.</li>
<li>Inexpensive.</li>
<li>Free of social pressure, personality influence, and individual dominance.</li>
<li>Allows sharing of information and reasoning among participants.</li>
<li>Conducive to independent thinking and gradual formulation.</li>
<li>A well-selected respondent panel—a mix of local officials, knowledgeable individuals, citizens of the community, regional official, academic social scientists, etc.—can provide a broad analytical perspective on local problems and concerns.</li>
<li>Can be used to reach consensus among groups hostile to each other.</li>
</ol>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="disadvantages">Disadvantages<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#disadvantages" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ol>
<li>Judgements are those of a selected group of people and may not be representative.</li>
<li>Tendency to eliminate extreme positions and force a middle-of-the-road consensus.</li>
<li>More time-consuming than the nominal group process.</li>
<li>Should not be viewed as a total solution.</li>
<li>Requires skill in written communication.</li>
<li>requires adequate time and participant commitment (about 30 to 45 days to complete the entire process).</li>
</ol>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="the-survey-approach">THE SURVEY APPROACH<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-survey-approach" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="purpose">Purpose<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#purpose" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>To collect information from a wide range of community residents concerning issues and community needs via their responses to specific questions included in an interview schedule or questionnaire.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="approach">Approach<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#approach" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>Information (data) is gathered through a carefully developed instrument administered to individuals identified via a sampling procedure.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="advantages">Advantages<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#advantages" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<ol>
<li>
<p>Perhaps the best approach for eliciting the attitudes of a broad range of individuals.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The data obtained are usually valid and reliable.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Techniques—mail survey, telephone survey, personal interview, drop-off and pick-up survey—may be selected in relation to desired cost or response rate.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Can be used to survey an entire population and provide an opportunity for many persons to feel involved in the decision-making process.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Secures information from individuals who may be the recipients of services initiated as a result of the findings, thereby eliciting data from individuals who are usually in a good position of critique present services.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Can be used to record behaviors as well as opinions, attitudes, knowledge, and beliefs.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>An excellent technique to use in conjunction with other systematic needs assessment techniques</p>
</li>
</ol>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="disadvantages">Disadvantages<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#disadvantages" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ol>
<li>This approach is often the most costly.</li>
<li>To ensure statistical meaning, samples must be carefully selected.</li>
<li>Results may not be valid if survey is not designed correctly.</li>
<li>May require time and expertise to develop the survey, train interviewers, conduct interviews, and analyze results.</li>
<li>Is subject to misinterpretation depending on how the questions and response categories are designed.</li>
<li>Tendency for scope of data to be limited—omission of underlying reasons, and actual behavioural patterns.</li>
<li>Individuals sometimes hesitant to answer questions. Individuals who do answer questions sometimes answer them in the most desirable way (i.e., perhaps their answers represent what they think the authors want to hear, not necessarily how the respondents really feel.</li>
<li>Surveys are often "one shot" affairs. For example, persons responding to a needs survey may not be resurveyed again in the future.</li>
<li>Individuals' attitudes can change rapidly. Attitudes can change due to a variety of "intervening factors."</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="principles-of-health-promotion">Principles of health promotion<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#principles-of-health-promotion" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p><strong>Empowerment</strong> – a way of working to enable people to gain greater control over decisions and actions affecting their health.</p>
<p><strong>Participative</strong> – where people take an active part in decision making.</p>
<p><strong>Holistic</strong> – taking account of the separate influences on health and the interaction of these dimensions.</p>
<p><strong>Equitable</strong> – ensuring fairness of outcomes for service users.</p>
<p><strong>Intersectoral</strong> – working in partnership with other relevant agencies/organisations.</p>
<p><strong>Sustainable</strong> – ensuring that the outcomes of health promotion activities are sustainable in the long term.</p>
<p><strong>Multi Strategy</strong> – working on a number of strategy areas such as programmes, policy.</p>
<p>The World Health Organisation took a leading role in action for health promotion in the 1980's with, the Ottawa Charter been published in 1986. It suggested that health promotion happens at five key levels.</p>
<ol>
<li>Developing Personal Skills</li>
<li>Creating Supportive Environments</li>
<li>Strengthening Community Action</li>
<li>Developing Public Policy</li>
<li>Re- orienting the Health Services.</li>
</ol>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="what-is-a-community-health-action-plan">WHAT IS A COMMUNITY HEALTH ACTION PLAN?<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#what-is-a-community-health-action-plan" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>A CHAP is a written document that lists plans for achieving health improvements in the community. This is developed in response to needs and gaps identified from data collection.</p>
<p>The plan describes:</p>
<ul>
<li>What your community wants to accomplish</li>
<li>What needs to be done, by whom, how it will be done, and by when</li>
<li>What resources (i.e. money, people) are needed to be successful</li>
<li>Who will be most affected</li>
</ul>
<p>Following a plan makes reaching far-off goals and changes possible</p>
<p>Having an action plan: (importance of an action plan)</p>
<ul>
<li>Lends credibility to your organization. An action plan shows members of the community (including grant makers) that your organization is well ordered and dedicated to getting things done.</li>
<li>Prevents the possibility of overlooking details.</li>
<li>Helps understand what is and isn't possible for your organization to do.</li>
<li>Helps your community be more efficient by saving time, energy, and resources.</li>
<li>Increase the chances that people will do what needs to be done by making them more accountable</li>
</ul>
<p>community Health Action Plan Terms</p>
<p>Vision Where the coalition is headed, what it is trying to do, what is important and why</p>
<p>Mission The coalition’s principles and purpose, determines the focus and sets the direction</p>
<p>Goals What is expected to be achieved in the long run</p>
<p>Objectives The process steps to meet the goals and how the coalition plans to achieve them</p>
<p>Activities The work of the community coalition that drives and supports the objectives and goals</p>
<p>Outcomes The desired effect on the community, what the measure of success would be</p>
<p>PLANNING STEPS</p>
<p>The following steps are meant to be a guide for developing a comprehensive CHAP.</p>
<p>Review and analyse results of community assessment. By the time an action plan is ready to be developed, there has been a substantial amount of information collected from data</p>
<p>Review and analyse the feedback and information gathered from the community; it is very significant and can provide some clues and priorities for what needs to be addressed in the plan.</p>
<p>Choose a group of people to work together on developing the CHAP. The writing of the plan can be limited to one or two main people.</p>
<p>The process of developing the CHAP should be a collaborative/partnership effort;</p>
<p>Too many writers can result in a fragmented plan</p>
<p>Identify outcomes that would address the issues.</p>
<p>Outcomes are focused at producing a healthy change in the community.</p>
<p>It is important to think through what would be most appropriate and achievable for those who will be carrying out the plan.</p>
<p>After the parameters of the overall project are determined, the next step is to set a goal for each outcome to be addressed and add objectives and activities that support the work</p>
<p>Identify challenges, obstacles, or potential barriers to successfully implementing interventions.</p>
<p>Part of deciding what would be most effective and achievable is to examine potential barriers that may exist to prevent successfully implementing interventions to address and improve the community’s health status</p>
<p>Identify necessary resources and where you will obtain them. A key task is to identify the abilities, capacity, skill sets, duties, and responsibilities of all people that will be asked to do the work of the CHAP.</p>
<p>The plan requires many people and organizations to contribute their unique assets and resources</p>
<p>Choose individuals and community partners who will support, identify with, and implement goals and objectives.</p>
<p>It is important for people to identify with the outcomes, goals, and objectives of the action plan to participate in the activities that will drive it.</p>
<p>Consider a timeline for conducting activities. Time is a valuable resource so it is important to state clearly and realistically community member’s contributions to the various parts of the plan.</p>
<p>Creating a timeline provides everyone working on the CHAP with a clear idea of what activities should be done and when to expect activities to be accomplished</p>
<p>Include monitoring and evaluation activities. It is essential to know how the plan is progressing as you carry out the objectives and activities—this is where evaluation fits in.</p>
<p>Review the completed action plan. Carefully check for completeness, make sure you are not leaving anything out that will affect the intended outcome(s) of the initiative</p>

<p>What is Resource Allocation?
Resource allocation is the distribution of resources – usually financial – among competing groups of people or programs.</p>
<p>When we talk about allocation of funds for healthcare, we need to consider three distinct levels of decision-making</p>
<p>Level 1: Allocating resources to healthcare versus other social needs.
Level 2: Allocating resources within the healthcare sector.
Level 3: Allocating resources among individual patients</p>
<p>Level 1: At this level, community members consider how to distribute the funds among one, two or three of the competing programs.</p>
<p>For example, should the funding be split in three equal portions or should one program, possibly under-funded in the past, get all or most of the money?</p>
<p>Level 2: Assuming that healthcare gets a portion of the KSH 100,000, the next decision community members face is how best to direct the spending among competing healthcare interests.</p>
<p>Should most or all of the funds go to hospital care and medical equipment? What about the public education program that promotes healthy lifestyles</p>
<p>and Level 3: The next level of decision making involves distributing the financial resources among individuals.</p>
<p>Most communities have policies and guidelines to insure fairness in these situations.</p>
<p>Decisions at this level include:
Who gets the next available heart for transplant? And, who sees the doctor first when there are many people
waiting in an emergency room? Behaviours (like exercise or immunizations) that prevent disease?</p>
<p>Implement action plan for health education and promotion</p>
<p>Levels of health education and promotion</p>
<p>The five action/level areas of health promotion:
1 Developing Personal Skills
2 Reorienting Health Services
3 Strengthen Community Action
4 Creating Supportive Environments
5 Building Healthy Public Policy</p>
<p>Developing Personal Skills – Improving this area increases an individual’s ability to access health information and services, enabling them to make an informed health decision.</p>
<p>Reorienting Health Services – Provides greater access to health services for those who are disadvantaged (ATSI).</p>
<p>These services support equity by providing quality health services.</p>
<p><strong>Strengthen Community Action</strong> – Describes access at a community level to things like a Healthy School canteen, exercise groups or opportunities to participate in groups which promote principles of diversity and equity in health</p>
<p><strong>Creating Supportive Environments</strong> – Improves the living conditions of individuals by increasing equity and improving access to health services and facilities. Local communities can create healthy environments by providing parks, facilities and other safe areas for individuals to enjoy and utilise.</p>
<p><strong>Building Healthy Public Policy</strong> – The government is responsible for acknowledging diversity and equity and has created initiatives like Medicare, beyond zero to ensure every individual has access to health care.</p>
<p>The Government can also create supportive environments by promoting health campaigns and introducing legislation, like no-smoking laws, to improve quality of life.</p>
<p><strong>Implementing action plans and policies on health education</strong></p>
<p>The word ‘implementation’ means to carry out.</p>
<p>It is the act of converting your planning, goals, objectives and strategies into action.</p>
<p>In other words, it is converting your planned activities into action — according to a plan of work.</p>
<p>Conducting health education activities at a community gathering, or during home visits, are examples of implementation, or carrying out health education activities.</p>
<p>Community members should be involved in all your health education activities whenever possible.</p>
<p>This should improve the uptake of your health education interventions, and enable you to pool community resources, including labour power. If the community seems reluctant to participate in health education activities, your plans stand much less chance of being successfully implemented.</p>
<p>In order to avoid this difficulty, you should try to make sure that as many members of the community as possible are ready to participate in health education activities.</p>
<p>To ensure participation, you should organise the community, and discuss with them the issues that you feel are important for the implementation of health education activities in their locality.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="1-health-talks">1. <p>You may consider that the best way of communicating your health messages in certain situations is by using health talks.</p>
<p>Talking is often the most natural way of communicating with people to share health knowledge and facts.</p>
<p>In the part of your job that involves health education, there will always be many opportunities to talk with people</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="2-lecture">2. Lecture<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#2-lecture" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>A lecture is usually a spoken, simple, quick and traditional way of presenting your subject matter, but there are strengths and limitations to this approach.</p>
<p>The strengths include the efficient introduction of factual material in a direct and logical manner.</p>
<p>However, this method is generally ineffective where the audience is passive and learning is difficult to gauge.</p>
<p>Experts are not always good teachers and communication in a lecture may be one-way with no feedback from the audience.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="3-group-discussion-involves-the-free-flow-of-communication-between-a-facilitator-and-two-or-more-participants">3. Group discussion involves the free flow of communication between a facilitator and two or more participants.<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#3-group-discussion-involves-the-free-flow-of-communication-between-a-facilitator-and-two-or-more-participants" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Often a discussion of this type is used after a slide show or following a more formal presentation.</p>
<p>This type of teaching method is characterised by participants having an equal chance to talk freely and exchange ideas with each other.</p>
<p>In most group discussions the subject of the discussion can be taken up and shared equally by all the members of the group</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="4-demonstration">4. Demonstration<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#4-demonstration" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>In your work as a health educator you will often find yourself giving a demonstration.</p>
<p>This form of health education is based on learning through observation.</p>
<p>There is a difference between knowing how to do something and actually being able to do it.</p>
<p>The aim of a demonstration is to help learners become able to do the skills themselves, not just know how to do them</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="5-role-play">5. Role play<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#5-role-play" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>In role play, some of the participants take the roles of other people and act accordingly.</p>
<p>Role play is usually a spontaneous or unrehearsed acting out of real-life situations where others watch and learn by seeing and discussing how people might behave in certain situations.</p>

It is a suitable teaching method for people who cannot read, because they often experience things visually. However the preparation and practice for a drama may cost time and money.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="3-basic-strategies">3 Basic Strategies<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#3-basic-strategies" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>There are three basic health promotion strategies</p>
<ol>
<li>Health communication helps people better understand health needs, equipping them for important individual health decisions.</li>
</ol>
<p>Taking verbal and written form, health communication encourages healthful activities and improves health literacy.</p>
<p>It also bridges understanding with cultural considerations about health. Flu vaccination posters, brochures for health screenings and discussions with patients about heart disease are all health communications.</p>
<ol start="2">
<li>Health education teaches people about different health conditions to boost prevention or early treatment.</li>
</ol>
<p>These learning sessions are often focused on target populations.</p>
<p>For example, poorer neighbourhoods may be candidates for lessons on nutrition and physical activity to prevent or combat Type 2 diabetes.</p>

<p>Policy change would include smoke-free entryways and safety equipment in the workplace.</p>
<p>Systems change deals with the use of innovations, technologies or certifications to change how health problems are solved.</p>
<p>Environmental change works to solve population health challenges with more parks and nutritious food availability</p>
