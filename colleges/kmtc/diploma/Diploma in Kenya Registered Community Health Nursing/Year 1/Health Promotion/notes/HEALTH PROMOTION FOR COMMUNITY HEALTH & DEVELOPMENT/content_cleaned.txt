<p>Before discussing about health education, it is imperative to conceptualize what health itself means. Health is a highly subjective concept. Good health means different things to different people, and its meaning varies according to individual and community expectations and context. Many people consider themselves healthy if they are free of disease or disability. However, people who have a disease or disability may also see themselves as being in good health if they are able to manage their condition so that it does not impact greatly on their quality of life.</p>

<p>While the history of health education as an emerging profession is only a little over one hundred years old, the concept of educating about health has been around since the dawn of humans. It does not stretch the imagination too far to begin to see how health education first took place during pre-historic era. Some one may have eaten a particular plant or herb and become ill. That person would then warn (educate) others against eating the same substance. Conversely, someone may have ingested a plant or herb that produced a desired effect. That person would then encourage (educate) others to use this substance.</p>

<p>Health education has been defined in many ways by different authors and experts. Lawrence Green defined it as “a combination of learning experiences designed to facilitate voluntary actions conducive to health.”</p>
<p>The terms “combination, designed, facilitate and voluntary action” have significant implications in this definition.</p>
<p><strong>Combination</strong>: emphasizes the importance of matching the multiple determinants of behavior with multiple learning experiences or educational interventions.</p>
<p><strong>Designed</strong>: distinguishes health education from incidental learning experiences as systematically planned activity. <strong>Facilitate</strong> means create favorable conditions for action.</p>
<p><strong>Voluntary action</strong> means behavioral measures are undertaken by an individual, group or community to achieve an intended health effect with out the use of force, i.e., with full understanding and acceptance of purposes.</p>

<p>Introduction</p>
<p>In the previous section, we have tried to discuss the concept of health and health education. Human behaviour is among the major determinants of the health of individuals, families or communities. Healthy behaviours contribute to the overall health of individuals and communities and unhealthy behaviours adversely affect the quality of life people at different levels. Most health issues cannot be dealt with by treatment alone. The promotion of health and prevention of diseases will usually involve some changes in life styles or human behaviour.</p>

<p>Behaviour is an action that has a specific frequency, duration and purpose whether conscious or unconscious. It is what we "do" and how we "act". People stay healthy or become ill, often as a result of their own action or behaviour. The following are examples of how people's actions can affect their health:</p>
<ul>
<li>Using mosquito nets and insect sprays helps to keep mosquito away.</li>
<li>Feeding children with bottle put them at risk of diarrhoea.</li>
<li>Defecating in an open field will lead to parasitic infection.</li>
<li>Unsafe sex predisposes people to unwanted pregnancy, HIV/AIDS and other STDs</li>
</ul>
<p>In health education it is very important to be able to identify the practices that cause, cure, or prevent a problem.</p>
<p>The words actions, practices and behaviours are different words of the same thing.</p>
<p>Life style: refers to the collection of behaviours that make up a person's way of life-including diet, clothing, family life, housing and work.</p>
<p>Customs: It represents the group behaviour. It is the pattern of action shared by some or all members of the society.</p>
<p>Traditions: are behaviours that have been carried out for a long time and handed down from parents to children.</p>

<p>Secondary prevention includes preventive measures that lead to an early diagnosis and prompt treatment of a problem before it becomes serious. The actions people take before consulting a health worker, including recognition of symptoms, taking home remedies ('self-medication'), consulting family and healers are called illness behaviors. Illness behaviors are important examples of behaviors for secondary prevention.</p>

<p>In the past, health education follows persuasion approach to force behavior change among individuals. However, contemporary health education program underscores an informed decision-making approach emphasizing community participation and empowerment. When the idea of primary health care was launched, community participation was one of the important principles identified for its implementation. Although everyone talks about it, real community participation is rarely practiced.</p>
<p>26</p>
<p>What is community?</p>
<p>Community could be defined as organized groups of people who share a sense of belonging, beliefs, norms, and leadership and who usually interact within a defined geographical area.</p>
<p>E.g. People living in a "Kebele" or Woreda"</p>
<p>People organized under one religion etc.</p>
<p>The concept of community participation</p>
<p>The health of the community will improve only if the people themselves become involved in planning, implementing, and having a say about their own health and health care. Nevertheless, involvement will not just happen.</p>
<p>Many people emphasize the importance of community participation for any development issues, including health promotion, to become a success. However, the question is how serious are we about involving individuals, families, and communities? Are we prepared - mentally and professionally – to listen to their concerns, to learn from them what they feel is important, to share with them appropriate information, to encourage and support them? Are we ready to assist them in choosing from alternative solutions, in setting their own targets and evaluating their own efforts? In many cases, so far, the answer is "NO".</p>

<p>In health education, we are concerned about how people actually feel, not how we think they should feel. We are interested in how people look at their own problems, not only in the problems we see ourselves. We want people to develop the confidence and skills to help themselves.</p>
<p>The traditional approach in planning health care or health education program involves the decision to be made by experts. This approach is sometimes called the 'top-down' approach and contrasted with the 'bottom-up' approach where members of the community make the decisions. In this model people are just told what to do. We make decisions and expect them to follow.</p>
<p>The concept of community participation or involvement encompasses the process by which individuals and families assume responsibility for the community and develop the capacity to contribute to their health and the community's development. It is a means by which the emphasis is on strengthening the capacity of communities to determine their own needs and take appropriate action. Communities should not be passive recipients of services.</p>
<p>The following are examples of actions that does not indicate genuine participation</p>
<ul>
<li>Involvement of individuals from the community in responding to health assessment survey questionnaires,</li>

<p>The role of experts should be limited to helping them identify their problems and to point out methods for dealing with the problems.</p>

<p>What is communication?</p>
<p>Communication is the process of sharing of ideas, information, knowledge, and experience among people to take action. Communication may take place between one person and another, between an individual and a group or between two groups. Communication facilitates creation of awareness, acceptance and action at individual, group and inter-group level. The process always involves a sender and a receiver regardless of the number of people concerned.</p>
<p>Why communication?</p>
<ul>
<li>To have dialogue with communities.</li>
<li>Influence decision makers to adopt health promoting policies and laws.</li>
<li>Raise awareness among decision makers on issues regarding poverty, human rights, equity, environmental issues, etc...</li>
<li>Ensure that the public gives support to government health promoting activities.</li>
<li>Communicate new laws and policies to the public</li>
<li>Raise public awareness in order to mobilize community participation.</li>
<li>Develop community action on health issues.</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="1-one-way-communication">1. One-way communication<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#1-one-way-communication" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This is a linear type of communication in which information flows from the source to the receiver. There is no input (feed back) from the receiver. It is commonly used in advertising; the message is designed to persuade the receiver to take action prescribed by the sender. The model is best used by organizations when the message is simple and needs to be communicated quickly, for example, the date and time of a public meeting. There is no opportunity to clear up misunderstanding and meaning is controlled by the receiver.</p>

The addition of feedback allows the sender to find out how the message is being received and so it can be monitored and adapted to better suit the receiver's needs.</p>

<p>Stage 1: Reaching the intended audience</p>
<p>Communication cannot be effective unless it is seen or heard by its intended audience. A common cause of failure is preaching to the converted.</p>
<p>E.g. Posters placed at the health post or talks given at the antenatal clinics. These only reach the people who attend the services and are already motivated. But the groups you are trying to reach may not attend clinics, nor have radios or newspapers. Communications should be directed where people are going to see them or hear them. This requires studying your intended audience to find out where they might see posters, what their listening and reading habits are.</p>
<p>Stage 2: Attracting the audience’s attention</p>
<p>Any communication must attract attention so that people will make the effort to listen/read it. At any one time we receive a wide range of information from each of our five senses – touch, smell, vision, hearing and taste. It is impossible to concentrate on all these at the same time. Attention is the process by which a person selects part of this complex mixture to focus on (i.e. to pay attention to) while ignoring others for the time being.</p>

<p>Role-playing consists of the acting out of real-life situations and problems. The player tries to behave in a way that the character might behave when faced with a given situation or problem. It is used to show different people feel about a problem and what they should do about it.</p>
<p>Role-playing can be used to start off a discussion, to see what possible consequences of a certain action are, and to develop a better understanding of why people feel as they do. We learn about our own behavior during a role-play, we can discover how our attitudes and values encourage cooperation and problem solving or, how our attitude and values create problems.</p>

<p>If measles is a serious problem in a community, a primary health care program to solve the problem might have the following as its health objective.</p>
<ul>
<li>Fewer children will get measles</li>
<li>Those who do get measles will recover quickly and suffer no disabilities</li>
<li>No children will die from measles.</li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li>Mothers will bring their children for immunization</li>
<li>Mothers whose children get measles will bring them quickly to the health worker for care.</li>

<p>It is not enough to decide what will be done, by whom and when, we also need to decide how it will be done.</p>
<p>Once a health worker understands the reason behind behavior that is causing a health problem, he or she can use many different methods to encourage a change in that behavior. Generally, there are some basic issues to consider before choosing health education methods:</p>
<ul>
<li>How fast do people change?</li>
<li>How many people are involved?</li>
<li>Is the method appropriate to the local culture?</li>
<li>What resources are available?</li>
<li>What combined methods are needed?</li>
<li>What methods fit the characteristics (age, sex, religion etc) of the target group?</li>
</ul>

<ul>

<p>Providing good ideas quickly to a large number of people is a very helpful step in health education. It creates awareness of a problem or idea. But it may not be enough to change health behavior. Posters, lectures, display, plays, newspapers, radio, films and village criers are appropriate for large groups.</p>
<p>Activities such as practicing new skills, discussing personal feelings, values and money matters, and sharing difficult experiences are best done person-to-person, or in small groups using story-telling, demonstrations, role-playing, case studies, discussions and educational games.</p>

<p>Therefore, try to look for methods that are inexpensive, culturally acceptable, understandable, and at the same time effective.</p>

<p>Introduction</p>
<p>From a profession point of view, ethical behavior is expected from professionals. Ethical conduct is particularly important for health educators, since they are working with a mission to serve the individual. This chapter will try to address:</p>
<p>Definition</p>
<p>114</p>
<p>Ethics is the philosophical study of the moral value of human conduct and the rules that govern it. It is the right thing to do for society and self. Moral refers to those beliefs about how people ought to behave.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="basic-ethical-principles">Basic ethical principles<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#basic-ethical-principles" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="1-the-principle-of-autonomy">1. The principle of autonomy<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#1-the-principle-of-autonomy" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>This principle means that people, being individuals with individual differences must have a freedom to choose their own ways and means of being moral with the framework of the other four principles.</p>
<p>Respect for autonomy involves respecting another person's rights and dignity such that a person reaches a maximum level of fulfillment as a human being. In the context of health promotion and health care this means that the relationship between health extension worker and community member is based on a respect for him or her as a person and with individual rights.</p>
<p>Rights in relation to health care are usually taken to include:</p>
<ul>
<li>The right to information</li>
<li>The right to privacy and confidentiality</li>
<li>The right to appropriate care and treatment</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="2-beneficence-doing-good">2. Beneficence (doing good)<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#2-beneficence-doing-good" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>

<p>Beneficence means doing or promoting good as well as preventing, removing and avoiding evil or harm.</p>
<p>E.g. provide information about emergency first aid to reduce the risks of HIV infection or accident.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="3-non-maleficence-doing-no-harm">3. Non-maleficence (doing no harm)<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#3-non-maleficence-doing-no-harm" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Non-maleficence holds a central position in the tradition of medical ethics and guards against avoidable harm to subjects. In short, it refers to non-inflation of harm to others. E.g. use of sterile needles.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="4-justice-fairness">4. Justice (fairness)<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#4-justice-fairness" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This principle states that human being should treat other human being fairly and justly in distributing goodness and badness among them. In other words justice should include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Fair distribution of scarce resources</li>
<li>Respect for individual and group rights</li>
<li>Following morally acceptable laws</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="5-the-principle-of-truth-telling-honesty">5. The principle of truth telling (honesty)<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#5-the-principle-of-truth-telling-honesty" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<p>At the heart of any moral relationship, there is communication. A necessary component of any meaningful communication is telling the truth, being honest.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="ethics-for-the-performance-of-health-extension-workers-as-health-educators">Ethics for the performance of health extension workers as health educators<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#ethics-for-the-performance-of-health-extension-workers-as-health-educators" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Health extension workers as health educators assume profound responsibility in using educational processes to promote health and influence human well-being. They are also responsible for the implementation of health extension package program. Ethical precepts that guide these processes must reflect the right of individuals and communities to make decisions affecting their lives.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="health-extension-workers-responsibilities-as-a-health-educator">Health extension workers responsibilities as a health educator<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#health-extension-workers-responsibilities-as-a-health-educator" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Affirm an equal right, believing that health is a basic human right for all.</li>
<li>Provide people with relevant and accurate information and resources to make their choices freely and intelligently.</li>
<li>Support change by freedom of choice and self-determination, as long as these decisions pose no threat to the health of others.</li>
<li>Be advocates for healthful change and legislation, and speak out on issues deleterious to public health.</li>
</ul>
