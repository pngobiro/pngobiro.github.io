<p>MODULE 6: Community Health Promotion</p>
<p>Code: CHP 108
Hours: 80
Credit: 08</p>
<p>Introduction to community health promotion</p>
<p>Module Competence
This module is designed to enable the learner promote health, prevent and control diseases in the community.</p>
<p>Module objectives
By the end of this module, the learners should be able to;</p>
<ol>
<li>Explain the concepts used in community health promotion</li>
<li>Describe the principles of advocacy, communication and social mobilization in community health.</li>
<li>Plan, implement and manage health education and facilitate session in the community.</li>
<li>Explain sustainability mechanisms in community health programs</li>
</ol>
<p>Module Outcomes
By the end of this module, the learner should:</p>
<ol>
<li>Explain the concepts in health promotion</li>
<li>Apply principles of advocacy, communication and social mobilization in community health.</li>
<li>Plan, implement and manage health education and facilitate session in the community.</li>
<li>Understand sustainability mechanisms in community health programs</li>
</ol>

<p>Definition of terms,</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="1-1-1-health">1.1.1: Health<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#1-1-1-health" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>A state of complete physical, mental and social well-being and not merely the absence of disease or infirmity (WHO).</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="1-1-2-community">1.1.2: Community<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#1-1-2-community" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>A group of people determined by geographical boundaries and sharing common values and interests.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h4 level="4" id="characteristics-of-a-community">Characteristics of a community<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#characteristics-of-a-community" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h4></div>
<ul>
<li>Have common interests and goals</li>
<li>Live in one geographical area</li>
<li>Share same resources such as water, health services</li>
<li>Have common beliefs, values, practices, norms and traditions</li>
<li>They interact and interrelate with one another</li>
<li>They have a common leadership structure (social institutions)</li>
</ul>

<p>Refers to the health status of the community members, the problems affecting their health, and the totality of health care provided to the community (WHO 1971).</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="1-1-4-health-promotion">1.1.4: Health promotion<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#1-1-4-health-promotion" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>Is the process of enabling people to increase control over, and to improve their health in order to attain a state of complete physical, mental and social well-being (WHO in 1986).</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="1-1-5-advocacy">1.1.5: Advocacy<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#1-1-5-advocacy" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>A set of targeted actions directed at decision or opinion makers in support of specific policy to create positive change for people and their environment.</p>

<p>Community health strategy is a community-based mechanism through which households and communities take an active role in health and health-related development issues.</p>

<p>This is an action developed by the ministry of health and its partners after realizing the importance of empowering households and communities to be responsible of their own health. This is achieved through delivery of Kenya essential package for health (KEPH) at level 1.</p>
<p>Community strategy was developed and launched on 26th June, 2006 by the ministry of health and its partners.</p>
<p>1.2.2: Household – It consists of one or more people who live in the same dwelling and also share meals or living accommodation, and may consist of a single family or some other grouping of people. A single dwelling will be considered to contain multiple households if either meals or living space are not shared.</p>
<p>1.2.3: Objectives of community strategy</p>
<p>i. To improve the health status of Kenyan communities through initiation and implementation of life-cycle focused health actions.
ii. Provide level 1 services for all cohorts and socio-economic groups taking in to account their priority needs.
iii. Build the capacity of community health extension workers (CHEWs) and community own resource persons (CORPS) to provide services at level 1.
iv. Strengthen health facility- community linkages through effective decentralization and partnership.
v. Strengthen the community to progressively realize their rights for accessible and quality care and to seek accountability from facility based health services</p>
<p>1.2.4: Services offered at level 1</p>
<p>i. Disease prevention and control to reduce mortality, morbidity and disability.
ii. Family health services to expand family planning, maternal, child health and youth services.
iii. Hygiene and environmental sanitation
iv. Health promotion through educating the communities
v. Referral to the link health facilities for further management.</p>
<p>1.2.5: Community health actors at level 1</p>
<p>i. Households
ii. Community members
iii. Community owned resource persons, community health volunteers, opinion leaders, women groups, youth groups and religious leaders.
iv. Community health assistants
v. Link health facility in-charge
vi. Sub county health management team</p>

<p>Community health unit is the lowest health administrative unit according to Kenya health governance structure. Community health unit is governed by community health committee which is a representative of all villages, considering one third gender rule, youth representatives, women groups, NGOs, people with disabilities, religious leaders and chaired by a respectable member of the community. Community health assistant is the secretary of the committee by law. One CHA oversees approximately 10 CHVs who are in charge of approximately 5,000 people (500-1000 households). One CHV serves approximately 20 households. The community based health unit structure involves one link facility, one CHC, one CHA/CHO.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="1-3-1-principles-of-health-promotion">1.3.1: Principles of health promotion<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#1-3-1-principles-of-health-promotion" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ol>
<li>Empowerment- Health promotion initiatives should enable individuals and communities to assume more power over the personal, socio-economic and environmental factors that affect their health.</li>
<li>Participation- Health promotion initiatives should involve those concerned in all stages of planning, implementation and evaluation.</li>
<li>Holistic- Health promotion initiatives should foster physical, mental, social and spiritual health.</li>
<li>Inter-sectoral- Health promotion initiatives should involve the collaboration of agencies from relevant sectors.</li>
<li>Equity and social justice- Health promotion initiatives should be guided by a concern for equity and social justice.</li>
<li>Sustainable- Health promotion initiatives should bring about changes that individuals and communities can maintain once initial funding has ended.</li>
<li>Multi-strategy- Health promotion initiatives should use a variety of approaches in combination with one another, including policy development, organizational change, community development, legislation, advocacy, education and communication.</li>
<li>Public accountability- This principle emphasizes that the public should be responsible, transparent and ready to be observed, monitored and evaluated on its health performance (social auditing).</li>
</ol>

<p>1.4.3: Tiers of health service delivery</p>
<ol>
<li>
<p>Tier 1: Community
The foundation of the service delivery system, with both demand creation (health promotion services), and specified supply services that are most effectively delivered at the community. In the essential package, all non-facility based health and related services are classified as community services. On average, for every 5,000 population a community unit needs to be established.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Tier 2: Primary care service units
The first physical level of the health system. Primary care service units are either health centres, or dispensaries (mobile clinics in areas where population density is very low, and/or mobile). The health sector aspires to upgrade all dispensaries into fully primary care units (model health centres) in the long run, ensuring every facility is able to at least carry out a normal delivery.</p>
</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>A level II facility should exist for every 10,000 persons on average, translating to an average of 30 dispensary OPD visits per day for any services, if everyone in the catchment area is to visit a health facility at least once a year for any form of services (curative, preventive, or health promotion activities).</li>
<li>A level III facility should exist for every 30,000 persons, allowing for at least 4 deliveries per day – a workload that is fair on the system and staff.</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="2-1-9-social-behavior-change-communication-sbcc">2.1.9: Social behavior change communication (SBCC)<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#2-1-9-social-behavior-change-communication-sbcc" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>SBCC – is a communication strategy which encourages individuals, household and communities to change their behaviours. This is a communication strategy that is aimed at triggering individuals, groups and communities to adopt positive health practices that promote health.</p>
<p>This is an interactive process (communication) with individuals, groups or communities to develop communication strategies that promote positive behaviors which are appropriate to their settings hence solving their most pressing health problems. This in turn provides a supportive environment which will enable people to initiate, sustain and maintain positive and desirable behavior outcomes.</p>
<p>SBCC, BCC and C4D are interchangeably used to mean the same thing - an interactive process with individuals, groups and communities to promote and sustain societal behaviour change hence maintain appropriate behaviours.</p>

<p>A good message should be able to call for a direct course of action to the reader or end user in order to maximize on its ability to cause behaviour change. The terminologies used should be easily understood by the beneficiary and the recommended action should be clearly described so that any user can understand the message irrespective of their education level. <p>2.3: Community dialogue and health action; (6 Hours)</p>
<p>2.3.1: Community dialogue</p>
<p>A community dialogue is an interactive participatory communication process of sharing information between people or groups of people aimed at reaching a common understanding and workable solution.</p>
<p>A community dialogue is a forum for community members to discuss community problems face-to-face, identify their root causes and consequences, and develop local solutions. It is a process of joint problem identification and analysis leading to modification and redirection of community and stakeholders' actions towards preferred future for all.</p>
<p>2.3.2: Objectives of conducting a community dialogue</p>
<ul>
<li>Generating deeper understanding of the nature of the problem among individuals and communities in order to influence change.</li>
<li>Surfacing common issues and the resources to address them, (helps identify barriers to positive change and uncover innovative ideas).</li>
<li>Providing a forum for the unheard to be heard.</li>
<li>Promoting social contacts among various groups in the community.</li>
<li>Promoting ownership and accountability.</li>
</ul>

<p>2.3.4: Types of community dialogue</p>
<ol>
<li>Debate: It is a competitive two way conversation (a format discussion on a particular matter in a public meeting in which opposing arguments are put forward).</li>
<li>Dialogue: A cooperative two way conversation (a conversation between two or more persons discussing to resolve a problem).</li>
<li>Discourse: Is a cooperative one way conversation (formal and orderly verbal interchange of ideas especially in conversation).</li>
<li>Diatribe: Is a competitive one way conversation (a forceful and bitter verbal attack or verbal onslaught against someone).</li>
</ol>
<p>2.3.4.1: The difference between a debate and a dialogue</p>
<p>In many countries, debating and aggressive disagreements are common.</p>
<ul>
<li>Community Dialogues take a participatory approach which are distinctly different from debates and lead to compromises and a common understanding. At no point should a participants argue or be disrespectful to one another.</li>
<li>With debates, such as the political debates often seen on TV, opponents deny opposing views.</li>
<li>However, with Community Dialogues the expression of all views are allowed. Similarly, with debates participants listen to refute others' views, however with Community Dialogues participants listen to gain a deeper understanding and develop a sense of empathy.</li>
<li>Debates and traditional teaching styles often asked closed questions such as ‘Don’t you think Afghanistan has a big problem of road infrastructure?’ or ‘Is GBV bad?’. This type of questioning gives a yes/no answer and does not allow learning. With Community Dialogues open questions are asked to allow the participant to volunteer more information on their position. For example, ‘Why do you think GBV happens in your community’ and often these questions are followed up with additional questions to respectfully tease out more information from the participant. It is vital to allow people to express their views, even if those views are in contrast to the aim of the session. Once participants have expressed their opinions, then further questioning and collective problem solving can happen.</li>
</ul>

<p>a. At this point the team will identify current problems/ issues. What the community is doing about these issues, whether the actions are giving the required outcomes and what are the constraints / challenges faced by the community.</p>

<p>a. Problem analysis involves a thorough analysis of the issue / situation at hand. Questions that can be asked under this section include:
b. What are the causes of the problem / issue at hand?
c. Is the issue / problem a shared problem in this community or it is perceived as a problem for only a few?
d. How is the community responding to the problem?
e. Has the community previously dialogued on the issue?

c. After the evaluation process the necessary feedback should be provided.
d. This promotes ownership of the process and the will to do better next time.</p>

<p>Participatory learning may be different from what most people are used to. It is not usually used in schools and other formal/informal learning settings.</p>
<p>Unlike the routine teacher-student relationship where the student is passive for the majority of time, participatory learning approaches involves a facilitator who guides and encourages participants to take an active role in their own learning process.</p>
<p>What this means in practice is that the facilitator speaks much less than a teacher would in conventional learning session. Instead the wealth of experience that the participants have is utilized and applied for collective problem solving.</p>
<p>Participatory learning has proven very useful working with participants who may not be used to being students. Furthermore, the participatory learning method is regarded as an effective approach to promote social change through mobilizing greater support of various community structures.</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Daily Routine Schedule</strong>: This method records workload by gender and age. Women and men are recorded separately, according to current activities or by seasons, and compared. The community brainstorms on activities by gender and age, and the ideas are given to groups to develop daily schedules.</li>
</ol>
<p>Groups present and discuss their schedules.</p>
<p>The facilitator summarizes for the record.</p>
<p>The community reflects on what they have discovered that may need action based on available resources as support for the participatory planning session.</p>
<ol start="2">
<li><strong>Seasonal Calendar</strong>: This method plots happenings, activities, diseases, food availability, etc. It also reflects gender and age.</li>
</ol>
<p>Brainstorm first by months or seasons, and then calendar the events in groups.</p>
<p>The groups present and discuss the calendars and generate a common calendar for the record. The group may discuss what they have discovered needs action and what action is needed.</p>
<ol start="3">
<li>
<p><strong>Time Trends</strong>: These are graphs to show how things have changed over time (crop yields, area under cultivation, livestock population, prices, births and deaths, rainfall, etc.).</p>

<li>
<p><strong>Direct Observation</strong>: Look, Listen and Learn (the 3 L’s)</p>
</li>
</ol>
<p>This means systematically observing objects, events, relationships or people’s behaviour, listening to what people talk about in an emotional way (excitement, anger, fear and concern), and learning and recording these observations in an organized manner.</p>
<p>This is a good way to check people’s responses (triangulation).</p>
<p>A checklist is necessary to ensure completeness of observation, based on the indicators that can be assessed through this method. Information-rich sites may include: marketplaces, shops, bars, worship sites, water points, festivals, buses, etc. The quality of observation can be improved by participating with the community in their activities. This then becomes participant observation, which requires more time than normally available for a rapid assessment exercise.</p>

<ol start="8">

The atmosphere should be informal, to promote equality and relative trust. The method of recording the conversation should be agreed upon with the group members.</p>
