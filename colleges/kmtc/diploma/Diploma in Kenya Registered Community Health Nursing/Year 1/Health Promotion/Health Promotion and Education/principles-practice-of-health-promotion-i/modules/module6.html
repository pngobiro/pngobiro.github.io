üìö 6.3 Advocacy

Advocacy is a combination of social actions designed to gain political commitment, policy support, social acceptance and systems support for a particular goal or program. It involves collecting and structuring information into a persuasive case; communicating the case to decision-makers and other potential supporters, including the public and consumers, through various interpersonal and media channels; and stimulating actions by social institutions, stakeholders and policy-makers in support of the goal or programme. Health promotion embraces advocacy in presenting health issues/problems as priority political and development agenda. The goal of advocacy is to make the issue a political or national priority that cannot be swept aside with a change in government.

Roles of advocacy in health promotion

  • Influence changes in policies, programmes, and environments.
  • Mobilizing resources and opinions to support the changes.
  • Increase the power of people and groups and to make institutions more responsive to human needs.
  • Widen the range of choices that people can have by increasing their power to define problems and solutions and participate in the broader social and policy issues.
  • Influencing decision makers to adopt policies that improve public health.
  • Facilitate the capacities of communities and vulnerable populations to make their needs and interests known.
  • Increase peoples participation in the processes of allocating societal resources and values among its members.
  • Creates a supportive environment among people.

üìö 6.4 Networking

Networking is working collaboratively across disciplines, sectors, and partners to enhance the impact and sustainability of health promotion programs and policies. Networking is useful for coordination, avoiding duplication and sharing information of mutual interest. It is essential in planning, implementing and evaluation of health promotion activities. Thus, knowing what the communication patterns are in a community is a valuable piece of information in building a good networking strategy. Building allies with people who are popular and respected in a community is a wise networking strategy. Networking entails bench making, social interaction, collaboration and coordination of activities among different agencies & sectors.

üìö 6.5 Communication

Communication is the process of exchanging information in the form of messages, symbols, thoughts, signs, and opinions.

Communication can be described as follows; Communication is a two-way process in which there is an exchange and progression of thoughts, feelings or ideas towards a mutually accepted goal or direction by speech, writing or signs.

Communication processes requires both intra and inter personal skills in processing, listening, observing, speaking, questioning, analyzing, and evaluating ideas. As shown in the diagram below

Types of and Skills for Communication

  • Verbal communication includes sounds, words, language, and speech. Speaking is an effective way of communicating and helps in expressing our emotions in words.
  • Nonverbal communication manages to convey the sender's message without having to use words. This form of communication supersedes all other forms because of its usage and effectiveness. Nonverbal communication involves the use of physical ways of communication, such as tone of the voice, touch, and expressions.
  • Written communication is the medium through which the message of the sender is conveyed with the help of written words. Letters, personal journals, e-mails, reports, articles, and memos are some forms of written communication. Unlike other forms of communication, written messages can be edited and rectified before it is communicated to the receiver. Thereby, making written communication an indispensable part of informal and formal communication.

üìö 6.6 Social Marketing

Social Marketing Is the application of marketing concepts and techniques to the marketing of various socially beneficial ideas and causes instead or products and services in the commercial Sense.

Product

  • does not necessarily mean a physical product
  • socially desirable goals e.g. behavioural, attitudinal, idea change to new habits, norms and values through learning

Price

  • represents the price the "buyer" must accept in order to obtain the "product"
  • includes costs in terms of money, opportunity, energy and pychological e.g.
    • buying seat belts and installing it; giving up the pleasures of smoking
    • giving up favourite food; going to GP vs long waiting time at government hospital

Place

  • important for providing adequate and compatible distribution and response channels
  • arranging for accessible outlets which permit translation of motivation to act
  • requires effective & efficient marketing strategy e.g. prime time announcements, strategic places for display, direct telephone linkages, information centres etc

Promotion

  • key element in all marketing as consumer demand responds to promotion and product advertising

üìö 6.7 Health Education

Health Education is any combination of learning experiences designed to help individuals and communities improve their health, by increasing their knowledge or influencing their attitudes. WHO defines health education as ‚Äúcomprising of consciously constructed opportunities for learning involving some form of communication designed to improve health literacy, including improving knowledge and developing life skills which are conducive to individual and community health.‚Äù Basic literacy is an essential building block for development and health promotion. Health literacy interventions need to be designed based on health, social and cultural needs. Purpose of health education is to positively influence health behavior of individuals and communities as well as their living and working condition.

Visual Products (IEC Materials) Health promotion activities rely on a variety of well-designed and effective visual products to help ensure success. Every brochure, poster, videotape or other piece of IEC material is the product of a decision, supported by research, to deal with a specific health concern, and to be well received and persuasive among a specific audience.

The success and impact of visual products depends largely on the understanding of the target audience. Working with target audience members throughout the development of visual products, and in developing usage strategies for those materials, helps ensure that visual products meet the needs of the intended target audience.

This handbook offers a set of basic steps for developing visual products to follow in the planning, design (or adaptation) and production. It outlines a clear, six-step approach, with each step supporting the next, which design teams should endeavor to follow.

The six steps include:

  1. STEP 1: Select appropriate visual products
  2. STEP 2: Develop a creative brief
  3. STEP 3: Prepare drafts/ prototype visual products or adapting existing materials
  4. STEP 4: Pre-test drafts/ prototypes or adopted materials
  5. STEP 5: Assess pre-test result and reviewing visual products
  6. STEP 6: Monitor the use and impact of visual product
