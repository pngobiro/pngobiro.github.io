‚ö†Ô∏è Adverse Events Following Immunization (AEFI)

Definition: This is a reaction that occurs in a client/pt following vaccination that is considered to be related to the vaccine until proved otherwise.

  • AEFIs may be classified as mild, moderate or severe.
  • AEFI occurs even in the most careful circumstances but only rarely is there a direct causal relationship between the vaccine and the adverse event.
  • AEFIs may be localized to the injection site or generalized to the whole body.
  • AEFIs may manifest immediately after vaccination or any time within 48 hours after vaccination.

Possible Causes of AEFI

1. Programmatic errors

Usually they H/service provider based i.e. an error in handling, reconstitution or administration of the vaccine.

2. Nature of the vaccine

Vaccine properties or individual response to the vaccine itself.

3. Coincidental

An event that has no causal association between the immunization and the medical condition of the child or woman.

4. Unknown cause

The cause of the event cannot be determined.

Common AEFIs

  • Anaphylactic shock: sudden antibody-antigen reaction. Itchy, urticarial rash (in over 90% of cases). Progressive, painless swelling (angioedema) - face and the mouth, preceded by itchiness, tearing, nasal congestion or facial flushing.
  • Respiratory symptoms: sneezing, coughing, wheezing, and laboured breathing; upper way swelling (indicated by hoarseness and/or difficulty swallowing) possibly causing airway obstruction.
  • Hypotension, which generally develops later in the illness and can progress to cause shock and collapse.
  • Injection site abscesses: hard or soft swelling containing pus at inj site eg redness, painful.
  • BCG lymphadenitis: swollen cervical or armpit lymph nodes. c/picture of TB.
  • Deaths that are related to immunization.
  • Cases requiring admission/hospitalization that is related to immunization.
  • Other unusual or severe medical incidents that are thought by health workers or the public to be related to vaccination - convulsions.

Non-significant Reactions

Non-significant reaction to vaccines includes:

  • Fever
  • Redness or swelling at the injection site
  • Rash

ü©∫ Management of AEFIs

Immediate Steps

  1. Take the affected child to the resuscitation room or a safe place away from the crowd & give the correct treatment as per the condition.
  2. Administer epinephrine 0.01 ml/kg (maximum 0.5 ml) of aqueous epinephrine 1:1000 by SC or IM injection in the limb (opposite limb to where the vaccination was given); speedy intervention is of paramount importance. Repeat at 20-minutes interval if necessary.
  3. Monitor vital signs and reassess the situation frequently, to guide medication use.
  4. Ask or shout for help from colleagues.
  5. Re-assure the parent as the child gets treatment.
  6. Collect the vaccine vial and the diluent which were used and document the details (date of manufacture, expiry date, batch number and manufacturer).
  7. Take a sample specimen as per the condition and take for laboratory analysis.
  8. Observe until the child‚Äôs condition improves. if the condition does not improve within 2 hours refer to the next level.
  9. Report the AEFI within 24 hours and investigate within 48 hours.

  1. Communicate to parents, community and public at large about AEFI‚Äôs and reassure them about immunization safety.
  2. Train all concerned persons as a corrective measure for the operational problem such as knowledge and skills gap.
  3. Conduct regular supportive supervision and give feedback.
  4. Improve availability of supplies and the working condition of the equipments to minimize program errors e.g. high temperatures could lead to growth of bacterial in opened unused multi-dose vaccines leading to adverse events. Freezing of freeze sensitive vaccines could lead to aseptic abscesses.

üõ°Ô∏è Reducing Incidences of AEFI

1. Contraindications - TRUE

  • Client history for allergies and previous adverse reactions before administering vaccines.
  • In the case of a possible serious allergy, consult supervisor appropriate before giving vaccine.
  • Minimizes the occurrence of anaphylaxis but will not remove the risk altogether.
üö´

Precaution on Allergic Reactions

Do not give the second or third DPT Hep+ Hib injection to a child who has suffered such a severe anaphylactic reaction to the previous dose.

False Contraindications

Do NOT withhold vaccination for:

  • Low-grade fever
  • Mild respiratory infections
  • Diarrhoea (not a contraindication to OPV)
  • Other minor illnesses eg mild children suffering from malnutrition.

2. Programme error

Improper handling of vaccines or faulty immunization technique requires action:

  • Logistics: Providing additional equipment (needles, syringes, sterilizers, vaccine carriers, cold packs), vaccine or diluent.
  • Training: Solve operational problems: lack of skills and knowledge & poor attitude.
  • Supervision: Non-serious AEFIs reported should alert the health worker to seek the cause.
  • Communication: Inform parents and the community about AEFIs, and assure them of immunization safety.

üîç Disease Surveillance

What is disease surveillance? The collection, analysis, & interpretation of data to determine disease trends and patterns. Enables managers to respond to existing problems & take steps to prevent anticipated problems.

Types of Disease Surveillance

Facility-Based Routine Surveillance

Health workers are required to report on the number of individuals that come to their facility and are diagnosed with notifiable diseases (passive surveillance) eg MOH 719.

Community-Based Surveillance

Properly trained community members can expand facility-based surveillance by detecting and reporting cases that may go undetected by the H/facility.

Sentinel Surveillance

(Active surveillance) - is the collection and analysis of data by designated institutions selected for their geographic location, medical specialty, and ability to accurately diagnose and report high quality data.

Surveillance Activities

  • Detection: Surveillance begins with case detection. To accurately detect disease, health workers need case definitions.
  • Investigation: Each facility should have a disease surveillance focal person.
  • Reporting: Ministry of health through HMIS require that facilities routinely report the total count of cases. For EPI target diseases reporting is case based i.e each case should be reported individually using the IDSR form.
  • Analysis and interpretation
  • Presentation
  • Response
