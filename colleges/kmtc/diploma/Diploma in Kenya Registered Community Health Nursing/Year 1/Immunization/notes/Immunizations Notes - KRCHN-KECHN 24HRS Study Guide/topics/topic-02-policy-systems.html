üá∞üá™ Vaccination Services in Kenya

Vaccination services in Kenya have evolved significantly over the years:

  • Vaccination against meningitis which is required by travelers proceeding for Umrah and Haj in Medina and Mecca in Saudi Arabia is obtained from private sector practitioners and through the Port Health Services.
  • The MOH established The Kenya Expanded Programme on Immunization (KEPI) in 1980 aimed at providing immunization against six killer diseases of childhood, namely tuberculosis, polio, diphtheria, whooping cough, tetanus and measles to all children in the country before their 1st birthday, and tetanus toxoid vaccination to all pregnant women.
  • In the 1990s, having achieved The Universal Child Immunization goals of immunizing at least 80% of the target population, KEPI‚Äôs focus changed to disease control, elimination and eradication.

Institutional Evolution

The Division of Communicable Disease Control has since July 2007 been restructured and renamed the Unit of Vaccines & Immunization Services (UVIS) - whereby introduced new antigens-pcv10, rotavirus&IPV.

Objectives of UVIS

The UVIS exists to achieve the following objectives:

  • To ensure equitable access to appropriate vaccination services for all persons in Kenya
  • To ensure universal immunization of children in Kenya with appropriate doses of MoH prescribed childhood vaccines.
  • To ensure universal immunization of special risk groups with MoH approved priority vaccines
  • To ensure optimum vaccination service delivery in response to specific situations of outbreak of life threatening vaccine or immunization preventable diseases; TB, polio, diphtheria, pertussis, Tetanus, measles, diarrhea, pneumonia, meningitis, yellow fever.

Priority Activities

Operational

  • Polio eradication through active surveillance.
  • Accelerated disease control
  • Improving performance of routine Immunization
  • Supplemental Immunization-campaigns

Strategic

  • Improving financial flows from donor to service delivery points.
  • Creating demand of immunization services through evidence-driven advocacy
  • Improving the capacity of health workers.

‚öôÔ∏è Immunization System Components

1. Immunization Service Delivery

  • UVIS programme endeavors to sustain and improve on the gains made over the years by providing Quality immunization services (potent antigens).
  • In Kenya, primarily most of immunizations take place in fixed posts ie H/facilities plus
  • Others; outreach clinic, mobile clinics and Supplemental Immunization Activities (campaigns) play a role in improving service delivery especially hard to reach popns.

2. Vaccine Supply, Quality and Logistics

  • UVIS ensures that adequate vaccines as well as injection materials are procured through WHO/UNICEF approved mechanisms (infant vaccines) as well as through the GoK. (KEPI/non-EPI vaccines). procurement system from manufacturer in Netherlands. Received at Airport to the national storage centre, distributed to respective regional centres the Subcounty H/offices order and collect. H/facilities order using S11.
  • Vaccines are received using Stock Control ledge books and stored in KEPI fridge.
  • Transported out and into fridge using vaccine carriers with sponge and frozen icepacks to maintain potency.
  • UVIS internal quality assurance mechanisms ascertain vaccine quality is maintained up to the point of utilization (SDP).
  • Logistics should be put in place before starting immunization session eg working equipment, accessories etc.

3. Disease Surveillance

  • The Disease Surveillance and Response Unit (DSRU) is responsible for disease surveillance and response activities working in liaison with county and Sub County Disease Surveillance Coordinators.
  • UVIS will liaise closely with DSRU for all vaccine preventable diseases.
  • Vaccine preventable disease surveillance data (Polio, measles, neonatal tetanus) is monitored so as to address gaps in immunization coverage in a timely manner as appropriate.

4. Advocacy, social Mobilization and Communication

  • Advocacy, social mobilization and communication are very crucial in EPI services.
  • The advocacy unit at UVIS aims to assist in effective implementation of the planned activities as well as increase demand for service by communities.
  • To this end, the unit develops and disseminates EPI communication plan for both routine immunization as well as SIAs.
  • In addition, the unit develops key EPI messages for both the health workers and the community, which are disseminated through various channels and strategies ie IEC materials in MCH/FP clinics.

5. Supportive components

a) Management

The management functions deals with people, resources and information. The roles of managers include:

  • Policy making
  • Setting standards
  • Proper utilization of resources located towards immunization activities.
  • Developing IEC materials distributed to the SDPs.

b) Sustainable financing

Vaccination services are expensive and often not adequately funded. UVIS therefore has to have capacity to do the following:

  • Budgeting
  • Identifying funding sources
  • Actions to increase resources allocation

c) Human & institutional resources

Like any medical field, immunization practices are constantly changing. The EPI programme must have the capacity to do the following:

  • Staffing
  • Training
  • Supervision
  • Institutional support
  • Technical information
  • Support to research projects
