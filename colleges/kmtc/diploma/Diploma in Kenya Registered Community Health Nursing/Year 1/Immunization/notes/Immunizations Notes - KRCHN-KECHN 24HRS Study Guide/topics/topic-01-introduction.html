üìñ Terminologies used in immunology

Basic Concepts

Immunity - ability to resist infections or protection against infectious disease

Immunology - science dealing with study of immune system.

Vaccinology - Field of science dealing with studies of vaccines.

Vaccine - antigen containing MOs in a weakened or killed state of proteins or toxins from organism.

Processes

Vaccination: The process of administering a vaccine (antigen) or manipulation of the immune system to induce protective immunity against specific disease. Aimed at reducing morbidity and mortality rates related to prevented infectious diseases.

Immunization - process by which a person immune system is made resistant to specific infections/diseases.

üß¨

Antigens & Antibodies

Antigen: antibody generator or any substance, usually a protein that is capable of eliciting an immune response by binding with antibodies and effector cells lymphocytes produced. The antigens that can cause a disease are called pathogens.

Antibody or immunoglobulin (IGs) - y globulins produced in response to antigen stimulation or a protein produced by plasma cells in response to an antigen eg IgA, M, D, G, E

Immunogenicity: The inherent ability of an antigen to induce an immune response.

Pathogen - disease causing organism

Opportunistic pathogen-cause diseases in immunosuppressed patients like HIV/AIDS

Virulence- Degree of pathogenicity within a group or species of microorganisms.

Pathogenicity ‚Äìability of microorganism to cause infection or disease.

Seroconversion: When an individual following a disease or vaccination generates antigen-specific antibodies.

Vaccine adjuvant: Substance that is added to a vaccine to its immunogenicity without having any specific antigenic effect in itself. These may include mineral salts (aluminium and calcium phosphate), organic adjuvants, particulate adjuvants, virosomes, microbial derivatives, oil emulsions and surfactant based formulations

Incubation period: the interval between exposure to an infectious agent and onset of clinical symptoms. The incubation period varies for different diseases from a few hours to several months or even longer for some diseases, e.g. leprosy.

How the body develops immunity

-two ways to acquire immunity against infections ‚Äì active immunity and passive immunity.

1. Active immunity

Is acquired when a person‚Äôs own immune system is stimulated to produce antigen specific antibodies and immune cells. This type of immunity often lasts for many years and it may be permanent.

Active immunity can be divided into

A) Natural active immunity-infections like measles disease.

produce antibodies, which when are in sufficient quantities, are able to identify the antigens and kill them or inactivate them. The patient recovers and the body's lymphocytes keep the memory of these organisms for life. This means that next time the same organism attacks the patient, the lymphocytes are ready to produce large amount of antibodies, which will overcome the organism. The patient will not get ill again hence he/she is said to have acquired natural immunity. For example, if a child has had measles and recovered, the child's lymphocytes produce antibodies any time the child encounters the measles virus through the body.

B) Artificial Active Immunity

This is the type of immunity given through vaccine administration.

A vaccine is made from an organism which is either killed or attenuated, that means it has lost its harmfulness, or its part or toxin rendered harmless (‚Äútoxoid‚Äù).

  • Also Artificial active immunity is acquired by-immunization by antigen eg LAV.

However, its antigenicity will still be identified by lymphocytes and will induce production of antibodies.

For example if a child gets the oral polio vaccine (attenuated live polio virus), the child's body will produce antibodies against poliovirus and hence will be protected against poliomyelitis without having been sick.

2. Artificial immunity

Short lasting immunity as the body doesn't participate directly in production of antibodies.

Natural Passive Immunity

Passively acquired antibodies are responsible for the protection of newborns and young infants against certain diseases. The transfer of antibodies from mother to foetus across the placenta during the last 2-3 months of pregnancy provides the newborn with a portion of the mother‚Äôs immunological experience.

Artificial Passive Immunity

‚ÄúBorrowed‚Äù antibodies can also protect one temporarily. These borrowed and prepared antibodies are from serum (antiserum) of person or animal that has been exposed to an antigen and has produced antibodies which are purified and are directly injected to the person at the site of infection to immediately counteract the offending antigen. Sources of passive artificial immunity include blood and blood products, immune or hyper-immune globulin, and animal antitoxins.

Herd Immunity

  • Immunity acquired when a higher proportion of immunized population in the community (children).
  • A community becomes susceptible to the disease if a large number of people who are not immune enter it either by birth or immigration (e.g. when an influx of unvaccinated refugees occurs in an area).

There are two ways of developing herd immunity:

  • High natural infection rate in the community eg measles infection
  • Artificial immunization. 80% immunized popns

Herd immunity -80% immunization of target popn. Larger popn acquiring a/bs via sickness.

  • Tetanus antibodies induced in the mother following immunization with tetanus toxoid easily passes across the placenta to the unborn child providing protection against tetanus in the neonatal period.
  • Measles antibodies made by the mother, passes through the placenta and breast milk, protecting the newborn during the first months of life.
  • Protection is better against some diseases (e.g. measles, rubella, tetanus) than others (e.g. polio, pertusis)

ü¶† Natural History & Vaccination Milestones

Natural History of Infection or disease

1st phase (entry and multiplication of infectious agent in the host) is marked by the progression from a healthy to a disease state. sub-clinical state.

2nd phase clinical phase - interaction between the pathogen / toxins and the body results in disease hence appearance of typical S/S. Is called incubation period. interval between exposure to an infectious agent and onset of clinical disease.

3rd phase-recovery or convalescence phase-healing or full recovery or with disability stage or death.. depends on how well the body handles the pathogen or the toxin.

The cause of infections

  • are caused by organisms which get into the body through inhalation, ingestion, or Penetration of the skin/mucus membrane, vector borne, contact, congenital.
  • These organisms multiply in the body tissues/blood and cause illness.
  • Disease causing organisms include: bacteria, viruses, parasites and fungi.
  • Some microorganisms produce chemicals called toxins, which cause illnesses.

History of vaccination

  • Over 200 years ago, Edward Jenner first demonstrated that vaccination offered protection against smallpox, by cutting an arm of a boy (James Phipps) and placing the materials from cowpox (mild disease) into the wound. Later, he injected the boy with fluid from smallpox and the boy did not contract the smallpox disease. This experiment led to the inoculation of persons with relatively harmless disease materials which could protect them from a more dangerous disease.
  • This was called vaccination ("vacca" is Latin for cow). Since then the use of vaccines has continued to reduce the burden of many bacterial and viral diseases.
  • Small pox has been eradicated, and poliomyelitis no longer occurs in many regions of the world as a result of widespread effective vaccination.
