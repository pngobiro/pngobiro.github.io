Traumatology 11: Comprehensive Review

Learning Outcomes Review

This lecture consolidates the management of traumatic injuries discussed throughout the course:

  • Management of upper limb injuries (Lectures 1-3)
  • Management of lower limb injuries (Lectures 4-7)
  • Management of head, chest, and spine injuries (Lecture 8)
  • Management of joint and soft tissue injuries (Lectures 9-10)

Definition of Traumatology

Traumatology is the study of the diagnosis and treatment of severe, acute physical injuries (e.g., from accidents, gunshot wounds) sustained by individuals requiring immediate medical attention.

It also encompasses the evaluation and treatment of psychological trauma in individuals affected by severe mental or emotional stress or physical injury.

Key Concepts: Upper Extremity Fractures

This section provides a summary of key points for fractures of the shoulder girdle, humerus, forearm, and hand.

  • Shoulder Girdle (Scapula, Clavicle): Often caused by high-energy trauma. Associated with significant risk to neurovascular structures (brachial plexus, axillary nerve) and pulmonary injuries. Treatment ranges from sling immobilization for clavicle fractures to ORIF for displaced scapular fractures.
  • Humerus (Proximal, Shaft, Distal): Management depends on location and patient age. Proximal fractures are common in the elderly. Shaft fractures risk radial nerve palsy. Distal fractures can lead to elbow stiffness and neurovascular complications.
  • Forearm (Radius/Ulna): "Both bone" fractures require precise anatomical reduction to preserve rotation (supination/pronation). Compartment syndrome is a critical concern.
  • Hand/Wrist (Carpals, Metacarpals, Phalanges): Scaphoid fractures have a high risk of avascular necrosis and nonunion. Metacarpal fractures often result from direct blows ("boxer's fracture"). Treatment prioritizes restoring hand function.

Key Concepts: Lower Extremity Fractures

This section provides a summary of key points for fractures of the pelvis, femur, tibia, fibula, and foot.

  • Pelvic Girdle: High-energy injuries with high mortality. Management priorities are resuscitation and achieving mechanical stability (pelvic binder, external fixation, ORIF).
  • Femur (Head, Neck, Shaft, Distal): Femoral neck fractures in the elderly are common and often require arthroplasty. Shaft fractures are major traumatic injuries. Distal femur fractures risk popliteal artery injury.
  • Tibia/Fibula: Tibial plateau and plafond fractures are complex intra-articular injuries requiring careful management to prevent post-traumatic arthritis. Compartment syndrome is a major risk in tibial shaft fractures.
  • Foot (Tarsals, Metatarsals): Calcaneus and talus fractures can have long-term morbidity. Stress fractures are common in metatarsals due to overuse.

Key Concepts: Spine and Soft Tissue Injuries

This section reviews critical concepts in spinal trauma and general soft tissue injuries.

  • Spinal Injuries: The primary goal is to prevent secondary spinal cord injury (SCI). Management involves immobilization, reduction of fractures/dislocations, and surgical stabilization when necessary. Types include compression, burst, and fracture-dislocations.
  • Soft Tissue Injuries (Dislocations, Sprains, Strains): A dislocation is a complete separation of a joint; a subluxation is partial. A sprain is a ligament injury, while a strain is a muscle/tendon injury. The RICE (Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation) principle is fundamental in acute management.
