Burns and Inhalation Injury

Inhalation Injury

Clinical Indications of Inhalation Injury

  • Face and/or neck burns.
  • Singeing of the eyebrows and around the nose.
  • Carbon deposits and acute inflammatory changes in the oropharynx.
  • Carbon particles seen in sputum.
  • Hoarseness.
  • History of impaired awareness (e.g., alcohol or head injury) and/or confinement in a burning environment.
  • Explosion, with burns to head and torso.
  • Carboxyhemoglobin level greater than 10%.

Management of Inhalation Injury

  • Early management may require endotracheal intubation and mechanical ventilation.
  • Transfer to a burn centre.
  • Stridor is an indication for immediate endotracheal intubation.
  • Circumferential burns of the neck may lead to swelling of the tissues around the airway and so require early intubation.

Carbon Monoxide (CO) Poisoning

Clinical Manifestations

Headache, muscular weakness, palpitation, dizziness, and confusion, which can progress rapidly to coma. Skin color, which can range from pink or cherry-red to cyanotic and pale, is not a reliable sign.

Details on CO Poisoning

CO poisoning has a much greater affinity than oxygen for hemoglobin and so displaces oxygen. Assume CO exposure in patients burned in enclosed areas. Diagnosis of CO poisoning is made primarily from a history of exposure. Patients with CO levels of less than 20% usually have no physical symptoms. Higher CO levels may result in headache and nausea, confusion, coma, and death. CO dissociates very slowly, but this is increased by breathing high-flow oxygen via a non-rebreathing mask.

Burn Assessment and Fluid Resuscitation

Two large calibre intravenous lines must be established. Patients with more than 15% of body surface area affected require fluid resuscitation.

Rule of 9's

For calculating the percentage of body burned in adults:

Fluid Calculation (Parkland Formula)

Formula: 4ml of Ringer's Lactate x Body Weight (kg) x % TBSA burn

Example: 100 kg patient with 50% TBSA burn:

4ml x 100 kg x 50 = 20,000 ml (20 Litres)

This is calculated for the first 24 hours post burn injury. Give half of this in the first 8 hours, and the remaining half over the next 16 hours.

Fluids

Adults

Ringer's lactate is the resuscitation fluid of choice for the first 24 hours. The goal of resuscitation is to maintain a urine output of 0.5 ml/kg/hr.

Children

Note

Resuscitation fluid as above plus maintenance (D5 0.45% Saline). Children have limited glycogen stores and are prone to hypoglycemia. Early nasogastric feeds or oral intake is encouraged.

General Management of Burns

  • Stop the burning process.
  • Check for associated injuries.
  • Analgesia (pain relief).
  • Tetanus prophylaxis.
  • Care of the burn wound.
  • Monitor peripheral circulation.
  • A nasogastric tube should be inserted in patients with nausea, vomiting, or burns greater than 20% TBSA.
  • Antibiotics are not given prophylactically.
  • Monitor urine output.
  • In circumferential burns, escharotomy may be indicated.

Management of Small Burns

  • Clean entire limb with soap and water (also under nails).
  • Apply topical antiseptic (no PO or IV antibiotic).
  • Dress limb in position of function, and elevate it.
  • Give pain meds as needed (PO, IM, or IV).
  • Rinse daily in clean water; in a shower is very practical.
  • Debride blister using simple instruments.

Dressing Hands and Legs

  • Allow use of the hands in dressings by day.
  • Splint in functional position by night.
  • Keep elevated to reduce swelling.
