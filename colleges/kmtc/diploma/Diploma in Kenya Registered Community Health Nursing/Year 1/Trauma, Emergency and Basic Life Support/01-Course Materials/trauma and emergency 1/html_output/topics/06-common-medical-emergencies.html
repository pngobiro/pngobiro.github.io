Common Medical Emergencies

Airway Obstruction

If the airway is completely obstructed, permanent brain damage or death will occur within 3 to 5 minutes secondary to hypoxia. Partial obstruction of the airway can lead to progressive hypoxia, hypercarbia, and respiratory and cardiac arrest.

Causes

Common causes include aspiration of foreign bodies, vomit, or blood.

Clinical Manifestations

Choking, apprehensive appearance, inspiratory and expiratory stridor, labored breathing, use of accessory muscles (suprasternal and intercostal retractions), flaring nostrils, increasing anxiety, restlessness, and confusion. Cyanosis and loss of consciousness develop as hypoxia worsens.

Keep the airway patent using the head-tilt-chin-lift maneuver, jaw thrust, intubation, or cricothyroidotomy.

Nose Bleeding (Epistaxis)

Causes

  • Frequent nose blowing
  • Allergic rhinitis
  • Hypertension
  • Bleeding disorders (e.g., hemophilia)
  • Low platelet count

Emergency Care

  1. Keep the person calm and still, as this can reduce bleeding.
  2. Get them to lean forward, not backwards, and make sure the blood drains out through the nose (not down the throat).
  3. Pinch the soft part of the nose for 10 minutes.
  4. Encourage them to breathe through their mouth.
  5. If bleeding does not stop, you can give Tranexamic acid (Cyclokapron) or pack the nose with epinephrine or liquid paraffin-soaked gauze.

Fainting (Syncope)

A sudden, brief loss of consciousness. It happens because, for a moment, there is not enough blood flowing to the brain.

Causes

  • Fatigue
  • Hypoglycemia
  • Emotional stress
  • Standing for long periods
  • Some medications (e.g., antihypertensives)
  • Forceful coughing

Manifestations

  • Brief loss of responsiveness, often causing them to fall to the ground.
  • A slow pulse.
  • Pale, cold skin and sweating.

Management

  1. Kneel down next to them and raise their legs, supporting their ankles on your shoulders to help blood flow back to the brain. Watch their face for signs that they're recovering.
  2. Make sure that they have plenty of fresh air—ask bystanders to move away, and if you're inside, then ask someone to open a window.
  3. Reassure the casualty and help them to sit up slowly.

Seizures

Due to an abnormal brain electrical activity.

Causes

  • Hereditary
  • Head injury
  • Brain tumor
  • Hypoglycemia in diabetic patients
  • Fever
  • Infection of the brain

Clinical Manifestations

  • Sudden loss of responsiveness
  • Rigid body with arching back
  • Noisy, difficult breathing
  • Convulsions (jerky, uncontrolled movements)
  • Loss of bladder and bowel control
  • Afterwards, they may be confused, tired, and fall into a deep sleep.

IMPORTANT

NOTE: DO NOT PUT ANYTHING IN THEIR MOUTH.

Management

  1. Do not restrain or move them.
  2. Protect them from hurting themselves; clear away any potentially dangerous objects, like hot drinks or sharp objects.
  3. Make a note of the time when the seizure started and how long it lasts.
  4. Protect their head by placing something soft underneath it, like a towel, and loosen any clothing around their neck.
  5. Once the seizure has stopped, check their breathing. If they are breathing, put them in the recovery position.

Recovery Position

Managing an Unresponsive Person

If someone is unresponsive and not breathing, you need to act straight away.

What you need to do:

  • Assess the scene for danger before approaching.
  • Check for a response.
  • Open the airway and check for breathing.
  • Call for emergency medical help before starting CPR.
  • Perform 30 chest compressions.
  • Perform 2 rescue breaths.
  • Continue CPR until help arrives or the person starts breathing.
