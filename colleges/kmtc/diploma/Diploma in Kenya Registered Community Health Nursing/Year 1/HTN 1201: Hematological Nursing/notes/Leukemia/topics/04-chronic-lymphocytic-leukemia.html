Chronic Lymphocytic Leukemia (CLL)

Background and Pathophysiology

Chronic Lymphocytic Leukemia (CLL) is a monoclonal disorder characterized by a progressive accumulation of functionally incompetent lymphocytes. It is the most common form of leukemia found in adults, typically affecting those aged 50-70 years.

The cells of origin in the majority of patients (75%) are clonal B cells arrested in their differentiation pathway. Morphologically, these cells resemble mature lymphocytes. These B-CLL lymphocytes typically express B-cell surface antigens (CD19, CD20, CD21, CD24) and also CD5, which is more commonly found on T cells.

The proto-oncogene bcl2 is known to be overexpressed in B-CLL. Bcl2 is a suppressor of apoptosis (programmed cell death), which results in a long life for the involved cells.

An abnormal karyotype is observed in most patients. Common abnormalities include:

  • •Deletion of 13q: Occurs in over 50% of patients and is associated with a relatively benign disease.
  • •Trisomy 12: Observed in 15% of patients, associated with atypical morphology and progressive disease.
  • •Deletions of 11q22-q23: Found in 19% of patients, associated with extensive lymph node involvement and aggressive disease.

Clinical Presentation

History

The onset is insidious, and the disorder is often discovered incidentally. Common symptoms include:

  • ✓Predisposition to repeated infections (e.g., pneumonia, herpes simplex).
  • ✓Enlarged lymph nodes.
  • ✓Early satiety and/or abdominal discomfort from an enlarged spleen.
  • ✓Mucocutaneous bleeding and/or petechiae due to thrombocytopenia.
  • ✓Tiredness and fatigue from anemia.
  • ✓Pruritic vesicular skin lesions.

Physical Examination

  • -Localized or generalized lymphadenopathy.
  • -Splenomegaly (30-40% of cases).
  • -Hepatomegaly (20% of cases).
  • -Petechiae and Pallor.

Staging Systems

Two main staging systems are used:

Rai-Sawitsky Staging System (5 Stages)

  • Stage 0: Lymphocytosis only. Survival > 120 months.
  • Stage I: Lymphocytosis + adenopathy. Survival ~95 months.
  • Stage II: Lymphocytosis + splenomegaly/hepatomegaly. Survival ~72 months.
  • Stage III: Lymphocytosis + anemia (Hb < 10 g). Survival ~30 months.
  • Stage IV: Lymphocytosis + thrombocytopenia (platelets < 100,000). Survival ~30 months.

Binet Staging System (3 Stages)

  • Stage A: Hemoglobin ≥ 100 g/L, platelets ≥ 100x10-9, and < 3 lymph node areas involved. Survival > 120 months.
  • Stage B: Same blood counts as A, but with ≥ 3 lymph node areas involved. Survival ~61 months.
  • Stage C: Hemoglobin < 100 g/L or platelets < 100x10-9. Survival ~32 months.

Investigations

  • 1.CBC with differential: Shows absolute lymphocytosis (> 5000 lymphocytes/mL), normocytic normochromic anemia, and thrombocytopenia. The WBC count can be elevated to 20,000-100,000/mm³.
  • 2.Peripheral Blood Film (PBF): Confirms lymphocytosis and shows the presence of "smudge cells," which are artifacts from damaged lymphocytes during slide preparation.
  • 3.Flow Cytometry: The most valuable test. It confirms circulating clonal B-lymphocytes expressing CD5, CD19, CD20(dim), and CD23.
  • 4.Bone Marrow Aspiration and Biopsy: Shows increased mature lymphocytes (up to 40% vs normal <5%) and decreased myeloid, erythroid, and megakaryocytic cells. Blast transformation may occur (Richter syndrome).
  • 5.Imaging Studies: Liver/spleen scan or CT may be used to demonstrate organomegaly or assess for organ compression by enlarged lymph nodes.

Management

Patients at stable, early stages (Rai 0, I, II) may only require periodic follow-up. Chemotherapy is initiated for patients with:

  • •Weight loss > 10%
  • •Extreme fatigue
  • •Fever or night sweats related to leukemia
  • •Progressive marrow failure
  • •Autoimmune anemia or thrombocytopenia not responding to prednisone
  • •Progressive splenomegaly or massive lymphadenopathy
  • •Progressive lymphocytosis (doubling time < 6 months)

Treatment Options

  1. Prednisolone: Useful for autoimmune manifestations.
  2. Nucleoside Analogs: Fludarabine, Cladribine, Pentostatin. Fludarabine has high response rates (80%).
  3. Chlorambucil and Prednisone: An older combination with response rates of 38-47%.
  4. Monoclonal Antibodies: Rituximab (Rituxan) is an example.

Management also involves antibiotics for infections.
