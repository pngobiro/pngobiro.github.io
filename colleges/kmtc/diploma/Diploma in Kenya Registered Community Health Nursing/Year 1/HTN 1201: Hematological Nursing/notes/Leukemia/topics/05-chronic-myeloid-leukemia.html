Chronic Myeloid Leukemia (CML)

Background and Pathophysiology

Chronic Myeloid Leukemia (CML), also known as Chronic Myelogenous Leukemia, is a myeloproliferative disorder characterized by increased proliferation of the granulocytic cell line without the loss of their capacity to differentiate. This results in a high number of granulocytes and their immature precursors in the peripheral blood.

CML is an acquired abnormality involving the hematopoietic stem cell. Its hallmark is a specific cytogenetic aberration known as the Philadelphia chromosome. This is a reciprocal translocation between the long arms of chromosomes 9 and 22, denoted as t(9;22).

This translocation relocates the abl oncogene from chromosome 9 to the BCR (Breakpoint Cluster Region) on chromosome 22. The resulting BCR/ABL fusion gene encodes a chimeric protein with strong tyrosine kinase activity, which leads to uncontrolled cell proliferation and the development of CML.

CML accounts for 20% of all adult leukemias and typically peaks between the ages of 40-50 years. Increased incidence has been reported among individuals exposed to radiation.

The Three Clinical Phases

The disease generally progresses through three distinct phases:

1. Chronic Phase

Most patients are diagnosed in this phase. It is characterized by splenomegaly and leukocytosis with few symptoms. The WBC count is usually controlled with medication. This phase can last for several years, especially with modern therapies.

2. Accelerated (or Transitional) Phase

This phase may last for several months and signals disease progression. It is characterized by poor control of blood counts, worsening anemia, and an increase in peripheral blast cells (≥15%), promyelocytes (≥30%), or basophils (≥20%). Bone pain and fever may occur. Survival in this phase is typically 1-2 years.

3. Blast Crisis (Acute Phase)

This final phase is similar to acute leukemia, with 30% or more blasts in the bone marrow and peripheral blood. Skin or tissue infiltration can also occur. Survival is typically 3-6 months. In two-thirds of cases, the blasts are myeloid; in one-third, they are lymphoid.

Clinical Presentation

History

The onset is often insidious and discovered incidentally. When symptoms occur, they are often nonspecific:

  • •Fatigue, weakness, anorexia, and weight loss.
  • •Symptoms related to splenomegaly: early satiety, abdominal discomfort, and left upper quadrant pain.
  • •Hypermetabolic symptoms: low-grade fever and excessive sweating.
  • •Rarely, symptoms of leukostasis (from extremely high WBC counts) like blurred vision, respiratory distress, or priapism.

Physical Findings

  • •Massive Splenomegaly: The most common physical finding.
  • •Hepatomegaly: Less common than splenomegaly.
  • •Sternal tenderness: A sign of marrow overexpansion.
  • •Funduscopy: May show papilledema, venous obstruction, and hemorrhages in cases of hyperviscosity.

Investigations

  • • Full Blood Count (FBC): The hallmark is an elevated WBC count, with a median of 150,000/μL. The peripheral blood film shows a "left-shifted" myeloid series with all stages of maturation present. Blasts are usually <5% in the chronic phase. Moderate anemia and thrombocytosis are common.
  • • Bone Marrow Aspiration: Hypercellular with myeloid hyperplasia. Myeloblast percentage helps define the phase (<10% in chronic).
  • • Cytogenetics: Essential for diagnosis. Detects the Philadelphia chromosome t(9;22) in peripheral blood or bone marrow. Molecular studies (PCR) can detect the bcr/abl fusion gene.
  • • Other Lab Tests: The Leukocyte Alkaline Phosphatase (LAP) score is invariably low. Serum vitamin B12 and uric acid levels are markedly elevated.

Management

The goals of treatment are to achieve hematologic remission (normal blood counts), cytogenetic remission (no Ph-positive cells), and molecular remission (negative PCR for BCR/ABL).

Treatment Options

  1. Tyrosine Kinase Inhibitors (TKIs): The cornerstone of CML therapy. Imatinib mesylate (Gleevec) was the first and directly inhibits the BCR/ABL protein's tyrosine kinase activity. It produces high rates of cytogenetic and molecular remission, dramatically improving survival. Newer generations of TKIs are also available.
  2. Hydroxyurea: An oral myelosuppressive agent used to control high WBC counts and achieve hematologic remission, often before starting a TKI.
  3. Busulfan: An older alkylating agent, now rarely used due to its side effects and difficulty in controlling counts.
  4. Interferon Alfa: Was a standard of care before TKIs, capable of inducing cytogenetic remission in some patients.
  5. Bone Marrow Transplant (BMT): A potentially curative option, considered for young patients with a matched sibling donor.
  6. Leukapheresis: A procedure to rapidly lower dangerously high WBC counts to alleviate symptoms of leukostasis.

Lymphoid blast crisis is treated with chemotherapy regimens used for acute lymphoblastic leukemia (e.g., vincristine, daunorubicin, prednisone).
