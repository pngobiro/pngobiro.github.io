Chapter Overview

Comprehensive study of emergency nursing principles, critical care management, and disaster preparedness in acute care settings.

Emergency Nursing

Definition and Scope

  • Emergency Nursing: Specialized care for patients with urgent, life-threatening conditions
  • Emergency Department (ED): 24/7 facility providing immediate care for acute illness and injury
  • Scope: Triage, stabilization, treatment, and disposition of emergency patients

Principles of Emergency Care

  • Rapid Assessment: Quick identification of life-threatening conditions
  • Prioritization: Triage system to determine treatment order
  • Stabilization: Immediate interventions to preserve life and prevent deterioration
  • Continuum of Care: Coordination with other healthcare services

Triage System

  • Level 1 (Resuscitation): Immediate life-threatening conditions
    • Cardiac arrest, severe trauma, respiratory failure
    • Seen immediately
  • Level 2 (Emergent): High risk of deterioration
    • Chest pain, severe bleeding, altered mental status
    • Seen within 15 minutes
  • Level 3 (Urgent): Potentially serious conditions
    • Moderate pain, minor fractures, vomiting
    • Seen within 30-60 minutes
  • Level 4 (Less Urgent): Non-urgent conditions
    • Minor injuries, mild symptoms
    • Seen within 1-2 hours
  • Level 5 (Non-Urgent): Minor problems
    • Chronic conditions, prescription refills
    • Seen within 2-4 hours

Primary Survey (ABCDE Approach)

  • A - Airway: Assess and maintain patent airway
    • Check for obstruction, foreign bodies
    • Cervical spine protection if trauma
    • Interventions: jaw thrust, airway adjuncts, intubation
  • B - Breathing: Assess ventilation and oxygenation
    • Respiratory rate, depth, effort
    • Oxygen saturation, breath sounds
    • Interventions: oxygen therapy, assisted ventilation
  • C - Circulation: Assess perfusion and control bleeding
    • Pulse, blood pressure, capillary refill
    • Signs of shock or hemorrhage
    • Interventions: IV access, fluid resuscitation, hemorrhage control
  • D - Disability: Assess neurological status
    • Level of consciousness (AVPU or GCS)
    • Pupil response
    • Motor function
  • E - Exposure: Complete examination
    • Remove clothing to assess injuries
    • Prevent hypothermia
    • Log roll if spinal injury suspected

Common Emergency Conditions

  • Cardiac Emergencies: MI, cardiac arrest, arrhythmias, heart failure
  • Respiratory Emergencies: Asthma, COPD exacerbation, pneumothorax, PE
  • Neurological Emergencies: Stroke, seizures, head injury, altered consciousness
  • Trauma: Multiple injuries, fractures, burns, penetrating injuries
  • Shock: Hypovolemic, cardiogenic, septic, anaphylactic
  • Poisoning/Overdose: Drug toxicity, environmental exposures

Issues in Emergency Nursing Care

  • Overcrowding: High patient volumes, long wait times
  • Violence: Aggressive patients, workplace safety
  • Ethical Dilemmas: End-of-life decisions, resource allocation
  • Compassion Fatigue: Emotional exhaustion from repeated trauma exposure
  • Legal Issues: Consent, documentation, mandatory reporting

Critical Care Nursing

Evolution and Definition

  • Critical Care Nursing (CCN): Specialized nursing for critically ill patients with life-threatening conditions
  • Evolution: Developed from need for specialized monitoring and intensive treatment
  • Setting: Intensive Care Units (ICU), Coronary Care Units (CCU), specialized units

Critical Care Unit/ICU

  • Purpose: Continuous monitoring and treatment of critically ill patients
  • Characteristics:
    • High nurse-to-patient ratio (1:1 or 1:2)
    • Advanced monitoring equipment
    • Immediate access to emergency interventions
    • Multidisciplinary team approach
  • Types of ICUs:
    • Medical ICU (MICU)
    • Surgical ICU (SICU)
    • Cardiac ICU (CICU)
    • Neurological ICU (NICU)
    • Pediatric ICU (PICU)
    • Neonatal ICU (NICU)

Critical Care Patient

  • Characteristics:
    • Life-threatening condition requiring intensive monitoring
    • Potential for rapid deterioration
    • Multiple organ system involvement
    • Dependence on life-support technology
  • Common Conditions:
    • Respiratory failure requiring mechanical ventilation
    • Sepsis and septic shock
    • Multiple organ dysfunction syndrome (MODS)
    • Post-cardiac arrest
    • Severe trauma
    • Post-major surgery

Management of Patients in Critical Care

  • Hemodynamic Monitoring:
    • Arterial lines for continuous BP monitoring
    • Central venous pressure (CVP)
    • Pulmonary artery catheter (Swan-Ganz)
    • Cardiac output monitoring
  • Respiratory Support:
    • Mechanical ventilation
    • Ventilator modes and settings
    • Weaning protocols
    • Airway management
  • Medication Management:
    • Vasoactive drugs (vasopressors, inotropes)
    • Sedation and analgesia
    • Continuous infusions
    • Titration based on patient response
  • Neurological Assessment:
    • Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS)
    • Pupil assessment
    • Intracranial pressure monitoring
    • Sedation scales

Stressors in ICU

  • Patient Stressors:
    • Pain and discomfort
    • Sleep deprivation
    • Loss of control and autonomy
    • Fear of death
    • Sensory overload or deprivation
    • Inability to communicate (intubation)
  • Family Stressors:
    • Uncertainty about prognosis
    • Separation from loved one
    • Unfamiliar environment and equipment
    • Financial concerns
    • Decision-making burden
  • Staff Stressors:
    • High-acuity patients
    • Moral distress
    • Burnout
    • Compassion fatigue

Problems Related to Family Members of ICU Patients

  • Communication Needs: Regular updates, clear explanations
  • Emotional Support: Coping with crisis, grief support
  • Visitation: Flexible policies, family presence during procedures
  • Participation in Care: Involvement in decision-making, bedside presence

Ethical Decision-Making in CCN

  • Common Ethical Issues:
    • Withdrawal or withholding of life support
    • Do Not Resuscitate (DNR) orders
    • Resource allocation
    • Informed consent
    • Futility of treatment
  • Ethical Principles:
    • Autonomy: Patient's right to make decisions
    • Beneficence: Acting in patient's best interest
    • Non-maleficence: Do no harm
    • Justice: Fair distribution of resources
  • Advance Directives: Living wills, healthcare proxies, DNR orders

Prevention of ICU Complications

  • Ventilator-Associated Pneumonia (VAP):
    • Elevate head of bed 30-45 degrees
    • Oral care with chlorhexidine
    • Subglottic suctioning
    • Daily sedation vacation and weaning trials
  • Pressure Injuries:
    • Frequent repositioning
    • Pressure-relieving devices
    • Skin assessment
  • ICU Delirium:
    • Early mobilization
    • Minimize sedation
    • Reorientation strategies
    • Sleep promotion
  • Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT):
    • Pharmacological prophylaxis
    • Sequential compression devices
    • Early mobilization

Disaster Nursing

Definition and Types

  • Disaster: Sudden event causing widespread destruction and distress
  • Types:
    • Natural: Earthquakes, floods, hurricanes, pandemics
    • Man-made: Terrorism, industrial accidents, mass casualties

Disaster Management Phases

  • Mitigation: Prevention and risk reduction
  • Preparedness: Planning, training, drills
  • Response: Immediate action during disaster
  • Recovery: Restoration of services and community

Hospital Emergency Preparedness Plans

  • Components:
    • Command structure (Incident Command System)
    • Communication systems
    • Resource management
    • Surge capacity planning
    • Decontamination procedures
    • Staff roles and responsibilities
  • Mass Casualty Triage:
    • START (Simple Triage and Rapid Treatment)
    • Color-coded tags: Red (immediate), Yellow (delayed), Green (minor), Black (deceased/expectant)
  • Decontamination:
    • Remove contaminated clothing
    • Wash with soap and water
    • Protect healthcare workers

Nurse's Role in Disaster

  • Preparedness: Training, drills, maintaining skills
  • Response: Triage, treatment, documentation
  • Psychological Support: Crisis intervention, stress management
  • Public Health: Disease surveillance, vaccination campaigns

Key Points

  • Emergency nursing requires rapid assessment and prioritization using triage systems
  • ABCDE approach is fundamental to emergency patient assessment
  • Critical care nursing involves intensive monitoring and management of life-threatening conditions
  • ICU patients require high nurse-to-patient ratios and advanced technology
  • Prevention of ICU complications (VAP, delirium, pressure injuries) is essential
  • Ethical decision-making is central to critical care practice
  • Family-centered care improves outcomes and satisfaction in ICU
  • Disaster preparedness requires planning, training, and coordinated response
  • Mass casualty triage differs from routine emergency triage
