Chapter Overview

This chapter provides foundational knowledge about medical-surgical nursing, including the historical evolution of medicine and nursing in India, fundamental concepts of health and illness, and essential principles of surgical nursing care.

Evolution of Medicine and Nursing in India

Key Historical Periods

  • 3000 BC - Indus Valley Civilization: Evidence of environmental sanitation, underground drains, public baths
  • 2000 BC - Vedic Period: Beginning of Ayurveda, emphasis on holistic health (physical, mental, social, spiritual)
  • 800 BC - 1000 AD - Golden Age: Development of Charaka-Samhita and Sushruta-Samhita
  • 1600 AD onwards: Introduction of Western medicine with British rule

Traditional Indian Medicine

  • Ayurveda: "Science of life and art of living"
    • Founded by Sage Atreya
    • Based on balance of three doshas: Vata (wind), Pitta (bile), Kapha (phlegm)
    • Emphasis on prevention and health promotion
  • Key Texts:
    • Charaka-Samhita (medicine, diagnosis, treatment)
    • Sushruta-Samhita (surgery, 300+ operations, 121 instruments)
    • Astanga Hridaya by Vagbhata (8 branches of Ayurveda)

Ancient Hindu Surgery

  • Advanced surgical techniques including:
    • Plastic surgery (rhinoplasty)
    • Lithotomy (bladder stone removal)
    • Cataract surgery
    • Anal fistula repair
  • Use of 20 sharp and 101 blunt instruments
  • Knowledge of anesthesia using alcohol

Development of Modern Nursing in India

  • Challenges: Low status of women, purdah system, caste system, illiteracy
  • 1664: First hospital for soldiers at Fort St. George, Madras
  • 1871: Government General Hospital, Madras began nurse training
  • 1883: Pestani Hormusji Cama Hospital founded
  • 1891: Bai Kashibai Ganpat - first Indian nurse trainee
  • 1909: Bombay Presidency Nursing Association established
  • Post-1947: Significant improvements after Independence

Concepts of Health and Illness

Definitions

  • Health (WHO): State of complete physical, mental, and social well-being, not merely absence of disease
  • Illness: Subjective experience of symptoms and suffering
  • Disease: Objective pathological condition diagnosed by healthcare professionals
  • Sickness: Social role adopted by ill person

Illness Behavior Stages

  1. Symptom Experience: Recognition that something is wrong
  2. Assumption of Sick Role: Seeking validation from others
  3. Medical Care Contact: Consulting healthcare professionals
  4. Dependent Patient Role: Accepting treatment and following advice
  5. Recovery/Rehabilitation: Returning to normal activities

Sick Role Behavior

  • Exemption from normal social responsibilities
  • Not held responsible for condition
  • Obligation to try to get well
  • Obligation to seek competent help

Effects of Hospitalization

  • On Patient:
    • Loss of independence and control
    • Anxiety and fear
    • Disruption of daily routine
    • Privacy concerns
    • Separation from family
  • On Family:
    • Emotional stress and worry
    • Financial burden
    • Role changes and responsibilities
    • Disruption of family routine

Infection and Inflammation

Chain of Infection

  1. Infectious Agent: Bacteria, viruses, fungi, parasites
  2. Reservoir: Where pathogen lives and multiplies
  3. Portal of Exit: How pathogen leaves reservoir
  4. Mode of Transmission: Direct/indirect contact, droplet, airborne, vector
  5. Portal of Entry: How pathogen enters new host
  6. Susceptible Host: Person at risk of infection

Asepsis

  • Medical Asepsis: Clean technique to reduce microorganisms
    • Hand hygiene
    • Use of barriers (gloves, gowns)
    • Environmental cleaning
  • Surgical Asepsis: Sterile technique to eliminate all microorganisms
    • Sterilization of instruments
    • Sterile field maintenance
    • Sterile gowning and gloving

Inflammation Process

  • Cardinal Signs:
    • Redness (rubor)
    • Heat (calor)
    • Swelling (tumor)
    • Pain (dolor)
    • Loss of function (functio laesa)
  • Phases: Vascular response → Cellular response → Tissue repair

Role of Nurses in Surgical Setting

General Preoperative Care

  • Assessment:
    • Medical history and physical examination
    • Psychological assessment (anxiety, fears)
    • Nutritional status
    • Medications and allergies
  • Patient Education:
    • Explanation of procedure
    • Preoperative instructions (NPO, bowel prep)
    • Postoperative expectations
    • Deep breathing and coughing exercises
  • Physical Preparation:
    • Skin preparation and surgical site marking
    • Removal of jewelry, dentures, prosthetics
    • Bladder emptying
    • Preoperative medications
  • Documentation:
    • Informed consent verification
    • Preoperative checklist completion
    • Vital signs recording

General Postoperative Care

  • Immediate Recovery (PACU):
    • Airway maintenance
    • Vital signs monitoring (every 15 minutes initially)
    • Level of consciousness assessment
    • Pain management
    • Wound and drainage monitoring
  • Ongoing Care:
    • Prevention of complications (DVT, pneumonia, infection)
    • Early mobilization
    • Fluid and electrolyte balance
    • Nutritional support
    • Wound care
  • Discharge Planning:
    • Patient education on home care
    • Medication instructions
    • Follow-up appointments
    • Warning signs to report

Intensive Care Unit (ICU)

  • Purpose: Continuous monitoring and care for critically ill patients
  • Equipment: Cardiac monitors, ventilators, infusion pumps, hemodynamic monitoring
  • Nursing Responsibilities:
    • Continuous assessment and monitoring
    • Management of life-support equipment
    • Prevention of complications
    • Family support and communication

Wound and Its Healing

Types of Wounds

  • By Cause:
    • Incised (clean cut)
    • Lacerated (torn, irregular)
    • Contused (bruised)
    • Puncture (deep, narrow)
    • Abrasion (scraped)
  • By Contamination:
    • Clean
    • Clean-contaminated
    • Contaminated
    • Dirty/infected

Phases of Wound Healing

  1. Inflammatory Phase (1-4 days):
    • Hemostasis and clot formation
    • Vasodilation and increased permeability
    • White blood cell migration
  2. Proliferative Phase (4-21 days):
    • Granulation tissue formation
    • Collagen synthesis
    • Epithelialization
    • Wound contraction
  3. Maturation Phase (21 days - 2 years):
    • Collagen remodeling
    • Increased tensile strength
    • Scar formation

Types of Healing

  • Primary Intention: Clean wound edges approximated, minimal scarring
  • Secondary Intention: Wound left open, heals by granulation, more scarring
  • Tertiary Intention (Delayed Primary): Initially left open, later sutured

Factors Affecting Wound Healing

  • Local Factors: Blood supply, infection, foreign bodies, tissue trauma
  • Systemic Factors: Age, nutrition, diabetes, medications (steroids), smoking

Complications

  • Infection
  • Hemorrhage
  • Dehiscence (wound separation)
  • Evisceration (organ protrusion)
  • Keloid formation

Key Points to Remember

  • Indian medicine has ancient roots dating back to 3000 BC with sophisticated understanding of health and disease
  • Ayurveda emphasizes holistic health and balance of three doshas
  • Ancient Indian surgeons performed advanced procedures including plastic surgery
  • Modern nursing in India developed gradually, overcoming social and cultural barriers
  • Health is multidimensional: physical, mental, social, and spiritual well-being
  • Illness behavior progresses through distinct stages
  • Aseptic technique is fundamental to preventing healthcare-associated infections
  • Perioperative nursing care is critical for patient safety and optimal outcomes
  • Wound healing occurs in three overlapping phases
  • Multiple factors can affect wound healing and must be addressed
