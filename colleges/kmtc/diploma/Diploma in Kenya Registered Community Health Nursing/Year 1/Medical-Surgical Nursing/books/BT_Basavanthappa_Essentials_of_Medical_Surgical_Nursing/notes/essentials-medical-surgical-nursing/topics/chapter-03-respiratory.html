Chapter Overview

This chapter covers comprehensive nursing assessment of the respiratory system and management of common respiratory disorders including infections, chronic conditions, and emergencies.

Nursing Assessment of Respiratory System

Health History

  • Chief complaint (dyspnea, cough, chest pain)
  • Past respiratory illnesses
  • Smoking history (pack-years)
  • Occupational exposures
  • Allergies
  • Current medications
  • Family history of respiratory disease

Physical Examination

  • Inspection:
    • Respiratory rate, rhythm, depth
    • Use of accessory muscles
    • Chest shape and symmetry
    • Skin color (cyanosis, pallor)
    • Clubbing of fingers
    • Sputum characteristics
  • Palpation:
    • Chest expansion
    • Tactile fremitus
    • Tracheal position
    • Tenderness or masses
  • Percussion:
    • Resonance (normal)
    • Dullness (consolidation, fluid)
    • Hyperresonance (air trapping)
  • Auscultation:
    • Normal breath sounds (vesicular, bronchial, bronchovesicular)
    • Adventitious sounds:
      • Crackles (rales) - fluid in airways
      • Wheezes - narrowed airways
      • Rhonchi - secretions in large airways
      • Pleural friction rub - inflamed pleura

Diagnostic Studies

  • Laboratory Tests:
    • Arterial Blood Gases (ABGs)
    • Sputum culture and sensitivity
    • Complete blood count
    • Pulmonary function tests (PFTs)
  • Imaging:
    • Chest X-ray
    • CT scan
    • MRI
  • Invasive Procedures:
    • Bronchoscopy
    • Thoracentesis
    • Lung biopsy

Respiratory Infections

Acute Bronchitis

  • Definition: Inflammation of bronchial mucosa
  • Causes: Viral (most common), bacterial, irritants
  • Signs & Symptoms:
    • Productive cough (initially dry, then productive)
    • Chest discomfort
    • Low-grade fever
    • Wheezing
  • Management:
    • Rest and increased fluid intake
    • Cough suppressants (if dry cough)
    • Expectorants (if productive cough)
    • Antibiotics (if bacterial)
    • Bronchodilators if wheezing

Pneumonia

  • Definition: Inflammation of lung parenchyma
  • Types:
    • Community-acquired pneumonia (CAP)
    • Hospital-acquired pneumonia (HAP)
    • Aspiration pneumonia
    • Ventilator-associated pneumonia (VAP)
  • Common Pathogens:
    • Streptococcus pneumoniae
    • Haemophilus influenzae
    • Mycoplasma pneumoniae
    • Viruses (influenza, COVID-19)
  • Signs & Symptoms:
    • High fever with chills
    • Productive cough (rust-colored sputum)
    • Pleuritic chest pain
    • Dyspnea, tachypnea
    • Crackles on auscultation
    • Confusion (especially in elderly)
  • Nursing Management:
    • Administer antibiotics as prescribed
    • Oxygen therapy to maintain SpO2 >90%
    • Encourage fluid intake (2-3 L/day)
    • Position in semi-Fowler's or high Fowler's
    • Chest physiotherapy
    • Monitor vital signs and respiratory status
    • Encourage deep breathing and coughing
    • Provide rest periods

Tuberculosis (TB)

  • Definition: Infectious disease caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis
  • Transmission: Airborne droplets
  • Types:
    • Latent TB (infected but not active)
    • Active TB (symptomatic and contagious)
    • Pulmonary TB (most common)
    • Extrapulmonary TB
  • Signs & Symptoms:
    • Persistent cough (>3 weeks)
    • Hemoptysis
    • Night sweats
    • Weight loss
    • Low-grade fever
    • Fatigue
  • Diagnosis:
    • Tuberculin skin test (Mantoux test)
    • Interferon-gamma release assays (IGRAs)
    • Chest X-ray
    • Sputum smear and culture
  • Treatment:
    • DOTS (Directly Observed Treatment, Short-course)
    • First-line drugs: Isoniazid, Rifampicin, Pyrazinamide, Ethambutol
    • Duration: 6-9 months minimum
    • Compliance is critical to prevent drug resistance
  • Nursing Management:
    • Implement airborne precautions
    • Ensure medication compliance
    • Monitor for drug side effects
    • Educate about disease transmission
    • Contact tracing and screening
    • Nutritional support
    • Emotional support

Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD)

Definition

  • Progressive, irreversible airflow limitation
  • Includes chronic bronchitis and emphysema
  • Major cause: Cigarette smoking

Chronic Bronchitis

  • Definition: Productive cough for 3 months in 2 consecutive years
  • Pathophysiology:
    • Hypertrophy of mucus glands
    • Increased mucus production
    • Chronic inflammation
    • Airway obstruction
  • Clinical Features:
    • Chronic productive cough
    • Dyspnea on exertion
    • Wheezing
    • Cyanosis ("blue bloater")
    • Peripheral edema

Emphysema

  • Definition: Destruction of alveolar walls
  • Pathophysiology:
    • Loss of elastic recoil
    • Air trapping
    • Hyperinflation
    • Decreased gas exchange
  • Clinical Features:
    • Severe dyspnea
    • Minimal cough
    • Barrel chest
    • Use of accessory muscles
    • Pursed-lip breathing
    • Weight loss ("pink puffer")

COPD Management

  • Pharmacological:
    • Bronchodilators (short and long-acting)
    • Inhaled corticosteroids
    • Combination inhalers
    • Antibiotics (for exacerbations)
    • Mucolytics
  • Oxygen Therapy:
    • Low-flow oxygen (1-2 L/min)
    • Caution: Risk of CO2 retention
    • Target SpO2: 88-92%
  • Pulmonary Rehabilitation:
    • Exercise training
    • Breathing techniques
    • Energy conservation
    • Nutritional counseling
  • Nursing Interventions:
    • Smoking cessation counseling
    • Teach proper inhaler technique
    • Encourage pursed-lip breathing
    • Position in high Fowler's
    • Small, frequent meals
    • Prevent infections (vaccinations)
    • Monitor for exacerbations
    • Provide emotional support

Asthma

  • Definition: Chronic inflammatory disorder with reversible airway obstruction
  • Triggers:
    • Allergens (pollen, dust mites, pet dander)
    • Respiratory infections
    • Exercise
    • Cold air
    • Stress
    • Medications (aspirin, NSAIDs)
  • Signs & Symptoms:
    • Wheezing (especially expiratory)
    • Dyspnea
    • Chest tightness
    • Cough (worse at night)
    • Use of accessory muscles
  • Management:
    • Quick-relief medications (SABAs)
    • Long-term control medications (ICS, LABAs)
    • Avoid triggers
    • Peak flow monitoring
    • Asthma action plan

Respiratory Emergencies

Pneumothorax

  • Definition: Air in pleural space causing lung collapse
  • Types:
    • Spontaneous (primary or secondary)
    • Traumatic
    • Tension pneumothorax (medical emergency)
  • Signs & Symptoms:
    • Sudden chest pain
    • Dyspnea
    • Decreased breath sounds on affected side
    • Hyperresonance on percussion
    • Tracheal deviation (tension pneumothorax)
  • Management:
    • Oxygen therapy
    • Chest tube insertion
    • Needle decompression (tension pneumothorax)
    • Monitor chest drainage system

Pulmonary Embolism (PE)

  • Definition: Blockage of pulmonary artery by thrombus
  • Risk Factors:
    • Immobility
    • Surgery
    • Oral contraceptives
    • Pregnancy
    • Cancer
    • DVT
  • Signs & Symptoms:
    • Sudden dyspnea
    • Chest pain (pleuritic)
    • Tachypnea, tachycardia
    • Hemoptysis
    • Anxiety
    • Hypoxemia
  • Management:
    • Oxygen therapy
    • Anticoagulation (heparin, warfarin)
    • Thrombolytics (massive PE)
    • Bed rest initially
    • Monitor for bleeding
    • Prevention: Early mobilization, compression stockings

Respiratory Failure

  • Definition: Inability to maintain adequate gas exchange
  • Types:
    • Type I (Hypoxemic): PaO2 <60 mmHg
    • Type II (Hypercapnic): PaCO2 >50 mmHg
  • Causes:
    • COPD exacerbation
    • Pneumonia
    • Pulmonary edema
    • ARDS
    • Neuromuscular disorders
  • Management:
    • Oxygen therapy
    • Mechanical ventilation if needed
    • Treat underlying cause
    • Monitor ABGs
    • Maintain airway patency

Chest Trauma

  • Rib Fractures:
    • Pain on breathing, coughing
    • Splinting of chest
    • Risk of pneumothorax, hemothorax
    • Management: Pain control, deep breathing exercises
  • Flail Chest:
    • Multiple rib fractures causing paradoxical movement
    • Severe respiratory distress
    • May require mechanical ventilation
  • Hemothorax:
    • Blood in pleural space
    • Decreased breath sounds, dullness on percussion
    • Management: Chest tube drainage

Key Points to Remember

  • Respiratory assessment includes inspection, palpation, percussion, and auscultation
  • Pneumonia requires prompt antibiotic therapy and supportive care
  • TB treatment requires long-term compliance with multiple drugs
  • COPD is progressive and irreversible; smoking cessation is crucial
  • Asthma is reversible with proper management and trigger avoidance
  • Oxygen therapy in COPD should be low-flow to prevent CO2 retention
  • Tension pneumothorax is a medical emergency requiring immediate decompression
  • Pulmonary embolism requires anticoagulation and close monitoring
  • Early mobilization and prophylaxis prevent many respiratory complications
