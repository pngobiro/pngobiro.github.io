Chapter Overview

This chapter covers assessment of the digestive system and nursing management of common gastrointestinal disorders affecting the mouth, esophagus, stomach, intestines, and accessory organs.

Nursing Assessment of Digestive System

Health History

  • Chief complaint (pain, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, constipation)
  • Dietary habits and patterns
  • Bowel elimination patterns
  • Past GI illnesses or surgeries
  • Medications (including OTC)
  • Alcohol and tobacco use
  • Family history of GI disorders

Physical Examination

  • Inspection:
    • Abdominal contour (flat, rounded, distended)
    • Skin color and condition
    • Visible peristalsis or pulsations
    • Scars, striae, or lesions
  • Auscultation (before palpation):
    • Bowel sounds (normal, hyperactive, hypoactive, absent)
    • Vascular sounds
  • Percussion:
    • Tympany (gas-filled areas)
    • Dullness (solid organs, fluid)
    • Liver span
  • Palpation:
    • Light palpation for tenderness
    • Deep palpation for masses
    • Rebound tenderness
    • Organ size and position

Diagnostic Studies

  • Laboratory: CBC, liver function tests, amylase, lipase, stool studies
  • Radiological: X-rays, barium studies, CT scan, MRI, ultrasound
  • Endoscopic: Esophagogastroduodenoscopy (EGD), colonoscopy, sigmoidoscopy
  • Other: Liver biopsy, paracentesis

Upper GI Disorders

Oral Problems

  • Stomatitis: Inflammation of oral mucosa
    • Causes: Infections, trauma, chemotherapy, nutritional deficiencies
    • Management: Oral hygiene, topical anesthetics, treat underlying cause
  • Oral Cancer:
    • Risk factors: Tobacco, alcohol, HPV
    • Signs: Non-healing ulcer, white/red patches, difficulty swallowing
    • Treatment: Surgery, radiation, chemotherapy

Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease (GERD)

  • Definition: Backflow of gastric contents into esophagus
  • Signs & Symptoms:
    • Heartburn (pyrosis)
    • Regurgitation
    • Dysphagia
    • Chronic cough
    • Hoarseness
  • Management:
    • Lifestyle modifications: Elevate head of bed, avoid trigger foods, weight loss
    • Medications: Antacids, H2 blockers, PPIs
    • Surgery: Fundoplication (severe cases)

Peptic Ulcer Disease (PUD)

  • Definition: Erosion in gastric or duodenal mucosa
  • Causes:
    • H. pylori infection (most common)
    • NSAIDs
    • Stress
    • Smoking, alcohol
  • Signs & Symptoms:
    • Epigastric pain (burning, gnawing)
    • Gastric ulcer: Pain worsens with eating
    • Duodenal ulcer: Pain relieved by eating
    • Nausea, vomiting
    • Hematemesis, melena (if bleeding)
  • Complications:
    • Hemorrhage
    • Perforation
    • Obstruction
  • Management:
    • H. pylori eradication: Triple therapy (PPI + 2 antibiotics)
    • PPIs or H2 blockers
    • Avoid NSAIDs, alcohol, smoking
    • Stress management
    • Surgery for complications

Gastritis

  • Definition: Inflammation of gastric mucosa
  • Types: Acute (sudden onset) vs Chronic (long-term)
  • Causes: H. pylori, NSAIDs, alcohol, stress
  • Management: Similar to PUD, avoid irritants

Nausea and Vomiting

  • Causes: GI disorders, medications, pregnancy, motion sickness, CNS disorders
  • Nursing Management:
    • Assess characteristics and triggers
    • Antiemetics as prescribed
    • Small, frequent meals
    • Avoid strong odors
    • Monitor for dehydration
    • IV fluids if severe

Lower GI Disorders

Diarrhea

  • Definition: Frequent loose, watery stools
  • Causes: Infections, medications, food intolerances, IBD
  • Complications: Dehydration, electrolyte imbalance
  • Management:
    • Oral rehydration solutions
    • BRAT diet (Bananas, Rice, Applesauce, Toast)
    • Antidiarrheals (loperamide)
    • Treat underlying cause
    • Monitor I&O, electrolytes

Constipation

  • Definition: Infrequent, difficult bowel movements
  • Causes: Low fiber diet, inadequate fluids, immobility, medications
  • Management:
    • Increase fiber intake
    • Adequate fluid intake (2-3 L/day)
    • Regular exercise
    • Establish bowel routine
    • Laxatives if needed (bulk-forming, osmotic, stimulant)

Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)

  • Crohn's Disease:
    • Can affect any part of GI tract (mouth to anus)
    • Transmural inflammation
    • Skip lesions
    • Symptoms: Diarrhea, abdominal pain, weight loss, fistulas
  • Ulcerative Colitis:
    • Affects colon and rectum only
    • Continuous inflammation of mucosa
    • Symptoms: Bloody diarrhea, urgency, tenesmus
  • Management:
    • Anti-inflammatory drugs (5-ASA, corticosteroids)
    • Immunosuppressants
    • Biologics
    • Nutritional support
    • Surgery (colectomy for UC, resection for Crohn's)

Appendicitis

  • Definition: Inflammation of appendix
  • Signs & Symptoms:
    • Periumbilical pain migrating to RLQ (McBurney's point)
    • Anorexia, nausea, vomiting
    • Low-grade fever
    • Rebound tenderness
    • Elevated WBC
  • Management:
    • NPO
    • IV fluids
    • Antibiotics
    • Appendectomy (surgical removal)
    • Monitor for perforation

Intestinal Obstruction

  • Causes: Adhesions, hernias, tumors, volvulus, intussusception
  • Signs & Symptoms:
    • Abdominal pain (colicky)
    • Distension
    • Vomiting
    • Constipation, no flatus
    • High-pitched bowel sounds (early), absent (late)
  • Management:
    • NPO
    • NG tube decompression
    • IV fluids and electrolytes
    • Surgery if complete obstruction

Colorectal Cancer

  • Risk Factors: Age >50, family history, IBD, polyps, diet high in red meat
  • Signs & Symptoms:
    • Change in bowel habits
    • Rectal bleeding
    • Abdominal pain
    • Weight loss
    • Anemia
  • Screening: Colonoscopy, fecal occult blood test, CEA
  • Treatment: Surgery, chemotherapy, radiation

Hemorrhoids

  • Definition: Swollen veins in rectum/anus
  • Causes: Straining, constipation, pregnancy, prolonged sitting
  • Management:
    • High-fiber diet
    • Sitz baths
    • Topical preparations
    • Avoid straining
    • Surgery for severe cases

Liver and Pancreatic Disorders

Hepatitis

  • Types:
    • Hepatitis A: Fecal-oral transmission, acute
    • Hepatitis B: Blood/body fluids, can be chronic
    • Hepatitis C: Blood transmission, often chronic
    • Hepatitis D: Requires HBV co-infection
    • Hepatitis E: Fecal-oral, acute
  • Signs & Symptoms:
    • Jaundice
    • Fatigue
    • Anorexia, nausea
    • Dark urine, clay-colored stools
    • Hepatomegaly
    • Elevated liver enzymes
  • Management:
    • Rest
    • Adequate nutrition
    • Avoid alcohol and hepatotoxic drugs
    • Antivirals for chronic HBV, HCV
    • Vaccination (HAV, HBV)
    • Standard precautions

Cirrhosis

  • Definition: Chronic, irreversible liver damage with fibrosis
  • Causes: Chronic alcohol abuse, viral hepatitis, NAFLD
  • Signs & Symptoms:
    • Jaundice
    • Ascites
    • Portal hypertension
    • Esophageal varices
    • Spider angiomas
    • Hepatic encephalopathy
    • Coagulopathy
  • Complications:
    • Variceal bleeding
    • Hepatorenal syndrome
    • Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis
    • Hepatocellular carcinoma
  • Management:
    • Abstain from alcohol
    • Low-sodium diet
    • Diuretics for ascites
    • Lactulose for encephalopathy
    • Beta-blockers for varices
    • Liver transplant (end-stage)

Pancreatitis

  • Acute Pancreatitis:
    • Causes: Gallstones, alcohol
    • Symptoms: Severe epigastric pain radiating to back, nausea, vomiting
    • Elevated amylase and lipase
    • Management: NPO, NG suction, IV fluids, pain control
  • Chronic Pancreatitis:
    • Progressive destruction of pancreas
    • Symptoms: Chronic pain, malabsorption, diabetes
    • Management: Pain control, pancreatic enzymes, insulin

Cholecystitis and Cholelithiasis

  • Cholelithiasis: Gallstones
  • Cholecystitis: Inflammation of gallbladder
  • Risk Factors: 4 F's - Female, Forty, Fat, Fertile
  • Signs & Symptoms:
    • RUQ pain (especially after fatty meals)
    • Nausea, vomiting
    • Jaundice (if bile duct obstruction)
    • Murphy's sign positive
  • Management:
    • Low-fat diet
    • Pain management
    • Cholecystectomy (surgical removal)

Key Points to Remember

  • Always auscultate abdomen before palpation to avoid altering bowel sounds
  • GERD management includes lifestyle modifications and medications
  • H. pylori is the most common cause of peptic ulcers
  • Appendicitis requires prompt surgical intervention to prevent perforation
  • IBD is chronic and requires long-term management
  • Hepatitis B and C can lead to chronic liver disease and cirrhosis
  • Cirrhosis complications include ascites, varices, and encephalopathy
  • Acute pancreatitis requires NPO and aggressive fluid resuscitation
  • Colorectal cancer screening is important for early detection
