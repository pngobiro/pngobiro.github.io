Chapter Overview

Comprehensive study of renal assessment, urinary tract infections, kidney stones, acute and chronic renal failure, and dialysis management.

Renal Assessment

Functions of Kidneys

  • Filtration and excretion of waste products
  • Regulation of fluid and electrolyte balance
  • Acid-base balance
  • Blood pressure regulation (renin-angiotensin system)
  • Erythropoietin production
  • Vitamin D activation

Assessment

  • Urinary patterns (frequency, urgency, nocturia, dysuria)
  • Urine characteristics (color, clarity, odor, amount)
  • Pain (flank, suprapubic)
  • Edema
  • Hypertension

Diagnostic Tests

  • Urinalysis
  • Urine culture and sensitivity
  • BUN, creatinine, GFR
  • Electrolytes
  • Ultrasound, CT scan, IVP
  • Cystoscopy
  • Renal biopsy

Urinary Tract Infections

Cystitis (Bladder Infection)

  • Causes: E. coli (most common), Staphylococcus saprophyticus
  • Risk Factors: Female gender, sexual activity, catheterization, diabetes
  • Signs & Symptoms:
    • Dysuria, frequency, urgency
    • Suprapubic pain
    • Cloudy, foul-smelling urine
    • Hematuria
  • Management:
    • Antibiotics (trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole, nitrofurantoin)
    • Increase fluid intake
    • Cranberry juice
    • Avoid irritants (caffeine, alcohol)

Pyelonephritis (Kidney Infection)

  • Definition: Infection of renal pelvis and parenchyma
  • Signs & Symptoms:
    • High fever, chills
    • Flank pain, CVA tenderness
    • Nausea, vomiting
    • Dysuria, frequency
  • Management:
    • IV antibiotics initially
    • Hospitalization if severe
    • Adequate hydration
    • Pain management

Prevention of UTI

  • Adequate fluid intake (2-3 L/day)
  • Void frequently, don't hold urine
  • Wipe front to back
  • Void after intercourse
  • Avoid irritants (bubble baths, douches)
  • Proper catheter care

Urolithiasis (Kidney Stones)

  • Types: Calcium oxalate (most common), uric acid, struvite, cystine
  • Risk Factors: Dehydration, diet high in protein/sodium, family history
  • Signs & Symptoms:
    • Severe colicky flank pain radiating to groin
    • Hematuria
    • Nausea, vomiting
    • Frequency, urgency
  • Management:
    • Pain management (NSAIDs, opioids)
    • Increase fluid intake (3-4 L/day)
    • Strain urine for stones
    • Lithotripsy (ESWL) for large stones
    • Ureteroscopy
    • Dietary modifications based on stone type

Renal Failure

Acute Kidney Injury (AKI)

  • Definition: Sudden decline in kidney function
  • Causes:
    • Prerenal: Hypovolemia, hypotension, heart failure
    • Intrarenal: ATN, glomerulonephritis, nephrotoxic drugs
    • Postrenal: Obstruction (stones, tumors, BPH)
  • Phases:
    • Initiation: Injury occurs
    • Oliguric: Urine output <400 mL/day
    • Diuretic: Gradual increase in urine output
    • Recovery: Return to normal function
  • Management:
    • Treat underlying cause
    • Fluid management (may need restriction)
    • Monitor electrolytes (hyperkalemia risk)
    • Dialysis if severe
    • Avoid nephrotoxic drugs

Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)

  • Definition: Progressive, irreversible loss of kidney function
  • Stages: Based on GFR (Stage 1-5, ESRD at Stage 5)
  • Causes: Diabetes, hypertension, glomerulonephritis, PKD
  • Signs & Symptoms:
    • Fatigue, weakness
    • Anemia
    • Hypertension
    • Edema
    • Uremia (nausea, pruritus, confusion)
    • Bone disease
  • Management:
    • Control BP and blood sugar
    • Low-protein, low-sodium, low-potassium diet
    • Phosphate binders
    • Erythropoietin for anemia
    • Dialysis (when GFR <15)
    • Kidney transplant

Dialysis

Hemodialysis

  • Principle: Blood filtered through artificial kidney (dialyzer)
  • Access: AV fistula (preferred), AV graft, central venous catheter
  • Frequency: 3-4 times per week, 3-4 hours per session
  • Nursing Care:
    • Monitor vital signs during treatment
    • Assess access site (thrill, bruit)
    • No BP or venipuncture on access arm
    • Watch for complications (hypotension, cramps, bleeding)
    • Strict fluid restriction between treatments

Peritoneal Dialysis

  • Principle: Peritoneum acts as dialyzing membrane
  • Types: CAPD (continuous), CCPD (cycler)
  • Advantages: Home-based, more dietary freedom, steady state
  • Nursing Care:
    • Strict aseptic technique
    • Monitor for peritonitis (cloudy effluent, abdominal pain, fever)
    • Assess catheter exit site
    • Monitor fluid balance

Key Points

  • Kidneys regulate fluid, electrolytes, and acid-base balance
  • UTIs more common in women; prevention through hygiene and hydration
  • Kidney stones cause severe colicky pain; hydration is key
  • AKI is potentially reversible; CKD is progressive
  • Diabetes and hypertension are leading causes of CKD
  • Dialysis replaces kidney function but not all functions
  • AV fistula care: no BP, no venipuncture on access arm
