Chapter Overview

Comprehensive study of perioperative nursing care across all phases of the surgical experience, from preoperative preparation through postoperative recovery.

Perioperative Nursing Overview

Definition

  • Perioperative Nursing: Nursing care provided before, during, and after surgery
  • Perioperative Nurse: Registered nurse who specializes in surgical patient care
  • Three Phases:
    • Preoperative: From decision for surgery to transfer to OR
    • Intraoperative: From entry to OR to transfer to recovery
    • Postoperative: From admission to recovery to discharge

Scope of Perioperative Nursing

  • Patient assessment and preparation
  • Patient advocacy and safety
  • Infection prevention and aseptic technique
  • Instrumentation and equipment management
  • Coordination of multidisciplinary team
  • Patient and family education

Classification of Surgical Procedures

  • By Urgency:
    • Emergency: Immediate surgery required (e.g., ruptured appendix, trauma)
    • Urgent: Surgery within 24-48 hours (e.g., acute gallbladder infection)
    • Elective: Scheduled surgery, no time pressure (e.g., joint replacement)
  • By Purpose:
    • Diagnostic: Biopsy, exploratory laparotomy
    • Curative: Tumor excision, appendectomy
    • Palliative: Pain relief, symptom management
    • Reconstructive: Plastic surgery, joint replacement
    • Cosmetic: Aesthetic improvement
  • By Extent:
    • Major: Extensive surgery, significant risk (e.g., cardiac surgery)
    • Minor: Minimal risk, often outpatient (e.g., cataract removal)

Surgical Risk Factors

  • Age: Very young and elderly at higher risk
  • Nutritional Status: Malnutrition delays healing
  • Obesity: Increased anesthesia risk, wound complications
  • Chronic Diseases: Diabetes, cardiac disease, respiratory disease
  • Medications: Anticoagulants, steroids, immunosuppressants
  • Smoking: Impaired wound healing, respiratory complications
  • Alcohol/Drug Use: Anesthesia interactions, withdrawal risk

Preoperative Care

Preoperative Assessment

  • Health History:
    • Medical and surgical history
    • Current medications and allergies
    • Previous anesthesia experiences
    • Tobacco, alcohol, drug use
  • Physical Examination:
    • Vital signs baseline
    • Cardiovascular and respiratory assessment
    • Nutritional status
    • Skin integrity
  • Psychosocial Assessment:
    • Anxiety level
    • Understanding of procedure
    • Coping mechanisms
    • Support systems

Preoperative Diagnostic Tests

  • Laboratory: CBC, electrolytes, coagulation studies, blood type and crossmatch
  • Imaging: Chest X-ray, ECG (for cardiac patients or age >40)
  • Specialized: Pulmonary function tests, cardiac stress test as indicated

Informed Consent

  • Requirements:
    • Explanation of procedure, risks, benefits, alternatives
    • Patient must be competent and consent voluntarily
    • Obtained by surgeon before sedation
    • Nurse witnesses signature
  • Special Considerations:
    • Minors: Parent/guardian consent required
    • Emergency: Implied consent if patient unable
    • Language barriers: Interpreter required

Patient Education

  • Preoperative Instructions:
    • NPO status (nothing by mouth) - typically 8 hours before surgery
    • Medication management (which to take/hold)
    • Bowel preparation if indicated
    • Shower with antimicrobial soap
  • What to Expect:
    • Preoperative holding area
    • Anesthesia process
    • Recovery room experience
    • Pain management options
  • Postoperative Teaching:
    • Deep breathing and coughing exercises
    • Incentive spirometry use
    • Leg exercises to prevent DVT
    • Early ambulation importance

Immediate Preoperative Preparation

  • Day of Surgery:
    • Verify NPO status
    • Vital signs assessment
    • Remove jewelry, dentures, contact lenses, nail polish
    • Provide hospital gown
    • Verify surgical site marking
    • Administer preoperative medications as ordered
  • Preoperative Medications:
    • Anxiolytics (e.g., midazolam)
    • Antibiotics (prophylactic)
    • Antiemetics
    • Continuation of essential medications
  • Documentation:
    • Preoperative checklist completion
    • Consent verification
    • Allergies clearly marked
    • Baseline vital signs

Surgical Site Preparation

  • Hair Removal: Only if necessary, use clippers (not razors) to prevent skin injury
  • Skin Antisepsis: Chlorhexidine or povidone-iodine preparation
  • Site Marking: Surgeon marks operative site to prevent wrong-site surgery

Intraoperative Care

Operating Room Environment

  • Physical Environment:
    • Positive pressure ventilation with HEPA filters
    • Temperature 20-24°C (68-75°F)
    • Humidity 30-60%
    • Restricted access to minimize traffic
  • Zones:
    • Unrestricted: Street clothes allowed
    • Semi-restricted: Surgical attire required
    • Restricted: Sterile attire and masks required

Surgical Team Roles

  • Sterile Team (Scrubbed):
    • Surgeon: Performs procedure
    • Surgical Assistant: Assists surgeon
    • Scrub Nurse/Technician: Manages sterile instruments and supplies
  • Non-Sterile Team:
    • Circulating Nurse: Coordinates care, manages environment, documents
    • Anesthesiologist/CRNA: Manages anesthesia and patient monitoring

Aseptic Technique and Sterile Field

  • Principles of Asepsis:
    • Sterile items touch only sterile items
    • Sterile field is at or above waist level
    • Edges of sterile field are considered contaminated
    • Moisture carries microorganisms (strike-through)
    • Movement around sterile field must not contaminate it
  • Surgical Hand Scrub:
    • Remove jewelry
    • Scrub hands and forearms for 3-5 minutes
    • Use antimicrobial soap or alcohol-based rub
    • Keep hands above elbows after scrubbing
  • Gowning and Gloving:
    • Don sterile gown with assistance
    • Closed gloving technique
    • Double gloving for high-risk procedures

Patient Positioning

  • Common Positions:
    • Supine: Most common, back surgeries
    • Prone: Spinal, posterior procedures
    • Lateral: Thoracic, renal surgeries
    • Lithotomy: Gynecologic, rectal procedures
    • Trendelenburg: Pelvic, lower abdominal surgeries
  • Safety Considerations:
    • Maintain body alignment
    • Pad pressure points
    • Prevent nerve compression
    • Secure patient to prevent falls
    • Maintain dignity and privacy

Anesthesia Types

  • General Anesthesia:
    • Complete loss of consciousness
    • Requires airway management (intubation)
    • Used for major surgeries
    • Stages: Induction, maintenance, emergence
  • Regional Anesthesia:
    • Spinal: Injection into subarachnoid space
    • Epidural: Injection into epidural space
    • Nerve blocks: Specific nerve or plexus
    • Patient remains conscious
  • Local Anesthesia:
    • Infiltration of surgical site
    • Used for minor procedures
    • Patient fully awake
  • Monitored Anesthesia Care (MAC):
    • Sedation with local anesthesia
    • "Twilight sleep"
    • Patient responsive but relaxed

Intraoperative Monitoring

  • Vital Signs: Continuous monitoring of HR, BP, RR, SpO2, temperature
  • Cardiac: ECG monitoring for arrhythmias
  • Respiratory: End-tidal CO2, oxygen saturation
  • Fluid Balance: IV fluids, blood loss, urine output
  • Neuromuscular: Depth of anesthesia, muscle relaxation

Surgical Count

  • Purpose: Prevent retained surgical items
  • Items Counted: Sponges, needles, instruments
  • Timing: Before incision, during closure, at end of procedure
  • Documentation: Circulating and scrub nurse verify counts

Specimen Handling

  • Label immediately with patient information
  • Place in appropriate container with preservative
  • Document in operative record
  • Send to pathology promptly

Postoperative Care

Post-Anesthesia Care Unit (PACU)

  • Purpose: Close monitoring during emergence from anesthesia
  • Duration: Typically 1-2 hours until stable
  • Nurse-to-Patient Ratio: 1:1 or 1:2 depending on acuity

Immediate Postoperative Assessment

  • Airway and Breathing:
    • Patent airway, adequate ventilation
    • Oxygen saturation >95%
    • Breath sounds bilateral and clear
    • Position for optimal breathing (semi-Fowler's)
  • Circulation:
    • Vital signs every 15 minutes initially
    • Cardiac rhythm monitoring
    • Peripheral pulses and capillary refill
    • Signs of bleeding or shock
  • Neurological:
    • Level of consciousness
    • Orientation to person, place, time
    • Pupil response
    • Movement of extremities
  • Surgical Site:
    • Dressing dry and intact
    • Drainage amount and character
    • No signs of excessive bleeding
  • Fluid Balance:
    • IV patency and infusion rate
    • Urine output (minimum 0.5 mL/kg/hr)
    • Drain output
  • Comfort:
    • Pain assessment (0-10 scale)
    • Nausea/vomiting
    • Temperature regulation

Common Postoperative Complications

  • Respiratory:
    • Airway obstruction: Position, suction, airway adjunct
    • Hypoventilation: Encourage deep breathing, oxygen
    • Aspiration: NPO until gag reflex returns, position on side
    • Atelectasis/Pneumonia: Incentive spirometry, early ambulation
  • Cardiovascular:
    • Hypotension: Fluid resuscitation, vasopressors
    • Hypertension: Pain control, antihypertensives
    • Arrhythmias: Monitor, treat underlying cause
    • Hemorrhage: Pressure, fluid replacement, return to OR if severe
  • Shock:
    • Hypovolemic: Most common, from blood/fluid loss
    • Signs: Hypotension, tachycardia, decreased urine output, cool skin
    • Treatment: Fluid resuscitation, blood products, identify source
  • Hypothermia:
    • Common after prolonged surgery
    • Warm blankets, forced-air warming devices
    • Warmed IV fluids
  • Nausea and Vomiting:
    • Common with general anesthesia
    • Antiemetics (ondansetron, metoclopramide)
    • Position on side to prevent aspiration
  • Pain:
    • Assess regularly using pain scale
    • Multimodal analgesia (opioids, NSAIDs, local anesthetics)
    • Patient-controlled analgesia (PCA) if appropriate

Discharge from PACU

  • Discharge Criteria (Aldrete Score):
    • Activity: Able to move extremities
    • Respiration: Breathing deeply, coughing
    • Circulation: BP within 20% of baseline
    • Consciousness: Awake and oriented
    • Oxygen saturation: >92% on room air
    • Score ≥9/10 required for discharge
  • Report to Floor Nurse:
    • Procedure performed
    • Anesthesia type
    • Vital signs and trends
    • Pain management
    • IV fluids and medications
    • Drains, catheters, dressings
    • Any complications

Ongoing Postoperative Care (Surgical Unit)

  • Respiratory Care:
    • Deep breathing and coughing every 2 hours
    • Incentive spirometry 10 times per hour while awake
    • Early ambulation
    • Position changes
  • Cardiovascular Care:
    • Sequential compression devices
    • Anticoagulation prophylaxis as ordered
    • Leg exercises
    • Early mobilization
  • Wound Care:
    • Assess dressing for drainage
    • Monitor for signs of infection (redness, warmth, purulent drainage)
    • Change dressings per protocol
    • Document wound appearance
  • Pain Management:
    • Regular pain assessment
    • Administer analgesics as ordered
    • Non-pharmacological methods (positioning, ice, relaxation)
    • Evaluate effectiveness
  • Nutrition and Elimination:
    • NPO until bowel sounds return
    • Progress diet as tolerated
    • Monitor for urinary retention
    • Assess bowel function
  • Activity:
    • Early ambulation (within 24 hours if possible)
    • Gradual increase in activity
    • Assist with ADLs as needed
    • Fall prevention measures

Discharge Planning

  • Patient Education:
    • Wound care instructions
    • Medication management
    • Activity restrictions
    • Signs of complications to report
    • Follow-up appointments
  • Discharge Criteria:
    • Stable vital signs
    • Pain controlled with oral medications
    • Able to tolerate oral intake
    • Voiding adequately
    • Ambulating safely
    • Understanding of discharge instructions

Key Points

  • Perioperative nursing encompasses preoperative, intraoperative, and postoperative phases
  • Informed consent must be obtained before surgery by the surgeon
  • NPO status is typically 8 hours before surgery to prevent aspiration
  • Aseptic technique is critical to prevent surgical site infections
  • Surgical counts prevent retained foreign objects
  • PACU monitoring focuses on airway, breathing, circulation, and consciousness
  • Early ambulation prevents postoperative complications
  • Deep breathing exercises and incentive spirometry prevent respiratory complications
  • Pain management improves outcomes and patient satisfaction
  • Discharge teaching is essential for safe recovery at home
