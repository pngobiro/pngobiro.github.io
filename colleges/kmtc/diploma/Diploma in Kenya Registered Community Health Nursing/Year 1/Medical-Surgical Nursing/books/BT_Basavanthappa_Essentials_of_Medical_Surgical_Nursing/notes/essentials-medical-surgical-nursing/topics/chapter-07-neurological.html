Chapter Overview

Study of neurological assessment, stroke, seizures, head and spinal cord injuries, and degenerative neurological diseases.

Neurological Assessment

Components

  • Level of Consciousness: Glasgow Coma Scale (3-15)
  • Cranial Nerves: Assessment of all 12 cranial nerves
  • Motor Function: Strength, tone, coordination
  • Sensory Function: Touch, pain, temperature, proprioception
  • Reflexes: Deep tendon reflexes, Babinski sign
  • Pupils: Size, equality, reaction to light (PERRLA)
  • Vital Signs: Cushing's triad (increased ICP)

Stroke (Cerebrovascular Accident)

Types

  • Ischemic (87%): Thrombotic or embolic occlusion
  • Hemorrhagic (13%): Intracerebral or subarachnoid bleeding
  • TIA: Transient ischemic attack - warning sign

Risk Factors

  • Hypertension (most important)
  • Atrial fibrillation
  • Diabetes, smoking
  • High cholesterol
  • Previous stroke/TIA

Signs & Symptoms (FAST)

  • Face drooping
  • Arm weakness
  • Speech difficulty
  • Time to call emergency
  • Other: Sudden severe headache, vision changes, confusion, ataxia

Management

  • Acute (Ischemic):
    • tPA (tissue plasminogen activator) within 4.5 hours
    • Mechanical thrombectomy
    • Antiplatelet therapy (aspirin)
    • BP management (permissive hypertension initially)
  • Hemorrhagic:
    • Control BP
    • Reverse anticoagulation
    • Surgery if indicated
  • Nursing Care:
    • Frequent neuro assessments
    • Maintain airway
    • Position head of bed 30 degrees
    • Prevent complications (aspiration, DVT, pressure ulcers)
    • Rehabilitation: PT, OT, speech therapy

Seizures and Epilepsy

Types of Seizures

  • Generalized:
    • Tonic-clonic (grand mal): Loss of consciousness, convulsions
    • Absence (petit mal): Brief loss of awareness
  • Focal (Partial): Localized to one area of brain
  • Status Epilepticus: Continuous seizures >5 min - medical emergency

Causes

  • Epilepsy (idiopathic)
  • Head trauma
  • Brain tumor
  • Stroke
  • Infections (meningitis)
  • Metabolic disorders
  • Drug withdrawal

Management

  • During Seizure:
    • Protect from injury (remove hazards)
    • Turn to side (prevent aspiration)
    • Do NOT restrain or put anything in mouth
    • Time the seizure
    • Observe and document
  • Medications: Phenytoin, carbamazepine, valproic acid, levetiracetam
  • Patient Education:
    • Medication compliance
    • Avoid triggers (stress, sleep deprivation, alcohol)
    • Safety precautions
    • Wear medical alert bracelet

Head and Spinal Cord Injuries

Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI)

  • Types:
    • Concussion: Mild TBI, temporary dysfunction
    • Contusion: Bruising of brain tissue
    • Hematomas: Epidural, subdural, intracerebral
  • Signs of Increased ICP:
    • Decreased LOC
    • Headache, vomiting
    • Pupil changes
    • Cushing's triad: Hypertension, bradycardia, irregular respirations
  • Management:
    • Maintain cerebral perfusion
    • Prevent increased ICP (elevate HOB 30°, avoid Valsalva)
    • Osmotic diuretics (mannitol)
    • Surgery if indicated

Spinal Cord Injury (SCI)

  • Levels:
    • Cervical: Quadriplegia/tetraplegia
    • Thoracic/Lumbar: Paraplegia
  • Spinal Shock: Temporary loss of reflexes below injury
  • Autonomic Dysreflexia: Life-threatening emergency (T6 and above)
    • Severe hypertension, headache, bradycardia
    • Triggered by full bladder/bowel, tight clothing
    • Treatment: Sit upright, remove stimulus, antihypertensives
  • Nursing Care:
    • Immobilization initially
    • Respiratory support (high cervical injuries)
    • Prevent complications (DVT, pressure ulcers, UTI)
    • Bowel and bladder management
    • Rehabilitation

Degenerative Diseases

Parkinson's Disease

  • Pathophysiology: Dopamine deficiency in basal ganglia
  • Signs: Tremor, rigidity, bradykinesia, postural instability
  • Management: Levodopa/carbidopa, dopamine agonists, PT

Multiple Sclerosis (MS)

  • Pathophysiology: Demyelination of CNS
  • Signs: Visual disturbances, weakness, numbness, fatigue
  • Management: Disease-modifying drugs, symptom management

Alzheimer's Disease

  • Progressive dementia with memory loss, confusion, behavioral changes
  • Management: Cholinesterase inhibitors, supportive care, safety

Key Points

  • Neurological assessment includes LOC, pupils, motor/sensory function
  • Stroke is time-sensitive emergency - FAST assessment
  • Never put anything in mouth during seizure
  • Increased ICP signs: Cushing's triad, decreased LOC
  • Autonomic dysreflexia is life-threatening in SCI patients
  • Rehabilitation is crucial for neurological recovery
