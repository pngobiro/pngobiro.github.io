Chapter Overview

This chapter covers the nursing management of common signs and symptoms encountered in medical-surgical nursing practice, including fluid and electrolyte imbalances, fever, unconsciousness, and pain.

Management of Fluid and Electrolyte Balance

Importance of Fluid Balance

  • Fluid is the most vital and abundant component of the human body
  • Life depends on constant source and regulation of body fluids
  • Fluid deprivation causes death more quickly than food deprivation
  • Electrolyte balance is closely related to fluid balance

Body Fluid Distribution

  • Total Body Water: Approximately 60% of body weight in adults
    • Intracellular Fluid (ICF): 40% of body weight (inside cells)
    • Extracellular Fluid (ECF): 20% of body weight
      • Interstitial fluid: 15% (surrounds cells)
      • Intravascular fluid: 5% (within blood vessels)

Normal Fluid Intake (24 hours)

  • Water intake: 1300 cc
  • Water from solid food: 1000 cc
  • Metabolic water (oxidation): 300 cc
  • Total: 2600 cc per day

Normal Fluid Output (24 hours)

  • Urine: 1500 cc
  • Insensible loss (lungs, skin): 900 cc
  • Feces: 200 cc
  • Total: 2600 cc per day

Major Electrolytes

  • Cations (Positive ions):
    • Sodium (Na+) - major extracellular cation
    • Potassium (K+) - major intracellular cation
    • Calcium (Ca2+)
    • Magnesium (Mg2+)
  • Anions (Negative ions):
    • Chloride (Cl-)
    • Bicarbonate (HCO3-)
    • Phosphate (PO4 3-)
    • Sulfate (SO4 2-)

Common Fluid Imbalances

  • Fluid Volume Deficit (Dehydration):
    • Causes: Vomiting, diarrhea, excessive sweating, inadequate intake
    • Signs: Dry mucous membranes, poor skin turgor, decreased urine output, tachycardia
    • Management: Oral or IV fluid replacement
  • Fluid Volume Excess (Overhydration):
    • Causes: Excessive IV fluids, heart failure, renal failure
    • Signs: Edema, weight gain, dyspnea, crackles in lungs
    • Management: Fluid restriction, diuretics

Common Electrolyte Imbalances

  • Hyponatremia (Low Sodium): Confusion, seizures, muscle weakness
  • Hypernatremia (High Sodium): Thirst, restlessness, seizures
  • Hypokalemia (Low Potassium): Muscle weakness, cardiac arrhythmias, constipation
  • Hyperkalemia (High Potassium): Cardiac arrhythmias, muscle weakness, paresthesia
  • Hypocalcemia (Low Calcium): Tetany, muscle cramps, seizures
  • Hypercalcemia (High Calcium): Confusion, constipation, kidney stones

Nursing Assessment

  • Daily weight monitoring
  • Intake and output recording
  • Vital signs assessment
  • Skin turgor and mucous membrane assessment
  • Laboratory values monitoring (electrolytes, BUN, creatinine)
  • Mental status evaluation

Nursing Interventions

  • Maintain accurate I&O records
  • Administer IV fluids as prescribed
  • Monitor for signs of fluid overload or deficit
  • Encourage appropriate oral fluid intake
  • Administer electrolyte replacements as ordered
  • Educate patient about fluid and dietary restrictions

Management of Fever

Definition

  • Elevation of body temperature above normal (>37°C or 98.6°F oral)
  • Pyrexia: Body temperature above 38°C (100.4°F)
  • Hyperpyrexia: Body temperature above 41°C (105.8°F)

Causes of Fever

  • Infections (bacterial, viral, fungal, parasitic)
  • Inflammatory conditions
  • Malignancies
  • Tissue injury or necrosis
  • Drug reactions
  • Dehydration
  • Heat stroke

Stages of Fever

  1. Onset (Chill Phase):
    • Increased heat production
    • Vasoconstriction
    • Shivering, chills
    • Pale, cold skin
  2. Course (Plateau Phase):
    • Temperature reaches peak
    • Flushed, warm skin
    • Increased pulse and respiration
    • Malaise, weakness
  3. Defervescence (Flush Phase):
    • Temperature returns to normal
    • Vasodilation
    • Profuse sweating
    • Warm, moist skin

Nursing Assessment

  • Monitor temperature regularly (every 4 hours or as ordered)
  • Assess for associated symptoms (chills, sweating, headache)
  • Monitor vital signs
  • Assess hydration status
  • Identify potential source of infection

Nursing Interventions

  • Antipyretic Measures:
    • Administer antipyretics as prescribed (paracetamol, ibuprofen)
    • Monitor response to medication
  • Physical Cooling:
    • Tepid sponging (avoid cold water)
    • Remove excess clothing and blankets
    • Use cooling blankets for hyperpyrexia
    • Maintain room temperature at comfortable level
  • Supportive Care:
    • Encourage increased fluid intake
    • Provide light, easily digestible diet
    • Ensure adequate rest
    • Change damp clothing and linens
    • Provide oral hygiene
  • Monitoring:
    • Record temperature trends
    • Watch for complications (dehydration, seizures)
    • Monitor for signs of infection

Management of Unconscious Patient

Definition

  • State of unawareness of self and environment
  • Inability to respond to sensory stimuli
  • Ranges from confusion to deep coma

Levels of Consciousness

  • Alert: Fully awake, oriented to person, place, time
  • Lethargic: Drowsy but arousable
  • Obtunded: Difficult to arouse, limited response
  • Stuporous: Responds only to vigorous stimulation
  • Comatose: No response to any stimuli

Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS)

  • Eye Opening (1-4):
    • 4 = Spontaneous
    • 3 = To speech
    • 2 = To pain
    • 1 = None
  • Verbal Response (1-5):
    • 5 = Oriented
    • 4 = Confused
    • 3 = Inappropriate words
    • 2 = Incomprehensible sounds
    • 1 = None
  • Motor Response (1-6):
    • 6 = Obeys commands
    • 5 = Localizes pain
    • 4 = Withdraws from pain
    • 3 = Flexion to pain
    • 2 = Extension to pain
    • 1 = None
  • Total Score: 3-15 (3 = deep coma, 15 = fully alert)

Common Causes

  • Head injury
  • Stroke
  • Metabolic disorders (hypoglycemia, uremia)
  • Drug overdose or poisoning
  • Infections (meningitis, encephalitis)
  • Hypoxia
  • Seizures

Nursing Assessment

  • Level of consciousness (GCS)
  • Pupil size, equality, and reaction to light
  • Vital signs (especially respiratory pattern)
  • Motor and sensory function
  • Reflexes
  • Signs of increased intracranial pressure

Nursing Interventions

  • Airway Management:
    • Maintain patent airway (priority)
    • Position patient on side to prevent aspiration
    • Suction as needed
    • Administer oxygen as prescribed
  • Neurological Monitoring:
    • Assess GCS regularly
    • Monitor pupillary responses
    • Check vital signs frequently
    • Report any deterioration immediately
  • Skin Care:
    • Turn patient every 2 hours
    • Use pressure-relieving devices
    • Keep skin clean and dry
    • Inspect for pressure ulcers
  • Eye Care:
    • Clean eyes with normal saline
    • Apply eye lubricants if corneal reflex absent
    • Protect eyes from injury
  • Oral Care:
    • Provide mouth care every 2-4 hours
    • Keep lips moist
    • Prevent aspiration during oral care
  • Nutrition and Hydration:
    • Maintain IV fluids as prescribed
    • Insert nasogastric tube for feeding if needed
    • Monitor intake and output
  • Elimination:
    • Insert urinary catheter if necessary
    • Monitor bowel function
    • Provide perineal care
  • Communication:
    • Talk to patient (assume they can hear)
    • Explain all procedures
    • Provide sensory stimulation
    • Support family members

Management of Pain

Definition

  • "Whatever the person says it is, existing whenever the person says it does" (McCaffery)
  • Unpleasant sensory and emotional experience
  • Subjective and personal

Types of Pain

  • By Duration:
    • Acute pain: Sudden onset, short duration, identifiable cause
    • Chronic pain: Persists beyond expected healing time (>3-6 months)
  • By Source:
    • Nociceptive pain: From tissue damage (somatic or visceral)
    • Neuropathic pain: From nerve damage
    • Psychogenic pain: Psychological origin
  • By Location:
    • Localized: Confined to site of origin
    • Radiating: Extends from source
    • Referred: Felt in area distant from source
    • Phantom: Felt in amputated body part

Pain Assessment

  • PQRST Method:
    • P - Precipitating/Palliative factors
    • Q - Quality (sharp, dull, burning, aching)
    • R - Region/Radiation
    • S - Severity (pain scale 0-10)
    • T - Timing (onset, duration, frequency)
  • Pain Scales:
    • Numeric Rating Scale (0-10)
    • Visual Analog Scale
    • Wong-Baker FACES Scale (for children)
    • FLACC Scale (for non-verbal patients)
  • Behavioral Indicators:
    • Facial expressions (grimacing)
    • Body movements (restlessness, guarding)
    • Vocalization (moaning, crying)
    • Physiological signs (increased HR, BP, sweating)

Pharmacological Management

  • WHO Analgesic Ladder:
    • Step 1: Non-opioids (paracetamol, NSAIDs)
    • Step 2: Weak opioids (codeine, tramadol)
    • Step 3: Strong opioids (morphine, fentanyl)
  • Adjuvant Medications:
    • Antidepressants (for neuropathic pain)
    • Anticonvulsants (for nerve pain)
    • Muscle relaxants
    • Topical agents

Non-Pharmacological Management

  • Physical Methods:
    • Heat/cold application
    • Massage
    • TENS (Transcutaneous Electrical Nerve Stimulation)
    • Positioning and immobilization
  • Cognitive-Behavioral Methods:
    • Distraction
    • Relaxation techniques
    • Guided imagery
    • Music therapy
    • Deep breathing exercises

Nursing Interventions

  • Assess pain regularly (5th vital sign)
  • Believe patient's report of pain
  • Administer analgesics as prescribed
  • Evaluate effectiveness of pain relief measures
  • Use combination of pharmacological and non-pharmacological methods
  • Teach patient about pain management strategies
  • Document pain assessment and interventions
  • Advocate for adequate pain control

Key Points to Remember

  • Fluid balance is essential for life; normal intake equals normal output (2600 cc/day)
  • Electrolytes are critical for cellular function and must be maintained within normal ranges
  • Fever is a protective response but requires management when excessive
  • Unconscious patients require comprehensive care focusing on airway, safety, and prevention of complications
  • Glasgow Coma Scale is the standard tool for assessing level of consciousness
  • Pain is subjective and should be assessed using patient self-report when possible
  • Effective pain management often requires combination of pharmacological and non-pharmacological approaches
  • Regular assessment and documentation are essential for all symptom management
