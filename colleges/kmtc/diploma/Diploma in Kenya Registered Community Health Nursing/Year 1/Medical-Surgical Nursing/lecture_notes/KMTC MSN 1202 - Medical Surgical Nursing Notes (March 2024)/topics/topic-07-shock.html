üìñ Definition and Types

Definition

Shock is a medical emergency characterized by a sudden drop in blood flow throughout the body. It results in inadequate oxygen delivery to tissues and organs, leading to cellular dysfunction and potentially life-threatening complications.

Types of Shock

1. Hypovolemic Shock

Caused by significant fluid loss (e.g., bleeding, dehydration).

2. Cardiogenic Shock

Occurs when the heart cannot pump effectively (e.g., heart attack, severe heart failure).

3. Distributive Shock

Results from widespread vasodilation (e.g., septic, anaphylactic, neurogenic).

4. Obstructive Shock

Due to obstruction of blood flow (e.g., pulmonary embolism, cardiac tamponade).

02

üíß Hypovolemic Shock

Hypovolemic shock occurs when the body loses a significant volume of blood or fluid, leading to inadequate blood flow to the organs and tissues.

Causes

  • Hemorrhage: Trauma, surgery-related blood loss, gastrointestinal bleeding.
  • Fluid Loss: Dehydration (severe vomiting/diarrhea), excessive sweating, burns, diuretic use.

Pathophysiology

Decreased Blood Volume ‚Üí Reduced venous return ‚Üí Heart rate increases (tachycardia) ‚Üí Vasoconstriction ‚Üí Activation of RAAS to promote fluid retention.

Symptoms

  • Early: Tachycardia, hypotension, cool/clammy skin, weakness, thirst.
  • Severe: Confusion, rapid/shallow breathing, very low blood pressure, decreased urine output.
üöë

Management

  • Fluid Resuscitation: Immediate IV fluids (crystalloids or blood products).
  • Control of Bleeding: Surgical intervention if necessary.
  • Monitoring: Vital signs and urine output.
  • Medications: Vasopressors if BP remains critically low after fluids.
03

‚ù§Ô∏è Cardiogenic Shock

Occurs when the heart's ability to pump blood effectively is severely compromised.

Causes:

  • Myocardial Infarction (Heart Attack)
  • Severe Heart Failure
  • Cardiomyopathy
  • Arrhythmias
  • Mechanical Problems (e.g., valve dysfunction, cardiac tamponade)

Pathophysiology:

Pump failure ‚Üí Decreased oxygen delivery ‚Üí Tachycardia (compensatory) ‚Üí Peripheral vasoconstriction (SNS activation) ‚Üí Fluid retention (RAAS activation).

Symptoms:

  • Early: Chest pain, tachycardia, dyspnea, fatigue.
  • Severe: Profound hypotension, cold/clammy skin, altered mental state, decreased urine output.
‚ö°

Management

  • Stabilization: Airway, breathing, circulation.
  • Medications: Inotropes (e.g., dobutamine), Vasopressors, Antiplatelets.
  • Mechanical Support: Intra-aortic Balloon Pump (IABP).
  • Revascularization: Angioplasty or CABG for heart attacks.
04

üîÑ Distributive Shock

Results from widespread vasodilation, leading to inadequate blood flow.

‚ñ∂ a) Septic Shock

Severe condition resulting from sepsis (widespread infection leading to systemic inflammation).

  • Causes: Bacterial, fungal, or viral infections.
  • Symptoms: High fever/hypothermia, rapid heart rate/breathing, confusion, warm/flushed skin (early) or cold (late), low BP.
  • Management: IV fluids, Antibiotics, Vasopressors, Supportive care.
‚ñ∂ b) Anaphylactic Shock

Severe, life-threatening allergic reaction occurring rapidly after exposure.

  • Causes: Foods (nuts, shellfish), insect stings, medications, latex.
  • Symptoms: Throat/tongue swelling, breathing difficulty, rapid BP drop, hives, nausea.
  • Management: Immediate epinephrine, antihistamines, corticosteroids, oxygen.
‚ñ∂ c) Neurogenic Shock

Occurs due to loss of sympathetic nervous system tone, leading to widespread vasodilation.

  • Causes: Spinal cord injuries (especially cervical), severe brain injuries.
  • Symptoms: Low BP, warm/dry skin (due to vasodilation), bradycardia (slow heart rate), weakness/paralysis.
  • Management: Spine stabilization, IV fluids, vasopressors.
05

üõë Obstructive Shock

Due to physical obstruction of blood flow to or from the heart.

  • Causes: Pulmonary embolism, tension pneumothorax, cardiac tamponade.
  • Management: Treat the obstruction (e.g., relieve tension pneumothorax, remove embolism).
06

üìâ Stages of Shock

i) Initial Stage

Decreased perfusion triggers anaerobic metabolism, leading to lactic acidosis. Clinical signs often not detectable.

ii) Compensatory Stage

Activation of Sympathetic Nervous System (adrenaline) and RAAS (conserve water/sodium). ADH released.

Signs: Tachycardia, mild hypotension, cool clammy skin, increased respiratory rate.

iii) Progressive Stage

Increased cellular dysfunction and hypoxia. Metabolic acidosis accumulates.

Signs: Severe hypotension, altered mental status, decreased urine output, organ dysfunction.

iv) Refractory Stage

Irreversible cellular damage, multi-organ failure, and death.

Signs: Profound unresponsive hypotension, multi-organ dysfunction, severe acidosis, unresponsiveness.

üè• Management and Complications

General Management of Shock

  1. Initial Assessment: Vital signs, physical exam (perfusion signs), history.
  2. Stabilization (ABCs): Ensure airway, breathing, and circulation. Supplemental oxygen.
  3. Fluid Resuscitation: Crystalloids (e.g., normal saline) or blood products to restore volume.
  4. Pharmacological Support: Inotropes (e.g., dobutamine), Vasopressors (e.g., norepinephrine), Antibiotics (for sepsis).
  5. Treat Underlying Cause: E.g., stop bleeding, treat infection, remove obstruction.
  6. Monitoring: Continuous vital signs, urine output, laboratory tests (electrolytes, lactate).

General Symptoms

  • Altered Mental Status (confusion, lethargy).
  • Skin Changes (cool/clammy/pale; warm/flushed in septic).
  • Vital Signs (tachycardia, hypotension, tachypnea, oliguria).

Diagnosis

  • History & Physical: Identify cause and assess status.
  • Labs: CBC, Electrolytes, Renal function, ABG (acid-base/oxygenation).
  • Imaging: Chest X-ray, Ultrasound, CT scan.

Potential Complications

  • Multi-Organ Failure.
  • Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC).
  • Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome (ARDS).
