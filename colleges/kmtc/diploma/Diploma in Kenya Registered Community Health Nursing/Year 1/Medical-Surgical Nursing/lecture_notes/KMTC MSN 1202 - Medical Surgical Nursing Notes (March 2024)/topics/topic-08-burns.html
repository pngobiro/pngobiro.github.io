üìñ Definition and Types of Burns

A burn is an injury that results from direct exposure to any thermal, electrical, chemical, or radiation source. It occurs when energy from a heat source is transferred into body tissues beyond what the body could hold, leading to tissue injury. It is characterized by severe skin damage that causes the affected skin cells to die.

Types of Burns

  • Thermal burns: Caused by any external heat source like a flame, hot liquids, or hot metals.
  • Chemical burns: Caused by substances such as strong acids, drain cleaners (lye), paint thinner, and gasoline touching the skin.
  • Radiation burns: Least common type. Includes sunburn and exposure to nuclear radiation or radiation therapy.
  • Electrical burns: Occur when the skin comes into contact with an electrical current passing through the body from faulty wiring.
  • Friction burns: Occur when a hard object rubs off some of the skin (e.g., motorcycle accidents, carpet burn). It is both an abrasion and a heat burn.
  • Cold burns (Frostbite): Occur when the skin comes into direct contact with something very cold for a prolonged period.
  • Inhalation injury: Caused by smoke associated with flame injury or inhaled carbon monoxide (a by-product of incomplete combustion).

üå°Ô∏è Signs and Symptoms

1. Local Response (Small Burns <25%)

Redness (Erythema)

Increased blood flow to the affected area as a sign of inflammation.

Swelling (Edema)

Fluid leaking from blood vessels into surrounding tissues.

Pain

Activation of nerve endings; a protective mechanism.

Blistering

Protective fluid layer in second-degree burns.

Other signs include dry/peeling skin (first-degree) or a leathery/waxy texture with loss of sensation (third-degree).

2. Systemic Response (Large Burns >25%)

The release of cytokines and inflammatory mediators has a systemic effect once burns reach ~30% TBSA.

  • Cardiovascular: Fluid loss leads to systemic hypotension, increased heart rate (tachycardia), and end-organ hypoperfusion.
  • Hypothermia: Inability to regulate temperature due to loss of skin function.
  • Respiratory: Hyperventilation, bronchoconstriction, and risk of ARDS.
  • Metabolic: Basal metabolic rate increases up to three times normal (hypermetabolism).
  • Immunological: Down-regulation of immune response.
  • Electrolyte Imbalances: Hyponatremia (sodium depletion) and Hyperkalemia (tissue necrosis) are common initially.

üìè Assessment of Burn Extent

1. Rule of Nines

A quick method to estimate Total Body Surface Area (TBSA) injured in adults by dividing the body into multiples of nine.

  • Head: 9%
  • Each Arm: 9%
  • Anterior Trunk (Chest/Abd): 18%
  • Posterior Trunk (Back): 18%
  • Each Leg: 18%
  • Perineum: 1%

2. Lund and Browder Method

More accurate for children as it accounts for age-related changes in body proportions (especially head and legs).

3. Palmer Method

The patient's palm (excluding fingers) represents approximately 0.5% TBSA, and the palm with fingers represents 1% TBSA.

üöë Emergent Phase Management

Duration: Time of injury to 48-72 hours (until hemodynamic stability).
Goal: Prevent hypovolemic shock and preserve vital organ function.

‚ñ∂ Airway Management
  • Oxygenation: 100% O2 via non-rebreather mask for CO poisoning until levels drop to 15%.
  • Intubation: Indicated for inhalation injuries or respiratory distress.
  • Positioning: Elevate head of bed to facilitate lung expansion.
‚ñ∂ Fluid Resuscitation (Parkland Formula)
$$ \text{Total Fluid (24h)} = 4\text{ml} \times \text{TBSA}(\%) \times \text{Body Weight}(\text{kg}) $$

Administration Schedule:

  • 1st 8 Hours: 50% of total calculated volume.
  • 2nd 8 Hours: 25% of total volume.
  • 3rd 8 Hours: 25% of total volume.

Note: Avoid colloids in first 24h to prevent edema aggravation.

Monitoring: Urine output goal is 0.5 to 1 mL/kg/h (thermal/chemical) or 75-100 mL/h (electrical).

‚ñ∂ Pain & Nutritional Management

Pain: IV analgesics (Morphine, Demerol) preferred. Avoid IM/SubQ due to poor absorption in hypovolemia.

Nutrition: High-calorie, high-protein diet to support hypermetabolism and wound healing.

üè• Acute Phase Management

Duration: From 48-72 hours until wound closure.

Infection Prevention

  • Monitor for signs (erythema, warmth, odor).
  • Initiate universal precautions (gowns, gloves).
  • Wound culture for MRSA.
  • Control hyperglycemia with insulin to improve healing.

Wound Care

Cleansing & Debridement

Hydrotherapy (showering) < 30 mins. Removal of necrotic tissue via mechanical, enzymatic, or surgical methods.

Topical Agents

Silver Sulfadiazine applied 1-2 times daily via open or closed dressing methods.

Surgical Management (Autografting)

Using patient's own skin to close wounds.

ü©π

Post-Op Graft Care

  • Immobilize graft site (bed rest ~10 days).
  • Keep free from pressure and weight-bearing.
  • Monitor for foul discharge (infection).
  • Roll gauze to remove exudate/blood as prescribed.
07

üö∂ Rehabilitation Phase

Begins immediately and extends for years.

  • Minimize Functional Loss: Physical therapy and exercises to prevent contractures.
  • Psychosocial Support: Counseling for body image, PTSD, and social reintegration.
08

‚ö†Ô∏è Complications

Acute Respiratory Failure

Due to inhalation injury, pulmonary edema from fluid resuscitation, pneumonia, or ARDS.

Distributive Shock

Caused by fluid loss, massive vasodilation from inflammation, and sepsis risk.

Acute Renal Failure

Prerenal (hypovolemia) or Intrinsic (myoglobinuria from muscle damage/rhabdomyolysis).

Compartment Syndrome

Increased pressure in circumferential burns compromising circulation. Requires escharotomy.

Paralytic Ileus

Decreased gut motility due to shock, stress response, or opioids. Requires NGT suction.

Curling's Ulcer

Stress-induced gastric ulcer due to ischemia and high acid. Treated with antacids/H2 blockers.
