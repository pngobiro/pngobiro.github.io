üíß Fluid Compartments and Mechanisms

Total Body Water

Total Body Water (TBW) refers to the total volume of water contained in the human body.

It constitutes a significant part of body weight approximately 50-70% of body weight, depending on age, sex, and body composition.

It is generally higher in infants and decreases with age.

It is essential for various physiological functions, including temperature regulation, nutrient transport, and waste removal.

Components of TBW:

a) Intracellular Fluid (ICF):

  • volume: About 60% of total body water, which is approximately two-thirds of the body's water content.
  • Functions: Contains water inside cells, crucial for maintaining cell shape, metabolism, and function.

b) Extracellular Fluid (ECF):

  • Volume: About one-third of the body's total water.
Subdivisions of ECF
  • Interstitial Fluid: Surrounds cells and facilitates nutrient exchange and waste removal.
  • Intravascular Fluid(plasma): Found in blood vessels; plays a role in blood pressure regulation and transportation of cells and nutrients.
  • Transcellular Fluid: Includes specialized fluids such as cerebrospinal fluid, synovial fluid, and pleural fluid

Physiological Importance of TBW

  • Regulation of Body Temperature: Water helps in temperature regulation through sweating and evaporation.
  • Nutrient and Waste Transport: Facilitates the movement of nutrients into cells and the removal of waste products from cells to excretory organs.
  • Maintaining Blood Pressure: Contributes to blood volume and pressure regulation through intravascular fluid.
  • Cell Function: Essential for maintaining cell hydration, shape, and function.
  • Acid-Base Balance: Helps buffer the body's pH through various physiological processes.

Fluid Balance Mechanisms

Fluid balance in the body is crucial for maintaining homeostasis. This balance involves several mechanisms and regulatory systems as described below;

  1. Fluid Loss and Intake
    • Fluid Intake: Maintained through drinking fluids and consuming food. Metabolic processes also generate water (metabolic water).
    • Fluid Loss: Occurs through urine, sweat, respiration, and feces. The body monitors these losses and adjusts fluid intake and retention accordingly.
  2. Kidney Function

    The kidneys play a central role in regulating fluid balance through:

    • Filtration: Blood is filtered in the glomeruli, forming a filtrate that enters the renal tubules.
    • Reabsorption: Essential substances and water are reabsorbed in the proximal tubule, loop of Henle, distal tubule, and collecting ducts, primarily regulated by hormones like ADH and aldosterone.
    • Excretion: Excess fluids and waste products are excreted as urine. The kidneys can adjust urine volume and concentration based on hydration status.
  3. Fluid Distribution
    • Compartments: Body fluids are distributed between intracellular (inside cells) and extracellular (outside cells) compartments. The movement of fluids between these compartments is primarily influenced by osmotic and hydrostatic pressures.
    • Osmosis: Water moves across cell membranes from areas of lower solute concentration (hypotonic) to higher solute concentration (hypertonic), helping to equalize solute concentrations.
  4. Regulatory Hormones

    Several hormones play key roles in fluid balance:

    • Antidiuretic Hormone (ADH): Released from the posterior pituitary gland in response to increased plasma osmolality (concentration of solutes). Promotes water reabsorption in the kidneys, reducing urine output and concentrating urine.
    • Aldosterone: Produced by the adrenal cortex in response to low blood volume or low sodium levels. Increases sodium reabsorption in the kidneys, leading to water retention and increased blood volume.
    • Natriuretic Peptides (ANP and BNP): Released by the heart in response to increased blood pressure or volume. Promote excretion of sodium and water, counteracting the effects of ADH and aldosterone. Atrial Natriuretic Peptide (ANP) is a hormone produced by the atria of the heart. Brain Natriuretic Peptide (BNP) is a hormone produced primarily by the ventricles of the heart.
  5. Thirst Mechanism

    The thirst center in the hypothalamus responds to changes in plasma osmolality and blood volume:

    • Increased Osmolality: Stimulates thirst, prompting fluid intake to dilute solutes.
    • Decreased Blood Volume: Also triggers thirst and can be influenced by baroreceptors that detect changes in blood pressure.
  6. Starling Forces

    Fluid movement between blood vessels and interstitial spaces is influenced by:

    • Hydrostatic Pressure: The pressure exerted by fluid in blood vessels that pushes fluid out into tissues.
    • Osmotic Pressure: The pressure exerted by proteins (especially albumin) in the blood that pulls fluid back into the bloodstream.

üìâ Types of Fluid Imbalances

A. Hypovolemia (Fluid Deficit)

Decrease in the volume of extracellular fluid (ECF).

Causes:
  • Inadequate Intake: Not drinking enough fluids.
  • Excessive Loss: Vomiting, diarrhea, diuretics, excessive sweating.
  • Third-Spacing: Fluid shift to interstitial or third spaces (e.g., ascites).
Symptoms:
  • Thirst: Initial symptom.
  • Dry Mucous Membranes: Mouth, lips, and throat.
  • Decreased Urine Output: Oliguria (low urine output).
  • Hypotension: Low blood pressure.
  • Tachycardia: Increased heart rate.
  • Skin Turgor: Decreased skin elasticity.
  • Confusion or Lethargy: Due to reduced blood flow to the brain.

B. Hypervolemia (Fluid Excess)

Increase in the volume of extracellular fluid.

Causes:
  • Excessive Intake: Overhydration, excessive intravenous fluids.
  • Impaired Fluid Removal: Renal failure, heart failure, liver cirrhosis.
  • Sodium Retention: From conditions like heart failure or chronic kidney disease.
Symptoms:
  • Edema: Swelling of extremities, face, or abdominal cavity.
  • Weight Gain: Rapid increase in weight due to fluid retention.
  • Increased Urine Output: Initial polyuria (high urine output) if kidneys are still functioning well.
  • Shortness of Breath: Due to pulmonary edema.
  • Hypertension: High blood pressure.

Definition: Electrolyte Imbalance refers to abnormal levels of electrolytes (sodium, potassium, calcium, magnesium, chloride, bicarbonate) in the blood. Electrolytes are crucial for many bodily functions including nerve impulse transmission, muscle contraction, and maintaining fluid balance.

Other Important Parameters:

  • Phosphate ($PO_4^{3-}$): Normal Range: 0.8-1.5 mmol/L. Plays a role in energy production, bone health, and cellular function.
  • Osmolality: Normal Range: 275-295 mOsm/kg. Measures solute concentration in blood.
  • Blood Volume: Approx. 5-6 liters for an adult (7-8% of total body weight).
  • Urine Output: Normal Range: 800-2000 mL/day.

üíâ Types of Intravenous Fluids

a) Hypotonic IV Fluids

Examples: 0.45% Sodium Chloride (1/2 NS), 0.33% Sodium Chloride (1/3 NS)

Mode of Action: Lower concentration of solutes than plasma. Fluids move into cells via osmosis, causing cells to swell.

Indications: Treat cellular dehydration (e.g., DKA), provide free water, correct hypernatremia.

b) Isotonic IV Fluids

Examples: 0.9% Sodium Chloride (Normal Saline), Lactated Ringer's, D5W (initially)

Mode of Action: Same osmolarity as plasma. No significant fluid movement in/out of cells. Maintains extracellular balance.

Indications: Maintain hydration, restore fluid volume (blood loss, dehydration), dilute medications.

c) Hypertonic IV Fluids

Examples: 3% Sodium Chloride, 5% Dextrose in 0.9% NS, Dextrose 10% in Water

Mode of Action: Higher concentration of solutes than plasma. Draws water out of cells causing them to shrink.

Indications: Treat hyponatremia, reduce cerebral edema, provide nutrition.

üßÇ Sodium Imbalance

‚ñ∂ Hyponatremia (Low Sodium) < 135 mEq/L

Causes:

  • Excessive Water Intake: Polydipsia, excessive IV fluids.
  • SIADH: Excessive ADH causing water retention.
  • Hypovolemic: Renal losses (diuretics), GI losses (vomiting, diarrhea).
  • Hypervolemic: Heart failure, cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome.
  • Medications: SSRIs, antiepileptics.

Symptoms:

  • Neurological: Headache, confusion, seizures, coma.
  • General: Nausea, vomiting, muscle cramps, fatigue.

Management:

  • Fluid Restriction.
  • Sodium Replacement (Oral or IV Hypertonic Saline for severe cases).
  • Address Underlying Causes.
‚ñ∂ Hypernatremia (High Sodium) > 145 mEq/L

Causes:

  • Inadequate Water Intake: Dehydration, unconscious patients.
  • Excessive Sodium Intake: Diet, medications.
  • Loss of Free Water: Diabetes Insipidus, hyperglycemia, severe burns, GI losses.
  • Endocrine: Hyperaldosteronism, Cushing‚Äôs Syndrome.

Symptoms:

  • Neurological: Thirst, confusion, agitation, seizures, coma.
  • General: Dry mouth, decreased skin turgor, fatigue.
  • Severe: Elevated heart rate, hypotension, hyperthermia.

Treatment:

  • Fluid Replacement: Oral fluids or IV (Isotonic/Hypotonic).
  • Gradual Correction: To avoid cerebral edema.
  • Treat underlying causes (e.g., Diabetes Insipidus).

üçå Potassium Imbalance

‚ñ∂ Hypokalemia (Low Potassium) < 3.5 mEq/L

Causes:

  • Inadequate intake.
  • Losses: GI (vomiting, diarrhea), Renal (Hyperaldosteronism, Diuretics).
  • Transcellular Shift: Insulin administration, Alkalosis.
  • Hypomagnesemia.

Symptoms:

  • Neuromuscular: Weakness, cramps, paralysis.
  • Cardiac: Arrhythmias, ECG changes (flattened T-waves, U-waves).
  • General: Fatigue, constipation.

Management:

  • Potassium Replacement (Oral or IV).
  • Treat GI losses and adjust medications.
  • Monitor ECG.
‚ñ∂ Hyperkalemia (High Potassium) > 5.0 mEq/L

Causes:

  • Increased intake (dietary, IV).
  • Decreased Excretion: Renal failure, Potassium-sparing diuretics, ACE inhibitors, Adrenal insufficiency.
  • Cellular Release: Hemolysis, Tumor Lysis Syndrome, Rhabdomyolysis, Acidosis.

Symptoms:

  • Neuromuscular: Weakness, cramps, paralysis.
  • Cardiac: Arrhythmias, ECG changes (peaked T-waves, widened QRS).
  • Severe: Respiratory distress, cardiac arrest.

Treatment:

  • Potassium Lowering: Calcium Gluconate (stabilize heart), Insulin + Glucose, Sodium Bicarbonate, Beta-agonists.
  • Excretion: Diuretics, Dialysis, Potassium binders.
  • Dietary restriction.

ü¶¥ Calcium Imbalance

‚ñ∂ Hypocalcemia (Low Calcium) < 8.5 mg/dL

Causes:

  • Inadequate intake or Vitamin D deficiency.
  • Losses: Renal failure, Hyperphosphatemia.
  • Hormonal: Hypoparathyroidism (e.g., post-thyroid surgery).
  • Acute Pancreatitis, Blood transfusions (citrate).

Symptoms:

  • Neuromuscular: Tingling, numbness (paresthesia), muscle cramps, Tetany, Laryngospasm.
  • Cardiac: Arrhythmias, prolonged QT interval.
  • Severe: Seizures.

Treatment:

  • Calcium Replacement (Oral or IV Calcium Gluconate).
  • Vitamin D supplementation.
  • Treat underlying causes (e.g., kidney disease).
‚ñ∂ Hypercalcemia (High Calcium) > 10.5 mg/dL

Causes:

  • Primary Hyperparathyroidism (excess PTH).
  • Malignancy: Metastatic cancer causing bone resorption.
  • Vitamin D excess, Thiazide diuretics, Immobilization.

Symptoms:

  • Neuromuscular: Weakness, fatigue, confusion, coma.
  • Renal: Polyuria, kidney stones.
  • GI: Nausea, vomiting, constipation.
  • Cardiac: Arrhythmias, shortened QT interval.

Treatment:

  • Hydration (IV fluids).
  • Loop Diuretics (promote excretion).
  • Bisphosphonates, Calcitonin.
  • Treat malignancy or hyperparathyroidism.

üß™ Magnesium, Chloride & Bicarbonate

Magnesium Imbalance

Hypomagnesemia: Caused by GI losses, alcoholism, diuretics. Symptoms: Tetany, tremors, arrhythmias (Torsades de pointes). Treated with Mg replacement.

Hypermagnesemia: Caused by renal failure, excessive intake (antacids/laxatives). Symptoms: Muscle weakness, bradycardia, hypotension, respiratory depression. Treated by stopping Mg, IV fluids, diuretics, Calcium gluconate.

Chloride Imbalance

Hypochloremia: Low chloride. Caused by vomiting, diuretics, metabolic alkalosis. Symptoms: Muscle twitching, alkalosis signs.

Hyperchloremia: High chloride. Caused by dehydration, renal failure, metabolic acidosis. Symptoms: Weakness, lethargy, deep breathing.

Bicarbonate Imbalance

Low Levels: Metabolic Acidosis.

High Levels: Metabolic Alkalosis.

Treatment involves addressing the underlying acid-base disturbance.

‚öñÔ∏è Acid-Base Imbalance

Normal Blood pH Range: 7.35 to 7.45

Condition Characteristics Causes Symptoms Treatment
Metabolic Acidosis Low pH, Low $HCO_3^-$ Diarrhea, Renal failure, DKA, Lactic acidosis. Kussmaul breathing, confusion, nausea. Correct cause, Bicarbonate replacement.
Metabolic Alkalosis High pH, High $HCO_3^-$ Vomiting, Antacid overuse, Diuretics. Muscle twitching, tetany, shallow breathing. Correct cause, electrolytes (K+, Cl-).
Respiratory Acidosis Low pH, High $pCO_2$ Hypoventilation (COPD, Asthma, Opioid overdose). Shortness of breath, confusion, drowsiness. Improve ventilation, treat lung condition.
Respiratory Alkalosis High pH, Low $pCO_2$ Hyperventilation (Anxiety, Pain, High altitude). Dizziness, tingling, rapid breathing. Address hyperventilation (paper bag), treat anxiety/pain.
