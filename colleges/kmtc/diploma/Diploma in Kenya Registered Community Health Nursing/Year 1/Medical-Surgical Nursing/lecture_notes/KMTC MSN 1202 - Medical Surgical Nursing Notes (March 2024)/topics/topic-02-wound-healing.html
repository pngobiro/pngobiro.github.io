üìã Types and Classification of Wounds

Types of Wounds

  • Acute Wounds: result from trauma, surgery, or injury. These wounds progress through the normal stages of healing. Examples: Surgical incisions, lacerations, abrasions.
  • Chronic Wounds: Do not progress through the normal stages of healing and may remain in the inflammatory or proliferative phase for extended periods. Examples: Pressure ulcers, venous ulcers, diabetic foot ulcers.

Classification of wounds

1. by degree of contamination

  • clean: uninfected wounds in which no inflammation is encountered and the respiratory, gastrointestinal, genital, and/or urinary tracts are not entered
  • clean/contaminated: uninfected wounds in which no inflammation is encountered but the respiratory, gastrointestinal, genital, and/or urinary tract have been entered
  • contaminated: open, traumatic wounds or surgical wounds involving a major break in sterile technique that show evidence of inflammation
  • infected: old, traumatic wounds containing dead tissue and wounds with evidence of a clinical infection (e.g., purulent drainage)

2. by depth

  • partial-thickness: involves only the epidermal and dermal layers of skin
  • full-thickness: involves the epidermal and dermal layers of skin, subcutaneous tissue and, possibly, muscle and bone

3. by descriptive qualities

laceration

involves tearing apart of tissues resulting in irregular wound edges

abrasion

involves scraping or rubbing the surface of the skin by friction

contusion

involves a blow from a blunt object resulting in swelling, discoloration, bruising, and/or ec Cymosis

incision

involves cutting the skin with a sharp instrument

puncture

involves penetration of the skin and, often, the underlying tissues by a sharp instrument

üíß Wound Discharges and Healing Types

Types of wound discharges

  • serous exudate: consists chiefly of serum derived from blood and serous membranes of the body
  • sanguineous: consists of serum and red blood cells
  • purulent: consists of serum and pus (leukocytes, liquefied living and dead bacteria, dead tissue debris)

Types of wound healing

‚ñ∂ Healing by primary intention

occurs in wounds with dermal edges that are close together (e.g a scalpel incision).

  • wound is closed using staples, stitches, glues, or other forms of wound-closing processes.
  • This reduces the tissue lost and allows the body to focus on closing and healing a smaller-area wound rather than the larger initial wound
  • It is usually faster than by secondary intention
  • The end result of healing by primary intention is (in most cases) a complete return to function, with minimal scarring and loss of skin appendages
‚ñ∂ Secondary Intention

occurs when the sides of the wound are not opposed, therefore healing must occur from the bottom of the wound upwards.

  • the wound that cannot be stitched because of a large amount of tissue loss.
  • relies on the body's own healing mechanisms.
  • This process takes longer, which may be due to increased wound size, the risk of infection and contamination, and other factors, such as the use of certain medications

Complication

A complication from wound healing (particularly in people with darker skin), are keloid scars, whereby there is excessive collagen production, leading to extensive scarring. This can occur in both primary and secondary intention healing

‚ñ∂ Tertiary wound healing
  • healing by delayed primary closure, occurs when there is a need to delay the wound-closing process.
  • E.g. infectious germs in a wound . allow the wound to drain or wait for the effects of other therapies to take place before closing the wound.

üîÑ Stages of Wound Healing

Wound healing occurs in four overlapping phases: hemostasis, inflammation, proliferation, and remodeling.

1. Hemostasis (Immediate Response)

Duration: Minutes to hours post-injury.

It is characterized by:

  • Vascular Constriction: Immediate constriction of blood vessels reduces blood flow to the wound area.
  • Platelet Aggregation: Platelets adhere to the damaged tissue and each other, forming a platelet plug to stop bleeding.
  • Clot Formation: The clotting cascade is activated, leading to fibrin clot formation, which serves as a temporary barrier and matrix for incoming cells.

2. Inflammation (Cleaning Up)

Duration: Days 1-4.

It is characterized by:

  • Vasodilation: Blood vessels dilate to increase blood flow to the area, allowing immune cells to access the wound.
  • Leukocyte Infiltration: Neutrophils are the first cells to arrive, followed by macrophages. They phagocytize debris, pathogens, and damaged cells.
  • Cytokine Release: Pro-inflammatory cytokines are released, coordinating the inflammatory response and preparing the wound bed for repair.

3. Proliferation (Rebuilding)

Duration: Days 4-21.

It is characterized by:

  • Angiogenesis: Formation of new blood vessels from existing vessels to supply oxygen and nutrients to the healing tissue.
  • Fibroplasia: Fibroblasts proliferate and synthesize collagen and extracellular matrix, providing structural support.
  • Re-epithelialization: Keratinocytes migrate across the wound bed, covering it with new epithelial tissue.
  • Wound Contraction: Myofibroblasts pull the edges of the wound together, reducing its size.

4. Remodeling (Maturation)

Duration: Weeks to years.

It is characterized by:

  • Collagen Remodeling: Type III collagen is gradually replaced by Type I collagen, increasing tensile strength of the wound.
  • Matrix Remodeling: The extracellular matrix is reorganized and strengthened.
  • Scar Formation: The final scar is less elastic and has fewer sweat glands and hair follicles compared to the surrounding skin.

‚öñÔ∏è Factors Affecting Wound Healing

Local Factors:

  • Infection: Presence of pathogens can prolong inflammation and impair healing.
  • Necrotic Tissue: Dead tissue must be removed for healing to progress.
  • Wound Oxygenation: Adequate oxygen supply is essential for cellular functions and collagen synthesis.
  • Size and depth of wound.

Systemic Factors:

  • Nutrition: Adequate intake of proteins, vitamins (especially Vitamin C and A), and minerals (such as zinc) is essential for healing.
  • Chronic Diseases: Conditions like diabetes, vascular disease, and immunosuppression can delay healing.
  • Medications: Certain drugs, such as corticosteroids and immunosuppressants, can impair the healing process.

Lifestyle Factors:

  • Smoking: Nicotine constricts blood vessels and impairs oxygen delivery to the wound site.
  • Alcohol Use: Excessive alcohol can affect immune function and delay healing.

ü©∫ Wound Management Principles

  1. Assessment:
    • Wound Type: Identify whether the wound is acute or chronic.
    • Wound Bed: Evaluate for signs of infection, necrosis, and tissue viability.
    • Surrounding Skin: Check for signs of maceration, irritation, or infection.
  2. Cleaning:
    • Wound Cleansing: Use appropriate solutions (e.g., saline, antiseptic solutions) to clean the wound without damaging healthy tissue.
    • Debridement: Remove necrotic tissue and foreign bodies to promote healing.
  3. Dressing:
    • Wound Protection: Use dressings that maintain a moist environment, absorb exudate, and protect the wound from contaminants.
    • Types of Dressings: Options include hydrocolloids, hydrogels, alginates, and foams. Selection depends on the wound type and exudate level.
  4. Infection Control:
    • Antiseptics: Apply topical antibiotics or antiseptics if infection is suspected or confirmed.
    • Systemic Antibiotics: Prescribed based on culture and sensitivity results if systemic infection is present.
  5. Patient Education:
    • Wound Care: Educate patients on proper wound care techniques, signs of infection, and when to seek medical attention.
    • Lifestyle Modifications: Encourage practices that promote healing, such as smoking cessation and proper nutrition.
  6. Follow-Up:
    • Monitoring: Regularly assess the wound for signs of progress or complications.
    • Adjustment of Treatment: Modify treatment plans based on wound healing progress and patient response.
