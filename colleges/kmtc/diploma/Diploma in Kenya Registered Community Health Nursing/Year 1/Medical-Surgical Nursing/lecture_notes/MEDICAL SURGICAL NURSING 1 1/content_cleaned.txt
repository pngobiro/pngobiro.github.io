<p>MAIN OBJECTIVE:
To acquire knowledge on medical-surgical nursing and develop skills and attitudes in the management of patients with medical and surgical conditions.</p>
<p>SUB-OBJECTIVES
The student will be able to:</p>
<ol>
<li>Discuss historical development of Medicine and Surgery.</li>
<li>Classify the disease/condition according to their etiology.</li>
<li>Describe the disease process (Pathophysiology)</li>
<li>Describe common medical-surgical conditions</li>
</ol>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="background">Background<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#background" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Medical /surgical nursing- broad nursing specialty that provide care for adult patients with either /both acute and chronic conditions.</li>
<li>This requires broad knowledge on body system, surgical and medical pathologies, clinical skills, clinical decision making as well as collaborative skills.</li>
<li>According to Florence Nightingale, the goal of nursing was to put the patient in the best condition for nature to act upon him.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="1651e361">Cont’---<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#1651e361" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Roles of the Nurse in medical surgical nursing</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Nurse practitioner- makes interventions, teaches patient, families and communities and also collaborates care</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Leadership role- this role demands decision making, facilitation, influencing and relating with other nurses in order to meet patients, families and community health needs.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Researcher role- every nurse should participate in research in order to answer the questions and improve on the practice and hence the need to understand research methods</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Expanded nursing roles- an increase in health care demands leads to the need for ability to independent decision making hence the need for specialization e.g critical care nursing, family health nursing, orthopedic nursing among many others.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="models-of-nursing-care-delivery">Models of Nursing Care Delivery<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#models-of-nursing-care-delivery" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>

</li>
<li>
<p>Primary nursing</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Team nursing</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Case based management</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Community-based / community health</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Health care delivery system</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Changes in delivery of health care have been propelled by:</p>
<ul>
<li>Demographic changes- increase in population due to improved health care, expanded lifespan, flexible global movement, urbanization etc</li>
<li>Emerging and reemerging diseases</li>
<li>Aging population- health promotion, disease prevention and rehabilitative services led to prolongation of life as well as reduction in acute illnesses but an increase in chronic illnesses</li>
<li>Technological advances- diagnostic and therapeutic equipments.</li>
<li>Economical changes- high costs of health care</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="concepts-and-definition-of-terms">Concepts and Definition of terms<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#concepts-and-definition-of-terms" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>
<p><strong>Medicine</strong> - The branch of medical science that deals with nonsurgical techniques of treating illnesses</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Surgery</strong> - The branch of medical science that treats disease or injury by operative procedures</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Health</strong> - state of complete physical, mental, and social well-being and not merely the absence of disease.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Sepsis</strong> - presence of pathogens or their toxins</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Surgical asepsis</strong> - Condition of being aseptic (sterile) aseptic treatment and technique</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Aseptic technique</strong> - All steps taken to prevent contamination of surgical site by infectious agents</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Sterile - complete absence of microbes via the cleansing process</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Curettage - surgery to remove tissue or growths from a bodily cavity (as the uterus) by scraping with a curette</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Debridement - surgical removal of foreign material and dead tissue from a wound in order to prevent infection and promote healing</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Dehiscence - Bursting open of a wound, especially a surgical abdominal wound</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Health-Illness continuum - considers a person as having neither complete health nor complete illness. Instead, a person's state of health is ever-changing and has the potential to range from high-level wellness to extremely poor health status.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The patient/client: the one with health care needs. Care should be focused on the patient /client. Identification of the immediate health care need is a fundamental nursing requirement</p>
</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="inflammation">INFLAMMATION:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#inflammation" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Definition 1: the local response of living body tissues to injury due to any agent.</li>
<li>Definition 2: The complex biological response of body tissues to harmful stimuli, such as pathogens, damaged cells, or irritants</li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li>
<p>Inflammation is a protective response involving;</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>immune cells,</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>blood vessels,</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>molecular mediators</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>It is also the body defense reaction – to eliminate or limit the spread of injurious agent</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Causes of Inflammation</p>
<ol>
<li>
<p>Infective agents like bacteria, viruses, fungi, parasites (and their toxins).</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Immunological agents like cell-mediated and antigen antibody reactions.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Physical agents like heat, cold, radiation, mechanical trauma.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Chemical agents like organic and inorganic poisons.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Inert materials such as foreign bodies (chemically inactive)</p>
</li>

<p>INFLAMMATORY RESPONSE/PROCESS/THE PROCESS OF INFLAMMATION</p>
<p>Involves the following events:</p>
<p>VASCULAR EVENTS/RESPONSE
a) Hemodynamic changes
b) Altered vascular permeability</p>
<p>CELLULAR EVENTS/RESPONSE
c) Exudation of leukocytes
d) Phagocytosis</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="vascular-events-response">VASCULAR EVENTS/RESPONSE<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#vascular-events-response" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="a-hemodynamic-changes">a) Hemodynamic changes<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#a-hemodynamic-changes" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Transient vasoconstriction: Is the immediate vascular response to achieve hemostasis irrespective of type of injury.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Persistent progressive vasodilation: Involves mainly arterioles but to a lesser extent, capillaries. It results in increased blood volume in the micro-vascular bed of the site of acute inflammation.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Elevated local hydrostatic pressure: Caused by progressive vasodilation which results in transudation of fluid into the local site causing edema.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Slowing/stasis of microcirculation follows, causing increased concentration of RBCs and thus raised blood viscosity.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p><img src="assets/images/image-20251219-e6da14d8.jpeg" alt="img-6.jpeg"></p>
<p><img src="assets/images/image-20251219-38255acf.jpeg" alt="img-7.jpeg"></p>
<p>b) Altered vascular-permeability</p>
<p>There are two mechanisms -:</p>
<ul>
<li>Chemical mediators of acute inflammation may cause retraction of endothelial cells, leaving intercellular gaps (chemical mediated vascular leakage).</li>
<li>Toxins and physical agents may cause necrosis of vascular endothelium, leading to abnormal leakage (injury induced vascular leakage).</li>
</ul>
<p>CELLULAR EVENTS/RESPONSE</p>
<p>Includes:</p>
<p>i) Formation of the Cellular Exudate</p>
<p>How do white blood cells get out of the circulation and into the area where they are needed?</p>
<ul>
<li>The movement of leukocytes from the vessel lumen in a directional fashion to the site of tissue damage is called chemotaxis.</li>
<li>All granulocytes and monocytes respond to chemotactic factors and move along a concentration gradient (from an area of lesser concentration of the factor to an area of greater concentration of the factor).</li>
</ul>
<p>ii) Phagocytosis</p>
<ul>
<li>The process whereby cells ingest solid particles is termed phagocytosis.</li>
<li>The first step in phagocytosis is adhesion of the particle to be phagocytosed to the cell surface. The phagocyte ingests the attached particle by sending out pseudopodia around it. These meet and fuse so that the particle lies in a phagocytic vacuole (also called a phagosome) bounded by cell membrane. Lysosomes, then fuse with phagosomes to form phagolysosomes. It is within these that intracellular killing of microorganisms occurs.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="inflammation-process">INFLAMMATION PROCESS;<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#inflammation-process" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p><img src="assets/images/image-20251219-c9648f0c.jpeg" alt="img-8.jpeg"></p>
<ol>
<li>Chemical signals released by activated macrophages and mast cells at the injury site cause nearby capillaries to widen and become more permeable.</li>
</ol>
<p><img src="assets/images/image-20251219-c1395182.jpeg" alt="img-9.jpeg"></p>
<ol start="2">
<li>Ruid, antimicrobial proteins, and clotting elements move from the blood to the site. Clotting begins.</li>
</ol>
<p><img src="assets/images/image-20251219-97747171.jpeg" alt="img-10.jpeg"></p>
<ol start="3">
<li>Chemokines released by various kinds of cells attract more phagocytic cells from the blood to the injury site.</li>
</ol>
<p><img src="assets/images/image-20251219-3ea1b62e.jpeg" alt="img-11.jpeg"></p>
<ol start="4">
<li>Neutrophils and macrophages phagocytose pathogens and cell debris at the site, and the tissue heals.</li>
</ol>

<p>MANAGEMENT OF PATIENTS WITH INFLAMMATION FEVER/PYREXIA: Occurs due to bacteremia. Administer prescribed antipyretics e.g. paracetamol. Give high calorie diet in the form of carbohydrates. This is to meet the increased metabolic demand in patients with fever.</p>
<p>LEUCOCYTOSIS: Usually in bacterial infections there is neutrophilia, viral infections cause lymphocytosis, parasitic infections cause eosinophilia. Administer prescribed antibacterial agents.</p>
<p>DIET: Provide easily digestible diet (light diet), keep the fluid balance, and give high protein diet for the formation of new tissue to build up the destroyed tissue.</p>
<p>SEDATION: Inflammation will produce pain, therefore sedative drugs may be given to induce sleep.</p>
<p>SHOCK: Systematic activation of coagulation pathway may occur leading to microthrombi throughout the body and results into DIC, bleeding and death. Severe tissue injury results in profuse systemic vasodilation, increased vascular permeability and intravascular volume loss causing hypotension and shock. Give plenty of I.V fluids.</p>
<p>PAIN: Immobilize the affected limb, administer prescribed analgesics. Give anti-inflammatory agents e.g ibuprofen, indomethacin, steroids e.g prednisone and dexamethasone.</p>
<p>REST: Elevate the affected limb. The inflamed part is rested by elevation. In case of arms use splints, and for lower limbs use pillows and clear.</p>
<p>LOCAL TREATMENT: If the inflammation is broken and septic, use antiseptics to kill the pathogenic microbes in the wound. Examples of antiseptics include: hydrogen peroxide, hibitane (Chlorexidine).</p>

<p>CLASSIFICATION
OF
DISEASES</p>
<p>INTRODUCTION</p>
<ul>
<li>A disease is a particular abnormal condition that affects part or all of an organism and is not caused by external force (injury).</li>
<li>Disease is often interpreted as a medical condition associated with specific symptoms and signs.</li>
<li>It may be caused by external factors such as pathogens or by internal dysfunctions, particularly of the immune system, such as an immunodeficiency, or by a hypersensitivity, including allergies and autoimmunity.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id=""><span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Diseases may be classified by cause, pathogenesis or by symptom(s).</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Alternatively, diseases may be classified according to the organ system involved, though this is often complicated since many diseases affect more than one organ.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The most known and used classification of diseases is the World Health Organization's ICD (International Statistical Classification of Diseases and Related Health Problems). This is periodically updated.</p>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="icd">ICD<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#icd" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>
<p>The International Classification of Diseases (ICD) is the international "standard diagnostic tool for epidemiology, health management and clinical purposes".</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>It's full official name in International Statistical Classification of Diseases and Related Health Problems.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The ICD is designed as a health care classification system, providing a system of diagnostic codes for classifying diseases.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>It contains codes for diseases, signs and symptoms, abnormal findings, complaints, social circumstances, and external causes of injury or diseases.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The first international classification edition, known as the International List of Causes of Death, was adopted by the International Statistical Institute in 1893.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="disease-classification">Disease classification :<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#disease-classification" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>There are four main classes of disease
i. Genetic disease
ii. Infectious disease (infections)
iii. Neoplasmic (benign or malignant) disease
iv. Traumatic disease (injuries)</p>
<ul>
<li>Diseases can also be classified as communicable and non-communicable.</li>
</ul>
<p>i. Genetic disease</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>A genetic disease is a genetic problem caused by one or more abnormalities formed in the genome.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Most genetic disorders are quite rare</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Genetic disorders may be hereditary or non-hereditary, meaning that they are passed down from the parents' genes.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>However, in some genetic disorders, defects may be caused by new mutations, altered phenotype, or changes to the DNA.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Examples: albinism, sickle-cell disease, hemophilia etc</p>
</li>
<li></li>
</ul>
<p>ii. Infectious disease (infections)</p>
<ul>
<li>Infectious diseases are disorders caused by organisms — such as bacteria, viruses, fungi or parasites.</li>
<li>Some infectious diseases can be passed from person to person.</li>
<li>Some are transmitted by insects or other animals. And one may get others by consuming contaminated food or water or being exposed to organisms in the environment.</li>
<li>Signs and symptoms vary depending on the organism causing the infection, but often include fever and fatigue.</li>
</ul>
<p>Mild infections may respond to rest and home remedies, while some life-threatening infections may need hospitalization.</p>
<ul>
<li>Many infectious diseases, such as measles and chickenpox, can be prevented by vaccines.</li>
<li>Frequent and thorough hand-washing helps to protect individuals from most infectious diseases.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id=""><span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"></span></h1></div>
<p>iii. Neoplasmic (benign or malignant) disease</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>A neoplasm is a type of abnormal and excessive growth, called neoplasia, of tissue.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The growth of a neoplasm is uncoordinated with that of the normal surrounding tissue, and it persists growing abnormally, even if the original trigger is removed.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>This abnormal growth usually (but not always) forms a mass. When it forms a mass, it may be called a tumor.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>ICD-10 classifies neoplasms into the following main groups: benign neoplasms, malignant neoplasms, and neoplasms of uncertain or unknown behavior. Malignant neoplasms are also simply known as cancers and are the focus of oncology.</p>
</li>
<li></li>
</ul>
<p>Types of neoplasms</p>
<ul>
<li>A neoplasm can be benign or malignant (cancer).</li>
<li>Benign tumors include uterine fibroids.</li>
<li>They are circumscribed, localized and do not transform into cancer.</li>
<li>Malignant neoplasms are commonly called cancer.</li>
<li>They invade and destroy the surrounding tissue, may form metastases and, if untreated or unresponsive to treatment, will generally prove fatal.</li>
<li>Secondary neoplasm refers to any of a class of cancerous tumor that is a metastatic offshoot of a primary tumor.</li>
<li>If a metastatic neoplasm has no known site of the primary cancer, it is classified as a cancer of unknown primary origin</li>
</ul>
<p>iv. Traumatic disease (injuries)</p>
<p>Trauma most often refers to:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Psychological trauma</strong>, a type of damage to the psyche that occurs as a result of a severely distressing event</li>
<li><strong>Traumatic injury</strong>, sudden physical injury caused by an external force, which does not rise to the level of major trauma</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="disease-classification">Disease Classification:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#disease-classification" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>The other widely used classifications of disease are :</p>
<p>(1) Topographic, by bodily region or system,
(2) Anatomic, by organ or tissue,
(3) Physiological, by function or effect,
(4) Pathological, by the nature of the disease process,
(5) Etiologic (causal)
(6) Epidemiological</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="1-topographic-classification">1. Topographic classification<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#1-topographic-classification" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>In the topographic classification, diseases are subdivided into such categories as gastrointestinal disease, vascular disease, abdominal disease, and chest disease.</li>
<li>Various specializations within medicine follow such topographic or systemic divisions, so that there are physicians who are essentially vascular surgeons, for example, or clinicians who are specialized in gastrointestinal disease.</li>
<li>Similarly, some physicians have become specialized in chest disease and concentrate principally on diseases of the heart and lungs.</li>
</ul>
<ol start="2">
<li>Anatomic classification</li>
</ol>
<p>In the anatomic classification, disease is categorized by the specific organ or tissue affected; hence, heart disease, liver disease, and lung disease.</p>
<ul>
<li>Medical specialties such as cardiology are restricted to diseases of a single organ, in this case the heart.</li>
<li>Such a classification has its greatest use in identifying the various kinds of disease that affect a particular organ.</li>
<li>The heart is a good example to consider.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="3-physiological-classification">3. Physiological classification<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#3-physiological-classification" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>The <strong>physiological classification</strong> of disease is based on the underlying functional derangement produced by a specific disorder.</li>
<li>Included in this classification are such <strong>subclasses</strong> as respiratory and <strong>metabolic disease</strong>. Respiratory diseases are those that interfere with the intake and expulsion of air and the exchange of oxygen for <strong>carbon dioxide</strong> in the lungs.</li>
<li>Metabolic diseases are those in which disturbances of the body’s chemical processes are a basic feature. Diabetes and <strong>gout</strong> are examples.</li>
</ul>
<ol start="4">
<li>Pathological classification</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>The pathological classification of disease considers the nature of the disease process.</li>
<li>Neoplastic and inflammatory disease are examples.</li>
<li>Neoplastic disease includes the whole range of tumors, particularly cancers, and their effect on human beings.</li>
<li>Examples of Inflammatory diseases include: appendicitis, cellulitis, otitis media etc</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="5-etiologic-classification">5. Etiologic classification<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#5-etiologic-classification" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>The <strong>etiologic classification</strong> of disease is based on the cause, when known.</li>
<li>This classification is particularly important and useful in the consideration of biotic disease.</li>
<li>On this basis disease might be classified as bacterial (e.g. staphylococcal) or fungal, etc.</li>
<li>Bacteria for example, cause skin infections, <strong>pneumonia</strong>, <strong>meningitis</strong>, abscesses in the liver, and kidney infections.</li>
<li>Diseases such as amoebiasis (parasitic), chicken pox (viral), tinea capitis (fungal) and gonorrhea (bacterial) are further examples of diseases classified by <strong>etiology</strong>.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="6-epidemiological-classification">6. Epidemiological classification<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#6-epidemiological-classification" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>The epidemiological classification of disease deals with the incidence, distribution, and control of disorders in a population.</li>
<li>To use the example of typhoid, a disease spread through contaminated food and water, it first becomes important to establish that the disease observed is truly caused by Salmonella typhi, the typhoid organism.</li>
<li>Once the diagnosis is established, it is important to know the number of cases, whether the cases were scattered over the course of a year or occurred within a short period, and what the geographic distribution is.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="common-medical-surgical-conditions">COMMON MEDICAL SURGICAL CONDITIONS<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#common-medical-surgical-conditions" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="abscess">ABSCESS<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#abscess" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="abscess">Abscess<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#abscess" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Definition 1: An abscess is a cavity filled with pus. It contains white blood cells, dead tissue and bacteria.</li>
<li>Definition 2: localized collection of pus surrounded by inflamed tissue</li>
</ul>
<p><img src="assets/images/image-20251219-454df8e4.jpeg" alt="img-12.jpeg"></p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="types-of-abscess">TYPES OF ABSCESS<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#types-of-abscess" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Abscesses can develop anywhere in the body.</li>
</ul>
<p>i. Skin abscesses – which develop under the skin
ii. Internal abscesses – which develop inside the body, in an organ or in the spaces between organs
iii. Incisional abscess – An incisional abscess is one that develops as a complication secondary to a surgical incision.</p>
<ul>
<li>It presents as redness and warmth at the margins of the incision with purulent drainage from it.</li>
<li>If the diagnosis is uncertain, an abscess should be aspirated with a needle, and the aspirated pus be presented for culture and sensitivity</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="other-abscesses">Other abscesses<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#other-abscesses" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>There are many other types of abscess, including:</li>
<li>Anorectal abscess – a build-up of pus in the rectum and anus</li>
<li>Bartholin's abscess – a build-up of pus inside one of the Bartholin's glands, which are found on each side of the opening of the vaginal orifice.</li>
<li>brain abscess – a rare but potentially life-threatening build-up of pus inside the skull (cranium)</li>
<li>Dental abscess – a build-up of pus under a tooth or in the supporting gum and bone</li>
<li>Quinsy (peritonsillar abscess) – a build-up of pus between one of the tonsils and the wall of the throat (as complication of tonsilitis)</li>
<li>spinal cord abscess – a build-up of pus around the spinal cord</li>
</ul>
<p>Causes of abscesses</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Most abscesses are caused by a bacterial infection, parasites, or foreign substances, but bacteria is most common cause.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>When bacteria enter the body, the immune system sends infection-fighting white blood cells to the affected area.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>As the white blood cells attack the bacteria, some nearby tissues die, creating a space which then fills with pus to form an abscess. The pus contains a mixture of dead tissue, white blood cells and bacteria.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Internal abscesses often develop as a complication of an existing condition, such as an infection elsewhere in your body. For example, if the appendix bursts as a result of appendicitis, bacteria can spread inside the abdomen and cause an abscess to form.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The most common bacterial organism responsible for the development of skin abscesses is Staphylococcus aureus</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>With the emergence of methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA), health care providers must now consider this organism as the possible cause when a skin abscess is encountered.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Pathophysiology:</p>
<ul>
<li>Upon entry of the organisms or foreign materials, <em>Staphylococcus aureus</em> for example kills the local cells, resulting in the release of cytokines.</li>
<li>The cytokines trigger an inflammatory response, which draws large numbers of white blood cells to the area and increases the regional blood flow.</li>
<li>The final structure of the abscess is an abscess wall, or capsule, that is formed by the adjacent healthy cells in an attempt to keep the pus from infecting neighboring structures.</li>
<li>However, such encapsulation tends to prevent immune cells from attacking bacteria in the pus, or from reaching the causative organism or foreign object.</li>
</ul>
<p>Signs and Symptoms of skin abscesses:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>A skin abscess often appears as a swollen, pus-filled lump under the surface of the skin.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Body malaise</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Other symptoms of an infection, such as a fever,</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>chills.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>warmth</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>redness (in the affected area)</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>A boil is a common example of a skin abscess.</p>
</li>
<li></li>
</ul>
<p>common signs and Symptoms of internal abscesses</p>
<ul>
<li>The signs and symptoms of an internal abscess can also vary depending on exactly where in the body the abscess develops.</li>
<li>For example, a liver abscess may cause jaundice, whereas an abscess in or near the lungs may cause a cough or shortness of breath.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="general-signs-and-symptoms-of-an-internal-abscess">General Signs and Symptoms of an Internal Abscess<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#general-signs-and-symptoms-of-an-internal-abscess" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Discomfort in the area of the abscess</li>
<li>Fever</li>
<li>Increased Sweating</li>
<li>Vomiting</li>
<li>Chills</li>
<li>Pain or Swelling in the abdomen</li>
<li>Loss of Appetite</li>
<li>Weight Loss</li>
<li>Extreme Tiredness (Fatigue)</li>
<li>Diarrhoea or Constipation</li>
</ul>
<p>Risk factors:</p>
<p>You're at increased risk for this bacterial infection if you have:</p>
<ul>
<li>Close contact with an individual who has a staph infection, (which is why these infections are more common in hospitals)</li>
<li>A chronic skin disease, like acne or eczema</li>
<li>Diabetes</li>
<li>A weakened immune system, which can be caused by infections such as HIV</li>
<li>Poor hygiene habits</li>
</ul>
<p>Risk factors cont..</p>
<p>Infected hair follicles (folliculitis)</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Infected hair follicles, or folliculitis, may cause abscesses to form in the follicle. Follicles can become infected if the hair within the follicle is trapped and unable to break through the skin, as can happen after shaving.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Trapped hair follicles are commonly known as ingrown hairs. Ingrown hairs can set the stage for an infection. Abscesses that are on or in a hair follicle will often contain this ingrown hair.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Folliculitis may also occur after spending time in an inadequately chlorinated pool or hot tub.</p>
</li>
<li></li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="investigations">Investigations<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#investigations" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>
<p>When taking medical history, ask:</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>How long the abscess has been present</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>If they recall any injury to that area</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>What drugs one may be taking</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>If they have any allergies</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>If they had a fever</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Physical exam. Examine the abscess and surrounding areas.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Take a culture or a small amount of fluid from the abscess to test for the presence of bacteria</p>
</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="difference-between-cyst-and-abscess">Difference between cyst and abscess<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#difference-between-cyst-and-abscess" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="cyst">CYST<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#cyst" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>A cyst is a closed sac that develops abnormally in some body structure</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Symptoms:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>A cyst grows slowly and isn't usually painful, unless it becomes enlarged.</li>
<li>When an already-formed cyst becomes infected, it becomes an abscess.</li>
<li>Not infected.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="abscess">ABSCESS<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#abscess" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>An abscess is a pus-filled infection in the body caused by, for example, bacteria or fungi.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Symptoms:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Pain, redness, and swelling and can cause symptoms elsewhere in the body.</li>
<li>An abscess doesn't have to begin as a cyst. It can form on its own.</li>
<li>An abscess is infected</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="skin-abscesses">Skin abscesses<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#skin-abscesses" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Some small skin abscesses may drain naturally &amp; get better without the need for treatment.</li>
<li>For larger or persistent skin abscesses, a course of Antibiotics may be prescribed to help clear the infection and prevent it from spreading.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="internal-abscesses">Internal abscesses<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#internal-abscesses" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>The pus usually needs to be drained from an internal abscess, either by using a needle inserted through the skin (percutaneous abscess drainage) or with surgery.</li>
<li>The method used will depend on the size of the abscess and where it is in the body.</li>
<li>Antibiotics will usually be given at the same time, to help kill the infection and prevent it from spreading. These may be given as orally or intravenously.</li>
</ul>
<p>Treatment:</p>
<ul>
<li>A small skin abscess may drain naturally, or simply shrink, dry up and disappear without any treatment.</li>
<li>Abscesses can be treated in a number of different ways, depending on the type of abscess and how large it is.</li>
<li>The main treatment options include:</li>
<li>antibiotics</li>
<li>a drainage procedure</li>
<li>warm compress</li>
<li>Surgery</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="surgery-incision-and-drainage">Surgery (Incision and drainage)<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#surgery-incision-and-drainage" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>If the skin abscess needs draining, the patient will have a minor surgical operation carried out under anesthesia—usually a local anesthetic.</li>
<li>During the procedure, an incision is made in the abscess, to allow the pus to drain out. A sample of pus may also be taken for testing.</li>
<li>Once all of the pus has been removed, the resulting hole that is left by the abscess is cleaned using sterile saline (a salt solution).</li>
<li>The abscess will be left open but covered with a wound dressing, so if any more pus is produced it can drain away easily.</li>
<li>If the abscess is deep, an antiseptic dressing may be placed inside the wound to keep it open. The procedure may leave a small scar.</li>
</ul>
<p><img src="assets/images/image-20251219-bb06d1c5.jpeg" alt="img-13.jpeg">
Abscess five days after incision and drainage</p>
<p>Percutaneous drainage</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>If the internal abscess is small, the surgeon may be able to drain it using a fine needle.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Depending on the location of the abscess, this may be carried out using either a local or general anaesthetic.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The surgeon may use ultrasound scans or computerised tomography (CT) scans to help guide the needle into the right place.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Once the abscess has been located, the pus is drained using the needle. A small incision may be made on the skin over the abscess, then insert a thin plastic tube called a drainage catheter into it.</p>
</li>
<li></li>
<li>
<p>The catheter allows the pus to drain out into a bag and may have to be left in place for up to a week.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>This procedure may be carried out as a day case procedure, which means the patient may be able to go back home the same day, although some people will need to stay in hospital for a few days.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>You may need to undergo surgery if:</p>
<p>i. Your internal abscess is too large to be drained with a needle
ii. A needle can't get to the abscess safely
iii. Needle drainage hasn't been effective in removing all of the pus</p>
<p>The type of surgery will depend on the type of internal abscess and where it is in the body.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="prevention-of-abscesses">Prevention of abscesses<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#prevention-of-abscesses" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Proper hygiene is the best way to avoid infection.</li>
<li>Keep cuts and wounds clean, dry, and covered to protect them from microorganisms.</li>
<li>Avoid sharing clothing, towels, razors, or bed linens with anyone else.</li>
<li>When these items get dirty, wash them separately in hot water.</li>
<li>Wash your hands well and often using soap and water for at least 20 seconds each time. It's OK to use alcohol-based instant hand sanitizers or wipes if you're not near any soap and water.</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="furuncle-and-carbuncle">Furuncle and Carbuncle<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#furuncle-and-carbuncle" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p><img src="assets/images/image-20251219-d9e2fc4b.jpeg" alt="img-16.jpeg"></p>
<p><img src="assets/images/image-20251219-3f325098.jpeg" alt="img-17.jpeg"></p>
<p>Carbuncle : Multiple pointing</p>
<ul>
<li>A hair follicle is a part of the skin, which grows a hair.</li>
<li>Attached inside the top of the follicle are sebaceous glands, which are tiny sebum-producing glands in almost all skin except on the palms, lips and soles of the feet.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Causes:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>A carbuncle usually develops when <em>Staphylococcus aureus</em> bacteria enter the hair follicles.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Entrance/Portal site:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Insect bite and broken skin make it easy for bacteria to enter the body and cause an infection.</li>
<li>This can result in boils or carbuncles (a cluster of boils) filled with fluid and pus.</li>
</ul>
<p>Location:</p>
<ul>
<li>Carbuncles are common on the back of the neck, shoulders, or thigh. They can also appear on the face, armpits, or buttocks</li>
</ul>
<p>Favourite environment of staph bacteria:</p>
<ul>
<li>The moist parts of the body are particularly susceptible to this infection because bacteria thrive in these areas where you sweat or experience friction.</li>
</ul>
<p>Risk factors:</p>
<ul>
<li>Being in close contact with someone who has a carbuncle (the infection can spread when people share space, materials, or devices, such as clothing)</li>
</ul>
<p>The following factors also increase the risk of developing a carbuncle:</p>
<ul>
<li>Poor hygiene</li>
<li>Diabetes (High levels of blood sugar, or glucose, can reduce the immune system's ability to respond to infection.)</li>
<li>a weak immune system</li>
<li>Skin conditions: Psoriasis, eczema, and acne increase susceptibility.</li>
<li>kidney disease (renal carbuncle may have metastasis</li>
<li>Medications: Some medications weaken the immune system.</li>
<li>shaving and other activities that break the skin</li>
<li>Men get carbuncles more often than women. (scraps on face during shave)</li>
</ul>
<p>Signs and Symptoms:</p>
<p>Boils can occur anywhere on your skin, but more likely on hair-bearing areas, where you're most likely to sweat or experience friction. Appear mainly on the face, back of the neck, armpits, thighs and buttocks.</p>
<p>Signs &amp; symptoms include:</p>
<ul>
<li>A painful, red bump that starts out small and can enlarge to more than 2 inches (5 centimeters)</li>
<li>Tenderness</li>
<li>Red, swollen skin around the bump</li>
<li>An increase in the size of the bump over a few days as it fills with pus</li>
<li>Development of a yellow-white tip that eventually ruptures and allows the pus to drain out.</li>
</ul>
<p>A carbuncle usually:</p>
<ul>
<li>Develops over several days</li>
<li>Have a white or yellow center (contains pus)</li>
<li>Weep, ooze, or crust</li>
<li>Spread to other skin areas</li>
</ul>
<p>Sometimes, other symptoms may occur including:</p>
<ul>
<li>Fatigue</li>
<li>Fever</li>
<li>General discomfort</li>
<li>Skin itching before the carbuncle develops</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="exams-and-tests">Exams and Tests<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#exams-and-tests" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>The diagnosis may base on clinical examination</li>
<li>A sample of the pus may be sent to a lab to determine the bacteria causing the infection (bacterial culture and sensitivity).</li>
<li>The test result helps determine the appropriate treatment.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Management:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Warm compresses -</li>
<li>Warn patient not to squeeze or incise the lesion</li>
<li>Systemic antibiotics (cloxacillin, erythromycin)</li>
<li>Bed Rest especially for genital area furuncles.</li>
<li>For severe pain: codeine, morphine</li>
<li>Antibiotics. Sometimes your doctor may prescribe antibiotics to help heal severe or recurrent infections.</li>
<li>For larger boils and carbuncles, treatment may include:</li>
<li>Incision and drainage. (when it is fluctuant). Deep infections that can't be completely drained may be packed with sterile gauze to help soak up and remove additional pus.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="lifestyle-and-home-remedies">Lifestyle and home remedies<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#lifestyle-and-home-remedies" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>For small boils, these measures may help the infection heal more quickly and prevent it from spreading:</p>
<ul>
<li>Warm compresses. Apply a warm washcloth or compress to the affected area several times a day, for about 10 minutes each time. This helps the boil rupture and drain more quickly.</li>
<li>Prevent contamination. Wash your hands thoroughly after treating a boil. Also, launder clothing, towels or compresses that have touched the infected area.</li>
</ul>
<p>Complications</p>
<ul>
<li>Rarely, bacteria from a boil or carbuncle can enter your bloodstream and travel to other parts of your body.</li>
<li>The spreading infection, commonly known as <strong>sepsis</strong>, can lead to infections deep within your body, such as your heart (endocarditis) and bone (osteomyelitis).</li>
<li><strong>Preventions:</strong></li>
<li>Wash your hands regularly with soap. Or use an alcohol-based hand rub often. Careful hand-washing is your best defense against germs.</li>
<li>Keep wounds covered. Keep cuts and abrasions clean and covered with sterile, dry bandages until they heal.</li>
<li>Avoid sharing personal items. Don't share towels, sheets, razors, clothing, athletic equipment and other personal items</li>
</ul>

<p>Pathophysiology</p>
<ul>
<li>After destruction of skin there is a marked loss of fluid from the body of about 10-20 times more through evaporation and damage of the blood vessels.</li>
<li>The trauma of the burn triggers an inflammatory response that increases vessel permeability leading to increase in fluid loss (both salt, water and proteins) leading to burn wound edema that is worst in the first 72hrs</li>
<li>Loss of skin barrier to microorganisms combined with the immunosuppression leads to increased bacterial proliferation and infection with gram +ve and later gram –ve leading to other infections e.g pneumonia, septicemia and wound infections</li>
</ul>

<p>Depends with type and degree of burns</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="wound-healing">WOUND HEALING<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#wound-healing" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Wound healing is a mechanism by which the body attempts to restore the integrity of the injured part.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="factors-influencing-healing-of-a-wound">Factors influencing healing of a wound<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#factors-influencing-healing-of-a-wound" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Site of the wound</li>
<li>Structures involved</li>
<li>Mechanism of wounding</li>
<li>Type of incision</li>
<li>Contamination (foreign bodies/bacteria)</li>
<li>Loss of tissue on the wound</li>
</ul>
<p>Cont’--</p>
<p>Local factors –––</p>
<p>• Vascular insufficiency (arterial or venous)</p>
<p>• Previous radiation</p>
<p>Systemic factors</p>
<p>• Malnutrition or vitamin and mineral deficiencies</p>
<p>• Disease (e.g. diabetes mellitus)</p>
<p>• Medications (e.g. steroids)</p>
<p>• Immune deficiencies [e.g. chemotherapy, acquired immunodeficiency syndrome (AIDS)]</p>
<p>• Smoking</p>
<p>Types of wounds</p>
<ul>
<li>Surgical- created intentionally to attend to a pathology</li>
<li>Traumatic wounds- accidental wounds e.g burns, crush, cuts etc</li>
<li>Chronic – develop due to chronic conditions e.g pressure ulcers, skin conditions, abscesses etc (more classifications exist)</li>
</ul>
<p>Healing may occur in one of the following:</p>
<ol>
<li>Healing by Primary intention</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>Healing through primary intention occurs when wounds are created aseptically, with a minimum of tissue destruction tissue reaction.</li>
</ul>
<p>Conditions in healing by primary intention</p>
<p>• Edges of an incised wound in a healthy individual are promptly and accurately approximated.</p>
<p>• Contamination is held to a minimum by rigid adherence to</p>
<p>• aseptic technique.</p>
<p>• Trauma is minimal.</p>
<p>• No tissue loss occurs.</p>
<p>• On completion of closure, no dead space remains to become a potential site of infection.</p>
<p>• Drainage is minimal.</p>
<p>Cont--</p>
<ol start="2">
<li>Healing by secondary intention</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>These type of wounds are characterized by tissue loss with an inability to approximate wound edges.</li>
<li>The wound heals from the inside toward the outer surface.</li>
<li>In infected wounds this process allows cleaning and dressing.</li>
<li>Healing is by granulation and eventually there is scarring.</li>
<li>These type of wounds include chronic, septic and traumatic wounds</li>
</ul>
<ol start="3">
<li>Healing by tertiary intention</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>Occurs when approximation of wound edges is intentionally delayed by 3 or more days after injury or surgery.</li>
<li>This could be due to heavy contamination of wound or condition of patient.</li>
<li>Debridement may be done then wound closed later</li>
</ul>
<p>Phases of wound healing</p>
<p>i. Hemostasis – Contraction of carpillaries to stop the bleeding</p>
<p>ii. Inflammatory(Reactive ) – A vascular and cellular response to dispose off bacteria, foreign material and dead tissue.
✓ Leukocytes (Neutrophils, Monocytes and macrophages) increase in number to fight bacteria in the wound area and by phagocytosis, help to remove damaged tissues, and foreign bodies.
✓ There is also an increase in platelets.</p>
<p>✓</p>
<p>Cont,.</p>
<p>✔ The vascular response is about dilatation of blood vessels to allow permability of exudate for supply of more oxygen, plasma proteins and bradykinins for healing process. This causes redness, local warmth, and swelling.</p>
<p>✔ The damaged tissue is glued together by strands of fibrin and a thin layer of clotted blood, forming a scab. Plasma seeps to the surface to form a dry, protective crust. This seal helps to prevent fluid loss and bacterial invasion.</p>
<p>iii. Proliferative(Regenerative )</p>
<p>• Epithelial cells migrate and proliferate to the wound area, covering the surface of the wound .</p>
<p>• Collagen synthesis, deposit and contraction of the wound occur in this phase. Underneath the epithelium layer, granulation continues. Epithelialization is limited to small wounds while larger ones may require grafting.</p>
<p>iv. Remolding(Maturation)</p>
<p>• The phase starts 2-4 wks after injury and may continue for years.</p>
<p>• There is deposition of collagen fibres and breakdown of earlier deposits, as well as realignment of the fibres till the scar is strongly formed.</p>

<ul>
<li>Depending on the type or the cause of the shock, treatments differ.</li>
<li>In general, fluid resuscitation (giving a large amount of fluid to raise blood pressure quickly) with an IV in the ambulance or emergency room is the first-line treatment for all types of shock.</li>
<li>The doctors or nurses will also administer medications such as epinephrine, norepinephrine, or dopamine to the fluids to try to raise a patient's blood pressure to ensure blood flow to the vital organs.</li>
<li>Tests (for example, blood tests, EKGs) will determine the underlying cause of the shock and uncover the severity of the patient's illness.</li>
<li>Nutritional needs</li>
</ul>
<p>Rx</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Septic shock</strong> is treated with prompt administration of antibiotics depending on the source and type of underlying infection.</li>
<li>These patients are often dehydrated and require large amounts of fluids to increase and maintain blood pressure.</li>
<li><strong>Anaphylactic shock</strong> is treated with diphenhydramine (Benadryl), epinephrine, steroid medications methylprednisolone</li>
</ul>
<p>Rx</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Cardiogenic shock is treated by identifying and treating the underlying cause.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>A patient with a heart attack may require cardiac catheterization to unblock an artery.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>A patient with congestive heart failure may need medications to support and increase the force of the heart's beat. In severe or prolonged cases, a heart transplant may be the only treatment.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Hypovolemic shock is treated with fluids (saline) in minor cases, but may require multiple blood transfusions in severe cases. The underlying cause of the bleeding must also be identified and corrected.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Neurogenic shock is the most difficult to treat.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Damage to the spinal cord is often irreversible and causes problems with the natural regulatory functions of the body.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Besides fluid monitoring:</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>☑ Immobilization (keeping the spine from moving),</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>☑ Anti-inflammatory drugs such as steroids</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>☑ Sometimes surgery are the main parts of treatment.</p>
</li>
</ul>

<li>K- intracellular 3.5 - 5.5mmol/l</li>

<ul>
<li>
<p>Treat the cause e.g. Give water or fluids with low sodium (hypotonic solution) with care not to bring down the levels so fast which may lead to brain edema.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Promptly manage systemic causes.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Monitor the serum sodium levels</p>
</li>
<li></li>
</ul>
<p>Cont’-</p>
<p>—</p>
<p>Hyperkalemia</p>
<p>Potassium levels above 5.5 mmol/l</p>
<p>Causes:</p>
<p>• Hemolysis due to trauma,</p>
<p>• Renal failure,</p>
<p>• Acidosis and physiologically after exercises,</p>
<p>• Iatrogenic causes (treatment related, potassium chloride, heparin, ACE inhibitors, nsaids, and potassium-sparing diuretics)</p>
<p>Effects</p>
<p>• Muscle weakness, at times paralysis,</p>
<p>• ECG changes such as narrow T waves and a shortened QT interval.</p>
<p>• Nausea,</p>
<p>• Diarrhea.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="management">Management<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#management" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Monitor Serum potassium levels and ECG changes</li>
<li>Restriction of dietary potassium for patients using potassium retention diuretics</li>
<li>Calcium gluconate intravenously (Ca salts antagonize the effects of potassium in cells)</li>
<li>Continuous monitoring of patient on ECG</li>
</ul>
<p>Hypokalemia-</p>
<p>Serum potasium below 3.5mmol/l</p>
<p>Causes</p>
<p>• Diarrhea</p>
<p>• Vomiting,</p>
<p>• Ileostomies,</p>
<p>• Metabolic alkalosis,</p>
<p>• Prolonged intestinal suctioning,</p>
<p>• Hyperaldostronism,</p>
<p>• Potassium-losing diuretics,</p>
<p>• Elderly,</p>
<p>Signs and symptoms</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Fatigue ,</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Anorexia,</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Nausea,</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Vomiting,</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Muscle weakness,</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Leg cramps,</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Paresthesias (numbness and tingling),</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>ECG changes - flat T wave</p>
</li>
<li></li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="management">Management<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#management" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>If not severe, dietary measures could elevate the levels</li>
<li>If severe (below 2mmol/l) then intravenous infusions with potassium is administered with care and should be at 10-20mmol/hr and not more</li>
<li>ECG monitoring</li>
<li>Serum potassium monitoring</li>
</ul>
<p>Read and make notes on hypo/hypercalcemia, hypo/hypermagnesia and hyper/hypophosphatemia**</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="acid-base-imbalance">ACID-BASE IMBALANCE<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#acid-base-imbalance" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="acid-base-imbalance">Acid-Base Imbalance<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#acid-base-imbalance" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>The imbalance is measured as per the serum pH level which is basically a measure of acidity.</li>
<li>Normal serum pH is 7.35-7.45</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="types-of-imbalances">Types of imbalances<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#types-of-imbalances" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Acidosis- a state when the pH level in the blood is below 7.3 and the alkaline buffers eg. Sodium bicarbonate has been used up.</p>
<p>Alkalosis – the pH level of blood is above 7.5 and the acidic reserve is used up.</p>
<p>The buffer system, kidneys and lungs are unable to contain the situation of regulating the pH therefore the 2 states develop.</p>
<p>Clinically, the following scenarios are significant:</p>
<p>a) Metabolic acidosis</p>
<p>b) Metabolic alkalosis</p>
<p>c) Respiratory acidosis</p>
<p>d) Respiratory alkalosis</p>
<p>a) Metabolic acidosis</p>
<ul>
<li>Can be acute or chronic.</li>
<li>A state where there is low pH and low bicarbonate in serum</li>
</ul>
<p>Causes</p>
<ul>
<li>Ketoacidosis,</li>
<li>Lactic acidosis,</li>
<li>Late phase of salicylate poisoning,</li>
<li>Uremia</li>
<li>Methanol toxicity</li>
</ul>
<p>Signs and symptoms</p>
<p>• Headache,</p>
<p>• Confusion,</p>
<p>• Drowsiness,</p>
<p>• Increased respiratory rate and depth,</p>
<p>• Nausea, and vomiting,</p>
<p>• Peripheral vasodilation</p>
<p>• Decreased cardiac output occur when the pH falls below 7.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="management">Management<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#management" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Blood gas analysis- ph level,</li>
<li>Sodium bicarbonate level,</li>
<li>Co2 and potassium levels.</li>
<li>ECG monitoring</li>
<li>Sodium bicarbonate is administered in low pH below 7 treat the underlying cause</li>
<li>Calcium gluconate is administered in chronic metabolic acidosis to prevent tetany</li>
</ul>
<p>b) Metabolic alkalosis</p>
<p>Characterized by increased pH of above 7.5 and an increased sodium bicarbonate level</p>
<p>Causes</p>
<ul>
<li>Gastric fluid loss through vomiting or suctioning,</li>
<li>Hyperaldosteronism</li>
<li>Cushing's syndrome,</li>
<li>Hypokalemia,</li>
<li>Long term diuretic therapy,</li>
<li>Overuse of antacids,</li>
<li>Chronic ingestion of milk and calcium carbonate.</li>
</ul>
<p>Signs and symptoms</p>
<p>• Tingling of fingers,</p>
<p>• Depressed respiratory rate,</p>
<p>• Decreased motility,</p>
<p>• Hypertonic muscles ,</p>
<p>• Hypokalemia</p>
<p>Management</p>
<p>• Blood gas analysis – level of ph is increased, the bicarbonate is high, the CO2 is high since the pt is hypoventilitating</p>
<p>• Restoration of fluid volume</p>
<p>• In order for kidneys to excrete bicarbonate, NaCl is given</p>
<p>• Treat the underlying condition.</p>
<p>c) Respiratory acidosis</p>
<ul>
<li>Could be either acute or chronic</li>
<li>Characterized by pH less than 7.35</li>
</ul>
<p>Causes</p>
<ul>
<li>Conditions that lead to retention of co2 e,g.</li>
<li>Acute pulmonary edema,</li>
<li>Obstruction by foreign object,</li>
<li>Atelectasis,</li>
<li>Pneumothorax,</li>
<li>Overdose of sedatives,</li>
<li>Severe pneumonia</li>
<li>Acute respiratory distress syndrome</li>
</ul>
<p>Signs and symptoms</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>High pulse rate,</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Increased respiratory rate,</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Increased blood pressure,</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Mental cloudiness,</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Feeling of fullness in the head.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Cerebrovascular vasodilation and increased cerebral blood flow,</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Increased intracranial pressure (icp)</p>
</li>
<li></li>
</ul>
<p>Management</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Blood gas analysis</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Improve ventilation by positioning or removal of obstructing body, put pt on mechanical ventilator if very severe. Otherwise administer oxygen via mask</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Give medications which could address the cause e.g antibiotics, bronchodilator etc.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Give fluids</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Monitor on ECG</p>
</li>
<li></li>
</ul>
<p>d) Respiratory_alkalosis</p>
<p>A state when the arterial pH is greater than 7.45.</p>
<p>Causes</p>
<ul>
<li>Extreme anxiety,</li>
<li>Hypoxemia,</li>
<li>The early phase of salicylate intoxication,</li>
<li>Gram-negative bacteremia,</li>
<li>Poor setting of mechanical ventilator machine</li>
</ul>
<p>Signs and symptoms</p>
<p>• Lightheadedness - decreased cerebral blood flow,</p>
<p>• Inability to concentrate,</p>
<p>• Numbness and tingling from decreased calcium ionization,</p>
<p>• Tinnitus,</p>
<p>• Loss of consciousness,</p>
<p>• Tachycardia</p>

<ul>
<li>Investigations: Blood U&amp;E- low potassium, decreased phosphate, hypercalcemia</li>
<li>Relieve anxiety by reassurance or sedation</li>
<li>Treat underlying cause.</li>
</ul>
