<p>History taking and patient assessment</p>
<p>⮚It is important to obtain a comprehensive health history which includes assessment of risk factors in a patient with upper or lower urinary tract dysfunction.</p>
<p>⮚Data about previous health problems or diseases provides the healthcare team with useful information for evaluating the patient’s current urinary status.</p>
<p>Health history</p>
<p>⮚Obtaining a Urologic health history requires excellent communication skills because many patients are embarrassed or uncomfortable discussing genitourinary function or symptoms.</p>
<p>⮚ Use simple language that a patient understands avoid medical jargons.</p>
<p>When obtaining health history, the Nurse should enquire about the following;</p>
<p>⮚The patient’s chief concern or reason for seeking health care, the onset of the problem and its effect on patient’s quality of life</p>
<p>⮚The location, character and duration of pain if present and its relationship to voiding, factors that precipitate pain, and those that relieve it.</p>
<p>Cont.</p>
<p>⮚History of urinary tract infections including past treatment or hospitalization for any Urinary tract infection.</p>
<p>⮚Fever or chills</p>
<p>⮚Previous renal or urinary diagnostic tests or use of indwelling urinary catheters.</p>
<p>⮚Dysuria and when it occurs during voiding (at initiation or termination of voiding)</p>
<p>Cont.</p>
<p>⮚Hesitancy, straining or pain during or after urination</p>
<p>⮚Urinary incontinence (stress incontinence, urge incontinence overflow incontinence or functional incontinence)</p>
<p>⮚Haematuria or change in colour or volume of urine</p>
<p>⮚Nocturia and its date of onset.</p>
<p>⮚Renal calculi (Kidney stones) passage of stones or gravel in urine</p>
<p>Female patients</p>
<p>⮚Number and type (Vaginal or Caesarean) of deliveries</p>
<p>⮚Use of forceps, vaginal infections, discharge or irritation, contraceptive practices</p>
<p>⮚Presence or history of genital lesions or sexually transmitted diseases</p>
<p>⮚Habits; use of tobacco, alcohol or recreational drugs</p>
<p>⮚Any prescription and over the counter medications including those prescribed for renal or urinary problems</p>
<p>Gerontologic considerations</p>
<p>⮚A thorough medication history is important for elderly patients for whom the increased occurrence of chronic illness often necessitates polypharmacy (Concurrent use of multiple medications)</p>
<p>⮚Aging affects the way the body absorbs, metabolizes and excretes drugs thus placing the elderly patients at risk for adverse reactions including compromised renal function.</p>
<p>Cont.</p>
<p>⮚Other key information includes an assessment of patient’s psychosocial status level of anxiety, perceived threats to body image.</p>
<p>⮚Obtaining this information during initial and subsequent nursing assessment enables the nurse to detect special needs, misunderstandings, lack of knowledge and need for patient teaching.</p>
<p>⮚Pain, changes in voiding and gastrointestinal symptoms (Nausea and vomiting) are particularly suggestive of urinary tract disease.</p>
<p>Cont.</p>
<p>⮚Gradual kidney dysfunction can be insidious in its presentation although fatigue is a common symptom, fatigue, shortness of breath and exercise intolerance all result from a condition known as anaemia of chronic disease</p>
<p>URINARY SYSTEMS TESTS</p>
<p>Urinalysis and Urine culture</p>
<p>⮚The urinalysis provides important clinical information on kidney function and helps diagnose other diseases like diabetes.</p>
<p>⮚The urine culture determines if bacteria are present in urine as well as their strains and concentration. Urine culture and sensitivity also identify the antimicrobial therapy that is best suited for the particular strains identified.</p>
<p>Urine examination includes the following;</p>
<p>⮚Urine colour</p>
<p>⮚Urine clarity and odour</p>
<p>⮚Urine pH and specific gravity</p>
<p>⮚Tests to detect protein, glucose, and ketone bodies in urine</p>
<p>⮚Microscopic examination of urine</p>
<p>Blood Urea Nitrogen &amp; Creatinine</p>
<p>⮚End products of protein metabolism excreted by kidneys</p>
<p>• BUN 10 – 20mg/dl</p>
<p>• Creatinine 0.7 – 1.4mg/dl</p>
<p>⮚Elevated BUN reflects reduced renal perfusion (Decreased cardiac output, or intravascular fluid volume deficit (dehydration, diuretic therapy)</p>
<p>Creatinine.</p>
<blockquote>
<p>➢Is a waste product produced by muscles from breakdown of a compound called Creatine (Recycling of ATP).</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Ranges: adult men – 0.74 – 1.35mg/dl (65.4-119.3 micromoles/l)</p>
<p>Adult women – 0.59 – 1.04mg/dl (52.2-91.9 micromoles/l)</p>
<p>Creatine</p>
<p>➢Is an organic acid that helps to supply energy to cells particularly muscle cells</p>
<p>➢It is higher in men than women due to body size and muscle mass</p>
<p>Electrolytes</p>
<p>⮚Minerals in body that have an electric charge ( found in blood, tissues and other body fluids.</p>
<p>⮚They dissociate in solution and have the ability to conduct an electrical current.</p>
<p>• Sodium, Calcium, Potassium, Chloride, Phosphate and magnesium.</p>

<ul>
<li>Sodium major cation - ECF (136 – 146mEq/l)</li>
<li>Potassium major cation - ICF (3.5 -4.5mEq/l)</li>
<li>Chloride major anion (96 – 106mmol/l)</li>
</ul>
<p>Functions of electrolytes</p>
<p>• Body water balance</p>
<p>• Balance body’s acid/base (pH) level</p>
<p>• Move nutrients into cells</p>
<p>• Move wastes out of cells</p>
<p>Urine analysis</p>
<p>Routine urine analysis (Urinalysis) includes chemical analysis to detect</p>
<p>⮚Protein</p>
<p>⮚Sugar</p>
<p>⮚Ketones</p>
<p>A urine test can include three parts:</p>
<p>➤ Visual exam. The urine will be looked at for colour and clearness.</p>
<ul>
<li>Blood may make urine look red or the colour of tea or cola.</li>
<li>An infection may make urine look cloudy.</li>
<li>Foamy urine can be a sign of kidney problems.</li>
</ul>
<p>Microscopic exam.</p>
<p>• A small amount of urine will be looked at under a microscope to check for:</p>
<p>• Red blood cells</p>
<p>• White blood cells (or pus cells)</p>
<p>• Bacteria (germs), or crystals (which are formed from chemicals in the urine and may eventually get bigger and become kidney stones).</p>
<p>Dipstick test.</p>
<p>➢A dipstick is a thin, plastic stick with strips of chemicals on it.</p>
<p>• It is dipped into the urine.</p>
<p>• The strips change colour if a substance is present at a level that is above normal.</p>

<p>Acidity (pH)</p>
<p>⮚Is a measure of the amount of acid in the urine.</p>
<p>• A pH that is above normal may be a sign of kidney stones,</p>
<p>• Urinary infections,</p>
<p>• Kidney problems, or other disorders.</p>
<p>Cont.</p>
<p>And microscopic examination to detect Red and White blood cells</p>
<ul>
<li>Tests can be simply and inexpensively performed at the bedside office and can detect level of variety of substances in the urine</li>
</ul>
<p>Cont.</p>
<p>A thin strip of plastic (dipstick) impregnated with chemicals that react with substances in the urine and change colour</p>
<p>⮚Protein</p>
<p>(Proteinuria) is a sign of kidney disease but may occur often strenuous exercise such as marathon running</p>
<p>Glucose</p>
<p>➢(Sugar) in the urine (Glycosuria) can be detected by dipstick (very accurate) The most common cause is diabetes.</p>
<p>➢If blood glucose is normal but appear in urine- kidney abnormality is probably the problem</p>
<p>Ketones</p>
<p>⮚In the urine (Ketonuria) Ketones are formed when the body breaks down fat.</p>
<p>⮚Starvation, uncontrolled diabetes and alcohol intoxication can produce ketones in urine.</p>
<p>Nitrates</p>
<p>⮚In urine (Nitrituria) are also detectable by dipstick. Nitrite levels increase when bacteria are present.</p>
<p>⮚This test is used to diagnose infection quickly</p>

<li>An enzyme found in certain WBC’s in urine</li>
<li>It is a sign of inflammation mostly caused by bacterial infection</li>
<li>False negative results – urine concentrated or contains glucose, bile salts, antibiotics rifampicin or vitamin C</li>

<p>Obtaining a clean-catch urine sample</p>
<ol>
<li>Head of a man’s penis or opening of a woman’s urethra is washed.</li>
<li>First few drops of urine are allowed to flow into the toilet washing out the urethra</li>
<li>Urination is resumed and a sample is collected from the stream into a sterile cup.</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id=""><span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"></span></h1></div>
<p>Intravenous urography</p>
<p>⮚Is an X-ray technique used to display the kidneys and lower urinary tract.</p>
<p>⮚A radiopaque substance (radio contrast) which can be seen on x-rays is given intravenously. It gets concentrated in the kidneys in less than 5 minutes then an x-ray film is taken.</p>
<p>⮚It provides a picture of the kidneys and passage of radiopaque substance through the ureter into the urinary bladder.</p>
<p>⮚Sudden kidney failure occurs as an adverse effect in 1 out of 20 cases.</p>
<p>Reasons for adverse effects are unknown- Risk is higher in those who are:</p>
<p>⮚Elderly</p>
<p>⮚Have prior kidney insufficiency</p>
<p>⮚Diabetes Mellitus</p>
<p>⮚Dehydration</p>
<p>⮚Multiple Myeloma</p>
<p>Cystography</p>
<p>An x-ray image of the bladder is obtained as part of the intravenous urography</p>
<p>• Retrograde cystogram is produced when radiopaque substance is introduced through the urethra</p>
<p>• It provides more information about Urinary bladder and ureter</p>
<p>• X-ray films are taken before, during and after urination.</p>
<p>Cystoscopy</p>
<p>➢A fibre optic viewing device is threaded up through the urethra to view the bladder</p>
<p>➢Can be used to get biopsies or removal of growths or tumours</p>
<p>DISORDERS OF THE KIDNEYS</p>
<p>Pyelonephritis</p>
<p>Is a bacterial infection of the renal pelvis, tubules, and interstitial tissue of one or both kidneys.</p>
<p>Causes</p>
<p>• Involve either the upward spread of bacteria from the bladder</p>
<p>• Spread from systemic sources reaching the kidney via the bloodstream.</p>
<p>Causes</p>
<ul>
<li>Pathogenic bacteria from a bladder infection can ascend into the kidney, resulting in pyelonephritis.</li>
<li>An incompetent ureterovesical valve or obstruction occurring in the urinary tract increases the susceptibility of the kidneys to infection because static urine provides a good medium for bacterial growth</li>
</ul>
<p>Cont.</p>
<p>Pyelonephritis may be acute or chronic.</p>
<p>Acute pyelonephritis</p>
<p>• manifested by enlarged kidneys with interstitial infiltrations of inflammatory cells.</p>
<p>• Abscesses may be noted on or within the renal capsule and at the corticomedullary junction.</p>
<p>• Eventually, atrophy and destruction of tubules and the glomeruli may result.</p>
<p>Cont.</p>
<p>When pyelonephritis becomes chronic</p>
<p>⮚The kidneys become scarred</p>
<p>⮚Contracted</p>
<p>⮚Non-functioning.</p>
<p>Chronic pyelonephritis is a cause of chronic kidney disease that can result in the need for renal replacement therapies such as transplantation or dialysis</p>
<p>ACUTE PYELONEPHRITIS</p>
<p>Clinical Manifestations
• Is acutely ill with chills
• Fever
• Leukocytosis, bacteriuria, and pyuria.
• Low back pain
• flank pain
• Nausea and vomiting
• headache,</p>
<p>Cont.</p>
<p>• Malaise</p>
<p>• painful urination are common findings.</p>
<p>• Physical examination reveals pain and tenderness in the area of the costovertebral angle.</p>
<p>• Symptoms of lower urinary tract involvement, such as urgency and frequency, are common</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="assessment-and-diagnostic-findings">Assessment and Diagnostic Findings<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#assessment-and-diagnostic-findings" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>

<li>Relief of obstruction is essential to prevent the complications and eventual kidney damage.</li>
<li>An IV pyelogram may be indicated with pyelonephritis if functional and structural renal abnormalities are suspected.</li>

<p>Assessment cont.</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Radionuclide imaging with gallium citrate and indium-111 (111In)– labelled WBCs may be useful to identify sites of infection that may not be visualized on CT scan or ultrasound.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Urine culture and sensitivity tests are performed to determine the causative organism so that appropriate antimicrobial agents can be prescribed.</p>
</li>
<li></li>
</ul>
<p>Medical Management</p>
<p>Patients with acute uncomplicated pyelonephritis are most often treated on an outpatient basis if they are not exhibiting:</p>
<p>• Dehydration</p>
<p>• Nausea or vomiting</p>
<p>• Symptoms of sepsis.</p>
<p>In addition, they must be responsible and reliable to ensure that all medications will be taken as prescribed.</p>
<p>Med mnx. Cont.</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Out patients, a 2-week course of antibiotics is recommended because renal parenchymal disease is more difficult to eradicate than mucosal bladder infections.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Commonly prescribed agents include same medications prescribed for the treatment of UTIs.</p>
</li>
<li></li>
</ul>
<p>Cont.</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Pregnant women may be hospitalized for 2 or 3 days of parenteral antibiotic therapy.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Oral antibiotic agents may be prescribed once the patient is afebrile and showing clinical improvement.</p>
</li>
<li></li>
</ul>
<p>Cont.</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>A possible issue in acute pyelonephritis treatment is a chronic or recurring symptomless infection persisting for months or years.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>After the initial antibiotic regimen, the patient may need antibiotic therapy for up to 6 weeks if a relapse occurs.</p>
</li>
<li></li>
</ul>
<p>Cont.</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>A follow-up urine culture is obtained 2 weeks after completion of antibiotic therapy to document clearing of the infection.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Hydration with oral or parenteral fluids is essential in all patients with UTIs when there is adequate kidney function.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Hydration helps facilitate “flushing” of the urinary tract and reduces pain and discomfort.</p>
</li>
<li></li>
</ul>
<p>CHRONIC PYELONEPHRITIS</p>
<p>Repeated bouts of acute pyelonephritis may lead to chronic pyelonephritis.</p>
<p>Clinical Manifestations</p>
<p>• The patient with chronic pyelonephritis usually has no symptoms of infection unless an acute exacerbation occurs.</p>
<p>Ssx.</p>
<p>Noticeable signs and symptoms may include:</p>
<p>• fatigue</p>
<p>• Headache</p>
<p>• poor appetite</p>
<p>• Polyuria</p>
<p>• excessive thirst, and weight loss</p>
<p>Cont.</p>
<p>• Persistent and recurring infection may produce progressive scarring of the kidney, resulting in renal failure.</p>
<p>Assessment and Diagnostic Findings</p>
<p>• IV urogram</p>
<p>• measurements of creatinine clearance</p>
<p>Dx. cont</p>
<p>• Blood urea nitrogen</p>
<p>• Creatinine levels.</p>
<p>• Bacteria, if detected in the urine, are eradicated if possible</p>

<ul>
<li>Include end-stage renal disease (from progressive loss of nephrons secondary to chronic inflammation and scarring)</li>
<li>Hypertension</li>
<li>formation of kidney stones (from chronic infection with urea-splitting organisms)</li>
</ul>
<p>Medical Management</p>
<ul>
<li>Long-term use of prophylactic antimicrobial therapy may help limit recurrence of infections and renal scarring.</li>
<li>Impaired renal function alters the excretion of antimicrobial agents and necessitates careful monitoring of renal function, especially if the medications are potentially toxic to the kidneys</li>
</ul>
<p>Nursing Management</p>
<p>• The patient may require hospitalization or may be treated as an outpatient.</p>
<p>• If a patient requires hospitalization</p>
<p>• fluid intake and output are carefully measured and recorded.</p>
<p>• Unless contraindicated, 3 to 4 L of fluids per day is encouraged to dilute the urine, decrease burning on urination, and prevent dehydration.</p>
<p>Nursing care</p>
<p>• The nurse assesses the patient’s temperature every 4 hours</p>
<p>• administers antipyretic and antibiotic agents as prescribed.</p>
<p>• Symptomatic patients are often more comfortable on bed rest.</p>
<p>Patient teaching</p>
<p>• focuses on prevention of further infection by consuming adequate fluids</p>
<p>• emptying the bladder regularly</p>
<p>• and performing recommended perineal hygiene.</p>
<p>• The importance of taking antimicrobial medications exactly as prescribed is stressed,</p>
<p>• need for keeping follow-up appointments</p>
<p>RENAL FAILURE</p>
<p>• Results when the kidneys cannot remove the body’s metabolic wastes or perform their regulatory functions.</p>
<p>• The substances normally excreted in urine accumulate in the body fluids as a result of impaired renal excretion</p>
<p>• Affecting endocrine and metabolic functions as well as fluid, electrolyte, and acid–base disturbances.</p>
<p>Cont.</p>
<ul>
<li>Renal failure is a systemic disease and is a final common pathway of many different kidney and urinary tract diseases.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="acute-renal-failure-arf-ckd">Acute renal failure (ARF) CKD.<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#acute-renal-failure-arf-ckd" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Is a rapid loss of renal function due to damage to the kidneys.</li>
<li>Potentially life-threatening metabolic complications can occur, including metabolic acidosis &amp; fluid and electrolyte imbalances.</li>
</ul>
<p>Cont.</p>
<p>⮚Treatment is aimed at:</p>
<p>• Replacing renal function temporarily to minimize potentially lethal complications</p>
<p>• Reduce potential causes of increased renal injury with the goal of minimizing long-term loss of renal function.</p>
<p>Cont.</p>
<ul>
<li>A widely accepted criterion for ARF is a 50% or greater increase in serum creatinine above baseline (normal creatinine is less than 1.0 mg/dL).</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="akd-cont">AKD Cont.<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#akd-cont" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Urine volume may be normal, or changes may occur.</li>
</ul>
<p>Possible changes include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Oliguria (less than 500 mL/day)</li>
<li>Non-oliguria (greater than 800 mL/day)</li>
<li>Anuria (less than 50 mL/day)</li>
</ul>
<p>Pathophysiology</p>
<p>Pathogenesis of ARF and oliguria is not always known, many times there is a specific underlying problem.</p>
<p>Conditions that reduce blood flow to the kidney and impair kidney function:</p>
<p>(1)Hypovolemia</p>
<p>(2)Hypotension</p>
<p>Cont.</p>
<p>(3) Reduced cardiac output and heart failure</p>
<p>(4)Obstruction of the kidney or lower urinary tract by tumour, blood clot, or kidney stone</p>
<p>(5) Bilateral obstruction of the renal arteries or veins.</p>
<p>If these conditions are treated and corrected before the kidneys are permanently damaged, the increased BUN and creatinine levels, oliguria, and other signs may be reversed.</p>
<p>Cont</p>
<p>Renal stones are not a common cause of ARF, some types may increase the risk for ARF.</p>
<p>• Primary struvite stones, and infection-related urolithiasis associated with anatomic and functional urinary tract anomalies</p>
<p>• Spinal cord injury may cause recurrent bouts of obstruction as well as crystal specific damage to tubular epithelial cells and interstitial renal cells.</p>
<p>Categories of Acute Renal Failure</p>
<p>⮚Pre-renal (hypoperfusion of kidney)</p>
<p>⮚Intrarenal (actual damage to kidney tissue)</p>
<p>⮚Post-renal (obstruction to urine flow).</p>
<p>Pre-renal ARF, which occurs in 60% to 70% of cases, is the result of impaired blood flow that leads to hypoperfusion of the kidney and a decrease in the GFR. Intrarenal ARF is the result of actual</p>
<p>Cont.</p>
<p>parenchymal damage to the glomeruli or kidney tubules.</p>
<p>Acute tubular necrosis (ATN) is the most common type of intrinsic ARF.</p>
<p>Characteristics of ATN are:</p>
<p>• Intratubular obstruction</p>
<p>• Tubular back leak (abnormal reabsorption of filtrate and decreased urine flow through the tubule)</p>
<p>Cont.</p>
<ul>
<li>Vasoconstriction, and changes in glomerular permeability.</li>
</ul>
<p>These processes result in</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Decrease of GFR</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Progressive azotemia (Elevated levels of urea and other compounds in the blood)</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Fluid and electrolyte imbalances.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Cont.</p>
<ul>
<li>CKD, diabetes, heart failure, hypertension, and cirrhosis can lead to ATN.</li>
<li>Postrenal ARF usually results from obstruction distal to the kidney.</li>
</ul>

<li>Diuresis</li>
<li>Recovery.</li>

<p>Cont.</p>

<p>The diuresis period is marked by:</p>
<p>• Gradual increase in urine output, which signals that glomerular filtration has started to recover.</p>

<p>• Although a permanent 1% to 3% reduction in the GFR is common, it is not clinically significant.</p>

<p>• The patient may appear critically ill and lethargic.</p>
<p>• The skin and mucous membranes are dry from dehydration.</p>
<p>Cont.</p>
<p>Central nervous system signs and symptoms include:
• Drowsiness
• Headache
• muscle twitching
• seizures.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id=""><span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"></span></h1></div>
<p>Cont.</p>
<p>⮚Patients with intra-renal azotemia usually have urinary sodium levels greater than 40 mEq/L with urinary casts and other cellular debris.</p>
<p>⮚Ultrasonography scan may show evidence of anatomic changes.</p>
<p>Cont</p>
<p>⮚The BUN level increases steadily at a rate dependent on the degree of catabolism (breakdown of protein), renal perfusion, and protein intake.</p>
<p>⮚Serum creatinine levels are useful in monitoring kidney function and disease progression and increase with glomerular damage.</p>
<p>Cont.</p>
<p>With a decline in the GFR, oliguria, and anuria, patients are at high risk for hyperkalemia.</p>
<p>⮚Protein catabolism results in the release of cellular potassium into the body fluids, causing severe hyperkalemia (high serum potassium levels).</p>
<p>⮚Hyperkalemia may lead to dysrhythmias, such as ventricular tachycardia and cardiac arrest.</p>
<p>Cont.</p>
<p>Sources of potassium include:</p>
<p>• normal tissue catabolism</p>
<p>• dietary intake</p>
<p>• blood in the GI tract, or blood transfusion</p>
<p>• other sources (eg, IV infusions, potassium penicillin, and extracellular shift in response to metabolic acidosis).</p>
<p>Cont.</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Progressive metabolic acidosis occurs in renal failure because patients cannot eliminate the daily metabolic load of acid-type substances produced by the normal metabolic processes.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>In addition, normal renal buffering mechanisms fail. This is reflected by a decrease in the serum CO2-combining power and blood pH.</p>
</li>
<li></li>
</ul>
<p>Cont.</p>
<ul>
<li>There may be an increase in blood phosphate concentrations</li>
<li>Calcium levels may be low due to decreased absorption of calcium from the intestine and as a compensatory mechanism for the elevated blood phosphate levels.</li>
</ul>

<p>• comorbid conditions</p>
<p>• pre-existing renal and vascular diseases.</p>
<p>A careful history is obtained to identify exposure to nephrotoxic agents or environmental toxins.</p>
<p>Cont.</p>

<p>• and at least twice a week while the patient is receiving them</p>

</li>
<li></li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="medical-management">Medical Management<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#medical-management" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Objectives of treatment of ARF are to:</p>
<ul>
<li>Restore normal chemical balance</li>
<li>Prevent complications until repair of renal tissue</li>
<li>and restoration of renal function can occur.</li>
</ul>
<p>Management includes</p>
<p>• Eliminating the underlying cause</p>
<p>• Maintaining fluid balance</p>
<p>• Avoiding fluid excesses</p>
<p>• when indicated, providing renal replacement therapy</p>
<p>Mnx. Cont.</p>
<p>Pre-renal azotaemia is treated by:</p>
<ul>
<li>optimizing renal perfusion
Post-renal failure is treated by:</li>
<li>relieving the obstruction.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="mnx-cont">Mnx cont.<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#mnx-cont" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Intrarenal azotemia is treated with</p>
<ol>
<li>Supportive therapy</li>
<li>Removal of causative agents</li>
<li>Aggressive management of pre-renal and postrenal failure, and avoidance of associated risk factors. Shock and infection, if present, are treated promptly</li>
</ol>
<p>Maintenance of fluid balance is based on:</p>
<ul>
<li>Daily body weight</li>
<li>Serial measurements of central venous pressure</li>
<li>Serum and urine concentrations</li>
<li>Fluid losses</li>
<li>Blood pressure</li>
</ul>
<p>Cont.</p>
<p>• The clinical status of the patient.</p>
<p>• The parenteral and oral intake and the output of urine</p>
<p>• Gastric drainage</p>
<p>• Stools</p>
<p>• Wound drainage</p>
<p>• and perspiration are calculated and are used as the basis for fluid replacement.</p>
<p>Cont.</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>The insensible fluid produced through the normal metabolic processes and lost through the skin and lungs is also considered in fluid management</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Fluid excesses can be detected by the clinical findings of</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Dyspnoea</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Tachycardia</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Distended neck veins.</p>
</li>
<li></li>
</ul>
<p>Cont.</p>
<p>• The patient’s lungs are auscultated for moist crackles.</p>
<p>• Because pulmonary Oedema may be caused by excessive administration of parenteral fluids, extreme caution must be used to prevent fluid overload.</p>
<p>• The development of generalized oedema is assessed by examining the pre-sacral and pretibial areas several times daily. Mannitol (Osmitrol), furosemide (Lasix), or ethacrynic acid (Edecrin) may be prescribed to initiate diuresis.</p>
<p>Cont.</p>
<p>Adequate renal blood flow in patients with pre-renal causes of ARF may be restored by:</p>
<p>• IV fluids</p>
<p>• Transfusions of blood products.</p>
<p>If ARF is caused by hypovolemia secondary to hypoproteinemia:</p>
<p>• an infusion of albumin may be prescribed.</p>
<p>.</p>
<p>Cont.</p>
<p>Dialysis may be initiated to prevent complications of ARF, such as:</p>
<p>• Hyperkalemia</p>
<p>• Metabolic acidosis</p>
<p>• Pericarditis</p>
<p>• Pulmonary Oedema.</p>
<p>Hemodialysis (a procedure that circulates the patient’s blood through a dialyzer to remove waste products and excess fluid)</p>
<ul>
<li>Peritoneal dialysis (PD) (a procedure that uses the patient’s peritoneal membrane [the lining of the peritoneal cavity] as the semipermeable membrane to exchange fluid and solutes), or a variety of continuous renal replacement therapies (CRRTs)</li>
</ul>
<p>Pharmacologic Therapy</p>
<p>• Hyperkalaemia is the most life-threatening of the fluid and electrolyte changes that occur in patients with renal disturbances.</p>
<p>The patient is monitored for hyperkaelemia through serial serum electrolyte levels (potassium value greater than 5.0 mEq/L [5 mmol/L])</p>
<p>• ECG changes (tall, tented, or peaked T waves)</p>
<p>• Changes in clinical status.</p>
<p>Other symptoms of hyperkalaemia include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Irritability</li>
<li>abdominal cramping</li>
<li>Diarrhoea</li>
<li>Paresthesia</li>
<li>and generalized muscle weakness.</li>
</ul>
<p>Cont</p>
<p>Muscle weakness may present as:</p>
<p>• slurred speech</p>
<p>• difficulty breathing</p>
<p>• paresthesia and paralysis.</p>
<p>As the potassium level increases, both cardiac and other muscular function declines, making this a true medical emergency.</p>
<p>Med mnx.</p>
<ul>
<li>The elevated potassium levels may be reduced by administering cation-exchange resins (sodium polystyrene sulfonate [Kayexalate]) orally or by retention enema.</li>
<li>Kayexalate works by exchanging sodium ions for potassium ions in the intestinal tract.</li>
<li>Sorbitol may be administered in combination with Kayexalate to induce a diarrhoea</li>
</ul>
<blockquote>
<p>⮚If the patient is hemodynamically unstable (low blood pressure, changes in mental status, dysrhythmia)</p>
<p>• IV dextrose 50%</p>
<p>• Insulin, and calcium replacement may be administered to shift potassium back into the cells.</p>
<p>• Albuterol sulfate (Ventolin HFA) by nebulizer can lower plasma potassium concentration by 0.5 to 1.5 mEq/L.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The shift of potassium into the intracellular space is temporary, so arrangements for dialysis need to be made on an emergent basis.</p>
<ul>
<li>Since many medications are eliminated through the kidneys, dosages must be reduced when a patient has ARF.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id=""><span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"></span></h1></div>
<p>⮚Examples of commonly used agents that require adjustment are:</p>
<ul>
<li>antibiotic medications (especially aminoglycosides),</li>
<li>Digoxin</li>
<li>ACE inhibitors</li>
<li>magnesium-containing agents</li>
</ul>
<p>Diuretic agents are often used to control fluid volume, but they have not been shown to improve recovery from ARF.</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>In patients with severe acidosis, the arterial blood gases and serum bicarbonate levels (CO2-combining power) must be monitored because the patient may require sodium bicarbonate therapy or dialysis.</p>
</li>
<li></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>
<p>If respiratory problems develop, appropriate ventilatory measures must be instituted.</p>
<ul>
<li>The elevated serum phosphate level may be controlled with phosphate-binding agents (eg, calcium or lanthanum carbonate) that help prevent a continuing rise in serum phosphate levels by decreasing the absorption of phosphate from the intestinal tract</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Nutritional Therapy</p>
<p>ARF causes severe nutritional imbalances due to:</p>
<p>• Nausea and vomiting (contribute to inadequate dietary intake)</p>
<p>• Impaired glucose use and protein synthesis</p>
<p>• Increased tissue catabolism.</p>
<p>Cont.</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>The patient is weighed daily and loses 0.2 to 0.5 kg (0.5 to 1 lb) daily if the nitrogen balance is negative (ie, caloric intake falls below caloric requirements).</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>If the patient gains or does not lose weight or develops hypertension, fluid retention should be suspected.</p>
</li>
<li></li>
</ul>
<p>Nutritional support is based on the</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Underlying cause of ARF</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The catabolic response</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The type and frequency of renal replacement therapy</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Comorbidities</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Nutritional status.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Replacement of dietary proteins is individualized to provide the maximum benefit and minimize uremic symptoms</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Caloric requirements are met with high-carbohydrate meals, because carbohydrates have a protein-sparing effect (ie, in a high-carbohydrate diet</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Protein is not used for meeting energy requirements but is “spared” for growth and tissue healing).</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Foods and fluids containing potassium or phosphorus (eg, bananas, citrus fruits and juices, coffee) are restricted</p>
</li>
<li></li>
</ul>

<p>The nurse:</p>
<ul>
<li>Monitors for complications</li>
<li>Participates in emergency treatment of fluid and electrolyte imbalances, assesses the patient's progress and response to treatment</li>
<li>Provides physical and emotional support.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>
<p>The nurse keeps family members informed about the patient’s condition</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Helps them understand the treatments,</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Provides psychological support.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Although the development of ARF may be the most serious problem, the nurse continues to provide nursing care indicated for the primary disorder (eg, burns, shock, trauma, obstruction of the urinary tract).</p>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Monitoring Fluid and Electrolyte Balance</p>
<p>• The nurse monitors the patient’s serum electrolyte levels and physical indicators of these complications during all phases of the disorder.</p>
<p>• Hyperkalaemia is the most immediate life-threatening imbalance seen in ARF.</p>
<p>• Parenteral fluids, all oral intake, and all medications are screened carefully to ensure that hidden sources of potassium are not inadvertently administered or consumed.</p>
<p>IV solutions must be carefully selected based on the patient’s fluid and electrolyte status.</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>The patient’s cardiac function and musculoskeletal status are monitored closely for signs of hyperkalaemia.</p>
</li>
<li></li>
</ul>
<p>The nurse monitors fluid status by paying careful attention to fluid intake (IV medications should be administered in the smallest volume possible)</p>
<ul>
<li>Urine output</li>
<li>Apparent Oedema</li>
<li>Distention of the jugular veins</li>
<li>Alterations in heart sounds and breath sounds</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Increasing difficulty in breathing.</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Accurate daily weights, as well as I&amp;O records, are essential.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Indicators of deteriorating fluid and electrolyte status are reported immediately to the physician, and preparation is made for emergency treatment.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Severe fluid and electrolyte disturbances may be treated with hemodialysis, PD, or CRRT</p>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p>INTERSTITIAL CYSTITIS (PAINFUL BLADDER SYNDROME)</p>
<p>Definition</p>
<p>⮚Is an inflammatory condition of the bladder wall that is characterised by uncomfortable bladder pressure, bladder pain and sometimes pain in the pelvis.</p>
<p>Cause</p>
<p>⮚Is unknown</p>
<p>⮚Scientists believe that it may be as a result of other bowel conditions such as fibromyalgia or irritable bowel system.</p>
<p>Risk Factors</p>
<p>➢Age: is diagnosed a 40s</p>
<p>➢Gender: Is more related to females more than males</p>
<p>➢Other conditions: Irritable bowel syndrome and fibromyalgia</p>
<p>Clinical manifestations</p>
<p>⮚ Severe irritable voiding (day and night frequency, nocturia and urgency)</p>
<p>⮚ Painful coitus</p>
<p>⮚ Painful urination</p>
<p>Assessment and Diagnostic findings</p>
<p>There is no specific diagnostic test since the symptoms resemble those of other conditions</p>
<p>⮚Doctors have to rule out other treatable conditions before considering a diagnosis of Interstitial Cystitis.</p>
<p>⮚The diagnostic tests that can help rule out other diseases are:</p>
<p>• Urinalysis, urine culture, Cystoscopy, biopsy of the bladder walls and urethra.</p>
<p>Medical management</p>
<p>The current treatment is aimed at relieving the symptoms</p>
<p>⮚Pentosan polysulfate sodium (elmiron) 100mg TDS. Repairs the defects that might have developed in the lining of the urinary bladder.</p>
<p>⮚Amitriptyline is a tricyclic antidepressant that helps reduce pain, increase bladder capacity and reduce urinary frequency and urgency.</p>
<p>Electrical nerve stimulation:</p>
<ul>
<li>Can be used to stimulate the nerves to the bladder.</li>
<li>Can be done over the skin (transcutaneous nerve stimulation) or through an implant.</li>
</ul>
<p>Surgical procedure:</p>
<p>⮚Bladder cystectomy</p>
<p>⮚Bladder augmentation-enlarging the bladder</p>
<p>⮚Fulguration and resection: Fulguration involves burning of the hunner’s ulcers with electricity or a laser. When the affected area heals, the dead tissues and ulcers fall off and a new tissue grows.</p>
<p>⮚Resection is cutting around and removing the ulcers</p>

<p>HYDRONEPHROSIS</p>
<p>Definition</p>
<p>⮚Is a condition where one or both kidneys become stretched and swollen as a result of build-up of urine inside the kidney(s)</p>
<p>Causes</p>
<p>⮚A blockage: prevent urine from draining out of the kidneys</p>
<p>⮚A reflux: Urine may flow back into the kidneys from the urinary bladder</p>
<p>Pathophysiology</p>
<p>⮚Obstruction to the normal flow of the urine to flow back, resulting in increased pressure in the kidney.</p>
<p>⮚If the obstruction is in the urethra or the bladder, the back pressure affects both kidneys</p>
<p>⮚If the obstruction is in one of the ureters or because of kidney stone or a kink, only one kidney is affected.</p>
<p>Cont.</p>
<blockquote>
<p>➢Whatever the type of obstruction (kidney stones or tumours) leading to accumulation of urine in the renal pelvis, it distends the pelvis and the calyces, leading to atrophy of the kidney.</p>
<p>➢This later results to compensatory hypertrophy (one kidney undergoes gradual destruction, while the other kidney gradually enlarges, hence renal failure.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Risk factors</p>
<p>• Pregnancy; during pregnancy, the enlargement of the foetus can sometimes place pressure on the ureters
• Endometriosis; is a condition where the tissue grows outside the uterus hence causing abnormal growth which may sometimes disrupt urinary flow.
• Recurrent Urinary tract infections and narrowing of the ureters; leads to build-up of pressure
• Enlarged prostate; leads to narrowing of the urethra hence pressure build up.</p>

<p>• Unexplained itching</p>
<p>• Fever</p>
<p>• Haematuria</p>
<p>• Abdominal mass</p>
<p>• Urinary tract infections</p>
<p>Diagnosis</p>
<ul>
<li>Take a comprehensive history, noting the signs and symptoms</li>
<li>Do a physical exam (examine the pelvis and the rectum to check for blockage)</li>
<li>Urine tests: (to check for blood, protein and other evidence of kidney damage)</li>
</ul>

<p>Urolithiasis and nephrolithiasis refer to stones (calculi) in the urinary tract and kidney, respectively.</p>
<ul>
<li>Urinary stones account for more than 320,000 hospital admissions each year.</li>
<li>The occurrence of urinary stones occurs predominantly in the third to fifth decades of life and affects men more than women. About half of patients with a single renal stone have another episode</li>
</ul>
<p>Pathophysiology</p>
<ul>
<li>Stones are formed in the urinary tract when urinary concentrations of substances such as calcium oxalate, calcium phosphate, and uric acid increase.</li>
<li>Referred to as super-saturation, this is dependent on the amount of the substance, ionic strength, and pH of the urine.</li>
</ul>

<li>Stones may be found anywhere from the kidney to the bladder and may vary in size from minute granular deposits, called sand or gravel, to bladder stones as large as an orange.</li>

<p>Cont.</p>

<li>
<p>Stone formation is not clearly understood, and there are a number of theories about their causes.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>One theory is that there is a deficiency of substances that normally prevent crystallization in the urine, such as citrate, magnesium, nephrocalcin, and uropontin.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Another theory relates to fluid volume status of the patient</p>
</li>
<li></li>
</ul>
<p>anatomic derangements such as polycystic kidney disease, horseshoe kidneys, chronic strictures, and medullary sponge disease.</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Urinary stone formation can occur in patients with inflammatory bowel disease and in those with an ileostomy or bowel resection because these patients absorb more oxalate.</p>

<li>

<p>Clinical Manifestations</p>
<p>Signs and symptoms of stones in the urinary system depend on the presence of</p>
<p>• Obstruction,</p>
<p>• Infection</p>
<p>• Oedema.</p>
<p>• When stones block the flow of urine, obstruction develops, producing an increase in hydrostatic pressure and distending the renal pelvis and proximal ureter.</p>
<p>Cont.</p>
<ul>
<li>Infection (pyelonephritis and UTI with chills, fever, and frequency) can be a contributing factor with struvite stones.</li>
<li>Some stones cause few, if any, symptoms while slowly destroying the functional units (nephrons) of the kidney;</li>
<li>others cause excruciating pain and discomfort</li>
</ul>
<p>Stones in the renal pelvis may be associated with an intense, deep ache in the costovertebral region.</p>
<p>• Haematuria is often present;</p>
<p>• pyuria may also be noted.</p>
<p>• Pain originating in the renal area radiates anteriorly and downward toward the bladder in the female and toward the testis in the male.</p>

<ul>
<li>
<p>Diarrhoea and abdominal discomfort may occur.</p>
</li>
<li>

</li>
<li></li>
</ul>
<p>Stones lodged in the ureter (ureteral obstruction) cause acute, excruciating, colicky, wavelike pain, radiating down the thigh and to the genitalia.</p>

<p>• This group of symptoms is called ureteral colic.</p>
<p>*Colic is mediated by prostaglandin E, a substance that increases ureteral contractility and renal blood flow and that leads to increased intra-ureteral pressure and pain.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="nutritional-therapy">Nutritional Therapy<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#nutritional-therapy" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Nutritional therapy plays an important role in preventing renal stone</li>
<li>Fluid intake is the mainstay of most medical therapy for renal stones.</li>
</ul>
<p>Preventing Kidney Stones</p>
<p>• Avoid protein intake; usually protein is restricted to 60 g/day to decrease urinary excretion of calcium and uric acid.</p>
<p>• A sodium intake of 3 to 4 g/day is recommended. Table salt and high-sodium foods should be reduced, because sodium competes with calcium for reabsorption in the kidneys.</p>
<p>Pt. education/Prevention</p>
<p>• Low-calcium diets are not generally recommended, except for true absorptive hypercalciuria.</p>
<p>Evidence shows that limiting calcium, especially in women, can lead to osteoporosis and does not prevent renal stones.</p>
<p>• Avoid intake of oxalate-containing foods (eg, spinach, strawberries, rhubarb, tea, peanuts, wheat bran</p>
<p>Cont.</p>
<p>• Unless fluids are contraindicated, patients with renal stones should drink eight to ten 8-ounce glasses of water daily or have IV fluids prescribed to keep the urine dilute.</p>
<p>• A urine output exceeding 2 L/day is advisable</p>
<p>Cont.</p>
<p>• During the day, drink fluids (ideally water) every 1 to 2 hours.</p>
<p>• Drink two glasses of water at bedtime and an additional glass at each night time awakening to prevent urine from becoming too concentrated during the night.</p>
<p>• Avoid activities leading to sudden increases in environmental temperatures that may cause excessive sweating and dehydration.</p>
<p>• Contact your primary health care provider at the first sign of a urinary tract infection.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="calcium-stones">Calcium Stones<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#calcium-stones" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Medications such as ammonium chloride may be used, and if increased parathormone production (resulting in increased serum calcium levels in blood and urine) is a factor in the formation of stones, therapy with thiazide diuretics may be beneficial in reducing the calcium loss in the urine and lowering the elevated parathormone levels.</li>
</ul>
<p>Uric Acid Stones</p>
<ul>
<li>For uric acid stones, the patient is placed on a low-purine diet to reduce the excretion of uric acid in the urine.</li>
<li>Foods high in purine (shellfish, anchovies, asparagus, mushrooms, and organ meats) are avoided, and other proteins may be limited.</li>
<li>Allopurinol (Zyloprim) may be prescribed to reduce serum acid levels and urinary acid excretion</li>
</ul>
<p>Cystine Stones</p>
<p>• A low-protein diet is prescribed, the urine is alkalinized, and fluid intake is increased.</p>
<p>Oxalate Stones</p>
<p>• A dilute urine is maintained and the intake of oxalate is limited. Many foods contain oxalate</p>
<p>• Foods that increase the urinary excretion of oxalate. These include spinach, strawberries, rhubarb, chocolate, tea, peanuts, and wheat bran</p>
<p>Interventional Procedures</p>
<ul>
<li>If the stone does not pass spontaneously or if complications occur, common interventions include endoscopic or other procedures. For example,</li>
<li>Ureteroscopy,</li>
<li>extracorporeal shock wave lithotripsy (ESWL), or</li>
<li>Endourologic (percutaneous) stone removal may be necessary</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id=""><span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"></span></h1></div>
<p>Cont.</p>
<ul>
<li>Ureteroscopy involves first visualizing the stone and then destroying it.</li>
<li>Access to the stone is accomplished by inserting a ureteroscope into the ureter and then inserting a laser, electrohydraulic lithotriptor, or ultrasound device through the ureteroscope to fragment and remove the stones.</li>
<li>A stent may be inserted and left in place for 48 hours or more after the procedure to keep the ureter</li>
</ul>
<p>Cont.</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>ESWL is a non-invasive procedure used to break up stones in the calyx of the kidney.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>After the stones are fragmented to the size of grains of sand, the remnants of the stones are spontaneously voided.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>In ESWL, a high-energy amplitude of pressure, or shock wave, is generated by the abrupt release of energy and transmitted through water and soft tissues.</p>
</li>
<li></li>
</ul>
<p>Cont.</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>When the shock wave encounters a substance of different intensity (a renal stone), a compression wave causes the surface of the stone to fragment.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Repeated shock waves focused on the stone eventually reduce it to many small pieces that are excreted in the urine.</p>
</li>
<li></li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="renal-stones">Renal Stones<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#renal-stones" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p><img src="assets/images/image-20251226-c66a17a3.jpeg" alt="img-10.jpeg"></p>
<p>Endourologic methods of stone removal</p>
<p>• may be used to extract renal calculi that cannot be removed by other procedures.</p>
<p>• A percutaneous nephrostomy or a percutaneous nephrolithotomy (which are similar procedures) may be performed.</p>
<p>A nephroscope is introduced through a percutaneous route into the renal parenchyma</p>
<p>Electrohydraulic lithotripsy</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>is a similar method in which an electrical discharge is used to create a hydraulic shock wave to break up the stone.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>A probe is passed through the cystoscope, and the tip of the lithotriptor is placed near the stone.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The strength of the discharge and pulse frequency can be varied. This procedure is performed under topical anesthesia.</p>
</li>
<li></li>
</ul>
<p>Cont.</p>
<p>• After the stone is extracted, the percutaneous nephrostomy tube is left in place for a time to ensure that the ureter is not obstructed by oedema or blood clots.</p>
<p>• The most common complications are</p>
<p>⮚Haemorrhage</p>
<p>⮚Infection</p>
<p>⮚urinary extravasation. After the tube is removed, the nephrostomy tract usually closes spontaneously</p>
<p>Chemolysis</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Stone dissolution using infusions of chemical solutions (eg, alkylating agents, acidifying agents) for the purpose of dissolving the stone, is an alternative treatment sometimes used in patients who are at risk for complications with other types of therapy, or who have stones (struvite) that dissolve easily.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>A percutaneous nephrostomy is performed, and the warm chemical solution is allowed to flow continuously onto the stone. The solution exits the renal collecting system by means of the ureter or the nephrostomy tube.</p>
</li>
<li></li>
</ul>
<p>Surgical Management</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Was the major mode of therapy before the advent of lithotripsy. However, today, surgery is performed in only 1% to 2% of patients.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Surgical intervention is indicated if the stone does not respond to other forms of treatment.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>It may also be performed to correct anatomic abnormalities within the kidney to improve urinary drainage.</p>
</li>
<li></li>
</ul>
<p>Cont.</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>If the stone is in the kidney, the surgery performed may be a nephrolithotomy (incision into the kidney with removal of the stone)</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Nephrectomy, if the kidney is non-functional secondary to infection or hydronephrosis.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Stones in the kidney pelvis are removed by a pyelolithotomy</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>those in the ureter by ureterolithotomy</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>those in the bladder by cystotomy</p>
</li>
<li></li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="assessment">Assessment<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#assessment" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>The patient with suspected renal stones is assessed for</li>
<li>➤ pain and discomfort</li>
<li>➤ Nausea</li>
<li>➤ Vomiting</li>
<li>➤ Diarrhoea</li>
<li>➤ abdominal distention.</li>
</ul>
<p>Cont.</p>
<p>• The severity and location of pain are determined, along with any radiation of the pain.</p>
<p>Nursing assessment also includes observing for signs and symptoms of</p>
<p>• UTI (chills, fever, frequency, and hesitancy)</p>
<p>• obstruction (frequent urination of small amounts, oliguria, or anuria).</p>
<p>• The urine is inspected for blood and is strained for stones or gravel.</p>
<p>Cont.</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>The history focuses on factors that predispose the patient to urinary tract stones or that may have precipitated the current episode of renal or ureteral colic.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The patient’s knowledge about renal stones and measures to prevent their occurrence or recurrence is also assessed.</p>
</li>
<li></li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="diagnosis-nursing-diagnoses-based-on-the-assessment-data">Diagnosis Nursing Diagnoses Based on the assessment data<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#diagnosis-nursing-diagnoses-based-on-the-assessment-data" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>The nursing diagnoses in the patient with renal stones may include the following:</li>
<li>Acute pain related to inflammation, obstruction, and abrasion of the urinary tract</li>
<li>Deficient knowledge regarding prevention of recurrence of renal stones</li>
</ul>
<p>Cont.</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Collaborative Problems/Potential Complications Based on assessment data,</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>potential complications that may develop include the following:</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Infection and urosepsis (from UTI and pyelonephritis)</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Obstruction of the urinary tract by a stone or oedema with subsequent acute renal failure</p>
</li>
<li></li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="planning-and-goals">Planning and Goals<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#planning-and-goals" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>The major goals for the patient may include</p>
<ul>
<li>relief of pain and discomfort,</li>
<li>prevention of recurrence of renal stones</li>
<li>and absence of complications.</li>
</ul>
<p>Nursing Interventions</p>
<p>Relieving Pain
• Severe acute pain is often the presenting symptom of a patient with renal and urinary calculi and requires immediate attention.
• Opioid analgesic agents (IV or intramuscular) may be prescribed and administered to provide rapid relief along with an IV NSAID.
• The patient is encouraged and assisted to assume a position of comfort</p>
<p>If activity brings pain relief, the patient is assisted to ambulate.</p>
<ul>
<li>The pain level is monitored closely, and an increase in severity is reported promptly to the physician so that relief can be provided and additional treatment initiated</li>
</ul>
<p>Monitoring and Managing Potential Complications</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Increased fluid intake is encouraged to prevent dehydration and increase hydrostatic pressure within the urinary tract to promote passage of the stone.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>If the patient cannot take adequate fluids orally, IV fluids are prescribed.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The total urine output and patterns of voiding are monitored.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Ambulation is encouraged as a means of moving the stone through the urinary tract.</p>
</li>
<li></li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="cont">Cont.<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#cont" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Renal stones increase the risk of:</li>
<li>infection</li>
<li>Sepsis</li>
<li>obstruction of the urinary tract</li>
</ul>
<p>Cont.</p>
<p>The patient is instructed to report:
• decreased urine volume
• bloody or cloudy urine
• Fever
• and pain</p>
<p>Cont.</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Patients with calculi require frequent nursing observation to detect the spontaneous passage of a stone.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The patient is instructed to immediately report any sudden increases in pain intensity because of the possibility of a stone fragment obstructing a ureter.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Vital signs, including temperature, are monitored closely to detect early signs of infection.</p>
</li>
<li></li>
</ul>
<p>Cont.</p>
<ul>
<li>UTIs may be associated with renal stones due to an obstruction from the stone or from the stone itself.</li>
<li>All infections should be treated with the appropriate antibiotic agent before efforts are made to dissolve the stone.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id=""><span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"></span></h1></div>
<p>Promoting Home and Community-Based Care</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Because the risk of recurring renal stones is high, the nurse provides education about the causes of kidney stones and recommendations to prevent their recurrence.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The patient is encouraged to follow a regimen to avoid further stone formation, including maintaining a high fluid intake because stones form more readily in concentrated urine.</p>
</li>
<li></li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>
<p>A patient who has shown a tendency to form stones should drink enough fluid to excrete greater than 2000 mL (preferably 3000 to 4000 mL) of urine every 24 hours.</p>
<ul>
<li>Urine cultures may be performed every 1 to 2 months the first year and periodically thereafter.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Cont.</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Recurrent UTI is treated vigorously.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Because prolonged immobilization slows renal drainage and alters calcium metabolism, increased mobility is encouraged whenever possible.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>In addition, excessive ingestion of vitamins (especially vitamin D) and minerals is discouraged.</p>
</li>
<li></li>
</ul>

<p>Expected Patient Outcomes</p>
<ol>
<li>
<p>Reports relief of pain</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>States increased knowledge of health-seeking behaviours to prevent recurrence</p>
</li>
</ol>

<p>Cont.</p>
<p>b) Participates in appropriate activity</p>
<p>c) Consumes diet prescribed to reduce dietary factors predisposing to stone formation</p>
<p>d) Recognizes symptoms (fever, chills, flank pain, haematuria) to be reported to health care provider</p>
