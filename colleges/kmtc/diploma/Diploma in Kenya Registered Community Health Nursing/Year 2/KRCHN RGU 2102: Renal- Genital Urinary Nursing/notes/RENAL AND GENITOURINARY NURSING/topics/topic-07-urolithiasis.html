üîç Overview & Pathophysiology

Definitions:
‚Ä¢ Urolithiasis: Stones (calculi) in the urinary tract.
‚Ä¢ Nephrolithiasis: Stones in the kidney.

Stones are formed when urinary concentrations of substances such as calcium oxalate, calcium phosphate, and uric acid increase. This process, referred to as super-saturation, depends on the amount of substance, ionic strength, and pH of the urine.

Formation Theories

  • Deficiency of Inhibitors: Lack of citrate, magnesium, nephrocalcin, and uropontin (substances that normally prevent crystallization).
  • Fluid Volume: Dehydration leads to concentrated urine.
  • Anatomic Derangements: Polycystic kidney disease, strictures, medullary sponge disease.

Signs depend on the presence of obstruction, infection, and edema.

‚ö°

Types of Pain

Renal Colic: Sudden acute pain with tenderness over the costovertebral area, nausea, and vomiting. Pain radiates anteriorly and downward toward the bladder (female) or testis (male).

Ureteral Colic: Stones lodged in the ureter cause acute, excruciating, colicky, wavelike pain radiating down the thigh to the genitalia. Often accompanied by desire to void but passing little bloody urine.

Other Symptoms

  • Hematuria: Often present due to abrasive action of the stone.
  • Pyuria: Pus in urine.
  • Gastrointestinal: Diarrhea and abdominal discomfort (due to reno-intestinal reflexes).
  • Infection Signs: Chills, fever, and frequency (especially with struvite stones).

üíä Medical & Interventional Management

Assessment & Diagnostics

  • Imaging: KUB X-ray, Ultrasonography, IV urography, Retrograde pyelography.
  • Labs: Blood chemistries, 24-hour urine (Calcium, uric acid, creatinine, sodium, pH).
  • Stone Analysis: Chemical analysis of recovered stones to determine composition (calcium oxalate, urate, etc.).

Immediate Management

  • Pain Relief: Opioids (IV/IM) to prevent shock/syncope. NSAIDs (inhibit prostaglandin E, reducing swelling).
  • Hydration: High around-the-clock fluid intake (unless contraindicated) to increase hydrostatic pressure.
  • Comfort: Hot baths or moist heat to flank areas.

Interventional Procedures

üîß Stone Removal Procedures
Procedure Description
ESWL (Lithotripsy) Non-invasive. High-energy shock waves break up stones in the kidney calyx into sand-like grains for spontaneous voiding.
Ureteroscopy Visualizing and destroying stone using laser or ultrasound device via ureteroscope. Stent often left in place for 48 hrs.
Percutaneous Nephrolithotomy Nephroscope introduced through percutaneous route. Stone extracted or broken up. Tube left in place to prevent obstruction.
Chemolysis Dissolution using chemical infusions (alkylating/acidifying agents) via percutaneous nephrostomy.

ü•ó Nutritional Therapy & Prevention

Fluid intake is the mainstay of therapy. Patients should drink enough to excrete >2000 mL (preferably 3000-4000 mL) of urine daily.

üçΩÔ∏è

General Dietary Guidelines

  • Protein: Restrict to 60 g/day (decreases urinary calcium and uric acid excretion).
  • Sodium: Restrict to 3-4 g/day (sodium competes with calcium for reabsorption).
  • Calcium: Low-calcium diets are NOT generally recommended (risk of osteoporosis) unless for true absorptive hypercalciuria.
ü•¶ Dietary Management by Stone Type
Stone Type Interventions Foods to Avoid/Limit
Calcium Stones Thiazide diuretics (reduce calcium loss). Ammonium chloride. Excessive vitamins (esp. Vit D).
Uric Acid Stones Low-purine diet. Allopurinol (Zyloprim). Shellfish, anchovies, asparagus, mushrooms, organ meats.
Cystine Stones Low-protein diet. Alkalinize urine. Increase fluids. High protein foods.
Oxalate Stones Maintain dilute urine. Limit oxalate intake. Spinach, strawberries, rhubarb, chocolate, tea, peanuts, wheat bran.

üë©‚Äç‚öïÔ∏è Nursing Management

Assessment

  • Pain intensity and location (radiation).
  • Presence of Nausea, Vomiting, Diarrhea, Abdominal distention.
  • Signs of UTI (chills, fever) or obstruction (oliguria/anuria).
  • Inspect urine for blood; strain urine for stones/gravel.

Nursing Interventions

  • Relieving Pain: Administer opioids/NSAIDs. Assist patient to position of comfort. Assist to ambulate if activity brings relief.
  • Monitoring Complications: Encourage fluid intake. Monitor total urine output. Strain all urine. Monitor VS for infection.
  • Patient Education: Teach about causes and prevention. Drink 8-ounce glass of water every 1-2 hours during day and 2 glasses at bedtime. Avoid excessive sweating/dehydration.

Expected Outcomes

  • Reports relief of pain.
  • States knowledge of health-seeking behaviors (fluids, diet).
  • Voids 200-400 mL per voiding of clear urine.
  • Absence of complications (infection, obstruction).
