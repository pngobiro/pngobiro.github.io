üìâ Hypospadias: Overview & Classification

Definition: Hypospadias is the most common congenital anomaly of the penis. In this anomaly, the urethral opening is situated on the ventral side (underside) of the shaft of the penis in one or several positions, just behind the glans.

Classification

Severity of the condition depends on the location of the opening:

üìä Degrees of Hypospadias
Degree Location Name Description
1st Degree Glandular / Coronal Opening near the glans or at the corona.
2nd Degree Penile Opening at the mid-shaft or proximal penile shaft.
3rd Degree Penoscrotal / Perineal Opening at the junction of penis and scrotum, or perineum.

Etiology & Pathophysiology

  • Causes: Unknown, arrested development in fetal life, familial factors, gene mutation, or lack of hormone production during fetal life (endocrinopathies).
  • Pathophysiology: Due to incomplete fusion of urethral folds along the midline.

üè• Assessment & Management of Hypospadias

Symptoms & Assessment

  • Abnormal Voiding Pattern: Stream of urine may be deflected downward.
  • Positioning: Child may void in sitting position (penoscrotal/perineal types). In glandular types, child may void standing by tilting penis upward.
  • Sexual/Fertility Issues: Sexual dysfunction and fertility problems may arise later in life.
  • Psychological: Potential psychological problems.

Surgical Management

There is no medical management for this defect; surgery is recommended. Ideally performed at 6 - 24 months of age.

Contraindication: Circumcision

Circumcision of the foreskin should be avoided. The foreskin is often used for the surgical repair (urethroplasty).

  • Goal 1: Urethroplasty - To bring the urethral opening to the tip of the penis.
  • Goal 2: To straighten the penis.

Nursing Management

Pre-operative Care

  • Prepare parents for child surgery.
  • Provide psychological support to parents.

Post-operative Care

  • Monitor vital signs.
  • Catheter Care: Essential to maintain patency.
  • Restraints: Put restraints so that child should not take out catheter or other tubings.
  • Infection Control: Urine examination to rule out infection.
  • Support and guidance of parents is very important.

üìà Epispadias

Definition: An epispadias is a rare type of malformation of the penis in which the urethra ends in an opening on the upper aspect (dorsal side) of the penis.

üîç Click to zoom

Fig. 6.4: Illustration of Epispadias

Causes & Risk Factors

  • Unknown etiology.
  • Males are 4x more likely to have epispadias than females.
  • Caucasian firstborn children are more likely to be born with this condition.
  • 1 in 70 chance of occurrence.

Classification (Types)

Penopubic Epispadias
Urinary meatus is found close to the body, near the pubic bone at the base of the penis.
Penile Epispadias
Urinary meatus is found on the shaft of the penis.
Glanular Epispadias
Urinary meatus is found on the head of the penis, but on the top rather than the tip.

Management

Signs & Symptoms: Dribbling of urine, UTIs, painful urination, hematuria, urgency/frequency.

Diagnostics: History, physical exam, urine tests, ultrasound, CT, or X-rays.

Surgical Techniques

  • Modified Cantwell Ransley Repair: Involves "rebuilding" the penis; taking it apart to move urethra to normal position.
  • The Mitchell Technique: Involves taking the penis apart completely and putting it back together to correct dorsal bend (chordee) and position urethra.
