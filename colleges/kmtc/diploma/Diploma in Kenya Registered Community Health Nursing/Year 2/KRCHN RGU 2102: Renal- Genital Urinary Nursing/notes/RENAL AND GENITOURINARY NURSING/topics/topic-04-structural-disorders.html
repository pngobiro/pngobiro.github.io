üî• Interstitial Cystitis (Painful Bladder Syndrome)

Definition: Is an inflammatory condition of the bladder wall that is characterised by uncomfortable bladder pressure, bladder pain and sometimes pain in the pelvis.

Cause: Is unknown. Scientists believe that it may be as a result of other bowel conditions such as fibromyalgia or irritable bowel system.

Risk Factors

  • Age: Is diagnosed at 40s
  • Gender: Is more related to females more than males
  • Other conditions: Irritable bowel syndrome and fibromyalgia

Clinical Manifestations & Diagnosis

  • Severe irritable voiding (day and night frequency, nocturia and urgency)
  • Painful coitus
  • Painful urination

There is no specific diagnostic test since the symptoms resemble those of other conditions. Doctors have to rule out other treatable conditions (Urinalysis, urine culture, Cystoscopy, biopsy) before considering a diagnosis of Interstitial Cystitis.

Medical Management

Current treatment is aimed at relieving symptoms:

  • Medications:
    • Pentosan polysulfate sodium (Elmiron) 100mg TDS: Repairs defects in bladder lining.
    • Amitriptyline: Tricyclic antidepressant that helps reduce pain, increase bladder capacity and reduce urinary frequency/urgency.
  • Electrical nerve stimulation: Stimulates nerves to the bladder (transcutaneous or implant).
  • Surgical procedures:
    • Bladder cystectomy or augmentation.
    • Fulguration: Burning of Hunner‚Äôs ulcers with electricity/laser.
    • Resection: Cutting around and removing ulcers.

Key Interventions

  • Pain Management: Assess effectiveness of analgesics.
  • Diet: Drink lots of water; avoid smoking (worsens pain, contributes to cancer).
  • Exercise: Easy stretching exercises may help reduce symptoms.
  • Bladder Training: Teach patient to urinate at set intervals (e.g., hourly) according to the clock rather than waiting for the urge. The bladder will learn to control the urge.
  • Support: Encourage stress management and involve family.

üåä Hydronephrosis

Definition: Is a condition where one or both kidneys become stretched and swollen as a result of build-up of urine inside the kidney(s).

Pathophysiology

Obstruction prevents normal flow, causing urine to flow back (reflux) or accumulate, resulting in increased pressure.

  • If obstruction is in urethra/bladder: Affects both kidneys.
  • If obstruction is in ureter/stone/kink: Affects one kidney.

Compensatory Hypertrophy: Accumulation of urine distends the pelvis and calyces, leading to atrophy of the affected kidney. The other kidney gradually enlarges (hypertrophy) to compensate.

Risk Factors & Symptoms

  • Pregnancy: Enlargement of foetus places pressure on ureters.
  • Endometriosis: Tissue growth outside uterus disrupts urinary flow.
  • Recurrent UTIs / Prostate Enlargement: Leads to narrowing and pressure build-up.

Symptoms: Pain on the flank (back, waist, lower abdomen, groin), Nausea/Vomiting, Unexplained itching, Fever, Haematuria, Abdominal mass.

Management

  • Fluid Imbalance: Monitor input/output accurately.

üöß Urethral Strictures

Definition: Narrowing of the urethra caused by injury, disease (UTI), or urethritis.

Causes

  • Injury/Trauma to the tract (Urethra runs between legs making it vulnerable).
  • Viral/Bacterial infection (STIs). Body's repair attempt creates scar tissue.
  • Instrumentation of urethra.
  • Passage of kidney stones.
  • Infants/Toddlers: Inflammation due to circumcision (noticed during toilet training).

Signs and Symptoms

During early stages: Pain during urination, inability to fully empty bladder (capacity may increase). A UTI is often present at diagnosis.

Obstructive Voiding Symptoms

  • Urinary terminal dribbling
  • Urine intermittency
  • Deflected urinary stream ("Spraying")
  • Decreased force of stream
  • Increased frequency
  • Acute or chronic retention

Management

Diagnosis: Cystoscopy, Urethrography.

Treatment Modalities

  • Urethral Dilatation: Using urethral sounds or bougies. Done at intervals (3-6 months).
    • Procedure: If stricture prevents catheter, filiform bougies used to find opening. Larger sound passed over filiform acting as guide.
    • Post-procedure: Hot sitz baths, non-opioid analgesics, antibiotics.
  • Placement of Foley catheter: For 7 days post-procedure.
  • Suprapubic catheter: May be necessary in severe cases/retention.
  • Surgery: Urethroplasty (excision) or internal urethrotomy.
