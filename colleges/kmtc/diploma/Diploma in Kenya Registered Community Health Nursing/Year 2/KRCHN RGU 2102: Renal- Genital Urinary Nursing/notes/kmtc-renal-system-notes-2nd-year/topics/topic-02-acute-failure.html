‚ö†Ô∏è Definition & Etiology

Acute Renal Failure (ARF) is defined as urine output less than 500mls or complete anuria over 24 hours. It is a significant finding in patients with renal failure.

Classification of Causes

The causes are categorized into three main groups:

1. Prerenal Causes (Shock/Perfusion)
  • Severe hemorrhage (abortion, RTA, retroplacental hemorrhage, GIT bleeding).
  • Fluid loss (severe burns, diarrhea, vomiting, DKA, Addison‚Äôs disease, fistula).
  • Anesthesia (general).
  • Cardiac arrest/insufficiency (cardiac tamponade, pulmonary embolism).
  • Incompatible blood transfusion or allergic reactions.
2. Renal Causes (Intrinsic Damage)
  • Acute glomerulonephritis.
  • Drug poisoning (e.g., Sulphonamides, Paracetamol).
  • Renal artery embolism or Disseminated Intravascular Coagulopathy (DIC).
  • Infection causing Acute Tubular Necrosis.
3. Post-Renal Causes (Obstruction)
  • Bilateral pyelonephritis.
  • Acute obstructive uropathies: Urethral strictures, Neurogenic bladder, Benign Prostatic Hypertrophy (BPH), Calculi.
  • Congenital issues: Urethral valves, Phimosis.

Mechanism: Obstruction causes back pressure ‚Üí accumulation of fluid in kidneys ‚Üí reduced oxygen perfusion/pressure ‚Üí necrosis.

üè• Clinical Features & Investigation

Symptoms & Signs

  • Oliguria or Anuria.
  • GIT bleeding.
  • Anemia (urea causes bone marrow suppression).
  • Features of Uremia: Hiccups, blood-stained vomitus, nausea.
  • Sweet smell (if acidosis present).
  • Pulmonary edema.

Critical Alerts

Cardiac Arrest Risk: High risk due to hyperkalaemia.

History: Check for sore throat (Post-streptococcal AGN).

Investigations

  • Urine: Reduced specific gravity.
  • Blood: Increased Urea and Creatinine levels.
  • Electrolytes: Check for imbalance.
  • Special: 24hr urine output for catecholamines.

üíä Treatment & Management

Primary Goal: Maintenance of volume homeostasis and correction of biochemical abnormalities.

‚ñ∂ Core Management Strategies
1. Fluid Overload Correction
Use Furosemide in high doses. Response indicates a good sign.
2. Severe Acidosis
Bicarbonate administration (important as a bridge to dialysis).
3. Hyperkalemia (Life-threatening)
  • Decrease dietary intake of potassium.
  • Exchange potassium across gut lumen using binding agents.
  • Promote intracellular shift (Insulin + Dextrose, Beta agonists).
  • Institute dialysis.
4. Vasodilators
Dopamine (1-5 mcg/kg/min) causes selective dilation of renal vasculature to improve perfusion.
5. Dietary Modification
Restriction of salt and fluid is crucial in oliguric renal failure.

Nephrotoxic Agents

The kidneys are vulnerable to toxic chemicals. Avoid or use with extreme caution: NSAIDS and other nephrotoxic drugs.

04

üîÑ Dialysis

Biocompatible membrane hemodialysis is preferred. Hemodialysis may delay recovery in some cases but is indicated for:

  • Volume expansion unmanageable by diuretics.
  • Hyperkalemia refractory to medical therapy.
  • Severe acid-base disturbance refractory to therapy.
  • Severe Azotemia (BUN > 80-100).
  • Uremia.

üõ°Ô∏è Prevention & Complications

Prevention

  • Regulate cardiovascular and intravascular disease.
  • Minimize nephrotoxic drugs.

Complications of Acute Renal Failure

  • Intravascular volume overload
  • Hyponatremia
  • Hyperkalemia
  • Metabolic acidosis
  • Hyperphosphatemia
  • Hypocalcemia
  • Hyperuricemia
