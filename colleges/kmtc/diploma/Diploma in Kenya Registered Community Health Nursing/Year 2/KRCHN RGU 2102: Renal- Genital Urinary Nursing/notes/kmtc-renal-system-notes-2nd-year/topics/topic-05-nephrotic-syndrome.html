02

üß¨ Pathogenesis

Increased permeability of the glomerulus causes leakage of proteins in urine (>3.5g/24hrs).

Mechanism of Edema

  1. Hyponatremia leads to reduction in oncotic pressure.
  2. Fluid enters intercellular spaces, reducing effective plasma volume.
  3. This reduction stimulates the Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System (RAAS), causing sodium retention.
  4. ADH system is stimulated, leading to fluid retention.
  5. Sympathetic system is stimulated, leading to further fluid retention.
  6. Result: Massive Passive Edema.

Systemic Effects of Protein Loss

  • Hypercholesterolemia: Due to increased loss of lipoproteins (responsible for lipid transport).
  • Hypothyroidism/Goiter: Due to loss of iodine-bound albumin (negative feedback increases thyroid hormone production).
  • Iron Resistant Anemia: Due to loss of transferrin proteins. Blood picture shows microcytic hypochromic anemia.
  • Hypocalcemia: Due to lack of Vitamin D.
  • Hypercoagulability: Loss of Thrombin III (Heparin cofactor) leads to increased coagulability, microembolism, and thromboembolism.

Note: Most patients present with pain in loins and haematuria due to bilateral renal thrombosis.

üè• Features & Investigation

Clinical Features

  • Massive edema
  • Anemia
  • Wasted appearance
  • Polyuria

Investigations

  • Blood for cholesterol levels
  • Serum protein markers (reduced)
  • Blood urea (increased)
  • Hb levels (reduced)
  • Urine protein analysis (>3.5g in 24hrs)

üíä Management & Complications

Treatment

  • Diuretics: Lasix 40mg daily.
  • Dietary: Reduce salt and protein intake.
  • Monitor Input/Output chart (if admitted).
  • Anticoagulants: May be given.
  • Primary Nephrotic Syndrome: Tapper off Prednisolone.

Complications

  • Acute Renal Failure
  • Congestive Cardiac Failure
  • Hypothyroidism
  • Renal Rickets
  • Anemia
  • Hypoglycemia
  • Thromboembolism

Differential Diagnosis

  • Congestive Cardiac Failure
  • Malnutrition
  • Liver Failure
  • Portal Hypertension
  • Anemia
