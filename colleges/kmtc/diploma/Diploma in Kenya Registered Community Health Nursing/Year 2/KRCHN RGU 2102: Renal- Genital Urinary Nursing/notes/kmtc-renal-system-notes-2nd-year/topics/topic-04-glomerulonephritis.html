‚ö° Acute Glomerulonephritis

Definition: Acute inflammation of the glomeruli affecting one or both kidneys.

Aetiology

  • Infections: Post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis, bacterial infective endocarditis, pneumococcal pneumonia, meningococcemia, typhoid fever, or syphilis. Viral infections (Hepatitis B, measles, varicella, echovirus) and parasitic infections.
  • Multisystemic Disease: Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE), Vasculitis, Goodpasture syndrome.
  • Primary Glomerular Diseases: Membrane proliferative glomerulonephritis.
  • Others: Wilms tumor, DPT vaccine, Guillain-Barr√© syndrome.

Pathology

There is a reduction in glomerular filtration rate (GFR) due to infiltration of capillaries by inflammatory cells or expansion of intrinsic glomerular cells, leading to a reduced filtration surface area.

  • Circulatory Congestion: Reduced GFR leads to retention of sodium and water, causing hypertension and edema.
  • Haematuria: Due to migration of erythrocytes across the damaged glomerulus.
  • Proteinuria: A consequence of loss in anionic charges of capillary wall and selective defect in reabsorption.
02

üîç AGN: Features & Diagnosis

Clinical Features

  • Common in children.
  • History of cutaneous/pharyngeal Group A Beta-hemolytic streptococcal infection.
  • Edema: Swelling of the face, especially in early mornings (puffy face).
  • Urine: Reduced output (oliguria), "coca cola" color, painful micturition mixed with blood.
  • Low grade fever.
  • Hypertension symptoms.

Examination Findings

  • Puffy face and ill-looking.
  • Edema.
  • Increased Blood Pressure.
  • Renal angle tenderness.

Investigations

Urinalysis
Red blood cells and RBC casts (indication of glomerular damage). White blood cell casts (inflammation).
Blood Work
  • CBC: Reduced hematocrit (dilutional anemia).
  • RFT: Elevated Urea and Creatinine.
  • ESR: Always increased.
Imaging & Biopsy
Ultrasound/CT for morphology. Biopsy determines histology for therapy and prognosis.
03

üíä AGN: Management

Treatment Protocol

  • Bed rest and monitoring of vital signs.
  • Urine monitoring for albumin levels.
  • Diet & Fluid: Restrict salt. Give fluids only if urine output > 500mls. Restrict proteins if urea/creatinine are increased.
  • Medication: Treat infections with Penicillins.

Complications

  • Hypertension & Hypertensive Encephalopathy
  • Chronic Glomerulonephritis
  • Uremia
  • Secondary Infections (e.g., Bronchitis)

üï∞Ô∏è Chronic Glomerulonephritis

Definition: A chronic renal disease resulting in the destruction of glomeruli. In children with AGN, 50-90% heal completely, while 10-50% progress to chronic forms.

Pathology

Destruction of the glomerulus (due to azotemia or hypertension), fibrosis of tubules, reduced glomeruli, and nephrosclerosis. Leads to Chronic Renal Failure with:

  • Nephron insensitivity to aldosterone.
  • Electrolyte imbalance.
  • Acid-base imbalance.
  • Uremia.

Clinical Features

Patients may be asymptomatic or present with:

  • General: Yellowness of eyes, anemia.
  • CVS: Hypertension, Left Ventricular Hypertrophy, Cardiac arrest (hyperkalemia), Pericarditis (uremia).
  • Respiratory: Acidosis (Kussmaul breathing).
  • GUT: Polyuria and Polydipsia.
  • GIT: Nausea, vomiting, melena stool, epigastric pain, hematemesis, bitter saliva.
  • CNS: Fits, tremors, drowsiness, coma.
  • Hematology: Anemia (bone marrow depression, hemolysis), bleeding tendencies.
05

‚öïÔ∏è CGN: Diagnosis & Treatment

Investigations

  • Urine: Specific gravity, proteins, RBC casts. Hyaline cast is typical of CGN.
  • Serum: Urea, Potassium, Creatinine, Phosphate, Calcium, Sodium.
  • Hemogram: Iron deficiency anemia.
  • Imaging: Bone X-ray (osteomalacia/osteoporosis), Chest X-ray (LVF), IV Urography, Ultrasound (kidney size, calcifications).

Treatment Plan

Diet & Fluids

Low protein diet (20-40 g/day). IV fluids (N/S 2-5 liters alternating with Dextrose). Restrict salt if HTN/edema present.

Medication

Antibiotics for infection. ACE inhibitors for HTN (avoid thiazides). Calciferol (5mg BD) for bone pains. Iron supplements for anemia.

Note: If no improvement, dialysis is preferred.
