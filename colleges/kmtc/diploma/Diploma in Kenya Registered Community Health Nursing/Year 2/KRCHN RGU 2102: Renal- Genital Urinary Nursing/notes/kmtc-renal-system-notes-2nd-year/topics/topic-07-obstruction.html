üöß Urinary Tract Obstruction

Obstruction can occur at any point between the kidney and the urethral meatus, resulting in dilatation above the obstruction (Hydronephrosis). In the elderly, obstruction is more common in men due to prostatic disease.

Aetiology (Causes)

‚ñ∂ Within the Lumen
  • Calculus (Stones)
  • Blood clot
  • Sloughed papilla (Diabetes, analgesic abuse, sickle cell)
  • Tumor of renal pelvis, ureter, or bladder
‚ñ∂ Within the Wall
  • Pelviureteric neuromuscular dysfunction (congenital)
  • Ureteric stricture (TB, calculus, surgery)
  • Ureterovesical stricture (ureterocele, schistosomiasis)
  • Congenital megaureter or bladder neck obstruction
  • Neuropathic bladder
  • Urethral stricture (Gonococcal, instrumentation)
  • Congenital urethral valve / Pin-hole meatus
‚ñ∂ Pressure from Outside
  • Pelviureteric compression (bands, aberrant vessels)
  • Tumors (Retroperitoneal, colon, cervix)
  • Diverticulitis or Aortic aneurysm
  • Retroperitoneal fibrosis (periaortitis)
  • Accidental ligation of ureter
  • Retrocaval ureter
  • Prostatic obstruction (BPH)
  • Phimosis

Pathophysiology

Obstruction leads to a progressive rise in intraluminal pressure, dilatation proximal to the site, and compression/thinning of the renal parenchyma. Acute obstruction causes transient vasodilation followed by vasoconstriction.

02

üè• Clinical Features

Upper Tract Obstruction

  • Loin pain: Dull or sharp, constant or intermittent. Provoked by high fluid intake/alcohol.
  • Complete Anuria: Strongly suggests complete bilateral obstruction or obstruction of a single kidney.
  • Polyuria: May occur in partial obstruction due to impaired concentrating capacity.
  • Infection: Malaise, fever, septicemia.

Bladder Outflow Obstruction

  • Hesitancy, narrowing of stream, terminal dribbling.
  • Sense of incomplete emptying.
  • Overflow Incontinence: Frequent passage of small volumes.
  • Acute Retention: Precipitated by infection.

Physical Signs

  • Loin tenderness.
  • Enlarged hydronephrotic kidney (palpable).
  • Enlarged bladder (palpable/percussable in retention).
  • Rectal/Vaginal exam essential to exclude prostatic or pelvic malignancy.
03

üî¨ Investigations

Biochemistry
Raised serum urea/creatinine, hyperkalemia, anemia of chronic disease.
Helical (Spiral) CT
High sensitivity. Can visualize radiolucent uric acid stones as small as 1mm.
Ultrasonography
Rules out upper tract dilatation. Limitation: Cannot distinguish baggy low-pressure systems from high-pressure obstruction (false positives); can miss ureteric stones.
Excretion Urography
Shows delayed nephrogram on affected side due to reduced GFR. Full-length film after voiding assesses emptying.
Antegrade Pyelography
Defines site/cause. Can be combined with percutaneous nephrostomy drainage.
Cystoscopy / Urethrography
Visualizes lesions in bladder/urethra (valves, strictures).
04

üíä Treatment

Goals: Relieve symptoms and preserve renal function.

Immediate Actions

  • Urgent Relief: Required for recent complete upper tract obstruction (especially with infection).
  • Temporary Drainage: Nephrostomy if definitive surgery is hazardous.
  • Dialysis: May be required pre-surgery.

Conservative

  • Partial obstruction (e.g., passing stone) may not need immediate surgery.

Post-Obstructive Diuresis

Massive diuresis may follow relief of bilateral obstruction due to sodium/water overload and osmotic effects. This is usually self-limiting but requires careful monitoring of electrolytes and fluid replacement.

05

üíé Nephrolithiasis

Pathology: Urine becomes supersaturated with insoluble material (excessive excretion or extreme water conservation). Crystals form and aggregate to form a stone.

Types: Calcium stones, Uric acid stones, Cystine.

Clinical Features

  • Pain: Acute onset, typically in flanks radiating downward anteriorly to testis/vulva.
  • Urinary Symptoms: Frequency, urgency, dysuria, haematuria.
  • May be asymptomatic or present as acute abdomen due to obstruction.

Management

Investigations

  • Serum & Urine Calcium
  • Serum Electrolytes
  • Urine Oxalate/Citrate levels

Treatment

  • High water intake (bed rest)
  • Analgesia: Pethidine or Morphine
  • Antispasmodics: Buscopan or Atropine
  • Shock wave lithotripsy
