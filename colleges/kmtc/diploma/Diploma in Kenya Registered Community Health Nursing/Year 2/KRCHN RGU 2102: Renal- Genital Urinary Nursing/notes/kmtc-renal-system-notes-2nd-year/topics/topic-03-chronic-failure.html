üìâ Definition & Etiology

Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) implies long-standing, and usually progressive, impairment in renal function. In many instances, no effective means are available to reverse the primary disease process.

Exceptions include: Correction of urinary tract obstruction, immunosuppressive therapy for systemic vasculitis/Goodpasture‚Äôs syndrome, treatment of accelerated hypertension, and correction of renal artery stenosis.

Accelerating Factors

  • Systemic hypertension
  • Decreased perfusion
  • Proteinuria
  • Elevated BUN
  • Hyperlipaemia

Causes of Chronic Kidney Disease

‚ñ∂ Congenital & Inherited Disease
  • Polycystic kidney disease (adult and infantile)
  • Medullary cystic disease
  • Tuberous sclerosis
  • Oxalosis & Cystinosis
  • Congenital obstructive uropathy
‚ñ∂ Glomerular Disease
  • Primary glomerulonephritides
  • Glomerulosclerosis
  • Secondary: SLE, Polyangiitis, Wegener's granulomatosis, Amyloidosis, Diabetic glomerulosclerosis, Accelerated hypertension, Haemolytic uraemic syndrome, Systemic sclerosis, Sickle cell disease.
‚ñ∂ Vascular Disease
  • Hypertensive nephrosclerosis (common in black Africans)
  • Reno-vascular disease
  • Small and medium-sized vessel vasculitis
‚ñ∂ Tubulointerstitial Disease
  • Tubulointerstitial nephritis (idiopathic, drugs like NSAIDS/analgesics, immunologically mediated)
  • Reflux nephropathy
  • Tuberculosis & Schistosomiasis
  • Nephrocalcinosis & Multiple myeloma
  • Renal papillary necrosis (diabetes, sickle cell, analgesics)
‚ñ∂ Urinary Tract Obstruction
  • Calculus disease
  • Prostatic disease
  • Pelvic tumours
  • Retroperitoneal fibrosis

üè• Clinical Presentation

History Taking

  • Duration of symptoms
  • Drug ingestion (NSAIDs, herbal remedies)
  • Medical/Surgical history (Chemotherapy, SLE, Malaria)
  • Previous urinalysis/creatinine checks
  • Family history of renal disease

Symptoms

Early stages are often asymptomatic. Symptoms typically appear when serum urea > 40 mmol/l.

General Symptoms

  • Malaise, loss of energy
  • Anorexia (loss of appetite)
  • Insomnia
  • Nocturia & Polyuria
  • Itching (Pruritus)
  • Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea

Advanced Uremia (>50-60 mmol/l)

  • Mental slowing
  • Clouding of consciousness
  • Seizures
  • Myoclonic twitching
  • Paraesthesia (polyneuropathy)
  • "Restless legs" syndrome

Note: Failure of tubular reabsorption may lead to high urine volumes even if GFR is profoundly depressed. Urine output is NOT a useful guide to renal function in CKD.

Physical Examination Findings

  • General: Pallor (anemia), Short stature (if childhood onset), Increased photosensitive pigmentation.
  • Skin: Brown nail discoloration, Scratch marks (uremic pruritus).
  • Cardiovascular: Signs of fluid overload, Pericardial friction rub, Flow murmurs.
  • Neurological: Glove and stocking peripheral sensory loss (rare).
  • Local: Kidneys usually impalpable unless enlarged by polycystic disease, obstruction, or tumor.

üî¨ Investigations

Urinalysis
  • Haematuria: Suggests glomerulonephritis.
  • Proteinuria: Suggests glomerular disease (or infection).
  • Glycosuria: Common in CKD with normal blood glucose.
Urine Microscopy
  • White cells: Infection (sterile pyuria suggests TB or papillary necrosis).
  • Eosinophiluria: Allergic nephritis or cholesterol embolization.
  • Casts: Granular (active disease), Red-cell (glomerulonephritis).
Biochemistry (Serum & Urine)
  • Urine Osmolality: Low in renal disease (loss of concentrating ability).
  • Serum: Urea, Creatinine (calc. eGFR), Electrophoresis (myeloma), CK (rhabdomyolysis).
Radiology
  • Ultrasound: Essential for renal size and excluding hydronephrosis.
  • CT Scan: Detects stones, retroperitoneal fibrosis, cortical scarring.
  • MRI: Angiography (Caution: Gadolinium in renal failure).

üíä Management & Complications

Management Strategy

  • Treat underlying cause.
  • Aggressive BP control (Target systolic 140 mmHg).
  • Dietary: Protein restriction.
  • Avoid nephrotoxic drugs (e.g., NSAIDs).
  • Anemia: Transfusion or Iron supplements.
  • Hyperphosphatemia: Phosphate binders.
  • Hypocalcemia: Calcium supplements (Calcitriol).
  • Fluid Overload: Diuretics.
  • Hyperlipidemia: Statins.
  • Aggressive glycemic control.
  • Use ACE inhibitors/ARBs for diabetes/proteinuria.

Complications

Anemia

Due to reduced erythropoietin production.

Renal Osteodystrophy

Hyperparathyroid bone disease, osteomalacia, osteoporosis, osteosclerosis.

Skin Disease

Pruritus (itching) due to retention of nitrogenous waste.

GI Complications

Reflux oesophagitis, peptic ulceration, pancreatitis, constipation.

Muscle Dysfunction

Uremia interferes with muscle energy metabolism.
