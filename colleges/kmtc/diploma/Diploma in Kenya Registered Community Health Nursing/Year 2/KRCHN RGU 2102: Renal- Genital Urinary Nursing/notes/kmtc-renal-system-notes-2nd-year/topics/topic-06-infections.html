üî• Pyelonephritis

Definition: A bacterial infection affecting both the parenchyma and pelvis of the kidney.

‚ñ∂ Predisposing Factors & Causative Agents

Predisposing Factors

  • Gender/Age: Common in women and children (short urethra).
  • Pregnancy: Pressure on the bladder by the uterus.
  • Obstruction: Urethral stricture, calculi, BPH, neurogenic bladder.
  • Comorbidities: Hypertension, Diabetes, Malignancies.

Causative Agents

  • E. coli (Most common)
  • Streptococcal / Staphylococcal
  • Proteus
  • Pseudomonas
  • Tuberculosis

Clinical Presentation

Symptoms

  • Acute onset.
  • Pain in the abdomen/renal angle radiating downward.
  • Painful micturition (dysuria).
  • Frequency of micturition.
  • High temperature with chills.

Examination Findings

  • Renal angle tenderness.
  • Loins are tender.
  • Patient is febrile.

Management

Investigations: Urine shows numerous pus cells. Do culture & sensitivity. Blood shows leukocytes.

Treatment: Bed rest, Paracetamol for pain/fever, Antibiotics (Penicillins) based on culture results.

Differential Diagnosis

Acute Glomerulonephritis, Acute Salpingitis, Ectopic Pregnancy, Acute Appendicitis, Acute Cystitis.

üï∞Ô∏è Chronic Pyelonephritis

Definition: Chronic inflammation of the renal parenchyma.

  • Aetiology: Same as acute pyelonephritis.
  • Clinical Features: Patients may have non-urinary symptoms. Often discovered during investigation for hypertension in young individuals. May present with late features of uremia.
  • Diagnosis: Often accidental in laboratory investigations.
03

üöΩ Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)

Definition: Presence of more than 100,000 organisms per ml in a midstream sample of urine. Lower UTI is more common in women than men.

Predisposing Factors

  • Active sexual intercourse (minor trauma).
  • Inadequate perineal hygiene.
  • Instrumentation of bladder.
  • Gynaecological abnormality.
  • Stasis of urine.

Clinical Features

  • Frequency and dysuria.
  • Suprabubic pain and tenderness.
  • Urine: Cloudy with unpleasant smell and pus cells.

Causative Organisms

E. coli, Proteus species, Pseudomonas, Streptococcus, Staphylococcus.

04

ü´Å Renal Tuberculosis

Usually a haematogenous spread from a primary focus. Initial lesion in renal cortex may involve bladder, epididymis, seminal vesicles, and prostate. More common in the young.

Clinical Features

  • Haematuria and Dysuria.
  • General features of TB.
  • Can lead to chronic renal failure.
üö©

Diagnostic Hallmark: Sterile Pyuria

Urine culture and sensitivity shows sterile urine (no growth on standard media) despite the presence of pus cells. This is a key indicator for Renal TB.

Treatment: Treat as any TB patient (Anti-TB drugs).
