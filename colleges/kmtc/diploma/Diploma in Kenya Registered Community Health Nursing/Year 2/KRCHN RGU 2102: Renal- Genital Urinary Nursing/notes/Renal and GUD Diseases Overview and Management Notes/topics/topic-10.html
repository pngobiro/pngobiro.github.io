üîó Genitourinary Fistulas

Definition: An abnormal passage between cavities or between a cavity and the surface of the body.

Common Sites of Leakage

  • Vesico-vaginal fistula (VVF): Between bladder and vagina.
  • Uretero-vaginal fistula: Between ureter and vagina.
  • Urethro-vaginal fistula: Between urethra and vagina.
  • Recto-vaginal fistula: Between rectum and vagina.

Pathophysiology of VVF

During obstructed labor, prolonged pressure of the baby's head against the mother's pelvis cuts off blood supply to soft tissues surrounding the bladder and vagina. The injured tissue undergoes necrosis (sloughing), leaving a hole or fistula.

Risk Factors & Causes

Causes

  • Obstetric injury (obstructed labor/CPD).
  • Surgical trauma (hysterectomy).
  • Radiation (Cervical CA).
  • Direct trauma/penetrating injuries.

Social Predisposing Factors

  • Early pregnancy/marriage.
  • Lack of education.
  • Low social status of women.
  • Limited access to medical services.

Management

  • Diagnosis: Methylene dye test, X-ray with dye, Pelvic ultrasound.
  • Small Lesions: Bladder catheter to allow spontaneous closure.
  • Large Lesions: Surgical repair (3-layered closure).
  • Recto-vaginal: May require diversionary colostomy and sphincter repair.

üß† Neurogenic Bladder

Definition: Loss of normal bladder function caused by damage to part of the nervous system (brain, spinal cord, or peripheral nerves).

Type Under-active (Flaccid) Over-active (Spastic)
Mechanism Non-contractile, unable to empty. Uncontrolled reflex emptying.
Cause Interruption of local nerves, Spina bifida. Cord/Brain damage (e.g., Multiple Sclerosis).
Symptoms Painless enlargement, overflow incontinence, infection risk. Frequent uncontrolled voiding, kidney damage from backflow.

Management

  • Catheterization: Indwelling (immediate prevention of damage) or Intermittent Self-Catheterization (preferred).
  • Medication: Anticholinergic drugs to relax overactive bladder.
  • Surgery: Urinary diversion (ostomy), sphincterotomy, or bladder augmentation.
  • Nursing: Monitor kidney function, encourage fluids (8 glasses/day), position changes.

Definition: Age-related, non-malignant enlargement of the prostate gland. Common in men aged 50‚Äì70 years.

Pathophysiology: Imbalance between cellular proliferation and cell death leads to increased epithelial/stromal cells in the transition zone. This compresses the urethra, causing Bladder Outflow Obstruction (BOO).

Clinical Features

  • Frequency, Nocturia, Urgency.
  • Hesitancy, weak stream, prolonged micturition.
  • Chronic retention (incomplete emptying).
  • DRE Finding: Large, rubbery, non-tender prostate.

Management

‚ñ∂ Medical & Pharmacologic
  • Alpha-blockers (e.g., Alfuzosin): Relax smooth muscle of bladder neck/prostate.
  • 5-alpha reductase inhibitors (e.g., Finasteride): Decrease prostate size by blocking testosterone conversion.
  • Catheterization: Immediate relief for retention.
‚ñ∂ Surgical Procedures
  • TURP (Transurethral Resection of the Prostate): Removal of inner prostate via endoscope.
  • TUNA (Transurethral Needle Ablation): Uses heat to destroy tissue.
  • Open Prostatectomy: For very large glands via suprapubic/retropubic approach.

Post-Operative Nursing Care (TURP/Prostatectomy)

  • Continuous Bladder Irrigation (CBI): Use normal saline for 72 hours to prevent clot formation.
  • Drainage Flow: Do not lift bag above bladder level; ensure no kinks.
  • Monitoring: Urine should change from bloody to amber. Watch for hemorrhage or blockage (distension/pain).
  • Patient Education: Avoid straining (Valsalva), heavy lifting, and spicy foods. Teach pelvic floor exercises.

üß¨ Other Structural Anomalies

Congenital Penile Anomalies

Hypospadias
Urethral opening is a groove on the underside (ventral) of the penis.
Epispadias
Urethral opening is on the dorsum (top) of the penis.

Treatment: Plastic surgery repair, usually when the boy is very young.

Urethral Strictures

Narrowing of a section of the urethra due to scarring (congenital, trauma, infection/instrumentation). Treated via dilation or urethrotomy.

Bladder Diverticuli

Outpouching of the mucosa through the muscular wall of the bladder.

  • Congenital: Weakness of ureterovesical junction (children <10).
  • Acquired: High intravesical pressure due to obstruction (e.g., BPH) in men >60.
  • Complications: Infection, stones, tumor formation within diverticulum.
