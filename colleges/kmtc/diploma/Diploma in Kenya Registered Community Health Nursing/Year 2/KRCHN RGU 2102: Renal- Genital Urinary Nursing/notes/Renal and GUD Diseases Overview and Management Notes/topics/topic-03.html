üìñ Overview & Pathophysiology

Definition: This is a bacterial infection of the kidneys, specifically of the renal pelvis, tubules, and interstitial tissues of one or both kidneys and medulla.

It is generally secondary to an ascending infection from the urethra or bladder to the kidneys.

‚ÑπÔ∏è

Classification

Pyelonephritis can be Acute or Chronic.

Chronic pyelonephritis is rare, common in children and in urinary obstruction.

Pathophysiology

  • Bacteria reach the bladder through the urethra and ascend to the kidney.
  • It is secondary to urine backup into the ureters usually at the time of voiding.
  • Infection spreads from the renal pelvis to the cortex.
  • The pelvis, calyces, and medulla of the kidney are primarily affected with white blood cell infiltration and inflammation.
  • The kidney becomes grossly edematous and localized abscesses may develop on the cortical surface of the kidney.

‚ö° Acute Pyelonephritis

Causes

  • UTI of E. Coli, spreading through the urethra, bladder, and then kidneys.
  • Septicemia with bacteremia can spread to kidneys.

Predisposing Factors

  • Women: More susceptible due to shorter urethra.
  • Obstruction: Strictures or stones increase susceptibility.
  • Immunosuppression: Diabetes Mellitus, cancer, HIV/AIDS.
  • Pregnancy: Pressure of a pregnant uterus.
  • Reflux: Vesico-ureteral reflux (backflow due to valve incompetence).
  • History: Recurrent urinary tract infections.
  • Prostate: Enlarged prostate in men.
  • Instrumentation: Long term urethral catheterization or cystoscopic examination.
  • Neurological: Nerve and spinal nerve damage.

Signs and Symptoms

Local Symptoms Urinary Findings Systemic Signs
  • Dysuria
  • Urgency in micturition
  • Flank, abdominal, back pain
  • Kidney enlargement
  • Proteins, pus cells, bacteria
  • Cloudy, unpleasant smell (fishy odor suggests E. coli)
  • Hematuria
  • Pyuria
  • Fever (> 38.9¬∞C)
  • Sweating, rigors, chills
  • General malaise
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Mental confusion (elderly)
  • Convulsions (children)

ü©∫ Diagnostics & Medical Management

Diagnostic Tests

  • Urinalysis: Shows high count of WBCs and bacteria.
  • Gram Stain & Culture: To identify infecting organism and sensitivity.
  • Blood Cultures: To rule out bacteremia.
  • Kidney Ultrasound: Detect stones or wounds/blockage.
  • Voiding Cystourethrography (VCUG): Assesses bladder and urethra during voiding.
  • Cystoscopy: Direct visualization of the bladder.

Medical Management

  1. 1
    Treat the Cause: Identify and remove underlying obstructions or sources.
  2. 2
    Antibiotics: Appropriate antibiotics after isolating micro-organism (for 6 weeks). Nitrofurantoin 50-100mg 6 hourly for 7 days (acts as microbial antiseptic).
  3. 3
    Symptomatic Relief: Antiemetics (e.g., Plasil 10 mg) for nausea; Analgesics for pain.
  4. 4
    Urine Alkalization: Sodium bicarbonate (citrate) 3 gms to keep urine alkaline.

Goals of Management

  • Correct and maintain normal urinary elimination
  • Pain management
  • Enhance knowledge
  • Improve sleep pattern
  • Correct hyperthermia
‚ñ∂ Diagnosis 1: Impaired Urinary Elimination

Related to: Urgency, hesitancy, dysuria.

Interventions:

  • Encourage increased fluid intake to 3-4 litres per day (improves renal blood flow).
  • Encourage 2-3 hourly voiding (prevents urine accumulation and reduces bacteria).
  • Health education: Female patients to wipe from front to back (minimizes contamination).
‚ñ∂ Diagnosis 2: Impaired Comfort (Acute Pain)

Related to: Inflammation of renal structures.

Interventions:

  • Assess pain description, severity, and nature.
  • Encourage oral fluid intake (2-3L) to flush out bacteria and toxins.
  • Encourage Sitz baths to reduce perineal pain and promote muscle relaxation.
  • Discourage coffee, tea, alcohol (irritants to urinary system).
  • Apply heating pads to suprapubic area or back.
  • Administer prescribed analgesics and antispasmodics.
‚ñ∂ Diagnosis 3: Deficient Knowledge

Related to: Disease process, management, predisposing factors.

Interventions:

  • Explain infection, treatment, and prevention to prevent recurrence.
  • Encourage compliance with treatment.
  • Preventive measures: Hygienic measures (avoid bath tubs), perineal hygiene after bowel movements.
  • Frequent bladder emptying (prevents distention).
  • Avoid tight constricting undergarments; use cotton fabrics.
  • Emphasize need for follow-up cultures.
‚ñ∂ Diagnosis 4: Disturbed Sleep Pattern

Related to: Nocturia, pain, fever.

Interventions:

  • Provide comfort measures (back rub, warm bath).
  • Encourage milk in evening (L-tryptophan induces sleep).
  • Reduce environmental distractions (noise, light).
  • Reduce fluid intake during night time.
‚ñ∂ Diagnosis 5: Hyperthermia

Related to: Inflammatory process.

Interventions:

  • Assess for signs of increased temp (sweating, shivering, headache).
  • Tepid sponging to enhance evaporation.
  • Adequate fluid intake to prevent dehydration.
  • Bed rest to reduce metabolic demands.
  • Monitor vital signs 4 hourly.

Potential Complications

  • Chronic pyelonephritis
  • Hypertension
  • Risk for imbalanced nutrition (due to nausea/anorexia)
  • Risk for fluid volume deficit
  • Anxiety

üïí Chronic Pyelonephritis

Definition: A long-standing urinary tract infection which affects one or more kidneys. It may progress to chronic renal failure.

Diagnosis

  • Intravenous Urogram
  • Elevated Creatinine levels
  • Blood Urea Nitrogen (BUN)
  • Creatinine Clearance Test

Management

  • Admit patient and enforce complete bed rest.
  • Maintain strict Input and Output chart.
  • Administer IV fluids.
  • Diet: Low salt and proteins.
  • Transfuse in case of anemia.
  • Take urine for Culture/Sensitivity (C/S).
  • Surgical intervention if obstruction is present.
  • Discharge with advice on hygiene, medication compliance, and seeking care for relapse.
