Renal Calculi (Kidney Stones)

üìò Overview & Pathophysiology

Definition: Renal calculi are hard stone-like masses that form anywhere in the renal structures and can cause pain, bleeding, obstruction of urinary flow, or infection. They are crystalline structures made up of materials excreted by the kidney.

Nephrolithiasis
Formation of stones in the kidney.
Urolithiasis
Formation of stones elsewhere in the urinary tract (e.g., bladder).

Pathophysiology

Normally, dissolved substances in urine are diluted and readily excreted. Stones form when the concentration of insoluble salts in the urine is very high (supersaturation). A nucleus forms around which crystals collect, making stable bonds to form a stone. Substances serving as a nucleus can include pus, blood, dead tissue, or catheter crystals.

üîç Types & Risk Factors

Types of Stones

Stone Type Characteristics & Causes
Calcium Stones (75-80%) Composed of Calcium Oxalate or Calcium Phosphate. Associated with hypercalcemia or hypercalciuria.
Struvite Stones Magnesium-Ammonium-Phosphate. Associated with UTI caused by urea-splitting bacteria (e.g., Proteus). Can form large "staghorn" calculi.
Uric Acid Stones Develop when urine uric acid concentration is high. More common in men, associated with gout and acidic urine.
Cystine Stones Rare; associated with a genetic defect in renal reabsorption of cystine (amino acid).

Predisposing Factors

  • Dehydration: Increased urine concentration.
  • Diet: Excessive intake of calcium, oxalate (spinach, nuts), or purines (meat).
  • Immobility: Causes bone demineralization (hypercalcemia) and urinary stasis.
  • Infection: Repeated UTIs provide a nucleus for stone formation.
  • Obstruction/Stasis: Strictures, prostatic hypertrophy, tumors.
  • Metabolic: Hyperparathyroidism, Gout, Inflammatory Bowel Disease.

Renal Colic

The hallmark symptom is Renal Colic: excruciating, intermittent pain in the flank that spreads across the abdomen and may radiate to the genitalia (groin/testicles/labia). The pain is often described as one of the most severe forms of pain.

  • Hematuria: Blood in urine due to trauma from the stone.
  • Gastrointestinal: Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea (due to proximity of renal and GI nerves).
  • Local: Tenderness/dull ache in loins/back, worse on movement.
  • Infection Signs: Fever, chills, pyuria if associated with UTI.

üî¨ Diagnostic Evaluation

  • KUB X-ray: Flat plate of abdomen; identifies radiopaque stones (calcium).
  • CT Scan: Non-contrast helical CT is the gold standard; detects stones, obstruction, and hydronephrosis.
  • Renal Ultrasound: Non-invasive; detects stones and hydronephrosis.
  • IVP (Intravenous Pyelogram): Visualizes urinary tract using contrast (assess for allergy).
  • Urinalysis: Assess for hematuria, crystals, pH, and infection.
  • Blood Work: Serum calcium, phosphorus, uric acid, BUN, creatinine.
  • Stone Analysis: Chemical analysis of strained stones to determine composition.
05

üè• Management

Medical Management

  • Hydration: IV fluids to flush stones and prevent formation.
  • Analgesia: Opioids (Morphine) or NSAIDs for severe colic.
  • Dietary Changes:
    • Avoid oxalate-rich foods (spinach, nuts, tea).
    • Low purine diet (limit meat, fish, poultry) for uric acid stones.
    • Adequate fluid intake (3-4L/day).

Surgical Intervention

  • ESWL (Extracorporeal Shock Wave Lithotripsy): Uses sound waves to break stones into fragments.
  • Cystoscopy/Ureteroscopy: Removal of stones from bladder or ureter.
  • Percutaneous Nephrolithotomy: Removal of large kidney stones through a small back incision.
  • Nephrectomy: Removal of a non-functional kidney secondary to infection or hydronephrosis.

Complications

  • Obstruction: Blockage of urine flow.
  • Hydronephrosis: Distension of kidney due to urine backup.
  • Infection: Stasis leading to pyelonephritis/sepsis.
  • Renal Failure: Post-renal failure if bilateral or solitary kidney obstruction.

Nursing Care

  • Pain Management: Administer analgesics and antispasmodics.
  • Fluid Balance: Encourage high fluid intake (unless contraindicated) to facilitate stone passage.
  • Strain Urine: Strain all urine to catch passed stones for analysis.
  • Monitor: Vital signs (for infection), intake/output.
  • Ambulation: Encourage mobility to help move the stone.
  • Education: Dietary modifications and prevention of recurrence.
