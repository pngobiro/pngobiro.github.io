üìò Definition & Pathophysiology

Definition: Hydronephrosis is the distension (dilation) of the kidney with urine, caused by backward pressure on the kidney when the flow of urine is obstructed.

üîÑ

Pathophysiology

Normally, urine flows out of the kidney at extremely low pressure. Following obstruction, urine backs up into the small tubes of the kidney and the central collecting area (renal pelvis). This distends the kidney, putting pressure on delicate tissues.

Consequence: The pressure from prolonged and severe hydronephrosis ultimately damages tissue, leading to gradual loss of kidney function.

02

üîç Causes (Etiology)

1. Ureteropelvic Junction Obstruction

Obstruction located at the junction of the ureter and renal pelvis.

  • Structural abnormalities (e.g., high insertion of ureter).
  • Kinking resulting from a kidney shifting downwards (Nephroptosis).
  • Stones in the renal pelvis.
  • Compression by fibrous bands, abnormal vessels, or tumors.

2. Lower Tract Obstruction

Obstruction below the ureteropelvic junction or from backflow (reflux).

  • Ureteral: Stones, tumors, narrowing (strictures) from injury/radiation, fibrous tissue formation.
  • Bladder/Pelvic: Ureterocele, cancers of bladder/cervix/prostate.
  • Outlet Obstruction: Prostate enlargement (BPH), inflammation, or cancer.
  • Functional: Neurogenic bladder, severe UTI preventing ureteral contraction.
  • Pregnancy: Enlarging uterus compressing ureters + hormonal changes relaxing ureters (usually resolves post-partum).

Signs and Symptoms

Symptom Description
Pain Excruciating, intermittent pain in the flank (Renal Colic) OR dull aching discomfort on the affected side.
Urinary Signs of UTI, bladder discomfort.
Gastrointestinal Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain.
Systemic Blood tests may show high urea levels (azotemia) if bilateral.

Diagnostic Evaluation

  • Ultrasound Scanning: Non-invasive visualization of dilation.
  • Cystoscopy: To view bladder outlet and ureteral orifices.
  • Intravenous Urography (IVP): assess function and site of obstruction.
  • Physical Examination: Palpable kidney in severe cases.

üè• Management & Complications

Treatment Strategies

üéØ

Primary Goals

1. Promptly treat Urinary Tract Infections and Kidney Failure.
2. Relieve the urinary obstruction.

  • Acute Hydronephrosis:
    • Drainage via needle (Nephrostomy) through the skin.
    • Catheter insertion into renal pelvis to temporarily drain urine.
  • Chronic Hydronephrosis: Correct the cause.
    • Ureteral Surgery: Removal of narrow section and reimplantation.
    • Bladder Junction: Surgical detachment and reattachment of ureters.
    • Urethral Obstruction: Dilation, hormone therapy (prostate), or surgery.

Complications

Potential Complications

  • Pyelonephritis: Infection due to stasis.
  • Hypertension: Due to increased renin from ischemic kidney.
  • Renal Failure: Loss of nephron function from pressure atrophy.
