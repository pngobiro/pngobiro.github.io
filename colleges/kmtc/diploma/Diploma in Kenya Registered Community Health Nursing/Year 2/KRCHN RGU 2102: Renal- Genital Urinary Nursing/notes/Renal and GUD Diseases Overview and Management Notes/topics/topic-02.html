ü©∫ Diagnostic Tests

Various diagnostic procedures are essential for evaluating renal structure and function. Below are the primary tests utilized in renal assessment:

Test Procedure Description & Purpose
Intravenous Urography (IVP) A radio-opaque contrast is administered intravenously to visualize the kidneys, ureters, and bladder via X-ray as the dye moves through the system. Includes excretory urography and infusion drip pyelography.
Nephrotomogram Visualizes different layers and structures within the kidney. Useful to differentiate solid masses/lesions from cysts.
Urethrography Assesses urine flow through the urethra. A catheter is inserted, contrast instilled, and then removed to allow voiding visualization. Often done for suspected urethral trauma.
Cystography (Cystogram) Evaluates vesico-urethral reflux (backflow). Contrast is instilled via catheter to outline bladder walls. Can detect bladder perforation.
Renal Biopsy Removal of a small section of kidney tissue for microscopic examination, often to exclude carcinoma.
Renal Angiogram Outlines renal blood supply using contrast medium.

Additional Imaging & Function Tests

  • Abdominal X-ray (KUB): Locates kidneys, ureters, and bladder.
  • Computed Tomography (CT) Scan: Provides cross-sectional views.
  • Renal Ultrasound: Non-invasive imaging using sound waves.
  • Renal Function Tests: Assesses the ability of the glomeruli to excrete creatinine and urea.

General Symptoms

General signs of genitourinary disorders often include fever, weight loss, and malaise.

Changes in Micturition (Voiding)

Frequency
Frequent micturition may result from injury to bladder mucosa, infection, foreign bodies, or tumors. Often a functional decrease due to pain from mild bladder stretching.
Urgency
Strong desire to urinate. Involuntary urination may occur if not immediate. Voiding is usually in small volumes.
Dysuria
Difficulty voiding. Suggests irritation/inflammation of the bladder neck or urethra, or bacterial infection.
Nocturia
Excessive urination at night. May result from decreased renal concentrating ability, renal disease, diabetes mellitus, excessive evening fluids, or poor bladder training.
Enuresis
Bed wetting at night. Physiological in first 2-3 years; later may be due to delayed neuromuscular maturation, distal urethral stenosis (girls), posterior urethral valves (boys), or neurogenic bladder.
Hesitancy
Undue delay and difficulty initiating micturition, straining, and decreased force/caliber of stream. Causes: obstruction distal to bladder, prostatic obstruction, urethral stricture.
Incontinence
Loss of urine without warning. Causes: bladder atrophy, epispadias, prostatectomy, childbirth, neurogenic bladder dysfunction.

Changes in Urine Output

Term Volume Criteria Common Causes
Polyuria > 2500 ml/day Diabetes Mellitus, Diabetes Insipidus, Diuretics
Oliguria < 500 ml/day Decreased renal perfusion, acute renal failure
Anuria < 100 ml/day Anemia, anaphylactic shock, complete obstruction

Changes in Urine Appearance

  • ü©∏
    Hematuria: Blood in urine (visible or microscopic). Result of renal, vesicle, or prostatic disease, bladder tumors, or kidney stones.
  • ü¶†
    Pyuria: Pus in urine (presence of WBCs). Urine appears cloudy. Indicates infection, cystitis, pyelonephritis, urethritis, or renal tuberculosis.

Other Physical Findings

  • Pain: Felt in flank or back (between 12th rib and iliac crest), radiating to epigastrium. Tenderness over kidney. Pain may be intermittent (colic) or constant (inflammation).
  • Urinary Retention: Causes agonizing suprapubic pain.
  • Edema: Excessive water/sodium retention in extracellular space due to abnormal renal excretion.
  • Hypertension: Due to increased renin production or fluid retention.
  • Skin Changes: Pallor (anemia), excoriations (pruritus/infection).
  • Palpation: Kidney, bladder, or prostate gland may be palpable if enlarged.
