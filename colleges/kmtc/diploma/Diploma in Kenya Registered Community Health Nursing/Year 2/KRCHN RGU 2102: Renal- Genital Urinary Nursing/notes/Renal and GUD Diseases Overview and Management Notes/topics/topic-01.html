ü©∫ The Kidneys

Fig. 1.1: Overview of the Renal System context.

The urinary system consists of the kidneys, ureters, bladder, and urethra. The kidneys are bean-shaped, brownish-red structures located retroperitoneally (behind and outside the peritoneal cavity).

  • Adrenal Gland: Placed on top of each kidney.
  • Renal Parenchyma: Divided into two distinct regions: the cortex (outer) and medulla (inner).
  • Internal Structures: The medulla contains loops of Henle, vasa recta, and collecting ducts. Each kidney contains 8‚Äì18 pyramids which drain into 2‚Äì3 major calices that open to the renal pelvis.
  • Vascular Supply: Supplied by the renal artery and drained by the renal vein (branches of the abdominal aorta and inferior vena cava).

üî¨ The Nephron

Each kidney contains approximately 1 million nephrons located within the parenchyma, responsible for the initial formation of urine. The structure includes afferent and efferent blood vessels enclosed in an epithelial structure called Bowman's capsule, followed by the proximal tubule, loop of Henle, distal tubule, and collecting duct.

Renin Production

Renin is produced by specialized macula densa cells located at the distal tubules.

üöΩ Ureters, Bladder, and Urethra

Ureters

Long fibro-muscular tubes (24‚Äì30 cm long) connecting each kidney to the bladder.

‚ö†Ô∏è

Critical Narrow Areas

The ureters have three narrow areas prone to obstruction by kidney stones:

  1. Ureteropelvic junction
  2. Ureteral segment near the sacroiliac joint
  3. Ureterovesical junction

Urinary Bladder

A muscular hollow sac located behind the pubic bone. Its vesicle contains two inlets and one outlet. The bladder neck contains involuntary smooth muscles forming part of the urethral sphincter.

Layers (from outside to inside):

  1. Adventitia: Connective tissue
  2. Detrusor: Smooth muscle layer
  3. Submucosal: Loose connective tissue
  4. Mucosal Lining: Innermost layer

Urethra

Arises from the base of the bladder. In males, it passes through the penis (prostate gland lies just below the bladder neck, surrounding the urethra). In females, it opens just anterior to the vagina.

‚öôÔ∏è Physiology: Urine Formation & Functions

Urine Formation

Formed in three steps: Glomerular Filtration, Tubular Reabsorption, and Tubular Secretion.

Substances filtered, reabsorbed, and excreted include sodium, chloride, bicarbonate, potassium, urea, creatinine, and uric acid.

‚ÑπÔ∏è

Note on Glucose & Amino Acids

Amino acids and glucose are filtered at the glomerulus but are fully reabsorbed by the tubules, so neither is typically excreted in the urine of a healthy individual.

Renin-Angiotensin Pathway (RAAS)

Triggered by a decrease in renal perfusion pressure or decreased salt delivery (e.g., hemorrhage, heart failure, cirrhosis, loop diuretics, reduced salt intake).

üîÑ

RAAS Mechanism

  1. 1
    Renin Release: Angiotensinogen (liver) is converted by Renin to Angiotensin I.
  2. 2
    ACE Action: Angiotensin I is converted to Angiotensin II by Angiotensin Converting Enzyme (ACE) from the lungs.
  3. 3
    Angiotensin II Effects:
    • Powerful vasoconstriction (increases pressure)
    • Increased myocardial contractility
    • Aldosterone release (Na+ & water reabsorption, K+ excretion)
    • ADH release

Functions of the Kidney

  • Urine formation
  • Excretion of waste products
  • Regulation of electrolytes
  • Regulation of acid-base balance
  • Control of water balance
  • Control of blood pressure
  • Renal clearance
  • Regulation of red blood cell production
  • Synthesis of Vit D to active form
  • Secretion of prostaglandins
  • Regulates calcium and phosphorus balance
  • Activates growth hormone
