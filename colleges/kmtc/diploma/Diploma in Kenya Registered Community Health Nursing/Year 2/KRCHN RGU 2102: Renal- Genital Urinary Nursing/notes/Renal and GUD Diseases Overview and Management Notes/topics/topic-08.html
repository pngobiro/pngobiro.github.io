Definition: A neoplasm of the prostate gland, typically a hormone-dependent adenocarcinoma (malignant growth of glandular epithelial tissue). It is stimulated by testosterone (androgen-dependent) and inhibited by estrogen. It is one of the most common cancers in men over 65 years.

Pathophysiology

  • Mutation in abnormal cells' DNA causes rapid growth and division.
  • Tumor cells invade nearby tissue.
  • Metastasis occurs commonly to lymph nodes, lungs, liver, and bones (often presenting as pathological fractures).

Risk Factors

  • Age: >50 years (70% occur in men >65).
  • Genetics: Family history (father, brother).
  • Diet: High in red meat, dairy, fats.
  • Other: Infections (prostatitis), Obesity.

Clinical Manifestations

Category Signs & Symptoms
Obstructive Urgency, reduced stream, hesitancy (due to encroachment on bladder neck).
Local/Systemic Hematuria, painful ejaculation, weight loss, oliguria.
Metastatic Back/hip pain (spine/pelvic bone metastasis), Pathological fractures, Anemia (bone marrow involvement).
Examination Digital Rectal Exam (DRE) reveals a stony hard, fixed nodule.

Diagnosis

  • Biopsy: Confirmatory test.
  • PSA (Prostate Specific Antigen): Elevated serum levels.
  • DRE: Palpation of nodules.
  • Transurethral Ultrasound: Detects non-palpable cancer and assists staging.
  • Acid Phosphatase: Elevated levels in blood.

    • LHRH agonists (e.g., Zoladex) to suppress testicular androgen.
  3. 3
    Radiation: Teletherapy (external) or Brachytherapy (internal seeds).
  4. 4

üöΩ Cancer of the Bladder

Arises commonly from transitional cell epithelium. More common in men aged 50-70 years. Considered an "industrial disease."

Types:

  • Superficial: 80-90% of cases, arises from urothelium.
  • Squamous cell & Adenocarcinoma.

Risk Factors

  • Cigarette Smoking: Most predominant cause.
  • Industrial Chemicals: Dyes, rubber, paint.
  • Chronic Infection/Inflammation: Recurrent UTI, bladder stones.
  • Other: Pelvic radiation, high cholesterol intake.

Signs & Symptoms

Hallmark Symptom

Painless Hematuria is the most common presenting symptom.

  • Urgency, frequency of micturition.
  • Pelvic/back pain (metastasis).
  • Acute anuric renal failure (if bilateral ureteral obstruction).

Management

  • Cystoscopy: Removal of superficial cancers.
  • Cystectomy: Partial or Total removal for deep tumors.
  • Chemotherapy/Radiation.

Urinary Diversion Methods (Post-Cystectomy)

Method Description
Orthotopic Neo-bladder A reservoir connected to the urethra. Patient empties by relaxing pelvic muscles and increasing abdominal pressure. Natural voiding preserved.
Continent Cutaneous Diversion Reservoir connected to a stoma on abdominal wall. No external bag needed. Patient self-catheterizes the stoma to empty.

ü©∫ Cancer of the Kidney

Signs & Symptoms:

  • Hematuria: Common first symptom (visibly red).
  • Pain in the side (flank) and fever.
  • Hypertension (ischemia/renin).
  • Polycythemia (high blood cell count).

Management:

  • Surgery: Radical Nephrectomy (removal of kidney and lymph nodes) offers reasonable cure if localized.
  • Immunotherapy: Enhancing immune system to destroy cancer cells.

ü¶† Cancer of Renal Pelvis & Ureters

Transitional cell carcinoma affecting the lining of the renal pelvis or ureters.

  • Symptoms: Hematuria, crampy flank/abdominal pain (due to obstruction).
  • Diagnosis: IVP, Retrograde urography, CT, Cytology, Ureteroscopy.
  • Treatment: Nephroureterectomy (removal of kidney and ureter). Chemotherapy for metastasis.
