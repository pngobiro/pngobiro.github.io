üìò Overview & Indications

Definition: Dialysis is the process of removing waste products and excess water from the body using a machine or the peritoneal membrane. It helps maintain fluid and electrolyte balance when kidney function is compromised.

Purpose of Dialysis

  • Prevent accumulation of fluids and wastes.
  • Control blood pressure.
  • Regulate levels of chemical elements in blood (Sodium, Potassium).
  • Correct acidosis and replenish bicarbonate levels.

Indications

  • Uremic encephalopathy
  • Pericarditis
  • Hyperkalemia (refractory)
  • Heart failure / Fluid overload
  • Drug overdoses and poisoning
  • Severe metabolic acidosis
  • Long-term therapy for chronic kidney failure

üíâ Hemodialysis

A clinical technique where substances move from the blood across a semi-permeable membrane into a dialysis solution (dialysate) using an artificial kidney called a hemodialyzer.

The Process

  1. 1
    Blood is removed from the body via vascular access.
  2. 2
    It is pumped into the hemodialyzer where toxins and excess water are filtered out.
  3. 3
    Filtered blood is returned to the body.

Frequency: Treatment typically lasts 3‚Äì5 hours and is performed 3 times a week.

Vascular Access Types

  • Arteriovenous (AV) Fistula: Preferred for long-term use (surgically connecting artery and vein).
  • Arteriovenous Graft: Synthetic tube connecting artery and vein.
  • Vascular Access Catheter: For temporary or emergency use.

Complications

Complication Cause/Description
Hypotension Rapid removal of fluid from vascular compartment.
Muscle Cramping Fluid and electrolyte shifts.
Dysrhythmias Potassium imbalance.
Anemia Blood loss during dialysis, hemolysis.
Dialysis Disequilibrium Rapid change in urea levels causing cerebral edema (headache, nausea, confusion).
Air Embolism Air entering the machine/tubing.
Bleeding Heparin usage to prevent clotting in the circuit.

üîÑ Peritoneal Dialysis (PD)

Uses the patient's own peritoneum as a semi-permeable membrane. A sterile fluid (dialysate) containing glucose and salts is infused into the abdominal cavity.

Principles of Action

  • Diffusion: Movement of solutes from high concentration (blood) to low (dialysate).
  • Osmosis: Movement of water using glucose in dialysate as the osmotic agent.
  • Ultrafiltration: Water removal via osmotic pressure gradient.

Phases of Exchange

  1. 1
    Inflow (Fill): Prescribed amount of dialysate infused (5-10 mins). Warmed to body temperature.
  2. 2
    Dwell (Equilibration): Fluid remains in abdomen; exchange of wastes/fluid occurs (minutes to hours).
  3. 3
    Drain: Fluid drained by gravity; contains waste and excess water.

Types of PD

  • CAPD (Continuous Ambulatory PD): Manual exchanges during the day; patient is awake/active.
  • CCPD (Continuous Cycling PD): Automated machine (cycler) performs exchanges at night while patient sleeps.

Indications for PD

  • Patients who cannot withstand hemodynamic changes of hemodialysis (e.g., severe heart disease).
  • Patients desiring independence/home therapy.
  • Poor vascular access (collapsed vessels, children).
  • Diabetic patients (better glucose control).
‚ùå

Contraindications

  • Peritonitis.
  • Recent abdominal surgery or adhesions.
  • Severe respiratory disease (fluid pressure on diaphragm).
  • Abdominal malignancies or hernias.
  • Obesity (excessive fat impairs catheter function).

Complications

  • Peritonitis: Infection of peritoneum (cloudy effluent, fever, abdominal pain).
  • Exit Site Infection: Redness/purulence at catheter site.
  • Hernias: Due to increased intra-abdominal pressure.
  • Hyperglycemia: Absorption of glucose from dialysate.
  • Protein Loss: Protein passes through peritoneal membrane.

üè• Continuous Renal Replacement Therapy (CRRT)

Used primarily in the Intensive Care Unit (ICU) for hemodynamically unstable patients with acute kidney injury. It provides slow, continuous fluid removal and solute clearance (12‚Äì24 hours/day), mimicking native kidney function more closely than intermittent hemodialysis.

Common Complications:

  • Hypothermia (blood cools in extracorporeal circuit).
  • Bleeding (anticoagulation required).
  • Electrolyte imbalances.
  • Filter clotting.
