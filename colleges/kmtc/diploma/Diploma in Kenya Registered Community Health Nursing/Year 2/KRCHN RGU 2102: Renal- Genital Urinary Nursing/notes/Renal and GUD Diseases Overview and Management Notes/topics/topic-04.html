üìò Overview

Renal Failure is a condition in which the kidneys are unable to remove accumulated wastes (metabolites) from the blood, leading to altered fluid, electrolyte, and acid-base balance. It represents the inability of the kidneys to adequately filter metabolic waste products from the blood.

‚ö° Acute Renal Failure (ARF)

Definition: A rapid decline in the kidney‚Äôs ability to clear the blood of toxic substances, leading to an accumulation of metabolic products such as urea.

Key Indicators

  • Oliguria: Urine output < 400ml/day (Normal: 1000‚Äì1500ml).
  • Elevated BUN: > 20 mg/dl (Normal: 7‚Äì18 mg/dl).
  • Elevated Creatinine: > 1.2 mg/dl (Normal: 0.6‚Äì1.2 mg/dl).

Classification of Causes

‚ñ∂ 1. Pre-renal Causes (Reversible)

Factors outside the kidney reducing renal blood flow and glomerular filtration.

  • Volume Depletion: Hemorrhage, vomiting, diarrhea, burns, excessive sweating.
  • Reduced Cardiac Output: Septic shock, heart failure.
  • Systemic Dilation: Sepsis, anaphylaxis.
‚ñ∂ 2. Intra-renal Causes

Direct damage to kidney parenchyma or nephrons.

  • Acute Tubular Necrosis (ATN): Ischemia or nephrotoxins (aminoglycosides like gentamicin, mercury).
  • Glomerular Disease: Acute glomerulonephritis.
  • Vascular: Malignant hypertension, DIC.
  • Pigment Release: Myoglobinuria (rhabdomyolysis from crush injuries/burns), Hemoglobinuria (transfusion reaction).
‚ñ∂ 3. Post-renal Causes

Obstruction of urine flow out of the body.

  • Prostatic hypertrophy (BPH).
  • Calculi (stones).
  • Strictures.
  • Tumors of surrounding organs.

Clinical Phases

Phase Characteristics
1. Initiation Begins with the initiating event (e.g., hemorrhage) and ends when tubular injury occurs. Lasts hours to days.
2. Maintenance (Oliguric) Significant fall in GFR and tubular necrosis. Oliguria may develop. Fluid retention (edema), electrolyte imbalance (hyperkalemia), and azotemia occur. Lasts 10‚Äì20 days.
3. Recovery Tubule repair and regeneration. Gradual return of GFR to normal. Diuresis may occur.

üíâ Management of ARF

Emergency Resuscitative Measures

  • Hyperkalemia Management:
    • IV Glucose + Insulin: Drives K+ into cells.
    • Calcium Gluconate: Stabilizes cardiac membrane.
    • Sodium Bicarbonate: Corrects acidosis, shifting K+ into cells.
    • Kayexalate Enema: Removes K+ via GIT.
    • Dialysis: For refractory hyperkalemia.
  • Fluid Resuscitation: Prompt transfusion or fluid replacement if volume depleted.
  • Diuretics: Lasix (Furosemide) 120mg stat then 40-80mg to challenge kidneys (if not anuric).

Nursing Management (Oliguric Phase)

  • Fluid Balance: Strict input/output. Daily intake = Output + 500ml (insensible loss).
  • Daily Weight: Expect loss of 0.2‚Äì0.5 kg/day (due to catabolism).
  • Diet: High carbohydrate, low protein (1g/kg), low potassium (restrict fruits), low phosphorus.
  • Infection Prevention: Aseptic technique, monitor temperature.
  • Skin Care: Prevent pressure sores and pruritus.

üïí Chronic Renal Failure (ESRD)

Definition: A progressive, irreversible deterioration in renal function where the body's ability to maintain metabolic, fluid, and electrolyte balance fails. Resulting in uremia (azotemia).

Pathophysiology

Progressive loss of nephrons leads to a decline in GFR. Remaining nephrons hypertrophy to filter a larger load. Eventually, the kidney loses the ability to concentrate urine (polyuria) and reabsorb electrolytes. As damage continues (GFR < 20ml/min), the body retains water, salts, and nitrogenous wastes, leading to severe uremia.

Stages of CRF

Stage Nephron Loss Clinical Features
1. Reduced Renal Reserve 40‚Äì75% Asymptomatic. Polyuria and nocturia (loss of concentrating ability).
2. Renal Insufficiency 75‚Äì90% Elevated BUN/Creatinine. Anemia, oliguria, edema. Reduced response to diuretics.
3. End Stage Renal Disease (ESRD) > 90% Severe impairment. Uremia, weight loss. Requires dialysis or transplant.

ü§í Systemic Manifestations of CRF

üîÑ

Impact on Body Systems

  • Electrolytes: Hyperkalemia (dysrhythmias), Hypocalcemia, Hyperphosphatemia, Hyponatremia (initially) then Hypernatremia.
  • Hematological: Anemia (low erythropoietin), Thrombocytopenia (bleeding tendencies).
  • Gastrointestinal: Uremic fetor (ammonia breath), metallic taste, anorexia, nausea/vomiting, stomatitis.
  • Dermatological: Uremic frost, pruritus, yellow/gray skin tone, ecchymosis.
  • Cardiovascular/Pulmonary: Hypertension, pericarditis, Kussmaul breathing, pulmonary edema.
  • Musculoskeletal: Renal osteodystrophy (osteomalacia/osteoporosis) due to Calcium-Phosphorus-Vit D imbalance.
  • Neurological: Apathy, confusion, seizures, peripheral neuropathy.
06

üíä Management of CRF

Pharmacological Management

  • Antihypertensives: e.g., Calcium channel blockers (Norvasc).
  • Erythropoietin: Synthetic EPO (Eposen) and Iron supplements for anemia.
  • Phosphate Binders: Aluminum hydroxide or Calcium acetate to lower phosphate levels.
  • Calcium Supplements: To correct hypocalcemia.
  • Diuretics: Furosemide (with caution).
  • Sodium Bicarbonate: To correct metabolic acidosis.
  • Anticonvulsants: Phenobarbitone/Phenytoin for seizures.

Dietary Management

  • Low Protein: Prevents urea accumulation.
  • High Calorie: Spares protein breakdown (2000-2500 kcal).
  • Restricted Salt & Water: Reduces edema and hypertension (e.g., 1500ml/day).
  • Restricted Potassium & Phosphorus: Limit fruits, dairy.
  • Vitamin Supplements: Water-soluble vitamins (lost in dialysis).

Nursing Care

  • Maintain strict input/output chart.
  • Maintain patency of AV fistulas/grafts (no BP/venipuncture on that arm).
  • Monitor weight daily.
  • Nurse propped up for dyspnea; administer O2 if needed.
  • Skin care: Moisturizers for dry skin, measures to relieve pruritus.
  • Provide psychological support and counseling.
