Pertinent Questions

In any musculoskeletal assessment, the examiner should seek answers to the following pertinent questions:

  1. What is the patient's age and sex?
  2. What is the patient's occupation?
  3. Why has the patient come for help? (history of the present illness or chief complaint)
  4. Was there any inciting trauma (macrotrauma) or repetitive activity (microtrauma)?
  5. Was the onset of the problem slow or sudden?
  6. Where are the symptoms that bother the patient?
  7. Where was the pain or other symptoms when the patient first had the complaint?
  8. Has the pain moved or spread?
  9. What are the exact movements or activities that cause pain?
  10. How long has the problem existed? What are the duration and frequency of the symptoms?
  11. Has the condition occurred before?
  12. Has there been an injury to another part of the kinetic chain as well?
  13. Are the intensity, duration, or frequency of pain or other symptoms increasing?
  14. Is the pain constant, periodic, episodic (occurring with certain activities), or occasional?
  15. Is the pain associated with rest? Activity? Certain postures? Visceral function? Time of day?
  16. What type or quality of pain is exhibited?
  17. What types of sensations does the patient feel, and where are these abnormal sensations?
  18. Does a joint exhibit locking, unlocking, twinges, instability, or giving way?
  19. Has the patient experienced any bilateral spinal cord symptoms, fainting, or drop attacks?
  20. Are there any changes in the color of the limb?
  21. Has the patient been experiencing any life or economic stresses?
  22. Does the patient have any chronic or serious systemic illnesses or adverse social habits?
  23. Is there anything in the family or developmental history that may be related?
  24. Has the patient undergone an x-ray examination or other imaging techniques?
  25. Has the patient been receiving analgesic, steroid, or any other medication? If so, for how long?
  26. Does the patient have a history of surgery or past/present illness?

SOCRATES Assessment Tool

  • Site - Where is the pain? Or the maximal site of the pain.
  • Onset - When did the pain start, and was it sudden or gradual? Include also whether if it is progressive or regressive.
  • Character - What is the pain like? An ache? Stabbing?
  • Radiation - Does the pain radiate anywhere?
  • Associations - Any other signs or symptoms associated with the pain?
  • Time course - Does the pain follow any pattern?
  • Exacerbating/Relieving factors - Does anything change the pain?
  • Severity - How bad is the pain?

Management for Pain

  • Medications ‚Äì Analgesics, NSAIDs, COX
  • Physiotherapy ‚Äì useful with chronic musculoskeletal conditions or where there has been long periods of muscle inactivity.
  • Distraction ‚Äì music, play, TV, conversation. Used to shift attention away from the pain & onto other activities.

ü©∫ Physical Examination

In physical examination of a patient you mainly use inspection and palpation.

Inspection

Assess for the presence of the following:

  • Skin changes (e.g., erythema, pigmentation, scars)
  • Changes in shape and/or alignment of the body part (e.g., due to edema, atrophy, lumps)
  • Abnormal position (e.g., limb contractures, scoliosis)

Palpation

  • Local temperature
  • Crepitus in the joints and soft tissues
  • Swelling
  • Painful areas, tenderness
  • Tonus

Auscultation and Percussion

Are needed in the chest and abdomen damages to determinate:

  • the blood level in the cavities;
  • presence of pneumothorax;
  • changes of breathing;
  • function of intestines

Movement

  • Determination of the range of motion in the joints
  • Movements in an abnormal range or plane
  • The cause of abnormal range of motion (pain, contraction, deformity)

Movement of a joint is either active (i.e. inducted by the patient) or passive (i.e. inducted by the examiner).

üî¨ Diagnostic Procedures

Arthroscopy

Arthroscopy is primarily used to help diagnose joint problems. This procedure, most commonly associated with knee and shoulder problems, allows accurate examination and diagnosis of damaged joint ligaments, surfaces, and other related joint structures.

Arthrocentesis

Arthrocentesis (also called joint aspiration) is a procedure where a doctor uses a needle to take fluid out of a joint. Joints are where two bones meet. They allow our bodies to move. The hips, knees, ankles, elbows, shoulders, and knuckles are all joints.

üí™ Muscle Strength Testing

Definition

According to the AMERICAN COUNCIL OF EXERCISE, muscular strength is the maximal force a muscle or a muscle group can exert during a contraction.

Factors related to muscle strength are:

  • The size of the muscle fibers
  • The ability of the nerves to activate the muscle fibers

Related Terms

  • Power: The combination of muscular force and speed of movement.
  • Endurance: The ability of the muscles to exert force against resistance over a sustained period of time or repetitions is known as muscular endurance.

Purpose

  • Diagnostic
  • Examine the improvement or deterioration of patients status overtime
  • Determine the extent of strength loss
  • Help in formulating a nursing diagnosis

Methods of Muscle Strength Testing

1. Mechanical / Instrumental Muscle Testing

In this the following instruments are used:

Strain Gauge

Electrical sensor used to measure and monitor strain. Resistance changes when force is applied.

Cable Tensiometer

Two cables where one end is attached to a fixed object and the other to a limb segment. Measures in relative units.

Hand Held Dynamometer

Mainly used for measuring strength of the hand and forearm muscles. Maximum grip strength is the measure of three trails.

Modified Sphygmomanometer

Inflate the cuff up to 20mmHg. Then squeeze the cuff and note the readings.

Pinch Meter

Used to assess strength of fingers (Tip pinch, Lateral/Key pinch, Palmar pinch).

2. Manual Muscle Testing

Manual muscle testing is the most popular way to test strength. The therapist will push the body parts in specific directions while the patient will resist the pressure.

The Oxford scale OR MRC Scale is commonly used by physiotherapists to manually assess muscle strength.

Grade Score Description
0 0 No visible or palpable contraction
Trace I Visible or palpable contraction (No ROM)
Poor- 2- Partial ROM, gravity eliminated
Poor II Full ROM, gravity eliminated
Poor+ 2+ Gravity eliminated/slight resistance or < 1/2 range against gravity
Fair- 3- > 1/2 but < Full ROM, against gravity
Fair III Full ROM against gravity
Fair+ 3+ Full ROM against gravity, slight resistance
Good- 4- Full ROM against gravity, mild resistance
Good IV Full ROM against gravity, moderate resistance
Good+ 4+ Full ROM against gravity, almost full resistance
Normal V Normal, maximal resistance
