Scoliosis is a side-to-side (lateral) curvature of the spine of 10 degrees or greater.

Viewed from the rear, the spine usually appears to form a straight vertical line. In scoliosis there is a lateral curve usually combined with a rotation of the vertebrae.

Causes

  • Idiopathic
  • Congenital scoliosis: Due to congenital birth defects in the spine.
  • Neuromuscular scoliosis: Due to loss of control of nerves/muscles (e.g., Cerebral Palsy, Muscular Dystrophy).
  • Degenerative scoliosis: Caused by disc degeneration or arthritis.

Management

Observation

For mild curves. Exam every 4-6 months for children; every 5 years for adults.

Bracing

For curves between 25-40 degrees in growing children. Stops progression in 80% of cases.

Surgery

For severe curves that continue to progress.

Types of Braces

  • Boston Brace
  • Milwaukee Brace
  • SpineCor Brace
  • Cheneau Brace

Exercise Therapy

The best treatment is exercise. Goals include:

  • Symmetrical exercises: Strengthen back/abdominal muscles.
  • Breathing exercises: Increase lung volume and thorax mobility.
  • Asymmetrical exercises: Lengthen shortened muscles, contract lengthened muscles.
  • Static exercises: Release tension along the spine (hanging/traction).

‚Ü©Ô∏è Lordosis

Lordosis ("Swayback") is an inward curvature of a portion of the lumbar and cervical vertebral column.

Clinical Features

  • Prominence of buttocks
  • Back pain
  • Pain down the legs

Common Causes

  • Spondylolisthesis: Lower vertebra slips forward.
  • Achondroplasia: Dwarfism.
  • Osteoporosis: Loss of bone density.
  • Obesity: Puts higher risk for strain.
  • Osteosarcoma: Bone cancer.

Treatment

Most people don't require medical treatment unless severe. Options include:

  • Medication (pain/swelling)
  • Physical therapy (strength/ROM)
  • Weight loss
  • Braces (children/teens)
  • Surgery (severe cases with neurological concerns)

üíø Intervertebral Disc Prolapse

A prolapsed (herniated) disc occurs when the outer fibers of the intervertebral disc are injured, and the soft material known as the nucleus pulposus ruptures out of its enclosed space.

Note: In about 95% of all disc herniation cases, the L4-L5 or L5-S1 disc levels are involved.

Types of Disc Herniation

1. Protrusion / Bulge

2. Disc Herniation

3. Sequestration (Rupture)

Symptoms

  • Arm or leg pain: Lower back herniation causes pain in buttocks, thigh, calf (Sciatica). Neck herniation causes shoulder/arm pain.
  • Numbness or tingling: In body part served by affected nerves.
  • Weakness: Muscles tend to weaken, causing stumbling or inability to lift items.

Cauda Equina Syndrome

A serious complication causing bladder or bowel dysfunction (incontinence or retention) and Saddle Anesthesia (loss of sensation in inner thighs, back of legs, and rectal area).

Prevention

  • Exercise: Strengthen trunk muscles.
  • Posture: Keep back straight, lift with legs.
  • Healthy Weight: Reduce pressure on spine.
  • Quit Smoking: Smoking lessens oxygen supply to discs.
