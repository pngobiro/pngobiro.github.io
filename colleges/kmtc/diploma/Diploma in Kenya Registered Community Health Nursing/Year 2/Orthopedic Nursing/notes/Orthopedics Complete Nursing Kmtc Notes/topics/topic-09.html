üë∂ Rickets

Rickets is a skeletal disorder that affects bone development in children. It causes bone pain, poor growth, and soft, weak bones that can lead to bone deformities.

Key Fact

Rickets is caused by a lack of vitamin D, calcium, or phosphate. It is most common in children between 6 and 36 months old because they are actively growing.

Causes

  • Nutritional Rickets: Usually caused by insufficient Vitamin D intake or exposure.
    • Insufficient sunlight exposure.
    • Dietary lack of Vitamin D (vegetarian/vegan diets, lactose intolerance).
    • Malabsorption conditions (Crohn‚Äôs, celiac disease).
    • Diet very low in calcium.
  • Inherited Rickets: Genetic diseases interfering with how the body absorbs Vitamin D or handles phosphorus.

Symptoms

Skeletal Deformities

  • Bowlegs (legs that bow out)
  • Oddly shaped skull
  • Bumps in the ribcage ("Rachitic rosary")
  • Protruding breastbone (Pigeon chest)
  • Curved spine

Other Symptoms

  • Pain or tenderness in bones
  • Stunted growth/short stature
  • Bone fractures
  • Muscle cramps
  • Dental deformities (delayed formation, cavities)

Management

  • Diet/Supplements: High doses of Vitamin D and Calcium.
  • Sunlight: Encouragement to get outside for natural Vitamin D synthesis.
  • Surgery/Bracing: For severe deformities to help straighten bones.

ü¶¥ Osteomalacia

Osteomalacia is the softening of the bones in adults (essentially adult rickets). It most often occurs because of a problem with vitamin D, which helps the body absorb calcium.

Pathophysiology & Causes

Insufficient calcium absorption from the intestine due to lack of calcium or resistance to Vitamin D action leads to increased renal phosphorus loss and uncalcified osteoid.

  • Dietary deficiency (Vitamin D, Calcium)
  • Inadequate sunlight exposure
  • Malabsorption syndromes (celiac disease, small bowel resection)
  • Severe renal insufficiency or liver disease
  • Prolonged anticonvulsant therapy

Clinical Manifestations

  • Bone pain and tenderness
  • Muscle weakness, waddling gait
  • Difficulty climbing stairs
  • Pathological fractures
  • Kyphosis, rib deformities
  • Tetany (carpopedal spasm, facial twitching) due to low calcium

Diagnosis: Looser's Zones

X-rays may show transverse lines or pseudofractures known as Looser's Zones. These are stress fractures often found at the pubic rami, axillary border of the scapula, ribs, or medial cortex of the femoral neck.

Labs: Low Calcium, Low Phosphate, High Alkaline Phosphatase.

Vitamin D Recommendations

Age Group Recommended Daily Intake
0-12 months 400 IU (10mcg)
1-70 years 600 IU (15mcg)
> 70 years 800 IU (20mcg)

üìâ Osteoporosis

Osteoporosis is a disease characterized by a decrease in the density of bone, decreasing its strength and resulting in fragile bones.

Classification

Primary Osteoporosis

  • Juvenile osteoporosis
  • Idiopathic osteoporosis
  • Postmenopausal osteoporosis
  • Senile (age-related) osteoporosis

Secondary Osteoporosis

  • Congenital
  • Diet/Drugs (steroids)
  • Endocrine disorders
  • Other systemic disorders

Risk Factors

  • Biological: Female gender (estrogen loss at menopause), older age, family history, low testosterone in men.
  • Lifestyle: Inactive lifestyle, smoking, alcohol abuse.
  • Diet: Low calcium/Vitamin D, excessive protein/sodium/caffeine.
  • Medications: Long-term steroid use, anticonvulsants.
‚ö†Ô∏è

Clinical Presentation

Often called a "silent disease" because people may not know they have it until they break a bone.

  • Vertebral Fractures: Persistent back pain, loss of height, stooped posture (Kyphosis/Dowager's hump).
  • Compression Fractures: Spontaneous or low-trauma fractures.

Management Goals

  • Prevent further bone loss.
  • Increase or stabilize bone density.
  • Relieve pain and prevent fracture.
  • Improve physical function and quality of life.

Prevention Strategies

  • Adequate Calcium and Vitamin D intake.
  • Regular weight-bearing exercise.
  • Gait training and fall prevention (alarm systems, assistive devices).
  • Regular bone densitometry screening.
