Fractures & Management

üìö Orthopedic Nursing
‚è±Ô∏è 25 min read
01

üìù Definition & Classification

A fracture is a partial or complete break in the bone. It occurs when force exerted against a bone is stronger than the bone can structurally withstand.

Open Fracture (Compound)

The bone pokes through the skin and can be seen, or a deep wound exposes the bone through the skin.

Closed Fracture (Simple)

The bone is broken, but the skin is intact.

Common Types of Fractures

  • Greenstick: This is an incomplete fracture. A portion of the bone is broken, causing the other side to bend.
  • Transverse: The break is in a straight line across the bone.
  • Spiral: The break spirals around the bone; common in a twisting injury.
  • Oblique: The break is diagonal across the bone.
  • Compression: The bone is crushed. This causes the broken bone to be wider or flatter in appearance.
  • Comminuted: The bone has broken into three or more pieces and fragments are present at the fracture site.
  • Segmental: The same bone is fractured in two places, so there is a "floating" piece of bone.
02

‚ö†Ô∏è Causes, Symptoms & Diagnosis

Causes

  • Trauma: Falls, direct blows, or kicks to the body.
  • Force: Applied force stronger than the bone can take. Bones are weakest when twisted.
  • Overuse: Repetitive motions can tire muscles and put more pressure on the bone (stress fractures).
  • Pathology: Diseases that weaken the bone, such as osteoporosis or cancer.

Symptoms

Pain

Sudden and severe pain at the site.

Dysfunction

Trouble using or moving the injured area or nearby joints.

Physical Signs

Swelling, obvious deformity, warmth, bruising, or redness.

Diagnostic Tests

  • X-ray: Diagnostic test using electromagnetic energy beams to image internal tissues, bones, and organs.
  • MRI: Imaging test using large magnets and radiofrequencies.
  • CT Scan: Imaging test using X-rays and a computer for detailed images of bones, muscles, fat, and organs.

ü©∫ Neurovascular Assessment

Critical assessment for any fracture patient involves checking the Five Ps to monitor for neurovascular compromise.

ü§ö

The Five Ps

  • Pain
  • Pulse
  • Pallor
  • Paresthesia (tingling, numbness, "pins and needles")
  • Paralysis

üîß Fracture Reduction

Goal: Replace the bone fragments to as near as normal anatomical position as possible.

Closed Reduction

Manipulation is used without surgically exposing the fractures.

Open Reduction

Surgical method to expose and realign bone fragments.

05

üî© Internal Fixation (ORIF)

Aim: Prevent movement of injured parts already reduced using internal devices inserted during open reduction (e.g., screws, nails, plates).

  • Advantage: Allows direct visualisation of fracture and surrounding tissues.
  • Disadvantage: Requires anaesthesia; risk of infection.
  • Indications: Compound fractures, widely separated fragments, soft tissue interposition, extensive neurovascular injury.

Care of Patient after ORIF (Open Reduction Internal Fixation)

Goals: Complete union in 4-6 months, prevention of complications, restoration of weight bearing ambulation.

üìã

Nursing Management

  • Pre-operative: Investigations (X-ray, Hb, GXM), assessment of underlying conditions, vital signs.
  • Post-operative: Compare peripheral pulses between affected/unaffected extremities, assess mobility and self-care needs.

Complications & Interventions

  • Deep Venous Thrombosis (DVT): Clot prophylaxis (low dose anti-coagulants).
  • Pressure Ulcers: Turning schedule and care of pressure areas.
  • Acute Confusion: Manage pain, metabolic imbalances, and provide psychological support.
06

‚öôÔ∏è External Fixation

Screws are placed into the bone above and below the fracture, and a device is attached to the screws from outside the skin, where it may be adjusted to realign the bone.

  • Indication: Extensive soft tissue and bone injury where internal device may not be used.
  • Advantage: Allows client to use contiguous joints while affected areas remain immobilized.

Nursing Management

Main concerns: Neurological deficits, Compartment syndrome, Pain, Risk of infection at pin sites.

  • Prophylactic antibiotics (48-72 hours prior).
  • Observe for loose pins or 'tenting' of skin.
  • Correct use of ambulatory aids to avoid stress on fracture site.
  • Pain management (analgesics, distraction).
  • Address body image issues.
07

‚õìÔ∏è Traction

Involves a steady pull on a body part. It is both reduction and immobilization to maintain fractures apart but allowing sufficient contact for healing.

Goals: Reduce fracture, maintain alignment, overcome muscle spasm, correct deformities.

ü©π Plaster & Cast Care

External fixation device applied over skin. Skin should be intact, clean, and well padded before application. Casts are molded over the affected body part after closed reduction.

Nursing Care for Patient with a Cast

  • Patient Education: Explain procedure, care of cast, and removal time. Instruct not to insert objects into cast.
  • Skin Care: Inspect skin around cast edges for redness/irritation. Wash and oil skin after removal.
  • Positioning: Elevate limb on pillow to decrease edema.
  • Monitoring: Observe for color changes and sensation. Tingling indicates the cast may be too tight.
09

üö® Complications

Complications can occur immediately (hours to days) or over time.

  • Blood vessel and nerve damage
  • Fat embolism
  • Infections
  • Problems with union (delayed, non-union)
  • Osteonecrosis
üÜò

Acute Compartment Syndrome

Increased pressure within anatomical compartments resulting in insufficient blood supply. Neuromuscular damage becomes irreversible within 4 to 6 hours. The limb can become useless within 24 to 48 hours.

Emergency Care:

  • Monitor compartment pressures.
  • Fasciotomy may be performed to relieve pressure.
  • Pack and dress wound after fasciotomy.
