ü¶∂ Congenital Talipes (Clubfoot)

Talipes Equinovarus is a congenital disorder in which an infant is born with one foot or both feet deformed into the shape of a club. The bones, muscles, and other connective tissues are usually present though deformed in structure.

It is associated with factors such as the fetus's position in the uterus and genetic factors.

Variations of Talipes

Management

  • Non-Surgical: Progressive casting during the first months of life; special braces worn for two or more months.
  • Surgery:
    • Soft tissue surgery: Releases tight tissues and lengthens tendons.
    • Bony procedures: Breaking/resetting bone or fusing joints to stabilize.
    • Tendon transfers: Moving tendons to a different position to correct foot position.

Nursing Focus

  • Pre and post-operative management.
  • Management of a patient on Plaster of Paris (PoP) for a long period.
  • Patient/client education on the condition and outcomes.

ü¶¥ Osteogenesis Imperfecta (OI)

Osteogenesis imperfecta (also known as brittle bone disease) is an inherited (genetic) bone disorder present at birth. A child born with OI may have soft bones that break (fracture) easily, bones that are not formed normally, and other problems.

Most babies with OI have a defect in one of two genes that help form collagen, a main part of connective tissue.

Types of OI

Type Severity Characteristics
Type I Mildest (Common) Few fractures and deformities. About 50% of cases.
Type II Most Severe Short limbs, soft skull, born with fractures. Usually lethal within weeks due to underdeveloped lungs.
Type III Severe Short limbs, fractures at birth, large head, triangle face, breathing problems.
Type IV Moderate Bowing of limbs, growth retardation.
Type V-VIII Variable Ranges from moderate to severe features similar to Type IV or II/III.

Symptoms

  • Easily broken bones
  • Bone deformities (bowing legs)
  • Blue or gray sclera (white of eye)
  • Barrel-shaped chest
  • Curved spine, Triangle-shaped face
  • Loose joints, Muscle weakness
  • Hearing loss (early adulthood)
  • Soft, discolored teeth

Management

  • Bisphosphonate medicines: To strengthen bones.
  • Rodding: Metal rods inserted to stabilize long bones.
  • Care of fractures: Using lightest possible casting materials.
  • Physical/Occupational therapy: Essential for maximizing function.

Critical Nursing Interventions

  • Support limbs: Do not pull on arms or legs or lift by the legs (prevents fractures).
  • Positioning: Position with extreme care.
  • Assessment: Check circulatory, motor, and sensory abilities.
  • Nutrition: Encourage diet high in protein and vitamins; fluids to prevent renal calculi/constipation.

ü¶¥ Hip Dysplasia

A spectrum of disorders related to abnormal development of the hip, involving a shallow acetabulum, subluxation, or dislocation.

Incidence: 10 per 1000 live births; 80% are female.

Risk Factors

  • Family history
  • Intrauterine position (Breech delivery)
  • Joint laxity / Hormonal factors
  • Postnatal positioning (Swaddling)

Symptoms

In Infants

  • One leg turns out more than the other.
  • Moving one leg less than the other.
  • Uneven/wrinkled skin on thighs/buttocks.

In Older Children/Adults

  • Hip pain (groin).
  • Feeling of loose/unstable hip.
  • Limping.
  • Different leg lengths.

Diagnosis

Most babies are diagnosed before 6 months of age using physical exam and ultrasound.

Physical Exam Tests

  • Ortolani Test: Flex hips/knees to 90¬∞, abduct legs. Positive sign is a 'clunk' as femoral head relocates.
  • Barlow Test: Adduct hip while applying posterior pressure. Positive if hip pops out of socket (dislocatable).

Treatment

  • Bracing: Harness (e.g., Pavlik) to hold hips in place; usually effective if started before 6 months.
  • Surgery: Osteotomy (realign bones) or Hip Arthroscopy.
  • Physical Therapy: Strengthen muscles and improve flexibility.
