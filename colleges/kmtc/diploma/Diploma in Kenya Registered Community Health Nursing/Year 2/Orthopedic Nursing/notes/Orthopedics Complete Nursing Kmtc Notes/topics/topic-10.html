üìã Overview & Classification

A bone tumor is a neoplastic growth of tissue in bone. Abnormal growths found in the bone can be either benign (noncancerous) or malignant (cancerous).

Neoplasia: Defined as a mass of tissue formed as result of abnormal, excessive, uncoordinated, autonomous and purposeless proliferation of cells.

ü§í Clinical Manifestations

Cardinal Symptoms

1. Pain

First & most common symptom. May initially occur intermittently and only at rest. Can become more intense, disturb sleep, and spread to adjacent joints. In severe cases, it becomes excruciating requiring opiates.

2. Swelling

Long duration in benign neoplasms. In malignant tumors, swelling develops more rapidly. May cause skin changes (tensed shining skin, prominent veins, livid coloring, hyperthermia).

3. Limited Mobility

May be limited in cases of lesions close to the joint. Reactive synovitis may also cause limitation.

4. Pathological Fracture

Fracture occurring with minimal trauma. Often causes the patient to seek attention immediately. May occur with no prior symptoms (e.g., juvenile cysts).

Systemic Symptoms

  • Fever and night sweats
  • Weight loss, malaise
  • Neurologic deficit: With spinal metastasis (pain, weakness, gait abnormality, paresthesia, paraplegia).

üî¨ Assessment and Diagnosis

Differential diagnosis is based on history, physical examination, and diagnostic studies.

üë∂

Age Considerations

  • Before age 5: Metastatic neuroblastoma.
  • 5 - 15 years: Osteosarcoma or Ewing sarcoma.
  • After 40 years: Metastasis or myeloma.

Diagnostic Studies

Imaging

  • X-rays: Show location, size, and shape. Chest x-rays for lung metastasis.
  • Bone Scan: Uses radioactive material to detect bone abnormalities.
  • CT Scan: Detailed images of bones, muscles, fat, organs.
  • MRI: Detailed pictures using magnets, good for soft tissue extent.

Laboratory

  • Alkaline Phosphatase (ALP): Elevated serum levels.
  • Calcium levels: Hypercalcemia.
  • Biochemical assay: Blood and urine analysis.

Biopsy

Most conclusive test to confirm malignancy and stage.

  • Needle Biopsy: Fine-needle aspiration (thin needle) or Core needle biopsy (wider needle/tube).
  • Incisional Biopsy: Small cut made to remove a piece of tissue.

üíä Therapeutic Management

Chemotherapy

Use of anticancer drugs to kill cancer cells. Usually received in combination.

Common Drugs:

  • Doxorubicin, Cisplatin, Carboplatin
  • Ifosfamide, Cyclophosphamide
  • Methotrexate, Vincristine
‚ö†Ô∏è

Side Effects & Management

  • Nausea, vomiting, hair loss, mouth sores.
  • Hemorrhagic cystitis: From Ifosfamide/Cyclophosphamide (Prevented by Mesna).
  • Neuropathy/Nephropathy: From Cisplatin.

Radiation Therapy

High energy x-rays to kill tumor cells. Often used if chemotherapy cannot treat the tumor (e.g., chondrosarcoma) or for palliation to reduce pain.

Surgical Management

  • Removal of entire tumor with negative margins.
  • Limb Salvage: Reconstructive surgery to avoid amputation.
  • Amputation: Removal of part or all of a limb. Used if removing the cancer requires removing essential nerves/arteries that would leave the limb functionless.

üë©‚Äç‚öïÔ∏è Nursing Management

Comprehensive care focuses on managing side effects of treatment, pain control, and psychosocial support.

‚ñ∂ Acute Pain

Related to disease process, nerve compression, or inflammation.

  • Assess pain history and intensity (0-10 scale).
  • Provide non-pharmacological measures: massage, repositioning, distraction (music, TV).
  • Use complementary therapies: relaxation, visualization, cutaneous stimulation (heat/cold).
  • Administer analgesics and evaluate relief.
‚ñ∂ Altered Nutrition: Less Than Body Requirements

Related to anorexia, nausea, stomatitis from chemo/radiation.

  • Monitor daily intake and weight.
  • Encourage high-calorie, nutrient-rich diet with adequate fluids.
  • Small, frequent meals.
  • Control environmental odors.
  • Administer antiemetics.
‚ñ∂ Risk for Infection

Related to immunosuppression.

  • Promote good handwashing. Screen visitors.
  • Monitor temperature and CBC (WBC, granulocytes).
  • Avoid invasive procedures; adhere to aseptic technique.
  • Administer antibiotics as indicated.
‚ñ∂ Risk for Impaired Skin Integrity

Related to radiation and chemotherapy.

  • Assess skin frequently; note breakdown.
  • Bathe with lukewarm water/mild soap; pat dry (do not rub).
  • Turn/reposition frequently.
  • Avoid heat/washing off radiation marks.
  • Soft, loose cotton clothing.
‚ñ∂ Psychosocial Diagnoses (Self-Esteem, Fear/Anxiety)
  • Discuss effects on personal life, body image (alopecia, surgery).
  • Encourage sharing of thoughts and feelings.
  • Explain all procedures and treatments honestly.
  • Foster interaction with support systems.
