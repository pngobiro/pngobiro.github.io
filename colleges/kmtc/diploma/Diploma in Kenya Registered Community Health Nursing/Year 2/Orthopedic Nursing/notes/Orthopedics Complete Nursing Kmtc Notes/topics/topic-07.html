ü¶¥ Bone Tuberculosis

Bone tuberculosis involves infection of the skeletal system by Mycobacterium tuberculosis. It can manifest in various forms:

  • Spine: Pott‚Äôs spine (50% of all cases of skeletal Tuberculosis)
  • Joints (Tubercular arthritis): Hip, Knee (Triple deformity), Shoulder (Caries Sicca), Elbow, Wrist, and Sacroiliac joints.
  • Bones: Tubercular osteomyelitis (long/flat bones) and Tubercular dactylitis/spina ventosa (short bones).

Tuberculosis of Spine (Pott's Disease)

First described by Sir Percival Pott in 1779. It typically involves two continuous vertebrae, but skip lesions (4-10%) and solitary vertebral involvement may occur.

Clinical Features

Constitutional Symptoms
  • Malaise
  • Loss of weight/appetite
  • Night sweats
  • Evening rise of temperature
Specific Symptoms
  • Pain/Night cries
  • Stiffness/spasm of vertebral muscles
  • Localized/Persistent backache
  • Deformity (Gibbus)
  • Cold Abscess and sinuses
  • Neurological deficit

Neurological Complications

10-30% of cases develop neurological deficit. The highest incidence of paraplegia occurs with TB of the lower thoracic vertebrae. Tuberculosis remains the commonest pathology for non-traumatic paraplegia in developing countries.

Predisposing Factors

General Factors
  • Malnutrition
  • Poor sanitation & crowding
  • Close contact with TB patients
  • Immune deficiency states
Local Factors
  • Trauma
  • Increased loads
  • Decreased vascularity

ü§í Osteomyelitis

Osteomyelitis is a serious infection of the bone that can be either acute or chronic. It is an inflammatory process involving the bone and its structures caused by pyogenic organisms.

Bacteria from nearby infected tissue or an open wound circulate in the blood (hematogenous spread) and settle in bone, where they multiply.

Causative Organisms

  • Staphylococcus aureus (more than 50% of bone infections)
  • Gram-positive organisms (streptococci, enterococci)
  • Gram-negative bacteria (Pseudomonas, Salmonella, E. coli)

Risk Factors

Category Specific Factors
Trauma Severe fractures, deep puncture wounds, orthopedic surgery with implanted hardware.
Circulation Diabetes, Peripheral artery disease (smoking), Sickle cell disease.
Invasive Lines Dialysis tubing, urinary catheters, central venous lines.
Immune System Cancer treatment, corticosteroids, poorly controlled diabetes, illicit drug use.

Clinical Manifestations

Systemic Symptoms

  • Fever
  • Fatigue
  • Malaise
  • Generalized weakness (acute hematogenous)

Local Symptoms

  • Swelling, warmth, redness (erythema)
  • Pain in the area of infection
  • Purulent drainage
  • Restricted movement/altered gait

üíä Management of Osteomyelitis

Prevention

  • Postpone elective orthopedic surgery if current infection exists.
  • Prophylactic antibiotics at surgery time and for 24 hours post-op.
  • Prompt removal of urinary catheters and drains.
  • Aseptic wound care.

Nursing Management

Assessment

  • Assess for signs and symptoms: localized pain, edema, erythema, fever, recurrent drainage.
  • Assess for risk factors: diabetes, steroid therapy, previous surgery/injury.
  • Assess gait: may be altered to avoid pressure/pain.
  • Monitor for systemic reaction: weakness, elevated temperature.

Nursing Diagnoses

Acute pain related to inflammation and edema.
Impaired physical mobility related to pain and immobilization devices.
Infection related to bone abscess formation.
Deficient knowledge related to the treatment regimen.

Complications

  • Osteonecrosis (Bone death) due to impeded blood circulation.
  • Septic arthritis (spread to nearby joint).
  • Impaired growth in children (if growth plates involved).
  • Skin cancer (squamous cell) in areas of chronic draining sinuses.
