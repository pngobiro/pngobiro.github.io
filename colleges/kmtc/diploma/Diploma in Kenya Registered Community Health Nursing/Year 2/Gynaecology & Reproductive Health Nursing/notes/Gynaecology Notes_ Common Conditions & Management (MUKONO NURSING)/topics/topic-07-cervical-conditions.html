üå∞ Cervical Polyps

Cervical polyps are benign growths, usually protruding from the surface of the cervical canal. They commonly occur during the reproductive years, especially after the age of 20.

Endocervical Polyps

The most common type; usually occur in premenopausal women. They typically arise from the cervical glands in the endocervix.

Ectocervical Polyps

More common in postmenopausal women and arise from the outer surface layer cells of the cervix within the ectocervix.

Etiology & Risk Factors

  • Congestion of cervical blood vessels disrupting blood flow.
  • Infection or chronic inflammation of the cervix.
  • Abnormal response to increased estrogen levels (often associated with endometrial hyperplasia).
‚ö†Ô∏è

Risk Factors

  • Premenopausal women
  • Multigravida
  • Sexually transmitted infections
  • Previous history of cervical polyps

Signs and Symptoms

Two out of three women are asymptomatic. Symptomatic women may present with:

  • Abnormal uterine bleeding (heavy, intermenstrual, or postmenopausal).
  • Vaginal discharge (yellowish if infected).
  • Infertility (rarely, if blocking the cervical canal).

Management

Treatment depends on clinical characteristics. Asymptomatic polyps may not need intervention, but symptomatic, large, or atypical polyps warrant removal.

  • Polypectomy: Twisting and rotating off polyps with slender pedicles using ring forceps.
  • Electrosurgical Excision: Required for polyps with a thick stalk.
  • Base Cauterization: Done after removal to prevent bleeding and reduce recurrence.
  • Histological Examination: Mandatory for every excised polyp to rule out malignancy (0.2 to 1.5% may be malignant).

üî¥ Cervical Erosion

It's the lesion consisting of a zone of columnar epithelium on the vaginal portion of the cervix in place of the stratified squamous epithelium that is normally found below the external os. (Also known as Cervical Ectropion).

Pathophysiology

The cervical canal is lined by columnar cells that secret mucus while the vaginal surface of the cervix is lined by squamous cells epithelium; in cervical erosion, the columnar replaces the squamous cell giving it a red velvet appearance but does not mean that the cervix is eroded.

üîç

Clinical Features

  • Vaginal discharge that is thick, creamy yellow.
  • Postcoital bleeding.
  • Dyspareunia.
  • Disturbance in micturition.
  • Backache (rarely).

Collaborative Care

Investigations

  • Cervical smears from squamous columnar junction for cytological examination.
  • Pap smears to rule out infection like herpes simplex virus.
  • Colposcopic examination.

Treatment

Diathermy coagulation is done to lower the erosion progression rate by destroying the transformation zone containing initiated cells. Cryotherapy is also done.

üéóÔ∏è Cervical Cancer

This is a malignant neoplasm arising from cells originating in the cervix uteri. It is the second commonest cancer among women worldwide, affecting mainly ages 35-55.

ü¶†

Primary Risk Factor

Human Papilloma Virus (HPV) infection is the greatest risk factor. HPV types 16 & 18 cause ~70% of cases, while 31 and 45 cause another 10%.

Risk Factors

  • Early sexual involvement (immature genital tract).
  • Multiple sexual partners.
  • HIV infection/immunosuppression.
  • Uncircumcised partners (smegma is suspected to be carcinogenic).
  • Family history and poor nutrition.
  • Exposure to chemicals (e.g., Diethylstilbestrol).

Clinical Staging

  • Stage 0: Carcinoma in situ (pre-invasive).
  • Stage 1a: Invasion is 3mm deep. 1b: Greater than 3mm deep.
  • Stage 2a: Extends beyond cervix to upper 2/3s of vagina. 2b: Extends into parametrium but not pelvic walls.
  • Stage 3a: Involves lower 1/3 of vagina. 3b: Involves lateral pelvic walls and/or blocks ureters (hydronephrosis).
  • Stage 4a: Involves neighbouring organs (bladder, rectum). 4b: Distant metastasis (lungs, brain, liver, bones).

Management

Depends on the stage of the disease and pregnancy status.

Surgical & Medical Options

  • Stage 0 to 1a: Laser therapy, Cryotherapy, Cauterization, LEEP (Loop electrical excisional surgical procedure).
  • Stage 1b to 2a: Radical hysterectomy (removal of uterus, tissues, nodes, distal vagina) combined with bilateral salpingectomy and oophorectomy.
  • Radiotherapy: External beam or intracavity implants (radium) to kill cancer cells.
  • Chemotherapy: Cisplatin therapy or combinations (Vincristine, cyclophosphamide, Methotrexate, Bleomycin). Can be combined with radiotherapy.
‚ñ∂ Prevention Strategies
Primary Prevention
  • Vaccination with HPV vaccines (Cervarix, Quadrivalent).
  • Discourage early sexual intercourse and multiple partners.
  • Encourage male circumcision and hygiene.
  • Behavior change (avoid alcohol/drug abuse).
Secondary Prevention
  • Visual inspection with acetic acid (VIA).
  • Annual screening/Pap smears.
  • Prompt treatment of pre-invasive lesions (CIN).
