Introduction

‚û¢The beginning of modem medicine is generally related to 2 events:

  1. 1The proof by Pasteur that many diseases are caused by microorganisms.
  2. 2
    The discovery of effective anti-infective drugs.
    • sulfonamides (1938).
    • Penicillin (1940).

‚û¢Some of the bacteria and other microorganisms have adapted to the anti-infectives and became resistant to certain antibiotics.

‚û¢Most resistant strains can be eradicated by:

  1. 1New and/or different antibiotics.
  2. 2Antibiotic combinations.
  3. 3Higher dosages.

‚û¢Awareness of the problem has prompted somewhat greater scrutiny by the physician as to when and how to prescribe antibiotics.

Anti-infective Classification

‚û¢Anti-infective drugs can be divided into:-

  1. 1Bacteriostatic: Stop the multiplication and further development of the infectious agent.
  2. 2Bactericidal: eradicate (kill) all living microorganisms.

‚û¢Some anti-infectives halt the growth or eradicate many different microorganism are termed as broad-spectrum antibiotics.

‚û¢Others affect only certain specific organisms and are termed narrow- spectrum antibiotics.

General Considerations

  • ‚û¢Some of anti-infectives elicit a hypersensitivity reaction in some persons. Some Penicillins cause more sever & more frequent hypersensitivity reactions than other drugs.
  • ‚û¢Antibiotics = drugs produced by microorganisms or other live organisms to kill other microorganisms. Nowadays, many are produced synthetically.
  • ‚û¢Because of differences in susceptibility of infectious agent to antiinfectives, the sensitivity of the microorganism to the drug ordered should be determined before treatment is initiated. Several sensitivity tests are commonly used for this purpose; e.g. (Culture and sensitivity).
  • ‚û¢Certain anti‚Äìinfectives have marked side-effects (some are serious) e.g. neurotoxity, nephrotoxicity & ototoxicity.
  • ‚û¢Another difficulty is that these drugs can eradicate the normal flora in the intestine which are necessary for proper digestion, synthesis of vitamin K and control of fungi that may gain access to the GIT (super infection).

Uses of ant-infectives:

A: Prophylactic

  1. 1To protect persons exposed to a known specific organism.
  2. 2To inhibit spread of infection from a clearly defined focus as after surgery or accidents.
  3. 3To sterilize the bowel or other areas of the body in preparation for extensive surgery.

B. Acute infections:

antibiotics are used to treat acute infections such as tonsillitis and acute upper respiratory tract infections.

Cephalosporins

‚û¢Are semisynthetic antibiotics that resemble the penicillins both chemically and pharmacologically.

‚û¢Are absorbed rapidly from the GI tract and quickly reach effective concentrations in the urinary, GI and respiratory tracts except in patients with pernicious anemia or obstructive jaundice & eliminated rapidly in patients with normal renal function.

‚û¢They are broad- spectrum antibiotics that have been classified as first, second and third generation drugs. The difference among generations is based on antibacterial spectra.

‚û¢Third generation cephalosporins have more activity against gram negative organisms and resistant organisms & less activity against gram positive organisms than first generation drugs. They are also stable against beta-lactemase enzymes.

‚û¢The cost increases from 1st to 3rd generation cephalosporins.

‚û¢Action: They interfere with the final step in the formation of the bacterial cell wall resulting in unstable cell membranes that undergo lysis, also cell division & growth are inhibited.

‚û¢N.B.: 1st & 2nd generation drugs don‚Äôt enter the CSF well, but 3rd generation drugs enter inflamed meninges readily.

‚û¢Cephabsporins are excreted rapidly by the kidneys.
