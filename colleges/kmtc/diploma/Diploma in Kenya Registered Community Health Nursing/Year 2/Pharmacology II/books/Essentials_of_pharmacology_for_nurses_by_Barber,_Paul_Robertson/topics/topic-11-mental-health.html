Introduction

One in four adults in the UK experience at least one diagnosable mental health problem in any one year. This chapter examines the pharmacological management of three key areas: Anxiety, Depression, and Psychosis.

01

Anxiety

Anxiety becomes pathological when it is intolerable or disabling. It involves the defence system (fight or flight) and the behavioural inhibition system (avoidance). Key neurotransmitters involved are Noradrenaline (NA), Serotonin (5-HT), and Gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA).

Pharmacological Management

Benzodiazepines

Examples: Diazepam, Temazepam, Lorazepam.

Action: Potentiate the effect of GABA (inhibitory neurotransmitter) by increasing the frequency of chloride channel opening. Safer than barbiturates but still carry dependence risks.

‚ö†Ô∏è Prescribing Guidance

Benzodiazepines are indicated for short-term relief (2‚Äì4 weeks only) of severe, disabling anxiety. They should not be used for mild anxiety. Patients must be warned about sedation and driving risks.

Beta-blockers

Examples: Propranolol, Oxprenolol.

Action: Treat physical symptoms (palpitations, tremor, sweating) rather than psychological feelings. Useful for situational anxiety (e.g., public speaking).

Azaspirodecanediones

Example: Buspirone.

Action: Affects serotonin receptors. Non-addictive but takes 2‚Äì3 weeks to have an anxiolytic effect.

Antidepressants in Anxiety

SSRIs (e.g., Fluoxetine, Paroxetine) are often used for chronic anxiety disorders, Panic Disorder, and OCD. TCAs may also be effective.

02

Depression

Depression involves disturbances of mood, energy, appetite, and sleep. The Monoamine Hypothesis suggests depression is caused by reduced levels of neurotransmitters (Noradrenaline and Serotonin).

Classes of Antidepressants

‚ñ∂ Tricyclic Antidepressants (TCAs)

Examples: Amitriptyline.

Action: Block reuptake of Noradrenaline and Serotonin.

Side-effects: Sedation, anticholinergic effects (dry mouth, blurred vision, urinary retention), and cardiac arrhythmias (dangerous in overdose).

‚ñ∂ Monoamine Oxidase Inhibitors (MAOIs)

Examples: Phenelzine.

Action: Blocks the enzyme (MAO) that breaks down monoamines.

üßÄ The "Cheese Reaction"

Patients must avoid foods containing tyramine (cheese, red wine, pickled herring) to prevent a hypertensive crisis.

‚ñ∂ Selective Serotonin Re-uptake Inhibitors (SSRIs)

Examples: Fluoxetine, Citalopram, Sertraline.

Action: Selectively inhibit the reuptake of Serotonin (5-HT) into the presynaptic neurone, increasing synaptic levels.

Pros/Cons: Safer in overdose than TCAs. Side effects include nausea, diarrhoea, and libido loss. Effect takes 2-3 weeks to develop.

Bipolar Depression

Treated with mood stabilizers like Lithium. Lithium has a narrow therapeutic index and requires regular blood monitoring to avoid toxicity. Dehydration increases toxicity risk. Anticonvulsants like Sodium Valproate are also used.

üåø St John's Wort

This herbal remedy induces liver enzymes, reducing the effectiveness of many prescribed drugs (including oral contraceptives and other antidepressants).

03

Psychosis (Schizophrenia)

Schizophrenia involves Positive symptoms (delusions, hallucinations) and Negative symptoms (social withdrawal, apathy). It is linked to overactivity of the Dopamine system.

Antipsychotic Medication

Class Examples Mechanism & Side Effects
Typical (1st Generation) Chlorpromazine, Haloperidol, Flupentixol Blocks D2 receptors. High risk of Extra-Pyramidal Side Effects (EPSEs): Parkinsonism, dystonia, akathisia, tardive dyskinesia.
Atypical (2nd Generation) Clozapine, Olanzapine, Risperidone Blocks D1/D4 and Serotonin receptors. Fewer EPSEs but higher risk of weight gain and diabetes.
ü©∏ Clozapine Warning

Clozapine can cause agranulocytosis (loss of white blood cells). Mandatory blood monitoring is required. Patients reporting sore throats or signs of infection need immediate checking.

Depot Injections

Used for patients with poor compliance. Given via IM injection (Z-track method) to release medication slowly over weeks. Side effects are not immediately reversible.

Review: Case Studies & Calculations

Case Studies

1. Debbie Butcher (Depression)

Scenario: 37-year-old starting an SSRI after previous depression episodes.

Explanation: Explain the Monoamine theory (low serotonin). SSRIs block the "re-uptake pump" on the nerve ending, leaving more serotonin in the gap (synapse) to improve mood. Emphasize the delay in onset (2-3 weeks) before feeling better.

2. Anil Khan (Psychosis)

Scenario: 53-year-old with schizophrenia switching from typical to atypical antipsychotics.

Explanation: Typical drugs block D2 dopamine receptors mainly (causing movement side effects). Atypical drugs block D1, D4, and Serotonin receptors. They are better for "negative symptoms" (withdrawal/apathy) and have fewer movement side effects, but watch for weight gain.

3. Rona McKenzie (Anxiety)

Scenario: Architect with performance anxiety (sweating, shaking) before presentations.

Recommendation: SSRIs or Benzodiazepines are not appropriate for situational physical symptoms. Beta-blockers (e.g., Propranolol) are best to manage the physical tremors and palpitations without sedation.
