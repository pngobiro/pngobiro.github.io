Introduction

Nurses are bound by the code of conduct laid out by the Nursing and Midwifery Council (NMC). In addition, nurses are governed by the legislation surrounding medicines. Administration of medicines is a skill that you will be exposed to during your pre-registration education. It is most important that you become involved in this procedure so that you can become confident and competent.

In 2008 the NMC set out guidance for the administration of medicines highlighting the importance of identifying the correct patient, the correct drug, the correct dose, the correct site and method of administration, and the correct procedure.

Safe Administration Guidelines

1. The Correct Patient

It is essential to know the background of the person (capacity to consent, allergies, diagnosis). In acute settings, the safest way to identify a patient is by checking their wristband (identity band) against the prescription chart. This should contain core identifiers: surname, forename, DOB, and NHS number. In community settings, verbal confirmation is often used.

2. The Correct Medicine

Most medicines have two names: the generic (approved) name and the proprietary (brand) name. Prescription charts usually use the generic name, while stocks may show brand names. Packaging can be misleading; you must ensure the drug being checked matches the prescription exactly. Never transfer drugs between containers.

3. The Correct Dose

The NMC suggests nurses must have an understanding of what they are giving, not just mechanically follow instructions. Knowledge of drug actions and using the British National Formulary (BNF) is imperative. Mathematical skills are crucial to avoid under- or over-dosing. If you are unsure about a calculation, do not be rushed; always recheck answers and do not agree simply to save face.

4. The Correct Site and Method

Drugs are designed for specific routes (e.g., sublingual vs buccal). Giving a drug by the wrong route can be fatal (e.g., IV drugs given intrathecally/epidurally). Ensure you understand the specific route required (SC, IM, IV, Oral, Rectal, etc.).

Ethical and Legal Dilemmas

Covert Administration

Covert administration (hiding medicine in food/drink) to a capable adult is, in law, trespass. Treatment requires consent. If a patient lacks capacity, professionals rely on the principle of necessity (acting in best interests). Covert administration should only happen in exceptional circumstances involving multi-disciplinary decisions, not as a routine practice.

Mental Capacity and Consent

The Mental Capacity Act (2005) protects those who cannot make decisions. It allows for advanced decisions and Lasting Power of Attorney. For children under 16, the Fraser Guidelines allow professionals to treat without parental consent if the young person understands the advice and it is in their best interests (originally specific to contraception but applied broadly).

Alteration of Medicines (Crushing)

Crushing tablets or opening capsules renders the use unlicensed (outside the product licence). This alters pharmacokinetics and can cause adverse effects. A nurse doing this undertakes liability. Always seek a liquid alternative or pharmacist approval first.

Reporting Errors

The NMC encourages an open, no-blame culture. If a drug error occurs, it must be reported immediately as a duty of care. Student nurses should not ignore errors they witness.

Controlled Drugs (CDs)

Regulated by the Misuse of Drugs Act (1971) and Misuse of Drugs Regulations (2001).

Key Procedures:

  • Storage: Locked cupboard within a locked cupboard. Keys kept by the nurse in charge.
  • Ordering: Only by the nurse in charge using a specific order book.
  • Administration: Two people (one qualified nurse/doctor). Must be checked against the CD register.
  • Recording: Details entered in the register (Date, Time, Patient, Amount given, Signatures, Stock balance).

Prescribing Frameworks

Patient Group Directions (PGDs)

Written instructions for the supply/administration of medicines to groups of patients who may not be individually identified before presentation (e.g., vaccination clinics). They allow nurses to supply meds without a specific doctor's prescription for that patient.

Non-Medical Prescribing

Evolution of nurse prescribing:

  • 1992/1994: First legislation allowed District Nurses to prescribe from a limited formulary.
  • Supplementary Prescribing (2003): Nurses prescribe against a Clinical Management Plan (CMP) agreed by a doctor (Independent Prescriber).
  • Independent Prescribing (2006): Nurses can prescribe any licensed medicine (including some CDs) within their competence without a CMP.

Unlicensed and Off-label

Unlicensed: No UK license (e.g., special liquid formations).
Off-label: Licensed drug used outside its terms (e.g., different age group, dose, or indication). Prescribers must be satisfied there is no licensed alternative.

Review: Case Studies & MCQs

Case Studies

1. Mr Ajani (Unsigned Prescription)

Scenario: Staff nurse asks you to give paracetamol, but the chart isn't signed. She says she will get it signed later.

Response: A prescription chart is a direction to administer. It must be signed by a registered prescriber before administration. Administering without a signature is unsafe and illegal. You should refuse.

2. Mrs Simpson (Nurse Prescribing)

Scenario: District Nurse prescribing paracetamol under a Clinical Management Plan (CMP).

Response: The nurse must be a qualified Supplementary Prescriber (V300 course). This allows quicker access to medication and holistic care. If she couldn't prescribe, Mrs Simpson might struggle to get to a pharmacy or GP.

3. District Nurse Identity Check

Scenario: Nurse doesn't check ID in patient's home.

Response: In the community, if the nurse visits regularly, they know the patient personally. However, if you (the student) do not know the patient, you should verify identity verbally ("Hello, are you Mr X?").

4. Josep Bosko (Swallowing Difficulty)

Scenario: Patient cannot swallow tablets; drug has no licensed liquid form.

Response: Options: 1. Prescribe a licensed alternative drug that comes in liquid. 2. Prescribe an unlicensed "special" liquid. 3. Prescribe a different route (patch/suppository). 4. Crush tablets (Off-label) only with pharmacist approval.
