<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="mental-health-mental-illness">Mental health &amp; Mental illness.<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#mental-health-mental-illness" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Mental health and mental illness are difficult to define precisely. People who can carry out their roles in society and whose behavior is appropriate and adaptive are viewed as healthy. Conversely, those who fail to fulfill roles and carry out responsibilities or whose behavior is inappropriate are viewed as ill. The culture of any society strongly influences its values and beliefs, and this in turn affects how that society defines health and illness. What one society may view as acceptable and appropriate, another society may see as maladaptive and inappropriate.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="health">Health<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#health" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The World Health Organization defines health as a state of complete physical, mental, and social wellness, not merely the absence of disease or infirmity.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="concept-of-mental-health">CONCEPT OF MENTAL HEALTH<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#concept-of-mental-health" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>People in the helping professions seem to agree that mental health is a positive state in which one is responsible, displays self-awareness, is self-directive, is reasonably worry free, and can cope with usual daily tensions. Such individuals function well in society, are accepted within a group, and are generally satisfied with their lives. Other definitions refer to the ability to solve problems; fulfill one's capacity for love and work; cope with crises without assistance beyond the support of family or friends; and maintain a state of well-being by enjoying life, setting goals and realistic limits, and becoming independent, interdependent, or dependent as the need arises without permanently losing one's independence.</p>
<p>Cultural beliefs influence how mental health and mental illness are determined. For instance, acceptable behavior in one cultural group may or may not be tolerated in another group.</p>
<p>Mental health has many components, and a wide variety of factors influence it.</p>
<p><strong>Individual.</strong> Or personal, factors include</p>
<ul>
<li>A person's biologic makeup</li>
<li>Autonomy and independence</li>
<li>Self-esteem</li>
<li>Capacity for growth</li>
<li>Vitality</li>
<li>Ability to find meaning in life</li>
</ul>
<p>10</p>
<ul>
<li>Emotional resilience or hardiness</li>
<li>Sense of belonging</li>
<li>Reality orientation</li>
<li>Coping or stress management abilities</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Interpersonal or relationship, factors include</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Effective communication,</li>
<li>Ability to help others, intimacy,</li>
<li>A balance of separateness and connectedness.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Social/cultural, or environmental, factors include</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>A sense of community,</li>
<li>Access to adequate resources,</li>
<li>Intolerance of violence,</li>
<li>Support of diversity among people,</li>
<li>Mastery of the environment,</li>
<li>A positive, yet realistic, view of one’s world.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="mental-illness">Mental Illness<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#mental-illness" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The American Psychiatric Association defines a <strong>mental disorder</strong> as “a clinically significant behavioral or psychological syndrome or pattern that occurs in an individual and is associated with</p>
<ul>
<li>Present distress (e.g., a painful symptom)</li>
<li>Disability (i.e., impairment in one or more important areas of functioning)</li>
<li>Significantly increased risk of suffering death, pain, disability,</li>
<li>loss of freedom”</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="general-criteria-to-diagnose-mental-disorders-include">General criteria to diagnose mental disorders include<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#general-criteria-to-diagnose-mental-disorders-include" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Dissatisfaction with one’s characteristics, abilities, and accomplishments</li>
<li>Ineffective or unsatisfying relationships</li>
<li>Dissatisfaction with one’s place in the world</li>

<li>Lack of personal growth.</li>
<li>The person’s behavior must not be culturally expected or sanctioned.
However, deviant behavior does not necessarily indicate a mental disorder.</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="ancient-times">Ancient Times<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#ancient-times" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>People of ancient times believed that any sickness indicated displeasure of the Gods and, in fact, was a punishment for sins and wrongdoing. Those with mental disorders were viewed as being either divine or demonic, depending on their behavior. Individuals seen as divine were worshipped and adored; those seen as demonic were ostracized, punished, and sometimes burned at the stake.</p>
<p>Later, Aristotle (382–322 BC) attempted to relate mental disorders to physical disorders and developed his theory that the amount of blood, water, and yellow and blackbile in the body controlled the emotions. These four substances, or humors, corresponded with happiness, calmness, anger, and sadness. Imbalances of the four humors were believed to cause mental disorders, so treatment was aimed at restoring balance through bloodletting, starving</p>

<li>Word salad: incoherent mixture of words and phrases.</li>
<li>circumstantially: indirect speech that is delayed in reaching the point but eventually gets from original point to desired goal; characterized by an over inclusion of details.</li>
<li>Incoherence: though, that generally is not understandable; running together of thoughts or words with no logical or grammatical connection, resulting in disorganization.</li>
<li>Perseveration: persisting response to a prior stimulus after a new stimulus has been presented often associated with cognitive disorders.</li>
<li>Verbigeration: meaningless repetition of specific words or phrases.</li>
<li>Echolalia: psychopathological repeating of words or phrases of one person by another; tends to be repetitive and persistent.</li>
<li>Flight of ideas: rapid, continuous verbalizations or plays on words producing constant shifting from one idea to another; the ideas tend to be connected, and in the less severe form a listener may be able to follow them.</li>
<li>Clang association: association of words similar in sound but not in meaning; words have no logical connection may include rhyming and punning.</li>
<li>Blocking: abrupt interruption in train of thinking before a thought or idea is finished; after a brief pause; the person indicates no recall of what was being said or was going to be said.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>C. Specific disturbances in content of thought</strong>:</p>
<ul>
<li>Overvalued ideas: unreasonable, sustained false belief maintained less firmly than a delusion.</li>
<li>Delusion: false belief, based on incorrect inference about external reality, not consistent with patient’s intelligence and cultural background that cannot be corrected by reasoning.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>A. Nihilistic delusion</strong>: false feeling that the self, others, or the world is nonexistent</p>
<p>or ending.</p>
<p>B. <strong>Delusion of poverty</strong>: false belief that one is deprived or will be deprived of all material possessions.</p>
<p>C. <strong>Somatic delusion</strong>: false belief involving functioning of one’s body ex., belief that one’s brain is rotting or melting.</p>
<p>D. <strong>Paranoid delusions</strong>: include persecutory delusions and delusions of reference, control and grandeur (distinguished from paranoid ideation, which is suspiciousness of less than delusional proportions) it includes:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p><strong>Delusion of persecution</strong>:
False belief that one is being harassed, cheated, or persecuted often found in litigious patients who have a pathological tendency to take legal action because of imagined mistreatment.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Delusion of grandeur</strong>:
Exaggerated conception of one’s importance, power, or identity.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Delusion of reference</strong>:
False belief that the behavior of others refers to one self; that events, objects, or other people have a particular and unusual significance usually of a negative nature; derived from idea of reference in which one falsely feels that one is being talked about by others.
ex., belief that people on T.V or radio are talking to or about the patient.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Delusion of control</strong>:
False feeling that one’s will, thought, or feeling is being controlled by external forces it includes:</p>
</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li><strong>Thought withdrawal</strong>: Delusion that one’s thoughts are being removed from one’s mind by other people or forces.</li>
<li><strong>Thought insertion</strong>: Delusion that thoughts are being implanted in one’s mind by other people or forces.</li>
<li><strong>Thought broadcasting</strong>: Delusion that one’s thoughts can be heard by others, as thought they were being broadcast into the air.</li>
<li><strong>Hypochondria</strong>: exaggerated concern about one’s health that is based not on organic pathology but, rather, on unrealistic interpretations of physical signs or sensations as abnormal.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>
<p><strong>Obsession</strong>: pathological persistence of an irresistible thought or feeling that cannot be eliminated from consciousness by logical effort, which is associated with anxiety. (Also termed rumination).</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Compulsion</strong>: pathological need to act on an impulse that, if resisted, produces anxiety; repetitive behavior in response to an obsession or performed according to certain rules, with no true end in itself other than to prevent something from occurring in the future.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Phobia</strong>: persistent, irrational, exaggerated and in variably pathological dread of some specific type of stimulus or situation, results in a compelling desire to avoid the feared stimulus.
a. <strong>Specific phobia</strong>: dread of spiders or snakes
b. <strong>Social phobia</strong>: as in fear of public speaking, performing, or eating in public.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>{ 17 }</p>
<p>c. Acrophobia: dread of high places.
d. Agoraphobia: dread of open places.
e. Claustrophobia: dread of closed places.</p>
<p><strong>V. Speech:</strong></p>
<p><strong>A. Disturbances in speech:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Pressure of speech: rapid speech</li>
<li>Poverty of speech: restriction in the amount of speech used</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>VI. Perception:</strong></p>
<p><strong>A. Disturbances of perception:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Hallucination:
False sensory perception not associated with real external stimuli; there may or may not be a delusional interpretation of the hallucinatory experience.
a. Auditory hallucination:
False perception of sound, usually voices
b. Visual hallucination:
(ex. flashes or light)
c. Olfactory hallucination:
False perception of smell;
d. Gustatory hallucination:
False perception of taste, such as unpleasant taste
e. Tactile hallucination:
False perception of touch or surface sensation,</li>
<li>Illusion:
Misperception or misinterpretation of real external sensory stimuli.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>VII. Memory:</strong></p>
<p><strong>A. Disturbances of memory:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Amnesia: Partial or total inability to recall past experiences; may be organic or emotional in origin.
a. Confabulation: unconscious filling of gaps in memory by imagined or untrue experiences that patient believes but that have no basis in fact; most often associated with organic pathology
b. Déjà vu: illusion of visual recognition in which a new situation is incorrectly regarded as a repetition of a previous memory.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>IX. Insight:</strong>
Ability of the patient to understand the true cause and meaning of a situation (such as symptoms).</p>
<p><strong>X. Judgment:</strong>
Ability to assess a situation correctly and act appropriately within that situation.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="1-accepting-patients-exactly-as-they-are">1- Accepting patients exactly as they are<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#1-accepting-patients-exactly-as-they-are" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Acceptance is a series of positive behaviors designed to convey to the patient respect, worth, and dignity.</p>
<p>Acceptance is expressed through non-judgmental and non-punitive relationship with psychiatric patient.</p>
<p>Direct and indirect expressions of interest in the patient recognition and reflection of the patient’s feelings.</p>
<p>Talking with understanding to the patient.</p>
<p>Listening to the patient and permitting him to express feeling.</p>
<p>To accept a patient, as a person does not mean we approve his behavior.</p>
<p>Talking provides a means of conveying acceptance to patient. The conversation should be centered on the patient on his needs and wants and on his interests.</p>
<p>A. In accepting patient through a non-judgmental approach we avoid all moral judgment and its expression, a patient's behavior is no right or wrong, no good or bad
B. In an accepting a non-punitive approach to the patient, it means that, the patient should not be punished directly or indirectly for his expressions as avoiding patient except when something must be done for him, calling attention to his defects,</p>
<p>Laughing about the patient's fears which bring more state of emotional instability and impact on his self esteem.</p>
<p>20</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="2-maintaining-contact-with-reality">2-Maintaining contact with reality:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#2-maintaining-contact-with-reality" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Most persons who have developed or who are susceptible to develop behavioral disorders have difficulties with their reality checking mechanisms. In fact most normal persons have some difficulties along such lines they tend to see reality as they want it to be, rather than as it is. When such problems overwhelm individual functioning and interfere markedly with social adjustment. In the care of such patient there must not be support of the patient’s unrealistic idea or behavior.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="3-seeking-validation-from-the-patient">3-Seeking validation from the patient:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#3-seeking-validation-from-the-patient" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>It is the meaning of feelings and behavior from the patient’s point of view.</p>
<p>That is of primary importance and only patient knows how experience looks to him. Therefore the logical procedure is to seek validation from the patient to check against the nurse’s interpretation of how he sees things.</p>
<p>Provide the patient with an opportunity to correct or to confirm the conclusion as only he can do.</p>
<p>The essence of the helping relationship is the ability to convey to the patient the sense of trying to understand him.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="4-self-understanding-used-as-a-therapeutic-tool">4-Self-understanding used as a therapeutic tool:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#4-self-understanding-used-as-a-therapeutic-tool" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>In developing need to participate effectively in the care of patients with personality disorders the nurse must know how to realistically approach.</p>
<p>The problem of bringing about change within herself she will become comfortable enough in her relationship with patients to be helpful only when she feels some security about her ability to respond appropriately to patient behavior.</p>
<p>The method of approaching this problem is important because it will probably determine how effective the nurse will eventually become.</p>
<p>The nurse needs to learn to accept herself as a part of learning how to accept others; she can try to identify what she actually feels and thinks as the first step in bringing about change in herself since this will identify what is to be changed.</p>
<p>The nurse needs to analyze her own behavior and her patient’s behavior. It is often helpful to do this with someone else in the situation rather than to try to do it alone.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="5-consistency">5-Consistency:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#5-consistency" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>No standard pattern of behavior for any situation in a psychiatric setting.</p>
<p>The same words used by two different nurses will have two different meanings to the same patient.</p>
<p>The stereotyped behavior responses to types of situations are potentially dangerous.</p>
<p>The resulting inconsistency between feelings and actions lessens the nurse’s effectiveness in a relationship.</p>
<p>One of the most effective measures to promote a sense of security is consistency in experience and attitude of personnel toward the patient.</p>
<p>Consistency between feelings &amp; thoughts of nurse and her behavior (action) is very important for effectiveness in a relationship.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="6-reassurance">6-Reassurance:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#6-reassurance" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>All of us need reassurance at one time and another and psychiatric patients need it constantly.</p>
<p>Reassurance is a great deal more suitable, than telling the patient that he will get well, that his fears are groundless, that he is a nice person, and that all will end well.</p>
<p>Reassurance is building patient's confidence or restoring confidence.</p>
<p>Verbal reassurance is effective only when it does not contradict to the patient needs.</p>
<p>It can be given through awareness and acceptance of how the patient really feels.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="7-avoiding-increasing-patient-anxiety">7-Avoiding increasing patient anxiety:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#7-avoiding-increasing-patient-anxiety" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>As a general rule, fear and anxiety are already problems with which the patient has been unable to cope carefully.</p>
<p>To insist that a depressed patient cheer up or that active patient to sit down and be quiet or that withdrawn patient initiates and carries through group activities simply places the patient in the position of having failed, again failure causes anxiety.</p>
<p>In persons already insecure, the use of medical and psychiatric terminology in front of patient can often produce anxiety.</p>
<p>The nurse must be careful to avoid judgment or comments that may add to a patient's anxiety.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="8-necessity-of-motor-and-sensory-stimulation">8-Necessity of motor and sensory stimulation:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#8-necessity-of-motor-and-sensory-stimulation" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Motor stimulation can be through encouraging and asking patient to accomplish his daily routine and needs, sensory stimulation can be through frequent application of various sensory functions as hearing, vision and touch within his environment.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="9-influence-of-expectations-on-behavior">9-Influence of expectations on behavior:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#9-influence-of-expectations-on-behavior" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>It has been demonstrated in a wide variety of school situations that where failure is expected a high degree of failures occur. Where the expectation is that the students can succeed a high percentage do so.</p>
<p>The willingness to help, the level at which help is offered and ultimately the depth of the belief in human worth and dignity. All of these factors influence the expectation of behavior on the part of others.</p>
<p>21</p>
<p>For the nurse this expectation is an awareness of what she expects patient behavior to be and why, the importance of seeing the potential for growth in every patient and the active seeking for his strengths and resources became important rule in therapeutic program.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="10-change-in-patient-behavior-through-emotional-experience">10-Change in patient behavior through emotional experience:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#10-change-in-patient-behavior-through-emotional-experience" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The major focus in psychiatric therapy and in psychiatric nursing is upon the feeling aspect of the personality not upon the intellectual aspects.</p>
<p>It is necessary to try to see it from the inside the patient’s emotional need for his beliefs.</p>
<p>Reason is not an effective weapon in changing patient behavior the ideal goal of therapy is to help the patient to such a degree of emotional security that he can develop and use an understanding of his behavior such understanding cannot be forced upon him from outside nor can he use his knowledge until he can emotionally accept it. The interpretation can be done only when the patient is ready or secure enough to tolerate it and able to apply it to alter his behaviors.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="11-consideration-of-reason-for-behavior">11-Consideration of reason for behavior:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#11-consideration-of-reason-for-behavior" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Everything the patient says and does should be observed recorded and reported.</p>
<p>That information is useful for directing the patient’s therapeutic program. In addition they are helpful for the nurse's information in care planning.</p>
<p>The patient’s behavior should be analyzed to seek its motivation and to understand what the patient is attempting to accomplish.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="12-realistic-nurse-patient-relationship">12-Realistic nurse patient relationship:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#12-realistic-nurse-patient-relationship" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<p>Communication Process</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="elements-of-communication-process">Elements of communication process<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#elements-of-communication-process" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Three elements of the process: perception, evaluation, and transmission.</p>
<p><strong>Perception</strong> occurs by activation of the sensory end organs of the receiver. The impulse is then transmitted to the brain. When the sensory impulse reaches the brain, <strong>Evaluation</strong> takes place.</p>
<p>Results in two responses:</p>
<p><strong>A cognitive response</strong> related to the informational, aspect of the message, <strong>Affective response</strong> related to the relationship aspect of the message. When the evaluation of the message is complete.</p>
<p><strong>Transmission</strong> takes place. This is perceived by the sender as feedback, thereby influencing the continued course of the communication cycle.</p>
<p>Feedback stimulates perception, evaluation, and transmission by the original sender. The cycle continues until the participants agree to end it or one participant physically leaves the setting.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="therapeutic-use-of-self">Therapeutic Use of Self<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#therapeutic-use-of-self" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>By developing self-awareness and beginning to understand his or her attitudes, the nurse can begin to use aspects of his or her personality, experiences, values, feelings, intelligence, needs, coping skills, and perceptions to establish relationships with patients.</p>
<p>Nurses use themselves as a therapeutic tool to establish therapeutic relationships with patients and to help patients grow, change, and heal.</p>
<p>One tool that is useful in learning more about oneself is</p>
<p><strong>The Joharri window</strong>, which creates four areas and indicates how well that person knows himself or herself and communicates with others.</p>
<p>1: Open/public self:
Qualities one knows about oneself and others also know.</p>
<p>2: Blind/unaware self:
Qualities known only to others.</p>
<p>3: Hidden/private self:
Qualities known only to oneself.</p>
<p>4: Unknown - an empty quadrant undiscovered by oneself or others.</p>
<p>The goal is to work toward moving qualities from quadrants (2, 3, and 4) into quadrant (1) doing so indicates that the nurse is gaining self-knowledge and awareness.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="how-to-conduct-psychiatric-interview">How to conduct psychiatric interview<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#how-to-conduct-psychiatric-interview" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="introduction">Introduction:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#introduction" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>Conduct the interview for assessment is the first step of the nursing process and involves the collection, organization, and analysis of information about the patient’s health. In psychiatric mental health nursing, this process is often referred to as a psychosocial assessment. The purpose of the psychosocial assessment is to construct a picture of the patient’s current emotional state, mental capacity, and behavioral function. This assessment serves as the basis for developing a plan of care to meet the patient’s needs. The assessment is also a clinical baseline used to evaluate the effectiveness of treatment and interventions or a measure of the patient’s progress.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="environment-for-conduct-an-interview">Environment for conduct an interview:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#environment-for-conduct-an-interview" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>The nurse should conduct the psychosocial assessment in an environment that is:</p>
<ul>
<li>Comfortable, private, and safe for both the patient and the nurse.</li>
</ul>
<p>31</p>
<ul>
<li>Fairly quiet with few distractions allows the patient to give his or her full attention to the interview.</li>
</ul>
<p>The nurse should not choose an isolated location for the interview, however, particularly if the patient is unknown to the nurse or has a history of any threatening behavior. The nurse must ensure the safety of self and patient even if that means another person is present during the assessment.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="input-from-family-and-friends">Input From Family and Friends<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#input-from-family-and-friends" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>If family members, friends, or caregivers have accompanied the patient, the nurse should obtain their perceptions of the patient’s behavior and emotional state. How this is done depends on the situation. Sometimes the patient does not give permission for the nurse to conduct separate interviews with family members. The nurse should then be aware that friends or family may not feel comfortable talking about the patient in his or her presence and may provide limited information.</p>
<p>Or the patient may not feel comfortable participating in the assessment without family or friends. This, too, may limit the amount or type of information the nurse obtains. It is desirable to conduct at least part of the assessment without others especially in cases of suspected abuse or intimidation. The nurse should make every effort to assess the patient in privacy in cases of suspected abuse.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="how-to-phrase-questions">How to Phrase Questions<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#how-to-phrase-questions" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The nurse may use open-ended questions to start the assessment. Doing so allows the patient to begin as he or she feels comfortable and also gives the nurse an idea about the patient’s perception of his or her situation. Examples of open-ended questions are as follows:</p>
<ul>
<li>What brings you here today?</li>
<li>Tell me what has been happening to you.</li>
<li>How can we help you?</li>

<p>If the patient cannot organize his or her thoughts or has difficulty answering open-ended questions, the nurse may need to use more direct questions to obtain information. Questions need to be clear, simple, and focused on one specific behavior or symptom; they should not cause the patient to remember several things at once. Questions regarding several different behaviors or symptoms—“How are your eating and sleeping habits, and have you been taking any over-the-counter medications that affect your eating and sleeping?”—can be confusing to the patient.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="content-of-the-assessment">CONTENT OF THE ASSESSMENT<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#content-of-the-assessment" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<p>&gt; <strong>Stranger role:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Peplau states that when the nurse and patient first meet, they are considered to be strangers to one another. Therefore, the patient should be treated with much respect, courtesy and equally as anybody else. The nurse should not prejudice the patient or give assumptions on the patient but take the patient as they are. The nurse should treat the patient as emotionally stable, unless evidence states otherwise.</li>
</ul>
<p>&gt; <strong>Resource role:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>The nurse provides answers to questions primarily on health information. The resource person is also in charge of relaying information to the patient about the treatment and plan of care. Usually the questions are arisen from larger problems therefore, the nurse would determine what type of response is appropriate for constructive learning whether giving straightforward answers or providing information on counseling.</li>
</ul>
<p>&gt; <strong>Teaching role:</strong>
The teaching role is a role that is a combination of all roles. Peplau determined that there are two categories that the teaching role consists of: Instructional and experimental. The instructional consists of giving a wide variety of information that is given to the patients and experimental is using the experience of the learner as a starting point to later form products of learning which the patient makes about their experiences.</p>
<p>&gt; <strong>Counseling role:</strong>
Peplau believes that counseling has the biggest emphasis in psychiatric nursing. The counselor role helps the patient understand and remember what is going on and what is happening to them in current life situations. Also, to provide guidance and encouragement to make changes.</p>
<p>&gt; <strong>Surrogate role:</strong>
The patient is responsible for putting the nurse in the surrogate role. The nurse's behaviors and attitudes create a feeling tone for the patient that trigger feelings that were generated in a previous relationship. The nurse helps the patient recognize the similarities and differences between the nurse and the past relationship.</p>
<p>&gt; <strong>Leadership role:</strong>
Helps the patient assume maximum responsibility for meeting treatment goals in a mutually satisfying way. The nurse helps the patient meet these goals through cooperation and active participation with the nurse.</p>

<p>In other words :</p>
<p>The primary role of a nurse is to <strong>advocate</strong> and care for individuals of all ethnic origins and religious backgrounds and support them through health and illness. However, there are various other responsibilities of a nurse that form a part of the role of a nurse, including to:</p>
<ul>
<li>Record medical history and symptoms.</li>
<li>Collaborate with team to plan for patient care.</li>
<li>Advocate for health and wellbeing of patient.</li>
<li>Monitor patient health and record signs.</li>
<li>Administer medications and treatments.</li>
<li>Operate medical equipment.</li>
<li>Perform diagnostic tests.</li>
<li>Educate patients about management of illnesses.</li>
<li>Provide support and advice to patients.</li>
</ul>
<p>&gt; Patient Care</p>
<p>A nurse is a caregiver for patients and helps to manage physical needs, prevent illness, and treat health conditions. To do this, they need to observe and monitor the patient, recording any relevant information to aid in treatment decision-making.</p>
<p>Throughout the treatment process, the nurse follows the progress of the patient and acts accordingly with the patient’s best interests in mind. The care provided by a nurse extends beyond the administration of medications and other therapies. They are responsible for the holistic care of patients, which encompasses the psychosocial, developmental, cultural, and spiritual needs of the individual.</p>
<p>&gt; Patient Advocacy</p>
<p>The patient is the first priority of the nurse. The role of the nurse is to advocate for the best interests of the patient and to maintain the patient’s dignity throughout treatment and care. This may include making suggestions in the treatment plan of patients, in collaboration with other health professionals.</p>
<p>This is particularly important because patients who are unwell are often unable to comprehend medical situations and act as they usually would. It is the role of the nurse to support the patient and represent the patients best interests at all times, especially when treatment decisions are being made.</p>
<p>&gt; Planning of Care</p>
<p>A nurse is directly involved in the decision-making process for the treatment of patients. It is important that they are able to think critically when assessing patient signs and identifying potential problems so that they can make the appropriate recommendations and actions.</p>
<p>As other health professionals, such as doctors or specialists, are usually in charge of making the final treatment decisions, nurses should be able to communicate information regarding patient health effectively. Nurses are the most familiar with the individual patient situation as they monitor their signs and symptoms on an ongoing basis and should collaborate with other members of the medical team to promote the best patient health outcomes.</p>
<p>&gt; <strong>Patient Education and Support</strong></p>
<p>Nurses are also responsible for ensuring that patients are able to understand their health, illnesses, medications, and treatments to the best of their ability. This is of essence when patients are discharged from hospital and will need to take control of their own treatments.</p>
<p>A nurse should take the time to explain to the patient and their family or caregiver what to do and what to expect when they leave the hospital or medical clinic. They should also make sure that the patient feels supported and knows where to seek additional information, if needed, is crucial.</p>

<p>Mental health nurses on a day-to-day basis have to make practice decisions that must be consistent with a number of legal frameworks.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="stress-and-stress-management">Stress and Stress Management<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#stress-and-stress-management" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p><strong>Stress</strong>: is a physical and emotional state always present in the person as a result of living; it is intensified in a non-specific response to an internal or external environmental change or threat.</p>
<ul>
<li>Stress reactions are purposeful and initially protective; the manifestation are physiological and psychological, structural and functional, overt and covert.</li>
<li>On the positive side, stress helps to maintain equilibrium as well as increase motivation, learning, creativity, development, productivity, and satisfaction.</li>
</ul>
<p><em>Complete freedom from stress is death or a nonexistent state.</em></p>
<p>In contrast, <strong>Distress</strong> is negative, noxious, unpleasant, or damaging stress. Distress occurs when needs can’t be met or when well-being and integrity are threatened.</p>
<ul>
<li>Each period of distress leaves psychological and emotional wear and tear, which is sometimes irreversible.</li>
<li>The terms stress and distress are often used interchangeably, although all stress isn’t negative.</li>
</ul>
<p>Stressors: tension-producing internal or external stimuli, agents, or factors placing a demand upon the body, and causing intensification of the stress state and disequilibrium.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="predisposing-factors">Predisposing Factors<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#predisposing-factors" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>A variety of elements influence how an individual perceives and responds to a stressful event. These “predisposing” factors strongly influence whether the response is adaptive or maladaptive.</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p><strong>Genetic influences</strong> are those circumstances of an individual’s life that are acquired by heredity. Examples include family history of physical and psychological conditions (strengths and weaknesses) and the individual’s temperament (behavioral characteristics present at birth that evolve with development).</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Past experiences</strong> are occurrences that result in learned patterns that can influence an individual’s adaptation response. They include previous exposure to the stressor or other stressors, learned coping responses, and degree of adaptation to previous stressors.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Existing conditions</strong> incorporate vulnerabilities that influence the adequacy of the individual’s physical, psychological, and social resources for dealing with adaptive demands. Examples include current health status, motivation, developmental maturity, severity and duration of the stressor, financial and educational resources, age, existing coping strategies, and a support system of caring others.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>44</p>
<ul>
<li>The person’s survival and health status depends on the intensity, duration, and location of the stress and the adaptive capacity of the person.</li>

During this stage, the physiological responses of the “fight or flight” syndrome are initiated.</p>

<p>Keeping track of your moods in a mood diary can help you work out what affects your mental wellbeing and recognize changes in your mood. For example, you may realize that eating certain foods or seeing a certain person has an effect on your mood. Or you may tend to experience a particular mood at a particular time, such as in winter. Knowing what affects your moods can help you take steps to avoid or change the situations that have a negative impact on you. Even if you can't change the situation, knowing your triggers can help you remember to take extra care of yourself during difficult times. You can create your own mood diary.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="12-look-after-yourself-during-difficult-times">12-Look after yourself during difficult times<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#12-look-after-yourself-during-difficult-times" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Everyone has times when they face challenging situations and find it difficult to cope. If you are experiencing a difficult time, or are unwell, it's important to look after yourself and try and get through. Be careful not to put too much pressure on yourself to carry on as normal. You may need to take a break from your usual responsibilities, for example reducing your social activities or workload. Take small steps and don't expect too much of yourself. Try to get enough sleep and eat regularly. If you are finding it difficult to cope on your own, don't be afraid to ask for help. For example, you may need time off work</p>
<p>47</p>
<p>or help with day-to-day tasks, such as cleaning or childcare. Stay safe. If your feelings become overwhelming, and you have suicidal thoughts or you think you may self-harm, remember that you can pick up the phone at any time of night or day and talk to the Samaritans.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="13-learn-to-accept-yourself">13-Learn to accept yourself<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#13-learn-to-accept-yourself" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The more I am at peace with myself and who I am, the more I am likely to be at peace with others and who they are.</p>
<p>One of the most important steps in staying mentally healthy is to learn to accept you. If you value yourself, you are more likely to have positive relationships with other people and find it easier to cope with difficult times in your life.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="here-are-some-tips-to-help-you-increase-your-self-esteem">Here are some tips to help you increase your self-esteem :<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#here-are-some-tips-to-help-you-increase-your-self-esteem" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Try not to compare yourself to other people.</li>
<li>Don't strive for perfection.</li>
<li>Acknowledge your positive qualities and things you are good at.</li>
<li>Learn to identify and challenge unhelpful thinking patterns.</li>
<li>Use self-help books and websites to help you change your beliefs.</li>
<li>Spend time with supportive people.</li>
<li>Be assertive – don't allow people to treat you with a lack of respect.</li>
<li>Engage in work and hobbies that you enjoy.</li>
</ul>
<p>What helped me was the realization that this is who I am, to stop fighting it and realize that life may not be what you expected it to be... Then, just start living again.</p>
<p>Learning to accept who you are can be very difficult, particularly if you are trying to do it on your own. If you feel that low self-esteem is having a significant impact on your mental health, you may find it helpful to talk your feelings through with a therapist.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="what-can-i-do-to-stay-mentally-well-if-i-have-a-mental-health-problem">What can I do to stay mentally well if I have a mental health problem?<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#what-can-i-do-to-stay-mentally-well-if-i-have-a-mental-health-problem" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Here are some tips that are directly relevant to maintaining your mental wellbeing if you have a mental health diagnosis.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="1-be-involved-in-your-treatment">1-Be involved in your treatment<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#1-be-involved-in-your-treatment" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>If you have a mental health problem, being involved in your treatment can help you stay well. Research shows that the more involved you are in decisions about your treatment, the more likely you are to recover from a mental health problem. Any mental health professional you see should discuss your treatment options with you, and you have the right to be involved in the decision-making process at every stage in your treatment.</p>
<p>48</p>
<p>However, under compulsory treatment, the doctor in charge of your treatment can override your preferences if they think another treatment is the most appropriate one for your mental health problem at the time.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="2-manage-your-medication">2-Manage your medication<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#2-manage-your-medication" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>If you are on medication, it is important to learn to manage this in a way that works for you. For example, if your medication makes you feel drowsy, you may want to ask your doctor if you could take it in the evening. Or you may find that you feel better if you avoid certain foods.</p>
<p>If you experience side effects because of your medication, you should discuss this with your doctor or psychiatrist. They can help you decide whether to continue taking the medication and give you advice about how to manage any side effects.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="3-tell-people-what-helps">3-Tell people what helps<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#3-tell-people-what-helps" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Different things work for different people when they're unwell. Telling your friends, family and any medical or social care professionals what works for you can help them understand what you need if you become ill. For example, you may want to let your doctor know that a particular therapy or medication has worked for you in the past. Or you may want to tell your friends and family that it helps to talk through your feelings or that you prefer to be left alone. You may also want to make a crisis plan, or advance decision, to tell people what you want to happen if you are in crisis. This can help reduce stress and address any worries about what will happen to you or your family if you become ill.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="what-other-support-is-available">What other support is available?<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#what-other-support-is-available" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>You may find that, despite your best efforts, you are unable to maintain your mental wellbeing on your own. In this case, you might want to seek professional help to address whatever is affecting your mental wellbeing.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="1-practical-help">1-Practical help<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#1-practical-help" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>If there is a particular situation that is affecting your mental wellbeing, the best thing to do is to seek specialist practical help to resolve the problem. Having someone professional who is familiar with your type of situation can see it objectively and usually get to the root of the problem more quickly. For example, if:</p>
<ul>
<li>You are caring for someone and finding the situation difficult, there are support groups that can help.</li>
<li>Problems at work are affecting your mental wellbeing, read your organization's policies to find out what rights you have and what support mechanisms are available.</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="2-peer-support">2-Peer support<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#2-peer-support" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Peer support means talking to people who have been through similar experiences; for example, in a support group. It can be a useful source of support and understanding. However, if you're accessing peer support online, think carefully about what information you want to share – you don't always know who you're talking to.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="3-mindfulness">3-Mindfulness<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#3-mindfulness" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Mindfulness is a way of paying attention to the present moment, using techniques like meditation, breathing exercises and yoga. It has been shown to help people become more aware of their thoughts and feelings, so that instead of being overwhelmed by them, it is easier to manage them.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="4-talking-treatments">4-Talking treatments<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#4-talking-treatments" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>If you are facing problems that are affecting your mental wellbeing, and you can't resolve this by yourself, you may find a talking treatment helpful.</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Cognitive behavior therapy (CBT) is a form of therapy that aims to identify connections between your thoughts, feelings and behavior, and to help you develop practical skills to manage them. It has been shown to be particularly effective for low self-esteem and anxiety-based conditions.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>If your problems stem from early life experiences you might find that other talking therapies, such as person-centered therapy, psychodynamic therapy or interpersonal psychotherapy (IPT), can help you address these experiences more thoroughly.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>If you want to try a talking treatment, your doctor can provide information and refer you to a local service.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="5-medication">5-Medication<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#5-medication" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>If you are unable to resolve any difficulties you are having yourself, and your feelings develop into a mental health problem, such as anxiety or depression, you may be offered prescription medication by your doctor. These drugs don't cure mental health problems, but aim to ease the most distressing symptoms.</p>
<ul>
<li>Many people find these drugs helpful, as they can lessen symptoms and allow them to continue with their normal activities. However, drugs can have side effects that may make some people feel worse rather than better. Your doctor should talk you through the potential advantages and disadvantages of taking any psychiatric medication and discuss possible alternative treatments.</li>

<li>solution-focused therapy.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="psychological-interventions">Psychological interventions<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#psychological-interventions" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>When working with children and adolescents with mental health problems the mental health nurse needs to be competent in delivering the required interventions. The nurse may in some cases be supervised by a specialist in this area or they may be required to follow an agreed plan of care, which may include:</p>
<ul>
<li>therapeutically engage and support recovery</li>
<li>take a “strengths approach”</li>
<li>work in partnership</li>
<li>listen and respect the individual’s experiences.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="aging-population-and-mental-health">Aging population and mental health<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#aging-population-and-mental-health" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="definitions">Definitions<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#definitions" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>As individuals live longer we have an increasingly aging population, and mental health nurses need to be able to respond effectively to this change.</p>
<p>Generally a functional disorder is different from an organic disorder in that organic disorders result from an identified biological cause whereas in functional disorders there is no apparent biological cause.</p>
<p>Functional disorders in older adults include:</p>
<ul>
<li>personality disorders</li>
<li>depression</li>
<li>anxiety disorders</li>
<li>mania</li>
<li>psychotic disorders.</li>
</ul>
<p>The assessment, diagnosis and management of these functional disorders is the same for older adults as for younger adults.</p>
<p>52</p>
<p>53</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="clinical-features">Clinical features<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#clinical-features" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>The normal aging process may mean that an individual experiences a number of changes, which can be physical, psychological, social and spiritual. These changes and also how society generally views the aging process can have an impact upon the presentation, diagnosis and treatment of a given functional disorder:</p>
<ul>
<li>Older adults are less likely to report low mood or suicidal thoughts.</li>
<li>Depression tends to be viewed as part of the aging process and due to this the appropriate treatment is not always given.</li>
<li>Certain psychiatric medications are to be used with caution where there is a coexisting physical illness.</li>
<li>Traumatic events such as a fall or a physical illness such as a stroke can lead to the onset of a functional disorder.</li>
<li>Mania is more likely to present in terms of irritability rather than overt elation.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="risk-factors">Risk factors<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#risk-factors" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Depression and anxiety disorders are the most common functional disorders diagnosed in older adults; the prevalence rates with all the functional disorders, except bipolar disorder, increase with age. Similar to functional disorders in younger adults the cause of functional disorders in older adults is not clear but there are a number of risk factors that may increase an individual’s vulnerability:</p>
<ul>
<li>the presence of a dementia increases the risk of depression</li>
<li>physical illness increases the risk of depression or mania</li>
<li>being socially isolated increases the risk of depression or a psychotic disorder</li>
<li>being in a nursing home or residential care increases the risk of depression</li>
<li>being a career for someone with dementia increases the risk of depression;</li>
<li>traumatic events increase the risk of an anxiety disorder</li>
<li>loss can increase the risk of depression or a psychotic disorder.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="management">Management<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#management" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Treatment is dependent on the functional disorder and its presentation, the individual’s circumstances and their specific needs. Ensuring the individual is not socially isolated is a key part of the recovery process. In terms of interventions treatment guidelines recommend:</p>
<ul>
<li>cognitive behavioral therapy – individual or group</li>
<li>treat any underlying physical illnesses</li>
<li>guided self-help</li>
<li>psycho education</li>
<li>group therapy</li>
<li>structured physical activity</li>
<li>behavioral activation</li>
<li>relaxation techniques</li>
<li>psychiatric medication</li>
<li>ECT in severe cases (depression).</li>
</ul>
<p>[ 54 ]</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="psychological-interventions">Psychological interventions<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#psychological-interventions" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>When nursing older adults diagnosed with a functional disorder it is important that the individual is thoroughly assessed; this includes establishing whether there is an underlying physical condition. Also the risk assessment and management process needs to be sensitive to the increased risk of completed suicide in older adults. Depending on the skill of the nurse the types of interventions they may deliver are:</p>
<ul>
<li>establish a collaborative therapeutic relationship</li>
<li>normalize an individual’s experiences of mental distress</li>
<li>reduce by modifying thought processes and enhancing coping strategies</li>
<li>prevent social isolation and promote social functioning</li>
<li>problem solving</li>
<li>signpost to self-help and relevant support groups.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="1-general-rules-related-to-psychotropic-medications">1- General rules related to psychotropic medications:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#1-general-rules-related-to-psychotropic-medications" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>The nurse should ensure that the patient receive his/her medication as prescribed. Some patients may refuse to take their psychotropic medication.</li>
<li>The nurse should instruct the patient to continue to take medication, even if feeling well. Symptoms may return if medication discontinued.</li>
<li>There should be a careful assessment of the medication therapeutic effects, the patient’s view and understanding of the medication regimen.</li>
<li>The nurse should reassure the patient that side-effects will be temporary and subside. In addition, the nurse should develop a plan of care to help patients deal with such side-effects in order to increase patient’s adherence to medications.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="d4c506c6">2- Nurse’s role toward side-effects of antipsychotics:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#d4c506c6" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>An important part of the psychiatric nurse’s role is to identify, minimize, and intervene with side-effects experienced by the patients as a result of their medication regimens. The followings are the care in this respect:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Patients should be instructed to rise slowly from a sitting or lying position, sit first with their legs dangling, wait for a minute, and sit or lie down if they feel faint, to prevent a sudden drop in blood pressure. Also, check blood pressure before giving medication in vulnerable patients can be helpful.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Dry mouth can be a troubling symptom. The patient can be advised to rinse out their mouths frequently with water and to chew sugarless gum. Also, good oral care (frequent teeth brush) is very important.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>For constipation, encourage foods high in fiber, and physical activity.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Oral antipsychotics may be administered with food to minimize gastrointestinal upset.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Use caution when driving or operating dangerous machinery. Drowsiness and dizziness may occur.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Patients are susceptible to photosensitivity. The patients should be instructed to not spend more than 30 minutes in sun, and should use sunscreens and wear protective clothing and sunglasses.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Patients should be instructed to not drink alcohol while on medication. These drugs potentiate each other's effect.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>In relation to appearance of extrapyramidal side-effects, stay with the patient and offer reassurance and support during this frightening time. The antiparkinsonian (anticholinergics) drugs should be administered, e.g., Cogentin and Akineton. Prophylaxis with antiparkinsonian drug may prevent the development of such symptoms.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>In tardive dyskinesia, antiparkinsonian (anticholinergic) drugs should not be given; Inderal (beta blocker) should be administered.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>To control weight gain, develop a realistic plan for a balanced food intake and encourage physical exercises.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="3-nurse-s-role-toward-complications-of-antipsychotics">3- Nurse's role toward complications of antipsychotics:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#3-nurse-s-role-toward-complications-of-antipsychotics" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Report occurrence of any of the following symptoms to the physician immediately: sore throat, fever, malaise, unusual bleeding, persistent nausea/vomiting, severe headache, muscle twitching, pale stool, yellow skin or eyes. If complications occurred, the drugs should be discontinued and appropriate medical treatment should be provided.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="as-related-to-a-granulocytosis">As related to a granulocytosis:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#as-related-to-a-granulocytosis" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<ul>
<li>Isolate the client.</li>
<li>White blood cell (WBC) count usually done every week, when the symptoms subside; it should be done every two months.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="as-related-to-obstructive-jaundice">As related to obstructive jaundice:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#as-related-to-obstructive-jaundice" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<ul>
<li>Bed rest and high protein and carbohydrate diet should be given.</li>
<li>Liver function tests every 6 months should be performed.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="as-related-to-neuroleptic-malignant-syndrome">As related to neuroleptic malignant syndrome:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#as-related-to-neuroleptic-malignant-syndrome" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<ul>
<li>Cool body to reduce fever.</li>
<li>Maintain hydration with oral and I.V fluids.</li>
<li>Correct electrolyte imbalance.</li>
<li>Arrhythmias should be treated.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="4-nurse-s-role-toward-compliance-with-medication">4- Nurse's role toward compliance with medication:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#4-nurse-s-role-toward-compliance-with-medication" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Medication compliance is defined as the extent to which patients follow medication regimens and treatment instructions as prescribed by the health care providers. Patients who do not follow the medication regimen as prescribed are</p>
<p>55</p>
<p>known as noncompliant. A major challenge to nurses in the psychiatric practice is medication noncompliance or non-adherence. Previous studies reported that up to 80% of all psychotic patients do not take their medications as prescribed. Noncompliance leads to severe symptoms, relapse, frequent hospitalization, and significant costs.</p>
<p>There are many factors affecting patient’s compliance with psychotropics, they may include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Lack of insight.</li>
<li>Unpleasant side-effects.</li>
<li>Suspiciousness.</li>
<li>Stigma.</li>
<li>Lack of knowledge about the illness and medication.</li>
<li>Lack of support from psychiatric professionals and family members.</li>
<li>Fear of addiction.</li>
</ul>
<p>Psychosocial nursing interventions for improving medication compliance:</p>
<ul>
<li>The first step to improve drug compliance is to identify and recognize patient’s reasons for noncompliance.</li>
<li>Explore patient’s attitudes, beliefs, and concerns about medications and their effects.</li>
<li>Assist patients in acquiring knowledge about medications, including actions, precautions, side-effects, signs of toxicity, and drug interactions.</li>
<li>Explain natures of and time span for onset of the drugs therapeutic results.</li>
<li>Help patients to develop strategies for dealing with common side-effects and missing doses of medication.</li>
<li>Relate medications to the target symptoms associated with patient’s illness.</li>
<li>Provide written and verbal instructions about medication to reinforce compliance.</li>
</ul>
<p>56</p>
<p>Nurse's role for antidepressant drugs:</p>
<ul>
<li>Teaching the patient and family about tricyclic antidepressant.</li>
<li>Tell them that the mood elevation may take from 7 to 25 days. It may take up 6-8 weeks for full effect you take place and for major depression symptoms to subside.</li>
<li>Reassure the patient that drowsiness, dizziness, and hypotension usually subside after the first few weeks.</li>
<li>When the patient starts taking tricyclic antidepressant, caution the patient to be careful working around machines, driving cars, because of possible altered reflexes.</li>
<li>Alcohol can block the effects of antidepressants. Tell the patient to refrain from drinking.</li>
<li>If the patient forgets the bedtime dose (or the once—day dose), the patient should take the dose within 3 hours; otherwise, the patient should wait for the next dose. The patient should not double the dose.</li>
<li>Avoid sudden stopping TCAs. It can cause nausea, altered heart beats, nightmares and cold seats in 2-4 days.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="nurse-s-role">Nurse's role:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#nurse-s-role" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>1- Instruct the patient and the patient family to avoid certain foods that contain tyramine.
2- Instruct patient to go to the emergency room right away if he develop severe headache.
3- Ideally, blood pressure should be monitored during the first six weeks of treatment.
4- After stopping the MAOI, the patient should maintain dietary and drug restrictions for 14 days.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="nurse-s-role-for-use-of-ssris">Nurse's role for use of SSRIs:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#nurse-s-role-for-use-of-ssris" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="patient-and-family-teaching">Patient and family teaching<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#patient-and-family-teaching" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<ul>
<li>Because of the potential for drowsiness, patient shouldn't drive operate machinery until these side effects are ruled out.</li>
<li>Avoid alcohol.</li>
<li>Don't discontinue medication abruptly; abrupt withdrawal can lead to serotonin withdrawal syndrome.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="nurse-s-role-in-treatment-with-lithium">Nurse's role in treatment with lithium:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#nurse-s-role-in-treatment-with-lithium" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The patient and the patient family should instruct about the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Lithium can treat your current problem and will also help prevent relapse, so it is important to continue with the drug after the current episode is resolved.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Lithium blood level should be monitored as mentioned before.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Lithium is irritating to the gastric mucosa, so take it with meals.</p>
</li>
<li>

<p><strong>Nursing Care for ECT</strong></p>
<p><strong>Emotional support and education</strong></p>
<p><strong>Pretreatment nursing care</strong></p>
<p>Providing quality nursing care for the patient receiving ECT include evaluating the pretreatment protocol to ensure that has been followed according to the hospital policy.</p>
<ul>
<li>This involves reviewing recommended consultation, nothing that any abnormalities in laboratory tests have been addressed.</li>
<li>And checking that equipment and supplies are adequate and functional.</li>
<li>A crash care with defibrillator should be ready available for emergency use. Patient preparation for ECT is similar to that for any brief surgical procedure.</li>
<li>General anesthesia is required, so fluids should be withheld from the patient for 6-8 hours before treatment to prevent the potential for aspiration.</li>
<li>The exception to this NPO status is in the case of patients who routinely receive cardiac medication and antihypertensive agents. These drugs should be administered several hours before treatment with small sips of water.</li>
<li>The patient should be encouraged to wear comfortable clothing which include loose-fitting clothes, pajamas, or a hospital gown, preferably clothing that can be opened in the front to facilitate the placement of monitoring equipment.</li>
<li>The patient's hair should be clean and dry to facilitate optimal electrode contact.</li>
<li>The patient should void immediately before receiving ECT to help prevent incontinence during the procedure and to minimize the potential for bladder distention or damage.</li>
</ul>
<p>59</p>
<p>Nursing care during the procedure</p>
<p><img src="assets/images/image-20251225-1208d277.jpeg" alt="img-7.jpeg"></p>
<p>The patient should be brought to the treatment suite either ambulatory or by wheelchair, accompanied by a nurse with whom the patient feels at ease.</p>
<ul>
<li>The nurse should remain with the patient throughout the treatment to provide support.</li>
<li>On arrival the patient should be introduced to the member of treatment team and given a brief explanation of each person 'role in ECT procedure.</li>
<li>The patient should then be assisted onto a stretcher and asked to remove shoes and socks.</li>
<li>This allows for the placement of blood pressure cuff on an ankle and clear observation of the patient's extremities during the treatment. Once the patient is positioned comfortably on the stretcher, a member of anesthesia staff inserts a peripheral intravenous line. One member of treatment team should explain the procedure while it is occurring.</li>
<li>Electroencephalographic monitoring consists of two or more electrodes placed on the forehead and mastoid. A set of three -lead ECG is placed on the patient's chest. A pulse oximeter is clipped to the patient's finger to monitor oxygen saturation.</li>
<li>Blood pressure monitoring throughout the treatment is accomplished by either manual or automatic cuff. A peripheral nerve stimulator helps to determine muscle relaxation.</li>
<li>The patient 'a head is then cleansed with mild soap at the site of electrode contact. This cleaning process facilitates optimal stimulus electrode contact during treatment.</li>
<li>Once the preparation is completed an ant cholinergic agent, such as glycopyrrolate (1.0- 0.4mg) or atropine(0.3-0.6mg), may be administered intravenously to decrease oral secretion and cardiac bradyarrhythmias in response to the electrical stimulus.</li>
</ul>
<p>60</p>
<p>[ 61 ]</p>
<p>Next an anesthetic, usually methohexital, or etomidate (0.15 mg/kg), is administered. When the patient is asleep the blood pressure cuff on the ankle is inflated, allowing it to serve as a tourniquet, so motor seizure activity can be visualized in that extremity. A muscle relaxant, succinylcholine (usual dose approximately 0.75 mg/kg) or rapacuronium (a nondepolarizing muscle relaxant recently approved by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) (usual dose 1.5 mg/kg) is then administered to minimize the patient's motor response to the ECT treatment.</p>
<p>Progressive muscle relaxation is monitored by the nerve stimulator, as well as by observing the patient for the cessation of muscle twitching.</p>
<p>As the muscle relaxant takes effect, the anesthesiologist provides oxygen by mask to the patient through positive pressure ventilation. Although most muscle become completely relaxed, jaw muscles are stimulated directly by the ECT, causing the patient's teeth to clench. This creates the need for a bite block to be inserted to the patient's mouth by the treatment nurse before the electrical stimulus.</p>
<p>This device prevents tooth damage and tongue or gum laceration during the stimulus.</p>
<p>The patient's chin is firmly supported against the bite block during delivery of the brief electrical stimulus.</p>
<p>After delivery of the stimulus, the bite block may be removed.</p>
<p>The electrical stimulus causes a brief generalized seizure.</p>
<p>Motor signs of the seizure can be observed in the cuffed foot.</p>
<p>Characteristic EEG changes also may be observed.</p>
<p>One member of the treatment team records the time elapsed during the seizure.</p>
<p>A seizure lasting 5-20 seconds is considered adequate to produce a therapeutic effect.</p>
<p>Seizures lasting longer than 2 minutes should be terminated to prevent a prolonged postictal state.</p>
<p>The seizure may be terminated by using a benzodiazepine, such as midazolam, or additional anesthetic given at have the induction dose.</p>
<p>Anesthesia staff continuously ventilate the patient with oxygen during the procedure until the patient is able to breathe spontaneously.</p>
<p>The nurse should monitor vital signs both before and after the ECT treatment.</p>
<p>Once the patient is stabilized, the anesthesiologist clears the patient for transfer to the recovery area.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="post-treatment-nursing-care">Post treatment nursing care<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#post-treatment-nursing-care" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The recovery area should be adjacent to the treatment area to provide accessibility for anesthesia staff in case of an emergency.</p>
<p>The area should contain oxygen, suction, pulse oximeter, vital sign monitoring, and emergency equipment.</p>
<p>The area should be adequately staffed and provide minimal sensory stimulation.</p>
<ul>
<li>once the patient is in the recovery area with pulse oximeter in place, a staff member should observe the patient until awakening.</li>
<li>When the patient awakens, a nurse should discuss the treatment and check vital signs as indicated.</li>
<li>The nurse should provide frequent reassurance and reorientation at frequent intervals.</li>
<li>Providing brief, distinct direction is most beneficial.</li>
<li>When the patient appears ready to return to the hospital room, the nurse verifies that the patient 'vital signs, oxygen saturation, and mental status have returned to an acceptable level.</li>
<li>Wheelchair transport from the recovery area is advisable</li>
<li>The ECT treatment nurse provide been information about the patient to the nursing staff.</li>
<li>This includes medications that have been given to the patient, as well as any change in the procedure that may impact the patient 'condition upon return to the unit.</li>
<li>Patients should be observed at least once every 15 minute. If the patients is agitated, confused, or restless, one to one observation may be required until the patient 'condition has stabilized.</li>
<li>If the patient is awake, the level of orientation should be assessed every 30 minutes until mental status returns to baseline.</li>
<li>After assessing the return of the gag reflex, medications and a meal may be offered.</li>
<li>When fully awake the patient should be on fall precautions.</li>

<li>The patient may respond well to restricted environmental stimulation, and frequent nursing contacts focusing on reorientation are helpful.</li>

<p>Recovery</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="introduction">Introduction<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#introduction" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Recovery as a therapeutic process is an integral part of mental health nursing practice. As a process mental health nurses often see recovery in terms of eliminating or controlling symptoms of mental distress. This view is quite a narrow, as recovery should be a whole-person approach where the meaning of recovery is embedded within the hopes and aspirations of the individual. Recovery is also about social inclusion where individuals are supported to live meaningful lives within society.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="competencies">Competencies<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#competencies" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Mental health nurses are required to:</p>
<ul>
<li>Effectively engage with individuals with mental health problems in a way that is person-centered and also promotes social inclusion and recovery.</li>
<li>Ensure their practice is recovery-focused whatever the context or setting and that it values, respects and explores the meaning of an individual’s mental distress.</li>
<li>Promote the self-determination and expertise of individuals with mental health problems while using their personal qualities and interpersonal skills to develop and maintain a recovery focused therapeutic relationship.</li>
<li>Work with people living with mental distress, other professionals and agencies to shape services in a way that aids recovery.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="the-context">The context<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-context" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Utilizing a recovery-based approach presents a significant challenge for mental health nurses especially where there are professional and policy drivers for such an approach but no single agreed definition of recovery. On this basis recovery should be seen as being relative to the individual and their circumstances, meaning that the recovery process for that individual is being constantly redefined by their ever changing needs. The challenge for the mental health nurse in these circumstances is that they need to be both receptive and responsive to the service user’s ever-evolving needs in a way that their practice is positively redefined by these experiences. Even though recovery as a process is relative in nature it can be seen to have aims that include:</p>
<ul>
<li>promoting wellbeing</li>
<li>maximizing opportunity</li>
<li>empowering individuals to take control</li>
<li>facilitating and supporting the individual in finding meaning and purpose.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="the-recovery-process">The recovery process<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-recovery-process" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Recovery can also be described in terms of a process that includes the following features:</p>
<ul>
<li>A whole-person approach is taken rather than just focusing on symptoms.</li>
</ul>
<p>63</p>
<ul>
<li>Recovery is viewed as a “journey rather than a destination”.</li>
<li>Optimism, commitment and hope are key values.</li>
<li>Support should be systematic but also innovative.</li>
</ul>
<p>The components of recovery</p>
<p>There are a number of “models” of recovery such as the:</p>
<ul>
<li>collaborative recovery model</li>
<li>strengths model</li>
<li>tidal model</li>
<li>wellbeing and recovery action plan approach.</li>
</ul>
<p>The tidal model is particularly pertinent to mental health nurses as it has been created by mental health nurses in collaboration with mental health service users. As an approach the tidal model is made up of three key components or domains:</p>
<ul>
<li>Self-domain – narrative or story-telling component.</li>
<li>World domain – where this narrative component is shared with others.</li>
<li>Others domain – this is where recovery is enacted through the care delivery process.</li>
</ul>
<p>The recovery approach</p>
<p>The mental health nurse in the recovery process must be able to support the individual in a way that the individual’s story is actively valued as a core part of the care delivery process. The nurse must also recognize that interventions have to be outcome focused but they also have to be adaptable to changing need.</p>
<p>Building on this the nurse must also be aware of the factors that positively influence the recovery process these include:</p>
<ul>
<li>positive and sustainable relationships</li>
<li>meaningful activity</li>
<li>autonomy</li>
<li>resilience</li>
<li>personal growth</li>
<li>a healthy living environment</li>
<li>a supportive social network.</li>
</ul>
<p>64</p>
<p>Physical health and wellbeing</p>
<p>Introduction</p>
<p>The role of the mental health nurse is to promote good physical health and wellbeing. This is especially important when considering that individuals diagnosed with a severe mental health problem are more likely to experience physical health problems than the general population. The first stage of promoting good physical health and wellbeing is to ensure that an individual’s physical health needs are identified. This process should take the form of regular physical assessments; any needs identified should be addressed through an integrated and holistic package of care. Mental health nurses will also be required to deliver physical health care and/or signpost individuals to the appropriate services</p>
<p>Competencies</p>
<p>Mental health nurses are required to:</p>
<ul>
<li>promote physical health and wellbeing through education, role modeling and effective communication</li>
<li>deliver physical care that meets the essential needs of people with mental health problems</li>
<li>recognize and respond to the physical needs of all individuals who come into their care</li>
<li>be able where required to signpost an individual with physical and mental health problems to the appropriate service.</li>
</ul>
<p>The context</p>
<p>Physical health problems are common in individuals diagnosed with severe mental illness, such as depression, schizophrenia and bipolar affective disorder. The types of physical health problems include:</p>
<ul>
<li>cardiovascular disease</li>
<li>respiratory problems</li>
<li>diabetes</li>
<li>digestive disorders</li>
<li>obesity;</li>
<li>musculoskeletal diseases</li>
<li>cancer – lung, colorectal and breast cancer</li>
<li>viral infections.</li>
</ul>
<p>It is also important to recognize that physical ill health can lead to a mental health problem. It is not uncommon for enduring physical health problems to be comorbid with depression; these include:</p>
<ul>
<li>cancer</li>
<li>heart disease</li>
</ul>
<p>65</p>
<ul>
<li>diabetes</li>
<li>musculoskeletal problems</li>
<li>respiratory problems.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="factors">Factors<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#factors" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>There are numerous factors that may account for a higher incidence of physical ill health in individuals with mental health problems.</p>
<p>These factors include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Psychiatric medication – some medications increase the risk of obesity, diabetes and cardiac problems.</li>
<li>Lifestyle – individuals with mental health problems have a higher rate of smoking, and drug and alcohol misuse; they also tend to eat less well, and exercise less.</li>
<li>Social – indirect factors such as poverty, poor housing, and unemployment may also have an adverse impact.</li>
</ul>
<p>There are also protective factors that keep mental health service users physically well, such as:</p>
<ul>
<li>supportive and nurturing social networks</li>
<li>employment</li>
<li>self-awareness and having a sense of hope</li>
<li>having a healthy lifestyle.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="physical-health-assessment">Physical health assessment<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#physical-health-assessment" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Even when these factors are taken into account, individuals with mental health problems are less likely to have their physical health needs recognized compared to the general population. On this basis a physical health assessment should include:</p>
<ul>
<li>the gathering of baseline physical health data including a medical history</li>
<li>a physical examination including baseline observations</li>
<li>baseline investigations including blood tests.</li>
</ul>
<p>After the initial assessment the individual should be monitored annually, usually by their general practitioner.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="managing-physical-health">Managing physical health<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#managing-physical-health" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The mental health nurse’s role in managing an individual’s physical health is to be a health promoter; this includes:</p>
<ul>
<li>providing education about medication and its side-effects</li>
<li>providing dietary advice or signpost to a dietician</li>
<li>promoting the benefits of physical exercise and monitoring weight</li>
<li>providing smoking cessation advice</li>
<li>liaising with the GP where required</li>
<li>signpost to family planning and sexual health services where required.</li>
</ul>
<p>66</p>
<p>A key part of health promotion is to work collaboratively with the individual to change unhealthy behaviors by:</p>
<ul>
<li>recognizing what behavior needs to change</li>
<li>developing an agreed action plan</li>
<li>implementing the plan of action</li>
<li>providing encouragement however small the change</li>
<li>monitoring outcomes</li>
<li>developing agreed strategies that maintain change</li>
<li>if the change does not happen, try again.</li>
</ul>

<li>The function of documentation</li>
<li>Documentation is used in many contexts and is used for a number of purposes such as:</li>
<li>Improving accountability.</li>
<li>Presenting and supporting the clinical decision-making process.</li>
<li>Supporting effective communication.</li>
<li>Providing documentary evidence of the care delivered.</li>
<li>Supporting the clinical risk management process.</li>
<li>Supporting clinical audit, research and the complaints process.</li>
<li>Documentation standards</li>
<li>The following is a summarized list of those standards; it is recommended that the guidance is read in full:</li>
<li>Handwriting should be legible and all entries should be fully signed, with the date and time.</li>
<li>The entry should be accurate, factual and the meaning clear with no unnecessary jargon.</li>
<li>Professional judgment should be used to decide what should be recorded.</li>
<li>Information related to a service user’s care should be fully recorded.</li>
<li>Records should not be altered and/or destroyed without the relevant authorization.</li>
<li>Any authorized alteration must be fully signed with the original entry record still clearly readable or auditable.</li>
<li>Ensure that the record-keeping process adheres to the relevant professional and legal frameworks as well as national and local policies.</li>
<li>Service users and carers should where appropriate be involved in the record-keeping process.</li>
<li>Information that is not clinically relevant should not be kept.</li>
<li>Service users need to be aware that their clinical records may be seen by other people or agencies involved in their care.</li>
<li>Improving record keeping</li>
<li>It is essential that mental health nurses adhere to the professional guidance on record keeping. But they also need to reflect on how they can continually improve their practice. When engaging in this process of reflection it might be useful to consider:</li>
<li>Does your entry provide accurate evidence of the standard of care delivered?</li>
<li>Is it person-centered?</li>
</ul>
