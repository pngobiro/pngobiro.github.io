üö® Psychiatric Emergencies

üîç Click to zoom

Definition

A condition where the patient has disturbance of thought, affect, and psychomotor activity leading to a threat to his existence or threat to people in the environment. Requires immediate intervention to safeguard life.

Common Emergencies

  • Suicidal threat / tendency
  • Violence / Aggression / Excitement
  • Panic attacks
  • Catatonic stupor
  • Delirium tremens (Alcohol withdrawal)
  • Drug-induced extrapyramidal symptoms
  • Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome (NMS)

üÜò Suicide & Self Harm

Definitions

  • Suicide: Act of taking one's life.
  • Deliberate Self Harm: Intentionally inflicting harm/injury on oneself.
  • Para-suicide: A cry for help without intention of killing oneself.
  • Suicidal Attempt: Unsuccessful suicidal act with non-fatal outcome.

Types (Durkheim)

Egoistic

Lack of integration/sense in society.

Altruistic

Self-sacrifice for society.

Anomic

Loss of direction/regulation in life.

Fatalistic

Result of oppressive situations.

Nursing Management

  • Safety First: Remove dangerous objects (glass, belts, razors). Windows/bulbs should not be accessible.
  • Observation: 24-hour monitoring. Frequent, irregular checks (especially at night).
  • Medication: Ensure swallowing (cheek for hoarding). Keep drugs locked.
  • Interaction: Do not joke about death. Be sensitive. Do not argue about feelings. Help identify positive aspects.

üò§ Violence & Aggression

Signs of Impending Violence

  • Recent acts of violence/damage to property.
  • Verbal or physical threats (menacing).
  • Progressive psychomotor agitation.
  • Paranoid features or command violent hallucinations.
  • Carrying potential weapons.

Management Strategies

4 Progressive Strategies

  1. De-escalation: Defusing/talking down.
  2. Environmental Manipulation: Removing triggers/audience.
  3. Rapid Tranquilization: Chemical restraint.
  4. Restraints & Seclusion: Physical containment (last resort).

Rapid Tranquilization

Mixture of benzodiazepines and antipsychotics offers best results.

  • Diazepam: 5-10 mg PO/IV
  • Lorazepam: 2mg IV
  • Haloperidol: 5-10mg IM
  • Olanzapine: 5-10mg IM

Self-Protection Guidelines

  • Never see a potentially violent patient alone.
  • Keep comfortable distance; be prepared to move.
  • Maintain a clear exit route (don't let patient block door).
  • Do not wear dangling attire (ties, necklaces).
  • Stand at an angle (open posture), not directly in front.

üõãÔ∏è Therapies in Mental Health

Therapeutic Nurse-Client Relationship

Goal-oriented, directed at learning and growth promotion. Requires rapport, trust, respect, and empathy.

Phases:

  1. Pre-interaction phase
  2. Orientation/Introductory Period
  3. Working Phase
  4. Termination Phase

Group Therapy

Treatment of individuals within a group setting. Learning/problem-solving where individual is involved with "own self" and "collective self".

Curative Factors (Yalom)

  • Universality: Realizing problems are not unique.
  • Altruism: Opportunity to help others boosting self-esteem.
  • Instillation of Hope: Seeing others progress.
  • Group Cohesion: Sense of belonging.
  • Catharsis/Ventilation: Release of intense feelings.

Electroconvulsive Therapy (ECT)

üîç Click to zoom

Induction of grand mal seizure via electrical pulse under anesthesia. 70-130 volts. Course: 8-12 sessions.

‚ñ∂ Indications & Contraindications

Indications: Severe depression (suicidal, refusal to eat), Mania, Catatonic Schizophrenia (excitement or stupor).

Relative Contraindications: Increased ICP (Absolute?), Recent MI, Vascular aneurysms, Retinal detachment.

‚ñ∂ Nursing Care

Pre-treatment

  • Informed consent, NPO (4 hrs min).
  • Remove dentures, jewelry, hair pins. Empty bladder.
  • Pre-medication (atropine to dry secretions).

During Procedure

  • Supine position, pillow under lumbar.
  • Administer anesthetic and muscle relaxant (scoline).
  • Oxygenation. Support jaw/limbs.

Post-treatment

  • Monitor vitals. Side-lying position.
  • Reorient patient (confusion/memory loss common).

üíä Pharmacotherapy

1. Antidepressants

  • Tricyclics (TCAs): Amitriptyline, Imipramine. Side effects: Anticholinergic (dry mouth, blurred vision, urinary retention), Cardiac toxicity in overdose.
  • SSRIs: Fluoxetine, Paroxetine. Safer in overdose. Side effects: GI upset, insomnia, sexual dysfunction.
  • MAOIs: Phenelzine. Danger: Hypertensive crisis with Tyramine-rich foods (Cheese, wine, avocado).

2. Mood Stabilizers

Lithium Carbonate

Therapeutic Range: 0.6 ‚Äì 1.2 mmol/L. Narrow therapeutic index.

Lithium Toxicity

Vomiting, coarse tremors, ataxia, confusion, seizures. Prevention: Maintain sodium/fluid intake. Avoid diuretics/NSAIDs.

Others: Carbamazepine (Tegretol), Sodium Valproate (Epilim).

3. Antipsychotics (Neuroleptics)

Block dopamine receptors. Treat psychosis (Schizophrenia, Mania).

  • Typical (1st Gen): Chlorpromazine (sedating), Haloperidol (high potency).
  • Atypical (2nd Gen): Olanzapine, Risperidone, Clozapine (risk of Agranulocytosis).

Side Effects (EPSEs): Dystonia, Parkinsonism, Akathisia, Tardive Dyskinesia.

Public Health Model of Prevention

Level Goal Interventions
Primary Reduce incidence (new cases). Prevent onset. Health education, stress management, parenting skills, identifying high-risk groups.
Secondary Reduce prevalence (duration/severity). Early diagnosis & treatment. Screening, crisis intervention, acute treatment, referral.
Tertiary Reduce residual defects (disability). Rehabilitation. Social skills training, vocational rehab, aftercare, support groups.
