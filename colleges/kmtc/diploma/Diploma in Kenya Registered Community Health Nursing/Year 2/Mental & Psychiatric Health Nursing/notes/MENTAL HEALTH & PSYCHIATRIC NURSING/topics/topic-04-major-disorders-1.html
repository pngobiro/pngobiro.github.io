Major Depressive Disorder (MDD)

Definition: Presence of depressed mood or loss of interest in pleasure with four or more symptoms for at least 2 weeks.

Diagnostic Symptoms

  • Feeling of worthlessness or guilt
  • Impaired concentration
  • Loss of energy or fatigue
  • Suicidal thoughts (passive or active)
  • Loss or increase of appetite and weight
  • Insomnia (initial, middle, or terminal) or excessive sleep
  • Psychomotor retardation or agitation

Forms of Depression

  • Reactive Depression (Exogenous): Experienced in response to external stressors/circumstances (e.g., death of family member, divorce).
  • Endogenous Depression: No obvious cause; believed to originate from within (genetic nature).

Etiology & Risk Factors

  • Genetic: Incidence higher among relatives (50% of people with MDD have 1st-degree relative with mood disorder).
  • Biochemical: Low levels of monoamines (Serotonin and Norepinephrine).
  • Cognitive: Narrow negative view of self, environment, and future.
  • Psychosocial: Unemployment, loss, stress, violence/abuse.
  • Gender: Women are twice as likely as men.

Management of Depression

‚ñ∂ Medical Management
  • First Line: TCAs (unless contraindicated by cardiac disease/urine retention).
  • Alternatives: SSRIs (safe in overdose, fewer side effects).
  • Physical Therapy: ECT indicated for severe depression with suicidal risk or resistance to drugs.
  • Relapse Prevention: Continue anti-depressants for minimum 6 months after resolution.
‚ñ∂ Nursing Management
  • Encourage expression of emotions (crying, ventilation).
  • Safety: Assess suicidal tendency and keep vigil.
  • Physiological: Monitor food intake and sleep patterns. Discourage day sleep.
  • Activity: Provide non-intellectual activities (cleaning, exercise) to discharge tension.
  • Education: Teach regarding drugs (lag time of 2-3 weeks for effect, side effects, avoid alcohol).

‚ö° Bipolar Disorder (Mania)

Characterized by episodes of mania and depression.

Mania: Clinical Features

  • Mood: Expansive, irritable, or extremely high ("top of the world").
  • Cognition: Delusions of grandiosity (special mission from God), Flight of ideas.
  • Behavior: Hyperactivity/psychomotor agitation, pressured speech, excessive make-up/jewelry.
  • Functioning: Easy distractibility, marked impairment in social/occupational activities.

Etiology

Genetic factors (runs in families) and Biochemical factors (excessive biogenic amines: norepinephrine and serotonin).

Management of Mania

Medical

  • Mood Stabilizers: Lithium, Sodium Valproate, Carbamazepine, Lamotrigine.
  • Antipsychotics: Olanzapine, Haloperidol (control behavior/psychosis).
  • Benzodiazepines: Lorazepam (complement antipsychotics).

Nursing Care

  • Avoid verbal confrontation; patient easily irritated.
  • Maintain therapeutic calm environment (reduce noise/lights).
  • Provide consistent limits on dressing and activities.
  • Nutrition: Ensure food intake (patient too busy to eat); supervise meals/fluids to prevent dehydration.
  • Channel energy into active games/occupational therapy.

üìä Other Mood Disorders

Disorder Characteristics
Disruptive Mood Dysregulation Disorder Chronic severe irritability; frequent temper outbursts (3-4 times/week). Developmentally inappropriate. Onset > 1 year.
Premenstrual Dysphoric Disorder Significant affective symptoms week prior to menses. Marked lability, depressed mood, irritability. Present in all cycles for past year.

üß© Schizophrenia

Definition & Pathophysiology

Common psychotic disorder characterized by abnormalities in perception, beliefs, and thought processing. Associated with dopamine hyperactivity in the mesolimbic cortex/basal ganglia.

Clinical Features

‚ñ∂ Positive Symptoms
  • Hallucinations: Auditory (voices), Visual, Olfactory, Tactile.
  • Delusions: Persecutory, Reference, Control/Influence.
  • Thought Alienation: Insertion, Broadcasting, Withdrawal.
  • Formal Thought Disorder: Loosening of associations, word salad, neologisms.
  • Behavior: Excitement, agitation, hostility.
‚ñ∂ Negative Symptoms
  • Alogia: Difficulty speaking, reduced output/fluency.
  • Asociability: Impairment in social relationships.
  • Avolition/Apathy: Lack of energy/grooming.
  • Anhedonia: Inability to feel pleasure.
  • Affective Blunting: Decreased facial expression.

Types of Schizophrenia

Type Key Characteristics
Paranoid Delusions of persecution or grandeur.
Disorganized Incoherence, foolishness, regressive behavior.
Hebephrenic Silly/childish behavior, prominent affective symptoms.
Catatonic Marked motor disturbance. Stupor: Mutism, rigidity, waxy flexibility. Excitement: Restlessness, increased speech.
Undifferentiated Variety of symptoms ("Simple" schizophrenia).
Residual Absence of prominent positive symptoms; presence of negative symptoms/odd beliefs.

Diagnostic Criteria Sets

Schneiderian First Rank Symptoms

  • Auditory hallucinations
  • Delusions of control
  • Thought broadcasting, withdrawal, insertion
  • Somatic passivity (bodily sensations imposed by external force)
  • Made volition (acts under external control)

Bleuler's Four A's

  • Autistic thinking: Unable to relate with others/environment.
  • Ambivalence: Contradictory emotions simultaneously.
  • Associative looseness: Inability to think logically.
  • Affective disturbance: Inappropriate emotional response.
05

üíä Management

Medical Management

  • Conventional Antipsychotics: Chlorpromazine (sedating), Haloperidol, Fluphenazine.
  • Atypical Antipsychotics: Clozapine (risk of agranulocytosis), Risperidone, Olanzapine, Quetiapine.
  • Depot Injections: E.g., Modecate (fluphenazine) for poor compliance (chronic phase).
  • ECT: Indicated for catatonic schizophrenia or severe depressive symptoms.

Nursing Management

Priority: Safety

Ensure safety of patient and others. Remove dangerous objects. Protect from absconding and suicide.

  • Provide less stimulating environment (dim light, less noise).
  • Approach with calmness, empathy, and gentle eye contact.
  • Provide structured environment with scheduled routine ADLs.
  • Delusions: Distract patient from delusions by engaging in constructive activities. Do not reinforce but do not argue.
  • Physiological: Provide food/fluid to meet needs. Assist with self-care.
  • Apply mechanical restraints strictly if justified/needed for safety.
