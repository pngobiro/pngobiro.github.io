üí° Concepts and Principles in Mental Health

Introduction

  • All humans strive to attain the highest level of health.
  • Mental health does not mean absence of mental illness but a sense of wellbeing felt.
  • Mental health and physical health are interrelated and interdependent.

Definitions

üìò

Health (WHO 1948)

‚ÄúA state of complete physical, mental and social well-being and not merely the absence of disease or infirmity‚Äù

üß†

Mental Health (WHO)

The capacity of an individual to form harmonious relationships with others and to participate in or contribute constructively to changes in the social environment.

‚ö†Ô∏è

Mental Illness/Disorder

‚ÄúA clinically significant behavioral or psychological syndrome or pattern that occurs in an individual and that is associated with present distress or disability or with a significantly increased risk of suffering death, pain, disability, or an important loss of freedom... and is not merely an expectable... response to a particular event‚Äù (APA; DSM-IV TR)

Alternatively: ‚ÄúMaladaptive responses to stressors from the internal or external environment, evidenced by thoughts, feelings, and behaviors that are incongruent with the local and cultural norms, and interfere with the individual‚Äôs social, occupational, and/or physical functioning‚Äù (Townsend, 2009)

Consequences of Mental Illnesses

  • Poverty
  • Poor/inhumane living conditions
  • Human rights violation
  • Marginalization & vulnerability
  • Higher rates of mortality & disability
  • Physical abuse
  • Sexual abuse
  • Neglect
  • Loss of civil and political rights

Determinants of Mental Health

  • Individual factors: e.g. ability to manage one‚Äôs thoughts, emotions, behaviours and interactions with others.
  • External factors: Social, Cultural, Economic, Political, and Environmental.

üå± Aetiological Factors of Mental Illness

Classification of Causes

The causes of mental illness can be classified into three broad categories:

  1. Predisposing Factors: Determine the likelihood of one getting a mental illness. Usually they are adverse experiences one undergoes in early life.
  2. Precipitating Factors: Events that take place shortly before the onset of a disorder. E.g. drug abuse, loss of a job.
  3. Perpetuating Factors: Once the disorder has been triggered, these factors do prolong the course of the disease. They may appear long after the original predisposing factors have been treated. Examples include secondary demoralization and withdrawal from social activities.

Specific Factors

1. Biological Causes

  • Genetic factors: inheritance of faulty genes and chromosomal abnormalities e.g. down syndrome, Alzheimer's disease.
  • Physical illness: leading to loss of mental capacity e.g. acute malaria
  • Emotional factors: anxiety and neurotic personality traits cause people to develop psychosomatic disorder
  • Infection, Disease and Toxins
  • Brain defects or injury: Defects in or injury to certain areas of the brain have also been linked to some mental illnesses
  • Others: prenatal damage (autism), nutrition related, substance abuse etc

2. Psychological Factors

  • Severe psychological trauma suffered as a child, such as emotional, physical, or sexual abuse
  • An important early loss, such as the loss of a parent
  • Neglect
  • Poor ability to relate to others

3. Social-Cultural Factors

  • Social influence- maladaptive behaviour
  • Low socio-economic class
  • Social role- a soldier kills then later develops guilty conscious leading to mental illness
  • Violence, Unemployment, Broken homes

üìã History Taking & Physical Exam

History taking differs from general health history because it focuses on psychological/behavioural components rather than physiological. Patients are allowed to tell their stories in their own words.

Comprehensive History Components

  • Identifying data
  • Chief complaint
  • History of present illness (Onset, Precipitating/perpetuating factors)
  • Hx of past illnesses (Psychiatric, Medical)
  • Alcohol and other substance history
  • Family history & Family tree
  • Personal history (anamnesis): Prenatal/perinatal, Childhood (Early/Middle/Late), Adulthood
  • Occupational, Marital, Military, Educational, Religious history
  • Social activity, Current living situation
  • Legal history/criminal record
  • Sexual history, Fantasies and dreams, Values

Physical Examination & Investigations

Should be comprehensive and carried out within a day of admission with special attention to the central nervous system.

Lab/Radiological Investigations:

  1. Biochemical examinations
  2. Chemical profile (Liver, Renal, Thyroid functions)
  3. Vitamin levels (e.g., B12)
  4. Psychoendocrinological examination (TRH stimulation test, Dexamethasone suppression test)
  5. Electroencephalographic examination
  6. Computer tomography (CT) of the brain

ü©∫ Mental Status Examination (MSE)

The MSE is a description of all the areas of the client‚Äôs mental functioning. It is essential for developing an appropriate plan of care and is done during admission and periodically to assess response to therapy.

Components of MSE

‚ñ∂ 1. Appearance
  • Grooming and dress: Unusual modes of dress, soiled clothing, use of makeup, neat or unkempt.
  • Hygiene: Body/breath odor, condition of skin, fingernails.
  • Posture: Upright, rigid, slumped over.
  • Height and weight: Accurate measurements.
  • Eye contact: Intermittent, fleeting, sustained, intense, or none.
  • Hair: Color, texture, cleanliness.
  • Distinguishing marks: Scars, tattoos, bruises, rashes.
  • Age comparison: Does appearance match chronological age?
‚ñ∂ 2. Motor Activity & Behavior
  • Tremors: Hands/legs trembling (continuous/specific times).
  • Tics/Stereotypical movements: Facial tics, jerking, spastic movements.
  • Mannerisms/Gestures: Facial/body movements, nail biting, grimacing.
  • Hyperactivity: Pacing, unable to sit still.
  • Restlessness/Agitation: Fidgeting, clenching hands.
  • Aggressiveness: Hostile, threatening, sarcastic.
  • Rigidity: Stiff posture.
  • Gait: Limping, ataxia, shuffling.
  • Echopraxia: Mimicking others' actions.
  • Psychomotor retardation: Slow movements/speech.
‚ñ∂ 3. Speech Patterns
  • Rate: Slowness or rapidity.
  • Pressure of speech: Frenzied, unable to be interrupted.
  • Intonation: Appropriate emphasis vs. monotone.
  • Volume: Loud, soft, pitch.
  • Impairments: Stuttering, hoarseness, slurring.
  • Aphasia: Difficulty forming words, incorrect words, neologisms.
‚ñ∂ 4. Mood & Affect

Mood: Patient's self-report of prevailing emotional state.

  • Depressed, despairing, irritable, anxious, elated, euphoric, fearful, guilty, labile.

Affect: Outward emotional expression.

  • Congruence (consistent with mood), Constricted/blunted (minimal), Flat (absent), Appropriate vs. Inappropriate.
‚ñ∂ 5. Thought Processes

Form of Thought (How ideas are put together)

  • Flight of ideas, Associative looseness, Circumstantiality, Tangentiality, Neologisms, Concrete thinking, Clang associations, Word salad, Perseveration, Echolalia, Mutism, Poverty of speech.

Content of Thought (What person is thinking)

  • Delusions: Persecutory, Grandiose, Reference, Control/influence, Somatic, Nihilistic.
  • Suicidal/homicidal ideas, Obsessions, Paranoia, Magical thinking, Religiosity, Phobias.
‚ñ∂ 6. Perceptual Disturbances
  • Hallucinations: Unrealistic sensory perceptions (Auditory, Visual, Tactile, Olfactory, Gustatory).
  • Illusions: Misinterpretation of real stimuli.
  • Depersonalization: Altered perception of self (feeling outside body).
  • Derealization: Altered perception of environment (surroundings feel strange/unreal).
‚ñ∂ 7. Sensorium, Cognition, Insight & Judgment
  • Alertness: Consciousness level (clear, confusion, stupor, coma).
  • Orientation: Time, Place, Person, Circumstances.
  • Memory: Immediate, Recent, Remote.
  • Concentration/Attention: Serial 3s or 7s, spelling backwards.
  • Abstract thought: Proverb interpretation.
  • Impulse Control: Aggression, hostility, fear, guilt, sexual feelings.
  • Judgment: Problem solving ability.
  • Insight: Awareness of illness (6 levels from denial to true emotional insight).
