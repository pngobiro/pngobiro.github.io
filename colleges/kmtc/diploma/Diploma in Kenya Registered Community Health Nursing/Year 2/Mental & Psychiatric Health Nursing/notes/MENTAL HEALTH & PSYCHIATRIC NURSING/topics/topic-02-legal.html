üéØ Objectives & Definitions

Learning Objectives

  • Differentiate among ethics, morals, values, rights, malpractice, and negligence.
  • Explain the ethical theoretical perspectives and principles in mental health.
  • Describe ethical and legal issues relevant to psychiatric/mental health nursing.
  • Describe the Mental Health Act: admission and discharge criteria.

Key Definitions

Ethics

The science that deals with the rightness and wrongness of actions.

Moral Behavior

Conduct that results from serious critical thinking about how individuals ought to treat others; such as the respect for autonomy, freedom, justice, honesty, and confidentiality.

Values

Ideals or concepts that give meaning to the individual's life.

Right

A valid, legally recognized claim or entitlement, encompassing both freedom from government interference or discriminatory treatment and an entitlement to a benefit or service.

üß≠ Ethical Considerations

Theoretical Perspectives

An ethical theory is a moral principle or a set of moral principles that can be used in assessing what is morally right or morally wrong, providing guidelines for ethical decision making.

‚ñ∂ Ethical Theories
  • Kantianism (Deontology): Decisions and actions are bound by a sense of duty. It is not the consequences that make an action right or wrong, but the principle or motivation ("I make this choice because it is morally right and my duty").
  • Utilitarianism: "The greatest-happiness principle." Promotes actions based on end results that produce the most good (happiness) for the most people. "The end justifies the means."
  • Christian Ethics: Do unto others as you would have them do unto you. Treats others as moral equals.
  • Natural Law Theories: Do good and avoid evil; evil acts are never condoned, even if intended to advance noble ends.
  • Ethical Egoism: Decisions are based on what is best for the individual making the decision (own advantage).

Ethical Principles

Fundamental guidelines that influence decision-making in healthcare.

Principle Description & Application
Autonomy Emphasizes the status of persons as autonomous moral agents whose rights to determine their destinies should always be respected. Presumes capacity to make independent choices (NOTE: Children, comatose, and seriously mentally ill may be incapable).
Beneficence One's duty to benefit or promote the good of others. Health care workers acting in clients' best interests; nurses serving as advocates.
Non-maleficence Abstaining from negative acts toward another; acting carefully to avoid harm ("do no harm"). Example: Administering chemotherapy knowing side effects occur but goal is prolonging life.
Justice "Justice as fairness." Duty to treat all individuals equally and fairly. Resources should be distributed evenly without respect to socioeconomic status.
Veracity One's duty to be truthful always. Not intentionally deceiving or misleading clients. Limitations exist when truth would knowingly produce harm or interfere with recovery.

üìú Legal Considerations (Mental Health Act)

The Mental Health Act (Cap 248) provides legal aspects concerning mental health facilities, admission/discharge processes, patient rights, and supervision. Note: Due for revision following devolution.

The Kenya Board of Mental Health

‚ñ∂ Composition of the Board
  • Chairman (Director/Deputy Director of Medical Services)
  • 1 Medical practitioner (specialized in mental health)
  • 1 Clinical officer (trained in mental health)
  • 1 Nurse (trained in mental health)
  • Commissioner for Social Services (or nominee)
  • Director of Education (or nominee)
  • Representative from each province
  • Deputy Director of Mental Health
  • Chief Nursing Officer
‚ñ∂ Functions of the Board
  • Coordinate mental health activities.
  • Advise government on mental health state and facilities.
  • Inspect mental health hospitals for standards.
  • Approve establishment of mental health hospitals.
  • Assist in administration when necessary.
  • Investigate matters referred by patients/relatives.
  • Advise on care of persons with mental sub-normality.
  • Initiate community/family-based programmes.

Admission Criteria & Procedures

‚ñ∂ Voluntary Admission

Over 16 Years: Person voluntarily submits to treatment. Written application to 'person in charge'.

  • Form: MOH 613 (in duplicate).

Under 16 Years: Parent/guardian applies.

  • Form: MOH 637 (in duplicate).
‚ñ∂ Involuntary Admission

Patients incapable of expressing willingness/unwillingness.

  • Application: Husband, wife, or relative fills Form MOH 614 (duplicate). If non-relative, must state reasons why relative couldn't apply.
  • Medical Recommendation: Doctor fills Form MOH 615 indicating expected benefit.
  • Duration: Not more than 6 months initially. Can be prolonged by 6 months (max total 12 months).
  • Validity: Forms must reach hospital within 14 days of signing.
‚ñ∂ Emergency/Forensic Admission

Person found dangerous to self or others.

  • Authority: Police officer, Chief, or Assistant Chief can arrest and take to mental hospital within 24 hours.
  • Form: MOH 638.
  • Review: After 72 hours. Discharged if sound mind; admitted as involuntary if unsound.
‚ñ∂ Armed Forces & Foreigners

Armed Forces

  • Admitted for observation if certified by armed forces medical officer (examined within 48 hrs).
  • Initial period: 28 days. Extension requires recommendation by 2 practitioners (1 psychiatrist).
  • Ceasing to be a member: Becomes involuntary patient.

Foreigners

  • Requires written approval from the Mental Health Board.
  • Application must show person was legally detained in foreign country (< 2 months).
  • Must be accompanied by warrant/documents.

Discharge & Offences

Discharge Process

'Person in charge' may order discharge upon recommendation of the medical practitioner in charge of treatment. (Note: Does not apply to those detained under Criminal Procedure Cap 75).

Delivery to Relative/Friend: Relative/friend may apply to take custody. Person in charge may order delivery upon conditions, consulting with the medical practitioner and the Board.

üö´

Offences under the Mental Health Act

  • Person other than medical practitioner signing certificates.
  • Medical practitioner knowingly certifying untrue information.
  • Assisting escape or harbouring an escaped patient.
  • Unlawfully permitting a patient to leave (by person in charge/staff).
  • Ill-treatment: Striking, abusing, or willfully neglecting a patient.

General Penalty: Fine not exceeding Ksh 10,000 or imprisonment not exceeding 12 months, or both.
