Overview

Characterized by a withdrawal of the child into self and a fantasy world. Onset occurs before age 3. Marked by abnormal development in social interaction and communication, with restricted activities and interests.

Diagnostic Criteria (DSM-V)

  • A
    Persistent deficits in social communication/interaction:
    ‚Ä¢ Deficits in social-emotional reciprocity
    ‚Ä¢ Deficits in nonverbal communication (eye contact, body language)
    ‚Ä¢ Deficits in developing/maintaining relationships
  • B
    Restricted, repetitive patterns (at least 2):
    ‚Ä¢ Stereotyped motor movements (lining up toys, echolalia)
    ‚Ä¢ Inflexible adherence to routines/sameness
    ‚Ä¢ Highly restricted, fixated interests
    ‚Ä¢ Hyper- or hypo-reactivity to sensory input

Assessment Findings

‚ñ∂ Key Impairments
  • Social Interaction: No interest in people, aversion to affection, failure to develop cooperative play.
  • Communication: Language may be absent, immature structure, idiosyncratic utterances. Nonverbal communication (gestures) often absent.
  • Activities: Resistance to change, attachment to moving objects (fans), stereotyped movements (rocking, hand-clapping), self-injurious behavior (head banging).

‚ö° ADHD

Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder

Persistent pattern of inattention and/or hyperactivity-impulsivity more frequent and severe than typical for development level. Highly distractible, unable to contain stimuli.

Symptoms

  • Short attention span
  • Constant fidgeting
  • Inability to sit still
  • Failure to stay quiet
  • Poor academic performance
  • Impulsivity

Etiology

  • Genetics: Strong evidence.
  • Biochemical: Dopamine, norepinephrine involvement.
  • Perinatal: Prematurity, fetal distress, maternal smoking/alcohol.
  • Environmental: Lead exposure, chaotic family environments.

Management

Pharmacological Intervention

Class Drugs Effects/Considerations
CNS Stimulants Methylphenidate (Ritalin), Dextroamphetamine Increase attention span, control hyperactivity. Side effects: Insomnia, anorexia, weight loss, tachycardia, growth suppression.
Non-Stimulants Atomoxetine Selective norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor. Risk of liver damage.
Antidepressants Bupropion, Imipramine Useful for attention/restlessness. Monitor cardiac side effects.

Nursing Implications

  • Administer stimulants after meals to reduce anorexia.
  • Last dose at least 6 hours before bedtime to prevent insomnia.
  • Monitor weight regularly (weekly).
  • "Drug Holidays" may be attempted to assess effectiveness/growth.
  • Do not withdraw abruptly.

Classification

Substance-Induced Disorders: Reversible syndromes due to recent ingestion (Intoxication) or cessation (Withdrawal).

Substance-Use Disorders: Pathological pattern of behaviors related to use. Diagnosed by 2+ criteria in 12 months:

‚ñ∂ 11 Diagnostic Criteria
  1. Taking larger amounts than intended.
  2. Persistent desire/unsuccessful efforts to cut down.
  3. Time spent obtaining/using/recovering.
  4. Craving.
  5. Failure to fulfill major role obligations.
  6. Continued use despite social problems.
  7. Activities given up/reduced.
  8. Use in hazardous situations.
  9. Continued use despite physical/psychological problems.
  10. Tolerance: Need for increased amounts.
  11. Withdrawal: Characteristic syndrome upon cessation.

üç∑ Specific Substances

Alcohol-Related Disorders

Clinical Features

  • Intoxication: Slurred speech, incoordination, unsteady gait, nystagmus, memory impairment.
  • Withdrawal: Tremors, insomnia, nausea, autonomic hyperactivity (pulse >100), anxiety, seizures.
  • Delirium Tremens (DTs): Severe withdrawal (3-4 days post-cessation). Confusion, visual/tactile hallucinations, fever, autonomic overactivity. Medical emergency.

CAGE Screening Tool

  • Have you ever felt you should Cut down?
  • Have people Annoyed you by criticizing your drinking?
  • Have you ever felt Guilty about drinking?
  • Have you ever had an Eye-opener (drink in morning)?

2+ "Yes" answers indicates positive screen.

Management of Intoxication/Withdrawal

  • IV fluids and B-Complex (Thiamine) to prevent Wernicke's encephalopathy.
  • Dextrose for hypoglycemia.
  • Benzodiazepines (Diazepam/Lorazepam) for withdrawal symptoms/seizures.
  • Disulfiram (Antabuse) for maintenance (aversion therapy).

Other Substance Classes

Substance Intoxication Features Withdrawal Features
Opioids (Heroin, Morphine) Pupillary constriction, drowsiness, slurred speech, respiratory depression (overdose). Dysphoria, nausea/vomiting, muscle aches, pupillary dilation, piloerection (gooseflesh), yawning, diarrhea.
Stimulants (Cocaine, Amphetamines) Tachycardia, pupillary dilation, elevated BP, agitation, euphoria, weight loss. Fatigue, vivid unpleasant dreams, increased appetite, psychomotor retardation ("Crash").
Cannabis Conjunctival injection (red eyes), increased appetite, dry mouth, tachycardia, impaired coordination. Irritability, anxiety, sleep difficulty, decreased appetite, restlessness.
Caffeine Restlessness, nervousness, insomnia, flushed face, diuresis, muscle twitching (>250mg). Headache (hallmark), fatigue, drowsiness, difficulty concentrating.

Gambling Disorder

Included in addictive disorders. Persistent problematic gambling behavior leading to distress. Criteria include needing to gamble with increasing amounts of money (tolerance), restlessness when stopping (withdrawal), and "chasing losses" (returning to get even).
