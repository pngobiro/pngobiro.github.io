üîç Introduction & Classification

Overview

Symptoms are vital in the diagnosis of psychiatric disorders. However, a diagnosis shouldn't be made on the basis of an individual symptom. Symptoms should be intense, persistent, and form a recognizable pattern (syndrome).

Systems of Psychological Functioning

Psychiatric signs and symptoms fall into groups referred to as systems of psychological functioning:

  • 1. Perception
  • 2. Thinking
  • 3. Speech
  • 4. Motor activity
  • 5. Memory
  • 6. Orientation
  • 7. Consciousness
  • 8. Cognitive (intellectual functioning)
  • 9. Mood / Affect

üëÅÔ∏è Disorders of Perception

Perception is the process of becoming aware of what is presented through the sense organs.

1. Illusion

Misperceptions of external stimuli (e.g., mistaking a stick for a snake). These occur when sensory stimulation is reduced (dusk), consciousness is reduced (delirium), or during strong affective states (anxiety/fear).

2. Hallucinations

A perception experienced in the absence of an external stimulus to the sense organs, experienced as a true perception coming from the outside world.

‚ñ∂ Types of Hallucinations
  • Auditory: Hearing voices/sounds not present. Voices may command violent acts. Common in depression and schizophrenia.
  • Visual: Seeing figures/images not present. Common in intense fear and organic psychosis.
  • Tactile (Haptic): Sensations of being touched/pricked. Includes formication ("cocaine bugs") and sexual hallucinations.
  • Olfactory: Perceiving peculiar non-existent smells. Common in temporal epilepsy and schizophrenia.
  • Gustatory: False perception of unpleasant tastes.
  • Reflex: Stimulus in one modality results in hallucination in another (e.g., music causes visual hallucination).
3. Depersonalization / Derealization
  • Depersonalization: Patient feels detached, views self as strange/unreal (change of self-awareness).
  • Derealization: Alteration of sense of reality of the environment. Familiar objects seem changed in size/shape.

üß† Disorders of Thinking

I. Disorders of Sequence/Process (Stream of Thought)

‚ñ∂ View Process Disorders
  • Flight of Ideas: Rapid shifting from one topic to another, fragmenting ideas. Common in Mania.
  • Retardation/Poverty of Thought: Stream of thought is slowed. Common in depression.
  • Perseveration: Thoughts persist beyond relevance; unable to break away from a theme.
  • Circumstantiality: Excessive unnecessary details, delayed reaching the goal.
  • Pressure of Thought: Compelled to think rapidly; thoughts abundant.
  • Thought Blocking: Sudden halt in train of thought.
  • Tangentiality: Like circumstantiality but never reaches the goal.
  • Fragmented Thinking/Loosening: Illogical shift of ideas. Common in schizophrenia.

II. Disorders of Content (Delusions)

Definition: Delusion

Fixed, false beliefs incompatible with one‚Äôs socio-cultural background, not explained by reality, and firmly held on inadequate grounds.

Type Description
Persecutory Belief that others are plotting against them or they are being persecuted.
Grandiose Belief of being an important person (e.g., Jesus, President) or having unusual abilities.
Nihilistic Belief that one is dead, parts of body are missing, or world has stopped.
Religious Obsession with beliefs of being holier than others (fanatics).
Jealous (Infidelity) Belief that partner is unfaithful (Othello syndrome).
Erotomanic (Amorous) Belief that one is loved by an inaccessible person (e.g., celebrity).
Reference Belief that unrelated events/remarks have specific meaning for the patient (e.g., TV speaking to them).
Control/Influence Belief that thoughts/behaviors are controlled by external forces.
Somatic/Hypochondriacal False belief about body functioning or having a dreadful disease.

III. Disorders of Possession of Thought

‚ñ∂ Obsessions, Compulsions & Alienation
  • Obsessions: Persistent, recurrent thoughts/ideas that cannot be eliminated by logic. Defining characteristic is the subjective resistance against them.
  • Compulsions: Repeated, stereotyped actions the person feels compelled to carry out to relieve obsession distress (e.g., hand washing, checking).
  • Phobias: Persistent, excessive, irrational fears leading to avoidance. Can be seen as obsessional fears.
  • Thought Alienation:
    • Insertion: Thoughts put into mind.
    • Withdrawal: Thoughts taken away.
    • Broadcasting: Thoughts transmitted to others.

üó£Ô∏è Speech, Motor, Mood & Memory

D. Disorders of Speech

  • Echolalia: Repetition of others' words.
  • Neologism: Inventing new words or meanings.
  • Mutism: Absence of speech.
  • Clang Association: Speech directed by sound (rhyming) rather than meaning.
  • Word Salad: Words put together randomly without logical connection.

E. Disorders of Motor Activity

‚ñ∂ View Motor Disorders
  • Stereotypes: Regular, purposeless repeated movements (e.g., rocking).
  • Tics: Irregular involuntary movements of muscle groups.
  • Mannerisms: Repeated movements with functional significance (e.g., saluting).
  • Waxy Flexibility (Catatonia): Plastic resistance; maintaining odd postures placed by others.
  • Echopraxia: Imitating movements of others.
  • Negativism: Doing the opposite of what is asked.
  • Stupor: Motionless, unaware of surroundings but conscious.

F. Disorders of Mood/Affect

  • Anxiety: Apprehension out of proportion to situation.
  • Euphoria: Mild unwarranted cheerfulness.
  • Elation: Excessive cheerfulness/ecstasy, often associated with mania.
  • Depression: Pervasive lowering of mood, sadness, anhedonia.

G. Disorders of Memory

  • Amnesia: Loss of memory (Anterograde: events after incident; Retrograde: events before).
  • Confabulation: Unconscious invention of experiences to fill memory gaps (common in Korsakoff's).
  • D√©j√† vu: Recognition of new events as familiar.
  • Jamais vu: Failure to recognize familiar events.

üí° Consciousness & Insight

H. Disorders of Consciousness

  • Confusion: Muddled thinking, impaired comprehension.
  • Clouding: State of drowsiness, incomplete reaction to stimuli.
  • Delirium: Acute clouding of consciousness (organic).

Insight

Defined as correct awareness of one's mental condition. Assessed by four criteria:

  1. Awareness of presenting abnormal phenomena.
  2. Recognition that these phenomena are abnormal.
  3. Acceptance that these are caused by mental illness.
  4. Awareness that treatment is required.
