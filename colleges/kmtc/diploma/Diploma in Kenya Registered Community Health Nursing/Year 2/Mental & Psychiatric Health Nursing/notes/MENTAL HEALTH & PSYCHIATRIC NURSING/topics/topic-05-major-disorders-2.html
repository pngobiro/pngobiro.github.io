Overview

Organic disorders are cognitive disorders associated with brain dysfunction. The two primary categories are:

  • Dementia: Chronic, often progressive.
  • Delirium: Acute, often reversible.
02

üë¥ Dementia

Definition

A chronic mental disorder characterized by gradual loss of memory and intellectual abilities, impaired reasoning, and deterioration of personality. The course may be progressive, stationary, or reversible.

Clinical Features

‚ñ∂ Early Signs & Symptoms
  • Memory impairment for recent events; poor retention of new info.
  • Reduction in range of interests.
  • Change in personality (irritable or aggressive).
  • Labile mood.
  • Restlessness and distractibility.
  • Poverty of thoughts and persecutory beliefs.
‚ñ∂ Late Signs & Symptoms
  • Further memory loss including distant events.
  • Disorientation (Time > Place > Person) leading to wandering.
  • Self-neglect and deterioration in self-care.
  • Restlessness (especially afternoon/evening - "Sundowning").
  • Incoherence and mutism.
  • Incontinence of urine and faeces.

Classification by Reversibility

Untreatable / Irreversible Treatable / Reversible
Huntington's Chorea Vascular / Multi-infarct dementia
Parkinson's Disease Intracranial lesions (tumors, hematomas)
Alzheimer's Disease Metabolic disorders (hepatic/renal failure)
Pick's Disease Endocrine disorders (Addison's, Hypothyroidism)
Infections (AIDS, Meningitis, Syphilis)
Deficiencies (B12, Folate, Thiamine)

Stages of Development

  • 1
    Stage One (2-4 yrs): Forgetfulness, declining interest in environment, poor work performance.
  • 2
    Stage Two (2-12 yrs): Progressive memory loss, difficulty following instructions, hesitancy, irritability, wandering, social isolation.
  • 3
    Stage Three (Final): Marked weight loss, inability to communicate, failure to recognize family, incontinence, loss of mobility.

üß† Alzheimer's Disease

üîç Click to zoom

Fig. 3.1: Auguste D, the first patient described by Dr. Alois Alzheimer in 1906.

Definition: A chronic, irreversible disease affecting brain cells, causing impairment of intellectual functioning. It destroys ability to reason, remember, imagine, and learn.

Ten Warning Signs

  1. Memory loss
  2. Difficulty performing familiar tasks
  3. Problems with language
  4. Disorientation to time and place
  5. Poor or decreased judgment
  6. Problems with abstract thinking
  7. Misplacing things
  8. Changes in mood or behavior
  9. Changes in personality
  10. Loss of initiative

Nursing Management for Dementia/Alzheimer's

  • Safety: Prevent accidents (secure floors, firm shoes, ID tags for wandering).
  • Routine: Maintain daily routines and fixed timetables (waking, toilet, meals).
  • Reality Orientation: Use calendars, clocks, simple face-to-face communication.
  • Environment: Stable, calm, reduced stimuli to decrease confusion.
  • Nutrition: Well-balanced diet, fluids (limit in evening to prevent incontinence/nocturia).
  • "Sundowning": Provide additional care during evening when condition often deteriorates.

üòµ Delirium (Acute Organic Syndrome)

Definition & Features

Acute organic mental disorder characterized by:

  • Impairment of consciousness (clouding).
  • Disorientation (mostly time).
  • Disturbance in perception (illusions/hallucinations) and restlessness.

Incidence: Highest among organic mental disorders (10-25% of medical-surgical patients).

Common Causes

‚óè Metabolic (Diabetes, Uremia, Renal failure)
‚óè Infections (Meningitis, Encephalitis, Malaria)
‚óè Trauma (Subdural/Epidural hematomas)
‚óè Withdrawal (Sedatives, Alcohol - DTs)
‚óè Deficiencies (Thiamine)
‚óè Toxins/Drugs

Management

  • Primary: Identify and treat underlying cause immediately (e.g., correct hypoglycemia).
  • Safety: Protect patient responding to hallucinations. Caregiver at bedside.
  • Environment: Well-lit room (reduces shadows/illusions), calm atmosphere.
  • Orientation: Repeatedly explain location, time, introduce people.
  • Symptomatic: Benzodiazepines (Lorazepam) or Antipsychotics (Haloperidol) for agitation.

üò∞ Anxiety Disorders

Anxiety vs. Fear

Fear Anxiety
Acute, immediate response to imminent danger. Sustained feeling of uneasiness about undefined threat.
Proximal threat (obvious source). Source often not obvious or seems minor.
Objective physiological changes (fight or flight). Threats to self-esteem, wellbeing, or psychological harm.

Levels of Anxiety

  • Mild: Adaptive day-to-day tension.
  • Moderate: Difficulty focusing, mild somatic complaints.
  • Severe: Unable to focus on problem solving, increased physical discomfort.
  • Panic: Awe, dread, terror, distortion of perception, disorganized personality.

Specific Disorders

‚ñ∂ Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD)

Excessive anxiety and worry occurring on most days for at least 6 months. Difficult to control.

Symptoms (3+ required): Restlessness/keyed up, fatigue, difficulty concentrating, irritability, muscle tension, sleep disturbance.

‚ñ∂ Panic Disorder

Recurrent unexpected panic attacks. Abrupt surge of intense fear peaking within minutes.

Symptoms (4+ required): Palpitations, sweating, trembling, shortness of breath, chest pain, nausea, dizziness, derealization, fear of losing control/dying, paresthesia.

‚ñ∂ Separation Anxiety Disorder

Excessive fear concerning separation from home or attachment figures. Must last 4 weeks in children, 6 months in adults.

‚ñ∂ Specific Phobias

Active avoidance of specific objects/situations (e.g., Acrophobia/heights, Claustrophobia/closed spaces, Nyctophobia/darkness). Fear is out of proportion to actual danger.

Management of Anxiety

Psychotherapy

  • CBT: Challenge negative thinking patterns.
  • Relaxation Techniques: Progressive muscle relaxation, Benson's meditation.
  • Systematic Desensitization: Habituation and flooding (for phobias).

Pharmacology

  • Benzodiazepines: Alprazolam (Panic), Diazepam. Use sparingly due to dependence.
  • Antidepressants: SSRIs (Fluoxetine), TCAs (Imipramine, Clomipramine) - often first line for chronic anxiety.
  • Beta-blockers: Propranolol for physical symptoms (palpitations, tremors).
  • Buspirone: For GAD (non-sedating).
