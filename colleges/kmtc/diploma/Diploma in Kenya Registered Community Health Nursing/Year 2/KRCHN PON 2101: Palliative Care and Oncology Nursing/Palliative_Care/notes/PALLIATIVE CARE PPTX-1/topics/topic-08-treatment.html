02

üî™ Palliative Surgery

Surgery can be an effective treatment modality for potential cure in specific cancers with early diagnosis.

Arguments Against Surgery

  • Survival is driven by the rate and volume of metastatic burden
  • Unhealthy patients that can't tolerate surgery
  • Risk/benefit ratio is heavily weighted towards risks
  • Theoretical risk of "angering the tumor" or "letting the air in"
  • Perception of advanced/incurable disease as a curse

Arguments For Surgery

  • Improving quality of life and symptom control
  • Debulking gives chemotherapy a fighting chance
  • Removing the primary tumor or metastases
  • Removing inhibitory peptides/molecules that depress local immune response, enhancing anti-tumor immunity

Roles of Surgery in PC

‚ñ∂ 1. Initial Evaluation

Ranges from minor procedures such as biopsy of suspicious lumps and/or lymph nodes to more invasive procedures like cystoscopy or endoscopy. Helps in making a diagnosis.

‚ñ∂ 2. Local Disease Control
  • Debulking: Aimed at decreasing tumor size (e.g., ovarian and nephroblastoma).
  • Fungating lesions: Control to achieve symptom control especially in breast cancer or peripheral lesions (Kaposi's sarcoma, head and neck).
‚ñ∂ 3. Control of Hemorrhage

Common among vesico-vaginal or recto-vaginal fistula as complication of cancer of the cervix (direct result or due to radiotherapy).

‚ñ∂ 4. Pain Control
  • Relief of obstruction from dysphagia (head, neck, esophageal cancer).
  • Insertion of gastrostomy and stents.
  • Relief of intestinal obstruction following ovarian, rectal, and colon cancer.
‚ñ∂ 5. Reconstruction & Rehabilitation

Useful in improving quality of life for patients with breast, head, and neck cancer.

03

üß™ Palliative Chemotherapy

Refers to use of drugs to stop or kill the growth of cancer cells. Given orally, intravenously, or into the peritoneal/pleural cavity.

The palliative/toxicity trade-off clearly favors symptom relief.

Treatment Cycles

A cycle consists of the days drugs are administered followed by a rest and recovery period. Usually lasts 1 to 4 weeks and is then repeated.

Potential Side Effects

  • Dizziness
  • Nausea & Vomiting
  • Fatigue / Malaise
  • Myelosuppression
  • Tumor lysis syndrome
  • Allergic reactions
  • Anemia

Target Cancers

  • Breast cancers
  • Multiple myeloma
  • Lymphomas
  • Ca prostate
  • Small cell lung cancer
  • Bladder / Ovarian / Testicular cancer

Nurse's Role: Monitoring

  • Assess effectiveness regularly (symptom relief)
  • Objective measurement (Clinical, Radiological, Serological markers)
  • Monitor side effects
  • Assess psychosocial burden

‚ò¢Ô∏è Radiotherapy

Radiation Oncology: Medical specialty involving controlled use of radiation to treat cancer (cure or symptom reduction).

Procedure: Quick and painless sessions (~15 mins), typically 5 times per week for 3-9 weeks. Types include External Beam and Internal (Brachytherapy).

Indications for Palliative Radiotherapy

  • ‚û§
    Pain Relief: Metastatic bone pain, painful lymphadenopathy, soft tissue infiltration, neuropathic pain.
  • ‚û§
    Neurological Deficit: Brain metastasis, spinal cord compression.
  • ‚û§
    Relief of Pressure Symptoms: SVC obstruction, dysphagia, increased intracranial pressure (ICP), hydronephrosis, urinary/intestinal obstruction.
  • ‚û§
    Hemostasis: Bleeding of anorectal, gynecological, or skin cancer.
  • ‚û§
    Prophylaxis: Preventing cord compression, pathological fractures.

Side Effects

Fatigue, nausea, vomiting, somnolence, tissue fibrosis, alopecia, sterility, diarrhea.

05

üíä Hormone Therapy

Drugs that block the effects or lower the levels of hormones used to treat some types of cancer.

  • Estrogen/Progesterone: Affect growth of some breast cancers.
  • Testosterone: Affects growth of some prostate cancers.

Common Agents:

  • Tamoxifen: Common adjuvant treatment, usually given for 5 years after surgery.
  • Aromatase Inhibitors: Block estrogen production by adrenal glands in post-menopausal women.
06

üçé Nutritional Support

Goal: Prevent/correct malnutrition, help tolerate treatment, reduce complications, and improve quality of life (rather than accelerate therapy).

Meaning of Food

Positive Meaning Negative Meaning (in advanced disease)
Hope Guilt
Comfort Fear
Pleasure Pain

Barriers to Feeding

  • Difficulty chewing/swallowing
  • Nausea & Vomiting
  • Anorexia / Early satiety
  • Overwhelmed by portions
  • Xerostomia (dry mouth)
  • Taste/smell changes

Interventions

  • Creativity & appealing presentation
  • Individual preference
  • Small, personalized portions
  • Flexible timetable (feed when they want)
  • Conducive environment
  • Family involvement (company)
  • Adapt consistency (give what patient can take)
  • Ice chips for taste/smell issues
