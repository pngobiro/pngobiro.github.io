Definition of Pain

An unpleasant sensory and emotional experience associated with actual or potential tissue damage. It is subjective and is whatever the patient describes as "hurts".

Pain is a common symptom among patients with life-threatening diseases:

  • Over 80% of patients with cancer have moderate to severe pain.
  • Over 50% of patients with PLWHIV (People Living with HIV) have moderate to severe pain.

üß© The Concept of Total Pain

Pain is not only physical but has emotional, spiritual, and psychological components. Effective management calls for a multidisciplinary team approach.

1. Physical

  • Can be due to disease
  • Other symptoms (e.g., nausea)
  • Physical decline and fatigue

2. Social

  • Relationship with family/carers
  • Role in family
  • Financial problems
  • Work life

3. Psychological

  • Anger, depression
  • Adjustment to conduct
  • Grief and anxiety

4. Spiritual

  • Religious faith
  • Existential issues
  • Meaning of life and illness
  • Personal value as a human being

üîç Types of Pain

1. Nociceptive Pain

Involves direct stimulation of intact mechanical, chemical, and thermal nociceptors, transmitted along functional nerves. Divided into:

  • A Somatic Pain: Skin, systematic tissue, muscles, and bone. Due to stimulation of the somatic nervous system. Described as aching or throbbing, easily localized.
  • B Visceral Pain: Cardiac, lung, GIT tract. Involves stimulation of the autonomic nervous system. Difficult to describe or localize.

Treatment: Nociceptive pain responds to opioids and/or co-analgesics.

2. Neuropathic Pain

Results from disordered function of the peripheral or central nervous system due to potential nerve damage.

  • Described as burning, tingling, numbness, shooting, stabbing, or electric.
  • May respond to opioid analgesics, but adjuvant analgesics (tricyclic antidepressants, anticonvulsants) are often required in combination.

Acute vs. Chronic Pain

Acute Pain Chronic Pain
Related to an easily identified pathophysiology Not related to an easily identified condition

The Golden Rule

Pain should be treated: By the mouth, by the clock, by the ladder, and by the patient.

WHO 3-Step Ladder

The WHO step ladder acts as a guide based on the severity of pain.

Ladder Levels

Step 3: Severe Pain

Morphine, Fentanyl, Oxycodone
+ Non-opioid
+ Adjuvants

Step 2: Moderate Pain

Codeine, Hydrocodone, Tramadol
+ Acetaminophen/ASA
+ Adjuvants

Step 1: Mild Pain

Aspirin (ASA), Acetaminophen, NSAIDs
+ Adjuvants

Note: Adjuvant analgesics are drugs that have a primary indication other than pain but control pain through other mechanisms (e.g., antidepressants, anxiolytics, antispasmodics, corticosteroids).
