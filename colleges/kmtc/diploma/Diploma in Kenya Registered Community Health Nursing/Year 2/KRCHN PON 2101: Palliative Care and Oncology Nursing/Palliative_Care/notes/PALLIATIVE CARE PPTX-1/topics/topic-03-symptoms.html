üìã Distressing Symptoms in Palliative Care

Patients with life-threatening illnesses experience various distressing symptoms that affect their quality of life. Common symptoms in palliative care include:

1. Anorexia & Cachexia
2. Pain
3. Breathlessness
4. Confusion
5. Constipation
6. Dehydration
7. Diarrhea
8. Distress
9. Fatigue
10. Malnutrition
11. Nausea & Vomiting

üçΩÔ∏è Anorexia and Cachexia

Weakness, profound weight loss, and poor appetite are common problems in advanced cancer, HIV/AIDS, and end-stage organ failure.

‚ö†Ô∏è

Critical Distinction

Cachexia is not associated with hunger or thirst, nor will it improve by forced feeding and hydration.

Causes

  • Reversible causes: Lack of available or digestible foods, dysphagia, sore mouth, altered taste, nausea, vomiting, constipation, pain, and metabolic disturbances (hypocalcemia or uremia).
  • Secondary to treatment: Chemotherapy, radiotherapy, or drugs.

Management

  1. Support family and patient.
  2. Identify the cause and manage using pharmacological and non-pharmacological means.
  3. Serve small appetizing meals and emphasize fluid intake.

ü´Å Breathlessness (Dyspnea)

Breathlessness is a subjective experience of breathing discomfort. Difficulty in breathing is a frightening experience; patients describe it as suffocating, "could not get enough air," choking, or "felt like I was about to die."

‚ñ∂ Common Causes
  • Respiratory: Primary or secondary lung cancer, pulmonary embolus, airway tumor, infection.
  • Cardiac: Superior Vena Cava (SVC) obstruction, anemia, cardiac failure, pericardial effusion.
  • Others: Ascites, pneumonectomy.

Management

  1. 1 Take holistic history (severity, duration).
  2. 2 Treat reversible conditions (e.g., anemia, infection).
  3. 3 Address underlying anxiety.
  4. 4 Use medication to relieve symptoms:
    • Morphine 2-2.5mg 4hrly
    • Diazepine 2-5mg for anxiety and panic
    • Dexamethasone 8-12mg daily for causes like SVC obstruction, lymphangitis
  5. 5 Consider other medication e.g., bronchodilators, diuretics.
  6. 6 Other supportive measures (e.g., aeration, positioning, fluid management).

üòµ Confusion

One of the most distressing and difficult to manage symptoms.

üîç

Potential Causes

Uncontrolled pain, urinary retention, severe constipation, hypoxia, metabolic disturbance, systemic infections, etc.

Management

  • Use pharmacological and non-pharmacological interventions.
  • Keep surrounding calm and familiar, orientate patient on time and place.
  • Avoid restraining unless for patient safety.
  • Support the families to be able to stay with patient.
  • Medication: Give haloperidol (0.5-5mg) tds and benzodiazepine e.g., diazepam 5-10 mg.
05

üöΩ Constipation

Defined as the passage of small, hard fecal materials infrequently and with difficulty.

Causes Management
  • Direct effect of disease (internal obstruction from tumor)
  • External compression from abdominal muscles
  • Low fiber in the diet
  • Decreased food intake
  • Dehydration
  • General body weakness
  • Damage to lumbo-sacral spinal cord
  1. Encourage fluid and fruit intake, vegetables, and fiber
  2. Encourage mobility and proper positioning
  3. Give laxatives with opioids e.g., bisacodyl 5mg
  4. Assess obstruction
