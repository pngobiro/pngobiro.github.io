Bereavement & Grief

üìñ Definitions

Bereavement
The loss of something of value (not only of a person, could be loss of jobs) or the situation of anyone who has lost a person they are attached to.
Grief
A person's reaction to loss; includes a range of emotions on the psychosocial, social, spiritual, physical, and emotional reaction to loss.
Mourning
Refers to the behavior and actions of someone who is bereaved; it includes traditional customs and practices around death.
Loss
Signifies absence of an object, position, ability, or attribute. Can include loss of face, security, retirement, marriage, divorce, income, and death.
02

üé≠ Types of Grief

1. Anticipatory Grief

Grief before loss, associated with diagnosis, acute and chronic illness, and terminal illness experienced by the patients, family, and care giver.

2. Normal Grief

Exhibited by normal psychological response/feelings. Characterized by anger, numbness, disbelief, and depression. Physical symptoms (fatigue, poor sleep) disappear along with emotional ones.

  • Anger: The pain of separation; anxiety, burden of guilt.
  • Numbness: A protective mechanism lasting from hours to a week.
  • Disbelief: Lasts for weeks or months, presuming the deceased has only gone away.

3. Abnormal Grief

Type Description
Delayed Absence of grieving within the 1st weeks or months. Often precipitated years later by further loss. Likely to be more severe.
Inhibited Mechanism of avoidance. Can present as irritability, restlessness, and depression. Guided mourning helps encourage facing reality.
Chronic Severe and occurs when a person fails to progress through all tasks of mourning. No fixed time period.
Persistent Hypochondriasis Bereaved takes on symptoms of the deceased or develops anxiety/depression-related symptoms.

Risk Factors Complicating Grief

  • Sudden or trauma death
  • Suicide / Homicide
  • Death of a child
  • Multiple losses
  • Unresolved anger or previous unresolved losses

Phases of Grief

‚ñ∂ 1. Initial Shock

Numbness and disbelief before emotional reality is felt. Can present as uncontrolled emotional excitement or thinking being slow/chaotic.

‚ñ∂ 2. The Pain of Separation

Affects behavior and emotions. Overwhelming sickness, searching for the deceased, dreaming of them. Visual/auditory hallucinations may occur. Anger and guilt are common.

‚ñ∂ 3. Acceptance & Resolution

Loss is accepted; life is adjusted. Emotional energy is reinvested. Development of a balanced memory of the deceased (both good and bad aspects).

04

üìâ Stages of Death & Grief

Based on Elisabeth Kubler-Ross model.

  1. 1 (Denial): Initial reaction.
  2. 2 Anger: Displaced and projected onto environment, family, or staff.
    Nurse's Role: Tolerate, do not feel offended, do not neglect patient.
  3. 3 Bargaining: Patient tries to extend time or postpone death, often by making promises to God.
  4. 4 Depression: Patient grieves impending loss. Becomes withdrawn, may refuse medication.
    Nurse's Role: Stay with patient, listen, provide moral support, respect privacy.
  5. 5 Acceptance: Peaceful and calm. Patient is neither depressed nor angry. Interests diminish. May write wills or bless children.
    Nurse's Role: Tenderly touch, reassure, continue support.
05

ü§ù Management & Support

Ways of Helping the Bereaved

  • Being there
  • Listening in an accepting, non-judgmental way
  • Tolerating silences
  • Encouraging them to talk about the deceased
  • Being familiar with your own feelings about loss
  • Offering reassurance
  • Not taking anger personally
  • Accepting that you cannot "make them feel better"
