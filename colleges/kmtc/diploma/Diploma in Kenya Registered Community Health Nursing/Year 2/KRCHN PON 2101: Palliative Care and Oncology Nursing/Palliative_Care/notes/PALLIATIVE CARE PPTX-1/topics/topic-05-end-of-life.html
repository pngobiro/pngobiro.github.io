Key Definitions

  • End of Life: Refers to the final phase of a patient's illness when death is impending.
  • End of Life Care (EOC): Issues related to death and dying and services addressed to these issues. Focuses on physical and psychosocial needs for the patient and the family.

Principles of Management

  • Have good symptom control
  • Review all drugs and symptoms
  • Avoid unnecessary intervention
  • Maintain effective communication
  • Ensure care for the family
  • Assess and refer early to specialist services for patient and family support if needed

üè• Management Strategies

Environment

  • Patient to be in a quiet, private room, well-ventilated and well-lit.
  • Limit visitation (2 at a time).
  • Presence of a loved one (holding hands, touching, praying) brings comfort.
  • Involve the patient in discussion at the bedside even if they appear unconscious.

Psychological Care

  • Identify the family and keep them informed; be honest and listen.
  • Reassure them that symptom control will continue.
  • Encourage family to do small tasks for the loved one.
  • Touch, smile, and understand the patient's feelings.

Physical Needs

  • Secretions: Position patient on side for secretions to flow; suction if necessary.
  • Mouth Care: Keep mouth clean and moist; lubricate lips with paraffin.
  • Eyes/Nose: Wipe eyes with cotton wool and normal saline; keep nose clear.
  • Elimination: Use pampers/catheterize for incontinence; address pressure areas.
  • Nutrition: Meet needs via IV or NG tube if appropriate (caution: may cause abdominal disturbance). If swallowing is intact, give sips of water or ice chips.

‚è≥ The Last 48 Hours

Common Emergencies

Stridor, Seizure, Hemorrhage, Pain, Confusion

Drug Administration

Routes of administration typically change as oral intake becomes difficult:

  • Sublingual: Sedatives / Anxiolytics
  • Transdermal: Opioids (Fentanyl)
  • Subcutaneous: Opioids (Morphine), Antiemetics (Cyclizine, Metoclopramide), Anticholinergics (Hyoscine Hydrobromide), Sedatives (Haloperidol)
  • Rectal: Opioids, NSAIDs, Sedatives

Symptom-Specific Medication

Symptom Intervention / Medication
Dyspnea Fan the face, low dose morphine. For anxiety/panic: Reassurance, breathing exercises.
Death Rattle Avoid overhydration, reposition patient. Give Hyoscine Butylbromide 20mg 4hrly.
Nausea/Vomiting Antiemetics (Cyclizine, Metoclopramide).
Delirium Haloperidol. For agitated delirium: Haloperidol + Diazepam (5mg).
Pain Opioids.
Convulsions Diazepam 5-10mg (repeat as required up to 30mg).

üìâ The Dying Process

The time before death is generally peaceful. There is a gentle winding down that may take several days. The body starts to 'let go' of life.

‚ñ∂ Appetite and Thirst

Decrease in appetite and thirst is natural and not painful. Sips of water or a moist mouth swab will help.

‚ñ∂ Sleep and Alertness

Patient may be drowsy and difficult to wake up. Talk to the person when they seem most alert. Speak softly and naturally; do not shake them.

‚ñ∂ Temperature

Extremities may become cool, or patient may be hot/clammy. Skin may become blotchy (mottling) due to slowed circulation.

‚ñ∂ Secretions

Saliva/mucus may collect in the throat (gurgling). This is usually not distressing to the patient. Reposition head to the side.

‚ñ∂ Breathing

Patterns change. May be fast or have long gaps (Cheyne-Stokes). This is due to slowed circulation and waste buildup.

Signs of Death

  • Breathing stops
  • No heartbeat or pulse
  • Person cannot be woken up
  • Eyelids may be half open; mouth may be open
  • Pupils are fixed

After Death

  • Confirm and certify death
  • Break news to the relatives
  • Allow relatives to view and touch the body
