WHO Definition (2002)

It's an approach that improves the quality of life of patients and their families facing problems associated with life threatening illness, through prevention and relief of suffering by means of early identification and impeccable assessment and treatment of pain and other problems: physical, psychosocial and spiritual.

02

üéØ Principles of Palliative Care

Palliative care adheres to the following core principles:

  • Provides relief from pain and other distressing symptoms
  • Affirms life and regards dying as a normal process
  • Intends neither to hasten nor postpone death
  • Integrates the psychological and spiritual aspects of patient care
  • Offers a support system to help patients live as actively as possible until death
  • Offers a support system to help the family cope during the patient's illness and in their own bereavement
  • Uses a team approach to address the needs of patients and their families including bereavement counseling if indicated
  • Will enhance quality of life and may also positively influence the course of illness
03

üï∞Ô∏è History of Palliative Care

  • In the late 1950s, Dr. Cicely Saunders played a role in establishing the modern science and art of caring for patients with life-threatening illnesses and founded the first modern hospice at St Christopher's in southern London that started in 1967.
  • The first Palliative Care service in Africa was Island Hospice in Zimbabwe which operated in 1979, followed by South Africa, Kenya, and Uganda.
04

üß± Fundamentals of Palliative Care

The following concepts are applicable for effective delivery of palliative care:

1. Holistic Care

This approach regards the patient as a whole person, recognizing them as comprising of physiological, social, and physical aspects and is culturally appropriate.

2. Patient Centered Care

PC advocates that the patient should be the center of care, focused around patient wishes. This aspect respects the patient's decision on their care and treatment options.

3. Teamwork and Partnership

No single profession can adequately address all issues arising from a life-limiting diagnosis. Constant exposure to patients' distressing circumstances can be emotionally draining on staff and caregivers. This calls for an interdisciplinary approach for effective PC.

4. Family and Community

Care for patients with life-limiting conditions will take place in a range of community-based settings. These include patients' homes, palliative care units, or residential care facilities. Referral pathways and linkages between community-based PC, hospital-based, and hospices play a big role.

üè• Hospice & Models of Service

Hospice Care

Hospice is a specialized program that addresses the needs of the seriously ill and their loved ones.

A team approach is provided in hospice that may involve the physician, nurses, social workers, clergy, home health aids, volunteers, and family caregivers. Hospice workers help a dying patient manage pain, provide medical services, and offer family support through every stage of the person from the time of diagnosis to bereavement.

Components of Hospice Care Program:

  • Client and family as the family unit
  • Coordinate home care with access to available inpatient and nursing home beds
  • Control of symptoms (physical, sociological, psychological, and spiritual)
  • Physician directed services
  • Provision of an interdisciplinary care team of physicians, nurses, spiritual advisors, social workers, and counselors
  • Nursing and medical services available at all times
  • Bereavement follow-up after client's death
  • Acceptance into the program on the basis of health care needs rather than the ability to pay

Approaches in Palliative Care

Models of service delivery include:

‚ñ∂ Home Based Care

Most common service and model in Africa. It is cheap and most of the time acceptable to patients and family as well as respecting their cultural practices.

‚ñ∂ Inpatient Model

Patients admitted at the hospital; is available in some countries but is relatively expensive. The inpatient consultation team improves patients' difficult-to-control symptoms, enhances family satisfaction and wellbeing.

‚ñ∂ Outpatient Clinic and Day Care Center

Where patients visit a palliative care facility for services. They also generate a renewed sense of meaning and purpose for the patient.

‚ñ∂ Hospital Based PC Program

Is an affordable model. It takes advantages of sharing resources within the hospital. The hospital team then links the home-based care team to ensure continuity of care for the patient. This model averts hospital admission in overcrowded services in low resource settings, improving quality of care in their home safety.

‚ñ∂ Interdisciplinary Consultation Model

Provides special level palliative care to all patients with serious illness irrespective of prognosis and occasionally provides primary care.
