ü¶¥ Spinal Cord Compression

Occurs secondary to pressure from expanding tumors. Early detection is essential: investigate all complaints of back pain or neurological changes.

Clinical Manifestations

  • Early symptoms: back and leg pain, coldness, numbness, tingling, paresthesias.
  • Progression: leads to bowel and bladder dysfunction, weakness, and paralysis.

Management

  • Treatment: aimed at reducing tumor size by radiation and/or surgery to relieve compression and prevent irreversible paraplegia; may receive corticosteroids to reduce cord edema.
  • Nursing Interventions: early recognition of symptoms, neurological checks and medication administration.

ü´Ä Superior Vena Cava Syndrome (SVCS)

Compression or obstruction of the superior vena cava (SVC). Usually associated with cancer of the lungs and lymphomas. Signs and symptoms are the result of blockage of venous circulations of head, neck, and upper trunk.

Symptom Progression

  • Early: Periorbital edema and facial edema.
  • Progressive: Edema of neck, arms, and hands, difficulty swallowing, shortness of breath.
  • Late: Cyanosis, altered mental status, headache, and hypotension.

Note: Death may occur if compression is not relieved.

Management

  • Treatment: High-dose radiation to shrink tumor and relieve symptoms.
  • Nursing Interventions:
    • Monitoring vital signs
    • Providing oxygen support
    • Preparing tracheostomy if necessary
    • Initiating seizure precautions
    • Administering corticosteroids to reduce edema

ü©∏ Disseminated Intravascular Coagulopathy (DIC)

üö®

Critical Condition

Severe disorder of coagulation, often triggered by sepsis. Abnormal clot formation occurs in the microvasculature which depletes clotting factors and platelets, allowing extensive bleeding to occur. Tissue hypoxia occurs as a result of blockage of blood vessels from clots.

Mortality is greater than 70% despite aggressive treatment.

Signs and Symptoms

Related to decreased blood flow to major organs and depleted clotting factors:

  • Tachycardia
  • Oliguria
  • Dyspnea
  • Abnormal bleeding and hemorrhage

Management

  • Treatment:
    • Anticoagulants to decrease stimulations of coagulation
    • Transfusion of: Fresh frozen plasma (FFP), cryoprecipitate, platelets, packed RBC
  • Nursing Interventions: Assessing client, monitoring for bleeding, applying pressure dressings to venipuncture sites, and preventing risk of sepsis.

Pericardial effusion secondary to metastases or esophageal cancer can lead to compression of heart, restricting heart movement and resulting in cardiac tamponade.

Signs and Symptoms

Related to cardiogenic shock or circulatory collapse:

Anxiety
Cyanosis
Dyspnea
Hypotension
Tachycardia
Tachypnea
Impaired LOC
Increased CVP

Management

  • Treatment: Pericardiocentesis is performed to remove fluid from pericardial sac.
  • Nursing Interventions:
    1. 1
      Administering oxygen
    2. 2
      Maintaining intravenous line
    3. 3
      Monitoring vital signs
    4. 4
      Hemodynamic monitoring
    5. 5
      Administration of vasopressor agents
