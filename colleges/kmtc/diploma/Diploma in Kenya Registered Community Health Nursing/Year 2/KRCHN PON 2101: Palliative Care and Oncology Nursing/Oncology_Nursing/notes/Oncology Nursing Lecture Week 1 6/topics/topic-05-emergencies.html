Infection & Pain

  • Infection arises from neutropenia (low neutrophils).
  • People with advanced cancer often experience pain.

üß™ Metabolic Emergencies

Hypercalcemia

Due to bone resorption (demineralization). Serum calcium level > 11 mg/dL.

Associated Cancers

  • Solid Tumors: Breast, Lung, Head & Neck, Renal CAs
  • Hematologic: Multiple Myeloma, Leukemia
‚ö†Ô∏è

Severe Complications

Severe hypercalcemia may lead to renal failure, coma, cardiac arrest, and death.

Management: Calcitonin (Miacalcin) and oral glucocorticoids are given to lower serum calcium.

Tumor Lysis Syndrome (TLS)

Destruction of large numbers of malignant cells rapidly releases intracellular potassium, phosphorous, and nucleic acid into circulation.

Risk Factors: Highly responsive malignancies (lymphomas, leukemias, small cell carcinoma) with large tumor burden.

Manifestations

Weakness, Nausea, Diarrhea
Flaccid paralysis, Muscle cramps/twitching
ECG changes
Oliguria (>80 ml, <400ml output), Edema
Hypotension, Altered mental status

Collaborative Management

  1. 1
    Intravenous hydration
  2. 2
    Allopurinol to decrease uric acid concentration
  3. 3
    Sodium bicarbonate with IV hydration to promote fecal excretion of excess phosphate
  4. 4
    Lowering of serum potassium (medications, retention enemas, IV 50% dextrose)

Syndrome of Inappropriate Antidiuretic Hormone (SIADH)

Abnormal production of ADH leading to water retention and decrease in sodium (dilutional hyponatremia).

Causes

  • Small cell lung cancer
  • Antineoplastic agents: Cytoxan (Cyclophosphamide), Oncovin (Vincristine), Velban (Vinblastin), Platinol-AQ (Cisplatin)
  • Infection, pulmonary/CNS disorders, emotional stress
Signs & Symptoms Management
Confusion, Irritability
Headache, Lethargy
Muscle weakness
Decreased urine output, Edema
Nausea/Vomiting, Anorexia 		Fluid excretion (diuretics)
IV hypertonic saline (3-5%) if severe
Monitor Intake & Output
Meds: Declomycin, Lithium, Urea

ü©∏ Hematologic Emergencies

Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC)

Characterized by development of extensive, abnormal clots in the microcirculation. Widespread clotting depletes clotting factors and platelets, leading to excessive bleeding.

‚ö†Ô∏è

The Paradox of DIC

DIC involves both widespread clotting (causing organ ischemia) and widespread bleeding (due to factor depletion).

Causes
Gram-negative infection/sepsis, release of clotting factors from cancer cells, blood transfusion. Common in leukemia and adenocarcinomas (lung, pancreas, stomach, prostate).
Diagnostic Findings
  • Prolonged PT/PTT and clotting times
  • Very low platelet count
Management
  1. Correction of basic problem (e.g., treat infection)
  2. Administer blood products
  3. IV heparin if thrombosis manifests (controversial)
  4. Monitor for bleeding

üß± Structural Emergencies

Spinal Cord Compression

Caused by direct pressure or vascular compromise. Back pain is often the only presenting manifestation. Can lead to irreversible paralysis.

Rx: Radiation therapy, Laminectomy, Steroids (reduce inflammation).

Superior Vena Cava Syndrome (SVCS)

Obstruction of SVC reduces venous return. Common in lung cancer/lymphoma.

Signs: Dyspnea, facial swelling, JVD, arm swelling, chest pain.

Rx: Radiation therapy, Chemotherapy.

Cardiac Tamponade

Fluid collects in the pericardial sac (pericardial effusion), compressing the heart.
