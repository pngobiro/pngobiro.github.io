Treatment depends on the type of tumor, the extent of the disease, the client's co-morbidities, performance status, and wishes.

01

üî™ Surgical Interventions

Diagnostic Surgery

Done by cytologic specimen collection and biopsy.

Preventive Surgery

Involves removal of precancerous lesions or benign tumors (e.g., subtotal colectomies for familial polyposis/ulcerative colitis).

Curative Surgery

Removal of an entire tumor and surrounding lymph nodes. Cancers are localized to the organ of origin and regional nodes.

Reconstructive Surgery

Done for improvement of the appearance and function of the organ affected.

Palliative Surgery

Done for relief of distressing signs and symptoms or for retardation of metastasis. Attempt to improve client's quality of life.

  • Reduce pain by interrupting nerve pathways or implanting pain control pumps
  • Relieve airway obstruction
  • Relieve obstruction in the GI and GU tracts
  • Relieve pressure in the brain and spinal cord
  • Prevent hemorrhage
  • Remove infected and ulcerating tumors
  • Drain abscesses

‚ò¢Ô∏è Radiation Therapy

Use of high-energy ionizing radiation that destroys a cell's ability to reproduce by damaging its DNA. Rapidly dividing cells like cancer cells are more vulnerable to radiation.

Goals of Therapy

  • Primary Therapy: The only treatment used and aims to achieve local cure (e.g., early stage skin cancer, Hodgkin‚Äôs disease).
  • Adjuvant Therapy: Done preoperatively or postoperatively to aid in destruction of cancer cells. Also used with chemotherapy.
  • Palliative Therapy: Used to relieve pain caused by obstruction, fractures, compression, and metastases.

Types of Radiation Therapy

1. External Radiation Therapy (Teletherapy, DXT)

  • Administered through high-energy X-ray machine (linear accelerator, cobalt).
  • Major advantage is its skin-sparing effect; maximum effect occurs deep in the body.
  • No need for isolation.

2. Internal Radiation Therapy (Brachytherapy)

Administered within or near the tumor or into systemic circulation.

Sealed Source Unsealed Source
Radioisotope enclosed in a sealed container placed within/near tumor. Administered IV, orally, or by instillation.
Intracavity: Placed in body cavity (e.g., cesium 137 for uterine CA).
Interstitial: Needles/seeds implanted directly into tumor.		 Circulates through the client's body.
Secretions are NOT radioactive. Urine, sweat, blood, vomitus ARE radioactive.

Principles of Radiation Protection (DTS)

  1. 1
    DISTANCE: The greater the distance, the less the exposure. Maintain at least 3 feet when not performing procedures.
  2. 2
    TIME: Limit contact to 5 minutes each time, total of 30 mins per 8-hr shift.
  3. 3
    SHIELDING: Use lead shield during contact with client.

Note: Pregnant staff should not be assigned. Staff must wear dosimeter badges.

Nursing Interventions: Internal Radiation (Sealed Source)

Procedure Rationale
Client's back is turned towards the door. Minimize exposure of staff entering the room.
Complete bed rest; Turn to sides at intervals. To prevent dislodgement of the radioisotope.
Low fiber diet (inhibit defecation) & Enema before procedure. To prevent dislodgement during bowel movements.
Foley catheter in place. Prevent bladder distention and irradiation of bladder (prevents fistula).
Have long forceps and lead container available. To pick up dislodged radioisotope safely.

Client Education: External Radiation (Skin Care)

  • It is painless.
  • Do not wash the treatment area until instructed. When permitted, use warm water/mild soap, pat dry (no rubbing).
  • Do not remove lines/ink marks on skin.
  • Avoid powders, lotions, creams, alcohol, deodorants on treated skin.
  • Wear loose-fitting cotton clothing.
  • Protect from direct sunlight and temperature extremes.

Side Effects & Management

‚ñ∂ Infection & Hemorrhage

Cause: Bone marrow suppression.

Interventions:

  • Monitor blood counts weekly (especially WBC and Platelets).
  • Teach signs of infection and hemorrhage (bleeding gums, black stools).
  • Avoid physical trauma and Aspirin.
‚ñ∂ Stomatitis & Xerostomia (Dry Mouth)
  • Administer analgesics before meals.
  • Bland diet; avoid smoking/alcohol.
  • Saline rinses every 2 hours.
  • Sugarless lemon drops/mints to increase salivation.
‚ñ∂ Fatigue & Weight Loss

Cause: High metabolic demand for tissue repair and toxic waste removal.

Interventions: Plenty of rest and good nutrition.

üíä Chemotherapy

Systemic intervention recommended when disease is widespread, risk of undetectable disease is high, or tumor cannot be resected. Objective: Destroy all malignant tumor cells without excessive destruction of normal cells.

Characteristics

  1. 1
    Affects both normal and cancer cells: Rapidly dividing cells (mucous membranes, blood cells, hair follicles) are vulnerable.
  2. 2
    Fraction cell-kill: Only a certain number of cells are killed with each course; must be given in a series.
  3. 3
    Cell-Cycle Specificity: Can be CCS (specific stage) or CCNS (any stage).

Classification of Agents

‚ñ∂ Cell Cycle - Specific (CCS)
  • Antimetabolites: Cytarabine, 5-FU, Methotrexate
  • Vinca Alkaloids: Vincristine, Vinblastine
  • Epipodophyllotoxins: Etoposide
  • Taxanes: Paclitaxel
‚ñ∂ Cell Cycle - Nonspecific (CCNS)
  • Alkylating Agents: Cisplatin, Cyclophosphamide (Cytoxan), Busulfan
  • Antitumor Antibiotics: Bleomycin, Doxorubicin (Adriamycin)
‚ñ∂ Hormonal Therapy
  • Glucocorticoids: Prednisone, Dexamethasone
  • Antiestrogens: Tamoxifen
  • Progestins: Megestrol acetate

Routes of Administration

  • Intravenous: Risk of extravasation (necrosis). Use of Vascular Access Devices (VADs) like Port-A-Cath or PICC lines preferred.
  • Topical: Fluorouracil cream for skin lesions.
  • Intra-arterial: High concentration to localized tumor.
  • Intracavity/Intraperitoneal: Direct instillation (e.g., bladder, abdomen).
  • Intrathecal: Into CNS via Ommaya reservoir or lumbar puncture (crosses blood-brain barrier).

Side Effects & Nursing Interventions

System/Effect Interventions
GI System
Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea		 Administer antiemetics. Replace fluids/electrolytes. Low-fiber diet for diarrhea; high fiber for constipation.
Hematopoietic
Anemia, Neutropenia, Thrombocytopenia		 Nadir: Lowest blood count point (7-14 days post-drug).
Isolate from infection. Avoid fresh fruits/flowers. Report fever/chills. Change IV sites q48h. Protect from trauma.
Integumentary
Alopecia, Stomatitis		 Reassure alopecia is temporary (regrowth in 8wks). Good oral care. Avoid hot/spicy food.
Renal/Reproductive
Hemorrhagic cystitis, Amenorrhea		 Force fluids (2-3L/day). Reassure menstruation may resume.

ü§ù Other Treatments & Psychosocial Care

Other Treatments

  • Biotherapy (Biologic Response Modifiers)
  • Hematopoietic Growth Factors
  • Monoclonal antibodies
  • Bone Marrow Transplantation (BMT)

Psychosocial Aspects

Promote support for client and family, positive self-concept, and coping.

Hospice Care

Trend in care for terminal cancer (prognosis 1-6 months). Characteristics:

  • Control of manifestations (pain relief).
  • Client and family treated as a unit.
  • Interdisciplinary team, 24/7 service.
  • Coordinated home/inpatient care.
  • Bereavement follow-up.
