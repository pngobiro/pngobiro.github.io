Surgical Intervention

  • Preventive surgery: removal of precancerous lesions or benign tumors
  • Diagnostic surgery: biopsy
  • Curative surgery: removal of an entire tumor
  • Reconstructive surgery: improvement of structures and function of an organ
  • Palliative surgery: relief of distress in signs and symptoms; retardations of metastasis

Radiation therapy is used to kill a tumor, reduce the tumor size, relieve obstruction, or decrease pain. It causes lethal injury to DNA, so it can destroy rapidly multiplying cancer cells, as well as normal cells.

It can be classified as internal radiation therapy (brachytherapy) or external radiation therapy (teletherapy).

A. Internal Radiation (Brachytherapy)

1. Sources of Internal Radiation

  • Implanted into affected tissue or body cavity
  • Ingested as a solution
  • Injected as a solution into the bloodstream or body cavity
  • Introduced through a catheter into the tumor

2. Nursing Management (Safety Principles)

üõ°Ô∏è

Principles of Radiation Protection

Exposure to small amounts of radiation is possible during close contact with persons receiving internal radiation.

  1. 1
    Time: minimize time spent in close proximity to the radiation source; a common standard is to limit contact time to 30 minutes total per 8-hour shift.
  2. 2
    Distance: maintain the maximum distance possible from the radiation source (minimum distance of 6 feet used when possible).
  3. 3
    Shielding: use lead shields and other precautions to reduce exposure to radiation.

3. Client Education

  • Avoid close contact with others until treatment is completed.
  • Maintain daily activities unless contraindicated, allowing for extra rest periods as needed.
  • Maintain balanced diet; may tolerate food better if consumes small, frequent meals.
  • Maintain fluid intake ensure adequate hydration (2-3 liters/day).
  • If implant is temporary, maintain bedrest to avoid dislodging the implant.
  • Excreted body fluids may be radioactive; double-flush toilets after use.
‚ö†Ô∏è

Safety Precautions

  • Place client in private room.
  • Instruct visitors to maintain at least a distance of 6 feet from the client and limit visitors to 10-30 minutes.
  • Ensure proper handling and disposal of body fluids, assuring the containers are marked appropriately.
  • In the event of a dislodged implant, use long-handled forceps and place the implant into a lead container; never directly touch the implant.
  • Do not allow pregnant women to come into any contact with radiation.
  • If working routinely near radiation sources, wear a monitoring device to measure exposure.

B. External Radiation (Teletherapy)

The radiation oncologist marks specific locations for radiation treatment using a semipermanent type of ink. Treatment is usually given 15-30 minutes per day, 5 days per week, for 2-7 weeks. The client does not pose a risk for radiation exposure to other people.

1. Side Effects

  • Tissue damage to target area (erythema, sloughing, hemorrhage)
  • Ulcerations of oral mucous membranes
  • Gastrointestinal effects such as nausea, vomiting, and diarrhea
  • Radiation pneumonia
  • Fatigue, Alopecia, Immunosuppression

2. Client Education (Skin Care)

üõÅ

Skin Care Guidelines

  • Wash the marked area of the skin with plain water only and pat skin dry.
  • Do not use soaps, deodorants, lotions, perfumes, powders or medications on the site during the duration of the treatment.
  • Do not wash off the treatment site marks.
  • Avoid rubbing, scratching, or scrubbing the treatment site.
  • Do not apply extreme temperatures (Heat or Cold) to the treatment site.
  • If shaving, use only an electric razor.
  • Wear soft, loose-fitting clothing over the treatment area.
  • Protect skin from sun exposure during the treatment and for at least 1 year after the treatment is completed.

ü¶¥ Bone Marrow Transplant (BMT)

BMT is used in the treatment of leukemias, usually in conjunction with radiation or chemotherapy.

Types of BMT

  • Autologous BMT: the client is infused with own bone marrow harvested during remission of disease.
  • Allogenic BMT: the client is infused with donor bone marrow harvested from a healthy individual.

The Process

  1. 1
    Harvesting: Bone marrow is usually harvested from the iliac crest, then frozen and stored until transfusion.
  2. 2
    Conditioning: Before receiving the BMT, the client must first undergo a phase of immunosuppressive therapy to destroy the immune system. Infection, bleeding, and death are major complications that can occur during this phase.
  3. 3
    Transfusion: After immunosuppression, the bone marrow is transfused intravenously through a central line.

Nursing Management

  • Monitor for graft-versus-host disease.
  • Provide private room for the hospitalized client; client will be hospitalized for 6-8 weeks.
  • Encourage contact with significant others by using telephone, computer, and other means of communication to reduce feelings of isolation.
  • Monitor for side effects: malnutrition, infection related to immunosuppression, bleeding related to thrombocytopenia.

üî¨ Other Therapeutic Interventions

1. Immunotherapy / Biologic Response Modifiers (BRM)

Enhances the person‚Äôs own immune responses in order to modify the biologic processes resulting in malignant cells. Currently considered experimental in use.

  • Monoclonal antibodies: antibodies are recovered from an inoculated animal with a specific tumor antigen, then given to the person with that particular cancer type; the goal is destruction of the tumor.
  • Cytokines: normal growth-regulating molecules possessing antitumor abilities.
    • Interleukin-2 (IL-2): increases immune response effective and destroys abnormal cells.
    • Interferons: substances produced by cells to protect them from viral infection and replication (e.g., interferon-alpha 2b).
    • Hematopoietic growth factors: such as granulocyte colony-stimulating factor (G-CSF) and erythropoietin, balance the suppression of granulocytes and erythrocytes resulting from chemotherapy.
  • Natural killer (NK) cells: exert a spontaneous cytotoxic effect on specific cancer cells; they also secrete cytokines and provide a resistance to metastasis.

2. Gene Therapy

Current use is investigational. Increases susceptibility of cancer cells to the destruction by other treatments; insertion of specific genes enhances ability of client‚Äôs own immune system to recognize and destroy cancer cells.

3. Photodynamic Therapy

Used to treat specific superficial tumors such as those of the surface of bladder, bronchus, chest wall, head, neck and peritoneal cavity.

  • Photofrin, a photosensitizing compound, is administered intravenously where it is retained by malignant tissue.
  • Three days after injection, the drug is activated by a laser treatment which continues for 3 more days.
  • The drug produces a cytotoxic oxygen molecule (singlet oxygen).
  • Nursing Care: During intravenous administration, monitor for chills, nausea, rash, local skin reactions, and temporary photosensitivity.
  • Education: Drug remains in tissues 4-6 weeks after injection; direct or indirect exposure to sun activates drug, resulting in chemical sunburn; educate client to protect skin from exposure to sun.
