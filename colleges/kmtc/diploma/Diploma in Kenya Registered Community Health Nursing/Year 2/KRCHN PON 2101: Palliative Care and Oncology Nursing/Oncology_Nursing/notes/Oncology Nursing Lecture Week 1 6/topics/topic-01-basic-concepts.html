üìñ Key Terminologies

Understanding oncology requires familiarity with specific medical terminology related to cell growth and tumor classification.

CANCER
A disease of the cell in which the normal mechanisms of the control of growth and proliferation have been altered.
NEOPLASIA
Abnormal cellular changes and growth of new tissues.
BENIGN NEOPLASM
A harmless growth that does not spread or invade other tissues.
MALIGNANT NEOPLASM
It is invasive, spreading directly to surrounding tissues as well as to new sites in the body.
METASTASES
Spread of cancer cells to distant parts of the body to set up new tumors.

Cellular Changes

  • HYPERPLASIA: Increase in cell number.
  • HYPERTROPHY: Increase in cell size.
  • METAPLASIA: Replacement of one adult cell type by a different adult cell type.
  • DYSPLASIA: Changes in cell size, shape, organization.
  • ANAPLASIA: Reverse cellular development to a more primitive or embryonic cell type.

Adenocarcinoma

Cancer that arises from glandular tissues (breast, lung, thyroid, colon and pancreas CA).

Carcinoma

Cancer that is composed of epithelial cells; develops in tissues covering or lining organs of the body such as skin, uterus or breast.

Sarcoma

Cancer of supporting or connecting tissues such as cartilage, bones, muscles or fats.

üî¨ Pathogenesis of Cancer

Two primary theories describe the development of cancer:

1
Cellular Transformation and Derangement Theory: Normal cells may be transformed into cancer cells due to exposure to some etiologic agents.
2
Failure of the Immune Response Theory: All individuals possess cancer cells; however, they are typically recognized and destroyed by the immune response system. Cancer develops when this surveillance fails.

‚ö†Ô∏è Etiologic Factors (Carcinogens)

1. Viruses and Bacteria

Oncogenic viruses may be one of the multiple agents acting to initiate carcinogenesis. Prolonged or frequent viral infections may cause breakdown of the immune system or overwhelm the immune system.

Pathogen Associated Cancer
Human Papilloma Virus (HPV) Cervical cancer
Epstein-Barr Virus Lymphoma
Hepatitis B and C Hepatocellular cancer
Helicobacter Pylori Gastric cancer

2. Chemical Carcinogens

Chemicals cause cell mutation or alteration in cell enzymes and proteins causing altered cell replication.

‚ñ∂ Industrial Compounds
  • Vinyl chloride: Used for plastic manufacture, asbestos factories, construction works.
  • Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons: From refuse burning, auto and truck emissions, oil refineries, air pollution.
  • Fertilizers, weed killers.
  • Dyes: Aniline dyes used in beauty shops, hair bleach.
‚ñ∂ Drugs & Hormones
  • Tobacco: (tar, nicotine); 90% of all cases of lung CA are due to smoking.
  • Alcohol.
  • Cytotoxic drugs.
  • Diethylstilbestrol (DES): Non-steroidal estrogen medication used for pregnancy support; hormone therapy for menopausal symptoms and estrogen deficiency.
  • Estrogen replacement therapy: Found to increase incidence of vaginal, cervical and uterine CAs.
‚ñ∂ Foods & Preservatives
  • Nitrates: Bacon, smoked meat.
  • Talc: Polished rice, salami, chewing gum.
  • Food sweeteners.
  • Nitrosamines: Rubber baby nipples.
  • Aflatoxins: Mold in nuts and grains, milk, cheese, peanut butter.
  • Polycyclic Hydrocarbon: Charcoal broiling.

3. Physical Agents

Radiation: From X-rays, radioactive isotopes, or sunlight/UV rays.
Physical Irritation/Trauma:
  • Pipe smoking
  • Multiple deliveries
  • Jagged tooth / Irritation of the tongue
  • Overuse of any organ/body part

4. Genetics

When an oncogene (hidden or repressed genetic code for cancer that exists in all individuals) is exposed to carcinogens, changes in cell structure occurs, and a malignant tumor develops.

Regardless of the cause, several cancers are associated with familial patterns.

05

üìä Predisposing Factors

AGE Older individuals are more prone to cancer because they have been exposed to carcinogens longer and have developed alterations in the immune system.
SEX The most common type of cancer in females is breast cancer, and prostate cancer in males.
URBAN vs RURAL Cancer is more common among urban dwellers than among rural residents, probably due to greater exposure to carcinogens, more stressful lifestyle, and greater consumption of preservatives.
GEOGRAPHIC Most common type of cancer in Japan is gastric cancer; in USA is breast cancer. May be due to influence of environmental factors such diet (raw foods greatly consist Japanese diet), ethnic customs, types of pollution.
OCCUPATION Greater risk of exposure to carcinogens among chemical factory workers, farmers, radiology department personnel.
HEREDITY Positive family history of cancer increases risk to develop the disease in adults (approx. 34% of cancers have a familial basis).
STRESS Depression, grief, anger, aggression, despair, or life stresses decrease immunocompetence because of affectation of hypothalamus and pituitary gland. Immunodeficiency may spur the growth and proliferation of cancer cells.
PRECANCEROUS LESIONS Pigmented moles, burn scars, senile keratosis, leukopenia, benign polyps or adenoma of the colon or stomach, fibrocystic disease of the breast may undergo transformation into cancerous lesions.
OBESITY Studies have linked obesity to breast and colorectal cancer.

‚öñÔ∏è Benign vs. Malignant Neoplasms

Characteristic BENIGN MALIGNANT
Growth Rate Grows slowly Grows rapidly
Localization Remains localized Infiltrates surrounding tissues
Capsule Encapsulated Not encapsulated
Differentiation Well-differentiated mature cells (though cells function poorly) Poorly-differentiated; anaplastic/embryonic type of cells
Recurrence Extremely unusual when surgically removed Commonly following surgery because of spread to other tissues
Metastasis Never occur Very common
Harm to Host Not harmful to host, unless it compresses tissues or obstructs vital organ Always harmful to host; may result in necrosis, ulcerations, hemorrhage, infection
Prognosis Very good Poor prognosis if cells are poorly differentiated and evidence of metastasis

üè∑Ô∏è Nomenclature of Neoplasia

Tumors are named according to:

  1. 1
    PARENCHYMA: e.g., Hepatoma (liver), Osteoma (bone), Myoma (muscle).
  2. 2
    PATTERN AND STRUCTURE:
    • Fluid-filled: Cyst
    • Glandular: Adeno
    • Finger-like: Papillo
    • Stalk: Polyp
  3. 3
    EMBRYONIC ORIGIN:
    • Ectoderm: usually gives rise to epithelium
    • Endoderm: glands
    • Mesoderm: connective tissues

Important Exceptions

Some malignant tumors end in -OMA:

  • Hepatoma
  • Lymphoma
  • Glioma
  • Melanoma

Other terms:

  • Teratoma: Tumor derived from three germ layers.
  • Hematoma: Non-neoplastic swelling of clotted blood (not a tumor).
