ü©∏ Leukemia

Leukemia is a malignant exacerbation in the number of leukocytes, usually at an immature stage, in the bone marrow.

üîç Click to zoom
  • Effects: Affects the bone marrow causing anemia, leukopenia, the production of immature cells, thrombocytopenia, and a decline in immunity.
  • Onset: May be acute (sudden onset, short duration) or chronic (slow onset, persistent symptoms over years).
üß¨

Etiology

Causes include genetic factors, viral infections, immunological factors, environmental factors, exposure to radiation, and certain medications.

Classifications

Type Cell Type Age of Onset
Acute Lymphocytic Leukemia (ALL) Mostly lymphoblasts Less than 15 years
Acute Myelogenous Leukemia (AML) Mostly myeloblasts Between 15 and 39 years
Chronic Myelogenous Leukemia (CML) Mostly granulocytes After 50 years
Chronic Lymphocytic Leukemia (CLL) Mostly lymphocytes After 50 years

Assessment

  • Anorexia, fatigue, weakness, weight loss
  • Bleeding (nosebleeds, gum bleeding, rectal bleeding, increased menstrual flow, petechiae, prolonged bleeding after minor abrasions)
  • Elevated Temperature
  • Lymphadenopathy and splenomegaly
  • Palpitations, tachycardia, orthostatic hypotension
  • Pallor, dyspnea on exertion, headache
  • Bone pain and joint swelling
  • Diagnosis: Positive bone marrow biopsy identifying leukemic blast phase cells; decreased Hgb, Hct, and platelets.

ü¶† Hodgkin's Disease

Malignancy of the lymph nodes that originates in a single lymph node or a single chain of nodes. It usually involves lymph nodes, tonsils, spleen, and bone marrow.

üîç Click to zoom
üîç

Diagnostic Hallmark

Characterized by the presence of the Reed-Sternberg cell in the nodes.

Staging

‚ñ∂ Stage I

Involvement of a single lymph node region or an extra lymphatic organ or site.

‚ñ∂ Stage II

Involvement of two or more lymph node regions on the same side of the diaphragm or localized involvement of an extralymphatic organ or site.

‚ñ∂ Stage III

Involvement of lymph node regions on both sides of the diaphragm.

‚ñ∂ Stage IV

Diffuse or disseminated involvement of one or more extralymphatic organs with or without associated lymph node involvement.

Assessment

  • Fever, malaise, fatigue, and weakness
  • Night sweats
  • Loss of appetite and significant weight loss
  • Enlarged lymph nodes, spleen and liver (Cervical nodes most often affected first)

Interventions

  • Stages I and II: Extensive external radiation of involved regions.
  • Extensive Disease: Radiation + Multi-agent chemotherapy.
  • Monitor for side effects (infection, bleeding).
  • Discuss sterility possibilities and sperm banking options.

ü¶¥ Multiple Myeloma

A malignant proliferation of plasma cells and tumors within the bone. Abnormal plasma cells invade bone marrow, destroy bone, and invade lymph nodes, spleen, and liver.

üß™

Bence Jones Protein

Abnormal plasma cells produce an abnormal antibody (myeloma protein or Bence Jones protein) found in the blood and urine.

üîç Click to zoom

Assessment

  • Bone pain (especially pelvis, spine, ribs)
  • Weakness, fatigue, recurrent infections
  • Osteoporosis, skeletal fractures
  • Elevated calcium and uric acid levels
  • Renal failure
  • Spinal cord compression and paraplegia

Interventions

  • Monitor for bleeding, infection, and fractures.
  • Encourage fluids (3-4 L/day) to prevent renal problems and hypercalcemia.
  • Encourage ambulation to slow bone resorption.
  • Provide skeletal support during moving to prevent pathological fractures.
  • Provide a hazard-free environment.

Malignant lesions of the skin caused by chronic friction, irritation, and exposure to ultraviolet rays. Diagnosis is confirmed by skin biopsy.

Types

  • Basal Cell: Most common, arises from basal cells in epidermis.
  • Squamous Cell: Second most common, tumor of epidermal keratinocytes, can infiltrate and metastasize.
  • Malignant Melanoma: Cancer of melanocytes, highly metastatic (brain, lungs, bone, liver, skin).
üîç Click to zoom

Assessment

  • Change in color, size, or shape of preexisting lesions.
  • Pruritus and local soreness.
  • Appearance: Waxy nodule; irregular, circular, bordered lesions with hues of tan, black, or blue; small red nodular lesion; oozing, bleeding, crusting lesion.

Interventions & Prevention

Preventive Measures

  • Wear layered clothing and use sun screening lotions.
  • Avoid sun exposure between 11 am to 3 pm.
  • Avoid contact with chemical irritants.
  • Monitor for lesions that do not heal or change characteristics.
