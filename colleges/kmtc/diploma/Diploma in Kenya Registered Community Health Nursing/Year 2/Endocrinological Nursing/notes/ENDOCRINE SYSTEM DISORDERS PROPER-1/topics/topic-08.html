‚ö° Acute Complications

There are three major acute complications of DM related to short term imbalances in blood glucose levels:

  1. Hypoglycemia
  2. Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA)
  3. Hyperglycemic hyperosmolar non ketotic syndrome (HHNS)

1. Hypoglycemia

Refers to low blood sugar (below 3.3mmol/l). Occurs when insulin is at their peak levels. Severe hypoglycemia is a medical emergency.

Causes

  • Too much insulin or Oral hypoglycemic agents
  • Excessive physical activity
  • Little food

Clinical Manifestations

Adrenergic Symptoms (Mild)

Sympathetic nervous system stimulation (surge of epinephrine):

  • Sweating, Tremor
  • Tachycardia, Palpitation
  • Nervousness, Hunger
CNS Symptoms (Moderate/Severe)

Deprivation of brain cells of fuel:

  • Inability to concentrate, Headache, Confusion
  • Numbness of lips/tongue, Slurred speech
  • Double vision, Drowsiness
  • Severe: Seizures, Difficulty arousing, Loss of consciousness
üç¨

Management Rule

15/15 Rule for Mild Hypoglycemia: Take 15 gm of rapid-acting sugar (juice/glucose). Wait 15 minutes. Monitor. If still low, repeat.

Severe (Unconscious): Administer 10 mls of 50% bolus dextrose followed by 5% infusion OR 1 mg Glucagon IM.

üß™ Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)

Caused by an absence or markedly inadequate amount of insulin. Results from breakdown of fat and overproduction of ketones. Common in type 1 DM.

Pathophysiology

  • Hyperglycemia: Liver increases glucose production.
  • Osmotic Diuresis: Kidneys excrete glucose, water, and electrolytes leading to dehydration.
  • Ketosis: Breakdown of fat (lipolysis) into free fatty acids and glycerol. Fatty acids converted to ketone bodies (acetone, acetoacetic acid, beta hydroxybutyric acid) ‚Üí Metabolic Acidosis.

Clinical Manifestations

Main Features

  • Hyperglycemia (300-800 mg/dl)
  • Dehydration & Electrolyte loss
  • Acidosis (Low pH 6.8-7.3, Low Bicarb < 15 mEq/L)

Specific Signs

  • Kussmaul's respirations (Deep breathing to blow off CO2)
  • Fruity breath (Acetone)
  • Nausea/Abdominal pain
  • Polyuria, Polydipsia
üö®

Emergency Management

  1. Rehydration: 0.9% NS at rapid rate (0.5-1 L/hr for 2-3 hrs).
  2. Insulin: IV infusion (slow, continuous rate, e.g., 5 units/hr). Monitor glucose hourly.
  3. Electrolytes: Restore Potassium (prevent fatal dysrhythmias).
  4. Note: Bicarbonate infusion is avoided unless severe acidosis due to risk of precipitating hypokalemia.
03

üå°Ô∏è Hyperglycemic Hyperosmolar Non-Ketotic Syndrome (HHNKS)

Extremely high blood sugars without presence of ketones. Enough insulin is secreted to prevent ketosis, but not enough to prevent hyperglycemia. Common in older people with Type 2 Diabetes.

Pathophysiology

  • Hyperglycemia leads to increased urine output and dehydration.
  • Kidneys retain glucose; glucose and sodium rise.
  • Severe hyperosmolar state develops leading to brain cell shrinkage.
Comparison of DKA and HHNS
Feature DKA (Diabetic Ketoacidosis) HHNS (Hyperosmolar Syndrome)
Patient Type Usually Type 1 Usually Type 2 (Older)
Onset Rapid (< 24 hrs) Slow (Days to Weeks)
Blood Glucose > 250 mg/dL (13.9 mmol/L) > 600 mg/dL (33.3 mmol/L)
Ketones Present (Blood/Urine) Absent
pH (Acidosis) < 7.3 (Acidotic) Normal
Osmolality Variable > 340 mOsm/L (Severe)
Mental Status Alert to Coma Often Altered/Coma

Emergency Management (HHNS)

  • Fluid replacement (0.9% or 0.45% NS).
  • Insulin administration (0.1 units/kg/hr).
  • Correction of electrolyte imbalances (Potassium).
  • Monitor for fluid overload and neurological status.

üï∞Ô∏è Chronic Complications

Chronic elevation of blood glucose level leads to damage of blood vessels (angiopathy). The endothelial cells take in more glucose, forming glycoproteins that thicken the basement membrane.

Macrovascular (Large Vessel)

Thickening, sclerosis, and occlusion of vessel walls by plaque.

  • Coronary Artery Disease
  • Cerebrovascular Disease (Stroke)
  • Peripheral Vascular Disease

Microvascular (Small Vessel)

Capillary basement membrane thickening.

  • Retinopathy (Eye)
  • Nephropathy (Kidney)
  • Neuropathy (Nerve)

Diabetic Retinopathy

Characterized by hemorrhage, swelling, and decreased vision. Most common cause of blindness among non-elderly adults.

  1. Background stage: Microaneurysms leak fluid (asymptomatic).
  2. Pre-proliferative stage: Increased destruction of vessels.
  3. Proliferative stage: Abnormal growth of new blood vessels (friable) that rupture and bleed into vitreous humor.

Diabetic Nephropathy

Glomerular changes leading to impaired renal function. First indicator is microalbuminuria. Most common cause of end-stage renal failure.

Diabetic Neuropathy

Demyelinization of nerves caused by hyperglycemia.

  • Peripheral: Numbness, tingling, pain (sensorimotor).
  • Autonomic: Affects organ systems (e.g., gastroparesis, urinary retention, sexual dysfunction/impotence).

ü¶∂ Diabetic Foot Care

People with diabetes are at increased risk of foot ulcers and amputations due to neuropathy (loss of sensation), peripheral vascular disease, and poor healing.

üõ°Ô∏è

Cornerstones of Management

  • Regular inspection of the "foot at risk".
  • Appropriate footwear (Never walk barefoot).
  • Early treatment of problems.
  • Optimize glucose control and stop smoking.
