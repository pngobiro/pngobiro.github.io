üí• Disorders of Adrenal Medulla: Pheochromocytoma

  • Pheochromocytoma is a benign tumor of adrenal gland.
  • Originates from the chromaffin cells of the adrenal medulla.
  • Adrenal medulla Produces catecholamines: adrenaline (epinephrine) and norepinephrine
  • The tumor causes Increased secretion of epinephrine and nor-epinephrine by the adrenal medulla
  • May occur at any age, but its peak incidence is between ages 40 and 50 years.

Clinical Manifestations

The typical triad of symptoms comprises headache, diaphoresis (profuse sweating) and palpitations.

üñêÔ∏è

The "Five Hs"

Signs are often associated with:

  1. Hypertension
  2. Headache
  3. Hyperhidrosis (excessive sweating)
  4. Hypermetabolism
  5. Hyperglycemia

Management

Medical

  • Alpha-blockers: Phentolamine (Regitine) or Phenoxybenzamine (Dibenzyline) to lower BP.
  • Beta-blockers: Propranolol (Inderal) for dysrhythmias (used after alpha-blockers).
  • Sodium Nitroprusside: For rapid BP control.

Surgical (Definitive)

  • Adrenalectomy: Removal of adrenal gland.
  • Preparation: Control BP/volume for 7-10 days pre-op.
  • Post-op: Corticosteroid replacement (Solu-Medrol) required if bilateral (or temporary for unilateral).

Nursing Alert: Hypertensive Crisis

Avoid palpating the gland (abdomen) as it stimulates the tumor that can cause increased BP and massive release of catecholamines.

üìâ Addison's Disease (Adrenal Insufficiency)

Definition: Adrenal insufficiency resulting from decreased secretion of adrenal cortex hormones, especially glucocorticoids and mineralocorticoids. Hypofunction of adrenal cortex.

Causes

  • Autoimmune destruction of the gland (80% of cases)
  • Infections like Tuberculosis (20%) and histoplasmosis
  • Surgical removal of both adrenal glands
  • Abrupt withdrawal of therapeutic corticosteroids

Clinical Manifestations

Physical Signs

  • Bronze pigmentation (Increased skin pigmentation due to high ACTH)
  • Weight loss
  • Muscle weakness, lethargy, fatigue
  • Dehydration and hypovolemia
  • Postural Hypotension

Metabolic/Lab Findings

  • Hyponatremia (Low Sodium)
  • Hyperkalemia (High Potassium)
  • Hypoglycemia (Low Glucose)
  • Low serum cortisol

Medical Management

  • Hydrocortisone (Solu-Cortef): Administered IV, followed by 5% dextrose in normal saline.
  • Vasopressors: Amines (epinephrine) if hypotension persists.
  • Lifelong Replacement: Corticosteroids and mineralocorticoids.

üö® Addisonian Crisis

A life-threatening disorder caused by acute severe adrenal insufficiency.

‚ö°

Precipitating Factors

Infection, Trauma, Severe stress, Dehydration, Exposure to cold, Surgery, or abrupt withdrawal of steroids.

Emergency Management

  • IV Fluids: Normal saline and 5% dextrose to correct volume and glucose.
  • Steroids: Hydrocortisone IV immediately.
  • Monitoring: VS (BP/Pulse), Electrolytes (Hyperkalemia/Hyponatremia), Glucose.
  • Position: Recumbent with legs elevated (shock position).

üåù Cushing's Syndrome

Definition: A condition resulting from the hyper-secretion of Glucocorticoids from the adrenal cortex.

Clinical Manifestations

Physical Changes

  • Moon-face (fat deposits on face)
  • Buffalo hump (fat between shoulders)
  • Central/Truncal obesity
  • Thin extremities (muscle wasting)
  • Reddish-purplish striae
  • Easy bruising (thinned skin)

Systemic Effects

  • Hypertension (Na+ & H2O retention)
  • Hyperglycemia
  • Osteoporosis
  • Hirsutism (excess hair)
  • Amenorrhea/Erectile dysfunction
  • Susceptibility to infection

Management

  • Surgery: Trans-sphenoidal hypophysectomy (if pituitary tumor) or Adrenalectomy (if adrenal tumor).
  • Enzyme Inhibitors: Metyrapone, Ketoconazole (block adrenal enzymes).
  • Tapering: If caused by therapeutic corticosteroids, slow tapering is required.

Nursing Management

  1. Skin Care: Meticulous care, avoid adhesive tape (fragile skin).
  2. Infection Control: Protect form exposure (immune suppression).
  3. Diet: LOW carbohydrate, LOW sodium, HIGH protein.
  4. Monitor: Glucose, Electrolytes, BP, Weight.

üßÇ Hyperaldosteronism (Conn's Syndrome)

Definition: Excess aldosterone secretion.

Types

  • Primary (Conn's): Aldosterone secreting adenoma or hyperplasia. Independent of renin-angiotensin.
  • Secondary: Increased renin-angiotensin system (CHF, Cirrhosis, Nephrotic syndrome).

Clinical Features

üí°

Key Triad

Aldosterone causes renal reabsorption of Sodium (and Water) and secretion of Potassium.

  • Hypertension (Moderate to severe)
  • Hypokalemia (Muscle weakness, paresthesia, arrhythmias)
  • Hypernatremia

Treatment

  • Pharmacotherapy: Spironolactone (Aldosterone antagonist).
  • Surgery: Adrenalectomy (removal of the affected gland).
