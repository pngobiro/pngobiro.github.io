üíä Oral Glucose Lowering Agents (OGLAs)

Indications: Failure of lifestyle modifications, Fasting blood glucose level > 11 mmol/L, Random blood glucose level > 15 mmol/L.

1. Sulphonylureas

Action: Stimulates pancreatic cells to secrete more insulin and increases sensitivity of peripheral tissues to insulin. Used for non-obese Type 2 diabetes.

  • Glibenclamide: 2.5 - 15 mg
  • Glipizide: 5 - 40 mg
  • Chlorpropamide: 250 - 500mg
  • S/E: Hypoglycaemia, weight gain, skin rashes

2. Biguanides

Action: Decreases overproduction of glucose by liver and makes insulin more effective. Used in obese diabetics. Does not stimulate insulin release.

  • Metformin (Glucophage): 500mg - 2550mg
  • S/E: Abdominal pain, lactic acidosis
  • C/I: Renal, heart and liver failure

3. Thiazolidinediones

Action: Sensitizes peripheral tissues to insulin. Used in obese diabetics.

  • Rosiglitazone: 4mg - 8mg
  • Pioglitazone: 15mg - 45mg
  • S/E: Liver impairment, fluid retention

4. Meglitinides

Action: Stimulates pancreatic cells to secret more insulin. Rapid onset.

  • Repaglinide: 1.0 mg - 16mg
  • S/E: Hypoglycemia, weight gain

Precautions

  • Long-acting sulphonylureas should be avoided in elderly patients.
  • Metformin should be used with care in the elderly and is contraindicated in renal/liver disease.

Self-Injection of Insulin

  1. With one hand, stabilize the skin by spreading it or pinching up a large area.
  2. Pick up syringe with the other hand and hold it as you would a pencil. Insert needle straight into the skin (or 45-degree).
  3. To inject the insulin, push the plunger all the way in.
  4. Pull needle straight out of skin. Press cotton ball over injection site for several seconds.
  5. Use disposable syringe only once and discard into hard plastic container.

Complications of Insulin Therapy

  • Hypoglycemia: Most common.
  • Lipodystrophy: Loss of subcutaneous fat from repeated injections at same site.
  • Insulin Resistance: Daily requirement of >200 IU.
  • Allergic Reactions: Local (redness, swelling) or Systemic (rare).
  • Somogyi effect: Rebound morning hyperglycemia following night hypoglycemia.

üçé Nutrition & Exercise

Nutritional Management

Goal: Control total caloric intake, maintain reasonable body weight, and control blood glucose levels.

  • Caloric Requirements: 50-60% carbohydrates, 20-30% fat, 10-20% protein.
  • Type 2 Principles:
    • Avoid pure (simple) sugars.
    • Eat at least three meals a day (avoid binge eating).
    • Measure food quantities.
    • Avoid Alcohol.

Exercise

Exercise lowers blood glucose by increasing uptake by muscles and improving insulin utilization.

Exercise Precautions

  • Monitor blood sugars before and after. Delayed hypoglycemia may occur.
  • Avoid strenuous exercise if:
    • Blood glucose > 250 mg/dl (14 mmol/L) with ketonuria.
    • Blood glucose < 80 mg/dl (4.5 mmol/L).
  • Proper footwear must always be worn.

üë©‚Äç‚öïÔ∏è Nursing Management

  • Monitor fasting and random blood sugars daily.
  • Administer prescribed insulin and OGLAs.
  • Continuous monitoring for signs of acute (hypoglycemia, DKA) and chronic complications.
  • Provide balanced diabetic diet.
  • Encourage physical activity.
  • Initiate safety precautionary measures (foot wear).
  • Provide health education on:
    • Signs of hypoglycemia/hyperglycemia.
    • Foot care (prevent ulcers/amputation).
    • Medication administration (insulin storage, sites).
