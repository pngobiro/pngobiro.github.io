üî¨ Anatomy and Physiology

  • Highly vascularised gland located in front of larynx at 5th, 6th, 7th cervical and 1st thoracic vertebrae (C5, C6, C7 and T1 vertebrae)
  • It's made up of two lobes
  • Blood is supplied through inferior and superior thyroid arteries.
  • Venous return is by thyroid veins
  • There are two parathyroid glands laying at posterior surface of each lobe
  • It is innervated by recurrent laryngeal nerve

THYROID HORMONES

There three major thyroid hormones- thyroxine (T4) Tri-iodothyronine (T3) and calcitonin

üîÑ

Regulation Loop

  1. Stress, Malnutrition, Low plasma glucose and Exercises stimulate hypothalamus to release thyroid releasing Hormone (TRH)
  2. Once released, the TRH is sent to anterior pituitary gland which stimulate thyroid stimulating hormone (TSH)
  3. TSH is sent to Thyroid gland to stimulate production of T3 and T4.
  4. Iodine react with tyrosine to form thyroid hormone

T3 and T4

  • Thyroxine (T4) is produced in large amounts (93%)
  • Tri-iodothyronine (T3) is only 7% but is more potent than T4.
  • 25% of T4 is converted to T3 by de-iodination.
  • Functions:
    • Essential for normal growth and development
    • Regulate metabolism of carbohydrates, fats and proteins
    • Increases basal metabolic rate and heat production

Calcitonin

  • Secreted by parafollicular or C-cells
  • Secreted when there is increased levels of calcium in the blood
  • Functions: Reduces calcium level in the plasma by:
    • stimulating calcium uptake in the bone matrix
    • inhibit reabsorption of calcium in the kidney tubules

üß™ Diagnostic Studies

1. Thyroid function tests (TFTs)

  • Measurements of TSH, T3 and T4.
  • Normal serum levels of T3 70-220ng/dl.
  • Elevated T3 and T4 with depressed TSH reflect primary hyperthyroidism.
  • Reduced T3 and T4 with depressed TSH reflect primary hypothyroidism.
TSH Level FT4 Result Interpretation
HIGH LOW Hypothyroidism (Under-active thyroid)
LOW HIGH Hyperthyroidism (Over-active thyroid)

2. Radioactive iodine uptake test

A client take an oral dose of a small amount radioiodine. Checked after 2, 6 or 24 hrs to determine how much iodine has been absorbed by thyroid.

  • Increased uptake may indicate hyperfunctioning of thyroid gland
  • Decreased uptake may indicate hypofunctioning of the gland

3. Thyroid scan

Performed to identify nodules or growth in the thyroid gland.

Pre-Procedure Care

  • Client is put on NPO
  • Thyroid medications are temporarily withheld

üìâ HYPOTHYROIDISM

Definition: refers to hyposecretion of thyroid hormones specifically T3 and T4. It is also referred to as thyroid deficiency. When thyroid deficiency is present at birth, the condition is known as cretinism.

Causes

  • Autoimmune thyroiditis (Hashimoto‚Äôs disease) - most common cause
  • Atrophy of thyroid gland with aging
  • Radioactive iodine treatment
  • Thyroidectomy
  • Medications (Lithium, Antithyroid meds)
  • Iodine deficiency

Clinical Manifestations

  • Extreme fatigue & Slowness of movement
  • Hair loss, brittle nails, dry skin
  • Cold intolerance
  • Weight gain
  • Constipation
  • Forgetfulness & Irritability
  • Menstrual disturbances (menorrhagia/amenorrhea)
  • Bradycardia

Medical Management

Hormone replacement therapy: synthetic levothyroxine is administered.

üíä

Levothyroxine (Synthroid)

  • Mode of Action: Increases metabolic rate of all cells.
  • Pharmacokinetics: Highly bound to proteins, half life of 7 days (therapeutic effects delayed for 2 weeks).
  • Administration: Starting dosage 12.5mcg/day. Take on an empty stomach 30-60 min before eating in the morning.
  • Duration: Replacement therapy must be continued for life.
  • Interactions: Increases need for insulin, increases effects of warfarin, increases cardiac response to catecholamines.

Nursing Management

  1. Promote independence in self-care by spacing activities to promote rest.
  2. Provide extra layer of clothing/blanket for cold intolerance (Avoid external heat sources like heating pads which cause vasodilation).
  3. Encourage increased fluid intake and foods high in fiber to prevent constipation.
  4. Monitor vital signs, especially body temperature and heart rate.
  5. Advise to report palpitation, tachycardia, and chest pain when using levothyroxine.

üÜò Myxedema & Myxedema Coma

Myxedema: Accumulation of mucopolysaccharides in tissues, causing skin thickening and non-pitting edema. Occurs in severe, long-standing hypothyroidism.

üö®

Myxedema Coma (Medical Emergency)

The most extreme, severe stage of hypothyroidism.

Presenting Features:

  • Severe hypothermia
  • Depressed respirations
  • Coma
  • Cool clammy skin

Emergency Management

  • Admit to ICU.
  • IV levothyroxine administered until consciousness is restored.
  • Ventilatory support if CO2 retention/hypoventilation occurs.
  • Provide warmth (blankets).
  • Fluids administered cautiously (danger of water intoxication).
‚ö°

Caution with IV Levothyroxine

Monitor for myocardial ischemia or infarction. Be alert for signs of angina, especially during early treatment, as the heart is forced to increase metabolism rapidly in a patient with atherosclerosis.

üìà HYPERTHYROIDISM

Definition: Hyperthyroid state characterized by increased circulating T3 and T4. Affects women 8x more frequently than men.

Causes

  • Graves disease: Most common type. Autoimmune stimulation by immunoglobulins.
  • Thyroiditis
  • Excessive ingestion of thyroid hormone
  • Tumors of thyroid or pituitary

Clinical Manifestations

  • Weight loss (despite increased appetite)
  • HEAT intolerance
  • Hypertension & Tachycardia (90-160 bpm)
  • Diarrhea
  • Flushed, warm, soft, moist skin
  • Fine tremors & nervousness
  • Exophthalmos (bulging eyes) with startled expression
  • Enlarged thyroid (Goiter)
  • Oligomenorrhea/Amenorrhea
  • Irritability, mood swings, agitation

Pharmacological Therapy

Propylthiouracil (PTU)

  • Action: Inhibits thyroid hormone synthesis and blocks conversion of T4 to T3.
  • Uses: Hyperthyroidism, Thyroid storm, Pregnancy (preferred).
  • Adverse Effects: Agranulocytosis (sore throat/fever), skin rashes.
  • Dose: 100-150mg every 6-8 hrs.

Carbimazole

  • Action: Decreases uptake of iodine.
  • Dose: Initial 15-40 mg daily; maintenance 5-15 mg.
  • Note: High rate of relapse in first year after discontinuation.

Other Therapies

  • Radioactive Iodine: Single oral dose to destroy thyroid tissue.
  • Beta-blockers (Propranolol): Control sympathetic effects (tachycardia, tremor, anxiety).

üå©Ô∏è Thyroid Storm (Thyrotoxic Crisis)

An acute LIFE-threatening condition characterized by excessive thyroid hormone.

üö®

Clinical Manifestations

  1. High fever (hyperpyrexia) > 38.5¬∞C
  2. Extreme tachycardia (> 130 bpm)
  3. Altered neurologic state (delirium, psychosis, coma)
  4. Severe weight loss, diarrhea, abdominal pain

Emergency Management

  • Oxygen: Maintain airway and ventilation.
  • Anti-thyroid Meds: PTU or Carbimazole to block synthesis.
  • Iodine (Lugol's solution): Decrease output of T4.
  • Beta-blockers (Propranolol): Reduce cardiac symptoms.
  • Hydrocortisone: Treat shock/adrenal insufficiency.
  • Cooling: Acetaminophen and cool environment (Avoid aspirin as it displaces thyroid hormone).
  • Fluids: IV dextrose to replace glycogen stores.
07

üß£ Goiter

Abnormal enlargement of thyroid gland due to inflammation or tumor that becomes visible. Common in women (4:1 ratio).

Toxic Goiter

Associated with hyperthyroidism.

  • Diffuse toxic goiter (Graves disease)
  • Toxic multinodular goiter

Non-Toxic Goiter

No hyper/hypothyroidism.

  • Diffuse non-toxic (hypertrophy/hyperplasia)
  • Multinodular non-toxic (most common cause of tracheal compression)
  • Simple goiter (iodine deficiency/goitrogens)

Causes

  • Iodine deficiency
  • Goitrogens (substances interfering with iodine uptake e.g., cassava, sweet potatoes)
  • Hashimoto's thyroiditis

üî™ Thyroidectomy

Surgical removal of the thyroid gland. Indicated for large goiters causing compression, neoplasms, or hyperthyroidism resistant to meds.

Pre-operative Preparation

  • Restore Euthyroid State: PTU administered until signs of hyperthyroidism disappear. Iodine (Lugol's) to reduce blood loss. Beta-blockers for heart rate.
  • Teaching: Coughing/deep breathing, supporting neck manually when turning to minimize stress on sutures.
  • High carb/protein diet.

Post-operative Management

  1. Airway Safety: Set Oxygen, suction, and tracheostomy tray at bedside (for potential airway obstruction).
  2. Position: Semi-Fowler‚Äôs, neck neutral.
  3. Monitor: Bleeding (check posterior dressing), tracheal compression (irregular breathing, swelling).
  4. Check for Tetany: Signs of hypocalcemia (tingling toes/fingers/mouth, twitching) due to accidental parathyroid removal.
  5. Assess Voice: For laryngeal nerve damage (hoarseness).
‚ö†Ô∏è

Complications

  • Thyroid Storm: Acute worsening of hyperthyroid symptoms.
  • Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve Damage: Vocal cord paralysis/hoarseness. Bilateral damage can cause airway obstruction.
  • Tetany: From hypocalcemia (parathyroid damage). Laryngeal stridor may occur.
  • Hemorrhage/Hematoma: Causing difficulty breathing.
