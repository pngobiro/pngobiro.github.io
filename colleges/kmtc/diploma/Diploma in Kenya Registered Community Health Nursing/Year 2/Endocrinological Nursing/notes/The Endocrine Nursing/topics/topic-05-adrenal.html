Adrenal Cortex

  • Zona Glomerulosa: Mineralocorticoids
  • Zona Fasiculata: Glucocorticoids
  • Zona Reticularis: Androgens

Adrenal glands (Suprarenal glands)

Composed of two parts - cortex and medulla.

Adrenal cortex

Produces 3 groups of steroid hormones collectively called adrenocorticocoids (corticosteroids). The groups are:

  1. glucocorticoids
  2. mineralocorticoids
  3. sex hormones (androgens).

1. Glucocorticoids ('steroids')

  • Cortisol (hydrocortisone), corticosterone and cortisone.
  • Regulates metabolism of carbohydrate and protein
  • stimulated by ACTH from the anterior pituitary and by stress.

Anti-inflammatory & Antiallergic Effects

  • Glucocorticoids inhibit the inflammatory response to tissue injury.
  • The glucocorticoids also suppress manifestations of allergic disease that are due to the release of histamine from tissues.
  • An additional action that combats local inflammation is inhibition of phospholipase
üí°

Physiological Effects

Large doses of exogenous glucocorticoids inhibit ACTH secretion to the point that severe adrenal insufficiency can be a dangerous problem when therapy is stopped.

This reduces the release of arachidonic acid from tissue phospholipids and consequently reduces the formation of leukotrienes, thromboxanes, prostaglandins, and prostacyclin.

2. Mineralocorticoids

  • Aldosterone - main mineralocorticoid.
  • Involved in maintaining water and electrolyte balance.
  • It stimulates the reabsorption of sodium (Na+), retention of water and excretion of potassium (K+) in the urine.
  • Regulates blood volume and blood pressure.
  • Angiotensin also stimulates the release of aldosterone.
‚ñ∂ Regulation of Mineralocorticoids (RAAS)

Action:

  • Aldosterone and other steroids with mineralocorticoid activity increase the reabsorption of Na‚Å∫ from the urine, sweat, saliva, and the contents of the colon.
  • Thus, mineralocorticoids cause retention of Na‚Å∫ in the ECF. This expands ECF volume.
  • In the kidneys, they act primarily on the principal cells (P cells) of the collecting ducts.

Effects of Angiotensin II & Renin:

  • The angiotensin II is formed in the body from angiotensin I, which is liberated by the action of renin on circulating angiotensinogen.
  • Renin is secreted from the juxtaglomerular cells that surround the renal afferent arterioles as they enter the glomeruli.
  • A drop in ECF volume or intra-arterial vascular volume leads to a reflex increase in renal nerve discharge and decreases renal arterial pressure. Both changes increase renin secretion.

Adrenal medulla

  • Produces the catecholamines: epinephrine, norepinephrine, and dopamine.
  • In humans, 90% of the cells are the epinephrine-secreting type and 10% are the norepinephrine- secreting type.

‚¨ÜÔ∏è Cushing's Disease/Syndrome

Hypersecretion of glucocorticoids (Cushing‚Äôs syndrome). Cortisol is the main glucocorticoid hormone secreted by the adrenal cortex.

ü§î

Disease vs. Syndrome

  • Cushing's disease is characterized by excess cortisol secretion resulting from secretion of too much adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH) by the pituitary.
  • Cushing's syndrome refers to symptoms of cortisol excess caused by other factors.

Pathophysiology

  • In Cushing‚Äôs syndrome, cortisol is hypersecreted without regard to stress or time of day.
  • When levels of cortisol are very high, effects related to excess aldosterone and androgens are also seen.

Etiology

  • hypersecretion of ACTH by the pituitary (most often pituitary adenoma).
  • hormone-secreting adrenal tumours.
  • abnormal secretion of ACTH by a non-pituitary tumour (ectopic ACTH), e.g. bronchial or pancreatic tumour.
  • Prolonged therapeutic use of systemic ACTH or glucocorticoids (e.g., prednisone) is the most common cause.

Signs and Symptoms

Physical Changes

  • Weight gain, truncal obesity with thin arms and legs
  • Buffalo hump and moon face (deposits of adipose tissue)
  • Muscle wasting and thin skin with purple striae
  • Hyperpigmentation of the skin may occur
  • Wounds heal poorly, bruises and ecchymoses

Metabolic & Systemic

  • Glucose intolerance / insulin-resistant diabetes mellitus
  • Hypertension (salt and water retention)
  • Osteoporosis (catabolic effects on bone)
  • Hypokalemia (potassium loss)
  • Mental status changes (irritability to psychosis)

Diagnostic Tests

  • Plasma and urine cortisol and plasma ACTH.
  • A 24-hour urine test for cortisol may be collected.
  • A dexamethasone suppression test may be done.
  • Serum potassium is measured.

Therapeutic Interventions

  • Surgical removal or radiation therapy (for tumors).
  • Drugs such as ketoconazole block production of adrenal steroids.
  • If caused by steroid medication: every-other-day schedule or once a day dosing in the morning may reduce side effects.
  • Diet: high-potassium, low-sodium, high-protein. Potassium supplements.

üßÇ Hypersecretion of Mineralocorticoids

Excess aldosterone affects kidney function, with consequences elsewhere:

  • excessive reabsorption of sodium chloride and water, causing increased blood volume and hypertension
  • excessive excretion of potassium, causing hypokalaemia, which leads to cardiac arrhythmias, alkalosis, syncope and muscle weakness.

Primary hyperaldosteronism is due to excessive secretion of mineralocorticoids, independent of the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system. It is usually caused by a tumour affecting only one adrenal gland.

Secondary hyperaldosteronism is caused by overstimulation of normal glands by the excessively high blood levels of renin and angiotensin that result from low renal perfusion or low blood sodium.

‚¨áÔ∏è Adrenocortical Insufficiency (Addison's Disease)

Adrenocortical insufficiency (AI) is the insufficient production of the hormones of the adrenal cortex. Primary AI is called Addison's disease. The onset of disease usually occurs when 90% or more of both adrenal cortices are dysfunctional or destroyed.

Etiology

  • Autoimmune: gland destroys itself in response to conditions such as tuberculosis, fungal infection, AIDS, or metastatic cancer.
  • Associated with other autoimmune diseases, such as Hashimoto‚Äôs thyroiditis.
  • Adrenalectomy.
  • Secondary AI: caused by dysfunction of the pituitary or hypothalamus, or abrupt discontinuation of prolonged corticosteroid drugs.

Signs and Symptoms

  • The most significant sign is hypotension (related to lack of aldosterone).
  • Hypoglycemia, weakness, fatigue, weight loss, confusion, and psychosis (low cortisol).
  • Hyperpigmentation of the skin (tanned or bronze appearance) in primary AI due to increased ACTH.
  • Anorexia, nausea, and vomiting.
  • Vitiligo (associated with autoimmune destruction).
  • Women: decreased body hair, amenorrhea. Men: impotence.

Diagnostic Tests

  • Serum and urine cortisol levels.
  • Blood glucose is low.
  • An ACTH stimulation test.
  • Serum sodium and potassium levels are monitored.
  • Imaging: CT scan (bilateral enlargement).
  • ECG: changes due to hyperkalemia.

Therapeutic Interventions

üíä

Hormone Replacement

Long-term treatment consists of replacement of glucocorticoids (hydrocortisone) and mineralocorticoids (fludrocortisone). Patients will need hormone replacement therapy for the rest of their lives.

Hormones are given in divided doses (two-thirds in morning, one-third in evening) to mimic diurnal rhythm.

During times of stress or illness, doses need to be increased to two to three times normal.

Complications: Adrenal Crisis

If a patient is exposed to stress (infection, trauma), the body may be unable to respond normally. Adrenal crisis can occur.

  • Loss of large amounts of sodium and water -> profound hypotension, dehydration, tachycardia.
  • Potassium retention -> cardiac dysrhythmias.
  • Severe hypoglycemia.
  • Treatment: Rapidly restore fluid volume (IV fluids with glucose) and cortisol levels (large doses of glucocorticoids).

Nursing Process

Risk for deficient fluid volume related to deficient adrenal cortical hormones

  • Monitor daily weights, I&O, fluid status.
  • Monitor serum glucose, potassium, sodium.
  • Monitor vital signs closely for orthostatic hypotension.

Risk for ineffective health maintenance

  • Teach importance of hormone replacement.
  • Teach to increase dose during stress.
  • Recommend medical alert identification.
  • Teach use of emergency IM hydrocortisone kit.
