üìë Overview & Classification

These fall into three main categories:

  1. abnormal secretion of thyroid hormones (T3 and T4) causing hyperthyroidism (thyrotoxicosis) or hypothyroidism (cretinism, myoxedema)
  2. goitre ‚Äì simple, malignant
  3. Tumours-adenoma, carcinoma

Abnormal thyroid function may arise not only from thyroid disease but also from disorders of the pituitary or hypothalamus; insufficient dietary iodine impairs thyroid hormone production. The main effects are caused by an abnormally high or low basal metabolic rate.

‚ö° Hyperthyroidism (Thyrotoxicosis)

  • Most common endocrine disease
  • Females > Males ‚Äì 8 : 1
  • Often unsuspected and grossly under diagnosed
  • arises as the body tissues are exposed to excessive levels of T3 and T4.
  • The main effects are due to increased basal metabolic rate. In older adults, cardiac failure is another common consequence as the ageing heart must work harder to deliver more blood and nutrients to the hyperactive body cells.
‚ÑπÔ∏è

Common Forms

The most common forms of hyperthyroidism include:

  1. diffuse toxic goiter (Graves disease),
  2. toxic multinodular goiter (Plummer disease),
  3. toxic adenoma.

Together with subacute thyroiditis, these conditions constitute 85-90% of all causes of thyrotoxicosis

Pathophysiology

  • The hypermetabolic effect of thyrotoxicosis affects every organ system.
  • Thyroid hormone is made in the thyroid but travels via the circulation to reach every cell in the body.
  • Thyroid hormone is necessary for normal growth and development, and it regulates cellular metabolism.
  • Excess thyroid hormone causes an increase in the metabolic rate that is associated with increased total body heat production and cardiovascular activity (increased heart contractility, heart rate, vasodilation).
‚ñ∂ Signs and Symptoms
  • The major symptoms of thyrotoxicosis include palpitation, nervousness, sweating, hyperdefecation, and heat intolerance.
  • Women often note a reduction in menstrual flow or oligomenorrhea.
  • Common signs of thyrotoxicosis include weight loss despite increased appetite, lid lag and stare, sinus tachycardia, atrial fibrillation or high output failure (in elderly individuals), fine tremor, and muscle weakness.
  • Thyrotoxicosis leads to an apparent increase in sympathetic nervous system symptoms, including nervousness, stare, tremor, and tachycardia

Diagnosis

  • Pulsation of the thyroid gland due to increased blood flow to the gland.
  • Enlarged thyroid gland
  • Increase serum T4

Management

Pharmacotherapy

  • radioactive iodine
  • antithyroid medications e.g. methimazole
  • aqueous iodine solution
  • beta blockers

Surgery

Not the treatment of choice; it's indicated in pregnant women, those allergic to drugs or are unable to take and those with large goitres.

Nutritional & Supportive Care

Promote nutritious status through:

  • Give high calorie, high protein, frequent meals
  • Encourage high fluid diet and electrolytes to replace that lost through diarrhoea and diaphoresis.
  • Monitor weight and food intake.
  • Manage diarrhoea- avoid carbonated drinks and alcohol
  • Provide privacy during meals

General Care:

  • Increase self esteem by showing understanding and eye care and protection
  • Maintain normal body temperature by maintaining cool environment, give cool baths and cold drinks.
  • Monitor to detect complication- vital signs

üëÅÔ∏è Specific Hyperthyroid Conditions

Graves' disease (Diffuse toxic goiter)

  • this condition accounts for 75% of cases of hyperthyroidism.
  • It affects more women than men and may occur at any age, being most common between the ages of 30 and 50 years.
  • It is an autoimmune disorder in which an antibody that mimics the effects of TSH is produced, causing:
    • increased release of T3 and T4 and signs of hyperthyroidism
    • goitre (visible enlargement of the gland, the antibody stimulates thyroid growth
    • exophthalmos in many cases.
‚ö†Ô∏è

Exophthalmos

This is protrusion of the eyeballs that gives the appearance of staring, which is due to the deposition of excess fat and fibrous tissue behind the eyes. It is often present in Graves‚Äô disease. Effective treatment of hyperthyroidism does not completely reverse exophthalmos, although it may lessen after 2‚Äì3 years. In severe cases the eyelids become retracted and may not completely cover the eyes during blinking and sleep, leading to drying of the conjunctiva and predisposing to infection. It does not occur in other forms of hyperthyroidism.

Toxic nodular goitre (Plummer disease)

  • In this condition one or two nodules of a gland that is already affected by goitre become active and secrete excess T3 and T4 causing the effects of hyperthyroidism.
  • It is more common in women than men and after middle age.
  • As this condition affects an older age group than Graves‚Äô disease, arrhythmias and cardiac failure are more common.
  • Exophthalmos does not occur in this condition.

üî™ Thyroid Surgery Management

PREOPERATIVE MANAGEMENT

  • Give high calorie, high protein, high carbohydrate diet
  • Give vitamin supplements especially vit. C and B12
  • Avoid stimulants e.g. caffeinated and carbonated drinks
  • Teach the patient on importance of the surgery, and how to support the neck postoperatively.
  • Ensure good rest before surgery

POSTOPERATIVE MANAGEMENT

  • Check dressings and reinforce them as necessary
  • Check for bleeding as the patient lies supine and monitor for any internal bleeding by pulse and B.P
  • Be alert for sensations of pressure and fullness at the incision site.
  • In case of difficulty in breathing insert an air way.
  • Assess the intensity of pain and administer analgesics.
  • Administer oxygen as necessary.
  • When turning the patient support the head and avoid tension
  • Position the patient in semi Fowler‚Äôs position.
  • Administer i.v. fluids but as soon as nausea subsides, give cold fluids and ice.
  • Instruct the patient to talk as little as possible so as to prevent oedema of the vocal cords. When the patient talks, note any voice changes -damage to the recurrent laryngeal nerve
  • Provide easy access to items
  • Ambulate the patient as soon as possible.
  • Continue giving high calorie diet
  • Sutures or the skin clips are removed on the 2nd post

üìâ Hypothyroidism

This condition is prevalent in older adults and is five times more common in females than males. Deficiency of T3 and T4 in adults results in an abnormally low metabolic rate and other effects. There may be accumulation of mucopolysaccharides in the subcutaneous tissues causing swelling (non-pitting oedema), especially of the face, hands, feet and eyelids (myxoedema).

The commonest causes are autoimmune thyroiditis, interventions, e.g. antithyroid drugs, surgical removal of thyroid tissue or ionising radiation.

Types of Hypothyroidism

  • Primary/ thyroidal hypothyroidism- Caused by thyroidal problems
  • Central hypothyroidism- caused by failure of the pituitary, hypothalamus or both
  • Secondary/ pituitary hypothyroidism- caused by pituitary problems
  • Tertiary/ hypothalamic hypothyroidism- caused by problems with hypothalamus
  • Cretinism- Thyroid deficiency at birth: foetal or neonatal

Chronic Thyroiditis (Hashimoto's Disease)

  • Chronic thyroiditis, which occurs most frequently in women between 30 and 50 years old, has been termed Hashimoto‚Äôs disease, or chronic lymphocytic thyroiditis;
  • its diagnosis is based on the histologic appearance of the inflamed gland.
  • In contrast to acute thyroiditis, the chronic forms are usually not accompanied by pain, pressure symptoms, or fever, and thyroid activity is usually normal or low rather than increased.
  • Cell-mediated immunity may play a significant role in the pathogenesis of chronic thyroiditis, and there may be a genetic predisposition to it.
  • If untreated, the disease runs a slow, progressive course, leading eventually to hypothyroidism.
  • The objective of treatment is to reduce the size of the thyroid gland and prevent hypothyroidism.
  • Thyroid hormone therapy is prescribed to reduce thyroid activity and the production of thyroglobulin.
  • If hypothyroid symptoms are present, thyroid hormone therapy is prescribed. Surgery may be required if pressure symptoms persist

ü¶í Simple Goitre

  • This is enlargement of the thyroid gland without signs of hyperthyroidism. It is caused by a relative lack of T3 and T4 and the low levels stimulate secretion of TSH resulting in hyperplasia of the thyroid gland
  • Sometimes the extra thyroid tissue is able to maintain normal hormone levels but if not, hypothyroidism develops.

Causes are:

  • persistent iodine deficiency. In parts of the world where there is dietary iodine deficiency, this is a common condition known as endemic goitre
  • genetic abnormality affecting synthesis of T3 and T4
  • iatrogenic, e.g. antithyroid drugs, surgical removal of excess thyroid tissue.

The enlarged gland may cause pressure damage to adjacent tissues, especially if it lies in an abnormally low position, i.e. behind the sternum. The structures most commonly affected are the oesophagus, causing dysphagia; the trachea, causing dyspnoea; and the recurrent laryngeal nerve, causing hoarseness
