<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="anatomy-physiology-of-the-endocrine-system">Anatomy &amp; Physiology of The Endocrine System<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#anatomy-physiology-of-the-endocrine-system" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>The endocrine system consists of glands widely separated with no anatomical links. The endocrine glands are termed as ductless and they secrete chemicals known as hormones.</p>
<p>Hormones are chemical messengers produced by ductless/endocrine glands, which flow directly into the blood where they are carried to another organ to influence activity, growth or nutrition. The hormones, together with the autonomic nervous system, regulate the internal environment.</p>
<p>The endocrine glands are:</p>
<ul>
<li>1 Pituitary gland</li>
<li>1 Thyroid gland</li>
<li>4 Parathyroid glands</li>
<li>2 Adrenal (suprarenal) glands</li>
<li>The pancreatic islets (islets of langerhans)</li>
<li>1 Pineal gland or body</li>
<li>1 Thymus gland</li>
<li>2 Ovaries in the female</li>
<li>2 Testes in the male</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="1-the-pituitary-gland-and-the-hypothalamus">1. The Pituitary Gland and the Hypothalamus<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#1-the-pituitary-gland-and-the-hypothalamus" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>The pituitary gland lies within the sellaturcica, a depression within the sphenoid bone, inferior to the hypothalamus.</p>
<p>It is composed of 3 lobes, the posterior, anterior and middle lobes.</p>
<p>The pituitary gland and the hypothalamus act as a unit. The hypothalamus has a direct controlling effect on the pituitary gland and an indirect effect on many others.</p>
<p>The pituitary gland has 3 parts:</p>
<ul>
<li>Anterior lobe (adenohypophysis)</li>
<li>Posterior lobe (neurohypophysis)</li>
<li>Intermediate lobe</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="a-the-posterior-lobe">a. The Posterior Lobe<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#a-the-posterior-lobe" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The posterior lobe of the pituitary secretes 2 hormones:</p>
<ul>
<li>Antidiuretic hormone (ADH)</li>
<li>Oxytocin</li>
</ul>
<p>The antidiuretic hormone: increases permeability to water of the distal renal tubules. It also stimulates the contraction of the smooth muscles in blood vessels hence raising blood pressure.</p>
<p>Oxytocin: causes contraction of the uterus and cells in the lactating breast.</p>
<p><strong>NB</strong>: These two hormones are actually produced by the hypothalamus but stored in the posterior pituitary gland</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="b-the-anterior-lobe">b. The Anterior Lobe<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#b-the-anterior-lobe" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The Anterior Lobe produces several hormones.</p>
<ul>
<li>The growth hormone (GH)</li>
<li>Thyroid stimulating hormone (TSH)</li>
<li>Adrenocorticotropic hormone</li>
<li>Follicle stimulating hormones</li>
<li>Luteinising hormones</li>
<li>Prolactin</li>
</ul>
<p>The growth hormone: promotes fat metabolism while promoting fat mobilisation and catabolism.</p>
<p>The thyroid stimulating hormone: stimulates the synthesis and secretion of the thyroid hormones.</p>
<p>The adrenocorticotropic hormone stimulates the growth of the adrenal cortex and secretion of glucocorticoids, for example, cortisol.</p>
<p>Follicle stimulating hormone: cause the growth of the Graafian follicle and spermatogenesis. They also facilitate the secretion of oestrogen.</p>
<p>Luteinising hormone: are required for ovulation. They also stimulate the corpus luteum to secrete progesterone.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="2-thyroid-gland">2. Thyroid Gland<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#2-thyroid-gland" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The thyroid gland is situated in the neck in front of the larynx and trachea. It consists of 2 lobes one on either side of the thyroid cartilage.</p>
<p>It's regulated by thyroid stimulating hormone from the anterior lobe.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="functions">Functions<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#functions" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The thyroid gland produces:</p>
<ul>
<li>Tetra-iodothyronine (T4, thyroxine)</li>
<li>Tri-iodothyronine T3</li>
<li>Calcitonin hormones</li>
</ul>
<p>Thyroxine and Tri-iodothyronine hormones: may enhance the affects of other hormones (adrenaline and noradrenaline), are essentials for physical growth and mental development and helps in regulating the metabolic rate of all body cells.</p>
<p>Calcitonin: controls calcium content of the blood by increasing bone formation and inhibiting bone breakdown and this results in decreased blood calcium levels.</p>

<p>The adrenal glands are located on the upper side of each kidney.</p>
<p>Each adrenal gland has:</p>
<ul>
<li>Adrenal cortex</li>
<li>Adrenal medulla</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Adrenal Medulla</strong>: produces the 2 catecholamines:</p>
<p>i. Epinephrine
ii. Norepinephrine</p>
<p>These perform the functions of stimulating the breakdown of glycogen by skeletal muscle, increasing blood concentration of free fatty acids, constricting most blood vessels in the body and constriction of bronchial smooth muscle.</p>
<p><strong>Adrenal Cortex</strong>: produces</p>
<p>i. Glucocorticoids
ii. mineralocorticoids
iii. sex hormones</p>

<li>Raising blood glucose level through gluconeogenesis</li>
<li>Promoting water and sodium re-absorption from the renal tubules</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="5-the-pancreas">5. The Pancreas<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#5-the-pancreas" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>The Pancreas is an elongated gland with the head lying in the C shaped beginning of the small intestine (duodenum), with its body extending horizontally behind the stomach and its tail touching the spleen.</p>
<p>This gland is located behind the stomach in the abdominal cavity.</p>
<p>It has both exocrine and endocrine properties.</p>
<p>The Islets of Langerhans in the pancreas produce:</p>
<ul>
<li>Insulin</li>
<li>Glucagon</li>
<li>Somatostatin</li>
</ul>
<p>Insulin: produced by beta cells (B cells) promotes cellular uptake of glucose, stimulates intracellular synthesis of glycogen, fat and proteins, and stimulates uptake of sodium and potassium.</p>
<p>Glucagon: produced by delta cells (D cells), on the other hand, antagonises insulin in the liver (breakdown of glycogen), inhibits protein synthesis and induces hepatic fatty acid breakdown.</p>
<p>Somatostatin: inhibits the secretion of the other two hormones.</p>

<p>Hypothyroidism is treated with an objective of restoring a normal metabolic rate. For this purpose, administration of the thyroid hormone (Levothyroxine, liotrix or thyroid extract) is one of the main treatment measures. At the time of administration, you must be on the lookout and observe for diuresis, exaggerated reflexes and high pulse rate. In severe hypothyroidism, you should endeavour to maintain vital functions through monitoring blood gases, assisted ventilation, monitoring fluid intake, replacement of the thyroid hormone and treating the precipitating factors.</p>
<p>Hospitalising the patient if they are in thyroid storm/crisis, administering sedatives and giving vitamin supplements can be used in the management of hyperthyroidism. Other drugs administered are digoxin for heart failure and propranolol (inderal) for arrhythmias and hypertension. Anti-thyroid drugs, including methimazole (Topazol) and Propylthiouracil, are also given.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="parathyroid-disorders">Parathyroid Disorders<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#parathyroid-disorders" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="hyperparathyroidism">Hyperparathyroidism<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#hyperparathyroidism" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>This involves secretion of increased levels of parathyroid hormone. We have learnt that this hormone is involved in calcium metabolism. The objective of management is to relieve symptoms and prevent complications. The choice of therapy depends on the urgency of the problem, the degree of hypercalcemia, the underlying disorder, the status of renal and hepatic function, the patient's clinical presentation and the merits of each mode of treatment.</p>
<p>In severe cases, surgery is performed. In less acute cases, conservative management involving close monitoring, high fluid, and moderate calcium, sodium, and phosphorous supplementation are choice actions. Diuretics may also be administered. Diuretic medication will act on the kidney to cause fluid loss and hence lower the possibility of kidney stone formation. One of the drugs used as a diuretic is Frusemide (e.g. lasix). Some drugs, for example, mithramycin lower serum calcium. Oestrogen therapy for post-menopausal women can also be helpful. Strict monitoring of intake and output and levels of calcium, potassium and phosphate are necessary.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="hypoparathyroidism">Hypoparathyroidism<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#hypoparathyroidism" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>In hypoparathyroidism, the main objectives of treatment are to treat tetany when present and prevent long-term complications of low calcium levels in plasma. IV calcium gluconate and vitamin D are used in this case.</p>
<p>Digitalisation and the use of side rails are important nursing alerts to prevent cardiac arrest and falls during tetany. Anti-seizure precautions should be instituted.</p>

<p>You learnt that insulin is a hormone involved in glucose metabolism. The other hormones involved in glucose metabolism are glucagon, adrenaline, growth hormone and somatotrophin. Try to remember them and their functions.</p>
<p>Diabetes mellitus can be divided into:</p>
<ul>
<li>Type I or Insulin Dependent Diabetes Mellitus (IDDM).</li>
<li>Type II or Non-insulin Dependent Diabetes Mellitus (NIDDM).</li>
<li>Type III, due to excessive corticosteroids, for example in Cushing's syndrome or over enthusiastic treatment of addison's disease with corticosteroids</li>
<li>Type IV, also called gestational diabetes. It's due to the presence of a hormone called human placental lactogen, which antagonises the effects of insulin.</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="management-of-diabetes-mellitus">Management of Diabetes Mellitus<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#management-of-diabetes-mellitus" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>In the management of diabetes mellitus, the goals are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Maintenance of a near-normal blood glucose level as possible</li>
<li>Achievement of optimal serum lipids</li>
<li>Provision of adequate calories for attaining reasonable weight</li>
<li>Prevention and treatment of complications</li>
<li>Avoidance of hypoglycaemia</li>
</ul>
<p>The three modalities utilised in the management are drugs, including insulin and oral hypoglycaemic drugs such as diabenese, diet and exercise. These three have to be well balanced to avoid complications. Patient education is also important to decrease complications. Patients require education, which cover medication, injection sites, use of foods, blood glucose monitoring, exercise and activity home management and lifestyle improvement.</p>
<p>Both short-term and long-term complications may arise. Short-term complications include hyperglycemia, hypoglycaemia, ulcers, and coma, while long-term effects are nephropathies (kidney involvement), neuropathies (involvement of the nervous system) and visual problems. Administering oxygen, establishing IV access, determining blood sugar levels and monitoring vital signs manage Ketoacidosis. IV glucose and glucagon can also be administered as necessary.</p>
<p>Hyperosmolar nonketosis is treated with IV fluids and insulin. Electrolyte and vital signs are then closely monitored. Micro-vascular complications are best treated by preventive measures. Care of the eyes, care of the feet and avoiding injurious substances to the kidney will improve the patient's life.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="type-i-diabetes">Type I Diabetes<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#type-i-diabetes" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>This type of diabetes has no clearly known cause. It is thought that some individuals have antibodies against cells producing insulin. It is also thought that hypersensitivity reactions may destroy the part of the pancreas involved in secretion of the hormones (the Islets of Langerhans). In this disorder, patients do not produce their own insulin. Hyperglycemia, ketoacidosis, polyuria and polydipsia, and loss of weight are characteristic manifestations.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="type-ii-diabetes">Type II Diabetes<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#type-ii-diabetes" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Individuals with Type II diabetes have insulin, which may be low, normal or high, but they may have insulin resistance. In Type II diabetes, there is glycosuria (sugar in urine) and hyperglycaemia. It can complicate with hyperosmolar non-ketosis with or without coma, susceptibility to infection, vascular, renal and ophthalmic disorders. Some of the patients with Type II diabetes may be obese. Type II diabetic patients can generally be well managed throughout their lives</p>
