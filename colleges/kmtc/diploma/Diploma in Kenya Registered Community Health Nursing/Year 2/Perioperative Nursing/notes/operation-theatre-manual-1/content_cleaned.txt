<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="chapter-1-operating-theatre-design-and-administration">CHAPTER 1: OPERATING THEATRE DESIGN AND ADMINISTRATION<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#chapter-1-operating-theatre-design-and-administration" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>This section will briefly deal with the design, layout, work flow and administrative responsibilities of the Operating Theatre (OT) Manager. The OT team works closely together with the surgeons, nurses and OT technicians. Therefore the work and relaxation areas for staff are well demarcated yet accessible from the outside if required.</p>
<p>There is a separate clean area for the entry of surgical instruments and other equipment, and there is a demarcated area for used or soiled surgical instruments to be rinsed before sending to the Central Sterile Services Department (CSSD). The layout accommodates the arrival of patients and the recovery area post operatively.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="1-1-layout-and-design">1.1 Layout and Design<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#1-1-layout-and-design" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Theatre managers, infection control team, surgeons and anaesthesiologists are involved in the planning of the theatre design/layout. The operating theatre suite is a purposely built independent complex located away from the main flow of traffic but it is in an area easily accessible to the critical care, surgical and maternity wards and the supporting service departments, e.g. CSSD, laboratory and X-ray departments.</p>
<p>The operating theatre should has an independent air handling unit with controlled ventilation such that the lay-up room and the OT table is under positive pressure and has the most Air Changes per Hour (ACH) i.e. 20-24.</p>
<p>The traffic within the operating suite is controlled. It only allows access to staff, patients and equipment from different entrances and exits. There is no thoroughfare through the OT.</p>

<p>Floors are smooth, without cracks and breaks, made of materials that reduce static and does not endanger the safety of personnel. <p>Sterility and maintenance of a high standard of aseptic techniques are of primary importance. Hygiene of the surroundings is maintained at all times.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="personnel">Personnel<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#personnel" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>A high standard of personal hygiene is expected and each staff member maintains a high standard of sterile technique. The nurse in-charge makes sure that nursing staff do not always work under pressure and stress. The organization of the operating section is such, that precautionary measures are possible for all patients, even if an extremely heavy burden is placed on the staff. Suturing material, syringes and instruments are kept sterile. The autoclave is kept in working condition and serviced regularly (refer to the CSSD Guidelines). The cleaning of the theatre after a septic case and the day's operating schedule is essentially done.</p>

<p>Members of OT staff, and or patients might sustain injuries during an operating session. These are to be considered as injury on duty for the staff and as medical negligence for the patient following proper investigations. All records are kept of the incident. Some examples are shown below.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="1-4-8-1-burns">1.4.8.1. Burns<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#1-4-8-1-burns" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Theatre lights without heat shields</li>
<li>Warm instruments, for example wound hook</li>
<li>Rinsing water used in the peritoneal cavity</li>
<li>Faulty diathermy machine and plate</li>
<li>Neglect to use the foam rubber mattress</li>
<li>Incorrect use of electric blanket or warm water bottle</li>

<li>Anaesthesia care is carefully planned and documented in the anaesthesia record</li>
<li>The plan considers information from other patient assessments and identifies the anaesthesia to be used, the method of administration, other medications and fluids, monitoring procedures, and the anticipated post-anaesthesia care</li>
<li>The anaesthesia planning process includes educating the patient and his or her family or decision-maker regarding the risks, potential complications, and options related to the planned anaesthesia and postoperative analgesia. This discussion occurs as part of the process of obtaining consent for anaesthesia. The anaesthesiologist or the qualified individual who will administer the anaesthesia provides this education</li>
<li>Since anaesthesia carries a high level of risk, its administration should be carefully planned. A pre-anaesthetic assessment should be conducted and recorded</li>
</ul>

<li>Interpret and act on any abnormal findings (e.g. blood pressure, haemoglobin, blood glucose, etc.) of the patient</li>

<li>Patients with chronic illnesses should be asked if they took their medications e.g. high blood pressure and diabetes treatment and how many hours prior to operation</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="2-3-2-1-skin-preparation-of-the-patient">2.3.2.1 Skin preparation of the patient<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#2-3-2-1-skin-preparation-of-the-patient" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>The purpose of skin preparation is to render the operative site as free as possible from transient and resident micro-organisms, dirt, and skin oil so that the incision can be made through the skin with minimal danger of infection from this source.</p>
<p>Skin preparation: Ordinary bathing with soap and water prior to the operation removes excessive dirt and grim and make the patient feel better and more presentable for the operation.</p>
<p>In some situations, the surgeons will ask that the patient be bathed in 2% chlorhexidine, especially for implant surgery. This is provided by the healthcare facility and used the night before the operation.</p>
<p>Patients whose operations will be performed on the face, eye, ear, or neck are advised to shampoo their hair prior to hospital admission as this may not be permitted for a few weeks post-operatively.</p>
<p>All patients shower or take bath after hospital admission as close to the time of departure to the OT as possible. The operative site and surrounding area is thoroughly cleansed with a rapid-acting antiseptic agent such as povidone iodine, if requested by the surgeon. Usually skin preparation takes place in the Operating Theatre.</p>
<p>&gt; NOTE: History of allergies must be obtained before applying any chemical agent to a patient’s skin.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="2-4-2-monitoring-and-recording-of-the-patient-s-post-anaesthetic-status">2.4.2 Monitoring and Recording of the Patient's Post-anaesthetic Status<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#2-4-2-monitoring-and-recording-of-the-patient-s-post-anaesthetic-status" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Each patient's post-anaesthetic status is monitored, and the patient is discharged from the recovery area in accordance with accepted guidelines. Physiological monitoring provides reliable information about the patient's status during the administration of anaesthesia and the recovery period.</p>
<p>Monitoring methods depend on the patient's pre-anaesthetic status, anaesthetic choice, and the complexity of the surgical or other procedure performed during anaesthesia. In all cases, however, the monitoring process is continuous, and the results are entered into the patient's record. Monitoring during anaesthesia provides the basis for monitoring during the post-anaesthetic recovery period. The ongoing, systematic collection and analysis of data on the patient's status in recovery may support decisions about moving the patient to other settings and less intensive services.</p>
<p>Only a suitably qualified and experienced registered nurse or designated member of the medical staff carries out monitoring in the recovery area. Recording of monitoring data provides the documentation to support discharge decisions. The anaesthesiologist decides whether the patient can be discharged from the recovery area to another level of care or from the organisation (as in the case of ambulatory anaesthesia).</p>
<p>Standardized criteria developed by medical personnel are used to make discharge decisions.</p>

<li>Monitoring findings are entered in the patient's record</li>
<li>Established criteria are used to make decisions regarding the patient's discharge from the recovery room</li>
<li>The decision to discharge the patient is recorded</li>
<li>Recovery area arrival and discharge times are recorded</li>
<li>The signatures of those handing over and those receiving the patient are recorded</li>

It is also to ensure that clean clothes are worn when operating on the patient to prevent harmful bacteria.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="definition-and-categories">Definition and Categories<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#definition-and-categories" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Upon arrival in the operating theatre suite, ALL personnel working in the OT changes out of their street clothes and into theatre attire. Those that work in the OT but are not part of the scrub team do not need to wear anything more than head cover. Those working in the OT and are part of the scrub team including working in the layup area will wear:</p>

<li>Head cover</li>
<li>Plastic apron</li>
<li>Surgical mask</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="3-3-general-considerations">3.3 General Considerations<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#3-3-general-considerations" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ol>
<li>Each OT should has a specific complete written policy or standard operating procedures on proper attire that is known to all persons</li>
<li>Only approved, clean OT attire is worn within the restricted area of the OT suite. Street clothes are never to be worn within the restricted area. This regulation applies to anyone entering the restricted area</li>
<li>Ideally, OT attire is not worn outside the OT suite. However, if they are, then an over-gown and CHANGE OF FOOTWEAR is recommended. Overshoes are not recommended.</li>
<li>Dressing rooms located adjacent to the OT suite are reached through the outer corridor.</li>
<li>Comfortable supportive shoes are worn to relieve fatigue</li>
<li>Proper personal hygiene is re-emphasized</li>
<li>No person with an acute infection, such as a cold or sore throat, or skin lesion, such as a furuncle or any contagious condition, is permitted within the OT</li>
<li>Persons with cuts, burns, or skin abrasions do not scrub or handle sterile equipment as this may increase the risk of infection to the patient</li>
</ol>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="3-4-2-surgical-mask">3.4.2. Surgical Mask<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#3-4-2-surgical-mask" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>A surgical mask is recommended for the scrub team working inside the OT suite. It is worn to contain and filter out droplets containing micro-organisms expelled from the oral and nasopharynx. During talking, sneezing, and coughing organism laden droplet nuclei are dispersed, but this breaks the barrier because of the moisture in the breath and it is reported that a surgical mask is effective for a short period of time. Therefore the surgical mask must be changed between each patient. The mask is designed for close fit but improper application can negate their efficiency.</p>
<p>Always tie the strings tightly, to prevent the string coming loose during an operation and contaminating the sterile gown. The upper strings are tied at the back of the head, and the lower strings behind the neck.</p>
<p>The surgical masks have an exterior pliable strip or noseband that can be bent to contour the mask over the bridge of the nose (refer to the IPC Guideline Chapter 4).</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="3-4-3-head-cover">3.4.3 Head Cover<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#3-4-3-head-cover" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>All facial and head hair must be covered in the restricted area. Practically all head covers are disposable and made of lint-free nonporous, soft, cloth-like fabric. If hair is long, a helmet or hood must be worn to cover the neck area. Headgear is worn well to prevent any escape of hair and to confine micro-organisms. A cap or hood is put on before a scrub suit or dress to protect the garment from contamination by hair.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="3-4-4-eye-protection-goggles-and-face-shields">3.4.4 Eye Protection: Goggles and Face Shields<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#3-4-4-eye-protection-goggles-and-face-shields" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Eye protection is worn as part of personal protective equipment to act as a barrier to infectious material entering the eye during all invasive surgical procedures, including endoscopic procedures, or in any situation where splash injury to the eyes could occur.</p>
<p>Eye protection is comfortable to allow for sufficient peripheral vision, and is adjustable to ensure a secure fit. For none disposable eyewear, it is recommended that each individual be provided his/her own eyewear that is only</p>
<p>Page 31</p>
<p>worn by that person in order to ensure the appropriate fit is maintained and minimize the potential of exposure to the wearer.</p>
<p>Eye protection is removed in an aseptic manner to minimize splashes of blood and body fluids to the mucous membrane, in particular the conjunctiva of the eye. Eye protection should be removed and not carried outside of the surgery department. After the use of the goggles and face shields cleaning should be done according to the manufactures recommendations.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="3-4-5-appropriate-operation-room-shoes-and-shoe-covers">3.4.5 Appropriate Operation Room Shoes and Shoe Covers<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#3-4-5-appropriate-operation-room-shoes-and-shoe-covers" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Appropriate operation room footwear i.e. slippers are to be worn at all times in the restricted area. Overshoes are not recommended.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="3-4-6-gown">3.4.6 Gown<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#3-4-6-gown" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Sterile cotton gowns are worn over scrub attire to permit the wearer to create and to come within the sterile field, in order to carry out a sterile technique during an operative procedure. Since these are made of cotton, a plastic apron should be worn underneath it. Although the entire gown is sterilized, the back is not considered sterile nor is any area below waist level, once the gown is donned. Wrap around gowns that provide sterile coverage to the back by generous overlap are recommended. The cuffs of gowns are rib-knit to tightly fit the wrist.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="3-4-7-gloves">3.4.7 Gloves<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#3-4-7-gloves" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Sterile gloves complete the attire for scrubbed team members. They are worn to permit the wearer to handle sterile supplies or tissues of the operative wound.</p>
<p>Glove packages are generally the peel-apart type. Prior to opening the package, the circulating nurse inspects for the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>Expiry date of the gloves. Expired gloves are never used.</li>
<li>Damages or wetness which would indicate contamination.</li>
</ul>
<p>When the inner paper is unfolded, the wearer puts the right glove to the right, the left glove to the left, palm side up. If a sterile glove is punctured or torn it must be changed immediately to prevent escape of micro-organisms from the skin beneath.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="3-5-principles-of-aseptic-technique">3.5 Principles of Aseptic Technique<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#3-5-principles-of-aseptic-technique" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>We observe sterile techniques in order to prevent the risk of infection.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="the-following-is-recommended">The following is recommended:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-following-is-recommended" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<ol>
<li>Only sterile items are used within a sterile field. If you are in doubt about the sterility of anything, consider it not sterile.</li>
</ol>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="3-6-the-need-for-sterile-technique">3.6. The Need for Sterile Technique<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#3-6-the-need-for-sterile-technique" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Strict aseptic and sterile techniques are needed at all times in the OT, because freshly incised or traumatized tissue can easily become infected. Therefore, anything unsterile in contact with the patient is potentially dangerous and can transfer microbes into the open wound. All operative procedures are performed under sterile conditions.</p>
<p>Conversely, terminal decontamination and sterilization of all material and equipment used during an operation is performed with the assumption that every patient is a potential source of infection for other persons.</p>
<p>It is essential that all members of the operating team know the common sources of contamination by micro-organisms in the OT, and the means by which they reach the sterile field and operative wound. Maintaining the sterile technique is the responsibility of everyone caring for the patient in the OT.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="definition">Definition<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#definition" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>The surgical scrub is the process of removing as many micro-organisms as possible from the hands and arms by <strong>mechanical washing and chemical antiseptics</strong> before participating in an operative procedure.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="mechanical-and-chemical-antiseptics-washing">Mechanical and Chemical Antiseptics Washing<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#mechanical-and-chemical-antiseptics-washing" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h4 level="4" id="purpose">Purpose<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#purpose" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h4></div>
<p>The purpose of the surgical scrub is to remove soil, debris, natural skin oils, hand lotions, and micro-organisms from the hands and forearms of sterile team members. The surgical scrub is done just prior to gowning and glowing for each operation.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h4 level="4" id="hand-washing-facilities">Hand washing facilities<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#hand-washing-facilities" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h4></div>
<p>Adequate scrubbing and hand washing facilities is provided for all operating team members. Preferably, taps are hand sensor or foot operated.</p>
<p>The sink is designed deep and wide enough to prevent splashing. A sterile gown cannot be put on over damp scrub attire without resulting in contamination of the gown by moisture oozing through. Scrub sinks are used only for scrubbing or hand washing. They are not to be used to clean or rinse contaminated instruments or equipment.</p>
<p>For safety and convenience, the scrub room is adjacent to the OT.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="3-7-1-preparation-for-surgical-scrub">3.7.1 Preparation for Surgical Scrub<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#3-7-1-preparation-for-surgical-scrub" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h4 level="4" id="general-preparation">General preparation<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#general-preparation" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h4></div>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h4 level="4" id="preparations-prior-to-scrub">Preparations prior to scrub<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#preparations-prior-to-scrub" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h4></div>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="3-7-2-surgical-scrub-procedure">3.7.2. Surgical Scrub Procedure<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#3-7-2-surgical-scrub-procedure" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<li>The surgical scrub takes three minutes. We use the clock in the OT or in the scrub room to check/ control the scrub.</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="drying-of-hands-and-arms">Drying of hands and arms<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#drying-of-hands-and-arms" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>After scrubbing, hands and arms are thoroughly dried before the sterile gown is donned to prevent contamination of the gown by strike through of organisms from skin and scrub attire. Two huckaback or disposable paper towels are placed on top of the gown during packaging for drying of hands.</p>
<ul>
<li>The circulating floor nurse opens the outer layer of the gown pack. Stand way from the trolley. Water does not drip on the trolley</li>
<li>The scrub-nurse takes one huckaback or disposable paper towel in the (R) hand and start drying the fingers, hand and arm up to the elbow of the (L) side. Keep away from the body. We do not go back touching or drying the fingers</li>
<li>Surgeon/s follows same procedures as the scrubbing nurse/s</li>
<li>Now take the second huckaback or disposable paper towel in the (L) hand and dry the (R) side in the same manner as the left side</li>
</ul>
<p>&gt; NOTE: During and after scrubbing, keep the hands higher than the elbows to allow water to flow from the cleanest area, the hands, to the marginal area of the upper arms.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="purpose">Purpose<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#purpose" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>

<ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="3-9-gloving-technique">3.9. <li>Repeat step 5 for the right cuff, using the left hand, and thereby completely gloving the right hand</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="4-1-surgical-instruments-and-packs">4.1 Surgical Instruments and Packs<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#4-1-surgical-instruments-and-packs" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Surgical instruments come in different variations in structure and design to meet a particular purpose and have specific requirements.</li>
<li>Surgical instruments are valuable tools and need to be respected and handled with care.</li>
<li>Surgical instruments should be purchased from reputable manufactures that have high standards concerning pattern, material and workmanship.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="4-1-1-management-of-surgical-instruments">4.1.1 Management of Surgical Instruments<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#4-1-1-management-of-surgical-instruments" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>For an effective surgical instrument management and control programme, the nursing staff knows the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>The instrument inventory.</li>
<li>The routine instruments needed for each type of operation</li>
<li>The correct use and handling of instruments</li>
<li>The method of instrument processing or preparation e.g. sterilisation</li>
<li>The aftercare of the instruments and</li>
<li>The individual surgeon's preferences</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="4-1-2-recommendations-in-relation-to-the-handling-and-caring-of-instruments">4.1.2. Recommendations in Relation to the Handling and Caring of Instruments<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#4-1-2-recommendations-in-relation-to-the-handling-and-caring-of-instruments" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ol>
<li>We inspect each instrument before and after use to detect imperfections. An instrument or appliance should function perfectly to prevent needless endangering of a patient's life and increasing the operative time because of failure of an instrument.</li>
<li>We set aside damaged instruments and send them for repair or replacement promptly.</li>
<li>We use instruments only for the purpose for which they are designed. Proper use prolongs their life. Fine clamps and dissecting scissors can be forced out of alignment, cracked, or broken if they are used on heavy tissue, gauze dressings or drainage tubing.</li>
<li>We handle instruments gently at all times and avoid bunching, dropping and weighing them down under heavy pieces of equipment.</li>
<li>We provide protection for cutting instruments, lensed instruments, and fine, delicate holding instruments that are unusually susceptible to damage by using specially designed racks and cases.</li>
</ol>

<ol start="6">
<li>We clean instruments meticulously. Make sure instruments, are perfectly clean and dry before storage.</li>
<li>Oil is kept away from instruments. Oil forms a bacterial protecting film that is difficult and time consuming to remove and it also interferes with steam penetration during the sterilization process, except for silicone instrument spray that can be used for stiff or rusty instruments. The silicone must be removed before sterilization.</li>
<li>We give instruments for regular maintenance e.g. such as sharpening of scissors.</li>
<li>We take inventory at periodic intervals during the year e.g. every 3 or 6 months. Shelved instruments are limited.</li>
<li>All newly received instruments are marked with a special instrument marker before being put into use.</li>
<li>We check for expiry date regularly but at least monthly.</li>
<li>We rotate sterile instruments in order to use item according to expiring date (First in, first out - FIFO).</li>
<li>Sterile packs are stored up to the expiry date after which it is returned to the CSSD for repackaging and sterilization.</li>
</ol>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="4-1-3-handling-of-instruments-during-surgery">4.1.3. Handling of Instruments during Surgery<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#4-1-3-handling-of-instruments-during-surgery" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ol>
<li>The scrub nurse, who takes care of an operative procedure, is responsible for the instruments. All instruments are checked and counted by the scrub nurse together with the circulating nurse before commencement of surgery. Artery forceps and clamps are packed in standard figures of 5 or 10 and counted, as well as detachable parts such as screws and blades of retractors</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>It is important that all instruments are counted particularly when surgery is to be performed in a cavity such as the abdominal, thoracic and pelvic cavities, extra peritoneal space, vagina, and hip or shoulder joints and along the spine</li>
<li>Each item is considered a foreign object that can cause unnecessary harm should it be left inside the patient. Therefore, to ensure adequate patient protection, these items are counted before and after use. A counting procedure is a method of accounting for items put on the sterile table for use during an operation</li>
<li>In the operation theatre the scrub nurse counts the instruments, big swabs, and small swabs in front of the surgeon in the beginning and at the end of the surgery.</li>
</ul>
<p>Downloaded by Reinhardt Kevin (<a href="mailto:reinhardtkevinlaw@gmail.com" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">reinhardtkevinlaw@gmail.com</a>)</p>
<p>All instruments are again counted before closure of peritoneum. The scrub nurse must inform the surgeon accordingly. The scrub nurse and the circulating nurse sign their names on the instrument list as well as the date when used.</p>
<ol start="2">
<li>Instruments are arranged in an orderly basic manner on the mayo table to facilitate checking and counting. We do not overload the mayo table initially. Additional instruments and supplies are added as the operation progresses</li>
<li>We never leave instruments on the patient, especially a scalpel handle with blade. It injures the patient. We always work from the mayo table or instrument trolley</li>
<li>The diathermy point is kept away from the patient to avoid causing a diathermy burn</li>
<li>Instruments are handled gently and with respect. It is the responsibility of the scrub nurse to see that the instruments are being used correctly by the surgeon and the assistant</li>
<li>It is ensured that all instruments handed to the surgeon are in perfect working condition</li>
<li>All instruments falling from the operative field are hung on the swab rack. In case an instrument is needed for surgery, it is first be cleaned and then sterilized with the approval of the scrub nurse</li>
<li>Under no circumstances any instrument is removed from the OT during surgery</li>
<li>The tip of an instrument during surgery is never touched.</li>
<li>The scrub nurse informs the surgeon aloud regarding the instrument as well as the swab count after checking</li>
<li>After counting, all instruments are fastened with instrument pins</li>
<li>All blades used during surgery are removed from the handles, counted and thrown directly in a safety box or sharps container</li>
</ol>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="4-2-sterile-instruments">4.2 Sterile Instruments<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#4-2-sterile-instruments" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The following is recommended:</p>
<p>a) The sterile storeroom is spacious with as many shelves as possible. The shelves are free-standing from the walls, which permit supplies to be put into one side and removed from the other, thus older dated sets and packages are always used first.
b) The shelves and open racks are damp dusted at least once a week and the floors are mopped daily. These cleaning duties are performed over a weekend when surgery is minimized to emergency cases only.</p>

<p>Shelves are marked clearly. Instrument sets and the various other packs are placed sorted of a kind on the shelves. It facilitates the duties of the personnel and they will exactly know where a certain item is placed, as well as to facilitate orientation of new personnel.</p>
<p>c) Torn wrapping of instrument sets and packs are unsterile and are re-wrapped and re-sterilized.
d) Check expired dates on all sets and packs routinely e.g. every four days.
e) Packs are rotated daily; that means older dated packs are used first (FIFO).
f) Instrument sets, linen and basin packs have a shelf life of six months if the wrapping is still intact, otherwise it is re-sterilized.
g) Single or loose packed instruments or items wrapped in mediplast pouches have a shelf life of six months if wrapping is still intact.
h) Some items deteriorate with repeated sterilization</p>
<ul>
<li>Many items are seldom used yet several must be kept sterile at all times. Older supplies are always used first so that they do not become outdated. Commercially sterilized packages usually have manufacturer's expiry date on the package. Check and return to the pharmacy/clinical supply at least two to three months prior to the expiry date</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="4-3-special-instruments">4.3 Special Instruments<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#4-3-special-instruments" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="4-3-1-lensed-instruments">4.3.1 Lensed Instruments<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#4-3-1-lensed-instruments" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>Just to summarize, lensed instruments are protected from breakage and distortion. Each instrument is kept straight at all times when not in use. Flexible endoscopes such as the gastroscope are never bent except during introduction into a passage within the patient. The junction of the flexible and rigid portions of the scope is the most vulnerable point. When a telescopic scope is sent for repair, it is wrapped gently and placed within a padded carton to protect the lens during transportation.</p>
<p><strong>General precaution for the care of lensed equipment such as endoscopy and urological instruments which we follow are:</strong></p>
<ol>
<li>Do not boil or autoclave instruments containing lamps or lenses</li>
<li>Do not place lensed instruments in solvent solutions such as alcohol</li>
<li>Never handle lensed instruments with forceps. A scratch on the instrument could cause injury to tissue or the mucous membrane lining of an orifice. Also the danger of dropping the instrument is greatly increased. The forceps could crush a telescope and ruin the optical system if held too tightly. Always handle sterile instruments wearing sterile gloves.</li>
<li>Never pile these delicate instruments one on top of another or mix them with other instruments</li>
</ol>
<p>Downloaded by Reinhardt Kevin (<a href="mailto:reinhardtkevinlaw@gmail.com" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">reinhardtkevinlaw@gmail.com</a>)</p>
<ol start="5">
<li>Avoid rough handling, jarring, or bending of parts. Lay them on a towel to absorb the impact and to prevent wear on the sheath</li>
<li>Check the light source for working order before use and after cleansing</li>
</ol>
<p>Thorough cleaning in clear tap water and mild soap solution is the first step toward decontamination. Special attention is given to surface joints and revises that may retain mucus. The lumen of every hollow instrument requires special attention. Thorough rinsing immediately after use is the best precaution to prevent clotting and crushing of blood and body fluids in the instruments.</p>
<p>The endoscope is soaked in 2% Glutaraldehyde for 15 minutes after it has been thoroughly cleaned. Then it is removed and rinsed several times in clean or sterile water before being introduced in to a patient. The protocol for endoscopic decontamination is followed.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="4-3-2-sharp-or-semi-sharp-instruments">4.3.2 Sharp or Semi-sharp Instruments<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#4-3-2-sharp-or-semi-sharp-instruments" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>We protect the edges of sharp instruments such as scissors, knives, osteotomes, Chisels, etc. during cleaning, sterilization and storage</li>
<li>Sharp instruments are kept separate from blunt ones and they demand respectful handling</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="4-3-3-micro-surgical-and-ophthalmic-instruments">4.3.3 Micro-surgical and Ophthalmic Instruments<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#4-3-3-micro-surgical-and-ophthalmic-instruments" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Each delicate instrument is separated from adjacent ones to prevent interlocking or crushing. We never pile them on top of each other or they are easily deformed</li>
<li>Exact alignment of the teeth is an absolute necessity in fine – toothed forceps. The microscopic teeth are very easily bent</li>
<li>We place instruments on a firm, flat surface for sorting and cleaning</li>
<li>Sharp blades and tips should touch absolutely nothing, not even a towel. They must never touch another instrument or any part of a receptacle in which they are placed for storage or sterilization</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="4-3-4-air-powered-instruments">4.3.4 Air-powered Instruments<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#4-3-4-air-powered-instruments" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>It is ascertained that air exhausts are directed away from the sterile field</li>
<li>We follow instructions for use, care and sterilization recommended by the manufacturer</li>
<li>Test instruments for working condition before surgeon is ready to use</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="4-3-5-electrical-instruments">4.3.5 Electrical Instruments<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#4-3-5-electrical-instruments" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Electrical powered instruments, such as saws, drills, dermatomes etc., are potential explosion hazards in the OT. Most motors are designed to be explosion proof. All must have spark proof connections. However, power switches should be off when plugging electrical cords into outlets</li>
<li>We alert the anaesthesiologist if electrical equipment will be used. He may change the anaesthesia to eliminate explosive gases</li>
<li>We do not immerse motor in liquid</li>
<li>We follow the manufacturer's recommended methods of cleaning, lubricating, sterilizing and using each piece of electrically powered equipment</li>
<li>Power cords and plugs are checked for cracks or breaks and tested for working condition before surgeon is ready to use instrument and the device is applied to the patient</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="4-4-linen-and-the-disposable-draping-material">4.4 Linen and the Disposable Draping Material<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#4-4-linen-and-the-disposable-draping-material" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>The use of linen in the OTs has significantly reduced since the introduction of disposable patient paper drapes in most of the hospitals. Woven linen has been found to cause "sterile" or non-microbial wound infections due to the lint and the fibres which settle in the wound and cause infection. Linen is however still being used in respect to OT attire, patient gowns, sheets, blankets and is responsible for an increased number of surgical site infections.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="4-4-1-general-considerations-in-the-usage-of-linen">4.4.1 General Considerations in the Usage of Linen<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#4-4-1-general-considerations-in-the-usage-of-linen" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>All linen is washed and cleansed</li>
<li>Linen is free from holes and tears</li>
<li>All old and torn linen is sent back to the laundry to be repaired as well as a requisition for replacement</li>
<li>We keep record of linen. It is only used for what it is designed for.</li>
<li>Linen is at least checked once a month and recorded in the inventory register</li>
<li>Loss of linen is reported to the person in charge immediately</li>
<li>We make sure that there is always sufficient linen in stock</li>
</ul>
<p>NOTE: A control mechanism (standard operating procedures) should be in place in theatre regarding the management of linen.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="4-4-2-care-and-handling-of-clean-linen">4.4.2 Care and Handling of Clean Linen<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#4-4-2-care-and-handling-of-clean-linen" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>All linen is kept in a linen room when not in use</li>
<li>Shelves are always kept neat and tidy</li>
<li>Clean and dirty linen is not kept in the same place</li>
</ul>

<p>The linen room is kept under lock and key when not used and the key kept by the person in charge</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="4-5-disposable-patient-paper-drapes">4.5 Disposable Patient Paper Drapes<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#4-5-disposable-patient-paper-drapes" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="4-6-draping">4.6 Draping<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#4-6-draping" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Draping is the procedure of covering the patient and surrounding areas with a sterile barrier to create and maintain an adequate sterile field during the operation. An effective barrier eliminates the passage of micro-organisms between non-sterile and sterile areas.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="techniques-to-remember-in-draping">Techniques to remember in draping<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#techniques-to-remember-in-draping" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>Since draping is a very important step in preparation of the patient for operation, drapes must be packed correctly and in sequence on the instrument trolley beforehand.</p>
<p>The scrub nurse as well as the entire team is familiar with the draping procedure.</p>

<li>A hair found on a drape is removed and the area covered immediately</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="4-7-suture-materials">4.7 Suture Materials<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#4-7-suture-materials" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="definition">Definition<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#definition" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>A suture is an all-inclusive term for any strand of materials used for tying up ends or approximating tissue and holding them until healing has taken place. Suturing usually takes place with a needle and strand which could be nylon, silk or other.</p>
<p>It is best if the needles used in suturing are solid so as to avoid the use of hollow needles and increasing the risk of HIV should a needle stick injury happen.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="4-8-classification-of-suture-materials">4.8 Classification of Suture Materials<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#4-8-classification-of-suture-materials" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Suture materials are divided into two categories namely:</p>
<ul>
<li>Absorbable sutures</li>
<li>Non-absorbable sutures</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="4-8-1-absorbable-sutures">4.8.1 Absorbable Sutures<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#4-8-1-absorbable-sutures" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>a) <strong>Surgical gut Suture:</strong></p>
<p>Surgical gut suture is composed of purified connective tissue (mostly collagen) derived from either the serosal layer of beef (bovine) or the sub mucosal fibrous layer of sheep (ovine) intestines. Surgical gut suture are available in plain or chromic. Chromic gut is processed to provide greater resistance to absorption.</p>

<p>Surgical gut suture is indicated for use in general soft tissue approximation and/or ligation, including use in ophthalmic procedures, but not for use in cardiovascular and neurological tissues. Surgical gut is digested by body enzymes and absorbed by tissue; thus no permanent foreign body remains.</p>
<p><strong>Plain surgical gut:</strong>
Plain surgical gut is used to ligate small vessels and to suture sub-cutaneous fat. It is not used to suture any layers of tissue likely to be subjected to tension during healing. Surgical gut is digested relatively quickly, usually in 5 to 10 days.</p>
<p><strong>Chromic surgical gut:</strong>
Chromic surgical gut is used for ligation of larger vessels and for suture of tissues in which non-absorbable materials are not usually recommended, as in the urinary and billiard tracts as well as in anastomosing of bowel. If the absorption rate is normal, chromic surgical gut will support the wound for about 14 days and absorb completely within 120 days.</p>
<p><strong>b) Vicryl</strong>
This is a synthetic absorbable suture material. These synthetic sutures are absorbed by a slow hydrolysis process in the presence of tissue fluids. It maintains tensile strength longer than surgical gut and then absorbs rapidly within ± 90 days.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="4-8-2-non-absorbable-sutures">4.8.2 Non-Absorbable Sutures<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#4-8-2-non-absorbable-sutures" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>These are strands of material that effectively resist enzymatic digestion or absorption in living tissue. During the healing process the suture mass becomes encapsulated and may remain for years in tissues without producing any ill effects.</p>
<p><strong>a) Surgical silk</strong>
Surgical silk is an animal product made from the fibre spun by the silkworm larvae in making their cocoons. Silk is not a true non-absorbable material. It loses much of its tensile strength after about one year and usually disappears after two or more years. It gives good support to wounds during early ambulation and generally promotes healing a little more rapidly than surgical gut, but is not as inert as most of the other non-absorbable materials.</p>
<p>It is frequently used in serosa of the gastrointestinal tract and to close fascia in the absence of infection. Silk sutures are dry. They lose tensile strength if wet. Therefore, do not moisten before use.</p>
<p><strong>b) Virgin silk</strong>
Virgin silk suture consist of several natural silk filaments drawn together and twisted to form 8 - 0 and 9 - 0 strands for tissue approximation of delicate structures, primarily in ophthalmic surgery.</p>
<p>Downloaded by Reinhardt Kevin (<a href="mailto:reinhardtkevinlaw@gmail.com" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">reinhardtkevinlaw@gmail.com</a>)</p>
<p>c) Dermal silk</p>
<p>Dermal suture is a strand of twisted silk fibres encased in a non-absorbing coating of tanned gelatine or other protein substance. This coating prevents the in-growth of tissue cells and facilitates removal after use as a skin suture.</p>
<p>It is used for suturing the skin particularly in areas of tension because of its unusual strength.</p>
<p>d) Linen</p>
<p>Surgical linen is pun from long-staple flax fibres, then twisted into tight strands and treated for smooth passage through tissue. Tensile strength is inferior to all other non-absorbable materials. Linen suture is used almost exclusively in gastrointestinal surgery.</p>
<p>e) Ethanol/ Nylon</p>
<p>Nylon is a smooth single strand of non-capillary material. The smaller the diameter becomes, the stronger the strand becomes proportionately. Nylon is used frequently in ophthalmic surgery because it has a desirable degree of elasticity. It is also used for microsurgery. Larger sizes are used for skin closure. It produces minimal tissue reaction.</p>
<p>f) Polypropylene</p>
<p>Polyprolene is an acceptable substitute for stainless steel in situations where strength and non-reactivity are required and the suture must be left in place for prolonged healing. It can be used in the presence of infection. It has become the material of choice for many plastic surgery and cardiovascular procedures because of its smooth passage through tissue as well as its strength and ineptness.</p>
<p>g) Skin clips</p>
<p>Clips made of non-corroding metal may be used to approximate skin edges. They tend to leave more scars than other methods of skin closure, but they may be applied quickly when time is a critical factor and cosmetic result is unimportant. They can be used in the presence of infection or drainage. A specially designed instrument is necessary to apply clips.</p>
<p>h) Marlex mesh</p>
<p>Available as non-sterile in sheets of 15 by 30 cm. Use wire scissors, not dissecting scissors, to cut it. Steel is opaque to x-ray, which may be a disadvantage for the patient in later life.</p>
<p>Advantages of synthetic mesh:</p>
<ul>
<li>It is easily cut to the desired size for the tissue defect</li>
<li>It is easily sutured underneath the edges of tissue to create a smooth surface</li>
<li>Fibrous tissue easily grows through the openings to incorporate the mesh into the tissue to maximize tensile strength</li>
</ul>

<p>Aside from its use in tying the umbilical cord on the new-born, this tape has other uses in surgery. In certain cardiovascular operations, it is used as a heavy tie, or as attraction suture. It may be put around a great vessel to retract it.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="4-9-common-suture-techniques">4.9 Common Suture Techniques<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#4-9-common-suture-techniques" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The primary suture line refers to those sutures that hold wound edges in approximation during healing by first intention. This line may have one continuous strand of suture material or a series of suture strands. A variety of techniques are used to place suture in tissues. The following are the most commonly used techniques.</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p><strong>Interrupted suture:</strong> Each stitch is taken and tied separately.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Continuous sutures:</strong> A series of stitches are taken with one strand of material and tied only at the ends of the suture line.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Retention sutures:</strong> Interrupted non-absorbable sutures are placed through tissue on each side of the primary suture line and a short distance from it to relieve tension on it.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Purse-string suture:</strong> A continuous suture is placed around a lumen and tightened, drawstring fashion, to close the lumen. This is used when inverting the stump of the appendix, for example.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Traction suture:</strong> A traction suture may be used to retract a structure to the side of the operative field, out of the way, e.g. the tongue in an operation in the mouth. Usually a non-absorbable suture is placed through the part.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Subcutaneous suture:</strong> A continuous suture is placed beneath the epithelial layer of the skin in short lateral stitches. The suture comes through the upper layer of the skin at each end of the incision only.</p>
</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="4-10-handling-of-sutures-during-surgery">4.10 Handling of Sutures during Surgery<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#4-10-handling-of-sutures-during-surgery" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ol>
<li>In the preparation and use of sutures in surgery, every precaution is taken to keep the sutures sterile and to prevent prolonged exposure and unnecessary handling</li>
<li>The scrub nursing team member prepares only one or two sutures during the preliminary preparation</li>
<li>Kinks should never be removed by running the fingers over the strand. The tensile strength of a gut suture should not be tested before it is handed to the surgeon. Sudden pulls or jerks used to test the tensile strength of a suture may damage it so that it will break when in use</li>
<li>A suture or free ligature should not be too long or too short. A long suture is difficult to handle and increases the possibility of contamination because it may be dragged across the sterile field or fall below it. A short suture usually slips from the eye of the needle as it is being inserted and makes tying most difficult</li>
<li>Surgical gut sutures are sealed in packets that contain fluid to keep the material pliable. This fluid is mainly alcohol and water, but may be irritating to ophthalmic tissues. Hold packet over a basin and open carefully to avoid spilling fluid on the sterile field or splashing it into your own eyes. Rinsing is necessary only for surgical gut to be implanted into the eye</li>
<li>Surgical gut should be used immediately after removal from their packets. When the material is removed and not used at once, the alcohol evaporates and the strand loses pliability</li>
<li>Do not soak surgical gut. Excessive exposure to water will reduce the tensile strength. Before unwinding the strand, it can be dipped momentarily in water or saline at room temperature, not hot as heat will coagulate the protein</li>
<li>Silk sutures are dry. They lose tensile strength if wet. Therefore, do not moisten before use.</li>
</ol>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="4-11-packing-and-storage-methods-of-suture-materials">4.11 Packing and Storage Methods of Suture Materials<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#4-11-packing-and-storage-methods-of-suture-materials" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Almost all suture materials are now supplied directly by manufactures in some form of sterile package ready for immediate use</li>
<li>The current dry packaging method seals the suture material in a primary inner packet, which may or may not contain fluid, inside a dry outer overwrap strip packet. This unit is sterilized. This method permits self-dispensing onto the sterile field</li>
<li>Packages may be stored in any moister-proof and dustproof container</li>
<li>Each primary suture packet is self-contained, and its sterility for each patient is assured as long as the integrity of the packet is maintained</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="principles-to-remember">Principles to Remember:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#principles-to-remember" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="4-13-swabs-needle-and-instrument-counts">4.13 Swabs, Needle and Instrument Counts<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#4-13-swabs-needle-and-instrument-counts" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>The purpose of the count is to ensure adequate patient protection because swabs, needles and instruments are foreign objects that can cause unnecessary harm if left inside the patient.</p>
<p>The type and number of swabs, needles and instruments needed for different operations will vary. Each item must be considered a foreign object that can cause unnecessary harm should it be left inside the patient. Therefore, to ensure adequate patient protection, these items are counted before, during and on completion of a surgical procedure. A counting procedure is a method of accounting for items put on the sterile table for use during an operation.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="5-1-ways-to-stop-surgical-bleeding">5.1 Ways to Stop Surgical Bleeding<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#5-1-ways-to-stop-surgical-bleeding" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>There are different ways to stop bleeding:</p>
<p>a) <strong>Artery clamp</strong></p>
<p>A surgeon has a big variety of methods at his disposal to stop the bleeding, of which the artery clamp is the most common. They change in size, for example Rochester Pean or the Criles artery clamp. The point can be straight or bent, but the purpose is the same, to clamp the blood vessel.</p>
<p>b) <strong>Tie off</strong></p>
<p>A strand of suture material can be used to tie off the blood vessel which is, for the time being, clamped with an artery clamp. The thickness of the suture material depends on the size of the blood vessel and the choice of the surgeon.</p>
<p>c) <strong>Heat</strong></p>
<p>Warm moist swabs can be used to stop capillary bleeding during extensive surgery, for example a radical mastectomy.</p>
<p>d) <strong>Oxycel/ Surgecel</strong></p>
<p>This is an absorbent, hemostatical agent. It can be placed into or on the bleeding point. It does not stick to the sides of the wound, but absorbs the blood and swells, becomes tough jelly. It causes haemostasis.</p>
<p>e) <strong>Bone Wax</strong></p>
<p>It is used to stop a bleeding on the bone and is made of bees wax.</p>
<p>f) <strong>Surgical diathermy</strong></p>
<p>A high frequency, electrical current, provided by an electro – surgical unit, is frequently used in the theatre to cut tissue and coagulate blood.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="5-2-use-of-diathermy">5.2 Use of Diathermy<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#5-2-use-of-diathermy" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>It must always be remembered that the use of diathermy during surgical operations carries a medical – legal risk. It is important that the necessary precautions are taken and regular maintenance is kept.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="risk-of-use-of-diathermy">Risk of use of diathermy<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#risk-of-use-of-diathermy" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Burns: originate when there is a break in the normal cycle, for example if the patient's arm touches the metal part of the table. The active electrode can touch a wound hook and the surgeon can, by accident, step on the pedal and activate the electrode which causes a burn. The machine can be connected incorrectly. Burns can also be caused if the surgical team's gloves are perforated.</p>

<p>Preparing the patient</p>
<ol>
<li>The patient does not wear any jewellery, for example a ring, earrings, chains or watch when s/he comes into the operating theatre, because this can cause diathermical burns, this is confirmed while taking patient in OT.</li>
<li>We determine whether the patient has a metal prosthesis and report this to the surgeon and anaesthesiologist</li>
<li>We make sure no piece of clothing comes between the patient's skin and the diathermy plate. This hinders the direct contact and may lead to burns</li>
<li>We never place the diathermical plate onto a hairy area. Shave the contact area if the patient is very hairy or has big lesions</li>
<li>We do not put the plate on to a bony area, for example the elbow or knee joint, which are pressure points</li>
<li>We keep the plate as close as possible to the operating area to lessen the electric current sent through the body</li>
<li>If electro-cardiogram electrodes are used on the patient together with diathermy, they are placed as far away as possible from the operating area</li>
<li>We make sure the plate under the patient is dry and clean, after the operating area is cleaned</li>
</ol>
<p>5.2.1 Precautionary Measures</p>
<ul>
<li>Before the day's operating schedule starts, the diathermy machine and plate is tested. Technician checks whether it is functional, whether there are any loose wires or broken isolation material. The apparatus is maintained regularly by him.</li>
<li>We make sure that all personnel are familiar with the use and handling of the machine</li>
<li>Most of the apparatus are equipped with an alarm, to warn the users that there is a break in the negative cable, or if it is incorrectly connected</li>
<li>Connections, cables and electrodes are checked for damage</li>
<li>We check the alarm by switching the machine on before the negative electrode is connected</li>
<li>The current control knobs are kept clean, clearly marked and easy to handle</li>
<li>The plate is kept dry and clean</li>
<li>The cables of the two electrodes may not be too long</li>
<li>The apparatus is kept close to the surgeon and as far as possible from the anaesthesiologist</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="5-8-cardiac-arrest">5.8 Cardiac Arrest<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#5-8-cardiac-arrest" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Intra-operatively, the anaesthesiologist normally takes charge if a cardiac arrest occurs. The nurse in-charge is notified. The circulating- or anaesthetic nurse is available immediately and assists the anaesthesiologist during the resuscitation of the patient. The orders given by the anaesthesiologist is carried out carefully and precisely. The nurse records the time when the cardiac arrest has been diagnosed.</p>
<p><strong>Procedures that may be carried out in case of cardiac arrest:</strong></p>
<p>a. The defibrillator is kept ready in case of cardiac arrest for the administering of cardiac treatment. The two electrodes are lubricated with a cardiac jelly and then placed on the patient's thorax, one over the apex of the heart and the other over the upper third of the sternum. The defibrillator is used by the anaesthesiologist or other doctors in the OT.</p>
<p>b. The surgeon may decide to perform an open cardiac massage. After opening the thorax s/he takes the heart in his hands and pumps it regularly at 80 – 100 times per minute.</p>
<p>The anaesthesiologist ensures an open airway and administers 100% oxygen to the patient. The surgeon stops surgery until the patient's condition stabilises. The surgeon and scrubbing nurse remains sterile. The surgeon continues surgery if the patient's condition improves.</p>
<p>The surgeon will decide whether or not to continue surgery if the patient's condition does not improve. The scrubbing nurse covers the wound and assists with the resuscitation if surgeon decides not to continue. External cardiac massage will be in the meantime applied by the surgeon or assistant surgeon. The patient is kept in the theatre or recovery room until his condition allows surgery to continue, or transferred back to the ward.</p>
<p><strong>ALL</strong> health personnel involved in the patient care i.e. scrub nurse, doctor, anaesthesiologist writes an incident report about the event in accordance with the policy of the institution. The scrub nurse records the event in red ink in the operation register next to the patient's name. The observations that were done and emergency medicines given are noted in the records.</p>

<p>A. The air conditioning requirements for Operation Theater at SKNMC, Pune have been deliberated at length with manufacturers, engineers, technical committee members and other stake holders.</p>
<p>B. operation theaters have been divided into groups:</p>
<ol>
<li>
<p>Super specialty OT: New OT complex has 2 OT's Super specialty OT meant for Neurosurgery and Orthopedics (Joint Replacement).</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>General OT: This includes operation theatres for Ophthalmology, Orthopedic surgery, Obstetrics &amp; Gynecology and all other basic surgical disciplines.</p>
</li>
</ol>
<p>Daycare centre: Daycare surgery for selected patients to hospital for a planned surgical procedure, returning home on the same day, would fall under the category of general OT.</p>
<p>C. The following basic assumptions have been kept in view:</p>
<ul>
<li>Occupancy: Standard occupancy of 5-8 persons at any given point of time inside the OT is considered.</li>
<li>Equipment Load: Standard equipment load of 5-7 kW considered per OT. For super- speciality OT, standard equipment load considered is 7-9 Kw.</li>
<li>Ambient temperature &amp; humidity at each location is considered while designing the system.</li>
</ul>

<p>REQUIREMENTS – Super Specialty OT</p>
<ol>
<li>Air Changes Per Hour:</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>Minimum total air changes followed are 25 based on international guidelines</li>
<li>The fresh air component of the air change is 5 air changes (ie 20%) out of total minimum 25 air changes.</li>
<li>The supply and return air ducts are of non-corrosive material.</li>
<li>There is no internal insulation and acoustic lining on ducts</li>
</ul>
<ol start="2">
<li>
<p>Air Velocity: The vertical down flow of air coming out of the laminar flow boxes is able to carry bacteria carrying particle load away from the operating table. The airflow is unidirectional and downwards on the OT table. The air velocity is recommended as per the guidelines and is 25-35 FPM from non-aspirating unidirectional laminar flow ceiling array's.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Positive Pressure: is maintained at all times in the OT (operational and non operational hours). Minimum positive pressure is 2.5 pascals.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Outdoor air intakes: The location of outdoor air intake is away from contaminated sources.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Air handling in the OT including air Quality: Air is supplied through Terminal HEPA filters in the ceiling. The minimum size of the filtration area is 1 feet on each side of the OT table to cover the entire OT table and surgical team. The minimum supply air volume to the OT (in CFM) is compliant with the desired minimum air change.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Air Filtration: The AHU has an air purification unit and air filtration unit. There are two sets of washable flange type pre filters of efficiency 90% down to 10 microns and 99% down to 5 microns with aluminumframe within the AHU. The necessary service panels are provided for servicing the filters, motors &amp; blowers. HEPA filters of efficiency 99.97% down to 0.3 microns are provided in the OT and not in the AHU.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Temp &amp; RH for Super-specialty OT: it is maintained 21 C +/- 3 C (except for Ortho for Joints replacement as 18 C +0 &amp; -2 C) with corresponding relative humidity of 60%. Appropriate devices to monitor and display these conditions inside the OT are installed.</p>
</li>
</ol>
