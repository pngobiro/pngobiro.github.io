<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="definition">Definition<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#definition" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>A drug or combination of drugs which will induce anaesthesia safely and reversibly when injected in sufficient doses</p>
<p>Ideal properties of IV anaesthetic agents :</p>
<p>Simple preparation</p>
<p>Compatible with other agents and IV fluids</p>
<p>Painless on administration</p>
<p>High potency and efficacy</p>
<p>Predictable action within one arm-brain circulation time</p>
<p>Minimal cardiovascular effects or other toxicity</p>
<p>Depression of airway reflexes for intubation</p>
<p>Rapid and predictable offset of effect</p>
<p>Rapid metabolism for minimal hangover</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="possible-mode-of-actions">Possible mode of actions :<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#possible-mode-of-actions" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>increasing the duration of Cl- channel opening hence preventing neurotransmission.</p>
<p>increased secretions of inhibitory neurotransmitters.</p>
<p>block of excitatory neurotransmitters.</p>

<p>Inhalational anesthetic agents are used to induce and maintain anesthesia. They are administered by passing oxygen with either air or nitrous oxide over a liquid anesthetic in a vaporizer.</p>

<p>Aseptic techniques</p>
<p>It is impossible to exclude all microorganisms from the environment, but for the safety of both patients and personnel, every effort is made to minimize and control these microorganisms. Microorganisms are present in the air and on animate and inanimate objects at all times. To effectively apply the principles of asepsis, environmental control, and sterile techniques discussed in this chapter, the meaning of terms related to aseptic technique must first be understood. Therefore the basis of prevention is the knowledge of causative agents and their control, as well as the principles of aseptic and sterile techniques. The methods by which microbial contamination is contained in the environment are referred to as aseptic technique. The OR in the restricted area is aseptic at best because the room and air cannot ever be 100% free of microbial content. Some key elements of asepsis include the following facts:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Aseptic technique is sometimes referred to as clean technique.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Items have been cleaned and decontaminated so they are safe to handle with clean, bare hands.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Items in use in patient care are handled with examination gloves for the protection of both the caregiver and the patient.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Items have been cleaned, decontaminated, disinfected, or terminally sterilized without a wrapper, and stored in a clean, dry place.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Items may start out sterile but are not maintained or used under sterile conditions. Skin preps may be packaged sterile, but skin cannot be sterilized. The process is aseptic.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Contamination is contained. Extraneous contamination is avoided.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Items are set up on clean towels or drapes and used with examination gloves.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Items are not sterile or maintained sterile during use. Extraneous contamination is avoided.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Disposable items are not cleaned and reused for another patient.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Items are classified as semi critical or noncritical by Spaulding’s classification of the importance of patient care items.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Items can be used outside the confines of the restricted area.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Items are used on intact skin or mucous membranes.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Items are not used when the patient’s vascular system will be entered.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="sterile-technique">Sterile Technique<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#sterile-technique" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Sterile technique incorporates many processes associated with asepsis but to a higher, more controlled degree. In sterile technique all microorganisms must be maintained at an irreducible minimum meaning as low as absolutely possible.</p>
<p>Keep in mind that a sealed sterile package must be opened at some point during patient care to use the contents. That means opening the package to the room air. Even in the restricted area the room air has a microbial count that we cannot eliminate. We try as hard as possible to minimize the room traffic and maintain environmental controls to maintain the sterile field. Essential elements of sterile technique include the following factors:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Items are used only in a sterile field in the restricted area.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Items are used by sterile team members wearing appropriate sterile attire.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Items are used in areas of the patient’s body where the site has been prepped.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Items may be used in invasive surgery.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Items may be used in body areas with non-intact skin and membranes and may enter the patient’s vascular system.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Items are classified as critical according to Spaulding’s level of importance of patient care items.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Items have been cleaned, decontaminated, and packaged before sterilization.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Items processed by sterilization are stored wrapped and remain so until use by a sterile team member.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Items are for individual patient use only. Reusable items can be reprocessed and resterilized for use with another patient. Disposable items are discarded after use. If opened and unused, disposable items are discarded.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Items that become contaminated are discarded and replaced immediately.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="patient-identification">Patient Identification<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#patient-identification" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Preadmission Procedures Some preoperative preparations can be performed in the surgeon's office. Patients are then referred to the preoperative testing center of the hospital or ambulatory care facility. Tests and records should be completed and available before the patient is admitted the day of the surgical procedure. Preadmission tests (PATs) are scheduled according to the guidelines of each facility. Some tests are acceptable only for a 30-day period or are repeated before admission. The type of testing performed depends on the patient's known or suspected condition and the complexity of the surgical procedure. The preoperative preparations include:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Medical history and physical examination.</strong> These are performed and documented by a physician, nurse practitioner, physician assistant (PA), or the registered nurse first assistant (RNFA). Allergies and sensitivities should be noted. The preoperative nurse establishes the baseline for the patient's vital signs</li>
<li><strong>Laboratory tests.</strong> Testing should be based on specific clinical indicators or risk factors that could affect surgical management or anesthesia. Tests include age, sex, preexisting disease, magnitude of surgical procedure, and type of anesthesia. Ideally these tests should be completed 24 hours before admission so the results are available for review. Some facilities perform laboratory studies the morning of the procedure.</li>
</ol>
<p>Blood type and cross match. If a transfusion is anticipated, the patient's blood is typed and cross matched. Many patients prefer to have their own blood drawn and stored for auto transfusion. Patients should be advised that blood banks charge an additional fee to store and preserve autologous blood for personal use. Even if the patient is to have an auto transfusion, his or her blood should still be typed and cross matched in the event that additional transfusions are needed. If the patient refuses to accept blood transfusions, the appropriate documentation of refusal should be completed according to the policies and procedures of the facility</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="types-of-surgeries">Types of surgeries<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#types-of-surgeries" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>There are various types of surgery and they can be classified based on different parameters. Based on Timing</p>
<p>Surgery can be classified based on the urgency and need to perform the surgery.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="1-elective-surgery">1. Elective Surgery<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#1-elective-surgery" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The name “elective” might imply that this type of surgery is optional, but that’s not always the case. An elective procedure is simply one that is planned in advance, rather than one that’s done in an emergency situation. A wide range of surgical procedures can be considered elective surgery. Cosmetic surgeries fall into this category, but so can things like ear tubes, tonsillectomies, and scoliosis surgery. Although these procedures may be done</p>
<p>“electively,” they can be significant and potentially life-changing operations.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="2-semi-elective-surgery">2. <p>Semi-elective surgery is a surgery that must be done to preserve the patient’s life, but does not need to be performed immediately.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="3-urgent-surgery">3. Urgent Surgery<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#3-urgent-surgery" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>An urgent surgery is one that can wait until the patient is medically stable, but should generally be done today or tomorrow</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="4-emergency-surgery">4. Emergency Surgery<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#4-emergency-surgery" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>An emergency surgery is one that must be performed without delay; the patient has no choice other than immediate surgery, if they do not want to risk permanent disability or death.</p>

<p>Surgery can also be classified based on the body part that is being operated on.</p>
<p>When surgery is performed on one organ system or structure, it may be classed by the tissue, organ or organ system involved. Examples include:</p>

<p>The floor surface is suitably hard, nonporous and appropriate for frequent cleaning and there are no cracks. The floors have a nonslip surface, to prevent staff from slipping and injuring themselves. When floors are being cleaned, a warning sign “wet floor” are put up to warn the personnel.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="verification-and-reception-of-patients-in-theatre">Verification and reception of patients in theatre<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#verification-and-reception-of-patients-in-theatre" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>This policy exists to ensure that there is a robust system in place that facilitates the identification of the correct patient for theatre which provides a comprehensive handover to ensure continuity of care and patient safety in the transfer from the pre-operative ward, through the intra-operative phase and return to the post-operative ward.</p>
<p>This policy should be invoked for every patient who is transferred to the operating theatre department</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="from-the-ward-admissions-units-to-theatre-pre-wait-area">From the Ward/Admissions Units to Theatre pre wait area<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#from-the-ward-admissions-units-to-theatre-pre-wait-area" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>The decision is made by the theatre team for a patient to be brought to theatre.</p>
<p>At team brief, clarification from the Surgeon/Anaesthetist is sought regarding the correct wards which all of the patients are on.</p>
<p>Any changes to the list order must be conveyed to the ward at this time.</p>
<p>When the team are ready to request a patient to be transferred to theatre, a phone call is made to the ward to inform the ward staff that a porter is going to be requested to collect the patient.</p>
<p>If the patient is not ready on the ward for any reason, this must be communicated to the theatre staff and reasons documented.</p>
<p>To ensure the correct patient is requested staff will:</p>
<ul>
<li>enter the patients' full name</li>
<li>date of birth</li>
<li>patients number</li>
<li>Ward patient to be transferred from</li>
</ul>
<p>The porter will arrive on the relevant ward and will locate the nurse in charge of the patient.</p>
<p>If a theatre practitioner goes to collect and escort a patient to theatre then they must take a theatre collection slip with them to use to check the correct details of the patient. The collection slip must have the following information recorded on it:</p>
<ul>
<li>patients' full name</li>
<li>date of birth</li>
<li>NHS number</li>
<li>Ward patient to be transferred from</li>
<li>Theatre patient to be transferred to the theatre patient</li>
</ul>
<p>The nurse in charge of the patient will then show the porter (and theatre practitioner) to the patient's bedside.</p>
<p>The patient will be correctly identified by checking the information on the porters' handheld device or theatre collection slip with both of the patient's ID wristbands information:</p>
<ul>
<li>patients' full name</li>
<li>date of birth</li>
<li>patient's number</li>
</ul>
<p>The Nurse responsible for the patient will: Check the patient understands the nature of the operation</p>
<p>Check that site of operation is marked and the consent form is signed</p>
<p>Check that any property going to theatre with the patient is identified and recorded in the Operating Theatres Record of Care and that provision is made for safe keeping in property envelopes or bags e.g. inhalers, hearing aids.</p>
<p>Ensure the correct notes matching the details on the patients ID wristbands accompany the patient.</p>
<p>Ensure the patients' prescription/infusion charts accompany the patient to theatre</p>
<p>Check the patient has 2 ID wristband theatre patient to be transferred to</p>
<p>The ward nurse must then check and sign the pre-operative checklist in the Operating Theatres Record of Care indicating the correct patient has been identified and the correct documentation is present.</p>
<p>If there is a discrepancy in any of the above checks it must be reported to the member of staff in charge of the relevant theatre before leaving the ward and documented accordingly.</p>
<p>Prior to leaving the ward and for the patient's safety the bed should be at its correct level for the individual in line with Moving and Handling requirements and have all the relevant attachments, e.g. Pillows, drip stands etc.</p>
<p>Patients who are willing and able to walk to the Anaesthetic Room may do so. They must be appropriately dressed e.g. dressing gown and slippers to ensure privacy and dignity is maintained. Trolley / bed should be organised for the patient and taken to theatre by the appropriate staff.</p>
<p>The patient should then be safely transferred to the pre-wait area or relevant anaesthetic room and be accompanied by a trained practitioner from the ward. (Unless a theatre practitioner is escorting the patient)</p>
<p>In reception/ pre-wait area (This step may be missed if the patient is transferred straight to the theatre)</p>
<p>The Practitioner in Charge of the individual theatre (or Deputy) is responsible for initiating patient collection from the reception/pre-wait area.</p>
<p>The trained practitioner designated to collect the patient from reception/pre-wait area should be given the collection slip and on entering the reception area/pre-wait area, should request the correct patient by stating to the reception/pre-wait area staff the:</p>
<ul>
<li>Patient’s name</li>
<li>Date of Birth</li>
<li>patient's number</li>
<li>Theatre number</li>
</ul>
<p>The designated theatre practitioner will:</p>
<ul>
<li>Check both of the patient’s ID wristbands and the documentation with the patient against the collection slip.</li>
<li>Any property with the patient should be identified and recorded on the checklist within the Operating Theatres Record of Care and provision made for safe keeping in property envelopes or bags e.g. inhalers, hearing aids, dentures</li>
<li>Receive any information that needs to be handed over from the ward to theatre from the reception staff</li>
</ul>
<p>If there is a discrepancy in any of the above checks it must be conveyed to the member of staff in charge of the relevant theatre before leaving reception.</p>
<p>The patient is transferred according to the appropriate patient moving and handling procedure to the anaesthetic room/theatre</p>
<ul>
<li>Transfer to the anaesthetic room (where applicable)</li>
<li>If a trolley is used for transfer then cot sides must be securely in position.</li>
<li>There MUST be sufficient members of staff available to safely transfer the patient to the anaesthetic room on the trolley.</li>
<li>Oxygen tubing, facemask and drip stand must be available and in working order.</li>
</ul>
<p>Patients who are willing and able to walk to the Anaesthetic Room may do so. They must be appropriately dressed e.g. dressing gown and slippers to ensure privacy and dignity is maintained. Trolley / bed should be organised for the patient and taken to theatre by the appropriate staff</p>
<p>The patient should be introduced to the receiving practitioner and Anaesthetist in the anaesthetic room.</p>
<p>The theatre checklist must be completed on arrival in the anaesthetic room (or theatre where there is no anaesthetic room)</p>
<p>Prior to the start of anaesthesia being given, the Safer Surgical Checklist, Sign In procedure must be completed by the Anaesthetist with the Anaesthetic Practitioner together</p>
<p>If either of the patient identification wristbands is removed at any time they should be replaced immediately by the person who removed it.</p>
<p>The patient should not be left unattended in the theatre department anywhere at any time</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="in-the-operating-theatre">In the Operating Theatre<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#in-the-operating-theatre" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="wheeling-of-patients-to-the-pt-table">Wheeling of patients to the PT table<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#wheeling-of-patients-to-the-pt-table" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<li>Airway: if airway compromise is possible, electively intubate the patient with an endotracheal tube.</li>
</ol>
<p>Some patients require a nasogastric tube placement to prevent gastric contents' aspiration during the transfer,</p>
<p>while other patients may require cervical spine stabilization.</p>

<li>
<p>Breathing: control ventilation with optimized arterial blood gas levels. Ensure adequate oxygenation at all times.</p>
</li>
<li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="anaesthetics-and-handling-of-anaesthetic-materials">Anaesthetics and handling of anaesthetic materials<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#anaesthetics-and-handling-of-anaesthetic-materials" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Equipment used for the administration and monitoring of anesthesia of patients must be maintained in optimal working order to ensure patient safety as well as convenience and economy of operation. After use, disinfection, sterilization, and cleaning of breathing circuits of anesthesia machines and mechanical ventilators is essential to avoid cross-contamination.</p>
<p>Common methods of sterilization involve the use of glutaraldehyde, heat, or ethylene oxide.</p>
<p>The last agent is strongly recommended in those situations where heat might damage the materials or equipment being treated. Mechanical checking of anesthesia machines is necessary to make sure that they can be relied upon to deliver known amounts of oxygen, nitrous oxide, and inhalation anesthetic agents.</p>
<p>Ideally, electronic monitoring devices should be kept in proper working order by a daily preventive program by trained, biomedical technicians</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="patient-monitoring-techniques">Patient monitoring techniques<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#patient-monitoring-techniques" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>A patient undergoing surgery under anaesthesia is usually experiencing a number of disturbances with regard to the normal functioning of several body systems.</p>
<p>This means that the patient’s physiological status needs to be monitored at all times.</p>
<p>The types of monitoring required vary with the procedure being undertaken</p>
<p>Basic monitoring modalities includes pulse oximetry, non-invasive blood pressure, electrocardiography, temperature and capnography. (measurement during inspiration and expiration)</p>
<p>Advanced cardiovascular monitoring includes invasive pressure monitoring, and invasive and non-invasive cardiac output monitoring devices.</p>
<p>Neurological monitoring systems which evaluate intracranial pressure, cerebral blood flow and brain electrical activity in the critical care and perioperative environment are also described acute brain injury.</p>
<p>Point-of-care testing (PoCT) modalities as all monitoring devices can be regarded as forms of PoCT devices.</p>

<p>as friends, parents, children, citizens, businesspeople, teachers, professionals, and so on.</p>
<p><strong>Ethics</strong> is a set of moral principles and a code for behavior that govern an individual’s actions with other individuals and within society</p>
<p>a physician must recognize responsibility to patients first and foremost, as well as to society, to other health professionals, and to self.</p>

<p>The principle that deals with fairness, equity and equality and provides for an individual to claim that to which they are entitled.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="monitoring-during-anesthesia">Monitoring during anesthesia<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#monitoring-during-anesthesia" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Both surgery and general anesthesia induce changes in the patient’s physiology.</p>
<p>The overall effects of drugs administered at anesthesia induction is to blunt consciousness and provide analgesia, but they also have other effects, such as reduction in muscle tone (even paralysis if a curare is administered), reduction in pulmonary functional residual capacity (FRC), decrease in CO, blunt of thermic homeostasis.</p>
<p>Moreover, respiratory drive suppression and careful and adequate patient monitoring are considered essential to titrate administration of anesthetic medication, to detect physiologic perturbations and allow intervention to prevent harmful conditions</p>
<p>Both anesthesia and surgical incision cause an increase in stress hormone excretion (e.g., cortisol and catecholamines) leading to significant modifications in blood flow, increase in gluconeogenesis, protein catabolism,</p>
<p>During and immediately after surgery, depending on the type of surgical procedure and patient conditions, there is an increased risk of deep vein thrombosis and acute pulmonary embolism (PE).</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="respiratory-function">Respiratory function<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#respiratory-function" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Proper monitoring of respiratory function may help the early detection of hypoxemia and hypoventilation.</p>
<p>fluid resuscitation and reduced CO may cause pulmonary edema.</p>
<p>Hypercoagulation and venous stasis are risk factors for developing a PE.</p>
<p>Peripheral saturation, airways patency, respiratory rate should be measured in every patient.</p>
<p>Patient at increased risk of developing respiratory complications should also have capnography, and hemodynamic status checked. Other respiratory monitoring techniques in high-risk surgical patients include arterial blood gas analysis, 12 derivations electrocardiogram, and imaging studies</p>

<p>Monitoring cardiovascular function is essential to avoid or to detect complications such as hypoperfusion, perioperative myocardial infarction, dysrhythmias, hypertension, hypovolemia, hyperthermia, hypoxemia, hypercarbia, bradycardia as a consequence of residual anesthetics effect, vagal stimulation, or increased intracranial pressure.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="clinical-monitoring-of-perioperative-and-postoperative-complications">Clinical monitoring of perioperative and postoperative complications<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#clinical-monitoring-of-perioperative-and-postoperative-complications" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Postoperative complications greatly depend on the type of surgery. Gastrointestinal complications are more frequently seen in patients undergoing abdominal surgery.</p>
<p>Patients at risk for gastrointestinal complications include patients with coagulopathies, perioperative impairment of tissue perfusion, spinal cord injuries, severe burns, hepatic or renal failure, polytrauma, organ transplantation, need for prolonged mechanical ventilation, hypercoagulable states, hypovolemia.</p>
<p>Following abdominal surgery, postoperative ileus can be observed. Depending on the gut segment involved, it may last up to 72 h, and time to first solid food intake must be planned accordingly</p>

<p>Adrenocortical insufficiency should be suspected in the event of hypotension in patients with chronic glucorticoid therapy who does not respond to fluid replacement and vasopressor agents</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="5cba165b">Documentation of patients’ condition<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#5cba165b" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>First, understanding the critical importance of good documentation is key.</p>
<p>There is so much more to documentation than mere legal protection. Medical records are a crucial form of communication.</p>
<p>And the importance of complete, accurate, concise, timed and dated documentation cannot be overstated.</p>
<p>there are three fundamental reasons to keep in mind when striving for excellent documentation:</p>
<ol>
<li>It’s a form of communication</li>
</ol>
<p>Good documentation promotes continuity of care through clear communication between all members involved in patient care. The medical record is a way to communicate treatment plans to other providers regarding your patient.</p>
<ol start="2">
<li>It’s a legal document</li>
</ol>
<p>A medical record is a legal document. So, understand that what you write is memorised permanently. In the case of any legal proceedings, documentation is heavily scrutinised to help support an argument either way. Documenting sensitive discussions regarding limits of care, prognosis, and treatment decisions clearly and transparently is crucial.</p>
<ol start="3">
<li>It’s a document of service</li>
</ol>
<p>This is a point often missed. Medical documentation is a document of service that has huge implications for hospital funding.</p>
<p>Each issue that is documented is coded and then translated into a cost for the hospital system.</p>
<p>Thorough documentation of all medical issues and treatments is therefore crucial for hospital funding.</p>
<p>Particularly in discharge summaries.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="the-basics-of-clinical-documentation">The basics of clinical documentation<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-basics-of-clinical-documentation" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Date, time and sign every entry.</p>
<p>Although simple, its importance cannot be overstated. The timing of events and reviews is often crucial in piecing together information about deteriorating patients.</p>
<p>Write your name and role as a heading and the names and roles of all others present at the encounter.</p>
<p>Make entries immediately or as soon as possible after care is given.</p>
<p>Prompt documentation reduces the risk of you forgetting key details.</p>
<p>It ensures all other team members are aware of any changes to a patient’s condition or management plan.</p>
<p>In reality, this isn’t always possible. If you are returning to the patient’s notes later, document clearly in the heading that it was written in retrospect, with the current date and time</p>
<p>Be legible.</p>
<p>There’s no point documenting well if no one can decipher it.</p>
<p>Be thorough, accurate, and objective.</p>
<p>Maintain a professional tone.</p>
<p>Sarcasm, attempted joking or a casual tone may reflect badly on you.</p>
<p>Only use approved abbreviations.</p>
<p>It is better to use no abbreviations at all to avoid confusion.</p>
<p>Addenda.</p>
<p>If an addendum is made, this should also be verbally communicated to other teams and nursing staff.</p>
<p>Sign off any addenda with the time and your full details.</p>
<p>Mistakes.</p>
<p>If a mistake is made, correct it with a single strikethrough.</p>
<p>Follow that by clearly signing and dating the correction.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="decontamination">Decontamination<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#decontamination" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Decontamination is a term used to describe a combination of processes including cleaning, disinfection and / or sterilisation used to make a re-useable item safe for further use. You cannot sterilise an instrument without decontamination</p>
<p>Cleaning is carried out manually to ensure that the instrument is free from contaminants, dust, dirt, secretions, marks, stains or unwanted matter etc. Cleaning must be thoroughly performed before disinfection or sterilisation is attempted.</p>
<p>Disinfection is a process in which most or nearly all micro-organisms, whether or not pathogenic, are killed through the use of chemicals, heat or ultraviolet rays. Sterilisation is a process of making objects free from bacteria or other micro-organisms usually by heat or chemical means</p>
<p>Sterilisation is distinct from disinfection, sanitisation and pasteurisation, in that sterilisation kills, deactivates, or eliminates all forms of life and other biological agents which are present.</p>
<p>Means of sterilisation include heat, chemicals, irradiation, high pressure and filtration</p>

<p>Radioactive wastes are the products of nuclear medical treatments, like cancer therapies.</p>
<p>These wastes can also come from medical equipment that uses radioactive isotopes.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="other-general-waste">Other General Waste<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#other-general-waste" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>General waste makes up the vast majority (85%) of the waste produced at medical facilities. However, if staff are not careful, one of these three other types can get mixed up in this type of waste, and be disposed of improperly.</p>
<p>Two common methods of disposing of hospital-generated medical waste include incineration or autoclaving.</p>
<p><strong>incineration</strong> is a process that burns medical waste in a controlled environment.</p>
<p><strong>Autoclaving</strong> utilizes heat in the form of steam to sterilize certain types of medical waste, effectively killing microorganisms.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="last-offices-services-in-operation-theatre">Last offices services in Operation Theatre<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#last-offices-services-in-operation-theatre" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>the term last offices relates to the care given to a body after death.</p>
<p>It is a process that demonstrates respect for the deceased and is focused on respecting their religious and cultural beliefs, as well as health and safety and legal requirements.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="health-and-safety-issues">Health and safety issues<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#health-and-safety-issues" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>The body, following death, must be prepared for transfer to the mortuary or funeral directors in a way that does not compromise health and safety. Medical practitioners must apply universal infection control precautions to last offices practice, as any infectious risk present before death will also be present after death.</p>
<p>Body fluids may leak after death and specific precautions, including the use of body bags, may be required. These may also be required for specific infectious diseases.</p>
<p>Good communication between medical staff and the patient’s family will be required as some of these practices, such as the use of danger of infection labels, may cause offence or concern.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="legal-issues">Legal issues<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#legal-issues" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Medical practitioners’ responsibility to a deceased patient continues until the body leaves the clinical environment. This requires the continuing need for excellent documentation standards until the body is removed.</p>
<p>Death must be verified before last offices commence. While this process has traditionally been undertaken by a medical practitioner, theatre technologist/technician who have undertaken training may verify death in certain cases.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="involving-significant-others">Involving significant others<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#involving-significant-others" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Family members may wish to be involved in the last office procedures - this may be a religious or cultural requirement.</p>
<p>If this is the case, excellent communication will be required to ensure the procedure aids the grieving process and minimises the distress. This will involve explaining the rationale for certain aspects and providing support and reassurance.</p>
<p>The procedure should not expose the family member to any risk, and guidance and advice should be offered to reduce these risks.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="the-procedure">The procedure<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-procedure" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The following is intended as a guide only and should be influenced by the patient's preferences expressed in life and those of the family or significant others after death.</p>
<p>Provide support to family as necessary.</p>
<p>Ensure that death has been verified and discuss with senior nursing and medical staff any specific legal and health and safety issues that may influence last offices practice.</p>
<p>Ascertain if there is any need for referral to the coroner.</p>
<p>Discuss with family any preferences they may have or that the patient, in life, may have requested that will influence last offices practice</p>
<p>Collect all equipment necessary.</p>
<p>Ensure the area where the procedure is to be performed is private and as free from interruptions as possible.</p>
<p>Remove tubes, lines and access devices as agreed with senior medical staff.</p>
<p>If lines are to remain, cut and spigot off any large-bore tubes and cover with gauze and adhesive dressing. Ensure that documentation alerts mortuary staff to their presence.</p></div></div></div></div></div></div></div><div data-testid="stAppIframeResizerAnchor" data-iframe-height="true" class="st-emotion-cache-1dumvfu eht7o1d9"></div></section></div></div></div><div data-testid="portal" id="portal" class="st-emotion-cache-1q6lfs0 e19n7mk11"></div></div><div class=""></div></div>
