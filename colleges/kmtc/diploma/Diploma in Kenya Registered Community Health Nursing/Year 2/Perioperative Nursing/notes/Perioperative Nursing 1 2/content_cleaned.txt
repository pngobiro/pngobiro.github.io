<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="definition">Definition;<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#definition" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Perioperative nursing describes the wide variety of nursing functions associated with the patient’s surgical management.</li>
<li>Perioperative Nursing is the care of a client or patient before, during, and after an operation.</li>
<li>It is a specialized nursing area wherein a registered nurse works as a team member of other surgical health care professionals.</li>
</ul>
<p>Perioperative nursing is used to describe the nursing care provided in the total surgical experience of the patient: preoperative, intra-operative and postoperative.</p>
<p>a. Preoperative Phase:</p>
<p>Extends from the time the client is admitted in the surgical unit, to the time he/she is prepared for the surgical procedure, until he is transported into the operating room.</p>
<p>b. Intra-operative Phase;</p>
<ul>
<li>Extends from the time the client is admitted to the Operating Room, to the time of administration of anesthesia, surgical procedure is done, until he/she is transported to the Recovery Room (RR)/PACU.</li>
</ul>
<p>c. Postoperative Phase:</p>
<ul>
<li>Extends from the time the client is admitted to the recovery room, to the time he is transported back into the surgical unit, discharged from the hospital, until the follow-up care.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="reasons-for-surgery">Reasons for surgery<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#reasons-for-surgery" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>To cure an illness or disease by removing the diseased tissue or organs.</li>
<li>To visualize internal structures during diagnosis.</li>
<li>To obtain tissue for examination.</li>
<li>To prevent disease or injury.</li>
<li>To improve appearance.</li>
<li>To repair or remove traumatized tissue and structures.</li>
<li>To relieve symptoms or pain.</li>
</ul>
<p>There are 4 Major Types of Pathologic Process Requiring Surgical Intervention; (OPET)</p>
<ul>
<li>Obstruction – impairment to the flow of vital fluids (blood, urine, CSF, bile)</li>
<li>Perforation – rupture of an organ.</li>
<li>Erosion – wearing off of a surface or membrane.</li>
<li>Tumors – abnormal new growths.</li>
</ul>
<p>Examples of pathologic process requiring surgery; Hydrocephalus (Obstruction), Burn (Erosion), Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (Tumor), Cholelithiasis (Obstruction), Intussusceptions (Obstruction), Perforation (Ruptured Aneurysm).</p>

<p>Operative Consent</p>

<p>This is part of the legal preparation for surgery.
Informed consent is an active, shared decision making process between the provider and recipient of care.</p>
<p><strong>Purposes of informed consent:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>To ensure that the client understand the nature of the treatment including the potential complications and disfigurement (explained AMD)</li>
<li>To indicate that the client’s decision was made without pressure.</li>
<li>To protect the client against unauthorized procedure.</li>
<li>To protect the surgeon and hospital against legal action by a client who claims that an authorized procedure was performed.</li>
</ul>
<p>Has 3 components to make it valid:</p>
<ol>
<li>Adequate Disclosure of:</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>☑ The diagnosis,</li>
<li>☑ Nature and purpose of the proposed treatment,</li>
<li>☑ Probability of successful outcome,</li>
<li>☑ Risks and consequences of moving forward with treatment or alternatives,</li>
<li>☑ The prognosis if treatment is not instituted, and</li>
<li>☑ If treatment is deviating from standard for their condition.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="essential-elements-of-informed-consent">Essential Elements of Informed Consent<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#essential-elements-of-informed-consent" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>The diagnosis and explanation of the condition.</li>
<li>A fair explanation of the procedure to be done and used and the consequences.</li>
<li>A description of alternative treatment or procedure.</li>
<li>A description of the benefits to be expected.</li>
<li>The prognosis, if the recommended care, procedure is refused.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="informed-consent-cont">Informed Consent (cont):<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#informed-consent-cont" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ol start="2">
<li>
<p>Understanding and Comprehension of above: this has to be assessed before sedating medicines can be given (minors can't give consent, severely mentally ill or severely developmentally challenged).</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Voluntary Consent: Can't be coerced into going through with a procedure.</p>
</li>
</ol>
<p>This consent can be revoked at any point leading up to a surgical procedure.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="operative-consent">Operative Consent<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#operative-consent" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="who-can-give-consent">Who can give consent?<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#who-can-give-consent" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>The patient</li>
<li>Next of kin (in order of kinship): Spouse, Adult Child, Parent, Sibling</li>
<li>Can be designated with a durable power of attorney in case of medical incapacitation</li>
</ul>
<p>Who has the legal responsibility of obtaining consent?</p>
<p>The Physician</p>
<ul>
<li>The nurse is not legally required to obtain consent</li>
<li>However, the nurse must make sure the consent was signed</li>
<li>Nurse has a primary role as a patient advocate.</li>
<li>Nurse can "witness" the consent, and sign it as such</li>
<li>If the patient has questions that you can answer to clarify things, you can do that</li>
<li>If the patient continues to have questions, or there is a question that they are not voluntarily giving consent, the doctor needs to come and speak with them again.</li>
<li>Very important that patient is consenting voluntarily and with knowledge of the situation</li>
</ul>
<p>What about emergency treatment?</p>
<ul>
<li>A true medical emergency may override the need to obtain consent.</li>
<li>When medical care is needed to protect the life of an individual, the next of kin/POA (Power of Attorney) can give consent.</li>
<li>Also, if there is a known and available Advanced Directive with healthcare decision making instructions, that can be used to assist in justifying consent. (be sure with country legal system and institution’s policy)</li>
</ul>
<p>What about emergency treatment contd...?</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>If the above are not available, and the doctor deems the procedure necessary for life, the doctor can chart that it was necessary, and go ahead with the procedure.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The nurse may need to write up an incident report and state that the emergency caused a deviation in the normal policy to obtain consent on everyone</p>
</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="environmental-safety-in-surgical-suite-contd">Environmental Safety in surgical suite contd..<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#environmental-safety-in-surgical-suite-contd" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>a. Size of the Procedure Room</p>
<ul>
<li>Usually rectangular or square in shape</li>
<li>20 x 20 x 10ft. with a minimum floor space of 360 sq. ft</li>
<li>Each procedure room must have the following equipment:</li>
<li>Communication System</li>
<li>Oxygen and vacuum outlets</li>
<li>Mechanical ventilation assistance equipment</li>
<li>Respiratory and Cardiac monitoring equipment</li>
<li>X ray film illumination boxes</li>

<li>Adequate room lighting</li>
<li>Emergency lighting system</li>
</ul>
<p>Environmental Safety in surgical suite contd..</p>
<p>b. Temperature and Humidity Control</p>
<ul>
<li>The temperature in the procedure room should be maintained between 20 -24 degrees C</li>
<li>Humidity level between 50 -55 % at all times</li>
</ul>
<p>c. Ventilation and Air Exchange System</p>
<ul>
<li>Air exchange in each procedure room should be at least 25 air exchanges/ hour, and five of that should be fresh air.</li>
<li>A high filtration particulate filter, working at 95% efficiency.</li>
<li>Each procedure room should maintain positive pressure, which forces old air out of the room and prevents air from surrounding areas from entering into the procedure room</li>
</ul>
<p>Environmental Safety in surgical suite contd..</p>
<p>d. Electrical Safety</p>
<ul>
<li>Faulty wiring, excessive use of extension cords, poorly maintained equipment and lack of current safety measures are hazardous factors that must be constantly checked</li>
<li>All electrical equipment, new/used, should be routinely checked by qualified personnel.</li>
<li>Equipment that fails to function at 100% efficiency should be taken out of service immediately.</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="table-19-3-principles-of-basic-aseptic-technique-in-the-operating-room">TABLE 19-3 Principles of Basic Aseptic Technique in the Operating Room<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#table-19-3-principles-of-basic-aseptic-technique-in-the-operating-room" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ol>
<li>All materials that enter the sterile field must be sterile.</li>
<li>If a sterile item comes in contact with an unsterile item, it is contaminated.</li>
<li>Contaminated items should be removed immediately from the sterile field.</li>
<li>Sterile team members must wear only sterile gowns and gloves; once dressed for the procedure, they should recognize that the only parts of the gown considered sterile are the front from chest to table level and the sleeves to 2 inches above the elbow.</li>
<li>A wide margin of safety must be maintained between the sterile and unsterile fields.</li>
<li>Tables are considered sterile only at tabletop level; items extending beneath this level are considered contaminated.</li>
<li>The edges of a sterile package are considered contaminated once the package has been opened.</li>
<li>Bacteria travel on airborne particles and will enter the sterile field with excessive air movements and currents.</li>
<li>Bacteria travel by capillary action through moist fabrics and contamination occurs.</li>
<li>Bacteria harbor on the patient’s and the team members’ hair, skin, and respiratory tracts and must be confined by appropriate attire.</li>
</ol>

<li>Team members fingers and hands should be scrubbed first with progression to the forearm and elbows.</li>
<li>The hands should be held away from the surgical attire.</li>

<ol start="2">
<li>Regional anesthesia</li>
</ol>
<p>This is the injection of a local anesthetic in or around a specific nerve or group of nerves.</p>
<p>Reduce all painful sensation in one region of the body without inducing unconsciousness.</p>
<p>Agents used are lidocaine and bupivacaine.</p>
<p>Techniques used in Regional Anesthesia:</p>
<p>a. Topical Anesthesia</p>
<ul>
<li>Applied directly to the skin and mucous membrane, open skin surfaces, wounds and burns.</li>
<li>Readily absorbed and act rapidly.</li>
<li>Used topical agents are lidocaine and benzocaine.</li>
</ul>
<p>b. Nerve blocks: usually done for the palliation of pain</p>
<ul>
<li>celiac plexus block</li>
<li>brachial plexus block</li>
</ul>
<p>c. Spinal/Epidural Anesthetic: injection of a local anesthetic into either the subarachnoid space and CSF (spinal) or epidural space (epidural)</p>
<p>i. Spinal blocks: cause autonomic, sensory and motor blockade, used for lower abdomen, perineal, groin, or lower extremity
Agents used are procaine, tetracaine, lidocaine and bupivacaine.
Can cause hypotension and vasodilation, also spinal headaches</p>
<p>ii. Epidural blocks: anesthetic is given to the epidural space
Agents use are chloroprocaine, lidocaine and bupivacaine.
lower incidence of headache</p>
<p>d. Peripheral Nerve Block: achieved by injecting a local anesthetic to anesthetize the surgical site.
Agents use are chloroprocaine, lidocaine and bupivacaine</p>
<p>E. Intravenous Block (Beir block)</p>
<ul>
<li>Often used for arm, wrist and hand procedure</li>
<li>An occlusion tourniquet is applied to the extremity to prevent infiltration and absorption of the injected IV agents beyond the involved extremity.</li>
</ul>
<p>F. Caudal Anesthesia</p>
<ul>
<li>Is produced by injection of the local anesthetic into the caudal or sacral canal</li>
</ul>
<p>G. Field Block Anesthesia</p>
<ul>
<li>The area proximal to a planned incision can be injected and infiltrated with local anesthetic agents</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="complications-and-discomforts-of-anesthesia">Complications and Discomforts of Anesthesia<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#complications-and-discomforts-of-anesthesia" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>

<li>Hypoventilation - inadequate ventilatory support after paralysis of respiratory muscles.</li>
<li>Oral Trauma</li>
<li>Malignant Hyperthermia - uncontrolled skeletal muscle contraction</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="malignant-hyperthermia">Malignant Hyperthermia:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#malignant-hyperthermia" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="venous-thrombosis">Venous Thrombosis:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#venous-thrombosis" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Results from venous stasis (inactivity, body positioning, pressure, dehydration)</li>
<li>postoperative patients who are elderly or obese are at higher risk of developing DVTs</li>
<li>DVTs can embolize and travel to the lung and cause pulmonary emboli</li>
<li>Assess for swelling (usually unilateral) in the lower extremities, redness and pain</li>
<li>Provide passive ROM of the lower extremities, or encourage active ROM if the patient is able</li>
<li>Encourage early ambulation</li>
<li>Apply compression stockings/sequential compression devices and give anticoagulants as ordered.</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="urinary-retention">Urinary Retention:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#urinary-retention" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Can occur in the postoperative period because the anesthesia can depress the nervous system, and impede the sensation of bladder filling as well as interfere with the ability to void.</li>
<li>More likely to occur after lower abdominal or pelvic surgery</li>
<li>Need to assess for urine output, both color and amount, urine output should be 0.5ml/kg/hr, and the patient should urinate within 6-8 hours of surgery</li>
<li>Nurse should facilitate voiding by normal positioning of the patient to void</li>
<li>Provide privacy to void, running water, pouring warm water over a female's perineum can assist with the ability to void, and ambulating to the commode/toilet can help</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="low-urine-production">Low Urine Production:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#low-urine-production" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>The diminished output of urine can be a manifestation of renal failure and is less common</li>
<li>May result from renal ischemia from inadequate renal perfusion or altered cardiovascular function</li>
<li>Need to assess urine output, color and amount</li>
<li>should be 0.5ml/kg/hr, if below that, palpate and percuss the bladder for fullness and report to MD</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="paralytic-ileus">Paralytic Ileus:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#paralytic-ileus" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>This is caused by bowel manipulation, anesthesia affects on the bowel, immobility, and pain medicines</li>
<li>Assess for bowel distention, bowel sounds, presence of flatus, or stool, bowel sounds and nausea or vomiting</li>
<li>Maintain NPO status is patient is showing signs of paralytic ileus, teach patient the importance of the NPO status</li>
<li>May need to place an NG tube if ordered by MD, and manage per hospital protocol</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="common-postoperative-complications-gastrointestinal">Common postoperative complications: Gastrointestinal<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#common-postoperative-complications-gastrointestinal" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>

<ul>
<li>Same causes as paralytic ileus</li>
<li>Assess for bowel distention, bowel sounds, passage of flatus, stool (color, caliber, form), assess bowel sounds, assess for nausea and vomiting</li>
<li>Early ambulation can assist with this</li>
<li>Use of stool softeners, suppositories and enemas as prescribed</li>
<li>Harris flush for gas</li>
<li>Molasses enemas, soap suds enemas, mineral oil enemas</li>
<li>positioning on the right side allows the gas to move up the transverse colon and out the rectum</li>
</ul>

<li>Support the patient and the family</li>

<ul>
<li>This is related to the altered skin integrity, inadequate nutrition and fluid balance, presence of environmental pathogens, invasive instrumentation, and immobility</li>
<li>Assess for s/s of infection (wound, V/S)</li>
<li>Provide clean or aseptic wound care (wounds and drains)</li>
<li>Note the characteristics of drainage to determine infection</li>
<li>Good pulmonary toilet</li>
<li>Work with the dieticians to provide optimal nutrition for the patients</li>
</ul>
