ü©∫ Overview & Types

Anaesthesia (Greek for "without sensation") is a state of temporary induced loss of sensation. It may include analgesia, paralysis, amnesia, or unconsciousness.

Goals of Anaesthesia

  • Provide analgesia (pain relief).
  • Reduce anxiety and discomfort.
  • Control the autonomic nervous system.
  • Provide muscle relaxation appropriate for the procedure.

Types of Anaesthesia

Local Anaesthesia

Used for minor procedures (e.g., tooth extraction, skin surgery). Patient remains conscious. Methods include infiltration, nerve block, and field block.

Regional Anaesthesia

Blocks sensation in a larger region (arm, leg, abdomen).

  • Spinal: Injection into Dural sac (CSF). Immediate effect. Used for abdomen/legs.
  • Epidural: Injection into epidural space via catheter. Can be topped up.

General Anaesthesia

Complete loss of consciousness. Used for major operations.

‚è±Ô∏è Phases of General Anaesthesia

  1. 1
    Pre-anaesthetic Phase: From decision to operate until induction. Includes history, exam, investigations, and premedication.
  2. 2
    Induction Phase: Administration of anaesthetic medication (oxygen, parenteral/inhalation agents, relaxants) to induce unconsciousness.
  3. 3
    Maintenance Phase: Prolonging unconsciousness during surgery using maintenance drugs (Nitrous Oxide, Halothane, Propofol).
  4. 4
    Emergence/Reversal: Anaesthesia is stopped. Reversal agents (e.g., Atropine + Neostigmine) are given to counteract muscle relaxants.
  5. 5
    Recovery Phase: Patient regains wakefulness, spontaneous respiration, and mobility in the PACU.

üíä Pharmacology

Premedication

Administered before surgery to reduce secretions, anxiety, and pain.

Drug Dose/Route Purpose/Notes
Atropine 0.6mg IM (Adult)
0.3mg (Child)		 Reduces respiratory secretions and prevents bradycardia. Given 1hr pre-op.
Pethidine 50-100mg IM (Adult) Analgesic effect.
Diazepam Oral/IM Relieves anxiety (amnesia effect). Can be given night before.
üçΩÔ∏è NPO Rule

Ensure the patient observes 'Nil by Oral' for at least six hours prior to the operation to prevent aspiration.

Anaesthetic Agents

  • Inhalation: Halothane (good induction, risk of hepatitis), Ether (flammable), Trilene (analgesic effect).
  • Intravenous (IV):
    • Thiopentone (STP): Rapid sleep induction.
    • Ketamine: Analgesic effect, can cause bad dreams/high BP.
    • Propofol (Diprivan): "Milk of anaesthesia", good for short procedures.

Muscle Relaxants

  • Suxamethonium (Scoline): Short-acting (depolarising). Used for intubation. Side effect: Bradycardia.
  • Curare/Pancuronium: Long-acting (non-depolarising). Reversed by Neostigmine.

üöë Emergencies & Recovery Care

Common Anaesthetic Emergencies

  • Cardiac Arrest
  • Anaphylactic Shock
  • Respiratory Failure
  • Asphyxia
  • Hypothermia
  • Hypovolemic Shock

Post-operative Care Principles (PACU)

Care starts in the recovery area and continues to the ward.

  • Clear Airway: Place patient in recovery position (three-quarters prone/left-lateral) to drain secretions. Suction if necessary.
  • Circulation Support: Maintain IV fluids, monitor input/output to support heart and kidneys.
  • Bleeding Control: Check wound dressings. If bleeding occurs, apply firm pressure and inform surgeon.
  • Pain Control: Administer analgesics (e.g., Pethidine) once conscious.
  • GI Function: Watch for return of bowel sounds/flatus before oral intake.
  • Early Ambulation: Prevents Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT) and pulmonary embolism.
üìã PACU Discharge Criteria
  • Stable vital signs.
  • Adequate urine output (>30mls/hr).
  • Orientation to person, place, time.
  • Control of pain and vomiting.
  • Satisfactory response to commands.
