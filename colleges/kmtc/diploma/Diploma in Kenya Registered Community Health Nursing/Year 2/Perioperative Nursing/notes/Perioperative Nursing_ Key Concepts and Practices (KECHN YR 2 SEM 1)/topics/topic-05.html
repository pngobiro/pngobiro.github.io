üìú Legal Issues & Consent

Legal is defined as "required" or "permitted by law". In theatre nursing, this refers to statutory requirements before, during, and after operations.

Common Legal Issues

Issue Description
Negligence Failure to exercise care that a reasonable person would. Includes failure to provide adequate info on risks.
Inadequate Documentation Improper/incomplete notes or lack of documentation.
Confidentiality Managing patient care within legal privacy rights.
Security/Safety Responsibility of the theatre team to ensure patient safety despite signed consent forms.
‚úçÔ∏è Informed Consent Requirements

Consent must meet three criteria to be legally valid:

  1. Legally Valid: Given by a person above 18 years (in Kenya), of sound mind, and voluntary (not coerced).
  2. Competent Administration: Administered by a competent doctor.
  3. Informed: Patient must understand the risks and procedure.

Note: Consent should be obtained BEFORE pre-medication, as drugs may affect reasoning capacity.

üåü Bioethical Principles

Perioperative nurses must recognize ethical dilemmas and take appropriate action. The code of ethics expresses the moral commitment to uphold the goals, values, and obligations of the profession.

Autonomy

Self-determination; respect for individual's right to make voluntary decisions.

Beneficence

The principle of doing good and acting in the best interest of the patient.

Non-maleficence

"Do no harm." Balancing potential good against potential harm.

Justice

Fairness; treating individuals equally regardless of status, race, or religion.

Veracity

Truth-telling; being honest to build trust and support autonomy.

Fidelity

Confidentiality and keeping promises.

ü§ù Patient Rights & Cultural Competence

Respect for People

The nurse practices with compassion and respect for the inherent dignity, worth, and uniqueness of every individual, unrestricted by social or economic status.

Supporting Choices

  • Self-Determination: Patients have the right to refuse procedures even against medical advice.
  • Advocacy: The nurse acts as a moral agent, advocating for patient rights and empowering them through education.
  • Age-Specific Care: Nurses often act as parental surrogates for children, balancing "best interests" with potential harm (e.g., pain management).

Cultural Competence

Nurses must be sensitive to values, beliefs, and health practices of different cultures. This includes:

  • Using language interpreters.
  • Allowing family presence when appropriate to alleviate anxiety.
  • Permitting cultural/religious items (e.g., rosary) in the OR (often in a plastic bag to maintain sterility).

üîç Case Study: Ethical Dilemma

üìÑ Scenario

A 44-year-old woman is scheduled for a vaginal hysterectomy. Preoperatively, she expresses extreme shyness and cultural concerns about privacy, requesting minimal staff in the room. As she transfers to the OR bed, the surgeon arrives with his 14-year-old son, stating it's "Take Your Child to Work Day" and asks if the son can scrub in to watch. The patient appears paralyzed and unable to answer, locking eyes with the nurse.

Analysis

The Ethical Conflict:

  • Autonomy & Duress: Asking for consent immediately before a procedure puts the patient under duress. Freedom to refuse is compromised.
  • Privacy & Dignity: The patient explicitly requested privacy based on cultural beliefs.
  • Safety Risks: An untrained observer (the son) poses contamination risks and potential injury (fainting).

Nursing Action Required

The perioperative nurse must advocate for the patient immediately:

  1. 1 Postpone Induction: Stop anesthesia induction immediately.
  2. 2 Private Discussion: Speak with the surgeon privately to explain the patient's prior concerns and the issue of coercion.
  3. 3 Reassurance: Reassure the patient that her wishes are honored and the observer will leave.
