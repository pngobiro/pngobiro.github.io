ü©∫ Preoperative Evaluation

A comprehensive preoperative evaluation is essential for patient safety and successful surgical outcomes. This process involves thorough documentation and clinical assessment.

  • History & Documentation: Complete history including menstrual, reproductive, sexual, allergies, and chronic conditions.
  • Clinical Examination: General head-to-toe assessment, vital signs, weight, height, and systems review.
  • Investigations: Full Haemogram, Urea and electrolytes, Liver Function Tests (LFTs), Blood cross-match, Random Blood Sugar (RBS), etc. All blood works should be within acceptable ranges.
‚úçÔ∏è Informed Consent
  • You should make sure that the surgeon explains clearly to the patient what will happen to them.
  • The surgeon should obtain an informed consent from the patient or parent/guardian/next of kin for those under age 18 in Kenya or not in a position to sign (e.g. unconscious person, mentally ill).
  • The nurse ensures that the patient has signed an informed consent, after the surgeon has explained the advantages and outcomes of the operation.

Physical Preparation & Protocols

  • NPO Status: Make sure that the patient observes a ‚ÄòNil by oral‚Äô rule. The fasting should usually start six hours before the operation.
  • Counseling: The patient should be counselled and reassured especially those receiving operations such as amputation, or mastectomy.
  • Skin Preparation: The site to be operated on should be shaved of hair and cleaned with warm soapy water, to reduce the bacteria on the patient's skin. The area shaved should be larger than the incision site.
  • Pre-procedures: Catheterisation and IV branula insertion may be necessary depending on the surgery.
  • Monitoring: Observations of vital signs, urine testing for sugars, proteins and acetone should be done.
  • Stabilization: Manage and treat/stabilize any pre-existing medical condition.
  • Lifestyle: Give up smoking 6/52 or at least over 48 hours to reduce carboxyl haemoglobin.
  • Reviews: Pre-anaesthetic review and Drug history.

Effective counseling helps alleviate anxiety and prepares the patient mentally for the procedure.

  • Patient has a right to know the extend and risks of any intended surgery.
  • An outline of the immediate postoperative recovery should be provided.

üî™ Surgery Fundamentals

Definition: This is a planned alteration of physiologic processes within the body in an attempt to arrest or eliminate disease or illness.

Purpose of Surgery

Type Description/Purpose
Diagnostic Removing a tissue for testing in the lab to determine whether cancerous or not.
Curative Performed to remove e.g. cancer from the body.
Ablative Destruction of tissue that is allowing incorrect electrical signals (e.g. causing abnormal heart rhythm).
Restorative Used to follow-up to other surgeries to change/restore patient‚Äôs appearance or of an organ/body part.
Reconstructive Restoration of normal function, appearance and deformity correction.
Palliative Help alleviate discomfort and minimise problems arising from the tumor itself or CA treatment but not to cure.
Debulking Removing as much of the cancerous tumour as possible, then radiation and chemotherapy follow for the remaining part. Done in situations when removing the whole tumour may cause too much damage to the organ or surrounding areas.

Classifications of Surgery

Elective

Planned in advance, not an immediate emergency.

Urgent

Required within a short period (e.g., 24-48 hours).

Emergency

Must be performed immediately to save life or limb.
