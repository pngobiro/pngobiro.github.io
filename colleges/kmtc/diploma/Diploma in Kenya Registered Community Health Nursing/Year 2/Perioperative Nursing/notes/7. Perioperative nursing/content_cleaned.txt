<ul>
<li>Theatre nursing has developed alongside the history of surgery.</li>
<li>Surgery is an old form of treatment that can be traced back through the history of man.</li>
<li>In the past, there were no theatres, no trained personnel, no anaesthesia and no equipment.</li>
<li>Operations were performed at home.</li>
<li>Problems during this time included <strong>infection, bleeding and pain</strong>.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="history-of-perioperative-nursing">History of perioperative nursing<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#history-of-perioperative-nursing" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>In 17 BC, alcohol and opium were used to relieve pain by Napoleon who performed an amputation while the patient slept for 24 hours.</li>
<li>By 1772, Joseph Priestly discovered the use of nitrous oxide as anaesthesia, and in 1842, Dr Crawford discovered the use of ether.</li>
<li>In 1847 James Young began to use chloroform.</li>
<li>In the 18th century a great breakthrough was made with the use of trilene thiopentone, clytopopaine and curare, which are muscle relaxants.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="history-of-perioperative-nursing">History of perioperative nursing<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#history-of-perioperative-nursing" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>By the end of 19th century, pain relief was an integral part of surgery</li>
<li>In order to control haemorrhaging, the ancient Greeks and Romans as far back as the 16th century BC, used strings as ligatures.</li>
<li>Later on, during the Middle Ages, they came up with the use of hot iron.</li>
<li>This idea has been developed into the use of cautery to control bleeding.</li>
<li>By the beginning of the 20th century, many types of ligatures were available, prepared from metal, nylon and cotton</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="history-of-perioperative-nursing">History of perioperative nursing<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#history-of-perioperative-nursing" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>The control of infection dates back to the efforts of Louis Pasteur, who proved that bacteria caused infections.</li>
<li>In 1865, Joseph Lister used carbonic acid to reduce the growth of bacteria in wounds.</li>
<li>In 1886 Von Bergemen introduced sterilisation of dressings.</li>
<li>Gloves were introduced in surgery in 1890.</li>
<li>Operating theatre nursing is a special branch of nursing.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="history-of-perioperative-nursing">History of perioperative nursing<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#history-of-perioperative-nursing" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>The theatre nurse has evolved together with the development of the theatre.</li>
<li>They are a member of a bigger team, all of whom work together to provide a safe passage through the operating theatre for every patient.</li>
<li>However small or insignificant the task to be performed, the theatre nurse is responsible for the success of the procedure.</li>
<li>They must, therefore, be <strong>highly skilled and trained</strong>, in order to be able to ensure a successful outcome for the patient.</li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li>Usually the treatment of a wide variety of illnesses and injuries includes some type of surgical intervention.</li>
<li>Surgery is an invasive method of treatment that may be planned or unplanned, major or minor, and that may involve any body part or system.</li>
<li>Surgical procedures require physical and psychosocial adaptations and are stressors for both the patient and the family, no matter what the extent might be.</li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li>The patient’s recovery from a surgical procedure requires skillful and knowledgeable nursing care whether the surgery is done on an outpatient basis or in the ideal operation room.</li>
<li>All phases of the nursing process are used perioperatively to make assessments, arrive at a diagnosis, make appropriate plans and provide interventions necessary to:</li>
<li>Promote the recovery of health,</li>
<li>Prevent further injury or illness, and</li>
<li>Facilitate coping with alterations in physical structure and function</li>
</ul>

<p>Perioperative patient care requires developing a plan of care and identifying expected outcomes through the nursing process</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Begins when the patient is informed of the need for surgery, includes the surgical procedure and recovery and continues until the patient resumes his or her usual activities.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>It is divided into three phases:</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Preoperative Nursing</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Intraoperative nursing</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Postoperative Nursing</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Throughout the entire perioperative period, the patient is continually assessed, the plan of care is modified, implementation is effected, and the cycle is evaluated for the attainment of outcomes</p>
</li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li>Operating Room Nurse is on the frontline of the most complex life-saving procedures.</li>
<li>She/he is responsible for planning, directing and coordinating activities of the operating room.</li>
<li>The OR nurse provides professional nursing care to patients undergoing surgery; prepares and maintains the operating room for all surgical procedures.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="functions-of-or-nurse">Functions of OR Nurse<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#functions-of-or-nurse" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Assembles the type and quality of materials and special equipment needed for daily procedures and possible emergencies</li>
<li>The OR nurse conducts both pre- and postoperative visits on patients.</li>
<li>She/he also coordinates with other units like recovery room (RR), intensive care unit (ICU), obstetric and nursing units to ensure patients' progress</li>
<li>Generally the OR nurse plans, directs, and coordinates activities of the OR, maintaining optimum standards of nursing care and practice through preoperative and postoperative assessments, planning, implementation, and evaluation of patient care</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="functions-of-or-nurse">Functions of OR Nurse<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#functions-of-or-nurse" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>The OR nurse manages operating room activities, directing and instructing personnel</li>
<li>Maintaining concise and complete records and reports,</li>
<li>Coordinating an ongoing in-service education program to continually update the knowledge and skills of all assigned operating room nursing personnel</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="phases-of-the-perioperative-period">Phases of the perioperative period<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#phases-of-the-perioperative-period" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Preoperative phase – from the time the decision is made for surgical intervention to the transfer of the patient to the operating room.</li>
<li>Intraoperative phase – from the time the patient is received in the operating room until s/he is admitted to the recovery room.</li>
<li>Postoperative phase – from the time of admission to the recovery room to the follow-up home or clinic evaluation</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="design-of-the-operating-room">Design Of The Operating Room<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#design-of-the-operating-room" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>The efficiency of the operating room depends much upon its physical organization and the organization of its personnel.</li>
<li>An intelligent design in the layout of the operating room facilitates the efficient movement of patients and staff and the economical use of space</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="principles-of-design">Principles of design<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#principles-of-design" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>The universal problem of environmental control to prevent wound infection exerts a great influence on the design of the operating room (OR) suite.</li>
<li>Clean and contaminated areas should be well differentiated.</li>
<li>Architects follow two principles in planning the physical layout of the OR suite:</li>
<li>Exclusion of contamination from outside the suite with sensible traffic patterns within the suite</li>
<li>Separation of clean areas from contaminated areas within the suite</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="principles-of-design">Principles of design<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#principles-of-design" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Physical planning of an OR suite, which separates clean from contaminated areas, makes it easier to carry out good aseptic techniques.</li>
<li>The clean area is often referred to as the restricted area</li>
<li>For operating rooms, there are many different designs.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="principles-of-design">Principles of design<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#principles-of-design" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>The basic design principles which are common to all operating rooms must fulfill the following criteria:</li>
</ul>
<ol>
<li>The design must always be <strong>simple</strong> and easy to keep it clean</li>
<li>Wall and floor surfaces should be <strong>smooth</strong> and made of nonporous materials</li>
<li>In order to prevent cross-contamination (the transfer of disease causing microorganisms from one source to another), there should be separate rooms for clean or sterile instruments and soiled ones</li>
<li>There should be <strong>sufficient</strong> space to ensure the <strong>safe</strong> transportation of patients and staff</li>
</ol>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="principles-of-design">Principles of design<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#principles-of-design" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ol start="5">
<li>
<p>The layout of the department should be convenient for the supervisor to control the incoming and outgoing traffics.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The recovery room should be near the operating room, so that patients can be transported safely and quickly following surgery</p>
</li>
</ol>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="space-allocation">SPACE ALLOCATION<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#space-allocation" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Space is allocated within the OR suite to provide for the work to be done, with consideration given to the efficiency with which it can be accomplished</li>
<li>The OR suite should be large enough to allow for correct technique, yet small enough to minimize the movement of patients, personnel, and supplies.</li>
<li>Provision must be made for traffic control</li>
<li>All persons – staff, patients, and visitors – should follow the delineated patterns in appropriate attire</li>
<li>Signs should be posted that clearly indicate the attire and environmental controls required</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="space-allocation">SPACE ALLOCATION<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#space-allocation" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>The OR suite is divided into three areas that are designated by the physical activities performed in each area</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="a-unrestricted-area">A. Unrestricted Area<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#a-unrestricted-area" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Street cloths are permitted.</li>
<li>A corridor on the periphery accommodates traffic from outside, including patients.</li>
<li>This area is isolated by doors from the main corridor and from other areas of OR suite.</li>
<li>It serves as an outside-to-inside access area.</li>
<li>Traffic, although not limited, is monitored at a central location.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="b-semi-restricted-area">B. <ul>
<li>Traffic is limited to properly attired (dressed) personnel</li>
<li>Body and head coverings are required</li>
<li>This area includes peripheral support areas and access corridors to the operating rooms.</li>
<li>The patient may be transferred to a clean inside stretcher on entry to this area.</li>
<li>The patient’s hair must be covered</li>
</ul>
<p>c. Restricted area</p>
<ul>
<li>Masks are required to supplement surgical attire</li>
<li>Sterile procedures are carried out in this area</li>
<li>The area includes the operating rooms, scrub sink areas and substerile rooms or clean core area(s) where unwrapped supplies are sterilized</li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li>Wall Clock: Since time is often critical during surgery, each room should have a wall clock that is easy to read. The clock is used to time tourniquet applications, administration of medications, the duration of cardiac and respiratory arrests and to note the time of events such as childbirth</li>
<li>X-ray Viewing Boxes: The surgeon may need to view an x-ray before or during the procedure</li>
<li>Lights: The overhead lights should specially designed to provide a range of intensity. They should be freely movable, shadowless and less heat emitting</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="operating-room-equipment-and-furniture">Operating room equipment and furniture<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#operating-room-equipment-and-furniture" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>

<li>The Operating Table: The table should be fully adjustable in all directions to create postures needed for various surgical positions.</li>
<li>Mayo Stands: This stand is used to hold instruments that will be used frequently during a particular case.</li>
<li>Back Table: The back table is used to place extra supplies and instruments used during surgery</li>
<li>Anesthesia equipment: Equipment, including the gas machine, physiological monitor, anesthesia supply cart, and sitting stools, is located in each room</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="operating-room-equipment-and-furniture">Operating room equipment and furniture<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#operating-room-equipment-and-furniture" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Ring Stand: The ring stand is used to hold basins which contains normal saline or sterile water during surgery.</li>
<li>Kick Bucket: The kick bucket (a bucket on wheels) is used to place soiled sponges during surgery.</li>
<li>Supply Cabinets: These cabinets are used to store frequently used items such as drapes, dressings, solutions, sutures, etc. Cabinets with doors are preferred to those without so as to reduce exposure of the content to dust.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="operating-room-personnel">OPERATING ROOM PERSONNEL<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#operating-room-personnel" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>When the patient arrives to the operating room, he/she is received and surrounded by a surgeon, one or two assistants, an anesthesia provider, a scrubbed nurse, a circulating nurse etc.</li>
<li>These individuals, each with specific functions to perform, form the operating team.</li>
<li>This team literally has the patient's life in its hands. The operating room team works in harmony with his/her colleagues for the successful accomplishment of the expected outcomes of the patient.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="operating-room-personnel">OPERATING ROOM PERSONNEL<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#operating-room-personnel" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>The operating room team is subdivided according to the functions of its members:</li>
</ul>
<ol>
<li>
<p>The sterile team consists of:
a. Surgeon
b. Assistants to the surgeon
c. Scrub nurse</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The unsterile team includes:
a. Anesthesia provider
b. Circulator/ Runner nurse
c. Others, such as students, cleaners and those who may be needed to set up and operate specialized equipment or monitoring devices</p>
</li>
</ol>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="responsibilities-of-each-personnel">RESPONSIBILITIES OF EACH PERSONNEL<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#responsibilities-of-each-personnel" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Sterile team members wash (scrub) their hands and arms, put on (don) a sterile gown and gloves, and enter the sterile field; the sterile field is the area of the operating room that immediately surrounds and is specially prepared for the patient</li>
<li>Unsterile team members: do not enter the sterile field; they function outside and around it.</li>
<li>They assume responsibility for maintaining sterile technique during the surgical procedure, but they handle supplies and equipment that are not considered sterile</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="a-responsibilities-of-the-surgeon">A. Responsibilities of the Surgeon<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#a-responsibilities-of-the-surgeon" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Preoperative diagnosis and care</li>
<li>Selection and performance of the surgical procedure</li>
<li>Postoperative management of care</li>
</ul>
<p>b. Responsibilities of the assistant Surgeon</p>
<ul>
<li>Under the direction of the operating surgeon, one or two assistants help to:</li>
<li>Maintain visibility of the surgical site</li>
<li>Control bleeding</li>
<li>Close wounds</li>
<li>Apply dressings</li>
</ul>
<p>C. Responsibilities of the Scrub Nurse</p>
<ul>
<li>Reviews anatomy, physiology, and the surgical procedure.</li>
<li>Assists with preparation of the room.</li>
<li>Scrubs, gowns, and gloves self and other members of the sterile surgical team.</li>
<li>Passes instrument to the surgeon in a prescribed manner.</li>
<li>Maintains sterile and an orderly surgical field.</li>
<li>Assists with the draping procedure.</li>
<li>Keeps track of irrigation solutions used for calculation of blood loss</li>
</ul>
<p>C. Responsibilities of the Scrub Nurse</p>
<ul>
<li>Keeps the instrument table neat so that supplies can be handed quickly and efficiently.</li>
<li>Anticipates and meets the needs of the surgeon by watching the progress of the surgery and knowing the various steps of the procedure.</li>
<li>Takes part in sponge, needle, and instrument counts</li>
<li>Identifies and preserves specimens properly</li>
</ul>
<p>d. Responsibilities of the Circulating Nurse</p>
<ul>
<li>Maintaining a neat, quiet, well-organized OR and must be able to anticipate and meet the needs of the other team members such as – the scrub nurse, the anesthesia provider, the surgeon and above all the patient.</li>
<li>Reviews anatomy, physiology, and the surgical procedure.</li>
<li>Assists with preparing the room, observes aseptic technique at all times to see that it is maintained properly.</li>
<li>Identifies and assesses the patient. Then plans and coordinates the intraoperative cares.</li>
</ul>
<p>d. Responsibilities of the Circulating Nurse</p>
<ul>
<li>Admits the patient to the operating room and assumes responsibility with the other members of the team for the comfort and the safety of the patient.</li>
<li>Keeps the "scrub" nurse with supplies e.g. suture materials, dressings etc.</li>
<li>Opens sterile supplies before and during the case, replace saline or water in basins as necessary.</li>
<li>Positions the patient on the surgery table</li>
<li>Assists the anesthetist when required</li>
</ul>
<p>d. Responsibilities of the Circulating Nurse</p>
<ul>
<li>Takes part in sponge and instrument counts and their documentation,</li>
<li>Ties the gowns of scrubbed personnel</li>
<li>Adjusts the surgical lights; attaches the suction apparatus and check to see its function; participates in insertion and application of monitoring devices.</li>
<li>Handles all nonsterile equipment in the room during surgery, places buckets properly to receive discarded sponges.</li>
</ul>
<p>d. Responsibilities of the Circulating Nurse</p>
<ul>
<li>Accompanies the patient to the recovery room.</li>
<li>Checks the chart and relates pertinent data</li>
<li>Measures blood and fluid loss</li>
<li>Documents and preserves any specimens received during surgery</li>
<li>Reports pertinent information to the recovery area nurses.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="circulating-nurse">Circulating nurse<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#circulating-nurse" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>From the responsibilities listed, it is essential that the circulating nurse must possess the ability to:</li>
<li>Organize activities and direct personnel with due understanding of interpersonal relations,</li>
<li>Anticipate needs,</li>
<li>Differentiate between situations that demand immediate attention and those of lesser importance,</li>
<li>Maintain a quiet, neat and well equipped unit,</li>
<li>Understand thoroughly the principles of asepsis,</li>
<li>Teach actively and by the examples he/she sets.</li>
</ul>
<p>E. Responsibilities of the Anesthesia Provider</p>
<ul>
<li>Anesthesia and surgery are two distinct, but inseparable disciplines; they are two parts of one entity.</li>
<li>Adequate communication between the surgeon and the anesthesia provider is the patient’s greater safeguard.</li>
<li>The anesthesia provider is an indispensable member of the OR team.</li>
<li>Functioning as a guardian of the patient, the anesthesia provider should observe the principles of aseptic technique.</li>
</ul>
<p>E. Responsibilities of the Anesthesia Provider</p>
<ul>
<li>The main activities of the anesthesia provider are:</li>
<li>Monitoring vital functions and parameters</li>
<li>Fluid and electrolyte administration</li>
<li>Administering anesthetic agent/anesthesia</li>
<li>Maintaining anesthesia at the required levels</li>
<li>Managing untoward reactions to anesthesia throughout the surgical procedure.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="recovery-room-nurse">Recovery room nurse<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#recovery-room-nurse" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Observe the general condition of the patient and the vital signs (temperature, blood pressure, pulse, respiration) on reception of patient every fifteen minutes.</li>
<li>A rise or fall in any of the vital signs indicates all is not well with the patient and alert the anaesthetist.</li>
<li>You should observe and ensure postoperative blood transfusion and other infusions are running as required.</li>
<li>You should prepare all the equipment and medications required in the recovery area.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="recovery-room-nurse">Recovery room Nurse<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#recovery-room-nurse" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Monitor and record both fluid input and output. This helps you to monitor kidney functions.</li>
<li>A decrease in urine, or lack of its production, calls for urgent action. Should this happen, inform the surgeon immediately.</li>
<li>If excess fluid runs in intravenously, administering a diuretic drug induces diuresis. This is recommended.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="theatre-attendant">Theatre Attendant<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#theatre-attendant" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Duties as a theatre attendant include cleaning the sluice room thoroughly and washing all the instruments after an operation.</li>
<li>Should also clean the mackintosh and arrange all the instruments for packing ready for sterilisation.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="nurse-administrator">Nurse administrator<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#nurse-administrator" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>The nurse administrator is the overall administrator of the theatre and sees that all staff and patients are safe.</li>
<li>They ensure that every area in theatre is satisfactorily staffed for 24 hours and the staff work as required.</li>
<li>This person should orientate new staff in theatre, and ensure availability of equipment needed in theatre.</li>
<li>They should also liaise with the specific wards and other departments for the smooth running of the theatre.</li>
<li>Finally they should maintain discipline in theatre.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="preoperative-care">Preoperative care<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#preoperative-care" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>The patient comes from the ward to the receiving area. They are then moved to the anaesthetic room, operating room, recovery ward, back transfer, and finally back to the ward.</li>
<li>The receiving area is where the ward nurse identifies the patient to the theatre nurse, discusses and hands over the patient's notes, and formally hands over the patient.</li>
<li>You should note that at this point the patient is usually apprehensive and hence needs to be reassured again.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="preoperative-care">Preoperative care<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#preoperative-care" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>You should check the patient’s identification bands, name on the notes, and in patient number (IP No.). All these should correspond.</li>
<li>Check whether the consent form is the correct one and is correctly signed, and that the consent obtained is relevant to the operation about to take place.</li>
<li>Check what pre-medication was given and indicated by ticking and signing, noting the time it was given on the preoperative checklist.</li>
<li>The patient should then be transferred from the ward trolley to the theatre trolley. Make a physical check that the patient has been prepared and tick the patient traffic in theatre list.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="preoperative-care">Preoperative care<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#preoperative-care" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Check for x-rays if indicated.</li>
<li>It is the responsibility of the ward nurse to check for blood from the blood bank and to bring it to theatre.</li>
<li>If these things are not properly done, patients should not be received.</li>
<li>The recovery area nurse should observe the patients waiting to go to the wards while still under general anaesthesia.</li>
<li>Observe for any abnormality, that is, the wound, vital signs and report any abnormalities to the anaesthetist or the surgeon</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="infection-prevention-in-theatre">INFECTION PREVENTION IN THEATRE<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#infection-prevention-in-theatre" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="principles-of-ipc">Principles of IPC<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#principles-of-ipc" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Consider every person potentially infectious and susceptible to infection.</li>
<li>Washing hands before and after any procedure is the most practical procedure for preventing cross contamination.</li>
<li>Donning (wearing) gloves before touching anything potentially infectious and wet such as broken skin, mucous membrane, body fluids, body secretions and excretions, or soiled instruments and other items – or before performing invasive procedures.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="principles-of-ipc">Principles of IPC<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#principles-of-ipc" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Using personal protective equipment (PPE) to provide barriers, if splashes or spills of any blood, body fluids, secretions or excretions are anticipated</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Using antiseptic agents for cleansing the skin or mucous membrane prior to surgery, cleaning wounds, or doing hand rubs or surgical hand scrub.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Using safe work practices, such as not recapping or bending needles, safely passing sharp instruments, and disposing sharps in puncture resistant containers</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Processing instruments and other items that come in contact with blood, body fluids, secretions and excretions.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Routinely cleaning and disinfecting equipment and furniture in patient care areas.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Disposing contaminated materials and contaminated waste properly</p>
</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="preparation-of-the-operating-room">Preparation of the Operating Room<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#preparation-of-the-operating-room" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>The theatre and equipment must be cleaned thoroughly every morning to minimise the number of micro-organisms.</li>
<li>Ensure high dusting of walls and clean trolleys, drip stand, operating tables and all equipment therein.</li>
<li>You should also ensure that the floor is scrubbed with soapy water and then mopped with a disinfectant recommended by the hospital.</li>
<li>After cleaning and drying the theatre floor, all the equipment must be returned to its proper place</li>
</ul>

</li>
<li>
<p>The medical and nurse practice acts are generally part of the written documents of the country's criminal statutes.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The acts make it a crime for unlicensed person to practice medicine or nursing.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The healthcare providers could face criminal charges if something goes wrong that causes the patient to suffer.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Patient Property:</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Patients occasionally arrive in the operating room with valuables, most commonly wedding rings, bracelets, ear rings, and necklaces.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Care should be taken to protect these items from loss or theft.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>A record of all property taken from the patient should be made, listing the items and either their place of storage or the person to whom they were given. This can prevent both loss and charges of theft</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Negligence</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Acts of carelessness are termed negligence.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>It is legally defined as the failure to exercise the care that a reasonably prudent person would exercise under similar circumstances.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The operating team members must be aware of situations in which carelessness could endanger the patient.</p>
</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="anesthesia">ANESTHESIA<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#anesthesia" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="introduction">Introduction<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#introduction" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Anaesthesia is the loss of pain and sensation to a part or the whole body induced by drugs.</li>
<li>There are two types of anaesthesia:</li>
<li>Local anaesthesia</li>
<li>General Anaesthesia</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="local-anesthesia">Local Anesthesia<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#local-anesthesia" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Local anaesthesia induces analgesia in the region where it is administered, for example, lignocaine, procaine hydrochloride, xylocaine and lidocaine.</li>
<li>The local anaesthesia last for forty five minutes to three hours depending on the type of anaesthesia used.</li>
<li>It is given locally to the affected part of the body by one of the following methods: Infiltration, nerve block, field block, refrigeration analgesia, spinal analgesia, epidural anaesthesia.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="local-anesthesia-methods">Local anesthesia methods<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#local-anesthesia-methods" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>
<p><strong>Infiltration</strong>
The drug is injected on and around (in various points of) the affected area.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Nerve Block</strong>
The nerve supplying the affected area is infiltrated by the anaesthetic drugs, inducing loss of sensation on the affected area supplied by that specific nerve.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Field Block</strong>
Similar to nerve block but cover a larger area and may involve more than one nerve.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="local-anesthesia">Local Anesthesia<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#local-anesthesia" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Refrigeration Analgesia
It is administered by use of a vapouriser. Drugs used include: Ethyl chloride or Diethyl ether.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Spinal Analgesia
Used for operations from the abdomen and below, e.g. caesarean section. A lumbar puncture is done and the local anaesthesia introduced through the spine. The drug paralyses the area below the puncture.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Epidural Anaesthesia
The drug is injected in the dura mater space of the spinal cord. Used for operations of the abdomen and below.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="general-anesthesia">General Anesthesia<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#general-anesthesia" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>General anaesthesia causes the patient to lose consciousness, for example, thiopentone and halothane.</li>
<li>Anaesthesia can be categorised into: pre-medication, preoperative and postoperative procedures</li>
</ul>
<p>a. Pre-medication</p>
<ul>
<li>The following procedures should be adhered to prior to the operation:</li>
<li>Atropine 0.6mg intramuscular (for adults) administered one hour before the operation to reduce Respiratory Secretion (RS) and to prevent bradycardia; Children should be given 0.3mg.</li>
<li>Pethidine 50 - 100mg intramuscular for adults, which has an analgesic effect on the patient; and 25 - 50mg for children depending on age and weight.</li>
<li>Valium can be given one night before to a very nervous patient.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="a1aa0c7e">Pre-medication …<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#a1aa0c7e" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Hyoscine 0.4mg for adults, which can also be given for pre-medication although it has the potential side effect of amnesia.</li>
<li>Morphine 10-15mg intramuscular can also be used.</li>
<li>Oral pre-medication is the best for children and should be administered two hours before operation.</li>
<li>Remember to make the patient observe nil by mouth for six hours prior to operation.</li>
</ul>
<p>b. Pre-operative Anesthesia</p>
<ul>
<li>Volatile Agents (Inhalations)</li>
<li>Volatile agents include ether, which is highly inflammable in the presence of diathermy and irritates the respiratory tract. It is cheap to administer.</li>
<li>Halothane is very good as an induction agent but can cause halothane hepatitis.</li>
<li>Trilene is not a very good induction agent but is a good maintenance anaesthetic agent. Its side effects include tachypnoea and vomiting. However it has a good analgesic effect postoperatively and it is cheap.</li>
<li>A mixture of O2 and NO2 and one of the volatile anaesthetic agents, is the best way of maintaining anaesthesia.</li>
</ul>
<p>b. Pre-operative Anesthesia</p>
<ul>
<li>Intravenous Agents</li>
<li>Intravenous agents include barbiturates, for example, thiopentone, which causes sleep very quickly.</li>
<li>Methohexitone can be used as an induction agent but cannot be used without equipment for resuscitation and is contraindicated in epilepsy.</li>
<li>These are mainly sedative drugs thus they do not have any analgesic effect.</li>
<li>Ketamine can be given IV or IM. It has an analgesic effect and can be used alone in minor surgeries. Side effects include bad dreams and elevated blood pressure.</li>
<li>Ketamine is also used with valium. It is contraindicated in hypertension</li>
</ul>
<p>b. Pre-operative Anesthesia</p>
<ul>
<li>Muscle Relaxants</li>
</ul>
<p>Muscle relaxants can be divided into two categories.</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Short acting (depolarising) relaxants include suxamethonium (scoline), which is mainly used for intubation. Its main side effect is that it causes bradycardia.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Long acting (non-depolarising) relaxants include curare, flaxedil and pancuronium. The action of these agents has to be reversed to revive the patient by neostigmine atropine.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>b. Pre-operative Anesthesia</p>
<ul>
<li>Analgesics</li>
<li>Analgesics are used to relieve pain and include pethidine, morphine and fentanyl.</li>
<li>The postoperative patient is given a drug for pain relief, for example, pethidine or valium, and an anti-emetic for instance, plasil (metoclopropamide), stemetil or Phenergan (promethazine).</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="1-ensuring-clear-airway">1. Ensuring Clear Airway<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#1-ensuring-clear-airway" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>You should place the patient in recovery position (three-quarters prone, or left-lateral).</li>
<li>This allows secretions from the lungs and mouth to drain out. Suck the secretions using a suction machine if they are excessive.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="2-supporting-circulation">2. Supporting Circulation<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#2-supporting-circulation" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>This is done in order to maintain the functions of the lungs, the heart and the kidney. This is achieved through adequate blood volume.</li>
<li>You should maintain the infusion running at the required rates.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="principles-of-postoperative-care">Principles of postoperative care<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#principles-of-postoperative-care" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="3-supporting-circulation">3. Supporting Circulation<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#3-supporting-circulation" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>In an adult, the body requires 35ml per kg body weight in 24 hours.</li>
<li>The insensible loss (loss through skin, normal faeces and breathing) is approximately 0.5ml per kg body weight per hour.</li>
<li>In children these figures vary by age as follows:</li>
<li>Below three months old, the maintenance requirement is 5mls/kg/hour, or 150ml/kg/24 hours</li>
<li>Infants above three months and weighing between 3-10kg require 5ml/kg/hour</li>
<li>Those weighing 10-20kg need 3ml/kg/hour</li>
<li>Children above 20kg need 2.5ml/kg/hour (Watters et al 1991)</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="principles-of-postoperative-care">Principles of postoperative care<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#principles-of-postoperative-care" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="4-controlling-bleeding-and-wound-care">4. Controlling Bleeding and Wound Care<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#4-controlling-bleeding-and-wound-care" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Monitor the wound for any signs of bleeding. Should this occur, apply a firm dressing and inform the surgeon.</li>
<li>After 24 hours, check for signs of infection, these include redness, tenderness, oedema and low grade fever.</li>
<li>If this occurs the sutures are removed to allow the pus to drain and the wound cleaned three times a day with antiseptic lotion.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="principles-of-postoperative-care">Principles of postoperative care<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#principles-of-postoperative-care" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="5-preventing-infection">5. Preventing Infection<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#5-preventing-infection" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Septicaemia is likely following an operation, due to peritonitis. Pneumonia may follow bed confinement.</li>
<li>This is indicated by a rise in body temperature and should this occur, you will need to administer antibiotic without delay.</li>
<li>In some hospitals it is a common practice to cover the patient with antibiotics following surgery, where septicaemia is likely.</li>
<li>The principle of infection prevention should be applied to prevent infection.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="principles-of-postoperative-care">Principles of postoperative care<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#principles-of-postoperative-care" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="6-monitoring-of-complications">6. Monitoring of Complications<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#6-monitoring-of-complications" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>You should monitor pulse, blood pressure, and respiration rate and body temperature until they are stable and within the normal ranges for the age and sex of the patient.</li>
<li>The recommended frequency is to observe the patient every 15 minutes for the first two hours, followed by every 30 minutes for the next two hours, then four hourly if they appear to be stable.</li>
<li>Other important observations to make at the same time are level of consciousness, and urine output.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="principles-of-postoperative-care">Principles of postoperative care<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#principles-of-postoperative-care" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="7-controlling-pain">7. Controlling Pain<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#7-controlling-pain" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>This is achieved by the administration of pain relief drugs once the patient is conscious.</li>
<li>You should administer an intermittent bolus of pethidine 50-100mg intramuscularly or morphine 10-15mg for adult.</li>
<li>Other measures include correct positioning of the patient so as to avoid pressure on the nerves, administering analgesics, use of heat/cold massage and guided imagery (a process of suppressing pain by focusing on something else).</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="principles-of-postoperative-care">Principles of postoperative care<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#principles-of-postoperative-care" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="8-ensuring-return-of-gastro-intestinal-motility">8. Ensuring Return of Gastro Intestinal Motility<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#8-ensuring-return-of-gastro-intestinal-motility" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Postoperatively you should assess the return of gastric motility.</li>
<li>This is indicated by the return of bowel sounds and passing of flatus.</li>
<li>Following abdominal surgery (laparatomy), gastro intestinal motility returns to normal in three to four days. The patient should not take food orally before this period is over.</li>
<li>The stomach is decompressed through nasal gastric tube suction. This should be removed when the aspirate falls below 400mls per day.</li>
<li>Should postoperative diarrhoea occur, reassure the patient, as this clears in two to three days, but ensure adequate hydration.</li>
<li>When bowel sounds are back give oral sips, fluid diet, light diet, then resume normal diet.</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="9-ensuring-early-ambulation">9. Ensuring Early Ambulation<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#9-ensuring-early-ambulation" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Encourage the patient to move out of bed as soon as their condition allows. This will prevent deep venous thrombosis (the development of a blood clot in a vein), which can complicate to pulmonary embolism (a circulating blood clot in the veins of the lungs).</li>
<li>The signs of thrombosis include, warm swollen painful limbs and low-grade fever. If noticed, the affected limb should be elevated until the swelling subsides. Heparin in a dose of 5000 units, eight hourly, is administered subcutaneously when the diagnosis is confirmed.</li>
<li>The postoperative care should start from the recovery area of a theatre, and continue in the postoperative ward where the patient is rehabilitated then discharged.</li>
</ul>

Preparing the Patient for Discharge and Home Based Care<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#10-preparing-the-patient-for-discharge-and-home-based-care" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>The postoperative patient needs to be made aware of the expected outcome of the surgery as well as the medical and nursing care that they will require at home. This will reduce the possibility of last minute crises on the day of discharge.</li>
<li>The patient should be given an opportunity to get ready to cope at home and in the community as they ask you how to deal with a changed body image. Home based care concepts will be covered in detail in unit seven of module three.</li>
</ul>
