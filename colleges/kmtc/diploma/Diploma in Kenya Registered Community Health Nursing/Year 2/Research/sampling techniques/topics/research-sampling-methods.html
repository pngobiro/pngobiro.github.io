SAMPLING

Core Concepts

Sample: A small group chosen for study.

Population: whole group which the sample represents

Sample size: The number of individuals or observations in the sample

Sampling: Choosing of a sample from a population. It refers to the technique or the procedure the researcher would adopt in selecting items for the sample

Sampling Frame

Is the source material or device from which a sample is drawn.

  • It is a list of all those within a population who can be sampled, eg households & institutions.
  • Every element of the population of interest is present in the frame.
  • Every element of the population is present only once in the frame.
  • Within the population, there will probably be only certain groups that will be of interest to your study, this selected category is your sampling frame
  • Out of the sample frame does one get a sample.

Study Area & Study Population

Study Area

Is a specific location, neighbourhood, community or city that is of interest to a student or researcher to find a particular problem and recommended ways to solve the problem.

Is the area in which you need to look to find articles relevant to your research.

Study population

Is a subset of the target population from which the sample is actually selected.

Is a group considered for a study or statistical reasoning. It is not limited to the human population only could be animals, objects.

For any research study to be effective, it is necessary to select the study population that truly represent the entire population.

We use sampling to choose a study population.

Bias and Error in Sampling

Sampling Bias

Or a biased sample in research occurs when members of the intended population are selected incorrectly.

Is the collection of samples that do not accurately represent the entire population. It occurs during the collection of data.

It poses a significant threat to the validity of research findings by distorting results towards a particular segment of a target population.

A biased sample can manifest in various forms, depending on the method of sample selection.

Common types of bias include: self-selection bias, nonresponse bias, undercoverage bias, survivorship bias, pre-screening/advertising bias & healthy user bias.

Sampling Error

Are incurred when the statistical characteristics of a population are estimated from a subset, or a sample of that population.

Are the discrepancy btw the xtics. of the population & the xtics of the sample, although the sample is drawn from that particular population.

Is due to errors inherent in random sampling & is never entirely under the control of the researcher. Becoz, a random sample, however accurately drawn, is never.

Causes of bias

  1. Different types of sampling methods.
  2. Inconsistencies in definition.
  3. Tools
  4. Measurement process
  5. Non response
  6. Carelessness

How to remove bias

  1. Adequate planning
  2. Training of research assistants
  3. Standardization of tools of instruments
  4. Supervision & monitoring of the study

Types of Sampling (Sample Designs)

Target population.

There are two broad categories:

  1. Probability
  2. Non-probability

Probability Sampling

Based on the concept of random selection. The goal is to select a reasonable No. of subjects, objects or cases that represent the target population.

Previous studies have indicated that probability sampling can provide us with accurate information about groups that are too large to study in their entity.

Provides us with an efficient system of capturing, in a small group, the variations or heterogeneity that exist in the target population.

Every sample has an equal chance of being selected.

Examples of some probability samplings are:

  1. Simple Random Sampling
  2. Stratified Random Sampling
  3. Systematic Sampling
  4. Cluster Sampling
  5. Multistage Sampling

Probability Sampling Techniques

1. Simple Random Sampling

Each member of the population has an equal chance of being selected in the sample.

Best applicable where a list of the sampling frame is available.

It assures randomness. eg, using lottery number & by use of table of random numbers.

2. Systematic Random Sampling

Every kth case in the frame is selected for inclusion in the sample.

To obtain a truly random sample using this method, the list of all the members in sampling frame must be randomized.

Once a population frame is randomized, the researcher then decides on sampling interval (distance btw the cases that are selected for the sample).

Rule of thumb in deciding the sampling interval is to divide the total population by the sample size.

In this method all sections of the population are adequately represented.

Advantages

  1. Simple and convenient: Results generally satisfactory.
  2. Gives similar results to those of stratified random samp.
  3. Independent. Sample obtained is the true representative sample as this method of selection is dependent on the property of population under study.
  4. Little chances of bias.
  5. Helps in random selection.

Disadvantages

  1. High chances of sampling error if there is periodicity pattern.
  2. Works only for random population.
  3. Not suitable for large population.

3. Stratified Random Sampling

The goal of this is to achieve desired representation from various subgroups in the population. In this, subjects are selected in such a way that the existing subgroups in the population are more or less reproduced in the sample.

Involves a method where a larger population can be divided into smaller groups, that usually don't overlap but represent the entire population together.

A common method is to arrange or classify by sex, age, ethnicity & similar ways.

Random sample is taken from each stratum, the whole pop is adequately represented.

For greater accuracy in the findings, the No. in each stratum should be based on the relative variability of the xtics under study rather than proportionate to the relative size of each subgroup in the population. eg male & female, if female have more variability choos

Steps involved:

Steps involved in stratified random sampling are the same as those in simple random sampling. However the sampling frame has to be identified in divided into the defined strata.

  1. Identify the population.
  2. Define criterion for stratification.
  3. List the population according to the defined strata or subgroups.
  4. Determine the required sample size and the appropriate representation in each stratum. This can be proportionate or equal.
  5. Select using random No. as appropriate No. of subjects for each stratum.

Note: stratification can be done on more than one variable.

Advantages

  • Accurately reflects the population being studied becoz researchers are stratifying the entire population by applying random sampling method.
  • Ensures each subgroup in popln receives proper representation within the sample.
  • provides better coverage of the population since the researchers have control over the subgroups to ensure all of them are represented in the sampling.

Disadvantages

Several conditions must be met for it to be used properly:

  1. Researcher must identify every member of a population being studied and classify each of them into one and only one, subpopulation.
  2. Disadvantageous when researchers cannot confidently classify every member of the population into a subgroup.
  3. Finding an exhaustive & definitive list of an entire population can be challenging.

4. Cluster Sampling

Used when it is not possible to obtain a sampling frame becoz the population is either very large or scattered over a large geographical area.

Usually analyzes a particular population in which the sample consists of more than a few elements eg. city.

All the members of such an intact group are the included in the sample & each member becomes a unit of observation.

In cluster sampling, it is the groups or clusters that are randomly selected & not the individuals or cases.

Used when it is too expensive to spread out your sample across the population as a whole.

Cluster sampling involves:

  1. Identify the population.
  2. Define the cluster forming the population.
  3. Determine the required sample size.
  4. list all the clusters in a random order.
  5. Using the table of random No, select the required No of clusters according to the sample size.
  6. All members in the selected clusters are included in the sample as units of observations.

Advantages

  1. Reduced costs
  2. Simplified field work
  3. Administration is more convenient.

Disadvantages

  1. Less accurate results due to higher sampling erra than simple sampling.

5. Multi-Stage Sampling

A modification of cluster sampling. It involves a process of drawing samples from selected clusters - sub-sampling.

It can be two-stage, 3 stage etc.

Example: province -> district -> division -> location.

Advantages

  1. Simplification
  2. Flexibility
    1. allows researcher to employ random sampling.
    2. by breaking clusters to a desired size.

Disadvantages

  1. Arbitrariness
    1. Becaz of lack of restrictions on the decision processes involved in choosing groups, multi stage sampling has a lvl of subjectivity.
    2. There will always be questions as to whether, the chosen groups were optimal. Researcher must justify their choices when presenting the study's findings.

Non-Probability Sampling

Used when a researcher is not interested in selecting a sample that is representative of the population.

Most qualitative studies use non-probability samples becoz the focus is on in-depth information and not making inferences or generalizations.

Relying on the judgement of the researcher or on accident, cannot generally be used to make generalizations about the whole populations.

Techniques

  1. Convenience
  2. Purposive
  3. Quota
  4. Snowball

1. Convenience Sampling

Also known as accidental, accessibility/haphazard, volunteer sampling.

Involves selecting cases or units of observation as they become available to researcher.

Cases, selected as they become available.

Ease of access being the sole concern.

e.g Radio users asking listeners questions

Used to create sample as per ease of access, readiness to be a part of the sample.

Advantages

  1. Ease of availability
  2. Saves time
  3. Saves money
  4. Useful in a pilot study - Helps to gain initial primary data about a topic.

Disadvantages

  1. Biased.
  2. High possibility of sampling error.
  3. Results cannot be generalized.

2. Purposive (Judgement) Sampling

Allows a researcher to use cases that have the required information with respect to the objectives of his/her study.

Cases of subjects are therefore, hand picked becoz they are informative or they possess the required xtics.

The researcher must specify the criteria for choosing a particular case.

Some researchers used this method as part of multi-stage sampling procedures.

Advantages

  • Economical
  • Proper representation
  • Avoid Irrelevant items
  • Accurate Results

Disadvantages

  • Personal Bias
  • No equal chance
  • No degree of accuracy
  • No possibility of calculating sample error
  • Unsuitable for large samples

3. Quota Sampling

Here, judgement & convenience.

Is similar to stratified random sampling & the objective is to include various groups or quotas of the population.

The selection of actual participants is not random since subjects are picked as they fit into identified quotas.

eg Religion (20% muslim, 5% Jews, 30% christians etc).

Advantages

  1. Can be executed in lesser time than that in any random method of sampling & hence it saves capital in research.
  2. Researcher has total control over chosen samples, since it is the researcher only who decides what to choose.
  3. Can be applied independently - no follow up.

Disadvantages

  1. Due to control over the sample, there are limited No. of decisions that can be made.
  2. Non-random, therefore there are less chances of variations.

4. Snowball Sampling

Initial subjects with the desired xtics are identified using purposive sampling techniques.

Few identified subjects name others that they know have the required xtics until the researcher gets the number of cases he/she requires.

Method based purely on referrals.

Advantages

  1. Recruiting participants
  2. Reaching hard to find populations

Disadvantages

  1. Bias
  2. Research Ethics Issues
