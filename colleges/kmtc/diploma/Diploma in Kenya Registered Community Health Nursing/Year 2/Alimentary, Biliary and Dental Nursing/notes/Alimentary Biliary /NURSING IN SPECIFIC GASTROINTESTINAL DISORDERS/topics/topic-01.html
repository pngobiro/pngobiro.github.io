4.1

üëã Introduction

The food that we ingest gets digested and absorbed in the normal process. Though there is an interaction with these processes, there is disruption, too. In this unit, we will learn these disruptions or disorders and plan for the care accordingly.

We all know what it means to us when our stomach or any other area of the gastrointestinal tract is upset. Disorders of this system cause much distress for the patient. Most of the disorders affect the appetite and the body's ability to digest and utilise the food. This in turn affects the nutritional status of the patient. Some disorders are acute, others are subacute or chronic while some others are life-threatening. However, effective use of the nursing process is essential to ensure that each patient's nursing needs are met. This unit will help you to learn to care for patients with gastrointestinal disorders which include discussion on disorders of mouth and oesophagus, stomach and intestines, rectum and anus, liver and gall bladder along with the necessary surgical interventions.

4.2

üß¨ Disorders of Mouth and Oesophagus

As you have already studied that food enters through the mouth where it is chewed properly mixed with digestive enzymes and swallowed. Swallowing results in the propulsion of the bolus of food into the oesophagus. Due to rhythmic contractions from top to bottom of the oesophagus, the bolus of food enters the stomach. Once the food enters the stomach, the lower oesophageal sphincter closes lightly to prevent reflux of stomach contents into the oesophagus. Disorders do happen within this process of chewing and swallowing.

Let us study some of the common disorders of the mouth.

4.2.1 Oral Disorders

Poor oral hygiene leads to infection and disorders throughout the gastrointestinal system. In the mouth, inflammation may affect the mucous membranes, gum or tongue. Let us discuss some of these disorders.

Stomatitis
is inflammation of the buccal mucosa due to disease, trauma, irritants, nutritional deficiencies or medications.
Herpes simplex
(cold sore or fever blister) is a condition that ensures vesicle formation in mouth, on lips or on nose due to herpes virus. It is self-limiting and will subside by itself.
Ulcerative stomatitis
(Vicent's infection) is the ulceration of the mucous membrane and painful swelling of the gums caused by combination of two pathogens‚Äîfusiform bacillus and spirochaete.
Candidiasis (moniliasis/thrush)
is caused by a form of fungus called Candida albicans. White patches appear on the tongue and mucous membranes of the mouth. It occurs often in the newborn due to the presence of the organism in the mother's birth canal. Prolonged antibiotic therapy and diabetes are predisposing causes.
Glossitis
is inflammation of the tongue and may be a prominent feature of stomatitis resulting from nutritional deficiency.
Leukoplakia
is a chronic condition, characterised by white, firm, smooth patches beginning at the side of tongue and later spreading over the dorsum. In the early stages the tongue is not painful but later, the patches are split by fissures with resultant tendencies. A biopsy should be taken to rule out carcinoma.
Parotitis
may be due to the virus of mumps or to bacterial infection of the salivary gland. Attention should be given to oral hygiene, prevention of dehydration and infection during febrile illnesses or after major abdominal operations.

Etiology

Several factors contribute to the development of oral inflammatory disorders. They are: (a) poor oral hygiene (b) stress (c) nutritional deficiencies (d) debilitating diseases (e) heavy smoking and (f) prolonged chemotherapy.

Pathophysiology

  • Irritation to the mucous membranes occurs due to malnutrition and poor oral hygiene, smoking, broken teeth and ingestion of highly spiced foods.
  • Interference with the body's immune response and decrease in the body defence system occurs due to stress, malnutrition and chemotherapy.
  • Inflammation of the mucous membrane often result in small painful ulcerations.
  • Inflammation of the gums may cause bleeding and loosening of teeth.
  • Thrush may result when antibiotics are given over a period of time or due to unhygienic handling of newborns, and
  • Cracking lips and blisters causes pain.

Treatment

The line of treatment is as follows:

  1. restore good oral hygiene
  2. adequate nutrition and rehydration with bland diet and drinks
  3. antifungal agents for thrush‚ÄîTab. Nystatin
  4. antibiotics (penicillin)
  5. analgesics and topical anesthesia (lidocaine) to relief pain.

Nursing Intervention

While caring for people with mouth disorders, special mouth care are as follows:

  • encourage thorough and frequent cleansing of the mouth and teeth to remove any foreign materials, rinse before and after each meal and lubricate the lips.
  • use alkaline mouth washes e.g., sodium bicarbonate or hydrogen peroxide 1:4 with normal saline (mix immediately before use only to avoid decomposition).
  • instruct the patient not to drink, rinse or spit for 1/2 hour after applying antifungal agents in the mouth‚Äîwhile using Tab. Nystatin allow the tablet to be rolled throughout the oral cavity.
  • encourage nutritious food that can be tolerated such as soft foods, cool drinks and prevent recurrence of sore mouth etc.
  • give health teaching on the maintenance of good oral hygiene, value of drinking plenty of water and importance of hand washing.

4.2.2 Oesophageal Disorders

Now let us discuss some of the common disorders of the oesophagus.

Types of Disorders

‚ÑπÔ∏è a) Achalasia

The esophageal motility may be impaired by physiologic dysfunction or lack of peristalsis which is called achalasia. As a result there is dysphagia for liquids and solids, weight loss and substernal chest pain. In severe achalasia, the portion of esophagus above the defect dilates and the person may have difficulty in swallowing food and fluids past that point.

‚ÑπÔ∏è b) Oesophageal Diverticulum

Is a bulging of the esophageal mucosa and submucosa through a weakened portion of the oesophageal muscles. As food is ingested, some of it may collect in the pouch formed by the weakened area, and when the pouch is filled it overflows into the esophagus and is regurgitated. There is always the danger of food getting aspirated in the trachea during sleep.

‚ÑπÔ∏è c) Gastroesophageal Reflux

This occurs when the lower esophageal sphincter at the junction of the esophagus and stomach becomes incompetent and permits reflux of the gastric material back into the esophagus. The acidity of gastric juice irritates the esophageal mucosa creating an uncomfortable sensation which occurs beneath the sternum. It is commonly called heart burn. Chronic reflux may lead to a stricture delaying passage of food into the stomach.

‚ÑπÔ∏è d) Hiatus Hernia

The esophagus passes through a hiatus (small opening) in the diaphragm to reach the stomach. When this opening becomes relaxed or stretched, a portion of the stomach can protrude through the hiatus.

The causes of hiatus hernia can include congenital weakness of the diaphragm, injury, pregnancy, obesity and aging. As a result, the function of the cardiac sphincter is lost, gastric juices enter the esophagus, edema and hyperemia may result. Heartburn results after eating food.

Signs and Symptoms

By taking a good history of the patients problem and by barium swallow procedure you would be able to identify the following signs and symptoms:

Subjective:
  • dysphagia of solids, liquids
  • regurgitation
  • foul odour of regurgitated material (esophageal diverticulum)
  • heartburn or pain radiating to neck and back starting from the substernal area, particularly soon after a heavy meal
  • nocturnal dyspnoea.
üëÅÔ∏è
Objective:
  • inability to swallow when pressing over the thyroid cartilage
  • barium swallow will reflux back into the esophagus if the lower esophageal sphincter is incompetent
  • barium swallow and endoscopy show protrusion of stomach through the diaphragm. In sitting or standing position, the stomach moves back into the abdominal cavity

Treatment

The line of treatment is as follows:

  1. antacids 30 ml, one hour after meals, to decrease heart-burn as prescribed by doctor. It neutralizes gastric secretions
  2. metoclopramide (Reglan) 30 ml before meals and at bedtime as prescribed by doctor
  3. esophageal dilation is done to remove any strictures
  4. gastrostomy is done to assist the healing process. An outline of the nursing care of patients with gastrostomy is given in Table 4.1
  5. esophageal surgery is done to repair any diverticulum or hernia
  6. small frequent, bland diet is given to prevent stimulating excessive gastric secretions
  7. eat food atleast two hours before bedtime to prevent regurgitation

Nursing Interventions

Give special instructions to patient and family regarding the patients food habits as follows:

  • eat a high protein, low-fat diet to prevent esophageal regurgitation
  • avoid foods containing caffeine (coffee, tea, colas), chocolate and alcohol)
  • eat small frequent feedings to prevent gastric distension and increased gastric acid secretions
  • avoid smoking
  • avoid lying down or bending over for 2 hours after eating to prevent regurgitation
  • avoid lifting or wearing tight belts or girdles after eating to prevent abdominal pressure
  • sleep with head elevated to facilitate flow of food by gravity
  • take antacids and Reglan regularly as prescribed by doctor.
