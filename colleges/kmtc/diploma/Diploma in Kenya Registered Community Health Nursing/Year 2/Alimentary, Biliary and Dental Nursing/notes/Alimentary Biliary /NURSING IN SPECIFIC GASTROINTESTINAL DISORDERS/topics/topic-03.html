Liver and gallbladder play an important role in the gastrointestinal functions. Any disorder of the liver should be taken as a serious threat to normal living.

4.4.1

ü¶† Hepatitis

Hepatitis is inflammation of the liver. This inflammation may be caused by viruses, toxins, or chemicals (including drugs).

Types of hepatitis:

  • Viral
  • Toxic
  • Chronic
  • Alcoholic

Viral Hepatitis Overview

There are number of specific viruses that cause hepatitis. The most common of these are hepatitis A virus, hepatitis B virus, hepatitis C virus, hepatitis D virus, and hepatitis E virus. Another hepatitis G virus has been discovered, but its role in acute viral hepatitis has not been established. Although the manifestations of infection are similar in hepatitis A through E plus G, these conditions differ in incubation period, mode of transmission, and severity.

Etiology and Risk Factors

üÖ∞Ô∏è Hepatitis A

Also known as infectious hepatitis, is caused by an RNA virus of the enterovirus family. Causes of epidemics include infected water, milk and food, especially raw shellfish from contaminated waters.

Hepatitis A is transmitted primarily by the fecal oral route. It spreads from person to person by close contact or by the handling of feces contaminated articles.

üÖ±Ô∏è Hepatitis B

Health care workers are at high risk for acquiring hepatitis B because of their close contact with the blood of carriers. Clients who have had multiple transfusions or dialysis are also vulnerable. Other high risk population are homosexual men, people who undergo tattooing and IV drug users.

The major sources of hepatitis B are carriers and clients in the acute phase of the infection. Contact with the serum of an infected person is the major mode of transmission. The virus also may be transmitted by other body fluids such as saliva and semen. Hepatitis B virus can survive on environmental surfaces for at least a week.

Cc Hepatitis C

Hepatitis C is transmitted parenterally through the blood, by personal contact, and possibly by the fecal-oral route. In contrast to Hepatitis A, but like Hepatitis B, Hepatitis C may be spread by carriers. Because Hepatitis C is also parenterally transmitted, the risk factors are similar to those for Hepatitis B.

Dd Hepatitis D

It is transmitted only through blood contact and is thus seen most commonly in clients exposed to blood and blood products, such as IV drug users and people with hemophilia. The risk factors for Hepatitis D are the same as for Hepatitis B.

Ee Hepatitis E

It is a water borne virus. It primarily affects young adults. It has a short incubation, and there is no evidence that it becomes chronic. The risk factors for Hepatitis E include travel to countries that have a high incidence of Hepatitis E and consuming food or water contaminated with the virus.

Gg Hepatitis G

It is spread through contact with blood, blood products, and body fluids. Risk factors are the same as those for Hepatitis B and Hepatitis C.

Pathophysiology

The pathophysiologic features are similar regardless to the cause. Hepatocytes undergo pathologic changes induced by the body's immune response to the virus. Inflammation of the liver with areas of necrosis occurs, and the resultant damage leads to impairment of function. The degree of functional impairment depends on the amount of hepatocellular damage. The endoplasmic reticulum- responsible for protein and steroid synthesis, glucuronide conjugation, and detoxification- is the first cellular organelle to undergo change, and liver functions that depend on these processes are altered. Kupffer cells increase in size and number. Vascular and ductular tissues undergo inflammatory changes. Healing of the damaged hepatic tissue generally occurs in 3-4 months.

Prevention Strategies

  • Hepatitis A: Good personal hygiene, hand-washing, treatment of municipal water supplies, serological screening of food handlers. Active immunization (vaccine) and passive immunization (immunoglobulin).
  • Hepatitis B: Screening of blood/donors, use of disposable equipment, good personal hygiene (not sharing razors/toothbrushes). Active immunization (vaccine in 3 doses) and passive immunization (immune globulin).
  • Hepatitis C: Similar safety measures as Hep B. No vaccine available.
  • Hepatitis D: Hepatitis B vaccine can help prevent Hep D as they coexist.
  • Hepatitis E: Personal hygiene and sanitation (water borne).

Clinical Manifestations

Manifestations during the early phase may include jaundice, lethargy, irritability, myalgia, arthralgia, anorexia, nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, diarrhea or constipation, fever and other flu-like symptoms. Deterioration of handwriting, drowsiness and irritability are early manifestation of hepatic encephalopathy.

Bleeding tendencies may develop either from reduced prothrombin synthesis by injured hepatic cells or from reduced absorption of the fat soluble vitamin K due to reduced levels of bile in the intestines.

Liver gets enlarged in hepatitis and is tender to palpation. Levels of serum aminotransferases first rise and then begin to fall as bilirubin starts to increase.

Management

  • Reduce Fatigue: Rest is advisable in proportion to severity.
  • Nutritional/Fluid Balance: Low fat, high carbohydrate diet. IV glucose if needed.
  • Reduce Effects: Antiemetics for nausea, parenteral vitamin K for bleeding tendencies, antihistamines for pruritis.
  • Medications to Avoid: Chlorpromazine, aspirin, Acetaminophen, and sedatives due to hepatotoxicity.

ü•É Cirrhosis of Liver

Cirrhosis of the liver refers to replacement of normal cells by fibrous tissue which results in hardening or scarring of the liver. These tissue changes are irreversible. Laennec‚Äôs type (nutritional/portal/alcoholic) is most common.

Pathophysiology

  • The alcoholics who develop liver disease first develop faulty degeneration of the liver which leads to acute inflammation
  • Pressure rises in the portal system (which drains blood from the digestive organs) causing stasis and backup in digestive organs called portal hypertension
  • Bleeding esophageal varices results from increased amounts of blood being routed to the esophageal veins from the liver when it is damaged
  • The small vessels of esophagus become tortuous and fragile and start bleeding (abrupt and sudden)
  • Hepatic coma may result from high blood ammonia levels when the liver is unable to convert the ammonia to urea.

Signs and Symptoms

4.4.3

ü™® Cholecystitis

Cholecystitis is inflammation of the gall bladder, usually caused by the presence of gall stones (cholelithiasis) or other obstructions in the bile passage. It is more common in women than in men.

Signs and Symptoms

  • Subjective: pain on the right upper quadrant of the abdomen radiating to the back, indigestion after eating fatty foods, nausea.
  • Objective: vomiting, elevated temperature, leucocytosis, elevated serum bilirubin. Ultrasonography determines presence of stones.

Treatment & Nursing

Treatment includes rest, nasogastric suctioning, antispasmodics, analgesics, low fat diet, lithotripsy (ultrasonic crushing of stones), or surgery (cholecystectomy).

T-Tube Care: If the common bile duct is explored, a T tube is inserted. Nursing care involves avoiding kinking, securing the bag, applying protective ointment, and watching for normal brown stool when the tube is removed (usually after 7 days).

4.4.4

üî• Acute Pancreatitis

Acute pancreatitis may result in autodigestion of the pancreas by its own enzymes. Etiology includes alcohol abuse, cholelithiasis, trauma, and metabolic factors.

Pathophysiology

Pathological changes are due to premature activation of proteolytic and lipolytic enzymes. Normally activated in the duodenum, in pancreatitis, activation occurs before secretion into the intestine, causing pancreatic tissue damage.

Clinical Manifestations

  • Extreme umbilical or epigastric pain radiating to the back
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Abdominal distension and tenderness
  • Turner‚Äôs sign: bluish discoloration of the left flank
  • Cullen‚Äôs sign: bluish discoloration of the periumbilical area

Management

Medical: Reduce pain (Meperidine), maintain volume status (fluids/electrolytes), maintain pancreatic rest (NPO, NG suction), treat complications (abscess, respiratory issues).

Surgical: Drainage of abscess, Subtotal pancreatectomy, or Whipple‚Äôs operation (pancreaticoduodenectomy) if confined to head of pancreas.

üîë Key Words

Achalasia
Failure of relaxation of a muscle sphincter causing dilatation of the part above, e.g. the esophagus above the cardiac sphincter
Alkalosis
An increase in the alkali reserve in the blood
Cholecystitis
Inflammation of the gallbladder
Cholangitis
Inflammation of the bile duct
Colostomy
The surgical formation of a temporary or permanent opening between the colon and anterior abdominal wall
Decompression
Removal of internal pressure
Diverticulitis
Inflammation of a diverticulum
Diverticulum
A pouch or pocket in the lining of a hollow organ as in the bladder, oesophagus or large intestine
Fissure
A painful, crack in the mucous membrane of the anus generally caused by injury from hard faeces
Fistula
An abnormal passage connecting the cavity of one organ with another, or a cavity with the surface of the body
Intussusception
A condition in which one part of the intestine becomes pushed or invaginated into another part beyond
Paracentesis
Puncture of the wall of a cavity with a hollow needle in order to draw off excess fluid or to obtain diagnostic material
Regurgitation
Backward flow, e.g. food from the stomach into the mouth
Strangulated
Compressed or constricted so that the circulation of the blood is arrested.
