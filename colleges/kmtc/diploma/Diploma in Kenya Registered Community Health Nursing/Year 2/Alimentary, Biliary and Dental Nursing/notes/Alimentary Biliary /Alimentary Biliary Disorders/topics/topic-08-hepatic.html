üß¨ Hepatic Cirrhosis

Definition: Cirrhosis is a chronic disease characterized by the replacement of normal liver tissue with diffuse fibrosis that disrupts the structure and function of the liver.

Types of Cirrhosis

Type Pathophysiology Primary Cause
Alcoholic Scar tissue characteristically surrounds the portal areas. Most common type. Chronic alcoholism. Episodes of necrosis occur repeatedly; scar tissue eventually exceeds functioning tissue.
Post-necrotic Broad bands of scar tissue form. Late result of a previous bout of acute viral hepatitis.
Biliary Scarring occurs in the liver around the bile ducts. Chronic biliary obstruction and infection (cholangitis).

Liver Enlargement

Early in the course of cirrhosis, the liver tends to be large, and cells are loaded with fat. The liver is firm and has a sharp edge noticeable on palpation. Abdominal pain may result from rapid enlargement.

üíß Ascites & Peritonitis

Portal Obstruction & Ascites

Late manifestations caused by failure of liver function and obstruction of portal circulation.

  • Blood from digestive organs collects in portal veins.
  • Ascites: Accumulation of fluid rich in protein in the peritoneal cavity.
  • Detection: Percussion for shifting dullness or fluid wave.

Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis (SBP)

ü¶†

Infection Risk

Bacterial peritonitis may develop in patients with cirrhosis and ascites without an intra-abdominal source of infection.

  • Cause: Bacteremia due to translocation of intestinal flora.
  • Diagnosis: Clinical signs may be absent; requires paracentesis.
  • Treatment: Antibiotic therapy.

Hepato-renal Syndrome

Severe complication of SBP involving renal failure unresponsive to fluids or diuretics. Characterized by functional renal failure without pathologic kidney changes.

04

üìà Portal Hypertension

Definition: Increase in pressure within the portal vein (which carries blood from digestive organs to the liver) caused by blockage in blood flow through the liver.

Symptoms & Clinical Signs

  • Varices: Large veins develop across esophagus and stomach to bypass blockage. These are fragile and bleed easily.
  • GI Bleeding: Black, tarry stools (melena) or hematemesis (vomiting blood) from ruptured varices.
  • Ascites: Fluid accumulation in abdomen.
  • Encephalopathy: Confusion/forgetfulness due to poor liver function and diverted blood flow (toxins reach brain).
  • Hematology: Reduced platelets or WBCs.

üè• Management & Treatment

1. First Level Treatment (Medical & Endoscopic)

Used for initial diagnosis of variceal bleeding.

  • Endoscopic Therapy:
    • Sclerotherapy: Injection of solution into bleeding varices to stop bleeding.
    • Banding: Using rubber bands to block blood supply to varices.
  • Medications: Beta blockers (Propranolol) or Nitrates (Isosorbide) to lower portal pressure.

2. Dietary & Lifestyle Changes

  • Avoid: Alcohol, street drugs, OTC meds without consultation.
  • Diet: Low-sodium (max 2g/day) to manage ascites.
  • Protein: Reduced intake ONLY if confusion (encephalopathy) is present.

3. Second Level Treatment (Decompression)

Used if first level fails to control bleeding.

Procedure Description Details
TIPS
(Transjugular Intrahepatic Portosystemic Shunt)		 Radiological procedure. Stent placed in middle of liver to reroute blood flow.
  • Not surgical (done via vessels).
  • Controls bleeding in >90%.
  • Risk: Encephalopathy (toxins bypass liver).
  • Issue: Shunt narrowing/occlusion in 30%.
DSRS
(Distal Splenorenal Shunt)		 Surgical procedure. Connects splenic vein to left kidney vein.
  • Selectively reduces pressure in varices.
  • General anesthesia, longer hospital stay (7-10 days).
  • Good long-term control.
  • Risk: Ascites.

4. Advanced Interventions

  • Liver Transplant: For end-stage liver disease.
  • Devascularization: Surgical removal of bleeding varices (if TIPS/Shunt not possible).
  • Paracentesis: Direct removal of accumulated fluid (ascites).
