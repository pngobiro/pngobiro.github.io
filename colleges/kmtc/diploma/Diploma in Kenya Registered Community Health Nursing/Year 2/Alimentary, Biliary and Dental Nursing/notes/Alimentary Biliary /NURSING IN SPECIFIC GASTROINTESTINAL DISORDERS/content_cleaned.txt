<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="4-1-introduction">4.1 INTRODUCTION<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#4-1-introduction" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The food that we ingest gets digested and absorbed in the normal process. Though there is an interaction with these processes, there is disruption, too. In this unit, we will learn these disruptions or disorders and plan for the care accordingly.</p>
<p>We all know what it means to us when our stomach or any other area of the gastrointestinal tract is upset. Disorders of this system cause much distress for the patient. Most of the disorders affect the appetite and the body's ability to digest and utilise the food. This in turn affects the nutritional status of the patient. Some disorders are acute, others are subacute or chronic while some others are life-</p>
<p>Nursing in Specific Gastrointestinal Disorders</p>
<p>threatening. However, effective use of the nursing process is essential to ensure that each patient's nursing needs are met. This unit will help you to learn to care for patients with gastrointestinal disorders which include discussion on disorders of mouth and oesophagus, stomach and intestines, rectum and anus, liver and gall bladder along with the necessary surgical interventions.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="4-2-disorders-of-mouth-and-oesophagus">4.2 DISORDERS OF MOUTH AND OESOPHAGUS<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#4-2-disorders-of-mouth-and-oesophagus" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>As you have already studied that food enters through the mouth where it is chewed properly mixed with digestive enzymes and swallowed. Swallowing results in the propulsion of the bolus of food into the oesophagus. Due to rhythmic contractions from top to bottom of the oesophagus, the bolus of food enters the stomach. Once the food enters the stomach, the lower oesophageal sphincter closes lightly to prevent reflux of stomach contents into the oesophagus. Disorders do happen within this process of chewing and swallowing.</p>
<p>Let us study some of the common disorders of the mouth.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="4-2-1-oral-disorders">4.2.1 Oral Disorders<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#4-2-1-oral-disorders" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Poor oral hygiene leads to infection and disorders throughout the gastrointestinal system. In the mouth, inflammation may affect the mucous membranes, gum or tongue. Let us discuss some of these disorders.</p>
<p><strong>Stomatitis</strong> is inflammation of the buccal mucosa due to disease, trauma, irritants, nutritional deficiencies or medications.</p>
<p><strong>Herpes simplex</strong> (cold sore or fever blister) is a condition that ensures vesicle formation in mouth, on lips or on nose due to herpes virus. It is self-limiting and will subside by itself.</p>
<p><strong>Ulcerative stomatitis</strong> (Vicent's infection) is the ulceration of the mucous membrane and painful swelling of the gums caused by combination of two pathogens—fusiform bacillus and spirochaete.</p>
<p><strong>Candidiasis (moniliasis/thrush)</strong> is caused by a form of fungus called <em>Candida albicans</em>. White patches appear on the tongue and mucous membranes of the mouth. It occurs often in the newborn due to the presence of the organism in the mother's birth canal. Prolonged antibiotic therapy and diabetes are predisposing causes.</p>
<p><strong>Glossitis</strong> is inflammation of the tongue and may be a prominent feature of stomatitis resulting from nutritional deficiency.</p>
<p><strong>Leukoplakia</strong> is a chronic condition, characterised by white, firm, smooth patches beginning at the side of tongue and later spreading over the dorsum. In the early stages the tongue is not painful but later, the patches are split by fissures with resultant tendencies. A biopsy should be taken to rule out carcinoma.</p>
<p><strong>Parotitis</strong> may be due to the virus of mumps or to bacterial infection of the salivary gland. Attention should be given to oral hygiene, prevention of dehydration and infection during febrile illnesses or after major abdominal operations.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="types-of-disorders">Types of Disorders<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#types-of-disorders" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>a) Achalasia: The esophageal motility may be impaired by physiologic dysfunction or lack of peristalsis which is called achalasia. As a result there is dysphagia for liquids and solids, weight loss and substernal chest pain. In severe achalasia, the portion of esophagus above the defect dilates and the person may have difficulty in swallowing food and fluids past that point.</p>
<p>b) Oesophageal Diverticulum: Is a bulging of the esophageal mucosa and submucosa through a weakened portion of the oesophageal muscles (see Fig. 4.1). As food is ingested, some of it may collect in the pouch formed by the weakened area, and when the pouch is filled it overflows into the esophagus and is regurgitated. There is always the danger of food getting aspirated in the trachea during sleep.</p>
<p><img src="assets/images/image-20251225-aedf0aeb.jpeg" alt="img-0.jpeg">
Fig. 4.1: Possible sites for the occurrence of esophageal diverticula</p>
<p>c) Gastroesophageal Reflux: This occurs when the lower esophageal sphincter at the junction of the esophagus and stomach becomes incompetent and permits reflux of the gastric material back into the esophagus. The acidity of gastric juice irritates the esophageal mucosa creating an uncomfortable</p>
<p>83</p>
<p>Musculoskeletal, Gastrointestinal and Oncology Nursing</p>
<p>sensation which occurs beneath the sternum. It is commonly called <strong>heart burn</strong>. Chronic reflux may lead to a stricture delaying passage of food into the stomach.</p>
<p>d) <strong>Hiatus Hernia</strong>: The esophagus passes through a hiatus (small opening) in the diaphragm to reach the stomach. When this opening becomes relaxed or stretched, a portion of the stomach can protrude through the hiatus (see Fig. 4.2).</p>
<p><img src="assets/images/image-20251225-ec9770f0.jpeg" alt="img-1.jpeg">
Fig. 4.2: (a) Sliding esophageal hernia, (b) Periesophageal hernia</p>
<p>The causes of hiatus hernia can include congenital weakness of the diaphragm, injury, pregnancy, obesity and aging. As a result, the function of the cardiac sphincter is lost, gastric juices enter the esophagus, edema and hyperemia may result. Heartburn results after eating food.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="4-3-1-gastric-disorders">4.3.1 Gastric Disorders<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#4-3-1-gastric-disorders" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="peptic-ulcer">Peptic Ulcer<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#peptic-ulcer" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>A peptic ulcer is an excavation formed in the mucosal wall of the stomach, the pylorus, the duodenum, or the esophagus (see Fig. 4.3). A peptic ulcer is frequently referred to as a gastric, duodenal, or esophageal ulcer, depending on its location. It is caused by the erosion of a circumscribed area of mucous membrane. This erosion may extend as deeply as the muscle layers or through the muscle to the peritoneum. Peptic ulcers are more likely to be in the duodenum than in the stomach. Chronic gastric ulcers tend to occur in the lesser curvature of the stomach, near the pylorus. There are certain factors that contribute to the development of peptic ulcers (see Table 4.2).</p>

<p>Once the diagnosis of peptic ulcer is established, the patient is informed that he can learn how to keep his problem under control, but he may expect remissions and recurrences. The line of treatment is primarily to control gastric secretions.</p>
<ul>
<li>The goals of therapy are to relieve pain, heal the ulcer, prevent complications and prevent recurrences,</li>
<li>Reduce physical activity and help in relaxation,</li>
<li>Encourage the patient to seek counselling or psychotherapy to explore the emotional components causing him stress,</li>
<li>Diet regulation through the use of suitable foods, and restriction of irritating substances such as nicotine, caffeine, alcohol, spices and so on. Patient is the best person to choose his diet.</li>
<li>Control of gastric secretions: Gastric acidity can be managed with appropriate sedation and neutralization of the gastric juice at frequent and regular intervals with drugs, non irritating foods and antacids. Drugs that block the acid-secreting action of histamine (H, blockers), such as ranitidine, cimetidine or famotidine or drugs that produce an acid-resistant barrier over the ulcer such as sucralfate have been shown to have dramatic effect in healing duodenal ulcers.</li>
</ul>
<p>Antispamodics may be given to reduce pylorospasm and intestinal motility.</p>
<p>Anticholinergic agents may be prescribed to inhibit gastric secretion.</p>
<p>Hospitalization can be limited to a few days, unless bleeding, obstruction, perforation or severe pain is present.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="enteritis-and-gastroenteritis">Enteritis and Gastroenteritis<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#enteritis-and-gastroenteritis" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>Inflammation of the intestine is called enteritis. Inflammation of the intestine accompanying gastritis is called gastroenteritis. It often occurs in conjunction with some infectious diseases such as typhoid fever, dysentery, intestinal tuberculosis and food poisoning. The signs and symptoms are usually abdominal cramps, fever, diarrhoea, dehydration and weakness. The cause can be identified by microscopic stool examination and cultures from the suspected food.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="pathophysiology">Pathophysiology<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#pathophysiology" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>Acute gastroenteritis can be caused by bacterial or viral infection or by the effect of neurotoxins produced by bacteria. This results in increased secretions of water and salt into the gut lumen or an increase in motility, causing excretion of large amount of undigested food and fluid.</p>
<p>In later stage large amount of gas and foul-smelling stool results. With profuse diarrhoea, large amount of fluid and electrolytes may be lost leading to dehydration, hyponatremia, hypokalemia and metabolic alkalosis.</p>
<p>The major goals of treatment and nursing interventions in patients with gastroenteritis, include identifying the causative factor and give the appropriate antibiotics, keeping up the required electrolyte and fluid balance in the body, maintaining a strict intake and output chart to replace the fluids and electrolytes lost due to diarrhoea, and control transmission of infection.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="regional-enteritis-or-crohn-s-disease">Regional Enteritis or Crohn's Disease<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#regional-enteritis-or-crohn-s-disease" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Regional enteritis or Crohn's disease is an inflammatory process involving any part of the alimentary tract from mouth to anus, but usually involves the distal ileum and colon. It is a slowly progressive persistent inflammation and is a disorder of the lymphoid tissue. Because of the rich lymph supply, the distal ileum is frequently involved. Although the causative mechanisms are unknown, there are varying theories involving genetic predisposition, auto-immune reaction or environmental causes.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="pathophysiology">Pathophysiology<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#pathophysiology" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Enlarged lymph nodes in the submucosa and Peyer's patches in the intestinal mucosa are usually present. These undergo superficial ulceration, granulomas and fissures. Cobblestone ulcerations form along the mucosal wall of the terminal ileum, cecum and ascending colon which form scar tissue and inhibit food and water absorption in the area. Ulceration may penetrate the bowel wall leading to fistulas and abscesses. The small bowel gets congested and thickened and the lumen narrows and becomes fibrosed causing obstruction.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="signs-and-symptoms">Signs and Symptoms<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#signs-and-symptoms" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The patient has severe pain in the right lower quadrant, malaise, fever, elevated WBC, mild diarrhoea and mucous but no blood and anaemia. A colonoscopy and proctosigmoidoscopy will help to rule out ulcerative colitis and diverticulitis and reveal skin lesions and cobblestone mucous. A biopsy will show presence of granulomas.</p>

<ul>
<li>give high-calorie, high-protein, high-residue diet</li>
<li>vitamin supplements including B₁₂</li>
<li>medications such as anticholinergics, analgesics, antibiotics, and immunosuppressive</li>
<li>surgery when fistulas or intestinal obstruction occurs; the involved area of intestine is removed and the ends are anastomosed. In case of involvement of large area of intestine, an ostomy is indicated.</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="ulcerative-colitis">Ulcerative Colitis<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#ulcerative-colitis" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Ulcerative colitis, unlike Crohn's disease which is transmural and segmental, is a continuous disease of the bowel. Often both Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis are confused with each other but are different entities (see Table 4.4). Ulcerative colitis usually begins in the rectum and spreads upward in the colon slowly involving the mucous membrane and all layers of the intestinal wall.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="incidence-and-etiology">Incidence and Etiology<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#incidence-and-etiology" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Ulcerative colitis occurs at all ages, but mostly in young adults. It is more common than Crohn's disease. The inflammatory changes may be caused by emotional stress, bacterial infections, allergic reaction, autoimmune response, or due to lack of protective substances in the bowel wall. The disease may be acute, progressing rapidly to a focal outcome or it may remain low grade. It usually attacks young adults.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="pathophysiology">Pathophysiology<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#pathophysiology" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>There is inflammation, thickening, congestion, edema and minute lacerations that ooze blood and slowly form an abscess. The bowel wall shortens and becomes thin and fragile. There is shortening of the intestinal wall which leads to diarrhoea.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="signs-and-symptoms">Signs and Symptoms<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#signs-and-symptoms" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<ul>
<li>weakness.</li>
<li>anorexia.</li>
<li>nausea and vomiting.</li>
<li>cramping abdominal pain.</li>
<li>colicky pain in lower left quadrant</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="objective">Objective<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#objective" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>

<p>If the patient has an ileostomy, keep him on a low residue diet for 6 weeks to decrease the amount of bulky and undigested foods as the intestinal tract recovers from surgical intervention. In such cases, keep the patient in a knee-chest position and gently massage the area below the stoma in order to prevent stomal edema with a food blockage. Diarrhoea usually follows the removal of the obstruction and the patient will need fluid replacement.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="pathophysiology">Pathophysiology<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#pathophysiology" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Most obstructions occur in the small intestine and affect the normal homeostasis of the body. When intestinal obstructions occur, there is an increase in peristaltic waves proximal to the area of obstruction so as to push the contents through the area of obstruction. Failure of peristalsis results from disturbances in neural stimulation of the bowel (paralytic ileus) or from obstruction of the gastrointestinal tract. When peristalsis ceases, the involved intestinal area becomes disturbed by gas or fluid resulting in increased pressure on the mucosal wall. If this is not relieved, there will be ischemia and neurosis, bacterial invasion and eventually peritonitis. Increased pressure on bladder may cause urinary retention.</p>
<p>Loss of fluids leads to hypovolemia and dehydration. The continuous vomiting causes loss of electrolytes and loss of Hcl from the stomach leading to alkalosis. Loss of sodium and chloride causes hypokalemic alkalosis.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="hernia">Hernia<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#hernia" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>A hernia is a protrusion of an organ or structure from its normal cavity through a congenital or acquired defect. The abdominal cavity has a muscular wall that provides support for most of the organs of digestion. The wall may become weakened at some point, permitting part of an organ to protrude through the weakened area. Protrusion of a loop of intestine most often occurs at the umbilicus or at the inguinal and femoral areas of the groin where large vessels pass through the peritoneum.</p>
<p>If the protruding structure can be manipulated back in place, the hernia is said to be reducible; if it cannot be done, it is considered as irreducible or incarcerated hernia. If the blood supply of the incarcerated area is cut off, strangulation occurs.</p>

<p>It is spread through contact with blood, blood products, and body fluids. Risk factors are the same as those for Hepatitis B and Hepatitis C.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="pathophysiology">Pathophysiology<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#pathophysiology" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The pathophysiologic features are similar regardless to the cause. Hepatocytes undergo pathologic changes induced by the body's immune response to the virus. Inflammation of the liver with areas of necrosis occurs, and the resultant damage leads to impairment of function. The degree of functional impairment depends on the amount of hepatocellular damage. The endoplasmic reticulum- responsible for protein and steroid synthesis, glucuronide conjugation, and detoxification- is the first cellular organelle to undergo change, and liver functions that depend on these processes are altered. Kupffer cells increase in size and number. Vascular and ductular tissues undergo inflammatory changes. Healing of the damaged hepatic tissue generally occurs in 3-4 months.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="hepatitis-a">Hepatitis A<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#hepatitis-a" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Antibodies to Hepatitis A virus (Anti-HAV) appear early in the course of the illness. Both immunoglobulin M (IgM) and immunoglobulin G (IgG) anti-HAV are detectable in the serum soon after the onset of illness. They are at peak during the first week of illness and disappear within 3 to 6 months.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="hepatitis-b">Hepatitis B<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#hepatitis-b" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Hepatitis virus is a DNA virus that has an inner core and a surface envelop. The body forms antibodies to the viral antigens HbA1G and HbA2G. The presence of HbA1G in the blood denotes--a previous or resolving infection with hepatitis B, a continuing, chronic infection, or immunization with immunoglobulin or hepatitis B vaccine.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="hepatitis-c">Hepatitis C<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#hepatitis-c" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The hepatitis C virus is a single stranded RNA virus with properties similar to those of hepatitis B and D viruses. Hepatitis C virus is thought to be a pathogenic factor in conditions with glomerulonephritis and autoimmune thyroiditis.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="hepatitis-e">Hepatitis E<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#hepatitis-e" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>The hepatitis E virus alters hepatocellular function in almost the same way as for the other types of hepatitis viruses. It causes necrosis and liver cell damage.</p>

<p>Hepatitis G virus, like hepatitis C virus, is a blood borne RNA virus. It is seen that people infected with hepatitis G virus are also infected with hepatitis C. Hepatitis G virus does not alter the severity of hepatitis C.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="prevention">Prevention<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#prevention" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="hepatitis-a">Hepatitis A<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#hepatitis-a" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p><strong>Personal Hygiene</strong>: Because transmission of Hepatitis A is by the fecal-oral route, good personal hygiene is important. Strict isolation is not necessary, but hand-washing after a bowel movement is required. Food handlers must wash their hands thoroughly. Care providers must supervise hand-washing by ambulatory residents. Personnel in day care centers need to wash their hands carefully after changing diapers.</p>
<p><strong>Water Supply</strong>: Treatment of municipal water supplies prevents transmission of hepatitis A. Shellfish that come from polluted waters are a major source of hepatitis A.</p>
<p><strong>Restaurants</strong>: It is important for local health authorities to inspect eating establishments consistently and thoroughly. Serological screening of food handlers for hepatitis A reduces its transmission.</p>
<p><strong>Active Immunization</strong>: An inactivated hepatitis A vaccine is available commercially. It is recommended for children and adults who travel, work, or live in countries with a high rate of hepatitis A and for people with chronic liver disease, homosexual men, and IV drug abusers. Children older than 2 years and adolescents receive two doses given 1 month apart, followed by booster dose 6 to 12 months after the first two doses. Adults receive an initial dose followed by a booster 6 to 12 months later. All injections are intramuscular. Adverse effects include headache, abdominal pain, diarrhea, syncope, fever, fatigue, jaundice and local soreness.</p>
<p><strong>Passive Immunization</strong>: Immunoglobulin may be given which is helpful both prophylactically before and after exposure but not after the development of clinical manifestations. Clients who live in or visit high risk areas can be protected for up to 3 months by immune globulin.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="hepatitis-b">Hepatitis B<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#hepatitis-b" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p><strong>Control of Blood, Blood Products, and Skin—Piercing Instruments</strong>: Because hepatitis B is transmitted by the serum of infected people, all blood, blood products, and instruments that pierce the skin and contact the vascular system are potential sources of infection. Some donor related precautions that reduce the incidence of hepatitis B are the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>Screening of donors blood for hepatitis B virus surface antigen (HbsAG), antibody to hepatitis B core antigen (anti-HBC), antibody to HbsAG (anti-HBs), and hepatitis B antigen (HbcAG).</li>
</ul>
<p>117</p>
<p>Musculoskeletal, Gastrointestinal and Oncology Nursing</p>
<ul>
<li>Use of volunteer rather than paid donors.</li>
<li>Registration of carriers</li>
<li>Sharing of accurate record between institutions</li>
<li>Testing of all pregnant women for HbsAG</li>
</ul>
<p>Other Measures: Using only the necessary amount of blood or blood products, cross checking lab. Results to reduce errors of reported results, avoiding commercially obtained blood, and encouraging clients who are having elective surgery to donate their own blood (autologous transfusion).</p>
<p>Use of disposable equipment especially needles and syringes, to reduce hepatitis transmission.</p>
<p>Personal Hygiene: Good personal hygiene reduces transmission. Clients with hepatitis B and hepatitis B carriers should not share razors, toothbrushes, washcloths, cigarettes, or other personal items.</p>
<p>Active Immunization: Hepatitis B vaccine provide active immunization before exposure to hepatitis B virus. It is given in three doses into the deltoid muscle. The first and second dose is given at the interval of one month. The third dose is administered at least 4 months after the initial dose. It is now included in the routine vaccination. The hepatitis B vaccine is highly recommended for all people in high risk categories for hepatitis B, including not only health care workers but also dialysis clients and attending personnel, clients requiring repeated transfusions, spouses of HbsAg-positive clients, people who have multiple sex partners, and prison workers.</p>
<p>Passive Immunization: Standard immune globulin contains antibodies against hepatitis B. However, given within 24 hours of exposure to hepatitis b or as soon as possible after exposure.</p>
<p>Hepatitis C</p>
<p>Transmission of hepatitis C is similar to that of hepatitis B. Many of the safety measures are useful in its prevention, although available evidence suggests that hepatitis C is less readily transmitted through sexual and household contacts than is hepatitis B. For passive immunization standard immune globulin after exposure to hepatitis C but there is no vaccine for active immunization against hepatitis C.</p>
<p>Hepatitis D</p>
<p>Because hepatitis D must coexist with hepatitis B, The hepatitis B vaccine can help prevent hepatitis D also. Same precautions can be taken for its prevention as for hepatitis B.</p>
<p>Hepatitis E</p>
<p>Because the hepatitis E virus is water borne and infection occurs by the fecal-oral route in various parts of the world, attention is given to matters of personal hygiene and sanitation to prevent the spread of this form of hepatitis.</p>
<p>Hepatitis G</p>
<p>Hepatitis g virus (HGV) is spread through contact with blood, blood products, and body fluids. HGV has been detected in 50% of IV drug users, 20% of people with haemophilia and 15% of clients with chronic hepatitis B or C.</p>

<p>Cirrhosis of the liver refers to replacement of normal cells by fibrous tissue which results in hardening or scarring of the liver. These tissue changes are irreversible. There are several types of cirrhosis of which Laennec’s type also called nutritional, portal or alcoholic cirrhosis is most common. We shall have a discussion on this type only, in this unit. It may also be caused by malnutrition associated with other diseases such as pancreatitis, diabetes mellitus and ulcerative colitis.</p>

<ul>
<li>The alcoholics who develop liver disease first develop faulty degeneration of the liver which leads to acute inflammation</li>
<li>Pressure rises in the portal system (which drains blood from the digestive organs) causing stasis and backup in digestive organs called portal hypertension</li>
<li>Bleeding esophageal varices results from increased amounts of blood being routed to the esophageal veins from the liver when it is damaged</li>
<li>The small vessels of esophagus become tortuous and fragile and start bleeding</li>
<li>Bleeding is abrupt and sudden</li>
<li>Hepatic coma may result from high blood ammonia levels when the liver is unable to convert the ammonia to urea.</li>

<p>Cholecystitis is inflammation of the gall bladder, which may be acute or chronic; it is usually by the presence of gall stones (cholelithiasis) which are made up of cholesterol, bile pigments and calcium; or it may be due to other obstructions in the bile passage. It is more common in women than in men; sedentary and obese persons are affected more often.</p>

<li>There is impaired circulation, edema and distension leading to ischemia, necrosis and gangrene.</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="4-4-4-acute-pancreatitis">4.4.4 Acute Pancreatitis<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#4-4-4-acute-pancreatitis" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Inflammation of the pancreas may be acute or chronic. Acute pancreatitis may result in autodigestion of the pancreas by its own enzymes.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="etiology-and-risk-factors">Etiology and Risk Factors<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#etiology-and-risk-factors" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>They are alcohol abuse, cholelithiasis, abdominal trauma, and metabolic factors.</p>
<p>Other causes include Hyperlipidaemia, Hypercalcaemia, Familial cases with no definite mechanism defined, pancreatic trauma such as penetrating or blunt external trauma, intraoperative manipulation or pancreatic ductal overextension during ERCP (endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography), pancreatic ischaemia during episodes of hypotensive shock and drugs such as estrogens.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="pathophysiology">Pathophysiology<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#pathophysiology" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>The etiologic mechanism of pancreatic damage remains unclear. The pathological changes occurring in the pancreas may be due to premature activation of proteolytic and lipolytic enzymes. These enzymes are normally activated in the duodenum. The pancreas normally release protease in an inactive form which later converts trypsinogen into trypsin in the intestine. In pancreatitis, however, activation of proteases and lipases occurs before secretion into the intestine. When these enzymes are activated before they are secreted into the intestine, pancreatic tissue damage occurs.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="clinical-manifestations">Clinical Manifestations<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#clinical-manifestations" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>Manifestations in clients presenting with acute pancreatitis are largely the result of activation of proteases and lipases and the resulting autodigestion of the pancreas. Manifestations vary from mild, nonspecific abdominal pain to profound shock with coma and death. The predominant clinical feature is abdominal pain caused by edematous distention of the pancreatic capsule, local peritonitis due to enzyme release into the peritoneum, ductal spasm, or pancreatic autodigestion stimulated by increased enzyme secretion when eating.</p>
<p>Pain normally begins in the mid-epigastrium region and achieves maximum intensity several hours later. An extreme umbilical or epigastric pain radiates to the back. Nausea and vomiting are frequently present.</p>
<p>Physical examination reveals a distressed, anxious client with abdominal distension and tenderness and with fever caused by paralytic ileus of the small bowel due to localized peritonitis.</p>
<p>Severe hemorrhagic pancreatitis may produce two distinctive features:</p>
<p>Turner’s sign (bluish discoloration of the left flank) and Cullen’s sign (bluish discoloration of the periumbilical area). Clients with severe pancreatitis may exhibit severe circulatory complications, such as hypotension, hypoperfusion, obtundation, pallor, cool and clammy skin, hypovolemia, and shock.</p>
<p>Chest film findings may show left basilar atelectasis, elevated left hemidiaphragm, and left pleural effusion. Abdominal films may reveal nonspecific abnormalities, such as 1) the presence of air in the duodenal loop, indicating duodenal ileus, 2) the “sentinel loop” sign, representing a dilated proximal jejunal loop, 3) the colon “cut off” sign, indicating distension of the transverse colon, 4) gallstones, or 5) pancreatic calcifications.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="medical-management">Medical Management<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#medical-management" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p><strong>Reduce pain:</strong> Pain is usually treated with narcotic analgesics, meperidine being the drug of choice. Morphine is contraindicated because it may cause spasm of the sphincter of the Oddi.</p>
<p><strong>Maintain volume status, electrolyte balance and nutritional status:</strong> It involves replacing lost body fluids, correcting hypovolemia, and restoring electrolyte balance. Clients with severe and persistent vomiting may require saline solutions containing potassium chloride. Treatment may involve attempts to suppress pancreatic exocrine function. Therapy to decrease these enzymes may include nasogastric suction or administration of histamine H2 receptor antagonists, antacids, anticholinergics, glucagons, calcitonin, somatostatin, and proglumide.</p>
<p><strong>Maintain pancreatic rest:</strong> It involves withholding food and liquids by mouth initially, because food ingestion increases pancreatic secretion, which may increase inflammation and pain. No food or fluids should be given orally until the client is free of pain and has bowel sounds. Clear liquids are then given, and gradual advancement to a regular low fat diet is prescribed. Clients with moderate to severe pancreatitis need to be supported nutritionally by total parenteral nutrition (TPN).</p>
<p><strong>Treat complications:</strong> Complications include pancreatic disorders (pancreatic abscess, infected necrosis), which may warrant surgery, or non pancreatic disorders (colonic or bile obstruction or metabolic, renal or pulmonary disorders). Clients with pulmonary or respiratory complications may require supportive measures, such as endotracheal intubation and positive pressure ventilation.</p>
<p><strong>Other measures:</strong> Calcium gluconate must be given intravenously if there is evidence of hypocalcemia with tetany.</p>
