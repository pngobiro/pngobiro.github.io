üìã Definition & Pathophysiology

Definition: Inflammation of the gastric or stomach mucosa.

Types of Gastritis

  • Acute: Lasting several hours to a few days.
  • Chronic: Repeated exposure to irritating agents or recurring episodes of acute gastritis.

Pathophysiology

  • Gastric mucosa membranes become oedematous and hyperaemic (congested with blood) and undergo erosion.
  • Secretes scanty juice with little acid.
  • Excessive mucus production.
  • Superficial ulceration.
  • Haemorrhage may occur.

ü¶† Causes & Risk Factors

Acute Gastritis

  • Diet: Highly seasoned or irritating foods.
  • Infection: Contamination with disease-causing micro-organisms.
  • Medication: Overuse of Aspirin and other NSAIDs.
  • Lifestyle: Excessive alcohol intake.
  • Other: Bile reflux, Radiation therapy.

Chronic Gastritis

Caused by prolonged inflammation associated with:

  • Benign or malignant ulcers of the stomach.
  • Bacteria: Helicobacter pylori.
  • Autoimmune disease: Pernicious anaemia.
  • Caffeine, NSAIDs.
  • Alcohol, smoking.
  • Chronic reflux of pancreatic secretions or bile into the stomach.

General Symptoms Chronic Specifics
  • Abdominal discomfort
  • Headache, Lassitude
  • Nausea, vomiting
  • Anorexia
  • Hiccups
  • Heartburn after meals
  • Belching, sour taste
  • Mild epigastric pain
  • Intolerance to fatty/spicy foods
  • Slight pain relieved by eating
  • Malabsorption: Evidence of Vitamin B12 deficiency (Pernicious anemia)

üî¨ Assessment & Diagnostic Findings

  • Acid Studies: Detection of Achlorhydria (absence), hypochlorhydria (low), or hyperchlorhydria (excessive) HCl.
  • Imaging: Upper GI x-ray series.
  • Direct Visualization: Endoscopy & histologic examination of biopsy.
  • Microbiology: Diagnosing Helicobacter Pylori.

üíä Medical Management

General Measures

  • Patient to refrain from alcohol & food until symptoms subside.
  • Provide non-irritating bland diet (when able to eat).
  • If symptoms persist, administer I.V fluids.
  • Haemorrhage: Blood transfusion, I.V Omeprazole.
  • Pharmacology: Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs), Antibiotics (for H. Pylori).

Emergency Management: Corrosive Gastritis

For gastritis due to strong acids or alkalis:

  • Dilute or neutralize the offending agent.
  • Contraindication: Avoid emetics (vomiting inducers) and lavage due to risk of perforation and damage to the oesophagus.

Chronic Gastritis Management

  • Modify patient's diet.
  • Promote rest and reduce stress.
  • Avoidance of alcohol and NSAIDs.
  • Initiating pharmacotherapy for H. Pylori.
06

üî™ Surgical Interventions

Used for severe cases, complications (obstruction, perforation), or reconstruction after partial gastrectomy.

Bilroth 1 (Gastroduodenostomy)

Reconstruction after partial gastrectomy (removal of the pylorus) in which the stomach (Gastric remnant) is anastomosed to the duodenum.

Note: Bilroth 1 is more physiologic, resulting in better protein and fat digestion.

  • Provide supportive therapy to patient and family.
  • Prepare patient for procedures (Endoscopy/Surgery).
  • Relieve anxiety due to pain or planned treatment.
  • Emergency measures for ingested acids/alkalis.

  • NBM (Nil By Mouth): For a few days during acute phase.
  • I.V Fluids: Maintain hydration.
  • Progression: Ice chips ‚Üí Clear liquids ‚Üí Solid foods (bland).
  • Avoid caffeinated beverages (CNS stimulants).
  • Discourage alcohol and cigarette use (refer for cessation).
‚ñ∂ Fluid Balance & Pain Relief
  • Monitor daily fluid Intake/Output.
  • Monitor electrolytes.
  • Watch for Hemorrhage: Hematemesis, Tachycardia, Hypotension.
  • Assess pain level and administer analgesics/sedatives.
  • Administer antacids and PPIs.
‚ñ∂ Home & Community Based Care
  • Teach patient self-care.
  • Evaluate knowledge about gastritis.
  • Review foods and substances to be avoided (Alcohol, NSAIDs, spicy foods).
