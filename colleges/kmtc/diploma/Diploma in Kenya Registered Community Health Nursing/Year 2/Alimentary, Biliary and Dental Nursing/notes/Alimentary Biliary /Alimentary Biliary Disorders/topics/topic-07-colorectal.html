ü©∫ Ulcerative Colitis

Definition: A recurrent ulcerative and inflammatory disease of the mucosal and submucosal layers of the colon and rectum.

  • Prevalence: Highest in Caucasians and people of Jewish heritage.
  • Complications: Accompanied by systemic complications and high mortality rate. Approximately 5% of patients develop colon cancer.

üå∞ Haemorrhoids

Overview

Haemorrhoids are dilated portions of veins in the anal canal. They are very common; by 50 years of age, about 50% of people have haemorrhoids.

Causes

  • Shearing of mucosa during defecation (sliding of vascular tissues).
  • Increased pressure in haemorrhoidal tissue (e.g., pregnancy).
Type Location Characteristics
Internal Above internal sphincter Not usually painful until they bleed or prolapse.
External Outside external sphincter Associated with severe pain, inflammation, and edema caused by thrombosis (clotting). May lead to ischemia and necrosis.

Management

Symptoms (itching, pain, bleeding) can often be relieved by conservative measures.

ü•¨

Conservative Treatment

  • Good Personal Hygiene: Keep area clean.
  • Avoid Straining: Reduce pressure during defecation.
  • Diet: High-residue diet (fruit, bran) and increased fluid intake to promote soft, bulky stools.
  • Supplements: Hydrophilic bulk-forming agents (e.g., psyllium) if diet is insufficient.
  • Symptom Relief: Warm compresses, Sitz baths, Analgesic ointments, Suppositories, Astringents (Witch hazel), Bed rest.

Interventional Treatments

‚ñ∂ Nonsurgical Procedures
  • Infrared Photocoagulation / Bipolar Diathermy / Laser Therapy: Used to affix mucosa to underlying muscle.
  • Injection Sclerotherapy: Effective for small, bleeding haemorrhoids.
  • Rubber-band Ligation: A rubber band is slipped over the internal haemorrhoid. Tissue becomes necrotic and sloughs off. Note: Can cause pain, secondary haemorrhage, or infection.
‚ñ∂ Surgical Procedures
  • Cryosurgical Haemorrhoidectomy: Freezing the haemorrhoid to cause necrosis. (Less used due to foul-smelling discharge and prolonged healing).
  • Laser (Nd:YAG): Excising haemorrhoids/tags. Quick and relatively painless.
  • Stapled Haemorrhoidopexy: Uses surgical staples for prolapsing haemorrhoids. Less pain than excision.
  • Haemorrhoidectomy (Excision): For advanced thrombosed veins. Removal of redundant tissue via clamp/cautery or ligation.

Post-Op: A small tube may be inserted to allow escape of flatus/blood. Gelfoam or Oxycel gauze may be applied.

üéóÔ∏è Colorectal Cancer

Tumors of the colon and rectum are relatively common. It is the third most common site of new cancer cases. The incidence increases with age (highest >85 years).

Etiology & Risk Factors

Exact cause unknown, but major risk factors include:

  • Demographics: Increasing age, Family history of colon cancer/polyps.
  • Medical History: Previous cancer/polyps, Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD), Gastrectomy, Genital/Breast cancer (in women).
  • Lifestyle: High alcohol consumption, Cigarette smoking, Obesity.
  • Diet: High-fat, High-protein (beef), Low-fiber diet.

Clinical Manifestations

Symptoms are determined by location, stage, and function of the affected segment. Common presenting symptoms include change in bowel habits and blood in stools.

Location Specific Symptoms
Right-Sided Lesions
  • Dull abdominal pain
  • Melena (black, tarry stools)
Left-Sided Lesions Associated with obstruction:
  • Abdominal pain and cramping
  • Narrowing stools
  • Constipation / Distention
  • Bright-red blood in stool
Rectal Lesions
  • Tenesmus (ineffective, painful straining)
  • Rectal pain
  • Feeling of incomplete evacuation
  • Alternating constipation and diarrhea

General Warning Signs

Symptoms often do not develop until disease is advanced. Watch for:

  • Unexplained anemia
  • Anorexia & Weight loss
  • Fatigue

Assessment & Diagnosis

Along with abdominal/rectal exam, the most important procedures are:

  • Fecal Occult Blood Testing: Screening tool.
  • Barium Enema: Imaging.
  • Colonoscopy: With biopsy or cytology smears. This (along with proctosigmoidoscopy) identifies the majority of cases.
