ü¶† Definition & Etiology

Definition: Peritonitis is inflammation of the peritoneum, the serous membrane lining the abdominal cavity and covering the viscera.

‚ö†Ô∏è

Critical Condition

Sepsis is the major cause of death from peritonitis. Shock may result from septicemia or hypovolemia.

Causes

It is usually a result of bacterial infection. Organisms come from diseases of the GI tract or internal reproductive organs.

  • External Sources: Injury or trauma (gunshot wound, stab wound).
  • Internal Organ Inflammation: Extension from organs outside the peritoneal area (e.g., kidney).
  • Common GI Causes: Appendicitis, perforated ulcer, diverticulitis, bowel perforation.
  • Medical Procedures: Abdominal surgery, peritoneal dialysis.

Common Bacteria Implicated

Escherichia coli, Klebsiella, Proteus, Pseudomonas, and Streptococcus.

Non-Gastrointestinal Causes

Pelvic infection via fallopian tube is responsible for a high proportion of non-gastrointestinal infection. Most common organisms are Chlamydia spp and Gonococci.

02

‚ö° Pathophysiology

Peritonitis is caused by leakage of contents from abdominal organs into the abdominal cavity (inflammation, infection, ischemia, trauma, or tumor perforation).

  1. Bacterial Proliferation: Occurs rapidly after leakage.
  2. Edema & Exudation: Tissue edema results; fluid in the peritoneal cavity becomes turbid with protein, WBCs, cellular debris, and blood.
  3. Hypermotility: Immediate response of the intestinal tract.
  4. Paralytic Ileus: Follows hypermotility, with accumulation of air and fluid in the bowel.

ü©∫ Clinical Manifestations

Symptoms

  • Pain: At first diffuse, then constant, localized, and more intense over the pathologic site. Aggravated by movement.
  • Tenderness & Rigidity: Affected area becomes extremely tender; muscles become rigid (board-like abdomen).
  • Rebound Tenderness: Present.
  • Systemic Signs: Anorexia, nausea, vomiting, fever (37.8¬∞C‚Äì38.3¬∞C), increased pulse rate. Progression leads to hypotension.

Diminished Pain Perception

Typical signs may be masked or diminished in:

  • Patients receiving corticosteroids or analgesics.
  • Patients with diabetes (advanced neuropathy).
  • Patients with cirrhosis who have ascites.

Blood Work WBC almost always elevated. Altered electrolytes (K, Na, Cl).
Abdominal X-ray Air and fluid levels; distended bowel loops.
Ultrasound Abscesses and fluid collections.

Peritoneal Aspiration Culture & Sensitivity studies to identify causative organisms.
05

üíä Medical & Surgical Management

Medical Management

Fluid, colloid, and electrolyte replacement is the major focus. Hypovolemia occurs because massive amounts of fluid move into the peritoneal cavity.

  • Fluid Resuscitation: Administration of isotonic solution (several liters).
  • Analgesics: Prescribed for pain.
  • Antiemetics: For nausea and vomiting.
  • Intestinal Intubation: Suction to relieve distention and promote function.
  • Oxygen Therapy: Nasal cannula/mask; intubation if septic shock occurs.
  • Antibiotics: Large doses of broad-spectrum IV antibiotics initiated early until specific organism is identified.

Surgical Management

Objectives: Remove infected material and correct the cause.

  • Procedures: Excision (appendix), resection with/without anastomosis (intestine), repair (perforation), drainage (abscess).
  • Ultrasound/CT-guided drainage: May allow avoidance or delay of surgery.
  • Fecal Diversion: May be needed with extensive sepsis.

üë©‚Äç‚öïÔ∏è Nursing Management

Intensive Care Monitoring

  • Blood Pressure: Monitored via arterial line if shock is present.
  • Hemodynamics: CVP or Pulmonary Artery Wedge Pressure monitoring.
  • Output: Accurate hourly urine output recording.
  • Ongoing Assessment: Pain, GI function, Fluid/Electrolyte balance.

Interventions

‚ñ∂ Pain Management & Positioning
  • Report nature/location of pain and any changes.
  • Administer analgesics.
  • Positioning: Place patient on the side with knees flexed. This decreases tension on abdominal organs.
‚ñ∂ Postoperative Care & Discharge
  • Monitor drains (character/volume of drainage). Ensure drains are not dislodged during moving.
  • Signs of Improvement: Decrease in temperature/pulse, softening of abdomen, return of peristaltic sounds, passing flatus/bowel movements.
  • Complications: Watch for wound evisceration and abscess formation. Sudden serosanguineous drainage suggests dehiscence.
  • Teaching: Care for incision/drains. Referral for home care if needed.
