üìñ Definition & Physiology

What is a Peptic Ulcer?

A peptic ulcer is an excavation (erosion) that forms in the mucosal wall of the stomach, pylorus, duodenum, or oesophagus. It occurs when the tissue cannot withstand the digestive action of gastric acid (HCl) and pepsin.

  • Prevalence: More likely to occur in the duodenum than in the stomach.
  • Age Group: Greatest frequency in people between 40 and 60 years of age.

Gastric Juice Components

Substance Function
Hydrochloric Acid (HCl) Creates acidic medium to facilitate pepsin action and kill germs.
Pepsin Enzyme that digests proteins into peptones.
Mucus Protects stomach walls from auto-digestion by HCl.

ü¶† Causes & Risk Factors

Helicobacter pylori (H. pylori)

Research confirms that peptic ulcers largely result from infection with the gram-negative bacteria H. pylori. Transmission occurs through ingestion of contaminated food/water or person-to-person contact.

Other Major Causes

  • Medications: Chronic use of NSAIDs inhibits mucus secretion that protects the mucosa.
  • Lifestyle: Excessive alcohol intake, smoking, and caffeine (stimulates acid secretion).
  • Genetics: Familial tendency. People with Type O blood are more susceptible.
  • Comorbidities: Chronic pulmonary disease or chronic renal disease.

Zollinger-Ellison Syndrome (ZES)

Rare condition characterized by:

  • Severe peptic ulcers.
  • Extreme gastric hyperacidity.
  • Gastrin-secreting tumors (gastrinomas) of the pancreas.

‚ö° Pathophysiology

Erosion occurs due to increased concentration/activity of acid-pepsin or decreased mucosal resistance. A damaged mucosa cannot secrete enough mucus to act as a barrier against HCl.

Stress Ulcers

Acute mucosal ulceration occurring after physiologically stressful events (burns, shock, severe sepsis, trauma).

  • Mechanism: Shock leads to decreased gastric mucosal blood flow and reflux of duodenal contents. Ischemia + Acid + Pepsin = Ulceration.
  • Reversibility: As the patient recovers, lesions typically reverse.

ü©∫ Clinical Manifestations

Symptoms may last for days/weeks, disappear, and reappear. 20-30% of patients may be asymptomatic before perforation/haemorrhage.

  • Pain: Dull, gnawing pain or burning sensation in the midepigastrium or back.
  • Relief Pattern: Pain is usually relieved by eating (food neutralizes acid) or alkali, but returns when stomach empties.
  • Tenderness: Sharply localized epigastric tenderness.
  • Pyrosis (Heartburn): Burning sensation in stomach/oesophagus moving to mouth.
  • Vomiting: Rare in uncomplicated cases; may indicate pyloric obstruction (scarring/spasm).
  • Bleeding: Hematemesis (coffee grounds) or melena (tarry stools).

NSAID-Induced Ulcer Prophylaxis

  • H2 Receptor Antagonists: Ranitidine, Famotidine, Nizatidine.
  • Misoprostol: Synthetic prostaglandin E1; cytoprotective effect.

Dietary Modification

  • Avoid extremes of food/beverage temperature.
  • Avoid alcohol, coffee (including decaf), and meat extracts.
  • Small frequent feedings are not necessary if antacids/blockers are used.
06

üî™ Surgical Management

Indicated for intractable ulcers (fail to heal after 12-16 weeks), life-threatening haemorrhage, perforation, obstruction, or ZES.

  • Vagotomy: Transecting nerves that stimulate acid secretion.
  • Pyloroplasty: Opening the pylorus to improve emptying.
  • Antrectomy: Removal of the pyloric portion of the stomach.

‚ñ∂ Pain & Anxiety Relief
  • Administer prescribed medications.
  • Encourage avoidance of aspirin, caffeine, and stimulating foods.
  • Meals should be eaten at regular intervals in a relaxed atmosphere.
  • Explain diagnostic tests and procedures to reduce anxiety.
  • Teach relaxation techniques (biofeedback, hypnosis).
‚ñ∂ Monitoring for Hemorrhage

Haemorrhage occurs in 10-20% of patients. Signs include hematemesis (bright red or coffee grounds) and melena.

  • Assess for faintness, dizziness, nausea.
  • Monitor VS for tachycardia, hypotension, tachypnea (signs of shock).
  • Monitor Hb/Hct levels.
  • Test stool for occult blood.
  • Monitor urinary output (detect anuria/oliguria).
  • Emergency: If bleeding recurs within 48h or >6-10 units of blood needed in 24h, surgery is likely.
