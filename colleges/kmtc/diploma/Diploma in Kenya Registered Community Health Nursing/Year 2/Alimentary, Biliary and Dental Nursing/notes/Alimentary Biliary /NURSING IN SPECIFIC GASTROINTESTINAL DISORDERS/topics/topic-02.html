Stomach & Intestinal Disorders

üìù Quiz Available

Some food may cause irritation and inflammation of the gastric or intestinal mucosa leading to nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain and diarrhoea. Gastritis and gastroenteritis are common disorders due to infected food. But peptic ulcers are more frequently seen in persons under stress or those who smoke or drink alcohol in excess. When such patient is experiencing life-threatening haemorrhage, perforation, obstruction or is not responding to medications, surgical intervention may be warranted. We shall now study some of the disorders of stomach and intestines, separately or together.

Gastritis

Gastritis: Gastritis is inflammation of the stomach resulting from substances that produce an irritation of the membrane. It may be caused by food infection, excessive ingestion of alcohol or excessive use of salicylates such as aspirin. When gastritis is accompanied by diarrhoea, it is called gastroenteritis which involve upper and lower GI tract, e.g. gastritis may indicate the presence of ulcers/malignancy or it may accompany uremia and liver disease.

History taking is very important. Keep the patient under observation for sometime. The signs and symptoms may be as follows:

  • if vomiting persists, give an antinausea drug and start I/V to prevent electrolyte imbalances,
  • observe for signs and dehydration and electrolyte imbalance,
  • treat the underlying causes, e.g. if caused by infection, appropriate antibiotics may be given, and
  • medications such as antispasmodics, anticholinergics or antacids may be given as prescribed.

Peptic Ulcer

A peptic ulcer is an excavation formed in the mucosal wall of the stomach, the pylorus, the duodenum, or the esophagus. A peptic ulcer is frequently referred to as a gastric, duodenal, or esophageal ulcer, depending on its location. It is caused by the erosion of a circumscribed area of mucous membrane. This erosion may extend as deeply as the muscle layers or through the muscle to the peritoneum. Peptic ulcers are more likely to be in the duodenum than in the stomach. Chronic gastric ulcers tend to occur in the lesser curvature of the stomach, near the pylorus.

Fig. 4.3: Peptic lesions may occur in the esophagus (esophagitis), stomach (gastritis) or duodenum (duodenitis)

Fig. 4.4: Locations of a peptic ulcer: a) Gastric, b) Duodenal

Pathophysiology

Erosion occurs when there is an increase in the concentration of gastric acid and pepsin or when there is a decrease in the normal resistance of the mucosa which acts as a protective barrier.

In times of stress the sympathetic nervous system inhibits the blood flow to the digestive organs and decreases the production of juices. In some people, chronic stress enhances the action of parasympathetic system on the abdominal organs thus stimulating gastric juice production and the entire digestive process leading to a peptic ulcer.

Severe injury or illness, burns, head injury or intracranial disease and ingestion of alcohol or drugs act on the ‚Äògastric mucosa and cause severe erosions called stress ulcers. It may be caused by a decrease in blood supply, which causes a decrease in energy metabolism within the gastric mucosa. The gastric mucosa barrier and its protective ability is disrupted and so the gastric juices in excess act on the mucosa and multiple lesions result.

Treatment

Once the diagnosis of peptic ulcer is established, the patient is informed that he can learn how to keep his problem under control, but he may expect remissions and recurrences. The line of treatment is primarily to control gastric secretions.

  • The goals of therapy are to relieve pain, heal the ulcer, prevent complications and prevent recurrences,
  • Reduce physical activity and help in relaxation,
  • Encourage the patient to seek counselling or psychotherapy to explore the emotional components causing him stress,
  • Diet regulation through the use of suitable foods, and restriction of irritating substances such as nicotine, caffeine, alcohol, spices and so on. Patient is the best person to choose his diet.
  • Control of gastric secretions: Gastric acidity can be managed with appropriate sedation and neutralization of the gastric juice at frequent and regular intervals with drugs, non irritating foods and antacids. Drugs that block the acid-secreting action of histamine (H, blockers), such as ranitidine, cimetidine or famotidine or drugs that produce an acid-resistant barrier over the ulcer such as sucralfate have been shown to have dramatic effect in healing duodenal ulcers.

Antispamodics may be given to reduce pylorospasm and intestinal motility.
Anticholinergic agents may be prescribed to inhibit gastric secretion.
Hospitalization can be limited to a few days, unless bleeding, obstruction, perforation or severe pain is present.

  • observe and record time and characteristic of pain
  • offer the drug as prescribed and as indicated
  • provide quiet environment
  • allow ample time to express feelings and concerns
  • encourage hydration to reduce anticholinergic side-effects and dilute the HCl in the stomach
  • give health teaching to promote healing of peptic ulcer
  • eat small, frequent meals to prevent gastric distension. With modem H‚ÇÇ blocker drug treatment a person could have 3 normal meals of his choice. Please ask your physician's opinion.
  • stop smoking
  • avoid food that increases gastric acid secretion or irritate gastric mucosa such as very spicy foods, alcohol, caffeine-containing foods and beverages, red or black pepper
  • eat meals in pleasant, relaxing surroundings to reduce acid secretion
  • avoid taking salicylates for prolonged time, avoid steroids etc.
  • avoid stressful situations.
  • observe for complications such as gastric haemorrhage, perforation and drug toxicity
  • observe and prevent complications after gastric resection such as:
    • Dumping syndrome i.e. rapid passage of food from the stomach to the jejunum, the food being hypertonic when high in Carbohydrates; draws fluid from circulating blood into the jejunum causing diaphoresis, faintness and palpitations
    • haemorrhage
    • pneumonia
    • pernicious anaemia
  • provide postoperative care after gastric resection
  • maintain a patent nasogastric tube to the suction apparatus
  • monitor vital signs, the colour and amount of nasogastric drainage, excessive bleeding, or the presence of bright red blood, and report it promptly
  • encourage deep breathing and coughing exercises and change of position
  • monitor intake and output
  • apply an abdominal binder

    • eat smaller meals at frequent intervals
    • avoid high carbohydrate diet
    • take liquids one hour before or after meals
    • lie down or rest after eating
  • prevent recurrence by teaching the patient to:
    • modify the dietary pattern, working and living pattern
    • maintain regularity in activities of daily living
    • continue regular follow ups.

Enteritis and Gastroenteritis

Inflammation of the intestine is called enteritis. Inflammation of the intestine accompanying gastritis is called gastroenteritis. It often occurs in conjunction with some infectious diseases such as typhoid fever, dysentery, intestinal tuberculosis and food poisoning. The signs and symptoms are usually abdominal cramps, fever, diarrhoea, dehydration and weakness. The cause can be identified by microscopic stool examination and cultures from the suspected food.

Pathophysiology

Acute gastroenteritis can be caused by bacterial or viral infection or by the effect of neurotoxins produced by bacteria. This results in increased secretions of water and salt into the gut lumen or an increase in motility, causing excretion of large amount of undigested food and fluid.

In later stage large amount of gas and foul-smelling stool results. With profuse diarrhoea, large amount of fluid and electrolytes may be lost leading to dehydration, hyponatremia, hypokalemia and metabolic alkalosis.

The major goals of treatment and nursing interventions in patients with gastroenteritis, include identifying the causative factor and give the appropriate antibiotics, keeping up the required electrolyte and fluid balance in the body, maintaining a strict intake and output chart to replace the fluids and electrolytes lost due to diarrhoea, and control transmission of infection.

Regional Enteritis or Crohn's Disease

Regional enteritis or Crohn's disease is an inflammatory process involving any part of the alimentary tract from mouth to anus, but usually involves the distal ileum and colon. It is a slowly progressive persistent inflammation and is a disorder of the lymphoid tissue.

Pathophysiology

Enlarged lymph nodes in the submucosa and Peyer's patches in the intestinal mucosa are usually present. These undergo superficial ulceration, granulomas and fissures. Cobblestone ulcerations form along the mucosal wall of the terminal ileum, cecum and ascending colon which form scar tissue and inhibit food and water absorption in the area.

Signs and Symptoms

The patient has severe pain in the right lower quadrant, malaise, fever, elevated WBC, mild diarrhoea and mucous but no blood and anaemia. A colonoscopy and proctosigmoidoscopy will help to rule out ulcerative colitis and diverticulitis and reveal skin lesions and cobblestone mucous. A biopsy will show presence of granulomas.

Treatment & Nursing Interventions

  • give high-calorie, high-protein, high-residue diet
  • vitamin supplements including B‚ÇÅ‚ÇÇ
  • medications such as anticholinergics, analgesics, antibiotics, and immunosuppressive
  • surgery when fistulas or intestinal obstruction occurs
  • advise rest in acute cases
  • assess the frequency of bowel movements and the appearance of stools
  • record intake, output and daily weights
  • assist patient in identifying irritating foods

Ulcerative Colitis

Ulcerative colitis, unlike Crohn's disease which is transmural and segmental, is a continuous disease of the bowel. Ulcerative colitis usually begins in the rectum and spreads upward in the colon slowly involving the mucous membrane and all layers of the intestinal wall.

Treatment

The line of treatment involves physical and emotional rest, psychological reassurance, and dietary management (high-protein, high-caloric and vitamins, restricted roughage). Medications include antiemetics, anticholinergics, antibiotics, sedatives, analgesics, tranquilizers, and antidiarrhoeals.

Surgery is indicated when haemorrhage, perforation of the colon, strictures and medically unresponsive toxic megacolon are present.

Two types of surgery may be performed:

  • a) Removal of diseased colon and rectum with the end of the ileum being brought out through the abdominal wall (ileostomy)
  • b) Continent ileostomy or ileal/Kock pouch. In this an intra-abdominal reservoir with a nipple valve is formed from the distal ileum to provide continence.

Nursing Intervention & Health Education

Ileostomy Care: If the patient has an ileostomy, keep him on a low residue diet for 6 weeks. Food blockage may occur with an ileostomy causing a bowel obstruction. In such cases, keep the patient in a knee-chest position and gently massage the area below the stoma.

Promote Nutrition: Start with bland low-residue foods. Introduce new foods gradually. Protect the skin from irritation. Affix an appliance with Karaya paste to prevent leakage around the stoma, empty it every 6 hours and change every 2-4 days.

Diverticulitis

Diverticulitis is inflammation of a diverticulum which is a blind outpouching or protrusion of intestinal mucosa through the muscular coat of the large intestine usually the sigmoid colon. It is common in men and women over 45 years of age. It may be acute or chronic.

Fig. 4.8: Diverticulitis

Etiology

a) atrophy or weakness of the bowel muscle
b) increased intra-lumen pressure
c) obesity
d) chronic constipations

Treatment and Nursing Intervention

  • Diverticulitis may be treated conservatively with medical intervention, which focuses on allowing the colon to rest
  • patients with acute diverticulitis are not given anything orally; they may have a nasogastric tube, and receive parenteral fluids until the pain, inflammation and temperature decrease
  • control of inflammation by administering antibiotics
  • surgery in case of complication such as haemorrhage, obstruction, abscesses or perforation
  • if colostomy is done nursing colostomy irrigation is to be done in the immediate postoperative period.

Appendicitis

Appendicitis is an inflammation of the appendix that develops most commonly in adolescents and young adults.

Signs and Symptoms

Subjective: Pain i.e., acute generalized abdominal pain comes in waves, starts in epigastrium or peritoneal region and gets localized at Meburney's point. Pain is acute and colicky. Nausea, rebound tenderness, loss of appetite, constipation.

Objective: Vomiting, low grade fever, coated tongue, bad breath, leucocytosis i.e., WBC count between 10,000 and 15,000/cu.mm.

Treatment

Surgical removal of the appendix without delay to decrease the chance of rupture and the risk of peritonitis. Prophylactic use of antibiotics postoperatively. Fluid and electrolyte maintenance. Analgesics for pain.

Peritonitis

Peritonitis is inflammation of the peritoneum. It can be caused by (a) infection from perforation of gastrointestinal tract or (b) by bacteria from any infected organ in the abdominal or pelvic cavity due to disease or injury. Peritonitis is a serious condition until the infection is under control.

Fig. 4.10: common primary causes of peritonitis

Signs and Symptoms

Severe abdominal pain, rebound tenderness, malaise, nausea. Board like abdomen, absence of bowel sounds, elevated temperature, shallow respiration, vomiting, signs of shock and circulatory failure.

Treatment

  • bed rest, withhold oral fluids
  • nasogastric aspiration under low suction
  • replacement of fluids and electrolytes
  • antibiotic therapy
  • surgery to correct the cause of peritonitis e.g. appendectomy, incision and drainage of abscesses or closure of a perforation.

üõë Intestinal Obstruction

Any condition that prevents the free passage of bowel contents through the intestine is considered as obstruction; it may be partial or complete and will occur either in small or large intestine. It could be mechanical or paralytic.

Etiology

An intestinal obstruction may be due to several causes:

  • strangulated hernia
  • volvulus (twisting of the intestines)
  • carcinoma of the bowel
  • postoperative adhesions
  • paralytic ileus
  • intussusception (telescoping of the bowel on itself)

Fig. 4.11: Two courses of intestinal obstruction: (a) Intussusception, (b) Volvulus of the sigmoid colon

Treatment

The line of treatment is to decompress above and below the site of obstruction in the lumen of the gut.

  • intestinal decompression by means of a nasogastric, Cantor or Miller-Abbott tube.
  • restriction of oral intake, administration of parenteral fluid and electrolytes
  • surgical correction of the cause (adhesions, hernias)
  • colostomy, cecostomy or ileostomy as necessary.

Hernia

A hernia is a protrusion of an organ or structure from its normal cavity through a congenital or acquired defect. The abdominal cavity has a muscular wall that provides support for most of the organs of digestion. The wall may become weakened at some point, permitting part of an organ to protrude through the weakened area.

üöΩ Anorectal Disorders

Haemorrhoids

Haemorrhoids are varicosities of lower rectum and anus that can be internal and external. Enlarged veins that are distended with blood are called varicose veins, when they occur on the rectal walls they are called haemorrhoids.

Fig. 4.14: Various types of anal lesions: (a) Fistula, (b) Fissure, and (c) External and internal haemorrhoids

Treatment & Postoperative Care

Treatment includes application of a topical and analgesic suppository, warm compresses, Sitz bath, use of laxatives, and surgical removal (haemorrhoidectomy). Postoperative care involves warm Sitz bath after each bowel movement, liquid/soft diet for first few days, relieving perianal irritation, and administering retention enema on 3rd day.

Rectal Fissure & Fistula

Rectal fissure (fissure-in-ano) is an ulceration or tear of the lining of the anal canal usually on the posterior wall. It may occur due to trauma caused by passage of hard-formed stool that overstretches the anal lining.

Ano Rectal Fistula (Fistula-in-ano) is a sinus tract between two body cavities or between a body cavity and the outside. An abscess in the rectum or anus can develop into a fistula. Fistula is a chronic condition for which surgery is the only cure.

ü•ó Malabsorption Syndrome

Malabsorption syndrome refers to a group of symptoms resulting from inadequate absorption of fat in the small intestine. Since vitamin A, D, E and K, the fat-soluble vitamins, require fat for absorption, decreasing absorption of these vitamins usually result in fat malabsorption. Also, fat malabsorption often is accompanied by decreased absorption of protein, and minerals.

Pathophysiology

Malabsorption results from: alterations of digestion, alterations in transportation of nutrients, alteration of transportation of fat from the villi through the lymphatic or circulatory systems, or lactase deficiency.

Signs and Symptoms

Subjective: bulky stool, flatulence, abdominal distension, weakness, anorexia, weight loss.

Objective: stool (light, greasy, bulky, mushy with foul odour - steatorrhoea), watery fermentative diarrhoea (lactose deficiency), vitamin deficiency signs, protein deficiency signs.

Treatment

  • If there is intolerance to specific substance, omit it from the diet
  • In adult lactase deficiency, avoid milk products and added lactase
  • If intolerance is to wheat (adult celiac disease) avoid all cereal grains
  • In acute malabsorption, provide total parenteral nutrition
