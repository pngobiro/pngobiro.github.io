ü´Ä Organs of the Hepatic and Biliary System

Liver

Located in the upper right portion of the abdominal cavity just under the diaphragm; vascular organ; protected by the rib cage. Blood flow into the liver is from two sources:

  • Portal vein carries venous blood from the stomach, intestines, pancreas, and spleen into the liver. The venous blood is rich in nutrients absorbed from the gastrointestinal (GI) system.
  • Hepatic artery provides oxygenated blood to the liver.

The portal vein and hepatic artery enter the liver via a common vessel and flow through the liver tissue; blood then leaves the liver via the hepatic vein and empties into the inferior vena cava. Because of pressure differences in the hepatic and portal veins, the liver may normally store 200 to 400 mL of blood.

The liver produces approximately 600 to 1200 mL of bile daily. Bile drains from the liver via the common bile duct. The common bile duct enters the duodenum, either close to or in conjunction with the pancreatic duct. It can sustain 90% damage with loss of tissue and still remain functional.

Gallbladder

A muscular, elastic sac located on the undersurface of the liver. The cystic duct from the gallbladder joins the common bile duct from the liver. The gallbladder is capable of storing 20 to 50 mL of bile.

When food enters the duodenum, the gallbladder contracts; the sphincter of Oddi, which controls the release of bile, relaxes; and bile enters the intestine via the common duct. When the sphincter of Oddi closes, bile flows back into the gallbladder for storage. The primary function of the gallbladder is concentration and storage of bile.

‚öôÔ∏è Functions of the Liver

  1. Synthesis of absorbed nutrients.
  2. Serum glucose regulation. Glucose from carbohydrate metabolism is converted to glycogen, which is stored in the liver and released according to the body's needs. Gluconeogenesis refers to the ability of the liver to produce glucose from noncarbohydrate sources, such as amino acids and the glycerol portion of fats. This process is initiated when the body's requirements for glucose are in excess of the stored glycogen in the liver.
  3. Lipid (fat) metabolism. Preliminary breakdown of fatty acids as a source of body energy. When the glucose available for metabolism is limited, fatty acids may be broken down for production of energy. This results in formation of ketone bodies. Utilization of fatty acids for production of cholesterol, lipoprotein, and phospholipid.
  4. Protein metabolism. Synthesis of plasma proteins. Deaminization: breakdown of amino acids for energy or for conversion to glucose or fat. Synthesis of nonessential amino acids. Ammonia is a by-product of protein metabolism. It is converted to urea by the liver and excreted by the kidneys. Synthesis of prothrombin for normal clotting mechanisms. Vitamin K is necessary for adequate prothrombin production.
  5. Vitamin and mineral storage. Produces and stores vitamins A and D. Vitamin B12 and iron are stored in the liver.
  6. Drug metabolism: barbiturates, amphetamines, and alcohol are metabolized by the liver.
  7. Production of bile and bile salts.
    1. Bile is continuously formed in the liver.
    2. Bile salts are produced in the liver; cholesterol is a necessary constituent of bile salts.
    3. Bile and bile salts emulsify fat for digestion and absorption in the intestine.
    4. Bilirubin, a bile pigment, is excreted by the liver.
      • The spleen removes and breaks down the hemoglobin in worn-out red blood cells. This results in the production of bilirubin.
      • Bilirubin is carried from the spleen to the liver for excretion.
      • Bilirubin is conjugated in the liver (made water soluble) and secreted into the bile.
        • A small amount of unconjugated bilirubin (indirect) is released into the circulation and is not water soluble.
        • Conjugated bilirubin (direct) has been processed through the liver to form a water-soluble substance.
        • Conjugated bilirubin is secreted into the bile and flows into the small intestine for fat digestion.
        • Conjugated bilirubin is converted into urobilinogen in the large intestine and is reabsorbed and returned to the liver or excreted in feces. Normally, only a small amount of urobilinogen is excreted in the urine.

üìã System Assessment

  1. History
    1. History of liver, gallbladder, or jaundice problems.
    2. History of bleeding problems.
    3. History of reproductive problems.
    4. Medication intake.
    5. Recent association with anyone with jaundice.
    6. Alcohol consumption.
  2. Physical assessment
    1. Inspection.
      • Skin: Presence of vascular angiomas, skin lesions, or petechiae; Hydration status; Color of the skin (jaundiced); Presence of peripheral edema.
      • Abdomen: Evidence of jaundice; Contour of the abdomen; Presence of visible abdominal wall veins.
    2. Palpation of the abdomen: Pain, tenderness, presence of distention; Hepatomegaly, splenomegaly.
  3. Nutritional assessment: Weight gain or loss; dietary intake; Problems of anorexia, nausea and vomiting.

‚ö†Ô∏è Pathophysiology of Jaundice

Jaundice may begin so gradually that it is not noticed immediately. The increased levels of bilirubin cause a yellowish discoloration of the skin. It may be first observed as a yellow color in the sclera of the eyes. Serum bilirubin levels must exceed $2\text{ mg/dL}$ for jaundice to occur. The yellow discoloration is due to deposits of bilirubin in the skin and body tissue.

Types of Jaundice

‚ñ∂ 1. Hemolytic Jaundice

Occurs with an increase in the breakdown of red blood cells, which causes an increase in the amount of unconjugated bilirubin in the blood.

  • The liver cannot handle the increased level of unconjugated bilirubin. The bilirubin is not water soluble; therefore it cannot be excreted. Unconjugated bilirubin is lipid soluble and is capable of entering nerve cells and causing brain damage.
  • The increased production of urobilinogen will increase the amount of bilirubin excreted in the urine and feces.

Causes:

  • Blood transfusion reactions
  • Sickle cell crisis
  • Hemolytic anemias
  • Hemolytic disease of the newborn
‚ñ∂ 2. Hepatocellular Jaundice

Results from the inability of the liver to clear normal amounts of bilirubin from the blood.

  • Increase in serum levels of unconjugated and conjugated bilirubin.

Causes:

  • Hepatitis
  • Cirrhosis
  • Hepatic cancer
‚ñ∂ 3. Obstructive Jaundice

Results from an impediment to bile flow through the liver and the biliary system. The obstruction may be within the liver, or it may be outside the liver.

Causes:

  • Hepatitis
  • Liver tumors
  • Cirrhosis
  • Obstruction of the common bile duct by a stone

üî¨ Diagnostic Procedures
Procedure Nursing Implications
Liver biopsy
Percutaneous needle aspiration of liver tissue
  1. Informed consent procedure.
  2. Client's status is NPO for 6 hours before procedure.
  3. Blood coagulation study results should be available on the chart before biopsy procedure.
  4. Immediately before needle insertion, have client take a deep breath, exhale completely, and hold breath. This immobilizes the chest wall and decreases the risk for penetration of the diaphragm with the needle.
  5. Keep client on bed rest for 12-14 hr. Client should be positioned on the right side for 2 hr postprocedure to apply pressure and decrease risk for hemorrhage.
  6. Assess for complications of pneumothorax and hemorrhage immediately after biopsy; assess for right upper abdominal pain or referred shoulder pain; observe for development of bile peritonitis.
‚ö†Ô∏èALERT

Monitor status of client after a procedure: Position the client on right side with a pillow under the costal margin to facilitate compression of the liver.
Percutaneous transhepatic cholangiography (PTC)
IV injection of radiopaque dye to visualize the biliary duct system
  1. Client's status is NPO for 8 hr before the test.
  2. Assess for sensitivity to iodine.
  3. Evaluate for iodine reaction after the test.
  4. Client should drink large amounts of fluid after test to increase excretion of dye.
Hepatobiliary scintigraphy (HIDA)
Shows size, shape and position of biliary system. Radionuclide (Tc-99m) injected IV; client positioned under a camera or counter to record distribution of tracer
  1. Explain to client that traces of radionuclide pose minimal danger.
  2. Needs to lie flat during scanning procedure.
Gallbladder ultrasound
Uses high-frequency sound waves to examine the gallbladder; provides information about presence of tumors and patency of vessels and detects gallstones
  1. Client is NPO for 8-12 hours, since gas can reduce quality of images and food can cause gallbladder contraction.
  2. Explain to client that a conductive gel (lubricant) will be applied to the skin and a transducer placed on the area.
Endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography (ERCP)
Fiberoptic endoscope and fluoroscopy inserted orally, descended into duodenum, then into common bile duct and pancreatic ducts, where contrast medium is injected for visualization of the structures.
  1. Client is NPO for 8 hours before procedure.
  2. Explain that sedative will be given before and during procedure.
  3. Check for allergy to contrast medium.
  4. Informed consent must be signed.
  5. Check vital signs‚Äîmonitor for perforation or infection; pancreatitis is most common complication.
  6. Check for return of gag reflex before giving fluids.
