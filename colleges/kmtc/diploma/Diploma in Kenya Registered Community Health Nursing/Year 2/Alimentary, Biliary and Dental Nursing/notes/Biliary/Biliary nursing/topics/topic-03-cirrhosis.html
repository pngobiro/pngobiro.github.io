üîç Pathophysiology & Types

Hepatic cirrhosis is a chronic, progressive disease of the liver, characterized by degeneration and destruction of liver cells. Liver regeneration is disorganized and results in the formation of scar tissue, which in time, will exceed the amount of normal liver tissue.

Types of Cirrhosis

  • Alcoholic (Portal/Nutritional/La√´nnec's): Associated with alcohol abuse; accumulation of fat in liver cells leading to widespread inflammation and destruction of liver cells, which results in widespread scar formation.
  • Postnecrotic: Can follow hepatitis or exposure to hepatotoxin.
  • Biliary: Diffuse fibrosis and scarring caused by chronic biliary obstruction and infection.
  • Cardiac: Increase in venous pressure associated with long-term right-sided heart failure.

‚ö†Ô∏è Complications of Cirrhosis

‚ñ∂ 1. Portal Hypertension

Structural changes in the liver result in obstruction of normal hepatic blood flow, which causes increased pressure in the portal circulation. Collateral circulation develops as the body attempts to reduce the increased portal pressure. Common areas for collateral channels are:

  • Lower esophagus at the area of the gastric vein.
  • Anterior abdominal wall.
  • Parietal peritoneum.
  • Rectum.

Consequences:

  • Esophageal varices form from collateral vessels in the lower esophagus.
  • Splenomegaly occurs from the increase of congestion in the splenic bed; may lead to leukopenia and thrombocytopenia.
  • Caput medusae: dilated veins around the umbilical area.
‚ñ∂ 2. Peripheral Edema and Ascites

Edema results from:

  • Impaired liver synthesis of protein, resulting in decreased colloid osmotic pressure (hypoalbuminemia).
  • Portal hypertension, malnutrition.

Ascites: accumulation of serous fluid in the peritoneal cavity.

  • With increased pressure in the liver, excessive protein and water leak out of the liver into the abdomen.
  • The presence of hypoalbuminemia results in decreased colloid osmotic pressure, which facilitates movement of fluid and protein into the abdominal cavity.
  • Hyperaldosteronism causes increased amounts of sodium and water to be retained.
‚ñ∂ 3. Hepatic Encephalopathy (Coma)

Results from the inability of the liver to detoxify ammonia. Ammonia is a by-product of protein metabolism. Large quantities of ammonia remain in the systemic circulation and cross the blood-brain barrier, producing toxic neurologic effects.

üìã Assessment

Risk Factors/Etiology

  • Excessive, prolonged alcohol consumption.
  • Nutritional deficiencies.
  • Frequently, a combination of alcoholism and nutritional deficiency.
  • Predisposing chronic hepatic and biliary infections.

Clinical Manifestations

  • GI disturbances: anorexia, indigestion, change in bowel habits, weight loss.
  • Changes in the skin: Jaundice (hepatocellular and biliary), pruritis; Spider angiomas on the face, neck, and shoulders; Palmar erythema (reddened areas on the palms).
  • Blood disorders: anemia.
  • Coagulation disorders: spontaneous bruising from thrombocytopenia.
  • Changes in sexual characteristics: gynecomastia, impotence in males; amenorrhea, vaginal bleeding in females.
  • Peripheral neuropathy: probably caused by inadequate intake of vitamin B complex.
  • Hepatomegaly, splenomegaly.
  • Portal hypertension: Esophageal varices that bleed easily; Hemorrhoids; Collateral veins visible on abdominal wall; Development of edema and ascites.
  • Portal-systemic encephalopathy:
    • Changes in mental responsiveness.
    • Level of concentration: ask client to repeat a series of numbers; if client has encephalopathy, he or she will be unable to repeat a 4- to 6-digit sequence.
    • Memory: determine client's ability to recall recent events (yesterday or past week) and remote events (last year).
    • Apraxia: deterioration in writing and drawing; inability to construct or draw a figure.
    • Asterixis (flapping tremors): clients with asterixis are unable to hold their hands out in front of them when asked; a flapping of the hands will occur.
    • Fetor hepaticus: musty, sweet odor to breath due to inability of liver to degrade digestive products.

üíä Medical Treatment

General Management

  • Rest.
  • Dietary modification: increase calories and carbohydrates; protein and fat may be consumed as tolerated.
  • Vitamin supplement, especially vitamin B complex.
  • Abstinence from alcohol.

Management of Ascites

  • IV albumin or other volume replacement after a high volume paracentesis.
  • Sodium restriction in diet.
  • Fluid restriction for cases of severe ascites.
  • Diuretics.
  • Paracentesis for temporary relief.
  • Peritoneovenous shunt (LeVeen shunt): a surgical procedure for reinfusion of ascitic fluid into venous system.
  • Surgical procedures to decrease portal hypertension by shunting portal blood flow: transjugular intrahepatic portosystemic shunt (TIPS).

Management of Esophageal Varices

  • Blood transfusions to restore volume from bleeding varices.
  • Administration of IV vasopressin (Pitressin) produces vasoconstriction of the splanchnic arterial bed, decreases portal blood flow, and portal hypertension.
  • Endoscopic sclerotherapy: injection of a sclerosing agent directly into esophageal varices; bleeding may recur because there has been no reduction in portal hypertension.
  • Endoscopic ligation or banding of the varices: often used in combination with sclerotherapy.
  • Balloon tamponade: mechanical compression of bleeding varices via esophageal gastric balloon tamponade (Minnesota or Sengstaken-Blakemore tube).
  • Shunting surgical procedures: decrease portal hypertension by shunting portal blood flow; usually performed after second major bleeding episode.
  • Management to prevent bleeding: beta adrenergic blockers (Inderal), repeated sclerotherapy, endoscopic ligation, and portosystemic shunts.

Management of Encephalopathy

  • Restriction of dietary protein intake.
  • Neomycin: decreases the normal flora in the intestines to reduce bacterial activity on protein.
  • Lactulose: used to reduce the amount of ammonia in the blood of clients with liver disease by drawing ammonia from the blood into the colon where it is removed from the body. May also be used to treat constipation, because it pulls water into the colon to facilitate movement of waste through the GI system.
  • Control of GI hemorrhage to decrease protein available in the intestine.

Goal: To promote health in the client with cirrhosis

  • Proper diet: increased protein as tolerated, adequate carbohydrates, vitamin supplements.
  • Adequate rest.
  • Avoid potential hepatotoxic over-the-counter drugs (aspirin and acetaminophen).
  • Check all body secretions for frank or occult blood.
  • Abstinence from alcohol.
  • Attention and care should be given the alcoholic client without being judgmental or moralizing.
  • Client should understand symptoms indicative of complications and when to seek medical advice.
  • Regular medical checkups.
üçΩÔ∏è ALERT

Monitor client's hydration and nutritional status.

Goal: To maintain homeostasis and promote liver function

  • Rest and activity schedule based on clinical manifestations and lab data.
  • Measures to prevent complications of immobility.
  • Assist client to maintain self-esteem: Maintain positive, accepting atmosphere; Encourage ventilation of feelings.
  • Promote nutritional intake:
    • Good oral hygiene; between-meal nourishment.
    • Provide food preferences when possible.
    • Administer antiemetic before meals, if necessary.
    • Iron and vitamin supplements, especially vitamin B complex.
    • Nasogastric or parenteral feeding, if client is unable to maintain adequate intake.
  • Decrease discomfort of pruritus caused by jaundice: cool rather than warm baths, avoid excessive soap.
  • Good skin care to prevent breakdown.
  • Evaluate serum electrolyte levels, especially potassium and sodium levels, because of the use of diuretics to decrease ascites and edema.
  • Monitor temperature closely because of increased susceptibility to infection.
  • Assess for bleeding tendencies and prevent trauma to the mucous membranes.
  • Measure abdominal girth to determine whether it is increasing from ascitic fluid.

Goal: If esophageal varices are present, decrease risk for active bleeding

  • Soft, nonirritating foods.
  • Discourage straining at stool.
  • Decrease esophageal reflux.
  • No salicylate compounds (aspirin).
  • Evaluate for active bleeding: Monitor vital signs; Assess for melena and hematemesis.

Goal: To decrease bleeding from esophageal and gastric varices

  • Gastric lavage with iced saline solution.
  • Assess and prevent complications associated with sclerotherapy:
    • Client is sedated and the throat is anesthetized before the procedure.
    • By means of endoscopy, the physician injects the sclerosing agent into the varices.
    • Bleeding from the varices should stop within minutes.
    • Client may experience chest discomfort for 2 to 3 days; administer an analgesic.
    • Esophageal perforation and ulceration are complications associated with treatment; observe client for development of severe chest pain.
    • Observe for return of active bleeding.

Goal: To decrease esophageal bleeding by using an esophageal tamponade balloon

  • Constant observation is required while the balloon is inflated.
  • The client is to receive absolutely nothing by mouth; provide frequent oral and nasal hygiene.
  • Constant tension/traction is applied to maintain the pressure against the esophageal sphincter by the gastric balloon. The gastric balloon is not to be deflated while tension is present and the esophageal balloon is inflated.
  • Keep the head of the bed elevated to decrease gastric regurgitation and nausea.
  • Keep scissors at the bedside in case the esophageal balloon moves into the oropharynx area and causes obstruction of the trachea. If this should occur, the lumen to the esophageal balloon should be cut to immediately deflate the balloon and relieve the obstruction.

Goal: To assess for and prevent complications associated with ascites

  • Decrease sodium intake.
  • Administer diuretics, potassium supplements.
  • Daily measurements of abdominal girth.
  • Maintain semi-Fowler's position to decrease pressure on the diaphragm.
  • Assess weight daily.
  • Monitor pulse oximetry for indications of respiratory distress.

Goal: To assess for and prevent complications of hepatic encephalopathy

  • Frequent assessment of responsiveness.
  • Assess for changes in level of orientation and motor abnormalities (asterixis).
  • Decrease production of ammonia: Increase carbohydrates and fluids; Decrease activity, because ammonia is a by-product of metabolism.
  • GI bleeding will increase ammonia levels as a result of the breakdown of red blood cells.
  • Lactulose to promote excretion of ammonia in the stool; diarrhea may occur.
  • Nonabsorbable intestinal antibiotics will decrease protein breakdown.
  • Prompt treatment of hypokalemia.

Goal: To provide appropriate preoperative and postoperative care if surgical procedure is indicated

  • Anastomosis of the high-pressure portal system to the low-pressure systemic venous system to create a shunt to decrease portal hypertension (portosystemic shunt), thereby decreasing problems with esophageal varices and ascites.
  • Client is at increased risk for postoperative complications: Hemorrhage, electrolyte imbalance; Seizures, delirium tremens.
  • Surgical procedures do not alter course of progressive hepatic disease.
