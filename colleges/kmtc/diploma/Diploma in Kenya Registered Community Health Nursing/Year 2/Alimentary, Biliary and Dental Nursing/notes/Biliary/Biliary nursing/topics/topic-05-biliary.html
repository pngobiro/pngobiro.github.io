üîç Overview & Pathophysiology

  • Cholelithiasis is the presence of stones in the gallbladder; this is the most common form of biliary disease.
  • Cholecystitis is an inflammation of the gallbladder, which is frequently associated with stones; this condition may be acute or chronic.

Risk Factors/Etiology

Cholelithiasis

  • Supersaturation of bile with cholesterol causes precipitate to occur.
  • Conditions upsetting cholesterol and bile balance include infection and disturbances of cholesterol metabolism.
  • Increased incidence in females, especially during pregnancy.
  • Increased incidence after age 40; obesity.

Cholecystitis

  • Associated with stones.
  • Escherichia coli is common bacteria involved.
  • May also be associated with neoplasms, anesthesia, or adhesions.
üë¥ OLDER ADULT PRIORITY

Incidence of gallstones increases with age. Older adults are more likely to go from asymptomatic gallstones to serious complications of gallstones without biliary colic.

Clinical Manifestations

‚ñ∂ Cholelithiasis

Severity of symptoms depends on the mobility of the stone and whether obstruction occurs.

  • Epigastric distress, feeling of fullness.
  • Abdominal distention.
  • Vague pain in the right upper quadrant after consumption of meals high in fat.

Obstruction of cystic ducts by stones (Biliary Colic):

  • Severe abdominal pain radiating to the back and shoulder.
  • Nausea, vomiting, tachycardia, diaphoresis.
  • Pain occurs 3-6 hours after consumption of a heavy meal, especially if high in fat.
  • Jaundice may occur with obstruction of bile flow.
  • Urine may become very dark, and stools may be clay colored.
‚ñ∂ Cholecystitis
  • Abdominal guarding, rigidity, rebound tenderness.
  • Fever.
  • Pain exacerbated by deep breathing.
  • Onset may be sudden with severe pain.

üíä Medical & Surgical Treatment

Cholecystitis Management

  • Anticholinergics to decrease secretions and promote relaxation of the gallbladder.
  • Analgesics: hydromorphone (Dilaudid) or morphine.
  • Antibiotics.
  • Atropine and dicyclomine (Bentyl) will relieve spasms and decrease pain.
  • Ketorolac (Toradol) may be used to decrease spasms and pain in older adults.

Surgical Management

Cholecystectomy: Surgical removal of the stones/gallbladder.

Laparoscopic Cholecystectomy

  • Three small incisions are made.
  • Decreases risk to client.
  • Day surgery or overnight stay.
  • Early ambulation and decreased pain.

Common postop problem of referred pain to the shoulder due to $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ that was not released or absorbed by body, which can irritate the phrenic nerve and diaphragm causing difficulty breathing.

üë©‚Äç‚öïÔ∏è Nursing Interventions

Goal: To decrease pain and inflammatory response

  • Low-fat liquid diet during acute attack.
  • Low-fat solids added, as tolerated.
  • IV fluids and gastric decompression if nausea and vomiting are severe.
  • Antibiotics and analgesics.
  • Assess for indications of infection.

Goal: To maintain homeostasis and prevent complications after cholecystectomy

  • General postoperative care for clients having abdominal surgery.
  • Evaluate tolerance to diet and progress diet gradually to low-fat solids.
  • Penrose drain may be in place; client will frequently have large amounts of serosanguineous drainage; change dressing as indicated.
  • Sims' position to facilitate the movement of $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ gas pocket away from the diaphragm.

T-Tube Management

A T-tube may be used to maintain patency of bile duct and to facilitate bile drainage until edema subsides. Typically not placed or used after a laparoscopic cholecystectomy.

  • Maintain tube to gravity drainage.
  • Observe amount and color of bile drainage (bloody initially, then greenish-brown).
  • Do not irrigate or clamp tube; do not raise tube above the level of the gallbladder.
  • Observe for bile drainage around the tube.
  • Drainage is usually around $500\text{ mL}$ per day for several days after surgery; drainage will gradually decrease, and the doctor will remove the tube.
  • Monitor urine and stool for changes in color.

Goal: Patient Education

  • Dietary teaching regarding low-fat diet.
  • Weight reduction, if appropriate.
  • Avoid heavy lifting.
  • Understand symptoms indicating bile obstruction (i.e., stool and urine changes) and advise physician accordingly.
