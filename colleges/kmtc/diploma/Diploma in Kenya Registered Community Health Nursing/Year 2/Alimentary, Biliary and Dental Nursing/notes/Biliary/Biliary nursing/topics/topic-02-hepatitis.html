üîç Overview & Types

Widespread inflammation of the liver tissue is called hepatitis. The inflammatory process causes hepatic cell degeneration and necrosis. Hepatitis A is generally self-limiting with liver regeneration and complete recovery. Hepatitis B and hepatitis C are more serious and can progress to total destruction of the liver.

‚ñ∂ Non-Viral Types
  • Toxic hepatitis: Systemic poisons (e.g., carbon tetrachloride, gold compounds).
  • Drug-induced hepatitis: Caused by medications such as isoniazid (INH), Diuril, methotrexate, methyldopa.
  • Autoimmune hepatitis: Cause is unknown; associated with other autoimmune diseases. Treated with corticosteroids and immunosuppressants.
‚ö†Ô∏è ALERT

Follow infection control guidelines; standard precautions include blood and body fluids. Teach health promotion information. Prevent transmission in the hospital in addition to teaching the importance of personal hygiene.

üìã Assessment

Regardless of the type of hepatitis, the clinical picture is similar. All clients experience inflammation of the liver tissue and exhibit similar symptoms.

Clinical Phases

  • Anicteric phase
    • Anorexia, nausea, malaise, headache.
    • Upper right quadrant discomfort.
    • Low-grade fever, hepatomegaly.
  • Icteric phase (jaundiced)
    • Dark urine caused by increased excretion of bilirubin.
    • Pruritus (itching).
    • Stools light and clay colored.
    • Liver remains enlarged and tender.
  • Posticteric phase (after jaundice)
    • Malaise, easily fatigued.
    • Hepatomegaly remains for several weeks.
üë∂ PEDIATRIC PRIORITY

Children often have diarrhea, and their symptoms are frequently attributed to gastroenteritis rather than hepatitis. Jaundice is seldom noted. Anicteric hepatitis (absence of jaundice) frequently occurs in children.

Onset Characteristics

  • Hepatitis A: Onset is more acute; symptoms are generally less severe.
  • Hepatitis B: Onset is more insidious; symptoms are more severe.
  • Hepatitis C: Most cases are asymptomatic or mild, but they tend to be persistent and lead to chronic liver disease.
03

üíâ Diagnostics & Treatment

Diagnostics

  • Increased alanine aminotransferase (ALT), aspartate aminotransferase (AST), and serum bilirubin levels.
  • Presence of HBsAg in serum of client with hepatitis B.
  • Presence of anti-HAV antibodies in blood with hepatitis A.
  • ELISA is initial screening test for hepatitis C. Recombinant immunoblot assay (RIBA) is confirmation test.

Treatment

  • Medication: No specific medications for HAV. Chronic HBV and HCV may be treated with $\alpha$-interferon and antivirals.
  • Nutrition: Encourage good nutrition; no specific dietary modifications; client will probably not tolerate a high-fat diet.
  • Activity: Decreased activity; promote rest.

Complications

  • Chronic, active hepatitis.
  • Fulminant hepatitis: severe, acute case of hepatitis.
  • Permanent destruction of liver cells, leading to cirrhosis.
  • Total destruction of the liver.

üë©‚Äç‚öïÔ∏è Nursing Interventions

Goal: To control and prevent hepatitis

Hepatitis A Prevention

  • Good personal hygiene, especially handwashing.
  • Participate in community activities for health education (environmental sanitation, food preparation).
  • Administer immune serum globulin (immunoglobulin G) within 2 weeks of exposure if anti-HAV antibodies are not present.
  • Preexposure prophylaxis: hepatitis A vaccine (single dose).
  • Implement standard precautions.
  • Abstain from sexual activity during periods of communicability.
üë¥ OLDER ADULT PRIORITY

Older adult clients are at higher risk for liver damage and complications of hepatitis.

Hepatitis B Prevention

  • Identify individuals at risk (oral/percutaneous contact with HBsAg-positive fluid, sexual contact with carriers).
  • Administration of hepatitis B vaccine.
  • Postexposure prophylaxis: HBV vaccine series started and hepatitis B immune globulin (HBIG) given within 24 hours of exposure.

Hepatitis C Prevention

  • No vaccine for HCV.
  • Immunoglobulin G, antivirals, or $\alpha$-interferon are not recommended for prevention.

Goal: To promote healing and regeneration of liver tissue

  • Rest: Bed rest with bathroom privileges initially; progressive activity according to liver function test results. Promote psychologic and emotional rest.
  • Nutrition: Small frequent feedings of favorite foods to combat anorexia. Good oral hygiene.
  • Hydration: Encourage increased fluid intake.
05

üè† Home Care

  • Continued need for adequate rest and nutrition until liver function test results are normal.
  • Avoid alcohol and over-the-counter medications, especially those containing acetaminophen and phenothiazine.
  • Clients with hepatitis B should avoid intimate and sexual contact until antibodies to the HBsAg are present and the client is no longer contagious.
  • If possible, client should have his or her own bathroom.
  • Client and family must understand importance of personal hygiene and good handwashing.
  • Client should not donate blood.
üíä ALERT

Evaluate client's use of over-the-counter medications.
