Foreword

Worldwide 2.6 million babies die every year from causes that are preventable and manageable. Approximately 80 percent of newborn deaths are as a result of prematurity/low birth weight, complications related to labour and delivery, neonatal sepsis and congenital anomalies amongst others.

With Vision 2030 target of 12/1000 livebirths, Kenya's neonatal mortality is still high at 22 / 1000 live births and of great public health concern. The National Neonatal Guidelines for Hospitals has been structured in a format that outlines the management of major causes of neonatal mortality. It is to be used by hospital based healthcare practitioners, to improve their knowledge and skills and provide high-quality evidenced-based care for the achievement of the overall objective-reduction of new-born mortality and that every newborn has a chance to survive, thrive and transform.

These guidelines will continue to be updated periodically and /or when necessary depending on feedback from the all the users, hospitals, academia, teaching institutions, regulatory bodies and emerging research/evidence-based data.

1.0 INTRODUCTION

1.1 HOW TO USE THESE GUIDELINES

In the application of these guidelines irrespective of your qualifications you will be asking yourself some questions down a decision action pathway.

KEY QUESTION ACTION PATHWAY
What is the problem? Problem solving begins with understanding the problem. If you are sure of the diagnosis, you can proceed to the next question. If in doubt refer to the guidelines or consult and reappraise your diagnosis. If this does not help you, REFER to the next level. Remember that if you do not know the diagnosis (you do not know the problem you are dealing with) it may cost the baby's life.
Given my knowledge, skills and the facilities available in my service delivery point, am I able to manage according to specific problem guidelines? This question only applies if you are sure of your diagnosis. In which case refer to the guidelines and manage the patient as per recommendations if you have the facilities and skills to do so. If you do not have the facilities or doubt your competencies, refer the baby to the next level. Delay in referral in the absence of competencies or systems to support management of the baby may result in neonatal death.
Is my management leading to success? As you manage the baby continuously evaluate the result of your management. If it is effective, continue; otherwise refer to the guidelines for further information. In the absence of observable success with your management, consult or refer to the next level. Remember to judge your success against time taken to the next referral level in case a need arises. It is better to refer early than be faced with an unanticipated emergency which you have neither the competencies nor the facilities to manage. This delay may be fatal.
NB: Always remember that TIME is an important factor in the practice of neonatology. Referral must be timely. You must match the time against the DISTANCE to the next level and the URGENCY required given the problem encountered.

2.0 ELEMENTS OF QUALITY OF CARE

KEY QUESTION ACTION PATHWAY
Promotion and protection of healthPeople need to know about pregnancy and childbirth and to understand the danger signs.
Accessibility and availability of servicesParents should be able to benefit from quality of care, understand the full range of services available for their newborns & receive care at the lowest appropriate level of the system close to where they live.
Technical competence of health care providersTechnical competence depends on regular training and retraining and on clear guidelines for clinical treatment
Essential supplies and equipmentNecessary equipment, supplies, drugs at each level of care and their availability should be ensured.
Quality of parentprovider interactionProviders must treat parents with respect, be responsive to their needs and avoid judgmental attitudes.
Parental orientation and visitation to the NBUParents should receive orientation to the unit and have unlimited access to their baby.
Information and counselling for the parentsParents should have the opportunity to talk to health care providers and should be offered guidance on any health problems identified.
Involvement of parents in decision-makingProviders should see parents as partners in health care and should involve them in decision making as regards to care of their baby. Parents' participation in rounds improves parental satisfaction, patient care, and teamwork.
Comprehensiveness of care and linkages to other servicesThese should be made as the need arises. Parents also can provide needed continuity with large and constantly changing care teams.
Continuity of care and follow-upNeonatal care should be part of continuing care comprising of antenatal, delivery & neonatal care. Adequate follow up is essential.

Support to health care providers

  • For adequate & happy function, health care workers need psychosocial support within their health care facility.
  • Health Care providers at all levels need support of the State/County and the communities that they work in.

4.0 REFERRAL SYSTEM AND TRANSPORT

It always preferable to have "in utero" transfer; it is safer and easier. If not then plan for baby as follows:

Referral to a more specialised unit

  • A baby requiring specialised care should be referred as soon as possible
  • Before referral the receiving hospital should be informed
  • Any baby being transferred must be stabilised before leaving the facility. WILL THS BABY SURVIVE DURING TRANSFER? evaluate the following:
    • Respiration - oxygen saturation, will you need to ventilate the baby during the journey?
    • Circulation-must be good, fluid status and vascular access
  • Proper transport must be arranged
    • Temperature management during transfer: Ambulance preferably equipped with transport incubator. Can use kangaroo care if baby is breathing well
    • Emergency box with equipment & drugs
  • Take care of airway - Sufficient equipment to maintain respiration.
  • Circulation as well prevention of hypoglycaemia Need to give feed or IV glucose infusion during transfer to prevent hypoglycaemia
  • + Oxygen supply
  • The baby must be accompanied by a qualified health worker capable of dealing with any emergency that may arise during the journey
    • Trained & up to date with newborn resuscitation
    • Able to handle any emergency
  • Information to the receiving hospital should include adequate maternal & baby history, investigations, X-rays and any imaging done should be sent, and treatment given before referral
  • As much as possible the mother should be transferred with her baby

Referral back to original health facility: this is usually not an emergency but still should be planned

  • A baby who has stabilized or is recuperating should be transferred back to the original facility if necessary and the facility has adequate expertise to look after the baby
  • Before referral back the receiving hospital should be informed
  • A full medical report should be sent to the receiving hospital
  • Proper transport must be arranged
  • Arrangement for follow up will depend on the condition and whether the original health facility has the expertise.

Refusal for transfer

  • Parents have the right to accept or refuse health care for their babies
  • The baby also has a right to adequate health care
  • All efforts must be taken to inform and counsel the parents on the need for the specialised care.
  • A signed refusal for transfer should be made and retained in the baby's hospital record

5.0 INFECTION CONTROL

5.1 GENERAL PRINCIPLES OF INFECTION CONTROL

  • It is the responsibility of all health care personnel in the NBU to minimise infection
  • Routine care of babies from birth onwards
  • Consider everybody (mother, baby, personnel) potentially infected
  • Protective clothing and gloves
  • Aseptic technique
  • Instruments & equipment
  • Cleaning of the neonatal unit
  • Waste disposal
  • Isolation or cohorting
  • Overcrowding - spaces between cots & incubators
  • Adequacy of personnel
  • Adequate feeding

5.2 HOUSEKEEPING AND WASTE DISPOSAL

Regular and thorough cleaning will decrease micro-organisms on surfaces and help prevent infection.

Cleaning schedule for the newborn special care unit

Frequency Cleaning Guidelines
Daily
  • Wet-mop floors with a disinfectant and detergent solution. Do not sweep floors or use cleaning methods that increase dust.
  • Wipe incubators and radiant warmers with a disinfectant solution.
  • Humidifier chamber must be emptied and cleaned and filled with fresh distilled water.
Between Babies
  • Wipe equipment, bassinets (cots), examination tables, weighing scales etc., with a cloth dampened with disinfectant solution.
  • Clean incubators and radiant warmers between each use, including the mattress, with a disinfectant solution. Allow the incubator to dry completely before placing a baby inside.
  • If same baby: After seven days, neonate should be shifted to another incubator and used incubator should be cleaned with antiseptic solution.
  • NB: Refer also to the manufacturer's manual for guideline on care of the incubator
As needed
  • Clean windows, walls, lamps, chairs, and to prevent accumulation of dust.
  • Remove and destroy or clean contaminated waste containers.
  • Remove and destroy the sharps-disposal container and replace with another suitable container.
  • Clean spills of blood or body fluids with a disinfectant solution.
Guidelines for cleaning instruments and equipment
Instrument Cleaning Guidelines (after each use)
Thermometers and stethoscopes -Wipe with a disinfectant solution between each use, and particularly between use with different babies.
Resuscitation bag and mask -Wipe exposed surfaces with gauze pad soaked in disinfectant solution
-Wash the face mask with soap and water
Oxygen head box -Wash with soap and water

5.3 PEOPLE AS SOURCES OF INFECTION

  • Do not allow personnel with skin infections or lesions to come into direct contact with babies.
  • Individuals handling the baby should observe hand hygiene

Note: Personnel can be infected by babies - rubella (women of child bearing age), CMV, hepatitis B

Clothing

  • Gowns hats and masks are not necessary when providing routine care for newborn babies.
  • Personal clothing must not touch the baby. All sleeves must be above elbow
  • White coats used in other parts of the hospital should be removed when entering a neonatal unit
  • Can wear apron to prevent soiling

Gloves

  • Sterile as indicated by procedure
  • Non sterile
  • Heavy duty for cleaners and waste disposal
  • Quality of gloves important
  • Remember to change between patients
  • Always decontaminate hands before and after use
  • Some people have allergy to latex

ASEPTIC TECHNIQUE

a. IV fluids & investigations:

  • Minimise invasive procedures
  • Adequate skin preparation when invasive procedure is performed
  • Sterile packs for IV insertion
  • Pour antiseptic on the swabs rather than dip into the container
  • Clean the port with repeated use of the line
  • Change sites 48-72 hrs
  • Change infusion set after 24-48 hrs of use
  • Remove line immediately after use
  • Watch the site regularly during use and remove assoon as infection is detected
  • Recommended antiseptics: tincture of iodine/ povidone iodine, 70-90% alcohol

b. Respiratory therapy

  • Aseptic technique during intubation, suctioning
  • Disinfect all respiratory equipment
  • Monitor colonisation by regular sputum cultures

c. Multiuse vials

  • Use sterile needle & syringe all the time
  • Do not leave needle in the vial after use
  • Storage according to manufacturer's direction
  • Indicate date of opening
  • Glass ampules - discard any left over

6.0 HAND HYGIENE

Most effective and least expensive method of preventing infection but there is poor adherence/compliance. Adherence to hand washing practice is a requirement for all health care personnel. But the hospital must ensure adequate supply of what is needed to keep hands clean. Active surveillance is important. Help each other to practice good hand hygiene.

What do you use to wash/clean hands?

Order from least to most effective:

  • Plain soap and water. But must be done if hands are visible soiled
  • Antiseptic soap and water
  • Alcohol based hand rubs. Effectiveness in turn depends on:
    • Type of alcohol (ethyl or isopropyl alcohol)
    • Concentration
    • Contact time
    • Volume used
  • Chlorhexidine alone or in combination with alcohol.

To wash hands (see hand hygiene)

  • Thoroughly wet hands
  • Wash hands (including wrists) for 10 to 15 seconds with plain or antiseptic soap and running water
  • Allow hands to air-dry or dry them with a clean paper towel.
  • Close tap with elbow or disposable paper towel after drying

To use alcohol-based hand rub:

  • Apply enough hand rub to cover the entire surface of hands and fingers;
  • Rub the solution into hands until they are dry. Dispenser system best if only one push is needed and the outlet must not be clogged.

Finger nails & nail polish: Avoid long nails or nail extensions Nail polish must be freshly applied (not chapped as this harbours microbes)

Jewellery: Rings high contamination some unit advise no rings at work

6.1 SKIN CARE

Vernix caseosa is antimicrobial - Do not wash it off. Top tailing probably enough

Avoid damaging the skin eg use of excessive strapping. Application of oil may help reduce infection Allow baby to be colonised by maternal organisms by practicing Kangaroo care

6.2 CORD CARE

Chorhexidine is the recommended antiseptic until the cord drops off

6.3 ISOLATION

Isolate babies with respiratory, skin and diarrhoea diseases and necrotising enterocolitis

Cohorting - if there is an outbreak leave all exposed whether sick or not in one room and do not admitting new ones. When they are all discharged clean and fumigate before reusing the room. Preferably have a separate group of personnel who do not care for other infants to look after this group.

6.4 GOOD NUTRITION

Ensure adequate nutrition in all babies irrespective of condition. Feeding reduces risk of infection and improves recovery from infection. Do not forget the value of breastmilk. Keep in mind that formula is not sterile All feeding utensils must be adequately sterilised

6.5 INFECTION SURVEILLANCES & AUDITS

Surveillance help to track changes in causative organisms and antibiotic sensitivity, detect epidemics These should be ongoing all the time. Regular discussions keep people up to date for all staff Take immediate action when you notice an outbreak

7.0 CLINICAL AUDIT

  1. Clinical audit is aimed at self-improvement and is not about finding who to blame.
  2. The aims are for hospitals to diagnose key problems in providing care it- is essential that identifying problems is linked to suggesting who needs to act, how and by when to implement solutions. Then follow up on whether progress is being achieved with new audits. Identify new problems and plan new actions etc.
  3. Hospitals should have an audit team comprising 4 to 8 members, led by a senior clinician and including nurses, admin, lab technicians and nutritionists etc. 1-2 people, usually MO or CO interns and nurses should be selected on a rotating basis to perform the audit and report back to the audit team and department staff.

Deaths and surviving cases can be audited Records of all deaths should be audited within 24 hours of death.

  1. Use an audit tool to compare care given with recommendations in these guidelines (e.g. for TB, HIV/AIDS) and the most up to date reference materials for less common conditions.
  2. Was care reasonable? Look for where improvements could be made in the system of care before the child comes to hospital (referral), on arrival in hospital (care in the OPD / MCH etc.), on admission to a ward, or follow up on the ward.
  3. Look at assessments, diagnoses, investigations, treatments and whether what was planned was done and recorded. Check doses and whether drugs / fluids / feeds are correct and actually given and if clinical review and nursing observations were adequate - if it is not written down it was not done!
  4. Look at several cases for each meeting and summarize the findings looking for the major things that are common and need improving. Then record the summaries and action points for reporting.
