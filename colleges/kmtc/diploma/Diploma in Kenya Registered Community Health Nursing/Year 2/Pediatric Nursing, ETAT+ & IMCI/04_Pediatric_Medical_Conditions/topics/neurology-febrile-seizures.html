Febrile Seizures

Definition and Criteria

A febrile seizure is a convulsion in a child triggered by a fever. It is the most common seizure disorder in childhood.

It is defined as a seizure occurring in a febrile child aged 6 months to 60 months (5 years) without intracranial infection, metabolic disturbance, or a history of afebrile seizures. The peak incidence is between 12 to 18 months.

Criteria for Febrile Convulsions

  • Associated with an elevated temperature above 38°C, with the seizure occurring within 24 hours.
  • Age between 6 months and 5 years.
  • Absence of CNS infection.
  • Absence of acute metabolic abnormality.
  • No history of previous afebrile seizures.

Classification

Febrile seizures are classified as either simple or complex.

Simple Febrile Convulsions

  • Generalized tonic-clonic in nature.
  • Lasts a few seconds and rarely up to 15 minutes.
  • Usually occurs only once in a 24-hour period.
  • No infection of the CNS.
  • Followed by a brief postictal period of drowsiness.
  • Have an excellent prognosis and are not associated with a reduction in later intellectual performance.

Complex Febrile Convulsions

  • Lasts longer than 15 minutes.
  • Repeated convulsions occur within 24 hours.
  • Could be focal in nature.
  • May have focal findings in the postictal period (e.g., Todd's paralysis).

Warning

A seizure lasting ≥ 30 minutes or repeated seizures for ≥ 30 minutes without regaining consciousness is often due to CNS infection and should be investigated as such.

Risk Factors and Causes

Causes of Fever

Most febrile seizures occur in the first 24 hours of an illness. Common causes of fever that may trigger a seizure include:

  • Upper Respiratory Tract Infection (URTI)
  • Human herpes virus 6 (HHV-6)
  • Influenza
  • Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)
  • Gastroenteritis

Recurrence Risk Factors

  • Age < 1 year at first seizure.
  • Duration of fever < 24 hours before the seizure.
  • Seizure occurs with a lower temperature.
  • Other minor risk factors: family history of febrile seizure or epilepsy, male gender, low serum sodium.

Risk Factors for Developing Epilepsy

The risk of developing epilepsy later in life is slightly higher than in the general population. Factors that increase this risk include:

  1. Complex features during the seizure/postictal period.
  2. Family history of epilepsy.
  3. Initial febrile seizure at < 12 months of age.
  4. Delayed developmental milestones.

Clinical Features and Approach

Symptoms

A febrile seizure may be as mild as the child's eyes rolling or limbs stiffening. A typical seizure may present as follows:

  • Sudden tightening (contraction) of muscles on both sides of the child's body.
  • The child may cry or moan.
  • The muscle tightening may last for several seconds or longer.
  • The child will fall if standing and may pass urine.
  • The child may vomit or bite the tongue.
  • Sometimes children do not breathe and may begin to turn blue.
  • The child's body may then begin to jerk rhythmically.
  • The child will not respond to the parent's voice.

Initial Approach and Management

  • Ensure that Airway is clear, the child is Breathing, and the Circulation is adequate (ABCs).
  • If actively convulsing: Stop the convulsion with diazepam 0.2 mg/kg as a slow IV injection or 0.5 mg/kg rectally.
  • Active measures to control the fever (e.g., antipyretics, tepid sponging).
  • Search for and treat the underlying cause of the fever.
  • Rule out potentially life-threatening conditions (meningitis, encephalitis, sepsis, head trauma, ingestion of poisons).

First Aid During a Seizure

  • Move the child only if they are in a dangerous location.
  • Remove objects that may cause injury.
  • Loosen any tight clothing, especially around the neck.
  • If the child vomits, or if saliva and mucus build up in the mouth, turn them on their side or stomach.
  • Do NOT try to force anything into the mouth.
  • Do NOT try to restrain the child or stop the seizure movements.

Investigations and Differential Diagnosis

Investigations

  • If any doubt exists about the possibility of meningitis, a lumbar puncture is indicated. It should be strongly considered in:
    • Children < 12 months of age.
    • Children > 12 months with complex seizures.
    • If sensorium remains clouded after a short postictal period.
  • Blood slide for malaria.
  • Random blood sugar.
  • Further testing may be needed if the child is younger than 9 months or older than 5 years, has a neurological disorder, or if the seizure was complex.

Differential Diagnosis

  • Meningitis
  • Encephalitis
  • Drug ingestion
  • Metabolic imbalance
  • Cerebrovascular Accident (CVA)
  • Seizure disorder with seizures triggered by fever (convulsions with fever).

Prevention and Prognosis

Prognosis (Outlook)

Simple febrile seizures are harmless. There is no evidence that they cause death, brain damage, epilepsy, a decrease in IQ, or learning problems. Most children outgrow febrile seizures by age 5.

Prevention of Recurrence

  • Antipyretics have not been shown to prevent seizure recurrences but reduce discomfort and are reassuring to parents.
  • Prolonged anticonvulsant prophylaxis is controversial and no longer recommended for simple febrile seizures. Phenytoin and carbamazepine do not prevent febrile seizures.
  • Phenobarbitone prevents recurrent febrile convulsions but may reduce cognitive function.
  • Sodium valproate is effective but carries a potential risk of fatal hepatotoxicity, which does not justify its use in a disorder with an excellent prognosis.
  • If parental anxiety is very high, intermittent prophylaxis with oral diazepam may be used at the onset of a febrile illness (0.3 mg/kg every 8 hours for the duration of the illness).

Counselling

  • Loosen clothing during a seizure.
  • Cool the child with a towel using lukewarm water.
  • Reassure the mother about the excellent prognosis.
