Seizures & Epilepsy in Children

ASHIONO E. M

Introduction

Epilepsy is a "common" neurological problem. 50 million people have epilepsy globally; 20% of them are children. 80% of 3.5 million new cases of epilepsy are from developing countries.

A seizure is a temporary involuntary disturbance of brain function that may be manifested as impaired consciousness, abnormal motor activity, sensory disturbances or autonomic dysfunction.

Definitions

Seizure

A transient occurrence of signs and/or symptoms resulting from abnormal excessive or synchronous neuronal activity in brain.

Epilepsy

Two or more unprovoked seizures more than 24 hrs apart - epileptic tendency. Or one seizure with abnormal Neurological Examination Or EEG Or MRI.

Situation related

Seizures provoked by illness / metabolic disturbance / toxic event, does not denote epileptic tendency.

Prodrome

Mood or Behavior change that precedes a seizure.

Convulsion

An epileptic seizure characterised by motor activity.

Epilepsy Syndrome

A clinical entity of relatively consistent clinical features, including seizure type(s), aetiology, EEG features, neurologic status, prognosis, and/or response to specific antiepileptic drugs.

Etiology of Seizures

Causes for Epileptic seizures

  • Idiopathic (70-80%) - cause unknown but presumed genetic
  • Secondary
    • Cerebral malformations
    • Cerebral vascular occlusion
    • Cerebral damage (ex; congenital infections, hypoxic-ischaemic encephalopathy)
    • Cerebral tumour
    • Neurodegenerative disorders
    • Neurocutaneous syndromes (Neurofibromatosis, Tuberous sclerosis)

Causes for Non-epileptic (Provoked) seizures

  • Febrile seizures
  • Metabolic (Hypoglycaemia, Hypocalcaemia, Hypomagnesaemia, Hypo/hyper natraemia)
  • Head trauma
  • Meningitis/Encephalitis
  • Poisons/Toxins (organophosphates, lead, carbamates)
  • Systemic disorders (Vasculitis, Encephalopathy of hypertension, Renal/Hepatic failure)
  • Drugs (Lead, Addictive drug withdrawal, Alcohol withdrawal)

Types of Seizures: Clinical Features

Focal (Partial) Seizures

Originate from a localized area of one hemisphere. Common causes include idiopathic, cryptogenic, and structural abnormalities. In young infants & children, focal seizures can be very subtle.

  • Simple Partial Seizure (SPS): Consciousness is preserved. Symptoms can be motor, sensory, autonomic, or psychiatric depending on the cortical area involved. Usually lasts 5-10 seconds. No postictal state. EEG may be normal or show focal spikes.
  • Complex Partial Seizure (CPS): Consciousness is impaired or lost. Characterized by aura, altered sensorium, motor activity, and automatisms. Typically lasts less than 3 minutes, followed by a postictal period of somnolence, confusion, and headache. No memory of the event.
  • Focal Seizure evolving to Secondary Generalized: The seizure starts focally and then spreads to involve both hemispheres.

Generalized Seizures

Involve synchronous involvement of both cerebral hemispheres from the onset.

  • Generalized Tonic-Clonic (GTCS): Usually lasts 1-2 minutes. Begins with a tonic phase (sudden loss of consciousness, body stiffness) followed by a clonic phase (rhythmic contractions and relaxation). Cyanosis, salivation, and incontinence can occur. Always followed by a postictal phase.
  • Absence Seizure: Common in childhood (5-8 years). Brief episodes of sudden interruption of activity, blank stare, and altered tone, lasting a few seconds. No aura or postictal period. Can be precipitated by hyperventilation.
  • Myoclonic Seizure: Brief, shock-like contractions of muscles, which may be restricted to a part of one extremity or may be generalized.
  • Atonic Seizure: Sudden loss of motor tone, causing the child to fall to the floor ("drop attacks").
  • Infantile Spasms (West Syndrome): Occurs in the first year of life. Characterized by sudden tonic movements (spasms) of the head, trunk, and extremities, often in clusters.

Evaluation of Patient

History

History is the most important part of the clinical evaluation.

  • Seizure details: Age of onset, aura, sequence of events, loss of consciousness, motor activity, duration, post-ictal state, recall of the event.
  • Medical History: Fever, diarrhea, trauma, perinatal insults, previous seizures, developmental history.
  • Family History: Epilepsy, febrile seizures.

Examination

  • Full clinical and neurological examination.
  • Measurements: Head circumference.
  • Physical signs: Dysmorphism, cutaneous markers (hypo/hyper-pigmented lesions, facial nevus), focal deficits, fundus examination.

Investigations

  • Laboratory: UECs, Ca, Mg, CBC with differential, toxicology screens, RBS. Anticonvulsant drug levels if applicable.
  • Lumbar Puncture: Consider if CNS infection is suspected.
  • EEG: Recommended for all patients with a paroxysmal event. Helps classify seizure type and syndrome. Video EEG is useful for differentiating seizures from non-epileptic events.
  • Neuroimaging (MRI/CT): Indicated for seizures in early infancy, focal seizures (except benign types), and developmental delay. Not typically needed for febrile seizures or absence seizures. MRI is preferred over CT.

Status Epilepticus

Definition: A continuous convulsion lasting longer than 20-30 minutes, or a series of convulsions without a return to consciousness. This is a medical emergency requiring an organized approach to minimize mortality and morbidity.

Etiology: Common causes include prolonged febrile seizures, underlying neurologic disorders (symptomatic SE), or sudden stoppage of antiepileptics (idiopathic SE).

Acute Seizure Management

  1. Initial (0-5 min): Assess and support Airway, Breathing, and Circulation (ABCs). Place in lateral recumbent position. Give oxygen. Check blood glucose.
  2. First-line Therapy (5-20 min): Administer a benzodiazepine.
    • Lorazepam: 0.05-0.1 mg/kg IV (drug of choice due to longer action).
    • Diazepam: 0.1-0.3 mg/kg IV or 0.2-0.5 mg/kg PR (rectal gel).
    • Midazolam: 0.15-0.3 mg/kg IV/IM or 0.5 mg/kg buccal/nasal.
  3. Second-line Therapy (20-40 min): If seizures persist, administer a longer-acting anticonvulsant.
    • Fosphenytoin/Phenytoin: 15-20 mg/kg IV. Fosphenytoin is preferred due to fewer side effects. Monitor for hypotension and arrhythmias.
    • Phenobarbital: 15-20 mg/kg IV. Be prepared for respiratory depression.
  4. Third-line Therapy (>40-60 min): For refractory status epilepticus, manage in an ICU. Options include continuous IV infusion of midazolam or propofol, or inducing a barbiturate coma (thiopental). Continuous EEG monitoring is essential.

Long-Term Management of Epilepsy

When to Start Medication

Treatment is typically initiated after a second unprovoked seizure. Treatment after a first seizure may be considered if there is a structural lesion, a definite epileptic pattern on EEG, or a history of brain injury.

Principles of Therapy

  • Monotherapy: Start with a single drug at a low dose and titrate slowly. Monotherapy controls seizures in 70-80% of cases.
  • Drug Choice: The choice of Antiepileptic Drug (AED) depends on the seizure type, syndrome, safety, age, and gender.
  • Duration: Generally given for a minimum 2-year seizure-free period. Duration is longer for symptomatic epilepsy.
  • Tapering: Taper AEDs slowly over 3-6 months. Relapse risk is highest in the first 1-2 years after discontinuation.

Treatment for Medically Intractable Epilepsy

If a patient fails adequate trials of two AEDs, their epilepsy is considered intractable. Other options should be explored:

  • Epilepsy Surgery: Removal of the seizure focus. Requires extensive evaluation but results can be superior to medical treatment.
  • Vagus Nerve Stimulator (VNS): An implanted device that sends electrical impulses to the vagus nerve.
  • Ketogenic Diet: A high-fat, low-carbohydrate diet used primarily in children whose seizures have not responded to several different medications.
