MENINGITIS IN CHILDREN

ASHIONO E.M

Definitions

  • Meningitis - inflammation of the meninges
  • Encephalitis - infection of the brain parenchyma
  • Meningoencephalitis - inflammation of brain + meninges
  • Aseptic meningitis - inflammation of meninges with sterile CSF

Epidemiology in Kenya

A study in a rural Kenyan hospital showed:

  • 390 cases (1.3% of all admissions), 88% were <5 years old.
  • The apparent minimum annual incidence in children under 5 increased from 120 to 202 per 100,000 between 1995 and 2000.
  • The most common organisms in infants <3 months were streptococci and Enterobacteriaceae.
  • Streptococcus pneumoniae (43.1%) and Haemophilus influenzae (41.9%) were predominant in the postneonatal period.
  • Overall mortality was 30.1%, and 23.5% of survivors developed neurologic sequelae.
  • Chloramphenicol resistance of H. influenzae rose from 8% in 1994 to 80% in 2000, accompanied by an apparent increase in mortality.

Kenyatta National Hospital (KNH) Neonates Study

  • The prevalence of meningitis amongst cases of suspected sepsis was 17.9%.
  • Feed intolerance and lethargy were the most common clinical features (73.3% and 60% respectively).
  • The most common aetiological agents were Escherichia coli (46.7%), Group B Streptococci (26.7%), and Klebsiella pneumoniae (13.3%).
  • Most isolates were resistant to ampicillin and gentamicin but showed good in-vitro sensitivities to amikacin, cefuroxime, and third-generation cephalosporins.
  • Resistance to first-line antibiotics (penicillin and gentamicin) is high, suggesting a need to change empirical antibiotic use.

Transmission and Pathogens

Transmission

  • Droplet transmission from person to person through close and prolonged contact (e.g., sneezing, coughing).
  • Incubation period is 2-10 days.

Routes of Infection

  • Nasopharynx
  • Bloodstream
  • Direct spread (skull fracture, meningo/encephalocele)
  • Middle ear infection
  • Infected VP shunts
  • Congenital defects
  • Sinusitis

Bacterial Meningitis Organisms

  • Neonates (0-7 days): Group B Streptococci (GBS), E. coli, other enteric bacilli, L. monocytogenes.
  • Neonates (>7 days): Same as above, plus consider antimicrobial-resistant gram-negative organisms and gram-positive organisms in hospitalized infants.
  • Older infants and children: Neisseria meningitidis, S. pneumoniae, Tuberculosis, H. influenzae.

Pathogenesis of Bacterial Meningitis

  • Entry of organism through the Blood-Brain Barrier (BBB).
  • Release of bacterial cell wall and membrane products.
  • Outpouring of polymorphs and fibrin.
  • Release of cytokines and chemokines.
  • Activation of inflammatory mediators.
  • Inflamed meninges become covered with exudate.

Microvascular Injury in Meningococcal Sepsis

The complex physiology of meningococcal sepsis is largely explained by four basic processes affecting the microvasculature:

  1. Increased vascular permeability
  2. Pathological vasoconstriction and vasodilatation
  3. Loss of thromboresistance and intravascular coagulation
  4. Profound myocardial dysfunction.

These events are largely responsible for the development of shock and multiorgan failure.

Neurologic Injury

  • Direct bacterial toxicity
  • Indirect inflammatory processes such as cytokine release, neutrophil activation, with resultant vasculitis, and cellular oedema
  • Cerebral oedema may be caused by increased secretion of CSF, diminished reabsorption of CSF, and breakdown of the blood-brain barrier

Symptoms and Signs of Bacterial Meningitis

  • Rapid Onset (25%) hospitalized < 24h
  • Respiratory Symptoms days - weeks
  • Headache 90%
  • Fever >90%
  • Altered consciousness 80%
  • Vomiting - 30%
  • Seizures - 30%
  • Petechiae/purpura 50%
  • Meningismus 85%
  • Focal neurosigns 25%
  • Ocular palsies - 10%
  • Hemiparesis
  • Myalgias 20%
  • Papilledema 1%

Clinical signs of meningeal irritation

Diagnosis and Management

Lumbar Puncture (LP)

LP is crucial for diagnosis. Contraindications include:

  • Respiratory distress (positioning)
  • ↑ ICP reported to increase risk of herniation
  • Cellulitis at area of tap
  • Bleeding disorder

Who Needs an LP?

At a minimum, LP should be performed on those with a history of fever and one of:

  • Bulging fontanelle
  • Stiff neck
  • Fits if age <6 months or >6 years
  • Partial or focal fits
  • Reduced consciousness

Value of LP Findings

75% of acute bacterial meningitis cases can be detected by examining for CSF cloudiness or turbidity at the bedside. Cloudiness usually appears at CSF WBC counts > 200 x 10⁶ Wbc per L. CSF should be crystal clear.

Microscopy - Gram Stain

  • Absence of organisms on Gram stain does not exclude the diagnosis.
  • Gram-positive diplococci suggest S. pneumoniae.
  • Gram-negative diplococci suggest N. meningitidis.
  • Small pleomorphic Gram-negative coccobacilli suggest Hib.
  • Gram-positive cocci or coccobacilli suggest group B streptococcus.
  • Gram-positive rods and coccobacilli suggest L. monocytogenes.

Note on Prior Antibiotics

Prior antibiotic use reduces the sensitivity of CSF gram stain by 20% and CSF culture by 30%. However, it takes 2-3 days of therapy to change the WBC cell count of CSF in true bacterial meningitis. Prior treatment is no excuse for not doing an LP!

Treatment

Empiric regimen for infants and children older than 1 month with bacterial meningitis include coverage for antibiotic-resistant S. pneumoniae, N. meningitidis, and Hib.

  • Cefotaxime (300 mg/kg/d [IV], maximum dose 12 g/day, in 3 or 4 divided doses) or
  • Ceftriaxone (100 mg/kg/d IV, maximum dose 4 g/day in 1 or 2 divided doses) plus
  • Vancomycin (60 mg/kg per day IV, maximum dose 2 g/day, in 4 divided doses)

Duration of Therapy

  • S. pneumoniae - 10 to 14 days
  • N. meningitidis - 5 to 7 days
  • Hib - 7 to 10 days
  • L. monocytogenes - 14 to 21 days
  • Gram-negative bacilli - 3 weeks or a minimum of 2 weeks beyond the first sterile culture, whichever is longer

Dexamethasone Use

  • Consider if H. flu & S. pneumo meningitis & > 6 wks old (0.6 mg/kg/day ÷ Q 6 h x 2 d).
  • ↓ local synthesis of TNF-α, IL-1, PAF & prostaglandins resulting in ↓ BBB permeability, ↓ meningeal irritation.
  • Debate if it ↓ incidence of hearing loss.
  • If used, needs to be given shortly before or at the time of antibiotic administration (within 1 hr).
  • May adversely affect the penetration of antibiotics into CSF.

Meningococcemia Prophylaxis

  • Treat within 24 hours of exposure.
  • Vaccinate affected population, if outbreak.
  • Rifampin: <1 mo 5 mg/kg PO Q 12 x 2 days; >1 mo 10 mg/kg (max 600 mg) PO Q 12 x 2 days.
  • Ceftriaxone: ≤12 y 125 mg IM x 1 dose; >12 y 250 mg IM x 1 dose.
  • Ciprofloxacin: ≥18 y 500 mg PO x 1 dose.

Tuberculous and Viral Meningitis

Tuberculous Meningitis (TBM)

A complication of childhood TB, common cause of prolonged morbidity, handicap, and death, especially in children below 5 years.

  • First Phase (Prodromal): Vague symptoms, child is dull, irritable, restless, or drowsy. Anorexia, vomiting, low-grade fever, personality change.
  • Second Phase (Meningitic): Confusion, drowsy, neck stiffness, protracted headache, vomiting, lethargy, convulsions, cranial nerve palsies.
  • Terminal Phase (Paralytic): Stupor and coma, opisthotonus, multiple focal paresis, high-grade fever.

Diagnosis: CSF shows profoundly low glucose and high protein. Steroids + anti-TB drugs are the treatment.

Viral Meningitis

Comprises most aseptic meningitic syndromes. Agents include Enterovirus, Herpesvirus, Paramyxovirus, etc.

Treatment: Supportive care, analgesia, fever control. No antibiotics needed. No isolation required (Standard precautions).

Outcomes: Adverse outcomes are rare. Infants <1 year have a higher incidence of speech & language delay. For Herpes simplex 1 encephalitis, treat with IV acyclovir.

Complications

Acute complications of meningitis include:

  • Hydrocephalus
  • Subdural effusion or empyema (~30%)
  • Stroke
  • Abscess
  • Dural sinus thrombophlebitis
