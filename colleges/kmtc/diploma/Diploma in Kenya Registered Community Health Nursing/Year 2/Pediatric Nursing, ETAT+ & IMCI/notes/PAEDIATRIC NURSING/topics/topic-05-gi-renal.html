üíß Diarrhea

Diarrhea is the frequent discharge of semi-solid or liquid faecal matter from the bowels, resulting from disorders involving digestive, absorptive, and secretory functions. It leads to abnormal intestinal water and electrolyte transport.

Types

  • Acute Diarrhea: Sudden increase in frequency and change in consistency. Usually self-limiting (< 14 days). Leading cause of illness in children under 5.
  • Chronic Diarrhea: Increase in stool frequency and water content lasting more than 14 days. Causes include malabsorption syndromes, inflammatory bowel disease, and immunodeficiency.

Etiology

  • ‚û§Viruses: Rotavirus (causes ~70%), Adenovirus.
  • ‚û§Bacteria: Salmonella, Shigella, Campylobacter, E. coli.
  • ‚û§Parasites: Giardia lamblia, Entamoeba histolytica.
  • ‚û§Dietary: Overfeeding, osmotic diarrhea from excess sugar/juice.
  • ‚û§Medications: Antibiotics, laxatives.

Management (Kenya Paediatric Guidelines 2016)

History of diarrhea/vomiting, age > 1 month.

üöΩ Urinary Tract Infections (UTI)

Presence of significant numbers of micro-organisms anywhere within the urinary tract. Common causative organism is Escherichia coli (80%).

Classification

  • Cystitis: Inflammation of the bladder.
  • Urethritis: Inflammation of the urethra.
  • Pyelonephritis: Inflammation of the upper urinary tract and kidneys.
  • Urosepsis: Febrile UTI with systemic signs of bacterial illness.

Clinical Manifestations

Infants (< 2 years): Non-specific signs resembling GI disorders (failure to thrive, vomiting, diarrhea, jaundice). Newborns may have fever/hypothermia, sepsis, strong smelling urine, persistent diaper rash.

Children (> 2 years): Classic symptoms include enuresis, daytime incontinence, fever, foul smelling urine, frequency, dysuria, urgency, abdominal/flank pain.

Management

Objectives: Eliminate infection, prevent urosepsis, preserve renal function.

  • Antibiotics (Penicillins, Sulphonamides, Cephalosporins, Nitrofurantoin).
  • Pyelonephritis requires admission and IV antibiotics for 48 hours.
  • Follow-up cultures at monthly intervals for 3 months.
  • Encourage adequate fluid intake.

üõë Acute Glomerulonephritis

Sudden inflammation of glomeruli resulting in acute renal failure. Peak onset 6-7 years; common in boys. Usually post-streptococcal (Group A) infection (2-3 weeks latency).

Pathophysiology

Immune complex reaction: Antibodies formed against streptococcal antigen are trapped in glomerular capillary loops causing inflammation. Results in decreased filtration, protein leak, and retention of sodium/water.

Clinical Manifestations

Hematuria Tea-colored or cola-colored urine ("smoky").
Edema Periorbital puffiness (morning) and dependent edema.
Hypertension Increased BP, risk of hypertensive encephalopathy.
Oliguria Diminished urinary output.

Management

  • Fluid Balance: Daily weights, strict I/O. Calculate insensible loss + urine output.
  • Diet: Moderate sodium restriction. Protein restriction only if azotemia/oliguria present.
  • Hypertension: Monitor BP q4-6h. Anti-hypertensives (Ca channel blockers, ACE inhibitors) if needed.
  • Medication: Diuretics (if renal failure not severe). Antibiotics only if persistent infection.

Defined by massive proteinuria, hypoalbuminemia, hyperlipidemia, and edema. Common in males 2-6 years.

Pathophysiology

Increased glomerular permeability to protein → Proteinuria → Reduced serum albumin (Hypoalbuminemia) → Fluid shift to interstitial space → Edema/Ascites → Hypovolemia → RAAS stimulation → Sodium/Water retention.

Management

Corticosteroid Therapy

Prednisolone: 60 mg/m¬≤/day OD for 6 weeks, then taper to 40 mg/m¬≤ alternate days for 6 weeks.

  • Diet: Salt restriction during edema phase. High protein diet (unless azotemic).
  • Care: Elevate swollen scrotum. Skin care to prevent breakdown. Infection prevention.
  • Immunosuppressants: Cyclophosphamide for frequent relapses.

üß† Meningococcal Meningitis

Acute inflammation of the meninges and CSF. Causative organism: Neisseria meningitidis. Transmitted by droplet infection.

Clinical Manifestations

  • Abrupt onset fever, chills, headache, vomiting.
  • Nuchal Rigidity: Stiff neck.
  • Positive Signs: Kernig‚Äôs sign and Brudzinski‚Äôs sign.
  • Petechial or purpuric rash.
  • Infants: Bulging fontanelle, high-pitched cry, irritability.
üöë

Medical Emergency

Requires early recognition and immediate therapy to prevent death and residual disabilities.

  • Isolation precautions.
  • Antibiotics: Preferably cephalosporins.
  • Reduction of intracranial pressure (elevate head).
  • Control of seizures and temperature.
