ü©∫ Wilm‚Äôs Tumour (Nephroblastoma)

Wilm‚Äôs tumour is a malignant tumour of the kidney and is the most common kidney tumour of childhood, commonly affecting children below 5 years of age. About 2% of cases are family-related.

Clinical Manifestations

  • Abdominal Mass: Painless, firm, non-tender swelling confined to one side, deep within the flank.
  • Hematuria: Blood in urine.
  • Systemic Signs: Weight loss, fever, anemia (due to hemorrhage within tumor).
  • Metastasis: Cough, dyspnea, shortness of breath (spread to lungs).
  • Hypertension: Due to excess production of renin.
üö´

Critical Nursing Alert

DO NOT PALPATE THE TUMOR unless absolutely necessary. Manipulation may cause dissemination of cancer cells to adjacent and distant sites.

Staging

Stage Description
Stage 1 Tumor is limited to kidney and completely resected.
Stage 2 Tumor extends beyond kidney but is completely resected.
Stage 3 Residual non-hematogenous tumor confined to abdomen.
Stage 4 Hematogenous metastasis to lung, liver, bone, or brain.
Stage 5 Bilateral tumor involvement at diagnosis.

Management

Treatment is based on clinical stage and histological pattern.

  • Surgery: Nephrectomy (usually within 24-48 hours of diagnosis).
  • Chemotherapy: Agents include Actinomycin D, Vincristine, Doxorubicin, and Cyclophosphamide.
  • Radiation: May be used depending on staging.

ü¶† Burkitt's Lymphoma

A form of non-Hodgkin's lymphoma starting in immune B-cells. It is recognized as the fastest-growing human tumor but is potentially curable with intensive chemotherapy.

Etiology

Associated with impaired immunity. 98% of African cases are associated with Epstein-Barr Virus (EBV) and malaria. Outside Africa, it is often associated with HIV/AIDS.

Types & Symptoms

‚ñ∂ Clinical Variants
  • Endemic (African): Primarily affects children 4-7 years (boys > girls). Starts as tumors of the jaw or facial bones. Can spread to CNS.
  • Sporadic / Immunodeficiency: Usually starts in the bowel forming a bulky abdominal mass. Massive involvement of liver, spleen, bone marrow.

Management

Burkitt's lymphoma spreads quickly; prompt diagnosis (Biopsy, CT, Bone Marrow) is essential.

Treatment & Prognosis

Intensive intravenous chemotherapy (Cyclophosphamide, Vincristine, Methotrexate) is the primary treatment. Intrathecal chemotherapy prevents CNS spread. Survival rates are 60%-90% with prompt treatment.

üîí Tetanus (Lockjaw)

Acute, preventable disease caused by an exotoxin (tetanospasmin) produced by Clostridium tetani. It is characterized by painful muscular rigidity.

üîç Click to zoom

Fig. 7.1: Neonatal tetanus. Note the rigid posture and clenched hands.

Pathophysiology

Spores enter through devitalized tissue (puncture wounds, umbilical stump). Bacteria multiply and release tetanospasmin, which travels to the CNS. It blocks inhibitory neurotransmitters, leading to unchecked reflex excitability and stiffness.

Clinical Manifestations

  • Trismus: Difficulty opening mouth (Lockjaw).
  • Risus Sardonicus: Sardonic smile due to facial spasms.
  • Opisthotonus: Arching of back due to trunk muscle involvement.
  • Board-like Rigidity: Abdominal stiffness.
  • Spasms: Triggered by light, touch, or noise.

Management

Intervention Rationale
Neutralization Tetanus Immunoglobulin (TIG) to neutralize unbound toxin.
Antibiotics Penicillin or Erythromycin to kill bacteria.
Control Spasms Diazepam (sedation/relaxation), Neuromuscular blockers (if ventilated).
Environment Dark, quiet room. Minimize handling to prevent triggering spasms.
Airway Maintain airway; mechanical ventilation may be required.

Prevention: Tetanus Toxoid (TT) for pregnant mothers, clean delivery practices, and wound care.
