Congenital Abnormalities & Other Conditions

Congenital Abnormalities

A congenital malformation is a condition present at birth that varies from the standard presentation. Causes can be genetic, environmental (drugs, radiation), infectious (TORCH infections), or related to maternal illness (e.g., diabetes).

  • •Cleft Lip and Cleft Palate: Failure of facial tissues to unite. Management involves specialized feeding techniques to prevent aspiration and surgical repair.
  • •Spina Bifida: A midline defect of the vertebral column. In meningocele, the meninges protrude; in myelomeningocele, both meninges and spinal cord protrude. Nursing care involves protecting the sac from injury and infection (e.g., covering with a sterile, saline-soaked gauze) and positioning the infant prone.
  • •Talipes Equinovarus (Club Foot): A range of foot deformities where the foot is turned inward and downward. Treatment involves manipulation and application of plaster of Paris (Ponseti method).
  • •Down Syndrome (Trisomy 21): Caused by an extra copy of chromosome 21. Characterized by physical growth delays, distinct facial features (flat face, upward slanting eyes), and intellectual disability. Associated with other medical conditions like congenital heart defects.

Other Neonatal Conditions

Infant of a Diabetic Mother (IDM)

Maternal hyperglycemia leads to fetal hyperglycemia and hyperinsulinemia. This results in increased fetal growth (macrosomia) and fat deposition. At birth, the glucose supply from the mother is cut off, but the baby's high insulin levels persist, putting them at high risk for severe hypoglycemia. They are also at risk for RDS, hyperbilirubinemia, and polycythemia.

Management: These infants require intensive observation. Blood glucose should be monitored within the first hour of birth and then hourly for 6-8 hours. Early and frequent feeding is crucial to prevent hypoglycemia. If oral feeding is not possible, an IV glucose infusion is started.

Fetal Alcohol Syndrome (FAS)

A pattern of abnormalities in a child caused by maternal alcohol consumption during pregnancy. There is no known safe amount of alcohol during pregnancy. FAS is characterized by small size, slow development, distinct facial features (e.g., thin upper lip, smooth philtrum), and potential for birth defects and mental retardation.

Management: There is no specific treatment; management focuses on creating a supportive environment and addressing specific learning or health needs.

Neonatal Tetanus

A form of generalized tetanus that occurs in newborns born to non-immune mothers, usually through infection of the unhealed umbilical stump. Symptoms begin 3-10 days after birth and include failure to suck, inability to open the mouth (trismus or lockjaw), and severe muscle spasms.

Management: Aims to remove the source of the toxin (wound debridement, antibiotics), neutralize circulating toxin (anti-tetanus serum), and provide supportive care (sedation, NG tube feeding, respiratory support). Prevention through maternal immunization is paramount.
