Low Birth Weight & Prematurity

Low Birth Weight, Preterm, and Small for Gestational Age Babies

Definitions and Classifications

  • •Low Birth Weight (LBW): An infant whose birth weight is less than 2500g, regardless of gestational age.
  • •Very Low Birth Weight (VLBW): Birth weight less than 1500g.
  • •Extremely Low Birth Weight (ELBW): Birth weight less than 1000g.
  • •Preterm Infant: A baby born before 37 completed weeks of gestation.
  • •Small for Gestational Age (SGA): A baby whose birth weight is below the 10th percentile for their gestational age, indicating intrauterine growth restriction.

The Preterm Baby

Predisposing Factors: Maternal factors (age <17 or >35, hypertension, anaemia), foetal factors (multiple pregnancy, congenital abnormalities), placental issues (placenta praevia, abruption), and social factors (stress, alcohol, smoking).

Clinical Features: Small stature (<2500g), reddish skin with plentiful lanugo, widely open sutures, soft and foldable ear pinnae, weak or absent sucking/swallowing reflexes, weak cry, poor muscle tone, undescended testes in males, and prominent labia minora in females.

Nursing Management:

  • 1.Warmth: Nurse in an incubator or use Kangaroo Mother Care (KMC) to maintain a temperature of 36-37°C.
  • 2.Feeding: Use an NG tube for expressed breast milk (EBM). Start with 60-65 ml/kg/day in 8 divided doses. If oral feeds are not tolerated, give IV 10% dextrose. Gradually introduce cup and spoon feeding.
  • 3.Observation: Monitor vital signs, respiratory rhythm for apnoeic attacks, and signs of infection.
  • 4.Supplements: Give nutritional supplements like iron, folic acid, and vitamins from the second week.
  • 5.Infection Prevention: Administer prophylactic antibiotics and maintain strict hygiene.
  • 6.Discharge: Discharge when the baby weighs 2000-2500g and is stable.

The Small for Gestational Age (SGA) Baby

SGA babies are at risk for fetal hypoxia, birth asphyxia, hypothermia, and hypoglycemia.

Clinical Features: Often born after 37 weeks, they have pale, dry, loose skin with minimal subcutaneous fat. Unlike preterm babies, their skull bones are hard, reflexes are present, they have a strong cry, and they feed well.

Nursing Management:

  • 1.Monitor for Distress: Closely monitor for fetal distress during labour.
  • 2.Prevent Hypoglycemia: Start breastfeeding as soon as possible. Feeds are calculated at 90 mls/kg/day in 8 divided doses.
  • 3.Maintain Warmth: Nurse in a warm environment to prevent hypothermia.
  • 4.Monitor Progress: Weigh on alternate days; weight gain is typically more rapid than in preterm babies.

Kangaroo Mother Care (KMC)

KMC is defined as early, prolonged, and continuous skin-to-skin contact between a mother (or surrogate) and her low-birth-weight infant. It is a simple, effective method that helps maintain body temperature, promotes exclusive breastfeeding, improves bonding, reduces infections, and decreases hospital stay.
