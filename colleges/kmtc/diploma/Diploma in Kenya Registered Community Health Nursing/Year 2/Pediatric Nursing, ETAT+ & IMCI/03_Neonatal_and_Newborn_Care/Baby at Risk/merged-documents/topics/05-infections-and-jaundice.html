Neonatal Infections & Jaundice

Neonatal Infections

Ophthalmia Neonatorum

This is a condition characterized by purulent discharge from the eyes within the first 21 days of life. It is often caused by organisms like Neisseria gonorrhoeae or Chlamydia trachomatis, acquired during passage through an infected birth canal.

Management: Take an eye swab for culture and sensitivity. Administer antibiotic eye drops (e.g., Gentamycin) and clean the eyes with warm saline. Prophylactic instillation of 1% tetracycline eye ointment (TEO) for all newborns at birth is a key preventive measure.

Complication: Partial or permanent blindness.

Neonatal Sepsis

A clinical syndrome of systemic illness accompanied by bacteremia in the first month of life. It can be early-onset (first 72 hours, acquired from mother) or late-onset (after 72 hours, acquired from the environment or human contact).

Risk Factors: Prematurity, prolonged rupture of membranes, and maternal infection.

Clinical Features: Signs are often non-specific and include temperature instability (hypothermia or fever), respiratory distress, feeding intolerance, lethargy, poor cry, jaundice, and irritability.

Management: Investigations include a septic screen and blood culture. Treatment involves broad-spectrum parenteral antibiotics (e.g., Ampicillin and Gentamicin) and supportive care for feeding, breathing, and temperature control.

Neonatal Jaundice

Jaundice is the yellow discoloration of the skin and sclera caused by excessive bilirubin in the blood. When unconjugated (fat-soluble) bilirubin levels are too high, it can cross the blood-brain barrier and cause brain damage, a condition known as kernicterus.

Types of Jaundice

  • •Physiological Jaundice: Appears after 24 hours of life, peaks around day 3-5, and usually resolves within 1-2 weeks. It is due to the newborn's immature liver and higher red blood cell turnover.
  • •Pathological Jaundice: Appears within the first 24 hours, rises rapidly, and may persist. It is caused by underlying disorders like blood group incompatibility (Rhesus, ABO), G6PD deficiency, or infection.

Treatment Modalities

  1. 1.Phototherapy: This is the primary treatment. The baby is placed under a special blue-green light that converts unconjugated bilirubin in the skin into a water-soluble form that can be excreted. The baby's eyes must be covered to prevent retinal damage. It is a safe and effective method for lowering bilirubin levels.
  2. 2.Exchange Transfusion: Used when bilirubin levels are dangerously high and phototherapy is not effective enough. The baby's blood is gradually removed and replaced with donor blood to wash out excess bilirubin and maternal antibodies.
  3. 3.Protoporphyrins: These are heme oxygenase inhibitors that reduce bilirubin production. They are used in combination with other therapies.

Care of a Baby on Phototherapy

  • •Expose the whole body, turning the baby every 2 hours.
  • •Cover the eyes with a protective pad.
  • •Provide extra fluids to prevent dehydration due to increased insensible water loss.
  • •Monitor temperature to prevent hyperthermia.
  • •Encourage breastfeeding and eye contact during feeds when the eye pads are removed.
