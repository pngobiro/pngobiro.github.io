Respiratory Emergencies

Asphyxia Neonatorum (Birth Asphyxia)

Asphyxia neonatorum is a condition in which a baby fails to initiate and sustain breathing at birth. The severity is determined by the Apgar score.

  • •Mild Asphyxia: Apgar score 6-7. Requires clearing the airway and stimulation.
  • •Moderate Asphyxia: Apgar score 4-5. Requires resuscitation with oxygen and drugs.
  • •Severe Asphyxia: Apgar score 0-3. Requires intensive resuscitation and intubation.

Signs of Severe Asphyxia: No attempt to breathe, no cry, entire body is cyanosed (blue), no response to stimuli, very low or absent pulse rate, and poor muscle tone.

Predisposing Factors: Conditions causing fetal distress (e.g., cord prolapse, prolonged labour, APH), prematurity, airway blockage, birth injuries, and maternal diseases.

Nursing Management:

  1. 1.Resuscitation: The primary goal is to establish effective ventilation and assist circulation if necessary. This may involve clearing the airway, using a bag and mask, and performing chest compressions.
  2. 2.Warmth: Nurse the baby in an incubator to maintain body temperature.
  3. 3.Oxygenation: Administer oxygen as needed to manage apnoeic attacks.
  4. 4.Fluid & Drug Therapy: Give IV fluids for rehydration. Administer drugs like Sodium Bicarbonate to combat acidosis and Vitamin K to prevent haemorrhage.
  5. 5.Observation: Closely monitor vital signs, skin colour, and neurological status for signs of Hypoxic Ischemic Encephalopathy (HIE).
  6. 6.Feeding: Once stable, initiate feeding via an NG tube.

Complications: Brain damage (HIE), cardiac arrest, respiratory distress syndrome, and multi-organ dysfunction.

Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS)

Also known as Hyaline Membrane Disease, RDS occurs due to a lack of surfactant in the lung tissue, common in premature infants. Without sufficient surfactant, the alveoli collapse on expiration, leading to severe difficulty in breathing.

Predisposing Factors: Prematurity, perinatal hypoxia, profound hypothermia, and being a complication of birth asphyxia.

Clinical Features: Tachypnoea (respirations >60/min), nasal flaring, costal and sternal retractions, grunting on expiration, and generalized cyanosis.

Nursing Management: Management is symptomatic until the disease resolves.

  • •Maintain Warmth: Nurse in an incubator.
  • •Oxygenation: Administer oxygen or provide artificial ventilation to prevent hypoxia. Surfactant administration directly into the lungs via an endotracheal tube may be performed.
  • •Acidosis Prevention: Monitor blood pH and administer sodium bicarbonate if acidosis occurs.
  • •Nutrition & Hydration: Keep the baby nil per oral. Administer IV fluids (e.g., 10% dextrose).
  • •Positioning: Position the baby to allow for greatest air entry (e.g., prone with head extended).

Prevention: Preventing preterm labour is key. If premature delivery is unavoidable, administering dexamethasone to the mother at least 48 hours before delivery can help stimulate fetal lung maturity.
