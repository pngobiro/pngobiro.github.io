Bleeding Disorders & Birth Injuries

Haemorrhagic Disease of the Newborn (HDN)

This condition involves bleeding that occurs in the first few days of life due to Vitamin K deficiency. Vitamin K is essential for the synthesis of clotting factors in the liver. Newborns have low levels as it does not cross the placenta well and their gut is initially sterile, lacking the bacteria that produce it.

Predisposing Factors: Prematurity, birth trauma, birth asphyxia, and mothers on certain drugs like anticoagulants or anticonvulsants.

Clinical Features: Continuous oozing of blood from the umbilical cord, bleeding from mucous membranes (presenting as melaena or haematemesis), bleeding from puncture sites, and haematuria.

Management & Prevention:

  • •Prevention: All newborns are given a prophylactic dose of Vitamin K (0.5-1 mg) intramuscularly at birth.
  • •Treatment: To arrest active bleeding, a larger dose of Vitamin K (1-2 mg) is administered. If bleeding is severe, a transfusion of fresh frozen plasma or whole blood is necessary to replace clotting factors and blood volume.

Complications: Anaemia, hypovolaemic shock, and brain damage.

Birth Injuries

Birth injuries refer to trauma sustained by a foetus during birth. Common sites include the head, nerves, bones, and soft tissues.

Predisposing Factors: Large baby (macrosomia), prematurity, cephalo-pelvic disproportion (CPD), malpresentation, and instrumental delivery.

Head and Scalp Injuries

Feature Caput Succedaneum Cephalohaematoma
Nature Oedematous swelling of the scalp (serum fluid) above the periosteum. Collection of blood between the skull bone and the periosteum.
Onset Present at birth. Appears several hours to a day after birth.
Boundaries Crosses suture lines. Does not cross suture lines; limited to one cranial bone.

Resolution Disappears within a few days (usually 36 hours). May persist for weeks to months.

Intracranial Haemorrhage: Bleeding within the cranial vault, which is a much more serious injury. Clinical features include asphyxia, bulging fontanelle, shock, twitching, and a high-pitched cry.

Nerve Injuries (Brachial Plexus Palsy)

These injuries are caused by stretching of the nerves of the brachial plexus during a difficult delivery, such as with shoulder dystocia or a breech presentation.

  • •Erb's Palsy: Injury to the upper nerve roots (C5-C6). The affected arm is adducted and internally rotated with the elbow extended and wrist flexed, known as the "waiter's tip" position. The grasp reflex is intact.
  • •Klumpke's Palsy: Injury to the lower nerve roots (C8-T1). This affects the muscles of the hand, causing a "claw hand" appearance with an absent grasp reflex. It is less common than Erb's palsy.

Management: Rest the affected arm for 7-10 days, followed by gentle physiotherapy and passive range-of-motion exercises to prevent contractures. Splinting may be required.

Fractures

The most common fractures at birth involve the clavicle, humerus, and femur.

  • •Clavicle Fracture: Often asymptomatic and may only be noticed when a callus forms. Usually requires no treatment other than careful handling.
  • •Humerus/Femur Fractures: Present with deformity or lack of movement (pseudoparesis) due to pain. Treatment involves splinting or bandaging the limb to the body.
