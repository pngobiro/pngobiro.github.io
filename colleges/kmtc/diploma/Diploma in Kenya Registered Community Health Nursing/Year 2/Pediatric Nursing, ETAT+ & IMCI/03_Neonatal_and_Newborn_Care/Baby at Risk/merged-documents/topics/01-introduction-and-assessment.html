Introduction and Initial Assessment

Introduction to the High-Risk Infant

A high-risk infant is any neonate, regardless of weight, size, or gestational age, who has a greater than average chance of morbidity or mortality, especially within the first 28 days of life. Risk factors can be present before conception, during the prenatal period, at birth, or in the postnatal period, and include any condition that interferes with the normal birth process.

The newborn unit (NBU) or special care baby unit (SCBU) admits not only babies at risk but also offers accommodation to normal neonates due to the unstable condition or death of the mother.

Admission Criteria into the Newborn Unit

  • •Prematurity or Low Birth Weight
  • •Asphyxia neonatorum (birth asphyxia)
  • •Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS)
  • •Infants of diabetic mothers (risk of hypoglycemia)
  • •Haemorrhagic disease of the newborn
  • •Ophthalmia neonatorum
  • •Birth injuries
  • •Congenital abnormalities (e.g., hydrocephalus)
  • •Neonatal sepsis
  • •Maternal death or unstable maternal condition

Infection Control in the Newborn Unit

Due to the low immunity of babies in the NBU, infection control is critical. This necessitates high infection control measures within the unit.

  • 1.Cleanliness: Keep the unit clean and free from dust. Windows should remain closed to prevent dusty air from entering. Daily damp dusting and cleaning of incubators and cots are mandatory.
  • 2.Isolation: Isolate infected babies for barrier nursing. Staff working in the isolation room should not move into other nurseries.
  • 3.Traffic Control: Restrict visitors to control human traffic. Visitors should see the babies through a window glass.
  • 4.Hand Hygiene: Wash hands before and after handling any baby for any procedure.
  • 5.Aseptic Technique: Strictly observe aseptic technique while performing procedures.
  • 6.Utensil Hygiene: Feeding utensils should be rinsed, decontaminated, cleaned thoroughly in soapy water, and kept in a disinfectant solution (e.g., Presept) until the next feed.
  • 7.Equipment Cleaning: Clean incubators upon discharge or death of a baby before the next baby is placed inside.
  • 8.Maternal Hygiene: Mothers should change into clean clothes whenever they come to feed their babies. They should be educated on the importance of personal hygiene.

First Examination of the Baby

This routine procedure is done after the third stage of labour but is also performed in the nursery as part of the admission procedure. The aims are to rule out congenital abnormalities, check for birth injuries, and assess the baby's maturity.

  • •Head: Measure head circumference (average 35 cm). Check for moulding, fontanelles, sutures, and injuries like caput succedaneum or cephalohaematoma.
  • •Eyes: Check for conjunctival haemorrhage, discharge, or squints.
  • •Nose: Check for deformities, bleeding, nasal flaring (a sign of respiratory distress), and blocked nostrils.
  • •Mouth: Look for abnormalities like cleft lip or palate, tongue-tie, bleeding, or frothing.
  • •Ears: Check for shape, malformations, and any bleeding or CSF leakage.
  • •Neck: Check for abnormalities like congenital goiter or meningocele.
  • •Chest: Assess shape, symmetry, and movement. Check apex beat and for breast swelling.
  • •Abdomen: Check skin colour, umbilical cord for ligation and bleeding, and for hernias.
  • •Genitalia: For males, check for descended testes. For females, check that labia are well-formed.
  • •Limbs & Hips: Rule out fractures, dislocations (including congenital hip dislocation), Erb's palsy, extra digits, and foot deformities like club foot.
  • •Back & Anus: Check for abnormalities like spina bifida and ensure the anus is patent.
  • •Reflexes: Check for sucking, Moro, rooting, grasping, and stepping reflexes.
