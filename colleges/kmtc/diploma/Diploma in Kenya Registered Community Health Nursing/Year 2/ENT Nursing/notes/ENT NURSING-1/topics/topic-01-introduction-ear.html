Ear, Nose, and Throat (ENT) nursing involves the care of patients with conditions affecting the head and neck region. This course covers the anatomy, physiology, assessment, and management of common ENT disorders.

üî§ Common ENT Terminologies

Understanding specific medical terminology is crucial for accurate assessment and documentation in ENT nursing.

Term Definition
Acute Otitis Media Inflammation of the middle ear lasting less than 6 weeks.
Cholesteatoma A benign tumor of the middle ear or mastoid or both; can destroy structures of the temporal bone.
Chronic Otitis Media Repeated episodes of acute otitis media causing irreversible tissue damage and persistent tympanic membrane perforation.
Conductive Hearing Loss Loss of hearing in which effective sound transmission to the inner ear is obstructed.
Dizziness Altered sensation of orientation in space.
Endolymphatic Hydrops Dilation of the endolymphatic space of the inner ear.
Exostoses Small, hard bony protrusions in the lower posterior bony portion of the ear canal.
Labyrinthitis Inflammation of the inner ear.
Meniere's Disease A condition of the inner ear characterised by a triad of symptoms: episodic vertigo, tinnitus, and fluctuating sensorineural hearing loss.
Myringotomy Incision of the tympanic membrane.
Ossiculoplasty Surgical reconstruction of the middle ear bones to restore hearing.
Otalgia Ear pain.
Otitis Externa Inflammation of the external auditory canal.
Otorrhea Drainage from the ear.
Otosclerosis A condition characterised by abnormal spongy bone formation around the stapes.
Presbycusis Progressive hearing loss associated with aging.
Rhinorrhea Drainage from the nose.
Sensorineural Hearing Loss Loss of hearing due to damage of the organ of hearing or cranial nerve VIII or both.
Tinnitus Subjective perception of sound with internal origin, unwanted noises in the ear.
Vertigo An illusion of movement in which the individual or surroundings are perceived to be moving.

ü©∫ Anatomy and Physiology of the Ear

The ear is the organ of hearing and balance/equilibrium. Except for the pinna, the structures of the ear are located within the petrous portion of the temporal bone. It is divided into three distinct parts: the Outer, Middle, and Inner Ear.

Fig. 1.1: General Anatomy of the Ear

‚ñ∂ The Outer Ear

The outer ear consists of the auricle (or pinna) and the external auditory canal.

  • Auricle (Pinna): The expanded portion projecting from the side of the head, composed of fibroelastic cartilage covered with skin. The lobule (earlobe) is the soft pliable part at the lower extremity.
  • External Acoustic Meatus: A slightly 'S' shaped tube about 2.5cm long, extending from the auricle to the tympanic membrane. It is lined with skin containing hairs. The lateral third contains numerous sebaceous and ceruminous glands.
  • Cerumen (Wax): A sticky material secreted by ceruminous glands containing lysozyme and immunoglobulins. Jaw movements during chewing help move wax towards the exterior.
  • Tympanic Membrane: Completely separates the external acoustic meatus from the middle ear. It is oval, pearly grey, translucent, and composed of three layers: outer hairless skin, middle fibrous tissue, and inner mucous membrane.
‚ñ∂ The Middle Ear

The middle ear is an irregularly shaped air-filled cavity within the temporal bone.

Fig. 1.2: Middle Ear Structures

Key Structures:

  • Eustachian (Auditory) Tube: Connects the middle ear to the nasopharynx. It is about 4 cm long, lined with ciliated epithelium, and serves to equalize pressure on both sides of the tympanic membrane and drain the middle ear.
  • The Ossicles: Three small bones that transmit sound vibrations.
    • Malleus (Hammer): Handle touches the tympanic membrane; head joins the incus.
    • Incus (Anvil): The middle bone; articulates with malleus and stapes.
    • Stapes (Stirrup): The medial bone; its footplate fits into the oval window.

Pediatric Consideration

In children, the Eustachian tube is shorter and wider, making them more susceptible to middle ear infections (otitis media). It is also more easily blocked by enlarged adenoids or inflammation.

‚ñ∂ The Inner Ear

The inner ear contains the sensory organs for hearing and balance. It consists of a bony labyrinth and a membranous labyrinth.

Fig. 1.3: Inner Ear Anatomy

  • Fluids: Perilymph fills the space between the bony and membranous labyrinth. Endolymph fills the membranous labyrinth.
  • Vestibule: Contains the oval and round windows (bony labyrinth) and the utricle and saccule (membranous labyrinth). Important for static balance.
  • Cochlea: Snail-shell shaped structure for hearing. Contains three compartments: Scala Vestibuli, Scala Tympani, and Scala Media (Cochlear Duct). The Organ of Corti lies on the basilar membrane and contains hair cells that act as auditory receptors.
  • Semicircular Canals: Three horseshoe-shaped canals lying in three planes of space. They contain hair-like nerve endings detecting motion. Important for kinetic balance.

üß† Physiology of Hearing & Balance

Physiology of Hearing

Sound transmission to the inner ear occurs through three routes:

  1. Through the ossicular chain (tympanic membrane to oval window).
  2. Directly across the middle ear via air waves.
  3. Bone transmission through the skull to the inner ear.
‚ö°

Mechanism of Hearing

  1. 1

    Sound waves enter external canal and strike the tympanic membrane.

  2. 2

    Ossicles vibrate and transmit vibrations to the oval window.

  3. 3

    Fluid in the cochlea is set in motion.

  4. 4

    Wave-like motion stimulates hair cells (receptors) in the Organ of Corti.

  5. 5

    Stimuli are sent via the vestibulocochlear nerve (CN VIII) to the temporal lobe of the brain for interpretation.

Balance and Equilibrium

Balance relies on the integration of inputs from multiple systems:

  • Labyrinth-vestibular system (Ears)
  • Visual input (Eyes)
  • Proprioceptors (Muscles and joints)
  • Tactile receptors (Skin)

Static Balance (at rest) is centered in the utricle and saccule. Kinetic Balance (movement) is associated with the semicircular canals.
