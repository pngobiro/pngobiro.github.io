üëÖ The Pharynx

It is a tube 12 - 14 cm long. It extends from the base of the skull to the level of the 6th cervical vertebra. It lies behind the nose, mouth, and larynx.

‚ñ∂ Anatomical Divisions
  • Nasopharynx: Lies behind the nose, above the level of the soft palate. Lateral walls contain openings for auditory tubes. Posterior wall contains pharyngeal tonsils (lymphoid tissue).
  • Oropharynx: Lies behind the mouth, from soft palate level to C3. Contains palatine tonsils between folds of the lateral walls.
  • Laryngopharynx: Extends from the oropharynx above and continues as the esophagus below.

Functions

  • Passage for air and food.
  • Warming and humidifying air.
  • Taste: Nerve endings in the epithelium of oral and pharyngeal parts.
  • Hearing: Eustachian tube equalizes pressure.
  • Protection: Through tonsils.
  • Speech: Provide a resonating chamber for sound.

üì¢ The Larynx (Voice Box)

Extends from the root of the tongue and hyoid bone to the trachea. It lies in front of the laryngopharynx and is composed of cartilages attached by ligaments and membranes.

Main Cartilages:

Cartilage Description
Thyroid (1) Forms laryngeal prominence. Incomplete posteriorly.
Cricoid (1) Shaped like a signet ring.
Arytenoid (2) Pyramid shaped. Form part of posterior wall. Attachment for vocal cords.
Epiglottis (1) Leaf shaped fibroelastic cartilage. Closes off larynx during swallowing to protect lungs.

Vocal Cords:

Two folds of mucous membrane extending from thyroid cartilage to arytenoid cartilages.

  • Abducted (Open): Muscles relaxed. Low pitched sound.
  • Adducted (Closed): Muscles contracted/tight. High pitched sound. Closed when not in use.

Functions:

  • Production of sound / Speech
  • Protection of lower respiratory tract
  • Passageway for air
  • Humidifying, filtering, and warming air

ü©∫ Assessment of the Throat

  • History: Sore throat, discharge, sense of lump/swelling, dyspnea, dysphagia, hoarseness, cough.
  • Physical Exam: Ask client to open mouth wide; use tongue depressor. View anterior/posterior pillars, palatine tonsils, uvula, and posterior pharynx. Inspect for color, size, symmetry, exudate, and ulceration.

ü¶† Tonsillitis and Adenoiditis

Tonsils (palatine) and Adenoids (pharyngeal) are lymphoid tissues. Infection is often caused by Group A Beta-hemolytic Streptococcus.

Manifestations

  • Sore throat
  • Fever
  • Snoring & Mouth breathing
  • Difficulty swallowing
  • Ear ache
  • Foul smelling breath

Management

  • Antibiotics (Penicillins)
  • Increased fluid intake
  • Analgesics
  • Salt water gargles
  • Surgery (Tonsillectomy/Adenoidectomy)
‚ÑπÔ∏è

Surgical Indications

  • Repeated episodes despite antibiotics
  • Hypertrophy causing obstruction/sleep apnea
  • Repeated attacks of purulent otitis media
  • Peritonsillar abscess occluding the airway
‚ö†Ô∏è

Contraindications

  • Age less than 5 years
  • Cleft palate (done after repair)
  • Bleeding diseases
  • Polio breakout
  • Active PTB

Nursing Management (Tonsillectomy)

‚ñ∂ Pre-operative Care
  • Routine pre-op care (consent, NPO 6 hrs, vitals).
  • Rehydrate patient.
  • Allay anxiety.
  • Warm water gargles to soothe throat.
‚ñ∂ Post-operative Care
  • Position: Prone with head turned to side (allows drainage).
  • Airway: Do not remove oral airway until gag/swallow reflex returns.
  • Bleeding: Observe for frequent swallowing, vomiting blood, increased pulse/temp.
  • Comfort: Ice collar to reduce edema. Analgesics. Cold water/drinks if no bleeding.
  • Diet: Liquid/semi-liquid for several days. Avoid spicy, hot, acidic, rough foods.
  • Education: Avoid vigorous brushing/gargling, too much talking, or coughing.

üíä Peritonsillar Abscess (Quinsy)

Collection of purulent exudate between the tonsillar capsule and surrounding tissues. Often caused by beta-hemolytic streptococcus.

üö®

Medical Emergency

Edema can cause airway obstruction, which is life-threatening.

Clinical Manifestations

  • Trismus: Inability to open mouth due to muscle spasm.
  • Drooling.
  • Raspy voice.
  • Odynophagia: Severe burning/squeezing pain when swallowing.
  • Dysphagia & Otalgia.
  • Swelling of soft palate / Tonsillar hypertrophy.

Management

  • Antibiotics.
  • Surgical: Needle aspiration, Incision & Drainage (I&D), or Tonsillectomy.
  • Airway: May require intubation/tracheotomy if obstructed.
  • Nursing: Saline gargles (warm pre-op, cool post-op), fluids.

üîá Laryngitis

Inflammation of the mucous membrane lining the larynx accompanied by edema of the vocal cords.

Causes

Voice abuse (shouting), dust/chemicals, URTI, temperature changes, smoking.

Manifestations

  • Hoarseness or Aphonia (complete loss of voice).
  • Severe dry cough, sore throat.
  • Persistent urge to clear throat.

Management

Acute

  • Rest the voice.
  • Avoid irritants (dust, smoke).
  • Steam inhalation (with menthol).
  • Antibiotics if bacterial.
  • Increase fluids.

Chronic

  • Eliminate infection.
  • Stop smoking.
  • Topical/inhalation corticosteroids.
  • Humidified environment.
‚ö†Ô∏è

Report Complications

Instruct patient to report: Loss of voice with difficult swallowing, Hemoptysis, Noisy respirations, or continued hoarseness after >5 days of voice rest.
