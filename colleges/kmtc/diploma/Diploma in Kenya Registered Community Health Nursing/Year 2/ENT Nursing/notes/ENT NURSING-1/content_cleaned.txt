<p>Review of anatomy and physiology of the ear</p>
<ul>
<li>It is the organ of hearing and balance/equilibrium. Except the pinnae, the structures of the ear are within the petrous portion of the temporal bone.</li>
<li>Its divided into three parts:</li>
</ul>
<p>OUTER EAR</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Consists of the auricle or pinnae and external auditory canal.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The auricle is the expanded portion projecting from the side of the head composed of fibroelastic cartilage, covered with skin.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The lobule/earlobe is the soft pliable part at the lower extremity.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The external acoustic meatus is a slightly ‘S’ shaped tube about 2.5cm long, extending from the auricle to the tympanic membrane.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>It is lined with skin containing hairs.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The lateral third has numerous sebaceous and ceruminous glands. Ceruminous glands secrete cerumen/wax – a sticky material containing lysozyme and immunoglobins.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Movements of the temporomandibular joint during chewing and speaking massage the cartilagionous meatus, moving the wax towards the exterior.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The tympanic membrane- completely separates the external acoustic meatus from the middle ear.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>It is oval in shape with three layers – outer covering of hairless skin, middle layer of fibrous tissue and an inner lining of mucous membrane.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Normally it appears pearly grey and translucent.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>INNER EAR</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>It is an irregularly shaped air filled cavity within the petrous portion of the temporal bone.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The medial wall is a thin layer of temporal bone with two openings, the round and oval window.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Air reaches the cavity through the eustachian or auditory tube, which is about 4 cm long and lined with ciliated epithelium. It connects the middle ear to the nasopharynx and equalises pressure on both sides of the membrane.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>It also drains the middle ear.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>In children it is shorter and wider thus making children more susceptible to middle ear infections. It is also more easily blocked by allergies, enlarged adenoids or inflammation of the nose and throat.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The middle ear has three bones called ossicles.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Malleus: hammer shaped. Its handle is in contact with the tympanic membrane and the head forms a movable joint with the incus.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Incus: is the middle anvil shaped bone. Its bony articulates with the malleus &amp; its long process with the stapes. Its held to the wall of the cavity by its short process.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Stapes: it is the medial stirrup shaped bone. Its head articulates with the incus and its footplate fits into the oval window.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>INNER EAR</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>It is filled with fluid. Between the bony labyrinth and the membranous labyrinth is filled with perilymph. The membranous labyrinth is filled with endolymph.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The vestibule is the expanded part nearest the middle ear. It contains the oval and round window in the bony labyrinth and the utricle and saccule in the membranous labyrinth.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The cochlea resembles a snail shell. It has a broad base continuous with the vestibule and a narrow apex. A cross section through shows three compartments : the scala vestibuli, scala tympani and scala media or cochlear duct.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Scala vestibuli originates at the oval window and scala tympani ends at the round window</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The cochlear duct has supporting cells and specialised cochlear hair cells lying on the basilar membrane, they contain auditory receptors that are connected to the tectorial membrane. These cells form the spiral organ/organ of corti.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Semicircular canals:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>They are three, horse shoe shaped and lie in the three planes of space.</li>
<li>They contain hair like nerve endings that are set in motion by the fluid in the canals.</li>
<li>They transmit information about the body's position to the brain.</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="physiology-of-hearing">Physiology of hearing:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#physiology-of-hearing" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Sound waves reach the inner ear through three routes:</li>
</ul>
<ol>
<li>Through the ossicular chain from the tympanic membrane to the oval window.</li>
<li>Directly across the middle ear by means of air waves.</li>
<li>Bone transmission through the walls of the external auditory canal to the inner ear.</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Sound waves enter through the external auditory canal and strike the tympanic membrane which vibrates at various speeds according to pitch.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Ossicles in middle ear vibrate and transmit vibrations to the oval window.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Fluid in the cochlea is set in motion.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Wavelike motion of fluid passes the vibrations onto the tiny hair like nerve endings (receptors) in the organ of corti.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Nerve endings stimuli are sent to the vestibulocochlear nerve to the temporal lobe of the cerebral cortex where sound is interpreted.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="balance-and-equilibrium">Balance and equilibrium<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#balance-and-equilibrium" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Sense of static balance (when a person is at rest) is centered in the utricle and saccule of the inner ear.</li>
<li>Balance with movement is associated with the semicircular canals.</li>
<li>Balance depends on:-</li>
<li>Receptors in the ears (labyrinth-vestibular system)</li>
<li>Tactile skin receptors</li>
<li>Visual input (eyes)</li>
<li>Proprioceptors in muscles (muscles n joints)</li>
<li>Send info about equilibrium n balance to the brain (cerebellar) for coordination and perception in the cerebral cortex.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="history-taking">History taking<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#history-taking" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>An otologic history includes:-</li>
<li>Demographic data</li>
<li>Chief complaint e.g. hearing loss, pain, tinnitus, vertigo, dizziness, drainage, loss of balance.</li>
<li>Frequency, duration, onset, precipitating factors</li>
<li>Past medical history</li>
<li>Childhood illnesses – otitis media, eardrum perforations, mumps, measles, meningitis, tonsillectomy, ear surgery</li>
<li>Medications</li>
<li>Ototoxicity drugs – aminoglycosides, quinine, chemotherapeutic agents. Asprin causes tinnitus.</li>
<li>Allergies</li>
<li>Family history of hearing loss</li>
<li>Social history – occupation, leisure activities &amp; hobbies.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="physical-examination">Physical examination<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#physical-examination" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ol>
<li>Inspection and palpation</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>Note size, configuration, inflammation, lumps, lesions, cysts.</li>
<li>Palpate and manipulate pinna to detect tenderness, nodules or tophi (small, hard nodules in the helix that are deposits of uric acid crystals characteristics of gout).</li>
</ul>
<ol start="2">
<li>Direct observation</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>Tip head slightly to the opposite side while pulling the pinnae up, back and out.</li>
<li>Use penlight to inspect canal for abnormality – excess wax, redness, scaliness, swelling, drainage and cysts.</li>
</ul>
<p>Assessment of the auditory system</p>
<ol>
<li>Otoscopy – using an otoscope. It has a light source and magnifying lens. Hold the otoscope in the right hand, grasp auricle with opposite hand and gently pull outwards to straighten the canal.</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>Insert the speculum and observe.</li>
<li>A normal tympanic membrane appears pearly grey and translucent. It is positioned obliquely at the base of the canal.</li>
</ul>
<ol start="2">
<li>Whisper test – the examiner covers the untested ear while testing the other ear.</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>Whisper softly from a distance of 1-2 feet from the unoccluded ear out of patients sight.</li>
<li>Patients with normal hearing can repeat what is whispered.</li>
</ul>
<ol start="3">
<li>Weber test – it uses bone conduction to test lateralization of sound.</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>A tuning fork is set in motion by striking it and is placed on the patient's vertex or forehead.</li>
<li>A patient with normal hearing hears the sound equally in both ears or describes the sound as centred in the middle of the head.</li>
<li>A patient with conductive hearing loss (otosclerosis, O.M) hears the sound better in the affected ear.</li>
<li>A patient with sensorineural hearing loss hears the sound better in the normal/better hearing ear.</li>
<li>It is useful in detecting unilateral hearing loss.</li>
</ul>
<ol start="4">
<li>Rinne test- useful for distinguishing between conductive and sensorineural hearing loss.</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>
<p>A vibrating tuning fork is placed on the mastoid bone, once the patient signals he does not hear the sound, the fork is put 2 inches in front of the ear.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Normally, air conduction is audible longer than bone conduction.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>In conductive hearing loss, bone conducted sound is audible as long as or longer than air conducted sound.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>In sensorineural hearing loss the air conducted sound is heard longer than the bone conducted sound.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<ol start="5">
<li>Audiometry – it is the most important diagnostic tool.</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Types of audiometry –
I. Pure tone audiometry – the sound stimulus consists of a pure or musical tone.
II. Speech audiometry- ability to hear and discriminate sounds and words. The louder the tone before the patient perceives it, the greater the hearing loss. The patient wears earphones and signals when the tone is heard.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>When evaluating hearing, three characteristics are important:-
a) Frequency – refers to the number of sound waves produced per second. It is measured per cycle or Hertz(Hz) per second.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>a normal human ear perceives sound in frequencies ranging from 20 – 20,000Hz.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Frequencies from 500 – 2000 Hz are important in understanding everyday speech and are referred to as speech range/frequencies
b) Pitch – used to describe frequency of a tone. 100 Hz is considered low pitch and greater than 10,000Hz high pitch.
c) Intensity – measures the loudness of sound in decibels(dB). It is the pressure exerted by sound. Hearing loss is measured in decibels.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Sound louder than 80dB is perceived by the human ear as harsh and can damage the inner ear.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The critical level of loudness is approximately 30dB.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="tests-to-detect-vestibular-conditions">TESTS TO DETECT VESTIBULAR CONDITIONS<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#tests-to-detect-vestibular-conditions" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ol>
<li>ROMBERG TEST – assesses the inner ear for balance.</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>The client stands with the feet together, arms by the side and eyes closed. Note the ability of the patient to maintain upright posture with only minimal amount of swaying.</li>
<li>If the client loses balance, it is a positive Romberg, indicating vestibular problems.</li>
</ul>
<ol start="2">
<li>CALORIC TEST – used to determine if an alteration exists in the vestibular origin of the vestibulococchlear nerve.</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>
<p>With the client either seated or supine, instil water into the external ear canal. Sometimes warm and cold water are alternated. Test the affected side first.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Normal response to this is</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Nystagmus (involuntary rapid rhythmic eye movements)</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Feeling of falling</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Vertigo (feeling of spinning)</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Nausea</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Vomiting</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Decrease or absence of these response within three minutes indicates abnormality.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Contraindications:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Perforated ear drum</li>
<li>Infection of the middle ear</li>
</ul>
<p>NB: anticipate vomiting and provide an emesis basin.</p>
<ol start="3">
<li><strong>ELECTRONYSTAGMOGRAPHY</strong> –electrodes are placed near the clients eyes to assess for alterations in the vestibular system.</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>caloric test is performed while eye movements are recorded on a graph. it assesses occulomotor and vestibular systems and their corresponding interaction.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="hearing-loss">HEARING LOSS<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#hearing-loss" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>It ranges from minor difficulty in understanding words or hearing certain sounds to total deafness.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="types">Types<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#types" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>A) <strong>Conductive hearing loss</strong>: Also called transmission hearing loss.</p>
<ul>
<li>Conduction of sound waves to the organs of hearing is disrupted.</li>
<li>It is further classified as</li>
<li>a. Air conduction loss – due to a defect in the external auditory canal</li>
<li>b. Bone conduction loss – due to a defect in the bones of the middle ear.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="causes">Causes<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#causes" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ol>
<li>Obstruction to the passage of sound through the external auditory canal.</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>Impacted cerumen</li>
<li>Otomycosis</li>
<li>Swelling of the canal walls(marked)</li>
<li>Foreign body</li>
</ul>
<ol start="2">
<li>Abnormalities of the tympanic membrane</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>Perforation</li>
<li>Thickening</li>
<li>Retraction</li>
<li>Scarring</li>
</ul>
<ol start="3">
<li>Pathologic change in the middle ear which interferes with the mobility of the ossicular chain.</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Otosclerosis (fixation of the ossicle)</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Secretions</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Granulations</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Otitis media</p>
</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="b-perceptive-sensorineural-hearing-loss">B) PERCEPTIVE/SENSORINEURAL HEARING LOSS<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#b-perceptive-sensorineural-hearing-loss" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Involves a disturbance of the organs of the inner ear or the transmitting nerve.</li>
<li>It is further classified as:</li>
<li>Sensory – due to damage in the cochlea</li>
<li>Neural – due to damage of the eighth cranial nerve</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="causes">Causes:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#causes" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ol>
<li>Toxic neuritis of the acoustic branch of the auditory nerve. May occur in mumps, influenza, diphtheria, rubella. mumps is a common cause of unilateral deafness.</li>
<li>Trauma – blow or falls causing</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Concussions of the labyrinth</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Fractures of the base of the skull – injure labyrinth</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Industrial noise and gunfire</p>
</li>
</ul>
<ol start="3">
<li>Drugs and poisons e.g quinine, salicylates, aminoglycosides, arsenic, mercury, lead.</li>
<li>Meningitis – destroys the nerves</li>
<li>Old age – presbycusis</li>
<li>Tumors – acoustic neuroma</li>
<li>Meniere’s disease</li>
<li>Congenital predisposition</li>
</ol>
<p>C) CENTRAL HEARING LOSS</p>
<ul>
<li>The brain is unable to interpret sound waves after they have been transmitted.</li>
<li>May occur in atherosclerosis or after CVA</li>
<li>The deficit is in the auditory nucleus of the cerebral cortex</li>
</ul>
<p>D) <strong>FUNCTIONAL HEARING LOSS</strong> – No organic cause is found and there is no damage to the auditory nerve.</p>
<ul>
<li>It is believed to arise from psychological or emotional conditions.</li>
</ul>
<p>E) <strong>MIXED HEARING LOSS</strong> – when the patient has both conductive and sensorineural hearing loss.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="clinical-manifestations">Clinical manifestations<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#clinical-manifestations" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Tinnitus</li>
<li>Inability to hear in groups</li>
<li>Answering questions inappropriately</li>
<li>Cupping hand around ear</li>
<li>Showing irritability with others</li>
<li>Increased volume of TV or radio</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="later-manifestations">Later manifestations<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#later-manifestations" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>
<p>speech deterioration – slur words, speech may sound flat, loud or soft.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Fatigue – due to straining while listening to conversation</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Indifference – disinterest as they cannot hear what is said</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Social withdrawal – keep to themselves as they don’t hear what is said.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Insecurity – lack of self confidence, fear that they will say the wrong things.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Indecision and procrastination - can't hear thus they doubt their ability to make decisions</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Suspiciousness – hear part of conversations and think others are talking about them.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>False pride – conceal hearing loss by pretending not to be interested in conversations.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Loneliness and unhappiness due to silence</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Tendency to dominate conversation</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Risk factors</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Family history of sensory neural hearing loss</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Congenital malformation of the ear esp. cranial structure</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Low birth weight less than 1.5 kgs</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Use of ototoxic medication</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Viral infections e.g. mumps, measles, chicken pox</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Age</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Exposure to loud noise</p>
</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="management">MANAGEMENT<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#management" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>It aims at restoring or assisting hearing, aural rehabilitation and managing tinnitus.</li>
</ul>
<ol>
<li>Restore hearing:</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>Provide antibiotics for bacterial infections</li>
<li>Acyclovir or oral corticosteroids for herpes virus infection</li>
<li>Stop any ototoxic medications. This lessens the progress of hearing loss or reverses the hearing loss.</li>
</ul>
<ol start="2">
<li>Assist hearing</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Use of hearing aids which amplify sound.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>A hearing aid has: - a microphone which receives sound waves from the air and changes them to electrical signals.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>An amplifier which increases the strength of the electrical signal</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Receiver / loudspeaker – which changes electrical signals into sound waves.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Battery</p>
</li>
</ul>
<ol start="3">
<li>Managing tinitus</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>There is no proven treatment.</li>
<li>Counsel the client and reassure</li>
</ul>
<ol start="4">
<li>Aural rehabilitation</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Aims at maximising the hearing impaired clients communication skills.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Rehab is directed towards teaching more effective use of other senses in communication e.g. vision.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Speech or lip reading – involves integration of lip movements, gestures, clues.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Sign language – using hands to represent words or phrases.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Non verbal aids – draw attention with hand movements. Avoid chewing, careless expressions the patient may misinterpret.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Verbal aids – speak normally and slowly. Don’t exaggerate facial expression, use simple sentences, write difficult words and avoid shouting and move closer to the better ear..</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Implantable hearing devices:</strong></p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Cochlear implant:</strong> contains a small computer chip which changes spoken word to electrical impulses which are transmitted through the auditory nerve endings in the cochlea to the brain where they are interpreted.</li>
<li><strong>Temporal bone stimulation:</strong> for patients with conductive hearing loss. It has an internal and external part. the external part is above the ear and transmits sound to the inner device which is implanted to the skull.</li>
</ol>
<p>Sound is transmitted to the inner ear through the skull.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="prevention-of-hearing-loss">PREVENTION OF HEARING LOSS<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#prevention-of-hearing-loss" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ol>
<li><strong>Primary</strong> – aims at minimizing risks e.g. noise, trauma, use of ototoxic drugs, and infection.</li>
<li><strong>Secondary</strong> – early detection of hearing impairment through screening and referral of any hearing problems.</li>
<li><strong>Tertiary</strong> – focuses on improvement of optimal functioning through rehabilitation programmes.</li>
</ol>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="conditions-of-the-external-ear">CONDITIONS OF THE EXTERNAL EAR<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#conditions-of-the-external-ear" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="impacted-cerumen">IMPACTED CERUMEN<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#impacted-cerumen" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Wax may form a solid plug or may be of a large amount and blocks the external auditory canal.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="symptoms">Symptoms:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#symptoms" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<ul>
<li>Sense of blockage</li>
<li>Conductive hearing impairment</li>
<li>Otalgia</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="management">Management:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#management" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<ol>
<li>removed by syringing/irrigating the outer ear with water warmed to body temperature. The jet of water is aimed above and behind the foreign body.</li>
<li>Manual removal of the wax using instruments i.e cerumen curette or aural suction. Warm glycerine is instilled 30 minutes before to soften the wax. Or mineral oil.</li>
</ol>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="foreign-body">FOREIGN BODY<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#foreign-body" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>A foreign body can either be animate or inanimate.</li>
<li>Determine the nature of the foreign body i.e food stuff, insect, wood, plastic etc.</li>
<li>If the foreign body is a food substance, do not attempt to irrigate as this will cause it to swell leading to pain and possible injury to the eardrum.</li>
<li>If it is a small insect, shine a light, the insect may be drawn to the light. Alternatively, apply a few drops of mineral oil or glycerine and let the patient lie on the affected side.</li>
<li>Visualise the foreign body and remove.</li>
<li>Do not attempt to remove an object without visualising as this pushes it further in.</li>
</ul>
<p>NB/irrigation,suction and instrumentation are the mainstay management</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="otitis-externa-external-otitis">OTITIS EXTERNA/EXTERNAL OTITIS<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#otitis-externa-external-otitis" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>It is inflammation or infection of the epithelium of the auricle and external auditory canal</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="causes">Causes<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#causes" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Water in the ear (swimmers ear)</li>
<li>Trauma to the skin of the ear canal</li>
<li>Systemic conditions e.g. vitamin deficiency, endocrine disorders.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="causative-organisms">Causative organisms<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#causative-organisms" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Pseudomonas</li>
<li>Staphylococcus</li>
<li>Aspergillus</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="clinical-manifestations">Clinical manifestations<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#clinical-manifestations" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Pain</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Discharge from external auditory canal</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Aural tenderness (not present in middle ear infections)</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Fever</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Cellulitis</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Lymphadenopathy</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Pruritus</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Otoscopy</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Ear canal is erythematous and edematous</li>
<li>Discharge may be yellow or green, foul smelling</li>
<li>Fungal infection – hairlike black spores may be visible.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Management</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Aims at relieving discomfort, reducing swelling and eradicating infection.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Administer analgesics for pain</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Antibiotics specific to the causative organism e.g. CAF, neomycin</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Corticosteroids for inflammation</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Warm moist compress for comfort</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Fungal infections give nystatin</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>If fever and cellulitis is present give systemic antibiotics.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Advice the patient to avoid cotton tipped applicators to clean external auditory canal as they may cause trauma which leads to infection.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Avoid getting ear canal wet, avoid swimming or wear ear plugs when swimming.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="conditions-of-the-middle-ear">CONDITIONS OF THE MIDDLE EAR<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#conditions-of-the-middle-ear" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="tympanic-membrane-perforation">TYMPANIC MEMBRANE PERFORATION<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#tympanic-membrane-perforation" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>It is caused by infection in the middle ear or by trauma.</li>
<li>Sources of trauma include – skull fracture, explosive injury, severe blow to the ears, foreign objects.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="management">Management:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#management" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Most heal spontaneously within weeks after rupture.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Some perforations may persist because scar tissue grows over the edges of the perforation, preventing extension of the epithelial cells across the margins and final healing.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Incase of head injury or temporal bone fracture observe for CSF leakage (otorrhea or rhinorrhea) – a clear, watery drainage from the ear or nose.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>While healing, protect the ear from water.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>For perforations that do not heal spontaneously surgical intervention is required.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Tympanoplasty – surgical repair of the tympanic membrane. It is based on:-</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The need to prevent water from entering the middle ear.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Desire to improve patients hearing.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Tissue, usually temporalis fascia is placed across the perforation to allow healing.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="acute-otitis-media">ACUTE OTITIS MEDIA<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#acute-otitis-media" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>It is an acute infection of the middle ear lasting less than six weeks.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="causes">Causes<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#causes" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Streptococcus pneumoniae</li>
<li>Haemophilus influenza</li>
<li>Moraxella catarrhalis</li>
<li>These organisms reach the middle ear after:-</li>
<li>eustachian tube dysfunction caused by obstruction related to URTI</li>
<li>Eustachian tube dysfunction due to inflammation of surrounding structures e.g. sinusitis, adenoid hypertrophy</li>
<li>Obstruction due to allergic reactions</li>
<li>Tympanic membrane perforation</li>
</ul>
<p>Clinical manifestations</p>
<ul>
<li>Otalgia relieved by perforation which can either be spontaneous or therapeutic.</li>
<li>Drainage from the ear</li>
<li>Fever</li>
<li>Conductive hearing loss</li>
<li>Otoscopic examination- reveals a normal external auditory canal. The tympanic membrane is erythematous and often bulging.</li>
</ul>
<p>Risk factors</p>
<ul>
<li>Age i.e. less than one year</li>
<li>Chronic upper respiratory tract infections</li>
<li>Medical conditions that predispose to ear infections e.g. Down’s syndrome, cleft palate.</li>
<li>Chronic exposure to second hand cigarette smoke</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="management">MANAGEMENT<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#management" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Early and appropriate broad spectrum antibiotics therapy – amoxycillin, cotrimoxazole or erythromycin for 10 days.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Analgesics for pain.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>If drainage occurs give antibiotic otic preparation.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Permanent hearing loss rarely occurs</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Antihistamines for allergic reactions</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Complications are rare but include: meningitis, brain abscess</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Myringotomy or tympanotomy – incision of the tympanic membrane. It is done to relieve pressure and to drain serous or purulent fluid from the middle ear thus relieving pain.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>It is done under local anesthesia and microscope guidance.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Drainage can be analysed for culture and sensitivity.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Incision heals within 24 – 72 hours.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>If AOM recurs a ventilating tube or pressure equalizing tube is inserted. It is retained for 6 – 18 months. It is extruded with normal skin migration of the tympanic membrane with the hole healing.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="serrous-otitis-media-middle-ear-effusion-glue-ear">SERROUS OTITIS MEDIA/MIDDLE EAR EFFUSION/GLUE EAR<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#serrous-otitis-media-middle-ear-effusion-glue-ear" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Involves fluid in the middle ear without evidence of active infection.</li>
<li>Results due to negative pressure in the middle ear secondary to eustachian tube obstruction, which can occur with:-</li>
<li>Radiation therapy</li>
<li>Barotrauma – sudden pressure changes e.g. deep sea diving, airplane descent.</li>
<li>Eustachian tube dysfunction from URTI or allergy.</li>
<li>Carcinoma – nasopharyngeal cancer obstructing the tube.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="clinical-manifestations">Clinical manifestations:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#clinical-manifestations" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Conductive hearing loss</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Fullness of the ear or a sensation of congestion</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Popping or crackling noises – occur as the eustachian tube attempts to open</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Otoscopy: tympanic membrane is dull</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>air bubble may be visualised</p>
</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="management">MANAGEMENT<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#management" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>No treatment is necessary unless infection occurs.</li>
<li>If significant hearing loss occurs, a myringotomy is done and a ventilatory tube inserted.</li>
<li>Low dose corticosteroids to reduce edema of the E.T. incase of barotrauma.</li>
<li>Valsalva maneuver, forcibly opens the E.T. by increasing nasopharyngeal pressure must be done cautiously as it may worsen the pain or cause tympanic membrane perforation.</li>
<li>Teach methods of opening the E.T. e.g. yawning, blowing out against closed nostrils, swallowing often.</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="nursing-diagnosis">Nursing diagnosis<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#nursing-diagnosis" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ol>
<li>
<p>Anxiety related to surgical procedure, potential hearing loss, potential taste disturbance, potential loss of facial movement.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Acute pain related to mastoid surgery</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Risk for infection related to surgery and surgical trauma to surrounding tissues</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Disturbed auditory perception related to ear disorder, surgery, packing of the ear.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Risk for trauma related to impaired balance or vertigo during the immediate post op period.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Disturbed sensory perception related to potential damage to the facial nerve.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Impaired skin integrity related to ear surgery and incision sites</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Deficient knowledge about mastoid disease, surgical procedure and post op care.</p>
</li>
</ol>
<p><strong>Interventions</strong></p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Reduce anxiety</strong> – reinforce the information discussed with the surgeon.</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>Encourage patient to discuss any anxieties or concerns.</li>
</ul>
<ol start="2">
<li>
<p><strong>Relieve pain</strong> – administer analgesics</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Prevent infection</strong> – impregnate the external auditory canal through wick/packing with antibiotic solution.</p>
</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>Prophylactic antibiotics are administered</li>
<li>Prevent water from entering the external auditory canal for 6 weeks. A cotton ball with insoluble substances e.g. petroleum jelly is placed loosely in the ear canal to prevent water from contaminating the external auditory canal.</li>
</ul>
<ol start="4">
<li><strong>Improving hearing and communication</strong> – hearing in the operated ear may be reduced for several weeks because of edema, accumulation of blood and tissue fluid in the middle ear and dressings or packing.</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Reduce environmental noise</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Face the patient when speaking</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Speak clearly and distinctly without shouting</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Provide adequate lighting if patient relies on speech reading.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Use non verbal clues e.g. facial expression, pointing, gestures.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Instruct family on effective ways to communicate with the patient.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<ol start="5">
<li><strong>Prevent injury</strong> – vertigo may occur if the semicircular canals are traumatized.</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>Give antiemetics</li>
<li>Antihistamines</li>
<li>Provide safety measures e.g. assisted ambulation to prevent injury/falls.</li>
</ul>
<ol start="6">
<li><strong>Preventing altered sensory perception</strong> – there is a potential complication of facial nerve injury.</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>Instruct the patient to report any evidence of facial nerve injury/weakness such as drooping of the mouth on the operated side, slurred speech, decreased sensation and difficulty in swallowing.</li>
</ul>
<ol start="7">
<li>
<p>Promote wound healing – instruct the patient to avoid heavy lifting, straining, exertion and nose blowing for 2 – 6 weeks post op to prevent dislodging of the prosthesis or the tympanic membrane.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Teach the patient self care – instruct about prescribed meds</p>
</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>provide information about expected effects and potential side effects of the medication.</li>
<li>Instruct on activity restriction</li>
<li>Prompt reporting of complaints e.g. infection, facial nerve weakness, taste disturbance.</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="types">Types<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#types" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Cochlear – fluctuating progressive sensorineural hearing loss associated with tinnitus and aural tenderness in the absence of vestibular symptoms.</li>
<li>Vestibular – characterized by occurrence of episodic vertigo associated with aural pressure but no cochlear symptoms</li>

<p>Management:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Low sodium diet (200mg/day)</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Limit foods high in salt</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Give plenty of fresh fruits, vegetables and whole grains.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Limit alcohol intake as it may change the volume of inner ear fluid and worsen symptoms.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Avoid aspirin as it increases tinnitus and dizziness</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Pharmacotherapy</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Antihistamines to suppress the vestibular system</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Tranquilizers e.g. diazepam in acute cases to help control vertigo.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Antiemetics e.g. promethazine to control nausea and vomiting and the vertigo because of the antihistamine effect.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Diuretics e.g. HCTZ to relieve symptoms by lowering the pressure in the endolymphatic system.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Surgical management aims at eliminating the attacks of vertigo.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Endolympathic sac decompression or shunting – equalises the pressure in the endolympatic space. A drain is inserted through a post auricular incision.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Middle and inner ear perfusion – ototoxic medication (streptomycin or gentamycin) are administered to the patient by infusion into the middle and inner ear. They destroy the vestibular function and decrease vertigo. There is risk of significant hearing loss.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Vestibular nerve sectioning – the nerve is cut thus preventing the brain from receiving input from the semicircular canals</p>
</li>
</ul>

<p>THE NOSE AND NASAL CAVITY</p>
<p>Anatomy and physiology:</p>
<ul>
<li>It is divided into two equal parts by a septum. The posterior bony part of the septum is formed by the perpendicular plate of the ethmoid bone and the vomer.</li>
<li>Anteriorly it consists of hyaline cartilage.</li>
<li>The roof is formed by the cribriform plate of the ethmoid bone, the sphenoid bone, frontal and nasal bones.</li>
<li>The floor – roof of the mouth with the hard palate anteriorly and soft palate posteriorly.</li>
<li>It is lined with highly vascular ciliated columnar epithelium which contains the mucus secreting goblet cells.</li>
</ul>
<p>Openings into the nasal cavity</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Anterior nares or nostrils – the openings from the exterior into the nasal cavity.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Posterior nares – openings from the nasal cavities into the pharynx.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Paranasal sinuses – are cavities in the bones of the face and cranium which contain air. There are tiny openings between the paranasal sinuses and the nasal cavity.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>They are lined with mucus membrane continuous with that of the nasal cavity.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The main sinuses are:-</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Maxillary sinuses – in the lateral walls</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Frontal and sphenoidal - in the roof</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Ethmoidal - in the upper part of the lateral walls.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The sinuses are used in speech resonance and they lighten the skull.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Nasolacrimal ducts extend from the lateral walls of the nose to the conjunctival sacs of the eyes. They drain tears from the eyes.</p>
</li>
</ul>

<p>EPISTAXIS (NOSEBLEED)</p>
<ul>
<li>It is hemorrhage from the nose caused by rupture of tiny distended vessels in the mucus membrane of the nose.</li>
<li>Common site is in the anterior septum, where 3 major blood vessels enter the nasal cavity i.e.</li>
<li>The anterior ethmoidal artery</li>
<li>The sphenopalatine artery</li>
<li>The internal maxillary branches</li>

<li>
<p>Systemic conditions e.g. hypertension</p>

<li>
<p>Drying of the mucus membrane</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Nasal inhalation of illicit drugs e.g. cocaine</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Trauma – from a foreign body or direct injury</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Tumors</p>

</ul>
<p><strong>Management:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Determine the site of bleeding (use nasal penlight or speculum)</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Apply direct pressure. The patient sits upright and tilts the head forward (this prevents aspiration and swallowing of blood) and pinches the nose for 5 – 10 minutes continuously.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Apply cold compress on the patient’s forehead.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>If bleeding persists, a cotton ball soaked in vasoconstrictor solution (adrenaline, epinephrine) is inserted into the nose to reduce blood flow and allow examination. The bleeding vessels are then cauterized.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>If the source is not identified – pack the nose with gauze impregnated with liquid paraffin, petroleum jelly or antibiotic ointment.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The pack may remain in place for 48 hours.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>A balloon catheter may be used to arrest posterior bleeding</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Antibiotics are prescribed due to the risk of sinusitis and toxic shock syndrome.</p>
</li>
</ul>

<li>Monitor vital signs</li>
<li>Assist in the control of bleeding</li>
<li>Relieve patient anxiety</li>
<li>Teach on ways to avoid epistaxis – forceful noseblowing, straining, high altitudes and nasal trauma e.g. nose picking.</li>
<li>Teach how to apply direct pressure.</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="nasal-fracture">NASAL FRACTURE<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#nasal-fracture" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>It is the most common facial fracture</li>
<li>It may affect the ascending process of the maxilla and the septum. the torn mucous membrane results in a nose bleed</li>
<li>It is usually as a result of direct assault.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="clinical-manifestations">Clinical manifestations:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#clinical-manifestations" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Pain</li>
<li>Bleeding from the nose internally into the pharynx</li>
<li>Swelling of the soft tissues adjacent to the nose</li>
<li>Periorbital ecchymosis(bruising)</li>
<li>Nasal obstruction</li>
<li>Deformity</li>
</ul>
<p>Assessment:</p>
<ul>
<li>Internal examination of the nose to rule out fracture of the nasal septum and a submucosal septal hematoma</li>
<li>Clear fluid draining from either nostril suggests a fracture of the cribriform plate with leaking CSF. It is differentiated from mucus through use of a glucostick. CSF contains glucose.</li>
<li>Palpate for deviations of the bone</li>
<li>X-ray to rule out extension of the fracture to the skull.</li>
</ul>
<p>Management</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Control bleeding by packing.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Cold compress to reduce or prevent edema</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Analgesics</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Ensure patent airway and rule out cervical spine fracture</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Uncomplicated fractures can be managed with antibiotics, analgesics and decongestant nasal spray.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Refer to a specialist after 3 -5 days to allow edema to subside for reduction of fracture or rhinoplasty to reshape the external appearance of the nose especially where reduction is delayed past 7-10 days and bone healing begins</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Nursing management:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Apply ice pack or cold compress. Keep the head elevated.</li>
<li>The patient breathes through the mouth because of the nasal pack thus give mouth rinses to help moisten oral mucous membranes.</li>
<li>Analgesics e.g. NSAID's</li>
<li>Advice patient to avoid sports activities for 6 weeks</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Complications</strong></p>
<p>Hematoma, infection, abscess and avascular/septic necrosis</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="nasal-polyps">NASAL POLYPS<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#nasal-polyps" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="deviated-septum">DEVIATED SEPTUM<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#deviated-septum" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="clinical-manifestations">Clinical manifestations:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#clinical-manifestations" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<li>Obstruction or nasal breathing</li>

<ul>
<li>Nasal septoplasty – surgical reconstruction and proper alignment of the deviated septum.</li>

<p>SINUSITIS</p>
<ul>
<li>Inflammation of the mucus membranes of the sinuses.</li>
</ul>
<p>ACUTE SINUSITIS</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>It is rapid onset infection in one or more of the paranasal sinuses that resolves with treatment.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Subacute sinusitis: is persistent purulent nasal discharge despite therapy with symptoms lasting less than three months.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Sinusitis occurs due to blockage/obstruction of the sinus cavities</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>It often follows a URTI or an exacerbation of allergic rhinitis. Nasal congestion caused by inflammation, edema and transudation of fluid secondary to URTI leads to obstruction of the sinus cavities. This provides an excellent media for bacterial growth.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Other conditions that block the normal flow of sinus secretions:-</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Abnormal structure of the nose</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Enlarged adenoids</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Tooth infection</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Trauma to the nose</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Tumors</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Pressure of foreign bodies</p>
</li>
</ul>

<li>
<p>Facial pain or pressure over the affected sinus</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Nasal obstruction</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Fatigue</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Purulent nasal discharge</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Fever</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Headache</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Ear pain</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Sense of fullness</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Dental pain</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Decreased sense of smell</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Early morning periorbital edema</p>
</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="diagnosis">Diagnosis<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#diagnosis" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="management">MANAGEMENT<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#management" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>The goals are to treat infection, shrink the nasal mucosa and relieve pain.</li>
<li>Antibiotics – 1st line – amoxyl, ampicillin, septin, erythromycin</li>
<li>2nd line cefuroxime, augmentin. For those with prolonged symptoms.</li>
<li>They are given for 10 – 14 days in serious bacterial infection</li>
<li>Nasal decongestants or nasal saline spray – improve patency of the openings of the sinuses and improve sinus drainage. Maximum use of 3 – 4 days.</li>
<li>Antihistamines for allergy e.g. cetrizine</li>
<li>Heated mist and saline irrigation to open blocked passages.</li>
<li>Mucolytics.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="nursing">Nursing<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#nursing" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Teach on signs of complications such as periorbital edema and severe pain.</li>
<li>Teach methods of promoting sinus drainage e.g.</li>
<li>Humidification of the air at home and use of steam inhalation</li>
<li>Warm compress to relieve pressure.</li>
<li>Avoid swimming, diving, air travel during acute infection</li>
<li>Stop smoking</li>
<li>Side effects of nasal sprays – rebound congestion</li>
<li>Completion and correct use of antibiotics</li>
<li>rehydrate</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="chronic-sinusitis">CHRONIC SINUSITIS<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#chronic-sinusitis" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>It is due to prolonged, repeated or inadequately treated acute sinus infection with symptoms lasting more than three months. Irreversible mucosal damage may occur.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="pathophysiology">Pathophysiology<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#pathophysiology" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Mechanical obstruction of the ostia of the frontal, maxillary and anterior ethmoidal sinuses.</li>
<li>Obstruction prevents adequate draining and resultant stagnation of secretions which provides an ideal media for bacterial growth.</li>
<li>Blockage that persists for more than three weeks in an adult may be due to:-</li>
<li>Infection</li>
<li>Allergy</li>
<li>Structural abnormalities</li>
</ul>
<p>Clinical manifestations:</p>
<ul>
<li>Cough because of thick discharge constantly dripping backwards into the nasopharynx.</li>
<li>Chronic hoarseness</li>
<li>Chronic headache – periorbital and facial pain</li>
<li>Mouth breathing – snoring and sore throat.</li>
<li>Decrease in smell and taste</li>
<li>Sense of fullness in the ears</li>
</ul>
<p>Complications</p>
<ul>
<li>Orbital cellulitis</li>
<li>Meningitis</li>
<li>Encephalitis</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="management">MANAGEMENT<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#management" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Antibiotics – augmentin, clarythromycin. Macrolides increase mucociliary clearance and improve sinus symptoms, decrease nasal secretions and polyp size associated with chronic sinusitis.</li>
<li>Nasal decongestants / saline sprays</li>
<li>Antihistamines</li>
</ul>
<p>Surgical management:</p>
<ul>
<li>Endoscopy – to correct structural deformities that obstruct the openings of the sinuses.</li>
<li>Excising and cauterising nasal polyps</li>
<li>Correcting deviated septum</li>
<li>Incising and draining the sinuses</li>
<li>Removing tumors</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="nursing-management">Nursing management<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#nursing-management" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Teach gentle nose blowing as forceful blowing increases symptoms</li>
<li>Increase environmental humidity</li>
<li>Take plenty of fluids</li>
<li>Apply local heat</li>
<li>Elevate the head of the bed to promote sinus drainage</li>
<li>Importance of following medication regimen</li>
<li>Signs that require follow up.</li>
</ul>
<p>THE PHARYNX</p>
<p>REVIEW OF ANAT &amp; PHYSIO</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>It is a tube 12 -14 cm long. It extends from the base of the skull to the level of the 6th cervical vertebra.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>It lies behind the nose, mouth and larynx. It is wider in its upper end.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>It is divided into three:-</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Nasopharynx – it lies behind the nose, above the level of the soft palate. On its lateral walls are the 2 openings for the auditory tubes. The posterior wall has the pharyngeal tonsils which consist of lymphoid tissue.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Oropharynx – it lies behind the mouth, from below the level of the soft palate to the level of the upper part of the body of C3. Its lateral walls blend with the soft palate to form two folds, between which there is the palatine tonsils.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>laryngopharynx – extends from the oropharynx above and continues as the esophagus below.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The pharynx is composed of three layers of tissue:-</p>
</li>
</ul>
<ol>
<li>
<p>The mucous membrane lining – the mucous varies slightly in different parts. In the nasopharynx it is ciliated columnar epth, in the oropharynx and laryngopharynx it is stratified squamous epithelium.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Fibrous tissue – it forms the intermediate layer. It is thicker in the nasopharynx where there is little muscle and becomes thinner towards the lower end where the muscle layer is thicker.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Muscle tissue – consists of several involuntary constrictor muscles that play a part in the mechanism of swallowing.</p>
</li>
</ol>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="larynx-voice-box">LARYNX / VOICE BOX<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#larynx-voice-box" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Extends from the root of the tongue and hyoid bone to the trachea.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Lies in front of the laryngopharynx.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>It is composed of several irregularly shaped cartilages attached to each other by ligaments and membranes.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The main cartilages are:-</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>1 thyroid cartilage</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>1 cricoid cartilage</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>2 arytenoid cartilages</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>1 epiglottis</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The thyroid cartilage forms the laryngeal prominence. It is incomplete posteriorly. Its upper part is lined with stratified squamous epithelium and its lower part with ciliated columnar epithelium.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The cricoid cartilage is shaped like a signet ring. It is lined with columnar epithelium.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Arytenoid cartilages – are roughly pyramid shaped hyaline cartilages. They form part of the posterior wall of the larynx. It gives attachment to the vocal cords and muscles. It is lined with ciliated columnar epithelium.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The epiglottis is a leaf shaped fibroelastic cartilage lined with stratified squamous epithelium. It closes off the larynx during swallowing, protecting the lungs from accidental inhalation of foreign objects.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The vocal cords are two folds of mucous membrane with cord like free edges which extend from the inner wall of the thyroid prominence anteriorly to the arytenoid cartilages posteriorly.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>When muscles are relaxed, vocal cords open and are said to be abducted. In this position they produce a low pitched sound.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>When muscles controlling vocal cords are contracted, vocal cords are stretched out tightly across the larynx and are said to be adducted. They produce a high pitched sound.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>When not in use the vocal cords are adducted.</p>
</li>
</ul>

</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="cancer-of-the-larynx">CANCER OF THE LARYNX<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#cancer-of-the-larynx" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>It is a malignant tumor in and around the larynx.</li>
<li>It is mostly a squamous cell carcinoma</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="predisposing-factors">Predisposing factors<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#predisposing-factors" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Carcinogens – tobacco, alcohol, asbestos, smoking, tar products, cement dust e.t.c</li>
<li>Straining the voice</li>
<li>Chronic laryngitis</li>
<li>Nutritional deficiencies</li>
<li>Age more than 60 years</li>
<li>Family predisposition</li>
<li>History of alcohol abuse</li>
</ul>
<p>Signs and symptoms</p>
<ul>
<li>Hoarse voice for more than 2 weeks – harsh, raspy, low pitched</li>
<li>Persistent cough</li>
<li>Sore throat</li>
<li>Pain and burning in the throat esp. consuming hot liquids or citrus juices.</li>
<li>Lump may be felt in the throat.</li>
</ul>
<p>Later symptoms</p>
<ul>
<li>Dysphagia</li>
<li>Dyspnea</li>
<li>Unilateral nasal obstruction or discharge</li>
<li>Persistent hoarseness</li>
<li>Foul breath</li>
</ul>
<p>Metastasis: weight loss, cervical lymphadenopathy, pain to the ear.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="categories">Categories<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#categories" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Supraglottic – involves the false vocal cords</li>
<li>Glottic – involves the true vocal cords</li>
<li>Subglottic – downward extension of the disease from the vocal cords.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="diagnosis">Diagnosis<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#diagnosis" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>History and physical exam</li>
<li>Laryngoscopy</li>
<li>Histology of tumor sample</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="management">Management<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#management" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Radiation – for early stages or together with surgery &amp; chemo</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Surgery – laryngectomy – surgical removal of part or all of the larynx and surrounding structures.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Partial laryngectomy – remove part of the larynx, one vocal cord and tumor. Airway is left intact</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Supraglottic laryngectomy – remove the hyoid bone, glottis and false vocal cords. Tracheostomy tube is left in place until glottic airway is established. Enteral feeds are given until healing occurs. Aspiration is a potential complication. Voice is preserved though it may change.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Hemilaryngectomy – remove one true cord, one false cord and the tumor. Pt has NGT and tracheostomy for several days post op, there is high risk of aspiration. Voice changes but airway and swallowing remain intact.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Total laryngectomy – remove all laryngeal structures – hyoid bone, epiglottis, cricoid cartilage and 2 or 3 rings of the trachea. Voice changes and a permanent tracheostomy is inserted.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Speech therapy – pre op counsel the patient on loss of voice and alternative methods of speech/communication e.g writing, lip reading, esophageal speech, artificial larynx etc.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="nursing-care-of-patient-undergoing-laryngectomy">NURSING CARE OF PATIENT UNDERGOING LARYNGECTOMY<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#nursing-care-of-patient-undergoing-laryngectomy" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="assessment">ASSESSMENT<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#assessment" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Health history- hoarseness, sorethroat, dypnea, dysphagia, pain or burning in the throat.</li>
<li>Physical examination- thorough head and neck examination with emphasis on patient airway. Palpate neck for swelling or adenopathy.</li>
<li>General state- nutritional status and BMI</li>
<li>Pre operative evaluation by a speech therapist</li>
<li>Family Social history- history of cancer in the family, history of alcohol intake.</li>
<li>Assess the psychological readiness of the patient and the family.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="nursing-diagnoses">NURSING DIAGNOSES<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#nursing-diagnoses" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Deficient knowledge about surgical procedure and post-op care</li>
<li>Anxiety and depression related to the diagnosis of cancer and impending surgery.</li>
<li>Ineffective airway clearance related to excess mucus production secondary to surgical alteration of the airway.</li>
<li>Impaired verbal communication related to anatomical deficit secondary to removal of the larynx and to edema.</li>
<li>Imbalanced nutrition less than body requirements related to inability to ingest food secondary to swallowing difficulties.</li>
<li>Disturbed body image and low self esteem secondary to major neck surgery, change in appearance and altered structure and function</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="nursing-diagnosis-continued">Nursing diagnosis continued<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#nursing-diagnosis-continued" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Self care deficit related to pain, weakness, fatigue, musculoskeletal impairment secondary to surgical procedure.</li>
<li>Pain</li>
<li>Potential for complications- respiratory distress (hypoxia, airway obstruction, tracheal edema), hemorrhage, infection, aspiration</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="nursing-interventions">NURSING INTERVENTIONS<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#nursing-interventions" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Teach the patient pre operatively.</li>
<li>Clarify misconceptions of disease, nature of surgical procedure and its effect on speech.</li>
<li>For complete laryngectomy make the patient understand that the natural voice will be lost but special training can provide a means of communication.</li>
<li>Review equipment and treatments for post op care with the patient and family. Teach important coughing and deep breathing exercises and assist the patient to perform a return demonstration.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="interventions-continued">INTERVENTIONS continued<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#interventions-continued" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Reduce anxiety and depression</li>
<li>Provide the patient and the family with opportunities to ask questions and verbalise feelings.</li>
<li>Arrange for someone post laryngectomy to visit the patient.</li>
<li>Active listening to the patient and providing an environment that promotes open communication and allows patient to verbalise feelings.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="nursing-interventions">Nursing interventions<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#nursing-interventions" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Maintain a patent airway</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Position the patient in fowlers or semi-fowlers position after recovery from anaesthesia. It reduces surgical edema and promotes lung expansion.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Observe patient for restlessness, dyspnea...</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Assess/auscultate lung sounds</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Use with caution drugs that depress respiration e.g. opiates. However ensure adequate pain relief as pain may cause shallow breaths and ineffective cough.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Encourage to turn, cough and take deep breaths. Suction to remove excess secretions.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Early ambulation to prevent atelectasis, pneumonia and DVT</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Perform laryngectomy tube care (similar to tracheostomy care). assignment: review tracheostomy care.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Clean stoma daily with saline solution or prescribed solution. Frequently patient coughs up large amounts of mucus through this opening. To prevent excess mucus production ensure the air is humidified.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Laryngectomy tube may be removed after stoma is well healed, within 3 – 6 weeks.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Teach how to clean and change tube.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Promoting alternative communication methods</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Establish means of communication pre op</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>E.g place a call bell within easy reach of the patient</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Writing material, insert IV in the opposite hand</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Hand signals</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Give adequate time as this method of communication takes time and can be frustrating to the patient.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Promote adequate nutrition and hydration</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Post op the patient may be NPO for several days. Alternative feeding and hydration methods are used.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>IV fluids, enteral feeding through NGT or gastrotomy tube, parenteral nutrition.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>When starting oral feeds, start with thick liquids as they are easy to swallow. Keep suction near for initial feeding. Introduce solid foods as tolerated.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Observe for and report difficulty in swallowing. Appetite may be altered for sometime due to loss of sense of smell as taste and smell are closely related.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Promoting positive body image and self esteem.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Due to disfiguring surgery and loss/altered communication pattern.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Encourage patient to express feelings and counsel or refer to a support group.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Promoting self care management</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Begin participation in care ASAP</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Encourage participation and provide positive reinforcement</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Monitoring and managing potential complications</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Respiratory distress and hypoxia – monitor for signs and symptoms e.g. restlessness, agitation, tachypnea, confusion, use of accessory respiratory muscles.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Hypoxia causes:- restlessness, initial increase in BP followed by hypotension and somnolence. Cyanosis is a late sign.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Immediately rule out obstruction by having the patient cough or breath in deeply.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Reposition to ensure an open airway.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Administer oxygen as prescribed.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Prepare always for possible intubation and mechanical ventilation.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Hemorrhage – notify the surgeon incase of active bleeding at the surgical site, drains or trachea.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Monitor vitals for changes – increase in pulse, decrease in BP, rapid deep respirations, cold clammy skin may indicate active bleeding.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Give IV fluid and blood components</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Manage shock</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Infection – monitor for signs of infection e.g. increase in temperature and pulse, change in type of wound drainage, increased areas of redness or tenderness at surgical site, purulent drainage, odor, increased wound drainage.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Administer antibiotics and culture drainage</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Wound breakdown – caused by infection, poor wound healing, radiation therapy or tumor growth. It can create an emergency as the carotid artery is close to the stoma site and may be eroded and rupture.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>observe stoma site for wound breakdown, hematoma, bleeding and report to surgeon.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Aspiration – risk due to depressed cough, sedating effects of anaesthesia and analgesics, alteration in the airway, impaired swallowing and the administration of tube feedings.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Assess for nausea &amp; administer antiemetics as prescribed.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Keep suction nearby</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>During tube feedings position the head of the bed at 30 degrees or higher and remain so for 30 -45 minutes after feeding.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Check tube position before each feed.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Teaching</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Tracheostomy and stoma care. Importance of humidification at home, avoid air conditioned air as it may be too cold or too dry.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Hygiene and safety measure in the shower to prevent water entering the stoma. No more swimming.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Signs of infection</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Handwashing before and after tracheostomy care. Proper disposal of mucus and soiled dressing.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Perform oral care regularly to avoid halitosis and infection.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Teach signs of complications.</p>
</li>
</ul></div></div></div></div></div></div></div><div data-testid="stAppIframeResizerAnchor" data-iframe-height="true" class="st-emotion-cache-1dumvfu eht7o1d9"></div></section></div></div></div><div data-testid="portal" id="portal" class="st-emotion-cache-1q6lfs0 e19n7mk11"></div></div><div class=""></div></div>
