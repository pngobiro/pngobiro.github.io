üêö Conditions of the External Ear

1. Impacted Cerumen

Wax may form a solid plug or may be of a large amount and blocks the external auditory canal.

Symptoms

  • Sense of blockage
  • Conductive hearing impairment
  • Otalgia (Ear pain)

Management

  • Irrigation: Syringing with water warmed to body temperature. Jet aimed above and behind the wax.
  • Manual Removal: Using cerumen curette or suction. Warm glycerine or mineral oil may be instilled 30 mins prior to soften wax.

2. Foreign Body

üö´

Critical Precaution

If the foreign body is a vegetable/food substance (e.g., bean, maize), DO NOT IRRIGATE. Water causes it to swell, leading to pain and impaction. Do not attempt to remove without visualization as this pushes it further in.

Management:

  • Insects: Shine a light (may draw insect out) or instill mineral oil/glycerine to suffocate/float it out.
  • Removal: Irrigation (for non-swelling objects), suction, or instrumentation.

3. Otitis Externa (Swimmer's Ear)

Inflammation or infection of the epithelium of the auricle and external auditory canal.

‚ñ∂ Causes & Manifestations

Causes: Water in ear, trauma to skin, systemic conditions (vitamin deficiency, endocrine disorders).

Organisms: Pseudomonas, Staphylococcus, Aspergillus (fungal).

Clinical Signs:

  • Aural tenderness (distinctive feature vs otitis media).
  • Pain, discharge, fever, cellulitis, lymphadenopathy, pruritus.
  • Otoscopy: Erythematous/edematous canal. Yellow/green foul discharge. Fungal infections show black hair-like spores.

Management:

  • Medications: Analgesics, Antibiotics (CAF, Neomycin), Corticosteroids (for inflammation), Antifungals (Nystatin).
  • Systemic: Antibiotics if fever/cellulitis present.
  • Nursing: Warm moist compress. Advise against cotton-tipped applicators. Use ear plugs when swimming.

üî® Conditions of the Middle Ear

1. Tympanic Membrane Perforation

Caused by infection or trauma (skull fracture, explosion, blow to ear, foreign objects).

‚ö†Ô∏è

Head Injury Warning

Observe for CSF leakage (otorrhea or rhinorrhea) - clear, watery drainage. Differentiate from mucus using glucose test (CSF contains glucose).

Management:

  • Most heal spontaneously within weeks. Protect ear from water.
  • Tympanoplasty: Surgical repair using tissue graft (temporalis fascia) if spontaneous healing fails.

2. Acute Otitis Media (AOM)

Acute infection lasting less than 6 weeks. Pathogens (Strep. pneumoniae, H. influenzae) reach middle ear via Eustachian tube dysfunction.

Manifestations Otalgia (relieved by perforation), drainage, fever, conductive hearing loss.
Otoscopy Normal canal. Tympanic membrane is erythematous and bulging.
Management
  • Antibiotics (Amoxicillin, Cotrimoxazole, Erythromycin) for 10 days.
  • Analgesics, Antihistamines.
  • Myringotomy: Incision of TM to relieve pressure/drain fluid.
  • Ventilating Tubes: For recurrent AOM.

3. Serous Otitis Media (Glue Ear)

Fluid in middle ear without active infection. Due to negative pressure from Eustachian tube obstruction (radiation, barotrauma, allergy, carcinoma).

  • Symptoms: Fullness/congestion, popping/crackling noises, conductive loss.
  • Otoscopy: Dull TM, air bubbles may be visible.
  • Management: Treat underlying cause. Myringotomy with tube if hearing loss significant. Valsalva maneuver (cautiously).

4. Chronic Otitis Media

Repeated AOM episodes causing irreversible tissue damage and persistent perforation.

Cholesteatoma

An ingrowth of skin into the middle ear forming a sac filled with degenerated skin/sebum. It is benign but can destroy structures (ossicles, mastoid bone). Appears as a white mass behind the TM.

Clinical Features: Foul-smelling otorrhea, hearing loss, pain (mastoiditis), dizziness, facial palsy.

Management:

  • Medical: Suctioning, wicking, antibiotic drops/powder.
  • Surgical:
    • Tympanoplasty: Reconstruct TM.
    • Ossiculoplasty: Reconstruct ossicles using prosthesis.
    • Mastoidectomy: Remove cholesteatoma and infected mastoid air cells.

ü¶¥ Otosclerosis & Mastoiditis

Otosclerosis

Abnormal spongy bone formation around the stapes, fixing it in place and preventing vibration. Hereditary, more common in women, worsened by pregnancy.

  • Signs: Progressive conductive loss, tinnitus. Rinne test: Bone conduction > Air conduction.
  • Medical: Sodium fluoride, Calcium, Vitamin D (retard bone resorption). Hearing aids.
  • Surgical: Stapedectomy (removal of stapes and replacement with prosthesis).

Mastoiditis

Inflammation of the mastoid process secondary to middle ear infection.

  • Signs: Tenderness/swelling over mastoid, protrusion of pinna, fever, pain.
  • Management: IV antibiotics, Mastoidectomy.
  • Complications: Deafness, Brain abscess, Meningitis.

üë©‚Äç‚öïÔ∏è Nursing Care: Mastoid Surgery

Nursing Diagnoses

  • Anxiety
  • Acute Pain
  • Risk for Infection
  • Risk for Trauma (Vertigo/Balance)
  • Disturbed Sensory Perception (Facial nerve injury)

Key Interventions

  1. 1

    Prevent Infection: Keep ear dry. Avoid water entering canal for 6 weeks (use cotton ball with petroleum jelly). Administer prophylactic antibiotics.

  2. 2

    Prevent Injury (Vertigo): Assist with ambulation. Administer antiemetics and antihistamines. Vertigo may occur if semicircular canals are traumatized.

  3. 3

    Facial Nerve Monitoring: Watch for drooping mouth, slurred speech, difficulty swallowing, or decreased sensation. Report immediately.

  4. 4

    Wound Healing: Avoid straining, heavy lifting, and nose blowing for 2-6 weeks to prevent dislodging prosthesis/graft. Sneeze with mouth open.

  5. 5

    Communication: Hearing may be reduced initially due to packing/edema. Speak clearly on the unoperated side.
