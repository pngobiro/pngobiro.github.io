üß¨ Anatomy and Physiology

The nose is divided into two equal parts by a septum. The posterior bony part of the septum is formed by the perpendicular plate of the ethmoid bone and the vomer. Anteriorly it consists of hyaline cartilage.

  • Roof: Formed by the cribriform plate of the ethmoid bone, the sphenoid bone, frontal and nasal bones.
  • Floor: Roof of the mouth with the hard palate anteriorly and soft palate posteriorly.
  • Lining: Highly vascular ciliated columnar epithelium which contains the mucus secreting goblet cells.

Openings into the Nasal Cavity

  • Anterior nares (nostrils): Openings from the exterior into the nasal cavity.
  • Posterior nares: Openings from the nasal cavities into the pharynx.
  • Paranasal sinuses: Cavities in the bones of the face and cranium which contain air. They communicate with the nasal cavity and are lined with continuous mucous membrane.
  • Nasolacrimal ducts: Extend from the lateral walls of the nose to the conjunctival sacs of the eyes. They drain tears from the eyes.
Sinus Location
Maxillary In the lateral walls
Frontal In the roof
Sphenoidal In the roof
Ethmoidal In the upper part of the lateral walls

Note: The sinuses are used in speech resonance and they lighten the skull.

  • Inspect the external nose for lesions, asymmetry or inflammation.
  • Examine internal structures: Patient tilts head back, tip of nose pushed back. Note mucosal colour, swelling, exudate, or bleeding.
  • Check septum for deviation, perforation, or bleeding.
  • Inspect the inferior and middle turbinates.
  • Palpate the frontal and maxillary sinuses for tenderness.
  • Transilluminate to check for fluid or pus ‚Äì light does not go through if present. (Passing a strong light through a bony area to inspect sinuses).

ü§ß Rhinitis

A group of disorders characterised by inflammation and irritation of the mucus membranes of the nose. It can be acute, chronic, or allergic.

Causes

  • Change in temperature, odors, foods
  • Infection (common cold)
  • Systemic diseases
  • Drugs (e.g., cocaine)
  • Medications (e.g., nasal decongestants)
  • Foreign bodies

Clinical Manifestations

  • Rhinorrhea: Excessive nasal drainage/runny nose
  • Nasal congestion
  • Sneezing
  • Pruritus of nose, roof of mouth, throat, eyes, ears
  • Headache (especially if sinusitis is present)

Management

  • Identify the cause.
  • Antihistamines for sneezing, pruritus, rhinorrhea (e.g., Chlorpheniramine, Cetirizine).
  • Oral decongestants for nasal congestion.
  • Saline nasal spray: Acts as a decongestant and liquefies mucus.
  • Nurse in a warm, well-ventilated environment.
  • Give plenty of hot fluids.

ü©∏ Epistaxis (Nosebleed)

Hemorrhage from the nose caused by rupture of tiny distended vessels in the mucus membrane. The common site is the anterior septum, where 3 major blood vessels enter the nasal cavity (Anterior ethmoidal artery, Sphenopalatine artery, Internal maxillary branches).

Risk Factors:

Local infections (rhinitis), systemic conditions (hypertension), drying of mucus membrane, inhalation of illicit drugs (cocaine), trauma (nose picking/foreign body), tumors, thrombocytopenia, Aspirin use.

Critical Intervention

Direct Pressure: The patient sits upright and tilts the head forward (to prevent aspiration/swallowing blood) and pinches the nose for 5‚Äì10 minutes continuously.

  • Determine bleeding site (penlight/speculum).
  • Apply cold compress to forehead.
  • Vasoconstrictors: If bleeding persists, insert cotton ball soaked in Adrenaline/Epinephrine.
  • Cauterization: Of bleeding vessels.
  • Packing: Gauze impregnated with liquid paraffin/petroleum jelly/antibiotic ointment (up to 48 hours).
  • Balloon Catheter: For posterior bleeding.
  • Antibiotics: Prescribed due to risk of sinusitis and toxic shock syndrome.

ü§ï Nasal Fracture

The most common facial fracture, usually resulting from direct assault. May affect the ascending process of the maxilla and septum.

Clinical Manifestations

  • Pain & Deformity
  • Bleeding (internal/external)
  • Swelling of soft tissues
  • Periorbital ecchymosis (bruising)
  • Nasal obstruction

Assessment

  • Internal exam to rule out septal hematoma.
  • CSF Check: Clear fluid draining suggests cribriform plate fracture. Differentiate from mucus using glucostick (CSF contains glucose).
  • X-ray.

Management

  • Control bleeding (packing) and apply cold compress.
  • Ensure patent airway; rule out cervical spine fracture.
  • Reduction: Refer to specialist after 3-5 days (to allow edema to subside). Rhinoplasty may be needed.
  • Nursing: Head elevated, mouth rinses (due to mouth breathing from packing), avoid sports for 6 weeks.

Complications: Hematoma, infection, abscess, avascular/septic necrosis.

ü¶¥ Structural Disorders

Nasal Polyps

Benign mucus membrane masses that form slowly in response to repeated inflammation. Appear as bluish-grayish projections.

  • Manifestations: Obstruction, discharge, speech distortion, visible polyp.
  • Management: Surgical removal (endoscopy), Corticosteroids to slow growth.

Deviated Septum

Septum is deflected from the normal straight position or disproportionate size. Caused by trauma or congenital disproportion.

  • Manifestations: Obstruction, dry mucosa, bleeding, sinusitis (if blocking sinus openings).
  • Management: Nasal Septoplasty (surgical reconstruction/alignment).
07

ü¶† Sinusitis

Inflammation of the mucus membranes of the sinuses due to blockage/obstruction. Often follows a URTI.

‚ñ∂ Acute vs. Chronic
  • Acute: Rapid onset, resolves with treatment.
  • Subacute: Persistent purulent discharge lasting less than 3 months.
  • Chronic: Symptoms lasting > 3 months. Irreversible damage may occur. Due to mechanical obstruction (polyps, deviation) or repeated infection.

Clinical Manifestations

  • Facial pain/pressure over affected sinus.
  • Purulent nasal discharge.
  • Headache, Fever, Fatigue.
  • Ear pain / fullness.
  • Dental pain.
  • Decreased smell/taste.
  • Early morning periorbital edema.
  • Chronic: Cough (post-nasal drip), chronic hoarseness, snoring.

Management

Medical:

  • Antibiotics: 1st line (Amoxicillin, Ampicillin, Erythromycin). 2nd line (Cefuroxime, Augmentin). 10-14 days.
  • Decongestants/Saline Spray: Max 3-4 days usage (risk of rebound congestion).
  • Antihistamines, Mucolytics.
  • Heated mist/steam inhalation.

Surgical: Endoscopy (correct deformities), Polypectomy, Incision and drainage.

üí°

Nursing Interventions

  • Teach gentle nose blowing.
  • Increase environmental humidity (steam).
  • Warm compress for pain relief.
  • Elevate head of bed to promote drainage.
  • Avoid swimming/air travel during acute infection.
