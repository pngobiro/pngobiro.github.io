üìã History Taking

An otologic history is critical for diagnosing ear disorders. It involves gathering comprehensive subjective data from the patient.

Components of Otologic History:

  • Demographic data: Age, gender, occupation.
  • Chief Complaint: e.g., hearing loss, pain (otalgia), tinnitus, vertigo, dizziness, drainage (otorrhea), loss of balance. Note frequency, duration, onset, and precipitating factors.
  • Past Medical History: Childhood illnesses (otitis media, eardrum perforations, mumps, measles, meningitis), tonsillectomy, previous ear surgeries.
  • Medications: Screen for ototoxic drugs (aminoglycosides, quinine, chemotherapeutic agents). Note that Aspirin causes tinnitus.
  • Family History: History of hearing loss (genetic predisposition).
  • Social History: Occupation, leisure activities, and hobbies (exposure to noise).

üëÄ Physical Examination

1. Inspection and Palpation

  • Note size, configuration, inflammation, lumps, lesions, or cysts on the external ear.
  • Palpate and manipulate the pinna to detect tenderness, nodules, or tophi (small, hard nodules in the helix that are deposits of uric acid crystals characteristic of gout).

2. Direct Observation

üí°

Technique

Tip the head slightly to the opposite side while pulling the pinnae up, back, and out.

Use a penlight to inspect the canal for abnormality ‚Äì excess wax, redness, scaliness, swelling, drainage, and cysts.

üëÇ Assessment of the Auditory System

1. Otoscopy

Performed using an otoscope, which has a light source and magnifying lens.

  • Hold the otoscope in the right hand.
  • Grasp the auricle with the opposite hand and gently pull outwards to straighten the canal.
  • Insert the speculum and observe.
‚úÖ

Normal Finding

A normal tympanic membrane appears pearly grey and translucent. It is positioned obliquely at the base of the canal.

2. Whisper Test

Used to evaluate gross auditory acuity.

  • The examiner covers the untested ear.
  • Whisper softly from a distance of 1-2 feet from the unoccluded ear, out of the patient's sight.
  • Result: Patients with normal hearing can repeat what is whispered.

3. Tuning Fork Tests

These tests distinguish between conductive and sensorineural hearing loss.

Feature Weber Test Rinne Test
Principle Uses bone conduction to test lateralization of sound. Compares Air Conduction (AC) vs. Bone Conduction (BC).
Technique Tuning fork placed on vertex or forehead. Fork placed on mastoid bone (BC), then 2 inches in front of ear (AC).
Normal Finding Sound heard equally in both ears (centered). AC > BC (Air conduction audible longer than bone).
Conductive Loss Sound heard better in the affected ear. BC > AC or BC = AC.
Sensorineural Loss Sound heard better in the normal/better ear. AC > BC (but duration is reduced).

4. Audiometry

It is the most important diagnostic tool. The patient wears earphones and signals when a tone is heard.

‚ñ∂ Types of Audiometry
  • Pure Tone Audiometry: The sound stimulus consists of a pure or musical tone.
  • Speech Audiometry: Tests ability to hear and discriminate sounds and words. The louder the tone required for perception, the greater the hearing loss.
üìä

Characteristics of Sound

  1. 1

    Frequency: Number of sound waves per second (Hertz/Hz). Normal perception: 20‚Äì20,000 Hz. Speech range: 500‚Äì2000 Hz.

  2. 2

    Pitch: Describes frequency (Low = 100 Hz, High > 10,000 Hz).

  3. 3

    Intensity: Loudness/pressure measured in Decibels (dB).
    Critical level: ~30 dB.
    Sounds > 80 dB can damage the inner ear.

‚öñÔ∏è Tests for Vestibular Problems

These tests assess the balance functions of the inner ear.

1. Romberg Test

  • Assesses the inner ear for balance.
  • Procedure: Client stands with feet together, arms by the side, and eyes closed.
  • Positive Romberg: Loss of balance (swaying/falling), indicating vestibular problems.

2. Caloric Test

Determines if an alteration exists in the vestibular origin of the vestibulocochlear nerve.

üö´

Contraindications

Do not perform if there is a perforated ear drum or infection of the middle ear.

Procedure: With client seated or supine, water (warm/cold) is instilled into the external ear canal. Test affected side first.

Normal Response: Nystagmus (rapid eye movement), feeling of falling, vertigo, nausea, vomiting.

Abnormal Response: Decrease or absence of these responses within 3 minutes.

NB: Anticipate vomiting and provide an emesis basin.

3. Electronystagmography

  • Electrodes are placed near the client's eyes to assess oculomotor and vestibular systems.
  • A caloric test is performed while eye movements are recorded on a graph.
