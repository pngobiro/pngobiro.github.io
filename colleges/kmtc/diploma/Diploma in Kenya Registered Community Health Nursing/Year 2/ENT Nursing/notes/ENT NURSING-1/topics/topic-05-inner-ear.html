üöó Motion Sickness

This is a disturbance of equilibrium caused by constant motion (e.g., in a vehicle, merry-go-round, or a ship).

Clinical Manifestations

  • Sweating
  • Pallor
  • Nausea
  • Vomiting

Management

  • Antihistamines: e.g., Dimenhydrinate. Relieves nausea and vomiting by blocking conduction of the vestibular pathway of the inner ear.
  • Anticholinergics: e.g., Scopolamine. Antagonises the histamine response. Side effects: dry mouth, drowsiness.
02

üåä Meniere's Disease

Abnormal inner ear fluid balance caused by malabsorption in the endolymphatic sac or in the endolymphatic duct.

Clinical Manifestations

  • Fluctuating, progressive sensorineural hearing loss
  • Tinnitus or roaring sound in the ear
  • Feeling of pressure or fullness in the ear
  • Episodic, incapacitating vertigo accompanied by nausea and vomiting

Types

  • Cochlear: Fluctuating progressive sensorineural hearing loss associated with tinnitus and aural tenderness in the absence of vestibular symptoms.
  • Vestibular: Characterized by occurrence of episodic vertigo associated with aural pressure but no cochlear symptoms.

Pharmacotherapy

  • Antihistamines: Suppress the vestibular system.
  • Tranquilizers: e.g., Diazepam in acute cases to help control vertigo.
  • Antiemetics: e.g., Promethazine to control nausea/vomiting and vertigo (antihistamine effect).
  • Diuretics: e.g., HCTZ to relieve symptoms by lowering pressure in the endolymphatic system.

Surgical Management

Aims at eliminating the attacks of vertigo.

  • Endolymphatic Sac Decompression/Shunting: Equalizes pressure in the endolymphatic space. A drain is inserted through a post-auricular incision.
  • Middle and Inner Ear Perfusion: Ototoxic medication (streptomycin or gentamicin) infused into middle/inner ear. Destroys vestibular function and decreases vertigo. Risk of significant hearing loss.
  • Vestibular Nerve Sectioning: Nerve is cut, preventing brain from receiving input from semicircular canals.

üî• Labyrinthitis

Inflammation of the inner ear due to bacteria or virus.

ü¶†

Etiology

Viral: Mumps, Rubella, Influenza.

Bacterial: Complication of otitis media. Bacteria enter middle ear through oval or round window.

Clinical Manifestations

  • Sudden onset vertigo with nausea and vomiting.
  • Sensorineural hearing loss.
  • Tinnitus.

Management

  • Bacterial: Intravenous antibiotics.
  • Fluid replacement therapy.
  • Antihistamines and Antiemetics.
  • Viral: Treatment is based on symptoms.
04

üíä Ototoxicity

Due to adverse effects of medication on the cochlea, vestibular apparatus, or Cranial Nerve VIII.

‚ö†Ô∏è

Key Facts

  • Most cause irreversible hearing loss (except aspirin and quinine).
  • High doses of Aspirin cause bilateral tinnitus.
  • IV meds, especially Aminoglycosides, are the most common cause and destroy hair cells of the Organ of Corti.

Prevention

  1. Counsel patients on side effects of these meds.
  2. Use with caution in high-risk patients (children, elderly, pregnant, kidney/liver problems, existing hearing disorders).
  3. Monitor hearing on those receiving long-term IV antibiotics.
05

üß† Acoustic Neuroma

Slow-growing benign tumors of Cranial Nerve VIII, usually arising from the Schwann cells of the vestibular portion of the nerve. Occurs where the nerve enters the internal auditory canal or temporal bone from the brain.

Manifestations & Diagnosis

Clinical Manifestations
  • Unilateral tinnitus
  • Sensorineural hearing loss
  • Mild intermittent vertigo
Diagnosis
  • History and Physical exam
  • Vestibular tests
  • Audiometry
  • MRI or CT scan with contrast dye

Management

  • Surgical removal of tumor.
  • Note: These tumors don't respond well to radiation or chemotherapy.

Surgical Complications

Facial nerve paralysis, Cerebrospinal fluid leakage, Meningitis, Cerebral edema.
