Vaccines

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Define a vaccine.
  • Describe live attenuated vaccines, inactivated vaccines, and toxoid vaccines.
  • Explain cellular fraction vaccines and combination vaccines.

📚 Introduction to Vaccines

A vaccine is a preparation of an antigen for preventive inoculation, which, when administered, stimulates specific antibody formation in the body. Vaccines are classified as follows:

📚 Live Attenuated Vaccines

These are prepared from live organisms that are treated to lose their capacity to induce full-blown disease but retain their immunogenicity. They are more potent compared to killed vaccines because:

  • Live organisms multiply in the host, and the resulting antigenic dose is more than what is injected.
  • Live vaccines have all the major and minor antigenic components.
  • Live organisms engage certain tissues of the body, for example, the intestinal mucosa during the administration of oral polio vaccine.

Administration of live vaccines is carried out in single doses except for the polio vaccine.

Examples of live vaccines include:

  • BCG
  • OPV
  • Measles
  • Mumps
  • Influenza
  • Rubella
  • Rotavirus vaccine

💡 Pro Tip

Live attenuated vaccines generally provide stronger and longer-lasting immunity compared to other types of vaccines, but they may not be suitable for everyone, such as those with compromised immune systems.

📚 Inactivated/Killed Vaccines

The organism is killed by heat or chemicals. They are usually less effective than live vaccines. Inactivated vaccines are used in double or triple doses to increase antigenic potency.

Usually the routes of administration are intramuscular.

Examples of inactivated vaccines include:

  • Pertussis
  • HBV

📚 Toxoids

Certain organisms produce exotoxins, for example, diphtheria and tetanus bacilli. The toxins produced by these organisms are detoxicated and used in the preparation of vaccines. The antibodies produced neutralize the toxic moiety produced during infection rather than acting upon the organism. These toxoids are safe and effective.

Examples of toxoid vaccines include:

  • Diphtheria
  • Tetanus

📚 Cellular Fractions

Some vaccines are prepared from extracted cellular fractions, for example, the meningococcal vaccine from the polysaccharide part of the cell wall and the pneumococcal vaccine from polysaccharide contained in the capsule of the organism.

📚 Combinations

If more than one kind of immunizing agent is included in the vaccine, it is called a mixed or combined vaccine. The aim of a combined vaccine is to simplify administration, reduce cost, and minimize the number of contacts with the patients.

Examples of combined vaccines include:

  • DPT (Diphtheria, Pertussis, Tetanus)
  • MMR (Measles, Mumps, Rubella)
  • Pentavalent (DPT, HBV, Haemophilus Influenzae)

✍️ Practice Exercise

Compare and contrast live attenuated vaccines and inactivated vaccines. Discuss their advantages and disadvantages.

View Solution

Live Attenuated Vaccines:

  • Advantages: Potent immune response, usually require fewer doses, provide long-lasting immunity.
  • Disadvantages: Can't be given to immunocompromised individuals, mild symptoms may appear, risk of reversion to a virulent form, require specific storage conditions.

Inactivated Vaccines:

  • Advantages: Safer for immunocompromised patients, more stable, easier to store.
  • Disadvantages: Less potent immune response, require multiple doses, shorter duration of immunity.

📚 Key Takeaways

  • Vaccines stimulate the body to produce antibodies against specific pathogens.
  • Live attenuated vaccines are made from weakened pathogens and are highly effective.
  • Inactivated vaccines are made from killed pathogens and are safer but less potent.
  • Toxoids are made from detoxified toxins and are effective against bacterial toxins.
  • Combination vaccines can provide protection against multiple diseases at once.
