Immunizable Diseases and Reactions

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Describe common reactions to immunizations.
  • Identify and explain the causes and symptoms of key immunizable diseases, including TB, Polio, Diphtheria, Pertussis, Tetanus and Measles.
  • Discuss prevention and management strategies for each disease.

📚 Reactions to Immunizations

Reactions after immunizations are usually mild and of short duration. They include:

  • Mild fever
  • Local pain and swelling at the site of injection
  • Mild rashes after a week of measles vaccinations
  • A lump or a papule may appear in the 3rd or 4th week after BCG vaccination. It is usually not painful, and it develops into a small scar.

Adverse reactions include convulsions.

Complications may develop due to the following reasons:

  • Abscess formation due to the use of unsterilized or inadequately sterilized syringes and needles.
  • Severe pain due to the use of blunt needles.
  • Sudden onset of diarrhea, vomiting, and fever within a few hours of a measles vaccine due to contamination of the vial.
  • Death due to contaminated vials.

Measures to minimize risk include:

  • Use sterile needles and syringes for both vaccinating and diluting vaccines.
  • Do not use opened vials in subsequent sessions.
  • Measles vaccines should be discarded at the end of the session.
  • Parents should be informed of the expected side effects.

📚 Immunizable Diseases Overview

The eight childhood immunizable diseases targeted by KEPI are:

  • Tuberculosis (TB)
  • Poliomyelitis (Polio)
  • Diphtheria
  • Pertussis (Whooping cough)
  • Tetanus
  • Measles
  • Hepatitis
  • Influenza
  • Pneumonia

📚 Tuberculosis (TB)

Tuberculosis, commonly abbreviated as TB, is a chronic infection that may affect any parts of the body, mainly the lungs, and is caused by bacteria. It is a common cause of illness and death in children if not properly treated. It spreads easily from one person to another through the infected persons’ droplets (from coughing) that contain Mycobacterium Tuberculosis. Due to HIV/AIDS, TB incidence is on the rise.

Children get TB from adults who are sick and who cough out TB bacilli into the air. When a child breathes in the bacilli, he/she slowly develops the disease. Although TB bacilli are spread in droplets by adults, who cough them in their sputum, children are very rarely infectious.

Signs and symptoms:

  • Chronic cough (cough which lasts for more than 30 days).
  • Enlarged lymph nodes in the neck.
  • Fever which comes and goes.
  • Loss of weight (wasting), often with loss of appetite.
  • When a child with Pneumonia or another type of acute cough does not get better after two weeks of treatment. This is especially true when there is fever and weight loss.

Prevention:

  • Immunizing all children at birth or first contact with the BCG vaccine.
  • Encouraging the community to avoid overcrowding and to have houses properly ventilated.
  • Encouraging good nutrition in the community.
  • Identifying and referring all suspected TB patients to the hospital for early diagnosis and treatment.
  • Early diagnosis and treatment of all TB patients.
  • Investigation of family members and close contacts of a case.

📚 Poliomyelitis (Polio)

Poliomyelitis, commonly referred to as polio, is a highly infectious disease that is caused by a virus that affects the motor neurons of the spinal cord and sometimes the brain, resulting in paralysis. It commonly affects the muscles of the limbs (arms and legs). There are three related types of polio virus. These are Types 1, 2, and 3. All three types cause paralysis, but the most frequent cause of epidemic polio is polio Type 1. Almost all the under-five children in Kenya who have not been immunized have a risk of getting polio infection.

Mode of transmission: Polio Virus is mainly spread through faeces and by droplet infection. The latter is a rare route of transmission. When an infected child passes the virus in stools, it spreads from child to child through contaminated water, food, utensils, and hands. After being swallowed, the virus multiplies in the throat and intestines and may spread to the child’s nerves through the blood stream. However, polio virus does not affect the sensory nerves.

Signs and symptoms:

  • Weakness and paralysis of one or more limbs.
  • There is no sensory loss. The sense of pain and touch remain normal.
  • The paralysis only affects the muscles (Flaccid paralysis).
  • Fever and general malaise
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Headache
  • Abdominal pain

The incubation period for polio is 10 - 21 days.

Prevention:

  • Poliomyelitis is mainly prevented by immunization using Oral Polio Vaccine. OPV effectively prevents polio.
  • Advise the community to improve general hygiene and sanitation in their homes as a preventive measure against poliomyelitis.

📚 Diphtheria

Diphtheria is an acute infectious disease of the throat and tonsils, caused by corynebacterium diphtheriae bacteria. These bacteria produce typical lesions on the mucous membranes of the upper respiratory tract. Toxins released from the lesions cause severe general symptoms that can damage the heart and peripheral nerves. The incubation period is 2 - 9 days. It is spread by droplet infection, but contaminated milk and dust can also carry the disease. Diphtheria is a rare disease in Kenya, but due to its severity and high cause of death, it should be prevented.

Key signs and symptoms of diphtheria

  • The child has fever and is acutely sick.
  • The neck is enormously swollen more swollen than you see in tonsillitis (Bull neck).
  • Sore throat with hoarseness and difficulty in swallowing.
  • Grey membrane at the back of the throat that spreads past the tonsils. The membrane looks like a small piece of a dirty cloth stuck to the child’s throat.

Prevention: Diphtheria can be prevented by Immunization using the pentavalent vaccine.

📚 Whooping Cough (Pertussis)

Whooping cough, which is also known as Pertussis, is an acute respiratory tract infection commonly found in children. There is no protection against Pertussis from maternal antibodies. Whooping cough is caused by a bacterium called Bordetella Pertussis. Coughing spells ending in a whoop are characteristic of the disease. Sometimes vomiting follows the whoop. The whoop may be absent in children under 6 months of age. Whooping cough is transmitted by droplet infection. The incubation period is 7-10 days.

Signs and symptoms of whooping cough:

  • Fever
  • A cough that lasts more than three weeks (in children under 6 months of age)
  • Fever, sneezing, watering of the eyes, and irritation of the throat (in children 6 months or older).
  • Paroxysms of coughing followed by a whooping sound at the end of long coughing spells.
  • The underside of the tongue may become sore and ulcerate.
  • Attacks are more frequent at night and the cough can last several weeks.

Prevention:

  • Giving pentavalent vaccine (immunization) to all children below 9 years of age.
  • Healthy children should avoid contact with children who are suffering from whooping cough.
  • Sending all suspected cases of whooping cough for treatment in the hospital.

📚 Neonatal Tetanus

Neonatal Tetanus is tetanus infection of the newborn (less than one-month-old infant) acquired after delivery. Tetanus is caused by a spore-forming bacterium known as clostridium tetanae. In the newborn, it enters the body through the umbilical stump, which may be contaminated during cutting, tying, and dressing or through other cuts. The disease usually presents with muscle spasms and a failure to breastfeed in a baby who was otherwise born healthy. Nearly all cases of neonatal tetanus occur within 4-10 days after birth.

Mode of transmission: Tetanus bacteria live in the intestines of grass-eating animals. In Kenya, the umbilical cord is the commonest site of bacterial entry in the newborn. The infection occurs when the umbilical cord is either cut with a contaminated instrument such as a knife or razor blade or when the wound is treated with cow dung or soil (as it is done in some communities). Delivery in a dirty environment can also lead to infection with tetanus.

Signs and symptoms:

  • The baby stops sucking the breast.
  • Muscle rigidity and spasms occur with the head bent backward, known as opisthotonus posture.
  • False smile. The baby looks as if he/she is smiling, yet he/she is not (Risus sardonicus).

Prevention:

  • Immunizing women of childbearing age (19-49yrs) and pregnant women with the Tetanus Toxoid vaccine.
  • Delivering mothers in clean environments.
  • Avoiding putting anything on the umbilical stump except antiseptic methylated spirit.
  • Cutting the cord with sterile equipment.
  • Tying the cord with sterile ligature, cloth, or clean thread.

📚 Measles

Measles is a very infectious and dangerous disease caused by a paramyxovirus virus. Its incubation is 10-14 days. It commonly occurs in children and has high morbidity and mortality among children less than 1 year of age. Measles is spread very rapidly by droplets. Measles usually occurs in epidemics among children aged 7 to 9 months old.

Signs and symptoms:

  • High fever, usually above 38.9 degrees centigrade before the rash appears.
  • Cough, possibly with a running nose before the rash appears.
  • Rash all over the body, lasting about seven days.
  • Conjunctivitis (red eyes)
  • Rash starting from the head and neck
  • Koplik spots white spots on a red background on the mucous membranes of the mouth, inside the child’s cheeks.
  • Diarrhea and vomiting.
  • Refusal to feed

Prevention:

  • Immunization at 9 months of age or at any visit of an unimmunized child less than five years of age.
  • Giving nutritious food.
  • Exclusive breastfeeding of infants up to 6 months.
  • Encourage good weaning practices.

📚 Measles and Nutrition

Measles interferes with the nutrition of the child.

  • When a child has measles his/her food intake is reduced, particularly when the child has a sore mouth or is too sick to take enough food and fluids and has loss of appetite.
  • Because of the diarrhea that accompanies measles, the child loses a lot of protein leading to protein energy malnutrition. This can progress to kwashiorkor or marasmus.
  • Specific deficiencies of Vitamin A may occur during measles, leading to the damage of the corneal epithelium and blindness.
  • High fever increases the breakdown of food and body protein, ending in malnutrition.
  • Traditional beliefs can interfere with the nutrition of the child. One dangerous traditional belief is that a child with measles should not eat eggs, meat, fish, or drink milk. This denies the child of proteins and energy.

Management of a child with Measles:

  • Lower the body temperature by tepid sponging.
  • Give plenty of fluids such as fruit juice, ORS, or soup to prevent dehydration.
  • Clean the eyes and apply tetracycline eye ointment 1% at six-hour intervals for seven days to prevent conjunctivitis.
  • Give Vitamin A supplement, 100 000 iu capsules once daily for three days.
  • Give frequent nutritious feeds to prevent malnutrition.

If a child presents with the following conditions, you should refer them to the hospital immediately:

  • Severe dehydration
  • Laryngo-trachea-bronchitis
  • Severe pneumonia
  • Severe malnutrition

📚 Key Takeaways

  • Reactions to immunizations are usually mild and temporary.
  • Tuberculosis (TB) is caused by bacteria and is spread through air droplets.
  • Poliomyelitis is a viral disease that can cause paralysis.
  • Diphtheria is a bacterial infection of the throat and tonsils.
  • Whooping cough (Pertussis) is a respiratory infection with characteristic coughing fits.
  • Neonatal tetanus is a serious infection that can be prevented through maternal vaccination and clean delivery practices.
  • Measles is a highly infectious viral disease with distinct symptoms, and is preventable through vaccination. Measles also interferes with nutrition.
