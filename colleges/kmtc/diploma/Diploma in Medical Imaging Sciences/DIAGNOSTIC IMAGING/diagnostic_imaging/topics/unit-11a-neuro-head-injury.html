Meninges

  1. 1. Dura:( 2 layers) Periosteal-outer layer attached to bone meningeal: inner layer closer to brain. The two layers are fused except in areas enclosing dural sinuses.

Pia and arachnoid form the leptomeninges.

üìä Imaging Modalities

  • ‚Ä¢ Plain skull radiographs (X-Ray): No role in the modern evaluation of TBI.
  • ‚Ä¢ 1/4 of patients with fatal brain injuries have no skull fracture at autopsy.

NECT

  • ‚Ä¢ Fast, available, sensitive for both bone and brain injury.
  • ‚Ä¢ Both bone and soft tissue reconstruction algorithms should be used. Soft tissue reconstructions should be viewed with both narrow ("soft tissue") and intermediate ("subdural") windows.
  • ‚Ä¢ 3D reconstruction and curved MIPs of the skull have been shown to improve fracture detection over the use of axial sections alone.

MRI

T2* MRI is more sensitive than CT in detecting;

  • ‚Ä¢ all stages of ICH,
  • ‚Ä¢ non hemorrhagic contusions,
  • ‚Ä¢ injuries in the posterior fossa,
  • ‚Ä¢ brainstem and
  • ‚Ä¢ DAI.

Susceptibility weighted imaging(SWI) MRI for head trauma increases sensitivity for detection of microhemorrhages and hemorrhagic axonal injury. (sensitive to compounds which distort the local magnetic field and as such make it useful in detecting blood products, calcium)

IMAGING MODALITIES

  • ‚Ä¢ ANGIOGRAPHY (CTA/CTV/MRA/MRV ): Suspected intracranial arterial or venous injury.
  • ‚Ä¢ Conventional angiography: In inconclusive CT or MR angiography and for intervention.

CISTERNOGRAPHY

  • ‚Ä¢ High-resolution non contrast skull base CT may be helpful to identify the source of the leak .
  • ‚Ä¢ CT or radionuclide cisternography (I-111 DTPA ) may have a secondary role if skull base CT is inconclusive.
  • ‚Ä¢ High-resolution MRI may have a role if post-traumatic cephalocele is suspected .

MR CISTERNOGRAPHY

  • ‚Ä¢ A-defect roof of sphenoid sinus
  • ‚Ä¢ B-T1 fat sat GD-DTPA contrast leakage in sphenoid sinus

CAPUT SUCCADENIUM

  • ‚Ä¢ It is a manifestation of birth trauma.
  • ‚Ä¢ Consists of a subcutaneous extraperiosteal serosanguineous fluid collection.
  • ‚Ä¢ Results from pressure on the presenting part of the skull against the dilating cervix.
  • ‚Ä¢ It extends across the midline and over suture lines and is associated with head moulding.
  • ‚Ä¢ Does not usually cause any complication and usually resolves over the first few days.

CEPHALOHEMATOMA

  • ‚Ä¢ Subperiosteal hematoma Between outer table of calvaria, periosteum.
  • ‚Ä¢ Elevates periosteum.
  • ‚Ä¢ Does not cross sutures.
  • ‚Ä¢ Extracranial equivalent of intracranial epidural hematoma.
  • ‚Ä¢ Usually unilateral.

SUBGALEAL HEMATOMA

  • ‚Ä¢ Hematoma under aponeurosis (galea) of occipitofrontalis muscle.
  • ‚Ä¢ External to periosteum.
  • ‚Ä¢ Not limited by sutures
  • ‚Ä¢ Ball point pen in-situ
  • ‚Ä¢ Ct scan

ü¶¥ SKULL FRACTURES

  1. 1. Linear skull fracture: Sharply marginated lucent line(s).
  2. 2. Depressed skull fracture: Comminuted fragments imploded inwardly.
  3. 3. Elevated skull fracture: Elevated, rotated skull segment.
  4. 4. Diastatic skull fracture: Widened suture or synchondrosis. Usually accompanied by linear skull fracture.
  5. 5. "Growing" skull fracture.
    • - Difficult to detect in acute stage.
    • - Progressively widening, unhealing fracture.
    • - Lucent lesion with rounded, scalloped margins.
    • - CSF and soft tissue trapped within expanding fracture.
    • - Brain is usually encephalomalacic.

    6. 2/3 of patients with moderate TBI have an associated skull fracture.

üß†ü©∏ Traumatic Subarachnoid Hemorrhage

    • ‚Ä¢ Layering on tentorium

üí®üß† Pneumocephalus
