ü´Ä LEFT TO RIGHT SHUNTS

Results from abnormal communication between chambers or great arteries

Clinical and radiological features appear with relatively big shunts and high rate of flow across the shunt

  • ‚Ä¢ If sys:pulm flow is >2:1 patient develops pulmonary plethora; if shunt is extreme (4:1) pulmonary hypertension, heart failure, Eisenmenger reaction (uncommon except in infants)
  • ‚Ä¢ If flow <2:1 there are neither clinical symptoms nor abnormal radiological features on conventional radiology
  • ‚Ä¢ Chamber enlangement occurs to the receiving R chamber and those R chambers distal to it
  • ‚Ä¢ The L chamber shunting blood enlarges (except ASD and some high VSD) together with those L chambers proximal to it

ATRIAL SEPTAL DEFECT (ASD) Incidence

  • ‚Ä¢ 8-14% of all CHD; M:F = 1:4

Types

  • A. Ostium Secundum ASD (60-70%) exaggerated resorptive process of septum primum leads to absence / fenestration of the foramen ovale flap
    • - occurring either:
  • B. Ostium Primum ASD (30%) defect of atrioventricular endocardial cushion
  • C. Sinus Venosus ASD (5%) defect of the superior inlet portion of the atrial septum superior to fossa ovalis near entrance of superior vena cava (SVC straddles ASD)
  • D. Lutembacher Syndrome = ASD + mitral stenosis

üíî ASD

Clinical Features

  • ‚Ä¢ most are asymptomatic in infancy and childhood
  • ‚Ä¢ presentation frequently > age 40 secondary to benign course
  • ‚Ä¢ mildly symptomatic (60%): dyspnea, fatigue, palpitations, arrhythmias
  • ‚Ä¢ severely symptomatic (30%): cyanosis, heart failure

Radidogical Features

Plain:

  • ‚Ä¢ normal (if shunt <2 x systemic blood flow)
  • ‚Ä¢ LA not enlarged except in some cases of ECD and Lutembacher syndrome
  • ‚Ä¢ loss of visualization of SVC and small appearing aorta with normal aortic knob (due to clockwise rotation of heart due to RV hypertrophy and RA enlargement)
  • ‚Ä¢ normal size of LA after shunt reversal (due to immediate decompression into RA) in Eisenmenger syndrome
  • ‚Ä¢ enlargement of pulmonary trunk + arteries (pulmonary plethora)

üíî VENTRICULAR SEPTAL DEFECT

VSD Types

  2. 2. Muscular VSD (maladie de Roger) consists of one or more defect, usually small in the muscular part of the IV septum; when multiple - Swiss Cheese VSD

üíô CYANOTIC CONGENITAL HEART

  • ‚Ä¢ patients with this type of CHD present with central cyanosis at birth/few hours after birth/few months or years later
  • ‚Ä¢ result of flow of deoxygenated blood into the systemic circulation
  • ‚Ä¢ anatomical deformities responsible for central cyanosis:
    • - Conotruncal septum displaced anteriorly, and its lowermost component, the infundibular septum, fails to fuse with the top of the IV septum.
    • - the combination of an abnormal communication and distal right heart block, leading to a R ‚Üí L shunt e.g. TOF, ER
    • - common chambers, leading to an admixture of saturated and desaturated blood e.g. PTA, TAPVD
    • - abnormal connections (discordance) e.g. UTGA, CCTGA (AV discordance)

ü´Ä Mitral Valve Disease

1) Mitral Stenosis(ms)

Almost always rheumatic in origin.

  • ‚Ä¢ In the elderly, heavy calcification of the valve apparatus can cause ms.
  • ‚Ä¢ A rare congenital form of ms is also recognized.
  • ‚Ä¢ In rms the valve orifice is slowly diminished by progressive fibrosis, calcification of valve leaflets, and fusion of the cusps and subvalvular apparatus.

Ms Cont

  • ‚Ä¢ Flow of blood from left atrium to left ventricle is restricted and left atrial pressure rises leading to pulmonary venous congestion and breathlessness.
  • ‚Ä¢ There is dilatation and hypertrophy of the left atrium and left ventricular filling becomes more dependent on left atrial contraction.
  • ‚Ä¢ mitral orifice is about 5 cm sq and may be reduced to 1 cm sq or less in severe ms.

ü´Ä Aortic Stenosis

Middle aged and elderly:

  • - senile degenerative aortic stenosis
  • - calcification of bicuspid valves.
  • - rheumatic aortic stenosis.
  • - Calcific aortic stenosis usually develops on the basis of congenitally bicuspid valves.
  • - Calcification begins in the 4th decade. Mobility is lost and stenosis develops.
  • - Valve calcification in this case is invariable since it is the stenosing process. (in mitral stenosis, valve calcification is a result of stenosis)
