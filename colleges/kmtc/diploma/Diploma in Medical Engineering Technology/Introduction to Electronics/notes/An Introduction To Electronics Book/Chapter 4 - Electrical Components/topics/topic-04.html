The Inductor

üìö Electrical Components
‚è±Ô∏è 10 min read
01

üìò Definition & Function

An inductor, also called a coil or reactor, is a passive two-terminal electrical component which resists changes in electric current passing through it. It consists of a conductor such as a wire, usually wound into a coil. When a current flows through it, energy is stored temporarily in a magnetic field in the coil. When the current flowing through an inductor changes, the time-varying magnetic field induces a voltage in the conductor, according to Faraday's law of electromagnetic induction, which opposes the change in current that created it.

An inductor is characterized by its inductance, the ratio of the voltage to the rate of change of current, which has units of henries (H). Inductors have values that typically range from $1\mu \mathrm{H}$ $(10^{-6}\mathrm{H})$ to $1\mathrm{H}$. Many inductors have a magnetic core made of iron or ferrite inside the coil, which serves to increase the magnetic field and thus the inductance. Along with capacitors and resistors, inductors are one of the three passive linear circuit elements that make up electric circuits. Inductors are widely used in alternating current (AC) electronic equipment, particularly in radio equipment. They are used to block the flow of AC current while allowing DC to pass; inductors designed for this purpose are called chokes. They are also used in electronic filters to separate signals of different frequencies, and in combination with capacitors to make tuned circuits, used to tune radio and TV receivers.

üëÅÔ∏è Overview & Theory

Inductance $(L)$ results from the magnetic field around a current-carrying conductor; the electric current through the conductor creates a magnetic flux. Mathematically speaking, inductance is determined by how much magnetic flux $q$ through the circuit is created by a given current $i$.

$$L = \frac {d \phi}{d i}$$

For materials that have constant permeability with magnetic flux (which does not include ferrous materials) $L$ is constant and simplifies to:

$$L = \frac {\phi}{i}$$

Constitutive equation

Any change in the current through an inductor creates a changing flux, inducing a voltage across the inductor. By Faraday's law of induction, the voltage induced by any change in magnetic flux through the circuit is:

$$v = \frac {d \phi}{d t}$$

From the definition of inductance, we get the constitutive relation:

$$v = \frac {d}{d t} (L i) = L \frac {d i}{d t}$$

So inductance is also a measure of the amount of electromotive force (voltage) generated for a given rate of change of current. For example, an inductor with an inductance of 1 henry produces an EMF of 1 volt when the current through the inductor changes at the rate of 1 ampere per second.

Lenz's law

‚ö°

Lenz's Law

The polarity (direction) of the induced voltage is given by Lenz's law, which states that it will be such as to oppose the change in current.

  • If current is increasing, the induced voltage opposes the entry of current (positive at entry terminal). The inductor is "charging".
  • If current is decreasing, the induced voltage is negative at the entry terminal, aiding the current. The inductor is "discharging" energy back to the circuit.
03

üöÄ Applications

Inductors are used extensively in analog circuits and signal processing. Applications range from the use of large inductors in power supplies to small inductance ferrite beads.

Common uses include:

  • Signal Filtering: In conjunction with capacitors, inductors remove residual hums (mains hum) in power supplies.
  • Energy Storage: Used in switched-mode power supplies to store energy and keep current flowing during "off" switching periods.
  • Transformers: Two coupled inductors form a transformer, fundamental to electric power grids.
  • Tuned Circuits: Used in radio transmitters and receivers as resonators for oscillating current.

üèóÔ∏è Construction & Types

An inductor usually consists of a coil of conducting material, typically insulated copper wire, wrapped around a core either of plastic or of a ferromagnetic material.

‚ñ∂ Air Core Inductor

The term air core coil describes an inductor that does not use a magnetic core made of a ferromagnetic material. These are free from core losses but have lower inductance. They are often used at high frequencies.

‚ñ∂ Radio Frequency Inductor

At high frequencies, specialized construction is used to minimize losses due to:

  • Skin effect: Resistance increases as current travels on the surface.
  • Proximity effect: Current concentration in parallel wires increases resistance.
  • Parasitic capacitance: Electric field between turns can cause self-resonance.

Construction techniques include basket-weave coils, spiderweb coils, and Litz wire.

‚ñ∂ Ferromagnetic Core Inductor

These use a magnetic core (iron or ferrite) to increase inductance by thousands of times. However, this introduces core losses (eddy currents and hysteresis) and nonlinearity (saturation).

  • Laminated core: Used at low frequencies to prevent eddy currents.
  • Ferrite core: Ceramic ferrimagnetic material used for higher frequencies (nonconductive).
  • Toroidal core: Doughnut-shaped core that reduces magnetic flux leakage.
‚ñ∂ Choke

A choke is designed specifically for blocking higher-frequency AC while allowing lower frequency or DC to pass. Its impedance increases with frequency.

‚ö° Circuit Theory

The relationship between time-varying voltage $v(t)$ and current $i(t)$ is:

$$v(t) = L \frac{di(t)}{dt}$$

Reactance

For sinusoidal AC, the inductive reactance $X_L$ is the ratio of peak voltage to peak current:

$$X_{\mathrm{L}} = 2\pi f L$$

Reactance increases with frequency. An ideal inductor has zero resistance but non-zero reactance.

Laplace Analysis (s-domain)

The impedance of an ideal inductor with no initial current is:

$$Z(s) = L s$$

where $s$ is the complex frequency.

Inductor Networks

Series Inductors: Current is the same, voltages add.

$$L_{\mathrm{eq}} = L_1 + L_2 + \dots + L_n$$

Parallel Inductors: Voltage is the same, currents add.

$$\frac {1}{L _ {\mathrm {e q}}} = \frac {1}{L _ {1}} + \frac {1}{L _ {2}} + \dots + \frac {1}{L _ {n}}$$

Stored Energy

The energy (in joules) stored by an inductor is:

$$E _ {\text {s t o r e d}} = \frac {1}{2} L I ^ {2}$$
06

üìè Q Factor

The quality factor ($Q$) is the ratio of inductive reactance to resistance at a given frequency, measuring efficiency.

$$Q = \frac{\omega L}{R}$$

A higher Q indicates behavior closer to an ideal, lossless inductor. High Q inductors are essential for narrow bandwidth resonant circuits.
