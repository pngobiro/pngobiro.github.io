üìä Introduction & Principle

Pulse oximetry is a rapid, noninvasive method of estimating oxygenation and is continuous, allowing detection of sudden changes in a patient‚Äôs clinical status.

A pulse oximeter is a device that non-invasively monitors the oxygen saturation of a patient's blood. The colored substance in blood, hemoglobin, is carrier of oxygen and the absorption of light by hemoglobin varies with the amount of oxygenation. Two different kinds of light (one visible, one invisible) are directed through the skin from one side of a probe, and the amount transmitted is measured on the other side.

The machine converts the ratio of transmission of the two kinds of light into a % oxygenation. There are TWO numerical values obtained from the pulse oximeter monitor:

  • Oxygen Saturation ($SpO_2$): The oxygen saturation of haemoglobin in arterial blood. The value is given together with an audible signal that varies in pitch depending on the oxygen saturation (falling pitch indicates falling saturation).
  • Pulse Rate: Beats per minute, averaged over 5 to 20 seconds. Some oximeters display a pulse waveform or indicator illustrating pulse strength (perfusion).

Oxygen Saturation Basics

Red blood cells contain haemoglobin. One molecule of haemoglobin can carry up to four molecules of oxygen. If all binding sites are carrying oxygen, the haemoglobin is 100% saturated. Most haemoglobin combines with oxygen as it passes through the lungs.

Normal Values vs. Cyanosis

  • Healthy Individual (Sea Level): Arterial oxygen saturation of 95% ‚Äì 100%.
  • Venous Blood: Dark red, saturation around 75%.
  • Cyanosis: Blue appearance of lips/tongue. Only visible when deoxygenated haemoglobin > 5 g/dl. Difficult to detect clinically, especially in dark-skinned or anaemic patients, until saturation is <90%.
  • Hypoxia Warning: Saturation of 94% or lower requires treatment. Less than 90% is a clinical emergency.

‚öôÔ∏è Components of a Pulse Oximeter

A pulse oximeter consists of the monitor containing the batteries and display, and the probe that senses the pulse.

The Pulse Oximeter Monitor

Contains the microprocessor and display showing oxygen saturation, pulse rate, and waveform. It updates calculations regularly to give immediate readings. The audible beep changes pitch with oxygen saturation value‚Äîan important safety feature allowing auditory monitoring without looking at the screen.

The Pulse Oximeter Probe

The probe consists of two parts:

  1. Light Emitting Diodes (LEDs): Shine beams of light through tissues.
  2. Photo-detector: Detects light transmitted.

The blood absorbs light differently depending on oxygen saturation. The photo-detector detects the light transmitted as blood pulses, and the microprocessor calculates $SpO_2$. The probe must be placed where a pulse can be detected (finger, toe, ear) with LEDs facing the detector.

Types of Probes

Hinged Probes

Most popular but easily damaged.

Rubber Probes

Most robust. Wrap-around design (caution: do not constrict blood flow).

Ear Probes

Lightweight; useful in children or vasoconstricted patients.

üîä Operation & Alarms

Practical Use Steps

  1. Turn on: Allow internal calibration.
  2. Select probe: Ensure correct sizing and clean site (remove nail varnish).
  3. Connect probe: Position carefully; not too loose or tight. Avoid arm with BP cuff.
  4. Wait for signal: Allow seconds for detection. Look for pulse indicator.
  5. Verify: Check readings. Verify probe function on your own finger if in doubt.
  6. Audio: Adjust volume (never silent). Ensure alarms are on.

Alarms

üö®

Critical Alarms

  • Low Saturation (Hypoxia): Triggered when $SpO_2 < 90\%$.
  • No Pulse Detected: Probe off, hypotension, hypovolaemia, or cardiac arrest. Check probe site then patient.
  • Pulse Rate: High or Low rate alarms.

Interference Factors

  • Light: Bright external light can affect readings. Shield the probe.
  • Shivering: Movement makes signal detection difficult.
  • Pulse Volume: Low BP, cardiac output, or arrhythmia causes weak pulse signal.
  • Vasoconstriction: Cold patients/peripheries reduce blood flow signal.
  • Carbon Monoxide: Binds to haemoglobin displacing oxygen. Gives falsely high saturation reading (bright red carboxyhemoglobin).

Maintenance

The oximeter probe is delicate and easily damaged.

  • Handle probe carefully; do not drop.
  • Align connector pins correctly before inserting.
  • Never pull by the cable; grasp the connector.
  • Loosely coil cable for storage; tight coiling damages wires.
  • Clean lens and detector with soapy water or alcohol-soaked swab.
