pH Meter

üß™ Introduction

The pH meter is a device used for determining the concentration of hydrogen ions [H+] in a solution. This equipment, provided it is carefully used and calibrated, measures the acidity of an aqueous solution. pH meters are sometimes called pH analyzers, pH monitors or potentiometers.

The pH meter is commonly used in any field of science related to aqueous solutions. It is used in areas such as agriculture, water treatment and purification, in industrial processes such as petrochemicals, paper manufacture, foods, pharmaceuticals, research and development, metal mechanics, etc.

In the health laboratory, its applications are related to the control of culture mediums and to the measurement of the alkalinity or acidity of broths and buffers.

Clinical Significance

In specialized laboratories, diagnostic equipment microelectrodes are used to measure the pH of liquid blood components. The plasma pH allows the patient's health to be evaluated. It normally measures between 7.35 and 7.45. This value relates to the patient's metabolism in which a multitude of reactions occurs where acids and bases are normally kept in balance.

‚öôÔ∏è Components & Construction

General Components

A pH meter generally has the following components:

  1. The body of the instrument: Containing the circuits, controls, connectors, display screens and measuring scales. Important controls include:
    • ON/OFF switch: Allows connection to electrical outlet.
    • Temperature control: Adjusts according to the temperature of the solution measured.
    • Calibration controls: Buttons or dials (Cal 1 and Cal 2) for one-point or two-point calibration.
    • Mode selector:
      • Standby mode (0): Protects electrodes during storage.
      • pH mode: Takes pH measurements.
      • Millivolt mode (mV): Performs millivoltage readings.
      • ATC mode: Automatic Temperature Control for varying solution temperatures.
  2. A combined electrode or probe: Must be stored in distilled water. It integrates a reference electrode (Calomel) and an internal electrode into the same body.

Construction of pH meter (combined electrode)

The pH probe of modern pH meter is a combined type, in which glass and reference electrode are placed into a rod like structure. The combined electrode consists of following parts:

  1. Glass bulb: It is a sensor that senses the H+ ion concentration and it is made from special type of glass and membrane. The glass bulb consists of 0.1M HCL.
  2. Internal electrode: It is the silver chloride electrode.
  3. Internal solution: The silver chloride electrode is dipped in buffer solution of 0.1 mol/L KCL of pH 7.
  4. Reference Electrode: It is also the silver chloride electrode.
  5. Internal Solution: The reference electrode is also dipped in buffer solution of 0.1 mol/L KCL of pH 7.
  6. Junction: It is made from ceramic junction also called as diaphragm that allows the contact of sample solution and reference electrolyte. It does not disturb the electric connection between both the electrodes.
  7. Filling hole: It is used for refilling the electrolyte.
  8. Electrode body: the body is from non-conductive glass or plastic.

‚ö° Working Principle

pH electrode is constructed of two half cells, which develop an electrical potential when connected together. Reference electrode maintains a constant potential and is bathed in KCl. Glass electrode (Sanz electrode) develops an electrical potential that is proportional to the amount of H+ present.

The working principle of the pH meter relies on the ions exchange from the sample solution to the inner solution (pH 7 buffer) of the glass electrode via the glass membrane. When the probe is submerged in a sample solution to measure the pH, hydrogen ions build up around the bulb and take the place of the metal ions. Similarly, some metal ions transfer from the glass (sensor) electrode to the sample solution.

Because of low sensitivity to pH changes or complete insensitivity to pH changes, the reference electrode potential offers a constant voltage. This generates some electricity captured by the silver wire by generating potential difference (hydrogen-ion activity). The pH meter converts the voltage of this electric flow into pH value by comparing the generated voltage to the reference electrode.

Nernst Equation

The pH meter measures the potential difference at both the electrodes and calculate the pH as per the Nernst equation:

General Form:
$$E = E^0 - \frac{RT}{zF} \ln Q$$
For pH Measurement (at 25¬∞C):
$$E = E^0 - \frac{0.0592}{z} \log [H^+]$$

Since pH = -log[H‚Å∫], this becomes:

$$E = E^0 + 0.0592 \times pH$$
Variable Definitions:
  • E = measured potential (volts)
  • E‚Å∞ = standard electrode potential (volts)
  • R = universal gas constant (8.314 J/mol¬∑K)
  • T = absolute temperature (Kelvin)
  • z = number of electrons transferred (for H‚Å∫, z = 1)
  • F = Faraday constant (96,485 C/mol)
  • Q = reaction quotient
  • [H‚Å∫] = hydrogen ion concentration (mol/L)

Key Point: At 25¬∞C, the theoretical slope is 59.2 mV per pH unit, meaning the electrode potential changes by approximately 59.2 millivolts for each unit change in pH.

Increasing the solution's acidity results in a higher concentration of hydrogen ions, which raises the voltage. The pH measurement on the pH meter decreases due to the increased voltage.

üìù Operating Procedure

  1. Let all the samples get to the same temperature since pH readings rely on temperature. It is advised to compensate for temperature if the samples are not at 25 ¬∞C.
  2. Uncover the sample beakers and prepare the samples.
  3. Rinse the pH electrode in the sample beaker after rinsing it with deionized water beforehand. Rinse over a waste beaker to prevent contamination.
  4. The electrode should be inserted into the first sample measurement beaker with the electrode tip and junction completely submerged. The sample should then be stirred moderately and uniformly.
  5. Set the meter to begin taking a reading.
  6. Record the pH and temperature of the sample after waiting at least 1 to 2 minutes for a stable reading in the sample.
  7. If more samples are needed, repeat steps 3 through 6. After measuring, clean the electrode with deionized water and put it in a pH electrode storage solution.

üî¢ Types & Applications

Types of pH meters

A. Based on Portability

  • Pen testers: Portable, inexpensive, compact. Used in hydroponics, food production, pools.
  • Handheld meters: More robust, interchangeable electrodes. Used in field research, aquaculture, agriculture.
  • Benchtop pH meters: Largest, most accurate. Used in laboratories, environmental monitoring, food processing.

B. Based on Usage

  • Laboratory pH meter: Large measuring range, highly accurate, versatile.
  • Industrial pH meter: Exceptional stability, environmental flexibility, anti-interference capabilities.

C. Based on Reading

  • Analog pH meter: Uses a pointer to show pH level. Requires care to obtain accurate findings.
  • Digital pH meter: Shows a number representing pH level. Simpler to obtain accurate results.

Applications

  • Agriculture: assessing soil pH for crops.
  • Water treatment: monitoring pH in facilities and RO purifiers.
  • Chemical industries: neutralizing wastewater.
  • Food industry: ensuring quality/safety, especially dairy.
  • Biological fluids: blood, urine, gastric acid analysis.
  • Detergent manufacturing.

‚ö†Ô∏è Analysis & Precautions

Advantages

  • Continuous automatic recording/control.
  • Rapid and reproducible measurements.
  • Simple control and operation.
  • Does not affect solution under examination.
  • Suitable for colloidal/turbid/colorful solutions.
  • Accurate and precise.

Limitations

  • Regular cleaning required to avoid contamination.
  • Glass tip is fragile and can break.
  • Temperature impacts readings.
  • Must be calibrated before use.
  • Deposits on electrode membranes affect process.
  • Special buffer solution needed for calibration.

Precautions

  • pH electrodes are sensitive and fragile; do not use as a stirring rod.
  • Calibrate daily before use with standard buffer solutions.
  • Do not expose to sunlight (temperature sensitive).
  • Clean all glassware with distilled water before use.
  • Use fresh solutions for measurement.
