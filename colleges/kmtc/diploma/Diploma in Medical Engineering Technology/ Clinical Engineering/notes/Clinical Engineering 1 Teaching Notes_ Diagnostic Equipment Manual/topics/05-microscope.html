üëÅÔ∏è Introduction & Principles

  • A microscope is a magnifying instrument. It is used in laboratories for viewing micro-organism, which cannot be viewed with naked eyes.
  • Is an instrument or equipment used to view objects and areas of objects that cannot be seen with the naked eye.
  • Microscopes are devices that allow the observer to an exceedingly close view of minute particles.
  • A microscope is an optical instrument that uses visible light and single or multiple lenses to magnify small objects

Microscopy is the technical field of using microscopes to view objects and areas of objects that cannot be seen with the naked eye (objects that are not within the resolution range of the normal eye). There are three well-known branches of microscopy:

  • Optical/ Light microscopy.
  • Electron microscopy.
  • Scanning probe microscopy.
üìê

Magnification Formula

Magnification, M, is defined as the ratio of the size of a feature in the image to the size of the corresponding feature in the object:

M = D / d

(where D is the size of feature in the image and d the size of feature in object).

Types of Microscopes

Microbiologists use a variety of microscopes, each with specific advantages and limitations. Microscopes are of two categories.

  1. Light Microscope: Magnification is obtained by a system of optical lenses using light waves. It includes:
    • Bright field
    • Dark field
    • Fluorescence
    • Phase contrast
    • UV Microscope
  2. Electron Microscope: A system of electromagnetic lenses and a short beam of electrons are used to obtain magnification. It is of two types:
    • Transmission electron microscope (TEM)
    • Scanning electron microscope (SEM)

üí° Light Microscopes

Any microscope that uses a source of light to create an image of the specimen and, essentially includes all compound and stereo microscopes. The most widely used microscope uses light to create images of light transmitting specimens which are projected onto either the eye or a camera. The key components are a light source, an optical system, a specimen holder and a recording medium.

1. Simple Microscope

A simple microscope is a microscope that uses only one lens for magnification, and is the original design of light microscope. It consists of a small, single converging lens mounted on a brass plate, with a screw mechanism to hold the sample or specimen to be examined.

A simple microscope works on the principle that when a tiny object is placed within its focus, a virtual, erect and magnified image of the object is formed at the least distance of distinct vision from the eye held close to the lens.

üìù

Magnification of Simple Microscope

M = 1 + D / F

Where D = the least distance of distinct vision
F = focal length of the convex lens

The focal length of the convex lens should be small because smaller the focal length of the lens, greater will be its magnifying power. The maximum magnification of a simple microscope is about 10.

2. Compound Microscope

The common light microscope used in the laboratory is called a compound microscope because it contains two types of lenses that function to magnify an object. There are two types of compound microscopes depending on the number of ocular lenses: Binocular and Monocular.

Parts of a Compound Microscope

Eyepiece and Body Tube
  • The eyepiece is the lens through which the viewer looks to see the specimen (usually 10X or 15X).
  • The body tube connects the eyepiece to the objective lenses.
Objectives and Stage Clips
  • Objective Lenses range from 4X to 100X.
  • Stage Clips are metal clips that held the slide in a place.
Arm and Base
  • The Arm connects the Body Tube to the base.
  • The Base supports the Microscope.
Illuminator and Stage
  • The illuminator is the light source (bulb).
  • The stage is the flat platform where the slide is placed.
Nosepiece and Aperture
  • Nosepiece is a rotating turret that holds objective lenses.
  • The aperture allows light from the illuminator to reach the specimen.
Condenser & Diaphragm
  • A condenser gathers and focuses light onto the specimen.
  • Iris diaphragm adjusts the amount of light reaching the specimen.

Magnification of Compound Microscope

To ascertain total magnification, multiply the power of the objective lens by the power of the eyepiece.

Example: 10x eyepiece √ó 40X objective = 400X magnification.

Oil Immersion

When light passes from glass (dense medium) to air (lighter medium), rays get refracted, leading to loss of resolution. Placing a drop of oil (Cedar wood oil) with the same refractive index (1.51) as glass between the cover slip and objective lens eliminates two refractive surfaces and considerably enhances resolution, so that magnifications of 1000x or greater can be achieved.

üåà Specialized Light Microscopes

I. Dark-Field Microscope

In dark-field microscopy, specimen is brightly illuminated against a dark background. This type of microscope possesses a special type of condenser, which prevents the parallel and the oblique rays entering in to the objective and thus making the microscopic field dark. In the presence of specimen, the oblique rays are scattered by reflection and refraction and the scattered rays enter the objective making the specimen brightly illuminated.

It is useful in studying the morphology and motility of microorganisms (e.g., live spirochetes).

II. Phase Contrast Microscope

Devised by Frederick Zernike, this microscope converts phase changes into differences in light intensity. It has a special type of condenser, objective and a special magnifier. In phase-contrast microscopy, unstained/living organisms can be examined. As a result, the internal contrast of various parts of a specimen against its surroundings is increased.

III. UV Microscope

Resolution of a microscope depends upon the wavelength of light used. UV rays of shorter wavelength are used as light source to increase resolving power. Since UV rays can't penetrate glass, quartz lenses are used. Since the UV rays are invisible, photographic plates or special sensors are used to record the image.

IV. Fluorescence Microscope

In fluorescence microscopy, a high intensity mercury lamp is used as the light source. The specimen is stained with fluorescent dye (acrydine orange). The stained portion of the specimen absorb blue light and emit green light. This technique is especially important in immunology in which the reactions of antigens and antibodies are studied.

‚öõÔ∏è Electron Microscopes

In electron microscope, short beam of electrons and magnetic condenser lenses are employed to produce the image. The electrons have short wavelength, which helps in better resolution. It is possible to resolve objects as small as 10 √Ö, which is 100 times more than that of light microscope. It can magnify object up to 200,000X.

a) Transmission Electron Microscope (TEM)

A high voltage established between the filament and the anode accelerates the electrons from the hot tungsten filament. The electron beam is focused on the specimen with an electromagnetic condenser. Ultra thin sections of the specimen must be prepared since electrons can penetrate matter only a short distance. TEM has a projector lens that project the image onto a fluorescent viewing screen or film plate.

b) Scanning Electron Microscope (SEM)

The specimen is coated with a thin layer of heavy metal and the specimen is subjected to a narrow beam of electrons, which rapidly moves and scans the surface of the specimen. The irradiated specimen depending upon its physical and chemical composition will release secondary electrons. These secondary electrons are then collected by anode detector, which generates an electronic signal. Magnification on SEM is about 75,000 to 1,00,000 times.

Limitations of Electron Microscopy

  • Specimen is kept under high vacuum on the path of electron beam. So, living cells can't be examined.
  • Electrons have low penetration capacity, hence ultra thin section and staining should be done which is time consuming and also sometimes alter or distort the structures of microorganisms.
  • High cost and specialized techniques prevent its use in all microbiology labs.

üõ†Ô∏è Care of Microscope

Prevent damage to the 40x and 100x objectives

  • Immediately remove any immersion oil that accidentally comes into contact with the 40x objective. The 40x objective is not sealed against oil penetration.
  • After finishing a microscopic examination, immediately clean the immersion oil from the 100x objective with lens paper and lens cleaner.
  • To protect the 100x objective lens, the objective should be rotated up when the microscope is not in use.

Storage

In warm humid climates, fungal growth is easily established on lenses and prisms. The microscope must be stored in dry conditions when not in use. For example, the microscope could be kept in a ‚Äúwarm cupboard‚Äù with a constant temperature and low humidity.

Cleaning Procedures

Materials required: Clean cloth, lens cleaning solution (ethyl ether, xylene), lens paper, fine chamois, rubber bulb, camel hair brush.

Warning: Some manufacturers do not recommend using alcohol or acetone as these can affect the cements and adhesives used for attaching lenses.

üö´

Precautions

  • Do not use ordinary paper to clean lenses as it could scratch their surface.
  • To prevent leaving fingerprints, do not touch lenses with bare fingers.
  • Do not disassemble optical components since this can produce misalignments.
  • Avoid touching the surface of the bulb with fingers when changing it. Fingerprints decrease the light intensity.

Special Antifungal Care

In humid climates, fungi can grow on lenses. Store the microscope in a box equipped with an electric light (max 40W) to reduce humidity. If fungal growth is detected, use a small piece of cotton dampened in an antifungal solution (ether or xylene) to clean the lens.

üîß Troubleshooting

Problem Probable Cause Solution
Lighting System
The lighting system does not come on. The electrical feed cable is disconnected. Connect the electrical feed system.
The protection fuse is burnt out. Replace the protection fuse.
The bulb is burnt out. Replace the light bulb.
The lighting system is not producing uniform light. The electrical system shows voltage errors. Check and repair electrical system or use voltage stabilizer.
The bulb is badly installed. Reinstall the bulb.
Optical/Mechanical System
The mechanical stage does not stay in position. The adjustment tension is slack. Adjust the tension mechanism.
Poor quality image with objective 40X. The lenses show fungi. Remove fungi using cleaning solution.
Lenses smeared with immersion oil. Remove oil carefully with lens paper.
Dust or visible dirt is in the field of vision. Dust on eyepiece or condenser. Remove dust with camel hair brush.
