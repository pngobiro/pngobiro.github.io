<ul>
<li>What is AKD</li>
</ul>
<p>Artificial kidney department (AKD) is where ESRD (End Stage Renal failure) patient are given Haemodialysis with continuous monitoring and handling of intradialytic complications with maximum safety and ease to the patient. AKD has skilled staff with the necessary knowledge and experience to conduct dialysis to such patients.</p>
<ul>
<li>What is Dialysis?</li>
</ul>
<p>Dialysis is a medical process through which a person's blood is cleansed of the 'Toxins', the kidney normally would flush out. It is generally used when a person's kidney no longer function properly. This can be a result of congenital kidney disease, long term diabetes, HTN or other condition.</p>
<ul>
<li>Need of AKD</li>
</ul>
<p>About 1.7 million patients worldwide depend on long-term renal replacement therapies. The no. of such patients worldwide is rising about 6% annually. Asia, Latin, America, Africa and Middle East are experiencing even higher proliferative rates up to 10%.</p>
<p>Haemodialysis being the most widely used form of therapy with an incidence of 89%.</p>
<ul>
<li>IDEAL DEPARTMENT</li>
</ul>

<li>Accessibility: The facility must be easily accessible for patients, including those using wheelchairs or stretchers. This means wide doorways (minimum 0.90m, preferably 1.10m-1.12m for wheelchairs), ramps, and appropriate toilet facilities for persons with disabilities (PWD).</li>

<li>Public Areas: Reception, waiting areas, and public toilets (separate for male and female).</li>
<li>Treatment Area (Dialysis Bay): The core area where patients receive dialysis.</li>
<li>Isolation Rooms: Separate rooms for patients requiring isolation (e.g., Hepatitis B positive or those with acute respiratory illness) with independent water supply and drainage.</li>

<p>IV. Mechanical, Electrical, and Plumbing (MEP) Systems:</p>
<ul>
<li>Water Treatment System: Crucial for dialysis.</li>
<li>Multi-stage purification: Typically includes sand filters, carbon filters (for chlorine/chloramine removal, with sufficient empty bed contact time), water softeners, and reverse osmosis (RO).</li>
<li>Distribution System: Piping (e.g., PVC, natural polypropylene, stainless steel) should be inert, opaque, and designed to prevent re-contamination.</li>
<li>Storage Tank: Inert material, opaque, sealed-top/coned-bottom design with a hydrophobic vent filter.</li>
<li>Additional components: May include deionization (DI), ultraviolet (UV) disinfection, and final filtration (0.05-micron or smaller).</li>
<li>Monitoring: Remote alarms at the nurse station (audible and visual), daily check-off sheets, regular testing for chlorine/chloramines, bacteria, and endotoxins.</li>
<li>Sanitization: Routine sanitization based on bacteria testing and preventative maintenance.</li>
<li>Quality Standards: Must comply with international standards like those from the International Organization for Standardization (ISO) and the Association for the Advancement of Medical Instrumentation (AAMI), which specify maximum allowable chemical contaminant levels.</li>
<li>HVAC (Heating, Ventilation, and Air Conditioning):</li>
<li>Temperature and Humidity Control: Maintain a comfortable temperature (e.g., 21°C ± 3°C, 70°F-72°F) and relative humidity (e.g., 20% to 60%, ideally 55%).</li>
<li>Air Changes Per Hour (ACH): Minimum total air changes (e.g., 20 ACH for high biological load areas like OTs, with 4 ACH fresh air component).</li>
<li>Air Filtration: High-efficiency filters (e.g., HEPA filters, 99.97% down to 0.3 microns) are recommended, especially for sensitive areas.</li>
<li>Positive Pressure: Maintain positive pressure in critical areas (e.g., treatment rooms) relative to adjacent areas to prevent unfiltered air entry.</li>
<li>Dedicated AHU: Dedicated Air Handling Units for specific areas (e.g., dialysis treatment area) are preferred.</li>
<li>Outdoor Air Intakes: Located away from potential contaminated sources.</li>
<li>Electrical System:</li>
<li>Stable electrical supply at each dialysis station with sufficient outlets (e.g., at least six 5/15A sockets).</li>
<li>Wiring should be safe and not pose a threat to patients or staff.</li>
<li>Emergency power supply (generator) is essential.</li>
<li>Plumbing: All plumbing should be installed to prevent backflow.</li>
</ul>

Infection Control and Safety:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#v-infection-control-and-safety" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Hand Hygiene: Adequate number of easily accessible handwashing sinks (one for every 2-4 chair/beds) and alcohol hand rub dispensers.</li>
<li>Aseptic Technique: Design should support aseptic practices, including dedicated areas for sterile tray preparation and medication.</li>
<li>Cleaning and Disinfection: Surfaces should be easy to clean and compatible with hospital-grade disinfectants. Clear policies for cleaning and disinfection of wall boxes and other frequently touched surfaces.</li>
<li>Waste and Sharps Disposal: Designated areas for yellow biohazard waste bags and puncture-resistant sharps containers.</li>
<li>Isolation Precautions: Dedicated isolation rooms with independent utilities.</li>
<li>Preventing Cross-Contamination: Restrict common supplies, medications, and equipment. Unused items taken to a patient's station should not be returned to common clean areas.</li>
<li>Staff Practices: Design should encourage compliance with infection control protocols (e.g., no eating, drinking, or smoking in treatment areas).</li>
<li>Emergency Equipment: Adequate space for resuscitation equipment.</li>
<li>Fire Safety: Non-combustible or flame-retardant materials for curtains and draperies.</li>
</ul>

<p>Designing a renal unit requires a holistic approach, prioritizing infection control, patient comfort, and efficient staff operations. Here's a detailed breakdown of design requirements for floors, walls, coloring, lighting, flexibility, and patient/staff traffic flow:</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="i-flooring">I. Flooring<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#i-flooring" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li><strong>Seamless and Non-Porous</strong>: This is paramount for infection control. Renal units generate a lot of fluid (blood, dialysate, water spills), and seamless flooring prevents the accumulation of pathogens in cracks and crevices. Materials like <strong>sheet vinyl, linoleum, or epoxy flooring</strong> are ideal. Avoid tiles with grout lines, as they are difficult to clean and disinfect.</li>
<li><strong>Easy to Clean and Disinfect</strong>: The chosen material must withstand frequent cleaning with hospital-grade disinfectants without degrading or becoming discolored.</li>
<li><strong>Slip-Resistant</strong>: Especially crucial in areas prone to spills (e.g., around dialysis machines, water treatment room) to prevent falls for both patients and staff.</li>
<li><strong>Coved Skirting</strong>: All floor-to-wall junctions should have coved skirting (a curved transition from floor to wall, typically 100-150mm high). This prevents dirt and fluids from accumulating in corners and facilitates thorough cleaning.</li>
<li><strong>Durability</strong>: The flooring must withstand heavy foot traffic, rolling equipment (dialysis machines, beds, wheelchairs), and the weight of water treatment equipment.</li>
<li><strong>Acoustic Properties</strong>: While not the primary concern, some sound absorption can contribute to a quieter and more peaceful environment for patients who spend hours in treatment.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="ii-walls">II. Walls<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#ii-walls" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li><strong>Smooth and Non-Porous</strong>: Similar to flooring, walls should be smooth, washable, and non-porous to prevent microbial growth and allow for easy cleaning and disinfection.</li>
<li><strong>Durable and Impact-Resistant</strong>: Walls in high-traffic areas (corridors, treatment bays) should be able to withstand bumps from equipment and beds. Wall protection (e.g., bumper guards, chair rails) is highly recommended.</li>
<li><strong>Moisture-Resistant</strong>: Especially important in areas where splashes are common, such as around sinks and in the water treatment room.</li>
<li><strong>Seamless Finishes</strong>: Avoid wallpaper or textured finishes that can harbor dust and microorganisms. Paint should be washable and low-VOC (Volatile Organic Compound) for better indoor air quality.</li>
<li><strong>Coloring (see next section)</strong>: While not a material requirement, the chosen paint affects the overall ambiance.</li>
</ul>

<li>Neutrals: Light grays, beiges, and off-whites provide a clean and bright backdrop.</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="iv-lighting-system">IV. Lighting System<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#iv-lighting-system" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Natural Light: Maximizing natural light is highly desirable. Large windows can improve patient mood, reduce feelings of confinement, and help regulate circadian rhythms. However, ensure that direct glare is controlled with blinds or shades.</li>
<li>Uniform and Glare-Free Illumination:</li>
<li>General Illumination: Provide sufficient, even, and diffuse ambient lighting to minimize shadows and reduce eye strain for both patients and staff.</li>
<li>Task Lighting: Supplemental task lighting should be available at each dialysis station for procedures, readings, and documentation. These should be adjustable to prevent glare for patients lying down.</li>
<li>Patient Comfort: Avoid harsh overhead lighting that shines directly into patients' eyes while they are reclining. Dimmable lights or individual patient lighting controls can enhance comfort.</li>
<li>Color Rendering Index (CRI): Choose lighting with a high CRI (ideally &gt;80) to ensure accurate color perception, which is important for clinical assessments.</li>
<li>Energy Efficiency: Utilize energy-efficient LED lighting with long lifespans to reduce operational costs and environmental impact.</li>
<li>Emergency Lighting: Essential for safety during power outages.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="v-flexibility">V. <ul>
<li>Adaptable Layout: The design should allow for future changes in technology, patient volume, or service offerings.</li>
<li>Modular Design: Consider a modular design for partitions or utility connections, allowing for easy reconfigurations of treatment areas.</li>
<li>Oversized Utilities: Install slightly oversized electrical and plumbing lines to accommodate potential future expansion or increased demand.</li>
<li>Technology Integration: Plan for current and future technology needs, including data ports, power outlets for new equipment, and potential telemedicine capabilities.</li>
<li>Multi-purpose Spaces: Where feasible, design spaces that can serve multiple functions, such as a training room that can also be used for staff meetings.</li>
<li>Expandability: If future expansion is anticipated, design the building structure to allow for vertical or horizontal growth without major renovations to existing functional areas.</li>
</ul>

<p>VI. Patient and Staff Traffic Flow</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Separation of Flows:</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Clean and Dirty Zones: Clearly delineate and separate "clean" pathways (e.g., for sterile supplies, medication preparation) from "dirty" pathways (e.g., for waste, soiled linens, used instruments). This is crucial for infection control.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Patient vs. Staff: While complete separation may not always be possible, minimize unnecessary crossing of patient and staff pathways to reduce congestion and improve efficiency.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Public vs. Clinical: The public waiting area should be distinct from the clinical treatment areas to maintain patient privacy and infection control.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Clear Circulation Paths: Design wide, unobstructed corridors and doorways to allow for easy movement of wheelchairs, stretchers, and equipment.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Minimum Corridor Width: Generally, corridors should be at least 2.4 meters (8 feet) wide, with wider sections at turns or in high-traffic areas.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Door Widths: Patient room doors and main access doors should be wide enough (e.g., 1.10-1.20 meters or 3'8" to 4') to accommodate wheelchairs and stretchers.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Strategic Placement of Services:</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Nurse Station: Centrally located to provide clear visibility of the treatment area and easy access to patients.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Supply Rooms: Easily accessible from the nurse station and treatment areas, but separated into clean and dirty utility rooms.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Toilets: Sufficient, accessible toilets for patients and staff, conveniently located.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Water Treatment Room: Located to minimize piping runs to the dialysis machines, but also in a secure and accessible location for maintenance.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Minimizing Travel Distances: The layout should minimize the distance staff need to travel for common tasks, such as fetching supplies, preparing medications, or accessing emergency equipment.</p>
</li>
<li>

<p>As a patient care doctor, technician nurse, your job is to help patients with chronic kidney disease (CKD) receive safe and effective dialysis. To do this well, it will help you to learn what dialysis is, how it was developed, how to ensure high-quality care for patients, and how to perform and carry out your duties in a professional manner. We cover all of these topics in this module.</p>
<p>While there are many treatment goals for patient with CKD, the main goal is to help each patient reach the highest level of wellness possible. Helping patients to reach this goal is one of the most rewarding parts of caring for patients with CKD. Dialysis is provided by a team that includes technicians, nurses, dieticians, social workers, doctors and most important of all, patients. Some patients choose to take a very active role in their care and know a lot about their treatment and how it affects them. The team should support this interest and knowledge: research shows that patients who self-manage their care and know more about it stay healthier and live longer. Involve patient in his own care. It takes a team to make sure that patient’s needs for treatment, nutrition, medications, and social services are met treat the patient as whole and not a disease. With good dialysis, many people with CKD can lead full and active good quality of life lives. With training and experience, a dialysis team can help patients feel their best, and at the same time, have a rewarding career.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="overview-of-dialysis">Overview of Dialysis<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#overview-of-dialysis" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>When the kidneys fail, patients need treatment to live. Dialysis is one the main treatment for end-stage renal disease (ESRD). It replaces three main kidney tasks: removing wastes from the blood, removing excess fluid from the blood, and keeping electrolytes (electrically charged particles) in balance. There are two main types of dialysis: hemodialysis (HD), and peritoneal dialysis (PD). HD is most common (See Figure 1). To do HD, an entry into the patient’s bloodstream is needed. This is called a vascular access. During a treatment, needles are placed into the access. Blood flows out of the patient, through an artificial kidney (dialyzer) where the blood is cleaned, and back to the patient. The dialyzer contains a semi-permeable membrane which allows some substances, such as wastes and excess water out, but keeps others, such as blood cells, in. Wastes and water pass through the membrane into a fluid called dialysate and some substances pass from the dialysate into the blood. The dialysis machine, or delivery system, controls the flow of blood to the dialyzer, includes safety alarms to monitor the machine during a treatment, and mixes and delivers dialysate. HD is most often done in a center 3 times a week, for about 14 hours per treatment. Some patients do HD at home, and may do short treatments 5 or 6 days a week. Or they may do longer treatment at night while they sleep for 3-7 nights per week. Access for PD is through a catheter (tube) placed in the abdomen. The blood never leaves the body; instead, the lining of the abdomen, which has many tiny blood vessels, acts as a filter in the same way as a dialyzer. In the most common type of PD, patients use a cycler machine at home at night, while they sleep, to cycle sterile dialysate in and out of the abdomen. PD can also be done by hand, usually with four exchanges of fresh dialysate for used dialysate each day. PD goes on 7 days per week. Manual PD can be done at home, at work, or while travelling.</p>

<p>History of Dialysis starts with Thomas Graham [1805-1869], professor of Chemistry at Anderson University, Glasgow, Scotland. This was at the same time as physician Richard Bright was describing the clinical features and diagnosis of renal failure in Edinburgh. After several experimentation which combined the physical process of osmosis and diffusion, he devised a phenomenon which he termed as dialysis and proposed, together with Richard Bright, that this would form the basis of a treatment for renal failure. He demonstrated that vegetable parchment acted as a semi-permeable membrane for the purpose of dialysis. (See Figure 2)</p>

<p>The next step of removing solute through semi-permeable membrane from the blood of an animal took place 50 years later in the United States when John J. Abel, Rowntree and Turner of the Department of pharmacology of John Hopkins Medical School described a method by which the blood of a living animal was submitted to dialysis outside the body and again returned to natural circulation.</p>

<li>When the motor turned the drum, blood was pulled through by gravity to "prime" the casing (fill it with blood). Wastes from the blood diffused through the tubing into the dialysate in the tank. <li>A1 and A2: waste fluid (used)</li>
</ul>
<p>As soon as one of the chambers (A1 or A2) is filled with dialysate, the solenoid valves are reversed in groups of four. The valves are reversed by the electronic evaluation of the current rise pulse of the drive motor of the pump (21), which receives this pulse upon membrane abutment.</p>

<p>Within the filling phase, F1 or F2 is filled with fresh dialysate by means of the loading pressure. In order to obtain a continuous flow, a second chamber is switched parallel to the first chamber. The second chamber is operated at an inverse sequence.</p>
<p>Each time the chamber is changed over (maximum deflection of the membrane), all valves are closed for approx. 100 ms (dead time).</p>
<p>From the balancing chamber, the dialysate flows through the conductivity cell (7) with integrated temperature sensor (3). The measured conductivity values are indicated on the monitor in ms/cm, related to 25 °C.</p>
<p>The temperature sensor (3) has the following functions:</p>
<ul>
<li>Temperature compensation of the conductivity display,</li>
<li>Indication of the dialysate temperature.</li>
</ul>
<p>Should the actual values (temperature or conductivity) of the dialysate exceed or fall below the limit settings, the bypass valve (26) opens, and the dialyzer valve (24) is closed. The system is now in the bypass mode. The dialysate is discharged into the drain not via the dialyzer, but via the secondary air separator (88b) and the balancing chamber (68).</p>
<p>If the actual conductivity and temperature values of the dialysate are within the set limits, the dialyzer valve (24) opens. The valve (26) is closed. The dialysate flows to the dialyzer.</p>
<p>After the dialyzer, the dialysate which is now loaded with the substances usually eliminated with the urine flows into the secondary air separator (88b) via a filter (73), the valve (24b) and the blood leak detector (8). The secondary air separator (88b) comprises the pressure transducer (9) and the level sensor (6).</p>
<p>With a hematocrit of 0.25, blood losses of 0.5 ml per minute are recognized in the dialysate by the blood leak detector.</p>
<p>Together with the venous back pressure, the signal of the pressure transducer (9) is evaluated and indicated on the monitor as TMP. The fluid level in the secondary air separator (88b) is monitored by the level sensor (6). Due to the secondary air separator (88b), only airless dialysate is always delivered into the balancing chamber (68). Any presence of air bubbles in the balancing chamber (68) would cause balancing errors.</p>
<p>The dialysate is pressed into the balancing chamber (68) by the flow pump (21). As mentioned above, the balancing chamber valves are reversed by the current rise pulses of the drive motor of the flow pump. Using the speed of this pump, the dialysate flow can be adjusted in the dialysis program: 300, 500, and 800 ml/min. In the cleaning programs, the flow of the dialysate is fixed.</p>
