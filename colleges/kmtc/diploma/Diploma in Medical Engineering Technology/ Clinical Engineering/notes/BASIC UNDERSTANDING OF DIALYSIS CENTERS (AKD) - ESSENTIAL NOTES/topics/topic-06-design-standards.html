Design Best Practices

  • Patient flow should minimize backtracking.
  • Maintain separation of clean and dirty workflows.
  • Use natural light and calming colors for patient comfort.
  • Ensure sufficient power, plumbing, and data outlets at each dialysis station.
  • ADA compliance (accessible paths, restrooms, etc.)
  • Soundproofing for private consultations and treatment privacy.

üåç External Planning

External planning of a renal department typically refers to the strategic, physical, and operational planning that occurs outside the department itself, taking into account broader organizational, regional, or governmental healthcare frameworks.

  • Needs Assessment and Demographics: Population health data (CKD, ESRD rates), Epidemiological trends, Access and equity.
  • Location and Infrastructure Planning: Proximity to patients, Integration with hospitals, Transport and accessibility.
  • Service Provision and Referral Pathways: Service scope, Referral networks, Telemedicine.
  • Regulatory and Policy Considerations: Compliance, Licensing and accreditation, Funding models.
  • Workforce Planning: Specialist availability, Training programs, Succession planning.

üè• Core Functional Areas

Dialysis Treatment Area (Hemodialysis Area)

üìè

Patient Stations

Each station needs sufficient space (minimum 6-10 sq. meters, with dimensions around 2m x 3m are often suggested) to accommodate the dialysis chair/couch, dialysis machine, overbed table, IV pole, and allow for easy movement of staff and emergency equipment.

  • Clearance: Adequate clearance between stations is crucial for patient privacy and staff access. Screens or curtains between stations are often used.
  • Nurse Base Station: Strategically located with clear visibility of all dialysis stations (excluding isolation rooms).
  • Electrical and Medical Gas Outlets: Each patient station requires stable electrical supply (multiple sockets), oxygen, and vacuum outlets.
  • Isolation Rooms: Dedicated rooms for patients requiring isolation (e.g., due to infection).

Water Treatment Room

Needs ample space (e.g., 12-15 sq. meters) to accommodate all necessary equipment (RO machines, tanks, etc.) and allow for maintenance.

Dialyzer Reprocessing Area (if applicable)

  • Space: Sufficient space for two persons to work simultaneously.
  • Sinks: At least two sinks (one for initial cleaning, another for sterilant, packing, and labeling).
  • Ventilation: Should be equipped with a hood and an exhaust fan.

üé® Finishes and Materials

I. Flooring

  • Seamless and Non-Porous: This is paramount for infection control. Materials like sheet vinyl, linoleum, or epoxy flooring are ideal. Avoid tiles with grout lines.
  • Easy to Clean and Disinfect: Must withstand frequent cleaning with hospital-grade disinfectants.
  • Slip-Resistant: Especially crucial in areas prone to spills.
  • Coved Skirting: All floor-to-wall junctions should have coved skirting (typically 100-150mm high).

II. Walls

  • Smooth and Non-Porous: Washable and non-porous to prevent microbial growth.
  • Durable and Impact-Resistant: Wall protection (e.g., bumper guards, chair rails) is highly recommended.
  • Seamless Finishes: Avoid wallpaper or textured finishes.

III. Coloring

  • Calming and Soothing Tones: Neutrals (Light grays, beiges) and Cool Colors (Soft blues and greens).
  • Avoid: Bright, harsh, or overly stimulating colors.
06

üí° Lighting & Flexibility

IV. Lighting System

  • Natural Light: Maximizing natural light is highly desirable. However, ensure that direct glare is controlled with blinds or shades.
  • General Illumination: Provide sufficient, even, and diffuse ambient lighting.
  • Task Lighting: Supplemental task lighting should be available at each dialysis station for procedures.
  • Patient Comfort: Avoid harsh overhead lighting that shines directly into patients' eyes.

V. Flexibility

  • Adaptable Layout: Allow for future changes in technology or patient volume.
  • Modular Design: Consider modular partitions or utility connections.
  • Oversized Utilities: Install slightly oversized electrical and plumbing lines to accommodate potential future expansion.

Separation of Flows

  • Clean and Dirty Zones: Clearly delineate and separate "clean" pathways from "dirty" pathways. This is crucial for infection control.
  • Patient vs. Staff: Minimize unnecessary crossing of patient and staff pathways.
  • Public vs. Clinical: The public waiting area should be distinct from the clinical treatment areas.
  • Clear Circulation Paths: Design wide, unobstructed corridors.
  • Minimum Corridor Width: Generally, corridors should be at least 2.4 meters (8 feet) wide.
  • Door Widths: Patient room doors should be wide enough (e.g., 1.10-1.20 meters) to accommodate wheelchairs and stretchers.
  • Strategic Placement: Nurse Station centrally located; Supply Rooms accessible but separated; Toilets conveniently located.
