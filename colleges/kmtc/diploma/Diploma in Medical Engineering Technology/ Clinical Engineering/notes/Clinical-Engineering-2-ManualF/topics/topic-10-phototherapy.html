Phototherapy lights from the phototherapy machine or unit is used exclusively within the newborn care ward for newborn patients that display symptoms of jaundice or hyper bilirubinemia (high bilirubin levels).

What is Jaundice?

Jaundice is a yellow discoloration of a newborn baby‚Äôs skin and eyes and it occurs because the baby‚Äôs blood contains excess bilirubin (a yellow pigment of red blood cells). It is common in preterm babies (born before 38 weeks) and some breastfeeding babies, occurring when the liver is not mature enough to get rid of excess bilirubin.

Phototherapy uses blue light transmitted on the patient‚Äôs skin within the wavelengths of 425 to 475 nm to break down unconjugated bilirubin to a water-soluble, non-toxic form that can be easily excreted.

Most jaundiced patients require treatment for 24 to 48 hours, and typically do not require treatment for any longer than seven days.

üî¶ Light Sources & Operation

Types of Light Sources

  • LEDs: Most effective and efficient. Provide blue-green light within 425 to 475 nm. Long life and minimal heat generation.
  • Halogen/Fluorescent: Less effective than LEDs, shorter lifetime, and may produce heat (risk of hyperthermia).
‚ö†Ô∏è

Not Recommended

  • UV lights: Not recommended due to melanoma risk.
  • Natural sunlight: Risk of UV radiation and temperature instability.

Operational Modes

Phototherapy Unit has a lamp that emits a spectral irradiance, optimized at a specific distance.

  • Standard Mode: 25-30 $\mu\text{W/cm}^2/\text{nm}$
  • Intensive Mode: 30-35 $\mu\text{W/cm}^2/\text{nm}$
03

üõ†Ô∏è Main Components

  1. On/Off Button: Powers the unit on/off.
  2. Control Panel: LCD display showing treatment time, mode, intensity indicators, and total device hours. Includes buttons for intensity selection and observation light.
  3. Power Supply Unit (PSU): Converts AC mains (110/220V) to regulated DC (typically 24V). Modern units use Switch Mode Power Supply (SMPS) for compactness and efficiency.
  4. Control PCB: Executes operations, controls LED intensity, monitors system, and handles errors.
  5. Lighting Panel: Mounted internally, houses LED assemblies emitting blue-green light (425-475 nm).
  6. Light Meter: Measures effective irradiance in $\mu \mathrm{W} / \mathrm{cm}^2/\mathrm{nm}$.
04

üìê Setting up for a Patient

  1. Collect phototherapy device, power cable, and light meter.
  2. Plug in and turn on. Check for blue-green light. (White light is usually for examination only, not therapeutic).
  3. Position the overhead lights approximately 20 - 30 cm above the cot.
  4. Use the light meter on the mattress to measure irradiance.
  5. Adjust height: Lower to increase irradiance, raise to decrease irradiance, until therapeutic range is met without obstructing care.

‚ö†Ô∏è Complications

Clinical Complications

  • Dehydration: Increased fluid loss with non-LED lights.
  • Hypothermia: Patients are nearly naked; LEDs generate minimal heat so thermal support may be needed.
  • Retinal damage: Eyes must be covered at all times.
  • Eye infections: Clean skin under eye pads daily.
  • Bronze baby syndrome: Greyish discoloration of skin/urine; resolves after treatment.
  • Acute bilirubin encephalopathy (Kernicterus): Permanent brain damage from extremely high bilirubin if treatment is ineffective.

Device Complications

Inadequate light: After 20,000 ‚Äì 50,000 hours, devices may lose ability to provide therapeutic light. Regular testing is required to ensure range remains 25 ‚Äì 35 $\mu \mathrm{W} / \mathrm{cm}^2$.

üîß Maintenance & Troubleshooting

Preventive Maintenance

Daily (After Each Use)

  • Turn off and unplug.
  • Wipe meter, control panel, and power button with 70% alcohol or diluted chlorine.
  • Visually inspect components.

Monthly

  • Test therapeutic irradiance at 40 cm using a light meter.
  • Check castors for movement and brakes.
  • Document cumulative run hours.
