üí° Overview

During the second stage of labor, when you're actively trying to push your baby out, sometimes the process of childbirth seems to stall. You're trying, but the baby isn't coming. This can go on for hours. While you're in labor, your healthcare team will monitor your vital signs and your baby's heart rate to stay alert to any signs of fetal distress. If you or your baby's health is in danger from prolonged labor, and labor isn't progressing, your healthcare team will look for ways to assist you in delivering your baby.

What is Vacuum Extraction?

Vacuum extraction is one way they can assist. If certain conditions are met ‚Äî your baby's head is low enough in the birth canal and they're in an appropriate position ‚Äî a vacuum extractor may be able to help guide them out. The vacuum extractor consists of a suction cup attached to a mechanical or electric pump. The cup is applied to your baby's head and the pump provides traction to pull while you push.

Vacuum extraction, or "ventouse," is one method healthcare providers use to help get your baby out when they aren't coming out on their own. There are other methods, including forceps delivery and C-section. Usually, surgery is considered a last resort. Vacuum extraction and forceps delivery offer additional ways for healthcare providers to assist laboring mothers before resorting to C-section delivery.

üìã Procedure Indications & Contraindications

Indications for Vacuum Extraction

  • Labor is prolonged or stalled in the second stage.
  • The baby's heart rate is considered "non-reassuring".
  • The mother can't push anymore, either due to exhaustion or a health condition.

Non-reassuring Fetal Heart Rate

Your baby's heart rate might be considered non-reassuring if:

  • It‚Äôs abnormally high.
  • It‚Äôs abnormally low.
  • It has a pattern of abrupt decreases.
  • It takes too long to return to the baseline rate after a contraction.

Interventions before Extraction

If your baby‚Äôs heart rate indicates possible distress, your healthcare team might first try to compensate by:

  • Giving you oxygen through a mask.
  • Giving you fluids through a vein in your arm.
  • Changing your position to facilitate faster delivery.

Contraindications

Sometimes you might need assistance, but vacuum extraction isn‚Äôt the right choice. This might be true if your baby:

  • Is premature.
  • Has not progressed low enough in the birth canal.
  • Might not fit through the birth canal.
  • Is not positioned headfirst.
  • Needs to be rotated.
  • Has a bleeding disorder.

üè• The Procedure & Risks

What happens during delivery?

Before the procedure, you‚Äôll be informed of the risks and benefits of vacuum assistance and asked for your consent. You‚Äôll be offered pain relief if you haven‚Äôt had it already. In some cases, your delivery assistant may need to perform an episiotomy ‚Äî an incision between the birth canal and the anus ‚Äî in order to widen the opening of the birth canal. They may also break your water and drain your bladder.

Your delivery assistant will carefully position the cup of the vacuum (ventouse) in just the right spot on your baby‚Äôs head, making sure not to trap any vaginal tissues under it. On your next contraction, they will apply traction with the mechanical or electric pump. The idea is to add the force of the vacuum to the force of your contraction, pulling when you push, and pausing when you pause.

The delivery assistant is trained in how to apply just the right amount of suction to the vacuum. Too much suction can cause scalp injury to the baby, but too little can cause the cup to detach from the baby‚Äôs scalp. While applying suction with one hand, they will grasp the cup with the other hand to try and guide the baby‚Äôs head through the birth canal.

When to Abandon the Procedure

  • When it‚Äôs been attempted for 20 minutes.
  • When the cup has detached from your baby's head multiple times.
  • When there is any sign of trauma to the baby‚Äôs scalp.

Risks & Complications

Risks to Mother

  • Vaginal tears.
  • Postpartum bleeding.
  • Difficulty urinating or incontinence.

Short-term Baby Side Effects

  • Caput succedaneum: Scalp edema (swelling).
  • Bruising / Jaundice: Due to breakdown of red blood cells.
  • Cephalohematoma: Blood collection between scalp and skull.

Rare Serious Complications for Baby:

  • Skull Fracture: Newborn skulls are soft; most heal on their own but some require surgery.
  • Intracranial Hemorrhage: Bleeding in or around the brain.
  • Subgaleal Hemorrhage: Rupture of emissary veins leading to blood accumulation. Can cause hemorrhagic shock.

‚öôÔ∏è Vacuum Extractors

The vacuum extractor usually has a metal or plastic cup (flexible or semi-rigid) that is applied to the fetal vertex. Care is taken in its application to ensure that an adequate seal has been created and that no maternal soft tissue is trapped between the suction device and the fetus. Traction is then applied to the fetal head in the line of the birth canal, in an effort to assist delivery.

N/B: Rocking movements and torque should not be applied to the suction cup.

Types of Vacuum Extractors

Metal-cup Vacuum Extractor

Has a mushroom-shaped metal cup varying from 40 to 60 mm in diameter. A centrally attached chain connects the cup to a detachable handle.

Pros: Higher success rate, easier placement in occipitoposterior (OP) position.

Cons: Increased risk of fetal scalp injuries, difficult and uncomfortable application.

Soft cup Vacuum Extractor

Can be bell or funnel shaped. Some have built-in vacuum-release valves. Can be disposable or reusable.

Pros: Cause fewer neonatal scalp injuries.

Cons: Higher failure rate compared to metal cups.

Components of a Vacuum Extractor

  • Manual pump - Creates vacuum.
  • Vacuum gauge - Indicates the amount of pressure/vacuum used.
  • Release valve - Releases excess pressure.
  • Secretion bottle - Holds or collects unwanted fluid from the mother.
  • Vacuum cup - Holds the head of the baby during delivery.

üîß Routines and Safety

This equipment must be kept in first-class condition. After each use all parts which can be sterilized should be autoclaved (check manufacturer's manual). Typically this includes the suction cap, vacuum jar, and rubber tubes.

Daily Routines

  • Check that a full set of the equipment is available
  • Check for visible damage to the rubber tubes, vacuum bottle or rubber plug
  • Check that the vacuum release valve has a knob and washer
  • Check the traction chain for wear or damage
  • Check that the stainless steel connector tubes and gauge fit firmly into the rubber plug
  • Check that the suction caps are not damaged, especially around their rims
  • Check that the silicone bottom plates are not damaged or split
  • Check the pump and gauge for external damage

Weekly/Monthly Maintenance

a) Check the vacuum pump:

  1. Unscrew the cap and withdraw the pump handle shaft
  2. Check the main washer for wear and apply a little Vaseline. This will help produce an airtight seal between the washer and the internal pump case refit the pump handle and shaft
  3. Unscrew the valve assembly cap and remove valve
  4. Check the pump seal (this may be an '0' ring)
  5. Check the silicone rubber membrane washer and replace if necessary
  6. Check any other washers
  7. Refit valve assembly

b) Check the system:

Assemble the equipment using the 40mm suction cap supplied for checking purposes. Do NOT use the cap from the clinical kit.

  • Make sure all connections fit tightly.
  • Apply a little Vaseline to the rubber plug.
  • Close the screw valve.
  • With the help of a colleague press the suction cap against your forearm and tell the colleague to pump. Check that the gauge is registering a vacuum. Check required levels with medical staff and do not exceed these when testing.
  • Open the screw valve and release vacuum.
