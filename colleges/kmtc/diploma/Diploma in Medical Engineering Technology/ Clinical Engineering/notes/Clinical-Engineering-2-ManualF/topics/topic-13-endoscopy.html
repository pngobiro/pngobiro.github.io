üí° Principles of Operation

A medical endoscope is a tubular optical instrument, used to inspect or view the internal parts of human body which are not visible to the naked eye. The photograph of the internal parts can also be taken using this endoscope.

How do endoscopes work?

  1. One of the two main endoscope cables carries light from a bright lamp in the operating room into the body, illuminating the cavity where the endoscope has been inserted.
  2. The light bounces along the walls of the cable into the patient's body cavity.
  3. The diseased or injured part of the patient's body is illuminated by the light shining in.
  4. Light reflected off the body part travels back up a separate fiber-optic cable, bouncing off the glass walls as it goes.
  5. The light shines into the physician's eyepiece so he or she can see what's happening inside the patient's body. Sometimes the fiber-optic cable is directed into a video camera or a CCD (Charge Coupled Device).
üì°

Fiber-optic cables: Medical vs. Telecom

Telecommunications cables are designed to carry data in digital form (binary) over very long distances. Endoscope cables carry pictures (analog form) over much shorter distances. While telecoms cables carry binary data representing images, endoscope cables carry the actual pictures of someone's insides.

üõ†Ô∏è Fiber Optic Construction

Using low quality, large diameter and short length silica fibers we can design a fiber optic endoscope or fibroscope.

1. Outer fiber ($f_0$)

Consists of many fibers bundled together without any particular order of arrangement, called an incoherent bundle. Enclosed in a thin sleeve for protection. Used to illuminate or focus the light onto the inner parts of the body.

2. Inner fiber ($f_i$)

Consists of a bundle of fibers in perfect order, called a coherent bundle. Used to collect reflected light from the object (Imaging Fibre Bundle). A tiny lens is fixed to one end to focus the light.

Working Principle

Light from the source is passed through the outer fiber ($f_0$) and illuminated on the internal part of the body. The reflected light is brought to focus using the telescope to the inner fiber ($f_i$). Here each fiber picks up a part of the picture. The whole picture is reproduced at the other end of the receiving fiber bit by bit.

‚ú®

Total Internal Reflection

The viewing bundle of a standard fibre-endoscope is $2-3 \cdot \mathrm{mm}$ in diameter and contains $20,000-40,000$ fine glass fibres, each close to $10 \cdot \mu\mathrm{m}$ in diameter. Light focused onto the face of each fibre is transmitted by repeated internal reflections.

Each individual glass fibre is coated with glass of a lower optical density to prevent leakage of light. This coating and the space between the fibres causes a dark 'packing fraction', which is responsible for the fine mesh frequently apparent in the fibreoptic image.

üß¨ Anatomy of a Flexible Scope

The anatomical structure of the flexible endoscope is composed of five basic sections. Each section has a protective outer layer and hollow inner area containing delicate internal systems.

  1. Light Guide Connector: The solid end connecting to the light source/video cart. Connects Suction, Air, Water, Light, Video System, and Electrical Ground.
  2. Light Guide Tube (Universal Cord): Hollow tube connecting the connector to the control body. Comprised of Steel coil, Fiber mesh, and Vulcanized rubber.
  3. Control Body: Contains physician controls (angulation knobs, valves, switches).
  4. Insertion Tube: The long flexible tube inserted into the body. Protected by steel coil and vulcanized rubber layers.
  5. Bending Section: Distal end of insertion tube. Articulating steel ribs allow flexibility. Contains the Video chip, Optical glass lens, Fiber optics, and Nozzles.
04

‚öôÔ∏è Internal Systems

Each flexible scope contains a number of internal systems that perform specific functions.

1. Light Transmission

Light enters through the light guide connector and travels to the distal end via a light fiber bundle. It exits through lenses to disperse light across the visual field.

2. Image Capturing

Fiber optic: Uses an image fiber bundle to carry image to an ocular lens.
Video endoscopes: Use a CCD or CMOS chip at the distal end to convert image to digital signal.

3. Angulation System

Wires guided by knobs on the control body manipulate the bending section. 4-way movement (up/down, left/right) is typical.

4. Biopsy/Suction System

A straight piece of uniform tubing running from distal end to control body. Allows passage of accessories and suctioning of materials.

5. Air/Water System

Thin flexible tubes controlled by valves to direct air and water over the objective lens for cleaning/insufflation.

üíä Future Technology: Pill Cam

Doctors hope to shrink endoscopes to the size of a tiny pill you can swallow. Inside the pill, a miniature camera picks up images and a radio transmitter beams them to a monitor.

How it works:

  1. You swallow the pill-cam and it tumbles through your body.
  2. LEDs at the front fire out enough light to generate an image.
  3. The CCD chip picks up reflected light and generates a digital image.
  4. The radio transmitter sends images to a computer outside the body.
