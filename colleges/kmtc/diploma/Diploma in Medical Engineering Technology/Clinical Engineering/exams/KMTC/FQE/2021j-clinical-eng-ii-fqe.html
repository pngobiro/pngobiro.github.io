Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions

40 Marks
1.
The two primary functions of the anode are
a) Emissions of electronics and heat dissipation
b) Heat dissipation and acceleration of electrons
c) Convert electronic energy into x-rays and dissipate heat
d) Convert electronic energy into x-rays and accelerate electrons
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Convert electronic energy into x-rays and dissipate heat

Explanation: The anode acts as the target for electrons (converting kinetic energy into X-rays) and as a thermal conductor to dissipate the tremendous heat generated (99% of energy).

2.
The basic functions of the cathode of an x-ray tube are?
a) Expel the electrons and focus them at the anode
b) Emit electrons and accelerate then towards the anode
c) Accelerate electronics and focus them towards anode
d) All of the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Expel the electrons and focus them at the anode

Explanation: The cathode consists of the filament (emits/expels electrons via thermionic emission) and the focusing cup (focuses the beam). Acceleration is primarily due to the potential difference (kVp) between cathode and anode, not the cathode itself, though the cathode is the source.

3.
The part of the tube where electrons are emitted by the thermionic emission process is?
a) Filament
b) Cathode
c) Anode
d) Focusing cup
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Filament (located in the Cathode)

Explanation: The filament (usually tungsten wire) is heated to emit electrons. It is the specific part within the cathode assembly.

4.
The envelope of the x-ray tube can be made from?
a) Glass
b) Metal
c) Ceramic
d) All of the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) All of the above

Explanation: Traditional tubes use Pyrex glass. Modern high-performance tubes use metal or ceramic envelopes to prevent arcing and extend tube life.

5.
Which of the following is a function of x-ray tube housing?
a) Provides enclosing and support
b) Acts as a shield and absorbs stray radiation
c) Dissipates heat creates in the tube
d) All of the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) All of the above

Explanation: The housing supports the insert, is lined with lead to absorb leakage radiation, and contains oil to dissipate heat and provide electrical insulation.

6.
Which of the following quantities cannot be adjusted by the x-ray control unit?
a) Electrical potential across the tube (kVp)
b) Exposure duration (Time)
c) The tube vacuum
d) Electrical current that flows through the tube (mA)
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) The tube vacuum

Explanation: The vacuum is established during manufacturing. The operator controls kVp, mA, and Time at the console.

7.
The interaction of high energy, high speed electrons with the anode material at the atomic nucleus are termed?
a) Characteristic interaction
b) Bremsstrahlung interaction
c) Kinetic interaction
d) Potential interaction
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Bremsstrahlung interaction

Explanation: Bremsstrahlung ("Braking Radiation") occurs when electrons interact with the nuclear force field, slow down, and change direction, emitting X-rays. Characteristic radiation involves inner-shell electrons.

8.
The characteristic collision between ionizing radiation and matter in which incident electron collides with a free electron is referred to as?
a) Compton effect
b) Photoelectric effect
c) Pair production
d) Thermionic effect
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Compton effect

Explanation: Compton scattering involves an incident X-ray photon colliding with a loosely bound (effectively free) outer-shell electron, ejecting it and scattering the photon.

9.
The ability of the MRI to distinguish between different types of tissues is based on the fact that?
a) Different tissues have different construction
b) Different tissues became magnetized to different levels
c) Tissues will change the rate of magnetization at the same rate
d) Tissues cannot be magnetized
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Different tissues became magnetized to different levels (or relax at different rates)

Explanation: MRI contrast depends on Proton Density and relaxation properties (T1 and T2). Different tissues (fat vs water) have different T1/T2 relaxation times, allowing them to be distinguished.

10.
The two important characteristics of MRI magnetic field are?
a) Frequency and field strength
b) Field strength and homogeneity (field strength of its strength - typo)
c) Field direction and strength
d) All of the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Field strength and homogeneity (Likely intended answer)

Explanation: The two most critical specs for an MRI magnet are Field Strength (Tesla) and Homogeneity (Uniformity over the imaging volume).

11.
In MRI field, homogeneity is obtained by the process known as?
a) Shielding
b) Shimming
c) Polarization
d) Relaxation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Shimming

Explanation: Shimming (Active or Passive) is the process of correcting inhomogeneities in the magnetic field to make it uniform.

12.
The primary advantage of a superconducting magnet is its ability to?
a) Produce homogeneous and stronger field
b) Produce more stable and uniform magnetic field
c) Produce stronger and unstable magnetic field
d) Produce stronger and stable magnetic field
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Produce stronger and stable magnetic field (or a/b)

Explanation: Superconducting magnets can generate very high field strengths (1.5T, 3T, etc.) with excellent temporal stability because the current flows without resistance.

13.
Which of the following is a function of gradients in MRI machine?
a) Reduce image artifacts
b) Produce the echo event and signals
c) Create the special characteristics (spatial characteristics)
d) All of the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Create the spatial characteristics (Spatial Encoding)

Explanation: Gradient coils vary the magnetic field linearly across the imaging volume to spatially encode the signal (Slice selection, Phase encoding, Frequency encoding).

14.
The nitrous oxide gas in theatre is used as?
a) Pain relief gas
b) An analgesic gas
c) Anaesthetic gas
d) All of the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) All of the above

Explanation: Nitrous Oxide ($N_2O$) is a weak anesthetic and a potent analgesic (pain relief). It is used for both purposes.

15.
Which of the following gases is NOT a supply to anesthetic machine?
a) Oxygen
b) Nitrous oxide
c) Compressed air
d) Nitrogen
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Nitrogen

Explanation: Anesthetic machines are typically supplied with Oxygen, Nitrous Oxide, and Medical Air. Pure Nitrogen is not a standard supply gas for anesthesia delivery (it is an asphyxiant if used alone).

35.

The cooling agent for the MRI is?

b) Xenon

Section 2: Short Answer Questions

40 Marks
1.
List any FIVE main parts of an MRI machine (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Main Magnet (Superconducting)
  2. Gradient Coils
  3. Radio Frequency (RF) Coils
  4. Computer System (Console/Reconstruction unit)
  5. Patient Table
  6. Shim Coils
2.
Highlights the stages involved in the creation of the highly amplified output light image in image intensifiers. (4 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Input Phosphor: Converts X-ray photons to light photons.
  2. Photocathode: Converts light photons to electrons.
  3. Electrostatic Lenses: Accelerate and focus the electron beam.
  4. Output Phosphor: Converts high-energy electrons back into a bright light image.
3.
Explain the THREE methods used to detect x-rays in x-ray imaging systems. (6 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Screen-Film System (Analog): X-rays strike intensifying screens (phosphors) which emit light to expose photographic film.
  2. Computed Radiography (CR): Uses Photostimulable Phosphor (PSP) plates to store energy, which is read out by a laser.
  3. Digital Radiography (DR): Uses Flat Panel Detectors (FPDs) - either Direct conversion (Amorphous Selenium) or Indirect conversion (Scintillator + Amorphous Silicon).
4.
State FOUR properties of x-rays. (4 marks)
Show Answer
  1. They travel in straight lines.
  2. They can penetrate matter.
  3. They can cause fluorescence.
  4. They affect photographic film.
  5. They cause biological effects (ionization).
5.
Explain the THREE types of magnets used to produce magnetic field in MRI machine. (6 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Permanent Magnets: Made of magnetized material (e.g., Neodymium iron boron). Always on, heavy, low field strength.
  2. Resistive (Electromagnets): Coils of wire with current. Can be turned off, high power consumption, limited field strength.
  3. Superconducting Magnets: Coils cooled by liquid helium to zero resistance. High field strength, high stability, expensive maintenance.

Section 3: Long Answer Question

20 Marks
1.

(a) Identify the SIX main parts of an imaging ultrasound machine and state their function. (12 marks)

(b) Distinguish between active and passive shielding as applied in MRI System (4 marks)

(c) State FOUR ways in which the image acquired in ultrasound imaging system can be stored. (4 marks)

Show Answer

(a) Parts of Ultrasound Machine:

  1. Transducer Probe: Converts electrical energy to sound waves and vice versa (piezoelectric effect). Sends/Receives echoes.
  2. Pulser (Transmitter): Generates the electrical pulses that excite the transducer elements.
  3. Receiver (Beamformer): Processes the returning electrical signals from the transducer (amplification, compensation, compression, demodulation, rejection).
  4. Display (Monitor): Visualizes the processed image data.
  5. CPU (Master Control): Controls the timing and synchronization of all components.
  6. Keyboard/Control Panel: User interface for inputting patient data and adjusting settings (Gain, Depth).
  7. Storage (Hard Disk/PACS): Stores images and patient data.

(b) MRI Shielding:

  • Passive Shielding: Involves surrounding the magnet with large amounts of steel/iron (in the room walls or magnet casing) to contain the fringe field. It is heavy and expensive.
  • Active Shielding: Uses secondary superconducting coils located outside the main magnet coils with current flowing in the opposite direction. This creates an opposing field that cancels out the fringe field outside the bore, confining it close to the magnet. Lighter and more common.

(c) Ultrasound Image Storage:

  1. Internal Hard Drive.
  2. PACS (Picture Archiving and Communication System) server via Network.
  3. USB Drive / External Hard Drive.
  4. Thermal Print (Hard copy).
  5. CD/DVD (Optical media).
