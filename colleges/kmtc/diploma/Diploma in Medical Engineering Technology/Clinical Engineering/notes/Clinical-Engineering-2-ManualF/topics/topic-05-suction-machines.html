üí° Overview & Principles

Suction machine (also known as aspirator) is a type of medical device that is primarily used for removing obstructions like mucus, saliva, blood, or secretions from a person's airway. When an individual is unable to clear secretions due to a lack of consciousness or an ongoing medical procedure, suction machines help them breathe by maintaining a clear airway.

They are found in operating theatres, delivery suites/rooms in maternity department, ENT and emergency departments. Smaller specialized suctions are used in dental departments.

üìã

Common Uses

  • Removing respiratory secretions when the patient is unable to.
  • Assisting a patient that is vomiting while seizing or unconscious.
  • Clearing blood from the airway.
  • Removing a foreign substance from a patient‚Äôs windpipe and/or lungs (pulmonary aspiration).

Principles of Operation

Suction is generated by a pump, which is normally an electrically powered motor, but manually powered versions are also often found. The pump generates a suction that draws air from a bottle. The reduced pressure in this bottle then draws the fluid from the patient via a tube. The fluid remains in the bottle until disposal is possible. A valve prevents fluid from passing into the motor itself.

üîÑ Types of Medical Suction Units

The suction pump can be either portable or standard. Usually, the standard medical suction pump is connected to the mains electrical supply for energy. Portable suction pumps may be battery operated or they are manually made to create a negative pressure by hand or feet. In addition, they may also use compressed gas as a source of energy.

Based on the vacuum principle that they operate on, suction pumps may be divided into 4 categories:

1. Electrically Operated

It has an Electric motor pump, which is an electrically powered motor that drives a pump to create the required vacuum.

2. Pneumatically Driven

The pump primarily works on the Venturi principle. The driving force may be attributed to air, stream or water.

3. Manually Operated

It is with bellows and unidirectional valves to create the necessary vacuum effectively.

4. Centrally Piped

System connected to a central vacuum source.

i) Manually operated suction unit

It has a piston pump with valves designed for suction or vacuum creation instead of compression or pressure creation. A clear glass or Perspex bottle with a rubber-sealed lid is included in the pneumatic circuit to reserve the vacuum created.

A flexible rubber or plastic pipe interacts directly with the liquid to be sucked and terminates in the bottle. Another similar pipe leaves the lid to the pump. With each backward stroke, negative pressure is created in the pneumatic system and if the free pipe is immersed in a fluid (e.g. the placental blood and amniotic fluid), the fluid is sucked into the bottle. The bottle is emptied once full. The pump may be designed to be hand operated or foot operated.

N/B: Manual suction unit has the following key parts:

  • Bellows/Foot pedals
  • Collecting bottles
  • Pressure Gauge
  • Machine and patient suction tubes

ii) Electrically operated suction Pump/ unit

The pump is operated electrically through an electric motor. The motors may take various forms depending on individual Engineering design and the anticipated capacity of the pump. Popular pump types used include: Centrifugal, reciprocating (piston) and Diaphragm.

A clear glass or Perspex bottle with a rubber-sealed lid is included in the pneumatic circuit to reserve the vacuum created. A second bottle may be included to boost the pump's capacity to hold sucked fluid which ensures longer suction before emptying is done.

A flexible rubber or plastic pipe interacts directly with the liquid to be sucked and terminates in the bottle. Another similar pipe leaves the lid to the pump. In the two bottle design, the outlet pipe from one bottle goes into the second bottle as input pipe.

The output of the second bottle then goes to the pump. With each backward stroke in the Reciprocating and diaphragm pumps, negative pressure is created in the pneumatic circuit. In the Centrifugal type, vacuum is created on the suction pipe which is part of the pneumatic circuit. In all cases if the free pipe is immersed in a fluid (e.g. the placental blood and amniotic fluid), the fluid is sucked into the bottle and the bottles emptied once full. A special float valve (called anti-overflow device) is included in the bottles in some designs. It interacts with the motor control to stop pumping once a pre-determined liquid level is reached.

iii) Central piped vacuum system

This is piped vacuum from a central source. Two motor operated pumps are interconnected to create vacuum in a reservoir. The pumps are operated by pressure switches which activate the pumps once pressure falls below a pre-determined level in the reservoir.

The pump piping is rigid but is connected to the reservoir via a flexible pipe which absorbs the shock of the pumping system before the reservoir during pumping. Outlets to the patient are connected to a central pipe leading to the reservoir. Each patient outlet is connected to the central pipe via a glass bottle trap (for collecting the liquid during suction) and Bacterial filter (for ensuring that pathogenic agents from the suction site and fluid don't pass on to the atmosphere). The air sucked in the system is released to the atmosphere through the exhaust pipe via silencers (which muffle the noise of the gas as it is released under pressure to the atmosphere). A flexible plastic or rubber pipe is connected to the patient outlet on one end and is immersed in the liquid on the other end. The vacuum in the reservoir effects suction of the liquid.

iv) Pneumatic driven Suction Pumps or Vacuum Ejector

A vacuum ejector or simply ejector is a type of vacuum pump, which produces vacuum by means of the Venturi effect.

In an ejector, a working fluid (liquid or gaseous) flows through a jet nozzle into a tube that first narrows and then expands in cross-sectional area. The fluid leaving the jet is flowing at a high velocity which due to Bernoulli's principle results in it having low pressure, thus generating a vacuum. The outer tube then narrows into a mixing section where the high velocity working fluid mixes with the fluid that is drawn in by the vacuum, imparting enough velocity for it to be ejected, the tube then typically expands in order to decrease the velocity of the ejected stream, allowing the pressure to smoothly increase to the external pressure.

üí°

Vacuum Strength

The strength of the vacuum produced depends on the velocity and shape of the fluid jet and the shape of the constriction and mixing sections, but if a liquid is used as the working fluid the strength of the vacuum produced is limited by the vapor pressure of the liquid (for water, $3.2\mathrm{kPa}$ or 0.46 psi or 32 mbar at $25^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ or $77^{\circ}\mathrm{F}$).

ü©∫ Functions & Components

Functions of medical suction pumps

  • Draining blood from operation sites allows the surgeon better visibility.
  • To drain pus from an abscess
  • To irrigate the side for washing and sucking out the excess water.
  • To clean debris from a wound
  • To suck out Mucus that may be obstructing the airways or other parts of the body.
  • In gynecological processes to allow better vision and for draining the excess fluid.
  • In dentistry to drain the blood and the irrigation fluid.
  • To remove blood from the cranium after an extradural or intracranial hemorrhage.
  • To suck out foreign bodies from the nose or ear or from other places.
  • As constant intubation to provide drainage from a surgical site where pus or fluid may collect.
  • To drain excessive edematous fluid from the peritoneal cavity in patients with ascites.
  • To drain fluid from the lungs in patients with kidney disease or other major disorders.

Types of Pumps used in Suction Units

  • Piston pumps
  • Diaphragm pumps
  • Rotary pumps

Types of Motors used in Suction Units

Commonly for suction pumps, capacitor run motors are used because centrifugal switches (to switch off starting winding and capacitor) cannot be manufactured‚Äîsparkless. In suction pumps for operating theatres, sparkless switches have to be used.

CAPACITOR - RUN - MOTOR:

To the starting winding in series, a capacitor is connected. This causes a phase displacement in the starting winding and in turn a rotating field which causes the rotor to turn.

SINGLE PHASE SPLIT PHASE MOTOR:

The higher resistance of the starting winding causes the phase displacement. The starting winding is switched off by the centrifugal switch when the motor attains about 80% of full speed to keep the power factor as high as possible.

CAPACITOR - START - MOTOR:

Additional to the starting winding of the single phase split phase motor a capacitor is connected in series to the starting winding to obtain a greater phase displacement. The centrifugal switch switches off capacitor and starting winding when a certain speed is reached in order to achieve a high power factor.

SHADED POLE MOTOR:

The phase displacement is achieved with a starting winding. The starting winding is just one closed turn of a heavy copper wire embedded in one side of the stator.
