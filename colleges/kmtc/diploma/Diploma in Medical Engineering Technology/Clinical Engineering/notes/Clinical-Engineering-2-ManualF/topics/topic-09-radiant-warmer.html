RADIANT WARMER

üìö Neonatal Thermal Support
‚è±Ô∏è 12 min read
01

üí° Overview & Function

Radiant warmers are equipment used to provide radiating heat to minimize energy requirements for heat production, decreasing the risks of low blood sugar and increased work of breathing associated with hypothermia.

They provide an area where resuscitations, procedures, and short-term observation can take place while keeping the baby warm. Warmers may vary in complexity, including only heating functionality or heating functionality with resuscitation and oxygen equipment.

Radiant warmers may be used on all neonatal patients admitted to the nursery ward, but especially for those with prematurity, low birth weight, reduced growth, low body temperature, or undergoing procedures.

Critical Note

Newborn babies can drop their body temperature very quickly, even within minutes. They must be kept warm from the moment of birth. Even small drops in temperature increase the likelihood of mortality.

02

üìâ Mechanisms of Heat Loss

Newborn babies lose heat through four main mechanisms:

Evaporation

Water loss through the skin.

Radiation

Heat radiating from the warmer patient towards cooler surfaces (e.g., windows or walls).

Conduction

Direct heat travelling from the warmer surface of the skin to the cooler mat or cot on which the patient rests.

Convection

Air currents move heat away from the skin/body.

Norm thermic axillary temperature in neonates ranges from 36.5¬∞C to 37.5¬∞C. Every effort must be made to keep a baby's temperature within the normal range as temperature below 36¬∞C is an independent risk factor for death in newborns.

‚öôÔ∏è Working Principle & Modes

A radiant warmer consists of a mounted overhead heating mechanism containing heating elements (ceramic or quartz) and a parabolic reflector to direct heat energy towards the baby cot.

The core temperature of the baby is continuously monitored by a temperature probe placed over the baby‚Äôs liver. Body temperature changes are displayed in real-time on an LCD panel.

Operational Modes

  • Pre-warm Mode: Provides constant low heat for a short amount of time (typically 10 minutes or less) to warm the cot underneath the warmer. Protects against conductive heat loss.
  • Automatic (Servo/Baby) Mode: Uses a temperature probe on the baby to automatically adjust heat output to maintain the patient‚Äôs temperature within the set range.
  • Manual Mode: Provides a constant, unadjusting heat output set by the user. (Warning: Patients should never be left unattended in manual mode.)
04

üõ†Ô∏è Main Components

  1. Power switch: Usually a rocker switch located on the back or side.
  2. Control Panel: Interface for operating the warmer. Displays temperature readings, set temperature, heat power output, and alarms. Can be physical buttons/knobs or an LCD touchscreen.
  3. Patient Temperature Probe: A thermocouple cable placed on the baby‚Äôs skin (over the liver) to provide feedback to the control system for temperature regulation.
  4. Baby Cot: Often includes removable side panels and a tilting mechanism.
  5. Power Supply Unit (PSU): Converts AC mains to regulated DC power.
  6. Control PCB: Contains the microcontroller that regulates system operations, processes probe data, and controls heater output via relays.
  7. Heating Elements: Usually 800W or 1000W, releasing far-infrared heat.
    • Ceramic: Heating wire encapsulated in ceramic.
    • Quartz: Coiled element enclosed in a quartz tube.
  8. Internal Battery: Operates alarms during power failure.
  9. Audiovisual Alarms: Temperature deviation ($\pm 1^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$), Probe failure, System failure, Heater failure, Power failure.
05

üè• Complications & Safety

Clinical Complications

  • Hypothermia & Cold Stress: If device is malfunctioning or baby is exposed for too long.
  • Hyperthermia & Heat Stress: Can occur in manual mode without monitoring or if probe falls off in servo mode.
  • Pressure Sores: From incorrect positioning or lying on tubing.
  • Falls: If cot sides are not secured.
  • Infection: From improper cleaning of probe or unit.

Device Complications

  • Probe Mismanagement: If probe falls off in servo mode, the warmer may overheat attempting to compensate for low readings (room temp).
  • Fire: If linen is placed on the heater head.

üîß Preventive Maintenance

After Each Use (Daily)

  • Turn off and unplug.
  • Wipe with 70% alcohol: Probe, Control panel, Mattress, Bassinet.
  • Visual inspection.

Weekly

  • Test heating elements: Feel progressive heating across the element.
  • Test temperature probe response.

Monthly

  • Test power loss alarm (unplug while on).
  • Check castors and brakes.
  • Check cot tilting mechanism.

Quarterly

  • Perform monthly steps.
  • Check self-diagnosis mode or information page functionality.
