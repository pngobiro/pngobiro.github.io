INFANT INCUBATOR

üìö Neonatal Intensive Care
‚è±Ô∏è 15 min read
01

üí° Overview & Functions

An infant incubator is a closed chamber in which a controlled environment is provided to the premature or critically ill baby. While the unit may serve several specific functions, it is generally used to provide a safe and stable/suitable environment for the growth of newborn infants, often those who were born prematurely or with low weight or with an illness or disability that makes them especially vulnerable for the first several months of life.

Premature babies, also known as preemies, are those that are born before the mother has reached 37 weeks of gestation. Depending on how premature the baby is, he or she may have an underdeveloped digestive tract, lungs, immune system, and even skin.

Functions of an Infant Incubator

  • Protection: Protects the newborn against infection, diseases, and noise.
  • Feeding: Through IV and feeding tubes.
  • Medication: Administration of medication to the newborn.
  • Oxygenation: Achieved through head hoods, mechanical ventilation, or continuous positive airway pressure.
  • Humidity Control: Controls the humidity levels.
  • Temperature Control: Controls the temperature.

Basic Requirements

Sterility

Freedom from germs (Oxygen supply filter employed).

Temperature

Gradually adjustable temperature up to $36^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ depending on the weight of the baby.

Relative Humidity

Up to 75% or even more if desirable.

Oxygen Concentration

Up to 40% (about twice the normal Oxygen content of the atmosphere).

‚öôÔ∏è Structure & Operation

Working Sequence

When the incubator is switched on, the motor is powered which in turn activates the fan. An electric fan draws room air through a bacterial filter which removes dust and bacteria. The filtered air flows over an electric heating element. The filtered and heated air then passes over a water tank where it is moistened. It then flows on to the incubator canopy.

üí®

Pressurized Canopy

The incubator canopy is slightly pressurized. This allows expired carbon dioxide to pass back into the room via the vent holes and most of the air to be re-circulated. It also prevents unfiltered air entering the system.

Main Parts Composition

  1. Hood part: Transparent windows for monitoring, covers compartments, provides protection.
  2. Access port: Door allowing treatment without opening the full hood.
  3. Front door: Allows placement of infant into the incubator.
  4. Mattress: Designed for placement of the infant.
  5. Air Flow System: Includes filters to supply fresh air.
  6. Fill/Drain Water Device: Water container supplying the humidity generator (must use distilled water).
  7. Caster: Wheels for movement with brake devices.
  8. Transfusion Rack: Holds transfusion bottles.

Key Components Detail

Canopy (Hood)

Made of Perspex glass to avoid breakage and allow clear view. Provides a confined, conditioned environment (heat, O‚ÇÇ, humidity) simulating the mother's womb.

Hand Ports

Allow clinicians to attend to the baby (turning, feeding, medication) without opening the hood, preserving the climate settings.

Heating Element

Resistive element controlled by a thermostat. Cut off at preset temps to keep canopy max at $36^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$.

Electrically Operated Fan

Draws fresh air, forces circulation over heater and water bath, ensuring uniform conditions throughout the canopy.

Water Reservoir

Basin supplied with distilled water. Warm air passes over it causing evaporation, carrying water vapor into the canopy for humidity.

Bacteria Filter

Traps bacteria and pathogenic agents from incoming air. Requires periodical replacement.

üìã Types of Incubators

  • Closed box incubators: Have a fresh air filtration system that minimizes infection risk and prevents moisture loss.
  • Double-walled incubators: Two walls to further prevent heat and air moisture loss.
  • Servo-control incubators: Automatically programmed to adjust temp/humidity based on skin sensors attached to the baby.
  • Open box incubators (Armstrong): Provide radiant heat below the baby but are otherwise open to air for easy access.
  • Portable/Transport incubators: Used to move the newborn from one part of the hospital to another.
04

üéõÔ∏è Control Systems

Temperature Control

Simple incubators utilize a thermostat (sensor + pressure can/expansion chamber). The liquid/gas in the chamber expands when warm, moving a diaphragm to activate a switch.

Electronically controlled systems use a cable sensor (thermistor) and can include an additional skin sensor taped to the baby for body temperature monitoring.

üö´

Safety Warning

For safety reasons mercury thermometers must not be used in infant incubators. Mercury is toxic.

Humidity Control

Regulated by closing/opening a deflector plate over the water container or using a water heater. Adjustable between 40-90%. Measured by a hygrometer.

üíß

Maintenance Note

The humidifier should be filled up only with distilled water in order to avoid corrosive damage to the incubator.

Oxygen Control

Most units have a hose connection for additional oxygen from a cylinder or central supply. Warmed/moistened air is enriched with oxygen. Alternatively, oxygen is delivered directly via a nasal cannula.

Alarms

Both audible and visible alarms are crucial:

  • Power supply failure alarm
  • Air circulation/fan fail alarm
  • Heater element alarm (monitors minimum temp)
  • Over heat alarm (maximum temp sensor)
  • Circuit mal-operation alarm
  • Under temperature alarm

Sensors

  • Incubator air temperature sensor: Detects air temp in incubator.
  • Relative humidity sensor: Covered with wet nonwoven fabric to detect evaporation heat.
  • Wall temperature sensor: Detects temp affected by radiation.
  • Over-temperature sensor: Independent circuit detecting excessive heat.
  • Air flow sensor: Detects internal circulation changes.
  • Skin temperature sensor: Attached to infant's skin.

üîß Maintenance Procedures

Daily Maintenance

  • Cleaning: Wipe dust off exterior, remove tape/paper/foreign bodies. Cover after checks.
  • Visual checks: Check all fittings and accessories are mounted correctly.
  • Function checks: Drain water tray. Run machine for 30 mins to dry tray. Refill with sterile water just before re-use.

Weekly Maintenance

  • Cleaning: Unplug, clean outside with damp cloth, dry off. Remove dirt from wheels. Wash/replace air filters.
  • Visual checks: Check mains plug screws, cable for damage, doors, and tray. Repair if needed.
  • Function checks: Check all controls. Verify readings of thermometer and oxygen sensors change when breathed upon. Check batteries.
