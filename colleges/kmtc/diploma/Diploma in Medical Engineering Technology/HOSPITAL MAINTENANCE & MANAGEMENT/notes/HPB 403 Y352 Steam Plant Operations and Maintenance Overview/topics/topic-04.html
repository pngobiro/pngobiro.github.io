üí• Water Hammer

WATER HAMMER:
→ Is an hydraulic shock or a pressure surge or a wave caused when a fluid (liquid or gas) is accelerated in motion is abruptly forced to stop suddenly due to an obstacle like (Tee joint, elbow or a closed valve).

Causes of water hammer

  1. Excessive condensate in the pipeline.
  2. Excessive velocity of steam.
  3. Obstacles in the pipeline e.g. (Tee, elbow and a closed valve)
  4. Pipe bords
  5. Pipe surging

Impact / effects of water hammer

  1. Rapturing pipe fittings
  2. Causing valve failures
  3. Break pipe welds and rapturing piping systems
  4. Causing failure of pipe support
  5. over stressing pressure gauges.
  6. Cracking steam trap bodies.

Prevention of water hammer

  1. Use of steam traps to discharge condensate.
  2. Avoid pipe surging.
  3. Avoid excessive steam velocity
  4. Install pressure regulators.
  5. Tighten hose pipes.

üöø Steam Traps Overview

Venturi-Nozzle steam traps → have no moving parts and operate by continuously removing condensate from the steam system.
→ The operation of the trap is based on the difference in density between water and steam.

STEAM TRAPS → is a type of automatic valve that filters out condensate and non-condensible gases and air and trapping live steam.

→ The purpose of installing steam traps is to obtain a fast heating of the product and equipment by keeping the steam lines and equipment free of condensate, air and other non-condensible gases.

→ A steam trap is a valve device that discharges condensate and air from the steam line without discharging steam (while trapping steam).

Functions of steam traps

  1. To discharge condensate as soon as it is formed.
  2. To trap not to allow steam to escape
  3. To discharge air from the steam line
  4. To discharge other non-condensible gases from the steam line.

üß∞ Types of Steam Traps

→ Steam traps are classified into three major classifications:

  • a) Thermostatic steam traps (temperature) e.g. bimetallic traps
  • b) Mechanical steam traps (density) e.g. inverted buckets and ball floats
  • c) Thermodynamic steam traps. (pressure) e.g., Disk steam traps, piston traps, Impulse traps, Orifice traps.

a) Thermostatic steam traps

→ These are steam traps which are operated by changes in fluid-temperature.
→ In the steam line, the temp of live steam is a bit higher than the temp of condensate, air and other non-condensable gases.
→ A thermostatic valve will discharge condensate because of its low temperature but trap steam because of its high temperature.
→ An example of thermostatic trap is bimetallic steam traps.

i) Bimetallic steam trap

→ It operates on the same principle as a thermostat.
→ It bends when subjected to a change in temp and when properly calibrated the valve closes against its seat when steam is present and opens when condensate, air and other non-condensable gases are present.

b) Mechanical steam traps

$\Rightarrow$ Are operated by changes in fluid density i.e. by sensing the difference in the steam and the condensate.
$\Rightarrow$ They include ball float steam traps, inverted buckets, open buckets.

i) Ball float steam traps

$\Rightarrow$ In this trap, the ball rises in the presence of condensate, opening valve hence discharging the condensate, and the ball falls when steam is in the system, closing the valve and trapping it.

→ In figure a) below condensate reaching the trap will cause the ball to float/rise, lifting the valve off its seat and discharging Condensate.
→ Float and thermostatic traps have an additional air vent or air cock to allow the trap to discharge air from the system.
→ The automatic air vent uses the same balanced pressure element inside the trap and is located in the steam space above the Condensate level.
→ It is also able to discharge other non-condensable gases.

Operation of ball float and thermostatic steam trap

→ During the boiler start-up the level of condensate is high as compared to steam, as the condensate gets into the steam trap, due to its high density. It will create a force that lifts the ball float thereby opening the valve discharging the Condensate.
→ At the same time due to the low temp of the Condensate the air cock will contract opening the air vent and discharging air and other non-condensable gases.
→ In (b) below, when steam builds up in the system, due to its low density which does not have enough force to keep the ball lifted, the ball float will fall thereby closing the valve and trapping steam, at the same time due high temp of steam the air cock will expand closing the air vent and steam will not escape.

Inverted bucket steam trap

As its name implies uses a mechanism of an inverted bucket which is attached by a lever to a valve. Since it is a mechanical type of steam traps, it uses difference in fluid density to discharge condensate and trap steam.

In figure @1 below the bucket hangs down pulling the valve off its seat. Condensate flows under the bucket filling the body and causing it to pass through the outlet because condensate has got no enough buoyancy to lift up the bucket.

In figure @2 the arrival of the steam causes the bucket to become buoyant, the bucket then rises up and the valve is shut by the valve closure hence blocking the steam from passing through the outlet.

‚öñÔ∏è Comparison & Selection

Trap Type Advantages Disadvantages
Inverted Bucket
  • Can withstand high pressure
  • Can resist water hammer
  • Can be used in superheated steam lines.
  • The failure mode is usually open.
  • Due to the trap lacking air vent it is not able to discharge air and other non-condensible gases efficiently.
  • Requires manual priming to provide water seal.
  • Does not withstand freezing temperatures.
Float & Thermostatic
  • Able to discharge air and other non-condensible gases.
  • Resistant to water hammer
  • Applied where steam lock can occur.
  • It has large capacity
  • Can be damaged by severe freezing, therefore the body should be well lagged / insulated.
Bimetallic
  • Can resist water hammer
  • Can not handle heavy condensate load
  • Can not discharge air efficiently.
Disc (Thermodynamic)
  • Resistant to water hammer.
  • Has a large capacity.
  • Can not discharge air efficiently.

Factors to consider in selecting a steam trap

  • The discharge capacity of the steam trap.
  • The highest pressure it can withstand.
  • The highest temperature it can withstand.
  • The type of steam produced by the plant
  • Initial cost and maintenance cost.
  • Overall efficiency of the trap.

üõ°Ô∏è Risk Reduction Strategies

Reducing the risks of water hammer in steam distribution System at the design stage

  1. Drainage → Avoid water hammer completely by draining out condensate as soon as they are formed.
  2. Steam trap → Install the right type and capacity steam trap to remove entire condensate.
  3. Steam quality → Improve steam quality, keeping steam as dry as possible to avoid condensate formation.
  4. Stem and water velocities → Do not allow steam - Velocities to become excessive as a result of system - modification. The higher the velocity the higher the force of impact.
  5. Piping → Correct any occurrences of pipe suggings, wet or damaged insulation that could cause condensate accumulation and exceed steam trap capacities.
  6. Air heating coils → These units must accommodate both condensate removal and air venting to prevent hammer.
  7. Avoid dangerous mix up of high pressure steam with sub cooled condensate.
  8. Make sure to boiler operates correctly under all conditions, without forming or carryover.
  9. Ensure start up and shut down procedures are strictly followed.
  10. Install pressure regulators to regulate pressure in the system.
  11. Tighten lose rate to avoid blocking of pipes that could cause water hammer.
