Bearings provide supports/stability for rotating members/shafts as well as permit free and smooth motion. Bearings are classified according to the direction of pressure with respect to the axis of the shaft.

Sliding Contact Bearings

Journal bearings, Pivot bearings, and Collar bearings are sliding contact type bearings in which the rotating shaft has sliding contact with the bearing and the friction between the two is relatively high.

Journal Bearings

The load on the bearing is perpendicular to the axis of the shaft. In solid and bushed bearings, the shaft can be inserted endwise only.

üîπ Solid Bearing

This simplest form of journal bearing is made of cast-iron and consists of a cylindrical block with a rectangular base.

  • A hole is bored in the cylindrical part equal to the diameter of the shaft.
  • Rectangular base-plate has two holes for bolting down.
  • An oil-hole is drilled at the top for lubrication.
  • Note: No provision for wear adjustment. Used for slow speeds and light loads.
üîπ Bushed Bearing

A modified version of the solid bearing, consisting mainly of two parts: the body and the bush.

  • Lined with a hollow bush of gun-metal or brass.
  • Bush is pressed inside and checked from sliding/rotating by a dowel-pin or grub-screw.
  • When the bush wears out, it is easily replaced.
üîπ Pedestal Bearing (Plummer Block)

Consists of cast-iron pedestal, gun-metal brasses (two halves), cast-iron cap, and mild-steel bolts.

  • Made in two halves for easy placing/removal of shaft.
  • Allows adjustment for wear in the brasses.
  • Rotary motion of bush checked by a snug at the bottom.
  • Square-headed bolts prevent rotation during fastening.

Pivot Bearing

The pressure is parallel to the axis of the shaft and the end of the shaft rests on the bearing surface.

Foot Step Bearing

Supports the lower end of a vertical shaft.

  • Block: Cast-iron block with a sole.
  • Bush: Gun-metal bush with a collar at the top (hollow for oil cup).
  • Disc: Concave steel disc supporting the shaft end.
  • Pin: Prevents disc from rotating.
  • Snug: Prevents bush from rotating.

Collar Bearing

The pressure is parallel to the axis of the shaft which is passed and extended through the bearing.

üí°

Key Feature

In a collar bearing, the shaft continues through and beyond the bearing, unlike the foot step bearing where it terminates.

  • Shaft may be vertical or horizontal.
  • Can use single collar or multi collars.
  • Collars are integral or rigidly fastened.
  • Used when foot step bearing is not practicable.
