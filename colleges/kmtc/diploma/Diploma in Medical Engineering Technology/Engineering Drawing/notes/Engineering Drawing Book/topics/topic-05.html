üìå Important Terms & Planes

Understanding the planes of projection is fundamental to orthographic drawing.

Horizontal Plane (HP)

A plane parallel to the floor is known as the horizontal plane. Orthographic projection of an object on the horizontal plane is known as Top View (Plan).

Vertical Plane (VP)

One plane conveniently selected out of the planes perpendicular to the horizontal plane is known as the vertical plane. The projection on this plane is known as Front View (Elevation).

Auxiliary Vertical Plane (AVP)

A plane perpendicular to both the horizontal plane and vertical plane is known as the auxiliary vertical plane. Orthographic projection on A.V.P. is known as End View or Side View.

Projection Definitions

  • Projection: If straight lines are drawn from various points on the contour of an object to meet a plane, the object is said to be projected at that plane. The figure formed by joining in correct sequence the points at which the lines meet the plane is called a projection of the object.
  • Projectors: The lines from the object to the plane are called projectors.
  • Plane of projection: The plane on which the projectors meet is known as the plane of projection.
  • Orthographic projection: When the projectors are parallel to each other and also perpendicular to the projection plane, the projection is called orthographic projection.

üîÑ First vs. Third Angle Projection

Characteristic First Angle Projection Third Angle Projection
Object Position Object is kept in the first quadrant. Object is assumed to be kept in the third quadrant.
Relationship The object lies between the observer and the plane of projection. The plane of projection lies between the observer and the object.
Plane Transparency Plane is assumed to be non-transparent. Plane is assumed to be transparent.
View Arrangement Plan comes below the elevation and left side view is drawn to the right of elevation. Plan comes above the elevation and left side view is drawn to the left side of the elevation.
Usage Now recommended in India. Used in USA and other countries.

üßä Multiview Projection

Multiview projection represents three dimensional objects in three separate views arranged in a standard manner. Each view represents only two dimensions.

üí°

The Glass Box Concept

Imagine the object suspended inside a glass box. The views are projected onto the sides of the box.

Opening the Box

To obtain multiviews on a flat sheet of paper, the glass box is "unfolded".

üèóÔ∏è Object Features

Recognizing how different features appear in orthographic views is essential.

Edges

Lines that represent the boundary between two faces.

Corners

Represent the intersection of two or more edges.

Surfaces

Areas that are bounded by edges or limiting elements.

Limiting Element

A line that represents the last visible part of a curve surface.
