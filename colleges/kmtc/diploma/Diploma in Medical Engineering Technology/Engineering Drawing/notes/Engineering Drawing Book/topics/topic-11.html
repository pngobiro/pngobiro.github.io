Keys, Cotter Joints & Pin Joints

üìã Introduction

Keys, cotter-joints, and pin-joints are employed as removable or temporary fasteners.

  • Keys are employed to secure gears, pulleys, discs, flange and other similar parts to shafts or axles.
  • Cotter-joints and pin joints are used for rods in tensile or compressive load.
üí°

Key Distinction

The cotter-joint does not permit any axial mis-alignment as it is a rigid-joint.
The pin-joint permits angular mis-alignment as it is a flexible-joint.

02

üóùÔ∏è Key Joints

A key is employed to connect two pieces, like shaft and a gear, in such a way that there is no relative rotational movement between the shaft and the parts mounted on it. Key is always made of steel because it is subjected to shearing and torsional stresses.

It is inserted in the axial direction between the shaft and the boss or hub of the mating piece. A keyway is the groove cut in the shaft as well as in the hub, to accommodate the key.

Taper Keys

It is tapered (standard taper is $1 \text{ in } 100$) in thickness but uniform in width. A taper key joint prevents relative rotational as well as axial movement.

1. Sunk Taper Key

Standard form (square or rectangular). Half part is sunk in the shaft keyway, remaining half fits in the hub.

2. Saddle Keys

Hollow: Underside hollow to fit curved shaft surface.
Flat: Inserted on a flat surface provided on the shaft.

3. Round Key (Pin Key)

Circular cross-section, usually tapered, inserted in a hole drilled partly in the shaft and partly in the hub.

4. Gib-head

A taper key provided with a head to facilitate its removal.

Parallel or Feather Keys

Used when there is a requirement of sliding or axial movement of the mating piece on the shaft. It is a sunk key of uniform width and thickness.

  • May be rectangular, square, or dove-tail.
  • Secured to either the shaft (using cap-screws) or the mating piece.
  • Classified as peg feather key, single headed, and double headed feather key.

Woodruff Key

This is a sunk key in the form of a segmental part of a circular disc having uniform thickness.

  • Inserted into a circular keyway cut in the shaft.
  • Projecting part fits in a keyway inside the mating piece.
  • Advantage: Once placed, the key tilts to align itself on the tapered shaft.
03

üî© Cotter Joints

A cotter is a flat wedge-shaped piece of rectangular cross section made of steel. It is used to connect two rods rigidly subjected to compressive or tensile forces and is inserted at $90^{\circ}$ to the axes of the rods.

‚ö†Ô∏è

Limitation

This joint is not suitable for connecting rotating members.

Types of Cotter Joints

1. Cotter joint with Socket and Spigot end

A socket is formed by expanding the end of one rod in which the spigot-end of the other rod is inserted. A cotter is driven tightly through the slots which are kept slightly misaligned.

Note: Clearances are absolutely required for proper functioning of the joint.

2. Sleeve Cotter Joint

The simplest form of cotter joint used for fastening two circular rods. The ends of the two rods are inserted from opposite sides in a cylindrical sleeve or steel box. Two cotters are inserted through slots.

04

üîó Pin-Joint (Knuckle Joint)

The joint is used for rods whose axes intersect (may not be in alignment). It is employed in tension or compression. The joint permits angular movement between the rods, therefore it is not rigid.

Construction

  • 1 One end of a rod is formed in a single-eye end.
  • 2 It is placed within a double-eye end or forked end of the other rod.
  • 3 A cylindrical pin is inserted through holes of single and double eye.
  • 4 The pin is kept in position by means of a collar and a taper pin.

The rods are quite free to swivel on the cylindrical pin.
