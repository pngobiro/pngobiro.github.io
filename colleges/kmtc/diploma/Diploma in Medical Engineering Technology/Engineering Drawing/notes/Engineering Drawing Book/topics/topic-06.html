Sectional Views

01

ü§î Why Sectional Views?

Invisible features of an object are typically shown by means of hidden lines in their projected views. However, when such lines are too many, they make the views more complicated and difficult to interpret.

üí°

The Solution

In such cases, it is customary to imagine the object as being cut through by a plane. The part of the object between the plane and the observer is assumed to be removed to reveal internal details.

02

üìñ Important Terms

Understanding the standard terminology is essential for creating correct sectional views.

Cutting Plane (or Section Plane)

The imaginary plane by which the object is assumed to be cut through. It is assumed to be parallel to the plane on which the view is projected.

Section

The surface produced by cutting an object by the section plane is called the section.

Sectional View

The projection of the section along with the remaining part of the object is called the sectional view.

Cutting Plane Line

The position of the cutting plane is indicated by this line. It is an edge view of the cutting plane.

  • The direction of viewing the section is shown by arrows resting on the cutting plane line.
  • The sight arrows are always perpendicular to the cutting plane.
  • It is designated by capital letters (e.g., A-A).
03

‚úèÔ∏è Section Lining (Hatching)

Section lines or cross-hatch lines are used to indicate the surfaces that are cut by the cutting plane.

üìê

Drafting Rules

  • The section lines should be drawn evenly spaced inclined at $45^{\circ}$ to the axis or to the main outline of the section.
  • They can also be drawn at $30^{\circ}$ or $60^{\circ}$ if necessary.
  • Section lines should be drawn with a 2H or 3H pencil.
  • The spaces between lines may vary from 1.5 mm for small sections to 3 mm for large sections.

Material Symbols

The section lines are different for each material type. However, for practical purposes, the cast iron symbol is used most often for general materials.

üîÑ Types of Section Views

Common types include Full sections, Half sections, Revolved sections, Removed sections, and Offset sections.

üîµ Full Section View

The view is made by passing the straight cutting plane completely through the part. The front half is assumed to be removed.

‚ö†Ô∏è

Important Rule

Hidden lines become visible in a section view.

üåì Half Section View

The object is assumed to be cut by two cutting planes at right angles to each other and containing two center lines of the object. One quarter of the object between two planes is removed.

  • A center line is used to separate the sectioned half from the unsectioned half of the view.
  • Hidden lines are omitted in the unsectioned half of the view.
