Muff Coupling

It consists a sleeve (muff) of cast iron fitted over the ends of the shaft which are to be connected. The sunk key is inserted in the key ways in the shafts and sleeve.

  • Box or Butt-muff coupling: Two shaft's ends butt against each other.
  • Half-lap coupling: Shaft's ends are tapered and overlap each other.
  • Split-muff coupling: Muff is split in two semi-cylindrical halves joined by bolts and nuts.
Flanged Coupling

This is an extensively used and standard form of coupling. Two cast-iron flanges are either fitted or provided at the ends of the two shafts and fastened together by means of a number of bolts and nuts.

  • Protected type: Flange provided with a shroud to shelter bolt-heads/nuts for safety.
  • Solid flanged: Flanges are forged solid integral with the both shafts.

A flexible coupling permits relative rotation and variation in the alignment of shafts axes within some limit.

  • 1 Bushed Pin type flanged coupling: Modified version of protected flange coupling where bolts are replaced by bushed pins fastened to one flange and kept loose in the other.
Universal Coupling

Used to couple two shafts whose axes intersect if extended. Two forks are pin-jointed to a center block. The angle between shafts may be varied even during rotary motion.

Oldham's Coupling

Used to connect two parallel shafts whose axes are at a small distance apart and not in alignment. Uses a central circular disc between two flanges.

With the help of this type of coupling, the shafts may be coupled together or disengaged when required, even while they may be in rotary motion.

Claw Coupling

A disengaging type of coupling, generally used for slow-speed shafts. One flange is fixed, the other slides. Claws engage/fit into corresponding recesses.

Cone Coupling

Consists of male cone, female cone, and keys. Operated similarly to claw coupling. The shafts are coupled by virtue of friction between the two conical surfaces.
