Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions

20 Marks
1.
A triac has three terminals namely:
a. Drain, source and gate
b. Two main terminals and a gate terminal
c. Cathode, Anode and gate
d. None of the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b. Two main terminals and a gate terminal

A TRIAC (Triode for Alternating Current) has three terminals: Main Terminal 1 (MT1), Main Terminal 2 (MT2), and Gate (G). It is bidirectional, unlike an SCR which has Anode/Cathode.

2.
Bistable circuit is also known as ____
a. Flip flop
b. Latch
c. Amplifier
d. Oscillator (sinusoidal)
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a. Flip flop

A bistable multivibrator has two stable states and requires a trigger to switch between them. This is the definition of a Flip-flop (or latch, though Flip-flop is the specific clocked term often used interchangeably in MCQs).

3.
Circuits which produce an output wave shape resembling that of a symmetrical or a symmetrical square wave are called:
a. Time generators
b. Frequency generators
c. Sinusoidal oscillators
d. Multivibrators
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d. Multivibrators

Multivibrators are non-sinusoidal oscillators that generate rectangular or square waveforms (relaxation oscillators).

4.
A stable vibrator contains:
a. One quasi state
b. Two quasi states
c. Three quasi states
d. Four quasi states
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b. Two quasi states

Correction: The question likely refers to an Astable Multivibrator (based on the options provided). An astable multivibrator has NO stable states, but two quasi-stable (temporary) states between which it oscillates.

5.
A UJT is sometimes referred to as:
a. Low resistance
b. High resistance
c. Single base
d. Double base
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d. Double base

A Unijunction Transistor (UJT) is often called a "Double Base Diode" because it has one emitter and two base terminals (B1 and B2).

6.
A diac (DIAC) is turned on by
a. Both gate voltage and gate current
b. Gate voltage
c. Gate current
d. Breakover voltage
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d. Breakover voltage

A DIAC is a two-terminal device with no gate. It turns on (conducts) only when the voltage across it exceeds its breakover voltage ($V_{BO}$).

7.
The device that exhibits negative resistance region is:
a. UJT
b. Triac
c. DIAC
d. SCR
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a. UJT

While some other devices can show negative resistance effects, the UJT is famously characterized by its negative resistance region in its I-V curve, which is utilized for relaxation oscillators.

8.
A DIAC is simply:
a. A single junction device
b. A three junction device
c. Triac without gate terminal
d. None of the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c. Triac without gate terminal

Structurally and functionally, a DIAC resembles a TRIAC without a gate electrode. It is a bidirectional trigger diode.

9.
After peak-point, UJT operates in ____ region.
a. A cut-off
b. Negative resistance.
c. Saturator
d. None of the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b. Negative resistance

When the emitter voltage reaches the peak point, the UJT turns on and enters the negative resistance region where voltage decreases as current increases.

10.
If a square wave is fed to a differentiating circuit, the output will be:
a. Sharp narrow pulses
b. Triangular wave
c. Saw tooth wave
d. Sine wave
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a. Sharp narrow pulses

A differentiator circuit produces an output proportional to the rate of change of the input. Since a square wave changes instantly (high $dv/dt$) at the edges and is constant elsewhere (zero $dv/dt$), the output consists of sharp spikes (impulses) at the edges.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions

60 Marks
1.

a) Using a diagram, describe how a bistable multivibrator works. (10 marks)

b) State and explain four features of laser light. (8 marks)

Show Answer

Answer:

(a) Bistable Multivibrator (Flip-Flop) Operation:

Circuit: Consists of two transistors (Q1 and Q2) cross-coupled such that the output of one collector is connected to the base of the other via a resistor.

Working:

  • Stable State 1 (Q1 ON, Q2 OFF): If Q1 is conducting (saturated), its collector voltage is low (~0.2V). This low voltage is applied to the base of Q2, keeping Q2 cutoff (OFF). Since Q2 is OFF, its collector voltage is high ($V_{CC}$). This high voltage drives the base of Q1, keeping Q1 ON. The circuit remains in this state indefinitely.
  • Triggering: Applying a negative pulse to the base of Q1 (or positive to Q2) will cut off Q1. Q1 collector rises to $V_{CC}$, turning ON Q2.
  • Stable State 2 (Q1 OFF, Q2 ON): Now Q2 is saturated and Q1 is cutoff. This is the second stable state.
  • The circuit "flips" and "flops" between these two states only when triggered.

(b) Features of Laser Light:

  1. Coherence: The light waves are in phase with each other in both space and time.
  2. Monochromaticity: The light consists of a single specific wavelength (color) or a very narrow bandwidth.
  3. Directionality (Collimation): The beam has very low divergence and travels in a straight line over long distances.
  4. High Intensity (Brightness): The energy is concentrated in a very small spatial area.
2.

a) Explain two ways how SCR can be triggered to conduction. (6 marks)

b) Explain how solar cells work / photo voltaic cells work using diagram. (14 marks)

Show Answer

Answer:

(a) SCR Triggering Methods:

  1. Gate Triggering: Applying a positive voltage pulse to the Gate terminal relative to the Cathode while the Anode is positive. This injects carriers into the P-layer, starting regenerative feedback that turns the device ON. This is the standard control method.
  2. Forward Voltage Triggering (Breakover): Increasing the Anode-Cathode voltage beyond the forward breakover voltage ($V_{BO}$). The high electric field causes avalanche breakdown at the reverse-biased junction J2, initiating conduction.

(b) Solar Cell Working (Photovoltaic Effect):

Diagram: Shows a P-N junction with light rays hitting the N-type surface. Depletion layer indicated.

Principle:

  • Absorption: Photons from sunlight strike the solar cell. If photon energy is greater than the bandgap energy of the semiconductor (Si), electron-hole pairs are generated.
  • Separation: The built-in electric field at the P-N junction depletion region separates these carriers. Electrons are swept to the N-side and holes to the P-side.
  • Collection: This accumulation creates a potential difference (Open Circuit Voltage). The front contact collects electrons and the back contact collects holes.
  • Current Flow: When an external load is connected, electrons flow from the N-side through the load to the P-side, generating electric current (DC).
3.

a) State four ways in which loses occur in optical fibre. (4 marks)

b) State five ideal features of opamps. (5 marks)

c) Draw a block diagram of regulated power supply and explain function of each block. (11 marks)

Show Answer

Answer:

(a) Fiber Losses:

  1. Absorption: Intrinsic absorption by glass material and extrinsic absorption by impurities (OH ions).
  2. Rayleigh Scattering: Scattering of light due to microscopic density variations in the glass.
  3. Macrobending: Loss due to large radius bends in the fiber.
  4. Microbending: Loss due to microscopic imperfections in fiber geometry.
  5. Coupling/Connector Losses: Losses at splices and connections (Fresnel reflection).

(b) Ideal Op-Amp Features:

  1. Infinite Open Loop Gain ($A_{vol} = \infty$).
  2. Infinite Input Impedance ($Z_{in} = \infty$).
  3. Zero Output Impedance ($Z_{out} = 0$).
  4. Infinite Bandwidth (flawless amplification at all frequencies).
  5. Zero Input Offset Voltage (0V input gives 0V output).

(c) Regulated Power Supply:

Block Diagram Order: Transformer $\rightarrow$ Rectifier $\rightarrow$ Filter $\rightarrow$ Regulator $\rightarrow$ Load.

Functions:

  • Transformer: Steps down the high voltage AC mains (e.g., 240V) to a lower voltage AC required for the circuit.
  • Rectifier: Converts the AC voltage into pulsating DC voltage (e.g., Bridge rectifier).
  • Filter: Smoothes out the pulsations (ripples) in the rectified DC to produce a cleaner DC voltage (using capacitors/inductors).
  • Voltage Regulator: Stabilizes the DC output voltage, keeping it constant regardless of variations in input voltage or load current (e.g., Zener diode or IC regulator).
