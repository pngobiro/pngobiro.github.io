Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions

20 Marks
1.
Diacs are primary used as
a) Pulse generators
b) Triggering devices
c) Surge protection devices
d) Power thyristor
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Triggering devices

DIACs (Diode for Alternating Current) are bidirectional diodes specifically designed to trigger TRIACs and SCRs in phase control circuits due to their symmetrical breakdown characteristics.

2.
The device processing the highest sensitivity is a
a) Photo conductive cell
b) Photo voltaic cell
c) Photo diode
d) Photo transistor
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Photo conductive cell (LDR)

While Phototransistors have gain ($h_{FE}$) making them more sensitive than Photodiodes, Photoconductive cells (LDRs) typically exhibit the largest change in parameter (resistance) for a given light level, often considered having high sensitivity in general applications, though slow. However, in many academic contexts, Photoconductive cells are cited for high sensitivity.

3.
A multivibrator that generates wave of its own is called
a) Monostable multivibrator
b) Bistable multivibrator
c) Astable multivibrator
d) Unijunction transistor
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Astable multivibrator

An astable multivibrator is a "free-running" oscillator that generates a continuous square wave output without needing an external trigger pulse.

4.
A voltage multiplier is a circuit that
a) Amplifier voltage
b) Raises the peak input voltage to 2, 3, 4 times
c) Changes the shape of input voltage
d) Cuts the output voltage or current
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Raises the peak input voltage to 2, 3, 4 times

Voltage multipliers (Doublers, Triplers, Quadruplers) use diodes and capacitors to clamp and rectify AC voltage to produce a high DC output voltage that is an integer multiple of the peak input voltage.

5.
The output of an active positive clamper is
a) Positive
b) Negative
c) Either positive or negative
d) A ramp
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Positive

A positive clamper shifts the entire waveform upwards so that the negative peak sits on the zero reference level (or reference voltage). Thus, the resulting waveform is predominantly positive (above ground).

6.
What is the need to achieve population inversion?
a) To bring most of the atoms to ground state
b) To excite most of the atoms
c) To achieve the time of laser production
d) To reduce the time of laser production
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) To excite most of the atoms

Population inversion creates a state where more atoms are in the higher energy (excited) state than in the lower energy state. This is essential for stimulated emission to exceed absorption, allowing light amplification (lasing).

7.
Which of the following is a unique property of laser
a) Directional
b) Wave length
c) Coherence
d) Speed
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Coherence

While other sources can be directional (with optics) or have specific wavelengths, Coherence (spatial and temporal phase consistency) is the most distinct and difficult-to-replicate property of laser light.

8.
The frequency of the absorbed or emitted radiation is related to the difference in energy between the two states and is given by $E=E_2 - E_1 = hf$. What does h stand for?
a) Gravitational constant
b) Permittivity
c) Attenuation constant
d) Plank's constant
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Plank's constant

Planck's constant ($h \approx 6.626 \times 10^{-34} J\cdot s$) relates the energy of a photon to its frequency.

9.
Acceptable angle is defined as the ______ angle of incidence at the end face of an optical fibre for which the ray can be propagated in the optical fibre.
a) Maximum
b) Minimum
c) Either maximum or minimum
d) None of the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Maximum

The acceptance angle is the maximum angle relative to the fiber axis at which light can enter the core and still be guided by Total Internal Reflection.

10.
As compared to LED displays, the distinct advantage of LCD display is that it requires
a) No illumination
b) Extremely low power
c) No forward bias
d) A solid crystal
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Extremely low power

LCDs (Liquid Crystal Displays) control light transmission (modulating external light) rather than emitting it, consuming very little power (microwatts) compared to LEDs which require current to generate light (milliwatts).

Section 3: Long Answer Questions

60 Marks
1.

a) Define the following terms: (5 marks)

i) Turn on time

ii) Pulse width

iii) Rise time

iv) Mark to space ratio (MSR)

v) Pulse repetition frequency

b) Name and draw the three different energy transition diagrams involved in laser action. (6 marks)

c) Explain the construction and working of phototransistor and mention its applications. (9 marks)

Show Answer

Answer:

(a) Definitions:

  • i) Turn on time ($t_{on}$): Sum of delay time and rise time. Time required for output to go from OFF state to ON state (reach 90%).
  • ii) Pulse width ($t_p$): The time duration between the 50% amplitude points of the rising and falling edges of the pulse.
  • iii) Rise time ($t_r$): Time taken for the signal to rise from 10% to 90% of its maximum amplitude.
  • iv) Mark to Space Ratio (MSR): Ratio of the pulse width (Mark/ON time) to the space width (OFF time). $MSR = T_{on} / T_{off}$.
  • v) Pulse Repetition Frequency (PRF): The number of pulses per second. $PRF = 1/T$, where T is the total period.

(b) Energy Transitions:

  1. Stimulated Absorption: Atom in ground state ($E_1$) absorbs a photon and moves to excited state ($E_2$).
  2. Spontaneous Emission: Atom in excited state ($E_2$) randomly drops to $E_1$, emitting a photon.
  3. Stimulated Emission: Incident photon hits excited atom, causing it to drop to $E_1$ and emit a second identical photon (Amplification).

(c) Phototransistor:

  • Construction: Similar to a normal BJT (NPN or PNP) but the base-collector junction is exposed to light through a lens window. The base terminal is often left open (unconnected).
  • Working: It operates in the active region. Light falling on the reverse-biased Base-Collector junction generates electron-hole pairs (photocurrent). This current acts as the base current. The transistor amplifies this current by a factor of $\beta$ (current gain). $I_C \approx \beta \times I_{ph}$.
  • Applications: Optical isolators, light detectors, position sensors, smoke detectors.
2.

a) Explain the following methods which are used in triggering of an SCR: (8 marks)

i) Thermal triggering

ii) Radiation triggering

iii) Voltage triggering

iv) dv/dt triggering

b) A hospital ward has a 12 V battery which supplied a continuous current of 0.5 A to various loads. Solar cells are used to keep the battery charged. The solar cells are illuminated by the sun for 2 hours in every 24 hours. If during exposure, each cell gives 0.5 V at 50 mA, determine the number of cells required with aid of a circuit diagram. Assume the battery charging voltage is 13.5 V. (8 marks)

c) State any four applications of unijunction transmitter (UJT). (4 marks)

Show Answer

Answer:

(a) Triggering Methods:

  • i) Thermal Triggering: As temperature increases, the leakage current increases. This can initiate regenerative action, turning the SCR on (usually undesirable/runaway).
  • ii) Radiation (Light) Triggering: Light (photons) entering the junction creates electron-hole pairs, increasing leakage current to trigger the device (used in LASCRs).
  • iii) Voltage Triggering (Breakover): Increasing forward voltage $V_{AK}$ beyond the breakover voltage $V_{BO}$ causes avalanche breakdown at J2, turning the SCR on.
  • iv) dv/dt Triggering: A rapid rise in anode voltage causes capacitive charging current ($i = C \cdot dv/dt$) across the junction J2, which acts as a gate current and triggers the SCR.

(b) Solar Cell Calculation:

Assumption: "Continuous current" means 0.5A is drawn 24h/day (e.g., critical equipment). Sun available only 2h.

  • Load Requirements:
    • Total Load (Charge) per day = $I \times t = 0.5 \text{ A} \times 24 \text{ h} = 12 \text{ Ah}$.
  • Charging Requirements:
    • Available Sun Time = 2 hours.
    • Required Charging Current ($I_{charge}$) = $\frac{\text{Total Charge}}{\text{Time}} = \frac{12 \text{ Ah}}{2 \text{ h}} = 6 \text{ A}$.
    • Required Charging Voltage = 13.5 V (given).
  • Solar Array Design:
    • Cells in Series (for Voltage): $\frac{\text{Required Voltage}}{\text{Cell Voltage}} = \frac{13.5 \text{ V}}{0.5 \text{ V}} = 27 \text{ cells}$.
    • Cells in Parallel (for Current): $\frac{\text{Required Current}}{\text{Cell Current}} = \frac{6 \text{ A}}{50 \text{ mA}} = \frac{6}{0.05} = 120 \text{ branches}$.
    • Total Cells: $27 \times 120 = 3240 \text{ cells}$.

(c) UJT Applications:

  • Relaxation Oscillators (Sawtooth generators).
  • Triggering SCRs and TRIACs (Phase control).
  • Timing circuits.
  • Pulse generation.
3.

a) With help of a diagram, explain the operation of a full wave voltage doubler. (8 marks)

b) A given silicon unijunction transistor has an interbase resistance of $10 k\Omega$. It has $R_{B1}=6 k\Omega$ with emitter current $I_E=0$. Calculate:

i) UJT current if $V_{BB}=20V$ and $V_E$ is less than $V_P$

ii) Intrinsic standoff ratio

iii) Voltage drop across $R_{B1}$

iv) Peak point voltage

c) With a neat characteristic curve, explain how a transistor is used as a switch. (7 marks)

Show Answer

Answer:

(a) Full Wave Voltage Doubler:

Circuit consists of two diodes and two capacitors.

  • Positive Half Cycle: D1 conducts, charging C1 to peak voltage $V_m$.
  • Negative Half Cycle: D2 conducts, charging C2 to peak voltage $V_m$.
  • Output: Taken across the series combination of C1 and C2. $V_{out} = V_{C1} + V_{C2} = V_m + V_m = 2V_m$.

(b) UJT Calculations:

Given: $R_{BB} = 10k\Omega, R_{B1} = 6k\Omega, V_{BB} = 20V$. (Implies $R_{B2} = 4k\Omega$).

  • i) Current ($I_{B2}$): With $I_E=0$ (Off state), the UJT acts as a resistor divider.
    $I = \frac{V_{BB}}{R_{BB}} = \frac{20V}{10k\Omega} = 2 \text{ mA}$.
  • ii) Intrinsic Standoff Ratio ($\eta$):
    $\eta = \frac{R_{B1}}{R_{BB}} = \frac{6k}{10k} = 0.6$.
  • iii) Voltage Drop across $R_{B1}$ ($V_A$ or $V_{RB1}$):
    $V_{RB1} = \eta V_{BB} = 0.6 \times 20V = 12 \text{ V}$.
  • iv) Peak Point Voltage ($V_P$):
    $V_P = \eta V_{BB} + V_D$ (Assume Diode drop $V_D \approx 0.7V$).
    $V_P = 12V + 0.7V = 12.7 \text{ V}$.

(c) Transistor as a Switch:

Using the Output Characteristics ($I_C$ vs $V_{CE}$):

  • Cut-off Region (OFF): Base current $I_B = 0$. $I_C \approx 0$. $V_{CE} = V_{CC}$. Switch is OPEN.
  • Saturation Region (ON): Base current is high ($I_B > I_{C(sat)}/\beta$). $V_{CE} \approx 0$ (Saturation voltage). $I_C$ is max. Switch is CLOSED.
  • Load line connects $V_{CC}$ (Cut-off) and $V_{CC}/R_L$ (Saturation). Switching moves operation rapidly between these two points.
