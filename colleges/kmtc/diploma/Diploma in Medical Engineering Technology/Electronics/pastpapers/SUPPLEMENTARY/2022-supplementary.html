7.
A Phototransistor excels a Photodiode in the matter of

Correct Answer: c) greater sensitivity

A phototransistor has internal gain ($h_{FE}$), making it much more sensitive to light than a photodiode, meaning it produces more current for the same light level. However, it is generally slower (switching speed) than a photodiode.

2.

A given UJT has an interbase resistance of 10 K. It has $R_{B1}$ (corrected from text) of 6 K with $I_E=0$. Find,

a) UJT current if $V_{BB}=20 V$ and $V_E$ is less than $V_P$

b) $\eta$ and $V_A$

c) Peak point voltage $V_P$

Show Answer

Answer:

Given: $R_{BB} = 10 k\Omega$, $R_{B1} = 6 k\Omega$. Implies $R_{B2} = 4 k\Omega$.

(a) UJT Current ($I$):

With $I_E=0$ (OFF state), the UJT acts as a resistor.

$I = \frac{V_{BB}}{R_{BB}} = \frac{20V}{10k\Omega} = 2 \text{ mA}$.

(b) Intrinsic Standoff Ratio ($\eta$) and $V_A$:

$\eta = \frac{R_{B1}}{R_{BB}} = \frac{6k}{10k} = 0.6$.

$V_A$ (Voltage across $R_{B1}$) = $\eta V_{BB} = 0.6 \times 20V = 12 \text{ V}$.

(c) Peak Point Voltage ($V_P$):

$V_P = V_A + V_D = \eta V_{BB} + V_D$.

Assuming silicon diode drop $V_D \approx 0.7V$.

$V_P = 12V + 0.7V = 12.7 \text{ V}$.

3.

In figure 1.1 below, a relay is controlled by a photoconductive cell of 100 K when illuminated and 1 K when in dark (Text implies inverted logic or typo, standard LDR decreases resistance with light. Let's assume text meant 100K Dark, 1K Light for standard operation, OR 100K Illuminated/1K Dark if it's a specific type. Prompt text says "100 K when illuminated and 1 K when in dark". If we assume "De-energized when dark", we need low current in dark. If R is 1K in dark, current would be high! This suggests a typo in the question text. Standard: 100K Dark, 1K Light. Let's solve for: Relay on when illuminated (R=1k).

The relay is supplied with 10 mA from a 30 V supply when the cell is illuminated and is required to be de-energized when the cell is in the dark. Calculate the required series resistance R and the value of dark current. (5 Marks)

Show Answer

Answer:

Correction Assumption: LDR Resistance is 1 $k\Omega$ when Illuminated and 100 $k\Omega$ when Dark (Standard behavior).

1. Calculate Series Resistance R:

Condition: Relay ON when Illuminated ($I = 10 \text{ mA}$).

Total Resistance required $R_{total} = \frac{V}{I} = \frac{30V}{10mA} = 3 k\Omega$.

In illumination, LDR = 1 $k\Omega$.

$R_{total} = R + R_{LDR(light)} \Rightarrow 3k = R + 1k$.

$R = 2 k\Omega$.

2. Calculate Dark Current:

In dark, LDR = 100 $k\Omega$.

$R_{total(dark)} = R + R_{LDR(dark)} = 2k + 100k = 102 k\Omega$.

$I_{dark} = \frac{30V}{102k\Omega} \approx 0.29 \text{ mA}$.

(Since 0.29 mA is much less than 10 mA, the relay de-energizes).

Differentiate between a clipper and clamper circuits. Support your answer with a sketch.
Show Answer

Answer:

5.
Draw a sketch of an optical isolator and explain its operation. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

Sketch: Shows an LED on the input side and a Phototransistor (or Photodiode) on the output side, enclosed in a light-tight box with an insulating gap.

Operation:

  1. Input electrical signal drives the LED, causing it to emit infrared light.
  2. The light travels across the gap and strikes the base of the phototransistor.
  3. The phototransistor conducts current proportional to the light intensity.
  4. This regenerates the signal on the output side while maintaining electrical isolation between input (low voltage) and output (high voltage).
6.
List five losses in optical fiber communication. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Absorption Loss: Due to impurities (OH ions, transition metals) in the glass.
  2. Rayleigh Scattering: Due to density fluctuations in the glass structure.
  3. Macrobending Loss: Due to large radius bends in the fiber cable.
  4. Microbending Loss: Due to microscopic deformities in the core-cladding interface.
  5. Coupling/Connector Loss: Insertion loss at splices and connectors (Fresnel reflection).
7.
Briefly describe the main components of lasers. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Active Medium (Gain Medium): The material (solid, liquid, gas) whose atoms are excited to produce laser light (e.g., Ruby, He-Ne gas).
  2. Pumping Source: The energy source (flash lamp, electric current) that excites the atoms in the active medium to achieve population inversion.
  3. Optical Resonator (Cavity): A pair of mirrors (one 100% reflective, one partially reflective) placed at the ends of the active medium to reflect photons back and forth, stimulating further emission (amplification).
8.
Show how two transistors can be used to make a Silicon Control Rectifier. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

An SCR can be modeled as two interconnected transistors: a PNP transistor ($Q_1$) and an NPN transistor ($Q_2$).

  • Connection:
    • The Collector of PNP ($Q_1$) is connected to the Base of NPN ($Q_2$).
    • The Collector of NPN ($Q_2$) is connected to the Base of PNP ($Q_1$).
    • Anode is Emitter of $Q_1$. Cathode is Emitter of $Q_2$. Gate is Base of $Q_2$.
  • Operation: A positive gate pulse turns on $Q_2$. $Q_2$'s collector current pulls $Q_1$'s base low, turning on $Q_1$. $Q_1$'s collector current feeds into $Q_2$'s base, latching both transistors ON (Regenerative feedback).

Section 3: Long Answer Questions

1.

a) Explain four unique characteristics of a laser light.

b) With the aid of sketches differentiate between reflection and refraction of light.

c) State the laws governing each phenomenon of (b) above.

Show Answer

Answer:

(a) Laser Characteristics:

  1. Coherence: Waves are in phase in time and space.
  2. Monochromaticity: Single wavelength (color).
  3. Directionality (Collimation): Parallel beam with low divergence.
  4. High Intensity: Concentrated energy.

(b) Reflection vs Refraction:

  • Reflection: Bouncing back of light into the same medium when it strikes a surface. (Sketch: Incident ray and Reflected ray at equal angles).
  • Refraction: Bending of light as it passes from one medium to another of different density. (Sketch: Incident ray bends towards/away from normal in second medium).

(c) Laws:

  • Reflection: 1. Angle of Incidence = Angle of Reflection. 2. Incident ray, reflected ray, and normal lie on the same plane.
  • Refraction (Snell's Law): 1. Ratio of sine of angle of incidence to sine of angle of refraction is constant (Refractive Index). $n_1 \sin i = n_2 \sin r$. 2. Incident ray, refracted ray, and normal lie on the same plane.
2.

a) Draw the characteristics curve of UJT and explain its operation.

b) With a properly labelled sketch, show how a bipolar Junction transistor can be used as an electronic switch.

c) Explain the working principle of a photoconductive cell.

Show Answer

Answer:

(a) UJT Characteristics:

Graph of $V_E$ vs $I_E$.

  • Cutoff: $V_E < V_P$. UJT OFF.
  • Negative Resistance: Between Peak Point ($V_P$) and Valley Point ($V_V$). As $I_E$ increases, $V_E$ decreases due to hole injection reducing resistance.
  • Saturation: $V_E > V_V$. Normal diode behavior.

(b) BJT as Switch:

  • Circuit: Common Emitter. Input at Base via $R_B$. Output at Collector (LED/Relay/Resistor to $V_{CC}$).
  • OFF State (Cutoff): $V_{in} = 0$. $I_B = 0, I_C = 0$. Switch Open. $V_{out} = V_{CC}$.
  • ON State (Saturation): $V_{in} = High$. $I_B$ saturates transistor. $I_C = Max$. $V_{CE} \approx 0.2V$. Switch Closed.

(c) Photoconductive Cell (LDR) Working:

It is made of semiconductor material (CdS). When dark, resistance is very high ($M\Omega$) because few charge carriers are free. When illuminated, photons provide energy to break covalent bonds, creating electron-hole pairs. This increases the conductivity of the material, drastically reducing its resistance (to $k\Omega$ or $\Omega$).
