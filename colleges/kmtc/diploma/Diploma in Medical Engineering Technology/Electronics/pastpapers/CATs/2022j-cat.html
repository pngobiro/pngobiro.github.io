1.
Explain any THREE properties of laser light.
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Monochromaticity: Laser light consists of a single wavelength (or a very narrow band of wavelengths), resulting in a pure specific color.
  2. Coherence: The light waves are in phase with each other in both space and time. The crests and troughs of the waves align perfectly.
  3. Directionality (Collimation): The beam has extremely low divergence, meaning it travels in a straight line over long distances with very little spreading.
  4. High Intensity (Brightness): The energy is concentrated in a very small spatial area and narrow wavelength band.
2.

The diagram below shows the equivalent circuit for unijunction transistor (UJT).

i) Draw and explain the I-V characteristics of a unijunction transistor.

ii) If $\eta=0.8$, $V_P=10.3 \mathrm{~V}$, and $R_{B2}=5 \mathrm{~K} \Omega$, determine $R_{B1}$ (denoted as $R_1$) and $V_{BB}$ for the UJT.

Show Answer

Answer:

(i) I-V Characteristics:

The curve plots Emitter Voltage ($V_E$) vs Emitter Current ($I_E$).

  • Cut-off Region ($V_E < V_P$): The emitter diode is reverse biased. Only a small leakage current flows. The UJT is OFF.
  • Negative Resistance Region: When $V_E$ reaches the Peak Point ($V_P$), the diode becomes forward biased. Holes are injected into the N-type bar, decreasing the resistance of $R_{B1}$. As current ($I_E$) increases, voltage ($V_E$) decreases. This is the switching region.
  • Saturation Region ($V_E > V_V$): Beyond the Valley Point ($V_V$), further increase in current leads to a small increase in voltage, behaving like a conventional forward-biased diode.

(ii) Calculations:

Given: $\eta=0.8, V_P=10.3V, R_{B2}=5k\Omega$. Assume Diode drop $V_D \approx 0.7V$.

Find $V_{BB}$:

$$V_P = \eta V_{BB} + V_D$$

$$10.3 = 0.8 V_{BB} + 0.7$$

$$9.6 = 0.8 V_{BB} \Rightarrow V_{BB} = \frac{9.6}{0.8} = 12 \text{ V}$$

Find $R_{B1}$:

$$\eta = \frac{R_{B1}}{R_{B1} + R_{B2}}$$

$$0.8 = \frac{R_{B1}}{R_{B1} + 5000}$$

$$0.8 R_{B1} + 4000 = R_{B1}$$

$$4000 = 0.2 R_{B1} \Rightarrow R_{B1} = \frac{4000}{0.2} = 20,000 \Omega = 20 \text{ K}\Omega$$

3.

A communication system uses 10 km of fibre that has a $2.5 \mathrm{~dB} / \mathrm{km}$ loss characteristic. Find the output power if the input power is 400 mW.

Show Answer

Answer:

1. Calculate Total Loss (Attenuation) in dB:

$$\text{Total Loss} = \text{Length} \times \text{Loss/km} = 10 \text{ km} \times 2.5 \text{ dB/km} = 25 \text{ dB}$$

2. Calculate Output Power:

Formula: $$\text{Loss (dB)} = 10 \log_{10}\left(\frac{P_{in}}{P_{out}}\right)$$

$$25 = 10 \log_{10}\left(\frac{400 \text{ mW}}{P_{out}}\right)$$

$$2.5 = \log_{10}\left(\frac{400}{P_{out}}\right)$$

$$10^{2.5} = \frac{400}{P_{out}}$$

$$316.22 = \frac{400}{P_{out}}$$

$$P_{out} = \frac{400}{316.22} \approx 1.265 \text{ mW}$$

4.

a) Explain the four main triggering methods used in thyristors.

b) If an optical fiber has a core of refractive index 1.52 and 1.42 for the cladding, how many modes can be propagated? (Note: Core radius/diameter and wavelength are missing from question data).

c) With help of a diagram, explain the construction of a Schockley diode. Explain its operation and V-I characteristics.

Show Answer

Answer:

(a) Thyristor Triggering Methods:

  1. Gate Triggering: Applying a positive voltage to the gate terminal. This is the most common and reliable method.
  2. Forward Voltage Triggering: Increasing the anode-cathode voltage until it exceeds the breakover voltage ($V_{BO}$), causing avalanche breakdown.
  3. dv/dt Triggering: A rapid rate of rise of anode voltage causes capacitive current flow across the junction, which can turn the device on.
  4. Thermal/Temperature Triggering: High temperature increases leakage current, which may trigger the device (usually undesirable).
  5. Light/Radiation Triggering: Light energy creates electron-hole pairs in the junction (used in LASCRs).

(b) Modes Propagation:

Note: To calculate the exact number of modes ($N$), the V-number (Normalized Frequency) is required, which depends on core radius ($a$) and wavelength ($\lambda$).

Formula: $$N \approx \frac{V^2}{2}$$

Where $$V = \frac{2\pi a}{\lambda} \times \text{NA} = \frac{2\pi a}{\lambda} \sqrt{n_{core}^2 - n_{clad}^2}$$

Numerical Aperture ($NA$) = $\sqrt{1.52^2 - 1.42^2} = \sqrt{2.3104 - 2.0164} = \sqrt{0.294} \approx 0.542$.

Without radius and wavelength, the specific number cannot be calculated.

(c) Schockley Diode:

  • Construction: It is a four-layer (P-N-P-N) semiconductor device with two terminals: Anode and Cathode. It is essentially an SCR without a Gate terminal.
  • Operation:
    • Forward Blocking: When Anode is positive, junctions J1 and J3 are forward biased, but J2 is reverse biased. Device is OFF.
    • Forward Conduction: If voltage exceeds Breakover Voltage ($V_{BO}$), J2 breaks down, and the device latches ON.
  • V-I Characteristics: Shows a high-impedance "off" state up to $V_{BO}$, then snaps back to a low-impedance "on" state (Negative resistance region).
