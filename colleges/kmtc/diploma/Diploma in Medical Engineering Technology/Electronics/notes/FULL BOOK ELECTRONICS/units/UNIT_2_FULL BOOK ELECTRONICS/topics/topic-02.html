üîã Transistor Voltages

In an NPN transistor, the base is biased to a higher positive voltage than the emitter, and, in turn, the collector is at a higher potential than the base. Fig. 2.8(a) gives the polarities of the terminal voltages. The direction of the arrow head indicates the transistor bias polarities as also the conventional current direction.

Fig. 2.8(b) shows the voltage source connections. The base bias voltage $(\mathrm{V_{BB}})$ is applied through base resistor $\mathbf{R}_{\mathrm{B}}$, and the supply voltage $(\mathrm{V_{CC}})$ is applied through $\mathbf{R}_{\mathrm{C}}$. The negative terminals of $\mathrm{V_{CC}}$ & $\mathrm{V_{BB}}$ are connected at the transistor emitter terminal. In order to ensure that the CB junction remains reverse-biased, $\mathrm{V_{CC}}$ is always much larger than $\mathrm{V_{BB}}$, then the collector (N-side) is positive and the base (P-side) is negative.

üí°

Typical Voltage Values

Typical base-emitter voltages are $0.7\mathrm{V}$ for a silicon transistor and $0.3\mathrm{V}$ for a germanium transistor. Usually collector voltages are of in the range between $3\mathrm{V}$ and $20\mathrm{V}$, but in some cases they may exceed $20\mathrm{V}$.

In a PNP transistor the base is made more negative than the emitter (Fig.2.9(a)). The arrowhead pointing inwards from the emitter (positive) to the base (negative), and the collector is made more negative than the base.

Fig. 2.9 (b) gives the circuit which shows the two voltage sources connected through resistors. The positive terminals of $\mathrm{V}_{\mathrm{CC}}$ and $\mathrm{V}_{\mathrm{BB}}$ are connected at the emitter. With $\mathrm{V}_{\mathrm{CC}}$ larger than $\mathrm{V}_{\mathrm{BB}}$, the P-type collector is more negative than the N-type base, ensuring that the C-B junction is reverse-biased.

All transistors (both NPN & PNP) are usually operated by forward biasing the B-E junction and reverse-biasing the C-B junction.

üìâ Current Gain: Common-Base Configuration

Consider a transistor (either NPN or PNP) in a common-base configuration as shown in Fig. 2.10 (a) or (b). Here the emitter current is the input current and collector current is the output current. The ratio of the transistor output current to the input current is called current gain of a transistor.

(a) Common-base d.c. current gain $(\alpha)$

It is defined as the ratio of collector current $I_{C}$ to emitter current $I_{E}$ and is usually designated by $\alpha$, or $\alpha_{dc}$ or $h_{FB}$. Mathematically, the common-base d.c. current gain is expressed as,

$$\alpha = I_{C}/I_{E} \tag{2.1}$$

We know that in a transistor, the collector current is always less than the emitter current. Therefore current-gain of a transistor in common-base configuration is always less than unity. For example, if $\mathrm{I_C} = 9.7\mathrm{mA}$ and $\mathrm{I_E} = 10\mathrm{mA}$, then common-base d.c. current gain,

$$\alpha = 9.7/10 = 0.97$$

(b) Common-base a.c. current gain $(\alpha_{\mathrm{g}})$

It is defined as the ratio of small change in collector current $(\Delta I_{\mathrm{C}})$ to a small change in emitter current $(\Delta I_{\mathrm{E}})$ for a constant collector-to-base voltage $(V_{\mathrm{CB}})$. It is designated by $\alpha_{0}$, or $\alpha_{\infty}$ or $h_{\mathrm{B}}$. Mathematically, the common-base a.c. current gain.

$$\alpha_{0} = \Delta I_{\mathrm{C}} / \Delta I_{\mathrm{E}} \tag{2.2}$$

The term $\alpha_0$ is also called common-base short-circuit current gain or small signal current gain.

‚ÑπÔ∏è

Understanding Gain < 1

It may be noted that current gain of a transistor in a common-base configuration $(\alpha)$ is less than unity. But still it is called current gain. It is due to the fact that the output resistance of a common-base transistor is much higher than the input resistance. This produces a large voltage gain and hence the large power gain.

Now,

$$I_{\mathrm{C}} = \alpha I_{\mathrm{E}}$$
$$I_{\mathrm{B}} = I_{\mathrm{E}} - I_{\mathrm{C}} = I_{\mathrm{E}} - \alpha I_{\mathrm{E}}$$
$$I_{\mathrm{B}} = (1 - \alpha) I_{\mathrm{E}} \tag{2.3}$$

This is shown in Fig. 2.10 (c).

üìà Current Gain: Common-Emitter Configuration

Consider a transistor (either NPN or PNP) connected in a common-emitter configuration as shown in Fig. 2.11 (a) and (b). Here, the base current is the input current and the collector current is the output current. The ratio of collector current to base current is called current gain $(\beta)$ of the transistor.

(a) Common-emitter d.c. current gain $(\beta)_t$

It is defined as the ratio of collector current $(I_C)$ to corresponding base current $(I_B)$ and is designated by $\beta$, or $\beta_{dc}$ or $h_{FE}$. Mathematically, the common-emitter d.c. current gain is given by the relation,

$$\beta = I_C / I_B \tag{2.4}$$

or

$$I_C = \beta I_B$$

Since collector current of a transistor is much larger than the base current, the value of $\beta$ is much greater than unity. Typical values of $\beta$ may range from 20 to 250. It is also called common-emitter dc forward transfer ratio and is written as $h_{FE}$. It is possible for $\beta$ to have as high a value as 500.

(b) Common emitter a.c. current gain $(\beta_0)_t$

It is defined as the ratio of small change in collector current $(\Delta I_C)$ to the corresponding small change in base current $(\Delta I_B)$ for a constant collector-to-emitter voltage $(V_{CE})$. It is designated by $\beta_0$ or $\beta_{ac}$ or $h_{fe}$. Mathematically common-emitter a.c. current gain is given by the relation,

$$\beta_0 = \Delta I_C / \Delta I_B \tag{2.5}$$

The term $\beta_0$ is also called common-emitter short circuit current gain or small-signal common-emitter current gain. It is interesting to know that $\beta_0$ or $h_{fe}$ is a parameter used in the analysis of small-signal transistor amplifier circuits. $\beta_0$ have a range of values similar to that mentioned for $\beta$. For all practical purposes, d.c. current gain and a.c. current gain are considered to be equal. i.e. $\beta = \beta_0$.

04

üîó Relationship between $\alpha$ and $\beta$

We know that emitter current $(I_E)$ of a transistor is the sum of its base current $(I_B)$ and collector current $(I_C)$. i.e.,

$$I_E = I_B + I_C$$

Dividing the above equation on both sides by $I_C$,

$$\frac{I_E}{I_C} = \frac{I_B}{I_C} + 1$$

Since $\frac{I_E}{I_C} = \frac{1}{\alpha}$ and $\frac{I_B}{I_C} = \frac{1}{\beta}$, then:

$$\frac{1}{\alpha} = \frac{1}{\beta} + 1 = \frac{1 + \beta}{\beta}$$
$$\therefore \quad \alpha = \frac{\beta}{\beta + 1} \tag{2.6}$$

The above expression may be written as

$$\alpha (\beta + 1) = \beta$$
$$\alpha \cdot \beta + \alpha = \beta$$
$$\alpha = \beta - \alpha \cdot \beta = \beta (1 - \alpha)$$

or

$$\beta = \frac {\alpha}{1 - \alpha} \quad \dots (2.7)$$
üìù

Useful Deductions

Also using above eqns., we can deduce:

  1. $I_{\mathrm{C}} = \beta I_{\mathrm{B}} = \alpha I_{\mathrm{E}} = \frac{\beta}{1 + \beta} \cdot I_{\mathrm{E}}$
  2. $I_{\mathrm{B}} = \frac{I_{\mathrm{C}}}{\beta} = \frac{I_{\mathrm{E}}}{1 + \beta} = (1 - \alpha)I_{\mathrm{E}}$
  3. $I_{\mathrm{E}} = \frac{I_{\mathrm{C}}}{\alpha} = \frac{1 + \beta}{\beta} \cdot I_{\mathrm{C}} = (1 + \beta) I_{\mathrm{B}} = \frac{I_{\mathrm{B}}}{(1 - \alpha)}$
  4. The three transistor dc currents always bear the following ratio:
    $I_{\mathrm{E}}: I_{\mathrm{B}}: I_{\mathrm{C}}:: 1: (1 - \alpha): \alpha$

üìä Current Gain: Common-Collector Configuration

Fig. 2.12 (a) and (b) show a NPN and PNP transistor connected in a common-collector configuration respectively. The voltages $\mathrm{V_{BB}}$ forward-biases the emitter-base junction, and $\mathrm{V_{CC}}$ reverse-biases the collector-base junction of the transistor. Here, the collector is not at d.c. ground. But it is at a.c. ground. It is due to the fact, that the voltage of d.c. supply source $(\mathrm{V_{CE}})$ has zero resistance to an a.c. signal. An external resistor $\mathrm{R_E}$ is connected from the emitter to ground. The output of the circuit is taken across the resistor $\mathrm{R_E}$.

It may be noted that in a common-collector transistor circuit, the input current is the base current $(\mathrm{I_B})$ and the output current is the emitter current $(\mathrm{I_E})$. Therefore, common-collector current gain is given by the relation,

$$\gamma = \frac {I_{\mathrm{E}}}{I_{\mathrm{B}}} = \frac {I_{\mathrm{E}}}{I_{\mathrm{C}}} \times \frac {I_{\mathrm{C}}}{I_{\mathrm{B}}} = \frac {1}{\alpha} \times \beta$$

Substituting the value of $\alpha = \beta / (1 + \beta)$ in the above equation, common-collector current gain $(\gamma)$ becomes

$$\gamma = \frac {\beta}{\beta / (1 + \beta)} = 1 + \beta \tag{2.8}$$

It means that the output current of a common-collector transistor circuit is $(1 + \beta)$ times that of the input current. We know that $\beta \gg 1$, therefore common-collector current gain $\gamma \approx \beta$.

‚ö†Ô∏è Leakage Currents

Leakage current in CB Transistor ($I_{CBO}$)

Consider the CB transistor circuit shown in Fig. 2.13. The emitter current (due to majority carriers) initiated by the forward-biased emitter-base junction is split into two parts:

  1. $(1 - \alpha)\mathrm{I}_{\mathrm{E}}$ which becomes base current $\mathrm{I_B}$ in the external circuit, and
  2. $\alpha \mathrm{I}_{\mathrm{E}}$ which becomes collector current $\mathrm{I_C}$ in the external circuit.

Though C-B junction is reverse-biased for majority charge carriers (i.e., holes in this case), it is forward-biased for thermally-generated minority charge carriers (i.e., electrons in this case) are concerned. This current flows even when emitter is disconnected from its dc supply as shown in Fig. 2.13 (a) where switch $S_{1}$ is open. It flows in the same direction as the collector current of majority carriers. It is called leakage current $I_{\mathrm{CBO}}$. The subscripts CBO stand for current from Collector to Base with emitter Open. Very often, it is simply written as $I_{\mathrm{CO}}$.

üî•

Temperature Dependence

$\mathrm{I}_{\mathrm{CBO}}$ is extremely temperature-dependent because it is made up of thermally-generated minority charge carriers. $\mathrm{I}_{\mathrm{CBO}}$ doubles for every $10^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ rise in temperature for Si and $6^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ for Ge.

It is seen that total collector current is actually the sum of two components:

  1. current produced by normal transistor action i.e., component controlled by emitter current. Its value is $\alpha I_{E}$ and is due to majority carriers.
  2. temperature-dependent leakage $\mathrm{I}_{\mathrm{co}}$ due to minority carriers
$$\therefore \quad I_{C} = \alpha I_{E} + I_{CO} \tag{2.9}$$
$$\therefore \alpha = \frac{I_C - I_{CO}}{I_E}$$

Since $I_{CO} \ll I_{C}$ hence $\alpha \cong \frac{I_{C}}{I_{E}}$.

Leakage current in CE Transistor ($I_{CEO}$)

Fig. 2.14 (a) shows a grounded-emitter circuit of an NPN transistor whose base lead is open. It is found that despite $\mathrm{I_B} = 0$, there is a leakage current from collector to emitter. It is called $\mathrm{I_{CEO}}$, the subscripts CEO standing for 'Collector to Emitter with base Open'.

Taking this leakage current into account, the total collector current becomes:

$$I_{\mathrm{C}} = \beta I_{\mathrm{B}} + I_{\mathrm{CEO}} = \beta I_{\mathrm{B}} + (1 + \beta) I_{\mathrm{CO}} = \beta I_{\mathrm{B}} + \frac {I_{\mathrm{CO}}}{(1 - \alpha)} \tag{2.11}$$

where $I_{CEO} = (1 + \beta)I_{CO}$.

üå°Ô∏è Thermal Runaway & Stability Factor

Thermal Runaway

In a CE circuit, we know that $I_{\mathrm{C}} = \beta I_{\mathrm{B}} + (1 + \beta) I_{\mathrm{CO}}$. The leakage current is extremely temperature-dependent. It almost doubles for every $6^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ rise in temperature in Ge and for every $10^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ rise in Si. Any increase in $\mathrm{I}_{\mathrm{CO}}$ is magnified $(1 + \beta)$ times i.e., 200 to 300 times. Even a slight increase in $\mathrm{I}_{\mathrm{CO}}$ will affect $\mathrm{I}_{\mathrm{C}}$ considerably.

üî•

Destructive Cycle

As $\mathrm{I}_{\mathrm{C}}$ increases, collector power dissipation increases which raises the operating temperature that leads to further increase in $\mathrm{I}_{\mathrm{C}}$. If this succession of increases is allowed to continue, soon $\mathrm{I}_{\mathrm{C}}$ will increase beyond safe operating value thereby damaging the transistor itself ‚Äî a condition known as thermal runaway. Hence, some form of stabilization is necessary to prevent this thermal runaway.

Stability Factor (S)

The stability factor may be defined as the rate of change of collector current $(I_C)$ with respect to the reverse saturation current $(I_{CO})$ keeping the common-emitter current gain $(\beta)$ and base current $(I_B)$ as constant. Mathematically, the stability factor is given by the relation,

$$S = \frac {d I_{\mathrm{C}}}{d I_{\mathrm{CO}}} \tag{2.17}$$

The general formula for Stability Factor is:

$$S = \frac {1 + \beta}{1 - \beta \left(\frac {d I_{\mathrm{B}}}{d I_{\mathrm{C}}}\right)} \tag{2.18}$$

Comparison of Stability Factors:

Configuration Stability Factor (S) Stability Performance
Common Base $S = 1$ Highly Stable
Common Emitter $S = 1 + \beta$ Poor Stability

Note: A higher value of stability factor indicates poor stability, whereas a lower value indicates good stability. Unity is the lowest value of stability factor. In CE circuit, if $\beta = 100$, then $S = 101$, meaning collector current changes 101 times the change in reverse saturation current. This indicates a strong need for bias stabilization in CE circuits.
