Common Emitter Characteristics

01

üìä Experimental Setup

The static characteristics of an NPN transistor connected in common-Emitter configuration can be determined by using circuit as shown in Fig. 2.20. Milliammeters are included in series with the base and collector circuits to measure $I_{B}$ and $I_{C}$. Similarly, voltmeters are connected across B and E to measure voltage $V_{BE}$ and across C and E to measure $V_{CE}$. The two potentiometer resistors $R_{1}$ and $R_{2}$ supply variable voltage from the collector and base d.c. supplies.

02

üì• Input Characteristics

It is a graph between $\mathrm{I_B}$ and $\mathrm{V_{BE}}$ when $\mathrm{V_{CE}}$ is kept constant. The method of determining this characteristic is as follows:

  1. $\mathrm{V_{CE}}$ is maintained constant at a convenient value and then $\mathrm{V_{BE}}$ is increased in steps. Corresponding values of $\mathrm{I_B}$ are noted at each step.
  2. The procedure is then repeated for a different but constant value of $\mathrm{V_{CE}}$.

A typical input characteristic is shown in Fig. 2.21. Like CB connection, the overall shape resembles the forward characteristic of a $P-N$ diode. The reciprocal of the slope gives the input resistance $\mathrm{R_{in}}$ of the transistor.

03

üì§ Output Characteristics

It is a graph between $\mathrm{I_C}$ and $\mathrm{V_{CE}}$ when $\mathrm{I_B}$ is kept constant. The method of determining this characteristic is as follows:

  1. $\mathrm{I_B}$ is set to a convenient value and maintained constant and then $\mathrm{V_{CE}}$ is increased from zero in steps and corresponding $\mathrm{I_C}$ is noted at each step.
  2. Next, $\mathrm{V_{CE}}$ is reduced to zero and $\mathrm{I_B}$ increased to another convenient value and the whole procedure is repeated. In this way, a family of output characteristic curves (Fig. 2.22) is obtained.
üí°

Regions of Operation

From the output characteristic curve shown in Fig. 2.22, we can identify three distinct regions of operation:

  • Active Region: Base-emitter junction is forward biased and collector-base junction is reverse biased. This is the normal operating region for amplification.
  • Saturation Region: When $V_{CE}$ has very low value (ideally zero), both junctions are forward biased. Here, change in $I_B$ does not produce a corresponding change in $I_C$.
  • Cut-off Region: Both junctions are reverse biased. $I_C$ is very small ($I_{CEO}$).

Key Observations:

  • As $V_{CE}$ increases from zero, $I_C$ rapidly increases to a near saturation level for fixed value of $I_B$.
  • A small amount of collector current flows even when $I_B = 0$. It is called $I_{CEO}$ (Collector to Emitter with base Open). Since main collector current is zero, the transistor is said to be cut-off.
  • If $V_{CE}$ is allowed to increase too far, C-B junction completely breaks down and due to this avalanche breakdown, $I_C$ increases rapidly and may cause damage to the transistor.

This characteristic can be used to find $\beta$ at a specific value of $I_B$ and $V_{CE}$:

$$\beta = \frac {\Delta I _ {C}}{\Delta I _ {B}}$$

The value of output resistance $\mathrm{R_{out}}$ ($= \Delta V_{CE} / \Delta I_{C}$) over the near horizontal part of the characteristic varies from 10 KŒ© to 50 KŒ©.

04

üîÑ Current Transfer Characteristic

It indicates how $\mathrm{I}_{\mathrm{C}}$ varies with changes in $\mathrm{I}_{\mathrm{B}}$ when $\mathrm{V}_{\mathrm{CE}}$ is kept constant at a given value. Such a typical characteristic is shown in Fig. 2.23 (a). Its slope gives

$$\beta = \frac {\Delta I _ {C}}{\Delta I _ {B}}$$

From the detailed view (Fig 2.23 b below), it is seen that a small collector current flows even when $\mathrm{I_B} = 0$. It is the common-emitter leakage current $\mathrm{I_{CEO}} = (1 + \beta) / \mathrm{I_{CO}}$. Like $\mathrm{I_{CO}}$, it is also due to the flow of minority carriers across the reverse-biased C-B junction.

05

üèÜ Why CE Configuration is Commonly Used?

The common Emitter configuration is commonly used because:

  1. Maximum Power Gain: CE configuration is only configuration which provides both voltage gain and current gain greater than unity. Thus the power gain is maximum compared to CB & CC configurations.
  2. Ideally Suited for Coupling: In CE configuration, the ratio of output resistance to input resistance is small (of $10\Omega - 100\Omega$). This makes CE configuration ideal for coupling between various transistor stages. However, in CB & CC connections, the ratio of output resistance to input resistance is very large so that coupling between stages is inefficient due to large mismatch of resistance.

Key Points

  • ‚úì

    Active Region: Emitter-Base forward biased, Collector-Base reverse biased.

  • ‚úì

    Saturation Region: Both junctions forward biased ($V_{CE} \approx 0$).

  • ‚úì

    Cut-off Region: Both junctions reverse biased ($I_B = 0, I_C = I_{CEO}$).

  • ‚úì

    CE is the preferred configuration for multi-stage amplifiers due to high power gain and manageable impedance matching.
