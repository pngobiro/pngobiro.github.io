1.
Listed below are ways of conveying information to the waiting list except?
A. Card created for every patient
B. Nurse or physician sending list of patients to be added to the waiting list
C. Letter from a consultant
D. Letter from in-patient department
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Card created for every patient

The waiting list is a mechanism for scheduling patients who require admission or specialized procedures. Information is conveyed to the list via referrals or direct requests, not by creating a card for "every" patient indiscriminately.

  • B, C, D: These are standard methods of communication (lists from wards, letters from consultants/departments) to add specific patients to the waiting list.
  • A: Creating a card for every patient is not a method of conveying specific waiting list information; it's a general registration task.
2.
There are different types of health records indices used and maintained in a health records department, these include the following except?
A. The outpatient index
B. The disease index
C. The operation index
D. The waiting list index
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. The waiting list index

Standard health indices facilitate the retrieval of records for research and administrative purposes.

  • Master Patient Index (MPI): The key to locating records.
  • Disease Index: Lists patients by diagnosis code.
  • Operation/Procedure Index: Lists patients by surgical procedure.
  • Physician Index: Lists patients by attending doctor.

While a "waiting list" exists, it is functionally a scheduling tool or register, not typically classified as a primary "index" in the same category as Disease or Operation indices in standard Health Information Management (HIM) theory.

3.
The following are checks to be made on the waiting list except?
A. Checking for completeness
B. Checking for accuracy
C. Checking for quality
D. Checking for duplication
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Checking for quality

Routine maintenance of a waiting list involves administrative checks to ensure the list is usable and reliable.

  • Completeness: Ensuring all required patient data is present.
  • Accuracy: Ensuring the data (phone numbers, diagnosis) is correct.
  • Duplication: Ensuring the patient isn't listed twice.
  • Quality: "Quality" is a broad concept usually applied to clinical care or overall data management (Quality Assurance), not a specific clerical check performed on a list entry like the others.
4.
Which of the following is not a quality of a Health Records and Information Officer?
A. Integrity
B. Generosity
C. Humility
D. Adaptability
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Generosity

Professional qualities for an HRIO include:

  • Integrity: Honesty and strict adherence to confidentiality.
  • Adaptability: Ability to handle changing technologies and workflows.
  • Humility/Discretion: Serving patients and staff with respect.

Generosity is a personal virtue but not a professional requirement or competency for Health Records management.

5.
The following are permanent records, which one is not?
A. Maternity records
B. Registers of birth
C. Registers of death
D. Registers of surgical procedures
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Maternity records

Retention policies classify records based on legal and historical value.

  • Registers (Birth, Death, Surgical): These are often kept permanently as legal logs of vital events and hospital activities.
  • Maternity Records: These are patient case files. While kept for a very long time (often 25+ years or until the child reaches majority plus a buffer), they are not strictly "permanent" in the same way statutory registers are often considered. However, this is context-dependent; some jurisdictions keep maternity case notes permanently. In the context of options, Registers are the definitive permanent archives.
6.
Case records can be stated as _____.
A. Records initiated for patients who got admitted into the wards and attend consultative clinics
B. Patients who continuously attend the consultant clinic
C. The person taking in the information
D. The range of documents to be included in the case records
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Records initiated for patients who got admitted into the wards and attend consultative clinics

Case records (or medical records) are the collection of documents tracking a patient's clinical history, treatments, and outcomes across both inpatient (wards) and outpatient (clinics) settings.

7.
The common tracer card has the following information written on it except?
A. Patient's full name
B. Date of appointment
C. Destination i.e. Ward, clinic
D. Patients date of birth
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Patients date of birth

A tracer card (or outguide) replaces a file removed from the shelf. Its primary purpose is to tell file clerks where the file is.

Key fields required:

  • File Number: To match the filing system.
  • Patient Name: For verification.
  • Destination: Who has the file?
  • Date removed/Date of appointment: When was it taken?

Date of Birth is generally not required for the tracking function of a tracer card, as the File Number and Name are sufficient for identification in the filing area.

8.
Auxiliary equipment includes the following except?
A. Filling trolleys
B. Ladders
C. Dividers
D. Preparation tables
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Dividers

"Equipment" usually refers to durable physical assets.

  • Auxiliary Equipment: Trolleys, step ladders, kick stools, sorting tables (prep tables). These are tools used to facilitate the work.
  • Dividers (File Guides): These are usually considered supplies or parts of the filing system itself, rather than distinct auxiliary equipment like a trolley or ladder.
9.
The most economical filling method as far as space is concerned is?
A. Mobile racking
B. Suspended filing
C. filling cabinets
D. Shelve filing
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Mobile racking

Mobile Racking (Compactus): This system eliminates the need for fixed aisles between every row of shelves. The units slide on rails, opening an aisle only where needed. It can save up to 50% of floor space compared to static shelving or cabinets, making it the most space-economical.

10.
Which of the following principles in medical practice entitles patients equity and fairness?
A. Autonomy
B. Fidelity
C. Justice
D. Beneficence
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Justice

Medical Ethics Principles:

  • Justice: Fairness, equity, and impartial distribution of resources and care.
  • Autonomy: The patient's right to decide.
  • Beneficence: Acting in the patient's best interest.
  • Fidelity: Faithfulness and keeping promises.
11.
Which of the following requirements is not to be considered when establishing a health records department?
A. Location of the department
B. Space requirement for records
C. System of communication within the department and other departments
D. System to ensure transportation of files within the department and other departments
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. System to ensure transportation of files within the department and other departments

This question requires careful interpretation. All options seem relevant.

However, location, space, and communication flow are fundamental strategic planning considerations (Architectural/Structural). File transportation (e.g., pneumatic tubes, dumbwaiters, or manual couriers) is an operational logistical detail that usually comes after the primary structural decisions. In many smaller contexts or modern EMR contexts, a complex physical transportation system is not a primary establishment requirement compared to Location and Space.

12.
---------- is the arrangement of all forms inside the unit folder in a prescribed manner
A. Editing
B. Auditing
C. Monitoring
D. Evaluation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Editing

In the context of physical medical records management, Editing (or assembling/grooming) refers to the process of checking the record after discharge to ensure all documents are present, signed, and arranged in the standard hospital order (e.g., chronological or source-oriented) before permanent filing.

Primary Purpose: To document the care provided to the patient to ensure communication between providers and continuity of treatment.

17.
The first real physician of health records in Egypt is?
A. Edwin Smith
B. Hippocrates
C. Imhotep
D. Franklin
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Imhotep

Imhotep (approx. 2600 BC) was an Egyptian polymath who is considered the first physician known by name in written history. He is often associated with the early systematic recording of medical cases.

  • Edwin Smith: An archaeologist who bought the famous medical papyrus (named after him).
  • Hippocrates: Greek "Father of Medicine," came much later (460 BC).

Hippocrates was known for the below mentioned except

19.
Informal admission refers to?
A. A patient is admitted voluntarily, he or she is aware he has mental illness. Admission is carried out in reference to Mental Health Act
B. A patient is admitted without legal or other formalities and the hospital has no right to detain him in the hospital against his will
C. A patient is admitted with legal formalities and the hospital has no right to detain him unless he/she is a threat to the public
D. Patient is admitted involuntarily, admission and discharge is carried out in reference to Mental Health Act
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. A patient is admitted without legal or other formalities and the hospital has no right to detain him in the hospital against his will

Informal admission (in mental health contexts) is similar to a standard medical admission. The patient enters voluntarily and is free to leave (discharge themselves) at any time. No specific section of the Mental Health Act is used to detain them.

20.
Wide spread implementation of electronic records has been hampered by perceived barriers including one of the following
A. Efficiency
B. Encouragement
C. Empowerment
D. Incompatibility
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Incompatibility

Interoperability/Incompatibility between different systems is a major barrier to EMR adoption. Other barriers include high cost, security concerns, and user resistance. Efficiency, encouragement, and empowerment are generally seen as goals or benefits, not barriers.

29.
In terminal digit filing system, the growth is not at one end because?
A. It is the easier way of filling
B. Busiest section of the library
C. It is used by the United States hospital
D. The library is divided into 100 major sections
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. The library is divided into 100 major sections

Terminal Digit Filing distributes new records evenly across 100 distinct sections (based on the last two digits: 00-99). Unlike serial numbering where all new files go to the end, TDF ensures even growth throughout the entire filing system.

41.
Describe five ways of disposing patients attended to at the accident and emergency department (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

Patients attended to at the Accident and Emergency (A&E) department can be disposed of in the following ways:

  1. Admission: The patient is admitted to a ward for inpatient care due to the severity of their condition.
  2. Discharge Home: The patient is treated and found fit to go home, often with medication or advice.
  3. Referral to Outpatient Clinic (OPC): The patient is stabilized and given an appointment to attend a specialist clinic for follow-up.
  4. Transfer/Referral to Another Facility: The patient is moved to another hospital (e.g., for specialized care like ICU or Neurosurgery not available at the current facility).
  5. Brought in Dead (BID) / Certified Dead: The patient passes away in the department or arrives deceased and is moved to the mortuary.

47.
Enumerate five advantages of terminal digit filling (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Even Distribution: New files are spread evenly throughout the 100 sections, preventing congestion at the end of the files.
  2. Workflow Balance: Work can be distributed evenly among file clerks (e.g., Clerk A handles sections 00-25).
  3. Reduced Misfiling: Focus on 2-digit groups reduces the cognitive load and error rate compared to reading long sequential numbers.
  4. Scalability: The system handles unlimited growth without needing to constantly shift files to make space at the end.
  5. Security: It is harder for unauthorized persons to browse sequential files as they are scattered.

49.
Suppose that you have been called for an interview as a recently qualified health records and information officer. Explain to the interview panel the procedure of admitting a patient to the hospital. (20 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

The admission procedure is a critical process ensuring the patient is legally and administratively accepted into the hospital for inpatient care. The procedure involves the following steps:

1. Authorization for Admission:

  • The process begins with a clinical decision. A doctor examines the patient (in casualty or outpatient clinic) and signs an Admission Order or completes an Admission Sheet.
  • Without this written authority, a patient cannot be admitted.

2. Reception at Admission Desk:

  • The patient or relative presents the admission notes to the Admissions Clerk/HRIO.
  • The officer verifies the details and checks the Master Patient Index (MPI) to see if the patient has a previous file.

3. Interview and Data Collection:

  • If New Patient: Complete registration details (Name, Age, Sex, Next of Kin, Address). Assign a new Unit Number.
  • If Old Patient: Retrieve the existing file. Verify and update demographic details (e.g., change of address or next of kin).

4. Documentation Preparation:

  • Prepare the Inpatient File. This typically includes attaching the:
    • Admission/Discharge Summary Sheet (Face Sheet).
    • Consent Forms for admission/treatment.
    • Clinical history/continuation sheets.
    • Temperature/Vitals charts.

5. Financial Clearance/Classification:

  • Determine the method of payment (NHIF, Private Insurance, Cash, Waiver).
  • Verify insurance validity or collect initial deposit if cash paying.

6. Bed Allocation:

  • Consult the Bed Bureau or Ward status board to identify an available bed in the appropriate ward (Medical, Surgical, Paediatric, etc.).

7. Updating Registers:

  • Enter patient details into the Admission Register (Daily Admission Book).
  • Update the computerized system (ADT - Admission, Discharge, Transfer system) if available.

8. Escorting the Patient:

  • The patient is escorted to the ward by a porter or nurse.
  • The physical file accompanies the patient to the ward.

9. Ward Reception:

  • The Ward Nurse receives the patient and file.
  • The patient is entered into the Ward Admission Register.

Conclusion: This standardized process ensures legal compliance, financial security for the hospital, and safety for the patient by ensuring all relevant history is available for the treating team.
