What is Demography?

Definition

The study of populations, especially with reference to size and density, fertility, mortality, growth, age distribution, migration, and vital statistics and the integration of all these with social and economic conditions. (Last)

Status of Population

Population characteristics include:

  • Age distribution
  • Sex composition
  • Education level
  • Income
  • Households and family structure
  • Urbanisation
  • Public utilities
  • Housing facilities
  • Ethnicity

Demographic Structure

Demographic structure is presented through various indicators:

  • Sex distribution (females and males) and sex ratio
  • Population by main age-groups (0-14, 15-39, 40-59, 60-X) and mean age
  • Age structure by life-years or five-year age-groups
  • Age composition, dependency ratio, ageing index
  • Distribution by race and ethnicity

Age-Sex Pyramid

A graphical demonstration of the demographic structure of society showing:

  • Horizontal (x-axis): Life years of women and men
  • Vertical (y-axis): Number of persons in each age group
  • Convention: Females on the right, males on the left

Types of Age-Sex Pyramids

  1. Stage 1: Expanding (rapid growth)
  2. Stage 2: Expanding (balanced growth)
  3. Stage 3: Stationary (growing older population)
  4. Stage 4: Contracting (decreasing population)

Natality

Birth (WHO definition 1950): A live birth occurs when a foetus exits the maternal body and shows signs of life:

  • Heartbeat
  • Pulsation of the umbilical cord
  • Any voluntary movement

Crude Birth Rate

The number of live births in a year per 1000 of the population:

$$\text{Birth rate} = \frac{\text{N of live births}}{\text{Mid-year population}} \times 1000$$

Fertility

Fertility is the child-bearing capacity of the population represented by women between ages 15 and 49 years.

Types of Fertility Rates

  1. General Fertility Rate (GFR): Births per 1000 women aged 15-49
  2. Age-Specific Fertility Rate: Births to women of a particular age group
  3. Total Fertility Rate (TFR): Average number of children a woman would bear during her reproductive lifetime

TFR Interpretation

From a biological viewpoint (without considering migration and at stable mortality):

  • TFR > 2 means growing population
  • TFR < 2 means decreasing population
  • TFR = 2 means replacement level

Mortality

Mortality is the relationship of death cases to the whole population.

Types of Mortality Rates

  1. General (crude) mortality rate or death rate
  2. Specific mortality rates:
    • Age and sex related
    • Cause related (diseases, injuries, suicide, homicide)
    • Special rates (infant mortality, maternal mortality)

Crude Death Rate (CDR)

$$\text{CDR} = \frac{\text{N of death cases}}{\text{Mid-year population}} \times 1000$$

Infant Mortality

Number of deaths of infants under one year old per 1,000 live births. This rate is often used as an indicator of the level of health in a country.

Categories of Infant Mortality

  1. Perinatal: Within 24 hours
  2. Postnatal: Within the first week of life
  3. Neonatal: Death occurring at 7 to 27 days of life
  4. Postneonatal: Death occurring between 28 and 365 days of life

Life Expectancy

Life expectancy is the average number of years an individual of a given age is expected to live if current age-specific mortality rates continue to apply.

Life expectancy at birth is the average number of years a newborn is expected to live if current mortality structure persists throughout its life.

Note

Life expectancy is calculated separately for men and women due to gender differences in mortality rates. It is a hypothetical measure and indicator of current health and mortality conditions.

Population Growth

Population growth is calculated as the difference between crude birth rate and crude death rate:

$$\text{PG} = \text{CBR} - \text{CDR}$$
  • Positive value = Natural increase
  • Negative value = Natural decrease
