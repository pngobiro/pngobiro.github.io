1.
Formal demography deals with all of the following except
a) Density of the population
b) Size of the population
c) Growth of the population
d) Age structure
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Density of the population

Formal demography focuses on the study of population size, growth, and structure (including age distribution). Population density refers to the number of people per unit area and is more related to geographic distribution rather than formal demographic analysis.

The 5-10 age cohort (ages 5-9) is preferred because children in this age group are typically in school and more likely to accurately report their ages. Younger children (0-4) may have less accurate age reporting, and the other options are not standard cohort groupings.

Sex ratio is primarily affected by biological factors (sex ratio at birth), mortality differences between sexes, and migration patterns. Fertility rate affects population growth but doesn't directly influence the sex ratio of the existing population.

12.
The following are errors experienced in demographic data except
a) Errors of coverage
b) Errors of contents
c) Sampling errors
d) Individual bias errors
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Sampling errors

Sampling errors occur in sample surveys, not in complete enumeration methods like censuses. Demographic data collection errors include coverage errors (missing or duplicate individuals), content errors (inaccurate reporting), and individual biases (deliberate misreporting).

14.

b) Migration
c) Mortality

Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Migration

Migration (both immigration and emigration) directly affects population size. Mortality and morbidity reduce population, while net increase is a result of population growth, not a cause.

Morbidity (illness) doesn't change population size directly - only births, deaths, and migration affect population numbers. Immigration, births, and deaths are the three components of population change.

Section 2: Short Answer Questions

40 Marks (5 marks each)
41.
Define the following terms as used in medical demography (5 Marks)

a. Maternal mortality

b. Population growth

Show Answer

Answer:

a. Maternal Mortality:

Maternal mortality refers to the death of a woman while pregnant or within 42 days of termination of pregnancy, irrespective of the duration and site of the pregnancy, from any cause related to or aggravated by the pregnancy or its management, but not from accidental or incidental causes.

Maternal Mortality Ratio (MMR): The number of maternal deaths per 100,000 live births in a given time period. Formula: $\text{MMR} = \frac{\text{Maternal Deaths}}{\text{Live Births}} \times 100,000$

b. Population Growth:

Population growth is the change in the size of a population over time, measured as the difference between births and deaths (natural increase) plus the difference between immigration and emigration (net migration).

Formula: Population Growth = (Births - Deaths) + (Immigration - Emigration)

Population growth can be positive (increasing), negative (declining), or zero (stable). It is typically expressed as a growth rate (percentage change per year).

42.
State 5 importance of a life table in public health (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

The importance of life tables in public health includes:

  1. Life Expectancy Calculation: Life tables provide accurate estimates of life expectancy at birth and at various ages, which is a key indicator of population health and development.
  2. Mortality Analysis: They allow for detailed analysis of age-specific mortality patterns, helping identify high-risk age groups and target interventions appropriately.
  3. Health Planning and Resource Allocation: Life tables help predict future population size and age structure, enabling better planning for healthcare services, pensions, and social security systems.
  4. Comparative Health Assessment: They enable comparison of mortality experiences between different populations, regions, or time periods, helping evaluate the effectiveness of public health interventions.
  5. Epidemiological Research: Life tables are essential for calculating survival probabilities, which are used in disease surveillance, clinical trials, and assessing the impact of diseases on population health.

State 5 uses of demographic data (5 Marks)

Answer:

Uses of demographic data include:

  1. Health Service Planning: Demographic data helps plan and allocate health resources based on population size, age structure, and geographic distribution to ensure adequate coverage.
  2. Policy Formulation: Governments use demographic information to develop policies related to family planning, education, employment, housing, and social welfare programs.
  3. Economic Planning: Demographic data informs labor force projections, market analysis, infrastructure development, and economic growth strategies.

44.
Name and explain any 5 mortality measures (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Crude Death Rate (CDR): The total number of deaths per 1,000 population in a given year. Formula: $\text{CDR} = \frac{\text{Total Deaths}}{\text{Mid-year Population}} \times 1000$. It provides a general measure of mortality but doesn't account for age structure.
  2. Infant Mortality Rate (IMR): The number of deaths of infants under one year of age per 1,000 live births in a given year. Formula: $\text{IMR} = \frac{\text{Deaths < 1 year}}{\text{Live Births}} \times 1000$. It's a sensitive indicator of overall health and socioeconomic conditions.
  3. Child Mortality Rate: The probability of dying between birth and exactly 5 years of age, expressed per 1,000 live births. Also called Under-5 Mortality Rate (U5MR). It reflects child health and nutrition status.
  4. Maternal Mortality Ratio (MMR): The number of maternal deaths per 100,000 live births. Formula: $\text{MMR} = \frac{\text{Maternal Deaths}}{\text{Live Births}} \times 100,000$. It measures pregnancy-related deaths and quality of maternal healthcare.
  5. Age-Specific Death Rate (ASDR): The number of deaths in a specific age group per 1,000 population in that age group. Formula: $\text{ASDR} = \frac{\text{Deaths in age group}}{\text{Population in age group}} \times 1000$. It allows detailed analysis of mortality by age.

46.
State factors that cause population growth (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

Factors that cause population growth include:

  1. High Birth Rate (Fertility): When the number of births exceeds deaths, the population grows. High fertility rates are influenced by cultural preferences for large families, lack of family planning, early marriage, and religious beliefs.
  2. Declining Death Rate (Mortality): Improvements in healthcare, nutrition, sanitation, and disease control reduce mortality rates, leading to population growth as more people survive to reproductive age and beyond.
  3. Immigration: The influx of people from other countries or regions increases the population size. Economic opportunities, political stability, and better living conditions attract immigrants.
  4. Improved Healthcare and Medical Technology: Advances in medicine, vaccines, antibiotics, and maternal/child health services reduce infant and maternal mortality, increasing life expectancy and population growth.
  5. Better Nutrition and Food Security: Adequate food supply and improved nutrition reduce malnutrition-related deaths and improve overall health, contributing to population growth.
  6. Socioeconomic Development: While long-term development eventually reduces growth, initial improvements in living standards reduce mortality faster than fertility, causing temporary population growth (demographic transition).
47.
State Whipple's index measurement and their corresponding scales (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

Whipple's Index: A measure of age heaping that assesses the quality of age reporting in census or survey data by examining the tendency of people to report ages ending in 0 or 5.

Formula:

$\text{Whipple's Index} = \frac{\text{Sum of population at ages ending in 0 and 5 (ages 25-60)}}{\frac{1}{5} \times \text{Total population ages 23-62}} \times 100$

Interpretation Scales:

Whipple's Index Value Quality of Age Reporting
Less than 105 Highly accurate data
105 - 109.9 Fairly accurate data
110 - 124.9 Approximate data
125 - 174.9 Rough data
175 and above Very rough data

Note: A value of 100 indicates no age heaping (perfect distribution), while values significantly above 100 indicate preference for ages ending in 0 or 5, suggesting poor age reporting quality.

48.
The major consequence of population growth according to Malthus is poverty. Explain the impact of poverty on an individual (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

Malthusian Theory Context: Thomas Malthus argued that population grows geometrically while food supply grows arithmetically, leading to poverty when population outstrips resources.

Impact of Poverty on Individuals:

  1. Poor Health and Nutrition: Poverty limits access to adequate food, leading to malnutrition, weakened immune systems, stunted growth in children, and increased susceptibility to diseases.
  2. Limited Access to Healthcare: Poor individuals cannot afford quality healthcare services, medications, or preventive care, resulting in higher morbidity and mortality rates.
  3. Reduced Educational Opportunities: Poverty forces children to work instead of attending school, perpetuating the cycle of poverty through limited skills and employment opportunities in adulthood.
  4. Poor Living Conditions: Poverty results in inadequate housing, overcrowding, lack of clean water and sanitation, increasing exposure to infectious diseases and environmental hazards.
  5. Psychological and Social Impact: Poverty causes stress, anxiety, depression, low self-esteem, and social exclusion, affecting mental health and overall quality of life.
  6. Limited Economic Mobility: Lack of capital, credit access, and opportunities traps individuals in poverty, making it difficult to improve their economic status.
  7. Higher Mortality Risk: Poverty is associated with higher infant, child, and maternal mortality rates due to inadequate nutrition, healthcare, and unsafe living conditions.

Section 3: Long Answer Question

20 Marks
49.
The following data was extracted from Lokichogio Sub-county population campus.
Age Women Births
15-19 61,240 18,784
20-24 23,212 20,446
25-29 32,191 25,584
30-34 31,334 14,600
35-39 23,082 11,461
40-44 9,236 3,838
45-49 8,978 2,715

a) Compute the following demographic indicators:

i. General Fertility Rate

ii. Total Fertility Rate

b) Interpret your answers in a) i and ii above.

Show Answer

Solution:

a) i. General Fertility Rate (GFR)

Formula:

$\text{GFR} = \frac{\text{Total Births}}{\text{Total Women aged 15-49}} \times 1000$

Calculation:

Total Women (15-49) = 61,240 + 23,212 + 32,191 + 31,334 + 23,082 + 9,236 + 8,978 = 189,273

Total Births = 18,784 + 20,446 + 25,584 + 14,600 + 11,461 + 3,838 + 2,715 = 97,428

$\text{GFR} = \frac{97,428}{189,273} \times 1000 = 514.8$ per 1,000 women

a) ii. Total Fertility Rate (TFR)

Formula:

$\text{TFR} = 5 \times \sum \text{ASFR}$ (where ASFR is Age-Specific Fertility Rate)

$\text{ASFR} = \frac{\text{Births in age group}}{\text{Women in age group}} \times 1000$

Step 1: Calculate ASFR for each age group

Age Group Women Births ASFR (per 1,000)
15-19 61,240 18,784 $\frac{18,784}{61,240} \times 1000 = 306.7$
20-24 23,212 20,446 $\frac{20,446}{23,212} \times 1000 = 880.8$
25-29 32,191 25,584 $\frac{25,584}{32,191} \times 1000 = 794.8$
30-34 31,334 14,600 $\frac{14,600}{31,334} \times 1000 = 465.9$
35-39 23,082 11,461 $\frac{11,461}{23,082} \times 1000 = 496.5$
40-44 9,236 3,838 $\frac{3,838}{9,236} \times 1000 = 415.6$
45-49 8,978 2,715 $\frac{2,715}{8,978} \times 1000 = 302.4$

Step 2: Calculate TFR

Sum of ASFRs = 306.7 + 880.8 + 794.8 + 465.9 + 496.5 + 415.6 + 302.4 = 3,662.7

$\text{TFR} = 5 \times \frac{3,662.7}{1000} = 5 \times 3.663 = \mathbf{18.3}$ children per woman

b) Interpretation

i. General Fertility Rate (514.8 per 1,000 women):

  • This means that for every 1,000 women of reproductive age (15-49 years) in Lokichogio Sub-county, there are approximately 515 births per year.
  • This is an extremely high fertility rate, indicating very active childbearing in the population.
  • It suggests limited use of family planning, early marriage, cultural preferences for large families, and possibly high infant mortality leading to replacement births.

ii. Total Fertility Rate (18.3 children per woman):

  • This indicates that if a woman experiences the current age-specific fertility rates throughout her reproductive life, she would have approximately 18 children.
  • This is an extraordinarily high TFR, far exceeding typical values even in high-fertility countries (usually 5-7 children).
  • Note: Such an unusually high TFR may indicate data quality issues, possible errors in the census data, or unique demographic circumstances in this sub-county.
  • For comparison, replacement level fertility is 2.1 children per woman, and most developing countries have TFRs between 3-6.
  • This extremely high fertility has major implications for:
    • Rapid population growth
    • High dependency ratios (many children to support)
    • Strain on healthcare and education systems
    • Maternal health risks from frequent pregnancies
    • Economic challenges in providing for large families
