Part 2: Short Answer Questions

30 Marks
1
Describe the basic demographic equation. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Equation: $Pt = P0 + (B - D) + (I - E)$

It states that the population at a given time ($Pt$) is equal to the initial population ($P0$) plus the Natural Increase (Births $B$ minus Deaths $D$) plus the Net Migration (Immigration $I$ minus Emigration $E$) during the interval.

2
Describe the Kenya's population policy. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Kenya's policy focuses on managing population growth to align with economic resources. Key goals include:

  • Reducing Total Fertility Rate (target 2.6 by 2030).
  • Reducing infant, child, and maternal mortality.
  • Promoting family planning and reproductive health services.
  • Empowering women and youth.
  • Ensuring environmental sustainability amidst growth.

The population of Kenya was 28 million in 2012 with a growth rate of 3% p.a. The female population was 30% of the total population. (10 marks)

  1. What was the sex ratio?
  2. When will the population double?
  3. What was the doubling time?
  4. List any 5 factors that could have made the population to grow faster.
Show Answers

a) Sex Ratio:
Females = 30%
Males = 100% - 30% = 70%
Sex Ratio = (Males / Females) × 100 = (70/30) × 100 = 233.3
(Meaning 233 males for every 100 females).

b) When will it double?
Doubling Time ($Td$) = 70 / Rate
$Td$ = 70 / 3 = 23.33 years
Year = 2012 + 23.33 = 2035

c) Doubling Time:
23.33 years

d) Factors for fast growth:
1. High birth rates.
2. Decline in death rates due to medical advances.
3. Early marriage.
4. Low usage of family planning.
5. Cultural values favoring large families.

2
Describe any 5 methods of population control. (10 marks)
Show Answer

(Note: This question appears duplicated from the SAQ section in the source text, but carries 10 marks here requiring more detail.)

  1. Family Planning: Ensuring access to affordable contraceptives (condoms, pills, implants) allows couples to decide family size.
  2. Education: Educated women are more likely to pursue careers, marry later, and have fewer, healthier children.
  3. Legislative Actions: Laws raising the minimum marriage age prevent early childbearing, which lengthens generation time.
  4. Economic Incentives: Tax breaks or subsidies for smaller families encourage people to limit births voluntarily.
  5. Urbanization: Moving to cities often increases the cost of raising children, naturally discouraging large families compared to agrarian settings.
