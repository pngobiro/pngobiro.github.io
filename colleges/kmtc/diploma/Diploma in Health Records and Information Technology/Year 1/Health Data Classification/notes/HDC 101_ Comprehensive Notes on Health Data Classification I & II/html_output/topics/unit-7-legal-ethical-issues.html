UNIT 7: LEGAL AND ETHICAL ISSUES IN BIRTHS AND DEATHS

Definitions of Terms

Ethics is as a method, procedure, or perspective for deciding how to act and for analyzing complex problems and issues.

The death certificate -

A legal document needed:

  • To proceed with burial or cremation of the body
  • To execute the deceased person's will
  • By police or the pathologist to access the body
  • By clinician or hospital to report details of the death to national authorities (MOH, CRD and KNBS)
  • To enter details of death and circumstances into a database for reporting of national morbidity and mortality statistics.

Death registration process

  • Notification - an appropriate authority (assistant chief or health worker) fills aform confirming the death event
  • Medical certification - clinician completes the certificate of cause of death
  • Clinical coding - coder translates the diagnosis into alphanumeric as per theICD guidelines
  • Civil Registration - civil registrar legally registers the death at the civilregistration office
  • Civil Certification - civil registrar issues the legal death certificate

Laws of Kenya related to certificate of deaths

The Data Protection Act (No. 24 of 2019)

The Act recognizes certain rights of Data Subjects and imposes certain duties on Data Controllers/ Processors

Data Controllers/Processors who process data beyond a certain threshold will need to register with the Data Protection Commission

Calls for developing sector specific guidelines (sec. 74)

Section 46 - Health data

(1) Personal data relating to the health of a data subject may only be processed-

  • (a) by or under the responsibility of a health care provider; or
  • (b) by a person subject to the obligation of professional secrecy under any law.

(2) The condition under subsection (1) is met if the processing -

  • (a) is necessary for reasons of public interest in the area of public health; or
  • (b) is carried out by another person who in the circumstances owes a duty of confidentiality under any law.

Births and deaths Act - CAP. 149, Sec 18 (1-4)

"18: Certificates of cause of death
In the case of the death occurring in Kenya of any person who has been attended during his last illness by a medical practitioner, that practitioner shall sign a certificate stating to the best of his knowledge and belief the cause of death.

Every such certificate of cause of death shall be forwarded forthwith by the medical practitioner by whom it is signed to the registrar, and the medical practitioner, on signing a certificate as aforesaid, shall give in the prescribed form to some person required by this Act to give information concerning the death notice in writing of the signing of the certificate.

Where an inquest is held on the body of any deceased person, a medical certificate of the cause of death need not be forwarded to the registrar, but the magistrate holding the inquest shall send to the registrar a copy of his finding, which shall be sufficient.

Every medical practitioner who refuses or fails without reasonable excuse to comply with any of the provisions of this section shall be guilty of an offence and liable to a fine not exceeding two hundred shillings."

Who certifiers the cause of death?

Responsibility of a medical practitioner or any other person authorized by law.
In the event that a patient dies in a hospital or medical facility, it is the statutory duty of the clinician who has attended in the last illness to issue the medical certificate of cause of death (D1)

If a person dies at home, the register of death (D2) is completed by the assistant chief

Ethical principles in use of health data

Usually applied to human-subject research protocols
Applicable when clinical and public health data about an individual is collected and stored
Three fundamental principles established in the Belmont Report (18th April 1979), Helsinki Declaration (2013), and Nuremburg Code (1947):

  • Respect for persons
  • Beneficence
  • Justice

Respect for persons Seeks to:

  • protect the autonomy of all people
  • treat people with courtesy and respect
  • allow for informed consent
  • Disclosure of intent
  • Individuals are capable of providing consent
  • Individuals are not coerced into giving their consent

Beneficence seeks to:

  • "Do no harm."
  • Maximize benefits for the population
  • Minimize the risks to the individual person

Justice;

Aims to ensures that procedures are fairly and equally administered

Legal issues, privacy and confidentiality

  • Legal requirements regarding death certification include who is qualified to certify a death, time period within which certification must be made, etc.
  • Certifiers and coders should observe confidentiality, ethics and honesty about the cause of death
  • the patient's right to privacy and the confidentiality of the information contained within the certificates should be maintained at all times
  • this duty is to the family of the deceased person
  • The physical medical record is the property of the hospital and the information in the medical record is private and confidential

The disclosure is permissible only when

  • expressed or implied consent of the patient
  • when there is order of court
  • when the clinician or hospital interest cannot otherwise be safeguarded
  • when there exists higher duty (i.e. Public interest issue)

Adherence to relevant laws and regulations e.g. do not reveal the details of a certificate of cause of death to a third party unless:

  • are legally required to do so
  • have obtained prior consent from the next of kin of the deceased

Information in the certificate of cause of death can be used for research purposes, as long as the deceased is not identified by name or other means
