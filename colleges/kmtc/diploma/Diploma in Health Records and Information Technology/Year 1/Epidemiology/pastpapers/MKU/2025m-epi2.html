Section A

1.
Define the following terms: (5 Marks)

a) Cross product ratio

b) Attributable risk

Show Answer
  • a) Cross Product Ratio (Odds Ratio): A measure of association which compares the odds of disease in those exposed to the odds of disease in those unexposed. Calculated as $\frac{ad}{bc}$ in a 2x2 table.
  • b) Attributable Risk (Risk Difference): The difference in risk (incidence) between the exposed group and the unexposed group ($I_e - I_u$). It measures the excess risk that can be attributed to the exposure.
2.
Explain the scope of epidemiology (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Epidemiology covers broad areas including:

  1. Causation: Identifying genetic, environmental, and social determinants of disease.
  2. Natural History: Defining the biology and course of disease in populations.
  3. Description of Health Status: Measuring burden of disease, morbidity, and mortality trends.
  4. Evaluation of Interventions: Assessing the effectiveness and efficiency of health services (e.g., vaccines, screening).
  5. Policy Development: Providing data for public health regulations and resource allocation.
3.
Outline five properties of a proportion in epidemiology (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. The numerator is included in the denominator ($a / a+b$).
  2. It does not include a time dimension (unlike a rate).
  3. Values range between 0 and 1 (or 0% to 100%).
  4. It indicates the magnitude of a part relative to the whole.
  5. It is dimensionless (has no units).
4.
There were 5 deaths among 100 people exposed in total population of 1000 community members who were diagnosed with HIV/AIDS in 2022. Calculate case fatality rate? (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Definition: Case Fatality Rate (CFR) = $\frac{\text{Number of deaths from disease}}{\text{Number of diagnosed cases of that disease}} \times 100$

Identify Data:

  • Deaths = 5
  • Cases (Diagnosed with HIV/AIDS) = 100 (The phrasing "100 people... who were diagnosed" implies these are the cases).

Calculation:

$$CFR = \frac{5}{100} \times 100 = 5\%$$

  1. Preterm birth complications: Prematurity.

6.
Differentiate the following terms: (5 Marks)

a) Incidence rate and incidence proportion

b) Crude fertility rate and crude birth rate

Show Answer

a) Incidence Rate vs Proportion:

  • Incidence Rate (Density): Denominator is Person-Time (e.g., person-years). Measures the speed of disease occurrence.
  • Incidence Proportion (Cumulative): Denominator is Population at risk at the start. Measures the probability (risk) of getting disease over a period.

b) Crude Fertility Rate vs Crude Birth Rate:

  • General Fertility Rate: (Note: "Crude fertility rate" is non-standard, usually implies General Fertility Rate). Denominator is Women of reproductive age (15-49).
  • Crude Birth Rate: Denominator is the Total Mid-year Population.
7.
Explain uses of rate in public health (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. To measure the speed or frequency of disease occurrence.
  2. To compare disease burden between different populations or different times (because it accounts for population size).
  3. To identify high-risk groups.
  4. To evaluate the effectiveness of control measures.
  5. To make hypotheses about causation.
8.
Describe frequency measures commonly used in epidemiology and give formula. (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  • Ratio: Comparison of two independent quantities.
    Formula: $x / y$
  • Proportion: Numerator is part of the denominator.
    Formula: $\frac{x}{x+y} \times k$
  • Rate: Incorporates time dimension.
    Formula: $\frac{\text{Events}}{\text{Population at risk} \times \text{Time}}$

9.

The following table shows the individuals who were vaccinated with vaccine A and the effectiveness of the vaccine against cholera.

Status Disease present Disease absent Total
Exposed (Vaccinated?) 20 40 60
Non-exposed 40 80 120
Total 60 120 180

Note: In vaccine studies, "Exposed" usually means "Vaccinated" and "Non-exposed" means "Unvaccinated", or vice versa depending on context. Assuming Exposed = Vaccinated based on typical prompt structure.

  1. Calculate primary attack rate (5 Marks)
  2. Calculate the relative risk (5 Marks)
  3. Calculate vaccine efficacy (5 Marks)
Show Answer

a) Attack Rates (Incidence):

  • Attack Rate in Exposed (Vaccinated) $I_e$: $\frac{20}{60} = 0.333$ (33.3%)
  • Attack Rate in Non-Exposed (Unvaccinated) $I_u$: $\frac{40}{120} = 0.333$ (33.3%)
  • Primary Attack Rate (Overall): $\frac{60}{180} = 33.3\%$

b) Relative Risk (RR):

$$RR = \frac{I_e}{I_u} = \frac{0.333}{0.333} = 1.0$$

Interpretation: The risk is the same in both groups.

c) Vaccine Efficacy (VE):

Formula: $VE = \frac{I_u - I_e}{I_u} \times 100$

Calculation: $\frac{0.333 - 0.333}{0.333} \times 100 = 0\%$

(Note: Based on the numbers provided in the table, the vaccine has zero efficacy).

10.
Discuss five steps used in calculating YPLL (Years of Potential Life Lost) from frequency distribution. (15 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Determine the Cut-off Age: Establish the endpoint age (e.g., 65 or 75 years) or life expectancy used as the reference.
  2. Calculate Midpoint for Age Groups: For grouped data, determine the midpoint of each age interval (e.g., for 20-29, midpoint is 24.5).
  3. Calculate Years Lost per Group: Subtract the midpoint of each age group from the cut-off age to find years lost for a death in that group.
  4. Multiply by Number of Deaths: Multiply the years lost per group by the frequency (number of deaths) in that age group.
  5. Summation: Sum the total years lost across all age groups to get the total YPLL.
11.

In Coast level 5 Hospital in the year 2022, there were a total of 2000 patients and 500 were women of reproductive age, out of these, 500 were children below 5 years. Out of these patients, 10 had TB of which 5 died of it. Also 50 children died of polio.

Calculate:

  1. Crude mortality rate (5 Marks)
  2. TB mortality rate (5 Marks)
  3. Infant mortality rate (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Data Extraction:

  • Total Population (Patients): 2000
  • Children < 5 years: 500
  • TB Deaths: 5
  • Polio Deaths (Children): 50
  • Total Deaths: 55 (Assuming these are the only deaths mentioned).

a) Crude Mortality Rate (CMR):

$$\frac{\text{Total Deaths}}{\text{Total Population}} \times 1000$$

Calculation: $\frac{55}{2000} \times 1000 = 27.5$ per 1000.

b) TB Mortality Rate (Cause-Specific):

$$\frac{\text{Deaths from TB}}{\text{Total Population}} \times 1000$$

Calculation: $\frac{5}{2000} \times 1000 = 2.5$ per 1000.

c) Infant Mortality Rate (IMR):

Note on Interpretation: Standard IMR requires "Deaths < 1 year" and "Live Births". The data provided only gives "Children < 5 years" and "Deaths of children". In this exam context, calculate the Age-Specific Mortality Rate (0-5) or Child Mortality.

$$\frac{\text{Deaths in children}}{\text{Population of children}} \times 1000$$

Calculation: $\frac{50}{500} \times 1000 = 100$ per 1000.
