The overall sex ratio is determined by the following except?
A) Morbidity difference
B) Difference in migration
C) Difference in mortality
D) Sex ratio at birth
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Morbidity difference

Reasoning: Morbidity (sickness) does not directly change the sex count unless it leads to Mortality. The three direct determinants of sex ratio are Sex Ratio at Birth, Sex differentials in Mortality, and Sex differentials in Migration.

C) The average number of years a newborn infant can be expected to live

Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) The average number of years remaining for any person of any age

Reasoning: While C is also true (e0), D is the broader definition of Expectation of Life (ex) which applies to the entire table.

C) It is recommended by the UN

Correct Answer: A) The citizens residing abroad are excluded

Reasoning: De facto counts people where they are found on census night. Therefore, citizens who are not in the country are excluded.

In the year 2020, the net migration of Kenya was 103,537. If 178,987 people migrated out of the country that year, how many people migrated to the country?
A) 178,987
B) 103,537
C) 75,450
D) 282,524
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) 282,524

Calculation: Net = In - Out.

Part 2: Short Answer Questions

30 Marks
1

Define the following demography concepts (5 Marks):

  1. Fecundity
  2. Rate
  3. Standardization
  4. Child
  5. Stable population
Show Answers
  • Fecundity: The physiological capacity of a woman (or couple) to reproduce.
  • Rate: The frequency of a demographic event in a population during a specified time period (e.g., Birth Rate).
  • Standardization: A technique used to adjust rates (like death rates) to remove the effect of difference in population structure (e.g., age) to allow valid comparison.
  • Child: In demography, typically refers to an individual under 15 years, or specifically aged 1-4 for child mortality.
  • Stable population: A population with constant age-specific birth and death rates over a long period, resulting in a constant age structure and constant growth rate.
2
List any five measures of mortality of a population. (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Crude Death Rate (CDR)
  2. Infant Mortality Rate (IMR)
  3. Under-5 Mortality Rate (U5MR)
  4. Maternal Mortality Ratio (MMR)
  5. Age-Specific Death Rate (ASDR)
3
Describe five measures of fertility. (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Crude Birth Rate (CBR): Live births per 1,000 population.
  2. General Fertility Rate (GFR): Live births per 1,000 women aged 15-49.
  3. Age-Specific Fertility Rate (ASFR): Births per 1,000 women in a specific age group.
  4. Total Fertility Rate (TFR): Average number of children a woman would have if she passed through her childbearing years conforming to current ASFRs.
  5. Child-Woman Ratio (CWR): Ratio of children under 5 to women aged 15-49.
4
State five factors that affect population mortality rates. (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Availability and quality of healthcare services.
  2. Nutritional status of the population.
  3. Sanitation and clean water supply.
  4. Level of education (especially female education).
  5. Prevalence of infectious diseases (e.g., HIV/AIDS, Malaria).
5
Explain five factors that affect population structure of Kenya. (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Fertility Rates: High fertility creates a broad base (young population).
  2. Mortality Rates: Improving life expectancy widens the top of the pyramid.
  3. Migration: Rural-urban migration of working-age adults changes regional structures.
  4. HIV/AIDS Pandemic: Affects specific age groups, altering the structure.
  5. Government Policy: Family planning initiatives affecting birth rates.
6
Describe five sources of demographic data to be used in calculation of population mortality measures. (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Civil Registration System: Death certificates.
  2. Population Censuses: Questions on deaths in the household.
  3. Demographic and Health Surveys (DHS): Birth and death histories.
  4. Health Facility Records: Hospital death registers.
  5. Demographic Surveillance Systems (DSS): In specific monitoring sites.

Compute standardized rate for POPULATION B, using the direct method.

Note: Rates in table are per 100,000.

Show Calculation

Direct Standardization Formula:
$$\frac{\sum(Standard Population \times Index Age Specific Rate)}{\sum Standard Population}$$

Step 1: Calculate Expected Deaths
Using Population B rates on Population A's structure. Rates are per 100,000.

  • 0-4: $1,900 \times \frac{80.8}{100,000} = 1.54$
  • 5-14: $3,100 \times \frac{6.7}{100,000} = 0.21$
  • 15-44: $9,400 \times \frac{21.3}{100,000} = 2.00$
  • 45-64: $4,900 \times \frac{168}{100,000} = 8.23$
  • 65+: $2,000 \times \frac{960}{100,000} = 19.20$

Step 2: Sum Expected Deaths
$1.54 + 0.21 + 2.00 + 8.23 + 19.20 = \textbf{31.18}$

Step 3: Calculate Standardized Rate
Rate = $\frac{31.18}{21,300} \times 100,000$
Rate = \textbf{146.38 per 100,000}

Note on Calculation: The math in the exam question source table (Expected Deaths column) contained inconsistent figures (e.g., 1535.2 instead of 1.54). The calculation above follows the strictly correct Direct Method logic using the provided rates and populations.
