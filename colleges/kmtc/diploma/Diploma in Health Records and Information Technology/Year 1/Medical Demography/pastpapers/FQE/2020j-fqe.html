Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions

40 Marks
1.
Natural increase in human population refers to:
a) The sum of the number of births and number of deaths
b) The difference between the number of births and the number of deaths
c) The difference between population size at given time A and time B
d) The number of children born naturally without planning
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) The difference between the number of births and the number of deaths

Explanation: Natural increase refers to the change in population size resulting only from births and deaths, excluding migration. It is calculated as: Natural Increase = Births - Deaths. When births exceed deaths, there is natural increase (population growth). When deaths exceed births, there is natural decrease (population decline).

2.
The largest population that the resources of a given environment can support is known as the:
a) Population density
b) Population structure
c) Carrying capacity
d) Optimum population
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Carrying capacity

Explanation: Carrying capacity is the maximum population size of a species that an environment can sustain indefinitely given the available resources (food, water, habitat, etc.). It represents the environmental limit on population growth. When a population exceeds carrying capacity, resources become scarce, leading to increased mortality and decreased birth rates until the population stabilizes.

3.
In a standard population pyramid, the age range of each bar is:
a) One year
b) 5 years
c) 10 years
d) 15 years
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) 5 years

Explanation: Standard population pyramids use 5-year age groups (e.g., 0-4, 5-9, 10-14, etc.). This grouping provides sufficient detail to show age structure patterns while keeping the pyramid readable and manageable. The 5-year intervals are conventional in demographic analysis and allow for consistent international comparisons.

4.
Stage 5 of the demographic transition cycle shows:
a) Natural increase
b) Natural decrease
c) Population equilibrium
d) Maximum population
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Natural decrease

Explanation: Stage 5 of the Demographic Transition Model (DTM) represents a theoretical stage where death rates exceed birth rates, leading to natural population decrease. This stage is characterized by very low fertility rates (often below replacement level of 2.1) and an aging population. Countries like Japan and some European nations are experiencing or approaching this stage.

5.
A rise in the median age of a population is known as:
a) Demographic aging
b) Population dependency
c) Demographic transition
d) Young population
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Demographic aging

Explanation: Demographic aging (or population aging) refers to the increase in the median age of a population, typically resulting from declining fertility rates and/or increasing life expectancy. This leads to a higher proportion of elderly people in the population. It's a major demographic trend in developed countries and has significant implications for healthcare, pensions, and social services.

6.
Which of the following is the correct order of the stages of demographic transition?
a) Post-industrial, pre-industrial, transitional, industrial
b) Pre-industrial, industrial, post-industrial, transitional
c) Transitional, pre-industrial, industrial, post-industrial
d) Pre-industrial, transitional, industrial, post-industrial
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Pre-industrial, transitional, industrial, post-industrial

Explanation: The Demographic Transition Model follows this sequence:

  1. Pre-industrial (Stage 1): High birth and death rates, slow population growth
  2. Transitional (Stage 2): Death rates fall, birth rates remain high, rapid population growth
  3. Industrial (Stage 3): Birth rates begin to fall, death rates low, slower population growth
  4. Post-industrial (Stage 4): Low birth and death rates, stable or declining population
7.
Pro-natalist population policies encourage:
a) Family planning
b) Large families
c) Family ties
d) Small families
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Large families

Explanation: Pro-natalist policies are government measures designed to encourage higher birth rates and increase population growth. These may include: financial incentives for having children (baby bonuses, tax breaks), subsidized childcare, extended parental leave, and other benefits for larger families. Countries with declining populations (e.g., Singapore, Russia, Japan) often implement pro-natalist policies.

8.
In which year was the one-child policy introduced in China?
a) 1989
b) 1976
c) 1967
d) 1979
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) 1979

Explanation: China's one-child policy was introduced in 1979 as an anti-natalist measure to control rapid population growth. The policy restricted most urban couples to having only one child, with some exceptions for rural families and ethnic minorities. It was officially ended in 2015, replaced by a two-child policy, and later a three-child policy in 2021 due to aging population concerns.

9.
The child mortality rate concerns children who die before their:
a) First birthday
b) 28th day of birth
c) Fifth birthday
d) 10th birthday
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Fifth birthday

Explanation: Child mortality rate refers to deaths of children aged 1-4 years (between first and fifth birthday). This is distinct from:

  • Infant Mortality: Deaths before first birthday
  • Neonatal Mortality: Deaths within first 28 days
  • Under-5 Mortality (U5MR): Deaths before fifth birthday

The Under-5 Mortality Rate (U5MR) is often used as a key indicator of child health and overall development.

10.
The difference between migration in and migration out of a given country is termed as:
a) Immigration
b) Emigration
c) Net migration
d) Demographic equation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Net migration

Explanation: Net migration is calculated as: Immigration - Emigration

  • Positive net migration: More people moving in than out (population gain)
  • Negative net migration: More people moving out than in (population loss)
  • Zero net migration: Equal in and out movement

Net migration is a key component of population change alongside natural increase.

11.
The natural increase in human population is calculated by:
a) CBR - CDR
b) CBR + CDR
c) CDR - CBR
d) CDR + CBR
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) CBR - CDR

Explanation: The Rate of Natural Increase (RNI) is calculated by subtracting the Crude Death Rate (CDR) from the Crude Birth Rate (CBR). The result is usually expressed as a percentage (divided by 10) or per 1,000 population. Formula: RNI = (CBR - CDR) / 10.

12.
Demographic transition theory states that as the country becomes industrialized:
a) The death rate declines but not the birth rate
b) The birth rate declines and then the death rate declines
c) The birth rate and death rate decline at the same time
d) The death rate declines then the birth rate
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) The death rate declines then the birth rate

Explanation: The classic Demographic Transition Model describes a sequence where death rates fall first (Stage 2) due to improvements in public health, sanitation, and food supply. Birth rates remain high initially, causing rapid population growth. Later (Stage 3), birth rates begin to decline due to social changes, urbanization, and family planning.

13.
Which one of the following statements best defines population density?
a) The number of persons living in a given country
b) The number of persons per unit area
c) The distribution of people in terms of age and sex
d) The growth or decline in the number of people
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) The number of persons per unit area

Explanation: Population density is a measurement of population per unit area (e.g., per square kilometer or square mile). It indicates how crowded a place is. Formula: Density = Total Population / Total Land Area.

14.
Who predicted that population would increase by geometric progression, while food production would increase in arithmetic progression?
a) Karl Marx
b) Thomas Malthus
c) Jean Gottman
d) Edmund Hailley
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Thomas Malthus

Explanation: In his 1798 "Essay on the Principle of Population," Thomas Malthus argued that population grows geometrically (1, 2, 4, 8, 16...) while food supply grows only arithmetically (1, 2, 3, 4, 5...), leading inevitably to a "Malthusian catastrophe" of famine, disease, or war unless population growth is checked.

15.
Formal demography is the study of human population in respect to their:
a) Size, distribution, structure and change
b) Distribution, density, growth and decline
c) Distribution, sex, age groupings
d) Size, growth, number and density
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Size, distribution, structure and change

Explanation: Formal demography (or pure demography) deals with the quantitative study of human populations. Its four main pillars are:

  • Size: Total number of people
  • Distribution: Where people are located
  • Structure: Composition by age and sex
  • Change: Growth or decline over time (Dynamics)

16.
The demographic transition model was developed primarily to explain:
a) Population changes caused by large changes in birth rates
b) Effects of changing death and birth rates on total population
c) Effects of population movement from rural to urban areas
d) Effects of mortality and fertility on human development
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Effects of changing death and birth rates on total population

Explanation: The DTM explains the historical shift from high birth and death rates to low birth and death rates as a society modernizes, and how these changes impact the overall rate of population growth.

17.
The number of live births given per 1000 women of child bearing age is:
a) Age specific birth rate
b) Total fertility rate
c) General fertility rate
d) Crude birth rate
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) General fertility rate

Explanation:

  • General Fertility Rate (GFR): Live births per 1,000 women aged 15-49.
  • Crude Birth Rate (CBR): Live births per 1,000 total population.
  • Total Fertility Rate (TFR): Average number of children a woman would have in her lifetime.
GFR is more refined than CBR because it relates births to the population at risk of giving birth.

18.
The population structure may be presented in the form of?
a) Population pyramid
b) Life tables
c) Whipples index
d) Histogram
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Population pyramid

Explanation: A population pyramid (age-sex pyramid) is the standard graphical representation of population structure. It shows the distribution of various age groups in a population, separated by sex.

19.
Whipples index is used to address the issues of:
a) Age misreporting errors
b) Age heaping
c) Age shifting
d) Age reporting biases
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Age heaping

Explanation: Whipple's Index measures the tendency for individuals to inaccurately report their age as ending in specific digits (usually 0 and 5). This phenomenon is called "age heaping" or digit preference. It is a method to evaluate the quality of age data from censuses or surveys.

21.
The opposite of fecundity is:
a) Sterility
b) Fertility
c) Fecundability
d) Nuptiality
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Sterility

Explanation: Fecundity is the biological capacity to reproduce (potential). Sterility (or infecundity) is the lack of that biological capacity. Fertility, on the other hand, refers to actual reproductive performance (live births).

22.
Demographers study the following except:
a) Mortality
b) Morbidity
c) Fertility
d) Migration
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Morbidity

Explanation: The three core components of population change (vital events) studied in formal demography are Fertility (births), Mortality (deaths), and Migration (movement). Morbidity (disease/illness) is primarily the domain of epidemiology, though it is closely related to mortality studies in medical demography.

23.
The current sex ratio of a given population may be determined by the following except:
a) Sex ratio at birth
b) Difference in migration
c) Difference in mortality
d) Difference in morbidity
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Difference in morbidity

Explanation: The sex ratio of a population is determined by three factors: 1. The sex ratio at birth (usually ~105 boys per 100 girls). 2. Differential mortality rates (males typically have higher mortality). 3. Differential migration (one sex may migrate more than the other). Morbidity (sickness) itself does not change the count of males vs females unless it leads to death (mortality).

24.
Which one of the following is true about De Jure population count?
a) It only counts the people present
b) It is easy to trace individuals
c) It does not include tourists from other countries
d) It is done after every 10 years
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) It does not include tourists from other countries

Explanation: A De Jure census counts people according to their usual place of residence, regardless of where they are on census night. Therefore, it excludes temporary visitors like tourists. A De Facto census counts people where they are physically present on census night.

25.
Which one of the following factors does not contribute to population aging?
a) Increase in life expectancy
b) Decline in fertility
c) Improved maternal and child health
d) Increased fertility rates
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Increased fertility rates

Explanation: Increased fertility rates add more children to the base of the population pyramid, making the population younger on average. Population aging is driven by people living longer (increased life expectancy) and fewer children being born (decline in fertility), which shifts the balance toward older age groups.

28.
Which one of the following is not a tool of demography?

30.
A female aged 50 should be included in the denominator of which of the following measure:
a) Total fertility rate
b) Crude birth rate
c) Age specific fertility rate
d) Maternal mortality rate
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Crude birth rate

Explanation: The denominator for Crude Birth Rate is the total mid-year population, which includes everyone regardless of age or sex. TFR, ASFR, and MMR typically use the female population of reproductive age (15-49) as the denominator, so a 50-year-old woman would be excluded.

31.
Which one of the following is not suitable to present the data of males and females attending a certain meeting?
a) Rate
b) Ratio
c) Proportion
d) Count
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Rate

Explanation: A meeting attendance is a cross-sectional event (a snapshot). You can count attendees (Count), compare males to females (Ratio), or calculate the percentage of males (Proportion). A Rate requires a time dimension (events per person-time) or risk period, which doesn't apply to a static attendance list.

32.
The following indicate a positive growth rate of a population except:
a) Increase in fertility rate
b) Decrease in child mortality rate
c) Longer live span
d) Increase in child spacing

Correct Answer: d) Increase in child spacing

Explanation: Increasing the time between births (child spacing) typically reduces the total number of children a woman has in her lifetime (lowers fertility), thus slowing population growth. The other options (higher fertility, lower mortality, longer life) all contribute to population increase.

Section 2: Short Answer Questions

40 Marks
1.
Defining all the symbols used, describe briefly the balancing equation which relates to the number of people in a population at two points in time. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

The demographic balancing equation is:

$$ P_2 = P_1 + (B - D) + (I - E) $$

Where:

  • $P_2$: Population at the end of the period (Time 2).
  • $P_1$: Population at the beginning of the period (Time 1).
  • $B$: Number of Births during the period.
  • $D$: Number of Deaths during the period.
  • $I$: Number of Immigrants (people moving in).
  • $E$: Number of Emigrants (people moving out).

Description: The equation states that the population at a future date is equal to the initial population plus the natural increase (Births minus Deaths) plus the net migration (Immigration minus Emigration).

2.
What is population doubling time? Compute the doubling time for a population whose growth rate is 3%. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

Definition: Population doubling time is the number of years it takes for a population to double in size, assuming a constant rate of natural increase.

Computation:

Using the "Rule of 70":

$$ T_d = \frac{70}{r} $$

Where $r$ is the growth rate in percentage.

Given $r = 3\%$:

$$ T_d = \frac{70}{3} = 23.33 \text{ years} $$

The population will double in approximately 23.3 years.

8.
Indicate the formulae of the following measures: (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  • i) Infant Mortality Rate (IMR): $$ \frac{\text{Deaths under 1 year}}{\text{Live Births}} \times 1000 $$
  • ii) Crude Death Rate (CDR): $$ \frac{\text{Total Deaths}}{\text{Total Mid-year Population}} \times 1000 $$
  • iii) Prevalence Rate: $$ \frac{\text{Total Cases (Old + New)}}{\text{Total Population}} \times 10^n $$
  • iv) General Fertility Rate (GFR): $$ \frac{\text{Live Births}}{\text{Women aged 15-49}} \times 1000 $$
  • v) Child Woman Ratio (CWR): $$ \frac{\text{Children 0-4 years}}{\text{Women aged 15-49}} \times 1000 $$
