Part 2: Short Answer Questions

30 Marks
1

Explain the following terms as used in demography:

  • a) Sex ratio
  • b) Young population
  • c) Age heaping
  • d) Desiderata
  • e) Natural increase
Show Answers
  • a) Sex ratio: The ratio of males to females in a population, usually expressed as the number of males per 100 females.
  • b) Young population: A population with a high proportion of children and adolescents, typically indicated by a broad-based population pyramid (high fertility).
  • c) Age heaping: The tendency of people to report their ages ending in certain digits (usually 0 or 5) instead of their exact age during census enumeration.
  • d) Desiderata: The essential features or ideal requirements of a good census/registration system (e.g., Universality, Periodicity, Simultaneity).
  • e) Natural increase: The difference between the number of births and the number of deaths in a population over a given period.
2
Describe the basic demographic equation.
Show Answer

Equation: $Pt = P0 + (B - D) + (I - E)$

Where $Pt$ is the final population, $P0$ is the initial population. The equation shows that population change is driven by the balance of Births ($B$) and Deaths ($D$), and the net balance of Immigration ($I$) and Emigration ($E$).

3
Outline the importance of sex ratio.
Show Answer
  • Indicates the balance between males and females for marriage markets (nuptiality).
  • Helps in planning for sex-specific needs (e.g., maternal health for women).
  • Indicates migration patterns (e.g., male-dominated labor migration).
  • Used to analyze mortality differentials between sexes.
4

Explain the challenges of the following census counts:

  • a) De-facto
  • b) De-jure
Show Answer

a) De-facto challenges:

  • Difficult to capture people in transit (travellers).
  • Distorts regional population figures if there is temporary migration (e.g., people returning to rural homes for census).

b) De-jure challenges:

  • Risk of double counting (counted at residence and where found).
  • Complex definition of "usual residence" (e.g., polygamous homes, students).
  • Memory recall issues if the person is absent.
5
State the differences between census data and registration system.
Show Answer
  • Periodicity: Census is periodic (e.g., every 10 years); Registration is continuous.
  • Method: Census uses enumerators visiting households; Registration relies on individuals reporting events to authorities.
  • Scope: Census captures the stock (state) of the population; Registration captures the flow (vital events).
  • Cost: Census is highly expensive; Registration is part of routine administration.
6
State the features of registration systems.
Show Answer
  • Compulsory: Legal requirement.
  • Universal: Covers all people in the territory.
  • Continuous: Ongoing collection of data.
  • Confidential: Data protection.
  • Permanent: Records are archived.

Part 3: Long Answer Question

20 Marks
1
Describe the demographic transition theory clearly stating what happens in each stage.
Show Answer

The Demographic Transition Theory describes the change in a population from high birth and death rates to low birth and death rates as a country develops economically.

Stage 1: High Stationary
- High Birth Rates (Lack of contraception, need for farm labor).
- High Death Rates (Famine, disease, poor sanitation).
- Result: Population size remains low and stable.

Stage 2: Early Expanding
- Birth Rates remain High.
- Death Rates fall rapidly (Improvements in medicine, sanitation, food supply).
- Result: Rapid population growth (Population Explosion).

Stage 3: Late Expanding
- Birth Rates begin to fall (Urbanization, education of women, access to family planning).
- Death Rates continue to fall but more slowly.
- Result: Population continues to grow but at a slower rate.

Stage 4: Low Stationary
- Low Birth Rates.
- Low Death Rates.
- Result: Population size is high but stable (growth is zero or very low).
