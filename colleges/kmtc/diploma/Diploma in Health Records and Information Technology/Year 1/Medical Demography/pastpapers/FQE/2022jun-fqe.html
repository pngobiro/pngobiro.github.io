1.
Formal demography deals with all of the following except
a) Density of the population
b) Size of the population
c) Growth of the population
d) Age structure
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Density of the population

Formal demography focuses on the study of population size, growth, and structure (including age distribution). Population density refers to the number of people per unit area and is more related to geographic distribution rather than formal demographic analysis.

c) To implement policies

Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) To implement policies

Policy implementation is a legitimate administrative reason for age adjustments in demographic data. Options a, b, and d represent manipulative or incentive-driven reasons for age misreporting, which are common issues in demographic data collection.

4.
Which of these cohorts is commonly preferred when collecting age data
a) 5-0
b) 10-15
c) 5-1
d) 5-10
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) 5-10

The 5-10 age cohort (ages 5-9) is preferred because children in this age group are typically in school and more likely to accurately report their ages. Younger children (0-4) may have less accurate age reporting, and the other options are not standard cohort groupings.

5.
Sex ratio is much affected by the following factors except
a) The sex ratio at birth
b) Fertility rate
c) Difference in migration
d) Sex difference in mortality
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Fertility rate

Sex ratio is primarily affected by biological factors (sex ratio at birth), mortality differences between sexes, and migration patterns. Fertility rate affects population growth but doesn't directly influence the sex ratio of the existing population.

Correct Answer: d) To encourage people to marry

Population policies typically focus on controlling population growth through fertility reduction and managing migration patterns. Encouraging marriage is not a standard goal of population policy; in fact, some policies might focus on family planning regardless of marital status.

7.
When calculating Whipple's index, the denominator considers ages between
a) 21 and 52 years
b) 23 and 62 years
c) 21 and 62 years
d) 23 and 52 years
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) 23 and 62 years

Whipple's index measures age heaping by comparing the number of persons reported in ages ending with 0 or 5 to those in adjacent ages. It uses ages 23-62 as the denominator because these ages are less likely to be affected by age misreporting.

8.

Sex ratio is conventionally expressed as the number of males per 100 females (or sometimes per 1,000 females) in a population. It measures the balance between males and females in a given population.

12.
The following are errors experienced in demographic data except
a) Errors of coverage
b) Errors of contents
c) Sampling errors
d) Individual bias errors
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Sampling errors

Sampling errors occur in sample surveys, not in complete enumeration methods like censuses. Demographic data collection errors include coverage errors (missing or duplicate individuals), content errors (inaccurate reporting), and individual biases (deliberate misreporting).

13.

Correct Answer: d) Fecundity

Fecundity refers to the biological ability to reproduce, while fertility measures actual childbearing performance. Mortality is death rate, and natality is birth rate.

14.
Which of the following is among the causes of population growth?
a) Net increase
b) Migration
c) Mortality
d) Morbidity
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Migration

Migration (both immigration and emigration) directly affects population size. Mortality and morbidity reduce population, while net increase is a result of population growth, not a cause.

15.
The following causes change in a population in a given geographical area except
a) People crossing the border into it
b) People born in it
c) People dying in it
d) People getting sick in it
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) People getting sick in it

Morbidity (illness) doesn't change population size directly - only births, deaths, and migration affect population numbers. Immigration, births, and deaths are the three components of population change.

16.
The following is the characteristic of crude birth rate except
a) It includes both sexes in the denominator
b) It is expressed per 1000 population
c) It is influenced by sex
d) It includes all ages in the denominator
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) It is influenced by sex

Crude birth rate uses total population (both sexes, all ages) as denominator and is expressed per 1,000 population. Sex composition affects the rate indirectly through population structure, but the rate itself is not sex-specific.

Dependency ratio measures the proportion of dependent population (young children and elderly) relative to the working-age population. High dependency ratios indicate either young populations (high youth dependency) or aging populations (high elderly dependency).

Life tables can be complete (showing mortality for each year of age) or abridged (showing mortality for age groups, typically 5-year intervals). Both are used in demographic analysis.

23.
Fertility can be studied as
c) Period and cohort
a) Period and sex
d) Period and age
b) Cohort and sex
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Period and cohort

Fertility can be analyzed from a period perspective (fertility rates for a specific time period) or cohort perspective (fertility experience of women born in the same time period). Age and sex are components used in fertility measures but not the primary dimensions of analysis.

32.
Why is crude birth rate sometimes misleading?
a) It doesn't take the infant mortality rate into account
b) It doesn't focus on women of childbearing age
c) It doesn't take the childhood mortality rate into account
d) It doesn't always use accurate population numbers
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) It doesn't focus on women of childbearing age

The Crude Birth Rate (CBR) uses the total population as the denominator, including men, children, and elderly women who cannot bear children. This makes it "crude" and potentially misleading when comparing populations with different age structures. The General Fertility Rate (GFR), which focuses only on women of reproductive age (15-49), provides a more accurate measure of fertility.

33.
Which of the following is an important determinant of population change?
a) Migration
b) Human population report
c) Net attendance ratio
d) Life expectancy
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Migration

The three fundamental determinants of population change are: Births (fertility), Deaths (mortality), and Migration (in-migration and out-migration). The population change equation is: Population Change = (Births - Deaths) + (Immigration - Emigration). Life expectancy is an outcome of mortality patterns, not a direct determinant.

34.
Which of the following cannot be drawn from the pyramid of a country?
a) Dependency rate
b) Population growth rate
c) Gender distribution within population
d) Total population size
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Population growth rate

A population pyramid shows the age-sex structure of a population at a specific point in time. From it, you can determine: dependency ratios (proportion of young and old), gender distribution (sex ratio), and total population size (sum of all bars). However, the growth rate requires data from multiple time periods or additional information about births, deaths, and migration rates, which cannot be directly calculated from a single pyramid.

What is the meaning of "demographic dividend"?
a) Working population of 15 to 59 years
b) 0-6 years population
c) Working population of 14 to 50 years
d) Working population above 60 years
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Working population of 15 to 59 years

The demographic dividend refers to the economic growth potential that results from shifts in a population's age structure, particularly when the working-age population (typically 15-59 or 15-64 years) is larger than the dependent population (children and elderly). This creates a window of opportunity for accelerated economic growth if the right policies are in place to harness this productive workforce.

Section 2: Short Answer Questions

40 Marks (5 marks each)
41.
State any five reasons why HRIO should learn medical demography
Show Answer

Answer:

Health Records and Information Officers (HRIOs) should learn medical demography for the following reasons:

  1. Health Planning and Resource Allocation: Understanding population size, structure, and distribution helps in planning health services and allocating resources appropriately to different demographic groups.
  2. Disease Surveillance and Epidemiology: Demographic data is essential for calculating disease rates, identifying high-risk populations, and monitoring health trends over time.
  3. Vital Statistics Management: HRIOs manage birth and death records, which are fundamental demographic data sources requiring knowledge of demographic concepts and measures.
  4. Health Information Analysis: Demographic knowledge enables proper analysis and interpretation of health data by age, sex, and other population characteristics.
  5. Policy Development and Evaluation: Demographic information supports evidence-based health policy formulation and evaluation of health program effectiveness across different population segments.
42.
State any five requirements for registration systems
Show Answer

Answer:

Requirements for effective vital registration systems include:

  1. Legal Framework: Comprehensive legislation mandating registration of vital events (births, deaths, marriages, divorces) with clear penalties for non-compliance.
  2. Universal Coverage: The system must cover the entire population within a defined territory without exclusions.
  3. Continuous Operation: Registration must be ongoing and available throughout the year, not limited to specific periods.
  4. Trained Personnel: Adequate numbers of trained registrars and staff to collect, process, and maintain registration records accurately.
  5. Standardized Forms and Procedures: Uniform registration forms, clear procedures, and standardized definitions to ensure data quality and comparability.
  6. Accessibility: Registration facilities should be easily accessible to all citizens, including remote and marginalized populations.
  7. Data Security and Confidentiality: Systems to protect personal information while allowing appropriate access for statistical and administrative purposes.

List the meaning of the following abbreviations as used in life tables
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. dx: Number of deaths occurring between age x and x+1 (or x+n in an abridged life table)
  2. nmx: Age-specific death rate for the age interval x to x+n; the central death rate

45.
Define the following demographic rates:
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Infant Mortality Rate (IMR): The number of deaths of infants under one year of age per 1,000 live births in a given year. Formula: $\text{IMR} = \frac{\text{Deaths under 1 year}}{\text{Live births}} \times 1000$
  2. Crude Death Rate (CDR): The number of deaths per 1,000 population in a given year. Formula: $\text{CDR} = \frac{\text{Total deaths}}{\text{Mid-year population}} \times 1000$
  3. Total Fertility Rate (TFR): The average number of children a woman would have during her reproductive years (15-49) if she experienced the current age-specific fertility rates throughout her childbearing life. It is the sum of all ASFRs.
  4. Crude Birth Rate (CBR): The number of live births per 1,000 population in a given year. Formula: $\text{CBR} = \frac{\text{Total live births}}{\text{Mid-year population}} \times 1000$
  5. Stillbirths: The delivery of a fetus that shows no signs of life (no breathing, heartbeat, or voluntary muscle movement) after complete expulsion or extraction from the mother, typically after 28 weeks of gestation or weighing at least 1,000 grams.

UNICEF's approach to improving birth registration includes:

  1. Legal and Policy Reform: Advocating for laws that make birth registration free, compulsory, and accessible to all children regardless of parents' legal status

47.
Differentiate between direct method of population standardization and indirect method of population standardization
Show Answer

Answer:

Direct Method of Standardization:

  • Uses age-specific rates from the population being studied (index population)
  • Applies these rates to a standard population structure
  • Calculates expected deaths/events in each age group of the standard population
  • Requires detailed age-specific rates from the study population
  • Produces directly standardized rates that can be compared across populations
  • Limitation: Requires large sample sizes to calculate stable age-specific rates

Indirect Method of Standardization:

  • Uses age-specific rates from a standard population
  • Applies these rates to the age structure of the study population
  • Calculates expected deaths/events in the study population
  • Compares observed deaths to expected deaths (Standardized Mortality Ratio - SMR)
  • Useful when age-specific rates in the study population are unreliable due to small numbers
  • Advantage: Can be used with small populations where direct standardization would be unstable

Key Difference: Direct method uses rates from the study population applied to a standard structure, while indirect method uses standard rates applied to the study population's structure.

48.
State any five reasons why someone may decide to exaggerate their ages
Show Answer

Answer:

Reasons for age exaggeration include:

  1. Access to Age-Restricted Benefits: To qualify for old-age pensions, social security, or senior citizen benefits that have minimum age requirements
  2. Retirement Eligibility: To meet mandatory retirement age requirements and access retirement benefits earlier
  3. Social Status and Respect: In some cultures, older age commands greater respect and authority, motivating people to claim to be older
  4. Avoiding Military Service: Claiming to be older than the conscription age limit to avoid mandatory military service
  5. Employment Purposes: Exaggerating age to appear more experienced or mature for certain job positions
  6. Legal Advantages: To avoid prosecution as a minor or to be tried as an adult in legal proceedings, depending on the situation
  7. Marriage Eligibility: To meet minimum legal age requirements for marriage in jurisdictions with age restrictions

Stage 1: High Stationary (Pre-Industrial)

  • Birth Rate: High (35-40 per 1,000)
  • Death Rate: High (30-35 per 1,000)
  • Population Growth: Very slow or stagnant
  • Characteristics: High infant mortality, poor healthcare, limited food supply, short life expectancy, large families needed for labor
  • Examples: Pre-industrial societies, some remote populations

Stage 2: Early Expanding (Developing)

  • Birth Rate: Remains high (35-40 per 1,000)
  • Death Rate: Rapidly declining (15-20 per 1,000)
  • Population Growth: Very rapid (population explosion)
  • Characteristics: Improved healthcare, sanitation, and nutrition; reduced infant mortality; birth rates remain high due to cultural factors
  • Examples: Many sub-Saharan African countries, parts of South Asia

Stage 3: Late Expanding (Maturing)

  • Birth Rate: Declining (15-25 per 1,000)
  • Death Rate: Low and stable (10-15 per 1,000)
  • Population Growth: Slowing down
  • Characteristics: Urbanization, increased education (especially for women), access to contraception, changing family values, economic development
  • Examples: Brazil, Mexico, India, Kenya (transitioning)

Stage 4: Low Stationary (Developed)

  • Birth Rate: Low (10-15 per 1,000)
  • Death Rate: Low (8-12 per 1,000)
  • Population Growth: Very slow or zero
  • Characteristics: High standard of living, advanced healthcare, high education levels, small family sizes, aging population
  • Examples: USA, UK, Canada, Australia

Stage 5: Declining Population (Post-Industrial) - Optional

  • Birth Rate: Very low (below 10 per 1,000)
  • Death Rate: Low but may exceed birth rate
  • Population Growth: Negative (population decline)
  • Characteristics: Aging population, very low fertility (below replacement level of 2.1), potential labor shortages
  • Examples: Japan, Germany, Italy, some Eastern European countries

Key Implications:

  • Demographic Dividend: Stage 3 offers economic opportunities when the working-age population is large
  • Dependency Ratios: High in Stages 1-2 (youth dependency) and Stage 5 (elderly dependency)
  • Policy Implications: Different stages require different health, education, and social policies
  • Universal Pattern: Most developed countries have followed this pattern, though timing and speed vary
