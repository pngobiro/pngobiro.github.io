21
Describe the basic demographic equation. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Equation: $Pt = P0 + (B - D) + (I - E)$

Where:

  • Pt: Population at end of period
  • P0: Population at start of period
  • B: Births
  • D: Deaths
  • I: Immigration
  • E: Emigration

It states that population change is the result of Natural Increase (Births - Deaths) and Net Migration (Immigration - Emigration).

22
Describe the Kenya's population policy. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Kenya's population policy (Sessional Paper No. 3 of 2012) aims to manage population growth to match available resources. Key targets include:

  • Reducing the Total Fertility Rate (target was often ~3 children per woman).
  • Reducing mortality rates (infant, child, maternal).
  • Improving family planning uptake.
  • Promoting gender equality and empowerment.
  • Managing urbanization and environmental sustainability.

The population of Kenya was 28 million in 2012 with a growth rate of 3% p.a. The female population was 30% of the total population. (20 marks)

  1. What was the sex ratio?
  2. When will the population double?
  3. What was the doubling time?
  4. List any 5 factors that could have made the population to grow faster.
Show Answers

a) Sex Ratio:
Females = 30%
Males = 100% - 30% = 70%
Sex Ratio = (Males/Females) × 100 = (70/30) × 100 = 233.3
(Note: This implies 233 males for every 100 females, which is a theoretical result based on the exam figures.)

b) When will population double?
Doubling Time (Td) = 70 / Rate (Rule of 70)
Td = 70 / 3 = 23.33 years
Year = 2012 + 23.33 = 2035

c) Doubling Time:
23.33 years (calculated above).

d) Factors for fast growth:
1. High fertility rates (culture, lack of contraceptives).
2. Rapid decline in death rates (better health) while birth rates stay high.
3. Early marriage age.
4. High immigration rates.
5. Momentum of a young population structure.

30
Describe any 5 methods of population control. (20 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Family Planning/Contraception: Promoting the use of condoms, pills, coils, and injections to space or limit births.
  2. Education of Women: Educated women tend to marry later and have fewer children, focusing on careers and child quality over quantity.
  3. Legislative Measures (Policies): Government policies setting minimum age for marriage or incentivizing smaller families (e.g., tax breaks for fewer kids).
  4. Economic Development: Industrialization and urbanization generally lead to a demographic transition where small families are economically advantageous.
  5. Public Awareness Campaigns: Educating the public on the benefits of small families and the consequences of overpopulation.
