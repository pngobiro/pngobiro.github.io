Instructions

  • Write the EXAMINATION NUMBER given on all the answer sheets provided and on the question paper.

Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions

40 Marks
1.
The study of demography is mostly conceived by the following except
a) Fertility
b) Mortality
c) Migrations
d) Morbidity
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Morbidity

Explanation: Demography is primarily the statistical study of populations, focusing on three main processes: fertility (births), mortality (deaths), and migration. While morbidity (illness) is related to health demography, the core components defining population change are fertility, mortality, and migration.

2.
The number of years it takes a population to be twice its size is known as
a) Whipples index
b) Population growth
c) Doubling time
d) Doubling point
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Doubling time

Explanation: Doubling time is the period of time required for a quantity to double in size or value. In demography, it refers to the time it takes for a population to double in size at a constant growth rate.

3.
The factors in demographic studies that make people move to a place are called
a) Push factors
b) Pull factors
c) Politics
d) Transitions
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Pull factors

Explanation: Pull factors are positive conditions or attributes of a destination that attract migrants, such as better job opportunities, safety, or better climate. Push factors drive people away from their origin.

4.
The change of a population over a period of time is best described in which theory
a) Demographic transition theory
b) Population balancing theory
c) Enumeration theory
d) Digit preference theory
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Demographic transition theory

Explanation: The Demographic Transition Theory describes the transition from high birth and death rates to lower birth and death rates as a country or region develops from a pre-industrial to an industrialized economic system.

5.
The process of adjusting the data from demographic processes is known as
a) Adjustment
b) Standardization
c) Editing
d) Verification
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Standardization

Explanation: Standardization is a method used to compare rates (like death rates) between populations with different age structures by adjusting for those differences, allowing for a fair comparison.

6.
The population policy advocates for ____ number of children per woman in Kenya
a) 3
b) 4
c) 2
d) 5
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) 2

Explanation: Kenya's population policy generally aims for a replacement level fertility, which is approximately 2 children per woman, to stabilize population growth.

7.
Which name is given to those who work in a population set up
a) Dependents
b) Reliables
c) Economically active
d) Pensioners
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Economically active

Explanation: The economically active population, also known as the labor force, comprises people who are employed or unemployed but looking for work, typically within the working-age range (15-64).

8.
Which of the following is not a source of demographic data
a) Surveys
b) Clergy
c) Census
d) Vital statistics records
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Clergy

Explanation: The main sources of demographic data are censuses, sample surveys, and vital registration systems (births, deaths, marriages). "Clergy" is not a standard data source, although church records can be a historical source.

9.
The physiological ability of a woman to give birth is known as
a) Natality
b) Fertility
c) Fecundity
d) Parity
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Fecundity

Explanation: Fecundity refers to the biological potential or physiological capacity to reproduce. Fertility, on the other hand, refers to the actual reproductive performance (number of live births).

10.
Which of the following is not a demographic tool
a) Rate
b) Ration
c) Proportion
d) Density
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Ration

Explanation: The standard tools for demographic measurement are Rates (frequency of events), Ratios (relation of one subgroup to another), and Proportions (relation of a subgroup to the total). "Ration" is not a demographic tool; the correct term is Ratio.

11.
The kind of population count that counts those present on census night only is called
a) De facto
b) De jure
c) Census
d) Enumeration
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) De facto

Explanation: A de facto census counts people where they are actually found at a specific moment (e.g., census night), regardless of their usual residence. A de jure census counts people at their usual place of residence.

12.
Tendency of people changing their ages to receive benefits is known as
a) Age shifting
b) Age heaping
c) Digit preference
d) Age adjustments
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Age shifting

Explanation: Age shifting involves misreporting age, often to cross a threshold for benefits (e.g., pension eligibility or voting age). Age heaping or digit preference refers to rounding ages to numbers ending in 0 or 5.

13.
The most common tool used to adjust ages of people is called
a) Myers blended index
b) Whipples index
c) UN age preferences
d) Age adjusting index
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Whipples index

Explanation: Whipple's Index is a widely used method to measure age heaping or digit preference (specifically for ages ending in 0 and 5). Myers' Blended Index is also used but Whipple's is often cited as a primary tool for detecting this specific type of error.

14.
The following are uses of demographic date except
a) Planning
b) Health indicators
c) Surveys
d) Political use
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Surveys

Explanation: Planning, health indicators, and political use (e.g., redistricting) are *uses* of demographic data. A survey is a *method* of collecting that data, not a use of the data itself.

15.
Demographic transition theory took place in ....... Stages
a) 2
b) 3
c) 1
d) 4
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) 4

Explanation: The classic Demographic Transition Model typically describes 4 stages: 1) High Stationary (High Birth/Death), 2) Early Expanding (High Birth, Falling Death), 3) Late Expanding (Falling Birth, Low Death), and 4) Low Stationary (Low Birth/Death). Some models add a 5th stage.

16.
The relationship between the economically active population and economically inactive is known as
a) Employment ratio
b) Dependency ratio
c) Critical ratio
d) Economic ratio
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Dependency ratio

Explanation: The dependency ratio measures the ratio of dependents (people younger than 15 or older than 64) to the working-age population (15-64). It indicates the economic burden the productive portion of the population must carry.

17.
Total count of people in a specified boundary is known as
a) Demography
b) Counting
c) Census
d) Population
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Census

Explanation: A census is the total count of a population within a specified boundary at a specific time.

18.
When the sex ratio is more than 100% it implies that
a) Population growth is constant
b) Females are migrating one
c) Females are more than males
d) Male mortality is low
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Male mortality is low

Explanation: A sex ratio is typically defined as (Males / Females) * 100. If it is > 100, there are more males than females. This could imply high female mortality, high male immigration, or low male mortality relative to females. Option (d) is a plausible contributor, though simply "Males are more than females" would be the direct meaning.

19.
Which of the following measures the number of births per 1000 women per year
a) Crude birth rate
b) Age specific fertility
c) General fertility rate
d) Total fertility rate
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) General fertility rate

Explanation: The General Fertility Rate (GFR) is the number of live births per 1000 women of childbearing age (usually 15-49) in a given year. Crude Birth Rate is per 1000 total population.

20.
Population censuses are of ____ types
a) 4
b) 6
c) 3
d) 2
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) 2

Explanation: The two main types of population census are De Facto (counting people where they are present) and De Jure (counting people where they usually reside).

21.
Rate of natural increase is equal to
a) MI + MO
b) B - D
c) D + B
d) MI + B
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) B - D

Explanation: The Rate of Natural Increase (RNI) is calculated as the crude birth rate minus the crude death rate (B - D). It excludes migration.

22.
Which of the following is not a feature of population census
a) Universality
b) Compilation and publication
c) Covers groups in population
d) Covers specified area
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Covers groups in population

Explanation: The essential features of a census are: Individual enumeration, Universality within a defined territory, Simultaneity, and Periodicity. While it produces data on groups, the process itself must cover *everyone* (Universality), not just specific "groups".

23.
Which of the following is not a source of content error
a) Enumeration done at night
b) Editing process
c) Coding process
d) Compilation and tabulation process
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Enumeration done at night

Explanation: Content errors relate to the accuracy of the data collected (e.g., wrong age, wrong sex). Errors in editing, coding, and tabulation can introduce content errors. The time of enumeration (night vs day) is a procedural detail, not a direct source of *content* error unless it leads to missed people (coverage error).

24.
Demographic transition theory was first described in this year
a) 1970
b) 1940
c) 1950
d) 1960
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) 1940

Explanation: The original Demographic Transition model was suggested by Warren Thompson in 1929, but Frank Notestein formalized the theory in 1945 (often cited as the 1940s).

25.
Measures of fertility include the following except
a) Gross reproduction rate
b) Total fertility rate
c) Natality rate
d) Child woman ratio
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Natality rate

Explanation: Gross Reproduction Rate, Total Fertility Rate, and Child Woman Ratio are standard fertility measures. "Natality rate" is a less precise term often used interchangeably with Crude Birth Rate, but in technical demography lists, it's often the outlier compared to the specific rates listed.

26.
Total number of deaths in a population from all causes is called
a) A.S.D.R.
b) I.M.R.
c) M.M.R.
d) C.D.R.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) C.D.R.

Explanation: C.D.R. stands for Crude Death Rate, which measures the total number of deaths in a population per 1,000 people per year, regardless of cause.

27.
How many types of age misreporting exist
a) 2
b) 4
c) 3
d) 7
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) 2

Explanation: The two main types of age misreporting are "Age Heaping" (digit preference) and "Age Shifting" (systematic over- or under-reporting of age).

28.
The arrangement of populations in space at a given time among residents is
a) Size
b) Structure
c) Distribution
d) Density
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Distribution

Explanation: Population distribution refers to how people are spread across a specific area or space.

29.
Which shape of a population pyramid depicts increased pandemic
a) Pyramidal
b) Rectangular
c) Hour glass
d) Oval
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Hour glass

Explanation: An "hour glass" shape indicates a hollowed-out middle section, representing high mortality in the young adult/middle-age groups, which is characteristic of pandemics like HIV/AIDS that affect the sexually active/working-age population.

30.
Deaths from specific cause are called
a) Maternal deaths
b) Mortality
c) Fatality
d) Cause specific deaths
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Cause specific deaths

Explanation: Cause-specific death rates measure deaths attributed to a specific cause (e.g., cancer, accidents) within a population.

31.
The following are measures of age according to W.H.O. except
a) Childhood
b) Old age
c) Teenage
d) Infancy
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Teenage

Explanation: WHO standard age groupings typically refer to Infancy, Childhood, Adolescence (not just "teenage"), Adult, and Old Age. "Teenage" is a colloquial term, whereas "Adolescence" is the technical term.

32.
The number of deaths that occur in the first one year of life are called
a) Infant mortality
b) Neonatal mortality
c) Child mortality
d) Perinatal mortality
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Infant mortality

Explanation: Infant mortality refers specifically to the death of a child before their first birthday (0-11 months).

33.
Which of the following is not an impact of high population density
a) Availability of resources
b) Availability of ready labour
c) Shrinking natural resources
d) Overcrowding of public facilities
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Availability of resources

Explanation: High population density typically strains resources, leading to scarcity (shrinking natural resources), not increased availability. It does provide ready labor and cause overcrowding.

34.
In population balancing equation pt1 donates
a) Population at the start of period
b) Birth
c) Population at the end of the period
d) Midyear population
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Population at the start of period

Explanation: In the equation $P_2 = P_1 + (B-D) + (I-E)$, $P_1$ (or $Pt_1$) represents the initial population at the beginning of the time period.

35.
Which of the following is not a source of demographic data
a) Census
b) Demographic surveys
c) Hospital records
d) Transitions
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Transitions

Explanation: Census, surveys, and administrative records (like hospital records) are data sources. "Transitions" is a concept (like Demographic Transition), not a data source.

36.
The age that divides a population into 2 equal sized groups is called
a) Average age
b) Median age
c) Older age
d) Model age
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Median age

Explanation: The median age is the age that divides a population into two numerically equal groups - half the people are younger than this age and half are older.

37.
Cause specific death is defined as
a) Death to all people in a population
b) Death from specific disease
c) Death due to outbreak of a disease
d) Death to infants and mothers
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Death from specific disease

Explanation: Cause-specific death refers to deaths attributed to a particular cause or disease.

38.
The two ways of approaching fertility studies include ____ and ____
a) Period and cohort
b) Age and sex
c) Month and years
d) Place and sex
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Period and cohort

Explanation: Fertility analysis is typically approached in two ways: Period analysis (looking at fertility in a specific time period, e.g., one year) and Cohort analysis (following the fertility of a specific group of women born in the same year throughout their reproductive lives).

39.
When the index is below 105 the interpretation of Whipples index would be
a) Accurate
b) Rough
c) Highly accurate
d) Very rough
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Highly accurate

Explanation: Whipple's Index ranges: < 105=Highly Accurate; 105-110=Fairly Accurate; 110-125=Approximate; 125-175=Rough;> 175 = Very Rough.

40.
The decrease in number of people in a place is defined as
a) Population growth
b) Population stagnation
c) Population decline
d) Population reduction
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Population decline

Explanation: Population decline (or depopulation) refers to a reduction in the size of a human population in an area.

Section 2: Short Answer Questions

40 Marks
1.
List the factors that cause population growth (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. High Birth Rate: More births than deaths lead to natural increase.
  2. Low Death Rate: Improvements in healthcare and sanitation reduce mortality.
  3. Immigration: People moving into a country increase its population.
  4. Lack of Family Planning: Limited access to contraceptives leads to higher fertility.
  5. Cultural/Religious Beliefs: Some cultures encourage large families.
  6. Early Marriage: Increases the reproductive span of women.
2.
List the methods of population control (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Contraception/Family Planning: Use of pills, condoms, IUDs, etc.
  2. Education (especially for women): Educated women tend to have fewer children.
  3. Government Policies: Laws limiting family size (e.g., China's former one-child policy) or incentives for small families.
  4. Raising Marriage Age: Delays childbearing.
  5. Sterilization: Permanent method for men (vasectomy) or women (tubal ligation).
  6. Abortion: Termination of pregnancy (where legal).
3.
State the W.H.O. measure of ages (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer: WHO standard age groupings typically include:

  1. Infancy: 0 to 1 year (or 0-11 months).
  2. Childhood: 1 to 9 years.
  3. Adolescence: 10 to 19 years.
  4. Adult: 20 to 59 years (sometimes further divided into young adult, middle age).
  5. Elderly/Old Age: 60 years and above.

Note: Specific cut-offs can vary slightly by context, but these are general WHO standards.

4.
State the uses of demographic data (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Development Planning: For housing, schools, hospitals, and infrastructure.
  2. Policy Formulation: Designing policies on health, migration, and employment.
  3. Resource Allocation: Distributing government funds based on population size and needs.
  4. Market Research: Businesses use data to target specific consumer groups.
  5. Electoral Boundaries: Determining constituencies based on population distribution.
  6. Health Monitoring: Tracking disease prevalence and mortality rates.
5.
State any 5 fertility measures (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Crude Birth Rate (CBR): Live births per 1,000 population.
  2. General Fertility Rate (GFR): Live births per 1,000 women aged 15-49.
  3. Age-Specific Fertility Rate (ASFR): Births to women of a specific age group.
  4. Total Fertility Rate (TFR): Average number of children a woman would have in her lifetime.
  5. Gross Reproduction Rate (GRR): Average number of daughters a woman would have.
  6. Child-Woman Ratio (CWR): Ratio of children 0-4 to women 15-49.
6.
Discuss the method of adjusting demographic data (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer: Methods for adjusting demographic data to correct errors include:

  1. Smoothing/Graduation: Using mathematical formulas to smooth out irregularities in age distributions (e.g., removing age heaping).
  2. Standardization: Adjusting rates (like death rates) to a standard age structure to allow comparison between populations.
  3. Prorating: Distributing "unknown" or "not stated" cases proportionally across known categories.
  4. Post-Enumeration Surveys (PES): Conducting a smaller, high-quality survey to estimate coverage error in the main census and adjust the total count.
  5. Demographic Analysis: Comparing census results with expected values derived from vital statistics and previous censuses.
7.
Explain the importance of standardization (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer: Standardization is important because:

  1. Comparability: It allows for the comparison of vital rates (like mortality) between two populations that have different age structures (e.g., a young population vs. an old population).
  2. Eliminates Confounding: It removes the effect of a confounding variable (usually age) to reveal the true underlying difference in rates.
  3. Trend Analysis: It allows for the comparison of rates within the same population over time as its age structure changes.
  4. Policy Making: Provides more accurate indicators for health and social policy planning.
8.
Discuss any 2 errors that occur in population census (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Coverage Error: Occurs when people are missed (undercount) or counted more than once (overcount).
    • Causes: Hard-to-reach areas, mobile populations, refusal to participate.
  2. Content Error: Occurs when the information collected about a person is incorrect.
    • Causes: Age misreporting (heaping/shifting), wrong sex recorded, misunderstanding the question, enumerator error.

Section 3: Long Answer Question

20 Marks
1.
Explain the demographic transition theory by elaborating clearly the events in each of the stages during this process.
Show Answer

Answer:

The Demographic Transition Theory describes the historical shift of birth and death rates from high to low levels in a population. It typically involves four stages:

Stage 1: High Stationary (Pre-Industrial)

  • Birth Rate: High and fluctuating.
  • Death Rate: High and fluctuating (due to disease, famine, war).
  • Population Growth: Very slow or stationary.
  • Characteristics: Agrarian society, lack of medical care, children seen as economic assets.

Stage 2: Early Expanding (Industrializing)

  • Birth Rate: Remains high.
  • Death Rate: Falls rapidly.
  • Population Growth: Very rapid (Population Explosion).
  • Characteristics: Improvements in healthcare, sanitation, and food supply reduce mortality. Cultural norms still encourage large families.

Stage 3: Late Expanding (Mature Industrial)

  • Birth Rate: Starts to fall.
  • Death Rate: Continues to fall, but more slowly.
  • Population Growth: Slows down.
  • Characteristics: Urbanization, increased cost of raising children, women entering the workforce, access to contraception, and lower infant mortality reduce the need for large families.

Stage 4: Low Stationary (Post-Industrial)

  • Birth Rate: Low.
  • Death Rate: Low.
  • Population Growth: Stable or very slow increase.
  • Characteristics: Highly developed society, strong healthcare, family planning is the norm, small families.

(Note: Some models include a Stage 5 where birth rates fall below death rates, leading to population decline.)
