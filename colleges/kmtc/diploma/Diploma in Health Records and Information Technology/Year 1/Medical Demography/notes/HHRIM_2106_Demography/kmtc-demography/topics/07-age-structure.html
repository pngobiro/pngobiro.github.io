8.0 Introduction

At the end of the module the student should be able to:

  • Define the age structure of the population
  • Describe age misreporting
  • Describe age heaping
  • Describe age shifting
  • Calculate and interpret whipples index

8.1 The determinants of age structure

The age structure and age distribution in any given population is entirely determined by the levels of mortality, fertility and migration occurring in the past. These three (3) factors affect distribution in different ways and to different extent. Changes in the levels of fertility will at first affect just younger age groups only over many years will it work through to affect older ages. The effect of fertility changes can thus be very dramatic, especially if the rise or fall is rapid.

Variations in the level of infant mortality tends to produce an effect very similar to that caused by change in fertility affecting initially the young ages, and only gradually working through to the whole age range.
A change in adult mortality has however, generally, a much less dramatic impact on the shape of an age distribution that either fertility or infant mortality. This is because it is largely not concentrated in one age group but is spread over all ages.

Migration can cause big distortion in age distributions because it is normally concentrated among young adults and is sometimes also sex selective. Areas from which substantial numbers have emigrated will in extreme cases have top heavy pyramids with a high proportion of the proportion in older age groups, few young adults and consequently, few children conversely areas where substantial numbers of immigrants have settled will have a young age structure, with many children and very few elderly.
Overall therefore, an age distribution is best regarded as being determined primarily by fertility and modified, to a greater or lesser extent, by mortality and migration.

8.2 Age Misreporting

Distortion in age distribution may be real and is caused by past changes in the levels or patterns of mortality, fertility or migration or they may be due to errors. The errors may be caused either by some individuals being incorrectly omitted from a survey or by ones individuals ages being wrongly reported.
A great problem for formal demography, frequently prohibiting the use of conventional techniques is that people simply do not tell the truth.
This is not a problem in developed countries but in many parts of the world, age misreporting can take two basic forms:-

  • Age heaping
  • Age shifting

Age Heaping

Age heaping or digit preference as it is often called. Too many people report ages ending with 0 or 5 while relatively few people give ages ending in 9, 1, 4 or 6. For example, if in this group ones age is 22, the individual will report this age as 20 years or 25 years.

Age Shifting

Age shifting is a more serious problem than age heaping largely because it is harder to detect and adjust. Typically older people tend to exaggerate their ages, particularly where age confers status. Young men may underrate their age to avoid state free services. While older unmarried women may underrate their age. Numerous techniques exist both to estimate the extent to which ages are misreported and to correct age distributions. Techniques for adjusting and smoothing ages are often complex and will not be discussed at this level. Whipple's index is a measure of preferences for ages ending in 0 and 5. It takes ages between 23 and 62 because outside this range shifting and other problems always tend to confuse the normal pattern of heaping. The index is calculated simply by expressing the population reporting age ending with 0 and 5 as a percentage of the total population age between 23 & 62 and multiplying by 5. The range of the index is then from 100 indicating no preference for 0 and 5 up to 500 indicating that only 0 and 5 were reported.
