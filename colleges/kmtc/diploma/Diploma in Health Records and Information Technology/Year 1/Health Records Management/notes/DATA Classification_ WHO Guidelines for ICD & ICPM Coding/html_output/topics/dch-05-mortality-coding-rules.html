Causes of Death

In 1967, the Twentieth World Health Assembly defined the causes of death to be entered on the medical certificate of cause of death as "all those diseases, morbid conditions or injuries which either resulted in or contributed to death and the circumstances of the accident or violence which produced any such injuries". The purpose of the definition is to ensure that all the relevant information is recorded. The definition does not include symptoms and modes of dying, such as heart failure or respiratory failure.

Underlying Cause of Death

The underlying cause of death has been defined as "(a) the disease or injury which initiated the train of morbid events leading directly to death, or (b) the circumstances of the accident or violence which produced the fatal injury". From the standpoint of prevention, it is necessary to break the chain of events at some point, and the most effective public health objective is to prevent the precipitating cause from operating.

Selection of the Originating Antecedent Cause

When more than one cause of death is recorded, the first step is to determine the originating antecedent cause from the lowest used line in Part I of the certificate. This is done by applying the General Principle or Selection Rules 1, 2, and 3.

General Principle

When more than one condition is entered on the certificate, the condition entered alone on the lowest used line of Part I should be selected only if it could have given rise to all the conditions entered above it.

Selection Rules

Rule 1. If the General Principle does not apply and there is a reported sequence terminating in the condition first entered on the certificate, select the originating cause of this sequence. If there is more than one sequence terminating in the condition mentioned first, select the originating cause of the first-mentioned sequence.

Rule 2. If there is no reported sequence terminating in the condition first entered on the certificate, select this first-mentioned condition.

Rule 3. If the condition selected by the General Principle or by Rule 1 or Rule 2 is obviously a direct consequence of another reported condition, whether in Part I or Part II, select this primary condition.

General Principle Examples

Example 6: I (a) Abscess of lung (b) Lobar pneumonia. Select lobar pneumonia (J18.1).

Example 7: I (a) Hepatic failure (b) Bile duct obstruction (c) Carcinoma of head of pancreas. Select carcinoma of head of pancreas (C25.0).

Rule 1 Examples

Example 11: I (a) Bronchopneumonia (b) Cerebral infarction and hypertensive heart disease. Select cerebral infarction (I63.9). There are two sequences ending in bronchopneumonia; the originating cause of the first-mentioned sequence is selected.

Example 12: I (a) Oesophageal varices and congestive heart failure (b) Chronic rheumatic heart disease and cirrhosis of liver. Select cirrhosis of liver (K74.6). The sequence is oesophageal varices due to cirrhosis of liver.

Rule 2 Examples

Example 16: I (a) Pernicious anaemia and gangrene of foot (b) Atherosclerosis. Select pernicious anaemia (D51.0). There is no reported sequence terminating in the first entered condition.

Example 17: I (a) Rheumatic and atherosclerotic heart disease. Select rheumatic heart disease (I09.9). There is no reported sequence.

Rule 3 Examples

Example 26: I (a) Bronchopneumonia; II Secondary anaemia and chronic lymphatic leukaemia. Select chronic lymphatic leukaemia (C91.1). Bronchopneumonia (selected by General Principle) and secondary anaemia are direct sequels of leukaemia.

Example 28: I (a) Acute nephritis, scarlet fever. Select scarlet fever (A38). Acute nephritis (selected by Rule 2) is a direct sequel of scarlet fever.

Modification of the Selected Cause

The selected cause of death may not be the most useful for tabulation. It may be necessary to modify the selection to improve the usefulness and precision of mortality data. The following rules should be applied after the initial selection.

Rule A. Senility and other ill-defined conditions: If the selected cause is ill-defined (e.g., senility, cardiac arrest) and a more specific condition is reported, reselect as if the ill-defined condition was not reported.

Rule B. Trivial conditions: If the selected cause is a trivial condition unlikely to cause death and a more serious condition is reported, reselect as if the trivial condition was not reported.

Rule C. Linkage: If the selected cause is linked by a provision in the classification with another condition on the certificate, code the combination.

Rule D. Specificity: If the selected cause is general and a more precise term is reported, prefer the more informative term.

Rule E. Early and late stages of disease: If the selected cause is an early stage of a disease and a more advanced stage is also reported, code to the more advanced stage.

Rule F. Sequelae: If death occurred from residual effects of a condition rather than its active phase, code to the appropriate "Sequelae of ..." category.

Examples of the Modification Rules

Rule A. Senility and other ill-defined conditions

Example 33: I (a) Senility and hypostatic pneumonia (b) Rheumatoid arthritis. Code to rheumatoid arthritis (M06.9). Senility, selected by Rule 2, is ignored and the General Principle applied.

Rule B. Trivial conditions

Example 39: I (a) Ingrowing toenail and acute renal failure. Code to acute renal failure (N17.9). Ingrowing toenail, selected by Rule 2, is ignored.

Rule C. Linkage

Example 43: I (a) Intestinal obstruction (b) Femoral hernia. Code to femoral hernia with obstruction (K41.3).

Rule D. Specificity

Example 55: I (a) Cerebral infarction (b) Cerebrovascular accident. Code to cerebral infarction (I63.9).

Rule E. Early and late stages of disease

Example 61: I (a) Tertiary syphilis (b) Primary syphilis. Code to tertiary syphilis (A52.9).

Rule F. Sequelae

Example 65: I (a) Pulmonary fibrosis (b) Old pulmonary tuberculosis. Code to sequelae of respiratory tuberculosis (B90.9).
