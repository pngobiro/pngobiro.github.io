CHAPTER ONE: INTRODUCTION TO MEDICAL TERMINOLOGY

Medical terminology is a professional language used to communicate facts and ideas in the field of medicine by doctors and other allied health professionals / practitioners.

Importance of learning Medical terminology

  • It helps to develop the ability to read and understand the language of medicine.
  • It helps to communicate to other health departments.
  • It helps to understand the standard abbreviations used in the field of medicine.
  • It promotes the ability to write and spell medical terms.
  • It helps to acquire knowledge of elements of medical terms.
  • It helps to appreciate logical method found in medical terminology.
  • It is applied in coding and indexing according to International Classification of diseases and health related problems.

Origin/ Historical background of medical terminology

The majority of medical terms claim Greek and Latin ancestory. Some have been adapted from modern languages especially German and French. The study of medical terminology can enrich our understanding.

Health information technicians

Every time a patient receives medical treatment, information is added to the patient's medical record which includes data about symptoms, medical history, test results, diagnoses and treatment.

Health information technicians organize and manage these records working closely with physicians, nurses and other health professionals to ensure that they provide a complete and accurate basis for quality patient care.

Accurate medical records are essential for administrative purposes. Health information technicians assign a code to each diagnosis and procedure a patient receives and this information is used for accurate patient billing. In addition, health information technicians analyze medical records to reveal trends in health and diseases. This research can be used to: improve patient care, manage costs, and help establish new medical treatments.

To read and interpret medical records, health information technicians need a thorough background in medical terminology. Most of these technicians work in hospitals and long-term care facilities. Others may work in medical clinics, government agencies, insurance companies and consulting firms.

BASIC ELEMENTS OF MEDICAL WORDS

Medical Word Elements

The language of medicine is a specialized vocabulary used by health care practitioners.

Many current medical word elements originated as early as the 1st century B.C., when Hippocrates practiced medicine.

With advancements in medicine, new terms have evolved to reflect these innovations. For example, radiographic terms, such as magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) and ultrasound (US), are now used to describe current diagnostic procedures.

A medical word consists of some or all of the following elements: word root, combining form, suffix, and prefix. How you combine these elements, and whether all or some of them are present in a medical term, determines the meaning of a word.

Word Roots

A word root is the foundation of a medical term and contains its primary meaning. All medical terms have at least one word root. Most word roots are derived from Greek or Latin language. Thus, two different roots may have the same meaning. For example, the Greek word dermatos and the Latin word cutane both refer to the skin.

As a general rule, Greek roots are used to build words that describe a disease, condition, treatment, or diagnosis. Latin roots are used to build words that describe anatomical structures. Consequently, the Greek root dermatis used primarily in terms that describe a disease, condition, treatment, or diagnosis of the skin; the Latin root cutaneis used primarily to describe an anatomical structure.

Combining Forms

A combining form is created when a word root is combined with a vowel. The vowel, known as a combining vowel, is usually an o, but sometimes it is an i.

The combining vowel has no meaning of its own, but enables two word elements to be connected. Like the word root, the combining form is the basic foundation to which other word elements are added to build a complete medical word.

Suffixes

A suffix is a word element placed at the end of a word that changes the meaning of the word. In the terms tonsil /itis, and tonsil/ ectomy, the suffixes are -itis (inflammation) and -ectomy (excision, removal).

Changing the suffix changes the meaning of the word. In medical terminology, a suffix usually describes a pathology (disease or abnormality), symptom, surgical or diagnostic procedure, or part of speech. Many suffixes are derived from Greek or Latin words.

Prefixes

A prefix is a word element attached to the beginning of a word or word root. However, not all medical terms have a prefix. Adding or changing a prefix changes the meaning of the word.

The prefix usually indicates a number, time, position, direction, or negation. Many of the same prefixes used in medical terminology are also used in the English language.

Basic Guidelines

Defining and building medical words are crucial skills in mastering medical terminology. Following the basic guidelines for each will help you develop these skills.

Defining Medical Words

Here are three basic steps for defining medical words using gastroenteritis as an example.

  1. Define the suffix, or last part of the word. In this case, the suffix -itis, this means inflammation.
  2. Define the first part of the word (which may be a word root, combining form, or prefix). In this case, the combining form gastr/o means stomach.
  3. Define the middle parts of the word. In this case, the word root enter means intestine.

When you analyze gastroenteritis following the three previous rules, the meaning is:

  1. Inflammation (of)
  2. Stomach (and)
  3. Intestine. Thus, the meaning of gastroenteritis is inflammation (of) stomach (and) intestine.

There are three basic rules for building medical words.

  • Rule #1 A word root links a suffix that begins with a vowel.

Pronunciation Guidelines

Although pronunciations of medical words usually follows the same rules that govern pronunciations of English words, some medical words may be difficult to pronounce when first encountered.

Therefore, selected terms in this book include phonetic pronunciation. Also, pronunciation guidelines can be found on the inside front cover of this book and at the end of selected tables. Use them whenever you need help with pronunciation of medical words.

Here are guidelines to help you pronounce medical terms as well as understand pronunciation marks used throughout this text and in most dictionaries.

Special Sounds

Here are rules regarding certain letter combinations and special sounds attributed to letters based on their placement in a medical word:

  • For ae and oe, only the second vowel is pronounced. Examples are bursae, pleurae, and roentgen.
  • The soft sound of s and j are given to c and g, respectively, before e, i, and y in words of Greek or Latin origin. Examples are cerebrum, circumcision, cycle, gel, gingivitis, giant, and gyrate.
  • Before other letters, c and g have a hard sound. Examples are cardiac, cast, gastric, and gonad.
  • The letters ch are sometimes pronounced like k. Examples are cholesterol, cholera, and cholemia.
  • When pn appears at the beginning of a word, p is silent and only n is pronounced. Examples are pneumonia and pneumotoxin.
  • When pn appears in the middle of a word, p and n are pronounced. Examples are orthopnea and hyperpnea.
  • When ps appears at the beginning of a word, p is silent and only sis pronounced. Examples are psychology and psychosis.
  • When forming the final letter(s) of a word, e and es are commonly pronounced as separate syllables. Examples are syncope, systole, and nares.
  • When i appears at the end of a word (to form a plural), it is pronounced eye. Examples are bronchi, fungi, and nuclei.
  • All other vowels and consonants have normal English sounds.

MEDICAL SPECIALITIES

A feature of modern medicine is specialization. The suffix "ology" means the science or study of.

  • EMERGENCY MEDICINE: Concerned with the diagnosis and treatment of conditions resulting from trauma and sudden illness.
  • ANAESTHESIOLOGY: An anaesthetist produces loss of sensation to touch or pain under general or local administration of an anaesthetic.
  • ANDROLOGY: Study of diseases of males; especially masculinity, male reproduction and secondary sex characteristics (e.g. undescended testes, impotence and infertility)
  • AVIATION MEDICINE: Diseases and pathologic states arising from air travel. This includes conditions caused by restricted body movement for long periods, jetlags, pressure or barotrauma.
  • BACTERIOLOGY: Study of micro-organisms or bacteria taken by swabs, smears, scrapes and specimens (e.g. urine). Cultures grown and examined by microscope to determine the source of infection, measure sensitivity to antibiotics and to identify drug resistant strains.
  • BIOCHEMISTRY: The chemistry of living organisms and life processes: detects abnormalities of altered function (e.g. electrolytes)
  • CARDIOLOGY: Study of heart diseases and the functioning of the heart, blood vessels and the circulatory system.
  • CYTOGENETICS: Study of the origin and the development of cells, especially chromosomes through karyotyping analysis.
  • CYTOLOGY: Study of the formation, structure and functions of cells carried out by microscopic examination (e.g. Pap smear)
  • DENTISTRY: Diagnosis and treatment of teeth and associated structures in the oral cavity, including gums, bone and nerves.
  • DERMATOLOGY: Diagnosis and treatment of skin diseases.
  • ENDOCRINOLOGY: Study of the function and diseases of gland of internal secretion and the endocrine system. This speciality includes conditions associated with growth, thyroid function and diabetes.
  • EPIDEMIOLOGY: Study of distribution and determinants of diseases in human population, their morbidity and mortality.
  • FORENSIC PATHOLOGY: Forensic pertains to the law. Under the law, post mortems are carried out when death occurs due to: violence; suicide; the time and cause is unknown; the person is in custody; post- operative or under anaesthesia: when not due to natural causes or when a doctor is unable to make a diagnosis.
  • GASTROENTEROLOGY: Study of the upper digestive system- the oesophagus, stomach, intestines, gallbladder and liver.
  • GENETICS: Study of hereditary, whether it is natural or a variation in development. Genetic counseling is provided for people who are known or potential carriers of genetic diseases.
  • GERONTOLOGY/ GERIATRIC MEDICINE: Study of all aspects regarding the aging process and old age (i.e. the physical, mental, social and economic)
  • GYNAECOLOGY: Study of the diseases of female reproductive, genital, urinary and rectal systems; includes some conditions of the breasts.
  • HAEMATOLOGY: Study of the function, nature and diseases of the blood and blood forming tissues, including the lymphatic system.
  • HISTOPATHOLOGY: Study of tissue and the function of its cells. Tissue is obtained by surgery, biopsy and microscopic examination is carried out to identify the disease entity.
  • HYPERBARIC MEDICINE: Uses of pure oxygen in the treatment of decompression sickness (bends), severe infection, tumor and carbon monoxide poisoning.
  • IMMUNOLOGY: Study of the immune system, natural and acquired body defence from infection, allergies and antibody reaction.
  • INTERNAL MEDICINE: Treatment used in non-invasive. Also known as physical medicine or general medicine.
  • MICROBIOLOGY: Study of micro-organisms (e.g. viruses, protozoa, bacteria) and their effects on living cells.
  • MYCOLOGY: Study of fungi and fungal diseases such as ringworms.
  • NEONATOLOGY: Study of the diseases of the newborn.
  • NEPHROLOGY: Study of diseases of the kidney and their associated structures; and treatment given, including dialysis and transplant. Also called Renal Medicine.
  • NEUROLOGY: Study of diseases of nervous system (e.g. brain, spinal cord and nerves)
  • NEUROPATHOLOGY: The investigation of diseases, structural and functional changes in the brain and nervous system. Usually associated with deceased patients.
  • NUCLEAR MEDICINE: The use of nuclear radiation (radio-isotopes) in the investigation, diagnosis, and treatment of diseases.
  • OBSTETRICS: Study of pregnancy, birth and puerperium ( 6 weeks period after birth)
  • OCCUPATIONAL MEDICINE: Study of work related diseases, their treatment and prevention. Includes screening for potential diseases ergonomics, and detection of workplace hazards.
  • ONCOLOGY: Study and treatment of tumours (e.g. neoplasm, cancer), especially malignant cancers.
  • OPHTHALMOLOGY: Study of diseases and treatment of the eye(s)
  • ORTHODONTICS: A division of dentistry dealing with correction of abnormally positioned teeth. Includes the use of bands or braces and the removal of wisdom teeth.
  • ORTHOPAEDICS: Study of diseases and treatment of the skeletal system and its associated structures (e.g. joints, muscles, tendons and cartilage).
  • OTOLOGY: Study of internal ear and its structures.
  • OTORHINOLARYNGOLOGY: Study of ear, nose and throat conditions (ENT)
  • PAEDIATRICS: Study of infants and children (usually to 15 years of age)
  • PALLIATIVE CARE: Concentrates on relieving pain and provision of medical and emotional support for terminally ill patients and their families.
  • PAIN MANAGEMENT: Usually requires an anaesthetist and uses a variety of techniques such as epidural and nerve block injections.
  • PATHOLOGY: Study of nature, effects and cause of disease which produces changes in structure and body function. This area has many sub-specialities.
  • PROCTOLOGY: The study of lower digestive system involving the colon, rectum and anus.
  • PSYCHIATRY: Study of cause, treatment and prevention of mental, emotional and behavioral disorders.
  • RADIOLOGY/MEDICAL IMAGING: Involves the use of radiation in a variety of forms (e.g. Plain or contrast, x-rays, ultra-sound, computed tomography, mammography, magnetic resonance imaging and fluoroscopy.
  • RADIOTHERAPY: Uses radioactive substances in the treatment of disease, especially cancer.
  • REHABILITATION MEDICINE: Treatment and care provided to reskill and educate physically and mentally disabled patients gain maximum function and independence.
  • RESPIRATORY or PULMONARY MEDICINE: Study of diseases of the chest (the lung and associated structures) and assessment of breathing function.
  • RHEUMATOLOGY: Study of inflammation degeneration or metabolic derangement of connective tissue structures and joints.
  • SEROLOGY: Study of serum or serous fluid in disease and immunity especially regarding infectious diseases.
  • SPORTS MEDICINE: Prevention and treatment of sports related trauma or injury, pathological or psychological.
  • SURGERY: Treatment of disease, injury or deformity with invasive methods using either manipulative or operative procedures. Surgery is classified as major, minor or day surgery.
  • THORACIC MEDICINE: Involves the diagnosis and non-invasive treatment of disorders within the chest wall.
  • TRAVEL MEDICINE: Study of health problems among travellers.
  • UROLOGY: Study of urinary system and male reproductive system.
  • VENEREOLOGY: Study of sexually transmitted diseases.
  • VIROLOGY: Study of viruses and viral diseases.

GENERAL TERMS

  • Acquired: Developed after birth; not inherited.
  • Acute: Of rapid onset and short duration; usually a sharp, severe episode.
  • Aetiology: The study of the cause of a disease.
  • Anatomy: The study of normal body structures or structure of an organism. Branch of science dealing with dissection or cutting apart.
  • Chronic: Of slow onset and long duration; shows little change over time.
  • Congenital: Present at birth; born with.
  • Diagnosis (Dx): Identifying a disease by means of signs, symptoms and/or investigation. Stating the nature of the disease and the cause of the illness.
  • Disease: A condition in which some abnormality of structure and/or function is present in some parts of the body.
  • Health: A state of physical, social, mental wellbeing.
  • Iatrogenic: A condition arising from medical or surgical procedure, including diagnosis and treatment (e.g. side effects of medication )
  • Idiopathic: A condition of unknown cause.
  • Local: Limited to one place or part of the body.
  • Medicine: A drug; Science of prevention of a disease; Investigation of a disease; Clinical speciality practiced by physicians using drugs and non-surgical procedures.
  • Physiology: Study of normal body functions; Science of living organism function and the chemical and the physical processes involved.
  • Placebo: No medical content contained in the treatment, but procedures a powerful effect on patient/ patients symptoms (May be positive or negative)
  • Prognosis: Prediction as to the nature, course and outcome of the disease.
  • MRSA: Methicillin- Resistant Staphylococcus Aureus; It is used to describe a number of strains of the bacteria staphylococcus Aureus that are resistant to a number of antibiotics.
  • Eponyms: These are names used in medical terminology that honors the discoverer of a condition, procedure, test, instrument or anatomical feature e.g. Parkinson's disease.
  • Acronyms: These are words formed from the initial letters of other words also known as abbreviations e.g. LASER- Light Amplification by Stimulated Emission of Radiation.
  • Syndrome: A combination of a number of symptoms (anatomical, physiological or biochemical) and signs which together characterize a condition.
  • Homonyms: These are words that sound alike but have a different spelling and meaning e.g. die and dye.
  • Antonyms: Are words having an opposite meaning e.g. good-bad, right-wrong
  • Synonyms: Are words or prefixes which have the same meaning as another e.g. half(English) -hemi(Greek)-semi(Latin)
