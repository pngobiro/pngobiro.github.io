Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions

40 Marks
1.
Which of the following is not a type of analytic studies?
a) Cross sectional surveys
b) Observational studies
c) Case control studies
d) Cohort studies
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Cross sectional surveys (or b)

Explanation: Cross-sectional surveys are primarily descriptive studies used to generate hypotheses, although they can sometimes be analytical. Case-control and Cohort studies are the classic analytic designs. "Observational studies" is a broad category that includes both descriptive and analytic types, so it's not a specific "type" of analytic study itself, but Cross-sectional is the most distinctively descriptive option listed.

2.
--- studies looks forward from an exposure to outcome
a) Retrospective studies
b) Cohort studies
c) Case control studies
d) Prospective studies
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Prospective studies (and b Cohort)

Explanation: Prospective studies (synonymous with prospective cohort studies) look forward in time from exposure to outcome. While "Cohort" is also correct, "Prospective" describes the directionality explicitly asked for.

3.
---- incidence provides an estimate of the probability that an individual will develop a disease during a specific period of time
a) Gross
b) Net
c) Density
d) Cumulative
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Cumulative

Explanation: Cumulative Incidence (Risk) estimates the probability/risk of an individual developing the disease over a specified time period. Incidence Density (c) measures the rate (speed) of new cases.

4.
Unusual medical occurrences that can represent the first clues in the identification of new diseases or adverse effects of exposure are documented inform of
a) Case manuals
b) Case report
c) Case series
d) Case data
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Case report

Explanation: A Case Report documents a single unusual medical occurrence. This is often the first step in identifying new diseases (e.g., the first reports of Kaposi's sarcoma in young men signaling HIV).

5.
---- refers to the application of a test to people who are yet asymptomatic for the purpose of classifying them with respect to their likelihood of getting a certain disease
a) Testing
b) Diagnosis
c) Screening
d) Validation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Screening

Explanation: Screening is the presumptive identification of unrecognized disease (asymptomatic phase) by applying tests to sort people into likely ill and likely healthy groups.

6.
Which type of trials involves evaluation of whether an agent or procedure reduces the risk of developing a disease
a) Therapeutic
b) Preventive
c) Control
d) Secondary
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Preventive

Explanation: Preventive (Prophylactic) trials evaluate agents (like vaccines) given to healthy individuals to prevent disease development. Therapeutic trials treat those who are already sick.

7.
What is the type of prevention that aims at reducing the impact of a disease?
a) Primary
b) Secondary
c) Tertiary
d) Middle
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Tertiary

Explanation: Tertiary prevention aims to reduce the impact, complications, and disability of an established disease (rehabilitation). Secondary prevention aims at early detection/cure. Primary aims at preventing onset.

8.
An individual who harbors the disease organism but is not infected is referred to as
a) Asymptomatic
b) Symptomatic
c) Latent
d) Carrier
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Carrier (Note: Phrasing is tricky)

Explanation: Standard definition: A carrier harbors the organism and can transmit it but shows no symptoms. Technically they are infected (colonized), but not diseased. The question phrasing "not infected" is epidemiologically imprecise but "Carrier" is the intended answer for "harbors organism but no disease".

9.
--- is the ability of the test to identify correctly those who have a disease
a) Validity
b) Reliability
c) Sensitivity
d) Specificity
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Sensitivity

Explanation: Sensitivity = True Positives / (True Positives + False Negatives). It measures the ability to correctly identify those with the disease.

10.
Another name for reliability of a test is
a) Sensitivity
b) Variation
c) Correctness
d) Repeatability
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Repeatability

Explanation: Reliability refers to the consistency or reproducibility of a test. If you repeat the test, do you get the same result? Hence, Repeatability/Reproducibility.

11.
Which one of the following is a good index of the severity of a short term acute disease?
a) Cause of specific disease
b) 5-year survival
c) Case fatality
d) Standardized mortality ratio
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Case fatality

Explanation: Case Fatality Rate (CFR) measures the proportion of diagnosed cases that die from the disease. It is the standard measure of disease severity/virulence for acute conditions (e.g., Ebola, Cholera).

12.
Randomization can be achieved by use of one of the following except
a) Epidemiology tables
b) Computers
c) Random number tables
d) Random numbers
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Epidemiology tables

Explanation: Computers (algorithms) and Random number tables are standard tools for randomization. "Epidemiology tables" is a vague term, likely referring to data presentation tables (2x2 tables), which are not used to generate random sequences.

13.
The major purpose of random assignment in clinical trials is to
a) Facilitate double blinding
b) Reduce selection bias in the allocation of treatment
c) Facilitate the measurement of outcome variables
d) Help ensure that the study subjects are representative of the general population
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Reduce selection bias in the allocation of treatment

Explanation: Randomization ensures that known and unknown confounders are equally distributed between groups, eliminating selection bias by the investigator in assigning treatment.

14.
Another name for odd ratio is
a) Against Odds
b) Odds Risk
c) Relative Odds
d) Risk Odds
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Relative Odds (or Cross-Product Ratio)

Explanation: The Odds Ratio is sometimes referred to as the Relative Odds or the Cross-Product Ratio (ad/bc).

15.
------- is the amount or proportion of disease incidence that can be attributed to a specific exposure
a) Attributable odds
b) Attributable risk
c) Attributable Risk Percent
d) Attributable Risk
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Attributable risk

Explanation: Attributable Risk (Risk Difference) quantifies the amount (absolute excess) of disease risk that is due to the exposure.

16.
All the following are important criteria when making causal inferences except
a) Constancy with existing knowledge
b) Prediction of events
c) Strength of association
d) Dose response relationship
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Prediction of events

Explanation: Based on Hill's Criteria of Causality: Consistency (a), Strength (c), and Biological Gradient/Dose-response (d) are core criteria. "Prediction of events" is a goal of science but not one of Hill's specific criteria for inferring causality from data.

17.
---- is a systematic relationship error in design conduct or analysis of a study that results in a mistaken estimate of an exposure effect
a) Interaction
b) Inference
c) Bias
d) Confounder
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Bias

Explanation: Bias is defined as systematic error in the design, conduct, or analysis of a study. Confounding (d) is a mixing of effects, not necessarily a design error.

18.
The extent to which a specific health care treatment, service or procedure, program or other intervention produces a beneficial result under ideal control is called
a) Efficacy
b) Effectiveness
c) Effect modification
d) Efficiency
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Efficacy

Explanation: - Efficacy: Works under ideal/controlled conditions. - Effectiveness: Works under real-world conditions. - Efficiency: Works with optimal resource use (cost-benefit).

19.
Which one of the following is termed as a fictitious rate?
a) A prevalence rate
b) Incidence rate
c) Standardize rate
d) Infant mortality rate
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Standardize rate

Explanation: A Standardized Rate (adjusted rate) is a hypothetical or "fictitious" number. It is calculated to allow comparison between populations with different age structures, but it does not represent the actual rate observed in the population (unlike crude or specific rates).

20.
------ is a means by which a causal factor leads to an outcome
a) Intervening variable
b) Validity
c) Modifier
d) Confounder
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Intervening variable (Mediator)

Explanation: An intervening variable (or mediator) is part of the causal pathway: Exposure -> Intervening Variable -> Outcome.

21.
Which of the following is a measure of risk of having a risk factor?
a) Kappa
b) Odds ratio
c) Relative risk
d) Risk ratio
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Odds ratio

Explanation: In Case-Control studies (where we start with the disease), we look back to see who had the risk factor. The Odds Ratio compares the odds of having the exposure (risk factor) in cases vs controls.

22.
The administration of human immune globulin after exposure of Hepatitis B is an example of
a) Herd immunity
b) Acute immunity
c) Passive immunity
d) Acquired immunity
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Passive immunity

Explanation: Administering pre-formed antibodies (immune globulin) provides immediate, temporary protection. This is Artificially Acquired Passive Immunity.

23.
Which one of the following is an example of tertiary prevention?

b) Occupational therapy following a stroke
c) Post exposure prophylaxis for rabies

27.

d) Infect a new host

Explanation: Essential for survival: Multiply (c), Exit host (a), Infect new host (d). Changing mode of transmission is an evolutionary trait but not an immediate requirement for survival.

36.

40.

Section 2: Short Answer Questions

40 Marks
41.
List and explain five advantages of passive surveillance. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Cost-Effective: It is relatively inexpensive as it uses existing health systems and personnel.
  2. Easy to Organize: Requires less training and infrastructure compared to active surveillance.
  3. Wide Coverage: Can cover large geographical areas and populations since it relies on routine reporting.
  4. Baseline Data: Provides good baseline data for monitoring long-term trends of disease.
  5. Sustainability: Easier to maintain over long periods because it is integrated into routine health services.
42.
State and explain three types of cases in the case definition of COVID-19. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Suspected Case: A person with acute respiratory illness (fever, cough) AND history of travel to an area with community transmission OR contact with a confirmed case.
  2. Probable Case: A suspected case for whom testing for the COVID-19 virus is inconclusive OR a suspected case for whom testing could not be performed for any reason.
  3. Confirmed Case: A person with laboratory confirmation of COVID-19 infection (e.g., Positive PCR test), irrespective of clinical signs and symptoms.
43.
Briefly explain the concept of confounding. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Confounding is a distortion of the association between an exposure and an outcome that occurs when a third variable (the confounder) is associated with both.

Criteria for a Confounder:

  • It must be a risk factor for the disease (outcome).
  • It must be associated with the exposure.
  • It must not be an intermediate step in the causal pathway between exposure and disease.

Example: Studying alcohol and lung cancer. Smoking is a confounder because smokers tend to drink alcohol (associated with exposure) and smoking causes lung cancer (risk factor).

44.
Explain the concept of herd immunity. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Herd Immunity (Community Immunity) is the resistance of a group or community to the invasion and spread of an infectious agent based on the resistance of a high proportion of individual members of the group.

  • It occurs when a sufficient percentage of the population is immune (via vaccination or prior infection), reducing the likelihood that an infected person will come into contact with a susceptible person.
  • This halts the chain of transmission, protecting even those who are not immune (e.g., newborns, immunocompromised).
45.
An area Y has a population of 100,000. 20 persons become sick of malaria in a year. 18 of the 20 persons die from malaria (5 marks)
i. Calculate the mortality rate from malaria
ii. Calculate the case fatality rate from malaria
Show Answer

i. Mortality Rate:

$$ \text{Mortality Rate} = \frac{\text{Deaths from Malaria}}{\text{Total Population}} \times 100,000 $$

$$ = \frac{18}{100,000} \times 100,000 = 18 \text{ per 100,000 population} $$

ii. Case Fatality Rate (CFR):

$$ \text{CFR} = \frac{\text{Deaths from Malaria}}{\text{Total Cases of Malaria}} \times 100 $$

$$ = \frac{18}{20} \times 100 = 90\% $$

46.
List and explain five guidelines for judging whether an observed association is causal. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Based on Bradford Hill's Criteria:

  1. Strength of Association: A large Relative Risk implies a stronger likelihood of causality.
  2. Consistency: The association is observed repeatedly by different persons, in different places, circumstances, and times.
  3. Temporality: The exposure must precede the disease in time (Essential criteria).
  4. Biological Gradient (Dose-Response): Greater exposure leads to greater incidence of the effect.
  5. Plausibility: The association fits with existing biological knowledge.
47.
List and explain five different types of epidemiology. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Descriptive Epidemiology: Describes disease distribution by time, place, and person.
  2. Analytical Epidemiology: Tests hypotheses to determine causes (determinants) of disease (e.g., Cohort, Case-Control).
  3. Experimental (Interventional) Epidemiology: Involves active manipulation of exposure (Clinical trials) to test efficacy.
  4. Clinical Epidemiology: Application of epidemiological principles to patient care (diagnosis, prognosis).
  5. Social Epidemiology: Studies how social structural factors affect health.
48.
Write short notes on incidence density. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  • Definition: Incidence Density (or Incidence Rate) is the number of new cases of a disease that occur during a specified period of time in a population at risk.
  • Denominator: Unlike cumulative incidence (which uses people), incidence density uses Person-Time (e.g., person-years) as the denominator.
  • Usefulness: It is particularly useful when study participants are followed for varying periods of time (e.g., due to attrition or late entry).
  • Formula: $\text{Incidence Density} = \frac{\text{Number of new cases}}{\text{Total Person-Time at risk}}$

49.
Using a well labeled diagram, explain cohort studies with use of coronary heart disease and smoking as an example. (10 marks)
Show Answer

Cohort Study Design (Prospective):

In a cohort study, we start with a population free of disease and classify them based on exposure status.

[Diagram Structure]
Start: Target Population (Free of CHD)
                
Select Sample & Assess Exposure (Smoking)
                                  
Exposed Group                Unexposed Group
(Smokers)                            (Non-Smokers)
                                            
Follow up over Time                Follow up over Time
                                              
Disease  No Disease        Disease  No Disease
(CHD)        (No CHD)           (CHD)        (No CHD)

Explanation:

  1. Identify a defined population (e.g., adults in a town) who do not have Coronary Heart Disease (CHD).
  2. Classify them into Smokers (Exposed) and Non-smokers (Unexposed).
  3. Follow both groups over a period (e.g., 10 years).
  4. Count how many develop CHD in the Smoker group vs. the Non-smoker group.
  5. Calculate the Relative Risk (RR) = Incidence in Smokers / Incidence in Non-smokers.
50.
The following table presents the results of a screening test.

Results of screening Disease (Sick) No disease (Well) Total
Positive 80a (TP) 100b (FP) 180
Negative 20c (FN) 800d (TN) 820
Total 100 900 1000

Calculate:
i. Sensitivity
ii. Specificity
iii. Positive predictive value
iv. Negative predictive value
Show Answer

Calculations:

i. Sensitivity: Ability to detect disease.

$$ \text{Sensitivity} = \frac{\text{True Positives (a)}}{\text{Total Diseased (a+c)}} \times 100 $$

$$ = \frac{80}{100} \times 100 = 80\% $$

ii. Specificity: Ability to detect absence of disease.

$$ \text{Specificity} = \frac{\text{True Negatives (d)}}{\text{Total Well (b+d)}} \times 100 $$

$$ = \frac{800}{900} \times 100 = 88.89\% $$

iii. Positive Predictive Value (PPV): Probability that a positive test means disease.

$$ \text{PPV} = \frac{\text{True Positives (a)}}{\text{Total Positives (a+b)}} \times 100 $$

$$ = \frac{80}{180} \times 100 = 44.44\% $$

iv. Negative Predictive Value (NPV): Probability that a negative test means no disease.

$$ \text{NPV} = \frac{\text{True Negatives (d)}}{\text{Total Negatives (c+d)}} \times 100 $$

$$ = \frac{800}{820} \times 100 = 97.56\% $$
