Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions

40 Marks
1.
In the definition of epidemiology, "distribution" refers to the following except:
a) Who
b) When
c) Where
d) Why
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Why

Explanation: "Distribution" describes the frequency and pattern of health events in terms of Time (When), Place (Where), and Person (Who). "Why" refers to the determinants (causes/risk factors).

2.
In the definition of epidemiology, "determinants" generally includes the following except:
a) Agents
b) Causes
c) Control measures
d) Risk factors
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Control measures

Explanation: Determinants are the factors that influence the risk of disease (Agents, Causes, Risk Factors). Control measures are the actions taken based on the knowledge of determinants, not the determinants themselves.

3.
Why should an investigator who has no clinical background nonetheless talk to a patient or two as an early step in the outbreak investigation?
a) To advise the patient about common risk factors and the usual course of the illness
b) To learn more about the clinical manifestations of the disease
c) To verify the clinical findings as part of verifying the diagnosis
d) To verify the laboratory findings as part of verifying the diagnosis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) To learn more about the clinical manifestations of the disease

Explanation: Even without clinical training, talking to patients helps the epidemiologist understand the symptoms and progression, which is crucial for developing a precise case definition.

4.
A case definition during an outbreak investigation should specify the following except:
a) Clinical features
b) Time
c) Person
d) Hypothesized exposure
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Hypothesized exposure

Explanation: A case definition generally includes Person, Place, Time, and Clinical criteria. Including the hypothesized exposure (e.g., "People who ate potato salad") biases the investigation because it excludes cases that might have been exposed differently, preventing you from testing the hypothesis fairly.

5.
A case report form devised for an outbreak investigation usually includes which of the following types of information except?
a) Unidentified information
b) Demographic information
c) Clinical information
d) Reporter, interviewer, or data abstractor information
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Unidentified information

Explanation: Forms require Identifying information (Name, ID, Contact) to track patients and prevent duplicates. "Unidentified information" is not a category of data collected; anonymity is maintained in analysis, but collection requires identifiers.

6.
Public health surveillance includes which of the following activities except:
a) Soliciting case reports of persons with symptoms compatible with SARs from local hospitals
b) Creating graphs of the number of dog bites by week and neighborhood
c) Writing a report on trends in seat belt use to share with the state legislature
d) Disseminating educational materials about ways people can reduce their risk of Lyme disease
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Disseminating educational materials...

Explanation: This is a Public Health Intervention/Control Measure. Surveillance involves Collection (A), Analysis (B), and Dissemination of Data/Information (C), but not the direct implementation of control programs like health education.

7.
A specific case definition is one that:
a) Is likely not to include only (or mostly) true cases
b) Is considered "loose" or "broad"
c) Will include more cases than a sensitive case definition
d) May exclude mild cases
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) May exclude mild cases

Explanation: A Specific definition is strict to ensure those counted are true cases (high specificity). This trade-off often means excluding mild, atypical, or early-stage cases (lower sensitivity). A "loose" definition (B) is sensitive, not specific.

8.
Comparing numbers and rates of illness in a community, rates are most preferred for:
a) Conducting surveillance for communicable diseases
b) Deciding how many doses of immune globulin are needed
c) Estimating subgroups at highest risk
d) Telling physicians which strain of influenza is most prevalent
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Estimating subgroups at highest risk

Explanation: Rates account for the population size, allowing for fair comparison of risk between different groups (e.g., age groups, sexes) to identify who is most at risk. Numbers alone (counts) can be misleading if population sizes differ.

9.
Possible explanations for a case that occurs substantially later than the other cases in an outbreak include the following except
a) Similar primary related disease
b) Secondary case
c) Case with unusually long incubation period
d) Time of exposure later than others
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Similar primary related disease

Explanation: A late case could be a Secondary case (B), a long incubator (C), or a late exposure (D). "Similar primary related disease" is vague but likely implies a different disease or unrelated event, which would be an "outlier" or misclassification, but B, C, D are the standard epidemiological explanations for "trailing" cases.

10.
A spot map is useful for
a) Pinpointing the geographic location of exposures
b) Pinpointing the geographic location of residences
c) Pinpointing the geographic location of employment sites
d) All of the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) All of the above

Explanation: Spot maps plot cases to identify clustering related to where people live, work, or were exposed.

11.
The following are used to display rates except
a) Spot maps
b) Area maps
c) Chloropleth maps
d) Shaded maps
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Spot maps

Explanation: Spot maps display the number/location of individual cases (Counts). Area/Chloropleth maps display rates (density) using shading/colors for geographic areas.

12.
Which of the following may be useful in generating hypotheses in an outbreak setting?
a) Review the literature
b) Look at the descriptive epidemiology
c) Talk with the local health authorities
d) All of the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) All of the above

Explanation: Hypothesis generation involves gathering all available clues: descriptive data (Person/Place/Time), local knowledge (authorities), and what is known about the pathogen (literature).

13.
The key feature of an analytic (epidemiologic) study is: (Select only one answer)
a) Analysis by time, place, and person
b) Calculation of a risk ratio or odds ratio
c) Use of Epidemiological Information to analyze the data
d) Presence of a comparison group
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Presence of a comparison group

Explanation: The defining characteristic that distinguishes analytic studies from descriptive studies is the inclusion of a comparison group (controls or unexposed) to test hypotheses.

14.
Disease control measures can be directed at the:
a) Agent
b) Source
c) Mode of transmission
d) All of the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) All of the above

Explanation: The Chain of Infection can be broken at any link: Agent (antibiotics), Source (isolation), or Mode of Transmission (handwashing/masks).

16.
What is the fraction of cases with the disease among the exposed that is attributable to the exposure?
UnexposedExposed
Disease917
No disease75
a) 0.27
b) 0.60
c) 0.30
d) 0.77
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) 0.27

Calculation (Attributable Fraction in Exposed): 1. Incidence in Exposed ($I_e$) = $17 / (17+5) = 17/22 = 0.7727$ 2. Incidence in Unexposed ($I_u$) = $9 / (9+7) = 9/16 = 0.5625$ 3. Attributable Fraction = $(I_e - I_u) / I_e$ 4. $(0.7727 - 0.5625) / 0.7727 = 0.2102 / 0.7727 = 0.272$ 5. Result = 0.27 (or 27%)

17.
The incidence rate (IR) differs from the incidence proportion (IP) because...
a) IP can be assessed in a closed population, IR not.
b) IR can be assessed in an open population, IP not.
c) IP takes competing mortality into account; IR does not.
d) IR can be assessed more precisely than IP.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) IR can be assessed more precisely than IP.

Explanation: Incidence Rate (Incidence Density) uses person-time in the denominator, allowing it to handle dynamic (open) populations, loss to follow-up, and varying observation times precisely. IP (Cumulative Incidence) assumes a fixed cohort followed for a full period.

18.
If you want to know the proportion of the disease that could be prevented by eliminating the exposure in the entire study population, you should calculate the
a) Attributable fraction
b) Attributable risk
c) Population attributable risk
d) Negative predictive value
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Population attributable risk

Explanation: Key phrase: "in the entire study population". Attributable risk refers to the exposed group only. Population Attributable Risk (PAR) refers to the benefit to the whole community.

19.
The precision of an estimate of a relative risk depends on the...
a) External validity of the study
b) Size of the study
c) Internal validity of the study
d) Study design
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Size of the study

Explanation: Precision (lack of random error) is primarily determined by sample size. Larger sample sizes produce narrower confidence intervals (higher precision). Validity relates to bias (systematic error).

20.
The problem of confounding can be solved by...
a) Choosing a prospective design
b) Increasing the precision of the measurements
c) Stratification during data analysis
d) This cannot be solved
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Stratification during data analysis

Explanation: Confounding can be controlled in the design phase (Randomization, Restriction, Matching) or in the analysis phase (Stratification, Multivariate adjustment).

21.
Which one of the following is not a determinant of a disease?
a) Political determinants
b) Social determinants
c) Non behavioral determinants
d) Clinical determinants
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Clinical determinants

Explanation: In public health models (like Social Determinants of Health), Political, Social, Behavioral, and Biological factors are standard categories. "Clinical determinants" is not a standard category for the causes of disease incidence in a population; clinical factors usually describe the course/outcome.

Explanation: A functional surveillance system requires Data Collection (core), Case Identification (definitions/diagnosis), and Logistic Management (resources to run the system).

b) Analytic epidemiology

25.
Which one of the following is not a mortality rate
a) Child mortality rate
b) Crude death rate
c) Infant mortality rate
d) Prevalence mortality rate
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Prevalence mortality rate

Explanation: "Prevalence mortality rate" is a nonsensical term. Prevalence refers to living cases. Mortality refers to deaths. You can have Mortality Rates or Prevalence Rates, but not a combination.

28.
Epidemiologically, which one of the following is not a component of chain of disease of transmission?
a) Agent and reservoir
b) Portal of exit and entry
c) Host and mode of transmission
d) Signs, symptoms and investigation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Signs, symptoms and investigation

Explanation: The Chain of Infection includes: Agent -> Reservoir -> Portal of Exit -> Mode of Transmission -> Portal of Entry -> Susceptible Host. Signs/Symptoms are clinical features, not part of the transmission chain model.

31.
An epidemiologist wants to assess the effect of tea drinking on blood pressure. He decides to do an intervention study. Which of the following measures does not increase the internal validity of the study?
a) Careful monitoring of blood pressure during the study
b) Inclusion of a control group
c) Randomization
d) Random selection of participants from the general population
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Random selection of participants from the general population

Explanation: - Random selection from the general population increases External Validity (Generalizability). - Randomization (C), Controls (B), and Accurate measurement (A) increase Internal Validity (Accuracy within the study).

32.
Which one of the following are not characteristics of screening tests
a) Repeatability and precision
b) Reliability and accuracy
c) Estimates of yields
d) Insensitivity and specificity
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Insensitivity and specificity

Explanation: A good screening test must be Sensitive and Specific. "Insensitivity" (Low Sensitivity) is a failure of a test, not a desired characteristic. A, B, and C are valid characteristics/metrics.

33.

b) Sickness
c) Disease
d) Health

Section 2: Short Answer Questions

40 Marks
1.
Define epidemiology and state the uses of epidemiological data (10 marks)
Show Answer

Definition: Epidemiology is the study of the distribution and determinants of health-related states or events in specified populations, and the application of this study to the control of health problems.

Uses of Epidemiological Data:

  1. Community Diagnosis: To assess the health status of a community and identify priority health problems.
  2. Completing the Clinical Picture: To identify the full spectrum of disease (including asymptomatic cases).
  3. Identifying Syndromes: To describe new diseases.
  4. Determining Causality: To identify causes and risk factors of disease.
  5. Evaluation: To evaluate the effectiveness, efficiency, and quality of health services.
  6. Planning: To plan for health resource allocation.
2.
List and explain types of association in epidemiology (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Spurious Association: Not real; caused by bias or chance.
  2. Indirect (Non-Causal) Association: The exposure and outcome are linked via a third factor (Confounder).
  3. Direct (Causal) Association: The exposure directly causes the outcome.
    • One-to-One: One cause -> One disease (Rare, e.g., Measles virus -> Measles).
    • Multi-factorial: Multiple causes -> One disease (Common, e.g., Smoking + Genetics -> Lung Cancer).
3.
State five sources of epidemiological data (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Census data.
  2. Vital Statistics (Birth/Death registers).
  3. Disease Surveillance Reports (Notifiable diseases).
  4. Hospital/Clinic Records.
  5. Health Surveys (e.g., KDHS).
  6. School Health Records.
4.
List and explain five levels of disease prevention (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Primordial Prevention: Preventing the emergence of risk factors in a population (e.g., policy to discourage smoking habits).
  2. Primary Prevention: Preventing the disease process from starting in a susceptible host (e.g., Immunization).
  3. Secondary Prevention: Early diagnosis and prompt treatment to halt progression (e.g., Screening).
  4. Tertiary Prevention: Reducing impairments/disabilities and rehabilitation (e.g., Physiotherapy after stroke).
  5. Quaternary Prevention: Preventing over-medicalization and harm from unnecessary interventions.
5.
Define reservoir and explain types of reservoirs (10 marks)
Show Answer

Definition: A reservoir is any person, animal, plant, soil, or substance in which an infectious agent normally lives and multiplies, on which it depends primarily for survival, and where it reproduces itself in such manner that it can be transmitted to a susceptible host.

Types:

  1. Human Reservoirs:
    • Cases: People with clinical disease.
    • Carriers: People without apparent disease but shedding the agent (Asymptomatic, Incubatory, Convalescent, Chronic).
  2. Animal Reservoirs (Zoonoses): Animals that harbor agents transmissible to humans (e.g., Dogs for Rabies, Rodents for Plague).
  3. Environmental (Non-living) Reservoirs: Soil (Tetanus), Water (Legionella), Plants.
6.
State five importance of surveillance (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. To detect outbreaks/epidemics early.
  2. To estimate the magnitude of a health problem.
  3. To monitor changes in infectious agents.
  4. To evaluate control measures/interventions.
  5. To facilitate planning and resource allocation.

Section 3: Long Answer Question

20 Marks
1.
As the integrated disease surveillance and response coordinator in your county, visitors from MOH have visited the county and are interested to know how well the county is prepared in terms of outbreaks. Briefly explain steps/components of disease surveillance and response (12 marks)
Show Answer

Core Functions/Steps of IDSR:

  1. Case Detection and Registration: Identifying cases using standard case definitions at the facility/community level.
  2. Case Confirmation: Collecting specimens for laboratory confirmation.
  3. Reporting (Data Collection): Sending data from lower levels to higher levels (Immediate/Weekly/Monthly reporting).
  4. Data Analysis: Analyzing data by time, place, and person to detect thresholds/trends.
  5. Preparedness and Response: Having RRTs (Rapid Response Teams) and buffer stocks ready to investigate and control outbreaks.
  6. Feedback: Providing information back to lower levels and stakeholders.
  7. Evaluation: Assessing the system's performance.
ii)
The following table shows serum level of calcium and true disease status of 80 participants tested. Complete the table below and answer the following questions. (8 marks)

Serum level of calcium True disease status Total
Diseased Non diseased
Positive 12 [3] [15]
Negative [8] 57 65
Total [20] 60 80

a) Absolute risk (2 marks)
b) Relative risk (2 marks)
c) Attributable risk (2 marks)
d) Odds ratio (2 marks)
Show Answer

Table Completion:

  • Total Negative = 65. Non-diseased Negative = 57. -> Diseased Negative = 65 - 57 = 8.
  • Total Non-diseased = 60. -> Diseased Positive = 60 (Wait, Non-diseased total is 60).
  • Total participants = 80. Total Diseased = 80 - 60 = 20.
  • Diseased Positive = 12. Diseased Negative = 20 - 12 = 8 (Matches above).
  • Non-diseased Positive = Total Non-diseased (60) - 57 = 3.
  • Total Positive = 12 + 3 = 15.

Calculations:

a) Absolute Risk (Incidence in Exposed/Positive):

$$ I_e = \frac{12}{15} = 0.8 $$

b) Relative Risk (Risk Ratio):

$$ I_u (\text{Incidence in Negative}) = \frac{8}{65} = 0.123 $$

$$ RR = \frac{0.8}{0.123} = 6.5 $$

c) Attributable Risk (Risk Difference):

$$ AR = I_e - I_u = 0.8 - 0.123 = 0.677 $$

d) Odds Ratio:

$$ OR = \frac{ad}{bc} = \frac{12 \times 57}{3 \times 8} = \frac{684}{24} = 28.5 $$
