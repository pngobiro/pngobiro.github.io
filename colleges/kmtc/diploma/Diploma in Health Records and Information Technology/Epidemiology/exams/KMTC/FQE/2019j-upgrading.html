Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions

20 Marks
1.
Microbes were discovered by?
a) John Snow
b) Pasteur and Koch
c) Hippocrates
d) Edward Jenner
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Pasteur and Koch

Explanation: Louis Pasteur and Robert Koch are the fathers of the Germ Theory of Disease. They identified specific microbes as causes of specific diseases (Koch's postulates).

2.
In the definition of epidemiology, determinants generally include
a) Agents
b) Control measures
c) Risk factors
d) Source
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Risk factors

Explanation: "Determinants" in the definition of epidemiology refers to the causes and risk factors that influence the occurrence of disease and health-related events.

3.
Which of the following is not part of the word epidemiology?
a) Demos
b) Logos
c) Epi
d) Demio
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Demio

Explanation: Epidemiology is derived from three Greek words: Epi (upon), Demos (people), and Logos (study). "Demio" is not one of the root words.

4.
The search for causes and other factors that influence the occurrence of health-related events is?
a) Pattern
b) Distribution
c) Determinants
d) Epidemiology
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Determinants

Explanation: Determinants are the "Why" and "How" of disease. The search for these causes is Analytical Epidemiology, focusing on determinants.

5.
When the occurrence of a disease is persistently higher than normal it's called
a) Holo-endemic
b) Hyperendemic
c) Pandemic
d) Endemic
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Hyperendemic

Explanation: Endemic refers to the constant presence of a disease. Hyperendemic refers to persistent, high levels of disease occurrence. Holo-endemic usually implies a disease affects essentially every individual in a population (often used for malaria).

6.
The measure of the magnitude of disease or other health related events is called
a) Counts
b) Percentage
c) Frequency
d) Proportion
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Frequency

Explanation: In the definition of epidemiology ("Study of distribution and determinants..."), "Distribution" involves the analysis of disease frequency (counts/rates) and pattern.

7.
Which of the following is not true about proportion?
a) Measures part of the population affected by disease compared to the total population
b) Can be expressed per 100
c) The time period may not be specified
d) The denominator is part of the numerator
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) The denominator is part of the numerator

Explanation: This is mathematically impossible for a standard proportion. In a proportion ($a / (a+b)$), the numerator is part of the denominator. Option D states the reverse.

8.
The natural history of the disease begins with the following except
a) Exposure to risk factors of the disease
b) Accumulation of factors capable of causing a disease
c) Susceptibility of the persons
d) Presence of signs and symptoms
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Presence of signs and symptoms

Explanation: The natural history of disease includes the "Pre-pathogenesis" phase (before disease starts). Exposure, susceptibility, and risk factor accumulation happen before clinical disease. "Presence of signs and symptoms" marks the Clinical stage, which is later in the timeline, not the beginning.

9.
The proportion of exposed persons who become infected is referred to as
a) Virulence
b) Infectivity
c) Pathogenicity
d) Holo endemic
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Infectivity

Explanation: - Infectivity: Ability to enter, survive, and multiply (Exposed -> Infected). - Pathogenicity: Ability to produce disease (Infected -> Sick). - Virulence: Ability to produce severe disease/death (Sick -> Severe/Dead).

10.
In order for a disease agent to survive, it must be able to do the following except
a) Infect a new host
b) Reach a new host
c) Originate from a reservoir
d) Multiply
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Originate from a reservoir

Explanation: While many agents do originate from reservoirs, the critical steps for survival of the pathogen species are multiplying, exiting, reaching a new host, and infecting them. "Originate from a reservoir" is a description of source, not necessarily an active capability required for ongoing survival in the transmission cycle (e.g., human-to-human transmission doesn't strictly require a separate "reservoir" if humans are the only host).

11.
Which of the following is not a type of carrier?
a) Asymptomatic carriers
b) Symptomatic carriers
c) Chronic carriers
d) Convalescent carriers
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Symptomatic carriers

Explanation: A "Carrier" is by definition someone who harbors the agent but does not have apparent clinical disease (symptoms). If they are symptomatic, they are a Case, not a carrier.

12.
Which of the following disease does not have animals as the reservoir?
a) Brucellosis
b) Legionnaires bacillus
c) Plague
d) Trichinosis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Legionnaires bacillus

Explanation: Legionella pneumophila resides in environmental reservoirs (water systems, cooling towers), not animals. Brucellosis (cattle/goats), Plague (rodents), and Trichinosis (pigs/bears) are zoonotic.

13.
The type of transmission by direct spray over a few feet before they fall on the ground is called?
a) Direct contact
b) Droplet spread
c) Mechanical
d) Droplet nuclei
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Droplet spread

Explanation: Large particles that travel short distances (usually < 1 meter) are Droplets. Droplet nuclei (airborne) are small residues that remain suspended for long periods.

14.
Which of the following is not a vector?
a) Ants
b) Mosquitoes
c) Fleas
d) Ticks
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Ants

Explanation: Mosquitoes, Fleas, and Ticks are classic biological vectors for diseases like Malaria, Plague, and Lyme disease. Ants are generally not considered primary vectors for human diseases, although they can mechanically carry pathogens on their feet.

15.
The type of immunity whereby antibodies develop in response to infection, vaccine or toxoid is called?
a) Active immunity
b) Passive immunity
c) Natural immunity
d) Herd immunity
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Active immunity

Explanation: Active immunity is when the host's own immune system produces antibodies (after infection or vaccination). Passive immunity involves receiving pre-formed antibodies (e.g., from mother or immunoglobulin shot).

16.
Which of the following is not a reservoir for yellow fever?
a) Aedes aegypti
b) Monkeys
c) Soil
d) Vertebrates
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Soil

Explanation: Yellow fever has a primate (human/monkey) reservoir and mosquito vector. It does not persist in soil.

17.
Which of the following is not true about relative risk?
a) Can also be referred to as risk ratio
b) Can compare the risk of disease in two groups
c) The group of primary interest is the un exposed group
d) The two groups are typically differentiated by demographic factors
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) The group of primary interest is the un exposed group

Explanation: In Relative Risk calculations (Incidence in Exposed / Incidence in Unexposed), the group of primary interest is the Exposed group to see if the exposure increases risk.

18.
Prevalence can be increased by the following except
a) Addition of new cases
b) Improved treatment
c) Recovery
d) Death
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Recovery (and d) Death)

Explanation: Recovery and Death remove people from the "case" pool, decreasing prevalence. "Improved treatment" (B) can increase prevalence if it prolongs life without curing (e.g., HIV treatment). "Addition of new cases" (A) clearly increases it. The question asks for "Except", but both C and D would decrease it. Often in these exams, "Death" is the intended distractor, or "Recovery". Since D is crossed out in the image and "Death" decreases it, C "Recovery" also decreases it. The question might have been "Which decreases prevalence" or implies "Which of these increases... except". Both C and D decrease it. Let's assume the question meant "Which factor *decreases* prevalence" or is asking which of the list does NOT increase it. Both C and D fit. However, usually "Death" is the primary "Exit" mechanism alongside cure.

19.
Which of the following is not true about attributable risk?
a) Is a measure of risk in cohort studies
b) The rate of disease among exposed persons that can be directly attributable to specific exposure
c) Is the risk difference between exposed and non-exposed individuals
d) It a measure of association
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) It a measure of association

Explanation: While Attributable Risk (Risk Difference) is calculated from association data, in epidemiology, "Measures of Association" typically refer to relative measures (Relative Risk, Odds Ratio) that establish the strength of the relationship. Attributable Risk is a "Measure of Impact" or "Absolute Measure".

20.
The ratio of the number of ways an event can occur to the number of events cannot occur is called?
a) Relative risk
b) Odds of an event
c) Odds ratio
d) Relative odds
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Odds of an event

Explanation: Odds = Probability (P) / (1 - P). It compares the probability of the event happening to the probability of it not happening.

Section 2: Short Answer Questions

30 Marks
1.
Name five components in the definition of epidemiology (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Study: Scientific/systematic approach.
  2. Distribution: Frequency and pattern (Time, Place, Person).
  3. Determinants: Causes and risk factors.
  4. Health-related states or events: Diseases, injuries, behaviors.
  5. Specified populations: Communities, not just individuals.
  6. Application: Using the knowledge to control health problems.
2.
Three terms are used to describe infectious diseases according to the various outcomes that may occur after exposure to its causative agent. State them (5 Marks)
Show Answer

The terms describing the biological capability of the agent to produce outcomes in the host:

  1. Infectivity: The ability of the agent to invade and multiply in the host (Outcome: Infection).
  2. Pathogenicity: The ability of the agent to produce clinical disease (Outcome: Illness/Symptoms).
  3. Virulence: The ability of the agent to produce severe disease or death (Outcome: Severe Illness/Death).
3.
Briefly explain how epidemiology is used in the evaluation of efficacy and effectiveness of therapeutic and preventive interventions (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  • Efficacy: Epidemiology uses Randomized Clinical Trials (RCTs) to determine if a treatment/vaccine works under ideal, controlled conditions (Does it work?).
  • Effectiveness: Uses observational studies or field trials to determine if the intervention works in real-world community settings (Does it help people?).
  • Efficiency: Evaluates the cost-benefit ratio (Is it worth it?).
  • By comparing outcomes (incidence, mortality) in treated vs. untreated groups using measures like Relative Risk and Attributable Risk.
4.
Epidemiologists and clinicians approach the same patient differently, explain (5 Marks)
Show Answer
Clinician Epidemiologist
Focuses on the individual patient. Focuses on the population/community.
Goal is treatment/cure of the case. Goal is prevention/control of spread.
Uses history, exam, labs for diagnosis. Uses surveillance, rates, data for community diagnosis.
Prescribes medication. Prescribes public health policy/interventions.
5.
State the two major classifications of causes of disease (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Causes can be broadly classified into:

  1. Necessary Cause: A factor that must be present for the disease to occur (e.g., Mycobacterium tuberculosis for TB).
  2. Sufficient Cause: A set of minimal conditions/factors that inevitably produce the disease (usually a combination of factors).

Alternatively:

  1. Primary (Direct) Causes: The specific agent (bacteria, virus).
  2. Secondary (Indirect/Predisposing) Causes: Risk factors like age, nutritional status, environment.
6.
Differentiate between holo-endemic and hyperendemic (5 marks)
Show Answer
  • Hyperendemic: Refers to a disease that is constantly present at a high incidence and/or prevalence rate in a population and affects all age groups equally.
  • Holo-endemic: Describes a disease that has a high level of infection beginning early in life and affecting essentially all of the child population. Adults often show acquired immunity (lower clinical disease) despite high infection rates (e.g., Malaria in high-transmission zones).

Section 3: Long Answer Questions

20 Marks
1.
a. Explain the design of case control study using smoking and lung cancer as examples (10 Marks)
b. What are interventional studies (5 Marks)
c. Name three advantages of interventional studies (3 Marks)
d. List two disadvantages of interventional studies (2 marks)
Show Answer

(a) Design of Case-Control Study (Smoking & Lung Cancer):

  1. Selection of Cases: Identify a group of individuals who have the disease (Lung Cancer). These are the "Cases".
  2. Selection of Controls: Identify a comparable group of individuals who do not have the disease (No Lung Cancer) but come from the same population. These are the "Controls".
  3. Matching: Match controls to cases based on confounding variables like age and sex to ensure comparability.
  4. Assessment of Exposure: Look back in time (retrospectively) to determine the history of exposure (Smoking) in both groups. Ask: "Did you smoke?"
  5. Analysis (2x2 Table):
    • Compare the frequency of smoking in cases vs. controls.
    • Calculate the Odds Ratio (OR): (Odds of smoking in cases) / (Odds of smoking in controls).
  6. Interpretation: If significantly more lung cancer patients smoked compared to healthy controls (OR > 1), an association is suggested.

(b) Interventional (Experimental) Studies:

These are epidemiological studies where the researcher actively manipulates the exposure (e.g., gives a drug, vaccine, or health education) to one group while withholding it from another (control group) to observe the outcome. The hallmark is Randomization.

(c) Advantages of Interventional Studies:

  1. Gold Standard: Provides the strongest evidence for causality.
  2. Control of Bias: Randomization eliminates selection bias and confounding (both known and unknown).
  3. Control of Exposure: The investigator knows exactly who received the intervention and the dose.

(d) Disadvantages of Interventional Studies:

  1. Ethical Issues: It may be unethical to expose people to harmful agents or withhold beneficial treatment.
  2. Cost and Time: They are typically very expensive and time-consuming to conduct.
  3. Feasibility: Not all exposures can be randomized (e.g., smoking).
