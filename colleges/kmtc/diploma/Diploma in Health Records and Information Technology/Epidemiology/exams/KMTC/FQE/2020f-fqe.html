1.
In the definition of epidemiology, the terms distribution and determinants taken together refer to:
a) Frequency, pattern and causes of health events
b) Dissemination of information to those who need to know
c) Knowledge, attitudes and practices related to health
d) Public health services and resources
e) Prevention and control of diseases
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Frequency, pattern and causes of health events

Explanation:

  • Distribution: Refers to Frequency (how many) and Pattern (Time, Place, Person).
  • Determinants: Refers to Causes and risk factors.
  • Combined, they cover Option A.

2.
Descriptive epidemiology includes all except:
a) What
b) Who
c) When
d) Where
e) Why
Show Answer

Correct Answer: e) Why

Explanation: Descriptive epidemiology covers Time (When), Place (Where), and Person (Who), and often the clinical characteristics (What). Why refers to the causes/determinants, which is the domain of Analytic Epidemiology.

11.
When analyzing data by age the categories should be:
a) The same for all diseases
b) 1 year, 1 to 4 years, 5 to 9 years, 10 to 14 years, 15 to 19 and 20 years for communicable diseases but not necessarily for chronic diseases.
c) Appropriate for each condition and narrow enough to detect any age-related patterns present in the data

Correct Answer: c) Appropriate for each condition and narrow enough to detect any age-related patterns present in the data

Because socio economic status is difficult to quantify, we commonly use all the following substitute measures except: -
a) Educational achievement
b) Family income

13.
Public health surveillance includes which activities
a) Data collection
b) Data analysis
c) Data interpretation
d) Data dissemination
e) Intervention
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a, b, c, d (Usually "Except" e)

Explanation: The definition of surveillance is "The ongoing systematic collection, analysis, and interpretation of health data... closely integrated with the timely dissemination...". 14.
The Framingham study, in which a group of residents have been followed since the 1950’s to identify occurrence and risk factors for heart disease is an example of which types of study: (circle all that apply)
a) Experimental
b) Observational
c) Cohort
d) Case cohort
e) Clinical trial

Correct Answer: b) Observational AND c) Cohort

Explanation: The Framingham Heart Study is the classic example of a prospective Cohort study, which is a type of Observational study (no intervention was applied).

15.
The cancer and steroid hormone (CASH) study in which women with breast cancer and a comparable group of women without breast cancer were asked about their prior use of oral contraceptives (the pill) is an example of which type of study?
a) Experimental
b) Observational
c) Cohort
d) Case cohort
e) Clinical trial
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Observational (Specifically Case-Control)

Explanation: This is a Case-Control study (Cases = Breast Cancer, Controls = No Breast Cancer; looking back at exposure). Case-Control is not listed as a specific option, but it falls under Observational. "Case cohort" is a specific hybrid design, but this description fits standard Case-Control.

16.
The primary difference between an experimental and observational study is
a) The investigator is blinded? (prevented from knowing the subjects’ true exposure status until the end of the study) in an experimental study
b) The investigator controls the subject’s exposure in an experimental study but not in an observational study
c) The investigator controls the subject’s outcome in an experimental study but not in an observational study
d) Experimental studies are conducted with animals; observational studies are conducted with humans
e) None of the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) The investigator controls the subject’s exposure in an experimental study but not in an observational study

Explanation: In experimental studies (trials), the researcher assigns the exposure (e.g., drug vs placebo). In observational studies, the researcher simply observes exposures that occur naturally.

19.
Direct transmission includes which of the following modes of transmission?
a) Droplet spread
b) Vehicle borne transmission
c) Vector-borne transmission
d) Airborne transmission

Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Droplet spread

20.
An epidemic curve which follows the classic log-normal pattern of sharp rise and more gradual decline is most consistent with which manner of spread?
a) Continuous source
b) Intermittent source
c) Point source
d) Propagated
e) Mixed
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Point source

Explanation: A Point Source epidemic (like food poisoning) typically shows a sharp rise to a peak (as everyone is exposed at once) followed by a log-normal (gradual) decline.

21.
Both incidence and prevalence can be represented by the formula (x/y) x 10^n for a specified time period. The primary difference between incidence and prevalence is in
a) x
b) y
c) 10^n
d) The time period of reference
e) Impossible to tell
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) x (The numerator)

Explanation: - Incidence: x = Number of NEW cases. - Prevalence: x = Number of EXISTING (new + old) cases. - While 'y' (denominator) also changes slightly (population at risk vs total population), the fundamental distinction is whether you are counting events (new cases) or states (existing cases).

22.
Both point prevalence and period prevalence can be represented by the formula (x/y) x 10^n for a specified time period. The primary difference between point prevalence and period prevalence is in:
a) x
b) y
c) 10^n
d) The time period of reference
e) Impossible to tell
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) The time period of reference

Explanation: - Point Prevalence: Time period is a single point (e.g., "Do you smoke now?"). - Period Prevalence: Time period is an interval (e.g., "Have you smoked in the last year?").

23.
In a recent survey, investigators found that the prevalence of disease A was higher than the prevalence of disease B. The incidence and seasonal pattern of both diseases are similar. Explanations consistent with this observation include:
a) Patients recover more quickly from disease A than from disease B
b) Patients recover more quickly from disease B than from disease A
c) Patients die quickly from disease A but not from disease B
d) Patients die quickly from disease B but not from disease A
e) None of the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) and d)

Explanation: Prevalence ($P$) depends on Incidence ($I$) and Duration ($D$) ($P \approx I \times D$). If $I$ is equal, for $P_A > P_B$, disease A must have a longer duration than B. - b) If patients recover quickly from B, B's duration is short, so $P_B$ is low. Consistent. - d) If patients die quickly from B, B's duration is short, so $P_B$ is low. Consistent. - a) and c) would make A's duration short, reducing $P_A$, which contradicts the finding.

24.
A recent train derailment at Kibera in Nairobi exposed residents of the slum community to a chemical hazard. Many residents became ill, some died. To calculate the probability or risk of illness, which denominator would you use?
a) The size of the population at risk at the beginning of the period
b) The size of the population at risk at the mid-point of the period
c) The size of the population at risk at the end of the period
d) The average size of the population at risk during the period
e) None of the above except guessing

Correct Answer: a) The size of the population at risk at the beginning of the period

Explanation: Risk (Cumulative Incidence) uses the population at risk at the start of the observation period as the denominator.

25.
During the Christmas week, 87 persons in a small community (population = 460) attended a social event which included a meal prepared by several of the participants. Within 3 days, 39 of the participants became ill with a condition diagnosed as salmonellosis. The attack rate among participants was:
a) 0.45/100
b) 8.5/100
c) 18.9/100
d) 44.8/100
e) Cannot be calculated from the information given
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) 44.8/100 (44.8%)

Calculation: $$ \text{Attack Rate} = \frac{\text{Ill}}{\text{Exposed (Participants)}} \times 100 $$ $$ \text{Attack Rate} = \frac{39}{87} \times 100 = 44.8\% $$ Note: The total community population (460) is irrelevant as they were not at the event (not exposed).

26.
In a community of 800 households (population = 4799), public health authorities found 120 persons with condition D in 80 households. A total of 480 persons lived in the 80 affected households. Assuming that each household had only one primary case, the secondary attack rate is
a) 8.5%
b) 10.0%
c) 16.7%
d) 25.0%
e) 30.0%
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) 10.0%

Calculation:

  1. Total cases: 120
  2. Primary cases: 1 per household * 80 households = 80 cases.
  3. Secondary cases: Total (120) - Primary (80) = 40 cases.
  4. Susceptible contacts: Total pop in affected households (480) - Primary cases (80) = 400.
  5. Secondary Attack Rate (SAR): (Secondary Cases / Susceptible Contacts) * 100
  6. SAR = (40 / 400) * 100 = 10%.

27.
A questionnaire was administered to the persons who attended a funeral of an assistant chief in a sub location. The two by two table shown below summarizes the relationship between consumption of potato salad and illness. The best estimate of the relative risk is approximately:
ILL WELL TOTAL
EXPOSED a = 36 b = 12 48
UNEXPOSED c = 3 d = 36 39
TOTAL 39 48 87
a) 1.7
b) 3.7
c) 9.7
d) 36.0
e) 87
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) 9.7

Calculation:

  1. Incidence in Exposed ($I_e$): $a / (a+b) = 36 / 48 = 0.75$
  2. Incidence in Unexposed ($I_u$): $c / (c+d) = 3 / 39 = 0.0769$
  3. Relative Risk (RR): $I_e / I_u = 0.75 / 0.0769 \approx 9.75$

28.
Of the following mortality rates, which one uses the same denominator
a) Crude mortality rate
b) Age specific mortality rate
c) Sex specific mortality rate
d) Race specific mortality rate
e) Course specific mortality rate
Show Answer

Correct Answer: e) Cause specific mortality rate (Presumed "Course" is typo for "Cause")

Explanation: - Crude Mortality Rate uses the Total Mid-Year Population. - Cause-Specific Mortality Rate (deaths from X / Total Pop) also uses the Total Mid-Year Population. - Age/Sex/Race specific rates use only the population of that specific subgroup (e.g., Pop > 65, Female Pop). Therefore, Crude and Cause-specific rates share the same denominator.

Section 2: Calculations & Short Answers

40 Marks
1.
The following data was obtained from a survey conducted in Nakuru town to determine whether smoking should be banned among the youths in Kenya.

Category Death rates from lung cancer per 1000 persons aged 30 or more per year
I Non-smokers 0.07
II Cigarette smokers 0.96

a) From the data above, compute:
i) Relative risk of smokers versus non-smokers (5 marks)
ii) Attributable risk of smokers versus non-smokers (3 marks)
iii) Explain your answers in (a) above in narrative form (4 marks)
b) What are the uses of relative risk to?
i) The clinician
ii) The researcher
c) What are the uses of attributable risk?
i) To the physician responsible for prevention programs? (5 marks)
ii) To the physician responsible for health planning for large groups (5 marks)
Show Answer

a) Computations:

i) Relative Risk (RR):

$$ RR = \frac{\text{Rate in Exposed (Smokers)}}{\text{Rate in Unexposed (Non-smokers)}} $$

$$ RR = \frac{0.96}{0.07} = 13.7 $$

ii) Attributable Risk (AR):

$$ AR = \text{Rate in Exposed} - \text{Rate in Unexposed} $$

$$ AR = 0.96 - 0.07 = 0.89 \text{ deaths per 1000 persons per year} $$

iii) Explanation:

  • Relative Risk (13.7): Smokers are 13.7 times more likely to die from lung cancer than non-smokers. This indicates a very strong association.
  • Attributable Risk (0.89/1000): Among smokers, 0.89 deaths per 1000 persons per year can be attributed specifically to smoking. If smoking were eliminated, 0.89 deaths per 1000 smokers could be prevented.

b) Uses of Relative Risk:

  • Clinician: Helps in counseling patients about the magnitude of risk associated with a behavior (e.g., "You are 14 times more likely to die if you smoke").
  • Researcher: Used to assess the strength of association between exposure and disease, helping to establish causality.

c) Uses of Attributable Risk:

  • i) Prevention Programs: It shows the potential impact of an intervention. It quantifies the excess disease burden that could be eliminated if the risk factor (smoking) is removed.
  • ii) Health Planning: It helps in resource allocation by estimating the public health impact. It tells planners how many lives can be saved or cases prevented in the community, guiding priority setting.
2.
Table 1: Prevalence and incidence of selected neurological diseases at Kenyatta National Hospital, Nairobi Kenya.

Diagnosis Rates per 100,000 population
Prevalence Incidence
i) Epilepsy 69 17.3
ii) Multiple sclerosis 376 30.8
iii) Parkinson's disease 157 20.0
iv) Motor neuron disease 55 5.0
v) CNS Neoplasms 7 1.7

a) From the data in table 1 above obtained from Health Records and Information department, compute the average duration, in years of the five chronic neurological conditions listed (15 marks)
b) In a recent survey conducted in Nairobi city it was found that male street children aged below 20 who are heavy smokers have relative risk for lung cancer of 10. Given that the absolute risk for lung cancer of parking boys of the same age who do not smoke is 0.1 per 1000, compute the probability of a male street boy (smoker) aged below 20 developing lung cancer per year (5 marks)
Show Answer

a) Average Duration Calculation:

Formula: $Prevalence \approx Incidence \times Duration \rightarrow Duration = \frac{Prevalence}{Incidence}$

  1. Epilepsy: $69 / 17.3 \approx 3.99 \text{ years}$
  2. Multiple Sclerosis: $376 / 30.8 \approx 12.21 \text{ years}$
  3. Parkinson's Disease: $157 / 20.0 = 7.85 \text{ years}$
  4. Motor Neuron Disease: $55 / 5.0 = 11.0 \text{ years}$
  5. CNS Neoplasms: $7 / 1.7 \approx 4.12 \text{ years}$

b) Probability Calculation:

  • Given: Relative Risk (RR) = 10
  • Given: Incidence in Unexposed ($I_u$) = 0.1 per 1000
  • Formula: $RR = \frac{I_e}{I_u}$
  • We need $I_e$ (Incidence in Exposed/Smokers):
  • $10 = \frac{I_e}{0.1}$
  • $I_e = 10 \times 0.1 = 1.0$

Answer: The probability (absolute risk) is 1.0 per 1000 per year.

Section 3: Long Answer Question

20 Marks
1.
Dr. Mohammed is a County Director of Health Services in Kwale County of Coast region. The county serves a population of 120,000. He spends all his time at the hospital where he handles essentially curative cases. Last month (January) alone, he treated many children suffering from diarrhea. All of them were from Kinango sub-county situated 35 kilometers from the country referral hospital. Dr. Mohammed has no information on Kinango which he last visited two years before. He likes curative care so much that he hardly moves out to supervise the health facilities in his area of jurisdiction or to attend health committee meetings. Therefore, he has no information on the population that his hospital is supposed to cover.

a) In your own opinion, why does Dr. Mohammed behave the way he does?
b) In reality, does this happen? Gives reasons for your answer
c) What information should Dr. Mohammed have on the health of his community?
d) How can he obtain the information in 'C' above?
Show Answer

a) Reasons for Dr. Mohammed's Behavior:

  • Clinical Bias: He likely has a clinical background (doctor/clinician) rather than public health training, leading to a focus on individual treatment ("Curative bias").
  • Lack of Resources: He may lack transport, fuel, or staffing to conduct field visits or supervision.
  • Understaffing: If he is the only doctor, he may be overwhelmed by the patient load at the referral hospital, leaving no time for administrative duties.
  • Lack of Motivation/Incentives: Poor supervision from national level or lack of allowances for field work.

b) Reality Check:

Yes, this is very common in reality. Reasons:

  • Shortage of Health Workers: Managers often have to double as clinicians due to staff shortages.
  • Poor Funding: Public health activities (prevention, supervision) are often underfunded compared to curative services (drugs, equipment).
  • Training Gaps: Many managers are appointed based on clinical seniority, not management or epidemiological competence.

c) Essential Health Information Needed:

  • Demographics: Population size, age/sex distribution, density in Kinango.
  • Morbidity/Mortality Data: Top causes of illness and death (e.g., Diarrhea incidence rates).
  • Environmental Factors: Water sources, sanitation coverage (latrines) in Kinango.
  • Health Service Coverage: Immunization rates, facility utilization, staffing levels in peripheral facilities.
  • Social Determinants: Poverty levels, education, cultural practices affecting health.

d) Methods to Obtain Information:

  • Routine Health Information System (DHIS2): Review monthly reports from Kinango health facilities.
  • Surveillance Reports: Check IDSR (Integrated Disease Surveillance and Response) data for outbreaks.
  • Field Visits/Supervision: Conduct supportive supervision visits to Kinango.
  • Community Health Volunteers (CHVs): Gather reports from Level 1 community units.
  • Surveys: Conduct rapid assessments or household surveys in the affected area.
  • Stakeholder Meetings: Attend health committee meetings to hear from community representatives.
