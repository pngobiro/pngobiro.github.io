Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions

40 Marks
1.
A longitudinal or prospective study is also referred to as:
a) Ecological study
b) Cross-sectional study
c) Cohort study
d) Observational study
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Cohort study

Explanation: A cohort study typically involves following a group of people (cohort) over a period of time (longitudinal/prospective) to observe outcomes.

2.
All of the following are true about odds ratio except:
a) It is an estimate of relative risk
b) It is the only measure of risk that can be obtained directly from a case control study
c) It is the ratio of incidences in exposed divided by incidence in exposed group
d) It can be calculated without data on rates (as in case of control study)
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) It is the ratio of incidences in exposed divided by incidence in exposed group

Explanation: This describes Relative Risk (Ratio of incidence in exposed / incidence in unexposed), not Odds Ratio. OR is the ratio of odds of exposure in cases vs controls.

3.
Which year was mortality data by birth, sex, region and season started?
a) 1662
b) 1954
c) 1800
d) 1980
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) 1662

Explanation: John Graunt published his analysis of the "Bills of Mortality" in London in 1662, marking the beginning of vital statistics.

4.
Which one of the following is not a term used in epidemiology?
a) Agent
b) Vector
c) Case
d) Data
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Data

Explanation: Agent, Vector, and Case are specific epidemiological concepts. "Data" is a general scientific term, not unique to or a structural component of epidemiological models (like the Triad or Chain of Infection) in the same way the others are.

5.
What type of a bias arises when individuals with a particular exposure of adverse outcome is likely to remember their experiences differently from those who are not similarly affected?
a) Selection bias
b) Recall bias
c) Information bias
d) Surrogate bias
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Recall bias

Explanation: Recall bias happens when cases (those with the adverse outcome) recall past exposures better or differently than controls.

6.
A county health personnel visits outpatient clinics and emergency rooms to determine the number of cases of influenza virus. This is an example of which type of surveillance?
a) Active surveillance
b) Passive surveillance
c) Sentinel surveillance
d) Syndromic surveillance
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Active surveillance

Explanation: The key is that the health personnel visits the facilities to find cases, rather than waiting for reports (Passive).

7.
Which of the following is not a scope of epidemiology?
a) Causation
b) Natural history of disease
c) Evaluation of intervention
d) Establish priorities
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Establish priorities (Arguably)

Explanation: Epidemiology studies causation (etiology), natural history, and evaluates interventions. While it provides data to help establish priorities, the act of "establishing priorities" is often considered a management/policy function rather than a core research scope of the science itself. However, all listed are often cited as uses.

8.
The following is not a measure of effect in epidemiology?
a) Odds ratio
b) Rate ratio
c) Proportion ratio
d) Risk ratio
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Proportion ratio

Explanation: "Proportion ratio" is not a standard term for a measure of effect in most epidemiology texts (Prevalence Ratio is used, but "Proportion Ratio" is vague). Odds Ratio, Risk Ratio (Relative Risk), and Rate Ratio are standard measures of effect.

9.
Which one of the following is a type of surveillance?
a) Point surveillance
b) Propagated surveillance
c) Sentinel surveillance
d) Continuous surveillance
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Sentinel surveillance

Explanation: Standard types of surveillance are Active, Passive, Sentinel, and Syndromic. "Point" and "Propagated" refer to types of epidemics, not surveillance.

10.
Which of the following is NOT a measures of variability?
a) Range
b) Variance
c) Mean
d) Standard deviation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Mean

Explanation: The Mean is a measure of Central Tendency. Range, Variance, and Standard Deviation are measures of Variability (Dispersion).

11.
Which of the following is NOT a type of validity?
a) Construct validity
b) Content validity
c) Face validity
d) Time validity
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Time validity

Explanation: Construct, Content, and Face validity are standard psychometric types of validity. "Time validity" is not a standard term in this context.

12.
_______ is a measure of health outcome
a) Prevalence
b) Incidence
c) Morbidity
d) Risk
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Morbidity

Explanation: Morbidity (illness) is a direct health outcome. Prevalence, Incidence, and Risk are measures or metrics used to quantify morbidity, but Morbidity itself is the outcome state.

13.
Which one of the following is not a disease marked for eradication and elimination by IDSR?
a) Neonatal Tetanus
b) Dracuniolosis
c) Plague
d) Poliomyelitis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Plague

Explanation: Polio, Dracunculiasis (Guinea Worm), and Neonatal Tetanus are targeted for eradication/elimination. Plague is a zoonosis with animal reservoirs, making eradication generally impossible.

14.
What type of surveillance provides stimulus of health care workers in the form of individual feedback?
a) Passive surveillance
b) Active surveillance
c) Sentinel surveillance
d) All of the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Active surveillance

Explanation: Active surveillance involves regular contact and visits, which naturally includes feedback and stimulation of the health workers to report.

15.
Which one of the following is not a type of non-communicable diseases?
a) Stroke
b) Measles
c) Heart disease
d) All of the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Measles

Explanation: Measles is a classic communicable (infectious) disease. Stroke and Heart disease are non-communicable.

16.
The following are the main types of validity except:
a) Construct validity
b) Content validity
c) Predictive validity
d) Face validity
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Face validity (Often considered the weakest)

Explanation: This is a trick question as all are types. However, Face validity is subjective ("looks like it measures") and is often excluded from the "Main" scientific types compared to Construct, Content, and Criterion (Predictive) validity. Some texts list Internal/External as the "Main" types in epidemiology.

17.
In the definition of epidemiology, "distribution" refers to:
a) WHO
b) WHEN
c) WHERE
d) WHY
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) WHY (This is the Exception)

Explanation: The question likely asks "refers to... [all except]" or implies "Which is NOT". Distribution = Who, When, Where. Why = Determinants. If the question is "refers to", then A, B, C are correct. Since D is the odd one out (Determinants), usually these questions are "Except" questions with the word missing.

18.
Which of the following statements is true?
a) A specific case definition is one that is likely to include only (or mostly) true cases
b) A specific case definition is one that is considered loose or broad
c) A specific case definition is one that will include more cases than a sensitive case definition
d) A specific case definition is one that may exclude mild case
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) A specific case definition is one that is likely to include only (or mostly) true cases

Explanation: Specificity aims to exclude non-cases, ensuring those identified are true cases. Option D is also a consequence of high specificity.

19.
For a disease to occur there must be an existence of:
a) Agent, carrier, environment
b) Agent, host, environment
c) Host, environment, cases
d) Agent, host, cases
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Agent, host, environment

Explanation: The Epidemiological Triad consists of Agent, Host, and Environment.

20.
Which of the following is NOT a type of prevalence?
a) Point prevalence
b) Period prevalence
c) Lifetime prevalence
d) Continuous prevalence
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Continuous prevalence

Explanation: Standard types are Point, Period, and Lifetime. "Continuous prevalence" is not a standard term.

21.
Which of the following are the IDSR priority diseases?
a) Cholera, poliomyelitis
b) Childhood pneumonia, childhood diarrhea leprosy
c) Dysentery, measles, typhoid fever
d) Malaria, cholera, measles
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Cholera, poliomyelitis (and D is also strong)

Explanation: Cholera and Polio are top priority diseases for immediate reporting. Malaria and Measles (D) are also priorities. Usually, "Epidemic prone" diseases like Cholera and Polio take precedence in these lists.

22.
Which of the following is a way of tertiary prevention of a disease?
a) Antibiotic therapy to a patient with pneumonia
b) Long term therapy to patient with chronic disease
c) Observing personal and food hygiene
d) Detecting and treating
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Long term therapy to patient with chronic disease

Explanation: Tertiary prevention involves managing chronic conditions to prevent complications and disability (Rehabilitation/Long-term management).

23.
Standardized measures that assist in the comparison of health issues in a population and identification of any change in health status are known as:
a) Health indicators
b) Health sentinels
c) Determinants of health
d) Health surveillance
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Health indicators

Explanation: Health indicators (like IMR, MMR) are standardized measures used to compare health status between populations and over time.

24.
Which of the following statements is true?
a) Herd immunity is Immunity resulting from vaccination of an individual
b) Herd immunity is an index case
c) Herd immunity is an Immunity of a herd of cows
d) Herd immunity is an Immunity of a large proportions of people
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Herd immunity is an Immunity of a large proportions of people

Explanation: Herd immunity refers to the resistance of a group to invasion/spread of an infectious agent based on the immunity of a high proportion of individual members.

25.
What type of data is found in the disease register?
a) Primary data
b) Secondary data
c) Disease data
d) Continuous data
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Secondary data

Explanation: Registers (like Cancer Registries) collate data collected by others (hospitals/labs), making it Secondary Data for the epidemiologist using the register.

26.
The following are principles of disease prevention except:
a) Attacking the source of the disease causing organism
b) Interrupting the transmission cycle
c) Killing the host
d) Protecting the susceptible host
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Killing the host

Explanation: In human medicine, we do not kill the host to prevent disease. We protect the host.

27.
The epidemiological triad of disease causation refers to:
a) Time, place, person
b) Source, mode of transmission, susceptible host
c) Agent, host, environments
d) John Snow, Robert Koch, Kenneth Rothman

Correct Answer: c) Agent, host, environments

Explanation: This is the definition of the Epidemiological Triad.

28.

Section 2: Short Answer Questions

40 Marks
41.
Outline five principles of epidemiology (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Exact Observation: Strict, standardized measurement of events.
  2. Correct Interpretation: Using appropriate statistical methods to analyze data.
  3. Rational Explanation: Deriving intelligent conclusions based on evidence.
  4. Scientific Construction: Using the data to build theories (e.g., germ theory).
  5. Application: Using findings to prevent and control disease.
42.
Explain the following terms as used in Epidemiology (5 marks)
i) Vector
ii) Confounding bias
Show Answer
  • i) Vector: An organism, typically a biting insect or tick, that transmits a disease or parasite from one animal or plant to another (e.g., Mosquitoes).
  • ii) Confounding Bias: A distortion of the estimated effect of an exposure on an outcome, caused by the presence of an extraneous variable associated with both the exposure and the outcome.
43.
Outline 5 challenges faced by IDSR in Kenya (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Inadequate funding/resources for surveillance activities.
  2. Shortage of trained staff/high turnover.
  3. Poor infrastructure (network/internet) for reporting.
  4. Incomplete or late reporting from facilities.
  5. Lack of feedback from national to lower levels.
44.
State 5 purposes of descriptive research (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. To describe the extent of a health problem (Magnitude).
  2. To identify populations at high risk.
  3. To provide data for planning and resource allocation.
  4. To generate hypotheses about disease causation.
  5. To monitor trends over time.
45.
State 5 methods of controlling communicable disease (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Vaccination/Immunization: Reducing susceptibility.
  2. Isolation/Quarantine: Separating reservoirs/sources.
  3. Treatment: Curing cases to stop transmission.
  4. Vector Control: Spraying, nets.
  5. Environmental Sanitation: Water treatment, hygiene.
46.
Outline 5 purposes of Disease Surveillance (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. To detect epidemics/outbreaks early.
  2. To monitor long-term disease trends.
  3. To evaluate the effectiveness of control programs.
  4. To identify changes in infectious agents.
  5. To facilitate planning.
47.
Identify 5 possible data sources in Epidemiology (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Census data.
  2. Vital Statistics (Births/Deaths).
  3. Medical Records/Hospital data.
  4. Disease Registers (Cancer/TB).
  5. Health Surveys (KDHS).
48.
State the steps in Disease Surveillance (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Data Collection: Detection and reporting of cases.
  2. Data Analysis: Processing and interpreting data.
  3. Interpretation: Determining significance.
  4. Dissemination: Sharing findings with stakeholders.
  5. Action/Response: Implementing control measures.

49.
UnexposedExposed
Diseased9 (c)17 (a)
Not Diseased7 (d)5 (b)

1. Calculate the fraction of cases with the disease among the exposed that is attributable to the exposure using the above table (10 Marks)
2. With the aid of a well labelled diagram, describe the 6 links of disease transmission cycle (10 marks)
Show Answer

1. Attributable Fraction in Exposed (Afe):

Formula: $Afe = \frac{I_e - I_u}{I_e}$

  • Incidence in Exposed ($I_e$): $a / (a+b) = 17 / (17+5) = 17/22 = 0.77$
  • Incidence in Unexposed ($I_u$): $c / (c+d) = 9 / (9+7) = 9/16 = 0.56$
  • Calculation: $(0.77 - 0.56) / 0.77 = 0.21 / 0.77 = 0.27$

Answer: 0.27 (or 27%)

2. Chain of Transmission Diagram:

[Circular Diagram]
1. Infectious Agent (Bacteria, Virus)
          
2. Reservoir (Human, Animal, Soil)
          
3. Portal of Exit (Mouth, Skin)
          
4. Mode of Transmission (Contact, Vector, Air)
          
5. Portal of Entry (Mouth, Broken Skin)
          
6. Susceptible Host (Low immunity)

Description: The chain consists of the pathogen (Agent) leaving its home (Reservoir) through an Exit, traveling via a Mode of Transmission, entering through an Entry into a new Susceptible Host.
