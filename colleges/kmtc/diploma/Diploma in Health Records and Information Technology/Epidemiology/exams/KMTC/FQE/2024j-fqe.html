Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions

40 Marks
1.
As an epidemiologist, what would you consider as true about the susceptibility phase of a disease?
A. Signs and symptoms of a disease are present
B. Biological process of the disease has started
C. Risk factors of the disease are present
D. There is some physical manifestation of the disease
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Risk factors of the disease are present

Explanation: The Susceptibility Stage (Pre-pathogenesis) is characterized by the presence of risk factors (e.g., smoking, poor diet) but the disease process itself has not yet begun (No biological process, no symptoms).

2.
A public health officer decides to do conduct health promotion targeting lifestyle factors. Which method of disease prevention is he using?
A. Tertiary prevention
B. Primordial prevention
C. Secondary prevention
D. Primary prevention
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Primary prevention (or B Primordial depending on context)

Explanation: Health promotion to prevent the onset of disease is Primary Prevention. If the action is at a policy level to prevent the emergence of the risk factors themselves (e.g., banning soda ads), it is Primordial. "Targeting lifestyle factors" usually falls under Primary (Health Promotion).

3.
Which branch of epidemiology investigates the association between a given disease and the causative factors of the same disease?
A. Applied epidemiology
B. Clinical epidemiology
C. Descriptive epidemiology
D. Analytic epidemiology
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Analytic epidemiology

Explanation: Analytic epidemiology is concerned with the "Why" and "How" – investigating associations and causes (determinants).

4.
Not everyone infected with Tuberculosis develops active disease. Other factors are needed to cause the disease. These factors are called
A. Necessary causes
B. Component causes
C. Sufficient causes
D. Important causes
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Component causes

Explanation: The TB bacteria is the Necessary cause. The other factors (malnutrition, HIV, overcrowding) that combine with it to produce disease are Component causes. Together they form a Sufficient cause.

5.
Counting is a key activity of epidemiologists. Which is the correct term that depicts the above statement?
A. Disease distribution
B. Disease frequency
C. Disease determinants
D. Disease control
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Disease frequency

Explanation: "Counting cases" relates to quantifying the magnitude/occurrence, which is termed Disease Frequency.

6.
A patient was admitted in hospital suffering from pneumonia. During the patients stay in the hospital, he developed an infection. Such types of infections are called
A. Nasocomial infection
B. Health infections
C. Patient acquired infections
D. Hospital infections
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Nasocomial infection

Explanation: Infections acquired during a hospital stay (that were not present on admission) are called Nosocomial (or Hospital-Acquired) infections.

7.
Handkerchiefs, drinking glasses, door handles, clothing and toys can convey infection to others when contaminated. Which is the correct name that refers to them?
A. Contaminants
B. Droplets
C. Fomites
D. Inanimates
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Fomites

Explanation: Fomites are inanimate objects that can harbor and transmit infectious agents.

8.
A person infected with HIV/AIDS might not have the signs and symptoms but is capable of transmitting the infection to others. Which of the following terms correctly describe the above statement?
A. Symptomatic
B. Asymptomatic
C. Clinical
D. Carrier
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Carrier (or B Asymptomatic)

Explanation: While the person is asymptomatic (B), the capability to transmit while infected without symptoms defines a Carrier (D). "Carrier" is the epidemiological role.

9.
Which one of the following is a zoonotic disease?
A. COVID
B. Rabies
C. Scabies
D. Chicken pox
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Rabies

Explanation: Rabies is a classic zoonosis transmitted from animals (dogs, bats) to humans. While COVID-19 has zoonotic origins, Rabies is the textbook example. Scabies is a mite infestation. Chicken pox is human-specific.

10.
During singing some saliva might land on the next person. If the saliva contains some disease agent, such transmission is called
A. Direct contact
B. Droplet nuclei
C. Direct spread
D. Direct projection
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Direct projection (or Droplet spread)

Explanation: This is typically called Droplet Spread. "Direct projection" is a synonym used in some older texts for the spray of droplets onto conjunctiva or mucous membranes. "Droplet nuclei" (B) implies airborne (long distance). Direct contact (A) implies skin-to-skin.

11.
The agent for malaria multiplies in the vector before transmission, such a vector is called?
A. Mechanical vector
B. Biological vector
C. Chemical vector
D. Physical vector
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Biological vector

Explanation: If the agent undergoes multiplication or development (biological change) inside the vector (like Plasmodium in mosquitoes), it is a Biological Vector.

12.
A person consumed food at a birthday party and developed diarrhea. Which type of transmission is this that occurred through food or water he may have consumed
A. Vector borne
B. Biological borne
C. Mechanical borne
D. Vehicle borne
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Vehicle borne

Explanation: Food and water are inanimate intermediaries called Vehicles.

13.
Which measure of disease frequency is calculated by dividing the number of cases by the population at risk during the period of observation
A. Proportion
B. Ratio
C. Rate
D. Risk
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Risk (Cumulative Incidence) / C. Rate

Explanation: "Number of cases / Population at risk" is the definition of Cumulative Incidence, which measures Risk. Rates usually involve person-time. However, colloquial usage often calls this a "Rate". "Risk" (D) is the most precise epidemiological term for probability.

14.
The total number of deaths during the year divided by the total population will result to what type of mortality rate
A. Crude mortality rate
B. Specific mortality rate
C. Standardized mortality rate
D. Total mortality rate
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Crude mortality rate

Explanation: Total deaths / Total population = Crude Death Rate.

15.
Which one of the following is the correct numerator for cumulative incidence?
A. Number of old & new cases in a specified time period
B. Number of new cases in a specified time period
C. Number of in new cases in a point in time
D. Number of new cases in unspecified time period
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Number of new cases in a specified time period

Explanation: Incidence counts only NEW cases occurring during the study period.

16.
While calculating prevalence of a disease, which is the correct denominator that you would apply?
A. Total population in the mid-year
B. All cases irrespective of when they started
C. Disease free population at the start of period
D. Total population at risk of being a case
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Total population at risk of being a case

Explanation: Technically, prevalence uses the total population (often mid-year) including cases. However, "Total population at risk" is the general denominator concept for rates/proportions. Option A is for rates. Prevalence usually uses the total population at that time.

17.
Which is the basic measure of disease frequency
A. Ratio
B. Rate
C. Simple count
D. Prevalence
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Simple count

Explanation: The count is the most basic measure.

18.
Which is the key term that differentiates an attack rate from incidence rate
A. Number of new case reported during a given time
B. Number of new cases among the exposed
C. Number of new cases during an epidemic
D. Number of new cases among the population at risk
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Number of new cases during an epidemic

Explanation: An Attack Rate is a type of cumulative incidence used specifically for epidemics/outbreaks (short duration).

19.
The total number of deaths in the year were 100. The total number of deaths from HIV/AIDS were 30. The total number of cases of HIV were 200. Calculate the proportionate mortality rate of HIV/AIDS
A. 30
B. 15
C. 20
D. 25
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. 30 (30%)

Calculation: PMR = (Deaths from HIV / Total Deaths) × 100 = (30 / 100) × 100 = 30%.

20.
The type of mortality rate that presents the killing power of a disease is called
A. Cause specific mortality rate
B. Proportionate mortality rate
C. Crude mortality rate
D. Case fatality rate
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Case fatality rate

Explanation: CFR measures severity/virulence (killing power) among those who are sick.

21.
A population of 100 smokers were followed up for 10 years to ascertain their development of lung cancer. The correct study for the above situation is
A. Case control study
B. Case senses study
C. Cohort study
D. Crossectional study
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Cohort study

Explanation: Following a group (smokers) forward in time (10 years) to see outcomes is a Cohort Study.

22.
In a case control study, which of the following is true
A. Disease rates are compared among exposed & non exposed
B. The investigator may choose not to have multiple companion groups
C. The proportion of cases with exposure is compared with proportion in controls
D. Recall bias is not a potential problem
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. The proportion of cases with exposure is compared with proportion in controls

Explanation: Case-Control studies compare exposure history between Cases and Controls. Rates cannot be calculated directly (A).

23.
The type of study designs that are employed to test specific etiological hypothesis and generate new etiological hypothesis are called
A. Descriptive studies
B. Analytic studies
C. Observational studies
D. Clinical trials

Correct Answer: B. Analytic studies

Explanation: Analytic studies test hypotheses. Descriptive studies generate them.

24.
The process of making cases and controls equivalent with respect to factors other than exposure factors being investigated is called
A. Randomization
B. Blinding
C. Comparing
D. Matching
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Matching

Explanation: In Case-Control studies, we Match controls to cases (e.g., by age/sex) to control for confounding.

25.
Loss to follow up is a major disadvantage in epidemiological studies. Which type of study is majorly affected by loss to follow up
A. Case control studies
B. Crossectional studies
C. Case series
D. Cohort studies
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Cohort studies

Explanation: Because Cohort studies run over long periods (years), participants often drop out (attrition bias).

26.
The type of errors that occur in epidemiology when there is a systematic difference between the characteristics of the people involved in the study are called
A. Systematic bias
B. Measurement bias
C. Selection bias
D. Characteristic bias

Correct Answer: C. Selection bias

Explanation: Systematic differences in who is in the study vs the target population is Selection Bias.

37.
The main reason for quarantine during an outbreak is to
A. To isolate the sick from healthy persons
B. To limit movement of health person to prevent exposure to disease
C. To confine unhealthy persons together for treatment
D. To ensure the incubation period of the disease has elapsed
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. To ensure the incubation period of the disease has elapsed (Conceptually B is close, but D is the mechanism)

Clarification: - Isolation: Separates sick from healthy (A). - Quarantine: Restricts movement of exposed (potentially ill) people for the duration of the incubation period to see if they become sick. Thus, preventing spread if they do.

42.
Consider a class with 100 enrolled students none of the students were ill at the beginning of the school year, on September 30th a total of 5 students reported having gastroenteritis. All the 5 continued to be ill on October 1st but all the 5 recovered within three days. On October 14th another 3 students developed gastroenteritis. All the students continued to be ill on October 15th but all the 3 recovered 5 days later. (5 marks)
a) Calculate the prevalence of gastroenteritis on October 1st
b) Calculate prevalence of gastroenteritis on October 30th
Show Answer

a) Prevalence on Oct 1st:

  • Cases active on Oct 1st = The first 5 students (ill since Sept 30, recovered 3 days later, so still ill on Oct 1).
  • Total Population = 100.
  • Prevalence = 5/100 = 5%.

b) Prevalence on Oct 30th:

  • First 5 recovered by Oct 3rd.
  • Next 3 developed illness Oct 14th, recovered 5 days later (Oct 19th).
  • On Oct 30th, 0 students are ill.
  • Prevalence = 0/100 = 0%.
43.
Describe the main similarities and differences between incidence rate and cumulative incidence. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  • Similarity: Both measure the occurrence of NEW cases of disease in a population over time (numerator is new cases).
  • Difference (Denominator):
    • Cumulative Incidence (Risk): Denominator is population at risk at the beginning of the period. Assumes fixed cohort. Range 0-1.
    • Incidence Rate (Density): Denominator is Person-Time at risk. Handles dynamic populations and variable follow-up times. Range 0 to infinity.

46.
During COVID 19 pandemic, physical and social distancing were main interventions advocated by Ministry of Health. Give the rationale behind this intervention. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  • Mode of Transmission: COVID-19 is transmitted via respiratory droplets expelled when an infected person coughs, sneezes, or talks.
  • Droplet Range: Larger droplets typically fall to the ground within 1-2 meters.
  • Rationale: Maintaining distance (>1.5m) prevents healthy individuals from inhaling these droplets, breaking the chain of transmission at the "Mode of Transmission" link. It reduces the contact rate ($R_0$).
47.
You have been appointed as the disease surveillance officer in a newly created Sub- County. Describe 5 major drawbacks you might encounter from the routine surveillance data. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Underreporting: Many cases are not reported by facilities.
  2. Incompleteness: Forms often have missing data fields.
  3. Lack of Timeliness: Reports arrive too late for effective action.

  5. Lack of Representativeness: Data mainly comes from public facilities, missing private sector and community cases.
48.
An outbreak of cholera has occurred in four jurisdictions. Identify 5 ways you will actively detect the cases. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Facility Visits: Regularly visiting health centers to review registers for acute watery diarrhea.
  2. Community Case Search: Using CHVs (Community Health Volunteers) to find sick people in households.
  3. Contact Tracing: Screening family and neighbors of confirmed cases.
  4. Laboratory Alert: Asking labs to immediately report any positive Vibrio cholerae samples.
  5. Rumor Surveillance: Monitoring social media and community leaders for reports of deaths or illness.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions

20 Marks
49.
You are a newly posted lecturer tasked with teaching 3rd Year students in health records and information Epidemiology. The head of department has assigned you to teach descriptive studies. Explain 4 descriptive studies you will teach the students (10 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Case Reports:
    - Detailed description of a single patient with an unusual disease or association.
    - Useful for identifying new diseases (e.g., first AIDS cases).
  2. Case Series:
    - Description of a group/series of patients with similar diagnoses.
    - Defines clinical characteristics and distributions of a disease.
  3. Cross-Sectional Studies (Prevalence Studies):
    - A "snapshot" of a population at a single point in time.
    - Measures exposure and disease simultaneously.
    - Calculates Prevalence. Good for hypothesis generation.
  4. Ecological (Correlational) Studies:
    - Studies where the unit of analysis is populations/groups (e.g., countries) rather than individuals.
    - Compares aggregate exposure levels with aggregate disease rates (e.g., Fat consumption vs Breast cancer rates by country).
50.
Discuss clinical trials under the following headlines (10 marks)
a) Placebo
b) Blinding
c) Experimental group
d) Control group
e) Effectiveness trial
Show Answer
  • a) Placebo: An inert substance (e.g., sugar pill) indistinguishable from the active drug, given to the control group to eliminate the psychological effect of receiving treatment ("Placebo Effect").
  • b) Blinding (Masking): A procedure where subjects and/or investigators do not know who is receiving the treatment vs. placebo. Reduces information/observation bias. (Single, Double, Triple blind).
  • c) Experimental group: The group of participants randomly assigned to receive the new intervention/treatment being tested.
  • d) Control group: The group assigned to receive the standard treatment or placebo. They provide the baseline for comparison to determine if the new treatment works.
  • e) Effectiveness trial (Pragmatic trial): A trial conducted under "real-world" conditions (not ideal/controlled) to see if the treatment works in routine practice with typical patients.
