Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions (20 Marks)

1.
Microbes were discovered by?
a. John Snow
b. Pasteur and Koch
c. Hippocrates
d. Edward Jenner
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b. Pasteur and Koch

Explanation: Louis Pasteur and Robert Koch are pivotal figures in the Germ Theory of Disease. Pasteur demonstrated that microorganisms cause fermentation and disease, while Koch developed postulates to identify specific causative agents.

Why other options are incorrect:

  • a. John Snow: Considered the father of modern epidemiology (famous for the Cholera outbreak investigation), not the discoverer of microbes.
  • c. Hippocrates: The father of medicine, lived long before microbes were discovered.
  • d. Edward Jenner: Pioneer of the smallpox vaccine (immunology).
2.
In the definition of epidemiology, determinants generally include
a. Agents
b. Control measures
c. Risk factors
d. Source
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c. Risk factors

Explanation: Epidemiology is the study of the distribution and determinants of health-related states. Determinants are factors that influence the risk of disease, commonly referred to as risk factors (physical, biological, social, behavioral). While agents are a type of determinant, "Risk factors" is the broader and most common synonym used in this context.

3.
Which of the following is not part of the word epidemiology?
a. Demos
b. Logos
c. Epi
d. Demio
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d. Demio

Explanation: The word Epidemiology is derived from three Greek words: Epi (upon/among), Demos (people), and Logos (study of). "Demio" is not part of the etymology.

4.
The search for causes and other factors that influence the occurrence of health-related events is?
a. Pattern
b. Distribution
c. Determinants
d. Epidemiology
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c. Determinants

Explanation: Determinants are the underlying causes and factors (biological, social, environmental) that influence the occurrence of disease. "Distribution" refers to frequency and pattern, while "Determinants" refers to causes.

5.
When the occurrence of a disease is persistently higher than normal it's called
a. Holo-endemic
b. Hyperendemic
c. Pandemic
d. Endemic
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b. Hyperendemic

Explanation: Hyperendemic refers to a disease that is constantly present at a high incidence and/or prevalence rate and affects all age groups equally. Endemic refers to constant presence at a baseline level.

6.
The measure of the magnitude of disease or other health related events is called
a. Counts
b. Percentage
c. Frequency
d. Proportion
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c. Frequency

Explanation: Frequency quantification involves counting the number of cases (magnitude). Rates, ratios, and proportions are specific types of frequency measures.

7.
Which of the following is not true about proportion?
a. Measures part of the population affected by disease compared to the total population
b. Can be expressed per 100
c. The time period may not be specified
d. The denominator is part of the numerator
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d. The denominator is part of the numerator

Explanation: This statement is mathematically incorrect. In a proportion, the numerator is part of the denominator (A / A+B). The option claims the reverse, making it the false statement.

8.
The natural history of the disease begins with the following except
a. Exposure to risk factors of the disease
b. Accumulation of factors capable of causing a disease
c. Susceptibility of the persons
d. Presence of signs and symptoms
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d. Presence of signs and symptoms

Explanation: The natural history of disease includes the period of pre-pathogenesis (before disease occurs), which involves susceptibility, exposure, and accumulation of risk factors. The presence of signs and symptoms marks the clinical stage, which occurs later in the process, not at the beginning.

9.
The proportion of exposed persons who become infected is referred to as
a. Virulence
b. Infectivity
c. Pathogenicity
d. Holo endemic
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b. Infectivity

Explanation: Infectivity is the capacity of an agent to enter, survive, and multiply in a host (measured by the proportion of exposed people who become infected). Pathogenicity is the ability to cause disease (symptoms), and virulence is the ability to cause severe disease.

10.
In order for a disease agent to survive, it must be able to do the following except
a. Infect a new host
b. Reach a new host
c. Originate from a reservoir
d. Multiply
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c. Originate from a reservoir

Explanation: While an agent typically has a reservoir, the active processes required for survival and propagation in the chain of infection are multiplying, leaving the reservoir/host, reaching a new host (transmission), and infecting them. "Originate from a reservoir" is a static property rather than a survival action like the others.

11.
Which of the following is not a type of carrier?
a. Asymptomatic carriers
b. Symptomatic carriers
c. Chronic carriers
d. Convalescent carriers
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b. Symptomatic carriers

Explanation: By definition, a carrier harbors the infectious agent without showing apparent clinical disease. A person who is "symptomatic" is considered a clinical case, not a carrier. Asymptomatic (healthy), chronic (long-term), and convalescent (recovering) are valid types of carriers.

12.
Which of the following disease does not have animals as the reservoir?
a. Brucellosis
b. Legionnaires bacillus
c. Plague
d. Trichinosis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b. Legionnaires bacillus

Explanation: Legionnaires' disease (Legionella) is caused by bacteria found naturally in the environment (water and soil), not animals. Brucellosis (livestock), Plague (rodents), and Trichinosis (pigs/game) are zoonotic diseases with animal reservoirs.

13.
The type of transmission by direct spray over a few feet before they fall on the ground is called?
a. Direct contact
b. Droplet spread
c. Mechanical
d. Droplet nuclei
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b. Droplet spread

Explanation: Droplet spread refers to the transmission of infection via large droplets (generated by coughing, sneezing, talking) that travel short distances (usually less than a few feet) before settling. Droplet nuclei are smaller residues that can remain suspended in air for long periods (airborne).

14.
Which of the following is not a vector?
a. Ants
b. Mosquitoes
c. Fleas
d. Ticks
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a. Ants

Explanation: Mosquitoes (Malaria, Dengue), Fleas (Plague), and Ticks (Lyme disease) are classic biological vectors. While ants can mechanically carry pathogens on their bodies, they are not standard vectors for major human diseases in the same category as the others.

15.
The type of immunity whereby antibodies develop in response to infection, vaccine or toxoid is called?
a. Active immunity
b. Passive immunity
c. Natural immunity
d. Herd immunity
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a. Active immunity

Explanation: Active immunity occurs when the host's own immune system produces antibodies in response to an antigen (either through natural infection or vaccination). Passive immunity involves receiving pre-formed antibodies (e.g., from mother to fetus).

16.
Which of the following is not a reservoir for yellow fever?
a. Aedes aegypti
b. Monkeys
c. Soil
d. Vertebrates
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c. Soil

Explanation: Yellow fever is maintained in cycles involving monkeys (sylvatic cycle) and humans (urban cycle). The vector is the mosquito (Aedes species). Soil is not a reservoir for the Yellow Fever virus.

17.
Which of the following is not true about relative risk?
a. Can also be referred to as risk ratio
b. Can compare the risk of disease in two groups
c. The group of primary interest is the un exposed group
d. The two groups are typically differentiated by demographic factors
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c. The group of primary interest is the un exposed group

Explanation: Relative Risk (RR) compares the risk in the exposed group to the risk in the unexposed group. The exposed group is typically the group of primary interest to see if the exposure increases risk.

18.
Prevalence can be increased by the following except
a. Addition of new cases
b. Improved treatment
c. Recovery
d. Death
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c. Recovery

Explanation: Prevalence is the total number of existing cases. It is increased by new cases (incidence) and by factors that prolong duration (like improved treatment that prevents death but doesn't cure). It is decreased by people leaving the "case" pool, either through Recovery (cure) or Death. Therefore, Recovery does NOT increase prevalence.

19.
Which of the following is not true about attributable risk?
a. Is a measure of risk in cohort studies
b. The rate of disease among exposed persons that can be directly attributable to specific exposure
c. Is the risk difference between exposed and non-exposed individuals
d. It a measure of association
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d. It a measure of association

Explanation: Attributable Risk (Risk Difference) is technically considered a measure of impact or effect, quantifying the excess risk. Relative Risk and Odds Ratio are the standard measures of association (strength of the relationship).

20.
The ratio of the number of ways an event can occur to the number of events cannot occur is called?
a. Relative risk
b. Odds of an event
c. Odds ratio
d. Relative odds
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b. Odds of an event

Explanation: The odds of an event are defined as the probability of the event occurring divided by the probability of it not occurring (P / 1-P), which matches the description "ratio of ways it can occur to ways it cannot".

Section 2: Short Answer Questions (30 Marks)

1.
Name five components in the definition of epidemiology (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Study: Systematic data collection and analysis (scientific discipline).
  2. Distribution: Frequency (counts) and pattern (person, place, time).
  3. Determinants: Causes and risk factors affecting health.
  4. Health-related states or events: Diseases, injuries, and other health conditions.
  5. Populations: Focus is on groups of people, not individuals.
  6. Application: Use of this knowledge to control health problems.
2.
Three terms are used to describe infectious diseases according to the various outcomes that may occur after exposure to its causative agent. State them (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

After exposure to a causative agent, the outcomes can be classified as:

  1. Infection (Subclinical/Inapparent): The agent enters and multiplies but produces no symptoms (colonization).
  2. Clinical Disease (Symptomatic): The agent causes apparent signs and symptoms of illness.
  3. Carrier State: The host harbors the agent and can transmit it but shows no symptoms (can be chronic, convalescent, or healthy carrier).

Note: Alternatively, in terms of community spread, terms like Endemic, Epidemic, and Pandemic are used, but the question refers to outcomes after exposure.

3.
Briefly explain how epidemiology is used in the evaluation of efficacy and effectiveness of therapeutic and preventive interventions (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

Epidemiology provides the methodology and study designs to assess interventions:

  • Efficacy Evaluation: Using Randomized Controlled Trials (RCTs) to determine if an intervention (drug/vaccine) works under ideal, controlled conditions.
  • Effectiveness Evaluation: Using observational studies (cohort, case-control) or pragmatic trials to determine if the intervention works in "real-world" conditions when applied to the general population.
  • Monitoring: Tracking health outcomes before and after implementation to measure impact.
4.
Epidemiologists and clinicians approach the same patient differently, explain (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Clinician: Focuses on the individual. Their goal is diagnosis, treatment, and care of the specific sick person. They ask "What is wrong with this patient?"
  • Epidemiologist: Focuses on the population. Their goal is prevention and control of disease in the community. They ask "Why is this patient sick relative to others? What are the sources and risk factors?" They view the individual as a data point in the larger distribution pattern.
5.
State the two major classifications of causes of disease (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

Causes are often classified in epidemiology as:

  1. Necessary Cause: A factor that must be present for the disease to occur (e.g., Mycobacterium tuberculosis for TB).
  2. Sufficient Cause: A set of minimal conditions or factors that inevitably produce the disease (usually a combination of risk factors).

Alternative Answer: Primary (Agent) and Secondary (Risk Factors) causes.

6.
Differentiate between holo-endemic and hyperendemic (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Hyperendemic: A disease that is constantly present at a high incidence and/or prevalence rate and affects all age groups equally.
  • Holo-endemic: A disease that is highly prevalent in the population, but mostly affects children. Adults acquire immunity and show lower prevalence/symptoms (e.g., Malaria in some regions).

Section 3: Long Answer Question (20 Marks)

Q1.

a. Explain the design of case control study using smoking and lung cancer as examples (10 Marks)

b. What are interventional studies (5 Marks)

c. Name three advantages of interventional studies (3 Marks)

d. List two disadvantages of interventional studies (2 Marks)

Show Answer

Answer:

a. Case Control Study Design (Smoking & Lung Cancer)

  1. Selection of Cases: Identify a group of patients who have been diagnosed with Lung Cancer (the outcome).
  2. Selection of Controls: Identify a group of individuals who do NOT have Lung Cancer, but are similar to the cases in other respects (age, gender) and come from the same population.
  3. Retrospective Assessment: Look back in time for both groups to determine their history of Smoking (the exposure).
  4. Analysis: Compare the frequency of smoking in the case group versus the control group.
  5. Calculation: Calculate the Odds Ratio (OR) to determine the strength of association between smoking and lung cancer.

b. Interventional Studies

Interventional (experimental) studies are epidemiological study designs where the investigator actively manipulates the exposure. The researcher assigns subjects to either an exposed group (intervention/treatment) or an unexposed group (control/placebo) to observe the outcome. The most common type is the Randomized Controlled Trial (RCT).

c. Advantages of Interventional Studies

  1. Provide the strongest evidence for causality (cause-effect relationship).
  2. The investigator has control over the exposure levels and variables.
  3. Randomization minimizes confounding bias.

d. Disadvantages of Interventional Studies

  1. Expensive and time-consuming to conduct.
  2. Ethical constraints (cannot expose subjects to harmful agents).
  3. May have limited generalizability if study conditions are too artificial.
