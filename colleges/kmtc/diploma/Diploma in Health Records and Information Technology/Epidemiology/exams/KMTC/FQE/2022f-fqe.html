Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions

40 Marks
1.
The bills of mortality quantified patterns of diseases and death in the population also analyzed reports of births and deaths were published by?
a) William Farr
b) John Snow
c) John Grant
d) Edward Jenner
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) John Grant (Likely referencing John Graunt)

Explanation: John Graunt (1620-1674) was a pioneer in vital statistics who analyzed the "Bills of Mortality" in London, quantifying patterns of birth and death.

2.
---- Epidemiology focuses on the determinants of disease by testing hypothesis
a) Descriptive
b) Correlation
c) Observational
d) Analytic
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Analytic

Explanation: Analytic epidemiology is concerned with the "Why" and "How" (determinants) and involves testing hypotheses using comparison groups (e.g., Case-Control, Cohort).

3.
Which of the following is not a type of descriptive studies?
a) Case reports
b) Case series
c) Observational
d) Cross sectional surveys
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Observational

Explanation: "Observational" is a broad category that includes both Descriptive and Analytic studies. It is not a specific type of descriptive study like Case reports, Case series, or Cross-sectional surveys.

4.
What type of a bias arises when individuals with a particular exposure of adverse outcome is likely to remember their experiences differently from those who are not similarly affected
a) Selection bias
b) Recall bias
c) Information bias
d) Surrogate bias
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Recall bias

Explanation: Recall bias occurs when cases (those with the outcome) remember or report past exposures differently (usually more accurately or with exaggeration) than controls.

5.
Which is basic measure of disease frequency in human population?
a) Simple count
b) Rate
c) Ration
d) Proportion
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Simple count (or Rate/Proportion depending on context)

Explanation: The simplest/most basic measure is a Count (number of cases). However, for comparing populations, Rates and Proportions are used. In many basic epidemiology contexts, "Count" is the starting point.

6.
---- quantifies the proportion of individuals in a population who have a disease at a specific instance and provides an estimate of the probability that an individual will be ill at a point in time
a) Attack rate
b) Prevalence
c) Incidence
d) Disease frequency
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Prevalence (Point Prevalence)

Explanation: Prevalence measures the status of disease at a specific point in time ("Do you have the disease now?"). It estimates the probability of currently being ill.

7.
Non clinical disease can be classified into the following except
a) Latent
b) Sub-clinical
c) Semi-clinical
d) Pre-clinical
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Semi-clinical

Explanation: Standard stages of non-clinical disease are Pre-clinical (not yet apparent), Sub-clinical (asymptomatic but detectable), and Latent (hidden/inactive). "Semi-clinical" is not a standard epidemiological term.

8.
The number of people at risk in whom a certain illness develops compared to the total number of people at risk is called?
a) Cumulative incidence
b) Attack rate
c) Period prevalence
d) Point prevalence
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Cumulative incidence (or b Attack Rate)

Explanation: The definition "New cases / Population at risk" is Cumulative Incidence. An Attack Rate is a specific type of cumulative incidence used for outbreaks. Given both options, Cumulative Incidence is the general term, but if the context is an outbreak, Attack Rate applies. However, option A is the formal definition.

9.
Which one of the following is a characteristic of a single exposure outbreak?
a) Frequent secondary cases
b) Increasing severity with increasing age
c) Explosive
d) Cases include both people who have been exposed and those who were not exposed
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Explosive

Explanation: A single exposure (point source) outbreak typically shows an explosive (sharp/sudden) rise in cases as everyone is exposed at the same time.

10.
The type of surveillance in which project staff are recruited to carry out is called?
a) Active
b) Passive
c) Sentinel
d) Syndromic
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Active

Explanation: In Active Surveillance, dedicated staff actively visit facilities to find and record cases, rather than waiting for reports.

11.
Prevalence can be decreased by the following except
a) Death
b) Cure
c) Reduced incidence
d) Increased incidence
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Increased incidence

Explanation: Increased incidence adds more cases to the pool, thereby increasing prevalence. Death, Cure, and Reduced incidence all lower prevalence.

12.
Which one of the following is not a limitation of hospital data?
a) Hospital admissions are selective
b) Cheap and easy to collect
c) They’re not designed for research
d) Population at risk is generally not defined
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Cheap and easy to collect

Explanation: Being "Cheap and easy to collect" is an advantage (strength), not a limitation. Options A, C, and D are valid limitations of hospital data.

13.
The examining of geographic or spatial differences in incidence can be done in a form of?
a) Epidemiologic studies
b) Time series graph
c) Spot map
d) Histogram
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Spot map

Explanation: Spot maps are used to display the spatial/geographic distribution of disease cases. Time series (B) and Histograms (D) show time or frequency distributions.

14.
The relationship between incidence and prevalence is demonstrated through one of the Following
a) Exposure
b) Time
c) No of cases
d) Rate
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Time (Duration)

Explanation: The formula is $P \approx I \times D$ (Prevalence = Incidence × Duration). Duration is a function of Time.

15.
Which one of the following is an advantage of active surveillance?
a) Requires less project staff
b) Is relatively inexpensive to employ
c) More accurate due to reduced reporting burden for health care providers
d) Reporting systems can be developed quickly
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) More accurate due to reduced reporting burden for health care providers

Explanation: Active surveillance is more accurate/complete because dedicated staff do the work, reducing the burden on clinical providers who might otherwise forget to report. Options A, B, and D are advantages of Passive surveillance.

16.
------ is the number of individuals dying during a specific period of time after disease onset divided by the number of individuals with the specified disease multiplied by 100
a) Disease specific fatality rate
b) Case fatality rate
c) Secondary attack rate
d) Primary attack rate
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Case fatality rate

Explanation: CFR = (Deaths from disease / Cases of disease) × 100. It measures severity/virulence.

17.
For a disease like pancreatic cancer, which is highly fatal and of short duration, one of the following statements is true
a) Incidence rates and mortality rates will be similar
b) Mortality rates will be much higher than incidence rates
c) Incidence rates will be much higher that mortality rates
d) Incidences rates will be unrelated to mortality rate
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Incidence rates and mortality rates will be similar

Explanation: For highly fatal diseases with short survival, almost everyone who gets it (incidence) dies (mortality). Thus, Incidence ≈ Mortality.

18.
The ability of a test to distinguish between those who have a disease and those who do not have a disease is called
a) Positive predictive value
b) Specificity
c) Reliability
d) Validity
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Validity

Explanation: Validity (Accuracy) is the general ability of a test to measure what it purports to measure (i.e., distinguish diseased from non-diseased). Sensitivity and Specificity are the two components of validity.

19.
The variation between those reading the test results is called
a) Intra observer variation
b) Intra subject variation
c) Inter observer variation
d) Inter subject variation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Inter observer variation

Explanation: Inter-observer variation is the difference in interpretation between two or more different observers/readers.

20.
Which one of the following does not happen in clinical phase of a disease?
a) Signs and symptoms of disease
b) Pathologic evidence of disease if sought
c) Seeking of medical care
d) Diagnosis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Pathologic evidence of disease if sought (This occurs in sub-clinical too)

Explanation: This is a tricky question. A, C, and D are definitive features of the Clinical Phase. Pathologic evidence (B) exists in the Sub-clinical/Pre-clinical phase as well (before symptoms). Therefore, B is the one that is not exclusive to the clinical phase or happens earlier.

21.
Which one of the following is not s purpose of randomized clinical trial?
a) Evaluation of new drugs and treatment
b) Testing of new health and mediocre technology
c) Assess new program for screening
d) Planning of health care services and allocation of resources
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Planning of health care services and allocation of resources

Explanation: RCTs are for evaluation of efficacy (A, B, C). Planning and allocation (D) rely on descriptive epidemiology (distribution, burden of disease), not clinical trials.

22.
------ is an inert substance that looks, tastes and smells like the active agent
a) Therapeutic
b) Masker
c) Blinder
d) Placebo
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Placebo

Explanation: A placebo is a dummy treatment used in control groups to blind participants/investigators.

23.
In clinical trials, phase two trials consist of how many subjects
a) 20 – 80
b) 1000 – 3000
c) 100 – 300
d) Over 3000
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) 100 – 300

Explanation: - Phase I: 20-80 (Safety). - Phase II: 100-300 (Efficacy). - Phase III: 1000-3000 (Confirmation). - Phase IV: Post-marketing (Thousands).

24.
Another term for group matching in case control studies
a) Frequency matching
b) Pair matching
c) Matched groups
d) Individual group matching
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Frequency matching

Explanation: Frequency matching involves selecting controls so that the proportion of controls with a characteristic (e.g., gender) matches the proportion of cases with that characteristic. Pair matching (B) is individual matching.

25.
In -----studies the controls are a sample of individuals who are at risk of the disease at the time each case of the disease develops
a) Case control
b) Nested case control
c) Prospective control studies
d) Multiple control studies
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Nested case control

Explanation: A Nested Case-Control study is conducted within a cohort. Controls are selected from those at risk at the time a case occurs (Incidence Density Sampling).

26.
Which one of the following is a good estimate of relative risk from case control studies?
a) Kapps statistics
b) Odds ratio
c) Rate adjustment
d) Epilogue
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Odds ratio

Explanation: In case-control studies (where incidence cannot be calculated), the Odds Ratio is used as an estimate of the Relative Risk (especially for rare diseases).

28.

29.
Which one of the following is not a type of information bias?

b) Surveillance bias
c) Reporting bias
d) Recall bias
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Selection bias

Explanation: Bias is broadly classified into Selection Bias and Information Bias. Recall (D), Reporting (C), and Surveillance (B) are types of Information Bias. Selection Bias is a separate category.

Explanation: Efficacy, Effectiveness, and Efficiency are the "3 Es" of health service evaluation. Effect modification (Interaction) is an analytical concept in epidemiology, not a service evaluation metric.

38.
---- must be associated with both exposure and outcome
a) External validity
b) Internal validity
c) Modifier
d) Confounder
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Confounder

Explanation: A confounding variable must be associated with the exposure AND be a risk factor for the outcome (disease).

39.
----- alters the nature of the true relationship between exposure and an outcome
a) Synergism
b) Confounder
c) Bias
d) Modifier
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Modifier (Effect Modifier)

Explanation: Effect Modification (Interaction) occurs when the magnitude of the effect of an exposure depends on the level of a third variable (the modifier). It alters the nature/strength of the relationship. Confounding (B) distorts/masks the true relationship, while Modification is part of the true nature.

Section 2: Short Answer Questions

40 Marks
1.
State three tools used when characterizing an epidemic (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Epidemic Curve: A histogram showing the number of cases over time. Helps determine the type of outbreak (point source vs propagated).
  2. Spot Map: A map showing the geographic location of cases to identify clustering.
  3. Attack Rate Table (Cross-tabulation): Analysis of cases by person characteristics (age, sex) or exposure (food items) to identify risk groups.
2.
Write short notes on vehicle mode of transmission (5 marks)
Show Answer
  • Definition: Transmission of disease via a non-living intermediary (vehicle) that conveys the infectious agent from its reservoir to a susceptible host.
  • Examples of Vehicles: Food (Salmonella), Water (Cholera), Biological products (Blood/Serum), Fomites (bedding, surgical instruments).
  • Mechanism: The agent may multiply or develop in the vehicle (e.g., bacteria in food) or simply be carried passively.
  • Control: Sanitation, food safety, sterilization.
3.
Epidemiology is usually concerned with health related states/events. Explain (5 marks)
Show Answer
  • Epidemiology studies not just "disease" (pathology), but a broad range of health phenomena.
  • Health States: Includes physiological conditions (e.g., blood pressure, pregnancy), mental health, and positive health indicators.
  • Events: Includes injuries, accidents, violence, suicides, and vital events (births, deaths).
  • Behaviors: Includes smoking, diet, physical activity, and use of health services.
  • This broad scope allows epidemiology to address the full spectrum of public health issues.

5.
Between the year 2019 and 2021, the average annual incidence rate of lung cancer in Country X, was 45.9 per 100,000 and the annual prevalence rate was 23 per 100,000.
a) Calculate the average duration of lung cancer (5 marks)
Show Answer

Formula: $Prevalence \approx Incidence \times Duration$ ($P = I \times D$)

Given:

  • Incidence ($I$) = 45.9 per 100,000 per year
  • Prevalence ($P$) = 23 per 100,000

Calculation:

$$ 23 = 45.9 \times D $$

$$ D = \frac{23}{45.9} $$

$$ D \approx 0.501 \text{ years} $$

Answer: The average duration is approximately 0.5 years (or 6 months). This suggests lung cancer is highly fatal or cures are very rapid (unlikely for cancer, so it implies high fatality).

6.
The population of the city of Atlantis on March 30th 2012 was 183,000. The number of new active cases of TB occurring between January 1st to June 30th, 2012 was 26. Number of active cases TB cases according to the city register on June 30 2012 was 264.
i. Calculate the incidence rate of active TB cases for the 6 months' period
ii. Calculate the prevalence rate of active TB as of June 2012 (5 marks)
Show Answer

i. Incidence Rate (Jan 1 - June 30):

  • Numerator (New Cases) = 26
  • Denominator (Population) = 183,000
  • $Rate = \frac{26}{183,000} \times 100,000 = \mathbf{14.2} \text{ per 100,000 per 6 months}$.

ii. Prevalence Rate (Point Prevalence, June 30):

  • Numerator (Existing Cases) = 264
  • Denominator (Population) = 183,000
  • $Rate = \frac{264}{183,000} \times 100,000 = \mathbf{144.3} \text{ per 100,000}$.
7.
In an Asian country with a population of 6 million people, 60,000 deaths occurred during the year ending 31st December 2010. These included 30,000 deaths from cholera in 100,000 people who were sick with cholera required;
i. What is the cause specific mortality rate of cholera in 2010
ii. What is the case fatality rate from cholera in 2010 (5 marks)
Show Answer

i. Cause Specific Mortality Rate (Cholera):

Formula: (Deaths from Cause / Total Population) × Multiplier

$$ = \frac{30,000}{6,000,000} \times 1,000 $$

$$ = 0.005 \times 1,000 = \mathbf{5} \text{ deaths per 1,000 population} $$

ii. Case Fatality Rate (Cholera):

Formula: (Deaths from Disease / Cases of Disease) × 100

$$ = \frac{30,000}{100,000} \times 100 $$

$$ = 0.3 \times 100 = \mathbf{30\%} $$

8.
Differentiate between selection and information bias on epidemiological studies (5 marks)
Show Answer
  • Selection Bias: Occurs during the recruitment/selection of study participants. It happens when the study population does not represent the target population (e.g., Non-response bias, Healthy worker effect). It distorts the relationship between exposure and outcome because of who is in the study.
  • Information Bias (Measurement Bias): Occurs during the data collection phase. It involves systematic errors in measuring exposure or outcome (e.g., Recall bias, Interviewer bias). It distorts the relationship because the data collected is inaccurate.

2.
Explain 5 methods that are being employed to interrupt the transmission of COVID – 19 In Kenya. (10 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Hand Hygiene: Frequent washing with soap and water or alcohol-based sanitizer to kill the virus on hands (Mode of transmission: Contact).
  2. Respiratory Hygiene/Masking: Wearing face masks to contain droplets from infected persons and protect susceptible persons (Mode: Droplet).
  3. Social Distancing: Maintaining physical distance (1.5 - 2 meters) to prevent droplet transmission.
  4. Vaccination: Administering vaccines (e.g., AstraZeneca, Pfizer) to build herd immunity and reduce susceptibility.
  5. Quarantine and Isolation:
    • Isolation: Separating sick (positive) people from healthy ones.
    • Quarantine: Restricting movement of exposed/suspected contacts.
