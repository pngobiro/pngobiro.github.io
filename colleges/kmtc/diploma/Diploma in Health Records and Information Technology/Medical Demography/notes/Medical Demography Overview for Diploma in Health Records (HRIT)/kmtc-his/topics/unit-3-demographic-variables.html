UNIT 3: DEMOGRAPHIC VARIABLES AND MEASURES

Measures of fertility

Fertility and Fecundity

Fertility relates to the number of live births a woman has actually had. Fertile means that a woman is or was a mother.

The physiological ability to bear children on the other hand is known as fecundity. Fecund is thus the opposite of sterile. It is thought that the maximum number of children an average woman can theoretically produce is about 15 if she starts child bearing as soon as possible after menarche, which occurs around ages 12-14 and continues until menopause in her middle or late forties.

Child Woman Ratio

This can be defined as:

$$ \text{C/W Ratio} = \frac{\text{Children Aged 0-4}}{\text{Woman aged 15-44/9}} $$

The usefulness of ratio arises from the fact that it requires only information on the age and sex structure of the population. No data on birth are needed at all. This makes it particularly useful when using census data.

Obviously it is an extremely crude measure but broadly if fertility is high, the child/woman ratio will be high while if fertility is low, the ratio will be low. It is however, quite sensitive to the level of infant mortality. So if it is dangerous to use the ratio to compare populations, with substantially different levels of infant and child mortality or where under reporting of young children is a problem.

Crude Birth Rate

The crude birth rate (CBR or b) in a population during a given year is defined as the total number of births (B) divided by the midyear population (P). It is normally expressed per thousand population, so we have $b=1000*(B/P)$.

This is the simplest and most frequently used as a measure of fertility. It is defined as: -

$$ \text{CBR} = \frac{\text{Birth in year}}{\text{Population at Midyear}} \times 1000 $$

It is always expressed per 1000 population. The reasons it is a "crude" rate is that it includes all ages and both sexes in the denominator.

No attempt is made to relate the births to the women at risk of having those births because of this it is strictly not a measure of fertility at all.

The range of CBR is from 10-50. A major problem is that it is affected very greatly by the composition of the population as regards age, sex and other characteristics. Thus it can be very misleading if it is used to compare different populations or the same population at different times because it may vary greatly in their composition. There are though 3 reasons why it is a useful measure: -

  • It is easy to understand
  • It requires few data and it is easy to calculate - all that is required is the total number of births and the total population.
  • It is possible to subtract the crude death rate from the crude birth rate to get the crude rate of natural increase, which along with the crude net migration rate can estimate, the present population. This refers to demographic equation.

General Fertility Rate (GFR)

This is a fertility rate rather than a birth rate because it expresses the births relative to the No. of women of the reproductive age. Sometimes the denominator includes women aged 45-49. Sometimes it doesn't. This is important because compiling a 15-44 GFR with 15-49 GFR could be misleading.

GFR of age 15-44/9

$$ \text{GFR} = \frac{\text{No. of Births during the year}}{\text{Women aged 15-44/9 at midyear}} \times 1000 $$

Example: GFR of age 15-49 = $3,253,480 \times 1000 / 13,224,700 = 246.02 / 1000$

The possible value is roughly 50-300. Note that more data are required to calculate the GFR than CBR. One has to know the age and sex composition of the population. The main advantage of GFR over CBR is that it substantially controls for age and sex structure by relating the births to roughly the women at risk of having them, but it does not control entirely for age structure since there may be substantial variations between populations within the reproductive age range.

Age-specific fertility rate (ASFR)

The age-specific fertility rate (ASFR) for women of age group i in a population during a given year is defined as the total number of births to women age i during the year (Bi) divided by the total (midyear) number of women age i (Wi). Expressed per thousand, we have

$$ \text{ASFRI} = 1000 * (\text{Bi} / \text{Wi}) $$

Total Fertility Rate (TFR)

It is probably the measure of fertility that is most widely used by demographers. It is calculated by adding up the ASFRs. Each ASFR usually relates to 5 years. It is therefore necessary to multiply the 5 -year rate by five. The TFR is almost always expressed per one (1) woman where as ASFRs are often expressed per 1000 women. If that is the case then it is necessary to divide by 1000.

$$ \text{TFR} = \frac{\text{Sum of ASFR} \times 5}{1000} \text{ or } \frac{\sum \text{ASFR} \times 5}{1000} $$

Example Bangladesh 1974:
TFR = $((200.4+337.3+310.9+261.5+197.0+95.4+13.5) \times 5) / 1000 = 7.08$

The great value of the TFR is that it is single figure measure that is independent of age structure. Formally, the TFR can be thought of as the number of children a woman would have if:

  • She survived to age 50.
  • Throughout her reproductive life she experienced exact ASFR for the year in question.

General marital fertility rate (GMFR)

$$ \text{GMFR} = \frac{\text{All Births}}{\text{Married women 15-44/49}} \times 1000 $$

Family Planning

Family planning allows people to attain their desired number of children and determine the spacing of pregnancies. It is achieved through use of contraceptive methods and the treatment of infertility.

Key facts

  • 214 million women of reproductive age in developing countries who want to avoid pregnancy are not using a modern contraceptive method.
  • Some family planning methods, such as condoms, help prevent the transmission of HIV and other sexually transmitted infections.
  • Family planning / contraception reduces the need for abortion, especially unsafe abortion.
  • Family planning reinforces people's rights to determine the number and spacing of their children.
  • By preventing unintended pregnancy, family planning /contraception prevents deaths of mothers and children.

Contraceptive methods

Modern methods

  • Combined oral contraceptives (COCs) or "the pill": It contains two hormones (estrogen and progestogen). It is more than 99% with correct and consistent use but only 92% as commonly used.
  • Progestogen-only pills (POPs) or "the minipill": Contains only progestogen hormone, not estrogen. It works by thickening cervical mucous.
  • Implants: They are small, flexible rods or capsules placed under the skin of the upper arm.
  • Progestogen only injectables: It is injected into the muscle or under the skin every 2 or 3 months.
  • Intrauterine device (IUD): copper containing: It is small flexible plastic device containing copper sleeves or wire that is inserted into the uterus.
  • Male condoms: Sheaths or coverings that fit over a man's erect penis.
  • Male sterilization (vasectomy): Permanent contraception to block or cut the vas deferens tubes that carry sperm from the testicles.
  • Female sterilization (tubal ligation): Permanent contraception to block or cut the fallopian tubes.
  • Emergency contraception pills: Pills taken to prevent pregnancy up to 5 days after unprotected sex.

Traditional methods

  • Lactational amenorrhea method (LAM): Temporary contraception for new mothers whose monthly bleeding has not returned; requires exclusive or full breastfeeding.
  • Standard Days Method or SDM: Women track their fertile periods (usually days 8 to 19 of each 26 to 32 day cycle).
  • Basal Body Temperature (BBT) Method: Woman takes her body temperature at the same time each morning before getting out of bed observing for an increase of 0.2 to 0.5 degrees C.
  • Withdrawal (coitus interruptus): Man withdraws his penis from his partner's vagina, and ejaculates outside the vagina.

MORTALITY

The acceleration of population growth in the developing countries of the world has not been due to a change in the birth rates; but to the great reduction in death rate following improvements life.

Measurements of Mortality

Crude death rate (CDR)

The crude death rate (cdr or d) in a population during a given year is defined as the total number of deaths (D) divided by the midyear population (P). It is normally expressed per thousand population, so we have $d = 1000 * (D/P)$.

This is the simplest measure, indicating the number of deaths in a particular year per 1,000 of the population.

$$ \text{CDR} = \frac{D}{P} \times 1000 $$

Age-Specific Death Rate (ASDR)

The age-specific death rate (asdr) for individuals age i in a population during a given year is defined as the total number of deaths to people age i during the year (Di) divided by the total (midyear) population age i (Pi). Expressed per thousand, we have

$$ \text{ASDRI} = 1000 * (\text{Di} / \text{Pi}) $$

Note that the crude death rate is simply a weighted average of age-specific death rates.

Infant mortality rate (IMR)

An aspect of mortality that receives special consideration from demographers is infant mortality. This is due to at least 2 reasons:

  1. Few, if any, human experiences are more tragic or emotionally devastating as the death of an infant child
  2. The death rate in the first year of life is much higher than in the succeeding several decades

Infant mortality rate- is the most common measure of infant death and is the number of deaths in a year to children under age 1 per 1,000 babies born in the year. It is expressed as:

$$ \text{IMR} = \frac{\text{Deaths under 1 year}}{\text{Live births}} \times 1000 $$

STANDARDIZATION

A legitimate desire is often felt for a single mortality rate, summing up the rates to be made between one rate and another. For this purpose, standardized death rate is generally used. This is a hypothetical figure which indicates what the crude death rate would be if the population being studied had the same age and sex composition as a population (any real or assumed population).

There are two common methods of deriving standardized rates, known as direct and indirect standardization.

Direct Method of the Standardization

In order to get over the problem of different population structure of the two populations to be compared, we choose a standard comparison. This we call the standard. The population to be standardized we call it the index population. We then apply the age (or characteristics) specific rates of the index population to the standard population.

Steps:

  1. Define the standard population
  2. Apply the Age Specific Death Rates for the index population to the numbers in each age group of the standard population to obtain the deaths we would expect in the population if the index rates applied.
  3. Add these expected deaths over the age groups to obtain the total number of the expected deaths.
  4. The crude rate obtained by dividing the total expected deaths by the total in the standard population; gives the standardized death rate for the index population.

Indirect standardization

The direct method of standardization uses the population of each age group as the standard. The indirect method of standardization uses the age specific rates as the standard. In detail, the procedure is as follows:

  1. Define the standard age specific mortality rates.
  2. Apply the standard age specific rates to the index population to get the expected deaths in each age group.
  3. Add the expected deaths of all the age groups to get the sum of the expected deaths. Divide the total observed deaths (real) by the total of the expected deaths to get the standardized mortality ratio
  4. Multiply the standardized mortality ratio by the crude death rate of the standard population.

LIFE TABLE

A life table is a life history of a hypothetical group or cohort of people as it is diminished gradually by deaths.

Assumptions of Life Table:

A life table is based on the following assumptions:

  1. A hypothetical cohort of life table usually comprises of 1,000 or 10,000 or 1,00,000 births.
  2. The deaths are equally distributed throughout the year.
  3. The cohort of people diminish gradually by death only.
  4. The cohort is closed to the in-migration and out-migration.
  5. The death rate is related to a pre-determined age specific death rate.
  6. The cohort of persons die at a fixed age which does not change.
  7. There is no change in death rates overtime.
  8. The cohort of life tables are generally constructed separately for males and females.

Age Misreporting

Distortion in age distribution may be real and is caused by past changes in the levels or patterns of mortality, fertility or migration or they may be due to errors. The errors may be caused either by some individuals being incorrectly omitted from a survey or by ones individuals ages being wrongly reported.

Age Heaping

Age heaping or digit preference as it is often called. Too many people report ages ending with 0 or 5 while relatively few people give ages ending in 9, 1, 4 or 6. For example, if in this group ones age is 22, the individual will report this age as 20 years or 25 years.
