Section 2: Short Answer Questions

40 Marks
41

Briefly define the following terms as used in demography (5 marks):

  1. Structure
  2. Size
  3. Nuptiality
  4. Change
  5. Distribution
Show Answers
  1. Structure: The composition of the population in terms of age and sex groups.
  2. Size: The total number of individuals residing in a defined territory.
  3. Nuptiality: The frequency, characteristics, and dissolution of marriages in a population.
  4. Change: The increase or decrease in population size/structure due to fertility, mortality, and migration.
  5. Distribution: The arrangement of the population in space (geographical density) at a given time.
45

Give the meaning of the following abbreviations as used in a life table (5 marks):

i) dx   ii) px   iii) nmx   iv) nqx   v) nPx

Show Answers
  • i) dx: Number of deaths between age x and x+1.
  • ii) px: Probability of surviving from age x to age x+1.
  • iii) nmx: Age-specific death rate between age x and x+n.
  • iv) nqx: Probability of dying between age x and x+n.
  • v) nPx: Total person-years lived (Population) in the interval x to x+n (in stationary population).

Section 3: Long Answer Question

20 Marks
49

Citing relevant examples, exhaustively explain the importance of learning demography. (20 marks)

View Model Answer

1. Health Planning and Resource Allocation:
Demography provides data on population size and structure, allowing governments to plan for hospitals, staff, and medicine. For example, a high birth rate indicates a need for more maternal and child health clinics.

2. Epidemiology and Disease Control:
Understanding the "denominator" (population at risk) is crucial for calculating morbidity and mortality rates. Demographic data helps track disease outbreaks and target interventions (e.g., vaccination campaigns for under-5s).

3. Policy Formulation:
Governments use demographic data to create population policies (e.g., family planning initiatives in Kenya to manage high fertility rates or social security planning for aging populations).

4. Economic Planning:
Knowing the dependency ratio (young/old vs working age) helps in planning for employment, education, and housing. A "youth bulge" requires investment in job creation.

5. Understanding Social Dynamics:
Migration patterns (urbanization) affect housing and sanitation. Demography helps predict future needs in urban centers versus rural areas.
