Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions

40 Marks
1.
The following statements are true about an epidemiologist except:
a) One who studies the etiology, diagnosis and prevention of disease in a community
b) One who uses a thermometer and stethoscope to draw inferences and conclusion about an individual's disease
c) Measures the frequency of disease in a specified population
d) Counts cases of disease in relation to census of the population at risk
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) One who uses a thermometer and stethoscope to draw inferences and conclusion about an individual's disease

Explanation: This describes a clinician (doctor/nurse), whose focus is on individual patient care. An epidemiologist focuses on populations, patterns, and causes of disease in communities (Options A, C, and D).

2.
Which of the following information is NOT included when calculating prevalence?
a) New cases occurring within a time period are included
b) All persons with the disease that have carried over previous time period
c) All people who recovered from a particular disease in the previous period
d) Recurrences during a succeeding time period (one year) are included
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) All people who recovered from a particular disease in the previous period

Explanation: Prevalence measures the burden of existing disease at a specific time. People who have recovered are no longer "cases" and are therefore removed from the prevalence pool (numerator).

3.
Which of the following is not a property of proportions in epidemiology?
a) Proportions are the most descriptive measures used in epidemiology
b) They are used to describe the amount of disease that can be attributed to a particular exposure
c) When calculating proportion, the numerator is included to the denominator
d) Proportion cannot be expressed in a fraction or decimal
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Proportion cannot be expressed in a fraction or decimal

Explanation: Proportions CAN and usually are expressed as fractions, decimals (0.0 - 1.0), or percentages. Option C is the defining characteristic of a proportion (numerator is part of the denominator).

4.
The following are examples of individual based descriptive study, which one is not?
a) Ecological/correlational studies
b) Case series
c) Cross-sectional studies
d) Longitudinal studies
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Ecological/correlational studies

Explanation: Ecological studies differ from other descriptive studies because the unit of analysis is populations or groups (e.g., countries, schools), not individuals.

5.
Which of the following statements is not true about cohort studies?
a) They are suitable for common diseases
b) They are suitable for rare diseases
c) In cohort studies differential misclassification of exposure is less likely to occur
d) Can be used to evaluate multiple health effects of one or more exposures
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) They are suitable for rare diseases

Explanation: Cohort studies are generally inefficient and expensive for investigating rare diseases because you would need a massive sample size to observe enough cases. Case-control studies are preferred for rare diseases.

6.
Which of the following is not a determinant prevalence?
a) Cure rate
b) Period
c) Fatality rate
d) Population growth rate
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Period

Explanation: Determinants of prevalence include incidence and duration ($P \approx I \times D$). Cure rate (A) and Fatality rate (C) affect duration (how long someone stays in the "prevalent" pool). Population growth (D) affects the denominator. "Period" describes the type of prevalence (point vs. period) rather than a biological or demographic factor driving the rate.

7.
The following statements are true about relative risk except:
a) It shows ones risk of developing a disease over a time period
b) It measures the strength of association between a risk of exposure and an outcome
c) It requires knowledge of all those exposed and un-exposed
d) It measures the risk of developing a disease relative to the exposure
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) It shows ones risk of developing a disease over a time period

Explanation: Option A describes Incidence (Absolute Risk), not Relative Risk. Relative Risk is a ratio comparing incidence in the exposed group to incidence in the unexposed group (as described in B and D).

8.
Which of the following is not an ingredient of a good disease surveillance system?
a) Good feedback and rapid response
b) Basic and sound knowledge of epidemiology
c) Availability of clear case definition
d) An effective process of identifying the diagnosis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) An effective process of identifying the diagnosis

Explanation: While diagnosis is important, surveillance systems often rely on syndromic reporting or case definitions (Option C) rather than definitive clinical diagnosis for every case, especially in early warning systems. A, B, and C are core components of the system itself.

9.
The following are sources of surveillance data except:
a) Laboratory reports of infections
b) Reports from investigation of outbreaks
c) Active surveillance for all diseases
d) Mortality reports from health care professionals describing individual cases
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Active surveillance for all diseases

Explanation: "Active surveillance" is a method of data collection, not a "source" of data like a lab report or mortality report. Furthermore, active surveillance is rarely done for "all" diseases due to cost; it is targeted.

10.
Which of the following cannot be a route disease infection?
a) Congenital
b) Urogenital
c) Skin
d) Gastro-intestinal
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Congenital

Explanation: This is a trick question regarding terminology. Congenital refers to a condition present at birth. While infections can be transplacental (vertical transmission), "Congenital" is usually the outcome or timing, whereas Urogenital, Skin, and GI are anatomical portals of entry/routes.

11.
All of the following are true about odds ratio except:
a) It is an estimate of relative risk
b) It is only a measure of risk and can be obtained directly from a case control study
c) It can be calculated without data on the rates (as in a case control study)
d) It is a ratio of incidence in exposed divided by incidence in unexposed
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) It is a ratio of incidence in exposed divided by incidence in unexposed

Explanation: Option D defines Relative Risk. The Odds Ratio (OR) compares the odds of exposure in cases vs. controls. OR is used in Case-Control studies because you cannot calculate incidence directly.

12.
What do we call the mode of transport of an infectious agent through an environment to a susceptible host?
a) Carrier
b) Reservoir
c) Vector
d) Vehicle
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Vehicle

Explanation: A vehicle is a non-living intermediary (like food, water, air, fomites) that conveys the infectious agent. A vector (C) is usually a living organism (mosquito). A carrier (A) is an infected person. Note: The phrasing "through an environment" strongly suggests Vehicle (e.g., air/water).

13.
A longitudinal or prospective study is also known as:
a) Ecological study
b) Cross-sectional study
c) Cohort study
d) Observational study
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Cohort study

Explanation: Cohort studies follow a group of people over time (longitudinal/prospective) to see who develops the outcome. Cross-sectional (B) is a "snapshot" at one point in time.

14.
What will be the agent when studying oral epidemiology?
a) Host
b) Environment
c) Cause of disease
d) Population
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Cause of disease

Explanation: In the Epidemiological Triad (Agent, Host, Environment), the Agent is the factor whose presence (or absence) causes the disease (e.g., bacteria, sugar intake in caries).

15.
What is the proportion that uses a standard denominator and include a time interval (1000, 10,000 or 100000 per year)?
a) Rate
b) Counts
c) Proportion
d) Index
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Rate

Explanation: A Rate differs from a proportion because it explicitly involves a time dimension (per year, per hour) in the denominator.

16.
In epidemiology the Greek word “demos” means what?
a) Native
b) Epidemiology
c) People
d) All of the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) People

Explanation: Epidemiology comes from Epi (upon), Demos (people), and Logos (study). Hence, "study of what is upon the people".

17.
Prevalence rate is useful to health care planners because it measures the following except:
a) The need for patients
b) The need for treatment
c) Health care manpower needs
d) Information important for planning health care services
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) The need for patients

Explanation: Health planners plan for resources (treatment, manpower, services). They do not plan for a "need for patients" (the goal is usually to reduce patients). Note: The phrasing of option A is awkward/distractor.

18.
Which one of the following in not a factor for secular changes of disease?
a) Economic changes
b) Successes of eradication programmes
c) Rapid urbanization
d) None of the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) None of the above

Explanation: Secular trends are long-term changes in disease patterns (over years/decades). Economic changes, eradication programs (e.g., smallpox), and urbanization are ALL major drivers of secular trends.

19.
The common outbreak of covid-19 disease can be prevented through the following except:
a) Water treatment
b) Health education
c) Case treatment
d) Quarantine
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Water treatment

Explanation: COVID-19 is a respiratory disease transmitted via droplets and aerosols. While hygiene is important, water treatment (purification) prevents water-borne diseases like Cholera, not respiratory ones like COVID-19.

20.
Which of the following is not among the principles of screening?
a) It should be acceptable
b) The cost of screening should balance the benefits
c) Knowledge on natural history of disease is not important
d) The interval for repeating the test should be determined
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Knowledge on natural history of disease is not important

Explanation: Understanding the natural history of the disease is crucial for screening (e.g., identifying the latent stage). Wilson and Jungner criteria explicitly state the natural history must be understood.

21.
In epidemiology the “BEINGS” model is useful paradigm for:
a) Allocating public health resources
b) Evaluating etiological factors
c) Assessing the impact of herd immunity
d) Determining the role of social policy on public health
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Evaluating etiological factors

Explanation: BEINGS stands for Biological, Environmental, Immunological, Nutritional, Genetic, and Social factors. It is a model used to categorize causes (etiology) of disease.

22.
Which one of the following is beyond the scope of activities undertaken by epidemiologists?
a) Analyzing and establishing modes of disease transmission
b) Preventing disease
c) Rationing health care resources
d) Providing data for genetic counselling
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Rationing health care resources

Explanation: While epidemiologists provide data that informs resource allocation (planning), the act of "rationing" (limiting access) is a policy/administrative function, not a core epidemiological activity. Options A, B, and D rely directly on epidemiological data.

23.
Which of the following is the goal of vaccination?
a) Prevent infection
b) Prevent disease
c) Prevent transmission
d) All of the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) All of the above

Explanation: Vaccines aim to prevent the individual from getting infected (A), prevent the clinical manifestation of disease (B), and reduce the spread in the community (C - Herd Immunity).

24.
An epidemic usually affecting a large proportion of the population, occurring over a wide geographical area such as a section of the nation is referred to as:
a) Sporadic
b) Endemic
c) Exotic
d) Hyper endemic
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Sporadic (This option list is missing "Epidemic" or "Pandemic" which fits best. However, reviewing context:)

Correction/Note: The definition "wide geographical area" usually implies a Pandemic or a very large Epidemic. - Sporadic: Occasional, irregular. - Endemic: Constant presence. - Hyperendemic: Consistently high levels. - Exotic: Imported. Note: Based on standard definitions, none of these options perfectly fits "wide geographical area" better than "Pandemic". However, if forced to choose based on "section of a nation" vs "global", it might refer to a large Epidemic. The question might be malformed or implying "Hyperendemic" if referring to high levels, but Hyperendemic implies *constant* presence, not an outbreak. Let's assume the question meant "Pandemic" but it's not listed. Given the options, none are strictly correct for "wide geographical area outbreak". Sporadic is the opposite. Endemic is constant.
Study Tip: - Endemic: Constant presence in a region (e.g., Malaria in Kisumu). - Epidemic: Sudden increase above normal (Outbreak). - Pandemic: Epidemic crossing international borders.

25.
Which of the following is NOT a vaccine preventable disease?
a) Measles
b) Varicella
c) Pneumonia
d) All of the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) All of the above (Wait, question asks "NOT")

Correction: This question is likely "Which of the following IS a vaccine preventable disease" or the options are confusing. - Measles: Vaccine exists (MMR). - Varicella (Chickenpox): Vaccine exists. - Pneumonia: Vaccines exist (Pneumococcal, Hib). Since ALL are vaccine preventable, and the question asks what is NOT, but option D says "All of the above". In MCQs, if A, B, and C are all vaccine preventable, option D is usually "All of the above are vaccine preventable". If the question stands as "NOT", none of the options are correct answers.
Likely intended answer: D (meaning all listed ARE vaccine preventable, treating the question as "Which are...").

26.
Which of the following can be considered to be reservoir?
a) Animals
b) Cases
c) Carriers
d) All of the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) All of the above

Explanation: A reservoir is any person, animal, plant, soil, or substance in which an infectious agent normally lives and multiplies. Animals (Zoonosis), Cases (Clinical), and Carriers (Asymptomatic) all serve as reservoirs.

27.
......... is the relative of usual frequency of the disease in the same area among the specified population at the same season of the year
a) Epidemicity
b) Infection
c) Zonostic
d) Convalescenticity
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Epidemicity

Explanation: Epidemicity refers to the frequency of a disease relative to its usual/expected frequency (endemic level) in the same area and season.

28.
Which of the following does not belong to investigation component in disease investigation?
a) Confirm diagnosis
b) Isolate and treat cases
c) Trace contacts
d) Conduct environmental assessments
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Isolate and treat cases

Explanation: Isolating and treating cases is a Control Measure, not an investigation step. Confirming diagnosis, tracing contacts, and environmental assessment are part of gathering information (Investigation).

29.
Which of the following is not among measures meant to protect a susceptible person?
a) Better nutrition
b) Isolation of the cases
c) Chemoprophylaxis
d) Immunization
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Isolation of the cases

Explanation: Isolation applies to the infected person (Source), not the susceptible person. Nutrition, Chemoprophylaxis, and Immunization are measures applied directly to the susceptible host to increase resistance.

30.
The following are diseases listed as of public health importance except:
a) Tuberculosis
b) Malaria
c) STI
d) Cholera
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) STI (Strictly speaking, all are important)

Explanation: This question is subjective without a specific context (like a specific country's priority list). However, TB, Malaria, and Cholera are major killer diseases often cited as top priorities. STIs are also critical. In many contexts, "STI" is a category, not a single disease. However, if forced to choose based on acute outbreak potential or mortality burden in specific contexts, it's difficult. *Standard answer keys often flag non-communicable or less fatal conditions, but here all are communicable.*

31.
Passive surveillance is done through the following except:
a) Case reports initiated by a reporter
b) Disease outbreak
c) Routine generation of health data
d) Data supplied by health department

Correct Answer: b) Disease outbreak

Explanation: A "disease outbreak" is an event, not a method of surveillance. Passive surveillance involves routine reporting (A, C, D) waiting for data to arrive.

33.
Disease distribution can be defined in terms of:
a) Time of occurrence
b) Person affected
c) Place of occurrence
d) All of the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) All of the above

Explanation: Descriptive epidemiology characterizes disease by Time, Place, and Person.

34.
Lung cancer is highly fatal disease and patients die in a very short duration after onset of disease. Which of the following statement is correct for this disease?
a) Incidence and mortality rates will be almost similar in a year
b) Mortality rates will be higher than morbidity rate
c) Incidence rates will be higher
d) There will be no relationship between the two rates
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Incidence and mortality rates will be almost similar in a year

Explanation: For highly fatal diseases with short survival times, almost everyone who gets the disease (Incidence) dies from it quickly (Mortality). Therefore, the incidence rate approximates the mortality rate.

35.
Place characteristics include the following except:
a) Schools
b) Work place
c) Rural differences
d) Environmental exposure
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Environmental exposure

Explanation: Schools, Workplaces, and Rural/Urban zones are distinct "Places". Environmental exposure is usually a characteristic of the Environment or an etiology, although it occurs in a place. However, purely as a category of "Place" in descriptive epidemiology, "Environmental exposure" is a factor, not a location description like the others.

36.
The type of measure in which the numerator is included to denominator is called:
a) Ratio
b) Rate
c) Proportion
d) Fraction
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Proportion

Explanation: In a Proportion, the numerator is a subset of the denominator (e.g., females / total population). In a Ratio, they are independent.

37.
Neonatal mortality rates include deaths of children under ..... of age
a) 1 month
b) 1 year
c) 5 years
d) 3 years
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) 1 month (Specifically 28 days)

Explanation: Neonatal mortality refers to deaths within the first 28 days of life.

38.
Disease control measures are generally directed to all of the following except:
a) Eliminating the host
b) Eliminating the reservoir
c) Eliminating the vector
d) Interrupting the mode of transmission
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Eliminating the host

Explanation: In human diseases, the "host" is the human being. We do not eliminate humans to control disease. We treat/protect them. We DO eliminate reservoirs (e.g., rats), vectors (mosquitoes), and transmission paths.

39.
Analytic epidemiology includes all of the following except:
a) Host factors
b) Agent factor
c) Causative factors
d) All of the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) All of the above

Explanation: Analytic epidemiology focuses on the Why and How (Determinants/Causes). This includes analyzing host factors, agent factors, and environment (causative factors). The "Except" in the question is tricky, but D implies "All of these are included", which is true.

40.
Which of the following surveillance method where the health authority do not stimulate reporting by reminding health care workers to report disease?
a) Active surveillance
b) Passive surveillance
c) Syndromic surveillance
d) Sentinel surveillance
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Passive surveillance

Explanation: In Passive surveillance, health authorities wait for reports to arrive without active stimulation or site visits. Active surveillance involves visiting facilities to find cases.

5.
State the difference between the following terms (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  • (i) Case reports and case series: Case report describes a single patient; Case series describes a group of patients with similar diagnoses.
  • (ii) Confounding factors and association: Association is a statistical relationship; Confounding is a distortion of that association by a third variable.
  • (iii) Portal of entry and portal of exit: Exit is where pathogen leaves the host (e.g., feces); Entry is how it enters the new host (e.g., mouth).
  • (iv) Risk factor and incubation period: Risk factor increases probability of disease; Incubation period is time from infection to symptom onset.
  • (v) Disease distribution and burden of disease: Distribution refers to Time/Place/Person patterns; Burden refers to the impact (cost, morbidity, mortality/DALYs).

8.
In a community the number of individuals who ate various foods led to further discoveries that 21 of the 26 persons who ate fish in 1000 household became ill. What is the attack rate for those who ate fish? Is this secondary or primary attack rate. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Calculation:

$$ \text{Attack Rate} = \frac{\text{Ill}}{\text{Exposed}} \times 100 $$

$$ \text{Attack Rate} = \frac{21}{26} \times 100 = 80.77\% $$

Type: This is a Primary Attack Rate (or Food-Specific Attack Rate) because it refers to the direct exposure to the food source, not transmission from person to person.

Section 3: Long Answer Question

20 Marks
1.
Covid-19 disease has become a common outbreak in the world... Assuming Kenya is a covid-19 free country and unfortunately James Koyiet who travelled from China to Narok County two weeks ago developed signs similar to those of a person suffering from covid-19 and upon being taken to Narok County hospital he passed on. Explain the steps you would take to investigate the outbreak assuming that you have been provided with all the required resources.
Show Answer

Steps in Outbreak Investigation:

  1. Verify the Diagnosis:
    - Collect samples (nasopharyngeal swabs) from the deceased (post-mortem) if possible, or review clinical history.
    - Confirm using laboratory tests (RT-PCR).
  2. Establish the Existence of an Outbreak:
    - Compare observing one case against the expected baseline (Zero, as Kenya is "covid-free"). One case constitutes an outbreak.
  3. Define a Case (Case Definition):
    - Suspected: Travel history + Symptoms.
    - Confirmed: Lab positive.
  4. Identify and Count Cases (Contact Tracing):
    - Trace James's contacts from travel (plane, transport to Narok, family, hospital staff).
    - Screen and isolate contacts.
  5. Perform Descriptive Epidemiology:
    - Describe cases by Time (arrival date, symptom onset), Place (Narok, Route), Person (Age, sex of contacts).
  6. Develop Hypotheses:
    - Source: Imported from China.
    - Mode of transmission: Droplet/Contact.
  7. Evaluate Hypotheses:
    - Analytical studies (likely not needed here as source is evident, but verifying transmission chains).
  8. Implement Control and Prevention Measures:
    - Quarantine contacts.
    - Isolate any new cases.
    - PPE for healthcare workers.
    - Public education in Narok.
  9. Communicate Findings:
    - Report to Ministry of Health.
    - Press release to public (risk communication).
  10. Maintain Surveillance:
    - Continue monitoring for 14-28 days (two incubation periods) after the last case.
