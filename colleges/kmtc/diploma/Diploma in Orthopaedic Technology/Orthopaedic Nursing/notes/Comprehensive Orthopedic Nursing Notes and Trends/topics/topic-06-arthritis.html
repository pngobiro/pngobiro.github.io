ü¶¥ 1. Osteoarthritis;

It‚Äôs often related to aging or to injury

Osteoarthritis is defined as a joint disorder in which there is a progressive softening and disintergration of articular cartilage, accompanied by new growth of cartilage and bone at the joint margins

Classification of osteoarthritis;

  • Primary or idiopathic
  • Secondary due to infection, dysplasia, trauma etc.

Etiology;

  • Genetic
  • Metabolic
  • Hormonal
  • Mechanical; disparity between stress applied to articular cartilage and strength of articular cartilage
  • Ageing, results in weak stiff or soft cartilage

Symptoms of osteoarthritis:

  • Pain
  • Swelling
  • Stiffness
  • Deformity
  • Instability
  • Loss of function

Treatment:

  • Analgesia
  • Oral viscosupplements
  • Intrarticular steroids
  • Intrarticular viscosupplements
  • Altered activity
  • Walking aids
  • Physiotherapy
  • Osteotomy
  • Arthroscopy
  • Excision arthroplsty
  • Arthrodesis

üß¨ 2. Rheumatoid arthritis

Is an autoimmune disease caused by chronic inflammation of unknown etiology, marked by symmetric, peripheral polyarthritis which results in joint damage and physical disability

Etiology;

Cause is unknown

It‚Äôs throught to be genetically inherited (heredity)

Suspected that certain infections in the environment may trigger the immune system to attack the body‚Äôs own tissues resulting in inflammation

Environmental factors

Smoking tobacco increased the risk for acquiring rheumatoid arthritis

Predisposing factors

  • Gender; women before menopause are affected 3 times more than men
  • Familial, hereditary
  • Genetic factors

Joints involved in RA;

  • Hands and wrists
  • Shoulders
  • Elbows
  • Feet
  • Knees
  • Hips
  • Cervical spine

Clinical presentation;

  • Symmetric peripheral polyarthritis
  • Morning joint stiffness >1 hr
  • Decreased grip strength
  • Carpal tunnel syndrome
  • Ulnar deviation
  • Myalgia
  • Fatigue
  • Low grade fever
  • Weight loss
  • Depression
  • Anemia

Laboratory investigations findings;

  • Leucopenia
  • Elevated CSR
  • Positive rheumatoid factor
  • Nomocytic normochromic anemia

Treatment:

The goal of treatment of RA is to;

  1. Prevent or control joint damage
  2. Prevent loss of function
  3. Decrease pain
  4. Maintain the patients quality of life
  5. To avoid or minimize adverse effects of treatment
  6. Preservation of muscle and joint function
  7. Return to the desired productive life

üö∂ Ankylosing spondylitis;

Is a form of arthritis that primarily affects the spine, although other joints can become involved.

It causes inflammation of the spinal joints (vertebrae) and can lead to severe, chronic pain and discomfort

The disease leads to the calcification of the spine ligament and discs and causes this soft structures to harden and fuse together with the joints and vertebral bodies. The spine becomes hard, brittle and susceptible to fracture

In some, AS can also affect the shoulders, ribs, hips, knees and feet. It can also affect areas where the tendons and ligaments attach to the bones. Sometimes it can affect the eyes, bowels and very rarely the heart and lungs.

Causes of ankylosing spondylitis;

The cause is unknown

The disorder does tend to run in families, so genetics plays a role

Risk factors;

  • Gender; men are as much as 3 times likelier to develop AS than women
  • The biggest risk factor is smoking
  • Age; onset generally occurs in late adolescence or early adulthood
  • Family history

Clinical manifestations;

  • Constant pain and stiffness in the low back, buttocks and hips areas that continue for more than 3 months
  • Pain in ligament and tendons
  • Eye inflammation such as redness, and pain in some people with spondylosis
  • Pain in the large joints such as hips and shoulders
  • Early morning stiffness
  • Loss of appetite
  • Weight loss
  • Fatigue
  • Anemia and low iron
  • Other parts of the body may be involved and so the patient may experience inflammation of the bowels, mild eye inflammation, heart valve inflammation and Achilles tendonitis.

Complications;

  • Overgrowth of the bones
  • Heart problems
  • Uveitis (eye inflammation)
  • Compression fractures in bones of people with AS
  • A hunched posture, a curled forward chin to chest stance can occur
  • Cauda equine syndrome; this rare condition involves extreme pressure and swelling of the nerves at the end of the spinal cord
  • One of the intervertebral discs or disc spaces becomes infected.

Diagnosis and tests;

  • A medical history
  • A physical examination
  • X-ray, MRI, CT scan image of the bones and joints
  • Blood tests such as, ESR, C- reactive protein (CRP)

Treatment;

  • Exercise
  • Diet
  • Medicine such as , NSAIDS, - (Aspirin, ibuprofen, naproxen, they fight pain and swelling in the body), disease modifying anti-rheumetic drugs also work in different ways to reduce pain and swelling. Biologic agents ‚Äì e.g. adalimumab (humira), work by blocking proteins involved in pain and swelling
  • Surgery, if AS cuses joint damage that makes daily activities difficult, joint replacement may be an option., other surgical interventions include, osteotomy of the spine, spinal fusion instrumentation, spinal decompression

Prevention and cure;

  • Exercises to stay active to ease pain, maintain flexibility and improve posture
  • Apply heat and cold compressors to ease pain and stiffness
  • Eating healthy
  • Do not smoke
  • Practice good posture

üìã GENERAL ARTHRITIS NOTES

Symptoms of arthritis;

  • Joint pain
  • Stiffness along the swelling of joints
  • Difficulty in walking
  • Fatigue
  • General body malaise
  • Muscle aches and pains
  • Tenderness
  • Difficulty in moving a joint

Causes;

  • When inflammation occurs, chemicals from the body are released into the blood or affected tissues.
  • This release of chemicals increase the blood flow to the area of injury or infection and may result in redness and warmth. Some of the chemicals cause leakage of fluid into tissues resulting I swelling
  • The process may stimulate nerves and cause pain

Diagnosis of arthritis;

Physical examination, it may show swollen warm or red joints with restriction of movement

Blood test, this is generally done for rheumatoid arthritis

Rheumatoid factor, is checked for rheumatoid arthritis, however rheumatoid factor can be found in people without rheumatoid arthritis but with other autoimmune disorders

Levels of ESR erythrocyte sedimentation rate and C ‚Äì reactive protein (CRP) levels, this are increased. By monitoring these levels you are checking the disease activity and also how well someone is responding to treatment.

Imaging scans such as X ‚Äì ray, CT scans and MRI are used to get the image of bones and cartilage to ascertain diagnosis

Management;

Physical exercises, during pain, exercise seems to be the last option, but muscle strengthening and stretching has proved to be very effective.

To relieve pain, pain relievers and NSAIDS (non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs) generally interfere with chemicals called prostaglandins in the body which may trigger pain, inflammation and fever

Surgery, joint replacement surgery is often performed for those who are not able to walk or have a lot of difficulty in walking.
