ü¶¥ DEFINITION & CAUSES

A fracture is a break in the continuity of the bone and is defined according to its type and extent.

A fracture is a break in any bone in the body

Causes of fractures

  1. Direct blows
  2. Crushing forces
  3. Sudden twisting motions
  4. Extreme muscle contractions
02

üóÇÔ∏è TYPES & PATTERNS

Types of fractures

  1. Complete fracture; a break across the entire cross section of the bone
  2. An incomplete fracture; the break occurs through only part of the cross section of the bone
  3. Closed fracture; (simple fracture), one that does not cause a break in the skin
  4. An open fracture; (compound or complex fracture) is one in which the skin or mucous membrane wound extends to the fractures bone.
  5. Pathologic fracture; occurs through the area of disease of the bone
‚ö†Ô∏è Open Fracture Grading

Open fractures are graded according to the following criteria;

  • Grade 1 is clean wound less than 1 cm long
  • Grade 2 is a larger wound without extensive soft tissue damage
  • Grade 3 is highly contaminated, has extensive soft tissue damage and is the most severe

Pattern of fractures;

  1. Greenstick; a fracture in which one side of a bone is broken while the other is bent (like a green stick
  2. Transverse; a fracture in which the break is across the bone at a right angle to the long axis of the bone
  3. Oblique; a fracture straight across the bone obliquely
  4. Spiral; also known as torsion fracture, a fracture that is shaft around the bone
  5. Comminuted; a bone splintered or crushed into more than three fragments
  6. Compression; a fracture caused by compression, the act of pressing together, compression fractures of the vertebrae are especially common with osteoporosis
  7. Depression; a fracture in which fragments are driven inward (seen frequently in fractures of the skull and facial bones)
  8. Avulsion; a fracture which occurs when a fragment of bone tears away from the main mass of the bone
  9. Impacted; that which occurs when a fragment of bone wedged into other bone fragments
  10. Fracture dislocation; a fracture complicated by the bone being out of the joints.

üî¨ PATHOPHYSIOLOGY & CLINICAL MANIFESTATIONS

Due to any etiology, a fracture occurs, muscle that were attached to the bone are disrupted and causes spasms

Proximal portion of bone remains in place, the distal portion can become displaced in response to both causative force and spasm in the associated muscles

In addition the periosteum and blood vessels in the cortex and marrow are disrupted.

Soft tissue damage occurs leading to bleeding and formation of hematoma between the fracture fragment and beneath the periosteum.

Bone tissue surrounding the fracture site dies, creating an intense inflammatory response, which leads to the release of chemical mediators, histamine and prostaglandins

This results in vasodilation, edema, pain, loss of function, leukocytes and infiltration of white blood cells.

ü©∫ MANAGEMENT

BONE HEALING;

ASS; refer to the bone healing process notes

  • Hematoma forms between the end of the bone and in surrounding soft tissue
  • Inflammation and accumulation of inflammatory exudates
  • Macrophages that phagocytes the hematoma and small fragment of the bone
  • Fibroblast migrate to the site
  • Granulation tissue and new capillaries develop
  • New bone forms as large number of osteoblast secrete spongy bone which unites the broken end and is protected by an outer layer of bone and cartilage
  • These new deposit of bone and cartilage are called callus, the callus mature and the cartilage is gradually replaced with new bone
  • Medullary canal is reopened through the callus
  • Bone heals completely with the callus and tissue completely replaced with the mature compact bone
  • The bone is thicker and stronger at the repair site than the original, and so a second fracture is more likely to occur at the different site.

EMERGENCY MANAGEMENT OF FRACTURES;

  • If a fracture is suspected, immobilize the fracture site by using the splint to prevent movement of the fracture fragments, that causes pain, bleeding and soft tissue damage
  • A splint may be used in the forms of leg by bandaging the legs together with the unaffected leg serving as a splint for the injured one
  • If upper extremity fracture, bandage to the chest or may be placed in a slings
  • Before and after splinting, neurovascular status should be assessed to determine the adequacy of the peripheral tissue perfusion and nerve function
  • With an open fracture, cover with a sterile dressing to prevent contamination
  • In emergency department, the patient is evaluated completely then remove the cloth from the uninjured site first then the injured site. The patent clothing may be cut away

MEDICAL/ SURGICAL MANAGEMENT;

1. REDUCTION;

This is setting the bone to restoration of the fracture fragments to anatomic alignment and rotation

OPEN REDUCTION; it‚Äôs a surgical approach, the fracture fragments are reduced. External / internal fixation devices (metallic pins, wires, screws, plates, nails or rods) may be used to hold the bone fragments in position until solid bone healing occurs.

CLOSED REDUCTION; the closed reduction is accomplished by bringing the bone fragment into apposition (placing the ends into contact) through manipulation and manual traction. Extremity is held in the desired position while the physician applies cast, splint or other device. Traction ( skin or skeletal) may be used in fracture reduction and immobilization.

2. IMMOBILIZATION;

Immobilization may be accomplished by external or internal fixation.

Methods of external fixation include;

  • Bandages,
  • Casts
  • Splints
  • Continuous traction
  • External fixators

Metallic implants used for internal fixation serve as internal splints to immobilize the fracture

3. MAINTAINING AND RESTORING FUNCTION;

Restlessness, anxiety and discomfort are controlled with a variety of approaches such as reassurance, position changes and pain relief strategies including use of analgesics

Exercises are encouraged to promote circulation.

Participation in activities of daily living is encouraged to promote independent functioning and self esteem

‚ö†Ô∏è COMPLICATION OF FRACTURES;

1. EARLY COMPLICATIONS;

  • Shock
  • Fat embolism
  • Compartment syndrome
  • Deep venous thrombosis
  • Disseminated intravascular coagulation
  • Infection

2. DELAYED COMPLICATIONS;

  • Delayed union and nonunion
  • Avascular necrosis of bone
  • Reaction of internal fixation devices

‚ñ∂ 1. Acute pain

Acute pain related to breakdown of continuity of the bone as evidenced by facial expression and verbalization of the patient

GOAL; patient will not feel pain

INTERVENTION;

  • Assess the onset, duration, location, severity and intensity of pain
  • Administer the analgesic according to physician order
  • Provide comfort devices like sand bags for immobilization of affected parts
  • Provide diversion therapy
‚ñ∂ 2. Impaired physical mobility

Impaired physical mobility related to application of traction or cast as evidenced by assessment

GOAL; patient will be able to move the affected area.

INTERVENTION;

  • Provide range of motion exercised to the patient
  • Assist the patient in ambulation after recovery of fracture
  • Provide assistance while using walker or crunches if required
  • Prevent from complication which usually occurs due to immobility
‚ñ∂ 3. Self-care deficit

Self-care deficit related to fracture as evidenced by poor personal hygiene

GOAL; patient will maintain the personal hygiene

INTERVENTION;

  • Assess the need for self-care
  • Encourage the patient or relatives to do self-care activity
  • Head to foot care to be provided to the patient
  • Educate about importance of maintaining personal hygiene
‚ñ∂ 4. Imbalanced nutrition

Imbalanced nutrition less than body requirement related to increased demand of nutrient for bone healing as evidenced by observation

GOAL; maintain the nutritional status of the patient

INTERVENTION;

  • Assess the nutritional status by intake / output chart, biochemical measures, body mass.
  • Encourage the patient to take protein rich diet
  • Plenty of fluids and frequent intake of meal is necessary
  • Try to assess the daily weight of the client.
