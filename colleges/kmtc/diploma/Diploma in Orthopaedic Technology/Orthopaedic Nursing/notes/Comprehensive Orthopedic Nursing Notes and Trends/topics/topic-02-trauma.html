üöë POLYTRAUMA;

DEFINITION; is a significant injury in at least two of the following six body regions;

  • Head, neck and cervical spine
  • Face
  • Chest and thoracic spine
  • Abdomen and lumbar spine
  • Limbs and bony pelvis
  • Skin

Polytrauma is expanded to include concurrent injury to two or more body parts or systems that result in cognitive, physical, psychological or other psychosocial impairment.

CRITERIA FOR POLYTRAUMA; include any one of the following combination injury

  • Two major system injury plus one major limb injury
  • One major system injury plus two major limb injury
  • One major system injury plus open grade 3 skeletal injury
  • Unstable pelvis with associated visceral injury

ETIOLOGY OF POLYTRAUMA;

  • RTA
  • Fall from height (blunt or penetrating injury)
  • Airplane crashes, train derailment
  • Assault
  • Blast
  • Thermal, chemical injury

üè• TRAUMA CARE & SHOCK

Types of shock in polytrauma patients

  1. Hemorrhagic shock; due to loss of blood, diagnosis based on vital sign and examination characterized by hypotension, tachycardia and cold skin
  2. Neurogenic shock; due to spinal cord injury that causes distribution of sympathetic outflow to heart and blood vessels. Its characterized by hypotension, bradycardia and warm skin.
  3. Hypoxic shock
  4. Septic shock

PHASES OF TRAUMA CARE;

  1. Triage
  2. Primary survey; resuscitation
  3. Secondary survey
  4. Stabilization
  5. Transfer
  6. Definitive care

CONCEPTS OF INITIAL ASSESSMENT

  1. Preparation
  2. Triage
  3. Primary survey; identify what is killing the patient

A ‚Äì Airway maintenance and control of cervical spine

B ‚Äì Breathing and ventilation

C ‚Äì Circulation and hemorrhage control

D ‚Äì Disability limitation

E ‚Äì Exposure and environment

F ‚Äì Fracture stabilization, folly catheter

  1. Resuscitation; treat what is killing the patient
  2. Adjuncts to primary survey and resuscitation
  3. Secondary survey; proceed to identify other injuries
  4. Adjuncts to secondary survey
  5. Continued post resuscitation monitoring and reevaluation
  6. Definitive care; develop a definitive management plan

Complications of polytrauma;

  1. Tetanus
  2. Acute respiratory distress syndrome.
  3. Fat embolism
  4. Disseminated intravascular coagulopathy
  5. Crush syndrome
  6. Multisystem organ failure

‚ö° NERVE INJURIES;

UPPER LIMB

Axillary nerve injury;

it offers sensory function of an oval shaped area over the lateral shoulder patch. Its motor function is that it innervates the deltoid (shoulder abduction) and the teres minor (shoulder external rotation) muscles.

Common causes of injury;

Trauma, usually with shoulder dislocation or humeral fracture

Axillary nerve injury signs and symptoms;

  • A sharply defined region off sensory loss over the lateral shoulder (sergeant‚Äôs patch)
  • The patient complains of shoulder weakness, although abduction can be initiated (by the supraspinus muscle) it cannot be maintained
  • There is wasting of the deltoid muscle

Radial nerve injury;

offers sensory function to the posterior arm and forearm, lateral 2/3 of the dorsum of the hand and proximal dorsal aspect of lateral 3 and half fingers. Its motor function is to the posterior compartment of the arm and forearm.

Causes of injury;

  • Fractures of proximal humerus shaft of humerus or radius
  • Stab wounds to the antecubital fossa, forearm or wrist

Signs and symptoms of radial nerve injury

  • Numbness of the skin over the posterior arm, posterior forearm and radial distribution of dorsum and hand
  • Weak elbow, wrist, thumb
  • Absent triceps and supinator reflexes
  • Wrist drop deformity

Median nerve injury;

offers sensory function to the skin over theanar eminence, lateral 2/3 palm of hand and palmar aspect of lateral 3 and half fingers. The motor function to the muscles of anterior compartment of the forearm except flexor carpi ulnaris and the medial two parts of flexor digitorum profundus

Causes of injury;

Supracondylar fractures of the humerus

Compression by carpal tunnel syndrome

Signs and symptoms of median nerve injury;

  • Numbness of the skin over theanar eminence and median distribution of the hand
  • Weak forearm pronation wrist flexion and abduction
  • Weak finger flexion
  • Weak pincer grip
  • Ape hand deformity
  • Wasting of theanar eminence

Ulnar nerve injury;

offers sensory over the skin hypothenar eminence, medial 1/3 palm of hand, palmar aspect of lateral 1 and half fingers. The motor function to two muscles of anterior compartment of forearm and most of the intrinsic muscles of the hand

Common causes of ulnar nerve injury;

  • Supracondylar fractures of the humerus
  • Compression cubital tunnel in the elbow

Signs and symptoms of ulnar nerve injury;

  • Numbness of the skin over hypothenar eminence and ulnar distribution of hand
  • Weak wrist flexion and adduction
  • Flexion of ring and little finger
  • Claw hand deformity
  • Wasting of hypothenar eminence

LOWER LIMB

Femoral nerve injury;

may be injured by a gunshot wound, hip or pelvic fractures, by pressure or traction during an operation.

Signs and symptoms of femoral nerve injury

  • Quadriceps action is missing (weak knee extension)
  • Numbness of the anterior thigh and medial aspect of the leg
  • The knee reflex is depressed
  • Severe neurogenic pain is common

Sciatic nerve injury;

division of the main sciatic nerve is rare except in gunshot wounds. Traction lesions may occur with traumatic hip dislocation and with pelvic fractures

Signs and symptoms;

  • The hamstrings and all muscles below the knee are paralysed
  • The ankle jerk is absent
  • Sensation is lost below the knee
  • The patient walks with a drop foot and a high stepping gait to avoid dragging the insensitive foot on the ground

Peroneal nerve injury;

the most common frequent site of injury is just below the knee as the nerve wraps around the lateral aspect of the fibula, immediately before diving into its deep and superficial branches

Causes

  • Trauma or injury to the knee
  • Fracture of the fibula
  • Crossing the legs
  • Protracted squatting and leg casts

Signs and symptoms or Peroneal nerve injury;

  • Drop foot deformity, the patient may either dorsiflex or avert the foot
  • He or she walks with a high stepping gait to avoid catching the toes
  • Sensation is lost over the front and outer half of the leg and the dorsum of the foot
  • Pain may be significant

üë§ FACIAL PALSY;

Facial nerve palsy due to birth trauma is the loss of voluntary muscle movement in the infant‚Äôs face due to pressure on the facial nerve in the face just before or at the time of birth.

Facial palsy is also known as bell‚Äôs palsy. Facial nerve gets injured as it remains unprotected after its exit through the stylomastoid foramen

Causes;

  • Direct pressure of the forceps blades
  • Compression of nerve against sacral promontory
  • Much pressure on the ramus of the mandible where the nerve crosses superficially

Signs and symptoms;

  • There is unilateral facial weakness with eyelid affected side remaining open and immobile
  • Absent rooting reflex on the affected side but sucking reflex remains unaffected. However if the baby cannot form effective seal on the nipple, some feeding difficulties arise
  • On crying the angle of the mouth is drawn over the unaffected side
  • It can be found bilateral, if in association with intracranial lesions

Management;

  • There is no specific treatment as the condition improves on 1- 2 weeks unless it is complicated by intracranial lesions
  • If the baby‚Äôs eye does not close all the way, an eyepad and eyedrops will be used to protect the eye
  • If the eyelids remain open, regular instillation of synthetic tear drops (1% methylcellulose eye drops) can help lubricate the eyeball
  • Feeding difficulties are usually overcome by the baby‚Äôs own adaptation, although alternative feeding position can be tried, (give expressed breast milk)
  • Surgery may be needed to relieve pressure on the nerve
  • Infants with permanent paralysis need definitive treatment like nerve transplantation

Prognosis;

  • Most of the cases of facial paralysis resolve spontaneously in 2 weeks. If movement of face is still limited at one year of age, permanent paralysis is likely.

üí™ BRACHIAL PALSY;

This refers to damage in the root or trunk of the nerves of the brachial plexus, a group of nerves at the apex of the axilla, lying under the clavicle due to stretching or effusion or hemorrhage inside the nerve sheath or tearing of fibers

Unilateral involvement is common

Causes;

  • Hyperextension of the neck during attempted delivery of shoulder dystocia or even in spontaneous vaginal delivery
  • Difficult extraction

Types of brachial palsy;

‚ñ∂ 1. Erb‚Äôs palsy

refers to injury to upper brachial plexus involving 5th and 6th and rarely the 7th cervical nerve roots

Clinical features of erb‚Äôs palsy:

  • Inward rotation of arms with extension of elbow
  • Pronation of the forearm
  • Flexion of the wrist
  • Arm limp although some movements of the fingers is possible
  • Moro reflex and biceps jerk are absent on affected side

Management:

  • Splint the arm in position which will most effectively relax the affected muscle. Use of splint helps to hold the arm abducted to right angle and externally rotated
  • Every joint of the affected arm should be kept in full range of movement atleast once a day
  • Care of skin
  • Physiotherapy
  • Suitable analgesics are prescribes for relief of pain
  • Usually complete recovery takes place within weeks or months, but in severe injury permanent disability may develop. Follow up is recommended as early surgical repair (nerve suture, nerve grafting and nerve decompression) has been effective in the 5% of babies who do not recover spontaneously within 4 ‚Äì 6 months.
‚ñ∂ 2. Klumpke‚Äôs palsy

refers to damage to the lower brachial plexus involving the 7th and 8th cervical nerve roots or even 1st thoracic nerve roots. The upper arm has normal movement, but the lower arm, wrist and hands are affected.

Clinical features:

  • Arm is flexed at elbow and wrist is extended
  • Forearm is supinated and a claw ‚Äì like deformity of the hand is observed
  • Wrist drop and flaccid paralysis of the hand with no grasp reflex
  • When the 1st thoracic nerve is involved, there may be homolateral ptosis with small pupil due to sympathetic nerve involvement (Horner‚Äôs syndrome)

Management:

  • Immobilization by splitting of arms and prevention of contractures
  • Physical therapy, gentle massage and passive movements are advocated
  • Suitable analgesics are prescribed for relief of pain
  • Full recovery takes weeks or even months. Severe laceration and hemorrhage in nerves may produce permanent disability.
  • If paralysis persists for more than 4 ‚Äì 6 months, nerve repair surgery is recommended
‚ñ∂ 3. Total brachial plexus palsy

refers to damage to all brachial plexus nerve

Clinical features:

  • Complete paralysis of the arm and hand with a lack of sensation and circulatory problems

Management:

  • Treatment to prevent contractures of the paralyzed arm by splinting and passive movements of the joint and limbs under direction of physiotherapist
  • Surgical repair of nerve may be recommended
  • Medications is primarily used for pain

Prognosis of brachial palsy;

  • Is usually good if it is due to stretching
  • Erb‚Äôs palsy tends to resolve more quickly than the other forms
  • Babies with no functional recovery by 4 months of age may require surgical repair by 6 months of age to achieve full function
  • But if it is due to hemorrhage or avulsion, the deformity may be permanent
‚ñ∂ 4. Phrenic nerve paralysis

refers to the damage to the phrenic nerve, a nerve that arises from the cervical plexus (C3,4,5) and innervated diaphragm which causes paralysis of the ipsilateral diaphragm

Causes;

  • Excessive stretching of the neck at birth usually during breech or difficult forceps delivery and shoulder dystocia

Clinical features:

  • Injury to phrenic nerve is usually unilateral, the lung on the affected side doesn‚Äôt expand and the respiratory efforts are ineffectual
  • Infants present with respiratory distress, cyanosis, and tachypnea
  • Diagnosis is made by USG showing paradoxical movement of the diaphragm

Management:

  • Treatment is supportive
  • To facilitate maximum expansion of the uninvolved lung, the infant is positioned on the affected side
  • Adequate warm and humidified oxygen in high concentration. If oxygen saturation of blood cannot be maintained at a satisfactory level and carbon dioxide level rises, infant will be required ventilator support
  • Recovery is usually complete in 1 ‚Äì 3 months.
