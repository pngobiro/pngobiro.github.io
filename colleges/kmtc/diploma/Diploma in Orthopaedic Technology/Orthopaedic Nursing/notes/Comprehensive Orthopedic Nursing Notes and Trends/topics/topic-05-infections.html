ü¶† TUBERCULOSIS

This is simply a form of TB that affects the spine, the long bones and the joints. The spine is the most commonly affected. A common form of spinal bone TB is known as Pott‚Äôs disease.

However bone TB could potentially affect any bone in your body.

Tuberculosis is an extremely infectious disease caused by the bacterium mycobacterium tuberculosis, and is one of the most common top 10 causes of death worldwide. When TB spreads outside the pulmonary system, it is referred to as extra pulmonary tuberculosis.

Etiology of bone tuberculosis;

  • It occurs when you contract tuberculosis and it spreads outside the lungs
  • Tuberculosis is spread from person to person through air, once you contract tuberculosis, it can travel through blood from the lungs or lymph nodes into bones, spine or joints
  • Bone TB typically begins due to the rich vascular supply in the middle of long bones and vertebrae.

Signs and symptoms of bone TB;

Its not easy to recognize signs of bone TB until its far advanced, sometimes because it may be painless in the early stages. When its diagnosed the signs are usually in advanced stages, the include

  • Severe back pain
  • Swelling
  • Stiffness
  • Abscesses
  • Neurological complications
  • Paraplegia / paralysis
  • Limb shortening in children
  • Bone deformities
  • Fatigue
  • Fever
  • Night sweats
  • Weight loss

Treatment on bone TB;

  • Early treatment with the right regimen of medications
  • In TB of the spine, spinal surgery is necessary, such as laminectomy (where part of the vertebrae is remove)
  • Bed rest
  • Advise on nutrition, to take a high protein diet
  • Medication are the first line of defense of bone tuberculosis and they include; anti-tuberculosis medications such as rifampicin, isoniazid, ethambutol and pyrazinamide.
  • All patients who have not been on TB therapy previously should have a 2 month initial phase of treatment consisting of isoniazid, rifampicin, pyrazinamide and ethambutol, followed by a continuation phase of ethambutol and isoniazid for 6 months, or isoniazid and rifampicin for 4 months.
  • Physiotherapy for improved patient

ü¶¥ OSTEOMYELITIS

This is bone infection that occurs due to the infection of bacteria in the bone. The infection is caused by the bacterium staphylococcus aureus (most common)

The infection begins in the medullary cavity then extends to cortical bone and eventually it reaches the periosteum.

Etiology;

  • Staphylococcus aureus (most common)

Other special pathogens that can cause osteomyelitis include;

  • Salmonella ‚Äì immunocompromised, sickle cell disease
  • Pasteurella multocida ‚Äì cat bites
  • Mycobacterium tuberculosis ‚Äì Hematogenous spread during primary disease
  • Fungi ‚Äì intravenous device infection or immunosuppression.

Pathophysiology;

  • Invasion of bacteria into cancellous bone leads to inflammation and edema in the bone marrow spaces.
  • This results in compression of blood vessels and severe compromise of blood supply to the bone tissue.
  • Inadequate blood supply is a main factor as the involved area becomes ischemic and bone becomes necrotic
  • Bacteria can then proliferate, because normal blood bone defenses do not reach the tissue, and the osteomyelitis spreads until it is stopped by medical and surgical therapy

Predisposing factors;

  • Fractures
  • Impaired blood flow
  • Impaired immunity
  • Poor nutrition
  • Radiation exposure
  • Odontogenic infections

Classification;

‚ñ∂ 1. Based on pathogenicity
  • Hematogenous (most in kids), affects the tubular bones and in adults it affects the spine, pelvis and small bones
  • Spread from adjacent soft tissue infection e.g. ex ulcers, diabetic foot ulcers
  • Direct inoculation such as in ex trauma or surgery
‚ñ∂ 2. Chronicity
  • Acute osteomyelitis; (present for 1 month) it has a contagious focus, progressive
  • Sub-acute osteomyelitis
  • Chronic osteomyelitis; is recurrent and multifocal. It may be suppurative or non suppurative

Risk factors;

  • People with diabetes
  • People receiving hemodialysis
  • People with weakened immune systems / immunosuppression
  • Sickle cell disease
  • Intravenous drug abusers
  • The elderly
  • Chronic steroid use
  • Chronic joint disease
  • Presence of a prosthetic orthopedic device
  • Open fracture

Clinical manifestations

Acute osteomyelitis

  • Constant bone pain that worsens with activity
  • Swelling, tenderness, warmth at infection site
  • Restricted movement of affected part
  • Later signs ‚Äì drainage
  • Failure to thrive
  • Drowsy
  • Irritable
  • Metaphyseal tenderness
  • Decreased range of motion

Chronic osteomyelitis

  • Bone infection lasting longer than a month
  • Infection that has failed to respond to initial course of antibiotic therapy
  • Systemic signs may be diminished and local signs of infection are more common

Diagnosis and Differential

Differential diagnosis;

  • Cellulitis
  • Acute septic arthritis
  • Acute rheumatism
  • Sickle cell crisis
  • Gaucher‚Äôs disease

Diagnosis;

  • Complete medical history
  • Physical examination
  • Full body examination and local tenderness
  • Elevated WBC count
  • Erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR)
  • Bone or soft tissue biopsy, a definitive way to determine causative organism
  • Patient‚Äôs blood and wound culture, may show frequently positive presence of causative organism, elevated WBCs
  • Radiologic signs, usually do not appear until 10 weeks after start of clinical symptoms
  • Magnetic resonance imaging
  • Computed tomography, helps to identify the extent of infection, including soft tissue involvement

Complications of osteomyelitis;

  • Septicemia
  • Metastatic infections
  • Septic arthritis
  • Altered bone growth
  • Chronic osteomyelitis
  • Bone abscess
  • Pathological fracture
  • Loosing of the prosthetic implant
  • Overlying soft tissue cellulitis
  • Draining soft tissue tract

üíâ MANAGEMENT OF OSTEOMYELITIS

Antibiotic therapy

Antibiotic are used to reduce the infection around the bone, they are give intravenously for at least 4 ‚Äì 6 weeks, and oral antibiotics are followed. The type of antibiotic to be used is depending upon the type of infecting pathogen. E.g.

  • Staphylococcus aureus ‚Äì vancomycin 1g IV every 12 hours
  • Streptococcus species ‚Äì penicillin G 2 ‚Äì 4 million IU IV every 4 hrs

During antibiotic therapy the patient is to be hospitalized

Surgery

Suppose the antibiotic therapy is not responding, then surgery is the final decision to save from spreading the pathogen into the surrounding environment

Painkilling medications are used to reduce pain that is developed during the infection

Surgery for the chronic form of osteomyelitis is needed. The following procedures are useful

  1. Draining, the surgeon makes draining the pus that filled around the bone by a small incision
  2. Debridement, the surgeon removes the necrotic bone tissue and also the infected tissue that surrounds the bone to avoid spread of the infection
  3. Vascularization, the gap left after the debridement is filled with a piece of synthetic bone cement (calcium phosphate) or skin or muscle from other parts of the body. The graft helps to repair the damaged blood vessels and forms a new bone
  4. Removal of foreign objects, i.e. from the previous surgery such as screws, plate etc. because these objects tend to form infection.
  5. Amputation, if the patient has diabetes it is not possible to cure the infection, this is so as to stop the spread of infection to other parts of the body

Prevention;

  • Look and take care of even small wounds and cuts by thorough washing and cleaning and also applying suitable antibiotics
  • Contact a doctor in case a wound is not healing after a long period of time
  • Use proper footwear and protective aids during sports activities to avoid injury
  • Give prompt medical attention to your allergies and any infections on your skin
  • Lifestyle changes such as quitting smoking, thereby preventing pathogens entering into the bone
