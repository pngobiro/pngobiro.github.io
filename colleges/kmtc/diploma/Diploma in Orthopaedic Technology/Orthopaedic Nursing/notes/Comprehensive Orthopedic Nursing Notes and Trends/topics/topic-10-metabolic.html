ü¶¥ RICKETS;

A disease of infancy and childhood due to disturbances in calcium and phosphate metabolism which results in defective mineralization of growing bones before fusion of epiphyses.

This is a bone disorder caused by a deficiency of vitamin D, calcium or phosphate.

It leads to softening and weakening of the bones and is seen mostly in children 6-24 months of age. Vitamin D promotes the absorption of calcium and phosphorus from the gastrointestinal tract. A deficiency of vitamin D makes it difficult to maintain proper calcium and phosphorus levels in bones which can cause rickets

Classification;

  1. Nutritional rickets;
  2. Vitamin D resistant rickets
  3. Vitamin D dependent type 1 (inability to hydroxylate)
  4. Vitamin D dependent type II (receptor insensitivity)
  5. Renal rickets

Etiology:

  1. Nutritional; There is a dietary deficiency of vitamin D leads to decreased absorption of calcium and phosphorus from the intestine
  2. Malabsorption; intestinal conditions like steatorrhea, celiac disease, common diarrhoea etc.
  3. Lack of sunlight exposure
  4. Hepatobiliary conditions e.g. extrahepatic biliary atresia, neonatal hepatitis, cystic fibrosis etc.
  5. Renal causes; defective absorption of phosphate through the renal tubule, renal tubular acidosis, or diminished intake or absorption of phosphates

Clinical features:

  • Muscular weakness, lethargic malaise and irritability
  • Tetani convulsions
  • Repeated diarrhea
  • Flattening of the vertex and occiput causing enlarged squared appearance
  • Delayed closure of fontanelle
  • A protuberant abdomen (pot belly) due to hypotonia of abdominal muscles
  • The spine may have kyphosis or scoliosis
  • Enlarged wrist and ankles, knees
  • Anterior bowing of tibia and femur
  • Incomplete fracture
  • Growth restriction
  • Hypocalcemic symptoms like tetani, seizures, stridor due to laryngospasm

Diagnosis:

  • History taking on the dietary intake, sun exposure, maternal risk factors, maternal risk factors, drug history, malabsorption and renal diseases
  • Classical clinical signs and symptoms
  • Radiological changes ‚Äì the earliest xray changes are often observed in lower ends of the ulnar and radius
  • Laboratory tests
  • X-ray;

Management:

Treatment of rickets consists of medical treatment, prevention of deformities and treatment of existing deformities

Oral or parenteral administration of vitamin D ( 15mg (600000 IU) vitamin D intramuscularly as a single dose every 2 weeks, once healing is started 400 IU can be given daily

It takes 2-4 weeks for X-ray evidence of healing to be evident

As the bones are soft and can bend easily by pressure or muscle strain, child‚Äôs movements should be controlled, so that no weight or pressure is exerted upon the limbs

Splints can be used of prevent deformity

Correction by osteotomy when the deformity is near a joint. Osteotomy can only be done when xrays indicate that at least third stage of rickets has been reached.

In renal rickets, acidosis can be reversed by administration of alkaline salts like sodium citrate, sodium lactate or calcium gluconate, this also helps to heal bone disease

Complications of rickets;

  • Failure to grow
  • Abnormally curved spine
  • Skeletal deformities
  • Dental defects
  • Seizures

ü¶¥ OSTEOMALACIA

This means soft bones

When normal bone is formed, fibres are coated with minerals, this process is called mineralisation. Osteomalacia happens if mineralisation doesn‚Äôt take place properly. In this case more and more bone is made up of collagen matrix without mineral covering, so the bones become soft

Causes of Osteomalacia;

  • Lack of calcium
  • Lack of vitamin D
  • Cancers
  • Kidney failure and acidosis
  • Lack of enough phosphates in the diet
  • Liver disease and so cannot convert vitamin D to its active form
  • Use of drugs used to treat seizures and so their side effects
  • Phosphorus deficiency

Risk factors of Osteomalacia;

  • Hereditary vitamin D metabolism disorders
  • Kidney failure
  • Mineral deficiency
  • Cancer
  • Liver disease

Symptoms of Osteomalacia;

  • Weak brittle bones that fracture easily
  • Muscle weakness
  • Bone pain especially in the hip, lower back, pelvis, legs and even your ribs
  • Irregular heart rhythms due to low calcium levels, numbness around your mouth, arms and legs, spasms in your hands and feet.
  • Complications of Osteomalacia;
  • Easily fracturing bones
  • Muscle weakness
  • Stiffness and difficulty in walking

Diagnosis;

  • X- ray slight cracks in your bones that are visible on X- rays are characteristic of Osteomalacia
  • Blood tests which will measure low levels of calcium, phosphorus, electrolytes and vitamin D
  • Bone biopsy

Treatment of Osteomalacia;

  • Treatment is mainly by easing the bone pain and muscle weakness, and may take several months
  • If the disease is due to lack of vitamin D, daily doses of 800 ‚Äì 2000IU vitamin D are often used
  • Calcium supplements of 500 ‚Äì 1000 mg/day may speed up bone healing, that is if the dietary intake is low
  • Alfacalcidol is a vitamin D tablet can be used for both adults and children to treat Osteomalacia
  • Self-help and daily living to be improved, this includes,
  • Having a diet rich in Vitamin D
  • Getting a healthy amount of sunshine
  • Reducing alcohol intake
  • Stopping smoking
  • Exercising regularly and maintaining a healthy weight.

Prevention of Osteomalacia;

  • Improve on diet, e.g. dairy products e.g. milk, yoghurt and cheese
  • Exposure to the sun for natural vitamin D
  • Use of supplements daily containing vitamin D and calcium or phosphorus.

ü¶¥ OSTEOPOROSIS;

This is a metabolic bone disorder that results in decreased bone mass. The bones become porous, brittle and abnormally vulnerable to fracture

Classification;

1. Primary also known as idiopathic osteoporosis (it affects children and adults alike)

‚ñ∂ Type I (OR postmenopausal osteoporosis)
  • Usually affects females ages 51 ‚Äì 75
  • Related to the loss of estrogen‚Äôs protective effect to the bone
  • Results in trabecular bone loss and some cortical bone loss
  • Vertebral and wrist fractures are common
‚ñ∂ Type II (OR senile osteoporosis)
  • Occurs most commonly between ages 70 ‚Äì 85
  • Trabecular and cortical bone loss
  • Fractures of the proximal humerus, proximal tibia , femoral neck and pelvis

2. Secondary due to an underlying disease

Etiology;

  • Genetics
  • Age; post menopause, advanced age
  • Nutrition; low calcium intake, low vitamin D intake, high phosphate and inadequate calories
  • Physical exercise; lack of weight bearing exercises, low weight and low body mass index
  • Lifestyle choices e.g. use of caffeine, alcohol, smoking and lack of exposure to sunlight.
  • Use of medications and their side effects, e.g. corticosteroids affects calcium absorption and metabolism

Pathophysiology;

  • The rate of bone resorption increases over the rate of bone formation this leads to loss bone mass
  • Calcium and phosphate salts are lost making the bones to be porous, and brittle. Occurs most commonly in postmenopausal women.

Complications;

  • Fractures; vertebral, femora neck, distal and radius bones
  • Progressive kyphosis; loss of weight
  • Chronic back pain from compression fracture

Clinical manifestations;

  • Asymptomatic until later stages
  • Fracture after a minor trauma may be the first indication
  • Joint stiffness
  • Bone pain
  • Weakness
  • Estrogen deficiency may be notes

Diagnosis;

  • X- Rays; shows changes only after 30% to 60% loss of bone
  • Serum urine calcium levels
  • Bone biopsy; shows thin, porous, otherwise normal bone
  • Spine CT scan shows demineralization

Management;

Goal;

  • To control bone loss
  • Prevent additional fractures
  • Control pain

Treatment;

  • Physical therapy program of gentle exercise and activity
  • Drug therapy to slow the disease progress
  • Supportive devices
  • Surgery; open reduction or internal fixation to correct pathologic fractures of the femur
  • Bisphosphonates to prevent bone loss and reduce the risk of fractures
  • Calcium and vitamin D supplements to support normal bone metabolism
  • Calcitonin to reduce bone resorption and slow the decline in bone mass
  • Back brace to support weakened vertebrae.
  • Reduction and immobilization with a cast for 4-10 weeks
  • Lifestyle modification to include; adequate dietary intake to include calcium, decreased alcohol intake, regular exercises and smoking cessation
  • Hormonal replacement therapy with estrogen and progesterone to decrease bone resorption and increase bone mass
  • Fluoride treatments may also offer preventive benefit

Nursing management;

‚ñ∂ Nursing diagnosis; (incomplete)
  • Chronic pain
  • Disturbed body image
  • Dressing and grooming self-care deficit
  • Imbalanced nutrition; less than body requirements
  • Impaired physical mobility
  • Risk for impaired skin integrity
  • Risk for injury
‚ñ∂ Expected outcomes;
  • The patient will experience increased comfort and decreased pain
  • Express positive feelings about herself
  • Perform daily living activities within the confines of the disease
  • Maintain adequate dietary intake maintain joint mobility and range of motion
  • Exhibit intact skin integrity
  • Demonstrate measures to prevent injury
‚ñ∂ Nursing interventions;
  • Design plan of care to consider the patient‚Äôs fragility
  • Concentrate on careful positioning and ambulation, prescribed exercises
  • Provide emotional support and reassuarance to help the patient cope with limited mobility
  • Give her opportunities to voice her feelings
  • If possible arrange for her to interact with others who have similar problems
  • Include the patient and her family in all phases of care
  • Answer questions as honestly as you can
  • Encourage the patient ot perform as much self care as he immobility and pain can allow
  • Allow her adequate time to perform these activities at her own pace
  • Check the patient‚Äôs skin daily for redness, warmth and new sited of pain which may indicate new fractures
  • Attend scheduled physical therapy sessions
  • Impose safety precautions, e.g. keep the bed rails up, move the patient gently and carefully at all times
  • Provide a balanced diet rich in nutrients, that support skeletal metabolism, such as vitamin D, calcium and protein
  • Administer analgesics and heat relieve pain as ordered, and assess the patients response
‚ñ∂ Patient teaching;
  • Explain all treatments, tests and procedures
  • Make sure the patient and her family clearly understand the prescribed drugs and tell them how to recognize adverse reactions
  • Teach the patient taking estrogen to perform breast examination on self and report lumps right away
  • Emphasize the need for regular gynecological examination
  • If patient takes calcium supplements, encourage adequate fluid intake
  • Report new pain sites especially after trauma
  • Sleep on a firm mattress and avoid excessive bed rest
