üìú HISTORY AND TRENDS IN ORTHOPEDIC NURSING

Orthopedic nurses are people who dedicate their nursing careers to improving the lives of those suffering from both acute injuries and chronic conditions involving the musculoskeletal system. They need to have a grasp of the orthopedic specialty so they can better understand patient needs.

Nursing is ancient profession, but orthopedic nursing dates back to the Victorian England period.

It was founded by DAME AGNES HUNT 1866 - 1948, who herself suffered from septic arthritis in the hip. She spent most of her time trying to improve the lives of wounded soldiers and children who had been crippled by orthopedic conditions

Today the field has expanded to include a variety of subspecialties and practice settings.

The orthopedic surgeon today is concerned with diseases and injuries of the trunk and limbs. His field is not confined to the bones and joints; it includes in addition the muscles, tendons, ligaments, nerves, and blood vessels.

ü©∫ ASSESSMENT OF ORTHOPEDIC PATIENTS;

Examination of a patient with an orthopedic problem includes

History taking (questioning). This must include information about:

  • Reason for consultation (complaints of the patient)
  • Circumstances of the trauma
  • Beginning of the illness
  • Previous treatment and its result
  • Occupation, habits and home circimstances
  • Information about parents and relatives
  • Associated and concurrent illness.

Typical complaints (symptoms) include:

  • Pain
  • Stiffness
  • Swelling
  • Deformity
  • Weakness
  • Instability
  • Change in sensibility
  • Loss of function
‚ùì Ask questions such as;
  • Does the pain change during the course of the day
  • Has the pain been there for a short of long time?
  • Where is the maximal site of pain?
  • Does the pain get better or worse as you move about?

Physical examination (Inspection, Palpation, Auscultation and percussion)

Inspection involves

  • Deformity and shortening; deformity results from misalignment of the bones forming the joint or from alteration of the relationship between the articular surfaces.
  • Swelling and wasting; causes of joint swelling include effusion, thickening of the synovial tissues and of the bony margins of the joints.
  • Scars and wounds
  • Presence or absence of limbs (part of the limb)
  • Skin color
  • Position (active, passive)
  • Posture and gait

Palpation for

  • Local temperature
  • Swelling
  • Painful areas and tenderness
  • Crepitus in the joints and soft tissues

Auscultation and percussion; are needed in the chest and abdomen to determine damages that include

  • Blood level in the cavities
  • Presence of pneumothorax
  • Changes of breathing
  • Function of intestines
  • Movements in the abnormal range of motion (pain, contraction, deformity)

Assessment of the function;

The normal gait, in normal walking each leg geos through a stance phase and swing phase alternately. The rhythmic repetition of such cycles provides grace to the gait. In assessment of the gait, watch how the patient stands and observe his gait on walking. Note that a patient with unstable or painful hip prefers to use a stick in the opposite hand, and tends to shorten the period of weight bearing on the affected limb.

The common pathological gait noticed in patients with orthopedic disorders include;

  • Antalgic gait; occurs in painful condition of lower limb
  • Trendelenburg gait; occurs in an unstable hip due to gluteus medius weakness
  • Stiff hip gait; occurs in ankylosis of the hip
  • Duck wadding (sailor‚Äôs gait) occur in bilateral congenital dislocation of the hip
  • Scissoring gait; occurs in cerebral palsy
  • High stepping gait; occurs in foot drop
  • Circumduction gait; occurs in hemiplegia
  • Charlie ‚Äì chaplain gait; occurs in tibial torsion

Special tests and measuring; such as

  • Positive Thomas test, that indicates a hip flexion contracture, the affected hip cannot be extended to the neutral position
  • Anterior drawer tests; the patient knee ifs flexed to 90 degrees. The doctor sits on the patient‚Äôs foot to stabilize it. The tibia is pulled with the examiner‚Äôs hands toward the examiner. If the tibia slides forward more than a few degrees, there may be a tear in the anterior cruciate ligament
  • Posterior drawer test; the examiner stays seated on the patient‚Äôs foot. The tibia is pushed posteriorly. If the patient‚Äôs tibia glides posteriorly on the femur, the posterior cruciate ligament, a ligament within the knee is likely torn.

üí™ MUSCLE STRENGTH TESTING;

According to the American council of exercise, a muscle strength is the maximal force a muscle or a muscle group can exert during a contraction.

Factors related to muscle strength are:

  1. The size of the muscle fibers
  2. The ability of the nerves to activate the muscle fibers

Terms related to muscle strength

  • Power; the combination of muscular force and speed of movement
  • Endurance; the ability of the muscles to exert force against resistance over a sustained period of time or repetitions

The purpose of muscle strength assessment include;

  1. Diagnostic purposes
  2. Examine the improvement or deterioration in a patients status over time
  3. Determine the extent of strength loss
  4. Determine the need for compensatory measures
  5. Helps in the formation of a treatment plan.

Methods of muscle strength assessment;

I. Mechanical or instrumental methods; in this the following devices are used to measure the strength

  • Cable tensiometer
  • Strain gauge
  • Hand held dynamometer or grip strength dynamometer
  • Modified sphygmomanometer
  • Pinch meter

II. Manual methods; is the most popular way to test strength. The therapist will push the body parts in specific directions, while the patient will resist the pressure.

Muscle strength analysis;

according to the oxford muscle grading system. Its populary used to access muscles strength in individual suffering from neuromuscular conditions. It ranges from grade 0 ‚Äì 5

üìä Oxford Muscle Grading System
Grade Description
Grade 0 the muscle cannot contract
Grade 1 there is contraction but zero range of motion
Grade 2 contraction with movement only in the gravity free plane (horizontally)
Grade 3 contraction with movement in the vertical plane
Grade 4 contraction with movement in the vertical plane + mild resistance
Grade 5 contraction with movement in the vertical + maximum resistance
