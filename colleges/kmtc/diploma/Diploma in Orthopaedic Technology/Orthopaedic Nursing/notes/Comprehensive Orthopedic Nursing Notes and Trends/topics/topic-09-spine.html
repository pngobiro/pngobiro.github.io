ü¶¥ INTERVERTEBRAL DISC PROLAPSE;

Also known as disc herniation or a slipped disc.

It is a medical condition affecting the spine in which a tear in the outer fibrous ring (intervertebral disc) allows a soft, central portion to bulge out beyond the damaged outer rings.

Is relatively avascular structure and the essential minerals and fluids required for regeneration enter the disk passively

Functions of the intervertebral disc;

  1. It supports the axial load on the column that is delivered by the body mass
  2. Assist a limited range of motion at the spine
  3. Shock absorbing system
  4. Serve ligamental functions between vertebral bodies
  5. Assist to keep the normal shape and curvature of each spinal region (cervical, thoracic etc.)

Majority of the spinal disc herniation cases occur in lumbar region (L4 ‚Äì L5 or L5 ‚Äì S1). The second most common site is the cervical region (C5 ‚Äì C6, C6 ‚Äì C7). The thoracic region accounts for few cases.

Causes;

  • Repetitive mechanical activities; frequent bending, twisting, lifting and other similar activities without breaks and proper stretching can leave the disc damaged
  • Traumatic injury to lumbar discs ‚Äì commonly occurs when lifting while bent at the waist, rather than lifting while the back is straight.
  • Living a sedentary lifestyle; individuals who rarely engage in physical activity are more prone to herniated discs because the muscles that support the back and neck weakens which increased strain on the spine.
  • Obesity, spinal degeneration can be quickened as a result of the burden of supporting excess body fat
  • Practicing poor posture; improper spinal alignment while siting, standing or lying down strains the back and the neck.
  • Tobacco abuse; the chemicals commonly found in cigarettes can interfere with the discs ability to absorb nutrients, which results in the weakening of the disc.

Symptoms:

  • Pain may or may not be present in some individuals
  • Loss of control of bladder and bowels
  • Numbness in the genital area and impotence in men
  • Numbness, pins and needles or tingling in one or both arms or legs
  • Pain behind the shoulder blade or in the buttocks
  • Pain running down on one or both arms or legs
  • Weakness involving one or both arms or legs

Diagnosis:

  • MRI lumbar spine
  • Myelogram
  • X- Ray ‚Äì lumbosacral spine
  • CT scan

Management:

  • Bed rest
  • NSAIDS
  • Patient education on proper body mechanics
  • Physical therapy to address mechanical factors and may include modalities to temporarily relieve pain (i.e. traction, electrical stimulation massage)
  • Oral steroids
  • Epidural cortisone injection
  • Weight control
  • Tobacco cessation
  • Lumbosacral back support
  • Anti-depressants
  • Artificial disc replacement is a type of arthroplasty.

Complications:

  • Chronic pain
  • Permanent nerve injury
  • Paralysis

‚ñ∂ 1. Acute pain

Acute pain related to breakdown of continuity of the bone as evidenced by facial expression and verbalization of the patient

GOAL; patient will not feel pain

INTERVENTION;

  • Assess the onset, duration, location, severity and intensity of pain
  • Administer the analgesic according to physician order
  • Provide comfort devices like sand bags for immobilization of affected parts
  • Provide diversion therapy
‚ñ∂ 2. Impaired physical mobility

Impaired physical mobility related to application of traction or cast as evidenced by assessment

GOAL; patient will be able to move the affected area.

INTERVENTION;

  • Provide range of motion exercised to the patient
  • Assist the patient in ambulation after recovery of fracture
  • Provide assistance while using walker or crunches if required
  • Prevent from complication which usually occurs due to immobility
‚ñ∂ 3. Self-care deficit

Self-care deficit related to fracture as evidenced by poor personal hygiene

GOAL; patient will maintain the personal hygiene

INTERVENTION;

  • Assess the need for self-care
  • Encourage the patient or relatives to do self-care activity
  • Head to foot care to be provided to the patient
  • Educate about importance of maintaining personal hygiene
‚ñ∂ 4. Imbalanced nutrition

Imbalanced nutrition less than body requirement related to increased demand of nutrient for bone healing as evidenced by observation

GOAL; maintain the nutritional status of the patient

INTERVENTION;

  • Assess the nutritional status by intake / output chart, biochemical measures, body mass.
  • Encourage the patient to take protein rich diet
  • Plenty of fluids and frequent intake of meal is necessary
  • Try to assess the daily weight of the client.

‚ôø SPINAL CORD INJURY;

This is trauma to the spinal cord resulting in change in the normal motor, sensory or autonomic function.

Etiology of spinal cord injuries;

  • Motor vehicle accidents
  • Violence related injuries
  • Fall
  • Sports related injuries
  • Non traumatic disorders such as cervical spondylosis with myopathy, osteoporosis, bone tumors etc.

Risk factors;

  • Age
  • Gender
  • Alcohol use
  • Drug use

Classification of spinal cord injuries;

  • Simple fracture
  • Compressed vertebral fracture
  • Comminuted fracture
  • Dislocation

Types of spinal cord injuries;

  • Concussion
  • Contusion
  • Compression
  • Laceration
  • Transection ‚Äì complete, incomplete, preserved motor nonfunctional, preserved motor functional
  • Hemorrhage
  • Damage or obstruction of spinal cord blood supply

Clinical manifestations of spinal cord injury;

Spinal shock;

  • 7-20 days up to 3 months
  • Skeletal muscle, bladder, bowel, sexual function and autonomic control
  • Paralysis and flaccidity, absence of sensation, loss of bladder and rectal control, drop in BP
  • Poor venous circulation
  • Poor thermal control
  • Sweating and capillary dilation

Neurogenic shock;

  • Loss of sympathetic outflow; vasodilation, hypotension, bradycardia and hypothermia

Diagnosis of spinal cord injury;

  • Physical examination
  • Radiologic examination ‚Äì chest and spinal x-rays
  • Myelographic examination
  • Arterial blood gases
  • CT scan
  • MRI

Management;

  • Immobilization
  • Decompression
  • Use of corticosteroids
  • Improve the nutritional uptake
  • Monitor Lung function
  • Check and take care of the skin integrity
  • Bowel and bladder management

ü§ï LOW BACK PAIN/DISORDER;

Incidence;

Very common among working group. 90% in people above 45 years old. 80% resolves with conservative treatment (in <3 months). Only 5 ‚Äì 10% may require operation.

Causes of the low back pain;

  • Degenerative (most common)
  • Instability (fracture, spondylolistesis)
  • Organic (tumor, infection)
  • Nerve compression / irritability (PID, root compression)
  • Rule out psychogenic cause (insurance claim, problem with employer etc.)

Referred pain from,

  1. Abdominal cavity; gastritis / peptic ulcer, pancreatitis, cholecystitis
  2. Urinary system, renal calculi, UTI
  3. Pelvic cavity; ovarian cyst, dysmenorrhea
  4. Aorta; aortic aneurysm.

Nature of the pain;

  • Mechanical vs non-mechanical;
  • Referred vs radicular
  • Claudation ‚Äì vascular vs spinal
üìù Note

Mechanical pain includes; muscle strain, ligament sprain, facet joint arthritis, instability ‚Äì spondylosis.

Non- mechanical pain includes; infection (tuberculosis), tumors (benign or malignant tumors)

Investigations:

  • Plain x- rays of the lower back
  • Blood investigations; FULL BLOOD COUNT, ESR, liver function test
  • Renal function tests
  • CT scan ‚Äì better visualization of bone pathology (e.g. cortical destruction) in the case of a fracture of a tumor.
  • MRI; to have better soft tissue visualization of the ligaments, nerves, bone marrow etc.
  • CT myelogram; to check for delineation of neural structures where MRI is not available / contraindicated
  • Bone scan; for suspicious multiple bone involvement e.g. with history of untreated/ treated carcinoma

Treatment:

Mainly conservative;

  • Bed rest / pelvic traction
  • Physiotherapy
  • Back exercise
  • Modification of daily activities
  • NSAIDS for pain relief

Surgery; to correct deformities, fusion and stabilization (instrumentation) of the spinal cord.

Indication for surgery include

  • Failed conservative management, client still has pain
  • Evidence of neurological deficit
  • Spinal instability (excessive spinal motion)
  • Unacceptable deformity (e.g. degenerative scoliosis)
