Section I: Multiple Choice Questions (20 Marks)

1.
You are nurse in surgical ward and prescription of Morphine Is made. Further teaching for patient include?
A. Sit /lie down if symptoms of dizziness occur
B. Not to increase my water intake
C. Take after meals
D. Slowly change my position
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Sit /lie down if symptoms of dizziness occur

Explanation: Morphine, like many opioids, causes vasodilation and orthostatic hypotension, leading to dizziness. Safety is the priority; instructing the patient to sit or lie down prevents falls. Option D is also correct advice (slow position changes), but A is the immediate intervention for symptoms.

2.
A patient asks the nurse to define a hypersensitivity reaction. The nurse begins by telling the patient that a hypersensitivity reaction is also called;
A. Synergistic reaction
B. Antagonistic reaction
C. Drug idiosyncrasy
D. Drug allergy
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Drug allergy

Explanation: Hypersensitivity is an immune-mediated response to a drug, commonly referred to as a drug allergy.

3.
If a patient takes a drug on an empty stomach, the nurse is aware that the drug will be.
A. Absorbed more slowly
B. Neutralized by pancreatic enzymes
C. Affected by enzymes in the colon
D. Absorbed more rapidly
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Absorbed more rapidly

Explanation: Food generally delays gastric emptying and can bind to drugs, slowing absorption. An empty stomach usually allows for faster dissolution and absorption of medication.

4.
In monitoring drug therapy, the nurse is aware that a synergistic drug effect may be defined as.
A. An effect greater than the sum of the separate actions of two or more drugs
B. An increase in the action of one of the two drugs being given
C. A neutralizing drug effect
D. A comprehensive drug effect
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. An effect greater than the sum of the separate actions of two or more drugs

Explanation: Synergism implies that 1 + 1 > 2. The combined effect is greater than if the drugs were given individually.

5.
The nurse correctly administers an intramuscular injection by;
A. Displacing the skin to the side before making the injection
B. Using a 1-inch needle
C. Inserting the needle at a 90-degree angle
D. Using a 25-gauge needle.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Displacing the skin to the side before making the injection

Explanation: This describes the Z-track method, which seals the medication in the muscle tissue, preventing leakage into subcutaneous tissue and reducing pain/staining. (Note: C is also correct for general IM, but A describes a specific correct technique often emphasized for irritating drugs).

6.
When preparing a drug for Subcutaneous administration, the nurse is aware that the usual volume of a drug injected by the SC route is.
A. 2 to 5 mL
B. 3 to 4 mL
C. 0.5 to 1 mL
D. 0.5 mL
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. 0.5 to 1 mL

Explanation: Subcutaneous tissue has fewer blood vessels and cannot accommodate large volumes of fluid. The standard maximum volume is 1 mL (sometimes up to 1.5 mL), making 0.5 to 1 mL the correct range.

7.
Patients receiving sulfasalazine for ulcerative colitis are told that the drug.
A. Is not to be taken with food B
B. Rarely causes adverse effects
C. May cause hair loss
D. May turn the urine orange-yellow in color
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. May turn the urine orange-yellow in color

Explanation: Sulfasalazine and other sulfonamides can discolor urine and skin to an orange-yellow color. This is a harmless side effect but patients should be warned to prevent alarm.

8.
The nurse can evaluate the patient’s response to therapy by asking him if;
A. He completed the entire course of therapy
B. His symptoms have been relieved
C. He has seen any evidence of blood in the urine
D. Has experienced any constipation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. His symptoms have been relieved

Explanation: Evaluation of therapy focuses on the effectiveness of the drug. The primary goal is usually symptom relief or cure. Options C and D evaluate side effects/toxicity, and A evaluates compliance, not the therapeutic response itself.

9.
When reviewing Ms. Robertson’s culture and sensitivity test results, the nurse learns that the bacteria causing Ms. Robertson’s infection are sensitive to penicillin. The nurse interprets this result to mean that.
A. Ms. Robertson is allergic to penicillin
B. Penicillin will be effective in treating the infection
C. Penicillin will not be effective in treating the infection
D. The test must be repeated to obtain accurate results
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Penicillin will be effective in treating the infection

Explanation: In microbiology, "Sensitive" means the bacteria are killed or inhibited by the antibiotic. "Resistant" means the antibiotic will not work.

10.
Mr. Thomas, who is receiving oral penicillin, reports he has a sore mouth. Upon inspection the nurse notes a black, furry tongue and bright red oral mucous membranes. The Doctor is notified immediately because these symptoms may be caused by.
A. A vitamin C deficiency.
B. A superinfection
C. Dehydration
D. Poor oral hygiene
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. A superinfection

Explanation: Broad-spectrum antibiotics like penicillin can kill normal flora, allowing overgrowth of opportunistic organisms like fungi (Candida) or others, leading to oral thrush or "black hairy tongue". This is a superinfection.

11.
The nurse observes a patient taking a cephalosporin for common adverse reactions, which include;
A. Hypotension, dizziness, urticaria
B. Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea
C. Skin rash, constipation, headache
D. Bradycardia, pruritus, insomnia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea

Explanation: Gastrointestinal disturbances (N/V/D) are the most common adverse effects of cephalosporins and most antibiotics.

12.
When giving a cephalosporin by the intramuscular route, the nurse tells the patient that;
A. A stinging or burning sensation and soreness at the site may be experienced
B. The injection site will be red for several days
C. All injections will be given in the same area
D. The injection will not cause any discomfort
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. A stinging or burning sensation and soreness at the site may be experienced

Explanation: Cephalosporins are irritating to tissues and can be very painful upon IM injection. The nurse should prepare the patient for this discomfort.

13.
The physician prescribes 1 g of Mefoxin (cefoxitin) for parenteral administration. Mefoxin is available in a solution of 250 mg/1 mL. What amount of Mefoxin would the nurse prepare?
A. 3ml
B. 4ml
C. 5ml
D. 6ml
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. 4ml

Explanation:
Desired Dose = 1 g = 1000 mg
Concentration = 250 mg / 1 mL
Calculation: 1000 mg / 250 mg = 4 mL.

14.
A patient asks the nurse why the primary health care provider prescribed an antibiotic when she was told that she has a viral infection. The most correct response by the nurse is that the antibiotic may be used to prevent a.
A. Primary fungal infection
B. Repeat viral infection
C. Secondary bacterial infection
D. Breakdown of the immune system
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Secondary bacterial infection

Explanation: While antibiotics do not treat viruses, they are sometimes prescribed prophylactically if the patient is at high risk for developing a secondary bacterial infection (e.g., bacterial pneumonia following flu) due to a weakened immune state.

15.
A patient is receiving erythromycin for an infection. The patient’s response to therapy is best evaluated by;
A. Monitoring vital signs every 4 hours
B. Comparing initial and current signs and symptoms
C. Monitoring fluid intake and output
D. Asking the patient if he is feeling better
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Comparing initial and current signs and symptoms

Explanation: The most objective way to determine therapeutic effectiveness is to compare the baseline clinical presentation (fever, WBC count, pain, drainage) with the current status to see if the infection is resolving.

16.
The nurse monitors the patient taking isoniazid for toxicity. The most common symptom of toxicity is.
A. Peripheral edema
B. Circumoral edema
C. Peripheral neuropathy
D. Jaundice
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Peripheral neuropathy

Explanation: Isoniazid (INH) depletes Vitamin B6 (Pyridoxine), leading to peripheral neuropathy (tingling/numbness). Vitamin B6 is often prescribed concurrently to prevent this. (Note: Hepatotoxicity/Jaundice is also a serious side effect, but neuropathy is the classic dose-related toxicity managed with B6).

17.
The following would the nurse report immediately in a 3-month-old patient receiving ribavirin;
A. Any worsening of the respiratory status
B. Refusal to take foods or fluids
C. Drowsiness
D. Constipation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Any worsening of the respiratory status

Explanation: Ribavirin is often used for RSV. Inhaled ribavirin can cause deterioration of respiratory function. In an infant, worsening respiratory status is a critical emergency.

18.
The nurse would withhold griseofulvin if the patient has.
A. Anemia
B. Respiratory disease
C. Had a recent myocardial infarction
D. Severe liver disease
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Severe liver disease

Explanation: Griseofulvin (antifungal) is metabolized by the liver and can be hepatotoxic. It is contraindicated in patients with hepatocellular failure.

19.
A patient asks how antimalarial drugs prevent or treat malaria. The nurse correctly responds that this group of drug .
A. Kills the mosquito that carries the protozoa
B. Interferes with the life cycle of the protozoa causing malaria
C. Ruptures the red blood cells that contain merozoites
D. Increases the body’s natural immune response to the protozoa
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Interferes with the life cycle of the protozoa causing malaria

Explanation: Antimalarials work by interrupting specific stages of the Plasmodium parasite's life cycle (e.g., inhibiting heme polymerase, affecting DNA replication) within the human host.

20.
At a team conference the nurse explains that the anti-inflammatory actions of the salicylates is most likely due to.
A. Decrease in the prothrombin time
B. Decrease in the productions of endorphins
C. The inhibition of prostaglandins
D. Vasodilation of the blood vessels
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. The inhibition of prostaglandins

Explanation: Salicylates (like Aspirin) inhibit the enzyme cyclooxygenase (COX), which is responsible for the synthesis of prostaglandins (mediators of inflammation, pain, and fever).

Section II: Short Answer Questions (40 Marks)

21.
Rowan, age 28 years, has been prescribed clomiphene to induce ovulation and pregnancy. Judy is very anxious and wants desperately to become pregnant. Her husband, Jim has come to the clinic with her. Outline the information the nurse would include in a teaching plan for Jim and Judy. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Mechanism: Clomiphene stimulates the ovaries to release eggs (ovulation).
  2. Timing of Intercourse: Advise on the fertile window (usually 5-10 days after the last pill) to maximize chances of conception.
  3. Side Effects: Educate on potential hot flashes, mood swings, breast tenderness, and nausea.
  4. Visual Disturbances: Report any blurring or spots immediately (drug may need stopping).
  5. Multiple Births: Explain the increased risk of twins/multiples (approx. 10% risk).
  6. Follow-up: Importance of returning for monitoring (ultrasound/blood work) to check response.
22.
State five health messages a nurse will share with a patient on iron supplements. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Enhance Absorption: Take with Vitamin C rich foods/drinks (orange juice) to increase absorption.
  2. Avoid Inhibitors: Do not take with milk, tea, coffee, or antacids as they decrease absorption (wait 2 hours).
  3. Stool Changes: Warn that stools will turn black or dark green (normal and harmless).
  4. Prevent Staining: If taking liquid iron, use a straw to prevent staining teeth.
  5. Manage GI Side Effects: Take with food if stomach upset occurs (though empty stomach is best for absorption) and increase fluid/fiber for constipation.
23.
Mr. Thomas has cancer of the lung and will begin a treatment regimen with methotrexate. State important pre-administration assessments you would perform before beginning therapy with methotrexate. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Complete Blood Count (CBC): To check for bone marrow suppression (WBC, platelets, Hb) before starting.
  2. Renal Function Tests: (BUN/Creatinine) as the drug is excreted by kidneys and is nephrotoxic.
  3. Liver Function Tests: (AST/ALT) due to risk of hepatotoxicity.
  4. Respiratory Assessment: Check for any pre-existing lung disease (risk of pulmonary fibrosis).
  5. Hydration Status: Ensure adequate hydration to prevent renal crystallization.
  6. Oral Mucosa: Inspect for stomatitis/sores (common side effect).
24.
Explain three (3) biology membranes which limit the distribution of drugs. (6 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Blood-Brain Barrier (BBB): A highly selective semipermeable border of endothelial cells that prevents solutes (and many drugs) in the circulating blood from non-selectively crossing into the extracellular fluid of the central nervous system. Only lipid-soluble or specific transport-carrier drugs can cross.
  2. Placental Barrier: A membrane separating maternal and fetal blood. While it allows nutrients, it restricts some drugs (though many do cross). It metabolizes some drugs, protecting the fetus.
  3. Cell Membranes (Lipid Bilayer): The fundamental barrier. Drugs must be lipid-soluble to pass through the phospholipid bilayer or have specific transport mechanisms to enter cells for action or metabolism.
25.
Explain three (3) patient education items about over the counter drugs. (6 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Read Labels Carefully: Instruct patient to always read dosage instructions, active ingredients, and warnings to prevent overdose (especially with combination products containing Acetaminophen).
  2. Drug Interactions: Advise that OTC drugs can interact with prescription medications (e.g., Aspirin with blood thinners), so always inform healthcare providers about all OTC usage.
  3. Duration of Use: Explain that OTC drugs are for short-term symptom relief. If symptoms persist beyond the recommended time (usually a few days), they should seek medical advice rather than continuing self-medication.
26.
State four (4) factors that contribute to antibiotic resistance. (4 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Overuse/Misuse: Prescribing antibiotics for viral infections (colds/flu).
  2. Incomplete Courses: Patients stopping medication when they feel better, leaving resistant bacteria to survive.
  3. Use in Agriculture: Excessive use of antibiotics in livestock to promote growth.
  4. Poor Infection Control: Spread of resistant strains in hospitals due to poor hygiene.
  5. Self-medication: Buying antibiotics without prescription.
27.
State four contraindications to diuretics. (4 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Anuria: Severe renal failure where kidneys cannot produce urine.
  2. Severe Electrolyte Depletion: Significant hypokalemia or hyponatremia.
  3. Hypovolemia/Severe Hypotension: Further fluid loss could cause shock.
  4. Hypersensitivity: Allergy to sulfonamides (for thiazides/loop diuretics).
  5. Hepatic Coma: Changes in fluid/electrolytes can worsen condition.
28.
Outline five goals of anti-retroviruses (ARVs). (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Viral Suppression: To reduce viral load to undetectable levels.
  2. Immune Restoration: To increase CD4 cell count and restore immune function.
  3. Reduction of Morbidity/Mortality: Prevent opportunistic infections and prolong survival.
  4. Improve Quality of Life: Allow patients to live normal, active lives.
  5. Prevention of Transmission: Reduce the risk of transmitting HIV to others (Treatment as Prevention).

Section III: Long Answer Questions (40 Marks)

29.
Jenny Davis, age 25, is pregnant. Jenny’s primary health care provider tells her that she may not take any medication without first checking with the health care provider during the pregnancy. Jenny is puzzled and questions you about this. Discuss how you would address Jenny’s concerns and the rights of drug administration. (20 marks)
Show Answer

Part 1: Addressing Jenny's Concerns (Why caution is needed) (10 Marks)

  1. Placental Transfer: Explain that most drugs can cross the placenta from the mother's blood to the baby. The baby's liver and kidneys are immature and cannot metabolize or excrete drugs as well as an adult.
  2. Teratogenicity (Birth Defects): Explain that certain drugs can cause physical malformations, especially during the first trimester (organogenesis - organ formation).
  3. Fetotoxicity: Drugs taken later in pregnancy (2nd/3rd trimester) can affect the baby's growth, organ function, or cause withdrawal symptoms after birth.
  4. Physiological Changes: Pregnancy changes how the mother's body processes drugs (absorption, distribution, excretion), potentially requiring dose adjustments to avoid toxicity or under-dosing.
  5. Pregnancy Safety Categories: Explain that drugs are classified (Category A, B, C, D, X) based on safety. The provider needs to check if a drug is safe (Category A/B) or if the benefits outweigh the risks (Category C/D).
  6. Self-Medication Risks: Over-the-counter drugs and herbal remedies often presumed safe can still be harmful in pregnancy.

Part 2: The Rights of Drug Administration (10 Marks)

To ensure safety for both Jenny and the fetus, the nurse observes the following rights:

  1. Right Patient: Verify Jenny's identity using two identifiers (Name, DOB/Number) before administering any drug.
  2. Right Drug: Check the medication label against the order three times. Ensure it is not teratogenic.
  3. Right Dose: Verify the dosage is appropriate for a pregnant woman (considering physiological changes). calculation.
  4. Right Route: Administer via the prescribed route (oral, IV, etc.) for proper absorption.
  5. Right Time: Administer at the correct frequency to maintain therapeutic levels without toxicity.
  6. Right Documentation: Record administration immediately after giving it.
  7. Right Reason: Ensure the medication is indicated for her condition.
  8. Right Response/Evaluation: Monitor Jenny and the fetal heart rate for therapeutic effects and adverse reactions.
  9. Right to Education: Teach Jenny about the drug, why it is needed, and specific side effects to report.
  10. Right to Refuse: Respect Jenny's autonomy if she declines, after explaining the consequences.
30.
Ms. Heggan is 82 years old and lives alone. She is prescribed several drugs by the primary health care provider but is worried about taking the drugs and the side effects that might occur. She comes to the outpatient clinic after 1 week, and you learn that she has not been taking the drugs and is admitted for 24 hours monitoring. Manage the patient using nursing process. (20 marks)
Show Answer

1. Assessment (Data Collection)

  • Subjective: Ask Ms. Heggan specifically why she stopped (cost? confusion? specific fear? difficulty swallowing?). Assess her knowledge of each drug.
  • Objective: Vital signs, physical exam to assess current health status (deterioration due to non-adherence?). Review the list of medications (Polypharmacy check). Check manual dexterity (can she open bottles?) and vision (can she read labels?).

2. Nursing Diagnosis

  • Ineffective Health Management related to fear of adverse effects and complexity of therapeutic regimen.
  • Deficient Knowledge regarding the purpose and safety of prescribed medications.
  • Risk for Injury related to potential drug interactions or untreated medical condition.

3. Planning (Goals/Outcomes)

  • Ms. Heggan will verbalize the purpose and administration schedule of her medications by discharge.
  • Ms. Heggan will agree to a simplified medication schedule.
  • Ms. Heggan will take her medications safely without adverse effects during the 24-hour admission.

4. Implementation (Interventions)

  • Education: Explain each drug simply. Focus on "What it is for" and "How it helps you." Address her specific fears about side effects honestly but reassure her about monitoring.
  • Simplification: Collaborate with the prescriber to reduce the number of drugs or frequency (e.g., once-daily dosing) if possible.
  • Aids: Provide a pill organizer (dosette box) or use large-print labels/color coding if vision is poor. Create a simple calendar chart.
  • Monitoring: During the 24 hours, administer meds and closely monitor for the side effects she fears to reassure her or adjust dosage (start low, go slow for elderly).
  • Support: Identify a neighbor or family member who can check on her, or arrange for a home health visit.

5. Evaluation

  • Ask Ms. Heggan to teach back how she will take her meds at home.
  • Observe if she takes medications willingly during the stay.
  • Check if clinical parameters (BP, sugar, etc.) have stabilized.
