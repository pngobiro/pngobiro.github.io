A. 0.5 ml, left thigh
B. 0.05 ml, left arm
C. 0.5 ml, right thigh
D. 0.05 ml, right thigh
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. 0.5 ml, right thigh

2.
Baby Q, born at home is brought to the clinic for immunization 3 weeks after birth. The antigens to be given to Baby Q on this first visit include:
A. Pentavalent 1, IPV 1
B. OPV Zero, BCG
C. OPV 1, BCG
D. BCG only
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. OPV Zero, BCG

Explanation: At birth (or first contact if missed at birth and still within eligible age), a baby receives BCG and Oral Polio Vaccine (OPV) Birth Dose (Zero dose). At 3 weeks, the baby is not yet eligible for the 6-week vaccines (Penta 1, OPV 1, PCV 1), so they catch up on the missed birth vaccines.

The health status is greatly affected and determined by which of the following:

Correct Answer: B. Socio-economic factors

6.
During a census, the de jure population refers to:
A. All people actually present in a given area at a given time
B. Total population of adults aged 18 years and above
C. People who belong to a given area at a given time by virtue of legal or usual residence
D. Total estimate of the people not present at home during the night of the census.

Correct Answer: C. People who belong to a given area at a given time by virtue of legal or usual residence

Explanation:

  • De jure: Counts people based on their usual/legal place of residence, regardless of where they are on census night.
  • De facto: Counts people where they are physically present on census night (Option A).

7.
The principle of equity in PHC means:
A. Health services made universally accessible to individuals
B. Health services equally distributed according to population data
C. Health services provided at a cost the community can afford always
D. Providing health services based on the needs of the population.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Providing health services based on the needs of the population.

Explanation: Equity means fairness; providing more to those who need more to ensure equal outcomes. It differs from equality (giving everyone the exact same thing). In PHC, it means targeting resources to the most vulnerable or needy populations.

8.
Total fertility rate refers to;
A. The average number of children that would be born to a married woman
B. The child bearing capacity of a woman
C. The number of live births per 1000 women in the reproductive age group in a given year
D. The number of live births per 1000 married women in reproductive age group in a given year.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. The average number of children that would be born to a married woman

Correction: The standard definition of Total Fertility Rate (TFR) is "The average number of children that would be born to a woman (married or unmarried) over her lifetime if she were to experience the exact current age-specific fertility rates through her lifetime." Option A is the closest conceptual fit, though TFR applies to all women, not just married ones. Option C is the definition of General Fertility Rate.

13.

D. Malaria, Onchocerciasis, Schistosomiasis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Malaria, Onchocerciasis, Schistosomiasis

Option A contains Scabies (Water-washed). Option B contains Typhoid (Water-borne). Option C contains Trachoma (Water-washed).

18.

A. Accidental exposure to HIV and they are HIV positive
B. Individuals who have been raped and are HIV negative

19.
People living with HIV/AIDS get opportunistic infection because:
A. The drug used to treat HIV cause infections
B. It is natural for people with AIDS to get opportunistic infection
C. HIV weakens the immunity and makes it difficult to fight disease
D. The HIV gets into the body and mutates into disease causing agents
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. HIV weakens the immunity and makes it difficult to fight disease

Explanation: HIV targets CD4 T-cells, which are crucial for immune response. As the virus destroys these cells, the immune system becomes compromised, allowing infections that a healthy body would normally fight off (opportunistic infections) to take hold.

21.
Primary prevention on health promotion includes:
A. Immunization, screening
B. Screening, diagnostic test
C. Healthy lifestyle habits, immunization
D. Diagnostic tests, heathy lifestyle habits
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Healthy lifestyle habits, immunization

Explanation: Primary prevention aims to prevent the disease from occurring at all. Immunization and healthy lifestyle (diet, exercise) are classic primary prevention strategies. Screening and diagnostic tests (Options A, B, D) are Secondary prevention (early detection).

22.
In a debate of centralization and decentralization, which of the following would you highlight as advantages of centralization in an organization;
A. High cost effectiveness, no duplication of efforts.
B. No duplication of efforts, quicker and better decision making.
C. High cost effectiveness, increased freedom and flexibility.
D. Increased freedom and flexibility of staff, no duplication of efforts
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. High cost effectiveness, no duplication of efforts.

Explanation: Centralization (decisions made at the top) ensures uniformity and prevents different departments from doing the same thing (no duplication), leading to cost effectiveness (economies of scale). Decentralization is associated with "quicker decisions" (Option B) and "freedom/flexibility" (Option D).

23.
You have been invited to conduct a health talk on family planning. Which advantages of intra uterine contraceptive device IUCD would you highlight?
A. Highly effective, offers long term protection, prevents ectopic pregnancy
B. Highly effective, pelvic examination not required, protects endometrial cancer
C. Offers long term protection, requires pelvic examination prevent ovulation
D. Easy to use, highly effective, decreases benefits of breast cancer.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Highly effective, offers long term protection, prevents ectopic pregnancy

Explanation: IUCDs are LARC (Long Acting Reversible Contraceptives) offering protection for 5-10 years. They are >99% effective. By preventing fertilization effectively, they reduce the overall risk of all pregnancies, including ectopic ones (though if a pregnancy does occur, it's more likely to be ectopic, the absolute risk is lower than no method). Option B is wrong because pelvic exam IS required. Option C is wrong because non-hormonal IUCDs do not prevent ovulation.

The correct statement about the emergency contraceptive pills is that they can be:
A. Taken within 5 days of unprotected intercourse
B. Used in replacement for regular oral contraception
C. Spontaneous abortion causing agents once taken after implantation
D. Containing the estrogen hormone in their main content.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Taken within 5 days of unprotected intercourse

Explanation: Emergency Contraceptive Pills (ECPs) like Levonorgestrel or Ulipristal Acetate are effective if taken up to 72 hours (3 days) or 120 hours (5 days) respectively after unprotected sex. 26.
High fiber diet in a diabetic client is recommended because it:
A. Quickens digestion but slows glucose absorption
B. Facilitates quick bowel motility
C. Prolongs digestion and reduces absorption of glucose

31.
Trachoma infection progresses in sequence. Characteristics of second stage infection include;
A. Formation of scars, eyelids turn inwards
B. Presence of follicles, tinny blood vessels crowd at cornea
C. Eye lids turn inwards, presence of tiny blood vessels
D. Whitish scars crowd conjunctiva, eye lids turn outwards.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Presence of follicles, tinny blood vessels crowd at cornea

Explanation: WHO Grading of Trachoma: 1. TF (Trachomatous Inflammation-Follicular): Presence of 5 or more follicles. 2. TI (Trachomatous Inflammation-Intense): Thickening/inflammation obscuring vessels. 3. TS (Trachomatous Scarring): Scars. 4. TT (Trachomatous Trichiasis): In-turned eyelashes. 5. CO (Corneal Opacity): Blindness. Option B describes the active inflammatory stages (TF/TI). Option A describes advanced stages (TS/TT).

32.
Clinical features of pertussis (whooping cough) include:
A. Vomiting, cyanosis, cough
B. Coryza, backing cough, fever
C. Malaise, nasal discharge, fever
D. Fatigue, fever, cough
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Vomiting, cyanosis, cough

Explanation: The classic paroxysmal stage of Pertussis is characterized by rapid coughs followed by a "whoop", often leading to post-tussive vomiting and cyanosis (turning blue) due to lack of oxygen during coughing spells. Option B ("backing cough") is a typo for "hacking" or "barking"? Option A is the most specific presentation.

33.

A. Malaria
B. Tetanus
C. Scabies
D. Measles

Correct Answer: B. Tetanus

Explanation: Tetanus is not contagious (person-to-person). It occurs sporadically when an individual is exposed to spores (soil/injury). Measles occurs in epidemics. Malaria is endemic. Scabies is not a vaccine-preventable disease (immunizable).

34.

A. Hepatis B
B. Hepatis E
C. Hepatitis A
D. Hepatitis C
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Hepatitis A

Explanation: Globally and in many developing regions, Hepatitis A (fecal-oral) is extremely common, often causing sporadic outbreaks or asymptomatic infections in childhood. However, Hepatitis B has a higher chronic burden. In exam contexts asking for "most common" incidence often points to Hep A.

35.
The period that starts when the body defense mechanism has been overpowered by the agent is:
A. Clinical horizon
B. Pathogenesis
C. Early disease stage
D. Advanced disease
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Early disease stage

Explanation: In the Natural History of Disease: - Pre-pathogenesis: Interaction of triad. - Pathogenesis: Begins when agent enters host. - When defenses are "overpowered" and signs appear, it crosses the "Clinical Horizon" into the Clinical Disease stage (Early -> Advanced). The specific transition from subclinical to clinical is the start of the early disease stage.

36.
Prevention and control measures of typhoid fever through attacking the source involves:
A. Vaccination of those at risk, Mass treatment
B. Isolation of infected, maintain hygiene
C. Maintain hygiene, Vaccination of those at risk
D. Notification of cases, Mass treatment.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Notification of cases, Mass treatment.

Explanation: "Attacking the source" in communicable disease control refers to measures applied to the reservoir (the infected human). This includes Notification (surveillance), Diagnosis, Treatment (to cure and stop shedding), and Isolation. Vaccination (A, C) protects the host. Hygiene (B, C) interrupts transmission.

41.
You have been assigned to give a talk on communicable diseases. Elaborate three (3) characteristics of communicable diseases that you will dwell on. (6 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Infectivity: The ability of the agent to invade and multiply in the host.
  2. Pathogenicity: The ability of the agent to cause disease (clinical symptoms) in the infected host.
  3. Virulence: The severity of the disease caused (case fatality rate).
  4. Transmissibility: The capacity to spread from one host to another (contagiousness).

45.
Eradication of poliomyelitis is a global goal in health care. Explain three (3) major interventions that must be applied in the pre-paralytic stage of the disease (6 marks)
Show Answer

Interventions in Pre-Paralytic Stage (Prodromal Phase):

  1. Isolation/Bed Rest: Strict rest is crucial as physical activity during the prodromal phase can increase the severity of paralysis. Isolate to prevent spread.
  2. Supportive Care: Administration of analgesics for pain, antipyretics for fever, and maintaining hydration.
  3. Proper Nursing Care: Changing positions to prevent sores, using a firm mattress to support the spine, and warm moist packs for muscle spasms (Sister Kenny method).
  4. Notification: Immediate reporting of the suspected AFP case for surveillance.
46.
Community action in community diagnosis process improves outcome of care and improves community health. As a community health nurse, formulate five (5) strategies you would utilize in facilitating community action (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Community Dialogue: Holding barazas or meetings to discuss issues and agree on solutions.
  2. Formation of Committees: Establishing Village Health Committees to lead the action.
  3. Capacity Building: Training CHVs and community leaders on health issues and management.
  4. Resource Mobilization: Encouraging community contribution (Harambee) of materials or labor.
  5. Feedback and Sharing: Regularly sharing data/results with the community to maintain motivation and trust.
47.
Community health nurses play an important role in ensuring vaccine effectiveness. Explain four (4) appropriate steps you would apply to prevent program errors related to adverse events following immunization (4 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Proper Reconstitution: Using the correct diluent for each vaccine and using it within the specified time (e.g., 6 hours for BCG/Measles).
  2. Correct Injection Technique: Using the right site, angle, and needle size to prevent abscesses or nerve damage.
  3. Sterile Equipment: Using a new Auto-Disable (AD) syringe for every injection to prevent infection.
  4. Cold Chain Maintenance: Ensuring vaccines are stored at correct temperatures to prevent loss of potency or adverse reactions from frozen vaccines (like Tetanus).
  5. Screening: Checking for contraindications before administration.
48.
Malnutrition among children in low income settlements retard the growth and development of the population. To address this, explain three (3) interventions you would apply as a county public health nurse to reduce cases of malnutrition. (6 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Nutritional Education: Teaching mothers about balanced diet, breastfeeding, and proper weaning foods using locally available and affordable foods (Kitchen gardening).
  2. Growth Monitoring and Promotion: routine weighing of children to detect growth faltering early and intervene.
  3. Supplemental Feeding Programs: Establishing centers for distribution of supplements (like Plumpy'Nut or fortified flour) to identified malnourished children.
  4. Deworming and Vitamin A: Routine administration to improve nutrient absorption and immunity.

49.
You are the nurse in-charge of a sub county hospital. In the recent past, there has been a rise in cases of complicated measles within the hospital. There were 20 deaths and 160 severe non-fatal cases. Many clients/patients have been reported to have deserted the hospital facility in the recent past. You have decided to make decisions that will target the staff related issues to rectify the situation
(a) List four (4) steps in the decision-making process (2 marks)
(b) Explain three (3) staff related issues that can lead to desertion of clients/patients from the unit. (6 marks)
(c) Describe how you would apply the decision-making process to improve provision of the nursing care services in the hospital (12 marks)
Show Comprehensive Answer

(a) Steps in Decision-Making Process (4 Steps)

  1. Identify and define the problem.
  2. Gather data/information and analyze the situation.
  3. Develop/Generate alternative solutions.
  4. Select the best alternative.
  5. Implement the decision.
  6. Evaluate the outcome.

(b) Staff Related Issues Leading to Desertion

  1. Poor Attitude/Customer Care: Rude, arrogant, or indifferent staff chase patients away. Patients feel unvalued and mistreated.
  2. Shortage/Absenteeism: If staff are rarely present or too few, waiting times become unbearable, leading patients to seek help elsewhere.
  3. Incompetence/Poor Quality of Care: If staff lack the skills to manage conditions (like complicated measles) leading to high death rates (20 deaths), the community loses trust and deserts the facility.
  4. Corruption/Informal Charges: Staff asking for bribes or selling free drugs forces poor patients to leave.

(c) Application of Decision-Making Process

  1. Problem Identification: Clearly define the problem as "High mortality from measles and patient desertion due to poor quality care/staff issues." Acknowledge the crisis.
  2. Data Collection/Analysis:
    • Investigate why 20 children died (Audit the files).
    • Interview remaining patients and staff to find out why people are leaving (Exit interviews).
    • Assess staff skills on measles management and cold chain status.
  3. Generate Alternatives:
    • Option A: Fire all current staff and hire new ones (Drastic).
    • Option B: Retrain staff on measles management and customer care (Capacity building).
    • Option C: Increase supervision and disciplinary measures for absenteeism.
    • Option D: Community dialogue to rebuild trust.
  4. Select Best Alternative: Choose a combination of B, C, and D. Retraining is immediate and necessary; supervision ensures compliance; dialogue brings patients back.
  5. Implementation:
    • Organize a CME on Measles management immediately.
    • Set up a strict duty roster and sign-in register.
    • Hold a baraza to apologize and reassure the community.
    • Ensure drugs/supplies are available.
  6. Evaluation:
    • Monitor death rates (aim for zero).
    • Monitor patient attendance numbers (check if they are returning).
    • Get feedback from patients on staff attitude.
