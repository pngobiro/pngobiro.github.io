1.
A nurse is preparing to have a counselling session with a client. Identify physical barriers that may negatively impact on the counselling session:
A. Cultural background
B. Interviewing room
C. Social background
D. Client based Barriers
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Interviewing room

Explanation: Physical barriers refer to environmental factors that interfere with communication. The condition of the interviewing room (e.g., poor lighting, noise, lack of privacy, uncomfortable seating) is a physical barrier.

Why other options are incorrect:

  • A & C: Cultural and social backgrounds are psychosocial or cultural barriers, not physical ones.
  • D: Client-based barriers (like anxiety or pain) are physiological or psychological.
2.
A nurse was health educating a group of clients to consume low salt diet. The target group would be having:
A. Liver disease
B. Burns injury
C. Renal disease
D. Peptic ulcer
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Renal disease

Explanation: In renal disease, the kidneys' ability to excrete sodium is often compromised, leading to fluid retention, edema, and hypertension. A low-salt diet is a standard therapeutic intervention.

Clinical Relevance: While sodium restriction may be used in liver disease (ascites), it is the hallmark dietary restriction for renal insufficiency and failure.

13.
The concept of epidemiological triad is key in prevention of communicable diseases. Components of interrupting transmission include:
A. Chemoprophylaxis, Immunization
B. Chemoprophylaxis, Vector Control
C. Water Sanitation, Immunization
D. Water Sanitation, Vector Control
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Water Sanitation, Vector Control

Explanation: In the chain of infection, "Transmission" is the link between the reservoir and the host.

  • Water sanitation blocks the vehicle of transmission for water-borne diseases.
  • Vector control eliminates the vector (mosquito, fly) that carries the pathogen.
  • Immunization (Host factor) increases resistance but doesn't necessarily stop transmission mechanisms in the environment (though it stops spread).
  • Chemoprophylaxis is usually a host protection or reservoir treatment measure.
Option D contains two direct environmental interventions that interrupt the physical transmission of the agent.

17.
Community diagnosis differ from patient diagnosis in that:

18.

19.
In immunization, herd immunity refers to:
A. When 80% of population is immunized
B. When 50 % of population is immunized
C. When Pregnant mother is immunized before delivery
D. During a disease outbreak
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. When 80% of population is immunized

Explanation: Herd immunity is achieved when a high percentage of the population is immune (usually >80-95% depending on the disease), which interrupts transmission and protects the non-immune individuals. 80% is the standard minimum threshold referenced for many vaccine-preventable diseases.

20.
The indicator used to measure utilization of immunization services is:
A. Immunization coverage rate
B. Drop out from Rota virus
C. Drop rate from Penta 1 to Penta 3
D. Drop out from polio 0 to polio 3
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Drop rate from Penta 1 to Penta 3

Explanation: The Pentavalent dropout rate (Penta 1 minus Penta 3 divided by Penta 1) is the standard WHO indicator for service utilization and continuity. A high dropout rate indicates that the system can reach children initially but fails to retain them for the full course.

45.
In health center B, incidences of anthrax have risen following cases of uninspected meat consumption. Explain three (3) ways of preventing the spread of the disease through attacking the source. (6 Marks)
Show Answer

Attacking the Source (Animal Reservoir):

  1. Vaccination of Animals: Routine immunization of healthy livestock (cattle, sheep, goats) in endemic areas to prevent them from contracting the disease.
  2. Proper Disposal of Carcasses: Carcasses of animals that die from anthrax should be buried deep with lime or incinerated (burned) completely. They should never be opened or slaughtered for meat, as this releases spores.

  4. Meat Inspection: Ensuring all meat for public consumption is inspected by public health/veterinary officers and condemned if infected.

49.
Community dialogue is a forum that draws participants from as many segments of the community as possible to engage on pertinent issues to their health concerns/issues. As a champion for family planning and Adolescent and Youth Sexual and Reproductive Health (AYSRH). Describe the steps you would follow in planning for a successful community dialogue with your target groups. (20 Marks)
Show Comprehensive Answer

Introduction:

Community dialogue is a participatory tool used to discuss and address health issues by bringing community members and leaders together. The planning process involves several systematic steps.

Phase 1: Preparatory/Planning Phase

  1. Problem Identification and Analysis:
    • Review data on Family Planning (FP) uptake and AYSRH issues (e.g., teenage pregnancy rates) in the area.
    • Identify specific gaps (e.g., myths about contraceptives).
  2. Identify Key Stakeholders:
    • Map out who needs to be involved: Local administration (Chiefs), Youth leaders, Religious leaders, Women's groups, Health workers.
  3. Form a Planning Team:
    • Select a small team (including youth representatives) to organize the event.
    • Assign roles (facilitator, note-taker, time-keeper).
  4. Set Objectives and Agenda:
    • Clearly define what the dialogue aims to achieve (e.g., "To dispel 3 common myths about condoms among youth").
    • Develop guiding questions for the discussion.
  5. Logistics and Budgeting:
    • Venue: Select a neutral, accessible, and safe venue (e.g., community hall, school).
    • Date/Time: Choose a time convenient for the target group (e.g., weekends for youth).
    • Materials: Flip charts, markers, IEC materials, refreshments.

Phase 2: Mobilization Phase

  1. Community Entry and Sensitization:
    • Visit local leaders (Chief/Elders) to explain the purpose and seek permission/support.
  2. Inviting Participants:
    • Use diverse channels: Posters, church announcements, youth peer networks, social media.
    • Ensure representation of all target groups (adolescents, parents, leaders).

Phase 3: Implementation Phase (The Dialogue)

  1. Setting the Climate:
    • Welcome and introductions.
    • Ground Rules: Establish rules for respect, confidentiality, and active listening.
  2. Facilitation:
    • Introduce the topic (FP & AYSRH) using a trigger (story, skit, or picture).
    • Guide the discussion using open-ended questions.
    • Ensure everyone participates, especially the youth (avoid dominance by elders).
    • Provide technical facts to correct myths when necessary.
  3. Action Planning:
    • Synthesize the main points discussed.
    • Develop a community action plan: What will be done? By whom? By when? (e.g., "Youth leader to organize monthly FP peer talks").

Phase 4: Post-Dialogue/Follow-up

  1. Documentation and Reporting:
    • Write a report of the proceedings, attendance, and decisions made.
  2. Monitoring and Evaluation:
    • Follow up on the implementation of the action plan.
    • Assess if the dialogue objectives (e.g., increased clinic visits by youth) were met.
