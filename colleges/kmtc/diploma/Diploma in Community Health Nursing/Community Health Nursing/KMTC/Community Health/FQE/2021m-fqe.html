Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions

40 Marks
1.
Some of the external factors that influence the health of a family include:
A. Size, locality, terrain
B. Type of members, biological characteristics, air
C. Housing, locality, climate
D. Values, terrain, climate
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Housing, locality, climate

Explanation: External factors refer to the environmental conditions surrounding the family. Housing (shelter), locality (geographic location), and climate are physical environmental determinants. "Size" and "Type of members" (Option A and B) are internal family structure factors. "Values" (Option D) are internal cultural factors.

2.
The role of the community health nurse that includes sharing of technical information known as:
A. Manager
B. Advisor
C. Evaluator
D. Trainer
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Trainer

Explanation: As a Trainer or Educator, the CHN imparts skills and technical knowledge to community members, health volunteers, or other staff. "Advisor" implies giving guidance, but "sharing technical information" (e.g., teaching how to use a method) aligns best with training/education.

10.
The following are the indications for the introduction of the Inactivated Polio Vaccine (IPV)
A. Increase coverage for polio vaccine, reduce risks of re-emergence of Type II polio outbreak, protect more children
B. Confer immunity to many children, immunize a wide age-group, hasten eradication
C. Reduce risks of re-emergency of type II polio outbreak, interrupt transmission, hasten eradication
D. Interrupt transmission incase of outbreaks, immunize a wider age-group, reduce vaccine related acute flaccid paralysis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Reduce risks of re-emergency of type II polio outbreak, interrupt transmission, hasten eradication

Explanation: IPV was introduced globally as part of the "Polio Endgame Strategy". Specifically, it was to mitigate the risk of cVDPV type 2 after the switch from tOPV to bOPV (removing type 2 from oral vaccine). IPV boosts immunity against all 3 types, especially type 2.

11.
Tetanus toxoid vaccine dose 3 is administered
A. 4 weeks after tetanus toxoid dose 2 and the level of protection is 3 years
B. 6 months after tetanus toxoid dose 2 and the level of protection is 5 years
C. 1 year after tetanus toxoid dose 2 and the level of protection is lifelong
D. 3 years after tetanus toxoid dose 2 and the level of protection is lifelong
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. 6 months after tetanus toxoid dose 2 and the level of protection is 5 years

Explanation: The standard schedule for Tetanus Toxoid (TT) for women of childbearing age is: - TT1: First contact. - TT2: 4 weeks after TT1 (3 years protection). - TT3: 6 months after TT2 (5 years protection). - TT4: 1 year after TT3 (10 years protection). - TT5: 1 year after TT4 (Lifelong protection).

12.
The shake test is designed to determine whether the following vaccines are frozen
A. Tetanus toxoid, Diphtheria tetanus, Hepatitis B
B. Yellow fever, Hepatitis B, Tetanus toxoid
C. Measles, Yellow fever, Hepatitis B
D. Tetanus toxoid, Tetanus Diptheria, Measles
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Tetanus toxoid, Diphtheria tetanus, Hepatitis B

Explanation: The Shake Test is used for freeze-sensitive vaccines that contain aluminum adjuvants. These include T-series vaccines (TT, Td, DPT, DT) and Hepatitis B. If they have been frozen, the adjuvant clumps, and the shake test will show rapid sedimentation. Measles and Yellow Fever (Options B, C, D) are freeze-dried and can be frozen (though diluents cannot).

17.
The term holoendemic refers to:
A. An unusual occurrence in a community of disease, specific health related behavior or other related health events in excess
B. Constant presence of a disease or infectious agent within a given geographic area or population group
C. Constant presence of a disease at high incidence and or prevalence rate and affects all age groups equally
D. The occurrence of a disease that affects a large proportion of the population in a wide geographical area
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Constant presence of a disease at high incidence and or prevalence rate and affects all age groups equally

Clarification: Strictly speaking, "Holoendemic" often implies a high level of infection beginning early in life and affecting most of the child population (e.g., Malaria), leading to a state of equilibrium in adults. However, in exam definitions, it is often distinguished from Hyperendemic by the high intensity across the population.

18.
The following is the dynamic of disease transmission (chain of infection)
A. Susceptible host, source of reservoir, modes of transmission
B. Modes of transmission, susceptible host, source of reservoir
C. Source of reservoir, susceptible host
D. Source of reservoir, modes of transmission, susceptible host
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Source of reservoir, modes of transmission, susceptible host

Explanation: The chain typically flows from: Agent -> Reservoir/Source -> Portal of Exit -> Mode of Transmission -> Portal of Entry -> Susceptible Host. Option D represents the correct sequential logic of transmission.

20.

Section 2: Short Answer Questions

40 Marks
1.
State FIVE characteristics of a healthy community (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Clean and safe physical environment: Access to safe water, sanitation, and housing.
  2. Access to health services: Availability of affordable and quality healthcare.
  3. Community participation: Active involvement of members in decision-making.
  4. Social support networks: Strong social cohesion and support systems.
  5. Economic stability: Employment opportunities and low poverty levels.
2.
Explain THREE water purification methods at the household level (6 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Boiling: Heating water to a rolling boil for at least 1 minute kills most pathogens (bacteria, viruses, parasites). It is simple but requires fuel.
  2. Chemical Disinfection (Chlorination): Adding chlorine tablets or liquid (e.g., WaterGuard) to water. It leaves a residual effect preventing recontamination but may alter taste.
  3. Solar Disinfection (SODIS): Exposing water in clear plastic bottles to direct sunlight (UV radiation and heat) for at least 6 hours. Effective against bacteria and viruses but depends on weather.
3.
Explain TWO responsibilities of health education (4 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Promoting Healthy Behaviors: Empowering individuals and communities with knowledge and skills to adopt lifestyles that prevent disease (e.g., handwashing, exercise).
  2. Creating Awareness: Disseminating information about health issues, available services, and disease outbreaks to ensure the community is informed and can take action.
4.
State FOUR core principles of Primary Health Care (4 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Equitable Distribution: Health services must be shared equally by all people irrespective of their ability to pay.
  2. Community Participation: Involvement of the community in planning, implementing, and maintaining health services.
  3. Intersectoral Coordination: Collaboration with other sectors (agriculture, education, housing) to improve health.
  4. Appropriate Technology: Using methods and technology that are scientifically sound, adaptable to local needs, and acceptable to the community.
5.
State FIVE roles and functions of a community health committee (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Leadership and Governance: Providing oversight and direction for community health activities.
  2. Resource Mobilization: Identifying and mobilizing local resources for health initiatives.
  3. Advocacy: Representing community health needs to the health facility and administration.
  4. Supervision of CHVs: Supporting and monitoring the work of Community Health Volunteers.
  5. Community Mobilization: Organizing community dialogue days and health action days.
6.
Explain THREE types of adverse events following immunization (6 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Vaccine Product-Related Reaction: Caused by the inherent properties of the vaccine itself (e.g., swelling at the injection site from DPT).
  2. Immunization Error-Related Reaction: Caused by errors in handling, prescribing, or administration (e.g., abscess from unsterile needle, wrong diluent).
  3. Immunization Anxiety-Related Reaction: Arising from fear or anxiety about the injection (e.g., fainting/syncope).
7.
State FIVE objectives of epidemiology (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. To identify the etiology (cause) of a disease and risk factors.
  2. To determine the extent of disease found in the community (burden of disease).
  3. To study the natural history and prognosis of disease.
  4. To evaluate new preventive and therapeutic measures and modes of health care delivery.
  5. To provide the foundation for developing public health policy.
8.
State FIVE services available for the older persons of Kenya (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Cash Transfer Programme (Inua Jamii): Financial support for vulnerable elderly persons.
  2. NHIF/Health Insurance Subsidy: Subsidized health insurance coverage for the elderly.
  3. Free Medical Services: In some government facilities for those above a certain age (often 65+).
  4. Institutional Care (Homes for the Aged): Though limited, government and private homes provide residential care.
  5. Priority Service Delivery: dedicated queues or service points in public institutions (banks, hospitals).

Section 3: Long Answer Question

20 Marks
49.
Mr. Zic comes to a health facility X and on examination the clinician has a high suspicion index that he is likely to be suffering from Yellow Fever.
a) Define Yellow Fever disease. (1 mark)
b) State the Standard case definition of Yellow Fever. (3 marks)
c) With an aid of a diagram, explain the THREE transmission cycles of Yellow Fever. (6 marks)
d) Describe the preventive measures for Yellow Fever disease. (10 marks)
Show Comprehensive Answer

a) Definition of Yellow Fever

Yellow fever is an acute viral haemorrhagic disease transmitted by infected mosquitoes, characterized by fever, jaundice (yellowing of eyes), and potential organ failure.

b) Standard Case Definition

Suspected Case: Any person with acute onset of fever, with jaundice appearing within 14 days of onset of the first symptoms.

c) Transmission Cycles

(Note: In an exam, you would draw a diagram linking Monkeys, Mosquitoes, and Humans)

  1. Sylvatic (Jungle) Cycle:
    • Transmission occurs between non-human primates (monkeys) and mosquitoes found in the forest canopy.
    • Humans working in the forest (loggers) are accidentally infected.
  2. Intermediate (Savannah) Cycle:
    • Transmission involves mosquitoes that bite both humans and monkeys.
    • Virus spreads from monkeys to humans or human to human in semi-urban areas. Most common cycle in Africa.
  3. Urban Cycle:
    • Transmission occurs purely between humans and urban mosquitoes (primarily Aedes aegypti).
    • An infected person brings the virus into a densely populated area with high vector density.

d) Preventive Measures

  1. Vaccination:
    • Routine Immunization: Giving YF vaccine at 9 months in endemic counties (e.g., Baringo, Elgeyo Marakwet).
    • Mass Campaigns: In response to outbreaks.
    • Travelers: Mandatory vaccination for travelers to/from endemic zones (Yellow Card). One dose provides lifelong immunity.
  2. Vector Control:
    • Eliminating breeding sites: Draining stagnant water, covering water containers, proper disposal of tires/tins.
    • Larviciding: Applying chemicals to water bodies to kill larvae.
    • Fogging/Spraying: To kill adult mosquitoes during outbreaks.
  3. Personal Protection:
    • Sleeping under Insecticide-Treated Nets (ITNs).
    • Wearing long-sleeved clothing to minimize skin exposure.
    • Using insect repellents.
  4. Surveillance:
    • Early detection and reporting of suspected cases (fever + jaundice).
    • Laboratory confirmation to trigger rapid response.
  5. Social Mobilization/Health Education:
    • Educating the community on symptoms, mode of transmission, and the importance of vaccination and environmental hygiene.
