Module I: Community Health Nursing II

Section I: Multiple Choice Questions (10 Marks)

1.
Preventive services offered at an integrated maternal child health and family planning clinic include:
A. Immunization
B. Good nutrition
C. Personal hygiene
D. Physical exercises
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Immunization

Explanation: Immunization is a primary preventive service (specific protection) offered directly in MCH clinics to prevent specific diseases. While good nutrition and hygiene are promoted, immunization is a direct clinical preventive service provided.

2.
The nursing skill required to create rapport with clients during home visiting is:
A. Interpretational
B. Teaching
C. Interpersonal
D. Observational
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Interpersonal

Explanation: Interpersonal skills (communication, empathy, active listening) are essential for establishing trust and rapport with clients and families in their home environment.

3.
An advantage of integrated maternal child health and family services to the health worker is:
A. Both child and mother are seen in one queue
B. It is economic
C. Broadens knowledge and skill
D. Enables attainment of total health care
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Broadens knowledge and skill

Explanation: For the health worker, integration allows them to manage various conditions (ANC, Immunization, FP) in one setting, broadening their clinical skills. Option A and B are advantages primarily for the client/system.

4.
One of the key functions of community health services is to provide deworming. School children should be dewormed at:
A. Every three months
B. Every six months
C. Every nine months
D. Every twelve months
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Every three months

Explanation: In areas with high prevalence of Soil Transmitted Helminths (STH), WHO and MoH Kenya guidelines often recommend deworming every 3 to 4 months (or at least twice a year) for school-age children to reduce worm burden.

5.
The pillars of Primary Health Care include:
A. Community participation, intersectoral collaboration
B. Appropriate technology, availability
C. Community participation, affordability
D. Intersectoral collaboration, acceptability
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Community participation, intersectoral collaboration

Explanation: The classic pillars of PHC are Community Participation, Intersectoral Collaboration, Appropriate Technology, and Equity (sometimes Support/Logistics). Availability, affordability, and acceptability are often termed principles or elements.

7.

9.
The following are service providers found in the primary care level facilities:
A. Midwives, doctors
B. Radiologists, nurses

Section II: Short Answer Questions (20 Marks)

11.
Highlight five (5) interventions to prevent burns from occurring at home. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Keep matches, lighters, and flammable liquids out of reach of children (store in high, locked cabinets).
  2. Turn pot handles inward when cooking on the stove to prevent children from pulling them down.
  3. Use raised cooking places (jikos) or keep cooking fires in a designated, protected area away from children's play areas.
  4. Do not hold a child while cooking or drinking hot beverages.
  5. Test bath water temperature with the elbow before bathing a child to ensure it is not too hot.
12.
State four (4) principles of Alma Atta declaration 1978. (4 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Equity: Health services should be distributed fairly according to need.
  2. Community Participation: Individuals and families should be actively involved in health planning and implementation.
  3. Intersectoral Collaboration: Health involves sectors beyond medical care (e.g., agriculture, education, housing).
  4. Appropriate Technology: Methods and technology used should be scientifically sound, adaptable, and affordable to the community.
13.
Explain three (3) pillars of vision 2030. (6 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Economic Pillar: Aims to achieve an average economic growth rate of 10% per annum and sustain the same until 2030 to move Kenya into a middle-income country.
  2. Social Pillar: Aims to build a just and cohesive society with social equity in a clean and secure environment. Health falls under this pillar.
  3. Political Pillar: Aims to realize a democratic political system that is issue-based, people-centered, result-oriented, and accountable to the public.
14.
State five objectives of a rural health unit. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. To provide comprehensive health care services (promotive, preventive, curative, and rehabilitative) to the rural population.
  2. To serve as a link between the community and the higher levels of the health system (referral).
  3. To mobilize the community for active participation in health development activities.
  4. To collect and analyze basic health data for planning and monitoring.
  5. To control and prevent endemic diseases prevalent in the rural area (e.g., malaria, waterborne diseases).

Section III: Long Answer Question (20 Marks)

15.
The Kenya health policy 2014 to 2030 gives directions to ensure significant improvement in overall status of health in Kenya by stipulating six policy objectives. Discuss five policy objectives with their specific strategies. (20 marks)
Show Answer

1. Eliminate Communicable Conditions

  • Strategy: Scale up preventive measures such as immunization, vector control (ITNs for malaria), and environmental hygiene.
  • Strategy: Improve early diagnosis and prompt treatment of infectious diseases like HIV/AIDS, TB, and Malaria.

2. Halt and Reverse the Rising Burden of Non-Communicable Conditions

  • Strategy: Promote healthy lifestyles (diet, physical activity) to reduce risk factors like obesity and hypertension.
  • Strategy: Implement screening programs for early detection of cancers, diabetes, and cardiovascular diseases.

3. Reduce the Burden of Violence and Injuries

  • Strategy: Enforce traffic safety laws and promote road safety awareness.
  • Strategy: Strengthen emergency response services and trauma care systems.

4. Provide Essential Health Services

  • Strategy: Ensure universal access to a defined package of essential health services (KEPH) across all life cohorts.
  • Strategy: Strengthen maternal, neonatal, child, and adolescent health services to reduce mortality.

5. Minimize Exposure to Health Risk Factors

  • Strategy: Strengthen health promotion interventions to address behavioral risk factors (tobacco, alcohol).
  • Strategy: Improve environmental health services, including water, sanitation, and hygiene (WASH).

6. Strengthen Collaboration with Private and Other Sectors (Health Systems Strengthening)

  • Strategy: Enhance public-private partnerships (PPP) in healthcare delivery.
  • Strategy: Adopt a "Health in all Policies" approach, working with sectors like agriculture and education.

Module II: Immunization

Section I: Multiple Choice Questions (20 Marks)

1.
Yellow fever vaccine is a live attenuated, it is contraindicated in which conditions:
A. HIV symptomatic child, severe malaria, Leukemia
B. HIV symptomatic child, Tuberculosis, severe disease
C. HIV positive child, Tuberculosis and severe disease
D. HIV symptomatic child, leukemia, yellow fever disease
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. HIV symptomatic child, severe malaria, Leukemia

Explanation: Live attenuated vaccines are generally contraindicated in severe immunosuppression. Symptomatic HIV, Leukemia (cancer of blood cells affecting immunity), and severe acute illness are key contraindications.

2.
Oral polio vaccine 0 (opv0) is recommended to be given within which period:
A. given after the age of 2weeks
B. Given at birth to 2 weeks
C. Given 4 weeks before opv1
D. Not given after the age of one month
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Given at birth to 2 weeks

Explanation: OPV0 is the "birth dose" aimed at early protection. It is most effective when given immediately at birth or within the first 14 days (2 weeks). If missed, it is usually not given after 14 days in routine immunization (though policies vary, B is the standard definition for OPV0).

3.
Tetanus Diphtheria is a vaccine administered in pregnancy to prevent neonatal tetanus, TD 3 is usually given at:
A. 1 year after TT2 and the level of protection is 10yrs
B. Four weeks after TT2 and the level of protection is six months
C. Four months after TT2 and the level of protection is 5 years
D. Six months after TT2 and the level of protection is 5years
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Six months after TT2 and the level of protection is 5years

Explanation: The Tetanus schedule is:
TT1: First contact
TT2: 4 weeks after TT1 (3 years protection)
TT3: 6 months after TT2 (5 years protection)
TT4: 1 year after TT3 (10 years protection)
TT5: 1 year after TT4 (Life long protection)

4.
Vaccines are antigens that are used under strict supervision to a void wastage, what do we understand vaccine wastage rate is?
A. A rate showing proportion of vaccine received from manufacture against amount of vaccine ordered
B. A rate showing the amount of vaccine lost for various reason; poor vaccination technique, cold chain breakdown, expired vaccine
C. A rate showing how much vaccine has been used during immunization
D. A rate showing amount of vaccine that have been returned to the fridge compare to what is used
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. A rate showing the amount of vaccine lost for various reason; poor vaccination technique, cold chain breakdown, expired vaccine

Explanation: Wastage rate calculates the percentage of vaccine doses supplied that were not administered to a child. It accounts for open vial wastage (discarded after session) and closed vial wastage (expiry, breakage).

5.
Vaccines needs to be arranged according to heat sensitivity, which antigens are more heat sensitive:
A. Pentavalent and polio vaccine
B. Measles vaccine and pneumococcal vaccine
C. BCG and tetanus vaccine
D. Measles vaccine polio vaccine and BCG vaccine
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Measles vaccine polio vaccine and BCG vaccine

Explanation: OPV is the most heat sensitive, followed by Measles and BCG (lyophilized forms). These are typically kept on the freezer shelves or coldest part of the fridge. Tetanus and Pentavalent are freeze-sensitive but more heat stable than OPV.

6.
You are the nurse on duty in MCH/FP clinic when an orphan is brought. Mother Child Booklet show no documentation of immunization however on examination there is a BCG scar at left mid arm. The child is 18 months which immunization will you administer:
A. Birth Polio, OPV 1, BCG Penta, Pneumo1 and Measles 1&2
B. Polio 1 Penta 1, Pneumo 1, And Measles 1
C. Polio 1, Penta 1, Pneumo 1, Rota 1 And Measles 1
D. Polio1, Penta 1, Pneumo1, Rota 1, Measles1
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Polio1, Penta 1, Pneumo1, Rota 1, Measles1

Explanation: 1. BCG Scar present: Do NOT repeat BCG. 2. No records: Assume no other vaccines given. 3. Age 18 months: Eligible for all missed vaccines. 4. Start schedule: Give OPV1, Penta 1, PCV 1, Rota 1 (if age appropriate, usually cut off is 24 weeks but in catch-up campaigns sometimes given, though standard guidelines restrict Rota >2 years. WHO says Rota restricted for age. However, standard catch up for <5 often involves starting the series). Importantly, Measles 1 is due at 9 months, so give Measles 1 immediately. 5. Note: Option C and D are identical. We administer the first dose of the multi-dose series plus the measles.

8.
The commonest type of human papilloma virus that causes 70% of the cervical cancers are known as:
A. Type 6 and 8
B. Type 11 and 16
C. Type 11 and 18
D. Type 16 and 18
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Type 16 and 18

Explanation: HPV Types 16 and 18 are the high-risk oncogenic types responsible for approximately 70% of cervical cancer cases globally. Types 6 and 11 cause genital warts.

9.
In relation to adverse events following immunization, programmatic error is:
A. An event that has no causal association between the immunization and the medical condition of the child or woman.
B. An error in handling, reconstitution or administration of the vaccine.
C. Individual response to the vaccine itself.
D. The cause of the event cannot be determined.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. An error in handling, reconstitution or administration of the vaccine.

Explanation: Programmatic errors (operational errors) are caused by human or system errors in the preparation, handling, storage, or administration of the vaccine (e.g., non-sterile injection, wrong diluent, wrong dose).

10.
Shake test is usually used in testing which vaccines;
A. Measles
B. BCG
C. Tetanus Diptheria
D. Rot virus
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Tetanus Diptheria

Explanation: The Shake Test is used to determine if freeze-sensitive vaccines containing aluminum adjuvants (like Tetanus, Pentavalent, PCV) have been damaged by freezing. It is not used for live lyophilized vaccines like Measles or BCG.

Section II: Short Answers Question (20 Marks)

11.
Outline five (5) health information a nurse would give to a mother before and after immunization. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Vaccine given: Name of the vaccine(s) administered today and disease prevented.
  2. Side effects: Possible expected reactions (fever, pain at site) and how to manage them (e.g., breastfeed, light clothing).
  3. Next visit: The exact date to return for the next dose.
  4. Importance: Why completing the schedule is crucial for full protection.
  5. Card keeping: Keep the MCH booklet safe and bring it every visit.
12.
State five (5) conditions for not discarding a non-lyophilized vaccine after vaccination session. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Based on the WHO Multi-Dose Vial Policy (MDVP), opened vials of liquid (non-lyophilized) vaccines (e.g., OPV, Penta, TT) can be used in subsequent sessions if:

  1. The expiry date has not passed.
  2. The vaccine has been stored under appropriate cold chain conditions (+2°C to +8°C).
  3. The vaccine vial septum has not been submerged in water.
  4. Aseptic technique was used to withdraw all doses.
  5. The Vaccine Vial Monitor (VVM), if present, has not reached the discard point.
13.
You are in-charge of immunization in service delivery point, you ordered and received 200 doses of BCG while you immunize 150 children, calculate your wastage rate and wastage factor. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Given:

  • Doses Supplied (Received) = 200
  • Doses Administered (Children Immunized) = 150

1. Wastage Rate Calculation:

$$Wastage Rate = \frac{\text{Doses Supplied} - \text{Doses Administered}}{\text{Doses Supplied}} \times 100$$

$$Wastage Rate = \frac{200 - 150}{200} \times 100 = \frac{50}{200} \times 100 = 25\%$$

2. Wastage Factor Calculation:

$$Wastage Factor = \frac{100}{100 - \text{Wastage Rate}}$$

$$Wastage Factor = \frac{100}{100 - 25} = \frac{100}{75} = 1.33$$

Answer: Wastage Rate is 25% and Wastage Factor is 1.33.

14.
Explain five (5) strategies used to reduce drop-outs. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Health Education: Educating parents on the schedule and importance of completion.
  2. Defaulter Tracing: Using community health volunteers to locate children who miss appointments.
  3. Friendly Services: Reducing waiting times and ensuring staff are respectful to mothers.
  4. Accessibility: Conducting outreach services in hard-to-reach areas.
  5. Reminder Systems: Writing return dates clearly on cards and using SMS reminders or community mobilization.

Section III: Long Answer Question (20 Marks)

15.
The goal of immunization is to vaccinate every eligible child hence acquire immunity against childhood immunisable diseases. Discuss five (5) characteristics of an ideal vaccine and immunization schedule for a child under one year in Kenya. (20 marks)
Show Answer

Part A: Characteristics of an Ideal Vaccine (10 Marks)

  1. Effective (Immunogenic): Must produce a strong and long-lasting immune response (high efficacy) to protect against the disease.
  2. Safe: Must not cause significant disease or severe side effects. Risks should be minimal compared to benefits.
  3. Stable: Should retain potency during transport and storage (thermostable), resisting heat or freezing damage.
  4. Affordable (Cheap): Should be cost-effective to manufacture and distribute to allow widespread use in developing countries.
  5. Easy to Administer: Preferably non-invasive (e.g., oral or nasal) or requiring few doses to achieve immunity (single dose is ideal).
