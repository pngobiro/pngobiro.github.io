Part A: Community Health - Multiple Choice Questions

1.
The sustainable development goal (SDG) responsible for health is?
a) SDG No. 1
b) SDG No. 3
c) SDG No. 7
d) SDG No. 8
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) SDG No. 3

Explanation: SDG 3 is titled "Good Health and Well-being" and aims to ensure healthy lives and promote well-being for all at all ages.

Why other options are incorrect:

  • a) SDG No. 1: No Poverty.
  • c) SDG No. 7: Affordable and Clean Energy.
  • d) SDG No. 8: Decent Work and Economic Growth.
2.
Socio-economic factors that have bearing on health include:
a) Education, live birth, blood test
b) Blood test, housing, beliefs
c) Housing, education, food
d) Fertility, beliefs, live birth
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Housing, education, food

Explanation: Socio-economic factors (Social Determinants of Health) include conditions in which people are born, grow, live, work, and age. Housing, education levels, and access to food (nutrition/security) are classic socio-economic determinants.

Why other options are incorrect:

  • a, b, d: Include medical/clinical factors (blood test, live birth, fertility) which are biological or clinical indicators, not strictly socio-economic factors.
3.
The principles of primary health care are:
a) Availability, accessibility, universal
b) Accessibility, availability, participation, accepted
c) Accessibility, availability, affordable
d) Sustainability, universal, affordable
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Accessibility, availability, affordable

Explanation: The classic principles of PHC (the "4 As" + Participation) usually include Accessibility, Affordability, Availability, and Acceptability (along with Community Participation). Option C captures the core logistical and economic principles best among the choices.

Note: While 'Participation' is a key pillar, option B's phrasing "accepted" typically refers to Acceptability. Option C is the most standard grouping of the "A" principles.

4.
The primary health care elements that were added to the ones identified at Alma Ata conference in Kenya are:
a) Health education
b) Nutrition
c) Mental health
d) Maternal and child health
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Mental health

Explanation: The original Alma Ata declaration (1978) listed 8 essential elements (Education, Water/Sanitation, Nutrition, MCH/FP, Immunization, Endemic diseases, Treatment of common diseases, Essential drugs). Kenya expanded these to include Mental Health and Dental Health.

Why other options are incorrect:

  • a, b, d: Health education, Nutrition, and MCH were all part of the original 8 elements.
5.
Activities of primary prevention of disease includes?
a) Screening, mass treatment and campaigns
b) Follow up, screening
c) Case finding, immunization
d) Medical checkup, adequate nutrition
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Medical checkup, adequate nutrition

Explanation: Primary prevention aims to prevent disease before it occurs. This includes health promotion (adequate nutrition, health education) and specific protection (immunization). While option C contains immunization, "case finding" is typically secondary prevention. Option D combines general health promotion (nutrition) and monitoring (checkups to maintain health).

Analysis of Options:

  • a) Screening: Secondary prevention (early detection).
  • b) Follow up: Tertiary prevention; Screening is Secondary.
  • c) Case finding: Secondary prevention.
  • d) Nutrition: Primary prevention (Health Promotion).

Note: Immunization is the classic example of Primary Prevention (Specific Protection). However, since option C pairs it with "Case finding" (Secondary), option D is often chosen in these exams if "Medical checkup" implies "well-baby clinic" or routine monitoring, combined with nutrition. If strict definitions apply, Immunization is the strongest Primary intervention.

6.
The pre-visiting activities for home visiting are:
a) Observing the home environment
b) Explaining the reasons for visit
c) Explaining the client
d) Gathering information
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Gathering information

Explanation: The pre-visit phase involves preparation before making contact. This includes reviewing client records, gathering necessary equipment, contacting the client to schedule (if applicable), and gathering information about the client's history and location.

Why other options are incorrect:

  • a, b: Observing the environment and explaining the reason for the visit occur during the visit (entry phase).
7.
The key players in the implementation of Kenya essential package of health (KEPH) in the community are:
a) Community owned resource person (CORPs), Community Health Extension Workers (CHEWs)
b) Health management team, Community Health Extension Workers.
c) Community owned resource persons, non-governmental organization.
d) Community Health Extension workers, Faith-Based Organizations.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Community owned resource person (CORPs), Community Health Extension Workers (CHEWs)

Explanation: At Level 1 (Community Strategy) of KEPH, the primary implementers are the Community Health Extension Workers (CHEWs) (now often called Community Health Assistants/Officers) who supervise Community Health Volunteers (CHVs) and CORPs.

8.
The Kenya health strategies investment plan was initiated to run from:
a) 2015- 2020
b) 2012- 2030
c) 2015- 2030
d) 2012- 2020
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) 2012- 2030

Explanation: This likely refers to the overarching Kenya Health Policy 2014-2030 which aligns with Vision 2030. While Investment Plans (KHSSP) are typically 5-year cycles (e.g., 2014-2018), the long-term strategic horizon is often cited as up to 2030.

9.
When health care is geographically financially within easy reach to the entire community the services is said to be:
a) Available to the community
b) Acceptable to the community
c) Accessible to the community
d) Affordable to the community
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Accessible to the community

Explanation: Accessibility encompasses geographical (distance) and financial (affordability) reach. It means services are located where people can get to them and priced so people can pay for them.

Why other options are incorrect:

  • a) Available: Means the services exist and function (hours, staff).
  • b) Acceptable: Means the culture and methods are approved by the community.
  • d) Affordable: Refers only to cost, not geography.
10.
Indicate whether the following statements are true or false:

i) Monitoring and evaluation is not necessary in Mobile clinic services.

Show Answer

Answer: False. M&E is critical for all health services to assess coverage, effectiveness, and resource utilization.

ii) Laboratory services is a priority during outreach services.

Show Answer

Answer: True. Basic diagnostic services (RDTs for Malaria, HIV testing, HB levels) are essential priorities to provide effective treatment during outreaches.

Part A: Community Health - Short Answer Questions

Q1.
State any five (5) principles of home visiting. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Planning: Visits should be planned with a clear purpose and objectives.
  2. Flexibility: The nurse should be flexible to adapt to the family's situation and needs.
  3. Technical competence: The nurse must possess the necessary skills and knowledge.
  4. Safety: Ensuring the safety of both the nurse and the client/family.
  5. Confidentiality: Respecting the privacy of the family's information.
  6. Documentation: Recording findings and interventions accurately.
  7. Regularity: Visits should be regular based on client needs.
  8. Evaluation: Outcomes of the visit should be evaluated against objectives.
Q2.
Explain three (3) pillars of vision 2030. (6 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Economic Pillar: Aims to achieve an average economic growth rate of 10% per annum and sustain the same until 2030 to move Kenya into a middle-income country.
  2. Social Pillar: Aims to build a just and cohesive society with social equity in a clean and secure environment. This includes sectors like Health, Education, and Housing.
  3. Political Pillar: Aims to realize a democratic political system that is issue-based, people-centered, result-oriented, and accountable to the public.
Q3.
State the KEPH (Kenya Essential Package of Health) life cycle cohorts. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Cohort 1: Pregnancy and the newborn (up to 2 weeks).
  2. Cohort 2: Early Childhood (2 weeks to 5 years).
  3. Cohort 3: Late Childhood (6 to 12 years).
  4. Cohort 4: Adolescents and Youth (13 to 24 years).
  5. Cohort 5: Adulthood (25 to 59 years).
  6. Cohort 6: Elderly (60+ years).
Q4.
State four (4) key implementers of primary health care in Kenya. (4 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Community Health Volunteers (CHVs): Frontline workers at the household level.
  2. Community Health Extension Workers (CHEWs) / CHAs: Technical supervisors of CHVs.
  3. Health Facility Management Committees (HFMC): Community representatives overseeing facility operations.
  4. Community Health Committees (CHCs): Provide oversight at the community unit level.
  5. County and Sub-County Health Management Teams (CHMT/SCHMT): Provide leadership and resources.

Part A: Community Health - Long Answer Questions

Q1.
You have been posted to county X where you are to initiate health programme.

a) State any three (3) objectives of school health programme. (3 marks)

Show Answer
  • To promote the health and well-being of school-going children.
  • To detect and treat health problems early (screening).
  • To create a healthy and safe school environment.
  • To provide health education and foster healthy habits.
  • To prevent the spread of communicable diseases within the school.

b) State any five (5) components of school health programme. (5 marks)

Show Answer
  • Health Education: Instruction on hygiene, nutrition, sexual health.
  • Health Services: Screening, deworming, immunization, first aid.
  • Healthy School Environment: Safe water, sanitation (WASH), safe structures.
  • Nutrition Services: School feeding programs, micronutrient supplementation.
  • Physical Education: Promotion of physical activity and sports.
  • Counseling and Social Support: Mental health support and guidance.

c) Explain role of a nurse in school health programme. (12 marks)

Show Answer
  • Care Provider: Treating minor ailments and injuries (First Aid).
  • Educator: Providing health education to students, teachers, and parents on hygiene, nutrition, and disease prevention.
  • Screener/Assessor: Conducting routine health screenings (vision, hearing, dental, growth monitoring) to identify defects early.
  • Advocate: Advocating for a safe school environment (safe water, adequate latrines) and policy implementation.
  • Counselor: Providing guidance and counseling to students on social, psychological, and reproductive health issues.
  • Liaison: Linking the school with the healthcare system/facility for referrals and specialized care.
  • Administrator/Manager: Planning, organizing, and evaluating school health activities and keeping records.
  • Consultant: Advising school administration on health-related matters and policies.

Part B: Nutrition - Multiple Choice Questions

1.
Which one of the following is a function of carbohydrates:
a) Protein sparing with adequate supply protein is not used for energy.
b) Synthesis of enzymes, plasma proteins, immunoglobulin and some hormones.
c) Participation in all the chemical reactions that occur in the body.
d) It is a catalyst in the metabolism of amino acids.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Protein sparing with adequate supply protein is not used for energy.

Explanation: The primary function of carbohydrates is energy provision. When sufficient carbohydrates are available, the body uses them for fuel, "sparing" protein so it can be used for its primary functions like tissue repair and growth rather than being burned for energy.

2.
Fat soluble vitamins include:
a) Vitamin E, K, C and D
b) Vitamin A,D, E and K
c) Vitamin A, C, D and K
d) Vitamin A, D, E and C
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Vitamin A, D, E and K

Study Tip: Remember "ADEK" for fat-soluble vitamins. Vitamins B and C are water-soluble.

3.
In starvation, the first nutrient to be depleted in the body is:
a) Fats
b) Proteins
c) Carbohydrates
d) Vitamins
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Carbohydrates

Explanation: The body first uses circulating blood glucose, then glycogen stores (carbohydrates) in the liver and muscles. Once these are depleted (usually within 24 hours), the body shifts to burning fats (ketosis) and eventually proteins.

4.
The main function of vitamin E is:
a) Immune regulation
b) Anti-oxidant activity
c) Hepatic microsomal carbolylation.
d) Maintenance of structure and function of epithelia.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Anti-oxidant activity

Explanation: Vitamin E (Tocopherol) is a powerful antioxidant that protects cell membranes from oxidative damage by free radicals.

5.
Which of the following is destroyed by heating and freezing:
a) Vitamin B2
b) Pantothenic acid
c) Nicotinic acid
d) Vitamin B1
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Vitamin B1 (Thiamine)

Explanation: Vitamin B1 is heat labile (sensitive to heat) and can be destroyed by cooking. Vitamin C is also very sensitive to heat and oxidation. Among the options provided, Thiamine (B1) is known to be unstable at high temperatures and high pH.

6.
Which one of the following is a function of calcium:
a) Hardening of bone and teeth
b) Maintenance of electrolyte balance in the body.
c) Transmission of nerve impulse.
d) Helps in muscle contraction.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Hardening of bone and teeth

Explanation: While Calcium is involved in nerve transmission (C) and muscle contraction (D), its primary structural role where 99% of body calcium is stored is in the mineralization (hardening) of bones and teeth.

7.
Deficiency of iodine results to:
a) Anemia
b) Goiter
c) Wernicke encephalopathy
d) Korsakoff syndromes
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Goiter

Explanation: Iodine is essential for thyroid hormone synthesis. Deficiency causes the thyroid gland to enlarge (Goiter) in an attempt to capture more iodine.

8.
The characteristics of marasmus include:
a) Oedema in the legs, feet and lower arms and hands.
b) The face looks swollen and round (moon face)
c) Severe wasting because the body breaks down muscles.
d) The child has less hair than usual, at pulls out easily and may be pale.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Severe wasting because the body breaks down muscles.

Explanation: Marasmus is caused by a severe deficiency of calories and protein (starvation), leading to gross wasting of muscle and fat (old man appearance). Oedema and moon face are characteristic of Kwashiorkor.

9.
Wasting is:
a) Having a low weight for length (WFLz) of less than – 2Z scores.
b) Having a weight for age (WFAz) Z – score of less than 2Z scores
c) Having a length height for age (hFaz) Z-score of less than -2 Z scores.
d) Having a low weight for length/height (WFLz) of less than – 3Z scores
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Having a low weight for length (WFLz) of less than – 2Z scores.

Explanation: Moderate Wasting is defined as Weight-for-Height/Length between -2 and -3 Z-scores. Severe Wasting is < -3 Z-scores. Option A correctly defines the threshold for wasting generally (below -2SD). Option C describes stunting. Option B describes underweight.

10.
First step in budgeting is:

Part B: Nutrition - Short Answer Questions

Q1.
State five (5) principles of meal planning. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Adequacy: Providing sufficient energy and essential nutrients.
  2. Balance: Consuming foods from all food groups in correct proportions.
  3. Calorie Control (Energy Control): Balancing energy intake with energy expenditure.
  4. Moderation: Avoiding excessive consumption of fat, salt, and sugar.
  5. Variety: Eating different foods within each food group to ensure broad nutrient intake.
Q2.
List four (4) methods of nutritional assessment. (4 marks)
Show Answer

Mnemonic: ABCD

  1. Anthropometric methods: Height, weight, MUAC, BMI.
  2. Biochemical methods: Lab tests (e.g., hemoglobin, serum albumin).
  3. Clinical methods: Physical exam for signs of deficiency (e.g., pallor, edema).
  4. Dietary methods: 24-hour recall, food frequency questionnaires.
Q3.
State four (4) pillars of food security. (4 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Availability: Sufficient quantities of food available on a consistent basis.
  2. Access: Having sufficient resources to obtain appropriate foods for a nutritious diet.
  3. Utilization: Appropriate use based on knowledge of basic nutrition and care, as well as adequate water and sanitation.
  4. Stability: Availability and access must be consistent over time (no risk of losing access due to shocks).
Q4.
State four (4) reasons for therapeutic diet. (4 marks)
Show Answer
  1. To maintain good nutritional status.
  2. To correct nutritional deficiencies (e.g., iron for anemia).
  3. To provide rest to a specific organ (e.g., low fat for gall bladder disease).
  4. To adjust the body's weight (gain or loss).
  5. To facilitate digestion and absorption (e.g., soft diet).
Q5.
With an aid of a diagram, illustrate the nutrition infection cycle. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Description of the Cycle:

The cycle illustrates the bidirectional relationship between malnutrition and infection.

      [ Malnutrition ]
           ^      |
           |      v
(Decreased)|   (Lowered Immunity)
(Appetite )|      |
           |      v
      [ Infection  ]
  1. Inadequate Dietary Intake: Leads to weight loss, growth faltering, and lowered immunity.
  2. Disease/Infection: Lowered immunity makes the body more susceptible to infections.
  3. Impact of Infection: Infection causes loss of appetite (anorexia), malabsorption of nutrients, altered metabolism, and increased nutrient needs.
  4. Result: This worsens the malnutrition, creating a vicious cycle.

Part B: Nutrition - Long Answer Questions

Q1.
Master V aged 9 months is admitted in pediatric ward with severe acute malnutrition (SAM).

a) State five (5) advantages of breast feeding to the baby. (5 marks)

Show Answer
  • Nutritional value: Contains all essential nutrients in the right proportions.
  • Immunity: Provides antibodies (IgA) protecting against infections.
  • Digestibility: Easily digested and absorbed by the infant's immature gut.
  • Bonding: Promotes emotional bonding between mother and baby.
  • Temperature: Always at the right temperature.
  • Cost-effective: It is free and requires no preparation.

b) State the admission criteria for the inpatient management of Master V. (5 marks)

Show Answer

Admission to inpatient care is required if the child has SAM (W/H < -3 SD or MUAC < 11.5cm or bilateral pitting edema) AND any of the following medical complications:

  • Generalized edema (+++).
  • Poor appetite (failed appetite test).
  • High fever / Hypothermia.
  • Severe dehydration.
  • Severe anemia.
  • Respiratory infection/pneumonia.
  • Unconscious or convulsions.

c) Describe the management for Master V. (10 marks)

Show Answer

Management follows the WHO 10 Steps for SAM:

Phase 1: Stabilization (Days 1-7)

  1. Treat/prevent Hypoglycemia: Give 10% glucose or F-75 immediately. Feed every 2-3 hours.
  2. Treat/prevent Hypothermia: Keep warm (Kangaroo care), cover head, maintain room temp.
  3. Treat/prevent Dehydration: Use ReSoMal (Rehydration Solution for Malnutrition), not standard ORS.
  4. Correct Electrolyte Imbalance: Give Potassium and Magnesium (usually in F-75). Restrict Sodium.
  5. Treat Infection: Broad-spectrum antibiotics (routine) and measles vaccine if due.
  6. Correct Micronutrient Deficiencies: Give Vitamin A, Folic acid, Zinc, Copper. (Iron is withheld in this phase).
  7. Cautious Feeding: Start with F-75 formula (low protein/sodium) to prevent refeeding syndrome. Small, frequent feeds.

Phase 2: Rehabilitation (Weeks 2-6)

  1. Rebuild Wasted Tissues: Transition to F-100 or RUTF (Ready-to-Use Therapeutic Food). Increase calories and protein. Introduce iron.
  2. Stimulation: Play therapy and emotional support.
  3. Prepare for Follow-up: Education on nutrition, hygiene, and discharge planning when weight gain is achieved.
