1.
The following are used for community diagnosis
a) Maps, weighing scales, specimen bottles, questionaires
b) Stethoscopes, maps, questionaires, specimen bottles
c) Record books, weighing scales, questionaires, specimen bottles
d) Thermometer, maps, weighing scales, specimen bottles
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Maps, weighing scales, specimen bottles, questionaires

Explanation: Community diagnosis utilizes tools that assess the population and environment. Maps (demography), questionnaires (survey), weighing scales (nutritional assessment), and specimen bottles (epidemiological sampling) are standard tools. Clinical tools like stethoscopes (B) and thermometers (D) are for individual patient diagnosis.

b) Cold accumulation in a vaccine Expiry time of a vaccine

Correct Answer: c) Sedimentation rate of a vaccine

Section 3: Long Answer Question

20 Marks
9.
You are a newly posted nurse at Iwenzo sub-county health facility. During your orientation, you were given some statistics that in the last 2 weeks there cases of Yellow Fever reported in the facility.
a) Outline the transmission process of Yellow Fever. (6 marks)
b) Explain THREE signs and symptoms of a patient suffering from Yellow Fever. (6 marks)
c) Describe the preventive management of Yellow Fever. (8 marks)
Show Comprehensive Answer

a) Transmission Process of Yellow Fever

Yellow Fever is transmitted by the bite of an infected mosquito (Aedes species). There are three transmission cycles:

  1. Sylvatic (Jungle) Cycle: Transmission occurs between monkeys and mosquitoes in the forest. Humans (loggers/workers) are infected when they enter the forest and are bitten.
  2. Intermediate (Savannah) Cycle: Transmission occurs between mosquitoes and both humans and monkeys in semi-urban areas. This is the most common cycle in Africa.
  3. Urban Cycle: An infected human brings the virus to a densely populated area. Urban mosquitoes (Aedes aegypti) bite the infected person and transmit it to other humans.

b) Signs and Symptoms

  1. Jaundice: Yellowing of the skin and eyes due to liver damage (giving the disease its name).
  2. Hemorrhagic Signs: Bleeding from the nose, mouth, eyes, or stomach (vomiting blood - "coffee ground" vomit).
  3. High Fever and Shock: Sudden onset of high fever, often accompanied by chills, severe headache, and back pain, which can progress to shock and organ failure in the toxic phase.

c) Preventive Management

  1. Vaccination:
    • Routine immunization of children at 9 months in endemic areas.
    • Mass vaccination campaigns during outbreaks.
    • Mandatory vaccination for travelers to endemic zones. One dose provides lifelong immunity.
  2. Vector Control:
    • Elimination of breeding sites (draining stagnant water, covering containers).
    • Spraying (IRS or fogging) to kill adult mosquitoes.
    • Larviciding water bodies.
  3. Personal Protection:
    • Sleeping under Insecticide Treated Nets (ITNs).
    • Wearing long-sleeved clothes.
    • Using insect repellents.
  4. Surveillance: Prompt detection and reporting of suspected cases to initiate rapid response.
