MODULE 1: MEDICAL SURGICAL NURSING - MCQs

1.
When assessing a patient's full-thickness burn injury during the emergent phase, what would the nurse expect to find?
A. Red, fluid-filled vesicles
B. Massive edema at the injury site
C. Leathery, dry, hard skin
D. Serous exudate on a shiny, dark brown wound
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Leathery, dry, hard skin

Explanation: Full-thickness (3rd degree) burns destroy the epidermis, dermis, and often underlying tissues. The destruction of blood vessels and nerves results in a wound that is dry, leathery (eschar), hard, and insensitive to pain (insensate).

Why other options are incorrect:

  • A: Red, fluid-filled vesicles (blisters) are characteristic of partial-thickness (2nd degree) burns.
  • B: While edema occurs systemically and locally, "massive edema" at the site is not the defining characteristic of the wound surface itself compared to the leathery eschar.
2.
Mr. T has been found burning following a fire outbreak at work place and you've been called to him. The correct sequence of actions you would take to rescue Mr. T is?
A. Remove the patient from the burn source, Establish and maintain an airway, Establish an IV line, Assessing for other injuries.
B. Remove the patient from the burn source, Establish an IV line, Establish and maintain an airway, Assessing for other injuries.
C. Establish and maintain an airway, Remove the patient from the burn source, Establish an IV line, assessing for other injuries.
D. Establish and maintain an airway, Assessing for other injuries, Remove the patient from the burn source, Establish an IV line.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Remove the patient from the burn source, Establish and maintain an airway, Establish an IV line, Assessing for other injuries.

Explanation: The priority in any burn emergency is Safety First (stop the burning process/remove from source). Once safety is secured, follow the ABCs (Airway, Breathing, Circulation). Therefore, removing the patient is step 1, followed by Airway, then Circulation (IV line), then Assessment.

3.
During the rehabilitation phase of a burn injury, what can control the contour of the scarring?
A. Pressure garments
B. Avoidance of sunlight
C. Splinting joints in extension
D. Application of emollient lotions
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Pressure garments

Explanation: Pressure garments (like Jobst garments) apply continuous pressure to the healing tissue, which aligns collagen fibers, flattens hypertrophic scars, and improves the aesthetic contour. They are typically worn 23 hours a day for up to a year.

4.
The nurse initially suspects the possibility of sepsis in the burn patient based on what changes?
A. Vital signs
B. Urinary output
C. Gastrointestinal function
D. Burn wound appearance
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Vital signs

Explanation: Early signs of sepsis in burn patients are often subtle changes in vital signs, such as tachycardia (increased heart rate), tachypnea (increased respiratory rate), and temperature instability (hyperthermia or hypothermia). These systemic indicators usually appear before local wound changes.

5.
The appropriate fluid to administer during emergency resuscitation in burns is
A. Colloids
B. Crystalloids
C. Frozen plasma
D. Packed red blood cells
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Crystalloids

Explanation: Lactated Ringer's (RL), a crystalloid, is the standard fluid for initial burn resuscitation (Parkland formula). It most closely resembles the electrolyte composition of plasma and helps buffer metabolic acidosis. Colloids are generally avoided in the first 24 hours due to capillary leak.

6.
The stage of shock characterized by a normal blood pressure is the:
A. Initial stage.
B. compensatory stage.
C. progressive stage.
D. irreversible stage.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. compensatory stage.

Explanation: In the compensatory stage of shock, the body's homeostatic mechanisms (SNS activation, vasoconstriction, increased heart rate) work to maintain cardiac output and blood pressure within normal limits, despite decreased tissue perfusion.

7.
The primary goal in treating cardiogenic shock is to:
A. improve the heart's pumping mechanism.
B. limit further myocardial damage.
C. preserve the healthy myocardium.
D. treat the oxygenation needs of the heart muscle.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. improve the heart's pumping mechanism.

Explanation: Cardiogenic shock is defined as pump failure. Therefore, the primary therapeutic goal is to improve myocardial contractility and cardiac output (the pumping mechanism) to restore tissue perfusion. This allows the other goals (limiting damage, oxygenation) to be met.

8.
Indicate whether the following statements are True or False about fluid resuscitation in shock

i) Patient receiving 0.9% sodium chloride will require large volume of infusion to restore fluid volume; which can cause hypernatremia

Answer: True (Normal Saline requires large volumes to stay intravascularly, and large amounts can cause hyperchloremic acidosis and hypernatremia).

ii) Patient receiving Ringer's lactate will require small volume of infusion; which can cause metabolic acidosis

Answer: False (RL is a crystalloid like NS and requires similar large volumes compared to colloids. Furthermore, the lactate in RL is converted to bicarbonate by the liver, which actually helps correct metabolic acidosis, not cause it).

9.
A patient who has been on potassium ion supplement for 2 weeks is more likely to present with?
A. Tingling of finger toes and acromial regions
B. Dysrhythmias, muscle weakness
C. Weight gain, papilledema
D. Ascites, weight gain
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Dysrhythmias, muscle weakness

Explanation: Supplementation carries the risk of Hyperkalemia (high potassium). Key signs include cardiac dysrhythmias (peaked T waves, widening QRS) and neuromuscular weakness (flaccid paralysis).

10.
During wound healing, generalized body response is most present in the following stage:
A. Inflammatory
B. Proliferative
C. Hemostasis
D. Maturation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Inflammatory

Explanation: The inflammatory phase involves a systemic response including increased blood flow, release of cytokines, potential fever, and leukocytosis (elevated white blood cells) as the body fights infection and clears debris.

MODULE 1: SHORT ANSWER QUESTIONS (20 MARKS)

11.
State five characteristics of malignant tumours (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Rapid growth: Often grow quickly and uncontrollably.
  2. Invasive: Infiltrate and destroy surrounding tissues (do not respect boundaries).
  3. Metastasis: Capable of spreading to distant sites via lymph or blood.
  4. Anaplasia/Undifferentiated: Cells lose their specific structure/function and look different from parent cells.
  5. Non-encapsulated: Do not have a capsule, facilitating invasion.
  6. Angiogenesis: Induce growth of new blood vessels to supply the tumor.
12.
Describe the pathophysiology of the compensatory mechanism in shock (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Baroreceptor Activation: Decreased blood pressure is detected by baroreceptors in the aortic arch and carotid sinus.
  2. Sympathetic Nervous System (SNS) Stimulation: This triggers the release of catecholamines (Epinephrine and Norepinephrine).
  3. Vasoconstriction: Norepinephrine causes peripheral vasoconstriction to shunt blood to vital organs (heart/brain).
  4. Increased Cardiac Output: Epinephrine increases heart rate and contractility.
  5. RAAS Activation: Reduced renal perfusion triggers Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System, leading to sodium/water retention (Aldosterone) and further vasoconstriction (Angiotensin II) to boost blood volume and pressure.
13.
Osmolality is the concentration of fluid that affects the movement of water between fluid compartments by osmosis. Outline factors that increase serum osmolality (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Dehydration (Water deficit): Loss of free water concentrates the blood.
  2. Hypernatremia: Increased sodium levels directly increase osmolality.
  3. Hyperglycemia: High blood glucose (as in Diabetes Mellitus) acts as an osmotic agent.
  4. Uremia/Azotemia: Elevated BUN (Blood Urea Nitrogen) in renal failure.
  5. Administration of Hypertonic Fluids: E.g., Mannitol or 3% Saline.

MODULE 1: LONG ANSWER QUESTION (20 MARKS)

15.
Master X aged 21 years is admitted to a surgical ward having sustained burns of the face, chest, abdomen, anterior limbs and genitals. On assessment, the patient presents with Rapid, shallow respiration, increasing hoarseness, Singed nasal hair, Sooty and smoky breath. Use the rule of nines to determine Total Body Surface Area of the burns and describe emergency Management of Inhalation Injury of master X (20 marks)
Show Answer

1. Calculation of Total Body Surface Area (TBSA) using Rule of Nines:

  • Face: The head is 9%. Anterior face represents half of the head = 4.5%.
  • Chest & Abdomen (Anterior Torso): The entire anterior torso is 18%.
  • Anterior Limbs (Arms): Each arm is 9%. Anterior aspect is 4.5%. Two arms = 4.5% + 4.5% = 9%.
  • Genitals: 1%.
  • TOTAL TBSA: 4.5% + 18% + 9% + 1% = 32.5%.

2. Emergency Management of Inhalation Injury:

The signs (singed nasal hair, hoarseness, sooty breath) indicate a high risk of airway obstruction due to laryngeal edema.

  1. Airway Management (Priority):
    • Prepare for immediate Endotracheal Intubation. Do not wait for obstruction to complete.
    • Maintain cervical spine immobilization if trauma is suspected.
  2. Breathing Support:
    • Administer 100% Humidified Oxygen via non-rebreather mask (pre-intubation) or T-piece/Ventilator.
    • Elevate head of bed (High Fowler's) to facilitate lung expansion if BP allows.
  3. Circulation & Fluid Resuscitation:
    • Establish 2 large-bore IV lines.
    • Start Parkland Formula resuscitation (RL) calculated on 32.5% TBSA.
  4. Monitoring:
    • Monitor Arterial Blood Gases (ABGs) for hypoxia and Carboxyhemoglobin levels (CO poisoning).
    • Continuous Pulse Oximetry (Note: CO poisoning can give false high readings).
  5. Medication:
    • Bronchodilators as prescribed to treat bronchospasm.
    • Nebulized Heparin/Mucomyst may be used for soot clearance (protocol dependent).

MODULE 2: SPECIALIZED PROCEDURES - MCQs

1.
You are providing health education on colostomy irrigation, what information should you include in your teaching?
A. Infuse 1500 to 2000 mL of warm tap water as irrigation fluid.
B. Allow 30 to 45 minutes for the solution and feces to be expelled.
C. Insert a firm plastic catheter 3 to 4 inches into the stoma opening.
D. Hang the irrigation bag on a hook about 36 inches above the stoma
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Allow 30 to 45 minutes for the solution and feces to be expelled.

Explanation: The return flow typically takes 30-45 minutes.

Why other options are incorrect:

  • A: Standard volume is 500-1000 mL. 2000 mL is too much.
  • C: A flexible cone tip is preferred to prevent perforation. If a catheter is used, it should be inserted gently, typically 2-3 inches, not "firmly".
  • D: The bag should be hung at shoulder height (approx. 18-24 inches) above the stoma. 36 inches creates too much pressure.
2.
A male patient who has undergone an anterior-posterior repair is worried about his sexuality. What is an appropriate nursing intervention for this patient?
A. Have the patient's sexual partner reassure the patient that he is still desirable.
B. Reassure the patient that sexual function will return when healing is complete.
C. Remind the patient that affection can be expressed in ways other than through sexual intercourse.
D. Explain that physical and emotional factors can affect sexual function but not necessarily the patient's sexuality.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Remind the patient that affection can be expressed in ways other than through sexual intercourse.

Explanation: This option opens therapeutic communication and addresses the patient's anxiety by offering immediate alternatives for intimacy while healing occurs, without making promises about physiological function (B) or dismissing the concern.

3.
During ward round, a request for abdominal paracentesis for patient X is made, a nurse understands that the reasons for abdominal paracentesis include?
A. To check for peripheral cancers
B. To facilitate breathing of the patient with difficulty in breathing
C. To diagnose infections of the peritoneal fluid
D. To examine of the amount of fluid in the peritoneal cavity
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. To diagnose infections of the peritoneal fluid

Explanation: Diagnostic paracentesis is used to analyze ascitic fluid for infection (Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis), cancer cells, or other pathology. (Option B is also a valid therapeutic reason, but C is a definitive diagnostic reason often prioritized in testing contexts).

4.
Following a tracheostomy, the nurse has been instructed to monitor patient S, a tracheostomy tube is left in situ for;
A. To be an avenue for inhalation
B. Remove secretions from the lungs
C. To prevent compromise of breathing pattern
D. To initiate therapeutic interventions e.g., passage of drugs
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Remove secretions from the lungs

Explanation: One of the primary purposes of maintaining a tracheostomy tube is to provide access for suctioning to remove secretions from the tracheobronchial tree, especially in patients unable to cough effectively.

5.
The patient has just had a myelogram. What should be included in the nursing care for this patient?
A. Restrict fluids until the patient is ambulatory.
B. Keep the patient positioned flat in bed for several hours.
C. Position the patient with the head of the bed elevated 30 degrees.
D. Provide mild analgesics for pain associated with the insertion of needles.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Keep the patient positioned flat in bed for several hours.

Explanation: Keeping the patient flat (supine) for several hours helps prevent cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) leakage from the puncture site and minimizes the risk of a spinal headache. Note: Some protocols for water-soluble dye suggest elevation, but "flat" is the classic answer for preventing leaks/headaches post-puncture.

6.
A patient with a diagnosis of meningitis is due for lumbar puncture; What should the nurse do to prepare the patient for the procedure?
A. Sedate the patient with medication before the test.
B. Withhold beverages containing caffeine for 8 hours.
C. Have the patient sit on the side of the bed, leaning on a padded over-the-bed table.
D. Position the patient in a lateral recumbent position with the hips, knees, and neck flexed.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Position the patient in a lateral recumbent position with the hips, knees, and neck flexed.

Explanation: The "fetal position" (knees to chest, chin to chest) opens up the intervertebral spaces, making it easier to insert the needle into the subarachnoid space.

7.
Renal biopsy has been taken from a patient medical ward. what is the nurse's priority action for the patient?
A. Offer warm sitz baths to relieve discomfort.
B. Test urine for microscopic bleeding with a dipstick.
C. Expect the patient to experience burning on urination.
D. Monitor the patient for symptoms of a urinary infection.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Test urine for microscopic bleeding with a dipstick.

Explanation: The kidney is very vascular. The highest risk post-biopsy is hemorrhage. Testing urine for blood helps identify internal bleeding early.

8.
Before colonoscopy, a nurse performs digital rectal examination, the rationale for this action is to;

9.
During cystoscopy examination, which one of the following is the most appropriate position for the patient?

10.
Patient from renal unit is being suspected to have cholelithiasis, which diagnostic procedure is most appropriate for this patient?

12.
The government encourages all males to undergo circumcision. Outline five benefits of voluntary male medical circumcision. (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. HIV Reduction: Reduces risk of heterosexually acquired HIV infection in men by approx 60%.
  2. Hygiene: Easier to maintain penile hygiene (no smegma).
  3. Reduced UTI Risk: Lower risk of urinary tract infections.
  4. Cancer Prevention: Reduced risk of penile cancer and cervical cancer in female partners (HPV reduction).
  5. STI Reduction: Lower risk of some STIs like ulcerative diseases.

Patient with cardiomyopathy has been referred to radiography unit for electrocardiogram. State five roles of a nurse during electrocardiography (5 Marks)

  1. Explanation: Explain the procedure is painless and harmless to reduce anxiety.

MODULE 2: LONG ANSWER QUESTION (20 MARKS)

15.
You are a staff nurse in an outpatient dialysis facility. A 51-year-old woman with End Stage Renal Disease is scheduled to be seen in the clinic; it is anticipated that she will undergo the first dialysis in one week's time. The patient lives alone and will require teaching about the dialysis options. Develop a teaching plan to explain the different types of dialysis, indications, and collaborative roles of the nurse and the patient during dialysis sessions. (20 marks)
Show Answer

Comprehensive Teaching Plan:

1. Introduction to Dialysis:

  • Explain that dialysis replaces kidney function by filtering waste and excess fluid from blood.
  • Indication: ESRD (Kidneys working <10-15%), severe electrolyte imbalance (hyperkalemia), fluid overload.

2. Hemodialysis (HD):

  • Mechanism: Blood is pumped out of body to a machine (dialyzer) for cleaning and returned.
  • Access: AV Fistula (gold standard) or Graft.
  • Schedule: Usually 3 times/week at a center (4 hours/session).
  • Pros/Cons: Staff does the work, social interaction vs rigid schedule, diet restrictions.

3. Peritoneal Dialysis (PD):

  • Mechanism: Uses lining of abdomen (peritoneum) as filter. Dialysate fluid is instilled via catheter, dwells, then drained.
  • Types: CAPD (Manual, daytime) or APD (Machine, nighttime).
  • Pros/Cons: Flexible, done at home, fewer diet restrictions vs Risk of Peritonitis, supply storage space needed.
  • Note: Since she lives alone, PD requires self-sufficiency or help. Assessment of her ability is key.

4. Collaborative Roles (Nurse & Patient):

  • Nurse Role:
    • Monitor vital signs (BP often drops).
    • Assess access site (bruit/thrill for fistula, infection for catheter).
    • Administer meds (EPO, Heparin).
    • Education on diet (Low Phos/K/Na) and fluid restriction.
  • Patient Role:
    • Adhere to schedule and diet.
    • Protect access arm (no BP/blood draws).
    • Report symptoms (dizziness, cramping, fever).
    • Daily weights.
