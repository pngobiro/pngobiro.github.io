Module I: Medical Surgical Nursing

Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions (10 Marks)

1.
A patient with potassium levels of 5.0 mEq/L is considered to be in a state of;
A. Hypokalemia
B. Hypernatremia
C. Hyperkalemia
D. Hypervolemia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Hyperkalemia

Explanation: The normal range for serum potassium is typically 3.5 to 5.0 mEq/L. A level of 5.0 mEq/L is at the upper limit of normal. In the context of exam questions where options represent pathological states, 5.0 is often considered the threshold for concern regarding hyperkalemia, distinguishing it from hypokalemia (<3.5).

2.
The main aim of Escharotomy in treatment of full thickness burns is;
A. Improve oxygenation of the burnt tissues
B. Remove the dead tissues
C. Allow underlying viable tissues to expand
D. Reduce pain
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Allow underlying viable tissues to expand

Explanation: Escharotomy involves incising the tough, inelastic dead tissue (eschar) of a full-thickness burn to relieve pressure, prevent compartment syndrome, and restore circulation to the underlying tissues.

3.
Furuncles (boils) are commonly caused by;
A. Streptococcus pyogenes
B. Clostridium tetani
C. Staphylococcus aureus
D. Bacillus aureus
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Staphylococcus aureus

Explanation: Staphylococcus aureus is the most common causative agent for furuncles (boils) and carbuncles, infecting the hair follicle.

4.
Anaphylactic shock is;
A. Associated with excessive blood loss
B. Caused by systemic antigen-antibody reaction
C. Associated with effects of adrenaline and nor-adrenaline
D. Usually common in patients on antibiotics
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Caused by systemic antigen-antibody reaction

Explanation: Anaphylactic shock is a severe, life-threatening hypersensitivity reaction (IgE-mediated antigen-antibody reaction) causing massive vasodilation and bronchoconstriction.

5.
An 11-month-old infant with dehydration and metabolic acidosis is likely to present with;
A. A decreased platelet count
B. Shallow respirations
C. Tachypnea
D. A reduced white blood cell count
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Tachypnea

Explanation: In metabolic acidosis, the body attempts to compensate by blowing off CO2 (an acid) through the respiratory system. This manifests as rapid, deep breathing (Kussmaul breathing/Tachypnea).

6.
A deteriorating condition for a patient with head injury will be indicated by;
A. Widening pulse pressure and irregular respiratory pattern.
B. Narrowing pulse pressure and escalation of discomfort.
C. Bradycardia and Kussmaul breathing.
D. Oliguria and narrow pulse pressure
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Widening pulse pressure and irregular respiratory pattern.

Explanation: This describes Cushing's Triad, a sign of increased Intracranial Pressure (ICP). It includes hypertension with widening pulse pressure, bradycardia, and irregular respirations.

7.
The immediate effects of burns on body fluids and electrolytes include;
A. Hypokalemia & Hyponatremia.
B. Reduced circulatory blood volume and hypernatremia.
C. Hyponatremia and Hyperkalemia.
D. Reduced urine output and hypokalemia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Hyponatremia and Hyperkalemia.

Explanation: In the initial (emergent) phase of burns, massive cell destruction releases potassium into the blood (Hyperkalemia), and increased capillary permeability allows sodium and fluid to shift into the interstitial space (Hyponatremia and Hypovolemia).

8.
During the assessment of a patient with inflammation, the nurse observes a wound with purulent drainage. Which nursing intervention is appropriate?
A. Apply a dry dressing to the wound
B. Irrigate the wound with sterile saline solution
C. Apply a warm compress to the wound
D. Leave the wound open to air
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Irrigate the wound with sterile saline solution

Explanation: Purulent drainage indicates infection and debris. Irrigating with sterile saline helps clean the wound bed without damaging tissue, facilitating assessment and healing.

9.
Sepsis is a severe illness caused by an overwhelming infection of the bloodstream by toxin producing bacteria. What is the most common portal of entry for microorganisms associated with sepsis;
A. Respiratory tract
B. GI tract skin
C. Skin
D. Urinary tract
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Respiratory tract

Explanation: While UTIs and skin infections are common, respiratory tract infections (pneumonia) are frequently cited as the leading cause (portal of entry) for sepsis in many clinical studies.

10.
Ana's postoperative vital signs are a blood pressure of 80/50 mm Hg, a pulse of 140, and respirations of 32. Suspecting shock, which of the following orders would the nurse question?
A. Put the client in modified Trendelenberg's position.
B. Administer oxygen at 100%.
C. Monitor urine output every hour.
D. Administer pethidine 50mg IM q4h
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Administer pethidine 50mg IM q4h

Explanation: The patient is hypotensive (BP 80/50) and tachycardic (Pulse 140), indicating shock. Pethidine (an opioid) causes vasodilation and can further lower blood pressure and depress respiration, worsening the shock state.

Section 2: Short Answer Questions (20 Marks)

11.
State the classification of burns by depth (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Superficial burn (First Degree): Involves only the epidermis.
  2. Superficial Partial-thickness burn (Second Degree): Involves epidermis and upper dermis.
  3. Deep Partial-thickness burn (Second Degree): Involves epidermis and deep dermis.
  4. Full-thickness burn (Third Degree): Involves destruction of epidermis and entire dermis.
  5. Deep Full-thickness burn (Fourth Degree): Extends to muscle, tendon, and bone.
12.
State the factors that influence wound healing (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Nutrition: Adequate protein, Vitamin C, and Zinc are essential for repair.
  2. Age: Elderly patients heal slower due to reduced collagen synthesis and circulation.
  3. Infection: Presence of bacteria prolongs inflammation and delays healing.
  4. Blood Supply/Oxygenation: Poor perfusion leads to tissue hypoxia and delayed healing (e.g., in diabetes).
  5. Medications: Corticosteroids and immunosuppressants inhibit the healing process.
  6. Chronic Diseases: Diabetes Mellitus, anemia, and cancer impair healing.
13.
Outline five management modalities for carbuncles (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Systemic Antibiotics: To treat the underlying Staphylococcus aureus infection.
  2. Incision and Drainage (I&D): Surgical release of pus to relieve pressure and facilitate healing.
  3. Warm Compresses: To promote localization of the infection and drainage.
  4. Analgesics: For pain management.
  5. Hygiene: Washing with antibacterial soap and proper dressing to prevent spread.
14.
State five causes of hyperkalemia. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Renal Failure: Kidneys fail to excrete potassium (most common cause).
  2. Tissue Trauma/Burns: Extensive cell destruction releases intracellular potassium into the blood.
  3. Metabolic Acidosis: Potassium shifts out of cells in exchange for hydrogen ions.
  4. Medications: Use of potassium-sparing diuretics (e.g., Spironolactone) or ACE inhibitors.
  5. Excessive Intake: Oral supplements or rapid IV administration of potassium.

Section 3: Long Answer Question (20 Marks)

15.
Miss J is brought in to the emergency department with heavy bleeding after being involved in a road accident. On examination the patient feels dizzy with vitals of BP 80/48mmHg, pulse rate 124 beats/min, Temperature of 35.8°C. Formulate nursing diagnoses and describe specific management of Miss J for the first 24 hours. (20 marks)
Show Answer

Diagnosis: Hypovolemic Shock secondary to Hemorrhage.

Nursing Diagnoses:

  • Deficient Fluid Volume related to active hemorrhage as evidenced by hypotension and tachycardia.
  • Decreased Cardiac Output related to altered preload (blood loss).
  • Ineffective Tissue Perfusion related to hypovolemia.

Specific Management (First 24 Hours):

  1. Airway & Breathing:
    • Administer high-flow oxygen via non-rebreather mask to maximize tissue oxygenation.
    • Maintain patent airway.
  2. Circulation & Fluid Resuscitation:
    • Establish 2 large-bore IV lines (14G or 16G).
    • Rapidly infuse crystalloids (Normal Saline or Ringer's Lactate).
    • Prepare for blood transfusion (Type and Crossmatch immediately). Transfuse Packed Red Blood Cells (PRBCs) as ordered to restore oxygen-carrying capacity.
  3. Control Bleeding:
    • Apply direct pressure to external wounds.
    • Prepare patient for surgical intervention if internal bleeding is suspected.
  4. Positioning:
    • Place patient in modified Trendelenburg position (legs elevated) to improve venous return to vital organs.
  5. Monitoring:
    • Monitor vital signs every 15 minutes until stable.
    • Insert Foley catheter to monitor hourly urine output (indicator of renal perfusion; goal >30ml/hr).
    • Monitor Level of Consciousness (GCS).
  6. Temperature Regulation:
    • Cover with warm blankets to prevent hypothermia (Temp is 35.8°C), which can worsen coagulopathy.

Module II: Specialised Procedures

Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions (10 Marks)

1.
Which parameter is commonly monitored during dialysis to assess fluid status?
A. Blood pressure
B. Heart rate
C. Serum creatinine levels
D. Weight gain or loss
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Weight gain or loss

Explanation: Pre- and post-dialysis weight is the most accurate indicator of fluid removal (ultrafiltration) and fluid status in dialysis patients.

2.
In peritoneal dialysis, which of the following is considered a key sign of peritonitis that should be reported to the healthcare provider?
A. Bradycardia
B. Clear drainage
C. Fever
D. Hypotension
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Fever

Explanation: The classic signs of peritonitis in PD are cloudy effluent (drainage), abdominal pain, and fever. Among the options, fever is a key sign of infection. (Clear drainage is normal).

3.
Why is it advised not to sleep on the arm with the vascular access site during hemodialysis?
A. It may cause pain or discomfort.
B. It can increase the risk of clotting or stenosis at the access site.
C. It promotes better blood flow to the arm.
D. It prevents the blood pressure cuff from causing injury during dialysis.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. It can increase the risk of clotting or stenosis at the access site.

Explanation: Pressure on the AV fistula or graft (from sleeping on the arm, tight clothes, or BP cuffs) can restrict blood flow, leading to stasis, thrombosis (clotting), and failure of the access.

4.
Nursing responsibility for Lumbar Puncture includes:
A. Ensuring the patient has a full bladder.
B. Placing the patient in the lateral recumbent position.
C. Straightening the patient's legs just before the needle.
D. Having the patient cough when the needle has been inserted.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Placing the patient in the lateral recumbent position.

Explanation: The lateral recumbent (fetal) position with knees flexed to chest opens the intervertebral spaces, facilitating needle insertion. The bladder should be emptied (A is wrong).

5.
What is the primary purpose of stomal irrigation?
A. To clean the stoma
B. To stimulate peristalsis
C. To prevent peristomal skin irritation
D. To manage excessive stoma output
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. To stimulate peristalsis

Explanation: Colostomy irrigation is used to regulate bowel movements by stimulating peristalsis and emptying the colon at a scheduled time, allowing some patients to go without a pouch.

6.
An ultrasound is used to visualize organ: -
A. Size, texture, posture.
B. Shape, Size, posture.
C. Size, shade, posture.
D. Shape, Size, location.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Shape, Size, location.

Explanation: Ultrasound imaging allows assessment of the size, shape, position (location), and texture (echogenicity) of internal organs.

7.
An ultrasound can be used visualize the: -
A. Scrotum.
B. Lungs.
C. Brain.
D. Fractured bone.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Scrotum.

Explanation: Ultrasound is excellent for soft tissues and fluid-filled structures like the scrotum (testicular ultrasound). It does not penetrate bone (Brain/Fracture) or air (Lungs) well.

8.
Chest CT Scan Indications include: -
A. Liver lesions.
B. Kidney lesions.
C. Pulmonary embolism.
D. Adrenal lesions.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Pulmonary embolism.

Explanation: CT Pulmonary Angiogram (CTPA) is the gold standard for diagnosing Pulmonary Embolism in the chest. Options A, B, and D refer to abdominal structures.

9.
Radiological examination superior for diagnosing unexplained headache is: -
A. CT scan.
B. MRI.
C. Sonography.
D. X-ray.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. MRI.

Explanation: MRI provides superior detail of soft tissues, brain parenchyma, and vascular structures compared to CT, making it better for investigating unexplained chronic headaches (tumors, vascular malformations) unless an acute bleed is suspected (where CT is faster).

10.
What is the purpose of contrast dye in cardiac catheterization?
A. To improve visualization
B. To prevent clot formation
C. To decrease blood pressure
D. To numb the insertion site
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. To improve visualization

Explanation: Radio-opaque contrast dye is injected to visualize the coronary arteries and heart chambers under fluoroscopy (angiography).

Section 2: Short Answer Questions (20 Marks)

11.
Describe nursing interventions for a patient undergoing underseal water drainage. (6 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Maintain System Integrity: Ensure all connections are tight and taped to prevent air leaks.
  2. Positioning: Keep the drainage bottle/system below the level of the patient's chest at all times to prevent backflow.
  3. Monitoring: Check for tidaling (fluctuation) in the water seal chamber and bubbling (air leak).
  4. Assess Drainage: Monitor and record the amount, color, and consistency of drainage. Mark the level on the bottle.
  5. Safety: Keep clamps at the bedside. Do not clamp the tube unless checking for leaks or changing the bottle (per policy).
  6. Patient Care: Encourage deep breathing and coughing to facilitate lung re-expansion.
12.
Explain five (5) complications of tracheostomy. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Obstruction: Blockage of the tube by thick mucus plugs or secretions.
  2. Infection: Stoma site infection or pneumonia.
  3. Accidental Decannulation: The tube dislodges from the trachea.
  4. Tracheal Stenosis: Narrowing of the trachea due to scar tissue formation.
  5. Subcutaneous Emphysema: Air leaking into the tissue around the stoma.
13.
State four (4) contraindications of barium swallow. (4 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Suspected Perforation: Risk of barium peritonitis (use water-soluble contrast instead).
  2. Complete Bowel Obstruction: Barium can impact and worsen obstruction.
  3. Severe Constipation: Risk of impaction.
  4. Pregnancy: Due to radiation exposure risks.
14.
State five (5) nursing responsibilities in hemodialysis. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Weight Monitoring: Weigh patient pre- and post-dialysis to determine fluid removal.
  2. Vascular Access Care: Assess fistula/graft for thrill and bruit. Do not use arm for BP/venipuncture.
  3. Vital Signs: Monitor BP closely during dialysis for hypotension.
  4. Medication Management: Withhold water-soluble and antihypertensive meds before dialysis if ordered.
  5. Complication Monitoring: Watch for disequilibrium syndrome, cramps, or bleeding.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions (20 Marks)

15.
Mr K, 68 years old is scheduled for cardiac catheterization. Describe pre and post-operative care of the patient in the first 48hrs. (20 marks)
Show Answer

Pre-operative Care (10 Marks):

  1. Informed Consent: Ensure the physician has explained the procedure and verify consent is signed.
  2. History & Allergies: Assess for allergies to shellfish, iodine, or contrast dye. Check medication history (e.g., anticoagulants, metformin).
  3. NPO Status: Ensure patient is NPO (nothing by mouth) for 6-8 hours prior to prevent aspiration.
  4. Baseline Assessment: Record vital signs and mark peripheral pulses (dorsalis pedis/posterior tibial) for post-op comparison.
  5. Renal Function: Check BUN/Creatinine levels as contrast dye can be nephrotoxic.
  6. Access: Establish patent IV access.
  7. Education: Inform patient they may feel a "flushing" sensation or warmth when dye is injected.
  8. Preparation: Have patient void before the procedure.

Post-operative Care (First 48 Hours) (10 Marks):

  1. Positioning: Keep the affected leg straight and patient on bed rest for 4-6 hours to prevent bleeding from the femoral insertion site.
  2. Insertion Site Assessment: Check the dressing/site for bleeding, hematoma, or swelling frequently.
  3. Neurovascular Checks: Assess the extremity distal to the puncture site for Color, Warmth, Sensation, Movement, and Pulses (compare with marked baseline) to detect arterial occlusion.
  4. Vital Signs: Monitor BP and Pulse q15min x 4, q30min x 2, q1h x 4, then q4h. Watch for hypotension (bleeding) or arrhythmias.
  5. Hydration: Encourage oral fluids or maintain IV fluids to flush the contrast dye from the kidneys and prevent nephrotoxicity.
  6. Pain Management: Assess for chest pain or back/groin pain. Report chest pain immediately (could indicate re-occlusion).
  7. Discharge Teaching: Advise on limiting activity for a few days and signs of infection/bleeding to report.
