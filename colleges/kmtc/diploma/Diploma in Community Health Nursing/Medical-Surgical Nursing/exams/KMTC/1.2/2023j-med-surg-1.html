Module I: Medical Surgical Nursing 1

Section II: Short Answer Questions (20 Marks)

1.
State any five (5) risk factors of developing cellulitis in an adult. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Skin Injury: Cuts, fractures, burns, or surgical wounds provide an entry portal for bacteria.
  2. Weakened Immune System: Conditions like Diabetes, HIV/AIDS, or leukemia impair the body's ability to fight infection.
  3. Skin Conditions: Chronic skin disorders such as eczema, athlete's foot, or shingles causing breaks in the skin.
  4. Chronic Swelling (Lymphedema): Swollen tissue can crack, allowing bacteria to enter.
  5. Obesity: Increases the risk of cellulitis and recurrence.
  6. History of Cellulitis: Previous infections increase the likelihood of recurrence.
2.
Outline any five (5) ways in which nurses can promote wound healing through various interventions (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Nutrition: Encourage a high-protein, high-calorie diet with Vitamin C and Zinc to support tissue repair.
  2. Infection Prevention: Maintain strict aseptic technique during dressing changes to prevent wound infection.
  3. Moisture Balance: Use appropriate dressings to keep the wound bed moist (but not macerated) to facilitate epithelialization.
  4. Circulation Promotion: Ensure adequate hydration and positioning (elevation) to reduce edema and improve blood flow.
  5. Pressure Relief: Regular turning/repositioning (every 2 hours) to prevent pressure ulcers and improve perfusion.
  6. Pain Management: Administer analgesics to ensure patient comfort and reduce stress response which can hinder healing.
3.
Explain the three (3) classification of burns. (6mks)
Show Answer

Burns are typically classified by depth:

  1. Superficial (First-Degree) Burns:
    • Involves only the epidermis. Skin is red, painful, and dry with no blisters (e.g., mild sunburn). Heals in 3-5 days without scarring.
  2. Partial-Thickness (Second-Degree) Burns:
    • Involves the epidermis and part of the dermis. Skin is red, blistered, swollen, and extremely painful. Can be superficial partial or deep partial thickness. Heals in 2-3 weeks, may scar.
  3. Full-Thickness (Third-Degree) Burns:
    • Destroys the entire epidermis and dermis, potentially reaching subcutaneous fat, muscle, or bone. Wound appears white, charred, or leathery. Pain is often absent due to nerve destruction. Requires grafting to heal.
4.
State any four (4) causes of respiratory acidosis (4 marks)
Show Answer

Respiratory acidosis is caused by hypoventilation (retention of CO2). Causes include:

  1. Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD): Emphysema, chronic bronchitis.
  2. CNS Depression: Overdose of opioids, sedatives, or anesthesia causing reduced respiratory drive.
  3. Chest Wall Abnormalities: Flail chest, severe kyphoscoliosis restricting lung expansion.
  4. Neuromuscular Disorders: Guillain-Barre syndrome, Myasthenia Gravis affecting respiratory muscles.
  5. Airway Obstruction: Foreign body aspiration, severe asthma attack.
  6. Pulmonary Edema/Pneumonia: Severe infection or fluid impairing gas exchange.

Section III: Long Answer Question (20 Marks)

1.
A 62-year-old male patient is admitted to the emergency department with symptoms of shock. The patient has a history of hypertension and undergoing treatment for coronary artery bypass graft surgery. He is presenting with hypotension, tachycardia, cool and clammy skin, altered mental status, and oliguria. The nurse suspects the patient is in a state of shock.

a) Explain the pathophysiology of shock (5 marks)

Show Answer

Shock is a state of inadequate tissue perfusion.

  1. Initial Insult: A precipitating event (e.g., heart failure in this case) leads to decreased Cardiac Output (CO) and hypotension.
  2. Compensatory Mechanisms: Baroreceptors detect low BP, stimulating the Sympathetic Nervous System (SNS). This causes vasoconstriction (cool skin), increased heart rate (tachycardia), and activation of the Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System (RAAS) to retain fluid (oliguria).
  3. Cellular Hypoxia: Despite compensation, perfusion remains inadequate. Cells switch from aerobic to anaerobic metabolism due to lack of oxygen.
  4. Lactic Acidosis: Anaerobic metabolism produces lactic acid, leading to metabolic acidosis. This impairs cellular function and cell membrane integrity.
  5. Organ Dysfunction: If untreated, cellular damage leads to Multiple Organ Dysfunction Syndrome (MODS) and death.

b) Based on the assessment findings in the case scenario, discuss the nursing interventions and priorities of care for a patient in shock (15 marks)

Show Answer

Given the history (CABG, HTN) and signs (hypotension, cool skin), the patient is likely in Cardiogenic Shock.

  1. Immediate Stabilization (ABC):
    • Airway/Breathing: Administer high-flow oxygen via non-rebreather mask to maintain SpO2 >94%. Prepare for intubation if mental status deteriorates further.
    • Circulation: Establish IV access. Caution with fluids; unlike hypovolemic shock, cardiogenic shock can be worsened by fluid overload. Listen to lung sounds for crackles before bolusing.
  2. Pharmacological Support:
    • Inotropes: Administer ordered inotropes (e.g., Dobutamine, Dopamine) to improve cardiac contractility.
    • Vasopressors: Administer Norepinephrine if hypotension persists to maintain MAP >65 mmHg.
    • Diuretics: Furosemide may be indicated if pulmonary edema is present (monitor carefully).
  3. Monitoring:
    • Cardiac Monitoring: Continuous ECG to detect arrhythmias (common post-CABG).
    • Vital Signs: Monitor BP, HR, RR, and SpO2 every 15 minutes.
    • Urine Output: Insert a Foley catheter to monitor hourly output (indicator of renal perfusion). Goal >0.5ml/kg/hr.
    • Hemodynamics: If available, monitor CVP/PCWP to guide fluid/drug therapy.
  4. Positioning:
    • Place patient in semi-Fowler's position to ease breathing (unless severe hypotension requires modified Trendelenburg, but caution with heart failure).
  5. General Care:
    • Keep patient warm but avoid overheating (which increases metabolic demand).
    • Reassure patient and family to reduce anxiety (reduces oxygen demand).

Module II: Specialised Procedures

Section II: Short Answer Questions (20 Marks)

1.
State five (5) contraindications for hemodialysis (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Hemodynamic Instability: Severe hypotension or shock (patient cannot tolerate fluid shift).
  2. Lack of Vascular Access: Inability to establish a fistula, graft, or catheter.
  3. Severe Coagulopathy: Inability to use anticoagulants (heparin) required for the circuit.
  4. Advanced Dementia/Psychosis: Inability to cooperate with the procedure.
  5. Terminal Illness: Where dialysis would not improve quality of life (relative contraindication).
2.
Outline five (5) diagnostic and therapeutic purposes for bronchoscopy (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Diagnostic: Visualization of tumors or strictures.
  2. Diagnostic: Biopsy of tissue for histology.
  3. Diagnostic: Collection of secretions (sputum) for culture/cytology.
  4. Therapeutic: Removal of foreign bodies.
  5. Therapeutic: Suctioning of thick mucous plugs/secretions.
  6. Therapeutic: Control of bleeding (hemostasis).
3.
Outline five (5) benefits of voluntary male medical circumcision (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Reduces risk of acquiring HIV infection in heterosexual men (by approx. 60%).
  2. Improves penile hygiene (smegma reduction).
  3. Reduces risk of other STIs (Herpes, Syphilis, HPV).
  4. Reduces risk of Penile Cancer.
  5. Reduces risk of Cervical Cancer in female partners (by reducing HPV transmission).
4.
State five (5) advantages of a tracheostomy (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Bypasses upper airway obstruction.
  2. Reduces anatomical dead space (improves ventilation efficiency).
  3. Facilitates easier removal of tracheobronchial secretions (suctioning).
  4. More comfortable than an endotracheal tube (ETT) for long-term ventilation.
  5. Allows the patient to eat and speak (with appropriate valves/cuffs) unlike ETT.

Section III: Long Answer Questions (20 Marks)

1.
Mr. X has been admitted in the medical-surgical ward with a diagnosis of diabetes mellitus, schedule to undergo below-knee amputation. He's due for blood transfusion prior undergoing the procedure.

a) Outline five (5) indications for blood transfusion (5 marks)

Show Answer
  1. Severe Anemia: To restore oxygen-carrying capacity (e.g., Hb < 7-8 g/dL).
  2. Acute Hemorrhage/Blood Loss: To restore blood volume (e.g., trauma, surgery).
  3. Coagulopathy: Deficiency of clotting factors (e.g., Hemophilia, DIC).
  4. Thrombocytopenia: Low platelet count causing bleeding.
  5. Hypoproteinemia: Severe protein deficiency (using Albumin/Plasma).

b) List six (6) blood transfusion products (3 marks)

Show Answer
  1. Whole Blood
  2. Packed Red Blood Cells (PRBCs)
  3. Fresh Frozen Plasma (FFP)
  4. Platelet Concentrate
  5. Cryoprecipitate
  6. Albumin

(Any 6 for 0.5 marks each)

c) State four (4) immediate immunologic blood transfusion reactions. (4 marks)

Show Answer
  1. Acute Hemolytic Transfusion Reaction: ABO incompatibility.
  2. Febrile Non-Hemolytic Reaction: Reaction to donor WBCs/cytokines.
  3. Allergic Reaction (Mild): Urticaria/hives due to plasma proteins.
  4. Anaphylactic Reaction: Severe allergic response (IgA deficiency).
  5. Transfusion-Related Acute Lung Injury (TRALI).

d) Describe the specific nursing roles during a blood transfusion. (8 marks)

Show Answer
  1. Verification: Two nurses must double-check the physician's order, patient ID (armband), blood type, Rh factor, expiration date, and unit number against the blood bag tag.
  2. Baseline Assessment: Assess vital signs (Temp, Pulse, BP, RR) and breath sounds immediately before starting. Assess skin for rashes.
  3. Venous Access: Ensure a patent large-bore IV cannula (18G or 20G preferred) is available. Use only Normal Saline with blood.
  4. Initiation: Start the transfusion slowly (no more than 2mL/min or 120mL/hr) for the first 15 minutes.
  5. Monitoring: Stay with the patient for the first 15 minutes (this is when severe reactions occur). Monitor vitals every 15 minutes for the first hour, then every 30 minutes.
  6. Education: Instruct patient to report any chills, itching, back pain, or shortness of breath immediately.
  7. Management of Reaction: If a reaction occurs, STOP the transfusion immediately, keep the line open with Normal Saline, and notify the physician/blood bank.
  8. Documentation: Record start/end time, volume infused, vitals, and patient response.
