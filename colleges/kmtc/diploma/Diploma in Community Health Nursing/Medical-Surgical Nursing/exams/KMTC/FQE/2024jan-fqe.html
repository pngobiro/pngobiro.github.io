SECTION 1: MULTIPLE CHOICE QUESTIONS (40 MARKS)

1.
A nursing measure that is indicated to reduce the potential for seizures and increased intracranial pressure in a patient with bacterial meningitis is:
A. Administering codeine for relief of head and neck pain
B. Controlling fever with prescribed drugs
C. Keeping the room darkened and quiet to minimize environmental stimuli
D. Maintaining the patient on a strict bed rest with the head of bed slightly elevated
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Keeping the room darkened and quiet to minimize environmental stimuli

Explanation: Patients with meningitis have meningeal irritation (photophobia, phonophobia). Environmental stimuli (bright lights, noise) can lower the seizure threshold and increase ICP. Reducing stimuli is a key seizure precaution.

2.
You are preparing to administer an antibiotic to a patient with impetigo. The culture report on the infection will indicate infection of:
A. Pseudomonas
B. Escherichia Coli
C. Group A Streptococci
D. Staphylococcus Aureus
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Staphylococcus Aureus

Explanation: Staphylococcus aureus is the most common causative organism for impetigo. Group A Streptococcus (Option C) is also a cause but Staph is statistically more common in non-bullous impetigo.

3.
The physician orders heparin, 7,500 units, to be administered subcutaneously every 6 hours. The vial reads 10,000 units per milliliter. You should anticipate giving how much heparin for each dose?
A. ¼ ml
B. ½ ml
C. ¾ ml
D. 1 ¼ ml
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. ¾ ml

Calculation:

$$ \text{Volume} = \frac{\text{Desired Dose}}{\text{Concentration}} $$

$$ \text{Volume} = \frac{7,500 \text{ units}}{10,000 \text{ units/ml}} $$

$$ \text{Volume} = 0.75 \text{ ml} = \frac{3}{4} \text{ ml} $$

4.
The nursing observation that points to complications of intestinal obstruction following exploratory laparotomy within the 1st 24 hours would be:
A. Protruding soft abdomen with frequent diarrhea
B. Distended abdomen with ascites
C. Minimal bowel sounds in all four quadrants
D. Distended abdomen with complaints of pain
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Distended abdomen with complaints of pain

Explanation: Post-operative ileus or mechanical obstruction manifests as abdominal distension and pain. Minimal bowel sounds (C) are expected in the first 24 hours (paralytic ileus). Frequent diarrhea (A) is unlikely with obstruction (usually constipation/obstipation). Ascites (B) takes time to develop.

5.
A client has just had a total knee replacement for severe osteoarthritis. You will suspect possible nerve damage if you observed:
A. Numbness of toes
B. Severe pain
C. Dislocation of the hip
D. Inability to stand
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Numbness of toes

Explanation: The peroneal nerve is at risk during knee surgery. Damage manifests as numbness, tingling (paresthesia), or inability to dorsiflex the foot (foot drop). Pain (B) is expected post-op; numbness indicates neurovascular compromise.

6.
One of the following would lead you to suspect that a patient with fracture of the right femur may be developing a fat embolus?
A. Acute respiratory distress syndrome
B. Migraine like headaches
C. Numbness in the right leg
D. Muscle spasms in the right thigh
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Acute respiratory distress syndrome

Explanation: The classic triad of Fat Embolism Syndrome (FES) is: 1. Respiratory distress (hypoxemia, dyspnea), 2. Neurological changes (confusion), 3. Petechial rash. ARDS is the severe respiratory manifestation. Migraines (B) are unrelated.

7.
During treatment of burns, one feature to look out for as a cause of contractures would be:
A. A secondary infection
B. Lack of adequate blood supply
C. Weakness of connective tissue
D. Excessive fibrous tissue formation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Excessive fibrous tissue formation

Explanation: Contractures occur when the burn scar matures and shortens due to hypertrophic scarring (excessive collagen/fibrous tissue). This pulls the surrounding skin tight and restricts movement.

8.
You find the skin of the arm of a burn patient white and hard. It is inelastic and insensitive to pressure. You will classify the burn as:
A. Superficial
B. Superficial partial thickness
C. Deep partial thickness
D. Full thickness
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Full thickness

Explanation: Full-thickness burns (3rd degree) are characterized by a waxy white, leathery, hard appearance (eschar). They are painless (insensitive) because nerve endings are destroyed.

9.
As a postpartum nurse providing instructions to a mother of a newborn infant with hyperbilirubinemia and who is being breastfed, which would be the appropriate instruction to the mother?
A. Switch to bottle-feeding the baby for 2 weeks
B. Stop the breastfeeding and switch to bottle-feeding permanently
C. Feed the newborn infant less frequently
D. Continue to breast-feed every 2-4 hours
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Continue to breast-feed every 2-4 hours

Explanation: Frequent feeding promotes regular bowel movements, which helps eliminate bilirubin in the stool. Stopping breastfeeding is rarely necessary unless bilirubin levels are critically high, and even then, it's temporary.

10.
One of the major disadvantage of peritoneal dialysis to look out for is:
A. Hypotension is a constant problem because of constant fluid removal
B. Blood loss can be extensive because of the use of heparin to keep the catheter patent
C. High concentration of dialysate necessary for ultrafiltration cause carbohydrate and lipid abnormalities
D. Solutes are removed more rapidly from the blood than from the central nervous system, causing disequilibrium syndrome
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. High concentration of dialysate necessary for ultrafiltration cause carbohydrate and lipid abnormalities

Explanation: Peritoneal dialysate contains high concentrations of glucose (dextrose) to create the osmotic gradient for fluid removal. Absorption of this glucose can lead to hyperglycemia, weight gain (lipids), and hypertriglyceridemia. Option D (Disequilibrium syndrome) is a risk of Hemodialysis, not PD.

11.
The nursing observation that indicates that a male client with a kidney stone is experiencing renal colic would be:
A. Severe flank pain radiating toward the testicles
B. Stress incontinence with full bladder
C. Hematuria and severe burning on urination
D. Enuresis with hyperalbuminemia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Severe flank pain radiating toward the testicles

Explanation: Classic renal colic presents as sudden, severe flank pain that radiates anteriorly and downward to the groin or testicles (in men) as the stone moves down the ureter.

12.
The main predisposing factors of lung cancer include:

D. Smoking, exposure to radiation, exposure to coal dust, alcoholism

A. Status asthmaticus

15.
A client diagnosed with type II diabetes mellitus, has been ordered a course of prednisone for her severe arthritic pain. An expected change that requires close monitoring is:
A. Increased blood glucose level
B. Increased platelet aggregation
C. Increased creatinine clearance
D. Increased ketone level in urine
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Increased blood glucose level

Explanation: Corticosteroids like Prednisone induce gluconeogenesis and increase insulin resistance, leading to hyperglycemia. In a diabetic patient, this can cause significant destabilization of blood sugar control.

17.
Microorganisms that are Gram negative bacilli include:
A. Eschrichia coli, Salmonella, Shigella
B. Salmonella, Staphylococcus, Streptococcus

18.
To open the airway of an adult suspected to have sustained neck and back injury, you will apply jaw thrust by placing
A. The hand on the person's forehead and using the other hand, gently lifting the chin
B. A soft pillow under the neck, then lifting the jaw upwards
C. The fingers under the angle of the jaw on both sides, then lifting the jaw upwards
D. The hand on the person's forehead and applying a little pressure to achieve the tilt
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. The fingers under the angle of the jaw on both sides, then lifting the jaw upwards

Explanation: This describes the Jaw Thrust Maneuver, which lifts the mandible to open the airway without tilting the head/neck, protecting the cervical spine.

19.
To ensure that learners have understood the concept of voluntary respiration, the teacher would expect them to indicate that it is controlled via;
A. Cerebral cortex
B. Brain stem system
C. Motor and pre-motor cortex
D. Pons varolii
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Cerebral cortex

Explanation: Voluntary control of breathing (e.g., holding breath, singing) originates in the cerebral cortex. Involuntary/automatic control (metabolic) originates in the brainstem (medulla/pons).

20.
You are evaluating a client's fluid intake and output record. What should be the relationship between fluid intake and urine output?
A. Fluid intake should double the urine output.
B. Fluid intake should approximately equal to the urine output.
C. Fluid intake should be half the urine output.
D. Fluid intake should be inversely proportional to the urine output.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Fluid intake should approximately equal to the urine output.

Explanation: In a healthy state of fluid balance, intake roughly equals total output (urine + insensible loss + stool). Since insensible loss is hard to measure, urine output is the primary indicator, and generally, intake slightly exceeds urine output to account for insensible loss, but they correlate closely.

24.
You are teaching a patient about the use of a respiratory inhaler. Which action by the patient indicate need for further teaching?

B. Presses the canister down with his finger as he breathes in.
C. Inhales the mist and quickly exhales.
D. Waits 1 to 2 minutes between puffs if more than one puff has been prescribed.

25.
The effective management of idiopathic thrombocytopenic purpura include:
A. Platelet transfusion, antibiotics, corticosteroids
B. Splenectomy, whole blood transfusion, anticoagulants
C. Corticosteroids, splenectomy, immuno-suppressive drugs
D. Transfusion with frozen, anticoagulants, immunosuppressive drugs
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Corticosteroids, splenectomy, immuno-suppressive drugs

Explanation: ITP is an autoimmune disorder where the spleen destroys platelets. Treatment focuses on suppressing the immune system (Corticosteroids, Immunosuppressants) and removing the site of destruction (Splenectomy). Platelet transfusion is usually ineffective as they are quickly destroyed.

27.

B. Central chest discomfort worsened by deep breath
C. Axillary stabbing pain worsened by deep breathing
D. Retro-sternal stabbing pain worsened on lying in Fowler's position
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Axillary stabbing pain worsened by deep breathing

Explanation: Pleuritic pain is sharp, stabbing ("knife-like"), and localized to the area of inflammation (often lateral/axillary). It is characteristically worsened by inspiration (breathing deeply), coughing, or movement.

28.
Renal surgery is a very delicate procedure that can easily complicate to observable signs of;
A. Pain and fever
B. Infection and shock
C. Haemorrhage and pain
D. Haemorrhage and shock
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Haemorrhage and shock

Explanation: The kidneys are highly vascular organs, receiving 20-25% of cardiac output. The most significant and immediate risk following renal surgery is severe bleeding (hemorrhage) leading to hypovolemic shock.

29.
Joint swelling in rheumatoid arthritis is easily diagnosed and suspected to be due to:
A. Haematoma
B. Hypertrophied ligament
C. Fluid accumulation
D. Bony overgrowth
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Fluid accumulation

Explanation: In RA, the synovium becomes inflamed (synovitis) and produces excess synovial fluid (effusion), causing boggy, soft swelling. Bony overgrowth (osteophytes) is characteristic of Osteoarthritis, not RA.

30.
Aseptic technique is core in infection control. The correct sequence of instrument processing would be;
A. Cleaning, decontamination, isterilization
B. Cleaning, drying, sterilization
C. Decontamination, cleaning, sterilization
D. Decontamination, drying, sterilization
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Decontamination, cleaning, sterilization

42.
Understanding the pharmacokinetics of furosemide is key:
a) State its classification (1 mark)
b) Explain its mode of action (2 marks)
c) State one specific side effect (1 mark)
Show Answer

a) Classification: Loop Diuretic.

b) Mode of Action: It acts by inhibiting the sodium-potassium-chloride cotransporter (NKCC2) in the thick ascending limb of the Loop of Henle in the kidney. This prevents the reabsorption of sodium, chloride, and water, leading to increased urine output (diuresis).

c) Specific Side Effect: Hypokalemia (low potassium), Ototoxicity (hearing loss), or Hypotension.

43.
Explain three (3) negative effects that can be observed on patients undergoing treatment of hypothyroidism (3 marks)
Show Answer

These effects usually result from overtreatment (hyperthyroid state):

  1. Cardiovascular stress: Tachycardia, palpitations, angina, or hypertension due to increased metabolic demand.
  2. CNS stimulation: Insomnia, tremors, anxiety, and nervousness.
  3. Weight loss and heat intolerance: Due to excessive metabolic rate.
44.
You have been invited to give a health talk on Diabetic Ketoacidosis. Explain three (3) emergency interventions you will highlight for patients, upon being diagnosed with this condition. (6 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Fluid Resuscitation (Rehydration): Administer large volumes of IV Normal Saline to restore blood volume, correct dehydration, and flush out ketones.
  2. Insulin Therapy: Administer Regular Insulin intravenously to lower blood glucose and stop the production of ketones.
  3. Electrolyte Replacement: Strictly monitor and replace Potassium (K+) as insulin will drive potassium into cells, causing dangerous hypokalemia.
45.
When not well managed, pneumonia can lead to death. Outline four (4) major causes of death as a result of pneumonia. (4 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Respiratory Failure: Inability to oxygenate blood (Hypoxia) or remove CO2 (Hypercapnia).
  2. Sepsis/Septic Shock: Infection spreads to the bloodstream causing systemic organ failure.
  3. Empyema/Lung Abscess: Severe accumulation of pus complicating treatment.
  4. Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome (ARDS): Widespread lung inflammation and fluid filling alveoli.
46.
Elaborate three (3) red flags that you would observe in a client to indicate menorrhagia diagnosis (6 marks)

  2. Prolonged Duration: Bleeding lasting longer than a week (7 days).

48.
Discuss any five (5) factors to consider in the selection of a route of drug administration (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Condition of the patient: Is the patient conscious? Can they swallow? (If unconscious, oral is contraindicated).
  2. Urgency of effect required: IV is fastest for emergencies; Oral is slower.
  3. Properties of the drug: Is it destroyed by gastric acid? (If yes, cannot give orally). Is it irritating to veins?
  4. Site of desired action: Local (topical/inhaler) vs Systemic effect.
  5. Metabolism (First Pass Effect): Oral drugs pass through liver first; some routes bypass this (SL, IV).

SECTION 3: LONG ANSWER QUESTION (20 MARKS)

49.
Miss V a 25 years old lady has been undergoing treatment and follow up following a diagnosis of temporal brain tumour. As a result of deterioration of her condition, craniotomy has been scheduled as her treatment option. Describing the major clinical features she may present with, discuss the specific pre and post-operative nursing care she will receive until discharge (20 marks)
Show Answer

Comprehensive Answer Guide:

1. Major Clinical Features (Temporal Lobe Tumor)

  • Seizures: Complex partial seizures are common in temporal lobe lesions.
  • Auditory Hallucinations: Hearing sounds/voices due to involvement of the auditory cortex.
  • Language Deficits (Aphasia): If on the dominant side (Wernicke's area), difficulty understanding language (Receptive Aphasia).
  • Memory Issues: Short-term memory loss (Hippocampus involvement).
  • Visual Field Defects: Superior quadrantanopia ("Pie in the sky" defect).
  • Increased ICP Signs: Headache (worse in morning), vomiting, papilledema.

2. Pre-operative Nursing Care

  • Neurological Assessment: Establish baseline GCS, pupil reaction, and motor/sensory function for post-op comparison.
  • Psychological Support: Address anxiety about brain surgery and potential body image changes (shaving hair). Obtain informed consent.
  • Medication Administration:
    • Corticosteroids (Dexamethasone) to reduce cerebral edema.
    • Anticonvulsants (Phenytoin) to prevent seizures.
  • Preparation: Shave head (if ordered), NPO status, remove jewelry/dentures.

3. Post-operative Nursing Care (Until Discharge)

A. Immediate Care (ICU/HDU):

  • Airway/Breathing: Maintain patent airway. Monitor ABGs (CO2 retention increases ICP).
  • Neurological Monitoring: Check GCS, pupils, and limb movement q15min initially. Report decline immediately (sign of bleed/edema).
  • Positioning: Elevate Head of Bed (30 degrees) to facilitate venous drainage and lower ICP. Avoid neck flexion. Position on non-operative side.
  • Fluid Balance: Strict I/O. Monitor for Diabetes Insipidus (polyuria) or SIADH (fluid retention). Restrict fluids slightly to prevent cerebral edema.

B. Ongoing Ward Care:

  • Wound Care: Monitor dressing for CSF leak ("halo sign") or bleeding. Strict asepsis.
  • Pain Management: Codeine/Acetaminophen (avoid strong opioids that mask neuro signs).
  • Safety: Seizure precautions (padded rails). Fall prevention.
  • Rehabilitation: Speech therapy if aphasic. Physiotherapy for mobility.

C. Discharge Teaching:

  • Medication adherence (Anticonvulsants).
  • Wound care instructions (keep dry).
  • Signs to report: Fever, stiff neck, worsening headache, seizure.
  • Avoid strenuous activity/straining (Valsalva) for several weeks.
