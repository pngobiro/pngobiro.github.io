SECTION 1: MULTIPLE CHOICE QUESTIONS (40 MARKS)

1.
You are reviewing laboratory results for a client with cirrhosis, and you note your patient has elevated ammonia levels. The diet you would recommend is:
A. High-protein diet
B. Moderate-fat diet
C. High carbohydrate diet
D. Low protein diet
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Low protein diet

Explanation: Ammonia is a byproduct of protein metabolism. In cirrhosis, the liver cannot convert ammonia to urea for excretion. Therefore, restricting protein intake helps lower ammonia levels and prevent hepatic encephalopathy.

2.
Bronchodilators are medications used to dilate (widen) the airways in the lungs. Among the conditions in which they are contraindicated is in patients with
A. chronic bronchitis
B. peptic ulcer disease
C. megaloblastic anemia
D. Chronic hepatitis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. peptic ulcer disease

Explanation: Certain bronchodilators, specifically xanthine derivatives like Theophylline/Aminophylline, can stimulate gastric acid secretion and relax the lower esophageal sphincter, potentially exacerbating peptic ulcers or GERD. Options A is an indication; C and D are not standard contraindications.

3.
You are considering prescribing furosemide to a patient. The electrolyte imbalance you should watch out for is:
A. Hyperkalemia
B. Hypernatremia
C. Hypokalemia
D. Hyponatremia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Hypokalemia

Explanation: Furosemide (Lasix) is a loop diuretic that blocks the reabsorption of sodium and chloride in the loop of Henle. This action causes a significant loss of potassium (K+) in the urine, leading to hypokalemia. It also causes hyponatremia (D), but hypokalemia is the most clinically immediate and dangerous side effect regarding cardiac function.

4.
A patient is on infusion of packed red blood cells and they develop new onset of tachycardia, bounding pulse, crackles and wheezes. The first and most appropriate action to take is:
A. Maintain patient in bed with legs elevated and start oxygen therapy.
B. Place the patient in high-fowler's position and start oxygen therapy.
C. Reduce the rate of intravenous fluid infusion and start oxygen therapy.
D. Stop the infusion of the packed cells and monitor vital signs
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Place the patient in high-fowler's position and start oxygen therapy.

Explanation: The symptoms (tachycardia, bounding pulse, crackles, wheezes) indicate Transfusion Associated Circulatory Overload (TACO). The priority is to improve breathing (High-Fowler's position drops the diaphragm and decreases venous return) and oxygenation. While stopping/slowing the infusion is critical (usually part of the protocol), positioning helps breathing immediately. Note: Some protocols prioritize stopping infusion, but option D implies only monitoring, whereas B actively treats the respiratory distress.

5.
When administering blood products intravenously, it is important to use a compatible intravenous solution. The recommended choice is:
A. Lactated Ringers
B. Sodium chloride
C. 5% dextrose
D. 10% dextrose
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Sodium chloride

Explanation: 0.9% Normal Saline (Sodium chloride) is the ONLY fluid compatible with blood products. Dextrose causes hemolysis (cell bursting) due to hypotonicity relative to cells, and Lactated Ringers contains calcium which can cause clotting in the line.

6.
In a client who sustained blunt chest trauma, acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS) can develop as a complication. The earliest sign of the condition is:
A. A sense of air hunger.
B. Decrease in respiratory rate
C. Increase in chest pain on inspiration
D. Pallor on extremities
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. A sense of air hunger.

Explanation: The earliest signs of ARDS are often subtle and include tachypnea (increased rate, not decreased) and dyspnea (air hunger), often without significant findings on auscultation initially.

7.
When assessing a patient who has suffered multiple fractured rib, you would expect to find various findings related to their respiratory status. These may include:
A. Rapid deep respirations
B. Paradoxical respirations
C. Slow deep respirations
D. Painful inspirations
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Paradoxical respirations

Explanation: Multiple rib fractures, especially if they cause a flail chest (segment of rib cage breaks and becomes detached), lead to paradoxical respiration where the affected chest wall moves inward during inspiration and outward during expiration, opposite to normal movement. Painful inspiration (D) is also true but paradoxical movement is the specific mechanical sign of multiple/flail fractures.

8.
For a client with emphysema, positioning can play a crucial role in easing breathing and improving oxygenation. The optimal position often recommended is:
A. Fowlers
B. Prone
C. Trendelenburg
D. Supine
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Fowlers

Explanation: High Fowler's or sitting position allows for maximum expansion of the chest cavity and descent of the diaphragm, facilitating breathing in COPD/emphysema patients. Patients often assume the "tripod position" naturally.

9.
In a patient experiencing acute episodes of cholecystitis, the appropriate physical region for assessment of pain is:
A. Right lower quadrant, radiating to the back
B. Right lower quadrant, radiating to the umbilicus
C. Right upper quadrant, radiating to the right shoulder
D. Right upper quadrant, radiating to the left shoulder
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Right upper quadrant, radiating to the right shoulder

Explanation: The gallbladder is located in the Right Upper Quadrant (RUQ). Pain from cholecystitis typically presents there and refers to the right shoulder or scapula (Boas's sign) due to phrenic nerve irritation.

10.
In a patient with kidney failure, the electrolyte you would monitor as priority is:
A. Sodium
B. Potassium
C. Chloride
D. Magnesium
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Potassium

Explanation: Kidneys are responsible for excreting 90% of the body's potassium. In renal failure, potassium accumulates (Hyperkalemia), which can cause fatal cardiac arrhythmias. This is the most immediately life-threatening electrolyte imbalance.

11.
Ensuring adequate and safe pain control for a patient diagnosed with cancer involves a comprehensive and individualized approach: the most appropriate choice would be:
A. Collaborate with the patient to establish a realistic pain mangement plan
B. Keep a baseline level of pain so that the patient does not become addicted
C. Rely primarily on prescription and the WHO analgesic ladder
D. Gradually increasing the pain medication dose for maximum pain relieve
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Collaborate with the patient to establish a realistic pain mangement plan

Explanation: Patient-centered care is the standard. Collaborating ensures the plan meets the patient's specific goals and needs. Addiction is rarely a concern in terminal cancer pain (making B incorrect). While the WHO ladder is a tool (C), collaboration is the overarching principle.

12.

A. adhesions and contractures
B. stiffness and inabilty to move

15.
When developing a plan of care for a patient with deep vein thrombosis, it's important to include measures aimed at preventing complications such as pulmonary embolism and promoting recovery. The appropriate advice to the client would be:
A. Elevate the affected leg above heart level when lying down
B. Lay flat on the bed and keep the affected extremity lower than the heart level
C. Sit up on the bed with the affected extremity flexed
D. Elevate the affected leg with the extremities immobilized
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Elevate the affected leg above heart level when lying down

Explanation: Elevating the leg facilitates venous return by gravity, reducing venous pressure and edema. Option D is incorrect because immobilization is no longer strictly recommended; early ambulation is now preferred, and the leg shouldn't be rigidly immobilized.

16.
In a patient being treated for heart failure with intravenous bumetanide, an outcome that indicates the medication has achieved the expected effect is
A. The patient cough becomes productive of frothy pink sputum
B. The serum potassium levels change from 3.8 to 3.1 mEq/L
C. B-natriuretic peptide factor increases from 200 to 260pg/ml
D. Urine output increases from 20ml/hour to 50 ml/hour
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Urine output increases from 20ml/hour to 50 ml/hour

Explanation: Bumetanide is a potent loop diuretic. Its intended effect is to increase renal excretion of water and sodium (diuresis). Increased urine output indicates the drug is working. (A indicates worsening pulmonary edema; B indicates a side effect of hypokalemia; C indicates worsening heart failure strain).

17.
When caring for a hearing-impaired client, it's essential to use an approach that ensures effective communication and promotes their comfort and safety. The best choice would be to :
A. Speak loudly and frequently and ensure they get used to the method of communication
B. Ensure you are facing the client directly and maintain eye contact when addressing them
C. Speak at a normal volume avoiding a lot of eye contact as they may feel embarrassed by constant staring
D. Speak to the impaired ear as loudly as possible and maintain this mode of communication
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Ensure you are facing the client directly and maintain eye contact when addressing them

Explanation: Facing the client allows them to read lips and observe non-verbal cues/facial expressions, which significantly aids understanding. Shouting (A, D) distorts sound and can be painful. Avoiding eye contact (C) hinders communication.

18.
In a patient who has just sustained a closed fracture and is experiencing intense pain despite initial interventions like elevation, ice application, and analgesic administration, several potential problems could be causing this persistent pain. Choose the most possible reason from the following:
A. Poorly managed fracture alignment
B. Underlying Infection under the cast
C. Anxiety related to immobility
D. The etiology of fracture
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Poorly managed fracture alignment

Explanation: Severe, persistent pain that is disproportionate to the injury and unresponsive to analgesics is a hallmark sign of Compartment Syndrome (often caused by swelling/tight casts) or poor alignment causing muscle spasm/nerve impingement. While Compartment Syndrome isn't an option, poor alignment is a direct physical cause. Note: Infection (B) takes time to develop; Anxiety (C) doesn't cause "intense physical pain" usually.

19.

20.

A. Pulmonary embolism
B. Urinary tract infection

22.
You are teaching novice nursing students. The acid-base imbalance you would teach them to expect from a patient with obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) is:
A. Metabolic acidosis
B. Metabolic alkalosis
C. Respiratory acidosis
D. Respiratory alkalosis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Respiratory acidosis

Explanation: COPD causes air trapping and hypoventilation (inability to exhale fully). This leads to retention of Carbon Dioxide ($CO_2$). Excess $CO_2$ combines with water to form carbonic acid, leading to Respiratory Acidosis.

24.

25.
A client has been brought to the emergency department having sustained burns on the entire front torso, front and back of left arm and the front left leg. Calculate the total burn surface area:
A. 36%
B. 27%
C. 45%
D. 18%
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. 36%

Explanation (Rule of Nines):

  • Entire Front Torso: Chest (9%) + Abdomen (9%) = 18%.
  • Left Arm (Front & Back): Entire arm = 9%.
  • Front Left Leg: Half of the leg (Total leg is 18%) = 9%.

Total: 18% + 9% + 9% = 36%.

26.
A patient has fallen from a roof and needs airway management in the emergency department, and you know the most appropriate method to open the airway depends on the patient's condition as there is suspected injuries, particularly to the cervical spine. The most appropriate method of opening the airway for this patient is:
A. Flexed position
B. Head tilt-chin lift
C. Jaw thrust maneuver
D. Modified head tilt-chin lift
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Jaw thrust maneuver

Explanation: The Jaw Thrust maneuver opens the airway by displacing the mandible forward without tilting the head or moving the neck. This is the only safe method for patients with suspected cervical spine injury to prevent paralysis.

27.
You are inserting a nasogastric tube and the patient starts coughing and having difficulty breathing, the most appropriate intervention is to:
A. Quickly complete inserting the tube and observe for further distress
B. Notify the attending doctor immediately and put patient on oxygen
C. Remove the tube and reinsert immediately and put patient on oxygen
D. Remove the nasogastric tube immediately and wait for the distress to resolve

Correct Answer: D. Remove the nasogastric tube immediately and wait for the distress to resolve

29.
A patient with glaucoma is prescribed eye drops to reduce intraocular pressure. As the nurse is administering the medication, the essential action to ensure optimal therapeutic effect is:
A. Administer the eye drops directly onto the cornea for faster absorption.
B. Apply gentle pressure to the lacrimal sac for after instilling the eye drops.
C. Instruct the patient to blink vigorously for better distribution of the medication.
D. Administer the eye drops in the conjunctival sac while the patient looks upward.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Apply gentle pressure to the lacrimal sac for after instilling the eye drops.

Explanation: This is called Punctal Occlusion. It prevents the medication from draining into the nasolacrimal duct, which minimizes systemic absorption (side effects) and keeps the drug in the eye longer for a local therapeutic effect. (Note: Drops should be placed in the conjunctival sac (D), but B is the specific action for optimal effect and safety often emphasized in exams).

31.
In a patient with Addison's disease admitted with an adrenal crisis, the nursing action that should be prioritized is:
A. Administering intravenous fluids
B. Administering intravenous corticosteroids
C. Monitoring blood glucose levels
D. Administering a high dose of corticosteroids
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Administering intravenous fluids

Explanation: Adrenal crisis causes severe hypovolemic shock due to loss of sodium and water (lack of aldosterone). The immediate life-saving priority is restoring volume with IV fluids (usually saline/dextrose) to prevent vascular collapse. Steroid replacement (B/D) is crucial but typically follows or is concurrent with fluid resuscitation.

41.

Show Answer
  1. Ineffective Airway Clearance related to edema/inhalation injury (burns to face/neck).
  2. Deficient Fluid Volume related to fluid shift from intravascular space to interstitial space (edema/blisters).

  4. Risk for Infection related to loss of skin barrier.

42.
The procedure for blood transfusion requires attention to detail. Explain three (3) key aspects the nurse must adhere to for successful transfusion of blood among patients (6 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Patient Identification & Verification: Two nurses must double-check the patient's ID, blood type, Rh factor, expiration date, and unit number against the blood bag and request form to prevent ABO incompatibility reactions.
  2. Vital Signs Monitoring: Obtain baseline vitals. Monitor closely for the first 15 minutes (when reactions are most likely) and then hourly to detect signs of reaction (fever, tachycardia, hypotension) early.
  3. Proper Administration Technique: Use a large-bore needle (18G preferred) to prevent hemolysis, use dedicated blood tubing with a filter, administer only with Normal Saline, and complete infusion within 4 hours to prevent bacterial growth.
43.
You have been allocated in the operating theatre where many surgical operations have been scheduled for the day. Outline six (6) major categories of instruments in a general surgical set (6 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Cutting and Dissecting: Scalpels, scissors (Mayo, Metzenbaum).
  2. Grasping and Holding: Forceps (Tissue forceps, Sponge forceps), towel clips.
  3. Clamping and Occluding: Hemostats (Artery forceps like Crile, Mosquito) to control bleeding.
  4. Retracting and Exposing: Retractors (Hand-held like Langenbeck, Self-retaining like West's).
  5. Suturing/Stapling: Needle holders.
  6. Suctioning and Aspirating: Suction tips (Yankauer, Poole) to clear the field.
44.
Outline six (6) specific responsibilities of a nurse towards a patient undergoing a liver biopsy (6 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Pre-procedure: Check coagulation profile (PT/INR, Platelets) to prevent bleeding risk.
  2. Pre-procedure: Verify informed consent is signed.
  3. During: Assist patient to maintain proper position (supine with right arm under head) and instruct to hold breath during needle insertion.
  4. Post-procedure: Position patient on the Right Side with a pillow under the costal margin to apply pressure and prevent bleeding.
  5. Monitoring: Assess vital signs frequently (every 15 mins initially) for signs of shock (hemorrhage) or peritonitis.
  6. Safety: Maintain bed rest for several hours (usually 6-24 hours) as ordered.
45.
The principles of drug administration in nursing encompass a set of guidelines and practices aimed at ensuring safe, effective, and ethical medication management for patients. Explain three (3) of these principles (6 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. The "Rights" of Medication Administration: Strictly adhering to the rights (Right Patient, Drug, Dose, Route, Time, Documentation) to prevent errors.

46.
An elderly lady of seventy (75) years has been admitted in the orthopaedic ward following fracture of the femur. State four (4) key issues that must be considered in the choice of management for the patient (4 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Pre-existing Medical Conditions (Comorbidities): Does she have heart disease, diabetes, or osteoporosis that affects surgical risk or healing?

  3. Cognitive Status: Presence of dementia or confusion affects compliance with traction or post-op instructions.
  4. Risk of Complications: Elderly are at higher risk for DVT, pneumonia, and pressure ulcers from immobility; early mobilization is usually preferred (surgery vs traction).

48.
In a young female aged 25 years, several hormones play predominant roles in regulating various physiological processes. Explain two (2) of the hormones and the role they play (4 Marks)

49.

(20 marks)
Show Answer

Structure for Answer:

Clinical Sign (Observation) Likely Alteration (Pathophysiology) Measures/Interventions to Reverse
1. Polyuria (Excessive urination) & Signs of Dehydration (Dry skin, hypotension) Hyperglycemia leads to Osmotic Diuresis. Glucose > renal threshold draws water out, causing severe volume depletion. Fluid Resuscitation:
- Start IV Normal Saline (0.9% NaCl) rapidly (1L in first hour) to restore volume.
- Monitor Input/Output strictly.
- Check BP/Pulse for shock.
2. Kussmaul Respirations (Deep, rapid breathing) & Fruity breath odor Metabolic Acidosis (Ketosis). Body breaks down fat for energy -> Ketones (acids) accumulate. Lungs try to blow off CO2 (acid) to compensate. Acetone on breath. Insulin Therapy:
- Administer Short-acting (Regular) Insulin IV (bolus then infusion) to stop ketone production and drive glucose into cells.
- Bicarbonate may be used only in severe acidosis (pH < 7.0).
3. Altered Level of Consciousness (Confusion, drowsiness) Hyperosmolarity and Acidosis. Severe dehydration and electrolyte shifts affect brain cell function. Electrolyte Replacement & Safety:
- Monitor neurological status (GCS).
- Protect airway if unconscious.
- Correct fluids and electrolytes.
4. Cardiac Arrhythmias (Irregular pulse) Electrolyte Imbalance (Potassium). Acidosis shifts K+ out of cells (hyperkalemia initially), but total body K+ is low due to diuresis. Insulin causes K+ to shift back into cells (risk of hypokalemia). Potassium Replacement:
- Monitor Potassium levels strictly.
- Add Potassium to IV fluids once urine output is established, even if serum K+ is normal/low, to prevent crash during insulin therapy.
- Continuous cardiac monitoring (ECG).
5. Nausea/Vomiting/Abdominal Pain Ketone accumulation / Acidosis causes gastric irritation and ileus. Supportive Care:
- Keep NPO initially.
- Treat the underlying DKA (Insulin/Fluids) which resolves the GI symptoms.

Summary of Priority Management:

  1. Hydration: Rapid IV fluids.
  2. Insulin: Regular insulin IV to reverse hyperglycemia and ketosis.
  3. Electrolytes: Aggressive potassium replacement.
  4. Monitoring: Blood glucose hourly, ABGs, electrolytes.
