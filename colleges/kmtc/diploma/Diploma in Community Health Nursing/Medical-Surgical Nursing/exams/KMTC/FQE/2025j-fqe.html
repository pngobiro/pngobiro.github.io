SECTION 1: MULTIPLE CHOICE QUESTIONS (40 MARKS)

1.
The plasma membrane of a cell presents a barrier to the movement of electrolytes through it. The one characteristic for this barrier is:
A. There are no channels in the membrane for the passage of electrolytes.
B. Electrolytes are not soluble in the lipid of the membrane.
C. Electrolytes are too large to pass through membrane channels.
D. Membrane proteins electrically repel charged particles.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Electrolytes are not soluble in the lipid of the membrane.

Explanation: The plasma membrane is composed primarily of a phospholipid bilayer, which has a hydrophobic (water-fearing) interior. Electrolytes are charged ions (hydrophilic/water-loving) and cannot pass through this non-polar lipid barrier without assistance. They require specific transport proteins (channels or carriers) to cross.

Why other options are incorrect:

  • A: There are specific ion channels (proteins) that allow electrolytes to pass; the membrane itself is the barrier.
  • C: Electrolytes (ions) are very small; size is less of an issue than charge/solubility.
  • D: While membrane potential exists, it doesn't simply "repel" all charged particles; specific electrochemical gradients drive movement.
2.
The blood PH is constantly maintained by buffer systems. The reaction of the blood's phosphate buffer when a hydrogen ion is encountered is:
A. HPO42- destroys the hydrogen ion and becomes H2PO4- and blood pH decreases very slightly
B. H2PO4- destroys the hydrogen ion and becomes HPO42- and blood pH decreases very slightly
C. HPO42- destroys the hydrogen ion and becomes H2PO4- and blood pH increases very slightly
D. H2PO4- destroys the hydrogen ion and becomes HPO42- and blood pH increases very slightly
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. HPO42- destroys the hydrogen ion and becomes H2PO4- and blood pH decreases very slightly

Explanation: In the phosphate buffer system, the monohydrogen phosphate ion ($HPO_4^{2-}$) acts as a weak base. When it encounters a strong acid ($H^+$), it accepts the hydrogen ion to form dihydrogen phosphate ($H_2PO_4^-$), which is a weak acid. By binding the free $H^+$, it prevents a drastic drop in pH. The pH still decreases, but only very slightly compared to if no buffer were present.

Reaction: $HPO_4^{2-} + H^+ \rightarrow H_2PO_4^-$

3.
The ovarian cycle consists of several phases that occur in a specific sequence, controlled by hormonal changes. These phases include:
A. Menarche, menstrual cycle, menopause
B. Luteal, follicular and proliferative
C. Follicular, ovulation and the luteal
D. Menses, proliferative and secretory
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Follicular, ovulation and the luteal

Explanation: The ovarian cycle (changes in the ovary) has three phases:

  1. Follicular phase: Egg maturation (Days 1-13).
  2. Ovulation: Release of the egg (Day 14).
  3. Luteal phase: Formation of corpus luteum (Days 15-28).

Note: Option D refers to the Uterine (Menstrual) cycle phases.

4.
When giving an intramuscular (IM) injection using the Z-track technique, the nurse should:
A. Use a needle at least 1-inch long.
B. Apply pressure to the injection site.
C. Inject the medication quickly, and steadily withdraw the needle.
D. Do not massage the site because it may cause irritation.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Do not massage the site because it may cause irritation.

Explanation: The Z-track method is used to seal medication deep within the muscle and prevent leakage into subcutaneous tissue. Massaging the site can force the medication out of the muscle and cause tissue irritation or staining. The technique involves displacing the skin, injecting slowly (not quickly as in C), holding for 10 seconds, withdrawing, and releasing the skin.

5.
If a child with asthma is not rinsing their mouth after using a glucocorticoid inhaler, a common symptom to look for is:
A. Enlarged tonsils
B. Grey membrane in the throat
C. Persistent cough
D. White patches in mouth
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. White patches in mouth

Explanation: Inhaled corticosteroids can alter the local flora in the mouth, leading to an overgrowth of Candida albicans (oral thrush/candidiasis), which presents as white patches. Rinsing the mouth after use removes residual medication and prevents this complication.

7.
You are teaching a junior student on breaths sounds. Which of the following should a junior student report to you about vesicular breath sounds on return demonstration?
A. They are high-pitched, harsh sounds, with expiration being longer than inspiration.
B. They are noisy, strenuous respirations.
C. They are high-pitched sounds heard on inspiration when there is a narrowing of the upper airway.
D. They are soft, low-pitched sounds heard best over the base of the lungs with inspiration longer than expiration
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. They are soft, low-pitched sounds heard best over the base of the lungs with inspiration longer than expiration

Explanation:

  • Vesicular sounds: Soft, breezy, low-pitched. Inspiration > Expiration (3:1). Heard over most lung fields.
  • Bronchial sounds: High-pitched, loud, harsh. Expiration > Inspiration. (Option A).
  • Stridor: High-pitched on inspiration due to narrowing (Option C).
8.
For question 8, indicate whether the statements given are true or false

A. In medical asepsis, areas are considered contaminated if they come into contact with any object or surface that is not sterile.

Answer: FALSE (This describes surgical asepsis. In medical asepsis/clean technique, areas are contaminated if they touch known pathogens or "dirty" items).

B. Wearing gloves is the main effective way to help prevent the spread of organisms.

Answer: FALSE (Hand hygiene/washing is the single most effective way).

9.
Mr. P is an obese 62-year-old man scheduled for heart surgery. The surgical risks related to obesity that should be considered when performing an assessment for this patient include:
A. Delayed wound healing and wound infection
B. Alterations in fluid and electrolyte balance
C. Respiratory distress
D. Hemorrhage
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Delayed wound healing and wound infection

Explanation: Adipose (fatty) tissue has a poor blood supply (is relatively avascular). This leads to slower delivery of nutrients and oxygen required for healing, increasing the risk of wound dehiscence and infection.

10.
The nursing diagnosis of "Risk for Injury" following a stapedectomy is based on several factors associated with the surgery and its potential complications. The nurse identifies this risk due to the following knowledge:
A. Nystagmus may result from perilymph disturbances caused by surgery.
B. Stimulation of the labyrinth during surgery may cause vertigo and loss of balance.
C. Blowing the nose or coughing may precipitate dislodgement of the tympanic graft.
D. Postoperative tinnitus may decrease the patient's awareness of environmental hazards.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Stimulation of the labyrinth during surgery may cause vertigo and loss of balance.

Explanation: Stapedectomy involves surgery on the middle ear near the inner ear structures (labyrinth) which control balance. Stimulation or edema here often causes vertigo (dizziness), placing the patient at high risk for falls and injury.

11.
Glaucoma is a group of eye conditions that can lead to optic nerve damage and vision loss, often due to increased intraocular pressure. The important health promotion nursing intervention for a middle-aged adult related to glaucoma would be:
A. Teach individuals at risk for glaucoma about early signs and symptoms of the disease.
B. Prepare patients with glaucoma for lifestyle changes necessary to adapt to eventual blindness.
C. Promote measurements of intraocular pressure every 2 to 4 years for early detection and treatment of glaucoma.
D. Inform patients that glaucoma is curable if eye medications are administered before visual impairment has occurred.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Promote measurements of intraocular pressure every 2 to 4 years for early detection and treatment of glaucoma.

Explanation: Open-angle glaucoma (the most common type) is often asymptomatic until vision loss occurs. Regular screening (tonometry) is the most effective health promotion strategy. Glaucoma is treatable/manageable but generally not "curable" (Option D) and eventual blindness is not inevitable if treated (Option B).

12.
A patient is admitted to the emergency department with a wood splinter imbedded in the right eye. The priority intervention by the nurse would be:
A. Irrigate the eye with a large amount of sterile saline.
B. Carefully remove the splinter with a pair of sterile forceps.
C. Cover the eye with a dry sterile patch and a protective shield.
D. Apply light pressure on the closed eye to prevent bleeding
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Cover the eye with a dry sterile patch and a protective shield.

Explanation: For a penetrating eye injury, the priority is to prevent further movement of the object and additional damage. Do NOT remove the object (Option B). Do NOT apply pressure (Option D). Do NOT irrigate (Option A). Stabilize the object with a shield (like a cup) and patch the unaffected eye as well to prevent conjugate eye movement.

13.
The nurse receives an evening report on a patient who underwent posterior nasal packing for epistaxis earlier in the day. The priority patient assessment the nurse should pay attention to is:
A. Patient's temperature
B. Level of the patient's pain
C. Drainage on the nasal dressing
D. Oxygen saturation by pulse oximetry
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Oxygen saturation by pulse oximetry

Explanation: Posterior nasal packing can slip and obstruct the airway. Furthermore, the packing itself can cause hypoventilation and hypoxia, especially in older adults or those with respiratory issues (nasopulmonary reflex). Airway/Breathing is the priority (ABC).

14.
A newly posted intern is considering either endotracheal or tracheostomy in the management of a patient with long term upper airway obstruction. You will inform him that an advantage of a tracheostomy over an endotracheal (ET) tube is:
A. A tracheostomy is safer to perform in an emergency.
B. An ET tube has a higher risk of tracheal pressure necrosis.
C. A tracheostomy tube allows for more comfort and mobility.
D. An ET tube is more likely to lead to lower respiratory tract infection.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. A tracheostomy tube allows for more comfort and mobility.

Explanation: Tracheostomies bypass the mouth and throat, allowing the patient to potentially speak (with valves), eat, and move the head more freely compared to an ET tube which goes through the mouth/vocal cords. They are preferred for long-term management.

15.
To determine whether a tension pneumothorax is developing in a patient with chest trauma, the confirmatory sign the nurse should assess for is:
A. Dull percussion sounds on the injured side
B. Severe respiratory distress and tracheal deviation
C. Muffled and distant heart sounds with decreasing blood pressure
D. Decreased movement and diminished breath sounds on the affected side
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Severe respiratory distress and tracheal deviation

Explanation: While diminished breath sounds occur in simple pneumothorax, the hallmark of tension pneumothorax is the mediastinal shift caused by trapped air pressure, leading to tracheal deviation away from the injured side and severe hemodynamic compromise.

16.
A patient is scheduled for bone marrow examination. The most appropriate teaching a nurse should give to the patient regarding the procedure is:
A. The procedure will be done under general anesthesia because it is so painful.
B. The patient will not have any pain after the area at the puncture site is anesthetized.
C. The patient will experience a brief, very sharp pain during aspiration of the bone marrow.
D. There will be no pain during the procedure, but an ache will be present several days afterward.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. The patient will experience a brief, very sharp pain during aspiration of the bone marrow.

Explanation: While local anesthesia numbs the skin and periosteum, it cannot numb the inside of the bone. When the marrow is aspirated (suctioned), patients typically feel a sudden, sharp pain that lasts only for the duration of the suction.

17.
A 20-yr-old female patient is in the emergency department for anorexia and fatigue management. She takes phenytoin for a seizure disorder and oral contraceptives. For which type of anemia is this patient most at risk of:
A. Aplastic anemia
B. Hemolytic anemia
C. Iron-deficiency anemia
D. Folic acid deficiency anemia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Folic acid deficiency anemia

Explanation: Phenytoin (Dilantin) interferes with the absorption and metabolism of folic acid. Long-term use without supplementation often leads to megaloblastic (folate deficiency) anemia.

18.
A patient has been having chest pain for the last two weeks. The assessment findings that will differentiate unstable angina from an myocardial infarction is:
A. ECG changes present at the onset of the pain
B. A chest x-ray indicating left ventricular hypertrophy
C. Serum troponin levels increased 4 to 6 hours after the onset
D. Creatine kinase MB (CK-MB) elevations that peak 6 hours after the pain
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Serum troponin levels increased 4 to 6 hours after the onset

Explanation: Unstable angina and Myocardial Infarction (MI) can present with similar pain and ECG changes (like ST depression or T wave inversion, though ST elevation is specific to STEMI). The definitive difference is cardiac tissue death, indicated by elevated cardiac biomarkers like Troponin.

19.
In a patient experiencing ventricular fibrillation (VF), the rationale for using cardiac defibrillation is to:
A. Enhance repolarization and relaxation of ventricular myocardial cells
B. Provide an electrical impulse that stimulates normal myocardial contractions
C. Depolarize the cells of the myocardium to allow the SA node to resume pacemaker function
D. Deliver an electrical impulse to the heart at the time of ventricular contraction to convert the heart to a sinus rhythm
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Depolarize the cells of the myocardium to allow the SA node to resume pacemaker function

Explanation: Defibrillation delivers a strong electrical shock that simultaneously depolarizes all myocardial cells. This induces a temporary asystole (standstill), allowing the heart's natural pacemaker (SA node) to regain control and re-establish a normal sinus rhythm.

20.
Patient N is diagnosed with infective endocarditis. To diagnose Janeway's lesions, what physical presentations would be a pointer:
A. Hemorrhagic retinal lesions
B. Black longitudinal streaks in nail beds
C. Painful red or purple lesions on fingers or toes
D. Flat, red, painless spots on the palm of hands and soles of feet
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Flat, red, painless spots on the palm of hands and soles of feet

Explanation:

  • Janeway lesions: Flat, painless, red spots on palms/soles (D).
  • Osler nodes: Painful, red nodules on fingers/toes (C).
  • Splinter hemorrhages: Streaks in nail beds (B).
  • Roth spots: Retinal hemorrhages (A).
21.
A patient with a gastric outlet obstruction has been treated with Nasogastric decompression. After the first 24 hours, the patient develops nausea and increased upper abdominal bowel sounds. The priority action by the nurse would be:
A. Check the patency of the nasogastric tube.
B. Place the patient in a recumbent position.
C. Assess the patient's vital signs and circulatory status.
D. Encourage the patient to deep breath and consciously relax.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Check the patency of the nasogastric tube.

Explanation: The NGT is in place to decompress the stomach. If the patient develops nausea and increased sounds (indicating accumulation), the tube is likely blocked/clogged. The priority is to restore function to relieve the obstruction/nausea.

41.
Between the two layers of dura mater are venous sinuses that collects blood from the brain. With the help of a well labelled diagram, illustrate these sinuses (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Answer Guide:

The student should draw a cross-section or sagittal view of the cranium showing:

  • Superior Sagittal Sinus: Along the superior falx cerebri.
  • Inferior Sagittal Sinus: Along the inferior free edge of falx cerebri.
  • Straight Sinus: Joining the inferior sagittal sinus to the confluence.
  • Transverse Sinuses: Running laterally from the confluence.
  • Sigmoid Sinuses: Draining into the Internal Jugular Vein.
  • Confluence of Sinuses: Meeting point at the occiput.

Diagram should be clearly labeled.

49.
Mrs. J a nurse who has been working in a medical surgical unit is admitted in medical ward presenting with jaundice 2+, swollen abdomen ankles and feet. She reports passing dark coloured urine, light coloured stool and an itchy skin. She experiences difficulty in breathing that improves when propped up. Two weeks prior to admission, before handing over the shift, she complained of stomachache and nausea. A diagnosis of decompensated cirrhosis is made. Using the nursing process, describe the nursing management of Mrs. J within 48hrs. (20 marks)
Show Answer

Comprehensive Answer Guide:

1. Assessment:

  • Respiratory: Assess dyspnea, O2 sat, lung sounds (pleural effusion?), abdominal girth restricting diaphragm.
  • Fluid/Electrolytes: Assess edema (ankles/feet), ascites (abdominal girth, fluid wave), daily weight, intake/output balance.
  • Skin: Assess jaundice, pruritus (scratch marks), skin integrity.
  • Neurological: Assess for hepatic encephalopathy (confusion, asterixis).
  • GI: Stool color (clay), urine (dark tea), nausea.

2. Nursing Diagnosis (Priority):

  • Ineffective Breathing Pattern r/t ascites pressure on diaphragm.
  • Excess Fluid Volume r/t compromised regulatory mechanism (low albumin, high aldosterone).
  • Impaired Skin Integrity r/t pruritus and edema.

3. Planning/Interventions:

  • Respiratory:
    • Position in Semi-Fowler’s or High-Fowler’s (as patient reported improvement).
    • Administer O2 if hypoxic.
  • Fluid Management:
    • Administer diuretics (Spironolactone/Furosemide) as ordered.
    • Restrict fluid and sodium intake.
    • Measure abdominal girth daily.
    • Daily weights.
    • Monitor albumin levels and administer replacement if ordered.
  • Skin Care:
    • Keep nails short to prevent scratching.
    • Use cool water/lotions for itch (calamine).
    • Turn every 2 hours to prevent sores on edematous tissue.
  • Safety:
    • Bleeding precautions (soft toothbrush, electric razor) due to potential coagulopathy.

4. Evaluation:

  • Patient breathes comfortably with O2 sat >95%.
  • Weight loss evident (fluid loss).
  • Reduced edema and abdominal girth.
  • Skin remains intact without injury.
