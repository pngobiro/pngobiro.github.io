Module I: Gender & Sexual Health

Section II: Short Answer Questions (20 Marks)

1.
List SIX (6) effects of early marriage on health. (3 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Obstructed Labour: Due to cephalopelvic disproportion in a growing pelvis.
  2. Obstetric Fistula: A devastating complication of prolonged obstructed labour.
  3. Maternal Mortality: Higher risk of death during childbirth.
  4. Poor Perinatal Outcomes: Increased risk of prematurity, low birth weight, and stillbirths.
  5. Malnutrition/Anemia: Increased nutritional demands of pregnancy on a growing adolescent body.
  6. Mental Health Issues: Depression, isolation, and psychological trauma.
  7. Increased susceptibility to STIs/HIV: Due to inability to negotiate safe sex.

3.
Explain THREE (3) tools used in Gender analysis. (6 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. The Harvard Analytical Framework (Activity Profile): Analyzes "who does what" (productive, reproductive, community roles), "who has what" (access to resources), and "who controls what" (decision making).
  2. The Moser Framework: Focuses on strategic gender needs (status, power) vs. practical gender needs (basic needs) and the triple role of women (productive, reproductive, community management).
  3. The Sara Longwe Framework (Women's Empowerment Framework): Assesses the level of women's empowerment across five levels: Welfare, Access, Conscientization, Participation, and Control.
  4. 24-Hour Activity Clock: A visual tool to map out the daily activities of men and women to highlight disparities in workload and leisure.
4.
State FIVE (5) roles of the community in preventing gender based violence. (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Reporting: Identifying and reporting cases of GBV to authorities (Police, Chiefs).
  2. Support: Providing safe spaces, shelter, and emotional support to survivors.
  3. Advocacy/Education: Sensitizing community members against harmful cultural practices (e.g., FGM, wife beating).
  4. Intervention: Community policing and intervening to stop violence when it occurs ("Nyumba Kumi").
  5. Social Sanctions: Stigmatizing perpetrators and refusing to accept violence as a norm.
  6. Resource Mobilization: Supporting victims with transport or funds to access healthcare/legal aid.

Section III: Long Answer Question (20 Marks)

1.
Rape is on the rise in Kenya affecting many families:

a) Describe the management of Rape survivor in the health facility. (10 Marks)

Show Answer

Management follows a holistic approach (Medical, Forensic, Psychological):

  1. Immediate Triage & First Aid: Treat life-threatening physical injuries (bleeding, fractures) immediately. Ensure privacy and obtain consent.
  2. History Taking: Take a detailed history of the incident (date, time, nature of assault) for medical and legal purposes. Document verbatim.
  3. Physical & Forensic Examination:
    • Examine the whole body for injuries.
    • Perform a speculum exam to assess genital trauma.
    • Collect forensic samples (HVS, clothing, debris) before cleaning the patient. Fill the PRC/P3 Form.
  4. STI Prevention: Administer prophylactic antibiotics for Gonorrhea, Chlamydia, Syphilis, and Trichomoniasis (e.g., Ceftriaxone, Azithromycin/Doxycycline, Metronidazole).
  5. HIV Prevention (PEP): Assess HIV risk. Start Post-Exposure Prophylaxis (PEP) within 72 hours (optimally <4 hours). Test baseline HIV status first.
  6. Pregnancy Prevention (ECP): Administer Emergency Contraception (Levonorgestrel) within 72-120 hours if the survivor is female and of reproductive age.
  7. Tetanus & Hep B: Give Tetanus Toxoid and Hepatitis B vaccine if indicated.
  8. Psychological Support: Provide immediate trauma counseling and schedule follow-up mental health care.

b) Explain THREE (3) immediate complications of Female Genital Mutilation. (6 Marks)

Show Answer
  1. Severe Pain and Shock: Neurogenic shock results from the cutting of sensitive nerve endings in the clitoris/labia without anesthesia.
  2. Hemorrhage (Bleeding): Cutting the clitoral artery or other vessels can lead to profuse bleeding and hypovolemic shock (can be fatal).
  3. Acute Urine Retention: Caused by swelling, pain, or fear of urinating on the raw wound, leading to bladder distension and potential infection.
  4. Infection (Sepsis): Use of unsterile instruments can introduce bacteria (Tetanus, Sepsis) causing severe systemic infection.

c) State FOUR (4) classification of female genital mutilation. (4 Marks)

Show Answer
  1. Type I (Clitoridectomy): Partial or total removal of the clitoris and/or the prepuce.
  2. Type II (Excision): Partial or total removal of the clitoris and the labia minora, with or without excision of the labia majora.
  3. Type III (Infibulation): Narrowing of the vaginal orifice with creation of a covering seal by cutting and appositioning the labia minora and/or majora (Pharaonic).
  4. Type IV: All other harmful procedures to the female genitalia for non-medical purposes (e.g., pricking, piercing, incising, scraping, cauterization).

Module II: Family Planning & Reproductive Health

Section II: Short Answer Questions (20 Marks)

1.
State five (5) health education shared with client after insertion of implants. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Wound Care: Keep the insertion site dry for at least 24 hours. Keep the pressure bandage on for 24-48 hours.
  2. Expected Side Effects: Inform about potential menstrual changes (spotting, irregular bleeding, or amenorrhea) which are normal.
  3. Pain Management: Mild pain or bruising at the site is common; take mild analgesics if needed.
  4. Effectiveness: The implant is effective within 24 hours (if inserted within first 7 days of cycle) or after 7 days otherwise.
  5. Return Date: Inform on the lifespan (3 or 5 years) but emphasize she can return anytime for removal or if she experiences problems (infection signs, severe pain).
2.
State FIVE (5) reasons for low uptake of long-term family planning methods in Kenya. (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Myths and Misconceptions: Fear of infertility, migration of implants in the body, or interference with libido.
  2. Provider Bias: Health workers may prefer short-term methods or deny LARC to nulliparous women/youth.
  3. Lack of Skilled Staff: Shortage of providers trained in insertion and removal of IUCDs/Implants.
  4. Stock-outs: Unreliable supply chain for LARC commodities and equipment.
  5. Cost: Although often free in public facilities, associated costs (transport, registration) or cost in private sector may be a barrier.
  6. Fear of Side Effects: Concerns about bleeding patterns or pain during insertion.
3.
State FOUR (4) advantages of lactational amenorrhea as a method of family planning. (4 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Effective: 98% effective if all three criteria (amenorrhea, exclusive breastfeeding, <6 months) are met.
  2. Cost-free: Requires no commodities or financial investment.
  3. Immediate: Can be started immediately after birth.
  4. Health Benefits: Promotes exclusive breastfeeding which boosts infant immunity and nutrition.
  5. No Side Effects: Natural method with no hormonal or physical side effects for the mother.
4.
State THREE (3) WHO Medical Eligibility Criteria guidelines on safety provision of contraceptive methods to clients with medical conditions. (6 marks)
Show Answer

The WHO MEC categorizes medical conditions to determine safety:

  1. Category 1: A condition for which there is no restriction for the use of the contraceptive method. (Use method in any circumstances).
  2. Category 2: A condition where the advantages of using the method generally outweigh the theoretical or proven risks. (Generally use).
  3. Category 3: A condition where the theoretical or proven risks usually outweigh the advantages of using the method. (Use only if no other method is available/acceptable; requires careful monitoring).
  4. Category 4: A condition which represents an unacceptable health risk if the contraceptive method is used. (Do not use).

Section III: Long Answer Question (20 Marks)

1.
Jane, 40 years old lady comes to your health facility for Family Planning Method. After counselling she makes an informed choice of IUCD.

a) State FOUR (4) benefits of IUCD (4 marks)

Show Answer
  1. Long-term protection: Copper T 380A is effective for up to 12 years.
  2. Reversibility: Fertility returns immediately after removal.
  3. Non-hormonal: Copper IUD has no hormonal side effects (suitable for those who cannot use hormones).
  4. Cost-effective: Low cost over time compared to short-term methods.
  5. Convenience: "Forgettable" method; requires no daily action or interruption of sex.

b) State SIX (6) conditions you will consider before initiating Family Planning method to Jane (6 Marks)

Show Answer

Before inserting an IUCD, screen for contraindications (MEC 3/4):

  1. Pregnancy: Must rule out pregnancy (LMP, symptoms).
  2. Pelvic Infection: Current or recent (within 3 months) PID or STI (Chlamydia/Gonorrhea).
  3. Unexplained Vaginal Bleeding: Suspicion of malignancy or serious pathology.
  4. Cervical or Endometrial Cancer: Known or suspected.
  5. Uterine Abnormalities: Fibroids distorting the uterine cavity which may prevent proper placement.
  6. Puerperal Sepsis: Postpartum infection within the last 48 hours to 4 weeks.
  7. Anemia: Severe anemia (for Copper IUD as it may increase bleeding).

c) "GATHER" is a counselling technique applied when counselling a client for Family Planning method. Discuss how you will implement "GATHER" when counselling Jane (10 Marks)

Show Answer
  • G - Greet: Welcome Jane warmly, offer her a seat, introduce yourself, and ensure privacy and confidentiality to build rapport.
  • A - Ask: Ask Jane about her reproductive goals (does she want more children?), medical history (age 40, parity), sexual history (STI risk), and why she prefers the IUCD. Assess her knowledge about it.
  • T - Tell: Explain the IUCD method to Jane. Tell her how it works (spermicidal), benefits (long acting), potential side effects (heavier menses initially), and danger signs. Confirm it suits her age (40 yrs) and needs.
  • H - Help: Help Jane confirm her decision. Verify she has no contraindications (screening checklist). Support her choice if she is medically eligible.
  • E - Explain: Explain the insertion procedure (speculum exam, cleaning, insertion). Teach her how to check the strings. Explain what to expect after insertion (cramping, spotting).
  • R - Return: Schedule a follow-up visit (usually after next menses or 3-6 weeks). Encourage her to return anytime if she experiences pain, expulsion, or infection signs. Provide the method (perform insertion).
