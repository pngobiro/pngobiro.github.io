Module I: Cardiovascular Nursing

Section I: Multiple Choice Questions (10 Marks)

1.
When taking vitals of a patient, you palpate popliteal pulse and feel a vibration at the site. You record this finding as?
A. Thready, weak pulse
B. Bruit at the artery site
C. Bounding pulse volume
D. Thrill of the popliteal artery
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Thrill of the popliteal artery

Explanation: A "thrill" is a palpable vibration caused by turbulent blood flow (often associated with an aneurysm or fistula). A bruit is an audible sound of turbulence heard with a stethoscope.

2.
MR. X in outpatient unit is diagnosed with hypertensive urgency and intravenous treatment is initiated. The priority nursing intervention for the patient is?
A. Monitoring hourly urine output for drug effectiveness
B. Titrating drug dosages based on BP measurements every 2 to 3 minutes
C. Providing continuous electrocardiographic (ECG) monitoring to detect side effects of the drug
D. Monitoring and recording blood pressure half hourly
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Titrating drug dosages based on BP measurements every 2 to 3 minutes

Explanation: If IV antihypertensives are used (typically for Hypertensive Emergency, though question says Urgency + IV), continuous/frequent BP monitoring (every 2-3 mins initially) is crucial to prevent rapid hypotension, which can cause ischemia. Urgency is usually treated orally over 24-48 hours, but the use of IV implies a need for close titration.

3.
When instructing the patient with angina about taking sublingual nitroglycerin tablets, what should the nurse teach the patient?
A. To lie or sit and place one tablet under the tongue when chest pain occurs
B. To take the tablet with a large amount of water so it will dissolve right away
C. That if one tablet does not relieve the pain in 15 minutes, the patient should go to the hospital
D. That if the tablet causes dizziness and a headache, stop the medication and call the doctor or go to the hospital
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. To lie or sit and place one tablet under the tongue when chest pain occurs

Explanation: Nitroglycerin causes vasodilation and hypotension/dizziness. Sitting prevents syncope/falls. The protocol is usually: take 1 tablet, wait 5 mins; if pain persists, take 2nd; wait 5 mins; take 3rd. If pain persists after 3 doses (or 5 mins after 1st dose per newer guidelines), call EMS. Option C is incorrect on timing (wait 5 mins, not 15). D is incorrect as headache is a common side effect, not an allergy.

4.
A patient is hospitalized after a successful resuscitation of an episode of sudden cardiac death. During the care of the patient, the most important nursing intervention is?
A. Continuous ECG monitoring
B. Frequent assessment of heart sounds
C. Auscultation of the carotid arteries
D. Monitoring of airway status and respiratory patterns
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Continuous ECG monitoring

Explanation: Patients who survive sudden cardiac death are at extremely high risk for recurrent dysrhythmias (VT/VF). Continuous ECG monitoring is the priority to detect and treat life-threatening rhythms immediately.

5.
You are caring for a patient diagnosed with acute left sided failure. The initial physical assessment finding you would expect to be present in a patient is?
A. Bubbling crackles and tachycardia
B. Hepatosplenomegaly and tachypnea
C. Peripheral edema and cool, diaphoretic skin
D. Frothy blood-tinged sputum and distended jugular veins
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Bubbling crackles and tachycardia

Explanation: Left-sided heart failure causes backup of blood into the lungs (pulmonary edema), manifesting as crackles (rales), dyspnea, and tachycardia. Hepatosplenomegaly, peripheral edema, and JVD (distended veins) are signs of Right sided heart failure.

6.
A patient with infective endocarditis of a prosthetic mitral valve develops a left hemiparesis and visual changes. Which of the following should the nurse expect to be included in collaborative management of the patient?
A. Embolectomy
B. Surgical valve replacement
C. Administration of anticoagulants
D. Higher than usual antibiotic dosages
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Surgical valve replacement

Explanation: The symptoms (hemiparesis, visual changes) suggest a septic embolus to the brain (stroke) from the valve vegetation. Prosthetic valve endocarditis with embolic complications is a strong indication for urgent surgical valve replacement to remove the source of emboli.

7.
A patient with acute pericarditis has markedly distended jugular veins, decreased BP, tachycardia, tachypnea, and muffled heart sounds. The nurse recognizes that these symptoms occur when?
A. The pericardial space is obliterated with scar tissue and thickened pericardium
B. Excess pericardial fluid compresses the heart and prevents adequate diastolic filling
C. The parietal and visceral pericardial membranes adhere to each other...
D. Fibrin accumulation on the visceral pericardium infiltrates into the myocardium...
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Excess pericardial fluid compresses the heart and prevents adequate diastolic filling

Explanation: This describes Cardiac Tamponade (Beck's Triad: Hypotension, JVD, Muffled heart sounds). Fluid accumulation in the pericardial sac compresses the heart, restricting filling and reducing cardiac output.

8.
A patient is scheduled for an open surgical valvuloplasty of the mitral valve. The true statement about this surgical procedure is?
A. Cardiopulmonary bypass is not required with this procedure.
B. Valve repair is a palliative measure, whereas valve replacement is curative.
C. The operative mortality rate is lower in valve repair than in valve replacement.
D. Patients with valve repair do not require postoperative anticoagulation...
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. The operative mortality rate is lower in valve repair than in valve replacement.

Explanation: Valve repair preserves the native valve structure and subvalvular apparatus (papillary muscles/chordae), leading to better left ventricular function and lower operative mortality/morbidity compared to replacement.

9.
A patient with a small abdominal aortic aneurysm is not a good surgical candidate. The best way to prevent expansion of the aneurysm is to?
A. Avoid strenuous physical exertion.
B. Control hypertension with prescribed therapy.
C. Comply with prescribed anticoagulant therapy.
D. Maintain a low-calcium diet to prevent calcification of the vessel.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Control hypertension with prescribed therapy.

Explanation: High blood pressure increases the stress on the arterial wall, promoting aneurysm expansion and risk of rupture. Controlling BP is the primary medical management.

10.
A nurse notes 2+ bilateral edema in the lower extremities of a client with myocardial infarction who was admitted 2 days ago. The nurse would plan to do which of the following next?
A. Review the intake and output records for the last 2 days
B. Change the time of diuretic administration from morning to evening
C. Request a sodium restriction of 1 g/day from the physician
D. Order daily weights starting the following morning
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Review the intake and output records for the last 2 days

Explanation: Bilateral edema suggests fluid overload or heart failure complication. The first step is to assess the fluid balance status (I/O records) to confirm retention before making changes.

12.
Patient Y, diagnosed with acute decompensated heart failure is on oxygen 2 L/min via nasal cannula. However, the SPO2 remains is 84%. Outline five specific interventions you will institute for the patient (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Escalate Oxygen Therapy: Switch to a non-rebreather mask or high-flow nasal cannula to deliver higher FiO2.
  2. Positioning: Place the patient in High Fowler's position (sitting upright) to maximize chest expansion and reduce venous return (preload).
  3. Diuretics: Administer IV loop diuretics (e.g., Furosemide) as ordered to remove pulmonary fluid.
  4. Vasodilators: Administer Nitroglycerin/Morphine (if ordered/BP allows) to reduce preload and afterload.
  5. Monitor: Continuous monitoring of Vital signs, ECG, and respiratory status (lung sounds). Prepare for possible CPAP/BiPAP if no improvement.
13.
Distinguish between left-sided and right-sided heart failure (5 marks)
Show Answer
Feature Left-Sided Heart Failure Right-Sided Heart Failure
Primary Pathophysiology Failure of LV to pump blood to body. Failure of RV to pump blood to lungs.
Congestion Site Pulmonary (Lungs). Systemic (Body).
Key Symptoms Dyspnea, Orthopnea, Cough (frothy sputum). Peripheral edema, Ascites, Weight gain.
Signs Bibasilar crackles (rales), S3 gallop. Jugular Venous Distension (JVD), Hepatomegaly.
Common Cause HTN, Coronary Artery Disease. Left-sided failure (Cor Pulmonale).
14.
A newly admitted patient is suspected to be having acquired valvular heart disease. State five (5) diagnostic procedures that will be carried out to confirm the diagnosis (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Echocardiogram (Echo): Transthoracic or Transesophageal (TEE) to visualize valve structure and function (stenosis/regurgitation). Gold standard.
  2. Chest X-ray (CXR): To check for heart enlargement and pulmonary congestion.
  3. Electrocardiogram (ECG): To detect arrhythmias and hypertrophy (e.g., atrial fibrillation, LVH).
  4. Cardiac Catheterization: To measure pressure gradients across valves and check coronary arteries.
  5. Exercise Stress Test: To evaluate functional capacity and symptom onset.

Section III: Long Answer Question (20 Marks)

15.
Mr. T, a 50-year-old bricklayer, is brought to the emergency department by ambulance with a suspected diagnosis of myocardial infarction. He appears ashen, is diaphoretic and with severe chest pain. His vital signs are temperature 38°C, pulse 100 Bpm, and Blood pressure 96/70 mmHg. Using the nursing process, describe the nursing management of Mr. T within the first 12 hours. (20 marks)
Show Answer

1. Assessment:

  • Pain: PQRST assessment of chest pain (Site, Quality, Radiation to jaw/arm, Severity).
  • Cardiovascular: Monitor BP (Hypotension 96/70), HR (Tachycardia 100), Rhythm (ECG monitoring for arrhythmias).
  • Respiratory: Check for dyspnea, lung sounds (crackles indicating LV failure).
  • Skin: Ashen, diaphoretic (signs of shock/poor perfusion).
  • History: Allergies (Aspirin), recent Viagra use (contraindication for Nitrates).

2. Nursing Diagnosis:

  • Acute Pain related to myocardial ischemia.
  • Decreased Cardiac Output related to altered contractility/rate/rhythm.
  • Ineffective Tissue Perfusion (Cardiopulmonary) related to interrupted blood flow.
  • Anxiety/Fear related to perceived threat of death.

3. Implementation (MONA Protocol & Care):

  • Oxygen: Administer oxygen (if SpO2 <94% or dyspneic) to improve myocardial oxygen supply.
  • Aspirin: Administer chewable Aspirin (160-325mg) immediately (if not given by EMS) to inhibit platelet aggregation.
  • Nitroglycerin: Administer sublingual NTG (monitor BP closely as he is borderline hypotensive; hold if SBP <90). Relieves pain and preload.
  • Morphine: Administer IV Morphine if pain persists after NTG. Reduces pain, anxiety, and oxygen demand.
  • ECG: Obtain 12-lead ECG within 10 minutes to confirm STEMI vs NSTEMI.
  • IV Access: Establish 2 large-bore IV lines for meds and fluids.
  • Labs: Draw cardiac enzymes (Troponin, CK-MB), electrolytes, coagulation profile.
  • Reperfusion Prep: Prepare for Thrombolytics (if indicated/no cath lab) or transfer to Cath Lab for PCI (Angioplasty) within 90 minutes.
  • Bed Rest: Maintain bed rest (Semi-Fowler's) to reduce myocardial oxygen consumption.
  • Reassurance: Provide calm reassurance to reduce anxiety and sympathetic stress on the heart.

4. Evaluation:

  • Relief of chest pain (Pain score 0/10).
  • Stable vital signs and ECG rhythm.
  • Effective reperfusion (ST-segment resolution).

Module II: Haematological Nursing

Section I: Multiple Choice Questions (10 Marks)

1.
The following refer to events that occur during haemostasis:
i. Lysis of clot
ii. Vascular response (spasm)
iii. Plasma clotting factors (Coagulation)
iv. Platelet plug formation
Which one represents the correct sequence of occurrence from the first to the last event?
A. iii, iv, ii, i
B. iii, iv, i, ii
C. ii, iv, iii, i
D. iv, i, iii, ii
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. ii, iv, iii, i

Explanation: Hemostasis sequence:
1. Vascular Spasm (ii) - immediate constriction.
2. Platelet Plug Formation (iv) - temporary seal.
3. Coagulation (iii) - formation of fibrin clot.
4. Clot Retraction and Lysis (i) - healing and removal.

2.
Hematopoiesis occur in the pluripotent stem cell, which of the following statements is correct?
A. Most of the bone marrow is inactive at birth
B. All lymphocytes originate in the bone marrow
C. Erythropoietin is produced by cells in the liver
D. Pro-erythroblast is the earliest red cell precursor
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Pro-erythroblast is the earliest red cell precursor

Explanation:
A: Marrow is active (red) at birth.
B: T-lymphocytes mature in Thymus.
C: Erythropoietin is mainly produced in Kidneys (small amount in liver).
D: Correct, Proerythroblast is the first committed cell in the RBC line.

3.
A newly admitted child has been diagnosed with Factor VIII deficiency, the nurse understands that the child is suffering from?
A. Von Willebrand disease
B. Sickle Cell Anemia
C. Hemophilia A
D. Hemophilia B
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Hemophilia A

Explanation: Hemophilia A is deficiency of Factor VIII. Hemophilia B (Christmas Disease) is Factor IX deficiency.

4.
In management of blood disorders, venesection is preferred in the management of: -
A. Erythrocytosis
B. Leucocytosis
C. Neutropenia
D. Thrombocytopenia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Erythrocytosis

Explanation: Venesection (Phlebotomy) removes excess blood volume and red blood cells, used to treat Polycythemia Vera (Erythrocytosis).

5.
The nurse should expect the following set of findings when assessing the patient with pernicious anemia:
A. Pallor, tachycardia, and a sore tongue
B. Angina, double vision, and anorexia
C. Sore tongue, dyspnea, and weight gain
D. Pallor, bradycardia, and reduced pulse pressure

Correct Answer: A. Pallor, tachycardia, and a sore tongue

Explanation: Classic signs of Pernicious Anemia (B12 deficiency) include neurological symptoms, general anemia signs (pallor, tachycardia), and Glossitis (beefy red, sore tongue).

7.
If a patient with blood type O Rh+ is given AB Rh– blood, what would the nurse expect to happen?
A. The patient’s Rh factor will react with the RBCs of the donor blood.
B. The anti-A and anti-B antibodies in the patient’s blood will hemolyze the donor blood.
C. The anti-A and anti-B antibodies in the donor blood will hemolyze the patient’s blood.
D. No adverse reaction is expected because the patient has no antibodies against the donor blood.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. The anti-A and anti-B antibodies in the patient’s blood will hemolyze the donor blood.

Explanation: Patient (Type O) has Anti-A and Anti-B antibodies in their plasma. Donor (Type AB) RBCs have A and B antigens. The patient's antibodies will attack the donor's RBCs (Hemolytic Reaction).

8.
A patient is admitted to the hospital for evaluation and treatment of thrombocytopenia. Which action is most important for the nurse to implement?
A. Taking the temperature every 4 hours to assess for fever
B. Maintaining the patient on strict bed rest to prevent injury
C. Monitoring the patient for headaches, vertigo, or confusion
D. Removing the oral crusting and scabs with a soft brush four times a day
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Monitoring the patient for headaches, vertigo, or confusion

Explanation: Thrombocytopenia poses a risk of bleeding. The most life-threatening bleeding is intracranial hemorrhage, which manifests as headache, vertigo, or confusion. Safety is key, but detecting cerebral bleeds is the critical assessment priority.

9.
A first year nursing student taking care of an anemic strict vegetarian patient, this condition may have developed due to?
A. Thalassemia
B. Folic acid deficiency
C. Iron-deficiency
D. Cobalamin deficiency
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Cobalamin deficiency

Explanation: Cobalamin (Vitamin B12) is found almost exclusively in animal products. Strict vegetarians (vegans) are at high risk of B12 deficiency anemia unless they take supplements.

10.
When teaching the patient about a new prescription for oral iron supplements, what does the nurse instruct the patient to do?
A. Increase fluid and dietary fiber intake
B. Take the iron preparations with meals
C. Use enteric-coated preparations with orange juice
D. Report the presence of black stools to the health care provider
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Increase fluid and dietary fiber intake

Explanation: Oral iron causes constipation in many patients. Increasing fiber and fluid helps manage this side effect. (Taking with meals decreases absorption, black stools are a normal side effect not requiring reporting, and enteric coating reduces absorption).

Section II: Short Answer Questions (20 Marks)

11.
State five (5) nursing interventions for a client on treatment for thalassemia (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Administer Blood Transfusions: Monitor for reactions and overload.
  2. Chelation Therapy: Administer Desferal to remove excess iron (hemosiderosis) from chronic transfusions.
  3. Prevent Infection: Educate on hygiene and vaccines (esp. after splenectomy).
  4. Nutritional Support: Folic acid supplements; avoid iron-rich foods.
  5. Psychosocial Support: Counseling for chronic illness and genetic nature.
12.
Patient X is admitted with clotting disorder, giving an example for each class, outline five classes of drugs that affect platelet function (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. NSAIDs: Aspirin, Ibuprofen (inhibit COX/Thromboxane A2).
  2. ADP Receptor Blockers: Clopidogrel, Ticagrelor.
  3. Glycoprotein IIb/IIIa Inhibitors: Abciximab, Eptifibatide.
  4. Thrombin Inhibitors: Dabigatran, Heparin (affects coagulation cascade but also platelet interaction).
  5. Antibiotics: Some Penicillins/Cephalosporins (high dose) can impair platelet aggregation.
  6. (Note: Thrombolytics affect fibrin, not primarily platelets, but Antiplatelets are the main class).
13.
Describe the pathophysiology of idiopathic thrombocytopenic purpura (5 marks)
Show Answer

Idiopathic (Immune) Thrombocytopenic Purpura (ITP):
It is an autoimmune disorder.
1. The immune system creates antibodies (IgG) against the patient's own platelets.
2. These antibody-coated platelets are recognized as foreign by macrophages.
3. Macrophages in the Spleen destroy the platelets.
4. The rate of platelet destruction exceeds the rate of production by the bone marrow.
5. Result is low platelet count (Thrombocytopenia) leading to bleeding/purpura.

  4. New pulmonary infiltrate on Chest X-ray.
  5. Hypoxia (Low oxygen saturation).

Section III: Long Answer Question (20 Marks)

15.
You are placed in a hematology-oncology ward where patient X’s laboratory results show hemoglobin level of 3.2 mg/dl. The patient is scheduled for blood transfusion with Packed Red Blood Cells (PRBCs). Describe the procedure of blood transfusion and after transfusion. (20 Marks)
Show Answer

Pre-Transfusion (Preparation):

  • Verify Order: Check physician's order and informed consent.
  • Type & Crossmatch: Ensure blood sample has been sent and blood is ready.
  • Access: Ensure patent IV (18-20G). Prime tubing with 0.9% Normal Saline.
  • Baseline Vitals: Take Temp, BP, Pulse, RR immediately before starting.
  • Verification: Two nurses verify Patient Name, ID, Blood Type, Rh, Unit Number, and Expiration Date at the bedside.

During Transfusion (Procedure):

  • Initiate: Start slowly (25-50ml in first 15 mins).
  • Monitor: Stay with patient for first 15 minutes (reaction window).
  • Vitals: Check vitals after 15 mins, then every 30-60 mins (per policy).
  • Rate: Increase rate if no reaction, complete within 4 hours.
  • Observe: Watch for chills, fever, itching, dyspnea, back pain.

After Transfusion:

  • Completion: Flush line with Normal Saline.
  • Disposal: Dispose of bag/tubing in biohazard bin (or return to lab if policy requires).
  • Post-Vitals: Take and record final set of vital signs.
  • Documentation: Document unit number, start/end time, volume infused, patient response, and any reactions.
  • Monitoring: Continue to observe for delayed reactions (hemolysis, TRALI).
