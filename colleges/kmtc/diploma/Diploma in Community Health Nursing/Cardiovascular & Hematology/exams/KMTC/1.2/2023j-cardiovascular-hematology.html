Section II: Short Answer Questions (20 Marks)

Questions 1-4 are missing from the source document. The section begins at Question 5.

5.
Distinguish any three (3) modifiable and non-modifiable risk factors for hypertension (6 marks)
Show Answer
Modifiable Risk Factors (Can be changed) Non-Modifiable Risk Factors (Cannot be changed)
1. Diet: High salt intake, low potassium intake. 1. Age: Risk increases with age.
2. Obesity/Overweight: Excess weight increases strain on the heart. 2. Family History/Genetics: Presence of HTN in close relatives.
3. Physical Inactivity: Sedentary lifestyle. 3. Race/Ethnicity: Higher prevalence in African descent.
4. Substance Use: Tobacco smoking, excessive alcohol consumption. 4. Gender: Men > Women until menopause.
6.
State five (5) types of emboli in venous conditions (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Thromboembolism: A blood clot (thrombus) that detaches (Most common).
  2. Fat Embolism: Fat globules released after long bone fractures.
  3. Air/Gas Embolism: Air bubbles entering the vein (e.g., via IV lines).
  4. Amniotic Fluid Embolism: Amniotic fluid entering maternal circulation during labor.
  5. Septic Embolism: Clumps of bacteria/pus from an infected site.
  6. Tumor Embolism: Fragments of cancer cells.
7.
State five (5) specific nursing management of a patient with hypertension (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Monitor Vital Signs: Regularly check Blood Pressure (standing and sitting) to assess control and detect orthostatic hypotension.
  2. Medication Administration: Administer anti-hypertensives as ordered and monitor for side effects (e.g., cough with ACEIs).
  3. Dietary Management: Enforce a low-sodium (DASH) diet and weight management strategies.
  4. Patient Education: Teach about the importance of adherence, lifestyle modifications (exercise, smoking cessation), and stress management.
  5. Monitoring Complications: Assess for signs of end-organ damage (vision changes, edema, chest pain, proteinuria).
8.
Outline the four (4) classifications of Heart Failure according to the patient's symptoms (4 marks)
Show Answer

NYHA (New York Heart Association) Functional Classification:

  1. Class I: No limitation of physical activity. Ordinary physical activity does not cause undue fatigue, palpitation, or dyspnea.
  2. Class II: Slight limitation of physical activity. Comfortable at rest. Ordinary physical activity results in fatigue, palpitation, or dyspnea.
  3. Class III: Marked limitation of physical activity. Comfortable at rest. Less than ordinary activity causes fatigue, palpitation, or dyspnea.
  4. Class IV: Unable to carry on any physical activity without discomfort. Symptoms of heart failure at rest. If any physical activity is undertaken, discomfort increases.

Section III: Long Answer Question (20 Marks)

1.
A 60- year old patient is admitted to the medical ward with a diagnosis of uncomplicated myocardial infarction.

e) Outline five (5) clinical manifestations of myocardial infarction. (5 marks)

Show Answer
  1. Severe Chest Pain: Crushing, squeezing, or radiating pain (to jaw, arm, back) unrelieved by rest or nitrates.
  2. Dyspnea: Shortness of breath.
  3. Diaphoresis: Profuse sweating (cold/clammy skin).
  4. Nausea and Vomiting.
  5. Anxiety: Feeling of impending doom.

f) State four (4) potential complications that may develop in a patient with myocardial infarction (4 marks)

Show Answer
  1. Arrhythmias/Dysrhythmias: e.g., Ventricular Tachycardia, Atrial Fibrillation (Most common cause of death).
  2. Heart Failure: Pump failure due to damaged myocardium (Cardiogenic shock).
  3. Pericarditis: Inflammation of the pericardium (Dressler's syndrome).
  4. Ventricular Aneurysm/Rupture: Weakening of the heart wall.

g) Describe the management of the patient based on priority in the next 24 hours (9 marks)

Show Answer
  1. Pain Relief (Priority): Administer Morphine IV (analgesic and vasodilator) and Nitroglycerin (sublingual/IV) to reduce myocardial oxygen demand and pain.
  2. Oxygenation: Administer Oxygen (via nasal prongs/mask) to maintain SpO2 >94% and reduce ischemia.
  3. Antiplatelet Therapy: Administer Aspirin (chewable) and Clopidogrel immediately to prevent further clot formation.
  4. Rest and Positioning: Maintain strict bed rest in Semi-Fowler's position to reduce cardiac workload.
  5. Monitoring: Continuous ECG monitoring for arrhythmias. Monitor Vital signs every 15-30 mins. Monitor intake/output.
  6. Reperfusion: Prepare for Thrombolytic therapy (if indicated) or transfer for Percutaneous Coronary Intervention (PCI).
  7. Anxiety Reduction: Reassure patient and explain procedures to lower sympathetic stress.

h) Formulate two priority nursing diagnoses for the patient. (2 marks)

Show Answer
  1. Acute Pain related to myocardial ischemia as evidenced by patient report of crushing chest pain and facial grimacing.
  2. Decreased Cardiac Output related to altered myocardial contractility/heart rate/rhythm as evidenced by hypotension/tachycardia.
  3. (Alternative: Ineffective Tissue Perfusion (Cardiopulmonary) related to interruption of blood flow).

Module II: Hematology Nursing

Section II: Short Answer Questions (20 Marks)

1.
State five (5) nursing interventions for a client on treatment for thalassemia (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Blood Transfusion Management: Administer packed RBCs as ordered to maintain Hb levels and monitor for transfusion reactions.
  2. Chelation Therapy: Administer iron-chelating agents (e.g., Desferal) to prevent iron overload (hemosiderosis) from frequent transfusions.
  3. Infection Prevention: Educate on hygiene and ensure timely vaccinations (especially if splenectomy is done).
  4. Nutritional Support: Advise on folic acid supplements; discourage iron-rich foods.
  5. Genetic Counseling: Provide information/referral for genetic counseling for the family.
2.
Elucidate five (5) major causes of thrombocytopenia (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Decreased Production: Bone marrow suppression (Aplastic anemia, Leukemia, Chemotherapy).
  2. Increased Destruction: Autoimmune disorders (ITP - Idiopathic Thrombocytopenic Purpura, Lupus).
  3. Sequestration: Hypersplenism (enlarged spleen traps platelets).
  4. Consumption: Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC).
  5. Drug-Induced: Heparin-Induced Thrombocytopenia (HIT), certain antibiotics.
3.
State any five (5) causes of folate deficiency anemia (5marks)
Show Answer
  1. Dietary Deficiency: Lack of green leafy vegetables, liver, yeast.
  2. Malabsorption: Celiac disease, Crohn's disease.
  3. Increased Demand: Pregnancy, lactation, rapid growth in infancy.
  4. Drugs: Anticonvulsants (Phenytoin), Methotrexate, Trimethoprim.
  5. Alcoholism: Alcohol interferes with folate absorption and metabolism.
4.
State five (5) clinical manifestations of a patient diagnosed with Polycythemia vera (5marks)
Show Answer
  1. Plethora: Ruddy complexion (reddish skin/face).
  2. Pruritus: Generalized itching, especially after a warm bath (aquagenic pruritus).
  3. Headache and Dizziness: Due to hyperviscosity and poor perfusion.
  4. Hypertension: Increased blood volume/viscosity.
  5. Thrombosis: Increased risk of clots (DVT, Stroke, MI).
  6. Splenomegaly: Enlarged spleen.

Section III: Long Answer Question (20 Marks)

1.
A 30- year is admitted to the medical ward with a diagnosis of sickle cell crisis.

a) Outline the three (3) three types of sickle cell crisis in the adult population (3 marks)

Show Answer
  1. Vaso-occlusive Crisis (Painful Crisis): Obstruction of blood flow causing ischemia and severe pain.
  2. Sequestration Crisis: Pooling of blood in the spleen (less common in adults due to autosplenectomy, but possible in liver/lungs).
  3. Aplastic Crisis: Temporary cessation of RBC production, often triggered by Parvovirus B19.
  4. (Hemolytic Crisis is another type - rapid destruction of RBCs).

b) State five (5) potential complications of clients with sickle cell anaemia (5 marks)

Show Answer
  1. Acute Chest Syndrome: Pneumonia-like condition caused by sickling in the lungs.
  2. Stroke (CVA): Due to blockage of cerebral vessels.
  3. Leg Ulcers: Chronic poor circulation to lower extremities.
  4. Renal Failure: Damage to kidneys from chronic ischemia.
  5. Osteomyelitis: Bone infection (commonly Salmonella).
  6. Priapism: Painful, prolonged erection.

c) Describe the management of a patient until discharge (10 marks)

Show Answer

1. Immediate Management (Acute Crisis):

  • Hydration (Priority): Aggressive IV fluids (Normal Saline 3L/day) to reduce blood viscosity and flush sickled cells.
  • Oxygenation: Administer Oxygen to prevent further sickling caused by hypoxia.
  • Pain Management: Administer analgesics (Opioids like Morphine/Pethidine) on a schedule (not PRN) to control severe pain.
  • Infection Control: Screen for infection (trigger) and administer antibiotics if fever is present.

2. Ongoing Care:

  • Blood Transfusion: Administer Packed RBCs if anemia is severe or during Acute Chest Syndrome (Exchange transfusion may be needed).
  • Hydroxyurea: Administer if prescribed (increases Fetal Hemoglobin which resists sickling).
  • Folic Acid: Daily supplementation for RBC production.

3. Discharge Planning & Education:

  • Trigger Avoidance: Educate on avoiding cold, dehydration, high altitude, and heavy exertion.
  • Hydration: Drink 3-4 liters of water daily.
  • Vaccination: Ensure vaccines (Pneumococcal, Meningococcal, Flu) are up to date due to functional asplenia.
  • Genetic Counseling: Advise on screening partners.

d) Formulate two (2) nursing diagnoses for a patient with sickle cell crisis (2 marks)

Show Answer
  1. Acute Pain related to tissue ischemia/vaso-occlusion as evidenced by pain score of 10/10 and guarding.
  2. Ineffective Tissue Perfusion (Peripheral) related to obstruction of blood vessels by sickled cells.
  3. (Risk for Infection related to splenic dysfunction).
