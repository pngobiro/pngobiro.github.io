Section II: Short Answer Questions (20 Marks)

1.
Outline five (5) methods of giving rescue breaths. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Mouth-to-Mouth: The standard method where the rescuer seals their mouth over the victim's mouth and pinches the nose.
  2. Mouth-to-Nose: Used when the victim's mouth cannot be opened (trismus), has injury, or a seal cannot be maintained. The rescuer seals their mouth over the victim's nose.
  3. Mouth-to-Mouth-and-Nose: Used for infants; the rescuer covers both the baby's mouth and nose with their mouth.
  4. Mouth-to-Stoma: Used for patients with a permanent tracheostomy/laryngectomy stoma.
  5. Mouth-to-Mask (Pocket Mask): Using a barrier device with a one-way valve to prevent direct contact.
  6. Bag-Valve-Mask (BVM): Manual resuscitation using a self-inflating bag (Ambu bag) and mask, typically by healthcare providers.

(Any 5 methods)

2.
Outline five (5) components of the primary survey protocol. (5 marks)
Show Answer

The Primary Survey follows the DRABC protocol to identify and treat life-threatening conditions immediately:

  1. D - Danger: Check for danger to self, bystanders, and the casualty.
  2. R - Response: Check the casualty's level of consciousness (AVPU - Alert, Voice, Pain, Unresponsive).
  3. A - Airway: Check if the airway is patent; perform head-tilt/chin-lift or jaw thrust if necessary.
  4. B - Breathing: Look, Listen, and Feel for normal breathing (not more than 10 seconds).
  5. C - Circulation: Check for a pulse (healthcare providers) and check for/control severe bleeding.
3.
Describe the ABCDE APPROACH as used in trauma and emergency. (5 marks)
Show Answer

The ABCDE approach is a systematic method for assessing and treating critically ill or injured patients:

  1. A - Airway (with C-spine protection): Assess for airway obstruction and ensure it is open/patent while protecting the cervical spine in trauma.
  2. B - Breathing: Assess respiratory rate, depth, and oxygen saturation. Look for signs of respiratory distress (cyanosis, accessory muscle use).
  3. C - Circulation (with Hemorrhage Control): Assess pulse, blood pressure, capillary refill, and skin color/temperature. Identify and control external bleeding.
  4. D - Disability (Neurological Status): Assess level of consciousness (AVPU or GCS) and pupil reaction to light. Check blood glucose.
  5. E - Exposure/Environment: Remove clothing to perform a full assessment for other injuries while preventing hypothermia (keep patient warm).
4.
State five (5) roles of a triage nurse. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Rapid Assessment: Quickly assessing presenting patients to determine severity of condition.
  2. Prioritization: Categorizing patients into urgency levels (e.g., Emergent, Urgent, Non-urgent) to ensure the sickest are treated first.
  3. Initiating First Aid/Emergency Care: Providing immediate interventions (e.g., controlling bleeding, applying oxygen) while waiting for the physician.
  4. Traffic Control/Flow Management: directing patients to the appropriate treatment areas to maintain order and efficiency.
  5. Communication: Liaising between patients, families, and the medical team regarding status and wait times.
  6. Documentation: Recording initial assessment findings, vital signs, and triage category.

Section III: Long Answer Question (20 Marks)

1.
A 30-year-old man is brought by police officer at the casualty with history of injury to the leg following road traffic accident bleeding profusely in shock.

a) Discuss five (5) signs and symptoms of hypovolemic shock. (10 marks)

Show Answer
  1. Tachycardia (Rapid Pulse): The heart beats faster (>100 bpm) to maintain cardiac output despite reduced blood volume.
  2. Hypotension (Low Blood Pressure): A late sign indicating significant blood loss (Class III/IV hemorrhage) where compensatory mechanisms fail (Systolic BP < 90 mmHg).
  3. Tachypnea (Rapid Breathing): Increased respiratory rate to improve oxygenation and compensate for metabolic acidosis.
  4. Cool, Clammy, Pale Skin: Result of peripheral vasoconstriction shunting blood away from the skin to vital organs.
  5. Oliguria (Decreased Urine Output): Kidneys conserve water due to reduced renal perfusion (Output < 30ml/hr).
  6. Altered Mental Status: Anxiety, restlessness, confusion, or unconsciousness due to cerebral hypoxia.
  7. Weak/Thready Pulse: Due to decreased stroke volume.

(Any 5 points well discussed for 2 marks each)

b) Describe the nursing intervention you will implement as the first aider. (10 marks)

Show Answer

1. Safety and Initial Assessment (DRABC):

  • Ensure the scene is safe (Standard Precautions - gloves).
  • Assess Airway patency and Breathing. Administer high-flow Oxygen if available.

2. Control Hemorrhage (Priority):

  • Direct Pressure: Apply firm, direct pressure immediately over the bleeding wound using a sterile dressing or clean cloth.
  • Elevation: Elevate the injured leg above the level of the heart (if no fracture is suspected/after splinting) to reduce hydrostatic pressure and bleeding.
  • Pressure Dressing: Apply a tight pressure bandage. If blood soaks through, add more layers—do not remove the first layer.
  • Tourniquet: If bleeding is life-threatening and uncontrolled by pressure, apply a tourniquet proximal to the wound (last resort for limb).

3. Treat for Shock (Positioning & Warmth):

  • Positioning: Lay the patient supine and elevate the uninjured leg (Modified Trendelenburg) to improve venous return to the heart.
  • Warmth: Cover the patient with a blanket to prevent hypothermia, which worsens clotting (the "Lethal Triad").

4. Fluid Resuscitation:

  • Establish two large-bore IV lines (14G or 16G).
  • Administer warm IV crystalloids (Normal Saline or Ringer's Lactate) rapidly as ordered to restore circulating volume.
  • Prepare for blood transfusion (Type and Crossmatch).

5. Monitoring and Reassurance:

  • Monitor vital signs (Pulse, BP, RR, GCS) every 5-15 minutes.
  • Keep the patient NPO (Nothing by Mouth) in preparation for surgery.
  • Reassure the patient to reduce anxiety and sympathetic demand.
