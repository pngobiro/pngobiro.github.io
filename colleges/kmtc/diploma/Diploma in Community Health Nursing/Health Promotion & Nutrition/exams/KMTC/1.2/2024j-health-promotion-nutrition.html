Module I: Health Promotion

Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions (10 Marks)

1.
A Community Health Nurse educator has a responsibility to:
A. Manipulating the behavior of the Community members
B. Disseminating information about the Prevention of Diseases and promotion of Health
C. Determine the proper focus for Health Promotion
D. Propagandizing about health and safety
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Disseminating information about the Prevention of Diseases and promotion of Health

Explanation: The primary role of a nurse educator in health promotion is to share accurate, evidence-based information to empower the community to make healthy choices, rather than manipulating or propagandizing.

2.
In Community Strategy, the health services at level one is directed to:
A. Sub-location Dispensaries
B. Division Health Centers
C. Households and Villages
D. Community Health Workers
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Households and Villages

Explanation: In the Kenya Essential Package for Health (KEPH), Level 1 services are delivered at the community level (villages/households) by Community Health Volunteers/Promoters.

3.
The following is the Primary goal of Health Promotion;
A. Early diagnosis and treat of illnesses
B. Focus solely on individual behavior change
C. Empower Individuals and Community to optimize health status
D. Provide rehabilitation services to the impaired persons
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Empower Individuals and Community to optimize health status

Explanation: Health promotion is defined as the process of enabling people to increase control over, and to improve, their health (Ottawa Charter).

4.
The following is an example of a community-based health promotion strategy;
A. Prescribing medication to a patient with high blood pressure
B. Preparing a community garden with access to fresh produce
C. Recommending a specific fitness app to a client
D. Performing a surgery to remove a malignant tumor
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Preparing a community garden with access to fresh produce

Explanation: A community garden creates a supportive environment for nutrition, addressing health at a population/structural level rather than an individual clinical level.

5.
Approach to health promotion that focuses on changing social and environmental factors that influence health behaviors;
A. Biomedical approach
B. Community development approach
C. Behavior Change approach
D. Individual empowerment approach
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Community development approach

Explanation: The community development approach aims to address the socio-economic and environmental determinants of health through collective action, rather than focusing solely on individual behavior.

6.
When developing a health promotion material, it's important to consider:
A. Use complex medical terminology
B. Focus solely on the negative health practices in the community
C. Present information in a single language only
D. Tailoring the message to the target audience
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Tailoring the message to the target audience

Explanation: Effective IEC materials must be culturally appropriate, readable, and relevant to the specific needs and literacy levels of the audience.

7.
The following is an example of primary prevention strategy in health promotion;
A. Encouraging healthy eating habits to maintain normal Body Mass Index
B. Early detection of breast cancer through screenings
C. Managing symptoms of Chronic diseases
D. Provide medication to lower High blood pressure
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Encouraging healthy eating habits to maintain normal Body Mass Index

Explanation: Primary prevention aims to prevent disease before it occurs (e.g., diet). Screening (B) is Secondary prevention. Treatment (C/D) is Tertiary prevention.

8.
The Ottawa Charter for Health emphasizes the importance of creating supportive environments for health. The following is an example of creating a supportive environment;
A. Creating designated areas for smoking in public places
B. Accessible parks and recreational facilities
C. Fast food advertising aimed at the malnourished children
D. Encouraging the community to utilize available resources to improve their health
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Accessible parks and recreational facilities

Explanation: Creating supportive environments involves making the healthy choice the easy choice. Parks facilitate physical activity. (Note: Creating smoking areas creates an environment for smoking, not health, though it protects non-smokers; parks are a more direct positive example).

9.
Health Education approach that focuses on addressing the underlying social, economic and environmental factors that influence health;
A. Individualist approach
B. Biomedical approach
C. Empowerment approach
D. Reactive approach
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Empowerment approach

Explanation: The empowerment (or social change) approach seeks to help people understand and change the social/environmental causes of ill health.

10.
A health message that is perceived to be trustworthy by the receiver is described as
A. Reliable
B. Consistent
C. Valid
D. Credible
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Credible

Explanation: Credibility refers to the trustworthiness and expertise of the source as perceived by the receiver.

Section 2: Short Answer Questions (20 Marks)

11.
Explain five key areas identified in the Ottawa Charter for Health Promotion. (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Building Healthy Public Policy: Putting health on the agenda of policy makers in all sectors (e.g., taxation, legislation).
  2. Creating Supportive Environments: Making living and working conditions safe, stimulating, and enjoyable (e.g., green spaces).
  3. Strengthening Community Actions: Empowering communities to control their own endeavors and destinies (e.g., self-help groups).
  4. Developing Personal Skills: Providing information and education for health to enhance life skills (e.g., health literacy).
  5. Reorienting Health Services: Moving beyond clinical/curative services to a focus on health promotion and prevention.
12.
Outline five objectives of Health Education. (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. To encourage people to adopt and sustain health-promoting lifestyles and practices.
  2. To promote the proper use of health services available to the community.
  3. To provide new knowledge and improve skills for making rational health decisions.
  4. To arouse interest and change attitudes regarding health matters.
  5. To stimulate individual and community self-reliance and participation in health development.
13.
Outline the five steps of community mobilization. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Entry into the Community: Establishing rapport with leaders and understanding community structures.
  2. Community Assessment/Diagnosis: Identifying needs, resources, and problems with the community.
  3. Planning: Developing an action plan with objectives and strategies together with the community.
  4. Implementation: Carrying out the planned activities utilizing community resources.
  5. Monitoring and Evaluation: Assessing progress and impact, then replanning.
14.
State five benefits of community dialogue. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Ownership: Promotes a sense of ownership of health problems and solutions by the community.
  2. Resource Mobilization: Helps identify and utilize local resources efficiently.
  3. Consensus Building: Helps resolve conflicts and reach agreements on priorities.
  4. Empowerment: Gives community members a voice and confidence to act.
  5. Sustainability: Solutions developed through dialogue are more likely to be culturally acceptable and sustainable.

Section 3: Long Essay Question (20 Marks)

1.
You are a practicing Community Health Nurse at Migosi Health Centre. In collaboration with the community leaders, a community dialogue has been scheduled to address the health challenges affecting the community. Discuss five principles of an Organized Community Dialogue and explain the Steps to be followed in conducting an Organized Community Dialogue. (20 Marks)
Show Answer

Part A: Principles of Organized Community Dialogue (10 Marks)

  1. Inclusivity: Ensure representation from all segments of the community (women, youth, elderly, marginalized groups) so diverse views are heard.
  2. Respect: Participants must respect each other's opinions and culture. The facilitator should ensure a safe, non-judgmental environment.
  3. Transparency: The purpose, process, and outcomes of the dialogue should be clear to all. No hidden agendas.
  4. Participation: Active engagement of all members should be encouraged. It should not be dominated by leaders or experts.
  5. Relevance: The dialogue must address issues that are felt needs of the community and are timely.

Part B: Steps in Conducting Organized Community Dialogue (10 Marks)

  1. Preparation (Pre-dialogue):
    • Define the specific topic/issue.
    • Identify key stakeholders and mobilize participants.
    • Select a suitable venue (neutral, accessible) and time.
    • Prepare materials (flip charts, markers).
  2. Introduction/Climate Setting:
    • Welcome participants and introduce facilitators/leaders.
    • Explain objectives and set ground rules (e.g., one person speaks at a time).
    • Create a relaxed atmosphere (ice breakers).
  3. Problem Identification & Analysis:
    • Facilitate discussion to define the problem clearly.
    • Explore root causes (Why is this happening?) and effects.
    • Use tools like problem trees or mapping.
  4. Action Planning (Way Forward):
    • Brainstorm solutions.
    • Agree on specific actions: What will be done? By whom? By when? With what resources?
    • Form a committee if necessary.
  5. Closing and Evaluation:
    • Summarize key decisions.
    • Agree on a date for the next meeting/follow-up.
    • Thank participants and close.

Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions (10 Marks)

1.
During growth monitoring of a child, Failure to thrive is diagnosed when.
A. A child presents with severe wasting.
B. The Weight is below 60% of normal weight.
C. The child present s with old man's face.
D. Weight is 80% of the normal weight.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. The Weight is below 60% of normal weight.

Explanation: While definitions vary (often simply stagnant growth curve), in the context of Gomez classification used in older curricula, severe malnutrition (often linked to Marasmus/FTT clinical picture) is weight <60% of expected.

C. Breast milk must be given prior to new food.

Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Avoid introducing variety of food at ago.

Explanation: New foods should be introduced one at a time to identify any allergies or intolerances.

A. Beans and meat.
B. Cowpeas and beans.
C. Meat and milk.

Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Meat and milk.

Explanation: First class proteins (High Biological Value) contain all essential amino acids and are typically of animal origin (meat, milk, eggs, fish).

A. Bleeding.
B. Tissue calcification.
C. Beriberi.
D. Bone demineralization.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Bone demineralization.

Explanation: Calcium deficiency leads to resorption of calcium from bones (osteomalacia, rickets, osteoporosis) to maintain blood levels.

Correct Answer: A. Proteins and carbohydrates.

Explanation: Macronutrients are Proteins, Fats, and Carbohydrates. Vitamins and Minerals are Micronutrients.

8.
The appropriate therapeutic diet for patients on tube feeds is;
A. High calorie, high protein, potassium controlled
B. High protein, high fat, low carbohydrate

Section 2: Short Answer Questions (20 Marks)

11.
Explain three (3) methods used in nutritional status assessment. (6 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Anthropometry: Measuring physical dimensions (weight, height, MUAC) to compare against standards (BMI, Z-scores).
  2. Biochemical Assessment: Analyzing body fluids (blood, urine) for nutrient levels (e.g., Hemoglobin for iron, Albumin for protein).
  3. Clinical Assessment: Physical examination for signs of deficiency (e.g., pallor, edema, hair changes).
  4. Dietary Assessment: Asking about food intake (24hr recall, food frequency) to estimate nutrient consumption.
12.
State four functions of protein. (4 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Body building and repair of tissues (growth).
  2. Production of enzymes and hormones.
  3. Production of antibodies (immune function).
  4. Source of energy (secondary function).
  5. Maintenance of fluid balance (oncotic pressure via albumin).
13.
State five (5) roles of a community health nurse in ensuring good nutrition in the community. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Nutritional Education: Teaching families about balanced diets and breastfeeding.
  2. Growth Monitoring: Regular weighing of children to detect malnutrition early.
  3. Supplementation: Distributing Vitamin A, Iron/Folate to vulnerable groups.
  4. Assessment: Screening for malnutrition signs in the community.
  5. Advocacy: Promoting food security initiatives like kitchen gardens.
14.
Outline five (5) factors to consider when feeding the elderly. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Reduced BMR: Lower calorie needs but high nutrient density required.
  2. Dental Issues: Texture may need modification (soft/minced) due to poor teeth/dentures.
  3. Reduced Taste/Smell: Food should be flavorful to stimulate appetite.
  4. Digestion: Reduced gastric acid/motility requires smaller, frequent meals and fiber for constipation.
  5. Mobility/Dexterity: May need assistance or adaptive utensils to eat.
  6. Hydration: Thirst mechanism is blunted; need deliberate fluid intake.

Section 3: Long Answer Question (20 Marks)

15.
Master P, 2 years old, presents in the hospital with severe wasting and Oedema of both lower limbs among other signs and symptoms of severe Protein Energy Malnutrition. He is for admission. Explain the four factors predisposing to Protein Energy Malnutrition and discuss the management of Master P till discharge. (20 marks)
Show Answer

A. Factors Predisposing to PEM (4 Marks):

  1. Inadequate Dietary Intake: Early weaning, diluted formula, or lack of food due to poverty (food insecurity).
  2. Infections: Recurrent illnesses (diarrhea, measles, respiratory infections) increase nutrient needs while decreasing appetite.
  3. Maternal Factors: Lack of breastfeeding, poor maternal nutrition, ignorance/lack of knowledge on proper feeding practices.
  4. Socio-economic Factors: Poverty, large family size, displacement/conflict preventing access to food.

B. Management of Master P (Marasmic-Kwashiorkor) (16 Marks):

The management follows the WHO 10 Steps for Severe Acute Malnutrition:

Phase 1: Stabilization (Days 1-7)

  1. Hypoglycemia: Treat/prevent with 10% dextrose or F-75 feed immediately. Feed every 2-3 hours.
  2. Hypothermia: Keep warm (Kangaroo care), cover head, keep room warm. No bathing initially.
