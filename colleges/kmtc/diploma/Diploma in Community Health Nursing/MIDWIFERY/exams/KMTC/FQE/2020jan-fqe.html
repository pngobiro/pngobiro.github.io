Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions (40 Marks)

1.
The statement last menstrual period means
A. The first day of the last menses that the woman had
B. The last day of the last menses that the woman had
C. The last month the woman had menses
D. The monthly period that occurred the month the woman attended the antenatal clinic
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. The first day of the last menses that the woman had

Explanation: In obstetrics, the Last Menstrual Period (LMP) specifically refers to the first day of bleeding of the last normal menstrual period. This date is used to calculate the Estimated Date of Delivery (EDD).

2.
The part of the fetus that lies in the lower pole of the uterus in a breech presentation is the
A. Sacrum
B. Meutum
C. Occiput
D. Brow
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Sacrum

Explanation: The Sacrum is the denominator in breech presentation. The buttocks (and sometimes feet/knees) lie in the lower pole, but the sacrum is the specific bony landmark used to describe position.

3.
The prelacunar stage of pregnancy happens on
A. 3rd day of gestation
B. 4th day of gestation
C. 7th day of gestation
D. 12th day of gestation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. 7th day of gestation (to 9th day)

Explanation: The pre-lacunar stage of trophoblast development occurs shortly after implantation (around day 7-8), before the appearance of lacunae (fluid-filled spaces) in the syncytiotrophoblast, which marks the lacunar stage (Day 9).

4.
Pregnant women experience tingling/pricking sensation around the nipple between 3rd to 4th week of pregnancy as a result of
A. Increased levels of estrogen in the blood
B. Decreased levels of estrogen in the blood
C. Increased levels of human gonadotrophic hormone
D. Increased blood supply around the nipple area
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Increased blood supply around the nipple area

Explanation: Early breast changes like tingling and increased sensitivity are due to increased vascularity (blood supply) and hormonal changes (Estrogen/Progesterone) preparing for lactation.

5.

A. Genital vaginitis

D. Gonorrhea
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Syphilis

Explanation: VDRL (Venereal Disease Research Laboratory) is a non-treponemal screening test for Syphilis (Treponema pallidum).

6.
Cervical effacement refers to
A. Dilatation of the cervix
B. Inclusion of the cervix from the lower uterine segment
C. Exclusion of the cervix from the lower uterine segment
D. Inclusion of the upper segment of the uterus
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Inclusion of the cervix from the lower uterine segment (Merging/Taken up)

Explanation: Effacement is the thinning and shortening of the cervix where it is "taken up" or included into the lower uterine segment, so that the cervix and lower uterine segment become one continuous channel.

7.
In the normal labour process, the membranes rupture
A. Just before the onset of the 1st stage of labour
B. During the 2nd stage of labour
C. When the midwife performs the first vaginal examination
D. At the end of the 1st stage of labour
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. At the end of the 1st stage of labour

Explanation: Physiologically, the membranes usually rupture at the end of the first stage of labor (full dilation) when the forewaters are no longer protected by the cervix and the head descends. (Though they can rupture anytime).

During an internal rotation, the presenting part rotates:

11.
Early clamping of the cord as part of active management of the 3rd stage of labour should be done in the first
A. 5 minutes after birth
B. 3 to 5 minutes after birth
C. 30 seconds to 3 minutes after birth
D. 1 minute to 4 minutes after birth
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. 1 minute to 4 minutes after birth (Wait... Current Guidelines say Delayed Clamping 1-3 mins. "Early" in historical AMTSL was immediate. However, current AMTSL includes delayed clamping. The options are confusing. Let's look at WHO.)

Clarification: WHO recommends delayed cord clamping (1-3 mins). If "Early" is specified as a historical or specific medical indication, it implies <1 minute. However, looking at the options: Option C (30s to 3m) covers the transition. In older texts, "Early" meant immediate. If the question refers to the current AMTSL guidelines which include delayed clamping, the range is 1-3 minutes. Option C (30 seconds to 3 minutes) or D (1 to 4 minutes) are closest to "Delayed". However, strict "Early Clamping" (e.g. for HIV/Asphyxia) is immediate. Given standard KMTC teaching might refer to older AMTSL protocols (Immediate), or newer (Delayed). Let's assume standard AMTSL: Clamping is usually done when pulsations cease (1-3 mins). Answer C seems most aligned with the concept of "Timed Clamping" in modern AMTSL context.

12.
A diagnosis of retained placenta is made when
A. Placenta remains undelivered 30 minutes after the baby’s birth

Correct Answer: A. Placenta remains undelivered 30 minutes after the baby’s birth

Explanation: Retained placenta is defined as lack of expulsion of the placenta within 30 minutes of the baby's delivery (with active management).

13.
A midwife will diagnose occipital posterior position when; on abdominal examination
A. The occiput is easily palpable at the fundus
B. The head has already engaged
C. There is a saucer shaped depression at the or near the umbilicus
D. The occiput is easily palpable at the lower pole of the uterus
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. There is a saucer shaped depression at the or near the umbilicus

Explanation: In Occipito-Posterior (OP) position, the fetal back is posterior (against mother's spine). The limbs are anterior. On abdominal palpation, the abdomen looks flattened below the umbilicus with a saucer-shaped depression near the umbilicus because of the limbs (dip between limbs).

14.

Correct Answer: D. Hips and knees are both flexed with feet tucked in beside the buttocks

Explanation: - Complete Breech: Hips flexed, Knees flexed (sitting cross-legged). - Frank Breech: Hips flexed, Knees extended (Option A). - Footling: Hips extended (Option B/C).

The recommended calorie intake requirement for a woman in labour is:

17.
A woman ruptures membranes and the midwife observes the cord lying outside the vaginal canal. The immediate action he/she will take is
A. Cover the cord with a clean cloth
B. Pack the vaginal canal with cotton wool
C. Replace the cord gently back into the vaginal canal
D. Instruct the woman to lie still in bed
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Instruct the woman to lie still in bed (and elevate hips/Trendelenburg)

Explanation: This is Cord Prolapse. The immediate goal is to relieve pressure. Do NOT handle/replace the cord (spasm risk - C is wrong). Do NOT pack (B is wrong). Covering (A) is good but positioning (D) to relieve pressure is the priority life-saving step. Ideally, manual elevation of the presenting part is the gold standard.

The placenta is fully functional by:

Section 2: Short Answer Questions (40 Marks)

41.
State FIVE precautions taken by a midwife to prevent fetal distress during labour. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Proper positioning: Encourage left lateral position to prevent supine hypotension and improve placental perfusion.
  2. Monitoring: Regular monitoring of fetal heart rate (every 30 mins in active phase, every 15 mins in 2nd stage) to detect deviations early.
  3. Hydration: Ensuring adequate maternal hydration to maintain blood volume and perfusion.
  4. Partograph Use: Correct use of partograph to detect prolonged labor or obstruction early.
  5. Avoid Hyperstimulation: Judicious use of oxytocin (Syntocinon) to avoid tachysystole which reduces placental reperfusion time.
  6. Emptying Bladder: Encouraging 2-hourly voiding to prevent obstruction and reflex uterine inhibition.
42.
Explain the active management of the third stage of labour approach (AMTSL). (6 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

AMTSL involves three main steps to prevent Postpartum Hemorrhage:

  1. Administration of Uterotonic: Give Oxytocin 10 IU IM within 1 minute of the delivery of the baby (after ruling out a second twin) to stimulate strong uterine contraction.
  2. Controlled Cord Traction (CCT): Apply gentle traction on the umbilical cord with one hand while applying counter-traction to the uterus (suprapubic pressure) with the other hand to deliver the placenta.
  3. Uterine Massage: Massage the fundus immediately after placental delivery until it is firm to ensure the uterus remains contracted and to expel clots. Monitor tone every 15 minutes.

45.
Explain THREE benefits of early initiation of breast feeding. (6 marks)
Show Answer

  2. Provision of Colostrum (Neonatal): Provides the baby with rich immunoglobulins (IgA), nutrients, and acts as a laxative to clear meconium.

46.

Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Eat a balanced diet containing all food groups (proteins, carbs, vitamins) to support recovery and milk production.
  2. Increase fluid intake (water, soup, juice) to maintain hydration for lactation.
  3. Eat foods rich in iron (green leafy vegetables, liver) to replenish blood lost during delivery.

Section 3: Long Answer Question (20 Marks)

49.
You have just delivered the baby and placenta and immediately the woman starts bleeding profusely.
a) Define post-partum haemorrhage (1 mark)
b) State FOUR possible causes of post-partum haemorrhage (4 marks)
c) Describe the management of the woman until discharge from the labour ward (15 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

a) Definition of Post-partum Haemorrhage (1 mark)

Postpartum haemorrhage (PPH) is defined as blood loss in excess of 500ml following a vaginal delivery or >1000ml following a cesarean section, or any amount of bleeding that causes hemodynamic instability in the mother.

b) Four Causes of PPH (The 4 Ts) (4 marks)

  1. Tone (Atonic Uterus): Failure of the uterus to contract (Most common cause).
  2. Trauma: Lacerations of the cervix, vagina, or perineum; Uterine rupture.
  3. Tissue: Retained placenta, placental fragments, or membranes.
  4. Thrombin: Coagulation disorders (e.g., DIC, Hemophilia).

c) Management of PPH until Discharge (15 marks)

1. Immediate Action ("Call for Help"):

  • Shout for help. Do not leave the patient alone.
  • Put the patient in a flat position (supine) to improve blood flow to vital organs.

2. Uterine Massage & Expulsion of Clots:

  • Immediately massage the fundus to stimulate contraction ("rub up a contraction").
  • Ensure the bladder is empty (catheterize if necessary) as a full bladder inhibits contraction.
  • Expel any clots once the uterus is firm.

3. Resuscitation & Pharmacological Management:

  • IV Access: Insert two large-bore IV cannulas (16G or 18G).
  • Fluids: Rapidly infuse crystalloids (Normal Saline or Ringer's Lactate) to restore volume.
  • Oxytocics:
    • Oxytocin: 20-40 IU in 1L fluids at 60 drops/min.
    • Ergometrine: 0.5mg IM (contraindicated in hypertension/heart disease).
    • Misoprostol: 600-800mcg rectally or sublingually if other drugs fail.

4. Identify and Treat Specific Cause:

  • Atony: Continue massage and oxytocics. Consider bimanual compression or aortic compression if bleeding persists.
  • Trauma: Inspect cervix and vagina for tears using a speculum and good light. Repair immediately.
  • Tissue: Check placenta for completeness. If retained, perform manual removal under anesthesia (if trained) or refer.
  • Thrombin: If blood doesn't clot, manage for coagulopathy (Fresh Frozen Plasma/Blood transfusion).

5. Ongoing Monitoring:

  • Monitor Vital Signs (BP, Pulse, Respiration) every 15 minutes until stable.
  • Monitor fundal height and consistency (firmness).
  • Monitor vaginal bleeding (lochia).
  • Monitor fluid intake and output.

6. Supportive Care:

  • Keep the mother warm to prevent hypothermia (which worsens coagulopathy).
  • Reassure the mother and partner to reduce anxiety.
  • Initiate breastfeeding when stable to promote endogenous oxytocin.

7. Documentation:

  • Record all events, drugs given, fluids administered, and estimated blood loss clearly.
