Part One: Multiple Choice Questions (20 Marks)

1.
During fetal development, brown fat begins to form at:
a) 12 weeks
b) 16 weeks
c) 20 weeks
d) 24 weeks
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) 20 weeks

Explanation: Brown adipose tissue (BAT) differentiation typically begins around the 17th to 20th week of gestation. It is essential for non-shivering thermogenesis in the newborn.

2.
The hormone responsible for the secretion of mucus by the endocervical cell during pregnancy is:
a) Progesterone
b) Oestrogen
c) Prostaglandins
d) Human chorionic gonadotrophin
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Oestrogen

Explanation: Estrogen stimulates the proliferation of cervical mucosa and the secretion of abundant, thin, clear mucus (which then forms the mucus plug under the influence of progesterone).

3.
A Rhesus positive foetus is affected by a rhesus negative mother due to:
a) Antibodies of the foetus crosses the placenta barrier to the mother’s circulation
b) Antibodies of the mother crosses the placenta into the foetal circulation
c) The foetal red blood cells enters the mother’s circulation
d) The maternal red cells enter the foetal circulation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Antibodies of the mother crosses the placenta into the foetal circulation

Explanation: In Rhesus isoimmunization, the mother's immune system produces IgG antibodies (Anti-D) which are small enough to cross the placenta and destroy fetal red blood cells (hemolysis).

4.
Retention of a portion of the placenta in the uterus after third stage of labour will:
a) Prevent the uterus from contracting effectively
b) Cause the blood vessels in the placental bed to dilate
c) Make the uterus to become hard as a tennis ball
d) Prevent the blood vessels in the placental bed to contract
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Prevent the uterus from contracting effectively

Explanation: Retained placental tissue prevents the myometrium from retracting fully (the "living ligature" action), leading to uterine atony and postpartum hemorrhage.

5.
The midwife will encourage the mother to empty the urinary bladder during first stage of labour mainly to:
a) Minimize infection during labour
b) Avoid bladder rupture during third stage of labour
c) Prevent contamination during second stage of labour
d) Avoid complications during second stage and third stage of labour
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Avoid complications during second stage and third stage of labour

Explanation: A full bladder can obstruct fetal descent (2nd stage) and inhibit effective uterine contraction leading to PPH (3rd stage). It is also at risk of injury.

6.
Ductus arteriosus is a bypass in a babys’ circulation from:
a) Umbilical vein to the inferior vena cava
b) Pulmonary artery to the aorta
c) Left atria and right atria
d) Internal iliac artery to umbilical artery
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Pulmonary artery to the aorta

Explanation: The ductus arteriosus shunts blood from the pulmonary artery directly to the descending aorta, bypassing the fluid-filled fetal lungs.

7.
A reduction in urinary output in prolonged labour may be associated with:
a) Antidiuretic effect of oxytocin,pyrexia,dehydration
b) Dehydration, pyrexia,diarrhoea
c) Antidiuretic effect of oxytocin, pyrexia,vomiting
d) Diarrhoea, pyrexia,vomiting
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Antidiuretic effect of oxytocin, pyrexia, vomiting

Explanation: Prolonged labor often causes dehydration (vomiting, lack of intake) and stress (pyrexia). Oxytocin (endogenous or exogenous) has an antidiuretic effect, further reducing output.

8.
In a normal delivery with a well flexed head the presenting diameters are:
a) Mentovertical 13.5cm and Bitemporal 8.2cm
b) Bitemporal 8.2cm and Sub-Mentobregmatic 9.5cm
c) Biparietal 9.5cm and Sub –Occipitobregmatic 9.5cm
d) Bitemporal 9.5cm and Occipitofrontal 11.5cm
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Biparietal 9.5cm and Sub –Occipitobregmatic 9.5cm

Explanation: In a vertex presentation with full flexion, the engaging diameter is the Sub-occipito-bregmatic (9.5 cm). The widest transverse diameter is the Biparietal (9.5 cm).

9.
The maternal characteristics a midwife will use to diagnose late obstructed labour through a partograph include:
a) Slow cervical dilatation, pyrexia, excessive moulding
b) Rapid pulse rate, pyrexia, excessive moulding
c) Slow cervical dilatation , excessive moulding, oliguria
d) Rapid pulse rate, pyrexia, oliguria
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Rapid pulse rate, pyrexia, oliguria

Explanation: These are the classic signs of maternal distress due to obstructed labor: Tachycardia (Rapid pulse), Fever (Pyrexia), and Dehydration/Ketosis (Oliguria).

10.
Maternal causes of shoulder presentation include:
a) Lax uterine muscles, uterine abnormality
b) Contracted pelvis, preterm pregnancy
c) Lax uterine muscles, multiple pregnancy
d) Uterine abnormality, multiple pregnancy
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Lax uterine muscles, uterine abnormality

Explanation: Lax uterine muscles (often in grand multiparity) allow the fetus to move freely into oblique/transverse lies. Uterine anomalies (e.g., septum) prevent longitudinal alignment.

11.
During the 2nd stage of labour , external rotation of the fetal head assists the midwife to realize that the:-
a) Foetal shoulders have descended.
b) Foetal body is in the lateral position.
c) Foetal shoulders have rotated into antero-posterior diameter of the outlet.
d) Occiput has slipped under the sub-pubic arch.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Foetal shoulders have rotated into antero-posterior diameter of the outlet.

Explanation: External rotation (Restitution) of the head occurs as the shoulders internally rotate to align with the AP diameter of the pelvic outlet for delivery.

13.
To prevent neural tube defect the midwife advises a woman planning to conceive to take:
a) Vitamin B12
b) Vitamin A
c) Ferrous sulphate
d) Folic acid
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Folic acid

Explanation: Folic acid supplementation (400mcg/day) prior to conception and during the first trimester significantly reduces the risk of Neural Tube Defects (NTDs) like Spina Bifida.

14.
The milk secreting cells in the breast are referred to as:
a) Acini cells
b) Myoepithelial cells
c) Alveolar cells
d) Montgomery tubercle
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Acini cells (and c) Alveolar cells)

Explanation: Acini (or Alveoli) are the functional units of the breast that synthesize and secrete milk. Myoepithelial cells surround them to eject the milk.

15.
The midwife would formulate the following nursing diagnosis for a mother who develops hypovolemic shock after uterine rupture:
a) Fluid volume deficit related to intra uterine bleeding evidenced by vaginal bleeding
b) Risk of fluid volume deficit related to uterine rupture
c) Impaired tissue perfusion as evidenced by blood loss
d) Ineffective gas exchange related to uterine rupture
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Fluid volume deficit related to intra uterine bleeding evidenced by vaginal bleeding (or signs of shock)

Explanation: The primary physiological problem in hypovolemic shock is the actual loss of fluid (blood) volume.

16.
State whether the following statements are TRUE or FALSE:
a) In puerperal psychosis Restlessness, agitation, insomnia, failure to eat and drink will be present
b) Haemorrhagic diseases of the newborn may occur due to hypoxia
Show Answer

Answers:

  • a) TRUE: These are classic symptoms of the manic phase of puerperal psychosis.
  • b) FALSE: Haemorrhagic Disease of the Newborn (HDN) is caused by Vitamin K deficiency, not hypoxia. (Though hypoxia can cause DIC, HDN specifically refers to Vit K deficiency).
17.
Maternal distress can be diagnosed from the partograph through:
a) Cervical dilatation, Urine testing, blood pressure
b) Urine testing, blood pressure, maternal pulse
c) Cervical dilatation, urine testing, amniotic fluid
d) Cervical dilatation, uterine contraction, blood pressure
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Urine testing, blood pressure, maternal pulse

Explanation: These are the specific maternal parameters recorded on the partograph. Tachycardia (Pulse), Hypotension/Hypertension (BP), and Ketones/Protein (Urine) indicate maternal condition.

18.
The direct causes of maternal deaths in Kenya include:
a) Haemorrhage, eclampsia.sepsis
b) Haemorrhage, malaria, sepsis
c) Malaria, anaemia, sepsis
d) Malaria, anaemia, haemorrhage
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Haemorrhage, eclampsia.sepsis

Explanation: Direct causes result from obstetric complications. Haemorrhage, Hypertensive disorders (Eclampsia), and Sepsis are the top three. Malaria and Anemia are considered indirect causes.

19.
Maternal minor complications which may occur during puerperium include:
a) Cracked nipples ,breast engorgement, bleeding
b) Depression, cracked nipples, sore perineum
c) Sore perineum, engorgement, cracked nipples
d) Depression, engorgement, bleeding
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Sore perineum, engorgement, cracked nipples

Explanation: Bleeding (PPH) and Depression are major complications. Option C lists minor, manageable conditions.

20.
Definitive indications for elective caesarean section include:
a) Cephalopelvic disproportion, major degree of placenta preavia
b) Cephalopelvic disproportion, breech presentation
c) High order multiple pregnancies, breech presentation
d) Major degree of placenta preavia, diabetes mellitus

Correct Answer: a) Cephalopelvic disproportion, major degree of placenta preavia

1.
Draw and label a diagram of a sagittal section of the pelvis. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer: (Diagram required). Key structures to label:

  • Bones: Sacrum, Coccyx, Pubic Symphysis.
  • Pelvic Inlet (Brim): From Sacral Promontory to upper border of Symphysis Pubis.
  • Pelvic Outlet: From tip of Coccyx to lower border of Symphysis Pubis.
  • Cavity: Space between Inlet and Outlet.
  • Axis of the Pelvis: Curve of Carus.
2.
Explain two (2) processes involved in involution of the uterus. (4 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Ischaemia: Contraction and retraction of uterine muscle fibers compress the blood vessels, reducing blood supply to the uterus. This "de-vascularization" leads to atrophy of the muscle fibers.
  2. Autolysis: Proteolytic enzymes digest the excess protoplasm (cytoplasm) of the muscle cells. The waste products are absorbed into the bloodstream and excreted by the kidneys. This reduces the size of individual muscle cells without reducing their number.
  3. (Phagocytosis is the 3rd process involving removal of waste tissue).
3.
Outline five (5) nursing intervention in management of premature rupture of membranes. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Admission and Bed Rest: Admit to hospital and encourage bed rest to prevent cord prolapse and fluid loss.
  2. Infection Prevention: Administer prophylactic antibiotics (e.g., Erythromycin) to prevent chorioamnionitis and limit vaginal examinations (sterile speculum only if needed).
  3. Monitoring: Monitor maternal vital signs (especially Temperature for fever) and Fetal Heart Rate (tachycardia) 4-hourly to detect infection.
  4. Assessment of Liquor: Observe color (meconium/blood) and odor (foul smell = sepsis) of the draining fluid.
  5. Perineal Hygiene: Instruct on changing sterile pads frequently to monitor drainage and maintain hygiene.
4.
Explain two (2) roles of the midwife in the prevention of hypothermia. (4 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Immediate Drying: Dry the newborn immediately after birth with a warm towel and discard the wet towel to prevent heat loss by evaporation.
  2. Skin-to-Skin Contact (Kangaroo Care): Place the naked baby directly on the mother's bare chest and cover both with a warm blanket. This transfers maternal heat to the baby via conduction.
  3. (Other: Delay bathing for 24h, Maintain warm room temperature >25°C).
5.
Explain three (3) characteristics a midwife will identify to determine low birth weight during examination of a pregnant woman. (6 marks)
Show Answer

Answer: (Note: Question asks for identification during *pregnancy* - i.e., IUGR/Small for Dates).

  1. Fundal Height Measurement: Symphysis-fundal height (in cm) is significantly less than the gestational age in weeks (e.g., >2-3 cm discrepancy).
  2. Abdominal Palpation: The uterus feels smaller than expected; amniotic fluid volume may be reduced (oligohydramnios); fetal parts are easily felt and the fetus feels small.
  3. Maternal Weight Gain: Poor or static maternal weight gain during antenatal visits despite adequate nutrition.
6.
For a patient who has undergone caeserian section, formulate three (3) nursing diagnoses and one intervention for each. (6 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Diagnosis: Acute pain related to surgical incision.
    Intervention: Administer prescribed analgesics (e.g., Diclofenac/Paracetamol) and support the incision site with a pillow during movement/coughing.
  2. Diagnosis: Risk for infection related to surgical wound and invasive procedures.
    Intervention: Maintain aseptic technique during wound dressing and monitor vital signs (Temp) for signs of sepsis.
  3. Diagnosis: Risk for fluid volume deficit related to blood loss during surgery.
    Intervention: Monitor intake/output, checking lochia and wound dressing for bleeding, and administer IV fluids as prescribed.
7.
Explain three (3) hospital related factors that can reduce maternal mortality rate. (6 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Availability of Skilled Staff: Adequate numbers of trained midwives and obstetricians available 24/7 to manage emergencies.
  2. Availability of Essential Supplies/Equipment: Ready access to emergency drugs (Oxytocin, MgSO4), blood for transfusion, and functional operating theatres.
  3. Effective Referral Systems: Timely and efficient protocols for managing and transferring critical patients between levels of care (e.g., ambulance services).
8.
State four (4) key points a service provider should consider before providing sulfadoxine pyrimethamine to a pregnant woman. (4 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Gestational Age: Ensure the pregnancy is beyond the first trimester (>16 weeks) or after quickening. SP is contraindicated in the first trimester.
  2. Allergy History: Confirm no history of allergy to Sulfa drugs (Sulphonamides).
  3. Concomitant Medication: Check if the woman is on Cotrimoxazole (Septrin) prophylaxis (e.g., for HIV). SP should NOT be given if on Septrin (toxicity risk).
  4. Last Dose: Ensure at least 4 weeks have passed since the last dose of SP (if applicable).
  5. Direct Observation: Administer as DOT (Directly Observed Therapy) in the clinic.

Part Three: Long Answer Questions (40 Marks)

1.
Mrs kokigoyoo aged 35 years comes to Ante Natal Clinic for the first visit at 29 weeks gestation:
a) Explain three (3) characteristics of a normal pregnancy (6 marks)
b) Describe the care that she will receive until she leaves the clinic (14 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

a) Characteristics of a Normal Pregnancy (6 marks)

  1. Duration: Lasts approximately 37 to 42 weeks (280 days) from the LMP.
  2. Fetal Growth: Consistent growth of the fetus corresponding to gestational age (fundal height matches dates).
  3. Maternal Vitals: Blood pressure remains within normal range (<140/90), no proteinuria, no edema.
  4. Fetal Well-being: Presence of fetal heart rate (110-160 bpm) and active fetal movements perceived by the mother.
  5. Single Fetus: Usually implies a singleton pregnancy in a longitudinal lie and vertex presentation near term.

b) Antenatal Care for Mrs. Kokigoyoo (14 marks)

  1. Welcome & History Taking: Establish rapport. Take comprehensive history (Medical, Surgical, Obstetric, Family, Social). Calculate EDD.
  2. Physical Examination:
    • General: Height, Weight, BP, check for pallor/edema.
    • Abdominal: Inspect, Palpate (Fundal height, Lie, Presentation, Fetal Heart Rate).
  3. Laboratory Tests (Profile):
    • Urinalysis (Protein/Sugar).
    • Blood: Hb, Blood Group/Rhesus, VDRL (Syphilis), HIV screening.
  4. Preventive Measures:
    • Tetanus Toxoid (TT): Administer first dose.
    • Malaria (IPTp): Give SP (Fansidar) as DOT (since >16 weeks). Provide ITN.
    • Anaemia: Supplement with Iron and Folic Acid (IFAS).
    • Deworming: Give Mebendazole if indicated.
  5. Health Education/Counseling:
    • Diet/Nutrition.
    • Individual Birth Plan (IBP).
    • Danger signs of pregnancy.
    • Hygiene and rest.
  6. Documentation & Return Date: Fill the ANC card (Mother & Child booklet). Schedule next visit (usually 4 weeks later).
2.
Mrs yoyoowo aged 30 years,para 2+0 gravida 3 comes to labour ward in 2nd stage of labour and a diagnosis of breech presentation with extended arms is made:
a) Outline two maternal and two foetal predisposing factors to breech presentation (4 marks)
b) Develop four (4) nursing diagnoses for mrs yoyoowo (4 marks)
c) Describe the management of mrs Yoyoowo until she delivers (12 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

a) Predisposing Factors (4 marks)

Maternal:

  • Uterine anomalies (e.g., Septate/Bicornuate uterus).
  • Pelvic tumors (e.g., Fibroids).
  • Grand multiparity (Lax uterus).

Fetal:

  • Prematurity (Small fetus moves freely).
  • Multiple pregnancy (Twins).
  • Hydrocephalus (Head too large to engage).
  • Polyhydramnios.

b) Nursing Diagnoses (4 marks)

  1. Risk for injury (maternal/fetal) related to malpresentation and complex delivery maneuvers.
  2. Acute pain related to rapid descent or maneuvers.
  3. Anxiety related to emergency situation/fear for baby's safety.
  4. Risk for impaired gas exchange (fetal) related to cord compression (after buttocks are born).

c) Management of Delivery (Lovset's Maneuver) (12 marks)

Since arms are extended, Lovset's Maneuver is indicated:

  1. Preparation: Call for help (obstetrician/anesthetist/paediatrician). Position mother in Lithotomy (buttocks to edge). Ensure bladder is empty. Perform episiotomy if necessary (under local anaesthesia).
  2. Delivery of Buttocks: Allow buttocks to be born spontaneously ("Hands off the breech") until the umbilicus is visible.
  3. Pull Down Loop of Cord: To prevent traction/spasm.
  4. Lovset's Maneuver (for extended arms):
    • Hold the baby by the bony pelvis (thumbs on sacrum, fingers on iliac crests). Do not compress the abdomen.
    • Rotate the baby 180 degrees via the back (keeping back anterior). This friction brings the posterior arm anteriorly under the pubic arch.
    • Deliver the arm that is now anterior by splinting the humerus and sweeping it down across the chest.
    • Rotate the baby 180 degrees back in the opposite direction (via the back) to bring the other arm anteriorly.
    • Deliver the second arm.
  5. Delivery of the Head (Burns-Marshall or MSV):
    • Allow the baby's body to hang by its own weight to promote flexion and descent of the head (Burns-Marshall).
    • Once the nape of the neck (hairline) is visible, grasp the ankles and lift the baby's body in a wide arc over the mother's abdomen.
    • Alternatively, use Mauriceau-Smellie-Veit maneuver: Place baby on forearm, middle finger in mouth (to flex chin), other hand forks shoulders for traction.
  6. Immediate Care: Resuscitate baby if needed (high risk of asphyxia). Active management of 3rd stage. Inspect for tears.
