1.
A woman comes to the antenatal clinic for her first ANC visit and reports that her Last Menstrual Period (LMP) was 24/12/2021, her Expected Date of Delivery (EDD) will be on;
A. 27/10/2022
B. 1/10/2022
C. 31/9/2022
D. 27/9/2022
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. 1/10/2022

Explanation: Using Naegele's Rule: Add 7 days to the first day of LMP, subtract 3 months, and add 1 year.
LMP: 24th Dec (12) 2021
Days: 24 + 7 = 31st
Months: Dec (12) - 3 = Sept (9)
Date would be 31st September. However, September has only 30 days.
Therefore: 30th Sept + 1 day = 1st October 2022.

4.
The nutritional supplement given to women during preconception care to prevent neural tube defects is;
A. Iron
B. Zinc
C. calcium
D. Folate
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Folate

Explanation: Folic acid (folate) supplementation before conception and during the first trimester significantly reduces the risk of neural tube defects like spina bifida and anencephaly.

5.
The hormones responsible for the regeneration and proliferation of the endometrium during each menstrual cycle, after menstruation has taken place are the:
A. Estrogens
B. Inhibin
C. Estradiol
D. Progesterone
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Estrogens

Explanation: Estrogens (specifically estradiol) produced by the developing follicles stimulate the proliferation (thickening) of the endometrium during the proliferative phase of the menstrual cycle.

6.
Urinary tract infection in pregnancy usually results from:
A. An increase in the renin- angiotensin mechanism
B. Dilation and kinking of ureters brought about by the effect of progesterone hormone on them
C. Decreasing in the filtration rate
D. The growing uterus makes it difficult for the woman to properly wipe herself thus contaminating the genitalia with fecal matter
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Dilation and kinking of ureters brought about by the effect of progesterone hormone on them

Explanation: Progesterone causes smooth muscle relaxation, leading to dilation and stasis of urine in the ureters. This stasis provides a medium for bacterial growth. The gravid uterus also compresses the ureters.

8.
While evaluating urine output for a mother in shock, the midwife is concerned if the urine output is:
A. Less than 30mls/hour
B. More than 30 mls/hour
C. Less than 150 mls/hour

9.
Which of the following sign may help a midwife diagnose shoulder dystocia in labor ward?
A. The fetal head remains tightly stuck in the vagina
B. The anterior shoulder is delivered but the posterior one is stuck
C. The chin retracts and depresses the perineum
D. Traction of the head delivers the shoulder
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. The chin retracts and depresses the perineum

Explanation: This is known as the "Turtle Sign", where the fetal head retracts against the perineum after emergence, indicating that the anterior shoulder is impacted behind the pubic symphysis.

10.
The first manoeuvre a nurse would choose in the management of shoulder dystocia is;
A. Zavanelli maneuver
B. Woodscrew maneuver
C. Rubin maneuver
D. McRoberts maneuver
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. McRoberts maneuver

Explanation: McRoberts maneuver (flexing the mother's thighs hyper-flexed onto her abdomen) is the first-line intervention as it flattens the sacrum and rotates the symphysis pubis, often freeing the impacted shoulder.

11.
Pressure exerted on amniotic fluid during uterine contractions is called: -
A. Fore water pressure
B. Hind water pressure
C. Foetal axis pressure.
D. General fluid pressure
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. General fluid pressure

Explanation: According to Pascal's law, pressure applied to a contained fluid is transmitted equally in all directions. In the uterus, this is referred to as general fluid pressure.

12.
Immediately after placenta is delivered, the Nurse must do the following: -
A. Inspect the placenta for completeness including the membranes
B. Place the placenta in a kidney dish for disposal
C. Massage the uterus and expel clots
D. Carefully examine it in the kidney dish and measure blood loss
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Massage the uterus and expel clots

Explanation: The immediate priority after placental delivery is to ensure the uterus is contracted to prevent Postpartum Hemorrhage (PPH). Inspection (A) follows once the mother is stable.

13.
To deliver the posterior shoulder, the midwife: -
A. Holds the baby by the parietals and pulls directly towards herself
B. Applies slight upward traction and moves baby’s head upward
C. Applies slight downward traction by moving baby’s head posteriorly
D. Holds the baby’s back and chest and pulls outwards
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Applies slight upward traction and moves baby’s head upward

Explanation: To deliver the posterior shoulder, the baby's head is lifted upwards (towards the mother's abdomen) to allow the posterior shoulder to sweep over the perineum.

14.
Intrauterine factors that enhance a newborns ability to breath at birth: -
A. Good uterine contractions which expel baby quickly
B. Presence of foetal breathing movements and foetal lung maturity
C. Adequate oxygenation and nutrition during pregnancy
D. Strong foetal axis pressure and a quick delivery
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Presence of foetal breathing movements and foetal lung maturity

Explanation: Fetal breathing movements (practice breathing) tone the respiratory muscles, and lung maturity (surfactant production) is essential for alveoli expansion at birth. These are the crucial physiological preparations.

15.
Immediate action after baby’s head is born should be: -
A. Put baby on mother’s abdomen, clamp and cut the cord.

D. Check for cord round the neck, wipe baby’s mouth, nose, and face

Correct Answer: D. Check for cord round the neck, wipe baby’s mouth, nose, and face

Explanation: Before delivering the shoulders, the midwife must check for a nuchal cord (cord around the neck). If present, it should be slipped over the head or clamped and cut if tight. Wiping the face clears the airway.

21.
The risks associated with prematurity include: -
A. Asphyxia, hypothermia, cerebral haemorrhage.
B. Infections, jaundice, genito-urinary malformations.
C. Infections, asphyxia, malformation of the gut.
D. Duodenal atresia, hypothermia, cerebral haemorrhage.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Asphyxia, hypothermia, cerebral haemorrhage.

Explanation: Preterm infants have immature lungs (Asphyxia/RDS), poor thermal regulation (Hypothermia), and fragile germinal matrix capillaries (Intraventricular/Cerebral Haemorrhage). Malformations (B, C, D) are congenital, not caused by prematurity itself.

31.
Mrs. X 32 years old para 2+ 0 in the third trimester visits the ante-natal clinic with complaints of lethargy, glossitis and gingivitis. Examination of the red cell indices reveals reduced Red Blood Cells which are enlarged, bone marrow examination shows both megoloblasts and normoblasts, the most likely type of anaemia will be
A. Vitamine B12 deficiency anaemia
B. Iron deficiency anaemia
C. Sickle anaemia
D. Folic deficiency anaemia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Folic deficiency anaemia

Explanation: The presence of megaloblasts (enlarged RBCs) indicates Megaloblastic Anemia. In pregnancy, the most common cause is Folic Acid deficiency due to increased fetal demands. While B12 deficiency also causes this, Folate is the primary suspect in this context. Glossitis is a common symptom.

32.
A midwife should watch out for the following complications in a woman diagnosed with polyhydramnious.
A. Cord presentation, placenta praevia, premature rupture of membranes
B. Malpresentation, prolonged pregnancy, unstable lie
C. Postpartum haemorrhage, premature labour, cord prolapse
D. Placenta abruption, prolapse of the uterus, congestive cardiac failure
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Postpartum haemorrhage, premature labour, cord prolapse

Explanation:

  • Premature Labour: Due to overdistension of the uterus.
  • Cord Prolapse: When membranes rupture, the rush of excess fluid can wash the cord down.
  • PPH: Overdistended uterus may fail to contract well (atony) after delivery.

33.

Section 2: Short Answer Questions (40 Marks)

41.
Preconception care has been observed as a positive influence among women who are at the risk of adverse pregnancy outcome. Explain three (3) components of preconception care that you would apply for a lady desiring to have a baby (6 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Medical Assessment and Management: Screening for and treating pre-existing conditions like diabetes, hypertension, or HIV to optimize maternal health before conception.
  2. Nutritional Supplementation: Prescribing Folic Acid (400mcg) daily for at least 3 months prior to conception to prevent neural tube defects.
  3. Lifestyle Modification: Counseling on cessation of smoking, alcohol, and drug use, as well as weight management to prevent complications.
42.
Pregnancy leads to many physiological changes among women. Outline four (4) changes that you would expect on the breasts of a pregnant woman (4 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Increased Size and Sensitivity: Breasts become larger, fuller, and tender due to estrogen/progesterone.
  2. Darkening of Areola: The areola becomes broader and more deeply pigmented (secondary areola).
  3. Montgomery's Tubercles: Sebaceous glands on the areola become hypertrophied and prominent.
  4. Colostrum Secretion: By the 16th week, thick yellow fluid (colostrum) may be expressed.
  5. Visible Veins: Superficial veins become prominent under the skin.

  1. Severe frontal headache.
  2. Visual disturbances (blurred vision, flashing lights/scotoma).
  3. Epigastric or Right Upper Quadrant pain.
  4. Hyperreflexia (brisk reflexes) and/or Clonus.

44.
During labour you have injected syntocinon drip. As you observe the progress of labour, you notice key features that would require stopping the syntocinon drip. State five (5) observable features that could inform your decision as a midwife (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Fetal Distress: Abnormal fetal heart rate (severe bradycardia, late decelerations).
  2. Hypertonic Uterine Contractions: Contractions lasting longer than 60-90 seconds.
  3. Uterine Tachysystole: More than 5 contractions in 10 minutes.
  4. Inadequate Relaxation: Less than 60 seconds resting tone between contractions.
  5. Signs of Uterine Rupture: Severe continuous pain, fresh vaginal bleeding, loss of station, maternal tachycardia.

Section 3: Long Answer Question (20 Marks)

49.
You are conducting delivery on a primigravida who is sixteen (16) years old. On examination, you determine that she is in second stage of labour. As the delivery progresses, she sustains a second degree tear. Describe the step-by-step procedure of how you will repair the tear and then discuss the major roles of a midwife in prevention of perineal tears during childbirth.
Show Answer

Answer:

Procedure for Repairing a Second Degree Tear:

  1. Preparation: Explain procedure, obtain consent, position mother in lithotomy, ensure good lighting, and observe aseptic technique (hand washing, sterile gloves).
  2. Analgesia: Clean perineum with antiseptic. Infiltrate the area with local anaesthetic (Lignocaine 1%) using a fan technique, pulling back on plunger to avoid intravascular injection. Allow time for effect.
  3. Examination: Place a vaginal pack (with tail visible) to stop bleeding from obscuring view. Identify the apex (top) of the vaginal tear and the extent of muscle/skin damage.
  4. Repair of Vaginal Mucosa: Start suturing 1cm above the apex of the vaginal tear using continuous locking stitches (with absorbable suture like Vicryl 2/0). Continue down to the hymenal ring (introitus). Match rugae to rugae.
  5. Repair of Perineal Muscles: Identify the torn bulbocavernosus and transverse perineal muscles. Approximate them with interrupted or continuous non-locking sutures. Ensure dead space is closed but avoid suturing through the rectum (perform rectal check if unsure).
  6. Repair of Skin: Close the skin edges using interrupted sutures or a continuous subcuticular stitch. Ensure edges are not inverted.
  7. Completion: Remove vaginal pack. Count instruments/swabs. Perform a gentle rectal examination to ensure no sutures have penetrated the rectal mucosa. Clean the area and apply a pad. Document the procedure.

Roles of a Midwife in Prevention of Perineal Tears:

  1. Controlled Delivery of the Head: Encourage the mother to stop pushing and "pant" as the head crowns. Control the speed of extension with the hand to prevent sudden expulsion.
  2. Perineal Support (Hands-on): Support the perineum with a warm pad/hand during crowning to reduce tension, while maintaining flexion of the fetal head.
  3. Alternative Positions: Encourage upright or lateral (side-lying) positions which reduce tension on the perineum compared to lithotomy.
  4. Warm Compresses: Apply warm compresses to the perineum during the second stage to increase elasticity and blood flow.
  5. Avoid Routine Episiotomy: Only perform episiotomy for strict indications (fetal distress, shoulder dystocia), as routine cuts extend easily.
  6. Perineal Massage: Encourage antenatal perineal massage from 34 weeks to increase elasticity.
