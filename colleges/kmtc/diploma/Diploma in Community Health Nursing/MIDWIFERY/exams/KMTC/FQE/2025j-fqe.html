Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions (40 Marks)

1.
The term fetus is used in pregnancy for;
A. Up to 0-2 weeks
B. Up to 0-4 weeks
C. Up to 2-8 weeks
D. Beyond 9 weeks
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Beyond 9 weeks

Explanation: In human embryology, the prenatal period is divided into three stages:

  • Pre-embryonic (Germinal) stage: From fertilization to 2 weeks (Option A/B covers part of this).
  • Embryonic stage: From 2 weeks to 8 weeks (Option C). This is the period of organogenesis.
  • Fetal stage: From 9 weeks until birth (Option D). This period is characterized by rapid growth and maturation of tissues and organs.
2.
This is the hormone that is primarily responsible for stimulating spermatogenesis in the testes
A. Testosterone
B. Luteinizing hormone
C. Follicle stimulating hormone
D. Oestrogen
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Follicle stimulating hormone

Explanation:

  • FSH (Follicle Stimulating Hormone): Acts directly on the Sertoli cells in the seminiferous tubules to initiate and maintain spermatogenesis.
  • LH (Luteinizing Hormone): Stimulates the Leydig cells to produce testosterone.
  • Testosterone: Essential for maintaining spermatogenesis, but FSH is the primary stimulator of the process itself via Sertoli cells.
3.
Which uterine ligament originates from the anterior aspect of the cervix and is responsible for pulling the cervix forward?
A. Pubocervical ligaments
B. Transverse cervical ligaments
C. Uterosacral ligaments
D. Round ligaments
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Pubocervical ligaments

Explanation:

  • Pubocervical ligaments: Extend from the anterior aspect of the cervix to the posterior surface of the pubic symphysis. They help maintain the cervix in its anterior position.
  • Uterosacral ligaments: Pull the cervix backwards and upwards, helping to maintain uterine anteversion (by keeping the cervix posterior).
  • Round ligaments: Maintain the uterus in anteversion by pulling the fundus forward, not the cervix.
  • Transverse cervical (Cardinal) ligaments: Provide lateral support.
4.
The true conjugate diameter of the pelvis is a critical measurement in midwifery. This diameter is measured from;
A. The sacral promontory to the upper edge of the pubic symphysis
B. The sacral promontory to the lower edge of the pubic symphysis
C. One ischial spine to the other ischial spine
D. The pubic symphysis to the coccyx
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. The sacral promontory to the upper edge of the pubic symphysis

Explanation:

  • True Conjugate (Anatomical Conjugate): Measured from the center of the sacral promontory to the upper border of the pubic symphysis. It measures approximately 11 cm.
  • Diagonal Conjugate: From sacral promontory to the lower border of the symphysis pubis (approx 12-13 cm).
  • Obstetric Conjugate: From sacral promontory to the most protruding point on the inner surface of the symphysis (approx 10.5 cm).
5.
In the fetal circulation, oxygenated blood enters the fetal circulation from the umbilical vein via;
A. Ductus venosus
B. Ductus arteriosus
C. Hypogastric arteries
D. Foramen ovale
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Ductus venosus

Explanation: Oxygenated blood from the placenta travels through the umbilical vein. A significant portion of this blood bypasses the liver through the ductus venosus to enter the inferior vena cava directly.

6.
A nurse is teaching a group of nursing students about physiology of pregnancy. The nurse tells the students that fetal weight gain is greatly increased in the;
A. First trimester
B. Second trimester
C. Third trimester
D. Implantation period
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Third trimester

Explanation: The most significant increase in fetal weight occurs during the third trimester, where the fetus gains subcutaneous fat and muscle mass. The first and second trimesters are primarily focused on organogenesis and length growth.

7.
Mrs. Q who is 35 weeks pregnant complains to the nurse during prenatal visit that her breathing has become difficult. The nurse should respond by telling the client that;
A. Her lower rib cage is more restricted by the fetus hence the breathing difficulty
B. Her diaphragm has been displaced upward hence the breathing difficulty
C. There is an increase in the size of her lungs hence the breathing difficulty
D. There is an increase in the size of her heart hence the breathing difficulty
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Her diaphragm has been displaced upward hence the breathing difficulty

Explanation: As the uterus enlarges, it pushes upward on the diaphragm, displacing it by about 4 cm. This can cause physiological dyspnea (shortness of breath) in late pregnancy before lightening occurs.

8.
How does a nurse identify true labour as opposed to false labour when admitting a patient in labour ward?
A. Cervical dilatation and effacement is progressive
B. Contractions stop when the client walks around
C. Contractions progress only in a side lying position
D. Contractions occur immediately after the membranes rupture
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Cervical dilatation and effacement is progressive

Explanation: The definitive sign of true labor is progressive cervical change (effacement and dilatation). False labor contractions (Braxton Hicks) are irregular, do not increase in intensity/frequency, and do not cause cervical change; they often stop with walking or hydration.

9.
During a normal vaginal delivery, the cardinal movements of labor describe the mechanisms the fetus undergoes to navigate through the birth canal. Which sequence correctly describes these movements?
A. Flexion, descent, extension, internal rotation, external rotation, expulsion
B. Descent, flexion, internal rotation, extension, external rotation, expulsion
C. Extension, internal rotation, flexion, descent, external rotation, expulsion
D. Internal rotation, descent, flexion, extension, external rotation, expulsion
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Descent, flexion, internal rotation, extension, external rotation, expulsion

Note: Descent is a continuous process that occurs throughout labor. However, the classic sequence is often listed as: Engagement -> Descent -> Flexion -> Internal Rotation -> Extension -> Restitution -> External Rotation -> Expulsion. Option B follows the most logical chronological order of distinct mechanisms.

10.
As a nurse is monitoring a mother during the active phase of the first stage of labor, the expected rate of cervical dilation for a primigravida and the clinical significance for the labor progress is;
A. 0.5 -1 cm per hour; slower dilation often suggests a need for intervention
B. 1-2 cm every 2 hours; normal rate indicating a prolonged labor process
C. 2-3 cm per hour; rapid dilation requiring close monitoring for potential complications
D. 1-2 cm per hour; indicates normal labor progression and active management strategies
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. 1-2 cm per hour; indicates normal labor progression and active management strategies

Explanation: The World Health Organization (WHO) and standard partographs define the active phase alert line at a rate of 1 cm/hr. For a primigravida, a rate of 1 cm/hr or more (1-1.2 cm/hr) is considered normal progress in the active phase.

11.
A nurse in the prenatal clinic is assessing a woman at 34 weeks gestation whose blood pressure is 166/100 mm Hg and her urine is +3 for protein. The client further states that she has severe headache and occasional blurring of vision. The nursing action to take is;
A. Recheck the client’s blood pressure within 4 hours
B. Arrange the client’s transportation to the hospital
C. Obtain a prescription for an antihypertensive for the client
D. Obtain a prescription for paracetamol to relieve the headache
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Arrange the client’s transportation to the hospital

Explanation: This client is exhibiting signs of Severe Pre-eclampsia (BP ≥160/110, proteinuria +3, cerebral symptoms like headache and visual disturbances). This is an obstetric emergency requiring immediate hospital admission for stabilization (MgSo4) and likely delivery. A nurse in a clinic cannot manage this alone or simply prescribe meds to go home.

12.
The midwife when assessing the uterine fundus of a mother within one hour after birth notes that the uterus feels soft and boggy. The nursing intervention to take is;
A. Push on the uterus to assist in expressing clots, empty the bladder
B. Elevate the patient’s legs, continuously monitor the patient
C. Monitor the patient’s bowel function, administer misoprostol
D. Massage the fundus until it is firm, administer oxytocin
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Massage the fundus until it is firm, administer oxytocin

Explanation: A "boggy" uterus indicates uterine atony, the leading cause of postpartum hemorrhage. The immediate first-line action is fundal massage to stimulate contraction. Oxytocics are then administered to sustain contraction. Pushing on a boggy uterus without supporting it can cause inversion.

13.
During puerperium, the expected timeline for the uterus to return to its non-pregnant size and the physiological changes that facilitate this process include;
A. 1 week; rapid contraction of myometrial fibers
B. 2 weeks; gradual shedding of the endometrium
C. 6 weeks; continued uterine contractions and autolysis of myometrial cells
D. 3 months; prolonged uterine contractions and slow endometrial regeneration
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. 6 weeks; continued uterine contractions and autolysis of myometrial cells

Explanation: Involution of the uterus takes approximately 6 weeks. The process involves contraction (to control bleeding), retraction, and autolysis (breakdown of excess protein in myometrial cells).

14.
During puerperium, the factor that is associated with an increased risk of developing postpartum depression is;
A. Multiparity with living children
B. Unplanned or unwanted pregnancy
C. High socioeconomic status
D. Short duration of labor
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Unplanned or unwanted pregnancy

Explanation: Major risk factors for PPD include history of depression, lack of social support, and an unplanned/unwanted pregnancy. High SES is generally a protective factor (though not absolute). Parity and labor duration are less consistent predictors compared to psychosocial factors.

15.
Breastfeeding has multiple benefits for both mother and baby during the puerperium. How does exclusive breastfeeding physiologically impact the mother’s hormonal balance and postpartum recovery?
A. Decreases prolactin levels, leading to earlier return of menstruation
B. Increases oxytocin release, promoting uterine involution and bonding
C. Inhibits milk production, reducing the risk of mastitis
D. Increases estrogen levels, improving mood stability
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Increases oxytocin release, promoting uterine involution and bonding

Explanation: Suckling stimulates the posterior pituitary to release oxytocin. Oxytocin causes the milk ejection reflex (let-down) and simultaneously causes uterine contractions ("afterpains"), which aids in involution (returning uterus to normal size) and reducing bleeding.

16.
Prior to discharge home following a successful spontaneous vertex delivery, a mother asks the midwife on how she should care for her newborn’s umbilical cord stump. The midwife should tell the mother to
A. Expect a moderate amount of drainage
B. Keep the area moist with sterile normal saline
C. Provide sponge baths until the cord falls off
D. Cover the cord with a small sterile dressing when bathing the newborn
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Provide sponge baths until the cord falls off

Explanation: The cord stump should be kept clean and dry to prevent infection and promote separation. Submerging the baby in a tub can keep the cord wet; therefore, sponge baths are often recommended until the cord falls off. Drainage (Option A) is abnormal and suggests infection.

17.
Skin-to-skin contact immediately after birth is known to have multiple benefits for both the newborn and the mother. The primary physiological benefits of this practice for the newborn include
A. Enhanced immune response due to early exposure to maternal skin flora
B. Increased risk of hypothermia due to prolonged exposure to room air
C. Delayed bonding with the mother, leading to attachment issues
D. Reduced incidence of breastfeeding success due to lack of early initiation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Enhanced immune response due to early exposure to maternal skin flora

Explanation: Skin-to-skin contact (Kangaroo Mother Care) promotes colonization of the newborn with the mother's commensal bacteria (immune benefit), stabilizes temperature (prevents hypothermia), stabilizes glucose, and promotes early breastfeeding/bonding. Options B, C, and D are factually incorrect outcomes of skin-to-skin.

18.
During subsequent examination of a 3 day-old newborn, the midwife notices yellow discoloration of the sclera and soles of the feet. What is the cause of this condition?
A. Immature liver function leading to decreased bilirubin conjugation
B. Increased red blood cell production leading to increased hepatotoxicity
C. Infections like sepsis which occur during delivery
D. Maternal-fetal blood group incompatibility
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Immature liver function leading to decreased bilirubin conjugation

Explanation: Jaundice appearing on day 3 is typical of Physiological Jaundice. This is caused by the breakdown of fetal red blood cells combined with the immature liver's inability to conjugate bilirubin efficiently. Pathological jaundice (infection, incompatibility) usually appears within the first 24 hours.

19.
During a routine newborn examination, a midwife assesses the newborn's reflexes. Which reflex is characterized by a sudden extension and then flexion of the arms and legs in response to a loud noise or sudden movement?
A. Rooting reflex
B. Sucking reflex
C. Moro reflex
D. Babinski reflex
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Moro reflex

Explanation: The Moro reflex (Startle reflex) involves abduction and extension of arms with fingers fanned (forming a 'C'), followed by adduction and flexion (embrace), often accompanied by crying. It is a response to sudden stimulation or loss of support.

20.
Hypoglycemia in newborns is a condition that requires prompt management. The newborn at risk for developing hypoglycemia is;
A. A full-term newborn with a birth weight of 3.5 kg
B. A newborn delivered via elective cesarean section at 39 weeks
C. A preterm newborn with a birth weight of 1.8 kg
D. A newborn born to a mother with gestational hypertension
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. A preterm newborn with a birth weight of 1.8 kg

Explanation: Preterm infants (and SGA infants) are at high risk for hypoglycemia because they have limited glycogen stores in the liver. Other high-risk groups include infants of diabetic mothers (LGA) and post-term infants.

21.
A preterm infant born at 30 weeks gestation presents with tachypnea, nasal flaring, and grunting shortly after birth. Chest X-ray shows a ground-glass appearance with air bronchograms. This is diagnostic of;
A. Meconium aspiration syndrome
B. Transient tachypnea of the newborn
C. Respiratory distress syndrome
D. Persistent pulmonary hypertension of the newborn
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Respiratory distress syndrome

Explanation: RDS (Hyaline Membrane Disease) is common in preterm infants due to surfactant deficiency. Clinical signs include grunting, flaring, and retractions. The classic X-ray finding is a "ground-glass" appearance (reticulogranular pattern) with air bronchograms.

22.
Teratogenic causes of congenital malformations include;
A. Infective agents and chromosomes
B. Radiation and substance of abuse
C. Prematurity and prophylactic antibiotic
D. Substance of abuse and postdatism
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Radiation and substance of abuse

Note: Option A includes "chromosomes" which are genetic, not teratogenic (environmental) causes, though typically grouped in etiology. However, Radiation (X-rays) and Substances (Alcohol, Drugs) are classic environmental teratogens. Postdatism and Prematurity (Options C/D) are outcomes or states, not teratogens.

23.
During initial examination of a newborn, the midwife makes a conclusion of a right-sided facial paralysis by;
A. The right eye is open and mouth drawn to the left side
B. The right eye is open and mouth drawn to the right side
C. The left eye is open and mouth drawn to the right side
D. The left eye is open and mouth drawn to the left side
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. The right eye is open and mouth drawn to the left side

Explanation: In right-sided facial nerve paralysis, the muscles on the right side are weak. The right eye cannot close (stays open). The mouth is pulled towards the unaffected (left) side because the healthy muscles pull against the paralyzed ones.

25.
Epidural anaesthesia given to relief labor pains is contra-indicated in women with:
A. Hypovolemia, multiple pregnancy, pre-eclampsia
B. Local infection, hypovolemia, coagulation
C. Breech presentation, multiple pregnancy, pre-eclampsia
D. Multiple pregnancy, coagulation disorders, breech presentation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Local infection, hypovolemia, coagulation

Explanation: Absolute contraindications include:

  • Hypovolemia: Epidurals cause vasodilation and hypotension, worsening shock.
  • Coagulopathy (Coagulation disorders): Risk of epidural hematoma.
  • Infection at site: Risk of meningitis/abscess.
Pre-eclampsia is often an indication (to lower BP), not a contraindication, unless platelets are very low.

28.

29.
The following criteria is used to initiate ART in women who are HIV positive and pregnant
A. WHO stage 3 and 4.
B. CD4 < 350
C. On the same day HIV diagnosis is made
D. At a gestational period of 28 weeks
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. On the same day HIV diagnosis is made

30.
A baby born to a diabetic mother should be observed closely for at least for 48 hours to detect and treat the following common complications.
A. Hyperglycemia, Respiratory Distress Syndrome, Asphyxia
B. Asphyxia, Birth Injury, Hypoglycemia
C. Hypoglycemia, Respiratory Distress Syndrome, Jaundice
D. Jaundice, Hyperglycemia, Respiratory Distress Syndrome

Correct Answer: C. Hypoglycemia, Respiratory Distress Syndrome, Jaundice

37.
The best position to place a patient during correction of inversion of the uterus is;
A. Exaggerated Sims position
B. Knee chest position
C. Lithotomy position
D. Trendelenburg position
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Trendelenburg position

Explanation: The Trendelenburg position (head down, feet up) helps to relieve the traction on the uterine ligaments and uses gravity to assist in replacing the uterus into the abdominal cavity. O'Sullivan's hydrostatic method also uses this principle.

38.
The outcome of labour in short internal rotation in occipito posterior position is;
A. Normal delivery
B. Deep transverse arrest
C. Brow presentation
D. Face to pubis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Face to pubis

Explanation:

  • Long rotation (3/8 circle): Occiput rotates anteriorly to OA -> Normal delivery.
  • Short rotation (1/8 circle): Occiput rotates posteriorly into the sacral hollow (Direct Occipito Posterior). The baby is born with the face looking up at the pubic symphysis (Face to Pubis).
  • No rotation/Incomplete: Deep transverse arrest.

39.
The characteristics of monozygotic twins include that they:
A. Have two amnions, two chorions, fertilized by one spermatozoa
B. Develop from one ovum, same sex, two placentae
C. Have one amnion, different sexes, one placenta
D. Are of same sex, develop from one ovum, have one placenta
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Are of same sex, develop from one ovum, have one placenta

Explanation: Monozygotic (identical) twins develop from one fertilized ovum. They are genetically identical and thus must be of the same sex. Most commonly (if division occurs at blastocyst stage), they share one placenta (monochorionic) but have separate sacs (diamniotic).

40.
Mrs. R is rhesus negative. Under what circumstance would she be eligible for RH (D) immune globulin (RhIG)
A. If she had multiple pregnancy
B. If her fetus was found to be Rhesus negative
C. If her fetus heartbeat was tachycardic
D. If her fetus blood type was found to be Rhesus positive

41.
Focused Antenatal Care (FANC) is a systematic approach to providing healthcare and monitoring during pregnancy. State four (4) objectives of Focused Antenatal Care. (4 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Early detection and treatment of complications: Identifying pre-existing conditions (e.g., HIV, anemia) or pregnancy complications (e.g., pre-eclampsia) early.
  2. Prevention of diseases: Providing prophylaxis such as Tetanus Toxoid (TT), IPTp for malaria, iron/folate supplementation, and deworming.
  3. Birth preparedness and complication readiness: Helping the woman plan for place of delivery, transport, funds, blood donor, and birth companion.
  4. Health promotion and education: Counseling on nutrition, hygiene, breastfeeding, family planning, and danger signs.
42.
Puerperium refers to the period immediately following childbirth during which the mother’s body undergoes physiological changes as it returns to its non-pregnant state. Explain three (3) common breast complications that may occur during puerperium. (6 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Breast Engorgement:
    • Description: Painful swelling of breasts due to increased milk production, lymphatic drainage, and vascular congestion (usually day 3-5).
    • Signs: Hard, warm, painful breasts; shiny skin.
  2. Cracked Nipples:
    • Description: Fissures or sores on the nipple surface usually caused by poor attachment/positioning.
    • Signs: Sharp pain during feeding, visible cracks, bleeding.
  3. Mastitis:
    • Description: Inflammation/infection of the breast tissue, often entering through cracked nipples or due to milk stasis.
    • Signs: Localized red/hot wedge-shaped area, fever, flu-like symptoms.
43.
Pain during labor is a common and significant experience for many women. It can have various effects, both physical and emotional. Explain two (2) effects of pain during labor. (4 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Maternal Hyperventilation: Severe pain can cause the mother to breathe rapidly, leading to respiratory alkalosis. This can cause dizziness and reduce oxygen transfer to the fetus.
  2. Increased Catecholamine Release: Pain triggers the stress response (adrenaline). High catecholamines can cause vasoconstriction, reducing placental perfusion (fetal distress) and can also inhibit uterine contractions, prolonging labor.

45.
In the mechanism of normal labor, after descent, the passive movements that facilitate the progress of the baby through the birth canal are many: Explain three (3) of those movement (6 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Flexion:

    When the fetal head meets resistance from the cervix/pelvic floor, the chin is tucked onto the chest. This reduces the presenting diameter from Occipito-frontal (11.5cm) to Sub-occipito-bregmatic (9.5cm), facilitating passage.

  2. Internal Rotation:

    The occiput rotates 1/8th of a circle anteriorly (from LOA to OA) to lie behind the symphysis pubis. This aligns the long diameter of the fetal head (anteroposterior) with the long diameter of the pelvic outlet.

  3. Extension:

    Once the occiput passes under the symphysis pubis, the head is born by extension. The face sweeps across the perineum (sinciput, face, and chin emerge).

46.
The Active Management of the Third Stage of Labor (AMTSL) is an evidence-based approach used to actively intervene in the process following the birth of the baby and delivery of the placenta. Explain three (3) of the key components (6 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Administration of Uterotonic: Administer oxytocin (10 IU IM) within one minute of the delivery of the baby. This stimulates strong uterine contractions to prevent atony.
  2. Controlled Cord Traction (CCT): Apply gentle traction on the cord with one hand while applying counter-traction to the uterus just above the pubic bone (suprapubic pressure) with the other hand to deliver the placenta.
  3. Uterine Massage: Massage the fundus immediately after the delivery of the placenta until it is firm to ensure the uterus remains contracted and to express clots.
47.
Oligohydramnios is a condition characterized by a lower than normal amount of amniotic fluid surrounding the fetus in the uterus during pregnancy. State three (3) causes, and three (3) clinical signs and symptoms of oligohydramnios. (6 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

Causes (Any 3):

  • Premature Rupture of Membranes (PROM) - leakage of fluid.
  • Renal Agenesis (Fetal kidneys fail to develop/produce urine).
  • Post-term pregnancy (Placental insufficiency reduces fetal output).
  • Intrauterine Growth Restriction (IUGR).

Clinical Signs (Any 3):

  • Smaller fundal height: Uterine size is less than expected for gestational dates.
  • Easily palpable fetal parts: The fetus feels "molded" around the mother's abdomen; fetal parts are very prominent.
  • Reduced fetal movements: Mother may report decreased kicks.

48.
Madam Sue has been admitted in the labour ward in advanced stage of labour. Under your care she has delivered normally but the baby is small for gestational age. Describe the characteristics of a small for gestational age and manage the baby for the first 48 hours (20 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

1. Definition:

A Small for Gestational Age (SGA) baby is one whose birth weight is below the 10th percentile for their gestational age. They may be constitutionally small or suffer from Intrauterine Growth Restriction (IUGR).

2. Characteristics of SGA Baby (Asymmetrical/Wasted Appearance):

  • Head Size: Head appears large in proportion to the rest of the body ("old man look").
  • Abdomen: Scaphoid (sunken) abdomen due to lack of liver glycogen stores.
  • Skin: Loose, dry, and peeling skin with decreased subcutaneous fat and muscle mass.
  • Cord: Umbilical cord may be thin and withered.
  • Behavior: Often alert and wide-eyed (due to chronic hypoxia stress), having a strong suck reflex (hungry), but may tire easily.
  • Sole creases: Normal for gestational age (unlike preterm).

3. Management for the First 48 Hours:

A. Immediate Care & Resuscitation

  • Dry thoroughly and wrap in warm towels immediately to prevent hypothermia (large surface area to body weight ratio).
  • Assess APGAR score; provide resuscitation if needed as they are prone to asphyxia.

B. Thermoregulation (Prevention of Hypothermia)

  • Skin-to-Skin (Kangaroo Mother Care): Initiate immediately to maintain warmth.
  • Clothing: Dress in warm clothes, hat, and socks.
  • Environment: Keep the room warm (25°C+), away from drafts. Delay the first bath until temperature stabilizes (>36.5°C).

C. Prevention of Hypoglycemia

  • Early Feeding: Initiate breastfeeding within the first hour of birth. SGA babies have low glycogen stores.
  • Frequent Feeding: Feed every 2-3 hours. If sucking is poor, use cup or NGT feeding.
  • Monitoring: Check blood glucose levels regularly (e.g., at 2 hrs, 4 hrs, then pre-feeds). Treat blood sugar < 2.6 mmol/L immediately.

D. Infection Prevention

  • Observe strict hand hygiene before handling the baby.
  • Keep the umbilical cord clean and dry (chlorhexidine if protocol allows).
  • Limit visitors and handling by others.

E. Monitoring and Observation

  • Vital Signs: Monitor temperature, heart rate, and respiratory rate (watch for RDS).
  • Weighing: Record birth weight and monitor daily weight (some loss is normal, but excessive loss is dangerous).
  • Vitamin K: Administer Vitamin K to prevent hemorrhagic disease.
  • Eye Care: Apply Tetracycline eye ointment.
