Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions (40 Marks)

1.
In the development of the zygote, one of the following processes correctly characterizes the early stages of cleavage:
A. Formation of the Zona pellucida.
B. Increased number of cells in the zygote.
C. Formation of a hollow ball of cells.
D. Increase in the size of cells in the zygote.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Increased number of cells in the zygote.

Explanation: Cleavage is a series of rapid mitotic cell divisions that increase the number of cells (blastomeres) without increasing the total mass/size of the zygote (which remains contained within the zona pellucida).

2.
In multiple pregnancy, superfetation refers to the conception of twins from:
A. Sperms of different men within the same menstrual cycle.
B. Sperms of different men within two different menstrual cycles.
C. Two coital acts in two different menstrual cycles.
D. Two coital acts within the same menstrual cycle.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Two coital acts in two different menstrual cycles.

Explanation:

  • Superfetation: Fertilization of a second ovum released during a subsequent menstrual cycle after a pregnancy has already started (embryos of different gestational ages).
  • Superfecundation: Fertilization of two ova released in the same cycle by sperm from separate acts of coitus (potentially different fathers).

3.
A nurse assessing the uterine fundus of a mother in the immediate postpartum period notes that the uterus feels soft and boggy. Which would be the most appropriate initial nursing intervention:
A. Push on the uterus to assist in expressing clots.
B. Elevate the patient's legs.
C. Massage the fundus until it is firm.
D. Encourage the patient to void.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Massage the fundus until it is firm.

Explanation: A boggy uterus indicates uterine atony (failure to contract). The first-line intervention is fundal massage to stimulate contraction and arrest bleeding. Pushing on a boggy uterus (A) can cause inversion.

10.

C. Laterally, the urinary bladder is pushed upwards into the abdomen.

Correct Answer: D. There's an increase in cardiac output by 30-40%.

Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Early breastfeeding initiation. (Note: C is also good, but A is critical for glucose/warmth).

12.

A. Breast engorgement.
B. Mastitis.
C. Thrush.

Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Thrush.

35.
Five hours after birth, a neonate is transferred to the newborn unit (NBU), where the nurse intervenes to prevent hypothermia. What is a common source of heat loss via radiation in the unit?
A. Low room humidity.
B. Cold weight scale.
C. Cool room temperature.
D. Cool incubator walls.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Cool incubator walls.

Explanation:

  • Radiation: Loss of heat to cooler solid objects not in direct contact (e.g., cold walls, windows, incubator sides).
  • Conduction: Direct contact (Cold scale - Option B).
  • Convection: Air currents (Cool room air - Option C).
  • Evaporation: Wet skin/low humidity.

Show Answer

49.
Mrs. T, a Para 1+0 is suspected to have multiple pregnancy at a gestation of 24 weeks. As a practicing midwife explain the concept of â€˜vanishing twin syndromeâ€™ then describe the specific prenatal management that Mrs. T will receive.
Show Answer

Answer:

Concept of Vanishing Twin Syndrome:

Vanishing Twin Syndrome refers to the spontaneous loss (miscarriage or resorption) of one or more fetuses in a multiple gestation pregnancy, while the other fetus(es) survive. It typically occurs in the first trimester. The tissue of the deceased twin is absorbed by the mother, the other twin, or the placenta, causing the twin to "vanish" on subsequent ultrasounds.

Specific Prenatal Management for Mrs. T (Multiple Pregnancy):

1. Focused Antenatal Care Schedule:

  • Frequency: More frequent visits (e.g., every 2-3 weeks initially) to monitor for complications like PIH, anemia, and preterm labor.
  • Referral: Refer to a higher-level facility (Level 4/5) with obstetrician and NBU services for delivery planning.

2. Nutrition and Supplementation:

  • Diet: High protein, high calorie diet to support multiple fetuses.
  • Supplements: Increased Iron (prevent anemia) and Folic Acid. Calcium supplementation to prevent Pre-eclampsia.

3. Monitoring for Complications:

  • Pre-eclampsia: Frequent BP checks and urine protein analysis (higher risk in twins).
  • Anemia: Regular Hb monitoring.
  • Preterm Labor: Assess for signs of labor early; advise on rest.
  • Fetal Growth: Serial ultrasounds (every 4 weeks) to monitor growth of both twins and check for discordance or Twin-to-Twin Transfusion Syndrome (TTTS).

4. Health Education:

  • Rest: Encourage adequate rest, especially in the third trimester, to improve uteroplacental blood flow and reduce preterm risk.
  • Danger Signs: Educate on signs like bleeding, severe headache, reduced movements, or premature contractions.
  • Birth Plan: Emphasize hospital delivery with skilled attendance. Plan for potential C-section if presentation is unfavorable.

5. Emotional Support:

  • Counseling regarding the increased physical and financial burden of twins.
  • Addressing anxiety regarding the "vanishing twin" if diagnosis was confirmed.
