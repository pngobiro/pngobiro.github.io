Section 1: General Revision Questions

1.
State 2 components of the central nervous system.
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Brain: The control center of the body, located within the skull.
  2. Spinal Cord: The bundle of nerves running down the vertebral column that connects the brain to the rest of the body.
2.
Explain the composition of the peripheral nervous system.
Show Answer

Answer:

The Peripheral Nervous System (PNS) consists of all nervous tissue outside the Central Nervous System (CNS). It is composed of:

  • Cranial Nerves: 12 pairs of nerves emerging directly from the brain.
  • Spinal Nerves: 31 pairs of nerves emerging from the spinal cord.
  • Ganglia: Clusters of neuron cell bodies located outside the CNS.
  • Sensory Receptors: Specialized endings that detect changes in the internal or external environment.
3.
State 4 compositions of plasma.
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Water: Constitutes about 90-92% of plasma.
  • Plasma Proteins: Albumin, globulins, and fibrinogen.
  • Nutrients: Glucose, amino acids, fatty acids, and vitamins.
  • Electrolytes (Inorganic Salts): Sodium, potassium, calcium, chloride, bicarbonate.
  • Other components include: Gases (Oxygen, CO2), Waste products (Urea, creatinine), and Hormones.
4.
State 6 factors that affect heart rate.
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Autonomic Nervous System: Sympathetic stimulation increases rate; Parasympathetic decreases it.
  2. Hormones: Adrenaline (epinephrine) and Thyroxine increase heart rate.
  3. Age: Heart rate generally decreases from infancy to adulthood.
  4. Gender: Females typically have a slightly higher heart rate than males.
  5. Physical Activity/Exercise: Increases heart rate to meet oxygen demand.
  6. Body Temperature: Fever or heat increases heart rate; hypothermia decreases it.
  7. Others: Emotions (fear/anxiety), Drugs, and Electrolyte balance.
5.
State three processes involved in the formation of urine.
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Glomerular Filtration: Passive filtration of blood plasma from the glomerulus into the Bowman's capsule.
  2. Tubular Reabsorption: Moving essential substances (water, glucose, ions) from the filtrate back into the blood in the renal tubules.
  3. Tubular Secretion: Active transport of wastes (H+, K+, drugs) from the blood into the tubular fluid for excretion.
6.
State 4 factors which affect cardiac output.
Show Answer

Answer:

Cardiac Output (CO) = Stroke Volume (SV) Ã— Heart Rate (HR). Factors affecting it include:

  • Preload (Venous Return): The volume of blood stretching the ventricles at end-diastole (Frank-Starling law).
  • Contractility: The strength of the heart muscle contraction.
  • Afterload: The pressure the heart must overcome to eject blood (vascular resistance).
  • Heart Rate: The number of beats per minute.
7.
Explain 3 functions of the cerebral cortex.
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Sensory Perception: Interprets sensory impulses (pain, temperature, touch, vision, hearing) from the body.
  2. Voluntary Motor Control: Initiates and controls voluntary skeletal muscle movements (Motor areas).
  3. Higher Intellectual Functions: Responsible for memory, reasoning, judgment, intelligence, and personality (Association areas).
8.
Explain 4 types of bones, giving an example in each type.
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Long Bones: Have a shaft (diaphysis) and two ends (epiphyses). Function as levers.
    Example: Femur, Humerus, Tibia.
  • Short Bones: Cube-shaped, contain mostly spongy bone.
    Example: Carpals (wrist), Tarsals (ankle).
  • Flat Bones: Thin, flattened, and usually curved. Protect internal organs.
    Example: Sternum, Scapula, Cranial bones, Ribs.
  • Irregular Bones: Complicated shapes that don't fit other categories.
    Example: Vertebrae, Hip bones.
  • (Bonus) Sesamoid Bones: Develop within tendons (e.g., Patella).
9.
State the functions of cerebrospinal fluid.
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Protection/Shock Absorption: Acts as a cushion for the brain and spinal cord against trauma.
  • Buoyancy: Supports the weight of the brain ("floats" the brain), reducing pressure on the base of the skull.
  • Nutrient Exchange: Transports nutrients to nervous tissue and removes metabolic waste products.
  • Chemical Stability: Maintains a stable optimal chemical environment for neuronal signaling.
10.
State the functions of reticular formation.
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Reticular Activating System (RAS): Controls the state of wakefulness and alertness; filters sensory input (selective attention).
  • Motor Control: Helps maintain muscle tone, balance, and posture.
  • Autonomic Regulation: Contains centers regulating heart rate, blood pressure, and respiration (vital centers).
11.
With an aid of well labeled diagram, explain the development of bone tissue.
Show Answer

Answer:

Bone development occurs via two processes:

  1. Intramembranous Ossification: Bone forms directly from fibrous connective tissue membranes (e.g., skull bones, clavicle).
  2. Endochondral Ossification: Bone forms by replacing hyaline cartilage (most bones).
    • Formation of a bone collar around the hyaline cartilage model.
    • Cavitation of the hyaline cartilage within the cartilage model.
    • Invasion of internal cavities by the periosteal bud and spongy bone formation.
    • Formation of the medullary cavity as ossification continues; appearance of secondary ossification centers in the epiphyses.
    • Ossification of the epiphyses; when completed, hyaline cartilage remains only in the epiphyseal plates and articular cartilages.

Diagram Note: In an exam, draw a long bone showing the epiphysis, diaphysis, epiphyseal plate, and medullary cavity.

12.
Explain the 3 types of muscles giving specific examples.
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Skeletal Muscle: Striated, voluntary muscle attached to bones. Responsible for movement.
    Example: Biceps, Triceps, Quadriceps.
  • Cardiac Muscle: Striated, involuntary muscle found only in the heart. Responsible for pumping blood.
    Example: Myocardium.
  • Smooth (Visceral) Muscle: Non-striated, involuntary muscle found in walls of hollow organs.
    Example: Walls of stomach, intestines, blood vessels, bladder.
13.
State 3 layers of tissue covering the walls of arteries and arterioles.
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Tunica Intima (Interna): Innermost layer, consists of endothelium (squamous epithelium) providing a smooth surface for blood flow.
  • Tunica Media: Middle layer, consists of smooth muscle and elastic fibers. Regulates diameter (vasoconstriction/vasodilation).
  • Tunica Adventitia (Externa): Outermost layer, consists of fibrous connective tissue (collagen) for protection and anchoring.
14.
State 2 mechanisms which control the blood pressure.
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Short-term Control: Neural regulation via the baroreceptor reflex and chemoreceptors affecting the cardiovascular center in the medulla (adjusting heart rate and vessel diameter).
  • Long-term Control: Hormonal regulation, specifically the Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System (RAAS) and Antidiuretic Hormone (ADH) regulating blood volume and vasoconstriction.
15.
List 8 arteries forming the circus arteriosus (Circle of Willis).
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Anterior Communicating Artery (1)
  2. Anterior Cerebral Arteries (2)
  3. Internal Carotid Arteries (2)

17.
Explain 2 types of nerves which transmit nerve impulses.
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Sensory (Afferent) Nerves: Transmit impulses from sensory receptors (skin, organs) towards the Central Nervous System (Brain/Spinal cord).
  • Motor (Efferent) Nerves: Transmit impulses from the Central Nervous System to effectors (muscles and glands) to bring about a response.
  • (Mixed nerves contain both sensory and motor fibers).
18.
Explain 3 layers of the meninges.
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Dura Mater: The outermost, tough, fibrous double-layered membrane. It provides strong protection and anchors the brain to the skull.
  • Arachnoid Mater: The middle, web-like layer. It provides a space (subarachnoid space) for cerebrospinal fluid circulation.
  • Pia Mater: The innermost delicate layer that clings tightly to the surface of the brain and spinal cord, following every convolution. It is rich in blood vessels.
19.
Explain the 3 functions of the cerebral cortex.
Show Answer

Answer:

(Duplicate of Question 7)

  • Mental activities: Memory, learning, reasoning.
  • Sensory perception: Interpreting pain, touch, temperature.
  • Motor control: Initiating voluntary movements.
20.
State the functions of insulin.
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Lowers blood glucose levels by facilitating glucose uptake into cells.
  • Promotes Glycogenesis: Conversion of glucose to glycogen in the liver and muscles.
  • Inhibits Glycogenolysis and Gluconeogenesis.
  • Promotes protein synthesis and lipogenesis (fat storage).
21.
Explain the three types of joints giving examples.
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Fibrous Joints (Immovable/Synarthrosis): Bones joined by fibrous tissue.
    Example: Sutures of the skull.
  • Cartilaginous Joints (Slightly Movable/Amphiarthrosis): Bones joined by cartilage.
    Example: Intervertebral discs, Pubic symphysis.
  • Synovial Joints (Freely Movable/Diarthrosis): Bones separated by a fluid-filled cavity.
    Example: Knee, Elbow, Hip, Shoulder.
22.
Discuss the functions of stomach.
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Temporary Storage: Stores food allowing for gradual release into intestines.
  • Mechanical Digestion: Churning action mixes food with gastric juices to form chyme.
  • Chemical Digestion: Begins protein digestion via the enzyme Pepsin (activated by HCl).
  • Protection/Defense: Hydrochloric acid (HCl) kills ingested microbes.
  • Absorption: Limited absorption of water, alcohol, and some drugs.
  • Intrinsic Factor Secretion: Essential for Vitamin B12 absorption.
23.
With an aid of a well labeled diagram of nephron, describe the process of urine formation.
Show Answer

Answer:

Urine formation occurs in three steps:

  1. Filtration: Occurs in the Glomerulus. High pressure forces plasma (minus proteins/cells) into Bowman's capsule.
  2. Selective Reabsorption: Occurs mainly in Proximal Convoluted Tubule (PCT). Useful substances (glucose, water, ions) are moved back into blood capillaries. Loop of Henle concentrates urine.
  3. Tubular Secretion: Occurs in Distal Convoluted Tubule (DCT). Wastes (H+, K+, creatinine) are actively moved from blood into tubules.

Diagram: Draw the Bowman's capsule, Glomerulus, PCT, Loop of Henle, DCT, and Collecting Duct.

24.
Explain the functions of the skin.
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Protection: Barrier against physical injury, chemicals, and microbes. Melanin protects against UV rays.
  • Thermoregulation: Regulates body temperature via sweating and vasodilation/constriction of blood vessels.
  • Sensation: Contains nerve endings for touch, pain, pressure, and temperature.
  • Vitamin D Synthesis: Produces Vitamin D precursor when exposed to sunlight.
  • Excretion: Eliminates small amounts of salts and urea via sweat.
  • Absorption: Limited absorption of fat-soluble substances/drugs.
25.
Explain the structural relationship between chromosomes, genes and DNA.
Show Answer

Answer:

DNA (Deoxyribonucleic Acid) is the fundamental chemical molecule carrying genetic information. It exists as a double helix.

Genes are specific segments of DNA that code for specific proteins or traits. They are the functional units of heredity.

Chromosomes are highly coiled and condensed structures made of DNA wound around histone proteins. They carry thousands of genes and are visible during cell division. Humans have 23 pairs.

Hierarchy: Nucleotides -> DNA -> Gene -> Chromosome -> Nucleus.

26.
a) Draw the diagram of the conducting system of the heart.
b) Describe the conducting system of the heart.
Show Answer

Answer:

b) Description:

  • Sinoatrial (SA) Node: The "Pacemaker" located in the right atrium. Initiates the impulse (60-100 bpm), causing atrial contraction.
  • Atrioventricular (AV) Node: Located at junction of atria and ventricles. Delays impulse briefly to allow ventricles to fill.
  • Bundle of His (AV Bundle): Conducts impulse from atria to ventricles.
  • Bundle Branches: Left and Right branches carry impulse down the interventricular septum.
  • Purkinje Fibers: Distribute impulse throughout ventricular walls, causing forceful ventricular contraction from the apex upwards.

Section 2: End of Semester MCQs

1.
The most fundamental structural level of the body is:
a) Chemical
b) Molecular
c) Atom
d) Biochemical
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Chemical

Explanation: The chemical level (atoms and molecules) is the most basic level of structural organization, followed by cellular, tissue, organ, system, and organismal levels.

a) A cell
b) A tissue
c) An organ
d) Organelles
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) An organ

Explanation: An organ consists of two or more different types of tissues working together to perform specific functions.

Correct Answer: d) Homeostasis

Explanation: Homeostasis is the state of maintaining a stable internal environment. Negative feedback is the primary method used to achieve it.

Section 3: Short Answer Questions (SAQ)

1.
State the four (4) functions of the cerebrospinal fluid (CSF).
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Supports and protects the brain and spinal cord by maintaining a uniform pressure.
  2. Acts as a shock absorber or cushion between the brain and the skull.
  3. Keeps the brain and spinal cord moist.
  4. Facilitates the exchange of nutrients and waste products between the blood and nervous tissue.
2.
State three (3) functions of reticular formation.
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Arousal and Alertness: The Reticular Activating System (RAS) maintains consciousness and sleep-wake cycles.
  2. Somatic Motor Control: Helps maintain muscle tone, balance, and posture via the reticulospinal tracts.
  3. Autonomic Control: Regulates visceral functions like heart rate, blood pressure, and breathing.
3.
State three (3) different types of joints.
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Fibrous Joints: Immovable (e.g., Skull sutures).
  2. Cartilaginous Joints: Slightly movable (e.g., Intervertebral discs).
  3. Synovial Joints: Freely movable (e.g., Knee, Shoulder).
4.
State four (4) functions of bones.
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Support: Provide structural framework for the body.
  2. Protection: Protect internal organs (e.g., Ribcage protects heart/lungs).
  3. Movement: Act as levers for muscles to pull on.
  4. Mineral Storage: Store calcium and phosphorus.
  5. Hematopoiesis: Blood cell formation in red marrow.
5.
With an aid of a labeled diagram, explain the development of bone tissue (5mks).
Show Answer

Answer:

Bone develops mainly via Endochondral Ossification (replacing cartilage):

  1. Hyaline cartilage model is formed.
  2. A bone collar forms around the diaphysis.
  3. Cartilage in the center calcifies and develops cavities.
  4. The periosteal bud invades, forming spongy bone (Primary Ossification Center).
  5. The diaphysis elongates and a medullary cavity forms.
  6. Secondary ossification centers appear in the epiphyses.

Note: Ensure you practice drawing the long bone stages for the exam.

6.
State five (5) constituents of plasma.
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Water (90%)
  2. Plasma proteins (Albumin, Globulins, Fibrinogen)
  3. Nutrients (Glucose, Amino acids)
  4. Electrolytes (Sodium, Potassium, Chloride)
  5. Waste products (Urea, Uric acid)
