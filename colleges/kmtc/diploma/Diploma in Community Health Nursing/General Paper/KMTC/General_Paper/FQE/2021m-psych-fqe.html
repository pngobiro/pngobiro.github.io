Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions (40 Marks)

1.
Gingivitis is a periodontal disease characterized by;
a) Reddened gums, loosening of teeth, pockets of ppus formation
b) Tooth decay, loose teeth, bleeding gums
c) Reddened gums, purulent discharge pockets formation
d) Bleeding gums, swollen gums, reddened gums
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Bleeding gums, swollen gums, reddened gums

Explanation: Gingivitis is the early stage of gum disease caused by plaque. Its classic signs are inflammation: redness (erythema), swelling (edema), and bleeding, especially during brushing. Loosening of teeth (A/B) indicates advanced Periodontitis.

2.
The type of fracture in which the bone splinters into several fragments is;
a) Comminuted
b) Avulsion
c) Impacted
d) Depressed
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Comminuted

Explanation: A comminuted fracture is one where the bone breaks into three or more pieces (splinters/shatters). Avulsion (B) is a ligament tearing a piece of bone off. Impacted (C) is bone ends driven into each other.

3.
A well functioning chest tube drainage system should have;
a) No fluctuation of water in the water seal chamber
b) Drainage system maintained above the patient's chest
c) Continuous bubbling in water chamber
d) Fluctuation of water in water seal chamber during inhalation and exhalation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Fluctuation of water in water seal chamber during inhalation and exhalation

Explanation: This is called "tidaling." It indicates the system is patent (open). No fluctuation (A) suggests a blockage or full lung re-expansion. Continuous bubbling (C) indicates an air leak. The system must be *below* the chest (B is wrong).

4.
Chvostek's sign indicates;
a) Hypocalcemic
b) Hypercalcemia
c) Hypomagnesia
d) Hypemagnesemia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Hypocalcemic

Explanation: Chvostek's sign is facial muscle twitching when the facial nerve is tapped, a classic sign of neuromuscular excitability due to low calcium (Hypocalcemia). It can also occur in hypomagnesemia, but Hypocalcemia is the primary answer.

5.
The inauguration of the safe motherhood initiative in Kenya was in;
a) 1982
b) 1987
c) 1975
d) 2000
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) 1987

Explanation: The Safe Motherhood Initiative was launched globally in Nairobi, Kenya, in 1987.

6.
All HIV exposed infants should be tested first at;
a) 4 months of age
b) 6 months of age
c) 6 weeks of age
d) 1 week of age
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) 6 weeks of age

Explanation: Guidelines recommend early infant diagnosis (EID) using DNA PCR at 6 weeks of age (or earliest opportunity thereafter).

7.
The ethical principle in nursing that states that the action one takes should promote good is;
a) Justice
b) Veracity
c) Beneficence
d) Autonomy
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Beneficence

Explanation: Beneficence is the duty to do good and act in the best interest of the patient. Veracity is truth-telling. Autonomy is self-determination.

8.
The fundamental responsibility of the nurse is;
a) Sustain a cooperative relationship
b) Safeguard individuals
c) Ensuring quality health care
d) Alleviate suffering
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Alleviate suffering

Explanation: According to the ICN Code of Ethics, the four fundamental responsibilities are: to promote health, to prevent illness, to restore health, and to alleviate suffering. While the others are duties, "Alleviate suffering" is a core definition.

9.
The term bioavailability means the amount of drug;
a) That must be given to reach therapeutic level
b) That reaches the general circulation unaltered from the site of administration
c) Excreted unchanged from the body tissues
d) Given as maximum dose
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) That reaches the general circulation unaltered from the site of administration

Explanation: Bioavailability is the fraction of an administered dose of unchanged drug that reaches the systemic circulation.

10.
The antibiotic with a bacteriostatic action is
a) Ampicillin
b) Gentamycin
c) Erythromycin
d) Cefalexin
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Erythromycin

Explanation: Erythromycin (a Macrolide) inhibits protein synthesis and is generally bacteriostatic. Ampicillin (Penicillin), Gentamycin (Aminoglycoside), and Cefalexin (Cephalosporin) are generally bactericidal (kill bacteria).

11.
Active transport involves transport of substances
a) Down the concentration gradient
b) That are negatively charged
c) Up the concentration gradient
d) That are positively charged
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Up the concentration gradient

Explanation: Active transport moves molecules against (up) the concentration gradient (from low to high), requiring energy (ATP).

12.
Macrophages found in the spaces between liver cells are called;
a) Neutrophils
b) Lymphocytes
c) Mast cells
d) Kupffer cells
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Kupffer cells

Explanation: Kupffer cells are the specialized resident macrophages of the liver, located in the liver sinusoids.

13.
A typical neuropathway is;
a) Effector -sensory nerve-PNS-motor nerve-receptor
b) Receptor-sensory nerve-CNS-motor nerve-effector
c) Effector-motor nerve-PNS-sensory nerve-receptor
d) Receptor-motor nerve-CNS-sensory nerve-effector
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Receptor-sensory nerve-CNS-motor nerve-effector

Explanation: The reflex arc pathway: Stimulus -> Receptor -> Sensory Neuron (Afferent) -> CNS (Interneuron) -> Motor Neuron (Efferent) -> Effector (Muscle/Gland).

14.
Indicate whether the following statements are true or false;
Show Answer
  • i) The sterilization process ensures that all micro-organisms including bacterial endorspores are destroyed: TRUE. This is the definition of sterilization.
  • ii) Glutaraldehyde (Cidex) is a high level disinfectant: TRUE. It can be a sterilant with prolonged exposure (10 hours) but is classified as a high-level disinfectant for shorter durations.
15.
When examining a patient with abdominal pain the nurse should assess,
a) Any quadrant first
b) The symptomatic quadrant first
c) The symptomatic quadrant last
d) The symptomatic quadrant first and then last
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) The symptomatic quadrant last

Explanation: Palpating the painful area last prevents the patient from tensing up (guarding) early in the exam, which would make the rest of the examination difficult and inaccurate.

16.
A female patient with a terminal illness is in denial if the following is present
a) Shock and dismay
b) Numbness
c) Stoicism
d) Preparatory grief
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Shock and dismay

Explanation: The first stage of Kubler-Ross's grief cycle is Denial. It is often accompanied by shock, disbelief, and dismay ("This cannot be happening to me"). Numbness is also a grief reaction, but shock/dismay most closely aligns with the initial refusal to accept reality.

17.
Passive immunization is obtained from
a) Tuberculosis
b) Enteric fever
c) Diphtheria
d) Cholera
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Diphtheria

Explanation: This refers to the Diphtheria Antitoxin (antibodies), which provides passive immunity. The other options (TB - BCG, Cholera vaccine) typically refer to active immunization vaccines. Diphtheria is classically treated/prevented post-exposure with antitoxin.

18.
The toxins produced by bacteria,
a) Encourage bacterial reproduction
b) Interfere with physiological process in the body
c) Kill viruses
d) Determine survival of the bacteria
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Interfere with physiological process in the body

Explanation: Bacterial toxins (exotoxins/endotoxins) damage host tissues and disrupt normal physiological functions (e.g., stopping protein synthesis, overactivating immune response).

19.
The nurse writes an external outcome statement in measurable terms. An example is;
a) Patient will report pain acuity less that 4 on a scale of 0-10
b) Patient will be pain free
c) Patient will have less pain
d) Patient will take pain medication every 4 hours around the cock
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Patient will report pain acuity less that 4 on a scale of 0-10

Explanation: A measurable outcome must have specific criteria (scale of 0-10, value < 4). "Pain free" (B) is vague (when?). "Less pain" (C) is subjective. Option D is a nursing intervention, not a patient outcome.

20.
The drugs classified as narcotic analgesics include;
a) Tramadol, diclomol
b) Acetaminophen, Tramadol
c) Morphine, Codeine
d) Morphine, Diclomol
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Morphine, Codeine

Explanation: Morphine and Codeine are classic opioid (narcotic) analgesics derived from opium. Acetaminophen and Diclomol (Diclofenac) are non-narcotic/NSAIDS. Tramadol is a synthetic opioid but Morphine/Codeine is the strongest "narcotic" pair listed.

21.
The management of a patient with congestive cardiac failure include;
a) Monitoring vital signs four hourly, give diet low in sodium
b) Weigh patient daily, give diet rich in sodium
c) Administer broad spectrum antibiotics, weigh the patient monthly
d) Monitor vital signs 12 hourly, give diet rich in sodium
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Monitoring vital signs four hourly, give diet low in sodium

Explanation: CCF management involves fluid/sodium restriction to reduce overload. Daily weights are crucial (not monthly). Vital signs should be monitored frequently (4 hourly is appropriate for unstable/acute phases).

22.
A risk factor of arteriosclerosis and atherosclerosis that can be modified is;
a) Race, age
b) Age, gender
c) Race, obesity
d) Obesity, smoking
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Obesity, smoking

Explanation: Modifiable risk factors are those you can change (lifestyle, weight, smoking). Non-modifiable factors include Age, Gender, and Race/Genetics.

24.
The following is a priority nursing diagnosis for a patient presenting with asthmatic attack.
a) High risk for respiratory distress related to inflammatory process
b) Ineffective airway clearance related to mucus production...
c) Ineffective breathing related to broncho spasms as evidenced by dyspnea.
d) Anxiety related to difficulty in breathing...
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Ineffective breathing related to broncho spasms as evidenced by dyspnea.

Explanation: In an acute asthma attack, the primary physiological problem is bronchospasm causing airway narrowing and difficulty breathing (Ineffective Breathing Pattern or Impaired Gas Exchange). While airway clearance (mucus) is an issue, the spasm is the immediate life-threat.

25.
The extent to which the drug is absorbed and transported to the target site is known as
a) Therapeutic dose
b) Steady state accumulation
c) Bioavailability
d) Distribution
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Bioavailability

Explanation: Repeated question (see Q9). Bioavailability defines the rate and extent to which the active drug reaches systemic circulation/target site.

26.
The action of hypnotics is to induce;
a) Vomiting
b) Calmness
c) Appetite
d) Sleep
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Sleep

Explanation: Hypnotics induce sleep. Sedatives induce calmness (B). Emetics induce vomiting (A).

27.
Addison's disease is caused by;
a) Pituitary gland tumour
b) Inadequate production of adrenocortical hormones
c) Low levels of thyroid hormone
d) Excess aldosterone production
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Inadequate production of adrenocortical hormones

Explanation: Addison's disease is primary adrenal insufficiency, leading to a deficiency in cortisol and aldosterone.

28.
The following hormone is associated with diabetes insipidus;
a) Antidiuretic hormone
b) Somatostatin
c) Insulin
d) Glucagon
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Antidiuretic hormone

Explanation: Diabetes Insipidus is caused by a deficiency of ADH (Vasopressin) or the kidney's inability to respond to it.

29.
One of characteristics of hyperthyroidism is;
a) Hyperthermic
b) Calmness
c) Sleepiness
d) Hypothermic
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Hyperthermic

Explanation: Hyperthyroidism increases metabolic rate, leading to heat intolerance and elevated body temperature (Hyperthermia). Hypothyroidism causes cold intolerance/Hypothermia (D) and Sleepiness (C).

30.
After insertion of implants, the client should be instructed to;
a) Keep insertion area dry for four to five days
b) Take a regular dose of paracetamol if soreness persists for more than a week
c) Remove adhesive tape after 10 days
d) Clean insertion site daily with warm normal saline
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Keep insertion area dry for four to five days

Explanation: Keeping the area dry prevents infection while the small incision heals. Soreness persisting >1 week (B) requires medical checkup, not just paracetamol.

31.
Reduction in bone density is a particular concern with the use of;
a) Depo vera
b) Implant
c) Progestin only pill
d) Intrauterine device
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Depo vera

Explanation: DMPA (Depo-Provera) is associated with a reversible loss of bone mineral density with long-term use.

32.
Symptoms of peritonitis include;
a) Right upper quadrant pain
b) Hyperactive bowel sounds
c) Abdominal distension
d) Abdominal mass
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Abdominal distension

Explanation: Peritonitis presents with rigid, board-like abdomen, distension, pain (generalized), and *absent* or hypoactive bowel sounds (paralytic ileus). Hyperactive sounds (B) indicate obstruction/gastroenteritis.

33.
Peptic ulcers characterized by haematemesis and pain that worsens with ingestion of food is;
a) Duodenal ulcer
b) Gastric ulcers
c) Esophageal ulcer
d) Both gastric and duodenal ulcer
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Gastric ulcers

Explanation: Gastric ulcer pain increases with food (acid secretion stimulates the ulcer). Duodenal ulcer pain is relieved by food (pyloric sphincter closes, protecting duodenum) and occurs 2-3 hours after meals.

34.
Orthostatic hypotension is;
a) Slight drop in blood pressure from supine position to standing position
b) Significant drop in blood pressure from supine position to standing position
c) Significant drop in blood pressure from standing up position to supine position
d) Slight drop in blood pressure from standing position to supine position
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Significant drop in blood pressure from supine position to standing position

Explanation: Orthostatic (postural) hypotension is a drop in BP (usually >20 systolic or >10 diastolic) upon standing up from a sitting/lying position.

35.
Clinical progression of acute renal failure includes;
a) Recovery diuretic oliguric, initiation phase
b) Oliguric, initiation, diuretic, recovery phase
c) Diuretic, recovery, initiation, oliguric phase
d) Initiation, oliguric, diuretic, recover phase
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Initiation, oliguric, diuretic, recover phase

Explanation: The standard phases of Acute Kidney Injury (AKI) are: 1. Initiation (onset), 2. Oliguric (maintenance), 3. Diuretic (polyuric), 4. Recovery.

36.
Hypoalbuminemia in nephrotic syndrome causes;
a) Deactivation of Renin angiotensin system
b) Hypotension
c) Increased oncotic pressure
d) Generalized edema
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Generalized edema

Explanation: Loss of albumin reduces plasma oncotic pressure (C is wrong). This allows fluid to leak into tissues, causing massive generalized edema (Anasarca). It stimulates the Renin-Angiotensin system (A is wrong).

37.
An example of electrolyte change that occur in patients with acute renal failure is;
a) Hyperkalemia
b) Hypophosphatemia
c) Hypercalcemia
d) Hyponatremic
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Hyperkalemia

Explanation: Kidneys fail to excrete potassium, leading to dangerous Hyperkalemia. They also fail to excrete phosphate (causing Hyperphosphatemia) and activate Vitamin D (causing Hypocalcemia).

38.
Reverse barrier nursing aims at;
a) Protecting a susceptible patient from acquiring nosocomial infection
b) Protecting infected patients from acquiring any other infection
c) Protecting uninfected patient from acquiring nosocomial infection
d) Protecting both the susceptible and uninfected patient...
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Protecting a susceptible patient from acquiring nosocomial infection

Explanation: Reverse isolation (protective isolation) is used for immunocompromised patients (e.g., burns, chemotherapy) to protect *them* from germs carried by staff/visitors.

39.
The most common micro-organism that causes urinary tract infection in females is;
a) Chlamydia trachomatis
b) Entamoeba histolyticum
c) Staphylococcus Aureus
d) Escherichia Coli
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Escherichia Coli

Explanation: E. Coli (from the GI tract) is responsible for the vast majority (approx 80-90%) of community-acquired UTIs.

40.
The immune system that recognizes antigens or pathogens circulating in blood or lymph is;
a) Cell mediated
b) Normal flora
c) Pathogenesis
d) Humoral mediated
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Humoral mediated

Explanation: Humoral immunity (B-cells) deals with extracellular pathogens (in blood/lymph) via antibodies. Cell-mediated immunity (T-cells) deals with intracellular pathogens (inside cells).

46.
Explain THREE characteristics of clostridium tetani (6 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Anaerobic: It thrives in environments without oxygen (e.g., deep puncture wounds).
  2. Spore-forming: Forms resilient spores that survive in soil/dust for years.
  3. Toxin-producing: Produces a potent neurotoxin (tetanospasmin) that causes muscle rigidity and spasms.

  4. Frequent infections.
  5. Splenomegaly (in early childhood).

Section 3: Long Answer Question (40 Marks)

49.
Nursing professionalism is a complex, varied and dynamic process that encompasses a set of values that are critical to elevating quality of patient care.
(i) Outline THREE codes of ethics that guide the members of nursing profession in their practice (3 marks)
(ii) State FIVE characteristics of a professional nurse (5 marks)
(iii) Outline SEVEN ethical principles in nursing (7 marks)
(iv) State five (5) patients' bill of rights (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer Guide:

(i) Codes of Ethics (ICN) - (3 Marks)

  1. Nurses and People: Primary responsibility is to people requiring care.
  2. Nurses and Practice: Personal responsibility for nursing practice and maintaining competence.
  3. Nurses and the Profession: Major role in determining and implementing standards of practice.
  4. (Bonus: Nurses and Co-workers).

(ii) Characteristics of a Professional Nurse (5 Marks)

  1. Competent: Has necessary knowledge and skills.
  2. Compassionate: Shows empathy and care.
  3. Integrity: Honest and ethical.
  4. Accountable: Takes responsibility for actions.
  5. Continuous Learner: Engages in lifelong learning.
  6. Advocate: Speaks up for patient rights.

(iii) Ethical Principles (7 Marks)

  1. Autonomy: Respecting patient's right to make own decisions.
  2. Beneficence: Duty to do good.
  3. Non-maleficence: Duty to do no harm.
  4. Justice: Fairness in care and resource distribution.
  5. Veracity: Telling the truth.
  6. Fidelity: Keeping promises/faithfulness.
  7. Confidentiality: Protecting private information.

(iv) Patients' Bill of Rights (5 Marks)

  1. Right to receive emergency treatment.
  2. Right to be informed (Information).
  3. Right to Refuse treatment.
  4. Right to Confidentiality and privacy.
  5. Right to complain.
  6. Right to a second opinion.
  7. Right to highest attainable standard of health.
