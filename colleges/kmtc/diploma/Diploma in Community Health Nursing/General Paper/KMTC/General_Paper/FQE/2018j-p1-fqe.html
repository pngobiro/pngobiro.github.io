Part I: Multiple Choice Questions (20 Marks)

1.
A skin condition characterized by non-infectious inflammatory lesions which are well defined with large adherent, silvery scales is:
a) Atopic eczema
b) Psoriasis
c) Seborrheic eczema
d) Erythrodermic psoriasis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Psoriasis

Explanation: The classic presentation of Plaque Psoriasis is raised, red patches covered with a silvery-white buildup of dead skin cells (scales), often on elbows and knees.

2.
The chief manifestations of Parkinson's disease include:
a) Fatigue, weakness, numbness, difficulties in coordinating and loss of balance
b) Chorea, intellectual decline, emotional disturbance, fatigue and loss of postural reflexes
c) Numbness, muscular rigidity, loss of balance, fatigue, tremors and loss of vision
d) Impaired movement, muscular rigidity, tremors, muscle weakness and loss of postural reflex
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Impaired movement, muscular rigidity, tremors, muscle weakness and loss of postural reflex

Explanation: The cardinal signs of Parkinson's (TRAP) are Tremor (resting), Rigidity (cogwheel), Akinesia/Bradykinesia (impaired movement), and Postural instability (loss of postural reflexes).

3.
While performing a physical examination of the abdomen, the correct sequence of the techniques is:
a) Inspection, palpation, percussion, auscultation
b) Inspection, percussion, palpation, auscultation
c) Inspection, auscultation, percussion, palpation
d) Inspection, percussion, palpation, auscultation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Inspection, auscultation, percussion, palpation

Explanation: For abdominal exams, auscultation is done second (after inspection) to avoid altering bowel sounds which might happen if percussion or palpation were done first.

4.
The inflammation of iris, ciliary body and the Choroid is known as:
a) Lephesitis
b) Keratitis
c) Uveitis
d) Iritis and choroiditis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Uveitis

Explanation: The Uvea consists of the Iris, Ciliary body, and Choroid. Inflammation of this tract is Uveitis. (Iritis is just the iris; Keratitis is the cornea).

5.
Coma in diabetic ketoacidosis is usually a result of:
a) Excessive circulating insulin
b) Collapse due to dehydration
c) Depressed cerebral function due to ketones
d) Decreased cerebral tissue perfusion
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Depressed cerebral function due to ketones

Explanation: While severe dehydration (shock) contributes, the altered mental status and coma in DKA are primarily attributed to the toxic effects of acidosis and ketone bodies on the central nervous system, alongside hyperosmolarity.

6.
The major blood groupings are determined by presence of:
a) Antibodies in the plasma and antigen on red cells membrane
b) Antigen in red blood cells and antibodies in white blood cells
c) Antibodies in Red blood cells and antigen on white blood cells
d) Antigens on red blood cells membrane, and antigen in the red cells nucleus
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Antibodies in the plasma and antigen on red cells membrane

Explanation: Blood type (e.g., A, B, AB, O) is determined by the specific antigens present on the surface of the Red Blood Cells (RBCs). The plasma contains antibodies against the antigens not present on the RBCs.

7.
Rheumatoid arthritis is characterized by:
a) Painful joint, swollen joint, gradual onset
b) Inflamed joint, joint deformity, fixed joint
c) Joint thickening, hotness of the affected limb, pain at the limb
d) Joint stiffness, painful joint, fever of 38Â°C
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Painful joint, swollen joint, gradual onset (or D is also likely depending on specific text)

Explanation: RA typically presents with symmetrical joint pain, swelling (synovitis), morning stiffness, and is a chronic systemic condition (gradual onset). Option B describes late-stage complications.

8.
When caring for a patient on hemodialysis, the nurse will keenly monitor the patient for:
a) Pneumonia, Hemorrhage, Urine retention and dysrhythmias.
b) Hypotension, muscle cramps, chest pain and exsanguinations.
c) Air embolism, chest pain, urine retention and pneumonia.
d) Hypertension, chest pain, hemorrhage and air embolism
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Hypotension, muscle cramps, chest pain and exsanguinations.

Explanation: Common and serious complications during/immediately after dialysis include Hypotension (from rapid fluid removal), Muscle Cramps (fluid/electrolyte shifts), and risk of Bleeding (heparin use). Exsanguination is a risk if lines disconnect.

10.
To prevent cross infection, the nurse should:
a) Disinfect working surfaces
b) Carry out isolation practices
c) Glove during procedures
d) Do handwashing
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Do handwashing

Explanation: Handwashing is universally recognized as the single most effective method to prevent the spread of infection (cross-infection) in healthcare settings.

11.
Manifestation of cystitis include:
a) Discharge, itching, frequency of urination
b) Dysuria, frequency of urination, urgency of micturition
c) Itching, frequency of urination, dysuria
d) Urgency of micturition, discharge, frequency of urination
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Dysuria, frequency of urination, urgency of micturition

Explanation: The classic triad of lower UTI (cystitis) symptoms includes Dysuria (painful urination), Frequency, and Urgency. Discharge usually points to urethritis/STI.

12.
The priority nursing intervention for a patient with dry heat burns of the face, neck and chest includes:
a) Prevention of infections, hydrating the patient
b) Prevention of contractures, monitoring electrolytes
c) Establishing patent airway, prevention of infections
d) Establishing patent airway, administering analgesic
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Establishing patent airway, administering analgesic (Or C, depending on priorities)

Explanation: Burns to the face/neck pose a high risk of airway edema and obstruction (inhalation injury). Airway is Priority #1. Analgesia is crucial for burns. Infection control is important but secondary to Airway.

13.
Effects of sympathetic nervous system input in the gastrointestinal system include:
a) Increased digestive juices secretion, increased motility
b) Decreased motility, increased absorption
c) Decreased sphincter contraction, decreased motility
d) Increased motility, decreased digestive juices secretion
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Decreased motility, increased absorption (Note: decreased motility is correct)

Correction: The sympathetic nervous system ("Fight or Flight") inhibits GI function. It decreases motility and secretions, and contracts sphincters (closes them). It directs blood away from the gut. Option B is the closest fit for "Decreased motility" although absorption is generally reduced due to reduced blood flow.

14.
The common bacteria causing tonsillitis is:
a) Staphylococci
b) Streptococci
c) Pneumococci
d) Clostridia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Streptococci

Explanation: Group A Beta-Hemolytic Streptococcus (GABHS) is the most common bacterial cause of acute tonsillitis/pharyngitis ("Strep throat").

15.
Management of a patient with myocardial infarction include:
a) Moderate physical activity, high calorie diet
b) Low sodium diet, low fiber-diet
c) Supine positioning, high calorie diet
d) Low calorie diet, bed-rest
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Low calorie diet, bed-rest

Explanation: In the acute phase of MI, the goal is to reduce myocardial oxygen demand. Bed rest reduces workload. A low calorie/light diet prevents drawing excessive blood to the gut for digestion and avoids straining (constipation management usually requires high fiber, but low calorie is key for cardiac load). Options A and C are contraindicated (activity/high calorie).

16.
During tracheostomy suctioning the nurse should;
a) Thin the secretions with 10mls of dextrose saline before suctioning.
b) Suction from inside outwards in circular motions for 10-15 seconds.
c) Suction the mouth and nose before suctioning the tracheostomy.
d) Decanulate the tracheostomy before suctioning.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Suction from inside outwards in circular motions for 10-15 seconds.

Explanation: Correct technique involves inserting the catheter without suction, then withdrawing while applying intermittent suction and rotating the catheter (circular motion) for no more than 10-15 seconds to prevent hypoxia. Tracheostomy is suctioned *before* the mouth to keep it sterile (C is wrong).

18.
When conducting physical examination on a patient, the nurse expects evidence of diminished cardiac output to include:
a) A narrow pulse pressure
b) Rapid capillary refill
c) Widened pulse pressure
d) Bounding pulses
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) A narrow pulse pressure

Explanation: Low cardiac output leads to a drop in systolic pressure while diastolic pressure may remain stable or rise due to vasoconstriction, resulting in a narrow pulse pressure (e.g., 90/80). Bounding pulses and widened pressure are signs of high output or aortic regurgitation.

19.
When performing CPR to a child, the recommended ratio for chest compressions to ventilation for two rescuers is:
a) 15:2
b) 30:2
c) 15:5
d) 5:2
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) 15:2

Explanation: For infants and children, the compression-to-ventilation ratio is 30:2 for a single rescuer, but changes to 15:2 for two rescuers (AHA Guidelines).

a) Rifampicin, pyrazinamide
b) Isoniazid, rifampicin
c) Ethambutol, rifampicin

Part II: Short Answer Questions (40 Marks)

1.
State five (5) indications for hemodialysis (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Answer: (Mnemonic: AEIOU)

  1. Acidosis: Severe metabolic acidosis not responding to meds.
  2. Electrolytes: Severe Hyperkalemia (>6.5 mmol/L).
  3. Intoxication: Overdose of dialyzable drugs (e.g., Salicylates, Lithium).
  4. Overload: Fluid overload (pulmonary edema) refractory to diuretics.
  5. Uremia: Symptoms like uremic pericarditis or encephalopathy (confusion).
2.
Explain three types of sickle cell crisis (6 Marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Vaso-occlusive Crisis (Pain Crisis): Sickled cells block blood flow to tissues/bones causing ischemia and severe pain.
  2. Sequestration Crisis: Sickled cells pool in the spleen (or liver), causing sudden drop in hemoglobin (anemia) and shock.
  3. Aplastic Crisis: Temporary cessation of red blood cell production by bone marrow, usually triggered by Parvovirus B19 infection.
  4. (Bonus: Hemolytic Crisis).
3.
Outline five (5) nursing diagnosis for a patient with burns of the back and the face (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Ineffective Airway Clearance related to edema/inhalation injury (face burns).
  2. Impaired Gas Exchange related to carbon monoxide poisoning or smoke inhalation.
  3. Deficient Fluid Volume related to fluid shift and evaporation from burn wound.
  4. Risk for Infection related to loss of skin barrier.
  5. Acute Pain related to exposed nerve endings.
  6. Impaired Skin Integrity related to burn injury.

Show Answer

  3. Tachycardia (rapid heart rate) or palpitations.

5.
Explain the pathophysiology of Pneumonia (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

Pathogens (bacteria/virus) enter the lungs via aspiration or inhalation, bypassing host defenses. They multiply in the alveoli, triggering an inflammatory response. White blood cells (neutrophils), fluid, and RBCs accumulate in the alveoli (Exudate). This fills the air spaces (Consolidation), impairing gas exchange (Oxygen/CO2 diffusion). This leads to hypoxia and the clinical signs of pneumonia (cough, fever, dyspnea).

7.
State five (5) nursing interventions for a patient with hip replacement surgery. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Positioning: Maintain abduction of the affected leg using a wedge pillow to prevent dislocation.
  2. Movement Restrictions: Avoid flexion of the hip >90 degrees (no low chairs, bending).
  3. Pain Management: Administer analgesics as prescribed.
  4. DVT Prophylaxis: Apply anti-embolism stockings, encourage ankle pumps, administer anticoagulants.
  5. Wound Care: Monitor incision site for signs of infection or excessive bleeding.
  6. Early Ambulation: Assist with walking using aids as tolerated.

1.
Mrs Havana 60 years old is admitted with a diagnosis of bacterial meningitis.
a) Draw and label a diagram illustrating the flow of the cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) (5 Marks)
b) List four clinical features Mrs. Havana may present with (2 Marks)
c) Describe the management of Mrs. Havana from admission till discharge (13 Marks)
Show Answer

Answer Guide:

a) Diagram of CSF Flow (5 Marks)

(Student should draw the ventricular system and label arrows indicating flow: Lateral Ventricles -> Foramen of Monro -> 3rd Ventricle -> Aqueduct of Sylvius -> 4th Ventricle -> Foramen of Luschka/Magendie -> Subarachnoid Space -> Arachnoid Villi -> Venous Sinuses).

b) Clinical Features (2 Marks) - 0.5 each

  1. Severe Headache.
  2. High Fever.
  3. Nuchal Rigidity (Stiff neck).
  4. Photophobia (Sensitivity to light).
  5. Altered Level of Consciousness (Confusion/Drowsiness).
  6. Positive Kernig's and Brudzinski's signs.

c) Nursing Management (13 Marks)

  • Immediate/Acute Care:
    • Isolation: Place in isolation (droplet precautions) for first 24 hours of antibiotic therapy.
    • Medication: Administer IV antibiotics (e.g., Ceftriaxone, Vancomycin) promptly as prescribed. Administer Corticosteroids (Dexamethasone) to reduce inflammation. Antipyretics for fever.
    • Neurological Monitoring: Assess Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS), pupil reaction, and vital signs frequently (q 1-2h) to detect increased Intracranial Pressure (ICP).
  • Environment: Keep room quiet and dark (photophobia). Minimize stimulation to prevent seizures.
  • Hydration/Nutrition: IV fluids to maintain hydration (careful not to overhydrate if SIADH risk). NG tube feeding if unconscious.
  • Safety: Seizure precautions (padded rails).
  • Comfort: Position head elevated 30 degrees. Analgesics for headache.
  • Rehabilitation/Discharge: Monitor for sequelae (hearing loss). Education on completing antibiotics.
2.
Mr. B a 37 years old farmer is admitted in the ward with a diagnosis of peptic ulcers.
a) State four (4) causes of peptic ulcers. (4 Marks)
b) Explain the pathophysiology of peptic ulcers. (6 Marks)
c) Describe the management of Mr. B till discharge. (12 Marks)
Show Answer

Answer Guide:

a) Causes (4 Marks)

  1. Helicobacter Pylori (H. Pylori) infection (Most common).
  2. NSAIDs usage: Chronic use of Aspirin, Ibuprofen, etc.
  3. Smoking/Alcohol: Risk factors that exacerbate ulcers.
  4. Stress (Physiological): Trauma, burns, major surgery (Curling's/Cushing's ulcers).
  5. Zollinger-Ellison Syndrome: Tumor causing excess acid.

b) Pathophysiology (6 Marks)

Peptic ulcers develop when there is an imbalance between aggressive factors (Gastric acid - HCl, Pepsin) and defensive factors (Mucus, Bicarbonate, Blood flow).
1. Aggressive factors (like H. Pylori or NSAIDs) damage the mucosal barrier.
2. H. Pylori produces urease (ammonia) which damages cells. NSAIDs inhibit prostaglandins (which normally protect the lining).
3. Once the barrier is breached, acid and pepsin digest the underlying tissue (autodigestion).
4. This causes inflammation, erosion, and eventual ulceration of the gastric or duodenal wall.

c) Management till Discharge (12 Marks)

  • Medical Management:
    • H. Pylori Eradication: Triple Therapy (PPI + Clarithromycin + Amoxicillin/Metronidazole) for 7-14 days.
    • Acid Suppression: Proton Pump Inhibitors (Omeprazole) or H2 Blockers (Ranitidine) to allow healing.
    • Cytoprotective agents: Sucralfate or Misoprostol.
  • Dietary Management:
    • Avoid irritants (spicy foods, caffeine, alcohol).
    • Small frequent meals rather than large ones.
    • Avoid eating late at night (increases nocturnal acid).
  • Nursing Care:
    • Pain Management: Assess pain, give meds.
    • Monitoring: Watch for complications like hemorrhage (hematemesis/melena), perforation (rigid abdomen), or obstruction.
    • Education: Stress management, smoking cessation advice (smoking delays healing). Adherence to medication.
