Module I: Fundamentals of Nursing

Section II: Short Answer Questions (20 Marks)

11.
Explain the following types of Nursing diagnosis (6 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Actual Nursing Diagnosis: Describes a human response to a health condition that currently exists in an individual, family, or community. It is supported by defining characteristics (signs and symptoms) that cluster in patterns. Example: Ineffective Airway Clearance.
  2. Risk Nursing Diagnosis: Describes human responses to health conditions that may develop in a vulnerable individual, family, or community. It is supported by risk factors (no signs/symptoms present yet). Example: Risk for Infection.
  3. Health Promotion (Wellness) Nursing Diagnosis: A clinical judgment of a person's, family's, or community's motivation and desire to increase well-being and actualize human health potential. Example: Readiness for Enhanced Nutrition.
  4. Syndrome Nursing Diagnosis: A cluster of actual or risk nursing diagnoses that are predicted to present because of a certain situation or event. Example: Rape-Trauma Syndrome.
12.
All nursing interventions are patient-focused and goal directed. Explain THREE (3) types of nursing interventions. (6 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Independent (Nurse-Initiated) Interventions: Actions that a nurse initiates without a physician's order, based on scientific rationale and nursing knowledge. Example: Patient positioning, health education, providing comfort measures.
  2. Dependent (Physician-Initiated) Interventions: Actions that require an order from a physician or other health care professional but are carried out by the nurse. Example: Administering medication, inserting an IV line, wound dressing (as ordered).
  3. Collaborative (Interdependent) Interventions: Therapies that require the combined knowledge, skill, and expertise of multiple health care professionals. Example: Monitoring a patient with a complex condition alongside physiotherapists, dietitians, and doctors.
13.
Scenario A: A conscious patient has been brought to your ward for admission and you are the admitting nurse.
Scenario B: A patient has suddenly collapsed while queuing outside the injection room.

i. State the source of data for scenario A (1 Mark)

Show Answer

Primary Source (The Patient): Since the patient is conscious, they can provide subjective information directly.

ii. State the type of data for scenario B (1 Mark)

Show Answer

Objective Data: Information that is observed, measured, or witnessed (e.g., the act of collapsing, vital signs taken immediately).

iii. State the type of data for scenario A (1 Mark)

Show Answer

Subjective Data: Information reported by the patient (symptoms, feelings, history).

(Note: Objective data will also be collected via physical exam, but the "conscious patient" distinction often emphasizes their ability to give Subjective data).

iv. State the source of data for scenario B (1 Mark)

Show Answer

Secondary Source (Observation/Witnesses): The patient is unconscious/collapsed, so data comes from observation, bystanders, or medical records, not the patient's verbal report.

14.
Outline FOUR (4) barriers to effective nursing care studies (4 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Inadequate Resources: Lack of equipment, supplies, or staff to implement planned care.
  2. Patient Non-compliance: The patient refusing care or failing to follow instructions.
  3. Communication Gaps: Poor communication between the nurse, patient, family, or other healthcare team members.
  4. Time Constraints: Heavy workload preventing thorough assessment and implementation of care.
  5. Lack of Knowledge/Skills: Incompetence or lack of training in specific nursing procedures.
  6. Cultural/Language Barriers: Differences that hinder understanding and acceptance of care.

Section III: Long Answer Question (20 Marks)

15.
Mr. Rocky 45 years old, a motorcycle rider, has been admitted to surgical ward with diagnosis of severe head injury after having been involved in a Road Traffic Accident.

a) Define the following terms (2 Marks)

Show Answer
  • i. Decerebrate posture: An abnormal body posture that involves the arms and legs being held straight out (extension), the toes being pointed downward, and the head and neck being arched backward. It indicates severe damage to the brain, specifically the brainstem.
  • ii. Decorticate posture: An abnormal body posture that involves the arms being flexed (bent) over the chest, hands clenched into fists, and legs extended. It indicates damage to the cerebral hemispheres, internal capsule, or thalamus.

b) Using Glasgow Coma Scale, describe how you will perform neurological assessment on Mr. Rocky. (12 Marks)

Show Answer

The GCS assesses consciousness based on three parameters. The total score ranges from 3 (Deep Coma) to 15 (Fully Alert).

  1. Eye Opening (E) - Max 4 Points:
    • Spontaneous (4): Observe if Mr. Rocky opens his eyes without stimulation.
    • To Speech (3): If eyes are closed, speak to him loudly. If he opens them, score 3.
    • To Pain (2): If no response to speech, apply pressure (e.g., nail bed, trapezius squeeze). If he opens eyes, score 2.
    • None (1): No eye opening to any stimulus.
  2. Verbal Response (V) - Max 5 Points:
    • Oriented (5): Ask him his name, location, and date. If correct on all, score 5.
    • Confused (4): If he answers but is disoriented (e.g., wrong year), score 4.
    • Inappropriate Words (3): If he speaks random words or swears but makes no sense, score 3.
    • Incomprehensible Sounds (2): If he only moans or groans, score 2.
    • None (1): No verbal sound.
  3. Motor Response (M) - Max 6 Points:
    • Obeys Commands (6): Ask him to lift his arm or squeeze your hand. If he does, score 6.
    • Localizes Pain (5): Apply painful stimulus. If he moves his hand to the site to remove the stimulus, score 5.
    • Withdraws from Pain (4): If he pulls his limb away (flexion) quickly from the pain source, score 4.
    • Abnormal Flexion (3): Decorticate posture (arms flexed to chest) in response to pain.
    • Extension (2): Decerebrate posture (arms extended) in response to pain.
    • None (1): No motor movement (flaccid).

c) State SIX (6) roles of the nurse in caring for the unconscious patient (6 Marks)

Show Answer
  1. Airway Maintenance: Keeping the airway patent (suctioning, positioning in lateral position) to prevent aspiration and ensure oxygenation.
  2. Monitoring Vital Signs: Frequent monitoring of BP, Pulse, Temp, and Respiration (especially checking for Cushing's Triad).
  3. Neurological Assessment: Frequent GCS monitoring and checking pupil reaction to detect deterioration (increased ICP).
  4. Skin Care/Positioning: Turning the patient every 2 hours to prevent pressure ulcers (bedsores).
  5. Nutrition and Hydration: Managing IV fluids or Nasogastric tube feeding to maintain metabolic needs.
  6. Safety: Putting up bed rails to prevent falls and protecting the patient from injury (e.g., during seizures).
  7. Hygiene: Performing mouth care, eye care (lubricating drops), and bed baths.
  8. Elimination: Managing catheter care and bowel movements.

Module II: Pulmonary Nursing

Section II: Short Answer Questions (20 Marks)

1.
Define the following respiratory disorders /terms (2 Marks)

a) Emphysema

Show Answer

Emphysema is a chronic respiratory condition characterized by the destruction of alveolar walls, leading to enlarged air spaces, loss of lung elasticity, and air trapping.

b) Hemothorax

Show Answer

Hemothorax is the accumulation of blood in the pleural cavity (the space between the chest wall and the lung), often caused by trauma or surgery.

2.
With a well labeled diagram draw the lower respiratory system (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Key Components to Draw and Label:

  • Trachea: The windpipe extending from the larynx.
  • Primary Bronchi: Right and Left main bronchi branching from the trachea.
  • Bronchioles: Smaller branches within the lungs.
  • Alveoli: Air sacs at the end of bronchioles (can be shown in a zoomed-in section).
  • Lungs: Right (3 lobes) and Left (2 lobes).
  • Diaphragm: Muscle sheet below the lungs.

(Diagram should show the tree-like structure branching down into the lungs).

3.
State five factors associated with increase in asthma attacks (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Allergens: Pollen, dust mites, pet dander, mold spores.
  2. Respiratory Infections: Viral colds, flu, pneumonia.
  3. Physical Activity: Exercise-induced asthma (especially in cold/dry air).
  4. Environmental Pollutants: Smoke, chemical fumes, strong odors/perfumes.
  5. Weather Changes: Cold air or extreme humidity.
  6. Emotions: Stress, anxiety, excessive laughing or crying.
  7. Medications: Aspirin, NSAIDs, Beta-blockers.
4.
State five types of Chest injuries (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Rib Fractures: Breakage of one or more ribs (most common).
  2. Flail Chest: Multiple consecutive rib fractures in two or more places, creating a floating segment.
  3. Pneumothorax: Air in the pleural space (can be Open, Closed, or Tension).
  4. Hemothorax: Blood in the pleural space.
  5. Pulmonary Contusion: Bruising of the lung tissue.
  6. Cardiac Tamponade: Blood in the pericardial sac compressing the heart.
5.
State three (3) teachings to a patient diagnosed with bronchiectasis (3 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Chest Physiotherapy: Teach postural drainage and percussion techniques to help clear secretions daily.
  2. Infection Prevention: Importance of annual flu vaccines and pneumonia vaccines; avoiding crowds during flu season.
  3. Hydration: Drink plenty of fluids (2-3 liters/day) to thin mucus for easier expectoration.
  4. Medication Adherence: Complete full courses of antibiotics and use bronchodilators/mucolytics as prescribed.
  5. Smoking Cessation: Avoid smoking and exposure to second-hand smoke.

Section III: Long Answer Question (20 Marks)

6.
Mr. John aged 40 years, following a road traffic accident sustained blunt chest injuries of the chest and got admitted in a Surgical ward where he has been diagnosed with Haemothorax and underwater seal drainage has been set up.

a) List four (4) clinical manifestations that Mr. John would present with. (2 marks)

Show Answer
  1. Dyspnea: Difficulty breathing or shortness of breath.
  2. Chest Pain: Pleuritic pain worsened by breathing.
  3. Tachycardia: Rapid heart rate (due to shock/hypoxia).
  4. Dullness on Percussion: Over the affected area (fluid).
  5. Decreased Breath Sounds: On the affected side.
  6. Hypotension: If significant blood loss occurred.

b) Explain the Nursing responsibilities on a patient on Underwater seal drainage (6 marks)

Show Answer
  1. Maintain System Integrity: Ensure all connections are taped securely to prevent air leaks. Ensure the tube is not kinked or compressed.
  2. Positioning: Keep the drainage bottle/unit below the level of the patient's chest at all times to prevent backflow of fluid into the lungs. Keep the unit upright.
  3. Monitoring Drainage: Mark the level of drainage on the bottle at the start and end of shifts. Monitor color, amount, and consistency. Report sudden increase (>100ml/hr) or stoppage.
  4. Monitoring Function: Check for tidaling (fluid fluctuation with breathing) in the water seal chamber. Check for bubbling (intermittent is normal if air is draining; continuous may indicate a leak).
  5. Safety Precautions: Keep two rubber-shod clamps at the bedside (for emergency clamping if the bottle breaks, though routine clamping is discouraged). Keep sterile water/saline available.
  6. Patient Care: Encourage deep breathing and coughing to aid lung re-expansion. Assist with position changes.

c) State two complications of chest injury (2 marks)

Show Answer
  1. Tension Pneumothorax: Life-threatening accumulation of air under pressure.
  2. Empyema: Infection of the pleural collection (pus).
  3. Atelectasis/Pneumonia: Due to shallow breathing from pain.
  4. Hypovolemic Shock: From internal bleeding.

d) Describe the Surgical and Nursing management of Mr. John during the first 72 hours of admission (10 marks)

Show Answer

Surgical Management:

  • Thoracostomy (Chest Tube Insertion): Insertion of a large-bore chest tube (usually 32-36 Fr) in the 5th or 6th intercostal space mid-axillary line to drain blood and re-expand the lung.
  • Thoracotomy: Emergency surgery indicated if initial drainage >1500ml or continuous bleeding >200ml/hr for 2-4 hours.

Nursing Management:

  • Respiratory Support: Administer oxygen to maintain SpO2 >94%. Position in Semi-Fowler’s or High-Fowler’s to facilitate breathing and drainage.
  • Pain Management: Administer analgesics (e.g., IV Morphine or PCA) regularly to allow for deep breathing and coughing exercises (prevent pneumonia). Splint chest with pillow when coughing.
  • Hemodynamic Monitoring: Monitor BP and Pulse frequently for signs of shock. Monitor fluid intake and output. Administer IV fluids or blood products as ordered to replace volume.
  • Wound Care: Monitor the insertion site for signs of infection (redness, purulent discharge). Ensure dressing is occlusive (airtight).
  • Activity: Encourage early mobilization and arm exercises on the affected side to prevent frozen shoulder.
  • Assessment: Auscultate lung sounds regularly to check for re-expansion. Monitor daily Chest X-rays.
