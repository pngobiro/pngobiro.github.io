Module I: Fundamentals of Nursing II

Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions (15 Marks)

1.
The primary goal of critical care nursing;
A. Administering medication
B. Preventing infections
C. Stabilizing patients
D. Monitoring vital signs
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Stabilizing patients

Explanation: While all options are tasks in critical care, the overarching primary goal is to stabilize the patient's physiological status and prevent further deterioration of life-threatening conditions.

2.
The following types of patients may require critical care nursing;
A. Stable patients with minor injuries
B. Patients undergoing routine surgeries
C. Patients with life-threatening conditions
D. Healthy individuals
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Patients with life-threatening conditions

Explanation: Critical care is specifically designed for patients with reversible, life-threatening conditions requiring intensive monitoring and support.

3.
The purpose of a ventilator in critical care nursing is;
A. To monitor blood pressure
B. To administer medications
C. To assist with breathing
D. To check oxygen levels
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. To assist with breathing

Explanation: Ventilators provide mechanical respiratory support for patients unable to breathe adequately on their own.

4.
Recommended nurse-to-patient ratio in a critical care unit;
A. 1:1
B. 1:4
C. 1:10
D. 1:20
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. 1:1

Explanation: Critical care requires intensive monitoring. The ideal standard ratio for unstable/critically ill patients is 1:1 (or 1:2 for stable ICU patients), ensuring constant observation.

5.
The correct sequence of performing physical examination on the abdomen is;
A. Inspection, percussion, palpation and auscultation
B. Inspection, palpation, percussion and auscultation
C. Inspection, auscultation, percussion and palpation
D. Auscultation, inspection, percussion and palpation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Inspection, auscultation, percussion and palpation

Explanation: For the abdomen, Auscultation comes second (after Inspection) because percussion and palpation can alter bowel sounds. (Standard order for other systems is Inspection, Palpation, Percussion, Auscultation).

6.
When prioritizing needs during the assessment phase, the nurse should focus on:
A. Convenience for the healthcare team.
B. The patient's chief complaint.
C. Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs
D. Financial implications of interventions.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs

Explanation: Maslow's Hierarchy (Physiological needs first) is the standard framework for prioritizing nursing care (ABC: Airway, Breathing, Circulation).

7.
The difference between a nursing diagnosis and a medical diagnosis;
A. A nursing diagnosis focuses on the patient's response to a health problem, while a medical diagnosis identifies the disease or condition.
B. Both diagnoses are the same and used interchangeably.
C. A nursing diagnosis is more specific than a medical diagnosis.
D. There is no difference between them.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. A nursing diagnosis focuses on the patient's response to a health problem, while a medical diagnosis identifies the disease or condition.

Explanation: This is the fundamental definition distinguishing the two professions' focus.

8.
The following is an example of a nursing diagnosis;
A. Pneumonia
B. Risk of impaired skin integrity
C. Diabetes mellitus
D. Complaint of pain
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Risk of impaired skin integrity

Explanation: Options A and C are medical diagnoses. Option D is a symptom/data. Option B follows the NANDA format for a nursing diagnosis.

9.
The SMART goal format used for setting nursing interventions?
A. Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound
B. Simple, Measurable, Achievable, Reflective, Time-limited
C. Specific, Measurable, Actionable, Realistic, Tangible
D. Sustainable, Measurable, Achievable, Realistic, Time-bound
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound

Explanation: This is the standard definition of SMART goals in nursing care planning.

10.
Before administering a new medication, the nurse should:
A. Delegate the task to a nursing assistant.
B. Check the medication against the MAR (medication administration record)
C. Assume the patient has received all necessary education about the medication.
D. Avoid verbal consent from the patient.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Check the medication against the MAR (medication administration record)

Explanation: The "Three Checks" and "Rights of Medication Administration" require verification against the MAR to prevent errors.

11.
When implementing discharge teaching, the nurse should:
A. Use complex medical terminology.
B. Focus on the patient's individual learning needs
C. Rely solely on written materials.
D. Avoid asking questions to assess understanding.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Focus on the patient's individual learning needs

Explanation: Effective teaching must be patient-centered, tailored to their specific needs, literacy level, and learning style.

12.
The priority indicator of successful nursing intervention is:
A. Completion of all planned tasks.
B. Improvement in the patient's outcome
C. Documentation of interventions in the chart.
D. Patient satisfaction with the care provided.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Improvement in the patient's outcome

Explanation: The ultimate goal of the nursing process is positive patient outcomes (health improvement). Task completion and documentation are means to that end.

13.
The nurse in charge identifies a patient's responses to actual or potential health problems during which step of the nursing process:-
A. Assessing
B. Diagnosing
C. Planning
D. Evaluating
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Diagnosing

Explanation: NANDA defines nursing diagnosis as a clinical judgment about individual, family, or community experiences/responses to actual or potential health problems.

14.
A female patient is diagnosed with deep-vein thrombosis. Which nursing diagnosis should receive the highest priority at this time:-
A. Impaired gas exchange related to increased blood flow
B. Fluid volume excess related to peripheral vascular disease
C. Risk for injury related to edema
D. Altered peripheral tissue perfusion related to venous congestion
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Altered peripheral tissue perfusion related to venous congestion

Explanation: The immediate problem in DVT is the obstruction of venous return causing congestion and altered perfusion in the limb. (Note: "Risk for Impaired Gas Exchange" due to PE would be high priority if listed, but among these, D addresses the direct DVT pathophysiology).

15.
A nurse is revising a client's care plan. During which step of the nursing process does such a revision take place: -
A. Assessment
B. Planning
C. Implementation
D. Evaluation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Evaluation

Explanation: Evaluation determines if goals were met. If not, the care plan is revised (re-assessment, re-diagnosis, re-planning) at this stage.

Section 2: Short Answer Questions (25 Marks)

16.
Explain three types of nursing diagnosis. (6 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Actual Diagnosis: Describes a problem that currently exists (e.g., Acute Pain). Validated by presence of major defining characteristics.
  2. Risk Diagnosis: Describes a problem that may develop in a vulnerable patient (e.g., Risk for Infection). No defining characteristics (signs/symptoms) are present yet, only risk factors.
  3. Health Promotion (Wellness) Diagnosis: Describes a patient's readiness to transition to a higher level of wellness (e.g., Readiness for Enhanced Nutrition).
  4. (Note: Syndrome diagnosis is a 4th type, clustering signs/symptoms).
17.
State five components of history taking. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Biodata/Demographic Data: Name, age, sex, occupation.
  2. Chief Complaint (Presenting Complaint): Main reason for visit.
  3. History of Present Illness (HPI): Details of the complaint (onset, duration, severity).
  4. Past Medical/Surgical History: Previous illnesses, surgeries, allergies.
  5. Family History: Hereditary diseases.
  6. Social/Lifestyle History: Smoking, alcohol, living conditions.
  7. Review of Systems (ROS).
18.
State five infection prevention measures used in critical care unit. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Hand Hygiene: Strict washing/sanitizing before/after patient contact (Five Moments).
  2. Use of PPE: Gloves, gowns, masks as indicated.
  3. Aseptic Technique: For all invasive procedures (IV lines, catheters, suctioning).
  4. Environmental Cleaning: Frequent disinfection of surfaces and equipment.
  5. Isolation Precautions: Cohorting or isolating patients with multidrug-resistant organisms.
  6. VAP Bundles: Oral care, head of bed elevation to prevent ventilator-associated pneumonia.
19.
State the AVPU scale. (4 marks)
Show Answer

The AVPU scale is a quick method to assess Level of Consciousness:

  • A - Alert: The patient is awake and responsive.
  • V - Verbal: The patient responds to verbal stimuli (voice).
  • P - Pain: The patient responds only to painful stimuli.
  • U - Unresponsive: The patient does not respond to any stimulus.
20.
Outline five measures can be taken to prevent complications associated with immobility in critically ill patients. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Repositioning: Turn patient every 2 hours to prevent pressure ulcers.
  2. Skin Care: Keep skin clean and dry; use pressure-relieving mattresses.
  3. Range of Motion (ROM) Exercises: Passive or active exercises to prevent contractures and muscle atrophy.
  4. DVT Prophylaxis: Use anti-embolism stockings (TEDs), sequential compression devices (SCDs), or anticoagulants (Heparin).
  5. Chest Physiotherapy/Suctioning: To prevent pneumonia and atelectasis.
  6. Hydration/Nutrition: Adequate intake to maintain skin integrity and prevent constipation.

Section 3: Long Answer Question (20 Marks)

21.
Mr. Smith, a 50-year-old male, is admitted to the intensive care unit (ICU) following a severe traumatic brain injury resulting from a motor vehicle accident. Upon admission, he is unresponsive and intubated. As a nurse in charge of Mr. Smith's care, explain the Scoring and Parameters of Glasgow Coma Scale. (20 marks)
Show Answer

The Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) assesses level of consciousness based on three parameters: Eye Opening, Verbal Response, and Motor Response. Total Score ranges from 3 (Deep Coma) to 15 (Fully Alert).

1. Eye Opening (E) - Max 4 Marks

  • 4: Spontaneous (Opens eyes on own).
  • 3: To Sound/Speech (Opens eyes when asked).
  • 2: To Pressure/Pain (Opens eyes to physical stimulus).
  • 1: None (No eye opening).

2. Verbal Response (V) - Max 5 Marks

  • 5: Oriented (Knows time, place, person).
  • 4: Confused (Converses but disoriented).
  • 3: Inappropriate Words (Random words, no conversation).
  • 2: Incomprehensible Sounds (Moaning/Groaning).
  • 1: None (No verbal response).
  • (Note: If intubated, record as 1T or VT).

3. Motor Response (M) - Max 6 Marks

  • 6: Obeys Commands (Follows instructions e.g., lift arm).
  • 5: Localizes Pain (Moves hand towards pain stimulus to remove it).
  • 4: Normal Flexion/Withdrawal (Pulls away quickly from pain).
  • 3: Abnormal Flexion (Decorticate posturing - arms flexed over chest).
  • 2: Extension (Decerebrate posturing - arms extended at sides).
  • 1: None (Flaccid, no movement).

Interpretation:

  • GCS 13-15: Mild Brain Injury.
  • GCS 9-12: Moderate Brain Injury.
  • GCS â‰¤8: Severe Brain Injury (Coma) - Usually requires intubation ("Less than 8, intubate").

Module II: Pulmonary Nursing

Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions (15 Marks)

1.
A nurse is caring for a patient diagnosed with pneumonia, the following pathophysiological mechanisms occur in the lung parenchyma leads to development of pneumonia:
A. Atelectasis
B. Inflammation
C. Bronchiectasis
D. Effusion
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Inflammation

Explanation: Pneumonia is an acute inflammation of the lung parenchyma caused by microbial agents. The inflammatory response leads to alveolar edema and consolidation (filling with exudate).

2.
After undergoing a left pneumectomy a female patient has a chest tube in place for drainage. The following action will be carries out by the nurse to manage the apparatus:
A. Monitor fluctuations in the water seal chamber
B. Encourage coughing and breathing deep
C. Turn the patients position every two hours
D. Monitor vital signs 4 hourly
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Monitor fluctuations in the water seal chamber

Explanation: For a chest tube, monitoring the water seal (tidaling/fluctuation with breathing) confirms patency. Note: After pneumonectomy (removal of whole lung), chest tubes are often clamped or not used to allow fluid to fill the empty space and prevent mediastinal shift, but if a tube is present per the question, monitoring the system (fluctuation) is the specific device management action.

3.
The first nursing action to be taken on a male patient that has a sucking stab wound to the chest is;
A. Drawing blood for hematocrit and hemoglobin level
B. Applying addressing over the wound and tapping it on three sides
C. Preparing a chest tube insertion tray
D. Through preparing to start an IV line
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Applying addressing over the wound and tapping it on three sides

Explanation: A sucking chest wound (Open Pneumothorax) is a life-threatening emergency. Immediate sealing with an occlusive dressing taped on three sides creates a one-way valve: air can escape during exhalation but cannot enter during inhalation, preventing tension pneumothorax.

4.
Type of pneumothorax caused by a blunt injury resulting in fractured ribs piercing the plural membranes is
A. Closed Heamothorax
B. Tension pneumothorax
C. Open pneumothorax
D. Closed Pneumothorax
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Closed Pneumothorax

Explanation: A fractured rib puncturing the lung internally without an external chest wall opening is a Closed pneumothorax. Open pneumothorax involves an external wound. Tension pneumothorax is a physiological progression (pressure build-up), not just the mechanism of injury.

5.
State whether the following statements are true or false

i. Tonsils help prevent bacteria and other pathogens from entering body.

Show Answer

Answer: True (Tonsils are lymphoid tissue acting as a defense mechanism).

ii. Bronchiectasis is characterized by abnormal dilation of the bronchus or bronchi.

Show Answer

Answer: True (This is the definition: permanent dilation and destruction of bronchial walls).

7.
State five symptoms of bronchiectasis. (5mks)
Show Answer
  1. Chronic Cough: Productive of copious amounts of purulent sputum (often foul-smelling).
  2. Hemoptysis: Coughing up blood.
  3. Dyspnea: Shortness of breath.
  4. Clubbing of fingers: Due to chronic hypoxia.
  5. Recurrent Respiratory Infections: Frequent pneumonia.
  6. Chest Pain: Pleuritic pain.
  7. Fatigue/Weight Loss.
8.
State five factors that can trigger an asthmatic attack. (5mks)
Show Answer
  1. Allergens: Pollen, dust mites, pet dander, mold.
  2. Respiratory Infections: Viral colds, flu.
  3. Physical Activity: Exercise-induced asthma.
  4. Cold Air/Weather Changes.
  5. Air Pollutants/Irritants: Smoke, strong odors, chemicals.
  6. Medications: Aspirin, Beta-blockers.
  7. Strong Emotions/Stress.

Section 3: Long Answer Question (15 Marks)

9.
Mr. T 58 years old is admitted in a medical ward having excess pleural fluid. Identify five clinical features that Mr. T will present with and the nursing and medical management from admission till discharge. (20 marks - though section header says 15 Marks, Question says 20)
Show Answer

Diagnosis: Pleural Effusion.

A. Clinical Features (5 Marks):

  1. Dyspnea: Shortness of breath (worse on exertion or lying flat).
  2. Pleuritic Chest Pain: Sharp pain worse on inspiration.
  3. Dry, Non-productive Cough.
  4. Decreased Breath Sounds: On the affected side.
  5. Dullness to Percussion: Over the fluid area.
  6. Decreased Tactile Fremitus.

B. Medical Management:

  • Thoracentesis: Aspiration of fluid for diagnosis (analysis) and therapeutic relief of dyspnea.
  • Treat Underlying Cause: Antibiotics for pneumonia (parapneumonic effusion), Diuretics for Heart Failure, Chemotherapy for malignancy.
  • Chest Tube Drainage: Inserted if effusion is recurrent or purulent (empyema) to drain fluid continuously.
  • Pleurodesis: Chemical sclerosis of the pleural space to prevent recurrence (for malignant effusions).

C. Nursing Management:

  • Respiratory Support: Position in High Fowler's to maximize lung expansion. Administer Oxygen if hypoxic. Encourage deep breathing and coughing.
  • Assist with Thoracentesis: Position patient (sitting on edge of bed leaning over table). Monitor vitals during procedure. Monitor for complications (pneumothorax, bleeding) post-procedure.
  • Pain Management: Administer analgesics as prescribed, especially before deep breathing exercises. Splint chest when coughing.
  • Activity Tolerance: Assist with ADLs to conserve energy. Plan rest periods.
  • Nutrition: High protein diet to replace protein lost in pleural fluid.
  • Education: Teach signs of recurrence (worsening breathlessness) and medication adherence.
