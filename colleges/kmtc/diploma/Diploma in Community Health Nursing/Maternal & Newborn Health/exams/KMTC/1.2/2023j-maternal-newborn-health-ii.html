Section II: Short Answer Questions (40 Marks)

1.
Outline five (5) preventive measures of iron deficiency in pregnancy. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Routine Iron Supplementation (IFAS): Administration of daily oral iron and folic acid supplements throughout pregnancy.
  2. Dietary Counseling: Encouraging consumption of iron-rich foods (e.g., red meat, green leafy vegetables, beans) and Vitamin C to enhance absorption.
  3. Malaria Control: Use of Insecticide Treated Nets (ITNs) and Intermittent Presumptive Treatment (IPTp) to prevent hemolysis.
  4. Deworming: Administration of anti-helminthic drugs (e.g., Mebendazole/Albendazole) in the second trimester to treat hookworm infestation.
  5. Birth Spacing: Encouraging family planning to allow replenishment of maternal iron stores between pregnancies (at least 2 years).
2.
Outline five (5) effects of Malaria in pregnancy to the mother. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Severe Anaemia: Due to destruction of red blood cells by the parasite.
  2. Hypoglycemia: As a complication of severe malaria or quinine therapy.
  3. Cerebral Malaria: Leading to convulsions and coma.
  4. Pulmonary Edema: Fluid accumulation in the lungs causing respiratory distress.
  5. Puerperal Sepsis: Increased susceptibility to infections post-delivery due to immunosuppression and anaemia.
  6. Maternal Death: In severe, complicated cases.
3.
Explain four major causes of post-partum haemorrhage. (8 marks)
Show Answer

Mnemonic: The 4 Ts

  1. Tone (Uterine Atony): The uterus fails to contract after delivery, leaving blood vessels at the placental site open. This is the most common cause (approx. 70%).
  2. Trauma: Lacerations or tears of the cervix, vagina, or perineum, or uterine rupture during delivery, leading to bleeding despite a contracted uterus.
  3. Tissue (Retained Tissue): Retention of placenta, placental fragments, or membranes prevents the uterus from contracting effectively, causing bleeding.
  4. Thrombin (Coagulopathy): Pre-existing or acquired bleeding disorders (e.g., DIC, Hemophilia) that prevent blood clotting.
4.
State five (5) steps of maternal perinatal death surveillance response (MPDSR). (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Identification and Notification: Detecting and reporting maternal and perinatal deaths within 24 hours.
  2. Review (Audit): Investigating the causes and circumstances (medical and non-medical) surrounding the death.
  3. Analysis: Aggregating data to identify trends, avoidable factors, and systemic gaps.
  4. Response (Action): Implementing recommendations and corrective actions to prevent future deaths.
  5. Monitoring and Evaluation: Assessing the implementation of recommendations and their impact on quality of care.
5.
State five (5) signs and symptoms of intrauterine foetal death. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Cessation of Fetal Movements: The mother reports the baby has stopped moving.
  2. Absence of Fetal Heart Tones: No heart beat detected on auscultation or Doppler.
  3. Regression of Pregnancy Signs: Breast engorgement subsides, and maternal weight may decrease.
  4. Uterine Size Discrepancy: Fundal height is smaller than dates or stops increasing.
  5. Brownish Vaginal Discharge: Sometimes present (old blood).
  6. Cold Abdomen: In some cases, the mother may report the abdomen feels cold or heavy.
6.
State five (5) predisposing factors to puerperal sepsis. (6 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Prolonged Labour: Increased exposure to ascending infections.
  2. Premature Rupture of Membranes (PROM): Loss of the protective amniotic barrier for >18-24 hours.
  3. Frequent Vaginal Examinations: Introducing bacteria into the genital tract (more than 5 exams is a risk factor).
  4. Anaemia/Malnutrition: Lowers maternal immunity and resistance to infection.
  5. Caesarean Section/Instrumental Delivery: Invasive procedures increase trauma and infection risk.
  6. Retained Products of Conception: Necrotic tissue provides a medium for bacterial growth.
7.
Outline five (5) causes of uterine rupture. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Previous Uterine Scar: From Caesarean section or myomectomy (scar dehiscence).
  2. Obstructed Labour: Neglected obstruction leads to thinning and rupture of the lower uterine segment.
  3. Injudicious Use of Oxytocics: Hyperstimulation of the uterus with Oxytocin or Misoprostol.
  4. High Parity (Grand Multiparity): Weakened uterine musculature from repeated pregnancies.
  5. Traumatic Maneuvers: Difficult forceps delivery, breech extraction, or internal podalic version.

Section III: Long Answer Questions (40 Marks)

1.
Baby Tina is admitted in the newborn unit with diagnosis of severe neonatal jaundice on phototherapy.

A. Define neonatal jaundice (1 mark)

Show Answer

Neonatal jaundice is the yellow discoloration of the skin, sclera, and mucous membranes in a newborn due to elevated levels of bilirubin in the blood (hyperbilirubinemia).

B. State four differences between pathological and physiological jaundice (4 marks)

Show Answer
Feature Physiological Jaundice Pathological Jaundice
Onset Appears after 24 hours (Day 2-3). Appears within the first 24 hours.
Severity Usually mild, bilirubin <15mg/dl. Severe, rapidly rising bilirubin (>5mg/dl/day).
Duration Resolves by Day 7-10 (term). Persists >14 days (term) or >21 days (preterm).
Treatment Self-limiting, no treatment needed. Requires phototherapy or exchange transfusion.

C. State three (3) side effects of phototherapy (3 marks)

Show Answer
  1. Dehydration: Due to increased insensible water loss.
  2. Hyperthermia: Overheating from the lights.
  3. Loose stools (Diarrhea): Greenish, watery stools due to increased bile excretion.
  4. Skin rash: Erythematous rash (phototherapy rash).
  5. Bronze Baby Syndrome: If conjugated bilirubin is high.

(Any 3)

D. Discuss the management of baby Tina until discharge (14 marks)

Show Answer

1. Phototherapy Care:

  • Exposure: Keep baby naked except for a diaper to expose maximum skin surface area.
  • Eye Protection: Cover eyes with opaque eye patches to prevent retinal damage.
  • Genital Protection: Cover gonads (with diaper) to prevent potential damage.
  • Distance: Ensure lights are at the correct distance (45cm) for effectiveness.
  • Positioning: Turn the baby every 2-3 hours to expose different areas of skin.

2. Hydration and Feeding:

  • Encourage frequent breastfeeding (every 2-3 hours) to prevent dehydration and promote excretion of bilirubin in stool.
  • Administer IVF if indicated/prescribed to correct dehydration.
  • Do not interrupt breastfeeding unless medically indicated.

3. Monitoring:

  • Temperature: Check temp every 3 hours to prevent hypothermia (if naked) or hyperthermia (from lights).
  • Hydration: Monitor urine output, skin turgor, and fontanelles.
  • Bilirubin Levels: Monitor serum bilirubin daily or as ordered to assess response.
  • Weight: Daily weighing.

4. Skin Care:

  • Do not apply oils or lotions (can cause burns under lights).
  • Keep the skin clean and dry.

5. Infection Prevention:

  • Maintain strict aseptic technique and hand hygiene.

6. Parent Education & Discharge:

  • Explain the condition and treatment to the mother to reduce anxiety.
  • Discharge when bilirubin levels are safe and stable off phototherapy.
  • Advise on follow-up and signs of recurrence (yellowing of eyes/skin).
2.
A 35 years old multipara is admitted with cord prolapse at 40 weeks' gestation.

a) Define cord prolapse (1 mark)

Show Answer

Cord prolapse is the descent of the umbilical cord through the cervix alongside (occult) or past (overt) the presenting part in the presence of ruptured membranes.

b) Outline five Predisposing factors to cord prolapse (5 marks)

Show Answer
  1. Malpresentation: Breech (especially footling) or Transverse lie where the pelvis is not filled.
  2. Prematurity: Small fetus allows space for the cord to slip through.
  3. Polyhydramnios: Sudden gush of fluid can wash the cord down.
  4. High Presenting Part: Head not engaged when membranes rupture.
  5. Multiple Gestation: Especially for the second twin.
  6. Long Umbilical Cord: Increased risk of prolapse.

c) Discuss the specific the management until discharge (14 marks)

Show Answer

1. Immediate Emergency Action (Relieve Cord Compression):

  • Call for Help: Shout for assistance; do not leave the patient.
  • Manual Elevation: Insert a gloved hand into the vagina and gently push the presenting part UP and AWAY from the cord. Maintain this position until the baby is delivered by C-section. Do not handle the cord to prevent vasospasm.
  • Positioning: Place mother in Knee-Chest position or Trendelenburg position (hips elevated) to use gravity to retract the fetus.
  • Bladder Filling: If delay is anticipated, fill the bladder with 500-700ml normal saline via catheter to elevate the presenting part.

2. Resuscitation & Preparation:

  • Oxygen: Administer 100% oxygen to the mother to improve fetal saturation.
  • Tocolysis: Give a tocolytic (e.g., Terbutaline) to stop contractions and reduce pressure on the cord.
  • IV Access: Start IV fluids and take blood for grouping and cross-match.

3. Definitive Management (Delivery):

  • Emergency Caesarean Section: This is the management of choice if the fetus is alive. Transport to theatre immediately while maintaining manual elevation.
  • Vaginal Delivery: Only considered if the cervix is fully dilated (10cm) and delivery is imminent/can be expedited by forceps/vacuum, OR if the fetus is already dead (confirmed IUD).

4. Postpartum Care:

  • Monitor mother for PPH and infection.
  • Assess newborn (APGAR scores) and admit to NBU if asphyxiated.
  • Provide psychological support and debriefing to the mother.
