Part I: Multiple Choice Questions (20 Marks)

1.
In infertility the term "pregnancy wastage" is used when:
A) A woman is able to conceive but unable to produce a live birth.
B) A woman is virtually not able to conceive.
C) A woman has previously conceived but is later unable.
D) A woman who are not ovulating and at risk of conception.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) A woman is able to conceive but unable to produce a live birth.

Explanation: Pregnancy wastage refers to the failure of a pregnancy to result in a live birth, encompassing miscarriage (spontaneous abortion), stillbirth, and sometimes neonatal death. The woman can conceive, but the pregnancy is "wasted" or lost.

2.
The main causes of maternal mortality include:
A) Haemorrhage, Malaria, Anaemia
B) Eclampsia, Haemorrhage, Anaemia
C) Haemorrhage, Sepsis, Eclampsia
D) Malaria, Eclampsia, Sepsis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Haemorrhage, Sepsis, Eclampsia

Explanation: The classic "Big 5" direct causes of maternal mortality are Haemorrhage (usually the leading cause), Sepsis (Infection), Hypertensive disorders (Eclampsia), Obstructed Labour, and Unsafe Abortion. Option C lists three of the top direct causes.

3.
The recommended practice in active management of third stage of labour include:
A) Intravenous fluids therapy, uterine massage, use of oxytocin.
B) Prophylactic use of oxytocin, uterine massage, controlled cord traction.
C) Episiotomy repair, uterine massage, intravenous fluids therapy.
D) Controlled cord traction, episiotomy repair, prophylactic use of oxytocin.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Prophylactic use of oxytocin, uterine massage, controlled cord traction.

Explanation: AMTSL (Active Management of Third Stage of Labour) consists of three key components to prevent PPH: 1. Administration of a uterotonic (Oxytocin) within 1 minute of birth; 2. Controlled Cord Traction (CCT) with counter-traction; 3. Uterine massage (after placental delivery to ensure contraction). *Note: Guidelines vary on timing of massage, but option B captures the core interventions best.*

4.
Maternal related causes of asphyxia neonatorum are:
A) Administration of narcotics and chronic conditions
B) Cord prolapse and pregnancy induced hypertension
C) Congenital abnormalities and obstruction of the airway.
D) Intrapartum hemorrhage and prematurity.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) Intrapartum hemorrhage and prematurity.

Explanation: Intrapartum hemorrhage (like Placenta Previa or Abruption) is a direct maternal/placental cause compromising blood flow. While narcotics (A) are maternal interventions, hemorrhage represents a severe physiological maternal event causing asphyxia. *Note: Cord prolapse (B) is mechanical/fetal; Congenital abnormalities (C) are fetal.*

5.
Vaginitis is caused by:
A) Candida albicans, Neisseria gonorrhea
B) Candida albicans, chlamydia trachomatis
C) Candida albicans, trichomonas vaginalis
D) Neisseria gonorrhea, chlamydia trichomatis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Candida albicans, trichomonas vaginalis

Explanation: Vaginitis involves inflammation of the vagina. The most common causes are Candida albicans (Yeast), Trichomonas vaginalis (Protozoa), and Gardnerella vaginalis (Bacterial Vaginosis). Neisseria and Chlamydia typically cause cervicitis (inflammation of the cervix) rather than vaginitis.

6.
A client with burning sensation, foamy greenish yellow discharge and itchiness in vagina is diagnosed to have:
A) Trichomoniasis
B) Candidiasis.
C) Gonorrhea
D) Bacteria vaginitis.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Trichomoniasis

Explanation: The classic presentation of Trichomoniasis includes a profuse, frothy (foamy), greenish-yellow, foul-smelling discharge associated with vulvar itching and "strawberry cervix". Candidiasis presents with thick, white, curdy discharge.

7.
The main cause of physiological oedema during pregnancy includes:
A) Oestrogen, increased plasma, proteins, reduced cardiac output.
B) Pre-eclampsia, renal disease, deep venous thrombosis, cardiac disease.
C) Weight gain, amniotic fluid, poor venous return.
D) Oestrogen, reduced plasma proteins, poor venous return.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) Oestrogen, reduced plasma proteins, poor venous return.

Explanation: Physiological edema results from: 1) Estrogen causing sodium and water retention; 2) Hemodilution leading to reduced colloid osmotic pressure (reduced plasma proteins relative to volume); 3) Mechanical pressure of the gravid uterus on pelvic veins causing poor venous return.

8.
Hyaline membrane disease in newborn babies is also known as:
A) Respiratory distress syndrome.
B) Asphyxia neonatorum.
C) Hypothermia neonatorum.
D) Ophthalmic neonatorum.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Respiratory distress syndrome.

Explanation: Hyaline Membrane Disease (HMD) is the older term for Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS) of the newborn, caused by surfactant deficiency in preterm infants.

9.
Normal growth and development of a healthy preterm requires:
A) High hygienic measures, nutrition and adequate warmth.
B) High hygienic measures, nutrition and adequate warmth. (Duplicate)
C) High hygienic measures, nutrition and adequate nutrition.
D) Nutrition, resuscitation, adequate warmth
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) High hygienic measures, nutrition and adequate warmth.

Explanation: The three pillars of preterm care are maintaining body temperature (Warmth), ensuring adequate Nutrition/feeding, and preventing infection (Hygiene). *Note: Option B is identical in source text; assuming it matches A.*

10.
The factors that initiate respiration in a new born at birth include:
A) Reduced oxygen concentration in the blood, high hemoglobin level
B) External stimuli, presence of surfactant in the lungs.
C) Compression of the chest wall, hypoxia, external stimuli.
D) Hypoxia, compression of the chest wall, heat regulation.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Compression of the chest wall, hypoxia, external stimuli.

Explanation: Respiration is triggered by: 1) Chemical factors (Hypoxia/hypercapnia from cord clamping); 2) Mechanical factors (Chest compression/recoil during birth); 3) Sensory/External stimuli (Cold, touch, light, noise). Surfactant (B) facilitates lung expansion but does not initiate the breath.

11.
To prevent infection in newborn baby, cord care should be done:
A) 6hourly
B) 4hourly
C) 2hourly
D) 1hourly
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) 4hourly

Explanation: In clinical settings (like KMTC exams), frequency of cord care is often taught as 4-hourly or with every diaper change to ensure dryness and prevent sepsis. Current WHO guidelines often recommend dry cord care or daily chlorhexidine, but in the context of this exam's options, frequent care (4-6 hourly) is standard protocol answer.

12.
Klumpe’s palsy is caused by:
A) Breech delivery
B) Vertex delivery
C) Brow delivery
D) Multiple deliveries.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Breech delivery

Explanation: Klumpke's palsy (injury to the lower brachial plexus, C8-T1) typically occurs when the arm is extended over the head during a breech delivery or excessive traction on the arm.

13.
The signs of intracranial injury in the newborn include:
A) High pitched cry, bulging anterior fontanelle, convulsions.
B) High pitched cry, jaundice, muscular twitching.
C) Muscular twitching, convulsions, sunken fontanelles.
D) Convulsions, sunken fontanelles, high pitched cry.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) High pitched cry, bulging anterior fontanelle, convulsions.

Explanation: Intracranial hemorrhage/injury causes increased intracranial pressure, manifesting as a bulging fontanelle, high-pitched "cerebral" cry, irritability, and convulsions. Sunken fontanelle indicates dehydration.

14.
An abnormal proliferation of chorionic villi of placenta is known as:
A) Chorioncarcinoma
B) Hydatidiform mole
C) Chromosomal degeneration.
D) Gonadotropin’s proliferation.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Hydatidiform mole

Explanation: A Hydatidiform mole (molar pregnancy) is characterized by abnormal proliferation of the trophoblast (chorionic villi), which become swollen and grape-like vesicles.

15.
Maternal causes of small for dates babies include:
A) Cervical incompetence.
B) Rhesus incompatibility
C) Poor maternal malnutrition.
D) Infections in Utero.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Poor maternal malnutrition.

Explanation: Maternal malnutrition is a direct maternal cause of Intrauterine Growth Restriction (IUGR/Small for Dates). Infection in utero (D) is a fetal/placental cause. Rhesus incompatibility (B) usually causes hydrops or anemia, not necessarily SFD. Cervical incompetence leads to preterm birth, not growth restriction.

16.
The statement that correctly describe the "threatened abortion" is:
A) The cervix is dilated, membranes intact, mild contractions.
B) Cervix is closed, membranes ruptured, mild contractions.
C) The cervix is closed, membranes intact, mild contractions.
D) Cervix is dilated, membranes ruptured, no contractions.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) The cervix is closed, membranes intact, mild contractions.

Explanation: In threatened abortion, the pregnancy is jeopardized but retrieval is possible. The cervix remains closed (os is closed), and membranes are intact. There may be mild bleeding and cramping (contractions).

17.
A mother develops maternal distress presents with:
A) Dehydration, beads of perspiration, normal pulse
B) Anxious expression, beads of perspiration, dehydration
C) Presence of ketones in urine, rapid respirations, frequency of micturition
D) Temperature above 37.5, severe headache, normal pulse rate
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Presence of ketones in urine, rapid respirations, frequency of micturition

Explanation: Signs of maternal distress (often in obstructed labour) include: Dehydration (dry mouth, concentrated urine), Ketonuria (starvation/exhaustion), Tachycardia (rapid pulse - note option D says normal pulse which is wrong), Fever, and Anxious/distressed demeanor. Option B is also strong but "normal pulse" in A and D rules them out. Option C's ketones and rapid respiration fit metabolic acidosis/distress.

18.
The appropriate management of heartburn in pregnancy is;
A) Taking spicy food, plenty of water.
B) Lying flat, avoiding spicy food
C) Taking milk, avoiding spicy food
D) Taking antacid tablets, taking fatty foods.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Taking milk, avoiding spicy food

Explanation: Management involves small frequent meals and avoiding triggers (spicy, fatty foods). Lying flat worsens it. Milk can help neutralize acid. Antacids are used, but fatty foods worsen it. The best combination of "Do" and "Don't" in the options is C.

19.
The cardiovascular physiological changes during pregnancy are:
A) Increased plasma proteins, blood pressure, plasma volume
B) Increased Cardiac output, blood volume, plasma volume.
C) Reduced Plasma proteins, blood viscosity, heamoglobin.
D) Reduced Blood pressure, hemoglobin, plasma protein.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Reduced Plasma proteins, blood viscosity, heamoglobin.

Explanation: While blood volume increases (B), the classic physiological change affecting lab values is Haemodilution. Plasma volume increases more than red cell mass, leading to physiological anemia (reduced Hb) and reduced viscosity. Plasma proteins concentration also decreases.

20.
The signs of impending eclampsia include:
A) Oedema,hypertension,proteinuria
B) Hypertension, epigastric pain, vomiting
C) Visual disturbance, epigastric pain, vomiting
D) Oedema,hypertension,visual disturbance
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Visual disturbance, epigastric pain, vomiting

Explanation: Impending eclampsia (severe pre-eclampsia symptoms warning of seizures) is characterized by cerebral and hepatic symptoms: Severe frontal headache, Visual disturbances (blurred vision, flashing lights), and Epigastric pain (liver edema). Vomiting and hyperreflexia are also signs. Edema/HTN/Proteinuria define Pre-eclampsia itself, not necessarily the "impending" crisis state.

Part II: Short Answer Questions (40 Marks)

1.
State five (5) functions of a mature placenta. (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Respiration: Gas exchange (Oxygen from mother to fetus, Carbon dioxide from fetus to mother).
  2. Nutrition: Transfer of nutrients (glucose, amino acids, vitamins) from maternal blood to fetal blood.
  3. Excretion: Removal of fetal waste products (urea, uric acid) into maternal circulation.
  4. Endocrine function: Production of hormones essential for pregnancy (HCG, HPL, Estrogen, Progesterone).
  5. Protection/Barrier: Prevents passage of certain bacteria and large molecules; provides passive immunity (IgG transfer).
2.
State five (5) warning signs of impending eclampsia. (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Severe frontal headache (not relieved by analgesics).
  2. Visual disturbances (blurring of vision, flashing lights/scotomata).
  3. Severe epigastric pain (due to liver capsule distension).
  4. Vomiting or nausea.
  5. Oliguria (reduced urine output).
  6. Hyperreflexia or Clonus.
3.
State four (4) characteristics of a preterm baby. (4 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Size: Small body size (usually < 2.5kg) with a large head relative to the body.
  2. Skin: Thin, translucent, red/pink skin with visible veins; abundant lanugo (fine hair).
  3. Fat: Minimal subcutaneous fat (looks wasted/wrinkled).
  4. Genitalia: In males, undescended testes; in females, labia majora do not cover labia minora.
  5. Posture: Hypotonic (frog-like posture), poor flexion of limbs.
  6. Sole creases: Few or absent creases on the soles of feet.
4.
Outline five (5) components of a partograph. (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Patient Identification: Name, Gravida, Parity, Hospital Number, Date/Time of admission.
  2. Fetal Condition: Fetal Heart Rate (FHR), Status of Membranes/Liquor, Moulding of fetal skull.
  3. Progress of Labour: Cervical dilation (plotted on graph), Descent of fetal head.
  4. Uterine Contractions: Frequency (per 10 mins) and Duration (intensity).
  5. Maternal Condition: Pulse, Blood Pressure, Temperature, Urine (volume, protein, ketones).
  6. Drugs and Fluids: Oxytocin, IV fluids, other medications.
5.
State three (3) indications of first examination of the newborn. (3 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. To detect any congenital abnormalities (e.g., cleft lip/palate, imperforate anus).
  2. To assess the baby's adaptation to extra-uterine life (APGAR scoring).
  3. To establish baseline measurements (weight, head circumference, length) for future monitoring.
  4. To determine the gestational age/maturity of the infant.
6.
Explain three (3) indications of vaginal examination during labour. (3 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. To assess progress of labour: Determine cervical dilation and descent of the presenting part.
  2. To confirm membrane status: Check if membranes have ruptured and assess liquor (meconium staining) or rule out cord prolapse after rupture.
  3. To confirm full dilation: Before allowing the mother to push.
  4. To determine presentation and position: Confirm fetal head position (e.g., occipito-anterior vs posterior).
7.
State five (5) causes of secondary amenorrhea. (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Pregnancy: The most common physiological cause.
  2. Lactation: Breastfeeding suppresses ovulation.
  3. Menopause: Cessation of menses.
  4. Polycystic Ovarian Syndrome (PCOS): Hormonal imbalance.
  5. Stress/Weight Changes: Excessive exercise, anorexia, or severe stress affecting the hypothalamus.
  6. Uterine causes: Asherman's syndrome (scarring).
  7. Contraceptive use: Depo-Provera or other hormonal methods.
8.
Draw and label a diagram of female pelvic floor (inlet). (5 Marks)

9.
State five (5) causes of ectopic pregnancy. (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID): Causes scarring and damage to fallopian tubes (e.g., Chlamydia/Gonorrhea).
  2. Previous Ectopic Pregnancy: Increases risk of recurrence.
  3. Tubal Surgery: Previous sterilization or reconstructive surgery.
  4. Intrauterine Contraceptive Device (IUCD): If pregnancy occurs with an IUD in situ, it is more likely to be ectopic.
  5. Endometriosis: Tubal adhesions affecting motility.
  6. Congenital Abnormalities: Hypoplasia or diverticula of the tube.

Part III: Long Answer Questions (40 Marks)

1.
Mrs. X aged 28 years, 15 weeks pregnant was admitted with complains of bleeding and abdominal cramping radiating to the back.

a) Define abortion (1 Mark)

Show Answer

Abortion is the expulsion or extraction of a fetus (embryo) from the uterus before it has reached the age of viability (usually defined as before 20-28 weeks of gestation or weighing less than 500g, depending on local legal definition).

b) State four (4) maternal causes of abortion (4 Marks)

Show Answer
  1. Infections: Acute fevers (Malaria, Typhoid) or TORCH infections.
  2. Chronic Diseases: Uncontrolled Diabetes, Hypertension, Renal disease.
  3. Uterine Abnormalities: Fibroids, septate uterus, cervical incompetence.
  4. Endocrine Imbalance: Progesterone deficiency (Luteal phase defect), Thyroid dysfunction.
  5. Trauma: Physical injury or surgery during pregnancy.

c) Outline five (5) types of abortion (5 Marks)

Show Answer
  1. Threatened Abortion: Pregnancy is jeopardized but may continue (cervix closed).
  2. Inevitable Abortion: Pregnancy cannot be salvaged (cervix open).
  3. Incomplete Abortion: Partial expulsion of products of conception.
  4. Complete Abortion: Total expulsion of all products of conception.
  5. Missed Abortion: Fetus dies but is retained in the uterus.
  6. Septic Abortion: Abortion complicated by infection.

d) Describe the management of Mrs. X till discharge (10 Marks)

Show Answer

Diagnosis: Likely Threatened or Inevitable Abortion (based on bleeding/cramping).

  1. Initial Assessment & Resuscitation:
    • Check vital signs (BP, Pulse) to assess for shock.
    • Secure IV access; start fluids (Normal Saline/Ringer's Lactate) if signs of shock/heavy bleeding.
    • Take blood for Grouping & Cross-match, Hemoglobin.
  2. Assessment of Pregnancy Status:
    • History: Amount of bleeding, passage of clots/tissue.
    • Speculum Exam: Check if cervical os is open or closed (Crucial to distinguish Threatened vs. Inevitable).
    • Ultrasound: Confirm fetal viability/heartbeat.
  3. Specific Management (Scenario Based):
    • If Threatened (Os Closed, Fetus Alive):
      • Rest: Bed rest and avoid strenuous activity.
      • Abstinence: Avoid sexual intercourse.
      • Medication: Sedation/Analgesia if needed; Progesterone support (if indicated).
      • Reassurance: Counsel the mother.
    • If Inevitable/Incomplete (Os Open, Heavy Bleeding):
      • Evacuation: Manual Vacuum Aspiration (MVA) or Dilation & Curettage (D&C) to remove retained products.
      • Oxytocics: Syntocinon/Ergometrine to control bleeding and contract uterus.
  4. Post-Procedure Care:
    • Monitor vitals and vaginal bleeding.
    • Administer Anti-D Immunoglobulin if Mrs. X is Rh Negative.
    • Antibiotics prophylaxis to prevent infection.
    • Pain management.
  5. Discharge & Counseling:
    • Counsel on family planning (can start immediately).
    • Psychological support for loss.
    • Advise on danger signs (fever, heavy bleeding).
2.
Mrs. J aged 18 years Para 0+0 has been in labour for 18 hours and on vaginal examination still 5cm dilated a diagnosis of obstructed labour was made.

a) Define obstructed labour. (1 Mark)

Show Answer

Obstructed labour is the failure of the fetal presenting part to descend through the birth canal despite strong uterine contractions, due to a mechanical blockage (disproportion between passenger and passage).

b) State five (5) predisposing factors to obstructed labour (5 Marks)

Show Answer
  1. Cephalo-Pelvic Disproportion (CPD): Small pelvis (contracted pelvis) or large baby (macrosomia).
  2. Malpresentation/Malposition: Brow, Face (mento-posterior), Shoulder presentation (Transverse lie), Occipito-posterior.
  3. Fetal Abnormalities: Hydrocephalus, fetal ascites, conjoined twins.
  4. Pelvic Tumors: Fibroids or ovarian cysts blocking the canal.
  5. Cervical/Vaginal Stenosis: Scarring from FGM or previous surgery preventing dilation.

c) Describe the management of Mrs. J until discharge (12 Marks)

Show Answer

1. Immediate Resuscitation (Pre-op):

  • Rehydration: Start IV fluids (Normal Saline/Ringer's Lactate) rapidly to correct dehydration and ketoacidosis (common in prolonged labour).
  • Antibiotics: Administer broad-spectrum IV antibiotics to prevent sepsis (prolonged labour increases risk).
  • Catheterization: Insert an indwelling catheter to drain the bladder (relieve obstruction, prevent injury, monitor output).
  • Grouping & Cross-match: Prepare blood for potential transfusion (PPH risk).

2. Delivery (Definitive Management):

  • Caesarean Section: Prepare for emergency C-section immediately (safest for live fetus and mother).
  • (Rarely: Symphysiotomy or Destructive operation if fetus is dead and CS not possible - but CS is standard).

3. Intra-operative Care:

  • Handle tissues gently (uterus may be thinned/friable).
  • Monitor for uterine rupture.

4. Postpartum Care:

  • Monitoring: Close monitoring of vitals, lochia, and uterine contraction (PPH risk due to atony).
  • Fistula Prevention: Keep catheter in situ for 10-14 days to rest the bladder and prevent VVF (Vesico-Vaginal Fistula) due to pressure necrosis.
  • Infection Control: Continue antibiotics.
  • Baby Care: Assess newborn for asphyxia or trauma; admit to NBU if needed.

5. Discharge & Counseling:

  • Counsel on birth spacing and family planning.
  • Advise that future deliveries must be in a hospital (CS likely).

d) List four (4) maternal complications of obstructed labour. (2 Marks)

Show Answer
  1. Ruptured Uterus: Life-threatening tear of the uterus.
  2. Obstetric Fistula (VVF/RVF): Leakage of urine/stool due to tissue necrosis.
  3. Postpartum Haemorrhage (PPH): Due to uterine atony or trauma.
  4. Puerperal Sepsis: Severe infection.
  5. Foot Drop: Nerve injury (lumbosacral plexus pressure).
