National Immunisation Schedule

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Understand the purpose of a national immunization schedule.
  • Describe the administration and dosage of BCG, Polio, and Pentavalent vaccines.
  • Explain the Tetanus Toxoid vaccine schedule for women of childbearing age.

📚 National Immunization Schedule Overview

The Kenya Expanded Program on Immunization (KEPI) follows a plan to administer vaccines. This plan clearly shows the vaccines that are given, the dosage, the time interval between doses, and the site of administration. This is called the National Immunization Schedule. This standard plan provides a guide to all health workers in the country involved in immunization. The immunization schedule varies from country to country and can change from time to time, depending on scientific discoveries. It is important for health workers to carefully study the National Immunization schedule and always use it as a reference guide when providing this service.

📚 BCG (Bacillus Calmette-Guerin) Vaccine

This is a live attenuated (weakened) bacterial vaccine. It is used in the immunization program to protect the child against Tuberculosis (TB). BCG vaccine is given in a single dose at birth or first contact. The vaccine is very sensitive to light and loses much of its potency when exposed to light. It is given by injecting the child intradermally (in the skin) on the left upper arm.

The amount of 0.05ml is recommended for children up to eleven months of age and 0.1ml for children after eleven months of age.

📚 Polio Vaccine

Polio vaccine is a live attenuated (weakened) virus used in the immunization program to protect children against poliomyelitis. The Sabin type is given orally (by mouth) in Kenya. Some countries use another type called Salk vaccine, which is given by injection.

Oral polio vaccine is given four times beginning at birth (polio 0); at 6 weeks (polio 1); at 10 weeks (polio 2); and at 14 weeks (polio 3), respectively. Two drops in the mouth are recommended for each dose. Booster doses are sometimes given to all children below five years of age in the entire country regardless of immunization status, which is done during National Immunization Days (NIDs). The primary objective is to eradicate poliomyelitis.

Polio Immunization Schedule:

  • Polio 0: Birth or first contact
  • Polio 1: 6 weeks
  • Polio 2: 10 weeks
  • Polio 3: 14 weeks

📚 Pentavalent Vaccine

Pentavalent has five vaccines, which include DPT, Hep B, and Hib. The DPT vaccine is commonly referred to as Triple Vaccine because it is used to prevent three diseases: diphtheria, pertussis, and tetanus. The diphtheria and tetanus parts of the vaccine are made from the respective toxins, while the pertussis vaccine is made of a killed bacterial antigen. It has become necessary to add Hepatitis B (Hep B) and Haemophilus Influenzae type b (Hib) vaccines to DPT to form what is now known as pentavalent vaccine (five vaccines).

The Pentavalent vaccine is given by injecting the child intramuscularly (in the muscle) at the left upper thigh. It is given three times, beginning at 6 weeks, 10 weeks, and 14 weeks respectively. A dose of 0.5 ml is recommended each time it is given.

Pentavalent Immunization Schedule:

  • DPT + Hep B + Hib I - 6 weeks
  • DPT + Hep B + Hib II - 10 weeks
  • DPT + Hep B + Hib III - 14 weeks

📚 Tetanus Toxoid Vaccine

This is a toxoid vaccine that prevents children against neonatal tetanus. KEPI targets all women of childbearing age (19-49 years) and pregnant mothers for Tetanus Toxoid (TT) vaccination. The aim is to use the TT vaccine to provide passive immunity for unborn babies through the transfer of the mother’s antibodies. This type of immunity reduces with time and is normally boosted by giving the child Pentavalent at six weeks after birth.

Tetanus Toxoid Vaccination Schedule: Every woman of childbearing age (19-49 years), including pregnant women, should get 9 doses of Tetanus Toxoid (TT) vaccine. The schedule is as follows:

  • First dose (TT₁): At first contact or as early as during pregnancy.
  • Second dose (TT₂): At least 4 weeks after the first dose.
  • Third dose (TT₃): At least 6 months after the second dose.
  • Fourth dose (TT₄): At least 1 year after the third dose.
  • Fifth dose (TT₅): At least 1 year after the fourth dose.

✍️ Practice Exercise

Describe the key components of the National Immunization Schedule. Explain the importance of following this schedule for effective immunization.

View Solution

The National Immunization Schedule outlines the specific vaccines to be administered, the correct dosage, the timing between doses, and the appropriate route of administration. Following the schedule ensures that children receive the necessary vaccines at the right time to achieve optimal protection against preventable diseases. Consistent adherence to the schedule also contributes to herd immunity, thus protecting the entire community.

📚 Key Takeaways

  • The National Immunization Schedule is a guide for administering vaccines.
  • BCG vaccine is given at birth to protect against TB.
  • Polio vaccine is given orally in multiple doses.
  • Pentavalent vaccine combines protection against five diseases and is given intramuscularly.
  • Tetanus toxoid vaccination is crucial for women of childbearing age to protect newborns from tetanus.
