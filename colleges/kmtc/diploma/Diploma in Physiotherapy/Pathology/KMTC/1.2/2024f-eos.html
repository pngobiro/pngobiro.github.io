SECTION I [Multiple Choice Questions]

1.
A patient with complains of poor vision and Paraparesis of extremities was referred for physiotherapy intervention. Further findings showed damage to myelin sheath of the brain. What could be the diagnosis for this patient?
A. Parkinsons disease.
B. Multiple sclerosis
C. GBS
D. All of the above.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Multiple sclerosis

Explanation: Multiple Sclerosis (MS) is an autoimmune disease characterized by demyelination (damage to the myelin sheath) in the Central Nervous System (brain and spinal cord). Classic symptoms include visual disturbances (optic neuritis) and motor weakness (paraparesis). GBS affects the peripheral nervous system. Parkinson's involves dopamine depletion in the basal ganglia, not primary demyelination.

2.
The most common bacterial microorganism that causes meningitis is?
A. Neisseria meningitidis
B. Staphylococcus meningitis
C. Bacterium meningitis
D. Staphylococcus pneumonia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Neisseria meningitidis

Explanation: Neisseria meningitidis (Meningococcus) and Streptococcus pneumoniae are the leading causes of bacterial meningitis in children and adults. Among the options provided, Neisseria meningitidis is the correct specific pathogen listed.

3.
While you were ambulating a patient in the medical ward who had been bedridden for one month, the patient fell down and started seating as she complained of severe chest pain. What could be the diagnosis of the patient.
A. Pulmonary embolism
B. Cardiac arrest
C. Heart failure
D. Stroke
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Pulmonary embolism

Explanation: Prolonged immobility (bedridden for one month) is a major risk factor for Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT). Mobilizing the patient likely dislodged a clot which traveled to the lungs (Pulmonary Embolism), causing sudden severe chest pain and collapse/dyspnea.

4.
A mother presented to physiotherapy outpatient clinic with a 1-year-old child with squinted eyes and an history of child not crying immediately after birth. What was the diagnosis of this child?
A. Meningitis
B. Diabetes type one
C. Cerebral palsy
D. Spina bifida
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Cerebral palsy

Explanation: A history of "not crying immediately after birth" suggests birth asphyxia (hypoxic-ischemic encephalopathy), which is a major risk factor for Cerebral Palsy. Squint (strabismus) and motor developmental issues are common features of CP.

5.
A patient was informed of the CT scan findings which indicated that her arteries were undergone hardening. The patient requested you to tell her the name of the condition she was suffering from. What was the name of the condition?
A. Atherosclerosis
B. Arteriosclerosis
C. Natriuresis
D. Arteriosus
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Arteriosclerosis

Explanation: Arteriosclerosis is the general term for the thickening and hardening of arterial walls (loss of elasticity). Atherosclerosis (Option A) is a specific type caused by plaque buildup. Given the description "arteries... undergone hardening", Arteriosclerosis is the most literal medical definition.

6.
Which of the following is a characteristic feature of Alzheimer's disease?
A. Lewy bodies
B. Neurofibrillary tangles
C. Amyloid plaques
D. Pick bodies
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Neurofibrillary tangles (and C. Amyloid plaques)

Explanation: Both Neurofibrillary tangles (tau protein) and Amyloid plaques (beta-amyloid) are the two hallmark pathological features of Alzheimer's disease. In multiple-choice questions where both are listed, either may be accepted, but tangles are intracellular while plaques are extracellular. Lewy bodies are found in Parkinson's/Lewy Body Dementia. Pick bodies are found in Pick's disease (Frontotemporal dementia).

7.
A 50-year-old alcoholic is rushed to the hospital with bleeding esophageal varices and expires. At autopsy, the patient's protruding abdomen is found to contain a large volume of serous fluid. What is the appropriate term used to describe this fluid?
A. Ascites
B. Exudate
C. Hemorrhage
D. Hydrothorax
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Ascites

Explanation: Ascites is the accumulation of fluid in the peritoneal cavity, commonly caused by portal hypertension in liver cirrhosis (often due to alcoholism). Esophageal varices are another complication of portal hypertension.

8.
Which measure is the first sign of respiratory muscle dysfunction in boys with Duchenne muscular dystrophy?
A. Vital capacity
B. Oxygen saturation
C. Maximal expiratory force
D. Negative inspiratory force
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Vital capacity (or C/D depending on sensitivity)

Explanation: A decline in Forced Vital Capacity (FVC) is the standard clinical measure used to track respiratory decline in DMD. However, reductions in maximal pressures (MIP/MEP) often precede the drop in vital capacity. In standard clinical monitoring contexts, Vital Capacity is the primary metric tracked.

9.
A 2-week-old newborn is brought to the physician because his lips have turned blue on three occasions during feeding; he also sweats during feeding. He was born at 38 weeks' gestation and weighed 2466 g; he currently weighs 2778 g. His temperature is 37.8°C, pulse is 170/min, respirations are 44/min, and blood pressure is 75/45 mm Hg. A grade 3/6 harsh systolic ejection murmur is heard at the left upper sternal border. An x-ray of the chest shows a small boot-shaped heart and decreased pulmonary vascular markings. Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis?
A. Atrial septal defect
B. Endocardial fibroelastosis
C. Tetralogy of Fallot
D. Total anomalous pulmonary venous return
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Tetralogy of Fallot

Explanation: The "boot-shaped heart" (Coeur en sabot) on X-ray is the classic hallmark of Tetralogy of Fallot (caused by RV hypertrophy and a small main pulmonary artery). Cyanosis during feeding ("Tet spells"), harsh systolic murmur (pulmonary stenosis), and decreased pulmonary markings are also characteristic.

10.
What is the primary site of involvement in Hodgkin's lymphoma?

Correct Answer: A. Peripheral lymph nodes

11.
Which of the following is a common complication of uncontrolled diabetes mellitus?
A. Osteoporosis
B. Diabetic ketoacidosis
C. Hyperthyroidism
D. Hypertension
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Diabetic ketoacidosis

12.
A 27-year-old man with history of alcoholism and repeated bouts of aspiration pneumonia comes to the emergency room with a high fever and pleuritic chest pain. Physical examination reveals dullness on percussion and absence of breath sounds in the right lower lung field. A chest x-ray demonstrates pleural fluid on the right side. Thoracentesis returns a thick, foul-smelling fluid. Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis?
A. Chylothorax
B. Empyema
C. Hemothorax
D. Pneumothorax
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Empyema

Explanation: Empyema is a collection of pus in the pleural cavity. The history of alcoholism and aspiration pneumonia suggests an anaerobic infection (aspiration of oral flora), leading to a "thick, foul-smelling fluid" (pus), which is characteristic of empyema. Chylothorax involves milky fluid (lymph). Hemothorax involves blood.

14.
Multiple Sclerosis (MS) autoimmune disorder characterized by inflammation, demyelination and gliosis. It mainly affects?
A. central nervous system
B. peripheral nervous system
C. autonomic nervous system
D. cranial nerves
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. central nervous system

Explanation: Multiple Sclerosis specifically targets the oligodendrocytes, which form myelin in the Central Nervous System (Brain and Spinal Cord). Demyelination in the Peripheral Nervous System is characteristic of Guillain-Barré Syndrome.

19.
What is the primary abnormality in von Willebrand disease?
A. Platelet dysfunction
B. Deficiency of clotting factor VIII
C. Deficiency of clotting factor IX
D. Deficiency of von Willebrand factor
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Deficiency of von Willebrand factor

Explanation: von Willebrand disease is the most common hereditary bleeding disorder, caused by a qualitative or quantitative deficiency of von Willebrand factor (vWF), which is required for platelet adhesion and also acts as a carrier for Factor VIII.

SECTION 2: Short answer questions

21.
Explain the term "neurodegenerative disease" and provide an example of a neurodegenerative disorder. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Model Answer:

Definition: A neurodegenerative disease is a condition characterized by the progressive loss of structure or function of neurons (nerve cells), often leading to their death. This process is usually irreversible and worsens over time.

Examples:

  • Alzheimer's Disease: Affects memory and cognition.
  • Parkinson's Disease: Affects movement (tremors, rigidity).
  • Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis (ALS): Affects motor neurons.
  • Huntington's Disease: Genetic disorder affecting muscle coordination and cognition.
22.
Discuss the pathophysiology of type 1 diabetes and explain the role of insulin in this condition. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Model Answer:

  1. Pathophysiology: Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus is an autoimmune disease where the body's immune system (T-cells) attacks and destroys the beta cells of the Islets of Langerhans in the pancreas. This results in an absolute deficiency of insulin production.
  2. Role of Insulin:
    • Under normal conditions, insulin acts as a key to unlock cells (especially muscle and adipose tissue) allowing glucose to enter from the bloodstream to be used for energy.
    • In Type 1 Diabetes, the lack of insulin means glucose cannot enter the cells. It accumulates in the blood (hyperglycemia) while cells starve of energy, triggering alternative metabolic pathways like ketogenesis.

26.
State 5 cardinal signs of Guillen barre' syndrome. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Model Answer:

  1. Ascending Muscle Weakness: Typically starts in legs and spreads upwards.
  2. Areflexia: Loss of deep tendon reflexes.
  3. Paresthesia: Numbness or tingling sensation in extremities.
  4. Symmetrical Involvement: Weakness usually affects both sides of the body equally.
  5. Respiratory Distress: In severe cases, weakness of respiratory muscles.
  6. Autonomic Dysfunction: Fluctuations in blood pressure or heart rate.

Explain 5 risk factors of peptic ulcers. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Model Answer:

  1. Helicobacter pylori (H. pylori) infection: Bacterial infection damaging the mucous coating.
  2. NSAIDs Usage: Long-term use of Non-Steroidal Anti-Inflammatory Drugs (Aspirin, Ibuprofen) inhibits protective prostaglandins.

28.
State five treatment goals of Parkinson’s disease. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Model Answer:

  1. Symptom Management: Control tremors, rigidity, and bradykinesia (using Levodopa, etc.).
  2. Improve Functional Mobility: Maintain gait and balance to prevent falls.
  3. Maintain Independence: In Activities of Daily Living (ADLs) for as long as possible.
  4. Manage Non-Motor Symptoms: Treat depression, sleep disorders, and constipation.
  5. Neuroprotection: Slowing disease progression (though limited options exist).
  6. Prevent Complications: Such as aspiration pneumonia and contractures.

SECTION 3: Long essay questions

29.
Describe cerebrovascular accident. (20 Marks)
Show Answer

Model Answer:

Definition: Cerebrovascular Accident (CVA), commonly known as a stroke, is a sudden neurological deficit caused by interruption of blood perfusion to the brain. It is a medical emergency.

Types of Stroke:

  • Ischemic Stroke (85%): Caused by blockage of an artery.
    • Thrombotic: Clot forms locally (often due to atherosclerosis).
    • Embolic: Clot travels from elsewhere (e.g., heart in Atrial Fibrillation).
  • Hemorrhagic Stroke (15%): Caused by rupture of a vessel.
    • Intracerebral: Bleeding into brain tissue (often hypertension-related).
    • Subarachnoid: Bleeding into subarachnoid space (often aneurysm rupture).
  • Transient Ischemic Attack (TIA): "Mini-stroke" where symptoms resolve within 24 hours; a warning sign.

Risk Factors:

  • Modifiable: Hypertension (major risk), Smoking, Diabetes, High Cholesterol, Obesity, Physical inactivity.
  • Non-Modifiable: Age (>55), Gender, Family history, Race.

Clinical Presentation (FAST):

  • Face drooping
  • Arm weakness (hemiplegia/hemiparesis)
  • Speech difficulty (aphasia/dysarthria)
  • Time to call emergency services.
  • Other signs: Vision loss, severe headache, dizziness/ataxia.

Management:

  • Immediate CT scan to distinguish ischemic vs hemorrhagic.
  • Thrombolytics (tPA) for ischemic stroke (within window period).
  • Rehabilitation: Physiotherapy, Occupational therapy, Speech therapy.
30.
Differentiate between restrictive lung diseases and chronic lung diseases. (20 Marks)
Show Answer

Model Answer:

Note: "Chronic Lung Diseases" often refers to Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD) in comparison contexts.

Feature Restrictive Lung Disease Obstructive (Chronic) Lung Disease
Primary Problem Difficulty filling the lungs with air. Lungs are stiff or chest wall is restricted. Difficulty emptying the lungs (exhaling). Airways are narrowed or obstructed.
Examples Pulmonary Fibrosis, Sarcoidosis, Scoliosis, Obesity, Neuromuscular disorders. COPD (Emphysema, Chronic Bronchitis), Asthma, Bronchiectasis.
Lung Volumes Total Lung Capacity (TLC) is decreased. Vital Capacity (VC) is decreased. Total Lung Capacity (TLC) is normal or increased (hyperinflation). Residual Volume (RV) increased (air trapping).
Spirometry (FEV1/FVC Ratio) Normal or Increased Ratio (>0.70). Both FEV1 and FVC decrease proportionally. Decreased Ratio (<0.70). FEV1 decreases significantly more than FVC.
Pathophysiology Decreased compliance (stiffness) of lung parenchyma or chest wall. Increased airway resistance and loss of elastic recoil.
Breathing Pattern Rapid, shallow breathing (Tachypnea). Prolonged expiration, pursed-lip breathing, barrel chest.
