Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions

1.
Which of the following is not considered as a risk associated with diagnosing of mitral valve Stenosis disease?
A. Right sided heart failure
B. Right ventricle hypertrophy
C. Left atrium hypertrophy
D. Left side ventricle hypertrophy
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Left side ventricle hypertrophy

Explanation: In mitral stenosis, blood flow from the left atrium to the left ventricle is obstructed. This causes pressure overload in the left atrium (leading to LA hypertrophy/dilation) and backwards into the pulmonary circulation, causing pulmonary hypertension and subsequently Right Ventricular Hypertrophy and Right Sided Heart Failure. The left ventricle, however, is protected from volume/pressure overload and is typically normal or small.

2.
Cardiomyopathies are classified according to the structural and functional abnormalities of the heart muscle. Which of the following statement best describes Arrhythmogenic cardiomyopathy?
A. Heart muscle becomes abnormally thick.
B. Ventricles stretch and become thin.
C. Heart is able to pump blood but not able to relax
D. There is increased deposition of fat.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. There is increased deposition of fat.

Explanation: Arrhythmogenic Right Ventricular Cardiomyopathy (ARVC) is characterized by the replacement of myocardial tissue (usually in the right ventricle) with fibro-fatty tissue.
A describes Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy.
B describes Dilated Cardiomyopathy.
C describes Restrictive Cardiomyopathy.

3.
Which of the following statement best describe heart failure class II according to New York heart association functional classification?
A. Activities of daily living do not cause symptoms
B. Normal activities cause symptoms which are relieved by rest
C. Symptom free at rest, minimal activity causes symptoms
D. Symptomatic at rest
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Normal activities cause symptoms which are relieved by rest

Explanation:
Class I: No limitation (A).
Class II: Slight limitation; comfortable at rest, but ordinary physical activity results in fatigue, palpitation, or dyspnea (B).
Class III: Marked limitation; comfortable at rest, but less than ordinary activity causes symptoms (C).
Class IV: Unable to carry out any physical activity without discomfort; symptoms at rest (D).

4.
A patient was diagnosed to be hypertensive after examination, which of the following readings correlate stage 3 hypertension?
A. SBP 230-210 –DBP 140-120
B. SBP 210-180 –DBP 120-110
C. SBP 180-160 –DBP 110-100
D. SBP 160-140 - DBP 100-90
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. SBP 210-180 –DBP 120-110

Explanation: Grade 3 (Severe) Hypertension is typically defined as SBP ≥ 180 mmHg and/or DBP ≥ 110 mmHg. Both A and B fall into this severe category, but B represents the typical starting range for Stage 3 classification. C correlates to Stage 2, and D correlates to Stage 1/2 depending on the guideline (ESH/ESC vs. ACC/AHA).

5.
A patient has been diagnosed with acute coronary syndrome with angina. Which of the following statement best describe unstable angina type?
A. Chest pain < 5minutes, result of fixed lesions of > 75% of the coronary artery lumen.
B. Chest pain > 5minutes and symptoms worsen i.e. Pain not relieved by medication (NTG) or rest.
C. Angina caused by spasm of coronary blood vessel.
D. Objective evidence of ischemia which is observed on an ECG monitor but the person does not complain of angina symptoms
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Chest pain > 5minutes and symptoms worsen i.e. Pain not relieved by medication (NTG) or rest.

Explanation: Unstable angina is characterized by new-onset pain, increasing frequency/intensity (crescendo), or pain at rest/not relieved by nitrates. Option A describes Stable Angina. Option C describes Prinzmetal's (Variant) Angina. Option D describes Silent Ischemia.

6.
Which of the following is not considered as the main cause of communicating hydrocephalus?
A. Obliteration of the subarachnoid cisterns
B. Malfunction of the arachnoid granulations
C. Blockage or occlusion of the sub arachnoid cisterns around the brain stem.
D. Obstruction at the cerebral aqueduct
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Obstruction at the cerebral aqueduct

Explanation: Obstruction at the cerebral aqueduct (Aqueduct of Sylvius) causes Non-Communicating (Obstructive) hydrocephalus because the ventricles do not communicate with the subarachnoid space. A, B, and C relate to absorption or flow issues outside the ventricular system, causing Communicating hydrocephalus.

7.
A patient was diagnosed with Spina bifida, on the handing over notes you read "a protrusion of the meninges through the bony defect, without accompanying nervous tissue" The statement best describes which form of Spina bifida?
A. Spina bifida occulta
B. Spina bifida cystica
C. Spina bifida Meningocele
D. Spina bifida Myelomeningocele
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Spina bifida Meningocele

Explanation: Meningocele involves the protrusion of the meninges (sac) only, filled with CSF. Myelomeningocele (D) involves protrusion of meninges AND spinal cord/nerves. Occulta (A) has no protrusion. Cystica (B) is the umbrella term for C and D.

8.
Gross motor functional classification is an assessment tool used in cerebral palsy based on ability to walk and need of assistive devices. Which of the following statement best describes LEVEL 2?
A. Walks without restriction, Limitations in high-level skills
B. Walks without devices, Limitations in walking outdoors
C. Walks with devices, Limitations walking outdoors
D. Limited mobility, Power mobility outdoors
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Walks without devices, Limitations in walking outdoors

Explanation: GMFCS Level II: Children walk in most settings but have difficulty walking long distances and balancing on uneven terrain, inclines, in crowded areas or confined spaces. They may walk with physical assistance, a handheld mobility device or used wheeled mobility over long distances.

9.
The following are the most common signs and symptoms of asthma;
A. Wheeze, chest tightness, shortness of breath and night sweat
B. shortness of breath, sputum, wheeze and night sweat
C. Sputum, chest tightness, shortness of breath and night sweat.
D. Wheeze, chest tightness, shortness of breath and Sputum production
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Wheeze, chest tightness, shortness of breath and Sputum production

Explanation: The classic triad of asthma symptoms is wheezing, dyspnea (shortness of breath), and cough (often with sputum production). Night sweats are more characteristic of tuberculosis or lymphoma, not primary asthma.

C. Fallot’s tetralogy

15.
A newborn infant is cyanotic at birth. You suspect transposition of the great arteries. A mixing lesion is necessary for life. Which of the following lesions would allow better mixing?
A. Ventricular septal defect.
B. Atrial septal defect.
C. Patent ductus arteriosus
D. Pulmonary valve atresia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Ventricular septal defect.

Explanation: Survival in Transposition of the Great Arteries (TGA) depends on mixing of blood between the parallel pulmonary and systemic circulations. While ASDs and PDAs allow mixing, a large Ventricular Septal Defect (VSD) typically allows for significant bidirectional shunting and "better" mixing compared to a small ASD or PDA alone, often deferring immediate severe cyanosis. However, clinically, an ASD (balloon septostomy) is often created to ensure mixing.

20.
A pneumothorax in which air enters the pleural cavity on inhalation but cannot leave on exhalation is known as ________
A. Spontaneous pneumothorax
B. Tension pneumothorax
C. Traumatic pneumothorax
D. Secondary pneumothorax
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Tension pneumothorax

Explanation: This "one-way valve" mechanism leads to a rapid accumulation of air in the pleural space, increasing intrathoracic pressure, collapsing the lung, and shifting the mediastinum (Tension Pneumothorax), which is a life-threatening emergency.

21.
Discuss the complications associated with Parkinson disease (5 marks)
Show Answer

Model Answer:

  1. Dysphagia & Aspiration: Difficulty swallowing can lead to malnutrition and aspiration pneumonia.
  2. Falls and Fractures: Postural instability and gait freezing increase the risk of falls and injuries.
  3. Dementia/Cognitive Decline: A significant portion of patients develop Parkinson's disease dementia.
  4. Depression and Anxiety: Mood disorders are very common non-motor symptoms.
  5. Immobility Complications: Such as deep vein thrombosis (DVT), pulmonary embolism, and pressure ulcers in advanced stages.
22.
What do you understand by the following terminologies (5 marks)
  1. Gullein barrier syndrome
  2. Multiple Sclerosis
  3. Myasthenia gravis
  4. Epilepsy
  5. A seizure
Show Answer

Model Answer:

  1. Guillain-Barré Syndrome: An acute autoimmune disorder where the immune system attacks the peripheral nerves (myelin sheath), causing rapid-onset muscle weakness and paralysis.
  2. Multiple Sclerosis: A chronic autoimmune disease of the Central Nervous System characterized by demyelination and scarring (sclerosis), disrupting nerve communication.
  3. Myasthenia Gravis: A chronic autoimmune neuromuscular disease that causes weakness in the skeletal muscles due to antibodies attacking acetylcholine receptors at the neuromuscular junction.
  4. Epilepsy: A chronic neurological disorder characterized by the tendency to have recurrent, unprovoked seizures.
  5. A Seizure: A transient occurrence of signs and/or symptoms due to abnormal excessive or synchronous neuronal activity in the brain.

Show Answer

  2. Fever and Flu-like Symptoms: Systemic sign of immune activation/inflammation.

25.
Describe the complications which arise in case of spinal cord injury (5 marks)
Show Answer

Model Answer:

  1. Pressure Ulcers: Skin breakdown due to immobility and loss of sensation.
  2. Autonomic Dysreflexia: Life-threatening hypertension triggered by stimuli below the injury level (in injuries above T6).
  3. Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT) & PE: Due to venous stasis from paralysis.
  4. Respiratory Complications: Pneumonia or atelectasis due to impaired cough and respiratory muscle weakness.
  5. Neurogenic Bladder/Bowel: Urinary retention, UTIs, and constipation/incontinence.
  6. Contractures/Spasticity: Shortening of muscles and stiff joints.

Show Answer

Model Answer:

  2. Quality: Described as pressure, squeezing, heaviness, or crushing (not sharp/stabbing).

SECTION III: Long Essay Questions

29.
Discuss the broad classes of stroke (20 marks)
Show Answer

Model Answer:

Stroke (Cerebrovascular Accident - CVA) is classified into two broad categories based on the underlying pathology: Ischemic Stroke and Hemorrhagic Stroke.

1. Ischemic Stroke (approx. 85-87% of cases)

Caused by blockage of blood flow to a part of the brain.

  • Thrombotic Stroke: Caused by a thrombus (blood clot) forming locally within a cerebral artery, often on top of an atherosclerotic plaque.
    • Large Vessel Thrombosis: Affects major arteries like the MCA or Carotid.
    • Small Vessel Disease (Lacunar Stroke): Affects deep penetrating arteries, often associated with hypertension and diabetes.
  • Embolic Stroke: Caused by an embolus (clot or debris) that forms elsewhere (usually the heart, e.g., in Atrial Fibrillation) and travels to lodge in a cerebral artery.
  • Systemic Hypoperfusion: Global decrease in blood flow (e.g., cardiac arrest) causing "watershed" infarcts.

2. Hemorrhagic Stroke (approx. 13-15% of cases)

Caused by rupture of a blood vessel leading to bleeding in or around the brain.

  • Intracerebral Hemorrhage (ICH): Bleeding directly into the brain parenchyma.
    • Causes: Hypertension (most common), amyloid angiopathy, AVMs, tumors.
  • Subarachnoid Hemorrhage (SAH): Bleeding into the subarachnoid space (between arachnoid and pia mater).
    • Presentation: Sudden "thunderclap" headache.
    • Causes: Rupture of a berry aneurysm (most common) or AVM.

Comparison: Ischemic strokes typically present with focal deficits corresponding to the vascular territory. Hemorrhagic strokes often present with headache, vomiting, and reduced consciousness due to increased intracranial pressure.

30.
Discuss the presentation of Spina bifida in the following areas (5 marks)
i) Neurological defects & impairment
ii) Musculoskeletal impairments
iii) Osteoporosis
iv) Common spinal deformities
Show Answer

Model Answer:

i) Neurological Defects & Impairment:

  • Paralysis: Flaccid or spastic paralysis of lower limbs depending on the level of the lesion (e.g., L3 vs S1).
  • Sensory Loss: Loss of touch, pain, and temperature sensation below the lesion, increasing risk of injury.
  • Bowel/Bladder Dysfunction: Neurogenic bladder (incontinence or retention) and bowel incontinence are nearly universal.
  • Hydrocephalus: Present in 80-90% of myelomeningocele cases, requiring shunting.

ii) Musculoskeletal Impairments:

  • Muscle Imbalance: Unopposed muscle action (e.g., strong flexors vs weak extensors) leads to contractures.
  • Joint Deformities: Hip dislocation or subluxation is common due to muscle imbalance. Clubfoot (Talipes equinovarus) is frequently seen at birth.
  • Gait Abnormalities: Dependence on orthotics (AFOs) or wheelchairs for mobility.

iii) Osteoporosis:

  • Cause: Reduced weight-bearing and lack of muscle pull on bones in paralyzed limbs lead to decreased bone mineral density.
  • Consequence: High risk of pathological fractures in the lower extremities (femur/tibia) with minimal trauma (e.g., during transfers).

iv) Common Spinal Deformities:

  • Scoliosis: Lateral curvature of the spine, very common (congenital or acquired due to paralysis), affecting sitting balance and lung function.
  • Kyphosis: Excessive outward curvature, often severe in the lumbar region (gibbus deformity) in high-level lesions.
  • Lordosis: Exaggerated inward curvature, often compensatory.
