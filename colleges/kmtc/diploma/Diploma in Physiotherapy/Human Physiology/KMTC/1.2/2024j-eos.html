SECTION I (Multiple Choice Questions)

1.
Why is secondary immune response of great importance in the control of communicable diseases?
A. It is non-specific and offers short-term protection
B. It produces antibodies in mass and is faster and long-lasting
C. It is mediated by immunoglobulin G and immunoglobulin M
D. It is the immune response against bacterial infections
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. It produces antibodies in mass and is faster and long-lasting

Explanation: The secondary immune response (anamnestic response) occurs upon re-exposure to an antigen. Due to immunological memory (memory B and T cells), it is characterized by a shorter lag phase, higher titer of antibodies (mainly IgG), and longer duration compared to the primary response.

2.
In which part of the nephron does vasopressin work by increasing the reabsorption of water?
A. The descending limb of the loop of Henle.
B. The distal convoluted tubule.
C. The thick ascending limb of loop of Henle.
D. The collecting ducts.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. The collecting ducts. (and late distal tubule)

Explanation: Vasopressin (Anti-Diuretic Hormone/ADH) acts on V2 receptors in the principal cells of the collecting ducts and the late distal convoluted tubule to insert aquaporin-2 channels, thereby increasing water permeability and reabsorption.

3.
The acrosome reaction
A. Involves the release of sperm nucleus to the ovum cytoplasm.
B. The fusion of the sperm and ovum nuclei.
C. Adherence of the sperm head to the zona pellucida.
D. Involves the release of lytic enzymes from the acrosome and the formation of the vitelline membrane.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Involves the release of lytic enzymes from the acrosome...

Explanation: The acrosome reaction is the exocytosis of the acrosome (a cap-like structure on the sperm head), releasing hydrolytic enzymes (like hyaluronidase and acrosin) that allow the sperm to penetrate the zona pellucida of the egg.

4.
The negative feedback effect of gonadotropins occurs by
A. Vasoconstriction of blood vessels from the gonads.
B. Autoregulation of the gonads by gonadol hormones.
C. Inhibition of gonadotropin releasing hormone by gonadol hormones.
D. Inhibition of the hypothalamus by gonadotropins from the anterior pituitary gland.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Inhibition of gonadotropin releasing hormone by gonadol hormones.

Explanation: High levels of gonadal hormones (Estrogen, Progesterone, Testosterone) exert negative feedback on the Hypothalamus to reduce GnRH secretion and on the Anterior Pituitary to reduce LH/FSH secretion.

5.
During embryonic development the absence of testosterone causes the genital tubercle which forms the penis to develop into
A. The urethra.
B. The vagina.
C. The clitoris.
D. The inguinal canal.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. The clitoris.

Explanation: The genital tubercle is a homologous structure. In the presence of dihydrotestosterone (DHT), it enlarges to form the glans penis. In the absence of androgens (female development), it becomes the glans clitoris.

6.
What is the difference between an exocrine gland and an endocrine gland?
A. An endocrine gland secretes neurotransmitters (an exocrine gland does not).
B. An endocrine gland secretes via a tube to the destination (an exocrine gland does not).
C. An exocrine gland secretes into the blood (an endocrine gland does not).
D. An endocrine gland secretes into the blood (an exocrine gland does not).
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. An endocrine gland secretes into the blood (an exocrine gland does not).

Explanation: Endocrine glands are ductless and secrete hormones directly into the bloodstream (interstitial fluid then blood). Exocrine glands secrete products into ducts that lead to an epithelial surface (skin or lumen of hollow organs).

7.
What effect does aldosterone have?
A. It causes glucose to be absorbed from the blood
B. It causes Na + to be absorbed in the kidneys
C. It causes Ca ++ to be absorbed from the gut
D. It causes K + to be absorbed from the filtrate.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. It causes Na + to be absorbed in the kidneys

Explanation: Aldosterone promotes sodium (Na+) reabsorption and Potassium (K+) secretion in the distal tubules and collecting ducts of the kidney.

8.
The hormones known as "catecholamines" (adrenaline, noradrenaline and dopamine) are not lipid-soluble. Therefore their receptor sites are:
A. On the inside of the plasma membrane.
B. On the outside of the plasma membrane.
C. In the cell cytoplasm.
D. In the cell nucleus.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. On the outside of the plasma membrane.

Explanation: Lipid-insoluble (water-soluble) hormones cannot cross the cell membrane. They bind to cell surface receptors (G-protein coupled receptors) on the extracellular side of the plasma membrane to activate second messenger systems.

9.
How are cortical nephrons different from juxtamedullary nephrons?
A. Cortical nephrons lie almost entirely outside the renal medulla.
B. Cortical nephrons have an associated vasa rectum.
C. Cortical nephrons have a longer tubule.
D. There are fewer cortical nephrons.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Cortical nephrons lie almost entirely outside the renal medulla.

Explanation: Cortical nephrons have short loops of Henle that barely penetrate the medulla. Juxtamedullary nephrons have long loops that extend deep into the medulla and are associated with vasa recta (important for concentrating urine).

10.
Which part of the renal tubule is impermeable to water?
A. The ascending limb of the loop of Henle
B. The collecting duct
C. The proximal convoluted tubule
D. The thin portion of the loop of Henle
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. The ascending limb of the loop of Henle

Explanation: The thick ascending limb of the Loop of Henle is impermeable to water but actively pumps out solutes (Na+, K+, 2Cl-), creating the hyperosmotic medullary gradient.

11.
To what does the "blood-brain barrier" refer?
A. The three meninges that surround the brain and spinal cord.
B. The tight junctions between endothelial cells of the capillaries that serve the brain.
C. The structures that prevent fat-soluble molecules from entering the brain from the blood.
D. The structure that produces cerebrospinal fluid from blood
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. The tight junctions between endothelial cells of the capillaries that serve the brain.

Explanation: The BBB is formed primarily by tight junctions between capillary endothelial cells, supported by astrocyte foot processes. It restricts the passage of solutes.

12.
In what respect does "gray matter" differ from "white matter"
A. gray matter refers to the CNS while white matter refers to the PNS.
B. white matter makes up the autonomic nervous system, gray matter does not.
C. gray matter is found in the cerebrum, while white matter occurs in the cerebellum and the diencephalon.
D. gray matter contains the cell bodies of nerve cells white matter contains axons.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. gray matter contains the cell bodies of nerve cells white matter contains axons.

Explanation: Gray matter consists primarily of neuronal cell bodies (soma), dendrites, and unmyelinated axons. White matter consists primarily of myelinated axons (the myelin gives it the white color).

13.
Which statement about the sympathetic and/or parasympathetic divisions is correct?
A. All sympathetic neurons release ACh as a neurotransmitter.
B. Sympathetic division fibres emerge from brain & sacral spinal cord.
C. Parasympathetic division stimulates adrenal gland to release norepinephrine & epinephrine.
D. Some organs are innervated by both sympathetic division and parasympathetic division.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Some organs are innervated by both sympathetic division and parasympathetic division.

Explanation: Most visceral organs have dual innervation with antagonistic effects (e.g., heart, lungs, stomach). A is false (postganglionic sympathetic usually release NE). B is false (that's parasympathetic; sympathetic is thoracolumbar). C is false (Sympathetic stimulates adrenal medulla).

14.
Accommodation refers to the eye's ability to focus light from objects whatever their distance from the eye. How is this achieved?
A. by altering the distance between the cornea and the eye's lens
B. by altering the distance between the lens and the retina
C. by altering the shape of the eye's lens
D. by altering the shape of the cornea
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. by altering the shape of the eye's lens

Explanation: Accommodation involves the ciliary muscle contracting or relaxing to change the curvature (convexity) of the lens, thereby changing its focal length.

15.
What does the ciliary muscle do when accommodation (focusing) in the eye occurs?
A. It contracts and the tension on the ciliary fibres increases making the eye lens less convex.
B. It relaxes and the tension on the ciliary fibres decreases allowing the eye lens to become more convex.
C. It contracts and the pull of the ciliary fibres decreases allowing the lens to become more convex.
D. It relaxes and the pull of the ciliary fibres increases to make the lens less convex.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. It contracts and the pull of the ciliary fibres decreases allowing the lens to become more convex.

Explanation: For near vision accommodation: Ciliary muscles contract → Suspensory ligaments (ciliary fibers) slacken/relax → Lens bulges (becomes more convex) due to its own elasticity → Refractive power increases.

16.
Motor neurons transmit information
A. From receptor organs to the brain for integration.
B. From the muscle cell or a gland to the central nervous system.
C. From the central nervous system away to muscle cells or a gland.
D. From receptor organs to the spinal cord portion of the central nervous system.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. From the central nervous system away to muscle cells or a gland.

Explanation: Motor (efferent) neurons carry signals from the CNS to effectors (muscles and glands).

17.
Which of the following is the appropriate physiologic response to parasympathetic stimulation?
A. The pupils dilate.
B. Heart rate increases.
C. Secretion from salivary glands reduces.
D. Gastric motility increases
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Gastric motility increases

Explanation: Parasympathetic system ("Rest and Digest") increases digestive activity (motility and secretion). It constricts pupils (A is sympathetic) and decreases heart rate (B is sympathetic). It increases watery salivary secretion (C is incorrect).

18.
Which of the following statements is true?
A. Secretion of insulin decreases after a meal as that of glucagon increases
B. Secretion of insulin increases after a meal as that of glucagon decreases
C. A rise in plasma Ca2+ increases synthesis of parathyroid hormone
D. Calcitonin maintains high level of Ca2+ in plasma while parathyroid hormone decreases plasma Ca2+
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Secretion of insulin increases after a meal as that of glucagon decreases

Explanation: After a meal, blood glucose rises, stimulating insulin release (to store glucose) and inhibiting glucagon. PTH increases calcium (stimulated by low Ca2+), and Calcitonin lowers it.

19.
Which of the following statements is true regarding the rods?
A. Over most of the retina, rods are 20 times less common than cones.
B. The rods are evenly distributed throughout the entire retina.
C. Rods are very sensitive to light and can function in very dim light, but they don't detect colour
D. Rods are very sensitive to colour, but function only in very bright light and they don't detect light.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Rods are very sensitive to light and can function in very dim light, but they don't detect colour

Explanation: Rods are responsible for scotopic (night) vision and are highly sensitive to light but monochromatic. Cones are for color and acuity. Rods are more numerous than cones (approx 120 million vs 6 million) and are absent in the fovea (so B is false).

20.
Given that the specific gravity of a urine sample is 1.009, which of the following statements is correct?
A. The patient is dehydrated
B. The sample contains 1.009 mmol/L of dissolved particles
C. The sample contains 1.009 millimoles of dissolved solutes
D. The urine has a density of 1.009 g/ml
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. The urine has a density of 1.009 g/ml

Explanation: Specific gravity is the ratio of the density of a substance to the density of water (1.000 g/ml). A SG of 1.009 implies a density of 1.009 g/ml. It is relatively dilute (normal 1.005-1.030), suggesting the patient is well-hydrated, not dehydrated.

22.
Distinguish between exocrine and endocrine glands. (5 marks).
Show Answer

Model Answer:

  1. Ducts: Exocrine glands have ducts to transport secretions; Endocrine glands are ductless.
  2. Site of Secretion: Exocrine glands secrete onto an epithelial surface (skin or lumen); Endocrine glands secrete directly into the bloodstream (interstitial fluid).
  3. Nature of Secretion: Exocrine secretions include enzymes, sweat, oil, mucus; Endocrine secretions are exclusively hormones.
  4. Target: Exocrine effects are usually local or specific to the duct opening; Endocrine hormones travel systemically to distant target organs.
  5. Examples: Exocrine: Sweat glands, Salivary glands; Endocrine: Thyroid, Pituitary, Adrenal glands.
23.
List five functions of the hypothalamus. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Model Answer:

  1. Temperature Regulation: Acts as the body's thermostat to maintain core temperature.
  2. Endocrine Control: Controls the pituitary gland via releasing/inhibiting hormones (anterior) and direct neural connection (posterior).
  3. Autonomic Control: Regulates the Autonomic Nervous System (heart rate, BP, digestion).
  4. Thirst and Water Balance: Contains osmoreceptors triggering thirst and ADH release.
  5. Appetite Regulation: Contains hunger (satiety) and feeding centers.
  6. Circadian Rhythms: Regulates sleep-wake cycles (Suprachiasmatic nucleus).
24.
State one function for each of the following parts of the central nervous system. (5 marks)
  • Frontal cerebral cortex
  • Temporal cerebral cortex
  • Parietal cerebral cortex
  • Occipital cortex
  • Cerebellum
Show Answer

Model Answer:

  • Frontal cerebral cortex: Voluntary motor control, planning, decision making, personality, and speech production (Broca's area).
  • Temporal cerebral cortex: Auditory processing (hearing), memory formation (hippocampus), and language comprehension (Wernicke's area).
  • Parietal cerebral cortex: Somatosensory processing (touch, pain, temperature, proprioception) and spatial awareness.

  • Cerebellum: Coordination of voluntary movements, balance, posture, and motor learning.
25.
Outline the actions of estrogens (5 marks).
Show Answer

Model Answer:

  1. Reproductive Tract Development: Promotes growth and maturation of the uterus, fallopian tubes, and vagina.
  2. Secondary Sexual Characteristics: Stimulates breast development, widening of hips, and female fat distribution.
  3. Menstrual Cycle Regulation: Stimulates proliferation of the endometrial lining (proliferative phase) and triggers LH surge for ovulation.
  4. Bone Health: Inhibits osteoclasts, preserving bone density (prevents osteoporosis).
  5. Metabolic Effects: Increases HDL (good cholesterol) and decreases LDL; promotes protein synthesis.
26.
Outline steps in urine formation (5 marks)
Show Answer

Model Answer:

  1. Glomerular Filtration: Blood is filtered at the glomerulus due to hydrostatic pressure, pushing water and solutes into Bowman's capsule to form ultrafiltrate.
  2. Tubular Reabsorption: Essential substances (water, glucose, ions) are reclaimed from the filtrate back into the peritubular capillaries (mainly in PCT).
  3. Tubular Secretion: Wastes, drugs, and excess ions (H+, K+) are actively transported from the blood into the tubular fluid (mainly in DCT).
  4. Concentration/Dilution: The Loop of Henle and Collecting Ducts adjust water content (under ADH control) to regulate urine osmolarity.
  5. Excretion: The final urine flows into the renal pelvis, ureters, and bladder for elimination.
27.
List 5 functions of hormones (5 marks)
Show Answer

Model Answer:

  1. Growth and Development: Regulate physical growth (GH) and maturation (Sex hormones).
  2. Homeostasis: Maintain constant internal environment (e.g., Insulin/Glucagon for blood sugar, Calcitonin/PTH for calcium).
  3. Reproduction: Regulate gamete production, sexual cycles, and pregnancy (FSH, LH, Estrogen, Progesterone).
  4. Metabolism: Control metabolic rate and energy balance (Thyroxine, Cortisol).
  5. Response to Stress: Prepare body for "fight or flight" (Adrenaline, Cortisol).
28.
A) Why does lactation not occur during pregnancy despite an increasing prolactin level? (2 marks)
B) Briefly describe the uterine cycle phases (3 marks).
Show Answer

Model Answer:

A) Lactation inhibition: High levels of Estrogen and Progesterone (secreted by the placenta) during pregnancy inhibit the action of prolactin on breast tissue, preventing milk production. After delivery, the placenta is expelled, these hormone levels drop, lifting the inhibition.

B) Uterine Cycle Phases:

  1. Menstrual Phase (Days 1-5): Shedding of the functional layer of the endometrium due to low hormone levels, manifesting as bleeding.
  2. Proliferative Phase (Days 6-14): Regrowth and thickening of the endometrium stimulated by rising Estrogen levels from the developing follicle.
  3. Secretory Phase (Days 15-28): Further thickening and vascularization of the endometrium with glandular secretion, driven by Progesterone from the corpus luteum, preparing for implantation.

3. Mechanism/Process:

  • Binding: Sperm binds to ZP3 receptors on the Zona Pellucida.
  • Fusion: This binding triggers the outer acrosomal membrane to fuse with the sperm's plasma membrane at multiple points.
  • Pore Formation: Pores develop, releasing the acrosomal enzymes (hyaluronidase and acrosin).
  • Digestion: Enzymes digest a path through the glycoprotein layer of the zona pellucida.

4. Physiological Importance:

  • Penetration: It is absolutely essential for the sperm to penetrate the protective layers (cumulus oophorus and zona pellucida) of the ovum.
  • Fusion competence: It modifies the sperm membrane to allow fusion with the oocyte plasma membrane.
  • Prevention of Polyspermy: Triggers the cortical reaction in the egg upon entry, ensuring only one sperm fertilizes the egg.
30.
Discuss the reabsorption and secretion at the proximal convoluted tubule. (20 marks)
Show Answer

Model Answer:

The Proximal Convoluted Tubule (PCT) is the site of bulk reabsorption, handling 65-70% of the glomerular filtrate.

A. Reabsorption in PCT

1. Sodium (Na+) Reabsorption:

  • Primary driving force. Active transport via Na+/K+ ATPase pump at the basolateral membrane creates a gradient.
  • Na+ enters cells apically via co-transport (with glucose/amino acids) or counter-transport (Na+/H+ exchange).

2. Water Reabsorption:

  • Occurs by osmosis. As solutes (Na+, glucose) are reabsorbed, water follows passively to maintain isosmolality.
  • Permeability is high due to constitutive Aquaporin-1 channels.

3. Glucose and Amino Acids:

  • 100% reabsorbed in healthy individuals.
  • Mechanism: Secondary active transport (Sodium-Glucose Co-Transporters - SGLT).

4. Bicarbonate (HCO3-):

  • 80-90% reabsorbed. Depends on H+ secretion.
  • Filtered HCO3- combines with secreted H+ to form H2CO3 -> CO2 + H2O (via Carbonic Anhydrase). CO2 diffuses into cell, reforms HCO3-, which is transported to blood.

5. Other Solutes:

  • Potassium, Calcium, Urea are reabsorbed via solvent drag and paracellular pathways.

B. Secretion in PCT

  • Hydrogen Ions (H+): Secreted via Na+/H+ antiporter. Critical for acid-base balance and bicarbonate reabsorption.
  • Organic Acids/Bases: Secretion of wastes like creatinine, uric acid, antibiotics (penicillin), and diuretics.
  • Ammonia (NH3): Produced by glutamine metabolism in PCT cells and secreted to buffer urine acid.

Summary: The PCT reabsorbs all useful organic nutrients and the majority of ions/water, while secreting metabolic wastes and protons for pH regulation.
