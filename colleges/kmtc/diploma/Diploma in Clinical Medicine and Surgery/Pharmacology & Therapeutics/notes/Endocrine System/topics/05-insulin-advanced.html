Insulin & Diabetes Mellitus

01

History & Physiology

  • 1921 Banting and Best
  • 1923 Nobel Prize (sharing)
  • 1926 crystalline form obtained by Abel
  • 1956-69 chemical and molecular structure was worked out by Sanger and Hodgkin.

Dog, Margorie was injected with the first insulin in 1921

Physiology

  • Alpha cells : Glucagon
  • Beta Cells : Insulin
  • Delta Cells : Gastrin
  • Normal range of Blood glucose: 70 - 120 $\mathrm{mg} / \mathrm{dl}(100 \mathrm{ml})$
  • Hypoglycemia: $<70 \mathrm{mg} / \mathrm{dl}$
  • Diabetes: > $120 \mathrm{mg} / \mathrm{dl}$ overnight fasting, 23 hr after meal.

Regulation Sequence

  1. Gastrin form delta cells
  2. Pancreas (islets of langerhans)
  3. Glucagon Released by Alpha Cells of Pancreas
  4. Liver Releases Glucose into Blood
  5. Insulin Released by Beta Cells of Pancreas
  6. Fat Cells Take in Glucose from Blood
  7. Achieve Normal Blood Glucose Levels
02

Diabetes Mellitus

Is a group of metabolic diseases characterized by increased levels of glucose in the blood (hyperglycemia) resulting from defects in insulin secretion, insulin action, or both

related to:

  • An endocrine disorder causes Abnormal insulin production
  • Impaired insulin utilization
  • Both abnormal production and impaired utilization

TYPES OF DIABETES

  • Type-I : deficiency of insulin (IDDM, Juvenile onset DM). Type-IA: autoimmune disease of the pancreatic cell. Type-IB is idiopathic
  • Type-II: inadequate insulin (Type II, NIDDM, Maturity onset DM)
  • Gestational Diabetes Mellitus (GDM): pregnancy and usually resolved during the postpartum period.

Diabetic Control & Diagnostics

  • Blood from vein determines blood glucose
  • Value is on day to day basis
  • Glycosylated haemoglobin $\left(\mathrm{HbA}_{\mathrm{IC}}\right)$ in RBC is directly proportional to glucose concentration over a period of time
  • Life span of RBC is 120 days
  • $6 \%=110 \mathrm{mg} / \mathrm{dL}$ of blood glucose. Value more than $8 \%$ shows poor control of diabetes.

Structure & Mechanism of Action

C - Peptide

  • Insulin & C-peptide released in equal amounts
  • Type-1 diabetes little or no C-peptide
  • Type-2 diabetes typically normal or high.
  • C-peptide as biological marker

Structural Components

  • A Chain
  • B Chain
  • S - S (Disulfide bonds)
  • Preproinsulin (Signal sequence)
  • Proinsulin

Insulin Receptor Mechanism

  1. The $\alpha$ subunit binds insulin (the signal).
  2. The $\beta$ subunit transmits a signal from bound insulin to the cytoplasm.
  3. The insulin signal activates the receptor's protein kinas domain in the cytoplasm.
  4. Protein kinases from the receptor phosphorylate insulin-response substrates triggering other chemical responses inside the cell.

Autophosphorylation | PI 3-kinase | GLUT4: Glucose transporter

Insulin Preparations & Modifications

Older insulin: porcine/bovine - contaminants

  1. purified by gel filtration
  2. After gel filtration further purified by ion exchange chromatography.
  3. Human Insulins: Decombinant DNA technology (E.coli).
  4. Insulin lispro, Insulin aspart, Insulin Glulisine - short acting
  5. Insulin glargine - long acting

Advantages of newer insulins

  • Less allergic
  • More stable
  • Less insulin resistance
  • Less lipodystrophy
  • Blood glucose level easily controlled

Modifications

LISPRO INSULIN

28th and 29th amino acids on the insulin B chain, lysine and proline = Lispro

Insulin aspart

Apartic acid at B28

Insulin Glulisine

Lysine at B23 and Glutamic acid at B29. Advantages and actions are similar to Insulin lispro

INSULIN GLARGINE

  • Onset of action: 1-1¬Ω hours
  • Maximum effect: 4-6 hours (peakless)
  • Duration of action: 24 Hours (ultra long acting)
  • Don't mix with other insulin preparations in the same syringe - it is acidic (pH 4)
  • Absorption pattern: independent of anatomic site of injection
  • Less immunogenicity
  • 6-7 fold greater binding than native insulin
06

Delivery & Clinical Management

NEWER INSULIN DELIVERY DEVICES

  • Insulin syringes: Prefilled insulin syringes
  • Pen devices: Syringes resemble pen, preset amount propelled through a trigger
  • Jet injectors: Painless rapid delivery through needless device
  • Insulin pumps: Insulin is infused at a calculated rate
  • Nasal insulin delivery
  • Rectal and subcutaneous pellets

DRUG INTERACTIONS

  • Beta adrenergic blockers suppress warning signs
  • Thiazides, frusemide, corticosteroids, oral contraceptives, salbutamol, calcium channel blockers raise the blood sugar by inhibiting the insulin secretion.
  • Alcohol precipitates hypoglycemia (depleting breaking the glycogen)
  • Salicylates, lithium and theophylline enhance insulin secretion and peripheral glucose utilization resulting the hypoglycemia.

USES OF INSULIN

Infection and ketoacidosis: Short term use

Long term use is advised in CVS, retinal, renal, neurological conditions etc.

IDDM: compulsory use

NIDDM: it is advised

  1. Failure of oral hypoglycaemic agents.
  2. Temporary use in infection, trauma, surgery, pregnancy.

INSULIN RESISTANCE

  • Requirement of insulin: more than 200 U/day
  • May be due to antibodies production
  • Resistance should be in the absence of ketoacidosis, Infection or stress.
  • Select more purified newer insulin
  • Oral hypoglycemic agents may be added

Ketoacidosis

Symptoms of Ketoacidosis

  • high blood sugar levels
  • frequent urination (polyuria) and thirst
  • fatigue and lethargy
  • nausea
  • vomiting
  • abdominal pain
  • fruity odor to breath
  • rapid, deep breathing
  • muscle stiffness or aching coma

Treatment of Ketoacidosis

  • Regular insulin by i.v.
  • -i.v. fluids
  • -KCl
  • -Bicarbonate to correct acidosis
  • -Phosphate (when required)
  • Antibiotics
