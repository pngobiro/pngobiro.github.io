üõë Antidiarrheal Agents

Increased motility of the gastrointestinal tract and decreased absorption of fluid are major factors in diarrhea.

Antidiarrheal drugs include:

  • anti-motility agents
  • adsorbents, and
  • drugs that modify fluid and electrolyte transport

Antimotility agents

Include: diphenoxylate and loperamide. Both are analogs of meperidine.

üîÑ

Mechanism of Action (MOA)

  • Have opioid-like actions on the gut, activating pre-synaptic opioid receptors in the enteric nervous system to inhibit acetylcholine release and decrease peristalsis
  • At the usual doses, they lack analgesic effects

Adverse Drug Reactions (ADRs)

  • Drowsiness
  • Abdominal cramps
  • dizziness
‚ö†Ô∏è

Contraindication

These drugs can contribute to toxic megacolon (abnormal dilatation of the colon) & should therefore not be used in young children or in patients with severe colitis.

Adsorbents

  • Include: bismuth subsalicylate
  • Methylcellulose
  • Aluminium hydroxide

MOA: Presumed to act by adsorbing intestinal toxins or microorganisms and/or by coating or protecting the intestinal mucosa.

  • Are much less effective than antimotility agents
  • They can interfere with the absorption of other drugs

Activated Charcoal

Indicated for the prevention and relief of intestinal gas, diarrhea, GI distress associated with indigestion. Dosage 2 capsules every 30-60 minutes.

Agents that modify fluid and electrolyte transport

  • E.g. Bismuth subsalicylate
  • Used for traveler's diarrhea
  • Decreases fluid secretion in the bowel
  • Its action may be due to its salicylate component as well as its coating action

üöΩ Laxatives

Introduction

Used to accelerate GIT function. They can be classified on the basis of their mechanism of action as:

  • Irritants or stimulants of the gut
  • Bulking agents
  • Stool softeners
  • Saline and osmotic laxatives
  • Lubricant laxatives
üí°

Important Considerations

  • They all have a risk of being habit-forming... (anorexia nervosa)
  • Also increase the potential of loss of pharmacologic effect of drugs that are poorly absorbed, delayed-acting, and extended-release oral preparations by accelerating their transit through the intestines
  • They may cause electrolyte imbalances when used chronically

Specific Agents

‚ñ∂ Senna (Senokot)
  • a stimulant laxative
  • Taken orally
  • Causes evacuation of the bowels within 8 to 10 hours
  • It also causes water and electrolyte secretion into the bowel
  • In combination with a docusate-containing stool softener, it is useful in treating opioid-induced constipation
  • 15 mg OD don't exceed 100mg/day
  • Not for use > 1 week
‚ñ∂ Bisacodyl (Dulcolax)
  • available as suppositories and enteric-coated tablets
  • A potent stimulant of the colon
  • Acts directly on nerve fibers in the mucosa of the colon
  • 5-15mg po od

Adverse effects: abdominal cramps and the potential for atonic colon with prolonged use.

‚ö†Ô∏è

Interaction Warning

Antacids should not be taken at the same time as the enteric-coated tablets, they would cause the enteric coating to dissolve prematurely in the stomach, resulting in stomach irritation and pain. The same adverse effects could be expected with milk, H2-receptor antagonists, and PPIs.

‚ñ∂ Castor Oil
  • Is broken down in the small intestine to ricinoleic acid, which is very irritating to the gut, and promptly increases peristalsis.
  • It should be avoided by pregnant patients, because it may stimulate uterine contractions

Categories of Laxatives

Bulk Laxatives

Include - methylcellulose, psyllium seeds, and bran.

  • The bulk laxatives include hydrophilic colloids (from indigestible parts of fruits and vegetables)
  • They form gels in the large intestine, causing water retention and intestinal distension, thereby increasing peristaltic activity
  • They should be used cautiously in patients who are bed-bound, due to the potential for intestinal obstruction.

Saline and Osmotic Laxatives

  • Saline cathartics (magnesium citrate, magnesium sulfate, sodium phosphate, magnesium hydroxide) are nonabsorbable salts that hold water in the intestine by osmosis and distend the bowel, increasing intestinal activity and producing defecation in a few hours.
  • Electrolyte solutions containing polyethylene glycol (PEG) are used as colonic lavage solutions to prepare the gut for radiologic or endoscopic procedures.
  • Lactulose: a semisynthetic disaccharide sugar that acts as an osmotic laxative. It cannot be hydrolyzed by intestinal enzymes. Oral doses are degraded in the colon by colonic bacteria into lactic, formic, and acetic acids. This increases osmotic pressure, accumulating fluid, distending the colon, creating a soft stool, and causing defecation.

Stool Softeners (Emollient Laxatives)

  • Surface-active agents that become emulsified with the stool produce softer faeces and ease passage.
  • Include docusate sodium, docusate calcium, and docusate potassium.
  • They may take days to become effective. They should not be taken together with mineral oil because of the potential for absorption of the mineral oil.

Lubricant Laxatives

  • Mineral oil and glycerine suppositories are considered to be lubricants.
  • They facilitate the passage of hard stools.
  • Mineral oil should be taken orally in an upright position to avoid its aspiration and potential for lipid or lipoid pneumonia.

Laxatives in Children

üë∂

Pediatric Caution

Laxatives use discouraged in children. Infrequent defaecation may be normal in breast-fed babies or in response to poor intake of fluid and fiber.

Delay for more than 3 days may cause hard stools, cause pain in passing stools, cause anal fissure. Children can tolerate:

  • osmotic laxatives e.g. lactulose or
  • Bulk - forming laxatives e.g. methyl cellulose.

Enemas and suppositories may be given to clear the mass. This may be done under heavy sedation or anesthesia (for manual evacuation).

‚ö° Antispasmodic Drugs

Definition

Antispasmodic drugs are medications used to relieve spasms of involuntary muscle. Primarily used to treat conditions involving muscle spasms, such as irritable bowel syndrome (IBS), and other gastrointestinal or urinary disorders.

üü¢ Anticholinergic Antispasmodics

Mechanism of Action: Block acetylcholine receptors, reducing muscle spasms.

Examples:

  • Atropine
  • Hyoscyamine
  • Dicyclomine
  • Oxybutynin

üîµ Smooth Muscle Relaxants

Mechanism of Action: Directly act on smooth muscles to relax them.

Examples:

  • Mebeverine
  • Alverine
  • Papaverine

Clinical Details

A) Anticholinergic Antispasmodics

  • Atropine: Use: Pre-anesthetic to reduce salivation, treat bradycardia. Side Effects: Dry mouth, blurred vision, urinary retention.
  • Hyoscyamine: Use: IBS, peptic ulcer. Side Effects: Similar to atropine.

B) Smooth Muscle Relaxants

  • Mebeverine: Uses: IBS. Side Effects: Dizziness, heartburn.
  • Papaverine: Use: Vasospasms. Side Effects: Hypotension, tachycardia.
Class Site of Action Side Effect Profile
Anticholinergic Antispasmodics Acts on nervous system More systemic side effects
Smooth Muscle Relaxants Direct action on muscles Fewer systemic side effects

Contraindications: Glaucoma, urinary retention, myasthenia gravis.

ü©π Ulcer Healing Drugs

Classified into:

  • H2 receptor antagonist
  • Proton pump inhibitors
  • Antimuscarinics
  • Chelates
  • Prostaglandin analogues

H2 Receptor Antagonists

Examples: cimetidine, famotidine, Ranitidine, Nizatidine

H2 receptors are found in the gut, increase gastric acid secretion.

üîÑ

Mechanism of Action

Production of HCL is mediated by histamine, gastrin hormone and acetylcholine. Histamine is necessary for the action of gastrin and acetylcholine in production of Hcl. H2 receptor antagonist reduce gastric acid output by blocking H2 receptors. This allows for healing of gastric and duodenal ulcers.

Cimetidine (Tagamet)

  • Readily absorbed from GIT
  • Reduces HCL secretion by 70% for 4-5 hours
  • Dosage: 400mg BD PO adult; 25-30 mg/kg daily in divided doses children over 1 year.

Side effects: GIT upset, headache, mental confusion, thrombocytopenia, gynecomastia, impotence, loss of libido.

‚ö°

Drug Interactions

Cimetidine increases metabolism of warfarin, theophylline, phenytoin. Decreases absorption of ketoconazole and itraconazole.

Proton Pump Inhibitors (PPIs)

Examples: omeprazole, Lansoprazole, Rabeprazole

  • Given orally as enteric coated tablets due to gastric instability
  • Duration of action is 2-3 days
  • Inhibits the H, K, ATPase proton pump of the gastric cells. These blocks the transport of acid from the cell into the lumen.
  • Usually used for short term treatment (4-8 weeks)

Dosage: omeprazole 20 mg BD or 40 mg od 7/7. Children >2 years 0.7-1.4 mg/kg daily 4-12 weeks.

Used with antibiotics to eliminate Helicobacter pylori (metronidazole, Clarithromycin, Amoxycillin).

Other Agents

Chelates (Bismuth)

Acts by direct toxicity to bacteria, stimulates mucosal prostaglandin, absorbs pepsin, inhibits H. pylori proteolytic enzymes.

Side effects: Black faeces, nausea and vomiting, darkened tongue.

Antimuscarinics

Examples: propantheline, scopolamine. Decrease acetylcholine stimulated secretion and motility of GIT, hence reduce acid secretion.

Prostaglandin Analogues

Exogenous prostaglandins. Act on the parietal cells to inhibit gastric acid secretion, stimulate bicarbonate and mucus production.

Misoprostol

Synthetic prostaglandin with antisecretory and cytoprotective properties.

Indications: NSAID peptic ulcers; Prophylaxis for people on long term NSAIDS.

üö´

Contraindication

Pregnancy or those planning pregnancy increases uterine contraction hence increasing chances of abortion.

ü§¢ Antiemetic Drugs

Introduction

Vomiting is a complex reflex and protective mechanism that promotes the rejection of ingested toxins but may be stimulated by other factors including like fear, pain, movement, pregnancy and drugs. There are 4 receptors that have been identified as being involved in mediating the emetic response namely:

D2
Dopamine
H1
Histamine
5HT3
Serotonin
M1
Muscarinic

Classes of Antiemetics

1. Acetylcholine Receptor Antagonist

Examples: Scopolamine, hyoscine

  • Pharmacodynamic: Blockages of muscarinic type one receptors in the vestibular system.
  • Indications: Prophylactic treatment for motion sickness; Preanaestetic agent (depress salivation and respiratory secretions).
  • Unwanted effects: Blurred vision, decreased sweating, constipation, dry mouth.

2. Antihistaminic Antiemetics

Examples: Cyclizine, Promethazine, Mechizine, Hydroxyzine, Buclizine

  • Pharmacodynamics: Block the action of histamine on H1 receptors. Most antihistamines group of drugs are not specific and also blocks acetylcholine receptors. This makes them effective anti emetics.
  • Indications: Motion sickness, Post-operations, Labyrinthine disorders.
  • Note: Should be taken 30 minutes before travel.

3. Dopamine Receptor Antagonist

Examples: Chlorpromazine, Domperidone, Metoclopramide, Haloperidol, Levomepromazine

  • Pharmacodynamics: Blocks the effect of dopamine on the chemoreceptor trigger zone (Dopamine antagonist).
  • Indications: Reduce vomiting caused by cancer chemotherapy and radiation therapy; Control of post operative vomiting. (NB; do not prevent motion sickness).
  • Unwanted effects: Extrapyramidal effects.

4. Serotonin (5HT3) Receptor Antagonist

Examples: Granisetron, Ondansetron, Tropisetron, Dolasetron

  • Pharmacodynamics: Blocks the 5HT receptors associated with the central connections of the vagus nerve in the brainstem in close proximity to the Chemoreceptor Trigger Zone.
  • Indications: Used in patients receiving cancer chemotherapy; Prevention of postop nausea and vomiting; Prevention of radiation induced nausea and vomiting.
