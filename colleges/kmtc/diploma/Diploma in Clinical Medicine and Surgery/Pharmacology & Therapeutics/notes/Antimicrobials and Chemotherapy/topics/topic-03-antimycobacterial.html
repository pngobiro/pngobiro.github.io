Antimycobacterial Drugs

üë§ Lecturer: mutiso üìÖ 14/12/2025
01

Introduction

Mycobacteria are intrinsically resistant to most antibiotics. Reasons include:

  1. 1 They grow slowly compared with other bacteria- antibiotics that are most active against growing cells are relatively ineffective
  2. 2 Mycobacterial cells can also be dormant and thus completely resistant to many drugs or killed only very slowly
  3. 3 The lipid-rich mycobacterial cell wall is impermeable to many agents
  4. 4 Mycobacterial species are intracellular pathogens, and organisms residing within macrophages are inaccessible to drugs that penetrate these cells poorly
  5. 5 Mycobacteria are notorious for their ability to develop resistance
  • Combinations of two or more drugs are required to overcome these obstacles and to prevent emergence of resistance during the course of therapy
  • Response of mycobacterial infections to chemotherapy is slow, and treatment must be administered for months
02

Drug Classification

They are classified into 1st line and 2nd line therapy. First-line agents are superior in efficacy and possess an acceptable degree of toxicity.

1st line therapy

Isoniazid and rifampin are the two most active drugs.

  • Isoniazid (INH)
  • Rifampin (or other rifamycin)
  • Pyrazinamide
  • Ethambutol
  • Streptomycin

2nd line therapy

They are more toxic and less effective. Are usually considered only In case of:

  1. 1 Resistance to first-line agents
  2. 2 Failure of clinical response to conventional therapy
  3. 3 Serious treatment-limiting adverse drug reactions
  4. 4 When expert guidance is available to deal with the toxic effects
  • Ethionamide
  • Capreomycin
  • Cycloserine
  • Aminosalicylic Acid (PAS)
  • Kanamycin & Amikacin
  • Fluoroquinolones
  • Thiacetazone
  • Rifabutin
  • Rifapentine
03

Standard Treatment Regimen

In practice, therapy is initiated with a four-drug regimen of isoniazid, rifampin, pyrazinamide, and either ethambutol or streptomycin.

Initial Phase (Intensive Phase)

  • 2RHZE or 2RHZE + S (Streptomycin)
  • Daily treatment with appropriate patient support for two months with: Streptomycin (S) + Ethambutol (E) + Rifampicin (R) + Isoniazid (H) + Pyrazinamide (Z)

Continuation Phase

  • 4RH or 4RHE
  • Daily treatment with appropriate patient support for five months with Ethambutol (E) + Rifampicin (R) + Isoniazid (H)
üìù Regimen Abbreviation

The whole regime is often abbreviated as: 2SRHZE/1RHZE/5RHE (For both Intensive and continuation phases in retreatment scenarios).

04

Treatment of TB in Pregnancy

Like most drugs anti-TB drugs have not been specifically studied in pregnancy. There is always some risk of teratogenicity with any drugs especially when the drug is given in the first trimester. In general, pregnancy should be avoided during anti TB treatment.

  • TB should be treated with a full complement of anti-TB drugs
  • Useful to give Pyridoxine with Isoniazid to avoid the small risk of damaging the infant's nervous system
  • Streptomycin should not be used in pregnancy because it may cause deafness in the infant
  • Aminoglycosides used in treatment of drug resistant TB like: Kanamycin, Amikacin and Capreomycin and the thioamides: Ethionamide and Prothionamide should not be used in pregnancy because of associated ototoxity
05

Drug Resistant TB

Classification based on exposure

  • Primary resistance- if there was definitely no previous treatment.
  • Initial resistance-when previous treatment cannot definitely be excluded.
  • Acquired resistance-if there is a definite history of previous treatment

Based on the type of resistance expressed by the TB bacilli

  1. 1 Mono-resistant TB- TB which is resistant to one first line anti -TB Drug.
  2. 2 Poly resistant TB- TB which is resistant to more than one anti-TB drug except both Rifampicin and Isoniazid.
  3. 3 Multi-Drug Resistant (MDR) TB- resistance to both Rifampicin and Isoniazid
  4. 4 Extensively drug Resistant (XDR) TB- is MDR-TB (i.e further to being resistant to Rifampicin and Isoniazid) it is also resistant to any fluoroquinolone and one or more of the following injectable drugs: Kanamycin, Amikacin and Capreomycin
06

Drug Therapy of Resistant TB

Mono-resistant TB

When there is resistance to any one only one first line antituberculosis drug. Only the offending drug should be substituted:

  • Ethambutol replaces Isoniazid
  • Fluoroquinolone replaces Rifampicin

Poly-resistant TB

When there is resistance to two or more first line anti TB drugs other than both Rifampicin and Isoniazid. The two offending drugs should be replaced.

MDR TB

  • This is mycobacteria that is resistant to both Isoniazid and Rifampicin
  • Treatment is in two phases: Intensive phase lasts 6 months; Continuation phase lasts for 18 months

07

RIFAMPIN

MOA: Binds to the beta subunit of bacterial DNA-dependent RNA polymerase and thereby inhibits RNA synthesis.

PK

  • Well absorbed after oral administration
  • Excreted mainly through the liver into bile
  • Undergoes enterohepatic circulation
  • Bulk is excreted as a deacylated metabolite in feces and a small amount in the urine
  • Dosage adjustment for renal or hepatic insufficiency is not necessary.
  • Distributed widely in body fluids and tissues. Relatively highly protein-bound.
  • Adequate CSF concentrations achieved only in the presence of meningeal inflammation

Clinical uses

  1. 1 Mycobacterial infections: rifampin, $600 \mathrm{mg} / \mathrm{d}(10 \mathrm{mg} / \mathrm{kg} / \mathrm{d})$ orally, coadministered with isoniazid to avoid emergence of resistance
  2. 2 An oral dosage of 600 mg twice daily for 2 days can eliminate meningococcal carriage
  3. 3 $20 \mathrm{mg} / \mathrm{kg} / \mathrm{d}$ for 4 days, is used as prophylaxis in contacts of children with Haemophilus influenzae
  4. 4 Combined with a second agent it is used to eradicate staphylococcal carriage
  5. 5 Rifampin combination therapy is also indicated for treatment of serious staphylococcal infections such as osteomyelitis and prosthetic valve endocarditis

ADR

  • Imparts a harmless orange color to urine, sweat, tears, and contact lenses (soft lenses may be permanently stained)
  • Occasional may cause rashes, thrombocytopenia, and nephritis
  • May also cause cholestatic jaundice and occasionally hepatitis
  • Commonly causes light-chain proteinuria
  • If administered less often than twice weekly, it causes a flu-like syndrome characterized by fever, chills, myalgias, anemia, and thrombocytopenia and sometimes is associated with acute tubular necrosis

Drug interaction

Induces most cytochrome P450 isoforms hence increases the elimination of other drugs including: methadone, anticoagulants, cyclosporine, some anticonvulsants, protease inhibitors, Non-nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitors etc. Administration of rifampin results in significantly lower serum levels of these drugs

08

ISONIAZID

  • A synthetic agent with a structural similarity to that of pyridoxine
  • MOA: Inhibits synthesis of mycolic acids (fatty acids), which are essential components of mycobacterial cell walls
  • Resistance: Is associated with mutations

PK

  • Readily absorbed from the gastrointestinal tract
  • A 300-mg oral dose (5 mg/kg in children) achieves peak plasma concentrations of 3-5 mcg/mL within 1-2 hours
  • It diffuses readily into all body fluids and tissues
  • Metabolism: Is by acetylation by liver N-acetyltransferase and is genetically determined; some individuals are slow acetylators while others are rapid acetylators
  • Metabolites and a small amount of unchanged drug are excreted mainly in the urine

Adverse Reactions

The incidence and severity ADRs are related to dosage and duration of Rx.

A. IMMUNOLOGIC REACTIONS

Fever and skin rashes; Drug-induced systemic lupus erythematosus

B. DIRECT TOXICITY

  • Isoniazid-induced hepatitis- risk increase with age, is rare in patients below 20 yrs. Clinical hepatitis with loss of appetite, nausea, vomiting, jaundice, and right upper quadrant pain occurs. Risk of hepatitis is greater in alcoholics, during pregnancy and the postpartum period.
  • Peripheral neuropathy -observed in 10-20% of patients- occur more in slow acetylators and patients with predisposing conditions. Occur due to a relative pyridoxine deficiency. Isoniazid promotes excretion of pyridoxine.
  • Central nervous system toxicity: memory loss, psychosis, and seizures.
  • Hematologic abnormalities, provocation of pyridoxine deficiency anemia, Tinnitus, Gastrointestinal discomfort.

Drug interaction

Isoniazid can reduce the metabolism of phenytoin, increasing its blood level and toxicity

09

ETHAMBUTOL

MOA: Inhibits mycobacterial arabinosyl transferases an enzyme involved in the polymerization reaction of arabinoglycan, an essential component of the mycobacterial cell wall.

PK

  • Well absorbed from the gut; blood level peak is reached in 2-4 hours
  • Excretion: About 20% excreted in feces and $50 \%$ in urine in unchanged form
  • The drug accumulates in renal failure and dose should be reduced by half if creatinine clearance is less than 10 $\mathrm{mL} / \mathrm{min}$
  • Ethambutol crosses the blood-brain barrier only if the meninges are inflamed

Clinical uses

  • Usually given as a single daily dose in combination with isoniazid or rifampin
  • A higher dose is recommended for treatment of tuberculous meningitis

Adverse Reactions

  • Hypersensitivity to ethambutol is rare
  • Most common serious ADR is optic neuritis- result in loss of visual acuity and red-green color blindness- a doserelated side effect (More likely to occur at doses of 25 $\mathrm{mg} / \mathrm{kg} / \mathrm{d}$ continued for several months)
  • Periodic visual acuity testing is desirable if the 25 mg/kg/d dosage is used
  • Relatively contraindicated in children too young to permit assessment of visual acuity and red-green color discrimination
10

PYRAZINAMIDE

  • Stable, and slightly soluble in water. Inactive at neutral pH, Inhibits tubercle bacilli and some other mycobacteria at pH 5.5
  • The drug is taken up by macrophages and exerts its activity against mycobacteria residing within the acidic environment of lysosomes
  • It is converted to pyrazinoic acid the active form of the drug by mycobacterial pyrazinamidase

PK

  • Well absorbed from the gastrointestinal tract
  • Widely distributed in body tissues, including inflamed meninges
  • The half-life is 8-11 hours
  • The parent compound is metabolized by the liver, metabolites are renally cleared

Adverse Reactions

  • Hepatotoxicity
  • Nausea, Vomiting, Drug fever
  • Hyperuricemia -may provoke acute gouty arthritis
11

STREPTOMYCIN

  • An aminoglycoside
  • Penetrates into cells poorly and is active mainly against extracellular tubercle bacilli
  • Streptomycin crosses the blood-brain barrier and achieves therapeutic concentrations with inflamed meninges

Clinical use

Used when an injectable drug is needed or desirable, principally in individuals with severe, possibly life-threatening forms of tuberculosis, e.g. meningitis and disseminated disease, and in treatment of infections resistant to other drugs

12

Retreatment

For patients who have previously been treated for TB for more than one month including those who:

  • Have relapsed after successful previous treatment, or
  • Those who defaulted from previous treatment or
  • Failed previous treatment, with sputum smears remaining positive at 4/5, 6/8 month
