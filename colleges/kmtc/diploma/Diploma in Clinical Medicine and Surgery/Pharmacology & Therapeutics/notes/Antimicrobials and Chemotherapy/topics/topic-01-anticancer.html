Anticancer Drugs

01

Learning Objectives

  • By the end of this lesson the student should be able to:
  • Describe the pathogenesis of cancer
  • Describe the drugs used in cancer management and their side effects.
02

Introduction to anti-cancers

  • Cancer is uncontrolled multiplication and spread of abnormal cells of the body.
  • Second common cause of death in the world.
  • In developed world lung and colon cancers are the leading then followed by breast and prostate cancers.
  • Benign and cancer are both uncontrolled multiplication of the cells however, the latter is invasive and spreads to other parts of the body.
  • Inherited and acquired gene mutations is linked to tumorgenesis
  • Cancer is treated by use of 3 modalities namely: surgical excision, irradiation and drug therapy (chemotherapy)
03

Pathogenesis of cancer

Cancer have four characteristics that distinguish them from the normal cells. Which are:

  1. Uncontrolled proliferation
  2. De-differentiation and loss of function
  3. Invasiveness
  4. Metastasis
  • Cancers cells start to form due to exposure to viruses or carcinogens (tobacco, asbestos) which causes DNA mutations.
  • In breast cancer for example, women inherit a defective copy of suppressor gene BRCA1 & BRCA2.
  • Exposure to carcinogens for a long time, increase the like hood of developing genetic mutation that results to cancer.
  • There is activation of proto-oncogenes to oncogenes (proto-oncogenes are genes that control cell division, apoptosis and differentiation).

Genesis of Cancer

  • Chemicals, viruses, irradiation, etc.
  • Acquired mutations
  • Inherited mutations
  • Altered gene expression
  • Proto-oncogenes
    • Oncogenes sis, erbB, ras, myc, gene for cyclin D, etc.
  • Decreased expression of tumour suppressor genes: p53, Rb1, etc.
  • Other factors
  • Uncontrolled cell proliferation, de-differentiation
  • Decreased apoptosis, alterations in telomerase
  • Development of primary tumour
  • Production of metalloproteinases etc.
  • Invasion of nearby tissue by tumour cells
  • Angiogenesis
  • Metastasis
  • Development of secondary tumours

Cellular Mechanisms

  • There is also inactivation of tumor suppressor genes (antioncogenes) and mutations which is caused by viral infections or chemical carcinogens.
  • Common gene mutations are; point mutations, gene amplification or chromosomal dislocations.
  • Cancer cells escape the mechanism that regulate cell division and tissue growth. This causes uncontrolled proliferation.
  • In cancerous cells there is also resistance to apoptosis (programmed cell death).
  • Cancer cells de-differentiate this means that cancer cells have no function they perform in the body.
04

General effects of cancer therapy

  1. Bone marrow toxicity (bone marrow suppression)
  2. Impaired wound healing
  3. Loss of hair (alopecia)
  4. Depression of growth in children
  5. Damage the gastrointestinal epithelium
  6. Sterility
  7. Severe nausea and vomiting
05

Classes of cancer therapy

  1. Cytotoxic drugs e.g. alkylating agents (imped replication), antimetabolites, cytotoxic antibiotics, plant derivatives (vinca alkaloids)
  2. Hormones e.g. glucocorticoids, estrogen
  3. Protein kinase inhibitors
  4. Monoclonal antibodies
06

1. Alkylating Agents

  • These drugs form covalent bond with nucleophilic substance e.g. DNA
  • They form carbonium ion which is highly reactive with hydroxyl, amine or sulfhydryl.
  • Insertion of alkyl group forms cross-links between the strands preventing separation which is necessary for replication.
  • All alkylating agents causes bone marrow depression, sterility especially men, git disturbances, and hair loss.
  • Examples are; nitrogen mustards, nitrosoureas, platinum compounds
  • Procarbazine inhibits DNA & RNA synthesis
  • Others are; hydroxycarbamide, decarbazine, temozolomide

Platinum compounds

  • Platinum compounds: cisplatin, carboplastin
  • Cisplastin has a central platinum atom surrounded by 2 chlorine atoms and 2 ammonia groups.
  • When dissolves in water, it dissociates into $\mathrm{Cl}^{-}$ which interacts with DNA.
  • Cisplastin is used to treat solid tumors of the ovary and testes.
  • Given slow intravenous or as an infusion.
  • Side effects: nephrotoxicity, myelotoxicity, severe nausea and vomiting, peripheral neuropathy, tinnitus, hearing loss, hyperuricemia and anaphylactic reactions.

Nitrogen mustards

  • Nitrogen mustards form ethylene immonium highly reactive compound which interacts with DNA.
  • Example of nitrogen mustards is cyclophosphamide which is metabolized in the liver by P450 into active form.
  • Cyclophosphamide is given orally or intravenously. It causes nausea and vomiting, bone marrow suppression and hemorrhagic cystitis.
  • Other examples of nitrogen mustards are; bendramustine, chlorambucil, and melphan.

Nitrosoureas

  • Nitrosoureas- this include lomustine and carmustine.
  • They are lipid soluble and cross the blood-brain barrier hence used to treat the tumors of the brain and meninges.
07

2. Antimetabolites

Folate antagonist

  • Folate antagonist e.g. methotrexate
  • Folate is essential in formation of purine nucleotide and thymidyalte which form the DNA.
  • Folate antagonist interfere with thymidylate synthesis.
  • Methotrexate block the enzyme dehydrofolate reductase required in formation of tetrahydrofolate (FH4) required for purine and DNA synthesis.
  • Methotrexate is given orally, intravenously or intramuscularly.
  • It is taken up by the cells and transported by folate transport system and is metabolized to polyglutamate derivatives.
  • Methotrexate is also used in treatment of psoriasis, rheumatoid arthritis and other autoimmune diseases.
  • Methotrexate adverse effects are; bone marrow suppression, nephrotoxicity, pneuomonitis, damage to GIT epithelium.

Pyridine & Purine analogues

  • Pyridine analogues e.g. - fluorouracil which inhibits DNA synthesis and is given parenterally and it causes myelotoxicity and GIT epithelial damage.
  • Other drugs are; capecitabine, tegafur, cytarabine (cytosine arabinoside), gemicitabine, azacitidine and decitabine.
  • Purine analogues are; cladribine, clofarabrine, fludarabine, pentostatin, captopurine,
08

3. Cytotoxic Antibiotics

Have direct action on DNA and should not be given together with radiotherapy because of high toxicity. Examples; doxorubicin, idarubicin, daunorubicin, epirubicin and mitoxantrone (derivative).

  1. Doxorubicin inhibits DNA and RNA synthesis through inhibition of topoisomerase II.
    • Doxorubicin is given through infusion and its side effects are; extravasation causes necrosis, cardiac damage, heart failure.
  2. Dactinomycin- inserts between guanosine-cytosine pairs interfering with RNA polymerase from transcription. Used to treat pediatric cancers.
  3. Bleomycins- metal-chelating glycoprotein antibiotics that degrades preformed DNA. Chelating occurs through ferrous iron and interaction with oxygen forming superoxide radicals. Used to treat germline cancers. Its less toxic to bone marrow however other serious side effects are; pulmonary fibrosis and allergic reactions.
  4. Mitomycin- functions as bifunctional alkylating agent at guanine nucleus. It degrades DNA by formation of free radicals. It causes kidney damage, lung fibrosis and myelosuppression.
09

4. Plant Derivatives

  1. Vinca alkaloids- vincristine, vinblastine, vindesine, vinflumine (fluorinated) and vinorelbine (semisynthetic vinca alkaloids).
    • MOA; they bind the tubulin preventing polymerization into microtubules therefore, it inhibits spindle fibers formation and arrest of metaphase.
    • They also inhibits other cellular activities that require microtubules like leucocyte phagocytosis.
    • Adverse effects; vincristine causes mild myelosuppression, neurotoxic, paraesthesia, abdominal pain and weakness.
    • Vinblastine is less neurotoxic, causes leuocopenia, reversible hair loss
  2. Paclitaxel and related compounds e.g. paclitaxel, docetaxel and cabazitaxel.
    • Derived from naturally occurring pacific yew tree (Taxus spp).
    • MOA: interferes with microtubules function and are given intravenous infusion. Used to treat breast and lung cancer.
    • Carboplastin and paclitaxel are used in combination to treat ovarian cancer.
    • Side effects: bone marrow suppression, neurotoxicity, hypersensitivity reaction and fluid retention causing edema.
  3. Camptothecins e.g. irinotecan and tiopotecan are derived from camptotheca acuminata tree. They interfere with topoisomerase I. They have less side effects like reversible bone marrow suppression and diarrhea.
  4. Etoposide- inhibits mitochondrion function, nucleoside transport and topoisomerase II. Causes nausea and vomiting, myelosuppression and hair loss.
10

5. Hormones

  • Some tumors originate from hormone sensitive tissues like the breast, prostate, uterus and ovaries.
  • These tumors are hormone dependent because they have hormone receptors in there cells.
  • Treatment targets hormone agonist, antagonist or inhibition of hormone synthesis.
  1. Glucocorticoids- prednisolone is used in treatment of lymphomas and leukemia. It inhibits lymphocyte proliferation. Dexamethasone is used to treat brain tumors
  2. Oestrogen- Diethylstilbestrol occasionally used in treatment of androgen dependent prostatic tumors.
  3. Progestogens- megestrol, medroxyprogesterone and norehisterone are used in treatment of endometrial cancer.
  4. Gonadotropin releasing hormone analogues e.g. goserelin, buserelin, triptorelin used in treatment of breast cancer and prostate cancer. Degaralix is gonadotropin releasing hormone antagonist used to treat prostate cancer.
  5. Somatostatin analogues- octreotide and lanreotide treat neuroendocrine tumors e,g glucagonomas, carcinoid tumors, gastrinomas
11

6. Hormones Antagonist

  • Antiestrogens e.g. tamoxifen, toremifene which block estrogen receptors in tumors which are hormone-dependent like breast cancer.
    • Unwanted effects are; endometrial cancer, thromboembolism.
  • Aromatase inhibitors block estrogen formation from androgens in the adrenal cortex examples are; anastrozole, letrozole and exemestane.
  • Antiandrogen- examples flutamide, cyproterone and bicalutamide may be combine with other agents to treat prostate cancer.
üí° Summary of anti-cancer agents: Hormones
  • Hormones or their antagonists are used in hormone sensitive tumors.
  • Glucocorticoids- leukemia and lymphomas
  • Tamoxifen- breast tumors
  • Gonadotropin releasing hormone analogue- prostate and breast tumors.
  • Antiandrogens- prostate cancer
  • Aromatase inhibitors- postmenopausal breast cancer.
12

7. Monoclonal Antibodies

  • Example are; rituximab used with other agents to treat Blymphomas. Given through infusion and can cause hypotension, rashes, fever
  • Trastuzumab-bind to oncogene protein slowing proliferation. Used in treatment of breast cancer.
  • Bevacizumab- neutralize vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) used to treat colorectal cancer.
  • Protein kinase inhibitors e.g. imatinib
‚ú® Summary
  • Monoclonal antibodies inhibit tumor cells proliferation and growth.
  • For example trastuzumab neutralizes the growth factors of some tumor cells.
  • Protein kinase inhibitors prevent down-stream signaling triggered by growth factors e.g. imatinib.
  • Some monoclonal acts directly on lymphocyte cells causing lysis e.g. rituximab.
13

Mechanisms of Anti-cancer Drugs Resistance

  1. Decreased accumulation of the drug in the cells.
  2. Decrease in drug taken up by the cells e.g. methotrexate
  3. Insufficiency activation of the drug e.g. fluorouracil should be converted to FDUMP.
  4. Increase in inactivation of the drug e.g. cytarabine
  5. Increased concentration of the target enzyme
  6. Decreased requirement of the substrate
  7. Increased utilization of alternative metabolic pathways
  8. Rapid repair of drug-induced DNA damage e.g. alkylating agents
  9. Altered activity of target e.g. modified topoisomerase II (Doxorubicin)
  10. Mutation of various genes
