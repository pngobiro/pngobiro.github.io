Introduction

Common protozoan infections include:

  • Malaria
  • Amoebiasis
  • Leishmaniasis
  • Trypanosomiasis
  • Trichomoniasis
  • Giardiasis
02

ANTIMALARIALS

A protozoan infection caused by plasmodium. Classified into two as either uncomplicated or severe malaria. Management is based on the classification.

Life cycle of the plasmodium

  • 1 Mosquito inject sporozoites into the blood while feeding
  • 2 Sporozoites are carried by the circulatory system to the liver and invade hepatocytes
  • 3 An asexual replication known as exoerythrocytic schizogony begin within the hepatocyte culminates in the production of merozoites which are released into the bloodstream
  • 4 Merozoites invade erythrocytes & begin to enlarge as a unnucleated cell called the ring trophozoite
  • 5 Ends with rounds of nuclear division resulting in formation of schizont

The erythrocyte ruptures & release toxins that bring about the cycle of fever & chills that is characteristic of malaria. Merozoites bud from the mature schizont, and are released following rupture of the infected erythrocyte (can re-infect more erythrocytes). The blood stage is responsible for the pathology associated with malaria.

Sexual Phase

Plasmodium enter the sexual phase when some merozoites in the erythrocyte develop into gametocytes. Erythrocytes containing gametes do not rupture. Gametocytes are incapable of producing gametes within their human host, they only do so when extracted from an infected human host by a mosquito.

03

Drug Classification

  • Tissue schizonticides: Drugs that eliminate developing or dormant liver forms
  • Blood schizonticides: Those that act on erythrocytic parasites
  • Gametocides: Those that kill the sexual stage and prevent transmission to the mosquito
  • Causal prophylactic drugs: capable of preventing erythrocytic infection

No one agent can reliably effect a radical cure, ie, eliminate both hepatic and erythrocytic stages.

Structural Classification

  • 4-aminoquinolones (chloroquine, amodiaquine)
  • 8-aminoquinololes (primaquine)
  • Quinoline methanol (quinine, quinidine, mefloquine)
  • Folate antagonists (sulfadoxine pyrimethamine, chlorproguanil, proguanil)
  • Amyl alcohol (lumefantrine)
  • Tetracyclines (doxycyline)
  • Others (artemisinin, halofantrine)
04

Quinine

Clinical Use: Treatment of multidrug-resistant malaria.

Mechanism of Action: Complexes with double stranded DNA to prevent strand separation, resulting in block of DNA replication and transcription to RNA. Quinine is only a blood schizonticide.

ADRs

Cinchonism symptoms include: Gastrointestinal distress, Headache, Vertigo, Blurred vision, and Tinnitus. Severe overdose results in disturbances in cardiac conduction.

Hematotoxic effects: hemolysis in glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PD)-deficient patients.

05

Antifolate Drugs

Includes Pyrimethamine, Proguanil, Sulfadoxine, Dapsone. All are absorbed orally and excreted in the urine.

  • Sulfonamides: act as antimetabolites of PABA and block folic acid synthesis by inhibiting dihydropteroate synthase.
  • Pyrimethamine and cycloguanil: selective inhibitors of protozoan dihydrofolate reductases.
  • Combination of pyrimethamine with sulfadoxine has synergistic effects through the sequential blockade of 2 steps in folic acid synthesis.

Clinical Use

  • Fansidar (Pyrimethamine + sulfadoxine): used in the treatment of chloroquine-resistant forms of P. falciparum.
  • Malarone (Proguanil + atovaquone): can be used for chemoprophylaxis of chloroquine-resistant malaria.
06

Artesunate, Artemether, Dihydroartemisinin

These artemisinin derivatives are metabolized in the food vacuole of the parasite forming toxic free radicals. They are blood schizonticides active against P. falciparum, including multidrug-resistant strains.

  • Clinical Uses: Treatment of malaria (best in combination). Not used for chemoprophylaxis because of short half-lives (1-3 h).
  • Adverse Effects: GIT disturbances. Safety in pregnancy not established but can be used.

08

Management of Severe Malaria

Recommended treatment is parenteral artesunate (IV preferred). Quinine is used in absence of artesunate.

Order of Preference

  1. 1 IM/IV Artesunate
  2. 2 Rectal Artesunate (suppositories)
  3. 3 IM/IV Quinine
  4. 4 IM Artemether
Artesunate Protocol 
Dissolve powder with 5% sodium bicarbonate.
Dilute with 5mls normal saline or 5% dextrose.
Administer 2.4 mg/kg stat by slow IV.
Repeat 2.4 mg/kg at 12 hrs and 24 hrs.
Thereafter 2.4 mg/kg daily until patient can take orally.
Then give complete course of AL.

Quinine Administration

  • Loading Dose: $20 \mathrm{mg} / \mathrm{kg}$ in 5% dextrose running over 4 hours. Omit if quinine given in previous 24 hours.
  • Maintenance: 8 hours from start, give $10 \mathrm{mg} / \mathrm{kg}$ every 8 hours until patient can take oral medication.
  • Follow up: Full course of AL or oral quinine ($10 \mathrm{mg} / \mathrm{kg}$ 8 hourly for 7 days total).
09

Treatment in Pregnancy

Uncomplicated Malaria

  • First Trimester: Oral Quinine (7 days). Avoid AL unless quinine not available.
  • 2nd & 3rd Trimester: AL (Artemether + Lumefantrine).

Severe Malaria

IV Artesunate or IV Quinine. Rx should be started in the facility where mother presents.

10

Chemotherapy for Amoebiasis

Classification

  • Mixed Agents: Metronidazole, Tinidazole (Effective against luminal and systemic forms).
  • Luminal Amoebicides: Diloxanide furoate, Tetracycline, Paromomycin (Act on parasites in bowel lumen).
  • Systemic Amoebicides: Emetine, Dehydroemetine, Chloroquine (Effective against parasites in systems).

Metronidazole

Agent of choice for Entamoeba histolytica, Giardia lamblia, and Trichomonas vaginalis. Also used for anaerobic bacteria.

  • PK: Rapidly absorbed, well distributed (CSF, saliva). Metabolized in liver.
  • ADRs: Metallic taste, GIT disturbances, Neurotoxicity, Disulfiram-like reaction with alcohol.
11

Leishmaniasis

Sodium Stibogluconate

  • MOA: Inhibits glycolysis in the parasite.
  • Administered parenterally (not absorbed orally).
  • ADRs: Pain at injection site, Cardiac arrhythmias, Pancreatitis.
12

Trypanosomiasis

Types: African (Sleeping sickness) and American (Chagas).

Drugs

  • Pentamidine: Alternative to suramin for early hemolymphatic stage. Does not cross BBB. ADRs: Hypotension, Hypoglycemia.
  • Suramin: First-line for early hemolymphatic African trypanosomiasis. Does not enter CNS. Tightly bound to protein.
  • Melarsoprol: First-line for advanced CNS African trypanosomiasis. Extremely toxic (Reactive encephalopathy).
  • Nifurtimox: Commonly used for American trypanosomiasis (Chagas' disease).
