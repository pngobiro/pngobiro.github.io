üî• Peptic Ulcer Disease (PUD)

Treatment aims to relieve symptoms, induce healing, prevent complications, and stop recurrence. Agents include acid suppressors, mucosal protectants, and antibiotics for H. pylori.

1. Acid Secretion Inhibitors

  • H2 Receptor Antagonists: Cimetidine, Ranitidine, Famotidine.
    Mechanism: Selectively block H2 receptors in parietal cells.
    Notes: Cimetidine inhibits CYP enzymes (increases levels of warfarin, phenytoin, theophylline) and has anti-androgenic effects (impotence, gynecomastia). Ranitidine has fewer interactions.
  • Proton Pump Inhibitors (PPIs): Omeprazole, Lansoprazole, Esomeprazole, Pantoprazole.
    Mechanism: Irreversibly inhibit H+/K+ ATPase (proton pump). Most effective.
    Adverse Effects: Increased risk of pneumonia (decreased gastric acidity), bone loss/osteoporosis with long-term use.
    Interaction: Omeprazole can increase Digoxin levels and decrease absorption of Ketoconazole.
  • Anticholinergics: Pirenzepine (Selective M1 blocker). Reduces acid secretion by 40-50%.

2. Mucosal Protective Agents

  • Sucralfate (Carafate): Changes into a thick substance that adheres to the ulcer crater for up to 6 hours. Needs acidic environment (do not give with antacids/H2 blockers).
  • Colloidal Bismuth Compounds: Binds to ulcer, inhibits pepsin, and has activity against H. pylori. Causes black tongue/stool.
  • Prostaglandin Analogues (Misoprostol): Increases mucus/bicarbonate secretion. Indicated for NSAID-induced ulcers. Contraindication: Pregnancy (Category X, induces labor).

H. Pylori Eradication Regimens

Standard therapy involves combination antibiotics for 7-14 days to prevent resistance.

Triple Therapy (1-2 weeks):
Clarithromycin + Amoxicillin (or Metronidazole) + PPI (e.g., Omeprazole)

Quadruple Therapy:
Tetracycline + Metronidazole + Bismuth Subsalicylate + PPI

ü§¢ Anti-Emetics

Used to suppress vomiting caused by surgery, chemotherapy, motion sickness, or pregnancy.

Class Examples Indication & Notes
Prokinetics (D2 Blockers) Metoclopramide, Domperidone Post-op, Chemotherapy. SE: Extrapyramidal symptoms (dystonia), Galactorrhea. Domperidone does not cross BBB (less SE).
5-HT3 Antagonists Ondansetron Chemotherapy-induced nausea/vomiting. Very effective.
Antimuscarinics Scopolamine (Hyoscine) Motion sickness prophylaxis. SE: Dry mouth, blurred vision.
H1 Antagonists Promethazine, Doxylamine Motion sickness, Morning sickness. SE: Sedation.
Adjuvants Dexamethasone, Cannabinoids (THC) Chemotherapy-induced emesis refractory to other drugs. Cannabinoids cause sedation/hallucinations.

üöΩ Laxatives & Anti-Diarrheals

Laxatives (Promote Defecation)

  • Bulk-Forming: Bran, Methylcellulose. Safest (mimic dietary fiber). Take with plenty of water.
  • Osmotic: Lactulose, Magnesium salts. Hold water in the gut. Lactulose is also used in hepatic encephalopathy to reduce ammonia.
  • Stimulants/Irritants: Bisacodyl, Senna, Castor Oil. Stimulate peristalsis. Can cause cramping and dependence. Avoid in pregnancy (can initiate labor).
  • Stool Softeners: Docusate, Liquid Paraffin. Emulsify stool.

Anti-Diarrheals

  • Antimotility (Opioids): Loperamide (Imodium), Diphenoxylate. Decrease peristalsis. Contraindication: Infective diarrhea (dysentery) and children <4 years.
  • Antisecretory: Racecadotril (Enkephalinase inhibitor). Reduces intestinal hypersecretion without affecting motility.
  • Probiotics: Lactobacillus. Restore normal flora (useful in antibiotic-associated diarrhea).

üíâ Diabetes: Insulin Therapy

Insulin promotes cellular uptake of glucose, converts glucose to glycogen, and moves potassium into cells. Essential for Type 1 DM and advanced Type 2 DM.

Type Examples Onset Duration Notes
Rapid-acting Lispro, Aspart < 15 min 3-4 hrs Take immediately before eating.
Short-acting Regular (Novolin R) 30-60 min 5-7 hrs Only type given IV (for DKA).
Intermediate NPH (Humulin N) 1-2 hrs 18-24 hrs Cloudy suspension. Can mix with Regular.
Long-acting Glargine (Lantus) 1 hr 24 hrs Basal insulin. No peak. Do not mix with others.
Premixed Humulin 70/30 30 min 24 hrs Mixture of NPH (70%) and Regular (30%).
‚ñ∂ Adverse Effects & Interactions

Hypoglycemia: Tremors, tachycardia (sympathetic); headache, confusion (parasympathetic). Tx: 15g Fast sugar (conscious) or IV Dextrose/Glucagon (unconscious).

Lipohypertrophy: Fat accumulation at injection site due to repeated injections. Rotate sites.

Interactions: Beta-blockers mask hypoglycemia symptoms. Thiazides/Glucocorticoids increase blood sugar (antagonize insulin).

üíä Oral Hypoglycemic Agents

Used for Type 2 Diabetes to control blood glucose along with diet and exercise.

1. Sulfonylureas (Insulin Secretagogues)

Examples: Glibenclamide, Glipizide, Glimepiride.

MOA: Stimulate pancreatic beta cells to release insulin.

Risks: Hypoglycemia, weight gain. Disulfiram-like reaction with alcohol.

2. Biguanides

Example: Metformin (Glucophage). First-line therapy.

MOA: Decreases hepatic glucose production; increases tissue sensitivity to insulin.

Adverse Effects: GI distress (weight loss), Vitamin B12 deficiency. Lactic Acidosis (rare but fatal - 50% mortality). Contraindicated in renal/hepatic failure.

3. Meglitinides

Examples: Repaglinide.

MOA: Stimulate insulin release (shorter duration than sulfonylureas). Take before meals.

4. Thiazolidinediones (Glitazones)

Examples: Rosiglitazone, Pioglitazone.

MOA: Decrease insulin resistance.

Risks: Fluid retention (edema), hepatotoxicity, elevated LDL.

5. Alpha-Glucosidase Inhibitors

Example: Acarbose.

MOA: Slows carbohydrate digestion/absorption in the gut.

SE: GI effects (gas, cramping, diarrhea). Risk of hepatotoxicity with long-term use.

6. Gliptins (DPP-4 Inhibitors)

Example: Sitagliptin.

MOA: Augments incretin hormones to promote insulin release. Well tolerated.

Pregnancy Considerations

Most oral agents are Pregnancy Category C or generally avoided in favor of Insulin during pregnancy. Metformin and Acarbose are Category B.
