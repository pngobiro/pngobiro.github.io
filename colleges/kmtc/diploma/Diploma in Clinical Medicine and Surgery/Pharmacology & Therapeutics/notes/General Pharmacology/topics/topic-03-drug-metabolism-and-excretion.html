üéØ Introduction to Drug Metabolism

Drug metabolism is the enzyme-mediated conversion of a lipophilic compound into a more water-soluble one and hence more easily excreted. This fundamental process is essential for understanding how the body handles medications and affects drug efficacy, duration of action, and potential toxicity.

üí°

Key Learning Objective

Understand the mechanisms of drug metabolism, including Phase I and Phase II reactions, factors affecting metabolism, and the importance of drug excretion pathways.

Primary Sites of Metabolism

  • Liver is the main organ of biotransformation of xenobiotics
  • The metabolism occurs mostly within the endoplasmic reticulum of the hepatocytes
  • Other organs involved: Kidneys, gut mucosa, lungs, and skin
02

üîÑ First-Pass Metabolism

All drugs absorbed from the GIT get to the liver through the portal blood system where three possibilities occur:

üü¢ Drug Passes Unchanged

Some drugs are not metabolized during first-pass

üî¥ Complete Metabolism

All or some drug is metabolized to inactive metabolite (e.g., Glycerly Trinitrate (GTN) is inactivated if swallowed)

üü° Active Metabolites

Drug is converted to an active metabolite

‚ö†Ô∏è

Clinical Significance

First-pass metabolism significantly affects oral drug bioavailability and may require dose adjustments or alternative routes of administration.

03

‚ùì Why Does Drug Metabolism Occur?

The Lipophilicity Problem

Most drugs are lipophilic chemicals, which favor their absorption from the GIT. However, this creates a problem:

1

Lipophilic compounds

Cross lipid cell membranes readily

2

Kidney reabsorption

Lipophilic drugs are readily reabsorbed in the kidney

3

Metabolism solution

The body must make them hydrophilic (water-soluble) to promote excretion in urine

‚öóÔ∏è Phase I Metabolism

Phase I Reactions Overview

Phase I metabolism involves four main types of reactions:

üß™ Oxidation

Most common reaction type

‚¨áÔ∏è Reduction

Addition of hydrogen or removal of oxygen

üíß Hydrolysis

Addition of water with substrate breakdown

üíß Hydration

Addition of water without substrate breakage

Key Characteristics of Phase I

  • Oxidation and reduction reactions create new functional groups
  • Hydrolysis and hydration reactions expose masked functional groups
  • Phase I reactions often produce a biologically inactive compound but may activate a prodrug
üíä

Prodrug Examples

Inactive (prodrug) ‚Üí Active:

  • Prontosil Red ‚Üí Sulphanilamide
  • Enalaprilat ‚Üí Enalapril
  • Codeine ‚Üí Morphine

Enzymes Involved in Phase I

Most oxidative and some reductive reactions are carried out by:

  • Cytochrome P450 (P450 or CYP) - primary enzyme system
  • Monoamine oxidase (MAO)
  • Alcohol dehydrogenase
  • Aldehyde dehydrogenase
  • Amine oxidase

Specific Phase I Reactions

Aromatic Hydroxylation

Example: Lignocaine hydroxylation

  • Lignocaine undergoes 3-hydroxylation
  • Oxygen is inserted in the 3-position to produce a hydroxylated product from local anaesthetic lignocaine

Reduction Reactions

Compounds are reduced by the addition of hydrogen or the removal of oxygen:

  1. Azo-reduction
  2. Nitro-reduction
  3. Epoxide-reduction
  4. Reductive defluorination

Hydrolysis

Involve the addition of water with the formation of two or more metabolites:

  1. Amide hydrolysis
  2. Ester hydrolysis
  3. Hydrazide hydrolysis

üîó Phase II Metabolism

Phase II Overview

Phase II metabolism involves conjugation - the addition of a second, usually relatively large and water-soluble chemical grouping to the phase I metabolite (parent drug).

Conjugation Additions

  1. Glucuronic acid
  2. Sulphate
  3. Acetyl group
  4. Amino acids
  5. Glutathione

Phase II Reactions

üîµ Glucuronidation

Most common conjugation reaction

üü¢ Methylation

Addition of methyl groups

üü° Sulphation

Sulfate group addition

üî¥ Acetylation

Acetyl group conjugation

üü£ Amino Acid Conjugation

Various amino acids

üü† Glutathione Conjugation

Detoxification pathway

‚úÖ

Phase II Characteristics

Conjugation reactions characteristically:

  • Substantially increase water solubility
  • Abolish pharmacological activity
  • Increase molecular weight sufficiently to sometimes allow biliary excretion

Note: Although there are exceptions to these rules, they apply in the majority of cases.

üö™ Drug Excretion

Definition and Importance

Excretion is a process whereby drugs are transferred from the internal to the external environment. The principal organs involved in this activity are:

üî¥ Kidneys

Primary excretion organ

üíö Liver/Biliary

Biliary excretion pathway

üí® Lungs

Volatile substances

üåø Intestines

Minor excretion route

08

üî¥ Renal Excretion

The kidney is the primary organ of removal for most drugs, especially for those that are water-soluble and not volatile.

Three Principal Processes

1

Glomerular Filtration

Passive filtration of drugs from blood into urine

2

Tubular Secretion

Active transport of drugs into renal tubules

3

Tubular Reabsorption

Mostly passive back-diffusion of drugs from tubules back to blood

09

üîÄ Other Excretion Routes

Biliary Excretion

Biliary excretion plays a major role (5-95% of the administered dose) in drug removal for some anions, cations, and certain un-ionized molecules, such as cardiac glycosides.

  • Important for excretion of some heavy metals
  • Most drugs secreted by liver into bile ‚Üí small intestine are NOT eliminated through feces

Pulmonary Excretion

  • Any volatile material has potential for pulmonary excretion
  • Gases and volatile substances excreted by simple diffusion
  • No specialized transport systems involved

Sweat and Saliva

  • Excretion occurs but has only minor importance for most drugs
  • Mechanism: diffusion of un-ionized lipid-soluble drug across epithelial cells

Milk Excretion

Many drugs in nursing mother's blood are detectable in her milk. Concentration depends on:

  1. Amount of drug in maternal blood
  2. Lipid solubility
  3. Degree of ionization
  4. Extent of active excretion
‚ö†Ô∏è

Breastfeeding Considerations

Important: Breast-feeding should be discouraged when inherent drug toxicity is known or when adverse pharmacological actions on the infant are likely.

Minimizing infant exposure:

  1. Short intermittent maternal drug use
  2. Drug dosing immediately after breastfeeding

üìç Drug Distribution

Drug distribution in the body is a continuous pharmacokinetic process. A drug will be highly distributed if it has:

üü¢ High Lipid Solubility

Favors membrane penetration

üü° Low Plasma Protein Binding

More free drug available

üîµ High Capillary Penetration

Ability to cross blood vessels

üü† High Blood Perfusion

Rapid blood flow to tissues

üü£ Specific Transport

Active transport mechanisms

üî¥ Tissue Permeability

Ability to cross tissue barriers

üß†

Blood-Brain Barrier

BBB prevents many drugs from entering the brain. Organs without preventive barriers will easily accumulate drugs.

‚öôÔ∏è Factors Influencing Metabolism

Physiological Factors

  • Starvation, liver diseases, cardiovascular problems - depress microsomal enzyme systems
  • Age: People with extreme ages have decreased metabolism
  • Genetic predisposition: Genetic differences in the rate of metabolism of some drugs exist
  • Prior drug administration: Repeated administration may cause induction or inhibition

Enzyme Induction

Enzyme induction is a situation whereby there is an increase in amount and activity of liver microsomal enzymes, usually due to exposure to certain substances such as drugs and endogenous substances.

A drug may induce its own metabolism

‚ö†Ô∏è

Pharmacological Importance of Enzyme Induction

  1. Drug interactions may occur
  2. Disease may result
  3. Tolerance (metabolic) to the drug

Enzyme Inhibition

Enzyme inhibition refers to decreased synthesis and activity of liver microsomal enzymes. It results in reduced metabolism of other drugs/inhibiting drug and endogenous substances.

General enzyme inhibition has greater pharmacological importance than enzyme induction.

üíä

Examples of Enzyme Inhibitors

Drugs that inhibit enzymes include:

  • Chloramphenicol
  • Cimetidine

Clinical Implications

  • NB: Delayed drug metabolism results in accumulation of drug in the body and prolonged effect of the drug
  • Deficiency of some enzymes may lead to drug toxicity because of poor or absence of metabolism
