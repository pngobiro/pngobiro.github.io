üéØ Introduction to Drug Administration

Drug administration is a critical aspect of healthcare that involves delivering medications to patients safely and effectively. Understanding the various routes, factors, and principles of drug administration is essential for healthcare professionals.

üí°

Key Learning Objective

Master the factors, routes, and principles that guide safe and effective drug administration in clinical practice.

02

‚öñÔ∏è Factors to Consider in Drug Administration

Several critical factors must be evaluated when determining the appropriate drug administration method:

  • Patient Convenience

    The route should be convenient and acceptable to the patient, considering their ability to self-administer or cooperate with the treatment.

  • Speed of Action Required

    Emergency situations require rapid drug delivery, while elective procedures allow for slower-acting routes.

  • Duration of Drug Availability

    Consider how long the drug needs to be available at the site of action to achieve therapeutic effects.

  • Organ Avoidance

    Determine which organs should not be exposed to the drug due to potential toxicity or adverse effects.

  • Cost Considerations

    Evaluate the cost-effectiveness of different administration routes and formulations.

  • Drug Interactions

    Consider what other medications are being administered simultaneously to avoid adverse interactions.

üõ§Ô∏è Common Routes of Drug Administration

1. Oral Administration

Description: Drugs are administered through the mouth and absorbed from the gastrointestinal tract.

Dosage Forms:

Form Type Description
Tablets Solid dose forms compressed from powdered drugs
Capsules Drug enclosed in a gelatin shell
Solutions Liquid preparations containing dissolved drug
Powders Granular or fine particles of drug substance
‚úÖ

Advantages

  • Extremely convenient and allows self-administration
  • Avoids fear of needles
  • Can be formulated for slow release
  • Large surface area of GIT for absorption
  • Easy to manufacture dosage forms
  • Safe, painless, and portable
  • Can mask unpleasant tastes
  • Easy to reverse effects in case of overdose
  • Can be formulated to protect from gastric juices
‚ö†Ô∏è

Disadvantages

  • Variable absorption depending on drug's properties
  • Affected by stomach contents (food, antacids)
  • Depends on peristalsis and stomach emptying
  • Slow onset of action
  • Risk of aspiration into lungs
  • Exposure to food and drug interactions
  • Depends on patient compliance
  • Cannot be used post-surgery
  • May cause tooth staining
  • Common drug-drug interactions

First Pass Metabolism

From GIT, absorption takes place and the drug passes through the portal system to the liver before reaching the rest of the body for distribution. This can significantly reduce the bioavailability of many drugs.

2. Intravenous (IV) Administration

Description: Drugs are administered directly into the vein.

Common Examples:

Penicillin, paracetamol, insulin, diclofenac, heparin, glucose

‚úÖ

Advantages

  • Rapid onset of action
  • Lower dose required compared to oral
  • Easy titration of the drug
  • Large volumes of fluid can be administered
  • No absorption required
  • High bioavailability (100%)
‚ö†Ô∏è

Disadvantages

  • Requires trained personnel
  • Can cause tissue damage
  • Accidental overdose has serious consequences
  • Can cause fluid overload
  • Can cause electrolyte imbalance
  • Risk of infection at injection site
  • More painful than IM
  • Fear of needles
  • Can cause phlebitis and embolism

3. Intramuscular (IM) Administration

Description: Drugs are administered into the muscles.

‚úÖ

Advantages

  • Similar to IV but easier to administer
  • Can accommodate larger volumes
‚ö†Ô∏è

Disadvantages

  • Painful
  • Self-administration not possible
  • Can form abscess at injection site
  • Variable absorption

4. Topical Application

Description: Direct application to body surfaces.

Common Forms:

  1. Ointments - Semi-solid preparations for skin application
  2. Drops - Liquid preparations for eyes, ears, or nose
  3. Gargles - Solutions for throat treatment
  4. Lozenges - Dissolvable tablets for mouth/throat
  5. Suppositories - Rectal or vaginal preparations
‚úÖ

Advantages

  • Good compliance
  • Drug applied directly to affected site
  • Fast action at site of action
  • Self-convenient
‚ö†Ô∏è

Disadvantages

  • There is wastage
  • Instructions may not be clear
  • Absorption may occur in bloodstream when not required
  • Could be irritant to openings
  • May stain clothes
  • May have bad smell

5. Inhalation

Description: Drugs are applied to the respiratory system.

‚úÖ

Advantages

  • Local action at target organ/lungs
  • Rapid absorption with rapid onset of action
  • No first pass metabolism
  • Lower dosage required compared to oral
  • Can regulate drug delivery
  • Can be used on unconscious patients
‚ö†Ô∏è

Disadvantages

  • Skill required in using inhaler gadget
  • High cost of gadget
  • Requires patient cooperation at home
  • Airway must be clear
  • Onset slower than IV and IM
  • More expensive compared to oral route

üéØ Specialized Routes

Topical Routes

  1. Skin - Including transdermal patches for systemic delivery
  2. Eyes - Ophthalmic preparations for ocular conditions
  3. Ears - Otic preparations for ear infections
  4. Nose - Nasal sprays for local or systemic effects
  5. Lungs - Inhalation for respiratory conditions
  6. Vagina - Vaginal preparations for local infections

Specialized Infusion Routes

Route Description Clinical Use
Intrathecal Injection into cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) Chemotherapy, spinal anesthesia
Intrapleural Injection into pleural cavity Drainage, chemotherapy
Intraosseous Injection into rich vascular network of long bone Emergency vascular access

‚öñÔ∏è Principles of Drug Administration

To provide safe administration of drugs, healthcare professionals should practice the "Rights of Drug Administration":

üèÜ

The 10 Rights of Drug Administration

  • 1

    Right Patient - Verify patient identity using two identifiers

  • 2

    Right Drug - Check medication name and concentration

  • 3

    Right Dose - Calculate and verify correct dosage

  • 4

    Right Route - Ensure correct method of administration

  • 5

    Right Time - Administer at prescribed intervals

  • 6

    Right Documentation - Record administration immediately

  • 7

    Right to Refuse - Respect patient's right to refuse medication

  • 8

    Right Assessment - Evaluate patient's condition before administration

  • 9

    Right Education - Provide patient education about the medication

  • 10

    Right Evaluation - Monitor therapeutic effects and adverse reactions

‚è∞

Right Time Exception

Under normal circumstances, the right time for drug administration is not indicated by the doctor. The doctor only indicates the number of times a drug is to be given.

üß™ Drug Development and Testing

Before drugs receive legal approval for public use, they must pass through several sequential stages of development and testing:

Testing Requirements

  • Not teratogenic: Should not cause birth defects
  • Not carcinogenic: Should not cause cancer
  • Toxicity studies: Must not be harmful to body systems
  • Specific action: Should be specific in action with understood mechanism
  • Optimal route: Should have an optimal route of administration
