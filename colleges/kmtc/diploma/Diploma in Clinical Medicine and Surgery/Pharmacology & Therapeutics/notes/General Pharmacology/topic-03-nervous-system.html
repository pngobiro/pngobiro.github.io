üíä Analgesics (Pain Management)

1. Opioid Analgesics (Narcotics)

Mechanism: Bind to specific opioid receptors (Mu, Kappa, Delta) in the CNS to alter perception and response to pain.

  • Agonists: Morphine, Pethidine (Meperidine), Fentanyl, Codeine, Methadone. Used for moderate to severe pain.
  • Antagonists: Naloxone (Narcan). Used to reverse opioid overdose.

Opioid Toxicity Triad

1. Coma
2. Respiratory Depression (Rate < 12/min)
3. Pinpoint Pupils

Antidote: Naloxone IV.

2. Non-Opioid Analgesics & NSAIDs

Mechanism: Inhibit Cyclooxygenase (COX) enzymes, reducing prostaglandin synthesis (responsible for pain, inflammation, and fever).

Drug Key Features Adverse Effects
Aspirin Anti-platelet effect (irreversible). analgesic, antipyretic, anti-inflammatory. GI bleeding, Tinnitus (Salicylism), Reye's Syndrome in children.
Acetaminophen (Paracetamol) Analgesic & Antipyretic (NOT anti-inflammatory). Hepatotoxicity in overdose. Antidote: Acetylcysteine.
Ibuprofen/Diclofenac Traditional NSAIDs. Anti-inflammatory. GI ulcers, Renal impairment.
Celecoxib COX-2 Selective inhibitor. Less GI risk, potential cardiovascular risk.

üíâ Anesthetics

General Anesthetics

Depress the CNS to permit surgery. Effects include analgesia, amnesia, loss of consciousness, and muscle relaxation.

  • Inhalation: Nitrous Oxide, Halothane, Sevoflurane. Risk: Malignant Hyperthermia.
  • Intravenous (Parenteral):
    • Thiopental: Barbiturate. Rapid onset for induction.
    • Propofol: Rapid onset/recovery. "Milk of amnesia".
    • Ketamine: Dissociative anesthesia. Maintains respiratory drive but causes hallucinations.

Local Anesthetics

Mechanism: Block voltage-gated Sodium (Na+) channels in nerves, stopping conduction.

Examples: Lidocaine, Bupivacaine (long-acting), Cocaine (vasoconstrictor). usually combined with adrenaline to prolong action (vasoconstriction).

üò¥ Sedative-Hypnotics & Anxiolytics

Drugs that depress CNS activity to relieve anxiety (sedative) or induce sleep (hypnotic). Main mechanism involves enhancing GABA (inhibitory neurotransmitter) activity.

‚ñ∂ Benzodiazepines

Examples: Diazepam, Midazolam (short-acting), Lorazepam.

Uses: Anxiety, insomnia, status epilepticus, alcohol withdrawal, muscle relaxation.

Adverse Effects: Drowsiness, dependence, respiratory depression (especially with alcohol).

Antidote: Flumazenil.

‚ñ∂ Barbiturates

Examples: Phenobarbital (Long-acting), Thiopental (Ultra-short).

Uses: Anticonvulsant (Phenobarbital), Anesthesia induction.

Risks: Low therapeutic index, potent enzyme inducers, severe respiratory depression in overdose.

‚ö° Anticonvulsants (Antiepileptics)

Drugs used to control seizures. Mechanisms include blocking Na+/Ca++ channels or enhancing GABA.

Drug Key Notes
Phenytoin Blocks Na+ channels. Side Effects: Gingival hyperplasia, hirsutism, teratogenic (cleft palate), nystagmus.
Carbamazepine Blocks Na+ channels. Enzyme inducer. Used for seizures and trigeminal neuralgia.
Valproic Acid Broad spectrum. Side Effects: Hepatotoxicity, neural tube defects (Spina Bifida) if used in pregnancy.
Ethosuximide Specific for Absence Seizures (Petit Mal).
Diazepam/Lorazepam Drug of choice for acute Status Epilepticus.

üßò Psychotherapeutic Agents

1. Antidepressants

  • SSRIs (Fluoxetine, Sertraline): First line. Block serotonin reuptake. SE: Sexual dysfunction, GI upset.
  • TCAs (Amitriptyline, Imipramine): Block NE/Serotonin reuptake. SE: Anticholinergic effects (dry mouth, urinary retention), cardiotoxicity in overdose.
  • MAOIs (Phenelzine): Inhibit MAO enzyme. Warning: Hypertensive crisis with Tyramine-rich foods (cheese, wine).

2. Antipsychotics (Neuroleptics)

Used for Schizophrenia and acute psychosis. Block Dopamine (D2) receptors.

  • Typical (1st Gen): Chlorpromazine, Haloperidol. High risk of Extrapyramidal Symptoms (EPS) like dystonia, parkinsonism, and tardive dyskinesia.
  • Atypical (2nd Gen): Clozapine, Risperidone, Olanzapine. Less EPS but risk of metabolic syndrome (weight gain, diabetes). Clozapine risk: Agranulocytosis.

3. Mood Stabilizers

Lithium Carbonate: Used for Bipolar Disorder. Narrow therapeutic index (0.6 - 1.2 mEq/L). Toxicity causes tremors, polyuria, confusion. Teratogenic.

06

üí™ Neuromuscular Blocking Agents

Used during surgery to produce muscle relaxation. They block Acetylcholine (ACh) at the neuromuscular junction.

  • Depolarizing: Succinylcholine. Causes initial twitching then paralysis. Short acting. Risk: Malignant Hyperthermia.
  • Non-Depolarizing: Pancuronium, Atracurium, Vecuronium. Competitive blockers. Reversed by Neostigmine.
