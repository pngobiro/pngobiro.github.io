üí° Introduction & Terminology

Pharmacology is the study of the effects of chemical substances on the function of living systems. It encompasses the preparation, use, and effects of drugs.

Key Definitions

Drug
Any substance used in diagnosis, cure, treatment, and prevention of disease/condition or any substance that brings a change in biological functions through its chemical actions.
Pharmacokinetics
The study of how the body acts on the drug. It involves Absorption, Distribution, Metabolism (biotransformation), and Excretion.
Pharmacodynamics
The study of how the drug acts on the body, including mechanism of action and dose-response relationships.
Pharmacognosy
The study of drugs derived from natural sources such as plants, animals, and minerals.
Toxicology
The branch dealing with undesirable effects of chemicals on living systems.

Drug Nomenclature

Drugs are typically identified by three names:

  1. Chemical Name: Describes the molecular structure (e.g., N-acetyl-para-aminophenol).
  2. Generic Name: The official, approved non-proprietary name (e.g., Paracetamol, Acetaminophen). This is the preferred name for medical use.
  3. Trade/Brand Name: The proprietary name given by the manufacturer (e.g., Panadol, Tylenol).

üî¨ Drug Development

Before a drug is approved for public use, it undergoes rigorous testing to ensure safety and efficacy.

Phase Focus Subjects
Pre-clinical Pharmacodynamics, pharmacokinetics, and toxicity (mutagenicity, carcinogenicity). Laboratory animals
Phase I Safety, dosage, and pharmacokinetics. Healthy human volunteers (20-50)
Phase II Therapeutic efficacy and dosing requirements. Patients with the disease (100-200)
Phase III Confirmation of efficacy and monitoring of adverse effects in a large population. Large patient groups (300-3000)
Phase IV Post-marketing surveillance/continuous evaluation of long-term safety. General population

Orphan Drugs

Drugs discovered for rare diseases that may not be financially viable for companies to develop without incentives.

üíâ Drug Administration

Principles of Administration

To ensure patient safety, the "Rights" of medication administration must be followed:

  • Right Patient: Verify ID bracelet or call out name.
  • Right Drug: Check label three times (before removal, before preparation, before replacement).
  • Right Dose: Calculate accurately; avoid splitting non-scored tablets.
  • Right Route: Verify the prescribed route matches the formulation.
  • Right Time: Administer at the correct intervals relative to food/sleep.

Common Routes

‚ñ∂ Enteral (Oral)

Pros: Convenient, safe, painless, economical, huge absorption surface area.

Cons: Variable absorption, first-pass metabolism, gastric irritation, requires patient compliance, slow onset.

‚ñ∂ Parenteral (IV, IM, SC)

Intravenous (IV): 100% bioavailability, rapid onset, precise control, allows large volumes. Risks: Fluid overload, infection, embolism, immediate toxicity.

Intramuscular (IM): Suitable for depot preparations, easier than IV. Risks: Painful, potential nerve damage, abscess formation.

‚ñ∂ Topical & Inhalation

Topical: High local concentration, minimal systemic effects. Includes skin, eyes, ears, vagina.

Inhalation: Rapid absorption due to large surface area of lungs, avoids first-pass effect. Used for local (asthma) or systemic (anesthesia) effects.

üîÑ Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Pharmacokinetics describes "what the body does to the drug." It determines the onset, duration, and intensity of a drug's effect.

1. Absorption

Movement of drug from site of administration into the bloodstream. Factors affecting absorption include:

  • Lipid Solubility: Non-ionized, lipid-soluble drugs cross membranes easily.
  • Route of Administration: IV bypasses absorption entirely.
  • Blood Flow: Higher flow (e.g., intestine vs. stomach) favors absorption.
üí°

Bioavailability

The fraction of the administered dose that reaches the systemic circulation unchanged. IV administration = 100% bioavailability. Oral drugs often undergo First-Pass Metabolism in the liver before reaching circulation, reducing bioavailability.

2. Distribution

Transport of drug via blood to tissues. Dependent on:

  • Blood Flow: Well-perfused organs (heart, liver, kidneys, brain) receive drugs first.
  • Protein Binding: Drugs bound to plasma proteins (like albumin) are inactive and trapped in the blood; only "free" drug is active and can distribute.
  • Barriers: Blood-Brain Barrier (BBB) and Placental Barrier restrict passage of certain drugs (polar/ionized).

3. Metabolism (Biotransformation)

The chemical alteration of the drug, primarily in the liver, to make it more water-soluble (hydrophilic) for excretion.

Phase Process Key Enzymes/Agents
Phase I Functionalization (Oxidation, Reduction, Hydrolysis). Introduces/exposes functional groups. Cytochrome P450 (CYP) enzymes
Phase II Conjugation. Adds large water-soluble groups to the molecule. Glucuronic acid, sulfate, glutathione, amino acids
‚ö†Ô∏è

Enzyme Induction & Inhibition

Inducers (e.g., Barbiturates): Increase enzyme activity, speeding up metabolism of other drugs (reducing their effect).

Inhibitors (e.g., Cimetidine): Decrease enzyme activity, slowing metabolism of other drugs (increasing toxicity risk).

4. Excretion

Elimination of drugs/metabolites from the body.

  • Renal (Kidney): Primary route. Involves glomerular filtration and active tubular secretion. Lipophilic drugs may be reabsorbed; hydrophilic ones are excreted.
  • Biliary/Fecal: Excretion via bile into intestines.
  • Other routes: Lungs (volatile agents), breast milk, sweat, saliva.

‚ö° Pharmacodynamics

Pharmacodynamics describes "what the drug does to the body." It studies biochemical and physiological effects and mechanisms of action.

Mechanism of Action

Drugs typically interact with receptors (regulatory proteins) to produce an effect. Other targets include enzymes, ion channels, and transport proteins.

üîë

Receptor Concepts

  • ‚óè
    Agonist: Binds to a receptor and activates it to produce a response (has affinity + intrinsic activity).
  • ‚óè
    Antagonist: Binds to a receptor but prevents activation; blocks agonists (has affinity + zero intrinsic activity).
  • ‚óè
    Partial Agonist: Binds to receptor but produces a sub-maximal response.
  • ‚óè
    Affinity: The strength of attraction between a drug and its receptor.
  • ‚óè
    Efficacy: The maximum response a drug can produce (intrinsic activity).
  • ‚óè
    Potency: The dose required to produce a specific effect (lower dose = higher potency).

Therapeutic Index (TI)

A measure of drug safety. It is the ratio of the toxic dose to the effective dose.

  • Wide TI: Safer drug (e.g., Penicillin). Large gap between effective and toxic dose.
  • Narrow TI: Dangerous drug (e.g., Digoxin, Warfarin). Small gap; requires careful monitoring.

Drug Interactions

  • Synergism: Combined effect is greater than sum of individual effects (1+1 > 2).
  • Additive: Combined effect equals sum of individual effects (1+1 = 2).
  • Antagonism: One drug opposes the action of another.
