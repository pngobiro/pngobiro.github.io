üß† Anterior Pituitary Hormones

The anterior pituitary secretes trophic hormones regulated by hypothalamic releasing or inhibiting factors.

Growth Hormone (GH)

Physiological Actions: Promotes linear growth (via IGF-1), protein anabolism, lipolysis, and insulin antagonism (diabetogenic).

Therapeutic Uses:
1. Pituitary Dwarfism: Recombinant hGH (Somatropin) is used.
2. Turner Syndrome and Chronic Renal Insufficiency in children.
3. AIDS Cachexia: To promote anabolism.

Drugs for Acromegaly (GH Excess)

  • Somatostatin Analogues: Octreotide, Lanreotide. Inhibit GH release. Long-acting. Also used for carcinoid syndrome and esophageal varices.
  • Dopamine Agonists: Bromocriptine (High dose). Less effective than octreotide.
  • GH Receptor Antagonist: Pegvisomant.

Prolactin

Regulated by tonic inhibition by Dopamine (PIF). Hyperprolactinemia causes galactorrhea, amenorrhea, and infertility.

Drugs for Hyperprolactinemia (Dopamine Agonists):
Bromocriptine: D2 agonist. Suppresses prolactin. Also used in Parkinsonism and Acromegaly. Side effects: Nausea, postural hypotension.
Cabergoline: Long-acting D2 agonist. More effective and better tolerated. Drug of choice.

ü¶ã Thyroid Hormones and Antithyroid Drugs

Thyroid Hormones

T4 (Thyroxine): Prohormone. Long half-life (7 days). Converted to T3 in tissues.
T3 (Triiodothyronine): Active hormone. Short half-life (1 day). Potent.

Uses:
1. Cretinism/Hypothyroidism: Levothyroxine (T4) is the drug of choice. Oral, once daily (empty stomach).
2. Myxedema Coma: Medical emergency. IV Levothyroxine (+ IV Hydrocortisone).

Antithyroid Drugs (For Hyperthyroidism)

1. Thioamides

Propylthiouracil (PTU), Carbimazole, Methimazole.
Mechanism: Inhibit Thyroid Peroxidase, preventing oxidation of iodide and coupling of iodotyrosines. PTU also inhibits peripheral conversion of T4 to T3.
Adverse Effects: Skin rash, arthralgia. Agranulocytosis (rare but serious - monitor sore throat/fever).
Pregnancy: PTU is preferred in the first trimester (less teratogenic); Methimazole/Carbimazole can be used later.

2. Iodides (Lugol's Iodine, SSKI)

Mechanism: Inhibit hormone release (Thyroid constipation) and vascularity.
Uses: Preoperative preparation (to make gland firm/less vascular), Thyroid Storm. Not for long-term use (escape phenomenon).

3. Radioactive Iodine ($^{131}I$)

Mechanism: Emits beta rays destroying thyroid follicles. "Medical Thyroidectomy".
Uses: Graves' disease (especially elderly), Toxic nodular goiter.
Contraindication: Pregnancy, Children.
Disadvantage: Delayed Hypothyroidism (permanent).

4. Beta Blockers (Propranolol)

Symptomatic control (tachycardia, tremor, anxiety). Also inhibits peripheral T4 to T3 conversion (weakly).

Thyroid Storm (Thyrotoxic Crisis) Management

  1. Propranolol: IV/Oral to control cardiac symptoms.
  2. Propylthiouracil: High dose (blocks synthesis + conversion).
  3. Iodides: (1 hour after PTU) to block release.
  4. Hydrocortisone: IV (prevents shock + blocks conversion).
  5. Supportive care (Cooling, fluids).

üíâ Diabetes Mellitus: Insulin and Oral Agents

Insulin

Mechanism: Binds to tyrosine kinase receptors. Increases glucose entry (GLUT-4) into muscle/fat, promotes glycogen/protein/lipid synthesis.
Indications: Type 1 DM, Type 2 DM (if uncontrolled, surgery, pregnancy, stress), DKA.

Insulin Preparations
Type Examples Onset/Duration Characteristics
Rapid Acting (Analogues) Lispro, Aspart, Glulisine 10-15 min / 3-5 hr Inject just before meal. Less hypoglycemia.
Short Acting Regular (Soluble) 30-60 min / 6-8 hr Only type for IV use (DKA). 30 min before meal.
Intermediate NPH (Isophane) 2 hr / 18-24 hr Cloudy (Protamine). Basal insulin.
Long Acting Glargine, Detemir, Degludec 2-4 hr / 24+ hr Peakless basal coverage. Less nocturnal hypoglycemia.

Oral Antidiabetic Drugs (OADs)

1. Biguanides (Metformin)

Drug of Choice for Type 2 DM (Obese).
Mechanism: Activates AMPK. Decreases hepatic gluconeogenesis. Increases peripheral sensitivity.
Benefits: No hypoglycemia, weight loss.
ADR: GI upset (common), Lactic acidosis (rare - avoid in renal/hepatic/cardiac failure).

2. Sulfonylureas

Glibenclamide, Glipizide, Glimepiride.
Mechanism: Block ATP-sensitive K+ channels in beta cells $\rightarrow$ Depolarization $\rightarrow$ Ca++ influx $\rightarrow$ Insulin release (Secretagogues).
ADR: Hypoglycemia (especially long acting agents in elderly), Weight gain.

3. Meglitinides (Glinides)

Repaglinide, Nateglinide. Rapid acting secretagogues. Take just before meals. Less hypoglycemia.

4. Thiazolidinediones (Glitazones)

Pioglitazone.
Mechanism: PPAR-gamma agonists. Increase insulin sensitivity (adipose/muscle).
ADR: Fluid retention (avoid in CHF), weight gain, bone fractures.

5. Alpha-Glucosidase Inhibitors

Acarbose, Voglibose.
Mechanism: Inhibit CHO digestion in gut. Reduce post-prandial glucose.
ADR: Flatulence, abdominal discomfort.

6. DPP-4 Inhibitors (Gliptins)

Sitagliptin, Vildagliptin.
Mechanism: Inhibit breakdown of incretins (GLP-1), increasing insulin and decreasing glucagon. Weight neutral.

7. SGLT-2 Inhibitors (Gliflozins)

Dapagliflozin, Empagliflozin.
Mechanism: Inhibit glucose reabsorption in kidney $\rightarrow$ Glycosuria.
Benefits: Weight loss, BP reduction, CV benefits.
ADR: Genital infections (UTI/Thrush).

üöë

Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA) Management

  1. Fluids: IV Normal Saline (Critical step).
  2. Insulin: IV Regular Insulin bolus + infusion.
  3. Potassium: Add K+ to fluids once urine output is established (Insulin shifts K+ into cells).
  4. Bicarbonate: Only if pH < 7.0.
  5. Treat precipitating cause (Infection).

üíä Adrenal Cortical Steroids

Glucocorticoids (Cortisol, Prednisolone, Dexamethasone)

Mechanisms:
1. Genomic: Bind to cytoplasmic receptors, translocation to nucleus, gene transcription modulation (slow).
2. Anti-inflammatory: Inhibit Phospholipase A2 (via Lipocortin), inhibit cytokines (IL-1, IL-6, TNF), inhibit cell migration.

Pharmacological Actions:
Metabolic: Gluconeogenesis (Hyperglycemia), Protein catabolism (muscle wasting), Lipolysis (redistribution of fat - moon face, buffalo hump).
Mineralocorticoid: Na+ retention, K+ loss (less with Dexamethasone/Betamethasone).
Other: Anti-inflammatory, Immunosuppressive, Anti-vitamin D (Osteoporosis).

Drug Anti-inflammatory Potency Na+ Retaining Potency Duration
Hydrocortisone 1 1 Short
Prednisolone 4 0.8 Intermediate
Methylprednisolone 5 0.5 Intermediate
Dexamethasone 25 0 Long
Betamethasone 25 0 Long

Adverse Effects (Cushing's Syndrome)

Hyperglycemia, Hypertension, Osteoporosis (vertebral fractures), Peptic ulcer, Psychosis, Infection susceptibility, Growth retardation in children, Cataract, Glaucoma, HPA axis suppression.

Withdrawal Protocol

Sudden withdrawal after prolonged use causes Acute Adrenal Insufficiency. Doses must be tapered slowly. During stress (surgery/infection), dose must be increased.

Mineralocorticoids

Aldosterone, Fludrocortisone.
Act on distal tubule to reabsorb Na+ and excrete K+/H+.
Use: Addison's disease (Replacement), Salt-losing CAH.
Antagonist: Spironolactone (Hyperaldosteronism, CHF).
