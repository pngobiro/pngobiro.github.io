üåê General Considerations

The Autonomic Nervous System (ANS) is independent of volitional control. It innervates the heart, the smooth muscles, the glands and the viscera. It comprises the parasympathetic (cholinergic), the sympathetic (adrenergic) and the enteric nervous systems.

Divisions

  • Parasympathetic: Craniosacral outflow. Supplies motor fibres to smooth muscle, glands, heart and viscera. Participates in tissue building.
  • Sympathetic: Thoracolumbar outflow ($\mathrm{T}_1$ to $\mathrm{L}_2$). Prepares body for 'flight or fight' (stress response).
  • Enteric (ENS): Highly organised neurons in the wall of GI tract (Myenteric and Submucosal plexuses). Controls GI motility and secretions.

Neurohumoral Transmission

Acetylcholine (ACh) acts as the transmitter at:

  • All autonomic ganglia (Sympathetic and Parasympathetic).
  • Postganglionic parasympathetic nerve endings.
  • Sympathetic postganglionic nerve endings supplying sweat glands.
  • Somatic motor nerve endings (Skeletal muscle).
  • Adrenal medulla.

Noradrenaline (NA) is the transmitter at postganglionic sympathetic nerve endings.

Dopamine (DA) is a transmitter in the basal ganglia, limbic system and CTZ.

üîÑ

Synthesis and Metabolism

ACh: Synthesised from Choline + Acetyl CoA by Choline Acetyl Transferase. Hydrolysed by Cholinesterase (True and Pseudo).

Catecholamines: Tyrosine $\to$ DOPA $\to$ Dopamine $\to$ Noradrenaline $\to$ Adrenaline. Metabolised by MAO (intracellular) and COMT (extracellular). Major inactivation mechanism is Uptake-1 (reuptake into nerve terminal).

Adrenergic Receptors

Classified by Ahlquist into Alpha ($\alpha$) and Beta ($\beta$) receptors.

Catecholamines

Adrenaline (Epinephrine): Stimulates $\alpha$ and $\beta$ receptors. Used in Anaphylaxis (Drug of choice), Cardiac arrest, Bronchial asthma (acute), with Local anaesthetics.

Noradrenaline (Norepinephrine): Acts mainly on $\alpha$ and $\beta_1$ receptors (weak on $\beta_2$). Powerful vasoconstrictor. Used in Shock.

Isoprenaline: Non-selective $\beta$ agonist. Powerful cardiac stimulant and bronchodilator. Used in Heart block.

Dopamine: Acts on $D_1$ (Renal vasodilation), $\beta_1$ (Cardiac stimulation) and $\alpha_1$ (Vasoconstriction) receptors depending on dose. Used in Shock (Cardiogenic/Septic).

Dobutamine: Selective $\beta_1$ agonist. Increases force of contraction without significant tachycardia. Used in CHF.

üí°

Dale's Vasomotor Reversal

Adrenaline normally causes a rise in BP ($\alpha$ effect). After an $\alpha$-blocker (e.g., ergot), Adrenaline produces a fall in BP because only the $\beta_2$ (vasodilator) action manifests. This is Dale's reversal.

Non-catecholamines

Effective orally, longer duration, cross BBB.

  • Ephedrine: Indirectly acting (releases NA) + Direct action. Used in Asthma, Hypotension.
  • Amphetamine: CNS stimulant. Releases NA/DA. Used in Narcolepsy, ADHD. High abuse potential.
  • Nasal Decongestants: Phenylephrine, Xylometazoline, Oxymetazoline ($\alpha$ agonists).

Adrenergic Antagonists

Alpha Blockers

  • Non-selective: Phenoxybenzamine (Irreversible), Phentolamine. Used in Pheochromocytoma.
  • Selective $\alpha_1$ blockers: Prazosin, Terazosin, Tamsulosin. Used in Hypertension, Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH).

Beta Blockers

  • Non-selective: Propranolol, Timolol, Nadolol.
  • Cardioselective ($\beta_1$): Atenolol, Metoprolol, Bisoprolol, Esmolol. Safer in asthma/diabetes.
  • With $\alpha$ blocking activity: Labetalol, Carvedilol.

Uses: Hypertension, Angina, MI, Arrhythmias, Heart Failure, Glaucoma (Timolol), Thyrotoxicosis, Anxiety, Migraine prophylaxis.

üçÑ Cholinergic Drugs

Choline Esters & Alkaloids

Acetylcholine: Not used clinically (rapid hydrolysis). Two types of actions: Muscarinic (blocked by atropine) and Nicotinic.

Bethanechol: Muscarinic selective. Resistant to cholinesterase. Used for Post-op urinary retention, Paralytic ileus.

Pilocarpine: Alkaloid. Miotic. Used in Glaucoma (opens drainage angle).

Anticholinesterases (Anti-ChE)

Inhibit cholinesterase enzyme $\to$ Accumulation of ACh.

Reversible

  • Physostigmine: Crosses BBB (Tertiary amine). Used in Glaucoma, Atropine poisoning.
  • Neostigmine: No CNS entry (Quaternary). Direct muscle action. Used in Myasthenia Gravis, Curare poisoning, Post-op ileus/retention.
  • Pyridostigmine: Longer acting. Preferred in Myasthenia.
  • Edrophonium: Very short acting. Diagnostic test for Myasthenia.
  • Donepezil, Rivastigmine: Cerebroselective. Used in Alzheimer's disease.

Irreversible (Organophosphates)

DFP, Parathion, Malathion (Insecticides), Nerve gases. Phosphorylate the enzyme irreversibly.

Organophosphorus Poisoning Treatment

  • Decontamination (wash skin/eyes).
  • Atropine: Drug of choice (2 mg IV every 10 min) till pupils dilate/secretions dry (atropinization). Blocks muscarinic effects.
  • Oximes (Pralidoxime/PAM): Cholinesterase reactivators. Effective only if given early (before enzyme aging). Relieve neuromuscular paralysis.
  • Supportive care (Ventilation).

üëÅÔ∏è Muscarinic Receptor Blocking Drugs

Atropine (Belladonna Alkaloid)

Competitive antagonist of ACh at muscarinic receptors.

Actions:

  • Secretions: Decreases salivary, bronchial, sweat (dry mouth, dry skin).
  • Eye: Mydriasis (pupil dilation), Cycloplegia (paralysis of accommodation), Increases IOP (dangerous in narrow angle glaucoma).
  • GI/GU: Antispasmodic (relaxes smooth muscle), Urinary retention.
  • Heart: Tachycardia (blocks vagal tone).
  • CNS: Mild stimulation (toxic doses $\to$ delirium).

Uses: Preanaesthetic medication, Organophosphorus poisoning, Heart block, Mydriatic (refraction testing), Colic.

‚ö†Ô∏è

Atropine Poisoning

Symptoms: "Dry as a bone, blind as a bat, red as a beet, mad as a hatter, hot as a hare".
Treatment: Physostigmine (crosses BBB).

Synthetic Substitutes

  • Mydriatics: Homatropine, Tropicamide (shorter acting).
  • Antispasmodics: Dicyclomine, Propantheline, Hyoscine butylbromide.
  • Bronchodilators: Ipratropium, Tiotropium (Inhalational).
  • Vesicoselective (For Overactive Bladder): Oxybutynin, Tolterodine, Solifenacin (M3 selective).
  • Antiparkinsonian: Trihexyphenidyl (Benzhexol).

üö¨ Ganglion Stimulating and Blocking Drugs

Nicotine

Stimulates both sympathetic and parasympathetic ganglia (Nicotinic $N_n$ receptors). Complex effects depending on dominant tone. High doses cause depolarization block.

Tobacco Smoking: Associated with Cancer (lung, oral), COPD, IHD, Peripheral vascular disease.

Therapy for Dependence: Nicotine replacement (gum, patch), Bupropion, Varenicline (partial agonist).

Ganglion Blockers

Hexamethonium, Mecamylamine, Trimethaphan. Block both sympathetic and parasympathetic ganglia. Pharmacological sympathectomy + parasympathectomy.

Effects: Hypotension (postural), Tachycardia, Mydriasis, Dry mouth, Constipation, Urinary retention. Obsolete clinically except Trimethaphan (rarely used for controlled hypotension).

üí™ Skeletal Muscle Relaxants

I. Peripherally Acting (Neuromuscular Blockers)

Used in anaesthesia for muscle relaxation.

A. Non-depolarizing (Competitive) Blockers

d-Tubocurarine (prototype), Pancuronium, Vecuronium, Atracurium, Rocuronium.

Mechanism: Competitively block Nicotinic ($N_m$) receptors at motor end plate.

Reversal: Neostigmine (increases ACh which displaces blocker) + Atropine.

‚ÑπÔ∏è

Drug Features

  • Atracurium: Hofmann elimination (safe in renal/hepatic failure).
  • Mivacurium: Short acting. Metabolised by plasma cholinesterase.

B. Depolarizing Blockers

Succinylcholine (Suxamethonium).

Mechanism: Persistent depolarization of motor end plate (Phase I block). Initial fasciculations $\to$ Paralysis.

Characteristics: Short acting (5 mins) due to hydrolysis by pseudocholinesterase. Not reversed by neostigmine.

Adverse Effects: Muscle pain, Hyperkalemia (danger in burns/trauma), malignant hyperthermia, succinylcholine apnea (in atypical pseudocholinesterase).

II. Centrally Acting Muscle Relaxants

Reduce muscle tone by action in CNS. Used for spasticity and muscle spasms.

  • Diazepam: Facilitates GABA.
  • Baclofen: $\mathrm{GABA}_{\mathrm{B}}$ agonist. Used in multiple sclerosis, spinal injuries.
  • Tizanidine: Central $\alpha_2$ agonist.
  • Methocarbamol, Chlorzoxazone: For acute muscle spasms.

III. Directly Acting

Dantrolene: Inhibits calcium release from sarcoplasmic reticulum. Drug of choice for Malignant Hyperthermia. Also used in spasticity.

Botulinum Toxin A: Inhibits ACh release. Used for local dystonias, cosmetic wrinkles (Botox).
