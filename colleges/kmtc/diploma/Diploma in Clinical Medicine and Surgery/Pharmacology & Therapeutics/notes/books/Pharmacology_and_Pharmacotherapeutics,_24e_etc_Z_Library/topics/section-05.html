üêù Histamine and Antihistaminic Drugs

Histamine (Tissue Amine) is an autacoid (local hormone) derived from the amino acid histidine. It is stored in mast cells and basophils along with heparin. It is released during allergic reactions (IgE mediated) and by certain drugs (morphine, tubocurarine).

Histamine Receptors

Receptor Location Major Effects Selective Agonist
H1 Smooth muscles (Bronchi, Gut), Endothelium, Brain Contraction of smooth muscle, Increased vascular permeability, Alertness (CNS). 2-Methylhistamine
H2 Gastric parietal cells, Heart, Vessels Gastric acid secretion, Cardiac stimulation, Vasodilation. Dimaprit
H3 Presynaptic (Brain) Inhibition of neurotransmitter release (Autoreceptor). (R)-alpha-methylhistamine
H4 Eosinophils, Mast cells Chemotaxis. -

Triple Response of Lewis

Intradermal injection of histamine produces:

  1. Red Spot: Local vasodilation (Direct action).
  2. Wheal: Edema due to increased permeability.
  3. Flare: Diffuse redness due to axon reflex.

H1 Receptor Antagonists (Antihistamines)

These drugs competitively block the effects of histamine at H1 receptors. They do not block H2 receptors (gastric acid secretion).

First Generation (Classical)

Lipid soluble, cross BBB. Cause Sedation and have Anticholinergic effects.

  • Diphenhydramine: Potent sedative, anticholinergic. Used in motion sickness.
  • Promethazine: Potent sedative, anti-emetic.
  • Chlorpheniramine: Less sedating. Common in cold remedies.
  • Cyclizine/Meclizine: Anti-vertigo/Motion sickness.
  • Cyproheptadine: Also blocks 5-HT. Used as appetite stimulant.

Second Generation (Newer)

Do not cross BBB significantly. Non-sedating. No anticholinergic side effects.

  • Cetirizine: Active metabolite of hydroxyzine. Minimal sedation.
  • Levocetirizine: Active isomer of cetirizine.
  • Loratadine: Long acting. No sedation.
  • Fexofenadine: Metabolite of terfenadine (non-cardiotoxic).
  • Azelastine: Topical (Nasal spray/Eye drops).

Therapeutic Uses:

  • Allergic disorders (Urticaria, Rhinitis, Hay fever).
  • Common cold (Palliative).
  • Motion Sickness (Promethazine, Diphenhydramine).
  • Vertigo (Cinnarizine, Betahistine).
  • Sedative/Hypnotic (Promethazine in children).
  • Parkinsonism (Anticholinergic property).

Betahistine: H1 agonist and H3 antagonist. Increases cochlear blood flow. Drug of choice for Meniere's disease.

ü§ï 5-HT (Serotonin) and Migraine

5-Hydroxytryptamine (Serotonin)

Found in Enterochromaffin cells (90%), Platelets, and CNS. It is synthesised from Tryptophan. Metabolised by MAO-A into 5-HIAA (diagnostic marker for Carcinoid syndrome).

Receptor Mechanism Clinical Importance
5-HT1 Gi (Inhibitory) 1A: Buspirone (Anxiety).
1B/1D: Triptans (Migraine).
5-HT2 Gq (Excitatory) Platelet aggregation, Smooth muscle contraction. Blocked by Cyproheptadine, Ketanserin.
5-HT3 Ion Channel CTZ (Vomiting). Blocked by Ondansetron.
5-HT4 Gs (Excitatory) GI Motility. Stimulated by Cisapride/Mosapride (Prokinetics).

Pharmacotherapy of Migraine

Migraine is a pulsatile, hemicranial headache often associated with nausea, vomiting, and photophobia. Pathophysiology involves initial vasoconstriction (aura) followed by excessive vasodilatation (headache) of cranial vessels, and neurogenic inflammation.

Treatment of Acute Attack (Abortive)

  • Mild/Moderate: NSAIDs (Aspirin, Paracetamol, Ibuprofen) + Antiemetic (Metoclopramide).
  • Moderate/Severe:
    • Triptans (Sumatriptan, Rizatriptan): Selective 5-HT1B/1D agonists. Cause constriction of dilated cranial vessels and inhibit neurogenic inflammation. Contraindicated in Ischaemic Heart Disease.
    • Ergot Alkaloids (Ergotamine): Non-selective 5-HT agonists + $\alpha$ adrenergic action. Potent vasoconstrictor. Risk of gangrene with overdose.

Prophylaxis (Prevention)

Indicated if attacks are frequent (>2-3/month) or severe.

  • Beta-blockers: Propranolol (Drug of Choice).
  • Calcium Channel Blockers: Flunarizine.
  • Anticonvulsants: Topiramate, Valproate.
  • Antidepressants: Amitriptyline (TCA).
  • 5-HT Antagonists: Pizotifen.

üß¨ Eicosanoids and Other Autacoids

Angiotensin

Renin-Angiotensin System (RAS): Renin (kidney) converts Angiotensinogen to Angiotensin I. ACE (lungs) converts Angiotensin I to Angiotensin II (Potent vasoconstrictor, releases Aldosterone).

Angiotensin Antagonists:

  • ACE Inhibitors: Captopril, Enalapril (See Chapter 30).
  • AT1 Receptor Blockers (ARBs): Losartan, Valsartan.

Kinins (Bradykinin)

Potent vasodilator, increases capillary permeability, and causes pain. Metabolised by Kininase II (same as ACE). ACE inhibitors increase bradykinin levels $\to$ Dry cough/Angioedema.

Prostaglandins (PGs) and Leukotrienes (LTs)

Derived from membrane phospholipid Arachidonic Acid.

Biosynthesis Pathways

  • Cyclooxygenase (COX) Pathway: Prostaglandins (PGE2, PGF2$\alpha$), Prostacyclin (PGI2), Thromboxane (TXA2). Inhibited by NSAIDs.
  • Lipoxygenase (LOX) Pathway: Leukotrienes (LTB4, LTC4, LTD4). Involved in Asthma. Inhibited by Zileuton.
Eicosanoid Major Actions Clinical Analogue/Antagonist
PGE2 Vasodilation, Uterine contraction, Gastric mucus secretion (cytoprotection), Fever, Pain. Dinoprostone (Induction of labour/abortion).
PGE1 Vasodilation, Patency of Ductus Arteriosus. Misoprostol (Peptic ulcer, Abortion). Alprostadil (Erectile dysfunction).
PGF2$\alpha$ Uterine contraction, Uveoscleral outflow (eyes). Carboprost (PPH). Latanoprost (Glaucoma).
PGI2 (Prostacyclin) Inhibits platelet aggregation, Vasodilation. Epoprostenol (Pulmonary hypertension, Dialysis).
TXA2 (Thromboxane) Promotes platelet aggregation, Vasoconstriction. Inhibited by Aspirin.
Leukotrienes (C4/D4) Potent Bronchoconstriction. Montelukast/Zafirlukast (Receptor antagonists for Asthma).
