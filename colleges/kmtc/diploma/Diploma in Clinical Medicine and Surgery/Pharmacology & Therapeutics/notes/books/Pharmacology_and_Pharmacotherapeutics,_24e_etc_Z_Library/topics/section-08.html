üíâ Drugs and Blood Coagulation

Drugs are often used in therapeutics to prevent blood coagulation and to arrest blood loss. They act on various stages of coagulation: platelet aggregation, fibrin formation, or fibrinolysis.

Hemostasis and Thrombosis

Hemostasis involves vasoconstriction, platelet adhesion/aggregation (primary hemostatic plug), and fibrin formation (coagulation cascade). Arterial thrombi (white thrombi) are platelet-rich, while venous thrombi (red thrombi) are fibrin-rich.

Antiplatelet Agents

These drugs inhibit platelet aggregation and are primarily used to prevent arterial thrombosis.

1. Aspirin

Mechanism: Irreversibly acetylates COX-1, inhibiting Thromboxane A2 (TXA2) synthesis for the life of the platelet (7-10 days). Low doses (75-150 mg/day) selectively inhibit platelet COX-1 while sparing endothelial Prostacyclin (PGI2) synthesis to some extent.

Uses: Secondary prevention of MI and stroke; unstable angina; post-angioplasty.

2. ADP Receptor Antagonists

Clopidogrel, Ticlopidine, Prasugrel, Ticagrelor.

  • Clopidogrel: Prodrug. Irreversibly blocks P2Y12 ADP receptors. Used in aspirin-intolerant patients and combined with aspirin in ACS/stenting. Adverse effects: Bleeding, interactions (activation inhibited by PPIs like omeprazole).
  • Ticagrelor: Reversible P2Y12 inhibitor. Faster onset.

3. Glycoprotein IIb/IIIa Antagonists

Abciximab, Tirofiban, Eptifibatide. Block the final common pathway of platelet aggregation (fibrinogen binding). Used IV in high-risk PCI/ACS.

4. Others

Dipyridamole: Phosphodiesterase inhibitor. Weak antiplatelet. Used with warfarin for prosthetic valves.

Anticoagulants

Used primarily for venous thrombosis and cardioembolic events.

1. Heparin (Unfractionated)

Mechanism: Activates Antithrombin III, which inactivates Thrombin (IIa) and Factor Xa. Rapid onset (IV/SC).

Monitoring: aPTT (Activated Partial Thromboplastin Time). Target: 1.5-2.5 times control.

Antidote: Protamine Sulfate (1 mg neutralizes 100 U heparin).

Adverse Effects: Bleeding, Heparin Induced Thrombocytopenia (HIT), Osteoporosis (long term).

2. Low Molecular Weight Heparins (LMWH)

Enoxaparin, Dalteparin. Selectively inhibit Factor Xa > IIa.

Advantages over Unfractionated Heparin:

  • Better bioavailability (SC).
  • Longer half-life (once/twice daily dosing).
  • Predictable response (no monitoring needed).
  • Lower risk of HIT and bleeding.

3. Oral Anticoagulants: Warfarin

Mechanism: Vitamin K antagonist. Inhibits synthesis of factors II, VII, IX, X and Protein C/S. Delayed onset (3-5 days).

Monitoring: PT/INR (Prothrombin Time/International Normalized Ratio). Target INR usually 2-3.

Antidote: Vitamin K1 (Phytonadione), Fresh Frozen Plasma (FFP).

Interactions: Extensive. Enzyme inducers (Rifampicin) decrease effect; Inhibitors (Cimetidine, Metronidazole) increase effect. Antibiotics increase effect (kill gut flora).

Contraindications: Pregnancy (Teratogenic - Fetal Warfarin Syndrome).

4. New Oral Anticoagulants (NOACs)

  • Dabigatran: Direct Thrombin Inhibitor.
  • Rivaroxaban, Apixaban: Direct Factor Xa Inhibitors.

Advantages: Fixed dosing, no monitoring, fewer interactions. Disadvantage: No specific antidote (historically; Idarucizumab now available for Dabigatran).

Fibrinolytics (Thrombolytics)

Streptokinase, Urokinase, Alteplase (tPA).

Mechanism: Activate Plasminogen to Plasmin, which lyses fibrin clots.

Uses: Acute MI (STEMI within 12h), massive Pulmonary Embolism, acute Ischemic Stroke (within 3-4.5h).

Adverse Effects: Hemorrhage, allergic reactions (Streptokinase).

Antifibrinolytics

Epsilon Aminocaproic Acid (EACA), Tranexamic Acid. Inhibit plasminogen activation. Used to treat overdose of fibrinolytics, menorrhagia, hemophilia.

üíä Drugs Effective in Iron Deficiency and Other Related Anemias

Anemia is a reduction in hemoglobin concentration below normal for age and sex.

Iron Deficiency Anemia

Iron Metabolism:

  • Total body iron: 3-5 g.
  • Sources: Heme iron (meat) is better absorbed than non-heme iron (vegetables).
  • Absorption: Duodenum/Jejunum. Enhanced by acid (Vit C), inhibited by phytates/phosphates/tannins.
  • Transport: Transferrin.
  • Storage: Ferritin (soluble), Hemosiderin (insoluble).

Treatment

1. Oral Iron: Preferred. Ferrous sulfate, Ferrous fumarate, Ferrous gluconate. Dose: 200 mg elemental iron/day.

Adverse Effects: Epigastric pain, nausea, constipation, black stools.

2. Parenteral Iron: Indicated for malabsorption, severe intolerance, non-compliance, rapid correction needed (pregnancy near term).

  • Iron Dextran: IM (Z-track) or IV. Risk of anaphylaxis (Test dose required).
  • Iron Sorbitol Citrate: IM only.
  • Iron Sucrose, Ferric Carboxymaltose: IV. Safer profile.

Acute Iron Poisoning

Common in children. Symptoms: Necrotizing gastroenteritis, shock, acidosis, liver failure.
Treatment: Gastric lavage with bicarbonate. Specific antidote: Desferrioxamine (Iron chelator).

Erythropoietin (EPO)

Growth factor produced by kidney. Stimulates RBC proliferation/maturation.

Uses: Anemia of Chronic Renal Failure, Cancer chemotherapy, Zidovudine therapy.

Adverse Effects: Hypertension, thrombotic events.

üß¨ Drugs Effective in Megaloblastic Anemias and Neutropenia

Vitamin B12 (Cyanocobalamin)

Essential for DNA synthesis (conversion of homocysteine to methionine and methylmalonyl CoA to succinyl CoA). Deficiency causes Megaloblastic Anemia and Neurological damage (Subacute Combined Degeneration).

Absorption: Requires Intrinsic Factor (secreted by gastric parietal cells) for absorption in terminal ileum.

Deficiency Causes: Pernicious anemia (IF deficiency), malabsorption, vegan diet.

Treatment: Parenteral Vitamin B12 (Hydroxycobalamin is preferred - better retention).

Folic Acid

Essential for DNA synthesis (one-carbon transfer reactions). Deficiency causes Megaloblastic Anemia but NO neurological damage.

Uses: Megaloblastic anemia (nutritional/pregnancy), Prophylaxis of Neural Tube Defects in fetus (start before conception), Methotrexate toxicity antidote (Folinic acid/Leucovorin).

The "Masking" Effect

Treating B12 deficiency with Folic acid alone corrects the anemia (hematological picture) but allows neurological damage to progress irreversibly. Always rule out B12 deficiency before treating megaloblastic anemia with folate.

Hematopoietic Growth Factors

  • Filgrastim (G-CSF): Stimulates neutrophil production. Used for neutropenia (chemotherapy-induced, aplastic anemia).
  • Sargramostim (GM-CSF): Stimulates granulocytes and macrophages.
  • Oprelvekin (IL-11): Stimulates platelet production (thrombocytopenia).

‚ö†Ô∏è Drug-Induced Blood Dyscrasias

Drugs can affect blood cells via direct toxicity or immunological mechanisms.

Condition Implicated Drugs
Aplastic Anemia Chloramphenicol, Cytotoxics, Phenylbutazone, Gold salts, Sulfonamides.
Agranulocytosis Clozapine, Antithyroids (Carbimazole), Analgin, Sulfonamides, Captopril.
Hemolytic Anemia G6PD Deficiency: Primaquine, Sulfonamides, Nitrofurantoin.
Autoimmune: Methyldopa, Penicillin.
Megaloblastic Anemia Methotrexate, Phenytoin, Trimethoprim (Folate antagonists).
Thrombocytopenia Heparin (HIT), Quinine, Thiazides, Sulfonamides.
