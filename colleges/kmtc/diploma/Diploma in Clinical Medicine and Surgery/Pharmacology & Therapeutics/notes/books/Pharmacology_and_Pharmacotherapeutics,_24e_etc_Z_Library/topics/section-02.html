üåê General Considerations

Anatomically, the nervous system in higher animals can be divided into:

  • The central nervous system (CNS); and
  • The peripheral nervous system consisting of somatic and autonomic nerve fibres.

The CNS is not only concerned with the regulation of specialised functions like circulation, digestion and respiration but it also is responsible for the psychic processes such as feeling, attitude, thoughts and memory. The ability to think logically, to learn from past experiences and to communicate appropriately are the unique qualities of man and can be attributed to the development of the highly specialised CNS.

Both the CNS and peripheral nervous system are analogous to an elaborate system of telegraphy wherein numerous wires and connections in the form of neurons and synapses bring in information from both internal and external sources. This information is received, 'decoded', decisions are made by various centres in CNS and instructions are sent out to various peripheral tissues to produce appropriate responses such as:

  • Psychic, as manifested by change in emotions, thought and attitude.
  • Somatic, as demonstrated by various types of body movements; and
  • Autonomic, as indicated by changes in respiration, circulation and visceral functions.
Table 5.1: Functions of various parts of central nervous system
Part of CNS Functions attributed
Cerebral cortex Higher functions like judgement, memory, fine voluntary movements, fine pain, tactile discrimination and temperature, special senses like vision, smell and hearing. It also acts as an important coordinator and exerts inhibitory control over lower centres.
Frontal lobes Thinking, integration of emotional behaviour.
Limbic system Integrates emotional state with motor and visceral activities.
Basal ganglia Extrapyramidal control of skeletal muscle tone, co-ordination of posture. Lesions produce tremors and rigidity in flexion.
Thalamus Relay centre for sensory pathways to cortex. Conscious appreciation of pain, temperature and crude touch sensations. Exerts regulatory control over visceral functions.
Hypothalamus Control of the autonomic nervous system, control of adenohypophysis. Important centre for integration of eating, drinking, sexual behaviour, temperature regulation, sleep, circadian rhythms and other vegetative functions.
Cerebellum Control of vestibular function and body posture.
Reticular formation Sleep-wakefulness cycle, control of blood pressure, respiration, swallowing, vomiting; transmission of crude pain, voluntary muscle tone and posture; control of upper motor neurons.
Spinal cord Reflex movements, control of muscle tone (particularly the red muscles) and the upper and the lower motor neurons through presynaptic and post-synaptic inhibition.

Drugs act on the CNS:

  • Directly on neurons and modify the neuronal functions.
  • Reflexly by sending afferent impulses to the CNS via the chemoreceptors, baroreceptors and peripheral nerves, thereby eliciting psychic, somatic or visceral responses; and
  • By affecting the nutrition and oxygen supply of the CNS by altering its blood supply or affecting its metabolism e.g. by causing hypoglycemia or ammonia intoxication.

To reach the CNS, a drug must have a high lipid solubility (high oil/water partition coefficient) or a specialised transport mechanism. Ionized drugs normally cannot cross BBB.

Neurochemical transmission in the nervous system: Because communication within the nervous system is almost exclusively through chemical messengers, neurosecretion is a fundamental property of all neurons. A neurosecretion is a chemical released at the nerve terminals; those released into the blood circulation are called neurohormones e.g. hypothalamic hormones released into the hypothalamo-pituitary portal circulation.

Table 5.2: Some neurotransmitters found in the nervous system
Name Distribution Effects
- Amines
Acetylcholine (ACh) Widely distributed in almost all regions in CNS, predominantly in cerebral cortex, ascending reticular system, cerebellum and spinal cord. Mainly excitatory and at some places inhibitory in nature. Central effects can be blocked by atropine.
Noradrenaline (NA) High concentration in the hypothalamus, limbic system and reticular formation in the brain stem. Generally excitatory and inhibitory in few areas.
Dopamine (DA) Mainly in the basal ganglia, to some extent in the median eminence of hypothalamus, adenohypophysis and frontal cortex. Predominantly inhibitory action and in some areas excitatory. Deficiency causes extrapyramidal disturbances.
5-Hydroxytryptamine (5-HT, serotonin) Extends into the cerebral cortex, raphe nucleus in pons and medulla, hippocampal areas. Both excitatory and inhibitory effects.
Histamine (H) Distribution similar to NA and 5-HT but irregular. Mainly inhibitory effects. Functions uncertain.
- Amino acids
L-glutamic acid and aspartic acid (EAA) Widely distributed in CNS and spinal cord Excitatory effects.
Gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA), glycine GABA in CNS; glycine mainly in the spinal cord and brain stem. Inhibitory effects in brain and spinal cord.
- Peptides
Substance P Hypothalamus, thalamus, basal ganglia; the dorsal column and dorsal roots of the spinal cord; enteric nervous system (ENS) Excitatory
Cholecystokinin Brain (cortex, hippocampus, limbic system and hypothalamus), pancreas Inhibits eating and stimulates oxytocin release.

üç∑ Aliphatic Alcohols

Ethyl Alcohol (Ethanol)

Pharmacological actions:

  • Topical: Applied to the skin, it evaporates quickly. It has a cooling and refreshing effect, and is used for reducing the temperature in fevers. Higher concentrations denature proteins. Alcohol in the concentration of 70% by weight acts as an antiseptic.
  • Gastrointestinal tract: Taken orally, it gives a local feeling of warmth and increases the salivary secretion. 50 ml of 7-10% alcohol increases the gastric secretion. Higher concentrations inhibit both gastric motility and secretion, and have a direct toxic effect on gastric mucosa.
  • Central nervous system: Ethanol is primarily a CNS depressant and acts by enhancing the inhibitory $\mathrm{GABA}_{\mathrm{A}}$ receptor activity and inhibiting the excitatory NMDA receptors. The initial CNS effect is due to depression of the reticular activating system. The cortex is thus released from the integrating control or inhibitions required for purposeful activity.
  • Cardiovascular system: Alcohol in moderate amounts dilates skin vessels by depressing the vasomotor centre, resulting in flushing and feeling of warmth. Regular consumption of alcohol is often associated with hypertension.
  • Liver: Ethanol causes dose-related hepatotoxicity. The common effects are fatty infiltration and hepatomegaly.

Acute Alcohol Intoxication

Table 6.1: Correlation of blood alcohol levels with behavioural changes
Blood concentration (mg per 100 ml) Behavioural changes Interpretation
<50 Not significant Not under the influence of alcohol but may affect the reaction time
50-100 Feeling of exaltation, talkativeness Possibly under the influence
100-200 Emotionally unstable, motor incoordination, nystagmus Probably under the influence
200-300 Staggering, loss of self control Definitely under the influence
300-400 Stupor-dead drunk Grossly intoxicated
400-500 Coma, anaesthetic effect
>500 Respiratory arrest and death

Chronic Alcoholism and Dependence

Withdrawal syndrome: Sudden withdrawal of alcohol in alcoholics leads to withdrawal syndrome. Delirium tremens, though rare, is the most severe component of the abstinence syndrome.

Table 6.2: Alcohol withdrawal syndrome
Minor Symptoms
(6-12 hours)		 Tremors, nausea, vomiting, sweating, tachycardia, hypertension, insomnia, anxiety and agitation.
Hallucinations
(12-24 hours)		 Visual, auditory or tactile hallucinations with intact orientation.
Seizures
(24-48 hours)		 Generalised tonic-clonic seizures.

(48-96 hours)		 Agitation, global confusion, disorientation, hallucinations, fever, autonomic hyperactivity.

Treatment of Alcohol Dependence:

  • Detoxification: Benzodiazepines (diazepam, chlordiazepoxide) are the treatment of choice.
  • Support therapy (Anti-craving drugs):
    • Naltrexone: 50-100 mg once daily, reduces craving.
    • Acamprosate: Analogue of GABA, reduces voluntary alcohol consumption.
    • Disulfiram: Inhibits aldehyde dehydrogenase. Causes toxic reaction (antabuse reaction) if alcohol is consumed.

Methyl Alcohol (Methanol)

This is used as a solvent. It is mainly oxidised to formaldehyde and subsequently to formic acid, which is toxic. Ingestion leads to CNS depression, acidosis, and toxic effects on retinal cells (blindness).

Treatment of Methanol Poisoning

  • Ethyl alcohol: 10% IV; competes for alcohol dehydrogenase.
  • Fomepizole: 100 mg IV; inhibits alcohol dehydrogenase.
  • Folinic acid/Folic acid: To promote metabolic degradation of formate.
  • Sodium bicarbonate: To correct acidosis.
  • Hemodialysis: In severe cases.

üíâ General Anaesthetics

General anaesthetics are the agents which bring about loss of all modalities of sensation, particularly pain (analgesia), inhibition of autonomic reflexes, relaxation of skeletal muscle, amnesia and a reversible loss of consciousness.

Inhalational Anaesthetics

  • Volatile liquids: Diethyl ether, Halothane, Enflurane, Isoflurane, Desflurane, Sevoflurane.
  • Gases: Nitrous oxide.

Intravenous Anaesthetics

  • Ultra short acting barbiturates: Thiopental sodium, Methohexital.
  • Propofol
  • Benzodiazepines: Midazolam, Diazepam.
  • Dissociative: Ketamine.
  • Opioids: Fentanyl.
  • Miscellaneous: Etomidate, Dexmedetomidine.

Stages of Anaesthesia (Guedel)

Refers mainly to ether.

  • Stage I: Stage of Analgesia: From beginning of inhalation to loss of consciousness. Analgesia is produced.
  • Stage II: Stage of Delirium: From loss of consciousness to beginning of surgical anaesthesia. Excitement, shouting, increased muscular activity, irregular respiration.
  • Stage III: Stage of Surgical Anaesthesia: Regular respiration, loss of reflexes, muscle relaxation. Divided into 4 planes.
  • Stage IV: Stage of Respiratory Paralysis: Severe depression of medullary centres, respiratory arrest.

Important Agents

Halothane: Non-inflammable, pleasant, potent. Causes bronchodilatation. Disadvantages: Poor analgesic, muscle relaxation inadequate, sensitises heart to catecholamines (arrhythmias), hepatotoxicity (rare).

Isoflurane: Muscle relaxant, bronchodilator, does not sensitise heart to catecholamines. Preferred agent in neurosurgery.

Nitrous Oxide: "Laughing gas". Good analgesic, poor anaesthetic (MAC 105%). Used with O2 and other agents. Disadvantages: Diffusion hypoxia, expansion of closed gas spaces (pneumothorax, etc.).

Thiopental: Ultra short acting barbiturate. Rapid pleasant induction. Action terminated by redistribution to fat. Poor analgesic/muscle relaxant.

Propofol: Rapid induction and recovery ("clear headed"). Anti-emetic. Used for day-care surgery. Pain on injection.

Ketamine: Produces "Dissociative Anaesthesia" (analgesia, amnesia, catatonia). Raises BP and heart rate. Bronchodilator. Causes hallucinations/delirium during recovery.

Neuroleptanalgesia: Combination of a neuroleptic (Droperidol) and an opioid (Fentanyl). Produces a state of apathy and analgesia where patient is conscious but indifferent.

üí§ Sedatives, Hypnotics and Sleep Disorders

A hypnotic drug is one which produces sleep resembling natural sleep. A sedative is a drug that reduces excitement, calms the patient, and is commonly used as an anxiolytic.

Benzodiazepines (BDZ)

Mechanism: Bind to specific BDZ binding site on $\mathrm{GABA}_{\mathrm{A}}$ receptor complex. They facilitate GABA action by increasing the frequency of chloride channel opening. (Barbiturates increase the duration).

Actions: Anxiolytic, Sedative-Hypnotic, Anticonvulsant, Muscle relaxant, Anterograde amnesia.

Antagonist: Flumazenil is a specific competitive antagonist used in BDZ poisoning.

Table 8.1: Benzodiazepine derivatives and oral doses
Name Hypnotic Dose (mg) Daily Anxiolytic Dose (mg) Half-life Category
Diazepam 5-10 10-30 Long (>24 hrs)
Flurazepam 15-30 15-30 Long
Nitrazepam 5-10 5-10 Intermediate (5-24 hrs)
Lorazepam 0.5-2 2-4 Intermediate
Temazepam 7.5-30 10-30 Intermediate
Midazolam 7.5-15 - Short (<5 hrs)
Triazolam 0.125-0.25 - Short

Non-benzodiazepine Hypnotics (Z-drugs)

Zolpidem, Zaleplon, Zopiclone: Bind selectively to the BDZ site on GABA receptors (specifically $\alpha1$ subunit). Potent hypnotics with minimal muscle relaxant or anticonvulsant effects. Less disruption of sleep architecture.

Barbiturates

Depress CNS in dose-dependent manner (Sedation -> Hypnosis -> Anaesthesia -> Coma). Induce liver enzymes. Low therapeutic index. Have largely been replaced by BDZ for insomnia.

Specific uses: Phenobarbitone (Epilepsy), Thiopental (Anaesthesia).

‚ö° Drugs Effective in Seizure Disorders

Classification of Seizures (Table 9.1)

  • I Generalised seizures: Absence (Petit mal), Tonic-clonic (Grand mal), Tonic, Atonic, Myoclonic, Infantile spasms.
  • II Focal seizures: Without cognitive impairment (Simple partial), With dyscognitive features (Complex partial), Focal leading to generalised.

Mechanisms of Antiepileptic Drugs (AEDs)

Table 9.2: Mechanism of Action of AEDs
Mechanism Drugs
Inhibition of Na+ channels Phenytoin, Carbamazepine, Lamotrigine, Topiramate, Zonisamide, Valproate
Inhibition of T-type Ca++ channels Ethosuximide, Valproate, Zonisamide
GABA facilitation Barbiturates, Benzodiazepines, Gabapentin (release), Vigabatrin (transaminase inhibition), Tiagabine (uptake inhibition), Valproate, Topiramate
Glutamate inhibition Lamotrigine, Topiramate, Felbamate

Specific Drugs

Phenytoin: Blocks voltage-gated Na+ channels. Kinetics: Saturation (zero order) kinetics at therapeutic levels. Induces liver enzymes. ADR: Gingival hyperplasia, hirsutism, megaloblastic anemia, osteomalacia, teratogenicity (fetal hydantoin syndrome), ataxia, nystagmus.

Carbamazepine: Blocks Na+ channels. Potent enzyme inducer. Used for partial and tonic-clonic seizures, and Trigeminal Neuralgia. ADR: Drowsiness, ataxia, hyponatremia.

Sodium Valproate: Broad spectrum. Blocks Na+ channels, T-type Ca++ channels, and increases GABA. Enzyme inhibitor. Used for absence, myoclonic, and tonic-clonic seizures. ADR: Hepatotoxicity (rare but serious), weight gain, hair loss, teratogenicity (spina bifida).

Ethosuximide: Blocks T-type Ca++ channels in thalamus. Specific for Absence Seizures.

Newer AEDs: Lamotrigine, Gabapentin, Topiramate, Levetiracetam, Tiagabine, Vigabatrin. Generally better tolerated, fewer interactions.

Management of Status Epilepticus

A medical emergency. Convulsive seizure lasting >15-30 mins.

üöë

Treatment Protocol (Table 9.9)

  1. Maintain airway, breathing, circulation.
  2. Lorazepam 4 mg IV slowly (drug of choice) OR Diazepam 10 mg IV.
  3. Follow with Phenytoin 15-20 mg/kg IV infusion (in saline, not glucose) to prevent recurrence.
  4. If resistant: Phenobarbitone, Valproate IV, or General Anaesthesia (Propofol/Thiopental).

üíä Opioid Analgesics and Antagonists

Pain is an unpleasant sensory and emotional experience, associated with actual or potential tissue damage. Analgesics are drugs which relieve pain without causing loss of consciousness.

Classification

  • Agonists: Morphine, Codeine, Pethidine, Methadone, Fentanyl, Tramadol.
  • Partial Agonists: Buprenorphine.
  • Mixed Agonist-Antagonists: Pentazocine, Nalbuphine, Butorphanol.
  • Antagonists: Naloxone, Naltrexone.

Mechanism of Action

Opioids act on specific G-protein coupled receptors: Mu ($\mu$), Kappa ($\kappa$), and Delta ($\delta$). They inhibit adenylate cyclase, facilitate $\mathrm{K}^+$ channel opening (hyperpolarisation), and inhibit $\mathrm{Ca}^{++}$ channel opening (preventing transmitter release).

Table 10.1: Pharmacological effects associated with opioid receptor types
Receptor Effects
Mu ($\mu$) Supraspinal/spinal analgesia, euphoria, respiratory depression, sedation, miosis, decreased GI motility, physical dependence.
Kappa ($\kappa$) Spinal analgesia, sedation, miosis, dysphoria, psychomimetic effects.
Delta ($\delta$) Spinal analgesia, decreased GI motility.

Morphine

Pharmacological Actions:

  • CNS: Analgesia, Euphoria (or Dysphoria), Sedation, Respiratory depression (dose-dependent), Cough suppression, Miosis (pin-point pupils), Nausea/Vomiting (CTZ stimulation).
  • CVS: Vasodilatation (histamine release), hypotension (postural). Used in LVF (relieves dyspnoea/anxiety, reduces preload).
  • GI Tract: Increases tone (spasm), decreases motility $\rightarrow$ Constipation. Increases biliary pressure.
  • GU Tract: Urinary retention.

Acute Morphine Poisoning: Triad of Respiratory depression, Pin-point pupils, and Coma. Treatment: Respiratory support and Naloxone (specific antagonist).

Pethidine (Meperidine)

Synthetic opioid. Differences from Morphine:

  • Less spasmogenic (less constipation/biliary spasm).
  • Has atropine-like action (tachycardia, dry mouth).
  • No antitussive activity.
  • Toxic metabolite (Norpethidine) causes excitation/convulsions.
  • Interacts with MAO inhibitors (excitation/coma).

Other Opioids

Methadone: Long acting, effective orally. Used for maintenance in opioid dependence (substitution therapy).

Tramadol: Weak $\mu$ agonist + inhibits NA/5-HT reuptake. Less respiratory depression/addiction.

Fentanyl: 100x more potent than morphine. Rapid onset, short duration. Used in anaesthesia and transdermal patches.

Buprenorphine: Partial $\mu$ agonist. Long duration, tight binding. Precipitates withdrawal in addicts.

Pentazocine: Mixed agonist/antagonist ($\kappa$ agonist, weak $\mu$ antagonist). Causes dysphoria/psychomimetic effects.

üî• NSAIDs and Antipyretic Analgesics

Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) relieve pain, reduce fever, and suppress inflammation by inhibiting the enzyme Cyclooxygenase (COX), thereby blocking prostaglandin (PG) synthesis.

üîÑ

Mechanism of Action

  • COX-1: Constitutive. physiologic (gastric protection, platelet aggregation, renal function).
  • COX-2: Inducible (at sites of inflammation).

Traditional NSAIDs inhibit both. Selective COX-2 inhibitors spare COX-1 (less GI toxicity) but may increase cardiovascular risk.

Salicylates (Aspirin)

Actions: Analgesic, Antipyretic, Anti-inflammatory. Irreversibly inhibits platelet COX (Antiplatelet action for 7-10 days).

Adverse Effects: Gastric irritation/ulceration, Bleeding tendency, Hypersensitivity (bronchospasm), Salicylism (tinnitus, vertigo), Reye's syndrome (avoid in viral fever in children).

Uses: Pain, Fever, RA, OA, Rheumatic fever, Prophylaxis of MI/Stroke (antiplatelet).

Para-aminophenol Derivatives (Paracetamol)

Potent analgesic and antipyretic. Weak/No anti-inflammatory action. Does not cause gastric irritation or bleeding.

‚ö†Ô∏è

Paracetamol Toxicity

Toxic metabolite (NAPQI) causes hepatic necrosis. Antidote: N-acetylcysteine (replenishes glutathione).

Other NSAIDs

Drug Key Characteristics
Ibuprofen Propionic acid derivative. Better tolerated than aspirin. Used for pain/inflammation.
Naproxen Longer acting propionic acid derivative. Potent anti-inflammatory.
Diclofenac Potent. Accumulates in synovial fluid. High GI/renal risk.
Indomethacin Very potent anti-inflammatory. High toxicity (CNS, GI). Used in acute gout, PDA closure.
Piroxicam Long acting (once daily). High GI toxicity.
Ketorolac Potent analgesic (comparable to morphine). Used for post-op pain.

Selective COX-2 Inhibitors

Celecoxib, Etoricoxib: "Stomach friendly". Do not inhibit platelet aggregation. Increase risk of thrombotic events (MI, stroke). Contraindicated in IHD.

‚òï CNS Stimulants

Xanthine Alkaloids (Caffeine, Theophylline)

Mechanism: Phosphodiesterase inhibition (increase cAMP), Adenosine receptor blockade.

Actions:

  • CNS: Stimulation, alertness, reduced fatigue. High doses cause insomnia, tremors, convulsions.
  • CVS: Heart stimulation (tachycardia), peripheral vasodilation (but cerebral vasoconstriction).
  • Kidney: Weak diuresis.
  • Smooth Muscle: Bronchodilation (Theophylline).

Psychomotor Stimulants

Amphetamines, Methylphenidate: Release NA/DA and block reuptake.

Uses:

  • ADHD: Methylphenidate, Atomoxetine.
  • Narcolepsy: Amphetamine, Modafinil.
  • Obesity: Anorexiants (Fenfluramine - withdrawn).

Adverse Effects: Addiction, psychosis, hypertension, growth retardation in children.

üß† Antipsychotic Drugs

Used for Schizophrenia and other psychoses. Also called Neuroleptics.

Typical (First Generation)

Block $\mathrm{D}_2$ receptors strongly. Treat positive symptoms. High risk of EPR.

  • Chlorpromazine: Low potency, high sedation/autonomic effects.
  • Haloperidol: High potency, less sedation, high EPR.

Atypical (Second Generation)

Block $5\text{-HT}_{2A} > \mathrm{D}_2$. Treat positive and negative symptoms. Low EPR risk. Metabolic side effects.

  • Clozapine: Gold standard for resistant cases. Risk of Agranulocytosis.
  • Risperidone: High EPS at high doses. Hyperprolactinemia.
  • Olanzapine: Weight gain, diabetes risk.
  • Quetiapine: Sedating.
  • Aripiprazole: Partial D2 agonist.

Adverse Reactions

  • Extrapyramidal Reactions (EPR): Acute dystonia, Parkinsonism, Akathisia (restlessness), Tardive Dyskinesia (late, orofacial movements).
  • Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome (NMS): Rigidity, hyperthermia, autonomic instability. Medical emergency.
  • Endocrine: Hyperprolactinemia (galactorrhea, amenorrhea, gynecomastia).
  • Autonomic: Dry mouth, constipation, urinary retention, postural hypotension.

üòå Anxiolytics and Antidepressants

Antidepressants

Increase monoamines (NA, 5-HT) in synapse. Effect takes 2-4 weeks.

Class Examples Features
TCAs (Tricyclic) Imipramine, Amitriptyline Block NA/5-HT reuptake. Also block M, $\alpha$, H1 receptors (Side effects: dry mouth, sedation, hypotension, cardiotoxicity).
SSRIs Fluoxetine, Sertraline, Escitalopram Selectively block 5-HT reuptake. Safer. Side effects: Nausea, sexual dysfunction. First line for Depression, OCD, Panic.
SNRIs Venlafaxine, Duloxetine Block NA/5-HT reuptake. Used in severe depression, neuropathic pain.
MAO Inhibitors Moclobemide (RIMA) Reversible inhibition of MAO-A. Safer than older irreversible MAOIs (Cheese reaction).

Mood Stabilizers

Lithium Carbonate: Treatment of choice for Mania and Bipolar Disorder (Prophylaxis). Narrow therapeutic index (0.5-1.5 mEq/L). Toxicity: Tremor, ataxia, convulsions, renal toxicity, hypothyroidism.

Alternatives: Valproate, Carbamazepine, Lamotrigine.

Anxiolytics

Benzodiazepines: (See Chapter 8). Acute anxiety.

Buspirone: Partial 5-HT1A agonist. Non-sedating, no dependence. Slow onset (weeks). For GAD.

Beta-blockers (Propranolol): For performance anxiety (somatic symptoms).

üö∂ Parkinsonism and Neurodegenerative Disorders

Parkinsonism: Degeneration of nigrostriatal dopaminergic neurons $\rightarrow$ Dopamine deficiency + Cholinergic preponderance. Features: Tremor, Rigidity, Bradykinesia.

Drug Classification

Dopaminergic System

  • Precursor: Levodopa (+ Carbidopa)
  • DA Agonists: Bromocriptine, Ropinirole, Pramipexole
  • MAO-B Inhibitor: Selegiline, Rasagiline
  • COMT Inhibitor: Entacapone, Tolcapone
  • DA Releaser: Amantadine

Cholinergic System

  • Central Anticholinergics: Trihexyphenidyl (Benzhexol), Benztropine. Used mainly for tremor and drug-induced parkinsonism.

Levodopa + Carbidopa: Gold standard. L-dopa crosses BBB and converts to DA. Carbidopa inhibits peripheral DOPA decarboxylase (does not cross BBB), reducing peripheral side effects and increasing brain entry. ADR: Nausea, hypotension, arrhythmias, dyskinesias, "On-off" phenomenon.

Alzheimer's Disease

Cholinergic deficiency. Treatment: Central Acetylcholinesterase inhibitors (Donepezil, Rivastigmine, Galantamine) and NMDA antagonist (Memantine).
