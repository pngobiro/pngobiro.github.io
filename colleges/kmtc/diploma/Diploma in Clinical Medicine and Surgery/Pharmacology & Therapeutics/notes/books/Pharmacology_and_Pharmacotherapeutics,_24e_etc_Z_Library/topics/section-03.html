üß¨ Cocaine, Procaine and Other Synthetic Local Anaesthetics

Local anaesthetics are drugs which, when applied directly to peripheral nerves, block nerve conduction and abolish all sensations in the part supplied by the nerve. They are applied to somatic nerves and act on axons, cell body, dendrites and synapses.

Classification

I. Natural

  • Cocaine

II. Synthetic Nitrogenous Compounds

  • Esters (Derivatives of PABA):
    • Freely soluble: Procaine, Amethocaine
    • Poorly soluble: Benzocaine, Orthocaine
  • Amides (Derivatives of acetanilide): Lignocaine (Lidocaine), Prilocaine, Bupivacaine, Ropivacaine
  • Quinoline derivatives: Cinchocaine (Nupercaine)
  • Acridine derivatives: Bucricaine

General Properties

Synthetic local anaesthetic drugs have many properties in common. They possess varying degrees of water and lipoidal solubility; since the nervous tissue is rich in lipid, lipoidal solubility helps the drug to move into the neuronal fibre, while water solubility helps to get the drug to the site of action from the site of injection or application.

Majority of the clinically useful local anaesthetics are nitrogenous compounds, either Esters (e.g. procaine, tetracaine) or Amides (e.g. lignocaine, prilocaine bupivacaine and ropivacaine). Their generic name ends with the suffix 'caine'.

Mechanism of Action

Sodium Channel Blockade

Local anaesthetics block both the generation and the conduction of the nerve impulse. They bind to receptors near the intracellular end of the voltage gated sodium channels. This prevents the increase in permeability of the cell membrane to $\mathrm{Na}^{+}$ ion, the first event in depolarisation. Thus, an action potential is not generated. This action is known as membrane stabilising effect.

A smaller nerve fibre presents a greater surface area per unit volume for the action of an anaesthetic than a larger fibre. Smaller fibres are, therefore, blocked first. The order of blockade is:

  1. Autonomic fibres
  2. Sensory fibres conducting temperature and pain
  3. Sensory fibres carrying touch, pressure and vibration sensations
  4. Motor fibres

Recovery of function occurs in the reverse order.

Note: Local anaesthetics are less effective when injected into an inflamed area (acidic pH causes ionization preventing entry into nerve).

Pharmacological Actions

  • Central nervous system: They stimulate the CNS and cause restlessness, tremors and in toxic doses, convulsions. Central stimulation is followed by depression; death is usually due to respiratory depression. Cocaine in smaller doses acts more prominently on the higher centres causing euphoria.
  • Cardiovascular system: These drugs are myocardiac depressants (membrane stabilising effect). Higher concentrations may lead to cardiac arrhythmias and cardiac arrest. All drugs (except cocaine) produce hypotension by direct action on vessel wall. Cocaine is a vasoconstrictor.

Absorption, Fate and Excretion

Esters (procaine) are metabolised by hydrolysis in both the liver and plasma (plasma pseudocholinesterase). Amides (lignocaine) are dealkylated by the liver. Slowly detoxified drugs produce greater systemic toxicity.

When used by infiltration, the absorption can be retarded by combining it with a vasoconstrictor agent like adrenaline (1:200,000).

Adverse Reactions

  • Allergic reaction: Mild dermatitis to anaphylaxis. Generally seen with ester type.
  • CVS: Hypotension and cardiac arrest. Treat with vasoconstrictors and cardiac massage.
  • CNS: Excitement and convulsions. Treat with diazepam or thiopentone.

  • Enquire about the history of allergy.
  • Drugs should be given cautiously in the presence of liver and myocardial damage.

Specific Drugs

Cocaine

Natural alkaloid. Only local anaesthetic with vasoconstrictor properties (blocks reuptake of NA). Potent CNS stimulant (Euphoria). Drug of abuse.

  • Acute Toxicity: Hypertension, tachycardia, ventricular arrhythmias, convulsions, mydriasis, hyperthermia.
  • Chronic Toxicity: Addiction, anorexia, emaciation, hallucinations.

Procaine

Short acting ester. Poorly absorbed from mucous membranes (no topical use). Metabolised to PABA (interferes with sulfonamides). Standard for comparison.

Lignocaine (Lidocaine)

Most commonly used amide. Stable, quick onset, high penetration. Effective topically. Used for infiltration (0.5-1%), nerve block (1-2%), surface anaesthesia (4%). Also used as antiarrhythmic.

EMLA (Eutectic Mixture of Local Anaesthetics): Lignocaine + Prilocaine. Oil form with lower melting point. Penetrates intact skin. Used for venipuncture.

Bupivacaine

Long acting amide (up to 8 hours). 4 times more potent than lignocaine. More cardiotoxic than lignocaine. Used for spinal and epidural anaesthesia.

Ropivacaine

Amide. Less potent than bupivacaine but less cardiotoxic. More motor sparing (good for labour).

Amethocaine (Tetracaine)

Potent long acting ester. Effective topically. High toxicity.

Therapeutic Uses

  • Surface Anaesthesia: Amethocaine, Lignocaine.
  • Infiltration Anaesthesia: Procaine, Lignocaine.
  • Nerve Block: Lignocaine.
  • Spinal Anaesthesia: Lignocaine, Bupivacaine (injected into subarachnoid space). Produces sympathetic blockade (hypotension).
  • Epidural Anaesthesia: Injected outside dura.
  • Systemic Uses: Lignocaine for cardiac arrhythmias.
