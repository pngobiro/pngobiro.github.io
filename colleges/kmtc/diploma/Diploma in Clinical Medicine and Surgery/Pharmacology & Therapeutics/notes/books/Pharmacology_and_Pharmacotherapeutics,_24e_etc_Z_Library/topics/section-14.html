Principles of Drug Application

Skin diseases are common in clinical practice. The skin forms a barrier between the organism and its external environment. The absorption of drugs into the skin is slow and incomplete. It depends on:

  • The lipid solubility of the preparation.
  • The state of hydration of the stratum corneum - Increased hydration increases the drug penetration.
  • Drug concentration in the vehicle.
  • Thickness of the skin (the thicker the skin the lower the drug penetration).
  • Quantity of the preparation applied.
  • The presence of inflamed skin which allows higher penetration of a drug.
  • The use of an occlusive dressing which increases the drug penetration into the skin.

Vehicles and Formulations

Topical preparations consist of two parts: (1) the base or vehicle and (2) the active ingredient(s).

Vehicle Type Characteristics & Uses
Powders Absorb moisture, reduce friction, cooling effect. (Talc, starch, zinc stearate).
Lotions Liquid preparations. Cooling effect via evaporation. Used for subacute inflammation.
Wet Dressings For acute exudative inflammation with marked oozing. (Potassium permanganate 0.01-0.05%).
Creams/Ointments Lubricating (Emollients). Soothe and hydrate dry/scaling disorders. Ointments are more hydrating than creams.
Pastes Stiffer, adhesive. Protect skin from external irritants and sunlight.

Drug Therapy of Skin Infections

1. Bacterial Infections

Impetigo: Superficial infection (Staph/Strep). Treatment: Cleaning/debridement + Topical antibiotics (Mupirocin 2%, Framycetin, Bacitracin). Systemic antibiotics (Cloxacillin, Erythromycin) indicated if risk of glomerulonephritis or severe infection.

Furuncle (Boil): Staphylococcal infection of hair follicle. Treatment: Local antiseptics, fomentation. Systemic antibiotics in severe cases.

2. Viral Infections

Herpes Simplex: Topical Acyclovir 5% (less effective than systemic).
Herpes Zoster: Oral Acyclovir (800 mg 5 times/day). Post-herpetic neuralgia treated with topical Capsaicin, analgesics, or amitriptyline.
Warts: Salicylic acid, Podophyllin (15-25%), Trichloracetic acid, Cryotherapy.

3. Fungal Infections

Superficial Mycoses (Ringworm, Candidiasis, Tinea Versicolor):
Topical Azoles: Clotrimazole, Miconazole, Ketoconazole, Econazole.
Whitfield's Ointment: Benzoic acid (6%) + Salicylic acid (3%). Cheap and effective.
Tolnaftate, Terbinafine (1%): Effective for dermatophytosis.
Systemic Therapy: Griseofulvin, Fluconazole, Itraconazole (for onychomycosis/extensive lesions).

4. Scabies and Pediculosis

Scabies (Sarcoptes scabiei)

  • Permethrin (5%): Drug of choice. Applied to whole body, left for 8-12 hours.
  • Benzyl Benzoate (25%): Emulsion. Effective but irritant.
  • Gamma Benzene Hexachloride (Lindane 1%): Neurotoxic potential. Avoid in children.
  • Ivermectin: Oral (200 mcg/kg). Single dose, repeat after 2 weeks.

Pediculosis (Lice)

  • Permethrin (1%): Cream/Lotion. Drug of choice.
  • Malathion (0.5%): Ovicidal. Inflammable.
  • Ivermectin: Oral.
  • DDT/Gamma BHC: Resistance common.

Acne Vulgaris

Pathophysiology involves increased sebum, hyperkeratinisation (comedones), and Propionibacterium acnes proliferation.

Class Drugs Mechanism/Use
Comedolytics Tretinoin (Retinoic acid), Adapalene Normalise keratinisation. Apply topically. Avoid sun exposure. Contraindicated in pregnancy.
Keratolytics Benzoyl Peroxide, Salicylic acid Peeling agents. Benzoyl peroxide is also antibacterial against P. acnes.
Antibiotics Topical: Clindamycin, Erythromycin
Systemic: Doxycycline, Minocycline		 Inhibit P. acnes and inflammation.
Sebostatics Isotretinoin (Oral) For severe cystic acne. Inhibits sebum. Highly Teratogenic.
Hormonal Estrogens, Cyproterone acetate Anti-androgenic action (in females).

Psoriasis

Chronic inflammatory dermatosis with plaques and scaling.

Topical Therapy

  • Emollients: Hydrate and soften scales.
  • Keratolytics: Salicylic acid (2-10%).
  • Coal Tar / Dithranol: Cytostatic. Stain skin/clothes.
  • Glucocorticoids: Topical. Rapid relief but relapse common.
  • Calcipotriol: Vitamin D analogue. Inhibits proliferation. Safe and effective.
  • Tazarotene: Topical retinoid.

Systemic Therapy (Severe Cases)

  • Phototherapy: PUVA (Psoralen + UVA) or UVB.
  • Methotrexate: Folic acid antagonist. Cytostatic/Immunosuppressant.
  • Acitretin: Oral retinoid. Teratogenic (avoid pregnancy for 3 years after stopping).
  • Cyclosporine: Immunosuppressant.
  • Biologicals: Etanercept, Infliximab, Adalimumab (TNF-$\alpha$ inhibitors), Ustekinumab (IL-12/23 blocker). Expensive.

Pharmacokinetics of Ocular Drugs

Most drugs are administered topically (drops/ointments). Only ~1% penetrates the cornea. Absorption depends on lipid solubility (epithelium is lipophilic, stroma is hydrophilic). Ointments increase contact time. Systemic absorption can occur via nasolacrimal duct.

Antimicrobial Agents

Class Drugs Indications
Antibacterial Sulfacetamide (10-30%), Chloramphenicol, Gentamicin, Tobramycin, Ciprofloxacin, Ofloxacin, Moxifloxacin. Conjunctivitis, Corneal ulcers, Blepharitis.
Antiviral Acyclovir (3% ointment), Trifluridine, Ganciclovir. Herpes simplex keratitis.
Antifungal Natamycin (5%), Amphotericin B, Fluconazole. Fungal keratitis.

Anti-inflammatory Agents

  • NSAIDs: Flurbiprofen, Diclofenac, Ketorolac drops. Used to prevent intraoperative miosis and for allergic conjunctivitis.
  • Glucocorticoids: Dexamethasone, Betamethasone, Prednisolone. Used for Uveitis, allergic conditions, post-op inflammation. Hazards: Glaucoma (steroid-induced), Cataract, superinfection (fungal/viral). Contraindicated in dendritic ulcer (Herpes simplex).

Mydriatics and Miotics

Mydriatics (Dilate Pupil)

  • Antimuscarinics: Atropine (Long acting, 7-10 days cycloplegia), Homatropine, Cyclopentolate, Tropicamide (Shortest acting, for refraction).
  • Sympathomimetics: Phenylephrine (No cycloplegia).

Miotics (Constrict Pupil)

  • Cholinomimetics: Pilocarpine.
  • Anticholinesterases: Physostigmine, Echothiophate (rarely used).

Drug Therapy of Glaucoma

Goal is to lower Intraocular Pressure (IOP) by increasing outflow or decreasing production of aqueous humor.

Class Drugs Mechanism
Prostaglandin Analogues Latanoprost, Bimatoprost, Travoprost Increase uveoscleral outflow. First line drugs (Once daily). ADR: Iris pigmentation, eyelash growth.
Beta Blockers Timolol, Betaxolol ($\beta_1$ selective) Decrease aqueous production.
Alpha-2 Agonists Brimonidine, Apraclonidine Decrease production + Increase uveoscleral outflow.
Carbonic Anhydrase Inhibitors Dorzolamide, Brinzolamide (Topical)
Acetazolamide (Systemic)		 Decrease aqueous production.
Miotics (Cholinomimetics) Pilocarpine Increase trabecular outflow (opens meshwork). Drug of choice for Acute Congestive Glaucoma.
Osmotic Agents Mannitol (IV), Glycerol (Oral) Rapidly lower IOP in acute attack by osmotic dehydration of vitreous.

Miscellaneous

  • Local Anaesthetics: Proparacaine, Tetracaine, Lignocaine. For tonometry, foreign body removal, surgery.
  • Diagnostic Dyes: Fluorescein (detects corneal abrasions/ulcers - stains green), Rose Bengal.
  • Artificial Tears: Methylcellulose, Polyvinyl alcohol. For dry eyes (Keratoconjunctivitis sicca).

Drug Induced Ocular Toxicity

  • Ethambutol: Optic neuritis, color blindness.
  • Chloroquine: Retinopathy (Bull's eye maculopathy).
  • Steroids: Cataract, Glaucoma.
  • Amiodarone: Corneal microdeposits.
  • Sildenafil: Blue vision (PDE-6 inhibition).
