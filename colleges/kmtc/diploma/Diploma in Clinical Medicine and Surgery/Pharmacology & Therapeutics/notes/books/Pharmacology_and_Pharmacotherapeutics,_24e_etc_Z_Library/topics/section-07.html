üíì Pharmacotherapy of Cardiac Arrhythmias

Cardiac arrhythmia is defined as disturbance of initiation or conduction of cardiac impulse. Anti-arrhythmic drugs are the drugs used to prevent or correct cardiac arrhythmias.

Electrophysiology of Cardiac Tissue

The cardiac cell is bounded by a lipoprotein membrane which has receptor channels crossing it. These are ion-selective membrane spanning proteins which permit passive movement of ions ($\mathrm{Na}^{+}, \mathrm{K}^{+}, \mathrm{Ca}^{++}$ and $\mathrm{Cl}^{-}$) down their electrochemical gradients during the open but not during the closed state of the channels.

Phases of Action Potential:

  • Phase 0 (Rapid Depolarisation): Due to fast influx of $\mathrm{Na}^{+}$ through fast sodium channels.
  • Phase 1 (Rapid Repolarisation): Transient, due to $\mathrm{K}^{+}$ outflow.
  • Phase 2 (Plateau): Balance between $\mathrm{Ca}^{++}$ influx (slow channels) and $\mathrm{K}^{+}$ efflux. Corresponds to ST segment.
  • Phase 3 (Rapid Repolarisation): Continued extrusion of $\mathrm{K}^{+}$. Corresponds to T wave.
  • Phase 4 (Resting): Maintenance of resting potential by $\mathrm{Na}^{+}-\mathrm{K}^{+}$ ATPase pump. In pacemaker cells (SA/AV node), there is spontaneous diastolic depolarisation (slope of phase 4).

Mechanisms of Arrhythmias

Disorders of Impulse Formation

Associated with spontaneous, irregular and rhythmic discharge from ectopic pacemaker activity (enhanced automaticity). Examples: Atrial tachycardia, atrial flutter, atrial fibrillation (AF).

Disorders of Impulse Conduction

Commonly referred to as re-entry disturbances. An impulse takes a circuitous route through depressed tissue and re-excites normal tissue. Examples: Ventricular tachycardia (VT), ventricular fibrillation (VF).

Classification of Antiarrhythmic Drugs (Vaughan Williams)

Class Mechanism Examples
Class I Fast Sodium Channel Blockers (Membrane Stabilisers)
IA Moderate Phase 0 depression, prolong repolarisation. Quinidine, Procainamide, Disopyramide
IB Weak Phase 0 depression, shorten repolarisation. Lignocaine, Phenytoin, Mexiletine

Specific Drugs

Quinidine (Class IA)

Actions: Depresses automaticity, excitability, and conduction velocity. Prolongs ERP (Direct action). Has vagolytic (antimuscarinic) action which may enhance AV conduction (paradoxical tachycardia).

ECG Changes: QT prolongation, QRS widening.

Adverse Reactions

  • Cinchonism: Tinnitus, vertigo, blurred vision.
  • Cardiac Toxicity: Torsade de pointes (polymorphic VT), hypotension, AV block.
  • Drug Interactions: Increases plasma digoxin levels.

Lignocaine (Lidocaine) (Class IB)

Actions: Selectively acts on diseased/ischemic myocardium. Shortens APD. No effect on AV conduction or hemodynamics. Given IV (high first pass metabolism).

Uses: Drug of choice for ventricular arrhythmias (post-MI, digitalis-induced).

Beta Blockers (Class II)

Propranolol, Metoprolol, Esmolol (Short acting, IV). Depress Phase 4 depolarisation. Useful in supraventricular tachyarrhythmias (SVT) associated with sympathetic overactivity.

Amiodarone (Class III)

Potent broad-spectrum antiarrhythmic. Has Class I, II, III and IV actions. Very long half-life (weeks).

Uses: Recurrent VT/VF, Atrial Fibrillation (maintenance of sinus rhythm).

Adverse Effects: Pulmonary fibrosis, Thyroid dysfunction (hypo/hyper), Corneal microdeposits, Photosensitivity, Hepatotoxicity.

Calcium Channel Blockers (Class IV)

Verapamil, Diltiazem. Act on slow response tissues (SA/AV nodes). Slow AV conduction.

Uses: PSVT (termination), Rate control in AF/Flutter.

Adenosine

Purine nucleoside. Activates $\mathrm{K}^{+}$ current (hyperpolarisation) in AV node. Ultra-short half-life (seconds). Drug of choice for terminating PSVT.

üíî Angina Pectoris, Acute MI and Peripheral Vascular Diseases

Ischemic Heart Disease (IHD) results from an imbalance between myocardial oxygen supply and demand. Angina Pectoris is the primary symptom.

Classification of Angina

  • Stable Angina: Pain on exertion, relieved by rest/nitrates. Fixed narrowing of coronaries.
  • Unstable Angina (UA): Pain at rest or worsening pattern. Pre-infarction state.
  • Variant (Prinzmetal) Angina: Pain at rest due to coronary vasospasm. ST elevation during attack.

Drug Classes Used in Angina

1. Organic Nitrates

Mechanism: Release Nitric Oxide (NO) $\rightarrow$ increases cGMP $\rightarrow$ vascular smooth muscle relaxation.

Actions:

  • Venodilatation (Preload reduction): Main effect. Reduces venous return and ventricular wall tension.
  • Arteriolar dilatation (Afterload reduction): Lesser extent.
  • Coronary dilatation: Dilates large epicardial vessels and collaterals.

Preparations:

  • Glyceryl Trinitrate (Nitroglycerine/NTG): Sublingual (0.5 mg) for acute attack. Action in 1-2 mins. Also transdermal/IV.
  • Isosorbide Dinitrate/Mononitrate: Longer acting oral forms for prophylaxis.

Adverse Effects: Headache (very common), flushing, hypotension, tachycardia. Tolerance develops on continuous use (needs nitrate-free interval of 8-10 hrs/day).

2. Beta-Adrenergic Blockers

Atenolol, Metoprolol. Reduce heart rate, contractility, and BP $\rightarrow$ Reduce Myocardial $O_2$ demand. Used for prophylaxis of stable angina. Contraindicated in Variant Angina (may worsen spasm).

3. Calcium Channel Blockers (CCB)

Block L-type $\mathrm{Ca}^{++}$ channels.

  • Verapamil/Diltiazem: Cardiac depressants (Rate/Contractility $\downarrow$). Vasodilators.
  • Dihydropyridines (Nifedipine, Amlodipine): Potent vasodilators (Afterload $\downarrow$, Coronary flow $\uparrow$). Nifedipine may cause reflex tachycardia.

Uses: Stable angina (especially if Beta blockers contraindicated), Variant angina (Drug of Choice).

4. Potassium Channel Activators

Nicorandil: Opens $K_{ATP}$ channels (vasodilation) + NO donor. Useful in refractory angina.

5. Others

Trimetazidine: Metabolic modulator (pFOX inhibitor). Improves myocardial energy utilisation.

Ranolazine: Inhibits late Sodium current. Prevents Calcium overload.

Ivabradine: Selectively inhibits $I_f$ current in SA node. Reduces heart rate without affecting contractility.

Management of Acute Myocardial Infarction (AMI)

Goals: Pain relief, restoration of flow (reperfusion), prevention of complications/death.

üöë

Immediate Treatment Protocol (MONA)

  • Morphine: IV for pain and anxiety. Also reduces preload.
  • Oxygen: To correct hypoxemia.
  • Nitroglycerine: Sublingual/IV to reduce preload and ischemic pain.
  • Aspirin: Chewed (160-325 mg) immediately. Antiplatelet effect.

Reperfusion Therapy:

  • Thrombolytics (Fibrinolytics): Streptokinase, Alteplase (tPA). Effective in STEMI if given early (within 12 hrs, best within 3 hrs). Risk of hemorrhage.
  • Primary PCI (Angioplasty): Preferred if available.

Adjunctive Therapy:

  • Beta-blockers: IV/Oral. Reduce infarct size, arrhythmias, and mortality.
  • ACE Inhibitors: Reduce ventricular remodeling and mortality. Started within 24 hrs.
  • Antiplatelets: Clopidogrel/Prasugrel added to Aspirin.
  • Anticoagulants: Heparin/LMWH.
  • Statins: High dose Atorvastatin.

Peripheral Vascular Diseases

Cilostazol: PDE3 inhibitor. Vasodilator + Antiplatelet. Used for intermittent claudication.

Pentoxifylline: Hemorrheologic agent (reduces blood viscosity).

ü©∫ Pharmacotherapy of Hypertension

Hypertension is defined as BP $> 140/90$ mmHg. It is a major risk factor for stroke, MI, heart failure, and renal failure.

Classification of Antihypertensive Drugs

1. Diuretics

Thiazides (Hydrochlorothiazide, Chlorthalidone): First line for many. Reduce plasma volume and PVR. Low dose (12.5-25 mg) preferred.

Loop Diuretics (Furosemide): Used in renal failure or resistant hypertension.

K+ Sparing (Spironolactone): Used as add-on.

2. RAAS Inhibitors

ACE Inhibitors (Captopril, Enalapril, Ramipril): Inhibit Angiotensin II formation and bradykinin breakdown. Drug of choice in Diabetes, Heart Failure, CKD. Side effects: Dry cough, Angioedema, Hyperkalemia.

ARBs (Losartan, Telmisartan): Block AT1 receptors. Similar benefits to ACEI but no cough.

Renin Inhibitors: Aliskiren.

3. Calcium Channel Blockers

Dihydropyridines (Amlodipine, Nifedipine): Vasodilators. Good for elderly and isolated systolic HTN. Side effect: Ankle edema.

Non-DHP (Verapamil, Diltiazem): Reduce heart rate and contractility.

4. Sympatholytics

Beta Blockers (Atenolol, Metoprolol): Reduce CO and renin release. Preferred in young patients and those with IHD.

Alpha Blockers (Prazosin): Vasodilators. Useful in BPH.

Central Sympatholytics (Clonidine, Methyldopa): Stimulate central $\alpha_2$ receptors. Methyldopa is safe in pregnancy.

Vasodilators

  • Hydralazine: Direct arteriolar vasodilator. Used in severe HTN/Pregnancy. Causes reflex tachycardia (give with beta blocker) and Lupus-like syndrome.
  • Minoxidil: Potent arteriolar vasodilator (K+ channel opener). Used in refractory HTN. Side effect: Hypertrichosis (hair growth).
  • Sodium Nitroprusside: Dilates both arterioles and veins. Releases NO. Used IV for Hypertensive Emergencies. Action instantaneous. Toxicity: Thiocyanate/Cyanide accumulation.

Management Guidelines

Step 1: Lifestyle modifications (Diet, Exercise, Salt restriction).

Step 2: Drug Monotherapy (A, C, D) - ACEI/ARB, CCB, or Diuretic. Beta blockers not first line for uncomplicated HTN.

Step 3: Combination therapy (e.g., ACEI + CCB, or ARB + Diuretic).

ü§∞

Hypertension in Pregnancy

Preferred Drugs: Methyldopa, Labetalol, Nifedipine, Hydralazine.
Contraindicated: ACE Inhibitors, ARBs, Diuretics (generally).

Pulmonary Hypertension

Treatment includes:

  • Endothelin Antagonists: Bosentan, Ambrisentan.
  • PDE-5 Inhibitors: Sildenafil, Tadalafil.
  • Prostacyclin Analogues: Epoprostenol, Iloprost.

üìâ Pharmacotherapy of Heart Failure

Heart failure (HF) is the inability of the heart to pump sufficient blood to meet metabolic needs. Compensatory mechanisms include: Sympathetic stimulation, RAAS activation (Fluid retention, vasoconstriction), and Ventricular remodeling (Hypertrophy/Dilatation).

Drugs for Heart Failure

1. Drugs Improving Survival (Neurohumoral Blockade)

  • ACE Inhibitors / ARBs: Cornerstone of therapy. Reverse remodeling, reduce preload/afterload.
  • Beta Blockers: (Metoprolol succinate, Carvedilol, Bisoprolol). Block sympathetic toxicity. Start low dose.
  • Aldosterone Antagonists: (Spironolactone, Eplerenone). Prevent fibrosis. Added in severe HF.

2. Drugs for Symptomatic Relief

  • Diuretics: (Furosemide). Relieve congestion/edema. Do not improve survival.
  • Digoxin: Positive inotrope (inhibits $\mathrm{Na}^{+}/\mathrm{K}^{+}$ ATPase). Increases contractility, slows heart rate (vagal).
‚ò¢Ô∏è

Digoxin Toxicity

Narrow therapeutic index. Enhanced by Hypokalemia.
Signs: Anorexia, Nausea, Vomiting, Visual disturbances (Yellow vision), Arrhythmias (Bigeminy, AV block, VT).
Treatment: Stop drug, correct K+, Atropine (for bradycardia), Lignocaine (for VT), Digibind (antibodies).

3. Acute Heart Failure (Acute LVF) Management

  • Diuretics: IV Furosemide (Lasix).
  • Vasodilators: Nitroglycerine IV or Nitroprusside.
  • Inotropes: Dobutamine, Dopamine, Milrinone (PDE3 inhibitor), Levosimendan (Calcium sensitiser).
  • Oxygen/Morphine.

‚ö° Pharmacotherapy of Shock

Shock is a state of inadequate tissue perfusion. Types: Hypovolemic, Cardiogenic, Septic (Distributive), Anaphylactic, Neurogenic.

General Management

  • Fluid Replacement: Crystalloids (Normal Saline, Ringer Lactate) or Colloids. Critical for hypovolemic and septic shock.
  • Oxygenation.
  • Acidosis Correction: Sodium bicarbonate (if severe).
