üß™ Sulfonamides and Quinolones

Sulfonamides

Mechanism: Competitively inhibit dihydropteroate synthase, preventing the conversion of PABA to dihydrofolic acid. Bacteriostatic.

Cotrimoxazole: Combination of Sulfamethoxazole + Trimethoprim (5:1 ratio). Sequential blockade of folate synthesis (Trimethoprim inhibits dihydrofolate reductase). Bactericidal.

Fluoroquinolones

Mechanism: Inhibit DNA Gyrase (Topoisomerase II) and Topoisomerase IV, preventing DNA supercoiling. Bactericidal.

Classification of Fluoroquinolones
Generation Drugs Activity
First Nalidixic Acid Urinary antiseptics only (Gram negative).
Second Ciprofloxacin, Ofloxacin, Norfloxacin Systemic Gram negative, some Gram positive. Urinary/GI infections.
Third Levofloxacin, Moxifloxacin "Respiratory Fluoroquinolones". Enhanced Gram positive (Strep. pneumoniae) and atypical activity.

Adverse Effects: Tendonitis/Tendon rupture (Achilles), QT prolongation (Moxifloxacin), Phototoxicity, CNS effects (dizziness, seizures). Contraindicated in pregnancy and children (cartilage damage).

üçÑ Penicillins and Other Gram-Positive Active Antibiotics

Mechanism: Beta-lactam antibiotics inhibit transpeptidase, preventing cross-linking of peptidoglycan in the bacterial cell wall. Bactericidal.

Classification of Penicillins

Natural Penicillins

Benzylpenicillin (Penicillin G): Acid labile (IV/IM). Short duration. Effective against Strep, Pneumococci, Meningococci, Treponema, Clostridia.
Repository Forms: Procaine Penicillin (12-24 hrs), Benzathine Penicillin (3-4 weeks, for Rheumatic fever/Syphilis prophylaxis).

Acid Resistant

Penicillin V: Can be given orally. Less potent.

Penicillinase Resistant

Cloxacillin, Dicloxacillin, Nafcillin: Resistant to staphylococcal beta-lactamase. Drug of choice for MSSA.

Extended Spectrum

Aminopenicillins: Ampicillin, Amoxicillin (Better absorption). Active against some Gram -ve (E. coli, H. flu).
Antipseudomonal: Carbenicillin, Ticarcillin, Piperacillin. (Used with aminoglycosides).

Beta-lactamase Inhibitors: Clavulanic acid, Sulbactam, Tazobactam. Combined with penicillins (e.g., Amoxicillin + Clavulanate) to protect the ring from destruction by bacterial enzymes.

Macrolides

Erythromycin, Clarithromycin, Azithromycin, Roxithromycin.
Mechanism: Bind to 50S ribosome, inhibiting protein synthesis. Bacteriostatic.
Uses: Atypical pneumonia (Mycoplasma, Chlamydia, Legionella), Pertussis, Diphtheria, H. pylori (Clarithromycin), Alt. for Penicillin allergy.
Adverse Effects: GI upset (motilin receptor stimulation), Cholestatic jaundice (Erythromycin estolate), QT prolongation, CYP450 inhibition (drug interactions).

Other Gram-Positive Active Drugs

  • Clindamycin (Lincosamide): Similar mechanism to macrolides. Excellent for anaerobes (B. fragilis) and Osteomyelitis (bone penetration). Risk: Pseudomembranous colitis (C. difficile).
  • Vancomycin (Glycopeptide): Inhibits cell wall synthesis. Drug of choice for MRSA and C. difficile (oral). Toxicity: "Red Man Syndrome" (histamine release), Nephrotoxicity, Ototoxicity.
  • Linezolid (Oxazolidinone): Inhibits protein synthesis (initiation). For VRSA, VRE. Risk: Thrombocytopenia.
  • Daptomycin: Lipopeptide. Depolarizes membrane. For MRSA skin infections. Inactivated by lung surfactant (not for pneumonia).

ü¶† Aminoglycosides

Streptomycin, Gentamicin, Tobramycin, Amikacin, Netilmicin, Neomycin.

General Properties

  • Mechanism: Bind to 30S ribosome, causing misreading of mRNA. Bactericidal.
  • Spectrum: Aerobic Gram-negative bacilli (Pseudomonas, E. coli, Klebsiella). Ineffective against anaerobes.
  • Pharmacokinetics: Polar cations. Not absorbed orally (given IM/IV). Excreted unchanged by kidney.
  • Toxicity (The "O-N-N" triad):
    • Ototoxicity: Vestibular (Streptomycin, Gentamicin) or Cochlear/Auditory (Amikacin, Neomycin). Irreversible.
    • Nephrotoxicity: Acute tubular necrosis (Reversible).
    • Neuromuscular Blockade: Curare-like effect.

Specific Agents

Streptomycin: First line for TB, Plague, Tularemia, Brucellosis.

Gentamicin: Workhorse for serious Gram -ve infections.

Amikacin: Resistant to modifying enzymes. Used for MDR-TB and gentamicin-resistant organisms.

Neomycin: Too toxic for systemic use. Used topically (skin/eye) or orally for gut sterilization (hepatic coma).

üíä Cephalosporins and Other Beta-Lactams

Cephalosporins

Classified by generations. As generation increases, Gram-negative activity increases, Gram-positive activity decreases (generally), and CNS penetration improves.

Generation Parenteral Oral Key Uses
First Cefazolin Cephalexin Surgical prophylaxis, skin/soft tissue (Staph/Strep).
Second Cefuroxime Cefaclor Respiratory infections, mixed infections.
Third Ceftriaxone, Cefotaxime, Ceftazidime Cefixime, Cefpodoxime Meningitis (Ceftriaxone), Pseudomonas (Ceftazidime), Typhoid, Gonorrhoea.
Fourth Cefepime - Resistant Gram -ve, Pseudomonas, Neutropenic fever.
Fifth Ceftaroline - MRSA activity.

Adverse Effects: Hypersensitivity (cross-reactivity with penicillin ~10%), Nephrotoxicity (synergy with aminoglycosides), Bleeding/Disulfiram-like reaction (Cefoperazone, Cefamandole - methylthiotetrazole group).

Carbapenems

Imipenem, Meropenem.
Broadest spectrum beta-lactams. Resistant to beta-lactamases.
Imipenem: Metabolised by renal dehydropeptidase I (DHP-I). Always combined with Cilastatin (DHP-I inhibitor) to prevent nephrotoxicity. Risk of seizures.
Meropenem: Stable to DHP-I. Less seizure risk.

Monobactams

Aztreonam. Active only against aerobic Gram-negative bacteria (like aminoglycosides). Safe in penicillin-allergic patients (no cross-reactivity).

üß¨ Tetracyclines and Chloramphenicol

Tetracyclines

Tetracycline, Doxycycline, Minocycline, Tigecycline.
Mechanism: Bind to 30S ribosome. Bacteriostatic. Broad spectrum (Rickettsia, Chlamydia, Mycoplasma, Spirochetes).
Pharmacokinetics: Chelated by Ca, Mg, Fe (avoid milk/antacids). Doxycycline is safe in renal failure (fecal excretion).
Adverse Effects: GI upset, Phototoxicity, Teeth discoloration/Bone deformity (Contraindicated in pregnancy and children <8 yrs), Hepatotoxicity (in pregnancy).

Chloramphenicol

Mechanism: Binds to 50S ribosome (inhibits peptidyl transferase). Bacteriostatic.
Adverse Effects:
1. Bone Marrow Depression: Dose-related (reversible) and Idiosyncratic Aplastic Anemia (irreversible, fatal).
2. Gray Baby Syndrome: In neonates (lack of glucuronyl transferase). Vomiting, flaccidity, cyanosis, shock.
Uses: Meningitis (excellent CNS penetration), Typhoid (historical), Anaerobes. Used as reserve drug due to toxicity.
