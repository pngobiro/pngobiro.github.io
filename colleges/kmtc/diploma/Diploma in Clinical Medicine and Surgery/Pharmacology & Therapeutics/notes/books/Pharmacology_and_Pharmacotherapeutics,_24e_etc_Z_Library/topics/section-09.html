‚öñÔ∏è Water, Sodium, Potassium and Hydrion Metabolism

Correction of body water and electrolytes forms an important aspect of therapy in clinical practice. The most important determinants of the amount of body water (% of body weight) are age and gender. The water content of the newborn, the middle aged adult and the elderly are 75%, 60% and 50% respectively.

Electrolyte Composition

Plasma (ECF): High in Sodium ($142 \text{ mMol/L}$), Chloride ($108 \text{ mMol/L}$), Bicarbonate ($24 \text{ mMol/L}$).
ICF: High in Potassium ($140 \text{ mMol/L}$), Magnesium ($20 \text{ mMol/L}$), Phosphate, Sulfate.

Water Metabolism

Water balance is maintained by intake (regulated by thirst) and output (regulated mainly by Antidiuretic Hormone, ADH). Thirst is induced by increased ECF osmolality (cellular dehydration) or decreased ECF volume (hypovolemia).

Disorders of Water Balance

  • Water Depletion: Causes hypernatremia and cellular dehydration. Treatment involves giving enough water (or 5% dextrose IV).
  • Water Excess (Intoxication): Causes hyponatremia and cerebral edema (convulsions, coma). Treatment involves restriction of fluids and hypertonic saline in severe cases.

Sodium Metabolism

Sodium is the principal cation of the ECF. Total body exchangeable sodium is about 45 mMol/kg. Serum sodium is maintained at $140.9 \pm 4.8 \text{ mMol/L}$.

Hormonal Control

  • Aldosterone: Promotes Na reabsorption in distal tubules.
  • Natriuretic Peptides (ANP, BNP): Released from myocardium in response to stretch. Increase GFR, decrease Na reabsorption (Natriuresis), and inhibit RAAS.
  • Prostaglandins ($PGE_2$): Natriuretic and vasodilator.

Sodium Depletion

Caused by GI loss (diarrhoea), renal loss (diuretics, salt-losing nephritis), or cutaneous loss.
Symptoms: Apathy, cramps, tachycardia, hypotension, loss of skin turgor, "sunken" eyeballs, extrarenal uremia.

Table 37.4: Treatment of Sodium Depletion
Fluid of Choice 0.9% (Normal) Saline.
Severe Cases Rapid infusion (2-3 Litres/2-3 hours). Monitor CVP.
Additives Potassium and Bicarbonate depending on type of loss (e.g., in diarrhoea).
Hyponatremia Severe ($<110 \text{ mMol/L}$): Hypertonic (3-5%) saline slow IV to relieve cerebral edema.

Potassium Metabolism

Potassium is the major intracellular cation ($140 \text{ mMol/L}$ vs $4.5 \text{ mMol/L}$ in plasma). Daily requirement is 50-150 mMol.

Hypokalemia ($K^+ < 3.5 \text{ mMol/L}$)

Causes: Diuretics (commonest), vomiting, diarrhoea, hyperaldosteronism, diabetic ketoacidosis treatment.
Effects: Muscle weakness, paralytic ileus, cardiac arrhythmias, digitalis toxicity.
Treatment: Oral KCl (40-100 mMol/day). In emergency (arrhythmias), IV KCl (max rate 20 mMol/hour, monitor ECG).

Hyperkalemia ($K^+ > 5.5 \text{ mMol/L}$)

Causes: Renal failure, K+ sparing diuretics, ACE inhibitors, acidosis, cell lysis.
Effects: Muscle weakness, arrhythmias, cardiac arrest (diastolic). ECG shows peaked T waves.

Treatment of Hyperkalemia

  1. Calcium Gluconate IV: Antagonizes membrane effects (protects heart).
  2. Insulin + Glucose IV: Shifts K+ into cells.
  3. Salbutamol (Nebulised/IV): Shifts K+ into cells.
  4. Potassium binders: Sodium polystyrene sulfonate (oral/rectal).
  5. Dialysis: For renal failure.

Acid-Base Balance

Regulated by buffers, lungs ($CO_2$), and kidneys ($HCO_3^-$).
Metabolic Acidosis: Low $HCO_3^-$, Low pH. Treated with IV Sodium Bicarbonate.
Metabolic Alkalosis: High $HCO_3^-$, High pH. Treated with saline infusion (chloride responsive) or K+ replacement.

ü•ó Nutritional Supplementation Therapy

Nutritional Requirements

Energy: Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR) + Activity. In illness/trauma/sepsis, requirements increase (110-200% of BMR).

Wilmore's Formula for BMR:

$$ \text{BMR (KCal/M}^2\text{/day)} = 24 \times \left(37 - \frac{\text{Age} - 20}{10}\right) $$

Rough estimate: $22 \text{ KCal/kg/day}$.

Protein: 0.6-1.5 g/kg/day depending on severity of illness (catabolic state).

Enteral Nutrition

Preferred whenever GI tract is functional. Preserves gut integrity and immunity. Can be oral or via tube (nasogastric/nasoduodenal).

Total Parenteral Nutrition (TPN)

Indicated when GI tract cannot be used (short bowel syndrome, prolonged ileus, severe pancreatitis, fistulae).

Composition: Dextrose (20-25%), Amino acids, Lipid emulsions (10-20%), Electrolytes, Vitamins, Trace elements.
Administration: Through central vein (due to hypertonicity).
Complications: Hyperglycemia, Sepsis (catheter related), Electrolyte imbalance, Liver dysfunction.

üíß Diuretic and Anti-Diuretic Drugs

Diuretics increase the rate of urine formation and natriuresis. They act primarily by inhibiting tubular reabsorption of sodium at different sites in the nephron.

Classification and Mechanisms

Class Site of Action Mechanism Examples
Carbonic Anhydrase Inhibitors Proximal Tubule Inhibit $H^+$ generation, preventing $Na^+-H^+$ exchange and $NaHCO_3$ reabsorption. Acetazolamide
Loop Diuretics (High Ceiling) Thick Ascending Limb of Loop of Henle Inhibit $Na^+-K^+-2Cl^-$ symport. Furosemide, Torsemide, Bumetanide
Thiazides Distal Convoluted Tubule (Early) Inhibit $Na^+-Cl^-$ symport. Hydrochlorothiazide, Chlorthalidone, Metolazone
Potassium Sparing Late Distal Tubule & Collecting Duct (a) Aldosterone antagonism
(b) Na+ channel blockade		 (a) Spironolactone, Eplerenone
(b) Amiloride, Triamterene
Osmotic Diuretics Proximal Tubule & Loop of Henle Osmotic retention of water. Mannitol, Glycerol

Specific Drugs

1. Acetazolamide

Weak diuretic. Causes alkaline diuresis and metabolic acidosis.
Uses: Glaucoma (decreases aqueous humor), Mountain sickness (prophylaxis), Alkalinization of urine.

2. Thiazides (Hydrochlorothiazide)

Moderately potent. Effective orally. Long duration.
Uses: Hypertension (first line), Mild Edema, Idiopathic Hypercalciuria (calcium retention), Nephrogenic Diabetes Insipidus (paradoxical effect).
Adverse Effects: Hypokalemia, Hyperuricemia (Gout), Hyperglycemia, Hyperlipidemia.

3. Loop Diuretics (Furosemide)

Most potent. Quick onset. Effective even in renal failure.
Uses: Acute Pulmonary Edema (IV), Severe Edema (CHF, Cirrhosis, Renal), Hypertension (with renal failure), Hypercalcemia (increases Ca excretion).
Adverse Effects: Hypokalemia, Hypovolemia, Ototoxicity, Hyperuricemia.

4. Potassium Sparing Diuretics

Spironolactone: Aldosterone antagonist. Used in Cirrhosis with ascites, CHF (improves survival), and to prevent hypokalemia. ADR: Gynecomastia, Hyperkalemia.
Amiloride: Blocks ENaC channels. Used in combination with thiazides.

5. Osmotic Diuretics (Mannitol)

Given IV. Not metabolized.
Uses: Cerebral Edema (reduces ICP), Acute Glaucoma, Prevention of acute renal failure.

Anti-Diuretic Agents

Vasopressin (ADH): Acts on $V_1$ (Vascular) and $V_2$ (Renal - water reabsorption) receptors.
Desmopressin (DDAVP): Selective $V_2$ agonist. Nasal spray/Oral/Injection.
Uses: Central Diabetes Insipidus, Nocturnal Enuresis, Hemophilia A/von Willebrand disease (releases Factor VIII/vWF).
Vasopressin Antagonists (Vaptans): Tolvaptan, Conivaptan. Used for hyponatremia (SIADH, CHF).
