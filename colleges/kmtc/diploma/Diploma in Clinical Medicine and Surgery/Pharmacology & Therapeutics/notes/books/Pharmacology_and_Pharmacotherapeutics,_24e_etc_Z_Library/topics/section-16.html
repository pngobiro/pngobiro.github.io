ü¶∂ Gout and Rheumatoid Arthritis

Pharmacotherapy of Gout

Gout is a metabolic disorder resulting from increased body pool of urate, characterized by arthritis (deposition of monosodium urate crystals) and tophi.

1. Acute Gouty Arthritis

  • NSAIDs: Indomethacin, Naproxen, Etoricoxib. First line for pain/inflammation. Aspirin is contraindicated (inhibits urate excretion).
  • Colchicine: Alkaloid. Inhibits microtubule assembly and neutrophil migration. Specific for gout. ADR: Diarrhoea (dose-limiting).
  • Glucocorticoids: Intra-articular or systemic (if NSAIDs/Colchicine contraindicated).

2. Chronic Gout (Hyperuricemia)

Indicated if tophi present, frequent attacks (>2/year), or urate stones.

Synthesis Inhibitors

Allopurinol: Inhibits Xanthine Oxidase. Reduces uric acid formation. Drug of choice for chronic gout and chemotherapy-induced hyperuricemia.
Febuxostat: Non-purine xanthine oxidase inhibitor. For patients intolerant to allopurinol.

Uricosurics

Probenecid: Inhibits tubular reabsorption of urate. Ineffective in renal failure. Drink plenty of water to prevent stones.

Rasburicase: Recombinant urate oxidase. Degrades uric acid to allantoin. Used in Tumor Lysis Syndrome.

Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA)

Autoimmune inflammatory disease. Goals: Relieve pain, preserve function, arrest tissue destruction.

1. Symptomatic Relief (Bridge Therapy)

  • NSAIDs: Reduce pain and swelling but do not alter disease course.
  • Glucocorticoids: Low dose prednisone. Rapid relief. Bridge to DMARDs.

2. Disease Modifying Anti-Rheumatic Drugs (DMARDs)

Slow acting. Arrest bone destruction.
Methotrexate: First line DMARD. Folate antagonist. Hepatotoxic/Teratogenic.
Sulfasalazine: Anti-inflammatory.
Hydroxychloroquine: Antimalarial. Mild RA. Risk of retinopathy.
Leflunomide: Pyrimidine synthesis inhibitor.

3. Biological Response Modifiers

Target Drugs
TNF-$\alpha$ Inhibitors Infliximab, Etanercept, Adalimumab
IL-6 Receptor Antagonist Tocilizumab
B-Cell Depletion (CD20) Rituximab
T-Cell Costimulation Blocker Abatacept

Risk: Reactivation of Tuberculosis and other infections.

Heavy Metal Poisoning

1. Arsenic

Acute: Severe GI pain, vomiting, diarrhea ("Cholera-like"), shock.
Chronic: Hyperpigmentation (Rain drop), Hyperkeratosis (palms/soles), Peripheral neuropathy, "Black foot" disease.
Antidote: Dimercaprol (BAL).

2. Lead (Plumbism)

Sources: Batteries, paint, petrol (historical).
Clinical Features: Abdominal colic ("Lead colic"), Constipation, Anemia (Basophilic stippling), Wrist drop (Peripheral neuropathy), Encephalopathy (in children), Burton's line (gums).
Treatment:
1. Calcium Disodium Edetate (Ca-Na$_2$ EDTA): IV/IM.
2. Dimercaprol (BAL): For encephalopathy.
3. Succimer (DMSA): Oral chelator.

3. Mercury

Acute: Corrosive GI damage, renal failure.
Chronic: Stomatitis, Tremors, Eretarism (psychic disturbance), Minamata disease.
Antidote: Dimercaprol (Acute), Penicillamine/Succimer (Chronic).

4. Copper (Wilson's Disease)

Hepatolenticular degeneration. Kayser-Fleischer rings.
Treatment: D-Penicillamine (Copper chelator). Trientine (alternative).

Chelating Agents

Agent Used For Route
Dimercaprol (BAL) Arsenic, Mercury, Gold, Lead (with EDTA) IM (Painful)
EDTA (Ca-Na$_2$) Lead IV
Penicillamine Copper, Mercury, Lead Oral
Desferrioxamine Iron IM/IV
Succimer Lead, Arsenic, Mercury Oral

üí® Gases: Therapeutic and Toxic

Therapeutic Gases

  • Oxygen: Indicated in hypoxia (COPD, Asthma, Pneumonia, Shock).
    Hyperbaric Oxygen: Oxygen at >1 atm pressure. Uses: CO poisoning, Gas gangrene, Decompression sickness.
  • Carbon Dioxide: Stimulates respiratory center. 5-7% in O2 (Carbogen) used for hiccups (rarely), CO poisoning (to stimulate ventilation - controversial).
  • Nitric Oxide (NO): Selective pulmonary vasodilator. Inhaled for persistent pulmonary hypertension of newborn.

Toxic Gases

‚ò†Ô∏è

Carbon Monoxide (CO) Poisoning

Odorless gas. Affinity for Hb is 200x that of Oxygen. Forms Carboxyhemoglobin.
Symptoms: Headache, cherry-red skin (rare), confusion, coma.
Treatment: 100% Oxygen (Hyperbaric O2 preferred if available) to displace CO.

Cyanide: Inhibits Cytochrome C Oxidase (cellular hypoxia).
Treatment:
1. Amyl Nitrite (inhalation) + Sodium Nitrite (IV) $\rightarrow$ Forms Methemoglobin (binds CN).
2. Sodium Thiosulfate (IV) $\rightarrow$ Converts CN to Thiocyanate (excreted).
3. Hydroxycobalamin (Vitamin B12a) $\rightarrow$ Forms Cyanocobalamin.

üçä Vitamins and Antioxidants

Fat Soluble Vitamins

Vitamin A (Retinol)

Functions: Vision (Rhodopsin), Epithelial tissue integrity.
Deficiency: Night blindness (Nyctalopia), Xerophthalmia, Bitot's spots, Keratomalacia.
Toxicity: Hypervitaminosis A (Dry skin, hepatomegaly, raised intracranial pressure). Teratogenic.
Analogs (Retinoids): Tretinoin (Acne), Isotretinoin (Severe Acne - highly teratogenic), Etretinate (Psoriasis).

Vitamin D

(See Chapter 70). Rickets (children), Osteomalacia (adults).

Vitamin E (Tocopherol)

Function: Antioxidant.
Deficiency: Rare. Hemolytic anemia in premature infants, neurological defects.

Vitamin K

Function: Gamma-carboxylation of Clotting Factors II, VII, IX, X.
Use: Warfarin overdose, Hemorrhagic disease of newborn.

Water Soluble Vitamins

Vitamin B Complex

  • B1 (Thiamine): Deficiency = Beriberi (Wet: Heart failure / Dry: Neuropathy) and Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome (Alcoholics).
  • B2 (Riboflavin): Deficiency = Glossitis, Cheilosis, Angular stomatitis.
  • B3 (Niacin): Deficiency = Pellagra (3 Ds: Dermatitis, Diarrhoea, Dementia).
  • B6 (Pyridoxine): Coenzyme for transaminases. Used in INH toxicity, morning sickness. Deficiency = Neuropathy, convulsions.
  • B12 / Folic Acid: (See Chapter 35). Megaloblastic anemia.

Vitamin C (Ascorbic Acid)

Functions: Collagen synthesis, Antioxidant, Iron absorption.
Deficiency: Scurvy (Bleeding gums, petechiae, poor wound healing).
Uses: Scurvy, Acidification of urine.

ü§∞ Drugs, Pregnancy and the Infant

Pharmacokinetics in Pregnancy

  • Reduced gastric emptying (Delayed absorption).
  • Increased Plasma Volume (Lower concentration of drugs).
  • Increased GFR (Faster excretion of renal drugs e.g., Ampicillin).
  • Altered protein binding (Low albumin).

Teratogenicity

Drug induced fetal abnormalities. Critical period: Organogenesis (Week 3-8).

Known Teratogens
Drug Effect
Thalidomide Phocomelia (Seal limbs).
Warfarin Fetal Warfarin Syndrome (Nasal hypoplasia, skeletal defects).
ACE Inhibitors/ARBs Renal failure, oligohydramnios, skull hypoplasia.
Retinoids (Isotretinoin) Craniofacial, cardiac, CNS defects. (Absolute Contraindication).
Tetracyclines Teeth discoloration, bone growth retardation.
Valproate/Carbamazepine Neural tube defects (Spina bifida).
Lithium Ebstein's anomaly (Cardiac).
Diethylstilbestrol (DES) Vaginal adenocarcinoma in female offspring.

US FDA Categories

  • A: Safe (Folic acid).
  • B: Animal studies safe, no human data OR Animal risk, human safe (Penicillins, Paracetamol).
  • C: Animal risk, no human data (Beta blockers). Use if benefit > risk.
  • D: Proven human risk (Phenytoin, Carbamazepine). Use in life-threatening situations only.
  • X: Contraindicated. Risk outweighs any benefit (Thalidomide, Isotretinoin, Statins).

Breast Feeding

Most drugs are excreted in milk but in low concentrations.
Avoid: Anticancer drugs, Lithium, Amiodarone, Radioactive iodine, Tetracyclines (long term).
