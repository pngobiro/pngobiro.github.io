üçî Appetite, Obesity and Hypolipidemic Agents

Appetite Stimulants

Used for anorexia in debilitating illnesses. Alcohol (small quantities) stimulates gastric secretion but chronic use causes gastritis. Cyproheptadine (5-HT antagonist) has appetite stimulating properties but causes drowsiness. Megestrol acetate is used in cancer cachexia.

Treatment of Obesity (Anorexiants)

Obesity is a disorder of energy balance. Drug therapy is adjunctive to diet and exercise.

1. Centrally Acting Anorexiants

  • Adrenergic agents: Amphetamine (high abuse potential), Phentermine, Diethylpropion.
  • 5-HT Agonists: Fenfluramine (Withdrawn due to valvular heart disease), Lorcaserin (selective $5-HT_{2C}$ agonist).
  • Sibutramine: Withdrawn due to cardiovascular toxicity.

2. Peripherally Acting Agents

Orlistat: Inhibits gastric and pancreatic lipases. Prevents absorption of about 30% of dietary fat.
Adverse Effects: Oily spotting, flatus with discharge, fecal urgency, steatorrhoea. May reduce absorption of fat-soluble vitamins.

Antihyperlipidemic Drugs

Used to treat elevated cholesterol (risk factor for atherosclerosis/IHD) and triglycerides (risk of pancreatitis).

1. Statins (HMG-CoA Reductase Inhibitors)

Mechanism: Inhibit the rate-limiting enzyme in cholesterol synthesis. Leads to upregulation of LDL receptors on hepatocytes and increased clearance of LDL from blood.

Drug Intensity Key Features
Atorvastatin High Long $t^{1/2}$. Taken any time of day. Potent.
Rosuvastatin High Potent. Long $t^{1/2}$.
Simvastatin Moderate Prodrug. Take at night. CYP3A4 interactions.
Pravastatin Moderate Hydrophilic. Less drug interactions.

Adverse Effects: Myalgia, Myopathy, Rhabdomyolysis (rare but serious, risk increased with fibrates/CYP inhibitors), hepatotoxicity.

2. Fibrates (Fibric Acid Derivatives)

Gemfibrozil, Fenofibrate. Activators of PPAR-$\alpha$. markedly reduce Triglycerides (by 50%) and increase HDL. Used in hypertriglyceridemia.

3. Cholesterol Absorption Inhibitors

Ezetimibe: Inhibits intestinal absorption of cholesterol. Synergistic with statins.

4. Bile Acid Sequestrants (Resins)

Cholestyramine: Binds bile acids in gut, preventing enterohepatic circulation. Unpleasant taste, GI bloating.

5. Nicotinic Acid (Niacin)

Reduces VLDL and LDL, increases HDL significantly.
Side Effects: Intense cutaneous flushing (mediated by PGs, reduced by aspirin), pruritus, hepatotoxicity.

ü§Æ Antiemetics and Drug Therapy of Diarrhoea

Pharmacotherapy of Vomiting

Vomiting centre is stimulated by CTZ (Chemoreceptor Trigger Zone), vestibular apparatus, and peripheral afferents.

Classification of Antiemetics

1. 5-HT3 Antagonists

Ondansetron, Granisetron. Potent. Block receptors in CTZ and GI tract. Drug of choice for chemotherapy/radiation induced vomiting and post-op vomiting.

2. D2 Blockers (Prokinetics)

Metoclopramide: Central antiemetic + Prokinetic (increases gastric emptying). Side effects: Extrapyramidal reactions (dystonia).
Domperidone: Does not cross BBB. Less side effects.

3. Antihistaminics/Anticholinergics

Promethazine, Dimenhydrinate, Scopolamine (Hyoscine). Useful for Motion Sickness and vestibular disorders.

4. NK1 Antagonists

Aprepitant. Blocks Substance P actions. Used for delayed chemotherapy induced vomiting.

Pharmacotherapy of Diarrhoea

Priorities: 1. Rehydration (ORT/IV), 2. Maintenance of Nutrition, 3. Specific Antimicrobials (if infective), 4. Symptomatic relief.

1. Oral Rehydration Therapy (ORT)

Based on coupled transport of Sodium and Glucose in the intestine.

2. Antimotility Drugs

Loperamide: Opioid agonist. Decreases motility and secretions. Contraindicated in acute infective diarrhoea (dysentery) and in children < 2 years (risk of ileus/respiratory depression).

Racecadotril: Enkephalinase inhibitor. Antisecretory. Safe in children.

3. Antimicrobials

Indicated for: Shigellosis, Cholera, Giardiasis, Amoebic dysentery. (See Section XII).

üöΩ Pharmacotherapy of Constipation

Laxatives (mild) or Cathartics (strong) promote defecation. Non-drug measures (fibre, water, exercise) are first line.

Classification

  • Bulk Laxatives: Ispaghula (Psyllium), Methylcellulose, Bran. Absorb water, swell, stretch bowel to stimulate peristalsis. Safe.
  • Osmotic Laxatives:
    • Lactulose: Non-absorbable disaccharide. Metabolised by colon bacteria to acids $\to$ osmotic retention of water. Also traps $NH_3$ (used in Hepatic Encephalopathy).
    • Magnesium Salts (Milk of Magnesia): Saline purgatives. Rapid action.
    • PEG (Polyethylene Glycol): For bowel preparation.
  • Stimulant (Irritant) Laxatives:
    • Bisacodyl, Sodium Picosulfate: Act on colon.
    • Senna: Anthraquinone derivative. May cause melanosis coli.
    • Castor Oil: Small intestine irritant. Drastic purgative. Avoid in pregnancy.
  • Stool Softeners (Emollients): Liquid Paraffin (interferes with vitamin absorption), Docusate Sodium.
  • Chloride Channel Activators: Lubiprostone (for IBS-constipation).

üî• Pharmacotherapy of Peptic Ulcer Disease

Peptic ulcer (Duodenal/Gastric) results from imbalance between aggressive factors (Acid, Pepsin, H. pylori, NSAIDs) and defensive factors (Mucus, Bicarbonate, Prostaglandins).

I. Drugs Inhibiting Acid Secretion

1. Proton Pump Inhibitors (PPIs)

Omeprazole, Pantoprazole, Lansoprazole, Rabeprazole, Esomeprazole.
Mechanism: Irreversibly inhibit $H^+-K^+$ ATPase (Proton Pump) in parietal cells. Most potent acid suppressors.
Administration: Prodrugs. Activated in acidic canaliculi. Best taken empty stomach (30 min before meal).
Uses: Peptic Ulcer, GERD (Drug of choice), Zollinger-Ellison Syndrome, H. pylori eradication, NSAID ulcer prevention.
Adverse Effects: GI upset, headache. Long term: B12 deficiency, osteoporosis risk.

2. H2 Receptor Antagonists

Ranitidine, Famotidine, Cimetidine.
Mechanism: Competitively block $H_2$ receptors on parietal cells. Suppress basal and nocturnal acid secretion.
Cimetidine: Inhibits CYP450 (drug interactions), Anti-androgenic (gynecomastia). Ranitidine/Famotidine are preferred (fewer side effects).

II. Mucosal Protective Agents

  • Sucralfate: Polymerises in acid pH to form a sticky gel that adheres to ulcer base. Physical barrier. Causes constipation.
  • Misoprostol: $PGE_1$ analogue. Cytoprotective and inhibits acid. Specific for NSAID-induced ulcers. Side effects: Diarrhoea, uterine cramps (abortifacient - avoid in pregnancy).
  • Bismuth compounds: Coat ulcer, anti-H. pylori action.

III. Antacids

Neutralize secreted acid. For symptomatic relief.
Systemic: Sodium bicarbonate (can cause alkalosis).
Non-systemic: Magnesium hydroxide (Laxative) + Aluminium hydroxide (Constipating). Often combined to balance bowel effect.

IV. Anti-H. pylori Therapy

Eradication prevents recurrence. Triple therapy is standard.
