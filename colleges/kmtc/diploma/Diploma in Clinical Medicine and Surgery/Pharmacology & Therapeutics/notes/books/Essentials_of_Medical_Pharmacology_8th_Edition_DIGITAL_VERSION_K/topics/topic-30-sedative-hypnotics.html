01

üìñ Definitions & Overview

Sedative-hypnotics are CNS depressants. The distinction between a sedative and a hypnotic is largely dose-dependent.

Sedative

A drug that reduces excitement, calms the patient (without inducing sleep), and decreases motor activity and mental acuity. Key feature: Anxiolysis.

Hypnotic

A drug that produces sleep resembling natural sleep. Higher doses of sedatives act as hypnotics.

üí°

Dose-Response Relationship

Barbiturates (Steep Curve): Linear progression from Sedation $\rightarrow$ Sleep $\rightarrow$ Anesthesia $\rightarrow$ Coma $\rightarrow$ Death. Low safety margin.

Benzodiazepines (Flat Curve): Ceiling effect. Sedation $\rightarrow$ Sleep $\rightarrow$ Hypnosis. Difficult to achieve anesthesia or fatal coma with oral doses alone.

üìä Classification

1. Benzodiazepines (BZDs)

  • Short acting: Triazolam, Midazolam (t¬Ω 2-3 hrs).
  • Intermediate acting: Alprazolam, Temazepam, Lorazepam, Oxazepam.
  • Long acting: Diazepam, Flurazepam, Nitrazepam.

2. Barbiturates

  • Long acting: Phenobarbitone.
  • Short acting: Pentobarbitone, Secobarbitone.
  • Ultra-short acting: Thiopentone (IV anaesthetic).

3. Newer Nonbenzodiazepines (Z-drugs)

Zolpidem, Zopiclone, Zaleplon, Eszopiclone.

4. Melatonin Receptor Agonists

Ramelteon.

03

‚ò†Ô∏è Barbiturates

General depressants of all excitable cells. Popularity has declined due to low therapeutic index, high abuse potential, and interactions.

Mechanism

Bind to specific site on GABAA receptor. Potentiate GABA action by increasing the Duration of Cl- channel opening ("Barbiturate Duration"). At high concentrations, they can directly open the channel (GABA-mimetic).

Adverse Effects

  • Hangover: Residual depression, drowsiness next morning.
  • Paradoxical excitement: In children and elderly.
  • Hyperalgesia: Increased sensitivity to pain in subanesthetic doses.
  • Drug Interactions: Potent Microsomal Enzyme Inducers (CYP450). Reduces efficacy of Warfarin, Steroids, OCPs.
  • Precipitation of Porphyria: Induce ALA synthase.
‚ö†Ô∏è

Acute Barbiturate Poisoning

Signs: Coma, respiratory depression, hypotension, cutaneous blisters (Barbiturate burns), renal failure.

Treatment: No specific antidote. Supportive care (Airway, Breathing, Circulation). Alkaline Diuresis (Sodium bicarbonate) promotes excretion of long-acting barbiturates (Phenobarbitone) because they are weak acids.

04

üíä Benzodiazepines (BZDs)

Replaced barbiturates as hypnotics/sedatives. High therapeutic index. No enzyme induction.

Mechanism

Bind to specific BZD site on the GABAA receptor complex. Potentiate GABA binding and increase the Frequency of Cl- channel opening ("Benzodiazepine Frequency"). Do not have direct GABA-mimetic action.

Actions

  • Sedation/Hypnosis: Decrease sleep latency, increase sleep duration. Alter sleep architecture (Stage 2 increased, REM and Slow Wave Sleep decreased).
  • Anxiolytic: Selective action on limbic system.
  • Muscle Relaxant: Central action.
  • Anticonvulsant: Diazepam/Lorazepam IV for status epilepticus.
  • Anterograde Amnesia: Useful for minor surgeries/endoscopy.

Pharmacokinetics

Lipid soluble. Metabolized in liver. Many have active metabolites (e.g., Diazepam $\rightarrow$ Desmethyldiazepam $\rightarrow$ Oxazepam), leading to cumulative effects.

üõ°Ô∏è

Advantages over Barbiturates

  • High safety margin (difficult to commit suicide).
  • Low abuse potential.
  • No enzyme induction (fewer interactions).
  • Less effect on REM sleep (less rebound dreaming).
  • Specific Antagonist Available: Flumazenil.
05

üß¨ GABAA Receptor Complex

A pentameric ligand-gated Chloride channel. Major inhibitory receptor in CNS.

Binding Site Ligand Effect
GABA Site GABA, Muscimol Opens Cl- channel (Hyperpolarization).
Barbiturate Site Barbiturates Prolongs opening duration.
Benzodiazepine Site BZDs (Agonist) Increases opening frequency.
Flumazenil (Antagonist) Blocks BZDs and Z-drugs.
$\beta$-Carbolines (Inverse Agonist) Anxiety, convulsions (closes channel).
Channel Pore Picrotoxin Blocks channel (Convulsant).
06

üÜï Newer Nonbenzodiazepine Hypnotics

Zolpidem, Zaleplon, Zopiclone.

Chemically distinct from BZDs but act on the same BZD receptor site. They are selective for the $\alpha$1 subunit of the GABAA receptor.

Advantages

  • Pure Hypnotic: Minimal anxiolytic, muscle relaxant, or anticonvulsant effects.
  • Sleep Architecture: Minimal effect on sleep stages (REM/SWS maintained).
  • Pharmacokinetics: Rapid onset, short duration. Minimal hangover/daytime sedation.
  • Tolerance/Dependence: Very low. Minimal rebound insomnia.
  • Reversal: Antagonized by Flumazenil.
07

üíâ Clinical Uses

  1. Insomnia:
    • Sleep onset difficulty: Zolpidem, Triazolam.
    • Frequent waking/early morning waking: Temazepam, Nitrazepam (intermediate/long acting).
    • Preferred agents: Z-drugs (Zolpidem) due to less tolerance/hangover.
  2. Anxiety Disorders: Diazepam, Alprazolam, Lorazepam. (For acute management; SSRIs preferred for long term).
  3. Anticonvulsant:
    • Status Epilepticus: Lorazepam/Diazepam IV.
    • Maintenance: Phenobarbitone, Clonazepam.
  4. Preanesthetic Medication: Midazolam (sedation + amnesia).
  5. Muscle Relaxation: Diazepam (tetanus, spasms).
  6. Alcohol Withdrawal: Diazepam/Chlordiazepoxide (cross-dependence prevents withdrawal seizures).

ü§î Problem Directed Study

üìù

Case 29.1

A 70-year-old man complains of insomnia (difficulty staying asleep). He was prescribed Diazepam 10 mg at bedtime. He now complains of daytime drowsiness, unsteadiness, and falls.

(a) Reason for Symptoms: Diazepam is a long-acting BZD with active metabolites. In the elderly, hepatic metabolism is reduced, leading to accumulation. The residual sedation ("hangover") causes drowsiness. BZDs also cause muscle relaxation, which in an elderly patient leads to ataxia and falls (hip fracture risk).

(b) Better Choice: A short-acting nonbenzodiazepine like Zolpidem or Zaleplon would be better. They have short half-lives (no hangover), preserve sleep architecture, and have minimal muscle relaxant effects (lower risk of falls). If a BZD must be used, Temazepam or Oxazepam (intermediate acting, no active metabolites, glucuronidation pathway preserved in elderly) are preferred over Diazepam.
