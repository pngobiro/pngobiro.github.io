üîå Adrenergic Transmission

Adrenergic transmission is restricted to the sympathetic division of the ANS. There are three closely related endogenous catecholamines (CAs) which act as signal molecules:

  • Noradrenaline (NA): Transmitter at postganglionic sympathetic sites and in brain.
  • Adrenaline (Adr): Major hormone secreted by adrenal medulla.
  • Dopamine (DA): Major transmitter in basal ganglia, limbic system, anterior pituitary, etc.

Synthesis of Catecholamines

Biosynthesis pathway:
Phenylalanine $\xrightarrow{\text{Hydroxylase}}$ Tyrosine $\xrightarrow{\text{Tyrosine hydroxylase}}$ Dopa $\xrightarrow{\text{Dopa decarboxylase}}$ Dopamine $\xrightarrow{\text{Dopamine } \beta \text{-hydroxylase}}$ Noradrenaline $\xrightarrow{\text{PNMT}}$ Adrenaline

Rate limiting step: Tyrosine hydroxylase (inhibited by $\alpha$-methyl-p-tyrosine).

Storage, Release and Uptake

  • Storage: NA is stored in synaptic vesicles complexed with ATP and chromogranin. Transport into vesicles is by VMAT-2 (Inhibited by Reserpine).
  • Release: Occurs by exocytosis. Modulated by presynaptic receptors ($\alpha_2$ inhibits, $\beta_2$ enhances).
  • Reuptake (Termination):
    • Axonal Uptake (Uptake-1): Active transport by NET. Most important mechanism for terminating action. Inhibited by cocaine, desipramine.
    • Extraneuronal Uptake (Uptake-2): Transport into other tissues. Minor importance.

Metabolism

The major enzymes involved are Monoamine oxidase (MAO) and Catechol-O-methyl transferase (COMT). Major metabolites excreted in urine are VMA (vanillylmandelic acid) and MOPEG.

üì° Adrenergic Receptors

Adrenergic receptors are G-protein coupled receptors classified into $\alpha$ and $\beta$ types.

Type Subtypes Mechanism Key Functions
$\alpha$ (Alpha) $\alpha_1$ Gq $\rightarrow$ $\uparrow$ IP3/DAG $\rightarrow$ $\uparrow$ Ca$^{2+}$ Vasoconstriction, Mydriasis, Gut relaxation, Contraction of genitourinary smooth muscle.
$\alpha_2$ Gi $\rightarrow$ $\downarrow$ cAMP Inhibition of transmitter release (presynaptic), Vasoconstriction, $\downarrow$ Insulin release.
$\beta$ (Beta) $\beta_1$ Gs $\rightarrow$ $\uparrow$ cAMP Cardiac stimulation ($\uparrow$ Rate, Force, Conduction), Renin release.
$\beta_2$ Gs $\rightarrow$ $\uparrow$ cAMP Bronchodilatation, Vasodilatation, Uterine relaxation, Glycogenolysis.
$\beta_3$ Gs $\rightarrow$ $\uparrow$ cAMP Lipolysis, Detrusor relaxation.

Rank order of potency of agonists:

  • $\alpha$ receptors: Adr > NA > Isoprenaline
  • $\beta$ receptors: Isoprenaline > Adr > NA
03

üíä Adrenergic Drugs (Sympathomimetics)

These drugs mimic the actions of sympathetic stimulation. They are classified based on mode of action:

  • Direct sympathomimetics: Act directly as agonists on $\alpha$ or $\beta$ receptors (e.g., Adr, NA, Isoprenaline, Phenylephrine, Salbutamol).
  • Indirect sympathomimetics: Act on adrenergic neurone to release NA (e.g., Tyramine, Amphetamine).
  • Mixed action sympathomimetics: Act directly as well as indirectly (e.g., Ephedrine, Mephentermine).
04

üß™ Catecholamines

1. Adrenaline (Epinephrine)

Potent stimulant of both $\alpha$ and $\beta$ receptors.

  • Heart ($\beta_1$): Increases rate, force, cardiac output and oxygen consumption.
  • Blood vessels: Constricts skin/mucous membrane vessels ($\alpha_1$); dilates skeletal muscle vessels ($\beta_2$). Low doses fall BP (vasodilation dominates), high doses rise BP.
  • Respiration ($\beta_2$): Powerful bronchodilatation.
  • Metabolism: Hyperglycaemia (glycogenolysis), Lipolysis.

Uses: Anaphylactic shock (Drug of choice, 0.5 mg i.m.), Cardiac arrest, with Local anaesthetics (prolongs action), Control of local bleeding.

2. Noradrenaline (Norepinephrine)

Predominantly acts on $\alpha$ receptors and $\beta_1$ receptors (Little action on $\beta_2$).

  • Causes generalized vasoconstriction ($\alpha$) $\rightarrow$ Systolic, Diastolic and Mean BP rise.
  • Reflex bradycardia occurs (due to rise in BP).
  • Use: As pressor agent in shock (rarely used now, Dopamine/Dobutamine preferred). Administered only by slow i.v. infusion ($2-4 \mu \text{g/min}$).

3. Isoprenaline

Potent non-selective $\beta$ agonist ($\beta_1 + \beta_2$). No $\alpha$ action.

  • Heart: Marked tachycardia, increased contractility.
  • BP: Systolic may rise, but Diastolic falls sharply (vasodilation). Mean BP falls.
  • Use: Heart block, Cardiac arrest (temporary measure).

4. Dopamine

Agonist at D1, D2, $\beta_1$ and (at high doses) $\alpha$ receptors.

  • Low dose: Dilates renal/mesenteric vessels (D1) $\rightarrow$ Increases GFR, Na+ excretion.
  • Moderate dose: Positive inotropic effect ($\beta_1$).
  • High dose: Vasoconstriction ($\alpha_1$).
  • Use: Cardiogenic or septic shock ($0.2-1 \text{ mg/min}$ i.v. infusion).

5. Dobutamine

Derivative of Dopamine. Relatively selective $\beta_1$ agonist.

  • Increases force of contraction with less effect on heart rate than Isoprenaline.
  • Use: Inotropic agent in pump failure, MI, CHF ($2-8 \mu \text{g/kg/min}$ i.v. infusion).
Drug Receptor Profile Main Effect
Adrenaline $\alpha_1, \alpha_2, \beta_1, \beta_2$ Cardiac stimulation, Bronchodilatation, Vasoconstriction/dilatation
Noradrenaline $\alpha_1, \alpha_2, \beta_1$ Vasoconstriction, BP rise
Isoprenaline $\beta_1, \beta_2$ Cardiac stimulation, Vasodilatation, Bronchodilatation
Dopamine D1, D2, $\beta_1, \alpha$ Renal vasodilatation, Cardiac stimulation

üíä Non-Catecholamines

Ephedrine

Alkaloid from Ephedra vulgaris. Mixed action (direct + indirect). Orally active, crosses BBB (CNS stimulation). Resistant to MAO. Longer acting than Adr. Used in mild asthma, hypotension.

Amphetamines

Synthetic. Potent CNS stimulant. Indirectly releases NA and DA. Orally active. High abuse potential. Used in Narcolepsy and ADHD (Alternatives preferred).

Phenylephrine

Selective $\alpha_1$ agonist. Raises BP, causes reflex bradycardia. Used as nasal decongestant, mydriatic, and pressor agent.

Nasal Decongestants

Xylometazoline, Oxymetazoline: $\alpha$-agonists (imidazoline compounds). Topical application produces vasoconstriction in nasal mucosa. Long acting (12 hrs). Side effects: Atrophic rhinitis on prolonged use.

Selective $\beta_2$ Stimulants

Salbutamol, Terbutaline: Bronchodilatation, Uterine relaxation. Less cardiac stimulation. Use: Bronchial asthma, Premature labour.

üí°

Other Specific Agents

  • Isoxsuprine: $\beta$ receptor stimulant, vasodilator, uterine relaxant. Used in threatened abortion (doubtful efficacy), peripheral vascular diseases.
  • Ritodrine: Selective $\beta_2$ agonist used as uterine relaxant to postpone labour.
  • Mirabegron: Selective $\beta_3$ agonist. Relaxes detrusor muscle. Used in overactive bladder.
06

üíâ Therapeutic Uses

1. Vascular Uses

  • Anaphylactic shock: Adrenaline (0.5 mg i.m.) is life saving.
  • Hypotension (Shock): Dopamine, Noradrenaline (as pressor agents).
  • Local haemostasis: Adrenaline pack for epistaxis.
  • With Local Anaesthetics: Adrenaline (1:200,000) prolongs duration.
  • Nasal Decongestion: Xylometazoline, Phenylephrine drops.

2. Cardiac Uses

  • Cardiac Arrest: Adrenaline (i.v./intracardiac).
  • Heart Block: Isoprenaline.
  • Congestive Heart Failure: Dopamine / Dobutamine (short term inotropic support).

3. Respiratory

Bronchial Asthma / COPD: Selective $\beta_2$ agonists (Salbutamol, Salmeterol) are primary drugs.

4. Ocular

Glaucoma: Dipivefrine (prodrug of Adr), Apraclonidine, Brimonidine ($\alpha_2$ agonists) reduce aqueous formation.

Mydriatic: Phenylephrine (without cycloplegia) for fundoscopy.

5. Central Uses

ADHD, Narcolepsy: Amphetamines (Methylphenidate, Modafinil now preferred).

6. Uterine Relaxant (Tocolytic)

Ritodrine, Isoxsuprine to delay premature labour.
