üîë Overview & Mechanism

NSAIDs (Nonsteroidal Antiinflammatory Drugs) are a class of drugs that have analgesic, antipyretic, and antiinflammatory actions. Unlike morphine, they do not depress the CNS, do not produce physical dependence, have no abuse liability, and are particularly effective in inflammatory pain.

Mechanism of Action

The major mechanism is the inhibition of Cyclooxygenase (COX), the enzyme responsible for the biosynthesis of prostaglandins (PGs), prostacyclin ($PGI_2$), and thromboxane ($TXA_2$) from arachidonic acid.

  • Aspirin: Inhibits COX irreversibly by acetylating a serine residue.
  • Other NSAIDs: Act as competitive and reversible inhibitors.
üí°

Beneficial Actions of PG Inhibition

  • Analgesia: Prevents sensitization of nerve endings by PGs (peripheral action) and raises pain threshold in CNS.
  • Antipyresis: Resets hypothalamic thermostat to normal by blocking $PGE_2$ production induced by pyrogens (cytokines).
  • Antiinflammatory: Suppresses signs of inflammation (pain, swelling, vasodilatation) mainly by inhibiting COX-2.
  • Antithrombotic: Inhibits platelet aggregation (mainly Aspirin).
  • Closure of ductus arteriosus: In newborns.
02

üíä Salicylates: Aspirin

Aspirin (Acetylsalicylic acid) is rapidly converted in the body to salicylic acid. It is the prototype NSAID.

Pharmacological Actions

  • Analgesic: Effective in low doses (0.3-0.6 g) for inflammatory, tissue injury related, connective tissue, and integumental pain. Weaker than opioids.
  • Antipyretic: Resets thermostat, promotes heat loss via sweating and cutaneous vasodilatation.
  • Antiinflammatory: Requires high doses (3-6 g/day).
  • Blood: Irreversibly inhibits platelet COX-1, blocking $TXA_2$ synthesis. Antiplatelet effect lasts for the life of the platelet (~7 days). Prolongs bleeding time.
  • Metabolic: High doses increase cellular metabolism (uncoupling of oxidative phosphorylation), stimulate respiration (respiratory alkalosis), followed by respiratory depression (acidosis) in toxicity.
‚ò£Ô∏è

Acute Salicylate Poisoning

Fatal dose: 15-30 g in adults.

Manifestations: Vomiting, dehydration, electrolyte imbalance, acidotic breathing, restlessness, delirium, hallucinations, hyperpyrexia, convulsions, coma, death due to respiratory failure.

Treatment: Symptomatic and supportive. External cooling, i.v. fluids with $Na^+$, $K^+$, $HCO_3^-$, and glucose. Alkaline diuresis enhances excretion.

Precautions & Contraindications

  • Peptic ulcer/Bleeding tendencies: Contraindicated.
  • Children: Contraindicated in viral infections (chicken pox, influenza) due to risk of Reye's syndrome (hepatic encephalopathy).
  • Pregnancy: Avoid near term (delayed labour, postpartum haemorrhage, premature closure of ductus).
  • Surgery: Stop 1 week before elective surgery.

üõ°Ô∏è Para-amino phenol Derivatives: Paracetamol

Paracetamol (Acetaminophen) has analgesic and antipyretic actions similar to aspirin but has negligible antiinflammatory action.

Mechanism

It is a poor inhibitor of PG synthesis in peripheral tissues (hence poor antiinflammatory effect) but more active on COX in the brain. It may inhibit a splice variant COX-3 in the brain. It does not stimulate respiration, affect acid-base balance, cause gastric irritation, or affect platelet function.

‚ö†Ô∏è

Acute Paracetamol Poisoning

Occurs with large doses (>10 g in adults). Mechanism: Depletion of glutathione leads to accumulation of the toxic metabolite N-acetyl-p-benzoquinoneimine (NABQI) which binds covalently to liver proteins causing centrilobular hepatic necrosis.

Specific Antidote: N-acetylcysteine (replenishes glutathione). Most effective if given early (<16 hours).

üéØ COX-2 Inhibitors

Developed to inhibit COX-2 (inflammation) without affecting COX-1 ("house keeping" functions like gastric protection and haemostasis). They cause less gastric mucosal damage.

Classification

Preferential COX-2 Inhibitors

  • Nimesulide
  • Diclofenac
  • Aceclofenac
  • Meloxicam
  • Etodolac

Selective COX-2 Inhibitors (Coxibs)

  • Celecoxib
  • Etoricoxib
  • Parecoxib (Injectable)
üíî

Cardiovascular Risk

Selective COX-2 inhibitors reduce endothelial $PGI_2$ (vasodilator/anti-aggregatory) production without affecting platelet $TXA_2$ (pro-aggregatory/vasoconstrictor). This imbalance exerts a prothrombotic influence, increasing the risk of myocardial infarction and stroke. They should be avoided in patients with history of ischaemic heart disease/hypertension.

06

‚ö†Ô∏è Shared Toxicities of NSAIDs

  • Gastrointestinal: Nausea, gastric irritation, erosions, peptic ulceration, bleeding (due to inhibition of protective PGs). Lowest with Paracetamol and COX-2 inhibitors.
  • Renal: $Na^+$ and water retention, edema, chronic renal failure, analgesic nephropathy (inhibition of renal vasodilator PGs).
  • Cardiovascular: Rise in BP, risk of MI/stroke (especially with COX-2 inhibitors and Diclofenac).
  • Haematological: Bleeding (inhibition of platelet aggregation).
  • Hypersensitivity: Rashes, pruritus, angioedema, asthma exacerbation (aspirin-sensitive asthma).
  • Pregnancy: Prolongation of labour, premature closure of ductus arteriosus.
07

‚úÖ Choice of NSAID

Choice is often empirical, based on individual response and risk factors.

Condition Preferred Choice
Mild-to-moderate pain (little inflammation) Paracetamol or low-dose Ibuprofen.
Acute musculoskeletal/dental pain Diclofenac, Ibuprofen, Ketorolac.
Rheumatoid arthritis / Acute Gout Naproxen, Piroxicam, Indomethacin, high dose Aspirin.
Gastric intolerance / High ulcer risk Selective COX-2 inhibitor or Paracetamol (often with PPI).
CV Risk (IHD, Hypertension) Avoid COX-2 inhibitors. Use Naproxen or low-dose Aspirin/Ibuprofen.
Paediatric patients Paracetamol, Ibuprofen. Avoid Aspirin (Reye's syndrome).
Pregnancy Paracetamol is safest.

Case 14.1

A 65-year-old lady with osteoarthritis of the knee and a history of heart attack (treated with angioplasty/stent 1 year ago) is taking aspirin 75 mg daily.

(a) Suitable Analgesic: Paracetamol (up to 4g/day) is the first choice for osteoarthritis, especially given her cardiac history. If insufficient, Naproxen is considered the safest NSAID regarding cardiovascular risk.

(b) To be Avoided: Selective COX-2 inhibitors (Etoricoxib, Celecoxib) and Diclofenac should be avoided due to increased risk of thrombotic cardiovascular events (MI/stroke), which is dangerous for a patient with history of MI.

(c) Topical Medication: Yes, topical NSAIDs (Diclofenac gel, etc.) can be helpful. They provide local relief with minimal systemic absorption, thus reducing the risk of systemic side effects (gastric/CV).
