Local Anaesthetics

‚öóÔ∏è Overview & Chemistry

Local anaesthetics (LAs) are drugs which, upon topical application or local injection, cause reversible loss of sensory perception, especially of pain, in a restricted area of the body. They consist of a hydrophilic amine and a lipophilic aromatic residue joined by an intermediate chain.

Stability Unstable in solution Stable

‚öôÔ∏è Mechanism of Action

LAs block voltage-gated $Na^+$ channels in the neuronal membrane, preventing depolarization and impulse conduction.

  1. Penetration: The unionized lipophilic free base ($B$) crosses the axonal membrane.
  2. Ionization: Inside the axoplasm (lower pH), it re-ionizes to the cationic form ($BH^+$).
  3. Blockade: The cationic form binds to a specific receptor site within the channel pore (intracellular side), stabilizing it in the inactivated state. This prevents $Na^+$ influx.
üí°

Frequency (Use) Dependence

LAs bind more avidly to activated (open) and inactivated channels than to resting channels. Thus, rapidly firing fibres (sensory/pain fibres) are blocked earlier and more intensely than resting fibres.

Order of Blockade

Small diameter fibres are blocked first. Myelinated fibres are blocked before non-myelinated fibres of the same diameter.

Autonomic (sympathetic) $\rightarrow$ Pain $\rightarrow$ Temperature $\rightarrow$ Touch $\rightarrow$ Deep Pressure $\rightarrow$ Motor

03

üìâ Factors Affecting Action

1. pH of Tissue

LAs are weak bases. In inflamed/infected tissue (acidic pH), the drug remains ionized outside the nerve and cannot penetrate the membrane. Thus, LAs are less effective in infected tissues.

2. Vasoconstrictors

Adrenaline (1:50,000 to 1:200,000) is often added to LAs.

  • Prolongs duration: Reduces wash-out by blood flow.
  • Reduces systemic toxicity: Slows absorption into circulation.
  • Provides bloodless field: Useful in surgery.
üö´

Contraindications for Adrenaline

Do not use Adrenaline-containing LAs in areas supplied by end arteries (fingers, toes, penis, pinna, tip of nose) due to risk of ischemia and gangrene.

üíä Individual Drugs

Esters

  • Cocaine: Natural alkaloid. Excellent surface anaesthetic but high abuse potential (CNS stimulant). Causes vasoconstriction (blocks NA reuptake). Rarely used now.
  • Procaine: Poor surface anaesthetic. Used for infiltration. Short duration. High allergy risk. PABA antagonist (inhibits sulfonamides).
  • Benzocaine: Highly insoluble. Used only as topical powder/ointment for wounds/ulcers (slow release).

Amides

  • Lidocaine (Lignocaine): Prototype amide. Rapid onset, intermediate duration. Versatile: Surface, infiltration, nerve block, spinal, epidural. Also an antiarrhythmic.
  • Bupivacaine: Potent, long-acting. Differential Sensory Block: At lower concentrations (0.125-0.25%), it blocks sensory fibres without significant motor block. Ideal for labour analgesia ("Walking Epidural") and postoperative pain relief.
  • Ropivacaine: Newer congener of bupivacaine. Less cardiotoxic. Good sensory-motor separation.
  • Prilocaine: Can cause Methaemoglobinaemia (metabolite o-toluidine).
  • Dibucaine (Cinchocaine): Most potent and toxic/long acting. Used in spinal anaesthesia.

Topical Combinations

EMLA (Eutectic Mixture of Local Anaesthetics): Lidocaine + Prilocaine. Liquefies at room temperature. Penetrates intact skin. Used for venepuncture, skin grafting.

‚ö†Ô∏è Systemic Toxicity & Complications

Occurs due to accidental intravascular injection or rapid absorption.

1. CNS Toxicity

Excitation (tremors, shivering, convulsions) followed by depression (respiratory failure, coma). Cocaine causes euphoria and hallucinations. Lidocaine causes drowsiness, perioral paresthesia, and seizures.

2. CVS Toxicity

Myocardial depression (quinidine-like), conduction block, vasodilatation (hypotension). Cocaine is the exception (vasoconstriction, hypertension, arrhythmias).

üíî

Bupivacaine Cardiotoxicity

Bupivacaine dissociates very slowly from cardiac $Na^+$ channels. Accidental i.v. injection can cause intractable ventricular arrhythmias and cardiac arrest (electromechanical dissociation). Resuscitation is difficult. Intralipid (20% Lipid emulsion) infusion is the specific antidote.

3. Spinal Anaesthesia Complications

  • Hypotension: Due to sympathetic blockade (vasodilatation). Treated with fluids and vasoconstrictors (Ephedrine/Mephentermine).
  • Respiratory Paralysis: If block ascends too high (intercostals/phrenic nerve).
  • Post-dural Puncture Headache (PDPH): Due to CSF leak. Worse on standing. Treated with bed rest, fluids, analgesics, epidural blood patch.
  • Cauda Equina Syndrome: Injury to nerve roots.

ü§î Problem Directed Study

üìù

Case 26.1

A healthy full-term primigravida aged 26 years demands pain relief during labour. There is no cephalopelvic disproportion.

(a) Recommendation: Yes, regional anaesthesia can be used. The most suitable method is Lumbar Epidural Analgesia.

(b) Drug Choice: Bupivacaine (0.125% to 0.25%) or Ropivacaine.

Reasoning: Epidural Bupivacaine at low concentrations produces "Differential Sensory Block". It relieves pain (sensory block) while sparing motor power. This allows the mother to actively participate in labour by "bearing down" during contractions and mobilizing ("Walking Epidural"), which is essential for normal vaginal delivery. Spinal anaesthesia produces intense motor block, hindering expulsion.
