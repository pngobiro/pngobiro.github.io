ü©∫ Diabetes Mellitus (DM)

Diabetes mellitus is a metabolic disorder characterized by hyperglycaemia (fasting plasma glucose $\geq 126$ mg/dL and/or 2 hr post-glucose $\geq 200$ mg/dL), glycosuria, hyperlipidaemia, negative nitrogen balance, and sometimes ketonaemia.

Type 1 (IDDM)

Insulin Dependent: $\beta$ cell destruction (autoimmune or idiopathic). Circulating insulin levels are low or absent. Prone to ketosis. Genetic predisposition is low.

Type 2 (NIDDM)

Non-Insulin Dependent: No loss or moderate reduction in $\beta$ cell mass. Insulin levels may be normal or high (relative resistance). Strong genetic predisposition. Associated with obesity/metabolic syndrome.

02

üß¨ Insulin Structure & Physiology

Insulin is a two-chain polypeptide (A-chain 21 AA, B-chain 30 AA). It is anabolic, promoting storage of nutrients (glycogen, triglycerides, protein).

Signal Transduction

Binding to RTK receptor $\rightarrow$ Autophosphorylation $\rightarrow$ IRS phosphorylation $\rightarrow$ PI3-Kinase/MAP-Kinase pathways $\rightarrow$ Translocation of GLUT4 to membrane (rapid action) and gene regulation (long-term).

üìù Therapy & Complications

Basal-Bolus Regimen: Long-acting analogue (Basal) + Rapid acting analogue before meals (Bolus).

Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)

  1. Fluid Replacement: Vital. Normal saline.
  2. Insulin: Regular Insulin i.v. infusion.
  3. Potassium ($K^+$): Correction of hypokalaemia.
  4. Bicarbonate: Only if severe acidosis (pH < 7.0).

Adverse Effects: Hypoglycaemia (most common), Lipodystrophy, Allergy, Edema.

05

üíä Sulfonylureas (KATP Blockers)

Mechanism: Block $K^+_{ATP}$ channels on pancreatic $\beta$ cells $\rightarrow$ depolarization $\rightarrow$ $Ca^{2+}$ influx $\rightarrow$ Insulin release.

  • Glibenclamide: Potent, long acting. High risk of hypoglycaemia.
  • Glipizide: Shorter acting, lower risk of hypoglycaemia.
  • Gliclazide: Antiplatelet properties. Suitable for elderly.
  • Glimepiride: Long acting, once daily. Less weight gain.

Adverse Effects: Hypoglycaemia, Weight gain.

Safety in pregnancy is not established. Use insulin.

06

‚ö° Meglitinides

Repaglinide, Nateglinide.

Mechanism: Like sulfonylureas, they block $K^+_{ATP}$ channels but have a quick and short lasting action. Designed to normalize meal-time glucose excursions.

Use: Administered before each major meal. Omit dose if meal is missed. Lower risk of serious hypoglycaemia than SUs.

07

‚úÇÔ∏è DPP-4 Inhibitors (Gliptins)

Sitagliptin, Vildagliptin, Saxagliptin, Teneligliptin.

Mechanism: Inhibit the enzyme Dipeptidyl peptidase-4 (DPP-4), preventing the degradation of incretins (GLP-1 and GIP). This potentiates insulin release and suppresses glucagon secretion in a glucose-dependent manner.

  • Advantages: Low risk of hypoglycaemia, body weight neutral, well tolerated.
  • Use: As adjuvant to Metformin/SUs/Glitazones.
  • Adverse Effects: Nasopharyngitis, headache, hypersensitivity.
08

üèóÔ∏è Biguanides: Metformin

Metformin is the first choice drug for Type 2 DM.

Mechanism: Activates AMPK (AMP-dependent protein kinase).

  1. Suppresses hepatic gluconeogenesis (major action).
  2. Enhances insulin-mediated glucose uptake in muscle/fat.
  3. Retards intestinal glucose absorption.

‚úÖ

Advantages

  • Antihyperglycaemic (not hypoglycaemic).
  • Promotes weight loss.
  • Reduces macrovascular and microvascular complications.

Adverse Effects: Abdominal pain, nausea, metallic taste, diarrhoea. Lactic acidosis (rare, avoid in renal/hepatic failure, hypoxia, alcoholics). Vitamin B12 deficiency.

09

üîã Thiazolidinediones (Glitazones)

Pioglitazone. (Rosiglitazone is largely withdrawn).

Mechanism: Selective agonist for nuclear PPAR$\gamma$ receptors. Enhances transcription of insulin responsive genes (e.g., GLUT4). Reverses insulin resistance.

Adverse Effects: Fluid retention, edema, weight gain, risk of heart failure (CHF), bone fractures in women. Contraindicated in liver disease and CHF.

10

üçû Alpha-Glucosidase Inhibitors

Acarbose, Miglitol, Voglibose.

Mechanism: Reversibly inhibit $\alpha$-glucosidases in the intestinal brush border. Slow down digestion/absorption of polysaccharides and sucrose. Reduces postprandial hyperglycaemia.

Adverse Effects: Flatulence, abdominal discomfort, loose stools (due to fermentation of unabsorbed carbohydrates).

11

üöΩ SGLT-2 Inhibitors

Dapagliflozin, Canagliflozin.

Mechanism: Inhibit Sodium-Glucose Co-Transporter-2 in renal tubules. Induce glycosuria and lower blood glucose. Also cause weight loss.

Adverse Effects: Genital/urinary tract infections, electrolyte imbalance, hypovolemia.

12

‚¨ÜÔ∏è Glucagon

Hyperglycaemic hormone from pancreatic $\alpha$ cells. Increases cAMP. Promotes glycogenolysis and gluconeogenesis.

Uses:

  1. Severe Hypoglycaemia: When oral glucose cannot be given and i.v. access is difficult (0.5-1 mg i.m./s.c.).
  2. Beta-blocker overdose: Stimulates heart via glucagon receptors (bypassing $\beta$ receptors).

Case 19.1

A 50-year male office executive with sedentary lifestyle was diagnosed with type 2 diabetes. Fasting glucose 130 mg/dl, PP 190 mg/dl, HbA1c 7.8%. BMI 27 kg/m¬≤. Asymptomatic.

Recommendation: Yes, he should be prescribed an antidiabetic medication in addition to lifestyle modification.

Choice: Metformin.

Reason: Metformin is the first choice drug for Type 2 DM, especially in obese/overweight patients (BMI 27). It improves insulin sensitivity, helps in weight control, does not cause hypoglycaemia, and has potential cardiovascular benefits. Early initiation is recommended to preserve $\beta$-cell function.
