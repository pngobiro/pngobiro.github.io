‚öñÔ∏è Calcium Regulation

Plasma calcium level ($9-11$ mg/dl) is precisely regulated by three principal hormones: Parathormone (PTH), Calcitonin, and Calcitriol (Active Vitamin D). They control intestinal absorption, bone exchange, and renal excretion.

Hormone Source Effect on Plasma Ca$^{2+}$ Major Action
PTH Parathyroid Glands Increases ($\uparrow$) Increases bone resorption, renal reabsorption, and Vit D activation.
Calcitonin Thyroid Parafollicular Cells Decreases ($\downarrow$) Inhibits bone resorption (inhibits osteoclasts).
Calcitriol Kidney (Activation) Increases ($\uparrow$) Increases intestinal absorption.
02

ü¶ã Parathormone (PTH) & Teriparatide

PTH secretion is regulated by plasma $Ca^{2+}$ concentration via calcium-sensing receptors (CaSR). Hypocalcaemia stimulates PTH release.

Actions

  • Bone: Promotes resorption (mobilizes Ca). Continuous high levels activate osteoclasts (via RANKL induction in osteoblasts). However, intermittent low doses stimulate bone formation.
  • Kidney: Increases $Ca^{2+}$ reabsorption (distal tubule) and phosphate excretion. Activates $1\alpha$-hydroxylase to generate active Vit D.
  • Intestine: Indirectly increases Ca absorption via Calcitriol.

Teriparatide

Recombinant human PTH (1-34 amino terminal). It is an anabolic agent for bone.

‚úÖ

Therapeutic Use

Used in severe osteoporosis (20 $\mu$g s.c. daily). Unlike bisphosphonates (which only stop resorption), teriparatide stimulates new bone formation, increasing bone mineral density and reducing fracture risk.

Limitation: Use restricted to 2 years due to risk of osteosarcoma (seen in rats).

Cinacalcet

A calcimimetic agent. It activates CaSR on parathyroid cells, suppressing PTH secretion. Used in secondary hyperparathyroidism (CKD) and parathyroid carcinoma.

03

üõë Calcitonin

Hypocalcaemic hormone. Used clinically as Salmon Calcitonin (more potent/longer acting than human).

Actions

Directly inhibits osteoclasts (via cAMP), reducing bone resorption. Also increases renal Ca excretion.

Uses

  • Hypercalcaemia: Rapid action (4-6 hrs), but weak and transient (refractoriness develops). Adjuvant to fluids/bisphosphonates.
  • Paget's Disease: Reduces bone pain and turnover. Second line to bisphosphonates.
  • Postmenopausal Osteoporosis: Nasal spray. Less effective than other drugs; risk of malignancy has restricted use.
04

‚òÄÔ∏è Vitamin D

Synthesized in skin (D3, Cholecalciferol) or ingested (D2, Ergocalciferol). It is a prohormone.

Activation Pathway

Skin/Diet $\rightarrow$ Liver (25-hydroxylation) $\rightarrow$ 25-OH D3 (Calcifediol) (Major circulating form) $\rightarrow$ Kidney ($1\alpha$-hydroxylation via PTH) $\rightarrow$ 1,25-(OH)2 D3 (Calcitriol) (Active hormone).

Actions

Mediated via nuclear Vitamin D Receptors (VDR).

  • Intestine: Induces synthesis of calcium binding protein (Calbindin) $\rightarrow$ Promotes Ca and Phosphate absorption.
  • Bone: Promotes mineralization (by maintaining plasma Ca/PO4). Essential for normal bone growth.
  • Kidney: Enhances tubular reabsorption.

Preparations & Uses

Preparation Indication
Cholecalciferol (D3) / Ergocalciferol (D2) Nutritional deficiency (Rickets, Osteomalacia). Prophylaxis.
Calcitriol (Active form) Renal Osteodystrophy (Kidney cannot activate D3), Hypoparathyroidism.
Alfacalcidol ($1\alpha$-OH D3) Prodrug activated in liver (bypasses renal activation). Useful in renal bone disease.

üß± Bisphosphonates

Analogues of pyrophosphate (P-C-P bond). Most effective antiresorptive drugs.

Mechanism

They concentrate in bones. When osteoclasts resorb bone, BPNs are released locally and internalized. They inhibit the mevalonate pathway (FPP synthase) $\rightarrow$ prevent protein prenylation $\rightarrow$ osteoclast apoptosis and loss of function.

Generations & Drugs

  • 1st Gen: Etidronate (Least potent, causes osteomalacia).
  • 2nd Gen (Amino-BPNs): Alendronate, Pamidronate. Potent.
  • 3rd Gen: Zoledronate, Risedronate, Ibandronate. Most potent.

Pharmacokinetics & Administration

Oral bioavailability is very low (<1%). Food interferes with absorption.

‚ö†Ô∏è

Instructions for Oral Alendronate

Must be taken on an empty stomach in the morning with a full glass of water. Patient must remain upright for 30 minutes. Why? To prevent prolonged contact with esophageal mucosa which causes severe esophagitis and ulceration.

Therapeutic Uses

  1. Osteoporosis: Postmenopausal, senile, and steroid-induced. Alendronate (70 mg/week), Risedronate, or Zoledronate (yearly i.v.) reduce fracture risk markedly.
  2. Paget's Disease: Drug of choice (High dose).
  3. Hypercalcaemia of Malignancy: Zoledronate (4 mg i.v.) or Pamidronate are highly effective (drug of choice).
  4. Bone Metastasis: Reduce pain and fractures (Zoledronate, Pamidronate).

Adverse Effects

Esophagitis (oral), flu-like symptoms (i.v.), renal toxicity (Zoledronate), Osteonecrosis of the Jaw (ONJ) (rare, mainly with high dose i.v. in cancer patients), atypical femoral fractures (long term use).

06

üÜï Other Bone Drugs

Denosumab

Fully human monoclonal antibody against RANKL. It mimics the action of Osteoprotegerin (OPG).

  • Mechanism: Binds RANKL $\rightarrow$ prevents activation of RANK on osteoclasts $\rightarrow$ inhibits osteoclast formation and activity.
  • Use: Osteoporosis (s.c. every 6 months), bone metastasis.
  • Advantages: Can be used in renal failure (unlike bisphosphonates).

Strontium Ranelate

Has dual action: inhibits bone resorption and stimulates bone formation. Used as reserve drug (CV risk).
