üß™ Pharmacology

Pharmacology is the science of drugs (Greek: Pharmacon‚Äîdrug; logos‚Äîdiscourse in). In a broad sense, it deals with interaction of exogenously administered chemical molecules with living systems, and any single chemical substance which can produce a biological response is a 'drug'. Pharmacology encompasses all aspects of knowledge about drugs, but most importantly those that are relevant to effective and safe use of drugs for medicinal purposes.

For thousands of years most drugs were crude natural products of unknown composition and limited efficacy. Only the overt effects of these substances on the body were rather imprecisely known, but how the same were produced was entirely unknown. Animal experiments, primarily aimed at understanding physiological processes, were started in the 18th century. These were pioneered by F. Magendie and Claude Bernard, who also adapted them to study effects of certain drugs. Pharmacology as an experimental science was ushered by Rudolf Buchheim who founded the first institute of pharmacology in 1847 in Germany. In the later part of the 19th century, Oswald Schmiedeberg, regarded as the 'father of pharmacology', together with his many disciples like J Langley, T Frazer, P Ehrlich, AJ Clark, JJ Abel propounded some of the fundamental concepts in pharmacology. Since then drugs have been purified, chemically characterized and a vast variety of highly potent and selective new drugs have been developed. The mechanism of action including molecular target of many drugs has been elucidated. This has been possible due to prolific growth of pharmacology which forms the backbone of rational therapeutics.

The Two Main Divisions

Pharmacodynamics (Greek: dynamis‚Äîpower)‚ÄîWhat the drug does to the body.

This includes physiological and biochemical effects of drugs and their mechanism of action at organ system/subcellular/macromolecular levels, e.g.‚ÄîAdrenaline $\rightarrow$ interaction with adrenoceptors $\rightarrow$ G-protein mediated stimulation of cell membrane bound adenylyl cyclase $\rightarrow$ increased intracellular cyclic $3', 5'$ AMP $\rightarrow$ cardiac stimulation, hepatic glycogenolysis and hyperglycaemia, etc.

Pharmacokinetics (Greek: Kinesis‚Äîmovement)‚ÄîWhat the body does to the drug.

This refers to movement of the drug in and alteration of the drug by the body; includes absorption, distribution, binding/localization/storage, biotransformation and excretion of the drug.

Example: Paracetamol is rapidly and almost completely absorbed orally attaining peak blood levels at 30‚Äì60 min; 25% bound to plasma proteins, widely and almost uniformly distributed in the body (volume of distribution $\sim 1 \text{ L/kg}$); extensively metabolized in the liver, primarily by glucuronide and sulfate conjugation into inactive metabolites which are excreted in urine; has a plasma half life $(\text{t}^{1/2})$ of 2‚Äì3 hours and a clearance value of 5 ml/kg/min.

Definitions

Drug (French: Drogue‚Äîa dry herb) It is the single active chemical entity present in a medicine that is used for diagnosis, prevention, treatment/cure of a disease.

This disease oriented definition of drug does not include contraceptives or use of drugs for improvement of health. The WHO (1966) has given a more comprehensive definition‚Äî"Drug is any substance or product that is used or is intended to be used to modify or explore physiological systems or pathological states for the benefit of the recipient."

Other Important Aspects

  • Pharmacotherapeutics It is the application of pharmacological information together with knowledge of the disease for its prevention, mitigation or cure.
  • Clinical pharmacology It is the scientific study of drugs (both old and new) in man. It includes pharmacodynamic and pharmacokinetic investigation in healthy volunteers as well as in patients. The aim of clinical pharmacology is to generate data for optimum use of drugs and the practice of 'evidence based medicine'.
  • Chemotherapy It is the treatment of systemic infection/malignancy with specific drugs that have selective toxicity for the infecting organism/malignant cell with no/minimal effects on the host cells.
  • Pharmacy It is the art and science of compounding and dispensing drugs or preparing suitable dosage forms for administration of drugs to man or animals. The large scale manufacture of drugs is called Pharmaceutics.
  • Toxicology It is the study of poisonous effect of drugs and other chemicals with emphasis on detection, prevention and treatment of poisonings.

üåø Nature & Sources of Drugs

Nature of Drugs

The molecular weight of majority of drugs falls in the range of 100‚Äì1000 D, because molecules smaller than 100 D do not generally have sufficiently specific features... On the other hand, larger molecules than 1000 D do not readily pass through membranes/barriers in the body to reach the target sites in various tissues/cells. However, few drugs are as small as lithium ion (7D), and some like heparin (10‚Äì20 KD), gonadotropins (>30 KD), enzymes, proteins, antibodies (>50 KD) are much bigger. Bulky molecule drugs have to be administered parenterally.

Sources of Drugs

  1. 1 Plants Many plants contain biologically active substances and are the oldest source of drugs.
    • Alkaloids: Alkaline nitrogenous bases (morphine, atropine, ephedrine, nicotine).
    • Glycosides: Heterocyclic nonsugar moiety linked to sugar (digoxin).
    • Oils: Fixed oils (castor oil) and Essential oils (eucalyptus, clove).
  2. 2 Animals e.g. adrenaline, thyroxine, insulin, liver extract, antisera, vaccines.
  3. 3 Microbes Most antibiotics are obtained from fungi, actinomycetes and bacteria (penicillin, gentamicin, tetracycline).
  4. 4 Minerals Iron salts, calcium salts, lithium carbonate, magnesium/aluminium hydroxide, iodine.
  5. 5 Synthetic chemistry This is now the largest source of medicines (benzodiazepines, thiazides, fluoroquinolones).
  6. 6 Biotechnology Peptides and proteins produced by recombinant DNA technology (human growth hormone, human insulin, interferon).

üè∑Ô∏è Drug Nomenclature & Compendia

A drug generally has three categories of names:

  1. a Chemical name: Describes the substance chemically, e.g. 1-(Isopropylamino)-3-(1-naphthyloxy) propan-2-ol for propranolol.
  2. b Non-proprietary name: Accepted by a competent scientific body/authority (USAN, BAN, rINN). In common parlance, termed generic name.
  3. c Proprietary (Brand) name: Assigned by the manufacturer, e.g. AMCARD, AMLOGARD for amlodipine.
üí°

Drug Compendia

  • Pharmacopoeias: Official compendia (IP, BP, USP) containing standards of purity, assay methods, etc.
  • Formularies: Booklets listing indications, dose, dosage forms, etc. (BNF, NFI).
  • Martindale: The Complete Drug Reference: Exhaustive non-official compendium.

üè• Essential Medicines & Orphan Drugs

The WHO has defined Essential Medicines as "those that satisfy the priority healthcare needs of the population." They are selected with due regard to public health relevance, evidence on efficacy and safety, and comparative cost effectiveness.

Orphan Drugs: These are drugs or biological products for diagnosis/treatment/prevention of a rare disease or condition, or a more common disease (endemic only in resource poor countries or areas) for which there is no reasonable expectation that the cost of developing and marketing it will be recovered from the sales of that drug.

üíä Dosage Forms of Drugs

Solid dosage forms

  • Powders: Dry and finely pulverised state.
  • Tablets: Compressed/moulded. Variations: Chewable, Dispersible, Sublingual, Enteric coated, Sustained/Extended release, Controlled release.
  • Pills: Spherical/oval bodies (archaic).
  • Capsules: Water soluble cylindrical containers made of gelatin. Spansules are extended release capsules.
  • Lozenges: Tablet-like bodies to be retained in the mouth.
  • Suppositories: Conical bullet-shaped dosage forms for insertion into the anal canal (Pessaries for vagina, Bougies for urethra).

Liquid dosage forms

  • Aqueous solutions: Drug dissolved in water.
  • Suspensions/Emulsions: Dispersion of insoluble drugs or uniform mixtures of immiscible liquids.
  • Elixirs/Syrups: Hydro-alcoholic solutions or concentrated sugar solutions.
  • Drops: Concentrated solutions for oral ingestion or external application.
  • Lotions/Liniments: External application without or with rubbing.
  • Injections: Sterile solutions or suspensions.

Semisolid dosage forms

  • Ointments/Creams: Greasy/emulsion preparations for external application.
  • Pastes: Nongreasy preparations of thick consistency.
  • Gels: Viscous colloidal solution of gelatin.

üõ£Ô∏è Routes of Administration

Most drugs can be administered by a variety of routes. The choice depends on drug (solubility, stability) and patient (condition, urgency) related factors.

Local Routes

  1. Topical: External application to surface (skin, mucous membranes, eye, ear, etc.).
  2. Deeper tissues: Intra-articular, infiltration, intrathecal, retrobulbar injection.
  3. Arterial supply: Close intra-arterial injection (angiography, anticancer drugs).

Systemic Routes

1. Oral

Oldest and commonest mode. Safer, convenient, noninvasive.

‚ñ∂ Limitations of oral route
  • Action is slower; not suitable for emergencies.
  • Unpalatable drugs difficult to administer.
  • May cause nausea and vomiting.
  • Cannot be used for uncooperative/unconscious/vomiting patient.
  • Absorption may be variable/erratic; some not absorbed (streptomycin).
  • Destroyed by digestive juices (insulin) or liver (first pass metabolism).

2. Sublingual (s.l.) or buccal

Tablet placed under tongue or crushed in mouth. Only lipid soluble and non-irritating drugs. Advantage: Liver is bypassed (GTN, buprenorphine).

3. Rectal

Suppositories or retention enema. Useful when patient is vomiting or unconscious. About 50% bypasses liver.

4. Cutaneous

Highly lipid soluble drugs applied over skin for slow and prolonged absorption (Transdermal therapeutic systems - TTS).

5. Inhalation

Volatile liquids and gases. Rapid action due to vast surface area of alveoli.

6. Nasal

Mucous membrane of nose absorbs many drugs (GnRH agonists, desmopressin). Digestive juices and liver are bypassed.

üíâ Parenteral Routes

(Par‚Äîbeyond, enteral‚Äîintestinal). Administration by injection which takes drug directly into tissue fluid or blood.

(i) Subcutaneous (s.c.)

Drug deposited in loose subcutaneous tissue. Slower absorption than i.m. Self-injection possible. Special forms: Dermojet, Pellet implantation, Sialistic implants.

(ii) Intramuscular (i.m.)

Injected in large skeletal muscles. Faster absorption. Less painful. Depot preparations can be used.

(iii) Intravenous (i.v.)

Bolus or infusion. Immediate effect (100% bioavailability). Risky route. Only aqueous solutions (not suspensions) to be injected.

(iv) Intradermal

Injected into skin raising a bleb (BCG vaccine, sensitivity testing).
