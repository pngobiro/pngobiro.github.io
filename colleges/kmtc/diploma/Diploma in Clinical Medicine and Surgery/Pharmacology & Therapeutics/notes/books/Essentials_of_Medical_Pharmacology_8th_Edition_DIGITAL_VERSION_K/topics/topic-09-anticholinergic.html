üõë Muscarinic Receptor Antagonists

Conventionally, the term 'anticholinergic drugs' is restricted to those which block actions of ACh on autonomic effectors and in the CNS exerted through muscarinic receptors. They are competitive antagonists. Atropine, the prototype drug of this class, is highly selective for muscarinic receptors.

Pharmacological Actions (Atropine as Prototype)

The actions of atropine can be largely predicted from knowledge of parasympathetic responses. Prominent effects are seen in organs which normally receive strong parasympathetic tone.

1. CNS

Atropine has an overall CNS stimulant action (high doses). Stimulates medullary centres (vagal, respiratory, vasomotor). Suppresses tremor/rigidity (Parkinsonism) and vestibular excitation (motion sickness).

2. CVS

Heart: Tachycardia due to blockade of $M_2$ receptors on SA node. Facilitates A-V conduction.
BP: No consistent effect, but blocks vasodepressor action of cholinergic agonists.

3. Eye

Mydriasis (pupil dilation), abolition of light reflex, and cycloplegia (paralysis of accommodation). IOP tends to rise, especially in narrow angle glaucoma.

4. Smooth Muscles

Relaxation of all visceral smooth muscles (stomach, intestine, bladder, bronchi). Constipation, urinary retention, bronchodilatation.

5. Glands

Marked decrease in sweat, salivary, tracheobronchial and lacrimal secretion ($M_3$ blockade). Skin/eyes become dry, talking/swallowing difficult. Gastric acid reduced but less efficacious than $H_2$ blockers.

6. Body Temperature

Rise in body temperature (Atropine fever), especially in children, due to inhibition of sweating and stimulation of temperature regulating centre.

üîÑ Atropine Substitutes

Synthetic compounds introduced to produce more selective action on certain functions.

Quaternary Compounds

Incomplete oral absorption, poor CNS/eye penetration.

  • Hyoscine butyl bromide: For esophageal/GI spasms.
  • Atropine methonitrate: For abdominal colics.
  • Ipratropium bromide: Inhalation for COPD/Asthma. Does not depress mucociliary clearance.
  • Tiotropium bromide: Long acting, high affinity for $M_1/M_3$. Once daily for COPD.
  • Propantheline: Antisecretory/antispasmodic.
  • Glycopyrrolate: Potent, rapidly acting. Used for preanaesthetic medication.

Tertiary Amines

Better absorption, can penetrate CNS/eye.

  • Dicyclomine: Direct smooth muscle relaxant + weak anticholinergic. Antispasmodic/Antiemetic.
  • Valethamate: Hastens cervical dilatation in labour.
  • Pirenzepine: Selective $M_1$ blocker. Inhibits gastric secretion (obsolete).
  • Oxybutynin: Vasicoselective. For urinary frequency/urgency.
  • Tolterodine: Relatively $M_3$ selective for overactive bladder.
  • Flavoxate: Urinary antispasmodic.
  • Darifenacin/Solifenacin: $M_3$ selective for urinary incontinence.

Mydriatics

Used for refraction testing and fundoscopy. Shorter acting than atropine.

Drug Onset Duration Use
Homatropine 45-60 min 1-3 days Standard mydriatic
Cyclopentolate 30-60 min ~1 day Cycloplegic refraction

üíâ Therapeutic Uses

  • 1 As antisecretory: Preanaesthetic medication (Glycopyrrolate/Atropine) to check secretions and prevent laryngospasm/vasovagal attack.
  • 2 As antispasmodic: Intestinal/renal colic, nervous diarrhoea, IBS, pylorospasm, urinary frequency/urgency (Oxybutynin, Tolterodine).
  • 3 Bronchial asthma/COPD: Ipratropium/Tiotropium inhalation. Effective in asthmatic bronchitis and COPD.
  • 4 Mydriatic and Cycloplegic: For refraction testing (Cyclopentolate/Tropicamide) and treatment of iridocyclitis/keratitis (Atropine).
  • 5 Cardiac vagolytic: Sinus bradycardia, partial heart block (Atropine).
  • 6 Central uses: Parkinsonism (mild cases), Motion sickness (Hyoscine).
  • 7 Antidote: For Anticholinesterase (organophosphate) poisoning and Mushroom poisoning (early type).

Symptoms of Atropine Poisoning

  • Dry mouth, difficulty swallowing.
  • Dry, hot, flushed skin.
  • Dilated pupil, photophobia, blurring.
  • Excitement, psychosis, delirium, hallucinations.
  • Hypotension, weak pulse, cardiovascular collapse, respiratory depression.

Treatment

  • Gastric lavage: With tannic acid (if ingested).
  • Supportive: Dark quiet room, cold sponging (for fever), diazepam (for convulsions).
  • Specific Antidote: Physostigmine ($1-3 \text{ mg}$ s.c. or i.v.). It antagonises both central and peripheral effects. Neostigmine does not block central effects.

Contraindications: Narrow angle glaucoma, prostatic hypertrophy (caution).

üö¨ Drugs Acting on Autonomic Ganglia

Acetylcholine is the primary excitatory neurotransmitter in both sympathetic and parasympathetic ganglia (Nicotinic $N_N$ receptors).

Nicotine

Principal alkaloid in tobacco. Stimulates both sympathetic and parasympathetic ganglia (low dose) followed by blockade (high dose).

  • Uses: Smoking cessation (Nicotine transdermal patch, chewing gum).
  • Related drugs: Varenicline (Partial agonist of $\alpha 4 \beta 2$ NRs), Bupropion (Antidepressant).

Ganglion Blocking Agents

Competitive antagonists (e.g., Hexamethonium, Trimethaphan, Mecamylamine). They block both sympathetic and parasympathetic ganglia.

Organ Dominant Tone Effect of Blockade
Heart Parasympathetic Tachycardia
Blood vessels Sympathetic Vasodilatation, Hypotension
Iris Parasympathetic Mydriasis
Intestine/Bladder Parasympathetic Constipation, Urinary retention

Note: Ganglion blockers are obsolete clinically due to multiple side effects.

Case Study 8.1

An elderly male aged 74 years was brought to the hospital since he had not passed urine for the past 24 hours and had severe pain in lower abdomen. On examination there was a bulge in the pubic region due to full urinary bladder. On catheterization, he passed 1.5 L urine and the pain was relieved.

He gave the history of having difficulty in passing urine, poor stream, frequent urge to urinate and post-void dribbling for the last 3 years. Over the past few days he had been experiencing episodes of vertigo for which he was prescribed a medicine that he was taking for 2 days. Examination of the prescription revealed that he was taking tab. Dimenhydrinate 50 mg 3 times daily.

(a) Could there be any relationship between the anti-vertigo medication and the episode of acute urinary retention?
