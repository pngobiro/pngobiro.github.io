üß† Ethanol: Pharmacological Actions

Ethanol (Ethyl alcohol) is a CNS depressant. The apparent stimulation seen at lower doses is actually due to depression of inhibitory control mechanisms (disinhibition).

Systemic Actions

  • CNS: Depression of reticular activating system (RAS) and cortex. Impairs judgment, memory, and motor coordination. Mechanism involves facilitation of GABAA receptors and inhibition of NMDA (glutamate) receptors.
  • CVS: Cutaneous vasodilatation (flushing, heat loss) leading to fall in BP and tachycardia. Moderate doses increase HDL (cardioprotective?); high doses cause cardiomyopathy.
  • Body Temperature: Depresses temperature regulating centre; produces hypothermia in cold surroundings (despite feeling of warmth due to cutaneous vasodilatation).
  • GIT: Dilute alcohol ($<20\%$) stimulates gastric secretion (acid/pepsin). High concentrations ($>40\%$) cause congestion, gastritis, and vomiting.
  • Kidney: Diuresis due to inhibition of ADH (Vasopressin) secretion.

‚öôÔ∏è Pharmacokinetics & Metabolism

Absorption occurs from stomach and small intestine. Presence of food delays absorption. Metabolism follows Zero Order Kinetics (Saturation kinetics) at pharmacological levels.

Metabolic Pathway

$$ \text{Ethanol} \xrightarrow[\text{NAD}^+ \to \text{NADH}]{\text{Alcohol Dehydrogenase (ADH)}} \text{Acetaldehyde} \xrightarrow[\text{NAD}^+ \to \text{NADH}]{\text{Aldehyde Dehydrogenase (ALDH)}} \text{Acetate} \to \text{CO}_2 + \text{H}_2\text{O} $$
üí°

Rate Limiting Factor

The availability of NAD+ is the limiting factor in alcohol metabolism. The fixed rate of metabolism allows calculation of blood alcohol concentration (BAC) over time.

Microsomal Ethanol Oxidizing System (MEOS): Induced in chronic alcoholics, contributing to metabolic tolerance.

üåÄ Alcoholism & Withdrawal

Chronic alcoholism leads to psychological and physical dependence.

Withdrawal Syndrome

Symptoms appear 6-12 hours after cessation: Anxiety, tremors, insomnia, palpitations.

üö®

Delirium Tremens (DTs)

Severe withdrawal (2-3 days later): Hallucinations, confusion, disorientation, convulsions, cardiovascular collapse. Treatment: Benzodiazepines (Chlordiazepoxide or Diazepam) and Thiamine (Vitamin B1).

Nutritional Deficiencies

Wernicke's Encephalopathy: Ophthalmoplegia, ataxia, confusion due to Thiamine deficiency. Treat immediately with IV Thiamine to prevent permanent Korsakoff's psychosis.

05

üíä Treatment of Alcohol Dependence

1. Aversion Therapy: Disulfiram (Antabuse)

Mechanism: Irreversibly inhibits Aldehyde Dehydrogenase (ALDH).

Effect: If alcohol is consumed, Acetaldehyde accumulates causing distressing symptoms: Flushing, throbbing headache, nausea, vomiting, sweating, hypotension (Aldehyde Syndrome).

Use: As a deterrent to maintain abstinence. Patient must be motivated.

2. Anti-craving Drugs

  • Naltrexone: Opioid antagonist. Blocks the reinforcing (pleasurable) effects of alcohol mediated by endogenous opioids. Reduces craving and relapse.
  • Acamprosate: NMDA receptor antagonist and GABA agonist. Restores excitatory/inhibitory balance disrupted by chronic alcohol. Reduces craving.
  • Topiramate: Antiepileptic. Reduces craving.

‚ò†Ô∏è Methanol Poisoning

Methanol (Wood alcohol) is metabolized by the same enzymes as ethanol but produces toxic metabolites.

$$ \text{Methanol} \xrightarrow{\text{ADH}} \text{Formaldehyde} \xrightarrow{\text{ALDH}} \textbf{Formic Acid} $$

Toxicity: Formic acid causes severe Metabolic Acidosis and specific retinal toxicity leading to Blindness.

Treatment

  1. Fomepizole: Specific inhibitor of Alcohol Dehydrogenase (ADH). Prevents formation of toxic metabolites. Drug of choice.
  2. Ethanol: Competes with methanol for ADH (enzyme has higher affinity for ethanol). Saturates the enzyme preventing methanol metabolism. Used if Fomepizole is unavailable.
  3. Hemodialysis: Removes methanol and formate; corrects acidosis.
  4. Folinic acid/Folate: Enhances metabolism of formate.
  5. Sodium Bicarbonate: To correct acidosis.

Case 28.1

A chronic alcoholic patient undergoing treatment for amoebiasis complains of severe vomiting, flushing, headache, and sweating after attending a party where he consumed alcohol.

(a) Likely Cause: This is a Disulfiram-like reaction caused by the interaction between Alcohol and Metronidazole (the drug used for amoebiasis).

(b) Mechanism: Metronidazole inhibits the enzyme Aldehyde Dehydrogenase. When alcohol was consumed, its metabolism stopped at the stage of Acetaldehyde. Accumulation of acetaldehyde caused the distressing symptoms.

(c) Advice: The patient should be strictly warned not to consume alcohol while taking Metronidazole and for at least 3 days after stopping it.
