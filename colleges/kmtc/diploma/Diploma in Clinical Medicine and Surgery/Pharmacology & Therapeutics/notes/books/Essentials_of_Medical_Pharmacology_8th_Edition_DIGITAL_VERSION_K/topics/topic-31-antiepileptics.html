üß† Overview & Classification

Epilepsy is a group of disorders characterized by chronic recurrent, paroxysmal changes in neurological function caused by abnormalities in electrical activity of the brain. Antiepileptic drugs (AEDs) suppress seizures but do not cure the underlying pathology.

Classification

02

‚öôÔ∏è General Mechanisms of Action

AEDs inhibit the generation of repetitive discharge or spread of electrical activity through three main mechanisms:

1. Na+ Channel Prolongation

Prolongation of the inactivated state of voltage-sensitive neuronal $Na^+$ channels. This prevents high-frequency firing (Use-dependent block).

Drugs: Phenytoin, Carbamazepine, Valproate, Lamotrigine.

2. GABA Facilitation

Enhancement of GABA-mediated inhibitory transmission (opening of $Cl^-$ channels).

Drugs: Barbiturates, Benzodiazepines, Valproate, Vigabatrin (inhibits GABA-transaminase), Tiagabine (inhibits reuptake).

3. Ca2+ Channel Inhibition

Inhibition of T-type $Ca^{2+}$ currents in thalamic neurons (specific for Absence Seizures).

Drugs: Ethosuximide, Valproate.

03

üíä Phenytoin (Diphenylhydantoin)

A major antiepileptic drug, nonsedative in therapeutic range.

Pharmacokinetics

Absorption is slow. Highly protein bound (90%). Metabolism follows saturation kinetics (Zero Order) at therapeutic concentrations. Small increments in dose can lead to disproportionate increase in plasma concentration and toxicity.

Uses

  • Generalized Tonic-Clonic Seizures (GTCS).
  • Partial Seizures.
  • Status Epilepticus (Fosphenytoin is preferred).
  • Trigeminal Neuralgia (Carbamazepine is better).
  • Ineffective in Absence Seizures.
‚ò£Ô∏è

Adverse Effects

  • Gum Hypertrophy: Common in children/adolescents. Minimized by oral hygiene.
  • Hirsutism & Acne: Cosmetic problems in young girls.
  • Fetal Hydantoin Syndrome: Teratogenic (Cleft lip/palate, microcephaly, digit hypoplasia).
  • Megaloblastic Anemia: Decreases folate absorption.
  • Osteomalacia: Increases Vitamin D metabolism.
  • Neurotoxicity: Ataxia, nystagmus, diplopia, vertigo (cerebellar-vestibular effects).
04

‚ö° Carbamazepine

Chemically related to tricyclic antidepressants. Mechanism similar to Phenytoin (Na+ channel block).

Uses

  • Partial Seizures (Complex/Simple): Drug of Choice.
  • GTCS: First line drug.
  • Trigeminal Neuralgia: Drug of Choice.
  • Manic Depressive Illness (Bipolar): Acute mania and prophylaxis.

Adverse Effects

Drowsiness, vertigo, ataxia. Water retention (ADH-like action - hyponatremia). Enzyme Induction: Potent inducer of CYP450 (Autoinduction - induces its own metabolism).

Oxcarbazepine: Congener of carbamazepine. Less enzyme induction, less toxic.

05

üõ°Ô∏è Valproic Acid (Sodium Valproate)

A broad-spectrum AED with multiple mechanisms: Na+ channel block, T-type Ca2+ channel block, and increased GABA (inhibits GABA-transaminase).

Uses

  • Absence Seizures: Drug of choice (especially if co-existent GTCS).
  • Myoclonic & Atonic Seizures: Drug of Choice.
  • GTCS and Partial Seizures: Highly effective.
  • Bipolar Disorder & Migraine Prophylaxis.
‚ö†Ô∏è

Toxicity

  • Hepatotoxicity: Rare but fatal fulminant hepatitis (more in children < 2 yrs).
  • Teratogenicity: Neural tube defects (Spina bifida). Avoid in pregnancy.
  • Others: Weight gain, hair loss (alopecia), tremor, thrombocytopenia.
06

üéØ Ethosuximide

Mechanism: Selectively inhibits T-type $Ca^{2+}$ channels in thalamic neurons.

Use: Only for Absence Seizures (Petit mal). Not effective for GTCS.

Adverse Effects: GI distress, fatigue, headache.

üÜï Newer Antiepileptics

Drug Key Features Major Use
Lamotrigine Blocks Na+ and Ca2+ channels. Broad spectrum. SJS risk (rash). Safe in pregnancy relative to Valproate. Refractory cases, Absence, Bipolar.
Levetiracetam Binds to synaptic vesicle protein SV2A. Very safe, no drug interactions. Add-on for Partial/GTCS.
Topiramate Multiple mechanisms (Na+ block, GABA potentiation, AMPA antagonist). Causes cognitive slowing ("Dopamax"). Refractory seizures, Migraine.
Gabapentin / Pregabalin GABA analogues but bind to $\alpha_2\delta$ subunit of Ca2+ channels. Neuropathic pain (Mainly).
Vigabatrin Irreversible GABA-transaminase inhibitor. Visual field defects (Tunnel vision). Infantile spasms.
08

‚úÖ Choice of Drug

  • Partial Seizures: Carbamazepine, Oxcarbazepine, Phenytoin, Lamotrigine, Levetiracetam.
  • GTCS: Valproate, Carbamazepine, Lamotrigine, Phenytoin.
  • Absence Seizures: Valproate, Ethosuximide, Lamotrigine. (Avoid Phenytoin/Carbamazepine - may worsen).
  • Myoclonic Seizures: Valproate, Levetiracetam, Clonazepam.
  • Febrile Seizures: Rectal Diazepam (intermittent).
09

üö® Status Epilepticus Management

Continuous seizure activity for > 5 min. A medical emergency.

  1. General measures: Airway, Oxygen, Glucose (if hypoglycemic).
  2. First Line: Lorazepam (0.1 mg/kg IV) or Diazepam (10 mg IV). Lorazepam is preferred due to longer duration in brain.
  3. Second Line: Fosphenytoin or Phenytoin IV infusion (loading dose). Prevents recurrence.
  4. Refractory: Phenobarbitone / Valproate / Levetiracetam IV.
  5. General Anaesthesia: Midazolam / Propofol / Thiopentone with intubation.

ü§î Problem Directed Study

üìù

Case 30.1

A 12-year-old girl suffering from GTCS was treated with Phenytoin 50 mg twice daily. Seizures were controlled initially but recurred after 2 months. The dose was increased to 100 mg twice daily. Within 10 days she developed giddiness, ataxia, headache, and nystagmus.

(a) Cause of Toxicity: Phenytoin exhibits saturation kinetics (Zero Order). At low doses, elimination is first order. As the enzymes get saturated at therapeutic levels, a small increase in dose (from 100 to 200 mg/day) leads to a disproportionately large increase in plasma concentration, precipitating toxicity (cerebellar-vestibular signs).

(b) Management: Withhold the drug temporarily until symptoms subside. Restart at a slightly lower dose or measure plasma levels to adjust. Phenytoin requires careful dose titration in small increments.
