General Anaesthetics

üß† Overview & Mechanism

General Anaesthetics (GA) are drugs which produce reversible loss of all sensation and consciousness. The cardinal features of general anaesthesia are:

  • Loss of consciousness (Hypnosis).
  • Loss of sensation (Analgesia).
  • Muscle relaxation.
  • Abolition of reflexes.
  • Amnesia.

Mechanism of Action

The unitary theory (Lipid theory) has been replaced by the protein/receptor theory. GAs act on specific ion channels.

GABAA Potentiation

Most GAs (Barbiturates, Benzodiazepines, Propofol, Etomidate, Halogenated volatiles) facilitate GABA-mediated Cl- influx, causing hyperpolarization and inhibition.

NMDA Inhibition

Ketamine and Nitrous Oxide antagonize the excitatory NMDA glutamate receptor (block cation channel), preventing neuronal excitation.

üìä Stages of Anaesthesia

Described by Guedel (using Ether), these are less distinct with modern fast-acting drugs.

  1. Stage I (Analgesia): From start of induction to loss of consciousness. Analgesia is present.
  2. Stage II (Delirium/Excitement): Loss of consciousness to irregular respiration. Struggling, shouting, sympathetic discharge (pupils dilate, BP rises). Breath holding may occur. Aim is to cross this stage rapidly.
  3. Stage III (Surgical Anaesthesia): Regular respiration to cessation of spontaneous breathing. Divided into 4 planes.
    • Plane 1: Rolling eyeballs.
    • Plane 2: Loss of corneal/laryngeal reflexes (Surgery done here).
    • Plane 3: Pupil dilation, light reflex lost. Muscle relaxation complete.
    • Plane 4: Intercostal paralysis, diaphragmatic breathing.
  4. Stage IV (Medullary Paralysis): Respiratory and vasomotor collapse. Death.

üìâ Pharmacokinetics of Inhalational Agents

1. Minimal Alveolar Concentration (MAC)

It is the concentration of the anaesthetic in the alveoli required to prevent movement in 50% of subjects in response to painful stimulus (skin incision). It is a measure of Potency.

üí°

MAC Relationship

Lower MAC = Higher Potency (High Lipid Solubility).

  • Halothane MAC: 0.75% (Potent)
  • Nitrous Oxide MAC: 105% (Weak)

2. Blood:Gas Partition Coefficient ($\lambda$)

It defines the solubility of the gas in blood. It determines the Rate of Induction and Recovery.

  • Lower Solubility (Low $\lambda$): Blood saturates quickly $\rightarrow$ alveolar partial pressure rises fast $\rightarrow$ rapid equilibrium with brain $\rightarrow$ Rapid Induction & Recovery. (e.g., Desflurane, Sevoflurane, N2O).
  • Higher Solubility (High $\lambda$): Blood acts as a reservoir ("sponge") $\rightarrow$ slow rise in alveolar pressure $\rightarrow$ Slow Induction & Recovery. (e.g., Ether, Halothane).

3. Second Gas Effect

Rapid uptake of a large volume of a gas (like N2O) from alveoli concentrates the remaining gases (e.g., Halothane/Oxygen), speeding up their uptake.

4. Diffusion Hypoxia

On discontinuing N2O, it rushes from blood to alveoli (reverse of uptake), diluting O2. Prevented by giving 100% O2 for a few minutes after stopping N2O.

üí® Inhalational Anaesthetics

Drug Features Adverse Effects
Nitrous Oxide (N2O) "Laughing gas". Good analgesic, poor anaesthetic (MAC > 100%). Used as carrier gas (50-70%) to reduce dose of volatile agents. Rapid induction/recovery. Diffusional hypoxia, expands air filled cavities (pneumothorax), bone marrow depression (long term).
Halothane Potent, non-irritant, pleasant (good for children). Potent bronchodilator. Relaxes uterus. Sensitizes heart to catecholamines (arrhythmias), Hepatotoxicity (Halothane hepatitis), Malignant Hyperthermia.
Isoflurane Widely used for maintenance. Muscle relaxant. Cardiac stable. Pungent (cough), coronary steal (rare).
Desflurane Lowest blood:gas coeff (0.42). Fastest induction/recovery. Ideal for day care. Very pungent (induces cough/laryngospasm - not for induction), Tachycardia.
Sevoflurane Rapid acting, pleasant smelling. Agent of choice for inhalation induction (esp. children). Nephrotoxicity (Compound A interaction with soda lime - theoretical).

üíâ Intravenous Anaesthetics

Inducing Agents

1. Thiopentone Sodium

Ultra-short acting barbiturate. Alkaline solution (irritant if extravasated). Unconsciousness in 15-20 sec (one arm-brain circulation time).

Action terminated by Redistribution from brain to muscle and fat (not metabolism). Accumulates on repeated doses (hangover).

Drawbacks: Poor analgesia, respiratory depression, cardiovascular collapse in hypovolemia, precipitates porphyria.

2. Propofol

"Milk of Amnesia" (oil emulsion). Most popular IV anaesthetic. Rapid onset and rapid recovery (metabolic clearance > hepatic flow). Less hangover ("Clear headed" recovery). Antiemetic property.

Uses: Induction, maintenance (TIVA - Total Intravenous Anaesthesia), Day care surgery.

Adverse Effects: Pain on injection, hypotension (more than thiopentone), Propofol infusion syndrome (rare).

3. Etomidate

Cardiovascular stability is the hallmark. Drug of choice for induction in patients with coronary artery disease or hypovolemia/shock.

Disadvantage: Pain on injection, myoclonus, Adrenocortical suppression (inhibits steroid synthesis).

06

üåÄ Ketamine: Dissociative Anaesthesia

Ketamine is a derivative of phencyclidine. It produces Dissociative Anaesthesia: patient appears awake (eyes open) but is unconscious and does not feel pain. There is dissociation between thalamocortical and limbic systems.

Mechanism

Blocks NMDA (N-methyl-D-aspartate) glutamate receptors.

Unique Features

  • CVS: Stimulates heart (tachycardia, raised BP) via sympathetic stimulation. (Good for shock).
  • Respiration: Potent Bronchodilator. Reflexes intact. (Good for asthmatics).
  • Analgesia: Profound somatic analgesia.

Emergence Delirium

Hallucinations, vivid dreams, and delirium occur during recovery (10-30%). Reduced by pre-medication with Benzodiazepines (Diazepam/Midazolam).

Uses: Burn dressing, short operations, asthmatics, hypovolemic patients, children.

üíä Preanesthetic Medication

Drugs given prior to anaesthesia to make it safer and more pleasant.

Goal Drug Class Examples
Relieve Anxiety/Sedation Benzodiazepines Diazepam, Midazolam, Lorazepam
Relieve Pain Opioids Morphine, Pethidine, Fentanyl
Reduce Secretions/Vagal Tone Anticholinergics Atropine, Glycopyrrolate
Prevent Vomiting Antiemetics Metoclopramide, Ondansetron
Reduce Gastric Acidity (Aspiration prophylaxis) H2 Blockers / PPIs Ranitidine, Pantoprazole

üìù

Case 27.1

A 5-year-old child requires a short surgical procedure for burn dressing. He is very apprehensive. He is also a known asthmatic.

(a) Anaesthetic Choice: Ketamine.

(b) Reasoning:

  • Asthma: Ketamine is a potent bronchodilator, making it safe for asthmatics (unlike Thiopentone which can precipitate bronchospasm).
  • Procedure: Burn dressing is painful but short. Ketamine provides profound somatic analgesia (Dissociative anaesthesia) which is ideal.
  • Route: Can be given intramuscularly (IM) if IV access is difficult in a child.

(c) Precaution: To prevent emergence phenomena (hallucinations), a benzodiazepine like Midazolam should be given.
