Components of ANS

  • Autonomic afferents: Most visceral nerves are mixed nerves. Cell bodies located in dorsal root ganglia or cranial nerve sensory ganglia. Mediate visceral pain and reflexes.
  • Central autonomic connections: Hypothalamus is the highest seat regulating autonomic functions (Posterior/Lateral nuclei $\rightarrow$ Sympathetic; Anterior/Medial nuclei $\rightarrow$ Parasympathetic).
  • Autonomic efferents: Anatomically divided into Sympathetic and Parasympathetic.

Physiological Antagonism?

While many organs receive both sympathetic and parasympathetic innervation which are functionally antagonistic, they are not merely check-and-balance systems. Some organs receive only one type of innervation:

  • Sympathetic only: Most blood vessels, spleen, sweat glands, hair follicles.
  • Parasympathetic only: Ciliary muscle, bronchial smooth muscle, gastric and pancreatic glands.

üå≠ Enteric Nervous System

Located in the gut wall, consisting of:

  • Submucosal (Meissner's) plexus
  • Myenteric (Auerbach's) plexus

It receives inputs from both sympathetic and parasympathetic divisions but functions independently to integrate bowel movements and regulate secretion/absorption.

‚ö° Neurohumoral Transmission

Neurohumoral transmission implies that nerves transmit their message across synapses and neuroeffector junctions by the release of humoral (chemical) messengers. Otto Loewi (1921) provided direct proof using frog hearts ("Vagusstoff" = Acetylcholine).

Criteria for a Neurotransmitter

  1. 1 Present in the presynaptic neurone.
  2. 2 Released in the medium following nerve stimulation.
  3. 3 Application should produce identical responses to nerve stimulation.
  4. 4 Effects antagonized or potentiated by specific substances.

Steps in Transmission

I. Impulse conduction

Resting membrane potential (-70 mV). Stimulation causes sudden increase in $\mathrm{Na}^{+}$ conductance $\rightarrow$ depolarization and overshoot (inside positive). $\mathrm{K}^{+}$ efflux causes repolarization. Ionic distribution normalized by $\mathrm{Na}^{+}-\mathrm{K}^{+}$ pump. Tetrodotoxin blocks nerve conduction.

II. Transmitter release

Transmitter stored in synaptic vesicles. Nerve impulse promotes fusion of vesicular and axonal membranes through $\mathrm{Ca}^{2+}$ entry. Contents extruded by exocytosis. Release can be modulated by presynaptic receptors (e.g., $\alpha_2$ inhibits NA release).

III. Transmitter action on postjunctional membrane

Transmitter combines with receptors causing:

  • EPSP (Excitatory Postsynaptic Potential): Increase in cation permeability ($\mathrm{Na}^{+}, \mathrm{Ca}^{2+}$) $\rightarrow$ Depolarization.
  • IPSP (Inhibitory Postsynaptic Potential): Increase in anion ($\mathrm{Cl}^{-}$) permeability or $\mathrm{K}^{+}$ efflux $\rightarrow$ Hyperpolarization.

IV. Postjunctional activity

Suprathreshold EPSP generates propagated action potential $\rightarrow$ nerve impulse / muscle contraction / secretion.

V. Termination of transmitter action

  • Local degradation: e.g., ACh by cholinesterase.
  • Active reuptake: e.g., NA taken back into neurone (Uptake-1) by NET or into tissues (Uptake-2).
  • Diffusion: Minor mechanism.

ü§ù Cotransmission

Most peripheral and central neurones release more than one active substance.

Common Cotransmitters

  • With ACh: Vasoactive Intestinal Peptide (VIP), ATP, Nitric Oxide (NO).
  • With NA: ATP, Neuropeptide Y (NPY).

The cotransmitter may regulate presynaptic release of the primary transmitter, modify postsynaptic sensitivity, serve as an alternative transmitter, or exert trophic influences.

NANC (Nonadrenergic, noncholinergic) transmission: Observed in gut, vas deferens, urinary tract, etc., where nerve stimulation evokes responses even in presence of adrenergic and cholinergic blockade (mediated by ATP, NO, peptides).
