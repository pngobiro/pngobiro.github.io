üß¨ Thyroid Hormones ($T_3$ & $T_4$)

The thyroid gland secretes 3 hormones:

  • Thyroxine ($T_4$): Major circulating hormone. Prohormone.
  • Triiodothyronine ($T_3$): Active hormone (5 times more potent than $T_4$).
  • Calcitonin: Regulates calcium metabolism (secreted by parafollicular 'C' cells).

$T_4$ (Thyroxine)

  • Secreted in larger amounts (60-90 $\mu$g/day).
  • Tightly bound to plasma proteins (TBG).
  • Long half-life (6-7 days).
  • Converted to $T_3$ in periphery.

$T_3$ (Triiodothyronine)

  • Secreted in small amounts (10-30 $\mu$g/day); mostly derived from peripheral deiodination of $T_4$.
  • Less protein bound.
  • Short half-life (1-2 days).
  • Binds avidly to nuclear receptors.

Physiological Actions

Thyroid hormones affect the function of practically every body cell.

  • Growth & Development: Essential for normal growth and nervous system development (myelination). Deficiency causes cretinism (mental retardation).
  • Calorigenesis: Increase BMR and heat production (uncoupling of oxidative phosphorylation at high doses).
  • Metabolism:
    • Carbohydrate: Hyperglycaemic (increases absorption, glycogenolysis).
    • Lipid: Lipolysis, lowers cholesterol (converts to bile acids).
    • Protein: Catabolic in excess (muscle wasting).
  • CVS: Hyperdynamic state. Tachycardia, increased cardiac output, bounding pulse. Sensitizes heart to catecholamines.
  • Nervous System: Anxious, nervous, tremors (hyperthyroidism); Sluggish, slow (hypothyroidism).

‚öóÔ∏è Synthesis & Regulation

Steps in Synthesis

  1. Iodide Trapping: Active transport of $I^-$ into thyroid by $Na^+$-Iodide Symporter (NIS). Stimulated by TSH.
  2. Oxidation: $I^-$ oxidized to iodine/hypoiodite by Thyroid Peroxidase.
  3. Organification: Iodine combines with tyrosine residues of thyroglobulin to form MIT and DIT.
  4. Coupling:
    • MIT + DIT $\rightarrow$ $T_3$
    • DIT + DIT $\rightarrow$ $T_4$
  5. Storage & Release: Stored as colloid. Proteolysis releases $T_3/T_4$ into blood. Stimulated by TSH.
  6. Peripheral Conversion: $T_4$ converted to $T_3$ by deiodinases in liver/kidney/tissues. Inhibited by Propylthiouracil, Propranolol, Glucocorticoids.
03

üíä Treatment of Hypothyroidism

Levothyroxine ($L-T_4$) is the drug of choice for replacement therapy. It provides a stable pool of prohormone which is converted to active $T_3$ physiologically.

Uses

  • Cretinism: Start early (8-12 $\mu$g/kg) to prevent permanent mental retardation.
  • Myxoedema (Adult Hypothyroidism): Start with low dose (50 $\mu$g) in elderly/cardiac patients to avoid angina/arrhythmia. Titrate every 4-6 weeks based on TSH.
  • Myxoedema Coma: Emergency. I.V. $T_4$ (200-500 $\mu$g) loading dose, followed by daily maintenance. I.V. Hydrocortisone to cover adrenal insufficiency. Warming.

üõë Thyroid Inhibitors

Drugs used to lower the functional capacity of the hyperactive thyroid gland (Thyrotoxicosis/Graves' disease).

Class Drugs Mechanism
Thioamides Propylthiouracil (PTU), Carbimazole, Methimazole Inhibit hormone synthesis (Peroxidase). PTU also inhibits peripheral conversion.
Ionic Inhibitors Perchlorate, Thiocyanate Inhibit Iodide Trapping (NIS). Toxic, rarely used.
Iodine/Iodides Lugol's Iodine, Ipodate Inhibit hormone release (Thyroid constipation).
Radioactive Iodine $^{131}I$ Destroys thyroid follicles.
05

‚öîÔ∏è Thioamides (Antithyroid Drugs)

Propylthiouracil (PTU) and Carbimazole/Methimazole.

Mechanism of Action

  1. Inhibit Thyroid Peroxidase: Prevent oxidation of iodide and iodination of tyrosine residues (organification).
  2. Inhibit Coupling of iodotyrosines ($MIT+DIT \neq T_3$).
  3. PTU only: Inhibits peripheral conversion of $T_4$ to $T_3$ (Type 1 deiodinase). Preferred in Thyroid Storm.

Note: They do not inhibit the release of pre-formed hormone, so there is a lag period before clinical response is seen (until stores are depleted).

Comparison

Feature Propylthiouracil (PTU) Carbimazole
Potency Lower High
Duration Short ($t_{1/2}$ 1-2 hr). Multiple doses. Longer ($t_{1/2}$ 6-10 hr). Single dose possible.
Peripheral action Inhibits $T_4 \rightarrow T_3$ No
Placental transfer Less (highly protein bound) More
Preferred in Pregnancy (1st trimester), Thyroid storm Routine therapy

Adverse Effects

  • Agranulocytosis: Rare but serious (1 in 500-1000). Reversible if stopped.
  • Skin Rashes: Common.
  • Liver Damage: Severe hepatitis with PTU (Black box warning). Cholestatic jaundice with Methimazole.
  • Goiter: Due to overtreatment (increased TSH).
06

üßÇ Iodine and Iodides

Lugol's Solution (5% Iodine + 10% KI). Fastest acting thyroid inhibitor.

Actions

  1. Thyroid Constipation: Inhibits release of thyroid hormones (inhibit proteolysis of thyroglobulin).
  2. Wolff-Chaikoff Effect: Excess iodide transiently inhibits organification.
  3. Gland involution: Makes the gland firm and less vascular.

Uses

  • Preoperative Preparation: For 10 days before thyroidectomy in Graves' disease (decreases vascularity).
  • Thyroid Storm: To rapidly stop hormone release.
  • Prophylaxis: Endemic goiter (Iodized salt).

Disadvantage: "Thyroid Escape" - effect is short-lived (10-15 days). Hyperthyroidism may return with greater vengeance.

07

‚ò¢Ô∏è Radioactive Iodine ($^{131}I$)

Concentrated by the thyroid gland. Emits $\beta$ particles which penetrate short distance (0.5-2 mm) and destroy thyroid follicles from within.

Uses

  • Hyperthyroidism (Graves' disease/Toxic nodular goiter): Treatment of choice after 25 years of age. Cures permanently.
  • Thyroid Carcinoma: Metastatic papillary/follicular ca (after thyroidectomy).

Advantages vs Disadvantages

Advantages

  • Simple, oral, inexpensive.
  • No surgical risk/scar.
  • Permanent cure.

Disadvantages

  • Hypothyroidism: High incidence delayed permanent hypothyroidism.
  • Long latent period (months).
  • Contraindicated in Pregnancy (fetal cretinism) and childhood.
08

üå©Ô∏è Thyroid Storm (Thyrotoxic Crisis)

An emergency due to decompensated hyperthyroidism. Treatment includes:

  1. Propranolol: Drug of choice for symptomatic relief. Also inhibits peripheral $T_4 \rightarrow T_3$ at high doses.
  2. Propylthiouracil (PTU): High dose (200-300 mg 6 hrly). Blocks synthesis and peripheral conversion.
  3. Iodides (Lugol's / Ipodate): To block hormone release immediately.
  4. Hydrocortisone: 100 mg i.v. To treat shock and inhibit peripheral conversion.
  5. Supportive: Fluids, cooling, treat precipitating cause.

ü§î Problem Directed Study

üìù

Case 18.1

A 20-year girl with Graves' disease was treated with Carbimazole 5 mg 2 tab TDS. Symptoms controlled in 3 months. Maintained on 5 mg BD. After one year, neck swelling reappeared, weight increased, felt dull/sleepy. TSH raised ($12 \mu U/ml$), Free $T_4$ low-normal.

(a) Initial delayed response: Antithyroid drugs (Carbimazole) inhibit hormone synthesis but do not stop the release of pre-formed hormone stored in the colloid. Clinical response appears only after stores are depleted (1-2 weeks). To afford rapid symptomatic relief, a $\beta$-blocker (Propranolol) should be given initially.

(b) Reappearance of swelling (Goiter): The patient has developed drug-induced hypothyroidism due to overtreatment (continued maintenance dose). Low $T_4$ caused loss of feedback inhibition $\rightarrow$ increased TSH $\rightarrow$ stimulation of thyroid gland growth (Goiter).
Measure: Reduce the dose of Carbimazole or stop it temporarily to restore euthyroidism.
