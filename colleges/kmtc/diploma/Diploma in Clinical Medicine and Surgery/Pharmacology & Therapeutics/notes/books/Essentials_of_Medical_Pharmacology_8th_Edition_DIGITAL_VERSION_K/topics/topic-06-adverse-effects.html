üìñ Definitions & Classification

All drugs are capable of producing adverse effects, and whenever a drug is given a risk is taken. The magnitude of risk has to be considered along with the magnitude of expected therapeutic benefit (Risk-Benefit Ratio).

‚ÑπÔ∏è

Key Definitions

  • Adverse Drug Reaction (ADR): Any noxious change which is suspected to be due to a drug, occurs at doses normally used in man, requires treatment or decrease in dose or indicates caution in the future use of the same drug.
  • Adverse Drug Event (ADE): Any untoward medical occurrence that may present during treatment with a medicine, but which does not necessarily have a causal relationship with the treatment.

Classification

Predictable (Type A) Unpredictable (Type B)
Based on pharmacological properties. Based on peculiarities of patient (genetics, immunology).
Augmented, qualitatively normal response. Bizarre, qualitatively abnormal response.
More common, dose related. Less common, non-dose related.
Includes side effects, toxic effects. Includes allergy, idiosyncrasy.

Severity Grading

  • Minor: No therapy, antidote or prolongation of hospitalization required.
  • Moderate: Requires change in drug therapy, specific treatment or prolongs hospital stay by at least one day.
  • Severe: Potentially life-threatening, causes permanent damage or requires intensive medical treatment.
  • Lethal: Directly or indirectly contributes to death of the patient.

üëÅÔ∏è Pharmacovigilance

Pharmacovigilance is the science and activities relating to the detection, assessment, understanding and prevention of adverse effects or any other drug related problems.

Causality Assessment

When an adverse event occurs, causality is assessed based on:

  • Temporal relationship: Time sequence.
  • Previous knowledge: Known reaction to the drug.
  • Dechallenge: Event subsides on stopping drug.
  • Rechallenge: Event reappears on re-administration (often not done).

Grading: Definite, Probable, Possible, Doubtful.

Prevention of Adverse Effects

  1. Avoid inappropriate use.
  2. Use appropriate dose, route and frequency.
  3. Elicit history of drug reactions/allergies.
  4. Rule out drug interactions.
  5. Adopt correct administration technique.
  6. Carry out laboratory monitoring.

üóÇÔ∏è Categories of Adverse Effects

1. Side Effects

Unwanted but often unavoidable pharmacodynamic effects that occur at therapeutic doses. Can be predicted.

Examples: Dry mouth with atropine, sedation with antihistamines.

2. Secondary Effects

Indirect consequences of a primary action of the drug.

Examples: Superinfections with tetracyclines, activation of latent TB with corticosteroids.

3. Intolerance

Appearance of characteristic toxic effects at therapeutic doses due to low threshold of the individual.

Example: Vomiting with one tablet of chloroquine.

4. Idiosyncrasy

Genetically determined abnormal reactivity. The drug interacts with some unique feature of the individual.

Examples: Excitement with barbiturates, aplastic anaemia with chloramphenicol.

‚ò†Ô∏è Toxic Effects & Poisoning

Toxic effects are the result of excessive pharmacological action of the drug due to overdosage or prolonged use. They are dose related and predictable.

Poisoning

Poison is a 'substance which endangers life by severely affecting one or more vital functions'. General management includes:

  1. 1 Resuscitation and maintenance of vital functions: Patent airway, artificial respiration, maintenance of BP/heart beat, prevention of seizures, maintenance of body temperature and blood sugar.
  2. 2 Termination of exposure (decontamination): Fresh air, washing skin/eyes. Induction of emesis or gastric lavage is useful only if performed within 2 hours of ingestion and is contraindicated in comatose patients or corrosive poisoning.
  3. 3 Prevention of absorption: Activated charcoal ($1\text{ g/kg}$) adsorbs many chemicals. Ineffective for strong acids/alkalies, metallic salts, iodine, cyanide, alcohol.
  4. 4 Hastening elimination: Alkalinization of urine (for acidic drugs like barbiturates, aspirin). Haemodialysis is effective for phenobarbitone, lithium, salicylate, etc.

ü§ß Drug Allergy (Hypersensitivity)

Immunologically mediated reaction producing stereotype symptoms unrelated to the pharmacodynamic profile. Requires prior sensitization. Occurs in a small proportion of population.

Type Mechanism Manifestations
Type I (Anaphylactic) IgE mediated. Release of histamine, leukotrienes. Urticaria, itching, angioedema, bronchospasm, anaphylactic shock. Immediate.
Type II (Cytolytic) IgG/IgM mediated. Drug + tissue component act as antigen. Thrombocytopenia, agranulocytosis, haemolysis, organ damage.
Type III (Retarded/Arthus) Circulating antibodies (IgG). AG:AB complexes precipitate in tissues. Serum sickness (fever, arthralgia), Stevens-Johnson syndrome. Develops in 3-4 days.
Type IV (Delayed) Cell mediated (T-lymphocytes). Contact dermatitis, rashes, fever. Takes 2-3 days to develop.

Treatment of Drug Allergy

  • Immediate cessation of the drug.
  • Antihistamines ($H_1$): For mild reactions (itching, urticaria).
  • Adrenaline (0.5 mg i.m.): The only life saving measure in anaphylactic shock and laryngeal angioedema.
  • Glucocorticoids (i.v.): For severe/prolonged reactions. Effective in Type II, III and IV reactions.

Photosensitivity

Phototoxic

Drug accumulates in skin, absorbs light, causes local tissue damage (sunburn-like). Dose related.
Drugs: Tetracyclines, nalidixic acid.

Photoallergic

Cell mediated immune response. Papular/eczematous contact dermatitis. Non-dose related.
Drugs: Sulfonamides, chloroquine.

üíä Dependence & Addiction

  • Drug Dependence: Altered physiological state produced by repeated administration necessitating continued presence of drug to maintain equilibrium. Withdrawal results in abstinence syndrome. (Physical dependence). Drugs: Opioids, barbiturates, alcohol.
  • Drug Addiction: Pattern of compulsive drug use characterized by overwhelming involvement with the use of a drug. Procuring and using the drug takes precedence over other activities. (Psychological dependence).
  • Drug Abuse: Use of a drug by self medication in a manner and amount that deviates from approved medical and social patterns.

Drug Withdrawal Reactions

Sudden interruption of therapy with certain drugs results in worsening of the clinical condition (rebound).

  • Corticosteroids: Acute adrenal insufficiency.
  • Clonidine: Severe hypertension, restlessness.
  • $\beta$-blockers: Worsening of angina, MI.

ü§∞ Teratogenicity & Other Effects

Capacity of a drug to cause foetal abnormalities. The thalidomide disaster (phocomelia) highlighted this. Drugs can affect the foetus at 3 stages: fertilization/implantation, organogenesis (18-55 days - most vulnerable), and growth/development.

Drug Abnormality
Thalidomide Phocomelia, multiple defects
Anticancer drugs (methotrexate) Cleft palate, hydrocephalus, foetal death
Androgens/Progestins Virilization of female foetus
Tetracyclines Discoloured/deformed teeth, retarded bone growth
Warfarin Depressed nose, eye/hand defects
Phenytoin Cleft lip/palate, microcephaly
ACE inhibitors Hypoplasia of organs, growth retardation
Isotretinoin Craniofacial, heart and CNS defects

Mutagenicity and Carcinogenicity

Capacity to cause genetic defects and cancer respectively. Usually due to covalent interaction with DNA. Drugs: Anticancer drugs, radioisotopes, estrogens.

Drug induced diseases (Iatrogenic)

Functional disturbances caused by drugs which persist even after withdrawal.

  • Peptic ulcer by NSAIDs.
  • Parkinsonism by phenothiazines.
  • Hepatitis by isoniazid.

Case Study 6.1

A 40-year-man weighing 60 kg suffering from chronic cough with expectoration and fever was diagnosed to have cavitary pulmonary tuberculosis. He was put on the standard 1st line antitubercular regimen consisting of isoniazid (H) + rifampin (R) + pyrazinamide (Z) + ethambutol (E). His condition improved, but in the 4th week he developed jaundice with enlarged tender liver and rise in serum bilirubin as well as serum transaminase levels. He was suspected to have developed antitubercular drug induced hepatitis.

(a) Should his antitubercular treatment be stopped or continued?
(b) How would you proceed to confirm and identify the causative drug, and then select the alternative regimen?
