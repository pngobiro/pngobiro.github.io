üõë Antirheumatoid Drugs

These are drugs which (except corticosteroids), can suppress the rheumatoid process, bring about a remission and retard disease progression, but do not have nonspecific antiinflammatory or analgesic action. They are used in rheumatoid arthritis (RA) in addition to NSAIDs, and are also referred to as disease modifying antirheumatic drugs (DMARDs) or slow acting antirheumatic drugs (SAARDs).

Rheumatoid arthritis (RA) is an autoimmune disease in which there is joint inflammation, synovial proliferation and destruction of articular cartilage. Immune complexes composed of IgM activate complement and release cytokines (mainly TNF $\alpha$ and IL-1) which are chemotactic for neutrophils.

üéØ

Goals of Drug Therapy

  • Ameliorate pain, swelling and joint stiffness.
  • Prevent articular cartilage damage and bony erosions.
  • Preserve joint function and prevent deformity.
02

üíä Nonbiological Drugs

Methotrexate (Mtx)

This dihydrofolate reductase inhibitor has prominent immunosuppressant and antiinflammatory property. Beneficial effects in RA are probably related to inhibition of cytokine production, chemotaxis and cell-mediated immune reaction.

  • Status: Mtx is now the DMARD of first choice and the standard treatment for most patients.
  • Dose: Oral low-dose (7.5‚Äì15 mg) weekly. Onset of symptom relief is relatively rapid (3‚Äì6 weeks).
  • Adverse Effects: Oral ulceration, g.i. upset, dose dependent progressive liver damage (cirrhosis) with prolonged therapy, increased chest infection. Contraindicated in pregnancy.

Sulfasalazine

It is a compound of sulfapyridine and 5-amino salicylic acid (5-ASA). In RA, sulfapyridine split off in the colon and absorbed systemically appears to be the active moiety. Efficacy is modest; used as a second line drug or combined with Mtx.

Hydroxychloroquine / Chloroquine

These antimalarial drugs induce remission in upto 50% patients but take 3‚Äì6 months. Their advantage is relatively low toxicity, but efficacy is also low; bony erosions are not prevented. Toxicity: Retinal damage and corneal opacity are the most disturbing; eye checkup is essential.

Leflunomide

Inhibits dihydro-orotate dehydrogenase and pyrimidine synthesis in actively dividing cells. Efficacy comparable to Mtx. Active metabolite has a very long $t_{1/2}$ (2‚Äì3 weeks). Hepatotoxic potential.

Tofacitinib

A new synthetic JAK-3 and JAK-1 kinase inhibitor for severe RA not responding to Mtx. Interferes with JAK-STAT signaling.

03

üß¨ Biological Agents

Recombinant proteins/monoclonal antibodies that bind to and inhibit cytokines, especially TNF $\alpha$ or IL-1. Used as reserve drugs for severe refractory disease.

TNF $\alpha$ Inhibitors

TNF $\alpha$ plays a key role in the inflammatory cascade. Inhibitors suppress macrophage and T-cell function.

Drug Nature Administration
Etanercept Recombinant fusion protein (TNF-receptor + Fc of IgG) s.c. weekly
Infliximab Chimeral monoclonal antibody i.v. infusion every 4-8 weeks
Adalimumab Recombinant human monoclonal antibody s.c. every 2 weeks

Adverse Effects: Increased susceptibility to opportunistic infections (tuberculosis, pneumocystis pneumonia).

Other Agents

  • Abatacept: Fusion protein inhibiting T-cell costimulation (binds CD80/86).
  • Rituximab: Monoclonal antibody depleting B-lymphocytes.
  • Anakinra: Recombinant IL-1 receptor antagonist.
04

‚ö° Corticosteroids in RA

Glucocorticoids have potent immunosuppressant and antiinflammatory activity. Symptomatic relief is prompt and marked, but they do not arrest the rheumatoid process.

Strategies:

  • Low doses (5‚Äì7.5 mg prednisolone) to supplement NSAIDs ("bridge therapy").
  • High doses for short periods in severe systemic manifestations (vasculitis).
  • Intraarticular injection for single joint involvement.

üî• Drugs for Acute Gout

Gout is a metabolic disorder characterized by hyperuricaemia (plasma urate $> 6$ mg/dl) and precipitation of urate crystals in joints.

1. NSAIDs

Strong antiinflammatory drugs like Naproxen, Piroxicam, Diclofenac, Indomethacin or Etoricoxib are given in high doses. They are quite effective in terminating the attack (12‚Äì24 hours). Preferred over colchicine due to better tolerability.

2. Colchicine

Alkaloid from Colchicum autumnale. Neither analgesic nor antiinflammatory generally, but specifically suppresses gouty inflammation.

üîÑ

Mechanism

Binds to fibrillar protein tubulin and inhibits granulocyte migration into the inflamed joint. Also inhibits release of chemotactic factors and glycoprotein.

Toxicity: Nausea, vomiting, watery/bloody diarrhoea (dose limiting). Risk of kidney damage and CNS depression in overdose.

3. Corticosteroids

Intraarticular injection of soluble steroid (e.g., triamcinolone) or systemic steroids for patients not tolerating NSAIDs/colchicine.

06

üöΩ Chronic Gout: Uricosurics

Used to treat chronic gout/hyperuricaemia by increasing uric acid excretion.

Probenecid

A highly lipid-soluble organic acid. Competitively blocks active transport of organic acids by OATP in renal tubules.

  • Mechanism: Inhibits active reabsorption of uric acid (which is largely reabsorbed). Net effect: increased excretion.
  • Interactions: Inhibits excretion of penicillin, cephalosporins, methotrexate. Aspirin blocks uricosuric action.
  • Contraindication: Renal insufficiency, renal stones.
07

üõë Chronic Gout: Synthesis Inhibitors

Allopurinol

Hypoxanthine analogue. Both allopurinol and its metabolite alloxanthine are inhibitors of Xanthine Oxidase, the enzyme responsible for uric acid synthesis.

  • Action: Lowers plasma uric acid; increases hypoxanthine/xanthine (which are more soluble). Tophi disappear, nephropathy halted.
  • Drug of Choice: For chronic gout (overproducers and underexcretors).
  • Interaction: Inhibits degradation of 6-mercaptopurine and azathioprine (doses must be reduced to 1/3).
  • Adverse Effects: Hypersensitivity (rashes, fever), Stevens-Johnson syndrome (rare).
  • Caution: Do not start during acute attack. Cover with NSAIDs/colchicine during initial months to prevent precipitation of acute attacks.

Febuxostat

Nonpurine xanthine oxidase inhibitor. Alternative to allopurinol. Can cause liver damage.

Pegloticase

Recombinant uricase enzyme (oxidizes uric acid to allantoin). Used for refractory gout.

Case 15.1

A 44-year-old lady with rheumatoid arthritis (RA) for 8 months is on Diclofenac and Pantoprazole. Symptoms are increasing, ESR and CRP are raised. Diagnosed as active RA.

(a) NSAID Status: Increasing diclofenac or substituting it will not be adequate. NSAIDs only provide symptomatic relief and do not arrest the disease process (bony erosions/joint destruction).

(b) Additional Drug: A DMARD (Disease Modifying Antirheumatic Drug) must be started immediately. Methotrexate is the first choice standard treatment. It arrests the immune-inflammatory process.

(c) Pre-requisites & Benefits: Check liver function, kidney function, and blood counts (bone marrow) before prescribing Methotrexate. Benefits (relief of pain/swelling, retardation of joint damage) are expected after 3-6 weeks.

(d) Pantoprazole: It was prescribed to prevent NSAID-induced gastric injury. It should be continued as long as she takes the NSAID.
