Corticosteroids

üìö Hormones and Related Drugs
‚è±Ô∏è 55 min read
01

üß¨ Biosynthesis & Regulation

The adrenal cortex secretes steroidal hormones which have glucocorticoid, mineralocorticoid and weakly androgenic activities. They are synthesized from cholesterol. The secretion is controlled by ACTH from the pituitary, which in turn is regulated by CRH from the hypothalamus and negative feedback by circulating corticoids (Hypothalamo-Pituitary-Adrenal or HPA axis).

Daily Secretion:

  • Hydrocortisone (Cortisol): 10-20 mg daily (circadian rhythm: peak in morning).
  • Aldosterone: 0.125 mg daily.
02

üßÇ Mineralocorticoid Actions

Aldosterone is the principal mineralocorticoid.

  • Action: Enhances $Na^+$ reabsorption from the distal convoluted tubule in kidney, associated with increased $K^+$ and $H^+$ excretion.
  • Mechanism: Gene mediated increased transcription of mRNA synthesis of Aldosterone Induced Proteins (AIP) like $Na^+K^+$-ATPase and epithelial $Na^+$ channels (ENaC).
  • Result: Salt and water retention, expansion of ECF volume, hypertension, hypokalemia, alkalosis.
03

‚ö° Glucocorticoid Actions

Glucocorticoids have widespread permissive and direct actions, preparing the body to withstand stress.

Metabolic Effects

  • Carbohydrate: Promotes gluconeogenesis, glycogen deposition, inhibits peripheral glucose utilization $\rightarrow$ Hyperglycaemia (Diabetogenic).
  • Protein: Catabolic. Muscle wasting, negative nitrogen balance.
  • Fat: Redistribution of fat (Moon face, buffalo hump). Permissive for lipolysis.

Systemic Effects

  • CVS: Maintains vascular tone (permissive for catecholamines). Restricts capillary permeability.
  • Muscle: Maintains contractility (weakness occurs in deficiency).
  • CNS: Mild euphoria, lowers seizure threshold.
  • Stomach: Increases acid/pepsin (ulcerogenic).

Anti-inflammatory & Immunosuppressive Actions

This is the basis for most clinical uses.

  • Suppression of Inflammation: Inhibits all stages (redness, heat, swelling, pain). Inhibits recruitment of leukocytes, suppresses production of PGs and Leukotrienes (by inducing Annexins/Lipocortin which inhibit Phospholipase A2).
  • Immunosuppression: Suppresses cell-mediated immunity (T-cells more sensitive). Suppresses graft rejection and delayed hypersensitivity.
  • Lympholysis: Destroys malignant lymphoid cells.
04

‚öôÔ∏è Mechanism of Action

Corticosteroids penetrate cells and bind to high affinity cytoplasmic receptors (GR). The complex migrates to the nucleus, binds to Glucocorticoid Response Elements (GRE) on DNA, and regulates transcription of specific genes (induction or repression). This process takes time (30-60 min lag), and effects persist long after the drug is gone.

üí°

Receptor Selectivity

11$\beta$-HSD2 enzyme converts active Hydrocortisone to inactive Cortisone in mineralocorticoid target tissues (kidney), preventing GR activation of Mineralocorticoid Receptors. This explains why Hydrocortisone has weak mineralocorticoid activity despite high affinity for the receptor.

üíâ Therapeutic Uses

A. Replacement Therapy

  • Acute Adrenal Insufficiency (Addisonian Crisis): Emergency. Hydrocortisone 100 mg i.v. stat + infusion + saline/glucose.
  • Chronic Adrenal Insufficiency (Addison's Disease): Oral Hydrocortisone + Fludrocortisone (if salt wasting persists).
  • Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia: Hydrocortisone (suppresses ACTH and androgen excess) + Fludrocortisone.

B. Pharmacotherapy (Non-endocrine)

Palliative use in inflammatory/immune diseases.

  • Rheumatoid Arthritis: Adjuvant in severe cases. Intraarticular inj for single joint.
  • Collagen Diseases: SLE, polyarteritis nodosa (life saving).
  • Severe Allergic Reactions: Anaphylaxis (adjunct to Adrenaline), angioedema, serum sickness.
  • Bronchial Asthma: Systemic for status asthmaticus; Inhaled for chronic control.
  • Autoimmune Diseases: Hemolytic anemia, ITP, active chronic hepatitis.
  • Skin Diseases: Pemphigus (life saving), eczema (topical).
  • Eye Diseases: Uveitis, optic neuritis (contraindicated in viral keratitis).
  • Malignancies: ALL, Hodgkin's lymphoma (lympholytic).
  • Organ Transplantation: To prevent rejection.
  • Cerebral Edema: Dexamethasone is preferred.
  • Foetal Lung Maturation: Betamethasone to mother in threatened premature delivery.
07

‚ö†Ô∏è Adverse Effects

Extension of pharmacological actions with prolonged use.

Metabolic & Systemic

  • Cushing's Habitus: Moon face, truncal obesity.
  • Hyperglycaemia: Precipitation of diabetes.
  • Muscle Wasting: Steroid myopathy.
  • Osteoporosis: Vertebral fractures.
  • Growth Retardation: In children.
  • Fluid Retention/HTN: (Mineralocorticoid effect).

Other Toxicities

  • Susceptibility to Infection: TB flare-up, fungal infections.
  • Peptic Ulcer: Silent perforation.
  • Psychiatric: Euphoria, psychosis.
  • Eye: Posterior subcapsular cataract, Glaucoma.
  • Skin: Striae, bruising, atrophy.
08

üìâ HPA Suppression & Withdrawal

Long term use suppresses the HPA axis causing adrenal atrophy. Abrupt withdrawal precipitates Acute Adrenal Insufficiency (malaise, hypotension, collapse).

üõë

Withdrawal Protocol

Withdrawal must be gradual over weeks/months. Patients on steroids need increased doses during stress (surgery, infection) to mimic the natural adrenal response.

Minimizing Suppression: Use short acting drugs, lowest effective dose, single morning dose, or alternate day therapy.

09

üö´ Inhibitors & Antagonists

  • Mifepristone: Glucocorticoid receptor antagonist (also antiprogestin). Used in inoperable Cushing's syndrome.
  • Metyrapone: Inhibits 11$\beta$-hydroxylase (blocks cortisol synthesis). Diagnostic use.
  • Ketoconazole: Antifungal; high doses inhibit steroid synthesis. Used in Cushing's syndrome.
  • Aminoglutethimide: Inhibits conversion of cholesterol to pregnenolone (medical adrenalectomy).

ü§î Problem Directed Study

üìù

Case 20.1

A 35-year female on Prednisolone (tapered to 10 mg/day) for inflammatory bowel disease meets with an accident and requires surgery.

(a) Additional Measure: The patient has likely suppression of the HPA axis due to chronic steroid therapy. She cannot mount a physiological cortisol response to the stress of surgery. She MUST receive supplemental corticosteroids (e.g., Hydrocortisone 100 mg i.v.) during the perioperative period to prevent acute adrenal crisis and cardiovascular collapse.

(b) Postoperative Period: Prednisolone therapy should not be discontinued. It should be continued (initially parenteral, then oral) to prevent flare-up of bowel disease and because her adrenals are suppressed. The dose may be temporarily increased to cover the stress of recovery.
