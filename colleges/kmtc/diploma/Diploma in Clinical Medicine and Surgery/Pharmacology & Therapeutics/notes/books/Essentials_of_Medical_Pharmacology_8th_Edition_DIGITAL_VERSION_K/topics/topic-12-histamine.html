üß¨ Histamine

Histamine, meaning 'tissue amine' (histos‚Äîtissue) is almost ubiquitously present in animal tissues and in certain plants, e.g. stinging nettle. Histamine is present mostly within storage granules of mast cells. Tissues rich in histamine are skin, gastric and intestinal mucosa, lungs, liver and placenta. Nonmast cell histamine occurs in brain, epidermis, gastric mucosa and growing regions. Turnover of mast cell histamine is slow, while that of nonmast cell histamine is fast.

Synthesis, Storage and Destruction

Histamine is $\beta$-imidazolylethylamine. It is synthesized locally from the amino acid histidine and degraded rapidly by oxidation and methylation.

In mast cells, histamine (positively charged) is held by an acidic protein and heparin (negatively charged) within intracellular granules. When the granules are extruded by exocytosis, $\mathrm{Na}^{+}$ ions in e.c.f. exchange with histamine to release it free. Increase in intracellular cAMP (caused by $\beta$ adrenergic agonists and methylxanthines) inhibits histamine release. Histamine is inactive orally because liver degrades all histamine that is absorbed from the intestines.

Histamine Releasers

A variety of mechanical, chemical and immunological stimuli are capable of releasing histamine from mast cells.

  • Tissue damage: trauma, stings and venoms, proteolytic enzymes, phospholipase A.
  • Antigen: antibody reaction involving IgE antibodies.
  • Polymers: dextran, polyvinyl pyrrolidone (PVP).
  • Basic drugs: tubocurarine, morphine, atropine, pentamidine, polymyxin B, vancomycin and even some antihistaminics release histamine by displacing it from the binding site.
  • Surface acting agents: Tween 80, compound 48/80.

H4 Receptor

A fourth receptor ($\mathrm{H}_{4}$) has been identified. It has considerable homology with $\mathrm{H}_{3}$ receptor. Eosinophils, mast cells and basophils are the primary cells expressing $\mathrm{H}_{4}$ receptors. Activation enhances chemotaxis of these cells, suggesting a role in allergic inflammation.

‚öôÔ∏è Pharmacological Actions

1. Blood vessels

Histamine causes marked dilatation of smaller blood vessels, including arterioles, capillaries and venules. Rapid i.v. injection causes fall in BP which has an early short lasting $\mathrm{H}_{1}$ and a slow but more persistent $\mathrm{H}_{2}$ component. Vasodilatation is partly indirect, mediated through 'endothelium dependent relaxing factor' (EDRF), i.e. NO ($\mathrm{H}_{1}$). $\mathrm{H}_{2}$ receptors mediating vasodilatation are located directly on the vascular smooth muscle. Larger arteries and veins are constricted ($\mathrm{H}_{1}$).

Histamine also causes increased capillary permeability due to separation of endothelial cells resulting in exudation of plasma ($\mathrm{H}_{1}$).

üî¥

Triple Response

Injected intradermally, histamine elicits:

  1. Red spot: due to intense capillary dilatation.
  2. Wheal: due to exudation of fluid from capillaries and venules.
  3. Flare: redness in the surrounding area due to arteriolar dilatation mediated by axon reflex.

2. Heart

Direct effects are not prominent in situ, but isolated heart is stimulated ($\mathrm{H}_{2}$).

3. Visceral smooth muscle

Histamine causes bronchoconstriction ($\mathrm{H}_{1}$); guinea pigs and patients of asthma are highly sensitive. Large doses cause abdominal cramps and colic.

4. Glands

Histamine causes marked increase in gastric secretion‚Äîprimarily of acid but also of pepsin. This is a direct action exerted on parietal cells through $\mathrm{H}_{2}$ receptors, mediated by cAMP.

5. Sensory nerve endings

Itching and pain occur when histamine is injected i.v. or intracutaneously ($\mathrm{H}_{1}$).

6. CNS

Histamine does not penetrate blood-brain barrier. However, intracerebroventricular administration produces rise in BP, cardiac stimulation, behavioural arousal, hypothermia, vomiting and ADH release.

04

ü©∫ Pathophysiological Roles

  • Gastric secretion: Histamine has dominant physiological role in mediating secretion of HCl. It acts synergistically with ACh and gastrin.
  • Allergic phenomena: Released from mast cells in immediate type hypersensitivity (Type I) reactions. Causative in urticaria, angioedema, bronchoconstriction and anaphylactic shock.
  • Transmitter: Afferent transmitter for itch and pain. Nonmast cell histamine in brain is involved in maintaining wakefulness ($\mathrm{H}_{1}$), suppressing appetite, regulating body temperature and thirst.
  • Inflammation: Mediator of vasodilatation and edema. Promotes adhesion of leukocytes.
  • Tissue growth and repair: High concentrations in growing tissues.
  • Headache: Implicated in vascular headaches.

üíä H1 Antagonists (Conventional)

These drugs competitively antagonize actions of histamine at the $\mathrm{H}_{1}$ receptors. Conventional antihistaminics have diverse chemical structures and often possess additional properties like sedative and anticholinergic effects.

Pharmacological Actions

  1. Antagonism of histamine: Effectively block bronchoconstriction, contraction of intestinal muscle, and triple response (wheal, flare, itch). Fall in BP is only partially blocked.
  2. Antiallergic action: Control urticaria, itching, angioedema. Asthma is practically unaffected.
  3. CNS: Variable degree of CNS depression (sedation, diminished alertness). Some are effective in motion sickness (Promethazine, Diphenhydramine) and parkinsonism. Cyproheptadine stimulates appetite.
  4. Anticholinergic action: Many have atropine-like actions (dry mouth, urinary hesitancy).
  5. Local anaesthetic: Membrane stabilizing property (Pheniramine, Promethazine).

Side Effects and Toxicity

  • Sedation: Diminished alertness, motor incoordination. Potentiate alcohol.
  • Anticholinergic: Dryness of mouth, blurred vision, urinary hesitancy, constipation.
  • GI: Epigastric distress.
  • Acute overdose: Central excitation, tremors, hallucinations, convulsions, fever, cardiovascular failure.

üÜï Second Generation Antihistaminics

Marketed after 1980, these have:

  • Absence of CNS depressant property (poor BBB penetration).
  • Higher $\mathrm{H}_{1}$ selectivity (no anticholinergic side effects).
  • Additional antiallergic mechanisms (inhibit late phase mediators like leukotrienes, PAF).

They do not impair psychomotor performance or sleep.

Fexofenadine

Active metabolite of terfenadine. Does not block cardiac $K^+$ channels (no Torsades de pointes). Minimally metabolized. $t_{1/2}$ 11-16 hr.
Dose: 120-180 mg OD.

Loratadine

Long-acting, fast acting. Metabolized by CYP3A4. No cardiac arrhythmia reported.
Dose: 10 mg OD.

Desloratadine

Active metabolite of loratadine. Effective at half dose (5 mg OD).

Cetirizine

Metabolite of hydroxyzine. Penetrates brain poorly but mild sedation possible. Inhibits eosinophil chemotaxis. $t_{1/2}$ 7-10 hr.
Dose: 10 mg OD.

Levocetirizine

Active $R(-)$ enantiomer of cetirizine. Less sedation.
Dose: 5 mg OD.

Azelastine

Topical intranasal spray. Quick relief. Also inhibits histamine release.

üè• Therapeutic Uses

1. Allergic disorders

Control itching, urticaria, seasonal hay fever, allergic conjunctivitis. Ineffective in bronchial asthma. Adjuvant in anaphylaxis.

2. Other conditions involving histamine

Insect bite, ivy poisoning.

3. Pruritides

Chlorpheniramine, diphenhydramine, cyproheptadine are first choice for idiopathic pruritus.

4. Common cold

Symptomatic relief by anticholinergic (reduce rhinorrhoea) and sedative actions.

5. Motion sickness

Promethazine, diphenhydramine, dimenhydrinate and meclozine have prophylactic value. Also for morning sickness and drug induced vomiting.

6. Vertigo

Cinnarizine is a $\mathrm{H}_{1}$ antihistamine with anti-vasoconstrictor and calcium channel blocking activity. Used in M√©ni√®re‚Äôs disease. Betahistine (histamine analogue) is also used.

7. Preanaesthetic medication

Promethazine (sedative + anticholinergic).

8. Cough

Components of cough remedies (Chlorpheniramine, Diphenhydramine).

9. Acute muscle dystonia

Parenteral promethazine or diphenhydramine relieves drug-induced dystonia.
