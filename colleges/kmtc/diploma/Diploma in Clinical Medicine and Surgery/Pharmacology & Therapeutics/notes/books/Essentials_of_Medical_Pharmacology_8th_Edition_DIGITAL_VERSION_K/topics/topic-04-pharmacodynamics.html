‚ö° Principles of Drug Action

Pharmacodynamics is the study of drug effects. It attempts to elucidate the complete action-effect sequence and the dose-effect relationship. Drugs do not impart new functions to any system, organ or cell; they only alter the pace of ongoing activity.

1. Stimulation

Selective enhancement of the level of activity of specialized cells (e.g., adrenaline stimulates heart). Excessive stimulation is often followed by depression.

2. Depression

Selective diminution of activity of specialized cells (e.g., barbiturates depress CNS, quinidine depresses heart).

3. Irritation

Nonselective, noxious effect on less specialized cells (epithelium, connective tissue). Can result in inflammation, corrosion, necrosis.

4. Replacement

Use of natural metabolites, hormones or their congeners in deficiency states (e.g., insulin in diabetes, iron in anaemia).

5. Cytotoxic action

Selective action on invading parasites or cancer cells without significantly affecting host cells (e.g., penicillin, chloroquine).

üîë Mechanism of Drug Action

Physical & Chemical Mechanisms

Only a handful of drugs act by virtue of their simple physical or chemical property.

  • Physical mass: Bulk laxatives (ispaghula).
  • Adsorptive property: Activated charcoal.
  • Osmotic activity: Mannitol, mag. sulfate.
  • Chemical neutralization: Antacids.
  • Chelation: EDTA, dimercaprol.

üîã Ion Channels & Transporters

Ion Channels

Proteins acting as ion selective channels participate in transmembrane signaling. Drugs can affect them directly or indirectly.

  • Direct physical block: Local anaesthetics block voltage sensitive Na+ channels.
  • Modulators:
    • Quinidine: Blocks myocardial Na+ channels.
    • Nifedipine: Blocks L-type voltage sensitive Ca2+ channels.
    • Sulfonylureas: Inhibit ATP-sensitive K+ channels in pancreatic $\beta$ cells.
    • Nicorandil: Opens ATP-sensitive K+ channels.

Transporters

Drugs interact with solute carrier (SLC) transporters to inhibit ongoing physiological transport.

Examples

  • Desipramine/Cocaine: Block Norepinephrine Transporter (NET).
  • Fluoxetine (SSRIs): Inhibits Serotonin Transporter (SERT).
  • Furosemide: Inhibits Na+K+2Cl- cotransporter.
  • Probenecid: Inhibits Organic Anion Transporter (OAT).

Mechanism

Interaction interferes with the translocation of the substrate (metabolite/ion) across the membrane, often resulting in accumulation of the substrate on one side.

05

üì° Receptors

Receptor: A macromolecule or binding site located on the surface or inside the effector cell that serves to recognize the signal molecule/drug and initiate the response to it, but itself has no other function.

Terminology

  • Agonist: Activates a receptor to produce an effect similar to physiological signal. (Affinity + Maximal Intrinsic Activity = 1)
  • Antagonist: Prevents action of agonist but has no effect of its own. (Affinity + Zero Intrinsic Activity = 0)
  • Partial Agonist: Activates receptor to produce submaximal effect but antagonizes full agonist. (Affinity + Submaximal Intrinsic Activity between 0 and 1)
  • Inverse Agonist: Activates receptor to produce effect in opposite direction to agonist. (Affinity + Negative Intrinsic Activity between 0 and -1)

Receptor Occupation Theory

Clark (1937) proposed that drug effect is a direct function of drug-receptor complexes formed.

$$D + R \rightleftarrows DR \rightarrow E$$

Two-State Receptor Model

Receptors exist in equilibrium between Active ($Ra$) and Inactive ($Ri$) states.

  • Agonist: Binds preferentially to $Ra$, shifting equilibrium to active state.
  • Antagonist: Binds equally to $Ra$ and $Ri$, maintaining equilibrium (no response, blocks agonist).
  • Partial Agonist: Slightly greater affinity for $Ra$.
  • Inverse Agonist: Binds preferentially to $Ri$, stabilizing inactive state (reduces constitutive activation).

2. Ion Channel Receptors

Ligand gated ion channels (fastest response, milliseconds). Example: Nicotinic cholinergic, GABAA, 5-HT3.

3. Transmembrane Enzyme-Linked Receptors

Intracellular domain has enzymatic activity (e.g., Tyrosine Kinase). Example: Insulin, EGF, ANP.

4. JAK-STAT Binding Receptors

Receptor has no intrinsic activity but binds Janus Kinase (JAK) $\rightarrow$ phosphorylates STAT $\rightarrow$ gene transcription. Example: Cytokines, Growth hormone.

5. Nuclear Receptors

Intracellular receptors regulating gene expression (slowest response, hours). Example: Steroids, Thyroxine, Vit D.

üìâ Dose-Response Relationship

The relationship between dose and response is described by a rectangular hyperbola, which becomes a sigmoid curve when dose is plotted on a log scale.

$$E = \frac{E_{max} \times [D]}{K_D + [D]}$$

Potency

Position on the dose axis. Amount of drug needed to produce a certain response. Relative potency compares $EC_{50}$.

Efficacy

The maximal response ($E_{max}$) that can be elicited by the drug. Efficacy is often more decisive than potency in clinical choice.

Therapeutic Index: Gap between therapeutic effect and adverse effect curves. $\text{TI} = \frac{LD_{50}}{ED_{50}}$.

08

ü§ù Synergism & Antagonism

Synergism

Action of one drug is facilitated by the other.

  • Additive: Effect of A + B = Effect of A + Effect of B (e.g., Aspirin + Paracetamol).
  • Supraadditive (Potentiation): Effect of A + B > Effect of A + Effect of B (e.g., Levodopa + Carbidopa).

Antagonism

One drug decreases the action of another.

  • Physical: Charcoal adsorbs alkaloids.
  • Chemical: KMnO4 oxidizes alkaloids.
  • Physiological: Drugs act on different receptors with opposite effects (Histamine vs Adrenaline).
  • Receptor Antagonism:
    • Competitive (Equilibrium): Antagonist binds to same site. Parallel shift of DRC to right. Surmountable by increasing agonist concentration. (ACh vs Atropine).
    • Noncompetitive: Antagonist binds to different site or interferes with downstream mechanism. DRC flattened, $E_{max}$ reduced. Unsurmountable. (Diazepam vs Bicuculline).
    • Nonequilibrium: Covalent binding. Irreversible. (Phenoxybenzamine).
