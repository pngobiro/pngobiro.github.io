‚öóÔ∏è Biotransformation (Metabolism)

Biotransformation means chemical alteration of the drug in the body. It is needed to render nonpolar (lipid-soluble) compounds polar (lipid-insoluble) so that they are not reabsorbed in the renal tubules and are excreted. In the absence of metabolism, body will not be able to get rid of lipophilic substances, and they will become very long acting. The primary site for drug metabolism is liver; others are‚Äîkidney, intestine, lungs and plasma.

‚ÑπÔ∏è

Outcomes of Biotransformation

  1. Inactivation: Most drugs and their active metabolites are rendered inactive or less active, e.g. ibuprofen, paracetamol, lidocaine.
  2. Active metabolite from an active drug: Many drugs have been found to be partially converted to one or more active metabolite, e.g. Codeine $\rightarrow$ Morphine, Diazepam $\rightarrow$ Desmethyl-diazepam.
  3. Activation of inactive drug (Prodrug): Few drugs are inactive as such and need conversion in the body to one or more active metabolites.

üß™ Phases of Metabolism

Biotransformation reactions can be classified into:

Nonsynthetic / Phase I / Functionalization reactions

A functional group ($-\mathrm{OH}, -\mathrm{COOH}, -\mathrm{CHO}, -\mathrm{NH}_{2}, -\mathrm{SH}$) is generated or exposed‚Äîmetabolite may be active or inactive.

  • Oxidation: The most important reaction, mostly carried out by monooxygenases in the liver involving cytochrome P-450 (CYP).
  • Reduction: Converse of oxidation (e.g., chloramphenicol, halothane).
  • Hydrolysis: Cleavage of drug molecule by taking up a molecule of water (e.g., esters like procaine, amides like lidocaine).
  • Cyclization/Decyclization: Formation or opening of ring structures.

Synthetic / Conjugation / Phase II reactions

An endogenous radical is conjugated to the drug‚Äîmetabolite is mostly inactive (except morphine-6-glucuronide).

  • Glucuronide conjugation: Most important, carried out by UDP-glucuronosyl transferases (UGTs). Increases molecular weight favoring biliary excretion and enterohepatic cycling.
  • Acetylation: For compounds with amino residues (e.g., isoniazid, sulfonamides). Shows genetic polymorphism (slow and fast acetylators).
  • Methylation: Amines and phenols (e.g., adrenaline, histamine).
  • Sulfate conjugation: Phenolic compounds and steroids.
  • Glutathione conjugation: Inactivates highly reactive intermediates (e.g., paracetamol toxic metabolite).

Cytochrome P450 Isoenzymes

In human beings, only a few members of three isoenzyme families (CYP 1, 2 and 3) carry out metabolism of most drugs.

‚öôÔ∏è Microsomal Enzyme Induction & Inhibition

Microsomal Enzyme Induction

Many drugs, insecticides and carcinogens interact with DNA and increase the synthesis of microsomal enzyme protein (CYP450, UGTs). Rate of metabolism increases by 2‚Äì4 fold. Takes 4‚Äì14 days to reach peak.

‚ö†Ô∏è

Consequences

  • Decreased intensity/duration of action (e.g., oral contraceptive failure with rifampin).
  • Increased intensity of action of prodrugs or toxic metabolites (e.g., paracetamol toxicity).
  • Tolerance (autoinduction) e.g., carbamazepine.
  • Precipitation of acute intermittent porphyria.

Inhibition of Drug Metabolism

Competitive inhibition of CYP450. Occurs quickly (within hours). Can precipitate toxicity of the object drug.

Important Inhibitors: Erythromycin, Ketoconazole, Cimetidine, Ciprofloxacin, Verapamil, Ritonavir, Omeprazole.

üö™ First-pass (Presystemic) Metabolism

This refers to metabolism of a drug during its passage from the site of absorption into the systemic circulation. All orally administered drugs are exposed to drug metabolizing enzymes in the intestinal wall and liver.

The Hepatic Extraction Ratio ($ER_{Liver}$):

$$E R=\frac{C L_{\text {Liver }}}{\text { Hepatic blood flow }}$$

Systemic Bioavailability ($F$):

$$F=\text { fractional absorption } \times(I-E R)$$

üöΩ Excretion

Excretion is the passage out of systemically absorbed drug.

  1. Urine: Most important channel.
  2. Faeces: Unabsorbed drug or drug excreted in bile. Enterohepatic cycling (drug excreted in bile $\rightarrow$ reabsorbed in gut) prolongs action (e.g., doxycycline, oral contraceptives).
  3. Exhaled air: Gases and volatile liquids (alcohol).
  4. Saliva/Sweat: Minor importance.
  5. Milk: Most drugs enter by passive diffusion. Important for suckling infant safety.
06

üíß Renal Excretion

Net renal excretion = (Glomerular filtration + tubular secretion) - tubular reabsorption

  • Glomerular filtration: Depends on plasma protein binding (only free drug filtered) and renal blood flow.
  • Tubular reabsorption: Passive diffusion of lipid-soluble drugs. pH dependent: Weak bases excrete faster in acidic urine; weak acids excrete faster in alkaline urine. Utilized in poisoning treatment.
  • Tubular secretion: Active transport of organic acids (OAT) and bases (OCT). Competitive inhibition occurs (e.g., Probenecid blocks penicillin secretion).

üìâ Kinetics of Elimination

Clearance (CL)

The theoretical volume of plasma from which the drug is completely removed in unit time.

$$C L=\frac{\text { Rate of elimination }}{C}$$

First Order Kinetics

Rate of elimination is directly proportional to drug concentration. Constant fraction of drug eliminated per unit time. $CL$ is constant. Applies to most drugs.

Zero Order Kinetics

Rate of elimination is constant irrespective of concentration. Constant amount eliminated. $CL$ decreases with concentration. E.g., Alcohol, Phenytoin (saturation kinetics).

Plasma Half-Life ($t_{1/2}$)

Time taken for plasma concentration to reduce to half.

$$\mathrm{t}^{1 / 2}=\frac{\ln 2}{k} = \frac{0.693 \times \mathrm{V}}{C L}$$

For first order kinetics:

  • 1 $t_{1/2}$: 50% eliminated
  • 2 $t_{1/2}$: 75% eliminated
  • 3 $t_{1/2}$: 87.5% eliminated
  • 4 $t_{1/2}$: 93.75% eliminated (clinical steady state usually)

üìã Dosage Regimens

Plateau Principle

When constant dose is repeated before expiry of 4 $t_{1/2}$, drug accumulates until elimination balances input (Steady State, $Cpss$).

$$\text { Cpss }=\frac{\text { dose rate }}{C L}$$
üí°

Dose Calculations

Loading Dose: To attain target concentration rapidly. Governed by $V$.

$$\text { Loading dose }=\frac{\text { target } C p \times V}{F}$$

Maintenance Dose: To maintain target $Cpss$. Governed by $CL$.

$$\text { Maintenance dose rate }=\frac{\text { target } C p s s \times C L}{F}$$

Therapeutic Drug Monitoring (TDM)

Useful for drugs with low safety margin (digoxin, theophylline, lithium) or where failure of response is not easily detected.
