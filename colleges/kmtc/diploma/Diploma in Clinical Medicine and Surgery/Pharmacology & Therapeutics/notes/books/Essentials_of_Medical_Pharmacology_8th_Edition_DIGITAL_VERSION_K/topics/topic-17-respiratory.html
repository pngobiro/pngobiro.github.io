Drugs for Cough and Bronchial Asthma

üó£Ô∏è Drugs for Cough

Cough is a protective reflex, its purpose being expulsion of respiratory secretions and foreign particles from air passages. It occurs due to stimulation of mechano- or chemoreceptors in throat and respiratory passages or stretch receptors in the lungs.

üí°

Types of Cough

  • Useful (Productive) Cough: Serves to drain the airway; suppression is not desirable unless the amount of expectoration is small compared to the effort.
  • Useless (Nonproductive) Cough: Should be suppressed.
02

üß™ Expectorants & Mucolytics

Demulcents and Expectorants

Pharyngeal demulcents soothe the throat and reduce afferent impulses from inflamed mucosa. Expectorants (Mucokinetics) increase bronchial secretion or reduce viscosity.

  • Guaiphenesin: Plant product; enhances secretion and mucociliary function. The only expectorant approved by US-FDA.
  • Sodium/Potassium citrate: Increase secretion by salt action.
  • Ammonium salts: Nauseating reflex increases respiratory secretions.

Mucolytics

Agents that liquefy tenacious sputum.

Drug Mechanism & Features Uses
Bromhexine Derivative of alkaloid vasicine. Depolymerises mucopolysaccharides, liberating lysosomal enzymes. Productive cough.
Ambroxol Metabolite of bromhexine with similar action. Productive cough.
Acetylcysteine Opens disulfide bonds in mucoproteins. Can be given orally or by inhalation. Viscid sputum, Paracetamol poisoning (antidote).
Carbocisteine Liquefies sputum. May break gastric mucus barrier (avoid in peptic ulcer). Chronic bronchitis.
03

üõë Antitussives

Drugs that act in the CNS to raise the threshold of cough centre or peripherally to reduce tussal impulses. Used for dry nonproductive cough.

Opioid Antitussives

  • Codeine: Standard antitussive; more selective for cough center than morphine. Abuse liability low but present. Constipation is a drawback.
  • Pholcodine: No analgesic/addicting property. Longer acting (12 hr).

Nonopioid Antitussives

  • Noscapine: Opium alkaloid (benzoisoquinoline series). No narcotic/analgesic effects. Equipotent to codeine. Releases histamine (bronchoconstriction in asthmatics).
  • Dextromethorphan: Synthetic central NMDA antagonist. $d$-isomer is antitussive, $l$-isomer is analgesic. Nonaddicting.
  • Chlophedianol: Central antitussive with weak antihistaminic/anticholinergic actions.

Antihistamines

Chlorpheniramine, Diphenhydramine, Promethazine: Added to formulations for sedative/anticholinergic action. No selective antitussive action.

üå¨Ô∏è Bronchial Asthma: Overview

Asthma is primarily an inflammatory condition characterized by hyperresponsiveness of tracheobronchial smooth muscle. Inflammation is initiated by mast cells and dominated by eosinophils, lymphocytes, and mast cells.

Mediators: Histamine, Protease enzymes, TNF $\alpha$, PGs, Leukotrienes (LTs), PAF, Interleukins.

‚ö†Ô∏è

COPD vs Asthma

COPD is also inflammatory (neutrophils/macrophages) but characterized by progressive emphysema and bronchiolar fibrosis. Airflow limitation is largely irreversible. Smoking is a major cause.

Approaches to Treatment

  1. Bronchodilators: Sympathomimetics, Methylxanthines, Anticholinergics.
  2. Anti-inflammatory: Corticosteroids, Mast cell stabilizers, Leukotriene antagonists, Anti-IgE antibody.
05

üöÄ Sympathomimetic Agents

Adrenergic drugs cause bronchodilatation through $\beta_2$ receptor stimulation $\rightarrow$ increased cAMP $\rightarrow$ relaxation.

Selective $\beta_2$ Agonists

Short Acting (SABA)

Salbutamol (Albuterol), Terbutaline.

  • Inhaled onset: 5 min.
  • Duration: 2-4 hours.
  • Use: Abort/terminate attacks (Rescue).

Long Acting (LABA)

Salmeterol, Formoterol.

  • Duration: >12 hours.
  • Use: Maintenance therapy, nocturnal asthma.
  • Caution: Should be used with inhaled steroids.

Adverse Effects: Muscle tremors (dose related), palpitation, restlessness, nervousness, hypokalaemia.

Bambuterol: Prodrug of terbutaline. Long acting (24 hr). Suitable for nocturnal asthma.

06

‚òï Methylxanthines (Theophylline)

Theophylline, Caffeine, and Theobromine are naturally occurring alkaloids.

Mechanism of Action

  • Inhibition of Phosphodiesterase (PDE): Increases cyclic nucleotides (cAMP/cGMP) $\rightarrow$ bronchodilatation, cardiac stimulation.
  • Blockade of Adenosine Receptors: Adenosine causes bronchoconstriction and CNS depression; theophylline antagonizes this.
  • Release of $Ca^{2+}$: From sarcoplasmic reticulum (high doses).
  • Histone Deacetylation: Enhancement at low doses suppresses inflammatory gene transcription.

Pharmacological Actions

  • CNS: Stimulation, alertness (Caffeine > Theophylline). High doses: convulsions.
  • CVS: Cardiac stimulation (rate/force), vasodilatation (systemic), vasoconstriction (cranial - Caffeine).
  • Smooth Muscle: Relaxation (Bronchi: Theophylline > Caffeine).
  • Kidney: Mild diuresis.

Pharmacokinetics

Narrow margin of safety. Metabolized by CYP1A2. Zero order kinetics at higher doses.

  • Plasma $t_{1/2}$: Adults 7-12 hr; Children 3-5 hr.
  • Interactions: Smoking, Phenytoin, Rifampicin increase clearance (dose increase needed). Erythromycin, Ciprofloxacin, Cimetidine decrease clearance (toxicity risk).

Adverse Effects

Headache, nervousness, nausea (early). Arrhythmias, seizures (severe).

07

üåµ Anticholinergics

Block $M_3$ receptor mediated cholinergic constrictor tone. Act primarily in larger airways.

Drug Duration Key Use
Ipratropium bromide Short (4-6 hr) COPD, Asthma (adjuvant)
Tiotropium bromide Long (24 hr) COPD maintenance

Note: Inhaled anticholinergics are the bronchodilators of choice in COPD.

08

üìâ Corticosteroids

Not bronchodilators, but suppress inflammation, reduce bronchial hyperreactivity, and mucosal edema. Most effective for long-term control.

Systemic Corticosteroids

Use: Severe chronic asthma, Status asthmaticus (IV Hydrocortisone).

Inhaled Corticosteroids (ICS)

High topical, low systemic activity.

  • Drugs: Beclomethasone, Budesonide, Fluticasone, Ciclesonide (prodrug, activated in lung).
  • Use: Persistent asthma. Not for acute attack.
  • Adverse Effects: Oropharyngeal candidiasis, hoarseness (minimize by spacer/gargling). Systemic effects rare at low doses.
09

üõ°Ô∏è Leukotriene Antagonists

Montelukast and Zafirlukast.

Competitively antagonize $cysLT_1$ receptors (mediators of bronchoconstriction/mucus). Effective in aspirin-induced asthma and exercise-induced asthma. Oral administration. Very safe.

Mast Cell Stabilizers

Sodium Cromoglycate: Inhibits degranulation of mast cells. Prophylactic use only. Not a bronchodilator.

Anti-IgE Antibody

Omalizumab: Humanized monoclonal antibody against IgE. Neutralizes free IgE. Used in severe extrinsic asthma.

10

üìã Management Guidelines (Stepwise)

  • Step 1 (Mild Episodic): Inhaled short-acting $\beta_2$ agonist (SABA) as needed.
  • Step 2 (Mild Persistent): Regular low-dose ICS + SABA for episodes.
  • Step 3 (Moderate): Low dose ICS + Long-acting $\beta_2$ agonist (LABA).
  • Step 4 (Severe): High dose ICS + LABA + (Leukotriene antagonist/Theophylline).
  • Step 5: Add oral steroids.
11

üö® Status Asthmaticus

Acute severe asthma; life-threatening. Treatment:

  1. Hydrocortisone hemisuccinate: 100 mg i.v. stat, then infusion.
  2. Nebulized Salbutamol + Ipratropium: Driven by $O_2$.
  3. High flow Oxygen.
  4. Salbutamol/Terbutaline: i.m./s.c. if inhalation not effective.
  5. Intubation/Ventilation: If needed.
  6. Antibiotics/Hydration.

Note: IV Aminophylline is outmoded.

ü§î Problem Directed Study

üìù

Case 16.1

30-year-old man with daily breathlessness, using salbutamol inhaler frequently. Spirometry shows moderate asthma.

(a) Treatment adequacy: Not adequate. Frequent use of SABA indicates lack of control. First line treatment: Inhaled Corticosteroid (ICS) like Beclomethasone or Budesonide should be started regularly.

(b) Salbutamol use: Yes, continue SABA only for symptomatic relief of acute wheezing episodes (rescue).

(c) Alternatives: If not willing for inhaler, oral Montelukast or Theophylline (SR) can be tried, but are less effective than ICS.

üìù

Case 16.2

60-year-old COPD patient on Ipratropium + Theophylline. Developed fever, took Erythromycin. Presented with epigastric pain, restlessness, palpitation, tremor, vomiting.

(a) Reason for illness: Theophylline Toxicity. Erythromycin inhibits CYP1A2, the enzyme that metabolizes theophylline, leading to increased plasma levels of theophylline.

(b) Prevention: Avoid Erythromycin. Use an antibiotic that does not interact (e.g., Azithromycin, Amoxicillin). Or reduce Theophylline dose.
