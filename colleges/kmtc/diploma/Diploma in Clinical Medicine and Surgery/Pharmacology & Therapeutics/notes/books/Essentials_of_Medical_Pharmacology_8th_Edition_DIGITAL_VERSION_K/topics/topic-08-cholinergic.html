Cholinergic Transmission and Cholinergic Drugs

01

‚ö° Cholinergic Transmission

Acetylcholine (ACh) is a major neurohumoral transmitter at autonomic, somatic as well as central sites. It is synthesized locally in the cholinergic nerve endings.

Key Steps & Interferences

  • 1 Synthesis: Choline is actively taken up by the axonal membrane ($\mathrm{Na}^{+}$:choline cotransporter). Acetylation occurs via choline acetyl transferase (ChAT). Hemicholinium (HC3) blocks choline uptake (rate limiting step).
  • 2 Storage: ACh is stored in synaptic vesicles via vesicle associated transporter (VAT). Vesamicol blocks this uptake.
  • 3 Release: Occurs by exocytosis triggered by nerve impulse. Botulinum toxin inhibits release; Black widow spider toxin induces massive release and depletion.
  • 4 Destruction: Rapidly hydrolysed by Cholinesterase (ChE) to choline and acetic acid.

Botulinum Toxin

Botulinum toxin A (BOTOX) causes long-lasting loss of cholinergic transmission. It is used to treat spastic neurological conditions (blepharospasm, cerebral palsy, strabismus) and for cosmetic removal of facial wrinkles.

Cholinesterases

There are two types of enzymes that hydrolyse acetylcholine:

Feature Acetylcholinesterase (True) Butyrylcholinesterase (Pseudo)
Distribution Cholinergic sites, RBC, gray matter Plasma, liver, intestine, white matter
Hydrolysis of ACh Very fast Slow
Methacholine Hydrolysed (slower than ACh) Not hydrolysed
Benzoylcholine Not hydrolysed Hydrolysed

üì° Cholinoceptors

Two classes of receptors for ACh are recognised: Muscarinic and Nicotinic.

1. Muscarinic Receptors (G-protein coupled)

Selectively stimulated by muscarine and blocked by atropine.

$M_1$ Autonomic ganglia, Gastric glands, CNS Depolarization, Acid secretion, Learning/Memory Gq (‚Üë $IP_3$/DAG, ‚Üë $Ca^{2+}$)

Selectively stimulated by nicotine and blocked by tubocurarine or hexamethonium.

üíä Cholinergic Agonists

A. Choline Esters

Comparison of properties:

Drug ChE Susceptibility Muscarinic Action Nicotinic Action Use
Acetylcholine High ++ ++ None (evanescent action)
Methacholine Low (AChE only) ++ (CVS selective) ¬± Rarely used
Carbachol Negligible + ++ Glaucoma
Bethanechol Negligible + (GI/Bladder selective) - Urinary retention, Post-op ileus

B. Natural Alkaloids

  • Pilocarpine: From Pilocarpus microphyllus. Prominent muscarinic action. Used as miotic in glaucoma (0.5-4% drops) and as sialogogue.
  • Muscarine: From Amanita muscaria. Toxicological importance only (Mushroom poisoning).
  • Arecoline: From Betel nut. Nicotinic + Muscarinic actions. No therapeutic use.

üõë Anticholinesterases

Agents which inhibit ChE, protect ACh from hydrolysis, and potentiate cholinergic effects. They act by reacting with the enzyme essentially in the same way as ACh.

Classification

Reversible (Carbamates)

  • Physostigmine
  • Neostigmine
  • Pyridostigmine
  • Rivastigmine
  • Donepezil
  • Edrophonium (Non-carbamate, very short acting)

Irreversible (Organophosphates)

  • Dyflos (DFP)
  • Echothiophate
  • Malathion, Parathion (Insecticides)
  • Nerve gases (Tabun, Sarin)

Physostigmine vs Neostigmine

Absorption Good (Oral/Corneal) Poor
CNS Action Present Absent
Direct Action on $N_M$ Absent Present
Main Uses Glaucoma, Atropine poisoning Myasthenia gravis, Urinary retention, Curare reversal

üíâ Therapeutic Uses

1. Glaucoma

Pilocarpine is the preferred miotic (3rd line drug now). Increases tone of ciliary muscle and sphincter pupillae $\rightarrow$ improves aqueous outflow.

2. Myasthenia Gravis

Autoimmune disorder with antibodies against nicotinic receptors ($N_M$) at muscle endplate.

  • Diagnosis: Edrophonium test (Ameliorative test). Reverses weakness for short duration.
  • Treatment: Neostigmine or Pyridostigmine (longer acting, less frequent dosing). Corticosteroids or immunosuppressants used in advanced cases.
‚ö†Ô∏è

Myasthenic vs Cholinergic Crisis

Myasthenic Crisis: Acute weakness due to disease exacerbation. Improves with Edrophonium.

Cholinergic Crisis: Weakness due to overdose of anti-ChE (persistent depolarization). Worsens with Edrophonium.

3. Postoperative Paralytic Ileus / Urinary Retention

Neostigmine or Bethanechol. Contraindicated if organic obstruction is present.

4. Curare Reversal (Postoperative decurarization)

Neostigmine (0.5‚Äì2 mg i.v.) preceded by Atropine (to block muscarinic side effects) rapidly reverses muscle paralysis caused by competitive blockers.

5. Belladonna Poisoning

Physostigmine (0.5‚Äì2 mg i.v.) is the specific antidote as it crosses BBB to antagonize central effects. Neostigmine is not effective against central toxicity.

6. Alzheimer's Disease

Brain cholinergic neurones are affected. Cerebroselective anti-ChEs like Rivastigmine, Donepezil, and Galantamine produce symptomatic improvement.

7. Cobra Bite

Neostigmine + Atropine prevent respiratory paralysis (neurotoxin has curare-like action).

06

‚ò†Ô∏è Anticholinesterase Poisoning

Common with agricultural organophosphate insecticides (accidental/suicidal).

Symptoms

  • Muscarinic: Miosis, salivation, sweating, bronchospasm, colic, involuntary urination/defecation, hypotension, bradycardia.
  • Nicotinic: Muscle fasciculations, weakness, respiratory paralysis.
  • CNS: Confusion, ataxia, convulsions, coma.

Treatment

  1. Decontamination: Fresh air, wash skin/mucous membranes, gastric lavage.
  2. Supportive: Maintain airway, positive pressure respiration, maintain BP (diazepam for convulsions).
  3. Specific Antidote: Atropine

    2 mg i.v. repeated every 10 min till signs of atropinization (dry mouth, dilated pupils) appear. Blocks muscarinic effects. Does not reverse muscle paralysis.

  4. Cholinesterase Reactivators: Oximes

    Pralidoxime (2-PAM): Reactivates phosphorylated ChE enzyme if given early (before "aging" of enzyme). Effective for skeletal muscle paralysis. Contraindicated in carbamate poisoning.
