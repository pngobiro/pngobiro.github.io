Antiadrenergic Drugs and Drugs for Glaucoma

üìö Drugs Acting on ANS
‚è±Ô∏è 45 min read

These drugs inhibit adrenergic responses mediated through the $\alpha$ or $\beta$ or both $\alpha$ and $\beta$ adrenergic receptors. They differ from "adrenergic neurone blocking drugs" (like reserpine or guanethidine) which act by interfering with storage or release of the adrenergic transmitter.

Feature Antiadrenergic drugs Adrenergic neurone blocking drugs
Locus of action Adrenergic receptors on effector cells Adrenergic neuronal membrane or contents
Effect of nerve stimulation Blocked Blocked
Effect of injected Adr Blocked Not blocked (may be potentiated)
Specificity Specific for $\alpha$ or $\beta$ receptors Sympathetic function decreased irrespective of receptor type
01

üõë $\alpha$ Adrenergic Blocking Drugs

These drugs inhibit adrenergic responses mediated through $\alpha$ adrenergic receptors without affecting those mediated through $\beta$ receptors.

General Effects

  • 1 Blockade of vasoconstrictor $\alpha_1$ (and $\alpha_2$) receptors: Reduces peripheral resistance $\rightarrow$ pooling of blood in capacitance vessels $\rightarrow$ fall in BP and postural hypotension.
  • 2 Vasomotor reversal of Dale: $\alpha$ blockers abolish the pressor action of Adrenaline (injected i.v.), which then produces only fall in BP due to $\beta_2$ mediated vasodilatation.
  • 3 Reflex tachycardia: Due to fall in BP and blockade of presynaptic $\alpha_2$ receptors (which normally inhibit NA release).
  • 4 Sodium and water retention: Due to hypotension reducing renal blood flow and reflex increase in renin release.
  • 5 Miosis and nasal stuffiness.
  • 6 Improvement of urine flow in BHP: By blocking $\alpha_1$ receptors in bladder trigone, prostate, and prostatic urethra.
02

üíä Specific $\alpha$ Adrenergic Blockers

Phenoxybenzamine

An irreversible (nonequilibrium) antagonist that forms strong covalent bonds with $\alpha$ receptors. Blockade lasts 3-4 days.

  • Action: Blocks $\alpha_1$ and $\alpha_2$. Causes postural hypotension and reflex tachycardia.
  • Use: Primarily in Pheochromocytoma (preoperative preparation and inoperable cases).
  • Side effects: Postural hypotension, palpitation, nasal blockage, inhibition of ejaculation.

Phentolamine

A rapidly acting, short duration non-selective competitive $\alpha$ blocker. Causes marked tachycardia. Used for diagnosis of pheochromocytoma (rarely now) and control of hypertensive emergencies (e.g., clonidine withdrawal, cheese reaction).

Selective $\alpha_1$ Blockers (Prazosin & Congeners)

These drugs block $\alpha_1$ receptors selectively (ratio $\alpha_1:\alpha_2$ is 1000:1). They cause fall in BP with only mild tachycardia (no blockade of presynaptic $\alpha_2$).

Prazosin

First selective $\alpha_1$ blocker. Effective orally. Short $t_{1/2}$ (2-3 hrs).
Adverse Effect: "First dose effect" (dizziness/fainting) - minimize by starting with low dose at bedtime.

Terazosin & Doxazosin

Longer acting congeners (once daily dosing). Used in HTN and BPH. May promote apoptosis in prostate.

Uroselective $\alpha_1$ Blockers (For BPH)

Benign Hypertrophy of Prostate (BPH) causes urinary obstruction. $\alpha_1$ blockers relax prostatic smooth muscle (dynamic obstruction).

  • Tamsulosin: Selective for $\alpha_{1A}$ subtype (predominant in prostate). Less vascular side effects. Risk of "Floppy iris syndrome" during cataract surgery.
  • Silodosin: Highly selective $\alpha_{1A}$ blocker. Low incidence of hypotension but high incidence of retrograde ejaculation.
  • Alfuzosin: Uroselective properties despite being non-subtype selective.

Yohimbine

Selective $\alpha_2$ blocker. Increases central sympathetic outflow. No valid clinical indications currently.

Clinical Uses of $\alpha$ Blockers

  1. Pheochromocytoma: Phenoxybenzamine (definitive/pre-op), Phentolamine (intra-op). NEVER use $\beta$ blockers without prior $\alpha$ blockade.
  2. Hypertension: Prazosin class (not first line). Phentolamine for hypertensive crises.
  3. Benign Hypertrophy of Prostate (BPH): Tamsulosin, Silodosin, Alfuzosin, Terazosin. Relieve voiding symptoms.
  4. Peripheral Vascular Diseases: Limited utility (Raynaud's).
03

üõë $\beta$ Adrenergic Blocking Drugs

All $\beta$ blockers are competitive antagonists. Propranolol is the prototype.

Pharmacological Actions (Propranolol)

1. Cardiovascular

Heart: Decreases HR, force of contraction, and cardiac output. Prolongs systole. Reduces cardiac work and $O_2$ consumption.
Blood Vessels: Blocks $\beta_2$ vasodilatation $\rightarrow$ acute rise in TPR. Chronic use lowers BP in hypertensives.

2. Respiratory

Increases bronchial resistance ($\beta_2$ blockade). Can precipitate life-threatening asthma in susceptible individuals.

3. Metabolic

Blocks adrenergically induced lipolysis and glycogenolysis. Delays recovery from insulin hypoglycemia (masking of warning signs like tachycardia).

4. Eye

Reduces secretion of aqueous humor $\rightarrow$ lowers intraocular tension (IOP).

Pharmacokinetics

Propranolol is well absorbed but has low bioavailability due to high first pass metabolism. It is highly lipid soluble (crosses BBB). Metabolism is dependent on hepatic blood flow.

‚ö†Ô∏è

Adverse Effects & Contraindications

  • Precipitation of CHF: In patients with low cardiac reserve.
  • Bradycardia: Heart block, arrest.
  • Worsening of Asthma/COPD: Contraindicated in asthmatics.
  • Cold extremities: Worsening of peripheral vascular disease.
  • Metabolic: Hypoglycemia unawareness in diabetics; altered lipid profile (increased TG, decreased HDL).
  • Withdrawal: Sudden withdrawal can cause rebound hypertension, angina, or sudden death (upregulation of receptors).

üíä Specific $\beta$ Blockers

Classification based on properties

Generation/Type Examples Features
Non-selective ($\beta_1 + \beta_2$) Propranolol, Timolol, Sotalol, Nadolol Block both receptors. More likely to cause bronchoconstriction.
Cardioselective ($\beta_1$) Metoprolol, Atenolol, Acebutolol, Bisoprolol, Esmolol Relatively safer in asthma/diabetes (but not totally safe). Less likely to compromise peripheral circulation.
With Intrinsic Sympathomimetic Activity (ISA) Pindolol, Acebutolol Partial agonists. Cause less bradycardia at rest.
Third Generation (Vasodilatory) Labetalol, Carvedilol ($\alpha$ blockade)
Nebivolol (NO release)
Celiprolol ($\beta_2$ agonism)		 Additional vasodilator properties.

Important Agents

  • Atenolol: Cardioselective, water soluble (low CNS entry), long acting (OD dose). Common antihypertensive.
  • Metoprolol: Cardioselective, lipid soluble. Used in HTN, Angina, CHF.
  • Esmolol: Ultra-short acting ($t_{1/2}$ < 10 min). Given i.v. for rapid control of ventricular rate in atrial fibrillation/flutter, or perioperative hypertension.
  • Nebivolol: Highly selective $\beta_1$ blocker + NO-mediated vasodilatation. Good for HTN.
  • Timolol: Non-selective. Widely used topically for glaucoma.
  • Sotalol: Non-selective. Has Class III antiarrhythmic property ($K^+$ channel blockade).

Therapeutic Uses of $\beta$ Blockers

Cardiovascular

  • Hypertension
  • Angina Pectoris
  • Cardiac Arrhythmias
  • MI (Secondary prophylaxis)
  • CHF (Carvedilol, Metoprolol, Bisoprolol)

Non-Cardiovascular

  • Glaucoma (Timolol)
  • Thyrotoxicosis (Propranolol)
  • Migraine Prophylaxis
  • Essential Tremor
  • Anxiety (Somatic symptoms)

üîó Combined $\alpha + \beta$ Blockers

Labetalol

Blocks both $\alpha_1$ and $\beta$ receptors. $\beta$-blocking potency is ~3 times $\alpha$-blocking potency. Causes fall in BP by reducing PVR (vasodilation) without reflex tachycardia.

Uses: Hypertensive emergencies (i.v.), Pheochromocytoma, Pregnancy induced hypertension.

Carvedilol

Blocks $\beta_1 + \beta_2 + \alpha_1$ receptors. Has antioxidant property.

Major Use: Congestive Heart Failure (CHF). It retards progression and prolongs survival in stable mild-to-moderate CHF.

üëÅÔ∏è Drugs for Glaucoma

Glaucoma is a progressive optic nerve damage usually associated with raised intraocular tension (IOT). Goal: Lower IOT to target level.

Mechanisms of IOT Reduction

  1. Reducing Aqueous Secretion: $\beta$ blockers, $\alpha_2$ agonists, Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors.
  2. Increasing Trabecular Outflow: Miotics (Pilocarpine).
  3. Increasing Uveoscleral Outflow: Prostaglandin analogues, $\alpha_2$ agonists.

Drug Classes

1. Prostaglandin Analogues ($PGF_{2\alpha}$)

First choice drugs for open angle glaucoma.

  • Latanoprost, Travoprost, Bimatoprost.
  • Mechanism: Increase uveoscleral outflow.
  • Advantages: High efficacy, once daily dosing, no systemic side effects.
  • Side effects: Iris pigmentation, eyelash growth, macular edema.

2. $\beta$ Adrenergic Blockers

Mechanism: Reduce aqueous formation (ciliary epithelium). No change in pupil size.

  • Timolol: Non-selective. Prototype.
  • Betaxolol: $\beta_1$ selective. Safer in asthmatics but less efficacious.
  • Side effects: Systemic absorption can cause bronchospasm, bradycardia.

3. $\alpha_2$ Adrenergic Agonists

  • Brimonidine: Reduces aqueous formation + Increases uveoscleral outflow. Neuroprotective?
  • Apraclonidine: Used for short term control of IOT spikes.

4. Carbonic Anhydrase Inhibitors (CAIs)

  • Dorzolamide, Brinzolamide: Topical. Reduce bicarbonate formation in ciliary body $\rightarrow$ reduce aqueous secretion.
  • Acetazolamide: Systemic (oral/i.v.). Used in acute cases/refractory cases. Systemic side effects limit long-term use.

5. Miotics (Cholinomimetics)

  • Pilocarpine: Contraction of ciliary muscle $\rightarrow$ pulls scleral spur $\rightarrow$ opens trabecular meshwork.
  • Use: Now 3rd line for open angle glaucoma. Definitive treatment for Angle Closure Glaucoma (to break pupillary block).
üö®

Management of Acute Congestive Glaucoma

Medical Emergency! Aim is to lower IOT rapidly to prevent vision loss.

  1. Hypertonic Mannitol (20%) or Glycerol i.v. (Osmotic dehydration of eye).
  2. Acetazolamide 0.5 g i.v.
  3. Miotic (Pilocarpine): Once IOT falls, to open the angle.
  4. Timolol / Apraclonidine topical.
  5. Definitive treatment: Laser Iridotomy.

Case Study 10.1

A 70-year-male with BPH was prescribed Tab. Terazosin 5 mg at bedtime. At night, he got up to pass urine, felt giddy and fainted. He recovered after being laid flat.

(a) Rationale of terazosin? Blocks $\alpha_1$ receptors in bladder trigone/prostate $\rightarrow$ relaxes smooth muscle $\rightarrow$ improves flow.

(b) Explanation for fainting? Postural hypotension ("First dose effect") due to blockade of vascular $\alpha_1$ receptors + pooling of blood.

(c) Prevention? Start with low dose (1 mg), take while lying down in bed, educate patient about posture.

‚ùì

Case Study 10.2

A 55-year-old lady with mild asthma used Timolol 0.5% eye drops for glaucoma. She developed severe breathlessness and wheezing (asthma attack).

(a) Explanation? Systemic absorption of Timolol via nasolacrimal duct $\rightarrow$ blockade of bronchial $\beta_2$ receptors $\rightarrow$ bronchoconstriction.

(b) Avoidance? Use cardioselective blocker (Betaxolol) or other class (Latanoprost). Compress inner canthus during instillation to reduce systemic absorption.
