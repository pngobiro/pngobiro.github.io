01

üè• Introduction

Heart failure is a progressive disorder in which the heart is unable to pump sufficient blood to meet the needs of the body.

  • Common causes of heart failure are artery coronary disease and hypertension. Others are; myocardial infarction, congenital heart anomalies, valvular heart diseases, and dilated cardiomyopathy.
  • In heart failure, the heart is impaired to fill or eject blood.
  • The impairment of the ability of the heart to pump blood depends on the side of the heart affected.
  • Right heart failure causes blood accumulation in the venous circulation leading to organ congestion and peripheral edema.
  • Other classifications of heart failure are;
    1. Systolic heart failure with reduced contractility and reduce ejection fraction.
    2. Diastolic heart failure with stiffening and reduced relaxation.
  • Left heart failures causes accumulation of blood in the pulmonary circulation and fluid in the lung.
  • A reduced cardiac output, causes reduced blood flow to the organs hence decreased blood flow to the kidneys, urine production is decreased culminating to fluid retention and more peripheral edema.
  • To compensate for reduced cardiac out put, the sympathetic nervous activity is elevated and renin-angiotensin aldosterone system (RAAS) is activated.
  • SNS stimulates the adrenal medulla to release noradrenaline and adrenaline which increases the heart rate and peripheral vasoconstriction.
  • RAAS is activated to correct poor perfusion of the kidneys. However, aldosterone released causes salt and water retention causing further congestion and edema.
  • Increased stretch of chamber walls leads to release of natriuretic factors; atrial natriuretic factor (atrial wall) and brain natriuretic factor (ventricular wall).
  • These/peptides decrease blood volume and blood pressure through; water and sodium excretion, and vasodilation of arterioles and venules.
  • At long run these compensatory mechanisms do harm than good. i.e the vasoconstriction increases the workload of the heart (afterload)>myocardial damage > more heart failure.
02

ü©∫ Pathophysiology of HF

Heart failure is a syndrome with many causes. Diastolic dysfunction occurs due to stiffening and myocardial hypertrophy. High output failure is common in hyperthyroidism, beriberi, anaemia and arteriovenous shunts.

Systolic dysfunction causes reduced cardiac output and reduce ejection fraction.

Decreased exercise tolerance and fatigue is consequence of reduced cardiac output.

Compensatory mechanisms occurs through sympathetic nervous system and renin-angiotensin-aldosterone response.

Increased sympathetic output leads to tachycardia, increased contractility and vascular tone.

üìä Classifications & Goals

10 New York HF Classification

  1. Class I- the patient is asymptomatic, no limitation to daily activities
  2. Class II- Slight limitation of physical activity (Mild HF); features of fatigue, dyspnea, palpitations or angina pectoris.
  3. Class III- Marked limitation of physical activity; ordinary activity causes symptoms (moderate HF)
  4. Class IV- symptomatic at rest; unable to carry any physical activity without discomfort (severe HF).

10 Goals in heart failure treatment

1.To Reduce the symptoms and slow HF progression. 2.Managing acute episodes of decompensated heart failure. 3. Decrease mortality and improve quality of life and survival . 4.To treat the cause if it can resolve.

12 Goals for pharmacological Intervention

  1. Reducing myocardial work load
  2. Decrease extracellular fluid volume
  3. Improve cardiac contractility
  4. Reduce the rate of cardiac remodeling

Strategy of Treatment

  1. Reduce the afterload (forward failure).
  2. Reduce the preload (backward failure).
  3. Improve contractility of the heart.
  4. Reduce fluid intake $ < 1.5 - 2 \text{ L/} $ day
  5. Reduce salt intake
  6. Treat comorbidities

Judicious use of diuretics and ACE-inhibitors 8. Use of inotropic agents only in acute HF

14 Classification of Drugs

  1. Diuretics
  2. Beta blockers
  3. Vasodilators
  4. Angiotensin converting enzyme inhibitors/angiotensin receptor blockers
  5. Cardiac glycosides (inotropic agents)
  6. Brain natriuretic peptide

Vasopressin receptor antagonist (aldosterone antagonist)

‚ù§Ô∏è Digitalis/Cardiac glycoside

  • Cardiac glycosides e.g. digoxin, digitoxin
  • Digoxin is obtained from digitalis lanata the foxglove white.
  • Used as second line therapy owing to their toxicity.
  • They act by influencing the movement of ions in and out of the myocardial fibers and alter activity of autonomic nervous system.
  • The ions most affected is sodium and calcium.
  • They have both positive inotropy and negative chronotropy.

Mode of Action of Digitalis

  1. Increase contraction of cardiac sarcomeres leading to increased free ca2+ hence increased cardiac contraction.
  2. Slow the rate and rhythm through slowing AV node conduction via vagal stimulation
  3. Increase force and contraction of the heart through inhibition of $\text{Na}^+/\text{K}^+$ ATpase pump, and increase $\text{Ca}^{2+}$ stores.
  4. Can affect other organs with smooth muscles like git, CNS,

The cardiac glycosides affect the movement of ions across the myocardial cell membrane and intracellular electrolyte levels (particularly sodium, potassium, and calcium) in four ways. 1 They inhibit the enzyme Na‚Å∫/K‚Å∫-ATPase, which powers the sodium‚Äìpotassium pump. 2 They affect sodium‚Äìcalcium exchange across the membrane. The net result of these effects is that sodium accumulates within the myocardial cell. The cardiac glycosides also elevate intracellular calcium levels by 3 openings membrane calcium channels, and 4 triggering the release of calcium ions from intracellular storage sites. The net result of the latter effects is that the force of myocardial contraction is enhanced.

Pharmacokinetics

  • Absorption is $ 65-80 \% $ after oral administration
  • Not fully metabolized in the human body
  • A third excreted unchanged in the urine
  • Half life is 36-40 hours.
  • They have narrow therapeutic index with only small difference between therapeutic dose and doses that are toxic

Pharmacodynamics

  • Inhibit Na+/K+ Atpase pump (sodium pump)
  • Inhibition of sodium pump leads to positive inotropy.
  • Increase heart muscle contraction by slowing conduction velocity through AV node. This is useful in atrial fibrillation
  • Neurohormonal inhibition‚Äîmechanism of action not well known. Low dose inhibit sympathetic activation with minimal effects on contractility. Used in HF with reduced injection fraction (ref)

Adverse Effects of Glycosides

  1. Nausea, vomiting and diarrhea
  2. Dysrhythmias
  3. Block of AV conduction
  4. Increased Ectopic pacemaker activity.
  5. CNS-hallucinations and visual disturbance
  6. Cardiac glycosides and potassium compete for the binding enzyme Atpase. Therefore, high levels of potassium reduce the effects of glycoside while low levels of potassium enhance the effects of these drugs.
  7. Gynaecomastia

Bigeminal rhythm (ectopic heart beat following a normal heart beat) 9. Incase of digitalis toxicity, Digibind (digoxin antibody) is given as an antidote.

üíä Other Pharmacological Agents

Diuretics

  • Act by increasing water and salt loss hence reducing the preload volume.
  • Reduced preload leads to improved cardiac function, reduction of peripheral oedema and decreased dyspnoea.
  • Loop diuretics can be given orally e.g. furosemide, bumetanide
  • Severe cases of HF give parenteral loop diuretics
  • Aldosterone antagonist can be added to loop diuretics to counter $\text{K}^+$ loss and improve efficiency of loop diuretics.

Vasodilators

  • Mixed arteriolar and venodilators reduce both afterload and preload e.g. sodium nitroprusside, calcium channel antagonist
  • Venodilatory predominance reduce preload;- nitrates
  • Arteriolar predominance reduce after load;- hydralazine, minoxidil
  • Vasodilators like hydralazine dilate arterioles leading to increased cardiac output.
  • Nitr√≥glycerin and sodium nitroprusside are given i.v in severe heart failure.

ACE-Inhibitors

  • They are the mainstay of treatment in heart failure.
  • Standard for treatment all types of HF
  • Inhibit conversion of angiotensin I to angiotensin II which is a potent vasoconstrictor.
  • They have shown to reduce the disease progression, improve quality of life and boost survival rate of people with NYHA class II, III and IV.

25 ACE-Inhibitors

  • Decrease vascular resistance leading to increased stroke volume and improved tissue perfusion
  • Decrease aldosterone production leading to sodium and water loss reducing the preload volume.
  • Reverse ventricular hypertrophy and remodeling by reducing AII and aldosterone levels

ACEIs reduce mortality, hospitalization and slow disease progression. ARBS competitively block AT1 receptors on the heart, peripheral vasculature and kidney leading to similar actions of ACEIs.

ARBS are given to patients who can not tolerate the cough effect of ACEIs.

Beta-Adrenergic Blockers

Nebivolol, carvedilol, metoprolol and bisoprolol block the effects of catecholamines on the heart.

Improve left ventricular structure and function. Decrease wall stress and improve ejection fraction. Decrease frequency of arrhythmias Improve patient progress and reduce hospitalization.

29 Aldosterone Antagonist

Spironolactone and eplerenone block actions of aldosterone Reduce preload volume Prevent effects of aldosterone like remodeling and ventricular hypertrophy They are potassium sparing Used in conjunction with other drugs in severe heart failure

Natriuretic peptides

Nesiritide in combination with brain natriuretic peptide(BNP) are used in acute compensated heart failure.

Vasodilator and administered intravenously, hypotension being common.

Bipyridines

  • Inamrinone and milrinone inhibit phosphodiesterase enzyme-3 (PDE-3)
  • Increase contractility through calcium influx in heart during action potential.
  • Also have vasodilating effects
  • Side effects;- nausea, and vomiting, arrhythmias, liver enzyme changes and thrombocytopenia.

üìù Summary & Revision

Summary

  • Stage A- high risk with no symptoms.
  • Reduce risk factors, treat hypertension, diabetes, dyslipidemia, ACE inhibitors or ARBs in some patients
  • Stage B- structural heart disease with no symptoms.
  • ACE inhibitors, $\beta$-blockers in some selected patients
  • Stage C- structural heart disease and obvious symptoms or current symptoms
  • ACE inhibitors, $\beta$-blockers in all patients, aldosterone antagonist, sodium restriction, digoxin and diuretics.

A. Chloride

B. Sodium

C. Potassium

D. Zinc

Answer

  • The answer is C. hypokalemia can lead to life-threatening arrhythmias and increases the potential of cardiac toxicity with digoxin.
  • Because of reduced competition at the $\text{K}^+$ binding site on the $\text{N}^+/\text{K}^+$ ATpase.
‚ùì 2. Which best describes the action of ACE inhibitors in heart failure?

A. ACE inhibitors increase vascular resistance

B. ACE inhibitors decrease cardiac output

C. ACE inhibitors reduce the preload

D. ACE inhibitors increase aldosterone

Answer

  • The answer is C. ACE inhibitors decrease vascular resistance leading to decreased preload, decreased afterload, and increase cardiac output.
  • ACE inhibitors blunt/block aldosterone release.
