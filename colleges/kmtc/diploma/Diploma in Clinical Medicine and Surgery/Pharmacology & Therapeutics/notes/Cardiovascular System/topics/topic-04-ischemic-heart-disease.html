01

ü´Ä Ischemic Heart Disease

  • Major cause is atherosclerosis leading to reduced blood supply to myocardium
  • They are of two types;
    1. Angina (ischemic chest pain)
    2. Myocardial infarction.

Angina

  • Occurs when there is reduced oxygen supply to the myocardium
  • Characterized by pain arising from the chest, moves to arms, jaws and the neck.
  • The pain is triggered by exertion, excitement or cold.

Types of Angina

  1. Stable angina
  2. Unstable angina
  3. Variant angina

Stable Angina

  • Also known as typical/classical angina
  • Predictable chest pain on exertion/exercise.
  • Occurs when there is narrowing of the coronary arteries dues to atheroma
  • Pain occurs when there is increased demand on the heart.
  • Called stable angina because the pattern of chest pains and the amount of effort needed to trigger chest pain do not vary with time.
  • Treatment is directed to altering cardiac work with organic nitrates, betaadrenoreceptor blockers, calcium channel blockers and treatment of underlying cause.

Unstable Angina

  • Characterized by chest pain that occurs with less exertion.
  • Chest pain occurs with increased intensity, frequency and duration and precipitated by less effort.
  • Chest pain at rest.
  • A form of coronary syndrome therefore, it requires aggressive therapy to avert progressive MI and death.
  • Caused by ruptured atheromatous plague without occlusion of the vessel.
  • Giving aspirin aims at reducing the atheroma/clot.

Variant Angina

  • Also known as Prinzmetal, vasospastic or rest angina
  • Uncommon
  • Chest pain occurs at rest
  • Caused by coronary artery spasms.
  • Associated with atheromatous diseases.
  • Treatment is giving coronary artery vasodilators- calcium antagonist, organic nitrates.

Myocardial Infarction

  • Occurs when a thrombus blocks a coronary artery.
  • It is fatal and can cause death.
  • Causes mechanical failure of the ventricles or dysrhythmias.
  • Cardiac myocytes rely on aerobic respiration therefore, reduced oxygen supply leads to necrosis/apoptosis.
  • To achieve the reversal of this, this treatment is given; oxygen, opioids, $\beta$-adrenoreceptors, aspirin/clopidogrel and ACEIs.

  2. Drugs that affect cardiac function indirectly- nitrates, ACEIs, antidiuretics
  3. Calcium antagonist-indirectly relaxes the arterioles.

Thrombolytic drugs

  • Aspirin
  • Oxygen
  • Nitrates

üíì Antidysrhythmic Drugs

  • Classified according to electrophysiological effects
  • Was proposed by William Vaughan in 1970
  • Consists four classes;-
    1. a) Class I- drugs that block voltage sensitive sodium channels
    2. b) Class II- $\beta$-adrenergic receptor blockers
    3. c) Class III- prolong cardiac action potential
    4. d) Class IV- calcium antagonist
‚ñ∂ Mechanism of Action Class I
  • They block sodium channels like local anesthetics do.
  • Inhibit propagation of action potential in excitable cells by binding to $\alpha$-subunit.
  • Referred as membrane stabilizing activity
  • Reduce maximum rate of depolarization during phase 0 .
  • Are subdivided into class IA, IB and IC according to the effects on the sodium channel.
  • Class Ia- Quinidine, procainamide, disopyramide
  • Class Ib- lidocaine
  • Class Ic- flecainide, encainide
‚ñ∂ Mechanism of Action of Class II
  • Example is propranolol, atenolol, metoprolol
  • Block the effects of adrenaline on the AV node therefore reducing the sympathetic effects to the heart.
  • Prevent paroxysmal attacks of atrial fibrillation
  • Reduce or prevent dysrhythmias.
‚ñ∂ Mechanism of Action of Class III
  • Amiodarone, sotalol
  • Prolog the cardiac potential
  • Block some potassium channels involved in cardiac repolarization and the outward (delayed) rectifier.
  • Reduce dysrhythmia through increasing ca2+ entry during prolonged action potential leading to increased after-depolarization.
  • Can cause prolongation of QT-interval
‚ñ∂ Mechanism action of Class IV
  • Verapamil
  • Act by blocking voltage-sensitive calcium channels.
  • Acts on L-type calcium channels.
  • Slows conduction in AV and SN.
  • Shorten the plateau of the action potential and reduce force of contraction.

Undesired Effects

  • Disopyramide class Ia- causes dry mouth, blurred vision, urine retention and constipation.
  • Lidocaine class Ib- drowsiness, disorientation, convulsions
  • Class I c- not commonly used because of sudden death resulting from ventricular fibrillation after myocardial infarction

Propranolol (class II)- causes bradycardia, fatigue, bronchospasm in asthmatic patients and negative inotropic effects.

Amiodarone(class III)- half life time 10-100days, causes photosensitivity skin rashes, bluish/slate-grey discoloration of the skin, thyroid abnormalities, corneal deposits, pulmonary fibrosis

Class IV see side effects on calcium channel blockers.

üíä Unclassified & Inotropic Drugs

Unclassified drugs

  • Adenosine-
  • chemical mediator with effects on; breathing, cardiac muscles, platelets and afferent nerves.
  • Acts on AV node through A1 receptors.
  • Used to terminate supraventricular tachycardia (SVT)
  • Half life time is 20-30minutes
  • Theophylline and dipyridamole block the action of adenosine potentiating it.
  • Side effects;- shortness of breath, chest pain, dizziness and nausea.

Drugs that Increase Myocardial Contraction

  • Cardiac glycosides- Digoxin
  • Has a sugar moiety, a steroid and a lactone
  • Mechanism of action;- inhibit Na+/K+ ATpase pump and increase vagal activity.
  • Slow AV conduction through vagal stimulation hence reduce the rhythm and the rate.
  • Increase force of heart contraction through increasing transient $\mathrm{ca} 2+$ channels prolonging the action potential.
  • Effects of cardiac glycosides increase when potassium levels decreases.
  • Given orally or intravenously
  • Eliminated through the kidney
  • Side effects; nausea, vomiting, diarrhea and confusion.

Beta-adrenoreceptor inhibitors e.g. dobutamine have positive inotropic effects.

Glucagon- increase contractility through increase synthesis of cAMP.

05

ü©∫ Anti-anginal Drugs

  • They increase perfusion
  • They reduce myocardial metabolic demand.
  • Calcium antagonist and organic nitrates increase the perfusion and reduce metabolic demand through vasodilatation.
  • Beta-adrenoreceptor antagonist reduce metabolic demand through slowing the heart rate.

1. Organic Nitrates

  • Nitroglycerin (glyceryl trinitrate)
  • Isosorbide mononitrate
  • Relax vascular smooth muscles
  • Veno-relaxation reducing the preload.
  • Dilate the coronary myocardial artery
  • Reduce myocardial oxygen consumption through reduction of preload and after load.
  • Redistribution of coronary blood flow towards ischemic areas via collaterals
  • Reduce coronary spasm
  • Nitric oxide relaxes cardiac muscles.

Pharmacokinetics

  • Inactivated by hepatic metabolism
  • Well absorbed after oral administration as a sublingual spray
  • Produce effects within minutes
  • Isosorbide is taken orally twice a day for prophylactic purposes.

Side effects of Organic Nitrates

  1. Tolerance
  2. Postural hypotension
  3. Headache

2. Potassium channel Activators

  • Nicorandil
  • Combined with nitric oxide to activate potassium ATpase channels.
  • Vasodilates both arterioles and venules
  • Causes flushing, headache, and dizziness.

3. Calcium Channel Antagonist

  • Antagonize the L-type calcium channels.
  • Phenylalkylamines- verapamil
  • Dihydropyridines- nifedipine, amlodipine
  • Benzothiazepines- diltiazem
  • Vascular dilatation especially the arteries/arterioles hence used to reduce the coronary spasms.
  • Have cytoprotective effects in ischemic tissues
  • Side effects; headache, ankle swelling, flushing, constipation,

4. Sodium Channel Blockers

- Ranolazine

  • Inhibit the late phase of the sodium current.
  • Improve oxygen and demand equation.
  • Reduce intracellular sodium and calcium overload hence improving diastolic function.
  • Poses both antianginal and antiarrhythmic properties.

5. Beta-adrenergic Antagonist

  • Decrease oxygen demand by the myocardium by blocking the $\beta 1$ receptors.
  • This causes reduction of the heart rate, contractility, cardiac output and blood pressure.
  • Reduce oxygen myocardial demand during exertion and rest.
  • Not used in vasospastic angina
