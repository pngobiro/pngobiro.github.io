Drugs used in Coagulation Disorders

01

ü©∫ Introduction

  • Hemostasis is important in clot formation to prevent blood loss.
  • However, there can be situations in the body there is formation of unwanted clot (thrombus) within the blood vessels. This is called hypercoagulable state.
  • Conditions like atherosclerosis, bedridden for long time and major surgeries can lead to hypercoagulable state (clot formation).
  • Also, blood may fail to form a clot in disorders like haemophilia A, deficiency of vitamin K which may warrant platelet transfusion or supplement of vitamin-K.
  • Rudolph Virchow defined three predisposing factors leading to abnormal clot formation within the blood vessels (commonly known as Virchow's triad)
  • The Virchow's triad consists of; vessel wall injury, altered blood flow and hypercoagulability state.
  • Drugs acting on hemostasis can act at one of the following points;
    ¬ª Blood coagulation (fibrin formation)
    ¬ª Platelet function
    ¬ª Fibrin removal (fibrinolysis)
02

üîÑ Embolus Vs. Thrombus

  • Thrombus- a clot that is adherent to the wall of the blood vessel
  • Embolus- a clot floating on blood and can be carried away causing blockage of blood flow.
  • A thrombus can detached from the wall of the blood vessel hence becomes an emboli
03

‚õìÔ∏è Coagulation Cascade

  • Coagulation is conversion of liquid blood to a clot.
  • The end results is conversion of soluble fibrinogen to insoluble strands of fibrin.
  • Blood clotting factors are inactive in blood but when the are activated they are designated by the suffix "a" e.g. VIIa, Xa, Xia, thrombin (IIa) etc.
  • When one factor is activated, it catalyses the formation of another factor in larger amount and so on.
  • Vascular endothelium and antithrombin III are the inhibitors of cascade formation.
  • There are two pathways of coagulation cascade; intrinsic pathway and extrinsic pathway.
  • Intrinsic pathway occurs in the blood vessels while extrinsic occurs when the tissue is injured and is initiated by the tissue factor (prothrombin)

The role of thrombin

  1. Thrombin (factor IIa) cleaves fibrinogen to form fibrin.
  2. Activates factor XIIIa a fibrinoligase which strengthens fibrin to fibrin linkages.
  3. It activates platelet aggregation.

üíä Specific Pharmacological Agents

1. Vitamin K

  • K stands for Koagulation in German
  • Fat-soluble vitamin
  • Essential for formation of clotting factor II, VII, IX and X.
  • Pharmacokinetics; naturally occurring vitamin K1(Phytomenadione) is given orally or by injection.
  • Vitamin K requires bile salts for absorption.
  • Synthetic vitamin K (menadiol sodium phosphate) it is water soluble therefore, do not require bile salts for absorption.
  • Synthetic vitamin K takes long to act that the natural one.

Clinical use of Vitamin K

  1. Treatment and/or prevention of bleeding like excessive oral anticoagulant like warfarin, hemorrhagic disease of the newborn
  2. Vitamin K deficiencies- obstructive jaundice, coeliac disease, steatorrhea.

Side effects of vitamin K

  1. Hypersensitivity reactions
  2. Skin reaction

2. Tranexamic acid & Aminocaproic acid

  • Used in fibrinolysis disorders
  • Control bleeding
  • Active orally, excreted in urine
  • Inhibit plasminogen activation
  • Tranexamic acid is 10 times potent than aminocaproic acid.
  • Side effect; intravascular thrombosis

3. Protamine sulfate

  • Derived from fish sperms or testes.
  • Rich in arginine
  • Antagonizes effects of anticoagulant heparin.
  • The positively charged protamine interacts with negative charged heparin.
  • Side effects; hypersensitivity reaction, bradycardia, hypotension, dyspnea, flushing
07

üõë Platelet Aggregation Inhibitors

  • Decrease formation of platelet-rich clot. Or,
  • Decrease the chemical signals that activate platelet aggregation.
  • Adding different agents from different classes adds synergistic effects of these drugs.
  • Used to prevent occlusive cardiovascular disease and thrombolytic therapy.
  • These agents act at the following sites;
    1. Inhibit cyclooxygenase 1(COX-1) enzyme
    2. Block glycoprotein IIb and IIIa
    3. Block adenosine diphosphate receptors (ADP).

1. Aspirin

  • M.O.A- inhibits thromboxane A2 synthesis by acetylation of a serine residue on the active site of COX-1 hence irreversibly inhibiting the enzyme.
  • The chemical mediators are shifted to favor antiaggregatory effects of prostacyclin hence prevents platelet aggregation.
  • Aspirin is the only antiplatelet agent that irreversibly inhibit platelet function.

Therapeutic use of Aspirin

  1. Prophylactic treatment of transient ischemia
  2. Reduce incidence of recurrent MI.
  3. Reduce mortality in primary and secondary prevention of MI.
  4. Recommend dose is $50-352 \mathrm{mg}$ daily.
  • Pharmacokinetics; given orally, absorbed by passive diffusion, hydrolyzed into salicyclic acid in the liver.
  • Salicyclic further is metabolized in the liver and excreted unchanged in urine.
  • Half-life of aspirin is 15-20minutes, salicyclic acid is 3-12hrs.
  • Unwanted effects; increases incidence of haemorrhagic stroke, GI bleeding
  • Drug interactions; Ibuprofen block the action of aspirin compete for the same enzyme. Give aspirin 60 minutes before or 8 hrs after administration of ibuprofen.

Ticlopidine, Clopidogrel, prasugrel & ticagrelor

  • P2Y12 ADP receptor inhibitors
  • Block platelet aggregation
  • Mechanism of action different from aspirin
  • M.O.A- inhibit binding of ADP to its receptors on the platelets therefore, inhibit activation of GP IIb and IIIa.
  • Hence this blocks platelets from binding to fibrinogen.
  • Ticagrelor binds to P2Y12 ADP receptors reversibly but others bind irreversibly.
  • Clopidogrel inhibit platelet function 3-5 days.
  • Therapeutic uses; prevent atherosclerosis, prophylaxis of thrombotic events, cerebral transient ischemic attack (TIA)
  • Pharmacokinetics; require loading dose for quicker antiplatelet effects, extensively bound to plasma proteins, ticlopidine absorption impaired by food.
  • Metabolized in the liver by cytochrome P450 (CPY), elimination is through the renal and fecal route.
  • Drug interactions; don't give clopidogrel with omeprazole and esomeprazole concurrently.
  • Side effects; agranulocytosis, thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura (TTP), aplastic anaemia, neutropenea, clopidogrel has few side effects
08

üíâ Indirect Thrombin Inhibitors

  • Exert their effect through anti-thrombin protein to prevent clot formation.
  • Example;
    1. Unfractionated heparin (high-molecular weight)
    2. Low molecular heparin
    3. Fondaparinux

Heparin

  • Heterogeneous mixture of sulfated mucopolysaccharides.
  • Binds to endothelial cell surface and a variety of plasma proteins.
  • Depends on anti-thrombin protein for its action.
  • Anti-thrombin inhibits factor IIa, IXa and Xa to stop clot formation.
  • The active heparin binds tightly to anti-thrombin and causes a conformational change in this inhibitor.
  • Heparin functions as a cofactor for antithrobin-protease reaction without being consumed.
  • High molecular weigh Heparin has high affinity for antithrombin inactivating all the 3 clotting factors.
  • The low molecular heparin inhibit activated factor X but has less effect on the thrombin.

Monitoring of Heparin

  • Close monitoring of heparin is essential due to its adverse effects.
  • Heparin is monitored by use of activated partial thromboplastin time (aPTT or PTT)
  • The common monitored is unfractionated heparin (UFH).
  • LMW-heparin is not monitored unless in patients who have renal insufficiency, obesity and pregnancy.

Toxicity of Heparin

  1. Bleeding - heparin needs close monitoring
  2. Allergy- heparin is of animal origin (bovine)
  3. Osteoporosis- pathological fractures in long term use
  4. Loss of hair and reversible alopecia.
  5. Accelerates clearance of postprandial lipemia through release of lipoprotein lipase from tissues.
  6. Mineralocorticoid deficiency in long term use.
  7. Heparin induced thrombocytopenia- systemic hypercoagulable state (thrombus formation). Treatment; stop heparin, administer direct thrombin inhibitor.
‚ö†Ô∏è Contraindications of Heparin

(1) Patients who recently had surgery of the brain, spinal cord, eye

(2) Lumber puncture patients

(3) Thrombocytopenia

(4) Hemophilia A

(5) Heparin induced thrombocytopenia (HIT)

(6) Allergic reaction to heparin

(7) Severe hypertension

8. Purpura

9. Infective endocarditis

10. Active tuberculosis

11. Threatened abortion

12. Visceral carcinoma

13. GIT ulcers

14. Renal or liver insufficiency

Administration & Dosage

  • Before administration of heparin, do baseline PT & aPTT.
  • Inquire about any bleeding tendencies from the patient.
  • Do aPTT 6 hours after administration of UFH.
  • LMW-heparin is preferred because there is no monitoring required.
  • Can be given through infusion or subcutaneous injection.
  • Never administer heparin intramuscularly - hematoma formation.
  • Dosage; 5000 units 8-12 hours subcutaneous injections
  • Prophylactic enoxaparin 30mg BD or 4 omg OD
  • Fondaparinux is dosed once daily.

Reversal of Heparin

  • Discontinue the heparin if bleeding occurs.
  • Excessive bleeding give antagonist protamine sulfate
  • For every 100 units of heparin remaining in the patient, give intravenously 1 mg of protamine sulfate.
  • Avoid excessive administration of protamine sulfate!!

üíä Rivaroxaban

  • Oral anti-thrombin agent that inhibit the formation of fibrin.
  • Used in treatment of thromboembolic disease
  • It selectively inhibit factor Xa in the coagulation cascade.
  • Inhibition of factor Xa prevents conversion of prothrombin to thrombin hence disrupt clot formation.
  • Side effects; gastrointestinal upset, anemia, hemorrhage, muscle cramping and syncope.
  • Uses- thromboembolism, thromboprophylaxis
10

üß™ Warfarin

  • Coumarin anticoagulant.
  • Antagonize cofactor vitamin K.
  • Monitored by international normalized ratio (INR).
  • Has narrow therapeutic index therefore, INR should be maintained at optimal range as possible.
  • Recommended INR is between 2-3.

M.O.A of Warfarin

  • Factors II, VII, IX & X requires vitamin K as a cofactor for synthesis in the liver.
  • Warfarin inhibit vitamin K epoxide reductase enzyme preventing regeneration of vitamin K.
  • Peak effects of warfarin is 72-96 hours. This is the time required to deplete the circulating pool of clotting factors.

Pharmacokinetic

  • Well absorbed after oral administration
  • Bound to plasma albumin hence do not cross into the BBB, breast milk and urine.
  • Drugs like sulfonamides can displace warfarin from plasma due to their greatest affinity to bind to plasma albumin.
  • Warfarin is metabolized by CYP450 isoenzymes therefore, some drugs can block its metabolism leading to ineffectiveness of this drug.

Therapeutic use of Warfarin

  1. Prevention and treatment of DVT & PTE.
  2. Stroke prevention
  3. Prosthetic heart valves
  4. Protein C and S deficiency
  5. Antiphospholipid syndrome
  6. Prevention of DVT formation in major surgeries like orthopedic or gynecological.

Adverse Effects of Warfarin

(1) Hemorrhage- stop the drug, give vitamin K

(2) Skin lesions

(3) Necrosis

(4) Purple toe syndrome

(5) Warfarin is teratogenic should not used in pregnancy.

Drugs Interactions

  • Those drugs that potentiate;
    1. Amiadorone
    2. Acute alcohol intoxication
    3. Fluconazole
    4. Metronidazole
    5. Sulfonamides
  • Those that cause attenuation;

    1 Barbiturates

    2 Chronic alcohol ingestion

    3 Dicloxacillin

    4 Rifampin
