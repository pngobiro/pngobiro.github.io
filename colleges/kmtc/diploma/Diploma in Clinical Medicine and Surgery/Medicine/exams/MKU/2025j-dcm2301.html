Section A: Multiple Choice Questions

30 Marks
1.
What is the most common type of anemia?
a) Aplastic anemia
b) Hemolytic anemia
c) Iron-deficiency anemia
d) Pernicious anemia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Iron-deficiency anemia

Explanation: Iron deficiency is the leading cause of anemia worldwide, often due to chronic blood loss or poor dietary intake.

2.
Hemophilia is primarily caused by:
a) Vitamin K deficiency
b) Platelet disorder
c) Deficiency of clotting factors
d) Liver disease
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Deficiency of clotting factors

Explanation: Hemophilia A is caused by Factor VIII deficiency, and Hemophilia B by Factor IX deficiency.

3.
Which test is commonly used to diagnose anemia?
a) ECG
b) Complete blood count (CBC)
c) MRI
d) Liver function test
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Complete blood count (CBC)

Explanation: A CBC measures hemoglobin, hematocrit, and red blood cell indices, which are essential for diagnosing anemia.

4.
Which vitamin deficiency leads to pernicious anemia?
a) Vitamin C
b) Vitamin D
c) Vitamin B12
d) Vitamin K
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Vitamin B12

Explanation: Pernicious anemia is an autoimmune condition that prevents the absorption of Vitamin B12 due to lack of intrinsic factor.

5.
Von Willebrand disease affects which of the following?
a) White blood cells
b) Hemoglobin
c) Platelet adhesion
d) Blood sugar
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Platelet adhesion

Explanation: Von Willebrand Factor (vWF) is crucial for platelet adhesion to the injured vessel wall and for carrying Factor VIII.

6.
Aplastic anemia results from:
a) Increased RBC production
b) Bone marrow failure
c) Excessive bleeding
d) Iron overload
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Bone marrow failure

Explanation: Aplastic anemia is characterized by pancytopenia due to hypocellularity (failure) of the bone marrow.

7.
What is a common treatment for iron-deficiency anemia?
a) Chemotherapy
b) Iron supplements
c) Radiation
d) Antibiotics
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Iron supplements

Explanation: Oral ferrous sulphate or IV iron is used to replenish iron stores.

8.
Which blood component is primarily involved in clotting?
a) Red blood cells
b) White blood cells
c) Platelets
d) Plasma
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Platelets

Explanation: Platelets (thrombocytes) form the initial hemostatic plug.

9.
A prolonged bleeding time may indicate a disorder in:
a) RBC count
b) Platelet function
c) Glucose metabolism
d) Kidney function
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Platelet function

Explanation: Bleeding time assesses primary hemostasis, which depends on platelet number and function.

10.
What is a common feature of Cushing syndrome?
a) Weight loss
b) Moon face
c) Hyperpigmentation
d) Bradycardia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Moon face

Explanation: Excess cortisol causes fat redistribution to the face (moon facies), upper back (buffalo hump), and trunk.

11.
Addison’s disease is characterized by a deficiency of:
a) Insulin
b) Thyroxine
c) Cortisol
d) Aldosterone only
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Cortisol

Explanation: Addison's disease (Primary Adrenal Insufficiency) leads to deficiency of both Cortisol (Glucocorticoid) and Aldosterone (Mineralocorticoid). Option C is the most diagnostic marker.

12.
Which of the following is a symptom of thyrotoxicosis?
a) Cold intolerance
b) Weight gain
c) Heat intolerance
d) Constipation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Heat intolerance

Explanation: Excess thyroid hormone increases metabolic rate, causing heat intolerance, weight loss, and diarrhea.

13.
Hyperpigmentation is typically seen in:
a) Cushing syndrome
b) Addison’s disease
c) Thyrotoxicosis
d) Hypothyroidism
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Addison’s disease

Explanation: In primary adrenal failure, low cortisol leads to elevated ACTH (and MSH), causing hyperpigmentation of skin and mucous membranes.

14.
A common cause of endogenous Cushing syndrome is:
a) Adrenal tumor
b) Iodine deficiency
c) Diabetes
d) Pituitary infarction
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Adrenal tumor

Explanation: While Pituitary Adenoma (Cushing's Disease) is the most common cause, Adrenal tumors (adenoma/carcinoma) are a common cause of ACTH-independent Cushing's syndrome. Pituitary infarction causes hypopituitarism.

15.
Which hormone is excessively produced in Cushing syndrome?
a) Insulin
b) Cortisol
c) Thyroxine
d) Estrogen
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Cortisol

Explanation: Cushing syndrome is defined by chronic exposure to excess glucocorticoids (Cortisol).

16.
The most common cause of Addison’s disease is:
a) Tuberculosis
b) Autoimmune destruction
c) Viral infection
d) Tumor
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Autoimmune destruction

Explanation: Autoimmune adrenalitis is the leading cause in developed countries. TB is a common cause in developing regions but autoimmune is generally cited as most common globally.

17.
What laboratory finding is common in Addison’s disease?
a) Hypernatremia
b) Hypokalemia
c) Hyponatremia
d) Hyperglycemia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Hyponatremia

Explanation: Lack of aldosterone leads to sodium loss (Hyponatremia) and potassium retention (Hyperkalemia).

18.
Graves’ disease is a common cause of:
a) Cushing syndrome
b) Addison’s disease
c) Thyrotoxicosis
d) Hypothyroidism
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Thyrotoxicosis

Explanation: Graves' disease is an autoimmune disorder causing hyperthyroidism (thyrotoxicosis).

19.
Exophthalmos is a clinical sign associated with:
a) Addison’s disease
b) Cushing syndrome
c) Thyrotoxicosis
d) Hypopituitarism
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Thyrotoxicosis

Explanation: Exophthalmos (proptosis) is a specific feature of Graves' disease (Thyrotoxicosis).

20.
Which of the following is the most common cause of acute liver failure in the U.S.?
a) Hepatitis B
b) Acetaminophen overdose
c) Alcoholic hepatitis
d) Hepatitis A
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Acetaminophen overdose

Explanation: Paracetamol (Acetaminophen) toxicity is the leading cause of ALF in many developed countries.

21.
Which hepatitis virus is transmitted through contaminated food and water?
a) Hepatitis B
b) Hepatitis C
c) Hepatitis D
d) Hepatitis A
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Hepatitis A

Explanation: Hepatitis A and E are transmitted via the fecal-oral route (food/water). B, C, and D are blood-borne.

22.
What is the hallmark lab finding in liver failure?
a) Elevated hemoglobin
b) Low AST and ALT
c) Elevated INR
d) High serum creatinine
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Elevated INR

Explanation: The liver synthesizes clotting factors. Failure leads to coagulopathy, marked by a prolonged Prothrombin Time (PT) and elevated INR.

23.
Which virus requires hepatitis B virus to replicate?
a) Hepatitis A
b) Hepatitis C
c) Hepatitis D
d) Hepatitis E
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Hepatitis D

Explanation: Hepatitis D is a defective virus that requires the HBsAg envelope from Hepatitis B to replicate.

24.
Which is NOT a typical symptom of hepatic encephalopathy?
a) Confusion
b) Asterixis
c) Seizures
d) Personality changes
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Seizures

Explanation: Classic signs are confusion, asterixis (flapping tremor), sleep reversal, and personality changes. Seizures are rare in HE (though possible in end-stage, they are not typical).

25.
Which lab abnormality is expected in liver failure?
a) Decreased ammonia
b) Hypokalemia
c) Hyperglycemia
d) Hypoalbuminemia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Hypoalbuminemia

Explanation: The liver synthesizes albumin. Failure leads to low albumin. Ammonia is typically *increased*. Hypoglycemia is common.

26.
Which hepatitis virus is most likely to become chronic?
a) Hepatitis A
b) Hepatitis B
c) Hepatitis C
d) Hepatitis E
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Hepatitis C

Explanation: About 75-85% of HCV infections become chronic. HBV chronicity is high in infants but low in adults (<5%). HAV and HEV are acute.

27.
What is the first-line treatment for acetaminophen toxicity?
a) Lactulose
b) N-acetylcysteine
c) Prednisone
d) Interferon-alpha
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) N-acetylcysteine

Explanation: NAC restores glutathione levels, preventing liver damage from the toxic metabolite NAPQI.

28.
Which is a common feature of cirrhosis and liver failure?
a) Hypercoagulability
b) Increased clotting factors
c) Portal hypertension
d) Increased bile production
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Portal hypertension

Explanation: Fibrosis increases resistance to blood flow, causing portal hypertension, which leads to ascites and varices.

29.
Which hepatitis virus has high mortality in pregnancy?
a) Hepatitis A
b) Hepatitis B
c) Hepatitis C
d) Hepatitis E
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Hepatitis E

Explanation: Hepatitis E carries a high mortality rate (up to 20-30%) in pregnant women, especially in the third trimester.

30.
Which of the following is a common symptom of anemia?
a) Weight gain
b) Fatigue
c) High blood pressure
d) Fever
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Fatigue

Explanation: Reduced oxygen-carrying capacity leads to tiredness, weakness, and fatigue.

Section B: Short Answer Questions

30 Marks (5 Marks Each)
1.
List five clinical features of acute peritonitis.
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Abdominal Pain: Severe, continuous, exacerbated by movement or coughing.
  2. Guarding and Rigidity: "Board-like" abdomen (involuntary muscle spasm).
  3. Rebound Tenderness: Pain upon release of pressure (Blumberg sign).
  4. Absent Bowel Sounds: Due to paralytic ileus.
  5. Systemic Signs: Tachycardia, Fever, Hypotension (Shock).
2.
Outline five common causes of secondary peritonitis.
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Perforated Peptic Ulcer.
  2. Perforated Appendicitis.
  3. Ruptured Diverticulum (Diverticulitis).
  4. Gangrenous Bowel/Ischemic Bowel perforation.
  5. Traumatic bowel injury (Penetrating trauma).
3.
Describe the initial management steps for a patient suspected of having generalized peritonitis.
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Resuscitation (ABC): Secure Airway, give Oxygen, Insert IV lines.
  2. Fluid Therapy: Aggressive IV fluids (Crystalloids) to correct hypovolemia/shock.
  3. Analgesia: IV opioids to control severe pain.
  4. Antibiotics: Broad-spectrum IV antibiotics (e.g., Ceftriaxone + Metronidazole) covering gram-negatives and anaerobes.
  5. Nasogastric Suction: To decompress the stomach and bowel (treat ileus).
  6. Urgent Surgical Consultation: For laparotomy/source control.
4.
Name five common causes of microcytic hypochromic anemia.
Show Answer

Answer:

Mnemonic: TAILS

  1. Thalassemia.
  2. Anemia of Chronic Disease (late stage).
  3. Iron Deficiency Anemia (Most common).
  4. Lead Poisoning.
  5. Sideroblastic Anemia.
5.
Explain five clinical symptoms or signs associated with severe anemia.
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Dyspnea: Shortness of breath, especially on exertion or at rest.
  2. Tachycardia/Palpitations: Heart beating fast to compensate for low oxygen.
  3. Extreme Pallor: Visible in conjunctiva, palms, and nail beds.
  4. Syncope/Dizziness: Fainting due to cerebral hypoxia.
  5. Angina: Chest pain due to myocardial ischemia.
  6. (Others: Heart failure signs, confusion).

Section C: Long Essay Questions

40 Marks (Answer TWO Questions - 20 Marks Each)
1.
A 30-year-old female presents with fatigue, dizziness, and palpitations. On examination, she is pale and has brittle nails. Lab findings show Hb: 7.5 g/dL, MCV: 68 fL, serum ferritin: low.

a) Discuss the diagnosis.

b) Differential diagnosis.

c) Causes and investigations.

d) Management of microcytic hypochromic anemia in adults.

Show Answer

Answer:

a) Diagnosis: Iron Deficiency Anemia (IDA).

  • Evidence: Low Hb (Anemia), Low MCV (< 80fL, Microcytic), Low Ferritin (Diagnostic of iron deficiency), Clinical signs (Brittle nails/Koilonychia, Pallor).

b) Differential Diagnosis (Microcytic Anemia):

  • Thalassemia Trait.
  • Anemia of Chronic Disease.
  • Sideroblastic Anemia.
  • Lead Poisoning.

c) Causes & Investigations:

Causes in 30yo Female:

  • Menorrhagia: Heavy menstrual bleeding (most common).
  • Pregnancy: Increased demand.
  • Dietary: Vegetarian/vegan diet with poor intake.
  • Malabsorption: Celiac disease.
  • GI Bleeding: Peptic ulcer, piles (less common in young female unless indicated).

Investigations:

  • Blood: Peripheral Blood Film (Pencil cells), TIBC (High in IDA), Serum Iron (Low).
  • Gynecological: Pelvic ultrasound (fibroids?).
  • GI: Stool for Occult Blood, Endoscopy/Colonoscopy (if indicated).
  • Screening: Celiac serology.

d) Management:

  • Treat Underlying Cause: E.g., OCPs/Tranexamic acid for menorrhagia, treat H. pylori.
  • Oral Iron Therapy: Ferrous Sulfate/Fumarate 200mg TDS for 3-6 months. Continue for 3 months after Hb normalizes to replenish stores.
  • Dietary Advice: Increase red meat, green leafy vegetables. Take iron with Vitamin C (orange juice) to enhance absorption. Avoid tea/coffee with meals.
  • IV Iron: If oral intolerant or malabsorption exists.
  • Monitoring: Check Reticulocyte count (increase in 1 week) and Hb (rise by 1g/dL every 2-3 weeks).
2.
A 12-year-old boy presents with recurrent nosebleeds, gum bleeding, and large bruises after minor trauma. He has a family history of similar symptoms in maternal uncles.

a) Describe the clinical features.

b) Diagnostic evaluation.

c) Management of inherited bleeding disorders. Include a discussion on Hemophilia A and von Willebrand disease.

Show Answer

Answer:

Diagnosis: Likely Hemophilia A (X-linked recessive pattern - maternal uncles affected, male patient). von Willebrand Disease (vWD) is also possible but typically autosomal dominant.

a) Clinical Features:

  • Hemarthrosis: Bleeding into joints (knees, elbows) causing pain/swelling (Hallmark of Hemophilia).
  • Muscle Hematomas: Deep tissue bleeding.
  • Mucocutaneous Bleeding: Epistaxis, gum bleeding (more common in vWD, but seen in Hemophilia).
  • Prolonged bleeding after cuts/surgery/tooth extraction.
  • Ecchymosis (Bruising).

b) Diagnostic Evaluation:

  • Coagulation Profile:
    • aPTT: Prolonged (Intrinsic pathway defect).
    • PT/INR: Normal.
    • Bleeding Time: Normal in Hemophilia, Prolonged in vWD.
  • Factor Assays: Low Factor VIII (Hemophilia A) or Factor IX (Hemophilia B).
  • vWF Antigen/Activity: To differentiate vWD.
  • Mixing Study: Corrects aPTT (indicates factor deficiency, not inhibitor).

c) Management:

General:

  • Avoid contact sports and NSAIDs/IM injections.
  • RICE (Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation) for joint bleeds.

Hemophilia A Management:

  • Factor Replacement: Recombinant Factor VIII concentrate (Prophylactic or On-demand).
  • Desmopressin (DDAVP): For mild Hemophilia A (releases stored Factor VIII).

von Willebrand Disease Management:

  • Desmopressin: First line for Type 1.
  • vWF/Factor VIII Concentrates: For severe types.
  • Antifibrinolytics: Tranexamic acid for mucosal bleeding.
3.
A 52-year-old male with a history of chronic alcohol use presents with abdominal distension, jaundice, and confusion. Physical examination reveals ascites, spider angiomas, and asterixis.

a) Discuss the pathophysiology.

b) Clinical features and complications.

c) Investigations, and management of liver cirrhosis.

Show Answer

Answer:

Diagnosis: Decompensated Liver Cirrhosis (Alcoholic).

a) Pathophysiology:

  • Chronic alcohol toxicity leads to hepatocyte injury, inflammation (hepatitis), and activation of stellate cells.
  • Stellate cells produce collagen, causing fibrosis and nodule formation.
  • This disrupts liver architecture, causing Portal Hypertension (increased resistance) and Hepatocellular Failure (loss of synthetic/metabolic function).

b) Clinical Features & Complications:

  • Stigmata: Spider angiomas, Palmar erythema, Gynecomastia, Jaundice.
  • Portal Hypertension signs: Ascites, Caput medusae, Splenomegaly.
  • Complications:
    • Hepatic Encephalopathy: Confusion/Asterixis (due to ammonia accumulation).
    • Esophageal Varices: Risk of massive bleeding.
    • Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis (SBP): Infection of ascites.
    • Hepatorenal Syndrome.
    • Hepatocellular Carcinoma.

c) Investigations & Management:

Investigations:

  • LFTs (Low Albumin, High INR, Elevated Bilirubin/AST/ALT).
  • Abdominal Ultrasound (Nodular liver, Ascites).
  • Endoscopy (Screen for varices).
  • Ascitic Tap (Cell count/Culture).

Management:

  • Lifestyle: Absolute alcohol cessation. High calorie/protein diet (unless severe encephalopathy).
  • Ascites: Salt restriction, Diuretics (Spironolactone + Furosemide), Paracentesis.
  • Encephalopathy: Lactulose (laxative to trap ammonia), Rifaximin.
  • Varices: Beta-blockers (Propranolol) prophylaxis, Banding.
  • Definitive: Liver Transplantation.
