Section B: Short Answer Questions

30 Marks (6 Marks Each)
1.
Outline the pathophysiology of peptic ulcer disease and list the common causes.
Show Answer

Answer:

Pathophysiology:

  • PUD results from an imbalance between aggressive factors (Gastric acid, Pepsin) and protective factors (Mucus, Bicarbonate, Prostaglandins, Blood flow).
  • Disruption of the mucosal barrier allows acid to diffuse back into the mucosa, causing inflammation and ulceration.

Common Causes:

  1. H. pylori infection: Bacteria produces urease and toxins that damage mucosa.
  2. NSAIDs (Aspirin, Ibuprofen): Inhibit COX enzymes, reducing protective prostaglandins.
  3. Lifestyle: Smoking, Alcohol (mucosal irritants).
  4. Stress: Physiological stress (burns/trauma) causing ischemia (Curling's ulcer).
  5. Hypersecretory states: Zollinger-Ellison syndrome.
2.
Outline the clinical features and management options for liver cirrhosis.
Show Answer

Answer:

Clinical Features:

  • Early: Fatigue, anorexia, weight loss.
  • Decompensated: Jaundice, Ascites (abdominal swelling), Pedal edema.
  • Stigmata: Spider naevi, palmar erythema, gynecomastia, caput medusae.
  • Complications: Hematemesis (Varices), Confusion (Encephalopathy).

Management Options:

  • Treat Cause: Stop alcohol, Antivirals for Hep B/C.
  • Diet: Low salt (for ascites), High protein (unless encephalopathic).
  • Medication: Diuretics (Spironolactone/Furosemide) for ascites; Lactulose/Rifaximin for encephalopathy; Beta-blockers for varices.
  • Procedures: Paracentesis (drain fluid), TIPS.
  • Definitive: Liver Transplantation.
3.
List the causes and risk factors for hypertension, and explain how it can be prevented or managed.
Show Answer

Answer:

Causes/Risk Factors:

  • Modifiable: Obesity, High salt intake, Sedentary lifestyle, Smoking, Alcohol, Stress.
  • Non-modifiable: Age, Family history (Genetics), African ethnicity.
  • Secondary: Renal disease, Endocrine tumors (Pheochromocytoma).

Prevention/Management:

  • DASH Diet: Low salt, high fruits/vegetables.
  • Exercise: Regular aerobic activity.
  • Weight Loss: Maintain normal BMI.
  • Pharmacotherapy: ACE Inhibitors, CCBs, Diuretics, Beta-blockers.
  • Cessation: Stop smoking and limit alcohol.
4.
Outline the pathophysiology of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) and its common symptoms.
Show Answer

Answer:

Pathophysiology:

  • Chronic inflammation of airways and lung parenchyma due to noxious particles (smoke).
  • Chronic Bronchitis: Hypertrophy of mucus glands -> mucus hypersecretion -> airway obstruction.
  • Emphysema: Destruction of alveolar walls -> loss of elastic recoil -> air trapping and impaired gas exchange.

Common Symptoms:

  • Chronic productive cough ("Smoker's cough").
  • Progressive dyspnea (shortness of breath on exertion).
  • Wheezing and chest tightness.
  • Frequent respiratory infections.
5.
Explain the different types of congenital heart defects and their common symptoms.
Show Answer

Answer:

Types:

  1. Acyanotic (L-to-R Shunt):
    • ASD: Hole between atria.
    • VSD: Hole between ventricles (Commonest).
    • PDA: Persistent connection between aorta/pulmonary artery.
  2. Cyanotic (R-to-L Shunt):
    • Tetralogy of Fallot: VSD, Pulmonary stenosis, Overriding aorta, RV hypertrophy.
    • Transposition of Great Arteries.
  3. Obstructive: Coarctation of Aorta.

Symptoms:

  • Cyanosis (blue skin/lips).
  • Poor feeding/Failure to thrive.
  • Dyspnea/Sweating during feeds.
  • Clubbing (chronic).
  • Heart murmurs.

Section C: Essay Questions

40 Marks (Answer BOTH Questions - 20 Marks Each)
1.
Discuss the pathophysiology, clinical presentation, and management strategies for heart failure. In your answer, include a discussion on diagnostic tests and treatment options.
Show Answer

Answer:

1. Pathophysiology:

Heart Failure (HF) occurs when the heart cannot pump blood effectively to meet metabolic needs.

  • Index Event: Damage (MI, HTN) decreases cardiac output (CO).
  • Compensatory Mechanisms (Maladaptive in long term):
    • SNS Activation: Increases HR and contractility (increases O2 demand).
    • RAAS Activation: Increases fluid retention (Preload) and vasoconstriction (Afterload), leading to remodeling and further failure.
    • Ventricular Remodeling: Hypertrophy and dilation.

2. Clinical Presentation:

  • Left Sided HF: Pulmonary congestion -> Dyspnea, Orthopnea, PND, Crackles, Cough.
  • Right Sided HF: Systemic congestion -> Pedal edema, Ascites, Raised JVP, Hepatomegaly.
  • General: Fatigue, exercise intolerance.

3. Diagnostic Tests:

  • Echocardiogram: Gold standard. Measures Ejection Fraction (HFrEF vs HFpEF).
  • BNP (Brain Natriuretic Peptide): Elevated in HF.
  • ECG: Check for arrhythmia/ischemia.
  • CXR: Cardiomegaly, pulmonary edema (Kerley B lines).

4. Management Strategies:

  • Lifestyle: Salt restriction (<2g/day), Fluid restriction, Weight monitoring.
  • Pharmacotherapy (The Pillars):
    • ACE Inhibitors (Enalapril): Reduce afterload/remodeling (mortality benefit).
    • Beta-Blockers (Carvedilol): Reduce sympathetic drive (mortality benefit).
    • MRA (Spironolactone): Blocks aldosterone.
    • SGLT2 Inhibitors: New standard.
    • Diuretics (Furosemide): Symptomatic relief of fluid overload.
  • Devices: CRT/ICD for advanced cases.
2.
Write an essay on tuberculosis, detailing its causes, clinical manifestations, diagnostic procedures, and treatment protocols. Also, discuss the complications of untreated tuberculosis.
Show Answer

Answer:

1. Introduction & Cause:

Tuberculosis (TB) is a chronic infectious disease caused by the bacillus Mycobacterium tuberculosis. It primarily affects the lungs (Pulmonary TB) but can affect any organ (Extrapulmonary TB). Transmission is via airborne droplets.

2. Clinical Manifestations:

  • Constitutional: Low-grade fever (evening rise), Drenching night sweats, Anorexia, Weight loss, Fatigue.
  • Pulmonary: Chronic cough (>2 weeks), Sputum production (mucoid/purulent), Haemoptysis, Chest pain.
  • Extrapulmonary: Depends on site (e.g., Lymphadenopathy, Back pain in Pott's spine, Meningism in TB meningitis).

3. Diagnostic Procedures:

  • GeneXpert (Xpert MTB/RIF): Rapid molecular test detecting DNA and Rifampicin resistance (Standard of Care).
  • Sputum Microscopy (ZN Stain): Detects Acid Fast Bacilli (AFB).
  • Chest X-Ray: Shows apical consolidation, cavitation, or pleural effusion.
  • Culture: Gold standard (LJ medium), but slow.
  • TST/IGRA: For latent TB.

4. Treatment Protocols (DOTS Strategy):

  • Intensive Phase (2 months): 4 Drugs - Rifampicin (R), Isoniazid (H), Pyrazinamide (Z), Ethambutol (E). (Regimen: 2RHZE).
  • Continuation Phase (4 months): 2 Drugs - Rifampicin and Isoniazid. (Regimen: 4RH).
  • Total duration: 6 months for uncomplicated PTB. Longer for Meningitis/Bone TB.

5. Complications of Untreated TB:

  • Lung Damage: Massive haemoptysis, Fibrosis, Cor Pulmonale (Right heart failure), Bronchiectasis.
  • Dissemination: Miliary TB.
  • Extrapulmonary spread: Meningitis, Spinal collapse (Pott's), Addison's disease (Adrenal), Infertility.
  • Death.
