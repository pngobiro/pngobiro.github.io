Section A: Multiple Choice Questions

30 Marks
1.
Which of the following is a major cause of shock?

b) Excessive exercise

Correct Answer: a) Dehydration

Explanation: Severe dehydration leads to Hypovolemic Shock due to the loss of intravascular volume.

2.
Rheumatic fever is a systemic complication of:
a) Viral infections
b) Group A streptococcal pharyngitis
c) Mycoplasma pneumonia
d) Viral endocarditis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Group A streptococcal pharyngitis

Explanation: Rheumatic fever is an autoimmune reaction following an untreated Group A Beta-hemolytic Streptococcus infection of the throat.

3.
Which type of cardiomyopathy is most common?
a) Dilated cardiomyopathy
b) Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy
c) Restrictive cardiomyopathy
d) Arrhythmogenic right ventricular cardiomyopathy
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Dilated cardiomyopathy

Explanation: Dilated cardiomyopathy (DCM) is the most common form, accounting for about 90% of cases.

4.
Infective endocarditis most commonly affects which heart structure?
a) Myocardium
b) Endothelium of heart valves
c) Coronary arteries
d) Pericardium
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Endothelium of heart valves

Explanation: Infective endocarditis typically involves the formation of vegetations on the endocardial surface of the heart valves.

5.
Pulmonary oedema in heart failure is a result of:
a) Systemic hypertension
b) Left ventricular dysfunction
c) Acute kidney injury
d) Low cardiac output
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Left ventricular dysfunction

Explanation: Failure of the left ventricle causes backup of blood into the pulmonary circulation, increasing hydrostatic pressure and leading to pulmonary edema.

Correct Answer: b) Intracerebral haemorrhage

a) Sinoatrial node
b) Purkinje fibers
c) Left bundle branch
d) Myocardium

Correct Answer: a) Sinoatrial node

Section B: Short Answer Questions

30 Marks (6 Marks Each)
1.
Write short notes on Barret's oesophagus.
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Definition: A condition where the normal stratified squamous epithelium of the distal esophagus is replaced by metaplastic columnar epithelium (intestinal metaplasia).
  • Cause: Chronic Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease (GERD). Acid damages the lining, leading to metaplasia.
  • Diagnosis: Upper GI Endoscopy showing "salmon-pink" mucosa extending up from the Z-line, confirmed by biopsy (goblet cells).
  • Significance: It is a pre-malignant condition that predisposes to Oesophageal Adenocarcinoma.
  • Management: High-dose PPIs, regular surveillance endoscopy, or ablation for dysplasia.
2.
Outline the clinical differences between Crohn’s disease and ulcerative colitis.
Show Answer

Answer:

Feature Crohn's Disease Ulcerative Colitis
Site Any part of GIT (Mouth to Anus), often Ileum/Cecum. Colon and Rectum only (Starts rectum, extends proximally).
Pattern Skip lesions (patchy). Continuous inflammation.
Depth Transmural (full thickness). Mucosal/Submucosal only.
Stool Usually non-bloody (unless colonic). Bloody diarrhea with mucus.
Complications Fistulas, Strictures, Abscesses. Toxic megacolon, Cancer risk (high).
3.
Outline the clinical presentation of anaphylactic shock.
Show Answer

Answer:

Anaphylaxis is a severe, rapid-onset systemic allergic reaction. Presentation includes:

  1. Cutaneous: Urticaria (hives), pruritus (itching), flushing, angioedema (swelling of lips/eyes).
  2. Respiratory: Bronchospasm (wheezing), Stridor (laryngeal edema), dyspnea, hoarseness.
  3. Cardiovascular: Hypotension (shock), Tachycardia, Dizziness/Syncope.
  4. Gastrointestinal: Nausea, vomiting, abdominal cramps.
  5. CNS: Anxiety, "sense of impending doom", confusion.
4.
Outline three complications associated with gastrointestinal bleeding.
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Hypovolemic Shock: Due to massive blood loss, leading to hypotension, tachycardia, and organ failure.
  2. Anemia: Acute (normocytic) or chronic (iron deficiency) anemia causing fatigue and pallor.
  3. Aspiration Pneumonia: Risk of vomiting blood (hematemesis) and inhaling it into lungs, especially if consciousness is reduced.
  4. (Others: Renal failure due to hypoperfusion/azotemia from digested blood).
5.
Briefly outline the pathophysiology of Rheumatic heart disease
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Infection: Starts with Group A Beta-Hemolytic Streptococcus pharyngitis (Sore throat).
  2. Immune Response: The body produces antibodies against the bacterial M-protein.
  3. Molecular Mimicry: The antibodies cross-react with human tissues (Heart valves, joints, brain) that share similar structural epitopes.
  4. Inflammation (Carditis): This autoimmune attack causes pancarditis (inflammation of endocardium, myocardium, and pericardium) during Acute Rheumatic Fever.
  5. Chronic Valvular Damage: Repeated attacks lead to scarring, fibrosis, and calcification of heart valves (Mitral/Aortic), causing stenosis or regurgitation (Rheumatic Heart Disease).

Section C: Long Answer Questions

40 Marks (Answer BOTH Questions - 20 Marks Each)
1.
A 50-year-old male presents with a 3-day history of swelling, redness, and pain in his left calf. He reports that the pain started after a long-haul flight. Examination shows left calf swelling, warmth, and tenderness. Positive Homan’s sign.

a) What is the likely diagnosis? (1 Mark)

b) Explain the pathogenesis of the condition? (5 Marks)

c) What investigations would you carry out? (7 Marks)

d) Outline the management of the above patient. (7 Marks)

Show Answer

Answer:

a) Diagnosis: Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT).

b) Pathogenesis (Virchow's Triad):

Thrombus formation is driven by three factors:

  1. Stasis of Blood: Slow blood flow due to immobility (Long-haul flight). This prevents clearance of activated clotting factors.
  2. Hypercoagulability: Increased tendency to clot (can be genetic or acquired e.g., dehydration during flight, age).
  3. Endothelial Injury: Damage to vessel wall (though less prominent here, stasis and hypoxia can trigger it).

c) Investigations:

  1. Compression Ultrasonography (Doppler US): The non-invasive gold standard. Checks for non-compressibility of veins.
  2. D-Dimer Test: Highly sensitive. If negative, DVT is unlikely. Elevated in thrombosis (fibrin breakdown product).
  3. Coagulation Profile (PT/INR, aPTT): Baseline before starting anticoagulation.
  4. Full Hemogram (CBC): Check Platelets and Hb.
  5. Renal Function Tests (U&Es): To guide dosing of anticoagulants (LMWH/NOACs).
  6. Wells Score Assessment: Clinical risk stratification.

d) Management:

  1. Anticoagulation (Mainstay):
    • Initial: LMWH (Enoxaparin) SC 1mg/kg BD or Fondaparinux.
    • Maintenance: Oral Warfarin (Bridge with heparin until INR 2-3) OR DOACs (Rivaroxaban/Apixaban - no bridging needed). Treat for 3-6 months.
  2. Mechanical: Graduated Compression Stockings (once pain resolves) to prevent Post-Thrombotic Syndrome. Leg elevation.
  3. Analgesia: NSAIDs or Paracetamol for pain.
  4. Mobilization: Early ambulation is encouraged once anticoagulated (does not increase PE risk).
2.
A 30-year-old female presents with burning epigastric pain, intermittent for weeks. Relieved by food but returns hours later. Bloating and nausea. Frequent NSAID use.

a) What’s the most likely diagnosis? (2 Marks)

b) Discuss the role of Helicobacter Pylori in the pathophysiology. (5 Marks)

c) Describe the diagnostic approach and management. (8 Marks)

d) List possible complications of the condition. (5 Marks)

Show Answer

Answer:

a) Diagnosis: Peptic Ulcer Disease (Specifically Duodenal Ulcer).

  • Reasoning: "Burning epigastric pain", "Relieved by food" (Hunger pain characteristic of DU), NSAID use is a risk factor.

b) Role of H. Pylori:

  • H. pylori is a gram-negative bacterium that colonizes the gastric antrum.
  • It produces Urease, converting urea to ammonia, which neutralizes acid around it, allowing survival.
  • It secretes cytotoxins (CagA, VacA) causing mucosal inflammation and damage.
  • It inhibits Somatostatin (D-cells), leading to increased Gastrin and Hyperacidity, which ulcerates the duodenum.

c) Diagnosis and Management:

Diagnosis:

  • Upper GI Endoscopy (OGD): Gold standard to visualize ulcer.
  • H. Pylori Testing: Urea Breath Test, Stool Antigen Test, or Rapid Urease Test (during biopsy).

Management:

  • Stop NSAIDs: Crucial to allow healing.
  • H. Pylori Eradication (Triple Therapy): PPI (Omeprazole) + Clarithromycin + Amoxicillin (or Metronidazole) for 14 days.
  • Acid Suppression: Continue PPI for 4-8 weeks to ensure healing.
  • Lifestyle: Avoid smoking, alcohol, and spicy foods.

d) Complications:

  1. Perforation: Ulcer eats through wall -> Peritonitis (Surgical emergency).
  2. Upper GI Bleeding: Erosion into vessel -> Hematemesis/Melena.
  3. Gastric Outlet Obstruction: Edema/Scarring at pylorus -> Vomiting.
  4. Penetration: Ulcer tunnels into adjacent organ (pancreas).
  5. Malignancy: (Mainly for gastric ulcers, less for duodenal).
