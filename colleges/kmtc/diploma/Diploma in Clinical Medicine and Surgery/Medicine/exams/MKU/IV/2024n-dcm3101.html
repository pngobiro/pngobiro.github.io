Section A: Short Answer Questions

30 Marks (6 Marks Each)
1.
Name the features of a migraine headache.
Show Answer

Answer:

Migraine features can be remembered by the mnemonic POUND:

  • Pulsating (throbbing) quality.
  • One-day duration (lasts 4-72 hours).
  • Unilateral location (one side of head).
  • Nausea or vomiting.
  • Disabling intensity (moderate to severe, aggravates with activity).

Other features include Photophobia (light sensitivity), Phonophobia (sound sensitivity), and Aura (visual disturbances).

2.
Outline the features of increased intracranial pressure (ICP).
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Headache: Worse in the morning or with coughing/straining.
  2. Vomiting: Often projectile and without nausea.
  3. Papilledema: Swelling of the optic disc (blurred vision).
  4. Cushing's Triad (Late signs):
    • Hypertension (widened pulse pressure).
    • Bradycardia.
    • Irregular respirations (Cheyne-Stokes).
  5. Altered Level of Consciousness: Drowsiness to coma.
  6. Cranial Nerve Palsies: especially CN VI (Abducens) palsy causing diplopia.
3.
Outline clinical features of hyperthyroidism.
Show Answer

Answer:

  • General: Weight loss despite increased appetite, Heat intolerance, Sweating.
  • Cardiac: Tachycardia, Palpitations, Atrial Fibrillation.
  • Neurological: Fine tremors, Anxiety, Irritability, Hyperreflexia.
  • GI: Diarrhea or increased bowel frequency.
  • Specific (Graves' Disease): Goitre (diffuse), Exophthalmos (protruding eyes), Pretibial myxedema.
  • Reproductive: Oligomenorrhea/Amenorrhea in females.
4.
Review the central nervous system in history taking.
Show Answer

Answer:

Key symptoms to inquire about during CNS review:

  1. Headache: Character, duration, red flags.
  2. Seizures/Convulsions: Type, loss of consciousness, post-ictal state.
  3. Dizziness/Vertigo: Spinning sensation vs lightheadedness.
  4. Visual Disturbances: Blurring, diplopia, loss of vision.
  5. Motor: Weakness (localized/generalized), paralysis, tremors, incoordination.
  6. Sensory: Numbness, tingling (paresthesia), loss of sensation.
  7. Sphincter Control: Urinary or fecal incontinence/retention.
  8. Higher Functions: Memory loss, confusion, speech difficulties (aphasia/dysarthria).
5.
Outline the clinical features of Acute Myeloid Leukemia (AML).
Show Answer

Answer:

Features result from bone marrow failure (pancytopenia) and tissue infiltration:

  1. Anemia: Fatigue, pallor, shortness of breath.
  2. Thrombocytopenia: Bleeding gums, epistaxis, petechiae, ecchymoses (bruising).
  3. Neutropenia: Recurrent or severe infections, fever.
  4. Tissue Infiltration:
    • Gum hypertrophy (specifically in AML M4/M5 subtypes).
    • Bone pain.
    • Hepatosplenomegaly (less common than in ALL/CML).
    • Chloromas (skin nodules).

Section B: Long Essay Questions

40 Marks (Answer ALL - 10 Marks Each)
1.
Describe the Glasgow coma scale for assessment of level of consciousness.
Show Answer

Answer:

The GCS is scored between 3 (Deep Coma) and 15 (Fully Alert). It assesses three parameters:

Response Score Description
Eye Opening (E) 4 Spontaneous eye opening.
3 To speech/command.
2 To pain only.
1 None.
Verbal Response (V) 5 Oriented (Time, Place, Person).
4 Confused conversation.
3 Inappropriate words.
2 Incomprehensible sounds (groaning).
1 None.
Motor Response (M) 6 Obeys commands.
5 Localizes pain (moves hand to stimulus).
4 Withdraws from pain (flexion withdrawal).
3 Abnormal flexion (Decorticate posture).
2 Extension (Decerebrate posture).
1 None (Flaccid).

Interpretation: Mild (13-15), Moderate (9-12), Severe (≤8 - requires intubation).

2.
Discuss TB meningitis under the following headings: a) Cerebrospinal analysis findings b) Management.
Show Answer

Answer:

a) CSF Analysis Findings in TBM:

  • Appearance: Clear or slightly turbid ("Cobweb" clot may form on standing).
  • Pressure: Elevated.
  • WBC Count: Elevated (usually 10-500 cells/µL). Predominantly Lymphocytes.
  • Protein: Significantly Elevated (often > 100 mg/dL).
  • Glucose: Low (< 40 mg/dL or < 50% of blood glucose).
  • Staining: ZN stain may show AFB (low sensitivity). GeneXpert on CSF is more sensitive.

b) Management:

  • Anti-TB Chemotherapy (Intensive Phase - 2 Months):
    • Rifampicin, Isoniazid, Pyrazinamide, Ethambutol (RHZE).
    • Note: Streptomycin may be used instead of Ethambutol for better CNS penetration in some guidelines.
  • Continuation Phase (10 Months):
    • Rifampicin and Isoniazid (RH). Total treatment duration is 12 months.
  • Corticosteroids (Adjunctive):
    • Dexamethasone or Prednisolone should be started immediately (tapered over 6-8 weeks) to reduce cerebral edema and mortality.
  • Supportive Care: Manage seizures, raised ICP (Mannitol), and nutrition.
3.
Classify cerebral palsy.
Show Answer

Answer:

Cerebral Palsy (CP) is classified based on the type of motor impairment and the distribution of limb involvement.

1. Physiological Classification (Type of Movement Disorder):

  • Spastic (70-80%): Increased muscle tone, hyperreflexia, "clasp-knife" rigidity. Damage to Pyramidal tracts/Cortex.
  • Dyskinetic (Athetoid/Choreoathetoid): Involuntary, uncontrolled, writhing movements. Damage to Basal Ganglia.
  • Ataxic: Poor balance, coordination, wide-based gait. Damage to Cerebellum.
  • Mixed: Combination of types (commonly Spastic + Dyskinetic).

2. Topographical Classification (Limb Involvement):

  • Monoplegia: One limb affected (rare).
  • Hemiplegia: One side of the body (arm > leg).
  • Diplegia: Both sides affected, legs worse than arms (common in prematurity).
  • Quadriplegia: All four limbs affected equally/severely (often with bulbar issues).
4.
Explain the process of GIT decontamination in poisoning.
Show Answer

Answer:

GIT decontamination aims to reduce absorption of ingested toxins. Methods include:

  1. Activated Charcoal (Single Dose):
    • Mechanism: Adsorbs toxins in the stomach, preventing absorption.
    • Indication: Within 1 hour of ingestion of adsorbable toxins (e.g., Aspirin, Paracetamol).
    • Contraindications: Unprotected airway, bowel obstruction, non-adsorbable agents (Iron, Lithium, Acids/Alkalis).
  2. Gastric Lavage ("Stomach Pumping"):
    • Procedure: Insertion of large-bore orogastric tube to wash out stomach contents with water/saline.
    • Indication: Life-threatening ingestion presenting within 1 hour.
    • Risks: Aspiration, esophageal perforation. Rare use now.
  3. Whole Bowel Irrigation:
    • Substance: Polyethylene Glycol (PEG) via NGT.
    • Indication: Sustained-release drugs, body packers, Iron/Lithium.
  4. Emesis (Ipecac):
    • Note: No longer recommended due to risk of aspiration and delay of activated charcoal.
