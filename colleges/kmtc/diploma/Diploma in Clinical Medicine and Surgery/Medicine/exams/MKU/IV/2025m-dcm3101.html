Section A: Multiple Choice Questions

30 Marks (1 Mark Each)
1.
Which of the following is a risk factor for recurrent Urinary Tract Infections?
a) Diabetes mellitus
b) Regular exercise
c) High fluid intake
d) Vegetarian diet
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Diabetes mellitus

Explanation: Diabetes mellitus increases the risk of UTIs due to glycosuria (sugar in urine promotes bacterial growth), impaired immune function, and potential bladder neuropathy (retention).

2.
The primary causative organism of Urinary Tract Infections is:
a) Staphylococcus aureus
b) Escherichia coli
c) Pseudomonas aeruginosa
d) Streptococcus pyogenes
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Escherichia coli

Explanation: E. coli is responsible for 80-90% of uncomplicated community-acquired UTIs.

3.
Which STI is caused by Treponema pallidum?
a) Gonorrhoea
b) Syphilis
c) Chlamydia
d) Herpes
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Syphilis

Explanation: Treponema pallidum is the spirochete bacterium that causes Syphilis.

4.
The most common clinical presentation of gonorrhoea in males is:
a) Penile discharge
b) Rash
c) Haematuria
d) Priapism
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Penile discharge

Explanation: Gonorrhea in males typically presents as acute urethritis with a profuse, purulent (yellow/green) penile discharge and dysuria.

5.
Nephrotic syndrome is characterized by all EXCEPT:
a) Proteinuria
b) Hyperlipidaemia
c) Haematuria
d) Oedema
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Haematuria

Explanation: Nephrotic syndrome is defined by massive proteinuria (>3.5g/day), Hypoalbuminemia, Edema, and Hyperlipidemia. Haematuria is a hallmark of Nephritic syndrome.

6.
Which of the following best describes acute renal failure?
a) Gradual decline in renal function over years
b) Sudden and severe loss of kidney function
c) Formation of kidney stones
d) Chronic inflammation of the kidneys
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Sudden and severe loss of kidney function

Explanation: Acute Kidney Injury (AKR/ARF) is characterized by a rapid (hours to days) decline in GFR and accumulation of nitrogenous waste.

7.
Which test is used to confirm the presence of renal calculi?
a) MRI
b) X-ray KUB
c) Echocardiogram
d) Colonoscopy
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) X-ray KUB

Explanation: KUB (Kidney, Ureter, Bladder) X-ray detects radio-opaque stones (calcium). Non-contrast CT KUB is the gold standard.

8.
A patient with urine retention is most likely to present with:
a) Anuria
b) Polyuria
c) Incontinence
d) Nocturia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Anuria (or c - Overflow Incontinence)

Explanation: Acute retention typically presents with the inability to pass urine (Anuria in the context of voiding). Chronic retention often presents with Overflow Incontinence (dribbling). Given the options, Anuria (inability to pass) is a direct description of the acute event, though technically anuria means kidneys aren't producing. In clinical exams, "inability to void" is often equated to anuria relative to the toilet.

9.
The following are causes of chronic renal failure EXCEPT:
a) Diabetes mellitus
b) Hypertension
c) Acute pyelonephritis
d) Polycystic kidney disease
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Acute pyelonephritis

Explanation: Acute pyelonephritis is a reversible infection. While Chronic pyelonephritis can cause CRF, a single acute episode typically does not lead to chronic failure. Diabetes and HTN are the leading causes.

10.
Management of nephritic syndrome includes:
a) High-protein diet
b) Diuretics
c) ACE inhibitors
d) Both b and c
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Both b and c

Explanation: Nephritic syndrome (e.g., PSGN) presents with hypertension and edema. Management focuses on controlling BP (ACE inhibitors) and fluid overload (Diuretics). High protein is avoided in uremia.

11.
Osteoarthritis primarily affects:
a) Small joints of the hands
b) Weight-bearing joints
c) Spine only
d) Shoulders only
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Weight-bearing joints

Explanation: OA is a degenerative joint disease commonly affecting knees, hips, and spine (weight-bearing), as well as DIP joints of hands.

12.
Rheumatoid arthritis is an example of :
a) Infective arthritis
b) Autoimmune disorder
c) Degenerative disease
d) Metabolic disorder
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Autoimmune disorder

Explanation: RA is a chronic systemic autoimmune inflammatory disease.

13.
Which of the following is a hallmark sign of ankylosing spondylitis?
a) Synovial fluid accumulation
b) Bamboo spine
c) Gouty tophi
d) Swan neck deformity
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Bamboo spine

Explanation: Fusion of the vertebrae (syndesmophytes) gives the radiographic appearance of a "Bamboo spine".

14.
Gout is caused by the deposition of:
a) Uric acid crystals
b) Calcium pyrophosphate crystals
c) Hydroxyapatite
d) Cholesterol crystals
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Uric acid crystals

Explanation: Monosodium Urate (Uric acid) crystals cause Gout. Calcium pyrophosphate causes Pseudogout.

15.
The most common autoimmune disorder associated with systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) is:
a) Type 1 diabetes
b) Rheumatoid arthritis
c) Glomerulonephritis
d) Multiple sclerosis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Glomerulonephritis

Explanation: Lupus Nephritis (Glomerulonephritis) is a major and common manifestation/complication of SLE itself. Sjogren's and RA can coexist, but renal involvement is a hallmark feature.

16.
A diet high in purines is a risk factor for:
a) Osteoporosis
b) Gout
c) Osteoarthritis
d) Ankylosing spondylitis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Gout

Explanation: Purines break down into uric acid. High purine foods (red meat, shellfish, alcohol) trigger gout.

17.
Which of the following is the correct clinical presentation of uraemia:
a) uremic frost, pericarditis, haematuria
b) anorexia, convulsions, itching
c) oliguria, hypertension, Malena stools
d) bad breath, dysuria, hypotension
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) anorexia, convulsions, itching

Explanation: Uremic syndrome includes GI symptoms (anorexia/nausea), Neurological signs (encephalopathy/convulsions), and Dermatological signs (Pruritus/itching). Pericarditis is also seen, but Option B contains a consistent triad of systemic uremic signs.

18.
The primary treatment for osteoporosis involves:
a) Antibiotics
b) Bisphosphonates
c) NSAIDs
d) Antiviral therapy
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Bisphosphonates

Explanation: Bisphosphonates (e.g., Alendronate) inhibit osteoclast activity and bone resorption, being the first-line therapy.

19.
A 50-year-old woman presents with fatigue, increased thirst, and frequent urination. History of bone pain/fractures. Calcium 12.5 mg/dL (High), Low Phosphate, High PTH. What is the diagnosis?
a) Primary Hyperparathyroidism
b) Malignancy-related Hypercalcemia
c) Vitamin D Toxicity
d) Renal Failure
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Primary Hyperparathyroidism

Explanation: The classic triad of "Bones, Stones, Groans, and Moans". High Calcium + High PTH indicates Primary Hyperparathyroidism (Parathyroid adenoma). In Malignancy, PTH would be suppressed (low).

20.

a) Scurvy
b) Rickets
c) Osteogenesis Imperfecta
d) Hypothyroidism

Correct Answer: b) Rickets

Explanation: Vitamin D deficiency Rickets presents with skeletal deformities (bow legs, widened wrists) and biochemical signs of low Ca/PO4 and high ALP.

21.

24.

b) Stage 2

d) Stage 4

25.

a) Asymmetrical joint involvement
b) Morning stiffness lasting over an hour
c) Absence of joint swelling
d) Normal inflammatory markers
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Morning stiffness lasting over an hour

c) Chronic renal failure
d) Urinary tract infection

Correct Answer: b) Nephritic syndrome

Explanation: The Nephritic picture is dominated by Inflammation (Haematuria, RBC casts) and Hypertension (salt retention). Nephrotic is dominated by Proteinuria/Edema.

27.

Explanation: Osteomalacia implies "soft bones" due to defective mineralization of the osteoid (usually Vitamin D deficiency). Osteoporosis is low bone mass but normal mineralization.

Section B: Short Answer Questions

30 Marks (5 Marks Each)
1.
List the pre renal causes of acute kidney injury.
Show Answer

Answer:

Pre-renal AKI is caused by renal hypoperfusion. Causes include:

  1. Hypovolemia: Hemorrhage, Dehydration (Diarrhea/Vomiting), Burns.
  2. Decreased Cardiac Output: Heart Failure, Cardiogenic Shock, MI.
  3. Systemic Vasodilation: Sepsis, Anaphylaxis.
  4. Renal Vasoconstriction: Drugs (NSAIDs, ACE inhibitors, Cyclosporine).
  5. Hepatorenal Syndrome.
2.
Justify the investigations carried out in chronic kidney disease.
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Serum Creatinine & eGFR: To stage the disease (Stage 1-5).
  2. Urinalysis: To check for proteinuria (albuminuria) and hematuria (glomerular damage).
  3. Renal Ultrasound: To assess kidney size (small/shrunken in chronic), exclude obstruction, and check cortico-medullary differentiation.
  4. Serum Electrolytes (K+, Na+, PO4, Ca): To monitor complications like hyperkalemia, hyperphosphatemia, hypocalcemia.
  5. Full Blood Count (Hb): To diagnose renal anemia (EPO deficiency).
  6. PTH Levels: To check for secondary hyperparathyroidism (Renal Osteodystrophy).

5.
Describe the metabolism of Vitamin D in the human body.
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Skin Synthesis: 7-dehydrocholesterol in skin absorbs UV light to form Cholecalciferol (Vit D3).
  2. Liver Hydroxylation: Vit D3 travels to the liver and is hydroxylated to 25-hydroxyvitamin D (Calcidiol) (main storage form).
  3. Kidney Activation: 25(OH)D travels to the kidney. Enzyme 1-alpha-hydroxylase converts it to the active form 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D (Calcitriol).
  4. Regulation: This step is stimulated by PTH and low Phosphate.
  5. Action: Calcitriol acts on the intestine to increase Calcium/Phosphate absorption and on bone for mineralization.
6.
Briefly describe the pathophysiology of Rheumatoid arthritis.
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Autoimmune Trigger: Genetic susceptibility (HLA-DR4) + Environmental trigger (Smoking/Infection) leads to citrullination of proteins.
  2. Immune Activation: T-cells and B-cells produce autoantibodies (Rheumatoid Factor, Anti-CCP).
  3. Synovitis: Immune cells infiltrate the synovial membrane, causing inflammation (Synovitis) and proliferation (Pannus formation).
  4. Destruction: The pannus invades and releases cytokines (TNF-alpha, IL-6) and enzymes that destroy articular cartilage and erode bone.
  5. Result: Joint deformity, pain, and loss of function (ankylosis).

Section C: Long Answer Questions

40 Marks (Answer TWO Questions - 20 Marks Each)
1.
A 60-year-old woman presents with progressive back pain and a recent wrist fracture (Colles') after minimal trauma. Height reduction and stooped posture noted. Menopause at 50.

a) What is the most likely diagnosis? (2 Marks)

b) What investigations would you carry out? (6 Marks)

c) Outline the management of the above patient. (7 Marks)

d) Give 5 differential diagnosis for the presentation. (5 Marks)

Show Answer

Answer:

a) Diagnosis: Post-menopausal Osteoporosis.

b) Investigations:

  • DEXA Scan (Dual-Energy X-ray Absorptiometry): Gold standard. T-score < -2.5 confirms Osteoporosis.
  • X-Ray Spine/Wrist: To check for vertebral compression fractures (wedging) and fracture type.
  • Serum Calcium, Phosphate, ALP: Usually normal in osteoporosis (excludes osteomalacia).
  • Vitamin D levels: To rule out deficiency.
  • Thyroid/Parathyroid Function Tests: Rule out secondary causes.
  • Serum Protein Electrophoresis: Rule out Myeloma.

c) Management:

  • Lifestyle: Weight-bearing exercise, Fall prevention, Smoking cessation.
  • Supplements: Calcium (1000-1200mg/day) and Vitamin D3 (800IU/day).
  • Pharmacotherapy (First line): Bisphosphonates (e.g., Alendronate 70mg weekly) to inhibit bone resorption.
  • Alternatives: Denosumab (RANKL inhibitor), Teriparatide (Anabolic - severe cases), SERMs (Raloxifene).
  • Pain Management: Analgesics for back pain.

d) Differential Diagnosis:

  1. Osteomalacia.
  2. Multiple Myeloma (pathological fractures).
  3. Metastatic Bone Disease.
  4. Primary Hyperparathyroidism.
  5. Paget's Disease of Bone.
2.
A 68-year-old man presents with fatigue, oliguria, and leg swelling. Recent history of severe gastroenteritis (diarrhea/vomiting). BP 85/60 (Hypotensive), Tachycardic, Dehydrated. BUN/Cr elevated, Ratio 25:1.

a) What is the most likely diagnosis (2 Marks)

b) Describe the pathophysiology of the condition named above (5 Marks)

c) Outline the management of the above patient (8 Marks)

d) List the complications of the condition (5 Marks)

Show Answer

Answer:

a) Diagnosis: Pre-renal Acute Kidney Injury (AKI) due to severe dehydration/hypovolemia.

b) Pathophysiology:

  • Severe volume depletion (Gastroenteritis) leads to renal hypoperfusion (decreased blood flow to kidneys).
  • Decreased Glomerular Filtration Rate (GFR).
  • Compensatory Mechanisms: Activation of RAAS and ADH causes avid reabsorption of Sodium, Water, and Urea.
  • This leads to oliguria (concentrated urine) and a disproportionate rise in Urea relative to Creatinine (High BUN:Cr ratio > 20:1). The kidney parenchyma is initially intact.

c) Management:

  • Fluid Resuscitation (Priority): IV Crystalloids (Normal Saline or Ringer's Lactate). Bolus followed by maintenance to restore BP and perfusion.
  • Monitor Output: Insert urinary catheter to monitor hourly urine output (target > 0.5ml/kg/hr).
  • Treat Underlying Cause: Manage gastroenteritis (antiemetics, treat infection if bacterial).
  • Stop Nephrotoxins: Withhold NSAIDs/ACEi/Diuretics.
  • Monitoring: Daily U&Es (Creatinine/Potassium) to check for recovery or progression to ATN.

d) Complications:

  1. Acute Tubular Necrosis (ATN): If ischemia is prolonged (progression to intrinsic AKI).
  2. Hyperkalemia: Arrhythmias.
  3. Metabolic Acidosis.
  4. Pulmonary Edema: If fluid resuscitation is too aggressive without output.
  5. Uremia: Encephalopathy, pericarditis (in severe prolonged cases).
