Section A: Multiple Choice Questions

30 Marks (1 Mark Each)
1.
Which of the following is the most common cause of peripheral neuropathy worldwide?
A. Alcoholism
B. HIV infection
C. Diabetes mellitus
D. Vitamin B12 deficiency
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Diabetes mellitus

Explanation: Diabetic neuropathy is the most common cause of peripheral neuropathy globally, affecting up to 50% of patients with diabetes. While leprosy is a major infectious cause in developing nations, among the options provided, diabetes leads the list.

2.
A "stocking and glove" distribution of sensory loss is characteristic of:
A. Radiculopathy
B. Peripheral neuropathy
C. Multiple sclerosis
D. Myasthenia gravis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Peripheral neuropathy

Explanation: This pattern describes length-dependent axonal polyneuropathy (distal symmetric polyneuropathy). The longest nerves are affected first, starting in the feet (stockings) and later the hands (gloves).

3.
Which of the following is a typical feature of Guillain-Barré Syndrome (GBS)?
A. Hyperreflexia
B. Ascending flaccid paralysis
C. Cranial nerve sparing
D. Slow progression over months
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Ascending flaccid paralysis

Explanation: GBS is an acute inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy characterized by rapid, ascending weakness (starting in legs and moving up) and loss of reflexes (areflexia, not hyperreflexia).

4.
Which vitamin deficiency is most associated with peripheral neuropathy?
A. Vitamin A
B. Vitamin D
C. Vitamin B1
D. Vitamin B12
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Vitamin B12

Explanation: Vitamin B12 (Cobalamin) deficiency causes Subacute Combined Degeneration of the spinal cord and peripheral neuropathy. Vitamin B1 (Thiamine) deficiency also causes neuropathy (Beriberi), but B12 is the classic answer for combined neurological deficits in exams.

5.
Which of the following investigations is most useful in confirming peripheral neuropathy?
A. MRI spine
B. Nerve conduction studies
C. CT brain
D. Blood glucose levels
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Nerve conduction studies

Explanation: Nerve Conduction Studies (NCS) and Electromyography (EMG) are the gold standard electrodiagnostic tests to confirm neuropathy, distinguish axonal from demyelinating types, and localize the lesion.

6.
Which drug is LEAST likely to cause peripheral neuropathy as a side effect?
A. Isoniazid
B. Vincristine
C. Metronidazole
D. Paracetamol
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Paracetamol

Explanation:

  • Isoniazid: Causes pyridoxine (B6) deficiency leading to neuropathy.
  • Vincristine: Causes dose-dependent neurotoxicity.
  • Metronidazole: Can cause neuropathy with prolonged use.
  • Paracetamol: Is an analgesic/antipyretic with liver toxicity risk, not neuropathy.

7.
In diabetic peripheral neuropathy, which symptom is typically seen first?
A. Muscle weakness
B. Loss of proprioception
C. Pain and burning in the feet
D. Visual disturbances
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Pain and burning in the feet

Explanation: While loss of vibration sensation is an early *sign*, the earliest *symptoms* reported by patients are often positive sensory phenomena like burning pain, tingling (paresthesia), or "pins and needles" in the feet.

8.
Areflexia and rapid progression of symmetrical weakness are typical of:
A. Bell’s palsy
B. Guillain-Barré Syndrome
C. Carpal tunnel syndrome
D. Charcot-Marie-Tooth disease
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Guillain-Barré Syndrome

Explanation: This is the classic presentation of GBS: Acute, symmetrical, ascending weakness associated with loss of deep tendon reflexes (areflexia).

9.
Which of the following is an inherited cause of peripheral neuropathy?
A. Guillain-Barré syndrome
B. Charcot-Marie-Tooth disease
C. Diabetic neuropathy
D. Chronic alcoholism
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Charcot-Marie-Tooth disease

Explanation: CMT (Hereditary Motor and Sensory Neuropathy) is the most common inherited neurological disorder causing neuropathy.

10.
First-line treatment for painful diabetic neuropathy typically includes:
A. NSAIDs
B. Gabapentin or pregabalin
C. Amoxicillin
D. Metformin
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Gabapentin or pregabalin

Explanation: Neuropathic pain responds poorly to NSAIDs. First-line agents are Calcium channel alpha-2-delta ligands (Gabapentin/Pregabalin) or SNRIs (Duloxetine).

11.
Which of the following best defines a seizure?
A. A sudden drop in blood pressure
B. A transient episode of abnormal electrical activity in the brain
C. A progressive neurological disorder
D. A psychogenic behavioral outburst
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. A transient episode of abnormal electrical activity in the brain

Explanation: A seizure is the clinical manifestation of an abnormal, excessive, hypersynchronous discharge of a population of cortical neurons.

12.
Which is the most common cause of seizures in children?
A. Head trauma
B. Brain tumor
C. Febrile illness
D. Stroke
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Febrile illness

Explanation: Febrile seizures are the most common type of seizure in children aged 6 months to 5 years.

13.
Which type of seizure is characterized by sudden loss of consciousness and stiffening followed by rhythmic jerking movements?
A. Absence seizure
B. Myoclonic seizure
C. Tonic-clonic seizure
D. Atonic seizure
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Tonic-clonic seizure

Explanation:

  • Tonic phase: Stiffening/rigidity.
  • Clonic phase: Rhythmic jerking.

14.
Which of the following is a feature of absence seizures?
A. Prolonged unconsciousness
B. Brief loss of awareness with staring spells
C. Sudden falling with no movement
D. Violent jerking of all limbs
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Brief loss of awareness with staring spells

Explanation: Absence seizures (Petit Mal) are characterized by brief (seconds) staring spells, often with blinking or lip smacking, and immediate recovery without confusion.

15.
Which imaging modality is most useful for evaluating structural causes of seizures?
A. Ultrasound
B. X-ray
C. CT or MRI of the brain
D. ECG
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. CT or MRI of the brain

Explanation: Neuroimaging (MRI is preferred over CT for detail) is required to identify structural causes such as tumors, vascular malformations, sclerosis, or scars.

16.
Status epilepticus is defined as a seizure lasting more than:
A. 10 seconds
B. 1 minute
C. 5 minutes
D. 30 minutes
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. 5 minutes

Explanation: While the traditional definition was 30 minutes, current operational guidelines define Status Epilepticus as a seizure lasting >5 minutes (for convulsive SE) or two seizures without full recovery of consciousness in between, requiring immediate treatment.

17.
Which antiepileptic drug is most commonly used for generalized tonic-clonic seizures?
A. Ethosuximide
B. Carbamazepine
C. Valproic acid
D. Lorazepam
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Valproic acid

Explanation: Sodium Valproate (Valproic acid) is a broad-spectrum AED and is the first-line choice for generalized seizures (tonic-clonic, myoclonic, absence). Carbamazepine is preferred for focal seizures. Ethosuximide is for absence. Lorazepam is for acute termination (status), not maintenance.

18.
A common adverse effect of phenytoin is:
A. Weight gain
B. Gingival hyperplasia
C. Hypertension
D. Hallucinations
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Gingival hyperplasia

Explanation: Gum overgrowth (gingival hyperplasia) is a well-known side effect of long-term Phenytoin use. Others include hirsutism and coarsening of facial features.

19.
In a patient with suspected seizure, the most helpful diagnostic test is:
A. EEG
B. Liver function test
C. Chest X-ray
D. Blood culture
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. EEG

Explanation: Electroencephalography (EEG) records brain electrical activity and is the specific diagnostic test for epilepsy classification.

20.
Which of the following is NOT typically associated with epilepsy?
A. Hypoglycemia
B. Sleep deprivation
C. Stroke
D. Hypertension
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Hypertension

Explanation:

  • Hypoglycemia: Metabolic cause of seizures.
  • Sleep deprivation: Common trigger for seizures.
  • Stroke: Structural cause of epilepsy (post-stroke epilepsy).
  • Hypertension: While it causes stroke, uncomplicated HTN itself is not a direct trigger or cause of seizures (unless Hypertensive Encephalopathy/Eclampsia).

21.
Which of the following is the most common type of stroke?
A. Hemorrhagic stroke
B. Ischemic stroke
C. Embolic stroke
D. Lacunar stroke
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Ischemic stroke

Explanation: Ischemic strokes account for approximately 85% of all strokes, while hemorrhagic strokes account for about 15%.

22.
Which artery is most commonly involved in ischemic stroke?
A. Anterior cerebral artery
B. Posterior cerebral artery
C. Middle cerebral artery
D. Basilar artery
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Middle cerebral artery

Explanation: The MCA is the largest cerebral artery and supplies the majority of the lateral surface of the brain (motor/sensory areas for face/arm, speech centers), making it the most frequent site of occlusion.

23.
The FAST acronym used in stroke recognition stands for:
A. Face, Arms, Speech, Time
B. Fever, Activity, Speech, Tremor
C. Facial droop, Alertness, Speech, Tremor
D. Face, Arms, Sensation, Temperature
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Face, Arms, Speech, Time

Explanation: Face (drooping), Arms (weakness), Speech (slurred), Time (to call ambulance).

24.
Which of the following is a modifiable risk factor for stroke?
A. Age
B. Gender
C. Family history
D. Hypertension
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Hypertension

Explanation: Hypertension is the single most important modifiable risk factor for both ischemic and hemorrhagic stroke. Age, gender, and genetics are non-modifiable.

25.
The most appropriate initial imaging study in suspected stroke is:
A. MRI brain
B. Non-contrast CT scan of the head
C. PET scan
D. X-ray skull
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Non-contrast CT scan of the head

Explanation: A non-contrast CT is the immediate first-line test to differentiate between Ischemic stroke (usually normal initially or early signs) and Hemorrhagic stroke (blood is white), determining eligibility for thrombolysis.

26.
Which medication is used acutely for eligible patients with ischemic stroke?
A. Aspirin
B. Clopidogrel
C. Tissue plasminogen activator (tPA)
D. Warfarin
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Tissue plasminogen activator (tPA)

Explanation: IV tPA (Alteplase) is the specific thrombolytic agent used to dissolve the clot in acute ischemic stroke if given within the therapeutic window (usually 4.5 hours). Aspirin is secondary prevention.

27.
A stroke affecting the left cerebral hemisphere is most likely to cause:
A. Left-sided weakness
B. Right-sided weakness and aphasia
C. Right facial droop and neglect
D. Bilateral limb paralysis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Right-sided weakness and aphasia

Explanation: The left hemisphere controls the right side of the body (motor decussation) and, in most people, contains the language centers (Broca's/Wernicke's). Thus, a left stroke causes Right Hemiparesis and Aphasia.

28.
Which of the following is a typical symptom of a cerebellar stroke?
A. Aphasia
B. Visual field loss
C. Ataxia
D. Hemiparesis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Ataxia

Explanation: The cerebellum coordinates movement and balance. Cerebellar stroke causes Ataxia (incoordination), vertigo, and nystagmus.

29.
Which of the following best describes a transient ischemic attack (TIA)?
A. Stroke with hemorrhage
B. Seizure following infarction
C. Brief neurological deficit resolving within 24 hours
D. Sudden memory loss due to embolism
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Brief neurological deficit resolving within 24 hours

Explanation: TIA is defined as a transient episode of neurological dysfunction caused by ischemia, without acute infarction, typically resolving in < 1 hour, but by definition < 24 hours.

30.
Which of the following is a common long-term complication after stroke?
A. Asthma
B. Hemophilia
C. Dysphagia
D. Nephrotic syndrome
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Dysphagia

Explanation: Dysphagia (difficulty swallowing) is a frequent complication due to damage to brainstem or cortical swallowing centers, posing a risk for aspiration pneumonia.

Section B: Short Answer Questions

30 Marks (6 Marks Each)
1.
List the modifiable risk factors of stroke. (6 Marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Hypertension: The most significant risk factor.
  2. Diabetes Mellitus: Increases atherosclerosis.
  3. Cigarette Smoking: Damages endothelium.
  4. Dyslipidemia: High LDL/Low HDL cholesterol.
  5. Cardiac Causes: Atrial Fibrillation (AFib), Valvular disease.
  6. Obesity and Physical Inactivity.
  7. Excessive Alcohol Consumption.
2.
Briefly with the help of a diagram outline the vascular supply to the brain. (6 Marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

The brain is supplied by two systems forming the Circle of Willis.

  1. Anterior Circulation (Carotid System):
    • Internal Carotid Arteries (ICAs) give rise to:
    • Anterior Cerebral Arteries (ACA) - Supply medial frontal/parietal lobes.
    • Middle Cerebral Arteries (MCA) - Supply lateral brain (face/arm areas).
  2. Posterior Circulation (Vertebro-Basilar System):
    • Vertebral Arteries fuse to form the Basilar Artery.
    • Supplies brainstem, cerebellum, and occipital lobes via Posterior Cerebral Arteries (PCA).
  3. Connecting Arteries: Anterior Communicating Artery (connects ACAs) and Posterior Communicating Arteries (connect ICA to PCA).

(In an exam, draw the Circle of Willis showing these connections.)

3.
Outline the components of Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS). (6 Marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

The GCS (Max 15, Min 3) assesses level of consciousness based on three responses:

  1. Eye Opening (E) - Max 4:
    • 4: Spontaneous
    • 3: To speech
    • 2: To pain
    • 1: None
  2. Verbal Response (V) - Max 5:
    • 5: Oriented
    • 4: Confused
    • 3: Inappropriate words
    • 2: Incomprehensible sounds
    • 1: None
  3. Motor Response (M) - Max 6:
    • 6: Obeys commands
    • 5: Localizes pain
    • 4: Withdraws from pain
    • 3: Abnormal flexion (Decorticate)
    • 2: Extension (Decerebrate)
    • 1: None
4.
Outline the causes of secondary headache. (6 Marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Trauma: Head or neck injury (e.g., Post-traumatic headache).
  2. Vascular: Stroke (Ischemic/Hemorrhagic), Subarachnoid Hemorrhage (Thunderclap), Temporal Arteritis.
  3. Intracranial: Tumors (Space Occupying Lesions), Abscesses, Raised/Low Intracranial Pressure.
  4. Infection: Meningitis, Encephalitis, Systemic infection (fever).
  5. Substance Use/Withdrawal: Analgesic overuse, Alcohol, CO poisoning.
  6. Metabolic: Hypoxia, Hypercapnia, Hypoglycemia.
5.
List the types of primary headache syndrome. (6 Marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Migraine: With or without aura.
  2. Tension-Type Headache: Episodic or chronic.
  3. Trigeminal Autonomic Cephalalgias (TACs):
    • Cluster Headache
    • Paroxysmal Hemicrania
  4. Other Primary Headaches: Cough headache, Exercise headache, Thunderclap headache (primary type).

Section C: Long Essay Questions

40 Marks (Answer ANY TWO - 20 Marks Each)
1.
A 6-year-old child is brought to your clinic by the mother who reports frequent episodes where the child suddenly stares blankly into space for a few seconds, sometimes followed by blinking or chewing movements. These episodes are brief and occur several times a day.

a) Describe the clinical features. (2 Marks)

b) Discuss diagnosis, and treatment of absence seizures in children. (10 Marks)

c) What are the differential diagnosis. (8 Marks)

Show Answer

Answer:

a) Clinical Features:

  • Brief staring spells: "Daydreaming" look lasting 5-15 seconds.
  • Automatisms: Eyelid fluttering, lip smacking, or chewing.
  • Immediate recovery: No post-ictal confusion; child resumes activity immediately.
  • Frequency: Can occur dozens of times a day.

b) Diagnosis and Treatment:

Diagnosis:

  • Electroencephalogram (EEG): The gold standard. Shows characteristic 3 Hz spike-and-wave discharge pattern.
  • Hyperventilation test: Asking the child to blow on a pinwheel for 3 minutes often triggers an attack in the clinic.
  • MRI Brain: usually normal, done to rule out structural causes if presentation is atypical.

Treatment:

  • Ethosuximide: The first-line drug of choice for simple absence seizures.
  • Sodium Valproate: Used if Ethosuximide is ineffective or if there are co-existing generalized tonic-clonic seizures.
  • Lamotrigine: Second-line alternative.

c) Differential Diagnosis:

  1. Focal Seizures with Impaired Awareness (Complex Partial): Often have longer duration, post-ictal confusion, and aura (unlike absence).
  2. ADHD (Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder): Inattention/daydreaming, but responsive to tactile stimulation (unlike seizure).
  3. Behavioral Daydreaming: Child can be interrupted/called out of it.
  4. Atypical Absence Seizures: Slower onset/offset, associated with Lennox-Gastaut syndrome.
2.
A 65-year-old man with a history of hypertension and type 2 diabetes mellitus presents with sudden-onset right-sided weakness and slurred speech. On examination, he is alert but has right hemiparesis and facial droop.

a) Discuss the types of stroke. (2 Marks)

b) Describe the diagnostic approach to this patient. (4 Marks)

c) Include the role of imaging, differentiation between ischemic and hemorrhagic stroke. (4 Marks)

d) Discuss the management and indications for thrombolytic therapy. (10 Marks)

Show Answer

Answer:

a) Types of Stroke:

  • Ischemic Stroke (85%): Caused by blockage (thrombus/embolus) cutting off blood supply.
  • Hemorrhagic Stroke (15%): Caused by rupture of a vessel (Intracerebral or Subarachnoid hemorrhage).

b) Diagnostic Approach:

  • History: Determine exact "Time Last Known Well" (critical for thrombolysis). Screen for risk factors (HTN, DM).
  • Examination: NIH Stroke Scale (NIHSS) to quantify deficit. Check Vitals (BP, Glucose).
  • Immediate Labs: Random Blood Glucose (rule out hypoglycemia mimic), Coagulation profile (INR), CBC, Electrolytes.

c) Role of Imaging:

  • Non-Contrast CT Brain: The primary initial test.
  • Role: To distinguish Ischemic from Hemorrhagic.
  • Differentiation:
    • Hemorrhagic: Shows as bright white (hyperdense) immediately.
    • Ischemic: Often appears normal in first few hours or shows subtle hypodensity (darker). The main goal is to *exclude* bleed to permit tPA.

d) Management & Thrombolysis:

General Management:

  • Admit to Stroke Unit.
  • BP Control: Permissive hypertension allowed in ischemic stroke (up to 220/120) to maintain perfusion, unless thrombolysis is used (then <185/110).
  • Antiplatelets: Aspirin 300mg stat (if hemorrhage excluded and no tPA).
  • Statins: High dose Atorvastatin.
  • DVT Prophylaxis, Swallow Screen.

Thrombolysis (IV tPA - Alteplase):

  • Indication: Acute Ischemic Stroke with significant deficit.
  • Time Window: Within 4.5 hours of symptom onset.
  • Contraindications: Hemorrhage on CT, recent major surgery/trauma, anticoagulation use (high INR), BP >185/110, previous intracranial bleed.
  • Mechanism: Dissolves the clot to restore flow (reperfusion).
3.
A 55-year-old man with a history of poorly controlled diabetes presents with numbness, burning pain, and tingling in both feet, gradually progressing over several months. On examination, there is reduced pinprick and vibration sensation in a stocking distribution.

a) Discuss the clinical features of peripheral neuropathies. (2 Marks)

b) Discuss its classification, and diagnostic approach to diabetic peripheral neuropathy. (4 Marks)

c) Outline the relevant investigations. (4 Marks)

d) Outline the principles of management, including both symptomatic relief and disease modification. (10 Marks)

Show Answer

Answer:

a) Clinical Features:

  • Sensory: Numbness, tingling (paresthesia), burning pain, loss of vibration/proprioception, sensory ataxia (unsteadiness).
  • Motor: Distal weakness, muscle wasting, foot drop, reduced reflexes.
  • Autonomic: Postural hypotension, erectile dysfunction, gastroparesis.

b) Classification & Diagnostic Approach:

  • Classification: Distal Symmetric Polyneuropathy (most common, "stocking-glove"), Mononeuropathy (e.g., Carpal tunnel), Radiculoplexus neuropathy (Amyotrophy).
  • Approach: Clinical exam using 10g Monofilament test (sensation), Tuning fork (128Hz for vibration), and Ankle reflexes. History of poor glycemic control supports diagnosis.

c) Investigations:

  • Glycemic control: Fasting Glucose, HbA1c.
  • Rule out other causes: Vitamin B12 levels, TSH (Thyroid), U&Es (Renal function), Serum protein electrophoresis.
  • Nerve Conduction Studies (NCS): To confirm axonal damage and severity.

d) Management Principles:

1. Disease Modification (Treat the Cause):

  • Strict Glycemic Control: Essential to slow progression. Target HbA1c < 7%.
  • Lifestyle: Diet, exercise, weight loss, stop smoking.
  • Foot Care: Daily inspection, comfortable shoes to prevent ulcers.

2. Symptomatic Relief (Pain Management):

  • First-line: Gabapentinoids (Pregabalin, Gabapentin) OR SNRIs (Duloxetine).
  • Second-line: Tricyclic Antidepressants (Amitriptyline) - use caution in elderly/cardiac patients.
  • Topical: Capsaicin cream, Lidocaine patches.
  • Avoid: Opioids are generally not recommended for chronic neuropathic pain.
