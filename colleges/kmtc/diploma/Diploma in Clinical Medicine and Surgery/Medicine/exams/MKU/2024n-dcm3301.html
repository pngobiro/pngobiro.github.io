Section A: Multiple Choice Questions

30 Marks (1 Mark Each)
1.
A known diabetic patient with uncontrolled sugars presents to the facility with interphalangeal itching and smelly feet. On local examination interdigital spaces are moist with whitish skin lesions and fissuring. Other systemic examinations are within normal. What is your most likely diagnosis?
a) Fungal dermatitis
b) Kaposis Sarcoma
c) Malaria
d) Halitosis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Fungal dermatitis

Explanation: The description of moist, whitish, fissured lesions in the interdigital spaces (toe webs) with itching is classic for Tinea Pedis (Athlete's foot), a fungal infection common in diabetics.

2.
Cat scratch disease is caused by which organism.
a) Toxoplasma gondii
b) Campylobacter jejuni
c) Hitoplasma
d) Infectious mononucleosis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Toxoplasma gondii (Note: Technical Inaccuracy in Question)

Explanation: Cat Scratch Disease is actually caused by Bartonella henselae. However, among the choices provided, Toxoplasma gondii is the primary organism transmitted by cats (feces). Campylobacter is food-borne, Histoplasma is fungal (bats/birds), and Infectious Mononucleosis is EBV. In the context of "Cat-associated diseases" often tested in MCQs, Toxoplasmosis is the intended link if Bartonella is absent, though clinically distinct.

3.
Toxoplasmosis presents with ring enhancing lesions on CT imaging
a) True
b) Sometimes
c) False
d) All the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) True

Explanation: Cerebral Toxoplasmosis is the most common cause of multiple ring-enhancing lesions in HIV/AIDS patients.

4.
Schistosomiasis definitive treatment is
a) Amoxicillin
b) Metronidazole
c) Praziquantel
d) Albendazole
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Praziquantel

Explanation: Praziquantel (40mg/kg) is the drug of choice for all species of Schistosoma.

5.
Concerning leishmaniasis
a) Is also called Kala-azar
b) Is more common in temperate than tropical regions
c) Causes acute fever within 2 days of transmission
d) Is a bacterial infection
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Is also called Kala-azar

Explanation: Visceral Leishmaniasis is commonly known as Kala-azar (Black Fever). It is a protozoal (not bacterial) infection, found in tropics, with an incubation period of weeks to months (not 2 days).

6.
Patients working in rice fields without proper footwear are more likely to suffer from…
a) Amoebiasis
b) Malaria
c) Filariasis
d) Anthrax
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Filariasis (or b. Malaria)

Explanation: Rice fields are breeding grounds for mosquitoes. Culex mosquitoes (vectors for Lymphatic Filariasis) and Anopheles (Malaria) breed in standing water. However, the mention of "footwear" usually hints at Schistosomiasis (snail penetration) or Leptospirosis (urine in water), neither of which are options. Between the options, vector-borne diseases (Malaria/Filariasis) are associated with the environment of rice fields. Filariasis is often linked to agricultural workers in wet areas.

7.
The following are all forms of anthrax except
a) Cutaneous anthrax
b) Multisystemic anthrax
c) wool sorter’s disease
d) Gastrointestinal anthrax
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Multisystemic anthrax

Explanation: The clinical forms are Cutaneous, Inhalational (Wool sorter's), and Gastrointestinal. "Multisystemic" is not a standard classification, although the disease can disseminate.

8.
The drug of choice for the management of anthrax is
a) Tetracycline
b) Ceftriaxone
c) Penicillins
d) Paracetamol
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Penicillins (or Ciprofloxacin/Doxycycline)

Explanation: Historically, Penicillin G was the drug of choice. Currently, Ciprofloxacin or Doxycycline are preferred for bioterrorism/systemic cases, but Penicillin remains a standard option for susceptible strains, especially in cutaneous anthrax.

9.
The causative organism for cryptococcosis can be identified through
a) Wet preparation
b) Indian ink
c) Gram staining
d) Bacterial culture
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Indian ink

Explanation: India Ink stain of CSF is the classic rapid test to visualize the capsule of Cryptococcus neoformans (appears as a clear halo).

10.
The case fatality rate of rabies is
a) 10%
b) 30%
c) 70%
d) 100%

Correct Answer: d) 100%

Explanation: Once clinical symptoms appear, Rabies is almost invariably fatal (CFR ~100%).

11.

12.
Systemic candidiasis is treated using
a) Amphotericin B
b) Topical clotrimazole
c) Intravenous Cephalosporins
d) Intravenous metronidazole
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Amphotericin B

Explanation: Systemic (invasive) fungal infections require potent IV antifungals like Amphotericin B or Fluconazole. Topical agents are for skin/mucosa.

13.
Which one is not a viral hemorrhagic fever
a) HIV
b) Ebola
c) Dengue
d) Marburg
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) HIV

Explanation: HIV causes immunodeficiency, not a hemorrhagic fever syndrome. Ebola, Dengue, and Marburg are classic VHFs.

14.

a) All tapeworms lack a scolex(head)

15.
Loeffler’s syndrome is caused by which stage of Ascariasis infection?
a) Migratory

Herpes Zoster virus is not
a) mostly associated with immunosuppression

18.
Corona virus disease (COVID-19)
a) Started in China
b) Vaccination is effective in preventing motality
c) Is highly infectious
d) Responds to high dose Amoxicillin treatment
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Is highly infectious (Note: A and B are also true statements)

Explanation: A, B, and C are all true statements about COVID-19. Option D is False (it's viral, doesn't respond to Amoxicillin). If the question asks what is *True*, C is a strong characteristic. If it asks what is *False*, D is the answer. Assuming "Which is true" context, C is the best descriptive answer for the disease nature.

19.
Measures for preventing spread of COVID_19 do not include
a) Wearing of masks
b) Preventing personal contact

d) Isolation of cases

20.
Strongyloidosis

b) Is a protozoa disease
c) Causes fevers and headache

21.
Diseases that respond to use of nitroimidazoles do not include:
a) Amoebiasis
b) Giardiasis
c) Anaerobic infections
d) Cholera
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Cholera

Explanation: Nitroimidazoles (Metronidazole/Tinidazole) treat Protozoa (Amoeba, Giardia) and Anaerobes. Cholera is caused by Vibrio cholerae (aerobic/facultative anaerobe) and is treated with Doxycycline/Azithromycin.

d) Polio

Correct Answer: c) Scarlet fever

Explanation: Scarlet fever (Group A Strep) has no vaccine. Hep B, Hib, and Polio are core KEPI vaccines.

29.

Section B: Short Answer Questions

Answer 5 Questions
1.
List down the classes of drugs used in management of HIV/AIDS.
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Nucleoside Reverse Transcriptase Inhibitors (NRTIs): e.g., Tenofovir, Lamivudine, Zidovudine.
  2. Non-Nucleoside Reverse Transcriptase Inhibitors (NNRTIs): e.g., Efavirenz, Nevirapine.
  3. Protease Inhibitors (PIs): e.g., Lopinavir, Ritonavir, Atazanavir.
  4. Integrase Strand Transfer Inhibitors (INSTIs): e.g., Dolutegravir, Raltegravir.
  5. Fusion Inhibitors: e.g., Enfuvirtide.
  6. CCR5 Antagonists (Entry Inhibitors): e.g., Maraviroc.
2.
List down the clinical features of malaria.
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Fever: Often high grade, intermittent/periodic (chills/rigors followed by sweating).
  • Headache: Severe.
  • Joint and Muscle Pains: Arthralgia/Myalgia.
  • Gastrointestinal: Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea.
  • General: Fatigue, malaise, loss of appetite.
  • Signs: Splenomegaly, mild jaundice (hemolysis), pallor (anemia).
3.
Write short notes on enterobiasis.
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Definition: Infection by the pinworm Enterobius vermicularis.
  • Transmission: Feco-oral route (ingestion of eggs) or retro-infection. Common in children.
  • Clinical Features: Intense nocturnal perianal pruritus (itching) due to female worms migrating to lay eggs. Can cause sleep disturbance.
  • Diagnosis: Scotch Tape Test (Cellophane tape) applied to anal skin in the morning to collect eggs. Stool exam is often negative.
  • Treatment: Mebendazole or Albendazole (Single dose, repeat in 2 weeks to kill hatched eggs). Treat entire family. Hygiene measures (wash bedding).

5.
Briefly explain how you would investigate fever in a 15 year old.
Show Answer

Answer:

Investigation follows a structured approach:

  1. History Taking: Duration, pattern of fever, travel history, associated symptoms (cough, rash, dysuria).
  2. Physical Exam: Look for focus (throat, chest, abdomen, stiffness).
  3. Laboratory Investigations:
    • Blood Slide (BS for MPS): To rule out Malaria (Commonest cause in tropics).
    • Full Haemogram (CBC): WBC count (Infection), Hb.
    • Urinalysis: To rule out UTI.
    • Typhoid tests: Stool culture / Widal (if indicated).
    • Chest X-ray: If respiratory signs present.
    • Blood Culture: If sepsis/enteric fever suspected.

Section C: Long Answer Questions

Long Essay
1.
Cholera

a) Causative organism. (2 Marks)

b) Clinical features. (6 Marks)

c) Diagnosis. (2 Marks)

d) Treatment. (6 Marks)

e) Supportive treatment. (4 Marks)

Show Answer

Answer:

a) Causative Organism: Vibrio cholerae (Serogroups O1 and O139).

b) Clinical Features:

  • Acute onset of profuse, painless, watery diarrhea.
  • "Rice Water" Stool: Pale, milky fluid with flecks of mucus.
  • Vomiting: Often projectile, follows diarrhea.
  • Signs of Dehydration: Sunken eyes, loss of skin turgor, dry mouth, washerwoman's hands.
  • Shock: Hypotension, Tachycardia, Oliguria/Anuria.
  • Muscle Cramps: Due to electrolyte loss (K+, Ca++, Na+).

c) Diagnosis:

  • Stool Culture: Isolation of V. cholerae on TCBS media (Gold Standard).
  • Rapid Diagnostic Tests (RDTs): Crystal VC dipstick.
  • Dark Field Microscopy: Shooting star motility.

d) Treatment:

  • Rehydration (Mainstay):
    • IV Fluids (Ringer's Lactate) for severe dehydration.
    • Oral Rehydration Salts (ORS) for mild/moderate.
  • Antibiotics: To shorten duration/shedding.
    • Adults: Doxycycline 300mg stat or Azithromycin 1g stat.
    • Children/Pregnant: Erythromycin or Azithromycin.
  • Zinc Supplementation: For children < 5 years.

e) Supportive Treatment:

  • Nutrition: Continue breastfeeding/feeding once rehydrated.
  • Electrolytes: Correction of Hypokalemia/Hypoglycemia.
  • Nursing Care: Monitoring Intake/Output, Vitals.
2.
Describe the:

a) Clinical features of uncomplicated malaria. (3 Marks)

b) Clinical features of complicated malaria. (6 Marks)

c) Management uncomplicated malaria with and without pregnancy. (4 Marks)

d) Management of complicated malaria. (7 Marks)

Show Answer

Answer:

a) Uncomplicated Malaria Features:

  • Fever (Hotness of body).
  • Headache.
  • Chills/Rigors.
  • Arthralgia/Myalgia.
  • Nausea/Vomiting.

b) Complicated (Severe) Malaria Features:

  • Cerebral Malaria: Coma, Seizures, Confusion (GCS < 11).
  • Severe Anemia: Hb < 5 g/dL.
  • Respiratory Distress: Acidotic breathing.
  • Hypoglycemia: Blood glucose < 2.2 mmol/L.
  • Renal Failure: Oliguria, high creatinine (Blackwater fever).
  • Jaundice: Evidence of organ dysfunction.
  • Shock: Algid malaria.

c) Management of Uncomplicated Malaria:

  • Non-Pregnant / 2nd & 3rd Trimester: Artemether-Lumefantrine (AL/Coartem) - 6 dose regimen over 3 days.
  • 1st Trimester Pregnancy: Oral Quinine (7 days) or Clindamycin + Quinine. (Note: WHO now allows AL in 1st trimester if Quinine unavailable, but Quinine is classic answer).

d) Management of Complicated Malaria:

  • Admission: ICU/High dependency.
  • Parenteral Antimalarial:
    • IV Artesunate: 2.4 mg/kg at 0, 12, 24 hours, then daily. (Drug of Choice).
    • OR IV Quinine (loading dose followed by maintenance).
  • Supportive Care:
    • Hypoglycemia: IV Dextrose.
    • Seizures: Diazepam.
    • Anemia: Blood Transfusion if Hb < 4-5.
    • Fluids: Cautious hydration to prevent pulmonary edema.
  • Switch to Oral: Finish with full course of AL once patient can tolerate oral meds.
