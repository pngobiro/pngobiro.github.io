1.
Kamau a 70-year-old man presents with generalized body swelling, difficulty in breathing, easy fatigability and denies exercises. On examination, he is sick looking uncomfortable when examined in supine position and has edema. He is not pale but has elevated jugular venous pressure.

a. What is the most probable diagnosis? [2mks]
b. Outline the treatment for Kamau [8mks]
Show Answer

a. Diagnosis:

Congestive Cardiac Failure (CCF) / Heart Failure

Reasoning: Generalized swelling (anasarca), Dyspnea (difficulty breathing), Orthopnea (uncomfortable in supine), Elevated JVP.

b. Treatment Outline:

1. Non-Pharmacological Management:

  • Positioning: Nurse in cardiac position (fowlers/semi-fowlers) to aid breathing.
  • Oxygen Therapy: Administer oxygen if SpO2 is low (< 94%) or patient is distressed.
  • Diet: Salt restriction (Low sodium diet) and fluid restriction if hyponatremic.
  • Monitoring: Daily weight, fluid input/output chart.

2. Pharmacological Management:

  • Diuretics: Furosemide (Lasix) IV/Oral to reduce fluid overload (preload).
  • ACE Inhibitors: (e.g., Enalapril) to reduce afterload and prevent remodeling.
  • Beta-Blockers: (e.g., Carvedilol, Bisoprolol) initiated when patient is stable to reduce myocardial oxygen demand.
  • Aldosterone Antagonists: Spironolactone (potassium-sparing diuretic) for severe failure.
  • Digoxin: May be indicated if there is atrial fibrillation or severe systolic dysfunction.

Clinical Pearl: "Uncomfortable in supine position" describes Orthopnea, a classic sign of left ventricular failure causing pulmonary congestion. The elevated JVP indicates right ventricular failure. Both combined suggest biventricular or Congestive Heart Failure.

2.
Moni travelled from Kisumu to Mombasa 3 days ago in a bus. Today she presents with a swollen painful left leg. The left leg is swollen, warm and tender at the calf. Moni is 108 kg and her height is 1.5 meters. Other systems are normal.

a. What is the most likely diagnosis [2mks]
b. List 4 differential diagnosis [2mks]
c. State two factors that precipitated her condition [2mks]
d. Calculate Moni’s BMI (body mass index) [3mks]
e. State the specific investigation for the patient [1mrk]
Show Answer

a. Diagnosis: Deep Venous Thrombosis (DVT)

b. Differential Diagnosis:

  • Cellulitis
  • Ruptured Baker’s Cyst
  • Muscular strain/tear (Gastrocnemius tear)
  • Superficial Thrombophlebitis
  • Lymphedema

c. Precipitating Factors (Virchow's Triad context):

  1. Stasis/Immobility: Long distance travel (Kisumu to Mombasa) sitting in a bus.
  2. Hypercoagulability (Risk factor): Obesity (Weight 108kg).

d. BMI Calculation:

Formula: $$BMI = \frac{\text{Weight (kg)}}{\text{Height (m)}^2}$$

Calculation: $$BMI = \frac{108}{1.5 \times 1.5} = \frac{108}{2.25} = 48 \text{ kg/m}^2$$

Interpretation: 48 kg/m² (Morbid Obesity)

e. Specific Investigation:

Doppler Ultrasound of the venous system (Duplex Scan) of the left leg.

3.
A known hypertensive patient comes to medical outpatient clinic for routine follow up. You discover his blood pressure is 200/125mmHg.

a. What is your diagnosis? [2mks]
b. What are the important investigations will you order [4mks]
c. Outline the treatment of this patient [8mks]
d. Mention 6 possible complications in this patient [6mks]
Show Answer

a. Diagnosis:

Hypertensive Emergency (Malignant Hypertension) or Hypertensive Urgency (depending on end-organ damage presence, but given the severity, emergency protocols are often initiated).

b. Important Investigations (Target Organ Damage check):

  • Urea, Electrolytes & Creatinine (UECs): To assess renal function.
  • Urinalysis: To check for proteinuria/hematuria.
  • Fundoscopy: To check for papilledema or hemorrhages (retinopathy).
  • ECG: To check for Left Ventricular Hypertrophy (LVH) or ischemia.
  • CT Head: If neurological signs are present.

c. Treatment Outline:

  • Admission: Immediate admission for monitoring.
  • Goal: Reduce Mean Arterial Pressure (MAP) by max 25% in the first hour. Do not normalize BP too quickly to avoid cerebral ischemia.
  • Medications (Parenteral/IV preferred for Emergency):
    • IV Labetalol
    • IV Hydralazine
    • Sodium Nitroprusside (in ICU settings)
  • Oral Loading (if urgency): Nifedipine (Retard/Sustained release - NOT sublingual), Amlodipine, or Captopril.
  • Monitoring: Continuous BP monitoring, urine output, neurological status (GCS).

d. Possible Complications:

  1. Stroke (Hemorrhagic or Ischemic)
  2. Hypertensive Encephalopathy
  3. Myocardial Infarction (Heart Attack)
  4. Acute Pulmonary Edema / Heart Failure
  5. Acute Renal Failure (Kidney failure)
  6. Aortic Dissection
  7. Retinopathy / Blindness
4.
Discuss complicated malaria under the following headlines:

a. Signs [4mks]
b. Symptoms [4mks]
c. Management [6mks]
d. Complications [6mks]
Show Answer

a. Signs (Objective findings):

  • Impaired consciousness or Coma (Glasgow Coma Scale < 11).
  • Prostration (Inability to sit, stand, or walk without support).
  • Respiratory distress (Acidotic breathing/Kussmaul's).
  • Multiple convulsions (>2 episodes in 24 hours).
  • Severe pallor (Palmar/Conjunctival).
  • Jaundice (Yellow sclera).
  • Dark urine (Coca-cola colored).
  • Shock (Cold extremities, weak pulse).

b. Symptoms (Subjective complaints):

  • High grade fever.
  • Severe headache.
  • Persistent vomiting.
  • Confusion or drowsiness.
  • Generalized weakness.
  • Dark colored urine.
  • Difficulty in breathing.

c. Management:

  1. Resuscitation (ABC): Maintain airway, breathing, and circulation. Treat hypoglycemia and convulsions immediately.
  2. Specific Antimalarial Therapy:
    • Drug of Choice: Parenteral Artesunate (IV/IM). Dose: 2.4 mg/kg at 0, 12, and 24 hours, then once daily for at least 24 hours.
    • Alternative: IV Quinine (Loading dose 20mg/kg over 4 hrs, then 10mg/kg every 8 hrs).
    • Switch to oral ACT (Artemether-Lumefantrine) once patient can tolerate oral meds to complete the course.
  3. Supportive Care: Antipyretics for fever, fluids for hydration, blood transfusion if Hb is very low (< 5g/dL), dialysis for renal failure.

d. Complications:

  • Cerebral Malaria (Coma).
  • Severe Malarial Anemia.
  • Hypoglycemia.
  • Metabolic Acidosis.
  • Acute Renal Failure.
  • Pulmonary Edema / ARDS.
  • Blackwater Fever (Hemoglobinuria).
  • DIC (Bleeding disorders).
  • Algid Malaria (Shock).
