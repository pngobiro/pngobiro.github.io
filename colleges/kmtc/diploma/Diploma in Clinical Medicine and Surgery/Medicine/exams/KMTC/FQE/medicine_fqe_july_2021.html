Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions

40 Marks
1.
One of the following is a non-modifiable risk for hypertension.
a) Obesity
b) Hypertension in first degree relative
c) Sedentary lifestyle
d) Acute Kidney Injury
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Hypertension in first degree relative

Explanation: Non-modifiable risk factors are those that cannot be changed, such as genetics (family history), age, gender, and ethnicity. A family history of hypertension (first degree relative) indicates a genetic predisposition. Obesity and sedentary lifestyle are modifiable lifestyle factors.

2.
About antibiotics and pneumonias, which one is incorrect
a) Preferred treatment is with Ampicillin
b) Atypical pneumonia should be treated for not less than 14 days
c) Add anaerobic antibiotics if suspected aspiration
d) Levotraxacin can be an alternative for Erythromycin
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Preferred treatment is with Ampicillin

Explanation: While Ampicillin is an antibiotic, standard guidelines for community-acquired pneumonia often recommend Amoxicillin (oral) or Benzylpenicillin/Crystalline Penicillin (IV) as first line, often combined with or replaced by macrolides (like Erythromycin) for atypicals. "Preferred treatment is with Ampicillin" is generally too broad or outdated compared to specific guideline-based regimens like Amoxicillin for outpatient or Ceftriaxone for inpatient.

3.
In Broncho pneumonia which is true?
a) There is consolidation in one lobe
b) There is widespread patchy infection
c) Common in adolescence age
d) Neutrophil leucocytosis is not present
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) There is widespread patchy infection

Explanation: Bronchopneumonia is characterized by patchy areas of consolidation surrounding bronchioles, often affecting multiple lobes bilaterally. Lobar pneumonia affects a distinct lobe (Option A). It is common in extremes of age (infants/elderly), not specifically adolescents.

4.
The most useful tool for evaluation of the condition of a patient with an acute asthmatic attack and in assessing the response to therapy is;
a) Chest radiography
b) Arterial blood gas measurement
c) Measurement of pulsus paradoxus
d) Measurement of peak expiratory flow or FEV1
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Measurement of peak expiratory flow or FEV1

Explanation: PEFR (Peak Expiratory Flow Rate) or FEV1 are the standard objective measures to assess airflow obstruction severity and response to bronchodilators in acute asthma settings.

5.
Concerning asthma;
a) Refers to inflammation of the airway.
b) Is hyper irritability of the airway.
c) Smoking is a known aetiology.
d) Age is a risk factor.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Refers to inflammation of the airway.

Explanation: Asthma is fundamentally a chronic inflammatory disorder of the airways (Option A). This inflammation leads to hyper-responsiveness (irritability), but the definition centers on inflammation. While smoking can exacerbate it, it's not the primary etiology like in COPD.

6.
The development of pyothorax (pyogenic pleural effusion) is commonly associated with;
a) Streptococcal pneumonia
b) Staphylococcal pneumonia
c) Mycobacterium tuberculosis
d) Mycoplasma pneumonia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Staphylococcal pneumonia

Explanation: Staphylococcus aureus pneumonia is notorious for causing complications such as lung abscesses, pneumatoceles, and empyema (pyothorax), especially in children and debilitated adults.

7.
Persons in direct contact with patients of bacteriologically confirmed TB have been found to benefit from chemoprophylaxis involving;
a) Broad spectrum antibiotics.
b) Augementin Caplets for 5 days.
c) Isoniazide prophylaxis.
d) No chemoprophylaxis is necessary unless TB is confirmed in the exposed person.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Isoniazide prophylaxis.

Explanation: Isoniazid Preventive Therapy (IPT) is the standard of care for contacts (especially children under 5 and HIV-positive individuals) to prevent latent TB from progressing to active disease.

8.
Young asthmatic male in emergency department. Resp rate 26/min, pCO2 27, SAO2 92%, struggling, talking in sentences. Given nebulised salbutamol, and ipratropium, 200mg IV hydrocortisone. After 30 minutes - no improvement. Further management: (NEXT step involve);
a) IV salbutamol
b) IV aminophylline
c) IV magnesium sulphate
d) Intubate and ventilate
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) IV magnesium sulphate

Explanation: In acute severe asthma not responding to initial bronchodilators and steroids, a single dose of IV Magnesium Sulphate (1.2-2g) is recommended as an adjunct therapy for its bronchodilator effect before considering aminophylline or intubation.

9.
Infective endocarditis
a) May follow a tooth extraction
b) I.V drug user are not at risk
c) A ten-day oral treatment is adequate for treatment
d) Modified John’s criteria is used in Diagnosis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) May follow a tooth extraction

Explanation: Dental procedures can cause transient bacteremia (often Viridans streptococci), which can seed damaged heart valves leading to endocarditis. IV drug users ARE at high risk (Option B is false). Treatment requires prolonged IV antibiotics (4-6 weeks), not 10 days oral (Option C is false). Diagnosis uses Duke Criteria, not Jones criteria (Option D is false - Jones is for Rheumatic Fever).

10.
A patient visited a hospital and the following investigations were done on separate occasions. Which values would imply prediabetes?
a) RBS >11.1mmol/dl
b) HbA1C >7.5 %
c) FBS 4.0mmol/dl
d) RBS 5.0mmol/dl
Show Answer

Correct Answer: (None of the options clearly represent standard prediabetes ranges, but by elimination/context):

  • A: RBS >11.1 is Diabetes.
  • B: HbA1C >7.5% is Diabetes (Cutoff is ≥6.5%).
  • C: FBS 4.0 is Normal.
  • D: RBS 5.0 is Normal.

Note: Standard Prediabetes ranges are: FBS 5.6-6.9 mmol/L, HbA1c 5.7-6.4%, 2h-OGTT 7.8-11.0 mmol/L. There might be a typo in the options or question. If forced to choose a "borderline" value not explicitly diabetic or normal, none fit well. However, in some contexts, elevated values below diabetic thresholds are key.

11.
The most sensitive diagnostic imaging for STROKE secondary to intracranial hemorrhage is;
a) Cranial- MRI
b) Cranial- CT scan
c) Skull X-ray
d) Full hemogram as it shows reduced HB
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Cranial- CT scan

Explanation: Non-contrast CT scan is the gold standard initial imaging for stroke because it is highly sensitive for acute hemorrhage (blood appears bright white), allowing immediate differentiation from ischemic stroke.

12.
Which of the following investigations is least necessary in diagnosis of acute bacterial meningitis?
a) Complete Blood Count
b) Serum crag test
c) Lumbar Puncture
d) Blood culture
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Serum crag test

Explanation: Serum CrAg (Cryptococcal Antigen) is specific for Cryptococcal meningitis (fungal), not bacterial meningitis. CBC, LP (CSF analysis), and Blood Culture are essential for working up bacterial meningitis.

13.
One of the following organisms is commonly implicated in causation of community acquired pneumonia;
a) Streptococcus pneumonia
b) Mycoplasma
c) Pneumococci
d) Hemophilus influenza
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Streptococcus pneumonia

Explanation: Streptococcus pneumoniae (Pneumococcus) is the most common cause of typical community-acquired pneumonia worldwide. (Note: Option C "Pneumococci" is the same organism, but A is the formal name).

14.
A known HIV patient diagnosed with Pulmonary TB belongs to WHO stage
a) I
b) II
c) III
d) IV
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) III

Explanation: According to WHO Clinical Staging of HIV/AIDS: Pulmonary TB is a Stage III condition. Extra-pulmonary TB is Stage IV.

15.
The investigation that is confirmatory of visceral leishmaniasis (kala-azar)
a) FHG-shows pancytopenia
b) Demonstrations of leishmania Donovani bodies in blood smears
c) Urinalysis shows haematuria
d) False positive typhoid test
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Demonstrations of leishmania Donovani bodies in blood smears

Explanation: Demonstration of parasites (LD bodies) in tissue aspirates (spleen, bone marrow, lymph node) or sometimes blood smears is confirmatory.

16.
The following drug is the first line treatment for severe/complicated malaria;
a) Artemether Lumefantrine combination.
b) Duocotexin (DHA/PPQ).
c) Parenteral artesunate.
d) Parenteral Quinine.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Parenteral artesunate.

Explanation: IV/IM Artesunate is the recommended first-line treatment for severe malaria as it reduces mortality significantly compared to quinine. Quinine is the alternative.

17.
The gold standard diagnostic test for malarial infection include;
a) Malaria rapid diagnostic test.
b) Duffy coat antigen test.
c) Malaria Parasite DNA PCR.
d) Blood slide microscopy.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Blood slide microscopy.

Explanation: Microscopy (Giemsa-stained thick and thin blood smears) remains the gold standard for clinical diagnosis, allowing quantification and species identification.

18.
The following is a feature of iron deficiency anaemia;
a) Normocytic hypochromic erythrocytes.
b) Normocytic normochromic erythrocytes.
c) Microcytic hypochromic erythrocytes.
d) Microcytic normochromic erythrocytes.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Microcytic hypochromic erythrocytes.

Explanation: Iron deficiency leads to small (microcytic, MCV < 80) and pale (hypochromic, MCH < 27) red blood cells due to defective hemoglobin synthesis.

19.
The following is a diagnostic test for sickle cell disease;
a) Sickling test.
b) Hb electrophoresis.
c) Peripheral blood film.
d) Full haemogram test.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Hb electrophoresis.

Explanation: Hemoglobin electrophoresis is the diagnostic test used to quantify HbS and distinguish between Sickle Cell Trait (AS) and Sickle Cell Disease (SS). Sickling test is a screening test.

20.
The following drug has no role in malaria chemoprophylaxis;
a) Duocotexin.
b) Cotrimoxazole.
c) Sulphadoxine Pyrimenthamine (SP) combination.
d) Carboxypenicillin.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Carboxypenicillin.

Explanation: Carboxypenicillin is an antibiotic (penicillin group) and has no antimalarial activity. Duocotexin (ACT) is for treatment. SP is used for Intermittent Preventive Treatment (IPTp). Cotrimoxazole is used for prophylaxis in HIV patients against opportunistic infections including malaria.

21.
Which of the following is not part of the goals in DKA management?
a) Correction of dehydration
b) Correction of acidosis
c) Identify and treat the precipitating factors
d) Correct hyperglycemia to 4mmol/l to avoid further complications
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Correct hyperglycemia to 4mmol/l to avoid further complications

Explanation: The goal is to lower blood sugar gradually, but NOT to hypoglycemia levels or very low normal (4mmol/L) too quickly, which risks cerebral edema and hypoglycemia. The target is usually around 10-12 mmol/L before switching to dextrose.

22.
In hemolytic jaundice, there is.
a) Pale stool
b) Pale conjunctival mucous membranes
c) Itching and scratching of the skin
d) Malaria infection is not a cause
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Pale conjunctival mucous membranes

Explanation: Hemolytic jaundice is caused by excessive breakdown of RBCs (hemolysis), which leads to anemia. Pale mucous membranes are a sign of anemia. Pale stool and itching (pruritus) are signs of obstructive jaundice (cholestasis), not hemolytic. Malaria IS a cause.

23.
In modified Jones Criteria, major features include;
a) Elevated acute phase reactants
b) Fever
c) Carditis
d) Arthralgia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Carditis.

Explanation: Major criteria (JONES): Joints (Polyarthritis), O (Carditis), Nodules (Subcutaneous), Erythema marginatum, Sydenham's chorea. Fever, Arthralgia, and Elevated acute phase reactants are Minor criteria.

24.
Features of heart failure.
a) Unilateral pedal pitting oedema.
b) Elevated jugular venous pressure.
c) Hypertension
d) Acute wasting
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Elevated jugular venous pressure.

Explanation: Elevated JVP is a specific sign of fluid overload and right heart failure. Heart failure edema is typically bilateral. Hypertension is a cause, not a feature of failure itself (BP often drops in severe failure).

25.
The following is TRUE on hypertension;
a) Secondary hypertension is the most prevalent in >95% of cases.
b) Essential Hypertension is rare.
c) JNC 7 is the latest classification of hypertension.
d) Obesity is a modifiable risk factor.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Obesity is a modifiable risk factor.

Explanation: Obesity is a major reversible risk factor. Essential (Primary) hypertension is the most prevalent (>90-95%), not secondary. JNC 8 (and newer guidelines like ACC/AHA) have superseded JNC 7.

26.
Which of the following correctly defines hypertensive emergency?
a) Systolic >180mmHg, diastolic >120mmHg.
b) Systolic >180mmHg, diastolic >120mmHg with impending end organ damage.
c) Systolic >180mmHg, diastolic >140mmHg.
d) Systolic <180mmHg, diastolic >140mmHg with impending end organ damage.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Systolic >180mmHg, diastolic >120mmHg with impending end organ damage.

Explanation: Hypertensive emergency is defined by severe elevation of BP (usually >180/120) WITH evidence of new or worsening target organ damage (e.g., encephalopathy, MI, AKI). Without organ damage, it is Hypertensive Urgency.

27.
Antiplatelet therapy in Venous thromboembolism
a) Ascard (Cardiac aspirin).
b) Diclofenac.
c) Warfarin.
d) Heparin.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: (Question contains a distractor in the title, likely asking for treatment of VTE). Warfarin/Heparin are Anticoagulants, Aspirin is Antiplatelet.

Analysis: Antiplatelets (Aspirin) have a minor role in VTE prevention but are NOT the standard therapy; Anticoagulants (Heparin/Warfarin) are. If the question asks "What is used?", usually Heparin/Warfarin. If it literally asks "Which is an Antiplatelet?", it's Aspirin. However, given the context of VTE treatment, C (Warfarin) or D (Heparin) are the standard drugs, but they are anticoagulants. If asking for the odd one out or specific antiplatelet mention, Aspirin is the antiplatelet.

Correction: The question likely tests knowledge that Antiplatelets are generally NOT primary VTE treatment compared to Anticoagulants, OR it's a "Best treatment" question phrased poorly. Standard VTE Tx = Heparin/Warfarin. Aspirin is sometimes used for secondary prevention after anticoagulation.

28.
Risk factors of co-pulmonale.
a) Pneumoconiosis.
b) Pneumonia.
c) Pulmonary TB
d) Congestive heart failure
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Pneumoconiosis.

Explanation: Cor pulmonale is right heart failure secondary to chronic lung disease. Pneumoconiosis (chronic occupational lung disease) causes pulmonary hypertension leading to cor pulmonale. Pneumonia is acute. CHF (Left heart failure) is the most common cause of right heart failure but by definition, Cor Pulmonale EXCLUDES right heart failure due to left heart disease.

29.
First line treatment of Urinary Tract Infection;
a) Sulphonamides e.g Cotrimoxazole.
b) Penicillins e.g. Amoxil.
c) Fluoroquinolones e.g Ciprofloxacin.
d) Doxycycline.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Sulphonamides e.g Cotrimoxazole (or Nitrofurantoin).

Explanation: For uncomplicated UTI (cystitis), Nitrofurantoin or Cotrimoxazole (Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole) are often first line. Ciprofloxacin is usually second line or for complicated UTI/Pyelonephritis to preserve efficacy.

30.
The following is a physiological diabetogenic state.
a) Pregnancy.
b) Septicaemia.
c) Malaria infection.
d) Pancreatitis.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Pregnancy.

Explanation: Pregnancy induces insulin resistance (due to placental hormones like hPL), creating a physiological diabetogenic state to ensure glucose supply to the fetus. This can lead to Gestational Diabetes. Septicaemia and Pancreatitis are pathological states.

31.
Which of the following is considered a ‘broad spectrum antibiotic’ in STI Syndromic treatment?
a) Ciprofloxacin.
b) Ceftriaxone.
c) Doxycycline.
d) Cefalexin.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Doxycycline.

Explanation: In STI syndromic management, Doxycycline is often used as the broad-spectrum agent covering Chlamydia, Mycoplasma, and some syphilis, typically combined with Ceftriaxone (for Gonorrhea) and Metronidazole (for Trichomoniasis/Anaerobes). Ciprofloxacin effectiveness is reduced due to resistance.

32.
Common indications of upper GI endoscopy include all of the following except
a) Dyspepsia despite treatment
b) Diarrhoea
c) Refractory vomiting
d) Dysphagia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Diarrhoea

Explanation: Diarrhea is typically a lower GI or small bowel issue, usually investigated via stool tests or colonoscopy if chronic. Upper GI endoscopy (OGD) visualizes esophagus, stomach, duodenum, indicated for dysphagia, persistent vomiting, and dyspepsia.

33.
The following is a lower GIT ulcerative disorder.
a) Duodenal ulcer.
b) Zolinger Ellison syndrome.
c) Gastric ulcer.
d) Crohns disease.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Crohns disease.

Explanation: Duodenal and Gastric ulcers are Upper GI (Peptic Ulcer Disease). Crohn's disease affects any part but most commonly the ileum/colon (Lower GI) and causes transmural ulceration.

34.
Pathophysiology of organophosphate poisons:
a) inhibit the activity of phosphorylase
b) inhibit the activity of acetylcholinesterase
c) inhibit the activity of malate dehydrogenase
d) stimulate the activity of pseudo cholinesterase.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) inhibit the activity of acetylcholinesterase

Explanation: Organophosphates bind irreversibly to acetylcholinesterase, preventing the breakdown of acetylcholine, leading to cholinergic toxicity (SLUDGE syndrome).

1.
Concerning malaria;
a. State any 2 clinical features. (2marks)
b. State 2 methods of diagnosis commonly used. (2marks)
c. State 2 drugs used In the treatment of uncomplicated malaria. (2marks)
d. List any 4 causes of hypoglycaemia in a patient diagnosed with severe malaria. (4marks)
Show Answer

a. Clinical features:

  • Fever (cyclical/periodic)
  • Chills and rigors
  • Headache
  • Joint pains (arthralgia)

b. Methods of diagnosis:

  • Blood Slide for Malaria Parasite (BS for MPS) - Microscopy
  • Rapid Diagnostic Test (mRDT)

c. Drugs for uncomplicated malaria:

  • Artemether-Lumefantrine (AL) - First line
  • Dihydroartemisinin-Piperaquine (DHA-PPQ) - Second line

d. Causes of hypoglycemia in severe malaria:

  • High glucose consumption by the malaria parasites.
  • Stimulation of pancreatic insulin secretion by Quinine (hyperinsulinemia).
  • Impaired liver gluconeogenesis (hepatic dysfunction).
  • Poor oral intake / vomiting / starvation.
  • High metabolic demand due to fever.

Section 3: Long Answer Question

20 Marks
1.
A 27-year-old patient is brought to the emergency department with the following presentation.
i. Patient is unconscious with cold extremities.
ii. Noted to be in an adult diaper, reported to have had several bouts of watery diarrhoea for the last 45 minutes with a sunken orbital fossa with skin turgor delayed at 2seconds on skin pinch test.
iii. Several family members accompanying him reports abdominal pain and two have had an episode of vomiting.

Discuss the management of this patient. (20 marks)
Show Answer

Diagnosis: Severe Dehydration secondary to Acute Watery Diarrhea (likely Cholera given the severity ("rice water" implication), speed of onset, and clustering in family).

Management Plan:

1. Resuscitation / Emergency Phase (Immediate):

  • Airway/Breathing: Ensure airway is patent. Administer Oxygen if necessary.
  • Circulation (Rehydration - Plan C for Severe Dehydration):
    • Establish IV access immediately (large bore cannula).
    • Start IV Ringer's Lactate (or Normal Saline) rapidly.
    • Give 100ml/kg:
      • First 30ml/kg in 30 mins.
      • Next 70ml/kg in 2.5 hours.
    • Monitor pulse and BP frequently (every 15 mins).

2. Investigation:

  • Random Blood Sugar (to rule out hypoglycemia).
  • Urea & Electrolytes (check for hypokalemia, renal failure).
  • Stool microscopy/culture (for Vibrio cholerae).
  • Full Hemogram.

3. Definitive Treatment:

  • Antibiotics: Once vomiting stops/patient stable.
    • Doxycycline 300mg stat (Single dose) OR
    • Azithromycin 1g stat OR
    • Ciprofloxacin.
  • Zinc Supplementation: 20mg daily for 10 days to reduce severity/duration.

4. Maintenance Phase:

  • Switch to Oral Rehydration Salts (ORS) once patient can drink and pulse is strong.
  • Encourage fluid intake to match losses.

5. Public Health Measures:

  • Notification: Notify public health authorities (Cholera is a notifiable disease).
  • Contact Tracing/Prophylaxis: Evaluate family members (vomiting/pain) and treat. Chemoprophylaxis (Doxycycline) for close contacts if protocol allows.
  • Education: Hygiene, water boiling/treatment (chlorination), proper waste disposal.
  • Isolation: Isolate patient to prevent spread (Cholera beds).
