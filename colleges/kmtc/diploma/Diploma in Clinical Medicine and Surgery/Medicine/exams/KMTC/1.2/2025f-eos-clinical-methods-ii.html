Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions (20 Marks)

1.
Which of the following is the correct sequence of steps in abdominal examination?
A. Inspection, Percussion, Palpation, Auscultation
B. Inspection, Auscultation, Percussion, Palpation
C. Percussion, Inspection, Palpation, Auscultation
D. Palpation, Auscultation, Percussion, Inspection
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Inspection, Auscultation, Percussion, Palpation

Explanation: For the abdominal examination, the standard sequence is modified to IAPP (Inspection, Auscultation, Percussion, Palpation). This is because percussion and palpation can stimulate bowel motility and alter bowel sounds, so auscultation is performed immediately after inspection.

2.
A 45-year-old male presents with generalized abdominal pain. On percussion, you note shifting dullness. This finding is most suggestive of:
A. Ascites
B. Appendicitis
C. Intestinal Obstruction
D. Peritonitis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Ascites

Explanation: Shifting dullness is a classic sign of free fluid in the peritoneal cavity (ascites). The area of dullness shifts when the patient rolls to one side as gravity moves the fluid.

3.
Which of the following is a key feature of bowel obstruction?
A. Increased bowel sounds
B. Absence of bowel sounds
C. Tenderness in the upper abdomen
D. Enlarged liver
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Increased bowel sounds

Explanation: In mechanical bowel obstruction, the bowel contracts vigorously to overcome the blockage, leading to hyperactive, high-pitched, or "tinkling" bowel sounds (borborygmi). Absence of bowel sounds (silent abdomen) is a feature of paralytic ileus or late-stage obstruction/peritonitis.

4.
When palpating the abdomen, guarding is most often associated with:
A. Appendicitis
B. Gallstones
C. Hernia
D. Intestinal obstruction
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Appendicitis

Explanation: Guarding (spasm of abdominal muscles) is a sign of peritoneal irritation (peritonitis). Appendicitis typically causes localized peritonitis in the right iliac fossa, leading to guarding. While gallstones can cause guarding if cholecystitis is present, appendicitis is the classic example for generalized or localized guarding in this context.

5.
In an abdominal examination, which is the first step to assess the patient?
A. Auscultation
B. Inspection
C. Percussion
D. Palpation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Inspection

Explanation: All physical examinations begin with Inspection (looking). This involves observing the contour, skin changes, scars, movement, and visible masses before touching the patient.

6.
A digital rectal examination (DRE) is performed to evaluate which of the following structures?
A. Prostate
B. Spleen
C. Kidneys
D. Appendix
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Prostate

Explanation: DRE is the primary clinical method for assessing the prostate gland (size, consistency, nodules) in males. It also assesses the rectum and anal tone.

7.
What is the most reliable method to confirm urinary retention during an examination?
A. Palpation of the lower abdomen
B. Urine dipstick test
C. Ultrasound of the bladder
D. Visual inspection
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Ultrasound of the bladder

Explanation: While palpation and percussion are used in physical exam, Ultrasound (Bladder Scan) is the most objective and reliable method to confirm and quantify urinary retention (residual volume), especially in obese patients where palpation is difficult.

8.
Which investigation is commonly performed to detect blood in the urine?
A. Urinalysis
B. Ultrasound
C. CT scan
D. Cystoscopy
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Urinalysis

Explanation: Urinalysis (using a dipstick) is the first-line screening test to detect hematuria (blood in urine). It is rapid, cheap, and effective.

9.
The GALS screen is used to assess:
A. Gait, Arms, Legs, Spine
B. Generalized Arthritic Lesions
C. General Anatomy of Ligaments and Skeleton
D. Gait Analysis and Limb Strength
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Gait, Arms, Legs, Spine

Explanation: GALS is a screening tool for musculoskeletal pathology focusing on four key areas: Gait, Arms, Legs, and Spine.

10.
In assessing joint range of motion, which technique is most commonly used?
A. Inspection
B. Passive Movement
C. Palpation
D. Active Movement
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Active Movement

Explanation: Assessment of Range of Motion (ROM) typically starts with Active Movement (the patient moves the joint themselves). This screens for function and pain. If active ROM is limited, the examiner then performs Passive Movement.

11.
What is the main purpose of the "Trendelenburg test"?
A. To assess hip stability
B. To evaluate knee strength
C. To test for wrist arthritis
D. To examine spinal alignment
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. To assess hip stability

Explanation: The Trendelenburg test assesses the function of the hip abductors (gluteus medius and minimus). It tests the stability of the hip joint when standing on one leg.

12.
The Glasgow Coma Scale evaluates:
A. Motor, Sensory, and Reflex Function
B. Eye Opening, Verbal Response, Motor Response
C. Cranial Nerves, Gait, Coordination
D. Cognitive and Mental Function
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Eye Opening, Verbal Response, Motor Response

Explanation: The GCS is composed of three parameters: Eye Opening (E), Verbal Response (V), and Motor Response (M). It is used to objectively record the conscious state of a person.

13.
Which cranial nerve is responsible for pupillary light reflex?
A. Optic Nerve (CN II)
B. Oculomotor Nerve (CN III)
C. Trochlear Nerve (CN IV)
D. Abducens Nerve (CN VI)
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Oculomotor Nerve (CN III)

Explanation: The pupillary light reflex involves two nerves: the Optic nerve (CN II) is the afferent (sensory) pathway, and the Oculomotor nerve (CN III) is the efferent (motor) pathway that constricts the pupil via the sphincter pupillae muscle. The question asks for the nerve responsible for the reflex action (constriction).

14.
The Babinski sign indicates:
A. Lower Motor Neuron Lesion
B. Upper Motor Neuron Lesion
C. Peripheral Neuropathy
D. Spinal Cord Compression
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Upper Motor Neuron Lesion

Explanation: A positive Babinski sign (extensor plantar response - big toe goes up, other toes fan out) is a pathognomonic sign of an Upper Motor Neuron (UMN) lesion (e.g., stroke, spinal cord injury) in adults.

15.
Which test assesses coordination in the upper limbs?
A. Finger-to-Nose Test
B. Heel-to-Shin Test
C. Romberg Test
D. Pronator Drift
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Finger-to-Nose Test

Explanation: The Finger-to-Nose test is the specific cerebellar coordination test for the upper limbs. Heel-to-shin is for the lower limbs.

16.
Signs meningitis include:
A. Nuchal Rigidity and Positive Kernig's Sign
B. Babinski Reflex and Dysdiadochokinesia
C. Romberg's Test and Ataxia
D. Tremors and Fasciculations
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Nuchal Rigidity and Positive Kernig's Sign

Explanation: Meningeal irritation manifests as Nuchal Rigidity (stiff neck), Kernig's Sign (pain on knee extension with hip flexed), and Brudzinski's Sign.

17.
Cranial nerve III palsy results in:
A. Diplopia and Ptosis
B. Loss of Taste
C. Hearing Loss
D. Facial Weakness
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Diplopia and Ptosis

Explanation: Oculomotor nerve palsy causes Ptosis (drooping eyelid) due to paralysis of levator palpebrae superioris, and Diplopia (double vision) because the eye is misaligned ("down and out").

18.
Which of the following is assessed by asking the patient to smile and close their eyes tightly?
A. Facial Nerve (CN VII)
B. Trigeminal Nerve (CN V)
C. Glossopharyngeal Nerve (CN IX)
D. Hypoglossal Nerve (CN XII)
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Facial Nerve (CN VII)

Explanation: The Facial nerve supplies the muscles of facial expression. Asking the patient to smile (orbicularis oris/zygomaticus) and close eyes tightly (orbicularis oculi) tests motor function of CN VII.

19.
Slurred speech and difficulty swallowing can suggest a problem with:
A. Cranial nerve IX and X
B. Cranial nerve VI
C. Spinal cord injury
D. Cerebellar dysfunction
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Cranial nerve IX and X

Explanation: The Glossopharyngeal (IX) and Vagus (X) nerves supply the pharynx and larynx. Lesions here cause Dysphagia (difficulty swallowing) and Dysarthria (slurred speech) or hoarseness. This is known as Bulbar Palsy.

20.
The inability to perform rapid alternating movements indicates dysfunction in:
A. The cerebellum
B. The basal ganglia
C. The brainstem
D. The spinal cord
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. The cerebellum

Explanation: This condition is called Dysdiadochokinesia and is a cardinal sign of cerebellar dysfunction.

Section 2: Short Answer Questions (40 Marks)

21.
Describe how you would assess a patient for splenomegaly, including the findings you might encounter. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Assessment Procedure:

  1. Positioning: Place the patient supine. Ensure hands are warm.
  2. Palpation: Start palpation in the Right Iliac Fossa (RIF). Move diagonally upwards towards the Left Upper Quadrant (LUQ).
  3. Instruction: Ask the patient to take deep breaths in and out. Move your hand superiorly with each expiration.
  4. Feeling: Feel for the splenic edge meeting your fingertips during inspiration.
  5. Alternate Position: If not palpable, turn patient to right lateral decubitus position and repeat.

Findings (Features of Enlarged Spleen):

  • Palpable notch on the anteromedial border.
  • Mass moves inferomedially (towards RIF) on inspiration.
  • You cannot get above the mass (it originates under the ribs).
  • Dullness to percussion over the mass.
22.
Explain how you would perform and interpret the fluid thrill test in a patient suspected of having ascites. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Technique:

  1. Patient Position: Supine.
  2. Assistant's Hand: Ask an assistant (or the patient) to place the ulnar edge of their hand firmly vertically down the midline of the abdomen. This stops the transmission of the impulse through the abdominal wall fat/muscle.
  3. Examiner's Hands: Place your left hand flat on the patient's right flank.
  4. Action: Flick or tap sharply on the patient's left flank with your right fingers.

Interpretation:

  • Positive Test: A sharp impulse or fluid wave is felt against the receiving hand (left hand). This indicates the presence of a large volume of free fluid (tense ascites).
23.
Describe the steps for conducting a suprapubic catheterization and outline the indications for this procedure. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Indications:

  • Acute urinary retention where urethral catheterization fails or is contraindicated.
  • Urethral trauma (e.g., pelvic fracture).
  • Urethral strictures.

Steps:

  1. Verify Distension: Confirm palpable bladder or use ultrasound.
  2. Anesthesia: Infiltrate local anesthetic 2cm above pubic symphysis.
  3. Incision: Make a small stab incision in the midline.
  4. Insertion: Insert the trocar and cannula vertically, then angle slightly inferiorly into the bladder until urine flows.
  5. Catheter Placement: Advance the catheter through the cannula, inflate the balloon, withdraw the cannula, and secure the catheter.
24.
Outline steps for conducting musculoskeletal exam. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Standard approach (Look, Feel, Move):

  1. Screening: GALS Screen (Gait, Arms, Legs, Spine).
  2. Inspection (Look): Observe for swelling, erythema, deformity, muscle wasting, and scars.
  3. Palpation (Feel): Assess temperature (warmth), tenderness, crepitus, and joint effusion.
  4. Movement (Move):
    • Active: Patient moves the joint.
    • Passive: Examiner moves the joint.
  5. Special Tests: Specific tests for stability and function (e.g., drawer tests for knee).
25.
Describe the clinical significance and procedure of lumbar puncture. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Clinical Significance:

  • Diagnosis: Meningitis (bacterial/viral/fungal), Subarachnoid Hemorrhage, Guillain-Barre syndrome.
  • Therapeutic: Administration of intrathecal drugs (chemotherapy/antibiotics), reduction of intracranial pressure (idiopathic intracranial hypertension).

Procedure:

  1. Position: Lateral recumbent (fetal position) or sitting, flexing the back to open intervertebral spaces.
  2. Site: Identify L3-L4 or L4-L5 interspace (level of iliac crests).
  3. Sterile Technique: Clean skin, drape, local anesthetic.
  4. Insertion: Insert spinal needle into subarachnoid space.
  5. Collection: Measure opening pressure and collect CSF into sterile tubes.
26.
What parameters do you consider while reporting a Glasgow Coma Scale of a patient? (5 Marks)
Show Answer

The GCS is reported as a total score out of 15, broken down into three components:

  1. Eye Opening (E) - Max 4: Spontaneous (4), To speech (3), To pain (2), None (1).
  2. Verbal Response (V) - Max 5: Oriented (5), Confused (4), Inappropriate words (3), Incomprehensible sounds (2), None (1).
  3. Motor Response (M) - Max 6: Obeys commands (6), Localizes pain (5), Withdraws from pain (4), Flexion/Decorticate (3), Extension/Decerebrate (2), None (1).
27.
Discuss the procedure and clinical interpretation of the Romberg test. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Procedure:

  1. Ask patient to stand with feet together and arms at sides.
  2. Ask patient to keep eyes open first (to ensure stability).
  3. Ask patient to close their eyes. Be prepared to support them if they fall.
  4. Observe for swaying or loss of balance.

Interpretation:

  • Positive Test: Patient is stable with eyes open but falls/sways significantly with eyes closed. This indicates Sensory Ataxia (loss of proprioception/dorsal column function).
  • Negative Test: Patient remains stable with eyes closed (minimal swaying is normal).
  • If patient is unstable even with eyes open, it suggests Cerebellar Ataxia (not a positive Romberg's).
28.
Describe two methods of conducting hearing test exam. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

1. Whisper Test (Voice Test):

  • Stand arm's length behind the patient.
  • Mask the non-test ear by rubbing the tragus.
  • Whisper a combination of numbers/letters (e.g., "3-B-6") and ask the patient to repeat them.
  • Repeat for the other ear.

2. Tuning Fork Tests (Rinne's & Weber's):

  • Rinne's Test: Place vibrating 512Hz fork on mastoid (Bone Conduction), then in front of ear (Air Conduction). Normal is AC > BC.
  • Weber's Test: Place vibrating fork on vertex of head. Sound should localize centrally. Lateralization indicates conductive or sensorineural loss.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions (40 Marks)

29.
A 60-year-old male presents with a two-month history of unintentional weight loss, loss of appetite, and upper abdominal pain. Outline a systematic approach to conducting a per abdominal examination in this patient. Discuss possible findings and their interpretations. (20 marks)
Show Answer

Systematic Abdominal Examination

1. Introduction & General Inspection:

  • Introduce self, explain procedure, gain consent. Position patient supine with one pillow, expose abdomen.
  • General Look: Check for wasting (cachexia) and pallor (anemia - possibly from GI bleed).
  • Hands/Face: Look for signs of liver disease (palmar erythema), anemia (conjunctival pallor), jaundice (sclera), and Virchow's node (left supraclavicular fossa).

2. Inspection of Abdomen:

  • Contour: Look for masses or distension (ascites). Scaphoid abdomen may indicate starvation.
  • Skin: Scars, dilated veins, striae.
  • Umbilicus: Sister Mary Joseph nodule (metastasis).
  • Movement: Visible pulsations (AAA) or peristalsis (obstruction).

3. Palpation (Superficial & Deep):

  • Ask about pain first. Start away from painful area.
  • Epigastrium: Feel for a hard, irregular, fixed mass (Suggests Gastric Carcinoma).
  • Liver: Palpate for hepatomegaly (metastases feel hard and knobbly).
  • Spleen: Palpate for splenomegaly.
  • Kidneys: Ballot kidneys.

4. Percussion:

  • Percuss over any masses (dull).
  • Check for shifting dullness (Ascites - peritoneal spread).

5. Auscultation:

  • Check bowel sounds.
  • Listen for succussion splash (indicates Gastric Outlet Obstruction if >4 hours post-meal).

Possible Findings & Interpretations

  • Hard Epigastric Mass: Primary Gastric Tumor.
  • Virchow's Node: Metastasis to left supraclavicular node (Troisier's sign).
  • Hepatomegaly: Liver metastases.
  • Ascites: Peritoneal carcinomatosis.
  • Succussion Splash: Pyloric obstruction by tumor.
  • Pallor: Anemia of chronic disease or GI blood loss.
30.
A 40-year-old female presents with weakness in the right arm, slurred speech, and difficulty walking. Outline a systematic neurological examination, emphasizing the possible findings and their interpretations in this case. (20 Marks)
Show Answer

Systematic Neurological Examination

The presentation suggests a left-sided brain lesion (Stroke/CVA) affecting the motor strip and speech area.

1. Higher Mental Function & Speech:

  • GCS: Assess consciousness.
  • Speech: Assess for Dysphasia (language difficulty - Broca's/Wernicke's) vs Dysarthria (slurred speech). Right-sided weakness implies Left Hemisphere lesion, where language centers usually are.

2. Cranial Nerves:

  • CN VII (Facial): Look for right-sided facial droop. UMN lesion spares the forehead; LMN affects whole side. Expect Right UMN facial palsy.
  • CN IX, X, XII: Assess gag reflex and tongue protrusion (deviates to weak side - Right).

3. Motor System (Compare Right vs Left):

  • Inspection: Check for muscle bulk (likely normal in acute phase) and posture (hemiplegic: flexed arm, extended leg).
  • Tone: Test resistance. Expect Hypertonia (Spasticity) on the Right side.
  • Power: Grade 0-5. Expect Weakness (Hemiparesis) on the Right side. Pattern: Flexors stronger in arm, Extensors stronger in leg.
  • Reflexes: Deep tendon reflexes. Expect Hyperreflexia (+++) on the Right side.
  • Plantar Response: Expect Positive Babinski (Upgoing toe) on the Right.

4. Sensory System:

  • Test light touch and pinprick. May show hemisensory loss on the Right side.

5. Coordination & Gait:

  • Gait: Observe walking. Expect Hemiplegic Gait (circumduction of right leg, right arm flexed).

Interpretations

The combination of Right Hemiparesis (Arm > Leg), Right UMN Facial Palsy, and Speech disturbance localizes the lesion to the Left Cerebral Hemisphere (likely Middle Cerebral Artery territory). The signs (Spasticity, Hyperreflexia, Babinski) confirm an Upper Motor Neuron (UMN) lesion.
