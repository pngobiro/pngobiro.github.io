Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions (20 Marks)

1.
Being empathetic while creating rapport during history means?
A. Showing concern
B. Feeling pity and sorrow
C. Greetings
D. Self-introduction
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Showing concern

Explanation: Empathy involves understanding and sharing the feelings of the patient ("putting yourself in their shoes") and demonstrating genuine concern for their wellbeing. This builds trust and rapport.

Why other options are incorrect:

  • B) Feeling pity and sorrow is "sympathy," not empathy. Sympathy implies feeling sorry for someone, whereas empathy implies feeling with them.
  • C) & D) These are procedural parts of starting an interview but do not define the emotional quality of empathy.
2.
Chronologically, physical examination relies on the following cardinal techniques
A. Inspection, Percussion, Palpation and Auscultation
B. Inspection, Palpation, Percussion and Auscultation
C. Inspection, Auscultation, Palpation and Percussion
D. Inspection, Auscultation, Percussion and Palpation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Inspection, Palpation, Percussion and Auscultation

Explanation: The standard order for most systems (Respiratory, Cardiovascular, etc.) is IPPA: Inspection, Palpation, Percussion, and Auscultation.

Note: For the Abdominal examination specifically, the order changes to Inspection, Auscultation, Percussion, Palpation (IAPP) to avoid altering bowel sounds. However, without a specific system specified, Option B represents the general standard rule.

3.
Back-and-forth oscillation of the eyes is called?
A. Nystagmus
B. Tremor
C. Ptosis
D. Strabismus
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Nystagmus

Explanation: Nystagmus is defined as repetitive, uncontrolled movements (oscillations) of the eyes. It can be horizontal, vertical, or rotary.

  • Tremor: Involuntary shaking of a body part (e.g., hands).
  • Ptosis: Drooping of the upper eyelid.
  • Strabismus: Misalignment of the eyes (squint).
4.
During abdominal examination you are able to bimanually palpate and ballot one of the intra-abdominal organ, which one?
A. Kidney
B. Liver
C. Urinary bladder
D. Spleen
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Kidney

Explanation: The kidneys are retroperitoneal organs that can be "balloted." This means if you place one hand anteriorly and one posteriorly in the flank, a sharp push from the posterior hand can float the kidney up to strike the anterior hand.

Why other options are incorrect:

  • Liver/Spleen: These are intraperitoneal (mostly) and usually palpated directly, not balloted in the same way (though an enlarged spleen is palpable, it is not typically described as "ballotable" like a kidney).
  • Urinary Bladder: Palpable when distended but not ballotable.
5.
During abdominal examination the following suggest presence of enlarged spleen
A. Its palpated beneath right costal margin
B. Its bimanually palpable
C. Splenic notch can be felt in the lower medial border
D. One can easily get above it
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Splenic notch can be felt in the lower medial border

Explanation: Characteristics of an enlarged spleen include: it enlarges towards the right iliac fossa (inferomedially), it has a palpable notch on its anterior/medial border, you cannot get above it (it originates from under the ribs), and it moves with respiration.

  • A: Spleen is on the LEFT, not right.
  • B: Bimanual palpation is characteristic of kidneys.
  • D: You cannot get above an enlarged spleen as it emerges from the costal margin.
6.
A positive shifting dullness is a sign of
A. Intestinal obstruction
B. Splenomegaly
C. Ascites
D. Pregnancy
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Ascites

Explanation: Shifting dullness is a specific sign for free fluid in the peritoneal cavity (ascites). The area of dullness shifts when the patient rolls to one side because the fluid moves due to gravity.

7.
The following is true regarding percussion during physical examination.
A. The thumb strikes the middle phalanx of the same middle finger
B. The middle finger strikes the middle phalanx of the other middle finger
C. The middle finger strikes the middle phalanx of the other index finger
D. The index finger strikes the middle phalanx of the other index finger
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. The middle finger strikes the middle phalanx of the other middle finger

Explanation: In standard indirect percussion, the pleximeter (finger on the body) is the middle phalanx of the non-dominant middle finger, and the plexor (striking finger) is the tip of the dominant middle finger.

8.
Regarding auscultation of the abdomen
A. Bowel sounds are best heard over the epigastrium
B. High pitched sounds suggest intestinal obstruction
C. Absence of bowel sounds for 5 seconds is abnormal
D. Normal bowel sounds are 20-25 per minute
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. High pitched sounds suggest intestinal obstruction

Explanation: High-pitched, tinkling bowel sounds are a classic sign of intestinal obstruction as fluid and air are forced under pressure through a narrowed lumen.

Why other options are incorrect:

  • A) Usually auscultated in the Right Lower Quadrant (ileocecal valve area).
  • C) Absence must be confirmed for at least 3-5 minutes, not seconds.
  • D) Normal range is typically 5-30 sounds per minute; while 20-25 is within range, B is a more distinct pathological finding often tested.
9.
The following is not included in Motor system during neurological examination.
A. Muscle bulk
B. Muscle tone
C. Coordination
D. Voluntary movements
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Coordination

Explanation: While related to movement, Coordination is strictly part of the Cerebellar examination. The Motor system examination formally consists of: Inspection (Bulk, Fasciculations), Tone, Power, and Reflexes. Voluntary movement assessments (Power) are part of the motor system.

10.
In assessing joint range of motion, which technique is most commonly used?
A. Inspection
B. Passive Movement
C. Palpation
D. Active Movement
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Active Movement

Explanation: The standard approach is to ask the patient to perform Active Movement first (screening for pain and limitations). If active motion is limited, the examiner then performs Passive Movement to assess the joint capsule/structure.

11.
What is the main purpose of the "Trendelenburg test"?
A. To assess hip stability
B. To evaluate knee strength
C. To test for wrist arthritis
D. To examine spinal alignment
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. To assess hip stability

Explanation: The Trendelenburg test assesses the strength and stability of the hip abductors (mainly Gluteus Medius). A positive test occurs when the pelvis drops on the unsupported side, indicating weakness on the standing side.

12.
Which of the following is the correct sequence for removing PPE?
A. Gloves, gown, goggles, mask
B. Mask, gown, goggles, gloves
C. Gown, gloves, masks, goggles
D. Gloves, mask, goggles, gown
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Gloves, gown, goggles, mask

Explanation: CDC guidelines for doffing (removing) PPE typically prioritize removing the most contaminated items first.
1. Gloves (dirtiest)
2. Gown (or Gown & Gloves together)
3. Hand Hygiene
4. Goggles/Face Shield (handle by strap)
5. Mask/Respirator (last to protect airway)

13.
The correct angle for intramuscular injection is
A. 15 degrees
B. 45 degrees
C. 60 degrees
D. 90 degrees
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. 90 degrees

Explanation: Intramuscular (IM) injections are administered at a 90-degree angle to penetrate deep into the muscle tissue. Subcutaneous is 45-90 degrees, and Intradermal is 10-15 degrees.

14.
Which of the following is not a risk associated with unsafe injection practices
A. Transmission of blood borne diseases
B. Development of antibiotic resistance
C. Local site infections
D. Needle stick injuries
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Development of antibiotic resistance

Explanation: Antibiotic resistance is caused by misuse or overuse of antibiotics, not by the procedure of injection itself. Unsafe injections (e.g., re-using needles) directly cause transmission of diseases (HIV, Hep B), abscesses (local infection), and injury to providers (needle sticks).

15.
Which of the following is not a recommended practice for injection safety
A. Use of single use syringes
B. Recapping needles after use
C. Using auto-disable syringes
D. Proper disposal in sharps container
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Recapping needles after use

Explanation: Recapping needles is a major cause of needle stick injuries and is strongly discouraged unless using a specific one-handed "scoop" technique, but generally it is considered an unsafe practice in standard safety guidelines.

16.
A painless scrotal swelling with a positive trans illumination test is most likely due to
A. Testicular torsion
B. Epididymitis
C. Varicocele
D. Hydrocele
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Hydrocele

Explanation: A Hydrocele is a fluid collection in the tunica vaginalis. Fluid transmits light (transilluminates) and is typically painless. Solid masses (tumors) or blood-filled swellings do not transilluminate.

17.
A digital rectal examination (DRE) is performed to evaluate which of the following structures?
A. Prostate
B. Spleen
C. Kidneys
D. Appendix
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Prostate

Explanation: DRE allows direct palpation of the posterior surface of the prostate gland in males. It is also used to assess the rectum, anal tone, and check for fecal impaction or masses.

18.
Signs of meningitis include:
A. Nuchal Rigidity and Positive Kernig's Sign
B. Babinski Reflex and Dysdiadochokinesia
C. Romberg's Test and Ataxia
D. Tremors and Fasciculations
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Nuchal Rigidity and Positive Kernig's Sign

Explanation: The classic signs of meningeal irritation are:
1. Nuchal Rigidity (stiff neck).
2. Kernig's Sign (pain on knee extension when hip is flexed).
3. Brudzinski's Sign (flexion of hips/knees when neck is flexed).

19.
Which investigation is commonly performed to detect blood in the urine?
A. Urinalysis
B. Ultrasound
C. CT scan
D. Cystoscopy
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Urinalysis

Explanation: Urinalysis (using a dipstick or microscopy) is the primary screening test to detect the presence of red blood cells (hematuria) in urine.

Section 2: Short Answer Questions (40 Marks)

21.
Describe how you would assess a patient for splenomegaly, including the findings you might encounter. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Assessment of Splenomegaly:

  1. Positioning: Place patient in supine position with abdomen exposed. Can also use right lateral decubitus position.
  2. Palpation Technique:
    • Start palpable in the Right Iliac Fossa (RIF).
    • Move diagonally towards the Left Upper Quadrant (LUQ) while asking the patient to breathe deeply in and out.
    • Feel for the spleen edge meeting your fingertips on inspiration.
  3. Percussion: Percuss over Traube's space (9th-11th rib left mid-axillary line). Resonance suggests normal; dullness suggests splenomegaly.

Findings (Characteristics of Splenic Enlargement):

  • Palpable notch on the anterior/medial border.
  • Enlarges inferomedially (towards RIF).
  • Cannot get above the swelling (starts under ribs).
  • Moves with respiration.
  • Dull to percussion.
22.
Explain how you would perform and interpret the fluid thrill test in a patient suspected of having ascites. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Procedure:

  1. Ask the patient (or an assistant) to place the ulnar border of their hand firmly vertically along the midline of the abdomen. This stops the transmission of a wave through the abdominal wall fat/muscles.
  2. Place your left hand flat on the patient's right flank.
  3. With your right hand, flick or tap sharply on the patient's left flank.

Interpretation:

  • Positive Test: You feel a distinct tap or fluid wave/impulse against your left hand. This indicates a large volume of free fluid (tense ascites) transmitting the shock wave across the abdomen.
  • Negative Test: No impulse is felt.
23.
Describe the steps for conducting a suprapubic catheterization and outline the indications for this procedure. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Indications:

  • Acute or chronic urinary retention where urethral catheterization has failed or is contraindicated.
  • Urethral trauma/stricture.
  • Management of incontinence in specific cases (e.g., spinal cord injury).

Steps:

  1. Verify: Ensure bladder is palpable/distended (using percussion or ultrasound).
  2. Preparation: Clean skin with antiseptic; infiltrate local anesthetic 2cm above pubic symphysis in midline.
  3. Incision/Insertion: Make small skin incision. Insert suprapubic trocar/cannula vertically then angled slightly downwards into the bladder.
  4. Confirmation: Urine flows out. Advance catheter through cannula.
  5. Securing: Inflate balloon, suture catheter to skin if needed, and attach drainage bag.
24.
Outline steps for conducting musculoskeletal exam. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Standard Approach (Look, Feel, Move):

  1. Screening: Perform GALS screen (Gait, Arms, Legs, Spine) for quick assessment.
  2. Inspection (Look): Look for swelling, deformity, redness, muscle wasting, scars, or asymmetry.
  3. Palpation (Feel): Feel for temperature (warmth), tenderness, crepitus, and joint effusion.
  4. Movement (Move):
    • Active: Patient moves joint themselves (assesses range and function).
    • Passive: Examiner moves joint (assesses capsule/joint surface).
  5. Special Tests: Perform joint-specific tests (e.g., Lachman's for knee, Phalen's for wrist).
25.
Outline the five components of motor system examinations in CNS. (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Inspection: Muscle bulk (wasting/hypertrophy) and involuntary movements (fasciculations, tremors).
  2. Tone: Resistance to passive movement (hypotonia vs. hypertonia - spasticity/rigidity).
  3. Power: Strength testing grading from 0 to 5.
  4. Reflexes: Deep tendon reflexes (biceps, triceps, knee, ankle, etc.).
  5. Coordination: Assessments like finger-nose test, heel-shin test (though often grouped separately, it assesses motor control). Alternatively, Gait is often included here.

27.
Discuss the procedure and clinical interpretation of the Romberg test. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Procedure:

  1. Ask patient to stand with feet together and arms at sides.
  2. Ask patient to keep eyes open initially (to establish stability).
  3. Ask patient to close their eyes while maintaining the position. Stand close to prevent falling.

Clinical Interpretation:

  • Positive Romberg's: Patient is stable with eyes open but sways/falls with eyes closed. This indicates Sensory Ataxia (loss of proprioception/dorsal column function). They rely on vision for balance; when vision is removed, they fall.
  • Cerebellar Ataxia: Patient is unstable even with eyes open. This is not a positive Romberg's sign technically, but a sign of cerebellar disease.
28.
Describe two methods of conducting hearing test exam. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

1. Whisper Test (Gross Hearing):

  • Stand behind patient (arm's length).
  • Mask the non-test ear by rubbing the tragus.
  • Whisper a combination of numbers/letters and ask patient to repeat.

2. Tuning Fork Tests (Rinne's & Weber's):

  • Rinne's Test: Place vibrating 512Hz fork on mastoid (Bone Conduction), then in front of ear (Air Conduction). Normal is AC > BC.
  • Weber's Test: Place vibrating fork on vertex of forehead. Sound should be heard equally in both ears. Lateralization indicates pathology (conductive vs sensorineural).

Section 3: Long Answer Questions (40 Marks)

29.
A 60-year-old male presents with a two-month history of unintentional weight loss, loss of appetite, and upper abdominal pain. Outline a systematic approach to conducting a per abdominal examination in this patient. Discuss possible findings and their interpretations. (20 marks)
Show Answer

Systematic Abdominal Examination

1. Preparation: Introduce self, consent, chaperone, position supine with one pillow, expose abdomen (nipples to knees), ensure privacy and warmth.

2. General Inspection:

  • Patient: Wasting (cachexia), pallor (anemia), jaundice (liver/biliary), supraclavicular lymphadenopathy (Virchow's node - Troisier's sign for gastric CA).

3. Inspection of Abdomen:

  • Contour: Distension (ascites/mass), Scaphoid (starvation).
  • Skin: Scars, dilated veins (caput medusae), striae.
  • Movement: With respiration. Visible pulsations (AAA). Visible peristalsis (obstruction).

4. Palpation (Ask about pain first):

  • Superficial: Assess tenderness, guarding, temperature.
  • Deep: Feel for masses. In this patient (epigastric pain), focus on epigastrium.
  • Organ palpation:
    • Liver: Hepatomegaly? (metastasis). Irregular edge?
    • Spleen: Splenomegaly?
    • Kidneys: Ballot.
    • Epigastric Mass: Hard, fixed mass suggests Gastric Carcinoma.

5. Percussion:

  • Liver Span: Estimate size.
  • Ascites: Shifting dullness/fluid thrill (peritoneal spread).

6. Auscultation:

  • Bowel sounds.
  • Renal/Aortic bruits.
  • Succussion splash (Gastric outlet obstruction).

Possible Findings & Interpretations

  • Hard Epigastric Mass: Gastric Cancer (primary tumor).
  • Hepatomegaly (Knobbly/Hard): Liver metastases.
  • Virchow's Node (Left Supraclavicular): Metastatic spread via thoracic duct.
  • Sister Mary Joseph Nodule: Umbilical nodule (metastasis).
  • Ascites: Peritoneal carcinomatosis.
  • Succussion Splash: Tumor blocking pylorus (Gastric Outlet Obstruction).
30.
A 40-year-old female presents with weakness in the right arm, slurred speech, and difficulty walking. Outline a systematic neurological examination, emphasizing the possible findings and their interpretations in this case. (20 Marks)
Show Answer

Systematic Neurological Examination (Focus: Stroke/CVA)

1. Higher Mental Function:

  • GCS: Level of consciousness.
  • Speech: Assess for Dysarthria (slurring - motor problem) vs Dysphasia (language problem - Broca's/Wernicke's).

2. Cranial Nerves:

  • CN VII (Facial): Look for facial asymmetry/droop. Upper motor neuron lesion (spares forehead) vs Lower (entire side). Right arm weakness suggests Left Hemisphere lesion, so expect Right facial droop (UMN).
  • CN IX/X/XII: Gag reflex, tongue deviation (deviates to affected side).

3. Motor System (Compare Right vs Left):

  • Inspection: Muscle wasting (chronic) vs normal bulk (acute). Posture (hemiplegic posture: flexed arm, extended leg).
  • Tone: Assess resistance. Expect Hypertonia (Spasticity) on the Right side (pyramidal sign). "Clasp-knife" rigidity.
  • Power: Grade 0-5. Expect reduced power (Hemiparesis) on Right side (e.g., Grade 2/5). Pattern: Extensors weaker in arms, Flexors weaker in legs.
  • Reflexes: Deep tendon reflexes. Expect Hyperreflexia (+++) on Right side.
  • Plantars (Babinski): Stroke outer sole. Expect Upgoing (Extensor) plantar response on Right side (UMN lesion sign).

4. Sensory System:

  • Test light touch/pinprick. May have hemisensory loss on Right side.

5. Coordination (Cerebellar):

  • Finger-nose test. May be difficult due to weakness, but if disproportionate, suggests cerebellar involvement.

6. Gait:

  • Hemiplegic Gait: Circumduction of the affected (Right) leg due to extensor hypertonia. Arm held flexed across chest.

Interpretations

The constellation of Right hemiparesis (arm weakness), speech disturbance (dysarthria/dysphasia), and UMN signs (spasticity, hyperreflexia, upgoing plantar) localizes the lesion to the Left Cerebral Hemisphere (likely Middle Cerebral Artery territory). The pathology is likely a Stroke (Ischemic or Hemorrhagic) or space-occupying lesion.
