ü©∫ Arteriosclerosis & Atherosclerosis

üìù

Definitions

Arteriosclerosis is a broad term referring to narrowing and hardening of arteries leading to poor blood circulation.

Atherosclerosis: it‚Äôs a cardiovascular disease that involves building up of fat, cholesterol and other substances in and on the artery walls causing obstruction to blood flow.

Predisposing Factors

  • Age
  • High cholesterol levels
  • High BP
  • Smoking
  • Diabetes
  • Lack of physical activity
  • Eating saturated fat
  • Insulin resistance
  • Obesity
  • Family history
  • Unhealthy diet

Signs and Symptoms

  • Chest pain
  • Sudden weakness in the arms
  • Difficult in speech
  • Temporary loss of vision in one eye
  • Drooping muscles of the face
  • Erectile dysfunction

Complications

  • Coronary artery disease
  • Carotid artery disease
  • Aneurism (bulging in the walls of an artery)
  • Chronic kidney disease

Prevention & Management

Prevention:

  • Quitting smoking
  • Eating health food
  • Maintaining a healthy weight
  • Checking and maintaining a healthy blood pressure
  • Checking and maintaining healthy cholesterol and blood sugar levels

Diagnosis: History taking about the family and personal life, Blood tests for cholesterol and glucose level, Electrocardiogram, Exercise stress test echocardiogram, Imaging tests (magnetic resonance angiogram).

Management: Use blood thinners like aspirin to minimize clot formation. Blood pressure medications should be taken in case of hypertension.

Specific Nursing Care

  • Stop smoking as this damage the arteries
  • Do regular exercises to improve on blood flow
  • Eat healthy diet i.e foods with low fat and cholesterol levels
  • Manage stress effectively
  • Manage any other chronic illness effectively

ü´Å Pulmonary Embolism (PE)

It‚Äôs a collection matter (solid, liquid or air) that enters the Venus circulation and lodges in the pulmonary vessel.

‚ö†Ô∏è

Embolus Types

The embolus formed may be of: Fat/oil, Air, Tumour, Amniotic fluid, Foreign bodies e.g. broken IV catheters, Injected particles.

Risk Factors for VTE/PE

  • Prolonged immobility
  • Central venous catheters
  • Surgery
  • Obesity
  • Advancing age
  • Conditions that increase blood clotting
  • History of thrombo- embolism
  • Estrogen therapy
  • Heart failure
  • Stroke
  • Cancer

Signs and Symptoms

  • Sudden onset
  • Dyspnea
  • Sharp stabbing chest pain inspiration
  • Restlessness
  • Cough
  • Haemoptysis
  • Crackles
  • Tachypnea
  • Pleural friction
  • Diaphoresis
  • Fever
  • Petechiae over chest and axillae
  • Decreased arterial circulation

Management

Aims: to allay anxiety, To restore the normal oxygen circulation.

  • Admit the patient on a medical ward
  • Asses the patient for respiration to rule out decreased gas exchange, difficulty in breathing, Pleuritic chest pain, haemoptysis due to pulmonary infarction, cyanosis etc
  • Position the patient in the most comfortable position
  • Reassure the patients and her relatives to allay anxiety
  • Take all the vital observations that is BP/TPR
  • Call the doctor who will reassess the patient, order for investigations and prescribe the drugs accordingly

Investigations: Computed tomography pulmonary angiography (CT‚ÄîPA), Chest x-ray, Doppler ultra sound used to document presence of VTE.

Specific Management:

  • Administer oxygen using a nasal cannula or mask
  • Place the patient in a high fowler‚Äôs position
  • Asses the level of oxygen circulation continuously with pulse oximeter
  • Asses the respiratory status at least 1/2 hourly listen to the lung sounds, ease of respiration should be noted, check the skin colour and capillary refill
  • Check the cardiac status, BP, distension of neck veins
  • Examine the throat for presence of Petechiae
  • Administer prescribed anticoagulants i.e heparin sodium 5000-10000 units bolus, then adjust to 1300 units /hour; Warfarin sodium 10-15 mg orally o.d 3/7 initially then 5-10 mg od
  • Asses for bleeding
  • Handle the patient gently

üß± Coronary Thrombosis / Occlusion

Definitions

Coronary thrombosis: This is the formation of blood clot (thrombus) in the coronary artery, resulting into coronary occlusion.

Coronary occlusion: this refers to the obstruction of the normal flow of blood through the coronary artery.

Causes & Predisposing Factors

Causes

  • Thrombosis - formation of a blood clot in a coronary artery
  • Embolism - dislodgement of an embolus
  • Infections or inflammation (vasculitis/ arteritis)
  • Arteriosclerosis
  • Atherosclerosis - formation of fatty plagues

Predisposing Factors

  • Hypertension
  • DM
  • Cigarette smoking
  • Obesity
  • High cholesterol diet
  • Sedentary life style
  • Stress
  • Limited exercises

Signs and Symptoms

  • Onset is sudden.
  • Sudden painful pressure felt in the upper part of the chest occasionary radiating to the arm, throat or back.
  • Sudden severe headache
  • Nausea and vomiting.
  • Profuse sweating.
  • Pallor.
  • Anxiety.
  • Severe dyspnoea with cyanosis.
  • Fatigue with dizziness.
  • Loss of balance.
  • Visual disturbance.
  • Signs of shock.
üí°

Differentiation Note

Coronary occlusion/thrombosis is different from angina pectoris in that; coronary occlusion persists for hours or days. Pain comes at rest not during exertion or exercises.

Management

Immediate care:

  • Admit patient in a quiet well ventilated room.
  • Position patient in a sitting up position to aid breathing.
  • Ensure complete bed rest and loosen all the tight clothing‚Äôs
  • Treat and prevent shock ‚Äì access and connect I.V fluids and keep the patient warm.
  • Observations ‚Äì vital observations and other observations.
  • Administer oxygen to support respiration and correct cyanosis.
  • Reassure patient and patient‚Äôs relatives and always stay and attend to the patient to allay anxiety.

Drug therapy:

Drug Class Example & Action
Pain Relievers Morphine 5-15 mg 3-4 times or whenever necessary or cardiac ASA (aspirin) 150-300 mg three times a day.
Emergency Adrenaline (epinephrine) 0.1 mg in order to raise blood pressure in case of shock.
Anticoagulants Heparin I.M./I.V 5000-10000 units for quick effects. Oral anticoagulant‚Äîwarfarin initially 15 mg once a day for 1-2 days then maintenance dose 2.5-7.5 mg once a day. (To prevent extension of clot).
Thrombolytic Streptokinase, urokinase to break down the clot.
Hypotensive Beta blockers, calcium channel blockers if there is high blood pressure.

NOTE: patient on anticoagulants must be monitored for bleeding tendencies. An antidote for the overdose of heparin is PROTAMINE SULPHATE I.V 1 mg for every 10 units of heparin.

Subsequent nursing care: Diet (cardiac diet), Patient‚Äôs continuous observations, Exercises (passive then active), Health educate on avoidance of predisposing factors.
