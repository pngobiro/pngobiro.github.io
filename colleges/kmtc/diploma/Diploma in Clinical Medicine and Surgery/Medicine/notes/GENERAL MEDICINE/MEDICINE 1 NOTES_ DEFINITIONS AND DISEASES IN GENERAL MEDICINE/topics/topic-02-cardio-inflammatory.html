ü©∫ Heart Disease Overview

Definition

Refers to the pathological condition of the heart i.e. the heart is imperfect either in structure, function or both.

Causes of Heart Disease

ü¶† Infections & Inflammation

  • Rheumatic fever- Leads to the narrowing of the heart valves causing obstruction of the blood flow within the heart, backflow of blood thus insufficiency of the heart function.
  • Syphilis-this causes aortic dilatation and aortic contraction which narrows it and causes backflow.
  • Infections- in acute infections, toxins may affect the heart muscles e.g. in typhoid fever, tonsillitis, pharyngitis etc.

üß¨ Structural & Systemic

  • Congenital abnormalities- heart disease occurs during growth and development of the heart during intra-uterine life (pregnancy) especially in 1st and 2nd trimester.
  • Hypertension-in hypertension, blood vessels hardens and narrows and this causes cardiac overload so as to pump blood through the narrowed arteries.
  • Thyrotoxicosis- this leads to increased metabolism due to increased tissue demand thus the heart has to work harder to meet tissues demand

Other causes include:

  • Chronic lung disease-heart disease which is secondary to lung condition is called Cor-pulmonale. Lung conditions like lung cancer, chronic bronchitis, lung abscess, and pleural effusion.
  • Anaemia-In anaemia the heart itself is not receiving enough oxygenated blood and necessary factors to enable it pump blood properly. The heart muscle becomes and eventually fails.
  • Alcoholism and smoking- alcohol weakens the heart muscles (this is called alcoholic cardiopathy). In smokers cigarette contains nicotine which constricts/narrows the blood vessels leading to hypertension.
  • Malnutrition-this leads to nutritional cardiopathy as a result of lack essential nutrients thus making the heart muscles weak.

Signs and Symptoms

  • Chest pain-this is the most common symptom. The pain varies according to condition like retrosternal heavy or gripping sensation with radiations to the left arm or neck provoked by exertion and relieved by rest especially in angina pectoris.
  • Dyspnoea (breathlessness)- this is due to oedema of the alveoli and pulmonary interstitial, dyspnoea may be orthopnea, paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnoea.
  • Palpitations- this is increased awareness of the heart beat or feeling of slow, rapid or irregular heartbeats/ rhythms.
  • Syncope-this is temporary transient loss of consciousness due to inadequate blood flow to the brain.
  • Fatigue-this is due to inadequate systemic perfusion
  • Peripheral oedema-due to salt and water retention brought about renal under perfusion and consequent activation of the Rennin Angiotensin Aldosterone System (RAAS)
  • Clubbing of the fingers
  • Cyanosis
  • Irregular thread pulse
  • Raised jugular venous pressure and extended jugular veins.
  • On auscultation heart murmurs are heard.

üå°Ô∏è Rheumatic Fever

Definition

Is an inflammatory disease that affects the connective tissues particularly the heart, joints, muscles and skin.

In the cardiovascular system, it is an inflammatory disease of the heart potentially involving all layers of the heart. This is known as rheumatic heart disease.

Causes & Pathophysiology

The exact cause is unknown; however rheumatic fever appears 2-3 weeks after a streptococcal infection in boils, tonsillitis, pharyngitis, sore throat. The streptococcal infection is particularly caused by group A Beta haemolytic streptococci. Rheumatic fever is mainly found in children (5-15 years) and also in young adults.

üîÑ

Pathophysiology

Rheumatic fever occurs after 2-3 weeks of group A Beta hemolytic streptococcal infection of upper respiratory tract infections like tonsillitis, pharyngitis, sore throat etc. In response to infection antibodies are produced causing immunological or inflammatory responses like irritation, oedema/swelling, pain, redness, heat and loss of function in the affected connective tissues.

Signs and Symptoms

  • Sudden onset (10-21 days after sore throat pharyngitis tonsillitis).
  • Raised body temperature
  • General malaise and fatigue
  • Loss of appetite
  • Weight loss
  • Chest pain
  • Abdominal pain
  • Migratory joint pains and tenderness (especially large joints)
  • Difficulty in breathing
  • Swelling, heat, redness and limited movement in knees, ankles, elbow, and shoulder and wrist joints
  • Subcutaneous nodules and erythema marginatum
  • Tachycardia, heart murmurs peripheral oedema
  • Pericardial friction rub on auscultation
  • Chorea- involuntary, purposeless, rapid movements
üí°

Clinical Aphorism

NOTE: Rheumatic fever bites the heart and licks the joints

Management Aims

  • To eradicate the causative microorganism
  • To relieve pain and fever
  • To reduce on the cardiac workload

Nursing & Medical Management

  • Admit a patient who is in acute phase
  • Ensure complete bed rest to rest the heart and the joints
  • Position- preferably sitting up position especially in breathlessness.
  • Apply a bed cradle to reduce weight or relieve pressure of clothes on the affected joints
  • Apply warm or cold compresses on the affected joints to relieve pains
  • Observations- vital observations i.e. Temperature, pulse, respiration and blood pressure and record them in the patient's file.
  • Tepid sponging to reduce on the raised body temperatures.
  • Diet- A light diet in acute phase to neutralize the toxins if there is no oedema severe heart involvement. Normal diet is started when the patient recovers from acute phase. It should be highly nutritious with plenty of proteins, vitamins fluids and roughages.

Drug Therapy

Category Medication & Dosage
Analgesics Aspirin 125-150mg/kg body weight in divided doses in children, 6-8 adults in acute phase in divided doses of 4-6 hours and 8 hourly when the condition improves. Aspirin is a drug of choice in joint pains & a high dose is given.
Antibiotics I.M Benzathine penicillin 1.2-2.4 mega units weekly and prophylactically every 2-4 months until 18 years (if not life time treatment).
Or phenoxymethyl penicillin (Pen V) orally
Or erythromycin or azithromycin 250-500mg if hypersensitive to penicillin

üíî Rheumatic Heart Disease (RHD)

This is a chronic condition characterized by scaring and deformity of the heart valves. It is a complication of rheumatic fever due to poorly treated upper respiratory tract infections like tonsillitis, pharyngitis and dental decays.

üìù

Rheumatic Pancarditis

Rheumatic fever episodes are marked by carditis and affect all the layers of the heart (endocardium, myocardium, and pericardium). This generalized involvement is called rheumatic pancarditis.

  • Rheumatic endocarditis: Affects the valves. Characterized by swelling and erosion of the valve leaflets. The mitral and aortic valves are affected most.
  • Rheumatic Myocarditis: Characterized by nodules called ASCOFF‚ÄôS BODIES.
  • Rheumatic Pericarditis: Affects both layers of the pericardium which become thickened and covered with a fibrous exudates.

Clinical Presentation

  • Dyspnoea
  • Palpitations from atrial fibrillation
  • Fatigue and general body weakness
  • Haemoptysis
  • Chest pain
  • Activity intolerance
  • Syncope
  • Pulmonary oedema
  • Heart murmurs on auscultation

Management

Drug Therapy:

  • Prophylactic antibiotic therapy in rheumatic fever and infective endocarditis.
  • Digitalis-digoxin 0.25mg-9.75mg once a day when the pulse is >60 b/m.
  • Diuretics ‚Äìlike furosemide 20-40 mg 2-3 times a day, max 80 mg to relieve oedema.
  • Nitrates- like glyceryl trinitrate 0.3-1mg sub-lingual.
  • Anticoagulants-like aspirin 75-150mg max 300mg, warfarin 5-7mg, max 15 mg (after determining the clotting and the bleeding time).

Surgical Interventions: Valvoplasty, Valvotomy, Valve replacement.

üõ°Ô∏è Pericarditis

This is the inflammation of the pericardium.

Causes

Pericarditis usually occurs as a complication of other diseases. These include:

  • Acute rheumatism
  • Infections like viral infections (mumps, HIV, herpes viruses), bacterial infections (streptococci, staphylococci, pneumococci, meningococcal, Haemophilus influenza), fungal infections (like Candida,)
  • Post myocardial infarction.
  • Malignant disorders- tumors of the heart (mesothelioma), metastatic Pericarditis due to breast and lung cancer, leukemia.
  • Excess blood urea (uremic) Pericarditis

Types

Acute Pericarditis: This is due to fibrous material being deposited into the pericardial space which can be dry with fibrous scars or wet with effusion.

Chronic or constrictive Pericarditis: As a result, the pericardium becomes thick, fibrous and calcified. If the pericardium becomes inelastic that it interferes with the diastolic filling of the heart, this is called constrictive Pericarditis.

Cardiac Tamponade

Collection of fluids within the potential space of the serous pericardial sac is called pericardial effusion. When large volumes of pericardial serous fluid collects in the pericardial space, causing ventricular filling to be compromised leading to embarrassment of the circulation is known as cardiac tamponade.

Clinical Manifestations

  • The onset is sudden with pericardial chest pain. The pain is central and sharp exacerbated by movement, swallowing, and respiration and lying down. It radiates to the shoulders and neck.
  • High body temperatures (in infections)
  • Rapid pulse
  • Difficulty in breathing‚Äîdue to pericardial fluid compression
  • Increased jugular pressure
  • On auscultation, a pericardial friction rub will be heard
  • On chest x-ray, pericardial effusion and cardiac tamponade will be seen

Management

  • Pericardio-centesis- to drain the excess pericardial fluids in order to relieve pressure on to the lungs and heart.
  • In chronic constrictive Pericarditis, percardiectomy is suggestive.
  • Drug therapy: Analgesics (aspirin, indomethacin), Antibiotics, Corticosteroids (prednisolone).

üí™ Myocarditis

This is the inflammation of the myocardium.

Causes

  • Idiopathic
  • Infections like: viral infections (adenovirus, cytomegalo virus, influenza, hepatitis, HIV.), bacterial infections (streptococci-most commonly rheumatic carditis, diphtheria-causes toxic-mediated heart block), fungal infections, Spirochetes- Lyme disease
  • Toxins: drugs like penicillins, sulphonamides, anti-TB agents, methyldopa- as these causes hypersensitivity reactions.
  • Radiations
  • Autoimmune
  • Alcohol

Signs and Symptoms

  • Initially it is asymptomatic if the inflammation is limited focal
  • Later: Fatigue, Palpitations, Chest Pains, Dyspnoea, Fainting Attacks, Tachycardia
  • On Auscultation, a broad heart sound, friction rub sound will be hear

Management

  • Admit patient in acute phase with exaggerated signs and symptoms.
  • Ensure complete bed rest in order to rest the diseased heart.
  • Avoid strenuous exercises for about 6 months
  • Drug therapy -Diuretics e.g. Beta blockers, ace inhibitors, Digitalis, Antibiotics.

üö™ Endocarditis

This is the inflammation of the endocardium. It is limited to the valves and the most commonly affected valves are the mitral (bi-cuspid valve). Endocarditis is mainly due to infections (Infective endocarditis).

Clinical Manifestations

  • Fever (persistent fever) with chills
  • General body weakness/ malaise
  • Joint and muscle pains (arthralgias and myalgias respectively).
  • Clubbing of fingers
  • Splinter hemorrhages
  • Petechiae - due to micro-embolization of vegetative lesions common in conjunctiva, lips, buccal mucosa, over the ankles and feet.
  • Osler‚Äôs nodes - painful, tender reddish purple pea size lesions found on the fingertips or toes.
  • Janeways‚Äô lesions - flat painless, small red spots found on the palms and soles.
  • Roth‚Äôs spots - these are haemorrhagic retinal lesions seen on funoscopic examination.

Management

  • Acute infective endocarditis requires hospitalization.
  • Complete bed rest.
  • Drug therapy- administer drugs as prescribed by the doctor i.e. Benzyl penicillin intravenously initially 2-4 mega units q.i.d and then 500000 units 4-6 hourly in case of streptococcus pyogens, aureus, and viridians.
  • Gentamycin 80-160mg intravenously/intramuscularly three times a day. Or tetracycline 250-500mg three times a day.
