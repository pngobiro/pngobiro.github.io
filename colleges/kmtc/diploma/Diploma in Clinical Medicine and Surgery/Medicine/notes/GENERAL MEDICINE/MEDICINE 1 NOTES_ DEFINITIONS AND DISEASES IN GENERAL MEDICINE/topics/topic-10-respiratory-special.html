ü¶† Pulmonary Tuberculosis (TB)

Definition

It's an infectious disease caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis. It's a chronic disease which affects the lungs due to tubercle bacillus. Tubercle bacillus is an acid fast bacillus.

Pathophysiology

Tubercle Formation: A tubercle is a collection of small endothelial cells and lymphocytes around the bacilli. The tubercles merge so that a wide area of tissue may be affected. This process may then be followed by caseation (necrosis or breakdown of tubercle into cheesy mass) and fibrosis (scar formation).

Types & Causes

  • Latent TB: Does not show signs and symptoms.
  • Active TB: Shows signs and symptoms.

Major Causes:

  • Mycobacterium tuberculosis: Spread from person to person mainly through droplet infection.
  • Mycobacterium bovis: Spread from infected cow's milk.

Signs and Symptoms

  • Weight loss
  • Profuse night sweats
  • Chest pain on coughing
  • Productive cough
  • Haemoptysis
  • Evening fevers which don't respond to anti malaria
  • Fatigue & Anorexia
  • Palpitations
  • Enlarged lymph nodes
  • Difficulty in breathing

Management of TB

Drugs Used (First Line):

  1. Isoniazid (INH) (H)
  2. Rifampcin (Rifadin) (R)
  3. Pyrazinamide (PZA) (Z)
  4. Ethambutol (Myambutol) (E)
  5. Streptomycin (S)

Short Course Treatment Regimen

Patient Category Definition Initial Phase (Intensive) Continuous Phase
New Case Patient getting TB treatment for the first time. 2 months RHZE 4 months HR
Defaulter Patient who abandoned treatment before completion. 2 months SRHZE / 1 month RHZE 5 months RHE
Relapse Patient declared cured but infection returned. 2 months SRHZE / 1 month RHZE 5 months RHE
Children / Other Any other form of TB and in children. 2 months RHZ 2 months RH

CBDOTS Strategy

When the patient improves, they are discharged and start taking drugs through Community Based Directly Observed Therapy Services (CBDOTS). Whenever the patient is to take drugs, they are observed by implementers (VHTs) to ensure adherence.

Prevention

Early childhood immunization (BCG), Isolation of positive cases, Good nutrition, Avoid over crowded places, Avoid drinking half cooked milk.

üëÇ Otitis Media

It's the inflammation of the mucus membrane of the middle ear, Eustachian tube and the mastoids. It is a common condition in children below 3 years and usually follows a common cold, sore throat, or measles.

Types

  • Acute otitis media: Acute infection less than 6 weeks.
  • Serous otitis media: Middle ear effusion (fluids without active infection).
  • Chronic otitis media: Recurrent acute otitis media causing irreversible tissue pathology (more than 12 weeks).

Signs and Symptoms

  • Severe ear ache (Otalgia)
  • Hearing loss
  • Otorrhea (drainage/pus from ear)
  • Fever and chills
  • Rubbing and pulling ears (in children)
  • Irritability and Insomnia

Management

Aims: To relieve pain, To restore the hearing balance, To prevent complications.

  • Antibiotics can be used oral or intravenous.
  • Analgesics for pain relief.
  • Ventilating tubes can be inserted into the opening of the ear drum to help fluids drain.
  • In chronic O.M, the doctor cleans the ear canal with suction and instills a solution of acetic acid with hydrocortisone.

Complications

Perforated tympanic membrane, Otosclerosis (hardening of ear), Mastoiditis, Deafness, Septicaemia.

üå¨Ô∏è Bronchial Asthma

It is a chronic inflammation of the airway leading to narrowing of the airway due to bronchospasms, over secretions of mucus and oedema. Asthma is reversible.

Types

Intrinsic Asthma (Late Onset)

Cause is not known but is predisposed by chronic chest infections, emotional upset, damp atmosphere.

Extrinsic Asthma (Early Onset)

Occurs in early childhood and is normally due to allergy from animal hair, moulds, scents, and other inhaled substances.

Signs and Symptoms

  • Difficulty in breathing with chest tightness
  • Wheezing
  • Dry cough usually persistent
  • Cyanosis in severe cases
  • Rapid pulse
  • In severe cases, patients may not be able to speak or complete a sentence

Status Asthmaticus

Severe Acute Asthma: A medical emergency diagnosed by failure of a patient to complete a sentence/word.

Management

Immediate Care:

  • Admit to medical ward in a well-ventilated room.
  • Position in a sitting up position.
  • Loosen all tight clothes.
  • Administer oxygen.
  • Drug Therapy:
    • Salbutamol nebulizer or inhaler (100mg or 1 puff every 30 seconds if improving).
    • Hydrocortisone IV (2 puffs or injection).
    • Aminophylline may be used.

Note: Never give beta blockers and aspirin in asthmatic patients. Give Panadol for pain relief.

Discharge Advice

  • Encourage the patient to avoid allergens like pollens, fumes.
  • Encourage early treatment of URTI.
  • Counsel on drug compliance.
  • In case of any attack, the patient should come back for management.
