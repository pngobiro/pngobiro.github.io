üß¨ Leukaemia

It is a term that describes a group of malignant disorders affecting the blood and blood forming tissues of the bone marrow characterized by excessive multiplication of WBCs. It affects bone marrow, lymph system and spleen.

üîç

Types of Leukemia

  • Acute myelogenous leukemia (AML): Characterized by uncontrolled proliferation of myeloblasts (Hyperplasia of bone marrow and spleen).
  • Acute lymphocytic leukemia (ALL): Characterized by proliferation of lymphocytes in the bone marrow.
  • Chronic Myelogenous Leukemia (CML): Due to excessive development of mature meoplastic granulocytes in the bone marrow.
  • Chronic Lymphocytic Leukemia: Caused by production and accumulation of functionally inactive but long-lived, mature ‚Äì appearing lymphocytes.

Signs and Symptoms

  • Fevers
  • Anaemia
  • Loss of weight
  • Bleeding tendencies
  • Frequent infections
  • Fatigue and general body weakness
  • Mouth ulcers
  • Bone, joint and abdominal pain
  • Lymphadenopathy
  • Hepato-splenomegaly
  • Headache

Management

Aims: To destroy leukaemia cells, To prolong life.

The treatment is directed to giving drugs that can temporarily destroy the leukaemia cells and therefore prolong life of a patient.

Therapeutic Measures:

  • Blood transfusion and placement of blood cells may relieve symptoms.
  • Administration of cytotoxic drugs like ‚Äì cyclophosphamide, mrthotrexate, Vincistne IV 1.4mg, Doxorubicin.
  • Steroids: Prednisone.
  • Antibiotics to treat any infections.
  • Bone marrow and stem cell transplant.
  • Radiotherapy.

Symptomatic Treatment:

  • Pain relievers ‚Äì strong analgesics
  • Blood transfusion in anaemia
  • Tepid sponge if there is fever
  • Psychotherapy ‚Äì encourage patient to live a normal life

üõë Haemophilia (Coagulation Disorder)

It is a sex-linked recessive genetic disorder caused by defective or deficient coagulation factor (it is blood disorder). It occurs only in males and transmitted by females (i.e. females are carriers and males are the sicklers/ sufferers).

Types

Haemophilia A (Classic)

Due to factor VIII deficiency. It is the most common one making up 80% of all cases.

Haemophilia B (Christmas disease)

Due to factor IX deficiency.

Signs and Symptoms

  • Slow, persistent and prolonged bleeding from minor trauma and small cuts
  • Delayed bleeding after minor injuries/ surgery
  • Uncontrollable haemorrhage after dental extraction
  • Epistaxis especially after a blow to the face
  • Haematuria
  • GI bleeding from ulcers and gastritis
  • Ecchymosis and subcutaneous haematoma
  • Bleeding into joints (haemarthrosis) which may lead to joint deformity causing crippling in the knees, elbow, hip shoulders and ankles.

Management

Aims: To raise the levels of clotting factors, To prevent and treat bleeding.

Immediate Intervention

  • Stop the topical bleeding as quickly as possible by applying direct pressure, ice packs. Packing the area with topical haemostatic agents like thrombin, Adrenaline.
  • Blood transfusion with fresh frozen plasma.
  • Administer the specific coagulation factor (Anti Haemophilia factor ‚Äì AHF). Antifibrinolytics like traxemic acid is given.
  • In Haemarthrosis- ensure complete bed rest to prevent crippling deformities. Apply a bed cradle. Apply ice compresses onto the painful joints. Administer analgesics like codeine, Morphine and then PCM to relieve pain (DONOT administer ASPIRIN).

Prevention

  • To avoid trauma as a result of fighting (blows to face)
  • To participate only in non- contact games like golf, tennis
  • Before any surgery, the patient should be given an infusion of plasma or blood transfused.
  • If a patient is to receive an injection, a small needle should be used.

üåô Sickle Cell Disease

Definition & Pathophysiology

Refers to a group of inherited, autosomal, recessive disorders characterized by the presence of an abnormal form of haemoglobin in the erythrocyte. The abnormal haemoglobin is called haemoglobin sickle (Hbs).

üß¨

Mechanism

Haemoglobin sickle or sickle cells come as a result of substitution of valine for glutamic acid on the globin chain of haemoglobin. The sickling episodes are commonly triggered by: - hypoxia caused by viral or bacterial infections, extreme coldness, and severe dehydration.

Types of Sickle Cell Crisis

Thrombotic Crisis

Occurs due to clotting of blood in the tinny blood vessels like capillaries causing stasis of blood. Other parts of the body will have less blood causing severe pain in the peripheral joints and muscles.

Haemolytic Crisis

This is due to excess break down of RBCs like in malaria, severe jaundice etc.

Sequestration Crisis

The vaso-occlusion produces an acute painful enlargement of the spleen. There is splenic pooling of the RBCs and Hypovolaemia leading to circulatory collapse and death in some patients.

Aplastic Crisis

Here the bone marrow is affected and cannot manufacture RBCs resulting into a rapid fall in haemoglobin with no reticulocytes in the peripheral blood.

Signs and Symptoms

  • Fevers due to infections
  • Jaundice due to excessive hemolysis
  • Sharp severe chest pain due to infection, fat embolism, pulmonary infarction.
  • Joint and bone pain due to clumping of RBCs in the bone marrow.
  • Acute pain in the hands and feet due to vaso-occlusion (Hand-foot syndrome).
  • Splenomegaly and hepatomegaly
  • Growth and development failure due to poor bone development
  • Bossing of the head(skull) due to poor flat bone development
  • Priapism- painful persistent erection due to occlusion and stasis of blood in the erectile organ

Management

Aims: To alleviate the symptoms, To rest the patient, To prevent complications. There is no specific treatment; instead all the triggering factors must be prevented.

Nursing Care

  • Admit and ensure complete bed rest.
  • Relieve pain ‚Äì morphine 5-15 mg or Pethidine 25-100 mg whenever necessary (PRN), paracetamol 250 mg ‚Äì 1 g 3 times a day.
  • Administer oxygen to treat hypoxia and control sickling.
  • Intravenous fluids and or plenty of oral fluids to reduce blood viscosity.

Drug Therapy

  • Antibiotics for any infections like penicillins.
  • Antimalarial like Fansidar.
  • Haematenics ‚Äì folic acid. DONOT GIVE FERROUS SULPHATE.
  • Anti-sickling agents like hydroxyurea.
  • Bone marrow transplantation ‚Äì it is the only treatment that can cure some patients with SCD.

Complications

Anaemia, Infections like pneumonia, Skin ulcers, Kidney damage, Gangrene, Blindness, Pathological fractures, Impotence.
