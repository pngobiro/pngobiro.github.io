ü¶† Influenza

Influenza is a viral infection that affects the U.R.T. These viruses include A, B and C but A is the most common cause of illness. These organisms have a higher tendency of genetic mutation. It's a serious pandemic of over 8 to 10 years as a result of genetic mutation.

Symptoms of Influenza

  • Headache
  • Fever
  • Chills
  • Muscle arches
  • Nasal discharge
  • Unproductive cough
  • Sore throat

Treatment

Antiviral drugs:

  • Amentidine orally or by aerosol administration. It's only effective against type A influenza. This inhibits viral fission, assembly and release from the infected host cell.
  • Neuraminidase inhibitors (zanamasir, oseltmisir). This is effective against both type A and B influenza. It inhibits the activity of viral neuraminidase enzyme that is necessary for spread of influenza virus.
  • Influenza vaccine: This provides protection against A, B influenza stains that are expected to be prevalent in a certain year. The vaccine must be updated and administered yearly to be effective.

Nursing Care

  • Give the patient enough bed rest
  • Give enough warmth
  • Diet- this should include more fluids to ensure hydration is adequate.

Definition

This is a type of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease in which the air sacs in the lungs become damaged and stretched. This results in chronic and difficulty breathing.

Pathology

The alveolus at the end of the bronchioles of the lung becomes enlarged because their walls breakdown/ the air sacs are destroyed, narrowed, collapsed, stretched or over inflated. Having fewer and larger damaged sacs means that there is a reduced surface area for the exchange of oxygen into blood and CO‚ÇÇ out of it. The damage is permanent thus the ability to breathe properly cannot be fully recovered.

Causes & Risk Factors

  • Cigarette smoking especially in people who are susceptible
  • Genetics
  • Inhaled toxins
  • Smoke from indoor cooking and heating
  • Deficiency of a protein called alantitiypsin. This is a genetic factor that can lead to a rare form of emphysema. It protects the lungs against destruction of the alveolar tissue by neutrophil elastase. It is congenital.

Signs and Symptoms

  • Shortness of breath
  • Chronic cough
  • Dyspnoea
  • Frequent lung infections
  • Wheezing
  • Weight loss
  • Fatigue
  • Cyanosis
  • Anxiety
  • Depression
  • Sleep problems
  • Morning headaches

Management

Aims: To restore normal breathing, To prevent further complications, To treat the cause, To relieve pain.

  • Admission of the patient in a medical ward which should be well ventilated and quiet
  • The patient is positioned in a sitting up position in order to aid breathing.
  • Administration of oxygen therapy due to shortness of breath. This improves oxygen delivery to the lungs.
  • The patient can be given inhalers in order to restore normal breathing.
  • Drug therapy: Corticosteroid drugs e.g. prednisone, opioid drugs to relieve severe coughing, bronchodilators e.g. albuterol, ipratopium and methlxanthines.

Complications

  • Pneumonia
  • Pneumothorax (lung deflation) or collapsed lungs
  • Bullae (lungs developing large air pockets/ holes)
  • Heart problems: Emphysema increases pressure in the arteries connecting lungs to the heart which causes an area/ part of the heart to expand and then weaken.

ü©∫ Pneumonia

It's an inflammatory process of the lung parenchyma that is commonly caused by infectious agents. It involves the small airways and air sacs (alveoli) and the tissue around.

Classification

  • Community acquired pneumonia (CAP) vs Hospital acquired pneumonia (Nasao-comial) (HAP)
  • According to site: Lobar pneumonia (Lobes) vs Broncho pneumonia (Diffuse)

Lobar Pneumonia

This is an acute specific inflammatory disease affecting one or more lobes of the lungs.

Cause: Gram positive diplococcic (Pneumococcus).

Signs and Symptoms

  • Sudden onset with rigors in adults (Convulsions in children)
  • Temperature rises quickly from 39¬∞C - 40¬∞C
  • Pleuritic pain often severe on deep breathing and coughing
  • Short dry cough
  • Rapid and shallow respiration with flaring nares
  • Scanty sputum and tenacious at first, becomes copius and nusty with blood cells later (Rusty sputum)
  • On auscultation, there are typical alterations in the breathing sounds over the consolidated lobe

Diagnosis: X-ray examination of the chest (solid lobe shows as dense shadow), Sputum for culture.

Broncho Pneumonia

This is primarily bronchiolitis. There is a patchy wide spread inflammation which may involve the alveoli.

Causes: Pneumococci, Staphylococci, Streptococci, Viruses like measles or influenza.

Note: This is a serious condition in infants because of the size of their bronchioles.

Management of Pneumonia

  • The patient is propped up in bed in a comfortable position and encouraged to cough up sputum.
  • Steam inhalation or tent is used in severe cases.
  • Oxygen may be helpful. This can be given using a mask in case of difficulty in breathing or cyanosis.
  • Drug therapy: Appropriate antibiotics are given e.g. Penicillin by injection is most successful in Pneumococcal pneumonia. In less severe cases, ampicillin or Amoxillin may be prescribed.

üå°Ô∏è Bronchiectasis

It's a chronic, irreversible dilation of the bronchi and bronchioles that results from destruction of muscles and the elastic connective tissue.

Types

  • Saccular: It occurs mainly in large bronchi characterized by cavity like dilations.
  • Cylindric: This is associated with medium sized bronchioles that are mildly due to moderate dilation.

Clinical Features

  • Chronic cough
  • Production of purulent sputum in copious amounts
  • Hemoptysis
  • Clubbing of the fingers due to respiratory insufficiency
  • Loss of weight
  • Wheezing

Diagnostic Findings: History of prolonged productive cough with sputum consistently negative for tubercle bacilli. CT scan reveals bronchial dilation.

Management

Aims: To promote bronchiole drainage so as to clear excessive secretions from the affected portion of the lungs. To control further spread of the infection.

üíä

Treatment Strategy

  • Postural drainage to drain bronchiectatic areas by gravity hence reducing the amount of secretion and the degree of infections.
  • Chest physiotherapy.
  • Antibiotics are administered depending on the laboratory results.
  • Bronchodilators may assist in secretion management e.g. salbutamol, Aminophylline.

Health education: Encouraging the patient to do physiotherapy by deep breathing exercise. Encouraging the patient to take high proteinous diet and also carbohydrates for energy.
