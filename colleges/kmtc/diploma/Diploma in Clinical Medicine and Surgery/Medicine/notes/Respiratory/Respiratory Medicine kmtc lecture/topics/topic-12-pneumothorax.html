üìñ Pneumothorax

This is air accumulation in the pleural space by partial or complete lung collapse. Increase in atmospheric pressure within the pleural cavity leads to lung recoil hence collapse

Aetiological types

  • Traumatic
  • Tension
  • Spontaneous
02

üöë Traumatic pneumothorax

Can be open or closed

Open

Penetrative chest wound creates a communication between the atmosphere and pleural space thus permeating air inflow to pleural space from the atmosphere. The air inflow causes increase in pressure more than in lungs hence lung recoil and collapse. It occurs in stab wounds.

Closed

Chest wall is air tight. Air flows from lungs in to pleural space increasing pleural space pressure hence lung collapse

Causes

Mediastinal procedures e.g. pleural aspiration or puncture, lung perforation by puncture by broken rib, thoracocentesis in pleural tap using improperly sealed needles, Oesphageal surgery

03

üéà Spontaneous pneumothorax

Air enters the pleural space without history of trauma

Causes

  • Rapture of subpleural emphysematous bullae or pleural bleb
  • Rupture of pleural end of pleural adhesion
  • Rupture of subpleural tuberculous focus in to the pleural space
  • Staphylococcus lung abscess
  • Pulmonary infarction
  • Bronchial carcinoma
  • Bronchial asthma
  • Emphysema
  • Sudden change in pressures- divers(caisons disease)
    • flying at very high altitudes
04

‚ö†Ô∏è Tension (valvular) pneumothorax

üö®

Mechanism

The communication between peura and lung is small and acts as a one way valve which allows air to enter the pleural space during inspiration and coughing, but prevents it from escaping. Very large amounts of air may be trapped in the pleural space and the intrapleural pressure may rise to well above the atmospheric pressure level this causes compression of underlying deflated lung and mediastinal displacement toward the opposite lung. Organ compression leads to increased jugular venous pressure, reduced venous return, circulatory collapse and respiratory failure.

05

ü©∫ Clinical features & Investigations

Clinical features

  • Varies according to severity
  • Onset is usually sudden with pain or a feeling of tightness on the affected side of the chest that may be aggravated by deep breathing.
  • Dyspnoea
  • Cyanosis in sever cases
  • Physical signs depend on size of pneumothorax

Investigations

  • Shows sharply defined edges of deflated lung
  • Absence of lung markings
06

  • Mild/moderate with slight breathlessness
    monitor the patient.

  • Large pneumothorax
    Insertion of a catheter through intercostal space to pleural cavity and connecting to an underwater seal drainage
  • Open traumatic
    Surgical intervention
  • Tension
    Medical emergency
    Intercostals tube insertion second interspace midclavicular line

üíß Pleural effusion

This is excess accumulation of fluid in the pleural cavity. It is classified as transudative or exudative

Transudate

In normal circumstances there is minimal fluid in the pleural cavity there fore a state of dynamic equilibrium is maintained by sterlings pressure between parietal and visceral blood vessels. A transudate accumulates if pathological conditions disturbs these pressure in such a way as to increase the rate at which it is absorbed.

Characteristics

  • Protein level is less than 3g/dl
  • Specific gravity is less than 1.015
  • Fluid is clear white or yellow
  • Does not clot on standing and has relatively low cell count

Exudate

This involves increased capillary permeability so that exudate contains relatively high proteins

Characteristics

  • Protein levels greater than 3g/dl
  • Dark yellow orange in colour
  • Often slightly cloudy
  • Clots on standing due to protein fibrinogen

Clinical features

  • Reduced chest movement on the affected side
  • Trachea shifted away
  • Reduced tactile vocal fremitus
  • Apex shifted due to mediastinal shift
  • Stony dull on percussion
