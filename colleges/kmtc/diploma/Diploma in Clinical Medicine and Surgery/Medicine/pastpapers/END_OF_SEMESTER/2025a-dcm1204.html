Section A: Short Answer Questions

30 Marks (6 Marks Each)
1.
Outline the contents of Gynaecological history in women.
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Menstrual History: Last Menstrual Period (LMP), age of menarche, cycle length/regularity, duration of flow, presence of clots/pain (dysmenorrhea).
  2. Obstetric History: Gravidity (number of pregnancies) and Parity (outcomes), history of miscarriages/abortions, complications in previous pregnancies.
  3. Sexual History: Sexual activity, number of partners, dyspareunia (painful intercourse), history of STIs.
  4. Contraceptive History: Current and past methods used, side effects.
  5. Vaginal Discharge: Color, odor, consistency, itching.
  6. Cervical Screening: History of Pap smears.
2.
Review the cardiovascular system in medical history taking.
Show Answer

Answer:

When reviewing the Cardiovascular System (CVS), ask about specific symptoms:

  1. Chest Pain: Site, onset, character (crushing/tight), radiation (jaw/arm), relieving factors (rest/GTN).
  2. Dyspnea: Shortness of breath on exertion (SOBOE).
  3. Orthopnea: Breathlessness when lying flat (number of pillows used).
  4. Paroxysmal Nocturnal Dyspnea (PND): Waking up gasping for air at night.
  5. Palpitations: Awareness of heart beating fast or irregularly.
  6. Syncope/Pre-syncope: Fainting or dizziness.
  7. Edema: Swelling of ankles/legs.
  8. Intermittent Claudication: Calf pain on walking.
3.
Define the following terms:

a) Orthopnoea.

b) Paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnoea.

c) Dysmenorrhea.

d) Oliguria.

e) Odynophagia.

Show Answer

Answer:

  • a) Orthopnoea: Dyspnea (shortness of breath) that occurs when lying flat and is relieved by sitting up or standing.
  • b) Paroxysmal Nocturnal Dyspnoea (PND): Attacks of severe shortness of breath and coughing that generally occur at night, waking the person from sleep.
  • c) Dysmenorrhea: Painful menstruation, typically involving abdominal cramps.
  • d) Oliguria: Abnormally low urine output (typically < 400ml/24 hours in adults).
  • e) Odynophagia: Painful swallowing (pain on the passage of food through the esophagus).
4.
List down the causes of finger clubbing.
Show Answer

Answer:

Causes can be categorized by system:

  1. Respiratory (Most Common):
    • Bronchogenic carcinoma (Lung cancer).
    • Suppurative lung diseases: Bronchiectasis, Lung Abscess, Empyema.
    • Idiopathic Pulmonary Fibrosis.
  2. Cardiac:
    • Cyanotic Congenital Heart Disease (e.g., Fallot's Tetralogy).
    • Infective Endocarditis (Subacute).
  3. Gastrointestinal:
    • Liver Cirrhosis.
    • Inflammatory Bowel Disease (Crohn's/Ulcerative Colitis).
  4. Idiopathic/Familial: No underlying disease.

6.
Write short notes on oedema.
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Definition: Oedema is the palpable swelling produced by expansion of the interstitial fluid volume (accumulation of fluid in tissue spaces).
  • Pathophysiology (Starling's Forces): Caused by:
    • Increased capillary hydrostatic pressure (e.g., Heart Failure).
    • Decreased plasma oncotic pressure (e.g., Hypoalbuminemia in Liver/Kidney disease).
    • Increased capillary permeability (e.g., Inflammation/Sepsis).
    • Lymphatic obstruction (Lymphedema).
  • Types:
    • Pitting: Indentation persists after pressure (Heart/Kidney/Liver failure).
    • Non-pitting: No indentation (Lymphedema, Myxedema).
    • Localized: (e.g., DVT, Inflammation) vs Generalized: (Anasarca).

Section B: Long Essay Questions

40 Marks
1.
Describe abdominal examination in history taking and physical examination.
Show Answer

Answer:

1. History Taking (Specific to Abdomen):

  • Pain: SOCRATES (Site, Onset, Character, Radiation, Associations, Time, Exacerbating/Relieving factors, Severity).
  • Gastrointestinal Symptoms: Nausea, vomiting (hematemesis?), dysphagia, dyspepsia, bowel habits (diarrhea/constipation, melena/hematochezia).
  • Genitourinary: Dysuria, hematuria, frequency.
  • Systemic: Weight loss, fever, jaundice, appetite changes.
  • Past History: Surgeries, ulcers, alcohol use.

2. Physical Examination:

  • Preparation: Patient supine, one pillow, expose xiphisternum to symphysis pubis, warm hands.
  • Inspection:
    • Contour (Scaphoid, distended, flat).
    • Skin (Scars, striae, dilated veins/caput medusae).
    • Umbilicus (Inverted/everted).
    • Movements (Respiratory, visible peristalsis, pulsations).
  • Auscultation (Performed BEFORE palpation):
    • Bowel Sounds (Normal, hyperactive, absent/silent).
    • Bruits (Renal/Aortic).
  • Palpation:
    • Superficial: Check for tenderness, guarding, temperature. Watch patient's face.
    • Deep: Check for masses, organomegaly.
    • Specific Organs: Liver, Spleen (should not be palpable usually), Kidneys (ballotable).
  • Percussion:
    • General (Tympanic/Dull).
    • Shifting Dullness (for ascites).
    • Liver span estimation.
2.
Describe Alopecia in terms of a) Definition b) Types c) Causes.
Show Answer

Answer:

a) Definition: Alopecia is the partial or complete absence of hair from areas of the body where it normally grows; baldness.

b) Types:

  • Non-Scarring (Non-cicatricial): Hair follicles are preserved, potential for regrowth. (e.g., Alopecia Areata, Androgenic Alopecia).
  • Scarring (Cicatricial): Hair follicles are destroyed and replaced by scar tissue; permanent. (e.g., Lichen planopilaris).
  • Localized vs Diffuse.

c) Causes:

  1. Autoimmune: Alopecia Areata (patchy hair loss).
  2. Hormonal/Genetic: Androgenic Alopecia (Male/Female pattern baldness).
  3. Physiological Stress: Telogen Effluvium (after childbirth, severe illness).
  4. Fungal Infection: Tinea Capitis (Ringworm).
  5. Mechanical: Traction Alopecia (tight hairstyles).
  6. Drugs/Chemotherapy: Anagen effluvium.
  7. Nutritional: Iron or Zinc deficiency.
  8. Endocrine: Hypothyroidism.
3.
In relation to Jaundice: a) Describe the metabolism of bilirubin b) Give pre and intra hepatic causes.
Show Answer

Answer:

a) Metabolism of Bilirubin:

  1. Production: RBCs break down in the spleen. Heme is converted to Biliverdin, then to Unconjugated Bilirubin (lipid soluble).
  2. Transport: Unconjugated bilirubin binds to albumin and is transported to the liver.
  3. Uptake & Conjugation: Hepatocytes take up bilirubin. Enzyme UDP-glucuronyl transferase conjugates it with glucuronic acid to form Conjugated Bilirubin (water soluble).
  4. Excretion: Conjugated bilirubin is secreted into bile, enters the intestine.
  5. Fate: Bacterial action converts it to Urobilinogen (excreted in urine/feces as stercobilin).

b) Causes:

  • Pre-hepatic (Haemolytic): Excess RBC breakdown overwhelms the liver.
    • Malaria.
    • Sickle Cell Anaemia.
    • G6PD Deficiency.
    • Incompatible blood transfusion.
  • Intra-hepatic (Hepatocellular): Damage to liver cells prevents conjugation/excretion.
    • Viral Hepatitis (A, B, C).
    • Alcoholic Liver Disease/Cirrhosis.
    • Drug toxicity (Paracetamol).
    • Hepatocellular Carcinoma.
4.
Discuss fever in the following: a) Areas to measure temperature in the body b) Terms used to describe classification of fever.
Show Answer

Answer:

a) Areas to Measure Temperature:

  1. Oral (Mouth): Sublingual. Standard.
  2. Axillary (Armpit): Safe, but less accurate (0.5°C lower than oral).
  3. Rectal: Most accurate reflection of core temp (0.5°C higher than oral).
  4. Tympanic (Ear): Infrared sensor, quick.
  5. Temporal Artery (Forehead): Scanner.

b) Terms for Classification/Patterns of Fever:

  • Intermittent: Temperature rises but returns to normal at least once every 24 hours (e.g., Malaria).
  • Remittent: Temperature fluctuates >2°C in 24 hours but does not return to normal (e.g., Typhoid, Infective Endocarditis).
  • Continuous (Sustained): Temperature remains elevated with little fluctuation (<1°C) (e.g., Pneumonia).
  • Relapsing: Periods of fever alternating with afebrile days (e.g., Brucellosis, Borreliosis).
  • Hyperpyrexia: Extremely high fever >41.5°C.
