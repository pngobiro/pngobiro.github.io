Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions (20 Marks)

1.
Choose the correct statement.
a. Viruses should be cultured in synthetic media
b. Viruses are the smallest of the microorganisms
c. Viruses can be seen on light microscope
d. Viruses can synthesize proteins
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b. Viruses are the smallest of the microorganisms

Explanation: Viruses are typically 20-300nm, much smaller than bacteria. They cannot be cultured in synthetic media (require living cells), cannot be seen with a standard light microscope (require electron microscopy), and lack ribosomes to synthesize their own proteins (must use host machinery).

2.
Which one of the following viruses causes infection in the eye?
a. Polio virus
b. Rota virus
c. Hepatitis E virus
d. Cytomegalovirus
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d. Cytomegalovirus

Explanation: Cytomegalovirus (CMV) is a common cause of retinitis, especially in immunocompromised individuals (e.g., AIDS patients). Polio affects the CNS/gut, Rota affects the gut, and Hep E affects the liver.

3.
Choose the correct answer regarding fungi.
a. They are saprophytic eukaryotic organisms
b. They are motile
c. They feed on bacteria
d. They do not have a cell wall but have filamentous structures
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a. They are saprophytic eukaryotic organisms

Explanation: Fungi are Eukaryotes and often saprophytes (feeding on decaying matter). They are non-motile, do not ingest bacteria (they absorb nutrients), and possess a cell wall made of chitin.

4.
Which one of the following is an RNA virus?
a. Epstein-barr virus
b. Human papilloma virus
c. Herpes simplex 1&2
d. Polio virus
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d. Polio virus

Explanation: Poliovirus is a Picornavirus (RNA). EBV, HPV, and HSV are all DNA viruses.

5.
Which one of the following is a common fungal disease of Man?
a. Ringworm
b. Carbuncle
c. Listeriosis
d. Impetigo
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a. Ringworm

Explanation: Ringworm (Tinea) is a fungal infection (Dermatophytosis). Carbuncle and Impetigo are bacterial (Staph/Strep). Listeriosis is bacterial (Listeria).

6.
Which one of the following is a clinical feature of mumps?
a. Parotid gland swelling
b. Macular rash
c. Jaundice
d. Koplik spots
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a. Parotid gland swelling

Explanation: Bilateral parotitis (swelling of salivary glands) is the hallmark of Mumps. Koplik spots are seen in Measles.

7.
Which one of the following is a DNA virus?
a. Rota virus
b. Varicella zoster virus
c. Mumps virus
d. Influenza viruses
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b. Varicella zoster virus

Explanation: VZV (Human Herpesvirus 3) is a DNA virus. Rota (Reovirus), Mumps (Paramyxovirus), and Influenza (Orthomyxovirus) are RNA viruses.

8.
Choose the correct statement regarding superficial mycoses.
a. They affect the outermost and innermost layers of the skin
b. They always cause hypersensitivity reactions
c. They never affect the hair
d. Most patients seek treatment for cosmetic purposes
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d. Most patients seek treatment for cosmetic purposes

Note: While they affect the outermost layers (stratum corneum), option A is incorrect because they don't affect "innermost" layers. They do affect hair (Tinea capitis). They are often asymptomatic or cause mild itching/discoloration (Pityriasis versicolor), so cosmetic concern is a primary driver for seeking care.

9.
Which one of the following drugs is synthesized from fungi?
a. Griseofulvin
b. Acetylsalicylic acid
c. Paracetamol
d. Carbamazepine
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a. Griseofulvin

Explanation: Griseofulvin is an antifungal drug derived from the mold Penicillium griseofulvum.

10.
Which one of the following laboratory methods is useful in diagnosis of Viral infections?
a. Real-time Polymerase Chain Reaction
b. Light microscopy
c. Paper Chromatography
d. Polarography
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a. Real-time Polymerase Chain Reaction

Explanation: PCR is a molecular method widely used to detect viral DNA/RNA. Light microscopy is generally not useful for viruses (too small) unless looking for inclusion bodies.

15.
Which one of the following spores is asexual?
a. Sporangiospores
b. Zygospores
c. Oospores
d. Ascospores
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a. Sporangiospores

Explanation: Sporangiospores are asexual spores formed within a sporangium. Zygospores, Oospores, and Ascospores are sexual spores.

18.
Which one of the following fungal agents cause mycosis that can be diagnosed by a latex agglutination test on the cerebral spinal fluid for capsular polysaccharide antigen?
a. Cryptococcus neoformans
b. Histoplasma capsulatum
c. Aspergillus fumigatus
d. Candida albicans
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a. Cryptococcus neoformans

Explanation: The CrAg (Cryptococcal Antigen) test is a highly specific latex agglutination test used to detect the polysaccharide capsule of C. neoformans in CSF.

Section 2: Short Answer Questions (40 Marks)

1.
Briefly describe five mechanisms through which viruses can be transmitted (5 Marks).
Show Answer
  1. Droplets: Some viruses are transmitted through droplet nuclei on sneezing or coughing and enter the human body by inhalation (e.g., Corona virus, Influenza, Measles, RSV).
  2. Ingestion/Faecal-oral route: Agents enter the body by ingestion of faecal matter as a result of contamination of food or water (e.g., Polio, Hepatitis A, Rotavirus).
  3. Inoculation: Introduction of the virus into the human skin through insect bites, animal bites, or human bites (e.g., Rabies, Yellow fever).
  4. Sexual contact: Unprotected vaginal, anal, or oral sex with an infected partner (e.g., HIV, Hepatitis B, Herpes simplex-2, HPV).
  5. Contaminated blood/blood products: Through blood transfusion, injections, cuts, and pricks (e.g., HIV, Hepatitis B).
2.
State any five characteristics of viruses (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  • They are the smallest of the microorganisms (10 to 100 times smaller than bacteria).
  • They can be seen only under electron microscopy.
  • They are obligate intracellular parasites (live in animal, human, or bacterial cells).
  • They cannot reproduce on their own; they reproduce by turning the host cell into a virus converting unit (replication).
  • They do not have metabolism of their own.
  • They cannot be cultured in synthetic media (require living tissue).
  • They are not sensitive to antibiotics.
  • They possess either DNA or RNA, but never both.
3.
List in sequence, the seven stages in the HIV life cycle (5 Marks).
Show Answer
  1. Binding/Attachment
  2. Fusion
  3. Reverse Transcription
  4. Integration
  5. Replication
  6. Assembly
  7. Budding
4.
Briefly explain the clinical features of Mpox virus Infection (5 Marks).
Show Answer

Features:

  • Rash that may be located on hands, feet, chest, face, or mouth or near the genitals (including penis, testicles, labia, vagina) and anus.
  • The rash can initially look like pimples or blisters and may be painful or itchy.
  • Rash goes through stages, including scabs, before healing.
  • Other symptoms include: fever, chills, swollen lymph nodes, exhaustion, muscle pains, backache, headache, and respiratory symptoms (e.g., sore throat, nasal congestion, cough).
5.
Define the following terms as used in mycology (5 Marks).
Show Answer
  • a. Hyphae: These are long, branching, filamentous structures of fungi.
  • b. Dimorphic fungi: Fungi able to change from mold to yeast and vice versa depending on temperature (yeasts/spherules in tissues at 35-37°C and mold at 25°C).
  • c. Zygospores: Fungal sexual spores that occur when morphologically identical cells fuse.
  • d. Conidia: A type of naked (not covered by sporangium) asexual (nonmotile) spores.
  • e. Homothallic: A situation whereby fungi contain both sexual (anamorph/imperfect) and sexual (teleomorph/perfect) states.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions (40 Marks)

1.
Discuss Rabies in terms of causative organism, transmission, epidemiology, pathogenesis, clinical presentation and disease outcome and prevention (20 marks)
Show Answer

a. Causative organism (1 Mark)

Rabies virus (Rabies lyssavirus) - is a neurotropic single strand RNA virus.

b. Transmission (2 Marks)

Mostly through the saliva of animals (after animal bite) and less commonly through contact with human saliva. Often present in the salivary glands of rabid animals.

c. Epidemiology (2 Marks)

Like many rhabdoviruses, has an extremely wide host range. The main burden of disease is reported in Asia and Africa.

d. Pathogenesis (3 Marks)

Incubation period is usually one to three months. After the bite the virus spreads to the Central Nervous System causing paralysis and death. Death is usually due to cardiac and respiratory failure.

e. Clinical presentation and disease outcome (8 Marks)

  • Depression, headache, nausea, seizures, anorexia, muscle stiffness, and hypersalivation.
  • Abnormal sensations, such as itching, around the site of exposure.
  • Hydrophobia (paralysis of throat muscles).
  • Maniacal excitement to dull apathy.
  • Coma and death usually within less than one week.
  • Once symptoms appear, death is almost always inevitable.

f. Prevention (4 Marks)

  • Vaccination of dogs (most cost effective).
  • Avoid animal bites / eliminate stray dogs.
  • Antirabies serum vaccine given early after exposure (within 24 hours).
  • Immediately wash the bite wound with soap and water.
2.
Discuss Cryptococcus neoformans under the following headings (20 Marks).
Show Answer

a. Disease it causes (1 Mark)

Cryptococcosis.

b. Morphology (3 Marks)

Encapsulated yeast-like organism that reproduces by budding. Round or ovoid (4-6 micrometer), surrounded by a capsule of variable size. Four capsular serotypes A to D.

c. Transmission (3 Marks)

Inhalation of aerosolized organisms. No evidence of person-to-person transmission. Rare routes: organ transplantation or cutaneous inoculation.

d. Risk factors (3 Marks)

  • T-cell defect (e.g., AIDS - defining illness).
  • Post-transplantation.
  • Corticosteroid therapy, Hodgkin's disease, Sarcoidosis.

e. Clinical presentations (6 Marks)

  • Meningitis: Acute or chronic. Fever may be absent, minimal nuchal rigidity. Papilloedema, cranial nerve palsy.
  • Pulmonary: Asymptomatic or dyspnoea, cough, chest pain.
  • Other: Skin lesions, bone lesions, oral lesions.

f. Laboratory diagnosis (4 Marks)

  1. CSF Findings: Elevated opening pressure, low glucose, high protein, WBCs.
  2. India Ink Smear: Reveals capsule (halo) against dark background.
  3. Fungal Culture.
  4. Cryptococcal Antigen Test (CrAg): Latex agglutination (highly specific).
