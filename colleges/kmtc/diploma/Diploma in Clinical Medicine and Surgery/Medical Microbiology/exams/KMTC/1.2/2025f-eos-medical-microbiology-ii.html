Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions (20 Marks)

1.
Choose the correct statement in synthetic media:
a) Viruses should be cultured in synthetic media.
b) Viruses are the smallest of the microorganisms.
c) Viruses can be seen on light microscope.
d) Viruses can synthesize protein.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Viruses are the smallest of the microorganisms.

Explanation:
- Viruses are obligate intracellular parasites and cannot be cultured on synthetic media (they need living cells).
- They are generally too small (20-300nm) to be seen with a standard light microscope (limit ~200nm); electron microscopy is needed.
- They lack ribosomes and cannot synthesize proteins on their own.

2.
Which one among the following viruses causes infection in the eye?
a) Polio virus
b) Rota virus
c) Hepatitis E virus
d) Adenovirus
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Adenovirus

Explanation: Adenovirus is a common cause of viral conjunctivitis ("Pink Eye") and epidemic keratoconjunctivitis. Polio affects the CNS/gut, Rota affects the gut, and Hep E affects the liver.

3.
Choose the correct answer regarding fungi:
a) They are saprophytic eukaryotic organisms.
b) They are motile.
c) They feed on bacteria.
d) They do not have cell wall but have filamentous structures.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) They are saprophytic eukaryotic organisms.

Explanation: Fungi are eukaryotic and often saprophytic (feeding on decaying matter). They are generally non-motile and possess a rigid cell wall made of chitin.

4.
Which one of the following is an RNA virus?
a) Epstein Barr virus
b) Human papilloma virus
c) Herpes simplex 1&2
d) Polio virus
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Polio virus

Explanation: Poliovirus is a Picornavirus, which is a single-stranded RNA virus. EBV, HPV, and HSV are all DNA viruses.

5.
Which one of the following is a common fungal disease of Man?
a) Ringworm
b) Carbuncle
c) Listeriosis
d) Impetigo
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Ringworm

Explanation: Ringworm (Tinea) is a fungal infection caused by dermatophytes. Carbuncle and Impetigo are bacterial (Staph/Strep). Listeriosis is bacterial (*Listeria monocytogenes*).

6.
Which one of the following is a clinical feature of mumps?
a) Parotid gland swelling
b) Macular rash
c) Jaundice
d) Koplik spots
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Parotid gland swelling

Explanation: Mumps typically presents with painful swelling of the salivary glands, specifically the parotid glands (Parotitis). Koplik spots are a sign of Measles.

7.
Which one of the following is a DNA virus?
a) Rota virus
b) Varicella zoster virus
c) Mumps virus
d) Influenza virus
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Varicella zoster virus

Explanation: Varicella Zoster (Chickenpox/Shingles) is a Herpesvirus, which are DNA viruses. Rota, Mumps, and Influenza are RNA viruses.

8.
Choose the correct statement regarding superficial mycoses.
a) They affect the outermost and innermost layers of the skin.
b) They always cause hypersensitivity reactions.
c) They never affect the hair.
d) Most patients seek treatment for cosmetic purposes.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Most patients seek treatment for cosmetic purposes.

Explanation: Superficial mycoses (e.g., Pityriasis versicolor) affect only the outermost layer of skin (stratum corneum) or hair/nails and are often asymptomatic, leading patients to seek help primarily for pigment changes (cosmetic). Option A is wrong because they don't affect inner layers. C is wrong because they often affect hair (Piedra).

9.
Which one of the following drugs is synthesized from fungi?
a) Griseofulvin
b) Acetylsalicylic acid
c) Paracetamol
d) Carbamazepine
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Griseofulvin

Explanation: Griseofulvin is an antifungal drug originally isolated from the mold Penicillium griseofulvum.

10.
Which one of the following laboratory methods is useful in diagnosis of Viral infections?
a) Real-time Polymerase Chain Reaction
b) Light microscopy
c) Paper Chromatography
d) Polarography
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Real-time Polymerase Chain Reaction

Explanation: RT-PCR is the gold standard for diagnosing many viral infections by detecting viral genetic material (DNA/RNA). Light microscopy is generally insufficient for viruses due to their small size.

11.
Which one of the following statements about aflatoxins is true?
a) They are produced by Aspergillus species.
b) They cannot be used in bioterrorism.
c) They are not carcinogenic.
d) They are associated with adequately dried maize.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) They are produced by Aspergillus species.

Explanation: Aflatoxins are produced by Aspergillus flavus and Aspergillus parasiticus. They ARE carcinogenic (liver cancer). They are associated with improperly dried/stored maize (damp conditions).

12.
Choose the correct statement regarding oncogenic viruses.
a) They do not induce cancer.
b) They are only DNA viruses
c) They are only RNA viruses
d) Human papilloma and Hepatitis C virus are very important.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Human papilloma and Hepatitis C virus are very important.

Explanation: Oncogenic viruses can be DNA (HPV, Hep B, EBV) or RNA (Hep C, HTLV-1). HPV causes cervical cancer, and Hepatitis C causes liver cancer.

13.
Which one of the following statements about body defenses against viruses is correct?
a) The intact skin does not play any role.
b) Cytokines do not play any role
c) Only lymphocytes and monocytes are important.
d) Immunity can be acquired through Vaccination
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Immunity can be acquired through Vaccination

Explanation: Vaccination is a key method of acquiring specific immunity against viruses. Intact skin is a primary barrier. Cytokines (Interferons) play a massive role.

14.
Hepatitis B virus infection is transmitted through ............
a) Sharing utensils
b) Sexual contact
c) Inhalation
d) Fecal-oral route.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Sexual contact

Explanation: Hepatitis B is transmitted via blood and body fluids (sexual contact, percutaneous injury, vertical transmission). Hep A and E are fecal-oral.

15.
Which one of the following spores is asexual?
a) Sporangiospores
b) Zygospores
c) Oospores
d) Ascospores
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Sporangiospores

Explanation: Sporangiospores (and Conidia) are produced asexually. Zygospores, Oospores, Basidiospores, and Ascospores are formed through sexual reproduction.

16.
Choose the correct statement regarding cryptococcus neoformans.
a) Causes viral meningitis in immunocompromised individuals.
b) Is an encapsulated yeast-like organism that reproduces by budding.
c) Is transmitted through direct body to body contact.
d) Should be treated with Griseofulvin.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Is an encapsulated yeast-like organism that reproduces by budding.

Explanation: Cryptococcus neoformans is a fungus (not a virus), specifically an encapsulated yeast. It causes fungal meningitis. It is transmitted by inhalation of spores (not body contact) and treated with Amphotericin B/Fluconazole (not Griseofulvin).

18.
Which one of the following fungal agents cause mycosis that can be diagnosed by a latex agglutination test on the cerebral spinal fluid for capsular polysaccharide antigen?
a) Cryptococcus neoformans
b) Histoplasma capsulatum.
c) Aspergillus fumigatus.
d) Candida albicans.

Correct Answer: a) Cryptococcus neoformans

Explanation: The Cryptococcal Antigen (CrAg) latex agglutination test detects the polysaccharide capsule of C. neoformans in CSF and serum. It is highly sensitive.

19.
Which one of the following mycotic diseases is also known as "rose-thorn or rose-gardeners' disease"?
a) Cryptococcosis

Section 2: Short Answer Questions (40 Marks)

21.
Briefly describe five mechanisms through which viruses can be transmitted (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Respiratory Route: Inhalation of aerosolized droplets (e.g., Influenza, COVID-19).
  2. Fecal-Oral Route: Ingestion of contaminated food or water (e.g., Polio, Hep A, Rotavirus).
  3. Sexual Transmission: Direct mucosal contact (e.g., HIV, Herpes Simplex, HPV).
  4. Parenteral/Blood-borne: Through contaminated needles, blood transfusion, or organ transplant (e.g., Hep B, Hep C).
  5. Vertical Transmission: From mother to child via placenta, birth canal, or breast milk (e.g., HIV, Rubella).
  6. Vector-borne: Transmission via insect bites like mosquitoes or ticks (e.g., Yellow Fever, Dengue).
22.
State any five characteristics of viruses (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Obligate Intracellular Parasites: They require a living host cell to replicate.
  2. Genetic Material: Contain either DNA or RNA, but never both.
  3. Size: They are sub-microscopic (20-300 nm) and cannot be seen with light microscopes.
  4. Structure: Consist of a nucleic acid core surrounded by a protein coat (capsid). Some have a lipid envelope.
  5. Metabolism: They lack metabolic machinery (ribosomes, enzymes) for energy production or protein synthesis.
  6. Replication: They replicate by assembly of components, not by binary fission.
23.
In its sequence, outline the seven stages in the HIV life cycle (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Binding (Attachment): HIV binds to receptors (CD4) on the surface of the CD4 cell.
  2. Fusion: The HIV envelope fuses with the CD4 cell membrane, allowing entry.
  3. Reverse Transcription: HIV releases its RNA and reverse transcriptase converts it into HIV DNA.
  4. Integration: HIV DNA enters the nucleus and integrase inserts it into the host's DNA (Provirus).
  5. Replication (Transcription/Translation): The host machinery uses the viral DNA to create long chains of HIV proteins and RNA.
  6. Assembly: New HIV proteins and RNA move to the surface of the cell and assemble into immature HIV.
  7. Budding (Maturation): The immature HIV pushes out of the host cell. Protease breaks up the long protein chains, creating mature, infectious virus.
24.
Briefly explain the clinical features of M-Pox virus Infection (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Mpox (formerly Monkeypox) presents with:

  1. Prodromal Symptoms: Fever, intense headache, back pain, and myalgia (muscle aches).
  2. Lymphadenopathy: Swelling of lymph nodes (distinctive feature differentiating it from smallpox/chickenpox).
  3. Rash: Develops 1-3 days after fever. Starts on the face and spreads to the body (centrifugal). It progresses through stages: Macules → Papules → Vesicles → Pustules → Crusts (Scabs).
  4. Lesion Distribution: Commonly affects palms of hands and soles of feet.
25.
Define the following terms as used in mycology (5 Marks)
a. Hyphae
b. Dimorphic fungi
c. Zygospores
d. Conidia
e. Homothallic.
Show Answer

a. Hyphae: The branching, thread-like tubular filaments that make up the mycelium of a fungus.

b. Dimorphic fungi: Fungi that can exist in two forms: as molds (filamentous) at room temperature (25°C) and as yeasts (unicellular) at body temperature (37°C).

c. Zygospores: Thick-walled sexual spores formed by the fusion of two similar gametangia (haploid cells) in Zygomycetes.

d. Conidia: Asexual, non-motile fungal spores that develop externally on the tip or side of a spore-bearing hypha (conidiophore).

e. Homothallic: Fungi that are self-fertile; a single individual can undergo sexual reproduction without a partner (has both male and female reproductive structures).

26.
Briefly describe the general morphology of fungi (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Fungi exist in two main morphological forms:

  1. Yeasts: Unicellular, round or oval cells that reproduce asexually by budding. They form smooth, creamy bacterial-like colonies on media.
  2. Molds (Filamentous fungi): Multicellular organisms consisting of long, branching filaments called hyphae. A mass of hyphae is called a mycelium.
    • Hyphae can be septate (divided by cross-walls) or aseptate (coenocytic).
    • They form fuzzy, cottony, or powdery colonies.
  3. Dimorphic: Capable of switching between yeast and mold forms depending on temperature.
27.
Describe five infections caused by dermatophytes (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Dermatophytosis (Ringworm/Tinea) is classified by the body site affected:

  1. Tinea capitis: Infection of the scalp and hair shafts.
  2. Tinea corporis: Infection of the glabrous skin (body) typically presenting as annular lesions.
  3. Tinea pedis: Infection of the feet ("Athlete's Foot"), particularly toe webs.
  4. Tinea cruris: Infection of the groin area ("Jock Itch").
  5. Tinea unguium (Onychomycosis): Infection of the finger or toe nails.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions (40 Marks)

29.
Discuss Rabies in terms of causative organism, transmission, epidemiology, pathogenesis, clinical presentation and disease outcome and prevention (20 Marks)
Show Answer

Causative Organism: Rabies virus, a single-stranded RNA virus of the genus Lyssavirus, family Rhabdoviridae. It has a characteristic bullet shape.

Transmission: Primarily through the bite of a rabid animal (dog, bat, racoon) where virus-laden saliva enters the wound. Rarely via organ transplant or aerosol in bat caves.

Epidemiology: It is a zoonotic disease prevalent worldwide (except Antarctica/Australia). Domestic dogs are the main reservoir in developing countries (Africa/Asia), responsible for 99% of human deaths.

Pathogenesis:

  • Virus replicates locally in muscle.
  • It enters peripheral nerves and travels via retrograde axonal transport to the spinal cord and brain (CNS).
  • In the brain, it causes encephalitis and neuronal degeneration (Negri bodies are diagnostic).
  • From the CNS, it spreads via nerves to salivary glands to be transmitted.

Clinical Presentation:

  • Prodrome: Fever, headache, and paresthesia/itching at the bite site.
  • Acute Neurologic Phase:
    • Furious Rabies (80%): Agitation, hallucinations, hydrophobia (fear of water due to throat spasms), aerophobia.
    • Paralytic Rabies (20%): Ascending paralysis, confusion, coma.

Outcome: Once symptoms appear, rabies is nearly 100% fatal. Death occurs due to respiratory/cardiac arrest.

Prevention:

  • Animal Vaccination: Vaccinating dogs is the most effective way to prevent human rabies.
  • Post-Exposure Prophylaxis (PEP): Immediate wound washing with soap/water + Rabies Vaccine (0, 3, 7, 14 days) + Rabies Immunoglobulin (RIG) if severe exposure.
  • Pre-exposure Prophylaxis: Vaccine for high-risk groups (vets).
30.
Discuss Cryptococcus neoformans under the following headings:
a) Its morphology (4 Marks)
b) Modes of transmission (3 Marks)
c) Risk factors (3 Marks)
d) Clinical presentations (6 Marks)
e) Laboratory diagnosis (4 Marks)
Show Answer

a) Morphology:

  • It is an encapsulated yeast cell.
  • Round to oval shape, 4-6 µm in diameter.
  • Surrounded by a thick polysaccharide capsule (prominent feature).
  • Reproduces by narrow-based budding.

b) Modes of Transmission:

  • Inhalation: Inhalation of aerosolized yeast cells or spores from the environment.
  • Source is usually soil enriched with bird droppings (especially pigeons) or decaying wood.
  • Not transmitted human-to-human.

c) Risk Factors:

  • Advanced HIV/AIDS (CD4 count < 100 cells/µL).
  • Prolonged corticosteroid use.
  • Organ transplantation (immunosuppression).
  • Malignancies (Leukemia/Lymphoma).

d) Clinical Presentations (Cryptococcal Meningitis):

  • Headache: Severe and persistent.
  • Fever: Often low grade.
  • Altered Mental Status: Confusion, lethargy, personality changes.
  • Meningism: Neck stiffness (may be mild in severe immunosuppression).
  • Visual Disturbances: Blurred vision or diplopia due to raised intracranial pressure.
  • Pulmonary symptoms: Cough, chest pain (if lung involvement).

e) Laboratory Diagnosis:

  • India Ink Stain (CSF): Demonstrates yeast cells with a clear halo (capsule) against a black background (50-80% sensitive).
  • Cryptococcal Antigen (CrAg) Test: Latex agglutination or Lateral Flow Assay (LFA) on serum or CSF. Highly sensitive (>95%) and specific.
  • Culture: Sabouraud Dextrose Agar (SDA) yields creamy, mucoid colonies. (Gold Standard).
  • CSF Biochemistry: Low glucose, high protein, lymphocytic pleocytosis.
