Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions (20 Marks)

1.
A 45-year-old patient presents with skin abscesses. Culture reveals gram-positive cocci in clusters. Which of the following is the most likely causative organism?
A. Escherichia coli
B. Staphylococcus aureus
C. Salmonella typhi
D. Vibrio cholerae
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Staphylococcus aureus

Explanation: Staphylococcus aureus typically presents as Gram-positive cocci arranged in grape-like clusters. It is a common cause of skin abscesses and soft tissue infections. E. coli and Salmonella are Gram-negative bacilli; Vibrio is a Gram-negative curved rod.

2.
A lab report from a patient with a fungal infection lists different fungal classifications. Which of the following is NOT a class of fungi?
A. Yeasts
B. Molds
C. Protozoa
D. Dimorphic fungi
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Protozoa

Explanation: Protozoa are single-celled eukaryotic organisms belonging to the kingdom Protista, distinct from Fungi. Yeasts, Molds, and Dimorphic fungi are all morphological classifications of fungi.

3.
A 25-year-old man who recently returned from a malaria-endemic region presents with cyclical fevers and chills. Which organism is the most likely cause?
A. Plasmodium falciparum
B. Giardia lamblia
C. Aspergillus fumigatus
D. Treponema pallidum
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Plasmodium falciparum

Explanation: Malaria is caused by Plasmodium species. Cyclical fevers (paroxysms) are a hallmark symptom. P. falciparum is the most dangerous species. Giardia causes diarrhea; Aspergillus is a fungus; Treponema causes syphilis.

4.
A microbiologist compares bacterial and viral structures during a lecture. Which of the following is present in bacteria but absent in viruses?
A. Cell wall
B. DNA or RNA
C. Protein coat
D. Capsid
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Cell wall

Explanation: Bacteria are cellular organisms with a complex cell wall (usually peptidoglycan). Viruses are acellular and do not have a cell wall; they consist of genetic material (DNA or RNA) surrounded by a protein coat (capsid).

5.
During a ward inspection, a nurse is observed frequently washing hands before and after patient contact. This is an example of which infection prevention and control measure?
A. Hand hygiene
B. Ignoring patient isolation
C. Avoiding sterilization
D. Underuse of personal protective equipment (PPE)
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Hand hygiene

Explanation: Hand hygiene is the single most important measure to prevent the spread of infections in healthcare settings.

6.
A 28-year-old patient presents with a painless genital ulcer. Dark-field microscopy reveals a spiral-shaped organism. Which of the following best identifies the organism?
A. Escherichia coli
B. Treponema pallidum
C. Candida albicans
D. Staphylococcus aureus
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Treponema pallidum

Explanation: Treponema pallidum is a spirochete (spiral-shaped bacterium) that causes Syphilis. The primary stage of syphilis manifests as a painless genital ulcer (chancre). Spirochetes are often visualized using dark-field microscopy as they do not stain well with Gram stain.

7.
A student reviewing lab results from a patient with suspected fungal pneumonia asks what field specializes in fungal organisms. What is the correct term?
A. Virology
B. Bacteriology
C. Mycology
D. Parasitology
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Mycology

Explanation: Mycology is the branch of biology concerned with the study of fungi.

8.
While reviewing Gram stains, a lab tech notes that the rigid bacterial wall stains purple. What is the main component of this structure?
A. Cellulose
B. Peptidoglycan
C. Chitin
D. Phospholipid
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Peptidoglycan

Explanation: Bacterial cell walls are primarily composed of peptidoglycan (murein). Gram-positive bacteria have a thick peptidoglycan layer that retains the crystal violet stain, appearing purple. Cellulose is in plants; Chitin is in fungi.

9.
A 50-year-old man with a chronic cough, night sweats, and weight loss has a positive sputum acid-fast bacillus (AFB) test. Which organism is most likely?
A. Streptococcus pneumoniae
B. Mycobacterium tuberculosis
C. Plasmodium vivax
D. Candida albicans
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Mycobacterium tuberculosis

Explanation: Mycobacterium tuberculosis is an Acid-Fast Bacillus (AFB) due to its waxy cell wall (mycolic acid). The clinical presentation (chronic cough, night sweats, weight loss) is classic for Tuberculosis (TB).

10.
A hospital is conducting training on reducing hospital-acquired infections. What is one of the primary objectives of infection prevention and control programs?
A. Promote antibiotic misuse
B. Prevent healthcare-associated infections
C. Eliminate all microorganisms in the body
D. Discourage use of personal protective equipment
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Prevent healthcare-associated infections

Explanation: The main goal of IPC (Infection Prevention and Control) is to prevent Healthcare-Associated Infections (HAIs) or Nosocomial infections among patients and staff.

11.
A patient receiving a vaccine develops an immune response involving antibody production. Which cells are primarily responsible for this?
A. T-cells
B. B-cells
C. Macrophages
D. Neutrophils
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. B-cells

Explanation: B-lymphocytes (B-cells) differentiate into plasma cells, which are responsible for producing and secreting antibodies (Humoral Immunity). T-cells are involved in Cell-Mediated Immunity.

12.
A child in a rural area presents with fever and chills. Microscopy reveals parasites inside red blood cells. Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis?
A. Tuberculosis
B. Malaria
C. Influenza
D. Pneumonia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Malaria

Explanation: The presence of parasites *inside* red blood cells (intraerythrocytic) is a diagnostic feature of Malaria (caused by *Plasmodium* spp.).

13.
A medical student asks why viruses can't be cultured on regular media. Which of the following best explains this?
A. They have both DNA and RNA
B. They are obligate intracellular parasites
C. They reproduce by binary fission
D. They have a cell wall
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. They are obligate intracellular parasites

Explanation: Viruses lack the metabolic machinery to reproduce on their own. They must enter a living host cell to replicate. Therefore, they cannot grow on inert nutrient agar (regular media); they require cell cultures, embryonated eggs, or living animals.

14.
A community health officer is educating residents on cholera outbreaks. What is the primary mode of transmission for Vibrio cholerae?
A. Airborne
B. Vector-borne
C. Fecal-oral
D. Sexual contact
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Fecal-oral

Explanation: Cholera is transmitted through the ingestion of food or water contaminated with fecal matter containing Vibrio cholerae.

15.
A patient is diagnosed with a bacterial infection and prescribed antibiotics. Which type of microorganism is typically targeted by antibiotics?
A. Viruses
B. Parasites
C. Fungi
D. Bacteria
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Bacteria

Explanation: Antibiotics are drugs specifically designed to kill or inhibit the growth of bacteria. They are ineffective against viruses, fungi, or parasites.

16.
A lab report shows a bacterium surrounded by a thick polysaccharide layer. Which structure is responsible for resistance to phagocytosis?
A. Cell membrane
B. Capsule
C. Flagella
D. Ribosome
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Capsule

Explanation: The capsule is a gelatinous outer layer that protects bacteria from engulfment by immune cells (phagocytosis), making it a significant virulence factor.

17.
An autoclave is being tested in a hospital's central sterilization unit. What is the purpose of sterilization in this context?
A. Kill only bacteria
B. Remove visible dirt
C. Destroy all forms of microbial life
D. Only reduce the number of pathogens
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Destroy all forms of microbial life

Explanation: Sterilization is defined as the complete elimination or destruction of all forms of microbial life, including bacteria, viruses, fungi, and highly resistant bacterial spores. Option D describes Disinfection.

18.
A biopsy from a patient with a systemic fungal infection shows a fungus that exists as a mold at 25°C and a yeast at 37°C. What type of organism is this?
A. Only a yeast
B. Only a mold
C. Both yeast and mold depending on temperature
D. Only as a spore
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Both yeast and mold depending on temperature (Dimorphic Fungus)

Explanation: This property is known as Thermal Dimorphism. Fungi like Histoplasma and Blastomyces grow as molds in the environment (cooler temps) and yeasts in the human body (warmer temps).

19.
A patient presents with bloody diarrhea after traveling to an endemic region. Stool microscopy reveals motile trophozoites. Which organism is most likely responsible?
A. Ascaris lumbricoides
B. Entamoeba histolytica
C. Candida albicans
D. Escherichia coli
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Entamoeba histolytica

Explanation: Entamoeba histolytica causes Amoebic Dysentery (bloody diarrhea). Its active feeding stage is the motile trophozoite, which can be seen in fresh stool samples.

20.
A nurse educates patients on hygiene and infection control. Which of the following practices should not be part of infection prevention measures?
A. Hand hygiene
B. Use of personal protective equipment (PPE)
C. Recapping needles after use (Inferred distractor)
D. Proper waste segregation (Inferred distractor)
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Recapping needles after use (Inferred)

Explanation: Recapping needles is a dangerous practice that frequently leads to needlestick injuries and transmission of blood-borne pathogens. It is strictly discouraged in standard infection prevention protocols.

Section 2: Short Answer Questions (40 Marks)

21.
A 21-year-old nursing student is admitted with an infection prevention module exam. She is asked to define key microbiological concepts. Define the following terms as used in clinical microbiology practice: (5 Marks)
A. Microorganism
B. Pathogen
C. Opportunistic infection
D. Sterilization
E. Nosocomial infection
Show Answer

A. Microorganism: A microscopic organism (living thing) that is too small to be seen with the naked eye, such as bacteria, viruses, fungi, and protozoa.

B. Pathogen: Any microorganism capable of causing disease in a host.

C. Opportunistic infection: An infection caused by an organism that does not normally cause disease in a healthy person but becomes pathogenic when the host's immune system is compromised.

D. Sterilization: The process of destroying or eliminating all forms of microbial life, including vegetative bacteria, viruses, fungi, and bacterial spores.

E. Nosocomial infection: An infection acquired in a hospital or healthcare facility that was not present or incubating at the time of admission (also called Healthcare-Associated Infection - HAI).

22.
During ward rounds, a medical student observes that laboratory reports classify bacteria based on their morphology. List three major shapes used to classify bacteria and provide one clinical example for each. (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Cocci (Spherical): e.g., Staphylococcus aureus (causes abscesses) or Streptococcus pneumoniae.
  2. Bacilli (Rod-shaped): e.g., Escherichia coli (causes UTI) or Mycobacterium tuberculosis.
  3. Spirilla/Spirochetes (Spiral/Curved): e.g., Treponema pallidum (Syphilis) or Vibrio cholerae (Comma-shaped).
23.
A clinician is educating a patient on the difference between bacterial and viral infections. Identify five differences between bacteria and viruses that have clinical implications. (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Cell Structure: Bacteria are living cells (prokaryotic) with a cell wall; Viruses are acellular (genetic material + protein coat) and require a host.
  2. Reproduction: Bacteria reproduce independently (binary fission); Viruses must hijack a host cell's machinery to replicate.
  3. Antibiotic Sensitivity: Bacteria are killed by antibiotics; Viruses are NOT affected by antibiotics (treated with antivirals or vaccines).
  4. Size: Bacteria are larger (can be seen with light microscope); Viruses are sub-microscopic (need electron microscope).
  5. Genome: Bacteria have both DNA and RNA; Viruses have either DNA or RNA, never both.
24.
A patient admitted with malaria exhibits progressive symptoms over several days. Outline the four typical stages of the malaria parasite development in the human host. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

The stages within the human Red Blood Cell (Erythrocytic Cycle):

  1. Ring Stage (Early Trophozoite): Young parasite looks like a ring inside the RBC.
  2. Trophozoite Stage (Growing): Parasite grows, metabolizes hemoglobin, and produces pigment.
  3. Schizont Stage: Parasite nucleus divides multiple times (schizogony), filling the RBC with merozoites.
  4. Gametocyte Stage: Sexual forms (male and female) develop, which are infective to mosquitoes.
  5. (Note: Merozoites are released when the schizont ruptures to infect new RBCs, causing fever).
25.
A 40-year-old diabetic patient presents with oral thrush and athlete's foot. Mention five common fungal infections of medical importance and indicate the body part each typically affects. (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Candidiasis (Oral Thrush): Affects the mouth, throat (mucous membranes).
  2. Tinea Pedis (Athlete's Foot): Affects the skin of the feet and toe webs.
  3. Tinea Capitis (Ringworm of Scalp): Affects the scalp and hair.
  4. Onychomycosis: Affects the fingernails or toenails.
  5. Cryptococcosis: Affects the lungs and Central Nervous System (Meningitis).
  6. Vaginal Candidiasis: Affects the female genital tract.
26.
A nurse is training healthcare workers on infection control. Describe five routes through which microorganisms can be transmitted to humans in clinical settings. (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Contact Transmission: Direct (touching infected patient) or Indirect (touching contaminated objects/fomites).
  2. Droplet Transmission: Large respiratory droplets from coughing/sneezing (travel <1m).
  3. Airborne Transmission: Small droplet nuclei suspended in air for long distances (e.g., TB).
  4. Vehicle Transmission: Contaminated items like food, water, or drugs.
  5. Vector-borne Transmission: Insects (mosquitoes, flies) carrying pathogens (less common indoors but possible).
  6. Parenteral Transmission: Needlestick injuries or blood transfusions.
27.
During an infection control seminar, a public health officer asks for practical measures to reduce hospital-acquired infections. List and briefly explain five standard precautions used to prevent and control infections in healthcare settings. (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Hand Hygiene: Washing hands with soap/water or using alcohol rub before/after patient contact.
  2. Use of PPE: Wearing gloves, gowns, masks, and eye protection based on risk assessment.
  3. Safe Injection Practices: Using sterile, single-use needles and not recapping needles.
  4. Respiratory Hygiene/Cough Etiquette: Covering mouth when coughing, using masks for symptomatic patients.
  5. Environmental Cleaning: Proper disinfection of patient care areas and equipment.
  6. Waste Management: Proper segregation and disposal of biomedical waste (Sharps, Infectious).
28.
A hospital is reviewing its infection control protocols. Outline five roles that clinical microbiology plays in preventing and controlling infections in healthcare environments. (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Identification of Pathogens: Accurately identifying the causative agent of infection to guide treatment.
  2. Antimicrobial Susceptibility Testing: Determining which antibiotics work, preventing misuse and resistance.
  3. Surveillance: Tracking infection rates and detecting outbreaks of hospital-acquired infections (e.g., MRSA).
  4. Screening: Testing staff and patients for carriage of resistant organisms.
  5. Sterilization Monitoring: Testing autoclaves and environment to ensure sterility standards are met.
  6. Policy Guidance: Advising on appropriate antibiotic policies and infection control guidelines.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions (40 Marks)

1.
A 46-year-old male patient is admitted with an open tibial fracture following a road traffic accident. The orthopedic team is concerned about the high risk of infection, especially given the contaminated wound. As part of the care team, explain the role of microbiology in guiding the prevention, diagnosis, and treatment of infections in such trauma cases. (20 Marks)
Show Answer

Role of Microbiology in Trauma Care

1. Prevention (Prophylaxis & Asepsis):

  • Understanding Flora: Knowledge of skin flora (Staph aureus) and environmental contaminants (Clostridium tetani from soil) guides the choice of prophylactic antibiotics (e.g., Cephalosporins + Metronidazole).
  • Sterile Technique: Microbiology principles dictate strict aseptic technique during debridement and surgery to prevent introducing new pathogens.
  • Tetanus Prophylaxis: Administering Tetanus Toxoid based on the risk of anaerobic Clostridium infection in dirty wounds.

2. Diagnosis:

  • Sample Collection: Taking deep tissue specimens or swabs during debridement *before* antibiotics are changed.
  • Microscopy (Gram Stain): Rapid identification of bacteria (e.g., Gram-positive cocci vs Gram-negative rods) allows immediate tailoring of empiric therapy.
  • Culture: Growing the organism to identify the specific species (e.g., *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* from soil).
  • Sensitivity Testing (AST): Determining which antibiotics the bacteria are sensitive or resistant to, which is crucial for treating resistant hospital or environmental strains.

3. Treatment:

  • Targeted Therapy: Switching from broad-spectrum to narrow-spectrum antibiotics based on culture results (De-escalation) to reduce toxicity and resistance.
  • Monitoring Response: Repeat cultures if the wound fails to heal or signs of sepsis persist.
  • Osteomyelitis Management: Long-term antibiotic planning for bone infections based on microbiological clearance.
2.
A new intern in your orthopedic unit struggles to understand the microbiological reports from the lab. As the senior clinical officer, explain the classification of microorganisms, including relevant clinical examples of each group, to help guide appropriate infection management. (20 Marks)
Show Answer

Microorganisms are classified into major groups based on their structure and biology. Each group requires different management strategies.

1. Bacteria

  • Description: Unicellular prokaryotes. Classified by shape (cocci/bacilli) and Gram stain (Positive/Purple vs Negative/Pink).
  • Examples:
    • Staphylococcus aureus (Gram +ve cocci): Causes osteomyelitis, wound infections.
    • Escherichia coli (Gram -ve rod): Causes UTIs, sepsis.
    • Clostridium tetani (Anaerobe): Causes Tetanus.
  • Treatment: Antibiotics (e.g., Penicillins, Cephalosporins).

2. Viruses

  • Description: Acellular, obligate intracellular parasites containing DNA or RNA.
  • Examples:
    • HIV: Immunosuppression affecting wound healing.
    • Hepatitis B/C: Blood-borne risk to surgeons.
    • Influenza: Respiratory infection.
  • Treatment: Antivirals, Vaccines, Supportive care. Antibiotics do NOT work.

3. Fungi

  • Description: Eukaryotic organisms (Yeasts or Molds).
  • Examples:
    • Candida albicans (Yeast): Oral thrush, systemic infection in immunocompromised.
    • Tinea pedis (Mold): Athlete's foot.
  • Treatment: Antifungals (e.g., Fluconazole).

4. Parasites

  • Description: Eukaryotic organisms that live on or in a host. Can be Protozoa (unicellular) or Helminths (worms).
  • Examples:
    • Plasmodium spp: Malaria (fever complication in trauma).
    • Sarcoptes scabiei: Scabies (skin infestation).
  • Treatment: Antiparasitics (e.g., Artemether, Ivermectin).

5. Prions (Rare)

  • Description: Misfolded proteins causing neurodegenerative disease. Highly resistant to sterilization.
  • Example: Creutzfeldt-Jakob Disease (CJD).
  • Relevance: Surgical instrument sterilization protocols.
