Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions (20 Marks)

1.
Which of the following viruses is most commonly associated with cervical cancer?
A. Hepatitis B virus
B. Human papillomavirus (HPV)
C. Epstein-Barr virus
D. Herpes simplex virus type 1
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Human papillomavirus (HPV)

Explanation: High-risk strains of HPV (particularly types 16 and 18) are the primary cause of cervical cancer. The virus integrates its DNA into host cells and produces oncoproteins (E6 and E7) that disrupt cell cycle regulation.

2.
Which virus is the primary cause of infectious mononucleosis?
A. Cytomegalovirus
B. Epstein-Barr virus
C. Human herpesvirus 6
D. Adenovirus
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Epstein-Barr virus

Explanation: Infectious mononucleosis ("Kissing Disease") is primarily caused by the Epstein-Barr Virus (EBV/HHV-4). It typically presents with fever, pharyngitis, and lymphadenopathy.

3.
What is the mechanism of action of acyclovir?
A. Protease inhibition
B. DNA polymerase inhibition
C. Neuraminidase inhibition
D. RNA polymerase activation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. DNA polymerase inhibition

Explanation: Acyclovir is a nucleoside analogue. Once phosphorylated by viral thymidine kinase, it incorporates into the viral DNA chain and terminates replication by inhibiting viral DNA polymerase.

4.
Which of the following viruses is transmitted through the fecal-oral route?
A. Hepatitis C
B. Hepatitis B
C. Hepatitis A
D. HIV
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Hepatitis A

Explanation: Hepatitis A (and E) are transmitted via the fecal-oral route (contaminated food/water). Hepatitis B, C, and HIV are transmitted via blood, sexual contact, or vertical transmission.

5.
Which virus is most commonly associated with Kaposi's sarcoma in HIV/AIDS patients?
A. HIV
B. HHV-6
C. HHV-8
D. EBV
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. HHV-8

Explanation: Human Herpesvirus 8 (HHV-8), also known as Kaposi's Sarcoma-associated Herpesvirus (KSHV), is the etiological agent of Kaposi's sarcoma, a vascular tumor common in AIDS patients.

6.
A patient presents with jaundice and is found to have elevated liver enzymes. Serology shows HBsAg positive, anti-HBs negative, and anti-HBc IgM positive. What is the diagnosis?
A. Immunity due to vaccination
B. Chronic hepatitis B
C. Acute hepatitis B
D. Past resolved infection
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Acute hepatitis B

Explanation:
- HBsAg (+): Active infection (Acute or Chronic).
- Anti-HBc IgM (+): IgM indicates a recent, acute infection. (In chronic infection, IgM is negative and IgG is positive).
- Anti-HBs (-): No immunity yet.

7.
What is the causative agent of chickenpox?
A. Variola virus
B. Varicella-zoster virus
C. Measles virus
D. Rubella virus
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Varicella-zoster virus

Explanation: Varicella-zoster virus (VZV/HHV-3) causes Chickenpox (primary infection) and Shingles (reactivation).

8.
What is the main viral cause of bronchiolitis in infants?
A. Rhinovirus
B. Influenza A
C. Respiratory syncytial virus (RSV)
D. Parainfluenza virus
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Respiratory syncytial virus (RSV)

Explanation: RSV is the most common cause of bronchiolitis and pneumonia in children under 1 year of age.

9.
Which of the following is a double-stranded RNA virus?
A. Rotavirus
B. Influenza virus
C. Coronavirus
D. Measles virus
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Rotavirus

Explanation: Rotavirus belongs to the family Reoviridae, which is characterized by a segmented double-stranded RNA (dsRNA) genome. Influenza, Coronavirus, and Measles are single-stranded RNA (ssRNA) viruses.

10.
Which of the following fungi is most commonly associated with oral thrush?
A. Aspergillus fumigatus
B. Cryptococcus neoformans
C. Candida albicans
D. Histoplasma capsulatum
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Candida albicans

Explanation: Candida albicans is a commensal yeast that causes opportunistic infections like oral thrush (candidiasis), especially in immunocompromised individuals or after antibiotic use.

11.
Which fungal pathogen is associated with meningitis in HIV-positive patients?
A. Aspergillus niger
B. Cryptococcus neoformans
C. Candida glabrata
D. Trichophyton rubrum
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Cryptococcus neoformans

Explanation: Cryptococcus neoformans is an encapsulated yeast that causes cryptococcal meningitis, a major AIDS-defining illness in patients with low CD4 counts.

12.
What is the most effective stain for identifying fungi in tissue specimens?
A. Ziehl-Neelsen stain
B. Giemsa stain
C. India ink
D. Periodic acid-Schiff (PAS) stain
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Periodic acid-Schiff (PAS) stain

Explanation: PAS stain (and Grocott's Methenamine Silver - GMS) is the standard histological stain for visualizing fungal elements (cell walls stain magenta) in tissue. India ink is used for CSF (Cryptococcus), and ZN is for mycobacteria.

13.
Which antifungal acts by binding to ergosterol and forming pores in the fungal membrane?
A. Amphotericin B
B. Fluconazole
C. Caspofungin
D. Terbinafine
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Amphotericin B

Explanation: Polyenes like Amphotericin B and Nystatin bind directly to ergosterol in the fungal cell membrane, creating pores that lead to cell leakage and death.

14.
Which antifungal drug inhibits fungal cell wall synthesis?
A. Amphotericin B
B. Fluconazole
C. Caspofungin
D. Griseofulvin
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Caspofungin

Explanation: Caspofungin belongs to the Echinocandin class, which inhibits the synthesis of $\beta$-(1,3)-D-glucan, a key component of the fungal cell wall.

15.
Which fungal infection is known as "rose gardener's disease"?
A. Blastomycosis
B. Sporotrichosis
C. Tinea corporis
D. Mucormycosis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Sporotrichosis

Explanation: Sporotrichosis is caused by Sporothrix schenckii, found in soil and plants. Infection typically occurs when the skin is pricked by a contaminated thorn (e.g., rose thorn), leading to a lymphocutaneous spread.

16.
A "spaghetti and meatballs" appearance on KOH prep is characteristic of which fungus?
A. Trichophyton rubrum
B. Candida albicans
C. Malassezia furfur
D. Sporothrix schenckii
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Malassezia furfur

Explanation: Malassezia furfur causes Pityriasis (Tinea) versicolor. Microscopy shows short, curved hyphae ("spaghetti") and clusters of yeast cells ("meatballs").

17.
Which fungus is commonly associated with fungal balls (aspergilloma) in lung cavities?
A. Histoplasma
B. Cryptococcus
C. Aspergillus
D. Candida
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Aspergillus

Explanation: Aspergillus fumigatus can colonize pre-existing lung cavities (e.g., from old TB) forming a fungal ball known as an Aspergilloma.

18.
What is the most appropriate treatment for vulvovaginal candidiasis?
A. Amphotericin B
B. Fluconazole
C. Griseofulvin
D. Itraconazole
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Fluconazole

Explanation: A single oral dose of Fluconazole (150mg) is the standard and convenient treatment for uncomplicated vulvovaginal candidiasis ("Yeast infection"). Topical azoles are also used.

19.
Which of the following fungi typically affects diabetic ketoacidosis patients with rhino cerebral involvement?
A. Candida
B. Histoplasma
C. Mucor
D. Aspergillus
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Mucor

Explanation: Mucormycosis (caused by Mucor/Rhizopus species) is an aggressive, invasive fungal infection that thrives in acidic, high-glucose environments (Diabetic Ketoacidosis). It classically presents as rhinocerebral mucormycosis with black eschar on the palate or nose.

20.
Which of the following fungi is known for producing aflatoxins, which are hepatocarcinogenic?
A. Candida
B. Aspergillus flavus
C. Penicillium
D. Rhizopus
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Aspergillus flavus

Explanation: Aspergillus flavus contaminates crops like maize and peanuts. It produces Aflatoxins, which are potent toxins that can cause acute liver failure and chronic Hepatocellular Carcinoma (Liver Cancer).

23.
Describe five routes of viral transmission with examples. (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Respiratory (Airborne/Droplet): Inhalation of viral particles (e.g., Influenza, COVID-19, Measles).
  2. Fecal-Oral: Ingestion of contaminated food/water (e.g., Hepatitis A, Poliovirus, Rotavirus).
  3. Sexual Contact: Direct mucosal contact (e.g., HIV, HPV, Herpes Simplex).
  4. Parenteral (Blood-borne): Needlesticks, transfusions (e.g., Hepatitis B, Hepatitis C).
  5. Vertical (Mother-to-Child): Placental, perinatal, or breast milk (e.g., HIV, Rubella, CMV).
  6. Vector-borne: Transmitted by insects like mosquitoes (e.g., Yellow Fever, Dengue).

25.
What are the main characteristics of fungi? (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Eukaryotic: Have a true nucleus and membrane-bound organelles.
  2. Cell Wall: Composed of Chitin (not cellulose like plants or peptidoglycan like bacteria).
  3. Cell Membrane: Contains Ergosterol (mammals have cholesterol).
  4. Heterotrophic: Obtain nutrients by absorption (secreting enzymes externally), not photosynthesis.
  5. Reproduction: Reproduce via spores (sexual or asexual) and budding (yeasts).
  6. Morphology: Can be unicellular (yeasts) or multicellular filamentous (molds).
26.
What roles do fungi play in the environment? (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Decomposers (Saprotrophs): Breakdown dead organic matter (cellulose/lignin), recycling nutrients like Carbon and Nitrogen into the ecosystem.
  2. Symbionts: Form Mycorrhiza with plant roots (aiding water/nutrient absorption) and Lichens (with algae).
  3. Pathogens: Cause diseases in plants (e.g., rusts, blights) affecting agriculture, and in animals.
  4. Food Source: Provide food for insects and animals.
  5. Soil Formation: Lichens contribute to the weathering of rocks.
27.
How are fungi beneficial to humans? (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Medicine: Production of antibiotics (e.g., Penicillin from Penicillium) and immunosuppressants (Cyclosporine).
  2. Food Industry: Yeast (S. cerevisiae) is used for baking (bread) and brewing (alcohol).
  3. Edible Fungi: Mushrooms (e.g., Agaricus) are a direct food source.
  4. Industrial Enzymes: Production of citric acid (A. niger), amylases, and lipases.
  5. Research: Used as model organisms for genetic studies (e.g., Neurospora, Yeast).
28.
What are the major types of fungi? (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Morphological classification:

  1. Yeasts: Unicellular fungi that reproduce by budding (e.g., Candida, Cryptococcus).
  2. Molds (Filamentous Fungi): Multicellular fungi forming hyphae and mycelium (e.g., Aspergillus, Penicillium, Dermatophytes).
  3. Dimorphic Fungi: Exist as molds in the environment (room temp) and yeasts in tissues (body temp) (e.g., Histoplasma, Blastomyces).

(Alternatively, taxonomic groups: Ascomycetes, Basidiomycetes, Zygomycetes).

Section 3: Long Answer Questions (40 Marks)

29.
Cryptococcal meningitis is a leading cause of morbidity and mortality, especially among immunocompromised individuals, particularly those with advanced HIV/AIDS explain the following sub headings: (20 Marks)
a) Etiology (2 Marks)
b) Pathophysiology (3 Marks)
c) Clinical Features (6 Marks)
d) Diagnosis (5 Marks)
e) Complications (4 Marks)
Show Answer

a) Etiology:

Caused by Cryptococcus neoformans, an encapsulated yeast found in soil contaminated with pigeon droppings. (Also Cryptococcus gattii).

b) Pathophysiology:

  • Inhalation: Spores/yeast cells are inhaled into the lungs.
  • Dissemination: In immunocompetent hosts, it is contained. In immunocompromised (HIV CD4 < 100), the fungus disseminates via the blood (hematogenous spread).
  • CNS Invasion: It crosses the blood-brain barrier due to its neurotropism, causing meningitis. The polysaccharide capsule inhibits phagocytosis.

c) Clinical Features:

  • Headache: Progressive and severe.
  • Fever: Usually low grade.
  • Meningism: Neck stiffness, photophobia (less common in severe HIV due to low inflammation).
  • Altered Mental Status: Confusion, irritability, coma.
  • Raised ICP signs: Vomiting, blurred vision (papilledema).
  • Cranial Nerve Palsies: Diplopia (VI nerve), hearing loss.

d) Diagnosis:

  1. CSF Microscopy (India Ink): Shows round yeast cells with a clear "halo" (capsule) against a black background.
  2. Cryptococcal Antigen (CrAg) Test: Latex agglutination or LFA on CSF or Serum (highly sensitive and specific).
  3. Culture: Sabouraud Dextrose Agar (SDA) yields creamy, mucoid colonies (Gold standard).
  4. Lumbar Puncture (LP): Check opening pressure (often very high, >20 cmH2O).
  5. CSF Chemistry: Low glucose, high protein, lymphocytic pleocytosis.

e) Complications:

  • Raised Intracranial Pressure (ICP): Leading to herniation and death.
  • Hydrocephalus: Obstruction of CSF flow.
  • Cranial Nerve Deficits: Blindness (optic atrophy) or deafness.
  • IRIS (Immune Reconstitution Inflammatory Syndrome): Paradoxical worsening after starting ARVs.
30.
Discuss the life cycle of a Virus. (20 Marks)
Show Answer

The viral life cycle generally consists of six main stages:

1. Attachment (Adsorption):

The virus binds to specific receptors on the surface of the host cell. This interaction determines the viral tropism (which cells it can infect). E.g., HIV gp120 binds to CD4 receptors.

2. Penetration (Entry):

The virus enters the cell.
- Enveloped viruses: Enter by fusion with the cell membrane or endocytosis.
- Non-enveloped viruses: Enter by receptor-mediated endocytosis or direct penetration.

3. Uncoating:

The viral capsid is removed/degraded, releasing the viral genome (DNA or RNA) into the host cell's cytoplasm.

4. Replication and Biosynthesis:

The virus hijacks the host's machinery to synthesize viral components:
- Early Proteins: Enzymes needed for replication (e.g., polymerases).
- Genome Replication: Making copies of the viral genetic material.
- Late Proteins: Structural proteins (capsids, spikes).

5. Assembly (Maturation):

New viral genomes and structural proteins are assembled into new virions (nucleocapsids). For enveloped viruses, this may occur at the cell membrane.

6. Release:

New viruses exit the cell to infect others.
- Lysis: Non-enveloped viruses typically burst (lyse) the host cell, killing it.
- Budding: Enveloped viruses acquire their envelope from the host membrane as they exit, often keeping the cell alive for some time.
