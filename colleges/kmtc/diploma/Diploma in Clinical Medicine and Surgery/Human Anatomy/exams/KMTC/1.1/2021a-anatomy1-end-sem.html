Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions

1.
The following are found attached to the surface of rough endoplasmic reticulum:
a. Ribosomes
b. Mitochondria
c. Golgi apparatus
d. Glycogen
e. Lysosomes
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a. Ribosomes

Explanation: The "rough" appearance of Rough Endoplasmic Reticulum (RER) is due to the presence of ribosomes attached to its cytosolic surface, which are involved in protein synthesis.

2.
Regarding cell division:
a. Mitosis involves two segmental cell divisions.
b. Chiasmata formation occurs in meiosis only.
c. Meiotic division results in production of four diploid daughter cells.
d. Prophase is followed by Telophase then the G2 phase.
e. None of the above.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b. Chiasmata formation occurs in meiosis only.

Explanation:

  • b is True: Chiasmata formation (crossing over) is a unique feature of Prophase I of Meiosis.
  • a is False: Mitosis is one division; Meiosis is two.
  • c is False: Meiosis produces four haploid cells.
  • d is False: Sequence is Prophase -> Metaphase -> Anaphase -> Telophase.

Section 2: Short Answer Questions (40 Marks)

1.
Explain giving examples the levels of structural organizations of human body. (10 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Chemical Level: Atoms combine to form molecules. (Example: Water, DNA, Proteins).
  2. Cellular Level: Molecules combine to form cells, the basic unit of life. (Example: Muscle cell, Neuron).
  3. Tissue Level: Similar cells work together to perform a specific function. (Example: Muscle tissue, Nervous tissue).
  4. Organ Level: Different tissues join to form structures with specific functions. (Example: Stomach, Heart).
  5. System Level: Organs work together to perform complex functions. (Example: Digestive system, Cardiovascular system).
  6. Organism Level: All systems working together to maintain life. (Example: The Human Being).
2.
a) State the functions of skeletal system. (5 marks)
b) State giving example the two major classes of bones and list with an example 3 types of bones. (5 marks)
Show Answer

a) Functions of Skeletal System:

  • Support: Provides structural framework for the body.
  • Protection: Protects vital organs (e.g., Skull protects brain).
  • Movement: Acts as levers for muscles.
  • Mineral Storage: Stores calcium and phosphorus.
  • Blood Cell Production (Hemopoiesis): Occurs in red bone marrow.

b) Classification of Bones:

Major Classes:

  1. Axial Skeleton: Skull, Vertebrae, Ribs.
  2. Appendicular Skeleton: Limbs, Pectoral and Pelvic girdles.

Types of Bones (by shape):

  1. Long Bones: Femur, Humerus.
  2. Short Bones: Carpals, Tarsals.
  3. Flat Bones: Sternum, Scapula.
  4. Irregular Bones: Vertebrae.
3.
Explain Ribs Using the two ways of classifying of ribs (10 marks)
Show Answer

1. Classification by Attachment to Sternum:

  • True Ribs (Vertebrosternal): Ribs 1-7. Attach directly to the sternum via their own costal cartilage.
  • False Ribs (Vertebrochondral): Ribs 8-10. Attach indirectly to the sternum; their cartilage joins the cartilage of the rib above (7th).
  • Floating Ribs (Vertebral): Ribs 11-12. Do not attach to the sternum at all; floating in the abdominal wall muscles.

2. Classification by Morphology (Features):

  • Typical Ribs: Ribs 3-9. Have a head with two facets, a neck, a tubercle, and a shaft with a costal groove.
  • Atypical Ribs: Ribs 1, 2, 10, 11, 12. Have unique features (e.g., Rib 1 is flat/broad with one facet; Ribs 11/12 have no neck or tubercle).

2.
Describe the spine (20 marks)
Show Answer

Structure and Regions:

The vertebral column consists of 33 vertebrae grouped into 5 regions:

  1. Cervical (7): Neck region. C1 (Atlas) and C2 (Axis) are atypical. Lordotic curve.
  2. Thoracic (12): Chest region. Articulate with ribs. Kyphotic curve.
  3. Lumbar (5): Lower back. Large bodies for weight bearing. Lordotic curve.
  4. Sacral (5 fused): Fused to form Sacrum. Kyphotic curve.
  5. Coccygeal (4 fused): Fused to form Coccyx (Tailbone).

Curvatures:

  • Primary (Kyphosis): Thoracic and Sacral (convex posteriorly). Present at birth.
  • Secondary (Lordosis): Cervical (develops when holding head up) and Lumbar (develops when walking). Convex anteriorly.

Typical Vertebra Structure:

  • Body: Anterior weight-bearing part.
  • Vertebral Arch: Posterior part formed by pedicles and laminae. Encloses the vertebral foramen (spinal cord).
  • Processes: Spinous (1), Transverse (2), Articular (4 - Superior/Inferior).

Intervertebral Discs: Fibrocartilaginous cushions between vertebrae acting as shock absorbers.

Functions: Protects spinal cord, supports head/body weight, allows flexibility/movement.
