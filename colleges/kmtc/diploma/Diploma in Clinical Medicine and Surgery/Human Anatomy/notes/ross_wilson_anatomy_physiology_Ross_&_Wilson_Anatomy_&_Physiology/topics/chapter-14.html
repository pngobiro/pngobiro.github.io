The Skin

üõ°Ô∏è Protection & Sensation üå°Ô∏è Thermoregulation

The skin (integumentary system) completely covers the body and is continuous with the membranes lining the body orifices. It protects the underlying structures from injury and from invasion by microbes, contains sensory nerve endings that enable discrimination of pain, temperature and touch, and is involved in the regulation of body temperature.

This chapter explores the structure and functions of the skin, describes the process of wound healing, discusses the effects of ageing, and reviews common skin conditions.

01

üß¨ Structure of the Skin

üéØ Learning Outcomes

After studying this section, you should be able to:

  • describe the structure of the skin

The skin is the largest organ in the body and has a surface area of about 1.5‚Äì2 square metres in adults. It varies in thickness, being thickest on the palms of the hands and soles of the feet. There are two main layers:

  • Epidermis: The superficial layer.
  • Dermis: The deeper layer.

Between the dermis and underlying structures is a subcutaneous layer (hypodermis) composed of areolar tissue and adipose (fat) tissue.

Epidermis

The epidermis is composed of stratified keratinised squamous epithelium. It contains no blood vessels or nerve endings; nutrients are provided by interstitial fluid from the dermis. There are several layers (strata) of cells, extending from the deepest basal layer to the most superficial stratum corneum.

Cells in the basal layer divide constantly and are pushed upwards. As they progress, they change shape and structure, eventually becoming flat, dead cells (squames) containing keratin. Complete replacement of the epidermis takes about a month.

Skin Colour:

  • Melanin: A dark pigment secreted by melanocytes in the basal layer. It protects against UV radiation. The amount of melanin determines skin colour.
  • Haemoglobin: Oxygenated blood in the dermis gives fair skin a pink colour. Low oxygen saturation causes cyanosis (bluish tint).
  • Bile pigments/Carotenes: Excess levels can cause yellowish discoloration (jaundice).

Dermis

The dermis is tough and elastic, formed from connective tissue containing collagen and elastic fibers. Rupture of elastic fibers leads to stretch marks (striae). Fibroblasts, macrophages, and mast cells are the main cell types. Structures found in the dermis include:

  • blood and lymph vessels
  • sensory nerve endings
  • sweat glands and ducts
  • hairs, arrector pili muscles, and sebaceous glands.

Sensory nerve endings

Sensory receptors in the dermis are sensitive to touch, temperature, pressure, and pain.

Sweat glands

Eccrine sweat glands: Widely distributed, open onto the surface via pores. Secrete clear, watery fluid for temperature regulation.
Apocrine sweat glands: Open into hair follicles (e.g., axilla, groin). Become active at puberty; bacterial decomposition of secretions causes odour.

Hairs

Hairs grow from follicles. The hair bulb contains dividing cells. As cells are pushed up, they become keratinised and die. The arrector pili are smooth muscles attached to follicles; contraction causes "gooseflesh" and raises the hair, trapping air for insulation.

Sebaceous glands

Secrete sebum (oily substance) into hair follicles. Sebum keeps hair/skin soft, waterproofs the skin, and has antimicrobial properties.

Nails

Hard plates of keratin protecting fingers and toes. The nail plate grows from the nail bed (germinal layer). The lunula is the pale half-moon shape at the base.

üéØ Learning Outcomes

After studying this section, you should be able to:

  • explain the principal functions of the skin.

Protection

The skin acts as a barrier against microbial invasion, chemicals, physical trauma, and dehydration. Dendritic (Langerhans) cells in the epidermis play a role in immunity. Melanin protects against UV radiation. Sensory endings trigger protective reflexes.

Regulation of body temperature

Body temperature is maintained at ~36.8¬∞C. The skin regulates heat loss through:

  • Radiation, Conduction, Convection: Loss of heat from the warm body surface to cooler air/objects.
  • Evaporation: Sweat evaporation cools the body.

Control mechanisms: The hypothalamus acts as the thermostat. If body temperature rises, dermal arterioles dilate (vasodilation) and sweat glands are stimulated. If temperature falls, arterioles constrict (vasoconstriction) to conserve heat, and shivering may occur.

Formation of vitamin D

7-dehydrocholesterol in the skin is converted to vitamin D by UV light.

Cutaneous sensation

Sensory receptors detect touch, pressure, temperature, and pain.

Absorption

Limited, but some drugs (transdermal patches) and chemicals can be absorbed.

Excretion

A minor role in excreting sodium chloride, urea, and aromatic substances (e.g., garlic).

ü©π Wound Healing

üéØ Learning Outcomes

After studying this section, you should be able to:

  • compare and contrast the processes of primary and secondary wound healing.

Primary healing (first intention)

Occurs when wound edges are in close apposition (e.g., surgical incision). Minimal tissue loss. Stages:

  1. 1 Inflammation: Blood clot forms; phagocytes remove debris.
  2. 2 Proliferation: Epithelial cells grow across the wound; granulation tissue develops.
  3. 3 Maturation: Granulation tissue is replaced by fibrous scar tissue.

Secondary healing (second intention)

Occurs when there is extensive tissue loss or edges cannot be brought together (e.g., ulcers). Healing takes longer and involves:

  • Inflammation: Separation of necrotic tissue (slough).
  • Proliferation: Granulation tissue grows from the base upwards.
  • Maturation: Fibrosis (scar formation) replaces granulation tissue. Scar tissue is shiny and lacks hair follicles/sweat glands.

Fibrosis (scar formation)

Fibrous tissue replacement can lead to complications such as adhesions, fibrosis of infarcts, and tissue shrinkage (causing stenosis or contractures).

Complications of wound healing

  • Infection: Formation of pus (suppuration). Can lead to abscesses (Fig. 14.10) or fistulae (Fig. 14.11).

üï∞Ô∏è Effects of Ageing on the Skin

üéØ Learning Outcomes

After studying this section, you should be able to:

  • describe the effects of ageing on the structure and function of the skin.

With age:

  • Epidermis and dermis thin; elastic/collagen fibers decrease $\rightarrow$ wrinkling and sagging.
  • Sweat and sebum secretion decrease $\rightarrow$ dry skin, risk of heatstroke, susceptibility to maceration.
  • Vitamin D production decreases.
  • Melanocyte activity decreases $\rightarrow$ greying hair, increased sun sensitivity.
  • Hair follicles become inactive $\rightarrow$ hair thinning.

ü§í Disorders of the Skin

üéØ Learning Outcomes

After studying this section, you should be able to:

  • list the causes of the diseases described here
  • explain the pathological features and effects of common skin conditions.

Infections

  • Viral: HPV (warts), Herpes zoster (shingles), Herpes simplex (cold sores).
  • Bacterial: Impetigo (Staphylococcus aureus), Cellulitis (spreading infection).
  • Fungal: Ringworm, Tinea pedis (athlete's foot).

Non-infective inflammatory conditions

  • Dermatitis (eczema): Atopic (allergic) or Contact (irritant). Redness, swelling, itching.
  • Psoriasis: Genetic/environmental. Accelerated cell division $\rightarrow$ red, scaly plaques (Fig. 14.12).
  • Acne vulgaris: Blocked/infected sebaceous glands, common in adolescence.

Pressure ulcers

Caused by prolonged pressure over bony prominences, leading to ischemia and necrosis (bedsores).

Burns

Classified by depth:

  • Superficial: Epidermis only.
  • Partial thickness: Epidermis and upper dermis (blistering).
  • Full thickness: Epidermis and dermis destroyed.

Rule of nines: Used to estimate burn extent (Fig. 14.13).
Complications: Dehydration, shock, hypothermia, infection, contractures.

Malignant tumours

  • Basal cell carcinoma: Common, least malignant (rodent ulcer).
  • Malignant melanoma: From melanocytes. Metastasizes early (Fig. 14.14).
  • Kaposi's sarcoma: Associated with HIV/AIDS.
