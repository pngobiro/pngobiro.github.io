Resistance and Immunity

üõ°Ô∏è Defense Mechanisms ü¶† Immune Response

From the months spent in the womb to the end of life, every individual is under constant attack from an enormous range of potentially harmful invaders. These threats include such diverse entities as bacteria, viruses, cancer cells, parasites and foreign (non-self) cells, e.g. in tissue transplant. The body has therefore developed a wide selection of protective measures, which can be divided into two categories.

Non-specific (innate) defence mechanisms

These protect against any of an enormous range of possible dangers and are present at birth: hence the term 'innate'.

Specific (adaptive) defence mechanisms

These are grouped together under the term 'immunity'. Resistance is directed against only one specific invader. This type of immunity is sometimes called adaptive immunity because it adapts, refines and strengthens its response to multiple exposures to the same antigen. This ability to 'learn' is responsible for the development of immunological memory, giving long-term immunity to specific infections. An antigen is anything that stimulates an immune response.

These are the first lines of general defence; they prevent entry and minimise further passage of microbes and other foreign material into the body. They offer a rapid response system, permanently mobilised and providing constant protection. There are five main non-specific defence mechanisms:

  • epithelial barriers
  • phagocytosis
  • natural antimicrobial substances
  • the inflammatory response
  • immunological surveillance.

Epithelial barriers

Healthy, intact skin and mucous membranes provide an efficient physical barrier protecting the body's exposed surfaces. The skin carries a rich population of bacteria, called commensals, that do not normally cause infections. Few pathogens can establish themselves on healthy skin. Sebum and sweat secreted on to the skin surface contain antibacterial and antifungal substances.

Epithelial membranes lining body cavities and passageways exposed to the external environment (e.g. the respiratory, genitourinary and digestive tracts) are more delicate but are also well defended. Epithelia produce antibacterial secretions, often acidic, containing antibodies and enzymes, as well as sticky mucus for trapping passing microbes.

Phagocytosis

The process of phagocytosis ('cell eating') is the body's first line of cellular defence. Phagocytes actively migrate (chemotaxis) to sites of inflammation and infection because neutrophils themselves and invading microbes release chemicals that attract them (chemoattractants). Phagocytes attack and engulf their targets (Fig. 15.1).

Neutrophils are very short-lived once activated, and destroy themselves as well as their targets with their own toxic products. Macrophages are much longer-lived phagocytes, and also have an important role as a link between the non-specific and specific defence mechanisms. After ingestion and digestion of an antigen, they act as antigen-presenting cells, displaying their antigen on their own cell surface to stimulate T-lymphocytes and activate the adaptive immune response.

Natural antimicrobial substances

  • Hydrochloric acid: Present in high concentrations in gastric juice and kills the majority of ingested microbes.
  • Lysozyme: This antibacterial enzyme is present in granulocytes, tears and other body secretions. It destroys bacterial cell walls.
  • Antibodies: Protective proteins that inactivate bacteria, found coating membranes exposed to the external environment and in body fluids.
  • Saliva: Secreted into the mouth, washes away food debris. Contains antibodies, lysozyme and buffers.
  • Interferons: Chemicals produced by T-lymphocytes, macrophages and virus-infected cells. They prevent viral replication within infected cells and reduce spread to healthy cells.
  • Complement: A system of about 20 proteins found in the blood and tissues. Activated by immune complexes or foreign sugars on bacterial cell walls, it destroys microbes, stimulates phagocytosis, and attracts phagocytic cells.

The inflammatory response

This is the physiological response to tissue damage and is accompanied by a characteristic series of local changes (Fig. 15.2). Its purpose is protective: to isolate, inactivate and remove both the causative agent and damaged tissue, so that healing can take place. The cardinal signs of inflammation are:

  • redness
  • heat
  • swelling
  • pain.

Acute inflammation

The acute inflammatory response involves a collection of overlapping events: increased blood flow, accumulation of tissue fluid, migration of leukocytes, increased core temperature, pain and suppuration. Release of inflammatory mediators into the blood leads to the acute phase response.

Outcomes of acute inflammation:

  • Resolution: Complete restoration of normal tissue.
  • Development of chronic inflammation: If resolution is not complete, inflammation persists.
  • Suppuration (pus formation): Formation of abscesses containing dead phagocytes, cells, and microbes.

Immunological surveillance

A population of lymphocytes, called natural killer (NK) cells, constantly patrol the body searching for abnormal host cells, such as virus-infected cells or mutated cells that might become malignant. Having detected an abnormal cell, the NK cell immediately kills it.

ü¶† Immunity (Specific Defence)

üéØ Learning Outcomes

After studying this section, you should be able to:

  • discuss the roles of the different types of T-lymphocyte in providing cell-mediated immunity
  • describe the process of antibody-mediated immunity
  • distinguish between artificially and naturally acquired immunity, giving examples of each
  • distinguish between active and passive immunity, giving examples of each.

Immunity possesses three key attributes not seen with non-specific defences:

  • Specificity: Response is directed against one specific antigen.
  • Memory: Generates immunological memory, making subsequent responses faster and more powerful.
  • Tolerance: Ability to distinguish 'self' from 'non-self' to avoid attacking the body's own tissues.

Lymphocytes

T- and B-cells are responsible for immunity. Each individual T- and B-cell carries antigen recognition molecules, allowing it to respond to only one specific antigen.

T-cells

Mature in the thymus gland. They provide cell-mediated immunity.

  • Cytotoxic T-cells: Directly inactivate cells carrying antigens (e.g., infected or cancer cells).
  • Helper T-cells: Essential for both cell-mediated and antibody-mediated immunity. Release cytokines to support other immune cells. Destroyed by HIV.
  • Regulatory T-cells: Act as 'brakes', turning off activated T- and B-cells to limit damage and prevent autoimmunity.
  • Memory T-cells: Long-lived cells that provide rapid response on re-exposure.

B-cells

Mature in bone marrow. They provide antibody-mediated (humoral) immunity. When activated (often with T-helper cell help), they divide to produce:

  • Plasma cells: Secrete massive quantities of antibodies.
  • Memory B-cells: Provide long-term immunity.

Antibodies (Immunoglobulins)

There are five main types of antibody (Table 15.2).

üìä Table 15.2 The five types of antibody
Type Function
IgA Found in body secretions (saliva, milk), protects epithelial membranes.
IgD Found on B-cell surfaces, activates B-cells.
IgE Found on basophils/mast cells, activates inflammatory response (allergy).
IgG Most common, longest-lived. Attacks pathogens, crosses placenta.
IgM First antibody produced in primary response, potent complement activator.

Acquired Immunity

Primary Response: Slow rise in antibody levels (mainly IgM) after first exposure.
Secondary Response: Faster, more powerful response (mainly IgG) on subsequent exposure due to memory cells (Fig. 15.6).

Types of Acquired Immunity (Fig. 15.7):

  • Active Naturally Acquired: From having the disease or subclinical infection.
  • Active Artificially Acquired: Vaccination (vaccines or toxoids).
  • Passive Naturally Acquired: Maternal antibodies crossing placenta or in breast milk.
  • Passive Artificially Acquired: Injection of ready-made antibodies (antisera).

03

üëµ Ageing and Immunity

Immunity declines with advancing age, increasing the risk of infection in older adults and lengthening recovery times. The thymus gland progressively shrinks. This is linked to decreased T-cell responsiveness, and consequently antibody levels also fall. Levels of autoantibodies and incidence of autoimmunity increase, and reduced NK cell function is associated with increasing cancer incidence.

Hypersensitivity (allergy)

Allergy is an inappropriate, powerful immune response to a harmless antigen (allergen). There are four types of hypersensitivity (Fig. 15.9).

  • Type I (Anaphylactic): Immediate reaction. Allergen binds to IgE on mast cells/basophils $\rightarrow$ histamine release. Examples: Hay fever, eczema, anaphylaxis.
  • Type II (Antibody-mediated): Antibodies bind to cell surface antigens $\rightarrow$ cell destruction. Examples: Blood transfusion reactions, haemolytic disease of newborn.
  • Type III (Immune complex-mediated): Antibody-antigen complexes deposit in tissues $\rightarrow$ inflammation. Examples: Glomerulonephritis, penicillin allergy.
  • Type IV (Delayed/Cell-mediated): T-cell over-reaction (no antibodies). Delayed onset. Examples: Contact dermatitis, graft rejection.

Autoimmune disease

The body fails to recognise its own tissues and attacks them. Examples include:

Immunodeficiency

AIDS (Acquired Immune Deficiency Syndrome): Caused by HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus), which destroys CD4 helper T-cells. This cripples both cell-mediated and antibody-mediated immunity, leading to opportunistic infections (e.g., pneumonia) and cancers (e.g., Kaposi's sarcoma).
