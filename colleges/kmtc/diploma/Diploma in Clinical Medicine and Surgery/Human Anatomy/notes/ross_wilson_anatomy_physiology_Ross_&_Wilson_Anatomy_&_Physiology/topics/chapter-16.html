The Musculoskeletal System

ü¶¥ Bones & Joints üí™ Muscles

The musculoskeletal system consists of the bones of the skeleton, their joints and the skeletal (voluntary) muscles that move the body. The characteristics and properties of joints and of bone and muscle tissue are discussed in this chapter. The effects of ageing on the musculoskeletal system are listed, and the illnesses section at the end of the chapter describes some disorders of bone, muscle and joints.

01

ü¶¥ Bone

üéØ Learning Outcomes

After studying this section, you should be able to:

  • state the functions of bones
  • list five types of bone and give an example of each
  • outline the general structure of a long bone
  • describe the structure of compact and spongy bone tissue
  • describe the development of bone
  • explain the process of bone healing and the factors that complicate it
  • outline the factors that determine bone growth.

Although bones are often thought to be static or permanent, they are highly vascular living structures that are continuously being remodelled.

Functions of bones

The functions of bones include:

  • providing the body framework
  • giving attachment to muscles and tendons
  • allowing movement of the body as a whole and of parts of the body, by forming joints that are moved by muscles
  • forming the boundaries of the cranium, thorax and pelvis, and protecting the organs they contain
  • haemopoiesis, the production of blood cells in red bone marrow (see Fig. 4.3)
  • mineral storage, especially calcium phosphate ‚Äî the mineral reservoir within bone is essential for maintenance of blood calcium levels, which must be tightly controlled.

Types of bone

Bones are classified by shape as long, short, irregular, flat and sesamoid (Fig. 16.1).

Long bones
These consist of a shaft and two extremities. As the name suggests, these bones are longer than they are wide. Most long bones are found in the limbs; examples include the femur, tibia and fibula.

Short, irregular, flat and sesamoid bones
These have no shafts or extremities and are diverse in shape and size. Examples include:

  • short bones ‚Äì carpals (wrist), metacarpals (ankle)
  • irregular bones ‚Äì vertebrae and some skull bones
  • flat bones ‚Äì sternum, ribs and most skull bones
  • sesamoid (shaped like a sesame seed) bones ‚Äì patella (knee cap).

Bone structure

Long bones

These have a diaphysis (shaft) and two epiphyses (extremities) (Fig. 16.2). The diaphysis is composed mainly of compact bone, with a central medullary canal containing fatty yellow bone marrow. The epiphyses consist of an outer covering of compact bone with spongy (cancellous) bone inside. The diaphysis and epiphyses are separated by epiphyseal cartilages, which ossify when growth is complete.

Long bones are almost completely covered by a vascular membrane, the periosteum, which has two layers. The outer layer is tough and fibrous, and protects the bone underneath. The inner layer contains osteoblasts and osteoclasts, the cells responsible for bone production and breakdown, and is important in repair and remodelling of the bone. The periosteum covers the whole bone except within joint cavities, allows attachments of tendons and is continuous with the joint capsule. Hyaline cartilage replaces periosteum on bone surfaces that form joints.

Short, irregular, flat and sesamoid bones

These have a relatively thin outer layer of compact bone, with spongy bone inside containing red bone marrow (Fig. 16.3). They are enclosed by periosteum except for the inner layer of the cranial bones, where it is replaced by dura mater.

Microscopic structure of bone

Bone is a strong and durable type of connective tissue. Its major constituent (65%) is a mixture of calcium salts, mainly calcium phosphate. This inorganic matrix gives bone great hardness but on its own would be brittle and prone to shattering. The remaining third is organic material, called osteoid, which is composed mainly of collagen fibres. Collagen is very strong and gives bone slight flexibility. It is used as the framework for building the calcium-rich inorganic matrix. The cellular component of bone contributes less than 2% of bone mass.

Bone cells

There are three types of bone cell:

  • osteoblasts, bone-building cells
  • osteocytes, mature bone cells
  • osteoclasts, bone-reabsorbing cells.

Osteoblasts: These bone-forming cells deposit both inorganic salts and osteoid in bone tissue. They are present at sites where bone is growing, repairing or remodelling.

Osteocytes: These mature bone cells do not divide. They monitor and maintain bone tissue, and are nourished by tissue fluid in the canaliculi that radiate from the central canals.

Osteoclasts: These cells break down bone, releasing calcium and phosphate. They are giant cells with up to 50 nuclei, which have formed from the fusion of many monocytes.

Compact (cortical) bone

Compact bone makes up about 80% of the body bone mass. It is made up of a large number of parallel tube-shaped units called osteons (Haversian systems), each of which is made up of a central canal surrounded by a series of expanding rings, similar to the growth rings of a tree (see Fig. 16.4). The central canal contains nerves, lymphatics and blood vessels.

Spongy (cancellous, trabecular) bone

About 20% of skeletal bone mass is spongy bone. To the naked eye, spongy bone looks like a honeycomb. This honeycomb arrangement has two main functions. The spaces contain red bone marrow, which produces blood cells; this means that spongy bone is much lighter than compact bone, reducing the weight of the skeleton. Microscopic examination reveals a bony framework formed from trabeculae.

Development of bone tissue

Also called osteogenesis or ossification, this begins before birth and is not complete until about the 21st year of life.

Development of long bones: In long bones the focal points from which ossification begins are small areas of osteogenic cells, or centres of ossification in the cartilage model (Fig. 16.7). The bone lengthens as ossification continues and spreads to the epiphyses. During childhood, long bones continue to lengthen because the epiphyseal plate at each end of the bone, which is made of cartilage, continues to produce new cartilage on its diaphyseal surface.

Hormonal regulation of bone growth

  • Growth hormone and thyroid hormones: Essential during infancy and childhood.
  • Testosterone and oestrogens: Influence physical changes at puberty and stimulate closure of epiphyseal plates.
  • Calcitonin and parathyroid hormone: Control blood calcium levels by regulating uptake into and release from bone.

Healing of bone

Following a fracture, the broken ends of bone are joined by the deposition of new bone. This occurs in several stages (Fig. 16.9):

  1. 1Haematoma formation: A collection of clotted blood forms between bone ends.
  2. 2Granulation tissue formation: Fibroblasts and osteoblasts migrate to the site.
  3. 3Callus formation: Osteoblasts deposit new bone (callus) which splints the fracture.
  4. 4Remodelling: External callus is converted to compact bone and the medullary cavity is recanalised.

Factors that delay fracture healing:

  • Tissue fragments between bone ends
  • Deficient blood supply
  • Poor alignment of bone ends
  • Continued mobility of bone ends
  • Infection

üíÄ Axial Skeleton

The axial skeleton consists of the skull, vertebral column, ribs and sternum.

Skull

The skull (Figs 16.11 and 16.12) rests on the upper end of the vertebral column and its bony structure is divided into two parts: the cranium and the face.

Cranium: The cranium is formed by a number of flat and irregular bones that protect the brain. The bones of the cranium are: 1 frontal bone, 2 parietal bones, 2 temporal bones, 1 occipital bone, 1 sphenoid bone, 1 ethmoid bone.

Face: The skeleton of the face is formed by 13 bones in addition to the frontal bone. These include: 2 zygomatic bones, 1 maxilla, 2 nasal bones, 2 lacrimal bones, 1 vomer, 2 palatine bones, 2 inferior conchae, 1 mandible.

Sinuses: Sinuses containing air are present in the sphenoid, ethmoid, maxillary and frontal bones. They communicate with the nasal cavity and give resonance to the voice.

Fontanelles: At birth, ossification of the cranial sutures is incomplete. Membranous areas called fontanelles (Fig. 16.19) allow for moulding of the baby's head during childbirth.

Vertebral column

There are 26 bones in the vertebral column (Fig. 16.20). It is divided into regions:

  • Cervical spine: 7 vertebrae (C1-C7).
  • Thoracic spine: 12 vertebrae.
  • Lumbar spine: 5 vertebrae.
  • Sacrum: 5 fused vertebrae.
  • Coccyx: 3-5 fused vertebrae.

Intervertebral discs: The bodies of adjacent vertebrae are separated by intervertebral discs, consisting of an outer rim of fibrocartilage (annulus fibrosus) and a central core of soft gelatinous material (nucleus pulposus). They act as shock absorbers.

Thoracic cage

The thorax is made up of the sternum anteriorly, 12 pairs of ribs forming the lateral bony cages, and the 12 thoracic vertebrae.

ü¶µ Appendicular Skeleton

The appendicular skeleton consists of the shoulder girdle with the upper limbs and the pelvic girdle with the lower limbs.

Shoulder girdle and upper limb

The shoulder girdle consists of two clavicles and two scapulae. The upper limb consists of the humerus (upper arm), radius and ulna (forearm), carpal bones (wrist), metacarpal bones (hand), and phalanges (fingers).

Pelvic girdle and lower limb

The pelvic girdle is formed from two innominate (hip) bones. Each hip bone consists of three fused bones: the ilium, ischium and pubis. The lower limb consists of the femur (thigh), tibia and fibula (leg), patella (knee cap), tarsal bones (ankle), metatarsals (foot), and phalanges (toes).

Arches of the foot

The arrangement of bones in the foot gives the sole an arched shape: the longitudinal arch and the transverse arch. This structure acts as a shock absorber and lever for propulsion.

üîó Joints

A joint is the site at which any two or more bones articulate. There are three main types:

Fibrous joints

The bones are linked with tough, fibrous material. Often immovable, e.g., sutures of the skull.

Cartilaginous joints

Formed by a pad of fibrocartilage. Permit limited movement, e.g., symphysis pubis, intervertebral discs.

Synovial joints

Characterised by the presence of a space or capsule between the articulating bones. They are the most movable joints.

Characteristics of a synovial joint:

  • Articular cartilage: Hyaline cartilage covers bone ends.
  • Capsule: Fibrous tissue holding bones together.
  • Synovial membrane: Lines the capsule and secretes synovial fluid.
  • Synovial fluid: Lubricates and nourishes the joint.

Types of synovial joint

  • Ball and socket: Wide range of movement (e.g., shoulder, hip).
  • Hinge: Movement in one plane (e.g., elbow, knee).
  • Gliding: Flat surfaces glide over each other (e.g., carpal bones).
  • Pivot: Rotation (e.g., atlas/axis).
  • Condyloid: Flexion, extension, abduction, adduction (e.g., wrist).
  • Saddle: Similar to condyloid but more flexible (e.g., thumb).

üí™ Skeletal Muscle

Organisation

Skeletal muscles are composed of muscle fibres (cells) collected into bundles called fascicles. Connective tissue layers (epimysium, perimysium, endomysium) bind the fibres and form tendons.

Structure of muscle fibres

Muscle cells are cylindrical and striated. They contain myofibrils made of thick (myosin) and thin (actin) filaments arranged in units called sarcomeres.

Contraction: Triggered by acetylcholine release at the neuromuscular junction. Calcium release triggers the sliding of actin and myosin filaments (sliding filament theory), shortening the sarcomere.

Action

Muscles work in antagonistic pairs (e.g., biceps and triceps). Contraction can be isotonic (shortening with constant tension) or isometric (increased tension without shortening).

Principal skeletal muscles

Key muscles include:

  • Face/Neck: Orbicularis oculi, masseter, sternocleidomastoid, trapezius.
  • Trunk: Intercostals, diaphragm, rectus abdominis, obliques.
  • Upper Limb: Deltoid, pectoralis major, biceps brachii, triceps.
  • Lower Limb: Gluteals, quadriceps femoris, hamstrings, gastrocnemius.

üï∞Ô∏è Effects of Ageing

Osteopenia: Bone mass decreases with age, increasing fracture risk. Oestrogen loss accelerates this in women.
Joints: Cartilage stiffens and degenerates, predisposing to osteoarthritis.
Muscles: Fibres become smaller and less elastic. Muscle mass and strength decline (sarcopenia).

‚öïÔ∏è Diseases and Disorders

Diseases of bone

  • Osteoporosis: Reduced bone density leading to fragility and fractures.
  • Paget's disease: Disordered bone remodelling causing deformity.
  • Rickets/Osteomalacia: Vitamin D deficiency causing inadequate mineralisation (soft bones).
  • Osteomyelitis: Bacterial infection of bone.
  • Tumours: Osteosarcoma (malignant), metastatic tumours.

Disorders of joints

  • Rheumatoid arthritis (RA): Autoimmune inflammatory disease affecting synovial membranes.
  • Osteoarthritis (OA): Degenerative disease affecting articular cartilage in weight-bearing joints.
  • Gout: Deposition of sodium urate crystals in joints causing acute inflammation.
