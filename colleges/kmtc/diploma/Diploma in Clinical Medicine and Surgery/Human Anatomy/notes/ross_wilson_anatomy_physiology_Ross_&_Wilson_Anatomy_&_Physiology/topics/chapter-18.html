The Reproductive Systems

‚ôÄÔ∏è Female System ‚ôÇÔ∏è Male System

The ability to reproduce is one of the properties distinguishing living from nonliving matter. The more primitive the animal, the simpler the process of reproduction. In mammals, including humans, the process is one of sexual reproduction, in which the male and female organs differ anatomically and physiologically, and the new individual develops from the fusion of two different sex cells (gametes). The male gametes are called spermatozoa (singular: spermatozoon) and the female gametes are called ova (singular: ovum).

This chapter explains the structure and functions of the female and male reproductive systems, including the production of gametes, provides a brief overview of fetal development, and discusses the effects of ageing on reproductive function. Finally, some significant reproductive disorders are described.

üéØ Learning Outcomes

The functions of the female reproductive system are:

  • formation of ova
  • reception of spermatozoa
  • provision of suitable environments for fertilisation and fetal development
  • parturition (childbirth)
  • lactation, the production of breast milk.

External genitalia (vulva)

The external genitalia (Fig. 18.2) are known collectively as the vulva.

  • Labia majora: Two large folds forming the boundary of the vulva, composed of fibrous tissue and fat, containing sebaceous and sweat glands.
  • Labia minora: Two smaller folds of skin between the labia majora.
  • Clitoris: Contains sensory nerve endings and erectile tissue.
  • Vestibule: The cleft between the labia minora where the vagina, urethra and ducts of the greater vestibular glands open.
  • Vestibular glands (Bartholin's glands): Situated one on each side near the vaginal opening; secrete mucus to keep the vulva moist.
  • Perineum: Roughly triangular area extending from the base of the labia minora to the anal canal.

Internal genitalia

The internal organs (Figs 18.3 and 18.4) lie in the pelvic cavity and consist of the vagina, uterus, two uterine tubes and two ovaries.

Vagina

A fibromuscular tube extending from the vestibule to the uterine cervix. It runs obliquely upwards and backwards at an angle of about 45 degrees between the bladder and rectum. The wall has three layers: an outer areolar tissue layer, a middle smooth muscle layer, and an inner lining of stratified squamous epithelium forming ridges or rugae. Normally, Lactobacillus acidophilus maintains a pH between 4.9 and 3.5, inhibiting other microbes.

Uterus

A hollow, muscular, pear-shaped organ lying in the pelvic cavity between the bladder and rectum. It has three main parts: the fundus, body and cervix.

Structure: The uterine wall has three layers (Fig. 18.5):

  • Perimetrium: Outer layer of peritoneum.
  • Myometrium: Thick middle layer of smooth muscle fibres.
  • Endometrium: Inner mucous membrane lining with columnar epithelium and glands. It has a functional layer (shed during menstruation) and a basal layer.

Supporting structures: Supported by the broad ligaments, round ligaments, uterosacral ligaments, transverse cervical ligaments, and pubocervical ligaments (Fig. 18.6).

Uterine tubes (Fallopian tubes)

Extend from the sides of the uterus to the ovaries. The trumpet-shaped lateral ends have finger-like projections called fimbriae. They propel the ovum from the ovary to the uterus and are the usual site of fertilisation.

Ovaries

The female gonads, lying in a shallow fossa on the lateral pelvic walls. They have two layers: a central medulla and an outer cortex containing ovarian follicles. About every 28 days, a mature (Graafian) follicle ruptures, releasing an ovum (ovulation). The ruptured follicle becomes the corpus luteum.

Functions: Maturation of follicles (stimulated by FSH) and secretion of hormones (oestrogen, progesterone, inhibin).

Puberty and the Reproductive Cycle

Puberty usually occurs between ages 12 and 14 (menarche). The hypothalamus secretes LHRH, stimulating the anterior pituitary to secrete FSH and LH. This initiates the cycle of ovarian activity and uterine changes.

The Reproductive Cycle (Fig. 18.10):

  • Menstrual phase (Days 1-4): The functional layer of the endometrium is shed if fertilization does not occur.
  • Proliferative phase (Days 5-14): Ovarian follicles mature (stimulated by FSH) and secrete oestrogen, thickening the endometrium. A surge in LH triggers ovulation around day 14.
  • Secretory phase (Days 15-28): The corpus luteum secretes progesterone and oestrogen, preparing the endometrium for implantation. If fertilisation does not occur, hormone levels fall, leading to menstruation.

Menopause

Usually occurs between 45 and 55 years. Ovarian follicles are exhausted, oestrogen levels fall, and reproductive cycles cease. Symptoms may include hot flushes, breast shrinkage, and bone mass loss (osteoporosis).

Breasts (Mammary Glands)

Accessory glands consisting of glandular tissue (lobes and lobules) that produce milk, and supporting adipose/connective tissue. Milk drains via lactiferous ducts to the nipple. Lactation is stimulated by prolactin and milk release by oxytocin.

üéØ Learning Outcomes

After studying this section, you should be able to:

  • describe the structure and function of the testes
  • outline the structure and function of the spermatic cords
  • describe the secretions that pass into the spermatic fluid
  • explain the process of ejaculation

The male reproductive system (Fig. 18.13) functions to produce, mature, and store spermatozoa and deliver them into the female reproductive tract.

Scrotum and Testes

The scrotum is a pouch of skin and muscle containing the testes. The testes are the male gonads, suspended by spermatic cords. They are surrounded by three layers (tunica vaginalis, albuginea, vasculosa) and contain seminiferous tubules where spermatogenesis occurs. Between the tubules are interstitial cells (of Leydig) that secrete testosterone.

Spermatozoa mature in the epididymis. Spermatogenesis requires a temperature slightly lower than body temperature ($~3^\circ\text{C}$ lower), facilitated by the scrotum's position.

Ducts and Glands

Sperm travel through the deferent duct (vas deferens), which joins the duct of the seminal vesicle to form the ejaculatory duct. This passes through the prostate gland to join the urethra.

Accessory Glands

  • Seminal vesicles: Secrete viscous, alkaline fluid (60% of semen) containing fructose to fuel sperm.
  • Prostate gland: Surrounds the urethra; secretes a thin, milky fluid (30% of semen) containing a clotting enzyme.

Urethra and Penis

The urethra carries both urine and semen. It passes through the penis, which contains three masses of erectile tissue: two corpora cavernosa and one corpus spongiosum. The tip is expanded into the glans penis.

Ejaculation: During male orgasm, sperm are expelled from the epididymis and propelled through the ducts by smooth muscle contraction. Secretions from the accessory glands are added to form semen.

Puberty

Occurs between ages 10 and 14. LH stimulates testosterone production, leading to secondary sexual characteristics: muscle/bone growth, voice deepening, hair growth, and enlargement of reproductive organs.

03

üë∂ Human Development

Fertilisation forms a zygote (46 chromosomes). It divides to form a blastocyst which implants in the uterus. The embryonic period lasts the first 8 weeks; thereafter it is a fetus. The placenta develops to supply oxygen and nutrients and remove wastes.

Differentiation of specialized tissues is largely completed in the first 12 weeks. The final months involve rapid growth.

ü¶† Infections of the Reproductive Tract

Sexually Transmitted Infections (STIs):

  • Chlamydia: Chlamydia trachomatis. Can cause pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) and infertility.
  • Gonorrhoea: Neisseria gonorrhoeae. Causes urethritis, can spread to other organs.
  • Syphilis: Treponema pallidum. Has primary (chancre), secondary, and tertiary stages.
  • Trichomonas vaginalis: Protozoan causing vulvovaginitis.
  • Genital Herpes: HSV1/HSV2. Painful ulcers, latent infection.

Candidiasis (Thrush): Caused by Candida albicans (yeast). Often follows antibiotic therapy which kills protective bacteria.

05

‚öïÔ∏è Diseases of Female Reproductive System

Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID): Upward spread of infection (often STIs) causing inflammation of uterus, tubes, ovaries. Can lead to infertility.

Disorders of the Uterus:

  • Cervical Carcinoma: Strongly associated with Human Papilloma Virus (HPV). Starts as dysplasia (CIN).
  • Endometriosis: Endometrial tissue growing outside the uterus.
  • Endometrial Carcinoma: Associated with high oestrogen.
  • Leiomyoma (Fibroid): Benign smooth muscle tumour. Very common.

Ovarian Disorders: Salpingitis (tube inflammation), Ectopic pregnancy, Ovarian tumours (benign/malignant).

Breast Disorders: Mastitis (inflammation), Fibroadenoma (benign), Breast Cancer (common malignancy, risk factors include age, genetics - BRCA genes, hormone exposure).

06

‚öïÔ∏è Diseases of Male Reproductive System

Infections: Balanitis (glans/prepuce), Urethritis (gonococcal/non-specific).

Testicular Disorders: Epididymitis, Orchitis (e.g. mumps), Undescended testis (cryptorchidism), Hydrocele (fluid accumulation), Testicular tumours (malignant, young men).

Prostate Disorders: Prostatitis, Benign Prostatic Enlargement (BPH - common in older men, causes obstruction), Malignant tumours (carcinoma).
