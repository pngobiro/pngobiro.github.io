1.
State the following terms as used in logistics management:
a. Lead time (2 marks)
b. FIFO (2 marks)
c. Buffer stock (2 marks)
d. Maximum stock level (2 marks)
e. Work ticket (2 marks)
Show Answer

Answers:

  • a. Lead time: The interval of time between placing an order (requisition) and the actual receipt of the goods or services.
  • b. FIFO (First In, First Out): An inventory management method where the items received first are used or issued first to prevent expiration or obsolescence.
  • c. Buffer stock (Safety stock): The minimum level of inventory held as a reserve to guard against uncertainties in demand or supply lead times (preventing stock-outs).
  • d. Maximum stock level: The upper limit of inventory that should not be exceeded to prevent overstocking, holding costs, and wastage (expiry).
  • e. Work ticket: An official document authorizing the use of a government or organizational vehicle, detailing the journey, driver, passengers, and mileage for accountability.

a. Explain the following terms as used in finance management:

  1. Cost sharing (2 marks)
  2. Waiver system (2 marks)

b. Highlight six (6) different groups of clients who are eligible for fee exemptions in our healthcare system (6 marks)

Show Answer

a. Definitions:

  1. Cost sharing: A financing mechanism where the cost of health services is shared between the government/provider and the user (patient), typically through user fees at the point of service.
  2. Waiver system: A mechanism to exempt patients who are financially unable to pay for health services (indigents) from paying user fees, ensuring equity in access.

b. Eligible groups for fee exemptions:

  1. Children under five years of age (U5).
  2. Pregnant women (Maternity services/Linda Mama).
  3. Prisoners/Remandees.
  4. Patients with specific chronic infectious diseases (TB, HIV/AIDS, Leprosy).
  5. Victims of sexual and gender-based violence (SGBV).
  6. The very poor/indigents (Social welfare cases).
  7. Mental health patients (in public psychiatric units).
3.

a. Explain five (5) sources/bases of power for a leader (5 marks)

b. State any five (5) qualities of a good leader according to Stogdill (5 marks)

Show Answer

a. Sources/Bases of Power (French & Raven):

  1. Legitimate Power: Power derived from one's formal position or title in the organization hierarchy (Authority).
  2. Reward Power: The ability to grant rewards (promotions, bonuses, praise) to influence behavior.
  3. Coercive Power: The ability to punish or withhold rewards (demotion, firing, criticism) to ensure compliance.
  4. Expert Power: Power based on special knowledge, skills, or expertise that others need.
  5. Referent Power: Power derived from the admiration, respect, and identification others have for the leader (Charisma).

b. Qualities of a good leader (Ralph Stogdill's Trait Theory):

  1. Intelligence/Intellect: Ability to understand complex issues.
  2. Alertness: Sensitivity to the social environment.
  3. Insight/Intuition: Ability to foresee outcomes.
  4. Responsibility: Willingness to assume duties and accountability.
  5. Initiative: Ability to start actions and innovate.
  6. Persistence: Determination to complete tasks despite obstacles.
  7. Self-confidence: Belief in one's own abilities.
  8. Sociability: Ability to interact well with others.

Douglas McGregor's Theory X Assumptions (Negative View):

  • The average employee inherently dislikes work and will avoid it if possible.
  • Employees must be coerced, controlled, directed, or threatened with punishment to achieve organizational goals.
  • Employees prefer to be directed, wish to avoid responsibility, have relatively little ambition, and want security above all.
  • Motivation occurs only at the physiological and safety levels.
  • Most people are resistant to change.

Douglas McGregor's Theory Y Assumptions (Positive View):

  • Physical and mental effort in work is as natural as play or rest.
  • External control and the threat of punishment are not the only means for bringing about effort toward objectives. People will exercise self-direction and self-control in the service of objectives to which they are committed.
  • Commitment to objectives is a function of the rewards associated with their achievement (especially ego satisfaction).
  • The average human being learns, under proper conditions, not only to accept but to seek responsibility.
  • The capacity to exercise a relatively high degree of imagination, ingenuity, and creativity in the solution of organizational problems is widely, not narrowly, distributed in the population.
5.
Explain any five (5) categories of leaves (10 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Annual Leave: Paid time off given to employees for rest and recreation (usually 30 days per year in Kenya public service).
  2. Maternity Leave: Paid leave granted to female employees typically around the time of childbirth (90 calendar days).
  3. Paternity Leave: Paid leave granted to male employees when their spouse gives birth (typically 10-14 days).
  4. Sick Leave: Leave granted when an employee is incapacitated by illness or injury. Requires medical certification.
  5. Compassionate Leave: Leave granted for personal reasons, such as the death of a close relative or serious family emergency.
  6. Study Leave: Leave granted for an employee to pursue further education or training, which may be paid or unpaid depending on policy.
  7. Terminal Leave: Leave taken immediately prior to retirement or resignation.
6.
Health Management Information Systems (HMIS) is an important aspect of the healthcare system.
a. State four functions of health management information systems (4 marks)
b. Explain three (3) measures that can be used to ensure the security of HMIS data and information (6 marks)
Show Answer

a. Functions of HMIS:

  1. Data Collection: Gathering raw data from service delivery points.
  2. Data Processing/Analysis: Converting raw data into meaningful information (indicators, trends).
  3. Data Storage and Retrieval: Keeping records safe and accessible for future use.
  4. Information Dissemination: Reporting findings to stakeholders for feedback.
  5. Decision Support: Providing evidence for planning, resource allocation, and policy making.

b. Measures to ensure security of HMIS data:

  1. Physical Security: Locking server rooms, filing cabinets, and record offices to restrict physical access to unauthorized persons.
  2. Access Control: Using usernames, strong passwords, and biometrics to ensure only authorized personnel can access digital systems. Assigning user privileges based on roles.
  3. Data Backups: Regularly backing up data (cloud or off-site storage) to prevent loss in case of system failure, fire, or theft.
  4. Antivirus and Firewalls: Installing security software to protect against malware, viruses, and cyber-attacks.
  5. Confidentiality Policies: Enforcing strict non-disclosure agreements and ethical guidelines for staff handling sensitive patient data.

40 Marks (Attempt any TWO)
1.
Explain the roles of the following entities in healthcare service provision in Kenya:
i. County Government (5 marks)
ii. Non-Governmental Organizations (5 marks)
iii. Faith Based Organizations (5 marks)
iv. Ministry of Health (5 marks)
Show Answer

i. County Government:

  • Responsible for service delivery at Level 1 (Community), Level 2 (Dispensary), Level 3 (Health Centre), and Level 4/5 (County Referral Hospitals).
  • Recruitment and management of health workforce within the county.
  • Procurement of medical supplies and equipment for county facilities.
  • Management of ambulance and emergency response services.
  • Implementation of public health programs (sanitation, disease surveillance).
  • Budgeting and financing of county health services.

ii. Non-Governmental Organizations (NGOs):

  • Complement government efforts by filling service gaps in underserved areas.
  • Provide funding and technical support for specific health programs (e.g., HIV/AIDS, Malaria, Maternal Health).
  • Advocacy and policy influence to improve health rights and standards.
  • Capacity building through training of health workers.
  • Provide humanitarian relief during emergencies and disasters.

iii. Faith Based Organizations (FBOs) e.g., Mission Hospitals:

  • Provide curative and preventive health services, often in rural and hard-to-reach areas where government presence is low.
  • Training of health professionals (many medical training colleges are FBO-owned).
  • Offer holistic care that addresses spiritual as well as physical needs.
  • Mobilize community resources and participate in primary health care.
  • Partner with the government (via NHIF/SHIF) to offer affordable care.

iv. Ministry of Health (National Government):

  • Policy formulation, standards, and guidelines regulation.
  • Management of National Referral Hospitals (Level 6) e.g., KNH, MTRH.
  • Capacity building and technical assistance to counties.
  • Disease surveillance and management of national epidemics/pandemics.
  • Training and regulation of health professionals (through regulatory councils).
  • Health research and national health information management.

a. Members of the Health Center Management Team (HCMT):

  2. Supervision: Overseeing the daily operations of the facility and supervising staff to ensure adherence to standards and protocols.
  3. Resource Management: Managing financial resources (AIEs, user fees), human resources, and infrastructure/equipment maintenance.
  4. Supply Chain Management: Quantification, ordering, and inventory management of essential medicines and medical supplies (commodities).
  5. Monitoring and Evaluation: Collecting, analyzing, and using health data to monitor performance indicators and improve service quality.
  6. Quality Assurance: Implementing Quality Improvement (QI) initiatives to ensure patient safety and satisfaction.
  7. Conflict Resolution: Managing disputes among staff or between staff and patients.
  8. Community Liaison: Linking the facility with the community through the Health Facility Committee (HFC) and Community Health Units.
  9. Staff Development: Identifying training needs and organizing Continuing Medical Education (CME) for staff.
3.
Decision making is an integral part of Health Service Management.
a. Define decision making (2 marks)
b. Outline the steps in decision making (14 marks)
c. Explain any two (2) types of decisions (4 marks)
Show Answer

a. Definition:

Decision making is the cognitive process of identifying and selecting a course of action from two or more possible alternatives to solve a specific problem or achieve a goal.

b. Steps in Decision Making:

  1. Identify and Define the Problem: Clearly understanding the issue that requires a decision (gap between current and desired state).
  2. Gather Information/Data Collection: Collecting relevant facts, data, and constraints related to the problem.
  3. Analyze the Problem: diagnosing the root causes and determining the scope of the issue.
  4. Develop Alternative Solutions: Brainstorming and generating multiple possible courses of action.
  5. Evaluate Alternatives: Assessing each option based on feasibility, cost, risks, and alignment with organizational goals (Pros and Cons).
  6. Select the Best Alternative: Choosing the optimal solution that maximizes benefits and minimizes costs.
  7. Implement the Decision: Putting the chosen solution into action (allocating resources, assigning tasks).
  8. Monitor and Evaluate (Follow-up): Assessing the results of the decision to ensure the problem is solved and making adjustments if necessary.

c. Types of Decisions:

  • Programmed (Routine) Decisions: Repetitive and well-structured decisions made using established rules, policies, or SOPs (e.g., ordering routine drug supplies).
  • Non-Programmed (Strategic) Decisions: Unique, novel, and unstructured decisions that require judgement, creativity, and intuition because there are no pre-set rules (e.g., handling a new disease outbreak or building a new wing).
  • Alternatively: Individual Decisions (made by one person) vs. Group Decisions (made collectively).
