Section A: Multiple Choice Questions (30 Marks)

1.
What percentage burn does a patient have who has suffered burns to one leg (circumferential), one arm (circumferential), and the anterior trunk?
a) 18%
b) 27%
c) 36%
d) 45%
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) 45%

Using the Rule of Nines: One Leg (18%) + One Arm (9%) + Anterior Trunk (Chest 9% + Abdomen 9% = 18%). Total = 18 + 9 + 18 = 45%.

2.
An 8-year-old boy... the burned area has weeping blisters and is very tender to the touch. What is the burn depth?
a) First degree
b) Second degree
c) Third degree
d) Fourth degree
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Second degree (Partial Thickness)

Blisters, weeping (moist), and extreme tenderness (pain) are classic signs of superficial partial-thickness burns.

3.
What is the fluid requirement of a 50-kg man with a circumferential second degree burn to his left and right lower limb, and a first degree burn to his left arm? What is the rate of initial fluid resuscitation?
a) 4.5 L over 8 hours, followed by 4.5 L over 16 hours
b) 4.5 L over 8 hours, followed by 6 L over 16 hours
c) 6 L over 8 hours, followed by 6 L over 16 hours
d) 6 L over 8 hours, followed by 9 L over 16 hours
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) 4.5 L over 8 hours, followed by 4.5 L over 16 hours

Calculation:
Usually, 1st degree burns are excluded.
TBSA (excluding arm) = Left Leg (18) + Right Leg (18) = 36%.
Parkland Formula = 4ml x 50kg x 36 = 7,200ml (3.6L in 1st 8 hrs).
However, looking at the options, if we include the arm (9%) as the question implies "burns to...", TBSA = 45%.
Formula = 4ml x 50kg x 45 = 9,000ml (9 Liters).
Half in first 8 hours = 4.5 Liters.
Remaining half in next 16 hours = 4.5 Liters.
This matches Option A exactly.

4.
An ideal antiseptic or disinfectant should have all except
a) Speedy action
b) Corrode metals
c) Stable
d) Safe and easy to use
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Corrode metals

An ideal disinfectant should be non-corrosive to instruments.

5.
When discussing possible surgical options with a patient, it is appropriate to do all of the following EXCEPT:
a) Document that the patient is capable of rendering informed consent
b) Ask the patient to identify a surrogate health care decision maker...
c) Avoid discussing the "pain and suffering" aspects of a treatment plan
d) Provide an opportunity for the patient to ask questions...
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Avoid discussing the "pain and suffering" aspects of a treatment plan

Informed consent requires a frank discussion of risks, including pain, recovery time, and suffering.

6.
Hypovolemic shock is not caused by;
a) Hemorrhage
b) Blood transfusion
c) Dehydration
d) Burn
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Blood transfusion

Blood transfusion is a treatment, not a cause. Hemorrhage, dehydration, and burns (plasma loss) all cause hypovolemia.

7.
Steroids play role in management of-
a) Neurogenic shock
b) Septic shock
c) Hemorrhagic shock
d) Cardiogenic shock
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Septic shock

Steroids (Hydrocortisone) are used in refractory septic shock. High-dose steroids were historically used in neurogenic (spinal) shock but are no longer routinely recommended. They have no role in hemorrhagic shock.

8.
In shock, the hydrostatic pressure ____ & oncotic pressure is constant:
a) Constant
b) No changes
c) Decreases
d) Increases
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Decreases

Shock (hypotension) leads to decreased capillary hydrostatic pressure, allowing fluid absorption from the interstitium (auto-transfusion).

9.
Which is most accurate about the presentation of snakebites?
a) Early and intense pain is rarely a true sign...
b) Viperid bites have a hemotoxic syndrome with systemic toxicity
c) Patients that present with signs of shock...
d) Elapid bites tend to be associated with profound local tissue damage
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Viperid bites have a hemotoxic syndrome with systemic toxicity

Vipers (e.g., Puff Adder) are hemotoxic/cytotoxic. Elapids (e.g., Mambas, Cobras) are primarily neurotoxic (except Spitting Cobras which cause tissue damage).

10.

The most common site for soft tissue sarcomas is the extremities (approx. 60%), specifically the thigh.

13.
What is the most common treatment modality for advanced cases of Madura foot?
a) Intravenous antibiotics alone
b) Topical antifungal creams
c) Surgical debridement or amputation, along with prolonged antifungal or antibacterial therapy

Correct Answer: c) Surgical debridement or amputation, along with prolonged antifungal or antibacterial therapy

Section B: Short Answer Questions (30 Marks)

1.
Briefly, outline the management of leprosy-related peripheral nerve damage and its implications for surgery. (6 Marks)
Show Answer
  • Medical Management: High dose Steroids (Prednisolone) to reduce inflammation (neuritis). Analgesics for pain.
  • Surgical Decompression: Indicated if nerve abscess forms or pain is intractable despite steroids.
  • Reconstructive Surgery: Tendon transfers to restore function in established palsies (e.g., foot drop, claw hand).
  • Protection: Education on protecting insensate limbs from trauma.
2.
State five common postoperative complications. (6 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Hemorrhage: Primary, reactionary, or secondary bleeding.
  2. Infection: Surgical Site Infection (SSI), pneumonia, UTI.
  3. Thromboembolism: Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT) and Pulmonary Embolism (PE).
  4. Respiratory Complications: Atelectasis, hypoxemia.
  5. Urinary Retention: Inability to void post-anesthesia.
  6. Wound Dehiscence: Separation of wound edges.
3.
Briefly, outline the classification of inhalational burn injuries.
Show Answer
  • Supraglottic Injury (Upper Airway): Thermal injury causing edema and obstruction (Stridor).
  • Infraglottic Injury (Lower Airway): Chemical injury from combustion products causing tracheobronchitis, edema, and ARDS.
  • Systemic Toxicity: Carbon Monoxide (CO) and Cyanide poisoning causing tissue hypoxia.
4.
List the general indications for referral of a burn patient to a burn unit.
Show Answer
  1. Partial thickness burns > 10% TBSA.
  2. Burns involving face, hands, feet, genitalia, perineum, or major joints.
  3. Third-degree (Full thickness) burns of any age.
  4. Electrical burns (including lightning).
  5. Chemical burns.
  6. Inhalation injury.
  7. Patients with significant comorbidities (Diabetes, Cardiac).
  8. Burn injury with concomitant trauma.
  9. Children (if local facility lacks pediatric expertise).
5.
Briefly, outline the management of a patient with a dog bite.
Show Answer
  • Wound Care: Extensive irrigation with saline/water. Debridement of devitalized tissue.
  • Closure: Generally left open (or loose approximation) to prevent infection, unless on the face (vascular).
  • Antibiotics: Prophylactic antibiotics (e.g., Co-amoxiclav) to cover Pasteurella, Strep, Staph, and Anaerobes.
  • Tetanus Prophylaxis: Booster if >5 years since last shot.
  • Rabies Prophylaxis: Assess risk. Give PEP (Vaccine days 0, 3, 7, 14, 28 +/- Immunoglobulin) if animal is suspected rabid.

Section C: Long Answer Questions (40 Marks)

1.
Discuss the factors that affect wound healing.
Show Answer

A. Local Factors:

  • Infection: Bacteria consume nutrients/oxygen and cause inflammation, delaying healing.
  • Blood Supply: Ischemia (e.g., PVD, pressure) prevents delivery of oxygen/nutrients.
  • Foreign Bodies: Sutures, glass, or dirt perpetuate inflammation.
  • Tissue Handling: Rough handling or crushing causes necrosis.
  • Wound Tension: Tight sutures cause ischemia.
  • Hematoma/Seroma: Provide culture medium for bacteria and separate wound edges.
  • Site: Face heals better than foot due to vascularity.

B. Systemic Factors:

  • Age: Healing slows with age.
  • Nutrition: Protein, Vitamin C, and Zinc deficiency impairs collagen synthesis.
  • Diabetes Mellitus: Causes microangiopathy, neuropathy, and immune dysfunction.
  • Medications: Steroids, Chemotherapy, and Anticoagulants impair inflammation and proliferation.
  • Smoking: Nicotine causes vasoconstriction; CO reduces oxygen delivery.
  • Jaundice/Uremia: Impair fibroblast function.
  • Anemia/Hypoxia: Reduces oxygen available for healing.
2.
Describe the two major types of Madura foot, clinical presentation, risk factors, diagnostic approach, and management.
Show Answer

Types:

  • Eumycetoma: Caused by true fungi (e.g., Madurella mycetomatis). Produces black or white grains. Slower progression.
  • Actinomycetoma: Caused by filamentous bacteria (e.g., Nocardia, Streptomyces, Actinomyces). Produces white, yellow, or red grains. Faster progression.

Clinical Presentation:

  • Triad: 1. Painless subcutaneous swelling (tumefaction). 2. Multiple draining sinuses. 3. Discharge containing grains (colonies).
  • Usually affects the foot (70%).

Risk Factors:

  • Walking barefoot (Thorn pricks inoculate the organism).
  • Agricultural work/Rural areas (Soil reservoir).
  • Male gender (20-40 years).

Diagnosis:

  • Microscopy/Macroscopy: Examination of grains (Color/Consistency).
  • Culture: Definitive identification (Sabouraud agar for fungi, Blood agar for bacteria).
  • Histology: Biopsy of the lesion.
  • Imaging: X-ray (bone destruction - "moth-eaten"), MRI ("Dot-in-circle" sign).

Management:

  • Actinomycetoma: Primarily Medical.
    Welsh Regimen: Cycles of Amikacin + Cotrimoxazole (or Dapsone/Streptomycin). High cure rate.
  • Eumycetoma: Combined Medical and Surgical.
    Medical: Antifungals (Itraconazole/Ketoconazole) for months.
    Surgical: Wide local excision or Amputation if bone involvement is extensive (as drugs penetrate poorly).
