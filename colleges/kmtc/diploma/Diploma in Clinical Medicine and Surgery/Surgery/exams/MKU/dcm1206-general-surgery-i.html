Section A: Multiple Choice Questions (20 Marks)

1.
Which of the following is not a common cause of ulcers?
a) Infection
b) Poor blood circulation
c) Excessive physical exercise
d) Prolonged pressure on the skin
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Excessive physical exercise

Infection (TB/Syphilis), Vascular issues (Varicose/Ischemia), and Pressure (Bed sores) are common causes.

2.
The primary feature of a contusion is:
a) A break in the skin
b) Swelling with no visible discoloration
c) Bruising due to blood vessel damage
d) Presence of an open wound
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Bruising due to blood vessel damage

A contusion is a bruise where capillaries rupture but the skin remains intact.

3.
What is the first line of treatment for a simple haematoma?
a) Antibiotics
b) Surgical drainage
c) Cold compress
d) Painkillers
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Cold compress

RICE therapy (Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation) is first line to limit bleeding.

4.
A common complication of hematomas is:
a) Infection
b) Increased mobility
c) Enhanced healing
d) Dehydration
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Infection

Pooled blood is an excellent culture medium for bacteria, leading to abscess formation.

5.
Which of the following best defines a wound?
a) A break in the skin caused by physical trauma
b) A lump under the skin
c) An infection of the skin
d) A muscle tear
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) A break in the skin caused by physical trauma

A wound is a disruption of the continuity of tissues.

6.
Which of these factors is least likely to influence wound healing?
a) Patient's age
b) Wound location
c) Type of food eaten
d) Wound size
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Type of food eaten

While general nutrition is critical, the specific "type of food" in a single meal is less directly influential compared to age, location (blood supply), and size of the wound.

7.
Ruptured tendons can primarily be treated by:
a) Rest and cold therapy
b) Medication
c) Surgical intervention
d) Steroid injections
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Surgical intervention

Tendons have poor blood supply and retract when ruptured; surgical repair is usually required for functional recovery.

8.
Which of the following is an example of a minor wound?
a) Gunshot wound
b) Deep laceration
c) Abrasion
d) Penetrating wound
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Abrasion

Abrasions (grazes) involve only the superficial epidermis.

9.
A typical method for diagnosing an ulcer involves:
a) Blood tests
b) Biopsy
c) X-ray
d) Physical examination and symptom review
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Physical examination and symptom review

Clinical examination of the edge, floor, and base is the primary diagnostic tool.

10.
The most common sign of a wound infection is:
a) Absence of pain
b) Increased body temperature
c) Bright red skin with no heat
d) Scab formation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Increased body temperature (Fever) / Local heat

Signs of inflammation (Heat, Redness, Swelling, Pain) and systemic fever indicate infection.

Section B: Short Answer Questions (30 Marks)

1.
Define an ulcer and give two common causes of ulcers.
Show Answer

Definition: An ulcer is a discontinuity of an epithelial surface (skin or mucous membrane) due to necrosis.

Causes:

  • Trauma (Mechanical, Chemical).
  • Vascular Insufficiency (Venous stasis, Arterial ischemia).
  • Infection (Tuberculosis, Syphilis).
  • Neuropathic (Diabetes, Leprosy).
  • Malignancy (SCC, BCC).
2.
Explain the process of diagnosing an ulcer.
Show Answer
  1. History: Duration, mode of onset, pain, associated diseases (diabetes).
  2. Physical Examination:
    • Inspect Edge, Floor (granulation tissue/slough), Base (induration), Discharge.
    • Examine regional lymph nodes and pulses.
  3. Investigations: Swab C&S, Biopsy (edge), X-ray (bone involvement), Doppler studies.
3.
Describe the clinical examination of ulcer edges.
Show Answer

The edge often indicates the etiology:

  • Sloping: Healing ulcer (venous).
  • Punched-out: Syphilitic (Gumma) or Trophic/Ischemic ulcer.
  • Undermined: Tuberculosis.
  • Raised and Everted: Malignant (Carcinoma).
  • Rolled: Basal Cell Carcinoma (Rodent ulcer).
4.
Define a contusion and a haematoma.
Show Answer
  • Contusion (Bruise): An injury where capillaries rupture under intact skin, causing extravasation of blood into tissues.
  • Haematoma: A localized collection of blood outside the blood vessels, often forming a palpable swelling or mass.
5.
List three clinical features of a hematoma.
Show Answer
  1. Localized swelling (Tumor).
  2. Pain and tenderness.
  3. Discoloration (Ecchymosis) over time.
  4. Fluctuation (if liquid).
6.
Describe the treatment approach for contusions.
Show Answer

RICE Protocol:

  • Rest: Immobilize the area.
  • Ice: Apply cold packs for 20 mins to constrict vessels.
  • Compression: Bandage to limit swelling.
  • Elevation: Raise limb above heart level to aid venous return.
  • Analgesia: Paracetamol/NSAIDs.
7.
Outline the complications that may arise from hematomas.
Show Answer
  • Infection: Forming an abscess.
  • Organization/Fibrosis: Leaving a hard lump.
  • Calcification: Myositis ossificans (in muscle).
  • Pressure effects: Compressing nerves or vessels (Compartment syndrome).
8.
Illustrate the classification of ulcers.
Show Answer
  • Clinical: Spreading, Healing, Callous (Chronic).
  • Pathological:
    • Non-specific: Traumatic, Vascular (Venous/Arterial).
    • Specific: Infective (TB, Syphilis), Malignant (SCC, BCC, Melanoma).
9.
Define a wound and classify wounds.
Show Answer

Definition: Disruption of the continuity of body structures caused by physical force.

Classification:

  • Closed: Contusion, Hematoma.
  • Open: Incised, Lacerated, Abrasion, Puncture, Penetrating, Crush.
  • Surgical: Clean, Clean-Contaminated, Contaminated, Dirty.
10.
List and describe two factors that influence wound healing.
Show Answer
  1. Infection: Bacterial burden prolongs the inflammatory phase and destroys tissue.
  2. Blood Supply: Oxygen is required for fibroblast activity; ischemia (e.g., tight sutures or vascular disease) delays healing.
  3. (Others: Nutrition, Diabetes, Age).

Section C: Essay Questions (50 Marks)

1.
Ulcers:
a) Define an ulcer and explain its common causes.
b) Discuss how an ulcer is diagnosed, including clinical examination of the ulcer edges.
c) Describe the different treatment options for ulcers, both medical and surgical.
d) Explain how complications can arise if an ulcer is left untreated.
Show Answer

a) Definition & Causes: See SAQ 1.

b) Diagnosis & Edges: See SAQ 2 & 3. Key is inspection of the edge to determine type (Malignant vs Healing vs Infective).

c) Treatment Options:

  • Medical: Treat underlying cause (Control Diabetes, antibiotics for infection). Wound Care (Debridement, dressings like Hydrocolloids/Honey/Saline). Compression bandaging for venous ulcers.
  • Surgical: Skin grafting (SSG) for large ulcers. Flap reconstruction. Revascularization (Bypass) for arterial ulcers. Vein stripping for venous ulcers. Amputation for gangrene.

d) Complications:

  • Infection (Cellulitis, Osteomyelitis, Sepsis).
  • Malignant change (Marjolin's ulcer - SCC in chronic scars).
  • Bleeding (erosion into vessels).
  • Deformity/Contracture.

2.
Contusions and Haematomas:
a) Define contusion and haematoma, and differentiate between the two.
b) Discuss the clinical features of both contusions and haematomas.
c) Explain the complications that can arise from haematomas and how these complications are managed.
d) Describe the treatment process for ruptured muscles and tendons.
Show Answer

a) Definitions: See SAQ 4. Diff: Contusion is diffuse infiltration; Haematoma is a distinct collection/pool of blood.

b) Features:

  • Contusion: Pain, swelling, discoloration (blue->green->yellow).
  • Haematoma: Palpable lump, fluctuation, localized pressure effects.

c) Complications & Management:

  • Infection -> Incision and Drainage + Antibiotics.
  • Calcification -> Physiotherapy, Surgical excision if limiting movement.
  • Pressure -> Aspiration or Evacuation.

d) Ruptured Muscles/Tendons:

  • Partial: RICE, Immobilization (splint/cast), Physiotherapy.
  • Complete: Surgical Repair (Tenorrhaphy/Myorrhaphy) to restore continuity and function, followed by rehabilitation.

3.
Wounds and Wound Healing:
a) Define a wound and classify the types of wounds.
b) Describe the factors that influence wound healing, focusing on the patient's health and wound care.
c) Discuss the complications of wound healing, specifying the time periods associated with each complication.
d) Explain how wound infections are managed, including preventive strategies.
Show Answer

a) Def & Class: See SAQ 9.

b) Factors:

  • Systemic: Age, Nutrition (Protein/Vit C), Anemia, Diabetes, Smoking, Steroids.
  • Local: Infection, Blood supply, Hematoma, Foreign body, Tension at edges.

c) Complications:

  • Immediate (<24hrs): Hemorrhage.
  • Early (days): Infection (Day 3-5), Dehiscence (Day 7-10).
  • Late (weeks/months): Hypertrophic scar, Keloid, Contracture, Marjolin's ulcer.

d) Infection Mgmt:

  • Prevention: Aseptic technique, Prophylactic antibiotics, Debridement of dead tissue.
  • Management: Remove sutures if pus present (drainage), Wound swab for C&S, Systemic antibiotics, Regular dressing changes.
