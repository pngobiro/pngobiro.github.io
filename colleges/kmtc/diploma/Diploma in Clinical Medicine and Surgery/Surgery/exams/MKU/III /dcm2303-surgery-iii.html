Section A: Multiple Choice Questions (30 Marks)

1.
The presence of a tension pneumothorax;
A. Presents with tracheal deviation
B. Is treated definitively by needle decompression
C. Wil present with dullness on percussion
D. All the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Presents with tracheal deviation

Tension pneumothorax causes tracheal deviation away from the affected side. It is hyper-resonant (not dull) and needle decompression is temporary (definitive treatment is a chest tube).

2.
A massive hemothorax is defined as;
A. An initial drainage of ≥ 1000 ml of blood
B. An initial drainage of ≥ 1500 ml of blood
C. Hemothorax presenting with shock
D. None of the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. An initial drainage of ≥ 1500 ml of blood

Or more than 1/3 of the patient's blood volume.

3.
An open pneumothorax;
A. Requires immediate needle decompression
B. Presents with dullness on the affected side
C. Requires an occlusive dressing taped on 3 sides
D. Requires an occlusive dressing taped on all sides
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Requires an occlusive dressing taped on 3 sides

Taping on 3 sides creates a flutter valve effect, allowing air out but not in.

4.
Which one is a surgical airway?
A. Naso-pharyngeal airway
B. Laryngeal mas airway
C. Esophageal-tracheal tube
D. Endotracheal tube
Show Answer

Correct Answer: None of the above options are strictly "surgical".

However, typically Cricothyroidotomy or Tracheostomy are surgical airways. In the context of options provided, Endotracheal Intubation (D) is the definitive airway, but not surgical. This might be a trick question or typo. Standard surgical airways are not listed.

5.
Which is done when assessing the airway in primary survey
A. Inspecting for patency
B. Counting number of breaths
C. Checking for use of accessory muscles
D. All the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. All the above

Airway assessment involves looking, listening, and feeling for signs of obstruction and effort.

6.
Which aspect of pulse changes first when there is considerable hemorrhage
A. Volume
B. Character
C. Rhythm
D. Rate
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Rate

Tachycardia (increased rate) is usually the earliest physiological response to blood loss (Class II shock).

7.
Your patient was impaled on a fence and presents with crackling sound on touching the chest wall. The medical term for this is;
A. Bony crepitus
B. Rice crispies
C. Subcutaneous emphysema
D. Omphalocele
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Subcutaneous emphysema

Air tracking under the skin produces a crackling sensation (crepitus) known as surgical or subcutaneous emphysema.

8.
When the steering wheel strikes a driver with rapid deceleration, which injury causes immediate death?
A. Ruptured myocardium
B. Tension pneumothorax
C. Hemopneumothorax
D. Aortic shearing
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Aortic shearing

Traumatic aortic rupture (shearing at the ligamentum arteriosum) is responsible for the majority of immediate deaths in such deceleration injuries.

9.
A 27-year-old male who was ejected from his motorcycle... complaining of sharp, stabbing chest pain that worsens with deep breaths. On examination, you notice subcutaneous emphysema in the neck region. You suspect:
A. Fractured sternum
B. Traumatic asphyxia
C. Myocardial contusion
D. Blunt cardiac injury
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Fractured sternum (or Pneumothorax/Bronchial injury not listed)

Subcutaneous emphysema in the neck suggests air leak from the respiratory tract (pneumothorax or tracheobronchial injury). Among the options, Sternal fracture is a high-energy injury often associated with these, but is not the direct cause of emphysema. However, this clinical vignette is classic for a major thoracic injury involving an air leak.

10.
Cushing's triad is diagnostic of;
A. Blunt cardiac injury
B. Cardiac tamponade
C. Ruptured esophagus
D. Aortic rupture
Show Answer

Correct Answer: None (Strictly) / B. Cardiac tamponade (Likely intended as Beck's Triad)

Cushing's Triad (Hypertension, Bradycardia, Irregular Resp) is for Raised Intracranial Pressure. Beck's Triad (Hypotension, Muffled heart sounds, JVD) is for Cardiac Tamponade. In the context of chest trauma questions, option B is the most likely intended answer due to common confusion.

11.
A patient presents to the ED with a pulse pressure of 70 mmHg, a pulse rate of 42beats/min and irregular respirations after he was kicked in the chest... What is your spot diagnosis?
A. Emphysema
B. Rib fractures
C. Cardiac tamponade
D. Head injury
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Head injury

The patient presents with Cushing's Triad: Bradycardia (42 bpm), Irregular respirations, and widened pulse pressure (implied). This indicates raised ICP/Head Injury, even if the history mentions a kick to the chest (distractor or multi-system trauma).

16.
A 64-year-old presents to your facility with history of knee pain and no history of trauma. Joint aspirate shows pus cells, negatively birefringent crystals, and no organisms. What is your diagnosis?
A. Septic arthritis
B. Reactive arthritis
C. Pseudogout
D. Gout
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Gout

Negatively birefringent needle-shaped crystals are pathognomonic for Uric Acid crystals (Gout). Pseudogout has positively birefringent rhomboid crystals.

21.
Jacod's triad in cavernous sinus thrombosis includes;
A. Ophthalmoplegia, trigeminal neuralgia, unilateral amaurosis
B. Ophthalmoplegia, facia nerve palsy. Trigeminal neuralgia
C. Ophthalmoplegia, bilateral amaurosis, blindness
D. Ophthalmoplegia, unilateral amaurosis, blindness
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Ophthalmoplegia, trigeminal neuralgia, unilateral amaurosis

Cavernous sinus thrombosis affects CN III, IV, VI (Ophthalmoplegia), V1/V2 (Trigeminal neuralgia), and can affect the optic nerve (Amaurosis).

24.
A patient presents to your facility with swelling around one eye, severe headache and drooping eyelids. He has no history of trauma but reports he had a dental abscess about a week prior to the current symptoms. What is your impression?
A. Head injury
B. Dental carries
C. Insect sting
D. Cavernous sinus thrombosis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Cavernous sinus thrombosis

Infection from the "danger triangle" of the face (e.g., dental abscess) can spread to the cavernous sinus via valveless veins, causing CST.

Section B: Short Answer Questions (30 Marks)

1.
Describe the positive signs that can be seen on x-ray diagnostic of osteomyelitis.
Show Answer
  • Soft tissue swelling: Earliest sign (first few days).
  • Periosteal reaction: Elevation of periosteum (periostitis) or new bone formation (involucrum).
  • Osteopenia/Rarefaction: Loss of bone density (requires >30% bone loss to be visible).
  • Sequestrum: Dense segment of dead bone separated from living bone.
  • Involucrum: Sheath of new bone surrounding the sequestrum.
  • Cloaca: Opening in the involucrum allowing pus discharge.
2.
With signs and symptoms of each stage, classify hypovolemic shock (6 Marks)
Show Answer
  • Class I (<15% loss): Pulse <100, BP Normal, RR 14-20, Urine >30ml/hr, Anxiety.
  • Class II (15-30% loss): Pulse >100, BP Normal/Decreased Pulse Pressure, RR 20-30, Urine 20-30ml/hr, Agitated.
  • Class III (30-40% loss): Pulse >120, BP Decreased, RR 30-40, Urine 5-15ml/hr, Confused. (Requires Blood).
  • Class IV (>40% loss): Pulse >140, BP Decreased, RR >35, Urine Negligible, Lethargic/Coma. (Life-threatening).
3.
With succinct differences in each, give the types of pneumothorax
Show Answer
  • Simple Pneumothorax: Air in pleural space without mediastinal shift or hemodynamic instability.
  • Tension Pneumothorax: One-way valve effect causing air trapping, mediastinal shift, venous return compression, and shock. Medical emergency.
  • Open Pneumothorax (Sucking Chest Wound): Chest wall defect >2/3 tracheal diameter. Air enters through chest wall, equilibrating pressures and collapsing lung.

Section C: Long Answer Questions (40 Marks)

1.
A patient presents to the ED after a road traffic accident with complaints of chest pain and respiratory distress. Discuss in details how you will go about primary survey.
Show Answer

The primary survey follows the ABCDE algorithm to identify and treat life-threatening conditions:

  • A - Airway & C-Spine Control:
    • Assess patency (talking?). Check for obstruction (blood, teeth).
    • Maintain C-spine immobilization.
    • Intervention: Suction, OPA/NPA, or Definitive airway (Intubation) if GCS<8 or airway threatened.
  • B - Breathing & Ventilation:
    • Inspect: RR, unequal expansion, bruising, open wounds.
    • Palpate: Tracheal deviation, subcutaneous emphysema, flail segments.
    • Percuss: Hyper-resonance (Pneumothorax) vs Dullness (Hemothorax).
    • Auscultate: Air entry.
    • Life-Threats to Rule Out: Tension Pneumothorax (Needle decomp), Open Pneumothorax (3-sided dressing), Massive Hemothorax, Flail Chest.
    • Give high-flow Oxygen.
  • C - Circulation & Hemorrhage Control:
    • Assess Pulse, BP, Capillary refill, Skin color/temp.
    • Identify sources of bleeding (External vs Internal).
    • Establish 2 large-bore IV lines. Start fluids (Crystalloids/Blood).
    • Consider Cardiac Tamponade (Beck's Triad).
  • D - Disability: Assess GCS and Pupils.
  • E - Exposure: Fully undress to inspect, prevent hypothermia.
2.
With reference to the pathophysiology of cavernous sinus thrombosis, discuss its possible complications. (20 Marks)
Show Answer

Pathophysiology:

  • Septic thrombosis of the cavernous sinus usually results from spread of infection from the face (danger triangle), nose, or teeth via valveless emissary veins.
  • The cavernous sinus contains CN III, IV, VI, V1, V2, and the Internal Carotid Artery.
  • Thrombosis causes venous congestion and compression of these structures.

Complications:

  • Cranial Nerve Palsies: Ophthalmoplegia (III, IV, VI), facial sensory loss (V).
  • Visual Loss: Due to ophthalmic vein congestion or arterial compression (blindness).
  • Meningitis/Brain Abscess: Spread of infection to meninges/brain parenchyma.
  • Septic Emboli: Pulmonary embolism.
  • Pituitary Necrosis: Spread to pituitary gland.
  • Stroke: Involvement of Internal Carotid Artery.
  • Death: High mortality if untreated.
