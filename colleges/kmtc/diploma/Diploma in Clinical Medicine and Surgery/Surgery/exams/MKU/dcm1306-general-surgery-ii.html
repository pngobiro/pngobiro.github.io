Section A: Multiple Choice Questions (30 Marks)

1.
Martin, a clinician, was clerking X when he discovered that his pt had a history of smoking 5 cigarettes for a period of ten years. On further probing, Martin realizes that each box of cigarettes has 20 cigarettes. What is the smoking pack years for this patient?
a) 5 years
b) 2.5 years
c) 10 years
d) None of the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) 2.5 years

Formula: (Cigarettes per day / 20) x Years smoked.
(5 / 20) x 10 = 0.25 x 10 = 2.5 pack years.

Section B: Short Answer Questions (30 Marks)

1.
List five complications of blood transfusion in haemorrhage. (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Acute Hemolytic Transfusion Reaction: ABO incompatibility.
  2. Transfusion Associated Circulatory Overload (TACO): Fluid overload.
  3. Transfusion Related Acute Lung Injury (TRALI).
  4. Infection Transmission: HIV, Hepatitis B/C.
  5. Electrolyte Imbalance: Hyperkalemia (from stored blood), Hypocalcemia (Citrate toxicity).
  6. Hypothermia: If cold blood is infused rapidly.
2.
Explain the complications of burns.
Show Answer
  • Immediate: Hypovolemic shock, Inhalation injury (Asphyxia), CO poisoning.
  • Early: Infection/Sepsis (commonest cause of death), Curling's ulcer, Acute Kidney Injury (Rhabdomyolysis).
  • Late: Contractures, Hypertrophic scarring/Keloids, Marjolin's ulcer (SCC in scar).
3.
State five admission/referral criteria for burns.
Show Answer
  1. Partial thickness burns > 10% TBSA.
  2. Burns involving face, hands, feet, genitalia, or major joints.
  3. Full thickness (3rd degree) burns of any size.
  4. Electrical burns (including lightning).
  5. Chemical burns.
  6. Inhalation injury.
  7. Burn injury in patients with pre-existing medical disorders complicating management.
  8. Patients with concomitant trauma.
4.
Explain the Jacksonian theory of zones of tissue injury. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Describes the three concentric zones of thermal injury:

  1. Zone of Coagulation (Central): Point of maximum damage. Irreversible tissue necrosis/coagulation.
  2. Zone of Stasis (Middle): Decreased tissue perfusion. Tissue is viable but at risk of necrosis if not resuscitated (Ischemic penumbra).
  3. Zone of Hyperemia (Outer): Increased tissue perfusion. Tissue will likely recover unless complications like severe sepsis or prolonged hypoperfusion occur.
5.
List the cardinal signs of inflammation.
Show Answer
  1. Rubor: Redness (vasodilation).
  2. Calor: Heat (increased blood flow).
  3. Dolor: Pain (mediators/pressure).
  4. Tumor: Swelling (edema).
  5. Functio Laesa: Loss of function.
6.
Explain five vital signs, stating the normal values that are important in history taking and examination.
Show Answer
  1. Body Temperature: 36.5°C - 37.5°C.
  2. Pulse Rate (Heart Rate): 60 - 100 beats per minute.
  3. Respiratory Rate: 12 - 20 breaths per minute.
  4. Blood Pressure: 90/60 mmHg to 120/80 mmHg.
  5. Oxygen Saturation (SpO2): 95% - 100% on room air.

Section C: Long Answer Questions (40 Marks)

1.
Haemorrhage:
a) Define haemorrhage.
b) Classify haemorrhage.
c) List the etiology of haemorrhage.
d) List four clinical features of haemorrhage.
Show Answer

a) Definition: Extravasation or escape of blood from the vascular system (arteries, veins, capillaries) due to vessel wall breach.

b) Classification:

  • By Time: Primary (immediate), Reactionary (within 24 hrs - slippage of ligature/clot dislodgement), Secondary (7-14 days - infection).
  • By Vessel: Arterial (bright red, pulsatile), Venous (dark red, steady flow), Capillary (oozing).
  • By Location: Internal (concealed), External (revealed).
  • By Severity (ATLS): Class I (<15%), Class II (15-30%), Class III (30-40%), Class IV (>40%).

c) Etiology:

  • Trauma: Penetrating (knife, gunshot) or Blunt injury.
  • Surgical: Intraoperative vessel injury.
  • Medical/Pathological: Coagulopathies (Hemophilia), Aneurysm rupture, Peptic ulcer, Malignancy erosion, Vitamin K deficiency.

d) Clinical Features:

  • Tachycardia (Pulse > 100).
  • Hypotension (Low BP).
  • Pallor (Pale skin/conjunctiva).
  • Cold, clammy skin.
  • Thirst and Dry mucous membranes.
  • Confusion/Restlessness (cerebral hypoperfusion).
2.
Burns:
a) Define a burn.
b) List the etiology of burns.
c) Classify burns according to depth of injury.
d) List three signs/symptoms indicating inhalational injury.
e) Discuss the first aid and the primary survey (initial management) of burns.
Show Answer

a) Definition: Tissue injury caused by thermal (heat), chemical, electrical, or radiation energy leading to protein denaturation and cell death.

b) Etiology:

  • Thermal: Dry heat (fire), Moist heat (scalds).
  • Chemical: Acids, Alkalis.
  • Electrical: High voltage, Lightning.
  • Radiation: UV light, X-rays.

c) Classification (Depth):

  • 1st Degree (Superficial): Epidermis only. Erythema, pain, no blisters. (e.g., Sunburn).
  • 2nd Degree (Partial Thickness):
    • Superficial: Papillary dermis. Blisters, wet, painful.
    • Deep: Reticular dermis. Dry, less painful/dull.
  • 3rd Degree (Full Thickness): Entire dermis destroyed. Leathery, white/black (eschar), painless (nerves destroyed).
  • 4th Degree: Involves muscle, tendon, bone.

d) Signs of Inhalational Injury:

  • Soot in nostrils/sputum.
  • Singed nasal hairs/eyebrows.
  • Hoarseness of voice / Stridor.
  • Burns to face/neck.

e) First Aid & Primary Survey:

  • First Aid: Stop the burning process (Stop, Drop, Roll). Cool the burn with running water (10-20 mins). Remove constricting items (jewelry). Cover with clean cling film/cloth. Do NOT apply ice or butter.
  • Primary Survey (ABCDE):
    • Airway: Assess patency. Check for inhalation injury. Intubate early if indicated (stridor, massive facial burns). C-spine control if trauma.
    • Breathing: Administer 100% O2. Check for circumferential chest burns (escharotomy needed?).
    • Circulation: IV Access (2 large bore). Start fluids (Parkland Formula) if >15-20% TBSA. Assess peripheral pulses.
    • Disability: GCS, Pupil response.
    • Exposure: Fully undress to assess extent (Wallace Rule of 9s). Prevent hypothermia (warm room).
