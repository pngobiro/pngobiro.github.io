Section A: Short Essay Questions

60 Marks
1.

Discuss the following on Osteogenic sarcoma.

a) List down Four clinical features. (4 marks)

b) Outline the expected X-ray findings. (6 marks)

Show Answer

a) Clinical Features:

  1. Pain: Persistent, deep bone pain, often worse at night.
  2. Swelling/Mass: A palpable, firm, tender mass at the site (commonly distal femur or proximal tibia).
  3. Pathological Fracture: Bone breakage with minimal trauma due to tumor weakening the bone.
  4. Limitation of movement: If the tumor is near a joint.
  5. Systemic signs: Weight loss, anemia, or fever (in advanced cases).

b) X-ray Findings:

  • Sunburst Appearance: Spicules of new bone radiating outwards from the cortex (periosteal reaction).
  • Codman's Triangle: Triangular elevation of the periosteum at the margin of the tumor.
  • Bone Destruction: Areas of osteolysis (radiolucency) mixed with osteosclerosis (density) - "Moth-eaten" appearance.
  • Soft Tissue Extension: Visible mass extending beyond the bone into surrounding tissue.
2.

Thyroid swellings are a common condition in surgical out-patient clinics:

a) Enumerate Five causes of thyroid enlargement. (5 marks)

b) State five indications of thyroidectomy. (5 marks)

Show Answer

a) Causes of Thyroid Enlargement (Goiter):

  1. Iodine Deficiency: Simple/Endemic Goiter.
  2. Autoimmune Disease: Graves' Disease (Toxic diffuse goiter) or Hashimoto's Thyroiditis.
  3. Neoplasms: Thyroid Carcinoma (Papillary, Follicular, Medullary, Anaplastic).
  4. Physiological: Puberty or Pregnancy.
  5. Multinodular Goiter: Multiple benign nodules.
  6. Drugs/Goitrogens: Lithium, Amiodarone, Cassava.

b) Indications for Thyroidectomy:

  1. Malignancy: Confirmed or suspected thyroid cancer.
  2. Compression Symptoms: Dysphagia (difficulty swallowing), Dyspnea (difficulty breathing), or hoarseness.
  3. Cosmetic Reasons: Large, unsightly goiter causing psychological distress.
  4. Thyrotoxicosis: Failure of medical therapy or patient preference (Toxic multinodular goiter).
  5. Retrosternal Goiter: Extension into the chest cavity.
3.

An 18 year old male patient weighing 50kg presents with history burns from spillage of boiling water. Examination the burns are estimated to be 2nd degree, 30% burns.

a) Using Parkland's formula, calculate the required amount of fluids and describe how it should be administered. (6 marks)

b) Enumerate Four early complications of burns. (4 marks)

Show Answer

a) Fluid Calculation (Parkland Formula):

$\text{Total Fluid (24 hrs)} = 4 \text{ ml} \times \text{Body Weight (kg)} \times \text{Percentage Burn area (TBSA)}$

$4 \text{ ml} \times 50 \text{ kg} \times 30 = \mathbf{6,000 \text{ ml}}$

Fluid Type: Ringer's Lactate (Hartmann's Solution).

Administration:

  • First 8 hours: Give 50% of total volume = 3,000 ml.
  • Next 16 hours: Give remaining 50% = 3,000 ml.
  • Note: The timing starts from the time of injury, not time of admission.

b) Early Complications:

  1. Hypovolemic Shock: Due to massive fluid loss/shift.
  2. Respiratory Failure/Inhalation Injury: Laryngeal edema or carbon monoxide poisoning (less likely in scalds but possible in fire).
  3. Acute Renal Failure: Due to hypoperfusion or rhabdomyolysis.
  4. Infection/Sepsis: Loss of skin barrier.
  5. Electrolyte Imbalance: Hyperkalemia (early) followed by hypokalemia.
  6. Compartment Syndrome: In circumferential burns (eschar effect).
4.

Posterior hip dislocation is commonly caused by road traffic accidents.

a) List down Four clinical features of posterior hip dislocation. (4 marks)

b) Describe the management of posterior hip dislocation. (6 marks)

Show Answer

a) Clinical Features:

  1. Pain: Severe pain in the hip/groin.
  2. Shortening: The affected leg appears shorter than the other.
  3. Adduction: The leg is crossed towards the midline.
  4. Internal Rotation: The foot/knee points inwards.
  5. Palpable Head: Femoral head may be palpable in the gluteal region.

b) Management:

Note: This is an orthopedic emergency to prevent Avascular Necrosis (AVN).

  • Initial Stabilization: ABCs (ATLS protocol), analgesia.
  • Imaging: X-ray AP pelvis to confirm dislocation and rule out fractures.
  • Reduction: Closed reduction under general anesthesia or conscious sedation.
    • Allis Maneuver: Patient supine, knee flexed 90°, hip flexed 90°. Traction applied vertically upwards while stabilizing pelvis.
    • Bigelow Maneuver: Traction with circumduction.
  • Post-Reduction: Check stability and neurovascular status (Sciatic nerve). Confirm with X-ray.
  • Traction: Skin or Skeletal traction for 3-6 weeks to allow capsule healing.
  • Rehabilitation: Gradual mobilization.
5.
Describe the Glasgow coma scale used in assessment of severity of head injury. (10 marks)
Show Answer

The GCS assesses conscious level based on three responses. Total Score: 15, Minimum: 3.

1. Eye Opening (E) - Max 4:

  • 4: Spontaneous
  • 3: To speech/voice
  • 2: To pain
  • 1: None

2. Verbal Response (V) - Max 5:

  • 5: Oriented (Time, Place, Person)
  • 4: Confused conversation
  • 3: Inappropriate words
  • 2: Incomprehensible sounds (moaning)
  • 1: None

3. Motor Response (M) - Max 6:

  • 6: Obeys commands
  • 5: Localizes pain
  • 4: Withdraws from pain (flexion)
  • 3: Abnormal flexion (Decorticate posturing)
  • 2: Extension (Decerebrate posturing)
  • 1: None

Interpretation: Severe Head Injury ≤ 8; Moderate 9-12; Mild 13-15.

6.
Explain the procedure of catheterizing a male patient with acute urinary retention. (10 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Preparation: Explain procedure, obtain consent, gather equipment (Foley catheter, urobag, sterile gloves, lignocaine gel, water for injection, antiseptic, drapes), and ensure privacy.
  2. Positioning: Patient supine with legs slightly apart.
  3. Asepsis: Wash hands, wear sterile gloves. Clean the glans penis with antiseptic (e.g., iodine/chlorhexidine) moving from meatus outwards in circular motion. Drape the area.
  4. Anaesthesia/Lubrication: Retract foreskin (if uncircumcised). Inject sterile Lignocaine gel (10-15ml) into the urethra. Hold the meatus closed for 3-5 minutes to allow effect.
  5. Insertion: Hold penis perpendicular to body (90°) to straighten penile urethra. Gently insert catheter tip into meatus. Advance steadily.
  6. Navigation: When resistance is felt (external sphincter/prostate), lower penis to 45° or parallel to legs and ask patient to take deep breaths/cough. Advance until urine flows and up to the "Y" junction.
  7. Retention: Inflate balloon with 10ml sterile water. Gently pull back until resistance is felt (balloon seating at bladder neck).
  8. Final Steps: Connect to urobag. Reposition foreskin (crucial to prevent paraphimosis). Secure catheter to thigh. Document volume/color of urine.

Section B: Long Essay Questions

40 Marks

Instructions: Select ANY TWO questions from this section.

1.

A 70-year-old man was brought to outpatient department complaining of itching of skin, urinating brownish dark urine and yellowish eyes which has been progressive over the last two weeks. On examining the man, you find scratch marks over his whole skin and a deeply yellowish sclera. The abdomen is unremarkable.

a) What is your diagnosis? (2 mks)

b) List FOUR possible causes of this condition. (8 mks)

c) Outline the diagnostic investigative modalities in this patient. (10 mks)

Show Answer

a) Diagnosis:

Obstructive Jaundice (Likely malignant etiology given age and progressive nature, e.g., Carcinoma of the Head of Pancreas).
Key features: Progressive jaundice, pruritus (itching), dark urine (bilirubinuria), age > 60.

b) Possible Causes:

  1. Carcinoma of the Head of the Pancreas: Commonest malignant cause in elderly.
  2. Cholangiocarcinoma: Tumor of the bile ducts.
  3. Choledocholithiasis: Stone in the Common Bile Duct (CBD) - usually painful but can be painless.
  4. Peri-ampullary Carcinoma: Tumor at the Ampulla of Vater.
  5. Mirizzi Syndrome: Stone in cystic duct compressing CBD.

c) Diagnostic Investigative Modalities:

  • Laboratory:
    • Liver Function Tests (LFTs): Elevated Direct Bilirubin, markedly elevated Alkaline Phosphatase (ALP) and GGT (Obstructive pattern).
    • Urinalysis: Positive for Bilirubin, negative for Urobilinogen.
    • Clotting Screen (PT/INR): Prolonged due to Vitamin K malabsorption.
    • Tumor Markers: CA 19-9 (Pancreatic cancer), CEA.
  • Imaging:
    • Abdominal Ultrasound: First line. Shows dilated biliary tree (intra/extra-hepatic ducts) and possibly the mass or stones.
    • CT Scan Abdomen (Contrast): To stage tumor, visualize pancreas head, and check for metastasis.
    • MRCP (Magnetic Resonance Cholangiopancreatography): Non-invasive visualization of biliary tree.
    • ERCP (Endoscopic Retrograde Cholangiopancreatography): Both diagnostic and therapeutic (stenting).
2.

A female patient in her early thirties was brought to your casualty department semiconscious. The relatives said she had vomited blood twice at night. On examination, you find that she is very pale and her pulse is thready and blood pressure is not recordable.

a) What is your diagnosis? (2 mks)

b) List the possible causes of this condition. (8 mks)

c) What is your approach to the management of this patient? (10 mks)

Show Answer

a) Diagnosis:

Hypovolemic Shock secondary to Upper Gastrointestinal Bleeding (Hematemesis).

b) Possible Causes of Upper GI Bleeding:

  1. Peptic Ulcer Disease (PUD): Bleeding duodenal or gastric ulcer (Commonest).
  2. Esophageal Varices: Secondary to Portal Hypertension (Liver Cirrhosis).
  3. Mallory-Weiss Tear: Tear at GE junction due to forceful vomiting.
  4. Gastritis/Erosions: NSAID induced or alcohol related.
  5. Gastric Carcinoma: Malignancy ulcerating.

c) Management Approach:

Immediate Resuscitation (ABC):

  • Airway/Breathing: Ensure patent airway (risk of aspiration). Administer high flow Oxygen. Place in recovery position.
  • Circulation (Priority):
    - Insert TWO wide-bore IV cannulae (14G or 16G).
    - Take blood for FBC, Cross-match (4-6 units), Urea/Electrolytes, Coagulation profile.
    - Rapid infusion of Crystalloids (Ringer's/Normal Saline) 2L fast.
    - Transfuse Whole Blood/Packed Red Cells ASAP (O-negative if unmatched in dire emergency).
  • Monitoring: Catheterize to monitor urine output (organ perfusion). Continuous cardiac monitoring.
  • Medical Therapy:
    - IV Proton Pump Inhibitor (Omeprazole) 80mg bolus then infusion.
    - IV Octreotide/Vasopressin (if varices suspected).
    - Vitamin K (if liver disease).
  • Definitive Treatment:
    - Upper GI Endoscopy (OGD): Diagnostic and Therapeutic (Injection sclerotherapy, banding, clipping) once stabilized.
    - Sengstaken-Blakemore tube if massive variceal bleed uncontrolled.
    - Surgery: If endoscopic control fails.
3.

A 10-year-old boy is brought to the hospital with a 4 days' history of pain and swelling of left knee joint. On examination, he is sick looking with a temperature of 39 degrees Celsius. The joint is swollen, hot, fluctuant and very tender.

a) What is the most likely diagnosis? (2 mks)

b) Outline three investigative modalities for this case and expected findings. (6 mks)

c) List down two differential diagnoses in this case. (2 mks)

d) Describe the treatment of this condition. (10 mks)

Show Answer

a) Diagnosis:

Acute Septic Arthritis (Left Knee).

b) Investigations:

  1. Joint Aspiration (Synovial Fluid Analysis): Findings: Turbid/purulent fluid, High WBC count (>50,000), Positive Gram stain/Culture (Staph aureus is commonest).
  2. Full Hemogram (CBC): Findings: Leukocytosis (High WBC), Neutrophilia.
  3. ESR and CRP: Findings: Markedly elevated (Inflammatory markers).
  4. X-ray Knee: Findings: Soft tissue swelling, widened joint space (early). Bone destruction (late).

c) Differential Diagnoses:

  • Acute Osteomyelitis (of distal femur/proximal tibia).
  • Juvenile Rheumatoid Arthritis (JRA).
  • Traumatic Hemarthrosis.
  • Rheumatic Fever.

d) Treatment:

This is an orthopedic emergency to prevent cartilage destruction.

  • Admission: Bed rest, splint the joint (skin traction) to relieve pain and prevent deformity.
  • Antibiotics: Start empirical IV antibiotics immediately after taking cultures.
    - Usually Anti-staphylococcal (e.g., Flucloxacillin + Benzylpenicillin or Ceftriaxone).
    - Continue IV for 2-3 weeks, then oral for 3-4 weeks (Total 6 weeks).
  • Surgical Drainage (Arthrotomy/Washout):
    - Aspiration: Can be done daily if pus is thin.
    - Arthrotomy: Open surgical drainage and irrigation (washout) if pus is thick, loculated, or patient not improving. This is the gold standard.
    - Arthroscopy: Minimally invasive washout.
  • Analgesia: NSAIDs/Opioids for pain control.
  • Rehabilitation: Physiotherapy once inflammation subsides to prevent stiffness.
