1.
Male circumcision is very essential medically, religiously and culturally. Design the correct suturing techniques in male circumcision.
A. Vertical mattress suturing dorsally, horizontal mattress ventrally and simple interrupted in between
B. Horizontal mattress ventrally, vertical mattress dorsally, and simple interrupted in between
C. Vertical mattress ventrally, horizontal mattress dorsally, and simple interrupted in between
D. Simple interrupted dorsally, horizontal mattress ventrally and vertical mattress in between
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Horizontal mattress ventrally, vertical mattress dorsally, and simple interrupted in between

Standard practice often involves a horizontal mattress suture at the frenulum (ventral side) to ensure hemostasis of the frenular artery, and simple interrupted sutures elsewhere. Some variations use vertical mattress dorsally to align the skin and mucosa.

2.
Development of decubitus is a possibility in bed ridden patients. Select the correct statement about decubitus ulcers.
A. Their development in spinal injury patient can be prevented by 4 hourly turning.
B. They respond well to application of topical antibiotics
C. Wound excision is a treatment modality
D. An ulcer with white base shows sign of healing
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Their development in spinal injury patient can be prevented by 4 hourly turning.

The primary prevention strategy for pressure sores (decubitus ulcers) is frequent turning (usually every 2 hours) to relieve pressure. Topical antibiotics are generally not effective for deep ulcers. A white base often indicates slough or necrosis, not healing (which appears as pink granulation tissue).

3.
Determine the correct statement regarding preoperative starvation.
A. After a short fast, insulin level falls
B. Muscle glycogen can be directly utilized
C. Fats are initially mobilized to meet energy requirements
D. Gluconeogenesis only takes place in the liver.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. After a short fast, insulin level falls

During the early stages of starvation (e.g., preoperative fasting), insulin levels drop, and glucagon levels rise to facilitate glycogenolysis and lipolysis. Muscle glycogen cannot be directly converted to blood glucose (lacks glucose-6-phosphatase). Glycogen is used before fats are fully mobilized.

5.
During routine ward rounds, as a clinical officer intern in surgical rotation, you observe that a patient with third degree circumferential burns of the upper right forearm has developed gross swelling of the right hand. Determine the correct statement concerning the patient.
A. Elevation of the limb is the definitive treatment modality
B. Patient has suffered compartment syndrome
C. Infection is the most likely cause of the swelling
D. Urgent surgical intervention is required
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Patient has suffered compartment syndrome (and D is the management)

Circumferential full-thickness (3rd degree) burns form a rigid eschar. As edema develops underneath, it constricts blood flow, leading to compartment syndrome. The immediate requirement is an escharotomy (urgent surgical intervention). While D is also true, B defines the condition.

6.
A 34 year old farmer suffered a cut wound on his left leg when using a farm implement. After delaying to seek medical services for 1 week he developed darkening of the foot with loss of sensation. Determine the correct diagnosis.
A. Necrotizing fasciitis
B. Dry gangrene
C. Gas gangrene
D. Pyomyositis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Dry gangrene

Darkening (blackening) and loss of sensation with a clear line of demarcation typically indicates dry gangrene, usually resulting from ischemia. However, if there was a dirty wound, Gas Gangrene (wet) is a risk, but that usually presents with crepitus, severe pain, and systemic toxicity. Darkening and loss of sensation alone suggests gangrenous change.

A. The written communication material must always be in English.

Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Legally, a signed consent from a patient is proof that a valid consent has been properly obtained.

While the process is more important than the signature, legally the form serves as the primary evidence. 8.
Arrange the correct order in the management of degloving injury.
A. Debridement, defatting, dressing and skin grafting
B. Defatting, dressing, debridement and skin grafting
C. Dressing, debridement, defatting and skin grafting
D. Debridement, dressing, defatting and skin grafting
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Debridement, defatting, dressing and skin grafting

The avulsed flap is often non-viable. The standard procedure is to debride devitalized tissue. If the skin is viable but the subcutaneous tissue is damaged, the skin can be "defatted" (turned into a full-thickness graft) and reapplied as a skin graft.

11.
Inguinal hernias are common in surgical outpatient clinic. Select the most common feature that distinguishes direct inguinal hernia from indirect inguinal hernia
A. Indirect inguinal hernia does not descend to the scrotum
B. Strangulation is more likely to occur in direct inguinal hernia
C. Cough impulse is absent in in direct inguinal hernia
D. Reducibility is readily observed when the patient lies down in direct inguinal hernia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Reducibility is readily observed when the patient lies down in direct inguinal hernia

Direct hernias (through Hesselbach's triangle) have a wide neck and typically reduce spontaneously when the patient lies supine. Indirect hernias have a narrower neck (deep ring) and are prone to strangulation (making B incorrect). Indirect hernias often descend into the scrotum (making A incorrect).

22.
A 21 year old male patient has been diagnosed with osteogenic sarcoma on his left tibia. Determine the appropriate information about this condition.
A. The best treatment modality is amputation of the limb
B. X-ray is likely to show onion peel appearance
C. Chemotherapy has no role in management of this patient
D. This condition is associated with Paget's disease of the bones
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. This condition is associated with Paget's disease of the bones

Osteosarcoma (Osteogenic sarcoma) has a bimodal distribution. While common in young adults, a secondary peak occurs in the elderly, often associated with Paget's disease of bone. C. Treatment with calcium is adequate therapy for this patient

Correct Answer: B. Metaphyseal cupping and widening is a diagnostic radiological feature

Radiological hallmarks of active rickets include cupping, splaying, and fraying of the metaphyses, and widening of the growth plates. Bowing is a later sign of weight-bearing. Treatment requires Vitamin D (and Calcium). Renal osteodystrophy (kidney dysfunction) is a known cause of rickets.

24.
A 52-year-old woman sustains a displaced and angulated fracture through the diaphysis of the right humerus. 42.
Elaborate five complications of blood transfusion stating mitigation measures for each one of them (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Acute Hemolytic Reaction: Mitigation: Proper cross-matching and patient identification (bedside check).
  • Febrile Non-Hemolytic Reaction: Mitigation: Use of leukoreduced blood products.
  • Allergic/Anaphylactic Reaction: Mitigation: Washing RBCs (removing plasma proteins); Pre-medication with antihistamines.

  • Infection Transmission (HIV, Hep B/C): Mitigation: Rigorous donor screening and testing of blood products.

49.

A 45-year-old obese woman, presents with pain on the right hypochondrium, she has nausea and vomiting. On examination, she is sick looking, slightly icteric tachycardia and a blood pressure of 110/60mmHg and a temperature of 37.7 degrees Celsius. Abdominal examination reveals tenderness on the right hypochondrium with hesitation of breathing on full inspiration when pressure is applied on the right hypochondrium

a) Determine the most likely diagnosis.

b) Investigate this patient

c) Formulate a treatment plan for this patient

d) Identify three possible complications likely to occur in this patient.

Show Answer

Answer:

a) Most Likely Diagnosis:

Acute Cholecystitis

Reasoning: The "4 Fs" risk profile (Fat, Female, Forty, Fertile), RUQ pain (Right Hypochondrium), fever, and positive Murphy's Sign (hesitation of breathing on inspiration during palpation).

b) Investigations:

  • Abdominal Ultrasound (Gold Standard): To look for gallstones, thickened gallbladder wall, pericholecystic fluid, and sonographic Murphy's sign.
  • Full Haemogram (CBC): To check for leukocytosis (elevated WBCs) indicating infection.
  • Liver Function Tests (LFTs): To assess bilirubin (patient is icteric), alkaline phosphatase, and transaminases (rule out CBD obstruction).
  • Urea & Electrolytes: To assess renal function and hydration status (due to vomiting).
  • Amylase/Lipase: To rule out Gallstone Pancreatitis.

c) Treatment Plan:

Conservative/Initial Management:

  • Admission to surgical ward.
  • Nil Per Os (NPO): Bowel rest to reduce gallbladder stimulation.
  • IV Fluids: Rehydration (e.g., Ringer's Lactate) to correct dehydration from vomiting.
  • Analgesia: NSAIDs (e.g., Diclofenac) or Opioids (e.g., Pethidine) for pain control.
  • Antibiotics: Broad-spectrum IV antibiotics (e.g., Ceftriaxone + Metronidazole) to cover enteric bacteria.
  • Nasogastric tube if severe vomiting/distension is present.

Definitive Management:

  • Cholecystectomy: Surgical removal of the gallbladder. Laparoscopic Cholecystectomy is preferred (usually within 72 hours of onset).

d) Possible Complications:

  1. Empyema of Gallbladder: Pus accumulation in the gallbladder.
  2. Gangrene/Perforation of Gallbladder: Leading to biliary peritonitis.
  3. Choledocholithiasis: Stone migration to Common Bile Duct causing obstructive jaundice.
  4. Gallstone Pancreatitis: Stone blocking the pancreatic duct.
