Section A: Short Essay Questions

1.
A 65-year old man presents to you with a swollen and painful right knee joint.
a) Outline five (5) other areas you will explore in history taking. (5 marks)
b) Outline five (5) investigations you will request for him and expected findings. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

a) History Taking Areas:

  1. History of Trauma: Any recent falls, twists, or direct blows to the knee.
  2. Associated Symptoms: Fever, chills, weight loss (suggesting infection or malignancy), or stiffness (especially morning stiffness).
  3. Medical History: History of gout, osteoarthritis, rheumatoid arthritis, or bleeding disorders.
  4. Functional Limitation: Difficulty walking, climbing stairs, or locking/giving way of the joint.
  5. Occupational/Social History: Type of work (manual labor), physical activity levels.

b) Investigations and Expected Findings:

  1. X-ray of the Knee (AP and Lateral): Osteophytes, joint space narrowing (OA), fractures, or lytic lesions.
  2. Full Blood Count (FBC): Elevated WBC (Septic arthritis) or normal.
  3. Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate (ESR) / C-Reactive Protein (CRP): Elevated in inflammatory conditions (RA, Septic arthritis).
  4. Serum Uric Acid: Elevated in Gout.
  5. Joint Aspiration (Arthrocentesis) & Fluid Analysis:
    • Cloudy/Purulent: Septic arthritis.
    • Crystals: Gout (needle-shaped) or Pseudogout (rhomboid).
    • Bloody: Trauma or hemophilia.
2.
A 70-year old lady presents with pain and swelling near the right wrist joint following a fall on an outstretched hand. On examination the region is swollen, tender with a dinner fork deformity.
a) What is the most probable diagnosis? (2 marks)
b) How will you manage this case? (8 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

a) Diagnosis: Colles' Fracture (Fracture of the distal radius with dorsal displacement).

b) Management:

  1. Initial Assessment (ATLS): Assess airway, breathing, circulation. Check neurovascular status of the limb (pulse, sensation, movement).
  2. Analgesia: Administer pain relief (e.g., IV Paracetamol, Morphine) and immobilize temporarily.
  3. Radiology: X-ray of the wrist (AP and Lateral) to confirm fracture and displacement.
  4. Reduction (Closed Reduction):
    • Perform under Hematoma block or Bier's block (regional anesthesia).
    • Traction and manipulation to restore normal anatomy (disimpact, flex, and ulnar deviate).
  5. Immobilization: Apply a Colles' cast (below-elbow back slab or full cast) with the wrist in slight flexion and ulnar deviation.
  6. Post-Reduction X-ray: Confirm acceptable alignment.
  7. Follow-up:
    • Elevate the limb to reduce swelling.
    • Encourage finger movements.
    • Review in fracture clinic (1-2 weeks). Cast stays for about 6 weeks.
  8. Rehabilitation: Physiotherapy after cast removal to restore range of motion.
3.
A 30-year old man sustained mid-shaft fracture of left femur:
a) Outline five (5) factors that will determine the success of open reduction with internal fixation (ORIF) in this case. (5 marks)
b) Enumerate five (5) indications of internal fixation. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

a) Factors Determining Success of ORIF:

  1. Soft Tissue Status: Integrity of skin and muscles; severe damage or infection risks failure.
  2. Bone Quality: Osteoporotic bone may not hold screws well.
  3. Fracture Pattern: Comminuted fractures are harder to fix than simple transverse ones.
  4. Surgical Technique: Anatomical reduction and stable fixation.
  5. Patient Factors: Compliance with non-weight bearing, nutritional status, smoking (delays healing).

b) Indications for Internal Fixation:

  1. Failure of closed reduction (cannot achieve or maintain alignment).
  2. Displaced intra-articular fractures (requires perfect alignment).
  3. Polytrauma patients (allows early mobilization).
  4. Pathological fractures (bone weakened by disease).
  5. Fractures associated with neurovascular injury (requires repair).
  6. Floating joints (fractures above and below a joint).

5.
Dysphagia is a common complain in patients suffering from oesophageal diseases.
a) Which other areas will you explore in history taking in a patient presenting to you with dysphagia? (5 marks)
b) Enumerate five (5) differential diagnoses in a 67-year old male patient with dysphagia. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

a) History Taking Areas:

  1. Onset and Duration: Sudden (foreign body) vs. Progressive (malignancy).
  2. Type of Food: Solids first then liquids (mechanical obstruction/cancer) vs. Liquids and solids together (motility disorder/achalasia).
  3. Associated Symptoms: Weight loss, regurgitation, heartburn (GERD), odynophagia (painful swallowing).
  4. Risk Factors: Smoking, alcohol consumption, history of caustic ingestion.
  5. Neurological Symptoms: Weakness, slurred speech (stroke, myasthenia gravis).

b) Differential Diagnoses (67-year old male):

  1. Oesophageal Carcinoma (Squamous cell or Adenocarcinoma).
  2. Peptic Stricture (from chronic GERD).
  3. Achalasia Cardia.
  4. Pharyngeal Pouch (Zenker's diverticulum).
  5. Extrinsic Compression (e.g., Goiter, Lung cancer).

A patient admitted two weeks ago in surgical ward following shot wounds on the chest is later found to have a haemoglobin level of 3 g/dL. After a thorough assessment you decide that he should be transfused. Write a treatment sheet indicating clear transfusion instructions. (10 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

Treatment Sheet / Transfusion Order

Patient Name: [Patient Name]    IP No: [Number]    Date: [Date]

Diagnosis: Severe Anaemia (Hb 3g/dL) secondary to Gunshot Wound

  1. Pre-transfusion:
    • Group and Cross-match 3 units of Whole Blood / Packed Red Blood Cells.
    • Check baseline vitals (BP, Pulse, Temp, RR).
    • Obtain informed consent.

Section B: Long Essay Questions

40 Marks
Answer any TWO questions in this section.
1.
With an increase in use of motor cycle as a mode of transport in Kenya, open (compound) fractures have become common admission in surgical wards.
a) Describe Gustilo-Anderson classification of open fractures. (10 marks)
b) Outline the principles of treating open fractures. (10 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

a) Gustilo-Anderson Classification:

  • Type I: Clean wound < 1 cm long. Simple fracture pattern. Minimal soft tissue injury.
  • Type II: Wound > 1 cm long. Moderate soft tissue damage. No extensive soft tissue avulsion or flaps. Simple or moderate fracture pattern.
  • Type III: High-energy injury with extensive soft tissue damage.
    • Type IIIA: Adequate soft tissue coverage of bone despite extensive laceration/flaps. High energy trauma.
    • Type IIIB: Extensive soft tissue loss with periosteal stripping and bone exposure. Usually requires flap coverage. Massive contamination.
    • Type IIIC: Open fracture associated with arterial injury requiring repair for limb salvage.

b) Principles of Treating Open Fractures:

  1. Initial Resuscitation (ATLS): Stabilize patient first (ABCDEs).
  2. Antibiotic Prophylaxis: Early administration (Cephalosporins +/- Aminoglycosides/Metronidazole) to prevent infection.
  3. Tetanus Prophylaxis: Toxoid +/- Immunoglobulin depending on immunization status.
  4. Wound Debridement (Toilet): Thorough irrigation and excision of dead/devitalized tissue in the operating theater. "The solution to pollution is dilution."
  5. Fracture Stabilization: External fixation is often preferred initially to allow soft tissue care, or IM nailing for specific cases (Type I/II).
  6. Soft Tissue Coverage: Early closure (delayed primary closure) or flap reconstruction (within 72 hours to 1 week).
  7. Rehabilitation: Early mobilization once stable.
2.
A 65-year old man comes to your clinic with complains of frequency, urgency, nocturia and incontinence. You suspect he has benign prostatic hypertrophy (BPH).
a) On systemic review, which other features will you enquire about? (5 marks)
b) What signs do you expect to elicit to confirm your diagnosis? (5 marks)
c) What are the complications of this condition? (10 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

a) Systemic Review / History:

  1. Obstructive Symptoms: Hesitancy, weak stream, straining to void, terminal dribbling, incomplete emptying.
  2. Haematuria: Blood in urine.
  3. Constitutional: Weight loss, bone pain (to rule out Prostate Cancer).
  4. Neurological: History of stroke, diabetes (neurogenic bladder).
  5. Medications: Use of diuretics, anticholinergics.

b) Signs to Elicit:

  1. Abdominal Exam: Palpable distended bladder (chronic retention).
  2. Digital Rectal Examination (DRE): Enlarged prostate, smooth surface, firm consistency, obliterated median sulcus (suggests BPH). Hard/nodular suggests cancer.
  3. External Genitalia: Exclude urethral stricture or stenosis.
  4. Neurological Exam: Anal tone, perineal sensation (to rule out neurogenic causes).

c) Complications of BPH:

  1. Acute Urinary Retention: Sudden inability to pass urine, painful.
  2. Chronic Urinary Retention: Painless, overflow incontinence.
  3. Urinary Tract Infections (UTI): Due to stasis.
  4. Bladder Stones (Calculi): Due to stasis.
  5. Bladder Diverticula: Herniation of mucosa due to high pressure.
  6. Hydronephrosis and Renal Failure: Back pressure affecting kidneys (Obstructive uropathy).
  7. Haematuria: From congested prostatic veins.
3.
A 45-year old man who works at a construction site present with a swelling on the left groin which increases in size on lifting heavy objects and disappears on lying down. He reports that the swelling first appeared about 2 years ago.
a) What is your diagnosis? (2 marks)
b) What are the predisposing factors to this condition? (4 marks)
c) What are the differential diagnoses? (4 marks)
d) Enumerate complications of this condition. (4 marks)
e) Outline its management. (6 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

a) Diagnosis: Left Inguinal Hernia (likely Indirect given the history, though Direct is possible in older men/weakness).

b) Predisposing Factors:

  1. Increased Intra-abdominal Pressure: Heavy lifting (construction work), chronic cough, constipation, straining.
  2. Weakness of Abdominal Wall: Aging, obesity, previous incisions.
  3. Congenital: Patent Processus Vaginalis.

c) Differential Diagnoses:

  1. Femoral Hernia
  2. Inguinal Lymphadenopathy
  3. Saphena Varix
  4. Hydrocele (can coexist or mimic if extending to scrotum)
  5. Undescended Testis
  6. Psoas Abscess

d) Complications:

  1. Irreducibility: Cannot be pushed back.
  2. Obstruction: Bowel loop blocked (vomiting, distension, constipation).
  3. Strangulation: Blood supply cut off (ischemia, gangrene) - Surgical Emergency.
  4. Incarceration: Stuck but not necessarily strangulated yet.

e) Management:

  1. Conservative (Wait and Watch): Only for asymptomatic/minimally symptomatic small hernias in poor surgical candidates (rarely recommended for young active men).
  2. Surgical Repair (Herniorrhaphy/Hernioplasty):
    • Open Mesh Repair (Lichtenstein): Gold standard. Tension-free repair using a mesh.
    • Laparoscopic Repair (TEP/TAPP): Minimally invasive.
    • Bassini/Shouldice Repair: Tissue-based repairs (if mesh unavailable).
  3. Lifestyle Advice: Avoid heavy lifting, treat chronic cough/constipation to prevent recurrence.
