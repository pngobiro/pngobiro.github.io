Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions

20 Marks
1.
The following are Common sites for Avascular necrosis except
A. Head of the femur (after fracture of the femoral neck or dislocation of the hip.
B. Proximal part of the scaphoid after fracture through its waist
C. Lunate following dislocation
D. Body of the calcaneus after fracture of its neck.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Body of the calcaneus after fracture of its neck.

Avascular Necrosis (AVN) is characteristic of bones with retrograde blood supply or precarious vascularity. The Head of Femur, Proximal Scaphoid, Lunate (Kienbock's disease), and Body of Talus are classic sites. The Calcaneus has a rich blood supply and rarely undergoes AVN.

2.
Shortening can be due to except
A. Mal-union,
B. Crushing or actual loss of bone,
C. fractures that split the epiphysis in children
D. non-union
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. fractures that split the epiphysis in children

Fractures that split the epiphysis (Salter-Harris Type III/IV) primarily risk articular incongruity and arthritis. While they can cause growth arrest, the most direct cause of shortening via growth arrest is crushing of the physis (Salter-Harris Type V). Malunion (with overlap), Bone loss, and Displaced Non-union (D) are direct causes of limb shortening. (Note: Non-union with gap/overlap causes shortening; however, in "except" questions, C is the least direct/immediate cause compared to crushing or loss).

3.
Fat embolism syndrome may occur after severe fractures of
A. Femur & tibia.
B. Ulna and radius
C. Ribs
D. Humerus
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Femur & tibia.

Fat Embolism Syndrome (FES) is most commonly associated with fractures of long bones, particularly the Femur and Tibia (lower limb), and pelvic fractures, due to the high fat content in the marrow.

4.
The following explains Myositis ossificans except
A. Heterotrophic bone formation
B. deposition of calcium in muscles
C. causing pain and swelling in muscles
D. occur due to lack of manipulation of fractures
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. occur due to lack of manipulation of fractures

Myositis ossificans is often precipitated by forceful manipulation, passive stretching, or massage of an injured muscle/joint (typically the elbow). Lack of manipulation (rest) is actually preventative.

5.
About tension Pneumothorax
A. It is a surgical emergency
B. May lead to circulatory failure
C. Under water seal drainage is indicated
D. Air moves in and out freely
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Air moves in and out freely

This statement is FALSE. In tension pneumothorax, air enters but cannot exit (one-way valve), leading to pressure buildup. "Air moves in and out freely" describes an Open Pneumothorax (Sucking Chest Wound).

6.
The following are causes of early death in chest injuries which one is not?
A. Flail chest
B. Cardiac tamponade
C. Tension pneumothorax
D. Sepsis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Sepsis

Flail chest, Cardiac Tamponade, and Tension Pneumothorax are immediate/early life threats ("Lethal Six"). Sepsis is a late complication.

7.
Clinical diagnosis of shock is based on the following except
A. Hypotension
B. Tachycardia
C. Cool extremities
D. Hypostatic
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Hypostatic

"Hypostatic" (e.g., hypostatic pneumonia) refers to fluid accumulation due to gravity/immobility, not a diagnostic sign of shock. The classic signs are Tachycardia, Hypotension, and Cool/Clammy skin.

8.
The Principles of Underwater seal drainage include the following which is not
A. The rate of fluid drainage is influenced by level of seal under water
B. The return of air/fluid is prevented by the underwater seal
C. Drainage of fluid is influenced by body weight.
D. Drainage of air is influenced by pressure difference of chest cavity and UWSD system
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Drainage of fluid is influenced by body weight.

Drainage is influenced by gravity (position of the bottle relative to the chest) and intrathoracic pressure, not the patient's body weight.

9.
Which one is wrong in Flail chest
A. Flail segment moves inward during inspiration
B. Chest wall stabilization with assisted ventilation is necessary
C. Paradoxical breathing is a common feature
D. Respiratory distress is not a common failure
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Respiratory distress is not a common failure

This statement is Wrong. Respiratory distress/failure is the primary and most dangerous complication of flail chest (due to underlying lung contusion and pain).

10.
About Monteggia fracture the following is true
A. A fracture of the upper half of the ulna with dislocation of the head of the radius.
B. Usually the cause is a fall on the elbow
C. Associated medial nerve injury is self-limiting.
D. The 'piano-key sign' is diagnostic.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. A fracture of the upper half of the ulna with dislocation of the head of the radius.

This is the definition of a Monteggia Fracture-Dislocation. (Piano key sign is for distal radioulnar joint instability).

11.
'Dinner-fork' deformity is a feature in
A. Galeazzi Fracture
B. Colles' Fracture
C. Smith's fracture
D. Barton's fracture
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Colles' Fracture

Dorsal displacement of the distal radius fragment creates the classic "Dinner Fork" appearance.

12.
Causes of inadequate fluid resuscitation in a burns patient include the following except
A. Inaccurate estimate of burn size
B. Undiagnosed inhalation injury
C. Concomitant traumatic injury
D. Mathematical accuracy
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Mathematical accuracy

Mathematical accuracy leads to correct fluid calculation. Inaccuracy, underestimation of TBSA (A), or unappreciated losses from other injuries (C) lead to inadequate resuscitation.

13.
The following are Patient factors for admission in burns which one is not
A. Extremes of age
B. Circumferential burns
C. concomitant trauma
D. pre-existing medical disorder
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Circumferential burns

All options are valid criteria for admission. However, if distinguishing between "Patient Factors" (Age, Comorbidities - A, C, D) and "Burn Characteristics" (B), B is the odd one out. Nevertheless, circumferential burns must be admitted. In the context of "Not an indication", all are indications. Assuming the question seeks the category difference or is slightly malformed, B is the least "patient-intrinsic" factor.

14.
Which one is false on skin graft
A. Failure in graft take may be associated with exudates
B. graft take may be associated with haemorrhages at graft site
C. Granulation tissue is important at recipient sit
D. Meshing of the skin graft increases the surface area.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. graft take may be associated with haemorrhages at graft site (Implied False)

Hemorrhage/Hematoma under a graft separates it from the bed, preventing revascularization and causing Graft Failure, not "graft take". A, C, and D are true statements.

15.
Concerning burns the following are true except
A. Extreme of age may influence the outcome of burns
B. Sulphur silver sulphadiazine is a topic ointment
C. Colloids are recommended as first line therapy
D. Inhalation burns don't influence management.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Inhalation burns don't influence management.

Inhalation burns significantly influence management (airway security, fluid needs). Option C is also debatable (Crystalloids are 1st line in first 24h), but D is definitely false.

16.
In stress response, which of the following statements is false?
A. It is graded.
B. Metabolism and nitrogen excretion are related to the degree of stress.
C. In such a situation there are physiological, metabolic and immunological changes.
D. The changes cannot be modified.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. The changes cannot be modified.

The metabolic stress response can be modified/attenuated by interventions such as adequate analgesia, minimally invasive surgery, and early enteral feeding.

17.
Which of the following statements is false for optimal perioperative care?
A. Volume loss should be promptly treated by large intravenous (IV) infusions of fluid.
B. Hypothermia and pain are to be avoided.
C. Starvation needs to be combated
D. Avoid immobility
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Volume loss should be promptly treated by large intravenous (IV) infusions of fluid.

While volume replacement is crucial, modern "Goal-Directed Fluid Therapy" advocates for balanced/targeted resuscitation. Blind "large" infusions can lead to fluid overload and edema. Options B, C (ERAS protocols avoid starvation), and D are pillars of modern care.

18.
In patient with snake bite the following is true except
A. Anaphylactic shock may occur
B. Tourniquet application is useful
C. Gangrene can occur
D. Cellulitis may be a complication
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Tourniquet application is useful

Tourniquets are generally contraindicated in snake bites as they concentrate venom, worsening local tissue necrosis/gangrene.

19.
In the management of patient with burns Using open method
A. Abed cradle is not useful
B. Tetanus toxoid is of no use
C. Silver sulphadiazine is necessary
D. Analgesia is contraindicated
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Silver sulphadiazine is necessary

In the open method (exposure), a topical antimicrobial agent like Silver Sulphadiazine (SSD) is applied to form a protective eschar. Bed cradles ARE useful. Tetanus and Analgesia are MANDATORY.

20.
Branulas are mostly preferred in giving fluids or blood than needles because.
A. Are sharper than needles
B. Are always aseptic
C. Cannot tissue easily
D. Are coated with antibiotics
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Cannot tissue easily

Branulas (plastic cannulae) are flexible. Once inserted (and the needle removed), they are less likely to puncture the vein wall ("tissue"/extravasate) during movement compared to rigid steel needles.

Enumerate five (5) signs in a patient presenting with Tension pneumothorax (should not include Tachycardia & Tachypnea).
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Tracheal Deviation: Away from the affected side.
  2. Distended Neck Veins (Elevated JVP): Due to impaired venous return.
  3. Hyper-resonance: On percussion of the affected side.
  4. Absent Breath Sounds: On the affected side.
  5. Hypotension/Shock: Due to obstructive shock.
  6. Surgical Emphysema: Subcutaneous air.

3.
Complication of blood transfusion.
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Acute Hemolytic Reaction (ABO incompatibility).
  • Febrile Non-Hemolytic Reaction.
  • Allergic/Anaphylactic Reaction.
  • Fluid Overload (TACO - Transfusion Associated Circulatory Overload).
  • TRALI (Transfusion Related Acute Lung Injury).
  • Transmission of Infection (HIV, Hep B/C).
  • Electrolyte Imbalance (Hyperkalemia, Hypocalcemia).
4.
Outline the Pathophysiology of Fracture Healing.
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Haematoma Formation (Inflammation): Bleeding forms a clot; inflammatory cells clean debris. (Days 1-5).
  2. Soft Callus Formation (Reparative): Fibroblasts and chondroblasts form a fibrocartilaginous bridge. (Weeks 2-3).
  3. Hard Callus Formation (Reparative): Osteoblasts mineralize the soft callus into woven bone via endochondral ossification. Clinical union. (Weeks 4-12).
  4. Remodelling: Woven bone is replaced by lamellar bone; medullary canal restored based on stress lines (Wolff's Law). (Months to Years).
5.
Briefly discuss the cardinal features of gangrene.
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Color Change: Black (Dry) or Green/Purple (Wet).
  • Loss of Sensation: Anesthesia in the affected part.
  • Loss of Temperature: Coldness.
  • Loss of Function.
  • Loss of Pulses: Absent distal pulses.
  • Line of Demarcation: Clear in dry gangrene; Indistinct in wet gangrene.
  • Putrefaction/Smell: Especially in wet gangrene.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions

40 Marks
1.
Mr. Tumbojoto a 65 year old man presents to Embu county and referral hospital where you are a clinician with abdominal features. Carefully outline the pre-operative and postoperative care if he is to undergo laparotomy. (20 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

A. Pre-operative Care:

  1. Admission & Assessment: Full history and physical exam to confirm diagnosis and assess fitness (ASA Grade).
  2. Resuscitation:
    • IV Fluids (Crystalloids) to correct dehydration/electrolyte imbalance.
    • Nasogastric Tube (NGT) for decompression ("Suck").
    • Urinary Catheterization to monitor output.
  3. Investigations: U&Es, CBC, Group & Cross-match, Coagulation profile, ECG & CXR (age >40).
  4. Consent: Explain procedure/risks and obtain informed written consent.
  5. Medication: Prophylactic antibiotics (within 1h of incision), Analgesia, DVT prophylaxis (Heparin/Stockings), Pre-medication (Anxiolytics/Antacids).
  6. Preparation: NPO (Nil Per Os) for 6-8 hours. Skin preparation (shaving if needed). Gowning/Labeling.

B. Post-operative Care:

  1. Immediate (Recovery): Airway patency, O2 therapy, Vital signs monitoring (every 15 mins), Consciousness level.
  2. Ward Care (Days 0-3):
    • Positioning: Propped up/Semi-Fowler's once conscious.
    • Fluids/Nutrition: NPO until bowel sounds return. Maintenance IV fluids. Monitor Input/Output.
    • Pain Management: Regular analgesia (Opioids/NSAIDs).
    • Wound Care: Check dressing for bleeding/soakage.
    • Tubes/Drains: Manage NGT, Catheter, and surgical drains.
    • Antibiotics: Continue therapeutic/prophylactic course.
  3. Rehabilitation: Chest physiotherapy, early mobilization to prevent DVT/Pneumonia.
  4. Diet Progression: Sips of water → Clear fluids → Soft diet upon return of flatus/bowel sounds.
2.
Discuss the principles of management of fractures. (20 Marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

The management of fractures follows the "4 R's":

  1. Resuscitation (Initial Management):
    • Follow ATLS protocols (Airway, Breathing, Circulation).
    • Treat life-threatening conditions first (Shock, etc.).
    • Splint the limb to prevent further damage and reduce pain.
    • Analgesia and Neurovascular assessment.
  2. Reduction (Restoring Anatomy):
    • Aim: To restore length, alignment, and rotation.
    • Closed Reduction: Manipulation under anesthesia/sedation (traction and counter-traction).
    • Open Reduction: Surgical exposure of fracture site (indicated if closed fails, intra-articular, or for internal fixation).
  3. Retention / Restriction (Immobilization):
    • Aim: To hold the reduction in place until union occurs.
    • Conservative: Casts (POP), Splints, Skin/Skeletal Traction, Functional Bracing.
    • Operative: Internal Fixation (Plates, Screws, Nails/IM Rods) or External Fixation (for open/complex fractures).
  4. Rehabilitation:
    • Aim: To restore function and prevent stiffness/atrophy.
    • Physiotherapy: Isometric exercises (while immobilized), Isotonic exercises (after union).
    • Joint mobilization and gait training.
