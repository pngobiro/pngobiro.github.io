Set 1: General Short Answer Questions

1.
Outline any 4 crystalloids, when they are indicated and normal fluids maintenance rules. (4 mks)
Show Answer

Crystalloids:

  • Normal Saline (0.9% NaCl): Indicated for resuscitation in shock, hyponatremia, and irrigation.
  • Ringer's Lactate (Hartmann's Solution): Indicated for burn resuscitation, trauma/blood loss, and metabolic acidosis.
  • Dextrose 5% (D5W): Indicated for hypoglycemia, hypernatremia (free water deficit). Not for resuscitation.
  • Dextrose Saline (D5NS): Maintenance fluid in children/adults.

Normal Fluid Maintenance Rules (4/2/1 Rule):

  • 4 mL/kg/hr for the first 10 kg.
  • 2 mL/kg/hr for the next 10 kg.
  • 1 mL/kg/hr for every kg thereafter.
  • Example for 70kg adult: (40 + 20 + 50) = 110 mL/hr.
2.
Outline the 5 Rs during management of fracture. (5 mks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Recognition: Diagnosing the fracture via history, exam, and X-ray.
  2. Reduction: Restoring the bone to normal alignment (Open or Closed).
  3. Retention: Holding the reduction in place (Immobilization via POP, traction, or fixation).
  4. Rehabilitation: Restoring function to the limb (Physiotherapy).
  5. Recording: Documenting findings and management.

Show Answer

Answer: Triple Assessment

  1. Clinical Assessment: History and physical examination (Palpation of lump and lymph nodes).
  2. Radiological Imaging: Mammography (for >35 yrs) or Ultrasound (for <35 yrs).
  3. Pathological/Histological Analysis: Fine Needle Aspiration Cytology (FNAC) or Core Biopsy for definitive tissue diagnosis.

5.
Define and state types of shock. (5 mks)
Show Answer

Definition: Shock is a state of inadequate tissue perfusion and cellular hypoxia, resulting in an imbalance between oxygen supply and demand.

Types:

  1. Hypovolemic Shock: Due to fluid/blood loss (e.g., hemorrhage, dehydration).
  2. Cardiogenic Shock: Pump failure (e.g., MI, arrhythmias).
  3. Distributive Shock: Abnormal vasodilation (e.g., Septic, Anaphylactic, Neurogenic).
  4. Obstructive Shock: Physical obstruction to blood flow (e.g., Cardiac tamponade, Tension pneumothorax, Pulmonary embolism).

Set 2: Case Studies & Clinical Scenarios

Case Study: An elderly man presents to you, complaining that he has noticed that over the last one month, his right foot has started becoming black, dry and he is now unable to use that limb.

a) What is the most likely diagnosis?

Show Answer

Diagnosis: Dry Gangrene (likely due to chronic ischemia/PVD).

b) Identify 3 clinical classifications of his condition.

Show Answer

Classifications of Gangrene:

  1. Dry Gangrene: Due to arterial occlusion, slow progression, distinct line of demarcation, minimal infection.
  2. Wet Gangrene: Due to venous obstruction or infection in moist tissue, rapid spread, no demarcation, systemic toxicity.
  3. Gas Gangrene: Caused by Clostridium species, crepitus present, severe toxicity (Surgical emergency).
Case Study: Abdi, 24 years old, presents at casualty with a history of a stab wound on the anterior right side of chest following a scuffle. After examination, the surgeon decided to put chest tube drainage.

a) What are indications of a chest tube in chest injury?

Show Answer
  1. Pneumothorax: Presence of air in pleural space (Simple or Tension).
  2. Haemothorax: Accumulation of blood in pleural space.
  3. Haemopneumothorax: Both blood and air.
  4. Empyema: Pus in pleural space (if infection develops).

b) What are possible complications that may arise with insertion of the chest tube?

Show Answer
  1. Infection: Empyema or insertion site infection.
  2. Injury to organs: Lung parenchyma, heart, liver, or spleen (if placed too low).
  3. Malposition: Tube placed subcutaneously or in fissures.
  4. Bleeding: Injury to intercostal vessels.
  5. Surgical Emphysema: Air leaking into subcutaneous tissue.
Case Study: A 23-year old lady had been caught by fire. She has burns involving her face, chest, abdomen and both arms. The surface area burnt was about 10% deep and 20% superficial burn. Weight 52kg.

a) Write 3 indications for her hospital admission.

Show Answer
  1. Extent of Burn: >10% TBSA (She has 30% total).
  2. Site of Burn: Face involvement (Risk of airway compromise/inhalation injury).
  3. Depth: Presence of Deep/Full thickness burns (10%).

b) What is the first action you will do in managing this patient?

Show Answer

Assess Airway (Primary Survey): Since she has facial burns and was in a fire, immediate assessment for inhalation injury and securing the airway (intubation if necessary) is priority A.

c) Write down the amount and type of fluid you will give from arrival up to 6:00 pm (assuming arrival 10am, burn at 6am).

Show Answer

Parkland Formula: 4mL x Weight (kg) x %TBSA

  • Total Volume = 4 x 52 x 30 = 6,240 mL in 24 hours.
  • Timing: Half given in first 8 hours from time of injury.
    Injury at 6:00 am. First 8 hours ends at 2:00 pm.
    She arrived at 10:00 am (4 hours post-burn).
    First Half (3,120 mL) needs to be given by 2:00 pm. Since she arrived at 10am, this must be given rapidly over remaining 4 hours.
  • Remaining Half: 3,120 mL given over next 16 hours (2:00 pm to 6:00 am next day).
  • Question asks "up to 6:00 pm":
    From 2:00 pm to 6:00 pm is 4 hours of the second half.
    Rate for second half = 3120 / 16 = 195 mL/hr.
    Volume for 4 hours = 195 x 4 = 780 mL.
  • Total by 6pm: 3,120 (1st half) + 780 (part of 2nd half) = 3,900 mL.
  • Fluid Type: Ringer's Lactate (Hartmann's).

Set 4: Orthopaedics & Trauma

1.
Regarding Skin traction: state the indications, contraindications, and complications.
Show Answer

Indications:

  • Temporary immobilization of femoral shaft fractures (e.g., in children - Gallows traction).
  • Correction of deformities / Muscle spasms.
  • Conservative management of hip pathology.

Contraindications:

  • Skin pathology (abrasions, ulcers, dermatitis) on the limb.
  • Severe varicose veins.
  • Weight requirement > 5-7 kg (Skin cannot tolerate heavy weights).

Complications:

  • Skin blistering/necrosis (shearing force).
  • Allergic reaction to adhesive.
  • Common peroneal nerve palsy (if bandage tight at fibular neck).
  • Compartment syndrome (if too tight).
2.
Describe the stages of fracture healing. (5 mks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Hematoma Formation (Inflammation): (Days 1-7) Blood clot forms, inflammatory cells migrate to site.
  2. Soft Callus Formation: (Weeks 2-3) Fibrocartilage collar forms, stabilizing ends.
  3. Hard Callus Formation: (Weeks 4-12) Osteoblasts convert soft callus to hard bone (ossification). Clinical union occurs here.
  4. Remodeling: (Months to Years) Excess bone is removed by osteoclasts, compact bone replaces spongy bone, medullary canal restored.
3.
Compartment syndrome is one of the major complications of fracture. Outline the clinical features and treatment.
Show Answer

Clinical Features (The 6 Ps):

  1. Pain: Out of proportion to injury, increased on passive stretch (Earliest sign).
  2. Paresthesia: Numbness/tingling (Early nerve ischemia).
  3. Pallor: Pale skin.
  4. Paralysis: Late sign.
  5. Pulselessness: Very late sign.
  6. Poikilothermia: Cold limb.

Treatment:

  • Remove constrictive dressings/casts immediately.
  • Keep limb at heart level (do not elevate excessively).
  • Emergency Fasciotomy: Surgical incision of fascia to release pressure.
4.
Concerning Chest injuries: Define flail chest, List clinical features, and Name investigations/Tx.
Show Answer

Definition: Flail chest occurs when a segment of the rib cage breaks due to trauma and becomes detached from the rest of the chest wall (two or more ribs broken in two or more places).

Clinical Features:

  • Paradoxical Breathing: The flail segment moves in during inspiration and out during expiration (opposite to normal).
  • Severe chest pain.
  • Dyspnea / Respiratory distress.
  • Crepitus on palpation.

Treatment:

  • Analgesia (critical).
  • Oxygen therapy.
  • Positive Pressure Ventilation (if respiratory failure).
  • Surgical stabilization (in severe cases).
