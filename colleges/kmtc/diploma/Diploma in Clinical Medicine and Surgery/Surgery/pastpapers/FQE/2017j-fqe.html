Section A: Short Essay / Structured Questions

60 Marks
1.
Discuss the following on Osteogenic Sarcoma:
a) List down Four clinical features. (4 marks)
b) Outline the expected X-ray findings. (6 marks)
Show Answer

a) Clinical Features:

  1. Pain: Severe, persistent pain at the tumor site, often worse at night.
  2. Swelling: A rapidly growing, painful, bony mass/swelling.
  3. Local Signs: Increased warmth (hotness) and dilated subcutaneous veins over the tumor (due to high vascularity).
  4. Functional Loss: Limping (if lower limb involved) or limitation of joint movement.
  5. Pathological Fracture: Fracture occurring with minimal trauma.

b) Expected X-ray Findings:

  • Sunburst Appearance: Radiating spicules of new bone formation perpendicular to the shaft (periosteal reaction).
  • Codman's Triangle: Triangular elevation of the periosteum at the margin of the tumor.
  • Bone Destruction: Areas of osteolysis (bone destruction) mixed with sclerosis (new bone formation).
  • Soft Tissue Extension: Calcification or mass extending into surrounding soft tissues.
  • Site: Typically affects the metaphysis of long bones (e.g., distal femur, proximal tibia).
2.
Thyroid swellings are a common condition in surgical out-patient clinics:
(a) Enumerate Five causes of thyroid enlargement.
(b) State five indications of thyroidectomy.
Show Answer

(a) Causes of Thyroid Enlargement (Goitre):

  1. Iodine Deficiency: Endemic goitre (Simple colloid goitre).
  2. Autoimmune Disease: Graves' disease (diffuse toxic goitre) or Hashimoto's thyroiditis.
  3. Malignancy: Thyroid carcinoma (Papillary, Follicular, Medullary, Anaplastic).
  4. Physiological: Puberty or Pregnancy (increased demand).
  5. Goitrogens: Dietary (cabbage, kale) or drug-induced (lithium).
  6. Thyroiditis: De Quervain's thyroiditis (viral/inflammatory).

(b) Indications for Thyroidectomy:

  1. Malignancy: Confirmed or suspected thyroid cancer.
  2. Pressure Symptoms: Compression of trachea (dyspnea/stridor), esophagus (dysphagia), or recurrent laryngeal nerve (hoarseness).
  3. Cosmesis: Large unsightly goitre causing social embarrassment.
  4. Toxic Goitre: Failed medical management or relapse of Graves' disease.
  5. Retrosternal Extension: Goitre extending into the chest.
3.
An 18 year old male patient weighing 50 kg presents with history burns from spillage of boiling water. On examination the burns are estimated to be 2nd degree, 30% burns.
(a) Using Parkland's formula, calculate the required amount of fluids and describe how it should be administered.
(b) Enumerate Four early complications of burns.
Show Answer

(a) Parkland Formula Calculation:

Formula: Total Fluid (Ringer's Lactate) = 4 ml × Body Weight (kg) × % Total Body Surface Area (TBSA) burned

  • Weight = 50 kg
  • % Burns = 30%
  • Total Fluid = 4 × 50 × 30 = 6,000 ml (6 Liters)

Administration Plan (First 24 Hours):

  • First 8 hours: Give 50% of total = 3,000 ml (3 Liters). Calculate from the time of injury, not time of arrival.
  • Next 16 hours: Give the remaining 50% = 3,000 ml (3 Liters).
  • Fluid of choice: Ringer's Lactate (Hartmann's solution).

(b) Early Complications of Burns:

  1. Hypovolemic Shock: Due to massive fluid loss and capillary leak.
  2. Inhalational Injury/Laryngeal Edema: Airway obstruction.
  3. Renal Failure (Oliguria): Due to hypoperfusion and hemoglobinuria/myoglobinuria.
  4. Electrolyte Imbalance: Hyperkalemia (tissue destruction) followed by hypokalemia; Hyponatremia.
  5. Hypothermia: Loss of skin barrier.
  6. Compartment Syndrome: In circumferential burns (eschar effect).
4.
Posterior hip dislocation is commonly caused by road traffic accidents.
(a) List down Four clinical features of posterior hip dislocation.
(b) Describe the management of posterior hip dislocation.
Show Answer

(a) Clinical Features:

  1. Position: The limb is held in Flexion, Adduction, and Internal Rotation.
  2. Shortening: The affected limb appears shorter (true shortening).
  3. Pain: Severe pain in the hip region; patient cannot move the leg.
  4. Palpable Head: The femoral head may be palpable in the gluteal region.
  5. High Trochanter: The greater trochanter is raised above Nelaton's line.

(b) Management:

  1. Emergency Reduction: This is an orthopedic emergency (to prevent avascular necrosis). Perform closed reduction under General Anesthesia and muscle relaxation.
  2. Methods of Reduction:
    • Allis Method: Patient supine, flex knee and hip to 90°, apply traction upwards while an assistant stabilizes pelvis.
    • Stimson’s Method: Patient prone, leg hangs off bed, apply downward pressure.
  3. Post-Reduction Check: Confirm reduction clinically (movement restored) and radiologically (check X-ray).
  4. Immobilization: Skin traction for 3-6 weeks to allow soft tissue healing.
  5. Rehabilitation: Gradual mobilization non-weight bearing initially.
5.
Describe the Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) used in assessment of severity of head injury.
Show Answer

The GCS scores a patient's level of consciousness based on three parameters. The total score ranges from 3 (deep coma) to 15 (fully conscious).

1. Eye Opening (E) - Max 4:

  • 4 - Spontaneous
  • 3 - To Speech/Voice
  • 2 - To Pain
  • 1 - None

2. Verbal Response (V) - Max 5:

  • 5 - Oriented (time, place, person)
  • 4 - Confused conversation
  • 3 - Inappropriate words
  • 2 - Incomprehensible sounds (groans)
  • 1 - None

3. Motor Response (M) - Max 6:

  • 6 - Obeys commands
  • 5 - Localizes pain
  • 4 - Withdraws from pain (flexion)
  • 3 - Abnormal flexion (Decorticate posture)
  • 2 - Extension (Decerebrate posture)
  • 1 - None (Flaccid)

Interpretation: Mild (13-15), Moderate (9-12), Severe (≤8 - usually requires intubation).

6.
Explain the procedure of catheterizing a male patient with acute urinary retention.
Show Answer
  1. Preparation: Explain procedure, obtain consent, gather equipment (sterile catheter pack, Foley catheter size 14-16F, urobag, syringe with water, xylocaine gel, antiseptic).
  2. Positioning: Place patient supine with legs slightly abducted.
  3. Cleaning: Wearing sterile gloves, clean the glans penis and meatus with antiseptic solution (retract foreskin if present).
  4. Anaesthesia/Lubrication: Instill 10-15ml of Xylocaine jelly into the urethra using the nozzle. Hold the meatus closed for 3-5 minutes to allow effect.
  5. Insertion:
    • Hold the penis vertically (perpendicular to body) to straighten the urethra.
    • Insert the catheter gently. If resistance is felt at the external sphincter (prostate), ask patient to breathe deeply and lower the penis slightly. Do not force.
    • Advance to the hilt (bifurcation) to ensure balloon is in the bladder.
  6. Confirmation: Urine should flow. If not, apply gentle suprapubic pressure.
  7. Fixation: Inflate the balloon with the specified amount of sterile water (usually 10ml). Gently pull back until resistance is felt.
  8. Connection: Connect to drainage bag. Reposition foreskin (to prevent paraphimosis).

Section B: Long Essay Questions

40 Marks
1.
A 70-year-old man was brought to outpatient department complaining of itching of skin, urinating brownish dark urine and yellowish eyes which has been progressive over the last two weeks. On examining the man, you find scratch marks over his whole skin and a deeply yellowish sclera. The abdomen is unremarkable.
a) What is your diagnosis? (2 mks)
b) List FOUR possible causes of this condition. (8 mks)
c) Outline the diagnostic investigative modalities in this patient. (10 mks)
d) What is your approach to the management of this patient? (10 mks)
Show Answer

a) Diagnosis: Obstructive Jaundice (Post-hepatic Jaundice).

b) Possible Causes:

  1. Carcinoma of the Head of Pancreas: Common in elderly, progressive painless jaundice.
  2. Cholangiocarcinoma: Cancer of the bile ducts.
  3. Choledocholithiasis: Stones in the common bile duct (though often painful, can be painless initially).
  4. Peri-ampullary Carcinoma: Tumor at the ampulla of Vater.
  5. Metastatic Lymph Nodes: Compressing the porta hepatis.

c) Diagnostic Investigative Modalities:

  • Liver Function Tests (LFTs): Will show cholestatic pattern (markedly elevated Conjugated Bilirubin and Alkaline Phosphatase).
  • Abdominal Ultrasound: First-line imaging. Looking for dilated intra/extra-hepatic ducts and masses in the pancreas or stones.
  • CT Scan Abdomen: Better visualization of pancreatic masses, staging of cancer, and lymph node involvement.
  • ERCP (Endoscopic Retrograde Cholangiopancreatography): Both diagnostic (visualize ducts) and therapeutic (stenting/biopsy).
  • Tumor Markers: CA 19-9 (Pancreatic cancer), CEA.
  • Clotting Profile (PT/INR): To assess Vitamin K deficiency status.

d) Management Approach:

  1. Pre-operative Optimisation:
    • Vitamin K: Administer parenteral Vitamin K to correct coagulopathy (INR correction).
    • Hydration: Aggressive IV fluids to prevent hepatorenal syndrome.
    • Antibiotics: Prophylactic coverage against cholangitis.
    • Nutrition: High carbohydrate diet.
    • Relief of Pruritus: Antihistamines or Cholestyramine.
  2. Definitive Treatment (Surgical):
    • If resectable tumor: Whipple's Procedure (Pancreaticoduodenectomy).
    • If gallstones: ERCP with sphincterotomy or Cholecystectomy + CBD exploration.
  3. Palliative Care (if unresectable):
    • Biliary Stenting (via ERCP) to relieve jaundice.
    • Surgical Bypass (Choledochojejunostomy) to relieve obstruction.
    • Pain management.
2.
A female patient in her early thirties was brought to your casualty department semiconscious. The relatives said she had vomited blood twice at night. On examination you find that she is very pale and her pulse is thready and blood pressure is not recordable.
a) What is your diagnosis? (2 mks)
b) List the possible causes of this condition. (8 mks)
c) What is your approach to the management of this patient? (10 mks)
Show Answer

a) Diagnosis: Hypovolemic Shock secondary to Upper Gastrointestinal Bleeding (Massive Hematemesis).

b) Possible Causes of Upper GI Bleeding:

  1. Peptic Ulcer Disease (PUD): Bleeding Duodenal or Gastric ulcer (most common).
  2. Esophageal Varices: Rupture due to portal hypertension (Liver disease).
  3. Mallory-Weiss Tear: Mucosal tear at GE junction due to severe vomiting.
  4. Gastric Erosions/Gastritis: NSAID induced or alcohol related.
  5. Gastric Carcinoma: Ulcerating tumor.

c) Management Approach:

1. Immediate Resuscitation (ABCDE):

  • Airway & Breathing: Ensure patent airway (risk of aspiration). Administer high-flow oxygen.
  • Circulation: Insert two large-bore IV cannulas (14G/16G). Take blood for grouping, cross-match, FBC, Urea/Electrolytes.
  • Fluid Therapy: Rapid infusion of crystalloids (Ringer's/Saline) to restore volume. If no response, initiate blood transfusion (O-negative or type-specific) immediately.
  • Catheterization: Monitor urine output as a guide to perfusion.

2. Pharmacological:

  • IV PPI: High dose Omeprazole/Pantoprazole bolus + infusion.
  • Vasoconstrictors: Terlipressin or Octreotide if varices are suspected.
  • Vitamin K/Tranexamic Acid: If indicated.

3. Definitive/Diagnostic:

  • Urgent Upper GI Endoscopy (OGD): Once stabilized, to diagnose source and treat (banding, sclerotherapy, adrenaline injection, clipping).
  • Sengstaken-Blakemore Tube: If variceal bleeding is uncontrollable.
  • Surgery: If endoscopic control fails or re-bleeding occurs (e.g., under-running the bleeding vessel).
3.
A 10-year-old boy is brought to the hospital with a 4 days' history of pain and swelling of left knee joint. On examination, he is sick looking with a temperature of 39 degrees Celsius. The joint is swollen, hot, fluctuant and very tender.
a) What is the most likely diagnosis? (2 mks)
b) Outline three investigative modalities for this case and expected findings. (6 mks)
c) List down two differential diagnoses in this case. (2 mks)
d) Describe the treatment of this condition. (10 mks)
Show Answer

a) Diagnosis: Acute Septic Arthritis (Pyogenic Arthritis) of the Left Knee.

b) Investigations:

  1. Joint Aspiration (Arthrocentesis): Finding: Turbid/purulent fluid, high WBC count (>50,000), bacteria on Gram stain/Culture.
  2. Full Haemogram (CBC) & ESR/CRP: Finding: Leukocytosis (high WBC), elevated ESR and CRP (inflammatory markers).
  3. X-ray of Knee: Finding: Early - Soft tissue swelling, widened joint space. Late - Joint destruction (avoid waiting for late signs).
  4. Blood Culture: Finding: May isolate causative organism (e.g., Staph aureus).

c) Differential Diagnoses:

  1. Acute Osteomyelitis (Distal femur or Proximal tibia).
  2. Traumatic Haemarthrosis.
  3. Juvenile Rheumatoid Arthritis (JRA).
  4. Tuberculous Arthritis.

d) Treatment:

This is an orthopedic emergency to prevent cartilage destruction.

  1. Admission & Resuscitation: Admit, analgesia, antipyretics, hydration.
  2. Joint Drainage (Decompression):
    • Arthrotomy (Open Washout): The gold standard. Open the joint, drain pus, wash thoroughly with saline.
    • Arthroscopic Washout: If available, less invasive.
    • Aspiration: Only if surgery is not immediately possible (repeated daily), but often insufficient for thick pus.
  3. Antibiotics: Start empiric IV antibiotics (e.g., Flucloxacillin + Ceftriaxone) immediately after taking cultures. Continue IV for 2-3 weeks, then oral for 3-4 weeks (Total 6 weeks). Adjust based on sensitivity.
  4. Immobilization: Splint the joint (skin traction or backslab) in functional position to relieve pain and prevent contractures initially.
  5. Rehabilitation: Start gentle passive range of motion once inflammation subsides to prevent stiffness.
