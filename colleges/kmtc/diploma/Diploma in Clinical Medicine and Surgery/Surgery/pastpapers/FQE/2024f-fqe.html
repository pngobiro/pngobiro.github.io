Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions

40 Marks
1.
A patient present to you with a 4 hours history of painful swelling on his right gluteal region. Upon examination, you made a diagnosis of a gluteal abscess. Formulate the treatment for this patient:
A. Give strong analgesics
B. Give broad spectrum antibiotics
C. Do skin grafting
D. Incision and drainage
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Incision and drainage

The definitive treatment for an abscess is drainage of the pus. Antibiotics alone cannot penetrate the abscess cavity effectively ("Ubi pus, ibi evacua" - where there is pus, let it out).

2.
Categorize principles of fracture management sequentially.
A. Resuscitation, alignment, reduction and immobilization
B. Resuscitation, reduction, alignment and immobilization
C. Reduction, Resuscitation, alignment, and immobilization
D. Resuscitation, alignment, immobilization, and reduction
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Resuscitation, reduction, alignment and immobilization

The priority is always to save life first (Resuscitation), followed by restoring anatomy (Reduction), maintaining that position (Alignment/Immobilization), and finally Rehabilitation.

3.
A patient present to a health facility with a bleeding cut injury. How will you determine that it is an arterial bleeding?
A. Presence of dark red blood
B. Oozing of blood from both ends of the vessel
C. Bleeding gushes at the proximal end of the vessel
D. Blood oozes from the blood vessel
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Bleeding gushes at the proximal end of the vessel

Arterial bleeding is characterized by bright red blood that spurts or gushes in time with the heartbeat due to high pressure, typically from the proximal end (closest to the heart).

4.
Determine an indication of skeletal traction?
A. Patients with posterior hip dislocation
B. Pelvic injuries in young adults
C. Some spinal injuries irrespective of age
D. Young adults with fractured femur
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Young adults with fractured femur

Skeletal traction allows for greater weight to be applied than skin traction and is often used for femoral shaft fractures in adults to maintain length and alignment before definitive fixation.

5.
Regarding patients presenting with complications of traction, determine which one of the following will be excluded.
A. Pressure sores
B. Parasitic infections
C. Hypostatic pneumonia
D. Injury to nerves
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Parasitic infections

Pressure sores, pneumonia, and nerve injury (e.g., peroneal nerve palsy) are known complications of traction/immobility. Parasitic infections are not directly related to the traction procedure.

6.
Among the following responses, choose early complication of compound fractures.
A. Chronic osteomyelitis
B. Bleeding
C. Non union
D. Delayed union
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Bleeding

Bleeding (hemorrhage) is an immediate/early complication. Osteomyelitis, non-union, and delayed union are intermediate or late complications.

7.
Gas gangrene is a common condition in surgical wards. Establish its aetiology.
A. Anaerobic organisms
B. Aerobic organisms
C. Clostridium tetani
D. Beta hemolytic streptococcus.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Anaerobic organisms

Gas gangrene is caused by Clostridium species (e.g., C. perfringens), which are anaerobic bacteria. Note: Clostridium tetani causes Tetanus, not Gas Gangrene.

8.
Glasgow coma scale entails

D. Gang reflex.

Correct Answer: C. Motor response

The GCS components are Eye Opening (E), Verbal Response (V), and Motor Response (M).

9.
Osteoporosis is a common condition in orthopedic ward. Choose the correct response about it among the following.
A. Common in female below 40 years of age
B. It is uncommon cause of fragility fractures
C. Causes increased bone density
D. Hormonal therapy plays a role in treatment
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Hormonal therapy plays a role in treatment

Osteoporosis is common in post-menopausal women (>50). It causes decreased density and is a major cause of fragility fractures. Hormone Replacement Therapy (HRT) or SERMs can help maintain bone density.

10.
The most common site for pressure sore is
A. The heel
B. Ischium
C. Occiput
D. Elbow region
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Ischium

For wheelchair-bound patients, the ischial tuberosity is the most common site. For bedridden patients, the sacrum and heels are very common. Given the options, Ischium is a major site.

11.
Regarding second degree burns, which of the following statement is correct?
A. All the skin appendages are affected
B. Very superficial layers of the epidermis are affected
C. Are always caused by radiation
D. Are caused by hot fluids
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Are caused by hot fluids

Second-degree burns (partial thickness) are commonly caused by scalds (hot fluids). They affect the epidermis and part of the dermis, often presenting with blisters.

12.

13.

14.

Section 2: Short Answer Questions

40 Marks
41.
Elaborate 5 precautions you would teach students to avoid transfusion overload.
Show Answer
  1. Check Volumetric Needs: Verify if the patient truly needs volume or just red cells (use packed cells if volume is an issue).
  2. Rate of Infusion: Transfuse slowly (e.g., over 3-4 hours) in susceptible patients (elderly, cardiac issues).
  3. Diuretics: Administer a diuretic (e.g., Furosemide) before or between units.
  4. Monitoring: Closely monitor vital signs (BP, Pulse, Respiratory Rate) and check for JVP elevation or basal crepitations.
  5. Positioning: Transfuse with the patient in a sitting or semi-recumbent position.
42.
Establish three aetiologies of limb shortening and two measures taken to correct this anomaly.
Show Answer

Aetiologies:

  1. Malunion of fractures (overlap or angulation).
  2. Growth plate (physeal) arrest due to injury or infection in children.
  3. Bone loss (segmental loss) from trauma or infection.
  4. Untreated dislocation (e.g., congenital hip dislocation).

Correction Measures:

  1. Conservative: Shoe raises/lifts for minor discrepancies.
  2. Surgical: Bone lengthening procedures (e.g., Ilizarov technique) or Epiphysiodesis (stopping growth of the longer limb in children).
43.
Elaborate five complications you anticipate in a patient after amputation.
Show Answer
  1. Haematoma/Haemorrhage: Bleeding from the stump.
  2. Infection: Wound infection or gas gangrene.
  3. Phantom Limb Pain: Sensation or pain in the missing limb.
  4. Flexion Contractures: Of the joint above the amputation.
  5. Necrosis of skin flaps: Poor blood supply to the edges.
  6. Neuroma formation: Painful nerve endings.

48.
Determine five methods of internal fixation of fractures.
Show Answer

Section 3: Long Answer Question

20 Marks
49.
A 40-year-old female patient presents with a two months' history of yellowness of eyes and mucous membranes, severe pruritus and passing yellow stools (likely pale/clay colored).
a) What is the diagnosis?
b) Propose 5 most relevant investigations.
c) Develop specific preoperative management.
Show Answer

a) Diagnosis: Obstructive Jaundice (Post-hepatic Jaundice).

Note: The combination of jaundice and pruritus strongly suggests obstruction (bile salt deposition). Common causes in a 40-year-old female include Choledocholithiasis (stones) or Carcinoma (Head of Pancreas/Cholangiocarcinoma).

b) Relevant Investigations:

  1. Liver Function Tests (LFTs): To check for elevated Conjugated Bilirubin and Alkaline Phosphatase (ALP) indicating obstruction.
  2. Abdominal Ultrasound: First-line imaging to visualize dilated biliary ducts and detect gallstones or masses.
  3. CT Scan of Abdomen: For better visualization of pancreatic masses or staging of malignancy.
  4. ERCP (Endoscopic Retrograde Cholangiopancreatography) or MRCP: To visualize the biliary tree and potentially treat stones (ERCP).
  5. Coagulation Profile (PT/INR): To check for Vitamin K deficiency due to malabsorption, crucial before any procedure.

c) Preoperative Management:

  1. Vitamin K Administration: Administer parenteral Vitamin K to correct clotting defects (obstruction prevents fat-soluble vitamin absorption). Monitor INR.
  2. Hydration/IV Fluids: Aggressive rehydration to prevent hepatorenal syndrome (renal failure due to bilirubin toxicity).
  3. Antibiotics: Prophylactic broad-spectrum antibiotics to prevent cholangitis (infection of stasis bile).
  4. Nutrition: Nutritional support (high carb, low fat) as patient may be malnourished.
  5. Pruritus Management: Antihistamines or Cholestyramine to manage itching.
  6. Blood Cross-match: Ensure blood is available for surgery.
  7. Consent: Informed consent explaining risks of surgery/procedure.
