Section 2: Short Answer Questions

40 Marks
1.
Enumerate five (5) signs in a patient presenting with Tension pneumothorax (should not include Tachycardia & Tachypnea).
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Tracheal Deviation: Shifted to the contralateral (opposite) side.
  2. Distended Neck Veins (Elevated JVP): Due to compression of the superior vena cava and impaired venous return.
  3. Hyper-resonance: On percussion of the affected side due to trapped air.
  4. Absent or Reduced Breath Sounds: On auscultation of the affected side.
  5. Hypotension (Shock): Due to decreased cardiac output from mediastinal shift.
  6. Surgical Emphysema: Crackling sensation on palpation of the chest wall/neck.
  7. Asymmetrical Chest Expansion: Reduced movement on the affected side.
  8. Cyanosis: Late sign of hypoxia.
2.
Identify five (5) typical clinical features in a patient presenting with inhalational burns.
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Singed Nasal Hairs: Or eyebrows/facial hair.
  2. Carbonaceous Sputum: Coughing up soot/black particles.
  3. Hoarseness of Voice: Indicating laryngeal edema/injury.
  4. Burns to the Face/Neck: Evidence of thermal injury near the airway.
  5. Stridor: Audible inspiratory sound indicating upper airway obstruction.
  6. Soot in the Oropharynx: Visible black deposits in the mouth/throat.
  7. Dyspnea/Respiratory Distress: Difficulty breathing.
3.
Briefly discuss the cardinal features of gangrene.
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Change in Color:
    • Dry Gangrene: Tissue turns black (mummified) due to deposition of iron sulfide from hemoglobin breakdown.
    • Wet Gangrene: Tissue appears purple, blue, or greenish-black.
  • Loss of Sensation (Anesthesia): The affected part is numb as nerves die due to ischemia.
  • Loss of Temperature: The area is cold to the touch compared to surrounding healthy tissue.
  • Loss of Function: Complete loss of motor function in the affected part.
  • Loss of Pulses: Distal pulses are absent.
  • Line of Demarcation: A clear separation between dead and healthy tissue (prominent in dry gangrene; absent/ill-defined in wet gangrene).
  • Foul Odour: Especially in wet/gas gangrene due to putrefaction by bacteria.
4.
List 5 general complications of fractures.
Show Answer

Answer:

Immediate/Early:

  1. Hypovolemic Shock: Due to hemorrhage (e.g., Pelvic/Femoral fractures).
  2. Fat Embolism Syndrome: Release of marrow fat into circulation.
  3. Compartment Syndrome: Increased pressure within osteofascial compartments.
  4. Deep Venous Thrombosis (DVT) / Pulmonary Embolism (PE).
  5. Infection: Especially in open fractures (Osteomyelitis, Gas gangrene, Tetanus).

Late:

  • Delayed Union / Non-Union / Mal-Union.
  • Avascular Necrosis.
  • Joint Stiffness / Osteoarthritis.
5.
Outline 5 steps in fracture healing.
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Haematoma Formation (Inflammation): Bleeding from bone/tissue forms a clot; inflammatory cells invade (Hours to Days).
  2. Cellular Proliferation: Osteoprogenitor cells differentiate; granulation tissue forms (Days to Weeks).
  3. Callus Formation (Soft Callus): Fibrocartilaginous callus bridges fracture ends; visible calcification begins (Weeks).
  4. Consolidation (Hard Callus): Soft callus is converted into rigid woven bone via endochondral ossification (Weeks to Months). Clinical union occurs here.
  5. Remodelling: Woven bone is replaced by strong lamellar bone; medullary canal is restored; bone shaped by mechanical stress (Wolff's Law) (Months to Years).

7.
Outline five enquiries to be considered in history taking in a female patient with a mass on the right breast.
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Duration and Progression: How long has the lump been there? Is it increasing in size?
  2. Pain: Is the lump painful? Is the pain cyclical (related to menstrual cycle)?
  3. Nipple Changes: Is there any discharge (bloody/serous)? Any retraction, inversion, or ulceration of the nipple?
  4. Skin Changes: Any dimpling, puckering, redness, or "orange peel" appearance (peau d'orange)?
  5. Family History: History of breast or ovarian cancer in first-degree relatives?
  6. Obstetric/Gynaecological History: Age at menarche/menopause, parity, age at first birth, breastfeeding history, use of hormonal contraceptives or HRT.
8.
(a) List down five clinical features of compartment syndrome
(b) Outline the management of compartment syndrome
Show Answer

Answer:

(a) Clinical Features (The 6 Ps):

  • Pain: Out of proportion to the injury and increased on passive stretching of muscles (Earliest and most reliable sign).
  • Paresthesia: Numbness or tingling (Early sign of nerve ischemia).
  • Pallor: Pale skin.
  • Paralysis: Late sign.
  • Pulselessness: Very late sign.
  • Poikilothermia: Limb feels cold.
  • (Also: Tense, woody hard swelling of the compartment).

(b) Management:

  1. Remove Constrictions: Immediately remove all external dressings, bandages, casts, or splints down to the skin.
  2. Limb Positioning: Keep the limb at heart level (do not elevate excessively as it reduces perfusion pressure; do not depend as it increases edema).
  3. Monitoring: Assess neurovascular status and compartment pressure if monitoring is available.
  4. Fasciotomy (Definitive): Urgent surgical decompression by incising the deep fascia to release pressure in all affected compartments.
  5. Post-Fasciotomy Care: Wound care, delayed closure or skin grafting once swelling subsides.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions

40 Marks
1.

A 35 year old male patient is brought to casualty following a road traffic accident. On examination, he is sick looking, very restless and sweating profusely. The blood pressure is 90/50 mmHg and has a weak radial pulse of 103 beats/minute.

On musculoskeletal examination he has a wound measuring approximately 10 cm on his left leg with a visible bone protruding through the skin.

Outline the management of this patient from casualty up to the time of discharge. (20 Marks)

Show Answer

Answer:

The patient presents with signs of hypovolemic shock (Hypotension, Tachycardia, Sweating, Restlessness) and an Open Fracture (Gustilo-Anderson Type IIIB - >10cm, bone exposed). Management follows ATLS protocols.

A. Primary Survey & Resuscitation (Casualty):

  1. Airway & C-Spine Control: Ensure patent airway; apply cervical collar if indicated.
  2. Breathing: Administer high-flow oxygen via non-rebreather mask.
  3. Circulation & Hemorrhage Control:
    • Control external bleeding with direct pressure (on the leg wound).
    • Insert 2 wide-bore IV cannulae (14G/16G).
    • Take blood for FBC, U&Es, Group & Cross-match.
    • Infuse IV Crystalloids (Hartmann's/Normal Saline) rapidly (1-2 Liters stat) to treat shock. Prepare for blood transfusion if no response.
  4. Disability: Assess GCS and pupils.
  5. Exposure: Fully undress to inspect for other injuries; keep warm to prevent hypothermia.

B. Specific Fracture Management (Casualty):

  • Antibiotics: Immediate broad-spectrum IV antibiotics (e.g., Ceftriaxone + Gentamicin + Metronidazole) for open fracture.
  • Tetanus Prophylaxis: Administer Tetanus Toxoid (0.5ml IM) and ATG if immunization status is unknown.
  • Analgesia: IV Morphine or appropriate pain relief.
  • Wound Care: Photograph wound (to avoid repeated uncovering). Cover with sterile saline-soaked gauze. Do not push bone back in casualty.
  • Splinting: Apply a splint (e.g., Back slab) to immobilize the limb, reduce pain, and prevent further soft tissue damage.
  • Imaging: X-ray of Left Tibia/Fibula (AP & Lateral) including knee and ankle joints. Chest X-ray/Pelvis X-ray if indicated by mechanism.

C. Definitive Surgical Management (Theatre):

  • Urgency: Patient taken to theatre within 6 hours for wound debridement.
  • Debridement: Under anesthesia, thorough irrigation (6-9L saline) and excision of all necrotic skin, muscle, and loose bone fragments.
  • Stabilization: Skeletal stabilization is mandatory. For Grade IIIB, External Fixation is often preferred initially to allow wound care. Intramedullary nailing may be considered if feasible.
  • Soft Tissue Cover: Do not close the wound under tension. Plan for delayed primary closure, skin graft, or flap (plastic surgery) once wound is clean.

D. Post-operative Ward Care:

  • Monitoring: Vitals, Fluid balance, Compartment Syndrome check (Neurovascular status of foot).
  • Medications: Continue antibiotics (therapeutic course), Analgesia, DVT prophylaxis (LMWH).
  • Nutrition: High protein diet to promote healing.
  • Limb Elevation: To reduce edema.

E. Rehabilitation & Discharge:

  • Physiotherapy: Crutch training (non-weight bearing initially), joint mobilization.
  • Discharge Criteria: Wound healing well/grafted, no infection, patient mobile with crutches, pain controlled.
  • Follow-up: Clinic appointments for X-rays and fixator removal/conversion.
2.

A 28 year old female patient was admitted in surgical ward with a diagnosis of acute intestinal obstruction. Upon review by a surgeon he was taken to theatre for an emergency laparotomy.

(a) Outline the post-operative management of this patient (10 Marks)

(b) Enumerate 10 post-operative complications likely to occur in this patient (10 Marks)

Show Answer

(a) Post-operative Management:

  • Immediate (Recovery Room): Maintain airway, breathing, circulation. Monitor vitals every 15 mins until stable. Assess consciousness.
  • Positioning: Nurse in propped-up/semi-Fowler's position once fully conscious to facilitate breathing and drainage.
  • Nil Per Os (NPO): Keep patient NPO until bowel sounds return and flatus is passed.
  • Nasogastric Tube (NGT): Keep on free drainage/intermittent suction to decompress stomach. Remove when aspirate decreases and bowel sounds return.
  • Intravenous Fluids: Maintain hydration (e.g., Dextrose Saline/Hartmann's) with K+ supplementation based on U&Es and daily requirements (3L/day + losses).
  • Analgesia: Adequate pain control (e.g., Pethidine/Morphine initially, then NSAIDs/Paracetamol).
  • Antibiotics: Continue IV antibiotics (e.g., Ceftriaxone + Metronidazole) as per surgical findings/contamination.
  • Output Monitoring: Maintain strict Intake/Output chart. Monitor urine output via catheter (aim >0.5ml/kg/hr).
  • DVT Prophylaxis: Early mobilization, compression stockings, LMWH if not contraindicated.
  • Wound Care: Check dressing for soakage. Change as needed.
  • Diet Reintroduction: Start sips of water -> clear fluids -> soft diet -> regular diet gradually upon return of bowel function.

(b) Post-operative Complications (10):

  1. Paralytic Ileus: Prolonged absence of bowel function.
  2. Wound Infection (Surgical Site Infection): Pus, redness, fever.
  3. Wound Dehiscence: Burst abdomen (breakdown of suture line).
  4. Reactionary/Secondary Haemorrhage: Bleeding internally or from wound.
  5. Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT): Calf pain/swelling.
  6. Pulmonary Embolism (PE): Sudden dyspnea/chest pain.
  7. Atelectasis / Pneumonia: Lung collapse or infection due to poor respiratory effort.
  8. Enterocutaneous Fistula: Leakage of bowel contents to skin.
  9. Intra-abdominal Abscess: Collection of pus (pelvic/subphrenic).
  10. Adhesions: Leading to recurrent intestinal obstruction (Late complication).
  11. (Others: Incisional Hernia, Keloids, Anastomotic leak).
