Operative & Peri-operative Care

Discuss the specific factors affecting operative risks.
Show Answer
  1. Patient's Health Status: Pre-existing conditions (diabetes, heart disease, obesity) increase complications.
  2. Type of Surgery: Major surgeries (e.g., organ transplant) carry higher risks than minor ones.
  3. Surgical Team Expertise: Experienced professionals reduce the likelihood of complications.
  4. Blood Loss and Transfusion: Risks associated with massive blood loss and compatibility/infection risks of transfusion.
  5. Complication Management Plan: Availability of ICU/HDU and a comprehensive plan reduces mortality.
Classify high risk patients according to the American Society of Anesthesiologists (ASA) score.
Show Answer
  • Class I: Normal healthy patient.
  • Class II: Patient with mild systemic disease (no functional limitation).
  • Class III: Patient with severe systemic disease that limits functional activity.
  • Class IV: Patient with severe systemic disease that poses a constant threat to life.
  • Class V: Moribund patient not expected to survive >24h with/without surgery.
  • Class E: Added to any of the above for Emergency surgery.
Briefly discuss the significance of obesity in the peri-operative period.
Show Answer

1. Cardiovascular System: Hypertension/Ischaemic heart disease is more common. BP measurement is difficult.

2. Respiratory System: Difficult airway (intubation). Reduced lung volumes. Increased risk of hypoxia, atelectasis, pneumonia, and PE.

3. Surgical Factors: Difficult surgical access/exposure. Increased risk of wound infection and dehiscence.

4. Other: Difficult venous access. Higher incidence of DVT and Diabetes.

Shock & Trauma

Briefly describe the types of shock giving clinical features of each.
Show Answer
  • Hypovolaemic Shock: Inadequate volume (hemorrhage, dehydration).
    Features: Pale cool skin, Tachycardia, Oliguria, Hypotension, Restlessness.
  • Cardiogenic Shock: Pump failure (MI, Arrhythmia).
    Features: Raised JVP, Basal crepitations, Distended neck veins, Lower limb edema.
  • Distributive Shock: Abnormal flow distribution.
    • Septic: Fever, warm/cold skin, rapid HR.
    • Neurogenic: Bradycardia, warm dry skin, hypotension.
    • Anaphylactic: Hives, swelling, wheezing, rapid pulse.
  • Obstructive Shock: Physical obstruction (Tamponade, Tension Pneumothorax, PE).
    Features: Distended neck veins, Tracheal deviation, Muffled heart sounds.
Briefly discuss the stages of shock.
Show Answer
  1. Non-progressive (Compensated) Stage: Body mechanisms (sympathetic reflexes, catecholamines) maintain perfusion. BP is normal, HR elevated.
  2. Progressive (Decompensated) Stage: Compensatory mechanisms fail. Tissue hypoperfusion leads to metabolic acidosis, electrolyte imbalance, and organ dysfunction. Positive feedback loops (cardiac depression, toxin release) worsen the state.
  3. Irreversible Stage: Severe cell damage/necrosis has occurred. Transfusion/therapy cannot save life. Features: Coma, irregular pulse, diastolic BP zero, agonal breathing.
Briefly discuss Flail Chest.
Show Answer

Definition: Fracture of two or more consecutive ribs in two or more places, creating a floating segment.

Pathophysiology: Paradoxical chest movement (inward during inspiration, outward during expiration) leads to hypoventilation and mediastinal flutter.

Management: Analgesia (crucial), Oxygen, Fluid therapy, Positive Pressure Ventilation (internal pneumatic stabilization) if respiratory failure occurs. Surgery is rarely indicated.

Wounds, Burns & Ulcers

Explain local factors that influence wound healing.
Show Answer
  • Infection: Causes tissue destruction and delays healing.
  • Blood Supply: Ischemia inhibits fibroblasts and defense mechanisms.
  • Foreign Bodies: Presence of dirt, sutures, or necrotic tissue.
  • Tissue Loss: Large defects require more time/granulation.
  • Movement/Tension: Disrupts delicate new tissue.
  • Type of Wound: Incision vs Crush/Avulsion.
Discuss the difference between a Keloid scar and Hypertrophic scar.
Show Answer
Feature Hypertrophic Scar Keloid Scar
Boundaries Confined to wound margins Extends beyond wound margins (invades normal tissue)
Regression Often regresses over time Rarely regresses
Response to Surgery Improves Worsens (High recurrence)
Timing Develops soon after injury May develop months later
Discuss burns according to depth of injury.
Show Answer
  • 1st Degree (Epidermal): Red, dry, painful. No blisters. Heals without scar. (e.g., Sunburn).
  • 2nd Degree (Partial Thickness):
    • Superficial: Red, moist, weeping blisters, blanches. Painful.
    • Deep: Cheesy white/waxy, dry, does not blanch. Risk of scarring/contracture.
  • 3rd Degree (Full Thickness): Charred, black/grey, leathery (eschar). Insensate (painless). Will scar, requires grafting.
  • 4th Degree: Involves muscle/bone (e.g., electrical burns).
Describe Wagner's classification of ulcers (Diabetic Foot).
Show Answer
  • Grade 0: No open lesion, but high risk (deformity, callus).
  • Grade 1: Superficial ulcer (skin only).
  • Grade 2: Deep ulcer (tendon, bone, joint capsule visible) but no abscess/osteomyelitis.
  • Grade 3: Deep ulcer with abscess or osteomyelitis.
  • Grade 4: Localized gangrene (toes/heel).
  • Grade 5: Extensive gangrene of whole foot.

Systemic Surgery

What are the different subtypes of breast cancer, and how do they influence treatment?
Show Answer
  • Hormone Receptor Positive (HR+): ER+ and/or PR+. Responds to hormone therapy (Tamoxifen, Aromatase inhibitors). Better prognosis (Luminal A).
  • HER2-Positive: Overexpression of HER2 protein. Aggressive but responds to targeted therapy (Herceptin/Trastuzumab).
  • Triple-Negative (TNBC): Lacks ER, PR, HER2. Aggressive, fewer targeted options. Treated primarily with Chemotherapy.
Describe management of diabetic foot.
Show Answer
  1. Blood Sugar Control: Tight glycemic control to aid healing and fight infection.
  2. Wound Care: Regular inspection, cleaning, and debridement of necrotic tissue.
  3. Infection Management: Antibiotics based on culture (often polymicrobial).
  4. Offloading: Special footwear, insoles, or Total Contact Casting to remove pressure.
  5. Vascular Assessment: Check pulses/Doppler. Revascularization if ischemic.
  6. Surgery: Debridement, or Amputation if gangrenous/septic.
