Section B: Short Answer Questions (30 Marks)

1.
A 30-year-old woman presents with a painless lump in her neck. On examination, it is located in the midline, moves with swallowing, and is consistent with a thyroglossal duct cyst. Write down the embryological origin of this condition and outline the definitive management. (6 Marks)
Show Answer

Embryological Origin:

  • It arises from a persistent remnant of the thyroglossal duct.
  • During development, the thyroid gland descends from the foramen caecum at the base of the tongue to its final position in the neck. The tract usually atrophies, but if it persists, it forms a cyst.

Definitive Management:

  • Sistrunk Procedure: This involves:
    1. Excision of the cyst.
    2. Excision of the entire thyroglossal tract up to the foramen caecum.
    3. Removal of the central portion of the hyoid bone (as the tract passes through or closely to it).
  • This extensive removal is necessary to prevent recurrence.
2.
Outline the signs and symptoms of testicular torsion. (6 Marks)
Show Answer

Symptoms:

  • Sudden, severe scrotal pain (often waking patient from sleep).
  • Nausea and vomiting.
  • Abdominal pain.

Signs:

  • High-riding testis (due to shortened cord).
  • Horizontal lie of the testis (Bell-clapper deformity).
  • Absent Cremasteric reflex.
  • Swollen, tender, erythematous hemiscrotum.
  • Negative Prehn's sign (elevation of testis does not relieve pain, unlike epididymitis).

A 45-year-old man presents with a recent onset of headaches, visual disturbances, and galactorrhea. Investigations reveal a pituitary adenoma. What are the possible hormonal imbalances associated with pituitary adenomas and their corresponding clinical manifestations? (6 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Hyperprolactinemia (Prolactinoma): Galactorrhea, gynecomastia, impotence/loss of libido in men; amenorrhea/infertility in women.
  2. Growth Hormone Excess: Acromegaly (in adults) - coarse features, large hands/feet, prognathism; Gigantism (in children).
  3. ACTH Excess (Cushing's Disease): Central obesity, moon face, buffalo hump, hypertension, striae.
  4. Panhypopituitarism (Compression of normal tissue): Hypothyroidism, adrenal insufficiency, hypogonadism due to lack of stimulating hormones.

Note: Visual disturbances are due to mass effect (compression of optic chiasm -> bitemporal hemianopia).

5.
Discuss the role of tumour immunology in the development and treatment of cancer. (6 Marks)
Show Answer

Development (Immune Surveillance vs Escape):

  • The immune system normally identifies and destroys neoplastic cells via T-cells and NK cells (Immune Surveillance).
  • Tumors develop when they evade this system by downregulating MHC molecules, secreting immunosuppressive cytokines (e.g., TGF-beta), or expressing checkpoint proteins (PD-L1) to inhibit T-cells.

Treatment (Immunotherapy):

  • Checkpoint Inhibitors: Drugs blocking PD-1/PD-L1 or CTLA-4 (e.g., Pembrolizumab) restore T-cell activity against cancer.
  • CAR-T Cell Therapy: Patient's T-cells are genetically engineered to recognize tumor antigens.
  • Monoclonal Antibodies: Target specific tumor antigens (e.g., Trastuzumab/Herceptin for HER2+ breast cancer).

Section C: Long Answer Questions (40 Marks)

1.
A 40-year-old woman presents with a palpable breast lump. Discuss the following:
i) Triple assessment
ii) Benign breast disorders
iii) Breast cancer treatment options
Show Answer

i) Triple Assessment:

  • Clinical Assessment: History and physical examination (inspection and palpation of breast and axilla). Score P1-P5.
  • Radiological Imaging:
    • Mammography: For women >35 years.
    • Ultrasound: For women <35 years or to characterize lumps (solid vs cystic). Score R1-R5.
  • Pathological Diagnosis (Biopsy):
    • Core Needle Biopsy (preferred for histology/receptor status).
    • Fine Needle Aspiration Cytology (FNAC). Score B1-B5.
  • Rationale: Increases diagnostic accuracy to >99%.

ii) Benign Breast Disorders:

  • Fibroadenoma: "Breast mouse". Mobile, rubbery, painless lump. Common in <30s. Mgmt: Reassurance, excision if large (>3cm).
  • Fibrocystic Changes: Cyclical pain, lumpiness. Mgmt: Support bra, analgesia, exclude cancer.
  • Breast Cyst: Fluid-filled sac. Smooth, halo on US. Mgmt: Aspiration.
  • Mastitis/Abscess: Infection (lactational). Pain, fever, redness. Mgmt: Antibiotics, drainage.
  • Duct Ectasia: Dilated ducts, green nipple discharge. Mgmt: Smoking cessation, excision if troublesome.

iii) Breast Cancer Treatment:

  • Surgical:
    • Breast Conserving Surgery (Lumpectomy/WLE) + Radiotherapy.
    • Mastectomy (Simple or Radical) if tumor is large, multicentric, or patient preference.
    • Axillary surgery: Sentinel node biopsy or Axillary clearance.
  • Adjuvant Therapy:
    • Chemotherapy: For high-grade, node-positive, or triple-negative tumors.
    • Radiotherapy: Post-WLE or post-mastectomy with risk factors.
    • Hormonal Therapy: Tamoxifen (pre-menopausal) or Aromatase Inhibitors (post-menopausal) for ER+ tumors.
    • Targeted Therapy: Trastuzumab (Herceptin) for HER2+ tumors.
2.
A 20-year-old man is brought to the emergency department following a motorcycle accident. He has sustained a head injury and is unconscious. Discuss:
i) Primary and secondary brain injury
ii) Raised intracranial pressure (ICP)
iii) Management
Show Answer

i) Primary vs Secondary Brain Injury:

  • Primary Injury: Damage occurring at the moment of impact (e.g., contusion, axonal shearing, skull fracture). Cannot be reversed.
  • Secondary Injury: Delayed damage occurring minutes to days later due to physiological insults.
    • Mechanisms: Hypoxia, Hypotension (ischemia), Raised ICP, Hematoma expansion, Seizures, Hyperthermia.
    • Goal: Prevention of secondary injury is the main focus of neurocritical care.

ii) Raised ICP Pathophysiology:

  • Monro-Kellie Doctrine: Cranium is a fixed volume (Brain + Blood + CSF). Increase in one must be compensated by decrease in others; if not, pressure rises.
  • Pathophysiology: Edema/Bleed -> Increased Volume -> Compensatory displacement of CSF/Venous blood -> Decompensation -> Herniation -> Brainstem compression -> Death.
  • Clinical Manifestations: Headache, Vomiting, Papilledema, Cushing's Triad (Hypertension, Bradycardia, Irregular respiration), Reduced GCS, Pupillary changes (blown pupil).

iii) Management:

  • ABCDE Assessment: Secure Airway (Intubate if GCS < 8), maintain Breathing (Target PaCO2 35-40 mmHg, avoid hypoxia), maintain Circulation (MAP > 80 mmHg to ensure CPP).
  • General Measures: Head elevation 30 degrees, neck neutral, treat fever, treat seizures.
  • ICP Reduction:
    • Osmotherapy: Mannitol or Hypertonic Saline.
    • Hyperventilation (Short term): vasoconstriction reduces cerebral blood volume.
    • Sedation/Paralysis: Reduces metabolic demand.
    • Surgical Decompression: Evacuation of hematoma (craniotomy) or Decompressive Craniectomy.
3.
Discuss transplantation immunology, focusing on:
i) Histocompatibility
ii) Allograft rejection
iii) Immunosuppression
Show Answer

i) Histocompatibility:

  • HLA System: Human Leukocyte Antigens (MHC molecules) are cell surface proteins that present antigens to T-cells.
  • Matching: The immune system recognizes non-self HLA. The closer the match between donor and recipient (especially HLA-A, HLA-B, and HLA-DR), the lower the risk of rejection.
  • Cross-matching: Checking for pre-formed antibodies in recipient blood against donor cells to prevent hyperacute rejection.

ii) Allograft Rejection:

  • Hyperacute (Minutes/Hours): Pre-formed antibodies (humoral) attack the graft. Thrombosis and necrosis occur. (Rare now due to cross-matching).
  • Acute (Weeks/Months): T-cell mediated (Cellular) or Antibody mediated. Commonest type. Signs: Organ dysfunction, fever, pain. Usually reversible with steroids.
  • Chronic (Months/Years): Slow fibrosis and scarring of the graft (e.g., chronic allograft nephropathy). Irreversible.

iii) Immunosuppression Strategies:

  • Induction: Strong biological agents at time of transplant (e.g., Basiliximab, Anti-thymocyte globulin) to prevent acute rejection.
  • Maintenance: Long-term cocktail to keep immune system dampened.
    • Calcineurin Inhibitors: Tacrolimus, Cyclosporine.
    • Antiproliferatives: Mycophenolate Mofetil (MMF), Azathioprine.
    • Steroids: Prednisolone.
    • mTOR inhibitors: Sirolimus.
  • Rescue: High dose pulse steroids or antibodies for acute rejection episodes.
