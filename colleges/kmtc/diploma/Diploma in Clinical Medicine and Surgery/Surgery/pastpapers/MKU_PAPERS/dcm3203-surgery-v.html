Section A: Multiple Choice Questions (30 Marks)

1.
Which is not a principle of compound fracture treatment?
A. Immediate wound closure
B. Aggressive antibiotic cover
C. Wound debridement
D. No tendon repair
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Immediate wound closure

Compound (open) fractures are generally left open or closed delayed primary to prevent gas gangrene and infection entrapment. Immediate closure is contraindicated in contaminated wounds.

2.
Medial meniscus is more vulnerable to injury because;
A. It is semi-circular in shape
B. Its fixity to tibial collateral ligament
C. Action of adductor magnus
D. Its attachment to fibrous capsule
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Its fixity to tibial collateral ligament

The medial meniscus is attached to the medial collateral ligament (MCL), making it less mobile and more prone to tearing during twisting injuries compared to the lateral meniscus.

3.
Injury to popliteal artery in fracture of lower end of femur is often due to
A. Proximal fragment pressing the artery
B. Tight plaster
C. Distal fragment pressing the artery
D. Hematoma
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Distal fragment pressing the artery

In supracondylar fractures of the femur, the gastrocnemius muscle pulls the distal fragment posteriorly, impinging on the popliteal artery.

4.
Most common type of hip dislocation is;
A. Anterior
B. Central
C. Dislocation with a fracture of the shaft
D. Posterior
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Posterior

Posterior dislocation accounts for 85-90% of hip dislocations, typically occurring in dashboard injuries (knee hitting dashboard with hip flexed).

5.
Internal fixation is done in all fractures except;
A. Compound
B. Multiple
C. Elderly
D. All the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Compound

In heavily contaminated compound fractures, internal fixation is often avoided initially due to high infection risk; external fixation is preferred.

6.
The most important factor in fracture healing is;
A. Immobilization
B. Adequate calcium intake
C. Organization of blood clot
D. None of the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Immobilization

Stability (immobilization) and blood supply are the two critical factors for union.

7.
The most preferred treatment of fracture neck of femur in a young person is;
A. Hemiarthroplasty
B. Total hip replacement
C. Internal fixation
D. Conservative treatment
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Internal fixation

In young patients, the priority is to preserve the natural femoral head to avoid the need for multiple revisions of joint replacements later in life. ORIF with cannulated screws or DHS is preferred.

8.
Which carpal bone fractures most frequently
A. Lunate
B. Hamate
C. Pisiform
D. Scaphoid
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Scaphoid

The scaphoid is the most commonly fractured carpal bone, typically from a fall on an outstretched hand (FOOSH).

9.
Which complication is least common in colle's fracture
A. Non- union
B. Malunion
C. Sudeck's atrophy
D. Stiffness of the wrist
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Non- union

Colles' fractures (distal radius) involve cancellous bone which heals very well. Non-union is rare. Malunion (dinner fork deformity) is the most common complication.

10.
What is the treatment of choice in 6 -week fracture neck of femur in a 65-year-old
A. SP nailing
B. Hemiarthroplasty
C. Open reduction internal fixation
D. Total hip replacement
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Hemiarthroplasty (or D)

A 6-week old fracture is a neglected fracture with high risk of non-union/AVN. In a 65-year-old, joint replacement (Hemiarthroplasty or THR) is preferred over fixation to allow immediate mobilization.

11.
Most common cause of a pathological fracture in children is;
A. Malignancy
B. Fibrous dysplasia
C. Bone cyst
D. All the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Bone cyst

Unicameral (simple) bone cysts are the most common cause of pathological fractures in children, often in the proximal humerus or femur.

12.
Which fracture neck of femur has a poor prognosis
A. Extracapsular
B. Inferior
C. Superior
D. Intracapsular
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Intracapsular

Intracapsular fractures disrupt the retinacular blood supply to the femoral head, carrying a high risk of Avascular Necrosis (AVN) and non-union.

13.
Which of the following is the least common complication in supracondylar fracture
A. Non-union
B. Median nerve injury
C. Volkmann's ischemic contracture
D. Cubitus Varus
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Non-union

Supracondylar fractures in children heal rapidly. Non-union is very rare. Malunion (Cubitus Varus/Gunstock deformity) and nerve injuries are common.

14.
Earliest symptom of Volkmann's ischemia is;
A. Absent pulse
B. Pain in flexor muscles
C. Pain on passive extension
D. Cyanosis of limb
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Pain on passive extension

Pain on passive stretch of the fingers is the earliest and most reliable sign of compartment syndrome (ischemia). Pulse is usually present until very late.

15.
Anterior dislocation of shoulder causes all the following except
A. Circumflex artery injury
B. Brachial plexus injury
C. Chip fracture of scapula
D. Avascular necrosis of head of femur
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Avascular necrosis of head of femur

Head of femur is in the hip. Shoulder dislocation involves the humerus. Circumflex (Axillary) nerve injury is common; circumflex artery injury is possible though rare.

16.
Which is a pathognomic sign of a traumatic fracture
A. Swelling
B. Redness
C. Pain
D. Crepitus
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Crepitus

Crepitus (grating sound/sensation) and abnormal movement are definitive (pathognomonic) signs of a fracture. Pain and swelling are non-specific.

17.

The periosteum is richly innervated with nociceptors (pain receptors), making fractures extremely painful.

21.
Which of the following complicate is considered an immediate complication of fractures?

22.
Which of the following induction agents increases ICP?
A. Propofol
B. Ketamine
C. Thiopentone
D. Fentanyl
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Ketamine

Ketamine increases cerebral blood flow and intracranial pressure, unlike Propofol and Thiopentone which decrease them.

23.
Which is not a complication of succinylcholine
A. Increased inatragastric pressure

Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Hypotension

Succinylcholine is more typically associated with Bradycardia (especially in children) or Hypertension. It causes Histamine release, increased intragastric/intraocular pressure, and is a trigger for Malignant Hyperthermia.

30.
The ----the MAC the---- the drug
A. Higher, more potent
B. Lower, less potent
C. Lower, more potent
D. MAC has no effect on potency
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Lower, more potent

MAC (Minimum Alveolar Concentration) is inversely proportional to potency. A drug with a low MAC (e.g., Halothane 0.75%) is more potent than one with a high MAC (e.g., Desflurane 6%).

Section B: Short Answer Questions (30 Marks)

1.
Briefly explain the 4 stages of general anesthesia. (6 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Stage I (Analgesia): From induction to loss of consciousness. Patient feels dizzy, pain sensation is reduced.
  2. Stage II (Excitement/Delirium): From loss of consciousness to onset of automatic breathing. Characterized by irregular breathing, increased muscle tone, struggling, and potential vomiting/laryngospasm. This stage is dangerous and should be passed through quickly.
  3. Stage III (Surgical Anesthesia): From onset of regular breathing to respiratory paralysis.
    • Plane 1: Regular breathing, pupils constrict, eyelid reflex lost.
    • Plane 2: Eyes fixed, respiration regular. Ideal for surgery.
    • Plane 3: Diaphragmatic breathing mostly, pupils dilate.
    • Plane 4: Apnea, flaccidity.
  4. Stage IV (Overdose/Medullary Paralysis): Respiratory and vasomotor centers are depressed. Apnea, hypotension, dilated non-reactive pupils, death if not reversed.
2.
Classify fractures. (6 Marks)
Show Answer

Fractures can be classified based on various criteria:

  1. Etiology:
    • Traumatic (Sudden force).
    • Pathological (Weak bone e.g., tumor, cyst).
    • Stress/Fatigue (Repetitive minor trauma).
  2. Skin Integrity:
    • Closed (Simple): Skin intact.
    • Open (Compound): Bone communicates with external environment.
  3. Pattern: Transverse, Oblique, Spiral, Comminuted, Greenstick.
  4. Displacement: Undisplaced vs Displaced (Angulated, Rotated, Shortened).
  5. Completeness: Complete vs Incomplete.
  6. Anatomical Location: Epiphyseal, Metaphyseal, Diaphyseal.
3.
What are the benefits and risks of spinal anesthesia? (6 Marks)
Show Answer

Benefits:

  • Patient remains conscious (good for C-section, elderly).
  • Excellent muscle relaxation.
  • Decreased blood loss (hypotension reduces bleeding).
  • Post-operative analgesia.
  • Reduced risk of aspiration compared to GA.

Risks/Complications:

  • Hypotension (Sympathetic blockade).
  • Post-dural puncture headache (PDPH).
  • High spinal (Respiratory paralysis if block goes too high).
  • Urinary retention.
  • Infection (Meningitis/Abscess).
  • Nerve injury (rare).
4.
Contrast and compare different methods of fracture management including surgical and non-surgical approaches. (6 Marks)
Show Answer

Non-Surgical (Conservative):

  • Methods: Casting (POP), Splinting, Skin/Skeletal Traction, Functional bracing.
  • Pros: No surgical risk (infection/anesthesia), preserves hematoma.
  • Cons: Prolonged immobilization (stiffness, DVT), less precise reduction, malunion risk.
  • Indications: Undisplaced fractures, children, stable fractures.

Surgical (Operative):

  • Methods: ORIF (Plates/Screws), IM Nailing, External Fixation, K-wires.
  • Pros: Accurate anatomical reduction, early mobilization, stable fixation.
  • Cons: Risk of infection, anesthesia risk, expensive, scars.
  • Indications: Displaced intra-articular fractures, open fractures, polytrauma, femoral shaft #.
5.
Describe the tile classification of pelvic fractures. (6 Marks)
Show Answer

Type A (Stable): Posterior arch intact.

  • A1: Avulsion fractures.
  • A2: Iliac wing or anterior arch fractures (undisplaced).
  • A3: Transverse sacrococcygeal #.

Type B (Rotationally Unstable, Vertically Stable): Incomplete disruption of posterior arch.

  • B1: Open book (External rotation).
  • B2: Lateral compression (Internal rotation).
  • B3: Bilateral B-type injuries.

Type C (Rotationally and Vertically Unstable): Complete disruption of posterior arch.

  • C1: Unilateral.
  • C2: Bilateral (one side B, one side C).
  • C3: Bilateral complete disruption.

Section C: Long Answer Questions (40 Marks)

1.
Explain the post-operative care of a patient who had an explorative laparotomy due to intestinal obstruction.
Show Answer

Immediate Post-Op (0-24 hrs):

  • Monitoring: Vitals (BP, Pulse, RR, SpO2, Urine output) q15min -> q1h. Monitor for shock/hemorrhage.
  • Fluids: Maintain IV fluids (Hartmann's/NS) to replace deficit and maintenance. Replace NG losses.
  • Analgesia: Adequate pain control (Opioids e.g., Pethidine/Morphine + NSAIDs/Paracetamol) to facilitate breathing.
  • Naso-Gastric Tube: Keep on free drainage/aspiration to decompress stomach until bowel sounds return.
  • Antibiotics: Continue prophylactic/therapeutic antibiotics (e.g., Ceftriaxone + Metronidazole).
  • DVT Prophylaxis: LMWH (Clexane) and compression stockings.

Intermediate (Day 1-3):

  • Mobilization: Encourage early ambulation to prevent DVT and chest infection. Chest physiotherapy.
  • Wound Care: Inspect dressing for soakage. Monitor drain output (color/volume).
  • Bowel Function: Monitor for return of bowel sounds and flatus. Keep NPO until then.
  • Labs: Check U&Es (hypokalemia from ileus) and Hb.

Late (Day 4+):

  • Diet: Start sips of water upon return of flatus -> clear fluids -> soft diet -> solid diet.
  • Drain Removal: Remove drains when output is minimal. Remove NG tube when aspirate is low and bowel sounds active.
  • Suture Removal: Typically day 10-14.
  • Discharge Planning: Verify tolerance of oral diet, wound healing, and pain control.
2.
Describe the process of fracture healing.
Show Answer

Fracture healing (Secondary healing) occurs in 5 stages:

  1. Hematoma Formation (Days 1-5):
    • Blood vessels rupture forming a clot around the fracture.
    • Inflammatory cells (macrophages, neutrophils) migrate to the site.
    • Osteocytes at the fracture ends die due to ischemia.
  2. Soft Callus Formation (Days 5-14):
    • Fibroblasts and chondroblasts invade the hematoma.
    • Formation of granulation tissue and fibrocartilage bridge (soft callus).
    • Fracture becomes sticky but not rigid.
  3. Hard Callus Formation (Weeks 2-6):
    • Osteoblasts mineralize the soft callus via endochondral ossification.
    • Woven bone (immature) is formed.
    • Fracture becomes clinically united (non-tender, no movement).
  4. Remodeling (Months to Years):
    • Woven bone is replaced by Lamellar bone (stronger) by osteoclasts and osteoblasts.
    • Medullary canal is restored.
    • Bone is reshaped according to stress lines (Wolff's Law).

Note: Primary healing (without callus) occurs only with rigid internal fixation and compression.
