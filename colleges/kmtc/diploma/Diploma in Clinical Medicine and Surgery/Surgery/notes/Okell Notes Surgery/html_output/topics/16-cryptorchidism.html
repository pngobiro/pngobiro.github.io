CRYPTORCHIDISM

Definition

Cryptorchidism is the most common genital problem encountered in pediatrics, refers to an un-descended or mal-descended testis.

Testicular Embryology

Normal testicular development begins at conception. The testis-determining factor is now identified as the SRY gene (sex-determining region on Y chromosome).

  • 3-5 weeks: Gonadal ridge or indifferent gonad develops.
  • 6 weeks: Primordial germ cell migration occurs. Sertoli cells develop and secrete müllerian-inhibiting substance (MIS), causing regression of müllerian ducts.
  • 9 weeks: Leydig cells develop and secrete testosterone.
  • Up to 28 weeks: Transabdominal movement to the internal inguinal ring.
  • 28-40 weeks: Transinguinal migration, thought to be under hormonal control, occurs, usually resulting in a scrotal testis by the end of a full term of gestation.

Occurrence

Seen in 3% of full-term newborn boys, decreasing to 1% in boys aged 1 year. Spontaneous descent after the first year of life is uncommon.

Etiology & Risk Factors

Predisposing Factors

  • Prematurity: The prevalence rate is 30% in premature boys.
  • Low birth weight
  • Twinning
  • Maternal exposure to estrogen during the first trimester.
  • Siblings of boys with undescended testes (A 7% incidence rate is seen).

Etiology

The multifactorial mechanism of occurrence involves:

  1. Differential body growth relative to spermatic cord/gubernaculums
  2. Increased abdominal pressure
  3. Hormonal factors, including testosterone, MIS, and extrinsic estrogen
  4. Development/maturation of the epididymis
  5. Gubernacular attachment
  6. Genitofemoral nerve/calcitonin gene-related peptide (CGRP)

Clinical Classification

The most useful classification is whether testes are palpable upon physical examination.

  • Non-palpable: May be intra-abdominal or absent (20-30%). The absent testis is thought to occur from an intrauterine or perinatal vascular event. Only 20-40% of nonpalpable testes are absent upon surgical exploration.
    • Abdominal: 10%
  • Palpable: May be undescended, ectopic, or retractile.
    • Inguinal: 68%
    • Prescrotal: 24%
    • Ectopic/Superficial inguinal pouch: 11.5%
  • Bilateral: 30%

Associated Anomalies/Conditions

  • Patent processus vaginalis
  • Abnormal epididymis (32-79%)
  • Cerebral palsy / Mental retardation
  • Wilms tumor
  • Abdominal wall defects (gastroschisis, omphalocele, prune belly)
  • Hypospadias
  • Inguinal hernia
  • Hemiscrotum / Hydrocele
  • Klinefelter's syndrome
  • Hypogonadotropic hypogonadism

Diagnosis

History

  • Determination of whether the testis has ever been palpable in scrotum
  • History of prior inguinal surgery
  • Prenatal history (assisted reproductive technique, maternal hormonal treatment, multiple gestations, prematurity)
  • Family history (cryptorchidism, hypospadias, intersex, precocious puberty, infertility, consanguinity)

Physical Examination

  • Warm, relaxed patient
  • Observation prior to examination
  • Frog-leg position
  • Milk down, palpating from iliac crest to scrotum (soap or lubrication on fingertips may help)
  • Scrotum features: hypoplastic, bifid, rugae, transposition, pigmentation
  • Contralateral testicular hypertrophy
  • Search ectopic sites: superficial inguinal pouch, transverse scrotal, femoral, prepenile, perineal, or contralateral hemiscrotum
  • Presence of hypospadias/chordee, normal stretched penile length
  • Serial examinations, if equivocal

Indications for Correction

  1. Possible improved fertility
  2. Self-examination for testis mass (cancer)
  3. Correction of associated hernia
  4. Prevention of testicular torsion
  5. Avoidance of injury against pubic bone
  6. Psychological effects of empty scrotum

Investigations

Lab Studies

For unilateral undescended testis without hypospadias, no lab studies are needed.

For unilateral or bilateral undescended testes with hypospadias or bilateral nonpalpable testes, tests include:

  • Testing to rule out intersex condition (mandatory)
  • 17-hydroxylase progesterone
  • Testosterone
  • Luteinizing hormone (LH)
  • Follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH)

Imaging Studies

Abdominopelvic ultrasonography to determine the location of ectopic testis and rule out other associated abnormalities in the upper urinary tracts.

MANAGEMENT

Timing

The appropriate time for treatment is when the individual is aged approximately 1 year. This is based on the rarity of spontaneous descent after age 1 year and the possible salvage of improved fertility by earlier intervention.

Medical Therapy

Primary hormonal therapy with hCG or gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH).

  • Human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG): Stimulates Leydig cells to produce androgens.
  • Gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH): Stimulates pituitary LH and FSH release, causing temporary increase of gonadal steroidogenesis.

Surgery

Non-palpable testis

Exploratory laparatomy and orchidopexy. Usually surgery should be done before the age of 2 years as past this age structural changes are noticed to occur in the testis which compromise the blood testis barrier and this lead to auto immunity.

Palpable testis (Standard Orchidopexy)

  1. Incision over the inguinal canal along the Langer lines.
  2. Care taken when incising Scarpa fascia (testis may be in superficial inguinal pouch).
  3. Mobilization: Divide distal gubernacular attachments and cremasteric muscle fibers.
  4. Separate cord structures from peritoneum above internal inguinal ring during ligation of the hernia sac.
  5. Divide lateral spermatic fascia to allow medial movement.
  6. Perform high ligation of the patent processus vaginalis.
  7. Relocate the testis into the scrotum in a subcutaneous or subdartos pouch.

Complications of Orchidopexy

  1. Inadequate testis position (most common).
  2. Testicular atrophy (5%) due to devascularization.
  3. Accidental division of the vas deferens (1-2%).
  4. Epididymoorchitis.
  5. Scrotal swelling (edema, bleeding/hematoma).
  6. Higher risk (20-46 times) of testicular cancer (risk may remain despite orchiopexy).
  7. Hernia development (25%).

Cancer Risk

Orchidopexy is not protective against subsequent testis cancer. Testicular tumors after previously successful orchidopexy most frequently are nonseminomatous germ cell tumors, while abdominal testes may develop seminomas.
