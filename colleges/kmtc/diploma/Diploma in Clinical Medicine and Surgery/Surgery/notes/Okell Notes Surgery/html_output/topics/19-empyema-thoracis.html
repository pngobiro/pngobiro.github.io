EMPYEMA THORACIS

Definition & Classification

Definition

Empyema is a localized or free collection of purulent material in the pleural space. It is an advanced Para pneumonic effusion.

Para pneumonic Pleural Effusions Classification

  1. Uncomplicated pleural effusion: Exudative predominantly neutrophilic effusion that occurs as the lung interstitial fluid increases during pneumonia. These effusions are resolved with appropriate antibiotic treatment of pneumonia.
  2. Complicated pleural effusion: Bacterial invasion of the pleural space that leads to an increased number of neutrophils, pleural fluid acidosis, and elevated lactic dehydrogenase (LDH) concentration. These effusions often are sterile because bacteria are usually cleared rapidly from the pleural space.
  3. Thoracic empyema: Characterized by either aspiration of pus on thoracentesis or the presence of bacterial organisms on Gram stain. A positive culture is not required for diagnosis.

Etiology

Predisposing Factors

  • Pulmonary infection (Unresolved pneumonia - Acute bacterial)
  • Pulmonary tuberculosis, mycotic infections
  • Bronchiectasis
  • Lung abscess
  • Subphrenic abscess
  • Trauma (Penetrating injury chest, Esophageal perforation)
  • Iatrogenic (postoperative infection, thoracocentesis, thoracotomy)
  • Generalized sepsis
  • Adjacent infections (Retropharyngeal, Mediastinal abscess, Osteomyelitis of ribs, Para vertebral abscess)

Bacteriology

  • Tubercular empyema
  • Nontubercular empyema: Staphylococcus aureus, Pneumococci, E. coli, Pseudomonas, Klebsiella, and anaerobes.

Pathophysiology (Stages)

American Thoracic Society identifies 3-stage development of empyema:

1. Exudative (acute) stage

Pleural inflammation results in increased permeability and a small fluid collection. The fluid is thin, contains few cellular elements mostly neutrophils, and is often sterile. Lasts 24-72 hours.

2. Fibrinopurulent (transitional) stage

Invasion of the organism into the pleural space with progressive inflammation and PMN invasion. Accumulation of protein and fibrinous material with formation of fibrin membranes (loculations/partitions). Decreased pleural fluid glucose and pH; increased protein and LDH. Lasts 7-10 days.

3. Organizing (chronic) stage

A thick pleural peel is formed by resorption of fluid and fibroblast proliferation. Lung parenchyma becomes entrapped (fibrothorax). Usually occurs within 2-4 weeks after presentation.

Clinical Presentation

History

Most patients present with clinical manifestations of bacterial pneumonia:

  • Acute febrile response
  • Pleuritic chest pain (Splinting of affected side)
  • Cough
  • Dyspnea
  • Hemoptysis

Severe cases: Toxic, comatose, cyanotic, hypotensive, dehydrated. Inflammation may cause abdominal pain and vomiting.

Physical Examination

Features of pleural effusion: Crackles, decreased air entry, and stony dullness.
Cyanosis, dehydration, and hypotension in toxic patients.

Differential Diagnosis

  • Heart Failure, Congestive
  • Hemothorax
  • Nephrotic Syndrome
  • Pulmonary Infarction / Sequestration / Abscess

Investigations

Laboratory

  1. FBC: WBC counts usually >15,000/dl (predominantly PMN's).
  2. Thoracocentesis: (Ideally before antibiotics)
    • Gram stain and AFB
    • Culture and sensitivity (Bacterial, mycobacterial, fungal)
    • Pleural fluid latex agglutination
    • Biochemistry: Glucose, LDH, pH, protein, amylase.
  3. Blood cultures
  4. Sputum (AFB, cultures)

Imaging

  1. CXR: Features of pleural effusion (obliteration of costophrenic angle, fluid level, diffuse opacity). Severe effusion may show mediastinal displacement.
  2. Ultrasound: For septation of fluids.
  3. Chest CT scan: Study of choice for complex fluid collections. Detects loculations and pleural peel.

MANAGEMENT

Objectives: Control infection, Drainage of purulent fluid, Eradication of the sac to allow lung re-expansion.

Medical Care

  • General: High protein/fluid intake, Physiotherapy/Breathing exercises, NSAIDS.
  • Antibiotics: Empiric selection based on common pathogens/age/history. IV for 10-14 days until afebrile, followed by oral for 1-3 weeks.

Interventional Procedures

1. Thoracentesis

Diagnostic and therapeutic. Sufficient for thin pus/small volume (e.g., Streptococcal infection) with pH >7.2, Glucose >40 mg/dl, LDH <1000 IU/l.

Tubercular empyema: Start anti-TB chemotherapy and aspirate pus. Course: 6-9 months.

2. Tube Thoracostomy

Chest tube drainage with underwater seal for Stage 2 (Fibrinopurulent). Effective in >50% of patients if fluid is free-flowing.

3. Interventional Radiology

Image-guided placement of small-bore catheters for loculated collections. Fibrinolytics (urokinase, streptokinase, TPA) may be used to break down fibrin.

Surgical Intervention

Indications: Chronic empyema, multiloculated empyema, failure of tube drainage, entrapped lung.

  1. Rib Resection: Necessary if pus is thick and loculated or patient remains toxic. Provides exposure to evacuate pus and break adhesions.
  2. Open Drainage: For chronic cases with thick pus (e.g., chronic tubercular empyema after 3-5 weeks of drainage). Wide bore tube left open to atmosphere.
  3. Thoracotomy and Decortication: Removal of the organized inflammatory membrane (pleural peel). Very effective (95% success) for fibrinopurulent effusions.
  4. Video-Assisted Thoracoscopic Surgery (VATS): Less invasive replacement for limited decortication. Not suitable for late stage 3 with thick pleura.
  5. Lobectomy and Pneumonectomy: Required if lung does not expand due to necrosis, or for preventing flooding of the other lung.

Indications for Resection in Tubercular Empyema

  • Resistant tuberculosis (severe cavitation, bronchiectasis, stenosis).
  • Mass lesion in area of tubercular involvement.
  • Massive life-threatening hemoptysis.
  • Broncho-pleural fistula unresponsive to tube thoracostomy.

Complications

  • Empyema necessitans: Swelling over chest wall communicating with empyema (positive cough impulse).
  • Fibrothorax: Rare complication in late presentation.
  • Broncho-pleural fistula (BPF)
  • Long-term effects: Restrictive lung disease.
