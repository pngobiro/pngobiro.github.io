CHRONIC LEG ULCERS

Introduction & Etiology

It is vital that any patient with leg ulceration has a full assessment. Lack of assessment can lead to long periods of inappropriate and potentially dangerous treatment (e.g., applying compression bandaging to an arterial ulcer).

Etiology

The most common cause of leg ulceration is tropical ulcers/venous ulcers. Damage to valves within the veins results in reflux, venous hypertension, edema, and leakage of blood cells/fibrin into tissues causing lipodermatosclerosis. These tissues are prone to injury and slow to heal.

Other Causes

  • Arterial insufficiency
  • Infections (Tropical ulcers)
  • Blood disorders (Sickle cell anaemia)
  • Neoplasms (Squamous cell carcinoma, Basal cell carcinoma)
  • Trauma
  • Neuropathic (Diabetes)
  • Mixed arterial and venous insufficiency
  • Vasculitis (Rheumatoid arthritis, SLE)
  • Lymphoedema
  • Factitious injury (rare)
  • Pyoderma gangrenosum

Assessment

History

  • Ulcer Details: Onset, location, number, discharge, bleeding, pain, pruritus, odour, healing/recurrence history.
  • Associated Symptoms: Leg swelling, fever, weight loss, chronic cough (TB), loss of sensation.
  • Past Medical History: DVT, fractures, diabetes, hypertension, sickle cell disease.
  • Risk Factors: Smoking, drugs.

Physical Examination

Ulcer Examination

  • Site and number
  • Size and Shape
  • Edges: Undermined (TB/Buruli), Overhanging (Malignant), Sloping (Venous), Punched out (Arterial/Syphilitic).
  • Floor: Clean, necrotic, granulation tissue, bleeding.
  • Base: Induration, tenderness, mobility.
  • Discharge: Purulent, serous, bloody.
  • Surrounding Skin: Eczema, maceration, blistering.

Leg Examination

  • Oedema, Varicosities, Lipodermatosclerosis (champagne bottle leg), Temperature.

Systemic Exam

  • Pulses: Dorsalis pedis, posterior tibial, popliteal, femoral. Measure ABPI.
  • Lymphadenopathy: Inguinal nodes (infection or malignancy).
  • Neurological: Sensory exam (neuropathy).

Clinical Features Comparison

Venous Disease Arterial Disease
Usually shallow ulcers (gaiter area) "Punched out" deep ulcers
Oedema present Base poorly perfused/pale
Eczema / Ankle flare Cold legs/feet
Lipodermatosclerosis (Hardening) Shiny, taut skin / Loss of hair
Hyperpigmentation (Hemosiderin) Dependent rubor / Pale on elevation
Varicose veins often present Gangrenous toes / Absent pulses
Less painful (unless infected) Very painful (Claudication/Rest pain)

Investigations & Management

Investigations

  1. FHG: Anemia, Infections, Leukemia.
  2. Doppler Ultrasound: Measure Ankle Brachial Pressure Index (ABPI). Essential to rule out arterial disease before compression.
  3. Blood/Urine Glucose: Diabetes screen.
  4. Microbiology: Swab for M/C/S.
  5. Biopsy: If malignant change suspected (Marjolin's ulcer) or chronic non-healing.
  6. Radiology (X-ray): To rule out osteomyelitis.

Management

Principles:

  1. Control Infection: Systemic antibiotics based on sensitivity.
  2. Promote Healing: Excision of indolent granulation/fibrosis/slough (Debridement). Correction of contractures.
  3. Epithelial Cover: Split-thickness skin graft once bed is granulating.
  4. Treat Underlying Cause: Compression for venous (if arterial status okay), revascularization for arterial, glycemic control for diabetic.

Malignant Transformation (Marjolin's Ulcer)

Signs: Rolled up/everted edges, bleeding, rapid growth, chronic history (burns scar).

Treatment: Wide excision and skin grafting for small lesions. Amputation for large lesions with extensive bone invasion.
