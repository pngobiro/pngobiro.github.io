CLEFT LIP AND PALATE

Overview

Are part of Orofacial clefts / defect anomalies:

  • Cleft lip [CL]
  • Cleft lip and palate [CLP]
  • Cleft palate [CP] alone
  • Median, lateral [transversal], oblique facial clefts

Prevalence & Demographics

Are among the most common congenital anomalies. Approximately 1 case of orofacial cleft occurs in every 500-550 births.

  • From a genetic standpoint, cleft lip with or without cleft palate is distinct from isolated cleft palate.
  • Sex: Cleft lip more common in males, the cleft palate in females.
  • Race: Racial variation exists. Incidence per 1000 live births: Asians (1.61), Whites (0.9), Blacks (0.31).

Clinical Findings & Problems

  • A cleft lip may be unilateral or bilateral and complete or incomplete.
  • It may occur with a cleft of the entire palate or just the primary (anterior and gingival ridge) or secondary (posterior) palate.
  • An isolated cleft palate can involve only the soft palate or both the soft and hard palates (V-shaped or wide horseshoe cleft).
  • Pierre Robin syndrome: A cleft associated with micrognathia and glossoptosis (a tongue that falls back and causes respiratory or feeding problems). High incidence of congenital heart disease (15%).
  • Associated Anomalies: Increased incidence of other congenital abnormalities, more common in isolated cleft palate. Associated abnormalities should be identified in the period immediately after birth and before surgery.

Problems associated with facial clefts

  1. Early feeding difficulties, which may be severe.
  2. Recurrent serous otitis media associated with fluctuating hearing and language delays.
  3. Speech problems, including hypernasality and articulation errors.
  4. Dental and orthodontic complications.

Management Protocol

Long-term management should ideally be through a multidisciplinary cleft palate clinic. Gaining weight and preventing aspiration and ear infections are the most important parts of caring for neonates with a cleft during their first days and weeks of life.

Common Treatment Timeline:

Age/Stage Intervention
Newborn
  • Diagnostic examination
  • General counseling of parents
  • Feeding instructions
  • Palatal obturator (if necessary)
  • Genetic evaluation & recurrence risk calculation
3 months Repair of Cleft Lip (and placement of ventilation tubes)
6 months Presurgical orthodontics (if necessary); first speech evaluation
9 months Speech therapy begins
9-12 months Repair of Cleft Palate (placement of ventilation tubes if not done at CL repair)
1-7 years Orthodontic treatment
7-8 years Alveolar bone graft
> 8 years Orthodontic treatment continues

Surgical Therapy

Undoubtedly, closure of the CL is the first major procedure that tremendously changes children's future development and ability to thrive.
Variations occur in timing of the first lip surgery; however, the most usual time occurs at approximately age 3 months.

The Rule of "Three 10s"

Pediatricians used to strictly follow this rule for identifying the child's status as suitable for surgery (though presently more flexible):

  • 10 lb (Weight)
  • 10 mg/dL (Hemoglobin)
  • 10 weeks (Age)

Multidisciplinary Team

Pediatrician, Nurse practitioner, Plastic surgeon, Pediatric dentist, Otolaryngologist.

Feeding Instructions

Breastfeeding an infant with a cleft

  • Isolated CL: Typically does not experience problems beyond learning how to "latch on".
  • CP: Infants must squeeze the milk out by compressing the nipple between the tongue and remaining palate (cannot create suction).
  • Massage breast and apply hot packs 20 mins before nursing.
  • Hold infant in semi-upright, straddle, or football position. Support the breast, ensuring the lower lip is turned out.
  • If the infant cannot hold onto the nipple, collect remaining milk via pump or hand expression and finish feeding with a bottle.

Feeding breast milk with a bottle

  • Particularly for infants with bilateral CLP, breastfeeding is often not possible.
  • Mother can use a breast pump and feed via bottle.

Other recommendations

  • Position: More upright or seated positions prevent milk from leaking to the nose and causing choking.
  • Nasal Regurgitation: Stop feeding and allow infant to cough or sneeze for a few seconds.
  • A palatal obturator may be used.
