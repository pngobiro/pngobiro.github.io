BREAST CANCER: DIAGNOSIS AND MANAGEMENT

Introduction

  • It is the commonest cause of cancer death in women
  • It accounts for 6% of all female death

Diagnosis

Diagnosis is by Triple Assessment:

  1. Clinical Evaluation: Lump and regional nodes
  2. Imaging: Ultrasound (< 35 years old) or Mammography (> 35 years old)
  3. Cytology or Histology: FNA or Core Biopsy

Clinical Presentation

History

Presenting complaints

  • Painless breast lump: more than 70% of patients.
  • Painful breast lumps: due to ischaemia/necrosis, inflammatory carcinoma, lymphedema, local invasion (nerves/muscles), or infection.
  • Mass rapid growth / Changes with menses.
  • Nipple discharge.
  • Ulceration, irritation, or eczematous skin change.

Advanced disease symptoms

  • Chronic cough (lung metastasis)
  • Weight loss, anorexia, generalized fatigue
  • Upper limb swelling (axillary lymph node involvement)
  • Back pain (metastasis to spine)
  • Yellowness of eyes (Jaundice)

Predisposing Factors (Risk Factors)

  1. Breast cancer in the contra lateral breast
  2. Benign breast disease with hyperplasia and atypia / multiple papillomas
  3. Advancing age > 50 years (Most ca 35-45 years)
  4. Family history of breast cancer, GIT or ovarian cancer
  5. Nulliparity
  6. Early menarche (< 13) and late menopause (> 50)
  7. Age at first delivery (If > 30 years, risk is 2x that of < 20 years)
  8. Oral contraceptives
  9. Hormonal replacement therapy (HRT)
  10. Whether breast fed and Length of breastfeeding
  11. Irradiation to chest (therapeutic or occupational)
  12. Obesity
  13. Cigarette smoking and Alcohol consumption
  14. Diet (Fatty diet)

Physical Examination

Inspection

  • Symmetry of the breasts, sizes, and Level of the nipples.
  • Nature of the nipples (everted/inverted, discharges).
  • Skin changes: ulcers, hyperpigmentation, peau d'orange.

Signs suggestive of carcinoma

Obvious size discrepancy, nipple inversion, skin dimpling, scaling, and edema (peau d'orange).

Palpation

  • Start from the normal side.
  • Examine all quadrants (lower outer, lower inner, upper inner, upper outer) and the axillary tail.
  • Describe masses: Site, size, shape, temperature, skin changes, tenderness, margins, mobility/attachments, consistency.
  • Squeeze nipple for discharge.

Lymph Nodes

Examine axillary nodes (apical, lateral, posterior, anterior) and supraclavicular nodes.

Differential Diagnosis

  • Giant fibroadenoma
  • Fibrosarcoma
  • Deep breast mycosis
  • Chronic breast abscess (e.g., TB)
  • Secondary malignancies (melanoma, lung)
  • Cystosarcoma phyllodes

Investigations

Imaging

Mammography

Most useful for detection of early breast cancer. Sensitivity decreased in young patients with dense tissue. Suggestive findings: stellate shape, irregular/spiculated margins, pleomorphic calcifications.

BI-RADS Classification
0Incomplete examination (requires further imaging)
INormal (follow-up in 1 year)
IIBenign (follow-up in 1 year)
IIILikely to be benign (follow-up in 6 months)
IVSuspicious (Consider biopsy)
VHighly suspicious for malignancy (Biopsy recommended)

Ultrasound

Useful in younger patients (< 35) or women with fibrocystic change. Distinguishes solid from cystic lesions.

Diagnostic Procedures

  • Fine-needle aspiration (FNA): First-line for palpable mass/cysts.
  • True-cut (core) needle biopsy: Gives architectural information and invasiveness. Distinct advantage over FNA.
  • Receptor Status: Estrogen (ER) and Progesterone (PR) receptors (for Tamoxifen benefit). Also BRCA 1/2.

Other Lab Investigations

FHG, LFT, U/E/C, Tumour markers (CEA, CA 15-3, CA 27.29), Chest x-ray.

Histology & Staging

WHO Classification

A) Epithelial

  • Non-invasive: Ductal carcinoma in situ (DCIS), Lobular carcinoma in situ (LCIS)
  • Invasive: Ductal (85%), Lobular (1%), Mucinous (5%), Papillary (<5%), Medullary (<5%)

TNM Staging

T (Tumor)T1: ≤ 2cm
T2: > 2cm but ≤ 5cm
T3: > 5cm
T4: Extension to chest wall/skin
N (Nodes)N0: None
N1: Mobile ipsilateral axillary
N2: Fixed ipsilateral axillary
N3: Ipsilateral supraclavicular/internal mammary
M (Metastasis)M0: None
M1: Distant metastases

Manchester Staging System

  • Stage I: Tumor confined to breast. Skin involvement < tumor size.
  • Stage II: Confined to breast + Palpable mobile axillary nodes.
  • Stage III: Extends beyond breast (skin fixation > tumor size, ulceration, fixity to fascia).
  • Stage IV: Distant metastases.

MANAGEMENT

Supportive

Hydration, Hematinics/transfusion, Analgesia, Nutritional build up, Antibiotics (if infected), Counselling.

Specific Management: Surgery

1. Breast Conserving Surgery (BCS)

Includes wide local excision (lumpectomy), quadrantectomy, or segmentectomy.

Indications: Small (< 5cm) single tumours in large breast, peripheral location, no local advancement.

Contraindications: Multicentricity, widespread multifocal disease, central tumours involving nipple, poor differentiation.

2. Mastectomy

Indications: Large tumours, central tumours, multifocal disease, local recurrence, palliative (toilet mastectomy), prophylaxis.

Types:

  • Total/Simple Mastectomy: Removal of breast parenchyma + nipple-areolar complex (no node dissection).
  • Modified Radical Mastectomy (MRM): Removal of breast + axillary nodes (Level I & II).
  • Patey MRM: Adds removal of Level III nodes + division of pectoralis minor.
  • Radical (Halsted): Obsolete. Removed breast, all nodes, and pectoralis major/minor.

Axillary Surgery

Sentinel Node Biopsy: Identifies first drainage node (radioisotope/blue dye). Avoids morbidity of full clearance if negative.

Axillary Clearance: Levels 1 (below pect. minor), 2 (behind), 3 (above/medial).

Radiotherapy

Used to clear breast bed of residual tumour (locoregional control).

Indications: After BCS (mandatory), extensive local disease, heavy node positive disease, bony metastasis (pain control).

Chemotherapy

Primary systemic (neoadjuvant) or Adjuvant (post-op).

  • CMF Regimen: Cyclophosphamide, Methotrexate, 5-Fluorouracil. (6 cycles monthly).
  • Greatest benefit in pre-menopausal women.

Hormonal Therapy

For ER/PR positive tumours.

  1. Tamoxifen (SERM): 20mg OD for 5 years. Reduces contralateral cancer risk by 40%. Side effects: Endometrial Ca, Thromboembolism.
  2. Aromatase Inhibitors (Anastrazole): For post-menopausal women.
  3. LHRH Analogues (Goserelin): Ovarian suppression in pre-menopausal women.
  4. Trastuzumab (Herceptin): Monoclonal antibody for HER2 positive tumours.

Special Conditions

Inflammatory Breast Cancer

Appearance of inflamed breasts (red, warm, dimpled/peau d'orange) due to infiltration of lymphatics. Aggressive. Treatment similar to Stage IIIB/IV (Chemo, Hormonal, Radiation, potentially Mastectomy if responsive).

Paget's Disease of the Nipple

Rare form beginning in milk ducts spreading to nipple/areola skin. Skin appears crusted, red, or oozing. Better prognosis if nipple changes are the only sign.

Recurrence

Local recurrence after lumpectomy: Mastectomy.

Recurrence after mastectomy: Surgery/Radiation + Systemic therapy.

Prevention & Prognosis

Prevention

  • Breast self-examination (monthly from age 18).
  • Annual mammography (> 40 years).
  • Tamoxifen for high-risk healthy women.
  • Lifestyle modification.
  • Prophylactic mastectomy (strong family history).

Prognostic Factors

Chronological

  • Age: Younger women have poorer prognosis.
  • Tumor size: Correlates directly with survival.
  • Lymph node status: Single best prognostic factor.
  • Metastases: Worsens survival.

Biological

  • Histological type: Tubular, mucinous have better prognosis.
  • Histological grade: Tubule formation, pleomorphism, mitotic frequency.
  • Receptor status: ER+ better prognosis.
