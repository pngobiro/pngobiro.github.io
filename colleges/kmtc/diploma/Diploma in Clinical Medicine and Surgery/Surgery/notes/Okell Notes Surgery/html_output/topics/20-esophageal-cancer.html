ESOPHAGEAL CANCER

Epidemiology & Risk Factors

Demographics

  • Sex Ratio: M:F = 25:1
  • Age: Average age 58.2 yrs (12-103 yrs).
  • Prevalent region: Central Nyanza province.

Predisposing Factors

Contribute to repeated long term minimal trauma.

A. Lifestyle & Diet

  • Smoking & Alcohol: Predispose to Squamous Cell Carcinoma (SCC).
  • Betel chewing.
  • Hot foods.
  • Charred meat, Smoked fish.
  • Deficiency of antioxidants (Selenium, Vit C, E, Retinoids).
  • Exposure to N-nitroso compounds (Nitrates & Nitrites).

B. Disorders of Esophagus

  • Achalasia.
  • Long standing oesophageal strictures (e.g. corrosive ingestion).
  • Post-irradiation.
  • Paterson-Brown-Kelly (Plummer-Vinson) Syndrome: Post cricoid web + Iron Deficiency Anemia.
  • Barrett's oesophagus: 44-fold increased risk of Adenocarcinoma if severe reflux for >10 yrs.

C. Genetics

  • Tylosis (Palmar hyperkeratosis).
  • Coeliac disease.
  • Epidermolysis bullosa.
  • P53 and RB genes.

Pathology & Staging

Types & Sites

  • Squamous cell carcinoma (SCC): Most common worldwide.
    • 20% - Upper ⅓
    • 50% - Middle ⅓
  • Adenocarcinoma: Most common in Western countries.
    • 30% - Lower ⅓
  • Oat cell carcinoma

Spread

  1. Local-regional: Submucosal infiltration (often discontinuous/satellite nodules), adjacent structures, regional lymph nodes.
  2. Systemic (Haematogenous): 10%. Mainly to Liver & Lungs.

TNM Staging

TisCarcinoma-in-situ
T1Invading lamina propria/submucosa
T2Invading muscularis propria
T3Invading adventitia
T4Invasion of adjacent structures
M1Distant metastasis (Includes Coeliac axis nodes for intrathoracic tumours)

Clinical Presentation

History (Symptomatology)

  1. Progressive dysphagia: Initially to solids then to liquids.
  2. Odynophagia: Painful swallowing (involvement of somatic structures).
  3. Regurgitation/Vomiting: No bile pigment (due to obstruction).
  4. Weight loss: Progressive, with anorexia and fatigue.
  5. Chest pain: Steady deep pain often indicates mediastinal invasion.
  6. Hoarseness: Recurrent laryngeal nerve involvement.
  7. Choking: Suggests Tracheoesophageal fistula (TOF).
  8. Respiratory: Cough, hemoptysis, dyspnea (pulmonary metastasis or aspiration).

Physical Examination

Usually non-revealing in early stages. In advanced disease:

  • General: Wasting, Dehydration, Anemia, Supraclavicular lymphadenopathy (Virchow's node).
  • Chest: Signs of aspiration pneumonia or effusion (TOF).

Investigations

Imaging

  1. Esophagoscopy and biopsy (Gold Standard): Visualization, measurement of length/distance, histological confirmation. Bronchoscopy may be added to check bronchial invasion.
  2. Barium swallow: If OGD not possible. Findings: Rat tail appearance, Proximal dilatation, Shouldering effects, Mucosal erosion.
  3. Endoscopic Ultrasonography (EUS): Best for depth of wall penetration and lymph node staging.
  4. CT-Scan: For local invasion, extension, and staging.
  5. CXR: Mediastinal widening, pleural effusion, lung nodules, tracheal deviation.

Laboratory

FHG (Anemia), U/E/C, LFTs (low albumin/protein in malnutrition, abnormal enzymes in liver mets).

MANAGEMENT

Goal: Cure (rare) or Palliation of dysphagia.

Resectability: Limited by direct spread to trachea/aorta, angulation of axis, TOF, hoarseness, or tumor > 10cm.

A. Curative Surgery (Subtotal Oesophagectomy)

1. Grey-Turner's Trans-hiatal Oesophagectomy

Indications: Upper-third or lower-third neoplasms.
Procedure: Laparotomy (mobilize stomach) + Left Neck incision (cervical anastomosis). Stomach brought up through posterior mediastinum. No thoracotomy.

2. Ivor Lewis (Laparotomy + Right Thoracotomy)

Indications: Mid- or lower-third lesions.
Procedure: Upper midline laparotomy (mobilize stomach) followed by Right thoracotomy. High intrathoracic anastomosis.

3. McKeown (3-Stage)

Procedure: Laparotomy + Right Thoracotomy + Neck Incision. Used for upper/middle 1/3 tumours or extensive nodal dissection.

4. Sweet's Left Thoraco-abdominal

Indications: Gastro-oesophageal junction (GOJ) or low carcinomas.
Procedure: Incision through 6th/7th interspace, diaphragm incised. Excellent exposure of GOJ.

B. Palliative Therapy (Advanced Disease T3/4)

For patients with unresectable disease to relieve dysphagia.

1. Intubation (Stenting)

For middle & lower ⅓ tumours.

  • Pulsion tubes (Endoscopic): Proctar Livingstone, Atkinson, Souttar. Pushed through tumor.
  • Traction tubes (Laparotomy): Celestin, Mousseau-Barbin. Pulled through stomach.

Complications of Tubes

Dislodgement, blockage (food/tumor), hemorrhage, perforation, pressure necrosis. Concomitant radiotherapy increases risk.

2. Other Modalities

  • Laser therapy: Vaporizes tumor core to open lumen.
  • Radiation therapy: Short-term palliation. Better for upper 1/3 SCC.
  • Chemotherapy: 5-FU, Cisplatin, Mitomycin.
  • Stenting: Expandable metal stents.

Prognosis

Resectable rate: 30%.
5-Year Survival Rate: 5-10%.
