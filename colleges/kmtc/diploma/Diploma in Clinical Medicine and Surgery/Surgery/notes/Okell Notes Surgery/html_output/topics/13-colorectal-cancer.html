COLORECTAL CANCER

Introduction

  • Ninety percent of colorectal cancers occur in patients over 50 years of age.
  • Adenomas precede carcinomas.
  • Early cancers are asymptomatic.
  • Fecal occult blood testing can detect preneoplastic lesions and early cancers.
  • Microcytic anemia in the elderly suggests the presence of colorectal cancer.
  • Hematochezia may be a sign of colorectal cancer (especially of the distal colon and rectum).
  • Weight loss and obstructive symptoms are late findings.

Diagnosis & Clinical Presentation

History - Symptoms

  1. Per rectal Bleeding: Most common symptom of rectal cancer (60%). Higher tumors may present with occult bleeding. Bleeding mixed with stool, usually no heavy bleeding. May be mucoid.
  2. Change in bowel habit: Often diarrhea (especially if large villous component), or alternating constipation and diarrhea.
  3. Incomplete evacuation: Tumors low in rectum cause tenesmus.
  4. Abdominal pain: Colicky pain and bloating from partial obstruction.
  5. Back pain: Late sign (nerve invasion/compression).
  6. Urinary symptoms: Invasion of bladder or prostate.
  7. Constitutional signs: Malaise, weight loss, night sweat, anorexia.
  8. Bowel obstruction: Complete obstruction of large bowel is rare.
  9. Emergencies: Peritonitis from perforation (3%) or jaundice from liver metastases (<1%).

Predisposing Factors

  1. Diet: Fatty diet. High-fiber, calcium, selenium, vitamins A, C, E are protective.
  2. Alcohol: Daily drinkers have 2-fold increased risk.
  3. Cigarette smoking
  4. Pelvic radiation
  5. Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD):
    • Ulcerative colitis: Incidence increases with duration. Dysplasia is precursor.
    • Crohn disease: 4-20 times greater risk. Cancers often develop in strictures/fistulas.
  6. Genetic predispositions:
    • Hereditary nonpolyposis colorectal cancer (HNPCC): Defective mismatch repair genes (Chr 2, 3, 7).
    • Familial adenomatous polyposis coli (FAP): Defect in APC gene (Chr 5). Hundreds of polyps, osteomas, desmoid tumors. 100% cancer risk by age 40 if untreated.

Pathology & Spread

Gross Morphology

  • Cauliflower: Least malignant form.
  • Annular: Tends to cause obstructive symptoms.
  • Ulcerative: Commonly presents with bleeding.
  • Tubular

Histology

Mostly Adenocarcinomas (well or poorly differentiated glandular structures).
Normal topological architecture (crypt-villous axis) is lost.
Anorectal lesions have a squamous morphology.

Spread

  1. Local spread: Submucosal infiltration.
  2. Lymphatic spread (90%):
    • N1: Epicolic nodes (immediate vicinity of bowel wall).
    • N2: Paracolic nodes (along colic arteries).
    • N3: Apical nodes (around superior/inferior mesenteric vessels) → Virchow's node (Left supraclavicular).
  3. Haematogenous (10%): Liver (portal system), Lung, Bone.
  4. Transcoelomic
  5. Iatrogenic

Investigations

Lab Studies

  • CBC: Anaemia (microcytic).
  • LFT and U/E/C: Liver function tests often normal even with metastases.
  • CEA (Carcinoembryonic antigen): Baseline and follow-up. >100 ng/mL usually indicates metastasis.

Imaging

  1. Proctosigmoidoscopy/Colonoscopy & Biopsy: Direct visualization, size estimation, biopsy. Distance from dentate line is critical for surgical decision.
  2. Double contrast barium enema: 'Apple core' appearance (short, irregular stenosis with sharp shoulders).
  3. Endorectal ultrasound (ERUS): Assesses depth of invasion and lymph node involvement.
  4. Metastatic workup:
    • Abdominal pelvic ultrasound (liver, bladder, other organs).
    • Chest radiograph (pulmonary metastases).
    • CT scan (liver, adrenals, ovaries, lymph nodes).
    • MRI (most sensitive for liver metastases).

Staging

Dukes Staging (Modified by Astler-Coller)

Stage ALimited to rectal mucosa
Stage B1Extend into muscularis propria
Stage B2Extend through muscularis propria
Stage C1B1 plus nodal involvement
Stage C2B2 plus nodal involvement
Stage DDistant metastasis

TNM Classification (AJCC)

Primary Tumor (T)

  • Tis: Carcinoma in situ
  • T1: Invades lamina propria
  • T2: Invades muscularis propria
  • T3: Invades through muscularis propria into subserosa/pericolic tissue
  • T4: Perforates visceral peritoneum or invades other organs

Regional Lymph Nodes (N)

  • N0: No metastasis
  • N1: 1-3 nodes
  • N2: 4 or more nodes
  • N3: Nodes along named vascular trunk

Distant Metastasis (M)

  • M0: No distant metastasis
  • M1: Distant metastasis

MANAGEMENT

Treatment of Polyps

10% of polyps > 1 cm contain carcinoma. Pedunculated polyps may be removed colonoscopically if criteria met (differentiation, margins clear). Sessile polyps with cancer require resection.

Surgical Management

Rectal Cancer Surgery

  1. Low Anterior Resection (LAR): For lesions in middle/upper third (sometimes lower). Lesion > 8 cm from dentate line. Uses Total Mesorectal Excision (TME).
  2. Abdominoperineal Resection (APR): For lower-third cancers where sphincter cannot be spared. Creates permanent end-sigmoid colostomy.

Colon Cancer Surgery

  • Right Hemicolectomy: Caecal, ascending, or proximal transverse colon tumours.
  • Left Hemicolectomy: Distal transverse or descending colon tumours.
  • Transverse Colectomy: For transverse colon tumours.
  • Sigmoid Colectomy: For sigmoid colon tumours.

Inoperable Tumour with Obstruction

  • Ascending colon: Bypass (ileocolic anastomosis).
  • Left colon: Transverse colostomy.
  • Pelvic colon: Left iliac fossa colostomy.

Adjuvant Therapy

  • Radiotherapy: Preoperative (down-staging) or Postoperative (decreases local recurrence).
  • Chemotherapy: 5-fluorouracil with levamisole. Irinotecan, methyl-CCNU.

Prevention & Screening

Goal: Eradicate adenomas before malignant transformation.

  • High-risk screening: For family history of FAP/HNPCC, personal history of polyps/cancer/IBD.
  • Methods: Colonoscopy, Double-contrast barium enema, FOBT + Sigmoidoscopy.

Prognosis

5-Year Survival: Stage I (72%), Stage II (54%), Stage III (39%), Stage IV (7%).

Poor prognostic factors: Pre-op high CEA (>5ng/mL), bowel wall penetration, adherence to organs, perforation, nodal involvement, venous invasion, poor differentiation, aneuploidy, chromosome 18q loss.
