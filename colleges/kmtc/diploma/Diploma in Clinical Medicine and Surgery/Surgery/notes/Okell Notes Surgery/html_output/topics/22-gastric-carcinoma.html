GASTRIC CARCINOMA

Predisposing Factors

  1. H. pylori infection
  2. Gastric ulcers
  3. Chronic gastritis (atrophic or pernicious anemia)
  4. Gastric adenomas
  5. Cigarette smoking and alcohol consumption
  6. Diet: Nitrosamines in food preservation, smoked fish/meat.
  7. Partial gastrectomy (bilious reflux)
  8. Barrett's esophagus
  9. Radiation therapy
  10. Genetic Factors (Familial history)

Clinical Presentation

History (The 5 A's)

Most gastric cancers present at an advanced stage. The presentations are often non-specific, classically summarized as the 5 A's:

  • Asthenia (Weakness/Fatigue)
  • Abdominal pain
  • Anorexia
  • Anaemia
  • Achlorhydria (Reduced stomach acid)

Specific Symptoms

  1. Weight loss: Most common symptom.
  2. Abdominal pain: Epigastric, substernal, or back. May mimic peptic ulcer disease.
  3. Vomiting: Especially if distal tumors cause pyloric obstruction. Associated with early satiety.
  4. Constipation: Due to reduced intake.
  5. Bleeding: Hematemesis and melena (acute or chronic).
  6. Dysphagia: For tumors at the cardioesophageal junction (fundus).

Physical Examination

May not reveal abnormality except in advanced disease.

  • General: Anemia, Dehydration, Wasting (cachexia).
  • Abdominal Mass: Palpable epigastric mass, hepatic enlargement, or succussion splash (obstruction).
  • Metastatic Signs:
    • Virchow's node (Troisier's sign): Left supraclavicular lymph node.
    • Sister Mary Joseph's nodule: Umbilical nodules.
    • Blumer's shelf: Metastatic deposits in the pouch of Douglas felt on DRE.
    • Krukenberg tumor: Ovarian metastases.
    • Trousseau's sign: Migratory superficial thrombophlebitis.
    • Jaundice/Ascites: Liver mets or peritoneal seeding.

Investigations

Imaging

  1. Gastroscopy & Biopsy (Gold Standard): Allows visualization and histological confirmation. Multiple biopsies (4-6) and brush cytology increase yield.
  2. Barium Meal (Double Contrast): If endoscopy unavailable. Findings: Filling defects, ulcerated mass, loss of distensibility ("linitis plastica"), enlarged folds.
  3. Endoscopic Ultrasonography (EUS): Best for assessing depth of invasion (T-stage) and regional nodes.
  4. CT Scan: For staging (metastasis, ascites, organ extension).
  5. Laparoscopy: To assess operability, peritoneal seeding, and obtain cytology.

Laboratory

  • FHG: Microcytic hypochromic anemia (blood loss) or Megaloblastic (pernicious anemia).
  • LFTs: Abnormal in liver metastasis.
  • Tumor Markers: CEA (Carcinoembryonic antigen) - helpful for follow-up.
  • Stool Occult Blood: Frequently positive.

TNM Staging

Primary Tumor (T) Regional Nodes (N) Metastasis (M)
Tis: Carcinoma in situ N1: Perigastric nodes M0: No metastasis
T1: Invades submucosa N2: Regional nodes M1: Distant metastasis
T2: Invades muscularis propria N3: IVC/Aorta nodes
T3: Penetrates serosa/adventitia
T4: Invades adjacent structures

MANAGEMENT

Surgical Management

1. Mucosal Resection

For carcinoma in situ (CIS) confined to mucosa (< 2cm). Done endoscopically.

2. Gastrectomy (Curative)

Goal: Surgical margins free of tumor (8-10 cm clearance).

  • Partial Gastrectomy (Billroth): For distal tumors. Reconstruction via Gastrojejunostomy.
  • Total Gastrectomy: For proximal (cardia/fundus) tumors. Reconstruction via Roux-en-Y esophago-jejunostomy (prevents alkaline reflux).

3. Lymphadenectomy (Extent)

R1 Gastrectomy: Removes Group I (N1) nodes (perigastric).
R2 Gastrectomy: Removes Group II (N2) nodes (along hepatic, splenic, celiac arteries) en bloc with omentum. Standard curative intent.
R3 Gastrectomy: Includes Group III (N3) nodes (para-aortic/retropancreatic). Often involves splenectomy/pancreatectomy.

Palliative Surgery

For unresectable tumors to relieve obstruction or bleeding.

  • Billroth II: Resection of distal stomach with gastrojejunostomy.
  • Gastrostomy: For feeding.
  • Stenting: For gastro-esophageal junction obstruction.

Adjuvant Therapy

  • Chemotherapy: Gastric cancers are relatively sensitive. Combination therapy (5-FU, Doxorubicin, Mitomycin C) is most effective.
  • Radiotherapy: Generally radio-resistant, but useful for palliation of dysphagia or pain.

Prevention

  • Triple therapy to eradicate H. pylori.
  • Dietary modification (High Vitamins C, E, A).
  • Surveillance endoscopy for high-risk groups (Family history, Post-gastrectomy).
