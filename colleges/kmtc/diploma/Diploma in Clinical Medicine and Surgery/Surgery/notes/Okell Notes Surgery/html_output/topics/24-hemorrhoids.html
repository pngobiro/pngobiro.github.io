HEMORRHOIDS

Definition & Classification

Definition

Hemorrhoids are vascular and connective tissue cushions in anal mucosa. They function as protective pillows that engorge with blood during the act of defecation, protecting the anal canal from direct trauma due to passage of stool.

Classification

Internal Hemorrhoids

Found superior to the dentate line and lined with columnar epithelium. They have autonomic innervation and are thus not painful.

Degree Characteristics
1st Degree Do not prolapse; only bleeding announces their presence.
2nd Degree Prolapse at defecation but reduce spontaneously.
3rd Degree Prolapse requiring manual replacement.
4th Degree Permanent prolapse (cannot be reduced).

External Hemorrhoids

Found below the dentate line and lined by squamous epithelium. Innervated by cutaneous nerves (pudendal nerve/sacral plexus) supplying the perianal area. Thus are very painful, especially when thrombosed.

Etiology & Pathophysiology

Etiology

  • Constipation / Straining with defecation
  • Low-fiber diets (small-caliber stools)
  • Pregnancy
  • Occupations requiring prolonged sitting
  • Loss of muscle tone in old age
  • Liver disease (Portal hypertension)
  • Colon malignancy
  • Obesity

Pathophysiology

Abnormal hemorrhoidal tissue development is related to chronic straining that leads to engorgement and vascular dilatation due to decreased venous return. This leads to stretching of the supporting connective tissue (which weakens with age).

Clinical Presentation & Diagnosis

Signs and Symptoms

  1. Constipation / Straining
  2. Episodic bleeding on stool (bright red)
  3. Feeling of incomplete evacuation
  4. Pruritus (itching)
  5. Pain: Severe acute pain suggests thrombosis (external) or strangulation. Classic internal hemorrhoids are painless.
  6. Ulceration

Differential Diagnosis

  • Carcinoma of the colon and rectum
  • Diverticular disease
  • Adenomatous polyps
  • Ulcerative colitis
  • Rectal prolapse (procidentia)

MANAGEMENT

Treatment is only indicated when hemorrhoids become symptomatic.

A. Medical Treatment

First-line for 1st and 2nd degree hemorrhoids.

  1. Diet Modification: High fiber (vegetables, fruits, bran) and increased water intake to avoid constipation.
  2. Avoid Straining: No prolonged stay on the toilet seat.
  3. Stool Softeners: e.g., Lactulose.
  4. Topical Agents: Suppositories/ointments with local anesthetics/steroids.
  5. Sitz Baths: Warm water baths 3 times/day for 30 mins.

B. Procedural/Interventional Treatment

  • Sclerotherapy: Injection of sclerosing agent (e.g., 5% phenol in oil) submucosally to induce fibrosis.
  • Rubber Band Ligation (Strangulation): Excellent for enlarged/prolapsing hemorrhoids. Band placed at base causes ischemic necrosis and sloughing. Complication: Pain (if placed too low).
  • Others: Cryosurgery, Infrared photocoagulation.

C. Surgical Therapy

Reserved for Grade III/IV, failed non-operative therapy, or thrombosed external hemorrhoids.

Excision (Hemorrhoidectomy)

For external thrombosed hemorrhoids: Excision of the overlying skin and underlying veins is preferred over simple enucleation to prevent recurrence.

Stapled Hemorrhoidopexy (PPH)

Procedure for Prolapse and Hemorrhoids. Uses a circular stapler to resect a circumferential strip of mucosa above the dentate line. Lifts the hemorrhoidal cushions (pexy) and interrupts blood supply. Less painful than open surgery.

Summary of Management (The 6 S's)

  1. Stool softeners and diet modification
  2. Suppositories and astringents
  3. Sitz baths
  4. Sclerotherapy
  5. Strangulation (Band ligation)
  6. Surgery

Complications

  • Stenosis (Anal stenosis)
  • Haemorrhage
  • Infection (Perianal abscesses)
  • Recurrence
  • Non-healing wounds
  • Fistula formation
  • Urinary retention (post-operative)
