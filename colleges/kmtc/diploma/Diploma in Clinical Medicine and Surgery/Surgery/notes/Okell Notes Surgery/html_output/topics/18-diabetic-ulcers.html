DIABETIC (NEUROPATHIC) ULCERS

Introduction & Pathophysiology

Ulceration is caused by several factors acting together, but particularly by neuropathy. Peripheral neuropathy results in loss of the protective sensation of pain and in autonomic dysfunction, with sympathetic denervation, dry skin, and warm feet.

Other important component causes of ulceration include peripheral vascular disease, callus, edema, and deformity.

The Triad

The triad of neuropathy, deformity, and trauma is present in almost two thirds of patients with foot ulcers.

Diagnosis

History

Information relating to ulcer history should be recorded in a structured format and may include:

  • Year first ulcer occurred
  • Site of ulcer and of any previous ulcers
  • Number of previous episodes of ulceration
  • Time to healing in previous episodes
  • Time free of ulcers
  • Past treatment methods (both successful and unsuccessful)
  • Previous operations on venous system
  • Previous and current use of compression
  • Intermittent claudication, varices, lack of sensation, pain
  • Family hx of DM, HTN
  • Features of ischemic heart dz, retinopathy, nephropathy

Ulcer Assessment

If the ulcer has been present for months and is asymptomatic and if foot pulses are palpable, neuropathy should be considered as a major cause.

However, given the variability of assessment of foot pulses, noninvasive assessment of the peripheral circulation is recommended if there is any suggestion of peripheral ischemia.

Callus formation and a plantar site of ulceration also suggest neuropathy as a major contributory cause. A combination of lack of sensation, limited joint mobility, autonomic dysfunction resulting in dry skin, and repetitive high pressure may lead to callus formation.

Vascular status must be assessed: pulses, pallor, paresthesia, pain, temperature.

Investigations

  • Pulses
  • BP
  • Random blood sugar
  • Doppler Ultrasound
  • Arteriogram

Infection & Osteomyelitis

Infection of the Ulcer

Infection is usually a consequence, rather than a cause, of ulceration, which allows the entry and multiplication of microorganisms.

Definition: Presence of systemic signs of infection (e.g., fever, leukocytosis) or purulent secretions, or two or more local symptoms or signs (redness, warmth, induration, pain, or tenderness).

If infection is present, a deep-tissue specimen should be obtained aseptically, if possible; such specimens are superior to superficial swab specimens for the isolation of resistant organisms. Polymicrobial isolates, including aerobic and anaerobic species, are common.

Osteomyelitis

  • Plain radiographs: Findings are often suggestive (bone destruction or periosteal reaction). Recommended when there is evidence of infection.
  • Histologic evaluation and culture of a bone-biopsy specimen are regarded as the gold standard.
  • MRI: Now considered the imaging test of choice when osteomyelitis is suspected; sensitivity and specificity are 90% or greater.

MANAGEMENT

The principles of management of neuropathic ulcers include eradication of infection and removal of pressure from the ulcer.

General Care

  1. Glycemic control: Important since leukocyte function is impaired in patients with chronic hyperglycemia.
  2. Stop smoking: Affects vascular factors and increases incisional-wound infection rates.
  3. Do not go barefoot; use well fitting shoes.
  4. Inspect the feet daily, especially between the toes.
  5. Keep the feet clean and dry.
  6. Seek help to trim toenails, or if corns/callouses develop.
  7. Comply with medications.
  8. Avoid exposure to cold, friction, moisture between toes.
  9. Avoid use of external heat (heat pad, hot water bottle, hydrotherapy).

Wound Management

  1. Sharp débridement: The removal of necrotic and senescent tissue as well as foreign and infected material from the wound.
  2. Treatment of local edema: Intermittent foot compression by a pneumatic pump results in a higher rate of healing.
  3. Offloading (Pressure removal): Techniques include casts, boots, half shoes, sandals, and felted foam dressings.
    Total-Contact Cast: Use of a non-removable cast over the involved limb has been shown to be superior to standard therapy. (Contraindications: infected or ischemic wounds).
  4. Dressings: Promote a moist environment to assist healing. Selection involves matching dressing properties (e.g., exudate control) with ulcer characteristics.

Infection Control

Choice of antibiotic is initially based on presumed pathogens.

  • Common broad-spectrum antibiotics: Clindamycin, Cephalexin, Ciprofloxacin, and Amoxicillin-clavulanic acid (Augmentin).
  • Intravenous options (serious infections): Imipenem, Ampicillin-sulbactam, Piperacillin-azobactam, and broad-spectrum Cephalosporins.
  • Duration:
    • Soft-tissue infections: 1-2 weeks.
    • Osteomyelitis: > 6 weeks (often with surgical débridement).
