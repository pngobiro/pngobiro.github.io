HEAD INJURY

Overview & Definition

Definition: Trauma to the head causing neurological manifestations.

Etiology: Motor vehicle accidents (most common), falls, assaults, sports-related injuries, penetrating trauma.

Demographics: Male-to-female ratio is nearly 2:1. More common in persons younger than 35 years.

Classification

A. Severity (Glasgow Coma Scale - GCS)

  • Severe Head Injury: Score < 8
  • Moderate Head Injury: Score 8 - 12
  • Mild Head Injury: Score 13 - 15

B. Anatomical Classification

1. Scalp

Consists of 5 layers (SCALP):

  • S - Skin
  • C - Connective tissue (Dense, vascular layer)
  • A - Aponeurosis (Galea)
  • L - Loose connective tissue (Areolar - "Dangerous area")
  • P - Pericranium

2. Skull Injuries

  • Simple/Closed Fractures:
    • Linear/Fissure fracture
    • Comminuted fracture
    • Ping-pong fracture (Green stick fracture in infants)
    • Depressed fracture (Significant if depression > thickness of diploe)
  • Compound/Open Fractures: Communication with atmosphere (e.g., Skull Base Fracture).
    • Signs: Rhinorrhea (CSF leak through nose), Otorrhea (CSF leak through ear).

3. Brain Injury

Primary Brain Injury: Occurs at time of impact.

  • Concussion (physiological disruption).
  • Contusion/Laceration.
  • Diffuse Axonal Injury (DAI): Shearing forces from acceleration/deceleration.

Secondary Brain Injury: Result of physiologic events after primary injury.

  • Intracranial Hematomas:
    • Epidural Hematoma (EDH): Between skull and dura. Usually arterial (Middle Meningeal Artery). Biconvex/Lentiform shape. Classic "lucid interval".
    • Subdural Hematoma (SDH): Between dura and arachnoid. Venous origin (bridging veins). Crescent shape. Acute vs Chronic (common in elderly/alcoholics).
    • Subarachnoid Hemorrhage (SAH): Blood in subarachnoid space.
    • Intraventricular Hemorrhage
  • Brain Edema: Localized or Diffuse.
  • Infection: Meningitis.
  • Hydrocephalus

Diagnosis

History

  • Mechanism of injury (RTA, Fall, Assault).
  • Loss of consciousness (LOC) and duration.
  • Lucid interval.
  • ENT bleed (Otorrhea/Rhinorrhea).
  • Seizures/Convulsions.
  • Vomiting, Headache, Visual disturbances.
  • Alcohol/Drug use.

Physical Examination

  • GCS Assessment: Eye opening, Verbal response, Motor response.
  • Vital Signs: Look for Cushing's Triad (Hypertension, Bradycardia, Irregular breathing) indicating raised ICP.
  • Head Exam: Lacerations, depressions.
  • Signs of Basal Skull Fracture:
    • Raccoon Eyes: Bilateral periorbital ecchymosis.
    • Battle's Sign: Post-auricular ecchymosis.
    • Hemotympanum (blood behind ear drum).
    • CSF leak (Halo sign on tissue/pillowcase).
  • Neurological Exam: Pupils (size, reactivity, anisocoria), Focal deficits, Cranial nerves (especially I, III, VI, VII).

Investigations

  1. CT Scan (Gold Standard):

    Indications: GCS < 13, Deteriorating consciousness, Focal signs, Penetrating injury, Post-traumatic seizure, Skull fracture, CSF leak.

  2. Plain Skull X-ray: Screening for fractures/intracranial air. AP, Lateral, Towne's views.
  3. MRI: For diffuse axonal injury or long-term evaluation.
  4. Beta-transferrin: Specific test for CSF leak.
  5. Baseline Labs: PCV, U/E/C, ABGs, Blood alcohol/glucose.

MANAGEMENT

Primary Survey (ABCDE)

A - Airway & C-Spine: Immobilize C-spine. Jaw thrust/Chin lift (clearing debris). Guedel airway. Intubate if GCS < 9.

B - Breathing: Oxygenation. Treat Pneumothorax/Hemothorax.

C - Circulation: Resuscitate shock. Goal: MAP > 90 mmHg.
Note: Isolated head injury rarely causes hypotension (except in infants). Look for other sources of bleeding if shock is present.

D - Disability: Baseline GCS, Pupil exam.

E - Exposure: Check for other injuries.

ICP Management

Targets

Normal ICP: 0-10 mmHg.
Treatment Threshold: > 20-25 mmHg.
Cerebral Perfusion Pressure (CPP) Goal: 60-70 mmHg.

  1. Head Elevation: 30 degrees to promote venous drainage.
  2. Hyperventilation: Maintain PCO2 30-35 mmHg (Vasoconstriction reduces cerebral blood volume). Avoid excessive hyperventilation.
  3. Osmotic Diuretics: Mannitol (0.25-1 g/kg bolus).
  4. Sedation/Analgesia: To reduce metabolic demand.
  5. Anticonvulsants: Phenytoin (for 7 days) to prevent early post-traumatic seizures.
  6. Temperature Control: Prevent pyrexia.

Surgical Management

  • Scalp Wounds: Debridement and suturing.
  • Depressed Skull Fracture: Elevate if depression > thickness of skull table or focal signs.
  • Extradural Hematoma: Craniotomy/Burr hole evacuation if > 1.5 cm thick or symptomatic.
  • Subdural Hematoma: Burr hole evacuation (Chronic SDH) or Craniotomy (Acute SDH).

General Care

  • Nutrition: Enteral feeds within 72 hours.
  • Bladder/Bowel: Catheterization (monitor output), Bowel care.
  • Skin Care: Prevent pressure sores (turning, padding).
  • Physiotherapy: Chest and limbs to prevent contractures/pneumonia.

Complications

  • Raised Intracranial Pressure / Herniation
  • Infection (Meningitis, Abscess)
  • Seizures (Epilepsy)
  • Hydrocephalus
  • CSF Fistulae (Rhinorrhea/Otorrhea)
  • Cranial Nerve Palsies
  • Vascular Injuries (Carotid-Cavernous Fistula)
  • Post-concussion Syndrome
  • Diabetes Insipidus / SIADH
