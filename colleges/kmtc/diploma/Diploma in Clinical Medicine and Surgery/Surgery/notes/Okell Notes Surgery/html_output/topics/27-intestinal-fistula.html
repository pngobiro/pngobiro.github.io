INTESTINAL FISTULA

Definition & Classification

Definition

A fistula is an abnormal tract between two or more epithelial lined surfaces. The tract is lined by granulation or epithelial tissue.
Enterocutaneous fistula: An abnormal communication between bowel and skin.

Classification

  • Anatomical: Based on site of origin/termination (e.g., jejunocutaneous, ileovesical).
  • Internal vs External:
    • Internal: Hollow organ to hollow organ (e.g., ileocolic).
    • External: Hollow organ to skin (e.g., duodenocutaneous).
  • Physiological (Output over 24 hrs):
    Low Volume< 200 ml/day
    Moderate Volume200 - 500 ml/day
    High Volume> 500 ml/day
  • Etiological: Spontaneous vs Iatrogenic (Post-surgical).
  • Tract Characteristics: Simple (short, direct) vs Complex (multiple tracts, abscesses).

Etiology & Clinical Features

Etiology

1. Iatrogenic (Commonest - 85-90%)

'Surgical misadventure'. Causes of anastomotic breakdown include:

  • Tension on suture line.
  • Distal obstruction.
  • Foreign body / Hematoma / Abscess at site.
  • Sepsis / Malnutrition / Tumor.
  • Unintentional enterotomy.

2. Spontaneous (10-15%)

  • Crohn's disease / Tuberculosis.
  • Malignancy.
  • Vascular insufficiency / Ischemia.
  • Radiation exposure.

Clinical Presentation

  • Sepsis: Fever, tachycardia, leukocytosis.
  • Abdominal Pain: Localized tenderness.
  • External Drainage: Enteric contents.
    • Watery: Gastric
    • Bile stained: Duodenal/Jejunal
    • Brown/Fecal: Colonic
  • Skin Excoriation: Severe irritation around the opening.
  • Dehydration & Malnutrition: Due to loss of fluid and nutrients.

MANAGEMENT

Goal: Achieve permanent closure in shortest time with lowest risk. Usually involves 4 Phases.

Phase A: Stabilization (Resuscitation)

  1. Fluid & Electrolyte Replacement:

    Rehydrate with Isotonic saline. Monitor CVP, Urine output (30-50ml/hr), and Electrolytes. Correct imbalances (Hypokalemia, metabolic acidosis).

  2. Control Sepsis (Infection Control):

    Drain abscesses (percutaneous or surgical). Start broad-spectrum antibiotics.

  3. Skin Protection:

    Vital to prevent excoriation. Use stoma bags (Stomahesive), Zinc oxide paste, or Aluminum paste to protect perifistular skin.

  4. Reduce Fistula Output:

    NPO (Nil by Mouth), Nasogastric suction. Drugs: H2 blockers/PPIs (reduce gastric acid), Somatostatin/Octreotide (inhibits GI secretions).

Phase B: Investigations

  • Lab: CBC (Leukocytosis), Electrolytes, Albumin (Nutrition status).
  • Fistulogram: (Day 7-10) Water-soluble contrast injected into tract. Defines origin, length, continuity, and distal obstruction.
  • CT Scan/Ultrasound: To detect intra-abdominal abscesses.

Phase C: Conservative Management (Nutrition)

"Sepsis control and Nutrition are the keys to spontaneous closure."

  • Total Parenteral Nutrition (TPN): Route of choice for high-output proximal fistulas to put bowel at rest.
  • Enteral Nutrition: If fistula is distal (colonic) or feeding can be delivered distal to the fistula (e.g., feeding jejunostomy for gastric fistula).
  • Wait for spontaneous closure (can take 4-6 weeks). Monitor output.

Phase D: Surgery

Indications for Surgery:

  1. Peritonitis / Evisceration / Hemorrhage (Emergency).
  2. Failure of spontaneous closure after 6-12 weeks of adequate nutrition/sepsis control.
  3. Distal obstruction.
  4. Complex fistulas / Short tract (< 2cm) / Epithelialization of tract.
  5. Underlying disease (Malignancy, Radiation, IBD).

Surgical Principles: Resection of the fistula segment with end-to-end anastomosis. Simple closure is associated with high recurrence. Ideally delayed until intra-abdominal sepsis has resolved (often > 12 weeks).
