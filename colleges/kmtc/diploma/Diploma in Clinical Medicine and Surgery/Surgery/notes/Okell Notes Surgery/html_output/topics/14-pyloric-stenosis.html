CONGENITAL HYPERTROPHIC PYLORIC STENOSIS

Introduction

A progressive stenosis of the pyloric canal occurring in infancy.

Etiology

  • The cause is not known.
  • Positive family history.

Incidence & Onset

  • Incidence: 1-8:1000 births, with a 4:1 male predominance.
  • Onset: Usually at 3-4 weeks of age.

Clinical Presentation

  1. Intermittent, non-bilious, projectile vomiting of increasing frequency and severity.
  2. Initially hungry and feeds avidly, later weakness due to electrolyte imbalances.
  3. Epigastric distention.
  4. Visible gastric peristalsis, from left to right.
  5. An olive-sized mass can often be felt on deep palpation in the right upper abdomen, especially after the child has vomited.
  6. Jaundice (occasional).
  7. Late signs: dehydration, weight loss.
  8. Constipation.

Investigations

Laboratory Findings

  • Hypochloremic metabolic alkalosis with potassium depletion (Classic finding).
  • Hemoconcentration is reflected by elevated hemoglobin and hematocrit values.
  • Elevated unconjugated bilirubin occurs in 2-5% of cases.

Imaging

  1. Ultrasonography (1st choice): Shows a hypoechoic ring with a hyperdense center. Thickness of circular muscle is greater than 4 mm in pyloric stenosis.
  2. Upper gastrointestinal series: Reveals delay in gastric emptying and an elongated narrowed pyloric channel with a double tract of barium ("String sign"). The enlarged pyloric muscle causes characteristic semilunar impressions on the gastric antrum ("Shoulder sign").

Differential Diagnosis

  • Esophageal stenosis or achalasia: The vomitus contains no gastric contents, and metabolic alkalosis is rare.
  • Annular pancreas, malrotation, volvulus, and small bowel obstruction: The vomitus is bilious.
  • Congenital adrenal hyperplasia: The absence of virilization and hyperkalemia generally rules this out.
  • Pylorospasm: May cause delay in gastric emptying during barium x-ray, but the elongated narrow pyloric canal is not seen and no mass is palpable.
  • Others: Antral webs or diaphragms, duplications, cysts.

MANAGEMENT

Ramstedt Pyloromyotomy

Is the treatment of choice and consists of incision down to the mucosa along the pyloric length.

Pre-operative Stabilization

Prior to surgery, it is imperative to correct dehydration and electrolyte abnormalities (specifically the hypochloremic metabolic alkalosis).
