BURNS

Introduction & Pathophysiology

Definition

Tissue injury caused by thermal, radiation, chemical, or electrical contact resulting in protein denaturation, loss of intravascular fluid volume due to increased vascular permeability and oedema.

Pathophysiology

Tissue damage leads to release of vasoactive substances e.g. prostaglandins, histamines, oxygen radicals, leukotrienes & platelet activating factors.

  • This cause microvascular instability with increased capillary permeability for the first 24-36 hrs fluid shift to extravasular compartment and oedema.
  • This leads to decreased right atrial filling pressures, decreased CO and Shock.
  • The body tries to compensate by: Increased peripheral resistance (sympathetic stimulation), Tachycardia, Decreased capacitance of great vessels.

If uncorrected: Cardiac output is diverted to vital tissues reducing perfusion of skin, muscle & gut (Skin feels cold & clammy). Reduced renal perfusion lead to acute RF. Irreversible shock occurs as an end stage.

Classification

A. According to Cause

  • Thermal: Open flames and Hot liquid (most common).
  • Chemical:
    • Acids: Cause coagulative necrosis (eschar limits penetration). Burning stops within 1-2hrs. Management: Irrigate with water.
    • Alkalis: Cause liquifactive necrosis (saponification of fats). Penetrate deeper. Burning continues for 7-14 days. Management: Generous irrigation for 1-2 hours.
  • Electrical:
    • Low voltage (<1000V) vs High voltage (>1000V).
    • Features: Myonecrosis, Myoglobinuria (Renal failure), Arrhythmias.
    • Management: TBSA is NOT an index for resuscitation. Titrate fluids against urine output (aim for 100ml/hr). Alkalinize urine (Mannitol/Bicarb). Fasciotomy for compartment syndrome.
  • Others: Radiation, Frostbite, Mechanical (Friction).

B. According to Depth

Degree Depth Appearance Sensation Healing
1st Degree Epidermis only Red, dry Hypersensitive/Painful No scarring
2nd Degree (Superficial) Epidermis + Upper 1/3 Dermis Red, moist, weeping blisters, blanche with pressure Painful 10-14 days (potential pigment changes)
2nd Degree (Deep) Epidermis + Upper 2/3 Dermis Cheesy white, wet or waxy dry; Do not blanche Variable 14-21 days (Severe scarring)
3rd Degree Full Epidermis + Dermis Grey/charred/black, leathery Insensate Needs grafting
4th Degree Involves Muscle Charred Insensate Amputation/Flaps
5th Degree Involves Bone Charred Insensate Amputation

C. According to Size (Wallace Rule of Nines)

  • Adults:
    • Head & Neck: 9%
    • Each Arm: 9% (Total 18%)
    • Anterior Trunk: 18%
    • Posterior Trunk: 18%
    • Each Leg: 18% (Total 36%)
    • Perineum: 1%
  • Children: Use Lund-Browder Chart (Head is larger relative to body).

Inhalational Injury

Causes upper airway obstruction secondary to reflex laryngospasm, oedema, or sloughing of mucosa.

Clinical Presentation

  • History of enclosed space fire.
  • Facial burns, singed nasal hairs.
  • Soot in nasopharynx, carbonaceous sputum.
  • Respiratory distress (Tachypnoea, Stridor, Flaring alae nasae).
  • Hoarseness (indicates vocal cord swelling).

Management

Critical Action

Intubate at the earliest opportunity to avoid difficulty once edema sets in.

  • Admit to ICU/HDU.
  • 100% Humidified Oxygen (displaces CO).
  • Bronchodilators for bronchospasm.
  • Bronchoscopy for bronchial toilet.
  • Fluid resuscitation: Patient needs about 10-15% more than calculated formula.

MANAGEMENT (The First 48 Hours)

Primary Survey (ABCDE)

Airway with C-spine control, Breathing, Circulation, Disability, Exposure.

IV Fluids Resuscitation

Parkland's Formula (The Gold Standard)

Total Fluid (24 hrs) = 4 mL x Body Weight (kg) x % TBSA

  • Use Crystalloids (Ringers Lactate/Hartmann's).
  • Give ½ of total in the first 8 hours (from time of burn).
  • Give remaining ½ in the next 16 hours.

Monitoring Resuscitation

Adequacy is measured by Urine Output:

  • Adults: 30-50 ml/hr
  • Children: 0.5-1 ml/kg/hr
  • Electrical Burns: Aim for 100 ml/hr (to flush myoglobin).

Medical Management

  • Analgesics: IV Opioids (Morphine/Pethidine). Avoid IM.
  • PPI/H2 Blockers: To prevent Curling's Ulcers (stress ulcers).
  • Tetanus Toxoid: Booster.
  • Antibiotics: Prophylactic if TBSA >15% or dirty wounds.

Wound Care

  1. Remove necrotic tissue/debris.
  2. Rupture blisters >1cm or on palms/soles.
  3. Wash with soap/water or saline.
  4. Topical Agents:
    • Silver Sulphadiazine (Standard).
    • 0.5% Silver Nitrate.
    • Mafenide Acetate (Penetrates eschar, painful).
  5. Dressing: Exposure (face/perineum) vs Occlusive (limbs/trunk).

Escharotomy

Surgical division of constricting eschars. Indicated for circumferential burns compromising circulation (limbs) or respiration (chest).

Nutritional Support

Burn injury causes a hypermetabolic state.

Curreri Formula:
$$ Energy = 25 \text{ KCal/Kg} + (40 \text{ KCal} \times \% \text{TBSA}) $$

Enteral route preferred to reduce intestinal villous atrophy.

Complications

Immediate

  • Inhalational injury (Airway obstruction)
  • Hypovolemic Shock
  • Carbon Monoxide poisoning

Early (Days)

  • Fluid/Electrolyte imbalance (Hyperkalemia initially, then Hypokalemia).
  • Infection (Sepsis).
  • ARDS.
  • Acute Renal Failure (Hemoglobinuria/Myoglobinuria).
  • Curling's Ulcer: Acute duodenal stress ulcer.

Late (>2 weeks)

  • Contractures.
  • Hypertrophic scars / Keloids.
  • Marjolin's Ulcer: Squamous cell carcinoma developing in old burn scar (years later).
  • Psychological trauma.
