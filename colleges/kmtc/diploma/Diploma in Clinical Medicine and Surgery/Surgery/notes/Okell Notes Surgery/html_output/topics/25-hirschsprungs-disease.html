HIRSCHSPRUNG'S DISEASE

(Aganglionic Megacolon)

Definition & Epidemiology

Definition

Presence of large, non-functional distal intestine (usually the rectum) with variable involvement of the colon and small gut due to absence of ganglion cells.

Sites of Involvement

  • Rectum only: 10%
  • Recto-sigmoid: 65%
  • More proximal colon: 10%
  • Entire colon (Total Colonic Aganglionosis) with small bowel: 10-15%

Demographics

  • Sex: Males affected 5x more than females (4:1) in short segment disease. Females tend to have longer aganglionic segments.
  • Race: Whites > Blacks > Asians.
  • Incidence: ~1:4500 live births (KNH ~12 cases/year).

Etiology & Pathophysiology

Etiology

Sporadic (80-90%) vs Familial (15-17%).

Genetics:

  • RET gene (Chromosome 10) - Autosomal Dominant (Long segment).
  • Endothelial Receptor B gene (Chromosome 13) - Autosomal Recessive.
  • Endothelial 3 gene (Chromosome 20) - Autosomal Recessive.

Associations: Trisomy 21 (Down's syndrome), Waardenburg's syndrome, Ondine's curse, Anorectal malformations.

Pathophysiology

Failure in the cephalo-caudal migration of neuro crest cells into the distal bowel. The absence of ganglion cells always begins at the anus and extends proximally.

Histological Hallmark: Aganglionosis in the submucosal plexus of Meissner and in the intermyenteric plexus of Auerbach.

Functional Defect: The nerves affected are the non-cholinergic non-adrenergic (NO, VIP, NADPH). This leads to unopposed autonomic nervous system functioning causing:

  1. Reduced or absent peristaltic (propulsive) activity.
  2. Increased intestinal sphincter tone (failure of relaxation).

Pathological Features

  1. Marked distension & hypertrophy of gut proximal to the aganglionic segment.
  2. Funnelling (coning) at the transition zone.
  3. Constricted or collapsed gut at & distal to the aganglionic portion.

Clinical Presentation

Symptoms

  • Delayed passage of meconium (Failure to pass within first 24-48 hours).
  • Chronic or intermittent constipation with diarrhea.
  • Passage of flatus and stool requires great effort (ribbon-like stools).
  • Progressive abdominal distention.
  • Vomiting (bilious) and Reluctance to feed.
  • Failure to thrive / Poor growth.

Toxic Megacolon

Some patients present with acute enterocolitis or toxic megacolon characterized by gross abdominal distension, vomiting, shock, sepsis, and fever.

Physical Signs

  • Small for age, irritable, dehydrated.
  • Abdomen: Impacted stools in dilated sigmoid palpable. Distension with increased bowel sounds.
  • DRE (Digital Rectal Exam):
    • Normal anal tone.
    • Rectum collapsed, tight, and empty.
    • Classic Sign: Rectal examination in the infant is often followed by explosive expulsion of stool and flatus, with remarkable decompression ("Blast sign").

Differential Diagnosis

  • Anorectal malformation
  • Meconium plug/ileus
  • Metabolic (Hypothyroidism)
  • Drugs (Maternal sedation)
  • Sepsis

Investigations

Imaging

  1. Plain Abdominal X-ray: Dilated loops of bowel, air-fluid levels, absence of air in the rectum.
  2. Barium Enema: (At least 48 hrs after birth, no prep).
    • Shows narrow aganglionic segment and dilated proximal bowel.
    • Funneling at the transition zone.
    • Delayed Evacuation Film: Contrast retention after 24 hours is very suggestive.
  3. Anorectal Manometry: Failure of internal sphincter relaxation on rectal balloon inflation.

Biopsy (Definitive Diagnosis)

  1. Suction Mucosal Biopsy: Taken from posterior rectal wall (no anesthesia).
    • Features: Aganglionic segment, Nerve trunk hyperplasia, Increased acetylcholinesterase level.
  2. Full Thickness Biopsy: Strip of mucosa and muscularis removed under GA if suction biopsy is equivocal.

MANAGEMENT

Supportive / Initial

  • Fluid and electrolyte management.
  • Rectal Irrigation: Insert soft rectal tube and irrigate with warm Normal Saline (5ml/kg) to decompress. Avoid soap.
  • Antibiotics if enterocolitis/sepsis.

Temporary Management

Colostomy and Serial Biopsy:

  • Laparotomy with serial biopsies to determine extent of disease (transition zone).
  • Fashion colostomy at the transition zone (level of ganglionic bowel).
  • Allows decompression, weight gain, and prevents enterocolitis before definitive surgery.

Definitive Surgery

Principles: Resection of aganglionic segment and anastomosis of proximal ganglionic bowel to distal segment.

Common Procedures

  1. Swenson Operation: Excision of aganglionic colon/rectum down to 2cm above dentate line with end-to-end anastomosis.
  2. Duhamel Procedure: Retains rectal stump. Proximal bowel brought down behind rectum and sutured side-to-side (stapled). Creates a pouch.
  3. Soave Procedure (Endorectal Pull-through): Mucosa of aganglionic rectum is stripped, and ganglionic bowel is pulled through the muscular cuff.
  4. Lynn Operation: Rectal myectomy (for short-segment disease).
  5. Martin's Procedure: For total colonic aganglionosis (uses ileum).

Approaches: Open Laparotomy, Laparoscopy, or Transanal (TEPT).
