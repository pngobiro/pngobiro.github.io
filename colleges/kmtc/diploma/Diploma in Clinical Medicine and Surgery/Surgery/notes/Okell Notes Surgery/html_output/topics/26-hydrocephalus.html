HYDROCEPHALUS

Definition & Pathophysiology

Definition

Hydrocephalus is the abnormal rise in cerebrospinal fluid volume with ventricular dilatation due to an imbalance of cerebrospinal fluid production and absorption. It is usually accompanied by increased intracranial pressure.

Hydrocephalus results from:

  • Impaired circulation of CSF
  • Impaired absorption of CSF
  • Increased production (rare, e.g., choroid plexus papilloma)

Pathophysiology (Flow of CSF)

CSF is produced in the lateral ventricle choroid plexus at 30 mL/hr (20 mL/hr in infants).

  1. Lateral Ventricles → Foramen of Monro
  2. 3rd Ventricle → Cerebral Aqueduct of Sylvius
  3. 4th Ventricle → Foramina of Lushka (2 lateral) & Magendie (1 medial)
  4. Subarachnoid Space/Cisterns (Basal cisterns, over cerebellum/cortex)
  5. Arachnoid Granulations → Superior Sagittal Sinus
  6. Venous Circulation

CSF Facts

  • Daily Production: ~720 ml/day (30ml/hr).
  • Total Volume: Infants (50 mls), Adults (125-150 mls).
  • Composition: Clear fluid, Protein 15-45 mg/ml, Glucose 60-80 mg/ml, <3 lymphocytes.

Types of Hydrocephalus

A. Obstructive / Non-communicating Hydrocephalus

Resulting from obstruction within the ventricular system (proximal dilatation).

  • Aqueduct Stenosis: Congenital (sex-linked recessive) or Acquired (Gliosis from infection/bleeds).
  • Lesions in the 4th Ventricle:
    • Chiari Malformation Type II: Associated with myelomeningocele. Elongation of 4th ventricle, kinking of brainstem.
    • Dandy-Walker Syndrome: Cystic expansion of 4th ventricle, agenesis of cerebellar vermis.
    • Tumors: Medulloblastomas, Ependymomas.
  • Space Occupying Lesions: Tumors, cysts causing compression.

B. Non-obstructive / Communicating Hydrocephalus

Resulting from obliteration of subarachnoid cisterns or malfunction of arachnoid granulations.

  • Infections: TB and Pneumococcal meningitis produce thick exudate obstructing basal cisterns.
  • Subarachnoid Hemorrhage (SAH): Blood clots obliterate cisterns/villi.
  • Leukemic Infiltrates
  • Superior Sagittal Sinus Thrombosis: Impairs venous drainage/reabsorption.
  • Overproduction: Choroid plexus papilloma.

C. Normal-Pressure Hydrocephalus (NPH)

Cerebral ventricular dilation with normal lumbar CSF pressure. Rare cause of dementia in the elderly.

Classic Triad of NPH

  1. Dementia
  2. Apraxia of Gait: "Magnetic gait" or "feet stuck to the floor".
  3. Urinary Incontinence

D. Ex Vacuo Hydrocephalus

Enlarged ventricles resulting from a loss of brain tissue (atrophy), not increased pressure.

Clinical Presentation

Infants (Open Sutures)

  • Accelerated head enlargement: Most prominent sign.
  • Irritability, lethargy, poor appetite, vomiting.
  • Delayed milestones / Failure to thrive.
  • Physical Signs:
    • Wide open, bulging anterior fontanel.
    • Dilated scalp veins.
    • "Setting-sun" eye sign: Downward deviation of eyes (pressure on tectum).
    • Macewen sign: "Cracked-pot" sound on percussion.
    • Hyperreflexia/Spasticity (Long tract signs).

Adults/Older Children (Closed Sutures)

Presentation is mainly signs of Increased Intracranial Pressure (ICP):

Symptoms

  • Severe bursting headache (worse in morning).
  • Projectile vomiting.
  • Blurring of vision.
  • Convulsions/seizures.
  • Drowsiness/Altered consciousness.

Signs

  • Cushing's Triad: Hypertension (High BP), Bradycardia (Low Pulse), Irregular breathing.
  • Papilledema.
  • Cranial nerve palsies (especially 6th nerve).

Investigations

  1. Ultrasonography: Through open anterior fontanelle in infants.
  2. CT Scan: Shows ventricular dilatation.
  3. MRI: Better detail for tumors/malformations.
  4. Skull X-ray (Infants): Separation of sutures, "Beaten-silver appearance" (chronic raised ICP).

Differential Diagnosis of Enlarged Head

  • Familial macrocephaly
  • Chronic anemia (Thalassemia) - thickened cranium
  • Rickets
  • Achondroplasia
  • Storage diseases

MANAGEMENT

Aims

  1. Reduce intracranial pressure (Medical or Diversion).
  2. Remove underlying cause (Definitive surgery).

Medical Treatment (Temporizing)

Used when surgery is contraindicated or for slowly progressive/transient cases.

  • Acetazolamide: Decreases CSF production. Monitor electrolytes.
  • Furosemide: Diuretic.
  • Serial Lumbar Punctures: For post-hemorrhagic hydrocephalus in neonates.

Surgical Treatment: CSF Diversion

1. Short-term (External Ventricular Drain - EVD)

Used for acute intraventricular bleeds or infections (high protein/cells would block permanent shunt).

2. Permanent CSF Diversion (Shunts)

Ventriculoperitoneal (VP) Shunt: Most common. Shunt placed from lateral ventricle to peritoneum.

Others: Ventriculo-atrial (VA) shunt, Ventriculopleural shunt.

Complications of Shunts

  • Infection: Usually Staphylococcus epidermidis.
  • Blockade: Mechanical kinks, choroid plexus, omentum.
  • Disconnection or Migration.
  • Over-drainage: Can lead to subdural hematoma (rapid decompression).

Definitive Surgery

Removal of obstructing lesions (e.g., posterior fossa tumor removal, endoscopic third ventriculostomy for aqueduct stenosis).
