BLADDER CARCINOMA

Introduction

  • Bladder cancer is a common urologic cancer.
  • 4th most common tumour in men after lung, prostate and colorectal cancer.
  • Most cases are transitional cell carcinoma (TCC) worldwide, often described as polyclonal field change defects. In Kenya the TCC and SCC are thought to be about 50% each.
  • Urothelium in the entire urinary tract may be involved, including the renal pelvis, ureter, bladder, and urethra.

Nonurothelial primary bladder tumors are rare and may include:

  1. Primary lymphoma
  2. Rhabdomyosarcoma
  3. Small cell carcinoma
  4. Carcinosarcoma
  5. Sarcoma

Demographics

  • Sex: The male-to-female ratio is 3:1. Women generally have a worse prognosis than men.
  • Age: The median age at diagnosis is 68 years, and the incidence increases directly with age
  • Race: Bladder cancer is more common in whites than blacks; however, blacks have a worse prognosis

Pathophysiology & Etiology

Pathophysiology

  • Adenocarcinomas: Account for less than 2% of primary bladder tumors. Observed most commonly in exstrophic bladders and persistent urachus. Poorly responsive to radiation and chemotherapy. Radical cystectomy is the treatment of choice.
  • Squamous cell carcinomas (SCC): Most are associated with persistent inflammations from chronic cystitis-schistosomiasis, long-term indwelling Foley catheters, or bladder stones.
  • Small cell carcinomas: Arise from neuroendocrine stem cells; aggressive tumors with poor prognosis.
  • Carcinosarcomas: Highly malignant tumors containing both mesenchymal and epithelial elements.
  • Leiomyosarcoma: Most common sarcoma of the bladder.
  • Rhabdomyosarcomas: Most commonly occur in children; poor prognosis.

Predisposing Factors

Environmental

  1. Cigarette Smoking: Nitrosamine, 2-naphthylamine, and 4-aminobiphenyl are possible carcinogenic agents.
  2. Industrial exposure: Aromatic amines in dyes, paints, solvents, leather dust, inks, combustion products, rubber, and textiles.
  3. Prior radiation treatments: To the pelvis.
  4. Cyclophosphamide: Exposure to acrolein (urinary metabolite) increases risk.
  5. Artificial sweeteners: (eg, saccharin, cyclamate).

Squamous Cell Carcinoma (SCC) Risk Factors

  • Chronic cystitis (Schistosomiasis)
  • Long-term indwelling Foley catheters
  • Bladder stone

Genetic

  • Mutations of tumor suppressor gene p53 (chromosome 17) and Rb gene (chromosome 13).
  • Mutations of tumor suppressor genes p15 and p16 (chromosome 9).

Clinical Presentation

History

  1. Painless global hematuria: mostly gross hematuria but may be microscopic.
  2. Irritative symptoms: dysuria, frequency, urgency.
  3. Obstructive symptoms: especially around the bladder neck (poor stream, straining, incomplete emptying).
  4. Passage of tissue particles.
  5. Mucosuria: Suggestive of Adenocarcinoma.
  6. Renal failure signs: edema.
  7. Advanced disease: Back pains, Hematochizia, Pain on defecation, Bone pain/weight loss.

Physical examination

Clinical examination is usually normal, although an abdominal or pelvic mass may be palpable in those with advanced disease.
Anemia - due to hematuria.

Differential Diagnosis (Causes of hematuria)

  • Hemorrhagic Cystitis: Noninfectious
  • Nephrolithiasis
  • Renal Cell Carcinoma
  • Transitional Cell Carcinoma, Renal
  • Ureteral Trauma
  • UTI

Investigations

Laboratory

  1. FHG: Hb (Anaemia), WBC (UTI), Platelets (Hemorrhage).
  2. Urinalysis: Nitrites (UTI). Microscopy: In surgical hematuria RBCs are not dysmorphic; in medical causes RBCs may appear dysmorphic.
  3. Urine cytology: For malignant cells.
  4. U/E/C: Upper tract involvement.
  5. Tumour markers: Bladder tumor antigen [BTA-Stat], Nuclear matrix protein [NMP-22], Fibrin/fibrinogen degradation products [FDP], Telomerase.
  6. Flexible cystoscopy: Urine for cytology, Biopsy of lesions, Resection of superficial tumors.

Imaging

  1. IVU (Intravenous Pyelogram): Criterion standard for upper-tract urothelium imaging. Picks distortion caused by tumor.
  2. Retrograde pyelography
  3. Pelvic ultrasonography: Cheap, available, no radiation. Picks tumours in kidneys/bladder but poor in ureters.
  4. CT-scan: For staging.
  5. CXR: Rule out pulmonary metastasis.

MANAGEMENT

Medical Therapy

Intravesical Immunotherapy (BCG)

Bacille Calmette-Guérin immunotherapy

Indication: CIS, T1 tumors, and high-risk Ta tumors. Decreases rate of recurrence and progression.

Mechanism: Live attenuated strain of Mycobacterium bovis. Induces nonspecific, cytokine-mediated immune response.

Contraindications & Side Effects

Use of BCG is contraindicated in patients with gross hematuria (risk of systemic sepsis).
Can cause granulomatous cystitis or prostatitis with bladder contraction. Can cause acute disseminated tuberculosis-like illness if enters bloodstream.

Intravesical Chemotherapy

  • Valrubicin: For CIS refractory to BCG.
  • Others: Thiotepa, mitomycin C, doxorubicin, epirubicin.

Chemotherapy for Metastatic Cancer

Standard treatment: MVAC (Methotrexate, Vinblastine, Adriamycin, Cisplatin).

Surgical Care

A) Superficial bladder cancer (Ta, T1, CIS)

Endoscopic resection and fulguration. Continued surveillance is mandatory.

B) Organ-confined, muscle-invasive bladder cancer (T2, T3)

Radical cystectomy with urinary diversions:

  • Men: Removal of bladder, prostate, and pelvic lymph nodes.
  • Women: Anterior pelvic exenteration (bladder, urethra, uterus, ovaries, anterior vaginal wall).
Urinary Diversions:
  • Ileal conduit (Incontinent): Segment of ileum used to conduct urine to stoma. Urine continuously collects in bag.
  • Indiana pouch (Continent): Reservoir created from right colon and terminal ileum. Continence mechanism is ileocecal valve. Empty via clean intermittent catheterization.

C) Locally advanced tumour (T4)

Treatment with radical radiotherapy. Role of salvage cystectomy if recurrence with radiotherapy.
