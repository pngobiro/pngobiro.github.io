INTESTINAL OBSTRUCTION IN PEDIATRICS

Causes & Clinical Presentation

Common Causes

A. Extramural causes

  • Congenital hernia
  • Malrotation
  • Peritoneal bands or volvulus
  • Annular pancreas

B. Intra luminal causes

  • Intestinal atresia or stenosis
  • Congenital hypertrophic pyloric stenosis
  • Meconium ileus / Meconium plug syndrome
  • Feccal impaction
  • Intussusception
  • Anorectal malformation

C. Mural causes

  • Hirschsprung's disease
  • Neonatal small left colon syndrome
  • Paralytic ileus (infections, electrolyte imbalances)

Clinical Presentation

The cardinal signs and symptoms are:

  1. Polyhydramnios in the mother: Prenatal diagnosis. Related to level of obstruction (5% in duodenal atresia, 15% in ileal atresia).
  2. Vomiting: Occurs early in upper obstruction. Bile-stained if distal to ampulla of Vater. Can lead to dehydration, shock, and hypokalemic/hypochloremic metabolic alkalosis.
  3. Abdominal distention: Related to the level of obstruction (marked in distal obstructions).
  4. Failure to pass meconium: Failure to pass within the first 24 hours is distinctly abnormal.
  5. Constipation: Absolute (obstipation) or Relative.

INTUSSUSCEPTION

Definition

This occurs when one portion of the gut becomes invaginated within an immediately adjacent segment (telescoping). The proximal segment (intussusceptum) enters the distal segment (intussuscipiens). It is the most common cause of intestinal obstruction in children under 2 years.

Type: Most common is Ileocolic. Others: Ileoileal, Ileoileo-colic, Jejunojejunal.

Demographics: M:F = 3:1. Peak age 5-9 months.

Etiology

  • Idiopathic (95%): Associated with lymphoid hyperplasia of Peyer's patches (often following viral URI/Rotavirus).
  • Lead Point (Pathological): Meckel's diverticulum, Polyps, Duplication cysts, Henoch-Schonlein purpura, Lymphoma.

Clinical Presentation

  1. Intermittent Colicky Abdominal Pain: Sudden onset of screaming and drawing up of legs in a previously well child. Attacks last a few minutes and recur every 15-20 mins.
  2. Reflex Vomiting: Follows pain.
  3. 'Redcurrant Jelly Stool': Late sign due to vascular compromise (mucus and blood).
  4. Lethargy/Pallor: Child appears drawn/listless between attacks.

Physical Examination

  • Abdominal Mass: Sausage-shaped lump in the right upper quadrant or mid-upper abdomen (concavity towards umbilicus).
  • Sign of Dance: Feeling of emptiness in the Right Iliac Fossa.
  • DRE: May show blood-stained mucus. Apex may be palpable.

Management of Intussusception

Investigations

  1. Abdominal Ultrasound (Diagnostic): Target sign / "Ring within a ring", Pseudo-kidney appearance.
  2. Abdominal X-ray: Soft tissue mass, absence of gas in cecum. Normal X-ray does not exclude it.
  3. Contrast Enema (Diagnostic & Therapeutic): Air or Barium. Contraindicated if peritoneal signs present.

Management

1. Supportive

IV fluids, Analgesia, NG tube decompression, Antibiotics (if peritonism).

2. Non-operative Reduction (First Line)

Pneumatic (Air) or Hydrostatic (Saline/Barium) Reduction:

  • Done under fluoroscopic or ultrasound guidance.
  • Contraindications: Peritonitis, perforation, shock, prolonged history (>24-48 hrs).
  • Success: Confirmed by reflux of air/contrast into terminal ileum and disappearance of mass.
  • Recurrence rate: 3-5%.

3. Surgical Reduction

Indications: Failed non-operative reduction, peritonitis, shock, pathological lead point.

Procedure:

  • Gentle manual reduction ("Squeezing" distally, never pulling).
  • Check viability.
  • Resection and anastomosis if gangrenous or irreducible.
  • Resection if lead point (e.g., Meckel's) found.
