INTESTINAL OBSTRUCTION

Classification & Etiology

Intestinal obstruction is classified based on the cause relative to the bowel wall:

A. Extramural Causes (Outside the bowel wall)

  • Hernias: Congenital or Acquired (Inguinal, Femoral, Incisional).
  • Adhesions/Bands: Post-surgical or inflammatory (Peritoneal bands).
  • Volvulus: Sigmoid, Caecal, or Midgut malrotation.
  • Annular Pancreas

B. Mural Causes (In the bowel wall)

  • Strictures: Ischemic, Inflammatory (Crohn's, TB), Anastomotic.
  • Tumors: Carcinoma (Colorectal), Lymphoma.
  • Congenital: Atresia, Stenosis, Hirschsprung's disease.
  • Paralytic Ileus: Functional obstruction due to infections or electrolyte imbalances (e.g., Hypokalemia).

C. Intraluminal Causes (Inside the lumen)

  • Fecal Impaction
  • Foreign Bodies / Bezoars
  • Gallstones: Gallstone ileus.
  • Meconium: Meconium ileus / Plug syndrome.
  • Intussusception: (Telescoping of bowel).
  • Parasites: Ascaris bolus.

Clinical Presentation

The cardinal signs and symptoms are:

  1. Abdominal Pain: Colicky in nature (cramping). Becomes continuous if strangulation occurs.
  2. Vomiting:
    • Early and profuse in high (proximal) obstruction.
    • Late and feculent in low (distal) obstruction.
    • Bile-stained: Indicates obstruction distal to the ampulla of Vater.
  3. Abdominal Distention: More marked in distal obstructions.
  4. Constipation:
    • Absolute (Obstipation): No passage of feces or flatus (Complete obstruction).
    • Relative: Passage of some flatus (Partial obstruction).

Signs of Strangulation/Peritonitis

Continuous pain, fever, tachycardia, localized tenderness, guarding, and leukocytosis suggest compromised blood supply and require urgent intervention.

MANAGEMENT

Initial Conservative Management ("Drip and Suck")

Indicated for uncomplicated obstruction, partial obstruction, or postoperative ileus.

  1. Nasogastric Tube (NGT): For gastrointestinal decompression (prevents aspiration, reduces distention).
  2. Intravenous Fluids: To correct dehydration and electrolyte imbalances (Hypokalemia, metabolic alkalosis/acidosis).
  3. Catheterization: To monitor fluid status (Input/Output).
  4. Antibiotics: Prophylactic or therapeutic (if bacterial translocation/sepsis suspected).
  5. Soapy Enema: May be used in partial distal obstruction (e.g., fecal impaction).

Surgical Management

Indications for Surgery

  • Peritonism (Signs of strangulation/perforation).
  • Continued deterioration despite conservative care.
  • Complete obstruction (virgin abdomen).
  • Irreducible hernia.

Surgical Procedures

  • Closed Bowel Procedures: Lysis of adhesions, reduction of intussusception, reduction of volvulus, reduction of incarcerated hernia.
  • Enterotomy: Opening bowel to remove intraluminal causes (bezoars, foreign bodies, gallstones).
  • Resection and Anastomosis: For gangrenous bowel, obstructing tumors, or strictures.
  • Bypass Procedures: To route contents around an unresectable obstruction.
  • Proximal Diversion: Enterocutaneous fistulae (Colostomy, Cecostomy) proximal to the obstruction (e.g., in unprepared bowel or unstable patients).
