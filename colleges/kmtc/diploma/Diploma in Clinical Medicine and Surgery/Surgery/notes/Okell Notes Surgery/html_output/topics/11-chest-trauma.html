CHEST TRAUMA

Classification & Overview

Broadly chest trauma could be:

  • Mechanical - high or low velocity
  • Chemical
  • Thermal

Mechanical trauma may be further classified as:

  1. Penetrating
  2. Non-penetrating - Blunt and Crush injuries
  3. Inhalational injury
  4. Aspiration of foreign body

2 major forces within the chest which lead to injury are the compression and distraction injuries. Compression results in destruction of vascular structures, haemorrhage, edema and impairment of function. Distraction injuries usually result in shearing forces which destroy the integrity of intrathoracic viscera.

Penetrating injuries

  • 61% of chest trauma admissions. Result in parenchymal damage related to track of missile or stabbing, implant used and velocity. Solid structures as the heart and major great vessels suffer more injuries
  • Most lethal complication is haemorrhage
  • Often associated with abdominal injury

Non-penetrating injuries

Blunt (acceleration and deceleration injuries): Sudden reduction in speed as in RTA. Aortic rapture as a cause of death on spot is not uncommon. Care should be taken while dealing with the very young or the elderly.

Crush injuries: Occurs where the elastic limit of the chest wall and its limit have been exceeded. Patients usually have AP deformity Majority have flail chests, hemothorax or pneumothorax Most has pulmonary contusion. Injuries of the heart, Aorta, liver, spleen, kidney are common.

In "Traumatic asphyxia" syndrome-constrictive forces are applied in wide area for as little as 2-5 minutes. Crush injuries have high mortality.

Clinical Presentation

History

  • History of chest trauma
  • Pain on breathing especially inspiration
  • Guarding of injury
  • Haemoptysis

Physical exam

  • Cyanosis, pale, cold skin
  • Tachypnea and tachycardia
  • Chest exam

Pathophysiology in chest injury

  1. Hypoxia - Reduced air exchange
  2. Acidosis - Anaerobic respiration
  3. Low Cardiac output - Hypovolemia and cardiogenic shock

2. SECONDARY SURVEY
3. INVESTIGATION

PRIMARY SURVEY

The majority of chest injuries are confined to the thoracic cage. These consist of rib fractures with underlying pulmonary contusion, haemothorax, or pneumothorax. Can be treated effectively simply by chest drain insertion and fluid restriction.

Early deaths caused by in chest injuries are usually caused by conditions that would impair breathing:

  1. Airway obstruction
  2. Open pneumothorax
  3. Massive hemothorax
  4. Tension pneumothorax
  5. Flail chest
  6. Cardiac tamponade

The basis for successful management of thoracic trauma is effective cardio pulmonary resuscitation followed by early detection and treatment of life-threatening injuries.

1. Airway and cervical spine

Maintain cervical spine immobilization in all unconscious or symptomatic (neck pain or tenderness) patients.

  • Inspect mouth remove debris by sweeping through
  • Chin lift/jaw thrust (tongue is attached to the jaw) and always airway in tongue falling back
  • Secure airway by Guedel airway/oropharyngeal airway.
  • Intubations; keep the neck immobilized in neutral position. Use sedation and short acting neuromuscular blockade if necessary.
  • If not possible to intubate: Tracheostomy or Cricothyrotomy

2. Breathing: Oxygenation and ventilation

Check for the six conditions that would impair breathing.

  • Inspect (LOOK): Respiratory rate, distress (Accessory muscles, flaring), Cyanosis, Chest movements, Penetrating injury, Flail chest.
  • Palpate (FEEL): Tracheal shift, Broken ribs, Subcutaneous emphysema, Chest expansion.
  • Percussion: Useful for diagnosis of haemothorax (dull) and pneumothorax (hyperesonant).
  • Auscultate (LISTEN): Decreased breath sounds, abnormal sounds.
Immediate intervention
  1. Tension pneumothorax: put through a needle then chest tube insertion
  2. Massive pneumothorax: chest tubes insertion
  3. Sucking wounds: strap the open wound then chest tube insertion
  4. Flail chest: positive pressure ventilation
  5. Cardiac tamponade

3. Circulation and arrest of bleeding

"Shock" is defined as inadequate organ perfusion and tissue oxygenation. Diagnosis is based on hypotension, hypothermia, tachycardia, tachypnoea, pallor, cold extremities, decreased capillary refill, decreased urine production.

  • Haemorrhagic (hypovolaemic): Due to acute loss. Femoral shaft fracture may lose up to 1.0-1.5 litres; Pelvic fracture > 2 litres.
  • Cardiogenic shock: Inadequate heart function (Myocardial contusion, Tamponade, Tension pneumothorax).
  • Neurogenic shock: Loss of sympathetic tone (spinal cord injury). Hypotension without reflex tachycardia.
  • Septic shock: Rare early, common cause of late death.
Resuscitation
  • Stop obvious bleeding.
  • Vascular access: 2 large bore size 16.
  • Fluids: 0.9% NaCl initially 2 L. Resuscitate to MAP > 90 mmHg.
  • Urinary catheter (monitor output 30-50 ml/hour).
  • Blood transfusion if unstable despite fluid infusion or Hb < 7 g/dl.

4. Neurological dysfunction

Establish preliminary level of consciousness by AVPU (Awake, Verbal, Painful, Unresponsive).

5. Exposure

Cover patient in case of shock and shivering.

ANATOMICAL CLASSIFICATION OF INJURIES

A. Skin and Subcutaneous tissue

Cuts and bruises. Superficial cuts: surgical toilet and suturing, analgesia and tetanus toxoid.

B. RIBS

1. Simple fractures

Main management is to provide analgesia, reduce pain and allow ventilatory movements. Analgesia may be systemic (Paracetamol, NSAIDS, Opiods) or local (Intercostal block).

2. Multiple fractures

Firm strapping of the thorax in a hemi-thorax fashion. Not done in older patients because may interfere with respiration.

3. Flail chest/segment

A portion of the chest wall becomes isolated by multiple fractures moves in and out with inspiration and expiration (paradoxical movement). Leads to atelectasis, hypercapnia, hypoxia.

Management options:

  • Hemi-thorax strapping
  • Positive pressure ventilation (Sedate patient)
  • Tracheostomy (Reduces dead space)
  • Thoracotomy and ORIF (Internal fixation)

C. PLEURA

Hemothorax

Classified by amount: Minimal (350 mL), Moderate (350-1500 mL), Massive (>1500 mL).
Management: Tube thoracostomy. In 85% of cases, tube thoracostomy and circulatory support are the only treatment required.

Indication for thoracotomy:

  • Initial drainage of fluid >1500 mL and still draining
  • Drainage rate >200 ml/hr for 3 or more hours

Pneumothorax

Types: Open, Closed, Tension.

  • Open: Prompt occlusive dressing before tube insertion.
  • Tension: Flap-valve leak allows air to enter but prevents escape. Relieve immediately (needle decompression) to avoid cardiac impairment.

Specific Organ Injuries

Trachea and Bronchus

Suspect with massive air leak or when lung does not re-expand after chest tube. Diagnosis: tracheobronchoscopy. Primary repair is indicated.

Lung Injury

Pulmonary contusion: Alveolar rupture with fluid/blood. leads to "wet lung". Clinical findings: loose, copious, blood-tinged secretions, respiratory distress. X-ray: patchy parenchymal opacification. Management: Mechanical ventilatory support.

Heart and Pericardium

Cardiac Tamponade: Distended neck veins, Muffled heart sounds, Shock (Beck's Triad). Immediate thoracotomy/decompression indicated.

Diaphragm

Penetrating injuries outnumber blunt (4:1). More common on left. Findings: Dyspnea, shoulder pain, unilateral breath sounds. Immediate repair required.

Esophagus

Perforation causes chest pain, fever, mediastinal emphysema (Hamman's sign). Management: NG tube, antibiotics, surgical closure.

Thoracic Duct

Chylothorax. Management: Chest tube, hyperalimentation. Ligation if chyle loss >1500 mL/day for 5 days.

The Sternum

Internal fixation required for deformity/pain.

Indications for Thoracotomy

  1. Cardiac tamponade
  2. Initial drain through the chest tube >1.5 L and continuing
  3. Hourly drain >200 ml/hr for more than 3 hours
  4. Electro cardiac dissociation
  5. Intraabdomial bleeding in excess indicating a thoracoabdominal injury
  6. Persistent pneumothorax even with adequate drainage suggesting injury to trachea or bronchi
  7. Diaphragmatic injury
  8. Large Chest wall defects
  9. High velocity injuries
  10. Impaled objects on the chest in situ
  11. Trans mediastinal injury
  12. Delayed indications: Clotted hemothorax, Lung abscess or fistula formation.
  13. Failure of resuscitation measures

Secondary Survey & Investigations

Basic Trauma investigation

  • CXR
  • Pelvic X-ray
  • C-spine x-ray
  • Skull x-ray

What to check for in CXR

  • Pneumothorax / Hemothorax
  • Subcuteneous emphysema
  • Pneumo-mediastinum / widening of mediastinum
  • Fractures (ribs, clavicle, sternum)
  • Pneumo or hemopericardium
  • Lung contusions
  • Aspiration

UNDERWATER SEAL DRAINAGE (U.W.S.D)

Indications

  1. Fluid in the pleural space
  2. Greater than 20% pneumothorax
  3. Tension pneumothorax

Principles

  • Drainage of fluid influenced by gravity.
  • Drainage of air influenced by pressure difference.
  • Return of air/fluid prevented by water seal.

Insertion Procedure

  1. Lateral position with pathology side up.
  2. Identify 4th intercostal space just above the 5th rib in triangle of safety (bordered anteriorly by anterior axillary line, posteriorly by mid axillary line and inferiorly by upper border of fifth rib).
  3. Clean, drape, infiltrate local anesthetic.
  4. Incision above the rib (avoid neurovascular bundle below rib).
  5. Blunt dissection into pleura.
  6. Insert tube (Posteriorly for hemothorax, towards apex for pneumothorax).
  7. Connect to UWSD system. Secure with suture.

Management of chest tube in situ

  • Monitor vital signs and respiratory pattern.
  • Observe UWSD for oscillation (swinging) and bubbling.
  • Keep bottle below patient level.
  • Never clamp except when changing bottles or lifting bottle above chest level.
  • Encourage deep breathing and coughing.

Removal

When hemothorax/pneumothorax drained (Clinical/Radiological check, drainage < 50-100 ml/24hrs).
Patient performs Valsalva maneuver during removal.

Complications

Acute: Haemothorax, Lung laceration, Diaphragm/Abdominal penetration, Tube malposition.

Late: Blocked tube, Retained haemothorax, Empyema, Pneumothorax after removal.
