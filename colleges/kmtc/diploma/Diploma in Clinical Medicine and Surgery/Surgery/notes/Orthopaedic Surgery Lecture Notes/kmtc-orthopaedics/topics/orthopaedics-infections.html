BONE AND JOINT INFECTIONS & TUBERCULOSIS

Pathology of Bone Infection

The pathological picture depends on:

  1. the patients age
  2. site of infection
  3. the virulence of organism and the host resistance.

Stages are:

  1. Inflammation
  2. Necrosis
  3. Reactive new bone formation
  4. Resolution.

The inflammation start with vascular congestion → exudation of fluid and infiltration by polymorphonuclear leukocyte → increase intra-osseous pressure → causing:

  1. Pain
  2. obstruction of blood flow
  3. intravascular thrombosis.

by the second or third day pus is formed within the bone and forces its way along the Volkmann's canals to the surface raising the periosteum

causing sub periosteal abscess with very severe pain. The pus spreads sub periosteally along the shaft to enter the bone at another level or burst in the surrounding soft tissue and push its way along the parts of least resistance to collect under the skin, later it may burst through the skin to open as a discharging sinus.

In infants infection may spread through the physis to the epiphysis and hence to the joint.

In older children the formed physis is a barrier to this intra articular spread, but in places where the metaphysis is intra capsular.

→ the pus may discharge through the periosteum to the joint cavity. In adults pus is likely to spread with in the medullary canal. Vertebral infection may spread through the end plate and inter vertebral disc to vertebral body.

Causes of bone death (by compromising blood supply):

  1. increase intra-osseous pressure.
  2. vascular stasis.
  3. infective thrombosis.
  4. periosteal stripping.
  5. Bacterial toxins and leukocytic enzymes.

Post Traumatic Osteomyelitis

It is osteomyelitis complicating open fractures predisposing factors are:

  1. tissue injury
  2. vascular damage.
  3. edema.
  4. hematoma.
  5. open pathway through skin wound.

staph aureus is the most common pathogen.

Clinical picture:

  1. Fever.
  2. pain.
  3. swelling.
  4. discharge from the fracture site.

Blood shows; raised ESR and TWBC.

Prophylaxis:

  1. Good cleaning of open fractures.
  2. Wound is left open.
  3. Use of antibiotics.

Treatment:

  1. Regular dressing.
  2. antibiotics.
  3. removal of dead tissue.
  4. use of external fixator to fix the fracture.

Chronic Osteomyelitis

It is chronic bone infection.

Causative organism:

usually mixed infection.

Pathology:

The bone is destroyed at: the focus of infection. cavities containing pus and dead pieces of bone sequestrum.

Surrounded areas of sclerotic bone.

Pus traces it is way to the skin sinus, which eventually close to open in a new site.

Factors of chronicity:

  1. The rigid walls of cavities does not close and prevent antibiotics to reach the bacteria.
  2. The sequestrum or implant act as adherent surface for bacteria until it is removed.

Clinical picture:

  1. pain.
  2. pyrexia.
  3. tenderness recurs on flare up.
  4. sinus discharge.
  5. pathological fracture.

Investigations:

  1. X-ray (show patchy bone resorption, cavitation, sequestrum and sclerosis of the surrounding bone).
  2. CT.
  3. MRI give more informations.
  4. ESR, TWBC is raised.
  5. Culture from the discharge repeatedly to identify the organism and it is sensitivity to antibiotics.

Treatment:

Antibiotic alone does not eradicate chronic osteomyelitis but it may help to prevent spread to healthy bone. to control acute flare ups.

Initial treatment:

  1. give NSAIDs.
  2. splint.

Local treatment:

  1. dressing of sinus.
  2. Abscess need drainage.

Operative treatment:

this method is limited only when sequestrum is present or if abscess or cavity is found (sequestrectomy, saucerisation).

By:

  1. Removal of dead tissues under antibiotic cover.
  2. Deroofing of cavities and saucerisation of walls of sclerotic bone.
  3. Intermittent irrigation with antibiotic using double lumen tube.
  4. Use of gentamicin beads.

Papineau technique which is deroofing and saucerisation then the cavity is obliterated by muscle flap.

Complication: malignant change.

Tuberculosis of Bone and Joint - Pathology

once the bacilli gain foot hold, they elicit chronic granulomatous reaction characterized by: collection of epithelioid multinucleated giant cells, surrounding an area of central necrosis and caseation, in the periphery round cells mainly lymphocytes.

In joint: The synovium is thickened and fibrosed giving marked effusion (TB arthritis).

In spine: pott's disease, paraplegia, quadriplegia.

In bone: destroyed with cavitation giving pathological fracture, and cavitation may open into a joint increased vascularity lead to hyperemia reflected as decreased bone density (osteoporosis).

If unchecked the process may proceed to cold abscess if the resistance is good it may overcome the inflammation and the lesion become dormant.

Later if the resistance decreased the inflammation may flare up again.

Also TB affect lung or gut (primary) or miliary TB (secondary), TB of bone and joint (tertiary).

Tuberculosis - Clinical Picture

  1. History of contact with T.B patient
  2. signs of chronic illness.
  3. loss of weight.
  4. low grade fever.
  5. night sweating.
  6. local pain. synovial thickening.
  7. swelling of one of the large joints with

In the spine pain may be mild until the patient present with cold abscess and muscle wasting.

Tuberculosis - Investigations

  1. X-ray show soft tissue swelling, periarticular osteoporosis - washed out bone - in children the epiphysis may be enlarged.
  2. ESR, TWBC is raised with relative lymphocytosis.
  3. Mantaux test is positive.
  4. Synovial Aspirate is yellow, straw coloured, cloudy with increased cells
  5. Synovial biopsy is more reliable and shows the characteristics of granuloma.
  6. MRI.

Tuberculosis - Diagnosis & Treatment

Differential diagnosis:

  1. Transient synovitis.
  2. Mono-articular rheumatoid arthritis.
  3. Hemorrhagic arthritis.
  4. Pyogenic arthritis.

Diagnostic Criteria:

  1. Long history.
  2. Involve only one joint.
  3. Marked synovial thickening.
  4. Marked muscle wasting.
  5. Peri-articular osteoporosis.
  6. Positive mantaux test.
  7. Other signs of tuberculosis.

Treatment:

  1. Rest and splintage.
  2. Anti-T.B. chemotherapy (combination of drugs).
  3. Operative drainage of T.B. focus or cold abscess.
  4. If spine collapse: decompression by vertebral fixation.

REHABILITATION

Rehabilitation is a crucial component of orthopaedic care, especially after infections and trauma. It involves restoring function, preventing complications, and helping patients return to their normal activities of daily living.

Key Points

  • •

    Acute osteomyelitis shows fever, pain, swelling and discharge from fracture site

  • •

    Chronic osteomyelitis requires surgical debridement plus antibiotics for treatment

  • •

    Tuberculosis presents with chronic illness, weight loss, and single joint involvement

  • •

    TB diagnosis requires positive Mantaux test and characteristic granuloma on biopsy
