FRACTURES & DISLOCATIONS OF THE LOWER LIMB

Differential Diagnosis of Hip Pain in Adults

  1. Fracture.
  2. Dislocation.
  3. Labrum tear.
  4. Septic arthritis.
  5. Osteoarthritis.
  6. Rheumatoid Arthritis.
  7. AVN.
  8. Gluteal tendonitis.

X-Ray of the hip; AP, lateral, judet (oblique view).

Pelvic Fracture

Most common fracture of pubic rami followed by ilial, ischial, acetabular, coccygeal, sacral bone. Tile Classification:

Type A:

Stable - Minimally displaced - Avulsion fracture not involving the pelvic ring (pubic rami fracture).

Type B:

Partially unstable - Rotationally unstable - Vertically stable (open-book fracture).

Type C:

Unstable - Rotationally and vertically unstable - Associated of rupture of ipsilateral ligaments (vertical shear fracture).

Diagnosis: By X-Ray and CT-scan.

Treatment:

ABCs, X-ray, CT, systemic approach.

Type A: symptomatic treatment.

Type B and C: ORIF with plates and screws.

Complications:

  1. Life-threatening hemorrhage.
  2. Bladder or bowel injury.
  3. Neurological damage.
  4. Post-traumatic Arthritis.
  5. Obstetrical difficulties.
  6. Persistent hip joint pain.

Hip Dislocation

Divided into:

  • Anterior (Rare - limb is fixed, externally rotated and abducted, diagnosed by CT).
  • Posterior (severe injury - hip is flexed, adducted, short and internally rotated associated with fracture of the acetabulum - diagnosed by CT scan).
  • Central (rare - associated with acetabular fracture).

Treatment:

  1. Muscle relaxation.
  2. Reduction.
  3. Skeletal traction.

If there is a fractured acetabulum - ORIF.

Complications:

  1. Post-traumatic arthritis.
  2. Femoral neck or shaft fractures.
  3. AVN.
  4. Sciatic nerve injury.
  5. Knee injury.
  6. Hip fracture.

Hip Fractures

Common fracture in elderly. More in female.

In young: due to high-velocity trauma.

Diagnosis by; history, examination and X-Ray. It gives antalgic gait.

Subcapital and Intertrochanteric Fractures

1. Subcapital Fractures

  • fracture between femoral head and intertrochanteric line
  • main vascular supply to femoral head from distal arterial ring to proximal head through femoral neck
  • fracture interrupts blood supply
  • articular surface restricts blood supply to femoral head
  • AVN risk depends on degree of displacement

2. Intertrochanteric Fracture

  • extra-capsular fracture, therefore good femoral head viability
  • fracture stability determined by amount of compromise to calcar femorale (medial cortex at neck/shaft junction)
  • greater and lesser trochanters may be separate fragments
  • posterior fragment may be avascular, therefore possible delayed union

Classification

  • 2 part - stable, trochanter intact
  • 3 part - one trochanter separated, unstable if large calcar fragment
  • 4 part - unstable, both trochanters separated

Treatment

ORFF (sliding hip screw) to preserve femoral head

Subtrochanteric Fracture & Femoral Shaft Fractures

3-Subtrochanteric fracture:

  • Fracture line 5 cm below the lesser trochanter.
  • Highly unstable.
  • Common in young.
  • Treated by intramedullary nail (PFN).

Fracture of the Femoral Shaft:

  • High-energy it can bleed 2 litre due: to gunshot or falling from a height.
  • Low-energy spiral fracture in children.
  • High morbidity and mortality (Hemorrhage, fat embolism and ARDS).
  • Blood replacement is essential.
  • There is soft tissue injury.
  • May be there is displacement.
  • Advantages of thomas splint.
  • In children high energy less commonly low energy.
  • In elderly due to pathological fracture May present with shock.
  • In children (<10 yrs) you can do hip spica cast.
  • Relief pain.
  • In child gallop fix
  • In Adult intramedullary nail good with weight bearing.
  • You can take off the plate after 6 month.

Treatment:

  1. ABCs.
  2. Analgesia.
  3. Intramedullary nail.
  4. Plate and screws.
  5. Elastic nail (in children).
  6. Early mobilization of hip and knee.

Knee, Ankle and Foot Injuries

Supracondylar fractures of the femur

  • High injury common in adults, associated with multiple trauma and knee ligamentous injury.
  • Poor prognosis, Usually comminuted
  • Treatment by: DCS or retrograde nailing DHS for femur neck.

Evulsion force or extension depend on age:

  • Elderly rupture quadriceps tendon.
  • 40 yr fracture of patella.
  • 20 yr they have avulsion of tibial tuberosity.
  • Children rupture of patellar tendon.

Treat by: K wire and screw.

Tibial fracture (mostly open)

  • avoid weight bearing Until 6 weeks then after 6 weeks we should bear weight (Wolff bear)
  • Sometimes angulation can be accepted up to 7° Shortening 1 cm.

Treatment:

  • ABC then ORIF,
  • if open fracture manage it.
  • elastic nails above knee cast.
  • bony walking cast injury can be open or closed fracture.

The knee:

Common knee symptoms:

  • Locking
  • Pseudo-locking, Instability - "giving out"
  • Traumatic knee swelling,
  • Non-traumatic knee swelling.

History:

  • Ligamental injuries → high-energy force.
  • Meniscal injury: Moderate force in young person Mild force in older person.

Examination:

  • Look: alignment Feel: crepitus, effusion Move: gait, strength.

Special tests of the knee:

  • Anterior and Posterior Draw tests - torn ACL and PCL.
  • Lachmann test - torn ACL.
  • Posterior Sag sign - torn PCL.
  • Pivot Shift sign - torn ACL
  • Collateral Ligament stress test - for collateral ligament.
  • Test for meniscal tear: Crouch compression + McMurray's test.

X-Rays of the Knee:

  • AP standing, lateral.
  • Skyline view.
  • Obliques for intra-articular fractures.
  • 3 foot standing view.

Ligamentous Injuries to the Knee

Hemarthrosis and/or effusion in acute phase.

Deceleration or pivoting injury.

1. Anterior cruciate ligament tear:

Indirect varus blow to the knee, inability to continue activity.

Positive: Lachmann, pivot shift, anterior draw.

Treatment: according to activity and functional impairment.

  • Stable: early mobilization, physiotherapy.
  • Instability: (ACL) anterior cruciate ligament reconstruction.

2. Posterior Cruciate Ligament Tear

Less common.

Positive posterior sag sign, positive posterior draw, reverse pivot shift sign.

Treatment: non-operative vs. surgical PCL reconstruction.

3. Medial Collateral Ligament Tear (MCL)

Valgus force to the knee.

Severe pain with partial tears but complete tear may be painless.

Tenderness above and below medial joint line.

Treatment:

  • Minor: immobilize briefly, early physiotherapy.
  • Moderate: Knee immobilizer or cast, early physiotherapy.
  • Severe or combined:
  • Surgical repair of associated injuries.
  • Surgery for isolated MCL repair is controversial.

4-Lateral Collateral Ligament Tear

Varus force to the knee.

Similar history and exam as for MCL.

There may be common peroneal nerve injury.

Treatment as for MCL.

Tibial, Ankle and Foot Fractures

Tibial Diaphysis Fracture:

  • High intensity injury, associated with open fracture.
  • Common soft tissue, nerve and vessel injury.
  • Displacement is difficult to control.
  • Good reduction is required.

Parameters of acceptance:

  • Shortening <1 cm.
  • Angulation in varus/valgus plane <5 degree.
  • Angulation in anterio-posterior plane <10 degree.
  • Rotation neutral to slight external rotation.
  • Apposition >50%
  • Healing time: 16 weeks.

Treatment:

  • ABCs
  • Closed injuries: closed reduction.
  • Open injuries: ORIF with ex.fix.
  • Unstable injuries or failed closed reduction require IM nail.
  • High risk for compartment syndrome (admit and observe).

The Ankle Joint

Fractures of the ankle involve:

  • Ipsilateral ligamentous tears or bony avulsion.
  • Contralateral shear fractures.

Pattern of fracture determined by: mechanism of injury:

  • Avulsion fractures are: transverse.
  • Shear fractures are: Oblique if pure inversion/eversion Spiral if rotational force.

X-ray Views:

AP, Lateral, Mortise (15 degrees internal rotation) Gives true view of talus in tibiotalar joint.

The foot

Types of fracture:

Talar fracture

  • 60% of talus is covered by articular surface → high risk of AVN.
  • Usually falling from a height.
  • X-rays: AP/lateral views with CT scan.
  • Treatment: BK cast NWB, ORIF.

Calcaneal fracture

  • Axial loading e.g. falling from a height.
  • Might be associated with other injuries e.g. spine...etc.
  • Heel is wider, shorter, flatter, varus tilt.
  • X-ray: Broden's view (oblique of subtalar joint) Bohler's angle is decreased.
  • Treatment: NWB cast, early mobilization.
  • ORIF if depressed centrally or tongue-type fracture.

Metatarsal fractures

  1. Avulsion of base of 5th MT - ORIF.
  2. Jones fracture midshaft 5th MT - symptomatic treatment.
  3. March "stress" fracture shaft 2nd, 3rd MT - ORIF.
  4. LisFranc fracture - ORIF.

Conditions of the foot:

Achilles Tendonitis: Chronic inflammation from running and shoe-wear.

May develop heel bumps.

Diagnosis: pain, crepitus on plantar flexion.

Treatment: rest, NSAIDs, proper footwear, do NOT inject steroids.

Key Points

  • •

    Hip dislocations are medical emergencies requiring immediate reduction

  • •

    Garden Classification determines treatment of femoral neck fractures

  • •

    ACL injuries present with positive Lachmann test and inability to continue activity

  • •

    Talar fractures have high AVN risk due to limited blood supply
