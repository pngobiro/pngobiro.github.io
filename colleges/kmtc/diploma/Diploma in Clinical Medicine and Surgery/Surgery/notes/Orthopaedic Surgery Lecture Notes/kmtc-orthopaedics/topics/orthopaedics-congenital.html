CONGENITAL DEFORMITIES

Overview

Remember varus and valgus conditions with joints it also congenital deformities, e.g. congenital coxa vara, genu varum...

Developmental Dysplasia of the Hip (DDH)

DDH defines the spectrum of hip instability ranging from the hip that is in joint but has a shallow (dysplastic) acetabulum and may be 'pushed out' (Barlow positive) to the dislocated hip that is irreducible (Ortolani negative).

Presentation according to age:

  • neonatal hips may be unstable.
  • toddler may limp.
  • adolescents may experience exercise-induced pain.
  • adult may have pain secondary to degenerative arthritis.

Aetiology of DDH

  • Gender: F > M
  • Breech presentation.
  • Birth order: common in firstborns and in the left hip
  • Oligohydramnios.
  • Family history.

Diagnosis:

  1. History and clinical assessment (Galeazzi, Barlow and Ortolani test).
  2. X-ray (von Rosens view).
  3. Arthrogram
  4. Ultrasound (antenatally for patient at risk).

DDH Management

(When diagnosed early, conservative treatment is usually successful, but after walking age surgery is required).

IN NEONATES:

Do not need treatment, most stabilize within first weeks of life.

IF remain unstable or dislocated at test treatment with:

harnesses (that allow controlled movement) or splints (that maintain hip reduction in the position of abduction and flexion).

IF the hips fail to relocate or stabilise then non-operative treatment should be discontinued.

IN INFANTS (>6 months):

IF fails conservative treatment, an examination under anaesthetic may result in a closed reduction, then do arthrogram if not show concentric reduction, it will indicate which structures are blocking reduction.

A closed reduction will need to be held reduced with a hip spica cast for several months.

IF the hip is irreducible or can only be held reduced in an extreme position with a small safe zone, then treatment must be abandoned and an open reduction considered via a medial or anterior approach.

In YOUNG CHILD:

  • open reduction.
  • pelvic osteotomy (may be required).

In ADOLESCENT and young adult

These hips are often subluxed rather than truly dislocated.

On investigation, the hip may be reducible and the joint can be recreated with a combination of pelvic and femoral osteotomies.

For the irreducible hip, acetabular augmentation may reduce symptoms and delay the onset of degenerative change.

Congenital Talipes Equinovarus (Clubfoot)

The clubfoot is deformed in three planes.

In true congenital talipes equinovarus (CTEV) the deformity is fixed.

Components: (CAVE)

  • C - cavus: increase planter arch.
  • A - adduction of forefoot.
  • V - varus: inversion of fore-mid-hind foot.
  • E - equinovarus: plantar flexion of ankle.

Treatment:

  1. By Ponseti method: (specific series of manoeuvres, followed by a series of well moulded above knee plaster casts).
    • The standard sequence is elevation of the first ray, gradual forefront abduction to 60° and, finally, dorsiflexion, usually following Achilles tenotomy.
    • Tibialis anterior transfer can be used to correct dynamic supination in older children
  2. Advantages: less stiff, less likely to be painful and less subject to overcorrection.
  3. 2- Surgical intervention required when conservative treatment fails. (ideally before walking age).

Slipped Upper Femoral Epiphysis (SUFE) & Perthes Disease

Slip of the upper (capital) femoral Epiphysis (SUFE)

The physis connects the proximal femoral epiphysis (the femoral head) to the metaphysis (femoral neck).

There is painful limitation of hip movement.

Hilton's law, which states that a joint is supplied by the same nerves as the muscles that move the joint, explains why many children present with knee pain although the pathology is in the hip.

Treatment:

  • AVN risk so reduce by prompt screw fixation into the centre of the epiphysis that stabilises the 'fracture'.
  • Realignment osteotomy (require if patient present with deformity leading to restriction of joint movement after healing).

Legg-Calvé-Perthes' disease

AVN leads to femoral head collapse, healing occurs with the return of the blood supply

Factors implicated in the pathogenesis of Perthes' disease

  1. Low birth weight
  2. High birth order
  3. Delayed bone age
  4. Low socioeconomic status

Causes of avascular necrosis of the femoral head

  1. Steroids
  2. Infection
  3. Perthes' disease
  4. Sickle cell disease
  5. Hypothyroidism
  6. Skeletal dysplasia - classically multiple epiphyseal dysplasia

Management aims to maintain femoral head sphericity

Treatment:

  • non-surgical (to maximise range of movement).
  • surgical (for containment or 'salvage')

The prognosis is better in younger children who have more remodelling potential.

Key Points

  • •

    DDH most common in female firstborns with breech presentation

  • •

    Ponseti method is the gold standard for clubfoot treatment

  • •

    SUFE requires urgent screw fixation to prevent AVN

  • •

    Perthes disease affects children with AVN of femoral head
