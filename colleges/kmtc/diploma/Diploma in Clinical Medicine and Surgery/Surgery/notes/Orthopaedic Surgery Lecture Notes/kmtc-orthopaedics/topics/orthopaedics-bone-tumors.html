BONE TUMOR

Overview

A swelling of a part of the body, generally without inflammation, caused by an abnormal growth of tissue, whether benign or malignant. cancerous growth, malignant growth, cancer, malignancy.

Classification according to:

Cell type: Bone, Cartilage, Fibrous, Marrow, others.

Benign and Malignant Tumors

Benign:

  • Osteoid osteoma
  • Chondroma
  • Osteochondroma
  • Fibroma
  • Hemangioma
  • Giant cell tumours

Malignant:

  • Osteosarcoma
  • Chondrosarcoma
  • Fibrosarcoma
  • Angiosarcoma
  • Malignant Giant cell Tumours

Stages of tumor by Enneking classification (American joint committee).

Stage I: All low grade sarcoma.

Stage II: His High grade lesions.

Stage III: Sarcoma which have metastasized.

Each stage A: intra compartmental. and B: Extracompartmental.

Clinical Features

History:

  • Chronicity prolonged = delay of get TT.
  • Age to some extend is useful clue.
  • Ewing's, Osteosarcoma usually childhood and adolescence,
  • Chondrosarcoma, fibrosarcoma 4th to 6th decade,
  • Myeloma seldom before 6th decade, >70 metastasis.

NOTE: patient may be completely asymptomatic.

History of trauma (Injuries initiates pathological change or draws Attention to what is already there....

Pain (The common cause by rapid expansion with stretching of Tissue,

Hemorrhage, fracture).

Swelling (Like lump if it become painful or continuous growing).

Paraesthesia

Numbness

Pressure upon or stretching of peripheral nerve

Always sign of aggressive tumours.

Examination:

Lump ill-defined, soft, hard, pulsatile, tender, diffuse

Skin warm inflamed

Effusion limitation of movement.

Lymphatic drainage

Investigations (imaging):

  • X-ray
  • CT
  • MRI

MANAGEMENT generally: it is multidisciplinary approach:

Pathologist, Radiotherapist, Surgeon ...

Intra capsular, marginal wide local, radical resection, excision.

Specific Bone Tumors

OSTEOID OSTEOMA

  • Tiny, symptom out of proportion to it size.
  • >30, Male. any bone except skull.
  • >50% femur, tibia.
  • Persistent pain localized or referred, relieved by Salicylates.
  • Small radiolucent area (nidus).
  • the matrix dark brown or reddish.
  • Treatment: Complete removal.

OSTEOCHONDROMA

  • Cartilage - capped Exostosis.
  • Developmental, Physeal plate, ends of long bone, ilium
  • Teenage, well defined exostosis emerging from Metaphysis.
  • Hyaline cartilage in Pathology, if continuous grows so considered as chondrosarcoma
  • Treatment: Excised if cause pain.

BONE CYST

  • Solitary cyst.
  • Metaphysis of humerus & femur.
  • well demarcated radiolucent, up to plate.
  • Fibrous dysplasia, cartilage tumours
  • Colored Straw by needle injection of methyl prednisolone.
  • Surgery bone chips.

CHONDROSARCOMA

  • Primary or secondary cartilage. 4th-5th.
  • M>F.
  • Dull ache pain, enlarge lump. Pathological -fractures
  • Any bone metaphysis.
  • Scapula and pelvis more susceptible for malignancy.
  • Enchondroma medullary can go too.
  • Treatment: Wide excision and prosthetic replacement
  • No chemotherapy or radiotherapy

OSTEOSARCOMA.

  • Classic intramedullary.
  • Highly malignant within the bone and spreading outwards.
  • Affect Children & adolescents.
  • Metaphysis, Knee, Humerus proximal.
  • O/E: Lump, pathological fracture, tender, swelling inflammation.
  • Ill defined endosteal, new bone formation.
  • On x-ray: Sunburst, Sun spikes, Codman's triangle.
  • Treatment: Chemotherapy + surgery.

MULTIPLE MYELOMA

  • B - cell lymphoproliferative disorder of bone marrow.
  • Plasma cells predominant, 45-65
  • Osteoclast increase - osteoporosis - lytic
  • Plasma protein abnormal - anemia- viscosity
  • Hypercalcemia common of osteoporosis vertebral fracture.
  • Treatment: Multiple punch.

Key Points

  • •

    Osteosarcoma affects children/adolescents and shows sunburst pattern on X-ray

  • •

    Chondrosarcoma occurs in 4th-5th decade and requires wide excision

  • •

    Osteoid osteoma causes pain out of proportion to size, relieved by salicylates

  • •

    Multiple myeloma is a plasma cell disorder causing osteoporosis and vertebral fractures
