PRINCIPLES OF FRACTURE MANAGEMENT

General Treatment of Injured Patients

  • First aid at the scene of accident
  • Transport to a hospital
  • Resuscitation: Airway, Breathing & Circulation

Specific Treatment of Fractures

  1. Reduce
  2. Immobilise
  3. Rehabilitate

REDUCE

Can be closed or open reduction.

Closed reduction or manipulation under anaesthesia for:

  1. Minimally displaced fractures.
  2. Fractures in children.
  3. Fractures that are stable after reduction.

Open reduction for:

  1. Failure of closed reduction.
  2. Displaced intra-articular fractures.
  3. Unstable fractures.
  4. Traction fractures

Fracture Immobilization

  1. CONTINUOUS TRACTION
  2. CAST SPLINTAGE
  3. FUNCTIONAL BRACING
  4. INTERNAL FIXATION
  5. EXTERNAL FIXATION

1. Continuous Traction

Is applied to the limb distal to the fracture to exert continuous pull in the long axis of the bone.

Types:

  1. skin traction
  2. skeletal

SKIN TRACTION

Two adhesive strips are stuck on to either side of the leg & weights are attached to these strips by a rope (for femoral shaft fractures in children).

Indications:

  1. for children
  2. Temporary management of femur neck fracture or intertrochanteric fracture preoperatively.
  3. Undisplaced fracture of acetabulum.
  4. After reduction of dislocation of hip.

Contraindication:

Skin damaged or sepsis in the area.

Complications:

  1. Allergic reaction
  2. Pressure sore
  3. Excoriation of skin

SKELETAL TRACTION

through a pin placed in the tibia for femoral shaft fractures in adults. This can be fixed or balanced by gravity - a hanging cast for displaced fractures of the humeral shaft.

Complications:

  1. Infection
  2. Incorrect placement of pin
  3. Stiffness of joint

2. Cast Splintage

Can be plaster of Paris or synthetic casts.

A posterior slab is usually used initially followed by a complete plaster when the swelling subsides.

Adequate padding must be used over bony prominences.

The joint above and below the fractured bone must be immobilised.

The limb must be elevated & circulation distally should be checked.

⚠ Complications

tight cast, pressure sore, ulcerations, infections, rashes, itching, burns, and allergic contact dermatitis...

Uses of plaster of paris:

  1. Fracture.
  2. dislocations.
  3. correcting deformities.

3. Functional Bracing

Is one way of preventing joint stiffness

Applied only after 3-6 weeks of traction or conventional plaster

Using either pop or one of the lighter materials

4. Internal Fixation

Indications:

  • failure of closed reduction
  • unstable fractures
  • pathological fractures
  • fractures that unite poorly (femoral neck)
  • multiple fractures

Methods:

  1. INTERFRAGMENTARY SCREWS
  2. WIRES (TRANSFIXING, CERCLAGE, TENSION-BAND)
  3. PLATE & SCREWS
  4. INTRAMEDULLARY NAILS
  5. STAPLES

Complications of IF:

  1. INFECTION
  2. NON-UNION
  3. IMPLANT FAILURE
  4. REFRACTURE

5. External Fixation

Transfixing screws are passed through the bone and are attached to an external frame.

Types:

  1. Tubular (AO)
  2. Ring (ILIZAROV)

Indications:

  1. Fractures associated with severe soft tissue injury.
  2. Severe multiple injuries (associated with chest injury).
  3. Pelvic fractures.
  4. Infected fractures.

Complications:

  1. Soft tissue injury
  2. Overdistraction
  3. Pin-track infection

EXERCISE: (Rehabilitation)

Initial Management:

  • Prevent edema - elevate limb.
  • Upper limbs - sling.
  • Lower limbs - elevate on pillows/chair.

Active exercise:

  • Helps circulation.
  • Decreases edema.
  • Helps to avoid joint stiffness.
  • Gentle assisted movements are also helpful (CPM).
  • Gradual return to functional activity.

Key Points

  • •

    Fracture treatment follows 3 principles: Reduce, Immobilise, Rehabilitate

  • •

    Skin traction is indicated for children; skeletal traction for adults

  • •

    Internal fixation complications include infection, non-union, and implant failure

  • •

    External fixation is used for severe soft tissue injuries and infected fractures
