PERIPHERAL NERVE INJURY

Nerve Structure and Function

Peripheral nerves are: bundles of axons conducting efferent motor impulses from AHC (anterior horn cells) of spinal cord to the muscles.

afferent sensory impulses from peripheral receptors via dorsal root ganglia to the spinal cord.

some nerves are mainly motor, some are mainly sensory some are mixed nerves.

Nerve structure and functions:

  • Myelination: increase speed of conduction of impulses.
  • Bundle of axons make fascicles which make the nerve.
  • Epineurium: cover group of nerves.
  • Perineurium: cover group of fascicles.
  • Endoneurium: layer directly cover the axon.

Nerve Injury Mechanisms

Nerves can be injured by:

  1. ischemia.
  2. compression.
  3. traction.
  4. laceration.
  5. burning.

According to Seddon nerve lesion are classified into:

  1. Neuropraxia: (it is reversible loss of nerve function).
  2. Axonotmesis: more severe injury; (its interruption of some axons, but the neural tube is intact).
  3. Neurotmesis: (is division of nerve trunk).

Clinical Presentation

After any trauma you should take history and examine the patient for peripheral nerve injury (motor, sensory, and observe for abnormal position/dysfunction).

Nerve Injury Management

Conservative:

if closed injuries that recover spontaneously.

Operative: (exploration) indicated if:

  1. (a) the nerve was seen to be divided.
  2. (b) if it is a cut wound or high energy injury.
  3. (c) if recovery is inappropriately delayed.

primary repair is best if it can be done safely or delayed repair may be indicated.

Technique of repair:

  1. (a) direct suture
  2. (b) nerve graft
  3. (c) nerve transfer.

Prognosis Factors

Prognosis depends on:

  1. type of lesion
  2. level of lesion
  3. type of nerve
  4. size of gap
  5. age of patient
  6. time of repair
  7. associated injuries
  8. surgical technique.

GOOD NERVE (Mnemonic)

  • G - growing age
  • O - only motor
  • O - only sensory
  • D - distal
  • N - neuropraxia
  • E - early nerve
  • R - radial nerve
  • V - vascularity
  • E - end to end

Median Nerve Injury

Remember

the injury in the forearm or wrist:

  • There will be paralysis of the small muscles of the thumb so that the thenar eminence is wasted.
  • The patient is unable to abduct the thumb.

Carpal Tunnel Syndrome

(The median nerve is compressed as it passes through the carpal tunnel).

This results in:

  1. wasting of the thenar eminence.
  2. diminished sensation.
  3. unpleasant pain (characteristically at night).
  4. paresthesia (numbness and tingling) in the thumb and radial two fingers and sometimes paresthesia extending up into the arm.

Examinations:

  1. Froments test or good test for weakness of interossei (ulnar nerve)
  2. OK sign test for (median nerve) also can be for all nerves of upper limb.
  3. Thumb up test for (radial nerve).

Lower Limb Nerves

1. Sciatic nerve:

This nerve injured due to:

  1. Penetrating injuries.
  2. torn in posterior dislocation of the hip associated with fracture of the posterior lip of the acetabulum (which the nerve is closely related).

-Injury is followed by paralysis of the hamstrings and all the muscles of the leg and foot.

-There is loss of all movement below the knee joint with foot drop deformity.

-Sensory loss is complete below the knee, except for an area extending along the medial side of the leg over the medial malleolus to the base of the hallux, which is innervated by the saphenous branch of the femoral nerve.

2. Common peroneal nerve:

  • It injured by direct trauma or compression, such as the pressure of a tight plaster cast, or in severe adduction injuries to the knee.
  • Damage is followed by foot drop (due to paralysis of the ankle and foot extensors) and inversion of the foot (due to paralysis of the peroneal muscles with unopposed action of the foot flexors and invertors).
  • There is loss of sensation over the anterior surface of the leg and foot.

Care of Paralyzed Part

⚠ Important

Special care must be taken of paralyzed limbs to prevent complications such as pressure sores, contractures, and joint stiffness.

Key Points

  • •

    Seddon classification: neuropraxia, axonotmesis, neurotmesis

  • •

    Sciatic nerve injury causes paralysis of hamstrings and all leg/foot muscles

  • •

    Common peroneal nerve injury leads to foot drop and loss of ankle dorsiflexion

  • •

    Carpal tunnel syndrome causes thenar wasting and nocturnal paresthesia
