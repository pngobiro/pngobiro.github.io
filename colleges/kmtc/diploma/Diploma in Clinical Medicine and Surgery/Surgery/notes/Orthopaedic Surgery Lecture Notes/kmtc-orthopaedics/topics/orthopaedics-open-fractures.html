OPEN FRACTURES & FRACTURE COMPLICATIONS

Gustilo & Anderson Classification

Grade I

  • Small, clean wound less than 2 cm long.
  • No crushing or comminution.

Grade II

  • Wound 2 cm to 5 cm long. Slight contamination
  • Moderate crushing and comminution.

Grade III

Wound greater than 5 cm.

  • (A) Marked damage to soft tissues and considerable contamination and comminution of fracture & periosteal stripping.
  • (B) As in A but bone cannot be adequately covered by soft tissue.
  • (C) Associated with nerve and vascular injury.

Early Management (Emergency Room)

  1. ABCs
  2. Dressing over the wound.
  3. IV antibiotics
  4. Tetanus prophylaxis

Compartment Syndrome

Increased interstitial pressure in compartment exceeds capillary perfusion pressure which leads to muscle necrosis and eventually nerve necrosis.

Intracompartmental pressure over 30 mmHg usually requires intervention

Causes:

  1. Fracture-dislocation
  2. Soft tissue damage and muscle swelling
  3. Crush injury
  4. Venous obstruction
  5. Increased venous pressure
  6. Constrictive cast or splint

Usually it is present in the tibia and forearm

Flexor compartment ‚Üí Volkmann's ischemic contracture

When intracompartmental pressure rises to $10-30 \text{ mmHg}$ of patient's diastolic pressure, inadequate perfusion and ischemia results.

  • Pain out of proportion to injury
  • Pain with passive motion
  • Paralysis, Paresthesia, Pallor, Poikilothermia, Pulselessness

Treatment:

  • Remove constrictive dressings
  • Place the limb at the level of the heart
  • Emergency fasciotomy to release compartments if the intracompartmental pressure rises to $40-45 \text{ mmHg}$ within $4-6$ hours from onset of symptoms and signs.
