PRINCIPLES OF ORTHOPAEDICS

Basic Definitions

What is valgus?

Is outward angulation of the distal segment of a bone or joint.

What is varus?

Is medial deviation of the distal bone.

The condition termed by latin word according to bone or joint affected:

  • hip = coxa
  • knee = genu
  • ankle = talipes
  • foot = pes
  • elbow = cubitus

Example: coxa valgus, coxa varus...

What is dislocation?

Is complete disruption of the joint, while subluxation is partial dislocation.

What are fractures?

Is a break in the structural continuity of bone

Types of Fractures

According to:

  1. Mechanism of injury
  2. Integrity of the skin
  3. Complete or incomplete
  4. Configuration of fracture
  5. Displacement

Causes of Fractures

TRAUMATIC

  • 1-DIRECT
  • 2-INDIRECT

STRESS

PATHOLOGICAL

  • 1- GENERALIZED BONE DISEASE
  • 2- LOCALIZED BONE DISEASE

Mechanism of Injury

1. TRAUMATIC INCIDENT

DIRECT FORCE

  • The bone breaks at the point of impact
  • Causes transverse fracture
  • Damage to the overlying skin

INDIRECT FORCE

  • The bone breaks at a distance from where force is applied
  • Soft tissue damage at the fracture site is not inevitable.

Indirect injury:

  • Twisting causes = a spiral fracture
  • Compression = a short oblique
  • Bending = butterfly fragment
  • Tension = transverse = avulsion

2. Stress Fracture

  • A stress fracture is an overuse injury.
  • It occurs when muscles become fatigued and are unable to absorb added shock. Eventually, the fatigued muscle transfers the overload of stress to the bone causing a tiny crack called a stress fracture.
  • This most often seen in tibia, fibula and metatarsals in athletes, dancers and army recruits.

3. Pathological Fracture

Abnormal weakening of the bone results in fracture with normal stresses.

Causes are:

a. Generalized bone diseases

  1. Osteogenesis imperfecta
  2. Postmenopausal osteoporosis
  3. Metabolic bone disease
  4. Myelomatosis
  5. Polyosteotic fibrous dysplasia
  6. Paget's disease

b. Local benign conditions

  1. Chronic infection
  2. Solitary bone cyst
  3. Fibrous cortical defect
  4. Chondromyxiod fibroma
  5. Aneurysmal bone cyst
  6. Chondroma c.Primary
  7. Monostotic fibrous dysplasia

c. Malignant tumours

  1. Chondrosarcoma
  2. Osteosarcoma
  3. Ewing's sarcoma

d. Metastatic tumours

Ca breast, lung, kidney, thyroid, colon and prostate

Integrity of the Skin

1. CLOSED OR SIMPLE

If the overlying skin remains intact

2. OPEN OR COMPOUND

If the skin or one of the body cavities is breached, fracture liable to contamination and infection

Complete or Incomplete

COMPLETE FRACTURE:

The bone is completely broken into two or more fragments.

  • Transverse = stable after reduction
  • Oblique or spiral = unstable
  • Impacted = the fracture line indistinct
  • Comminuted = is one in which there are more than two fragments = unstable

INCOMPLETE:

the bone is incompletely divided and the periosteum remains in continuity

Example:

  • Greenstick fracture = the bone is buckled or bent. this is seen in children
  • Compression fracture = cancellous bone

Displaced or Undisplaced

Causes of displacement:

  1. The force of the injury
  2. The gravity
  3. The pull of muscles

Description of displacement:

  1. Translation or shift - backwards, forwards, sideways or longitudinally with impaction or overlap.
  2. Alignment or angulation - sideways, backwards or forwards
  3. Rotation or twist in any direction.
  4. Length

Open Displaced:

  • Through the skin
  • Not aligned
  • Can have displaced fx that do not come through the skin

Fracture Healing

  1. Tissue damage and hematoma formation.
  2. Inflammation and cellular proliferation.
  3. Callus formation.
  4. Consolidation.
  5. Remodeling

Gap healing or contact healing less than 200 μm

Clinical Symptoms

  • Injury.
  • Inability to use the affected limb.
  • Pain.
  • Swelling.
  • Deformity and crepitation
  • Weakness

History and Examination

History:

You should write patient story of presenting illness to know mechanism of injury and analyse the symptoms and complete detail history (systemic review, drug history ...).

Examination:

  • Exclude vital injuries.
  • Examine the limb for the fractures and dislocation.
  • Examine for nerve, vascular or tendon damage.

O/E (Look, feel, move)

Look:

Wound, Bruises, Swelling, Deformity, Shortening, Abnormal posture.

Feel:

Tenderness.

Feel the distal part to exclude nerve or vascular injury.

Move:

Crepitation.

Abnormal movement.

Description of the Fracture

  1. Closed or open.
  2. Which bone is broken and where.
  3. Anatomical location - joint
  4. Articular involvement.

By x-ray describe fracture shape or configuration - simple or comminuted

  1. Stability.
  2. Any complications and secondary injury.

X-ray and Imaging

Remember the rule of twos:

  • Two views - AP/LAT
  • TWO JOINTS, esp in forearm and leg
  • TWO LIMB, in children for comparison
  • TWO INJURY, severe force often causes injury more than one level
  • TWO OCCASION, some Fx are notoriously difficult to detect soon after injury

Read the x-ray:

  1. Name of patient
  2. when and where (date and place)
  3. film projection
  4. quality of x-ray
  5. describe the bone (anatomical site, bone cortex, medullary canal, bone density, if there break in the continuity of bone describe it - Don't say fracture -, if there articular loss - Don't say dislocation -)
  6. your diagnosis (fracture type and site in the bone / dislocation bilateral or unilateral anterior or posterior, anatomical position).

SPECIAL IMAGING:

  • TOMOGRAPHY.
  • COMPUTED TOMOGRAPHY - CT
  • MAGNETIC RESONANCE IMAGING - MRI
  • RADIOISOTOPE SCANNING

Key Points

  • •

    ORTHOPAEDICS is a branch of surgery concerned with conditions involving the musculoskeletal system

  • •

    Valgus is outward angulation, varus is medial deviation of the distal bone

  • •

    Fracture healing involves: tissue damage, inflammation, callus formation, consolidation, and remodeling

  • •

    Remember the rule of twos for X-ray imaging
