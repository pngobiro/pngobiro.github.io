In grading of pelvic organ prolapse grading. Which one is NOT correctly matched.
A. Grade I – Leading edge of the prolapse remains 1 cm or more above the hymen
B. Grade II – leading edge of prolapse extends from 1 cm above to the level of the hymen
C. Grade III – Leading edge of prolapse extends beyond hymen but without complete vaginal eversion
D. Grade IV – Complete eversion of the vagina
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Grade II – leading edge of prolapse extends from 1 cm above to the level of the hymen

Explanation: Under the POP-Q system, Stage II is defined as the leading edge being between 1 cm above and 1 cm below the hymen. The description in option B is incomplete or slightly inaccurate compared to standard definitions (which include up to 1 cm below), making it the "not correctly matched" or least accurate description in this context.

B. Occurs in women with a mean age of 60 years
C. Prognosis is poor regardless of the stage
D. Is associated with progesterone effects on the endometrium
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Occurs in women with a mean age of 60 years

Explanation: Endometrial cancer is primarily an adenocarcinoma (not squamous) affecting postmenopausal women. Prognosis is generally good if detected early (Stage I). It is associated with unopposed estrogen, not progesterone (which is protective).

40.
During resuscitation, you assess for breathing and you establish that there is no chest movement. The following is the best action to take;
A. Do cardiopulmonary resuscitation at 3 chest compressions to one breath.
B. Give 40-60 ventilations in one minute
C. Put up oxygen via nasal prong at one litre per minute.
D. Give five rescue breaths.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Give 40-60 ventilations in one minute

Explanation: In neonatal resuscitation, if the baby is not breathing (apneic), positive pressure ventilation (PPV) is the priority. The target rate is 40-60 breaths per minute. Chest compressions (3:1 ratio) are only indicated if the heart rate remains < 60 bpm after adequate ventilation.

Section 2: Short Answer Questions

40 Marks
41.
Mary, Para 1+0 gravida 2 at 30 weeks gestation presents to you with complaints of lower abdominal pains which started at night. She describes the pains as on and off. She also reports of a blood mucoid discharge per vagina. No urinary symptoms reported. The fetal heart rate is 140b/min, on pelvic examination, the cervical os is at 2cm. Outline your plan of management. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Diagnosis: Preterm Labour.

Plan of Management:

  1. Admission: Admit Mary to the antenatal ward for bed rest and monitoring.
  2. Corticosteroids: Administer Dexamethasone 6mg IM 12-hourly for 4 doses to accelerate fetal lung maturity.
  3. Tocolysis: Administer tocolytics (e.g., Nifedipine) to suppress uterine contractions and delay delivery for at least 48 hours to allow steroids to work.
  4. Neuroprotection: Administer Magnesium Sulphate (MgSO4) for fetal neuroprotection (since gestation is < 32 weeks).
  5. Antibiotics: Give prophylactic antibiotics if infection is suspected or membranes rupture.
  6. Monitoring: Monitor maternal vitals, fetal heart rate, and labor progression.
42.
Fatuma, 30-year-old primigravida at 33 weeks gestation presents to the outpatient department with complaints of severe headache, epigastric pain, and swelling of the lower limbs. On Examination, her pulse rate is 86 beats/minute, Blood pressure 160/118mmHg, the Fetal Heart rate is at 132 beats/Minute and regular. Describe the principles of management of Fatuma. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Diagnosis: Severe Pre-eclampsia.

Principles of Management:

  1. Seizure Prophylaxis: Administer Magnesium Sulphate (Loading dose 4g IV + 10g IM, followed by maintenance) to prevent eclampsia.
  2. Blood Pressure Control: Administer rapid-acting antihypertensives (e.g., Hydralazine 5mg IV or Labetalol) to lower BP safely (target diastolic 90-100 mmHg).
  3. Fluid Management: Restrict fluids (e.g., 80ml/hr) and insert a Foley catheter to strictly monitor urine output and prevent pulmonary edema.
  4. Fetal Monitoring & Maturation: Assess fetal well-being and administer corticosteroids for lung maturity.
  5. Delivery: Stabilize the mother and plan for delivery (induction or C-section) as definitive treatment, given the severity.
43.
Post-partum haemorrhage (PPH) is a leading direct cause of maternal mortality. Describe how you will actively manage third stage of labour to prevent PPH. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Active Management of Third Stage of Labour (AMTSL):

  1. Rule out 2nd Twin: Palpate the abdomen to ensure no other baby is present.
  2. Administer Uterotonic: Give Oxytocin 10 IU IM within 1 minute of the baby's birth.
  3. Controlled Cord Traction (CCT): Apply gentle traction on the cord with one hand while applying counter-traction to the uterus just above the pubic bone (Brandt-Andrews maneuver) during a contraction.
  4. Uterine Massage: Massage the fundus immediately after placental delivery until it is firm and contracted.
  5. Monitor: Check for bleeding and uterine tone every 15 minutes for the first 2 hours.
44.
Jane presents to you with abnormal uterine bleeding. Outline how you will evaluate her in order to make a diagnosis. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. History Taking: Determine the nature of bleeding (frequency, duration, volume/clots), menstrual history (LMP, cycle), obstetric history, contraceptive use, and medical history (coagulopathies).
  2. Physical Examination: Perform a general exam (pallor/anemia signs), abdominal exam (masses), and pelvic exam (speculum for cervical lesions; bimanual for uterine size/tenderness/fibroids).
  3. Laboratory Investigations: Urine pregnancy test (to rule out pregnancy complications), Full Blood Count (Hb/Platelets), and hormonal profile if indicated.
  4. Imaging: Pelvic Ultrasound scan to assess the uterus (fibroids, endometrial thickness) and ovaries.
  5. Histology: Endometrial biopsy or D&C samples for histology to rule out hyperplasia or malignancy (especially in older women).
45.
You have been instructed by the medical officer to perform a Manual Vacuum Aspiration for a patient who has been admitted with incomplete abortion at 8 weeks gestation. Outline the signs that you will consider as an indication for complete evacuation. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Signs of Complete Evacuation during MVA:

  1. Red Foam: Presence of pinkish/red foam (bubbles) in the cannula/aspirate (indicating empty cavity).
  2. Gritty Sensation: Feeling a rough, gritty sensation as the cannula moves over the uterine wall (indicates myometrium is reached).
  3. Uterine Contraction: The uterus contracts and grips the cannula (makes it harder to move the cannula).
  4. Absence of Tissue: No more tissue is seen passing into the syringe.
  5. Cessation of Bleeding: Vaginal bleeding decreases significantly or stops.
46.
APGAR score determines the immediate care of the new born. Describe the components of the APGAR scoring system. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  • A - Appearance (Skin Color): Blue/pale (0), Acrocyanosis (1), Completely Pink (2).
  • P - Pulse (Heart Rate): Absent (0), <100 bpm (1), >100 bpm (2).
  • G - Grimace (Reflex Irritability): No response (0), Grimace (1), Cry/Cough/Sneeze (2).
  • A - Activity (Muscle Tone): Flaccid (0), Some flexion (1), Active motion/Well flexed (2).
  • R - Respiration: Absent (0), Slow/irregular (1), Good cry (2).

48.
A 32-year-old female presents to the casualty department with severe lower abdominal pain radiating to the shoulder and per vaginal bleeding. Her last normal menstrual period was 8 weeks ago and her pregnancy test is positive. On examination, she is sick looking, her pulse rate is 110 beats/minute and blood pressure is 90/60mmHg. Abdominal examination reveals tenderness.
a) What relevant investigation will you order to confirm your diagnosis (1 Mark)
b) Outline your plan of management for the patient (4 Marks)
Show Answer

Diagnosis: Ruptured Ectopic Pregnancy.

a) Investigation:

  • Pelvic Ultrasound (FAST scan): To detect adnexal mass/gestational sac outside uterus and free fluid in the Pouch of Douglas.

b) Plan of Management:

  1. Resuscitation: Secure wide-bore IV access, administer IV fluids (crystalloids/colloids) rapidly to restore volume.
  2. Blood Transfusion: Group and cross-match blood immediately and transfuse to correct shock/anemia.
  3. Emergency Surgery (Laparotomy): Urgent transfer to theatre for salpingectomy (removal of the ruptured tube) to arrest internal bleeding.
  4. Supportive Care: Administer analgesics and monitor vitals closely. Administer Anti-D immunoglobulin if the patient is Rhesus negative.

Section 3: Long Answer Question

20 Marks
49.
A married couple living together presents to the reproductive health clinic with history of failure to conceive after eight years of marriage.
a) Outline the possible causes of infertility in this couple (6 marks)
b) Describe your approach on how you will evaluate this couple (14 marks)
Show Answer

a) Possible Causes of Infertility:

  • Male Factors (30-40%):
    • Sperm abnormalities: Oligospermia (low count), Azoospermia (no sperm), Teratospermia (abnormal shape), Asthenospermia (poor motility).
    • Testicular issues: Undescended testes, Varicocele, Infections (Mumps orchitis).
    • Obstruction: Blockage of vas deferens.
    • Erectile/Ejaculatory dysfunction.
  • Female Factors (40-50%):
    • Tubal Factors: Blockage due to PID, STIs (Gonorrhea/Chlamydia), Endometriosis, or previous surgery.
    • Ovulatory Disorders: PCOS, Hyperprolactinemia, Premature Ovarian Failure, Thyroid disorders.
    • Uterine Factors: Fibroids, Asherman's syndrome, Congenital anomalies.
    • Cervical Factors: Hostile cervical mucus, Stenosis.
  • Unexplained Infertility (10-20%)

b) Approach to Evaluation:

1. Detailed History Taking (Both Partners)

  • General: Age, duration of infertility (8 years), coital frequency and timing (fertile window).
  • Female History: Menstrual history (regularity/pain), Obstetric history (previous pregnancies/abortions), Medical history (PID, STIs, surgeries), Contraceptive history.
  • Male History: History of trauma, mumps, STIs, surgeries (hernia), erectile dysfunction, lifestyle (smoking/alcohol).

2. Physical Examination

  • Female: BMI assessment, Secondary sexual characteristics, Abdominal exam (masses/scars), Pelvic exam (tenderness/discharge/fibroids).
  • Male: Genital exam (Testicular size/consistency, presence of vas deferens, varicocele, penile abnormalities).

3. Investigations (The Work-up)

  • Male Partner (First):
    • Seminal Fluid Analysis (SFA): Assessing volume, count, motility, and morphology. Repeat after 6 weeks if abnormal.
  • Female Partner:
    • Confirming Ovulation: Day 21 Serum Progesterone (Mid-luteal phase). Basal body temperature charts.
    • Assessing Tubal Patency: Hysterosalpingogram (HSG) to visualize tubes and uterine cavity. Diagnostic Laparoscopy with dye test (Gold standard).
    • Assessing Uterine Anatomy: Pelvic Ultrasound (for fibroids, ovarian morphology/PCOS).
    • Hormonal Profile: FSH, LH (Day 2-5), Prolactin, TFTs to assess ovarian reserve and endocrine causes.
  • Infection Screen: HIV, VDRL, HBsAg for both partners.

4. Counseling and Plan

  • Review results, explain diagnosis, counsel on lifestyle modifications, and refer to a specialist/gynecologist for assisted reproductive technology (ART) or specific treatment based on findings.
