Section A: Short Essay Questions

60 Marks
1.

a) List five causes of obstructed labor. (5 Marks)

b) Outline five clinical signs of obstructed labor. (5 Marks)

Show Answer

Obstructed labor occurs when there is no advance of the presenting part despite strong uterine contractions.

a) Causes of Obstructed Labor:

  • Cephalopelvic Disproportion (CPD): Mismatch between fetal head size and maternal pelvis.
  • Malpresentation/Malposition: Shoulder presentation (transverse lie), brow presentation, or persistent occipito-posterior position.
  • Fetal Abnormalities: Hydrocephalus, fetal ascites, or conjoined twins.
  • Pelvic Tumors: Uterine fibroids or ovarian cysts blocking the birth canal.
  • Pelvic Deformities: Contracted pelvis due to rickets, osteomalacia, or kyphosis.

b) Clinical Signs of Obstructed Labor:

  • Maternal Distress: Exhaustion, dehydration, tachycardia, and anxiety.
  • Bandl's Ring: A pathological retraction ring visible as a depression across the abdomen (sign of impending rupture).
  • Severe Caput Succedaneum/Moulding: Excessive swelling of the fetal scalp and overlapping of skull bones.
  • Edematous Vulva: Swelling of the external genitalia due to prolonged pressure.
  • Fetal Distress: Fetal heart rate abnormalities (tachycardia >160 or bradycardia <110), or meconium-stained liquor in vertex presentation.
2.

a) List five male and five female causes of infertility. (5 Marks)

b) Outline five investigations for each partner in a couple with infertility. (5 marks)

Show Answer

a) Causes of Infertility:

Male Causes:

  1. Low Sperm Count (Oligospermia): Or absence of sperm (Azoospermia).
  2. Erectile Dysfunction/Impotence: Inability to achieve or maintain erection.
  3. Varicocele: Dilated veins in the scrotum affecting sperm production.
  4. Retrograde Ejaculation: Semen enters bladder instead of emerging through penis.
  5. Infections: Post-mumps orchitis or untreated STIs leading to blockage.

Female Causes:

  1. Tubal Blockage: Often due to Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID).
  2. Anovulation: E.g., Polycystic Ovarian Syndrome (PCOS).
  3. Uterine Fibroids: Interfering with implantation.
  4. Endometriosis: Causing adhesions and distortion of pelvic anatomy.
  5. Cervical Factors: Hostile cervical mucus or stenosis.

b) Investigations:

  • Male: Seminal Fluid Analysis (SFA), Scrotal Ultrasound, Hormonal Profile (Testosterone, FSH), Urinalysis (for retrograde ejaculation), VDRL/STI screening.
  • Female: Hysterosalpingogram (HSG) to check tubal patency, Pelvic Ultrasound, Hormonal Profile (FSH, LH, Progesterone, Prolactin), Laparoscopy (dye test), Hysteroscopy.
3.

a) Classify the various types of abortion. (5 Marks)

b) Outline the components of post abortal care. (5 Marks)

Show Answer

a) Classification of Abortion:

  1. Threatened Abortion: Vaginal bleeding with closed cervix; pregnancy may continue.
  2. Inevitable Abortion: Vaginal bleeding with open cervix; pregnancy cannot continue.
  3. Incomplete Abortion: Partial expulsion of products of conception.
  4. Complete Abortion: Complete expulsion of all products of conception.
  5. Missed Abortion: Fetus dies in utero but is retained.
  6. Septic Abortion: Any abortion complicated by infection.
  7. Recurrent/Habitual Abortion: Three or more consecutive pregnancy losses.

b) Components of Post Abortal Care (PAC):

  • Emergency Treatment: Management of incomplete abortion (e.g., MVA) and complications like hemorrhage or sepsis.
  • Counseling: Psychological support and addressing patient concerns.
  • Family Planning/Contraception: Provision of contraceptive methods to prevent unplanned pregnancies immediately.
  • Reproductive Health Linkage: Screening for STIs, HIV, and cervical cancer.
  • Community Partnership: Engaging the community to prevent unsafe abortions and mobilize resources.
4.
Outline the components of a partograph. (10 marks)
Show Answer

A partograph is a graphical record of the progress of labor and key maternal/fetal conditions.

Components:

  1. Patient Identification Details: Name, Gravida, Para, Admission Date/Time, Membrane status/time of rupture.
  2. Fetal Heart Rate: Recorded every 30 minutes (Range 110-160 bpm).
  3. Amniotic Fluid (Liquor): Key codes: I (Intact), C (Clear), M (Meconium), B (Blood), A (Absent).
  4. Moulding of Fetal Skull: Codes: 0 (Separated), + (Touching), ++ (Overlapping but reducible), +++ (Overlapping and irreducible).
  5. Cervical Dilatation: Plotted with an 'X' against time. Includes Alert Line and Action Line.
  6. Descent of Head: Measured in fifths palpable per abdomen, plotted with 'O'.
  7. Uterine Contractions: Frequency per 10 mins and Duration (Dots <20s, Diagonal lines 20-40s, Solid blocks >40s).
  8. Drugs and IV Fluids: Record of oxytocin or other medications administered.
  9. Maternal Vital Signs: Blood Pressure (every 4 hours), Pulse (every 30 mins), Temperature (every 2 hours).
  10. Urinalysis: Protein, Acetone, and Volume.
5.

a) Define fetal distress. (2 marks)

b) State 8 predisposing factors for fetal distress. (8 marks)

Show Answer

a) Definition:

Fetal distress is a compromise of the fetus during the antepartum or intrapartum period, typically characterized by hypoxia (low oxygen) and acidosis, manifested clinically by abnormal heart rate patterns or meconium passage.

b) Predisposing Factors:

  1. Cord Accidents: Cord prolapse, nuchal cord, or true knots reducing blood flow.
  2. Placental Abruption: Premature separation of placenta reducing gas exchange.
  3. Placenta Previa: Major hemorrhage leading to fetal compromise.
  4. Maternal Hypotension: E.g., supine hypotension syndrome or post-epidural.
  5. Uterine Hyperstimulation: Excessive contractions (often from Oxytocin misuse) preventing placental refill.
  6. Pre-eclampsia/Eclampsia: Vasospasm reducing placental perfusion.
  7. Post-term Pregnancy: Placental aging/insufficiency.
  8. Intrauterine Growth Restriction (IUGR): Reduced fetal reserve to tolerate labor stress.
  9. Maternal Anemia: Reduced oxygen-carrying capacity.
6.

a) What is postpartum hemorrhage? (2 marks)

b) Outline the causes of postpartum hemorrhage. (8 marks)

Show Answer

a) Definition:

Postpartum hemorrhage (PPH) is defined as blood loss >500ml following a vaginal delivery or >1000ml following a cesarean section. It can also be defined as any amount of blood loss that causes hemodynamic instability in the mother.

b) Causes (The 4 T's):

  1. Tone (Uterine Atony): The uterus fails to contract after delivery (Most common cause - 70%). Predisposed by big baby, twins, polyhydramnios, prolonged labor.
  2. Trauma: Lacerations of the cervix, vagina, or perineum; Uterine rupture; Uterine inversion.
  3. Tissue: Retained products of conception (placenta fragments, membranes) or blood clots preventing uterine contraction.
  4. Thrombin (Coagulopathy): Pre-existing bleeding disorders (e.g., Hemophilia, Von Willebrand) or acquired issues like DIC (Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation) from HELLP syndrome or sepsis.

Section B: Long Essay Questions

40 Marks

Instructions: Select ANY TWO questions from this section.

1.

Rehema, a 36-year-old lady, presents to GOPC with a history of abnormal per vaginal discharge, dyspareunia, with occasional post coital bleeding. On speculum examination you notice she has cervical erosion.

i. List 3 differential diagnoses of Rehema's case. (3 Marks)

ii. Outline four methods that you can use to screen Carcinoma of the cervix. (4 Marks)

iii. List the various modes of treatment of carcinoma of the cervix. (6 Marks)

iv. Outline the preventive measures for carcinoma of the cervix. (4 Marks)

v. List the complications for carcinoma of the cervix. (3 Marks)

Show Answer

i. Differential Diagnosis:

  • Carcinoma of the Cervix (Cervical Cancer)
  • Chronic Cervicitis (Infective)
  • Cervical Polyp
  • Cervical Ectropion (Erosion)

ii. Screening Methods:

  • Pap Smear (Papanicolaou test): Cytological examination of cervical cells.
  • VIA (Visual Inspection with Acetic Acid): Cervix turns acetowhite if abnormal.
  • VILI (Visual Inspection with Lugol's Iodine): Abnormal areas do not take up iodine stain.
  • HPV DNA Testing: Testing for high-risk Human Papillomavirus strains.
  • Colposcopy: Magnified visual inspection of the cervix.

iii. Modes of Treatment:

  • Surgery:
    • Cone biopsy/LEEP (Early stages/CIN).
    • Hysterectomy (Simple or Radical/Wertheim's) for invasive cancer.
  • Radiotherapy: External beam radiation or Brachytherapy (internal radiation).
  • Chemotherapy: Use of cytotoxic drugs (e.g., Cisplatin).
  • Palliative Care: Pain management and symptom control for advanced stages.

iv. Preventive Measures:

  • HPV Vaccination: Administering vaccines (Gardasil/Cervarix) to girls before sexual debut (9-14 years).
  • Regular Screening: Routine Pap smears or VIA to detect pre-cancerous lesions.
  • Safe Sexual Practices: Barrier methods (condoms) to reduce HPV transmission and limiting sexual partners.
  • Health Education: Awareness about risk factors like smoking and early sexual debut.

v. Complications:

  • Severe Hemorrhage: Leading to anemia.
  • Fistula Formation: Vesico-vaginal (VVF) or Recto-vaginal (RVF) fistulae due to tumor invasion.
  • Renal Failure: Due to ureteric obstruction (hydronephrosis).
  • Metastasis: Spread to lungs, liver, or bones.
2.

Maria, a 28-year-old para 4+0 G5 presents to the ANC clinic at 32 weeks' gestation with a history of a gush of fluid 2 hours ago. The fluid was so much that it soaked her beddings.

i. What is your most likely diagnosis? (2 Marks)

ii. Outline five predisposing factors for the above condition. (5 Marks)

iii. Outline the management for this patient. (10 Marks)

iv. List three maternal complications. (3 Marks)

Show Answer

i. Diagnosis:

Preterm Premature Rupture of Membranes (PPROM). (Membranes ruptured before 37 weeks and before onset of labor).

ii. Predisposing Factors:

  • Infection: Urinary Tract Infections (UTI), Bacterial Vaginosis, or STIs.
  • Polyhydramnios: Excessive amniotic fluid distending the membranes.
  • Multiple Gestation: Twins/triplets causing uterine overdistension.
  • Cervical Incompetence: Weak cervix opening prematurely.
  • History of PPROM: Previous occurrence increases risk.
  • Smoking/Substance Abuse.

iii. Management:

  • Admission: Admit Maria for bed rest and monitoring.
  • History & Exam: Confirm gestation, color of liquor (clear/meconium), exclude cord prolapse (sterile speculum exam, avoid digital vaginal exam to prevent infection).
  • Investigations: Full Hemogram (WBC for infection), Urinalysis/Culture, Obstetric Ultrasound (fetal wellbeing, liquor volume/AFI).
  • Antibiotics: Erythromycin (or Ampicillin) to prevent chorioamnionitis and prolong latency.
  • Corticosteroids: Dexamethasone (6mg IM q12h x 4 doses) or Betamethasone to accelerate fetal lung maturity since she is 32 weeks.
  • Tocolysis: Short-term use (e.g., Nifedipine) only if contractions present, to allow time for steroid effect (unless infection is evident).
  • Monitoring: 4-hourly temperature/pulse (maternal), Daily Fetal Heart Rate monitoring, Daily pad checks.
  • Delivery Plan: Aim to reach 34 weeks if stable. Deliver immediately if signs of chorioamnionitis (fever, foul discharge, uterine tenderness) or fetal distress develop.

iv. Maternal Complications:

  • Chorioamnionitis: Severe infection of membranes/uterus.
  • Postpartum Endometritis: Infection after delivery.
  • Placental Abruption: Associated with sudden decompression.
  • Sepsis/Septicemia.
3.

Nafula, a 17-year-old primi-gravida at 34 weeks of gestation age, presents to the antenatal ward with a history of persistent headache, facial puffiness, and severe epigastric pains. On examination, she has bilateral pitting tender edema and a BP 180/115 mmHg.

i. What is your diagnosis? (2 Marks)

ii. Outline five relevant investigations for this patient. (5 Marks)

iii. Explain the principles of management of this patient. (10 Marks)

iv. List 6 maternal complications that may occur in this patient. (3 Marks)

Show Answer

i. Diagnosis:

Severe Pre-eclampsia (Imminent Eclampsia). Note: The presence of severe headache, epigastric pain, and very high BP indicates severity and impending convulsions.

ii. Investigations:

  • Urinalysis: To quantitate proteinuria (Dipstick or 24hr protein).
  • Full Hemogram: Check Hb and Platelets (Thrombocytopenia check for HELLP).
  • Liver Function Tests (LFTs): Check liver enzymes (AST/ALT) for HELLP syndrome.
  • Renal Function Tests (U/E/C): Urea, Electrolytes, Creatinine to assess kidney function.
  • Obstetric Ultrasound: Fetal growth, amniotic fluid, Doppler studies.

iii. Principles of Management:

  • Stabilization: Admit to a quiet, dark room (or High Dependency Unit). Minimize stimuli to prevent convulsions.
  • Seizure Prophylaxis: Administer Magnesium Sulfate (MgSO4). Loading dose (4g IV + 10g IM) followed by maintenance dose (5g IM q4h) for 24 hours. Monitor respiratory rate, reflexes, and urine output.
  • Antihypertensive Therapy: Rapidly lower BP to safe levels (aiming for diastolic 90-100 mmHg). Use Hydralazine IV, Labetalol IV, or Nifedipine oral.
  • Fetal Monitoring: Monitor Fetal Heart Rate continuously.
  • Corticosteroids: Dexamethasone for fetal lung maturity (if delivery can be delayed 24-48hrs and mother is stable).
  • Delivery: Delivery is the definitive treatment. Since she has severe features (epigastric pain, BP 180/115), stabilization followed by prompt delivery (likely Cesarean Section due to urgency) is required, regardless of gestation.
  • Fluid Management: Strict fluid balance chart to prevent pulmonary edema.

iv. Maternal Complications:

  • Eclampsia (Convulsions).
  • HELLP Syndrome (Hemolysis, Elevated Liver enzymes, Low Platelets).
  • Cerebral Hemorrhage (Stroke).
  • Pulmonary Edema.
  • Acute Renal Failure.
  • Placental Abruption.
  • Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC).
