Set 1: General Short Answer Questions

1.
Outline the 5 IMPORTANT findings that you must examine for in obstetric abdominal examination using LEOPOLD maneuver. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Fundal Height: To estimate gestational age and fetal growth.
  2. Lie: The relationship of the longitudinal axis of the fetus to that of the mother (Longitudinal, Transverse, Oblique).
  3. Presentation: The part of the fetus occupying the lower pole of the uterus (Cephalic, Breech, Shoulder).
  4. Attitude: The relationship of fetal parts to one another (Flexion, Extension).
  5. Engagement: Whether the presenting part has entered the pelvic inlet (measured in fifths of head palpable).

5.
Define ectopic pregnancy and outline any four sites of occurrence (5 marks)
Show Answer

Definition: Ectopic pregnancy is the implantation of a fertilized ovum outside the uterine cavity.

Sites of Occurrence:

  1. Fallopian Tube (Tubal): Ampulla (most common), Isthmus, Fimbriae.
  2. Ovary: Ovarian pregnancy.
  3. Cervix: Cervical pregnancy.
  4. Abdominal Cavity: Peritoneal/Abdominal pregnancy.
  5. Cornua/Interstitial: Interstitial part of the tube.

7.
Briefly discuss the physiological changes that occur in the cardiovascular system during pregnancy (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Cardiac Output: Increases by 30-50% mainly due to increased stroke volume.
  • Blood Volume: Plasma volume increases by 40-50%, causing physiological anemia (hemodilution).
  • Heart Rate: Increases by 10-15 beats per minute.
  • Blood Pressure: Decreases in the second trimester due to decreased peripheral resistance (vasodilation), then returns to pre-pregnancy levels in the third trimester.
  • Supine Hypotension: Compression of the inferior vena cava by the gravid uterus when lying flat reduces venous return.
8.
Outline any 5 functions of the placenta (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Respiration: Gas exchange (O2 uptake, CO2 elimination).
  2. Nutrition: Transfer of nutrients (glucose, amino acids) to the fetus.
  3. Excretion: Removal of fetal waste products (urea).
  4. Endocrine: Production of hormones (hCG, Progesterone, Estrogen, HPL).
  5. Protection/Barrier: Prevents passage of some bacteria (though viruses/drugs may cross) and provides passive immunity (IgG).

Set 1: Multiple Choice Questions

1.
The following are true concerning vaginal examination EXCEPT:
A. Assesses pelvic adequacy
B. To rule out cord presentation
C. Rule out cord prolapse
D. To check for fetal movement
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. To check for fetal movement

Explanation: Fetal movement is assessed via abdominal palpation or maternal report. Vaginal examination is used for cervical dilatation, presentation, cord prolapse, and pelvic adequacy.

2.
A 30 year old lady comes to your clinic for routine checkup. She has 2 living children and 1 lost pregnancy at 16 weeks and 2 children at the age of 1 and 2 years. She is?
A. Para 2+3
B. Para 2+1 gravida 4
C. Para 4+1
D. Para 2+1
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Para 2+1

Explanation:

  • Parity (Para): Number of deliveries after age of viability (usually 28 weeks). She has 2 living children (assuming single births) = Para 2.
  • Abortions (+): Pregnancy lost at 16 weeks = +1.
  • Gravida: Total pregnancies. 2 (children) + 1 (loss) + 1 (current visit implied? Question says "routine checkup" but context usually implies ANC. If not pregnant now, she is P2+1 G3. If pregnant, G4. Options are mixed format).
  • Best Fit: Para 2+1 describes her history accurately.

4.
Signs and symptoms of IUGR include:
A. Reduced fetal movement
B. Fundal height smaller than gestation by age
C. Increased maternal weight
D. Reduced heart sound
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Fundal height smaller than gestation by age

Explanation: Intrauterine Growth Restriction (IUGR) presents clinically as a fundal height that lags behind the gestational age (e.g., 2cm less than expected). Reduced movement is a sign of distress, not specifically IUGR, though they are linked.

5.
Which of the following factors influence menopause?
A. Childbearing
B. Multiparity
C. Prolonged use of oral contraceptives
D. Smoking
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Smoking

Explanation: Smoking is a known modifiable risk factor that accelerates follicular depletion, leading to earlier menopause (often 1-2 years earlier). Parity and OCP use generally do not affect the age of menopause.

6.
Concerning dysmenorrhea, which one is TRUE?
A. It is not relieved by NSAIDs
B. Secondary dysmenorrhea has an underlying cause like fibroids
C. Primary dysmenorrhea has an underlying cause like fibroids
D. Chronic dysmenorrhea does not require gynecological review
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Secondary dysmenorrhea has an underlying cause like fibroids

Explanation:

  • Primary Dysmenorrhea: Pain without pelvic pathology (prostaglandin mediated).
  • Secondary Dysmenorrhea: Pain due to pathology (Fibroids, Endometriosis, PID).
  • NSAIDs are the treatment of choice for Primary Dysmenorrhea.

Set 2: Case Studies & Clinical Scenarios

Case Study 1: Halima, 27 year old para 2+0 gravida 3 presents to you at the emergency unit with complains of severe lower abdominal pain, per vaginal bleeding and a 6 week period of amenorrhea. She is anxious, pale and with tenderness on the abdomen.

a) What is your probable diagnosis? And give a reason.

Show Answer

Diagnosis: Ruptured Ectopic Pregnancy.

Reason: The classic triad of Amenorrhea (6 weeks), Abdominal Pain (severe, tender), and Vaginal Bleeding. Signs of shock (pale, anxious) and peritoneal irritation (tenderness) suggest rupture and internal hemorrhage.

b) What is your impression on Management?

Show Answer

Management (Emergency):

  1. Resuscitation: Secure IV access (2 large bore cannulae), fluids (crystalloids/colloids), group and cross-match blood.
  2. Surgery: Immediate Exploratory Laparotomy (Salpingectomy) to stop bleeding.
  3. Post-op: Monitor vitals, Anti-D if Rhesus negative, Counseling.

Case Study 4: Jane Kamau, 30 years para 0+1 G2 at 24/40 with blood group O Rhesus –ve comes to your ANC Clinic for the 1st visit. How do you go about evaluating possible Rhesus incompatibility / Isoimmunization?
Show Answer
  1. History: Check husband's blood group. Ask about previous sensitizing events (abortion, ectopic, trauma, previous Anti-D administration).
  2. Antibody Screening: Perform Indirect Coombs Test (ICT) on the mother to detect antibodies against Rh antigen.
  3. Interpretation:
    • If ICT Negative: Not sensitized. Give Anti-D immunoglobulin (Rhogam) at 28 weeks and 34 weeks (or within 72h of delivery if baby is Rh+).
    • If ICT Positive: Sensitized. Monitor antibody titers. If titers high (>1:16), monitor fetus for anemia (MCA Doppler USG).

Set 3: Past Paper Questions

1.
Explain the risk factors that influence transmission of HIV from mother to child. (5 mks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Maternal Factors: High viral load, Low CD4 count, Advanced HIV disease (AIDS), Vitamin A deficiency, Breast infection (Mastitis/Cracked nipples).
  • Obstetric Factors: Prolonged rupture of membranes (>4 hours), Chorioamnionitis, Invasive procedures (Episiotomy, Forceps), Vaginal delivery (vs Elective CS).
  • Fetal/Infant Factors: Prematurity, Low birth weight, Oral lesions (thrush) in infant, Mixed feeding.
2.
Outline the 4 WHO pillars of PMTCT (4 mks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Primary prevention of HIV infection among women of reproductive age.
  2. Prevention of unintended pregnancies among women living with HIV.
  3. Prevention of HIV transmission from a woman living with HIV to her infant (Perinatal transmission).
  4. Provision of appropriate treatment, care, and support to mothers living with HIV and their children and families.

Set 4: Handwritten & Additional Questions

1.
Briefly describe the procedure of performing clinical pelvimetry. (10 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

Performed at 36+ weeks or during labour to assess pelvic adequacy.

  1. Preparation: Explain procedure, empty bladder, lithotomy position, sterile gloves.
  2. Inlet Assessment: Try to reach the sacral promontory with the middle finger. If felt, measure Diagonal Conjugate. Usually not felt (adequate).
  3. Cavity Assessment: Palpate the sacral curve (should be hollow/concave). Palpate Ischial Spines (should not be prominent). Side walls (should be parallel, not convergent).
  4. Outlet Assessment: Subpubic arch (should admit 2-3 fingers, >90 degrees). Intertuberous diameter (admit 4 knuckles of closed fist).
  5. Conclusion: Adequate, Borderline, or Contracted.
2.
Describe the female bony pelvis (20 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

Bones (4): 2 Innominate bones (Hip bones), Sacrum, Coccyx.

Joints (4): 2 Sacroiliac joints, Symphysis Pubis, Sacrococcygeal joint.

Divisions:

  • False Pelvis: Above the pelvic brim. Supports intestines.
  • True Pelvis: Below the brim. Forms the birth canal. Divided into Inlet, Cavity, and Outlet.

Planes & Diameters:

  • Inlet: AP (11cm), Transverse (13cm), Oblique (12cm).
  • Cavity: Circular (12cm all around).
  • Outlet: AP (13cm), Transverse (11cm).

Types: Gynecoid (Female, optimal), Android (Male, heart-shaped), Anthropoid (Oval AP), Platypelloid (Flat kidney).
