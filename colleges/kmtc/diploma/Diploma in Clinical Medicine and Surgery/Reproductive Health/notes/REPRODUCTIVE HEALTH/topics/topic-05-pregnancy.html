üß¨ Fertilization & Implantation

Fertilization

Fertilization is the process of fusion of the spermatozoon with the mature ovum. It begins with sperm egg collision and ends with production of a mononucleated single cell called the zygote. Its objectives are:

  1. To initiate the embryonic development of the egg and
  2. To restore the chromosome number of the species.

Almost always, fertilization occurs in the ampullary part of the uterine tube.

üîÑ

Process of Fertilization

  • Sperm Transport: A few thousand sperm reach the ampulla aided by uterine contractions.
  • Penetration: Hyaluronidase from the acrosomal cap dissolves the corona radiata and facilitates penetration of the zona pellucida.
  • Fusion & Block: One sperm touches the oolemma. Zona reaction (hardening) and oolemma block prevent polyspermy.
  • Meiosis II: The oocyte completes the second meiotic division, forming the female pronucleus and the second polar body.
  • Zygote Formation: Male and female pronuclei unite, restoring the diploid number (46 chromosomes).

Implantation (Nidation)

Implantation occurs in the endometrium of the anterior or posterior wall of the body near the fundus on the 6th day after fertilization which corresponds to the 20th day of a regular menstrual cycle.

Stages of Implantation

1. Apposition

Occurs through pinopod formation (microvilli) from the endometrial cell surface which absorb endometrial fluid (rich in glycogen/mucin) to provide initial nutrition.

2. Adhesion

Blastocyst adheres to the endometrium through adhesion molecules like integrin, selectin and cadherin.

3. Penetration & Invasion

Facilitated by histolytic action. Syncytial cells penetrate stroma, erode maternal capillaries, and form lacunae. Nutrition switches to aerobic metabolic pathway from maternal blood.

4. Completion

Completed by 10th or 11th day. This type of deeper penetration is called interstitial implantation.

üë∂ Fetal Development

Three periods are distinguished in the prenatal development:

  • Ovular/Germinal period: First 2 weeks following ovulation.
  • Embryonic period: 3rd week to 10 weeks of gestation (8 weeks post conception). Major organogenesis occurs.
  • Fetal period: Begins after 8th week following conception and ends in delivery. Characterized by growth and maturation.

The Fetal Circulation

Key features include the ductus venosus (shunts oxygenated blood from umbilical vein to IVC), foramen ovale (shunts blood from right atrium to left atrium), and ductus arteriosus (shunts blood from pulmonary trunk to descending aorta). The mean cardiac output is high (350 mL/kg/min).

üë© Maternal Physiological Changes

Maternal physiology undergoes many changes secondary to progesterone and estrogen, enabling the fetus to grow and preparing the mother for childbirth.

‚ñ∂ Reproductive System
  • Uterus: Weight increases from 70g to 1100g; capacity from 10mL to 5L. Hypertrophy of muscle cells. Dextrorotation common.
  • Cervix: Softens (Goodell's sign) and becomes bluish (Chadwick's sign). Mucus plug forms.
  • Ovaries: Ovulation is suspended. Corpus luteum active in early pregnancy.
  • Vagina: Hypertrophied, edematous, vascular. Secretion becomes copious, acidic pH (3.5-6).
  • Breasts: Tenderness, enlargement, pigmentation of nipples/areolae. Montgomery's tubercles enlarge. Colostrum secretion from 3rd month.
‚ñ∂ Cardiovascular System
  • Cardiac Output: Increases by 40-50%, peaking at 30-34 weeks.
  • Heart Position: Displaced upward and outward (apex at 4th ICS).
  • Blood Pressure: Systemic vascular resistance (SVR) decreases (-21%). Diastolic BP drops 5-10 mmHg mid-pregnancy.
  • Supine Hypotension: Compression of IVC by gravid uterus in supine position.
‚ñ∂ Hematological System
  • Blood Volume: Plasma volume increases 40-45%. RBC mass increases 20-30%. Result is physiological hemodilution.
  • Coagulation: Pregnancy is a procoagulant state. Fibrinogen and clotting factors increase; fibrinolytic activity decreases.
  • WBC: Neutrophil count rises. Immune function (T/B lymphocytes) suppressed.
‚ñ∂ Respiratory System
  • Anatomy: Diaphragm elevated (~4cm).
  • Function: Tidal volume increases 30-40%. Hyperventilation due to progesterone (lowered CO2 threshold).
  • Blood Gas: Respiratory alkalosis compensated by renal excretion of bicarbonate.
‚ñ∂ Renal System
  • Anatomy: Kidneys enlarge. Ureters dilate (hydroureter) due to progesterone and compression. Predisposes to UTI.
  • Function: GFR increases 40-65%. Decreased BUN and Creatinine. Glycosuria is common (lowered threshold).
‚ñ∂ Gastrointestinal System
  • Mouth: Gums hypertrophic (epulis), ptyalism (salivation).
  • Stomach/Gut: Progesterone relaxes smooth muscle -> Heartburn (pyrosis), Constipation, delayed emptying.
  • Gallbladder: Emptying slows -> increased risk of gallstones.
‚ñ∂ Endocrine System
  • Thyroid: Enlarges. Euthyroid state maintained.
  • Pancreas: Insulin resistance increases (diabetogenic state) to ensure glucose for fetus.
  • Hormones:
    • HCG: Maintains corpus luteum.
    • Progesterone: "Hormone of pregnancy" - smooth muscle relaxant, thermogenic.
    • Estrogen: Growth of uterus/breasts, water retention.

üè• Antenatal Care (ANC)

Antenatal care (ANC) is the medical care given to a pregnant woman from the moment she realizes she is pregnant until she delivers. The goal is to ensure a successful pregnancy outcome.

FANC Activities by Visit

  • 1 First Visit (8-12w): Confirm pregnancy/EDD, Classify risk, Screen (Hb, Syphilis, HIV, Proteinuria, Blood group), Tetanus toxoid, Iron/Folate, Birth plan.
  • 2 Second Visit (24-26w): Assess maternal/fetal well-being, Exclude PIH/Anaemia, Preventive measures (IPTp for malaria).
  • 3 Third Visit (32w): Assess well-being, Exclude PIH/Anaemia/Multiple pregnancy.
  • 4 Fourth Visit (36-38w): Exclude malpresentation, Birth plan review.

üåç WHO Recommendations

Nutritional Interventions

Diet & Supplements

  • Healthy eating counseling.
  • Iron & Folic Acid: Daily (30-60mg Iron + 0.4mg Folic) to prevent anaemia. Intermittent if daily not acceptable.
  • Calcium: 1.5-2.0g daily in populations with low intake to reduce pre-eclampsia risk.
  • Caffeine: Restrict intake (>300mg/day) to reduce pregnancy loss.

Maternal Assessment

  • Anaemia: FBC recommended method.
  • Asymptomatic Bacteriuria (ASB): Urine culture recommended. Treat with 7-day antibiotics to prevent pyelonephritis/preterm birth.
  • IPV: Clinical enquiry about Intimate Partner Violence.
  • GDM: Hyperglycaemia first detected in pregnancy.

Fetal Assessment

  • Ultrasound: One scan before 24 weeks is recommended to estimate gestation, detect anomalies, and reduce induction for post-term.
  • SFH Measurement: Replacing abdominal palpation with Symphysis-Fundal Height is not recommended to improve outcomes (no change from usual practice).
  • Daily Movement Counting: Only recommended in research context.

Preventive Measures

  • Tetanus Toxoid: Recommended for all.
  • Malaria (IPTp): Intermittent preventive treatment with SP in endemic areas (Africa). Start 2nd trimester, at least 3 doses.
  • Deworming: Preventive anthelminthic treatment after 1st trimester in endemic areas.

üõ°Ô∏è EMTCT of HIV & Syphilis

Elimination of Mother to Child Transmission (EMTCT) targets both HIV and Syphilis. Dual elimination improves broad maternal and child health outcomes.

ü¶†

Impact of Infections

HIV: Without treatment, 15-45% of infants born to HIV+ women become infected. Primary prevention and lifelong ART for mothers can virtually eliminate new infections.

Syphilis: Untreated maternal syphilis results in adverse outcomes (stillbirth, neonatal death) in over 50% of pregnancies. Screening and Benzathine penicillin treatment cures both mother and infant.

Guidelines on Infant Feeding (Mothers with HIV)

WHO recommends that mothers living with HIV who are on treatment and supported should exclusively breastfeed for the first 6 months, then introduce complementary foods while continuing breastfeeding for at least 12 months (up to 24 months).

Guidelines for HIV-Exposed Infants

  • Testing: Early infant diagnosis (PCR) at 4-6 weeks. Repeat at 18 months or when breastfeeding ends.
  • Prophylaxis:
    • Breastfeeding: Once-daily nevirapine for 6 weeks.
    • Replacement feeding: Once-daily nevirapine (or AZT) for 4-6 weeks.
  • Treatment: All HIV+ infants should start ART immediately.
