Confidentiality

During history taking, the medical student should at all times show the patient the respect that is due to her; while full confidentiality must be maintained at all times bearing in mind that the relationship between the professional and his client is based on mutual trust and respect.

Classical Hippocratic Oath state: "All that may come to my knowledge in the exercise of my profession or in daily commerce with men, which ought not to be spread abroad, I will keep secret and will never reveal."

Summary of Code of Ethics

  • Informed consent; rapport
  • Confidentiality; Privacy; Dignity: chaperon
  • Woman's views
  • Informed consent for clinical evaluation and management
  • Professional etiquette
  • Ultimate primacy of the patient in making treatment decisions

History Taking Procedure

  1. Introduce yourself and obtain consent to take history: "Hello. I am Mr/Ms *****, a medical student. Do you mind if I ask you some questions about your medical condition?"
  2. Particulars: Name, age, address, marital status, occupation, religion, sex. LMP, parity, gravity, EDD - Naegele's rule.
    Gravidity is no. of pregnancies including current pregnancy (regardless of the outcome Normal or abortion)
    Parity is no. of births beyond 24 wk gestation
  3. Presenting Complaint: "What is the problem that brought you to the hospital/clinic?" Best to record this in the patient's own words. "Were you referred by your doctor or did you self-refer yourself to the hospital/clinic?" Duration of the presenting complain.
  4. History of Present Illness (HPI): In the obstetric patient, its may be best to consider the "presenting complaint" in two parts:
    • The history of present illness or complaint; and
    • The history of the current pregnancy.
    Patient may not furnish sufficient details, in which case it will be necessary to amplify with specific Directed questions. E.g. SOCRATES relating to pain:
    • Site: where, local/diffuse
    • Onset: rapid/gradual, pattern, worse/better since onset
    • Character: sharp/dull/stabbing, burning/cramp/crushing
    • Radiation: "Does the pain affect you anywhere else?" [to thigh/loin/ elsewhere]
    • Alleviating factors: "What do you do to make yourself comfortable?" "Is the pain better after menstruation?"
    • Time course: "When did the pain start?"; if pain is chronic "What made you seek attention now" "Is the pain worse at any particular time of the cycle?"
    • Exacerbating factors: "Is there anything that brings on the pain or makes it worse?"
    • Severity & Impact on life: "On a scale of 1 to 10, at what level would you classify the pain?" "Does it interrupt your life?"
  5. Systemic Enquiry of Associated Symptoms: GIT system, Respiratory system, Cardiovascular system, Urinary system, CNS, Musculoskeletal system.
  6. Obstetric History:
    • History of Present Pregnancy:
      • Menstrual history: The date of the first day of the last menstrual period (or LMP). The length of the menstrual cycle refers to the time interval between the first day of the period and the first day of the subsequent period. This may vary from 21 to 35 days in normal women, but menstruation usually occurs every 28 days.
      • The estimated date of delivery (EDD): Can be calculated from the first day of the last menstrual period (LMP) by adding 9 months and 7 days to this date (Naegele's rule).
      • Gravity: The term 'gravidity' refers to the number of times a woman has been pregnant, irrespective of the outcome of the pregnancy.
      • Parity: describes the number of liveborn children and stillbirths a woman has delivered after 24 weeks or with a birth weight of 500 g.
      • Focused Antenatal Clinic: When started, number of visits so far, antenatal profile (haemoglobin, blood groups & rhesus factor, syphilis test, HIV test, Urinalysis, Optional: blood slide for MPS, Stool for ova & cyst), Medication presently and previously, Radiological examinations during pregnancy.
      • Symptoms of pregnancy and their severity: Nausea and vomiting (Hyperemesis gravidarum), Increased frequency of micturition, Excessive lassitude or lethargy, Breast tenderness and heaviness, First maternal perception of fetal movements ('quickening'), Pica, Pseudocyesis, Leg cramps, Limb swelling.
      • Complications: Per vaginal discharge, bleeding, LAPs, Headache, Visual disturbance, Pain on micturation.
    • Previous Obstetric History: Detail each previous pregnancy - dates of deliveries, where birth took place, delivered at what gestation, how long did labour take, mode of delivery, condition of baby at birth, sex, birth weight, other complications at delivery and postpartum, any blood transfusion, did she breast fed if not why, Length of labour & complications, outcome, Previous miscarriages.

ü©∫ General Examination Techniques

Prerequisites when examining a patient

  • Use your senses well - listen, look, touch, smell, when examining
  • Knowledge of anatomical land marks
  • Ensure adequate lighting in the room & Secondary tangential lighting from a lamp
  • Ensure the place is quiet to allow proper percussion and auscultation
  • Ensure presence of necessary instruments
  • Ensure privacy
  • Explain the procedure to the patient and ask permission to examine
  • Be thorough without wasting time, systemic without being rigid, gentle yet not afraid to cause discomfort
  • Try to look calm, organized and competent even if you do not exactly feel that way
  • Avoid expressions of disgust, alarm, distaste, or other negative reaction
  • Sequence the comprehensive examination in a manner designed to minimize the patient's need to change positions and maximize your efficiency. Variations are possible and you may wish to develop a method of your own. In general it is helpful to move from head to toe.
  • Examine patient from the right side. Working from one side helps you master skills more quickly and promotes efficiency. Left-handed students find it awkward but are encouraged to practice it for convenience of themselves and others

General Techniques

1. Inspection

The process of observing signs indicative of a healthy or a pathological state. Should start as the patient enters the consultation room (posture, gait, appropriate clothing, colour and moisture of skin, unusual odours).

2. Palpation

Use of hands and fingers to gather information through sense of touch. Palmar surface & finger pads are most suitable to assess position, texture, size, form, consistency. Ulnar surface for vibration. Dorsal surface for temperature.

3. Percussion

Striking one object against another to produce vibration and subsequent sound waves. Sound waves are heard as percussion tones (notes) that arise from vibrations in the body tissue.

4. Auscultation

Listening for sounds produced by the body. Should be carried out last (except for abdomen). Requires quiet environment and placing stethoscope on naked skin.

Clinical Parameters

Pallor

Examine: Conjunctiva, Tongue, Palms, Nail bed (capillary refill), Sole of foot, Anus/perineum.

Jaundice

Yellowish discoloration. Examine: Sclera, mucous membranes, skin, palms and soles.

Cyanosis

Bluish discolouration. Central cyanosis (lips & frenulum) and peripheral cyanosis (extremities).

Finger Clubbing

Nail base angle increases and approaches or exceeds 180 degrees. Nail bed feels boggy.

Dehydration

Fontanels (sunken), Eyes (sunken, absent tears), Mucous membranes (dry), Skin turgor.

Oedema

Sites: face (peri-orbital), sacrum, abdomen, extremities (1 cm above medial malleolus). Report as bi- or unilateral, tender/non-tender, pitting/non-pitting.

Lymph Nodes

Small, mobile and painless nodes are often palpable in healthy individuals. Painful = inflammatory. Firm/fixed = malignant.

Vital Signs

Pulse Rate: Rhythm, Character, Volume.
Blood Pressure: Use appropriate cuff size.
Temperature: Axillary, Oral, Groin, Rectal.
Respiratory Rate: Note character and rhythm.

ü§∞ Obstetric Abdominal Examination

Regular abdominal examination is an important component of both antenatal care and monitoring labour. It helps ascertain: size of uterus, size of foetus, lie/presentation/attitude, relation to pelvic brim, foetal life, and presence of abnormal conditions (polyhydramnios, twins).

Requirements

  • Foetoscope
  • Watch with second hand
  • A bowl with dry gauze and cotton swabs
  • Tape measure
  • Receiver for the dirty swabs
  • Couch and Bed sheet

Leopold's Maneuvers

1. Fundal Grip

Facing the mother, palpate the fundus with both hands. Assess for shape, size, consistency and mobility. Fetal head: firm, hard, round, moves independently (ballottement). Breech: softer, bony prominences, moves with the rest of the form.

2. Umbilical Grip (Lie)

Determine position of the back. Still facing the mother, place both palms on the abdomen. Feel for the firm, smooth back. Absence of poles in flanks = longitudinal lie. Presence of poles on flanks = transverse lie.

3. Pawlick's Grip (Presentation)

Determine what part is lying above the inlet. Using the right hand apply the pawlik's grip (single handed palpation) to feel the part of foetus overlying the pelvic inlet.

4. Pelvic Grip (Engagement)

Facing the mother's lower limbs, place both hands on the lower abdomen. If fingers meet below the presenting part, engagement has not occurred. Assess cephalic prominence to determine flexion (Vertex) vs extension (Face/Brow).

Descent and Engagement

Descent is recorded in terms of the span of the foetal head still palpable in the abdomen above the level of symphysis pubis. The whole span of the head is subdivided into 5 fifths.

  • Completely free: 5/5
  • Widest diameter entered (engagement): 2/5 palpable

Auscultation

Place the foetoscope on the back of the foetus, apply sufficient pressure to exclude external sounds. Take note of foetal heart rate count and regularity.

Contraindications

  • Suspected Premature Rupture of Membranes (PROM)
  • Suspected Placenta Praevia
  • Intact hymen (for Gynaecological exam)

Obstetric Vaginal Examination

Indications: To confirm labour, pelvic assessment, assess progress of labour, assessment of cervical ripening, diagnose complications.

Procedure:

  • Explain procedure, empty bladder, privacy, aseptic technique.
  • Inspection: Inspect external genitalia for scars, warts, discharge, etc.
  • Vulva Toilet: Aseptic cleaning with sterile swabs.
  • Digital Examination: Assess cervix (dilatation, effacement), membranes, presenting part (moulding, caput, position), and pelvic assessment (sacral promontory, ischial spines, sub-pubic angle).

Gynaecological Pelvic Examination

Method: Speculum Examination

  • Maintain infection prevention. Inspect external genitalia.
  • Choose speculum size and warm blades.
  • Insert closed blades obliquely, then rotate clockwise.
  • Open blades to expose cervix. Inspect for erosion, colour, growths, discharge.
  • Take lab specimens if necessary (PAP smear, swabs).
  • Inspect vaginal wall while removing.

Digital Vaginal Examination:

  • Examine cervix (ostium, consistency, mobility, tenderness).
  • Bimanual Examination: Place left hand on abdomen, right fingers in vagina. Palpate uterus (size, shape, position) and adnexa (masses, tenderness).
  • Check for cystocele/rectocele (ask patient to cough).

Examination of Male Genitalia

Inspection: Pubic hair, glans penis (colour, smegma, meatus, discharge), Scrotum (colour, texture, asymmetry, swelling).

Palpation: Penis (tenderness, induration), Urethra (strip for discharge), Scrotum/Testis (consistency, size, tenderness, nodules), Inguinal canal (hernia), Lymph nodes, Cremasteric reflex.

Examination of Female Breast and Axilla

Inspection: Size, symmetry, contour, skin colour, venous pattern, lesions. Inspect nipple for size, retraction, discharge, ulceration.

Palpation: Systematically palpate breast, axilla, and supra-clavicular regions. Use flat part of fingers. Palpate areola and nipple (squeeze for discharge). Palpate tail of Spence and axillary lymph nodes.

üß™ Common Investigations

In Obstetrics

  • ANC Profile: Hb & HCT, VDRL (Syphilis), Blood Group/Rhesus, Urinalysis, HIV.
  • Ultrasound: Dating, anomalies, growth, well-being, placental localization.
  • Infections: UTI (Urinalysis/Culture), Malaria (MPs), Septicemia (Blood culture).
  • Complications: Hypertensive disease (RFTs, Uric acid, LFTs), Diabetes (Blood sugar, HbA1c), Rhesus isoimmunization (Coombs test), APH (Coagulation screen).

In Gynaecology

  • Full Blood Count: Infection vs non-infective.
  • Ultrasound: Pelvic masses (solid/cystic), fluid in pelvis, infertility monitoring.
  • Radiology: Hysterosalpingogram (HSG) for tubal patency/uterine cavity.
  • Infertility (Male): Semen analysis (count, motility, morphology).
  • Infertility (Female): Hormonal profile (FSH, LH, Prolactin, Progesterone), HSG, Laparoscopy.
  • Endoscopy: Laparoscopy (endometriosis, ectopic), Hysteroscopy (fibroids, polyps).
  • Cervical Cancer Screening: Pap smear, Colposcopy, Biopsy.
  • GTDs: Beta-hCG levels, Ultrasound ('snow storm').
