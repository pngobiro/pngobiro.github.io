üå± Physiology of Puberty

Puberty is the period when the endocrine and gametogenic functions of the gonads have first developed to the point where reproduction is possible.

This is characterized by sequence of events by which a child becomes a young adult:

  • The beginning of gametogenesis
  • Secretion of gonadal hormones
  • Development of secondary sexual characters and reproductive functions
  • Sexual dimorphism is accentuated.

Endocrinology in Puberty

The levels of gonadal steroids and gonadotropins are low until the age of 6-8 yrs. This is mainly due to the negative feedback effect of estrogen to the hypothalamic pituitary system (Gonadostat). The gonadostat remains very sensitive (6-15 times) to the negative feedback effect, even though the level of estradiol is very low ($10 \mathrm{pg} / \mathrm{ml}$) during that time. As puberty approaches this negative feedback effect of estrogen is gradually lost.

Developmental Stages

Prepubertal Stage (8-9 yr)

The hypothalamic-anterior pituitary-gonadal axis is suppressed by neuronal restraint pathways and negative feedback provided by minute amounts of circulating gonadal steroids. Thus there are undetectable serum levels of luteinizing hormone (LH) and sex hormones.

Peripubertal Period (1-3 yr before onset)

Pulsatile secretion of low levels of LH during sleep secondary to endogenous episodic discharge of hypothalamic gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH). Nocturnal pulses of LH continue to increase in amplitude.

GnRH is the major, if not the only, hormone responsible for the onset and progression of puberty.

Puberty in Girls (8-13yr)

  • Thelarche (Development of Breasts) - Breast bud - 10-11 yrs
  • Pubarche (Development of axillary and pubic hair) - Appearance of pubic hair - 6-12 mo later
  • Peak height velocity occurs early (at breast stage II-III, typically between 11 and 12 yr of age) in girls and always precedes menarche.
  • Menarche (first menstrual period) Interval to menarche - 2-2.5 yr but may be as long as 6 yr after thelarche. Mean age of menarche - 12.75 yr. (13.5 yrs in rural girls)

Puberty in Boys (9-14yr)

  • Growth of the testes ($>3 \mathrm{~mL}$ in volume or 2.5 cm in longest diameter)
  • Thinning of the scrotum and pigmentation of the scrotum
  • Growth of the penis, seminal vesicles and prostrate
  • Pubic hair then appears. Appearance of axillary hair usually occurs in midpuberty, 2 yr after pubic hair.
  • In boys, unlike girls, acceleration of growth (5-15cm/yr in early adolescence but later drops) begins after puberty is well under way and is maximal at genital stage IV-V (typically between 13 and 14 yr of age).

Precocious Puberty

The term precocious puberty is reserved for girls who exhibit any secondary sex characteristics before the age of 8 or menstruate before the age of 10.

  • Isosexual: features are due to excess production of estrogen.
  • Heterosexual: features are due to excess production of androgen (from ovarian and adrenal neoplasm).

Forms of Precocious Puberty

GnRH Dependent (Central/True)
  • 80% of cases
  • Constitutional (most common)
  • Juvenile primary hypothyroidism
  • Intracranial lesions (trauma, tumor, infection)
GnRH Independent (Peripheral/Pseudopuberty)
  • Ovarian tumors (Granulosa cell, Theca cell)
  • Adrenal Hyperplasia or Tumor
  • McCune-Albright syndrome
  • Iatrogenic (Estrogen/androgen intake)
üíä

Treatment Goals & Management

Goals: To reduce gonadotropin secretions, suppress gonadal steroidogenesis, decrease growth rate to normal (slowing skeletal maturation), and protect the girl from sex abuse.

GnRH Agonist Therapy: Drug of choice for GnRH dependent precocious puberty. It suppresses the premature activation of the hypothalamopituitary axis due to down regulation. Examples: Buserelin, Medroxyprogesterone acetate, Cyproterone acetate, Danazol.

Delayed Puberty

Puberty is said to be delayed when the breast tissue and/or pubic hair have not appeared by 13-14 years or menarche appears as late as 16 years. The normal upper age limit of menarche is 15 years.

  • Hypergonadotropic hypogonadism: Gonadal dysgenesis (Turner syndrome 45 XO), Pure gonadal dysgenesis, Ovarian failure.
  • Hypogonadotropic hypogonadism: Constitutional delay, Chronic illness/malnutrition, Primary hypothyroidism, Kallmann's syndrome, Intracranial lesions.
  • Eugonadism (Anatomical causes): M√ºllerian agenesis, Imperforate hymen, Transverse vaginal septum, Androgen insensitivity syndrome.

üîÑ The Menstrual Cycle

A periodic physiologic vaginal hemorrhage, occurring at approximately $28 \pm 7$ days interval, and having its source from the shedding of uterine mucous membrane. Usually the bleeding is preceded by ovulation and predecidual changes in the endometrium.

The first menstruation (menarche) occurs between 11-15 years with a mean of 13 years. It is more closely related to bone age than to chronological age.

1. The Ovarian Cycle

Def: is the cyclic hormonal changes and other series of changes that occur in the ovary to mature the immature follicle and recruit the oocyte.

Follicular Phase

Extends from the beginning of menstruation (day 1) to the onset of ovulation. Average length 10-14 days. Includes recruitment of follicles (preantral), antrum formation, and selection of the dominant follicle.

Luteal Phase

Extends from ovulation to the beginning of menstruation. Most predictable and constant (14 days) in length. Characterized by corpus luteum formation and progesterone production.

üí°

Two Cell, Two Gonadotrophin Hypothesis

Steroidogenesis is compartmentalized in two cell types within the follicle:

  • Theca cells: Responsive to LH. LH stimulates production of androgens from cholesterol.
  • Granulosa cells: Responsive to FSH. FSH stimulates the conversion of thecally derived androgens to oestrogens (aromatization).

Ovulation

Occurs 36 hours after the onset of the LH surge. The dominant follicle ruptures, releasing the oocyte. The hormonal trigger is a positive-feedback effect of rising estrogen levels on the pituitary, leading to a surge of LH.

2. The Endometrial Cycle

The endometrium consists of two distinct zones: the basal zone (uninfluenced by hormones, source of regeneration) and the functional zone (under influence of estrogen/progesterone, shed during menstruation).

  • Stage of Regeneration: Starts before menstruation ceases. Completed 2-3 days after end of menstruation.
  • Stage of Proliferation: Day 5-14 (till ovulation). Due to estrogen. Glands become tubular, epithelium columnar.
  • Secretory Phase: Day 15 to 5-6 days prior to menstruation. Due to combined estrogen/progesterone. Glands become corkscrew-shaped, glycogen secretion (vacuoles).
  • Menstrual Phase: Degeneration and casting off. Regression of corpus luteum leads to hormone withdrawal, spiral arteriole spasm, ischemia, and shedding.

ü©∫ Menstrual Disorders

1. Mittelschmerz's Syndrome (Ovular Pain)

Abdominal pain appearing in the midmenstrual period. Probable factors include increased tension of the Graafian follicle, peritoneal irritation by follicular fluid, and contraction of tubes/uterus.

2. Dysmenorrhea

Painful menstruation of sufficient magnitude so as to incapacitate day-to-day activities.

Primary (Spasmodic)

No identifiable pelvic pathology. Incidence 15-20%. Risk factors: duration of flow >5 days, early menarche, smoking. Pain usually lasts for few hours to 24-48 hours.

Secondary (Congestive)

Associated with pelvic pathology ( endometriosis, adenomyosis, fibroids, PID, IUCD). Pain is dull, situated in back/front, appears 3-5 days prior to period and relieves with bleeding.

Treatment: NSAIDs (Mefenamic acid), Oral contraceptives, Treat underlying cause.

3. Premenstrual Syndrome (PMS)

Cyclical psychoneuroendocrine disorder occurring during the luteal phase (last 7-10 days) and resolving by menstruation. Symptoms include bloating, weight gain, mastalgia, fatigue, depression, irritability.

Treatment: Lifestyle (salt/caffeine restriction), Vitamin B6, Diuretics (Spironolactone), SSRIs (Fluoxetine), OCPs.

4. Abnormal Uterine Bleeding (AUB)

Classification system by FIGO (2011) known by the acronym PALM-COEIN:

Structural Causes (PALM)

  • Polyp
  • Adenomyosis
  • Leiomyoma
  • Malignancy and hyperplasia

Non-structural Causes (COEIN)

  • Coagulopathy
  • Ovulatory dysfunction
  • Endometrial
  • Iatrogenic
  • Not yet classified

Specific Types of AUB

Menorrhagia
Causes include fibroids, adenomyosis, DUB, coagulation disorders. Treatment: Tranexamic acid, NSAIDs, OCPs, Mirena IUS, Surgery (Myomectomy/Hysterectomy).
Polymenorrhea
Seen in adolescence, premenopause, PID.
Metrorrhagia
Includes contact bleeding (cervical ca, polyps) and intermenstrual bleeding.
Oligomenorrhea
Common in PCOS, hyperprolactinemia, adolescence.
Amenorrhea
Primary (no menses by 16) or Secondary (cessation > 6 months). Causes: Pregnancy, lactation, outflow tract disorders, ovarian failure, pituitary/hypothalamic disorders.

üéóÔ∏è Polycystic Ovarian Syndrome (PCOS)

A syndrome of ovarian dysfunction characterized by hyperandrogenism and polycystic ovary morphology. Associated with insulin resistance and elevated LH levels.

Clinical Features

  • Oligomenorrhoea/amenorrhoea (65-75%)
  • Hirsutism (30-70%)
  • Subfertility (75%)
  • Obesity (40%)
  • Acanthosis nigricans (insulin resistance marker)

Diagnosis

  • Elevated testosterone levels.
  • Decreased sex hormone binding globulin (SHBG).
  • Elevated LH levels and LH:FSH ratio (> 2:3.1).
  • Ultrasound: $\geq 10$ peripheral cysts (2-8 mm) - 'string of pearls' sign. Increased ovarian stromal volume $>8 \mathrm{~cm}^{3}$.

Management

Treatment Options

  • Weight loss: Lifestyle modification.
  • Menstrual disturbance: Cyclic progestagens or OCPs.
  • Hirsutism: Anti-androgens (cyproterone acetate, spironolactone), cosmetic therapy.
  • Infertility: Induction of ovulation (clomiphene, letrozole), Metformin.
  • Metabolic: Metformin for insulin resistance.

üõë Postmenopausal Bleeding (PMB)

Vaginal bleeding after the menopause. In women not taking HRT, any bleeding is abnormal and must be investigated.

Differential Diagnosis

  • Atrophic vaginitis (majority)
  • Endometrial carcinoma (10% of women with PMB)
  • Endometrial hyperplasia
  • Endometrial polyps
  • Cervical malignancy
