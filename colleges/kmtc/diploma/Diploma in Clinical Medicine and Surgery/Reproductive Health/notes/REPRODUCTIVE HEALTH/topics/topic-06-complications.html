‚ö†Ô∏è Ectopic Pregnancy

Ectopic pregnancy refers to the implantation of a fertilized egg in a location outside of the uterine cavity. It is the result of a flaw in human reproductive physiology that allows the conceptus to implant and mature outside the endometrial cavity, which ultimately ends in the death of the fetus.

üìç

Implantation Sites

Fallopian tubes: Approximately 97.7%

  • Ampulla: 80% (Most common)
  • Isthmus: 12%
  • Fimbria: 5%
  • Cornua/Interstitia: 2-3%

Risk Factors

Tubal Damage

Result of infections such as PID or salpingitis, abdominal surgery, or tubal ligation.

Smoking

May contribute to decreased tubal motility by damage to the ciliated cells.

Altered Motility

Progesterone-only contraception and progesterone intrauterine devices (IUDs).

Assisted Reproduction

History of 2 or more years of infertility.

Clinical Presentation

The classic clinical triad is pain, amenorrhea, and vaginal bleeding; unfortunately, only about 50% of patients present with all 3 symptoms.

  • Abdominal pain (98.6%)
  • Amenorrhea (74.1%)
  • Irregular vaginal bleeding (56.4%)
  • Signs of shock: Orthostatic blood pressure changes and tachycardia (up to 20% of cases).

Diagnosis

Serum $\beta$-HCG Levels

Serial serum $\beta$-HCG levels are necessary. The discriminatory zone (level above which a gestational sac should be visible) is:

  • 1500-1800 mIU/mL: Transvaginal ultrasonography
  • 6000-6500 mIU/mL: Abdominal ultrasonography

Management Options

Expectant

For asymptomatic, hemodynamically stable patients with declining $\beta$-HCG levels.

Medical (Methotrexate)

Single-dose IM injection. Patient must be stable, have no severe pain, and normal liver/renal function.

Surgical

Salpingostomy: Incision over site, products removed, tube left open.
Salpingectomy: Removal of tube if damaged or future fertility not desired.

üçá Gestational Trophoblastic Diseases (GTDs)

A spectrum of interrelated but histologically distinct tumors originating from the placenta, characterized by the tumor marker $\beta$-hCG.

Classification

  1. 1Hydatidiform mole (Complete & Partial)
  2. 2Invasive mole
  3. 3Placental site trophoblastic tumor (PSTT)
  4. 4Choriocarcinoma
  5. 5Epithelioid trophoblastic tumor (ETT)

Hydatidiform Mole Types

Feature Complete Mole Partial Mole
Karyotype Diploid (46,XX usually). Paternal origin (Androgenesis). Triploid (69,XXX, 69,XXY). Maternal + 2 Paternal sets.
Fetal Tissue Absent. Present (often nonviable/malformed).
Villous Edema Diffuse, "bunch of grapes". Focal.
Clinical Large uterus, Theca-lutein cysts, Hyperemesis, Preeclampsia early. Missed/Incomplete abortion signs. Small/Normal uterus.
Malignant Risk Higher (15-20%). Lower (< 5%).

Ultrasound Findings

Complete Mole: Multiple hypoechoic areas corresponding to hydropic villi, described as a "snowstorm" pattern. No fetus.

Partial Mole: Focal cystic changes and fetal tissue may be noted.

Management & Follow-up

  • Termination: Suction curettage is the method of choice.
  • Monitoring: Serial $\beta$-hCG levels weekly until undetectable for 3 weeks, then monthly for 6-12 months.
  • Contraception: Effective contraception (OCPs) recommended during surveillance.

ü©∏ Abortion / Miscarriage

Defined as the spontaneous or induced loss of an early pregnancy. In Kenya, this is loss before 28 complete weeks of gestation (fetal viability).

Types of Spontaneous Abortion

Threatened Abortion
Bleeding arising from within the uterus. Patient may have cramping. No passage of tissue.
Inevitable Abortion
Impending abortion. Products of conception have not passed yet.
Incomplete Abortion
Bleeding and cramping continue. Severe pain often present.
Complete Abortion
Cervix is closed. Slight bleeding/cramping.
Missed Abortion
Cervix closed. No spontaneous passage. Often no symptoms.
Septic Abortion
Fevers, chills, abdominal pain, malodorous discharge. Potential for systemic spread.

Management Options

Expectant

Allows spontaneous passage. Avoids surgery risks. Unpredictable timing.

Medical

Misoprostol induces uterine contractions. Avoids surgery.

Surgical

D&C or MVA (Manual Vacuum Aspiration). Standard for heavy bleeding or septic cases.

üîÑ

Recurrent Miscarriage

Defined as 3 or more consecutive pregnancy losses. Affects up to 5% of couples. Causes include genetic factors (parental cytogenetics), anatomic defects (septum, fibroids), endocrine factors (thyroid, diabetes), and immunologic factors (antiphospholipid syndrome).

ü§¢ Hyperemesis Gravidarum (HEG)

Defined as unexplained intractable nausea, retching, or vomiting beginning in the first trimester, resulting in dehydration, ketonuria, and weight loss of more than 5% of prepregnancy weight.

Complications

  • Maternal: Wernicke's encephalopathy (thiamine deficiency), Acute tubular necrosis, Mallory-Weiss tear.
  • Psychological: Depression, anxiety.

Management Algorithm

  1. 1 Dietary/Lifestyle: Frequent small meals, dry foods (crackers), ginger, acupressure.
  2. 2 First Line Meds: Vitamin B6 (10-30 mg) + Doxylamine (12.5 mg).
  3. 3 Second Line: Antihistamines (Promethazine, Dimenhydrinate).
  4. 4 Persistent Symptoms: Prochlorperazine, Metoclopramide, Ondansetron (check safety).
  5. 5 Severe Dehydration: IV hydration, Thiamine replacement (to prevent Wernicke's), Steroids (Methylprednisolone) if refractory.
