POPULATIONS WITH SPECIAL NEEDS & LABOUR INTERVENTIONS

POPULATIONS WITH SPECIAL NEEDS DURING NORMAL LABOUR

People Living with Disability (PLWD)

People Living with Disability (PLWD) have less access to health care and health promotion services. Women with disabilities are recognized as particularly disadvantaged, experiencing exclusion double burden to their gender and disability condition and often very vulnerable to abuse.

Throughout the developing world, it is reported that people with disabilities have less access to healthcare services due to barriers and often experience unmet healthcare needs. Women with disabilities face more challenges in accessing healthcare, particularly maternal healthcare-resulting in higher risk situations and poorer outcomes than women without disabilities.

Disability is defined as a long-term physical, mental, intellectual or sensory impairment which in interaction with various barriers may hinder a person's full and effective participation in society on an equal basis with others.

Recommendations

Special programmes are needed to meet the special needs of women with disabilities. These should include:

  • Training service providers on providing services to the women with disabilities e.g. in communication skills with sensory disabled and to provide individualized care on types of disability
  • Disseminating guidelines for staff on how to deal with persons with disabilities during pregnancy, labour childbirth and in the postnatal period
  • Ensure that the existing and proposed physical infrastructure is universally accessible and friendly to PWDs in all aspects to include pregnancy, labour and childbirth and postnatal care
  • Integrate reproductive health and HIV/AIDS information and services for PLWD accessing pregnancy, delivery, and postnatal services.
  • Designing services that can provide properly packaged information for PWDs.
  • Make MCH programmes more inclusive by investing in outreach and family-based approaches

INTRAPARTUM FETAL SURVEILLANCE

Globally, approximately 140 million births occur every year. Over a third of maternal deaths and a substantial proportion of pregnancy-related life-threatening conditions are attributed to complications that arise during labour, childbirth or the immediate postpartum period, often as result of haemorrhage, obstructed labour or sepsis.

The burden of maternal and perinatal deaths is disproportionately higher in low- and middle-income countries (LMICs) compared to high-income countries (HICs). Almost all perinatal deaths occur in low- and middle-income countries (LMICs), and half occur intrapartum.

The partograph is associated with improved perinatal outcomes and is recommended for use with intermittent auscultation for intrapartum monitoring in low resource settings. In the active first stage of labour, auscultation is usually performed every 15-30 minutes, whereas in the second stage it is usually performed every 5 minutes/after every contraction.

WHO intrapartum fetal surveillance

  • Routine cardiotocography is not recommended for the assessment of fetal well-being on labour admission in healthy pregnant women presenting in spontaneous labour. (Not recommended)
  • Auscultation using a Doppler ultrasound device or Pinard fetal stethoscope is recommended for the assessment of fetal wellbeing on labour admission.

Clinical screening of Non reassuring fetal status (NRFS)

NRFS can be identified in any of the following ways:

  • Fetal heart rate that uses intermittent use of the fetoscope assessed before, during and after a uterine contraction.
  • Presence of meconium stained liquor and grade either following spontaneous rupture of membranes or artificial rupture of membranes
  • Non-stress testing done when monitoring the fetal heart rate during evaluation of the biophysical profile and doppler studies velocity- middle cerebral artery, umbilical artery (ultrasound)
  • Fetal movement by maternal perception
  • Fetal pulse oximetry, scalp blood pH assessment
  • Fetal surveillance intrapartum: The relation of the passage and power to the passenger during the events of labour (monitored with use of the partograph)
  • Fetal Stress testing associated with spontaneous labour or induction of labor or augmentation of labor
  • By use of the CTG

ANALYSING A CTG TRACING

CTG analysis starts with the evaluation of basic CTG features (baseline, variability, accelerations, decelerations, and contractions) followed by overall CTG classification. A record of the tracing needs to be put in the patient file and with back up archiving accessible to clinical staff for review.

Mnemonic for Interpretation: DR C BRAVADO

MNEMONIC PARTS NOTES
DR: Determine risk High, medium, or low risk (i.e., risk in terms of the clinical situation)
C: Contractions Rate, rhythm, frequency, duration, intensity, and resting tone
BRA: Baseline rate Bradycardia (< 110 bpm), normal (110 to 160 bpm), or tachycardia (> 160 bpm); rising baseline
V: Variability Reflects central nervous system activity: absent, minimal, moderate, or marked
A: Accelerations Spontaneous; stimulated; none. Rises from the baseline of $\geq$ 15 bpm, lasting $\geq$ 15 seconds. Preterm: $\geq$ 10 bpm, lasting $\geq$ 10 seconds
D: Decelerations Absent, early, variable, late, or prolonged
O: Overall assessment and written plan Stoplight algorithm or National Institute of Child Health and Human Development categorization. Assessment includes implementing an appropriate management plan

Fetal Heart Rate Characteristics

Peak $\geq$ 15 bpm above baseline, duration $\geq$ 15 seconds, but < 2 minutes from onset to return to baseline; before 32 weeks' gestation: peak $\geq$ 10 bpm above baseline, duration $\geq$ 10 seconds

Late The nadir of the deceleration occurs after the peak of the contraction

Action in suspected fetal hypoxia/acidosis

When fetal hypoxia/acidosis is anticipated or suspected i.e. suspicious and pathological CTG tracings, action is required to avoid adverse neonatal outcome. Good clinical judgement is required to diagnose the underlying cause for a suspicious or pathological CTG, to judge the reversibility of the conditions with which it is associated, and to determine the timing of delivery, with the objective of avoiding prolonged fetal hypoxia/acidosis, as well as unnecessary obstetric intervention.

  • Excessive uterine activity: is the most frequent cause of fetal hypoxia/acidosis and it can be detected by documenting tachysystole in the CTG tracing and/or palpating the uterine fundus. It can usually be reversed by reducing or stopping oxytocin infusion, removing administered prostaglandins if possible, and/or starting acute tocolysis.
  • Aortocaval compression: can occur in the supine position and lead to reduced placental perfusion. Turning the mother to her left side is frequently followed by normalization of the CTG pattern.
  • Transient cord compression: is another common cause of CTG changes (variable decelerations), and these can sometimes be reverted by changing the maternal position or by performing amnioinfusion.
  • Sudden maternal hypotension: can also occur during labor, usually after epidural or spinal analgesia, and it is usually reversible by rapid fluid administration and/or an intravenous ephedrine bolus.

AUGMENTATION OF LABOUR

Augmentation of labour is the process of stimulating the uterus to increase the frequency, duration and intensity of contractions after the onset of spontaneous labour. Common underlying causes include inefficient uterine contractions, abnormal fetal presentation or position, inadequate bony pelvis or soft tissue abnormalities of the mother.

Indications

  • Augmentation of labour has commonly been used to treat delayed labour when uterine contractions are assessed to be insufficiently strong or inappropriately coordinated to dilate the Cervix
  • Augmentation of labour should be performed only when there is a clear medical indication and the expected benefits outweigh the potential harms.
  • Retained second twin

Contraindications

  • Cephalopelvic disproportion. (This principle is relevant for all women but is even more crucial for multiparous women).
  • Previous uterine rupture
  • Previous Cesarean scar
  • Non reassuring fetal status in early labour
  • Meconium-stained liquor

Treatment/ Augmentation process

The traditional methods of labour augmentation have been with the use of intravenous oxytocin infusion and artificial rupture of the membranes (amniotomy). Oxytocin augmentation is recommended in women with ineffective contractions. Oxytocin Augmentation shortens birth outcomes but eventual reduction in instrumental delivery is not documented.

Administration of oxytocin

IV 5 IU in 500 mls of N/saline starting at 10 drops/min (Primigravida) and titrating by 10 drops/min up to a max of 60 drops/min. For Multiparous, start at IV 2.5 IU at 10 drops/min up to the max of 60drops /min. The fetus should be monitored continuously with CTG when the labour is accelerated with oxytocin. Duration, Strength and frequency of contractions should be continuously recorded on the partograph.

INDUCTION OF LABOUR

Induction of labor is done after viability at gestational ages of 28 weeks and above. It's the process of artificially stimulating the uterus to start labour. This is commonly performed when the risks of continuing the pregnancy are perceived to outweigh the risks of shortening the duration of the pregnancy.

Indications

Maternal

  • Hypertensive disorders with pregnancy: Severe pre-eclampsia, Imminent eclampsia, Eclampsia, Essential hypertension, Chronic nephritis
  • Antepartum haemorrhage: Placenta praevia type I&IIA, Accidental haemorrhage
  • Diabetes mellitus: To avoid intrauterine foetal death and dystocia due to macrosomia
  • Chorioamnionitis
  • Term rupture of membranes with positive culture of maternal group B Streptococcus (GBS)

Foetal

  • Post-term pregnancy confirmed gestation of 41+ weeks.
  • Intrauterine growth restriction.
  • Intrauterine foetal death.
  • Rh-isoimmunization.
  • Gross congenital anomalies

Methods of induction

Conservative method

  • Stripping of the membranes: by introducing the index or the middle finger into the cervical canal to separate the membranes from the lower uterine segment all around as much as possible. This stimulates natural prostaglandin production.
  • Sweeping of membranes: It solely improves the rate of entering spontaneous labour.
  • Mechanical method: Extra-amniotic insertion of foley catheter and inflated to 30-40 mls of normal saline and with sustained traction. The cervix will progressively dilate to 3-4 cm, followed by amniotomy and putting up oxytocin.

Amniotomy

Mode of action: Release of prostaglandins and Bringing the presenting part closer to the lower uterine segment so the uterine activity will be reflexly encouraged. Oxytocin should be commenced immediately after amniotomy.

Pharmacological modalities

  • Oxytocin: 5 units of syntocinon/Oxytocin in 500 ml of normal saline and start the IV drip by 10 drops/min to be increased by 10 drops/min every 15 min up to a maximum of 60 drops/min or until 3 moderate contractions / 10 minutes achieved.
  • Prostaglandins: They induce ripening of the cervix and uterine contractions. Dinoprostone intracervical gel or vaginal pessary.
  • Misoprostol: Oral misoprostol (200 $\mu$g, 2-hourly) is recommended for induction of labour. Vaginal low-dose misoprostol (25 $\mu$g, 6-hourly) is recommended. Misoprostol is not recommended for women with previous caesarean section.

Contraindications to Induction of Labour by Cervical Ripening

  • Placenta Previa- type 2B,3&4, vasa Previa, or a cord presentation
  • Abnormal foetal lie or presentation (e.g., a transverse lie or footling breech)
  • Prior classical Caesarean section or inverted "T" uterine incision
  • Significant previous uterine surgery (i.e., full thickness myometrial incision)
  • Active genital herpes simplex virus
  • Pelvic structural deformities
  • Invasive cervical carcinoma
  • Previous uterine rupture

VACUUM EXTRACTION

It is traction of the foetal head by a created negative pressure through a cup applied to the head. Vacuum extractor is composed of a specially designed cup, a rubber tube attaching the cup to a glass bottle with a screw in between to release the negative pressure, a manometer, and another rubber tube connecting the bottle to a suction piece.

Types of cups: Malmstrom cup (metal), Bird's cup, Soft cup (silastic).

Indications

  • To shorten the duration of second stage e.g. maternal distress in second stage, cardiac disease and CCF
  • Non reassuring fetal status in second stage
  • During the 1st stage: The small cup 3 or 4 cm may be used in a soft, stretchable cervix of not less than 7 cm dilatation.
  • During caesarean section: It may be used to extract the foetal head through the uterine incision.

Contraindications

  • Moderate or severe cephalopelvic disproportion.
  • Other presentations than vertex.
  • Premature infants.
  • Intact membranes.

Procedure

  1. Lithotomy position. Antiseptic measures for the vagina, vulva and perineum.
  2. Vaginal examination to check pelvic capacity, cervical dilatation, presentation, position, station and degree of flexion of the head and that the membranes are ruptured.
  3. Application of the cup: The largest cup that can easily passed is introduced sideways into the vagina by pressing it backwards against the perineum. It is then applied as near as possible to the posterior fontanelle over the mid sagittal line with its edge 3 cm from the anterior fontanelle.
  4. Creating the negative pressure: holding the cup in place, the negative pressure is gradually increased by 0.2 kg/cm¬≤ every 1 minute until -0.8 kg/cm¬≤ is attained. This creates an artificial caput within the cup.
  5. Traction: on the handle is made perpendicular to the cup and intermittently during uterine contractions, the direction of pull is changing as the head descends through the birth canal.
  6. Release of the cup: when the head is delivered the vacuum is reduced as slowly as it was created using the screw as this diminishes the risk of scalp damage.

Bird's safety rules for vacuum extraction:

  • The head must be completely or partially delivered with no more than 3 pulls.
  • The head should begin to move with the first pull.
  • The cup must not be applied more than thrice
  • Application of the cup must not exceed 20 minutes.

NB: Failed vacuum delivery conduct emergency caesarean section

CAESAREAN SECTION

A caesarean section (CS) is the process through which a fetus is delivered through an abdominal and uterine incision. Caesarean sections have become increasingly common over the past three decades. This has resulted in a major public health concern and cause worldwide debates due to potential maternal and perinatal risks associated with this increase, inequity in access and cost issues.

The incidence of caesarean sections is represented as a rate-Caesarean section rate- which is defined as the number of cesarean deliveries over the total number of live births, and is usually expressed as a percentage. The Caesarean section rate (CS rate) is between 10-15%.

Robson Classification

This system was proposed by WHO in 2015 in order to provide a classification system to monitor and compare CS rates at facility level in a standardized, reliable, consistent and action-oriented manner. The Robson system classifies all women admitted for delivery (not women who've only had a caesarean section) into one of 10 groups based on: number of previous pregnancies, previous uterine scars, current gestation type (singleton vs multiple gestation), gestational age, presentation and lie of the baby (cephalic, breech, transverse) and onset of labor.

Indications for caesarean sections

Absolute indications

  • Absolute disproportion: Small maternal pelvis, making vaginal birth impossible
  • Chorioamnionitis (amniotic infection syndrome)
  • Maternal pelvic deformity
  • Eclampsia and HELLP syndrome
  • Fetal asphyxia or fetal acidosis
  • Umbilical cord prolapse
  • Placenta praevia
  • Abnormal lie and presentation
  • Uterine rupture
  • More than 1 previous caesarean section scar

Relative indications

  • Pathological cardiotocography (CTG)
  • Failure to progress in labor (prolonged labor, secondary arrest)
  • Previous cesarean section: It is widely assumed that having had one cesarean section makes it impossible to have a vaginal delivery in subsequent pregnancies. However, the VBAC (Vaginal Birth After Caesarean delivery) criteria should be applied in instances of one previous scar and judgment based on this

Vaginal Birth After Caesarean (VBAC)

Criteria for VBAC:

  • One previous low-transverse cesarean delivery
  • Clinically adequate pelvis
  • No other uterine scars or previous rupture
  • Availability of skilled personnel available throughout active labor capable of monitoring labor and performing an emergency cesarean delivery.

FEMALE GENITAL MUTILATION AND CHILD BIRTH

FGM is practised for a variety of complex reasons and socio- culturally is believed that it is beneficial for the girl. It involves removing and damaging healthy and normal female genital tissue, and interferes with the natural function of girls and women's bodies with no health benefit but detrimental reproductive health consequences. These risks increasing with the severity of the procedure. FGM is a human rights violation and a form of child abuse, breaching the United Nations Convention on the Rights of the Child, and is a severe form of violence against women and girls. In Kenya this harmful practice is illegal and includes the medicalization of the same.

FGM classification

  • Type I: Often referred to as clitoridectomy, this is the partial or total removal of the clitoris (a small, sensitive and erectile part of the female genitals), and in very rare cases, only the prepuce.
  • Type II: Often referred to as excision, this is the partial or total removal of the clitoris and the labia minora, with or without excision of the labia majora.
  • Type III: Often referred to as infibulation, this is the narrowing of the vaginal opening through the creation of a covering seal. The seal is formed by cutting and repositioning the labia minora, or labia majora, sometimes through stitching, with or without removal of the clitoris.
  • Type IV: This includes all other harmful procedures to the female genitalia for non-medical purposes, e.g. pricking, piercing, incising, scraping and cauterizing the genital area.

Management of childbirth in a woman with FGM

Antenatal booking visits: Ask all women about FGM. Women who have undergone Type I, II and III FGM must be examined at their first antenatal attendance to assess the degree of damage. Record the appearance of the vulva on the clients ANC card.

Management during labor:

  • IF FGM has caused a tight introitus there is a need to increase the vaginal opening by performing an episiotomy
  • Women with type I, tend to be able to deliver vaginally without episiotomy unless there is extensive scarring causing inelasticity of the perineum
  • Women with type III: must be opened up during the second stage of labor when the presenting part is pressing on the vulva
  • EPISIOTOMY usually performed during the second stage of labor however can be performed earlier
  • If a woman presents with type III C/S is more appropriate than deinfibulation and vaginal delivery; Explain the need for operation and seek her consent to perform it Or refer her to a CEMONC

Postpartum management: Check for retained products make sure uterus is well contracted. Check the bladder -empty if necessary, or administer uterotonics drugs. Check for tears on the vulva and inside the birth canal. if there is bleeding or tears take appropriate action immediately.
