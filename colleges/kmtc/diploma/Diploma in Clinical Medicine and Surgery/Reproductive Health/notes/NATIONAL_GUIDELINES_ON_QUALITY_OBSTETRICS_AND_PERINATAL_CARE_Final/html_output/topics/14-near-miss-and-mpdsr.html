MATERNAL NEAR MISS AND MPDSR

MATERNAL NEAR MISS

A maternal near miss is defined as "a woman who nearly died but survived a complication that occurred during pregnancy, child birth or within 42 days of termination of pregnancy". Studying maternal near miss cases is a proxy to measure the quality of obstetric care, as maternal deaths are considered "the tip of the iceberg".

WHO Maternal Near Miss Criteria

Criteria include dysfunction in the following systems:

  • Cardiovascular: Shock, Cardiac arrest, Use of vasoactive drugs.
  • Respiratory: Acute cyanosis, Gasping, Intubation not for anesthesia.
  • Renal: Oliguria non-responsive to fluids/diuretics, Dialysis for acute renal failure.
  • Coagulation: Failure to form clots, Massive transfusion ($\ge$ 5 units).
  • Hepatic: Jaundice in pre-eclampsia.
  • Neurologic: Prolonged unconsciousness/coma, Stroke, Uncontrollable fits.
  • Uterine: Hysterectomy due to infection or hemorrhage.

Adapted Sub-Saharan Africa Tool

Includes additional parameters relevant to low-resource settings:

  • Eclampsia
  • Uterine rupture
  • Sepsis or severe systemic infection
  • Pulmonary edema
  • Severe abortion complications
  • Severe malaria
  • Severe pre-eclampsia with ICU admission
  • Laparotomy other than caesarean section

BREAKING THE SAD NEWS

Delivering bad news regarding maternal or perinatal death requires professionalism and empathy.

Recommended Steps

  1. Identify team leader and assemble team.
  2. Set a private environment and provide adequate time.
  3. Introduce team and assess recipient's emotional state.
  4. Disclose news clearly and honestly ("forecasting" or direct approach).
  5. Offer empathy, reassurance, and address concerns.
  6. Allow viewing/holding of body if desired.
  7. Address administrative aspects and offer follow-up.

Principles of Bereavement Care

  • Individualized care respecting culture/religion.
  • Good communication using sensitive language (e.g., "your baby").
  • Shared decision making.
  • Recognition of parenthood and memory making.
  • Acknowledging partner's and family's grief.
  • Ongoing emotional and practical support.

MATERNAL & PERINATAL DEATH SURVEILLANCE AND RESPONSE (MPDSR)

MPDSR is a quality improvement approach involving:

  • Surveillance: Identifying all maternal and perinatal deaths.
  • Review: Determining underlying causes and contributory factors.
  • Response: Implementing actions to prevent future deaths.

Guiding Principles

  • No blame policy.
  • Focus on health systems improvement.
  • Zero-reporting principle.
  • Confidentiality and anonymity.
  • Action-oriented (Response is key).

Reporting Timelines

  • Maternal deaths notified within 24 hours.
  • Perinatal deaths notified within 72 hours.
  • Facility reviews within 7 days.
  • Community reviews (Verbal Autopsy) within 30 days.

Response Strategy

Actions should be implemented at all levels (Community, Facility, Sub-county, County, National). Use evidence-based approaches to address avoidable factors.
