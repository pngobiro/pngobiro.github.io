MATERNAL MENTAL HEALTH

Introduction

Mental health is defined as "a state of well-being in which the individual realizes his or her own abilities, can cope with the normal stresses of life, can work productively and fruitfully, and is able to make a contribution to his or her community."

Maternal mental health aims to address mental health issues in women along a continuum so as to include preconception care, antenatal care, intrapartum care and postpartum care. Previously, maternal mental health issues mostly concentrated on the postpartum period. Major maternal mental health issues are now being referred to in the context of the perinatal period as opposed to the post-partum period.

Mental illness is treatable and requires the ability to recognize, assess and treat for sustained maternal, new-born and family well-being. The illness may be pre-existent or develop in the course of the mother's life cycle or specific to a given pregnancy. Challenges with continuation, change or cessation of psychotropic drugs exist during pregnancy and lactation.

A multi- disciplinary approach is recommended in maternal mental ill health.

Specific Disorders and Management

Peri-natal anxiety disorders

Anxiety disorders are the most prevalent psychiatric disorders with women, particularly those in their child bearing years being most affected. Generalized anxiety disorder (GAD) is more common in postpartum women relative to the general population. Medical diagnoses such as hypo/hyperthyroidism, anemia, and hypertension have a similar phenotype and should be ruled out.

Peri-natal depression

Depression related disorders are common during pregnancy and in the post-partum period. The point prevalence of depression (major and minor) is estimated to be up to 11% in pregnancy and 13% in the first 3 months postpartum. Perinatal onset of symptoms relating to depression defines symptoms occurring anytime during pregnancy up to 1-month post-partum. In addition to depressed mood, loss of interest in pleasurable activities and decreased energy, perinatal depression with a postpartum onset is characterized by obsessive-compulsive, particularly intrusive violent thoughts, psychotic symptoms, and anxiety.

For women with suspected post- natal depression, the Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale can be used for screening purposes prior to referral to a psychiatrist for further evaluation and management.

Postpartum blues

Postpartum blues also known as baby blues is the commonest constellation of mood symptoms experienced by women in the immediate postpartum period. This affects approximately 50-80% of women. It is transient in nature with an onset after the third postpartum day, typically peaking by day five and resolving by day ten postpartum.

Symptoms include:

  • Mood lability (rapid mood shifts)
  • Tearfulness -not related to depressive episodes
  • Irritability
  • Anxiety
  • Insomnia
  • Lack of energy and increased fatigue
  • Loss of appetite
  • A general experience of feeling overwhelmed-particularly with regard to newborn caregiving tasks

Management: No need for pharmacological treatment. Psychosocial treatment modalities apply and can include Psychoeducation and encouraging the mother to seek for help from the spouse, family members and friends.

Postpartum psychosis

Postpartum psychosis occurs in association with mood disorders (major depressive or manic episodes). It occurs in approximately 1-2 women per 1000 childbirths. The syndrome is often characterized by the mother's depression, delusions, and thoughts of harming either herself or her infant. The onset of florid psychotic symptoms is preceded by prodromal symptoms such as insomnia, restlessness, agitation, lability of mood, and mild cognitive deficits.

Postpartum psychosis is a psychiatric emergency. Management is multi- disciplinary with the involvement of a psychiatrist.

General principles of care of maternal mental health disorders

Preconception advice for women with severe mental illness

  • Planning for motherhood: Discussions of family and advice on methods of contraception should be offered to avoid unplanned pregnancies.
  • Potential effects of pregnancy on illness: For each woman, her individual risk of a relapse or recurrence should be discussed based on the severity of her mental illness and coexisting family history in relation to childbirth.
  • Optimisation of physical and mental health: Preconception is an ideal time to address a range of issues key to a healthy pregnancy. These issues will include smoking, nutrition, drug and alcohol use, domestic violence.
  • Medication: The risks and benefits of all options should be discussed, including continuation, stopping and switching of medication.

Care during pregnancy

  • A perinatal care plan for women with current or past severe mental illness should be present.
  • Antenatal services should therefore identify women with histories that would put them at increased risk of a severe episode in the perinatal period, even if they are currently well.
  • Other factors that can trigger or increase the risk of relapse need to be addressed. These include stress levels and sleep disorders.
  • Proper parenting support systems should be established during this period.

Decision making regarding admission in the post-partum period

Many women with severe mental illness in the post-partum period will require admission to hospital. The following factors/ presentation may necessitate admission:

  • Severe episode with significant impairment
  • Suspicion of post-partum psychosis-an acute onset of a new episode in the immediate postpartum
  • Delusions involving the baby
  • Poor social support from partner and other family members
  • Previous severe episodes of illness
  • Suicidal ideation
  • Aggression to others
  • Risk of harm to infant including poor infant care and lack of bonding

NORMAL LABOUR

Introduction

Quality maternal healthcare is key for the achievement of SDG-3.1 and 3.2. This includes: preconception care, antenatal care (including counselling for birth preparedness and possible complications), intrapartum care and post-partum care. All pregnancies should be handled with caution. Amongst women selected for low obstetric risk, approximately 25% will develop peri-partum complications necessitating transfer to an obstetrician led service.

Intrapartum care for positive childbirth experience articulates the norms of good practice to the desired end point of a healthy mother and healthy new-born. A positive childbirth experience defined as one that fulfils or exceeds a woman's prior personal and sociocultural beliefs and expectations, including giving birth to a healthy baby in a clinically and psychologically safe environment with continuity of practical and emotional support from a birth companion(s) or kind, technically competent clinical staff.

Definition

Normal labour is a physiological process which commence spontaneously at term (37 completed weeks) with rhythmic regular contractions of increasing intensity and frequency accompanied by progressive cervical effacement and dilation, and descent of presenting part (preferably cephalic) resulting in expulsion of a healthy foetus, a complete placenta and membranes and a health mother.

Stages of Normal Labour

  • 1st Stage: from onset of labour to full dilatation of the cervix.
    • Latent first stage: a period of time characterized by painful uterine contractions and variable changes of the cervix, including some degree of effacement and slower progression of dilatation up to 5 cm for first and subsequent labours.
    • Active first stage: a period of time characterized by regular painful uterine contractions, a substantial degree of cervical effacement and more rapid cervical dilatation from 5 cm until full dilatation for first and subsequent labours.
  • 2nd Stage: from full dilatation to expulsion of the foetus.
    • Definition: The second stage is the period of time between full cervical dilatation and birth of the baby, during which the woman has an involuntary urge to bear down, as a result of expulsive uterine contractions.
    • Duration: In first labors, birth is usually completed within 3 hours whereas in subsequent labors, birth is usually completed within 2 hours.
  • 3rd Stage: from delivery of the baby, to delivery of placenta.
  • 4th Stage: Up to one hour after expulsion of placenta

Third stage of labour

Care option Recommendation
Prophylactic uterotonics The use of uterotonics for the prevention of postpartum haemorrhage (PPH) during the third stage of labour is recommended for all births. Oxytocin (10 IU, IM/IV) is the recommended uterotonic drug.
Delayed umbilical cord clamping Delayed umbilical cord clamping (not earlier than 1 minute after birth) is recommended for improved maternal and infant health and nutrition outcomes.
Controlled cord traction (CCT) In settings where skilled birth attendants are available, controlled cord traction (CCT) is recommended for vaginal births if the care provider and the parturient woman regard a small reduction in blood loss and a small reduction in the duration of the third stage of labour as important.
Uterine massage Sustained uterine massage is not recommended as an intervention to prevent postpartum haemorrhage (PPH) in women who have received prophylactic oxytocin.

Care of the new-born

  • Routine nasal or oral suction: In neonates born through clear amniotic fluid who start breathing on their own after birth, suctioning of the mouth and nose should not be performed.
  • Skin-to-skin contact: New-borns without complications should be kept in skin-to-skin contact (SSC) with their mothers during the first hour after birth to prevent hypothermia and promote breastfeeding.
  • Breastfeeding: All new-borns, including low-birth-weight (LBW) babies who are able to breastfeed, should be put to the breast as soon as possible after birth when they are clinically stable, and the mother and baby are ready
  • Haemorrhagic disease prophylaxis using vitamin K: All new-borns should be given 1 mg of vitamin K intramuscularly after birth (i.e. after the first hour by which the infant should be in skin-to-skin contact with the mother and breastfeeding should be initiated).
  • Bathing: Bathing should be delayed until 24 hours after birth. If this is not possible due to cultural reasons, bathing should be delayed for at least six hours.

The Labour Care Guide

In February 2018, the World Health Organization (WHO) published a consolidated set of recommendations on intrapartum care for a positive childbirth experience. To facilitate effective implementation of the above recommendations, WHO reviewed and revised the design of the previous partograph. The Labour Care Guide (LCG) was designed for health personnel to monitor the well-being of women and babies during labour through regular assessments to identify any deviation from normality.

The LCG has seven sections:

  1. Identifying information and labour characteristics at admission
  2. Supportive care
  3. Care of the baby
  4. Care of the woman
  5. Labour progress
  6. Medication
  7. Shared decision-making

Labour monitoring to action

To ensure the systematic and consistent application of the LCG, health providers are encouraged to use the Assess, Record, Check, Plan approach, which involves:

  • Assess: (assess the well-being of woman and her baby, and progress of labour)
  • Record: (document labour observations)
  • Check reference threshold: (compare labour observations with reference values in the "Alert" column)
  • Plan: (decide whether and what interventions are required, in consultation with the woman, and document accordingly).
