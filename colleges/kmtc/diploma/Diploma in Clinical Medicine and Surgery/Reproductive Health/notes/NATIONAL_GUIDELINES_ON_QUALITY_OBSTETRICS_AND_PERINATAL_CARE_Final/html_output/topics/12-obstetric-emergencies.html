OBSTETRIC EMERGENCIES

MATERNAL SHOCK

Shock refers to a state of compromised tissue perfusion that results in cellular hypoxia and is defined as a syndrome initiated by acute hypoperfusion, leading to tissue hypoxia and vital organ dysfunction.

Types of shock

  • Hypovolemia: from internal or external fluid loss
  • Cardiogenic factors: e.g., acute myocardial infarction, peri-partum cardiomyopathy, end-stage cardiomyopathy, advanced valvular heart disease, myocarditis, or cardiac arrhythmias
  • Obstruction: e.g., pulmonary embolism, amniotic fluid embolism, cardiac tamponade, or tension pneumothorax
  • Distributive factors: e.g., severe sepsis or anaphylaxis from the release of inflammatory mediators

Triage (MEOWS Chart)

Regular observation of all vital signs (including temperature, pulse rate, blood pressure and respiratory rate) should be recorded on a Modified Early Obstetric Warning Score (MEOWS). MEOWS chart has proved to be a useful bedside screening tool for prediction of obstetric morbidity and should be used routinely in every obstetric unit.

HYPOVOLEMIC SHOCK

Hypovolemic shock occurs when circulating blood volume is significantly decreased to result in inadequate or uneven tissue oxygenation.

Causes of major obstetric hemorrhage

  • Antepartum/Intrapartum: Ectopic pregnancy, Placental abruption, Uterine rupture, Placenta previa, Trauma, Coagulopathy, Placenta accreta spectrum
  • Postpartum (4 Ts): Tone (Uterine atony), Tissue (Retained placental fragments), Thrombosis (Coagulopathy), Trauma (Genital tract lacerations, Uterine inversion)

Management

  • Call for help
  • Position the woman (left lateral with legs higher)
  • Start CPR if unresponsive (30 compressions: 2 breaths)
  • Establish airway, give oxygen
  • Two wide bore IV lines, send bloods
  • Restoration of blood volume (Crystalloids, Colloids, Blood)
  • Detect cause and arrest hemorrhage (Medical/Surgical interventions)

ANAPHYLACTIC SHOCK

Anaphylaxis is a rare, life-threatening generalized hypersensitivity reaction occurring during pregnancy, typically resulting in maternal hypotension and/or fetal morbidity.

Symptoms

  • Respiratory and gastrointestinal (dyspnea, vomiting)
  • Skin and mucosal (urticaria, itchy rash)
  • Cardiovascular and CNS (hypotension, seizures)

Management

  • Immediate cessation of triggering factors
  • Airway/BP support, 100% oxygen
  • Aggressive fluid resuscitation
  • Medications: Epinephrine (IM), Antihistamines, Corticosteroids

CARDIOGENIC SHOCK

A life-threatening condition resulting from inadequate circulation due to primary failure of the ventricles.

Major causes

  • Valvular heart disease
  • Peri-partum cardiomyopathy
  • Arrhythmias
  • Amniotic embolism
  • Myocardial infarction

SEPTIC SHOCK

Life-threatening clinical syndrome caused by decreased tissue perfusion and oxygen delivery as a result of severe infection.

Management

1. Restoration of circulatory function and oxygenation

  • Volume replacement
  • Corticosteroids (e.g. Hydrocortisone)
  • $\beta$-adrenergic stimulants
  • Supplemental oxygen

2. Eradication of infection

  • Antibiotic therapy: Broad spectrum empirical IV antibiotics (e.g. Ceftriaxone + Metronidazole + Gentamycin OR Clindamycin + Gentamycin). Start within an hour.
  • Surgical treatment: Removal of retained infected tissues (e.g. suction evacuation, hysterectomy).

OBSTRUCTIVE SHOCK

Caused by obstruction of the great vessels or the heart itself.

Causes (4 H's and 4 T's rule)

  • Pericardial tamponade
  • Tension pneumothorax
  • Massive pulmonary thromboembolism

AMNIOTIC FLUID EMBOLISM (AFE)

A serious complication where amniotic fluid or fetal debris enters maternal circulation leading to cardiovascular collapse.

Clinical manifestations

  • Sudden anxiety, agitation
  • Dyspnea, cyanosis
  • Hypotension, Cardiopulmonary arrest
  • Coagulopathy

Management

  • Resuscitation: Oxygenation (100%), Fluid resuscitation.
  • Correcting coagulopathy: Blood products (FFP, platelets, cryoprecipitate).
  • Vasopressors: Epinephrine, Phenylephrine, Dopamine/Noradrenaline.
  • Delivery: Delivery of fetus. Hysterectomy may be required for hemorrhage.

PROLONGED LABOUR

Prolonged latent phase: > 8 hours.
Prolonged active phase: > 12 hours without full assessment.

Causes (3 Ps)

  • Power: Poor/uncoordinated uterine action
  • Passenger: Fetal head too large, malposition, malpresentation
  • Passage: Abnormal pelvis, obstruction

Management

  • Supportive: Monitor vitals/FHR, hydration (IV fluids), bladder emptying, antibiotics if indicated.
  • Definitive:
    • First stage: Augmentation (oxytocin) if no obstruction/malpresentation.
    • Second stage: Vacuum extraction or C-section depending on fetal head station.

OBSTRUCTED LABOUR

Arrest of vaginal delivery due to mechanical obstruction despite strong contractions.

Clinical features

  • Maternal distress: Exhaustion, fever, rapid pulse, dehydration.
  • Abdominal: Hard/tender uterus, tetanic contractions, Bandl's ring, fetal distress/absent FHS.
  • Vaginal: Oedematous vulva, dry vagina, foul smelling liquor, large caput, severe moulding.

Treatment

  • Supportive: Resuscitate, Rehydrate, Catheterize, Antibiotics.
  • Definitive: Delivery via Caesarean section is safest. Craniotomy if fetus dead. Vacuum only if cervix fully dilated and head low (0 station).

RUPTURED UTERUS

Catastrophic event associated with high mortality.

Risk factors

  • Previous scar (C/S, myomectomy)
  • Obstructed labour
  • Misuse of uterotonics (oxytocin/misoprostol)
  • Grand multiparity

Diagnosis

Sudden severe abdominal pain, cessation of contractions, fetal distress/death, regression of presenting part, shock.

Management principles

  • Immediate stabilization
  • Urgent Laparotomy
  • Delivery of fetus
  • Repair of tear or Hysterectomy depending on extent of injury and desire for future childbearing.

OBSTETRIC FISTULA

Abnormal opening between genital tract and urinary tract/rectum caused by prolonged obstructed labour (ischemic necrosis).

Classification (Waaldijk)

  • Type I: Not involving closing mechanism
  • Type II: Involving closing mechanism
  • Type III: Ureter/exceptional fistulas

Diagnosis

History of leakage, Speculum exam, Dye test (Methylene blue), 3 swab test.

Management

  • Immediate: Continuous bladder drainage (foley) for 1 month (may heal small fistulas).
  • Surgical: Repair by specialist.
  • Post-op: Continuous drainage (10-14 days), high fluid intake.

POST ABORTION CARE (PAC)

Initial assessment

Triage for shock, severe bleeding, sepsis, intra-abdominal injury.

Treatment of Incomplete Abortion

  • < 12 weeks: Manual Vacuum Aspiration (MVA) or Medical management (Misoprostol).
  • > 12 weeks: Surgical evacuation in theatre or Medical induction.

MVA Procedure

  • Prepare patient (pain meds, antibiotics).
  • Cervical antiseptic prep.
  • Para-cervical block.
  • Dilate cervix (if needed).
  • Insert cannula, attach aspirator.
  • Suction contents (rotate cannula).
  • Inspect tissue.
