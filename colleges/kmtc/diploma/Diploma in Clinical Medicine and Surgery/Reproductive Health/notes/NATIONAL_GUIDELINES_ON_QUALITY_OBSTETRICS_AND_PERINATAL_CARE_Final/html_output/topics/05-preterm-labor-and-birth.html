PRETERM LABOR

Definition

Preterm labour denotes regular uterine contractions accompanied by progressive cervical dilation with or without effacement at less than 37 weeks of gestation.

Preterm birth occurs between 24- and 37weeks gestation. In two-thirds of cases it occurs following spontaneous onset of labour. Only a minority of women who present with preterm contractions known as threatened premature labour (TPTL) progress to actual labour and delivery

Spontaneous preterm labor, a syndrome caused by multiple pathologic processes, contributes to about 70% of preterm deliveries.

Causes of preterm labour

Maternal causes

Demographic factors:

  • Maternal age <18 years and >35 years
  • Maternal weight <55 kg
  • Uterine distortion due to: m√ºllerian duct abnormalities, fibroids
  • Cervical incompetence due to: trauma, cone biopsy
  • Infection e.g., chorioamnionitis, UTI
  • Physical injury
  • History of spontaneous preterm birth
  • Hormonal imbalances

Pregnancy/ fetal causes

  • Preterm pre-labour rupture of membranes in current pregnancy
  • Antepartum hemorrhage
  • Uterine over distension due to multi-fetal gestation or poly-hydramnios
  • Fetal anomaly
  • Utero-placental insufficiency (e.g., hypertension, insulin-dependent diabetes, drug abuse, smoking>10 cigarettes/day, alcohol consumption)

Prevention of Preterm Labour and Delivery

Progesterone Therapy

In women with single gestation pregnancy and a history of spontaneous preterm delivery, antenatal progesterone therapy is the most effective strategy to decrease the risk of a recurrent preterm delivery. Progesterone supplementation is beneficial in these women starting at 16 to 24 weeks' gestation and continuing through 34 weeks' gestation.

Two types of progesterone therapy are currently in use for the prevention of preterm delivery. They include a weekly intramuscular injection of 17-alpha hydroxyprogesterone caproate or medroxy progesterone acetate 5 mg tablet and daily administration of natural micronized progesterone vaginal gel, vaginal suppository, or oral capsule.

Cervical Cerclage and Pessary

Cervical cerclage, an encircling suture placed around the cervix before or during pregnancy, has been used to help correct structural defects or cervical weakening in high-risk women with a shortened cervix.

Studies have shown that cerclage is associated with a decrease in preterm delivery and in perinatal death when used in women with a prior preterm delivery and a cervical length of 25 mm or less.

Guidance on evaluation and management of a shortened cervical length (CL)

  • CL should be confirmed using a standardized technique, with the TV US being the current gold standard.
  • A multidisciplinary team approach should be initiated, with involvement of an obstetrician and neonatologist to discuss risks of PTB if the patient is found to have a short cervix.
  • Repeat TV US in 1-2 weeks, or consider commencement of progesterone for a CL of 20-25 mm.
  • Progesterone should be offered for prevention of PTB in women at low risk if CL is <20 mm at 18-22 weeks.
  • For women deemed to be high risk, cervical cerclage should be considered if there is cervical dilatation pre-viability or if the cervix continues to shorten during surveillance.
  • Progesterone is generally the preferred treatment because of the lower risk of surgical complications.

Other prevention methods of preterm births

  • Preconception care package, including immediate post pregnancy family planning (e.g., Birth spacing and adolescent friendly services), education and nutrition especially for girls, and STI prevention
  • Antenatal care packages for all women, including screening for and management of STIs, high blood pressure and diabetes; behavior change for lifestyle risks; and targeted care of women at increased risk of preterm birth
  • Provider education to promote appropriate induction and caesarean section.
  • Policy support including smoking cessation and employment safeguards of pregnant women

Diagnosis

Assessment in a woman with suspected preterm labor

  • Establish dates:
    • Review history of pregnancy (estimated date of delivery, menstrual history, ultrasounds)
    • Examine the prenatal record for menstrual history, estimated date of delivery, information from dating ultrasound, and clinical growth
  • Evaluate contractions:
    • History (frequency, intensity, duration, changes with time)
    • Abdominal examination for uterine activity using a tocodynamometer, if available
  • Cervical assessment:
    • Sterile speculum exam initially to rule out preterm pre-labor rupture of membranes (PPROM) and to obtain cultures
    • If indicated digital examination can be performed after premature rupture of membranes is ruled out for the purposes of determining cervical position, dilatation and effacement.

NB: Women should be instructed early in their antepartum care to be vigilant for signs and symptoms of impending preterm labour and to contact their health care provider or go to their health care facility. Signs and symptoms may include:

  • Contractions
  • Vaginal fluid loss
  • Vaginal bleeding
  • Maternal perception of changes in pelvic pressure, low dull backache, or vaginal discharge

Clinical suspicion should be raised by the presence of uterine contractions combined with a cervical examination suggesting early dilation and/or effacement. This approach facilitates early institution of therapy but results in a 50% over-diagnosis of preterm labor (PTL).

Investigations

Initial investigations include:

  • Non-stress cardiotocogram
  • Tocography
  • Trans-vaginal ultrasound of the cervix
  • Cervico-vaginal swab for fetal fibronectin

Other investigations to consider:

  • Nitrazine test
  • Microscopy, culture and sensitivity of vaginal fluid
  • Kleihauer-Betke blood test
  • Urine toxicology screen
  • TORCH (toxoplasmosis, other infections i.e. syphilis, rubella, cytomegalovirus infection, herpes simplex)
  • Immunoglobulin (IgG and IgM) screening whenever historical or clinical suspicion is present.

In patients with a history of mid-trimester loss, laboratory tests for risk assessment include:

  • Rapid plasma reagin test
  • Gonorrhea and chlamydia screening
  • Vaginal pH/wet smear/whiff test
  • Anticardiolipin antibody (e.g., anticardiolipin immunoglobulin [Ig] G and IgM , anti-beta2 microglobulin)
  • Lupus anticoagulant antibody
  • Activated partial thromboplastin time
  • One-hour glucose challenge test

Management of preterm labour

Prolongation of pregnancy

No intervention has been shown to reduce the incidence of preterm birth. However, tocolysis has been shown to prolong pregnancy for 48 hours or more.

This provides the window of opportunity for the administration and absorption of corticosteroids. It also allows for the transportation of the woman to a tertiary center if necessary.

Tocolytic treatments (acute and maintenance treatments) are not recommended for women at risk of imminent preterm birth for the purpose of improving newborn outcomes.

Contraindications to Tocolysis

In a significant percentage of women in preterm labor, tocolysis may be contraindicated:

  • Gestational hypertension with proteinuria or other medical indication
  • Chorioamnionitis
  • Mature fetus
  • Imminent delivery
  • Intrauterine fetal death or lethal fetal abnormality

Antenatal corticosteroid therapy

Dexamethasone and betamethasone cross the placenta and induce enzymes that accelerate fetal pulmonary maturity. It takes 48 hours after the first dose for the full benefit to be achieved.

Four doses of dexamethasone (6 mg IM) 12 hours apart are recommended for acceleration of fetal lung maturity. Alternatively, two doses of betamethasone (12 mg IM) 24 hours apart can be used.

Antenatal corticosteroid therapy is recommended for women at risk of preterm birth from 24 weeks to 34 weeks of gestation when the following conditions are met:

  • Gestational age assessment can be accurately undertaken;
  • Preterm birth is considered imminent;
  • There is no clinical evidence of maternal infection;
  • Adequate childbirth care is available (including the capacity to recognize and safely manage preterm labour and birth);
  • The preterm newborn can receive adequate care if needed (including resuscitation, thermal care, feeding support, infection treatment and safe oxygen use).

Mode of delivery

Routine delivery by caesarean section for the purpose of improving preterm newborn outcomes is not recommended, regardless of cephalic or breech presentation. Caesarean section should only be performed for obstetric indications. (Conditional recommendation based on very low-quality evidence).

Pertaining to the following interventions, there is no evidence of any benefit. However, there may not have been sufficient studies to evaluate the effectiveness of some of these interventions:

  • Bed rest
  • Fluid bolus
  • Sedation, narcotics
  • Home uterine activity monitoring

Options for antenatal corticosteroids not shown in the algorithms above that can be used based on availability include:

  • Dexamethasone 6 mg BD for 2 days (total 24 mg)

Other options for tocolysis not shown in the algorithms above that can be used based on availability include:

  • Magnesium sulphate which has the added advantage of neuroprotection. it is given at a dose of 4 g IV over 15 to 20 minutes then 1 g/hour for 24 hours
  • Beta blockers: Ritodrine 0.15 mg IV bolus or Terbutaline subcutaneously (0.25 mg every 20 minutes for max 3 doses (or 0.25 IV bolus)
  • Nitroglycerine 100-200 mcg intravenously
  • Atosiban (oxytocin receptor blocker) loading dose of IV 6.75 mg in 0.9 ml over one minute plus 18 mg per hour for 3 hours. The maintenance dose is IV 6 mg per hour will be given for up to 45 hours, or until contractions have stopped.
  • Indomethacin orally 50-100 mg loading dose then 25-50 mg every 4 hours for 48 hours (Indomethacin, an NSAID, should not be used after 32 weeks gestation as it can cause premature closure of the ductus arteriosus).

PRETERM BIRTH

Definition

This is the birth that occurs between 24- and 37-weeks' gestation. In two-thirds of cases it occurs following spontaneous onset of labour. Only a minority of women who present with preterm contractions known as threatened premature labour (TPTL) progress to actual labour and delivery.

Spontaneous preterm labor, a syndrome caused by multiple pathologic processes, contributes to about 70% of preterm deliveries.

The risk of preterm deliveries increases with low social-economic status. Countries with the highest rates of preterm deliveries also report that these deliveries occur in persons of lower socioeconomic status.

Preterm birth is the leading cause of mortality in children under the age of 5 with the incidence increasing in babies of lower gestational ages.

Epidemiology

Yearly, an estimated 15 million babies are born preterm (before 37 completed weeks) with 60% of this happening in Africa and South Asia.

The rate of preterm births in countries analyzed in the WHO Global Action report ranged between 5 - 18% with Kenya having an estimated range of between 10 - 15%.

A study at the Kenyatta National Referral Hospital in Kenya in 2018 put the prevalence at the institution at 18.3%.

Recommendations on preterm births/deliveries

  • Deferring delivery to the 39th week is not recommended if there is a medical or obstetric indication for earlier delivery.
  • Recommendations for the timing of delivery for a number of specific conditions (Table 1)
  • In the case of an anticipated late-preterm delivery a single course of antenatal betamethasone is recommended within 7 days of delivery in select women who have not received a previous course of antenatal corticosteroids.
  • However, a medically indicated late-preterm delivery should not be delayed for the administration of antenatal corticosteroids
  • The decisions regarding delivery timing are complex and must take into account relative maternal and new-born risks, practice environment, and patient preferences.
  • Secondly, late-preterm or early-term deliveries may be required for maternal benefit or new-born benefit, or both.
  • In these cases, health care providers will need to weigh competing risks and benefits for the woman and her fetus and the support at the level of health facility adequate or referral mechanisms provided prior to delivery decision.

Mandatory Responsibility for health Care Providers

  • Prepare the mother and partner for the outcome of the pregnancy

Classification and etiology of preterm labour

Preterm birth can be termed as a syndrome with a variety of causes which can be classified into two broad subtypes:

  • Spontaneous preterm birth - spontaneous onset of labor or following pre-labor premature rupture of membranes (PPROM)
  • Provider-initiated preterm birth - defined as induction of labor or elective caesarian birth before 37 completed weeks of gestation for maternal or fetal indications that could be urgent or discretionary or not related to any medical conditions

Babies born preterm are further categorized based on their gestational age as follows:

  • Extremely preterm (less than 28 weeks)
  • Very preterm (28 to 32 weeks)
  • Moderate to late preterm (32 to 37 weeks)

Other than the gestational age, babies can also be categorized based on their birth weight. For preterm births, birth weight is a big determinant on the kind and quality of care needed to ensure survival. This categorization is as follows:

  • Extreme Low Birth Weight (< 1,000 g)
  • Very Low Birth Weight (< 1,500 g)
  • Low Birth Weight (< 2,500 g)
  • Normal Birth Weight (< 4,000 g)
  • High Birth Weight (> 4,000 g)

Complications associated with preterm birth

Neonatal risks

  • Feeding difficulties in babies less than 34 weeks of gestation since the coordinated suck and swallow process only starts at 34weeks gestation.
  • Severe infections are more common with increased chances of neonatal sepsis.
  • Respiratory Distress Syndrome due to lung immaturity and lack of surfactant in the alveoli, resulting in collapsing lungs that take extra pressure to inflate.
  • Jaundice is more common in premature babies since the immature liver cannot easily metabolize bilirubin.
  • Brain injury in preterm babies is most commonly intra-ventricular hemorrhage.
  • Necrotizing enterocolitis. Formula feeding increases the risk ten-fold compared to babies who are fed breast milk alone
  • Retinopathy of prematurity due to abnormal proliferation of the blood vessels around the retina of the eye.
  • Anemia of prematurity

Maternal risks

  • Risk of preterm deliveries in subsequent pregnancies
  • Mental distress
  • Increased hospital stays

Preterm Birth in Multiple Gestation

Twin pregnancies are associated with a three-fold greater perinatal mortality than singleton pregnancies. Prematurity is a main contributor, with 50% of twin pregnancies delivering before 37 weeks and 10% delivering before 32 weeks of gestation. The etiology of preterm delivery in twin pregnancies is likely multifactorial and different from that of singletons.

Cervical cerclage reduces preterm birth rates in singletons but has mixed results in twins with some studies showing harm.

The use of progesterone to prevent preterm birth in singletons has conflicting results and has not been proven to prevent preterm birth in twins.

Studies continue to determine whether the cervical pessary is effective in preventing preterm birth in multiple pregnancies.

There is a paucity of data available on the prevention of preterm birth in triplets/higher order multiples but similar principles to twin pregnancy apply

Care of the premature baby

Care of the premature baby entails the following:

  • Essential and extra newborn care, especially feeding support
  • Neonatal resuscitation
  • Kangaroo Mother Care
  • Chlorhexidine cord care
  • Management of premature babies with complications, especially respiratory distress syndrome and infection
  • Comprehensive neonatal intensive care, where capacity allows

Kangaroo Mother Care (KMC)

Kangaroo mother care is defined as care of preterm infants or infants weighing less than 2000 g carried skin-to-skin with the mother. It aims to promote the health and wellbeing of infants born preterm or at term.

This method of care was developed in Colombia as an alternative method of care in resource poor settings for preterm infants who had no underlying medical condition and only needed to feed and grow.

Kangaroo mother care can either be:

  • Continuous: This is the recommended modality of care, in which the baby is maintained on skin to skin contact with the mother full time except during times of feeding and cleaning.
  • Intermittent care: is recommended rather than conventional care in instances where continuous kangaroo care is not possible.

When to start kangaroo care:

At birth, preterm babies and those small for gestational age are expected to have certain complications. These complications should be managed as per the institutions/ national guidelines and once clinically stable then kangaroo mother care can be initiated. However, in instances where no complications are present, kangaroo mother care can be initiated after birth.

Birth weight ranges are given as guides for initiation as follows:

  • Babies weighing 1800 g or more at birth (gestational age 30-34 weeks or more) may have some prematurity-related problems, such as respiratory distress syndrome (RDS). This may raise serious concerns for a minority of those infants, who will require care in special units. In most cases, however, KMC can start soon after birth.
  • Babies with birth weight between 1200 and 1799g (gestational age 28-32 weeks), prematurity related complications are expected and therefore initial management in specialized units is needed and once the baby is stable, then kangaroo care can be initiated, usually after a week or more depending on the severity of the complications.
  • Babies weighing less than 1200 g (gestational age below 30 weeks) incur frequent and severe problems due to preterm birth: mortality is very high and only a small proportion survives prematurity-related problems. These babies benefit most from transfer before birth to an institution with neonatal intensive care facilities. It may take weeks before their condition allows initiation of KMC.

When to wean the baby off kangaroo care

KMC tends to be used until the baby reaches term (gestational age around 40 weeks) or 2500 g. Around that time the baby also outgrows the need for KMC. Certain subtle signs from the baby indicate discomfort associated with skin to skin contact. At this point, it is recommended that the mother weans the baby off KMC however; she can still use the method in instances of extreme cold temperatures.

Factors to consider when counselling the mother on KMC

  • Willingness: the mother must be willing to provide KMC;
  • Full-time availability to provide care: other family members can offer intermittent skin-to-skin contact but they cannot breastfeed;
  • General health: if the mother suffered complications during pregnancy or delivery or is otherwise ill, she should recover before initiating KMC;
  • Adequate support systems

Advantages of kangaroo mother care.

  • Early, continuous and prolonged skin-to-skin contact between the mother and the baby;
  • Promotes exclusive breastfeeding
  • Can be carried out at home
  • Short hospital stays since babies pick up quickly
  • It is a gentle, effective method that avoids the agitation routinely experienced in a busy ward with preterm infants.
  • Can be done by any member of the family including the father. This is especially useful to assist the mother in taking breaks to rest.
