Definitions

Communication is the act of giving, receiving, and sharing information through, talking, writing, listening, reading.

Networking is the exchange of information and ideas among people with a common profession or special interest, usually in an informal social setting. Networking often begins with a single point of common ground.

Types of Communication

Communication can be categorized into three basic types:

  • Verbal communication, in which you listen to a person to understand their meaning;
  • Written communication, in which you read their meaning; and
  • Nonverbal communication, in which you observe a person and infer meaning.

Benefits of Effective Communication in Healthcare

Effective communication can:

  • Enhance the patient experience;
  • Reduce complaints among the healthcare workers.
  • Increase self-confidence to the healthcare workers,
  • Help in career prospects and job satisfaction
  • Help to Reduce stress in the workplace.

üé§ Public Speaking

Public speaking: is the process of speaking to a few or many people with the purpose of informing, motivating, persuading, educating or entertaining the listeners.

üí°

The Five Elements

The five elements of public speaking are:

  1. who
  2. is saying what
  3. to whom
  4. using what medium
  5. with what effects?

Public speaking is a skill that is best mastered through continuous practice. A talkative person may not necessarily be an eloquent speaker. To give a great speech, you must understand the type of speech you are expected to give, and prepare along those lines. An effective public speaker understands the needs of the audience and is flexible enough to adopt changes that may arise even while delivering the speech.

Benefits to Speaking in Public

  • improves confidence
  • better research skills
  • stronger deductive skills
  • ability to advocate for causes

Factors that Influence Effective Communication

1. Voice Control

Your voice is the most basic communication tool you possess. Learning to use it properly is key for improving your public speaking.

Diaphragmatic breathing is a useful voice control technique. It can give your voice greater power and clarity and prevent the shortness of breath that anxiety causes.

2. Body Language

Your body language is the combination of your gestures, facial expressions, and movements. It‚Äôs an integral part of how we communicate. If your body language contrasts with what you want to transmit or seems incoherent, your speech will be confusing.

3. Delivery

Delivery is the way you speak. Good delivery is essential for your audience to understand your speech.

  • Speak at the speed of a normal conversation.
  • Pause regularly.
  • Don't mumble or eat your words ‚Äî articulate clearly.
  • Avoid making noises such as ‚Äúahh‚Äù or ‚Äúumm‚Äù between words.

4. Audience Relations

If you want your audience to listen to you, you need to engage them from the beginning.

  • Smile and greet the audience. Thank them for being there.
  • Look for people in the audience who seem actively engaged.
  • Make eye contact with as many people as possible.

üìù Report Writing

A report is a short, sharp, concise document which is written for a particular purpose and audience. It is purely based on observation and analysis. A report gives an explanation of any circumstance.

It brings significant and reliable information to the limelight of top management in an organization. Hence, on the basis of such information, the management can make strong decisions. Reports are required for judging the performances of various departments in an organization.

Report Writing Steps:

  1. Determine the objective of the report, i.e., identify the problem.
  2. Collect the required material (facts) for the report.
  3. Study and examine the facts gathered.
  4. Plan the facts for the report.
  5. Prepare an outline for the report, i.e., draft the report.
  6. Edit the drafted report.
  7. Distribute the draft report to the advisory team and ask for feedback and recommendations.
‚ú®

Essentials of Effective Report Writing

  • Know your objective, i.e., be focused.
  • Analyse the niche audience.
  • Decide the length of report.
  • Disclose correct and true information.
  • Discuss all sides of the problem reasonably and impartially.
  • Concentrate on the report structure and matter.
  • The report should be neatly presented and carefully documented.
  • Highlight and recap the main message.
  • Encourage feedback.
  • Use graphs, pie-charts, etc to show numerical data.
  • Attempt to generate reader‚Äôs interest (paragraphs, headings, bullets).

Report Structure

The recommended structure for reports is generally in this order:

  1. Title Page: Title, author name, date, details of confidentiality.
  2. Contents Page: List of sections/chapters with page numbers.
  3. Executive summary
  4. Terms of reference
  5. Background/introduction
  6. Information-gathering activities
  7. Findings
  8. Conclusions
  9. Recommendations
  10. References
  11. Appendices

ü§ù Negotiation

Negotiation is a type of discussion used to settle conflicts and reach agreements between two or more sides. Negotiation is a process of ‚Äúgive and take‚Äù resulting in a compromise where each side makes a concession for the benefit of everyone involved.

12 Important Negotiation Skills

Communication

Identifying nonverbal cues and verbal skills to express yourself in an engaging way. Skilled negotiators can change their communication styles to meet the listener‚Äôs needs.

Active Listening

The ability to engage and later recall specific details without needing information repeated. Crucial for understanding another‚Äôs opinion.

Emotional Intelligence

The ability to control your own emotions and recognize others' feelings. Allows you to remain calm and focused on core issues.

Expectation Management

Maintaining a balance between being a firm negotiator and a collaborative one. Adjusting expectations when needed.

Patience

Negotiations often take time. Rather than seeking a quick conclusion, negotiators often practice patience to properly assess a situation.

Adaptability

The ability to adapt quickly and determine a new plan if the situation changes from one day to the next.

Persuasion

The ability to influence others to support your point of view. Includes being assertive when necessary.

Planning

Determining what you want, your best possible outcome, and your least acceptable offer. Thinking ahead is crucial.

Integrity

Strong ethical and moral principles. Being thoughtful, respectful and honest allows the other side to trust what you say.

Rapport Building

Creating a friendly environment where both sides feel supported and understood. Helps ease tensions and promotes collaboration.

Problem-Solving

The ability to see the problem and find a unique solution. E.g., finding ways to lower a high price.

Decision Making

Acting decisively during a negotiation. Thinking through options carefully without overthinking.

Types of Negotiation

üî¥ Distributive Negotiations

Also called "distributive bargaining" or "win-lose". Both sides try to gain control of a limited amount of resources. One side‚Äôs gain equals the other side‚Äôs loss.

üü¢ Integrative Negotiations

Also referred to as a "win-win". Occurs when everyone benefits from the agreement. There is usually more than one issue to be negotiated so there are opportunities for tradeoffs.

Example: A client wants to pay $800, the company wants $1000. They might negotiate a $900 service. Both "win" $100.

How to Skillfully Prepare for a Negotiation

  1. Do your research: Evaluate all sides and consider their goals. Understand the limitations of the negotiator.
  2. Know your priorities: Determine what is most important and what you are willing to settle for in its place.
  3. Consider the opposition: Write down all potential oppositions and gather information to argue your case.
  4. Know when to walk away: Recognize when no further compromises can be made and be prepared to leave the deal.

üîó Intersectoral Collaboration (ISC)

Intersectoral collaboration for health is defined as ‚Äúactions undertaken by sectors outside the health sector, possibly, but not necessarily, in collaboration with the health sector, on health or health equity outcomes or on the determinants of health or health equity.‚Äù

It is cooperation among different social groups that enables them to solve common problems, e.g., a public health crisis.

Why engage in ISC?

  • Provides a collective approach to address social determinants of health.
  • Allows working across structural levels and attracting diverse participants.
  • Mobilizes social capital to address health equity.
  • Coordinates policies to limit structured inequality.

Where does ISC happen?

  • Small groups working across formal organizational boundaries.
  • Inter-agency meetings, community coalitions, coordinated care teams.
  • Can range from informal to formal, horizontal or vertical.

When is ISC needed?

ISC is effective to address service integration problems, duplications, gaps, and inconsistencies in service provision. It helps in the efficient use of limited resources and pooling of skills. It is especially helpful for coordinating service delivery for populations that are difficult to reach.

Who is part of ISC?

Participants need to "own" a common vision. Potential collaborators include:

  • Service providers in health and non-health sectors
  • Various levels of government
  • Private sector institutions
  • Members of the public (Community involvement is critical)

üìÖ Planning and Conducting Meetings

Running or chairing a meeting means more than just moving the group through the agenda. You are responsible for the well-being of the group and the members in it.

‚ñ∂ Phase 1: Planning the Meeting
  • Decide The Goal of the Meeting: No clear goal? A boring and unfocused meeting may result!
  • Decide Who Needs to Be There: Think before you send out those meeting notices!
  • Plan with Others: Get three or four interested folks together to hash out the agenda.
  • Good Agendas Count: List the amount of time planned for each item and send it out at least a week ahead.
‚ñ∂ Phase 2: Setting Up the Meeting
  • Start and End on Time: It's disrespectful to abuse members' time.
  • Sign Them In: Helps update membership lists and gives names for follow-up.
  • Make Everyone Comfortable: Meeting spaces should be convenient and the right size.
  • All Work and No Play Is No Good: Have informal time before and after for socializing.
  • A Regular Cycle: Have a regular meeting cycle so people can save the date.
‚ñ∂ Phase 3: Running the Meeting
  • Do Introductions: Introduce yourself and get everyone to introduce themselves.
  • Get Agreement on Agenda and Rules: Everyone needs to "buy in" to the agenda.
  • Keep The Discussion on Track: Pull people back in if they go off-agenda. Honor time limits.
  • Summarize What You Hear: Wrap-up each agenda item by summarizing conclusions.
  • Encourage Participation: Try to draw everyone in, not just the usual suspects.
  • Use The Power Of Your Position Wisely: Don't take sides, and be fair to everyone.
  • Develop New Leaders: Try rotating chairing responsibility.
‚ñ∂ Phase 4: Following Up
  • Gather Feedback From The Group: Find out what could be improved.
  • Make Follow-Up Calls: Serve as the glue that holds the group together.
  • Summarize The Meeting: Have a list of decisions made and follow-ups (Minutes).

Tips on Handling Difficult Members

  • Have the group decide: If someone refuses to stick to the agenda.
  • Use the agenda and ground rules: If someone verbally attacks others.
  • Be honest: Say what's going on if you feel undermined.
  • Use humor: If there is tension in the room.
  • Accept, deal, or defer: If someone expresses doubts or puts down suggestions.
  • Use body language: To quiet side conversations or re-focus attention.
  • Take a break: Confront disrupters outside the meeting room.
  • Confront in the room: Only if appropriate and the group will support you.
