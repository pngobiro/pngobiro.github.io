Definitions

Project management is the application of processes, methods, skills, knowledge and experience to achieve specific project objectives according to the project acceptance criteria within agreed parameters. Project management has final deliverables that are constrained to a finite timescale and budget.

Project planning is a discipline addressing how to complete a project in a certain timeframe, usually with defined stages and designated resources.

Importance of Planning

Planning is significant as it directs us where to go, provides direction, and decreases the danger of risk by making predictions.

Efficient Use of Resources

Provides information to make effective decisions about allocating resources (time, money, labor) to reach objectives. Maximizes productivity and prevents waste on projects with low success chance.

Establishing Organizational Goals

Setting aggressive but realistic goals challenges the organization. The process can be a wake-up call for complacent managers and allows analysis of variances between forecast and actual results.

Managing Risk and Uncertainty

Encourages development of "what-if" scenarios and contingency plans to deal with unforeseen events before financial consequences become severe.

Team Building and Cooperation

Promotes a spirit of cooperation. Everyone understands their responsibilities and how they contribute to the whole. Reduces conflict when managers have input.

Creating Competitive Advantages

Helps get a realistic view of current strengths/weaknesses relative to competitors. Helps identify overlooked opportunities (e.g., emerging markets).

üìã Types of Plans

‚ñ∂ 1. Operational Planning

"How things need to happen." Describes the day-to-day running of the company.

  • Single use plans: For events/activities with a single occurrence (e.g., a marketing campaign).
  • Ongoing plans: Policies, rules, and procedures for accomplishing objectives.
‚ñ∂ 2. Strategic Planning

"Why things need to happen." High-level overview of the entire business. Foundational basis dictating long-term decisions.

  • Scope: Next 2 to 10 years.
  • Components: Vision, mission, values.
‚ñ∂ 3. Tactical Planning

"What is going to happen." Supports strategic planning. Breaks down strategic plans into actionable chunks.

  • Scope: Often less than one year.
  • Focus: Specific questions about what needs to happen to accomplish a strategic goal.
‚ñ∂ 4. Contingency Planning

Made when something unexpected happens or when something needs to be changed. Essential for moments when changes can't be foreseen in the complicated business world.

üîÑ Phases of Project Management

  1. Phase 1: Project Initiation

    Turning an abstract idea into a meaningful goal. Tasks include:

    • Determine need and create a Project Charter (goals, manager, budget, timeline).
    • Identify key stakeholders and create a stakeholder register.
  2. Phase 2: Project Planning

    Lays out the project‚Äôs roadmap. Can take almost half the project's timespan. Tasks include:

    • Identify technical requirements and develop detailed schedule.
    • Create communication plan and set goals/deliverables.
    • Define scope and develop Work Breakdown Structure (WBS).
    • Risk mitigation strategy.
    • Change management plan: Crucial to avoid scope creep and bottlenecks.
  3. Phase 3: Project Execution

    Where the team does the actual work. Manager's job is to establish efficient workflows, monitor progress, and maintain effective collaboration between stakeholders.
  4. Phase 4: Project Monitoring and Controlling

    Runs simultaneously with execution. Ensures objectives are met.

    • Establish Critical Success Factors (CSF) and Key Performance Indicators (KPI).
    • Quantitatively track effort and cost to ensure project remains within budget.
  5. Phase 5: Project Closing

    Indicates the end of the project after final delivery.

    • Terminate contracts and complete paperwork.
    • Hold a reflection meeting to contemplate successes and failures for future improvement.

Community development is a process where community members come together to take collective action and generate solutions to common problems.

Types of Community Projects

1. Income Generating Activities (IGAs)

Small businesses managed by a group to increase household income through livelihood diversification. Focuses on creating opportunities to productively use locally-available resources for self-reliance.

üìä

Key Factors for IGAs

Quantity sold & Price: For non-perishables (grains, blocks), demand is rarely limiting. For perishables (vegetables), seasonality is critical. For specialized products, market demand and pricing strategies are complex.

2. Non-Income Generating Projects

Projects where there is no market (e.g., watershed protection, new primary school). Demand is estimated by identifying direct beneficiaries (users) and indirect beneficiaries (entire population of area served).

Core Functions of Community Health Nursing Practices

  • Identification of community culture and resources.
  • Evaluate community health conditions, risks, and problems to identify demands.
  • Plan and implementation of comprehensive community health interventions and programs.
  • Develop health policy at the local level to drive collaborative endeavors.

Community Development Approaches

Multidisciplinary Approach

Draws from multiple disciplines to redefine problems outside normal boundaries. Guided by holism rather than reductionism. Often faces barriers from specialists protecting their expertise boundary.

Directive Approach

Agency/worker decides, plans, and provides for people. "Initiative and final say remain with them." Effective for short-term needs but less effective for long-term potential realization.

Non-Directive Approach

Worker does not decide for people. Tries to get them to decide for themselves what their needs are and how to act. Stimulates self-determination and self-help.
