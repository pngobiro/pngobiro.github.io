üè• Healthcare Financing

Healthcare financing refers to the ‚Äúfunction of a health system concerned with the mobilization, accumulation and allocation of money to cover the health needs of the people, individually and collectively, in the health system."

üéØ

Purpose

The purpose of health financing is to make funding available, as well as to set the right financial incentives to providers, to ensure that all individuals have access to effective public health and personal health care.

The financing system is often divided conceptually into three inter-related functions:

  1. Revenue collection
  2. Fund pooling
  3. Purchasing/provision of services

According to the WHO framework, a well-functioning health system ensures equitable access to essential medical products, vaccines and technologies of assured quality, safety, efficacy and cost effectiveness.

üí∏ Sources of Healthcare Financing

üèõÔ∏è Public Sources

Tax-based funding: Common in high income countries. Ensures comprehensive cover and equity through progressive tax mechanisms.

Mandatory Health Insurance: Legal requirement for groups to join (e.g., NHIF in Kenya). Membership is mandatory for formal sector workers and voluntary for informal sector.

ü§ù Private Sources

Out of pocket spending: Can result into catastrophic health spending, pushing households into poverty.

Private health insurance: Individuals pay premiums. Often results in a stratified healthcare system.

Donor funding: Grants, loans, technical assistance, free drugs (Common in developing countries).

Other Mechanisms

  • User fees: Charges made to the patient for treatment received (consultation, drugs, tests). Commonly charged for curative services.
  • Prepayment (Insurance): Regular payment of a predetermined sum regardless of health status. Costs are shared between sick and healthy people.
  • Direct transfers and grants: Provision of resources (money or supplies) which does not have to be repaid.

üìä Budgeting

Budget: A written projection of a particular department‚Äôs financial performance, a specific project, a business unit, or an organization for the period under consideration.

Budgeting: The activity of preparing a budget.

Importance of Budgeting

  • Estimates revenue, plans expenditure and restricts unauthorized spending.
  • Ensures money allocation supports strategic objectives.
  • Helps everyone understand business priorities.
  • Provides opportunities to involve staff in the vision.
  • Highlights strengths and weaknesses when reviewing actuals vs budget.

Types of Budgets

Master Budget

Sum total of the company‚Äôs budget including allocation of funds to different activities. Includes sales, working capital, operating expenses, etc.

Operating Budget

Involves costs related to operational activities (production, overhead, labour, administrative costs) and income flow from sales.

Financial Budget

Ensures right types of funds are available (short/medium/long term). Manages outflows with inflows. Vital for mergers and acquisitions.

Cash Flow Budget

Manages cash inflow/outflow. Determines if accounts payable/receivable are dealt with timely and predicts cash shortages.

Static Budget

Remains the same regardless of changes in factors affecting the budget (e.g., sales volume). Presumes input/output values of the future.

Budgeting Methods / Techniques

‚ñ∂ 1. Traditional Budgeting

Considers last year‚Äôs budget as the base. Adjustments are made based on inflation, demand, etc. Only items over and above the last year‚Äôs budget need justification. Rigid but easy to prepare.

‚ñ∂ 2. Incremental Budgeting

Prepares current budget by making changes (additions/reductions) to the past year‚Äôs budget. Gives no priority to vital activities. Quick and easy.

‚ñ∂ 3. Zero-Based Budgeting (ZBB)

Preparation begins from scratch (zero base). Every expense must be justified. Resources allocated based on priority of activities. Time-consuming but gives accurate results.

‚ñ∂ 4. Activity-Based Budgeting

Top-down approach. Output/targets are decided first, then cost drivers are analyzed to determine resources needed. Aligns business activities with goals. Increases efficiency.

üîí Financial Procedures & Controls

Financial procedures are collection of statements describing how to handle funds (e.g., receiving checks, documenting cash, authorizing signatures).

Financial controls are procedures/policies to monitor and control direction, allocation, and usage of financial resources.

Importance of Financial Controls

  • Cash flow maintenance: Monitors inflows/outflows for efficient operations.
  • Resource management: Crucial for managing financial resources which enable all other resources.
  • Operational efficiency: Ensures smooth functioning of departments.
  • Profitability: Increases productivity which relates to profitability.
  • Fraud prevention: Preventative measure against theft and employee fraud.

Examples of Financial Controls

Implementation
  • Employing qualified staff
  • Direct communication chain
  • Periodic training
  • Financial analysis
  • Segregation of duties
Cash Inflows
  • Credit reporting policy
  • Periodic reconciliation
  • Review policy with customers
  • Secure backups of data
Cash Outflows
  • Monitoring subscription payments
  • Vendor database maintenance
  • Detailed expense reports
  • Receipt verifications

üå± Resource Mobilization

Resource mobilization is the process by which an organization acquires and manages the financial, human and logistical resources it needs to fulfill its mission.

Why is it important?

  • Ensures continuation of service provision.
  • Supports organizational sustainability.
  • Allows for improvement and scale-up of products/services.
  • Organizations must generate new business to stay in business.

Resource management is the practice of planning, scheduling, and allocating people, money, and technology to a project or program. It results in the right resources being available at the right time.

Advantages of Resource Management

  • Avoids unforeseen hiccups.
  • Prevents burnout (avoids overallocation).
  • Provides a safety net (justifies effort).
  • Builds transparency.
  • Measures efficiency (ROI).
  • Minimizes costs (right mix of resources).
  • Improves billable utilization.

üèõÔ∏è Governance Roles in Finance

Role of Parliament

In Kenya, Parliament has three critical functions regarding Public Finance:

  1. Revenue mobilization: Imposition of taxes and borrowing.
  2. Allocation of resources.
  3. Supervisory function: Based on audits and reports.

Only Parliament can impose taxes and authorize public borrowing. It also ensures proper collection of taxes and spending of public funds.

Office of the Auditor General

The Auditor-General audits and reports on accounts of national/county governments, courts, commissions, parliament, political parties (publicly funded), and public debt. The report confirms if public money has been applied lawfully and effectively.

Health Care Financing Committee (HCF)

To make the voice of the private sector present in policy making. Terms of reference include engaging government on financing challenges, representing Kenya Health Financing in forums, and reviewing regulations.

The Treasury

Coordinates financial planning, budgeting, investment activities, and ensures compliance with accounting principles.

üßæ Financial Accounting Systems

Financial accounting involves recording, summarizing, and reporting financial transactions to ascertain financial results (profit/loss) and position.

Source Documents

Documents used as evidence for transactions:

Document Purpose
Cash Memo Records cash sales and purchases.
Invoice / Bill Records credit transactions (sales/purchases). Invoice is for seller, Bill is for purchaser.
Receipt Evidence of making payment.
Pay in Slip Evidence of depositing money into a bank account.
Cheque Unconditional order to a banker to pay a sum of money.
Debit Note Shows business has raised debit against a party (e.g., for returned defective goods).
Credit Note Shows business has given credit to a party (e.g., for goods received back).
Vouchers Prepared to record transactions in books of accounts based on source documents.
