Key Terminology

Health Information Management (HIM) is the collection, analysis, storage, and protection of patient health information and medical records. It is a multidisciplinary field composed of technology, research, and health care experts.

Health Information System (HIS) refers to a system designed to include systems that collect, store, manage and transmit a patient's electronic medical record (EMR), a hospital's operational management or a system supporting healthcare policy decisions.

Health Information Technology (HIT) involves the development of health information systems.

Health information systems also include those systems that handle data related to the activities of providers and health organizations. As an integrated effort, these may be leveraged to improve patient outcomes, inform research, and influence policy-making and decision-making.

üñ•Ô∏è Examples of Health Information Systems

Electronic Medical Record (EMR) & Electronic Health Record (EHR)

The electronic medical record replaces the paper version of a patient‚Äôs medical history. The electronic health record includes more health data, test results, and treatments. It also is designed to share data with other electronic health records so other healthcare providers can access a patient‚Äôs healthcare data.

Practice Management Software

Helps healthcare providers manage daily operations such as scheduling and billing. Healthcare providers, from small practices to hospitals, use practice management systems to automate many of the administrative tasks.

Master Patient Index (MPI)

Connects separate patient records across databases. The index has a record for each patient that is registered at a healthcare organization and indexes all other records for that patient. MPIs are used to reduce duplicate patient records and inaccurate patient information that can lead to claim denials.

Patient Portals

Allow patients to access their personal health data such as appointment information, medications and lab results over an internet connection. Some patient portals allow active communication with their physicians, prescription refill requests, and the ability to schedule appointments.

Remote Patient Monitoring (RPM)

Also known as telehealth, allows medical sensors to send patient data to healthcare professionals. It frequently monitors blood glucose levels and blood pressure for patients with chronic conditions.

Clinical Decision Support (CDS)

Systems analyse data from various clinical and administrative systems to help healthcare providers make clinical decisions. The data can help prepare diagnoses or predict medical events such as drug interactions.

üìÇ Sources of Health Data

  • Surveys: An important means of collecting health and social science information from a sample of people in a standardized way. Allows researchers to collect empirical data in a relatively short period of time.
  • Medical Records: Used to track events and transactions between patients and health care providers. Offer information on diagnoses, procedures, lab tests, and other services. They are usually accurate and detailed.
  • Claims Data: Also known as administrative data. Collect information on millions of doctors' appointments, bills, insurance information, and other patient-provider communications.
  • Vital Records: Collected by the National Vital Statistics System. Include births, deaths, marriages, divorces, and fetal deaths. Useful for detailed information on rare disorders that end in death.
  • Surveillance: The ongoing systematic collection, analysis, and interpretation of data, closely integrated with the timely dissemination of these data. Usually associated with the study of infectious diseases.

üìù Methods of Data Collection

‚ñ∂ 1. Observation Method

Used when the study relates to behavioural science. This method is planned systematically. It is subject to many controls and checks. Types include:

  • Structured and unstructured observation
  • Controlled and uncontrolled observation
  • Participant, non-participant and disguised observation
‚ñ∂ 2. Interview Method

The method of collecting data in terms of oral or verbal responses. It is achieved in two ways:

  • Personal Interview: Face to face questioning. Can be structured or unstructured.
  • Telephonic Interview: Interviewer obtains information by contacting people on the telephone.
‚ñ∂ 3. Questionnaire Method

A set of questions are mailed to the respondent. A good survey should be:

  • Short and simple
  • Should follow a logical sequence
  • Provide adequate space for answers
  • Avoid technical terms
  • Should have good physical appearance
‚ñ∂ 4. Schedules

Similar to the questionnaire method but enumerations are specially appointed for the purpose of filling the schedules. Enumerators explain the aims and objects of the investigation and may remove misunderstandings.

üíæ Data Storage

Data storage refers to magnetic, optical or mechanical media that records and preserves digital information for ongoing or future operations.

üè¢ Onsite data storage

Healthcare facilities overwhelmingly prefer on-premises data storage for greater control over internal data. Data is accessed from a secure, native data centre.

‚òÅÔ∏è Public cloud data storage

Simplifies delivering patient data, making it quick and easy. Cloud storage is scalable, requiring less capital investment for implementation and maintenance.

üîÑ Hybrid cloud data storage

üìâ Data Analysis

Data analysis is defined as a process of cleaning, transforming, and modelling data to discover useful information for business decision-making. The purpose is to extract useful information and take decisions based upon the analysis.

Types of Data Analysis Methods

  • Cluster analysis: Grouping a set of data elements so that said elements are more similar to each other than to those in other groups. Used to find hidden patterns.
  • Cohort analysis: Uses historical data to examine and compare a determined segment of users' behaviour. Useful in marketing to understand campaign impact.
  • Regression analysis: Uses historical data to understand how a dependent variable's value is affected when independent variables change. Helps anticipate possible outcomes.
  • Neural networks: Forms the basis for intelligent algorithms of machine learning. Attempts to understand how the human brain would process insights and predict values.
  • Factor analysis: Also called "dimension reduction". Used to describe variability among observed, correlated variables in terms of a lower number of unobserved variables called factors.
  • Data mining: Engineering metrics and insights for additional value. Uses exploratory statistical evaluation to identify dependencies, relations, patterns, and trends.
  • Text analysis: Also known as text mining. Taking large sets of textual data and arranging it to make it easier to manage.

üõ†Ô∏è Data Analysis Techniques

  1. Collaborate your needs: Sit down with key stakeholders to decide on primary goals and gain understanding of insights needed.
  2. Establish your questions: Consider which questions need answering to achieve your mission.
  3. Data democratization: Connect data from various sources efficiently so anyone can access it.
  4. Clean your data: Fundamental before visualizing. Eliminate duplicates, add missing codes, fix empty fields.
  5. Set your KPIs: Set Key Performance Indicators to track, measure, and shape progress.
  6. Omit useless data: Trim informational fat. Eliminate stats/metrics that don't align with business goals.
  7. Build a data management roadmap: Help store, manage, and handle data internally for sustainability.
  8. Integrate technology: Integrate the right decision support software and technology.
  9. Answer your questions: Start answering your most burning business questions using the data.
  10. Visualize your data: Tell a story with metrics using online data visualization.
  11. Interpretation of data: Give meaning to the analytical information and drive a concise conclusion.
  12. Build a narrative: Tell a story with a clear beginning, middle, and end to make analysis accessible.
  13. Consider autonomous technology: Use AI and machine learning for advancement.
  14. Share the load: Use tools that present metrics in a digestible format for everyone in the organization.
  15. Data analysis tools: Use tools and software that ensure the best results.
