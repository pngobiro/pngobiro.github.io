üëî Human Resource Management (HRM)

Human resource management (HRM) is the process of employing people, training them, compensating them, developing policies relating to them, and developing strategies to retain them. As a field, HRM has undergone many changes over the last twenty years, giving it an even more important role in today's organizations.

The Role of HRM

Most experts agree on the following roles that HRM plays in organizations:

Staffing

Staffing involves the entire hiring process from posting a job to negotiating a salary package. You need people to perform tasks and get work done in the organization.

Recruitment

This involves finding people to fill the open positions. It is a positive process of posting job openings and attracting prospective employees.

Selection

Involves eliminating the unsuitable candidates through the process of tests or interviews and identifying the suitable ones, which are the best fit for the positions.

Development of Policies

HRM develops verbiage surrounding policies to ensure fairness and continuity (e.g., discipline process, vacation time, dress code, ethics).

Compensation and Benefits

Determining fair compensation (pay, health benefits, retirement plans, bonuses) that meets industry standards to entice people to work for the organization.

Retention

Keeping and motivating employees to stay. Factors include compensation, job satisfaction, management, and workplace environment.

Training and Development

Ensuring employees are trained to do the job and continue to grow (job skills, communication, team-building, legal training).

Performance Management

Appraising the performance of employees and enhancing it through encouragement and fair rewards.

üîç Recruitment Process

Recruitment is a core function of the Human Resource department. The recruitment lifecycle consists of seven interrelated steps:

  1. Identifying the Hiring Needs

    Analyze job specifications including knowledge, skills, and experience needed. Determine gaps in the current team, track input vs. output, and be mindful of exits.
  2. Preparing the Job Description

    Determine duties and responsibilities. A JD must include Title, Duties & Responsibilities, Qualification & Skills, Location, and Compensation/Perks.
  3. Talent Search

    Identifying appropriate talent and motivating them to apply via internal advertisements, social networking, job boards, or industry publications.
  4. Screening and Shortlisting

    Steps to effectively screen:

    1. Screen based on minimum qualifications.
    2. Sort resumes with preferred credentials.
    3. Shortlist candidates with both qualifications and credentials.
    4. Flag concerns for clarification during interviews.
  5. Interviewing

    Can involve telephonic/video screening followed by face-to-face interviews. This is a two-way process where the candidate also assesses the company.
  6. Evaluation and Offer of Employment

    Final evaluation and making the job offer to the selected candidate.
  7. Introduction and Induction

    Once the offer is accepted, pre-employment screening is conducted, followed by introduction to the organization and the induction process (welcome kit, contract signing).

üìã Orientation & Job Descriptions

Orientation of Employee

Orientation is the planned introduction of new employees to their jobs, coworkers, and the organization. Major objectives are to gain commitment, reduce anxiety, help understand expectations, and convey what to expect.

üéØ

Purposes of Orientation

  • Reduce Startup Costs: Helps employee get "up to speed" quickly.
  • Reduce Anxiety: Provides guidelines for behavior and conduct.
  • Reduce Employee Turnover: Shows the organization values the employee.
  • Save Time for Supervisor & Co-workers: Less time spent teaching the employee.
  • Develop Realistic Job Expectations: Promotes positive attitudes and job satisfaction.
  • Productivity Enhancement: Helps reach full productivity levels sooner.
  • Organizational Overview: Reviews history, structure, and key information.

Job Descriptions

Job description refers to the technique used in ranking jobs in an organization based on responsibilities and duties. It results in the job being assigned a specific range of payment.

Why Job Description refuses to go out of fashion:

  • Reduces Employee Turnover: Benchmarking jobs against the labor market ensures competitive pay.
  • Minimizes Conflicts: Results in fairness in payment rates, lessening grievances over relative wages.
  • Simplifies Pay Structures: Helps come up with simple, easy-to-understand pay structures for diverse classes of employees.
  • Fits New Jobs: Makes it simpler to fit new vacancies into previously set pay structures.

üìà Performance Appraisal

Performance Appraisal is a structural and systematic procedure to measure, assess and influence the attributes, behavior and results related to work, with the aim of discovering how productive an employee is, and if their future performance could be improved.

‚úÖ Advantages

  • Improve organization‚Äôs general performance.
  • Increase employee motivation.
  • Strengthen commitment to the company.
  • Helps spot internal talent.
  • Improves communication.
  • Helps design career paths.
  • Enables development of compensation policies.
  • Facilitates decisions about promotions/dismissals.

‚ùå Disadvantages

  • Biases may occur, rendering objectives useless.

üí° Motivation and Work Climate

Counselling: A process where an individual meets with a professional to talk about issues/problems.

Coaching: A form of development supporting a learner in achieving a specific personal or professional goal.

Motivation: The process that initiates, guides, and maintains goal-oriented behaviours.

Motivation Theories

‚ñ∂ 1. Maslow's Hierarchy of Need Theory

Classifies human needs into a hierarchy. Once a need is satisfied, it no longer motivates.

  1. Physiological Needs: Basic survival (food, shelter).
  2. Safety Needs: Economic security, protection from danger.
  3. Social Needs: Belongingness, love, interaction.
  4. Esteem Needs: Self-respect, status, recognition.
  5. Self-Actualization Needs: Fulfillment of potential.
‚ñ∂ 2. Herzberg‚Äôs Motivation Hygiene Theory

Proposed that the opposite of satisfaction is not dissatisfaction. Removal of dissatisfying characteristics (hygiene factors) does not necessarily make the job satisfying.

‚ñ∂ 3. McClelland‚Äôs Need Theory

Focuses on three acquired needs: Achievement, Power, and Affiliation. Needs are learned from culture and experience.

‚ñ∂ 4. McGregor's Participation Theory (X & Y)
  • Theory X (Negative): Assumes people are lazy, lack ambition, self-centered, and need strict supervision/coercion.
  • Theory Y (Positive): Assumes people are not passive, want responsibility, want the organization to succeed, and are capable of self-direction.
‚ñ∂ 5. Urwick's Theory Z

Based on four postulates: Strong Bond between Organization and Employees, Employee Participation, No Formal Organization Structure, and Human Resource Development.

‚ñ∂ 6. Argyris's Theory

Proposes that individuals develop from immaturity to maturity. Management practices should shift to allow this growth.

Immaturity Maturity
PassivityActivity
DependenceIndependence
Capable of few behaviorsCapable of many behaviors
Shallow interestDeep interest
Short-term perspectiveLong-term perspective
Subordinate positionSuperordinate position
Lack of self-awarenessSelf-awareness and control
‚ñ∂ 7. Vroom's Expectancy Theory

People are motivated when they believe effort leads to performance and performance leads to rewards.

$$Motivation = Valence \times Expectancy \times Instrumentality$$
  • Valence: Value placed on reward.
  • Expectancy: Effort-performance relationship.
  • Instrumentality: Performance-reward relationship.
‚ñ∂ 8. Porter and Lawler‚Äôs Expectancy Theory

Improvement on Vroom's theory. Posits that motivation does not equal satisfaction or performance. Effort is mediated by abilities, traits, and role perceptions. Performance leads to satisfaction (based on rewards).

‚öîÔ∏è Conflict Resolution

Conflict is a clash between individuals arising out of differences in thought process, attitudes, understanding, interests, etc.

Types of Conflict

  • Interpersonal: Between two individuals.
  • Intrapersonal: Within an individual (psychological).
  • Intragroup: Among individuals within a team.
  • Intergroup: Between different teams within an organization.

Conflict Management Styles

Accommodating

Setting aside own concerns to maintain peace (Self-sacrifice).

Avoiding

Withdrawal from conflict. Ignoring the problem.

Collaborating

Cooperating to find a creative solution that satisfies all needs.

Competing

Assertive, aggressive pursuit of own goal at expense of others.

Compromising

Mutual give-and-take. Both sides sacrifice something to reach agreement.

Conflict Resolution Skills

  • Active listening
  • Bias for action
  • Perspective taking
  • Facilitation
  • Mediation
  • Problem solving
  • Responsibility

‚öñÔ∏è Employee Discipline & Change Management

Employee Discipline

Employee discipline is defined as regulations imposed to correct or prevent detrimental behaviors. Importance includes:

  • Promotes Appropriate Behavior
  • Efficiency
  • Peaceful Environment
  • Fairness
  • Ensures Safety

Reasons Why People Resist Change

Leaders must understand universal sources of resistance:

  1. Loss of control: Change interferes with autonomy.
  2. Excess uncertainty: "Better the devil you know".
  3. Surprise, surprise!: Sudden decisions are resisted.
  4. Everything seems different: Too many differences are distracting.
  5. Loss of face: Defensive about past directions.
  6. Concerns about competence: Fear of obsolete skills.
  7. More work: Change requires effort.
  8. Ripple effects: Disrupts other departments/people.
  9. Past resentments: Old wounds reopen.
  10. Real threat: Job loss, investment loss.

How to Manage Change

  • Be persistent.
  • Be flexible and creative.
  • Think outside the box.
  • Accept uncertainty and be optimistic.

‚ö†Ô∏è Occupational Hazards & CPD

Occupational Hazards

Risks of illnesses or accidents in the workplace. Types include:

Biological hazards

Substances like toxins, viruses, or microorganisms that threaten health.

Chemical hazards

Exposure to chemicals (carcinogens, neurotoxins) causing acute or long-term effects.

Physical hazards

Radiation, heat/cold stress, vibrations, noise.

Psychosocial hazards

Workplace violence, stress, affecting psychological health.

Continuing Professional Development (CPD)

Lifelong process of learning for professionals. Benefits include:

  • Expands knowledge base.
  • Boosts confidence and credibility.
  • Increases earning potential and hireability.
  • Provides networking opportunities.
  • Keeps professionals current on trends.
  • Opens doors to career changes.

‚è±Ô∏è Time Management

Process of planning and exercising conscious control of time spent on specific activities to increase effectiveness, efficiency, and productivity.

Techniques:

  • Set Priorities (High Priority first).
  • Finish assignments within stipulated time.
  • Understand difference between urgent and important.
  • Stay focused (Avoid unnecessary strolls).
  • Include time for breaks.
  • Set realistic and achievable targets.
  • Do not overburden yourself (Learn to say no).
  • Be disciplined and punctual.
  • Keep things organized (Files in proper places).
  • Change attitude (Don't just work for money/when boss is around).
  • Use an organizer.
