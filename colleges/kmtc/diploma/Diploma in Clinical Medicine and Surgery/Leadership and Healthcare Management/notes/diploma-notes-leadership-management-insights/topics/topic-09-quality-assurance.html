Quality assurance (QA) is any systematic process of determining whether a product or service meets specified requirements.

Quality Management is the act of overseeing different activities and tasks within an organization to ensure consistent delivery of products and services. It consists of four key components:

  • Quality Planning: Identifying quality standards.
  • Quality Improvement: Purposeful change to improve reliability.
  • Quality Control: Effort to uphold integrity and reliability.
  • Quality Assurance: Systematic actions to offer sufficient reliability.

Principles of Quality Management

The International Standard for Quality Management adopts these principles:

‚ñ∂ 1. Customer Focus

The primary focus should be to meet and exceed customers' expectations. Understanding current and future needs leads to customer loyalty and increased revenue.

‚ñ∂ 2. Leadership

Great leadership establishes unity and purpose. Leaders should involve employees in setting clear goals, motivating them to improve productivity and loyalty.

‚ñ∂ 3. Engagement of People

Management engages staff at all levels. Empowering employees, involving them in decision making, and recognizing achievements boosts confidence and accountability.

‚ñ∂ 4. Process Approach

Emphasizes efficiency and effectiveness in organizational processes. Good processes result in consistency, reduced costs, and waste removal.

‚ñ∂ 5. Continuous Improvement

Businesses should constantly improve to maintain performance, organizational flexibility, and the ability to embrace new opportunities.

‚ñ∂ 6. Evidence-based Decision Making

Making decisions based on verified and analyzed data improves understanding of the marketplace and helps explain cause-and-effect relationships.

‚ñ∂ 7. Relationship Management

Creating mutually beneficial relations with suppliers and retailers. Optimizing relationships with interested parties leads to sustained success.

Benefits of Quality Management

  • Achieves greater consistency in production.
  • Increases efficiency and reduces wastage.
  • Improves customer satisfaction.
  • Enables effective marketing and exploitation of new markets.
  • Facilitates integration of new employees.
  • Enables continuous improvement.

üìè Methods & Tools for Measuring Quality

Methods for Measuring Service Quality

1. Post-service ratings

Asking customers to rate the service right after delivery while the memory is fresh.

2. Follow-up surveys

Email surveys allowing for detailed responses and holistic overview, though response rates may be lower.

3. In-app surveys

Questions asked while the visitor is on the website or in the app.

4. Documentation analysis

Qualitative approach of reading/listening to service records to check if agents took the right actions.

5. First contact resolution ratio

Measuring if a customer's issue was resolved in the first session without follow-ups.

Tools of Measuring Quality

  • Stratification: Sorting data, objects, and people into separate groups to reveal patterns.
  • Histogram: Bar graph representing frequency distribution of data to identify areas for improvement.
  • Check sheet (Tally sheet): Collects data in the form of check marks to zero in on defect patterns.
  • Cause-and-effect diagram (Fishbone/Ishikawa): Identifies various factors (causes) leading to a problem (effect). Causes grouped into categories like materials, personnel, environment, etc.
  • Pareto chart (80-20 rule): Assumes 80% of problems are caused by 20% of major factors. Highlights the "vital few" factors.
  • Scatter diagram: Depicts the relationship between two variables to identify cause and effect correlations.
  • Control chart (Shewhart chart): Determines if a process is stable and predictable using historical data limits.

Standards of care are considered the baseline for quality care. They outline professional expectations, guide nurses on proper protocol, and provide an objective standard for evaluation.

Importance of Standards

  • Outline professional expectations.
  • Guide nurses on proper protocol.

üìä Monitoring and Evaluation (M&E)

Monitoring

Systematic process of collecting, analyzing and using information to track progress toward objectives. Focuses on processes (when, where, who). Checks progress against plans.

Evaluation

Assessing a completed or ongoing project systematically and objectively. Appraises data to inform strategic decisions. Delves deeper into relationships between results and impact.

Evaluation Criteria (OECD DAC)

  • Relevance: Extent to which activity is suited to priorities of target group/donor.
  • Effectiveness: Measure of the extent to which activity attains its objectives.
  • Efficiency: Measures outputs in relation to inputs (least costly resources).
  • Impact: Positive/negative changes produced, intended or unintended.
  • Sustainability: Whether benefits are likely to continue after donor funding is withdrawn.

üõ†Ô∏è Monitoring Tools & KPIs

Monitoring Tools

  • Employee Monitoring Software: Tracks time, distractions, and productive vs. unproductive activity.
  • Video Surveillance: CCTV for safety and preventing misbehavior.
  • Network and Email Monitoring: Analyzes traffic and prevents misuse of official email.
  • Keycards and Biometric Terminals: Restricts access and monitors attendance/location.
  • GPS Tracking: Tracks field employees (drivers, engineers).

Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)

1. Customer Satisfaction

Ultimate measure of success. Feedback obtained via surveys.

2. Productivity

Output compared to input (e.g., revenue per employee).

3. Cost Efficiency

Ratio of value earned to cost incurred.

4. Time

Cycle time from beginning to end. Shorter cycle = faster ROI.

5. ROI

Total benefit divided by total cost. Measures return for every dollar.

6. Alignment

Ensuring projects align with organizational goals.

Setting SMART Targets

  • Specific
  • Measurable
  • Achievable
  • Realistic

Work Plan

A set of goals and processes to accomplish them. Key sections include:

  • Introduction and Background
  • Project Scope and Team
  • Schedule and Timelines
  • Budget Planning and Monitoring
  • Quality Control and Continuity Plan

Comprehensive Report Writing Guide

Format of a Report

  1. Title Section: Author, date, etc.
  2. Summary: Overview of content, key points, conclusion.
  3. Introduction: Problem, purpose, definition of terms.
  4. Body: Major points, subparts, arranged by importance.
  5. Conclusion: Connects everything, final thoughts.
  6. Recommendations: Suggestions for action.
  7. Appendices: Technical details, statistics.

Types of Reports

  • Long vs. Short Reports
  • Internal vs. External Reports
  • Vertical vs. Lateral Reports
  • Periodic Reports (Annual, Quarterly)
  • Formal vs. Informal Reports
  • Informational vs. Analytical Reports
  • Proposal Reports
  • Functional Reports (Marketing, Financial)
