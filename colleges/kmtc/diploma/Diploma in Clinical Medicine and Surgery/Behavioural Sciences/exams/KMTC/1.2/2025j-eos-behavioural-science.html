Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions (20 Marks)

1.
A patient expresses suicidal thoughts after a recent cancer diagnosis. What is the first step in counselling?
A. Develop a long-term therapy plan.
B. Assess the immediacy of risk and ensure safety.
C. Provide reassurance ("Everything will be fine").
D. Contact the patient's family without consent.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Assess the immediacy of risk and ensure safety.

Explanation: In any situation involving suicidal ideation, the immediate priority is to assess the risk (Does he have a plan? Means? Intent?) and ensure the patient is safe from self-harm. Long-term therapy comes later.

2.
After a counselling session on marijuana cessation, a patient says, "I'm not sure if I can." According to the Stages of Change Model, which stage is this patient in?
A. Precontemplation
B. Preparation
C. Contemplation
D. Action
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Contemplation

Explanation: The Contemplation stage is characterized by ambivalence. The patient acknowledges the problem and is thinking about change but is unsure of their ability or willingness to commit ("I'm not sure if I can").

3.
A counsellor in a HIV clinic learns that a patient is engaging in unprotected sex without disclosing their status. What is the counsellor's best ethical action?
A. Respect confidentiality and avoid intervention.
B. Report the patient to authorities immediately.
C. Encourage the patient to disclose their status to partners and discuss harm reduction.
D. Warn the patient's partner without consent.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Encourage the patient to disclose their status to partners and discuss harm reduction.

Explanation: The initial ethical step is to work with the patient to encourage voluntary disclosure and safer practices. Breaching confidentiality (D) is a last resort measure only when there is an immediate, identifiable threat to a specific person and all other means have failed (Duty to Warn), but C is the "best" initial clinical action.

4.
A patient with hypertension admits to unhealthy eating habits but says, "It's hard to change." Which counselling technique is most effective in this situation?
A. Directive counselling
B. Setting strict dietary goals for the patient.
C. Giving a pamphlet on healthy eating.
D. Reflective counselling
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Reflective counselling

Explanation: Reflective counselling (a core skill of Motivational Interviewing) helps the patient explore their ambivalence and resistance ("It's hard to change") without creating defensiveness. It validates their feelings and empowers them to find their own solutions.

5.
Karl Marx's concept of "false consciousness" refers to:
A) Workers recognizing their exploitation under capitalism
B) The proletariat developing revolutionary class consciousness
C) The bourgeoisie manipulating media to control ideology
D) Workers accepting capitalist ideology against their own interests
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) Workers accepting capitalist ideology against their own interests

Explanation: False consciousness is a state where the working class (proletariat) unknowingly accepts and adopts the ideology of the ruling class (bourgeoisie), preventing them from recognizing their own exploitation.

6.
A government policy that imposes democratic elections on a traditionally consensus-based society reflects:
A) Cultural diffusion
B) Ethnocentrism
C) Cultural relativism
D) Participant observation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Ethnocentrism

Explanation: Ethnocentrism is the belief that one's own culture (e.g., Western democracy) is superior and the correct standard by which to judge or organize other societies. Imposing it suggests a lack of respect for the local culture's methods (consensus).

19.
A student struggling with financial instability finds it difficult to focus on academic goals. This aligns with Maslow's idea that:
A. Self-actualization is the primary motivator
B. Lower-level needs must be met before higher ones
C. Esteem needs override safety needs
D. External validation is key to motivation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Lower-level needs must be met before higher ones

Explanation: Financial instability threatens Physiological and Safety needs (the base of the pyramid). Maslow argued that until these deficiency needs are met, an individual cannot focus on higher-level growth needs like cognitive/academic achievement (Self-actualization).

20.
A person who smokes despite knowing the health risks may reduce cognitive dissonance by:
A. Quitting smoking immediately
B. Avoiding information about smoking hazards
C. Acknowledging the risks but continuing to smoke
D. Convincing themselves that isn't as harmful as research suggests
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Convincing themselves that isn't as harmful as research suggests

Explanation: Cognitive Dissonance is the mental discomfort of holding conflicting beliefs (I smoke vs Smoking kills). To reduce this, people often use Rationalization or denial (Option D) to justify the behavior, rather than changing the behavior (Option A, which is harder).

Section 2: Short Answer Questions (40 Marks)

21.
Anthropology teaches that a people's view of the cause of ill-health influences their health-seeking behavior. In this connection, state the naturalistic theory and what it means for the concerned community's health-seeking behavior of the people. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Naturalistic Theory:

This theory posits that illness is caused by an imbalance in natural forces (e.g., heat, cold, wind, dampness) or the body's equilibrium (humors, Yin/Yang), rather than by supernatural agents.

Impact on Health-Seeking Behavior:

  • Patients seek remedies to restore balance (e.g., herbs, dietary changes, massage, acupuncture).
  • They may avoid "hot" or "cold" foods depending on the illness.
  • They are often more open to pluralistic medical systems (using traditional healers alongside biomedicine if biomedicine is seen as "strong medicine" for symptoms but not root cause balance).
22.
Giving examples, critically analyze the relationship between socioeconomic status and health outcomes. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Socioeconomic Status (SES) is the strongest predictor of health. The relationship is positive: Higher SES correlates with better health.

  • Access to Care: Low SES limits access to quality healthcare, preventive screenings, and medications (e.g., delayed cancer diagnosis).
  • Environment: Low SES individuals often live in areas with pollution, poor sanitation, and overcrowding (higher infectious disease risk like TB).
  • Lifestyle: Financial strain leads to poor diet (cheap, calorie-dense food -> obesity) and high chronic stress (Hypertension).
  • Education: Lower health literacy affects adherence to treatment and understanding of disease prevention.

25.
Describe the counseling process. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

The counseling process typically follows these stages (GATHER or SOLER models apply, but general phases are):

  1. Relationship Building (Rapport): Establishing trust and safety.
  2. Assessment/Problem Definition: Exploring the client's issues and gathering information.
  3. Goal Setting: Collaboratively deciding what the client wants to achieve.
  4. Intervention/Action: Implementing strategies to solve the problem or change behavior.
  5. Termination/Follow-up: Ending the professional relationship when goals are met and planning for maintenance.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions (40 Marks)

29.
Discuss Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs in detail, including each of the five levels. Provide real-life examples for each stage. (20 marks)
Show Answer

Abraham Maslow proposed that human motivation is arranged in a hierarchy of five needs. Individuals must satisfy lower-level "deficiency" needs before progressing to higher-level "growth" needs.

1. Physiological Needs (Base):

  • Description: Basic biological requirements for survival. If these are not met, the human body cannot function.
  • Examples: Air, food, water, shelter, sleep, clothing.
  • Clinical Relevance: A homeless patient cannot prioritize medication adherence if they are starving.

2. Safety Needs:

  • Description: The need for security, order, and protection from elements and danger.
  • Examples: Personal security, employment, resources, health, property.
  • Clinical Relevance: A patient in an abusive relationship lacks safety, affecting their mental health.

3. Love and Belongingness Needs (Social):

  • Description: The need for interpersonal relationships and feelings of connectedness.
  • Examples: Friendship, intimacy, family, sense of connection.
  • Clinical Relevance: Isolation and loneliness are risk factors for depression.

4. Esteem Needs:

  • Description: The need for self-worth and respect from others. Divided into (i) esteem for oneself (dignity, achievement) and (ii) desire for reputation/respect from others (status).
  • Examples: Recognition, freedom, strength, status.
  • Clinical Relevance: Chronic illness can strip patients of their independence and self-esteem.

5. Self-Actualization (Top):

  • Description: The realization of a person's potential, self-fulfillment, seeking personal growth and peak experiences. "What a man can be, he must be."
  • Examples: Pursuing talent, creativity, parenting, utilizing one's abilities to the fullest.
30.
Membership to groups is integral to social life. Every college student and indeed adult therefore needs understanding of at least the basic concepts and principles of group dynamics.
(a) Describe briefly the dynamics of the various phases of formation of a group (15 marks)
(b) Explain 5 roles of a group leader (5 marks)
Show Answer

(a) Phases of Group Formation (Tuckman's Model)

  1. Forming:
    • Dynamics: Members are polite, anxious, and unsure of their roles. They look to the leader for guidance. The focus is on orientation and understanding the task.
    • Behavior: "Ice-breaking," testing boundaries.
  2. Storming:
    • Dynamics: Conflict arises as members resist group influence and vie for position/power. Differences in opinion surface. This is the most critical stage.
    • Behavior: Arguments, criticism, challenging the leader.
  3. Norming:
    • Dynamics: Conflict resolves, and unity emerges. Norms (rules of behavior) and roles are established. Cohesion develops.
    • Behavior: Cooperation, mutual support, "We" feeling.
  4. Performing:
    • Dynamics: The group is fully functional and focused on the task. Energy is directed towards achieving goals.
    • Behavior: Productive work, problem-solving, autonomy.
  5. Adjourning (Mourning):
    • Dynamics: The task is completed, and the group disbands.
    • Behavior: Sense of loss or satisfaction, recognition of achievements.

(b) Roles of a Group Leader

  1. Initiator/Director: Defining the task, suggesting procedures, and keeping the group focused on goals.
  2. Mediator/Harmonizer: Resolving conflicts and reducing tension between members.
  3. Motivator: Encouraging participation and boosting morale.
  4. Gatekeeper: Ensuring open communication and that everyone has a chance to speak (managing dominant members).
  5. Representative: Acting as the spokesperson for the group to the outside world.
