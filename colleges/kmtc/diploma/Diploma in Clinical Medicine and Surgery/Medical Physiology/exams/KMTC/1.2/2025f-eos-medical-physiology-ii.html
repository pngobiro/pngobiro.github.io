Section 2: Short Answer Questions (40 Marks)

21.
Explain the process of action potential generation in a neuron. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

The generation of an action potential follows a specific sequence of events:

  1. Resting State: The neuron membrane is polarized at approximately -70mV. Voltage-gated Na+ and K+ channels are closed.
  2. Depolarization: A stimulus causes the membrane potential to reach threshold (-55mV). Voltage-gated Sodium (Na+) channels open rapidly, allowing Na+ to rush into the cell. The interior becomes positive (up to +30mV).
  3. Repolarization: Na+ channels close (inactivate), and Voltage-gated Potassium (K+) channels open. K+ flows out of the cell, restoring the negative charge inside.
  4. Hyperpolarization: K+ channels remain open slightly longer than needed, causing the potential to dip below the resting level (undershoot).
  5. Return to Resting Potential: The Sodium-Potassium pump (Na+/K+ ATPase) restores the original ionic balance (3 Na+ out, 2 K+ in).
22.
Describe the role of the cerebral cortex in motor control and give two examples of motor functions it regulates. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Role: The cerebral cortex, specifically the Primary Motor Cortex (precentral gyrus) and Premotor areas, is responsible for the planning, initiation, and execution of voluntary muscle movements. It sends signals via the corticospinal (pyramidal) tracts to lower motor neurons in the spinal cord.

Examples of functions:

  1. Fine Motor Skills: Controlling precise movements of the fingers (e.g., writing or typing).
  2. Speech Production: Broca's area (in the frontal cortex) coordinates the complex muscle movements required for speech.
23.
Describe the neural pathway of vision from the retina to the visual cortex. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

The visual pathway consists of the following sequential steps:

  1. Retina: Photoreceptors (Rods/Cones) convert light to electrical signals, which pass to Bipolar cells and then Ganglion cells.
  2. Optic Nerve (CN II): Axons of ganglion cells exit the eye.
  3. Optic Chiasm: Fibers from the nasal retina of each eye cross over (decussate) to the opposite side.
  4. Optic Tract: Carries fibers to the thalamus.
  5. Lateral Geniculate Nucleus (LGN): Relay station in the thalamus where fibers synapse.
  6. Optic Radiation: Fibers project from the LGN through the parietal and temporal lobes.
  7. Primary Visual Cortex (V1): Located in the occipital lobe, where visual processing occurs.
24.
Explain how mechanoreceptors contribute to the sensation of touch and pressure. Include their location and function. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Mechanoreceptors detect mechanical deformation of the skin.

  • Meissner's Corpuscles: Located in the dermal papillae (hairless skin). Detect light touch and low-frequency vibration.
  • Pacinian Corpuscles: Located deep in the dermis/subcutaneous tissue. Detect deep pressure and high-frequency vibration.
  • Merkel Discs: Located in the basal epidermis. Detect static touch and texture (shapes/edges).
  • Ruffini Endings: Located in the dermis. Detect skin stretch and sustained pressure.

Function: They transduce mechanical energy into action potentials sent to the somatosensory cortex.

25.
Define negative feedback in the endocrine system and give one specific example illustrating this mechanism. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Definition: Negative feedback is a regulatory mechanism where a change in a physiological variable triggers a response that counteracts the initial change, restoring the variable to its set point (homeostasis). The output of the pathway inhibits the input.

Example (Thyroid Hormone Regulation):

  • The Hypothalamus secretes TRH -> stimulates Pituitary to secrete TSH -> stimulates Thyroid to secrete T3/T4.
  • Feedback: High levels of T3/T4 in the blood inhibit the Hypothalamus and Pituitary, reducing TRH and TSH secretion, thus preventing overproduction of thyroid hormones.

27.
Briefly explain how the hypothalamus and pituitary gland interact in regulating hormone production. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

The hypothalamus acts as the master control center linking the nervous and endocrine systems:

  1. Anterior Pituitary Interaction: The hypothalamus secretes Releasing Hormones (e.g., GHRH, TRH) or Inhibiting Hormones (e.g., Somatostatin) into the Hypophyseal Portal System. These travel directly to the anterior pituitary to stimulate or suppress the secretion of tropic hormones (e.g., GH, TSH).
  2. Posterior Pituitary Interaction: Hormones (ADH and Oxytocin) are synthesized in hypothalamic neurons. They are transported down axons via the Hypothalamic-Hypophyseal Tract to be stored and released by the posterior pituitary directly into the blood.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions (20 Marks Each)

29.
Describe the different classifications of hormones and explain how each class works. (20 Marks)
Show Answer

Hormones are chemically classified into three main groups, which determines their mechanism of action.

1. Peptide and Protein Hormones

  • Structure: Chains of amino acids. Examples: Insulin, Glucagon, Pituitary hormones (GH, TSH).
  • Solubility: Water-soluble (Hydrophilic). They travel freely in blood but cannot cross the cell membrane.
  • Mechanism of Action:
    • Bind to specific cell surface receptors (G-protein coupled receptors or Tyrosine kinase receptors).
    • Activate Second Messengers inside the cell (e.g., cAMP, IP3, Calcium).
    • This triggers a phosphorylation cascade (enzyme activation) leading to rapid cellular responses.

2. Steroid Hormones

  • Structure: Derived from Cholesterol. Examples: Cortisol, Aldosterone, Testosterone, Estrogen.
  • Solubility: Lipid-soluble (Hydrophobic). They require transport proteins in blood but can diffuse directly through cell membranes.
  • Mechanism of Action:
    • Enter the cell and bind to intracellular receptors (cytoplasmic or nuclear).
    • The Hormone-Receptor complex binds to DNA (Hormone Response Elements).
    • This regulates Gene Transcription (protein synthesis), leading to slower but long-lasting effects.

3. Amine Hormones

  • Structure: Derived from the amino acid Tyrosine.
  • Sub-types:
    • Catecholamines (Adrenaline/Noradrenaline): Behave like peptide hormones (water-soluble, surface receptors, fast action).
    • Thyroid Hormones (T3/T4): Behave like steroid hormones (lipid-soluble, nuclear receptors, slow action on metabolic rate).
30.
Describe the key organs involved in the digestion and absorption of carbohydrates, proteins, and fats, and explain how each organ contributes to the overall process. (20 Marks)
Show Answer

Digestion involves mechanical and chemical breakdown of nutrients.

1. Mouth (Oral Cavity)

  • Mechanical: Mastication increases surface area.
  • Carbohydrates: Salivary Amylase begins breakdown of starch into maltose.
  • Fats: Lingual Lipase (minor activity).

2. Stomach

  • Proteins: Chief cells secrete Pepsinogen, activated by HCl (from Parietal cells) to Pepsin, breaking proteins into peptides. HCl also denatures proteins.
  • Mechanical: Churning forms chyme.

3. Small Intestine (Duodenum) - Major Digestion Site

  • Liver/Gallbladder: Secrete/Store Bile. Bile salts emulsify fats (break large drops into micelles), increasing surface area for lipase.
  • Pancreas: Secretes enzymes into duodenum:
    • Amylase: Continues starch digestion.
    • Lipase: Breaks triglycerides into fatty acids and monoglycerides.
    • Proteases (Trypsin, Chymotrypsin): Break peptides into smaller peptides.

4. Small Intestine (Jejunum/Ileum) - Final Digestion & Absorption

  • Brush Border Enzymes: Located on microvilli.
    • Carbs: Maltase, Sucrase, Lactase break disaccharides into monosaccharides (Glucose, Fructose, Galactose).
    • Proteins: Peptidases break small peptides into amino acids.
  • Absorption Mechanism:
    • Carbs/Proteins: Absorbed into blood capillaries via active transport/facilitated diffusion -> Portal Vein -> Liver.
    • Fats: Fatty acids reassembled into Chylomicrons inside cells, absorbed into Lacteals (Lymphatics) -> Systemic circulation.
  • Structure: Villi and microvilli massively increase surface area for absorption.
