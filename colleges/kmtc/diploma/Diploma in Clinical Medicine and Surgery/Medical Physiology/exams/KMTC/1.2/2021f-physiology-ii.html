Section A: Multiple Choice Questions (20 Marks)

1.
Endocrine messengers:-
a) long-distance chemical signals that travel in the blood or lymph
b) chemicals that exert effects on the same cells that secrete them
c) locally acting chemicals that affect cells other than those that secrete them
d) Examples are acetylcholine and dopamine.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) long-distance chemical signals that travel in the blood or lymph

Explanation: This is the definition of a hormone (endocrine messenger). They are secreted into the bloodstream and travel to distant target organs.

  • b) Describes autocrine signaling.
  • c) Describes paracrine signaling.
  • d) These are primarily neurotransmitters.
2.
Defense against infection is a function of the lungs, this is effected by the:-
a) lymphocytes and plasma cells
b) humidification
c) mucus production
d) warming of the air
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) lymphocytes and plasma cells

Explanation: While mucus production (c) traps pathogens (mechanical defense), lymphocytes and plasma cells (part of the Bronchus-Associated Lymphoid Tissue - BALT) provide the specific immune defense (production of IgA, killing of infected cells) to effectively fight infection.

12.
Factors that increase dissociation of oxyhaemoglobin include
a) low O2 levels
b) high O2 levels
c) High pH
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) low O2 levels

Explanation: Low partial pressure of Oxygen (low pO2) in tissues is the primary driver for hemoglobin to release (dissociate) oxygen.

Note on Bohr Effect: Factors that shift the curve to the right (facilitating dissociation) include: Low pH, High Temperature, High PCO2, and High 2,3-DPG. Option (c) High pH causes association (holding onto O2).

13.
Hypoxemia, or decreased PaO2, is caused by
a) low altitude
b) hyperventilation
c) diffusion defects
d) Left to right shunts
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) diffusion defects

Explanation: Diffusion defects (like pulmonary fibrosis or edema) prevent O2 from crossing into the blood, causing hypoxemia.

  • a) High altitude causes hypoxemia due to low atmospheric pO2.
  • b) Hyperventilation increases PaO2.
  • d) Right-to-Left shunts cause hypoxemia (mixing venous blood). Left-to-right shunts generally do not cause early hypoxemia.

Section B: Short Answer Questions (40 Marks)

1.
State two functions of each of the following hormones (10 marks)
Show Answer

a) Oxytocin:

  • Stimulates uterine contractions during labor (positive feedback).
  • Stimulates milk ejection ("let-down" reflex) during breastfeeding.

b) Anti-diuretic hormone (ADH):

  • Increases water reabsorption in the kidney collecting ducts (via aquaporins).
  • Causes vasoconstriction at high concentrations (vasopressin) to raise blood pressure.

c) Testosterone:

  • Promotes spermatogenesis.
  • Development and maintenance of male secondary sex characteristics.

d) Insulin:

  • Lowers blood glucose by facilitating cellular uptake of glucose.
  • Promotes storage of energy (glycogenesis, lipogenesis, protein synthesis).

e) Parathyroid Hormone (PTH):

  • Increases blood calcium levels by stimulating bone resorption (osteoclasts).
  • Increases Calcium reabsorption in kidneys and activation of Vitamin D.
2.
Briefly describe the functions of the kidneys (10 marks)
Show Answer

Functions of the Kidneys:

  1. Excretion of Metabolic Waste: Removal of urea, creatinine, uric acid, and bilirubin.
  2. Regulation of Water and Electrolyte Balance: Maintaining homeostasis of Na+, K+, Cl-, and water volume.
  3. Regulation of Arterial Pressure: Long-term regulation via Na/Water balance and the Renin-Angiotensin system.
  4. Acid-Base Balance: Excretion of H+ and reabsorption/generation of Bicarbonate (HCO3-).
  5. Erythrocyte Production: Secretion of Erythropoietin (EPO) to stimulate RBC production in bone marrow.
  6. Vitamin D Activation: Conversion of 25-hydroxyvitamin D to active 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D.
3.
Discuss five (5) functions of the pharynx (10 marks)
Show Answer

Functions of the Pharynx:

  1. Passageway for Food: The oropharynx and laryngopharynx allow the passage of bolus from mouth to esophagus during deglutition (swallowing).
  2. Passageway for Air: The nasopharynx conducts air from the nasal cavity to the larynx.
  3. Warming and Humidifying: Mucosa aids in conditioning the air before it reaches lungs.
  4. Immune Defense: Contains the tonsils (Waldeyer's ring: pharyngeal, palatine, lingual) which trap and destroy pathogens.
  5. Resonating Chamber: Acts as a resonating chamber for speech production, affecting voice quality.
  6. Pressure Equalization: The auditory (Eustachian) tube opens into the nasopharynx to equalize middle ear pressure.
4.
Outline the leutal and follicular phase of menstrual cycle (10 marks)
Show Answer

1. Follicular Phase (Days 1-14):

  • Hormones: Driven by rise in FSH from pituitary.
  • Ovary: Recruitment and maturation of ovarian follicles; one becomes dominant (Graafian follicle).
  • Secretion: Dominant follicle secretes increasing amounts of Estrogen.
  • Uterus: Estrogen causes repair and proliferation of the endometrium.
  • End: High estrogen triggers LH surge, leading to ovulation.

2. Luteal Phase (Days 15-28):

  • Event: Occurs after ovulation.
  • Structure: Ruptured follicle transforms into the Corpus Luteum.
  • Hormones: Corpus Luteum secretes high levels of Progesterone (and some estrogen).
  • Uterus: Progesterone makes endometrium secretory, vascular, and ready for implantation.
  • Outcome: If no fertilization, corpus luteum degenerates (corpus albicans), progesterone drops, and menstruation begins.

Section C: Long Answer Questions (40 Marks)

1.
Explain how TRH from the hypothalamus and TSH from the anterior pituitary contribute to regulation of thyroid hormone. Be sure to indicate where the hormones travel and how. (20 marks)
Show Answer

Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Thyroid (HPT) Axis Regulation:

1. Hypothalamus (TRH Release)

  • The hypothalamus monitors metabolic rate and blood levels of thyroid hormones.
  • When levels are low or demand increases (cold, stress), it secretes Thyrotropin-Releasing Hormone (TRH).
  • Travel: TRH travels via the hypothalamic-hypophyseal portal system directly to the anterior pituitary gland.

2. Anterior Pituitary (TSH Release)

  • TRH stimulates thyrotrophs in the anterior pituitary.
  • These cells release Thyroid Stimulating Hormone (TSH).
  • Travel: TSH enters the systemic circulation and travels via the blood to the thyroid gland.

3. Thyroid Gland (T3/T4 Production)

  • TSH binds to receptors on follicular cells of the thyroid.
  • Effects of TSH:
    • Increases iodide uptake (trapping).
    • Stimulates iodination of thyroglobulin.
    • Stimulates release of T3 (Triiodothyronine) and T4 (Thyroxine) into blood.

4. Negative Feedback Loop

  • As blood levels of T3 and T4 rise, they exert a negative feedback effect.
  • High T3/T4 inhibits the hypothalamus (reducing TRH) and the anterior pituitary (reducing sensitivity to TRH).
  • This prevents overproduction and maintains homeostasis.
2.
How do the process of filtration, reabsorption and secretion affect the composition of blood (20 marks)
Show Answer

The kidneys regulate blood composition through three main processes occurring in the nephron:

1. Glomerular Filtration (Indiscriminate Removal)

  • Process: Blood pressure forces plasma, water, and small solutes from the glomerular capillaries into Bowman's capsule.
  • Effect on Blood: Removes a large volume of fluid and solutes (glucose, amino acids, ions, wastes) indiscriminately based on size. Blood cells and large proteins are retained.
  • Result: Blood volume decreases slightly; filtrate is iso-osmotic to plasma.

2. Tubular Reabsorption (Selective Recovery)

  • Process: Useful substances are moved from the renal tubules back into the peritubular capillaries (blood).
  • Effect on Blood:
    • Nutrients: 100% of glucose and amino acids are returned to blood (preserving energy).
    • Ions: Na+, K+, Ca2+ are reabsorbed based on body needs (regulated by aldosterone/PTH).
    • Water: 99% of water is reabsorbed (regulated by ADH), maintaining blood volume and pressure.

3. Tubular Secretion (Selective Removal)

  • Process: Substances are actively transported from the peritubular capillaries into the renal tubules.
  • Effect on Blood:
    • pH Balance: Excess H+ ions are removed from blood to correct acidosis; K+ is secreted to prevent hyperkalemia.
    • Detoxification: Drugs (e.g., penicillin), toxins, and metabolic wastes (urea, uric acid, creatinine) are actively cleared from the blood.

Summary

Urine Excretion = Filtration - Reabsorption + Secretion. Through these steps, the kidney ensures the blood retains necessary nutrients and water while being cleansed of toxic wastes and excess ions.
