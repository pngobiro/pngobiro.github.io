Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions

1.
Removal of the thyroid gland (without replacement therapy) leads to an increased
a) Blood metabolic rate
b) Basal metabolic rate
c) Blood glucose level during an oral glucose tolerance test
d) Tremor of the outstretched hand
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Blood glucose level during an oral glucose tolerance test (See explanation)

Explanation: This question is tricky. Let's analyze:
Removal of thyroid gland causes Hypothyroidism.
a) & b) Metabolic rate decreases in hypothyroidism.
d) Tremor is a sign of Hyperthyroidism.
c) Hypothyroidism can lead to delayed uptake of glucose by tissues and decreased insulin sensitivity, potentially leading to prolonged elevation of blood glucose during a tolerance test (decreased glucose tolerance), though fasting glucose might be low/normal. However, by elimination, A, B, and D are clearly characteristics of Hyperthyroidism (or the opposite of removal).
Alternative interpretation: If the question implies TSH levels, they would increase. Given the options, the most plausible "increase" related to hypothyroidism pathology involves cholesterol or TSH. However, looking at option C, hypothyroidism slows absorption but also slows clearance. The most distinct clinical feature of *lack* of thyroid is low BMR. If the question meant "leads to a *decreased*", B would be the answer.
Correction/Context: Often these questions test "What happens in Hypothyroidism vs Hyperthyroidism". Hypothyroidism = Low BMR. Hyperthyroidism = High BMR, Tremor.
Let's re-read option C carefully. "Blood glucose level...". Hypothyroidism decreases the rate of glucose absorption from the gut, which would actually *flatten* the GTT curve initially.
Possibility of typo: Could the question be "leads to an increased... cholesterol"? (Not listed).
Let's reconsider D: Tremor is definitely hyper.
Let's reconsider A/B: Definitely decrease.
Best fit by exclusion of A, B, D: C is the only remaining option, though physiologically complex. However, if the question meant "Decreased", B is the classic answer.

2.
Excess glucocorticoid production (Cushing's syndrome) causes an increase in
a) Skin thickness
b) Bone strength
c) Blood glucose
d) Rate of wound healing
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Blood glucose

Explanation: Glucocorticoids (Cortisol) promote gluconeogenesis and inhibit glucose uptake by tissues, leading to hyperglycemia (adrenal diabetes).
a) Skin thickness decreases (thin skin/striae).
b) Bone strength decreases (osteoporosis).
d) Wound healing is delayed/poor.

3.
In adrenal failure there is likely to be a fall in the
a) Intracellular fluid volume
b) Total red cell mass
c) Arterial blood pressure
d) Blood urea
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Arterial blood pressure

Explanation: Adrenal failure (Addison's disease) leads to deficiency of Aldosterone (causing Na+ and water loss) and Cortisol (loss of vascular tone permissive effect), resulting in hypotension.

6.
The ovaries
a) Begin to develop ova at puberty when acted on by FSH
b) Are required for cyclical menstrual activity
c) Must have double follicular rupture if identical twins are conceived

Show Answer

Explanation: The ovarian cycle (Follicular/Luteal phases) drives the endometrial cycle via Estrogen and Progesterone.

11.
Sympathetic
a) Ganglionic transmission is mediated by acetylcholine
b) Neuromuscular transmission at the heart is mediated by adrenaline
c) Neuromuscular transmission in hand skin arterioles is mediated by acetylcholine
d) Neuroglandular transmission at sweat glands is mediated by noradrenaline
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Ganglionic transmission is mediated by acetylcholine

Explanation: All preganglionic autonomic fibers (Sympathetic and Parasympathetic) release Acetylcholine at the ganglion.
b) False: Mediated by Noradrenaline (Norepinephrine) at the effector.
d) False: Sweat glands are the exception; sympathetic postganglionic fibers there release Acetylcholine (Cholinergic sympathetic).

12.
Nerve impulses
a) Can travel in one direction only in a nerve fibre
b) Can travel in one direction only across a synapse
c) Travel at the speed of an electric current
d) Can be transmitted at higher frequencies in autonomic than in somatic nerves
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Can travel in one direction only across a synapse

Explanation: Synapses are valves that ensure one-way transmission (Law of Forward Conduction). In a nerve fiber (axon), impulses can experimentally travel both ways, but physiologically travel one way due to the refractory period of the previous segment.
c) False: Much slower than electricity.
d) False: Somatic nerves (myelinated A fibers) conduct faster and can handle higher frequencies than autonomic (unmyelinated C/B fibers).

13.
Parasympathetic nerves
a) Have opposite effects to sympathetic nerves on intestinal smooth muscle
b) Have opposite effects to sympathetic nerves on iris smooth muscle
c) Cause vasodilatation in skeletal muscle during prolonged exercise
d) Cause sweat secretion in skin when body temperature rises
e) Have longer postganglionic than preganglionic fibres
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Have opposite effects to sympathetic nerves on intestinal smooth muscle

Explanation:
Intestine: Parasympathetic stimulates peristalsis; Sympathetic inhibits. (Direct antagonism).
Iris: Parasympathetic constricts pupil (circular muscle); Sympathetic dilates (radial muscle). While the *effect on pupil size* is opposite, they innervate *different* muscles, whereas in the gut they influence the same tissue. However, both A and B are acceptable descriptions of physiological antagonism. A is the classic example of direct opposition on motility.
e) False: Parasympathetic has long preganglionic and short postganglionic.

14.
Nerve fibres continue to conduct impulses when
a) Extracellular sodium is replaced by potassium
b) Extracellular sodium is replaced by a non-diffusible cation
c) Temperature is lowered to below 0oC provided freezing does not occur
d) The sodium-potassium pump is inactivated
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) The sodium-potassium pump is inactivated

Explanation: Immediate conduction of action potentials relies on existing concentration gradients of Na+ and K+. Even if the pump (which restores gradients long-term) is stopped, the nerve can fire thousands of impulses before the gradients are depleted.
a) & b) Removing extracellular Sodium prevents depolarization (AP generation).
c) Cold blocks conduction (cold block).

15.
An action potential in a nerve fibre
a) Occurs when its membrane potential is hyperpolarized to a critical level
b) Is associated with a transient decrease in membrane permeability to potassium
c) Induces local electrical currents in adjacent segments of the fibre
d) Has an amplitude which varies directly with the strength of stimulus
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Induces local electrical currents in adjacent segments of the fibre

Explanation: This is the mechanism of propagation. The depolarized region sets up local currents that depolarize the adjacent resting membrane to threshold.
a) False: Occurs when *depolarized* to threshold.
b) False: K+ permeability *increases* (repolarization).
d) False: Action potentials follow the "All-or-None" law; amplitude is constant.

16.
Olfactory cells
a) Are epithelial cells which synapse with olfactory nerves
b) Generate impulses when stimulated which are relayed in the thalamus
c) Are chemoreceptors
d) Show little adaptation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Are chemoreceptors

Explanation: They respond to chemical molecules (odorants).

17.
Adaptation for vision in poor light is
a) Complete after 2-3 minutes
b) Due mainly to dilatation of the pupil
c) Due to regeneration of rod but not cone pigments
d) Faster if red goggles are worn before entering the dark environment
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Due to regeneration of rod but not cone pigments

Explanation: Dark adaptation involves the regeneration of rhodopsin in rods, which takes time (20-30 mins). Cones adapt quickly but are not sensitive enough for low light.
d) True statement in practice, but C explains the *mechanism* of the adaptation itself. Red light does not bleach rods, preserving rhodopsin.

18.
The cones in the retina differ from rods in that they are
a) Numerous
b) Concerned with colour vision
c) Sensitive to light
d) Affected by vitamin A deficiency
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Concerned with colour vision

Explanation: Cones mediate color vision (photopic). Rods are more numerous (120 million vs 6 million cones), more sensitive to light (scotopic), and are affected earlier by Vitamin A deficiency (night blindness).

19.
Aldosterone
a) Is a steroid hormone secreted by the adrenal medulla
b) Production ceases following removal of the kidneys and their juxtaglomerular cells
c) Secretion results in increased potassium reabsorption by the nephron
d) Secretion results in a fall in urinary pH
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Secretion results in a fall in urinary pH

Explanation: Aldosterone stimulates Na+ reabsorption and secretion of K+ and H+ in the distal tubule. Secretion of H+ acidifies the urine (lowers pH).
a) False: Adrenal cortex (Zona glomerulosa).
b) False: While Renin (kidney) is the primary driver, high K+ levels and ACTH can still stimulate Aldosterone secretion directly.
c) False: Increases Potassium *secretion* (excretion).

20.
Aldosterone secretion tends to raise the volume of
a) Plasma
b) Intracellular fluid
c) Urine
d) Cerebrospinal fluid
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Plasma

Explanation: By reabsorbing Sodium (and water follows osmotically), Aldosterone increases Extracellular Fluid (ECF) and Plasma volume, raising blood pressure. Urine volume typically decreases.

Section 2: Short Answer Questions (40 Marks)

21.
Briefly describe the sequence of events in a neuromuscular junction transmission (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Arrival of Action Potential: A nerve impulse reaches the synaptic knob of the motor neuron.
  2. Calcium Influx: Voltage-gated Calcium channels open, allowing Ca²⁺ to enter the neuron.
  3. Vesicle Fusion: Calcium causes synaptic vesicles to fuse with the presynaptic membrane.
  4. ACh Release: Acetylcholine (ACh) is released into the synaptic cleft via exocytosis.
  5. Binding: ACh diffuses across the cleft and binds to Nicotinic ACh receptors on the motor end plate.
  6. Depolarization: Receptors open Na⁺ channels, causing Na⁺ influx and generation of an End Plate Potential (EPP), leading to a muscle action potential.
22.
How are neurons described according to medical physiology? (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Answer: Neurons are classified structurally and functionally:

1. Structural Classification (based on processes):

  • Multipolar: Many dendrites, one axon (Most common, e.g., Motor neurons).
  • Bipolar: One dendrite, one axon (e.g., Retina, Olfactory).
  • Unipolar (Pseudounipolar): Single process splits into two (e.g., Sensory ganglia).

2. Functional Classification:

  • Sensory (Afferent): Transmit impulses from receptors to CNS.
  • Motor (Efferent): Transmit impulses from CNS to effectors (muscles/glands).
  • Interneurons: Connect sensory and motor neurons within the CNS.
23.
Starting from the outermost to the innermost layer outline the histological layers of a human retina (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Answer: (Direction: Choroid to Vitreous)

  1. Pigment Epithelium: Outermost layer absorbing stray light.
  2. Layer of Rods and Cones: Photoreceptor outer/inner segments.
  3. External Limiting Membrane: Separation between segments and nuclei.
  4. Outer Nuclear Layer: Nuclei of rods and cones.
  5. Outer Plexiform Layer: Synapses between photoreceptors and bipolar cells.
  6. Inner Nuclear Layer: Nuclei of bipolar, horizontal, and amacrine cells.
  7. Inner Plexiform Layer: Synapses between bipolar and ganglion cells.
  8. Ganglion Cell Layer: Nuclei of ganglion cells.
  9. Nerve Fiber Layer: Axons of ganglion cells (forming optic nerve).
  10. Internal Limiting Membrane: Innermost boundary.
24.
Briefly describe any five (5) actions of insulin hormone (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Promotes Glucose Uptake: Increases GLUT4 insertion in muscle and adipose tissue cells.
  2. Stimulates Glycogenesis: Promotes glycogen synthesis in liver and muscle.
  3. Inhibits Gluconeogenesis: Stops production of glucose from non-carbohydrate sources in the liver.
  4. Promotes Lipogenesis: Stimulates fat synthesis and storage; inhibits lipolysis.
  5. Promotes Protein Synthesis: Increases amino acid uptake and protein formation; inhibits proteolysis.
  6. Potassium Uptake: Promotes movement of K⁺ into cells.

27.
Describe the role of the kidneys in compensated shock considering a report given for a patient who was diagnosed with compensated hemorrhagic shock as a result of a deep cut wound on the left thigh. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Answer: The kidneys help restore blood volume and pressure via:

  1. Activation of RAAS: Reduced renal perfusion triggers Renin release -> Angiotensin II (vasoconstriction) -> Aldosterone secretion.
  2. Sodium and Water Retention: Aldosterone increases Na⁺ reabsorption; Angiotensin II increases proximal tubule reabsorption, retaining water to boost blood volume.
  3. Conservation of Water: Low blood pressure stimulates ADH release, increasing water reabsorption in collecting ducts (oliguria).
  4. Erythropoietin Production: In response to hypoxia, kidneys secrete EPO to stimulate RBC production (long-term).
28.
Describe how hydrochloric acid is formed in the stomach (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Answer: Mechanism in Parietal Cells:

  1. H⁺ Formation: CO₂ + H₂O react (catalysed by Carbonic Anhydrase) to form H₂CO₃, which dissociates into H⁺ and HCO₃⁻.
  2. H⁺ Secretion: H⁺ is pumped into the stomach lumen by the H⁺-K⁺ ATPase pump (proton pump) in exchange for K⁺ entering the cell.
  3. HCO₃⁻ Absorption: HCO₃⁻ is transported into the blood in exchange for Cl⁻ (Chloride shift).
  4. Cl⁻ Secretion: Cl⁻ diffuses from the cell into the lumen through chloride channels.
  5. HCl Formation: H⁺ and Cl⁻ combine in the canaliculi/lumen to form Hydrochloric Acid.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions (40 Marks)

29.
Mrs. Muturi is a known hyperthyroidism patient currently on treatment and on follow up at Kadogo Hospital. She recently experienced a thyroid storm and presented in casualty in critical condition.
a. Describe how thyroxine (T4) and tri-iodothyronine (T3) are synthesized in the thyroid gland (10 Marks)
b. In relation to T4 and T3 hormone action, describe the likely features Mrs. Muturi could have presented with in the casualty (10 Marks)
Show Answer

a. Synthesis of T3 and T4:

  1. Iodide Trapping: Active transport of Iodide (I⁻) from blood into follicular cells via Na⁺-I⁻ Symporter (NIS).
  2. Synthesis of Thyroglobulin: Follicular cells produce thyroglobulin (Tgb) protein and secrete it into the colloid.
  3. Oxidation of Iodide: Iodide is oxidized to Iodine ($I_2$) by Thyroid Peroxidase (TPO) at the apical membrane.
  4. Organification (Iodination): Iodine binds to tyrosine residues on thyroglobulin to form Monoiodotyrosine (MIT) and Diiodotyrosine (DIT).
  5. Coupling:
    • MIT + DIT = Triiodothyronine (T3)
    • DIT + DIT = Thyroxine (T4)
  6. Storage: Hormones remain attached to Tgb in the colloid.
  7. Release: Upon TSH stimulation, colloid is endocytosed, Tgb is proteolyzed, and T3/T4 are released into blood.

b. Features of Thyroid Storm (Severe Hyperthyroidism):

Due to excessive metabolic stimulation by T3/T4:

  • High Fever (Hyperpyrexia): Often >40°C due to massively increased BMR and heat production.
  • Cardiovascular: Tachycardia (rapid heart rate, >140 bpm), arrhythmias (atrial fibrillation), palpitations, hypertension (widened pulse pressure), potential heart failure.
  • CNS: Severe agitation, anxiety, confusion, delirium, psychosis, or coma. tremors.
  • Gastrointestinal: Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, jaundice.
  • General: Profuse sweating (diaphoresis), warm moist skin, severe weakness/muscle wasting.
30.
Nervous system is a complex network in human body that allows one to interact with the environment. Describe;
a. Functional organization of nervous system (6 Marks)
b. Any four properties of a nerve (4 Marks)
c. Synthesis, flow and drainage of cerebral spinal fluid (10 Marks)
Show Answer

a. Functional Organization of Nervous System:

  • Sensory Division (Afferent): Transmits information from receptors to the CNS.
    • Somatic sensory (skin, muscle)
    • Visceral sensory (organs)
  • Integrative Division (CNS): The Brain and Spinal Cord process, store, and decide on responses.
  • Motor Division (Efferent): Transmits commands from CNS to effectors.
    • Somatic Nervous System: Voluntary control of skeletal muscles.
    • Autonomic Nervous System: Involuntary control of smooth muscle, cardiac muscle, and glands. Divided into Sympathetic (Fight/Flight) and Parasympathetic (Rest/Digest).

b. Properties of a Nerve:

  1. Excitability: Ability to respond to stimuli by generating an action potential.
  2. Conductivity: Ability to propagate the impulse along the fiber.
  3. Refractory Period: Period during which it cannot be re-excited (Absolute) or needs stronger stimulus (Relative).
  4. All-or-None Law: Responds maximally or not at all to a threshold stimulus.
  5. Adaptation (Accommodation): Decrease in excitability with constant sub-threshold stimulation.

c. CSF Synthesis, Flow, and Drainage:

Synthesis:

  • Formed primarily by the Choroid Plexus (capillary networks covered by ependymal cells) located in the Lateral, Third, and Fourth ventricles.
  • Produced via active secretion and filtration of plasma. Rate approx 500ml/day.

Flow (Circulation):

  • Lateral Ventricles → Foramen of Monro (Interventricular foramen) → Third Ventricle.
  • Third Ventricle → Cerebral Aqueduct (of Sylvius) → Fourth Ventricle.
  • Fourth Ventricle → Foramina of Luschka (Lateral) and Magendie (Medial) → Subarachnoid Space (surrounding Brain and Spinal Cord) and Central Canal.

Drainage (Absorption):

  • Reabsorbed into the venous blood through Arachnoid Granulations (Villi) which project into the dural venous sinuses (primarily Superior Sagittal Sinus).
  • Driven by pressure gradient (CSF pressure > Venous pressure).
