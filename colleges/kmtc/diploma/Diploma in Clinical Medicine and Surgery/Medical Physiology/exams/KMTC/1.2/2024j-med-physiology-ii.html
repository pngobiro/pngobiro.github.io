Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions (20 Marks)

1.
Which of the following statements concerning the general functional role of the cerebellum is correct?
a) The cerebellum directly stimulates motor neurons required to make a movement
b) The cerebellum is unable to make corrective adjustments to the movement once it is performed
c) The cerebellum is not involved in the planning of a movement, only its execution
d) The cerebellum plays an active role in the coordination of the muscles required to make a movement
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) The cerebellum plays an active role in the coordination of the muscles required to make a movement

Explanation: The cerebellum is essential for the coordination, precision, and timing of movements. It receives input from sensory systems and other parts of the brain and fine-tunes motor activity.

  • a) Incorrect: The cerebellum does not directly stimulate motor neurons; it modifies signals in the motor cortex and brainstem.
  • b) Incorrect: It actively makes corrective adjustments (comparator function) during movement.
  • c) Incorrect: It is involved in both planning (cerebrocerebellum) and execution.
2.
The two hemispheres of the brain are connected by which nerve fibers or pathways?
a) Lateral lemniscus
b) Corticofugal fibers
c) Corpus callosum
d) Arcuate fasciculus
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Corpus callosum

Explanation: The corpus callosum is the largest white matter structure in the brain, consisting of millions of axons that connect the left and right cerebral hemispheres, facilitating interhemispheric communication.

3.
Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) provides a cushioning effect both inside and outside the brain. Which of the following spaces lies outside the brain or spinal cord and contains CSF?
a) Lateral ventricle
b) Third ventricle
c) Subarachnoid space
d) Epidural space
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Subarachnoid space

Explanation: The subarachnoid space lies between the arachnoid mater and the pia mater. It surrounds the brain and spinal cord and is filled with CSF, providing buoyancy and protection. Ventricles (a, b) are inside the brain.

5.
Nasal, lacrimal, salivary, and gastrointestinal glands are stimulated by which of the following substances?
a) Acetylcholine
b) Norepinephrine
c) Serotonin
d) Dopamine
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Acetylcholine

Explanation: These glands are primarily stimulated by the parasympathetic nervous system. The postganglionic parasympathetic neurons release Acetylcholine (ACh), which acts on muscarinic receptors to increase secretion.

6.
Which of the following statements accurately describes a feature of temperature sensation by the nervous system?
a) Cold receptors continue to be activated even if skin temperature is lowered well below its freezing point
b) Both cold and warm receptors each have very specific, nonoverlapping ranges of temperature sensitivity
c) Warm and cold receptors respond to both steady state temperatures and to changes in temperature
d) Temperature receptor function is the result of ion conduction changes and not changes in their metabolic rate
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Warm and cold receptors respond to both steady state temperatures and to changes in temperature

Explanation: Thermoreceptors are slowly adapting receptors. They respond to steady temperatures (static response) but are especially sensitive to rapid changes in temperature (dynamic response). When temperature changes rapidly, the receptor firing rate changes drastically before settling to a new steady rate.

7.
Which of the following is the middle ear ossicle that is attached to the tympanic membrane?
a) Incus
b) Malleus
c) Modiolus
d) Stapes
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Malleus

Explanation: The auditory ossicles are arranged in the order: Malleus (hammer), Incus (anvil), Stapes (stirrup). The Malleus is the bone directly attached to the inner surface of the tympanic membrane (eardrum).

Memory Aid: MIS - Malleus (attached to eardrum), Incus (middle), Stapes (attached to oval window).

8.
Seven days after ovulation, pituitary secretion of luteinizing hormone (LH) decreases rapidly. What is the cause of this decrease in secretion?
a) The anterior pituitary gland becomes unresponsive to the stimulatory effect of gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH)
b) Estrogen from the developing follicles exerts a feedback inhibition on the hypothalamus
c) The rise in body temperature inhibits hypothalamic release of GnRH
d) Secretion of estrogen and progesterone by the corpus luteum suppresses hypothalamic secretion of GnRH and pituitary secretion of LH
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Secretion of estrogen and progesterone by the corpus luteum suppresses hypothalamic secretion of GnRH and pituitary secretion of LH

Explanation: During the luteal phase (after ovulation), the corpus luteum secretes high levels of progesterone and estrogen. These hormones exert a negative feedback effect on the hypothalamus and anterior pituitary, suppressing the release of FSH and LH to prevent new follicle maturation while the uterus prepares for potential implantation.

9.
Which of the following statements about antidiuretic hormone (ADH) is true?
a) It is synthesized in the posterior pituitary gland
b) It increases salt and water reabsorption in the collecting tubules and ducts
c) It stimulates thirst
d) It has opposite effects on urine and plasma osmolality
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) It increases salt and water reabsorption in the collecting tubules and ducts

Correction Note: Technically, ADH primarily increases water reabsorption by inserting aquaporins. Aldosterone handles salt. However, in this context, option B is the intended answer regarding kidney function. Option D is also technically true (decreases plasma osmolarity, increases urine osmolarity), but B is the direct mechanism of action often tested.

Why A is wrong: ADH is synthesized in the hypothalamus (supraoptic and paraventricular nuclei), stored in the posterior pituitary.

10.
Exposure to UV light directly facilitates which of the following?
a) Conversion of cholesterol to 25-hydroxycholecalciferol
b) Conversion of 25-hydroxycholecalciferol to 1,25-dihydroxycholecalciferol
c) Transport of calcium into the extracellular fluid
d) Storage of vitamin D3 in the liver
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Conversion of cholesterol to 25-hydroxycholecalciferol

Explanation: While the specific biochemical step is the conversion of 7-dehydrocholesterol (a cholesterol derivative in the skin) to cholecalciferol (Vitamin D3), option A is the closest fit describing the initial synthesis step triggered by UV light. The subsequent hydroxylation steps occur in the liver (25-OH) and kidney (1,25-OH).

11.
Parathyroid hormone directly:
a) controls the rate of 25-hydroxycholecalciferol formation
b) controls the rate of calcium transport in the mucosa of the small intestine
c) controls the rate of formation of calcium binding protein
d) controls the rate of formation of 1, 25-dihydroxycholecalciferol
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) controls the rate of formation of 1, 25-dihydroxycholecalciferol

Explanation: PTH stimulates the enzyme 1$\alpha$-hydroxylase in the kidneys. This enzyme converts 25-hydroxycholecalciferol into 1,25-dihydroxycholecalciferol (the active form of Vitamin D). Active Vitamin D then increases calcium absorption in the gut.

12.
During the 12-hr period preceding ovulation, which of the following is true?
a) The plasma concentration of estrogen is rising
b) A surge of luteinizing hormone is secreted from the pituitary
c) A fall in the plasma concentration of progesterone
d) The number of developing follicles is increasing
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) A surge of luteinizing hormone is secreted from the pituitary

Explanation: The LH surge is the primary trigger for ovulation. It occurs approximately 24-36 hours before ovulation, peaking about 10-12 hours prior. This surge induces the rupture of the Graafian follicle.

13.
Very early in embryonic development, testosterone is formed within the male embryo. What is the function at this stage of development?
a) Stimulation of bone growth
b) Stimulation of development of male sex organs
c) Stimulation of development of skeletal muscle
d) Inhibition of luteinizing hormone secretion
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Stimulation of development of male sex organs

Explanation: In the male embryo, testosterone (secreted by Leydig cells under the influence of hCG) is responsible for the differentiation of the Wolffian ducts into the male internal genital tract (epididymis, vas deferens, seminal vesicles) and, via dihydrotestosterone, the external genitalia.

14.
Which of the following is produced by the trophoblast cells during the first 3 weeks of pregnancy?
a) Estrogen
b) Luteinizing hormone
c) Oxytocin
d) Human chorionic gonadotropin
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG)

Explanation: Trophoblast cells (part of the developing placenta) secrete hCG. This hormone "rescues" the corpus luteum, maintaining progesterone secretion to support the pregnancy until the placenta takes over.

15.
Which of the following is filtered most readily by the glomerular capillaries?
a) Albumin in plasma
b) Polycationic dextran with a molecular weight of 25,000
c) Polyanionic dextran with a molecular weight of 25,000
d) Red blood cells
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Polycationic dextran with a molecular weight of 25,000

Explanation: Glomerular filtration depends on size and charge. The glomerular basement membrane is negatively charged.

  • Size: Smaller molecules filter easier.
  • Charge: Positive (cationic) molecules filter more easily than neutral or negative (anionic) ones because they are attracted to the negative basement membrane.

Therefore, a cationic (positive) molecule filters more readily than an anionic (negative) one of the same size.

16.
At which renal tubular sites would the concentration of creatinine be expected to be highest in a normally hydrated person?
a) End of the proximal tubule
b) Distal tubule
c) Collecting duct
d) The concentration would be the same in all of the above, since creatinine is neither secreted or reabsorbed
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Collecting duct

Explanation: Creatinine is freely filtered and minimally secreted, but virtually not reabsorbed. As filtrate moves through the nephron, water is reabsorbed (approx 99% total). As water leaves, the remaining solutes that aren't reabsorbed (like creatinine) become increasingly concentrated. Therefore, the concentration is highest at the end of the system (collecting duct).

17.
Which of the following changes would you expect to find in a dehydrated person deprived of water for 24 hours?
a) Decreased plasma renin activity
b) Decreased plasma antidiuretic hormone concentration
c) Increased plasma atrial natriuretic peptide concentration
d) Increased water permeability of the collecting duct
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Increased water permeability of the collecting duct

Explanation: Dehydration leads to increased plasma osmolarity. This triggers the release of ADH (vasopressin). ADH acts on the collecting duct cells to insert aquaporins, increasing water permeability and reabsorption to conserve water.

18.
Which of the following is not normally found in abundance in the portal blood?
a) Amino acids
b) Glucose
c) Short-chain fatty acids
d) Triglycerides
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Triglycerides

Explanation: Most lipids (Long-chain fatty acids and monoglycerides) are re-esterified into triglycerides within enterocytes and packaged into chylomicrons. These enter the lymphatic system (lacteals), bypassing the portal vein. Amino acids, glucose, and short-chain fatty acids enter the portal blood directly.

a) Contract, contract, contract, contract
b) Contract, contract, relax, relax
c) Relax, contract, contract, relax
d) Relax, relax, contract, contract

Section 2: Short Answer Questions

1.
Outline the function(s) of the five (5) types of neuroglia cells. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Complete Answer:

  1. Astrocytes (CNS): Form the blood-brain barrier (BBB), provide structural support, and regulate the ionic environment (K+ buffering).
  2. Oligodendrocytes (CNS): Form myelin sheaths around axons in the Central Nervous System, insulating them to increase conduction speed.
  3. Microglia (CNS): Act as the immune defense cells (phagocytes) of the CNS, removing debris and pathogens.
  4. Ependymal Cells (CNS): Line the ventricles of the brain and central canal of the spinal cord; participate in the production and circulation of Cerebrospinal Fluid (CSF).
  5. Schwann Cells (PNS): Form myelin sheaths around axons in the Peripheral Nervous System and aid in axon regeneration.
2.
Identify the steps of conduction of an impulse across a synapse. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Sequential Steps:

  1. Arrival of Action Potential: The impulse reaches the synaptic knob (axon terminal).
  2. Calcium Influx: Voltage-gated Calcium channels open, causing Ca2+ to enter the presynaptic terminal.
  3. Vesicle Fusion: Calcium triggers synaptic vesicles containing neurotransmitters to fuse with the presynaptic membrane.
  4. Neurotransmitter Release: Neurotransmitters are released into the synaptic cleft via exocytosis.
  5. Receptor Binding: Neurotransmitters diffuse across the cleft and bind to specific receptors on the postsynaptic membrane, causing ion channels to open and generating a postsynaptic potential.
3.
Describe the steps involved in the perception of the sense of taste and nerves involved. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Process of Taste Perception:

  1. Dissolution: Chemical substance (tastant) dissolves in saliva.
  2. Receptor Activation: Tastant enters taste pores and binds to receptors on gustatory hair cells.
  3. Depolarization: Receptor potential is generated, triggering neurotransmitter release.
  4. Nerve Transmission: Impulse is transmitted via Cranial Nerves:
    • Facial (CN VII): Anterior 2/3 of tongue.
    • Glossopharyngeal (CN IX): Posterior 1/3 of tongue.
    • Vagus (CN X): Epiglottis/Pharynx.
  5. Cortical Processing: Signal travels to the medulla (nucleus tractus solitarius), thalamus, and finally the primary gustatory cortex in the insula/parietal lobe.
4.
Explain three (3) mechanisms by which hormone secretion is regulated. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Mechanisms:

  1. Humoral Stimuli: Secretion in response to changing blood levels of ions and nutrients (e.g., PTH released when blood Calcium is low; Insulin released when blood glucose is high).
  2. Neural Stimuli: Nerve fibers stimulate hormone release (e.g., Sympathetic nervous system stimulates adrenal medulla to release Catecholamines).
  3. Hormonal Stimuli: Hormones stimulate other endocrine organs to release their hormones (e.g., Hypothalamic hormones stimulate Anterior Pituitary, which stimulates Thyroid/Adrenal/Gonads).

Note: Most of these operate under negative feedback loops.

8.
Briefly describe the accessory glands of the gastrointestinal system. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Accessory Organs:

  1. Salivary Glands (Parotid, Submandibular, Sublingual): Secrete saliva containing amylase for carbohydrate digestion.

1.
Tabulate the changes that take place to the female body during puberty (10) and after menopause (10). (20 marks)

Part A: Changes During Puberty (Driven by Estrogen/Progesterone)

System/Area Changes
Reproductive Organs Enlargement of uterus, vagina, and ovaries. Onset of Menstruation (Menarche).

System/Area Changes
Reproductive System Cessation of menstruation. Atrophy (thinning) of vaginal and uterine lining. Vaginal dryness.
Skeletal System Accelerated bone loss (Osteoporosis) due to lack of estrogen's protective effect on bone density.
Cardiovascular Increased risk of atherosclerosis and heart disease (loss of lipid profile protection). Vasomotor instability (Hot flashes).
Integumentary Thinning of skin, loss of elasticity, and wrinkling.
Psychological Mood swings, irritability, or depression often associated with hormonal fluctuations.
2.
Identify and describe how digestion and absorption of lipids in humans occur in sequential stages. (20 marks)
Show Answer

Step-by-Step Lipid Digestion & Absorption:

1. Emulsification (Stomach & Small Intestine)

  • Action: Mechanical breakdown of large fat globules into smaller droplets.
  • Agents: Bile salts (from liver/gallbladder) and lecithin.
  • Purpose: Increases surface area for enzymatic action.

2. Enzymatic Digestion (Small Intestine)

  • Enzyme: Pancreatic Lipase (with colipase).
  • Reaction: Triglycerides $\rightarrow$ 2 Free Fatty Acids + 2-Monoglyceride.

3. Micelle Formation

  • Process: Bile salts surround the fatty acids and monoglycerides to form water-soluble micelles.
  • Function: Ferries lipids through the unstirred water layer to the brush border of enterocytes.

4. Absorption (Diffusion)

  • Micelles contact the epithelial membrane.
  • Lipids diffuse out of the micelle and enter the enterocyte (bile salts remain in the lumen to be reabsorbed later in the ileum).

5. Re-esterification (Inside Enterocyte)

  • Inside the smooth Endoplasmic Reticulum, fatty acids and monoglycerides are reassembled back into Triglycerides.

6. Chylomicron Formation

  • Triglycerides are packaged with cholesterol, phospholipids, and proteins (Apo B-48) to form Chylomicrons.

7. Exocytosis & Transport

  • Chylomicrons are too large for capillaries.
  • They are released via exocytosis into the Lacteals (lymphatic capillaries).
  • They travel via the Thoracic Duct to enter the venous blood supply.
