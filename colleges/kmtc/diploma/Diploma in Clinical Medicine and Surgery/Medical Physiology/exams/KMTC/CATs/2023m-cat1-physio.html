Section A: Short Answer Questions (10 Marks)

1.
Define the internal environment (1 mark) give the distribution of the water that it contains (2 marks) and two reasons why it is important (2 marks).
Show Answer

Definition:

The internal environment refers to the Extracellular Fluid (ECF) that surrounds and bathes the cells of the body. It is the medium in which cells live.

Distribution of Water in ECF:

The ECF makes up approximately 20% of body weight (or 1/3 of Total Body Water). It is distributed as:

  • Interstitial Fluid: ~75% (or 3/4) of ECF.
  • Plasma: ~25% (or 1/4) of ECF.
  • Transcellular fluid: (small amounts e.g., CSF, synovial fluid).

Importance:

  1. Nutrient Delivery: Provides cells with essential nutrients (glucose, amino acids) and oxygen needed for metabolism.
  2. Waste Removal: Acts as a medium for the removal of metabolic waste products (CO2, urea) from the cells.
  3. Homeostasis: Maintains stable physical and chemical conditions (pH, temperature, ion concentration) necessary for optimal cellular function.
2.
Define membrane transport (1 mark) and list all the main types of membrane transport which are categorized as bulk transport (4 marks).
Show Answer

Definition:

Membrane transport is the movement of particles (solutes, ions, water, or large molecules) across or through a cell membrane.

Types of Bulk Transport (Vesicular Transport):

  1. Endocytosis: Transport into the cell.
    • Phagocytosis (Cell eating)
    • Pinocytosis (Cell drinking)
    • Receptor-mediated endocytosis
  2. Exocytosis: Transport out of the cell (Secretion/Excretion).

Section B: Restricted Long Answer Question (20 Marks)

3.
Discuss homeostasis under the following sub-titles:
a) Definition (1 mark).
b) Components of a homeostatic system (3 marks).
c) Functions of each component. Give at least ONE function for each (3 marks).
d) Five conditions in the body that are regulated through homeostasis (5 marks).
e) Mechanisms of homeostasis (4 marks).
f) The role of the hypothalamus in the regulation of plasma volume (4 marks).
Show Answer

a) Definition:

Homeostasis is the maintenance of a relatively constant/stable internal environment (ECF) despite fluctuations in the external environment.

b) Components of a homeostatic system:

  1. Receptor (Sensor)
  2. Control Center (Integrator)
  3. Effector

c) Functions of each component:

  • Receptor: Detects deviations or changes in a physiological variable (stimulus) and sends an input signal to the control center.
  • Control Center: Receives input, compares it to the "set point" (normal range), determines the appropriate response, and sends output signals.
  • Effector: Receives output from the control center and produces a response that alters the controlled variable (correcting the error).

d) Five conditions regulated through homeostasis:

  1. Body Temperature (Thermoregulation)
  2. Blood Glucose Concentration
  3. Blood pH (Acid-Base Balance)
  4. Blood Pressure
  5. Fluid and Electrolyte Balance (Osmolality, Na+, K+, Ca2+ levels)
  6. Blood Gas Partial Pressures (pO2, pCO2)

e) Mechanisms of homeostasis:

  1. Negative Feedback Mechanism: The most common mechanism where the response opposes or reverses the initial disturbance (e.g., insulin lowering high blood sugar). It restores stability.
  2. Positive Feedback Mechanism: The response amplifies or reinforces the initial change (e.g., Oxytocin in childbirth, blood clotting). This drives a process to completion rather than maintaining stability.

Note: Feedforward mechanism (anticipatory control) is also a valid homeostatic mechanism.

f) The role of the hypothalamus in the regulation of plasma volume:

  • Detection: Osmoreceptors in the hypothalamus detect increased plasma osmolality (associated with low water volume/dehydration).
  • Synthesis of ADH: The supraoptic and paraventricular nuclei synthesize Antidiuretic Hormone (ADH).
  • Secretion: ADH is released from the posterior pituitary gland into the blood.
  • Kidney Action: ADH acts on the collecting ducts of the kidney to increase water reabsorption, reducing urine loss and conserving plasma volume.
  • Thirst Mechanism: The hypothalamus stimulates the thirst center, compelling the individual to drink water, thereby restoring volume.
