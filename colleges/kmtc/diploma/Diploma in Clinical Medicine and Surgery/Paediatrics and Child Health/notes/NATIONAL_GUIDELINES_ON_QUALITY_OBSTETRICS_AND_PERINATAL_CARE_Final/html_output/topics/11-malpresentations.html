MALPRESENTATIONS AND MALPOSITIONS

BREECH PRESENTATION

It accounts for 3.5% of term singleton deliveries and about 25% of cases before 30 weeks of gestation as most cases undergo spontaneous cephalic version up to term. It is more common in preterm deliveries. In breech presentation, labour and birth carry increased risk of morbidity and mortality compared to cephalic presentation. Caesarean delivery is often recommended as a safer method of birth for breech presentation, but carries risk for the mother both immediately and in future pregnancies.

External Cephalic Version (ECV), a procedure to turn the foetus from a breech to a cephalic presentation, can reduce the incidence of breech presentation in labour and should be offered to some mothers late in the pregnancy.

Recommendations for breech presentation

RECOMMENDATION LEVEL OF EVIDENCE
Women with a breech presentation at term should be offered external cephalic version (ECV) unless there is an absolute contraindication. They should be advised on the risks and benefits of ECV and the implications for mode of delivery A
Women who have a breech presentation at term following an unsuccessful or declined offer of ECV should be counselled on the risks and benefits of planned vaginal breech delivery versus planned caesarean section. A
Women should be informed that planned caesarean section leads to a small reduction in perinatal mortality compared with planned vaginal breech delivery. Any decision to perform a caesarean section needs to be balanced against the potential adverse consequences that may result from this. A
Women should be informed that routine caesarean section for breech presentation in spontaneous preterm labour is not recommended. The mode of delivery should be individualised based on the stage of labour, type of breech presentation, foetal wellbeing and availability of an operator skilled in vaginal breech delivery. C
Women should be informed that the evidence is limited, but that planned caesarean section for a twin pregnancy where the presenting twin is breech is recommended C

Types of Breech

  • Complete breech: The feet present beside the buttocks as both knees and hips are flexed. More common in multipara.
  • Frank breech: Accounts for approximately 85% of all breech babies. It is breech with extended legs where the knees are extended while the hips are flexed. More common in primigravida.
  • Footling presentation: The hip and knee joints are extended on one or both sides. More common in preterm singleton breeches.
  • Knee presentation: The hip is partially extended and the knee is flexed on one or both sides.

External Cephalic Version (ECV)

Timing: this should be conducted at 36 weeks and in facilities able to provide comprehensive obstetric care. Success rate: 50-70%.

Contraindications: Contracted pelvis, Multiple pregnancy, Hydrocephalus, Antepartum haemorrhage, Uterine scar, Hypertension, Elderly primigravida, Ruptured membranes.

Vaginal Breech Delivery

Prerequisites: Frank breech, Estimated foetal weight not more than 3.75 kg, Gestational age: 36-42 weeks, Flexed head, Adequate pelvis, Normal progress of labour, Uncomplicated pregnancy, Multiparas, An experienced obstetrician.

TRANSVERSE LIE

Transverse lie refers to a foetal presentation in which the foetal longitudinal axis lies perpendicular to the long axis of the uterus. It constitutes an absolute foeto-pelvic disproportion, and vaginal delivery is impossible. This is an obstetric emergency.

Interventions

At the end of pregnancy: External version 4 to 6 weeks before delivery, in a CEmONC facility. If this fails, delivery should be carried out by caesarian section.

During labour, in a CEmONC facility:

  • Fetus alive, membranes intact: Gentle external version. If fails, caesarian section.
  • Fetus alive, membranes ruptured: Complete dilation (Internal version and total breech extraction for multipara/relaxed uterus; Caesarean for others). Incomplete dilation (Caesarean section).
  • Fetus dead: Embryotomy.

Internal Version

Manual intrauterine procedure to convert one presentation to another, usually a transverse lie into a breech.

UNSTABLE LIE OF THE FOETUS

Unstable lie refers to the frequenly changing of fetal lie and presentation in late pregnancy beyond 37 weeks. Diagnosis is usually made when a varying foetal lie is found on repeated clinical examination in the last month of pregnancy.

General principles

  • 85% of foetal lies will become longitudinal before rupture of the membranes or labour
  • Hospital admission from 37 weeks onwards is recommended
  • Immediate clinical assistance if membranes rupture or signs of labour
  • May attempt external version to cephalic presentation in early labour with access to facilities for immediate delivery if indicated

Intrapartum management

If SROM occurs, perform vaginal examination to exclude the presence of a cord or malpresentation.

  • If the lie is longitudinal: Normal labour management.
  • If the lie is not longitudinal: Consider external version to correct the lie. CAUTION must be done in a health facility equipped to provide comprehensive Obstetric and neonatal care. A stabilising ARM should be done with caution.
  • If the lie is not longitudinal and cannot be corrected: Emergency Caesarean section.

OCCIPITO- POSTERIOR POSITION

It is a vertex presentation with foetal back directed posteriorly. The incidence is 10% at onset of labour. Right occipito-posterior (ROP) is more common than left occipito-posterior (LOP).

Mechanism of Labour

A certain degree of deflexion is present. Taking in consideration the rule that the part of the foetus that meets the pelvic floor first will rotate anteriorly, the degree of deflexion determines the mechanism of labour as follow:

  • Normal mechanism (90%): Deflexion is corrected, complete flexion occurs. Long anterior rotation 3/8 circle occurs bringing the occiput anteriorly.
  • Abnormal mechanism (10%):
    • Deep transverse arrest (1%): In mild deflexion, the occiput rotates 1/8 circle anteriorly and the head is arrested in the transverse diameter.
    • Persistent occipito-posterior (3%): In moderate deflexion, no internal rotation and the head persists in the oblique diameter.
    • Direct occipito-posterior (face to pubis) (6%): In marked deflexion, the sinciput meets the pelvic floor first, rotates 1/8 circle anteriorly and the occiput becomes direct posterior.

Management of Labour

First stage: Exclude contracted pelvis/cord prolapse. Inertia and prolonged labour are expected. Avoid premature rupture of membranes.

Second stage:

  • Long internal rotation 3/8 circle: occurs in about 90% of cases and delivery is completed as in normal labour.
  • Direct occipito-posterior (face to pubis): Occurs in about 6% of cases. The head can be delivered spontaneously or by aid of outlet forceps. Episiotomy is done.
  • Deep transverse arrest (1%) and persistent occipito-posterior (3%): The labour is obstructed. Options: Vacuum extraction, Manual rotation and extraction by forceps, or Caesarean section.

SHOULDER DYSTOCIA

Shoulder dystocia is the failure of the shoulders to spontaneously deliver following delivery of the baby's head due to impaction of the shoulders behind the symphysis pubis.

Diagnosis

  • Difficulty with delivery of the face and chin
  • Retraction of the chin against the perineum ('turtle-neck sign')
  • Failure of the anterior shoulder to deliver with normal routine traction.
  • Failure of restitution of the head

Management: HELPERR Mnemonic

  • H - Call for help. Multi-disciplinary team.
  • E - Evaluate for episiotomy. Consider to make room for rotation maneuvers.
  • L - Legs (McRobert's maneuver). Flexing and abducting the maternal hips. Resolves >40% of cases.
  • P - Suprapubic pressure. Pressure over fetal anterior shoulder to adduct it.
  • E - Enter maneuvers (internal rotation). Rubin Maneuver (vaginal approach) or Wood's screw maneuver.
  • R - Remove the posterior arm. Shortens the bisacromial diameter.
  • R - Roll the patient on all fours (Gaskin maneuver).
