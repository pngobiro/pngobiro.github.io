Clinical Cases

6.

John aged 1½ years presents to you with a history of dry cough, fever, red conjunctiva and a maculopapular rash starting from the hair line down to the trunk. The child is in respiratory distress.

(a) What is the most likely diagnosis? (2 Marks)

(b) Outline the management of this child. (6 Marks)

(c) Enumerate 4 early complications in the above child. (2 Marks)

Show Answer

The clinical triad of cough, coryza, and conjunctivitis ("3 Cs"), followed by a maculopapular rash starting from the hairline, is pathognomonic for Measles.

(a) Most Likely Diagnosis

Measles (Rubeola) complicated by Pneumonia (indicated by respiratory distress).

(b) Management

  1. Isolation: Isolate the child to prevent spread to other children.
  2. Vitamin A Supplementation: Immediate administration is crucial to reduce severity and mortality.
    • Dose for >1 year: 200,000 IU orally stat.
    • Repeat the same dose the following day (Day 2).
  3. Treat Complications (Pneumonia):
    • Administer antibiotics (e.g., IV Crystalline Penicillin or Ceftriaxone) due to respiratory distress.
    • Oxygen therapy if saturation is <90% or cyanosed.
  4. Supportive Care:
    • Antipyretics: Paracetamol for fever.
    • Fluids/Nutrition: Encourage breastfeeding and increased fluid intake. Clean eyes with warm water if discharging.
  5. Monitoring: Monitor respiratory rate and temperature.

(c) Early Complications

  • Pneumonia (Bronchopneumonia)
  • Otitis Media (Ear infection)
  • Laryngotracheobronchitis (Croup)
  • Diarrhea and Dehydration
  • Stomatitis (Mouth ulcers)
7.

A child aged 5 months presents with a history of difficulty in breathing, mild cough and swelling of the body. On examination he has severe pallor and the Hb is 3 gm/dl. There is no obvious bleeding.

(a) What is your impression?

(b) Outline your plan of management.

Show Answer

The child presents with severe anemia (Hb 3g/dl) leading to High Output Cardiac Failure (evidenced by difficulty breathing/dyspnea and body swelling/edema).

(a) Impression

Severe Anaemia with Congestive Cardiac Failure (CCF).

Possible underlying causes at 5 months: Nutritional anemia (Iron deficiency), Malaria (if endemic area), or Sickle Cell Disease.

(b) Plan of Management

  1. Admission: Admit immediately as this is a life-threatening emergency.
  2. Blood Transfusion:
    • Type: Packed Red Blood Cells (PRBCs) are preferred to minimize volume overload.
    • Dose: 10 ml/kg of packed cells.
    • Diuretic Cover: Administer IV Furosemide (1mg/kg) before or during transfusion to prevent fluid overload and worsening failure.
    • Rate: Transfuse slowly (over 4 hours).
  3. Oxygen Therapy: Administer oxygen if the child is in respiratory distress.
  4. Treat Underlying Cause:
    • Blood slide for malaria (treat if positive).
    • Sickle cell screening later.
    • Nutritional support after stabilization.
  5. Hematinics: Start Folic Acid and Iron supplementation upon discharge/stabilization (not during acute infection/inflammation).
8.

A 4 year old child presents with a history of dry cough, vomiting worms and the temperature is 36.9°C.

(a) What is your impression? (2 Marks)

(b) What complications do you anticipate from the above condition? (3 Marks)

(c) Preventive measures? (3 Marks)

Show Answer

(a) Impression

Ascariasis (Roundworm infestation). The "vomiting worms" confirms the diagnosis. The dry cough suggests Loeffler's Syndrome (pulmonary migration of larvae).

(b) Complications

  • Intestinal Obstruction: A mass of worms can block the gut (acute abdomen).
  • Malnutrition: Competition for nutrients (Vitamin A, proteins).
  • Biliary Ascariasis: Migration into the bile duct causing obstructive jaundice or cholecystitis.
  • Peritonitis: Due to intestinal perforation (rare but serious).
  • Aspiration: Vomited worms can enter the airway causing asphyxia.

(c) Preventive Measures

  • Hygiene/Sanitation: Proper disposal of feces (use of latrines) to prevent soil contamination.
  • Hand Washing: Before eating and after using the toilet.
  • Food Safety: Washing fruits and vegetables thoroughly before eating (eggs are ingested from contaminated soil/food).
  • Deworming: Regular deworming (Albendazole/Mebendazole) every 6 months for children >1 year.
9.

A 1 year old baby presents with a 2 day history of cough, hotness of the body and difficulty in breathing despite prior antimalarial therapy.

(a) What are the differential diagnoses (Mention 4)? (4 Marks)

(b) What other features would you rely on to support your diagnosis? (2 Marks)

Show Answer

The persistence of symptoms despite antimalarials and the presence of cough/difficulty breathing points primarily to a respiratory pathology.

(a) Differential Diagnoses

  1. Severe Pneumonia: Most likely (Bacterial or Viral).
  2. Bronchiolitis: Common in infants/young children (Viral - RSV).
  3. Foreign Body Aspiration: Sudden onset, but infection can supervene.
  4. Severe Malaria (with respiratory distress): "Acidotic breathing" can mimic pneumonia, though less likely if treated effectively (unless resistant).
  5. Pulmonary Tuberculosis: If history of contact or failure to thrive exists.
  6. Asthma (Acute Exacerbation): If there is a history of wheezing/atopy.

(b) Features supporting diagnosis (Pneumonia)

  • Fast Breathing (Tachypnea): Respiratory rate ≥ 50 breaths/min for a 1-year-old.
  • Chest Indrawing: Lower chest wall moves in when the child breathes in.
  • Auscultation: Presence of crepitations (crackles) or bronchial breath sounds.
  • Fever: Supports infectious etiology.
10.

An 8 year old girl who was previously treated for a sore throat 2 weeks ago presents to you with fever and migratory joint pains.

(a) Outline the revised Duckett Jones criteria for making the above diagnosis. (5 Marks)

(b) What are the complications of the above condition? (2 Marks)

(c) What is the long term management of the above condition?

Show Answer

History of sore throat followed by fever and migratory joint pains is classic for Acute Rheumatic Fever (ARF).

(a) Revised Jones Criteria

Diagnosis requires evidence of preceding Group A Streptococcal infection (e.g., elevated ASOT, positive culture) PLUS: 2 Major OR 1 Major + 2 Minor criteria.

  • Major Criteria:
    1. Carditis: Inflammation of the heart (murmurs, CCF).
    2. Polyarthritis: Migratory, involving large joints.
    3. Sydenham's Chorea: Involuntary movements.
    4. Erythema Marginatum: Rash on trunk/limbs.
    5. Subcutaneous Nodules: Painless lumps over bony prominences.
  • Minor Criteria: Fever, Arthralgia, Elevated ESR/CRP, Prolonged PR interval on ECG.

(b) Complications

  • Rheumatic Heart Disease (RHD): Permanent valve damage (Mitral Stenosis/Regurgitation, Aortic Regurgitation).
  • Heart Failure: Due to severe carditis or valve damage.
  • Infective Endocarditis: Damaged valves are prone to infection.

(c) Long Term Management

  • Secondary Prophylaxis: Benzathine Penicillin G injections every 3-4 weeks to prevent recurrent Strep infections.
  • Duration:
    • Without carditis: For 5 years or until age 21 (whichever is longer).
    • With carditis (mild/resolved): For 10 years or until age 21.
    • With severe valve disease: Lifelong prophylaxis is often recommended.
  • Regular follow-up with Echocardiography.
