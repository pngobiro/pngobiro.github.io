Section 2: Short Answer Questions – (40 Marks)

1.
Classify congenital heart diseases giving 2 examples in each (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Classification of Congenital Heart Diseases (CHD):

  1. Acyanotic CHD (Left-to-Right Shunts):
    • Ventricular Septal Defect (VSD)
    • Atrial Septal Defect (ASD)
    • Patent Ductus Arteriosus (PDA)
  2. Cyanotic CHD (Right-to-Left Shunts):
    • Tetralogy of Fallot (TOF)
    • Transposition of the Great Arteries (TGA)
    • Truncus Arteriosus
  3. Obstructive Lesions (Acyanotic but obstruction to flow):
    • Coarctation of the Aorta
    • Pulmonary Stenosis
    • Aortic Stenosis
2.
State the criteria for diagnosis of child with rheumatic fever (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Revised Jones Criteria:

Diagnosis requires evidence of preceding Group A Streptococcal infection (Positive throat culture, Rapid antigen test, or Elevated ASOT) PLUS either 2 Major criteria OR 1 Major + 2 Minor criteria.

  • Major Criteria:
    1. Carditis (Pancarditis)
    2. Polyarthritis (Migratory)
    3. Chorea (Sydenham's)
    4. Erythema Marginatum
    5. Subcutaneous Nodules
  • Minor Criteria:
    1. Fever
    2. Arthralgia
    3. Elevated acute phase reactants (ESR, CRP)
    4. Prolonged PR interval on ECG
3.
A known child with congenital heart disease developed a fever with migratory polyarthritis. On examination he had a changing murmur. Echocardiogram showed mitral incompetence.
Show Answer

a) What is your impression (1 Mark)

Infective Endocarditis (Subacute Bacterial Endocarditis) - suggested by fever, new/changing murmur, and underlying CHD.

b) Outline your management (4 Marks)

  1. Admission: Admit for inpatient care.
  2. Investigations: Take at least 3 sets of Blood Cultures before starting antibiotics.
  3. Antibiotics: Start high-dose IV antibiotics for 4-6 weeks (e.g., Benzylpenicillin + Gentamicin or Ceftriaxone + Gentamicin) based on sensitivity.
  4. Monitoring: Monitor for heart failure and embolic phenomena.
  5. Surgical Consultation: If severe valve damage, heart failure, or persistent infection.
4.
Describe the management of a child in CCF (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Positioning: Prop up the child (semi-fowler's) to relieve orthopnea.
  2. Oxygen Therapy: Administer oxygen if hypoxic or in respiratory distress.
  3. Diuretics: IV Furosemide (Lasix) 1mg/kg to reduce fluid overload. Spironolactone may be added for chronic management.
  4. Inotropes: Digoxin (if indicated for contractility, loading dose required) or Dopamine/Dobutamine in acute severe failure.
  5. Afterload Reduction: ACE Inhibitors (Captopril/Enalapril) to reduce workload.
  6. General Measures: Fluid restriction (maintenance or restricted), low salt diet, treat precipitating cause (e.g., anemia, infection), monitor vitals and daily weight.
5.
A 1 year old child presents to you with history of diarrhoea and vomiting. On examination she is lethargic, has sunken eyes, reduced skin turgor taking 3 seconds. She weighs 10 kgs. Write a treatment sheet for this patient. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Diagnosis: Severe Dehydration (Plan C).

Treatment Sheet:

  1. IV Fluids: Start IV Ringer's Lactate (or Normal Saline) immediately.
    • Total Volume: 100ml/kg = 100ml x 10kg = 1000ml.
    • Rate (Child 12 months+):
      • First 30%: 30ml/kg (300ml) over 30 minutes.
      • Next 70%: 70ml/kg (700ml) over 2 ½ hours.
  2. Review: Reassess the child every 15-30 minutes. Monitor pulse volume and radial pulse.
  3. Oral Rehydration: Start ORS (5ml/kg/hr) as soon as the child can drink, even while on IV.
  4. Zinc Supplementation: 20mg Tablet OD for 14 days (start once vomiting stops).
  5. Continuing Feeding: Encourage breastfeeding/feeding once rehydrated.
  6. Antibiotics: Not routinely indicated unless Cholera/Dysentery is suspected or severe malnutrition exists.
6.
Outline the clinical & laboratory manifestation of a child with hypertrophic pyloric stenosis (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Clinical Manifestations:

  • Non-bilious projectile vomiting: Usually starts at 2-6 weeks of age, occurs immediately after feeding.
  • "Hungry Vomiter": Baby is eager to feed immediately after vomiting.
  • Visible Peristalsis: Left-to-right wave across the upper abdomen.
  • Palpable Mass: "Olive-shaped" mass in the right upper quadrant (epigastrium).
  • Failure to thrive/Weight loss and Dehydration.

Laboratory Findings:

  • Hypochloremic Hypokalemic Metabolic Alkalosis: Due to loss of gastric acid (HCl).
  • Elevated Urea/Creatinine (pre-renal azotemia from dehydration).
7.
A 5 year old presents to you with history of reduced urine output and passing blood stained urine. On examination you find mild facial swelling and high blood pressure.
Show Answer

a) What is your diagnosis (1 Mark)

Acute Glomerulonephritis (Nephritic Syndrome).

b) Outline your treatment (4 Marks)

  1. Admission: Bed rest.
  2. Fluid Restriction: Limit fluids to insensible loss (400ml/m2) + urine output.
  3. Diet: Salt restriction (Sodium restriction) and Protein restriction (if uremic).
  4. Diuretics: IV Furosemide (1-2mg/kg) to manage edema and fluid overload.
  5. Antihypertensives: Nifedipine or Hydralazine if BP remains high despite diuretics.
  6. Antibiotics: Oral Penicillin V (or Erythromycin) for 10 days to eradicate streptococcal carriage.
8.
A 7 month baby is brought to you with history of refusal to feed, fever and convulsions. On examination he is sick looking prostrated, febrile and severely pale and jaundice. The neck was soft. A blood slide for malaria parasites done is positive. He weighs 8kgs. Write a treatment sheet for this baby. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Diagnosis: Severe Malaria (Features: Prostration, Convulsions, Severe Anemia/Pallor, Jaundice).

Treatment Sheet:

  1. Admit to ward. Triage as Emergency.
  2. Hypoglycemia Management: Check RBS. If low (<3mmol/L), give 10% Dextrose 5ml/kg (40ml) IV bolus.
  3. Anticonvulsant: Rectal Diazepam 0.5mg/kg (4mg) or IV Diazepam 0.3mg/kg (2.4mg) to control convulsions.
  4. Antimalarial: IV Artesunate 3mg/kg (24mg) at 0, 12, and 24 hours. Then once daily until oral tolerance.
  5. Blood Transfusion: Transfuse Packed Red Cells 10ml/kg (80ml) or Whole Blood 20ml/kg (160ml) for severe anemia. Premedicate with Furosemide 1mg/kg.
  6. Supportive: Paracetamol 10-15mg/kg (80-120mg) for fever. Maintenance fluids.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions – (40 Marks)

1.
A 5 year old girl presents to you with generalised body swelling that was incidious in onset, started with periorbital oedema then generalized. A urinalysis done showed proteinuria > 200mg/mmol.
Show Answer

a) What is your impression (2 Marks)

Nephrotic Syndrome.

b) Outline your investigations and expected results (8 Marks)

  1. Urinalysis: Massive proteinuria (3+ or 4+), Hyaline casts. No/microscopic hematuria.
  2. Serum Albumin: Low (<25 g/L) - Hypoalbuminemia.
  3. Lipid Profile: Elevated Cholesterol and Triglycerides (Hyperlipidemia).
  4. Urea, Electrolytes, Creatinine (UECs): Usually normal renal function, but Creatinine may be elevated if hypovolemic. Sodium may be low (dilutional).
  5. Full Haemogram: High Hb/Hct (hemoconcentration), Elevated platelets.
  6. Complement Levels (C3/C4): Normal (differentiates from Nephritic/AGN).
  7. 24hr Urine Protein: >40mg/m2/hr (Nephrotic range).

c) Manage this patient (10 Marks)

  1. Admission: Admit for initial workup and monitoring.
  2. Diet: "No added salt" diet. Normal protein intake (do not restrict or supplement excessively). Fluid restriction only if severe edema/hyponatremia.
  3. Monitoring: Daily weight, daily urine dipstick for protein, BP monitoring, Input/Output chart.
  4. Edema Control:
    • Mild: Salt restriction.
    • Severe: IV Furosemide (1-2mg/kg) +/- Albumin infusion (if albumin very low and symptomatic).
  5. Infection Prophylaxis: Oral Penicillin V or Erythromycin due to loss of immunoglobulins. Treat active infections aggressively.
  6. Definitive Therapy (Steroids):
    • Prednisolone 60mg/m2/day (or 2mg/kg/day) usually max 60mg, given as a single morning dose for 4-6 weeks (Induction).
    • Followed by 40mg/m2/day alternate days for another 4-6 weeks (Tapering).
  7. Gastric Protection: H2 blockers (Ranitidine) or PPIs while on high dose steroids.
  8. Calcium/Vitamin D: Supplementation to prevent bone loss from steroids.
2.

a) Classify Anaemias (4 Marks)

b) State the causes (6 Marks)

c) Outline the management of a child with sickle cell anaemia (10 Marks)

Show Answer

a) Classify Anaemias (4 Marks)

Anemia can be classified morphologically (based on MCV):

  1. Microcytic Hypochromic: (MCV < 80 fL) e.g., Iron deficiency, Thalassemia.
  2. Normocytic Normochromic: (MCV 80-100 fL) e.g., Acute blood loss, Anemia of chronic disease.
  3. Macrocytic: (MCV > 100 fL) e.g., Megaloblastic (B12/Folate def).
  4. Hemolytic: (Based on mechanism) e.g., Sickle Cell, G6PD.

b) State the causes (6 Marks)

  1. Decreased Production:
    • Nutritional deficiencies (Iron, B12, Folate).
    • Bone marrow failure (Aplastic anemia, Leukemia).
    • Chronic infection/disease.
  2. Increased Destruction (Hemolysis):
    • Intrinsic: Sickle Cell Disease, G6PD deficiency, Spherocytosis.
    • Extrinsic: Malaria, Autoimmune hemolytic anemia.
  3. Blood Loss:
    • Acute: Trauma, Surgery.
    • Chronic: Hookworm infestation, Peptic ulcer, GI bleeding.

c) Outline the management of a child with sickle cell anaemia (10 Marks)

Acute Crisis Management:

  • Hydration: IV fluids at 1.5x maintenance to reduce blood viscosity. Encourage oral intake.
  • Analgesia: Aggressive pain control (Step ladder: Paracetamol/NSAIDs -> Opioids like Morphine) for vaso-occlusive crisis.
  • Oxygen: If SpO2 < 92% or respiratory distress (Acute Chest Syndrome).
  • Antibiotics: Treat infections aggressively (e.g., Ceftriaxone) as they precipitate crises.
  • Blood Transfusion: For severe anemia (Hb < 5g/dl), splenic sequestration, or Acute Chest Syndrome.

Chronic/Preventive Management:

  • Hydroxyurea: To increase HbF and reduce crisis frequency.
  • Prophylaxis: Penicillin V daily (until age 5) to prevent pneumococcal sepsis. Folic acid daily (5mg) for RBC production.
  • Malaria Prevention: ITNs and Prophylaxis.
  • Vaccination: Standard KEPI + Pneumococcal, Meningococcal, Influenza vaccines.
  • Education: Avoid triggers (cold, dehydration, high altitude).
