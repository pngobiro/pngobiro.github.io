1.

A one year old child is brought to pediatric out patient clinic with a three day history of diarrhea and vomiting. On examination the child is lethargic, has cold hands and feet. Capillary refill is >3 seconds and has weak pulse. Body weight is 10 kg and has no edema.

(a) What is your diagnosis and what are the possible causative agents? (3 marks)

(b) Outline your plan of management of this patient for the next 24 hours giving details of dosages and duration of treatment. (7 marks)

Show Answer

The clinical presentation of lethargy, cold extremities, prolonged capillary refill (>3s), and weak pulse indicates shock due to severe dehydration.

(a) Diagnosis and Causative Agents

  • Diagnosis: Severe Dehydration with Shock (Hypovolemic Shock) secondary to Acute Gastroenteritis.
  • Possible Causative Agents:
    • Viral: Rotavirus (most common in this age group), Adenovirus.
    • Bacterial: E. coli, Shigella, Salmonella, Campylobacter jejuni.
    • Parasitic: Giardia lamblia, Entamoeba histolytica (less likely to cause acute shock without dysentery).

(b) Management Plan (Next 24 Hours)

  1. Resuscitation (Treatment of Shock):
    • Administer IV Fluids immediately (Ringer's Lactate or Normal Saline).
    • Dose: 20ml/kg bolus. For 10kg child = 200 ml as a bolus over 15-20 minutes.
    • Reassess after the bolus. Repeat bolus up to 2 times if signs of shock persist.
  2. Rehydration (Plan C):
    • Once circulation is restored, switch to Plan C for severe dehydration.
    • Total Volume: 100ml/kg = 1000 ml.
    • Age 12 months (Infant schedule):
      • First 30ml/kg (300 ml) over 1 hour.
      • Next 70ml/kg (700 ml) over 5 hours.
    • Note: If treated as a "child" (>12 months), WHO guidelines suggest 30ml/kg over 30 mins and 70ml/kg over 2.5 hours. Given "one year old", the infant rate is safer.
  3. Monitoring:
    • Monitor pulse, respiratory rate, and capillary refill every 15-30 minutes during rehydration.
    • Check blood glucose to rule out hypoglycemia. Treat with 5ml/kg of 10% Dextrose if low.
  4. Maintenance & Oral Rehydration:
    • Start ORS (5ml/kg/hr) as soon as the child can drink, even while on IV fluids.
    • Provide Zinc supplementation: 20mg orally once daily for 14 days.
  5. Antibiotics:
    • Indicated if septicemia is suspected or confirmed dysentery/cholera. E.g., IV Ceftriaxone.
  6. Nutrition:
    • Continue breastfeeding throughout. Encourage soft foods once rehydrated.
2.

Define and classify anemia. (4 marks)

(a) What laboratory test would you do to diagnose the different classes of anemia? (2 marks)

(b) Outline management of anemia in a child with severe acute malnutrition. (4 marks)

Show Answer

Definition: Anemia is a condition characterized by a hemoglobin (Hb) concentration or red blood cell (RBC) count lower than the normal range for the patient's age and sex (typically Hb < 11.0 g/dL in children under 5 years), resulting in reduced oxygen-carrying capacity of the blood.

Classification:

  • Morphological Classification (based on MCV):
    • Microcytic Hypochromic (e.g., Iron deficiency, Thalassemia).
    • Normocytic Normochromic (e.g., Hemorrhage, Hemolysis, Chronic disease).
    • Macrocytic (e.g., B12/Folate deficiency).
  • Etiological Classification:
    • Impaired production (Nutritional deficiencies, bone marrow failure).
    • Increased destruction (Hemolysis: Malaria, Sickle Cell).
    • Blood loss (Acute or chronic hemorrhage).

(a) Laboratory Tests

  • Full Blood Count (FBC/CBC): To assess Hb, MCV, MCH, MCHC.
  • Peripheral Blood Film (PBF): To view cell morphology (microcytic, macrocytic, sickling).
  • Reticulocyte Count: To distinguish between production failure (low) and hemolysis/loss (high).
  • Hb Electrophoresis: For Sickle Cell Disease.
  • Iron Studies (Ferritin): For iron deficiency.
  • Stool microscopy: For hookworm infestation.

(b) Management in Severe Acute Malnutrition (SAM)

CRITICAL NOTE: Do NOT give iron supplementation in the initial stabilization phase (first 7 days) as free iron promotes bacterial growth and worsens sepsis in SAM.

  1. Blood Transfusion: Only indicated if:
    • Hb < 4 g/dL.
    • Hb < 6 g/dL with signs of respiratory distress/cardiac failure.
    • Use packed cells (10ml/kg) slowly over 3-4 hours with diuretic cover (Furosemide).
  2. Treat Underlying Infection: Administer broad-spectrum antibiotics and antimalarials if smear positive.
  3. Micronutrients:
    • Give Folic Acid 5mg on day 1, then 1mg daily.
    • Vitamin A (unless given recently).
  4. Iron Therapy:
    • Start oral iron ONLY in the rehabilitation phase (usually after 7 days) when the child has regained appetite and infections are treated.
    • Dose: 3mg/kg elemental iron daily.
  5. Deworming: Mebendazole/Albendazole in the rehabilitation phase.
3.

Otieno 4 years old is brought to the hospital with a 4 day history of headache, fever vomiting and anorexia. He was treated at the health centre for malaria and has completed a course of AL (Artemether-Lumefantrine). On examination he is febrile, AVPU is at P (responds only to Pain) and he has one tonic clonic convulsion during the examination.

(a) What are the differential diagnoses? (4 marks)

(b) Which lab tests would help you make a definitive diagnosis? (3 marks)

(c) What is the recommended 1st line treatment for this condition? (3 marks)

Show Answer

The child presents with fever, altered consciousness (Coma scale P), and convulsions despite previous oral antimalarial treatment. This is a medical emergency.

(a) Differential Diagnoses

  1. Severe (Cerebral) Malaria: Possible drug resistance, poor adherence, or vomiting of initial oral medication.
  2. Bacterial Meningitis: Fever, vomiting, headache, convulsions, and altered consciousness are classic signs.
  3. Viral Encephalitis: Inflammation of the brain parenchyma.
  4. Brain Abscess: Space-occupying lesion causing raised ICP.
  5. Severe Hypoglycemia: Often complicates severe malaria or severe infection.

(b) Laboratory Tests

  1. Blood Slide (BS) / RDT for Malaria: To confirm presence of parasites (especially if resistance is suspected).
  2. Lumbar Puncture (CSF Analysis): To evaluate for meningitis (white cells, protein, glucose, gram stain/culture). *Contraindicated if signs of raised ICP are evident, but essential for definitive diagnosis of meningitis.*
  3. Random Blood Glucose (RBG): To rule out hypoglycemia.
  4. Full Blood Count: Leukocytosis suggests bacterial infection.

(c) Recommended 1st Line Treatment

Given the severity (altered consciousness and convulsions), this must be treated as Severe Malaria until proven otherwise, covering for Meningitis if LP is delayed.

  • For Severe Malaria:
    • Intravenous Artesunate: 2.4 mg/kg IV stat, repeat at 12 hours, and 24 hours, then daily until the patient can tolerate oral medication.
    • Alternative: IV Quinine (Loading dose 20mg/kg in 5% dextrose over 4 hours, then 10mg/kg every 8 hours).
  • Supportive:
    • IV Diazepam (0.3mg/kg) for convulsions.
    • Dextrose for hypoglycemia.
4.

You are the clinician on duty at Rabuor health centre when a mother is brought by relatives having delivered at home 2 days ago. When you examine the baby you find a tiny infant weighing 1800g. The baby is cold with bluish lips and a weak cry.

(a) List 4 signs of prematurity. (3 marks)

(b) What life threatening complications is this baby likely to experience? (3 marks)

(c) Outline your plan of management of this patient. (4 marks)

Show Answer

The baby is Low Birth Weight (1800g), Hypothermic ("cold"), and showing signs of Respiratory Distress/Cyanosis ("bluish lips").

(a) Signs of Prematurity

  • Abundant Lanugo hair (fine body hair).
  • Poorly formed ear pinna (soft, slow recoil).
  • Breast nodule absent or very small (<5mm).
  • Genitalia: Undescended testes in males; wide labia minora/prominent clitoris in females.
  • Sole creases: Faint red marks on anterior third only (smooth soles).
  • Skin: Thin, translucent, gelatinous.

(b) Life-Threatening Complications

  1. Hypothermia: Baby is already cold; risk of metabolic acidosis and death.
  2. Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS): Due to surfactant deficiency.
  3. Neonatal Sepsis: High risk due to home delivery.
  4. Hypoglycemia: Low glycogen stores.
  5. Apnea of Prematurity: Cessation of breathing.

(c) Management Plan

  1. Resuscitation/Stabilization:
    • Warmth: Immediately wrap in warm towels, place under radiant warmer or initiate Kangaroo Mother Care (KMC) once stable. Re-warm slowly.
    • Airway/Breathing: Administer oxygen via nasal prongs if cyanosed (bluish lips). Maintain saturation >90%.
  2. Infection Prevention:
    • Start empiric antibiotics (e.g., Ampicillin and Gentamicin) due to home birth and distress.
    • Administer Vitamin K (1mg IM) to prevent hemorrhagic disease.
    • Apply Tetracycline eye ointment.
  3. Fluids and Feeding:
    • Establish IV access. Give 10% Dextrose (2ml/kg bolus if hypoglycemic) or maintenance fluids (60ml/kg/day for day 2).
    • Pass Nasogastric (NG) tube for expressed breast milk feeding if unable to suckle effectively.
  4. Referral: Since this is a health centre, stabilize and refer to a hospital with a Newborn Unit (NBU) for specialized care.
5.

A three year old child is brought to hospital by his step mother with a 2 month history of diarrhea, poor feeding and progressive leg swelling. He also has wounds on his legs and perineum. His weight is 7 kg. When you examine him you find he is in shock and has kwashiorkor. You get worried because the wounds on his legs look like cigarette burns and he has other wounds on his body at different stages of healing.

(a) What is your differential diagnosis? (3 marks)

(b) What is the possible etiology of malnutrition in this child? (2 marks)

(c) Outline fluid management for correcting shock then list the ten steps of managing this patient. (5 marks)

Show Answer

(a) Differential Diagnosis

  1. Severe Acute Malnutrition (Kwashiorkor): Evidenced by edema ("leg swelling"), poor feeding, and low weight (7kg at 3 years is severely underweight).
  2. Non-Accidental Injury (Child Abuse): Indicated by "cigarette burn" wounds, wounds at different stages of healing, and step-mother history.
  3. Septicemia: Complicating the malnutrition and wounds, leading to shock.
  4. Chronic Diarrhea/Gastroenteritis: 2-month history.
  5. HIV/AIDS: Underlying cause of chronic diarrhea and failure to thrive.

(b) Etiology of Malnutrition

  • Social/Neglect (Abuse): Poor feeding and physical abuse by the caregiver (step-mother) leading to deprivation.
  • Inadequate Dietary Intake: Lack of protein and calories.
  • Chronic Infection: Diarrhea causing nutrient loss and malabsorption.

(c) Management

Fluid Management for Shock in SAM:

Standard rehydration (Plan C) is CONTRAINDICATED as it can cause heart failure.

  • Give IV 15ml/kg of Half Strength Darrow’s (HSD) with 5% Dextrose or Ringer's Lactate with 5% Dextrose over 1 hour.
  • Monitor pulse and respiration every 10 minutes.
  • If improving, repeat 15ml/kg over another hour.
  • Then switch to oral/NG rehydration with ReSoMal (10ml/kg/hr).
  • Administer whole blood (10ml/kg) if hemoglobin is low or shock persists.

The 10 Steps of Management (WHO Protocol):

  1. Treat/prevent Hypoglycemia.
  2. Treat/prevent Hypothermia.
  3. Treat/prevent Dehydration (Caution with fluids).
  4. Correct Electrolyte Imbalance (Potassium/Magnesium).
  5. Treat/prevent Infection (Antibiotics).
  6. Correct Micronutrient Deficiencies (Vit A, Folic acid, etc. - No iron initially).
  7. Start Cautious Feeding (F-75).
  8. Achieve Catch-up Growth (Transition to F-100/RUTF).
  9. Provide Sensory Stimulation and emotional support.
  10. Prepare for Follow-up and recovery (includes addressing the social/abuse issue).

1.
Discuss sickle cell disease, pathophysiology, diagnosis and management of sickling crises. (10 marks)
Show Answer

1. Definition & Pathophysiology:

  • Definition: Sickle Cell Disease (SCD) is a hereditary hemoglobinopathy where normal Hemoglobin A (HbA) is replaced by abnormal Hemoglobin S (HbS) due to a genetic mutation (valine replaces glutamic acid).
  • Pathophysiology:
    • Under conditions of low oxygen tension (deoxygenation), HbS polymerizes and forms rigid, rod-like structures.
    • This distorts the RBC into a "sickle" or crescent shape.
    • Vaso-occlusion: Sickled cells are sticky and rigid, blocking small capillaries. This causes ischemia, pain, and tissue infarction (Painful Crises).
    • Hemolysis: Sickled cells are fragile and break down prematurely (lifespan 10-20 days vs 120 days), leading to chronic anemia and jaundice.

2. Diagnosis:

  • Sickling Test: Uses sodium metabisulfite to induce sickling in vitro (Screening test).
  • Hemoglobin Electrophoresis: Definitive test. Separates Hb types (HbS, HbF, HbA). In SCD (SS), mostly HbS is found with no HbA.
  • Full Blood Count (FBC): Shows low Hb, Reticulocytosis.
  • Peripheral Blood Film: Shows sickle cells, target cells.

3. Management of Sickling Crises (Acute Vaso-Occlusive Crisis):

  • Rehydration: Vigorous IV fluids (3L/m²/day or 1.5x maintenance) to reduce blood viscosity and dilute sickled cells.
  • Analgesia (Pain Control):
    • Mild: Paracetamol/NSAIDs.
    • Severe: Opioids (Morphine/Pethidine). Pain management is a priority.
  • Oxygen Therapy: To increase oxygen saturation and reverse sickling in hypoxic patients.
  • Antibiotics: Treat any precipitating infection (e.g., Ceftriaxone for pneumonia/osteomyelitis).
  • Blood Transfusion: Indicated for Aplastic crisis (severe drop in Hb) or Sequestration crisis. Exchange transfusion may be needed for Acute Chest Syndrome or Stroke.
  • Hydroxyurea: (Long term) Increases HbF to prevent sickling.
2.
Discuss hematopoiesis and the role of different blood cell lines, the role of erythropoietin(EPO) and apoptosis. (10 marks)
Show Answer

1. Hematopoiesis:

Hematopoiesis is the process of blood cell formation from hematopoietic stem cells (HSCs). It occurs in different sites depending on developmental stage:

  • Fetal Life: Yolk sac (first few weeks) → Liver and Spleen (2nd-7th month) → Bone Marrow (7th month onwards).
  • Adults: Occurs primarily in the red bone marrow of axial skeleton (pelvis, vertebrae, sternum).

2. Role of Blood Cell Lines:

HSCs differentiate into two main lineages:

  • Myeloid Lineage:
    • Erythrocytes (RBCs): Transport oxygen (via hemoglobin) to tissues and remove CO2.
    • Thrombocytes (Platelets): Essential for hemostasis (blood clotting).
    • Granulocytes (Neutrophils, Eosinophils, Basophils):
      • Neutrophils: Phagocytosis of bacteria (acute inflammation).
      • Eosinophils: Combat parasitic infections and allergic reactions.
      • Basophils: Release histamine in inflammatory/allergic responses.
    • Monocytes/Macrophages: Phagocytosis and antigen presentation.
  • Lymphoid Lineage:
    • T-Lymphocytes: Cell-mediated immunity (kill infected cells, regulate immune response).
    • B-Lymphocytes: Humoral immunity (produce antibodies).

3. Role of Erythropoietin (EPO):

  • EPO is a glycoprotein hormone produced mainly by the kidneys (peritubular interstitial cells) in response to hypoxia (low oxygen).
  • It acts on the bone marrow to stimulate the proliferation and differentiation of erythroid progenitors (CFU-E) into mature Red Blood Cells.
  • It prevents apoptosis of erythroid precursors, ensuring adequate RBC count.

4. Apoptosis:

  • Definition: Programmed cell death. It is a controlled mechanism to remove old, damaged, or unneeded cells without causing inflammation.
  • Role in Hematopoiesis: Maintains homeostasis by balancing cell production and cell death. It eliminates autoreactive lymphocytes (preventing autoimmune disease) and removes excess blood cells after an immune response is over.
