Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions (40 Marks)

1.
In congestive cardiac failure, which of the following is a cardinal feature in infants?
A) pedal edema
B) tachycardia
C) bradycardia
D) arrhythmias
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) tachycardia

Explanation: In infants, the cardinal signs of heart failure are Tachycardia (often the earliest sign), Tachypnea, and Hepatomegaly. Pedal edema is rare in infants; they tend to have facial or sacral edema.

2.
Which of the following is a side effect of gentamycin?
A) Steven Johnson Syndrome
B) Gum hypertrophy
C) ototoxicity
D) adrenal failure
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) ototoxicity

Explanation: Gentamicin is an aminoglycoside antibiotic known for two major side effects: Ototoxicity (hearing loss/balance issues) and Nephrotoxicity (kidney damage).

3.
Which of the following conditions is highly infectious in children?
A) tuberculosis
B) tetanus
C) bronchiolitis
D) asthma
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) bronchiolitis

Explanation: Bronchiolitis, primarily caused by RSV, is highly contagious via droplets. While TB is infectious, pediatric TB is often paucibacillary and less infectious than adult TB. Tetanus and Asthma are not infectious.

4.
About diabetic ketoacidosis, which statement is true?
A) may cause cerebral edema
B) rarely occurs in newly diagnosed children
C) is associated with hypotonic dehydration
D) severe acidosis is irreversible
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) may cause cerebral edema

Explanation: Cerebral edema is the most serious complication of DKA and its treatment in children. DKA commonly occurs in newly diagnosed type 1 diabetes (often the presenting condition). It causes hypertonic/isotonic dehydration due to osmotic diuresis.

5.
In regards to IMCI which of the following is not a danger sign?
A) inability to feed
B) hyperpyrexia
C) convulsing
D) vomiting everything
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) hyperpyrexia

Explanation: The four general danger signs in IMCI are: 1. Inability to drink or breastfeed, 2. Vomiting everything, 3. History of convulsions (or convulsing now), 4. Lethargic or unconscious. Hyperpyrexia is a sign of severe febrile disease but is not one of the four "General Danger Signs" listed for initial screening.

6.
Early neonatal infections can be prevented by all the following except
A) hand washing
B) chlorhexidine cream for cord care
C) prophylactic antibiotics to all babies
D) keeping the child warm
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) prophylactic antibiotics to all babies

Explanation: Routine administration of antibiotics to all babies is not recommended and increases resistance. Antibiotics are reserved for those with risk factors or signs of sepsis. Hand washing, cord care, and thermal protection are standard preventive measures.

7.
Which of the following is an important cause of neonatal jaundice
A) prematurity
B) sickle cell disease
C) congenital heart disease
D) Hyperthyroidism
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) prematurity

Explanation: Prematurity is a major cause of neonatal jaundice due to liver immaturity. Sickle cell usually presents later (after fetal hemoglobin declines). Hypothyroidism (not Hyper) causes prolonged jaundice.

8.
Which of the following is a normal developmental milestone?
A) smiles at 6 weeks
B) can stand at 7 months
C) says mama or dada at 9 months with meaning
D) pincer grasp by 6 months
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) smiles at 6 weeks

Explanation: Social smile typically develops by 6 weeks. Standing is usually 9-11 months. Specific "mama/dada" is around 12 months (babbling earlier). Pincer grasp is 9-10 months.

9.
Measles vaccine
A) contraindicated in HIV
B) is a toxoid
C) is live attenuated
D) is administered at 8 months
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) is live attenuated

Explanation: Measles vaccine is a live attenuated virus. It is given at 9 months (and 18 months) in Kenya, not 8. It is recommended in HIV unless severely immunocompromised. Tetanus is a toxoid.

10.
The least likely cause of death is severe acute malnutrition is
A) dehydration
B) hypothermia
C) hypogycaemia
D) flaky paint dermatitis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) flaky paint dermatitis

Explanation: Dehydration, hypothermia, and hypoglycemia are acute life-threatening complications managed in the stabilization phase of SAM. Dermatosis is a skin manifestation but not a direct cause of mortality.

11.
which of the following is not a cause of haematuria in children
A) schistosoma haematobinum
B) trauma
C) acute glomerulonephritis
D) beverages
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) beverages

Explanation: Certain foods/beverages (like beets or blackberries) can cause red urine (beeturia), mimicking hematuria, but they do not cause actual blood in the urine (hematuria). Schisto, trauma, and AGN are pathologic causes of true hematuria.

12.
The following present with stridor except
A) epiglottitis
B) LTB (Laryngotracheobronchitis/Croup)
C) pneumonia
D) retropharyngeal abscess
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) pneumonia

Explanation: Stridor is a sign of upper airway obstruction. Epiglottitis, Croup (LTB), and Retropharyngeal abscess all affect the upper airway. Pneumonia affects the lower airway (alveoli) and typically presents with grunting, crackles, or wheezing, not stridor.

13.
One of the following is not a feature of infective endocarditis
A) changing heart murmurs
B) petechiae Haemorrhage
C) macroscopic haematuria
D) osler nodes
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) macroscopic haematuria

Explanation: While microscopic hematuria (due to emboli/glomerulonephritis) can occur, frank macroscopic hematuria is not a classic "cardinal" sign compared to changing murmurs, petechiae, splinter hemorrhages, Janeway lesions, and Osler nodes.

14.
The following are features of WHO paediatric HIV clinical state III except
A) Hairy leucoplakia
B) Angular cheilitis
C) Pulmonary tuberculosis
D) Unexplained persistent fever
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Angular cheilitis

Explanation: Angular cheilitis is typically a Stage II condition. Hairy leukoplakia, Pulmonary TB, and persistent fever (>1 month) are Stage III defining conditions.

15.
Which of the following is not a behavioural disorder
A) thumb sucking
B) enuresis
C) eucopresis (encopresis)
D) syncope
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) syncope

Explanation: Syncope (fainting) is a physical/physiological event (cardiovascular or neurological), not a behavioral or psychiatric disorder. Thumb sucking, enuresis, and encopresis are classified as behavioral issues in pediatrics.

16.
One of the following is not an emergency sign in triage
A) central cyanosis
B) cold hands
C) temperature >39°C
D) convulsing
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) temperature >39°C

Explanation: Emergency signs (ETAT) require immediate treatment: Airway/Breathing (Cyanosis), Circulation (Cold hands/Shock), Coma/Convulsions. High temperature alone is a Priority sign (3TPR MOB), requiring prompt assessment but after emergencies are handled.

17.
F-75 used in the initial phase in management of malnourished children stands for
A) food with 75gms of protein
B) formulae feeds with 75kcl/100mls
C) feeding the child 75 times a day
D) formulae was introduced in the market in 1975
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) formulae feeds with 75kcl/100mls

Explanation: F-75 stands for Formula 75, which contains 75 kcal of energy per 100ml. It is low in protein and sodium, designed for the stabilization phase of severe malnutrition.

18.
All are features of neonatal tetanus except
A) trismus
B) Dirty cord
C) muscle spasms
D) ability to breastfeed
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) ability to breastfeed

Explanation: A cardinal sign of neonatal tetanus is the loss of ability to suck/breastfeed (often the first sign noted by mothers) due to jaw muscle spasm (trismus).

19.
Which of the following is contraindicated in treatment of kerosene poisoning
A) oxygen
B) gastric lavage
C) antibiotics
D) feeds
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) gastric lavage

Explanation: Gastric lavage (inducing vomiting) is contraindicated in hydrocarbon (kerosene) ingestion because of the high risk of aspiration pneumonia, which is more dangerous than the systemic absorption of the kerosene.

20.
A four year old child with circumscribed hair loss, scaling and itching of the scalp
A) start the child on Amphotericin B
B) he has tinea corporis
C) it is a hereditary phenomenon
D) griseofulvin is the drug of choice
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) griseofulvin is the drug of choice

Explanation: The description fits Tinea Capitis (fungal infection of scalp). Systemic antifungal therapy is required as topicals don't penetrate hair follicles well. Oral Griseofulvin is the standard treatment of choice.

21.
congenital malformations can be prevented by all except
A) malaria prophylaxis in pregnancy
B) vaccinations
C) copious intake of alcohol during first trimester
D) supplementation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) copious intake of alcohol during first trimester

Explanation: Alcohol is a teratogen. Copious intake causes Fetal Alcohol Syndrome (malformations). It causes, not prevents, malformations.

22.
Cerebral palsy
A) hyperactivity is a feature
B) is a non-progressive motor deficit disorder
C) speech development is normal
D) diagnosis is by a skull x-ray
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) is a non-progressive motor deficit disorder

Explanation: The definition of Cerebral Palsy is a group of permanent disorders of the development of movement and posture, causing activity limitation, that are attributed to non-progressive disturbances that occurred in the developing fetal or infant brain.

23.
Which of the following is not a predisposing factor of child abuse?
A) mental retardation
B) post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD)
C) domestic violence
D) stable families
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) stable families

Explanation: Stability in the family is a protective factor. Factors like disability (mental retardation), parental stress/PTSD, and domestic violence increase the risk of abuse.

24.
Common causes of prolonged neonatal jaundice include the following except
A) congenital hypothyroidism
B) biliary atresia
C) ABO incompatibility
D) neonatal hepatitis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) ABO incompatibility

Explanation: ABO incompatibility causes hemolysis typically resulting in early onset jaundice (within 24 hours). Prolonged jaundice (>14 days) is more characteristic of Hypothyroidism, Biliary Atresia, or Hepatitis.

25.
Anaemia in the newborn may be caused by the following except
A) haemophilia
B) birth trauma
C) foetal maternal transfusion
D) blood group incompatibility
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) haemophilia

Explanation: While Hemophilia causes bleeding, it rarely causes significant anemia in the newborn period unless there is major trauma (like circumcision). Birth trauma (cephalohematoma), fetomaternal transfusion, and hemolysis (incompatibility) are common specific causes of neonatal anemia. Hemophilia is often diagnosed later when mobility increases.

26.
Which of the following is a cyanotic heart disease
A) ventricular septal defect
B) tetralogy of fallot
C) atrial septal defect
D) patient ductus arteriosus
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) tetralogy of fallot

Explanation: Tetralogy of Fallot is the most common cyanotic congenital heart disease. VSD, ASD, and PDA are left-to-right shunts and are initially acyanotic.

27.
Which of the following is not a feature of severe pneumonia
A) oxygen saturation of >90%
B) grunting
C) inability to drink
D) cyanosis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) oxygen saturation of >90%

Explanation: Severe pneumonia is characterized by hypoxia (SpO2 <90%). Therefore, having saturations >90% is not a feature of severity (it would be a good sign). Features of severe pneumonia include cyanosis, grunting, inability to drink, and chest indrawing.

28.
In regard to neonatal resuscitation, which statement is not true
A) head positioning is crucial for airway management
B) the ratio of cardiac compression to ventilation is 3:1
C) monitor pulse rate regularly
D) IV adrenaline is recommended is the baby is gasping
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) IV adrenaline is recommended is the baby is gasping

Explanation: If a baby is gasping, the immediate action is Positive Pressure Ventilation (PPV). Adrenaline is reserved for when the heart rate remains <60 bpm despite adequate ventilation and chest compressions. It is not the first line for gasping.

29.
All the following are metabolic complications of an infant of a diabetic mother except
A) hypoglycemia
B) hyperglycemia
C) hypocalcaemia
D) hypomagnesemia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) hyperglycemia

Explanation: Infants of diabetic mothers (IDM) have fetal hyperinsulinemia. After birth, when the maternal glucose supply is cut, this high insulin causes Hypoglycemia. Hyperglycemia is generally not a complication; low sugar is the main risk.

30.
Which of the following is a feature of acute glomerulonephritis
A) polyuria
B) normal blood pressure
C) hypotension
D) oliguria
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) oliguria

Explanation: Acute Glomerulonephritis typically presents with the triad of Hematuria, Hypertension, and Edema, often accompanied by Oliguria (reduced urine output) due to reduced GFR.

31.
About nephrotic syndrome, which one is true?
A) hyperalbuminemia is a feature
B) most idiopathic cases lead to minimal change lesions
C) blood lipids are reduced
D) steroids have no role in management
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) most idiopathic cases lead to minimal change lesions

Explanation: In children, about 85-90% of idiopathic nephrotic syndrome cases are due to Minimal Change Disease. It features hypoalbuminemia (not hyper) and hyperlipidemia (not reduced). Steroids are the mainstay of treatment.

32.
The following is a major criterion in Rheumatic fever
A) fever
B) increased ESR
C) migratory polyarthritis
D) elevated C-reactive protein
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) migratory polyarthritis

Explanation: Jones Criteria: Major are Carditis, Polyarthritis, Chorea, Erythema Marginatum, Subcutaneous Nodules. Fever, ESR, and CRP are Minor criteria.

33.
Geographical origin of a child will help in the diagnosis of:
A) Whooping cough
B) Kala-azar
C) Tuberculosis
D) Covid-19
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Kala-azar

Explanation: Kala-azar (Visceral Leishmaniasis) is endemic in specific semi-arid regions of Kenya (e.g., Baringo, West Pokot, Turkana). Geographical history is a crucial diagnostic clue.

34.
The following organisms cause acute diarrhea except
A) Escherichia Coli
B) Rota Virus
C) Shigella
D) Klebsiella
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) Klebsiella

Explanation: E. Coli, Rotavirus, and Shigella are classic and common causes of acute gastroenteritis/diarrhea. Klebsiella typically causes pneumonia or sepsis (often hospital-acquired) but is not a primary common cause of acute community-acquired diarrhea.

35.
Which of the following conditions causes massive spleenomegally
A) Acute myeloid leukaemia
B) sickle cell anaemia
C) Tuberculosis
D) Typhoid Fever
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Acute myeloid leukaemia

Explanation: Massive splenomegaly is often defined as the spleen crossing the midline or extending into the pelvis. It is seen in CML, Malaria (Hyperreactive Malarial Splenomegaly), Kala-azar, and sometimes acute leukemias. In Sickle Cell, the spleen is usually autosplenectomized (shrunken) by childhood (though enlarged initially). Typhoid causes mild/moderate enlargement.

37.
Side effects of isoniazid
A) optic neuritis
B) peripheral neuropathy
C) Ref colored urine
D) Gum hypertrophy
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) peripheral neuropathy

Explanation: Isoniazid (INH) causes Pyridoxine (B6) deficiency leading to peripheral neuropathy. Optic neuritis is Ethambutol. Red urine is Rifampicin. Gum hypertrophy is Phenytoin.

38.
In pyogenic meningitis, one of the investigations is true concerning CSF analysis
A) polymorphs are increased
B) protein is 0.4gm/l
C) sugar is >2.5mml/L
D) clear colour
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) polymorphs are increased

Explanation: Bacterial (pyogenic) meningitis CSF findings: Turbid/Cloudy color, High Polymorphs (Neutrophils), High Protein, Low Sugar (<2.2 mmol/L or <40% plasma glucose).

39.
Spina bifida occulta present with all the following except
A) Lipoma
B) tuff of hair
C) flaccid paralysis
D) dimple
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) flaccid paralysis

Explanation: Spina bifida occulta is often asymptomatic and cutaneous stigmata (tuft of hair, dimple, lipoma) mark the defect. Flaccid paralysis indicates significant neural involvement (Myelomeningocele/Spina bifida cystica), which is not "occulta".

40.
Differential diagnosis of a floppy infant:
A) Downs Syndrome
B) underweight
C) Turner's syndrome
D) hyperthyroidism
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Downs Syndrome

Explanation: Hypotonia (floppiness) is a hallmark feature of Down Syndrome. Underweight is a nutritional status. Hyperthyroidism usually causes jitteriness/hypertonia. Turner's doesn't classically present as a "floppy infant" in the same way.

Section 2: Short Answer Questions (40 Marks)

1.
Exclusive breast feeding is recommended in the first 6 months in infants
a) List four advantages of breast feeding (2 marks)
b) Outline four signs of good attachment you would look for when a baby is being breastfed (3 marks)
Show Answer

a) Advantages:

  • Provides perfect nutrition and hydration.
  • Contains antibodies (immunity) protecting against infections.
  • Promotes bonding between mother and baby.
  • Cost-effective and hygienic.
  • Promotes uterine involution in mother.

b) Signs of Good Attachment:

  • Chin touching the breast.
  • Mouth wide open.
  • Lower lip turned outwards.
  • More areola visible above the baby's mouth than below.
2.
An eight-month old infant is brought to the OPD with a history of having 10 bouts of watery stool in the last 24 hours and 3 bouts of vomiting in the last 3 hours. There is no blood in stool. On examination she is weak, unable to suckle, eyes are deeply sunken and skin pinch goes back more than 2 seconds
a) Classify her illness (1 mark)
b) What immediate treatment do you give this child (4 marks)
Show Answer

a) Classification: Severe Dehydration (Plan C).

b) Treatment:

  • Start IV fluids immediately (Ringer's Lactate or Normal Saline).
  • Give 100ml/kg divided: 30ml/kg in 1 hour (infant) then 70ml/kg in 5 hours.
  • Reassess the child every 1-2 hours.
  • Give ORS as soon as the child can drink (usually after 3-4 hours).
  • Administer Zinc supplements once vomiting stops/eating resumes.
3.
A 2 1/2 year-old child weighing 13kg is brought to outpatient convulsing
a) What are your immediate concerns, outline two (1 mark)
b) If the convulsion does not stop by 10 minutes after treatment, what action will you take. (4 marks)
Show Answer

a) Immediate Concerns:

  • Airway patency (Aspiration risk).
  • Hypoxia (Breathing).
  • Hypoglycemia.

b) Action if Convulsion Persists (Status Epilepticus Protocol):

  1. Repeat the dose of Diazepam (0.3mg/kg IV or 0.5mg/kg PR).
  2. If still convulsing, load with Phenobarbitone (15-20mg/kg IV) or Phenytoin (15-20mg/kg IV).
  3. Monitor Airway, Breathing, Circulation constantly.
  4. Consider consulting senior/ICU support if refractory.
4.
A two weeks old baby is brought to the paediatric filter clinic with history of refusal to breastfeed. On examination, the temperature is 37.5°C and the umbilical cord has pus
a) What is the probable diagnosis (1 mark)
b) Outline the definitive treatment (4 marks)
Show Answer

a) Diagnosis: Neonatal Sepsis (specifically Omphalitis with systemic signs).

b) Definitive Treatment:

  • Admit the baby.
  • Septic Screen (Blood culture, CBC).
  • IV Antibiotics: Ampicillin and Gentamicin (First line for neonatal sepsis).
  • Local cord care (clean with chlorhexidine/spirit).
  • Supportive care (fluids/feeding).
5.
A three year old child presents to you with a 2 day history of cough and difficulty in breathing. On examination the child has a temperature of 38°C and respiratory rate of 48 cycles per minute. There is notable grunting and he is unable to feed
a) What is the diagnosis? (1 mark)
b) How would you treat this child (4 marks)
Show Answer

a) Diagnosis: Severe Pneumonia (Danger signs: Grunting, Inability to feed).

b) Treatment:

  • Admit to ward.
  • Administer Oxygen (if SpO2 <90% or central cyanosis/grunting).
  • Start IV Crystalline Penicillin and Gentamicin.
  • Supportive care: Fever control (Paracetamol), IV fluids if unable to feed initially, then NGT feeding.
6.
A four year old child from a high risk malaria area presents with fever, history of convulsions and inability to feed. On examination he has a temperature of 38°C and is moderately pale. He weighs 16kg
a) What is the diagnosis? (1 mark)
b) outline the treatment (4 marks)
Show Answer

a) Diagnosis: Severe Malaria.

b) Treatment:

  • Admit: Start IV lines.
  • Antimalarial: IV Artesunate (3mg/kg for <20kg) = 16*3 = 48mg at 0, 12, 24 hours. Then daily for 6 days or switch to oral AL when tolerating.
  • Treat Complications:
    • Blood Transfusion if Hb <4g/dL or distress.
    • Diazepam for convulsions.
    • Glucose for hypoglycemia.
7.
Lorua was born three days ago. Her mother noted yellow coloration of eyes on her bay some few hours after delivery. The yellowness has spread to the whole body.
a) What is the diagnosis (1 mark)
b) List four important investigations you would order for this baby (4 marks)
Show Answer

a) Diagnosis: Pathological Neonatal Jaundice (Appearance <24 hours "few hours after delivery" is always pathological).

b) Investigations:

  1. Serum Bilirubin Levels (Total and Direct).

  3. Direct Coombs Test (DCT).

  5. (Peripheral blood film for hemolysis).
8.
A six-year-old girl is brought to you with history of headache, puffiness of face which is worse in the morning and has passed tea coloured urine of reduced volume. She was treated for sore throat two weeks ago
a) What is your diagnosis? (1 mark)
b) List four important investigations and expected results (4 marks)
Show Answer

a) Diagnosis: Acute Glomerulonephritis (Post-Streptococcal).

b) Investigations:

  • Urinalysis: RBCs, Red cell casts, Proteinuria.
  • ASO Titer (Anti-Streptolysin O): Elevated (indicating recent strep infection).
  • Urea & Electrolytes (U/Es): Elevated Urea/Creatinine (Renal impairment).
  • Serum Complement (C3): Reduced.
