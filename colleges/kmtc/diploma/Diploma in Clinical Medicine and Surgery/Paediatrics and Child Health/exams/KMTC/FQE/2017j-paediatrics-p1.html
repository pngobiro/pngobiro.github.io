Section A: Short Essay Questions

60 Marks
1.

A six-day-old baby who was born at home is brought to you with a history of fever, failure to breastfeed, and irritability. On examination, the baby has a temperature of 37.9°C. There is an inflamed umbilical stump with a visibly discharging umbilicus.

a) What is your diagnosis? (2 marks)

b) Give four important investigations that you should do. (4 marks)

c) Give the specific antibiotic management. (4 marks)

Show Answer

a) Diagnosis:

Neonatal Sepsis (secondary to Omphalitis).

b) Investigations:

  • Full Hemogram (CBC): To check for leukocytosis (high WBC), neutrophilia, or thrombocytopenia.
  • Blood Culture & Sensitivity: To isolate the causative organism (Gold standard).
  • Pus Swab (Umbilical): For microscopy, culture, and sensitivity.
  • C-Reactive Protein (CRP): Acute phase reactant marker for infection.
  • Random Blood Sugar (RBS): To rule out hypoglycemia (common in septic neonates).

c) Specific Antibiotic Management:

First-line treatment for Neonatal Sepsis (Kenya Basic Paediatric Protocols):

  • Inj. Ampicillin (or Crystalline Penicillin) 50mg/kg IV every 12 hours.
  • PLUS Inj. Gentamycin 3mg/kg IV (or 5mg/kg depending on weight/protocol) once daily.
  • Duration: Usually for 7-10 days depending on clinical response.
2.

Breastfeeding is an important part of paediatric nutrition.

a) List eight advantages of breastfeeding. (4 marks)

b) Briefly explain proper breastfeeding technique in terms of the following:

  I. Positioning (3 marks)

  II. Attachment (3 marks)

Show Answer

a) Advantages of Breastfeeding:

  • Contains antibodies (IgA) providing immunity against infections.
  • Provides perfect nutrition (correct temperature, easily digestible).
  • Promotes bonding between mother and baby.
  • Protects against allergies and eczema.
  • Cheap/Cost-effective (no need to buy formula).
  • Promotes involution of the uterus (reduces PPH).
  • Lactational Amenorrhea Method (LAM) acts as contraception.
  • Reduces risk of breast and ovarian cancer in the mother.

b) Technique:

I. Positioning:

  • Baby's head and body should be in a straight line.
  • Baby's face facing the breast, nose opposite the nipple.
  • Baby's body held close to the mother's body.
  • Baby's whole body supported (not just the head).

II. Attachment (Latching):

  • Baby's mouth wide open.
  • Lower lip turned outwards.
  • Baby's chin touching the breast.
  • More areola visible above the baby's mouth than below.
3.

A four-year-old presents with fever, acidotic breathing and is prostrated. The child had gone to Kisumu for a family visit.

a) What is the most probable diagnosis? (2 marks)

b) List three important investigations that you will do. (3 marks)

c) Give the specific intravenous management with correctly calculated dosages. (5 marks)

Show Answer

a) Diagnosis:

Severe Malaria (Clinical features: Fever, Acidotic breathing, Prostration, Travel history to endemic zone).

b) Investigations:

  • Blood Slide (BS) for Malaria Parasites (MPs) / mRDT: To confirm diagnosis.
  • Random Blood Sugar: To rule out hypoglycemia.
  • Hemoglobin (Hb) / Full Hemogram: To check for severe anemia.

c) Specific IV Management (Weight estimation: Age x 2 + 8 = 16kg):

  • Drug: Inj. Artesunate (First line for Severe Malaria).
  • Dose: 2.4 mg/kg (for children < 20kg).
  • Calculation: $16 \text{ kg} \times 2.4 \text{ mg/kg} = 38.4 \text{ mg}$.
  • Administration: Give 38.4 mg IV stat (at 0 hours), repeat at 12 hours, and then every 24 hours until the child can take oral medication.
  • Alternative (if Artesunate unavailable): Inj. Quinine 10mg/kg loading dose in 5% Dextrose over 4 hours.
4.

A two-year-old presents with a cough, difficulty in breathing, and fever for three days. She is grunting and is unable to breastfeed. On examination, there is lower chest wall indrawing.

a) What is the most likely diagnosis? (2 marks)

b) What other three clinical features would suggest respiratory distress? (3 marks)

c) Outline the specific management as would appear on your treatment sheet. (5 marks)

Show Answer

a) Diagnosis:

Severe Pneumonia (Danger signs: inability to breastfeed/drink, grunting, chest indrawing).

b) Signs of Respiratory Distress:

  • Fast breathing (Tachypnea > 40/min for 2yo).
  • Nasal flaring.
  • Use of accessory muscles (suprasternal/intercostal recession).
  • Cyanosis (blue tongue/lips).

c) Specific Management (Weight approx. 12kg):

  • Admit to ward.
  • Oxygen therapy: If saturation < 90% or central cyanosis present.
  • Antibiotics:
    • Inj. Crystalline Penicillin 50,000 IU/kg IV QID (600,000 IU).
    • PLUS Inj. Gentamycin 7.5 mg/kg IV OD (90 mg).
    • (Alternatively: Inj. Ceftriaxone 50mg/kg OD if first line fails or very severe).
  • Supportive:
    • IV Fluids (maintenance) if unable to drink.
    • Paracetamol 10-15mg/kg for fever > 38.5°C.
5.

A mother presents to hospital carrying one three-year-old baby with a two days history of diarrhea. On examination, the child has sunken eyeballs, sunken anterior fontanel, and is drinking poorly.

a) What is the most likely diagnosis? (2 marks)

b) Outline your management. (5 marks)

c) List three possible complications. (3 marks)

Show Answer

a) Diagnosis:

Severe Dehydration (secondary to Gastroenteritis/Diarrhea). Key signs: Sunken eyes, drinking poorly/unable to drink (2 signs present).

b) Management (Plan C):

  • Admit the patient.
  • IV Resuscitation: Start IV fluids immediately (Ringer's Lactate or Normal Saline).
    • Give 100ml/kg total.
    • For a 3-year-old (approx. 14kg): Give 30ml/kg in the first 30 mins, then 70ml/kg over the next 2.5 hours.
  • Reassess: Check vital signs and hydration status frequently.
  • Oral Rehydration Solution (ORS): Start giving ORS (5ml/kg/hr) as soon as the child can drink, alongside IV fluids.
  • Zinc Supplementation: 20mg daily for 14 days once vomiting stops.

c) Complications:

  • Hypovolemic Shock.
  • Acute Renal Failure (Pre-renal).
  • Electrolyte imbalances (Hypokalemia, Hyponatremia).
  • Metabolic Acidosis.
6.

A two-year-old presents with generalized tonic-clonic convulsions. There is fever of 39°C and no loss of consciousness. This is the fourth episode and the child was admitted with the same seven months ago. There is no history of travel to a malaria endemic zone.

a) What is the most likely diagnosis? (2 marks)

b) List four investigations that you would carry out. (4 marks)

c) Outline the management of this child. (4 marks)

Show Answer

a) Diagnosis:

Complex Febrile Convulsions (Repeated episodes, high fever). Note: Could also be Epilepsy triggered by fever or Meningitis, but febrile convulsion is the primary working diagnosis given the age and fever.

b) Investigations:

  • Lumbar Puncture (CSF Analysis): Crucial to rule out Meningitis given the fever and convulsions.
  • Random Blood Sugar: To rule out hypoglycemia.
  • Full Hemogram: Screen for infection.
  • Serum Electrolytes (Na, Ca, Mg): To rule out imbalances causing seizures.
  • Urinalysis: To check for UTI as a cause of fever.

c) Management:

  • ABC: Maintain Airway (recovery position), Breathing, Circulation.
  • Stop Convulsion: Rectal Diazepam (0.5mg/kg) or IV Phenobarbital if persisting.
  • Control Fever: Tepid sponging and Paracetamol (15mg/kg).
  • Treat Cause: Antibiotics if bacterial infection (e.g., Meningitis, UTI) is confirmed.
  • Education: Reassure parents about the nature of febrile seizures.

Section B: Long Essay Questions

40 Marks

Instructions: Select ANY TWO questions from this section.

1.

A ten-year-old boy presents with painful knee, ankle, and elbow joints. He has easy fatiguability and on several occasions has had uncoordinated jerky convulsion-like movements. Further examination reveals a low grade fever and macular rashes on the trunk.

a) What is the most likely diagnosis? (2 marks)

b) Outline the criteria for diagnosis of the above condition. (15 marks)

c) List the two most common complications of the above condition. (3 marks)

Show Answer

a) Diagnosis:

Acute Rheumatic Fever (ARF). (Symptoms: Polyarthritis, Chorea [jerky movements], Erythema Marginatum [rash], Fever).

b) Diagnostic Criteria (Modified Jones Criteria):

Diagnosis requires evidence of a preceding Group A Streptococcal infection PLUS 2 Major criteria OR 1 Major + 2 Minor criteria.

Evidence of Preceding Strep Infection: Positive throat culture for Group A Strep OR Elevated Anti-Streptolysin O (ASO) titer.

Major Criteria:

  1. Carditis: Inflammation of the heart (murmurs, tachycardia, pericardial friction rub).
  2. Polyarthritis: Migratory, painful, swollen joints (knees, elbows, ankles).
  3. Sydenham's Chorea: Involuntary, jerky, purposeless movements (St. Vitus' dance).
  4. Erythema Marginatum: Pink rings on the trunk/inner surfaces of limbs (non-itchy).
  5. Subcutaneous Nodules: Painless, firm lumps over bony prominences.

Minor Criteria:

  • Fever.
  • Arthralgia (joint pain without swelling).
  • Elevated acute phase reactants (ESR, CRP).
  • Prolonged PR interval on ECG.

c) Complications:

  1. Rheumatic Heart Disease (RHD): Permanent valve damage (Mitral stenosis/regurgitation).
  2. Heart Failure: Secondary to severe carditis.
2.

Peter is a twelve-year-old child weighing 30 kgs and has had several transfusions over the past few years. He presents with pallor, fever, severe pain on his hands and feet and shortness of breath. Further examination reveals splenomegaly.

a) Following dehydration, stress or an infection, outline the possible outcomes (types of crises) of the above condition. (10 marks)

b) Peter presents with severe pain in his joints making movement unbearable. Write a treatment sheet for Peter. (10 marks)

Show Answer

The diagnosis is Sickle Cell Disease presenting with a Vaso-occlusive Crisis.

a) Possible Outcomes (Types of Crises):

  1. Vaso-occlusive (Painful) Crisis: Sickled cells block microcirculation causing ischemia and severe pain in bones/joints (Hand-Foot syndrome).
  2. Hemolytic Crisis: Accelerated breakdown of RBCs leading to severe anemia and jaundice.
  3. Sequestration Crisis: Rapid pooling of blood in the spleen (or liver), causing massive splenomegaly and shock (Medical Emergency).
  4. Aplastic Crisis: Bone marrow shutdown usually triggered by Parvovirus B19 infection, leading to severe drop in Hb.
  5. Acute Chest Syndrome: Lung infiltrates causing fever, chest pain, tachypnea, and hypoxia.

b) Treatment Sheet (Management of Painful Crisis):

Drug/Instruction Dose/Freq Route Notes
Admit Patient - - Keep warm, rest.
IV Fluids (N/S or D5%) Maintenance x 1.5 IV Hydration helps unblock vessels. (30kg: approx 2500ml/24hr).
Inj. Morphine 0.1-0.2 mg/kg (3-6mg) IV/IM Every 4-6 hours for severe pain.
Inj. Paracetamol 450 mg (15mg/kg) IV/Oral TDS for fever/pain.
Inj. Ceftriaxone 1.5g (50mg/kg) IV Daily (Treat underlying infection).
Oxygen 2-4 L/min Nasal If SpO2 < 92% or respiratory distress.
Blood Transfusion 10ml/kg PRBCs IV Only if Hb is very low (<5g/dl) or hemodynamic instability.
Folic Acid 5 mg PO Daily.
Hydroxyurea - PO Continue if previously prescribed.
3.

A five-year-old girl presents with headache, history of puffiness of the face that is worse in the morning. She has passage of straw colored urine that is reduced in volume. Three weeks ago she had been treated for a sore throat at the local health centre. She is anxious with a blood pressure of 140/100mmHg.

a) Give your diagnosis and two possible differentials? (4 marks)

b) During your management of the above patient, give specific drug dosages. (16 marks)

Show Answer

a) Diagnosis:

Acute Post-Streptococcal Glomerulonephritis (AGN). (Key features: Edema/puffiness, Oliguria, Hypertension, history of sore throat [Strep] 2-3 weeks prior).

Differentials:

  1. Nephrotic Syndrome (usually massive proteinuria, hypoalbuminemia, generalized edema).
  2. Acute Renal Failure (other causes).
  3. Urinary Tract Infection (UTI).

b) Management (Specific Drug Dosages - Weight approx. 18kg):

  • 1. Management of Hypertension (Emergent due to 140/100):
    • Nifedipine: 0.25-0.5 mg/kg orally/sublingual. (Approx 5-10mg). Monitor BP closely.
    • Furosemide (Diuretic): 1-2 mg/kg IV/Oral. (18-36mg) once or twice daily. Helps with edema and BP.
    • If hypertensive encephalopathy: Hydralazine 0.15-0.5 mg/kg IV.
  • 2. Eradication of Streptococcus:
    • Penicillin V (Oral): 250 mg QID for 10 days.
    • OR Inj. Benzathine Penicillin: 600,000 IU IM stat dose (for <30kg).
    • If allergic to Penicillin: Erythromycin 10mg/kg QID for 10 days.
  • 3. Supportive Care (Non-drug):
    • Strict Fluid Restriction: (Insensible loss [400ml/m2] + Urine output).
    • Salt Restriction (Low sodium diet).
    • Daily Weights and BP monitoring.
    • Bed rest during acute phase.
