1.
A mother comes to you with a 4 months old baby who is unable to support the neck. The baby has had convulsions on and off since birth. On examination, you find head lag with tonic limbs. What is your impression?
A. Cerebral palsy
B. Birth asphyxia
C. Meningitis
D. Delayed milestones
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Cerebral palsy

Explanation: The clinical picture suggests Cerebral Palsy (CP). The key features are:

  • History: Convulsions since birth (neonatal seizures are a major risk factor).
  • Development: Unable to support neck at 4 months (motor delay/head lag).
  • Examination: "Tonic limbs" indicates hypertonia/spasticity, which is characteristic of spastic CP.

While birth asphyxia (B) is a cause, the clinical impression or diagnosis of the resulting condition is Cerebral Palsy. Delayed milestones (D) is a symptom, not a specific diagnosis.

2.
A 2 days old baby is brought to you with history of yellowness of the eyes and body that seem to be increasing each hour. You request for bilirubin level and you find it elevated. How will you manage?
A. Antibiotics
B. Exchange transfusion
C. Phototherapy
D. Reassure the mother
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Exchange transfusion

Explanation: Jaundice appearing within the first 24-48 hours (pathological jaundice) that is "increasing each hour" indicates a rapid rise in bilirubin, likely due to hemolysis (e.g., ABO or Rh incompatibility). Rapidly rising bilirubin carries a high risk of kernicterus. Exchange transfusion is the emergency management for rapidly rising severe hyperbilirubinemia to wash out antibodies and bilirubin.

Note: While phototherapy (C) is used for jaundice, the description of "increasing each hour" in a 2-day-old suggests a severe, rapid process requiring more aggressive intervention or at least immediate preparation for it. However, if the levels are in the phototherapy range, that would be first line. Without specific values, "Exchange transfusion" is the answer for severe/rapidly progressing pathological jaundice unresponsive to or too high for phototherapy alone.

3.
Which of the following is a sign of correct positioning during breast feeding?
A. Mouth wide open
B. Lower lip turned outward
C. More areola seen above than below
D. Baby's body straight and close to the mother
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Baby's body straight and close to the mother

Explanation: The question asks for a sign of positioning, not attachment.

  • Positioning signs: Baby's body straight, head and body in line, body close to mother's body, baby's whole body supported.
  • Attachment signs: Mouth wide open (A), lower lip turned outward (B), chin touching breast, more areola visible above than below (C).

Therefore, D is the only option describing correct positioning.

4.
A PP1 pupil presents to you with a generalized maculopapular rash, fever, cough, running nose and red eyes. A play mate had similar symptoms. What is your impression?
A. Chicken pox
B. Scabies
C. Measles
D. Eczema
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Measles

Explanation: The clinical presentation is classic for Measles:

  • Prodrome: Fever + The "3 Cs" (Cough, Coryza/runny nose, Conjunctivitis/red eyes).
  • Rash: Generalized maculopapular rash.
  • Epidemiology: Highly contagious (playmate affected).

Chicken pox typically presents with a vesicular rash (dewdrop on a rose petal) at different stages.

5.
A HIV positive child is due for immunization. He is 6 weeks now and has not had any immunization, the mother says he is not sick. Which immunization will you give today?
A. Pentavalent
B. BCG
C. OPV 0
D. Pneumococcal
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. BCG

Explanation: The child has missed birth vaccines. According to KEPI (Kenya Expanded Programme on Immunization) guidelines:

  • At 6 weeks: The child is due for Pentavalent 1, OPV 1, PCV 1, and Rotavirus 1.
  • Catch-up: The child missed BCG and OPV 0 at birth.
  • HIV Context: BCG is given to HIV-exposed or infected infants unless they have clinical signs of symptomatic HIV (AIDS). The mother reports "he is not sick" (asymptomatic), so BCG is safe and indicated.

While the child will also receive Pentavalent and Pneumococcal today (as part of the 6-week schedule), BCG is the specific answer highlighting the catch-up for the missed birth dose which is crucial and safe in asymptomatic HIV.

8.
A 3 year old presents to you with fever, vomiting and chills, a blood slide for malaria parasite is positive. You gave her AL but no response after 5 days. What will be your second line treatment?
A. I.V articulate (Artesunate)
B. I.V Quinine
C. Dihydroartemisin piperaquine
D. I.M artemether
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Dihydroartemisin piperaquine

Explanation: In Kenya, the first-line treatment for uncomplicated malaria is Artemether-Lumefantrine (AL). If there is treatment failure with AL within 14 days, the recommended second-line treatment is Dihydroartemisinin-Piperaquine (DHA-PPQ). IV Artesunate or Quinine is reserved for severe malaria, but the patient presented with uncomplicated malaria symptoms initially (though treatment failure is a concern, oral second-line is standard unless signs of severity develop).

A. Stage I
B. Stage II
C. Stage III
D. Stage IV

Correct Answer: C. Stage III

Explanation: According to WHO Clinical Staging of HIV/AIDS for children:

  • Stage III: Includes Pulmonary Tuberculosis (PTB).
  • Stage IV: Includes Extrapulmonary TB.

The patient has classic symptoms of Pulmonary TB (cough, night sweats) and a positive GeneXpert (sputum), which classifies as WHO Clinical Stage III.

10.
A child presents to you with diarrhea not blood stained, foul smelling, mucoid associated with abdominal pains. What is your impression?
A. Amoebiasis
B. Typhoid fever
C. Shigellosis
D. Cholera
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Amoebiasis

Explanation:

  • Amoebiasis: Characterized by foul-smelling, mucoid stools (sometimes with blood, but often just mucus in mild cases or "amoebic colitis"), and abdominal pain/cramps.
  • Shigellosis (Dysentery): Typically presents with bloody diarrhea, fever, and tenesmus.
  • Cholera: "Rice water" stool, not foul-smelling initially, voluminous, no abdominal pain usually.
  • Typhoid: Fever is the main symptom; diarrhea may be "pea soup" like, but abdominal pain and fever predominate.
11.
A Child is brought to you with dyspnea, cough and fever. On examination you find him grunting, head nodding, cyanosis and chest in drawing. What will be your first line antibiotics for management of this patient?
A. Ceftriaxone
B. Crystalline penicillin and gentamycin
C. Amoxicillin
D. Cefotaxime
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Crystalline penicillin and gentamycin

Explanation: The child has signs of severe pneumonia (grunting, head nodding, cyanosis, chest indrawing). The standard first-line inpatient treatment for severe pneumonia in children (under 5) is intravenous Benzylpenicillin (Crystalline Penicillin) and Gentamicin.

Amoxicillin (C) is for outpatient management of non-severe pneumonia (fast breathing only). Ceftriaxone (A) is usually second-line.

12.
You are called to resuscitate a newborn. You decide to give oxygen, what will be the best delivery method to achieve 80-90% oxygen?
A. Nasal prong
B. Nasal catheter
C. Non rebreather mask
D. Bag and mask
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Non rebreather mask

Explanation: To deliver high concentrations of oxygen (80-90% or higher) to a spontaneously breathing patient, a non-rebreather mask with a reservoir bag is the most effective method among the options. Nasal prongs typically deliver 24-40%. Bag and mask is for positive pressure ventilation, not just oxygen delivery to a breathing patient (though it can deliver 100% if used for ventilation).

13.

A. Refer to ENT for ear irrigation
B. Clean and dry ear
C. Wicking and per oral Amoxyl
D. Admit for I.V ceftriaxone
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Wicking and per oral Amoxyl

Explanation: This is a case of Acute Otitis Media (AOM) with perforation/discharge. The management includes:

  • Local care: Dry mopping/wicking to remove pus.

Ear irrigation (A) is generally contraindicated in perforated eardrums. Admitting for IV (D) is unnecessary unless there are severe complications like mastoiditis.

14.
A pregnant woman had folic acid deficiency. What will be the expected effect on the baby?
A. Spina bifida
B. Megaloblastic anemia
C. Growth retardation
D. Down syndrome
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Spina bifida

Explanation: Folic acid deficiency in early pregnancy is strongly associated with Neural Tube Defects (NTDs), the most common of which is Spina Bifida. This is why folic acid supplementation is recommended preconceptionally and during the first trimester.

15.
A 3 weeks baby is brought to you convulsing. What drug will you administer as a loading dose?
A. I.V Diazepam 0.3mg/kg
B. I.V Phenobarbitone 20mg/kg
C. I.M Phenobarbitone 15mg/kg
D. PR Diazepam 0.5 mg/kg
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. I.V Phenobarbitone 20mg/kg

Explanation: The baby is 3 weeks old (neonate). The first-line anticonvulsant for neonatal seizures is Phenobarbitone. The loading dose is 20 mg/kg IV slowly.

Diazepam (A, D) is generally avoided in neonates due to the risk of respiratory depression and bilirubin displacement (kernicterus), though it may be used if Phenobarbital fails.

16.
Which Cyanotic heart disease presents with cyanosis at birth?
A. Tetralogy of fallot
B. Transposition of great arteries
C. PDA
D. VSD
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Transposition of great arteries

Explanation: Transposition of the Great Arteries (TGA) typically presents with severe cyanosis immediately at birth because the systemic and pulmonary circulations are parallel and separate. Survival depends on mixing via PDA or VSD.

Tetralogy of Fallot (A) often presents later (days to weeks) as the ductus closes or infundibular stenosis worsens ("blue spells"). PDA (C) and VSD (D) are acyanotic heart diseases.

A. Mitral stenosis
B. Tricuspid atresia
C. Pulmonary stenosis
D. Aortic stenosis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Mitral stenosis

Explanation: The history of sore throat and migratory polyarthritis suggests Rheumatic Fever. Rheumatic Heart Disease (RHD) most commonly affects the Mitral Valve. A murmur at the apex (mitral area) strongly supports a mitral valve lesion (Mitral Stenosis or Regurgitation). Mitral stenosis is a classic long-term complication of RHD.

Which type of fluid will you give to a child diagnosed of malnutrition and severe dehydration?
A. I.V ringers lactate
B. I.V Normal saline
C. P.O Resomal
D. ORS
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. P.O Resomal

24.
A preterm needs resuscitation at birth. Which group require continuous positive airway pressure prophylaxis (CPAP)?
A. Floppy newborn
B. Stable newborn 1000-1300 grams
C. Grunting newborn
D. Newborns with convulsion
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Grunting newborn

Explanation: Grunting is a sign of respiratory distress and an attempt to maintain Functional Residual Capacity (FRC) by closing the glottis (auto-PEEP). CPAP provides this positive pressure externally, preventing alveolar collapse. It is the standard prophylaxis/treatment for preterm infants with respiratory distress (grunting, retractions) to prevent Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS) progression.

26.
A child presents to you with history of passing little dark urine, his blood pressure is 150/100 mmhg, facial puffiness that improves as the day progresses. What is your impression?
A. Nephrotic syndrome
B. Acute glomerulonephritis
C. Heart failure
D. Hypertension
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Acute glomerulonephritis

Explanation: The classic triad of Acute Glomerulonephritis (AGN) consists of:

  1. Hematuria: "Little dark urine" (coca-cola colored).
  2. Hypertension: BP 150/100 mmHg.
  3. Edema: Facial puffiness (typically worse in the morning, improving during the day as fluid moves to legs).

Nephrotic syndrome typically presents with massive edema and proteinuria but usually normal blood pressure and no gross hematuria.

27.
Which of the following is a laboratory characteristic feature of nephrotic syndrome?
A. Massive edema
B. Hyperlipidemia
C. Hypoalbuminaemia
D. Red blood cell cast
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Hypoalbuminaemia

Explanation: The classic features of Nephrotic Syndrome are:

  • Massive Proteinuria (>3.5g/day or 3+ on dipstick).
  • Hypoalbuminemia (Low serum albumin <2.5g/dL) - This is a laboratory finding.
  • Edema (Clinical finding).
  • Hyperlipidemia (Laboratory finding).

Comparing B (Hyperlipidemia) and C (Hypoalbuminemia): Both are lab features. However, Hypoalbuminemia is the direct consequence of proteinuria and the primary driver of the pathophysiology (edema). It is a defining criterion. RBC casts (D) are characteristic of Nephritic syndrome (Glomerulonephritis).

28.
You are asked to give advice to a mother of a newborn on cord care of her baby. What will be your advice?
A. Keep Warmth
B. Give Antibiotic
C. Apply Chlorhexidine daily
D. Apply spirit daily
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Apply Chlorhexidine daily

Explanation: Current guidelines (including WHO and Kenya Ministry of Health) recommend the application of 4% Chlorhexidine digluconate (7.1% chlorhexidine digluconate gel or solution) to the umbilical cord stump daily for the first week of life to prevent neonatal sepsis/omphalitis. The old practice of using surgical spirit is no longer recommended as it delays cord separation.

29.
A 3 year old weighting 15kgs child, presents to you with weak thready pulse and cold clammy extremities. He had diarrhea and vomiting for two days. How much fluid will you give?
A. I.V 300mls ringers lactate
B. I.V 450mls ringers lactate
C. I.V 1500mls ringers lactate
D. I.V 1050mls ringers lactate
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. I.V 450mls ringers lactate

Explanation: The child has signs of Shock (weak thready pulse, cold clammy extremities). This is Plan C (Severe Dehydration with Shock).

  • Treatment for Shock: IV bolus of Ringer's Lactate or Normal Saline at 20-30 ml/kg (guidelines vary, often 20ml/kg stat).
  • Calculation: 15 kg 20 ml/kg = 300 ml. Or 15 kg 30 ml/kg = 450 ml.
  • WHO Plan C for severe dehydration (without shock) is 30ml/kg in first 30 mins (older child) or 1 hour (infant). 15 kg * 30ml = 450ml.
  • Given the options, 450ml corresponds to the 30ml/kg bolus usually given for severe dehydration stabilization.

B. I.V artesunate 48mg
C. I.V quinine 48mg
D. P.O AL 2 tablets

Correct Answer: A. I.V artesunate 60mg

Explanation: This is Severe Malaria (convulsions + severe anemia Hb 4g/dl). The treatment of choice is IV Artesunate.

    • 20 * 2.4 = 48 mg. So Option B looks correct.
    • However, often in clinical practice, or older guidelines, 3.0mg/kg was used generally? Or maybe 2.4 is for adults? WHO 2015 guidelines: "Children weighing less than 20 kg should receive a higher dose of artesunate (3 mg/kg per dose) than larger children and adults (2.4 mg/kg per dose)".

33.
A 6 year old is brought to you with an abdominal mass that was coincidentally found during bathing. It is fast growing and you suspect a tumor. What is your impression?
A. Neuroblastoma
B. Nephrolastoma
C. Hepatoma
D. Rhabdomyosarcoma
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Nephrolastoma (Wilms tumor)

Explanation: Nephroblastoma (Wilms Tumor) is the most common renal malignancy in children. The classic presentation is an asymptomatic abdominal mass found incidentally by parents while bathing or dressing the child. It typically occurs in children aged 2-5 years.

Neuroblastoma usually presents as a mass that crosses the midline and the child is often quite sick (fever, bone pain). Wilms tumor usually does not cross the midline and the child often appears well ("happy child with a mass").

34.
A 6 year old is brought to you with fever, bruising, easy fatigability and gum swelling. On examination she is pale and febrile, full hemogram shows neutrophilia. What is your impression?
A. Lymphoma
B. Septicemia
C. Leukemia
D. Anemia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Leukemia

Explanation: The clinical presentation is highly suggestive of Acute Leukemia (specifically Acute Myeloid Leukemia - AML, which often presents with gum hypertrophy/swelling).

  • Bone Marrow Failure signs: Pallor/Fatigability (Anemia), Bruising (Thrombocytopenia).
  • Infiltration signs: Gum swelling (gingival hypertrophy), Fever (neutropenia/infection, though neutrophilia is mentioned, high white cell counts with blasts are common).

Lymphoma (A) typically presents with lymphadenopathy. Septicemia (B) causes fever and sickness but gum swelling is distinct for leukemia. Anemia (D) is a sign, not the diagnosis.

35.
A 8 year child is brought to you with history of fever and night sweats for 3 weeks now. On examination, there are cervical node swelling, and the child appears wasted. Which is the appropriate investigation for this child in this case?
A. Bone marrow aspirate
B. Cervical lymph node biopsy
C. Blood culture and sensitivity
D. Gene expert
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Cervical lymph node biopsy

Explanation: The presentation (chronic fever, night sweats, wasting, cervical lymphadenopathy) suggests TB Adenitis or Lymphoma.

  • Lymph node biopsy (specifically Excision Biopsy) is the definitive investigation to distinguish between Tuberculosis (granulomas, caseation) and Lymphoma/Malignancy.
  • Fine Needle Aspiration (FNA) is often done first, but Biopsy provides architecture.
  • Gene Xpert (D) is excellent for pulmonary TB (sputum), but for lymph nodes, tissue diagnosis (biopsy) or FNA with GeneXpert is needed. Between the options, Biopsy is the most "appropriate" diagnostic standard for persistent lymphadenopathy to rule out malignancy vs TB.
36.
A known child with epilepsy is brought to you with a history of prolonged convulsions taking 1 hour, on examination he is unconscious. What will be your first step?
A. Give Oxygen
B. IV diazepam
C. IM Phenobarbitone
D. Give 10% dextrose
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Give Oxygen

Explanation: The child is in Status Epilepticus (>30 mins of seizure). The management follows the ABCD resuscitation protocol:

  1. A (Airway) & B (Breathing): Ensure patent airway and Give Oxygen (high flow).
  2. C (Circulation): IV access.
  3. D (Drugs/Dextrose): Check blood sugar (Give Dextrose if low) and give Anticonvulsants (IV Diazepam).

While stopping the seizure with Diazepam (B) is critical, the absolute first step in any unconscious/convulsing patient is to ensure oxygenation to prevent hypoxic brain injury.

A 4 year old weighing 15kgs is brought to you unconscious. He complained of headache, fever and convulsions. On examination you find neck stiffness, kerning sign positive. What drug and dose will you give?
A. IV X-pen and gentamycin
B. IV Ceftriaxone 750mg 12 hourly
C. IV ceftriaxone 1500mg 24 hourly
D. IV crystalline and gentamycin 12hourly
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. IV ceftriaxone 1500mg 24 hourly

Explanation: The clinical presentation (Fever, Headache, Convulsions, Neck Stiffness, Positive Kernig's sign) indicates Meningitis.

  • Drug of Choice: IV Ceftriaxone (crosses blood-brain barrier effectively).
  • Dose: 100 mg/kg/day given once daily (OD) or divided BD.

38.
A child is brought to you with history of delayed speech he does not play with others and he cannot withstand any changes in the environment. What is your impression?
A. Attention deficit hyperactive disorder (ADHD)
B. Autism
C. Conduct disorder
D. Opposition defiance disorder (ODD)
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Autism

39.
A 10 year old is brought to you with pain in the lower limb. He attributes it to injury while playing, an x-rays shows reduced bone density and sunburst appearance. What is your impression?
A. Ewing's sarcoma
B. Osteomyelitis
C. Osteosarcoma
D. Myositis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Osteosarcoma

Explanation:

  • Sunburst appearance: This is a classic radiographic sign of Osteosarcoma (due to calcified tumor spicules radiating from the bone).
  • Reduced bone density: Osteolysis.
  • Clinical: Bone pain in a 10-year-old (often misattributed to minor trauma).

Ewing's Sarcoma typically shows an "Onion-skin" appearance. Osteomyelitis would show lytic lesions, sequestrum, or involucrum, not a sunburst pattern.

40.
A child is rescued from a swimming pool. What will be the immediate cause of death?
A. Hypoxemia
B. Cardiac arrest
C. Fluid overload
D. Hypothermia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Hypoxemia

Explanation: In drowning/submersion injuries, the primary physiological insult is Hypoxemia (lack of oxygen). Water aspiration causes surfactant washout and alveolar collapse, or laryngospasm prevents air entry. This profound hypoxemia leads to cardiac arrhythmias and eventually Cardiac Arrest (B). However, hypoxemia is the cause that leads to the arrest. The question asks for the "cause of death" - in pathophysiological terms, hypoxemia is the primary driver. If forced to choose the "mechanism" of death, it is cardiac arrest, but hypoxemia is the underlying cause.

41.
A 2 weeks old baby is brought to you because she is not breast feeding and is sleeping a lot. On examination she is febrile and weighs 4kgs. Write a treatment sheet for this baby. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Diagnosis: Neonatal Sepsis (Danger signs: Not feeding, lethargy/sleeping, fever).

Treatment Sheet:

  1. Admit to Newborn Unit (NBU).
  2. Resuscitate: Ensure airway is patent, give oxygen if SpO2 <90% or respiratory distress.
  3. Fluid Management:
    • If able to tolerate oral feeds: Expressed Breast Milk (EBM) via NGT or cup.
    • If unable to feed/unstable: IV 10% Dextrose/Saline maintenance (60-150ml/kg depending on day of life).
  4. Antibiotics (First Line):
    • Inj. Ampicillin 200mg (50mg/kg) IV 12 hourly. (Calculation: 4kg 50mg = 200mg).
    • Inj. Gentamicin 12mg (3mg/kg) IV daily (OD). (Calculation: 4kg 3mg = 12mg).
  5. Supportive Care:
    • Control fever: Tepid sponging or Paracetamol 60mg (15mg/kg) if temp >38.5C.
    • Keep warm (Kangaroo mother care or incubator).
    • Monitor vitals (Temp, RR, HR, SpO2).
42.
A known asthmatic child is brought to you wheezing. On examination you find head nodding and cyanosis. Outline the management steps. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Diagnosis: Severe Acute Asthma Attack (Life-threatening features: Cyanosis, Head nodding).

Management Steps:

  1. Immediate Oxygen Therapy: Give high-flow oxygen via non-rebreather mask to maintain SpO2 >94%.
  2. Rapid Bronchodilation:
    • Nebulize with Salbutamol (2.5mg if <5yrs, 5mg if >5yrs) immediately.
    • Repeat nebulization every 20-30 minutes for the first hour.
    • Add Ipratropium Bromide (250mcg) to the nebulization if response is poor.
  3. Systemic Corticosteroids:
    • Give IV Hydrocortisone (4mg/kg) or Oral Prednisolone (1-2mg/kg) immediately to reduce inflammation.
  4. Magnesium Sulfate: If no improvement after 1 hour, consider IV Magnesium Sulfate (50mg/kg) over 20 mins.
  5. Monitoring & Reassessment: Monitor respiratory rate, work of breathing, and SpO2 continuously. If deteriorating, prepare for ICU/Ventilation.
43.
A 4-year-old child is brought to you because he was abandoned. He is wasted with MUAC of 11 cm and lower limb swelling. Outline your management steps. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Diagnosis: Severe Acute Malnutrition (SAM) - Marasmic-Kwashiorkor (Wasted + Edema).

Management (WHO 10 Steps):

Stabilization Phase (Days 1-7):

  1. Treat/Prevent Hypoglycemia: Give 10% Dextrose or F-75 feed immediately.
  2. Treat/Prevent Hypothermia: Keep warm, kangaroo care, dry promptly.
  3. Treat/Prevent Dehydration: Use ReSoMal (oral) conservatively; avoid IV fluids unless in shock.
  4. Correct Electrolyte Imbalance: Give Potassium and Magnesium (usually in F-75/Resomal); Do NOT give Sodium.
  5. Treat Infection: Give broad-spectrum antibiotics (e.g., Amoxicillin or Ampicillin/Gentamicin) to all SAM children.
  6. Correct Micronutrient Deficiencies: Vitamin A (stat), Folic acid, Zinc, Multivitamins (Iron is withheld in this phase).
  7. Start Cautious Feeding: F-75 formula (low protein/sodium, high carb) every 2-3 hours.

Rehabilitation Phase (Week 2-6):

  1. Catch-up Growth: Transition to F-100 or RUTF (Ready-to-Use Therapeutic Food); add Iron.
  2. Sensory Stimulation: Play therapy and loving care.
  3. Prepare for Follow-up: Education, immunizations, discharge planning.
44.
A 3-year-old presents to you with easy fatigability and headache, on examination you find him pale. Explain your investigations to rule out 3 differential diagnosis. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Main Sign: Severe Pallor (Anemia).

Investigations & Differentials:

  1. Blood Slide (BS) / Rapid Diagnostic Test (RDT):
    • To rule out: Malaria (parasites cause hemolysis).
  2. Stool Microscopy:
    • To rule out: Hookworm Infestation (chronic blood loss).
  3. Full Blood Count (FBC) / Peripheral Blood Film (PBF):
    • To rule out: Iron Deficiency Anemia (Microcytic Hypochromic) vs Megaloblastic Anemia (Macrocytic).
    • To rule out: Leukemia (presence of blasts).
  4. Sickling Test / Hb Electrophoresis:
    • To rule out: Sickle Cell Disease (hemolytic anemia).
45.
A child has history of fever, convulsions, jaundice, and tiredness. On examination he is pale. He was diagnosed of severe malaria. Manage (5 marks)
Show Answer

Diagnosis: Severe Malaria.

Management:

  1. Admit the patient.
  2. Specific Antimalarial Treatment:
    • Give IV Artesunate 2.4 mg/kg (or 3.0 mg/kg if <20kg) stat.
    • Repeat dose at 12 hours and 24 hours.
    • Then daily for 6 days (or switch to full oral AL course once patient can tolerate oral meds).
  3. Manage Complications:
    • Convulsions: IV Diazepam 0.3mg/kg.
    • Anemia (Pale): Transfuse Packed Red Blood Cells (if Hb <4g/dl or <6g/dl with distress) at 10-15ml/kg.
    • Hypoglycemia (Tiredness): Check RBS, give 10% Dextrose 2-5ml/kg if low.
    • Fever: Paracetamol.
  4. Monitoring: Vital signs, neurological status (AVPU), urine output (for blackwater fever/AKI), and blood glucose.
46.
A 2-year-old is brought to you unconscious. He mistakenly took an unknown substance about 2 hours prior. On examination he is unconscious with pin pointed pupils, frothing and a wet chest. He smells of a chemical. What is your diagnosis? Manage this patient. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Diagnosis: Organophosphate Poisoning (Cholinergic toxidrome: Pinpoint pupils, excessive secretions/frothing, wet chest/bronchorrhea, chemical smell).

Management:

  1. Resuscitation (ABC):
    • Secure Airway, Suction secretions vigorously.
    • Give High-flow Oxygen.
  2. Decontamination:
    • Remove contaminated clothing, wash skin (if dermal exposure).
    • Gastric lavage (if within 1-2 hours and airway protected) - caution due to aspiration risk.
  3. Antidote (Atropinization):
    • Give IV Atropine 0.05mg/kg every 15 minutes.
    • Target: Titrate until signs of atropinization (Dry secretions/chest clear, pupils dilate, tachycardia, flushing).
    • Maintain atropinization.
  4. Specific Antidote (Oximes):
    • Administer Pralidoxime (if available) to regenerate Acetylcholinesterase.
  5. Supportive: IV fluids, monitor vitals, admit to ICU.
47.
A child presents to you with history of fever, arthralgia, and previously has had history of repeated episodes of upper respiratory tract infection. On examination there is tenderness on the left knee joint. What is the treatment of this child? (5 marks)
Show Answer

Diagnosis: Acute Rheumatic Fever (Jones Criteria: Arthralgia/Arthritis, Fever, Hx of recurrent Strep throat).

Treatment:

  1. Eradication of Streptococcus:
    • Single dose of Benzathine Penicillin G IM (600,000 IU for <27kg, 1.2M IU for >27kg).
    • Or Oral Penicillin V for 10 days.
  2. Anti-inflammatory Therapy:
    • Aspirin: High dose (80-100 mg/kg/day) for arthritis relief. Taper over weeks.
    • (Steroids are reserved for cases with severe carditis/heart failure).
  3. Supportive Care:
    • Bed rest (strict if carditis present).
    • Manage heart failure if present.
  4. Secondary Prophylaxis:
    • Initiate long-term Benzathine Penicillin G injections every 4 weeks to prevent recurrence.
48.
A 2year old child presents to you with polyuria, polydipsia and not gaining weight. On examination he has acetone breath and is severely dehydrated. State your investigations and treatment for this patient. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Diagnosis: Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA) - New onset Type 1 Diabetes.

Investigations:

  • Random Blood Sugar (RBS): Will be high (>11 mmol/L).
  • Urinalysis: Check for Ketones (+++) and Glucose.
  • Blood Gas (ABG/VBG): Check for metabolic acidosis (pH <7.3).
  • Urea & Electrolytes (U/Es): Check for Hypo/Hyperkalemia and dehydration status.

Treatment:

  1. Fluid Resuscitation:
    • Start IV Normal Saline (0.9%).
    • Give bolus 10-20ml/kg slowly over 1 hour (caution: risk of cerebral edema).
    • Calculate deficit and replace over 24-48 hours.
  2. Insulin Therapy:
    • Start IV Soluble Insulin infusion (0.1 units/kg/hour).
    • Do NOT give bolus insulin. Start 1 hour after fluids have started.
  3. Potassium Replacement:
    • Add KCl to fluids once urine output is established (insulin drives K+ into cells, risking hypokalemia).
  4. Monitor: Hourly glucose, neuro status (for cerebral edema), Vitals.

Section 3: Long Answer Question (20 Marks)

49.
A 3 year old is brought to you unconscious. The mother reports of 2 episodes of convulsions at home. He was previously treated for malaria. On examination you find an unconscious child febrile neck stiffness and a positive kerning sign. Discuss your investigations and management of this patient (20 marks)
Show Answer

Diagnosis: Bacterial Meningitis (likely partially treated or resistant Malaria complication, but clinical signs strongly point to Meningitis: Neck stiffness, Kernig's sign, Fever, Convulsions).

1. Immediate Stabilization (Resuscitation)

  • Airway & Breathing: Ensure patent airway, give oxygen to maintain SpO2 >94%.
  • Circulation: Secure IV access. Treat shock if present.
  • Control Seizures: If convulsing, give IV Diazepam (0.3mg/kg) or Phenobarbitone.
  • Hypoglycemia: Check RBS. If <3mmol/L, give 10% Dextrose 5ml/kg.

2. Investigations

  • Lumbar Puncture (CSF Analysis):
    • Crucial for diagnosis. Check: Appearance (cloudy), Cell count (high neutrophils), Protein (high), Glucose (low).
    • Contraindication check: Ensure no signs of raised ICP (papilledema, Cushing's triad) before LP.
    • Culture & Sensitivity: To identify organism (e.g., Pneumococcus, Meningococcus, H. influenzae).
  • Full Blood Count (Hemogram): Check for leukocytosis (neutrophilia) and anemia.
  • Blood Culture: To detect septicemia.
  • Blood Slide/mRDT: To re-evaluate malaria status (cerebral malaria differential or co-infection).
  • Urea & Electrolytes: Monitor hydration and sodium (SIADH risk).
  • Blood Glucose: Monitor for hypoglycemia.

3. Definitive Management (Antibiotics)

  • Start empiric antibiotics immediately after taking samples (or before LP if delayed).
  • Choice: IV Ceftriaxone 100 mg/kg/day (Max 4g) once daily.
  • OR IV Crystalline Penicillin (50,000 IU/kg q6h) + Chloramphenicol (25mg/kg q6h) if Ceftriaxone unavailable.
  • Duration: 10-14 days depending on organism.

4. Adjuvant Therapy

  • Dexamethasone: 0.15 mg/kg q6h for 4 days. Start before or with first dose of antibiotics to reduce hearing loss and neurological sequelae (especially for H. influenzae/Pneumococcal).

5. Supportive Care & Monitoring

  • Fluids: Restrict fluids to 2/3 maintenance initially (due to risk of SIADH/Cerebral Edema). Monitor hydration.
  • Fever Control: Paracetamol and tepid sponging.
  • Feeding: NGT feeding if unconscious (once stable), aiming for calories.
  • Neurological Monitoring: Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) or AVPU, pupil size/reaction regularly.
  • Seizure Control: Maintain on Phenobarbitone if seizures recur.

6. Prevention/Public Health

  • Check vaccination status (Hib, PCV, Meningococcal).
  • Chemoprophylaxis for close contacts if Meningococcal (Rifampicin/Ciprofloxacin).
