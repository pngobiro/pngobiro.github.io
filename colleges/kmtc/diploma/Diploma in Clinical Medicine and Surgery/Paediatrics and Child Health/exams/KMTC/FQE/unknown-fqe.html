SECTION A: Multiple Choice Questions (MCQs) - 40 MARKS

1.
When taking history in children, which statement is true?
a) Closed questions should not be use
b) A 4-year-old child can be relied on to give history
c) A child should remain still for the clinician to successfully take the history
d) The presenting complaints should be listed in the order in which they are stated by the mother
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) The presenting complaints should be listed in the order in which they are stated by the mother

Explanation: Presenting complaints are typically recorded in the informant's (mother's) words and order or chronological order of symptom appearance.
Why others are wrong:

  • a) Closed questions are necessary for specific details (e.g., "Did he vomit?").
  • b) A 4-year-old lacks cognitive maturity for reliable medical history; the caregiver is primary.
  • c) Children are naturally active; enforcing stillness is often impossible and hinders rapport.

2.
As regards physical examination in children:
a) Abdominal auscultation should be done before palpation
b) The apex is normally found in the 5th intercostal space mi-clavicular line
c) Pleural effusion gives a resonant note on percussion
d) ENT examination should be done at the beginning of the systemic examination
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Abdominal auscultation should be done before palpation

Explanation: In abdominal exams, palpation can alter bowel sounds, so auscultation is performed first.
Why others are wrong:

  • b) In children <7 years, the apex is usually in the 4th ICS lateral to MCL. 5th ICS is for older children/adults.
  • c) Pleural effusion causes stony dullness, not resonance.
  • d) ENT exams are invasive/distressing and are done last to maintain cooperation.

3.
TORCHES include the following EXCEPT:
a) German measles
b) Cryptococcus infection
c) Toxoplasmosis
d) Herpes simplex
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Cryptococcus infection

Explanation: TORCH stands for Toxoplasmosis, Other (Syphilis, Varicella, Parvo B19), Rubella (German measles), CMV, Herpes/HIV/Hepatitis. Cryptococcus is not a congenital TORCH infection.

4.
TRUE of neonatal infections is that:
a) A negative blood culture rules out sepsis
b) The commonest cause of ophthalmia neonatorum is Neisseria gonorrhea
c) The correct duration for the treatment of neonatal sepsis is 5 day
d) The treatment of choice for cord infection currently in Kenya is 7% Chlorhexidine
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) The treatment of choice for cord infection currently in Kenya is 7% Chlorhexidine

Explanation: 7.1% Chlorhexidine digluconate gel is the standard care for cord care in Kenya.
Why others are wrong:

  • a) Clinical sepsis can exist with negative cultures (culture-negative sepsis).
  • b) Chlamydia trachomatis is more common than Gonorrhea.
  • c) Treatment is typically 7-10 days (or 14-21 for meningitis).

5.
In the care of large for gestational age infants, the most common problem in the first 24 hours after delivery is:
a) Hyperglycemia
b) Hypoglycemia
c) Birth injury
d) Hypothermia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Hypoglycemia

Explanation: LGA infants (often infants of diabetic mothers) have hyperinsulinemia, which leads to rapid hypoglycemia after the maternal glucose supply is cut off at birth.

7.
A one-year-old is brought to the MCH clinic for the routine monitoring. What statement below reflects normal growth and development at this age?
a) A weight of 14kgs
b) A head circumference of 47cm
c) Crawling
d) A 2-word sentence
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) A head circumference of 47cm

Explanation: Average HC at 1 year is ~45-47cm.
Why others are wrong:

  • a) Average weight at 1 year is ~10kg (Triple birth weight). 14kg is >97th centile.
  • c) Crawling is typically 8-9 months. 1-year-olds walk (or cruise).
  • d) 2-word sentences appear around 2 years. 1-year-olds say single words (Mama, Dada).

8.
Poliomyelitis is a notifiable disease. A correct statement about poliomyelitis is that:
a) Most of the infections result in paralysis
b) It leads to an ascending paralysis similar to Gullian Barre Syndrome
c) Full recovery follows paralytic disease
d) Intramuscular injections increase the risk of paralytic disease
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Intramuscular injections increase the risk of paralytic disease

Explanation: This is known as Provocation Polio. IM injections during the viremic phase can precipitate paralysis in the injected limb.
Why others are wrong:

  • a) >90% are asymptomatic; <1% result in paralysis.
  • b) Polio is typically asymmetric; GBS is ascending symmetric.
  • c) Paralytic polio often leaves residual weakness; full recovery is not guaranteed.

9.
Choose among the listed conditions an acyanotic congenital heart disease:
a) Patent ductus arteriosus
b) Truncus arteriosus
c) Ebstein anomaly
d) Tetralogy of fallot
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Patent ductus arteriosus

Explanation: PDA is a Left-to-Right shunt (Acyanotic). The others (Truncus, Ebstein's, TOF) are Cyanotic heart defects.

10.
Congestive cardiac failure in children is characterized by which of the following cardinal features on physical examination?
a) Crying a lot
b) Cyanosis
c) Elevated temperature
d) Displaced apex
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Displaced apex

Explanation: Cardinal signs of CCF in children are Tachycardia, Tachypnea, Tender Hepatomegaly, and Cardiomegaly. A displaced apex beat is the clinical sign of cardiomegaly.

11.
Identify among the following that which belongs to the minor criteria for the diagnosis of Acute Rheumatic fever:
a) Sydenham’s chorea
b) Elevated Antistreptolysin O titres
c) Prolonged PR interval
d) Migratory Polyarthritis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Prolonged PR interval

Explanation:

  • Major Criteria: Carditis, Polyarthritis (d), Chorea (a), Erythema Marginatum, Subcutaneous Nodules.
  • Minor Criteria: Fever, Arthralgia, Elevated ESR/CRP, Prolonged PR interval.
  • ASOT (b) is evidence of antecedent infection, not a criterion itself.

12.
Joe is admitted with features that are highly suggestive of pulmonary tuberculosis. Which of the following is true of PTB in children?
a) Children suffer open as opposed to close PTB
b) Isoniazid is associated with peripheral neuropathy
c) Sputum is easily obtained through coughing up in all ages of children
d) The total duration of treatment is 4 months
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Isoniazid is associated with peripheral neuropathy

Explanation: Isoniazid (INH) causes pyridoxine (Vit B6) deficiency leading to neuropathy; B6 is usually co-administered.
Why others are wrong:

  • a) Children usually have paucibacillary (closed) TB.
  • c) Children swallow sputum; gastric lavage or induced sputum is needed.
  • d) Standard treatment is 6 months (2RHZE + 4RH).

13.
Hookworms in the paediatric age group are likely to cause:
a) Iron deficiency anaemia
b) Bloody stools
c) Intestinal obstruction
d) Severe vomiting
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Iron deficiency anaemia

Explanation: Hookworms attach to the intestinal wall and suck blood, leading to chronic blood loss and IDA. Obstruction is typical of Ascaris.

14.
Dysentery is most likely to be caused by which of the following organism?
a) Rota virus
b) Entamoeba histolytica
c) Giardia lamblia
d) Campylobacter jejuni
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Entamoeba histolytica

Explanation: Dysentery is defined as bloody diarrhea. The classic causes are Shigella (Bacillary dysentery) and Entamoeba histolytica (Amoebic dysentery). Rota and Giardia cause watery diarrhea.

15.
True of upper airway infections in children is:
a) Epiglottitis is commonly caused by viruses
b) Enlarged adenoids are likely to present with mouth breathing
c) Laryngotracheobronchitis (croup) is best treated with antibiotics
d) It is safe to use a spatula to examine the throat if one suspects diphtheria
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Enlarged adenoids are likely to present with mouth breathing

Explanation: Adenoids obstruct the nasopharynx, forcing mouth breathing.
Why others are wrong:

  • a) Epiglottitis is bacterial (Hib).
  • c) Croup is viral; treated with steroids/adrenaline (not antibiotics).
  • d) Spatulas can dislodge pseudomembranes in diphtheria causing airway obstruction.

16.
Regarding the WHO classification and management of dehydration:
a) Some dehydration is best managed with IV Ringers Lactate
b) A weak or absent pulse is a sign of severe dehydration
c) Hypovolemic shock is first treated with IV boluses of Normal saline
d) No dehydration does not require any treatment in the presence of loose stools
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Hypovolemic shock is first treated with IV boluses of Normal saline

Explanation: Shock requires rapid fluid resuscitation with isotonic crystalloid boluses (20ml/kg).
Note: B is also true (weak pulse = severe dehydration/shock), but C is a specific management action often tested. Given "Hypovolemic shock is first treated...", C describes the action. However, looking at WHO guidelines, shock is severe dehydration. IV fluids are for severe. "Some" gets ORS. "No" gets Plan A (Zinc/Fluids). Option C is the most distinct "Action" statement, but B is factually correct as a sign. Usually, shock management questions prioritize the bolus.

17.
UTI in young children is likely to present with:
a) Dysuria
b) Frequency
c) Enuresis
d) Failure to thrive
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Failure to thrive

Explanation: In young children/infants, UTI symptoms are non-specific (fever, vomiting, poor feeding, FTT). Specific urinary symptoms (Dysuria, Frequency) are reported by older children.

18.
Which of the statement below is correct about Nephrotic syndrome?
a) It presents with mild periorbital oedema
b) It occurs after a throat infection with GABH streptococcus
c) Bee stings is a differential diagnosis
d) IV Dexamethasone is the main drug used in its treatment
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Bee stings is a differential diagnosis

Explanation: Severe allergic reaction (Angioedema) from bee stings causes rapid swelling and is a differential for sudden onset edema.
Why others are wrong:

  • a) Presents with massive generalized edema.
  • b) This describes Nephritic (APSGN).
  • d) Oral Prednisolone is the mainstay, not IV Dexamethasone.

19.
Acute kidney injury:
a) Mostly results in chronic kidney disease
b) Severe dehydration is a common cause in children
c) Hypertension is present in most cases
d) Convulsions do not occur in this condition
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Severe dehydration is a common cause in children

Explanation: Pre-renal causes (gastroenteritis/dehydration) are the most common cause of AKI in children. Most recover (don't get CKD). HTN is variable. Convulsions can occur (uremia/hypertensive encephalopathy).

20.
A feature of Hypothyroidism in infants among the following is:
a) Crying all the time
b) Good appetite
c) Poor cold tolerance
d) Small tongue
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Poor cold tolerance

Explanation: Features include hypothermia/cold intolerance, lethargy (quiet, not crying), poor feeding (not good appetite), and macroglossia (large tongue).

23.
The test that is included as part of cerebrospinal fluid analysis is:
a) The protein level

24.
The correct level of placing the lumbar puncture needle in children is between:
a) L1 and L2
b) L2 and L3
c) L3 and L4
d) L4 and L5
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) L4 and L5

Explanation: In infants and young children, the spinal cord may terminate lower (L3) than adults (L1/L2). Therefore, a lower interspace (L4-L5) is safer to avoid cord damage.

25.
Convulsions are common in children. True of convulsions is that:
a) Febrile convulsions occur in the 6 months to 4 years’ window
b) Low magnesium levels are a contributing factor
c) A history of a fall from a height is not significant
d) The most common type is the tonic type
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Febrile convulsions occur in the 6 months to 4 years’ window

Explanation: The standard age range for febrile seizures is 6 months to 5 (or 6) years. Option A is the best definition match.
Note: Low magnesium CAN cause seizures, but A is a definitional truth of the most common type. The most common seizure type is generalized tonic-clonic, not just tonic.

26.
Sickle cell disease:
a) Is an autosomal dominant inherited disorder
b) Presents with hand and foot syndrome in children older than 2 years
c) Life span in these patients is less than 20 years
d) The painful crisis is the same as the thrombotic crisis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) The painful crisis is the same as the thrombotic crisis

Explanation: The painful vaso-occlusive crisis is caused by sickled cells blocking blood vessels (thrombosis/ischemia).
Corrections: Autosomal Recessive (a); Hand-foot syndrome occurs in infants/toddlers <2yrs (b); Lifespan is significantly longer now (c).

31.
Which of the following is true concerning HIV infection in children?

b) An HIV virologic test is necessary to make a diagnosis of HIV in children of all ages.

34.

Section 2: SHORT ANSWER QUESTIONS (SAQs) - 40MARKS

1.
Baby Anita is born at 30 weeks’ gestation. She is stable but is admitted to the new born unit.
Show Answer

a) State 6 features of a prematurity. (3 Marks)

  1. Small size (Low birth weight).
  2. Thin, transparent skin with visible veins.
  3. Abundant Lanugo hair.
  4. Little subcutaneous fat.
  5. Undescended testes (boys) or gaping labia (girls).
  6. Soft ear cartilage (slow recoil).
  7. Weak cry and poor sucking reflex.

b) State 4 aspects of supportive management for preterm babies. (2 Marks)

  1. Thermoregulation: Keep warm (Incubator/KMC).
  2. Nutrition: Feeding via NGT or cup (Breastmilk) + Fluids.
  3. Respiratory Support: Oxygen or CPAP if distressed.
  4. Infection Prevention: Hygiene, cord care.

Bacterial Meningitis.

  1. Lumbar Puncture (CSF Analysis): Cell count, Protein, Glucose, Gram Stain.
  2. Complete Blood Count (CBC): Leucocytosis.

  1. IV Ceftriaxone (or Benzylpenicillin + Chloramphenicol).
  2. IV Dexamethasone (Adjunct).

SECTION 3: LONG ANSWER QUESTION (LAQs) - 20MARKS

1.
Abigael is a 3-year-old who accompanied the parents to visit the grand mum at her home in Nyanza 2 weeks ago. She now presents with hotness of the body and vomiting in the last 24 hours. She is also reported to have had a convulsion 2 hours ago. On examination she has severe pallor and jaundice. Abdominal examination reveals a tender hepatomegaly of 4cm below the costal margin. The clinical diagnosis is severe Malaria and the haemoglobin is 4g/dl.
Show Answer

a) Name 4 stages of the malaria parasite in the human host. (4 Marks)

  1. Hepatic Schizonts: Exo-erythrocytic stage in liver.
  2. Merozoites: Released from liver into blood.
  3. Trophozoites (Ring stage): Inside RBCs.
  4. Erythrocytic Schizonts: Mature parasites in RBCs.
  5. Gametocytes: Sexual stage infective to mosquitoes.

b) State the role of the thick and thin film in the diagnosis of Malaria. (4 Marks)

  • Thick Film: Used for detection of parasites (higher sensitivity) and quantification of parasite density.
  • Thin Film: Used for species identification (e.g., P. falciparum vs P. vivax) and assessing parasite morphology.

c) What is the definitive treatment for Abigael given the above narrative? (4 Marks)

Abigael has Severe Malaria (Severe Anemia Hb 4, Jaundice, Convulsions).
Treatment:

  • IV Artesunate 3mg/kg (since <20kg) at 0, 12, and 24 hours.
  • Followed by a full course of Artemether-Lumefantrine (AL) once she can tolerate oral medication.

d) Outline 4 ways in which malaria can be effectively controlled at the community level. (4 Marks)

  1. Use of Insecticide Treated Nets (ITNs).
  2. Indoor Residual Spraying (IRS).
  3. Drainage of stagnant water (Environmental management).
  4. Clearing bushes around homesteads.
  5. Public education on early treatment seeking.

e) How would you manage Abigael if she presented to you having a convulsion? (4 Marks)

  1. Airway/Breathing: Place in left lateral position, clear airway, give Oxygen.
  2. Anticonvulsant: Give Rectal Diazepam (0.5mg/kg) or IV Diazepam (0.3mg/kg) slowly.
  3. Hypoglycemia: Check RBS; if low, give 10% Dextrose 5ml/kg IV.
  4. Fever: Tepid sponging/Paracetamol (per rectum).
