SECTION 1: MULTIPLE CHOICE QUESTIONS (40 MARKS)

1.
In regards to newborn examination, which of the following statements is correct?
A) Initial examination should be performed after one week
B) Breast hypertrophy is uncommon and when present denotes a problem
C) The liver is usually not palpable
D) Imperforate anus is not always visible and may require evidence by gentle insertion of the little finger.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) Imperforate anus is not always visible and may require evidence by gentle insertion of the little finger.

Explanation:

  • A: Initial exam is done immediately at birth and within 24 hours.
  • B: Breast hypertrophy is common due to maternal hormones and is normal.
  • C: The liver is usually palpable 1-2 cm below the costal margin in newborns.
  • D: Checking patency of the anus is a critical part of the newborn exam, as some membranous obstructions may not be immediately obvious on inspection alone.

2.
Which of the following is a complication of phototherapy?
A) Vomiting
B) Cyanosis
C) Respiratory distress
D) Skin rashes
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) Skin rashes

Explanation: Common side effects of phototherapy include skin rashes (maculopapular), dehydration, loose stools, hyperthermia, and "Bronze Baby Syndrome" (if conjugated bilirubin is high). Vomiting and cyanosis are not direct complications.

3.
Which of the following reflexes is correctly matched with the appropriate age?
A) Moro reflex at birth
B) Grasp reflex at 1 year
C) Ankle clonus at 73 months
D) Extensor plantar at 48 months.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Moro reflex at birth

Explanation:

  • A: The Moro reflex is present at birth and disappears by 3-6 months.
  • B: Palmar grasp disappears by 3-4 months.
  • D: Extensor plantar (Babinski) is normal up to 2 years, but abnormal at 48 months.

4.
What is most likely cause of diarrhoea in children less than five (5) years according to World Health Organization?
A) Arbovirus
B) Bacteria
C) Parvovirus
D) Rota virus
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) Rota virus

Explanation: Rotavirus is the leading cause of severe dehydrating diarrhea in children under 5 years globally.

5.
Corticosteroid are indicated primarily at what symptom of Acute rheumatic fever?
A) Rash
B) Fever
C) Arthritits
D) Severe carditis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) Severe carditis

Explanation: Corticosteroids (Prednisolone) are reserved for severe Rheumatic Carditis (especially with cardiomegaly or heart failure) to reduce inflammation. Aspirin is used for arthritis and fever.

6.
Which of the following is indicated in the management of hemophilia in children?
A) Omeprazole
B) Fresh frozen plasma
C) Avoid Aspirin
D) Digoxin
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Fresh frozen plasma

Explanation: Fresh Frozen Plasma (FFP) contains clotting factors (including Factor VIII and IX) and is used when specific factor concentrates are unavailable.
Note: Option C "Avoid Aspirin" is a correct precaution/contraindication, but "indicated in management" usually refers to a therapeutic intervention.

7.
Earliest sign of rickets is;
A) Rachitic rosary
B) Craniotabes
C) Bow legs
D) Harrison groove
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Craniotabes

Explanation: Craniotabes (softening of the skull bones, often feeling like a ping-pong ball) is usually the earliest physical sign of rickets in infants (3-6 months). Rachitic rosary and Harrison's groove appear later.

8.
A 5 year old boy is admitted with convulsions. A sample of CSF was taken to the lab. The appearance was cloudy, WBC count is high and the glucose content was low. Which type of meningitis is this most likely to be?
A) Bacterial
B) Viral
C) Fungal (cryptococal)
D) Tuberculous (TB)
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Bacterial

Explanation:

  • Bacterial: Cloudy appearance, High Neutrophils, Low Glucose (<50% plasma), High Protein.
  • Viral: Clear, Lymphocytes, Normal Glucose.
  • TB: Fibrin web, Lymphocytes, Very Low Glucose.

9.
An infant is brought to the casualty unconscious. Random blood sugar done reveals the child has hypoglycaemia. On further examination, you find that the child has malnutrition. The correct dose of I.V. 10% glucose for this child would be:
A) 5ml/kg over 5-10 minutes
B) 2 ml/kg over 5-10 minutes
C) 2.5ml/kg over 5 minutes
D) 3 ml/kg stat
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) 5ml/kg over 5-10 minutes

Explanation: The standard bolus dose for hypoglycemia correction in infants and children (including those with malnutrition) is 2-5 ml/kg of 10% Dextrose. In severe malnutrition, guidelines often recommend 5ml/kg of 10% Dextrose orally/NGT or IV if unconscious.

10.
Anticipated problems in a preterm infant include the following except;
A) Hypoglycaemia
B) Polycythaemia
C) Infection
D) Temperature instability
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Polycythaemia

Explanation: Preterm infants are prone to Anemia of prematurity, not polycythemia. Polycythemia is more common in Small for Gestational Age (SGA) or Post-term infants. Hypoglycemia, Infection, and Hypothermia are classic preterm complications.

11.
Which one of the following is not a risk factor for mother to child transmission of HIV infection?
A) Mixed feeding
B) Small for gestation baby
C) Advanced maternal HIV disease
D) Low CD4 counts
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Small for gestation baby

Explanation: While HIV can cause SGA, being SGA itself is not a direct risk factor for transmitting the virus. Mixed feeding, high viral load (advanced disease), and low CD4 counts significantly increase transmission risk.

12.
Tetralogy of fallot include the following anatomical defects except;
A) Congestive Heart failure
B) Ventriculoseptal defect
C) Over-riding of Aorta
D) Pulmonary stenosis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Congestive Heart failure

Explanation: Congestive Heart Failure is a complication or presentation, not an anatomical defect. The 4 anatomical defects of TOF are: 1) VSD, 2) Pulmonary Stenosis, 3) Overriding Aorta, 4) Right Ventricular Hypertrophy. Note that CHF is actually rare in TOF (they present with cyanosis).

13.
Which of the following statements about Rheumatic fever is correct?
A) Commonly follows skin infection due to staphylococcal pyogenes
B) Arthritis causes destruction of joints
C) Rheumatic chorea commonly causes permanent disability of motor co-ordination
D) Aspirin therapy is effective in rheumatic carditis.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) Aspirin therapy is effective in rheumatic carditis.

Explanation:

  • A: Follows pharyngeal infection (throat) by Group A Streptococcus (not Staph).
  • B: Arthritis is migratory and does not cause permanent destruction.
  • C: Chorea (Sydenham's) is self-limiting and resolves without permanent disability.
  • D: Aspirin is the mainstay for arthritis and mild carditis (though steroids are used for severe carditis).

14.
With regards to DKA; which of the following option is not true?
A) Random blood sugars of 8mmol/L
B) Coma occurs
C) Polyuria
D) Responds to insulin therapy
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Random blood sugars of 8mmol/L

Explanation: Diabetic Ketoacidosis is defined by hyperglycemia > 11 mmol/L (usually >200 mg/dL). 8 mmol/L is not diagnostic of DKA.

15.
Regarding nephrotic syndrome;
A) High protein diet is indicated in its management
B) Malaria can cause it
C) Abdominal ultrasound is an important diagnostic tool
D) Steroids are not indicated in treatment.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Malaria can cause it

Explanation:

  • A: High protein diet is controversial/avoided; normal protein is recommended to prevent further kidney damage.
  • B: Plasmodium malariae is a known cause of Quartan Malaria Nephropathy (a form of Nephrotic Syndrome).
  • D: Steroids ARE the primary treatment.

16.
Burkitt’s lymphoma;
A) Is a non-Hodgkin’s lymphoma
B) Commonly presents with abdominal mass
C) May involve the central nervous system.
D) Wilm’s tumor is a differential diagnosis.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Is a non-Hodgkin’s lymphoma

Explanation: Burkitt's is a high-grade B-cell Non-Hodgkin Lymphoma. While sporadic cases present with abdominal masses (B), the endemic African form (common in Kenya) classically presents with a jaw mass.

17.
Diazepam is contraindicated in neonates. One major side-effect of diazepam is;
A) Hyperactivity
B) Drowsiness
C) Dryness of mucous membrane
D) Respiratory depression
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) Respiratory depression

Explanation: Diazepam can cause severe respiratory depression and apnea in neonates. It also displaces bilirubin from albumin, increasing the risk of kernicterus.

18.
Jaundice observed soon after birth (less than 24 hours) in newborn is most likely;
A) Physiologic jaundice
B) Pathological jaundice
C) Neonatal jaundice
D) Obstructive jaundice
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Pathological jaundice

Explanation: Jaundice appearing within the first 24 hours is always pathological until proven otherwise (e.g., hemolysis from ABO/Rh incompatibility, sepsis).

19.
A 10-year old child from the slum presents to you with profuse, watery diarrhoea. It is frequent, has rice water consistency and it is not blood stained/mucoid. What is the most likely diagnosis is?
A) Dysentery
B) Typhoid fever
C) Cholera
D) Amoebiosis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Cholera

Explanation: "Rice water stool" is the classic description of Cholera (Vibrio cholerae). Dysentery and Amoebiasis present with bloody/mucoid stool.

20.
A four-week old boy is brought to you with a history of non-bilious, projectile, vomiting immediately after breastfeeding. On examination there is a peristaltic wave on the abdomen and a palpable olive mass on right side of the umbilicus. What is the most likely diagnosis?
A) Intussusception
B) Hirschsprung’s disease
C) Gastroesophageal reflux
D) Hiatal hernia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: [Diagnosis Missing in Options] - Hypertrophic Pyloric Stenosis

Explanation: The description (4 weeks old, projectile non-bilious vomiting, visible peristalsis, palpable olive mass) is classic for Infantile Hypertrophic Pyloric Stenosis (IHPS). None of the provided options (A, B, C, D) fit the description. Intussusception presents with a sausage-shaped mass and red currant jelly stool at 5-9 months.

21.
The following are classified as cardinal features of CCF in children under five years except;
A) Tachycardia
B) Sacral Oedema
C) Cardiomegaly
D) Hepatomegaly
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Sacral Oedema

Explanation: In infants and young children, the cardinal signs of heart failure are Tachycardia, Tachypnea, and Hepatomegaly (tender). Sacral edema is a sign of right heart failure in bedridden adults; children more commonly present with facial/periorbital edema if edema is present.

22.
In taking a history of a peadiatric patient with constipation, which of the following is the least important question to ask?
A) History of fever
B) Associated abdominal Distention
C) Radiating abdominal pain
D) History of vomiting
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) History of fever

Explanation: Vomiting and distention are critical signs of intestinal obstruction (e.g., Hirschsprung's). Pain is relevant. Fever is less specific to simple constipation but may indicate enterocolitis. In the context of functional constipation history, fever is the least directly relevant compared to signs of obstruction.

23.
Which of the following statements regarding Hepatitis A is correct?
A) It is transmitted through blood transfusion
B) Has no vaccine
C) Incubation Period 2- 3 weeks
D) Has no relationship with sanitary status
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Incubation Period 2- 3 weeks

Explanation: Hepatitis A is fecal-oral (related to sanitation), has a vaccine, and the incubation period is typically 15-50 days (average 28 days/4 weeks, but 2-3 weeks falls within range). It is rarely blood-borne.

24.
A 4 year old presents with some dehydration secondary to gastroenteritis. The fluid deficit should be replaced;
A) Within an hour
B) Within 48 hours
C) Over 6 hours
D) As a bolus
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Over 6 hours

Explanation: For "some dehydration" (Plan B), oral rehydration is given over 4 hours. For "severe dehydration" (Plan C) in infants, it's over 6 hours (3-6 hours). 48 hours is for hypernatremic dehydration. Bolus is for shock.

25.
With regards to Measles infection, which statement is true?
A) Exclusively affects children under 5 years
B) Mainly spread through droplet infection
C) Patient is infectious throughout the entire course of the disease
D) Has over 90% case fatality if infected
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Mainly spread through droplet infection

Explanation: Measles is a highly contagious airborne/droplet disease. It can affect adults (A is false). Infectivity is from prodrome to 4 days after rash onset (C is false). Case fatality is usually 1-5% in developing countries, not 90% (D is false).

26.
The hallmark of refeeding syndrome is the development of severe:
A) Hypophosphatemia
B) Hypokalemia

Persistence Umbilical cord beyond which time should propmpt consideration of an underlying abnormality?

29.
Which of the following defines spina bifida oculta?
A) Infection of the spine

Correct Answer: D) Congenital anomaly due to an unclosed posterior vertebral arch.

Explanation: Spina bifida occulta is a defect in the closure of the posterior vertebral arch without protrusion of the meninges or spinal cord (hidden/occult).

32.
Which is the most commonly used index for assessing nutritional status in children?
A) Height-for-Age
B) Weight-for-Height
C) Mid Upper arm Circumference
D) Weight-for-Age
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) Weight-for-Age

Explanation: Weight-for-Age is the standard measure used in growth monitoring charts (Mother-Child Booklet) for general nutritional assessment over time. Weight-for-Height is specific for wasting (acute malnutrition).

33.
The aim of the rehabilitation phase in the treatment of malnutrition is to
A) Repair Cellular function
B) Correct fluid and electrolyte imbalance
C) Catch up growth
D) Prevent death from Infection
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Catch up growth

Explanation: The Rehabilitation phase (Phase 2) focuses on rapid weight gain ("catch-up growth"). Phase 1 (Stabilization) focuses on correcting fluid/electrolytes, treating infection, and repairing cellular function.

Which of the following is the commonest causative organism of pneumonia in children aged 5 years and older?
A) Streptococcus Pneumoniae
B) Mycoplasma Pneumoniae

36.
Delayed closure of the fontanels can be caused by
A) Dehydration
B) Neonatal Sepsis
C) Rickets
D) Opthalmia Neonatarum
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Rickets

Explanation: Rickets (Vitamin D deficiency) causes delayed bone mineralization and is a classic cause of delayed fontanel closure. Dehydration causes a sunken fontanel; Sepsis causes a bulging fontanel.

39.
John is a 6 year old boy brought to Casualty with a history of diarrhea and vomiting for 2 days. On examination, he is unconscious. What other feature are you likely to find as you examine?
A) Weak thready pulse
B) Fruity breath
C) Hyperactive precordium
D) Jaundice
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Fruity breath

40.
When conducting a newborn resuscitation, what is the correct ratio of giving chest compressions to ventilations?
A) 2:1
B) 5:2
C) 15:2
D) 10:2
Show Answer

Correct Answer: 3:1 (Standard) - None of the options match standard Neonatal Resuscitation Guidelines.

Explanation:

SECTION 2: SHORT ANSWER QUESTIONS (40 MARKS)

41.
A 6 year old girl was admitted with features suggestive of acute glomerulonephritis.
Show Answer

a) State four (4) cardinal features of Acute glomerulonephritis. (2 marks)

  1. Haematuria (Cola-colored urine)
  2. Oedema (Periorbital/facial)
  3. Hypertension
  4. Oliguria (Decreased urine output)

b) Indicate three (3) common laboratory findings that would support this diagnosis. (3 marks)

  1. Urinalysis: RBC casts, dysmorphic RBCs, proteinuria.
  2. Serology: Elevated ASOT (Anti-Streptolysin O Titer) or Anti-DNAse B.
  3. Blood: Low C3 Complement levels; Elevated Urea and Creatinine.
42.
Good care and breastfeeding of neonates is essential for normal growth.
Show Answer

a) Briefly outline five (5) features of good attachment and good positioning in breastfeeding. (2.5 marks)

  1. Chin touching breast.
  2. Mouth wide open.
  3. Lower lip turned outwards.
  4. More areola visible above the baby's mouth than below.
  5. Baby's body straight and facing the mother (tummy to tummy).

b) Outline five (5) advantages of breast feeding. (2.5 marks)

  1. Provides perfect nutrition and easy digestibility.
  2. Transfers maternal antibodies (immunity) protecting against infections.
  3. Promotes bonding between mother and baby.
  4. Promotes uterine involution and reduces postpartum bleeding in mothers.
  5. Cost-effective and always available at the right temperature.
43.
Neonatal sepsis is common in paediatric units in Kenya.
Show Answer

a) Mention four (4) common bacterial causes of Neonatal sepsis. (2 marks)

  1. Group B Streptococcus (GBS)
  2. Escherichia coli (E. coli)
  3. Staphylococcus aureus
  4. Klebsiella pneumoniae

b) Briefly discuss the Specific management of child with neonatal sepsis in new born units. (3 marks)

  • Admission: Admit to Newborn Unit (NBU).
  • Antibiotics: Start empirical IV antibiotics after taking cultures.
    • 1st Line: Ampicillin/Penicillin + Gentamicin.
    • 2nd Line: Ceftriaxone/Cefotaxime (if resistant or meningitis suspected).
  • Supportive Care: Maintain thermoregulation (warmth), correct hypoglycemia, maintain fluid/electrolyte balance, and oxygen therapy if required.

45.
A four-year old child presents at the outpatient clinic with a history of jaundice, dactylitis, hepatosplenomegaly. On laboratory investigation, the full heamogram reveals that he has chronic anaemia.
Show Answer

a). What is the probable diagnosis? (2 marks)

Sickle Cell Disease (Dactylitis/Hand-foot syndrome is classic in young children, along with jaundice, HSM, and anemia).

b). Outline possible crisis indicating most common precipitating factors (3 marks)

  • Vaso-occlusive Crisis (Painful crisis): Precipitated by cold, dehydration, stress.

46.
Concerning asthma in children;
Show Answer

a) Enumerate four (4) clinical features of Asthma in an 8 year old child. (2 marks)

  1. Recurrent wheezing.
  2. Cough (worse at night or after exercise).
  3. Shortness of breath (Dyspnea).

SECTION 3: LONG ANSWER QUESTION (20 MARKS)

49.
A four-year-old was admitted with complications of diarrhoea and vomiting. On examination, the child has a weight of 9kg, oedema of the feet and MUAC (Mid-upper arm circumference) of less than 11.5 cm.
Show Answer

a) What is your probable diagnosis? (2 Marks)

Severe Acute Malnutrition (Kwashiorkor). Diagnosis is based on MUAC < 11.5cm, Weight for age very low (9kg at 4 years is ~ -3SD), and presence of bilateral pitting oedema.

b) What are the predisposing factors to the condition? (6 Marks)

  1. Poverty: Lack of food security.
  2. Infections: Recurrent diarrhea/vomiting (as in history), measles, HIV/TB.
  3. Ignorance/Education: Poor maternal knowledge on weaning/diet.
  4. Early cessation of breastfeeding: Or poor weaning practices.
  5. Large family size: Competition for resources.
  6. Social factors: Broken families, neglect.

c) Which other features will you find on examination? (4 Marks)

  1. Hair changes: Sparse, silky, brownish/discolored.
  2. Skin changes: Flaky paint dermatosis, hyperpigmentation.
  3. Moon face: Rounded cheeks.
  4. Apathy/Irritability: Mental changes.
  5. Hepatomegaly: Fatty liver.

d) Outline the management of the above child. (8 Marks)

Management follows the WHO 10 Steps:

  1. Treat/Prevent Hypoglycemia: Give 10% Dextrose if low. Feed immediately (F-75).
  2. Treat/Prevent Hypothermia: Keep warm, Kangaroo Mother Care.
  3. Treat/Prevent Dehydration: Use ReSoMal (Rehydration Solution for Malnutrition), NOT standard ORS. Avoid IV fluids unless in shock.
  4. Correct Electrolyte Imbalance: Give Potassium and Magnesium. Limit Sodium.
  5. Treat/Prevent Infection: Give empirical broad-spectrum antibiotics.
  6. Correct Micronutrient Deficiencies: Vitamin A, Zinc, Folic Acid, Multivitamins. (Do not give Iron in stabilization phase).
  7. Cautious Feeding (Stabilization): Start F-75 formula (low protein/sodium, high carb) small frequent feeds.
  8. Rehabilitation (Catch-up growth): Switch to F-100 or RUTF once appetite returns and edema resolves.
  9. Sensory Stimulation: Play therapy, tender loving care.
  10. Prepare for Follow-up: Education, vaccinations, discharge planning.
