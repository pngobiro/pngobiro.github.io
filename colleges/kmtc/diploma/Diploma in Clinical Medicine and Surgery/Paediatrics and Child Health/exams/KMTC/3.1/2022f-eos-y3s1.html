SECTION 2: SHORT ANSWER QUESTIONS

1.
A one year old child is brought to pediatrics outpatient clinic with a three day history of diarrhea and vomiting. O/E the child is lethargic has cold hands and feet, capillary refills is > 3 seconds and has a weak pulse. Body weight is 10 kg and has no oedema.
Show Answer

a) What is your diagnosis and what are possible causative agents (3mks)

  • Diagnosis: Severe Dehydration with Hypovolemic Shock (Based on lethargy, cold peripheries, weak pulse, and prolonged CRT).
  • Possible Causative Agents:
    • Viral: Rotavirus (Most common).
    • Bacterial: E. coli, Salmonella, Shigella, Vibrio cholerae.
    • Parasitic: Giardia lamblia, Entamoeba histolytica.

b) Outline your plan of management of this patient giving details of dosages and duration of treatment (7 mks)

  1. Resuscitation (Treat Shock):
    • Give IV Normal Saline or Ringer's Lactate 20ml/kg bolus stat.
    • Dose: $20 \times 10 = 200\text{ml}$ as a bolus.
    • Repeat if signs of shock persist.
  2. Rehydration (Plan C):
    • Once shock resolves, give 100ml/kg IV fluids.
    • Total volume: $100 \times 10 = 1000\text{ml}$.
    • Rate:
      • First 30% (300ml) over 30 minutes (since >12 months old, though some guidelines say 1hr for infants, strictly >1yr is 30 mins).
      • Next 70% (700ml) over 2.5 hours.
  3. Zinc Supplementation: 20mg orally once daily for 14 days.
  4. ORS: Start oral rehydration as soon as the child can drink (5ml/kg/hr).
  5. Antibiotics: If Cholera is suspected or septic shock is considered, give Ciprofloxacin or Ceftriaxone.
  6. Monitoring: Vitals every 15-30 minutes.
2.
Otieno 4 years old is brought to the Hospital with a 4 day history of headache, vomiting and anorexia. He was treated at the health center for malaria and had completed a course of AL on examination he is febrile, AVPU is at P and he has one tonic clonic convulsions during the examination.
Show Answer

a) What are the differential diagnoses - 4 mks

  1. Severe Malaria (Cerebral Malaria): Despite AL treatment (possibility of resistance, vomiting of meds, or severe progression).
  2. Bacterial Meningitis: Fever, headache, vomiting, altered consciousness (P), seizures.
  3. Viral Encephalitis: Similar presentation.
  4. Brain Abscess: Headache, fever, focal signs.

b) Which lab tests would help you make a definite diagnosis - 3 mks

  1. Blood Slide (BS) / RDT for Malaria: To check for parasites (re-infection or treatment failure).
  2. Lumbar Puncture (CSF Analysis): Biochemistry (protein/glucose), Cell count, Gram stain/Culture (Definite for Meningitis).
  3. Full Haemogram (CBC): WBC count (Leukocytosis).
  4. Blood Culture: For sepsis.

c) What is the recommended treatment for this child (3 mks)

  1. Anticonvulsant: IV Diazepam 0.3mg/kg to stop the active convulsion.
  2. Empirical Antibiotics: IV Ceftriaxone 100mg/kg/day (covers Meningitis and Sepsis).
  3. Antimalarial: IV Artesunate 2.4mg/kg (at 0, 12, 24hrs) if malaria slide is positive or high suspicion despite previous AL.
  4. Supportive: Paracetamol for fever, IV fluids for hydration.
3.
A three year old child is brought to hospital by his step mother with a 2 month history of diarrhea, poor feeding and progressive leg swelling. He also has wounds on his legs and perineum. His weight is 7 kg when you examine him you find he is in shock and has kwashiorkor. You get worried because the wounds on his legs look like cigarette burns and he has other wounds on his body at different stages of healing.
Show Answer

a) What is your differential diagnosis (3 mks)

  1. Severe Acute Malnutrition (Kwashiorkor): Edema, low weight (7kg at 3yrs).
  2. Non-Accidental Injury (Child Abuse): Cigarette burns, wounds at different stages of healing, history from stepmother.
  3. Septic Shock: Due to infected wounds and chronic diarrhea.
  4. Chronic Gastroenteritis/Malabsorption: 2 month diarrhea.

b) What is the possible etiology of malnutrition (2 mks)

  1. Social/Neglect: Child abuse/neglect (indicated by burns/wounds).
  2. Inadequate intake: Poor feeding.
  3. Pathological losses: Chronic diarrhea.

c) Outline fluid management for correcting shock then list the ten steps of managing this patient. (5 mks)

Fluid Management for Shock in SAM:

  • Fluids: IV Half Strength Darrow's (HSD) with 5% Dextrose (or Ringer's Lactate with 5% Dextrose).
  • Rate: 15ml/kg over 1 hour.
  • Monitoring: Monitor pulse and respiratory rate every 10 minutes. If improving, repeat 15ml/kg over next hour. If failing, assume septic shock or heart failure.
  • Note: Do NOT use standard shock management (20ml/kg bolus of NS) as it causes heart failure in SAM.

10 Steps of Management (WHO):

  1. Treat/Prevent Hypoglycemia.
  2. Treat/Prevent Hypothermia.
  3. Treat/Prevent Dehydration (ReSoMal).
  4. Correct Electrolyte Imbalance.
  5. Treat/Prevent Infection (Antibiotics + Wound care).
  6. Correct Micronutrient Deficiencies.
  7. Start Cautious Feeding (F-75).
  8. Achieve Catch-up Growth (F-100/RUTF).
  9. Sensory Stimulation (TLC, Play).
  10. Prepare for Follow-up (Social work involvement for abuse).

SECTION 3: LONG ANSWER QUESTIONS

1.
Discuss sickle cell disease, Pathophysiology, diagnosis, and management of sickling crisis (20 mks)
Show Answer

1. Pathophysiology:

  • Genetic Defect: Autosomal recessive disorder caused by a point mutation in the beta-globin gene (Valine substitutes Glutamic acid at position 6).
  • Sickling: Under conditions of low oxygen tension, dehydration, or acidosis, Hemoglobin S (HbS) polymerizes into long rigid chains.
  • RBC Deformation: Red blood cells assume a rigid, crescent "sickle" shape.
  • Consequences:
    • Vaso-occlusion: Sickled cells block small capillaries, leading to tissue ischemia, pain, and organ damage.
    • Hemolysis: Sickled cells are fragile and destroyed rapidly (lifespan 10-20 days vs 120), leading to chronic anemia and jaundice.

2. Diagnosis:

  • Screening Tests: Sickling test (Sodium metabisulfite) or Solubility test (Turbidity).
  • Confirmatory Test: Hemoglobin Electrophoresis (detects HbS, HbF, HbA). HPLC is also used.
  • Complete Blood Count: Low Hb (6-9 g/dl), Reticulocytosis (high).
  • Peripheral Blood Film: Sickle cells, target cells, Howell-Jolly bodies (autosplenectomy).

3. Management of Sickling Crisis (Vaso-Occlusive Crisis):

  1. Pain Management (Analgesia):
    • Mild: Paracetamol + NSAIDs (Ibuprofen).
    • Moderate/Severe: Opioids (Morphine, Tramadol). Patient Controlled Analgesia is effective.
  2. Hydration:
    • Administer fluids at 1.5 times maintenance requirement.
    • Encourage oral intake; use IV Normal Saline if unable to drink. Hydration reduces blood viscosity and unsickles cells.
  3. Oxygen Therapy:
    • Administer if SpO2 < 92% or if in respiratory distress. Oxygen inhibits polymerization of HbS.
  4. Treat Precipitating Factors:
    • Infection: Screen (Malaria, Pneumonia, UTI) and treat with broad-spectrum antibiotics (e.g., Ceftriaxone) as infection is the most common trigger.
    • Acidosis: Correct if severe.
  5. Blood Transfusion:
    • Indicated for: Severe anemia (Hb < 5g/dl), Acute Chest Syndrome, Splenic Sequestration, or Stroke.
    • Simple top-up or Exchange transfusion depending on severity.
  6. Monitoring: Vitals, Pain score, Hydration status.
