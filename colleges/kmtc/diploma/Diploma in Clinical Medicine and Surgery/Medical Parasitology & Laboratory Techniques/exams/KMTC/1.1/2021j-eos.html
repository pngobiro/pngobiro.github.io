Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions (20 Marks)

1.
Definitive host is one:
a) In which sexual multiplication takes place and harbors adult form
b) In which asexual multiplication takes place and harbors adult form
c) In which sexual multiplication takes place and harbors larval form
d) In which asexual multiplication takes place and harbors adult form
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) In which sexual multiplication takes place and harbors adult form

Explanation: A definitive host is defined as the host in which the parasite reaches sexual maturity and undergoes sexual reproduction. It typically harbors the adult stage of the parasite.

Compare: An Intermediate host harbors the larval or asexual stages of the parasite.

2.
Which of the following cestode does not have a rostellum and hooks?
a) Echinococcus granulosus
b) Taenia solium
c) Taenia saginata
d) Hymenolepis nana
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Taenia saginata

Explanation: The scolex of Taenia saginata (Beef tapeworm) has 4 suckers but lacks a rostellum and hooks (it is "unarmed").

  • T. solium: Has a rostellum with a double row of hooks.
  • E. granulosus: Has a rostellum with hooks.
  • H. nana: Has a retractile rostellum with a single ring of hooks.
3.
Larval form of Echinococcus granulosus is seen in:
a) Dog
b) Man
c) Wolf
d) Fox
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Man

Explanation: Humans act as accidental intermediate hosts for Echinococcus granulosus. The larval form (Hydatid cyst) develops in the organs (liver, lungs) of intermediate hosts (sheep, cattle, humans). Dogs, wolves, and foxes are definitive hosts harboring the adult worm.

4.
Dwarf tapeworm refers to:
a) Echinococcus granulosus
b) Loa loa
c) Hymenolepis nana
d) Schistosoma mansoni
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Hymenolepis nana

Explanation: Hymenolepis nana is commonly known as the dwarf tapeworm because it is the smallest tapeworm infecting humans (only 2-4 cm long).

5.
Which of the following is not a cestode?
a) Diphyllobothrium latum
b) Taenia saginata
c) Schistosoma mansoni
d) Echinococcus granulosus
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Schistosoma mansoni

Explanation: Schistosoma mansoni is a trematode (fluke), specifically a blood fluke. All other options are cestodes (tapeworms).

6.
Which of the following cestodes doesn't need an intermediate host to complete the life cycle?
a) Hymenolepis nana
b) Taenia saginata
c) Diphyllobothrium latum
d) Echinococcus granulosus
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Hymenolepis nana

Explanation: Hymenolepis nana is unique among human tapeworms because it does not require an intermediate host. It can complete its life cycle entirely within a single human host (direct cycle/autoinfection).

7.
Man is the intermediate host for:
a) Strongyloides stercoralis
b) Plasmodium vivax
c) Entamoeba histolytica
d) Enterobius vermicularis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Plasmodium vivax

Explanation: In malaria (caused by Plasmodium), sexual reproduction occurs in the mosquito (definitive host), while asexual reproduction (schizogony) occurs in humans (intermediate host). For the other parasites listed, man acts as the definitive host.

8.
Which of the following parasite passes its life cycle through three hosts?
a) Fasciola hepatica
b) Fasciolopsis buski
c) Schistosoma haematobium
d) Clonorchis sinensis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Clonorchis sinensis

Explanation: Clonorchis sinensis (Chinese liver fluke) involves three hosts:
1. Definitive host: Man/fish-eating mammals.
2. First intermediate host: Snail.
3. Second intermediate host: Freshwater fish.
Fasciola and Fasciolopsis involve two hosts (Man/Mammal + Snail) with encystment on plants (not a biological host). Schistosoma involves two hosts (Man + Snail).

9.
Humans acquire cysticercus cellulosae infection by all except:
a) Not ingestion of Contaminated vegetables (Note: Option phrasing is awkward, implies "Ingestion of contaminated vegetables")
b) Autoinfection
c) Reverse peristalsis
d) Ingestion of contaminated pig's meat
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Ingestion of contaminated pig's meat

Explanation: This question asks how humans acquire Cysticercus cellulosae (Cysticercosis), which is the larval infection.
Cysticercosis is acquired by ingesting EGGS of Taenia solium via:
- Contaminated vegetables/water (fecal-oral).
- Autoinfection (poor hygiene).
- Reverse peristalsis (bringing eggs from gut to stomach).
Ingesting contaminated pig's meat (containing the larva Cysticercus cellulosae) leads to the development of the adult tapeworm (Intestinal Taeniasis), NOT cysticercosis.

10.
Which of the following cestode eggs are NOT bile stained?
a) Hymenolepis nana
b) Diphyllobothrium latum
c) Echinococcus granulosus
d) Taenia solium
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Hymenolepis nana

Explanation: Eggs of Hymenolepis nana are colorless and transparent (non-bile stained). Eggs of Taenia spp., Echinococcus, and Diphyllobothrium latum are bile-stained (brownish) when passed in feces.

11.
Which parasite enters the body by piercing the skin?
a) Trichuris trichiura
b) Ascaris
c) Necator americanus
d) Plasmodium
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Necator americanus

Explanation: Filariform larvae of hookworms (Necator americanus and Ancylostoma duodenale) infect humans by actively penetrating the skin (usually of the feet). Trichuris and Ascaris enter via ingestion of eggs. Plasmodium is injected by a mosquito bite.

12.
Autoinfection is seen in all except:
a) Hymenolepis nana
b) Enterobius vermicularis
c) Taenia solium
d) Ascaris lumbricoides
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Ascaris lumbricoides

Explanation: Ascaris eggs are unembryonated when passed and require a period of maturation in soil to become infective; thus, direct autoinfection is not possible. H. nana, Enterobius, and T. solium (leading to cysticercosis) can all cause autoinfection.

13.
The following is true about cysticercus bovis:
a) It is the larval form of T. saginala.
b) The larva (cysticercus bovis) is infective stage for humans.
c) It is the larval form of T. solium
d) Cysricercus bovis is unknown in humans.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) The larva (cysticercus bovis) is infective stage for humans.

Explanation: Humans get infected with Taenia saginata by eating raw/undercooked beef containing the larva Cysticercus bovis. Thus, it is the infective stage.
Note on option (a): It contains a typo ("saginala" instead of saginata), making it technically incorrect compared to (b) which is factually accurate regarding transmission.

14.
Second intermediate host of Diphyllobothrium latum is:
a) Cyclops
b) Man
c) Snail
d) Fresh water fish
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Fresh water fish

Explanation: The life cycle of D. latum involves:
1. 1st Intermediate Host: Cyclops (Copepod).
2. 2nd Intermediate Host: Freshwater fish.
3. Definitive Host: Man.

15.
Which of the following flukes is carcinogenic?
a) Fasciola
b) Clonorchis
c) Paragonimus
d) Gastrodiscoides
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Clonorchis

Explanation: Chronic infection with Clonorchis sinensis (and Opisthorchis viverrini) is strongly associated with the development of cholangiocarcinoma (bile duct cancer).

16.
All of the following lead to bloody diarrhea except:
a) Schistosoma japonicum
b) Entamoeba histolytica
c) Schistosoma mansoni
d) Schistosoma haematobium
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Schistosoma haematobium

Explanation: S. haematobium inhabits the vesical plexus around the bladder and causes hematuria (blood in urine), not bloody diarrhea. S. mansoni, S. japonicum, and E. histolytica affect the intestine and can cause bloody diarrhea (dysentery).

17.
The second intermediate host of Fasciola hepatica is:
a) Snail
b) Fresh water fish
c) Crab
d) Aquatic plants
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Aquatic plants

Explanation: Cercariae of Fasciola hepatica encyst on aquatic vegetation (like watercress) to form metacercariae. These plants act as the vehicle for transmission, effectively serving the role of a passive second intermediate host/vector.

18.
Largest trematode infecting humans:
a) Fasciola hepatica
b) Fasciolopsis buski
c) Schistosoma haematobium
d) Paragonimus westermani
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Fasciolopsis buski

Explanation: Fasciolopsis buski (Giant intestinal fluke) is the largest trematode found in humans, measuring up to 7.5 cm in length.

19.
Terminal spined eggs are seen in:
a) Schistosoma haematobium
b) Schistosoma mansoni
c) Schistosoma japonicum
d) Clonorchis sinensis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Schistosoma haematobium

Explanation:
- S. haematobium: Terminal spine.
- S. mansoni: Lateral spine.
- S. japonicum: Small lateral knob/rudimentary spine.

20.
Organism causing biliary tract obstruction:
a) Ancylostoma duodenale
b) Clonorchis sinensis
c) Strongyloides stercoralis
d) Enterobius vermicularis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Clonorchis sinensis

Explanation: Clonorchis sinensis adults live in the biliary tree and can cause physical obstruction, inflammation, and fibrosis of the bile ducts. (Note: Ascaris can also cause this, but it is not listed).

Section 2: Short Answer Questions (10 Marks each)

1.
Differentiate between:
a) Direct and indirect life cycle
b) Definitive host and intermediate hosts
Show Answer

a) Direct vs. Indirect Life Cycle:

  • Direct Life Cycle: The parasite requires only a single host species to complete its life cycle. There is no intermediate host. Transmission is usually from one definitive host to another (e.g., Entamoeba histolytica, Ascaris lumbricoides).
  • Indirect Life Cycle: The parasite requires two or more different host species to complete its life cycle (at least one definitive and one intermediate host). (e.g., Plasmodium spp., Taenia spp.).

b) Definitive vs. Intermediate Host:

  • Definitive Host: The host in which the parasite reaches sexual maturity and reproduces sexually (harbors the adult form). E.g., Humans for Taenia saginata.
  • Intermediate Host: The host in which the parasite undergoes larval development or asexual reproduction (harbors the larval form). It is essential for the completion of the life cycle. E.g., Cows for Taenia saginata.
2.
Describe briefly the general characters of cestodes.
Show Answer

General Characteristics of Cestodes (Tapeworms):

  1. Morphology: They are dorso-ventrally flattened, tape-like, and segmented worms.
  2. Body Structure: The body consists of three parts:
    • Scolex (Head): Organ of attachment, often bearing suckers and hooks.
    • Neck: Region of growth.
    • Strobila: Chain of segments called proglottids (immature, mature, and gravid).
  3. Alimentary Canal: Absent. They absorb nutrients directly through their body surface (tegument).
  4. Reproduction: They are hermaphroditic (monoecious); each mature segment contains both male and female reproductive organs.
  5. Body Cavity: Absent.
  6. Life Cycle: Usually indirect, requiring one or more intermediate hosts (except Hymenolepis nana).

Section 3: Long Answer Questions (20 Marks each)

1.
Describe the sources of transmission of various parasitic infections and the modes of transmission of the same.
Show Answer

Sources of Infection:

  1. Soil: Contaminated with eggs/larvae of geohelminths (e.g., Ascaris, Hookworms, Strongyloides).
  2. Water: Containing infective forms like cysts (Giardia, Entamoeba), cercariae (Schistosoma), or cyclops (Dracunculus).
  3. Food:
    • Meat (Beef - T. saginata, Pork - T. solium).
    • Fish (D. latum, Clonorchis).
    • Vegetables (Fasciola, Ascaris eggs).
  4. Insect Vectors: Mosquitoes (Plasmodium, Wuchereria), Tsetse fly (Trypanosoma).
  5. Animals (Zoonosis): Dogs (Echinococcus), Cats (Toxoplasma).
  6. Self (Autoinfection): Enterobius, Strongyloides, H. nana.

Modes of Transmission:

  1. Ingestion (Fecal-Oral): Swallowing infective stages in food/water (e.g., Entamoeba, Ascaris, Polio).
  2. Skin Penetration: Larvae actively boring through skin (e.g., Hookworms, Schistosoma).
  3. Vector Borne (Inoculation): Bite of infected insect (e.g., Malaria, Sleeping sickness).
  4. Sexual Contact: (e.g., Trichomonas vaginalis).
  5. Congenital (Vertical): Mother to fetus (e.g., Toxoplasma, Plasmodium).
  6. Inhalation: Breathing in eggs/dust (e.g., Enterobius, Cryptosporidium).
2.
Briefly describe the life cycle of Strongyloides stercoralis.
Show Answer

Life Cycle of Strongyloides stercoralis:

The life cycle is complex, alternating between free-living and parasitic cycles.

1. Free-Living Cycle (in soil):

  • Rhabditiform larvae passed in stool can develop into free-living adult males and females in soil.
  • They mate and produce eggs which hatch into rhabditiform larvae.
  • These can develop into infective Filariform larvae.

2. Parasitic Cycle:

  • Infection: Filariform larvae in soil penetrate human skin (usually feet).
  • Migration: Enter venous circulation Heart Lungs (break into alveoli).
  • Swallowing: Ascend trachea Glottis Swallowed into gut.
  • Maturation: Develop into adult female worms in the small intestine (males are not usually seen in the host). Females are parthenogenic.
  • Reproduction: Eggs are laid in the intestinal mucosa, hatch immediately into Rhabditiform larvae.
  • Excretion: Rhabditiform larvae are passed in stool (diagnostic stage).

3. Autoinfection:

  • Rhabditiform larvae in the gut may molt into infective Filariform larvae before leaving the body.
  • These penetrate the intestinal mucosa (internal autoinfection) or perianal skin (external autoinfection) to restart the cycle.
  • This causes persistent infection (Hyperinfection syndrome) in immunocompromised hosts.
