Section A: Multiple Choice Questions

1.
The following are intestinal nematodes except?
a) Enterobius vermicularis
b) Entamoeba histolytica
c) Trichiuristichiuria
d) Ancylostomaduodenale
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Entamoeba histolytica

Explanation: Entamoeba histolytica is a protozoan (ameba), not a nematode (roundworm). All other options listed (Enterobius, Trichuris, Ancylostoma) are intestinal nematodes.

2.
Which one of the following is correct in regard to the general characteristics of nematodes?
a) They are multicellular
b) They are single celled
c) They are usually hermaphrodites
d) They always have a vector in their life cycle
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) They are multicellular

Explanation: Nematodes are helminths (worms) and are multicellular metazoans. Protozoa are single-celled. Nematodes generally have separate sexes (dioecious), unlike cestodes/trematodes which are often hermaphrodites. Most intestinal nematodes have a direct life cycle without a vector (except filarial nematodes).

3.
Identify the correct statement about the nematodes
a) Man is the definitive host in all nematodes
b) Egg fertilization takes place outside the host in ascaris Lumbricoides
c) Fresh eggs of ascaris Lumbricoides are highly infectious
d) None of the above statements is correct
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) None of the above statements is correct

Explanation:
a) False: Man is an accidental host for some (e.g., Toxocara) or intermediate host for others.
b) False: Fertilization occurs in the small intestine of the human host.
c) False: Fresh eggs are unembryonated and non-infective. They need 2-3 weeks in soil to embryonate and become infective.

4.
In Parasitic infections;
a) Zoonosis means diseasesprincipally confined to animals only
b) Man acts a main reservoir of infection
c) Paratenic host means the parasite develops further in the host
d) Man is always a definitive host
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Man acts a main reservoir of infection

Explanation: In anthroponotic infections (like Ascaris, Enterobius), humans are the main reservoir.
a) False: Zoonoses are diseases transmissible from animals to humans.
c) False: A paratenic (transport) host carries the parasite but no development occurs.
d) False: Man can be an intermediate host (e.g., Malaria, Hydatid disease).

5.
The following options include the important features of helminthes. Which one is correct?
a) They multiply in human bodies
b) They are sexually transmitted
c) They do not possess organs of locomotion
d) They have a pseudopodium
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) They do not possess organs of locomotion

Explanation: Adult helminths generally do not have distinct organs of locomotion (like legs or pseudopodia) though they have muscular systems for movement.
a) False: Most helminths do not multiply in the human host (one egg = one worm), with exceptions like Strongyloides.
b) False: Sexual transmission is rare (mostly fecal-oral or skin penetration).
d) False: Pseudopodia are features of protozoa (amoebas).

7.
Parasitic infections are transmitted in the following methods, except?
a) Inoculation
b) Ingestion
c) Neutralization
d) Direct contact
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Neutralization

Explanation: Neutralization is an immunological process or chemical reaction, not a mode of transmission. Inoculation (vectors), Ingestion (oral), and Direct contact (skin/sexual) are valid transmission modes.

9.
The following nematodes shows heart lung migration.
a) Schistosoma species
b) Hook worms
c) Enterobius vermicularis

Correct Answer: b) Hook worms

Explanation: Hookworm larvae penetrate skin, enter circulation, go to heart, then lungs, then trachea, then swallowed. Schistosoma also migrate through lungs but are trematodes, not nematodes (question asks for nematodes). Ascaris and Strongyloides are other nematodes with lung migration.

2.
Question Two
a) Outline the prevention of Taenia Saginata (6marks)
b) Outline the mode of infection of Taenia Saginata (4 Marks)
Show Answer

a) Prevention of Taenia Saginata:

  • Thorough Cooking: Cook beef thoroughly to at least 63C (145F) to kill cysticerci.
  • Meat Inspection: Examination of beef for Cysticercus bovis at slaughterhouses.
  • Sanitation: Proper disposal of human feces to prevent contamination of grazing lands/cattle feed.
  • Treatment: Treating infected individuals to stop egg shedding.
  • Freezing: Freezing beef at -20C for at least 48 hours kills the larvae.

b) Mode of Infection:

  • Man acquires infection by ingesting raw or undercooked beef containing the infective larval stage, Cysticercus bovis.
3.
Define the following terms as used in parasitology (10 Marks)
a) Parasite
b) Symbiosis
c) Commensalism
d) Ecto parasite
e) Endo parasite
Show Answer
  • a) Parasite: An organism that lives on or within another organism (the host) and derives its sustenance from the host, often causing harm.
  • b) Symbiosis: A close and long-term biological interaction between two different biological organisms. It includes mutualism, commensalism, and parasitism.
  • c) Commensalism: A relationship between two organisms where one benefits and the other is neither harmed nor benefits.
  • d) Ectoparasite: A parasite that lives on the outer surface of the host's body (e.g., lice, ticks).
  • e) Endoparasite: A parasite that lives inside the body of the host (e.g., intestinal worms, malaria parasites).
4.
Outline the general measures in control of intestinal parasites (10 Marks)
Show Answer
  • Sanitation: Proper disposal of human excreta (latrines/toilets) to prevent soil and water contamination.
  • Hygiene: Hand washing with soap and water before eating and after using the toilet.
  • Safe Water: Boiling or treating drinking water.
  • Food Safety: Thoroughly washing vegetables/fruits and cooking meat properly.
  • Health Education: Educating the community about transmission and prevention.
  • Chemotherapy: Mass Drug Administration (MDA) (e.g., deworming programs) in endemic areas.
  • Footwear: Wearing shoes to prevent hookworm infection (skin penetration).

Section D: Long Answer Questions

1.
a) With the aid of a diagram, illustrate the lifecycle of trichuristrichuria (10 Marks)
b) Outline the clinical syndromes shown by trichuristrichuria (10 Marks)
Show Answer

a) Life Cycle of Trichuris trichiura (Whipworm):

          (Soil)
    Unembryonated Egg (Feces)
            |
    Embryonation (2-3 weeks in soil)
            |
      Embryonated Egg (Infective Stage)
            |
      INGESTION by Human (Food/Water/Hands)
            |
      Larvae hatch in Small Intestine
            |
    Larvae penetrate villi -> develop
            |
    Migrate to Cecum/Colon (Maturation)
            |
    Adult Worms in Cecum (embed in mucosa)
            |
    Female lays eggs -> Passed in Feces

b) Clinical Syndromes:

  1. Asymptomatic Infection: Light infections often show no symptoms.
  2. Chronic Dysentery (Trichuris Dysentery Syndrome): In heavy infections, especially in children. Characterized by bloody, mucoid diarrhea.
  3. Rectal Prolapse: Due to increased straining and weakness of rectal muscles in heavy infection (classic sign in children).
  4. Iron Deficiency Anemia: Due to chronic blood loss from the colon.
  5. Growth Retardation: In children with heavy, chronic burdens.
  6. Abdominal Pain: Tenesmus and abdominal tenderness.
2.
Describe hook worm under the following headings; (20 marks)
a) Distribution and incidence
b) Transmission
c) Effects of migrating larvae
d) Effects due to adult worms
Show Answer

a) Distribution and Incidence:

  • Worldwide distribution, mostly in tropical and subtropical regions.
  • Necator americanus: Americas, Sub-Saharan Africa, Asia.
  • Ancylostoma duodenale: Mediterranean, Middle East, Northern India, China.
  • High incidence in areas with warm, moist soil and poor sanitation.

b) Transmission:

  • Mode: Active penetration of the skin (usually feet) by infective Filariform larvae present in the soil.
  • Ancylostoma duodenale can also be transmitted via ingestion (oral route).

c) Effects of Migrating Larvae:

  • Ground Itch: Local dermatitis, itching, and erythema at the site of skin penetration (usually toes/feet).
  • Pulmonary Symptoms (Loeffler's Syndrome): As larvae migrate through lungs, they can cause pneumonitis, cough, wheezing, and eosinophilia.

d) Effects due to Adult Worms:

  • Iron Deficiency Anemia: Adults attach to intestinal mucosa and suck blood. They produce anticoagulants causing persistent bleeding.
  • Hypoproteinemia: Loss of blood proteins leading to edema/anasarca.
  • Gastrointestinal Symptoms: Epigastric pain, indigestion, nausea, vomiting.
  • Constitutional Symptoms: Weakness, fatigue, pallor due to anemia.
  • Pica: Desire to eat non-food items (soil/dirt) due to iron deficiency.
