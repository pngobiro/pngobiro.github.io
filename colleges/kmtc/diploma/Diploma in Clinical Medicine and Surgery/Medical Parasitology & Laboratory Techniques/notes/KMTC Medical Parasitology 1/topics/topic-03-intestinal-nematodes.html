üß¨ General Characteristics

All the important human parasites of the Phylum Nemathelminthes (Aschelminthes) belong to the Class Nematoda.

  • They are un-segmented, elongated and cylindrical.
  • They have separate sexes with separate appearances.
  • They have a tough protective covering or cuticle.
  • They have a complete digestive tract with both oral and anal openings.
  • The nematodes are free living or parasites of humans, plants or animals.
üìã

Classification of Parasitic Nematodes

The parasitic nematodes are divided into:

  1. Intestinal nematodes
    1. Intestinal nematodes with tissue stage
      • A. Ascaris lumbricoides
      • B. Hookworms (Ancylostoma duodenale & Necator americanus)
      • C. Strongyloides stercoralis
    2. Intestinal nematodes without tissue stage
      • A. Enterobius vermicularis
      • B. Trichuris trichuira.
  2. Tissue and blood dwelling nematodes
    1. Filarial worms
    2. Dracunculus medinensis
    3. Trichinella
    4. Larva migrans

üçù Ascaris lumbricoides

These are common roundworms infecting more than 700 million people worldwide.

Morphology

  • Male adult worm measures 15-20 cm in length. The posterior end is curved ventrally.
  • The female worm measures 20-40cm in length. Its posterior end is straight.

Infection

The egg containing larva when ingested with contaminated raw vegetables causes ascariasis.

üîÑ

Transmission & Life Cycle

  1. Transmission: Infective eggs ingested in food or from contaminated hands.
  2. Human host: The larvae hatch and penetrate the intestinal wall and circulate in the blood.
  3. From the heart they migrate to the lungs, ascend to the trachea, descend to the esophagus and finally reach the small intestine to become adult.
  4. The female pass immature eggs which pass to the soil and mature in 2 weeks.
  5. Environment: Eggs become infective (embryonated) and contaminate the environment.

PATHOGENECITY AND CLINICAL FEATURES

  • Adult worms in the intestine cause abdominal pain, nausea, diarrhea, and vomiting and may cause intestinal obstruction especially in children.
  • During their migration the Larvae in the lungs may cause inflammation of the lungs (Loeffler‚Äôs syndrome) ‚Äì pneumonia-like symptoms.
  • Intestinal muscle may become damaged and absorption impaired
  • Protein digestion or absorption is impaired, fat absorption decreases and lactase activity in the small intestine is reduced.
  • A. lumbricoides infection in children is also known to affect gastrointestinal function.
  • The children are also vitamin A deficient and low albumin levels.
  • Other complications include liver abscesses and appendicitis caused by migrating worms.

LABORATORY DIAGNOSIS

  1. Examination of stool for eggs by direct saline smear method. The egg is oval or round, 75x60 microns, covered by albuminous coat (mammilated).
  2. Demonstration of adult worms.

PREVENTION AND CONTROL

  • Using latrines and avoiding the use of untreated human faeces as fertilizer.
  • Treating infected individuals.
  • Washing hands before eating.
  • Avoiding eating uncooked vegetables, green salads and fruits.
üíä

Treatment

Ascariasis can be treated by: Mebendazole or Albendazole or Piperazine

ü™ù Hookworms

There are two species of hookworm: Ancylostoma duodenale and Necator americanus. The adults are found in the small intestines of man. Mixed infection is common.

Ancylostoma duodenale

  • Grayish-white in color. The body is slightly ventrally curved. The anterior end follows the body curvature.
  • The mouth of A.duodenale is large and has teeth like structures.
  • This worm produces twice as many eggs as compared to N.americanus.

Necator americanus

  • Found predominantly the tropics, far east, south Asia, tropical Africa.
  • The anterior end is hooked against the body curvature.
  • The mouth has cutting plates.
üîÑ

Life Cycle of Hookworms

  1. Transmission: Infective filariform larvae penetrate the skin e.g the feet. A. duodenale is also transmitted by ingestion of larvae.
  2. Human Host: Larvae migrate. Pass up trachea and are swallowed.
  3. They become mature worms in small intestine (attach to wall and suck blood).
  4. Eggs produced and passed in faeces.
  5. Environment: Eggs develop. Rhabditiform larvae hatch. Feed in soil.
  6. They develop into infective filariform larvae in about 1 week.

PATHOGENECITY

  • The first sign of hookworm infection is a skin reaction at the site of larval penetration. This is known as "ground itch"
  • Mild respiratory symptoms may develop during migration of the larvae
  • There is also eosinophilia
  • Adult worms in the intestine feed on blood causing iron deficiency anemia.
  • The larvae may cause inflammation of the lungs.

LARVA MIGRANS

There are three types of larva migrans:

A. Cutaneous Larva Migrans (Creeping Eruption)

Various animals harbor hookworms. Two species of dogs and cats are important: Ancylostoma braziliens and Ancylostoma caninum.

If man comes in contact with infective larvae, penetration of the skin may take place; but the larvae are then unable to complete their migratory cycle. Trapped larvae may survive for weeks or even months, migrating through the subcutaneous tissues. They may evoke a fairly severe reaction - pruritus and dermatitis. The dermatitis leads to scratching and then bacterial superinfection.

B. Visceral Larva Migrans (Toxocariasis)

A syndrome caused by the migration of parasitic larvae in the viscera of a host for months or years. Caused by Toxocara canis (Dog ascarid) and Toxocara catis (Cat ascarid).

  • Transmission: Ingestion of eggs of Toxocara species in contaminated food or soil or direct contact with infected patients.
  • Clinical features: Majority are asymptomatic. Eosinophilia. Cerebral, myocardial and pulmonary involvement may cause death.
  • Treatment: Thiabendazole: 25 mg/kg twice daily for 5 days.

üêõ Strongyloides stercoralis

The worms may be present as parasitic in the host or free living in the soil.

LIFE CYCLE

  • Larvae penetrating the skin from the soil or by autoinfection are carried by the blood to the lungs, ascend to the trachea, descend to the esophagus and mature in the small intestine.
  • Adult male and female worms live in the small intestine.
  • After fertilization, the female penetrates the mucosa of the small intestine and lay eggs.
  • The eggs hatch into Rhabditiform larvae in the intestine.
  • The Rhabditiform larvae are: either passed in faeces or become filariform larvae in the intestine causing autoinfection.
‚ö†Ô∏è

Clinical Presentation

  • During penetration on the skin, larvae can cause an itchy dermatitis and rash.
  • During migration of the larvae, allergic and respiratory symptoms may occur.
  • Heavy infections (especially common in children,) can cause dysentery, malabsorption, steatorrhoea, and dehydration with electrolyte disturbance.
  • Hyper infection: Autoinfection with S.stercoralis can become overwhelming and fatal when the body's normal immune responses are reduced e.g drugs, steroids, malnutrition, or pregnancy or when other diseases are present which cause immunosuppression.

TREATMENT

Thiabendazole: 25 mg/kg twice daily for 3 days.

üß∂ Enterobius vermicularis (Pin Worm)

Enterobius vermicularis is a small white worm with thread-like appearance. The worm causes enterobiasis. Infection is common in children.

MODE OF INFECTION

  • Transmission is by ingesting infective eggs. The eggs are deposited on the anal skin usually during night hours.
  • By direct infection from a patient (Fecal-oral route).
  • Autoinfection: The eggs cause intense irritation and scratching of the infected area and leads to the fingers becoming contaminated with eggs.
  • Retroinfection: Occasionally larvae hatch from the eggs on the perianal skin and migrate back into the intestine where they grow into mature worms.
  • Aerosol inhalation from contaminated sheets and dust.

CLINICAL PRESENTATION

  • The migration of the worms causes allergic reactions around the anus and during night it causes nocturnal itching (pruritus ani) and enuresis.
  • In females, infection of the urinary and genital tract can also occur.
  • The worms may obstruct the appendix causing appendicitis

DIAGNOSIS

  • Peri-anal swab: The peri-anal region is swabbed with a piece of adhesive tape (cellotape) hold over a tongue depressor. The adhesive tape is placed on a glass slide and examined for eggs. The swab should be done in the early morning before bathing and defecation.
  • Eggs in stool: Examination of the stool by direct saline smear is positive in only about 5% of cases because the eggs are glued to the peri-anal skin.

ü•® Trichuris trichiura (Whip Worm)

The worm is divided into a thin whip-like anterior part measuring 3/5 of the worm and a thick fleshy posterior part of 2/5 the length.

Infection & Life Cycle

Infection is by ingestion of eggs containing larvae with contaminated raw vegetables.

  • Ingested eggs hatch in the small intestine and the larvae migrate to the large intestine to become adult.
  • After mating, the female lays immature eggs, which pass with the stool to the soil and mature in 2 weeks.

Diagnosis

Finding of characteristic eggs. The egg of trichuris is barrel-shaped, 50x25 microns. The shell is thick with a one mucoid plug at each pole.

SYMPTOMS

  • The patient complains of dysentery (blood and mucus in stool together with tenesmus).
  • Rectal prolapse is also possible.

TREATMENT

Mebendazole: 1 tablet twice daily for 2 days.
