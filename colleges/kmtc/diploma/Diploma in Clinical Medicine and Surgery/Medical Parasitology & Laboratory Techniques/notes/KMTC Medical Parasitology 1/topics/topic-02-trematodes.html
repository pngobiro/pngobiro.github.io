LEARNING OBJECTIVES

At the end of this session, you should be able to:

  • Understand medically important helminthes including their life cycles, modes of transmissions, clinical features, diagnosis, treatment and prevention.
  • Describe blood, intestinal, liver and lung flukes.
  • Understand common round worms.
  • Understand different species of Cestodes.

INTRODUCTION

  • Medical helminthology is concerned with the study of helminthes or parasitic worms. Helminthes are trophoblastic metazoa (multicellular organisms).
  • Helminthes are among the common parasitic causes of human suffering.
  • They are the cause of high morbidity and mortality of people worldwide.
  • They cause different diseases in humans, but few helminthic infections cause life-threatening diseases.

CHARACTERISTICS OF HELMINTHS

  • Multicellular
  • They are divided into three groups
  • They are bilaterally symmetrical animals having three germ layers
  • They do not have organs of locomotion
  • They include Cestodes, Trematodes and Nematodes.

MODES OF INFECTION

  • Penetration e.g. Strongyloides stercolaris
  • Ingestion e.g. Ascaris lumbricoides
  • Insect vector e.g. Wuchereria bancrofti
  • Mother to child transmission
  • Retroinfection e.g. Enterobias vermicularis
  • Inhalation e.g. Haemophilus influenza
  • Skin contact e.g. scabies.

SOURCE OF PARASITES

The sources of the parasites are different. Exposure of humans to the parasites may occur in one of the following ways:

  1. Contaminated soil (Geo-helminthes), water (cercariae of blood flukes) and food (Taenia in raw meat).
  2. Blood sucking insects or arthropods (as in filarial worms).
  3. Domestic or wild animals harboring the parasite (as in echinococcus in dogs).
  4. Person to person (as in Enterobius vermicularis, Hymenolopis nana).
  5. Oneself (auto-infection) as in Enterobius vermicularis

ü¶† Trematodes (Flukes)

Trematodes belong to the phylum platyhelminthes. They are found in a wide range of habitats. The great majority inhabit the alimentary canal, liver, bile duct, ureter and bladder of vertebrate animals.

According to the sites they inhabit, there are four groups of flukes. These are:

1. Blood Flukes

Reside mainly in the blood vessels.

2. Intestinal Flukes

Reside in the intestine.

3. Liver Flukes

Reside in the liver and bile ducts.

4. Lung Flukes

Reside in the lungs.

ü©∏ Blood Flukes (Schistosomiasis)

It is estimated that about 600 million people in 79 countries suffer from schistosomiasis (Bilharziasis). Schistosome is the only fluke with separate sexes. The female worm lies in the gynecophoral canal of the male.

MEDICALLY IMPORTANT SPECIES

  • Schistosoma mansoni: causes intestinal schistosomiasis. Lives in the veins of the intestine. Found in Africa, South America, Middle East. Snail host: Biomphalaria.
  • Schistosoma haematobium: causes vesical (urinary) schistosomiasis. Lives in veins of the bladder. Snail hosts: Bulinus and Physopsis.
  • Schistosoma japonicum: causes intestinal schistosomiasis. Found in Japan, China, Philippines.
  • Schistosoma intercalatum: causes intestinal schistosomiasis. Found in Western and Central Africa.
  • Schistosoma mekongi: causes intestinal schistosomiasis.
üîÑ

Life Cycle of Schistosomes

  1. Cercariae penetrate skin when person in contact with contaminated water.
  2. Cercariae-Schistosomula migrate through lungs and liver.
  3. They become mature flukes in portal venous system. They pair up (male and female).
  4. They migrate to veins of lower large intestine (S. haematobium to veins of bladder).
  5. The eggs laid in venules burrow through into intestine (eggs of S. haematobium into bladder).
  6. The eggs passed in faeces (S. haematobium in urine).
  7. The eggs reach water and Miracidia hatch.
  8. The Miracidia penetrate snail. They become sporocyst and multiply. The sporocysts then become cercariae.
  9. Cercariae leave snail into the water.

SYMPTOMS AND COMPLICATIONS

  • Patients infected with S. haematobium suffer from terminal haematuria and painful micturition. There is inflammation of the urinary bladder (cystitis), and enlargement of spleen and liver. S. haematobium causes squamous cell carcinoma in the bladder.
  • Patients infected with S. mansoni suffer from cercarial dermatitis (swimmers itch) and dysentery (mucus and blood in stool with tenesmus) as well as enlargements of the spleen and liver.

LABORATORY DIAGNOSIS

S. Mansoni

  • Microscopic examination of the stool for eggs after concentration by sedimentation method. The egg has characteristic lateral spine.
  • Rectal snip

S. haematobium

  • Examination of the urine after allowing it to sediment in a conical urinalysis glass. A drop from the sediment is taken and examined for eggs. Egg has terminal spine.
  • Biopsy from bladder

MEASURES TO PREVENT AND CONTROL SCHISTOSOMIASIS

  • Avoiding contact with water known to contain cercariae.
  • Preventing water becoming contaminated with eggs (Sanitation, Health Education).
  • Destroying snail intermediate hosts (Molluscicides, removing vegetation).
  • Minimizing risk from irrigation schemes.
  • Treating water supplies (Chlorine, Storage for 48hrs, Filtering).

üçÉ Intestinal Flukes

FASCIOLOPSIS BUSKI

F. buski is the largest fluke that infects humans (giant intestinal fluke). The pig and buffalo are the normal definitive host. Infection is by ingesting metacercariae from water chestnuts, water caltrops or other aquatic edible plants.

üîÑ

Life Cycle of F. buski

  1. Metacercariae ingested from aquatic plants, e.g when peeling stems with teeth.
  2. Metacercariae excyst, attach to wall of the small intestine.
  3. They become mature flukes.
  4. Eggs are produced and passed in faeces.
  5. The eggs reach water and hatch into miracidia.
  6. The miracidia enter snail host.
  7. Miracidia grow into sporocysts -> rediae -> Cercariae.
  8. Cercariae leave snail and encyst on aquatic plants (metacercariae).

Signs and Symptoms

  • Inflammation and ulceration of intestinal wall.
  • Diarrhoea and abdominal pain.
  • Oedema of face/limbs, ascites (due to toxins).
  • Blood eosinophilia.

Prevention

  • Treating/cooking water plants.
  • Latrines and health education.
  • Avoid untreated faeces as fertilizer.
  • Snail control.

ü•© Liver Flukes

CLONORCHIS (OPISTHORCHIS) SINENSIS

Chinese liver fluke. Adult worms live in bile ducts. Transmitted by eating raw, undercooked, smoked or pickled fish containing infective metacercariae.

Life Cycle

  1. Metacercariae ingested in raw fish.
  2. Excyst in duodenum, migrate to bile duct.
  3. Mature in biliary tract, eggs passed in faeces.
  4. Snail ingests eggs -> miracidia -> sporocysts -> rediae -> cercariae.
  5. Cercariae infect fish (metacercariae).

Symptoms of Clonorchiasis

Damage to liver and bile ducts, jaundice, hepatitis, cirrhosis, biliary obstruction. Can cause cancer of the bile duct.

FASCIOLA HEPATICA & FASCIOLA GIGANTICA

Sheep liver fluke (F. hepatica) lives in liver and bile ducts of sheep/cattle. Infection is by ingesting metacercariae on aquatic plants.

Life Cycle

  1. Metacercariae ingested on aquatic plants.
  2. Excyst in duodenum, immature flukes penetrate liver.
  3. Mature in biliary duct, eggs passed in faeces.
  4. Eggs hatch in water -> miracidia -> enter snail.
  5. Sporocyst -> rediae -> cercariae -> leave snail.
  6. Cercariae encyst on aquatic plants.
