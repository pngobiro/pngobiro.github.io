üß¨ Tissue and Blood Dwelling Nematodes

This group includes the filarial worms, the guinea worm (Dracunculus medinensis) and Trichinella spiralis.

FILARIAL WORMS

  • The filarial worms have complex life cycles involving a developmental stage in an insect vector.
  • They require an arthropod vector for their transmission.
  • The worms inhabit either the lymphatic system or the subcutaneous tissues of man.
  • The female worm gives rise to a young worm called microfilaria.
  • The microfilariae, when taken by the arthropod intermediate host during biting, develop into filariform larvae, which are the infective stages.
  • Humans get infected when bitten by the infected arthropod intermediate host.

ü¶µ Wuchereria bancrofti

This is a parasite of lymph nodes and lymphatic vessels - causing lymphatic filariasis. It is believed that over 100 million people are infected.

üåô

Nocturnal Periodicity

The microfilariae are nocturnal - seen in greatest numbers in peripheral blood in the night between 10 PM - 2 AM. The physiological basis of this nocturnal periodicity is not understood.

TRANSMISSION AND PATHOGENESIS

  • The filariform larvae are introduced through the skin by the bite of the arthropod intermediate host (mosquitoes).
  • The larvae invade the lymphatics, usually the lower limb, where they develop into adult worms.
  • The microfilariae are librated into the blood stream.
  • Presence of the adult worms causes lymphatic blockage and gross lymphedema, which sometimes lead to elephantiasis.

Clinical Features

  • Lymphangitis
  • Lymphedema
  • Fever, Headache, Myalgia
  • Hydrocele
  • Chyluria
  • Bancroftian elephantiasis (thick fissured skin)

Diagnosis & Treatment

Diagnosis: Blood film examination after staining by Giemsa or Leishman stain to detect microfilaria. The film should be taken by night.

Treatment: Diethyl carbamazine (DEC): 2 mg/kg 3x daily for 2 weeks.

üëÅÔ∏è Onchocerca volvulus (River Blindness)

Infection by this filarial worm is common in Ethiopia. Endemic foci are found in Bebeka, Gojeb valley, Dedessa valley, Agaro, Metekel, and in Northwestern Ethiopia around Gondar.

Morphology

  • Male: Similar to that of Wuchereria bancrofti.
  • Female: The female measures 30-50 cm in length. It is present inside of a fibrous nodule (onchocercomata or onchocerca tumor).
  • Microfilaria: Measures 300 microns in length. Non-sheathed. Present in subcutaneous tissue fluids, not blood.

Intermediate Host & Vector

Female Simulium (Simulium damnosum), Black fly, found around plantations following rivers or river basins.

PATHOGENECITY AND CLINICAL MANIFESTATIONS

The disease, onchocerciasis or river blindness includes:

  • Skin fibrous nodules (onchocercomata) enclosing female worms. The nodules are common in neck, iliac crest and the coccyx.
  • Skin hypo- or hyper- pigmentation. Dermatitis is present. In advanced cases, the skin becomes thickened and wrinkled, showing lizard or leopard skin appearance.
  • Elephantiasis of the external genitalia and corneal opacity and optic atrophy may finally cause blindness.
Aspect Details
Diagnosis Superficial biopsy (skin snip) is taken from the skin using sharp razor blade. The specimen is allowed to stand for 30 minutes in saline before it is examined microscopically for microfilariae.
Treatment Ivermectin: 50 mg/kg bodyweight, given every 6 or 12 months. Kills microfilariae but not adult worms; retreatment necessary.
Prevention Vector control, Mass treatment, Establishment of villages away from Simulium breeding places, Use of repellents, Protective clothing.

ü™∞ Loa loa (Eye Worm)

Causes Loiasis. The insect vectors include mango flies of Chrysops - Chrysops silacea, Chrysops dimidiata. Endemic in Central and West Equatorial Africa.

Morphology

  • Adult male worms: 30-34 mm in length
  • Adult female worms: 40-70 mm in length
  • Microfilaria have a sheath. Diurnal periodicity (10:00 AM to 4:00 PM).

Pathogenesis & Clinical Features

  • Incubation period is about one year.
  • Causes calabar swelling beneath the skin due to parasites.
  • Fever, pain, pruritus, urticaria, allergic reactions, retinopathy, glomerulonephritis, meningo-encephalitis.

Diagnosis: Detection of microfilaria in peripheral blood, urine, sputum, CSF - stained with Giemsa or unstained.

üêâ Dracunculus medinensis (Guinea Worm)

Causes dracunculiasis. Endemic to Asia and Africa (India, Nile Valley, central/western Africa, lowlands of Ethiopia and Eritrea).

üîÑ

Life Cycle & Pathogenicity

  1. Infection acquired by drinking unfiltered/not boiled water containing Cyclops species.
  2. Larvae released in stomach, penetrate intestinal wall, find way to subcutaneous tissue.
  3. Mating in axillary/inguinal regions (3 months). Male dies.
  4. Female moves down to limbs (within 10 months).
  5. Female provokes formation of burning blister in skin of legs.
  6. Blister bursts in water, worm extrudes, releases larvae.
  7. Larvae ingested by Cyclops, become infective in 3 weeks.

CLINICAL FEATURES

  • Allergic reactions: Erythema, Urticaria, Pruritus, Fainting, Asthma, Dyspnea.
  • Blister on legs which ruptures on contact with water.
  • Wound ulceration.
  • Arthritis, pericarditis, abscesses from worm migration.

Diagnosis & Treatment

  • Observation of blister, worm or larvae.
  • Surgical excision when the worm is in the leg.
  • Niridazole (Ambilhar) or DEC.

Prevention

  • Boiling or filtering of drinking water.
  • Treating patients and educating not to enter water bodies.
  • Using insect larvicides to kill Cyclops.

ü•ì Trichinosis (Trichinella spiralis)

Caused by Trichinella spiralis. Major reservoir host for human infections is swine (pigs).

üîÑ

Life Cycle

  1. After ingesting infected meat, the capsule of the encysted larvae is digested by gastric juice.
  2. Larvae released in duodenum/jejunum, molt to become adult.
  3. Male dies after mating. Female delivers embryos 4-7 days after infection.
  4. Larvae penetrate intestinal wall, migrate via lymphatics to blood stream.
  5. Skeletal muscles and diaphragm are most frequently parasitized.

CLINICAL FEATURES

There are two clinical phases:

  1. The intestinal phase: lasting 1-7 days - asymptomatic; sometimes cause nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, constipation, pain, etc.
  2. The muscle phase: which causes myalgia, palpabral edema, eosinophilia, fever, myocarditis, meningitis, bronchopneumonia etc.

Diagnosis

  • Muscle Biopsy
  • Detection of larvae in blood or CSF
  • ELISA

Treatment

Thiabendazol

Prevention

  • Cooking of all meat before consumption
  • Inspection of pigs
