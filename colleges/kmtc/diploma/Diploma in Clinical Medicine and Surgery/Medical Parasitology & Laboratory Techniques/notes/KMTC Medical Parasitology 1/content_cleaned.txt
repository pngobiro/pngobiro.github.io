<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="introduction">INTRODUCTION<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#introduction" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Medical parasitology is the science that deals with organisms living in the human body (the host) and the medical significance of this host-parasite relationship</li>
<li>Man and other living things on earth live in an entangling relationship with each other.</li>
<li>They don’t exist in an isolated fashion. They are interdependent; each forms a strand in the web of life.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="association-between-parasite-and-host">ASSOCIATION BETWEEN PARASITE AND HOST<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#association-between-parasite-and-host" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>A parasite is a living organism, which takes its nourishment and other needs from a host; the host is an organism which supports the parasite.</li>
<li>The parasites included in medical parasitology are protozoa, helminthes, and some arthropods.</li>
<li>The hosts vary depending on whether they harbor the various stages in parasitic development.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="different-kinds-of-parasites">DIFFERENT KINDS OF PARASITES<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#different-kinds-of-parasites" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Ectoparasite – a parasitic organism that lives on the outer surface of its host, e.g. lice, ticks, mites etc.</li>
<li>Endoparasites – parasites that live inside the body of their host, e.g. <em>Entamoeba histolytica</em>.</li>
<li>Obligate Parasite – This parasite is completely dependent on the host during a segment or all of its life cycle, e.g. <em>Plasmodium spp</em></li>
</ul>
<p>○ Facultative parasite - an organism that exhibits both parasitic and non-parasitic modes of living and hence does not absolutely depend on the parasitic way of life, but is capable of adapting to it if placed on a host. E.g. Naegleria fowleri</p>
<p>○ Accidental parasite - when a parasite attacks an unnatural host and survives. E.g. Hymenolepis diminuta (rat tapeworm).</p>
<p>○ Erratic parasite - is one that wanders in to an organ in which it is not usually found. E.g. Entamoeba histolytica in the liver or lung of humans.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="different-kinds-of-hosts">DIFFERENT KINDS OF HOSTS<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#different-kinds-of-hosts" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Definitive host – a host that harbors a parasite in the adult stage or where the parasite undergoes a sexual method of reproduction.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Intermediate host – harbors the larval stages of the parasite or an asexual cycle of development takes place. In some cases, larval development is completed in two different intermediate hosts, referred to as first and second intermediate hosts.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Paratenic host – a host that serves as a temporary refuge and vehicle for reaching an obligatory host, usually the definitive host, i.e. it is not necessary for the completion of the parasites life cycle.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Reservoir host – a host that makes the parasite available for the transmission to another host and is usually not affected by the infection.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Natural host – a host that is naturally infected with certain species of parasite.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Accidental host – a host that is under normal circumstances not infected with the parasite.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="types-of-associations">TYPES OF ASSOCIATIONS<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#types-of-associations" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="mutualism">Mutualism<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#mutualism" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This is an association in which both partners are metabolically dependent upon each other and one cannot live without the help of the other; however, none of the partners suffers any harm from the association.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="commensalism">Commensalism<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#commensalism" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This is an association in which the commensal takes the benefit without causing injury to the host. E.g. Most of the normal floras of the humans' body can be considered as commensals</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="parasitism">Parasitism<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#parasitism" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This is an association where one of the partners is harmed and the other lives at the expense of the other. E.g. Worms like <em>Ascaris lumbricoides</em> reside in the gastrointestinal tract of man, and feed on important items of intestinal food causing various illnesses</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="effect-of-parasites-on-the-host">EFFECT OF PARASITES ON THE HOST<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#effect-of-parasites-on-the-host" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>The damage which pathogenic parasites produce in the tissues of the host may be described in the following ways;</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Mechanical injury - may be inflicted by a parasite by means of pressure as it grows larger, e.g. Hydatid cyst causes blockage of ducts such as blood vessels producing infraction.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Deleterious effect of toxic substances- in Plasmodium falciparum production of toxic substances may cause rigors and other symptoms.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Deprivation of nutrients, fluids and metabolites - parasite may produce disease by competing with the host for nutrients e.g. hookworm sucks blood, D.latum selectively remove vitamin B12</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Immunological reaction: Tissue damage may be caused by immunological response of the host, e.g. nephritic syndrome following Plasmodium infections.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Excessive proliferation of certain tissues due to invasion by some parasites can also cause tissue damage in man, e.g. fibrosis of liver after deposition of the ova of Schistosoma.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Destruction of tissues e.g. trophozoites of E.histolytica causes necrosis of liver.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Opening path way to secondary infections e.g. ulcer formed as a result of D.medinensis infection exposes to bacterial, viral infection.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Transmission of pathogens to man e.g. lice transmitting Rickettsia</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Predisposition to malignancy e.g. infection with bilharziasis</p>
</li>
</ul>

<p>In medical parasitology, each of the medically important parasites are discussed under the standard subheadings of morphology, geographical distribution, means of infection, life cycle, host/parasite relationship, pathology and clinical manifestations of infection, laboratory diagnosis, treatment and preventive/control measures of parasites.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="morphology">MORPHOLOGY<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#morphology" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>This includes size, shape, color and position of different organelles in different parasites at various stages of their development.</li>
<li>This is especially important in laboratory diagnosis which helps to identify the different stages of development and differentiate between pathogenic and commensal organisms.</li>
<li>For example, Entamoeba histolytica and Entamoeba coli.</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="geographical-distribution">GEOGRAPHICAL DISTRIBUTION<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#geographical-distribution" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Even though revolutionary advances in transportation has made geographical isolation no longer a protection against many of the parasitic diseases, many of them are still found in abundance in the tropics.</li>
<li>Distribution of parasites depends upon:
a. The presence and food habits of a suitable host:</li>
<li>Host specificity, for example, <em>Ancylostoma duodenale</em> requires man as a host where <em>Ancylostoma caninum</em> requires a dog.</li>
<li>Food habits, e.g. consumption of raw or undercooked meat or vegetables predisposes to Taeniasis</li>
</ul>
<p>b. Easy escape of the parasite from the host—the different developmental stages of a parasite which are released from the body along with faeces and urine are widely distributed in many parts of the world as compared to those parasites which require a vector or direct body fluid contact for transmission.</p>
<p>c. Environmental conditions favoring survival outside the body of the host, i.e. temperature, the presence of water, humidity etc.</p>
<p>d. The presence of an appropriate vector or intermediate host – parasites that do not require an intermediate host (vector) for transmission are more widely distributed than those that do require vectors.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="life-cycle-of-parasites">LIFE CYCLE OF PARASITES<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#life-cycle-of-parasites" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>

</li>
<li>
<p>It can either be simple, when only one host is involved, or complex, involving one or more intermediate hosts.</p>
</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="host-parasite-relationship">HOST PARASITE RELATIONSHIP<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#host-parasite-relationship" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Infection is the result of entry and development within the body of any injurious organism regardless of its size. Once the infecting organism is introduced into the body of the host, it reacts in different ways and this could result in:</p>
<p>a. Carrier state - a perfect host parasite relationship where tissue destruction by a parasite is balanced with the host's tissue repair. At this point the parasite and the host live harmoniously, i.e. they are at equilibrium.</p>
<p>b. Disease state - this is due to an imperfect host parasite relationship where the parasite dominates the upper hand. It can result either from lower resistance of the host or a higher pathogenicity of the parasite.</p>
<p>c. Parasite destruction - occurs when the host takes the upper hand.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="laboratory-diagnosis">LABORATORY DIAGNOSIS<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#laboratory-diagnosis" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Depending on the nature of the parasitic infections, the following specimens are selected for laboratory diagnosis:</p>
<p>a) <strong>Blood</strong> – in those parasitic infections where the parasite itself in any stage of its development circulates in the blood stream, examination of blood film forms one of the main procedures for specific diagnosis eg. in malaria the parasites. In Bancroftian and Malayan filariasis, microfilariae are found in the blood plasma.</p>
<p>b) <strong>Stool</strong> – examination of the stool forms an important part in the diagnosis of intestinal parasitic infections and also for those helminthic parasites that localize in the biliary tract and discharge their eggs into the intestine.</p>
<p>In protozoan infections, either trophozoites or cystic forms may be detected.</p>
<p>c) Urine – when the parasite localizes in the urinary tract, examination of the urine will be of help in establishing the parasitological diagnosis. Eg. in urinary Schistosomiasis, eggs of Schistosoma haematobium are found in the urine.</p>
<p>d) Sputum – examination of the sputum is useful in the following:</p>
<ul>
<li>In cases where the habitat of the parasite is in the respiratory tract.</li>
</ul>
<p>e) Biopsy material – varies with different parasitic infections. For example spleen punctures in cases of kala-azar, muscle biopsy in cases of Cysticercosis.</p>
<p>f) Urethral or vaginal discharge – for Trichomonas vaginalis</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="indirect-evidences">INDIRECT EVIDENCES<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#indirect-evidences" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Indirect evidences – changes indicative of intestinal parasitic infections are:</p>
<p>a. <strong>Cytological changes in the blood</strong> – Eosiniphilia often gives an indication of tissue invasion by helminthes, a reduction in white blood cell count is an indication of kala-azar, and anemia is a feature of hookworm infestation and malaria.</p>
<p>b. <strong>Serological tests</strong> – are carried out only in laboratories where special antigens are available.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="treatment">TREATMENT<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#treatment" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Many parasitic infections can be cured by specific chemotherapy.</li>
<li>The greatest advances have been made in the treatment of protozoal diseases.</li>
<li>For the treatment of intestinal helminthiasis, drugs are given orally for direct action on the helminthes.</li>
<li>To obtain maximum parasiticidal effect, it is desirable that the drugs administered should not be absorbed and the drugs should also have minimum toxic effect on the host.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="factors-that-contribute-to-the-spread-of-and-increase-in-incidence-of-parasitic-infections">FACTORS THAT CONTRIBUTE TO THE SPREAD OF AND INCREASE IN INCIDENCE OF PARASITIC INFECTIONS<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#factors-that-contribute-to-the-spread-of-and-increase-in-incidence-of-parasitic-infections" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Lack of health education</li>
<li>Inadequate sanitation and unhygienic living conditions leading to faecal contamination of the environment.</li>
<li>Insufficient water and contaminated water supplies.</li>
<li>Failure to control vectors due to ineffective interventions</li>
<li>Poverty, malnutrition</li>
<li>Development schemes introducing opportunities for vector breeding and infection of the workforce e.g. poorly designed irrigation schemes</li>
<li>Failure of drugs to treat parasitic infections effectively</li>
<li>Climatic factors</li>
<li>Population migrations causing poor health, exposure to new infections.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="prevention-and-control">PREVENTION AND CONTROL<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#prevention-and-control" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Measures may be taken against every parasite infecting humans. Preventive measures designed to break the transmission cycle are crucial to successful parasitic eradication.</p>
<p>Such measures include:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Reduction of the source of infection- the parasite is attacked within the host, thereby preventing the dissemination of the infecting agent. Therefore, a prompt diagnosis and treatment of parasitic diseases is an important component in the prevention of dissemination.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Sanitary control of drinking water and food.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Proper waste disposal – through establishing safe sewage systems, use of screened latrines, and treatment of night soil.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The use of insecticides and other chemicals used to control the vector population.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Protective clothing that would prevent vectors from resting in the surface of the body and inoculate pathogens during their blood meal.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Good personal hygiene.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Avoidance of unprotected sexual practices.</p>
</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="introduction">INTRODUCTION<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#introduction" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Medical helminthology is concerned with the study of helminthes or parasitic worms. Helminthes are trophoblastic metazoa (multicellular organisms).</li>
<li>Helminthes are among the common parasitic causes of human suffering.</li>
<li>They are the cause of high morbidity and mortality of people worldwide.</li>
<li>They cause different diseases in humans, but few helminthic infections cause life-threatening diseases.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="characteristics-of-helminths">CHARACTERISTICS OF HELMINTHS<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#characteristics-of-helminths" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Multicellular</li>
<li>They are divided into three groups</li>
<li>They are bilaterally symmetrical animals having three germ layers</li>
<li>They do not have organs of locomotion</li>
<li>They include Cestodes, Trematodes and Nematodes.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="modes-of-infection-of-helminths">MODES OF INFECTION OF HELMINTHS<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#modes-of-infection-of-helminths" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Penetration e.g. Strongyloides stercolaris</li>
<li>Ingestion e.g. Ascaris lumbricoides</li>
<li>Insect vector e.g. Wuchereria bancrofti</li>
<li>Mother to child transmission</li>
<li>Retroinfection e.g. Enterobias vermicularis</li>
<li>Inhalation e.g. Haemophilus influenza</li>
<li>Skin contact e.g. scabies.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="source-of-parasites">SOURCE OF PARASITES<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#source-of-parasites" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>The sources of the parasites are different. Exposure of humans to the parasites may occur in one of the following ways:</p>
<ol>
<li>Contaminated soil (Geo-helminthes), water (cercariae of blood flukes) and food (Taenia in raw meat).</li>
<li>Blood sucking insects or arthropods (as in filarial worms).</li>
<li>Domestic or wild animals harboring the parasite (as in echinococcus in dogs).</li>
<li>Person to person (as in Enterobius vermicularis, Hymenolopis nana).</li>
<li>Oneself (auto-infection) as in Enterobius vermicularis</li>
</ol>
<p>They enter the body through different routes including: mouth, skin and the respiratory tract by means of inhalation of airborne eggs.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="classification-of-helminthes">CLASSIFICATION OF HELMINTHES<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#classification-of-helminthes" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>The helminthes are classified into three major groups. These are:</p>
<ol>
<li>Trematodes (Flukes)</li>
<li>Nematodes (Round worms)</li>
<li>Cestodes (Tape worms)</li>
</ol>
<p>The Trematodes and Cestodes are groups of flat worms.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="differentiating-features-of-helminths">DIFFERENTIATING FEATURES OF HELMINTHS<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#differentiating-features-of-helminths" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="trematodes">TREMATODES<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#trematodes" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="medically-important-trematodes-flukes">MEDICALLY IMPORTANT TREMATODES (FLUKES)<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#medically-important-trematodes-flukes" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="introduction">INTRODUCTION<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#introduction" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>Trematodes belong to the phylum <em>platyhelminthes</em>.</p>
<p>They are found in a wide range of habitats. The great majority inhabit the <em>alimentary canal</em>, liver, bile duct, ureter and bladder of vertebrate animals.</p>
<p>According to the sites they inhabit, there are four groups of flukes.</p>
<p>These are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Blood flukes,</li>
<li>Intestinal flukes,</li>
<li>Liver flukes, and</li>
<li>Lung flukes.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="blood-flukes">BLOOD FLUKES<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#blood-flukes" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>These are flukes that reside mainly in the blood vessels of various organs.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="schistosomiasis-bilharziasis">SCHISTOSOMIASIS (BILHARZIASIS)<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#schistosomiasis-bilharziasis" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>It is estimated that about 600 million people in 79 countries suffer from schistosomiasis (Bilharziasis). The schistosomes cause intestinal, hepato-splenic, pulmonary, urogenital, cerebral and other forms of schistosomiasis. Schistosome is the only fluke with separate sexes. The female worm lies in the gynecophoral canal of the male. This condition is important for transportation.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="there-are-five-medically-important-species">There are five medically important species:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#there-are-five-medically-important-species" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ol>
<li>Schistosoma mansoni: causes intestinal schistosomiasis.</li>
<li>Schistosoma haematobium: causes vesical (urinary) schistosomiasis.</li>
<li>Schistosoma japonicum: causes intestinal schistosomiasis.</li>
<li>Schistosoma intercalatum: causes intestinal schistosomiasis.</li>
<li>Schistosoma mekongi: causes intestinal schistosomiasis. This seems to cause milder disease in man. It causes disease in other vertebrate hosts.</li>
</ol>
<p>The first two schistosomes (S. mansoni and S. haematobium) are the most prevalent.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="schistosoma-mansoni">SCHISTOSOMA MANSONI<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#schistosoma-mansoni" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Habitat - This species lives in the veins of the intestine.</p>
<p>Geographical distribution: It is found in Africa, South America, Middle East (some Arab countries) etc. Stream and lake-based transmission is common.</p>
<p>The snail hosts that harbor <em>S. mansoni</em> are the genera: Biomphalaria (<em>B. glabrata</em>) and Trobicorbis. These have oval shells.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="morphology">Morphology<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#morphology" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Male: The male ranges in size from 1-1.4 cm in length and the body is covered by coarse tubercles. It has 6-9 testes</li>
<li>Female: The female is 1.5-2.0 cm in length. The ovary is present in the anterior third and Vitelline glands occupy the posterior two-thirds. It lays about 100-300 eggs daily. The uterus is short containing few ova.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="schistosoma-haematobium">SCHISTOSOMA HAEMATOBIUM<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#schistosoma-haematobium" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="also-called-urinary-schistosomiasis">Also called URINARY SCHISTOSOMIASIS<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#also-called-urinary-schistosomiasis" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Habitat - The worm lives in the veins of the bladder of humans.</li>
<li>The peak prevalence is the 10-14 year age group. The snail hosts that harbor <em>S. haematobium</em> are the genera <em>Bulinus</em> (Bulinus africanus, <em>B. truncatus</em>) and <em>Physopsis</em>.</li>
<li>Male: The male ranges in size from 1-1.5 cm in length. The body is covered by fine tubercles. It has 4-5 testes.</li>
<li>Female: The female ranges in size from 2-2.5 cm in length. The ovary is present in the posterior third. Vitelline glands occupy the posterior thirds. Uterus is long containing many ova. It lays about 20-200 eggs daily.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="schistosoma-japonicum">SCHISTOSOMA JAPONICUM<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#schistosoma-japonicum" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>The female adult worm lays about 500-3500 eggs daily.</li>
<li>The eggs are ovoid, bearing only a minute lateral spine or a small knob postero-laterally.</li>
<li>It is found in Japan, China, and Philippines, etc.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="schistosoma-intercalatum">SCHISTOSOMA INTERCALATUM<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#schistosoma-intercalatum" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>This is the rarest and least pathogenic schistosome that matures in man.</li>
<li>It is found in Western and Central Africa.</li>
<li>The daily egg output is about 300.</li>
<li>The eggs have a terminal spine.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="life-cycle-of-schistosomes">LIFE CYCLE OF SCHISTOSOMES<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#life-cycle-of-schistosomes" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Adult worms reside in pairs: the female lying in the gynecophoral canal of the male.</p>
<p>After fertilization, eggs are passed into the venules.</p>
<p>A larval form – the miracidium – develops within the egg.</p>
<p>Its lytic enzymes and the contraction of the venule rupture the wall of the venule liberating the egg into the perivascular tissues of the intestine (S. mansoni) or urinary bladder (S. haematobium).</p>
<p>The eggs pass into the lumens and organs and are evacuated in the feces (S. mansoni) or the urine (S. haematobium).</p>
<p>On contact with fresh water the miracidia hatch from the eggs and swim about until they find the appropriate snail, which they penetrate.</p>
<p>After two generations of sporocyst development and multiplication within the snail, the fork-tailed cercariae emerge.</p>
<p>Infection to man takes place during bathing or swimming.</p>
<p>The cercariae penetrate the skin, are carried into the systemic circulation and pass through to the portal vessels.</p>
<p>Within the intrahepatic portion of the portal system, the worms feed and grow to maturity.</p>

</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="fresh-water">Fresh water<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#fresh-water" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ol start="7">
<li>The eggs reach water and Miracidia hatch.</li>
</ol>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="snail-host">Snail host<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#snail-host" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ol start="8">
<li>The Miracidia penetrate snail. They become sporocyst and multiply. The sporocysts then become cercariae.</li>
<li>Cercariae leave snail into the water. (S. japonicum attaches to water vegetation)</li>
</ol>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="symptoms-and-complications">SYMPTOMS AND COMPLICATIONS<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#symptoms-and-complications" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Patients infected with <em>S. haematobium</em> suffer from terminal haematuria and painful micturition.</li>
<li>There is inflammation of the urinary bladder (cystitis), and enlargement of spleen and liver.</li>
<li>Patients infected with <em>S. mansoni</em> suffer from cercarial dermatitis (swimmers itch) and dysentery (mucus and blood in stool with tenesmus) as well as enlargements of the spleen and liver.</li>
<li><em>S. haematobium</em> causes squamous cell carcinoma in the bladder.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="laboratory-diagnosis">LABORATORY DIAGNOSIS<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#laboratory-diagnosis" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="s-haematobium">S. haematobium:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#s-haematobium" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Examination of the urine after allowing it to sediment in a conical urinalysis glass. A drop from the sediment is taken and examined for eggs. Egg has <strong>terminal spine</strong>.</li>
<li>Biopsy from bladder</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="prevention">PREVENTION:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#prevention" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ol>
<li>
<p>Health education:
A. On use of clean latrines and safe water supply.
B. Avoid urination and defecation in canals, avoid contact with canal water</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Snail control:
A. Physical methods:
i. Periodic clearance of canals from vegetations.
ii. Manual removal of snails and their destruction.
B. Biological methods: Use of natural enemies to the snails such as Marisa.
C. Chemical methods: Molluscides are applied in the canals to kill the snails.</p>
</li>
</ol>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="measures-to-prevent-and-control-of-schistosomiasis">MEASURES TO PREVENT AND CONTROL OF SCHISTOSOMIASIS<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#measures-to-prevent-and-control-of-schistosomiasis" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Avoiding contact with water known to contain cercariae by:</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Providing safe water supplies in the village</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Constructing foot bridges across infested rivers and streams.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Providing safe recreational bathing sites, especially for children.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Preventing water becoming contaminated with eggs by:</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Health education and providing sanitation facilities</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Treating infected persons</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Protecting water supplies from faecal pollution</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Destroying snail intermediate hosts mainly by:</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Using molluscicides that will not harm animal and plant life.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Removing vegetation from locally used water places, draining swamps to eradicate snail habitat.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Preventing seasonal flooding which results in snail numbers and transmission.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Minimizing the risk of infection from new water conservation and irrigation schemes by:</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Treating workers when necessary</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Sitting settlements away from canals, drains and irrigation channels.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Lining canals with cement and keeping them free from silt and vegetation in which snails can breed.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Treating water supplies by:</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Using a chlorine disinfectant where possible</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Storing water for 48hrs to allow time for any cercariae to die.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Using filter systems at water inputs to prevent cercariae from entering.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="intestinal-flukes">INTESTINAL FLUKES<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#intestinal-flukes" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li><strong>Fasciolopsis buski</strong>: These giant intestinal flukes (2-7.5 cm in length) are found in some Asian countries.</li>
<li><strong>Heterophyids</strong>: Minute flukes acquired by ingestion of raw fresh water fish.</li>
<li>They are found in Asian countries.</li>
<li>Neither are found in Ethiopia.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="fasciolopsis-buski">FASCIOLOPSIS BUSKI<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#fasciolopsis-buski" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>
<p>F. buski is the largest fluke that infects humans. It is also known as the giant intestinal fluke.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The pig and buffalo are the normal definitive host.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Infection with F. buski is by ingesting metacercariae from water chestnuts, water caltrops or other aquatic edible plants grown in water contaminated with faeces.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Infection frequently occur when peeling the outer covering off plants with the teeth or eating the fruits of water caltrop on which metacercariae have encysted.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="indirect-lifecycle-of-f-buski">INDIRECT LIFECYCLE OF F.BUSKI<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#indirect-lifecycle-of-f-buski" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Transmission</li>
</ul>
<ol>
<li>Metacercariae ingested from aquatic plants, e.g when peeling stems with teeth.</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>Human host</li>
</ul>
<ol start="2">
<li>Metacercariae excyst, attach to wall of the small intestine.</li>
<li>They become mature flukes</li>
<li>Eggs are produced and passed in faeces</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>Freshwater.</li>
</ul>
<ol start="5">
<li>The eggs reach water and hatch into miracidia.</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>Snail host</li>
</ul>
<ol start="6">
<li>The miracidia enter snail</li>
<li>Miracidia grow into sporocysts, they multiply and change into rediae which then change into Cercariae.</li>
<li>Cercariae leave snail</li>

<li>Aquatic plants</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="signs-and-symptoms-of-fasciolopsiasis">SIGNS AND SYMPTOMS OF FASCIOLOPSIASIS<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#signs-and-symptoms-of-fasciolopsiasis" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Most infections with F.buski are light and asymptomatic.</li>
<li>Heavy infections can cause inflammation and ulceration of the intestinal wall with diarrhoea and abdominal pain.</li>
<li>Toxins produced by the flukes can cause oedema of the face and limbs, ascites and other allergic reactions.</li>
<li>Plasma albumin levels may fall due to protein loss</li>
<li>There is blood eosinophilia and slight macrocytic anaemia.</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="measures-to-prevent-and-control-fasciolopsiasis">MEASURES TO PREVENT AND CONTROL FASCIOLOPSIASIS<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#measures-to-prevent-and-control-fasciolopsiasis" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Treating water plants which may be infected using boiling water or cooking them before eating or teeth-peeling.</li>
<li>Providing latrines and health education to prevent eggs from reaching the water.</li>
<li>Avoiding the use of untreated human or pig faeces as fertilizer.</li>
<li>Identifying and destroying snail hosts and their habitats.</li>
<li>Treating infected individuals.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="liver-flukes">LIVER FLUKES<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#liver-flukes" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li><strong>Clonorchis sinensis</strong>: Chinese liver fluke - adult worms live in bile ducts.</li>
<li><strong>Faciola hepatica</strong>: Sheep liver fluke - is a common parasite, cosmopolitan in distribution. It is large (3 cm in length). Adult worms reside in the large biliary passages and gall bladder.</li>
<li>Other: <strong>Faciola gigantica</strong>: lives in the liver of cattle. Human infections are very rare.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="clonorchis-opisthorchis-sinensis">CLONORCHIS(OPISTHORCHIS)SINENSIS<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#clonorchis-opisthorchis-sinensis" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Its endemic in China, South Korea, Japan</li>
<li>High infections are found in areas where human untreated and animal faeces pollute fish culture ponds or other water bodies which contain the snail and fish intermediate hosts.</li>
<li>C.sinensis is transmitted by eating raw, undercooked, smoked or pickled fish or fish products containing infective metacercariae.</li>
<li>Reservoir hosts include fish eating cats, dogs, pigs and rodents.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="symptoms-of-clonorchiasis">Symptoms of Clonorchiasis<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#symptoms-of-clonorchiasis" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="prevention-and-control-of-clonorchiasis">PREVENTION AND CONTROL OF CLONORCHIASIS<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#prevention-and-control-of-clonorchiasis" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="fasciola-hepatica-fasciola-gigantica">FASCIOLA HEPATICA &amp; FASCIOLA GIGANTICA<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#fasciola-hepatica-fasciola-gigantica" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>They are important animal pathogens.</li>
<li>They live in the liver and bile ducts of sheep, cattle and other animals causing serious disease liver rot.</li>
<li><em>F. gigantica</em> infections in cattle lead to economic loss especially in some African countries. Very occasionally Fasciola flukes infect humans.</li>
<li>Infection with <em>F. hepatica</em> is more common than infection with <em>F. gigantica</em> which is less adapted to humans.</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="general-characteristics-of-nematodes">GENERAL CHARACTERISTICS OF NEMATODES<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#general-characteristics-of-nematodes" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<li>They are un-segmented, elongated and cylindrical.</li>
<li>They have separate sexes with separate appearances.</li>

<ol>
<li>Intestinal nematodes

B. Hookworms (Ancylostoma duodenale &amp; Necator americanus)

A. Enterobius vermicularis
B. Trichuris trichuira.</li>

<p>B. VISCERAL LARVA MIGRANS</p>
<p>A syndrome caused by the migration of parasitic larvae in the viscera of a host for months or years.</p>
<p>It may be caused by transient larval migration in the life cycles of several parasites such as hookworm, <em>Ascaris lumbricoides</em>, <em>T. spiralis</em>, <em>S. strecoralis</em> and other filarial worms.</p>
<p>Toxocariasis</p>
<p>This is a kind of visceral larva migrans caused by</p>
<ul>
<li>Toxocara canis (Dog ascarid) and</li>
<li>Toxocara catis (Cat ascarid).</li>
</ul>
<p>These cause persistent larval migration and thus the visceral larva migrans is called toxocariasis.</p>
<p>Morphology</p>
<ul>
<li>The larvae of <em>Toxocara canis</em> and <em>Toxocara catis</em> measure about 400 µm in length.</li>
<li>The life cycle of these parasites in their respective hosts is similar to that of <em>A. lumbricoides</em> in humans.</li>
</ul>
<p>Epidemiology</p>
<p>Visceral larva migrans is cosmopolitan in distribution.</p>
<p>Transmission:</p>
<ul>
<li>Ingestion of eggs of Toxocara species in contaminated food or soil or direct contact with infected patients. Children are more at risk.</li>
</ul>
<p>Clinical features:</p>
<ul>
<li>Majority are asymptomatic.</li>
<li>Eosinophilia</li>
<li>Cerebral, myocardial and pulmonary involvement may cause death.</li>
</ul>
<p>Diagnosis - Identification of larvae in tissue.</p>
<p>Treatment - Thiabendazole: 25 mg/kg twice daily for 5 days.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="life-cycle">LIFE CYCLE<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#life-cycle" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>

<li>After fertilization, the female penetrates the mucosa of the small intestine and lay eggs.</li>
<li>The eggs hatch into Rhabditiform larvae in the intestine.</li>
<li>The Rhabditiform larvae are:</li>
<li>either passed in faeces or</li>
<li>become filariform larvae in the intestine causing autoinfection.</li>
<li>If the environmental conditions are favorable, the larvae will become free-living worms. They then produce more Rhabditiform larvae.</li>

<ul>
<li>Skin fibrous nodules (onchocercomata) enclosing female worms. The nodules are common in neck, iliac crest and the coccyx.</li>
<li>Skin hypo- or hyper- pigmentation. Dermatitis is present. In advanced cases, the skin becomes thickened and wrinkled, showing lizard or leopard skin appearance.</li>
<li>Elephantiasis of the external genitalia and corneal opacity and optic atrophy may finally cause blindness.</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="pathogenecity-and-life-cycle">PATHOGENECITY AND LIFE CYCLE<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#pathogenecity-and-life-cycle" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Infection is acquired by drinking unfiltered or not boiled water that contains Cyclops species.</p>
<p>The larvae are released in the stomach, penetrate the intestinal wall and find their way to the subcutaneous tissue.</p>
<p>Mating takes place in the axillary or inguinal regions 3 months after infection.</p>
<p>The male worms then die in the tissue and the female worms move down to the limbs within 10 months.</p>
<p>In about 1 year, female worms in the subcutaneous tissue provoke the formation of a burning blister in the skin of the legs.</p>
<p>When in water, the blister bursts, and about 5 cm of the worm is extruded from the resulting ulcer - thus releasing many thousands of first stage larvae.</p>
<p>The larvae swim in water and are ingested by the intermediate host - Cyclops species- within about 4 days.</p>
<p>Inside the Cyclops, the larvae molt twice and become infective in 3 weeks.</p>

<p>It occasionally penetrates the eyeball and causes loss of the eye.</p>

<p>It causes trichinosis - a cosmopolitan infection.</p>
<p>More than 100 different animal species can be infected with Trichinella species, but the major reservoir host for human infections is swine.</p>

<p>Adult female worm measures 3-4 mm in length and the adult male worm measures 1.4-2.6 mm in length.</p>
<p>The encysted larvae measure 800-1300 µm in length.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="pathogenecity-and-life-cycle">PATHOGENECITY AND LIFE CYCLE<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#pathogenecity-and-life-cycle" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>After ingesting infected meat, the capsule of the encysted larvae is digested by gastric juice, and the larvae are released in the duodenum or jejunum where they molt four times to become adult worm.</li>
<li>After mating, the male worm dies and the female worm begins to deliver the embryos 4-7 days after the infection.</li>
<li>The larvae penetrate the intestinal wall and migrate through the lymphatic vessels to the blood stream, which carries them to various organs.</li>
<li>Skeletal muscles and diaphragm are most frequently parasitized.</li>
<li>Others include the tongue and ocular muscles.</li>
</ul>

<ol>
<li>The intestinal phase: lasting 1-7 days - asymptomatic; sometimes cause nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, constipation, pain, etc, and</li>
<li>The muscle phase: which causes myalgia, palpabral edema, eosinophilia, fever, myocarditis, meningitis, bronchopneumonia etc.</li>
</ol>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="cestodes-tapeworms">CESTODES (TAPEWORMS)<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#cestodes-tapeworms" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="introduction">INTRODUCTION<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#introduction" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>The tapeworms are hermaphroditic and require an intermediate host.</li>
<li>The adult tapeworms found in humans have flat body, white or grayish in color.</li>
<li>They consist of an anterior attachment organ or scolex and a chain of segments (proglottids) also called strobilla.</li>
<li>The strobilla is the entire body except the scolex.</li>
<li>The scolex has suckers or grooves. It has rosetellum, which has 1 or 2 rows of hooks situated on the center of the scolex.</li>
<li>Adult tapeworms inhabit the small intestine, where they live attached to the mucosa.</li>
<li>Tapeworms do not have a digestive system.</li>
<li>Their food is absorbed from the host’s intestine.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="hymenolepis-nana-dwarf-tapeworm">HYMENOLEPIS NANA (DWARF TAPEWORM)<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#hymenolepis-nana-dwarf-tapeworm" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="morphology">Morphology<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#morphology" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Adult worm measures 1-3 cm in length. It is made up of head (scolex), neck and segmented body.</p>
<p>The head carries four suckers and a rostellum armed with one row of hooks. The segments of the body are divided into mature and gravid segments.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="infective-stage-and-mode-of-infection">Infective stage and mode of infection<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#infective-stage-and-mode-of-infection" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The egg, which is immediately infective when passed by the patient, is rounded, about 40 microns in diameter. It contains a six- hooked oncosphere within a rigid membrane (the embryosphere). This embryosphere has two polar thickening or knobs from which project 4-8 long, thin filaments called polar filaments.</p>
<p>Infection takes place by:</p>
<ol>
<li>Ingestion of egg with contaminated raw vegetables.</li>
<li>Direct infection from a patient</li>
<li>Auto infection: the eggs of H. nana are infective as soon as they are passed with feces by the patient. If the hands of the patient are contaminated by these eggs, she/he infects herself/himself again and again.</li>
</ol>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="pathogenecity">Pathogenecity<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#pathogenecity" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Light infections produce no symptoms. In fairly heavy infections, children may show lack of appetite, abdominal pain and diarrhea.</p>
<p>Treatment. Niclesomide: 4 tablets chewed in a single dose daily for 5 days.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="hymenolepis-diminuta-rat-tapeworm">HYMENOLEPIS DIMINUTA (RAT TAPEWORM)<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#hymenolepis-diminuta-rat-tapeworm" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Hymenolepis diminuta differs from Hymenolepis nana in that:</p>
<ul>
<li>The adult worm measures about 10-60 cm</li>
<li>The rosetellum on the head has no hooks</li>
<li>In the mature segment, there are two testes at one side and another testis on the other side.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="life-cycle">Life cycle<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#life-cycle" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>The adult worms are present in the small intestine of man and rats.</li>
<li>Eggs passed in stool are similar to the eggs of <em>H. nana</em> but are brown in color with no polar filaments arising from the polar thickening.</li>
<li>The eggs are ingested by the rat flea where they develop to cysticercoid stage.</li>
<li>Infection to man takes place accidentally by food or contaminated hands by cysticercoid stage.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="pathogenecity">Pathogenecity<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#pathogenecity" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Most infections are asymptomatic, but occasionally, patients may present with nausea, anorexia and diarrhea.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="echinococcus">ECHINOCOCCUS<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#echinococcus" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>There are two different species. These are: <em>Echinococcus granulosus</em> and <em>Echinococcus multilocularis</em></p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="echinococcus-granulosus-dog-tape-worm">Echinococcus granulosus (dog tape worm)<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#echinococcus-granulosus-dog-tape-worm" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Responsible for most cases of echinococcosis.</p>
<p>Echinococcosis is caused by larval tapeworms.</p>
<p>The disease is common in East Africa (the highest prevalence is seen in Kenya: 10-15%).</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="morphology">Morphology<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#morphology" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The adult worm measures 3-6 mm in length (up to 1 cm).</p>
<p>It has scolex, neck and strobilla. Adult worms live in small intestine of definitive host (dog).</p>
<p>Man is an intermediate host - carrying the hydatid cyst (larva).</p>
<p>Man contracts infection by swallowing eggs in excreta of definitive host.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="life-cycle-and-pathogenecity">Life cycle and Pathogenecity<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#life-cycle-and-pathogenecity" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Oncosphere hatch in duodenum or small intestine into embryos (oncosphere) which:</p>
<ul>
<li>Penetrate wall</li>
<li>Enter portal veins</li>
<li>Migrate via portal blood supply to organs: eg: lungs, liver, brain etc., thus, causing extra intestinal infections. In these organs, larvae develop into hydatid cysts.</li>
</ul>
<p>The cysts may be large, filled with clear fluid and contain characteristic protoscolices (immature forms of the head of the parasite). These mature into developed scolices, which are infective for dogs.</p>

<p>i) Ingestion of water or vegetables polluted by infected dog feces.

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="clinical-features">Clinical features<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#clinical-features" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Asymptomatic infection is common, but in symptomatic patients</p>
<ul>
<li>It may cause cough - with hemoptysis in lung hydatid disease.</li>
<li>Hepatomegaly - with abdominal pain and discomfort</li>
<li>Pressure - from expanding cyst</li>
<li>Rupture of cyst - severe allergic reaction - anaphylaxis.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="diagnosis">Diagnosis:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#diagnosis" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<li>Demonstration of protoscolices in cyst after operation</li>

<ul>
<li>In adult stage, T. saginata inhabits the upper jejunum where it may survive for as long as 25 years.</li>
<li>It causes intestinal infection, Taeniasis.</li>
<li>It has worldwide distribution.</li>
<li>These are one of the true and segmented tapeworms. Their body is divided into three regions;</li>

<li>Neck: posterior to the scolex</li>

<ul>
<li>Adult worm measures 5-10 meters in length.</li>
<li>The pyriform scolex has 4 suckers but no rostellum.</li>
<li>The mature segments have irregularly alternate lateral genital pores.</li>
<li>Each of the terminal segments contains only a uterus made up of a median stem with 15-30 lateral branches.</li>
</ul>

<li>The adult worm lives in the small intestine of man.</li>
<li>Gravid segments pass out in the stool and become disintegrated and eggs come out to the soil.</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="taenia-solium-pork-tapeworm">TAENIA SOLIUM (PORK TAPEWORM)<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#taenia-solium-pork-tapeworm" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>The adult worms of <em>T. solium</em> reside or inhabit the upper jejunum.</p>
<p>Infection has worldwide distribution.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="morphology">Morphology:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#morphology" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Adult worm measures about 3 meters in length. The globular scolex has rostellum with 2 rows of hooklets. There are &lt;1000 proglottids.</p>
<p>Gravid proglottid liberates about 30,000-50,000 eggs.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="life-cycle">LIFE CYCLE<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#life-cycle" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Embryonated eggs passed with stool are ingested by pig and the embryo is released.</li>
<li>It penetrates the intestinal wall and is carried by vascular channels to all parts of the body.</li>
<li>After a period of 2-3 months of development the encysted larval stage called cysticerci or bladder worm occurs in the striated muscles of the tongue, neck, trunk brain, eye, and the nervous system.</li>
<li>The cysticercus survives for 5 years.</li>
<li>Humans become infected by eating pork containing larvae, cysticercus cellulosae.</li>
<li>When improperly cooked cysticercus infected meat is eaten by man, the scolex remains undigested and attaches itself to the intestinal wall and chain of proglottids begin to grow to adult worm.</li>
</ul>
<p>Clinical manifestations
Resembles that of T. saginata infection</p>
<p>Diagnosis
Demonstration of eggs in stool specimen</p>
<p>Treatment
Niclosamide: 2 gm PO stat</p>
<p>Prevention:</p>
<ul>
<li>Treatment of infected persons.</li>
<li>Thorough cooking of pork and proper processing</li>
<li>Proper disposal of human excreta (good hygiene/sanitation).</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="morphology">Morphology<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#morphology" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Diphylobotrium latum is the broadest and longest tapeworm. The adult worm measures up to 30 feet with 3000-4000 proglottids, which are wider than they are long. The tapeworm has no rostellum hooks or suckers.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="life-cycle">Life cycle<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#life-cycle" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Unlike Taenia, the gravid segments are retained by the worm.</li>
<li>Operculated eggs passed in feces hatch into small ciliated coracidium larvae which swim about freely.</li>
<li>These are eaten by crustaceans - Cyclops or Diaptomus - in which the larvae develop into second stage larvae- the procercoid.</li>
<li>When the crustaceans are swallowed by fresh water fish, the larvae migrate into the flesh of the muscle fish and develop to pleurocercoid or sparganum larvae.</li>
<li>Humans are infected by ingesting raw or improperly cooked fish. The tapeworm matures in the intestine and after 3 weeks, the adult worm discharges eggs.</li>
<li>The life cycle requires two intermediate hosts.</li>
</ul>
