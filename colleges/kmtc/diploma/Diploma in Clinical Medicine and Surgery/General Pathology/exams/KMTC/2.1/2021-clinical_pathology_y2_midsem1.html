1.
Briefly state the differences in the 3 clinical patterns of angina pectoris (10 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

The three clinical patterns of angina pectoris differ in their pathophysiology, triggers, and clinical presentation:

Feature Stable Angina (Typical) Unstable Angina (Crescendo) Prinzmetal's Angina (Variant)
Pathophysiology Fixed atherosclerotic narrowing (>70%) reducing coronary reserve. Disruption of plaque (rupture/erosion) with superimposed partial thrombosis. Coronary artery vasospasm (not necessarily atherosclerosis).
Triggers Predictable exertion, emotional stress, cold (Increased O2 demand). Occurs at rest or with minimal exertion. Occurs at rest, often at night/early morning. Unrelated to exertion.
Relief Rest or Nitroglycerin. Poorly relieved by rest or Nitroglycerin. Responds promptly to Nitroglycerin and Calcium Channel Blockers.
ECG Changes ST-segment depression (subendocardial ischemia). ST-segment depression or T-wave inversion. Transient ST-segment elevation (transmural ischemia).
Risk Predictable, lower immediate risk of MI. High risk of progressing to Acute Myocardial Infarction (Pre-infarction). Can cause arrhythmias; variable risk.
2.
Outline 5 conditions that causes cardiac failure due to increased volume load (2.5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

Increased volume load (preload) forces the heart to pump more blood than normal, leading to dilation and eventual failure.

  1. Valvular Regurgitation: Mitral regurgitation or Aortic regurgitation (blood leaks back, increasing volume).
  2. Intracardiac Shunts: Ventricular Septal Defect (VSD) or Atrial Septal Defect (ASD) (recirculation of blood).
  3. Patent Ductus Arteriosus (PDA): Shunting of blood from aorta to pulmonary artery.
  4. Hypervolemia (Fluid Overload): Iatrogenic (excess IV fluids) or Renal failure (fluid retention).
  5. High Output States: Severe anemia (heart pumps faster/more to deliver O2), Thyrotoxicosis, or AV fistulas.
3.
Discuss the different types of Cardiomyopathy, and how they differ. Include examples in the answer. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

Cardiomyopathies are diseases of the heart muscle. They are classified into three main functional types:

  1. Dilated Cardiomyopathy (DCM):
    • Pathology: Dilation and impaired contraction of ventricles (Systolic dysfunction). All 4 chambers are often enlarged.
    • Difference: Heart is flabby and hypocontractile. Ejection fraction is low.
    • Examples: Alcohol abuse, Peripartum cardiomyopathy, Chagas disease, Idiopathic.
  2. Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy (HCM):
    • Pathology: Massive hypertrophy of the left ventricle, often asymmetrical (septal), leading to impaired filling (Diastolic dysfunction) and outflow obstruction.
    • Difference: Heart is thick-walled and hypercontractile. Ejection fraction is normal or high.
    • Examples: Genetic mutations (Beta-myosin heavy chain), Friedreich ataxia.
  3. Restrictive Cardiomyopathy:
    • Pathology: Primary decrease in ventricular compliance (stiff walls) resulting in impaired filling (Diastolic dysfunction).
    • Difference: Ventricles are normal size or slightly enlarged, but very stiff. Myocardium is firm.
    • Examples: Amyloidosis, Sarcoidosis, Endomyocardial fibrosis.
4.
Using the Jones criteria, how do you make a diagnosis of rheumatic fever (2.5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

A diagnosis of Rheumatic Fever is made using the Revised Jones Criteria.

Requirement: Evidence of a preceding Group A Streptococcal infection (e.g., positive throat culture, rapid antigen test, or elevated ASOT titers) PLUS:

  • Two Major Criteria OR
  • One Major and Two Minor Criteria.

Criteria:

  • Major Criteria (CASES):
    • Carditis (Pancarditis)
    • Arthritis (Migratory polyarthritis)
    • Subcutaneous Nodules
    • Erythema Marginatum
    • Sydenham's Chorea
  • Minor Criteria (FRAPPE):
    • Fever
    • Raised ESR/CRP (Acute phase reactants)
    • Arthralgia
    • Prolonged PR interval (on ECG)
    • Previous Rheumatic Fever
