Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions

20 Marks
1.
Primary etiology of a disease includes all of the following except one. Which one?
a. Genetic aberrations
b. Fungal infections
c. Unknown causation
d. Autoimmune
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c. Unknown causation

Explanation: While "idiopathic" (unknown cause) is a category used in medicine, strictly speaking, every disease has a cause (etiology), even if we haven't discovered it yet. "Unknown causation" is not an agent or mechanism of disease like genetic mutations, infections, or immune responses. It is a placeholder for ignorance rather than a true etiological category.

  • Genetic aberrations: A primary intrinsic cause (e.g., Down syndrome, Cystic fibrosis).
  • Fungal infections: A primary acquired cause (biologic agent).
  • Autoimmune: An immunologic etiology.
2.
Gross morphologic change in a stressed cell may include:
a. High sodium influx in a cell
b. Hypertrophy
c. Hypercalcemia
d. Microscopic cell injury
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b. Hypertrophy

Explanation: "Gross" morphology refers to changes visible to the naked eye. Hypertrophy (increase in the size of an organ due to increase in cell size) is a gross change (e.g., enlarged heart in hypertension).

  • Sodium influx: A molecular/ionic event.
  • Hypercalcemia: A biochemical finding in blood, not a gross morphologic change of a cell.
  • Microscopic cell injury: By definition, visible only under a microscope.
3.
A 54-year-old with persistent fever, swelling of cervical lymph nodes, night sweats and negative TB test has been told that one of the nodes has to be removed for further investigations. Which of the following procedures best describes the removal of nodes?
a. Excisional biopsy
b. Incisional biopsy
c. Fixation
d. Minor surgery
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a. Excisional biopsy

Explanation: Excisional biopsy involves the removal of the entire lesion or node. For lymph nodes, this is preferred over incisional biopsy (sampling a part) to preserve the architecture for diagnosis of lymphomas.

4.
In question 3 above, the node has been removed and immediately put in liquid. Which of the following best describes this process?
a. Excisional biopsy
b. Incisional biopsy
c. Fixation
d. Minor surgery
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c. Fixation

Explanation: Placing tissue in a liquid preservative (like formalin) immediately after removal is called fixation. This prevents decay (autolysis) and preserves cellular structure.

5.
The following statements explain why the node in question 3 was put in a liquid. Which one is UNTRUE?
a. to enhance autolysis
b. to coagulate the tissue to prevent loss of easily diffusible substances
c. to fortify the tissue against the effects of tissue processing
d. to allow tissue staining
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a. to enhance autolysis

Explanation: The purpose of fixation is to prevent autolysis (self-digestion by enzymes), not to enhance it. Fixation "fixes" or freezes the tissue in its current state.

6.
In question 4 above, which of the following best describes the liquid used?
a. Hematoxylin
b. Formaldehyde
c. Paraffin
d. Normal saline
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b. Formaldehyde

Explanation: The most common fixative used for routine histopathology is 10% Neutral Buffered Formalin (which contains formaldehyde).

  • Hematoxylin: A stain (dye).
  • Paraffin: Wax used for embedding.
  • Normal saline: Used for washing, not fixation (tissue will rot in saline).
7.
Which of the following organs might need ultrasound guided fine needle aspiration?
a. Breast
b. Thyroid
c. Lymph node
d. Liver
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d. Liver

Explanation: The liver is a deep intra-abdominal organ. While palpable masses in breast, thyroid, and superficial lymph nodes can often be aspirated blindly (by palpation), liver lesions almost always require image guidance (Ultrasound or CT) to ensure the needle reaches the correct target safely.

8.
A 21-year-old female student at KMTC presents to the outpatient department at a county teaching and referral hospital with complains of fever, lower abdominal pains and yellow vaginal discharge. Which of the following cytological methods is applicable in examination of the vaginal discharge?
a. Exfoliative cytology
b. Abrasive cytology
c. Biopsy
d. Fine needle aspiration cytology
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a. Exfoliative cytology

Explanation: Examination of cells that have shed spontaneously into body fluids or secretions (like vaginal discharge) is called exfoliative cytology. A Pap smear is a classic example.

9.
While on night shift at a county teaching and referral hospital, a clinical officer is called to certify death of a previous critically ill patient. Which of the following is a sure sign of death?
a. Absent pulse
b. No respiratory movements
c. Absent gag reflex
d. Cold body
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d. Cold body

Explanation: While absent pulse and respiration are signs of somatic death, they can be mimicked by suspended animation. Post-mortem changes like Algor Mortis (cooling of the body), Rigor Mortis, and Livor Mortis are considered "sure" signs of death because they occur irreversibly after death. Among the choices, "Cold body" represents Algor Mortis.

10.
A 45-year-old has been smoking tobacco for the last 12 years, averagely 12 sticks per day. Which of the following best describes the adaptive changes on the bronchial tree epithelial cells?
a. Hypertrophy
b. Hyperplasia
c. Atrophy
d. Metaplasia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d. Metaplasia

Explanation: In chronic smokers, the fragile ciliated columnar epithelium of the respiratory tract transforms into tougher stratified squamous epithelium to withstand the irritation of smoke. This reversible change of one adult cell type to another is called Squamous Metaplasia.

11.
Gloria is a known hypertensive patient on unknown medications for the last 5 years. Today she came to the hospital complaining of chest pains, easy fatigability and coughing. A chest x ray was done and it showed an enlarged heart. Which of the following best explains the enlarged heart?
a. Hypertrophy
b. Hyperplasia
c. Atrophy
d. Metaplasia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a. Hypertrophy

Explanation: The heart muscle (myocardium) cannot divide (no hyperplasia). In response to increased workload (pressure overload from hypertension), the individual muscle cells increase in size, leading to enlargement of the organ. This is Left Ventricular Hypertrophy.

12.
Cardinal signs of inflammation do NOT include which of the following?
a. Cyanosis
b. Redness
c. Pain
d. Oedema
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a. Cyanosis

Explanation: The 5 cardinal signs of inflammation are: 1. Redness (Rubor) 2. Heat (Calor) 3. Swelling (Tumor/Oedema) 4. Pain (Dolor) 5. Loss of function (Functio laesa) Cyanosis (bluish discoloration) is a sign of hypoxia, not inflammation.

13.
Which of the following is NOT part of sequence in leukocyte recruitment at the site of injury?
a. Margination, adhesion to endothelium and rolling along the vessel wall
b. Firm adhesion to the endothelium
c. Transmigration
d. Immigration of chemotactic cells
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d. Immigration of chemotactic cells

Explanation: The term "immigration" is not used in the standard sequence. The steps are: 1. Margination & Rolling 2. Adhesion 3. Transmigration (Diapedesis) 4. Chemotaxis (Migration along a chemical gradient) Option 'd' is the odd one out due to incorrect terminology.

14.
After extravasating from blood, leukocytes migrate toward sites of infection along a chemical gradient by a process called?
a. Margination
b. Chemotaxis
c. Rolling
d. Transmigration
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b. Chemotaxis

Explanation: Chemotaxis is the unidirectional movement of cells along a chemical gradient towards the site of injury or infection.

15.
Which of the following is NOT a step in phagocytosis?
a. Recognition and attachment
b. Engulfment
c. Transmigration
d. Killing and degradation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c. Transmigration

Explanation: Transmigration is how the leukocyte gets out of the blood vessel. Phagocytosis happens after the cell has arrived. The steps of phagocytosis are: 1. Recognition/Attachment, 2. Engulfment, 3. Killing/Degradation.

16.
Which of the following mediators of inflammation are preformed?
a. Reactive Oxygen species
b. Bradykinin
c. Histamine
d. Nitric Oxide
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c. Histamine

Explanation: Histamine is stored in granules within mast cells and basophils (preformed) and is released immediately upon stimulation. ROS and Nitric Oxide are synthesized de novo on demand.

17.
Which of the following mediators are newly synthesized at the site of injury?
a. Bradykinin
b. Leukotrienes
c. Serotonin
d. Histamine
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b. Leukotrienes

Explanation: Leukotrienes and Prostaglandins are metabolites of arachidonic acid synthesized de novo after cell membrane phospholipids are broken down. Histamine and Serotonin are preformed.

18.
The principal mediator of increased vascular permeability during acute inflammation is:
a. Bradykinin
b. Prostaglandins
c. Histamine
d. Integrin
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c. Histamine

Explanation: While Bradykinin also causes permeability, Histamine is the principal mediator of the immediate transient phase of increased vascular permeability.

19.
The main source of serotonin during acute inflammation is:
a. Neutrophils
b. Mast cells
c. Liver
d. Platelets
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d. Platelets

Explanation: Serotonin (5-hydroxytryptamine) is primarily found in platelet granules and is released during platelet aggregation.

20.
Regarding cell injury and death:
a. Initially there is a reduction in cell size during cell necrosis
b. Apoptosis is associated with an inflammatory response
c. Necrosis results from caspases sequestered in mitochondrial membranes
d. Cellular swelling and fatty change indicate reversible cell injury
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d. Cellular swelling and fatty change indicate reversible cell injury

Explanation: - d (True): These are the two hallmarks of reversible injury. - a (False): Necrosis involves cell swelling (oncosis); apoptosis involves shrinkage. - b (False): Apoptosis is non-inflammatory; necrosis causes inflammation. - c (False): Caspases are the executioners of apoptosis, not necrosis.

Section 2: Short Answer Questions

40 Marks
1.
Define the following terms: (10 marks)
a) Pathology
b) Etiology
c) Pathogenesis
d) Pathophysiology
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Pathology: The scientific study of disease (logos = study, pathos = suffering). It involves the investigation of the causes (etiology) of disease and the associated changes at the cellular, tissue, and organ levels.
  2. Etiology: The origin or underlying cause of a disease. It answers the question "Why" a disease happens (e.g., genetic defects, bacteria, toxins).
  3. Pathogenesis: The sequence of events in the response of cells or tissues to the etiologic agent, from the initial stimulus to the ultimate expression of the disease. It answers "How" the disease develops.
  4. Pathophysiology: The study of the disordered physiological processes associated with disease or injury. It bridges the gap between the structural changes (pathology) and the clinical signs/symptoms.
2.
Explain any five causes of cell injury. (10 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Hypoxia (Oxygen Deprivation): One of the most common causes. Can result from ischemia (reduced blood flow), inadequate oxygenation of blood (e.g., pneumonia), or reduced oxygen-carrying capacity (e.g., anemia, CO poisoning). Lack of oxygen stops aerobic respiration, depleting ATP.
  2. Physical Agents: Trauma (mechanical injury), extremes of temperature (burns or deep cold), changes in atmospheric pressure, radiation, and electric shock can directly damage cells.
  3. Chemical Agents and Drugs: Substances like glucose or salt in hypertonic concentrations, oxygen in high concentrations, trace poisons (arsenic, cyanide), environmental pollutants, recreational drugs (alcohol), and therapeutic drugs can cause injury.
  4. Infectious Agents: These range from submicroscopic viruses to tapeworms. Rickettsiae, bacteria, fungi, and higher parasites cause injury via toxins, direct destruction, or immune responses.
  5. Immunologic Reactions: The immune system can cause injury (autoimmune diseases) or overreact to external agents (allergic reactions), damaging host cells (e.g., anaphylaxis).

Other causes include: Genetic defects and Nutritional imbalances.

3.
Briefly outline 5 physiologic situations in which apoptosis is useful. (10 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Embryogenesis: Programmed cell death is crucial during development (e.g., removal of webs between fingers/toes, implantation, organogenesis).
  2. Hormone-dependent Involution: Regression of tissues upon hormone withdrawal, such as endometrial cell breakdown during the menstrual cycle or regression of the lactating breast after weaning.
  3. Cell Deletion in Proliferating Populations: Maintenance of cell number homeostasis, such as the destruction of cells in intestinal crypts to maintain constant villi numbers.
  4. Death of Host Cells that have Served their Purpose: Elimination of neutrophils after an acute inflammatory response or lymphocytes at the end of an immune response.
  5. Elimination of Potentially Harmful Self-Reactive Lymphocytes: To prevent autoimmune reactions (central tolerance in thymus/bone marrow).
4.
Write short notes on:
a. Fine needle aspiration cytology (5 marks)
b. Outline the main applications of cytology (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

a. Fine Needle Aspiration Cytology (FNAC):

  • Definition: A diagnostic procedure where a thin needle (23-25G) is inserted into a lesion (palpable or image-guided) to aspirate cells for microscopic examination.
  • Uses: Diagnosis of lumps in breast, thyroid, lymph nodes, salivary glands, and soft tissues.
  • Advantages: Rapid, cost-effective, minimally invasive, outpatient procedure, no anesthesia required, low complication rate.
  • Disadvantages: Only provides cellular details (cytology), not tissue architecture (histology); chance of inadequate sampling.

b. Main Applications of Cytology:

  • Screening for Disease: E.g., Pap smears for cervical cancer screening to detect precancerous lesions.
  • Diagnosis of Tumors: Differentiating benign from malignant lumps (using FNAC or exfoliative cytology).
  • Follow-up/Monitoring: Checking for recurrence of cancer after treatment.
  • Diagnosis of Infections: Identification of specific pathogens (e.g., Candida, Trichomonas in vaginal smears, Pneumocystis in sputum).
  • Hormonal Evaluation: Assessing hormonal status in women via vaginal smears (Maturation Index).

Section 3: Long Answer Questions

40 Marks
1.
Discuss the five (5) types of necrosis. (20 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Coagulative Necrosis:
    • Mechanism: Denaturation of structural proteins and enzymes blocks proteolysis. The basic cell outline is preserved for days.
    • Characteristic: Typical of hypoxic death (ischemia) in all solid organs except the brain.
    • Appearance: Pale, firm tissue. Microscopically, "tombstones" of cells (eosinophilic anucleate cells) are seen.
    • Example: Myocardial infarction, Kidney infarct.
  2. Liquefactive Necrosis:
    • Mechanism: Enzymatic digestion of dead cells predominates, transforming tissue into a viscous liquid mass (pus).
    • Characteristic: Typical of bacterial/fungal infections (due to leukocytes) and hypoxic death in the CNS.
    • Appearance: Creamy yellow pus (in infections) or cystic space (in brain).
    • Example: Brain abscess, Stroke (cerebral infarction).
  3. Caseous Necrosis:
    • Mechanism: A combination of coagulative and liquefactive necrosis.
    • Characteristic: Distinctive form found in Tuberculosis.
    • Appearance: "Cheese-like" (white, friable) gross appearance. Microscopically, amorphous granular debris within a granuloma.
    • Example: Pulmonary Tuberculosis.
  4. Fat Necrosis:
    • Mechanism: Destruction of fat by pancreatic lipases (enzymatic) or trauma. Fatty acids released bind calcium (saponification).
    • Characteristic: Specific to adipose tissue.
    • Appearance: Chalky white deposits (calcium soaps). Microscopically, shadowy outlines of necrotic fat cells with basophilic calcium deposits.
    • Example: Acute Pancreatitis, Traumatic fat necrosis of breast.
  5. Fibrinoid Necrosis:
    • Mechanism: Deposition of immune complexes and fibrin in vessel walls.
    • Characteristic: Seen in immune reactions involving blood vessels.
    • Appearance: Not gross visible. Microscopically, bright pink (eosinophilic) amorphous "fibrin-like" material in vessel walls.
    • Example: Malignant hypertension, Vasculitis (Polyarteritis nodosa).
2.
a). Explain the 5 cardinal signs of inflammation (10 marks)
b). Elucidate stages of Healing by first intension (10 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

a) The 5 Cardinal Signs of Inflammation:

  1. Rubor (Redness): Caused by vasodilation and increased blood flow (hyperemia) to the inflamed area.
  2. Calor (Heat): Also caused by increased blood flow carrying core body heat to peripheral tissues (less prominent in internal organs).
  3. Tumor (Swelling): Caused by increased vascular permeability allowing fluid (exudate) and cells to leak from blood vessels into the tissue spaces (oedema).
  4. Dolor (Pain): Caused by pressure from swelling on nerve endings and by chemical mediators like bradykinin and prostaglandins stimulating pain receptors.
  5. Functio Laesa (Loss of Function): Result of pain, swelling, and tissue injury preventing the organ or tissue from functioning normally.

b) Stages of Healing by First Intention (Primary Union):

This occurs in clean, uninfected surgical incisions approximated by sutures.

  1. Hemostasis & Inflammation (< 24 hours):
    • Blood clot forms, sealing the wound.
    • Neutrophils migrate into the clot to clean debris/bacteria.
  2. Epithelialization (24-48 hours):
    • Basal cells from wound edges migrate across the dermis, meeting in the midline.
  3. Granulation Tissue Formation (Day 3):
    • Macrophages replace neutrophils.
    • Fibroblasts and new blood vessels (angiogenesis) invade the incision space.
  4. Collagen Deposition (Day 5):
    • Maximum angiogenesis. Fibroblasts produce collagen bridging the incision. Epidermis regains normal thickness.
  5. Maturation & Remodeling (Week 2 onwards):
    • Inflammatory cells and vessels recede (blanching).
    • Collagen is remodeled and cross-linked to increase tensile strength.
    • By end of 1st month, scar is cellular connective tissue without inflammatory cells.
