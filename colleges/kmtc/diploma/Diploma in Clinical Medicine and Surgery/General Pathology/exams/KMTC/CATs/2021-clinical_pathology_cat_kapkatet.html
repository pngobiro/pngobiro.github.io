Short Answer Questions

20 Marks
1.
About congenital heart diseases: (10 marks)
a) List 5 acyanotic congenital heart diseases.
b) List 5 cyanotic congenital heart diseases.
Show Answer

Answer:

a) 5 Acyanotic Congenital Heart Diseases (Left-to-Right Shunt or Obstructive):

  1. Atrial Septal Defect (ASD): Hole in the interatrial septum.
  2. Ventricular Septal Defect (VSD): Hole in the interventricular septum (most common).
  3. Patent Ductus Arteriosus (PDA): Persistent connection between aorta and pulmonary artery.
  4. Coarctation of the Aorta: Narrowing of the aortic lumen.
  5. Pulmonary Stenosis: Narrowing of the pulmonary valve/outflow tract (can be acyanotic if mild/moderate).

b) 5 Cyanotic Congenital Heart Diseases (Right-to-Left Shunt):

  1. Tetralogy of Fallot (TOF): The most common cyanotic heart defect.
  2. Transposition of the Great Arteries (TGA): Aorta and pulmonary artery are switched.
  3. Truncus Arteriosus: Single common arterial trunk.
  4. Tricuspid Atresia: Absence of tricuspid valve.
  5. Total Anomalous Pulmonary Venous Return (TAPVR): Pulmonary veins drain into right atrium instead of left atrium.

Study Tip: Remember the "5 Ts" of Cyanotic heart disease: Tetralogy, Transposition, Truncus, Tricuspid atresia, Total anomalous return.

2.
About angina: (10 marks)
a) Define angina.
b) Differentiate between stable and unstable angina.
Show Answer

Answer:

a) Definition of Angina Pectoris:

Angina pectoris is a clinical syndrome characterized by paroxysmal and recurrent attacks of substernal or precordial chest discomfort (pressure, squeezing, or pain) caused by transient myocardial ischemia that falls short of inducing necrosis. It occurs when myocardial oxygen demand exceeds supply.

b) Differentiation between Stable and Unstable Angina:

Feature Stable Angina Unstable Angina
Pathology Fixed atherosclerotic stenosis (usually >70% occlusion). Disruption of atherosclerotic plaque (rupture/erosion) with partial thrombosis.
Trigger Predictable; occurs with exertion, stress, or heavy meals (increased demand). Unpredictable; occurs at rest or with minimal exertion.
Duration Short duration (< 15-20 mins). Prolonged duration (> 20 mins).
Relief Relieved by rest or Nitroglycerin. Poorly relieved or not relieved by rest/Nitroglycerin.
Pattern Unchanging frequency/severity over time. Crescendo pattern (increasing frequency, severity, and duration).
Risk Lower immediate risk of infarction. High risk of progressing to Acute MI (Medical Emergency).

Long Answer Question

20 Marks
3.
Classify heart failure and give the clinical manifestations and causes of each classification. (20 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

Heart failure is commonly classified based on the side of the heart affected (Left vs. Right). Other classifications include Systolic vs. Diastolic and High vs. Low Output.

1. Left-Sided Heart Failure (LHF)

Pathophysiology: Failure of the left ventricle to pump blood into systemic circulation causes back-pressure into the pulmonary circulation.

Causes:

  • Ischemic Heart Disease (IHD) / Myocardial Infarction.
  • Systemic Hypertension (Pressure overload).
  • Valvular diseases (Aortic Stenosis, Mitral Regurgitation).
  • Cardiomyopathies.

Clinical Manifestations:

  • Dyspnea: Breathlessness on exertion.
  • Orthopnea: Dyspnea when lying flat (needs pillows).
  • Paroxysmal Nocturnal Dyspnea (PND): Waking up gasping for air.
  • Cough: Often with frothy, blood-stained sputum (pulmonary edema).
  • Fatigue: Due to reduced tissue perfusion.
  • Signs: Bibasilar crackles (rales), S3 gallop.

2. Right-Sided Heart Failure (RHF)

Pathophysiology: Failure of the right ventricle to pump blood into the lungs causes back-pressure into the systemic venous circulation.

Causes:

  • Left-sided heart failure: The most common cause (pure RHF is rare).
  • Cor Pulmonale: RHF due to primary lung disease (COPD, Pulmonary Hypertension).
  • Pulmonary Stenosis.

Clinical Manifestations:

  • Peripheral Edema: Pitting edema in legs/ankles (dependent edema).
  • Ascites: Fluid in peritoneal cavity.
  • Hepatomegaly: Enlarged, tender liver ("Nutmeg liver").
  • Splenomegaly: Congestive enlargement of spleen.
  • Distended Neck Veins: Elevated Jugular Venous Pressure (JVP).

Other Classifications (Briefly):

  • Systolic Failure: Failure of pumping action (Reduced Ejection Fraction). Caused by MI, dilated cardiomyopathy.
  • Diastolic Failure: Failure of relaxation/filling (Preserved Ejection Fraction). Caused by hypertension, hypertrophy, fibrosis.
  • High-Output Failure: Heart pumps normal/high volume but cannot meet excessive metabolic demand. Caused by severe anemia, thyrotoxicosis, AV fistulas.
