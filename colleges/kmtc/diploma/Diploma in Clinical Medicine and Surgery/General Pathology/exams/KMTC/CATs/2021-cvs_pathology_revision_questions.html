Cardiovascular Pathology

Note

This document contains three sets of revision questions covering the cardiovascular system. These questions are excellent for preparing for CATs and End of Semester examinations.

Set 1: Garissa Campus (Source: Najwa)

1.
Using Jones criteria, how do you make a diagnosis of rheumatic fever?
Show Answer

Answer:

Diagnosis requires evidence of a preceding Group A Streptococcal infection (positive throat culture, rapid antigen test, or elevated ASOT titers) PLUS:

  • 2 Major Criteria OR
  • 1 Major and 2 Minor Criteria.

Major Criteria (CASES):

  • Carditis (Pancarditis)
  • Arthritis (Migratory polyarthritis)
  • Subcutaneous Nodules
  • Erythema Marginatum
  • Sydenham's Chorea

Minor Criteria:

  • Clinical: Fever, Arthralgia
  • Lab: Elevated ESR or CRP
  • ECG: Prolonged PR interval
2.
Discuss DVT in relation to Virchow triad.
Show Answer

Answer:

Virchow's Triad describes the three primary factors contributing to thrombosis (DVT):

  1. Endothelial Injury: Damage to the vessel wall (e.g., from trauma, surgery, or hypertension) exposes the subendothelial collagen, triggering platelet adhesion and the clotting cascade.
  2. Stasis or Turbulent Blood Flow: Slow blood flow (stasis) prevents dilution of activated clotting factors and clearance by the liver. Commonly seen in bedridden patients, long flights, or heart failure. Turbulence contributes to endothelial injury.
  3. Hypercoagulability: An increased tendency of blood to clot. This can be primary (genetic, e.g., Factor V Leiden) or secondary (acquired, e.g., cancer, oral contraceptives, pregnancy, smoking).
3.
Differentiate the 3 clinical patterns of angina pectoris.
Show Answer
  • Stable Angina: Predictable chest pain triggered by exertion/stress. Caused by fixed atherosclerosis (>70% stenosis). Relieved by rest or nitroglycerin.
  • Unstable Angina: Unpredictable, occurs at rest or with minimal exertion. Increasing frequency/severity (crescendo). Caused by plaque rupture/thrombosis. High risk of MI.
  • Prinzmetal's (Variant) Angina: Occurs at rest (often at night). Caused by coronary artery vasospasm. Transient ST elevation on ECG. Responds to calcium channel blockers.
4.
Define aneurysm and outline the classification of aneurysm.
Show Answer

Definition: An aneurysm is a localized abnormal dilation of a blood vessel or the heart.

Classification:

  • Based on Morphology (Shape):
    • Saccular (Berry): Spherical outpouching involving only a portion of the vessel wall (common in Circle of Willis).
    • Fusiform: Diffuse, circumferential dilation of a long vascular segment (common in Abdominal Aortic Aneurysm).
  • Based on Integrity of Wall (Functional):
    • True Aneurysm: The wall of the sac consists of all three layers of the artery (intima, media, adventitia). Examples: Atherosclerotic, Syphilitic.
    • False Aneurysm (Pseudoaneurysm): A breach in the vessel wall leads to an extravascular hematoma that communicates with the intravascular space ("pulsating hematoma"). The wall is formed by connective tissue, not vessel layers. Example: Post-traumatic.

Set 2: Voi Campus (Source: Issa)

1.
Highlight the modifiable and non-modifiable risk factors for atherosclerosis.
Show Answer

Non-Modifiable (Constitutional) Factors:

  • Age: Risk increases with age.
  • Gender: Men are at higher risk; women are protected until menopause.
  • Genetics/Family History: Familial hypercholesterolemia, family history of MI.

Modifiable (Acquired) Factors:

  • Hyperlipidemia: High LDL, Low HDL.
  • Hypertension: Damages endothelium.
  • Cigarette Smoking: Major risk factor.
  • Diabetes Mellitus: Induces hypercholesterolemia.
  • Inflammation: Elevated CRP.
2.
List 5 differences between transmural MI and Subendocardial MI.
Show Answer
Feature Transmural MI (STEMI) Subendocardial MI (NSTEMI)
Extent Involves full thickness of ventricular wall. Limited to inner 1/3 to 1/2 of wall.
Vessel Occlusion Usually complete, persistent occlusion. Often partial or transient occlusion.
ECG ST-segment Elevation, Q-waves. ST-segment Depression, No Q-waves.
Cause Plaque rupture + global thrombosis. Plaque disruption + lysis, or hypotension.
Complications Higher risk of rupture, aneurysm. Lower risk of rupture.
3.
Highlight the 4 features of Tetralogy of Fallot.
Show Answer

Answer: (Mnemonic: PROV)

  1. Pulmonary Stenosis (Obstruction of right ventricular outflow).
  2. Right Ventricular Hypertrophy (Result of high pressure pumping against stenosis).
  3. Overriding Aorta (Aorta sits over the VSD, receiving blood from both ventricles).
  4. Ventricular Septal Defect (VSD).
4.
List ten complications of Myocardial Infarction.
Show Answer

Answer: (Mnemonic: DARTH VADER)

  1. Death (Sudden cardiac death due to arrhythmias).
  2. Arrhythmias (VF, VT, Heart block).
  3. Rupture (Free wall rupture -> Tamponade; Septal rupture -> VSD; Papillary muscle rupture -> Mitral regurgitation).
  4. Tamponade (Cardiac).
  5. Heart Failure (Acute pulmonary edema).
  6. Valve Disease (Acute mitral regurgitation).
  7. Aneurysm (Ventricular aneurysm).
  8. Dressler's Syndrome (Autoimmune pericarditis weeks later).
  9. Embolism (Mural thrombus -> Stroke).
  10. Recurrence (Re-infarction).
  11. Shock (Cardiogenic).

Set 3: Kapkatet Campus (Source: Macruf)

1.
Outline the syndromes of ischemic heart disease.
Show Answer

Answer:

Ischemic Heart Disease (IHD) typically presents as one of four syndromes:

  1. Angina Pectoris: Transient ischemia without cell death (Stable, Unstable, Prinzmetal).
  2. Acute Myocardial Infarction (MI): Ischemia sufficient to cause myocardial necrosis.
  3. Chronic Ischemic Heart Disease with Heart Failure: Progressive heart failure due to accumulated ischemic damage/scarring.
  4. Sudden Cardiac Death: Lethal arrhythmia (usually VF) induced by ischemia.
2.
Which is the most commonly involved coronary artery in myocardial infarction?
Show Answer

Answer: Left Anterior Descending (LAD) artery.

Distribution:

  • LAD (40-50%): Affects anterior wall of LV and anterior septum ("Widow maker").
  • RCA (30-40%): Affects posterior wall, posterior septum, and right ventricle.
  • LCX (15-20%): Affects lateral wall of LV.
3.
Libman-Sacks endocarditis is seen in which condition?
Show Answer

Answer: Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE).

Characteristics: Sterile (non-infective) vegetations found on both sides (upper and lower surfaces) of the valve leaflets, cords, and endocardium.

4.
Outline classical signs of cardiac tamponade.
Show Answer

Answer: Beck's Triad

  1. Hypotension: Low blood pressure (due to reduced cardiac output).
  2. Distended Neck Veins (JVD): Increased jugular venous pressure (impaired diastolic filling).
  3. Muffled Heart Sounds: Fluid in pericardial sac insulates sound.

Other sign: Pulsus Paradoxus (drop in SBP >10mmHg during inspiration).
