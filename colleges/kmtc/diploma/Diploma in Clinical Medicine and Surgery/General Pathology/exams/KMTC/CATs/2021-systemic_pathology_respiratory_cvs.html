Paper A

Section 2: Short Answer Questions (40 Marks)

1.

a) Define Hypertension. (5 Marks)

b) Explain the causes of hypertension. (5 Marks)

Show Answer

a) Definition:

Hypertension is defined as a sustained elevation of systemic arterial blood pressure, typically resting systolic blood pressure ≥140 mmHg and/or diastolic blood pressure ≥90 mmHg.

b) Causes:

  • Primary (Essential) Hypertension (90-95%): Idiopathic (unknown cause), multifactorial involving genetics, salt intake, stress, and obesity.
  • Secondary Hypertension (5-10%):
    • Renal: Chronic kidney disease, Renal artery stenosis, Glomerulonephritis.
    • Endocrine: Cushing's syndrome, Pheochromocytoma, Hyperaldosteronism (Conn's).
    • Cardiovascular: Coarctation of the aorta.
    • Neurologic: Increased ICP, Sleep apnea.
2.

a) Regarding acute rheumatic fever, define it. (5 Marks)

b) Explain how you would make a diagnosis. (5 Marks)

Show Answer

a) Definition:

Acute Rheumatic Fever is a systemic, post-infectious inflammatory disease that occurs 2-4 weeks after a Group A Beta-Hemolytic Streptococcal (GAS) pharyngitis. It is an autoimmune reaction mediated by molecular mimicry, affecting the heart, joints, skin, and brain.

b) Diagnosis (Jones Criteria):

Diagnosis requires evidence of preceding GAS infection (Throat culture, Rapid antigen test, or Rising ASOT) PLUS:

  • 2 Major Criteria OR
  • 1 Major + 2 Minor Criteria.

Major Criteria: Carditis, Polyarthritis, Chorea, Erythema Marginatum, Subcutaneous Nodules.

Minor Criteria: Fever, Arthralgia, Elevated ESR/CRP, Prolonged PR interval.

3.

a) State the pathophysiology of Pulmonary tuberculosis. (5 Marks)

b) How you would make a diagnosis of pulmonary tuberculosis. (5 Marks)

Show Answer

a) Pathophysiology:

  1. Inhalation: Droplets containing M. tuberculosis reach alveoli.
  2. Phagocytosis: Macrophages engulf bacilli but cannot kill them (due to cell wall mycolic acids); bacteria multiply.
  3. Sensitization (2-4 weeks): T-cell mediated immune response develops (Type IV hypersensitivity).
  4. Granuloma Formation: Macrophages differentiate into epithelioid cells and Langhans giant cells to wall off infection, forming a Ghon Focus. Central caseous necrosis occurs.
  5. Ghon Complex: Ghon focus + hilar lymphadenopathy.
  6. Outcome: Usually heals with calcification (Latent TB). If immunity fails, reactivation leads to Secondary TB (cavitation).

b) Diagnosis:

  • Sputum Smear Microscopy: Ziehl-Neelsen staining for Acid Fast Bacilli (AFB).
  • GeneXpert (CB-NAAT): Detects MTB DNA and Rifampicin resistance (Gold standard).
  • Chest X-Ray: Upper lobe infiltrates, cavitation, hilar lymphadenopathy.
  • Mantoux Test / IGRA: Evidence of exposure/infection.
4.

a) Compare and contrast lobar and broncho pneumonia. (5 Marks)

b) State the causes of pneumonia. (5 Marks)

Show Answer

a) Comparison:

Feature Lobar Pneumonia Bronchopneumonia
Pattern Diffuse, affects entire lobe. Patchy, multifocal consolidation.
Spread Rapid spread via Pores of Kohn. Extension from bronchioles to alveoli.
Common Agent Strep. pneumoniae (95%). Staph. aureus, Gram negatives (Klebsiella).
Population Healthy young adults. Infants, elderly, debilitated.
Stages 4 distinct stages (Congestion, Hepatization, etc.) No distinct stages.

b) Causes:

  • Bacterial: S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae, S. aureus, Mycoplasma.
  • Viral: Influenza virus, RSV, Adenovirus, SARS-CoV-2.
  • Fungal: Pneumocystis jirovecii (in HIV), Histoplasmosis.
  • Aspiration: Gastric contents, chemical pneumonitis.

Section 3: Long Answer Question (40 Marks)

1.
About cardiac failure outline the cardinal signs, Adaptive mechanisms of the heart, causes of cardiac failure and pathophysiology. (20 Marks)
Show Answer

Cardinal Signs: Tachycardia, Edema (pedal/sacral), Dyspnea (exertional/PND), Cardiomegaly, Raised JVP.

Adaptive Mechanisms:

  • Frank-Starling Law: Dilation increases sarcomere length to increase contractility.
  • Myocardial Hypertrophy: Thickening of muscle to reduce wall stress and increase force.
  • Neurohumoral Activation:
    • Sympathetic NS (Increase HR, contractility).
    • RAAS activation (Fluid retention to maintain BP).

Pathophysiology:

Failure leads to Forward Failure (decreased cardiac output -> fatigue, renal hypoperfusion) and Backward Failure (venous congestion -> pulmonary edema in LHF, systemic edema in RHF). The adaptive mechanisms eventually become maladaptive, increasing cardiac work and remodeling, leading to decompensation.

2.
Concerning infective endocarditis, elaborate the causes, state types, the predisposing factors and investigations. (20 Marks)
Show Answer

Definition: Microbial infection of the heart valves or endocardium leading to vegetation formation.

Types & Causes:

  • Acute Bacterial Endocarditis: High virulence organism (Staphylococcus aureus) attacking normal valves. Rapidly destructive.
  • Subacute Bacterial Endocarditis (SBE): Low virulence organism (Viridans Streptococci) attacking damaged valves. Indolent course.
  • IV Drug Users: Often affects Right Heart (Tricuspid) - S. aureus, Pseudomonas.
  • Prosthetic Valve: Staph epidermidis.

Predisposing Factors:

  • Rheumatic heart disease (scarred valves).
  • Congenital heart defects (VSD, bicuspid aortic valve).
  • Prosthetic heart valves.
  • Intravenous drug use.
  • Indwelling catheters/procedures.

Investigations:

  • Blood Culture: Determine organism (3 sets required).
  • Echocardiography (TTE/TEE): Visualize vegetations and valve damage.
  • CBC: Leukocytosis, Anemia.
  • ESR/CRP: Elevated.
  • Urinalysis: Microscopic hematuria (emboli).

Paper B

Section C: Short Answer Questions (40 Marks)

1.
Outline the effects of hypertension on the following organs:
a) Kidneys
b) Heart (10 Marks)
Show Answer

a) Kidneys (Benign Nephrosclerosis):

  • Hyaline Arteriolosclerosis: Thickening of arteriolar walls, narrowing lumen.
  • Ischemia: Reduced blood flow leads to tubular atrophy and interstitial fibrosis.
  • Gross Appearance: Granular surface (grain leather kidney) and reduced size.
  • Outcome: Chronic renal failure (Proteinuria, elevated creatinine).

b) Heart (Hypertensive Heart Disease):

  • Pressure Overload: Increased afterload forces LV to work harder.
  • Left Ventricular Hypertrophy (LVH): Concentric thickening of LV wall (box-car nuclei microscopically).
  • Dilation: Eventually, the muscle fails and dilates (decompensation).
  • Ischemia: Increased muscle mass outgrows blood supply -> Angina/MI.

3.
Explain how stable angina differs from unstable angina (10 Marks)
Show Answer

See Paper A, Section 1, Question 3 (similar concept). Brief summary:

  • Stable Angina: Triggered by exertion, relieved by rest/nitro. Caused by fixed plaque. Lower risk.
  • Unstable Angina: Occurs at rest or changing pattern. Poor relief. Caused by plaque rupture + thrombosis. High risk (Pre-infarction).

Section D: Long Answer Questions (40 Marks)

1.
Regarding atelectasis:
a) Describe types using diagrams (diagrams described in text)
b) List 4 causes in each type (12 Marks)
Show Answer

Types of Atelectasis (Lung Collapse):

1. Resorption (Obstructive) Atelectasis:

  • Mechanism: Complete obstruction of an airway prevents air from reaching alveoli. Existing air is absorbed into blood, and alveoli collapse. Mediastinum shifts towards the affected side.
  • Causes: Mucus plugs (asthma, post-op), Foreign body aspiration, Bronchial tumor, Enlarged lymph nodes compressing bronchus.

2. Compression (Passive) Atelectasis:

  • Mechanism: Fluid, blood, or air accumulates in the pleural cavity, mechanically compressing the lung. Mediastinum shifts away from affected side.
  • Causes: Pleural effusion (CHF), Pneumothorax, Hemothorax, Empyema.

3. Contraction (Cicatrization) Atelectasis:

  • Mechanism: Local or generalized fibrosis impairs lung expansion and elastic recoil. Irreversible.
  • Causes: Tuberculosis fibrosis, Pulmonary fibrosis (IPF), Scarring post-pneumonia, Radiation pneumonitis.
2.
Concerning ischemic heart disease:
a) Define
b) Describe syndrome presentations
c) How would you diagnose pathologically (10 Marks)
Show Answer

a) Definition: A group of pathophysiologically related syndromes resulting from myocardial ischemia (imbalance between myocardial oxygen supply and demand), usually caused by atherosclerotic narrowing of coronary arteries.

b) Syndrome Presentations:

  • Angina Pectoris: Chest pain due to transient reversible ischemia.
  • Acute Myocardial Infarction: Ischemia causing frank cardiac muscle necrosis.
  • Chronic IHD with Heart Failure: Progressive decompensation due to accumulated damage.
  • Sudden Cardiac Death: Fatal arrhythmia arising from ischemia.

c) Pathological Diagnosis (Post-Mortem/Clinical):

  • Gross (Autopsy): 12-24h (Pallor/mottling), 3-7 days (Yellow-tan center with hyperemic border), >2 months (White fibrous scar).
  • Microscopic: Coagulative necrosis (loss of nuclei), neutrophilic infiltration (1-3 days), macrophage cleanup (3-7 days), granulation tissue, collagen scar.
  • Clinical Pathology (Labs): Elevated Cardiac Troponins (I or T) and CK-MB.
