Section 2: Short Answer Questions

1.

a. Define heart failure (1 mark)

b. Outline four main results of backward failure of the heart (4 marks)

Show Answer

a. Definition of Heart Failure:

Heart failure is a clinical syndrome where the heart is unable to pump blood at a rate sufficient to meet the metabolic requirements of the tissues, or can do so only at an elevated filling pressure.

b. Main Results of Backward Failure (Congestion):

Backward failure refers to the buildup of pressure behind the failing ventricle.

  1. Pulmonary Edema/Congestion: (In Left Heart Failure) Back pressure into the lungs causes fluid leakage into alveoli, leading to dyspnea and orthopnea.
  2. Peripheral Edema: (In Right Heart Failure) Increased hydrostatic pressure in systemic veins causes swelling in dependent areas (legs, ankles).
  3. Hepatomegaly (Nutmeg Liver): Congestion of the liver due to raised systemic venous pressure.
  4. Ascites: Accumulation of fluid in the peritoneal cavity due to portal hypertension from hepatic congestion.
  5. Raised Jugular Venous Pressure (JVP): Visible distension of neck veins.
2.

a. Cardiac hypertrophy in a patient with heart failure is a feature commonly expressed as cardiomegaly on X-ray. List one positive and one negative consequence of hypertrophy. (2 marks)

b. Explain how the forward heart failure effects of cyanosis and easy fatigability come about in a patient with heart failure (Pathogenesis) (3 marks)

Show Answer

a. Consequences of Hypertrophy:

  • Positive Consequence: Increased force of contraction allowing the heart to maintain cardiac output against an increased load (pressure or volume) temporarily.
  • Negative Consequence: The increased muscle mass has higher oxygen requirements which the vasculature may not meet, leading to ischemia, fibrosis, and eventual pump failure.

b. Pathogenesis of Forward Failure Effects:

Forward failure is defined by reduced cardiac output (inadequate perfusion).

  • Easy Fatigability: Reduced cardiac output means insufficient blood reaches skeletal muscles. The muscles are deprived of adequate oxygen and nutrients required for energy (ATP) production during exertion, leading to early anaerobic metabolism and fatigue.
  • Cyanosis: Reduced perfusion leads to sluggish blood flow in the periphery. This allows tissues to extract more oxygen than usual from the passing blood, resulting in a higher concentration of deoxygenated hemoglobin (>5g/dL) in the capillaries, appearing as a bluish discoloration (Peripheral Cyanosis).

1. a)
Outline the classification of congenital heart disease (10 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

Congenital Heart Diseases (CHD) are classified based on hemodynamics and the presence of cyanosis:

1. Acyanotic Congenital Heart Diseases (Left-to-Right Shunts):

  • Blood flows from the high-pressure systemic side (left) to the low-pressure pulmonary side (right). No deoxygenated blood enters systemic circulation initially.
  • Atrial Septal Defect (ASD): Hole between atria.
  • Ventricular Septal Defect (VSD): Hole between ventricles (most common).
  • Patent Ductus Arteriosus (PDA): Persistence of fetal connection between aorta and pulmonary artery.

2. Cyanotic Congenital Heart Diseases (Right-to-Left Shunts):

  • Blood bypasses the lungs, moving from the right side directly to the left, causing systemic hypoxia and cyanosis.
  • Tetralogy of Fallot (TOF): Most common cyanotic CHD (VSD, Overriding Aorta, Pulmonary Stenosis, RV Hypertrophy).
  • Transposition of Great Arteries (TGA): Aorta arises from RV and Pulmonary Artery from LV.
  • Truncus Arteriosus: Single vessel for both outputs.

3. Obstructive Congenital Heart Diseases:

  • Obstruction to blood flow without shunting (usually acyanotic unless severe).
  • Coarctation of the Aorta: Narrowing of the aorta.
  • Pulmonary Stenosis / Atresia.
  • Aortic Stenosis / Atresia.
1. b)
Write short notes on rheumatic endocarditis under the following topics:
  • Cause (1 mark)
  • Consequences (5 marks)
  • Pancarditis (4 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

1. Cause (Etiology):

Rheumatic endocarditis is a sequel to infection by Group A Beta-Hemolytic Streptococcus (pharyngitis). It is an autoimmune reaction where antibodies produced against streptococcal M-proteins cross-react with host cardiac antigens (molecular mimicry).

2. Consequences:

  • Valvular Stenosis: Chronic inflammation and fibrosis lead to leaflet thickening and fusion (e.g., "fish-mouth" deformity in mitral stenosis), obstructing blood flow.
  • Valvular Regurgitation: Scarring causes retraction of leaflets, preventing proper closure and allowing backflow.
  • Thromboembolism: Damaged valves and dilated atria (due to stenosis) predispose to thrombus formation, which can embolize to the brain (stroke) or other organs.
  • Infective Endocarditis: Damaged valves are sites for bacterial colonization during bacteremia.
  • Heart Failure: Chronic valvular dysfunction increases hemodynamic load, leading to myocardial hypertrophy and eventual failure.

3. Pancarditis:

Rheumatic fever characteristically affects all three layers of the heart, termed pancarditis:

  • Endocarditis: Inflammation of the inner lining and valves, forming small vegetations (verrucae) along lines of closure.
  • Myocarditis: Inflammation of the heart muscle containing characteristic Aschoff bodies (granulomas). This is the most common cause of death in the acute phase.
  • Pericarditis: Inflammation of the pericardial sac, often resulting in a "bread-and-butter" fibrinous exudate.
