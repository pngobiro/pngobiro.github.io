Part 1: Proteins & Biomolecules

1.
Explain the classification of proteins based on function (20 marks)
Show Answer

Proteins are classified by function into the following categories:

  1. Catalytic Proteins (Enzymes): Speed up biochemical reactions.
    • Examples: Amylase (digests starch), DNA Polymerase (replicates DNA), Hexokinase (glycolysis).
  2. Structural Proteins: Provide mechanical support and shape to cells and tissues.
    • Examples: Collagen (connective tissue), Keratin (hair/nails), Elastin (ligaments).
  3. Transport Proteins: Carry substances in the blood or across cell membranes.
    • Examples: Hemoglobin (oxygen), Albumin (fatty acids/drugs), GLUT transporters (glucose).
  4. Defense Proteins (Immunoglobulins): Protect the body against foreign pathogens.
    • Examples: Antibodies (IgG, IgM), Fibrinogen (blood clotting).
  5. Regulatory/Hormonal Proteins: Coordinate physiological activities.
    • Examples: Insulin (blood sugar regulation), Glucagon, Growth Hormone.
  6. Contractile Proteins: Facilitate movement in muscles and cells.
    • Examples: Actin and Myosin.
  7. Storage Proteins: Store nutrients/amino acids.
    • Examples: Ferritin (iron storage), Casein (milk protein).
  8. Receptor Proteins: Detect chemical signals and transmit them into the cell.
    • Examples: Insulin receptors, Neurotransmitter receptors.
2.

a. Discuss the importance of amino acids (10 marks)

b. Discuss the classification of amino acids (10 marks)

Show Answer

a. Importance of Amino Acids:

  • Protein Synthesis: They are the monomers (building blocks) for all proteins/enzymes.
  • Energy Source: Carbon skeletons can be oxidized to produce ATP (glucogenic/ketogenic).
  • Precursors for Specialized Molecules:
    • Tyrosine -> Catecholamines (Dopamine, Epinephrine), Melanin, Thyroid hormones.
    • Tryptophan -> Serotonin, Niacin, Melatonin.
    • Glycine -> Heme, Purines.
    • Histidine -> Histamine.
  • Neurotransmitters: Glutamate and Glycine act directly as neurotransmitters.
  • Nitrogen Balance: Essential for transporting nitrogen (e.g., Glutamine, Alanine) between tissues.

b. Classification of Amino Acids:

  1. Based on Nutritional Requirement:
    • Essential: Cannot be synthesized by the body; must be in diet (PVT TIM HALL - Phenylalanine, Valine, Threonine, Tryptophan, Isoleucine, Methionine, Histidine, Arginine, Leucine, Lysine).
    • Non-essential: Body can synthesize them (e.g., Alanine, Glycine).
  2. Based on R-Group (Side Chain) Properties:
    • Non-polar (Hydrophobic): Glycine, Alanine, Valine, Leucine, Isoleucine, Methionine, Proline, Phenylalanine, Tryptophan.
    • Polar Uncharged: Serine, Threonine, Cysteine, Asparagine, Glutamine, Tyrosine.
    • Acidic (Negatively charged): Aspartate, Glutamate.
    • Basic (Positively charged): Lysine, Arginine, Histidine.
  3. Based on Metabolic Fate:
    • Glucogenic: Can form glucose (e.g., Alanine).
    • Ketogenic: Can form ketone bodies (Leucine, Lysine).
    • Both: Isoleucine, Phenylalanine, Tryptophan, Tyrosine.

4.
Explain the following biochemical reactions (6 marks)
Show Answer
  • a) Nucleophilic reaction: A reaction where an electron-rich species (nucleophile) attacks an electron-deficient species (electrophile) to form a bond. (e.g., formation of peptide bond).
  • b) Elimination reaction: Removal of atoms or groups from a molecule, typically resulting in the formation of a double bond (e.g., dehydration of 2-phosphoglycerate to PEP).
  • c) Addition reaction: Two or more molecules combine to form a larger one (e.g., hydrogenation of unsaturated fats).
  • d) Isomerization reaction: Rearrangement of atoms within a molecule to form an isomer with the same chemical formula but different structure (e.g., Glucose-6-P to Fructose-6-P).
  • e) Oxidation reaction: Loss of electrons or hydrogen, or gain of oxygen. Often releases energy (e.g., conversion of lactate to pyruvate).
  • f) Hydrolysis reaction: Breaking of a chemical bond using a water molecule (e.g., digestion of starch into glucose).
5.
Classify vitamins (20 marks)
Show Answer

Vitamins are classified based on their solubility:

1. Fat-Soluble Vitamins (A, D, E, K):

  • Soluble in lipids, require fat for absorption.
  • Stored in the liver and adipose tissue (can cause toxicity if excess).
  • Vitamin A (Retinol): Vision (rhodopsin), epithelial maintenance. Deficiency: Night blindness.
  • Vitamin D (Calciferol): Calcium absorption, bone health. Deficiency: Rickets/Osteomalacia.
  • Vitamin E (Tocopherol): Antioxidant, protects cell membranes. Deficiency: Hemolytic anemia.
  • Vitamin K (Phylloquinone): Blood clotting (Factors II, VII, IX, X). Deficiency: Bleeding disorders.

2. Water-Soluble Vitamins (B-Complex and C):

  • Soluble in water, excess excreted in urine (rarely toxic).
  • Act primarily as coenzymes.
  • Vitamin C (Ascorbic Acid): Collagen synthesis, antioxidant. Deficiency: Scurvy.
  • Vitamin B1 (Thiamine): Decarboxylation reactions. Deficiency: Beriberi.
  • Vitamin B2 (Riboflavin): FAD precursor (redox). Deficiency: Cheilosis/Glossitis.
  • Vitamin B3 (Niacin): NAD precursor. Deficiency: Pellagra (4Ds).
  • Vitamin B5 (Pantothenic Acid): Component of Coenzyme A.
  • Vitamin B6 (Pyridoxine): Amino acid metabolism.
  • Vitamin B7 (Biotin): Carboxylation reactions.
  • Vitamin B9 (Folate): DNA synthesis/One-carbon metabolism. Deficiency: Megaloblastic anemia.
  • Vitamin B12 (Cobalamin): DNA synthesis, nerve function. Deficiency: Pernicious anemia.

Part 2: Carbohydrates & Water

6.
Classify carbohydrates (20 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Monosaccharides (Simple Sugars): Cannot be hydrolyzed further.
    • Trioses (3C): Glyceraldehyde.
    • Pentoses (5C): Ribose, Deoxyribose.
    • Hexoses (6C): Glucose (blood sugar), Fructose (fruit sugar), Galactose.
  2. Disaccharides: Two monosaccharides joined by a glycosidic bond.
    • Maltose: Glucose + Glucose ($\alpha$-1,4 bond). Found in malt.
    • Lactose: Glucose + Galactose ($\beta$-1,4 bond). Found in milk.
    • Sucrose: Glucose + Fructose ($\alpha$-1,2 bond). Table sugar.
  3. Oligosaccharides: Short chains of 3-10 monosaccharides.
    • Found on cell membranes (glycoproteins/glycolipids) for cell recognition.
    • Examples: Raffinose, Stachyose.
  4. Polysaccharides (Glycans): Long chains of >10 units.
    • Homopolysaccharides: Same monomer unit.
      • Storage: Starch (plants - amylose/amylopectin), Glycogen (animals - highly branched).
      • Structural: Cellulose (plants - $\beta$-1,4 bonds), Chitin (fungi/insects).
    • Heteropolysaccharides: Different monomer units.
      • Glycosaminoglycans (GAGs): Heparin, Hyaluronic acid, Chondroitin sulfate (ECM components).
7.
Explain the importance of studying biochemistry (10 marks)
Show Answer
  • Understanding Physiology: Explains bodily functions at the molecular level (e.g., muscle contraction, nerve impulse).
  • Diagnosis of Disease: Biochemical markers (enzymes, hormones, metabolites) are used to diagnose conditions (e.g., high blood sugar for diabetes, troponins for heart attack).
  • Drug Discovery/Pharmacology: Helps design drugs that target specific enzymes or receptors.
  • Nutrition: Determines dietary requirements for vitamins, minerals, and macronutrients.
  • Genetics: Understanding DNA/RNA helps in diagnosing and treating genetic disorders.
  • Pathology: Explains the molecular basis of diseases (e.g., sickle cell anemia is a single amino acid substitution).
8.
State 5 properties of water (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Polarity/Dipole Nature: Acts as a universal solvent for polar substances.
  2. High Specific Heat Capacity: Resists temperature changes, stabilizing body temperature.
  3. High Heat of Vaporization: Allows for cooling via sweating.
  4. Cohesion and Adhesion: Allows water transport (e.g., blood flow).
  5. Density Anomaly: Ice is less dense than liquid water (important for aquatic life, less relevant clinically).
  6. High Surface Tension.

10.
Explain the following terms (10 marks)
Show Answer
  • a. Covalent bond: Strong chemical bond formed by the sharing of electron pairs between atoms (e.g., peptide bonds, phosphodiester bonds).
  • b. Hydrogen bond: Weak electrostatic attraction between a hydrogen atom covalently bonded to an electronegative atom (N, O) and another electronegative atom. Critical for DNA base pairing and protein secondary structure.
  • c. Lipid: A diverse group of hydrophobic organic molecules, including fats, oils, waxes, and steroids, that are insoluble in water but soluble in organic solvents.
  • d. Biochemistry: The branch of science that explores the chemical processes and substances within and related to living organisms.
  • e. Secondary structure of a protein: Local folding of the polypeptide chain into regular structures like Alpha-helices and Beta-pleated sheets, stabilized by hydrogen bonds between the backbone amide and carbonyl groups.

Explain:

a. The reactions of amino acids (5 marks)

b. Metabolism of amino acids (5 marks)

c. The derivatives of amino acids and their importance (10 marks)

Show Answer

a. Reactions of Amino Acids:

  • Transamination: Transfer of amino group to a keto acid (catalyzed by ALT/AST).
  • Deamination: Removal of amino group as ammonia.
  • Decarboxylation: Removal of carboxyl group to form amines (e.g., Histidine -> Histamine).
  • Peptide bond formation: Reaction between carboxyl group of one and amino group of another.

b. Metabolism of Amino Acids:

  • Involves removing the amino group (to Urea Cycle in liver) and utilizing the Carbon Skeleton.
  • Carbon skeletons enter the Krebs cycle or Glycolysis as intermediates (Pyruvate, Acetyl-CoA, alpha-ketoglutarate, etc.).
  • Classified as Glucogenic (make glucose) or Ketogenic (make ketone bodies).

c. Derivatives & Importance:

  • Histamine (from Histidine): Vasodilator, allergic response mediator.
  • Serotonin (from Tryptophan): Mood regulation, sleep, appetite.
  • Catecholamines (Dopamine, Epinephrine from Tyrosine): Neurotransmitters, "Fight or flight" response.
  • GABA (from Glutamate): Inhibitory neurotransmitter.
  • Melanin (from Tyrosine): Skin pigmentation.
  • Nitric Oxide (from Arginine): Vasodilation.
  • Thyroid Hormones (from Tyrosine): Metabolism regulation.

Proteins can be classified based on Shape, Solubility, or Composition:

1. Based on Shape/Solubility:

  • Fibrous Proteins: Long, rod-like, insoluble. Structural function. (e.g., Collagen, Keratin, Elastin).
  • Globular Proteins: Spherical, soluble. Functional roles. (e.g., Hemoglobin, Enzymes, Immunoglobulins).

2. Based on Composition:

  • Simple Proteins: Only amino acids upon hydrolysis (e.g., Albumin).
  • Conjugated Proteins: Protein + Non-protein group (Prosthetic group).
    • Glycoproteins: + Carbohydrate (antibodies).
    • Lipoproteins: + Lipid (LDL/HDL).
    • Hemoproteins: + Heme (Hemoglobin).
    • Phosphoproteins: + Phosphate (Casein).
    • Nucleoproteins: + Nucleic acid (Histones).

15.
Explain the process of protein synthesis (10 marks)
Show Answer

Protein synthesis occurs in two main stages: Transcription and Translation.

  1. Transcription (in Nucleus):
    • DNA unwinds. RNA Polymerase binds to the promoter region.
    • A complementary mRNA strand is synthesized from the DNA template (using U instead of T).
    • mRNA is processed (capping, tailing, splicing) and moves to the cytoplasm.
  2. Translation (in Cytoplasm at Ribosome):
    • Initiation: Ribosome assembles around mRNA. The first tRNA carrying Methionine binds to the start codon (AUG).
    • Elongation: Ribosome reads mRNA codons. tRNA molecules bring specific amino acids matching the codons. Peptide bonds form between amino acids.
    • Termination: Ribosome reaches a stop codon (UAA, UAG, UGA). Release factor binds, and the polypeptide chain is released.
  3. Post-translational modification: Folding and chemical modification of the protein.

Part 4: Lipids & Vitamins

16.
State 5 functions of lipids (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Energy Storage: Triglycerides in adipose tissue provide long-term energy.
  2. Structural Component: Phospholipids and Cholesterol form the cell membrane bilayer.
  3. Insulation: Subcutaneous fat prevents heat loss.
  4. Protection: Fat pads protect internal organs (kidneys, heart).
  5. Hormone Synthesis: Steroid hormones (Cortisol, Estrogen, Testosterone) derived from cholesterol.
  6. Vitamin Absorption: Required for absorbing fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K).
17.
Classify lipids (20 marks)
Show Answer

Lipids are classified into three main groups:

  1. Simple Lipids: Esters of fatty acids with alcohol.
    • Fats/Oils: Esters of fatty acids + Glycerol (Triglycerides).
    • Waxes: Esters of fatty acids + high MW alcohol.
  2. Compound (Complex) Lipids: Esters of fatty acids + alcohol + additional group.
    • Phospholipids: Contain phosphoric acid (e.g., Lecithin, Cephalin). Main membrane component.
    • Glycolipids: Contain carbohydrate + nitrogen base (e.g., Cerebrosides, Gangliosides). Nerve tissue.
    • Lipoproteins: Lipid + Protein complex (Chylomicrons, LDL, HDL). Transport.
  3. Derived Lipids: Produced by hydrolysis of simple/compound lipids or related structures.
    • Steroids: Cholesterol, Bile acids, Steroid hormones.
    • Terpenes: Vitamin A, E, K.
    • Eicosanoids: Prostaglandins, Leukotrienes (signaling).
    • Fatty Acids: Saturated vs Unsaturated.
18.
Enumerate the functions of carbohydrates (10 marks)
Show Answer
  • Primary Energy Source: Glucose is the main fuel for brain and RBCs.
  • Energy Storage: Glycogen (liver/muscle) in animals; Starch in plants.
  • Structural Role: Cellulose in plants; Chitin in insects; Glycosaminoglycans in connective tissue (cartilage).
  • Sparing Protein: Prevents breakdown of muscle protein for energy.
  • Preventing Ketosis: Necessary for complete lipid oxidation.
  • Cell Recognition/Signaling: Glycoproteins and glycolipids on cell surfaces act as ID tags.
  • Dietary Fiber: Cellulose promotes bowel health.
19.
Explain the properties of vitamins (10 marks)
Show Answer
  • Organic Nature: They are organic compounds (contain carbon).
  • Micronutrients: Required in very small amounts (mg or micrograms).
  • Essentiality: Most cannot be synthesized by the body in sufficient quantities and must be obtained from diet (except Vit D, K, B7, B3 somewhat).
  • Specificity: Deficiency of a specific vitamin causes a specific disease syndrome.
  • Catalytic Role: Many act as coenzymes or cofactors in metabolic reactions (especially B vitamins).
  • Sensitivity: Many are destroyed by heat, light, or oxidation (cooking loss).
  • Non-caloric: Do not provide energy directly, but help extract energy from food.
