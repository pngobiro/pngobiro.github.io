Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions

1.
Which of the following explains why glucose-6-phosphate is not transported out of most cells?
A. It is too large to pass through membrane pores
B. It is rapidly converted to glycogen
C. It lacks a specific transporter and is ionically trapped
D. It inhibits the glucose transporter
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. It lacks a specific transporter and is ionically trapped

Explanation: Phosphorylation of glucose to glucose-6-phosphate adds a negatively charged phosphate group. Since the cell membrane is hydrophobic and lacks specific transporters for G6P, the molecule is effectively "trapped" inside the cell to undergo metabolism.

2.
During anaerobic glycolysis in muscle, the main reason for lactate production is to:
A. Prevent accumulation of pyruvate
B. Recycle NADH to NAD+ for glycolysis to continue
C. Provide ATP via oxidative phosphorylation
D. Enhance mitochondrial activity
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Recycle NADH to NAD+ for glycolysis to continue

Explanation: Glycolysis requires NAD+ as a cofactor at the Glyceraldehyde-3-Phosphate Dehydrogenase step. Under anaerobic conditions, the ETC cannot regenerate NAD+. Lactate dehydrogenase reduces pyruvate to lactate, oxidizing NADH back to NAD+, allowing glycolysis to generate ATP.

3.
A mutation in the pyruvate dehydrogenase complex impairs aerobic respiration. Which compensatory metabolic shift is likely to occur?
A. Increased fatty acid synthesis
B. Increased gluconeogenesis
C. Increased anaerobic glycolysis
D. Increased pentose phosphate pathway activity
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Increased anaerobic glycolysis

Explanation: The Pyruvate Dehydrogenase (PDH) complex converts pyruvate to Acetyl-CoA for the Krebs cycle. If PDH is impaired, pyruvate cannot enter aerobic respiration. The cell compensates by shifting to anaerobic glycolysis to produce ATP, leading to increased lactate (Lactic Acidosis).

4.
In the liver, high levels of ATP inhibit isocitrate dehydrogenase. Which of the following consequences is most likely?
A. Increased glycolysis
B. Increased flow through the pentose phosphate pathway
C. Accumulation of citrate, promoting fatty acid synthesis
D. Activation of the urea cycle
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Accumulation of citrate, promoting fatty acid synthesis

Explanation: Isocitrate dehydrogenase is a rate-limiting enzyme in the Krebs cycle. Its inhibition leads to the accumulation of isocitrate and citrate. Excess citrate leaves the mitochondria and activates Acetyl-CoA Carboxylase, the key enzyme for fatty acid synthesis (lipogenesis).

5.
Which of the following best explains the coordinated regulation of glycolysis and gluconeogenesis?
A. They occur simultaneously in the liver
B. They are controlled by the same hormones in the same direction
C. They are reciprocally regulated at key irreversible steps
D. They share no common intermediates
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. They are reciprocally regulated at key irreversible steps

Explanation: To prevent a futile cycle (wasting energy), these pathways are reciprocally regulated. For example, high levels of Fructose-2,6-Bisphosphate activate PFK-1 (glycolysis) while inhibiting FBPase-1 (gluconeogenesis).

6.
Why does the pentose phosphate pathway operate more actively in rapidly diving cells?
A. To maximize ATP production
B. To generate NADPH for fatty acid oxidation
C. To provide ribose-5-phosphate for nucleotide synthesis
D. To facilitate glycogen breakdown
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. To provide ribose-5-phosphate for nucleotide synthesis

Explanation: Rapidly dividing cells require significant DNA and RNA synthesis. The Pentose Phosphate Pathway (non-oxidative phase) produces Ribose-5-Phosphate, which is the sugar backbone for nucleotides.

7.
Which situation would most likely increase the rate of glycogenolysis in the liver?
A. High insulin levels postprandially
B. Elevated cAMP in response to glucagon
C. Increased ATP levels in hepatocytes
D. Increased malonyl-CoA
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Elevated cAMP in response to glucagon

Explanation: Glucagon (secreted during fasting) binds to liver receptors, increasing cAMP. This activates Protein Kinase A (PKA), which phosphorylates and activates Glycogen Phosphorylase Kinase, leading to glycogen breakdown (glycogenolysis) to release glucose into the blood.

8.
A patient with G6PD deficiency is more vulnerable to oxidative damage. This is due to impaired production of:
A. ATP
B. NADH
C. NADPH
D. FADH2
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. NADPH

Explanation: G6PD is the first enzyme in the Pentose Phosphate Pathway, the primary source of cytosolic NADPH. NADPH is essential for regenerating reduced glutathione, which neutralizes reactive oxygen species (ROS) and protects cells from oxidative stress.

9.
How does insulin promote the storage of glucose in liver and muscle cells?
A. By activating glycogen synthase
B. By increasing glucose-6-phosphatase activity
C. By activating glycogen phosphorylase
D. By decreasing GLUT4 expression
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. By activating glycogen synthase

Explanation: Insulin promotes the dephosphorylation of key enzymes. It activates Glycogen Synthase (promoting storage) and inhibits Glycogen Phosphorylase (preventing breakdown).

10.
A researcher inhibits the enzyme aldolase B in hepatocytes. Which pathway is most directly affected?
A. Glycogenesis
B. Gluconeogenesis from fructose
C. Glycolysis from galactose
D. Glycogenolysis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Gluconeogenesis from fructose

Explanation: Aldolase B is found in the liver and cleaves Fructose-1-Phosphate into DHAP and Glyceraldehyde. Inhibition blocks fructose metabolism (fructolysis), which feeds into glycolysis or gluconeogenesis. Deficiency leads to Hereditary Fructose Intolerance.

11.
Which of the following is commonly used to assess kidney function?
A. Alanine aminotransferase (ALT)
B. Serum creatinine
C. Troponin I
D. Total bilirubin
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Serum creatinine

Explanation: Creatinine is a breakdown product of creatine phosphate in muscle. It is filtered by the kidneys at a constant rate. Elevated serum creatinine indicates decreased Glomerular Filtration Rate (GFR) and impaired kidney function.

12.
Which test is most specific for diagnosing myocardial infarction (MI)?
A. C-reactive protein (CRP)
B. Creatinine kinase (CK)
C. Troponin I or T
D. B-type natriuretic peptide (BNP)
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Troponin I or T

Explanation: Cardiac Troponins (cTnI and cTnT) are proteins specific to the heart muscle. Their presence in the blood is the most sensitive and specific marker for myocardial damage (heart attack).

13.
A patient presents with fatigue and pallor. A low serum ferritin level is most indicative of:
A. Vitamin B12 deficiency
B. Iron-deficiency anemia
C. Hemolytic anemia
D. Thalassemia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Iron-deficiency anemia

Explanation: Ferritin is the primary iron storage protein. Low serum ferritin is the earliest and most specific indicator of depleted iron stores, diagnosing iron-deficiency anemia.

14.
The HbA1c test is used primarily to:
A. Detect acute hypoglycemia
B. Assess long-term glucose control
C. Diagnose type-I diabetes only
D. Measure insulin levels
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Assess long-term glucose control

Explanation: Glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c) forms when glucose attaches to hemoglobin. Since RBCs live for ~120 days, HbA1c provides an average of blood glucose levels over the preceding 2-3 months.

15.
Most enzymes are:
A. Globular proteins that digest large molecules
B. Globular proteins that catalyze reactions
C. Fibrous proteins that catalyze reactions
D. Fibrous proteins that digest large molecules
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Globular proteins that catalyze reactions

Explanation: Enzymes are biological catalysts. Structurally, almost all enzymes are globular proteins, which are compact and water-soluble, allowing them to function in the aqueous environment of the cell.

16.
Enzymes are specific because:
A. They are globular proteins
B. They are affected by temperature and PH
C. Their tertiary structure gives them a uniquely shaped active site
D. They may have cofactors
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Their tertiary structure gives them a uniquely shaped active site

Explanation: Enzyme specificity is determined by the precise 3D arrangement of amino acids in the active site (tertiary structure), which allows only specific substrates to bind (Lock and Key or Induced Fit models).

17.
The shape of an enzyme's active site and its substrate are:
A. Complementary
B. The same
C. Similar
D. Related
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Complementary

Explanation: The substrate fits into the enzyme's active site like a key fits into a lock. They are not identical ("the same"), but complementary in shape and charge distribution.

18.
Catalysts:
A. Speed up a reaction and are used up in the process
B. Slow down a reaction and are used up in the process
C. Slow down a reaction and are not used up in the process
D. Speed up a reaction and are not used up in the process
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Speed up a reaction and are not used up in the process

Explanation: A catalyst lowers the activation energy of a reaction, increasing the rate, but it emerges unchanged at the end of the reaction to be used again.

19.
Which enzyme is deficient in classic galactosemia, leading to accumulation of galactose phosphate?
A. Galactokinase (GALK)
B. Aldolase B
C. Galactose-1-phosphate uridylyl transferase (GALT)
D. UDP galactose 4 epimerase (GALE)
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Galactose-1-phosphate uridylyl transferase (GALT)

Explanation: Classic Galactosemia is caused by GALT deficiency. This prevents the conversion of Galactose-1-Phosphate to UDP-Galactose, causing toxic accumulation of Gal-1-P in tissues.

20.
A patient presents with fasting hypoglycemia, lactic acidosis and hepatomegaly. Which glycogen storage disease is most likely?
A. Pompe's disease
B. Mcardle's disease
C. Von-Gierke's disease
D. Cori disease
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Von-Gierke's disease (Type I)

Explanation: Von-Gierke's disease involves a deficiency in Glucose-6-Phosphatase. The liver cannot release glucose during fasting (hypoglycemia). The trapped G6P forces glycolysis (lactic acidosis) and glycogen storage (hepatomegaly).

Section 2: Short Answer Questions

21.
Define an enzyme and state four properties of enzymes. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Definition: An enzyme is a biological catalyst, usually a protein, that speeds up specific chemical reactions in living organisms by lowering the activation energy, without being consumed in the reaction.

Properties:

  1. Specificity: Enzymes are highly specific to their substrates (lock and key mechanism).
  2. Catalytic Power: They increase reaction rates by a factor of $10^6$ to $10^{12}$.
  3. Reversibility: Most enzyme-catalyzed reactions are reversible.
  4. Regulation: Their activity can be regulated by pH, temperature, inhibitors, or activators.
  5. Unchanged: They remain unchanged at the end of the reaction.

24.
Outline the steps of oxidative phase of the pentose phosphate pathway. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

The oxidative phase is irreversible and generates NADPH.

  1. Oxidation: Glucose-6-Phosphate is oxidized to 6-Phosphogluconolactone by Glucose-6-Phosphate Dehydrogenase (G6PD). (Produces NADPH).
  2. Hydrolysis: 6-Phosphogluconolactone is hydrolyzed to 6-Phosphogluconate by Lactonase.
  3. Oxidative Decarboxylation: 6-Phosphogluconate is converted to Ribulose-5-Phosphate by 6-Phosphogluconate Dehydrogenase. (Produces NADPH and CO2).

Section 3: Long Answer Questions

29.

a. Provide the overall equation of the following metabolic pathways: (6 Marks)

  • i) Glycolysis
  • ii) Gluconeogenesis
  • iii) Krebs cycle

b. Outline the steps of the Krebs Cycle. (14 Marks)

Show Answer

a. Overall Equations:

  • i) Glycolysis:
    $Glucose + 2NAD^+ + 2ADP + 2Pi \rightarrow 2Pyruvate + 2NADH + 2H^+ + 2ATP + 2H_2O$
  • ii) Gluconeogenesis:
    $2Pyruvate + 4ATP + 2GTP + 2NADH + 2H^+ + 4H_2O \rightarrow Glucose + 4ADP + 2GDP + 6Pi + 2NAD^+$
  • iii) Krebs Cycle (per Acetyl-CoA):
    $Acetyl-CoA + 3NAD^+ + FAD + GDP + Pi + 2H_2O \rightarrow 2CO_2 + 3NADH + FADH_2 + GTP + CoA-SH + 3H^+$

b. Steps of the Krebs Cycle:

  1. Condensation: Acetyl-CoA (2C) combines with Oxaloacetate (4C) to form Citrate (6C). Enzyme: Citrate Synthase. (Irreversible).
  2. Isomerization: Citrate is converted to Isocitrate via cis-aconitate. Enzyme: Aconitase.
  3. Oxidative Decarboxylation 1: Isocitrate is oxidized to Alpha-Ketoglutarate (5C). Releases CO2 and produces NADH. Enzyme: Isocitrate Dehydrogenase. (Rate-limiting step).
  4. Oxidative Decarboxylation 2: Alpha-Ketoglutarate is oxidized to Succinyl-CoA (4C). Releases CO2 and produces NADH. Enzyme: Alpha-Ketoglutarate Dehydrogenase.
  5. Substrate-Level Phosphorylation: CoA is removed from Succinyl-CoA to form Succinate. Produces GTP (which converts to ATP). Enzyme: Succinyl-CoA Synthetase.
  6. Oxidation (Dehydrogenation): Succinate is oxidized to Fumarate. Produces FADH2. Enzyme: Succinate Dehydrogenase (part of Complex II).
  7. Hydration: Water is added to Fumarate to form Malate. Enzyme: Fumarase.
  8. Oxidation: Malate is oxidized to regenerate Oxaloacetate. Produces NADH. Enzyme: Malate Dehydrogenase.
30.

Giving examples, discuss the following about enzymes:

  • i) Classification (6 Marks)
  • ii) Types of inhibitors (6 Marks)
  • iii) Diagnostic uses (8 Marks)
Show Answer

i) Classification (IUBMB Classes):

Enzymes are classified into 6 major classes based on the reaction catalyzed:

  • Oxidoreductases: Catalyze redox reactions (e.g., Lactate Dehydrogenase).
  • Transferases: Transfer functional groups (e.g., Hexokinase, ALT).
  • Hydrolases: Catalyze hydrolysis (bond breaking with water) (e.g., Amylase, Lipase).
  • Lyases: Cleave bonds without water/redox, often forming double bonds (e.g., Aldolase).
  • Isomerases: Catalyze structural rearrangements (e.g., Phosphoglucose Isomerase).
  • Ligases: Join two molecules using ATP (e.g., DNA Ligase, Pyruvate Carboxylase).

ii) Types of Inhibitors:

  • Reversible Competitive: Inhibitor resembles substrate and binds to the active site. Can be overcome by adding more substrate. $V_{max}$ stays same, $K_m$ increases. (e.g., Methotrexate inhibiting Dihydrofolate Reductase).
  • Reversible Non-competitive: Binds to an allosteric site (not active site). Substrate can still bind, but reaction is blocked. $V_{max}$ decreases, $K_m$ stays same.
  • Irreversible: Forms a covalent bond with the enzyme, permanently disabling it. (e.g., Aspirin inhibits Cyclooxygenase, Penicillin inhibits bacterial transpeptidase).

iii) Diagnostic Uses (Clinical Enzymology):

Measurement of serum enzyme levels helps identify tissue damage:

  • Amylase & Lipase: Elevated in Acute Pancreatitis.
  • CK-MB & Troponins: Elevated in Myocardial Infarction (Heart Attack). Troponins are proteins but used similarly.
  • ALT (Alanine Transaminase): Specific for liver damage (Viral Hepatitis).
  • AST (Aspartate Transaminase): Liver damage, heart damage, muscle damage.
  • ALP (Alkaline Phosphatase): Elevated in biliary obstruction (Cholestasis) and bone diseases (Rickets, Paget's).
  • GGT: Marker for alcohol abuse and biliary obstruction.
  • LDH (Lactate Dehydrogenase): General marker for tissue breakdown (hemolysis, cancer).
  • Acid Phosphatase: Historically used for Prostate Cancer.
