Section A: Short Essay/Structured Questions

1.
Discuss applications of biochemistry studies with regard to Biochemistry and diseases (10 marks)
Show Answer

Biochemistry is fundamental to understanding diseases in several ways:

  1. Elucidation of Causes: It reveals the molecular mechanisms of diseases (e.g., sickle cell anemia as a single amino acid substitution in hemoglobin).
  2. Diagnosis: Biochemical tests (enzymes, blood sugar, hormones) are used to screen and diagnose conditions (e.g., elevated Troponins in heart attack, HbA1c in diabetes).
  3. Treatment Monitoring: Tracking biochemical markers helps assess the effectiveness of treatment (e.g., viral load in HIV, cholesterol levels with statins).
  4. Drug Discovery: Understanding biochemical pathways (e.g., receptors, enzymes) allows for the design of targeted drugs (pharmacology).
  5. Genetics/Screening: Identifying genetic metabolic disorders (e.g., Phenylketonuria) early allows for dietary management.
  6. Prevention: Nutritional biochemistry helps prevents deficiency diseases (e.g., Scurvy, Rickets).
2.
With examples, explain the following Hydrogen chemical bond (10 marks)
Show Answer

Definition: A hydrogen bond is a weak electrostatic attraction between a hydrogen atom covalently bonded to a highly electronegative atom (like Oxygen or Nitrogen) and another electronegative atom with a lone pair of electrons.

Characteristics:

  • Weaker than covalent bonds but crucial for stability in numbers.
  • Directional and specific.

Examples & Biological Importance:

  • Water: Hydrogen bonds between water molecules give water its unique properties (high boiling point, cohesion, solvent capabilities).
  • DNA Structure: Hydrogen bonds hold the two strands of the DNA double helix together (A pairs with T via 2 bonds; G pairs with C via 3 bonds).
  • Protein Structure: Stabilize secondary structures like Alpha-helices and Beta-pleated sheets (bonding between amide H and carbonyl O).
3.
Discuss the role of water in the human body (10 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Universal Solvent: Dissolves minerals, vitamins, glucose, and other solutes, enabling biochemical reactions.
  2. Transport Medium: Main component of blood plasma, transporting oxygen, nutrients, and waste products throughout the body.
  3. Thermoregulation: High heat capacity stabilizes body temperature; evaporation (sweating) dissipates heat.
  4. Lubrication and Cushioning: Lubricates joints (synovial fluid), protects the brain/spinal cord (CSF), and moistens mucous membranes.
  5. Metabolic Reactant: Directly participates in hydrolysis reactions (breaking down macromolecules).
  6. Acid-Base Balance: Acts as a medium for buffering systems.

Section B: Long Essay Questions

1.
Outline the different classes of enzymes that catalyze the various biochemical reactions in the human body (20 marks)
Show Answer

Enzymes are classified by the IUBMB into six major classes based on the type of reaction they catalyze:

  1. Oxidoreductases:
    • Catalyze oxidation-reduction reactions (transfer of electrons/hydrogen).
    • Examples: Lactate Dehydrogenase (LDH), Cytochrome oxidase.
  2. Transferases:
    • Catalyze the transfer of a functional group (e.g., methyl, phosphate, amino) from one molecule to another.
    • Examples: Hexokinase (transfers phosphate), Alanine Transaminase (ALT).
  3. Hydrolases:
    • Catalyze the cleavage of bonds (ester, ether, peptide, glycosidic) with the addition of water (hydrolysis).
    • Examples: Amylase, Lipase, Pepsin, Trypsin.
  4. Lyases:
    • Catalyze the cleavage of C-C, C-O, C-N bonds by means other than hydrolysis or oxidation, often forming double bonds.
    • Examples: Aldolase, Decarboxylases.
  5. Isomerases:
    • Catalyze geometric or structural changes within a single molecule (creating isomers).
    • Examples: Phosphoglucose isomerase, Epimerases.
  6. Ligases (Synthetases):
    • Catalyze the joining of two molecules coupled with the hydrolysis of a high-energy phosphate bond (ATP).
    • Examples: DNA Ligase, Pyruvate Carboxylase, Glutamine Synthetase.
2.
Discuss the functions of amino acids in living systems (20 marks)
Show Answer

Amino acids are the building blocks of life and serve diverse critical functions:

  1. Protein Synthesis: Serve as monomers for synthesizing structural proteins (collagen, keratin), enzymes, and transport proteins (hemoglobin).
  2. Energy Production: Carbon skeletons of amino acids can be oxidized to produce ATP (glucogenic or ketogenic).
  3. Neurotransmitter Synthesis:
    • Tryptophan $\rightarrow$ Serotonin.
    • Tyrosine $\rightarrow$ Dopamine, Epinephrine, Norepinephrine.
    • Glutamate itself is an excitatory neurotransmitter.
  4. Hormone Synthesis:
    • Thyroid hormones (T3, T4) derived from Tyrosine.
    • Melatonin derived from Tryptophan.
  5. Precursors for Important Biomolecules:
    • Heme: Synthesized from Glycine and Succinyl-CoA.
    • Purines/Pyrimidines: Glutamine, Glycine, Aspartate contribute atoms to nucleotide rings.
    • Histamine: From Histidine (immune response/vasodilation).
    • Nitric Oxide: From Arginine (vasodilator).
    • Creatine: From Arginine and Glycine (energy storage in muscle).
    • Melanin: From Tyrosine (skin pigment).
  6. pH Buffering: Amino acids have amphoteric properties (can act as acid or base), helping maintain blood/tissue pH (e.g., Histidine residues in Hemoglobin).
  7. Transport of Nitrogen: Alanine and Glutamine transport toxic nitrogen from tissues to the liver for excretion (Urea Cycle).

Section C: Multiple Choice Questions

1.
Which of the following is inorganic?
a) Water
b) Ascorbic acid
c) Glycine
d) All of the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Water

Explanation: Water ($H_2O$) does not contain carbon-hydrogen bonds, making it an inorganic compound. Ascorbic acid (Vitamin C) and Glycine (an amino acid) are organic carbon-based molecules.

2.
A chemical bond
a) Is a strong force of attraction holding atoms together in a molecule or crystal.
b) Is the tendency of an atom or a functional group to attract electrons towards itself
c) Does not include electrostatic bonds
d) All of the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Is a strong force of attraction holding atoms together in a molecule or crystal.

Explanation: This is the standard definition of a chemical bond. Option B describes "electronegativity".

3.
Which of the following is a cofactor during enzyme catalysis?
a) FMN
b) NAD+
c) Zinc
d) Tetrahydrofolate
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Zinc (Note: Context dependent)

Explanation: All options listed function as cofactors/coenzymes. However, in many contexts, "cofactor" is used specifically for inorganic metal ions (like $Zn^{2+}$, $Mg^{2+}$), while organic non-protein helpers are called "coenzymes" (NAD+, FMN, THF). Zinc is a classic metalloenzyme cofactor (e.g., in Carbonic Anhydrase).

4.
Amino acids combine with each other through:
a) Glycosidic bonds
b) Esteric bonds
c) Peptide bonds
d) A and C are both correct
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Peptide bonds

Explanation: Amino acids polymerize to form proteins via peptide bonds (amide linkages) formed between the carboxyl group of one amino acid and the amino group of another.

5.
The major cation in extracellular fluid is:
a) Cl-
b) Ca 2+
c) Na+
d) Fe2+
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Na+

Explanation: Sodium ($Na^+$) is the primary cation in ECF, critical for maintaining fluid balance and nerve impulse transmission. Potassium ($K^+$) is the major intracellular cation.

6.
The following is a covalent bond type:
a) Triple bond
b) Electrostatic bond
c) Hydrophobic interaction
d) Hydrogen bond
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Triple bond

Explanation: A triple bond involves the sharing of electron pairs, which is the definition of a covalent bond. The others are non-covalent interactions.

7.
The following amino acid is acidic and negatively charged at physiological pH:
a) Glycine
b) Cysteine
c) Glutamate
d) Tyrosine
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Glutamate

Explanation: Glutamate (Glutamic acid) and Aspartate (Aspartic acid) have carboxyl groups in their side chains that lose protons at neutral pH, becoming negatively charged.

8.
The major anion in extracellular fluid is
a) Chloride
b) Bicarbonate
c) Sulphate
d) Phosphate
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Chloride

Explanation: Chloride ($Cl^-$) is the most abundant anion in the extracellular space, often shifting to balance Sodium.

9.
The smallest known amino acid is
a) Alanine
b) Tyrosine
c) Tryptophan
d) Glycine
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Glycine

Explanation: Glycine has a single hydrogen atom as its side chain ($R = H$), making it the smallest and simplest amino acid.

10.
Which of the following vitamins is fat-soluble?
a) Niacin
b) Alpha-tocopherol
c) Ascorbic acid
d) Thiamine
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Alpha-tocopherol

Explanation: Alpha-tocopherol is Vitamin E, which is fat-soluble (along with A, D, and K). Niacin (B3), Thiamine (B1), and Ascorbic Acid (C) are water-soluble.

11.
Amino acids can be differentiated from each other by the structure of their:
a) Carboxyl group
b) Amino group
c) R-group or side chain
d) Hydrogen group
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) R-group or side chain

Explanation: All amino acids share the same basic backbone (amino group + carboxyl group + H). The R-group (side chain) is unique to each amino acid and determines its chemical properties.

12.
Which of the following amino acids is basic and positively charged at physiological pH?
a) Histidine
b) Phenylalanine
c) Valine
d) Isoleucine
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Histidine

Explanation: The basic amino acids are Histidine, Lysine, and Arginine. They accept protons at neutral pH, carrying a positive charge. The others listed are non-polar.

13.
Pantothenic acid is also referred to as:
a) Pyridoxine
b) Coenzyme A
c) Acetylcholine
d) Vitamin D
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Coenzyme A (See Note)

Explanation: Pantothenic acid is Vitamin B5. It is the essential precursor component of Coenzyme A. While they are not synonyms, in the context of limited options, it is most closely associated with Coenzyme A.

14.
The alpha carbon of the following amino acids is chiral except for:
a) Glycine
b) Histidine
c) Serine
d) Cysteine
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Glycine

Explanation: Chirality requires a carbon atom to be attached to four different groups. Glycine's side chain is a Hydrogen atom, meaning the alpha carbon is attached to two Hydrogens, making it achiral.
