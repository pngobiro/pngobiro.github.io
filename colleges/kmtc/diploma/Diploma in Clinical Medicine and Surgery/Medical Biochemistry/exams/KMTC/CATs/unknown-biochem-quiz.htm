Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions

1.
Which of the following explains why glucose-6-phosphate is not transported out of most cells?
A. It is too large to pass through membrane pores
B. It is rapidly converted to glycogen
C. It lacks a specific transporter and is ionically trapped
D. It inhibits the glucose transporter
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. It lacks a specific transporter and is ionically trapped

Explanation: Phosphorylation of glucose to glucose-6-phosphate adds a charged phosphate group. Charged molecules cannot easily cross the lipid bilayer, and there are no specific transporters for G6P in most cells, effectively trapping it inside for metabolism.

2.
During anaerobic glycolysis in muscle, the main reason for lactate production is to:
A. Prevent accumulation of pyruvate
B. Recycle NADH to NAD+ for glycolysis to continue
C. Provide ATP via oxidative phosphorylation
D. Enhance mitochondrial activity
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Recycle NADH to NAD+ for glycolysis to continue

Explanation: Glycolysis requires NAD+. Under anaerobic conditions, the electron transport chain cannot oxidize NADH back to NAD+. Lactate dehydrogenase converts pyruvate to lactate, oxidizing NADH to NAD+ in the process, allowing glycolysis to proceed.

3.
A mutation in the pyruvate dehydrogenase complex impairs aerobic respiration. Which compensatory metabolic shift is likely to occur?
A. Increased fatty acid synthesis
B. Increased gluconeogenesis
C. Increased anaerobic glycolysis
D. Increased pentose phosphate pathway activity
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Increased anaerobic glycolysis

Explanation: If the PDH complex is impaired, pyruvate cannot enter the Krebs cycle (aerobic respiration). The cell must rely on anaerobic glycolysis to generate ATP, leading to increased lactate production.

4.
In the liver, high levels of ATP inhibit isocitrate dehydrogenase. Which of the following consequences is most likely?
A. Increased glycolysis
B. Increased flow through the pentose phosphate pathway
C. Accumulation of citrate, promoting fatty acid synthesis
D. Activation of the urea cycle
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Accumulation of citrate, promoting fatty acid synthesis

Explanation: Inhibition of isocitrate dehydrogenase leads to an accumulation of isocitrate and citrate. Citrate can leave the mitochondria and activate acetyl-CoA carboxylase, the rate-limiting enzyme in fatty acid synthesis.

5.
Which of the following best explains the coordinated regulation of glycolysis and gluconeogenesis?
A. They occur simultaneously in the liver
B. They are controlled by the same hormones in the same direction
C. They are reciprocally regulated at key irreversible steps
D. They share no common intermediates
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. They are reciprocally regulated at key irreversible steps

Explanation: To prevent a futile cycle, when glycolysis is active, gluconeogenesis is inhibited, and vice versa. This is achieved through allosteric regulation and hormonal control at key steps like PFK-1/FBPase-1.

6.
Why does the pentose phosphate pathway operate more actively in rapidly diving cells?
A. To maximize ATP production
B. To generate NADPH for fatty acid oxidation
C. To provide ribose-5-phosphate for nucleotide synthesis
D. To facilitate glycogen breakdown
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. To provide ribose-5-phosphate for nucleotide synthesis

Explanation: Rapidly dividing cells need to synthesize DNA and RNA. The pentose phosphate pathway provides Ribose-5-Phosphate, a key precursor for nucleotide biosynthesis.

7.
Which situation would most likely increase the rate of glycogenolysis in the liver?
A. High insulin levels postprandially
B. Elevated cAMP in response to glucagon
C. Increased ATP levels in hepatocytes
D. Increased malonyl-CoA
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Elevated cAMP in response to glucagon

Explanation: Glucagon binds to liver receptors, increasing cAMP. This activates PKA, which phosphorylates and activates glycogen phosphorylase (via phosphorylase kinase), triggering glycogen breakdown (glycogenolysis).

8.
A patient with G6PD deficiency is more vulnerable to oxidative damage. This is due to impaired production of:
A. ATP
B. NADH
C. NADPH
D. FADH2
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. NADPH

Explanation: Glucose-6-Phosphate Dehydrogenase (G6PD) is the first enzyme in the pentose phosphate pathway, which produces NADPH. NADPH is essential for maintaining glutathione in its reduced state to fight oxidative stress.

9.
How does insulin promote the storage of glucose in liver and muscle cells?
A. By activating glycogen synthase
B. By increasing glucose-6-phosphatase activity
C. By activating glycogen phosphorylase
D. By decreasing GLUT4 expression
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. By activating glycogen synthase

Explanation: Insulin promotes glycogenesis (storage). It activates protein phosphatase-1, which dephosphorylates and activates glycogen synthase, while inhibiting glycogen phosphorylase.

10.
A researcher inhibits the enzyme aldolase B in hepatocytes. Which pathway is most directly affected?
A. Glycogenesis
B. Gluconeogenesis from fructose
C. Glycolysis from galactose
D. Glycogenolysis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Gluconeogenesis from fructose (or Fructose Metabolism)

Explanation: Aldolase B is critical for fructose metabolism. It cleaves Fructose-1-Phosphate. Deficiency causes Hereditary Fructose Intolerance.

11.
Which of the following is commonly used to assess kidney function?
A. Alanine aminotransferase (ALT)
B. Serum creatinine
C. Troponin I
D. Total bilirubin
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Serum creatinine

Explanation: Creatinine is a waste product of muscle metabolism excreted by the kidneys. Elevated serum creatinine levels suggest impaired kidney filtration (GFR).

12.
Which test is most specific for diagnosing myocardial infarction (MI)?
A. C-reactive protein (CRP)
B. Creatinine kinase (CK)
C. Troponin I or T
D. B-type natriuretic peptide (BNP)
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Troponin I or T

Explanation: Cardiac Troponins (I and T) are highly specific and sensitive biomarkers for cardiac muscle damage, making them the gold standard for diagnosing MI.

13.
A patient presents with fatigue and pallor. A low serum ferritin level is most indicative of:
A. Vitamin B12 deficiency
B. Iron-deficiency anemia
C. Hemolytic anemia
D. Thalassemia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Iron-deficiency anemia

Explanation: Ferritin reflects the body's iron stores. Low serum ferritin is the most specific biochemical marker for iron deficiency anemia.

14.
The HbA1c test is used primarily to:
A. Detect acute hypoglycemia
B. Assess long-term glucose control
C. Diagnose type-I diabetes only
D. Measure insulin levels
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Assess long-term glucose control

Explanation: Glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c) reflects average blood glucose levels over the past 2-3 months (the lifespan of a red blood cell).

15.
Most enzymes are:
A. Globular proteins that digest large molecules
B. Globular proteins that catalyze reactions
C. Fibrous proteins that catalyze reactions
D. Fibrous proteins that digest large molecules
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Globular proteins that catalyze reactions

Explanation: The vast majority of enzymes are globular proteins, which allows them to form specific active sites to catalyze biochemical reactions.

16.
Enzymes are specific because:
A. They are globular proteins
B. They are affected by temperature and PH
C. Their tertiary structure gives them a uniquely shaped active site
D. They may have cofactors
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Their tertiary structure gives them a uniquely shaped active site

Explanation: Specificity is determined by the precise 3D shape (tertiary structure) of the active site, which fits only specific substrates (Lock and Key or Induced Fit models).

17.
The shape of an enzyme's active site and its substrate are:
A. Complementary
B. The same
C. Similar
D. Related
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Complementary

Explanation: The active site and substrate fit together like a lock and key (or hand in glove), meaning their shapes are complementary, not identical.

18.
Catalysts:
A. Speed up a reaction and are used up in the process
B. Slow down a reaction and are used up in the process
C. Slow down a reaction and are not used up in the process
D. Speed up a reaction and are not used up in the process
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Speed up a reaction and are not used up in the process

Explanation: By definition, a catalyst increases the rate of a chemical reaction by lowering the activation energy but is not consumed or permanently altered in the process.

19.
Which enzyme is deficient in classic galactosemia, leading to accumulation of galactose phosphate?
A. Galactokinase (GALK)
B. Aldolase B
C. Galactose-1-phosphate uridylyl transferase (GALT)
D. UDP galactose 4 epimerase (GALE)
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Galactose-1-phosphate uridylyl transferase (GALT)

Explanation: Classic galactosemia is caused by a severe deficiency in GALT. This leads to the accumulation of Galactose-1-Phosphate, which is toxic to the liver, kidneys, and nervous system.

20.
A patient presents with fasting hypoglycemia, lactic acidosis and hepatomegaly. Which glycogen storage disease is most likely?
A. Pompe's disease
B. Mcardle's disease
C. Von-Gierke's disease
D. Cori disease
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Von-Gierke's disease (Type I)

Explanation: Von-Gierke's disease is caused by Glucose-6-Phosphatase deficiency. The liver cannot release glucose into the blood (fasting hypoglycemia) and G6P builds up, leading to increased glycolysis (lactic acidosis) and glycogen accumulation (hepatomegaly).

Section 2: Short Answer Questions

21.
Define an enzyme and state four properties of enzymes. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Definition: An enzyme is a biological catalyst, typically a protein, that speeds up the rate of a specific chemical reaction in a living organism without being consumed in the process.

Properties:

  1. Catalytic Efficiency: Greatly increase reaction rates.
  2. Specificity: Act on specific substrates (substrate specificity) or catalyze specific reaction types.
  3. Reversibility: Can catalyze reactions in both forward and reverse directions (usually).
  4. Regulation: Activity can be controlled by inhibitors, activators, or environmental factors (pH, Temp).
  5. Unchanged: Remain unchanged at the end of the reaction.
22.
Identify 5 B-complex vitamins and the role each plays as a cofactor. (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Thiamine (B1) - TPP: Cofactor for oxidative decarboxylation (e.g., Pyruvate Dehydrogenase).
  2. Riboflavin (B2) - FAD/FMN: Cofactor in redox reactions (e.g., Succinate Dehydrogenase).
  3. Niacin (B3) - NAD+/NADP+: Electron carrier in redox reactions (e.g., Glycolysis).
  4. Pantothenic Acid (B5) - Coenzyme A: Acyl group carrier (e.g., Acetyl-CoA formation).
  5. Pyridoxine (B6) - PLP: Cofactor for transamination and amino acid metabolism.
  6. Biotin (B7): Cofactor for carboxylation reactions (e.g., Pyruvate Carboxylase).
23.
Enumerate the equation of the reactions that occur at the different complexes of the electron transport chain. (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  • Complex I (NADH Dehydrogenase): $NADH + H^+ + Q \rightarrow NAD^+ + QH_2$ (Protons pumped: 4H+)
  • Complex II (Succinate Dehydrogenase): $Succinate + Q \rightarrow Fumarate + QH_2$ (No protons pumped)
  • Complex III (Cytochrome bc1): $QH_2 + 2Cyt~c~(ox) + 2H^+ \rightarrow Q + 2Cyt~c~(red) + 4H^+$
  • Complex IV (Cytochrome c Oxidase): $4Cyt~c~(red) + O_2 + 4H^+ \rightarrow 4Cyt~c~(ox) + 2H_2O$
24.
Outline the steps of oxidative phase of the pentose phosphate pathway. (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Oxidation: Glucose-6-Phosphate is oxidized to 6-Phosphogluconolactone by Glucose-6-Phosphate Dehydrogenase (G6PD). Produces NADPH.
  2. Hydrolysis: 6-Phosphogluconolactone is hydrolyzed to 6-Phosphogluconate by Lactonase.
  3. Oxidative Decarboxylation: 6-Phosphogluconate is converted to Ribulose-5-Phosphate by 6-Phosphogluconate Dehydrogenase. Produces NADPH and releases CO2.
25.
Identify the irreversible steps of glycolysis and their respective enzymes. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

There are three irreversible regulatory steps in glycolysis:

  1. Step 1: Glucose $\rightarrow$ Glucose-6-Phosphate. Enzyme: Hexokinase (or Glucokinase).
  2. Step 3: Fructose-6-Phosphate $\rightarrow$ Fructose-1,6-Bisphosphate. Enzyme: Phosphofructokinase-1 (PFK-1).
  3. Step 10: Phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP) $\rightarrow$ Pyruvate. Enzyme: Pyruvate Kinase.
26.
Differentiate between type I and type II diabetes mellitus. (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  • Cause: Type I is autoimmune destruction of beta cells (absolute insulin deficiency). Type II is insulin resistance and relative insulin deficiency.
  • Onset: Type I usually childhood/early adulthood. Type II usually adulthood/middle age.
  • Treatment: Type I requires Insulin. Type II managed by diet, exercise, oral hypoglycemics (metformin), and potentially insulin later.
  • Ketosis: Prone to Ketoacidosis in Type I. Rare in Type II.
  • Body Habitus: Type I often thin/normal. Type II often associated with obesity.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions

29.

a. Provide the overall equation of the following metabolic pathways: (6 Marks)

  • i) Glycolysis
  • ii) Gluconeogenesis
  • iii) Krebs cycle

b. Outline the steps of the Krebs Cycle. (14 Marks)

Show Answer

a. Overall Equations:

  • i) Glycolysis:
    $Glucose + 2NAD^+ + 2ADP + 2Pi \rightarrow 2Pyruvate + 2NADH + 2H^+ + 2ATP + 2H_2O$
  • ii) Gluconeogenesis:
    $2Pyruvate + 4ATP + 2GTP + 2NADH + 2H^+ + 4H_2O \rightarrow Glucose + 4ADP + 2GDP + 6Pi + 2NAD^+$
  • iii) Krebs Cycle (per Acetyl-CoA):
    $Acetyl-CoA + 3NAD^+ + FAD + GDP + Pi + 2H_2O \rightarrow 2CO_2 + 3NADH + FADH_2 + GTP + CoA-SH + 3H^+$

b. Steps of the Krebs Cycle:

  1. Condensation: Acetyl-CoA + Oxaloacetate $\rightarrow$ Citrate (Enzyme: Citrate Synthase).
  2. Isomerization: Citrate $\rightarrow$ Isocitrate (Enzyme: Aconitase).
  3. Oxidative Decarboxylation 1: Isocitrate $\rightarrow$ Alpha-Ketoglutarate + NADH + CO2 (Enzyme: Isocitrate Dehydrogenase).
  4. Oxidative Decarboxylation 2: Alpha-Ketoglutarate $\rightarrow$ Succinyl-CoA + NADH + CO2 (Enzyme: Alpha-Ketoglutarate Dehydrogenase).
  5. Substrate-Level Phosphorylation: Succinyl-CoA $\rightarrow$ Succinate + GTP (Enzyme: Succinyl-CoA Synthetase).
  6. Oxidation: Succinate $\rightarrow$ Fumarate + FADH2 (Enzyme: Succinate Dehydrogenase).
  7. Hydration: Fumarate $\rightarrow$ Malate (Enzyme: Fumarase).
  8. Oxidation: Malate $\rightarrow$ Oxaloacetate + NADH (Enzyme: Malate Dehydrogenase).
30.

Giving examples, discuss the following about enzymes:

  • i) Classification (6 Marks)
  • ii) Types of inhibitors (6 Marks)
  • iii) Diagnostic uses (8 Marks)
Show Answer

i) Classification (IUBMB Classes):

  • Oxidoreductases: Catalyze redox reactions (e.g., Lactate Dehydrogenase).
  • Transferases: Transfer functional groups (e.g., Hexokinase).
  • Hydrolases: Hydrolysis reactions (e.g., Lipase, Amylase).
  • Lyases: Bond cleavage/formation without water/redox (e.g., Aldolase).
  • Isomerases: Structural rearrangement (e.g., Phosphoglucose Isomerase).
  • Ligases: Joining molecules using ATP (e.g., DNA Ligase).

ii) Types of Inhibitors:

  • Reversible Competitive: Binds to active site, competes with substrate. Vmax unchanged, Km increases. (e.g., Statins inhibiting HMG-CoA reductase).
  • Reversible Non-competitive: Binds to allosteric site. Vmax decreases, Km unchanged.
  • Irreversible: Covalently binds and permanently destroys enzyme activity (e.g., Aspirin on Cyclooxygenase, Organophosphates on Acetylcholinesterase).

iii) Diagnostic Uses (Clinical Enzymology):

  • Amylase/Lipase: Diagnosis of Acute Pancreatitis.
  • CK-MB / Troponin: Diagnosis of Myocardial Infarction.
  • ALT/AST: Diagnosis of Hepatocellular damage (Viral hepatitis).
  • ALP: Diagnosis of Obstructive Jaundice or Bone disease.
  • Acid Phosphatase: Prostate cancer (historical, now PSA).
  • GGT: Alcohol abuse marker.
  • LDH: General tissue damage/Hemolysis.
