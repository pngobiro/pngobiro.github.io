üß† Biochemistry of the Brain

The adult brain forms only 2% of body weight but receives 15% of total blood supply to support its high oxygen utilization. It is completely dependent on a continuous supply of blood glucose and oxygen. If oxygen supply is cut off for 4-5 minutes or glucose supply cut off for 10-15 minutes, brain damage will occur.

üöß

Blood Brain Barrier (BBB)

A complex mechanism controlling the kind and rate of substances entering the brain extracellular fluid. Endothelial cells lining brain capillaries are sealed by continuous tight junctions.

  • Lipid soluble substances: Enter quickly (e.g., heroin, nicotine, alcohol). Heroin (two acetyl groups) is more lipid-soluble than morphine (two hydroxyl groups) and crosses faster.
  • Polar nutrients: Glucose and amino acids require specific membrane transport proteins.
02

ü©∏ Biochemistry of Erythrocytes

Erythrocytes (RBCs) lack mitochondria and organelles. Their metabolism is unique:

  • Anaerobic Respiration: They rely entirely on glycolysis for energy, producing lactate.
  • Pentose Phosphate Pathway: Essential for producing NADPH.
  • Glutathione: A strong antioxidant that neutralizes reactive oxygen species (ROS). It requires NADPH to function.

Clinical Correlation: G6PD Deficiency

Drugs that increase oxidative stress (e.g., aspirin, antimalarials) can cause haemolytic anaemia in people deficient in glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PD), the first enzyme of the pentose phosphate pathway.

ü´Ä Biochemistry of the Liver

The liver is the largest internal organ (~1.5 kg). It receives blood from the Hepatic Artery (oxygenated) and the Hepatic Portal Vein (nutrient-rich from digestion).

Major Functions

Metabolic

Carbohydrate (glycogen storage, gluconeogenesis), Protein (urea cycle, plasma proteins), and Lipid metabolism (cholesterol synthesis, lipogenesis).

Secretory & Excretory

Formation and secretion of bile. Conjugation and excretion of bilirubin and foreign dyes.

Storage

Stores Glycogen, Vitamins (A, D, B12), and Iron (as Ferritin).

Detoxification

Metabolizes drugs, toxins, and hormones. Kupffer cells remove bacteria.

Liver Cell Types

Cell Type Function
Hepatocytes 80% of liver cells. Perform metabolic, synthetic, and secretory functions.
Kupffer Cells Macrophages lining sinusoids; phagocytose bacteria and debris.
Stellate Cells Fat-storing cells; store Vitamin A.
04

‚ôªÔ∏è Bilirubin Metabolism

Bilirubin is the end product of haem metabolism.

  1. Creation: Reticuloendothelial cells (spleen) break down old RBCs. Haem is converted to Biliverdin, then Unconjugated Bilirubin (water-insoluble).
  2. Transport: Unconjugated bilirubin binds to albumin and travels to the liver.
  3. Conjugation: In hepatocytes, glucuronyl transferase adds glucuronic acid to form Conjugated Bilirubin (water-soluble).
  4. Excretion: Excreted into bile. In the intestine, bacteria convert it to Urobilinogen. Most is excreted as Stercobilin (feces), some reabsorbed (enterohepatic circulation), and a small amount excreted as Urobilin (urine).

Types of Jaundice

Type Cause Bilirubin Profile
Pre-hepatic Haemolysis (Malaria, Sickle cell) ‚Üë Unconjugated (Indirect)
Hepatic Cellular damage (Viral hepatitis, Drugs) ‚Üë Both (Total, Direct & Indirect)
Post-hepatic Obstruction (Gallstones, Tumours) ‚Üë Conjugated (Direct)

üß™ Liver Function Tests (LFTs)

LFTs measure the functional capacity of the liver and cellular damage.

1. Serum Bilirubin

Measured using the Diazo method (Van den Bergh reaction).

  • Total Bilirubin: 0.2 ‚Äì 1.0 mg/dl
  • Direct (Conjugated): 0.1 ‚Äì 0.4 mg/dl
  • Indirect (Unconjugated): 0.1 ‚Äì 0.6 mg/dl

2. Serum Proteins

Measures synthetic function. Albumin is exclusively synthesized by the liver.

A:G Ratio (Albumin:Globulin): Normal is 1.2 ‚Äì 1.5. In liver disease, albumin falls and globulins may rise (due to infection/inflammation), leading to a reversal of ratio (< 1).

3. Prothrombin Time (PT) & INR

Measures clotting factors (II, VII, IX, X) synthesized by the liver. Prolonged PT indicates impaired synthetic function or Vitamin K deficiency.

INR = (PT test / PT control)ISI

4. Liver Enzymes

Enzyme Significance Specific For
ALT (SGPT) Hepatocellular damage Liver (More specific than AST)
AST (SGOT) Tissue damage Liver, Heart, Muscle, RBCs
ALP Cholestasis (Obstruction) Biliary tract, Bone, Placenta
GGT Biliary obstruction, Alcohol Biliary tree (Highly specific)
üìù

De Ritis Ratio

In acute viral hepatitis, ALT > AST. In alcoholic liver disease, AST > ALT (often 2:1 ratio).

üí°

Markers of Cholestasis

ALP and GGT are markers of cholestasis. Raised ALP with normal GGT suggests bone origin. Raised ALP with raised GGT confirms liver/biliary origin.
