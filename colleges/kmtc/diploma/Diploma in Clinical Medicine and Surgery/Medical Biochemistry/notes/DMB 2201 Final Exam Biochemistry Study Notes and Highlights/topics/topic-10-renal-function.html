üß¨ Biochemistry of the Kidney

The kidneys remove, in urine, products of metabolism such as urea, uric acid and creatinine, and retain substances such as glucose, amino acids, and proteins. They also metabolize and remove drugs and toxins. The kidney also has an endocrine function and produces the peptide hormone erythropoietin which stimulates erythropoiesis in the bone marrow. It also produce the enzyme renin that activates the renin angiotensin system. During renal disease there is production of excess renin leading in renal hypertension and reduced erythropoietin causing anaemia.

Functions of the kidney

  • To get rid the body of waste product of metabolism
  • To get rid of foreign and non-endogenous substances
  • To maintain salt and water balance
  • To maintain acid base balance of the body
  • To produce the hormone erythropoietin and the enzyme renin
  • 1-Œ±-hydroxylase enzyme catalyzes the conversion of the relatively inactive 25-hydroxycholecalciferol into calcitriol (1,25-dihydroxycholecalciferol)

Functional Components

The functional unit of the kidney is called a nephron. It consists of two main parts, the glomerulus and the tubular system.

The Glomerulus: Composed of a bowman's capsule and a tuft of leaky blood vessels. The primary purpose is filtrations. The leaky vessels filter into the glomerulus almost all the water, electrolytes, small proteins, nutrients such as sugar etc and excretory products such as urea etc. The filtrations is dependent on the size (average pore size 8 nm) and charge of the particles.

The Tubular System: Responsible for reabsorption of most of the water, electrolytes, nutrients as well as excretion of the remaining nutrients by means of secretion into the tubules. These tubules are responsible for the concentration of urine.

ü©∏ Serum Markers

Serum Urea

Urea is the end product of protein catabolism produced in the liver. It undergoes filtration at the glomerulus as well as secretion and reabsorption at the tubules.

Normal range: 20-45 mg/dl.

Limitations of Urea

Urea level only rises when the glomerular function is reduced below 50%. Levels are also affected by diet (high protein increases urea) and hydration status. It is less specific than creatinine.

Blood Urea Nitrogen (BUN)

Serum urea levels can be expressed as BUN. Since nitrogen contributes 28/60 (0.47) of the weight of urea:

BUN = Serum Urea √ó 0.47

A rise in blood nitrogen level is known as azotemia.

Serum Creatinine

Creatine is a small tripeptide found in muscles. It is released during regular wear and tear and converted to creatinine. Creatinine is freely filtered at the glomerulus and is not significantly reabsorbed.

Normal range: 0.6 to 1.5 mg/dl.

Serum creatinine is a better indicator of renal function than urea. However, levels depend on muscle mass; athletes will have higher levels than sedentary individuals.

‚öñÔ∏è Clearance Tests (GFR)

Clearance is defined as the volume of plasma or blood which is completely cleared of a substance in one minute.

Urea Clearance

Because urea is partially reabsorbed by tubules, urea clearance is less than GFR.

1. Maximum Urea Clearance (C_m)

Used when urine output is > 2 ml/minute.

C_m = \frac{U \times V}{B}

Normal Value: 75 ml/min

2. Standard Urea Clearance (C_s)

Used when urine output is < 2 ml/minute. It uses the square root of volume to correct for increased reabsorption.

C_s = \frac{U \times \sqrt{V}}{B}

Normal Value: 54 ml/min

Creatinine Clearance (Endogenous)

Since creatinine is freely filtered and not significantly reabsorbed/secreted, it is a good estimate of GFR. Measured over 24 hours.

C_{cr} = \frac{U \times V}{P} \times 1440

Where U = Urine conc, V = Urine volume, P = Serum creatinine.

Normal range: 100 ‚Äì 120 ml/min (Males), 95 ‚Äì 105 ml/min (Females).

Inulin Clearance

Inulin is a polymer of fructose. It is the ideal substance for GFR measurement because:

  • It is non-toxic and not metabolized.
  • It is completely filtered at the glomerulus.
  • It is neither secreted nor reabsorbed by tubules.
C_{In} = \frac{U \times V}{P}

Normal Value: 125 ml/min. This is the Gold Standard for GFR.

Renal Plasma Flow (PAH Clearance)

Para-aminohippurate (PAH) is filtered and secreted, such that it is almost completely removed from plasma in one pass. Therefore, PAH clearance measures Renal Plasma Flow (RPF).

Normal RPF: ~574 ml/min.

Filtration Fraction (FF)

The fraction of plasma passing through the kidney which is filtered.

FF = \frac{GFR (Inulin Clearance)}{RPF (PAH Clearance)}

Normal: 0.16 ‚Äì 0.21 (approx 20%)

üß™ Tubular Function Tests

Concentration Tests

Measures the ability of tubules to reabsorb water. Fishberg Concentration Test: Patient is fluid restricted from evening until morning. Specific gravity of morning urine is measured.

Interpretation: Sp. Gr. should be > 1.025. If fixed at 1.010 (Isosthenuria), it indicates severe renal damage.

Dilution Test

Patient drinks 1200ml water. Urine collected every hour for 4 hours.

Interpretation: Sp. Gr. should fall to 1.003. Failure to dilute indicates tubular dysfunction.

Parameter Significance
pH Range 4.5 - 8.0. Reflects acid-base status.
Specific Gravity 1.002 - 1.035. Measure of concentration ability.
Protein >150 mg/day is abnormal. Massive proteinuria (>3.5g) = Nephrotic syndrome.

Microscopic Examination

Cells

  • RBCs: Hematuria (glomerular damage, stones, trauma). Dysmorphic RBCs suggest glomerular disease.
  • WBCs: Pyuria (Infection/Inflammation). >2/HPF is abnormal.
  • Epithelial: Squamous (contamination), Transitional, or Renal Tubular (tubular damage).

Casts

Formed in the distal tubules/collecting ducts.

  • Hyaline: Tamm-Horsfall protein. Can be normal.
  • RBC Casts: Pathognomonic for Glomerulonephritis.
  • WBC Casts: Pyelonephritis (kidney infection).
  • Waxy/Broad Casts: End-stage renal disease (dilated tubules).

Crystals

Common crystals: Calcium oxalate, Triple phosphate. Pathological crystals: Cystine (cystinuria), Tyrosine/Leucine (severe liver disease).
