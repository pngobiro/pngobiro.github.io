1. Galactosaemias

A group of inherited enzyme deficiencies that feature elevation in the levels of blood galactose are referred to as galactosaemia. The classical form of galactosaemia is caused by "galactose-1-phosphate uridyltransferase" (GALT) gene defect and presents in infancy with failure to thrive, vomiting and intracranial hypertension. Other clinical symptoms may include mental retardation, jaundice, hepatosplenomegaly and cataracts. dose-dependent galactosuria. Three kinds of enzymes of the galactose metabolism viz. UDP-Hexose-1-Phosphate Uridyl Transferases, Galactokinase and 4-Epimerases have been implicated in these disorders characterized by galactosaemia with slight variations in the signs and symptoms.

Fructosuria (Hepatic Fructokinase Deficiency)

Fructosuria is a benign, asymptomatic defect of intermediary metabolism characterised by the presence of large amounts of fructose in urine sufficient to give a positive reducing sugar test. Small amounts of fructose occur in the urine of normal individuals ingesting a regular diet but amounts sufficient to give a positive test for reducing sugar in the routine examination occur only in essential fructosuria, familial fructose intolerance, and advanced liver disease.

Hereditary Fructose Intolerance (Fructosaemia)

Most of the cases of fructose intolerance are severely ill infants with recurrent hypoglycaemia and vomiting, occurring at the time of weaning when fructose or sucrose is added to the diet and resulting in marked malnutrition. Hyperuricaemia and hyperuricosuria are commonly associated features. The defect resides in aldolase B. In aldolase 'B'-deficient tissues, cytoplasmic accumulation of fructose-1-phosphate leads to sequestration of inorganic phosphate.

3. Lactose Intolerance

Lactose intolerance is the inability to digest lactose, a disaccharide found in milk and other dairy products. It is caused by a deficiency of the enzyme lactase.

Biochemical: Lactose cannot be completely digested and absorbed in the small intestine and passes into the large intestine, where bacteria convert it to toxic products that cause abdominal cramps and diarrhoea. The problem is further complicated because undigested lactose and its metabolites increase the osmolarity of the intestinal contents, favouring the retention of water in the intestine.

4. Glucose-6-Phosphate Dehydrogenase Deficiency

Glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PD) deficiency increases the vulnerability of erythrocytes to oxidative stress. Clinical presentations include acute hemolytic anemia, chronic hemolytic anaemia, neonatal hyperbilirubinaemia, and an absence of clinical symptoms.

Biochemical: G6PD catalyses nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate (NADP) to its reduced form, NADPH, in the pentose phosphate pathway. NADPH protects cells from oxidative damage. Because erythrocytes do not generate NADPH in any other way, they are more susceptible than other cells to destruction from oxidative stress.

1. Phenylketonuria (PKU)

Phenylketonuria is an inborn error of metabolism resulting from a deficient activity of phenylalanine hydroxylase (EC 1. 14.16.1) and is characterized by hyperphenylalaninemia and mental retardation. The clinical features include a 'mousy' odor in urine; light pigmentation; peculiarities of gait, stance, and sitting posture; eczema; and epilepsy.

The basic biochemical abnormality is the block at the conversion of phenylalanine to tyrosine. The resultant accumulation of phenylalanine and its alternate metabolic products i.e. (phenyl pyruvic acid, phenyl lactic acid and phenyl acetic acid) along with the resultant deficiency of tyrosine contributes to most of the clinical features.

Diagnosis of PKU

  • Blood Levels: Normal blood phenylalanine is 58+/- 15 \mu M/l. In untreated PKU, levels as high as 2.4 mM/l can be found.
  • Ferric Chloride Test: Urine turns transient green or blue on adding ferric chloride due to phenylpyruvic acid.
  • Guthrie's Test: Bacterial inhibition assay using B. subtilis.

2. Tyrosinemias

Tyrosinemias are a group of inherited disorders characterized by levels of amino acid tyrosine and their catabolic intermediates in blood and their excretion in urine.

  • Tyrosinemia Type I (Hepatorenal): Deficiency of Fumarylacetoacetase. Presents with liver failure, cabbage-like odor, and renal tubular damage.
  • Tyrosinemia Type II (Oculocutaneous): Deficiency of Tyrosine Aminotransferase. Characterized by corneal ulcers and painful keratoses of palms/soles.
  • Tyrosinemia Type III: Deficiency of p-hydroxyphenylpyruvate dioxygenase. Rare, mild mental retardation, no liver damage.

3. Alkaptonuria

(Homogentisic Acid Oxidase Deficiency). The manifestations are urine that turns dark on standing and alkalinization, black ochronotic pigmentation of cartilage and collagenous tissues, and arthritis, especially characteristic in the spine. Homogentisic acid accumulates and oxidizes to Benzoquinone Acetic Acid (BQA) which deposits in tissues.

4. Homocystinuria

(Cystathionine \beta-Synthase Deficiency). Characterized by dislocation of eye lenses (ectopia lentis), spinal osteoporosis, and thromboembolic events (recurrent strokes). Mild to moderate mental retardation is common. Homocysteine accumulates in plasma and is excreted in urine.

5. Maple Syrup Urine Disease (MSUD)

(Branched-Chain Ketoaciduria). Defect in Branched-Chain Alpha-Keto Acid Dehydrogenase. Leads to accumulation of branched-chain keto acids (from Leucine, Isoleucine, Valine). Clinical features: mental retardation, feeding problems, and a maple syrup odour to the urine.

6. Albinism

Arises from inherited defects in pigment cells (melanocytes) of eye and skin. Melanin is synthesized from phenylalanine/tyrosine through DOPA. Tyrosinase-negative albinos completely lack visual pigment (mutation in tyrosinase gene). Tyrosinase-positive have normal enzyme activity in vitro but cannot act in vivo (transport defect).

üß¨ Purine and Pyrimidine Disorders

  • Lesch-Nyhan Syndrome: X-linked recessive defect of hypoxanthine-guanine phosphoribosyltransferase (HGPRT). Leads to increased purine salvage pathway from PRPP. Results in severe gout, renal failure, spasticity and tendency for self-mutilation.
  • Adenosine Deaminase Deficiency: Associated with severe immunodeficiency (SCID) where both T cells and B cells are deficient. Accumulation of deoxyribonucleotides inhibits DNA synthesis.
  • Primary metabolic gout: Inherited metabolic defect leading to excessive rate of conversion of glycine to uric acid.
  • Orotic Aciduria: Type I is due to defect in orotate phosphoribosyltransferase and OMP decarboxylase. Causes megaloblastic anemia and accumulation of orotate.
