üß† Introduction to the Endocrine System

The endocrine system consists of glands widely separated from each other with no direct anatomical links. Endocrine glands consist of groups of secretory cells surrounded by an extensive network of capillaries which facilitates diffusion of hormones (chemical messengers) from the secretory cells into the bloodstream. They are commonly referred to as the ductless glands because the hormones are secreted and diffuse directly into the bloodstream.

Types of Hormones

Most hormones are synthesised from amino acids (amines, polypeptides and proteins, or are cholesterol-based lipids (steroids).

Regulation

The level of a hormone in the blood is variable and self-regulating within its normal range. A hormone is released in response to a specific stimulus and usually its action reverses or negates the stimulus through a negative feedback mechanism. This may be controlled either indirectly through the release of hormones by the hypothalamus and the anterior pituitary gland, or directly by blood levels of the stimulus.

The effect of a positive feedback mechanism is amplification of the stimulus and increasing release of the hormone until a particular process is complete and the stimulus ceases, e.g. release of oxytocin during labour.

üëë Pituitary Gland & Hypothalamus

The pituitary gland (hypophysis) and the hypothalamus act as a unit, regulating the activity of most of the other endocrine glands. The pituitary gland consists of two distinct lobes:

  • Anterior pituitary (adenohypophysis): An upgrowth of glandular epithelium from the pharynx. Connected to the hypothalamus via the pituitary portal system.
  • Posterior pituitary (neurohypophysis): A down growth of nervous tissue from the brain. Connected via the hypothalamohypophyseal tract (nerve fibers).

Anterior Pituitary Hormones

The release of an anterior pituitary hormone follows stimulation of the gland by a specific releasing hormone produced by the hypothalamus. The whole system is controlled by a negative feedback mechanism.

Posterior Pituitary Hormones

Oxytocin and antidiuretic hormone (ADH) are synthesised in the hypothalamus and released from axon terminals within the posterior pituitary.

Oxytocin

Stimulates uterine smooth muscle contraction during childbirth and myoepithelial cells of the breast for milk ejection. This operates via a positive feedback mechanism.

ADH (Vasopressin)

Increases water reabsorption in the distal tubules and collecting ducts of the kidney. Secretion is stimulated by high osmotic pressure (dehydration) or low blood volume.

03

ü¶¥ Calcium Regulation (Thyroid & Parathyroid)

Calcitonin

Secreted by the parafollicular or C-cells of the thyroid gland. It reduces blood calcium (Ca^{2+}) levels when raised by inhibiting osteoclasts (bone resorption) and inhibiting renal reabsorption of calcium. It is important during childhood bone growth.

Parathyroid Hormone (PTH)

Secreted by the Chief cells of the four parathyroid glands. Its secretion is stimulated by low blood calcium levels. PTH increases blood calcium by:

  • Stimulating osteoclasts to resorb bone.
  • Increasing renal reabsorption of calcium.
  • Indirectly increasing intestinal absorption of calcium (via Vitamin D activation).

The adrenal glands consist of an outer cortex (essential for life) and an inner medulla.

Adrenal Cortex (Corticosteroids)

Produces steroid hormones from cholesterol:

‚ñ∂ Glucocorticoids (e.g., Cortisol)

Essential for life, regulating metabolism and response to stress. Secretion is stimulated by ACTH. Effects include:

  • Gluconeogenesis and hyperglycaemia.
  • Lipolysis and protein breakdown.
  • Anti-inflammatory and immunosuppressive actions in high doses.
‚ñ∂ Mineralocorticoids (e.g., Aldosterone)

Maintains water and electrolyte balance. Stimulates reabsorption of Na^+ and water, and excretion of K^+. Regulated by the Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System (RAAS) and blood potassium levels.

Adrenal Medulla (Catecholamines)

Part of the sympathetic nervous system. Secretes Adrenaline (Epinephrine) and Noradrenaline (Norepinephrine). These potentiate the "fight or flight" response:

  • Increased heart rate and blood pressure.
  • Diversion of blood to essential organs (heart, brain, muscles).
  • Increased metabolic rate and pupil dilation.

Response to Stress

Stressors threaten homeostasis. The response involves an immediate sympathetic phase (Adrenal Medulla) and a longer-term resistance phase mediated by ACTH and Cortisol (Adrenal Cortex).

ü•ñ Pancreatic Islets & Glucose Regulation

The Islets of Langerhans contain three main cell types regulating blood glucose (Normal: 2.5‚Äì5.3 mmol/L):

Cell Type Hormone Function
Alpha (\alpha) Glucagon Increases blood glucose via glycogenolysis and gluconeogenesis.
Beta (\beta) Insulin Decreases blood glucose. Promotes storage (glycogenesis, lipogenesis) and cellular uptake of glucose.
Delta (\delta) Somatostatin Inhibits secretion of both insulin and glucagon.

Amylin: Co-secreted with insulin by beta cells. It inhibits glucagon secretion, slows gastric emptying, and promotes satiety. Synthetic amylin is used in Type 2 diabetes treatment.
