üß™ Introduction to Enzymes

Enzymes are proteins that act as biological catalysts by accelerating chemical reactions. The molecules upon which enzymes may act are called substrates, and the enzyme converts the substrates into products. Almost all metabolic processes in the cell need enzyme catalysis in order to occur at rates fast enough to sustain life. Metabolic pathways depend upon enzymes to catalyze individual steps. The study of enzymes is called enzymology. Other biocatalysts are catalytic RNA molecules, called ribozymes. Enzymes that work inside the cells that synthesise them are called intracellular enzymes and those which work outside the cells that synthesize it are extracellular enzymes.

Constitutive enzymes are These enzymes are produced in constant amounts without regard to the physiological demand or the concentration of the substrate. Inducible enzyme one whose production requires or is stimulated by a specific small molecule, the inducer, which is the substrate of the Enzymes or of a substance resembling its substrate. Enzymes can regulate metabolic pathways and these enzymes are regulatory enzymes which are usually allosteric enzymes. In some genetic disorders, there may be a deficiency one or several enzymes (E.g. albinism).

According to IUPAC enzymes have a common name which we use for day to day use like trypsin and a systematic name based on the reaction they catalyse. The systematic name removes confusion and enzymes are classified into 7 classes and an EC: number like EC:2,3,4,1 where the first number is the class, second the subclass, third sub-sub-class and the fourth the serial number of the enzyme.

Properties of Enzymes

  • Enzymes are proteins in nature and they are affected by denaturing agents and inhibitors
  • Catalytic Property- The enzymes have extraordinary catalytic power. Enzymes have extra-ordinary (superfast) catalytic power. They are active in very small quantities therefore A small amount of enzyme is enough to convert a large quantity of substrates. Enzymes accelerate reactions up to 10^{14} to 10^{20} times. Turn over number: The number of substrate molecules converted by one molecule of enzyme per second when its active site is saturated with the substrate.
  • They function in an aqueous solution The enzymes remain unchanged after the reaction so can bind to other substrates
  • Catalysts are not required in stoichiometric quantities
  • Enzymes have a high degree of specificity for their substrates and reactions. They can be absolute specificity where it acts on only 1 substrate, group specificity- the enzyme works on a group of related substrates, stereospecificity- acts on a specific stereoisomer and reaction specificity- catalyses specific reaction
  • Enzymes do not change the equilibrium of reaction
  • Most of the enzymes catalyzed reactions are reversible. The reversibility of the reaction depends upon the requirements of the cell. In some cases, there are separate enzymes for forward and reverse reaction

üß© Structure & Mode of Action

Structures of enzymes

Enzymes are globular proteins and they may be tertiary or quaternary protein. They are larger than their substrate and the substrate binds to a specific site called the active site. The active site has a substrate binding site and the catalytic site. The active site is a hydrophobic cleft on the enzyme lined by amino acids required for substrate binding and catalysis. The active site has specific symmetry that is responsible for its specificity. Even though most of the enzymes contain thousands of amino acids only 2-4 amino acids are directly involved in the catalysis.

Allosteric site: Additional sites other than the active site for the binding of allosteric modulators. Allosteric modulators are involved in the regulation of enzymatic activity and can be activators or inhibitors.

Simple enzymes contain amino acids only in their active form while complex proteins contain a cofactor to be active. The protein part alone (apoenzyme) is inactive and combined to the cofactor forms the holoenzyme (active).

Cofactors

Cofactors can be metal ions like Mg2+ which stabilize the protein structure of involved in electron transfer and coenzymes which are small organic compounds mainly derived from water soluble vitamins. The body converts them to coenzymes e.g. folic acid is converted to tetrahydrofolate (THF). They are involved to electron transfer or transfer of functional group.

Mode of action of enzymes

the enzyme must bind to the substrate at the active site to form an enzyme substrate complex before it is converted to product. Enzymes find an alternative pathway in converting substrate to product. Enzyme reduces the activation energy of the reaction. The minimum energy that must be input to a chemical system, containing potential reactants, in order for a chemical reaction to occur. For a chemical reaction to proceed at a reasonable rate, there should exist an appreciable number of molecules with energy equal to or greater than the activation energy. The activation energy of a reaction is denoted as Ea.

Temperature

enzymes work best at the optimum temperature. Low temperatures below optimum deactivate the enzyme and high temperatures above optimum denature the enzymes. In humans the optimum temperature is close to normal body temperature. Enzymes are very sensitive to heat and temperature. (They are thermo-labile.). At higher temperature (60-70¬∞C), the enzyme is destroyed or denatured. Do you know an enzyme active at very high temperature? It is Taq-Polymerase used in PCR reactions. The optimum temperature for Taq-polymerase is 75 to 80¬∞C.

pH

Some enzymes are active in acidic pH (pH below 7), some are active in basic pH (pH above 7) and some others are in neutral pH depending at their site of action. Although some enzymes exhibit activity in a broad range of pH, many show maximal activity at a narrow pH range [4‚Äì6], referred to as the pH optimum

Inhibitors

it is a substance that slows down or blocks the catalytic activity of an enzyme. inhibitors are molecules that decrease enzyme activity, and activators are molecules that increase activity. Many therapeutic drugs and poisons are enzyme inhibitors. Inhibitors can bind reversibly of irreversibly.

  • Irreversible inhibitors bind covalently to the enzyme and destroy it.
  • Reversible inhibitors bind reversibly with the enzyme. They include:
    • Competitive inhibitors which compete with the natural substrate of the enzyme for the active site and binds only to the free enzyme. It can be overcome by increasing concentration of the substrate. Many drugs are competitive inhibitors e.g. allopurinol acts as a competitive inhibitor of xanthine oxidase.
    • Non-competitive inhibitors- the inhibitor binds at an allosteric site separate from the active site of substrate binding and prevent the conversion of substrate to product. They bind to free enzyme or the enzyme substrate complex.

üéõÔ∏è Enzyme Regulation

Feedback inhibition

Feedback inhibition is a specific type of allosteric enzymatic activity regulation mechanism in cells. In some multi-enzyme pathways, the regulatory enzyme is specifically inhibited by the end product of the pathway whenever the concentration of the end product exceeds the cell's requirements. This type of regulation by the end product inhibiting to the first enzyme of the multi-enzymatic pathway is called feedback inhibition. Example of allosteric feedback inhibition is the biosynthesis of L-isoleucine from L-threonine in bacteria

Feedback regulation

A type of enzymatic activity regulation. Here the end product of an enzymatic pathway directly inhibit the synthesis of concerned enzyme by interfering with the gene of that enzyme. Enzyme is not directly inhibited by the end product. End product reduces the concentration of enzyme by inhibiting the synthesis of new enzyme molecules. Best example is the reduction of HMG CoA reductase enzyme by dietary cholesterol which is the key rate limiting enzyme in cholesterol synthesis.

Isoenzymes

Isoenzymes (also known as isozymes) are enzymes that differ in amino acid sequence but catalyze the same chemical reaction. Believed to be originating from closely linked genes or from multiple gene loci. Evolution from a single form possibly due to long-term mutations. They vary with respect to their kinetic parameters, electrophoretic mobility, and localization. They all have independent action. E.g. Lactate dehydrogenase have 5 isoenzymes (LDH1, LDH2, LDH3, LDH4 & LDH5). They can be used to identify the specific affected tissues. They can be differentiated from each other and can be clinically quantified in the lab. Phosphodiesterase enzymes are found in a number of isoenzymes inhibited by different substances and found in different tissues in the body.
