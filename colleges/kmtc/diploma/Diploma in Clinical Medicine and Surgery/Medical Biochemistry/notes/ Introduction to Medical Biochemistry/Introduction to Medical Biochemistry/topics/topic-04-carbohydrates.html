üìñ Introduction

A carbohydrate is a biomolecule consisting of carbon (C), hydrogen (H) and oxygen (O) atoms, usually with a hydrogen : oxygen atom ratio of 2:1 (as in water) and thus with the empirical formula C_m(H_2O)_n, where m may or may not be different from n.

However, not all carbohydrates conform to this precise stoichiometric definition (e.g., uronic acids, deoxy-sugars such as fucose), nor are all chemicals that do conform to this definition automatically classified as carbohydrates (e.g. formaldehyde and acetic acid).

The term is most common in biochemistry, where it is a synonym of saccharide, a group that includes sugars, starch, and cellulose. The saccharides are divided into four chemical groups: monosaccharides, disaccharides, oligosaccharides, and polysaccharides.

Monosaccharides and disaccharides, the smallest (lower molecular weight) carbohydrates, are commonly referred to as sugars. The word saccharide comes from the Greek word (s√°kharon), meaning "sugar".

Carbohydrates are central to nutrition and are found in a wide variety of natural and processed foods. Starch is a polysaccharide. It is abundant in cereals (wheat, maize, and rice), potatoes, and processed food based on cereal flour, such as bread, pizza or pasta.

Sugars appear in human diet mainly as table sugar (sucrose, extracted from sugarcane or sugar beets), lactose (abundant in milk), glucose and fructose, both of which occur naturally in honey, many fruits, and some vegetables.

üóÇÔ∏è Classification of Carbohydrates

1. Monosaccharides

Are the simplest groups of carbohydrates and often called simple sugars since they cannot be further hydrolyzed. They are colourless, crystalline solid which are soluble in water and insoluble in a non-polar solvent.

These are compound which possesses a free aldehyde or ketone group. Their general formula is C_n(H_2O)_n or C_nH_{2n}O_n.

They are classified according to three different characteristics:

Placement of Carbonyl Group

If the carbonyl group is an aldehyde, the monosaccharide is an aldose. If the carbonyl group is a ketone, the monosaccharide is a ketose.

Number of Carbon Atoms

Trioses (3), Tetroses (4), Pentoses (5), Hexoses (6), Heptoses (7). Example: Glucose is an aldohexose.

Chiral Handedness

D or L isomers based on the orientation of the asymmetric carbon furthest from the carbonyl group.

Common Monosaccharides:

Monosaccharides can exist as a linear chain or as ring-shaped molecules. In aqueous solutions they are usually found in ring forms. Glucose in a ring form can have two different arrangements of the hydroxyl group (‚ÄìOH) around the anomeric carbon:

  • Alpha (Œ±) position: If the hydroxyl group is below carbon number 1.
  • Beta (Œ≤) position: If the hydroxyl group is above the plane.

2. Oligosaccharides

Oligosaccharides are compound sugars that yield 2 to 10 molecules of the same or different monosaccharides on hydrolysis. The monosaccharide units are joined by glycosidic linkage.

Disaccharides

Disaccharides form when two monosaccharides undergo a dehydration reaction. Common disaccharides include:

  • Lactose: Glucose + Galactose (found in milk).
  • Maltose: Glucose + Glucose (malt sugar).
  • Sucrose: Glucose + Fructose (table sugar).

3. Polysaccharides

Also called glycanes. They contain more than 10 monosaccharide units and can be hundreds of sugar units in length. They are primarily concerned with structural function and the storage of energy.

Types of Polysaccharides

  • Homopolysaccharides: Contain monosaccharides of the same type (e.g., starch, glycogen, cellulose, pectin).
  • Heteropolysaccharides: Contain monosaccharides of different types (e.g., Hyaluronic acid, Chondroitin).

Key Polysaccharides:

  • Starch: The stored form of sugars in plants. Made of a mixture of amylose (unbranched, Œ± 1-4 linkages) and amylopectin (branched, Œ± 1-6 linkages at branch points).
  • Glycogen: The storage form of glucose in humans and vertebrates. Highly branched molecule usually stored in liver and muscle cells.
  • Cellulose: The most abundant natural biopolymer. Found in plant cell walls. Made of glucose monomers linked by Œ≤ 1-4 glycosidic bonds.
  • Chitin: Found in arthropod exoskeletons and fungal cell walls. Made of repeating units of N-acetyl-Œ≤-d-glucosamine.

‚ö° Functions of Carbohydrates

  1. Living organisms use carbohydrates as accessible energy to fuel cellular reactions. They are the most abundant dietary source of energy (4kcal/gram) for all living beings.
  2. Carbohydrates along with being the chief energy source, in many animals, are instant sources of energy. Glucose is broken down by glycolysis/ Kreb's cycle to yield ATP.
  3. Serve as energy stores, fuels, and metabolic intermediates. It is stored as glycogen in animals and starch in plants.
  4. They form structural and protective components, like in the cell wall of plants and microorganisms. Structural elements in the cell walls of bacteria (peptidoglycan or murein), plants (cellulose) and animals (chitin).
  5. Carbohydrates are intermediates in the biosynthesis of fats and proteins.
  6. Carbohydrates aid in the regulation of nerve tissue and is the energy source for the brain
  7. Carbohydrates get associated with lipids and proteins to form surface antigens, receptor molecules, vitamins, and antibiotics.
  8. Formation of the structural framework of RNA and DNA (ribonucleic acid and deoxyribonucleic acid).
  9. They are linked to many proteins and lipids. Such linked carbohydrates are important in cell-cell communication and in interactions between cells and other elements in the cellular environment.
  10. In animals, they are an important constituent of connective tissues.
  11. Carbohydrates those are rich in fiber content help to prevent constipation.
  12. Also, they help in the modulation of the immune system.

‚öóÔ∏è Properties of Carbohydrates

Physical Properties

  1. Stereoisomerism: Compounds sharing the same structural formula but they differ in spatial configuration. Example: Glucose has two isomers with respect to the penultimate carbon atom. They are D-glucose and L-glucose.
  2. Optical Activity: It is the rotation of plane-polarized light forming (+) glucose and (-) glucose.
  3. Diastereo isomers: It the configurational changes with regard to C2, C3, or C4 in glucose. Example: Mannose, galactose.
  4. Annomerism: It is the spatial configuration with respect to the first carbon atom in aldoses and second carbon atom in ketoses.

Chemical Properties

  1. Osazone formation: Osazone are carbohydrate derivatives when sugars are reacted with an excess of phenyl hydrazine. e.g. Glucosazone
  2. Benedict's test: Reducing sugars when heated in the presence of an alkali gets converted to powerful reducing species known as enediols. When Benedict's reagent solution and reducing sugars are heated together, the solution changes its color to orange-red/ brick red.
  3. Oxidation: Monosaccharides are reducing sugars; their carbonyl groups oxidize to give carboxylic acids. In Benedict's test, D-glucose is oxidized to D-gluconic acid, thus, glucose is considered a reducing sugar.
  4. Reduction to alcohols: The carbonyl (C=O) groups in open-chain forms of carbohydrates can be reduced to alcohols by sodium borohydride, NaBH‚ÇÑ, or catalytic hydrogenation
