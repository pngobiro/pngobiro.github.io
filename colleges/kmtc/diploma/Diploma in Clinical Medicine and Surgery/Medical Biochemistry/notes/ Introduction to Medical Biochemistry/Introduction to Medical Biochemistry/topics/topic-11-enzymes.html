üìò Introduction

Enzymes are proteins substances that acts as a catalyst in living organisms, regulating the rate at which chemical reactions proceed without itself being altered in the process.

Enzymes can be defined as biological polymers that catalyze biochemical reactions.

üß¨

Ribozymes

The macromolecular components of all enzymes consist of protein, except in the class of RNA catalysts called ribozymes. The word ribozyme is derived from the ribonucleic acid enzyme.

Metabolic processes and other chemical reactions in the cell are carried out by a set of enzymes that are necessary to sustain life. The initial stage of metabolic process depends upon the enzymes, which react with a molecule and is called the substrate. Enzymes convert the substrates into other distinct molecules and are called the products.

Enzymes are found in all tissues and fluids of the body. Catalysis of all reactions taking place in metabolic pathways are carried out by intracellular enzymes. The enzymes in plasma membrane govern the catalysis in the cells as a response to cellular signals and enzymes in the circulatory system regulate clotting of blood.

üóÇÔ∏è Enzymes Classification

To ease the confusion surrounding enzyme nomenclature, a classification system has been developed based on the type of reaction the enzyme catalyzes. There are six principal categories:

‚ñ∂ 1. Oxidoreductases

These catalyze oxidation and reduction reactions where electrons tend to travel from one form of a molecule to the other.

Example: Pyruvate dehydrogenase, catalyzing the oxidation of pyruvate to acetyl coenzyme A.

‚ñ∂ 2. Transferases

These catalyze transferring of the chemical group from one to another compound.

Example: Transaminase, which transfers an amino group from one molecule to another.

‚ñ∂ 3. Hydrolases

These catalyze the hydrolysis of a bond by adding water to cleave the bond.

Example: Pepsin hydrolyzes peptide bonds in proteins.

‚ñ∂ 4. Lyases

These catalyze the breakage of bonds without catalysis (without hydrolysis or oxidation), often forming double bonds.

Example: Aldolase catalyzes the splitting of fructose-1, 6-bisphosphate.

‚ñ∂ 5. Isomerases

They catalyze the formation of an isomer of a compound (structural shifts present in a molecule).

Example: Phosphoglucomutase catalyzes the conversion of glucose-1-phosphate to glucose-6-phosphate.

‚ñ∂ 6. Ligases (Synthetases)

Ligases catalyze the association of two molecules coupled with the breakdown of ATP.

Example: DNA ligase catalyzes the joining of two fragments of DNA by forming a phosphodiester bond.

‚öôÔ∏è Mechanism & Cofactors

Cofactors

Cofactors are non-proteinous substances that associate with enzymes. A cofactor is essential for the functioning of an enzyme. An enzyme without a cofactor is called an apoenzyme. An enzyme and its cofactor together constitute the holoenzyme.

  • Prosthetic groups: Cofactors tightly bound to an enzyme at all times (e.g., FAD).
  • Coenzyme: Binds to an enzyme only during catalysis (e.g., NAD+).
  • Metal ions: Required at the active site to form coordinate bonds (e.g., Zn2+, Mg2+).

Mechanism of Enzyme Action

In most chemical reactions, an energy barrier exists that must be overcome for the reaction to occur. This barrier prevents complex molecules such as proteins and nucleic acids from spontaneously degrading.

Enzymes lower the activation energy level through the use of a catalyst. They react with the substrate to form an intermediate complex (a transition state) that requires less energy for the reaction to proceed.

Only a certain region of the enzyme, called the active site, binds to the substrate. The active site is a groove or pocket formed by the folding pattern of the protein. This three-dimensional structure permits only a particular substrate to bind to the site, determining the enzyme's specificity.

üå°Ô∏è Factors Affecting Activity

1. Concentration of Enzyme

As the concentration of the enzyme is increased, the velocity of the reaction proportionately increases.

2. Concentration of Substrate

The rate of enzymatic reaction increases as the substrate concentration increases until a limiting rate is reached (saturation), after which further increase produces no significant change.

3. Effect of Temperature

Enzyme activity occurs within a narrow range of temperatures. The point of maximum activity is called the optimal temperature (37 to 40¬∞C). Heating beyond this leads to denaturation and loss of activity.

4. Effect of pH

Each enzyme has a pH value that it works at with maximum efficiency called the optimal pH. Most enzymes work at neutral pH 7.4, but pepsin works at low pH (acidic) and trypsin at high pH (basic).

5. Effect of Activators

Some enzymes require inorganic metallic cations (Mg2+, Mn2+, Zn2+, etc.) for optimum activity.

ü©∏ Hemoglobin & Myoglobin

Myoglobin

Myoglobin is a monomeric protein found in muscles that binds oxygen with its heme group. It acts as an oxygen reservoir within muscle tissues. It has a single heme group containing an iron atom.

Hemoglobin

Hemoglobin is an iron-containing protein in red blood cells that transports oxygen to tissues. It consists of four protein chains (\alpha_1, \alpha_2, \beta_1, \beta_2), each attached to a heme group. It is a tetramer capable of binding four oxygen molecules.

üåà Porphyrins & Bile Pigments

Porphyrins

Porphyrins are intermediate molecules in the biosynthetic pathway of heme. Disorders of heme biosynthesis are called porphyrias. They present with neurological symptoms, abdominal pain, and dark/red urine. Porphyrias can be exacerbated by hormonal changes, alcohol, or drugs.

Bile Pigments

When old red blood cells disintegrate, hemoglobin is degraded. The iron is stored (ferritin/hemosiderin), protein breaks down into amino acids, and the heme is converted into pigments:

  • Biliverdin: Green pigment formed first from heme oxidation.
  • Bilirubin: Yellow pigment formed by the reduction of biliverdin.
