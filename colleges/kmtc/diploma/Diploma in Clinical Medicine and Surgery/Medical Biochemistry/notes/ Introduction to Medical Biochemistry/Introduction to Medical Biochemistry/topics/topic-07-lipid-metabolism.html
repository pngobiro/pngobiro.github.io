Lipid Metabolism

üîÑ Overview

Lipid metabolism is the synthesis and degradation of lipids in cells, involving the breakdown or storage of fats for energy and the synthesis of structural and functional lipids, such as those involved in the construction of cell membranes.

üí°

Lipogenesis

Lipogenesis is the process of synthesizing fats. The majority of lipids found in the human body from ingesting food are triglycerides and cholesterol.

Lipid metabolism is often considered as the digestion and absorption process of dietary fat; however, there are two sources of fats that organisms can use to obtain energy: from consumed dietary fats and from stored fat. Vertebrates (including humans) use both sources of fat to produce energy for organs such as the heart to function.

Since lipids are hydrophobic molecules, they need to be solubilized before their metabolism can begin. Lipid metabolism often begins with hydrolysis, which occurs with the help of various enzymes in the digestive system.

üçΩÔ∏è Digestion & Absorption

Lipid Digestion

Digestion is the first step to lipid metabolism, and it is the process of breaking the triglycerides down into smaller monoglycerides units with the help of lipase enzymes and bile salts.

  1. Mouth: Digestion of fats begins in the mouth through chemical digestion by lingual lipase.
  2. Stomach: Chemical digestion continues by gastric lipase and mechanical digestion begins (peristalsis).
  3. Small Intestine: The majority of lipid digestion occurs here. Chemicals from the pancreas (pancreatic lipase family and bile salt-dependent lipase) are secreted to help breakdown the triglycerides.

It is the pancreatic lipase that is responsible for signaling for the hydrolysis of the triglycerides into separate free fatty acids and glycerol units. Ingested cholesterol is not broken down by the lipases and stays intact until it enters the epithelium cells of small intestine.

Lipid Absorption

Short chain fatty acids can be absorbed in the stomach, while most absorption of fats occurs only in the small intestines.

  • Micelle Formation: Once broken down, fatty acids, glycerols, and cholesterol aggregate into structures called micelles.
  • Diffusion: Fatty acids and monoglycerides leave the micelles and diffuse across the membrane to enter the intestinal epithelial cells.
  • Recombination: In the cytosol of epithelial cells, fatty acids and monoglycerides are recombined back into triglycerides.
  • Chylomicrons: Triglycerides and cholesterol are packaged into bigger particles called chylomicrons which are amphipathic structures that transport digested lipids.
03

üöö Lipid Transportation

Due to the hydrophobic nature of membrane lipids, triglycerides and cholesterols, they require special transport proteins known as lipoproteins. The amphipathic structure of lipoproteins allows the triglycerols and cholesterol to be transported through the blood.

Lipoprotein Function
Chylomicrons Carry digested lipids from small intestine to the rest of the body.
VLDL (Very-Low-Density Lipoproteins) Carry synthesized triglycerides by our body.
LDL (Low-Density Lipoproteins) Transport cholesterol to peripheral tissues.

Triglycerides are specifically transported through the blood to appropriate tissues (adipose, muscle, etc.) by lipoproteins such as VLDL. Triglycerides present on the VLDL undergo lipolysis by the cellular lipases of target tissues, which yields glycerol and free fatty acids. Free fatty acids released into the blood are then available for cellular uptake.

Free fatty acids not immediately taken up by cells may bind to albumin for transport to surrounding tissues that require energy. Serum albumin is the major carrier of free fatty acids in the blood.

04

üî• Lipolysis

This is the metabolic pathway through which lipid triglycerides are hydrolyzed into a glycerol and three fatty acids. It is used to mobilize stored energy during fasting or exercise, and usually occurs in fat adipocytes.

‚öôÔ∏è

Regulation

The most important regulatory hormone in lipolysis is insulin; lipolysis can only occur when insulin action falls to low levels, as occurs during fasting. Other hormones that affect lipolysis include glucagon, epinephrine, norepinephrine, growth hormone, and cortisol.

Mechanism

In the body, stores of fat are referred to as adipose tissue. Intracellular triglycerides are stored in cytoplasmic lipid droplets. Each step of hydrolysis leads to the removal of one fatty acid.

  1. 1 Adipose triglyceride lipase (ATGL): Carries out the first and rate-limiting step, catalyzing the hydrolysis of triacylglycerol to diacylglycerol.
  2. 2 Hormone-sensitive lipase (HSL): Catalyzes the hydrolysis of diacylglycerol to monoacylglycerol.
  3. 3 Monoacylglycerol lipase (MGL): Catalyzes the hydrolysis of monoacylglycerol to glycerol.

While lipolysis is triglyceride hydrolysis (breakdown), esterification is the process by which triglycerides are formed. They are essentially reversals of one another.

‚ö†Ô∏è Disorders of Lipid Metabolism

Lipid metabolism disorders are illnesses where trouble occurs in breaking down or synthesizing fats. These disorders are associated with an increase in the concentrations of plasma lipids in the blood such as LDL cholesterol, VLDL, and triglycerides which most commonly lead to cardiovascular diseases.

As a result of such defects, lipids may become deposited in the walls of blood vessels, which can lead to atherosclerosis (a disease characterized by abnormal thickening and hardening of the walls of the arteries).

üß¨

Familial Hypercholesterolemia

An autosomal dominant disease caused by the deficiency of the LDL receptor on the surface of cells in the liver and other organs. As a result, cholesterol is not moved into the cells. The disease is characterized by early coronary vascular disease, strokes, and fatty deposits on the tendons. Blood cholesterol levels are very high from birth.

üìã Lipids Involved in Metabolism

Membrane Lipids

  • Phospholipids: Major component of the lipid bilayer of the cell membrane.
  • Sphingolipids: Mostly found in the cell membrane of neural tissue.
  • Glycolipids: Facilitate cell recognition and maintain lipid bilayer stability.
  • Glycerophospholipids: Neural tissue (including the brain) contains high amounts.

Other Types

Cholesterols

Main precursors for hormones (progesterone, testosterone) and control cell membrane fluidity.

Steroids

Important cell signalling molecules.

Triacylglycerols

Major form of energy storage in human body.

Fatty Acids

Precursors for lipid membrane and cholesterol biosynthesis; used for energy.

Bile Salts

Secreted from liver; facilitate lipid digestion in the small intestine.

Eicosanoids

Made from fatty acids; used for cell signalling.

Ketone Bodies

Made from fatty acids in the liver; produce energy during starvation.
