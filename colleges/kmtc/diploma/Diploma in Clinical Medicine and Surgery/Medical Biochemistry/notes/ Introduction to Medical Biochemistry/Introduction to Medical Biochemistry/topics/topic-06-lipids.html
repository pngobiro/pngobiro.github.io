üß¨ Introduction

Lipids are essential components of all living organisms. A fat molecule consists of two main components - glycerol and fatty acids.

  • Glycerol is an organic compound (alcohol) with three carbons, five hydrogens, and three hydroxyl (OH) groups.
  • Fatty acids have a long chain of hydrocarbons to which a carboxyl group is attached, hence the name fatty acid. The number of carbons in the fatty acid may range from 4 to 36. The most common are those containing 12 to 18 carbons.

In a fat molecule, the fatty acids attach to each of the glycerol molecule's three carbons with an ester bond through an oxygen atom. Joining three fatty acids to a glycerol backbone in a dehydration reaction, forms triacylglycerol (triglyceride). The three fatty acids in the triacylglycerol may be similar or dissimilar.

Lipids are structurally and functionally diverse group of hydrocarbons. Hydrocarbons are chemical compounds that consist of carbon and hydrogen atoms. The carbon-carbon and carbon-hydrogen bonds are nonpolar, which means that the electrons between the atoms are shared equally. The individual nonpolar bonds impart an overall nonpolar characteristic to the hydrocarbon compound. Additionally, nonpolar compounds are hydrophobic, or water-hating. This means they do not form hydrogen bonds with water molecules, rendering them nearly insoluble in water.

Solubility

Lipids have the common property of being relatively insoluble in water and soluble in nonpolar solvents such as ether and chloroform.

Saturated vs. Unsaturated Fats

Fats are either saturated or unsaturated depending on the presence or absence of double bonds in the hydrocarbon chains of their fatty acids.

Saturated Fatty Acids

If a fatty acid chain does not have double bonds between the carbon atoms, the individual carbon atoms bind a maximum number of hydrogen. Such a fatty acid is completely saturated with hydrogen. The flexible fatty acid chains can pack tightly with each other; hence saturated fats are mostly solid at room temperature. Examples: butter, milk, cheese, lard.

Unsaturated Fatty Acids

If the fatty acid contains one or more double bonded carbon atoms, it is called an unsaturated fatty acid. Most naturally occurring unsaturated fatty acids are in cis conformation. The presence of cis-double bonds causes a bend in the hydrocarbon chain which makes the long hydrocarbon chain less flexible and difficult to pack. As a consequence, most unsaturated fatty acids are liquid at room temperature. Examples: olive oil, peanut oil, cod liver oil.

üìä Main Types of Lipids

The three main types of lipids are triacylglycerols (also called triglycerides), phospholipids, and sterols.

1. Triacylglycerols

Also known as triglycerides. Make up more than 95 percent of lipids in the diet. Commonly found in fried foods, vegetable oil, butter, whole milk, cheese, cream cheese, and some meats. Naturally occurring triacylglycerols are found in avocados, olives, corn, and nuts.

2. Phospholipids

Make up only about 2 percent of dietary lipids. They are water-soluble and are found in both plants and animals. Crucial for building the protective barrier, or membrane, around the body's cells. In blood and body fluids, they form structures in which fat is enclosed and transported.

3. Sterols

Least common type of lipid. Cholesterol is the best well-known sterol. The body gets only a small amount through food‚Äîthe body produces most of it. Cholesterol is an important component of the cell membrane and is required for the synthesis of sex hormones, vitamin D, and bile salts.

üèóÔ∏è Structural Characteristics

Lipids are a diverse group of biological substances made up primarily or exclusively of nonpolar groups. They are grouped together on the basis of solubility in organic or non-polar solvents. They are insoluble in water (or polar solvent) and vary greatly in structure and function.

Lipids have larger nonpolar alkyl groups and are insoluble in water. As the size of an alkyl group increases in an organic compound, the water solubility of the compound decreases. As a result of their nonpolar character, lipids typically dissolve more readily in nonpolar solvents such as acetone, ether, chloroform, and benzene, than in water.

This solubility characteristic is of extreme importance in cells because lipids tend to associate into nonpolar groups and barriers, as in the cell membranes that form boundaries between and within cells.

‚öóÔ∏è Properties of Lipids

  • Lipids may be either liquids or non-crystalline solids at room temperature.
  • Pure fats and oils are colourless, odourless, and tasteless.
  • They are energy-rich organic molecules
  • Insoluble in water
  • Soluble in organic solvents like alcohol, chloroform, acetone, benzene, etc.
  • No ionic charges
  • Solid triglycerides (Fats) have high proportions of saturated fatty acids.
  • Liquid triglycerides (Oils) have high proportions of unsaturated fatty acids.

‚öôÔ∏è Functions of Lipids in the Body

‚ñ∂ 1. Storing Energy

The excess energy from the food we eat is digested and incorporated into adipose tissue, or fatty tissue. Most of the energy required by the human body is provided by carbohydrates and lipids. While glycogen provides a ready source of energy, lipids primarily function as an energy reserve.

Fats are packed together tightly without water and store far greater amounts of energy in a reduced space. A fat gram is densely concentrated with energy; it contains more than double the amount of energy than a gram of carbohydrate.

‚ñ∂ 2. Regulating and Signaling

Triacylglycerols control the body's internal climate, maintaining a constant temperature. Triacylglycerols also help the body produce and regulate hormones. For example, adipose tissue secretes the hormone leptin, which regulates appetite.

Fats also play important functional roles in sustaining nerve impulse transmission, memory storage, and tissue structure. Lipids serve as signaling molecules; they are catalysts of electrical impulse activity within the brain.

‚ñ∂ 3. Insulating and Protecting

Up to 30 percent of body weight is comprised of fat tissue. Vital organs such as the heart, kidneys, and liver are protected by visceral fat. Subcutaneous fat, or fat underneath the skin, insulates the body from extreme temperatures and pads our hands and buttocks preventing friction.

‚ñ∂ 4. Aiding Digestion and Increasing Bioavailability

Dietary fats carry fat-soluble nutrients (Vitamins A, D, E, and K) through the digestive process, improving intestinal absorption. Fats also increase the bioavailability of compounds known as phytochemicals.

‚ñ∂ 5. High Energy Source

Fat-rich foods naturally have a high caloric density. 1 gram of fat or oil provides 9 kilocalories of energy, compared with 4 kilocalories found in 1 gram of carbohydrate or protein.

üóÇÔ∏è Classification of Lipids

Lipids are commonly subdivided into four main groups:

1. Fatty acids (saturated and unsaturated)

Fatty acids are long-chain monocarboxylic acids. As a consequence of their biosynthesis, fatty acids generally contain an even number of carbon atoms. The general formula for a saturated fatty acid is CH_3(CH_2)_nCOOH, in which n in biological systems is an even integer.

2. Glycerides (glycerol-containing lipids)

Glycerides are lipid esters that contain the glycerol molecule and fatty acids. They may be subdivided into two classes: neutral glycerides and phosphoglycerides.

  • Neutral glycerides: Produced by the esterification of glycerol with a fatty acid (monoglycerides, diglycerides, or triglycerides). Triglycerides are the major component of fat cells.
  • Phosphoglycerides: Have a polar region (the phosphoryl group) in addition to the nonpolar fatty acid tails. The most abundant membrane lipids are derived from glycerol-3-phosphate.

3. Nonglyceride lipids

  • Sphingolipids: Not derived from glycerol. Amphipathic, structural components of cellular membranes.
  • Steroids: Members of a large, diverse collection of lipids called the isoprenoids. Built from one or more five-carbon units called isoprene.
  • Waxes: Generally composed of a long-chain fatty acid esterified to a long-chain alcohol. Extremely hydrophobic and solid at room temperature.

4. Complex lipids (lipoproteins)

Complex lipids are lipids that are bonded to other types of molecules. The most common and important complex lipids are plasma lipoproteins, which are responsible for the transport of other lipids in the body.

Lipoproteins are classified based on their density:

  1. Chylomicrons
  2. Very low-density lipoproteins (VLDL)
  3. Intermediate density lipoproteins (IDL)
  4. Low-density lipoproteins (LDL) ‚áî bad cholesterol‚Üë
  5. High-density lipoproteins (HDL) ‚áî good cholesterol‚Üë
