üìò Introduction

Vitamins are organic compounds required by the body in small amounts for metabolism (B complex), for protection, for maintenance of health and proper growth.

They cannot be synthesized by the body, therefore they must be obtained from outside sources like diet, rumen of bacteria & sun.

Vitamins also assist in the formation of hormones, blood vessels, nervous system chemicals and genetic materials. Some generally act as catalysts, combining with proteins to create metabolically active enzymes that are essential for life reactions.

Classification of Vitamins

Vitamins are classified according to their ability to be absorbed in fat or water.

1. Fat Soluble Vitamins

These are oily and hydrophobic compounds; they are stored in the liver and not excreted out of the body. Bile salts and fats are needed for their absorption. Vitamins A, D, E and K are fat soluble (ADEK).

The fat/lipid soluble vitamins are not as easily eliminated and may accumulate to toxic levels if consumed in large quantity.

2. Water Soluble Vitamins

Vitamin B complex and Vitamin C are water soluble. They are not stored in the body; therefore, they are required daily in small amounts.

Water soluble vitamins, such as vitamin C, are rapidly eliminated from the body and their dietary levels need to be relatively high.

‚ñ∂ Vitamin B1 (Thiamine)

Source: Whole grains; breads; cereals.

Function: Thiamine acts as a precursor molecule for Thiamine pyrophosphate (TPP), which is a coenzyme for enzymes involved in carbohydrate and amino acid metabolism (e.g., pyruvate dehydrogenase, transketolase).

Deficiency

Beriberi: Affects cardiovascular (wet beriberi) and nervous systems (dry beriberi).

Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome: Linked to alcohol excess and thiamine-deficient diet.

‚ñ∂ Vitamin B2 (Riboflavin)

Source: Bread; cereals; milk.

Function: Precursor for flavin adenine dinucleotide (FAD) and flavin mononucleotide (FMN). Primarily involved in energy production and helps vision and skin health.

Deficiency (Ariboflavinosis)

Symptoms include dermatitis, glossitis (inflammation of the tongue), corneal vascularization, angular cheilitis (cracking at corners of lips), weakness, and anaemia.

‚ñ∂ Vitamin B3 (Niacin)

Source: Chicken; fish; whole grains; cereals. Can be synthesized from tryptophan.

Function: Precursor for coenzymes NAD and NADP, which act as electron acceptors in oxidation-reduction reactions (Citric acid cycle, electron transport chain).

Deficiency (Pellagra)

Characterized by the three Ds: Dementia, Diarrhoea, and Dermatitis. Can lead to death if untreated.

‚ñ∂ Vitamin B5 (Pantothenic Acid)

Source: Chicken; beef; cereals; potatoes.

Function: Constituent of coenzyme A and fatty acid synthase. Needed to metabolize carbohydrates, proteins, fats and alcohol.

‚ñ∂ Vitamin B6 (Pyridoxine)

Source: Cereal grains, legumes, vegetables, liver, fruit.

Function: Precursor for pyridoxal phosphate (PLP). Acts as a carrier of amine groups during transamination reactions in amino acid breakdown and heme synthesis.

üíä

Deficiency Causes

Dietary malnutrition, alcoholism, pregnancy, or pharmacologic agents like isoniazid (tuberculosis treatment) or oral contraceptives.

‚ñ∂ Vitamin B7 (Biotin)

Source: Eggs, milk, bananas. Also synthesized by intestinal flora.

Function: Cofactor for carboxylase enzymes (Pyruvate carboxylase, Acetyl CoA carboxylase). Needed for energy metabolism and fat synthesis.

Deficiency: Associated with long-term antibiotic use or increased ingestion of raw egg whites (avidin).

‚ñ∂ Vitamin B9 (Folic Acid)

Source: Dark green leafy vegetables, liver, lentils.

Function: Needed for DNA synthesis, cell growth, and red blood cell formation.

ü§∞

Pregnancy Note

Crucial to reduce the risks of neural tube defects such as spina bifida in the baby. Deficiency can also cause megaloblastic anemia.

‚ñ∂ Vitamin B12 (Cobalamin)

Source: Liver, fruits, meat. (Mainly animal sources).

Function: Cofactor in methionine metabolism (homocysteine to methionine) and propionyl CoA metabolism. Maintains myelin surrounding nerve cells.

Absorption: Requires intrinsic factor secreted by gastric parietal cells. Absorbed in distal ileum.

ü©∏

Deficiency

Causes Pernicious Anaemia. Can result from lack of intrinsic factor, gastrectomy, or strict vegan diet.

‚ñ∂ Vitamin C (Ascorbic Acid)

Source: Citrus fruits, peppers, broccoli.

Function: Facilitates collagen synthesis (hydroxylation of proline and lysine), antioxidant properties, and increases iron absorption.

ü¶¥

Deficiency (Scurvy)

Symptoms include weakness, anemia, gum disease, and skin problems due to defective collagen synthesis.

üßà Fat Soluble Vitamins

Vitamin A (Retinol)

Function: Vision (adjusts to light changes), bone growth, reproduction, cell division, and immune system regulation.

Source: Dairy, fish, liver. Beta-carotene (precursor) in carrots, pumpkin, dark green leafy vegetables.

üëÅÔ∏è

Deficiency & Toxicity

Deficiency: Xerophthalmia, night blindness.

Toxicity: Dry skin, headache, nausea, birth defects.

Vitamin D (Calciferol)

Comes in two forms: D2 (ergocalciferol) from plants, and D3 (cholecalciferol) from animal sources and sunlight synthesis.

Function: Critical role in the body's use of calcium and phosphorous. Increases calcium absorption from the small intestine.

ü¶¥

Deficiency

Children: Rickets (long, soft bowed legs).

Adults: Osteomalacia and Osteoporosis.

Vitamin E (Tocopherol)

Function: Acts as an antioxidant, protecting vitamins A and C, red blood cells, and essential fatty acids from destruction.

Source: Vegetable oils (soybean, corn), nuts, seeds.

Deficiency: Rare. Usually occurs in premature infants or those unable to absorb fats.

Vitamin K

Function: Essential role in normal blood clotting and bone health.

Source: Green leafy vegetables (spinach, cabbage). Also produced by bacteria in the intestines.

ü©∏

Deficiency

Causes hemorrhaging. Newborns lack intestinal bacteria to produce it and need a supplement. Long-term antibiotic use can also cause deficiency.

Key Points

  • ‚úì

    Vitamins are classified as either Fat Soluble (A, D, E, K) or Water Soluble (B Complex, C).

  • ‚úì

    Water soluble vitamins are generally not stored in the body and excess is excreted, whereas fat soluble vitamins are stored in the liver.

  • ‚úì

    B-complex vitamins largely act as coenzymes in metabolic pathways (e.g., Thiamine in TPP, Niacin in NAD).

  • ‚úì

    Vitamin C is crucial for collagen synthesis and prevention of scurvy.

  • ‚úì

    Vitamin D regulates calcium absorption; deficiency leads to rickets or osteomalacia.
