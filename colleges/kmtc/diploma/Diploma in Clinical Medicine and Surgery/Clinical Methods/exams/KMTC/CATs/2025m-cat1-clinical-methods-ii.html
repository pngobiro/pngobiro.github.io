Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions (20 Marks)

1.
What is the first step in obtaining a complete history for a patient with digestive complaints?
a) Ordering a colonoscopy
b) Performing a physical examination
c) Obtaining a detailed medical history from the patient
d) Reviewing the patient's lab results
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Obtaining a detailed medical history from the patient

Explanation: The history is always the first and most important step in the clinical method. It guides the physical examination and investigations. Jumping to exams or tests first is incorrect practice.

2.
A patient reports abdominal pain, nausea, and vomiting. What is the MOST important step in the history taking process?
a) Focusing on the timing and nature of their symptoms
b) Immediately ordering imaging studies
c) Obtaining a complete medical history
d) Asking about the patient's family history of stomach cancer
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Focusing on the timing and nature of their symptoms

Explanation: While a complete history (C) is the broad goal, characterizing the presenting complaint (SOCRATES - Site, Onset, Character, Radiation, Associations, Timing, Exacerbating/Relieving factors, Severity) is the specific "most important step" to form a differential diagnosis for acute abdominal pain.

3.
When examining the abdomen, what is the MOST important thing to do after the inspection?
a) Percussing the abdomen
b) Palpating the abdomen
c) Auscultating the abdomen
d) Performing an ultrasound
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Auscultating the abdomen

Explanation: In abdominal examination, the order is Inspection → Auscultation → Percussion → Palpation. Auscultation is done second because palpation and percussion can stimulate bowel motility and alter bowel sounds.

4.
Which of the following is a characteristic of a positive abdominal rebound tenderness?
a) Pain that is relieved when pressure is applied
b) Pain that is exacerbated when pressure is applied
c) Pain that is only felt on palpation
d) Pain increases when pressure applied to the abdomen is released
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Pain increases when pressure applied to the abdomen is released

Explanation: Rebound tenderness (Blumberg's sign) occurs when the examiner presses slowly into the abdomen and then withdraws the hand quickly. Sharp pain upon withdrawal indicates peritoneal irritation (peritonitis).

5.
Which of the following is a diagnostic test used to visualize the upper gastrointestinal tract?
a) Colonoscopy
b) Gastroscopy
c) Abdominal ultrasound
d) CT scan of the abdomen
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Gastroscopy

Explanation: Gastroscopy (or OGD - Oesophago-Gastro-Duodenoscopy) visualizes the esophagus, stomach, and duodenum. Colonoscopy is for the lower GI. Ultrasound and CT are imaging modalities, not direct endoscopic visualization.

6.
Which of the following is NOT a key component of a genitourinary system history?
(A) Past history of urinary tract infections (UTIs)
(B) Menstrual history (for female patients)
(C) Frequency of bowel movements
(D) History of kidney stones
Show Answer

Correct Answer: (C) Frequency of bowel movements

Explanation: Bowel movements relate to the Gastrointestinal (GI) system. While the systems are anatomically close, frequency of defecation is not a component of the Genitourinary (GU) history, unlike UTIs, menstruation, and kidney stones.

7.
Which of the following statements is false?
A) Female per vaginal examination can be done in the supine position
B) Rectal examination is done in the left lateral position
C) A Sim's speculum is used to examine women when in the supine position
D) Male doctors should examine female patients with a chaperone present
E) Examination starts by inspection
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) A Sim's speculum is used to examine women when in the supine position

Explanation: A Sims' speculum is typically used with the patient in the Sims' position (left lateral position), primarily for examining vaginal wall prolapse or fistulas. For the supine (lithotomy) position, a Cusco's speculum (bivalve) is standard.

8.
Auscultation of a bruit in the epigastrium or upper abdomen:
a) May be palpable in children and thin adults
b) Is difficult to palpate
c) Is diagnostic for renal artery stenosis
d) Suggests turbulent blood flow
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Is diagnostic for renal artery stenosis (or d, depending on context, but C is specific)

Explanation: While bruits indicate turbulent flow (D), a bruit specifically in the epigastrium radiating laterally is highly suggestive of renal artery stenosis (C). Note: Palpability refers to pulsations (AAA), not the bruit itself which is auditory. Option C is the most clinically distinct answer often sought in exams.

9.
What findings can be obtained from pelvic bimanual examination that cannot be obtained from imaging tests?
a) Involvement of peri vesical fat by bladder cancer
b) Bladder stones
c) Bladder mass
d) Mobility/fixation of pelvic mass
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Mobility/fixation of pelvic mass

Explanation: Imaging (CT/MRI) is excellent for visualizing size and invasion (A, B, C), but physical examination is uniquely capable of assessing the mobility or fixation of a mass to surrounding structures through direct manipulation.

10.
Which percussion note is elicited over the liver?
a) Dullness
b) Resonance
c) Hyperresonance
d) Flatness
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Dullness

Explanation: Solid organs like the liver produce a dull note on percussion. Lungs produce resonance; hollow viscera (stomach/bowel) produce tympany; muscles/bone produce flatness.

Section 2: Short Answer Questions (40 Marks)

11.
A 26 year old female patient presents with complaints of right upper quadrant pain for 3 days. Using your clinical knowledge, outline how you would explore the above pain (8 marks)
Show Answer

To explore the pain systematically, use the SOCRATES mnemonic:

  1. Site: Confirm it is in the Right Upper Quadrant (RUQ).
  2. Onset: Sudden (e.g., biliary colic) vs Gradual (e.g., hepatitis).
  3. Character: Colicky, dull ache, sharp, burning?
  4. Radiation: Does it move to the right shoulder/scapula (gallbladder) or back?
  5. Associations: Nausea, vomiting, fever, jaundice, dark urine, pale stools, change in bowel habit.
  6. Time course: Continuous or intermittent? Duration (3 days).
  7. Exacerbating/Relieving factors: Worse with fatty meals (gallstones)? Better with leaning forward (pancreatitis)?
  8. Severity: Scale of 1-10. Does it interfere with sleep?

Additional History: LMP (rule out ectopic/pregnancy), alcohol use, travel history.

12.
Outline features of an enlarged spleen on physical examination of the abdomen? (5 marks)
Show Answer

Features of Splenomegaly:

  1. Notch: A palpable notch is present on the anterior/medial border.
  2. Direction of Growth: Enlarges inferomedially towards the Right Iliac Fossa.
  3. Insusceptibility: You cannot get above the swelling (it originates from under the left costal margin).
  4. Movement: Moves with respiration (descends on inspiration).
  5. Percussion: Dull to percussion.
13.
Outline common signs and symptoms of GIT conditions (5 marks)
Show Answer
  • Abdominal Pain: Visceral, somatic, or referred.
  • Nausea and Vomiting: Including hematemesis (vomiting blood).
  • Dysphagia: Difficulty swallowing.
  • Change in Bowel Habits: Diarrhea or Constipation.
  • GI Bleeding: Melena (black tarry stools) or Hematochezia (fresh blood).
  • Jaundice: Yellow discoloration of skin/sclera.
  • Abdominal Distension: Bloating or ascites.
14.
Define Finger clubbing and highlight how to examine finger clubbing (2 marks)
Show Answer

Definition: Finger clubbing is the bulbous enlargement of the ends of the fingers/toes with loss of the normal angle between the nail bed and the nail fold (Lovibond angle).

Examination (Schamroth's Window Test):

  • Ask the patient to place the dorsal surfaces of the terminal phalanges of corresponding fingers together (nail to nail).
  • Look for the diamond-shaped gap (Schamroth's window) between the nail beds.
  • Normal: Diamond gap is visible.
  • Clubbing: Gap is obliterated/absent.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions (40 Marks)

15.
A 54 year old patient presents to the emergency department with history of lower abdominal pain and pre-vaginal discharge for 6 days.

a. Which system is affected (2 marks)
b. Describe the physical examination you will perform on the patient (10 marks)
c. Highlight the investigations you would carry out on this patient (8 marks)
Show Answer

a. Affected System:

The Reproductive System (specifically the female genital tract) and potentially the Genitourinary System.

b. Physical Examination:

  1. General Exam:
    • Assess vitals (Temperature for fever, BP/Pulse for shock).
    • Check for pallor (anemia from bleeding).
  2. Abdominal Exam:
    • Inspection: Distension, scars, masses.
    • Palpation: Tenderness (suprapubic/iliac fossae), guarding, masses (fibroids/ovarian).
  3. Pelvic Examination (with Chaperone):
    • Inspection (External Genitalia): Ulcers, discharge, warts, prolapse.
    • Speculum Exam: Visualize cervix (cervicitis, polyps, cancer) and vaginal walls. Characterize discharge (color, odor, consistency).
    • Bimanual Palpation: Assess uterine size, mobility, and tenderness (Cervical Motion Tenderness). Assess adnexa for masses or tenderness (PID).

c. Investigations:

  1. Microbiology:
    • High Vaginal Swab (HVS) for microscopy, culture, and sensitivity (bacteria/candida).
    • Endocervical swab (Gonorrhea/Chlamydia).
  2. Urinalysis: To rule out Urinary Tract Infection (UTI).
  3. Blood Tests:
    • Full Blood Count (FBC): WBC for infection, Hb for anemia.
    • CRP/ESR: Inflammatory markers.
    • Beta-hCG: Rule out pregnancy complications (even at 54, to be thorough depending on menopause status).
  4. Imaging:
    • Pelvic Ultrasound (Transvaginal/Abdominal): Visualize uterus (fibroids, endometrium) and ovaries (cysts, abscesses).
  5. Cytology/Histology: Pap smear or Endometrial biopsy if indicated (post-menopausal bleeding/discharge concerns).
