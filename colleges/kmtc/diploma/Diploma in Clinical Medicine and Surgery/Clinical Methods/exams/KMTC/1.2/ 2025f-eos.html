Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions

1.
Which of the following is the correct sequence of steps in abdominal examination?
A. Inspection, Percussion, Palpation, Auscultation
B. Inspection, Auscultation, Percussion, Palpation
C. Percussion, Inspection, Palpation, Auscultation
D. Palpation, Auscultation, Percussion, Inspection
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Inspection, Auscultation, Percussion, Palpation

Explanation: In abdominal examination, auscultation is performed before percussion and palpation. This is because physical manipulation of the abdomen (percussion and palpation) can alter bowel sounds, potentially leading to inaccurate findings.

Memory Aid: I A P P (Inspection, Auscultation, Percussion, Palpation) - Only for Abdomen. For other systems, it is usually IPPA.

2.
A 45-year-old male presents with generalized abdominal pain. On percussion, you note shifting dullness. This finding is most suggestive of:
A. Ascites
B. Appendicitis
C. Intestinal Obstruction
D. Peritonitis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Ascites

Explanation: Shifting dullness is a specific sign for free fluid in the peritoneal cavity (ascites). As the patient turns to the side, the fluid moves under gravity to the dependent area, causing the percussion note to change from resonant (bowel gas) to dull (fluid).

3.
Which of the following is a key feature of bowel obstruction?
A. Increased bowel sounds
B. Absence of bowel sounds
C. Tenderness in the upper abdomen
D. Enlarged liver
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Increased bowel sounds

Explanation: In the early stages of mechanical bowel obstruction, bowel sounds are typically hyperactive, high-pitched, and "tinkling" as the gut attempts to push contents past the obstruction. Absence of bowel sounds (B) is characteristic of paralytic ileus or late-stage obstruction/peritonitis.

4.
When palpating the abdomen, guarding is most often associated with:
A. Appendicitis
B. Gallstones
C. Hernia
D. Intestinal obstruction
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Appendicitis

Explanation: Guarding is a voluntary or involuntary contraction of abdominal muscles to protect inflamed underlying organs. It is a classic sign of localized peritonitis, such as that seen in appendicitis (typically in the right iliac fossa).

5.
In an abdominal examination, which is the first step to assess the patient?
A. Auscultation
B. Inspection
C. Percussion
D. Palpation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Inspection

Explanation: All physical examinations begin with inspection (observation). This provides critical clues about the patient's general state, skin changes, distension, scars, or visible masses before any contact is made.

6.
A digital rectal examination (DRE) is performed to evaluate which of the following structures?
A. Prostate
B. Spleen
C. Kidneys
D. Appendix
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Prostate

Explanation: In males, DRE is the primary physical examination method to assess the prostate gland for size, consistency, nodules, and tenderness.

7.
What is the most reliable method to confirm urinary retention during an examination?
A. Palpation of the lower abdomen
B. Urine dipstick test
C. Ultrasound of the bladder
D. Visual inspection
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Ultrasound of the bladder

Explanation: While a distended bladder may be palpable, a bladder scan (ultrasound) provides a reliable, non-invasive, and quantitative measurement of urine volume in the bladder, confirming retention accurately even in obese patients.

8.
Which investigation is commonly performed to detect blood in the urine?
A. Urinalysis
B. Ultrasound
C. CT scan
D. Cystoscopy
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Urinalysis

Explanation: Urinalysis (using a dipstick or microscopy) is the initial screening test to detect the presence of red blood cells (hematuria) in the urine.

9.
The GALS screen is used to assess:
A. Gait, Arms, Legs, Spine
B. Generalized Arthritic Lesions
C. General Anatomy of Ligaments and Skeleton
D. Gait Analysis and Limb Strength
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Gait, Arms, Legs, Spine

Explanation: GALS is a rapid screening tool used to assess the musculoskeletal system. It stands for Gait, Arms, Legs, and Spine.

10.
In assessing joint range of motion, which technique is most commonly used?
A. Inspection
B. Passive Movement
C. Palpation
D. Active Movement
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Active Movement

Explanation: Assessment of range of motion usually begins with active movement (the patient moving the joint themselves). If limitation is found, passive movement (examiner moving the joint) is then performed to distinguish between soft tissue/muscle issues and joint pathology.

11.
What is the main purpose of the "Trendelenburg test"?
A. To assess hip stability
B. To evaluate knee strength
C. To test for wrist arthritis
D. To examine spinal alignment
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. To assess hip stability

Explanation: The Trendelenburg test assesses the strength and stability of the hip abductor muscles (gluteus medius and minimus). A positive test indicates weakness in these muscles on the stance leg side.

12.
The Glasgow Coma Scale evaluates:
A. Motor, Sensory, and Reflex Function
B. Eye Opening, Verbal Response, Motor Response
C. Cranial Nerves, Gait, Coordination
D. Cognitive and Mental Function
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Eye Opening, Verbal Response, Motor Response

Explanation: The GCS is a neurological scale which aims to give a reliable and objective way of recording the conscious state of a person. It scores Eye opening (max 4), Verbal response (max 5), and Motor response (max 6).

13.
Which cranial nerve is responsible for pupillary light reflex?
A. Optic Nerve (CN II)
B. Oculomotor Nerve (CN III)
C. Trochlear Nerve (CN IV)
D. Abducens Nerve (CN VI)
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Oculomotor Nerve (CN III)

Explanation: The pupillary light reflex involves two nerves: the Optic nerve (CN II) carries the sensory input (afferent limb) detecting the light, and the Oculomotor nerve (CN III) carries the motor signal (efferent limb) that causes the pupil to constrict. While both are involved, the Oculomotor nerve is responsible for the actual motor action of constriction.

14.
The Babinski sign indicates:
A. Lower Motor Neuron Lesion
B. Upper Motor Neuron Lesion
C. Peripheral Neuropathy
D. Spinal Cord Compression
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Upper Motor Neuron Lesion

Explanation: A positive Babinski sign (extensor plantar response - big toe goes up, other toes fan out) in an adult indicates damage to the corticospinal tract, which is an Upper Motor Neuron lesion.

15.
Which test assesses coordination in the upper limbs?
A. Finger-to-Nose Test
B. Heel-to-Shin Test
C. Romberg Test
D. Pronator Drift
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Finger-to-Nose Test

Explanation: The Finger-to-Nose test specifically evaluates cerebellar function and coordination in the upper extremities. Heel-to-Shin assesses the lower extremities.

16.
Signs meningitis include:of
A. Nuchal Rigidity and Positive Kernig's Sign
B. Babinski Reflex and Dysdiadochokinesia
C. Romberg's Test and Ataxia
D. Tremors and Fasciculations
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Nuchal Rigidity and Positive Kernig's Sign

Explanation: Classic signs of meningeal irritation (meningitis) include nuchal rigidity (stiff neck), Kernig's sign (resistance to leg extension), and Brudzinski's sign.

17.
Cranial nerve III palsy results in:
A. Diplopia and Ptosis
B. Loss of Taste
C. Hearing Loss
D. Facial Weakness
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Diplopia and Ptosis

Explanation: CN III (Oculomotor) palsy causes the eye to be "down and out," along with ptosis (drooping eyelid) and mydriasis (dilated pupil), leading to diplopia (double vision).

18.
Which of the following is assessed by asking the patient to smile and close their eyes tightly?
A. Facial Nerve (CN VII)
B. Trigeminal Nerve (CN V)
C. Glossopharyngeal Nerve (CN IX)
D. Hypoglossal Nerve (CN XII)
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Facial Nerve (CN VII)

Explanation: The facial nerve controls the muscles of facial expression. Testing involves asking the patient to smile, frown, show teeth, puff out cheeks, and close eyes tightly.

19.
Slurred speech and difficulty swallowing can suggest a problem with:
A. Cranial nerve IX and X
B. Cranial nerve VI
C. Spinal cord injury
D. Cerebellar dysfunction
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Cranial nerve IX and X

Explanation: CN IX (Glossopharyngeal) and X (Vagus) control pharyngeal muscles and the palate. Dysfunction leads to dysphagia (difficulty swallowing), dysarthria (slurred speech), and loss of gag reflex (bulbar palsy).

20.
The inability to perform rapid alternating movements indicates dysfunction in:
A. The cerebellum
B. The basal ganglia
C. The brainstem
D. The spinal cord
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. The cerebellum

Explanation: Dysdiadochokinesia (inability to perform rapid alternating movements) is a hallmark sign of cerebellar dysfunction.

Section 2: Short Answer Questions

21.
Describe how you would assess a patient for splenomegaly, including the findings you might encounter. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Assessment Procedure:

  1. Position: Patient supine with knees flexed.
  2. Palpation: Start palpation in the Right Iliac Fossa (since the spleen enlarges obliquely towards the RIF). Ask the patient to breathe deeply. Move diagonally towards the Left Upper Quadrant (LUQ) with each expiration, feeling for the splenic edge on inspiration.
  3. Right Lateral Decubitus: If not felt, roll the patient to their right side and palpate the LUQ again (gravity brings spleen forward).
  4. Percussion: Percuss Traube's space (usually resonant; dullness suggests splenomegaly). Perform Castell's sign (percuss lowest intercostal space in anterior axillary line; should remain resonant on inspiration; dullness = positive).

Findings suggesting Splenomegaly:

  • Palpable mass in LUQ.
  • Presence of a splenic notch on the medial border.
  • Moves inferiorly and medially with inspiration.
  • Dullness to percussion.
  • Cannot get above it (unlike a kidney).
22.
Explain how you would perform and interpret the fluid thrill test in a patient suspected of having ascites. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Procedure:

  1. Patient lies supine.
  2. Ask the patient (or an assistant) to place the ulnar edge of their hand firmly vertically along the midline of the abdomen. (This stops the shock wave from being transmitted through the subcutaneous fat).
  3. Place your left hand flat on the patient's right flank.
  4. Flick or tap the patient's left flank sharply with your right fingers.
  5. Feel for an impulse (thrill) striking your left hand.

Interpretation:

  • Positive: A palpable impulse is felt on the opposite flank. This indicates a large amount of free fluid (tense ascites) conducting the fluid wave.
  • Negative: No impulse is felt.
23.
Describe the steps for conducting a suprapubic catheterization and outline the indications for this procedure. (5 Marks)
Post-Procedure: Secure the catheter and monitor for complications such as infection or bleeding. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Indications:

  • Acute urinary retention when urethral catheterization fails or is contraindicated (e.g., urethral stricture, false passage).
  • Urethral trauma/disruption.
  • Long-term bladder management (e.g., spinal cord injury).
  • Prostatitis or epididymitis.

Steps:

  1. Preparation: Verify palpable/distended bladder (use ultrasound if available). obtain consent. Position supine.
  2. Sterility: Clean suprapubic area with antiseptic solution and drape.
  3. Anesthesia: Infiltrate local anesthetic 2cm above pubic symphysis in midline.
  4. Incision/Puncture: Make a small skin incision. Insert the trocar/cannula unit vertically or slightly angled caudally into the bladder.
  5. Insertion: Advance until urine flows. Advance catheter through cannula (or use Seldinger technique).
  6. Secure: Inflate balloon, suture catheter to skin, and connect drainage bag.

Post-Procedure & Complications:

  • Monitor for hematuria (bleeding).
  • Watch for signs of infection (fever, turbid urine).
  • Ensure catheter is patent (flushing if necessary).
  • Potential bowel injury is a serious complication (avoid by confirming bladder distension).
24.
Outline steps for conducting musculoskeletal exam. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

The standard approach follows the Look, Feel, Move sequence, often preceded by a screening exam (GALS).

  1. Look (Inspection): Observe for swelling, deformity, erythema, muscle wasting, scars, and asymmetry. Assess gait and posture.
  2. Feel (Palpation): Assess for temperature (warmth), tenderness, swelling (effusion vs synovial thickening), and crepitus.
  3. Move:
    • Active Movement: Patient moves the joint. Assess range and pain.
    • Passive Movement: Examiner moves the joint. Assess range and end-feel.
  4. Function/Special Tests: Perform specific tests for the joint (e.g., Lachman's for knee, Phalen's for wrist) and assess neurovascular status.
25.
Describe the clinical significance and procedure of lumbar puncture. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Clinical Significance (Indications):

  • Diagnosis of CNS infections (Meningitis, Encephalitis).
  • Diagnosis of Subarachnoid Hemorrhage (xanthochromia).
  • Measurement of opening pressure (Idiopathic Intracranial Hypertension).
  • Therapeutic (intrathecal chemotherapy).

Procedure:

  1. Position: Lateral decubitus (fetal position) to open vertebral spaces.
  2. Identify Site: L3-L4 or L4-L5 interspace (at level of iliac crests).
  3. Sterile Prep: Clean skin and drape. Apply local anesthetic.
  4. Insertion: Insert spinal needle in midline, angled slightly cephalad (towards umbilicus).
  5. Advance: Feel for "pop" as it passes ligamentum flavum/dura. Remove stylet to check for CSF flow.
  6. Collection: Measure opening pressure, collect fluid in sterile tubes, replace stylet, remove needle.
26.
What parameters do you consider while reporting a Glasgow Coma Scale of a patient? (5 Marks)
Show Answer

The GCS is reported as a total score (out of 15) broken down into three components:

  1. Eye Opening (E) - Max 4:
    • 4: Spontaneous
    • 3: To speech
    • 2: To pain
    • 1: None
  2. Verbal Response (V) - Max 5:
    • 5: Oriented
    • 4: Confused
    • 3: Inappropriate words
    • 2: Incomprehensible sounds
    • 1: None
  3. Motor Response (M) - Max 6:
    • 6: Obeys commands
    • 5: Localizes pain
    • 4: Withdraws from pain
    • 3: Abnormal flexion (Decorticate)
    • 2: Extension (Decerebrate)
    • 1: None

Reporting: Document as GCS 15/15 (E4 V5 M6).

27.
Discuss the procedure and clinical interpretation of the Romberg test. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Procedure:

  1. Ask patient to stand with feet together and arms at their sides.
  2. Ask patient to keep eyes open first (to establish stability).
  3. Ask patient to close their eyes while maintaining the position.
  4. Observe for swaying or loss of balance (examiner must stand close to prevent falling).

Clinical Interpretation:

  • Positive Romberg: Patient is stable with eyes open but loses balance when eyes are closed. This indicates Sensory Ataxia (loss of proprioception/dorsal column function). They rely on vision to compensate; when vision is removed, they fall.
  • Negative (Cerebellar Ataxia): Patient is unstable even with eyes open. This suggests cerebellar dysfunction, as they cannot maintain balance regardless of visual input.
28.
Describe two methods of conducting hearing test exam. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

1. Whisper Voice Test (Screening):

  • Stand behind the patient (arm's length).
  • Mask the non-test ear (rub tragus).
  • Whisper a combination of numbers/letters and ask patient to repeat.

2. Tuning Fork Tests (512 Hz) - Rinne and Weber:

  • Rinne's Test: Place vibrating fork on mastoid (Bone Conduction), then in front of ear (Air Conduction). Normal is AC > BC.
  • Weber's Test: Place vibrating fork on midline of forehead. Ask where sound is heard best. Normal is equal in both ears (no lateralization).
