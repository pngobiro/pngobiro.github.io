Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions

1.
Being empathetic while creating rapport during history means?
A. Showing concern
B. Feeling pity and sorrow
C. Greetings
D. Self-introduction
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Showing concern

Explanation: Empathy in a clinical setting involves understanding and sharing the patient's feelings and showing genuine concern for their wellbeing, without necessarily experiencing the same pity or sorrow (sympathy).

2.
Chronologically, physical examination relies on the following cardinal techniques
A. Inspection, Percussion, Palpation and Auscultation
B. Inspection, Palpation, Percussion and Auscultation
C. Inspection, Auscultation, Palpation and Percussion
D. Inspection, Auscultation, Percussion and Palpation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Inspection, Palpation, Percussion and Auscultation

Explanation: The standard sequence for most systems (General, Respiratory, Cardiovascular) is IPPA: Inspection, Palpation, Percussion, Auscultation.
Note: For the Abdominal exam specifically, the order is IAPP (Inspection, Auscultation, Percussion, Palpation) to avoid altering bowel sounds. However, the general rule (chronologically) is usually taught as IPPA unless specified for abdomen. Given the options, B represents the standard general sequence.

3.
Back-and-forth oscillation of the eyes is called?
A. Nystagmus
B. Tremor
C. Ptosis
D. Strabismus
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Nystagmus

Explanation: Nystagmus is defined as a repetitive, uncontrolled movement of the eyes. These movements can be horizontal, vertical, or rotary.

4.
During abdominal examination you are able to bimanually palpate and ballot one of the intra-abdominal organ, which one?
A. Kidney
B. Liver
C. Urinary bladder
D. Spleen
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Kidney

Explanation: The kidneys are retroperitoneal organs that are examined using bimanual palpation. Ballottement is a specific technique used to confirm an enlarged kidney, where the organ is bounced between the hands.

5.
During abdominal examination the following suggest presence of enlarged spleen
A. Its palpated beneath right costal margin
B. Its bimanually palpable
C. Splenic notch can be felt in the lower medial border
D. One can easily get above it
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Splenic notch can be felt in the lower medial border

Explanation: Distinguishing features of a spleen (vs. kidney):

  • It has a palpable notch on its medial edge.
  • You cannot get above it (it originates from under the ribs).
  • It moves towards the right iliac fossa on inspiration.
  • It is dull to percussion.
Option A is incorrect because the spleen is on the left. Option B is incorrect because kidneys are bimanually palpable/ballotable, spleens generally are not ballotable.

6.
A positive shifting dullness is a sign of
A. Intestinal obstruction
B. Splenomegaly
C. Ascites
D. Pregnancy
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Ascites

Explanation: Shifting dullness is a clinical sign used to detect the presence of free fluid (ascites) in the peritoneal cavity.

7.
The following is true regarding percussion during physical examination.
A. The thumb strikes the middle phalanx of the same middle finger
B. The middle finger strikes the middle phalanx of the other middle finger
C. The middle finger strikes the middle phalanx of the other index finger
D. The index finger strikes the middle phalanx of the other index finger
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. The middle finger strikes the middle phalanx of the other middle finger

Explanation: The standard technique involves placing the non-dominant middle finger (pleximeter) firmly on the skin and striking its middle phalanx with the tip of the dominant middle finger (plexor).

8.
Regarding auscultation of the abdomen
A. Bowel sounds are best heard over the epigastrium
B. High pitched sounds suggest intestinal obstruction
C. Absence of bowel sounds for 5 seconds is abnormal
D. Normal bowel sounds are 20-25 per minute
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. High pitched sounds suggest intestinal obstruction

Explanation:

  • A: Usually heard best in the Right Lower Quadrant (ileocecal valve area).
  • B: True. Tinkling/high-pitched sounds indicate fluid and gas under pressure (obstruction).
  • C: False. Must be absent for up to 5 minutes to declare "silent abdomen".
  • D: False. Normal is 5-30 sounds per minute, but 20-25 implies a specific narrow range. High pitched/obstruction is the stronger clinical correlation.

9.
The following is not included in Motor system during neurological examination.
A. Muscle bulk
B. Muscle tone
C. Coordination
D. Voluntary movements
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Voluntary movements

Explanation: The standard components of a motor exam are Inspection (Bulk, Fasciculations), Tone, Power (Strength), Reflexes, and Coordination. "Voluntary movements" is a descriptive term for Power/Strength, but often Coordination is considered a separate entity (Cerebellar function) in some texts, though usually grouped with motor. However, voluntary movements is vague. In many contexts, "Voluntary movements" is not a specific header like Tone or Bulk.
Self-Correction: Sometimes Coordination is separated from the "Motor System" proper (Pyramidal) and put under "Cerebellar". However, D is the least technical term.

10.

A. Cranial nerve number I
B. Cranial nerve number II

11.
Which of the following is a sensory exam during Central nervous system exam
A. Gait
B. Vibration
C. Reflexes
D. Coordination
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Vibration

Explanation:

13.
The correct angle for intramuscular injection is

14.
Which of the following is not a risk associated with unsafe injection practices
A. Transmission of blood borne diseases
B. Development of antibiotic resistance
C. Local site infections
D. Needle stick injuries
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Development of antibiotic resistance

Explanation: While unsafe injections can transmit diseases (HIV, Hep B/C), cause abscesses (local infection), and injure workers (needlesticks), antibiotic resistance is caused by the misuse/overuse of the drug itself, not the mechanical act of an unsafe injection.

15.
Which of the following is not a recommended practice for injection safety
A. Use of single use syringes
B. Recapping needles after use
C. Using auto-disable syringes
D. Proper disposal in sharps container
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Recapping needles after use

Explanation: Recapping needles is a primary cause of needle-stick injuries and is strongly discouraged. Needles should be disposed of immediately into a sharps container without recapping.

16.
A painless scrotal swelling with a positive trans illumination test is most likely due to
A. Testicular torsion
B. Epididymitis
C. Varicocoele
D. Hydrocele
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Hydrocele

Explanation: Hydroceles are fluid-filled sacs around the testicle. Fluid transmits light (transilluminates) and is typically painless. Solid masses (tumors) or blood (hematocele) do not transilluminate.

Section 2: Short Answer Questions

21.
Describe how you would assess a patient for splenomegaly, including the findings you might encounter. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Method:

  1. Palpation: Start in Right Iliac Fossa. Ask patient to breathe deeply. Move diagonally towards Left Hypochondrium. Feel for the splenic edge descending on inspiration.
  2. Positioning: If not felt, turn patient to right lateral decubitus and palpate again.
  3. Percussion: Percuss Traube's space (should be resonant) or Castell's point (lowest intercostal space, anterior axillary line) - dullness indicates enlargement.

Findings:

  • Palpable mass in LUQ.
  • Splenic notch on medial border.
  • Cannot get above the mass.
  • Moves diagonally with respiration.
  • Dull to percussion.
22.
Explain how you would perform and interpret the fluid thrill test in a patient suspected of having ascites. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Technique:

  • Patient supine.
  • Assistant places hand firmly on midline of abdomen (stops fat conduction).
  • Examiner places one hand on flank.
  • Flick the opposite flank.

Interpretation:

  • If a tap/impulse is felt by the receiving hand, it indicates a positive fluid thrill.
  • This suggests tense/gross ascites.
23.
Describe the steps for conducting a suprapubic catheterization and outline the indications for this procedure. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Indications: Failed urethral catheterization, urethral stricture, urethral trauma, prostate obstruction.

Steps:

  1. Confirm palpable/distended bladder.
  2. Clean and drape suprapubic area.
  3. Administer local anesthesia 2cm above pubic symphysis.
  4. Incise skin.
  5. Insert trocar vertically into bladder.
  6. Insert catheter, inflate balloon, and secure.
24.
Outline steps for conducting musculoskeletal exam. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

The standard sequence is Look, Feel, Move:

  1. Inspection (Look): Gait, posture, scars, swelling, muscle wasting, deformities.
  2. Palpation (Feel): Temperature, tenderness, joint line, crepitus, effusion.
  3. Movement (Move):
    • Active Range of Motion (patient moves).
    • Passive Range of Motion (examiner moves).
  4. Function/Special Tests: Specific tests for instability or impingement.
25.
Outline the five components of motor system examinations in CNS. (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Inspection: Muscle bulk, fasciculations, involuntary movements.
  2. Tone: Resistance to passive movement (hypotonia vs hypertonia).
  3. Power: Strength grading (0-5).
  4. Reflexes: Deep tendon reflexes.
  5. Coordination: Cerebellar function (finger-nose, heel-shin).

27.
Discuss the procedure and clinical interpretation of the Romberg test. (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Procedure:

  • Patient stands with feet together, eyes open (establishes balance).
  • Patient closes eyes. Examiner observes for swaying/falling.

Interpretation:

  • Positive: Falls when eyes closed = Sensory Ataxia (Proprioceptive deficit). Vision was compensating.
  • Negative (or Ataxia with eyes open): Cerebellar Ataxia (balance poor regardless of vision).
28.
Describe two methods of conducting hearing test exam. (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Whispered Voice Test: Stand behind patient, occlude one ear, whisper numbers/letters, ask patient to repeat.
  2. Tuning Fork Tests (Rinne & Weber): Use 512Hz fork to distinguish Conductive vs Sensorineural hearing loss.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions

29.
A 60-year-old male presents with a two-month history of unintentional weight loss, loss of appetite, and upper abdominal pain. Outline a systematic approach to conducting a per abdominal examination in this patient. Discuss possible findings and their interpretations. (20 marks)
Show Answer

Systematic Approach:

  1. Preparation: Introduce, consent, position (supine, one pillow), exposure (nipples to knees/mid-thigh), ensure comfort.
  2. Inspection:
    • General: Cachexia (malignancy), jaundice, pallor (anemia).
    • Abdomen: Distension, scars, visible masses, pulsations, dilated veins (caput medusae).
  3. Palpation:
    • Superficial: Tenderness, guarding, temperature.
    • Deep: Organomegaly (Liver, Spleen, Kidneys), masses (epigastric mass).
    • Lymph nodes: Virchow's node (Left supraclavicular).
  4. Percussion: Liver span, shifting dullness (ascites).
  5. Auscultation: Bowel sounds, bruits (renal/aortic), friction rubs (hepatic).
  6. Digital Rectal Exam: Check for metastases (Blumer's shelf) or melena.

Possible Findings & Interpretation:

  • Hard, irregular hepatomegaly: Liver metastases.
  • Epigastric mass: Gastric or pancreatic carcinoma.
  • Virchow's Node: Metastatic gastric cancer (Troisier's sign).
  • Ascites: Peritoneal malignancy (carcinomatosis).
  • Jaundice: Biliary obstruction (e.g., Pancreatic head tumor).

Clinical Context: The history strongly suggests an upper GI malignancy (Stomach or Pancreas). The exam aims to confirm the primary tumor and stage the disease (metastases).

30.
A 40-year-old female presents with weakness in the right arm, slurred speech, and difficulty walking. Outline a systematic neurological examination, emphasizing the possible findings and their interpretations in this case. (20 Marks)
Show Answer

Systematic Neurological Examination:

  1. Mental Status: GCS, speech assessment (Dysarthria vs Dysphasia).
  2. Cranial Nerves:
    • Facial (VII): Facial asymmetry/droop.
    • Glossopharyngeal/Vagus (IX/X): Gag reflex, palate elevation (swallowing/speech).
    • Hypoglossal (XII): Tongue deviation.
  3. Motor System (Upper & Lower Limbs):
    • Inspection: Muscle wasting, fasciculations.
    • Tone: Spasticity vs Flaccidity.
    • Power: Compare Right vs Left (Hemiparesis).
    • Reflexes: Hyperreflexia (UMN) vs Hyporeflexia (LMN). Babinski sign.
  4. Sensory System: Light touch, pinprick, proprioception.
  5. Coordination: Finger-nose, heel-shin (Ataxia).
  6. Gait: Hemiplegic gait (circumduction).

Possible Findings & Interpretation:

  • Right-sided Hemiparesis (Weakness): Indicates a lesion in the Left hemisphere (Corticospinal tract crosses at medulla).
  • Dysarthria/Dysphasia: Left hemisphere involvement (Broca's area) if dominant.
  • Hypertonia/Hyperreflexia/Babinski (+): Signs of Upper Motor Neuron (UMN) lesion (e.g., Stroke/CVA).
  • Gait abnormality: Due to weakness or loss of coordination.

Diagnosis: This presentation (acute onset, focal neurological deficit) is classic for a Stroke (Cerebrovascular Accident) involving the Left Middle Cerebral Artery territory.
