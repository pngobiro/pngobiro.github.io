Endocrine Disorders

Introduction

The endocrine system comprises the classic endocrine organs: hypothalamus/pituitary, thyroid, parathyroid, adrenal, pancreatic islet cells, and gonads. The mode of presentation of endocrine disorders does not fit neatly into a system-based model, the symptoms rarely being specific to a particular system. Frequently, endocrine disease is suggested by a constellation of non-specific symptoms.

The history

As in other systems, the history consists of presenting symptoms, the history of the development of the illness and the family history.

Presenting symptoms

There are a number of symptom complexes that particularly suggest endocrine disease.

Common Endocrine Symptom Complexes

  • Thirst and polyuria
  • Weight loss or gain
  • Muscle weakness
  • Temperature intolerance
  • Sweating and tremor
  • Palpitations
  • Visual disturbance
  • Neck pain/swelling
  • Sexual dysfunction
  • Change in appearance

Thirst and polyuria

Excessive thirst (polydipsia) and increased urine output (polyuria) are the most important presenting symptoms of diabetes mellitus. Polydipsia and polyuria may also be due to impairment of renal concentrating capacity as a result of a deficiency of antidiuretic hormone (cranial diabetes insipidus) or a failure of antidiuretic hormone action (nephrogenic diabetes insipidus). Sometimes, apparent polydipsia and polyuria may be due to increased fluid intake (psychogenic polydipsia). Generally, nocturnal polyuria is not a feature of psychogenic polydipsia.

Weight loss

Loss of weight is a feature of decreased food intake or increased metabolic rate. Thyroid overactivity (hyperthyroidism) is nearly always associated with a combination of weight loss and increased appetite. Weight loss is rarely the sole presenting symptom of hyperthyroidism and other clinical features often predominate (Box 16.1). Other endocrine conditions in which weight loss is a major feature are listed in Box 16.2.

Box 16.1 Clinical features of hyperthyroidism

  • Tachycardia/AF
  • Lid lag/retraction
  • Exophthalmos (Graves')
  • Sweating
  • Tremor
  • Weight loss with appetite
  • Thyroid enlargement/bruit
  • Thinning of hair
  • Proximal weakness
  • Heat intolerance
  • Palpitations
  • Chorea

Box 16.2 Endocrine causes of weight loss

  • Hyperthyroidism
  • Type 1 diabetes mellitus
  • Hypopituitarism
  • Adrenocortical failure (Addison's disease)
  • Anorexia nervosa

Weight gain or redistribution

An increase in body weight (Box 16.3) is a predictable result of a reduction in metabolic rate, such as in primary hypothyroidism. Glucocorticoid hormone excess (Cushing's syndrome) results in an increase in body fat predominantly involving abdominal, omental and interscapular fat (truncal obesity), with paradoxical thinning of the limbs due to muscle atrophy.

Box 16.3 Conditions with weight gain

  • Simple obesity (energy imbalance)
  • Primary hypothyroidism
  • Cushing's syndrome
  • Hypothalamic lesions
  • Leptin deficiency

Muscle weakness

Symptomatic muscular weakness not due to neurological disease is a feature of several metabolic disorders (Box 16.4). The metabolic myopathy causes symmetrical proximal weakness, mainly involving the shoulder and hip girdle musculature. The major symptom is difficulty in climbing stairs or rising from a sitting position.

Box 16.4 Metabolic myopathy

Painless: Hyperthyroidism, Cushing's syndrome, Acromegaly.

Painful: Vitamin D deficiency, Osteomalacia, Hypothyroidism.

Temperature intolerance

An abnormal sensation of cold may indicate underlying hypothyroidism (Box 16.6). Heat intolerance, where the patient is comfortable at an ambient temperature that others find unpleasantly cold, is highly specific for thyroid overactivity.

Box 16.6 Clinical features of hypothyroidism

  • Weight gain
  • Dry skin/Sallow complexion
  • Cold intolerance
  • Deepened voice
  • Slow activity (physical/mental)
  • Thinning of hair
  • Unsteadiness
  • Tingling in extremities
  • Aching muscles/cramps
  • Mild proximal weakness
  • Bradycardia
  • Shortness of breath

Sweating and tremor

A recent increase in sweat secretion may be an early indication of thyroid overactivity. Increased sweating also occurs in acromegaly. A fine rapid resting tremor is a cardinal feature of thyrotoxicosis, distinguished from the coarser tremor of anxiety (usually with cool skin). Essential tremor is variable and often involves the head and neck.

Visual disturbance

Bitemporal hemianopia is a major feature of suprasellar extension of pituitary adenomas compressing the optic chiasm. Diplopia may result from extraocular muscle tethering in dysthyroid eye disease.

Neck pain and swelling

Pain arising from the thyroid is comparatively unusual; severe tenderness suggests viral subacute thyroiditis (de Quervain's). Sudden localized pain indicates bleeding into a thyroid nodule. Painless enlargement (goitre) usually presents with pressure effects (dysphagia, stridor) or cosmetic disturbance.

Sexual symptoms

Impotence: May be due to testosterone deficiency, hyperprolactinaemia, or diabetes (autonomic neuropathy).

Gynaecomastia: Breast tissue development in men (Box 16.7). Can be physiological (puberty) or pathological (liver disease, thyrotoxicosis, tumours, drugs like spironolactone).

Amenorrhoea: Absence of menses. Causes include hypothalamic-pituitary dysfunction, ovarian failure, or thyroid dysfunction.

Galactorrhoea: Inappropriate lactation. Causes include prolactinomas, hypothyroidism, or drugs (dopamine antagonists).

Box 16.7 Causes of gynaecomastia

  • High oestrogen/testosterone ratio: Chronic liver disease, Thyrotoxicosis.
  • Drugs: Spironolactone, digoxin.
  • Hormonal: Hypogonadism, hCG producing tumours, Leydig cell tumours.
  • Genetic: Klinefelter's syndrome (XXY), Kennedy syndrome.

The examination

General assessment

Start by assessing nutrition, weight, height, and BMI. Note fat distribution: truncal obesity with moon face and striae suggests Cushing's syndrome (Fig 16.1, 16.6). Acromegaly presents with coarsening of features, prognathism, and large hands/feet (Fig 16.2).

Look for short stature (Turner's syndrome), tall stature (gigantism, Marfan's), or specific signs like the short 4th/5th metacarpals in pseudohypoparathyroidism (Fig 16.3).

The skin

Pigmentation (buccal, palmar scars) suggests Addison's disease (Fig 16.4). Vitiligo (Fig 16.5) is associated with autoimmune endocrine disease. Violaceous striae (Fig 16.6) occur in Cushing's. Dry skin and coarse hair suggest hypothyroidism (Fig 16.7).

The thyroid

Inspection: Look for the gland moving up on swallowing. Palpation: Best done from behind (Fig 16.8). Feel the isthmus below the cricoid cartilage, then the lateral lobes.

Assess if the gland is diffusely enlarged, nodular (Fig 16.9), or has a single nodule. Check for retrosternal extension and bruits (untreated Graves').

Breasts and genitalia

In men, check for gynaecomastia (Fig 16.10) and measure testicular volume using a Prader orchidometer (Fig 16.11). In women, look for virilization (clitoromegaly).

The eyes

Check for corneal calcification (hyperparathyroidism, Fig 16.12). In thyroid disease, look for exophthalmos, lid retraction, and ophthalmoplegia (Fig 16.13). Visual fields should be tested for bitemporal hemianopia (pituitary tumours).

Investigation

Investigation involves measuring hormones (basal, stimulation, or suppression tests) and imaging. Stimulation tests (e.g., Synacthen for adrenal) assess reserve. Suppression tests (e.g., dexamethasone for Cushing's, glucose load for acromegaly) assess autonomous secretion.

Imaging

Plain X-ray: Limited value, but can show nephrocalcinosis (Fig 16.14) or pituitary fossa expansion.

CT: Good for adrenals (Fig 16.15).

MRI: Best for pituitary tumours (Fig 16.16).

Isotope scans: Show functional activity (e.g., thyroid nodules, Fig 16.17).
