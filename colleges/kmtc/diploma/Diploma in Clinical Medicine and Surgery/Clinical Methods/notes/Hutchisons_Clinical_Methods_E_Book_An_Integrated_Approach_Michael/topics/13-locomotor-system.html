Introduction

Musculoskeletal symptoms are a major cause of pain and disability. In later life, musculoskeletal diseases are the single most important cause of disability. Early referral to specialist care to consider disease-modifying therapy is critical in preventing disability. The objectives of performing a musculoskeletal assessment are to make an accurate diagnosis, to assess the severity and consequences of the condition, and to construct a clear management plan.

General assessment: the 'GALS' locomotor screen

The GALS (Gait, Arms, Legs, Spine) screen is a rapid and sensitive screening method for detecting musculoskeletal disorders.

GALS Screening Questions

  • 1Do you have any pain or stiffness in your muscles, joints or back?
  • 2Have you ever had gout or arthritis?
  • 3Can you dress yourself completely without difficulty?
  • 4Can you walk up and down stairs without difficulty?

Positive answers imply the need for a more detailed assessment.

Gait

Watch the patient walking and turning back towards you. Normal: symmetry, smooth movement, arm swing, no pelvic tilt, normal stride length, ability to turn quickly.

Spine

Inspect from behind, side, and front. Look for scoliosis, lordosis, kyphosis. Assess movements.

Legs

Examine with patient lying on a couch.

  • Flex hip and knee: check for crepitus and range.
  • Internal rotation of hip (in flexion): usually first movement lost in hip disease.
  • Patellar tap / Bulge test: for effusion.
  • Inspect feet: deformities, callosities.
  • MTP squeeze test.

Specific locomotor history

Pain patterns can be diagnostic. Referred pain is common:

  • Neck pain: to occiput, vertex, shoulder, arm.
  • Shoulder pain: to elbow.
  • Hip pain: to knee.
  • Lumbar root pain: to buttock, leg, foot (sciatica).

Joint disease patterns

Distinguishing inflammatory from non-inflammatory disease and the pattern of joint involvement aids diagnosis.

Box 13.1 Distribution of joint involvement

Typically Symmetrical
  • Rheumatoid arthritis: Small joints hands/feet.
  • SLE: Similar to RA but less erosive.
Typically Asymmetrical
  • Psoriatic arthritis: Dactylitis, oligoarthritis.
  • Reactive arthritis: Lower limb predominant.
  • Gout: 1st MTP (podagra).

Examination: general principles

Examination of the joints can be summarized as 'look, feel and move'.

Extra-articular features

Look for subcutaneous nodules, rashes, nail changes, and eye inflammation.

Examination of individual joints

The spine

Check for lordosis, kyphosis, and scoliosis. Palpate for tenderness and step deformity. Test flexion (Schober's test), extension, lateral flexion, and rotation.

The hand

Look for deformities: Heberden's nodes (DIP OA), Bouchard's nodes (PIP OA), Ulnar deviation (RA), Swan neck, Boutonni√®re.

The hip

Assess flexion, abduction, adduction, and rotation. Thomas' test detects fixed flexion deformity. Trendelenburg test assesses hip abductor strength.

The knee

Look for valgus/varus deformity and effusion. Test for stability (collaterals, cruciates). A "bulge test" detects small effusions; "patellar tap" detects large ones.

Investigations

Synovial fluid examination

Aspiration is diagnostic for septic arthritis (organisms) and crystal arthritis (urate/pyrophosphate crystals). Blood suggests trauma or haemophilia.

Box 13.11 Synovial fluid findings (Knee)

  • Cloudy/Pus: Bacterial infection
  • Crystals: Gout (urate) or Pseudogout (pyrophosphate)
  • Blood: Trauma, haemophilia, villonodular synovitis
  • Pink fluid: Torn meniscus (sometimes)

Radiological examination

Plain X-rays are standard for fractures and arthritis changes. MRI is superior for soft tissue, spinal cord, and early inflammatory changes. CT is useful for complex bone anatomy.
