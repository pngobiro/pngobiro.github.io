Introduction

This chapter describes the assessment of potential diseases of the ear, nose and throat. There is a close functional and anatomical relationship between these areas; disease in one may have manifestations in another, and referred pain is common. ENT is a highly clinical subject as much is visible, especially with modern examination techniques.

The Ear

Anatomy

The ear consists of the external, middle and inner ears. The external ear includes the pinna and external auditory canal. The middle ear contains the ossicles (malleus, incus, stapes) which transmit sound from the tympanic membrane to the oval window. The inner ear contains the cochlea (hearing) and vestibular system (balance). The facial nerve runs through the temporal bone and is important in otological practice.

Symptoms of Ear Disease

The five main symptoms are otalgia, otorrhoea, hearing loss, tinnitus, and vertigo.

Otalgia

Earache may be primary (otological) or referred from the pharynx, larynx, teeth, or cervical spine.

Otological

  • Barotrauma
  • Perichondritis
  • Herpes zoster (Ramsay Hunt)
  • Malignant otitis externa
Non-otological
  • Tonsillitis/Quinsy
  • Dental disease
  • TMJ dysfunction
  • Cervical spine disease
  • Upper aerodigestive tract malignancy

Otorrhoea

Discharge varies by pathology. Profuse mucoid discharge suggests a perforation. Foul-smelling discharge suggests cholesteatoma or anaerobes.

Clinical Examination of the Ear

External Examination

Inspect the pinna and post-auricular area. Look for scars, sinuses, or deformity. Palpate for tenderness (mastoiditis, Fig 20.7).

Otoscopy

Hold the otoscope like a pen. Straighten the canal by pulling the pinna upwards and backwards in adults (Fig 20.8). Inspect the canal for wax, debris, or inflammation.

The Tympanic Membrane

Examine for colour, translucency, position (retracted/bulging), and integrity (perforation).

Assessment of Hearing

Voice Test: Whisper numbers at 60cm and 15cm. Mask the non-test ear.
Rinne Test: 512Hz fork. Place on mastoid (BC) then beside ear (AC). Normal/Sensorineural = AC > BC (Positive). Conductive = BC > AC (Negative).
Weber Test: Fork on vertex. Normal = central. Conductive = lateralizes to deaf ear. Sensorineural = lateralizes to good ear.

Assessment of Balance

Includes cranial nerve exam, gait (heel-to-toe), Romberg's test, and Unterberger's test. The Dix-Hallpike test is specific for BPPV.

Special Investigations

Audiometry

Pure Tone Audiometry: Determines hearing thresholds for air and bone conduction.

Tympanometry: Measures compliance of the drum. Useful for detecting fluid (glue ear) or ossicular disruption.

Radiology

CT: Best for bony detail (cholesteatoma, trauma).
MRI: Best for soft tissue and retrocochlear pathology (Vestibular Schwannoma).

The Nose and Paranasal Sinuses

Anatomy

The nose functions for respiration, filtration, humidification, and smell. The lateral wall contains three turbinates. The paranasal sinuses (maxillary, frontal, ethmoid, sphenoid) drain into the meatuses.

Symptoms

  • Blockage: Deviation, polyps, rhinitis.
  • Rhinorrhoea: Purulent (infection), watery (allergic/vasomotor), unilateral foul (foreign body), clear dripping (CSF leak).
  • Epistaxis: Usually Little's area (anterior septum).
  • Smell disturbance: Anosmia (polyps, viral, trauma), Cacosmia (anaerobic infection).
  • Facial Pain: Sinusitis (often over-diagnosed), dental, neuralgias.

Examination

Inspect external nose. Anterior rhinoscopy using a Thudicum speculum or otoscope. Look for septal deviation, perforation, or polyps. Nasal endoscopy allows detailed view of the middle meatus and postnasal space.

The Throat

Anatomy

Includes the oral cavity, pharynx, and larynx. The larynx protects the airway and produces voice. Innervation is via cranial nerves IX and X.

Symptoms

  • Dysphonia (Hoarseness): Laryngitis, nodules, polyps, carcinoma, vocal cord palsy. >4 weeks needs visualization.
  • Stridor: Noisy breathing due to obstruction. Emergency.
  • Dysphagia: Difficulty swallowing. Structural or neurological.
  • Sore Throat: Viral, tonsillitis, quinsy, glandular fever.
  • Neck Lump: Lymph nodes, thyroid, cysts.

Box 20.7 Causes of Stridor

Children: Croup, Epiglottitis, Foreign body.
Adults: Laryngeal carcinoma, Trauma, Laryngitis, Vocal cord palsy.

Examination

Examine mouth, tongue, and tonsils. Indirect laryngoscopy uses a mirror. Flexible nasendoscopy provides the best view of the larynx and pharynx.

Examination of the Neck

Systematic palpation of lymph node levels, thyroid, and parotid glands. Characterize any lumps (site, size, consistency, fixation).
