Introduction

In recent years there have been impressive advances throughout the whole spectrum of neurological and muscle diseases. However, advances in investigations have not rendered careful clinical assessment redundant. The fundamental importance of the clinical history and examination cannot be overemphasized. In the diagnosis of neurological disease, it is the history that is paramount, so it is even more important for this to be comprehensive than the examination.

The neurological history

The repertoire of neurological symptoms is limited. The time course of evolution and resolution of neurological symptoms often indicates the nature of the problem.

Box 14.1 Common Neurological Symptoms

  • Headache
  • Loss of consciousness/awareness
  • Dizziness/Vertigo/Falls
  • Visual disturbances (loss, double)
  • Speech/Swallowing difficulty
  • Weakness
  • Abnormal movements (tremor)
  • Sensory changes (numbness, pain)
  • Cognitive symptoms (memory)
  • Sphincter disturbance

Loss of consciousness: Syncope vs Seizure

Distinguishing between syncope and seizure relies heavily on the history, particularly from witnesses.

Headache

The vast majority of headaches are not caused by life-threatening disorders but by primary headache syndromes.

The neurological examination

Cranial Nerves I & II

I. Olfactory Nerve: Tested using bottles with distinct smells. Unilateral anosmia may suggest a subfrontal meningioma.

II. Optic Nerve: Examination includes visual acuity, colour vision, visual fields, and funduscopy. The visual pathway anatomy determines the type of field defect (Fig. 14.1).

Cranial Nerves III, IV, VI

Oculomotor, Trochlear, Abducens: Horizontal movement of the eye outwards (laterally) is termed abduction and inwards (medially) is termed adduction. Vertical movement upwards is termed elevation and downwards is depression. Nystagmus denotes rhythmic oscillations of one or (more usually) both eyes.

Gaze Palsies

Horizontal gaze paresis and internuclear ophthalmoparesis (INO) localize lesions to the brainstem pathways (PPRF and MLF).

Cranial Nerve V: Trigeminal

This is a mixed motor and sensory nerve. The sensory component supplies the face (ophthalmic V1, maxillary V2, mandibular V3). The motor component supplies the muscles of mastication.

Corneal Reflex: A sensory stimulus applied to the cornea causes a reflex blink. Its absence on one side establishes unequivocally the presence of a trigeminal lesion. Jaw Jerk: A pathologically brisk jaw jerk suggests an upper motor neurone lesion above the cervical spinal cord.

Cranial Nerve VII: Facial

The facial nerve supplies all facial muscles and the platysma. It also carries taste (chorda tympani) and secretomotor fibres. Testing: Ask the patient to raise eyebrows, screw eyes shut, blow out cheeks, and show teeth. A lower motor neurone lesion affects all muscles on one side (Bell's palsy). An upper motor neurone lesion spares the upper face (forehead) due to bilateral cortical representation.

Cranial Nerve VIII: Vestibulocochlear

Cochlear: Hearing is tested by whispering numbers or rubbing fingers. Rinne's and Weber's tests distinguish conductive from sensorineural deafness. Vestibular: Vertigo and nystagmus suggest vestibular dysfunction.

Cranial Nerves IX & X: Glossopharyngeal & Vagus

Tested together. Note any dysphonia or dysphagia. Observe the palate; in a unilateral vagus lesion, the uvula deviates to the unaffected side on saying "Ahh".

Cranial Nerve XI: Accessory

Supplies sternocleidomastoid and upper trapezius. Test by asking patient to shrug shoulders (trapezius) and turn head against resistance (sternocleidomastoid).

Cranial Nerve XII: Hypoglossal

Supplies tongue muscles. A unilateral lesion causes the tongue to deviate towards the weak side on protrusion. Wasting and fasciculation suggest a lower motor neurone lesion (e.g., motor neurone disease).

The Motor System

Assessment includes Inspection (wasting, fasciculation), Tone, Power, Reflexes, and Coordination.

Inspection & Tone

Look for muscle wasting, fasciculations, and involuntary movements. Tone is assessed by passive movement. Spasticity (clasp-knife) suggests UMN lesion. Rigidity (lead-pipe or cogwheel) suggests extrapyramidal disease (Parkinsonism).

Muscle Power

graded using the MRC scale (0-5). See Tables 14.6 and 14.7 for specific muscle testing.

Box 14.3 MRC Scale for Muscle Strength

  • 0No contraction
  • 1Flicker or trace of contraction
  • 2Active movement with gravity eliminated
  • 3Active movement against gravity
  • 4Active movement against gravity and resistance
  • 5Normal power

Reflexes

Tendon reflexes (biceps, triceps, supinator, knee, ankle) are tested. Brisk reflexes suggest UMN lesion; absent reflexes suggest LMN or sensory neuropathy. The Plantar Reflex is crucial: extension of the big toe (Babinski sign) indicates an UMN lesion.

Cerebellar Function

Test for ataxia and incoordination using:

  • Finger-nose test: Look for intention tremor and past-pointing.
  • Dysdiadochokinesis: Impaired rapid alternating movements.
  • Heel-shin test: Incoordination of the lower limb.

The Sensory System

Sensation is divided into spinothalamic (pain/temperature) and dorsal column (proprioception/vibration) modalities. Patterns of sensory loss help localize lesions (e.g., dermatomal, glove-and-stocking, hemisensory).

Proprioception

Joint position sense is tested in the toes and fingers. Loss of proprioception causes sensory ataxia and positive Romberg's test. Pseudoathetosis (wandering fingers when eyes closed) indicates severe proprioceptive loss.

Brown-S√©quard Syndrome

Caused by hemisection of the spinal cord. Results in ipsilateral motor weakness and vibration/proprioception loss, with contralateral pain/temperature loss.

Gait and Posture

Gait assessment is diagnostic. Common abnormalities include hemiparetic gait (circumduction), parkinsonian gait (shuffling, festinant), ataxic gait (wide-based), and steppage gait (foot drop).

Table 14.11 Common Gait Disorders

  • Hemiparetic: Unilateral stiffness, circumduction of leg, flexed arm.
  • Parkinsonian: Shuffling, stooped, reduced arm swing, festination.
  • Ataxic: Wide-based, unsteady, veering (cerebellar).
  • Steppage: High lifting of legs due to foot drop (neuropathy).
  • Myopathic: Waddling, pelvic tilt.
