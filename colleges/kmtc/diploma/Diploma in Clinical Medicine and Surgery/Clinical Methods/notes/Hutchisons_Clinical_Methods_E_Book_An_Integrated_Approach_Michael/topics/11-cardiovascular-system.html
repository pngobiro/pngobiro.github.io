Introduction

Patterns of cardiovascular disease have changed significantly. While rheumatic, syphilitic, and tuberculous heart disease have declined in the developed world, coronary artery disease remains a major cause of premature death. Modern cardiology utilizes an array of high-tech diagnostic tools, yet the bedside diagnosis based on careful history and physical examination remains the foundation of clinical practice.

The cardiac history

The history should record details of presenting symptoms, risk factors, and family history.

Box 11.1 Structure for the cardiac history

  • Presenting complaint (PC): Commonly chest pain, dyspnoea, palpitation, dizziness, or syncope.
  • History of presenting complaint (HPC): Define nature, onset, duration, provoking/relieving factors.
  • Risk factors: Smoking, hypertension, hypercholesterolaemia, diabetes, family history.
  • Past medical history (PMH): Rheumatic fever, stroke, previous MI.
  • Family history: Premature vascular disease, sudden death, cardiomyopathies.
  • Drug history: Include alcohol quantification.

Chest pain

Myocardial ischaemia, pericarditis, aortic dissection, and pulmonary embolism are common causes of acute, severe chest pain.

Box 11.2 Angina Pectoris

üë§ Typical Patient

Middle-aged or elderly with risk factors (smoking, hypertension, diabetes, lipids).

‚ö° Major Symptoms

Exertional retrosternal chest pain ("heaviness", "tightness"). Radiates to arms, neck, jaw. Relieved by rest.

üîç Diagnosis

History is key. ECG may be normal or show Q waves. Stress testing or angiography confirms.

Box 11.5 Acute Coronary Syndromes (ACS)

Includes Unstable Angina, Non-STEMI, and STEMI.

  • Symptoms: Severe, prolonged chest pain at rest. Sweating, vomiting.
  • STEMI: ST elevation on ECG. Requires immediate reperfusion.
  • Non-STEMI: ST depression/T-wave inversion + Raised Troponin.
  • Unstable Angina: Ischaemic ECG changes but Normal Troponin.

Dyspnoea

Breathlessness is a major symptom of heart failure. It may present as:

  • Exertional dyspnoea: Symptom of pulmonary congestion and reduced cardiac output.
  • Orthopnoea: Breathlessness on lying flat, relieved by sitting up.
  • Paroxysmal Nocturnal Dyspnoea (PND): Waking from sleep gasping for air. Relieved by standing/sitting. Specific for heart failure.

Box 11.10 Congestive Heart Failure

Major Symptoms
  • Fatigue
  • Exertional dyspnoea
  • Orthopnoea
  • PND
Major Signs
  • Raised JVP
  • Basal crackles
  • Peripheral oedema
  • S3 gallop

The cardiac examination

A methodical approach is recommended, starting with inspection and proceeding to palpation and auscultation.

Box 11.11 Routine for Cardiovascular Examination

  1. 1Recline patient at 45¬∞. Wash hands. Introduce yourself.
  2. 2Hands: Clubbing, splinter haemorrhages, perfusion. Pulse (rate/rhythm).
  3. 3Face: Anaemia, cyanosis, xanthelasma.
  4. 4Neck: JVP (height/waveform), Carotid pulse (character/volume).
  5. 5Precordium: Inspect scars/pulsations. Palpate apex beat, heaves, thrills.
  6. 6Auscultation: Heart sounds (S1, S2) and murmurs at 4 areas.
  7. 7Back: Lung bases for crackles, sacral oedema.
  8. 8Abdomen/Legs: Hepatomegaly, peripheral oedema, peripheral pulses.

Arterial pulse

Assess rate, rhythm, volume, and character. The character is best assessed at the carotid artery.

Jugular venous pulse (JVP)

The JVP reflects right atrial pressure. It is measured vertically from the sternal angle.

Box 11.13 Characteristics of the JVP

  • Double waveform (in sinus rhythm)
  • Varies with respiration (falls with inspiration)
  • Varies with posture
  • Impalpable
  • Obliterated by pressure at root of neck
  • Hepatojugular reflux (rises with abdominal pressure)

Auscultation

Listen for heart sounds (S1, S2) and added sounds (S3, S4, clicks, snaps) and murmurs.

The electrocardiogram (ECG)

The ECG is essential for evaluating cardiac rhythm, conduction, and ischaemia.

Normal ECG

Myocardial Infarction

STEMI shows characteristic evolution: peaking T waves -> ST elevation -> Q waves -> T wave inversion.

Arrhythmias

Common arrhythmias include Atrial Fibrillation (irregularly irregular, no P waves), SVT (regular, narrow complex, rapid), and VT (regular, broad complex).

Cardiac Imaging

Chest X-ray

The CXR assesses heart size (cardiothoracic ratio) and pulmonary vasculature (oedema, plethora).

Echocardiography

Non-invasive ultrasound imaging of cardiac structure and function. Vital for assessing valve disease, wall motion, and pericardial fluid.

Valvular Heart Disease Summary

Aortic Stenosis

  • Symptoms: Angina, Syncope, Dyspnoea.
  • Signs: Slow-rising pulse, narrow pulse pressure, Ejection Systolic Murmur radiating to carotids.

Mitral Regurgitation

  • Symptoms: Dyspnoea, fatigue, palpitations (AF).
  • Signs: Pansystolic murmur at apex radiating to axilla, S3.

Aortic Regurgitation

  • Symptoms: Dyspnoea.
  • Signs: Collapsing pulse, wide pulse pressure, Early Diastolic Murmur.

Mitral Stenosis

  • Symptoms: Dyspnoea, haemoptysis.
  • Signs: Malar flush, tapping apex, Loud S1, Opening Snap, Mid-diastolic rumble.
