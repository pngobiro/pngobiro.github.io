Introduction

Psychiatry is the medical specialty that concerns itself with the diagnosis and management of mental illness. Medical students as well as lay people often feel confused about the distinction between psychiatry and other disciplines in which the prefix 'psych' appears, for example psychology and psychotherapy. The distinction is quite clear. Psychiatry is practised by medically qualified practitioners and concerns itself with diseases of the mind, just as gastroenterology is concerned with disease of the gut and cardiology with disease of the heart. The proper practice of psychiatry involves an understanding of the normal workings of the mind and human behaviour (the study of psychology) and a thorough knowledge of pathological manifestations of the mind and behaviours when there is disease of the mind or mental illness.

As in any medical specialty, the cornerstone of becoming a successful practitioner is mastery of how to take a detailed history and perform an examination of the mind, referred to as a mental state examination. It is also essential to perform a general physical examination.

History taking

In addition to providing the cornerstone on which to make a diagnosis, the initial history taking is an important beginning of the development of a therapeutic rapport with the patient upon which effective management rests.

Possibly more than in other specialties, there are steps to be taken even before commencing the conversation with the patient. The first step is to consider the context of the interview and where it will take place. The second is to think about what background information is accessible.

Context of the interview

There are important factors which will tend to be different in different settings, for example outpatients, the emergency department, medical or psychiatric ward.

Safety

The doctor should sit nearest the door and neither the patient nor any obstacles should block the doctor's ability to exit. There should be an alarm buzzer and someone available to respond to the alarm. In the emergency department where patients have been brought to hospital in a disturbed state, other safety measures may also be needed, for example checking for weapons and having a security presence.

Privacy

The patient should be interviewed in a quiet private space where he is not likely to be overheard by other patients and staff. Clearly there is a balance to be struck, and the need for police or security presence nearby will sometimes outweigh the privacy and confidentiality requirement.

The psychiatric history

Box 7.1 Suggested order for psychiatric history

  • Presenting complaint
  • History of presenting complaint
  • Previous medical history - psychiatric and physical
  • Current medication
  • Family history
  • Personal history (Childhood, Schooling, Occupation, Psychosexual)
  • Present social circumstances
  • Forensic history
  • Premorbid personality

History of presenting complaint

Obtain a detailed description of how symptoms began, enquiring about the relationship to any life events that might have precipitated their onset. Establishing if the symptoms are recent or longer-lasting helps to clarify if the current presentation is part of longstanding personality traits or due to the acute onset of psychiatric disorder.

Box 7.2 Features supporting a diagnosis of depression

  • Persistent/pervasive low mood, often tearfulness
  • Diurnal variation of mood, typically worse in the morning
  • Loss of motivation/interest
  • Anhedonia, i.e. inability to experience pleasure in things
  • Irritability and/or poor concentration
  • Lethargy, fatigue and lack of energy
  • Sleep disturbance, usually early-morning waking
  • Appetite and weight loss (increased in atypical depression)
  • Constipation and/or loss of libido
  • Ideas of hopelessness, worthlessness, guilt, persecution, nihilism
  • Loss of confidence and social withdrawal

Box 7.3 Somatic symptoms of anxiety

  • Headaches and muscular aches
  • Palpitations
  • Breathlessness
  • Chest pain
  • Faintness, light headedness
  • Sweating, flushes or pallor
  • Gastrointestinal disturbance

Box 7.4 Psychic symptoms of anxiety

  • Feelings of anxiety
  • Irritability
  • Inability to relax
  • Inability to concentrate
  • Worry/anxious rumination
  • Feeling of impending doom
  • Depersonalization

Alcohol use

Ascertain quantity and frequency of alcohol intake. If the level of alcohol use is above safe drinking limits, or if there are other indicators of a problem with alcohol, a full drinking history should be taken.

‚ö†Ô∏è Box 7.10 The CAGE questionnaire
  • C - Have you ever felt you needed to Cut down on your drinking?
  • A - Have people Annoyed you by criticizing your drinking?
  • G - Have you ever felt bad or Guilty about your drinking?
  • E - Have you ever had a drink first thing in the morning (Eye-opener)?

Two or more positive replies are indicative of alcohol misuse.

Box 7.9 Features of alcohol dependence

  • A strong desire or sense of compulsion to drink alcohol (craving)
  • Difficulty controlling intake of alcohol
  • Withdrawal symptoms when alcohol intake is reduced or stopped
  • Tolerance (increased amount required to achieve same effects)
  • Neglect of alternative activities
  • Continued drinking despite harm caused

Personality assessment

Do not rely too heavily on a patient's self-assessment of personality, though it is important to hear how he views himself. The important areas to cover are shown in Box 7.11.

Box 7.11 Personality assessment

  • Usual mood: Pessimistic and worrying or optimistic. Stable mood or mood swings.
  • Relationships with others: Deep and enduring or superficial short term relationships, quality of marriage, work relationships.
  • Character: Shy, sensitive, introvert, extrovert, quarrelsome, laid back, dependent, controlling. Any obsessional traits.
  • Leisure activities and habits: Risk taking activities, collecting, passive spectator or active participant.
  • Personal standards and attitudes: Religious belief, moral views, attitudes to health.

The mental state examination

The mental state examination is the cornerstone of the psychiatric assessment. It enables a systematic observation of clinical symptoms and signs on which a differential diagnosis is based. A basic comprehensive structure is outlined in Box 7.12.

Box 7.12 The mental state examination

  • Appearance and behaviour
  • Speech
  • Mood: subjective and objective
  • Thought: form and content
  • Perception
  • Cognitive assessment
  • Insight

Appearance and behaviour

Describe how the patient appears. Does his dress and general presentation seem appropriate? Is he relaxed or anxious? Is he agitated, angry or behaving as though suspicious?

‚ñ∂ Movement changes (Box 7.13)
  • Dystonia: due to older major antipsychotic drugs
  • Choreiform: Huntington's chorea
  • Tics: quite frequent in childhood and stress
  • Mannerisms: habitual repetitive voluntary movements
  • Stereotypies: repeated uniform purposeless movements
  • Tardive dyskinesia: abnormal movements (facial grimaces, writhing) often from medication
  • Catatonia: stupor, sometimes alternating with excitement

Speech

This is usually divided into two components: rate and structure of speech.

Box 7.15 Disorders of structure of speech

  • Circumstantial: frequent digressions, taking a long time to get to the point.
  • Tangentiality: answers seems unrelated or only slightly related to questions.
  • Flight of ideas: speech speeds from one idea to an associated one but with loss of the end goal.
  • Loosening of associations: jumps from one idea into another apparently unrelated one ('Knight's Move' speech).
  • Perseveration: repetition of a theme or answer when no longer appropriate.

Thought

Attention should be paid to the content and theme of what the patient discusses. It is important always to establish whether or not a patient experiences suicidal thoughts.

‚ö†Ô∏è Box 7.17 Risk factors for suicidal behaviour
  • Previous self-harm
  • Continuing suicidal thoughts
  • Current depressive or schizophrenic psychosis
  • Male, aged over 40 years
  • Social isolation
  • Chronic painful illness
  • Alcoholism and drug abuse

Abnormal beliefs

The main types of abnormal belief are primary and secondary delusions, overvalued ideas and sensitive ideas of reference.

Box 7.19 Definitions

Delusion: A fixed false belief, held with complete conviction, which is not amenable to alteration by any argument, and which is not shared by others of the same social and cultural background.

Overvalued ideas: Beliefs which are very prominent in the patient's mind and with which he is unreasonably preoccupied, but the patient is able to acknowledge that the belief may not be true.

Perception

Abnormal perception can occur in any of the sensory modalities. Hallucinations are perceptions that are not based on any real external stimulus. Illusions occur when the object is real but is misperceived.

Box 7.20 First-rank symptoms of schizophrenia

The presence of any one symptom in the absence of organic brain disease is regarded as diagnostic of schizophrenia (Schneider).

  • 1Audible thoughts (thought echo)
  • 2Argumentative voices
  • 3Running commentary voices
  • 4Thought insertion or withdrawal
  • 5Thought broadcasting
  • 6Passivity 'made' experiences (impulses, sensations, acts)
  • 7Delusional perception

Cognition

An assessment of cognitive function is a routine part of any mental state examination. A useful measure is the Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE).
