Introduction

Although, proportionally, a greater contribution towards the diagnosis of ophthalmic disorders is made by the examination compared to the history, it is important to obtain a detailed account of the presenting complaint and associated visual symptoms from the patient with an ocular problem.

History

Disturbance of vision is the most important ocular symptom. It may be sudden or gradual, unilateral or bilateral, and lead to loss of central vision or partial field loss. Sudden visual disturbance should be assessed urgently.

The Red Eye

A red eye is a common presentation. The presence of pain, visual loss, or photophobia helps distinguish serious from minor causes.

Examination

Eye examination includes testing visual acuity, colour vision, visual fields, pupillary reactions, and ophthalmoscopy. Detailed examination of the anterior segment requires a slit lamp.

Visual Acuity

Tested at 6 meters using a Snellen chart. If the patient wears distance glasses, they should be worn. Test each eye separately.

If vision is less than 6/6, use a pinhole. If acuity improves with the pinhole, the cause is a refractive error. Near vision is tested using a near vision chart (Fig. 19.2).

Recording Visual Acuity

Visual acuity is recorded as a fraction: Distance / Line Read.

  • 6/6: Normal vision.
  • 6/60: Can only read top letter at 6m.
  • CF: Counting Fingers (if cannot read chart).
  • HM: Hand Movements.
  • PL: Perception of Light.

Colour Vision

Tested using Ishihara plates (Fig. 19.5). Acquired defects (e.g., optic neuritis) often affect red-green discrimination. Red desaturation (red appears "washed out") is a sensitive sign of optic nerve dysfunction.

Visual Fields

Tested by confrontation. Automated perimetry (Humphrey field analyzer) provides a quantitative map of the visual field.

Pupils

Examine for size, shape, and reaction to light (direct and consensual) and accommodation.

Common Pupil Abnormalities

  • Relative Afferent Pupillary Defect (RAPD): Pupil paradoxically dilates when light is swung from the normal to the affected eye. Indicates optic nerve or extensive retinal disease.
  • Horner's Syndrome: Miosis (small pupil), ptosis, anhidrosis. Pupil reacts normally to light.
  • Holmes-Adie Pupil: Dilated pupil, slow reaction to light, better to accommodation.
  • Argyll Robertson Pupil: Small, irregular pupils. Accommodate but do not react to light (Neurosyphilis).

Direct Ophthalmoscopy

Systematic examination of the fundus is essential. Look at the optic disc, vessels, and retina/macula.

Box 19.5 Technique of Ophthalmoscopy

  • 1Use your RIGHT eye for the patient's RIGHT eye.
  • 2Start at arm's length to check the red reflex. Opacities (cataract) appear black.
  • 3Move in close. Find a vessel and follow it to the optic disc.
  • 4Focus using the lens wheel.

The Optic Disc

Assess colour, margins, and the cup.

Retinal Vascular Disease

Look for arteriolar narrowing, AV nipping (hypertension), haemorrhages, and exudates.

Box 19.9 Common Retinal Abnormalities

White/Yellow
  • Cotton Wool Spots: Retinal ischaemia (infarction of nerve fibre layer).
  • Hard Exudates: Lipid leakage (Diabetes).
  • Drusen: Waste deposits (Age-related macular degeneration).

  • Dot/Blot Haemorrhages: Diabetes.
  • Flame Haemorrhages: Hypertension, Vein occlusion.
  • New Vessels: Proliferative retinopathy.

Special Examination Techniques

Slit Lamp Examination

Provides a stereoscopic magnified view of the anterior segment. Essential for diagnosing iritis (cells/flare), corneal ulcers (staining), and cataracts.

Tonometry

Measures intraocular pressure (IOP). Goldmann applanation tonometry is the gold standard. Normal range 10-21 mmHg.

Eyelids and Orbit

Inspect for ptosis, retraction, entropion/ectropion, and lumps. Exophthalmometry measures proptosis (e.g. Thyroid Eye Disease).
