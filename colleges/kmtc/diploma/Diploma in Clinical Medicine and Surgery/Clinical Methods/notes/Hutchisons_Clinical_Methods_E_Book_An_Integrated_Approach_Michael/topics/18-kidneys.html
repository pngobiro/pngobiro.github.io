The diagnostic process in nephrology and urology

These systems are dependent on laboratory, histopathology and imaging techniques to complete the diagnostic process. Diseases of the kidney and urinary tract manifest a restricted array of symptoms and signs, but almost all patients can be categorized into a well-defined renal or urological syndrome.

Symptoms of renal and urological disease

Pain

Pain is often due to obstruction, infection or tumour. Renal pain is felt in the flank or loin. Ureteric obstruction (e.g. stone) causes colic radiating to the iliac fossa, testicle or labia. Acute bladder outflow obstruction presents with severe suprapubic pain. Cystitis causes suprapubic pain, dysuria, frequency and strangury.

Haematuria

Blood in the urine may be macroscopic (visible) or microscopic. It is crucial to determine if the source is glomerular (usually painless, continuous, microscopic) or from the lower tract (often intermittent, painful, macroscopic).

Box 18.1 Causes of haematuria

Renal
  • Infarct/papillary necrosis
  • Trauma/Tuberculosis
  • Stones
  • Tumours (Renal cell, Wilms')
  • Glomerulonephritis
Postrenal
  • Ureteric stones/neoplasms
  • Bladder tumours
  • Cystitis (radiation, drug, bacterial)
  • Prostatic enlargement
  • Urethral neoplasms

Voiding symptoms

  • Oliguria/Anuria: <500ml/day or complete absence. Suggests renal failure or obstruction.
  • Polyuria: High volume. Causes include osmotic diuresis (diabetes), diabetes insipidus, or psychogenic polydipsia.
  • Nocturia: Waking to void. Causes include polyuria, reduced bladder capacity, or cardiac failure (fluid redistribution).
  • Dysuria: Pain on micturition ('burning', 'scalding'). Usually implies infection.
  • Hesitancy/Poor stream: Suggests bladder outflow obstruction (e.g. prostatic hypertrophy).

Physical signs in renal and urological disease

General features

Patients with chronic renal failure may exhibit sallow skin, pallor (anaemia), bruises, and scratch marks (pruritus). Uraemic frost is a preterminal sign. In children, renal failure causes growth retardation and rickets.

The circulation

Assessment of fluid status is crucial. Hypervolaemia manifests as hypertension, raised JVP, and oedema. Hypovolaemia shows postural hypotension and tachycardia.

Abdominal examination

Palpable kidneys are usually enlarged (hydronephrosis, polycystic disease, tumour). A distended bladder is palpable suprapubically. Rectal examination allows assessment of the prostate in men.

Laboratory assessment: The urine

Urinalysis is essential. Mid-stream specimens (MSU) minimize contamination.

General Properties

Quantity: Normal 750-2500 ml/24h.
Colour: Pale (dilute), dark (concentrated/bile), red (blood/drugs/beetroot), brown (myoglobin).
SG/Osmolality: In renal failure, urine becomes isosthenuric (fixed osmolality ~300 mOsmol/kg, SG 1.010).

Proteinuria

Detection of protein is a key indicator of renal disease. Albumin:creatinine ratio (ACR) or protein:creatinine ratio (PCR) are used for quantification.

Microscopy

Examination of the urine sediment can reveal cells and casts indicative of parenchymal disease.

Measurement of GFR

GFR is the best overall index of renal function. Plasma creatinine is a useful but crude surrogate. Estimated GFR (eGFR) formulae are now standard.

Imaging of the urinary tract

Ultrasound

First-line test. Provides renal size, detects obstruction (hydronephrosis), cysts, and tumours. Doppler assesses blood flow.

Intravenous Urography (IVU)

Excellent for visualizing the collecting system and ureters. Limited use in renal failure.

Radionuclide studies

DTPA or MAG3 scans assess differential function and excretion/obstruction. DMSA assesses cortical morphology and scarring.

CT and MRI

CT defines anatomy and retroperitoneal pathology. Spiral CT is the choice for stones. MRI avoids ionizing radiation and iodinated contrast (though gadolinium has risks in severe renal failure).
