Gastrointestinal System

Introduction

The human gastrointestinal (GI) tract is a complex system approximately 8m in length. Symptoms of GI disorders are often non-specific. The liver, biliary system, and pancreas are embryologically part of the GI tract. This chapter also covers the examination of the kidneys, groins, and genitalia from a systematic standpoint.

Symptoms of gastrointestinal disease

It is helpful to remember that patients present with functional or disease processes rather than anatomical sites. History taking that follows these processes is more likely to lead to a meaningful diagnosis.

Box 12.1 Common symptoms

  • Dysphagia and odynophagia
  • Heartburn, reflux, and indigestion (dyspepsia)
  • Vomiting and haematemesis
  • Abdominal pain and distension
  • Constipation, diarrhoea, and melaena
  • Jaundice
  • Weight loss and anorexia

Abdominal pain

Abdominal pain is a common symptom. It is important to characterize its site, intensity, character, radiation, duration, and aggravating factors. The embryological origin of the gut often determines where visceral pain is felt (foregut: upper abdomen; midgut: periumbilical; hindgut: lower abdomen).

Bleeding

Haematemesis results from bleeding in the upper GI tract. Melaena describes jet-black, tarry stools indicating bleeding above the ileocaecal valve. Rectal bleeding (bright red) usually indicates a colonic or rectal source.

Physical examination

General signs

Systemic features of GI disease include jaundice, lymphadenopathy, and specific stigmata of chronic liver disease.

Box 12.3 Stigmata of chronic liver disease

  • Hands/Skin: Spider naevi, palmar erythema, clubbing, leuconychia, bruising, Dupuytren's contracture.
  • Endocrine: Gynaecomastia, testicular atrophy, loss of body hair.
  • Other: Parotid swelling, hepatic foetor, hepatic flap (asterixis).

Other Palpable Structures

Percussion: Ascites

There are three common causes of diffuse abdominal enlargement: Ascites, Ovarian Cyst, and Intestinal Obstruction. Percussion distinguishes them.

Box 12.4 Signs of Ascites

  • Dull in flanks
  • Umbilicus everted
  • Shifting dullness positive
  • Fluid thrill positive (Fig. 12.25)

The Groins

Examination of the groins is essential. Ask the patient to cough to detect an expansile impulse.

Rectal examination

The left lateral position is best. Inspect for tags, haemorrhoids, fissures, or fistulae. Palpate for tone, prostate (men), cervix (women), and rectal masses.

Investigations

Endoscopy

Upper GI endoscopy and colonoscopy are key for visualizing mucosa and obtaining biopsies.

Radiology

Plain X-rays are useful for obstruction and perforation. Barium studies define anatomy. CT and MRI are essential for staging malignancy and examining solid organs.
