Sexually Transmitted Infections

‚úçÔ∏è Beng T. Goh ‚è±Ô∏è 40 min read

Introduction

There are two main principles in the management of sexually transmitted infections (STIs). First, an infected patient implies that at least one other person is also infected; treating a patient in isolation will not control the spread of disease. Second, a patient may harbour more than one sexually transmitted infection. Many infections are asymptomatic. Patients are often uncomfortable or embarrassed to give their sexual history; the interview must be carried out in private, in strict confidence, and avoiding any judgmental attitude.

Box 21.1 Common Sexually Transmitted Agents

Bacteria
  • Treponema pallidum (Syphilis)
  • Neisseria gonorrhoeae (Gonorrhoea)
  • Chlamydia trachomatis (NGU, LGV)
  • Haemophilus ducreyi (Chancroid)
Viruses
  • Herpes simplex (HSV 1 & 2)
  • Human papilloma virus (Warts)
  • HIV
  • Hepatitis A, B, C

History

Enquiry into the less embarrassing aspects of the medical history fosters confidence before moving to intimate matters. Details of the sexual history should be obtained by simple, direct questions.

Box 21.2 History Checklist

  • Urethral/Vaginal discharge
  • Dysuria
  • Genital ulcer (painful/painless)
  • Painful scrotal swelling
  • Pubic/Genital itch
  • Genital rash
  • Dyspareunia (superficial/deep)
  • Anorectal symptoms

Presenting Symptoms

Urethral discharge: Purulent suggests gonorrhoea; mucoid suggests chlamydia/non-specific urethritis.
Dysuria: "Burning" or "stinging". Distinguish from false dysuria (urine contacting inflamed skin).
Vaginal discharge: Itchy (thrush/trichomonas), offensive (bacterial vaginosis/trichomonas/foreign body).
Anogenital ulcer: Painful (Herpes, Chancroid) vs Painless (Syphilis, LGV).
Genital lumps: Warts (Fig 21.1), Molluscum contagiosum.

Sexual History Questions

  • Last intercourse: Date establishes incubation period.
  • Partners: Gender (Men/Women/Both), Regular/Casual.
  • Practices: Oral, Vaginal, Anal (Insertive/Receptive). This dictates examination sites.
  • Protection: Condom use.
  • Travel: Sexual contact abroad (tropical STIs).

Genital Examination

Must be performed in a well-lit room with gloves. Chaperoning is essential for female patients examined by male clinicians.

Male Genitalia

Inspect penis, prepuce (retract if present), glans, and meatus. Look for discharge, warts, or ulcers. Palpate the scrotal contents.

Female Genitalia

Examine in lithotomy position. Inspect vulva, perineum, and anus. Speculum examination allows visualization of the cervix and vaginal walls. Look for cervicitis (Fig 21.3) or discharge. Bimanual examination assesses pelvic tenderness (PID).

Anorectal Examination

Important in MSM or women with symptoms. Inspect for warts, fissures, or discharge. Proctoscopy allows visualization of the rectal mucosa.

HIV Infections

HIV infection is categorized into clinical stages. Assessment includes looking for signs of immunosuppression and opportunistic infections.

Box 21.3 CDC Clinical Categories

  • AAsymptomatic: Acute infection, Persistent generalized lymphadenopathy.
  • BSymptomatic: Oral thrush, Hairy leukoplakia, Shingles (>1 dermatome), Fever/Diarrhoea >1 month.
  • CAIDS-defining: Oesophageal candidiasis, PCP, Kaposi's sarcoma, TB, CMV retinitis.

Systemic Signs

Look for oral candidiasis (Fig 21.7) and oral hairy leukoplakia, both common markers of HIV progression.

Special Investigations

Microscopy

Gram Stain: Essential for urethral discharge. Gram-negative intracellular diplococci indicate Gonorrhoea (Fig 21.8). Pus cells without organisms suggest NGU (often Chlamydia).

Dark-ground Microscopy: Used for syphilis chancres. Shows spirochaetes (Treponema pallidum) with characteristic corkscrew motility (Fig 21.9).

Other Tests

  • NAATs (PCR): Gold standard for Chlamydia and Gonorrhoea (urine or swab).
  • Serology: Syphilis (VDRL/RPR + Specific tests), HIV, Hepatitis B/C.
  • Herpes Swab: PCR or culture from base of ulcer/blister (Fig 21.10).
