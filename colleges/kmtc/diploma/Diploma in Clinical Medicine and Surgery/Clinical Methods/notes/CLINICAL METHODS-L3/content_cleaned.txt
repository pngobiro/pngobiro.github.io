<p>Aims of Clinical Methods</p>
<ul>
<li>A student should learn the art of taking detailed informative history. A good history is an important aspect of medicine.</li>
<li>Both the important positive and important negative physical signs should be noted so as to reach a conclusion.</li>
<li>Clinical methods are acquired by a combination of study and experience</li>
<li>There is always something new to learn</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id=""><span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"></span></h1></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="cont">CONT'<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#cont" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>It requires:</p>
<ul>
<li>Knowledge of disease and its patterns of presentation</li>
<li>Ability to interpret a patient's symptoms and signs</li>
</ul>
<p>Patient – Doctor Relationship</p>
<ul>
<li>It is essential that both patient and clinician feel at ease, neither feels threatened by the encounter</li>
<li>Does the patient smile, or appear anxious?</li>
<li>Do they make good eye contact?</li>
<li>Are they frightened or depressed?</li>
<li>Are posture and stance normal?</li>
<li>Is the patient short of breath, or wheezing?</li>
<li>Students/clinicians need to approach patients not as “cases” or “diseases”, but as individuals whose problems/symptoms are to be heard empathetically.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id=""><span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"></span></h1></div>
<p>Skills in History Taking</p>
<ul>
<li>It is vital for the doctor to steer and mould the process</li>
<li>Ensures information gathered is complete, coherent and, logical</li>
<li>It is very important to use words the patient will understand and use appropriately</li>
<li>During any consultation non-verbal communication is as important as what the patient says</li>
<li>The history of a patient should contain all the facts of medical significance in the life of a patient</li>
<li>The history should be recorded in a chronological order, and recent events should be given most attention.</li>
<li>Allow the patients to narrate his/her history in his/her own way and language without any interruption.</li>
<li>Physicians/clinicians should be careful not to suggest the answers to the questions being posed.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id=""><span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>NB: History taking is not a mere records of some questions and answers between the doctor and the patient, listen to the patient, he/she is telling you the diagnosis.</li>
</ul>
<p>History Techniques</p>
<ul>
<li>If a patient gives a positive response it leads to further questioning e.g.</li>
<li>Headache – which part, when, always on/off etc, while a negative response moves the clinician on the next question</li>
<li>Technical words should be avoided , the patient need to understand what is being asked/said</li>
<li>Remember the public/lay men are increasingly aware of medical terms or medical matters through the internet and mass media, but this does not necessarily mean they understand the terms</li>
<li>Therefore certain terms having different meaning may be clarified if used by the patient</li>
</ul>
<p>Types of Enquires/Questions</p>
<ol>
<li>Open ended questions:- they do not exclude any answer category. They give minimal structure and direction to the answer so that the client has the freedom to answer in his/ her own way.</li>
</ol>
<p>• Examples</p>
<p>• Tell me about yourself</p>
<p>• When you are unable to sleep, what are you thinking about?</p>
<ol start="2">
<li>Closed ended questions:- they provide limited options for answers which are often short(yes or no). They are easily suggestive/leading</li>
</ol>
<p>• Example</p>
<p>• Was the discharge smelling</p>
<p>• Is the abdominal pain on the right or the left</p>
<ol start="4">
<li>PROBING-This is whereby you try to encourage the patient to give more specific information e.g what happened next, how did it make you feel .</li>
</ol>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="general-history-taking">GENERAL HISTORY TAKING<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#general-history-taking" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>This is an important component of medical and health care practice because it is what provides the necessary information and the relevant details for making of a diagnosis and subsequently the decision on patient management</li>
</ul>
<p>Requirements</p>
<p>• A quiet and conducive consultation room.</p>
<p>• Table, chair, examination couch.</p>
<p>• Writing materials</p>
<p>• An interpreter/ informant</p>
<p>• Chaperon.</p>
<p>Procedure for history taking</p>
<ul>
<li>Welcome the patient(greet) and ensure his or her comfort(offer a seat/ the couch)</li>
<li>Introduce yourself</li>
<li>Assure confidentiality</li>
<li>Establish the language of communication</li>
<li>Conduct the interview using relevant communication skills.</li>
<li>Quick assessment of the patient</li>
</ul>
<p>Does the patient smile, or appear furtive or anxious?</p>
<p>Does the patient make good eye contact?</p>
<p>Is he frightened or depressed?</p>
<p>Are posture and stance normal?</p>
<p>Is he short of breath, or wheezing?</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Note taking is important during consultations while being able to see the patient and establish eye contact. Show empathy and awareness of his needs during the discussion of symptoms, much of which may be distressing or even embarrassing.</p>
</li>
<li></li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="challenges-in-history-taking">Challenges in History Taking<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#challenges-in-history-taking" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Anger/hostility</li>
<li>Intoxicated patients</li>
<li>Handling depressed cases</li>
<li>Sexually attractive or seductive patients</li>
<li>Confusing behavior /histories</li>
<li>Limited intelligence interfering with recall</li>
<li>Development disabilities, mental retardation</li>
<li>Communication barriers - due to language social, cultural, religion differences, sight , speech, hearing impairments</li>
<li>Unconscious patients- clinician not able to get facts relating to the illness.</li>
</ul>
<p>AIM OF HISTORY TAKING</p>
<ul>
<li>To understand patients owns perception of their problem and to start or complete the diagnosis process. This requires knowledge of disease pattern and ability to interpret patients signs and symptoms.</li>
<li>Symptoms: what a patient complains of</li>
<li>Signs: what you find on examination of the patient</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id=""><span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"></span></h1></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="history-taking-components-of-history-taking">History taking Components of history taking<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#history-taking-components-of-history-taking" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="i-patients-particulars-biodata">(i) Patients Particulars (Biodata)<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#i-patients-particulars-biodata" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Date &amp; time- for medical records, administrative and legal purposes, to judge and determine the progress of patient who are admitted in the ward.</li>
<li>3 names (to avoid confusion, coincidence of names, mistaken identity)</li>
<li>Exact age (for admission purposes, making diagnosis, prescription purposes)</li>
<li>Sex/gender (to decide the ward for admission, for making diagnosis)</li>
<li>Inpatient/outpatient number (for record keeping, for identification purposes)</li>
<li>Occupation (some conditions are related to a person's occupation),</li>
<li>Marital status</li>
<li>Religion ((religious believes have dimensions on management, the religion could have contributed to the disease, some religion forbids some form of treatment- blood transfusion)</li>
<li>Address and residence (including telephone contacts, physical address i.e. the village, sub-location, location, county. Helps to identify common diseases in certain areas- epidemic diseases, for patient follow-up or home visits. For tracing relatives and next of kin incase of death or abscondement for legal and medical purposes</li>
<li>Informant (this is the person who gives information or history of the patient on behalf of the patient/if the patient is not able to speak- very ill/children/patients who are in coma. The information can be given by a parent, relative, friend, neighbour, colleague, policeman. In case of a child, the best informant would be the mother/caretaker.</li>
<li>Next of kin (important to trace relatives in case of death or abscondement, for legal and medical purposes)</li>
</ul>
<p>(ii)Chief Complain (CC)</p>
<p>• Main complaints for which the patient is seeking medical attention and the duration, this is the patient’s chief symptom(s) in their own words e.g. haemoptysis vs. coughing up blood.</p>
<p>• Ask the patient an open ended question such as “what is your problem today”? “What brought you to hospital today?”</p>
<p>• The complaints may be one, two or more. All should be recorded in a chronological order.</p>
<p>• Always seek clarification of the patients symptoms</p>
<p>• Headache on and off x 2/12</p>
<p>• Difficulty in breathing x 2/52</p>
<p>• Chest pain x 1/7</p>
<p>• The duration of a complaint may be difficult especially in elderly patients or in uneducated patients. In such situations, approximate duration may be asked.</p>
<p>• NB: Make every attempt to use /quote the patient own words e.g. what is your main complaint and when were you last in your usual state of health.</p>
<p>(iii) History of the Presenting Illness (HPI)</p>
<p>• Ask the patient to tell the detailed story of his/her illness from the day it started till that day he/she has sought medical attention.</p>
<p>• Patient should not be interrupted while narrating the history</p>
<p>• Ask about and document the details of the presenting complaint, nature of the problem, how it started.</p>
<p>• For each symptom determine;</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>The exact nature of symptom</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The onset- the time/date it began (suddenly/gradually), if longstanding, why is the patient seeking help now?</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>• If the patients describes the complaints in medical terms rheumatism/arthritis (to mean joint pains), malaria (to mean chills/rigors), migraine (to mean headache), then he /she should be asked to tell what actually happens during these complaints.</p>
<p>• Periodicity and frequency- is the symptom constant or intermittent</p>
<p>• Change of the symptom over time- is it improving or deteriorating over time</p>
<p>• Exacerbating factors- what makes the symptom worse</p>
<p>• Relieving factors- what makes the symptom better</p>
<p>• Any other associated symptom</p>
<p>• Clinician should enquire about each and every complaints presented by the patient.</p>
<p>For example: Analysis of a complaint / symptoms</p>
<p>• The most common complaint that brings the patient to hospital is pain. Ask about the following in relation to pain.</p>
<p>• Site- where exactly is the pain, ask the patient to point to the site with one finger</p>
<p>• Onset- mode and rate of onset, how did it start, over how long (the duration)?</p>
<p>•</p>
<p>• Radiation- is the pain static or moves from one place to another?</p>
<p>• Character- is the pain dull, aching, stabbing or burning?</p>
<p>• Severity – how severe is the pain does it interfere with his/her daily activities? Pain is scored out of 10 (0-10).</p>
<p>• Timing- is there specific time when patient experiences this pain, is it on /off?</p>
<p>• Exaggeration- what brings about the pain, what makes the pain worse?</p>
<p>• Relief – what relief the pain, what makes it better e.g. change in position, rest, food, defecation or passage of wind.</p>
<p>• Other associated symptoms- is the pain associated with other symptoms e.g. nausea, vomiting, dyspepsia, shortness of breath etc</p>

<p>• Perform a brief screen of the other bodily systems</p>

<p>• Is it paroxysmal or intermittent, it is induced or relieved by exercises?</p>

<p>• Ask about cramp-like pains in the calves when walking relieved by rest.</p>

<p>Ask about headache</p>

<p>• Is the headache induced by stress (tension headache)?</p>
<p>• Is it radiating to other part(s) etc?</p>
<p>• What relieves the headache?</p>

<p>(v)Past medical and Surgical History</p>
<p>• Any previous/past history should be asked.</p>

<p>xii) Summarizing a case</p>
<p>Purpose of the summary- provides sufficient information to understand the factors which led to the present illness, something of the person in whom the illness is found, and sufficient detail of history, clinical findings and special investigations for the reasoning behind your diagnosis to be appreciated. This is described in detail below. However, it is frequently necessary to describe a patient’s problems in outline only, without formally presenting the case.</p>
<p>Cont’</p>
<p>Important points to remember;</p>
<ul>
<li>For a summary, make sure that every point you make is a vital point; do not be tempted to rehash the whole presentation. Make sure the few points you choose to give are stated in a logical order; i.e. beginning with the factors which underlie the current illness, proceeding to the details of that current illness, and followed by subsequent course and plans. For a summary, brevity( concise/exact use of words) is vital. Your summary should not require more than 4 or 5 sentences.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="xiii-general-examination">xiii) General examination<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#xiii-general-examination" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>It is assumed that you clinicians/physicians will act with professionalism, integrity, honesty, and with respect for the dignity and privacy of your patients</li>
<li>Chaperone should be present during any intimate examination- ideally be the same gender as the patient.</li>
</ul>
<ul>
<li>
<p>The right approach</p>
<ul>
<li>One important rule is that you should always stand at the patient's right hand side.</li>
<li>This gives them a feeling of control over the situation (most people are right handed)</li>
<li>All the standard examination techniques are formulated with this orientation in mind</li>
<li>The general examination starts as soon as the patient enters the examination room. The physician should always try to assess the patient’s general appearance which include demeanour, personal cleanliness/hygiene and the nature and status of clothing</li>
<li>Use all senses well (sight,hearing,tactile,smell etc)</li>
<li>A good general examination requires a co-operative patient and a quiet, warm and well lit room. Day light is better than artificial light, which may mask changes in skin colour e.g. the faint yellow tinge of slight jaundice.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Cont’</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Every attempt should be made to reassure and relax the patient</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>A chaperone should always be present when a male physician/clinician is examining a female patient and vice versa and during rectal and vaginal examination- both to reassure the patient and to protect the physician from subsequent accusations of improper conduct</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Expose appropriately</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Start the examination in a manner that will appear relevant to the patient e.g. a patient presenting with cough, chest pains, start with examination of the chest (affected system)</p>
</li>

<p>(2) Examine for Cyanosis</p>
<ul>
<li>Cyanosis refers to a bluish discoloration of the skin and mucous membranes, produced by presence of reduced haemoglobin in the blood either locally as in impaired peripheral circulation or generally, when oxygenation of the blood is defective.</li>
</ul>
<p>Cont’</p>
<ul>
<li>Cyanosis occur due to reduction in blood supply or a slowing of the peripheral circulation.</li>
<li>Arises through exposure to cold, reduced cardiac output or peripheral vascular disease.</li>
<li>One cannot have central cyanosis without demonstrating peripheral cyanosis, but peripheral cyanosis can occur alone</li>
</ul>
<p>Types of Cyanosis</p>
<ul>
<li>Central cyanosis- results from imperfect oxygenation of blood, as seen in heart failure and some lung disease or from mixture of desaturated venous blood with arterial blood due to right to left (venous-arterial) shunt in the heart.</li>
<li>In this case the cyanosis is general and characteristically affects the tongue, lips which appear blue as well as the limbs. The cyanosed extremities are warm.</li>
<li>Peripheral cyanosis- is due to excessive reduction of Oxyhaemoglobin in the capillaries when the flow of blood is slowed. This happens due to exposure to cold, when there is venous obstruction or in heart failure. The cyanosed extremities (toes, fingers) appear blue and are cold, the tongue is unaffected.</li>
<li>Cyanosis of heart failure is often due to both central and peripheral causes. Carbon monoxide poisoning produces a generalized cherry-red discolouration, due to the presence of carboxyhaemoglobin</li>
</ul>
<ol start="3">
<li>Examine for Jaundice</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>Jaundice is yellow pigmentation/discolouration of those tissues in the body which contain elastin (skin, sclera, and mucosa) Occurs due to an increase in plasma bilirubin (visible at &gt;35μmol/L or ≥ 2.5mg %) The level of bilirubin &lt; 2.5mg % produce sub-clinical jaundice which may not be detected.</li>
</ul>

<li>Lymphadenopathy is the enlargement of lymph nodes.</li>
<li>In health patients, palpable glands can be detected especially in the axilla and the groin. They are usually less than 1cm in diameter; lymph nodes greater than 2cm in groin are considered abnormal and pathological</li>
</ul>
<p>Important groups of lymph nodes includes:</p>
<p>• Pre-auricular- in front of the ear</p>
<p>• Post-auricular (posterior auricular)- superficial to the mastoid process behind the ear.</p>
<p>• Occipital- below the occiput at the base of the skull.</p>
<p>• Tonsilar- at the angle of the mandible</p>
<p>• Submandibular- midway between the angle and the tip of the mandible</p>
<p>• Submental- in the midline, a few centimeters behind the tip of the mandible</p>
<p>• Superficial cervical- superficial to sternocleidomastoid</p>
<p>• Posterior cervical- along the anterior of the trapezius</p>
<p>• Deep cervical- lymph node chain deep to the sternocleidomastoid and often inaccessible to examination</p>
<p>• Supraclavicular- deep to the angle formed by the clavicle and sternocleidomastoid</p>
<p>• Axillary- 5 groups i.e. lateral group, central group, apical group, pectoral group and subscapular group</p>
<p>• Inguinal and femoral lymph nodes- horizontal and vertical groups</p>
<p>• Popliteal lymphnodes- behind the knee</p>

<ul>
<li>Site: Acute and chronic infections and metastatic carcinoma will cause localized lymphadenopathy</li>
<li>Number: how many nodes are enlarged?</li>
<li>Size: normal nodes are not palpable. Palpable nodes therefore are enlarged. Measure their length and width</li>
<li>Consistency: malignant lymph nodes feel firm or hard and irregular.</li>
<li>Enlarged nodes secondary to infection may feel rubbery</li>
<li>Tenderness: painful, tender nodes usually imply infection.</li>
<li>Fixation: nodes that are fixed to surrounding tissue are highly suspicious of malignancy.</li>
<li>Matted glands may occur in tuberculous lymphadenopathy.</li>
<li>Overlying skin: inflamed nodes may cause redness and swelling in the overlying skin.</li>
<li>Spread of a metastatic carcinoma into the surrounding tissue may cause oedema and surface texture changes</li>
</ul>

<p>Using the pads of your index and middle fingers, move the skin over the underlying tissues in each area.</p>
<p>The patient should be relaxed, with neck flexed slightly forward and slightly toward the side being examined.</p>

<p>Tender nodes suggest inflammation</p>
<p>Hard or fixed nodes suggest malignancy</p>

<p>Examine the Nutritional Status of the patient</p>
<p>• Assessment of nutritional state of a patient is an important part of clinical examination because nutritional depletion may eventually result in malnutrition which has its functional consequences such as reduced immune response, muscle weakness, oedema, confusion, or neuropathy.</p>
<p>• Examine for wasting or thinness of muscles, oedema, pallor, weakness, loss of skin elasticity, and other signs of nutrients and vitamins deficiency are pointers towards poor nutrition or malnutrition</p>
