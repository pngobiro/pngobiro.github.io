Essay Questions

1.
STIs are common communicable diseases of Public Health significance:
a) Outline the risk factors of STIs (6 marks)
b) Outline the holistic management of STIs (2 marks)
c) State the control measures for STIs (2 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

a) Risk factors for STIs:

  • Behavioral: Multiple sexual partners, unprotected sex (no condom use), early sexual debut, alcohol and drug abuse (impairs judgment).
  • Demographic: Young age (adolescents/young adults), female gender (biological vulnerability).
  • Socio-economic: Poverty (sex work for survival), migration/mobility, lack of education.
  • Biological: Presence of ulcerative STIs facilitates HIV transmission.

b) Holistic Management (Syndromic Approach +):

  • 4 Cs:
    • Counseling (risk reduction).
    • Compliance (adherence to treatment).
    • Condom promotion/provision.
    • Contact tracing (treating partners).
  • Plus effective chemotherapy (drugs) for the patient.

c) Control Measures:

  • Health education (ABC strategy: Abstinence, Be faithful, Condom use).
  • Screening of high-risk groups (CSWs, truck drivers).
  • Vaccination (e.g., HPV, Hepatitis B).
  • Early diagnosis and treatment (Syndromic management).
  • Male circumcision (VMMC).
2.
With regard to diseases caused by faecal contamination:
a) Differentiate between bacillary and amoebic dysentery (2.5 marks)
b) What are the prevention and control measures in amoebiasis (2.5 marks)
c) What steps would you take when as the in-charge of the health centre you suspect there is an outbreak of cholera (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

a) Differences between Bacillary and Amoebic Dysentery:

Feature Bacillary (Shigellosis) Amoebic (Amoebiasis)
Onset Acute Gradual/Slow
Fever/Toxicity Common, patient looks toxic Rare, patient walks about
Stool frequency Frequent (>10/day), small volume Less frequent (6-8/day), bulky
Stool Appearance Bright red blood, pus, mucus, no smell Dark red blood mixed with stool, foul smell ("offensive")
Tenesmus Severe Moderate

b) Prevention of Amoebiasis:

  • Safe disposal of human excreta (latrines).
  • Proper hand washing before eating and after toilet.
  • Washing raw vegetables and fruits thoroughly.
  • Protecting food from flies.
  • Drinking boiled/treated water.

c) Steps for Cholera Outbreak:

  1. Verify Diagnosis: Collect stool/rectal swabs for culture to confirm Vibrio cholerae.
  2. Notify Authorities: Report immediately to the Sub-County/District surveillance officer (Notifiable disease).
  3. Case Management: Set up an isolation unit (CTC), initiate rehydration (ORS/IV fluids), and antibiotics for severe cases.
  4. Epidemiological Investigation: Define cases, active case search, map cases to find source (water/food).
  5. Prevention/Control: Distribute chlorine/Aqua tabs, health education on hygiene, ban food hawking, supervise burials.
3.
With respect to maternal heath:
a) Define safe motherhood (2 marks)
b) Outline the pillars of safe motherhood (4 marks)
c) State eight important services in efforts on reduction of maternal morbidity and mortality in Kenya (4 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

a) Safe Motherhood: A global initiative aimed at ensuring that every woman receives the care she needs to be safe and healthy throughout pregnancy, childbirth, and the postpartum period.

b) Pillars of Safe Motherhood:

  1. Family Planning: To prevent unwanted pregnancies and ensure spacing.
  2. Antenatal Care (ANC): To detect and treat complications early.
  3. Clean/Safe Delivery: Ensuring skilled attendance at birth.
  4. Essential Obstetric Care (EOC): Access to emergency care for complications (basic and comprehensive).

c) Services to Reduce Maternal Mortality:

  • Focused Antenatal Care (FANC).
  • Free Maternity Services (Linda Mama).
  • Prevention of Mother to Child Transmission of HIV (PMTCT).
  • Post-abortion care.
  • Tetanus toxoid immunization.
  • Iron/Folate supplementation.
  • Malaria prevention (ITNs, IPTp).
  • Skilled Birth Attendance.
  • Postnatal Care (PNC).
4.
Disasters are common place occurrence in Kenya
a) Describe the stages of disaster management (5 marks)
b) Classify disasters giving an example in each case (3 marks)
c) State factors that increase vulnerability of a community to disasters (2 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

a) Stages of Disaster Management:

  1. Mitigation/Prevention: Actions to reduce risk or severity before a disaster (e.g., building dykes, vaccination).
  2. Preparedness: Planning and training for response (e.g., drills, stockpiling supplies, early warning systems).
  3. Response: Immediate actions during/after impact to save lives (e.g., search and rescue, first aid).
  4. Recovery: Restoring normalcy (Rehabilitation and Reconstruction).

b) Classification:

  • Natural Disasters: Caused by nature (e.g., Floods, Droughts, Earthquakes).
  • Man-made (Anthropogenic) Disasters: Caused by human action/error (e.g., Terrorist attacks, Industrial accidents, Transport accidents).
  • Hybrid/Complex: Combination (e.g., Famine caused by war and drought).

c) Vulnerability Factors:

  • Poverty (poor housing, lack of resources).
  • Location (living in flood plains or fault lines).
  • Lack of awareness/education.
  • Poor infrastructure.
  • Environmental degradation.
5.
Outline the risk factors for the following non-communicable diseases
a) Ischemic heart disease (5 marks)
b) Cancer (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

a) Risk factors for Ischemic Heart Disease:

  • Modifiable: Hypertension, Smoking, High Cholesterol (Hyperlipidemia), Diabetes Mellitus, Obesity, Physical Inactivity, Unhealthy Diet, Stress.
  • Non-modifiable: Age (older), Gender (Male > Female pre-menopause), Family History/Genetics.

b) Risk factors for Cancer:

  • Tobacco use: (Lung, oral, bladder cancer).
  • Infections: HPV (Cervix), Hepatitis B/C (Liver), H. pylori (Stomach), HIV (Kaposi's).
  • Diet/Lifestyle: Alcohol, obesity, low fruit/veg intake, red/processed meats.
  • Environmental: UV radiation (Skin), Air pollution, Occupational carcinogens (Asbestos).
  • Genetics: Inherited mutations (BRCA).
6.
Epidemiology and demography have profound effects on the health status of a community:
a) Outline the causes of migration (5 marks)
b) Describe the epidemiological study designs (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

a) Causes of Migration (Push and Pull Factors):

  • Economic: Searching for jobs, better wages (Pull), or unemployment/poverty (Push).
  • Social: Marriage, education opportunities, family reunification.
  • Political: War, persecution, political instability (Push).
  • Environmental: Drought, floods, famine, natural disasters (Push).

b) Epidemiological Study Designs:

  • Descriptive Studies: Describe distribution (Time, Place, Person). E.g., Case reports, Case series, Cross-sectional (Prevalence) studies.
  • Analytical Studies: Test hypotheses about associations.
    • Observational:
      • Cohort: Follow exposed/unexposed over time (Incidence/Risk).
      • Case-Control: Compare cases (sick) vs controls (healthy) for past exposure (Odds Ratio).
    • Experimental (Intervention): Researcher manipulates exposure. E.g., Randomized Clinical Trials (RCTs).
7.
A nutritionist at a local hospital is following up under five year old children who are on nutritional schedule after suffering malnutrition obtained the following body weight (in kgs).
9.5, 6.5, 13.6, 12.9, 6.7, 7.8, 8.8, 9.1, 5.4, 6.7, 8.4, 9.2, 8.8, 8.3, 4.7
a) Determine the range (1 mark)
b) Determine the measures of central tendency of the children (5 marks)
c) How does this data vary from the mean? (4 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

Data set (Ordered): 4.7, 5.4, 6.5, 6.7, 6.7, 7.8, 8.3, 8.4, 8.8, 8.8, 9.1, 9.2, 9.5, 12.9, 13.6 (n=15)

a) Range:

Range = Max - Min = $13.6 - 4.7 = 8.9$ kg

b) Measures of Central Tendency:

  • Mean ($\bar{x}$): Sum / n.
    Sum = 126.4
    Mean = $126.4 / 15 = 8.43$ kg
  • Median: The middle value ($8^{th}$ value).
    Median = 8.4 kg
  • Mode: Most frequent value(s).
    6.7 and 8.8 (Bimodal).

c) Variation from Mean (Standard Deviation):

Formula: $s = \sqrt{\frac{\Sigma(x - \bar{x})^2}{n-1}}$

Calculating squared differences from 8.43:

  • Sum of squares $\approx 77.0$
  • Variance = $77.0 / 14 \approx 5.5$
  • Standard Deviation = $\sqrt{5.5} \approx 2.35$ kg

The data varies from the mean by an average of approximately 2.35 kg.

Many governments in the world are implementing the PHC/MDGs to improve the wellbeing of their citizens
a) Explain the relationship between PHC and MDGs (2 marks)
b) State four strategies the Kenya government is deploying in implementation of each of the MDGs (8 marks)

Answer:

a) Relationship: PHC (Primary Health Care) is the strategy or vehicle used to achieve the health-related MDGs (Millennium Development Goals). MDGs provide the targets, PHC provides the implementation framework.

b) Strategies for MDGs (Context 2013):

  • MDG 4 (Reduce Child Mortality):
    • Expanded Programme on Immunization (KEPI).
    • IMCI (Integrated Management of Childhood Illnesses).
  • MDG 5 (Improve Maternal Health):
    • Free Maternity Services.
    • Training skilled birth attendants.
    • FANC (Focused Antenatal Care).
  • MDG 6 (Combat HIV/AIDS, Malaria, etc.):
    • Free ARVs and VCT services.
    • Distribution of ITNs (Bed nets) and IRS.
    • Free TB treatment (DOTS).
  • MDG 1 (Eradicate Extreme Poverty/Hunger):
    • School feeding programs.
    • Nutritional supplementation.
9.
Behavioural sciences such sociology, psychology and anthropology influence the health status of a community in many ways'
a) Outline the various types of marriages in African societies (5 marks)
b) Describe the types of families in Kenya (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

a) Types of Marriages:

  • Monogamy: One man, one wife.
  • Polygamy: Multiple partners.
    • Polygyny: One man, multiple wives (common).
    • Polyandry: One woman, multiple husbands (rare).
  • Levirate (Wife Inheritance): Brother inherits deceased brother's widow.
  • Sororate: Man marries wife's sister (often after wife's death or infertility).
  • Woman-to-woman marriage: Social arrangement for lineage continuity (not sexual).
  • Ghost marriage: Marriage in the name of a deceased son.

b) Types of Families:

  • Nuclear Family: Father, mother, and children.
  • Extended Family: Nuclear family plus relatives (grandparents, aunts, uncles) living together or closely connected.
  • Single-parent Family: One parent raising children (due to death, divorce, or choice).
  • Child-headed Family: Siblings raising each other (often due to HIV/AIDS orphans).
  • Polygamous Family: Man, multiple wives, and their children.
10.
You are the clinical officer in charge of the health facility where a disease outbreak has occurred. Explain how you will investigate the situation (10 marks)
Show Answer

Answer: (1 mark per step)

  1. Preparation for field work: Assemble team, supplies, transport, administrative approval.
  2. Verify the diagnosis: Review clinical findings, lab confirmation (rule out lab error).
  3. Establish existence of an outbreak: Compare observed cases vs expected (baseline) numbers.
  4. Define a case (Case Definition): Standard criteria (Person, Place, Time, Clinical features) to identify cases.
  5. Find cases and record information: Active surveillance, line listing.
  6. Perform Descriptive Epidemiology: Analyze data by Time (Epi curve), Place (Spot map), and Person (Age/Sex).
  7. Develop Hypotheses: Best guess on source, mode of transmission, exposures.
  8. Evaluate Hypotheses: Analytical studies (Case-control or Cohort).
  9. Implement Control and Prevention measures: Treat cases, block transmission (e.g., chlorinate water), educate public. (Note: Control often starts immediately).
  10. Communicate Findings: Outbreak report to authorities and feedback to community.
