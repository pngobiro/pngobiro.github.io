Section A: Short Essay Questions

60 Marks
1.

a) Define infection prevention and control. (2 Marks)

b) Discuss the standard/universal precautions for infection prevention and control and the application. (8 Marks)

c) Discuss the classification of health care waste giving the colour codes of each category. (10 Marks)

Show Answer

a) Definition:

Infection Prevention and Control (IPC) refers to the scientific approach and practical solutions designed to prevent harm caused by infection to patients and health workers. It involves policies and procedures (like hand hygiene and waste management) to stop the spread of infections in healthcare settings.

b) Standard Precautions:

Standard precautions are the basic level of infection control that should be used in the care of all patients all of the time, regardless of their diagnosis. They include:

  1. Hand Hygiene: Washing hands with soap and water or using alcohol-based rub before and after patient contact.
  2. Personal Protective Equipment (PPE): Using gloves, gowns, masks, and eye protection depending on the risk of exposure to body fluids.
  3. Respiratory Hygiene/Cough Etiquette: Covering mouth/nose when coughing and hand hygiene.
  4. Safe Injection Practices: Using sterile, single-use needles and syringes; avoiding recapping.
  5. Safe Handling of Contaminated Equipment/Surfaces: Proper cleaning, disinfection, and sterilization of instruments and environmental surfaces.

c) Healthcare Waste Classification & Color Codes (Kenya MOH Guidelines):

  • Infectious Waste (Yellow): Waste suspected to contain pathogens (e.g., cultures, wound dressings, swabs, cotton wool contaminated with blood). Placed in yellow liners.
  • Sharps (Yellow/Safety Box): Needles, scalpels, blades, broken glass. Placed in puncture-proof safety boxes.
  • Highly Infectious/Anatomical Waste (Red): Placenta, body parts, tissues, organs. Placed in red liners (sometimes yellow in some settings, but Red denotes anatomical/pathological).
  • Non-Infectious/General Waste (Black): Office paper, food wrappers, packaging material that hasn't contacted fluids. Placed in black liners.
  • Pharmaceutical/Chemical Waste (Brown): Expired drugs, spilled reagents. Placed in brown liners.
2.
Outline the advantages and disadvantages of using questionnaires as data collection tools. (10 Marks)
Show Answer

Advantages:

  • Cost-effective: Cheaper to administer to large populations compared to interviews.
  • Time-saving: Can collect vast amounts of data quickly.
  • Anonymity: Respondents may be more honest about sensitive topics if they remain anonymous.
  • Standardization: All respondents get the exact same questions, reducing interviewer bias.
  • Convenience: Can be completed at the respondent's leisure (e.g., mailed or online).

Disadvantages:

  • Low Response Rate: People often ignore them, leading to non-response bias.
  • Inflexibility: Cannot probe deeper or clarify misunderstandings once distributed.
  • Literacy Requirement: Respondents must be able to read and write (excludes illiterate populations).
  • Incomplete Answers: Respondents may skip questions they don't understand or don't want to answer.
  • Lack of Rapport: Missing the personal connection that helps in qualitative data collection.
3.
Outline five (5) advantages of community participation in health-related matters. (10 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Ownership & Sustainability: When the community is involved in planning and implementation, they own the program, making it more likely to continue after external support ends.
  2. Resource Mobilization: Communities can contribute local resources (labor, materials, money, venues) which reduces costs.
  3. Cultural Acceptability: Participation ensures health interventions are culturally appropriate and sensitive to local customs, reducing resistance.
  4. Empowerment: It builds the community's capacity and confidence to identify and solve their own problems (self-reliance).
  5. Better Health Outcomes: Interventions are more effective because they address the actual (felt) needs of the community rather than assumed needs.
4.

STIs are common communicable diseases of Public Health significance:

a) State four risk factors of STIs. (2 Marks)

b) Outline the holistic management of STIs. (4 Marks)

c) State the control measures for STIs. (4 Marks)

Show Answer

a) Risk Factors:

  • Multiple sexual partners.
  • Unprotected sex (non-use of condoms).
  • Alcohol and drug abuse (impairs judgment).
  • Sex work (commercial sex).
  • Young age (sexual debut).

b) Holistic Management (Syndromic Approach + 4Cs):

  • Compliance: Ensure the patient completes the full course of treatment.
  • Counseling: Educate on risk reduction and HIV testing.
  • Condoms: Provide condoms and demonstrate correct use.
  • Contact Tracing: Identify and treat sexual partners to prevent reinfection.
  • (Plus medical treatment of the syndrome).

c) Control Measures:

  • Health education on safe sex practices (ABC strategy: Abstinence, Be faithful, Condom use).
  • Screening of high-risk groups (e.g., sex workers, youth).
  • Vaccination (e.g., HPV, Hepatitis B).
  • Early diagnosis and effective treatment of cases to reduce the reservoir of infection.
5.
Outline the risk factors associated with Cancer. (10 Marks)
Show Answer

Biological/Genetic Factors:

  • Age: Risk increases with age for most cancers.
  • Genetics/Family History: Inherited mutations (e.g., BRCA1/2 for breast cancer).

Lifestyle Factors:

  • Tobacco Use: Smoking causes lung, throat, and bladder cancers.
  • Alcohol Consumption: Linked to liver, esophageal, and breast cancers.
  • Diet: High red/processed meat intake (colon cancer); low fruit/vegetable intake.
  • Obesity/Physical Inactivity: Linked to various cancers.

Environmental Factors:

  • Carcinogens: Exposure to asbestos, benzene, aflatoxins (in moldy grains).
  • Radiation: UV rays from sun (skin cancer), ionizing radiation.

Infectious Agents:

  • Viruses: HPV (Cervical), Hepatitis B/C (Liver), EBV (Burkitt's Lymphoma).
  • Bacteria: H. pylori (Stomach cancer).
6.

Substance abuse is increasingly becoming a major public health problem globally more so in Kenya.

a) Define substance abuse. (2 Marks)

b) What are the factors contributing or predisposing to substance abuse? (8 Marks)

Show Answer

a) Definition:

Substance abuse is the harmful or hazardous use of psychoactive substances, including alcohol and illicit drugs, leading to significant impairment or distress (e.g., failure to fulfill obligations, use in hazardous situations, legal problems).

b) Predisposing Factors:

  • Peer Pressure: Influence from friends to fit in.
  • Stress/Trauma: Using drugs as a coping mechanism for poverty, unemployment, or family conflict.
  • Availability/Accessibility: Easy access to drugs and alcohol in the community (porous borders, cheap liquor).
  • Media Influence: Glorification of drug use in movies and music.
  • Family History: Genetics or growing up in a home where substance use is normalized.
  • Lack of Parental Supervision: Weak family structures.
  • Curiosity/Experimentation: Typically in adolescence.

1.

A nutritionist at a local hospital is following up under five-year-old children who are on nutritional schedule after suffering malnutrition obtained the following body weight (in kgs).

Data: 4.7, 5.4, 6.5, 6.7, 6.7, 7.8, 8.3, 8.4, 8.8, 8.8, 9.1, 9.2, 9.5, 12.9, 13.6

a) Determine the range. (2 Marks)

b) Determine the measures of central tendency (Mean, Mode, Median). (12 marks)

c) How does this data vary from the mean? (Standard Deviation/Variance concept - 6 marks)

Show Answer

Data Set (n=15): 4.7, 5.4, 6.5, 6.7, 6.7, 7.8, 8.3, 8.4, 8.8, 8.8, 9.1, 9.2, 9.5, 12.9, 13.6

a) Range:

$\text{Range} = \text{Highest Value} - \text{Lowest Value}$

$\text{Range} = 13.6 - 4.7 = \mathbf{8.9 \text{ kg}}$

b) Measures of Central Tendency:

Mean ($\bar{x}$):

$\bar{x} = \frac{\sum x}{n} = \frac{4.7+5.4+6.5+6.7+6.7+7.8+8.3+8.4+8.8+8.8+9.1+9.2+9.5+12.9+13.6}{15}$

$\bar{x} = \frac{126.4}{15} = \mathbf{8.43 \text{ kg}}$

Median: (Middle value)

Since $n=15$ (odd), position is $\frac{n+1}{2} = 8^{th}$ value.

$8^{th} \text{ value} = \mathbf{8.4 \text{ kg}}$

Mode: (Most frequent)

6.7 appears twice. 8.8 appears twice.

The distribution is Bimodal (6.7 kg and 8.8 kg).

c) Variation from Mean:

This question asks for a descriptive or calculation based answer on variation (Standard Deviation). The data is skewed to the right (positive skew) because Mean (8.43) > Median (8.4). There are potential outliers (12.9, 13.6) pulling the mean up. The spread is wide (Range 8.9), indicating significant variability in the children's recovery/nutritional status.

2.

The Ottawa Charter defines HEALTH PROMOTION as 'the process of enabling people to increase control over, and to improve, their health.

a) Mention the Health promotion priority actions identified in the Ottawa Charter. Write brief notes under each action. (12 marks)

b) Mention eight childhood immunisable diseases in Kenya. (8 marks)

Show Answer

a) Ottawa Charter Priority Actions (5 Action Areas):

  1. Build Healthy Public Policy: Health must be on the agenda of policy makers in all sectors, not just health (e.g., taxation on tobacco, seatbelt laws).
  2. Create Supportive Environments: Linking people and their environment (work, leisure, living). Making it easier to make healthy choices (e.g., safe parks, smoke-free zones).
  3. Strengthen Community Actions: Empowering communities to control their own health initiatives and set priorities (Community participation).
  4. Develop Personal Skills: Providing information and education (health literacy) to enable individuals to make healthy choices and cope with illness.
  5. Reorient Health Services: Moving beyond just curative services to include health promotion and prevention, and researching health needs.

b) Childhood Immunisable Diseases in Kenya:

  1. Tuberculosis (BCG)
  2. Polio (OPV/IPV)
  3. Diphtheria (Pentavalent)
  4. Whooping Cough / Pertussis (Pentavalent)
  5. Tetanus (Pentavalent)
  6. Hepatitis B (Pentavalent)
  7. Haemophilus Influenzae type B (Pentavalent)
  8. Measles (Measles-Rubella)
  9. Rubella (Measles-Rubella)
  10. Pneumonia (PCV - Pneumococcal)
  11. Diarrhea (Rotavirus)
  12. Yellow Fever (Selected counties)
3.

Rehema, a five-year-old, is brought to the clinic where you practice. She has a history of vomiting a pink looking worm, has brown-looking hair and a big abdomen. On examination you find the following: slight pallor, no edema, jaundice nor cyanosis. Has no organomegaly. Mother says she eats a lot but does not add weight. She weighs 16.5 kg.

a) What is the likely diagnosis? (2 marks)

b) State the preventive and control measures of the above condition. (6 marks)

c) Mention two drugs that can be used to treat the above condition. (2 marks)

d) With help of a flow chart/diagram, outline the life cycle of the causative agent of the above condition. (10 marks)

Show Answer

a) Likely Diagnosis:

Ascariasis (Infestation with Ascaris lumbricoides). The "pink looking worm" vomited is a classic presentation of Ascaris.

b) Preventive and Control Measures:

  • Sanitation: Proper disposal of feces (use of latrines) to prevent soil contamination with eggs.
  • Hygiene: Hand washing with soap and water before eating and after using the toilet.
  • Food Safety: Washing fruits and vegetables thoroughly (especially those eaten raw) to remove eggs.
  • Water Safety: Drinking treated/boiled water.
  • Deworming: Regular deworming of children (e.g., every 6 months in endemic areas).

c) Treatment Drugs:

  • Albendazole (400mg single dose)
  • Mebendazole (100mg BID for 3 days or 500mg single dose)
  • Levamisole
  • Pyrantel Pamoate

d) Life Cycle of Ascaris lumbricoides:

  1. Ingestion: Man ingests embryonated eggs (infective stage) from contaminated food/water.
  2. Intestine: Larvae hatch in the small intestine.
  3. Migration (Lung Phase): Larvae penetrate the gut wall, enter blood/lymphatics, and migrate to the heart and then the Lungs.
  4. Ascent: Larvae break into alveoli, ascend the bronchial tree to the trachea and pharynx.
  5. Swallowing: Larvae are swallowed back into the esophagus.
  6. Maturation: Larvae reach small intestine again and mature into adult worms.
  7. Reproduction: Adult females lay eggs which are passed in feces to the soil to embryonate.
