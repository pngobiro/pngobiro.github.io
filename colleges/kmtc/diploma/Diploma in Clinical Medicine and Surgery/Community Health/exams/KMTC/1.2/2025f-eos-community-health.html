Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions

1.
Which one of the following is the most effective way of killing waterborne pathogens?
A. Filtration
B. Distillation
C. Chlorination
D. Sedimentation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Chlorination

Explanation: Chlorination is a chemical disinfection method that actively kills bacteria, viruses, and some protozoa. While distillation (boiling/condensing) also kills pathogens, chlorination is the standard method for large-scale and household water treatment due to its residual effect which prevents recontamination.

  • Filtration and Sedimentation remove particles but do not necessarily kill all microorganisms.

A. Avoid refrigeration
B. Separating raw and cooked food items
C. Separate cutting board for meats and vegetables
D. Exposing the unprepared food ingredients to smoke to kill microorganisms
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Separating raw and cooked food items (and C is also a correct specific example)

Explanation: The fundamental principle of preventing cross-contamination is separating raw foods (especially meat/poultry which carry pathogens) from ready-to-eat/cooked foods. Option B is the broad principle; Option C is a specific application of that principle.

Section 2: Short Answer Questions

21.
How does malnutrition affect community health, and what strategies can be employed for its prevention? (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Impact on Community Health:

  • Increased Morbidity/Mortality: Weakened immunity leads to higher susceptibility to infections (e.g., measles, pneumonia).
  • Reduced Productivity: Sick or stunted adults have reduced physical and cognitive capacity, affecting economic output.
  • Developmental Delays: Stunting in children leads to poor cognitive development and school performance.

Prevention Strategies:

  • Promotion of Breastfeeding: Exclusive breastfeeding for 6 months.
  • Dietary Diversification: Educating on balanced diets using local foods (Kitchen gardens).
  • Fortification: Using iodized salt and fortified flour.
  • Supplementation: Vitamin A and Zinc supplementation for children; Iron/Folic acid for pregnant women.
  • WASH: Improving Water, Sanitation, and Hygiene to prevent diarrheal diseases that cause malnutrition.

23.
Explain common hazards associated with poor housing. (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Respiratory Diseases: Poor ventilation and overcrowding lead to spread of TB, Influenza, and Meningitis. Indoor smoke causes COPD.
  2. Water-borne Diseases: Lack of sanitation/drainage leads to Cholera, Typhoid, and Malaria (mosquito breeding).
  3. Skin Infections: Overcrowding promotes Scabies, Ringworm, and lice infestations.
  4. Structural Accidents: Poor construction leads to collapse, fires, and falls.
  5. Psychological Stress: Lack of privacy, noise, and insecurity contribute to mental health issues.

Highlight the significance of waste segregation in effective waste management in urban areas. (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Recycling Efficiency: Separating plastics, glass, and paper allows them to be recycled directly, reducing landfill volume.
  2. Safety: Separating hazardous/biomedical waste prevents injury and infection to waste handlers and scavengers.
  3. Cost Reduction: Only hazardous waste needs expensive treatment (incineration). General waste is cheaper to manage.
  4. Resource Recovery: Organic waste can be segregated for composting (manure) or biogas production.
  5. Environmental Protection: Prevents toxic substances (batteries, chemicals) from contaminating the general environment.

  • Biomass fuels (wood, charcoal, dung) burnt in open fires/inefficient stoves.
  • Tobacco smoke.
  • Household chemicals (pesticides, cleaning agents).

Section 3: Long Answer Questions

29.
Explain the sources, health impacts, and control measures of noise pollution in urban environment. (20 Marks)
Show Answer

1. Sources of Noise Pollution:

  • Transport: Road traffic (horns, engines), railways, and aircraft.
  • Industrial: Machinery, construction sites (drills, excavators).
  • Commercial/Entertainment: Loud music from clubs, religious institutions, and markets.
  • Domestic: Generators, loud appliances.

2. Health Impacts:

  • Auditory Effects:
    • Noise-Induced Hearing Loss (NIHL) - permanent damage to cochlear hair cells.
    • Tinnitus (ringing in ears).
    • Temporary Threshold Shift (TTS).
  • Non-Auditory Effects:
    • Sleep Disturbance: Insomnia leading to fatigue.
    • Cardiovascular: Stress response increases BP and heart rate (Hypertension).
    • Psychological: Irritability, anxiety, and reduced cognitive performance/concentration.

3. Control Measures:

  • Source Reduction: Use of silencers on vehicles/machinery; regular maintenance.
  • Transmission Interruption:
    • Planting trees (green belts) as sound buffers.
    • Installing sound barriers (walls) along highways.
    • Zoning: Separating industrial areas from residential zones.
  • Receiver Protection: Use of Ear Protective Devices (EPDs) like earplugs/muffs in industries.
  • Legislation: Enforcing laws on maximum decibel levels (NEMA regulations) and time restrictions for loud music.
30.
Explain the various stages of water treatment process, highlighting the purpose and function at each stage. (20 Marks)
Show Answer

The water treatment process makes water safe for consumption through the following stages:

1. Screening:

  • Purpose: To remove large floating objects.
  • Function: Metal screens trap sticks, rags, plastics, and dead animals to protect downstream pumps and pipes from blockage.

2. Aeration:

  • Purpose: To remove volatile gases and oxidize metals.
  • Function: Water is sprayed into air. This removes bad tastes/odors (H2S), expels CO2 (reducing acidity), and oxidizes Iron/Manganese so they can be filtered out.

3. Coagulation and Flocculation:

  • Purpose: To destabilize and clump suspended particles.
  • Function: Chemicals (Alum) are added rapidly (Coagulation) to neutralize the charge of dirt particles. Slow mixing (Flocculation) allows these particles to collide and form larger, heavy clumps called "floc".

4. Sedimentation (Clarification):

  • Purpose: To settle out the floc.
  • Function: Water moves slowly through large tanks. Gravity causes the heavy floc (containing dirt/bacteria) to settle at the bottom as sludge, which is removed. Clearer water moves to the top.

5. Filtration:

  • Purpose: To remove remaining suspended particles and microbes.
  • Function: Water passes through layers of sand and gravel (Rapid Sand Filters). This traps fine particles, protozoa, and some bacteria that escaped sedimentation.

6. Disinfection:

  • Purpose: To kill pathogenic microorganisms.
  • Function: Chlorine is added to kill bacteria and viruses. A residual amount is left to prevent recontamination in the distribution pipes. Ozone or UV light can also be used.

7. Storage:

  • Purpose: To buffer supply and demand.
  • Function: Treated water is stored in covered reservoirs to allow contact time for disinfection and ensure supply during peak usage.
