Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions

1.
Protein are made up of----------------- amino acids
A. 8
B. 10
C. 22
D. 26
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. 22 (or 20 in standard biology)

Explanation: There are 20 standard amino acids used by cells to synthesize proteins. However, in some contexts, two additional amino acids (Selenocysteine and Pyrrolysine) are recognized, bringing the count to 22. Given the options, 22 is the intended answer for the "building blocks" count in this specific syllabus context.

Note: Option A (8) typically refers to the number of Essential Amino Acids (sometimes 9).

2.
Example of saturated fat include—
A. Butter
B. Vegetable fat
C. Glycerol
D. Benzene
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Butter

Explanation: Butter is an animal fat that is high in saturated fatty acids and remains solid at room temperature. Vegetable fats (oils) are typically unsaturated (liquid), though palm/coconut oils are exceptions. Glycerol is a backbone of triglycerides, not a fat itself.

3.
Iron is found in the muscle pigment called?
A. Globin
B. Myoglobin
C. Enzymes
D. Haemoglobin
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Myoglobin

Explanation: Myoglobin is the iron-containing oxygen-binding protein found specifically in muscle tissue. Haemoglobin is found in red blood cells. Globin is the protein part.

9.
Which vitamins help in production of red blood cells and supplies oxygen to cells?
A. Iron
B. Protein
C. Cooper
D. Calcium
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Iron (Nutrient, not vitamin)

Explanation: The question asks for a "vitamin" but lists minerals/nutrients. Iron is the critical component of hemoglobin, which transports oxygen in Red Blood Cells. Vitamin B12 and Folic Acid are the actual vitamins involved in RBC production, but given the options, Iron is the intended answer for "supplies oxygen".

10.
These are substances that are required in a minute amount----
A. Macro nutrient
B. Micro nutrient
C. Polylactides
D. Phytochemicals
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Micro nutrient

Explanation: Micronutrients (vitamins and minerals) are required by the body in small (minute) amounts, unlike macronutrients (carbs, proteins, fats) which are needed in large amounts.

13.
Vitamin A deficiency may lead to—
A. Beriberi
B. Scurvy
C. Night blindness
D. Rickets
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Night blindness

Explanation: Vitamin A is essential for the formation of rhodopsin, a pigment in the retina used for low-light vision. Deficiency causes Night Blindness (Nyctalopia).
- Beriberi = Vit B1 (Thiamine)
- Scurvy = Vit C
- Rickets = Vit D

14.
Which one of the is type 2 micronutrients?
A. Magnesium
B. Iodine
C. Iron
D. Vitamin c
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Magnesium

Explanation: According to the Golden classification of nutrients:
- Type 1 (Functional): Deficiency leads to specific clinical signs but not growth failure (e.g., Iodine, Iron, Vit C, Vit A).
- Type 2 (Growth): Deficiency leads to growth failure/stunting, no specific signs (e.g., Magnesium, Zinc, Potassium, Protein).

20.
Which of the following is a result of high exposure of radiation?
A. Loss of white blood cells
B. Hair loss
C. Tooth decay
D. Leg fractures
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Loss of white blood cells

Explanation: Ionizing radiation affects rapidly dividing cells. The bone marrow is highly sensitive, leading to a drop in blood cell counts (Leukopenia/Pancytopenia) very quickly after exposure. Hair loss (alopecia) also occurs, but bone marrow suppression (A) is a critical and immediate systemic effect of Acute Radiation Syndrome.

Section 2: Short Answer Questions

21.
State principle of food preservation. (5 marks)
Show Answer

The general principles of food preservation are:

  1. Prevention/Delay of Microbial Decomposition:
    • By keeping out microorganisms (Asepsis).
    • By removing microorganisms (Filtration).
    • By hindering growth (Low temp, drying, anaerobic conditions).
    • By killing microorganisms (Heat/Cooking, Radiation).
  2. Prevention/Delay of Self-Decomposition:
    • Destruction of food enzymes (Blanching).
    • Prevention of chemical reactions (Antioxidants).
  3. Prevention of damage by insects/animals.
22.
List the role of carbohydrate. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Energy Source: Primary source of energy for the body (4 kcal/g). Essential for brain function.
  2. Protein Sparing: Prevents protein from being used for energy so it can be used for growth/repair.
  3. Fat Metabolism: Essential for proper oxidation of fats ("Fats burn in the flame of carbohydrates"). prevents ketosis.
  4. Structure: Component of cell membranes (glycoproteins/glycolipids) and nucleic acids (ribose).
  5. Dietary Fiber: Non-digestible carbs promote bowel health and satiety.
23.
Elaborate the ways to avoid or prevent iron deficiency anaemia and vitamin deficiency anaemia. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Dietary Diversification: Consume iron-rich foods (red meat, liver, green leafy vegetables) and vitamin-rich foods (B12 in animal products, Folate in greens/legumes).
  2. Food Fortification: Use fortified flour, cereals, and salt.
  3. Supplementation: Iron/Folic Acid supplementation (IFAS) for pregnant women and vulnerable groups.
  4. Enhancers: Consume Vitamin C rich foods (oranges/lemons) with meals to boost iron absorption.
  5. Inhibitors: Avoid tea/coffee with meals (tannins inhibit absorption).
  6. Disease Control: Treat parasites (hookworms) and malaria that cause blood loss/hemolysis.
24.
Vitamin D is necessary for the utilization and retention of calcium and phosphorus. Explain how it happens? (5 marks)
Show Answer

Vitamin D (specifically its active form, Calcitriol) maintains Calcium/Phosphate balance via:

  1. Intestinal Absorption: Increases the synthesis of calcium-binding protein (Calbindin) in the intestine, significantly increasing the absorption of dietary Calcium and Phosphorus.
  2. Bone Resorption: Works with Parathyroid Hormone (PTH) to mobilize calcium from bone into the blood if levels are low (or mineralize bone if levels are adequate).
  3. Renal Reabsorption: Stimulates the kidneys to reabsorb Calcium and Phosphorus from urine back into the blood.
25.
Explain factors influencing the environment. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Population Growth: Increases demand for resources, leading to deforestation, pollution, and waste.
  2. Industrialization: Emissions of greenhouse gases, chemical pollutants, and industrial waste.
  3. Urbanization: Concentration of people leads to sanitation challenges, slum development, and air pollution.
  4. Agricultural Practices: Use of pesticides/fertilizers (soil/water pollution) and land clearing.
  5. Deforestation: Loss of carbon sinks, soil erosion, and biodiversity loss.
26.
Mention the hierarchy of hospital waste management. (5 marks)
Show Answer

The waste management hierarchy (from most preferred to least preferred):

  1. Prevention/Minimization: Reducing waste at the source (e.g., paperless records).
  2. Segregation: Separating waste at point of generation (Color coding).
  3. Reuse/Recycle: Sterilizing equipment for reuse; recycling plastics/glass.
  4. Treatment: Incineration, autoclaving, or chemical disinfection to make waste safe.
  5. Disposal: Landfilling (least preferred, for residual ash/non-recyclables).
27.
What are the disadvantages of temporary housing. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Overcrowding: Often leads to rapid spread of communicable diseases (TB, Scabies).
  2. Poor Sanitation: Lack of proper toilets and waste disposal facilities (Cholera/Typhoid risk).
  3. Safety/Security: Poor structural integrity (collapse risk) and lack of security (crime).
  4. Weather Exposure: Inadequate protection from cold, heat, or rain.
  5. Psychological Stress: Lack of privacy and stability affects mental health.
28.
The main goals of safe water system are. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Quality: To provide water free from pathogens (bacteria/viruses) and harmful chemicals.
  2. Quantity: To ensure adequate supply for hygiene, consumption, and domestic use.
  3. Accessibility: Water source should be within reasonable distance (e.g., <1km or 30 mins).
  4. Reliability/Continuity: Regular supply without frequent interruptions.
  5. Affordability: Cost should not prevent people from accessing safe water.

30.
Discuss ten (10) importance of waste segregation. (20 marks)
Show Answer

Importance of Waste Segregation:

  1. Infection Control: Prevents the spread of infectious diseases by isolating infectious waste from general waste.
  2. Injury Prevention: Separating sharps (needles/scalpels) prevents needle-stick injuries to waste handlers.
  3. Cost Reduction: Treatment of hazardous waste (incineration) is expensive. Segregation ensures only hazardous waste is treated this way, reducing volume and cost.
  4. Recycling Promotion: Allows for easier identification and recovery of recyclable materials (plastics, paper), promoting a circular economy.
  5. Environmental Protection: Prevents hazardous chemicals/pharmaceuticals from leaching into soil/water if dumped in general landfills.
  6. Legal Compliance: Adheres to national environmental management (NEMA) and public health laws regarding waste handling.
  7. Operational Efficiency: Streamlines the waste disposal process at the facility level, making collection easier.
  8. Resource Recovery: Segregated organic waste can be composted for manure or used for biogas generation.
  9. Aesthetic Value: Proper segregation and containment keep the healthcare or community environment clean and pleasant.
  10. Occupational Safety: Protects municipal workers and scavengers from exposure to toxic or infectious hazards.
