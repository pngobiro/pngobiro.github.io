Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions (40 Marks)

1.
Secondary prevention of disease involves
A) Child spacing
B) Rehabilitation
C) Surveillance
D) Immunization
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Surveillance (in the context of Early Diagnosis)

Explanation:

  • Primary Prevention: Prevents disease onset (e.g., Immunization, Child Spacing).
  • Secondary Prevention: Early diagnosis and prompt treatment (e.g., Screening). Surveillance often involves active case finding and early detection of outbreaks, fitting best here.
  • Tertiary Prevention: Reducing disability (e.g., Rehabilitation).

2.
One of the following is true concerning community health
A) Diagnosis is based on demographic data
B) History taking and examination is a key component
C) Treatment and rehabilitation forms part of implementation
D) Evaluation of health outcome is based on follow-up and assessment
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Diagnosis is based on demographic data

Explanation: In Community Health (as opposed to Clinical Medicine), the "patient" is the community. Diagnosis is made using **vital statistics, demographic data, and epidemiological studies** rather than individual history and physical examination (which is Clinical Medicine).

3.
Complete count of everyone alive at any one time within a stated geographical boundary is
A) Sample surveys
B) Sentinel survey
C) Civil registration
D) Census
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) Census

Explanation: A **Census** is the total enumeration of a population at a specific time within a specific boundary. Civil registration is continuous (births/deaths).

4.
The process where a community is fully involved in identification of their problem is referred to as:
A) Community mobilization
B) Community sensitization
C) Community participation
D) Community diagnosis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) Community diagnosis

Explanation: **Community Diagnosis** is the specific process of identifying and quantifying the health problems in a community, ideally involving the community members themselves (Participatory Hygiene and Sanitation Transformation - PHAST).

5.
A frequency measure of disease usually used to record chronic disease is
A) Incidence
B) Prevalence
C) Mortality rate
D) Mobility rate
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Prevalence

Explanation: **Prevalence** measures the total number of existing cases (old + new), making it ideal for chronic diseases with long durations. **Incidence** measures new cases (better for acute diseases).

6.
Malaria is endemic in many parts of eastern Africa. Malaria endemicity where transmission occurs all the years round is
A) Holo-endemic
B) Hyper-endemic
C) Hypo-endemic
D) Mega-endemic
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Holo-endemic

Explanation: **Holoendemic** malaria is characterized by intense, perennial (year-round) transmission, resulting in a high degree of immunity in the adult population (spleen rate >75% in children).

7.
Vitamin A deficiency is associated with
A) Dryness of the eye
B) Susceptibility to infections
C) Osteomalacia
D) Rickets
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Dryness of the eye (Xerophthalmia)

Explanation: The classic clinical sign of VAD is **Xerophthalmia** (dry eyes) leading to night blindness and keratomalacia. C and D are Vitamin D deficiencies.

8.
A vaccine which can be frozen is
A) Pentavalent
B) Tetanus toxoid
C) BCG
D) Measles
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) Measles (and OPV, BCG)

Explanation: **Measles** and **BCG** are freeze-stable (kept frozen at national stores, though usually 2-8Â°C at facility). **Pentavalent** and **Tetanus** (adsorbed vaccines containing aluminum adjuvants) are **freeze-sensitive** and are destroyed if frozen.

9.
A mechanical method of contraception
A) Lactational amenorrhea method
B) IUCD
C) Coitus interruptus
D) Rhythm method
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) IUCD

Explanation: The **Intrauterine Contraceptive Device (IUCD)** acts mechanically (and chemically if Copper/Hormonal) to prevent implantation. Options A, C, and D are **Natural** family planning methods.

10.
Which one of the following is a water related disease?
A) Trachoma
B) Schistosomiasis
C) Amoebiasis
D) Bacillary dysentery
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Schistosomiasis

Explanation:

  • Water-Based: Schistosomiasis (host lives in water).
  • Water-Washed: Trachoma (A).
  • Water-Borne: Amoebiasis, Dysentery (C, D).
In many classifications, Water-Based is a subset of "Water Related" (needing water for the lifecycle).

11.
Trachoma develops in four (4) stages. Stage one (1) is consistent with:
A) Entropies
B) Diffuse corneal haze
C) Pannus
D) Follicle from inside the upper lid
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) Follicle from inside the upper lid

Explanation: WHO grading (TF - Trachomatous Inflammation-Follicular) involves the presence of 5 or more follicles in the upper tarsal conjunctiva. This is the active early stage.

12.
Severe malaria includes any episode of slide confirmed falciparum malaria with
A) Parasitemia <5%
B) Anemia >5g/dl
C) Hyperglycemia
D) Renal failure
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) Renal failure

Explanation: Features of severe malaria include **Renal failure** (Blackwater fever), Severe Anemia (<5g/dl), **Hypo**glycemia (not Hyper), and Cerebral malaria. Parasitemia >5-10% is usually considered hyperparasitemia.

13.
Late symptom of pulmonary tuberculosis includes:
A) Productive cough for more than 2-3 weeks
B) Blood-stained sputum
C) Positive tuberculin test
D) Night sweats
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Blood-stained sputum (Hemoptysis)

Explanation: Hemoptysis is typically a sign of advanced tissue destruction (cavitation), making it a later symptom compared to the initial chronic cough and night sweats.

14.
The mean is the best measure of central tendency because
A) It is frequently occurring observation
B) It uses all the observations in the data
C) It is an outlier
D) It shows variability
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) It uses all the observations in the data

Explanation: The mean takes into account the value of every single observation, making it the most information-rich measure of central tendency for normally distributed data.

15.
In population health, a characteristic that takes on different values in different population, persons or places is
A) Parameter
B) Population
C) Sample
D) Variable
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) Variable

Explanation: A **Variable** is any characteristic, number, or quantity that can be measured or counted and is capable of varying (changing) from one individual/group to another.

16.
Which one of the following is a descriptive epidemiological study design?
A) Cohort study
B) Cross-sectional study
C) Correlational study
D) Case series
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Cross-sectional study (and D Case Series)

Explanation: Descriptive studies include Case Reports, Case Series, and Cross-sectional studies (prevalence studies). Cohort and Correlational (Ecological - often analytic but can be descriptive) are generally considered Analytic. Cross-sectional is the classic answer for describing prevalence.

17.
Which one of the following is a non-probability sampling method?
A) Snowball sampling
B) Multistage sampling
C) Simple random sampling
D) Cluster sampling
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Snowball sampling

Explanation: Snowball sampling is a non-probability method where subjects recruit future subjects. B, C, and D are probability (random) methods.

18.
A team of clinicians visited a primary school to screen all the teachers for visual impairment. Which type of disease screening would be ideal in this set up?
A) Case finding
B) Selective screening
C) Mass screening
D) Multiphase screening
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Selective screening

Explanation: Selective (or Targeted) screening is applied to specific groups (e.g., teachers, factory workers) who may be at risk or are a convenient focus group, rather than the entire general population (Mass screening).

19.
A screening test that has the ability to correctly identify those who do not have the disease is said to demonstrate?
A) Specificity
B) Sensitivity
C) Predictively
D) Reliability
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Specificity

Explanation: **Specificity** is the ability to correctly identify Negatives (those without disease). **Sensitivity** identifies Positives.

20.
The best common type of surveillance in humanitarian emergencies and communicable diseases is?
A) Passive surveillance
B) Active surveillance
C) Sentinel surveillance
D) Syndromic surveillance
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) Syndromic surveillance

Explanation: In emergencies, lab confirmation takes too long. **Syndromic surveillance** (tracking symptom patterns like "Acute Watery Diarrhea" or "Fever with Rash") provides an early warning system for rapid response.

21.
Coverage as a quality epidemiological indicator in assessing programme outcome,
A) Measures the actual services received compared with the standards
B) Measures the proportion of the target population that has received services
C) Measures the population of the target population that can use the service
D) Measures the number of services compared with total target population
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Measures the proportion of the target population that has received services

Explanation: Coverage is defined as $\frac{\text{Number of people receiving service}}{\text{Total target population}} \times 100$.

22.
Graphical display of data includes the following except?
A) Stem-and-leaf
B) Pie charts
C) Scatter diagrams
D) Multivariable tables
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) Multivariable tables

Explanation: Tables are a form of **tabular** presentation, not **graphical**. Stem-and-leaf is a hybrid but often considered a plot. Pie and Scatter are definitely graphs.

23.
Sewage treatment involves various stages. Which one of the following is a process involved in secondary treatment stage?
A) Lagooning
B) Disinfection
C) Activated sludge
D) Selection
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Activated sludge

Explanation:

  • Primary: Screening, Sedimentation.

25.
Wastes that should not be incinerated include?
A) Residues of cytotoxic drugs
B) Anatomical parts
C) Volatile toxic waste such as mercury
D) Non-chlorinated plastics
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Volatile toxic waste such as mercury

Explanation: Heavy metals like Mercury and Lead should never be incinerated as they release toxic vapors into the atmosphere. Pressurized containers (aerosols) are also excluded.

Which one of the following is constant with step 2 approach to occupational health and safety?

Section 2: Short Answer Questions (40 Marks)

1.
Define the following terms as used in epidemiology (5 marks)
a) Epidemic
b) Endemic
c) Holoendemic
d) Pandemic
e) Morbidity
Show Answer

Definitions:

  • a) Epidemic: The occurrence of disease cases in excess of normal expectancy in a specific community or region.
  • b) Endemic: The constant presence or usual prevalence of a disease within a geographic area or population group.
  • c) Holoendemic: A disease that is highly prevalent in a population, affecting a high proportion of individuals, especially children (e.g., Malaria in some regions).
  • d) Pandemic: An epidemic that has spread over several countries or continents, usually affecting a large number of people.
  • e) Morbidity: The state of being diseased or unhealthy within a population; often expressed as a rate of illness.

a) Sampling: The process of selecting a subset (representative part) of a population for the purpose of making observations and drawing inferences about the whole population.

b) Methods of Probability Sampling:

  3. Stratified Random Sampling
  4. Cluster Sampling
3.
Explain five elements of primary health care (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Education: Educating communities about prevailing health problems and methods of preventing and controlling them.
  2. Food Supply & Nutrition: Promotion of food supply and proper nutrition.
  3. Safe Water & Sanitation: Provision of an adequate supply of safe water and basic sanitation.
  4. Maternal & Child Health: Care for mothers and children, including family planning.
  5. Immunization: Vaccination against major infectious diseases.
4.
Using examples list five classes of hazards in a work place (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Physical Hazards: Noise, radiation, vibration, heat, cold.
  2. Chemical Hazards: Solvents, pesticides, fumes, dusts (asbestos).
  3. Biological Hazards: Bacteria, viruses (Hep B), fungi, parasites.
  4. Ergonomic Hazards: Repetitive movements, poor posture, improper lifting.
  5. Psychosocial Hazards: Workplace stress, violence, harassment, burnout.
5.
With examples, differentiate between emerging and re-emerging diseases (5 marks)
Show Answer

Emerging Diseases: Diseases that have appeared in a population for the first time, or that may have existed previously but are rapidly increasing in incidence or geographic range.

  • Examples: COVID-19, Ebola, HIV/AIDS, SARS.

Re-emerging Diseases: Diseases that were previously controlled or had declined significantly but are now coming back to become a major health problem.

  • Examples: Tuberculosis (due to HIV/MDR), Malaria (drug resistance), Measles (vaccine gaps), Cholera.

  [MITIGATION] <-------- [PREPAREDNESS] ----> [RESPONSE]
           ^                                |

8.
In experimental study designs, list the key features of a clinical trial (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Randomization: Random assignment of participants to intervention or control groups to reduce bias.
  2. Control Group: A group that receives placebo or standard care for comparison.

Section 3: Long Answer Question (20 Marks)

1.
Explain three (3) methods in which infectious diseases can be prevented and controlled (20 marks)
Show Answer

Introduction: Prevention and control of infectious diseases aims to reduce incidence, prevalence, and eliminate transmission. The methods target the reservoir, route of transmission, or susceptible host.

1. Immunization (Vaccination):

  • Mechanism: Stimulates the body's immune system to produce antibodies against specific pathogens (viruses/bacteria) without causing the disease.
  • Application: Routine immunization (KEPI) for children (Measles, Polio, DPT). Mass vaccination campaigns during outbreaks.
  • Impact: Creates herd immunity, protecting vulnerable populations and leading to eradication (e.g., Smallpox) or elimination (Polio).

2. Water, Sanitation, and Hygiene (WASH):

  • Safe Water: Treating water (chlorination, boiling) prevents water-borne diseases like Cholera and Typhoid.
  • Sanitation: Proper disposal of human waste (latrines/toilets) breaks the fecal-oral transmission cycle. Proper waste management controls vectors.
  • Hygiene: Handwashing with soap breaks transmission of diarrheal and respiratory infections (Covid-19). Food hygiene prevents food poisoning.

3. Vector Control:

  • Environmental Management: Draining stagnant water to destroy mosquito breeding sites (Malaria/Dengue).
  • Chemical Control: Indoor Residual Spraying (IRS) and use of larvicides.
  • Physical Barriers: Use of Insecticide-Treated Nets (ITNs) to prevent bites.
  • Biological Control: Using natural enemies (e.g., fish eating larvae).

(Alternative/Additional Point) 4. Health Education & Behavioral Change:

  • Empowering the community with knowledge on disease transmission and prevention (e.g., safe sex practices for HIV, social distancing for respiratory diseases).
