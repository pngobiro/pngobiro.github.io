Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions (40 Marks)

1.
Which principle underpins community participation where any member of the community may have input in the process?
A) Human rights
B) Democracy
C) Citizenship
D) Dictatorship
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Citizenship

Explanation: In the context of community health and participation, citizenship implies the right and responsibility of community members to be involved in decisions affecting their health and well-being. It frames participation not just as a privilege but as a civic duty and right.

Why other options are incorrect:

  • A) Human rights: While participation is a human right, the specific principle of input in the process as a member of a polity is best described by citizenship.
  • B) Democracy: This is a political system; while participation is democratic, "citizenship" is the operational principle at the individual level.
  • D) Dictatorship: This involves top-down control with little to no community input.

What is the advantage of using pamphlets as health education material?
A) Provide one-way communication
B) Effective to illiterate people
C) Ensures intended group has read and understood the message
D) Propagate message to the mass rapidly

Option A is a disadvantage (one-way). Option C is actually a limitation (you can't ensure they read it). However, in many contexts, the "advantage" cited is that the user can keep it and read it. Wait, looking at the options provided:

  • A: Disadvantage.
  • B: False (requires literacy).

Most likely intended answer: D) Propagate message to the mass rapidly (via distribution campaigns).

13.
Which one is NOT a vital statistic?
A) Migration
B) Death
C) Morbidity
D) Marriage
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Migration

Explanation: Vital statistics are data derived from civil registration systems concerning vital events in a lifetime: Births, Deaths, Fetal deaths, Marriages, and Divorces. Morbidity (illness) is a health statistic but strictly speaking, "Vital Statistics" often refers to the civil registration events. However, usually, Migration is considered a demographic statistic, not a "Vital Event" in the civil registration sense (Birth/Death/Marriage). Between Morbidity and Migration, Migration is the least likely to be classified as a "Vital Statistic" in traditional public health definitions (CRVS - Civil Registration and Vital Statistics).

Note: Morbidity data usually comes from disease surveillance, not the vital registration system.

14.

A) Hepatitis
B) Influenza
C) Rabies
D) Pertussis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Rabies (and B. Influenza)

Explanation: The Kenya Expanded Programme on Immunization (KEPI) includes vaccines for Tuberculosis, Polio, Diphtheria, Pertussis, Tetanus, Hepatitis B, Haemophilus Influenzae type b, Pneumonia, Rotavirus, Measles, Rubella, and HPV.

15.

Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) Can be prevented by proper grain storage

Explanation: Aflatoxin is produced by the fungus Aspergillus flavus which grows on grains (maize, peanuts) when stored in damp/humid conditions. Drying grains properly and storing them in dry conditions prevents fungal growth.

  • B: Should not be fed to animals as toxins accumulate in milk/meat.

16.
About epidemiological study designs, which one is an example of analytical study?
A) Randomized control trial
B) Non randomized control trial
C) Community trial
D) Cohort studies
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) Cohort studies

Explanation: Epidemiological studies are divided into Observational and Experimental.

  • Observational Analytical: Cohort, Case-Control.
  • Experimental (Intervention): Randomized Control Trial (RCT), Community Trial.

While RCTs are technically "analytical" (they test hypotheses), in classification questions, "Analytical" often refers to Observational Analytical (Cohort/Case-Control) as distinct from "Experimental". However, if the distinction is Descriptive vs Analytical, all options are Analytical. If the distinction is Observational vs Experimental, D is Observational Analytical, A/B/C are Experimental. Usually, in this context, Cohort studies is the classic answer for "Analytical Observational".

30.
Which one is a primary health care essential service adopted during the Alma Ata conference?
A) Dental Health
B) Mental Health
C) Equity
D) Safe water and sanitation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) Safe water and sanitation

Explanation: The Alma Ata Declaration (1978) outlined 8 essential components of PHC:

  1. Education concerning prevailing health problems
  2. Food supply and proper nutrition
  3. Safe water and basic sanitation
  4. Maternal and child health care
  5. Immunization
  6. Prevention and control of locally endemic diseases

Section 2: Short Answer Questions (40 Marks)

41.
Outline five (5) methods of meat preservation. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Drying/Sun-drying: Removing moisture to inhibit bacterial growth (e.g., Nyirinyiri).
  2. Salting/Curing: Using salt to draw out moisture and create a hypertonic environment hostile to bacteria.
  3. Smoking: Exposing meat to smoke which dries it and adds antimicrobial compounds (phenols).
  4. Refrigeration/Freezing: Lowering temperature to slow down or stop bacterial multiplication.
  5. Canning: Heating meat in sealed containers to kill bacteria and create a vacuum seal.
  6. Use of chemicals/preservatives: E.g., Nitrates/Nitrites (often used in processed meats).

Show Answer

47.
Briefly discuss methods of waste management. (5 marks)
Show Answer

  2. Landfill/Controlled Tipping: Disposing of waste in designated pits which are covered with soil.
  3. Recycling/Reuse: Processing waste materials (plastic, glass) into new products or reusing items.
  4. Composting: Allowing organic waste to decompose to form manure.

48.
Describe challenges encountered in implementation of universal health coverage. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Inadequate Funding: Insufficient budget allocation and reliance on donor funding.
  2. Shortage of Human Resources: Lack of adequate doctors, nurses, and specialists, especially in rural areas.
  3. Poor Infrastructure: Inadequate health facilities and equipment in remote areas.
  4. Inequality/Inequity: Disparities in access between rich/poor and urban/rural populations.

  6. High Out-of-Pocket Expenditure: Many citizens still pay for services due to gaps in insurance (NHIF/SHIF) coverage.

Section 3: Long Answer Question (20 Marks)

49.
Health care workers are prone to various types of occupational hazards.
a) Classify types of occupational hazards and give examples. (10 marks)
b) Discuss hierarchy of occupational hazard control. (10 marks)
Show Answer

a) Classification of Occupational Hazards:

  1. Biological Hazards: Risks from infectious agents.
    • Examples: HIV, Hepatitis B (needle sticks), Tuberculosis (airborne), COVID-19.
  2. Chemical Hazards: Risks from toxic substances.
    • Examples: Disinfectants (Glutaraldehyde), Chemotherapy drugs, Latex allergy, Mercury (broken thermometers).
  3. Physical Hazards: Risks from energy sources.
    • Examples: Radiation (X-rays), Noise, Heat/Cold stress, Slips and falls on wet floors.
  4. Ergonomic Hazards: Risks from poor body positioning.
    • Examples: Back injuries from lifting patients, Repetitive strain injury (typing), Prolonged standing.
  5. Psychosocial Hazards: Risks to mental well-being.
    • Examples: Burnout, Stress from workload, Workplace violence, Shift work fatigue.

b) Hierarchy of Occupational Hazard Control (from most to least effective):

  1. Elimination (Most Effective): Physically removing the hazard.
    • Example: Stopping the use of mercury thermometers completely.
  2. Substitution: Replacing the hazard with something less dangerous.
    • Example: Using water-based cleaners instead of toxic solvents.
  3. Engineering Controls: Isolating people from the hazard.
    • Example: Using safety-engineered needles (auto-retract), Installing ventilation systems (negative pressure rooms for TB), Shielding for X-rays.
  4. Administrative Controls: Changing the way people work.
    • Example: Staff rotation to reduce exposure time, Training on safety, Vaccination policies (Hep B), Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs).
  5. Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) (Least Effective): Protecting the worker with gear.
    • Example: Wearing gloves, masks, gowns, goggles, and lead aprons. It is the last line of defense.

Note: The hierarchy is an inverted triangle, with elimination at the top (most effective) and PPE at the bottom (least effective but most commonly used).
