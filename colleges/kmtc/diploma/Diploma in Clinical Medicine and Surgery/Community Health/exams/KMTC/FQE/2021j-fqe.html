7.
Concerning mid upper arm circumference (MUAC)
A) Severe malnutrition is defined by MUAC < 11.5cm or 115 mm in children aged 6-59 months
B) It is the circumference of the left upper arm
C) It is measured at the mid-point between the hip of the shoulder and the wrist joint
D) It is used to access stunting
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Severe malnutrition is defined by MUAC < 11.5cm or 115 mm in children aged 6-59 months

Explanation:

  • A: Correct definition of Severe Acute Malnutrition (SAM).
  • B: Correctly measured on the Left arm (standard convention), but A is a definitive diagnostic value.
  • C: Incorrect. It is measured at the midpoint between the acromion (shoulder tip) and the olecranon (elbow), not the wrist.

17.
Infectivity refers to
A) The proportion of exposed persons who become infected
B) The proportion of infected individuals who develop clinically apparent disease
C) The proportion of clinically apparent cases are severe or fatal
D) The proportion of exposed persons who are asymptomatic
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) The proportion of exposed persons who become infected

Explanation:

  • Infectivity: Ability to invade and multiply (Exposed -> Infected).
  • Pathogenicity: Ability to cause disease (Infected -> Sick) (Option B).
  • Virulence: Ability to cause severe disease/death (Sick -> Severe/Dead) (Option C).

18.
Which of the following statement regarding factors of disease causation is FALSE?
A) Enabling factors assist in the development of a disease e.g. socio-economic status
B) Precipitating factors are associated with immediate exposure to the disease agent e.g. drinking contaminated water
C) Reinforcing factors create a state of susceptibility so that the host becomes vulnerable to the agent e.g. sex
D) Risk factors increase the chances of an individual to develop a disease
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Reinforcing factors create a state of susceptibility...

Explanation:

  • Predisposing factors (not reinforcing) create susceptibility (e.g., Age, Sex).
  • Reinforcing factors: Strengthen or perpetuate a behavior (e.g., peer pressure, reward).
  • Enabling factors: Facilitate the disease/behavior (e.g., poverty, availability).
  • Precipitating factors: Trigger the onset (e.g., exposure).
So statement C is False because it describes Predisposing factors but calls them Reinforcing.

19.
The first (1st) step in investigating a disease outbreak is;
A) Defining the population at risk
B) Confirming the existence of the outbreak
C) Verification of the diagnosis
D) Finding cases systematically and recording information
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Verification of the diagnosis (or B depending on source)

Explanation: CDC steps: 1. Prepare for field work. 2. Establish the existence of an outbreak. 3. Verify the diagnosis. Sometimes 2 and 3 are swapped or grouped. However, logic dictates you must know what disease you are dealing with (Verify diagnosis) to know if the numbers exceed baseline (Confirm outbreak). But most guidelines list "Confirm existence" or "Prepare" first. In MCQs, "Confirming existence of outbreak" (comparing observed vs expected) is often Step 1 or 2. If "Prepare" is missing, B is usually the best answer. Wait, C "Verification of diagnosis" ensures you aren't chasing false alarms. Standard text: 1. Prepare, 2. Verify Diagnosis, 3. Establish Existence. If following that order -> C is first among the options (Step 2 vs Step 3).

47.
500 students out of the total 2000 students in KMTC Nairobi Campus had gastroenteritis after taking their evening meal
i) calculate the true attack rate (2.5 marks)
ii) calculate incidence rate (2.5 marks)
Show Answer

i) Attack Rate:

Formula: $\frac{\text{Number of new cases}}{\text{Total population at risk}} \times 100$

Calculation: $\frac{500}{2000} \times 100 = 25\%$

ii) Incidence Rate:

In the context of an acute outbreak (food poisoning), the Attack Rate is essentially a special form of incidence rate. The calculation remains the same because the time period is the duration of the outbreak.

Incidence = $\frac{500}{2000} \times 1000$ (if expressed per 1000) = 250 per 1000 students.

Note: Usually Attack Rate is % and Incidence is per 1000 or 100,000. Both measure new cases.

Section 3: Long Answer Question (20 Marks)

1.
Communicable diseases are among the leading causes of death and disability in Kenya. Describe the major ways of controlling communicable diseases (20 marks)
Show Answer

Introduction: Control of communicable diseases involves breaking the chain of transmission at one or more links: the infectious agent, the reservoir, the mode of transmission, or the susceptible host.

1. Controlling the Reservoir (Source):

  • Diagnosis and Treatment: Early identification and treatment of cases to reduce their infectiousness (e.g., treating TB patients).
  • Isolation: Separating infected individuals from healthy ones (e.g., Cholera/Ebola wards) to prevent spread.
  • Quarantine: Restricting movement of exposed contacts to see if they develop disease.
  • Surveillance: Tracking cases to identify sources.
  • Animal Reservoir Control: Vaccinating dogs (Rabies), culling infected animals.

2. Interrupting the Route of Transmission:

  • Environmental Sanitation: Safe disposal of human waste (Latrines) to prevent fecal-oral diseases.
  • Water Safety: Chlorination, boiling, and protecting water sources.
  • Vector Control:
    • Chemical: Indoor Residual Spraying (IRS).
    • Physical: Insecticide Treated Nets (ITNs), draining stagnant water.
    • Biological: Using larvivorous fish.
  • Food Hygiene: Inspection of food handlers, proper cooking, and storage.
  • Personal Hygiene: Hand washing, cough etiquette.
  • Disinfection/Sterilization: Cleaning medical equipment and surfaces.

3. Protecting the Susceptible Host:

  • Immunization (Vaccination): The most effective measure (e.g., KEPI vaccines) to build individual and herd immunity.
  • Chemoprophylaxis: Giving drugs to prevent infection (e.g., Malaria prophylaxis for travelers, PEP for HIV).
  • Nutrition: Improving nutritional status to boost the immune system (Vitamin A).
  • Personal Protective Equipment (PPE): Using masks, gloves, condoms to create a barrier.

4. Health Education:

  • Empowering the community with knowledge to adopt protective behaviors.
