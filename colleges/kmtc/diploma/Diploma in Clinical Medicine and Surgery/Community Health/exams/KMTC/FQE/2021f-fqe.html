Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions (40 Marks)

1.
With regards to occupational health and safety, which of the following is a type of pneumoconiosis
A) Silicosis of lungs
B) Carbonization of lungs
C) Kalic pneumoniosis
D) Silicon poisoning
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Silicosis of lungs

Explanation: Pneumoconiosis is a group of interstitial lung diseases caused by the inhalation of mineral dusts. Silicosis is the most common form, caused by inhaling crystalline silica dust. Other types include Asbestosis and Coal Workers' Pneumoconiosis (Black Lung).

2.
In regards to ergonomic hazards
A) Not wearing PPEs is a risk
B) It involves risk of awkward movement
C) Poor lighting and ventilation is a risk factor
D) It is the same as physical hazard
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) It involves risk of awkward movement

Explanation: Ergonomic hazards are physical conditions that may pose a risk of injury to the musculoskeletal system, such as repetitive strain, awkward postures, or improper lifting. Poor lighting is a physical hazard, and lack of PPE is a safety management failure.

3.
Examples of international notifiable diseases include the following, except?
A) Ebola
B) SARS
C) Yellow fever
D) Leprosy
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) Leprosy

Explanation: Under the International Health Regulations (IHR 2005), diseases that constitute a Public Health Emergency of International Concern (PHEIC) must be notified. These classically include Yellow Fever, Cholera, and Plague, and now include emerging threats like Ebola and SARS/COVID-19. Leprosy is a chronic disease and is not an immediate international notifiable emergency.

4.
The most hygienic method of disposing body of a Covid-19 victim is
A) Sea burial
B) Earth burial
C) Cremation
D) Home burial
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Cremation

Explanation: Cremation is considered the most hygienic method as high temperatures completely destroy all pathogens. While deep burial is permitted and safe if protocols are followed, cremation eliminates the biological risk entirely.

5.
Water washed disease include
A) Malaria, typhoid, amoeba
B) Dysentery, typhoid, amoeba
C) Filariasis, yellow fever, measles
D) Trachoma, schistosomiasis, amoeba
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) Trachoma, schistosomiasis, amoeba

Explanation:

  • Water-washed diseases: Caused by lack of water for hygiene (e.g., Trachoma, Scabies).
  • Water-borne: Transmitted by drinking (Typhoid, Cholera).
  • Water-based: Host lives in water (Schistosomiasis).
Note: The options here are mixed. Trachoma is the classic water-washed example. Schistosomiasis is water-based. Amoebiasis is water-borne. However, among the choices, Option D is the only one containing a classic water-washed disease (Trachoma). Options A, B, C lists vectors or pure water-borne/airborne.

6.
Concerning water treatment, the next phase after aeration is
A) Filtration
B) Sedimentation
C) Flocculation
D) Chlorination
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Flocculation

Explanation: The typical sequence is: Screening → Aeration → Coagulation/Flocculation → Sedimentation → Filtration → Disinfection (Chlorination). After aeration (to remove gases/tastes), chemicals are added to clump particles together (flocculation).

7.
Concerning communicable disease transmission
A) Mosquitoes transmit yellow fever and filariasis
B) Sand flies transmit onchocerciasis and malaria
C) Ticks transmit dengue and relapsing fever
D) Fleas transmit relapsing and dengue fever
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Mosquitoes transmit yellow fever and filariasis

Explanation:

  • Mosquitoes: Transmit Malaria (Anopheles), Yellow Fever (Aedes), Filariasis (Culex/Anopheles), Dengue (Aedes).
  • Sand flies: Transmit Leishmaniasis (Kala-azar). Onchocerciasis is by Black flies.
  • Ticks: Transmit Relapsing fever. Dengue is by mosquitoes.
  • Fleas: Transmit Plague and Typhus.

8.
About clinical waste from hospital
A) Black is for sharps and general wastes
B) Red is for nonhazardous wastes
C) Yellow is for infectious wastes
D) Safely box is for waste with blood stains
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Yellow is for infectious wastes

Explanation:

  • Yellow: Infectious waste (cotton, gloves, tissue).
  • Red: Highly infectious waste (lab cultures).
  • Black: General/Non-infectious waste.
  • Safety Box: Sharps (needles, blades).

9.
Human fascioliasis arises from consumption of vegetables contaminated by
A) Urine
B) Faeces
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Faeces

Explanation: Fasciola hepatica (liver fluke) eggs are passed in the feces of infected animals (sheep, cattle) or humans. They hatch in water, infect snails, and the larvae encyst on aquatic vegetation (watercress). Humans get infected by eating these plants.

10.
Cyst of echinococcus granulosus is usually found in uncooked vegetables contaminated by excreta of
A) Human being
B) Dog
C) Goat
D) Pigs
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Dog

Explanation: Dogs are the definitive host for Echinococcus granulosus (Hydatid disease). They pass eggs in their feces. Humans are accidental intermediate hosts who get infected by ingesting eggs from dog feces (via contaminated food/water/hands).

13.
The vector involved in transmission of Trachoma
A) Pulex irritants
B) Musica domestica
C) Aedes egyptae
D) Plebotomas martin
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Musica domestica (Musca domestica)

Explanation: Trachoma is caused by Chlamydia trachomatis. It is spread by direct contact with eye/nose discharges, but the House Fly (Musca domestica) acts as a mechanical vector carrying the bacteria from eye to eye.

14.
A high BOD (Biological Oxygen Demand) indicates
A) Water is clean
B) Absence of microbial action
C) High level of microbial pollution
D) Low level of microbial pollution
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) High level of microbial pollution

Explanation: BOD measures the amount of oxygen required by aerobic microorganisms to decompose organic matter in water. High BOD means there is a lot of organic waste (pollution) for bacteria to eat, depleting oxygen for fish.

17.
In regards to rabies
A) It is a water bone disease
B) Bacterial infection
C) Usually treatable
D) It’s a zoonotic disease
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) It’s a zoonotic disease

Explanation: Rabies is a viral zoonosis (transmitted from animals to humans), typically through the bite of a rabid dog. It is almost always fatal once symptoms appear (not "usually treatable" in that sense, though preventable with PEP).

18.
The presence of high coliform counts in water indicate
A) Contamination by human wastes
B) Decreased biological oxygen demand
C) Hydrocarbon contamination
D) Phosphorous contamination
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Contamination by human wastes

Explanation: Coliform bacteria (like E. coli) inhabit the intestines of warm-blooded animals. Their presence in water indicates fecal contamination and the potential presence of other pathogens.

19.
Aluminum sulphate is usually added at which level of water treatment
A) Disinfection
B) Coagulation
C) Flocculation
D) Filtration
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Coagulation

Explanation: Aluminum sulphate (Alum) is a chemical coagulant. It destabilizes the charges on suspended particles, allowing them to come together. The process of adding and rapid mixing is coagulation.

20.
The most effective way to prevent water washed diseases
A) Provision of sufficient amount of clean water
B) Provision of high quality water
C) Minimize wastage of water
D) All the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Provision of sufficient amount of clean water

Explanation: Water-washed diseases (like scabies, trachoma) are caused by lack of water for personal hygiene. Therefore, the quantity (sufficiency) of water is more important than quality for preventing these specific diseases.

27.
Concerning cancer, which statement is false
A) Viruses can be a causative agent
B) Usually hereditary
C) Prostate cancer is common in males above 60 years
D) Cancer of the cervix can be treated with human papilloma vaccine
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Usually hereditary

29.
Which is not a factor responsible for change in population
A) Marriages
B) Migration
C) Death
D) Age groups
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) Age groups

Explanation: The three direct components of population change are Fertility (Births), Mortality (Deaths), and Migration. Marriage is a proximate determinant of fertility. Age groups are a structural result, not a dynamic factor of change itself in the same category as the "Vital Events".

Explanation: Age of marriage (affects reproductive span), birth interval (affects number of children), and religion (affects contraceptive use/values) all directly influence fertility. Climate has negligible direct influence.

35.
Which is NOT a component of epidemiological triad

Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Time (or D Reservoir)

Explanation: The classic Epidemiological Triad (Triangle) consists of Agent, Host, and Environment.

Section 2: Short Answer Questions (40 Marks)

1.
Define the following terms as used in epidemiology (5 marks)
a) Epidemic
b) Endemic
c) Holoendemic
d) Pandemic
e) Morbidity
Show Answer

Definitions:

  • a) Epidemic: The occurrence of disease cases in excess of normal expectancy in a specific community or region.
  • b) Endemic: The constant presence or usual prevalence of a disease within a geographic area or population group.
  • c) Holoendemic: A disease that is highly prevalent in a population, affecting a high proportion of individuals, especially children (e.g., Malaria in some regions).
  • d) Pandemic: An epidemic that has spread over several countries or continents, usually affecting a large number of people.
  • e) Morbidity: The state of being diseased or unhealthy within a population; often expressed as a rate of illness.

Explain five elements of primary health care (5 marks)
Show Answer

4.
Using examples list five classes of hazards in a work place (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Physical Hazards: Noise, radiation, vibration, heat, cold.
  2. Chemical Hazards: Solvents, pesticides, fumes, dusts (asbestos).
  3. Biological Hazards: Bacteria, viruses (Hep B), fungi, parasites.

5.
With examples, differentiate between emerging and re-emerging diseases (5 marks)
Show Answer

Emerging Diseases: Diseases that have appeared in a population for the first time, or that may have existed previously but are rapidly increasing in incidence or geographic range.

  • Examples: COVID-19, Ebola, HIV/AIDS, SARS.

Re-emerging Diseases: Diseases that were previously controlled or had declined significantly but are now coming back to become a major health problem.

  • Examples: Tuberculosis (due to HIV/MDR), Malaria (drug resistance), Measles (vaccine gaps), Cholera.

8.
In experimental study designs, list the key features of a clinical trial (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Randomization: Random assignment of participants to intervention or control groups to reduce bias.
  2. Control Group: A group that receives placebo or standard care for comparison.

Section 3: Long Answer Question (20 Marks)

1.
Explain three (3) methods in which infectious diseases can be prevented and controlled (20 marks)
Show Answer

Introduction: Prevention and control of infectious diseases aims to reduce incidence, prevalence, and eliminate transmission. The methods target the reservoir, route of transmission, or susceptible host.

1. Immunization (Vaccination):

  • Mechanism: Stimulates the body's immune system to produce antibodies against specific pathogens (viruses/bacteria) without causing the disease.
  • Application: Routine immunization (KEPI) for children (Measles, Polio, DPT). Mass vaccination campaigns during outbreaks.
  • Impact: Creates herd immunity, protecting vulnerable populations and leading to eradication (e.g., Smallpox) or elimination (Polio).

2. Water, Sanitation, and Hygiene (WASH):

  • Safe Water: Treating water (chlorination, boiling) prevents water-borne diseases like Cholera and Typhoid.
  • Sanitation: Proper disposal of human waste (latrines/toilets) breaks the fecal-oral transmission cycle. Proper waste management controls vectors.
  • Hygiene: Handwashing with soap breaks transmission of diarrheal and respiratory infections (Covid-19). Food hygiene prevents food poisoning.

3. Vector Control:

  • Environmental Management: Draining stagnant water to destroy mosquito breeding sites (Malaria/Dengue).
  • Chemical Control: Indoor Residual Spraying (IRS) and use of larvicides.
  • Physical Barriers: Use of Insecticide-Treated Nets (ITNs) to prevent bites.
  • Biological Control: Using natural enemies (e.g., fish eating larvae).

(Alternative/Additional Point) 4. Health Education & Behavioral Change:

  • Empowering the community with knowledge on disease transmission and prevention (e.g., safe sex practices for HIV, social distancing for respiratory diseases).
