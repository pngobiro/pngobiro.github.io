Question 4

State 6 factors that affect heart rate.

Answer

  1. The terrain: A walk uphill increases the heart rate due to higher oxygen demand to compensate for the much-used oxygen. A walk downhill does the vice versa.
  2. Temperature: An increase in body temperature causes the body to sense thermal stress, hence an increase in heart rate to supply blood more superficially and rapidly to your skin to dissipate heat. Similarly, a decrease in body temperature increases blood supply to warm you.
  3. Dehydration: As one becomes increasingly dehydrated, their blood becomes thicker due to the accumulation of waste products. This causes the heart rate to increase in order to maintain the cardiac output volume.
  4. Diminishing glycogen: The body stores energy in tissues in the form of ATP and glucose. A reduction in the optimum levels causes the HR to increase in order to supply the insufficient glycogen present in the blood to the rest of the body systems.
  5. Emotions and anxiety: Our body's natural response to emotions like stress is to produce stress hormones that include cortisol and adrenaline, which cause our heart to beat more rapidly and blood vessels to narrow and help blood reach the central parts. In turn, they also increase blood pressure and blood sugar.
  6. Medication: Some medication when taken can either increase or decrease the heart rate due to impairment of the normal electrical signals in your heart and cause an increased/faster heart rate (Tachycardia), causing dizziness or lightheadedness.

Question 11

With an aid of a well-labeled diagram, explain the development of bone tissue.

Answer

Endochondral ossification follows five steps:

  1. Mesenchymal cells differentiate into chondrocytes that produce a cartilage model of the future bony skeleton.
  2. Blood vessels on the edge of the cartilage model bring osteoblasts that deposit a bony collar.
  3. Capillaries penetrate cartilage and deposit bone inside the cartilage model, forming the primary ossification center.
  4. Cartilage and chondrocytes continue to grow at the ends of the bone while the medullary cavity expands and remodels.
  5. Secondary ossification centers develop after birth.
  6. Hyaline cartilage remains at the epiphyseal (growth) plate and at the joint surface as articular cartilage.

Question 14

State 2 mechanisms which control the blood pressure.

Answer

The following mechanisms are used in blood pressure regulation:

  1. Nervous Control: A variety of cardiovascular control systems manage the cardiac output in 3 ways:
    • Stimulating the cardiac output by increasing the heart rate through sympathetic cardiac nerves.
    • Inhibition of cardiac output by decreasing the heart rate through parasympathetic nerve impulses.
    • The vasomotor center regulates blood vessel diameter through sympathetic motor neurons.

    Note: The cardiac center receives information through baroreceptors located in the carotid sinus, aortic arch, and the right atrium, and also through chemoreceptors which detect CO2 and O2 concentration at the carotid sinus and aortic arch.

  2. Hormonal Regulation: This is done by the kidney through regulation of blood volumes (Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System, RAAS). In response to low blood pressure, juxtaglomerular cells secrete renin into the blood system. Renin converts angiotensinogen to angiotensin I, which is then converted to angiotensin II. Angiotensin II is a potent vasoconstrictor that constricts blood vessels throughout the body. This raises blood pressure and reduces blood volume delivered to the kidney. In this effect, the kidney has a reduced potential to excrete water, meaning blood pressure is raised by increasing blood volume.

Question 20

State the functions of insulin.

Answer

Insulin is a natural hormone produced by the beta cells of the pancreas that regulates the usage and storage of blood sugar (glucose). Its primary functions are:

  • Facilitating the uptake of glucose from the blood into cells for energy.
  • Stimulating the liver to store glucose in the form of glycogen.
  • Promoting the synthesis of fatty acids and storage of fat in adipose tissue.
  • Inhibiting the production of glucose by the liver (gluconeogenesis).

Question 21

Explain the three types of joints giving examples.

Answer

  • Fibrous joints: Permits no movement e.g. skull joints (sutures).
  • Cartilaginous joints: Formed by a pad of tough fibrocartilage acting as a shock absorber e.g. between vertebral bodies.
  • Synovial joints: Characterized by the presence of a capsule between the articulating bones, allowing for a wide range of motion e.g. hinge, ball and socket, gliding joints.

Question 22

Discuss the functions of stomach.

Answer

  • Temporary storage for food: Food passes from the esophagus to the stomach where it is held for 2 hours or longer.
  • Mixing and breakdown of food: Achieved by contraction and relaxation of the muscle layers in the stomach, churning food into chyme.
  • Digestion of food: Secretion of gastric juices containing enzymes like pepsin (for protein digestion) and hydrochloric acid (to kill microbes and provide an acidic environment).
  • Secretion of hormones: The stomach secretes digestive hormones like gastrin.

Question 23

With an aid of a well-labeled diagram of nephron, describe the process of urine formation.

Answer

The process of urine formation in the kidneys includes the following steps:

  1. Glomerular Filtration: Urine formation begins when blood is filtered by the glomerulus and enters the Bowman's capsule, forming the glomerular filtrate. The afferent arteriole entering the glomerulus is wider than the efferent arteriole, which increases the blood pressure within the glomerulus, aiding filtration. The glomerular filtrate is composed of all blood constituents except corpuscles and plasma proteins.
  2. Tubular Reabsorption: When the glomerular filtrate passes into the proximal convoluted tubule, essential nutrients are reabsorbed back into the blood. Glucose, amino acids, water, and ions like sodium, potassium, chloride, bicarbonate, magnesium, and calcium are reabsorbed. As the filtrate moves to the collecting duct, Antidiuretic Hormone (ADH) acts to reabsorb excess water, concentrating the urine and decreasing its volume.
  3. Tubular Secretion: When the glomerular filtrate is in the proximal tubule, waste products like creatinine and uric acid are secreted into it from the blood. As the filtrate moves to the distal convoluted tubule, urea, potassium ions, and some hydrogen ions are also secreted into it. This process removes additional wastes from the blood and helps regulate blood pH.

Question 25

Explain the structural relationship between chromosomes, genes and DNA.

Answer

The relationship between DNA, genes, and chromosomes can be understood as a hierarchy of organization within the cell's nucleus.

  • DNA (Deoxyribonucleic Acid): This is the fundamental molecule of heredity. It is a long, double-helix structure composed of nucleotides. The sequence of these nucleotides contains the genetic instructions for building and maintaining an organism.
  • Gene: A gene is a specific segment of a DNA molecule. Each gene carries the code for a particular protein or functional RNA molecule. Genes are the basic units of heredity, determining specific traits (like eye color) or functions (like producing an enzyme).
  • Chromosome: To fit inside the tiny nucleus of a cell, the long DNA molecule is tightly coiled and compacted around proteins called histones. This highly organized, condensed structure of DNA and proteins is called a chromosome. Humans have 46 chromosomes in each somatic cell, organized into 23 pairs.

In summary, a gene is a section of the DNA molecule, and the entire DNA molecule, when tightly packaged, forms a chromosome. Therefore, chromosomes are essentially carriers of thousands of genes.

Question 1

Differentiate between the two terminologies:

(a) Tachycardia

(b) Tachypnoea

(c) Bradycardia

Answer

(a) Tachycardia: An abnormally fast heart rate, typically over 100 beats per minute in a resting adult.

(b) Tachypnoea: A condition that refers to abnormally rapid breathing, typically more than 20 breaths per minute in an adult.

(c) Bradycardia: An abnormally slow or reduced heart rate, typically below 60 beats per minute in a resting adult.

Question 4

List 4 observations noted on respirations.

Answer

  1. Tachypnoea: Abnormally fast breathing, over 20 breaths per minute.
  2. Bradypnea: Abnormally slow breathing, less than 12 breaths per minute.
  3. Apnoea: The absence of respiration for several seconds, which can lead to respiratory arrest.
  4. Dyspnoea: Difficulty in breathing, where the patient gasps for air.
  5. Cheyne-Stokes respiration: The breathing is shallow, very slow and laboured with periods of apnoea. This type of breathing is often seen in the dying patient.
  6. Hyperventilation: Patients may breathe rapidly due to a physical or psychological cause.

Question 8

Define the following terms:

a. Isolation nursing.

b. Barrier nursing.

c. Reverse barrier nursing.

Answer

a. Isolation nursing: It is an infection prevention method of nursing an infected patient separately from the rest of the patients to prevent the spread of the infectious agent.

b. Barrier nursing: The local isolation of a patient with an infectious disease by using personal protective equipment (PPE) such as gowns, caps, overshoes, gloves, and masks to avoid spread.

c. Reverse barrier nursing: A range of nursing care practices used to protect highly susceptible or immunocompromised hospital patients from acquiring infections from others.

Question 9

One of the methods of handing over reports is by moving from patient to patient, reporting on each patient. List any 2 advantages and 2 disadvantages of this type of report.

Answer

Advantages:

  1. It gives confidence to the patients by allowing them to meet their incoming caregiver and participate in their care.
  2. It allows for immediate visual assessment of the patient and their environment while the report is being given.
  3. It helps the patient in creating rapport with the incoming nurse.
  4. It is open, transparent, and more professional.

Disadvantages:

  1. It is time-consuming as it involves moving around the ward.
  2. It can cause disturbance and anxiety to resting or critically ill patients.
  3. Patient confidentiality may not be well maintained if sensitive information is discussed openly.
  4. It may lead to the exposure of staff weaknesses or disagreements in front of the patient.

Question 10

List the 6 components of a nursing care plan.

Answer

  1. Assessment: Client health assessment, including subjective and objective data.
  2. Nursing Diagnosis: Identification of the client's health problems based on the assessment data.
  3. Outcomes/Goals: Expected client outcomes are outlined. These may be long and short term.
  4. Nursing Interventions: Specific actions documented in the care plan to achieve the goals.
  5. Rationale: The scientific reason for the interventions, in order to provide evidence-based care.
  6. Evaluation: Assessment of whether the goals have been met and the effectiveness of the interventions.

Question 11

For the following statements, indicate whether they are TRUE or FALSE:

a. Nursing diagnosis remains constant while medical diagnosis keeps changing.

b. Dehydration is a nursing diagnosis when a patient loses fluid through diarrhea and vomiting.

Answer

a. False. A nursing diagnosis can change as the patient's response to their health problem changes, while a medical diagnosis often remains constant until the condition is cured.

b. True. Dehydration (or more accurately, 'Deficient Fluid Volume') is a NANDA-approved nursing diagnosis that describes the state of a patient who has lost significant fluid.
