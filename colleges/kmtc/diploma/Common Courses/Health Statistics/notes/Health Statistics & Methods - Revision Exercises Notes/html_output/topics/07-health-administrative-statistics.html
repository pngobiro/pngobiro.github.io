Chapter 7: Health Administrative Statistics

Introduction to Health Administrative Statistics

The collection of meaningful statistics is an important function of a hospital or clinic. Health records are the primary source of data used in compiling health care statistics.

The health records department staff, therefore may be responsible for the collection, analysis, interpretation and presentation of statistical data wherever possible.

Statistics are only accurate as the original sources from which they are taken. The health information mx/health record profession should see that health records and other source documents are complete and readily available to meet the requirements for the production of useful statistics.

Health service data are used for:

  1. Comparisons of present and past performance of the hospital or clinic.
  2. Guide for planning future development of the hospital
  3. Appraisal of work performed by the medical, nursing and other staff.
  4. Hospital funding from GOK, NGOs etc
  5. Research.

Key definitions to understand H.A.S

Admissions

The formal process whereby a person is accepted by a hospital for the process of Rx as an inpatient.

If an in-patient is formally discharged from the hospital and then returns for further Rx, the admission process is repeated and a second admission is recorded in the statistics.

Hospital administration statistics

Health administrative data is generated from health institutions where health services are provided to the patients or clients. The information obtained indicates a detailed account of the activities that are carried out in a health institution. It forms a data base which is used by health managers/administrators in the Mx and planning of health services.

Examples of Uses

1. Management of health services

Managers of health services require data concerning patients in order to manage them more effectively and efficiently. This include the mx of scarce resources such as patients food, linen, transport, staff, finances available, physical facilities such as beds and theatre etc. Data concerning family planning and maternal child health services (MCH/FP) are also required for evaluation and better mx for such services. The requirements of doctors and other health workers needs statistical information concerning diagnosis, operations, Rx and investigations. Adequate information would improve the efficiency of the process involved in arriving at the diagnosis and subsequent Rx, thereby making it possible to eliminate wasteful process.

2. Planners

Health information aids planners in making accurate plans for day to day and future requirements of health activities with accurate and timely data, planners are able to estimate for service requirements such as drugs, finances and physical facility requirements. The health information data bank enables health workers and information officers to create an analytical frame work commonly referred to as Health Activity Analysis.

3. Health Activity Analysis

This is a systematic way of collection, analysis and presentation of health data. It concerns data obtained from patients/clients attendances, staff, bed use, physical facilities, service delivery data, finances etc.

4. Patient/client attendance

Patients/clients make use of health facilities either as in-patient or out-patient clients.

Tools for Collecting H.A.S: In-patient Statistics

In-patient indicates the activities of in-patient services in a health institution. The data is collected by a form commonly known as Daily Bed Return. (DBR). The form is designed to be useful in specific circumstances.

DBR-MOH 328

Explanation of sections.

Section A-E

It's normally computed by in charges of each ward. This is done after doing a physical counting of patients in the ward, who remain after discharges or deaths have been removed from the ward. The physical count is normally carried out preferably at midnight of each day. This is the time when patient's movement is minimal.

This section is divided into 2 main sections:

  1. Admissions
  2. Discharges
Admissions

All patients admitted from home, transferred from another ward, coming from parole or returned from having absconded are indicated on admission section. All patients indicated on this section are added to patients who remained in the ward the previous day and the total is obtained which is the total number of patients in that ward.

Discharges

These are discharges from the wards which are discharged home, those who are dead, transferred to another ward, absconders (run away from hospitals) and those who are granted hospital leaves (parole). The total discharges are subtracted from the totals obtained in admission section. The results obtained must be equal to the physical count of patients who are in the ward at the time of physical count. This is the figure entered in section E of the DBR in the column of patients who are either sleeping on the bed or cots (accommodated).

Section F

Section F of the DBR is normally for records use only. The officer in charge of health information completes this section. This section is used to balance the DBR to check the accuracy of entries in all other sections. The last total that's obtained after adding all the admissions to the previous day, number of patients who remained in the ward and subtracting all discharges must be equal to patients indicated in section E, that is today's daily return numbers of patients.

When DBR is returned to the records department is properly checked, summarized on daily summary in patients statistics form. The daily summary form, summarizes the day statistics. It indicates the total admissions and discharges in the ward. There is a column for OBD (occupied Bed Days), this is the total number of patients remaining in the ward each day.

Inpatient Administration Statistics: Terminology Used

  1. Bed compliment/staffed bed: It refers to the number of beds permanently allocated to a hospital for Rx of inpatients
  2. Available beds: This is the number of beds physically available at the ward on a specific time.
  3. Bed states: The number of patients occupying hospital bed at any given time.
  4. Staffed beds allocated: The number of available staffed beds and temporary unavailable beds.
  5. Bed turnover/turnover per bed/through put per bed: The average number of patients expected to be treated per bed.
  6. Turn over interval: The average number of days beds lie vacant between successful patients (the one being discharged and the one being admitted to occupy the same bed at a given time).
  7. Percentage occupancy: This is the percentage of actual patients' days to the maximum available patient days as determined by bed at any given period.
  8. Occupied bed days: This is the total number of patients remaining in the hospital ward each day added together over the reference period.
  9. Average length of stay (ALOS): This is the average number of days a patient occupies a hospital bed during his/her stay for Rx .
  10. Average occupancy/average number of patients: This is the average number of patients in a hospital during a specified period of time.
  11. Amenity bed: This is a bed provided in a single room or small ward for the time being required by any patient on medical grounds for which a patient may be charged part of the cost.
  12. In-patient: This is a patient who has gone through the full admission procedure of the hospital and who is occupying a bed in the in-patient department at the hour of the fixed count.
  13. Day cases: These are persons attending as non-resident patients for investigations, therapeutic tests, operative procedure or other Rx and who requires some form of preparation period of recovery or both involving the provision of accommodation and other services.
  14. Census: A count of in-patients at a given time. The census is always taken in a hospital at the same time each day, usually midnight. It provides the number of in-patients at census taking time.

Analytical Formulae

To be able to obtain the measures explained above we need to apply the following:

  1. Available bed days = available beds x days in period
    ABD = AB x DIP (month, year)
  2. Available beds = total authorized beds in a hospital (ward) during a specified period or available bed days divided by days in period
    AB = ABD / DIP
  3. Occupied bed days = total patients remaining each day or in-patient days added together for the days in the period or (% occ x available bed days) / 100
    OBD = %CC x ABD / 100
  4. % Occupancy = (OBD / ABD) x 100
  5. Average number of occupied beds (average number of patients)
    OB = OBD / DIP
  6. Vacant bed days (excess patient days)
    VBD = ABD - OBD
  7. ABD = VBD + OBD
  8. OBD = ABD - VBD
  9. Average length of stay
    ALOS = OBD / (Discharge & deaths)
  10. OBD = ALOS x D&D
  11. D&D = OBD / ALOS
  12. Turnover per bed/discharges & death per available bed/through put per bed
    TOB = D&D / AB
  13. D&D = TOB x AB
  14. AB = D&D / TOB
  15. Turn over interval (TOI) = VBD / D&D
  16. VBD = TOI x D&D
  17. D&D = VBD / TOI
