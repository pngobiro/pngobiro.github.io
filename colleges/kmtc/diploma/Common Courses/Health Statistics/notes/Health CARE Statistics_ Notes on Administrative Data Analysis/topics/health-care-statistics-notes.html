FORMULAS FOR COMPUTING HOSPITAL ADMINISTRATIVE STATISTICS

  1. Available bed days = Period (days) × Allocated beds
    $$ \mathrm{ABD} = \mathrm{P} \times \mathrm{AB} $$
  2. Occupied bed days / in patient days = Direct summary of DBR state
    $$ \mathrm{OBD} = \text{Sum of Daily IP days for the Period} $$
  3. % occupancy = (Occupied bed days / Available bed days) × 100
    $$ \% \mathrm{OCC} = \frac{\mathrm{OBD}}{\mathrm{ABD}} \times 100 $$
  4. Vacant bed days = Available bed days - Occupied bed days
    $$ \mathrm{VBD} = \mathrm{ABD} - \mathrm{OBD} $$
  5. Excess bed days = Occupied bed days - Available bed days
    $$ \mathrm{EBD} = \mathrm{OBD} - \mathrm{ABD} $$
  6. Length of stay (Average) = Occupied bed days / Discharges and deaths
    $$ \mathrm{LOS} = \frac{\mathrm{OBD}}{\mathrm{D}+\mathrm{D}} $$
  7. Turnover per bed = Discharges & deaths / Number of available beds
    $$ \mathrm{TOB} = \frac{\mathrm{D}+\mathrm{D}}{\mathrm{AB}} $$
  8. Turnover interval = Vacant bed days / Number of deaths & discharges
    $$ \mathrm{TOI} = \frac{\mathrm{VBD}}{\mathrm{D}+\mathrm{D}} $$

TERMINOLOGY (TERMS)

  1. Allocated beds / Available beds / Staffed beds / bed compliment - This is the number of beds constantly (permanently) allocated to a ward or hospital for treatment of patients.
  2. In-patient - Person who has gone all through admission procedures and is expected to occupy a bed in hospital ward at the in-patient departments at a specified occurrence or session.
  3. Period - This is usually the available time in which a patient can be treated while admitted and is usually counted in days. The common period of reference is usually 365/366 which is one year. However, months or part of the year can be computed as half a year or quarter year.
  4. Bed state - Refers to the number of patients occupying hospital beds during a given period of time.
  5. In-patient days (IPD) - It refers to occupied bed days (OBD). This is the total number of patients remaining or occupying a bed in the ward or hospital at each day.
  6. Percentage occupancy - This is the percentage of the actual in-patient days in comparison to the maximum available patient days as determined by the allocated beds capacity during a given period.
  7. Available bed days - Is the total number of bed days available of which the patient can occupy. Those particular beds in ward or hospital. This usually gives the available bed space for patient to use.
  8. Turnover per bed / through put for bed / bed turnover - refers to the number of patients expected to have used each bed available in a particular ward or hospital during the specified period of time.
  9. Turnover interval - Is the average period of time in which beds stay vacant between two successive patients' use of the same bed. This may also be said to be the period between a discharge and admission on the same bed in ward or hospital. It is also the number of days beds lie vacant between two successive admissions.
  10. Length of stay - number a patient occupies a bed thus from admission time of a particular patient to the discharge time of the same patient.
  11. Occupied bed days - is the total number of patients remaining in the hospital / ward each day added together over the reference period.
  12. Average occupancy/ average number of patients - This is the average number of patients in a hospital during a specified period.
  13. Amenity beds - is a bed provided in a single room or small ward for the time being not required by any patient on medical grounds for which a patient is charged part of the cost.
  14. Day case - are patients attending as a non-resident patient for investigations, therapeutic test, operative procedures or other treatment and who require some form of preparation period of recovery or both involving provision of accommodation and services.

MEDIAN

Denoting or relating to a value or quantity lying at the midpoint of a frequency distribution of observed values or quantities, such that there is an equal probability of falling above or below it.

To find the Median, place the numbers you are given in value order and find the middle number. Example: find the Median of {13, 23, 11, 16, 15, 10, 26}. Put them in order: {10, 11, 13, 15, 16, 23, 26}. The middle number is 15, so the median is 15. (If there are two middle numbers, you average them.)

MEDIAN IN GROUPED DATA

To obtain median in grouped frequency distribution some of principles identified in the ungrouped data computations are observed i.e. arranging of the data in order of magnitude from the smallest to the highest and identifying the median position. However, there are two common methods followed in determining median among grouped frequency distribution, and this includes;

  1. Calculation method
  2. Diagrammatic method

CALCULATION METHOD

Procedures

  1. Obtain data and ensure that its grouped and in order of classes and with their sizes.
  2. Workout the cumulative frequencies in a separate column usually abbreviated as CF (cf).
  3. Identify the median position by dividing the total frequency by two. Usually abb. As $N/2$ (some abb it as $\sum f/2$).
  4. Identify the location of median position in the distribution using the cumulative totals. The class in which the median position falls is referred to as the median class.
  5. Take note of the following of the identified median class:
    1. The lower class limit abbreviated as $L_{1}$
    2. Cumulative total level of the class before the median class abbreviated as $CF_{b}$
    3. The frequency of the median class abbreviated as $f_{m}$
    4. Class interval abbreviated as (i)
  6. Obtain median by substitution of the values in the following formula:
$$ \text{Median} = L_{1} + \left( \frac{\frac{N}{2} - CF_{b}}{f_{m}} \right) \times i $$

MODE

Is the measure of central tendency that represents a value or item or observation that occurs most frequently. It is also a representative value in a distribution or a series of figures which has the highest frequency. Mode is usually abbreviated as (Z).

MODE IN UNGROUPED DATA

Mode is obtained by inspection of the distribution to determine the item or observation that has occurred more than the others with the highest number of occurrences.

Procedure

  1. Must obtain data.
  2. Inspect the data by looking at each item and determining the number of times it occurs.
  3. Select the item that has occurred most and that would be the mode for that particular distribution.

Example 1

Using data on diploma 2002 students weights on those born in the month of 1st quarter of the year were 50, 92, 71, 60, 70, 75, 71, 74, 60, 74, 69, 63, 62, 50, 71, 80, 80.

Steps: Arrange the values from the smallest to the highest and tally the values.

The mode is 71 kgs for the above data, as it appears three times.

Interpretation

Most of the students in the diploma class born in the month of the 1st quarter of the year were weighing 71kgs.

SKEWNESS

This is a comparable characteristic which helps us to understand distributions further. This is especially where one has similar mean and standard deviation in distribution but the end diagrammatic view appearance is different among the distributions.

A measure of skewness indicates the difference between the manner in which items are distributed or spread in particular groups of items in a distribution in relation to the normal distribution.

The presentation showing the difference in appearance are referred as asymmetrical and the one which is normal (no skewedness) is referred to as symmetrical.
