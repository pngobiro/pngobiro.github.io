Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions

40 Marks
1.
A longitudinal or prospective study is also referred to as:
a) Ecological study
b) Cross-sectional study
c) Cohort study
d) Observational study
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Cohort study

A cohort study follows a group of people (cohort) over time (longitudinal/prospective) to see who develops the outcome.

2.
All of the following are true about odds ratio except:
a) It is an estimate of relative risk
b) It is the only measure of risk that can be obtained directly from a case control study
c) It is the ratio of incidences in exposed divided by incidence in exposed group
d) It can be calculated without data on rates (as in case of control study)
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) It is the ratio of incidences in exposed divided by incidence in exposed group

The Odds Ratio compares the odds of exposure in cases vs controls. The ratio of incidences is the Relative Risk (Risk Ratio).

3.
Which year was mortality data by birth, sex, region and season started?
a) 1662
b) 1954
c) 1800
d) 1980
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) 1662

John Graunt published the "Natural and Political Observations Mentioned in a Following Index, and Made Upon the Bills of Mortality" in 1662, marking the beginning of vital statistics.

4.
Which one of the following is not a term used in epidemiology?
a) Agent
b) Vector
c) Case
d) Data
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Data

"Data" is a general term used in all sciences. Agent, Vector, and Case are specific technical terms within the epidemiological triad and surveillance context. (Note: This is a "best fit" answer based on specificity).

5.
What type of bias arises when individuals with a particular exposure of adverse outcome is likely to remember their experiences differently from those who are not similarly affected?
a) Selection bias
b) Recall bias
c) Information bias
d) Surrogate bias
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Recall bias

Recall bias occurs (typically in case-control studies) when cases remember/report past exposures differently than controls due to the trauma or presence of the disease.

6.
A county health personnel visits outpatient clinics and emergency rooms to determine the number of cases of influenza virus. This is an example of which type of surveillance?
a) Active surveillance
b) Passive surveillance
c) Sentinel surveillance
d) Syndromic surveillance

Correct Answer: a) Active surveillance

Active surveillance involves health department staff contacting or visiting healthcare providers to solicit reports, rather than waiting for reports to arrive (passive).

7.
Which of the following is not a scope of epidemiology?
a) Causation
b) Natural history of disease
c) Evaluation of intervention
d) Establish priorities
Show Answer

Answer: This question is ambiguous as all options are valid scopes. However, "Establish priorities" is often a function of health management using epidemiology, whereas the others are core scientific scopes. If forced to choose, identifying causation, natural history, and evaluation are the "hard science" pillars.

8.
The following is not a measure of effect in epidemiology?
a) Odds ratio
b) Rate ratio
c) Proportion ratio
d) Risk ratio
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Proportion ratio

Odds Ratio, Rate Ratio, and Risk Ratio are standard measures of association/effect. "Proportion ratio" is less commonly used as a distinct standard term, though technically calculable ( Prevalence Ratio).

9.
Which one of the following is a type of surveillance?
a) Point surveillance
b) Propagated surveillance
c) Sentinel surveillance
d) Continuous surveillance
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Sentinel surveillance

Sentinel surveillance involves selecting specific reporting sites to monitor trends, rather than collecting data from all sites. Point and Propagated refer to epidemic curves/sources, not surveillance types.

10.
Which of the following is NOT a measures of variability?
a) Range
b) Variance
c) Mean
d) Standard deviation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Mean

The Mean is a measure of central tendency. Range, Variance, and Standard Deviation are measures of variability (dispersion).

11.
Which of the following is NOT a type of validity?
a) Construct validity
b) Content validity
c) Face validity
d) Time validity
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Time validity

Construct, Content, and Face validity are established psychometric/research concepts. "Time validity" is not a standard type of validity.

12.
____ is a measure of health outcome
a) Prevalence
b) Incidence
c) Morbidity
d) Risk
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Morbidity

Morbidity refers to the state of being diseased. Prevalence and Incidence are measures of morbidity. However, in exam contexts, "Morbidity" is often used as the umbrella category for health outcomes alongside Mortality.

13.

Polio and Guinea Worm are targeted for eradication. Neonatal Tetanus is targeted for elimination. Plague is a priority disease for surveillance but due to its animal reservoir, it is controlled rather than targeted for total eradication.

18.
Which of the following statements is true?
a) A specific case definition is one that is likely to include only (or mostly) true cases
b) A specific case definition is one that is considered loose or broad

d) A specific case definition is one that may exclude mild case

Correct Answer: a) A specific case definition is one that is likely to include only (or mostly) true cases

Section 2: Short Answer Questions

40 Marks
41.
Outline five principles of epidemiology.
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Distribution: Analyzing disease by Time, Place, and Person.
  2. Determinants: Identifying causes and risk factors (Agent, Host, Environment).
  3. Population Focus: Focusing on groups/communities rather than individuals.
  4. Scientific Approach: Using systematic data collection, analysis, and interpretation (Surveillance).
  5. Prevention and Control: Applying findings to reduce morbidity and mortality.
42.
Explain the following terms as used in Epidemiology:
i) Vector
ii) Confounding bias
Show Answer

Answer:

  • i) Vector: An organism (usually an arthropod like a mosquito, tick, or flea) that does not cause disease itself but transmits pathogens from one host to another (e.g., Anopheles mosquito transmitting malaria).
  • ii) Confounding bias: A distortion of the association between an exposure and an outcome that occurs when a third variable (confounder) is associated with both the exposure and the outcome, but is not in the causal pathway (e.g., age or smoking status).
43.
Outline 5 challenges faced by IDSR in Kenya.
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Inadequate Funding: Lack of resources for surveillance activities and response.
  2. Poor Reporting: Late or incomplete submission of reports from facilities.
  3. Lack of Personnel: Shortage of trained staff for data analysis and investigation.
  4. Poor Infrastructure: Issues with internet connectivity, computers, and transport.
  5. Laboratory Capacity: Delays in specimen transport and confirmation of results.
44.
State 5 purposes of descriptive research.
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Measure Disease Burden: Quantify morbidity and mortality rates.
  2. Identify High-Risk Groups: Determine who is most affected (Age, Sex).
  3. Generate Hypotheses: Provide clues about etiology for analytic studies.
  4. Resource Allocation: Help planners decide where to send medicine and staff.
  5. Monitor Trends: Detect epidemics or changes in disease patterns over time.
45.
State 5 methods of controlling communicable disease.
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Vaccination/Immunization: Increasing host resistance (e.g., Measles vaccine).
  2. Vector Control: Reducing transmission agents (e.g., IRS, Bed nets).
  3. Isolation and Quarantine: Separating sick or exposed individuals.
  4. Treatment of Cases: Reducing the reservoir of infection (e.g., Antibiotics).
  5. Environmental Sanitation: Safe water and waste disposal (e.g., Cholera control).
46.
Outline 5 purposes of Disease Surveillance.
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Detect Outbreaks: Early warning system for epidemics.
  2. Monitor Trends: Tracking disease progress over time.
  3. Evaluate Control Measures: Checking if interventions (like vaccines) are working.
  4. Estimate Disease Burden: Knowing the magnitude of the problem.
  5. Facilitate Planning: Providing data for health policy and resource distribution.
47.
Identify 5 possible data sources in Epidemiology.
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Census Data: Population counts and demographics.
  2. Vital Registration: Birth and death certificates.
  3. Health Facility Records: Hospital admission and outpatient registers.
  4. Disease Notifications: Mandatory reporting of notifiable diseases.
  5. Surveys: Demographic and Health Surveys (DHS).
48.
State the steps in Disease Surveillance.
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Case Detection and Registration: Identifying cases using standard case definitions.
  2. Case Confirmation: Epidemiological or Laboratory confirmation.
  3. Reporting/Notification: Sending data to the next level (Immediate or Weekly/Monthly).
  4. Data Analysis and Interpretation: Converting data into information (Time, Place, Person).
  5. Feedback: Sending information back to the lower levels.
  6. Action/Response: Public health intervention based on findings.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions

20 Marks
49.

1. Calculate the fraction of cases with the disease among the exposed that is attributable to the exposure using the table below (10 Marks):

Unexposed Exposed
Diseased 9 17
Not Diseased 7 5

2. With the aid of a well labelled diagram, describe the 6 links of disease transmission cycle (10 marks).

Show Answer

Part 1: Calculation

The question asks for the "fraction of cases... attributable to exposure". This is the Attributable Fraction (Exposed).

Step 1: Identify Values

  • Exposed with Disease ($a$) = 17
  • Exposed No Disease ($b$) = 5
  • Total Exposed = 17 + 5 = 22
  • Unexposed with Disease ($c$) = 9
  • Unexposed No Disease ($d$) = 7
  • Total Unexposed = 9 + 7 = 16

Step 2: Calculate Incidence Rates

Incidence in Exposed ($I_e$) = $\frac{a}{a+b} = \frac{17}{22} \approx 0.7727$ (77.3%)

Incidence in Unexposed ($I_u$) = $\frac{c}{c+d} = \frac{9}{16} = 0.5625$ (56.3%)

Step 3: Calculate Attributable Fraction (AF)

Formula: $AF_e = \frac{I_e - I_u}{I_e} \times 100$

Calculation: $\frac{0.7727 - 0.5625}{0.7727} = \frac{0.2102}{0.7727} \approx 0.272$

Answer: 27.2% (or 0.27)

Part 2: Chain of Infection Diagram

The 6 links of the disease transmission cycle are:

(1) INFECTIOUS AGENT (Bacteria, Virus, Parasite) | v (2) RESERVOIR (Human, Animal, Environment) | v (3) PORTAL OF EXIT (Blood, Saliva, Stool) | v (4) MODE OF TRANSMISSION (Contact, Droplet, Vector) | v (5) PORTAL OF ENTRY (Skin, Mouth, Nose) | v (6) SUSCEPTIBLE HOST (Low immunity, Unvaccinated)

Description of Links:

  1. Infectious Agent: The pathogen (germ) that causes disease.
  2. Reservoir: Where the pathogen lives and multiplies (e.g., humans, water).
  3. Portal of Exit: How the pathogen leaves the reservoir (e.g., coughing).
  4. Mode of Transmission: How it travels to the new host (e.g., air, direct contact).
  5. Portal of Entry: How it enters the new host (e.g., broken skin).
  6. Susceptible Host: An individual with low resistance who gets infected.
