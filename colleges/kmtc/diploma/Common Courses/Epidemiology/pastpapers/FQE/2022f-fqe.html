Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions

40 Marks
1.
The bills of mortality quantified patterns of diseases and death in the population also analyzed reports of births and deaths were published by?
a) William Farr
b) John Snow
c) John Graunt
d) Edward Jenner
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) John Graunt

John Graunt (1620–1674) analyzed the "Bills of Mortality" in London, laying the foundation for vital statistics and epidemiology.

2.
___ Epidemiology focuses on the determinants of disease by testing hypothesis
a) Descriptive
b) Correlation
c) Observational
d) Analytic
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Analytic

Analytic epidemiology tests hypotheses about the relationship between health problems and possible risk factors (determinants).

3.
Which of the following is not a type of descriptive studies?
a) Case reports
b) Case series
c) Observational
d) Cross sectional surveys
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Observational

"Observational" is a broad category that includes both descriptive and analytic studies. Case reports, case series, and cross-sectional studies are specific types of descriptive designs.

4.
What type of a bias arises when individuals with a particular exposure of adverse outcome is likely to remember their experiences differently from those who are not similarly affected
a) Selection bias
b) Recall bias
c) Information bias
d) Surrogate bias
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Recall bias

Recall bias occurs when participants (usually cases) recall past exposures with different accuracy than controls, often due to the presence of the disease.

5.
Which is basic measure of disease frequency in human population?
a) Simple count
b) Rate
c) Ration
d) Proportion
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Simple count

A simple count (number of cases) is the most basic measure before calculating rates, ratios, or proportions.

6.
___ quantifies the proportion of individuals in a population who have a disease at a specific instance and provides an estimate of the probability that an individual will be ill at a point in time
a) Attack rate
b) Prevalence
c) Incidence
d) Disease frequency
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Prevalence

Specifically, Point Prevalence measures disease status at a specific point in time.

7.
Non clinical disease can be classified into the following except
a) Latent
b) Sub-clinical
c) Semi-clinical
d) Pre-clinical
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Semi-clinical

"Semi-clinical" is not a standard epidemiological term. Latent, sub-clinical, and pre-clinical are recognized stages of non-clinical disease.

8.
The number of people at risk in whom a certain illness develops compared to the total number of people at risk is called?
a) Cumulative incidence
b) Attack rate
c) Period prevalence
d) Point prevalence
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Cumulative incidence

This is the proportion of a population at risk that develops the outcome of interest over a specified time period.

9.
Which one of the following is a characteristic of a single exposure outbreak?
a) Frequent secondary cases
b) Increasing severity with increasing age
c) Explosive
d) Cases include both people who have been exposed and those who were not exposed
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Explosive

Single exposure (point source) outbreaks typically show a sharp, "explosive" rise in cases in the epidemic curve.

10.
The type of surveillance in which project staff are recruited to carry out is called?
a) Active
b) Passive
c) Sentinel
d) Syndromic
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Active

Active surveillance involves dedicated staff actively seeking out cases (visiting facilities, communities), as opposed to waiting for reports (passive).

11.
Prevalence can be decreased by the following except
a) Death
b) Cure
c) Reduced incidence
d) Increased incidence
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Increased incidence

Increased incidence adds new cases to the "pool," thereby increasing prevalence. Death and cure remove cases (decreasing prevalence).

12.
Which one of the following is not a limitation of hospital data?
a) Hospital admissions are selective
b) Cheap and easy to collect
c) They're not designed for research
d) Population at risk is generally not defined
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Cheap and easy to collect

Being cheap and easy to collect is an advantage (strength), not a limitation. The other options are significant limitations of hospital-based records.

13.
The examining of geographic or spatial differences in incidence can be done in a form of?
a) Epidemiologic studies
b) Time series graph
c) Spot map
d) Histogram
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Spot map

Spot maps specifically display geographic locations of cases to identify spatial clustering.

14.
The relationship between incidence and prevalence is demonstrated through one of the Following
a) Exposure
b) Time
c) No of cases
d) Rate
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Time (Duration)

The relationship is $P \approx I \times D$, where P is prevalence, I is incidence, and D is the Duration (Time) of the disease.

15.
Which one of the following is an advantage of active surveillance?
a) Requires less project staff
b) Is relatively inexpensive to employ
c) More accurate due to reduced reporting burden for health care providers
d) Reporting systems can be developed quickly
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) More accurate due to reduced reporting burden for health care providers

Active surveillance yields more complete and accurate data because dedicated staff collect it, rather than relying on busy healthcare providers.

16.
___ is the number of individuals dying during a specific period of time after disease onset divided by the number of individuals with the specified disease multiplied by 100
a) Disease specific fatality rate
b) Case fatality rate
c) Secondary attack rate
d) Primary attack rate
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Case fatality rate

CFR measures the severity of a disease: $\frac{\text{Deaths from disease}}{\text{Cases of disease}} \times 100$.

17.
For a disease like pancreatic cancer, which is highly fatal and of short duration, one of the following statements is true
a) Incidence rates and mortality rates will be similar
b) Mortality rates will be much higher than incidence rates
c) Incidence rates will be much higher that mortality rates
d) Incidences rates will be unrelated to mortality rate
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Incidence rates and mortality rates will be similar

If a disease kills almost everyone who gets it quickly (short duration), the number of new cases (incidence) will be very close to the number of deaths (mortality) in the same period.

18.
The ability of a test to distinguish between those who have a disease and those who do not have a disease is called
a) Positive predictive value
b) Specificity
c) Reliability
d) Validity
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Validity

Validity (accuracy) is the overall ability of a test to measure what it is supposed to measure, comprised of sensitivity and specificity.

19.
The variation between those reading the test results is called
a) Intra observer variation
b) Intra subject variation
c) Inter observer variation
d) Inter subject variation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Inter observer variation

"Inter" means between. Inter-observer variation is the difference in interpretation between two different people (observers).

20.
Which one of the following does not happen in clinical phase of a disease?
a) Signs and symptoms of disease
b) Pathologic evidence of disease if sought
c) Seeking of medical care
d) Diagnosis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Pathologic evidence of disease if sought

While pathologic evidence exists in the clinical phase, the phrase "evidence if sought" typically characterizes the Sub-clinical or Pre-clinical phase where disease is present but not yet manifesting signs/symptoms.

21.
Which one of the following is not a purpose of randomized clinical trial?
a) Evaluation of new drugs and treatment
b) Testing of new health and mediocre technology
c) Assess new program for screening
d) Planning of health care services and allocation of resources
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Planning of health care services and allocation of resources

Planning and allocation are typically functions of Descriptive Epidemiology (distribution of disease), not Randomized Clinical Trials (which test efficacy).

22.
___ is an inert substance that looks, tastes and smells like the active agent
a) Therapeutic
b) Masker
c) Placebo
d) Blinder
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Placebo

A placebo is used in control groups to account for the psychological effect of receiving treatment.

23.
In clinical trials, phase two trials consist of how many subjects
a) 20-80
b) 1000-3000
c) 100-300
d) Over 3000
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) 100-300

Phase I is small (20-80), Phase II is medium (100-300) to test efficacy, and Phase III is large (1000-3000+) to confirm findings.

24.
Another term for group matching in case control studies
a) Frequency matching
b) Pair matching
c) Matched groups
d) Individual group matching
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Frequency matching

Frequency matching ensures the proportion of controls with a certain characteristic (e.g., % males) matches the proportion in cases, without matching specific individuals.

25.
In ___ studies the controls are a sample of individuals who are at risk of the disease at the time each case of the disease develops
a) Case control
b) Nested case control
c) Prospective control studies
d) Multiple control studies
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Nested case control

This design ("incidence density sampling") involves selecting controls from the cohort at the exact time a case occurs.

26.
Which one of the following is a good estimate of relative risk from case control studies?
a) Kappa statistics
b) Odds ratio
c) Rate adjustment
d) Epilogue
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Odds ratio

The Odds Ratio approximates the Relative Risk when the disease is rare (Rare Disease Assumption).

27.
Studies show that approximately 85% of lung cancer cases are due to cigarette smoking. This measure is an example of
a) Prevalence risk
b) Incidence risk
c) Attributable risk
d) Relative risk
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Attributable risk

Specifically, this is the Attributable Risk Percent (AR%), indicating the proportion of disease cases that can be attributed to the exposure.

28.
If a factor is believed to be the cause of a disease, exposure to the factor must have occurred before the disease occurrence. This is called?
a) Dose response relationship
b) Biologic plausibility
c) Strength of association
d) Temporal relationship
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Temporal relationship

Temporality (Cause precedes Effect) is the only essential criterion for causality.

29.
Which one of the following is not a type of information bias?
a) Selection bias
b) Surveillance bias
c) Reporting bias
d) Recall bias
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Selection bias

Selection bias is a separate category of bias from Information bias (which includes recall, reporting, and surveillance bias).

30.
The following terms are used when dealing with evaluation of health services except?
a) Efficacy
b) Effect modification
c) Effectiveness
d) Efficiency
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Effect modification

Efficacy, Effectiveness, and Efficiency are the "3 Es" of evaluation. Effect modification is an analytical concept (interaction).

31.
The characterization of the potential adverse health effects of human exposure to environmental hazards is referred to as
a) Environmental impact assessment
b) Risk assessment
c) Risk exposure analysis
d) Assessment of exposure
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Risk assessment

Risk assessment involves hazard identification, dose-response assessment, exposure assessment, and risk characterization.

32.
In epidemiology, who advocated for careful monitoring when sharing personal information in the interest of societal benefit
a) Edward Jenner
b) William Farr
c) John Snow
d) Hippocrates
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) William Farr

William Farr (1807–1883) established the system of vital statistics and advocated for the "sanctity of the death certificate" balanced with public health utility.

33.
For an infectious disease to occur, there must be interaction between
a) Agent and vector
b) Vector and host
c) Host and agent
d) Vector and environment
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Host and agent

The primary interaction is between the Host and the Agent (often mediated by Environment). Vectors are not required for all diseases (e.g., flu).

34.
THE "BEINGS" model is a useful paradigm for
a) Evaluating etiological factors in disease
b) Assessing the impact of herd immunity
c) Establishing the importance of social contact
d) Allocating of public health resources
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Evaluating etiological factors in disease

BEINGS stands for Biological, Behavioral, Environmental, Immunological, Nutritional, Genetic, and Social/Spiritual factors.

35.
Which one of the following is mostly used as an overall index of the health status?
a) Age specific death rate
b) Cause of specific death rate
c) Infant mortality rate
d) Crude death rate
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Infant mortality rate

IMR is widely regarded as the most sensitive indicator of a community's overall health status and socioeconomic development.

36.
The outbreak of a disease should be reported to the local or state health department, in what context
a) If the diagnosis is uncertain
b) Under all circumstances
c) If the disease is infectious
d) If the disease is serious
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) If the disease is infectious

While unusual outbreaks should generally be reported, mandatory reporting (Integrated Disease Surveillance and Response) is primarily structured around infectious diseases with epidemic potential.

37.
An official from the state department of public health visits outpatient clinics and emergency rooms to determine the number of cases of post exposure prophylaxis of rabies. The official action is an example of
a) Active surveillance
b) Passive surveillance
c) Syndromic surveillance

The health official is physically visiting facilities to find data ("active"), rather than waiting for reports to be sent.

38.
___ must be associated with both exposure and outcome
a) External validity
b) Internal validity
c) Modifier
d) Confounder
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Confounder

A confounder is a third variable that is associated with the exposure and is a risk factor for the outcome (independent of exposure).

39.
___ alters the nature of the true relationship between exposure and an outcome
a) Synergism
b) Confounder
c) Bias
d) Modifier
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Modifier (Effect Modifier)

Effect modification occurs when the magnitude of the effect of the primary exposure on the outcome differs depending on the level of a third variable.

40.
A case control study may have a particular advantage over what study when the disease in question is
a) Fatal
b) Infectious
c) Rare
d) Virulent
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Rare

Case-control studies are efficient for rare diseases because you start by identifying the cases, whereas a cohort study would require an enormous sample size to find enough cases of a rare disease.

Section 2: Short Answer Questions

40 Marks
1.
State three tools used when characterizing an epidemic. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Spot Map: Shows the geographic distribution of cases (Place).
  2. Epidemic Curve: A histogram showing the number of cases over time (Time).
  3. Attack Rate Table: Analysis of cases by personal characteristics (age, sex, occupation) or exposure (food eaten) to identify risk factors (Person).
2.
Write short notes on vehicle mode of transmission. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

Vehicle transmission involves an inanimate object (the vehicle) transmitting the infectious agent to a susceptible host. Types include:

  • Water-borne: e.g., Cholera.
  • Food-borne: e.g., Salmonellosis.
  • Fomite-borne: Contaminated objects like surgical instruments or bedding.
  • Biological products: Blood, plasma, or tissues.
3.
Epidemiology is usually concerned with health related states/events. Explain. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

While epidemiology historically focused on infectious disease epidemics, modern epidemiology encompasses all health-related states and events:

  • Diseases: Infectious (Malaria) and Chronic (Cancer).
  • Health behaviors: Smoking, exercise.
  • Health outcomes: Injuries, disabilities, life expectancy.
  • Provision of health services: Access and utilization.
4.
Differentiate between Retrospective and Prospective studies. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Retrospective (Backward-looking): The investigator looks back in time. Both exposure and outcome have already occurred at the start of the study (e.g., Case-Control Studies).
  • Prospective (Forward-looking): The investigator follows a group of individuals into the future. The study starts before the outcome has occurred (e.g., Prospective Cohort Studies).
5.
Between the year 2019 and 2021, the average annual incidence rate of lung cancer in Country X was 45.9 per 100,000 and the annual prevalence rate was 23 per 100,000. Calculate the average duration of lung cancer. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

Formula: Prevalence ($P$) $\approx$ Incidence ($I$) $\times$ Duration ($D$)

Therefore, $D = \frac{P}{I}$

Calculation: $D = \frac{23}{45.9} \approx 0.5$ years

Result: The average duration is approximately 6 months (0.5 years).

Note: The question's values (Prevalence lower than Incidence) imply a very short duration/high fatality disease, or potentially a typo in the exam paper values. Standard epidemiological theory holds $P > I$ for chronic diseases. However, based on the calculation $P = I \times D$, the math holds.

6.
The population of the city of Atlantis on March 30th 2012 was 183,000. The number of new active cases of TB occurring between January 1st to June 30th, 2012 was 26. Number of active cases TB cases according to the city register on June 30 2012 was 264.
i. Calculate the incidence rate of active TB cases for the 6 months' period
ii. Calculate the prevalence rate of active TB as of June 2012 (5 marks)
Show Answer

i. Incidence Rate:

Formula: $\frac{\text{New Cases}}{\text{Population at risk}} \times 10^n$

Calculation: $\frac{26}{183,000} \times 100,000 = 14.2$ per 100,000 population (for 6 months).

ii. Prevalence Rate:

Formula: $\frac{\text{Total Existing Cases}}{\text{Total Population}} \times 10^n$

Calculation: $\frac{264}{183,000} \times 100,000 = 144.3$ per 100,000 population.

7.
In an Asian country with a population of 6 million people, 60,000 deaths occurred during the year ending 31st December 2010. These included 30,000 deaths from cholera in 100,000 people who were sick with cholera.
i. What is the cause specific mortality rate of cholera in 2010?
ii. What is the case fatality rate from cholera in 2010? (5 marks)
Show Answer

i. Cause Specific Mortality Rate (CSMR):

Formula: $\frac{\text{Deaths from Cholera}}{\text{Total Population}} \times 100,000$

Calculation: $\frac{30,000}{6,000,000} \times 100,000 = 500$ deaths per 100,000 population.

ii. Case Fatality Rate (CFR):

Formula: $\frac{\text{Deaths from Cholera}}{\text{Cases of Cholera}} \times 100$

Calculation: $\frac{30,000}{100,000} \times 100 = 30\%$

8.
Differentiate between selection and information bias on epidemiological studies (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Selection Bias: Systematic error resulting from the way participants are selected or retained in the study. The study population does not represent the target population (e.g., Healthy Worker Effect, Loss to follow-up).
  • Information Bias: Systematic error in the measurement or collection of data (exposure or outcome) from study participants. The data collected is inaccurate (e.g., Recall bias, Interviewer bias).

Section 3: Long Answer Questions

20 Marks
1.

A hypothetical cohort study of cigarette smokers and non-smokers was carried out. 84 smokers developed coronary heart disease (CHD) while 4913 non-smokers did not develop coronary heart disease. (Note: Assuming Total Smokers = 3000, Total Non-smokers = 5000 based on standard exam patterns).

i. Present the above information in a 2x2 table

ii. Calculate the incidence of CHD among the smokers

iii. Calculate the incidence of CHD among nonsmokers

iv. Calculate Relative risk

v. Interpret your relative risk

Show Answer

Note on Question Data: The provided question text contained OCR ambiguities ("300 smokers... 4913 non-smokers did not..."). For this calculation, we assume standard cohort sizes of 3000 Smokers and 5000 Non-smokers to make the "4913" figure logical (5000 - 4913 = 87 cases).

i. 2x2 Table:

+---------------+-------------+-----------------+-------+ | | Developed | Did Not Develop | Total | | | CHD (Yes) | CHD (No) | | +---------------+-------------+-----------------+-------+ | Smokers | 84 | 2916 | 3000 | | (Exposed) | (a) | (b) | (a+b) | +---------------+-------------+-----------------+-------+ | Non-Smokers | 87 | 4913 | 5000 | | (Unexposed) | (c) | (d) | (c+d) | +---------------+-------------+-----------------+-------+

ii. Incidence in Smokers ($I_e$):

Formula: $\frac{a}{a+b} \times 1000$

Calculation: $\frac{84}{3000} \times 1000 = 28$ per 1000 population (2.8%).

iii. Incidence in Non-Smokers ($I_u$):

Formula: $\frac{c}{c+d} \times 1000$

Calculation: $\frac{87}{5000} \times 1000 = 17.4$ per 1000 population (1.74%).

iv. Relative Risk (RR):

Formula: $\frac{I_e}{I_u}$

Calculation: $\frac{28}{17.4} = 1.61$

v. Interpretation:

Smokers are 1.61 times more likely to develop coronary heart disease compared to non-smokers. This indicates a positive association between smoking and CHD.

2.
Explain 5 methods that are being employed to interrupt the transmission of COVID-19 In Kenya. (10 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Vaccination: Deployment of COVID-19 vaccines (AstraZeneca, Pfizer, etc.) to increase herd immunity and reduce susceptibility.
  2. Social Distancing: Maintaining physical distance (1.5 - 2 meters) to prevent droplet transmission from infected individuals.
  3. Barrier Measures (Masks): Mandatory wearing of face masks in public places to contain respiratory droplets (Source control and protection).
  4. Hygiene Practices: Regular hand washing with soap and water or using alcohol-based sanitizers to kill the virus on hands (breaking vehicle transmission).
  5. Isolation and Quarantine: Isolating confirmed cases and quarantining contacts to prevent further spread to the community.
  6. Movement Restrictions: (Historical context) Curfews and cessation of movement in hotspots to limit population mixing.
