1.
In the definition of epidemiology, the terms distribution and determinants taken together refer to
a) Frequency, pattern and causes of health events
b) Dissemination of information to those who need to know
c) Knowledge, attitudes and practices related to health
d) Public health services and resources
e) Prevention and control of diseases
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Frequency, pattern and causes of health events

Distribution = Frequency + Pattern (Who, When, Where). Determinants = Causes/Risk factors (Why).

2.
Descriptive epidemiology includes all except
a) What
b) Who
c) When
d) Where
e) Why
Show Answer

Correct Answer: e) Why

"Why" refers to causes/determinants, which is the domain of Analytic Epidemiology. Descriptive epidemiology covers Person (Who), Place (Where), and Time (When).

3.

b) Seasonal trend
c) Epidemic curve
d) Endemic curve

Correct Answer: c) Epidemic curve

An epidemic curve is a histogram that visually displays the onset of illness among cases associated with an outbreak over time.

13.
Public health surveillance includes which activities?

b) Data analysis

Correct Answer: a, b, c, d (All of the above)

Surveillance is "Information for Action". It involves Collection, Analysis, Interpretation, and Dissemination. Intervention (e) is the action taken based on surveillance, not the surveillance itself, though they are linked.

20.
An epidemic curve which follows the classic log-normal pattern of sharp rise and more gradual decline is most consistent with which manner of spread?
a) Continuous source
b) Intermittent source
c) Point source
d) Propagated

Correct Answer: c) Point source

A point source outbreak exposes a group at one time, leading to a cluster of cases within one incubation period, creating the steep rise and gradual fall.

21.

22.
Both point prevalence and period prevalence can be represented by the formula $(x / y) \times 10^n$. The primary difference is in:
a) x
b) y
c) 10^n
d) The time period of reference
e) Impossible to tell
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) The time period of reference

Point prevalence is at a single point in time. Period prevalence is over a specified duration (e.g., 1 year).

23.
In a recent survey, investigators found that the prevalence of disease A was higher than the prevalence of disease B. The incidence and seasonal pattern of both diseases are similar. Explanations consistent with this observation include:
a) Patients recover more quickly from disease A than from disease B
b) Patients recover more quickly from disease B than from disease A
c) Patients die quickly from disease A but not from disease B
d) Patients die quickly from disease B but not from disease A
e) None of the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Patients recover more quickly from disease B than from disease A (or d)

Prevalence depends on Incidence and Duration ($P \approx I \times D$). If I is the same, but $P_A > P_B$, then Duration of A > Duration of B. This means B must be shorter (quicker recovery or quicker death). Option B fits.

c) The size of the population at risk at the end of the period

Correct Answer: a) The size of the population at risk at the beginning of the period

For an acute outbreak (attack rate), we use the population at risk at the start of the exposure event to calculate risk (Cumulative Incidence).

26.
In a community of 800 households... 120 persons with condition D in 80 households. A total of 480 persons lived in the 80 affected households. Assuming each household had only one primary case, the secondary attack rate is:
a) 8.5%
b) 10.0%
c) 16.7%
d) 25.0%
e) 30.0%
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) 10.0%

Calculation:

  • Total people in affected HHs = 480
  • Primary cases (1 per 80 HHs) = 80
  • Susceptible contacts = Total - Primary = 480 - 80 = 400
  • Total cases = 120
  • Secondary cases = Total - Primary = 120 - 80 = 40
  • SAR = (Secondary Cases / Susceptible Contacts) * 100 = (40 / 400) * 100 = 10%

Section 2: Short Answer Questions

40 Marks
1.

The following data was obtained from a survey conducted in Nakuru town regarding smoking and lung cancer.

Category Death rates from lung cancer per 1000 persons
I Non-smokers 0.07
II Cigarette smokers 0.96

a) From the data above, compute:

  • i). Relative risk of smokers versus non-smokers (5 marks)
  • ii). Attributable risk of smokers versus non-smokers (3 marks)
  • iii). Explain your answers in (a) above in narrative form (4 marks)

b) What are the uses of relative risk to:

  • i). The clinician
  • ii). The researcher

c) What are the uses of attributable risk:

  • i). To the physician responsible for prevention programs? (5 marks)
  • ii). To the physician responsible for health planning for large groups (5 marks)
Show Answer

a) Computations:

i) Relative Risk (RR): $\frac{\text{Incidence in Smokers}}{\text{Incidence in Non-Smokers}} = \frac{0.96}{0.07} = 13.7$

ii) Attributable Risk (AR): $\text{Incidence in Smokers} - \text{Incidence in Non-Smokers} = 0.96 - 0.07 = 0.89$ per 1000

iii) Explanation:

  • RR: Smokers are 13.7 times more likely to die from lung cancer than non-smokers. This indicates a very strong association.
  • AR: The excess risk of death from lung cancer among smokers that is directly attributable to smoking is 0.89 per 1000 per year. Eliminating smoking would remove this excess risk in the group.

b) Uses of Relative Risk:

  • Researcher: To measure the strength of association between exposure and disease, aiding in causal inference.
  • Clinician: To assess the likelihood of disease in a patient with the risk factor compared to one without.

c) Uses of Attributable Risk:

  • Prevention Programs: To determine the potential impact of a prevention program. It shows how much disease can be prevented if the risk factor is removed.
  • Health Planning: To estimate the public health burden caused by a specific risk factor and allocate resources accordingly to reduce that burden.
2.

Table 1: Prevalence and incidence of selected neurological diseases at Kenyatta National Hospital, Nairobi Kenya.

Diagnosis Prevalence (per 100k) Incidence (per 100k)
i). Epilepsy6917.3
ii). Multiple sclerosis37630.8
iii). Parkinson's disease15720.0
iv). Motor neuron disease555.0
v). CNS Neoplasms71.7

a) From the data in table 1 above... compute the average duration, in years of the five chronic neurological conditions listed (15 marks)

b) In a recent survey conducted in Nairobi city it was found that male street children aged below 20 who are heavy smokers have relative risk for lung cancer of 10. Given that the absolute risk for lung cancer of parking boys of the same age who do not smoke is 0.1 per 1000, compute the probability of a male street boy (smoker) aged below 20 developing lung cancer per year (5 marks)

Show Answer

a) Average Duration Calculation:

Formula: $\text{Prevalence} (P) \approx \text{Incidence} (I) \times \text{Duration} (D)$

Therefore, $D = P / I$

  1. Epilepsy: $69 / 17.3 \approx 4.0$ years
  2. Multiple Sclerosis: $376 / 30.8 \approx 12.2$ years
  3. Parkinson's Disease: $157 / 20.0 = 7.85$ years
  4. Motor Neuron Disease: $55 / 5.0 = 11.0$ years
  5. CNS Neoplasms: $7 / 1.7 \approx 4.1$ years

b) Probability Calculation:

  • Relative Risk (RR) = 10
  • Incidence in Unexposed ($I_u$) = 0.1 per 1000
  • Formula: $RR = I_e / I_u$
  • $10 = I_e / 0.1$
  • $I_e = 10 \times 0.1 = 1.0$ per 1000

Answer: The probability is 1 per 1000 (or 0.001) per year.

Section 3: Long Answer Question

20 Marks
1.

Dr. Mohammed is a County Director of Health Services in Kwale County... He spends all his time at the hospital where he handles essentially curative cases... He has no information on Kinango sub-county... treated many children suffering from diarrhea from there.

a) In your own opinion, why does Dr. Mohammed behave the way he does?

b) In reality, does this happen? Gives reasons for your answer.

c) What information should Dr. Mohammed have on the health of his community?

d) How can he obtain the information in 'C' above?

Show Answer

This question addresses Health Systems Management and Applied Epidemiology.

a) Why he behaves this way:

  • Curative Bias: He is likely a clinician by training and feels more competent treating patients than managing systems.
  • Lack of Public Health Training: He may not understand the value of surveillance and preventive health.
  • Resource Constraints: He might be covering for staff shortages at the hospital.
  • Immediate Gratification: Curative care offers immediate results (patient gets better), whereas public health results are long-term and invisible.

b) Reality Check:

Yes, this is very common.

  • Many medical superintendents/directors are appointed based on clinical seniority, not management skills.
  • The demand for curative services often overwhelms the time available for administrative and field duties.

c) Required Information:

  • Demographics: Population size, distribution, age/sex structure.
  • Morbidity/Mortality: Top 10 causes of disease and death (like the diarrhea outbreak).
  • Service Delivery: Number of functional facilities, staffing levels, drug availability.
  • Environmental Health: Water sources, latrine coverage in places like Kinango.

d) How to obtain information:

  1. DHIS2 Reports: Review monthly data submitted by facilities.
  2. Supportive Supervision: Visit Kinango and other sub-counties to inspect records and talk to staff.
  3. Community Dialogue: Engage Community Health Volunteers (CHVs) and local leaders (Barazas).
  4. Surveillance: Set up active surveillance for the diarrhea cases to pinpoint the source.
