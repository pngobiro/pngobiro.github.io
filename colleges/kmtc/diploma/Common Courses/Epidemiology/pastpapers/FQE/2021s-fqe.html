Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions

40 Marks
1.
In the definition of epidemiology, "distribution" refers to the following except:
A. Who
B. When
C. Where
D. Why
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Why

"Why" refers to determinants (causes). Distribution refers to frequency and pattern (Time, Place, and Person).

2.
In the definition of epidemiology, "determinants" generally includes the following except:
A. Agents
B. Causes
C. Control measures
D. Risk factors
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Control measures

Determinants are the physical, biological, social, cultural, and behavioral factors that influence health (causes/risk factors). Control measures are interventions.

3.
Why should an investigator who has no clinical background nonetheless talk to a patient or two as an early step in the outbreak investigation?
A. To advise the patient about common risk factors
B. To learn more about the clinical manifestations of the disease
C. To verify the clinical findings as part of verifying the diagnosis
D. To verify the laboratory findings
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. To verify the clinical findings as part of verifying the diagnosis

Talking to patients helps develop a clear clinical picture and verify that the reported cases meet the clinical definition.

4.
A case definition during an outbreak investigation should specify the following except:
A. Clinical features
B. Time
C. Person
D. Hypothesized exposure
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Hypothesized exposure

A case definition defines "who, where, when, and what". Including the exposure in the definition biases the study (you can't test if the exposure causes the disease if you only count people who were exposed).

5.
A case report form devised for an outbreak investigation usually includes which of the following types of information except?
A. Unidentified information
B. Demographic information
C. Clinical information
D. Reporter, interviewer, or data abstractor information
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Unidentified information

Forms require identifying information (Name/ID) for tracking, though it may be anonymized later for analysis.

6.
Public health surveillance includes which of the following activities except:
A. Soliciting case reports of persons with symptoms
B. Creating graphs of the number of dog bites by week
C. Writing a report on trends in seat belt use
D. Disseminating educational materials about ways people can reduce their risk
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Disseminating educational materials...

This is a Control/Intervention activity. Surveillance involves collection (A), analysis (B), and interpretation/reporting (C) of data.

7.
A specific case definition is one that:
A. Is likely not to include only (or mostly) true cases
B. Is considered "loose" or "broad"
C. Will include more cases than a sensitive case definition
D. May exclude mild cases
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. May exclude mild cases (or A, context dependent)

A specific definition aims to exclude false positives. By being strict, it may miss mild or atypical cases (unlike a sensitive definition). Note: Option A phrasing is confusing.

8.
Comparing numbers and rates of illness in a community, rates are most preferred for:
A. Conducting surveillance for communicable diseases
B. Deciding how many doses of immune globulin are needed
C. Estimating subgroups at highest risk
D. Telling physicians which strain of influenza is most prevalent
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Estimating subgroups at highest risk

Rates account for population size, allowing comparison of risk between different subgroups (e.g., age or sex).

9.
Possible explanations for a case that occurs substantially later than the other cases in an outbreak include the following except:
A. Similar primary related disease
B. Secondary case
C. Case with unusually long incubation period
D. Time of exposure later than others
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Similar primary related disease

Late cases are typically secondary cases, long incubators, or late exposures. An unrelated disease (or "similar" disease) is a misdiagnosis, not an epidemiological explanation for the timing of a true case.

10.
A spot map is useful for:
A. pinpointing the geographic location of exposures
B. pinpointing the geographic location of residences
C. pinpointing the geographic location of employment sites
D. all of the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. all of the above

Spot maps show the geographic distribution of cases according to any relevant place variable (home, work, or exposure site).

11.
The following are used to display rates except:
A. Spot maps
B. Area maps
C. Chloropleth maps
D. shaded maps
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Spot maps

Spot maps display numbers/counts of cases (dots). Area/Choropleth maps display rates (using shading/colors) because they account for population density in defined areas.

12.
Which of the following may be useful in generating hypotheses in an outbreak setting?
A. Review the literature
B. Look at the descriptive epidemiology
C. Talk with the local health authorities
D. All of the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. All of the above

All these sources provide clues to formulate a hypothesis about the source and spread.

13.
The key feature of an analytic (epidemiologic) study is:
A. Analysis by time, place, and person
B. Calculation of a risk ratio or odds ratio
C. Use of Epidemiological Information to analyze the data
D. Presence of a comparison group
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Presence of a comparison group

The hallmark of analytic epidemiology (vs. descriptive) is testing a hypothesis using a comparison group (e.g., exposed vs unexposed).

14.
Disease control measures can be directed at the:
A. Agent
B. Source
C. Mode of transmission
D. All of the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. All of the above

Control measures can target any link in the chain of infection.

15.

17.
The incidence rate (IR) differs from the incidence proportion (IP) because...
A. IP can be assessed in a closed population, IR not.
B. IR can be assessed in an open population, IP not.
C. IP takes competing mortality into account; IR does not.
D. IR can be assessed more precisely than IP.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. IR can be assessed in an open population, IP not.

Incidence Rate (Density) accounts for person-time and dynamic populations. Incidence Proportion (Cumulative Incidence) assumes a fixed cohort followed for a specified time.

18.
If you want to know the proportion of the disease that could be prevented by eliminating the exposure in the entire study population, you should calculate the:
A. Attributable fraction
B. Attributable risk
C. Population attributable risk
D. Negative predictive value
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Population attributable risk

PAR measures the impact of the exposure on the total population, not just the exposed group.

19.
The precision of an estimate of a relative risk depends on the...
A. External validity of the study.
B. Size of the study.
C. Internal validity of the study.
D. Study design.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Size of the study.

Precision (narrowness of the confidence interval) is primarily determined by sample size.

20.
The problem of confounding can be solved by...
A. Choosing a prospective design.
B. Increasing the precision of the measurements.
C. Stratification during data analysis.
D. This cannot be solved.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Stratification during data analysis.

Confounding can be controlled in analysis via stratification or multivariate analysis, or in design via matching/randomization.

21.
Which one of the following is not a determinant of a disease?
A. Political determinants
B. Social determinants
C. Non behavioral determinants
D. Clinical determinants
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Clinical determinants

"Clinical" usually refers to the manifestation or course of disease, not the underlying cause (determinant) of its occurrence in a population.

27.
The following are key factors in communicable disease, except
A. Host
B. Agent
C. Sex
D. Environment
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Sex

The Epidemiological Triad consists of Host, Agent, and Environment. Sex is a host characteristic, not a primary pillar itself.

A psychological dysfunction is called

37.
Which one of the following is true about relative risk
A. Rate incidence risk
B. Measure of association between disease and exposure stated in form of ratio

Section 2: Short Answer Questions

40 Marks
1.
Define epidemiology and state the uses of epidemiological data (10 marks)
Show Answer

Definition: Epidemiology is the study of the distribution and determinants of health-related states or events in specified populations, and the application of this study to the control of health problems.

Uses:

  1. Community Diagnosis: Assessing community health status.
  2. Completing the Clinical Picture: Identifying natural history of disease.
  3. Searching for Causes: Identifying risk factors (Etiology).
  4. Evaluation: Assessing effectiveness of health services/interventions.
  5. Individual Risk Assessment: Estimating risk for patients.
2.
List and explain types of association in epidemiology (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Spurious Association: False association due to chance or bias.
  2. Indirect (Confounded) Association: Association exists only because both variables are linked to a third factor.
  3. Direct (Causal) Association: Exposure directly causes the outcome.
    • One-to-one (Necessary and Sufficient)
    • Multifactorial (Necessary but not sufficient, etc.)
3.
State five sources of epidemiological data (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Census data
  2. Vital registration (Births/Deaths)
  3. Health facility records
  4. Disease notification/surveillance systems
  5. Population surveys (DHS)
4.
List and explain five levels of disease prevention (5 marks)
Show Answer

(Note: Usually there are 3 main levels, sometimes 4 including Primordial/Quaternary)

  1. Primordial: Preventing the emergence of risk factors (e.g., policy).
  2. Primary: Preventing disease onset (e.g., vaccination).
  3. Secondary: Early detection and treatment (e.g., screening).
  4. Tertiary: Rehabilitation and preventing complications.
  5. Quaternary: Preventing over-medicalization/harm from treatment.
5.
Define reservoir and explain types of reservoirs (10 marks)
Show Answer

Definition: The habitat in which an infectious agent normally lives, grows, and multiplies.

Types:

  1. Human Reservoirs:
    • Cases (Symptomatic)
    • Carriers (Asymptomatic)
  2. Animal Reservoirs (Zoonoses): e.g., Cattle (Anthrax), Dogs (Rabies).
  3. Environmental (Abiotic) Reservoirs: e.g., Soil (Tetanus), Water (Legionella).
6.
State five importance of surveillance (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Detect outbreaks early.
  2. Monitor disease trends.
  3. Evaluate control measures.
  4. Estimate future disease impact.
  5. Allocate public health resources effectively.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions

20 Marks
1.

i) As the integrated disease surveillance and response coordinator in your county, visitors from MOH have visited the county and are interested to know how well the county is prepared in terms of outbreaks. Briefly explain steps/components of disease surveillance and response (12 marks)

ii) The following table shows serum level of calcium and true disease status of 80 participants tested. Complete the table and answer the following questions. (8 marks)

Serum level True disease status Total
Diseased Non diseased
Positive 12 (3) (15)
Negative (8) 57 65
Total (20) 60 80

a) Absolute risk (2 marks)
b) Relative risk (2 marks)
c) Attributable risk (2 marks)
d) Odds ratio (2 marks)

Show Answer

i) IDSR Steps:

[1. Identify] --> [2. Report] --> [3. Analyze] ^ | | v [6. Feedback] <-- [5. Respond] <-- [4. Investigate]
  1. Identify/Detect cases: Use standard case definitions at facility/community level.
  2. Report/Notify: Submit data (immediate or weekly) to the next level.
  3. Analyze and Interpret: Look for trends (Time, Place, Person) and thresholds.
  4. Investigate and Confirm: Verify diagnosis (lab) and determine scope.
  5. Respond: Public health action (treatment, vaccination, education).
  6. Feedback: Information loop back to lower levels.

ii) Calculations:

Completed Values:

  • Negative Total = 65
  • Negative/Non-Dis = 57 â†’ Negative/Dis = 65 - 57 = 8
  • Non-Dis Total = 60 â†’ Total Dis = 80 - 60 = 20
  • Total Positive = 80 - 65 = 15
  • Positive/Dis = 12 â†’ Positive/Non-Dis = 15 - 12 = 3

Values: a=12, b=3, c=8, d=57

a) Absolute Risk (Incidence in Positive group):

$I_e = \frac{a}{a+b} = \frac{12}{15} = 0.8$ (80%)

b) Relative Risk (Risk Ratio):

$I_u (\text{Incidence in Negative}) = \frac{c}{c+d} = \frac{8}{65} = 0.123$

$RR = \frac{I_e}{I_u} = \frac{0.8}{0.123} = 6.5$

c) Attributable Risk (Risk Difference):

$AR = I_e - I_u = 0.8 - 0.123 = 0.677$

d) Odds Ratio:

$OR = \frac{ad}{bc} = \frac{12 \times 57}{3 \times 8} = \frac{684}{24} = 28.5$
