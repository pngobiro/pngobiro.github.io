1.
Define the following terms as used in epidemiology; (8 Marks)

a) Sporadic epidemic

b) Endemic disease

c) Pandemic

d) Health (WHO, 1948)

Show Answer
  • a) Sporadic: A disease that occurs infrequently and irregularly in a population (e.g., Tetanus).
  • b) Endemic: The constant presence and/or usual prevalence of a disease or infectious agent in a population within a geographic area.
  • c) Pandemic: An epidemic that has spread over several countries or continents, usually affecting a large number of people.
  • d) Health (WHO): A state of complete physical, mental, and social well-being and not merely the absence of disease or infirmity.
2.
Differentiate between the following terms as used in epidemiology. (4 Marks)

a) Plausibility and consistency

b) Sensitivity and specificity

Show Answer

a) Plausibility vs Consistency (Causal Criteria):

  • Plausibility: Refers to whether the association makes biological sense based on current scientific knowledge.
  • Consistency: Refers to whether the association has been repeatedly observed by different persons, in different places, circumstances and times.

b) Sensitivity vs Specificity (Screening):

  • Sensitivity: The ability of a test to correctly identify those with the disease (True Positive Rate).
  • Specificity: The ability of a test to correctly identify those without the disease (True Negative Rate).
3.
Using examples, describe four factors that plays part in disease causation. (4 Marks)
Show Answer

These are often categorized via the Epidemiological Triad:

  1. Biological Agents: Bacteria (TB), Viruses (HIV), Parasites (Malaria).
  2. Host Factors: Genetic susceptibility, age, sex, immune status, behavior (smoking).
  3. Environmental Factors: Crowding, sanitation, climate, pollution.
  4. Nutritional Factors: Deficiencies (e.g., Vitamin C/Scurvy) or excesses (Obesity).
4.
Describe any three attributes of a good surveillance system. (8 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Simplicity: The structure and ease of operation. A simple system is easy to understand and implement.
  2. Flexibility: Ability to adapt to changing information needs or operating conditions with little additional cost.
  3. Sensitivity: The proportion of cases of a disease detected by the surveillance system.
  4. Timeliness: Speed between steps in the system (onset to reporting to action).
  5. Acceptability: Willingness of persons conducting surveillance to participate.
5.
In relation to Pasteur’s and Koch’s work, explain four rules for determining whether a specific living organism causes a particular disease. (8 Marks)
Show Answer

Koch's Postulates:

  1. The microorganism must be found in abundance in all organisms suffering from the disease, but should not be found in healthy organisms.
  2. The microorganism must be isolated from a diseased organism and grown in pure culture.
  3. The cultured microorganism should cause disease when introduced into a healthy organism.
  4. The microorganism must be re-isolated from the inoculated, diseased experimental host and identified as being identical to the original specific causative agent.
6.
In relation to disease causation describe the causal pies as per Rothmans, 1976. (8 Marks)
Show Answer

Rothman's Causal Pie model describes how multiple factors work together to cause a disease.

SUFFICIENT CAUSE (The Whole Pie) /-----------\ / A \ / B C \ | X | \ D E / \ F / \-----------/
  1. Sufficient Cause (The Pie): A complete set of minimal conditions/events that inevitably produce the disease. There can be multiple sufficient causes for a single disease.
  2. Component Cause (A Slice): Any one of the set of conditions which are necessary for the completion of a sufficient cause (e.g., A, B, C).
  3. Necessary Cause (Slice X): A component cause that is a member of every sufficient cause. Without it, the disease cannot occur (e.g., Mycobacterium tuberculosis for TB).

30 Marks (Choose Two)
7.
There is an outbreak that has occurred in Gatundu area and the residents are worried of the prevalence that might increase, you have been called upon to advice on factors that will lead to increase of cases. List and discuss the factors. (15 Marks)
Show Answer

Factors that increase the Prevalence of a disease (Prevalence Pool):

  1. Longer Duration of Disease: If the disease is chronic or recovery is slow, cases remain in the "pool" longer.
  2. Prolongation of Life (without cure): Better medical care that prevents death but doesn't cure the disease (e.g., ARVs for HIV) keeps people in the prevalence pool.
  3. Increase in New Cases (Incidence): An active outbreak (like the one in Gatundu) pours new cases into the prevalence pool.
  4. In-migration of Cases: Sick people moving into Gatundu from other areas.
  5. Out-migration of Healthy People: If healthy people leave the area, the relative proportion of sick people increases.
  6. In-migration of Susceptible People: If susceptible people move in and then get sick, incidence (and thus prevalence) increases.
  7. Improved Diagnostic Facilities: Better reporting makes it appear as though prevalence is increasing (detection bias).
8.
Screening methods used in epidemiology plays key role in disease prevention. Justify with relevant examples. (15 Marks)
Show Answer

Screening is the presumptive identification of unrecognized disease or defects by the application of tests, examinations, or other procedures. It plays a key role in Secondary Prevention.

Justification and Roles:

  1. Early Detection and Treatment: Identifies disease at the pre-symptomatic or early stage when treatment is more effective.
    • Example: Pap smears for cervical cancer allow for removal of pre-cancerous lesions, preventing invasive cancer.
  2. Breaking Chain of Transmission: Identifies asymptomatic carriers who might spread infection.
    • Example: Screening blood donors for HIV/Hepatitis prevents transfusion-transmitted infections.
  3. Reducing Morbidity and Mortality: Early intervention reduces severity and death rates.
    • Example: Mammography for breast cancer reduces mortality through early removal of tumors.
  4. Resource Planning: Screening data helps estimate disease burden (prevalence) for allocating healthcare resources.
    • Example: Hypertension screening helps plan for cardiovascular clinics.
  5. Genetic Counseling: Identifying carriers of genetic traits.
    • Example: Sickle cell screening in newborns or pre-marital screening.
9.
In sequential, describe the steps involved in investigation and control of outbreaks. (15 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Prepare for field work: Assemble team, supplies (PPE, labs), administrative approval.
  2. Establish the existence of an outbreak: Compare current cases to baseline (expected) data.
  3. Verify the diagnosis: Review clinical findings and laboratory confirmation.
  4. Construct a working case definition: Define criteria for Time, Place, Person, and Clinical features.
  5. Find cases systematically and record information: Passive reporting and Active surveillance. Create a line listing.
  6. Perform descriptive epidemiology: Analyze data by Time (Epi curve), Place (Maps), and Person (Age/Sex).
  7. Develop hypotheses: Formulate ideas about the source, mode of transmission, and exposure.
  8. Evaluate hypotheses epidemiologically: Conduct analytic studies (Cohort or Case-Control) to calculate Risk Ratios or Odds Ratios.
  9. Reconsider, refine, and re-evaluate hypotheses: If initial analysis is inconclusive, look for new vehicles or modes of transmission.
  10. Compare and reconcile with laboratory and/or environmental studies: Test food samples, water, or surfaces.
  11. Implement control and prevention measures: Break the chain of transmission (Isolation, Treatment, Vaccination).
  12. Initiate or maintain surveillance: Monitor to ensure control measures are working.
  13. Communicate findings: Write a report and disseminate to stakeholders.
