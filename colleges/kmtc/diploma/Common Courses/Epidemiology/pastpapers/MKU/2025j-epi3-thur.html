Section A

1.
Define the following terms; (6 Marks)

a) Outbreak

b) Passive immunity

c) Case definition

Show Answer
  • a) Outbreak: The occurrence of disease cases in excess of what is normally expected in a defined community, geographical area, or season (essentially a localized epidemic).
  • b) Passive Immunity: Immunity acquired by the transfer of antibodies from another individual (e.g., mother to fetus/baby) or animal (e.g., antiserum), providing immediate but short-lived protection.
  • c) Case Definition: A set of standard criteria (clinical signs, time, place, and person) used to decide whether an individual should be classified as having the health condition of interest.
2.
Explain the significance of herd immunity to the population. (4 Marks)
Show Answer

Herd immunity provides indirect protection to individuals who are not immune (e.g., newborns, immunocompromised, or those who cannot be vaccinated).

  • When a high percentage of the population is immune (vaccination/prior infection), it breaks the chain of transmission.
  • The pathogen has fewer susceptible hosts to infect, preventing wide spread (epidemics).
  • It can lead to the elimination or eradication of a disease (e.g., Smallpox, Polio).
3.
Explain four principles of controlling communicable diseases. (8 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Attacking the Source (Reservoir): Treating cases (to reduce infectiousness), isolating sick individuals, or destroying animal reservoirs (e.g., culling in avian flu).
  2. Interrupting the Mode of Transmission: Environmental sanitation, water treatment, vector control (spraying, bed nets), and hygiene practices (hand washing).
  3. Protecting the Susceptible Host: Immunization (active/passive), chemoprophylaxis (preventive drugs), and improving general nutrition/health status.
  4. Surveillance and Notification: Early detection and reporting to initiate rapid response measures.
4.
Differentiate between point source and continuous epidemic. (4 Marks)
Show Answer

Point Source Epidemic:

  • Exposure occurs at a single point in time (or brief period).
  • All cases occur within one incubation period.
  • Curve: Sharp upward slope and gradual downward slope.

Continuous (Common Source) Epidemic:

  • Exposure is prolonged over an extended period.
  • Cases continue to occur beyond one incubation period.
  • Curve: Rises sharply but plateaus (stays high) until the source is removed.

5.
Explain the importance of understanding natural history of a disease. (4 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. It helps in determining the appropriate timing for interventions (Primary, Secondary, Tertiary prevention).
  2. It aids in prognosis (predicting the likely outcome of the illness).
  3. It helps in establishing the baseline to evaluate the effectiveness of new treatments (comparing treated vs natural course).
  4. It assists in identifying the incubation period, which is crucial for quarantine and contact tracing.
6.
Describe three modes of indirect transmission of diseases with examples. (6 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Vehicle-borne transmission: Transmission via inanimate objects or substances like water, food, or blood.
    • Example: Cholera (Water), Salmonellosis (Food).
  2. Vector-borne transmission: Transmission via a living organism (usually an arthropod).
    • Example: Malaria (Mosquito), Plague (Flea).
  3. Airborne transmission: Transmission via droplet nuclei or dust suspended in the air for long periods.
    • Example: Tuberculosis, Measles.
7.
Describe four determinant factors of an infectious agent in occurrence of disease. (8 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Infectivity: The ability of the agent to invade and multiply in the host.
  2. Pathogenicity: The ability of the agent to cause clinical disease (symptoms) in an infected host.
  3. Virulence: The ability of the agent to cause severe disease or death.
  4. Immunogenicity (Antigenicity): The ability of the agent to stimulate specific immune response in the host.
  5. Stability/Viability: Ability to survive in the environment outside the host.

30 Marks (Choose Two)
8.
Systematically, discuss the steps applied in outbreak investigation. (15 Marks)
Show Answer

Outbreak investigation follows a logical sequence to identify the source and control the spread:

Start --> [1. Prepare] --> [2. Confirm Outbreak] | [5. Find Cases] <---- [4. Define Case] <--- [3. Verify Dx] | [6. Describe (Time/Place/Person)] --> [7. Hypothesize] | [10. Report] <-- [9. Control] <-- [8. Test Hypothesis]
  1. Prepare for field work: Mobilize team, resources, and approvals.
  2. Establish existence of outbreak: Compare observed cases to expected baseline.
  3. Verify diagnosis: Clinical and laboratory confirmation.
  4. Define a case: Establish strict criteria (Time, Place, Person, Clinical).
  5. Find cases and record information: Active surveillance and line listing.
  6. Describe epidemiology: Analyze data by Time (Epi curve), Place (Map), Person.
  7. Develop hypotheses: Based on descriptive findings (e.g., "The water is contaminated").
  8. Evaluate hypotheses: Analytic studies (Case-Control or Cohort) to calculate risk.
  9. Refine hypotheses/Additional studies: Environmental testing or lab analysis of food/water.
  10. Implement control measures: Break the chain of transmission.
  11. Communicate findings: Report to authorities and community.
9.
Discuss any five factors that can decrease the prevalence of an outbreak. (15 Marks)
Show Answer

Prevalence is the total number of existing cases. It decreases when cases leave the "pool" of disease.

  1. High Case Fatality Rate (Death): If the disease kills patients quickly, they are removed from the prevalence pool (e.g., Ebola).
  2. High Cure Rate: Rapid recovery due to effective treatment or natural immunity removes cases from the pool.
  3. Decreased Incidence (Prevention): Successful control measures (vaccination, quarantine) stop new cases from occurring. If Incidence drops, Prevalence eventually drops.
  4. Out-migration of Cases: Sick people moving out of the defined geographical area reduces local prevalence.
  5. In-migration of Healthy People: A large influx of healthy people increases the denominator (total population), which mathematically reduces the prevalence rate.
10.
Discuss any five types of notifiable diseases in Kenya. (15 Marks)
Show Answer

Notifiable diseases are those required by law to be reported to government health authorities. In Kenya (IDSR), these include:

  1. Poliomyelitis (AFP): Targeted for eradication. Any case of Acute Flaccid Paralysis in a child <15 years must be reported immediately.
  2. Cholera: An epidemic-prone disease causing severe dehydration. A single case constitutes an outbreak.
  3. Measles: Highly infectious vaccine-preventable disease. Targeted for elimination.
  4. Yellow Fever: Viral hemorrhagic fever transmitted by mosquitoes. A single suspected case requires immediate notification.
  5. Viral Hemorrhagic Fevers (Ebola, Marburg, Rift Valley Fever): Diseases of high public health concern due to high fatality and spread potential.
  6. Neonatal Tetanus: Targeted for elimination.
  7. Anthrax: Zoonotic disease with potential for bioterrorism and severe outcomes.
