Section A

40 Marks
1.
Define the following terms (10 Marks):
Show Answer
  • a) Epidemiology: The study of the distribution and determinants of health-related states or events in specified populations, and the application of this study to the control of health problems.
  • b) Risk Ratio (Relative Risk): A measure of association comparing the incidence of disease in an exposed group to the incidence in an unexposed group ($RR = I_e / I_u$).
  • c) Passive Immunity: Temporary immunity achieved by receiving antibodies produced by another organism (e.g., maternal antibodies to fetus, or immunoglobulin injection).
  • d) Disease: A physiological/psychological dysfunction or deviation from a healthy state, often having a known cause, distinct symptoms, and treatments.
  • e) Health (WHO, 1948): A state of complete physical, mental, and social well-being and not merely the absence of disease or infirmity.
2.
Explain any three levels of disease prevention. (6 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Primary Prevention: Action taken prior to the onset of disease to remove the possibility that the disease will ever occur (e.g., Immunization, Health Education).
  2. Secondary Prevention: Action which halts the progress of a disease at its incipient stage and prevents complications (e.g., Early diagnosis/Screening and prompt treatment).
  3. Tertiary Prevention: All measures available to reduce or limit impairments and disabilities, and to promote the patients' adjustment to irremediable conditions (e.g., Rehabilitation, physical therapy).
3.
Describe the scope of epidemiology. (6 Marks)
Show Answer

The scope includes:

  1. Causation: Identifying the causes/determinants of disease.
  2. Natural History: Understanding the progression of disease from susceptibility to recovery/death.
  3. Health Status of Populations: Measuring disease burden, morbidity, and mortality.
  4. Evaluation of Interventions: assessing effectiveness of health services and programs.
4.
Highlight six importance of learning epidemiology. (6 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. To determine the magnitude and trends of disease in the community.
  2. To identify the etiology or cause of a disease and risk factors.
  3. To determine the extent of disease found in the community.
  4. To study the natural history and prognosis of disease.
  5. To evaluate existing and newly developed preventive and therapeutic measures.
  6. To provide the foundation for developing public health policy.
5.
Explain any three importance of conducting a screening program. (6 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Early Detection: Identifies disease in the pre-symptomatic stage when treatment is most effective (e.g., Pap smear).
  2. Reducing Morbidity/Mortality: Prevents disease progression and death.
  3. Breaking Transmission: Identifies asymptomatic carriers in infectious diseases (e.g., HIV testing) to prevent spread.
6.
Elaborate any three control measures aimed at breaking the chain of infection. (6 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Controlling the Reservoir: Treatment of cases, isolation, quarantine, or destruction of animal reservoirs.
  2. Interrupting Transmission: Hand washing, vector control (spraying/nets), environmental sanitation, sterilization.
  3. Protecting the Susceptible Host: Immunization/Vaccination, chemoprophylaxis, use of PPE, and better nutrition.

Section B

30 Marks (Choose Two)
7.
Discuss any five factors that will increase prevalence of a disease. (15 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Longer Duration of Disease: If a disease lasts a long time (chronic), cases accumulate in the prevalence pool.
  2. Prolongation of Life (without cure): Improved treatment that stops patients from dying but doesn't cure them keeps them in the prevalence pool longer (e.g., Insulin for Diabetes, ARVs for HIV).
  3. Increase in New Cases (Incidence): More people getting sick adds more cases to the prevalence pool.
  4. In-migration of Cases: People with the disease moving into the population.
  5. Out-migration of Healthy People: Healthy people leaving decreases the denominator, increasing the relative rate.
  6. Improved Diagnostic Facilities: Better reporting makes it appear as though prevalence is increasing (detection bias).
8.
Illustrating a well labeled diagram, discuss the causal pies as per Rothman. (15 Marks)
Show Answer

Rothman's Causal Pies (Sufficient-Component Cause Model):

Rothman proposed that a cause of disease is not a single factor, but a constellation of component causes that form a "Sufficient Cause".

[ PIE 1 ] [ PIE 2 ] ___________ ___________ / A \ / A \ / B C \ / D E \ | X | | X | \ D E / \ F G / \_________/ \_________/
  1. Sufficient Cause (The Pie): A set of minimal conditions/events that inevitably produce the disease. There can be multiple sufficient causes (Pie 1, Pie 2) for the same disease.
  2. Component Cause (Slices A-G): An individual factor (e.g., smoking, gene, asbestos) within a sufficient cause. Alone, it may not cause disease.
  3. Necessary Cause (Slice X): A component cause that is present in every sufficient cause. Without it, the disease does not occur (e.g., HIV virus for AIDS, Mycobacterium for TB). In the diagram above, 'X' is necessary.
9.
Discuss any significance of studying communicable disease epidemiology. (15 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Outbreak Control: Essential for investigating and stopping epidemics (e.g., Cholera, Ebola, COVID-19) before they spread widely.
  2. Surveillance: Helps in monitoring the trends of infections to detect emerging threats early.
  3. Resource Allocation: Identifies high-risk groups (children, elderly) and areas to target vaccination and treatment resources.
  4. Prevention Strategy: Understanding modes of transmission (vector, air, water) allows for the design of specific interventions (bed nets, sanitation).
  5. Evaluation of Programs: Assessing the impact of control programs like immunization (Herd Immunity thresholds).
  6. Global Health Security: Preventing cross-border spread of diseases.
