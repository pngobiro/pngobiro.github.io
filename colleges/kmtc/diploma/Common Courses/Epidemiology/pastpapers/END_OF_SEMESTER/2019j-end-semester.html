Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions

20 Marks
1.
Microbes were discovered by?
a. John Snow
b. Pasteur and Koch
c. Hippocrates
d. Edward Jenner
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b. Pasteur and Koch

Louis Pasteur and Robert Koch are the founders of the Germ Theory of disease. John Snow is the father of Epidemiology. Edward Jenner developed the Smallpox vaccine.

2.
In the definition of epidemiology, determinants generally include:
a. Agents
b. Control measures
c. Risk factors
d. Source
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c. Risk factors (and Causes/Agents)

Determinants are the "Why" and "How" - the causes and risk factors that influence the occurrence of disease.

3.
Which of the following is not part of the word epidemiology?
a. Demos
b. Logos
c. Epi
d. Demio
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d. Demio

The word comes from Greek: Epi (upon) + Demos (people) + Logos (study). "Demio" is incorrect.

4.
The search for causes and other factors that influence the occurrence of health-related events is?
a. Pattern
b. Distribution
c. Determinants
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c. Determinants

Determinants = Causes. Distribution = Frequency and Pattern.

5.
When the occurrence of a disease is persistently higher than normal it's called
a. Holo-endemic
b. Hyperendemic
c. Pandemic
d. Endemic
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b. Hyperendemic

Endemic is constant presence. Hyperendemic is persistently high levels. Pandemic is worldwide.

6.
The measure of the magnitude of disease or other health related events is called
a. Counts
b. Percentage
c. Frequency
d. Proportion
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c. Frequency

Frequency refers to the number of health events (magnitude) and its relationship to the population size.

7.
Which of the following is not true about proportion?
a. Measures part of the population affected by disease compared to the total population
b. Can be expressed per 100
c. The time period may not be specified
d. The denominator is part of the numerator
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d. The denominator is part of the numerator

This statement is false (and physically impossible). In a proportion, the numerator is part of the denominator ($A / (A+B)$). Option C refers to the fact that simple proportions (like prevalence) measure a state at a point, unlike rates which measure events over time intervals.

8.
The natural history of the disease begins with the following except
a. Exposure to risk factors of the disease
b. Accumulation of factors capable of causing a disease
c. Susceptibility of the persons
d. Presence of signs and symptoms
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d. Presence of signs and symptoms

Natural history begins with the Stage of Susceptibility (a, b, c). Signs and symptoms appear later, in the Stage of Clinical Disease.

9.
The proportion of exposed persons who become infected is referred to as
a. Virulence
b. Infectivity
c. Pathogenicity
d. Holo endemic
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b. Infectivity

Infectivity is the ability to invade and multiply (causing infection). Pathogenicity is the ability to cause disease (symptoms). Virulence is the ability to cause severe disease/death.

10.
In order for a disease agent to survive, it must be able to do the following except
a. Infect a new host
b. Reach a new host
c. Originate from a reservoir
d. Multiply
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c. Originate from a reservoir

To survive as a species, the agent must multiply, exit (reach new host), and enter (infect new host). "Originating" from a reservoir is part of the cycle, but technically the reservoir is where it does these things, not an action the agent performs to survive in the same active sense as multiplication/transmission.

11.
Which of the following is not a type of carrier?
a. Asymptomatic carriers
b. Symptomatic carriers
c. Chronic carriers
d. Convalescent carriers
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b. Symptomatic carriers

By definition, a carrier sheds the pathogen without apparent clinical symptoms. A symptomatic person is a "Case", not a "Carrier".

12.
Which of the following disease does not have animals as the reservoir?
a. Brucellosis
b. Legionnaires bacillus
c. Plague
d. Trichinosis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b. Legionnaires bacillus

Legionella pneumophila has an environmental reservoir (water sources, cooling towers), not animals. Brucellosis (Livestock), Plague (Rodents), and Trichinosis (Pigs) have animal reservoirs.

13.
The type of transmission by direct spray over a few feet before they fall on the ground is called?
a. Direct contact
b. Droplet spread
c. Mechanical
d. Droplet nuclei
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b. Droplet spread

Large droplets travel short distances (< 1 meter) and fall (Direct transmission). Droplet nuclei (Airborne) remain suspended (Indirect).

14.
Which of the following is not a vector?
a. Ants
b. Mosquitoes
c. Fleas
d. Ticks
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a. Ants

Mosquitoes, Fleas, and Ticks are major disease vectors. Ants are generally not significant vectors of human disease.

15.
The type of immunity whereby antibodies develop in response to infection, vaccine or toxoid is called?
a. Active immunity
b. Passive immunity
c. Herd immunity
d. Natural immunity
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a. Active immunity

The host's immune system actively produces antibodies. Passive immunity involves receiving pre-formed antibodies.

16.
Which of the following is not a reservoir for yellow fever?
a. Aedes aegypti
b. Monkeys
c. Soil
d. Vertebrates
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c. Soil

Yellow Fever reservoirs are Monkeys (Jungle cycle) and Humans (Urban cycle), transmitted by mosquitoes ($Aedes$). It is not soil-borne.

17.
Which of the following is not true about relative risk?
a. Can also be referred to as risk ratio
b. Can compare the risk of disease in two groups
c. The group of primary interest is the unexposed group
d. The two groups are typically differentiated by demographic factors
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d. The two groups are typically differentiated by demographic factors (or c)

In cohort studies, groups are differentiated by Exposure Status, not just demographics. Also, the group of interest is usually the Exposed group (to see if risk increases), not the unexposed.

18.
Prevalence can be increased by the following except
a. Addition of new cases
b. Improved treatment
c. Recovery
d. Death
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d. Death (and c. Recovery)

Death and Recovery remove cases from the prevalence pool, thus decreasing it. New cases and improved treatment (that prolongs life without cure) increase it.

19.
Which of the following is not true about attributable risk?
a. Is a measure of risk in cohort studies
b. The rate of disease among exposed persons that can be directly attributable to specific exposure
c. Is the risk difference between exposed and non-exposed individuals
d. It a measure of association
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d. It a measure of association

Attributable Risk is a Measure of Impact/Effect. Relative Risk is the Measure of Association (Strength).

20.
The ratio of the number of ways an event can occur to the number of events cannot occur is called?
a. Relative risk
b. Odds of an event
c. Odds ratio
d. Relative odds
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b. Odds of an event

Odds = Probability (P) / (1-P). It compares occurrences to non-occurrences.

Section 2: Short Answer Questions

30 Marks
1.
Name five components in the definition of epidemiology (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Study: Scientific, systematic, data-driven.
  2. Distribution: Frequency and pattern (Time, Place, Person).
  3. Determinants: Causes and risk factors.
  4. Health-related states or events: Diseases, conditions, behaviors.
  5. Specified Populations: Focus on groups, not just individuals.
  6. Application: To control health problems (Prevention).
2.
Three terms are used to describe infectious diseases according to the various outcomes that may occur after exposure to its causative agent. State them (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Infectivity: The ability of the agent to enter, survive, and multiply in the host.
  2. Pathogenicity: The ability of the agent to cause clinical disease (symptoms) in an infected host.
  3. Virulence: The ability of the agent to cause severe disease or death in a diseased host.
3.
Briefly explain how epidemiology is used in the evaluation of efficacy and effectiveness of therapeutic and preventive interventions (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  • Efficacy (Ideal): Epidemiology uses Randomized Clinical Trials (RCTs) to test if an intervention (e.g., vaccine) works under optimal, controlled conditions by comparing outcomes in intervention vs control groups.
  • Effectiveness (Real-world): Epidemiology uses observational studies (Cohort, Case-Control) or field trials to assess if the intervention works in the general population under routine conditions, considering factors like compliance and access.
4.
Epidemiologists and clinicians approach the same patient differently, explain (5 Marks)
Show Answer
Clinician Epidemiologist
Focuses on the individual patient. Focuses on the community/population.
Aims to diagnose and treat the illness. Aims to prevent and control spread.
Uses lab tests/history to determine cause for this person. Uses biostatistics/surveillance to identify risk factors for the group.
5.
State the two major classifications of causes of disease (5 Marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Sufficient Cause: A set of conditions without which the disease would not have occurred (the whole pie).
  2. Necessary Cause: A factor that must be present for the disease to occur (e.g., HIV virus for AIDS).

Alternative Classification:

  1. Direct Causes: Proximal factors (e.g., pathogen, trauma).
  2. Indirect Causes: Distal factors (e.g., poverty, sanitation, genetics).
6.
Differentiate between holo-endemic and hyperendemic (5 marks)
Show Answer
  • Hyperendemic: Disease is constantly present at a high incidence and/or prevalence rate and affects all age groups equally.
  • Holo-endemic: A high level of infection begins early in life and affects most of the child population, leading to a state of equilibrium such that the adult population shows evidence of the disease less frequently than children (e.g., Malaria in high transmission zones).

Section 3: Long Answer Question

20 Marks
1.

a. Explain the design of case control study using smoking and lung cancer as examples (10 Marks)

b. What are interventional studies (5 Marks)

c. Name three advantages of interventional studies (3 Marks)

d. List two disadvantages of interventional studies (2 marks)

Show Answer

a) Case Control Design (Smoking & Lung Cancer):

  1. Selection of Cases: Identify a group of individuals who have Lung Cancer (Cases).
  2. Selection of Controls: Identify a comparable group of individuals who do not have Lung Cancer (Controls), matched by age, sex, etc.
  3. Assess Exposure (Retrospective): Look back in time for both groups to determine their history of Smoking (Exposure).
  4. Analysis: Compare the odds of having smoked among the cancer patients vs. the non-cancer patients.
  5. Measure: Calculate the Odds Ratio. If smokers are disproportionately higher in the Case group, smoking is associated with lung cancer.

b) Interventional Studies (Experimental):

These are studies where the investigator intentionally alters one or more factors (e.g., gives a drug, vaccine, or health education) to a group of people and controls the exposure to study its effect on an outcome. They involve randomization and control groups.

c) Advantages:

  1. Provide strongest evidence for causality.
  2. Minimizes bias and confounding through randomization.
  3. Investigator has control over exposure levels.

d) Disadvantages:

  1. Expensive and time-consuming.
  2. Ethical constraints (cannot expose people to harmful agents).
  3. May not reflect real-world conditions (artificial environment).
