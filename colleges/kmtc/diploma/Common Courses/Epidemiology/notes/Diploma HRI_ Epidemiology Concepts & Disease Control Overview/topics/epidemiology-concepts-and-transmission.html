Epidemiology I (30 hours)

Unit 1. Concepts to epidemiology

1.1. Definition of epidemiology

  • Branch of medicine which deals with incidence, distribution, and control of diseases and other factors relating to health
  • Analysis of the distribution, patterns and determinants of health and disease conditions in defined populations.
  • Study of distribution and determinants of health-related states or events, and the application of this study to the control of diseases and other health problems
  • Study of how often diseases occur in different groups of people and why

1.2. Origin / history of epidemiology

  • Epidemiologic thinking has been traced from Hippocrates
  • In 400 B.C, Hippocrates explained that environmental and host factors such as behaviors might influence the development of disease.
  • In 1662, John Graunt published analysis of mortality data that quantified patterns of birth, death, and disease occurrence by sex, region and seasonal variations
  • In 1800, William Farr collected and analyzed Britain's mortality statistics and developed basic practices that are being used today in vital statistics and disease classification.
  • In 1854, John Snow conducted a study when an epidemic of cholera erupted in the Golden Square of London.
  • In the $18^{th}$ and $19^{th}$ Century, epidemiological methods began to be applied in the investigation of acute infectious diseases.
  • In the 1930s and 1940s, epidemiologists extended their methods to noninfectious diseases
  • During the 1960s and early 1970s health workers applied epidemiologic methods to eradicate naturally occurring smallpox worldwide
  • In the 1980s, epidemiology was extended to the studies of injuries and violence.
  • In the 1990s, the related fields of molecular and genetic epidemiology took root.

1.3. Scope of epidemiology

  • Causation - Describes how diseases are caused by interaction between genetic and environmental factors
  • Natural history of disease - Explains the course and outcome of a disease in individual or group
  • Health status of the population - Describes health situation of the population
  • Evaluation of interventions - Evaluates effectiveness and efficiency of intervention

1.4. Purposes of epidemiology

The following are purposes /uses of epidemiology:

  • Count health-related problems
  • Clarification of natural history of disease
  • Describe the distribution of health-related events in the population
  • Describe disease patterns
  • Describes health status of population
  • To determine the progression and extent of disease
  • Identify risk factors for developing diseases
  • Identify causes (etiology) or determinants of disease
  • Identify control and/or preventive measures
  • Establish priorities for allocating resources
  • Select interventions for prevention and control
  • Evaluate effectiveness of programs and interventions
  • Conduct research (descriptive or experimental i.e. drug trials / vaccine trials)

1.5. Principles of epidemiology

The following are principles of epidemiology:

  • Show distribution with the frequency and pattern of health events in a population.
  • Search for causes and determinants of disease occurrence
  • Concerned with health of people in specified populations
  • Provides data that can be applied for public health action
  • Use of scientific methods to describe and analyze situations
  • Define and count cases to determine their distribution, calculate rates and compare these rates

1.6. Terms used in epidemiology

  • Agents - are biological, physical, or chemical factors that contribute to the occurrence of a disease e.g. viruses, bacteria, fungi, poisons or allergens, radiation, noise, or heat
  • Host - A person or living organism that can be infected by an infectious agent under natural conditions
  • Host factor - An intrinsic factor (age, race, sex, behaviors, etc.) which influences an individual's exposure, susceptibility, or response to a causative agent
  • Environment - the habitat in which the biological agent and the host exist, survive or originate
  • A vector is an organism that helps transmit infection from one host to another.
  • Vehicle - An intermediary in the indirect transmission of an agent that carries the agent from a reservoir to a susceptible host
  • Case - an individual with the particular disease
  • Incidence - number of new cases in a population at risk of developing the disease over a given period of time.
  • Incidence rate - is the number of new cases divided by the total disease free person-time of observation seen in the population at risk
  • Prevalence - quantifies the number of existing cases in a population at a point in time or during a period of time
  • Observations - is one form of data collection through direct seeing or hearing how people act.
  • Endemic - means the constant presence/occurrence of a disease in a population and the disease is able to maintain itself in a population or region without cases entering the population or region from outside
  • Epidemic refers to outbreak (sudden increase in the number) of cases of a disease above what is normally expected in that population in that area.
  • Pandemic - An epidemic occurring over a very wide area (several countries or continents) and usually affecting a large population i.e. Covid-19.
  • Hyperendemic refers to persistent, high levels of disease occurrence.
  • Holoendemic - A disease is holoendemic when essentially every individual in a population is infected
  • Carrier - A person or animal without apparent disease who harbors a specific infectious agent and is capable of transmitting the agent to others.
  • Case control study - A type of observational analytic study where enrollment into the study is based on presence ("case") or absence ("control") of disease.
  • Case definition - A set of standard criteria for deciding whether a person has a particular disease or health-related condition.
  • Case fatality rate - The proportion of persons with a particular condition (cases) who die from that condition
  • Cohort - A well-defined group of people who have had a common exposure, and are followed up for the incidence of new diseases or events.
  • Cohort study -starts by defining groups by the study factor (e.g. exposure present or absent) and then follows-up these exposure groups to detect the outcome (e.g. disease present or absent).
  • Determinant - Any factor that brings change in a health condition
  • Pathogenicity - The proportion of persons infected, after exposure to a causative agent, who then develop clinical disease
  • Reservoir - The habitat in which an infectious agent normally lives, grows and multiplies. I.e. human reservoirs, animals' reservoirs, and environmental reservoirs.
  • Risk - The probability that an event will occur, e.g. that an individual will become ill or die within a stated period of time or age.
  • Risk factor - Any personal behavior or lifestyle, environmental exposure, inborn or inherited characteristic that is associated with an increased occurrence of disease or other healthrelated event or condition.
  • Trend - A long-term movement or change in frequency, usually upwards or downwards.
  • Virulence - The proportion of persons with clinical disease, who after becoming infected, become severely ill or die.
  • Zoonosis - An infectious disease that is transmissible under normal conditions from animals to humans.
  • Odds ratio - A measure of association which quantifies the relationship between an exposure and health outcome from a comparative study
  • Attributable risk - difference between the incidence of the outcome in the exposed group (or intervention) and the incidence of the outcome in the unexposed group (or control).
  • The relative risk (RR) - is the ratio of the probability of an outcome in an exposed group to the probability of an outcome in an unexposed group
  • Absolute risks - It is the ratio of people who have a medical event compared to all of the people who could have an event.
  • Causality - identifying the causes of disease
  • Chronic disease - a long-lasting, persistent or recurrent disease
  • Confounding bias - occur if the effect of the study factor on the outcome is mixed in the data with the effect of another variable
  • Placebo - is a substance or procedure with no known effect used as mock treatments for the control group in experimental studies.
  • Positive predictive value - probability that a person with a positive test result will actually have the disease.
  • Surveillance - is the ongoing systematic collection, analysis and interpretation of data for planning, implementation, and evaluation.
  • Target population - is the population about which one wants to draw conclusions.
  • Analytical epidemiology - concerned with the search for health-related causes and effects by quantifying the association between exposures and outcomes
  • Applied epidemiology - The application or practice of epidemiology to address public health issues.
  • Association - Statistical relationship between two or more events, characteristics, or variables.
  • Active immunity - Resistance developed in response to stimulus by an antigen
  • Sensitivity - The ability of a system to truly detect presence of epidemics
  • Specificity - Ability of a system to truly detect absence of a disease
  • Sporadic - A disease that occurs infrequently and irregularly
  • Validity - The degree to which a measurement actually measures or detects what it is supposed to measure.

Unit 2. Epidemiology and disease transmission

2.1. Causation of disease

A number of models of disease causation have been proposed. Among the simplest of these is the epidemiologic triad or triangle

The triad consists of an external agent, a susceptible host, and an environment that brings the host and agent together.

In this model, disease results from the interaction between the agent and the susceptible host in an environment that supports transmission of the agent from a source to that host

Agent

  • Is referred to an infectious microorganism or pathogen: a virus, bacterium, parasite, or other microbe.
  • Agent factors - These factors must be present for a disease to occur
  • The agent must be present for disease to occur; however, presence of that agent alone is not always sufficient to cause disease.
  • A variety of factors influence whether exposure to an organism will result in disease, including the organism's pathogenicity (ability to cause disease) and dose.
  • Over time, the concept of agent has been broadened to include chemical and physical causes of disease or injury.

Host

  • Refers to the human who can get the disease
  • Is the living being that the bacteria, virus, protozoan, or other disease-causing microorganism normally resides in
  • A variety of factors intrinsic to the host, sometimes called risk factors, can influence an individual's exposure, susceptibility, or response to a causative agent.
  • Host factors - Are factors that influence individual's exposure, susceptibility, or response to a causative agent. Age, race, sex, socio-economic status and behaviors are some of the many host factors which affect a person's likelihood of exposure

Environment

Refers to extrinsic factors that affect the agent and the opportunity for exposure

Environmental factors include:

  • Physical factors such as geology and climate
  • Biologic factors such as insects that transmit the agent
  • Socioeconomic factors such as crowding, sanitation, and the availability of health services.

Note

Agents, host and environmental factors interrelate in a variety of complex ways to produce a disease in humans.

2.2. Disease and risk factors

Diseases are categorized in to communicable and non-communicable:

Communicable diseases

  • These are infectious diseases caused by microorganisms such as bacteria, viruses, parasites and fungi that can be spread, directly or indirectly, from one person to another.
  • A communicable disease is one that is spread from one person to another through a variety of ways that include:
    • Contact with blood and body fluids;
    • breathing in an airborne virus; or
    • by being bitten by an insect
  • Common communicable diseases are:
    • Influenza - Influenza (also known as the flu) is a contagious respiratory illness caused by flu viruses. It can cause mild to severe illness, and at times can lead to death. The flu usually comes on suddenly.
    • Measles - Measles is a highly contagious virus that lives in the nose and throat mucus of an infected person. The symptoms of measles generally appear about seven to 14 days after a person is infected.
    • Meningitis - Meningitis is the inflammation of the protective membranes covering the brain and spinal cord. Meningitis can be caused by various pathogens with the majority being either a bacterial or viral infection of the fluid surrounding these areas.
    • Ebola - Ebola is a rare and deadly hemorrhagic fever caused by an infection from one of the Ebola virus strains. Ebola can cause disease in humans and non-human primates
    • Mumps - Mumps is a contagious disease caused by a virus. An outbreak is considered when three confirmed cases occur at any time of the year
    • Tuberculosis - This is an airborne disease caused by the bacteria, which are spread person-to-person through the air. This bacterium mainly affects the lungs, but may also affect other organs
    • Covid-19 - this is a communicable disease caused by a new strain of coronavirus that causes illness. Originally identified in China in 2019 and became pandemic in 2020
  • Risk factors of communicable diseases are:
    • Crowded conditions
    • Lack of vaccination (poor immunity)
    • Poor nutrition and diet
    • Forced displacement
    • Poor quality shelter
    • Poor water, sanitation and hygiene conditions
    • Lack of health care facilities
    • Lack of adequate surveillance

Non-communicable diseases

  • Referred to as a "lifestyle" disease, because the majority of these diseases are preventable illnesses
  • These include diabetes, cancer, osteoporosis, stroke, and heart disease.
  • Almost four-fifths of all deaths from non-communicable diseases occur in developing countries
  • Risk factors of non-communicable are:
    • Lack of physical activities
    • Poor diet (high consumption of sugar, salt, saturated fats, and trans fatty acids)
    • Alcohol use
    • Metabolic/physiological changes (raised blood pressure, overweight/obesity, raised blood glucose and raised cholesterol)
    • Heart disease, stroke, cancer, diabetes and chronic lung disease

2.3. Natural history of disease

  • This is the course a disease takes in individual people from its pathological onset until its eventual resolution through complete recovery or death
  • Progress of a disease in an individual over time in the absence of intervention
  • The process begins with exposure to factors capable of causing the disease
  • Without medication intervention, the process ends with recovery, disability or death

2.4. Chain of infection

Is also called a transmission cycle. It is made up of six different links: pathogen (infectious agent), reservoir, portal of exit, means of transmission, portal of entry, and the new host. Each link has a unique role in the chain, and each can be interrupted, or broken, through various means.

  • Transmission cycle - occurs when the agent leaves its reservoir or host through a portal of exit, and is conveyed by some mode of transmission, and enters through an appropriate portal of entry to infect a susceptible host.
  • Reservoir - is the habitat in which an infectious agent normally lives, grows and multiplies. This includes humans, animals and the environment.
  • Two types of human reservoir are:
    • Persons with symptomatic illness - Symptomatic persons are usually less likely to transmit infection widely because their symptoms increase their likelihood of being diagnosed and treated.
    • Carriers - Asymptomatic carrier, a person or organism infected with an infectious disease agent, but displays no symptoms.
  • Environmental reservoir - Plants, soil and water are also reservoir for some infectious disease. Many fungal agents such as those causing histoplasmosis live and multiply in the soil
  • Animal reservoir - Infectious diseases that are transmittable under normal conditions from animal to humans are called zoonoses. Such disease includes brucellosis (cows and pigs), anthrax (sheep), plague (rodents), rabies (bats, dogs)
  • Portal of exit and entry - Is the path by which an agent leaves the source host or enters. Tubercle bacilli and influenza viruses exit the respiratory tract; Schistosome through urine; cholera vibrio through feces etc.
  • Modes of transmission - After an agent exits its natural reservoir, it may be transmitted to a susceptible host in numerous ways. These modes of transmission are classified as:
    • Direct (Direct contact and direct spread) - This is immediate transfer of the agent from a reservoir to a susceptible host by direct contact or droplet spread. It occurs through kissing, skin-to-skin contact. Also refer to contact with soil or vegetation harboring infectious organisms.
    • Indirect (Airborne, vehicle borne, Vector borne) - This can be mechanical or biologic. An agent is carried from a reservoir (a susceptible host) by suspended air particles or by animate (vector).
  • Most vectors are arthropods such as mosquitoes, fleas and ticks. This is in contrast of instances in which an agent undergoes part of its life cycle inside a vector before being transmitted to a new host.
  • When the agent undergoes changes within the vector, the vector is serving as both intermediate host and a mode of transmission. This type of direct transmission is called a biologic transmission
  • Vehicles that may directly transmit an agent include food, water, blood, handkerchiefs, bedding etc.

2.5. Determinants of disease

Any of a group of variables or factors, that directly or indirectly influence the frequency or distribution of a disease

These can be:

  • Environmental factors - the greatest burdens relate to unsafe drinking water, poor sanitation & hygiene, and pollution of the indoor and outdoor air
  • Biological factors - This includes agents, such as Bacteria, Fungi, Parasite and Virus
  • Genetics factors - inheritance plays a part in determining lifespan, healthiness and the likelihood of developing certain illnesses.
  • Physical factors - safe water and clean air, healthy workplaces, safe houses, communities and roads all contribute to good health. Employment and working conditions - people in employment are healthier
  • Individual factors - Diet, Physical activity, life style such taking alcohol, cigarette, and other drug use, and Hand washing
  • Social economic factors - higher income and social status are linked to better health. The greater the gap between the richest and poorest people, the greater the differences in health.

2.6. Occurrences of diseases

A disease and other health events do not occur randomly in a population, but are more likely to occur in some members of the population than others. This is because of risk factors that may not be distributed randomly in the population

The amount of a particular disease that is usually present in a community is referred to as the baseline or endemic level of the disease. This level is not necessarily the desired level, but rather the observed level

Explanation of the Iceberg concept of diseases

  • From bottom - In a population, majority are exposed to diseases but don't get infected
  • Next up - the number that get infected with disease do not become sick
  • Next up - the number that become sick get healed without being noticed
  • Next up - the number of noticed sick people become severely sick and go to hospital
  • Next up - The number of severely sick people who goes to hospital eventually die

2.7. Methods of controlling communicable diseases

The following are methods of controlling communicable diseases:

  • Diagnosis and treatment - This is identifying and treating cases as early as possible. Its purposes are:
    • Reducing the severity of the disease for the patient,
    • Avoiding progression to complications, disability and death
    • Reducing the risk of transmission to others.
  • Screening - detection of an infection in an individual who does not show any signs or symptoms of the disease. It is carried out using specific tests called screening tests. It helps in:
    • Detecting an infection early
    • Organizing appropriate treatment so as to reduce complications
    • Preventing transmission to others
  • Isolation - separate patients from others to prevent transmission to healthy people. The isolation period lasts until the risk of transmission from the infected person has reduced or stopped.
  • Quarantine - Restriction on movement of people who may have been exposed to a disease to prevent spread of disease or pest
  • Reporting - Cases of communicable diseases should be reported to a nearby health center or health head office periodically, using the national surveillance guidelines
  • Terminating modes of transmission - The measures that can be applied to interrupt transmission of infectious agents in water, food, other vehicles and by vectors i.e. sterilization, disinfection, boiling water etc.
  • Removing susceptible host - help to protect the susceptible host either from becoming infected, or from developing the stage of infectious disease if they are exposed to the infectious agents i.e.
    • Vaccination - administration of vaccines to increase the resistance of the susceptible host against specific vaccine-preventable infections. For example, measles vaccination helps to protect the child from measles infection, and BCG vaccination gives some protection from tuberculosis
    • Chemoprophylaxis - drugs given to exposed and susceptible hosts to prevent them from developing an infection. For example, individuals from non-malarial areas who are going to a malaria endemic area can take a prophylactic drug to prevent them from developing the disease if they become infected with malaria parasites from a mosquito bite.
    • Maintaining a healthy lifestyle - Proper nutrition and exercise improves a person's health status, supports the effective functioning of their immune system, and increases resistance to infection
  • Limiting exposure to reservoirs of infection - Measures taken to decrease contact with reservoirs of infection include:
    • Condom use to prevent transmission of HIV and other STI
    • Use of insecticide treated nets (ITNs) over the bed at night, insect repellants and wearing protective clothing to prevent diseases transmitted by insect vectors.
    • Wearing surgical or very clean gloves and clean protective clothing while examining patients, particularly if they have wounds, or the examination involves the genital area.
    • Keeping personal hygiene i.e. hand washing

2.8. Level of disease prevention

There are 3 levels of disease prevention: Primary, Secondary and Tertiary

  • Primary level - It aims to prevent disease or injury before it ever occurs
    • This is done by preventing exposures to hazards that cause disease or injury, altering unhealthy or unsafe behaviors that can lead to disease or injury, and increasing resistance to disease or injury when exposure occur
    • Examples include:
      • legislation and enforcement to ban use of asbestos
      • Education about healthy and safe habits (e.g. eating well, exercising regularly, not smoking)
      • Immunization against infectious diseases
  • Secondary level - This level aims to reduce the impact of a disease or injury that has already occurred.
    • This is done by:
      • Detecting and treating disease or injury as soon as possible to halt or slow its progress
      • Encouraging personal strategies to prevent re-injury or recurrence
      • Implementing programs to return people to their original health and function
    • Examples include:
      • Regular exams and screening tests to detect disease in its earliest stages (e.g. mammograms to detect breast cancer)
      • Daily, low-dose aspirins and/or diet and exercise programs to prevent further heart attacks or strokes
  • Tertiary level - This level aims to soften impact of an ongoing illness/injury that has lasting effects.
    • This is done by helping people manage long-term health problems and injuries (e.g. chronic diseases, permanent impairments) in order to improve as much as possible their ability to function, their quality of life and their life expectancy.
    • Examples include:
      • Cardiac or stroke rehabilitation programs, chronic disease management programs (e.g. for diabetes, arthritis, depression, etc.)
      • Support groups that allow members to share strategies for living well
      • Vocational rehabilitation programs to retrain workers for new jobs when they have recovered as much as possible.
