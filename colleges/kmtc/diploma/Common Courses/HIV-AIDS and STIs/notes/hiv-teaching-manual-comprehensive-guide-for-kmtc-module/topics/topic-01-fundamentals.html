üìñ Definition of Terms

H

HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus)

HIV attacks cells in the immune system (the body‚Äôs natural defense against illness). The virus destroys a type of white blood cells in the immune system called T-helper cells also referred to as CD4 cells and uses this cells to make copies of itself. This weakens a person‚Äôs immune system.

A

AIDS (Acquired Immune Deficiency Syndrome)

A set of symptoms and illnesses that develop when advanced HIV infection has destroyed the immune system. If HIV is left untreated, it may take up to 10-15 years for the immune system to be so severely damaged that it can no longer defend itself.

üï∞Ô∏è History of HIV/AIDS

HIV is believed to have originated from Kinshasa in DRC around 1920 when HIV crossed species from chimpanzees to humans. Available data suggests that current epidemics started in the mid- late 1970s. By 1980s HIV had already spread to North America, South America, Europe, Africa and Australia.

1981
Initial Cases

Cases of Pneumocystis carinii pneumonia (PCP) in five young gay men in Los Angeles. Reports of aggressive Kaposi‚Äôs Sarcoma. First cases of PCP in people who inject drugs. By year end: 270 cases, 121 deaths.

1982
Naming the Disease

CDC used the term 'AIDS' for the first time. Disease reported in haemophiliacs and Haitians. In Uganda, cases of a fatal wasting disease known as 'slim' were reported.

1983
Transmission Routes Identified

AIDS reported in female partners of infected men. CDC ruled out casual contact transmission. WHO held first meeting on global AIDS situation.

1984
Virus Identified

National Cancer Institute announced cause was HTLV-III (later HIV). Blood test creation began.

1985
Testing & Recommendations

FDA licensed first commercial blood test (ELISA). Blood bank screening began. First recommendations for preventing mother to child transmission issued.

1986
Official Naming

Virus officially named HIV (human immunodeficiency virus).

Epidemiology in Kenya (2019)

Kenya has the joint third-largest epidemic in the world, alongside Mozambique and Uganda. In recent decades Kenya has been a huge prevention success story in the region.

  • 1.5 million people living with HIV
  • 4.5% Prevalence (ages 15-49 years)

  • 21,000 AIDS related deaths

üîÑ Transmission and Risk Factors

The HIV virus is present in blood, semen, vaginal fluids, and breast milk.

Major Modes of Transmission

80-90% of transmission

ü©∏

Infected blood transfusion, tattooing, circumcision, drug injection

ü§∞

Mother to Child

Through labour, delivery, or breastfeeding

Key Risk Factors

  • High Risk Behavior: Unprotected anal or vaginal sex, multiple sexual partners, partners with high risk behavior, homosexuality, drug and substance abuse.
  • Medical History: Recent history of genitourinary diseases (GUD) or any other STI. History of blood transfusion especially if not screened.
  • Procedures: History of any surgical operation involving unsterilized instruments (tattooing, ear piecing, circumcision).

The Seven Stages of the Life Cycle:

1
Binding

The virus attaches itself to the cell surface of the host cell (target CD4 cell).

2
Fusion

Outer envelope is removed to release the RNA of the virus into the cytoplasm of the host cell.

3
Reverse Transcription

Reverse transcriptase enzyme convert viral RNA into DNA.

4
Integration

Viral DNA penetrates T-cell nucleus and integrate into host cell chromosome by the help of the enzyme integrase.

5
Replication

Viral DNA commands chromosome to make more viral DNA.

6
Assembly

Viral nucleic acid and protein structural substances assemble into capsome and then releases them into capsids to enclose the viral nucleic mediated by the enzyme protease.

7
Budding

The new virus eventually kills the swollen host cell which then releases it into the blood stream.

ü§í Opportunistic Infections (OIs)

Opportunistic infections are illnesses that occur more frequently and are more severe in people with HIV because they have damaged immune systems. They can occur if a person does not know they have HIV, are not on treatment, or treatment is not working.

ü©∫ HIV Counseling and Testing

Types of Testing

  • Provider Initiated Counseling and Testing (PICT): Routinely offered by healthcare providers to all patients.
  • Client Initiated Counseling and Testing (CICT/VCT): Clients present specifically for testing voluntarily.
  • Routine testing and counseling
  • Required testing
  • Testing for blood and tissue donation
  • Testing for medical research and surveillance

The 5 Cs of HIV Counseling and Testing

1Ô∏è‚É£

Informed Consent

Given relevant information to accept/refuse. Written and signed. Special considerations for minors, couples, illiteracy, incapacitation.

2Ô∏è‚É£

Confidentiality

Discussions not disclosed without consent. Shared confidentiality encouraged.

3Ô∏è‚É£

Counselling

Pre-test information and high-quality post-test counseling.

4Ô∏è‚É£

Correct Results

QA mechanisms essential for correct diagnosis.

5Ô∏è‚É£

Connection

Linkage to prevention, treatment, and care services.

Post-Test Services

HIV Negative
Risk reduction counseling, focus on keeping negative, linkage to prevention services.
Inconclusive
Explain definitive diagnosis not possible yet. Draw blood for ELISA. Follow-up plan (return in 7 days).
HIV Positive
Explanation of results, ART information, where to get ART (active referral), prevention of transmission, partner/family testing.

Components of HIV Testing Protocol

01
Assess Need for Testing

Introduce self, address relation between current disease and HIV, advise on testing to understand primary cause of illness.

02
Benefits of Testing

Explain benefits (planning, reducing risk/anxiety if negative; access to services, ART, PMTCT if positive), implications of results, explain process, offer test.

03
HIV Testing

Conduct the test or show patient where to go.

04
Post-test Counseling

Communicate results. If negative: re-testing advice, VCT referral. If positive: reassure, treat current illness, refer to comprehensive care center.

Myths and Misconceptions

HIV cannot be transmitted through:

  • Touch, tears, sweat, saliva, or stool
  • Breathing the same air
  • Touching toilet seats or door handles
  • Drinking from water fountains
  • Casual contact (hugging, kissing, shaking hands)
  • Sharing eating utensils
  • Using gym equipment
  • Coughing
  • Insect bites (e.g., mosquitoes)
