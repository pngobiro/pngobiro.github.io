5.1.1 Definition

Home based care is the care an HIV/AIDS person receives that is extended from the hospital or health care facility to the patient‚Äôs home through family participation and community involvement within available resources and in collaboration with health care worker. This is a collaborative effort between hospital, family and community. It includes components such as physical, psychological and spiritual support.

5.1.2 Objectives

01

To facilitate the continuity of care of the PLWHA from the health facility to the home and community.

02

To promote family and community awareness of HIV/AIDS prevention and care.

03

To empower the PLWHA, the family, and the community with the knowledge needed to ensure long-term care and support.

04

To empower the PLWHA in self-care and positive living.

05

To facilitate quality community care for the infected and affected.

06

To raise the acceptability levels of PLWHAs by the family/community in order to reduce the stigma associated with AIDS.

07

To streamline the patient/client referral from the institutions into the community and from the community to appropriate health and social facilities.

08

To mobilize the resources necessary for sustainability of the service.

5.1.3 Rationale

The rationale for emphasizing home-based care rests in its benefits to PLWHAs, the families of PLWHAs, the community, and the healthcare system:

PLWHA

Allows persons living with HIV/AIDS to take responsibility for their own welfare and well-being; receive care in a familiar environment; continue participating in family affairs; retain a sense of belonging to social groups; and accept their condition more easily.

Contributes to family solidarity; helps the family accept the infected person's condition; makes it easier to provide care/support; can reduce health care costs; and makes it easier for family members who provide care to attend to other responsibilities.

Community

Helps reduce health care costs; affords opportunities for community members to confront stigma and provide support to persons living with HIV/AIDS; contributes to community cohesiveness; and raises awareness about the causes and impact of HIV/AIDS.

Health System

The health care system benefits because home-based care helps ease the demand on health care facilities; does not require the creation of extra services where none exist; and extends responsibility to individuals, families, and communities.

5.1.4 Role of Various Stakeholders

Providers of Home Based Care

  1. The patient: provides own care if not very sick
  2. The family members: relative friends and community members, to enable families take care of the infected person at home, there is need for health care worker to guide, advice (education) and counseled on home based care, nursing skills, psychological and material support. Families often face difficulties of certain death, and different types of illness --- increased care.
  3. Health care workers: CBHW, CBO, traditional healers, and faith based organization.
‚ñ∂ Key players of home based care
  1. The health facility: Making the initial diagnosis and delivering clinical care, recruiting the PLWHA into the programme, identifying needs at various levels, preparing the PLWHA for discharge home, preparing the family caregiver for the caring responsibility at home, supplying simple drugs and basic home nursing supplies, facilitating training and supervision of community health workers in home care and the use of simple drugs and supplies, caring for terminally ill PLWHAs depending on their wish. Some non-clinical aspects of these functions may be filled by non-government/community-based organizations or other responsible entity.
  2. The family and other caregivers: Caring for the PLWHA at home, collaborating with other care providers, e.g., religious institutions, support groups, and health and social institutions, consulting and involving the PLWHA on matters concerning them, accepting the reality of the situation, and helping the PLWHA to prepare for death.
  3. The community: Accepting the situation of the PLWHA, collaborating with existing agencies to meet the needs of those infected, forming support groups, advocating for the rights of PLWHAs.
  4. The PLWHA/patient: Identifying the primary or alternative caregiver, participating in the care process, writing a will, identifying own spiritual/pastoral needs, resolving to take personal responsibility to stop the further transmission of HIV, advocating for behaviour change.
  5. The government: Creating a supportive policy environment, developing policies and guidelines, developing and maintaining standards, providing/coordinating training, providing drugs and commodities.

üß© Components of HCBC

  1. Clinical care: this includes early diagnosis, rational treatment and planning for follow up care for HIV/AIDS related illness.
  2. Nursing care: is aimed at promoting and maintaining good health for the infected person e.g. hygiene, nutrition.
  3. Counseling and psychological care to include:
    • Reducing stress and anxiety
    • Promoting positive living
    • Helping the individual make informed decisions/ choices on HIV testing, plans for the future, behavior change and involve sexual partner(s) in such decisions
  4. Social support: the person is given information about social support groups, welfare services and legal advice for the individual and family. Surviving family members are involved and where possible provision of material assistance.
  5. Palliative care: care for patients with terminal diseases e.g. cancer
  6. Community mobilization

Community Mobilization

In community mobilization, both the national and even global communities have emerged with shared concerns to prevent the spread of the virus, to care for those affected by HIV and AIDS, and to advocate for health and human rights.

Characteristics of a "mobilized community":

  • Members are aware - In a detailed and realistic way ‚Äî of their individual and collective vulnerability to HIV/AIDS.
  • Members are motivated to do something about this vulnerability.
  • Members have practical knowledge of the different options they can take to reduce their vulnerability.
  • Members take action within their capability, applying their own strengths and investing their own resources ‚Äî including money, labour, materials or whatever else they have to contribute.
  • Members participate in decision-making on what actions to take, evaluate the results, and take responsibility for both success and failure.
  • The community seeks outside assistance and cooperation when needed.
