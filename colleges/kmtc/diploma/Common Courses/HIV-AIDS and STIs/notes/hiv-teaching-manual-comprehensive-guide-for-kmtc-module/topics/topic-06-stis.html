Syphilis

Contagious sexually transmitted disease caused by the bacteria Treponema pallidum.

‚ñ∂ Congenital Syphilis

A pregnant mother can transmit T. pallidum to the foetus through the placenta beginning from 10 weeks gestation.

  • Intrauterine Complications: Death, miscarriages, and stillbirth.
  • After Birth Complications: Interstitial keratitis, Hutchinson teeth, saddle nose, and periostitis.
‚ñ∂ Acquired Syphilis (Stages)
  • Primary Stage: Painless, hard, red papule (chancre) at inoculation site which later ruptures. Associated lymphadenopathy.
  • Secondary Stage (6 weeks - 2 years): Highly infectious. Symptoms include headaches, swollen lymph nodes, fatigue, fever, weight loss, hair loss, aching joints.
  • Tertiary Stage (2-15 years): Lesions in skin, CVS (aortic valve disease, aneurysm), Bones (osteitis), and CNS (blindness, deafness, mental illness, neurosyphilis).
üíä Diagnosis: VDRL and RDT.
Treatment: Antibiotics (Tetracycline, Doxycycline, Azithromycin, Ceftriaxone injection, Penicillin injection).

Gonorrhea

Caused by Neisseria gonorrhoeae. Affects urethra and lower genital tract. Incubation period 3-10 days.

Signs and Symptoms

  • Urinary frequency
  • Dysuria
  • Yellowish discharge
  • Salpingitis (tubal infection)
  • Lower abdominal/back pain
üíä Diagnosis: Pus swab smear (Gram stain) and culture.
Treatment: Ceftriaxone injection or oral azithromycin, or doxycycline.

Chancroid

Also called ‚Äúsoft chancre‚Äù or ‚Äúulcus molle‚Äù. Caused by Haemophilus ducreyi. Incubation 1-5 days (up to 30).

Signs & Symptoms

  • Ulcers with soft center (gray/yellowish-gray), defined edges, bleed easily if touched.
  • Pain during sex or urination.
  • Swollen lymph nodes.

Types of Chancroid

Follicular

Originates in hair follicles.

Dwarf

Small lesions.

Transient

Small lesions, typical chancroidal type.

Giant

Extends rapidly covering considerable area.

Phagedenic

Large, destructive, widespread necrosis.

Papular

Starts as ulcer, becomes raised.

üíä Treatment: Azithromycin, Ceftriaxone, Ciprofloxacin, Erythromycin.

Chlamydia

Caused by Chlamydia trachomatis. Often asymptomatic.

Key Points

  • Symptoms: Pain during sex/urination, green/yellow discharge, lower abdominal pain.
  • Complications: Infertility, PID, infections of urethra/prostate/testicles.
  • Newborn Risk: Pneumonia, Eye infections, Blindness.
  • Treatment: Doxycycline 500mg twice a day for 7 days.

Candidiasis

Fungal infection caused by Candida albicans (Yeast). Common in immunosuppression.

  • Symptoms: Itching, soreness, dyspareunia, dysuria, heavy curdy white discharge.
  • Treatment: Fluconazole, Nystatin, Topical antifungals.

Viral STIs

Genital Warts
Caused by HPV. Soft, cauliflower-like growths. Treated via topical agents or surgery.
Herpes (HSV)
HSV-1 (Oral), HSV-2 (Genital). Painful blisters/ulcers. Treated with Antivirals (Acyclovir).

Management Principles

  • Treatment should cover all common causes of the syndrome.
  • Adherence (6.7): Compliance is key to preventing drug resistance.
  • Contact Tracing: Treat partners to prevent reinfection.
6.8

ü§ù Association & Impacts

Association (6.8): Presence of a past or current STI is a major risk factor for HIV transmission. STIs facilitate HIV entry and shedding.

6.9 Effects/Impacts of HIV

  • Affects physical, emotional, moral, and economic well-being of individuals, families, and communities.
  • Psychosocial: Loneliness, stigma, disruption of family/peer relationships, fear of disclosure.
  • Economic: Discrimination in employment and insurance services.
  • Family Burden: Secondary stigmatization, financial constraints for care, death/incapacity of loved ones.
