Behaviour Change

The major route of HIV transmission is through sex. Reduce risk by correct use of condom every time you have sex, limiting number of sexual partners and being faithful to your sexual partner.

Use of Condom

Correct and consistent use of male and female condoms during vaginal or anal penetration can protect against the spread of STIs, including HIV. Evidence shows that male latex condoms when used consistently have an 85% or greater protective effect against HIV and other STIs.

üíä Pharmacological Interventions

Use of ARVs for Prevention

Secondary prevention benefits of ART: Several studies confirmed that if an HIV-positive person is taking ART and is virally suppressed they do not transmit HIV to their uninfected sexual partners. WHO recommended that all people living with HIV should be offered ART with the main aim of saving lives and contributing to reducing HIV transmission.

2.3.1 Pre-exposure Prophylaxis (PrEP)

WHO recommends PrEP as a prevention choice for HIV negative people who are at substantial risk of HIV infection as part of a combination of prevention approaches. PrEP is highly effective if taken consistently and correctly, it is safe and reduces ones risk of acquiring HIV by over 90%.

Key Target Populations: Sero-discordant heterosexual couples, men who have sex with men, transgender women, high-risk heterosexual couples (sex workers), and people who inject drugs.

üöë

2.3.2 Post-exposure Prophylaxis (PEP)

PEP is the use of ARVs within 72 hours of exposure to HIV to prevent infection. PEP includes counselling, first aid care, HIV testing, and administration of a 28-day course of ARV drugs with follow-up care.

WHO recommends PEP use for both occupational and non-occupational exposures, and for adults and children.

ü§∞ Elimination of Mother to Child Transmission (eMTCT)

The transmission of HIV from an HIV-positive mother to her child during pregnancy, labour, delivery or breastfeeding is called vertical or mother-to-child transmission (MTCT).

Risk Without Intervention

In the absence of any interventions during these stages, rates of HIV transmission from mother-to-child can be between 15% and 45%.

Risk With Intervention

The risk of MTCT can almost be eliminated if both the mother and her baby are provided with ARV drugs as early as possible in pregnancy and during the period of breastfeeding.
