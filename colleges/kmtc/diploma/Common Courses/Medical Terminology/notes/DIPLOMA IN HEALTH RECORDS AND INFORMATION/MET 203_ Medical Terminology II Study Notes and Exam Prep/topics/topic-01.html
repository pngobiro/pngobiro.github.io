üß≠ 1.4: Directional Terms

These terms are essential for describing the relative locations of different body structures as illustrated in diagram 2. These terms include:

  • Anterior (or ventral) Describes the front or direction toward the front of the body. The toes are anterior to the foot.
  • Posterior (or dorsal) Describes the back or direction toward the back of the body. The popliteus is posterior to the patella.
  • Superior (or cranial) describes a position above or higher than another part of the body proper. The orbits are superior to the oris.
  • Inferior (or caudal) describes a position below or lower than another part of the body proper; near or toward the tail (in humans, the coccyx, or lowest part of the spinal column). The pelvis is inferior to the abdomen.
  • Lateral describes the side or direction toward the side of the body. The thumb (pollex) is lateral to the digits.
  • Medial describes the middle or direction toward the middle of the body. The hallux is the medial toe.
  • Proximal describes a position in a limb that is nearer to the point of attachment or the trunk of the body. The brachium is proximal to the antebrachium.
  • Distal describes a position in a limb that is farther from the point of attachment or the trunk of the body. The crus is distal to the femur.
  • Superficial describes a position closer to the surface of the body. The skin is superficial to the bones.
  • Deep describes a position farther from the surface of the body. The brain is deep to the skull.

üíâ 1.8: Terms Relate To Body System Procedures

1.8.1 Circulatory System Procedures

  • Cardiologist ‚Äì a physician specializing in the diagnosis and treatment of diseases of the circulatory system, especially, the heart. However, after diagnosis, he/she may refer to a cardiovascular surgeon. A cardiologist does not do surgery.
  • Hematologist ‚Äì a physician specializing in diseases of the blood.
  • Electrocardiogram (ECG/EKG) ‚Äì a printout recording of the electrical activity of the heart. A frequently used instrument in the hands of a cardiologist.
  • Echocardiography ‚Äì using ultra high frequency sound waves (beyond human hearing), similar to ‚Äúsonar,‚Äù to form an image of the inside of the heart. This procedure can demonstrate valve damage, congenital (before birth) defects and other abnormalities.
  • Cardiac catheterization ‚Äì a long hollow tube, a catheter, can be threaded into an artery up into the heart. Then material opaque to X-rays can be released into the blood flow through the heart imaging the details of coronary arteries. Typically used to identify a blockage and location in the coronary circulation.
  • Phlebotomist/venipuncturist ‚Äì the specially trained nurse or technician draws blood for lab tests and may also start IV‚Äôs (intravenous fluids). The Greek and Latin versions of ‚Äúcutting into a vein.‚Äù

1.8.2: Nervous System Procedures

  • Neurologist ‚Äì a physician specializing in diseases of the brain, spinal cord and nerves. He/she may refer a patient to a neurosurgeon. Neurologists do not do surgery.
  • Lumbar (spinal) puncture or tap (LP) ‚Äì introducing a needle between the lower bony vertebrae of our spinal column allows a physician to sample the fluid, cerebrospinal fluid (CSF), surrounding the brain and spinal cord.
  • Brain scan ‚Äì introducing a radioactive element into the blood can image possible tumors in the brain. The radioactive dose is very low and detectable only with special, very sensitive instruments that are much more sophisticated than the old Geiger counters.
  • Electroencephalography (EEG) ‚Äì EEGs are used to diagnose different types of seizure disorders such as epilepsy, brain tumors, and are used in sleep research to identify stages of sleep.
  • Computed tomography (CT) ‚Äì a specialized X-ray machine that takes multiple images of a body area from different angles and has a computer that integrates the multiple images into ‚Äúslices‚Äù of the body.
  • Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) ‚Äì A powerful magnetic field, radiofrequency waves, and a computer are used to produce images of organs and body structures in specialized detail, including their locations in relation to one another.

1.8.3: Digestive System Procedures/specialists

  • Gastroenterologist ‚Äì a physician specializing in diseases of the digestive system including esophagus, stomach and intestines.
  • Proctologist ‚Äì a physician specializing in diseases of the rectum and anus. Proctology is a surgical subspecialty.
  • Guaiac test (Hemoccult, Fecult) ‚Äì a special chemical test to identify blood in the stool (feces).
  • Upper GI series ‚Äì a series of X-rays of the esophagus and stomach and small intestines. The This procedure may be used to identify problems with swallowing, stomach ulcers, twisting of the small intestines.
  • Lower GI series ‚Äì a series of X-rays using a barium enema to show the large intestine and rectum. This procedure can be used to identify problems such as diverticulitis/diverticulosis, and tumors.
  • Endoscopy ‚Äì Sound waves are generated from a tube inserted through the mouth and into the esophagus.
  • Ultrasonography (ultrasound) ‚Äì a procedure using high frequency sound waves to visualize internal organs. Primarily used to visualize abdominal and pelvic organs

1.8.4: Respiratory System Procedures

  • Pulmonologist ‚Äì a physician specializing in diseases of the lungs.
  • Respiratory Therapist ‚Äì a specially trained technician who administers, among other treatments, inhalation therapy to patients with lung disease.
  • Pulmonary angiography ‚Äì special X-rays of the vessels of the lungs.
  • Laryngoscopy ‚Äì visual examination of the larynx.
  • Endotracheal intubation ‚Äì passing a special air-tube into the trachea so oxygen can be reliably supplied directly to the lungs without risk of inhaling vomit from the stomach.

1.8.5: Female Reproductive System Procedures

  • Obstetrician ‚Äì A physician specializing in the diagnosis and management of pregnancy and delivering babies.
  • Gynecologist ‚Äì a physician specializing in diseases of the female reproductive system and surgery of this area. Most physicians currently specialize in combined practice of OB/GYN.
  • Episiotomy ‚Äì a surgical procedure cutting into the perineal area, the area between the vagina and anus in order to prevent tearing of tissues when the baby‚Äôs head traverses the vaginal opening.
  • Hysterosalpingogram ‚Äì special X-rays of the uterus and uterine tubes involving passing an opaque dye backwards up through the uterus to determine if the tubes are patent. Since the tubes are open into the abdominal (peritoneal) cavity, if patent, dye should spill out of the end of the tubes and be manifest on the X-ray.
  • Colposcopy ‚Äì using a magnifying instrument to inspect the interior of the vagina and cervix, the entrance to the uterus.
  • Dilation and curettage (D & C) ‚Äì dilating the cervix, the entrance into the uterus, and passing instruments that enable scraping off superficial layers of the endometrium. May be done as an early therapeutic abortion, or following a normal pregnancy to remove residual tissue remaining in the uterus, or may be done as a diagnostic procedure to examine lining tissue of the uterus.
  • Mammoplasty ‚Äì Surgical reconstruction of the breast may involve breast enlargement or reduction or cosmetic reconstruction after mastectomy.
  • Pudendal block ‚Äì An anesthetic administered to block sensation around the lower vagina and perineum.

1.8.6: Musculoskeletal System Procedures

  • Orthopedist ‚Äì An orthopaedic surgeon, a doctor who corrects congenital or functional abnormalities of the bones with surgery, casting, and bracing. Orthopaedists also treat injuries to the bones.
  • Arthroscopy ‚Äì A fiberoptic instrument is introduced into a joint cavity in order to visualize surfaces of bones entering into a joint, find tears in internal joint structures and evaluate sources of inflammation.
  • Bone scan ‚Äì A bone scan is an imaging test used to help diagnose problems with your bones.
  • Electromyography ‚Äì The recording of the electrical activity of muscle tissue, or its representation as a visual display or audible signal, using electrodes attached to the skin or inserted into the muscle.
  • Muscle biopsy ‚Äì Cutting out a small tissue sample of muscle in order to examine it under a microscope. This procedure can be useful in diagnosing muscular dystrophy and other neuromuscular disorders.
