üìñ Overview & Etiology

Mood (Affective) Disorders are a group of psychiatric diagnoses characterized by disturbances in emotional and behavioral response patterns ranging from elation and agitation to extreme depression and a serious potential for suicide. It is essentially a decreased or entire loss of control over mood.

Common Etiological Theories

  • Genetic Theory: If one parent has a bipolar disorder, there is 25% chance of transmission to the child.
  • Aggression Turned Inward: Overdeveloped superego leads to depression.
  • Psychoanalytic Theory: Mania is a defense against an underlying depression; Depression is due to a rigid superego.
  • Biologic Factor: Mania is related to increased norepinephrine while depression is related to low norepinephrine.

Precipitating Factors

  • Loss of a loved one
  • Major life events / Critical life events
  • Role strain
  • Decreased coping resources
  • Physiological changes

‚òÅÔ∏è Depressive Disorders

Characterized by anergia (lack of energy), exhaustion, agitation, noise intolerance, and slowed thinking process.

‚ñ∂ Major Depression

Severe depression which lasts for at least 2 weeks during which the person experiences a depressed mood or loss of pleasure in nearly all activities.

Diagnostic Criteria (4+ of the following):

  • Changes in appetite or weight (unintentional change of 5% or more in a month)
  • Changes in sleep pattern
  • Changes in psychomotor activity (agitation or retardation)
  • Decreased energy / Tiredness
  • Feelings of worthlessness or guilt
  • Difficulty thinking, concentrating or making decisions
  • Recurrent thoughts of death or suicidal ideation/plans
‚ñ∂ Dysthymic Depression

Less severe than major depression. Characterized by at least 2 years of depressed mood for more days than not.

‚ñ∂ Depression Not Otherwise Specified (DNOS)

Depression that lasts for 2 days to 2 weeks.

03

‚ö° Bipolar Disorders

Diagnosed when a person's mood cycles between extremes of mania and depression.

Phases of Mood

Mania

Requires at least 1 week of unusual/incessantly heightened mood plus 3+ symptoms:

  • Exaggerated self-esteem
  • Sleeplessness
  • Pressured speech
  • Flight of ideas
  • Reduced ability to filter stimuli

Hypomania

  • Less severe than mania
  • Lasts for at least 4 days

Subtypes

  • Bipolar I: History of Mania. Exhibits manic episodes, periods of normal behavior, and periods of profound depression.
  • Bipolar II: No history of mania. Exhibits depression, normal behavior, and at least one hypomanic episode.
  • Cyclothymia: Numerous episodes of hypomania and depressed mood (not meeting full criteria) that last for at least two years.

üè• Nursing Interventions: Mania

üõ°Ô∏è

Safety First

Provide for physical safety of client and others. Assess directly for suicidal ideation and thoughts of hurting others. Monitor whereabouts and behaviors frequently.

Communication & Limit Setting

  • Set limits: Clearly identify unacceptable behavior and convey expected appropriate behavior.
  • Speech: Use short, simple sentences. Keep channels open regardless of pressured/rapid speech.
  • Clarification: Ask clients to explain relationships between topics ("What happened then?").
  • Patience: Frequently request client to speak slowly ("I am having trouble following you").

Physiological Needs

  • Nutrition: Frequently provide finger foods high in calories and protein (sandwiches, shakes) as client may be too busy to sit.
  • Rest/Sleep: Decrease environmental stimulation (quiet room, no TV). Allow wandering in confined area until tired. Establish bedtime routine.

Behavioral Management

Protect client's dignity during inappropriate behavior (provocative/risky sexual behavior). Treat with respect; do not scold.

06

üíô Nursing Interventions: Depression

üö®

Suicide Risk

First Priority: Determine if client is suicidal. Ask about intent and plan. Institute suicide precautions (removal of harmful items, increased supervision).

Therapeutic Relationship

  • Spend non-demanding time with client. Use silence to convey worth.
  • Encourage verbalization and description of emotions.

ADLs & Nutrition

  • Assist with ADLs only as necessary to prevent dependency.
  • Break tasks into small, concrete segments to prevent overwhelm.
  • Offer small, frequent meals. Sit with client during meals.

Sleep & Rest

  • Encourage staying out of bed during the day.
  • Monitor sleep hours.
