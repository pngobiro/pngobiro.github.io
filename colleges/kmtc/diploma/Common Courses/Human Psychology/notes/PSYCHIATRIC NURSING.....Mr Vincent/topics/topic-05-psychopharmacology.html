üß† Antipsychotic Drugs (Neuroleptics)

Used to treat symptoms of psychosis, such as delusions and hallucinations. Also used for acute mania, psychotic depression, and drug-induced psychosis.

Mechanism of Action

The major action of all antipsychotics in the nervous system is to block receptors for the neurotransmitter dopamine.

  • Typical Antipsychotics: Potent antagonists (blockers) of dopamine receptors D2, D3, and D4. This makes them effective in treating target symptoms but also produces many extrapyramidal side effects.
  • Atypical Antipsychotics: Relatively weak blockers of D2, which may account for the lower incidence of extrapyramidal side effects. They also inhibit the reuptake of serotonin, making them more effective for depressive aspects of Schizophrenia.

Drug Alert: Clozapine (Clozaril)

Produces fewer traditional side effects but has the potentially fatal side effect of agranulocytosis. Develops suddenly and is characterized by fever, malaise, ulcerative sore throat, and leukopenia. Can occur up to 24 weeks after initiation.

Treatment: Weekly blood samples. Discontinue immediately if WBC drops by 50% or to less than 3,000.

üìâ Antipsychotic Side Effects & Management

Extrapyramidal Symptoms (EPS)

Serious neurologic symptoms caused by blockage of D2 receptors in the midbrain.

Acute Dystonia

Acute muscular rigidity and cramping, stiff/thick tongue, difficulty swallowing. Can lead to laryngospasm.

Signs: Torticollis (twisted neck), Opisthotonus (arched back), Oculogyric crisis (eyes rolled back).

Treatment: IM Benztropine (Cogentin) or IM/IV Diphenhydramine (Benadryl).

Pseudoparkinsonism

Drug-induced Parkinsonism. Symptoms: stiff stooped posture, masklike facies, shuffling gait, cogwheel rigidity, drooling, tremor, pill-rolling movements.

Treatment: Change antipsychotic or add anticholinergic/amantadine.

Akathisia

Intense need to move about. Client appears restless, anxious, agitated. Often leads to non-compliance.

Treatment: Change medication or add beta-blocker, anticholinergic, or benzodiazepine.

Tardive Dyskinesia (TD)

Syndrome of permanent, involuntary movements. Irreversible once developed. Symptoms: Tongue-thrusting, lip-smacking, blinking, grimacing.

Prevention: Keep dosage low, change meds. Clozapine does not cause TD.

üö®

Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome (NMS)

A potentially fatal, idiosyncratic reaction.

  • Symptoms: Rigidity, High fever, Autonomic instability (BP, diaphoresis), Delirium, Elevated CPK.
  • Treatment: Immediate discontinuance of antipsychotic, supportive care (rehydration, hypothermia).

Anticholinergic Side Effects

Symptoms usually decrease after 3-4 weeks.

  • Orthostatic hypotension, Dry mouth, Constipation, Urinary retention, Blurred vision, Photophobia.

‚òÄÔ∏è Antidepressant Drugs

Used for major depressive illness, panic disorder, anxiety disorders, and bipolar depression. Interact with norepinephrine and serotonin.

Classification & Mechanism

  1. Cyclic Antidepressants (TCAs): Block reuptake of norepinephrine and serotonin. Block cholinergic receptors (causing anticholinergic side effects).
  2. Monoamine Oxidase Inhibitors (MAOIs): Interfere with enzyme metabolism.
  3. Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors (SSRIs): Block reuptake of serotonin.
üçΩÔ∏è

MAOI Hypertensive Crisis

Ingesting foods containing Tyramine while taking MAOIs can cause a life-threatening hypertensive crisis.

Symptoms: Severe hypertension, Hyperpyrexia, Tachycardia, Diaphoresis, Cardiac Arrythmias.

Foods to Avoid: Aged cheeses (except cottage/cream cheese), Aged meats (pepperoni, salami), fermented products.

Drug Interactions: MAOIs + SSRIs/Cyclics/Meperidine/Buspirone/Dextromethorphan = Fatal.

‚ö°

Serotonin Syndrome

Can result from taking an MAOI and an SSRI at the same time or too close together.

Symptoms: Agitation, Sweating, Fever, Tachycardia, Hypotension, Rigidity, Hyperreflexia. Can lead to coma and death.

‚öñÔ∏è Mood Stabilizing Drugs

Used to treat bipolar affective disorder by stabilizing mood and avoiding highs (mania) and lows (depression).

Lithium Carbonate

  • Action: Normalizes reuptake of Serotonin, Norepinephrine, Acetylcholine, Dopamine. Reduces release of norepinephrine.
  • Dosage: 900 mg to 3,600 mg daily.
  • Serum Level: Should be about 1.0 mEq/L.
  • Note: No parenteral forms are available.

Anticonvulsants

Used as mood stabilizers:

  • Carbamazepine (Tegretol)
  • Valproic Acid (Depakene, Depakote)
  • Gabapentin (Neurontin), Lamotrigine (Lamictal)
  • Clonazepam (Klonopin) - also an anti-anxiety agent used for acute mania.

Alcohol Warning

Benzodiazepines strongly enhance the effects of alcohol. Clients should not drink alcohol when taking benzodiazepines.

  • Pemoline (Cylert)

Sensitizing Drugs: Disulfiram (Antabuse)

Used as a deterrent to drinking alcohol. Inhibits aldehyde dehydrogenase.

‚ö†Ô∏è

Disulfiram-Alcohol Reaction

5-10 minutes after ingesting alcohol:

  • Facial/body flushing, Throbbing headache, Sweating, Nausea/Vomiting.
  • Severe cases: Hypotension, confusion, coma, death.

Education: Avoid all alcohol products including shaving cream, aftershave, cough syrup, vanilla extract.

‚ö° Electroconvulsive Therapy (ECT)

Application of electrodes to the head to deliver an electrical impulse causing a seizure. Believed to correct chemical imbalance.

Procedure Facts

  • Voltage: 70 ‚Äì 150 volts
  • Duration: 0.5 to 2.0 seconds
  • Course: 6 ‚Äì 12 treatments
  • Frequency: Every 48 hours
  • Consent: Required

Indications & Contraindications

Indications: Depression, Mania, Catatonic Schizophrenia.

Contraindications: Increased intracranial pressure (tumor), Recent fracture, Retinal detachment, Cardiac condition, Pregnancy (relative).

Medications Used During ECT

  • Atropine sulfate: To decrease secretions.
  • Succinylcholine (Anectine): Muscle relaxant to prevent fractures.
  • Methohexital Sodium (Brevital): Anesthetic agent.

Common Complications: Loss of memory, Headache, Apnea.
