üìñ Overview & Etiology

Schizophrenia causes distorted and bizarre thoughts, perceptions, emotions, movements and behavior. It cannot be defined as a single illness; rather it is thought of as a syndrome or disease process with many different varieties and symptoms.

  • There is disharmony between the patient's thinking, feeling and acting.
  • The main problem in schizophrenia is Altered Thought Process.
  • The most acceptable theory on the cause of schizophrenia is the Biologic Theory which says that schizophrenia is due to increased dopamine.
üîç

Bleuler's Fundamental Signs (4 A's)

  • Associative Looseness
  • Autism
  • Apathy
  • Ambivalence

Patient Characteristics

  • Patients are usually of the asthenic or slender, lightly muscled body type.
  • They tend to be introverted, deficient in their affective response ability, self conscious, retiring, moody and sensitive.
  • There is failure in adapting to objective reality (usually utilize the mechanism of denial and withdraw from reality).
  • Patient acts out in ways which would ordinarily be subject to social restraint.

üè∑Ô∏è Types of Schizophrenia

‚ñ∂ Paranoid Type

Characterized by:

  • Persecutory (feeling victimized or spied on) delusions.
  • Grandiose delusions.
  • Hallucinations.
  • Occasionally, excessive religiosity or hostile and aggressive behavior.
‚ñ∂ Catatonic Type

Characterized by marked psychomotor disturbance, either motionless or excessive motor activity.

  • Catatonic Stupor: Marked decrease in reactivity to the environment; reduction in spontaneous movement/activity or mutism.
  • Catatonic Negativism: Apparently motive-less resistance to all instruction or attempts to be moved.
  • Catatonic Rigidity: Maintenance of a rigid posture against efforts to be moved.
  • Catatonic Excitement: Excited motor activity, apparently purposeless and not influenced by external stimuli.
  • Catatonic Posturing: Voluntary assumption of inappropriate posture.
  • Waxy Flexibility (Catalepsy): Maintaining a position for a long time.
‚ñ∂ Disorganized Type
  • Incoherence, marked loosening of associations, or grossly disorganized behavior.
  • Flat or grossly inappropriate affect.
  • Does not meet the criteria for the catatonic type.
‚ñ∂ Undifferentiated Type

Characterized by mixed schizophrenic symptoms along with disturbances of thought, affect and behavior. Prominent delusions, hallucinations, incoherence or grossly disorganized behavior. Patients whose manifestations cannot be fitted into one or the other types.

‚ñ∂ Residual Type

Absence of prominent delusions, hallucinations, incoherence or grossly disorganized behavior. Continuing evidence of the disturbance indicated by:

  • Marked social isolation or withdrawal.
  • Marked impairment in role functioning (wage-earner, student, homemaker).
  • Marked peculiar behaviors.
  • Marked impairment in personal hygiene and grooming.
  • Odd beliefs or magical thinking.
  • Marked lack of initiative/interest.

ü©∫ Detailed Symptoms

Symptom Categories

Positive (Hard) Symptoms

  • Delusions
  • Hallucinations
  • Grossly disorganized thinking, speech, and behavior

Negative (Soft) Symptoms

  • Alogia (poverty of speech)
  • Anhedonia (lack of pleasure)
  • Flat/Blunted Affect
  • Apathy & Lack of Volition
  • Catatonia

General Signs & Symptoms

1. Perceptual Changes

  • Illusions: Client misperceives or exaggerates stimuli in the external environment.
  • Hallucinations: Hallmark of schizophrenia. May be visual, olfactory, gustatory, tactile, or auditory.

2. Disturbances in Thought

Thoughts are disconnected or disjointed.

üí≠

Delusions

Fixed false beliefs about one's environment. Types include:

  • Persecutory or Paranoid
  • Grandiose
  • Religious
  • Somatic
  • Referential (Ideas of Reference)

3. Changes in Communication

  • Thought Blocking: Sudden stop in the middle of a sentence; silent for seconds to a minute.
  • Tangential Thinking: Veering into unrelated topics; never answering original question.
  • Circumstantiality: Unnecessary details but eventually answers the question.
  • Alogia: Poverty of content; lack of real meaning.
  • Stilted Language: Use of flowery, excessive, and pompous words (e.g., "Would you be so kind... as to do me the honor of providing just a wee bit of refreshment?").

4. Altered Thought Processes (Specific)

  • Magical Thinking: Belief that events happen simply because one wishes them to.
  • Thought Insertion: Belief that others are placing thoughts in their mind.
  • Thought Withdrawal: Belief that others are taking thoughts out of their head.
  • Thought Broadcasting: Belief that others can hear their thoughts (via radio/TV).

Other Manifestations

Disruptions in emotional responses (Restricted/inappropriate), Motor Behavior Changes (Catatonia), Self-care deficits (neglect bathing, bizarre clothing), and Activity Intolerance (ambivalence about simple actions).

üè• Nursing Management

1. Promote Adequate Communication

If a client complains of physical symptoms, consider them real until proven otherwise.

2. Promote Compliance with Medical Regimen

Administer medications and observe for therapeutic effects/side effects. Non-compliance often stems from: misunderstanding instructions, disorganization, or uncomfortable side effects.

3. Assist with Grooming and Hygiene

  • Establish clear expectations.
  • Avoid power struggles. If initial prompts fail, leave the client alone for a short period.

4. Promote Organized Behavior

The first rule is to go slowly and keep calm. Provide direction and limits.

5. Promote Social Interaction & Activity

Address withdrawal stemming from fear of rejection. Use Social Skills Training to find rewarding activities within capacity.

üö®

Managing Hallucinations & Illusions

  • Reassure client of their safety.
  • Protect from physical harm (responding to altered perceptions).
  • Intervene quickly (medication or quiet room).
  • Engage in reality-based activities (cognitive/verbal involvement).
  • Teach that hallucinations are part of the disease process.

6. Intervene with Delusions

  • Do not argue with general beliefs.
  • Focus on reality-based aspects of communication.
  • Protect from acting on delusions.
  • Observe for precipitating stressors.
