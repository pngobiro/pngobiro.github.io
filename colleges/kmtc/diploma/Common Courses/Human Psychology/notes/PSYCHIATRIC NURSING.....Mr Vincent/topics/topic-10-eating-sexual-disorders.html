üìâ Anorexia Nervosa

A life-threatening eating disorder characterized by the client‚Äôs refusal or inability to maintain a minimally normal body weight, intense fear of gaining weight or becoming fat, significantly disturbed perception of body shape/size, and inability to acknowledge the seriousness of the problem.

Key Diagnostic Features

  • Body weight 85% less than expected.
  • Amenorrhea for at least three consecutive cycles.
  • Preoccupation with food and food-related activities.

Physical & Behavioral Symptoms

  • Emaciation, Hypotension, Bradycardia, Hypothermia.
  • Hypertrophy of salivary glands.
  • Elevated BUN, Electrolyte imbalances, Leukopenia.
  • Fear of gaining weight (Main Sign).
  • Depressive symptoms, social withdrawal.
  • Rigid need to control the environment.

Characterized by recurrent episodes of binge eating (consuming large amounts in <2 hours) followed by inappropriate compensatory behaviors (purging, fasting, excessive exercise).

Physical Consequences

  • Loss of dental enamel (chipped, ragged teeth).
  • Esophageal tears.
  • Fluid and electrolyte abnormalities (Metabolic alkalosis from vomiting).
  • Mildly elevated serum amylase levels.

üë©‚Äç‚öïÔ∏è Nursing Interventions: Eating Disorders

Anorexia Nervosa

  • Promote Nutrition: Frequent, small meals. Set time limits (30 mins). Weigh daily. Record I&O. Observe during meals and bathroom visits.
  • Attitude: Calm, matter-of-fact. Meeting nutritional goals is non-negotiable. Avoid discussing food/recipes.
  • Coping: Involve client in treatment planning. Set firm, clear limits. Explore feelings about control and family roles.

Bulimia Nervosa

  • Coping with Anxiety: Help recognize events that create anxiety to avoid binge/purge response.
  • Identify Triggers: Situations preceding binges. Teach assertive communication to diminish guilt.

‚ö§ Sexual Disorders & Gender Identity

Gender Identity: Personal/private sense of being female or male.
Transsexualism: Gender identity disorder where a person feels trapped in a body of the wrong sex.

Paraphilias

Unconventional sexual behaviors. Pathologic when insistent, coercive, or harmful.

Non-Coercive Paraphilias

  • Fetishism: Arousal by inanimate objects or body parts.
  • Autoerotic Asphyxia: Neck constriction to enhance masturbation (risk of accidental death).
  • Sexual Masochism: Erotic interest in receiving pain/humiliation.
  • Transvestitism: Wearing clothes of the opposite sex.

Coercive Paraphilias

  • Exhibitionism: Exposing genitals to unsuspecting persons.
  • Voyeurism: Secretly observing others undressing/sex.
  • Frotteurism: Rubbing against non-consenting persons.
  • Pedophilia: Sexual interest in children.
  • Sadism: Inflicting pain for erotic pleasure.
  • Telephone Scatologia: Obscene phone calls.
  • Urophilia/Coprophilia: Involving urine/feces.

Therapy for Paraphilias

Behavior Modification: Uses reinforcement.

Aversion Therapy: Pairing stimulus with painful/unpleasant outcome.

Token Economy: Rewarding desired behavior.

üå™Ô∏è Sexual Addiction

A progressive disease where sex is used to numb pain. Addicts engage in a cycle of Preoccupation, Ritualization, Compulsivity, and Despair.

Many addicts have a history of abuse and equate sex with love.

‚öôÔ∏è Sexual Dysfunctions

‚ñ∂ Classification of Disorders
  • Desire Disorders: Inhibited Sexual Desire (low interest), Sexual Aversion (fear/distaste, often from trauma).
  • Arousal Disorders: Male Erectile Disorder, Female Sexual Arousal Disorder.
  • Orgasm Disorders: Inhibited orgasm (male/female), Rapid Ejaculation.
  • Pain Disorders: Vaginismus (spasms preventing penetration), Dyspareunia (pain during sex).

Nursing Interventions

  • Communication: Teach "I" language ("I am concerned...") instead of "You" language ("You only have sex on your mind").
  • Education: Anatomy, response cycle.
  • Anxiety Reduction: Analyze anxiety-producing situations.

Sex Therapy Components

Includes information/education, experiential/sensory awareness (tuning into bodily cues), insight, and cognitive restructuring.
