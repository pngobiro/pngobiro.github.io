üç∑ Alcoholism

WHO Definition: A chronic disease or a disorder characterized by excessive alcohol intake and interference in the individual's health, interpersonal relationship, and economic functioning.

It is considered to be present when there is 0.1% or 10 ml for every 1,000 ml of blood.

Metabolism of Alcohol

$$ \text{Ethanol } (CH_3CH_2OH) \xrightarrow{\text{Alcohol dehydrogenase}} \text{Acetaldehyde } (CH_3CHO) \xrightarrow{\text{Aldehyde dehydrogenase}} \text{Acetyl Coenzyme A} $$
$$ \text{Acetyl CoA} \xrightarrow{\text{Citric Acid Cycle}} CO_2 + H_2O + \text{Energy} $$

Phases of Progression

  1. Pre-alcoholic Phase: Starts with social drinking until tolerance begins to develop.
  2. Prodromal Phase: Alcohol becomes a need; blackouts occur; denial begins to develop.
  3. Crucial Phase: Cardinal symptoms of alcoholism develop (loss of control over drinking).
  4. Chronic Phase: The person becomes intoxicated all day.

Withdrawal Syndromes

1. Tremulousness

Occurs during drinking period up to 2 hours afterwards. Symptoms: Anxiety, agitation, irritability, progressing to tremors, tachycardia, and diaphoresis.

2. Hallucinations

Begins 12‚Äì48 hours after stopping. GI symptoms (nausea, vomiting) often present.

Delirium Tremens (DTs)

A medical emergency beginning a few days after cessation (1-5 days duration). Symptoms include:

  • Severe memory disturbance, agitation, anorexia.
  • Visual and tactile hallucinations (bugs crawling).
  • Elevated temp, severe diaphoresis, hypertension, tachycardia.
  • Autonomic hyperactivity and seizures.

üß† Alcohol-Related Syndromes

Chronic alcohol use leads to thiamine (Vitamin B1) deficiency, causing specific neurological conditions.

Syndrome Cause Key Features
Wernicke's Encephalopathy Acute Thiamine Deficiency Confusion, Ataxia, Ophthalmoplegia (eye paralysis).
Korsakoff's Psychosis Chronic Thiamine/Niacin Deficiency Confabulation (making up stories), Retrograde/Anterograde Amnesia.

üíä Pharmacologic & Nursing Management

Pharmacologic Treatment

  • Benzodiazepines (Lorazepam, Chlordiazepoxide): Suppress withdrawal symptoms. Monitor vital signs.
  • Thiamine (Vit B1): Prevent/treat Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome (100 mg/day).
  • Disulfiram (Antabuse): Deterrent to drinking.
‚ö†Ô∏è

Disulfiram (Antabuse) Warning

Mechanism: Inhibits aldehyde dehydrogenase, causing acetaldehyde accumulation.

Reaction: Flushing, throbbing headache, sweating, nausea/vomiting, hypotension. Can be fatal.

Education: Avoid ALL alcohol products (cough syrup, aftershave, vinegar, vanilla extract) for up to 2 weeks after stopping the drug.

Nursing Interventions

Common Behavioral Problems (The 5 D's): Denial, Dependency, Demanding, Destructive, Domineering.

Strategies:

  • Confrontation: Teach consequences (job loss, poor health, family breakup).
  • Education: Use videos and talks by recovered alcoholics.
  • Lifestyle Changes: Encourage new interests and coping strategies.
  • Support: Refer to Alcoholics Anonymous (AA).

üö¨ Other Substance Use Disorders

Cannabis (Marijuana)

Active Ingredient: THC (Tetrahydrocannabinol). Hashish is the resin.

Effects: Euphoria, relaxation, time distortion, food craving. Classified as a hallucinogen.

CNS Stimulants: Cocaine

Mechanism: Blocks reuptake of dopamine (sympathomimetic).

Acute Effects

Euphoria ("rush"), increased alertness/energy, restlessness, irritability, potential delusions/hallucinations (formication - bugs crawling).

Overdose Management

  • Beta blockers: For autonomic hyperactivity.
  • Nitroglycerine: For angina.
  • Calcium channel blockers: For hypertension.
  • Cooling: For hyperthermia.

Nicotine

Highly lipophilic; crosses blood-brain barrier. Stimulates release of Epi, NE, and ACTH. Chronic use associated with heart disease, lung disease, and cancer.
