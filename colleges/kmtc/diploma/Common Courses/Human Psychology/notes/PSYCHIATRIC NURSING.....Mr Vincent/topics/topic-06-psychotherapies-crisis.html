üõãÔ∏è Common Psychotherapeutic Interventions

A variety of therapeutic modalities are used to facilitate positive behavioral change and emotional healing.

‚ñ∂ Remotivation Therapy

Treatment modality that promotes expression of feelings through interactions facilitated by discussion of neutral topics. Reality orientation for rehabilitative patients only and not for actively psychotic patients.

Five Steps:

  1. Climate of acceptance (Welcome clients, introduce self)
  2. Creating a bridge to reality (Orientation to topic)
  3. Sharing the world we live in (Discussion of topic)
  4. Appreciation of the works of the world (Ask patient to reflect)
  5. Climate of appreciation (Express gratitude)
‚ñ∂ Group Therapy

Treatment modality involving therapeutic interactions of three or more patients with a therapist to relieve emotional difficulties, increase self-esteem, develop insight and improve behavior. (Ideal number: 8-10 members).

Types of Groups:

  • Therapeutic Group: To gain insight into problems (e.g., AA).
  • Socialization Group: To enhance interaction.
  • Life Review/Reminiscing Group: To lessen isolation.
‚ñ∂ Milieu Therapy

Consists of treatment by means of controlled modification of the patients' environment to facilitate positive behavioral change.

‚ñ∂ Family Therapy

Focuses on the total family as an interactional system. Best suited for families where there is domestic violence.

Goals: Enhancement of communication, mobilizing strengths, strengthening problem-solving.

‚ñ∂ Psychoanalysis

Focuses on exploration of the unconscious to facilitate identification of defenses. Utilizes dream analysis and free association.

‚ñ∂ Other Therapies
  • Music Therapy: Facilitate relaxation and expression of feelings.
  • Play Therapy: Enables expression of intense emotion in a safe environment (e.g., dolls for child abuse victims).
  • Hypnotherapy: Inducing a trance state where patient becomes submissive to instructions.
  • Humor Therapy: Therapeutic laughing to lessen tension.

üß† Behavior Modification & Cognitive Therapy

Behavior Modification

Application of learning principles to change maladaptive behavior through reinforcement.

Reinforcement

  • Positive Reinforcement: Giving attention/feedback when desired behavior is shown (e.g., assertiveness).
  • Negative Reinforcement: Removing a stimulus immediately after a behavior occurs so it is more likely to occur again (e.g., volunteering to speak to avoid anxiety of waiting).

Techniques

  • Aversion Therapy: Painful stimulus introduced to avoid another stimulus (e.g., rubber band snap for intrusive thoughts).
  • Token Economy: Rewarding desired behavior with tokens/privileges.
  • Desensitization: Periodic exposure to a feared object until behavior disappears.

Cognitive Therapy

Short term structured therapy oriented towards present problems and solutions. Main focus is often depressive disorders.

Techniques include: Anxiety Reduction, Relaxation Training, Cognitive Restructuring, Thought Stopping, and Learning New Behavior.

üÜò Rape & Crisis Intervention

‚öñÔ∏è

Definition: Rape

Penetration of an act of sexual intercourse with a female against her will and without her consent, whether her will is overcome by force, fear of force, drugs, or intoxicants. (Also applies to other forms of penetration under Republic Act 8353).

Essential Elements: Use of threat/force, Lack of consent, Actual penetration.

Types of Rape

Anger Rape

Physical violence and cruelty. Rapist believes he is a victim of an unjust society. Done as a means of retaliation.

Power Rape

Intent is to command and master another. Rapist has insecure self-image. Done to prove masculinity.

Sadistic Rape

Involves brutality and torture necessary for rapist's excitement. Assault is eroticized. Done to express erotic feelings.

Rape Trauma Syndrome

  1. Acute Phase: Shock, numbness, disbelief.
  2. Denial Phase: Refusal to talk about the event.
  3. Heightened Anxiety: Fear, tension, nightmares.
  4. Stage of Reorganization: Life normalizes.

Nursing Care for Rape Victims

  • Provide immediate emotional support; do not rush the victim.
  • Give control back to the victim (allow them to make decisions).
  • Priority: Preservation of evidence. (Victim must sign consent before photos/samples are taken).
  • Offer prophylactic treatment for STDs and pregnancy.

üè† Spouse / Partner Abuse

Mistreatment involving emotional, physical, or sexual abuse in an intimate relationship.

‚ö†Ô∏è

Warning Signs of Relationship Violence

  • Emotional abuse (insults, belittling).
  • Controlling behavior (friends, dress, life elements).
  • Unreasonable jealousy.
  • Intimidating behavior (invading space).
  • Inability to handle frustration without anger.

Battered Wife Syndrome

Cycle of domestic violence. Common trait of abusive men is low self-esteem; common trait of abused women is dependence.

Phases of Abuse

  1. Tension Building: Minor battering incidents.
  2. Acute Battering: More serious battering occurs.
  3. Aftermath / Honeymoon: Husband becomes loving and gives hope.
üöë

Nursing Priority

Provision of shelter is the priority in nursing care for the abused spouse.

DOs

  • Ensure confidentiality.
  • Listen and affirm ("I am sorry you have been hurt").
  • Express concern for safety.
  • Recommend support groups/counseling.
  • Help identify community resources.

DON'Ts

  • Disclose communications without consent.
  • Preach, moralize, or doubt the client.
  • Minimize the impact.
  • Imply the client is responsible.
  • Direct the client to leave (must be their choice).
  • Recommend couples' counseling.

üß∏ Child Abuse

Intentional injury of a child. Includes Physical Abuse, Sexual Abuse, Neglect, and Psychological Abuse.

Common Indicators

  • Serious injuries in various stages of healing.
  • Apathy, no reaction.
  • Excessive knowledge of sex.
  • Low self-esteem.

Priorities

  • Ensure the child's safety and well-being.
  • Report suspected cases to authorities.
  • Family and Individual therapy for the child.
