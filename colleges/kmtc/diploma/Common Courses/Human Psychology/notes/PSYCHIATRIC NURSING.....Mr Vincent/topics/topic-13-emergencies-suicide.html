üö® Psychiatric Emergencies

Situations requiring immediate intervention to prevent harm. Common psychiatric emergencies include:

  • Suicide
  • Delirium
  • Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome (NMS)
  • Violent/assaultive behaviour
  • Acute withdrawal
  • Anxiety or panic behaviour

‚ö†Ô∏è Suicide Overview

It is the intentional act of killing oneself. It is the ultimate form of self-destruction and often a cry for help.

Note: Women are more likely to overdose on medication.

Risk Factors

Psychiatric Disorders

  • Depression
  • Bipolar disorder
  • Schizophrenia
  • Substance abuse
  • PTSD
  • Borderline personality disorder

Environmental Factors

  • Isolation
  • Recent Loss
  • Lack of social support
  • Unemployment
  • Critical life events
  • Family history of depression or suicide
üìà

History & Timing

  • A history of suicide attempts increases risk.
  • Highest Risk Period: The first two years after an attempt, especially the first three months.
  • Those with a relative who committed suicide are at increased risk.

Demographic Variables

Suicide rates are higher among:

  • Single, Divorced, Separated or Widowed people
  • People confused about sexual orientation
  • People who have experienced a recent loss (job, prestige, status)
  • Protestants or those with no religious affiliation
03

üß† Theoretic Foundations

Psychodynamic Theories

  • Freud: Conflict between the instinct for life and the instinct for death. Suicide occurs when the wish for death predominates.
  • Aggression Inward: Aggression intended for others turned inward against the self.

Sociologic Theories

Social and cultural contexts influence suicidality. Four types:

  • Egoistic Suicide
  • Anomic Suicide
  • Fatalistic Suicide
  • Altruistic Suicide

Family Characteristics

Poor family history, early trauma, rigid/disorganized systems, disturbed parent-child relationship, unresolved loss, history of abuse.

üìã Lethality Assessment

Suicidal Ideation

  • Active: Thinks about and seeks ways to commit suicide.
  • Passive: Thinks about wanting to die/wishes to be dead but has no plans.
‚ùì

Guide Questions for Assessment

  • Does the client have a plan? Is it specific?
  • Are the means available? (e.g., access to a gun?)
  • Is the plan likely to be lethal?
  • Has the client made preparations for death (giving away possessions, suicide note)?
  • Where and when? Is the time a special date or anniversary?

üè• Nursing Care & Interventions

Priority Nursing Diagnosis: Risk for injury ‚Äì Self directed.

Core Interventions

  • Provide one-on-one monitoring.
  • Have frequent unscheduled rounds/visits.
  • Avoid use of metals and glass utensils.
  • Monitor for signs of impending suicide.

Prevention & Support

  • Assist with somatic therapies and treatment of drug abuse.
  • Help client promote self-esteem.
  • Listen to patient's embarrassments.
  • Identify client's choice of social support.
