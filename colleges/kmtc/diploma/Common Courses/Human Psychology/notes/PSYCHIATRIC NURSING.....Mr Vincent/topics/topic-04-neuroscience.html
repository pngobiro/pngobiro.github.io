üß¨ Anatomy of the Brain

Major Regions

The central nervous system (CNS) integrates sensory information and coordinates activity. Key regions include:

  1. Cerebrum
  2. Diencephalon (Thalamus + Hypothalamus + MFB)
  3. Brainstem
  4. Cerebellum
02

üß© The Cerebral Cortex

The cerebral cortex contains three types of functional areas:

  • Motor: Control voluntary motor functions
  • Sensory: Allow for conscious recognition of stimuli
  • Association: Integration

Lobes of the Cerebrum

Frontal Lobes

Abnormalities in the frontal lobes are associated with:

  • Schizophrenia
  • ADHD
  • Dementia

Parietal Lobes

Involved with:

  • Interpreting sensations of taste and touch
  • Assisting in spatial orientation

Temporal Lobes

Function as centers for:

  • Hearing
  • Memory
  • Expressions of emotions

Occipital Lobes

Assist in:

  • Coordinating language generation
  • Visual interpretation
  • Depth perception

üé≠ Cerebellum & Limbic System

The Cerebellum

It is the center for coordination of movements and postural adjustments.

‚ö†Ô∏è

Clinical Correlation

Inhibited transmission of DOPAMINE in this area is associated with a lack of smooth, coordinated movements in diseases such as PARKINSON'S DISEASE and DEMENTIA.

The Limbic System

It is our emotional/affective brain. The system is linked to the prefrontal lobe.

Key Functions

  • Amygdala: Elicits fear response.
  • Cingulate gyrus: Mediates gestures.
  • Odours: Often trigger emotional response.
  • Hypothalamus: Limbic output is relayed mostly through here (see psychosomatic illness).

Clinical Implications

Disturbances in the limbic system have been implicated in mental illnesses such as:

  • Memory loss seen in DEMENTIA
  • Poorly controlled emotions and impulses seen in PSYCHOTIC or MANIC BEHAVIOR

‚ö° Neurotransmission

Neurons or nerve cells communicate information with each other by sending electrochemical messages from neuron to neuron, in a process called NEUROTRANSMISSION.

The Process

  1. Neurotransmitter (NT) is released from the Sending Cell (Presynaptic Axon Terminal).
  2. NT crosses the synaptic gap.
  3. Activation: NT binds to receptors on the Receiving Cell (Postsynaptic Axon/Dendritic Spine).
    • Opens ion channels.
    • EPSP (Excitatory Postsynaptic Potential): Na+ into cell.
    • IPSP (Inhibitory Postsynaptic Potential): K+ out of cell.
  4. Termination: Reuptake occurs via reuptake channels.

These are chemical substances manufactured in the neuron that aid in the transmission of information throughout the body. They either excite (stimulate) or inhibit (stop) an action.
