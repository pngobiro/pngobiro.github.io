üë§ Personality Disorders

Personality is the relatively stable and predictable emotional and behavioural traits that characterize persons in their day to day living. They assist an individual to respond to a wide range of situations.

When these traits are inflexible and maladaptive and causing distress and impairment in the functional abilities of an individual they constitute a personality disorder. These represent significant deviations from the way average individuals in a given culture perceive, think, feel and relate to one another.

Key Characteristics

  • Individuals are often unaware of their conditions and are likely to reject treatment.
  • Alloplastic: Capable of adopting and altering external environment.
  • Egosyntonic: Acceptable to the ego.
  • They are not anxious and routinely ignore complaints from other members of the society.

üîÆ Cluster A: Odd & Eccentric

‚ñ∂ Paranoid Personality Disorder

Characterized by persistent and long standing suspicion, sensitivity and mistrust of people.

  • Avoid responsibility for actions, blame others.
  • Hostile, angry, irritable.
  • Interpret actions of others as deliberately demeaning/threatening.
  • Pathologically jealous and argumentative.
‚ñ∂ Schizoid Personality Disorder

Lifelong pattern of detachment and social withdrawal.

  • Emotionally cold, self-sufficient, detached.
  • Prefer solitary activities (e.g. reading).
  • Sexual life may exist only in fantasy; often unmarried.
  • Prefer lonely non-competitive solitary jobs.
‚ñ∂ Schizotypal Personality Disorder

Patients are eccentric, suspicious and show poor interpersonal relationships. Strikingly odd.

  • Extrasensory perception (telepathy), magical thinking, superstitions.
  • Inappropriate affect, odd thinking and speech (vague, metaphorical).
  • Social isolation.
03

üé≠ Cluster B: Dramatic & Emotional

‚ñ∂ Antisocial (Dissocial) Personality Disorder

Also known as sociopath or psychopath. Chronic antisocial behavior that violates others' rights or social norms, predisposing to criminal behaviour.

  • Failure to conform to social norms (repeated arrests).
  • Deceitfulness (lying, conning).
  • Impulsivity, irritability, aggressiveness.
  • Consistent irresponsibility.
  • Lack of remorse (indifferent to hurting others).
‚ñ∂ Histrionic Personality Disorder

Excessive display of emotions, dramatization and extroversion. Attention seeking behavior.

  • Uncomfortable when not center of attention.
  • Inappropriate sexually seductive/provocative behaviour.
  • Rapidly shifting and shallow emotions.
  • Self-dramatization, theatricality.
  • Considers relationships more intimate than they are.
‚ñ∂ Narcissistic Personality Disorder

Pattern of grandiosity, need for admiration and lack of empathy.

  • Grandiose sense of self-importance.
  • Preoccupied with fantasies of unlimited success/power.
  • Believes they are "special" and unique.
  • Sense of entitlement and interpersonally exploitative.
  • Lacks empathy.
  • Arrogant, haughty behaviours.
04

üò® Cluster C: Anxious & Fearful

‚ñ∂ Avoidant (Anxious) Personality Disorder

Pattern of social inhibition, feelings of inadequacy and hypersensitivity to negative evaluation.

  • Avoids occupational activities involving interpersonal contact due to fear of criticism/rejection.
  • Unwilling to get involved unless certain of being liked.
  • Preoccupied with being criticized or rejected.
  • Views self as socially inept, unappealing, or inferior.
‚ñ∂ Dependent Personality Disorder

Pervasive patterns of dependent and submissive behaviour related to an excessive need to be taken care of.

  • Difficulty making everyday decisions without excessive advice.
  • Needs others to assume responsibility for life.
  • Difficulty expressing disagreement (fear of loss of support).
  • Feels uncomfortable or helpless when alone.
  • Urgently seeks another relationship when one ends.

‚ù§Ô∏è Sexual Disorders

Persistent impairment of the normal patterns of sexual interest or response.

1. Gender Identity Disorders

Disturbed sense of one's masculinity or femininity.

  • Transsexualism: Persistent discomfort regarding one's anatomic sex; wish to adopt sex characteristics of other sex.
  • Gender identity disorder of childhood: Similar to transsexualism, early onset.
  • Dual role transvestism: Wearing clothes of opposite sex for temporary experience, no desire for permanent change.
  • Intersexuality: Gross anatomical/physiological features of the other sex (e.g., Turner's syndrome).

2. Disorders of Sexual Preference (Paraphilias)

Sexual arousal occurs persistently in response to objects not part of normal sexual arousal.

  • Fetishism: Non-living object (bras, shoes).
  • Transvestism: Wearing clothes of opposite sex.
  • Sexual sadism: Humiliation/suffering of partner.
  • Sexual masochism: Humiliation/injury to self.
  • Exhibitionism: Exposure of genitalia to strangers.
  • Voyeurism: Observing unsuspecting naked persons.
  • Frotteurism: Touching/rubbing against non-consenting person.
  • Pedophilia: Activity with prepubertal children.
  • Zoophilia (Bestiality): Activity with animals.

3. Sexual Dysfunctions

Significant disturbance in sexual response cycle not due to organic cause.

  • Frigidity: Absence of desire.
  • Impotence: Inability to have/sustain erection.
  • Premature ejaculation.
  • Non-organic vaginismus: Involuntary spasm of lower 1/3 of vagina.
  • Non-organic dyspareunia: Pain during coitus.

ü©∫ Nursing Interventions

ü§ù

Therapeutic Attitude

An accepting and non-judgmental attitude on the part of the nurse is highly essential for successful resolution of these problems as these are highly sensitive issues and may be causing significant distress to the patient.

  • Assess patient's sexual history and previous satisfaction.
  • Note cultural, social, ethnic, racial and religious factors contributing to conflicts.
  • Assess for medication affecting libido.
  • Provide information regarding sexuality (normal human response).
  • Refer for additional counseling or sex therapy if required.
  • Assist therapist in behavior modification plans.
