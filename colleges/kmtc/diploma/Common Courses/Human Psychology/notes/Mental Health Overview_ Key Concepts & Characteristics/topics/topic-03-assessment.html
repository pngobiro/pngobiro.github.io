üó£Ô∏è The Psychiatric Interview

This interview is the most important tool in psychiatry. It is used to understand the patient‚Äôs problem, elicit signs and symptoms, make appropriate diagnosis, initiate treatment and predict outcome.

Offers patients an opportunity to express themselves and others in a non-critical and non-judgmental atmosphere.

Reasons Why a Patient Consults a Mental Health Worker

  • Due to disturbing experiences
  • Pressure from the family, relatives, friends or the employer.
  • Others may be forced through the courts.

The Setting of the Psychiatric Interview

  • Should provide privacy and assure confidentiality.
  • The patient should receive full attention of the medial practitioner.
  • The sitting positions determine how the interview proceeds and what is said during the interview.
  • The patient to sit at right angles to the clinician with the distance between the patient and the clinician permitting easy communication without shouting and at the same time avoiding the discomfort of close physical proximity.

General Principles in Psychiatric Interviews

‚ñ∂ Active observation and awareness behavior

This begins from the moment the patient walks into the consultation room. The gait, the physical appearance and greetings, as well as the general attitude to the interview are all important. Focus on verbal and non-verbal communication.

‚ñ∂ Assessment and evaluation is a two-way process

While the medical worker is assessing the patient, the patient is also evaluating the medical worker on sensitivity and genuine desire to help. The patient may not reveal a great deal about himself unless he senses that the clinician is interested and concerned about him. If for instance the clinician looks at his watch, it may give the impression that he finds the patient boring.

‚ñ∂ Acceptance of the behavior of the patient

All behavior including what appears at first sight as odd has a meaning to the patient. Such behavior may invite ridicule and laughter. The clinician should accept such odd behavior. Accepting does not however mean approving it.

‚ñ∂ Avoid arguments with the patient

Avoid getting drawn into an argument with the patient. It may the patient‚Äôs way of relating to others or seeking help. The clinician should try to find the underlying relationship problems.

‚ñ∂ Do not assume you understand the patient

At all times the clinician should make sure he understands what the patient says or feels. One method of clarifying one‚Äôs thoughts of what the patient says and feel, is for the clinician to summarize a number of times during the interview by repeating what the patient has said and the feelings the patient has expressed. The patient can correct the clinician if he has been misunderstood.

‚ñ∂ Stress on feelings

The patient should be offered the opportunity to unburden these feelings, which may have a cathartic effect. The clinician should focus on feelings and emotionally charged areas should be explored.

‚ñ∂ Other Principles
  • Focus on interpersonal relationships: Interpersonal sense of love, acceptance, security and discipline reveal factors responsible in shaping the personality.
  • Avoid being moralistic or judgmental: Patients may avoid sharing problems if they fear judgment.
  • Show empathy: Sensing the client‚Äôs inner world of private personal meaning as if it were your own.
  • Try to tolerate the silence: Silence may mean the patient is sorting thoughts or is aroused.

The Stages of a Psychiatric Interview

1. The Initial Phase

Begins from the first contact. Clinician greets and introduces himself. Questions should be open ended and simple. Avoid "why" questions and technical terms. Mainly covers the patient‚Äôs illness.

2. The Middle Phase

Focuses on background: upbringing, family life, educational/vocational life, and interpersonal relationships (past and present). Explores influences on personality and current problems.

3. Closing Phase

Give the patient time to ask questions or express points not covered. Discuss the patient‚Äôs areas of concern and the feelings associated with it.

üìù The Psychiatric Assessment/History

This is obtained from the parent as well as from the family, relatives or friends. This is because in some cases the patient may not be responsive or may be confused and in others the history given by the patient may be inaccurate.

An accurate history and MSE are the cornerstones of diagnoses and treatment in psychiatry.

When do you write the psychiatric history?
It is best to write the entire history and MSE at the end of the session. However note down important points like dates, presence of psychopathological symptoms as it may be difficult to recall this later.

When do you interview the family?
In severely disturbed patients, it is important to involve the family during the initial interview. So much information may be obtained from the family, which may not be readily available from the patient.

Components of the Psychiatric History

1. Personal / Identifying Data

Information pertaining to age, sex, marital status, occupation, residence and nationality. Note the source of referral.

2. Chief Complaint

A brief statement of why the patient seeks help. It should contain the description of the problem and should be stated in the patient‚Äôs own word.

3. History of Presenting Illness

The patient‚Äôs problems are explored in detail and in chronological order. Contains psychosocial stress factors, physical illness, and relationship with presenting symptoms. Ask about onset, duration, allegations, and pre-morbid history.

4. Past Medical and Psychiatric History

Medical conditions the patient has suffered and previous incidences of admission into a mental unit/hospital and outcome of treatment.

5. Family History

Components depend on family type. Note name, age, sex, marital status, occupation, relationship with patient, current health condition. Check for economic dependence and history of mental illness in nuclear or extended family.

6. Personal History

The past period of the life of the patient should be reviewed.

  • Pregnancy, birth and early development (up to ~6 years): Unwanted pregnancy, birth problems, family type (extended/nuclear), significant incidents (divorce, death), school performance, neurotic traits.
  • Six years to puberty: Sense of identity, school performance, discipline, peer group activities.
  • Adolescence to 19 years: Period of heightened sexual awareness. Social achievements, relationships, professional interests, future goals.
  • Occupational history: Age of first work, income activity, nature of work, job satisfaction, absenteeism.
  • Marital history: Age at marriage, type of marriage (personal/arranged), feelings towards it, partner's health, children.
  • Sexual History: Attitude towards sex, sexual partners, past experiences, frequency, moral/religious attitude.

üß† Mental Status Assessment/Examination

This is used to determine whether a patient is experiencing abnormalities in thinking and reasoning ability, feelings or behaviour.

General Appearance

Grooming, posture, gait, physical characteristics, facial expression, eye contact, motor activity, specific mannerisms. State of consciousness (stupor, delirium, coma).

Speech

Rate, pitch, volume, clarity, speech abnormalities such as dysarthria.

Mood

Expression of emotion (subjective). Described as dysphoric, euthymic, expansive, irritable, labile, elevated, euphoric, ecstatic, depressive or anhedonic.

Affect

Appropriate or inappropriate, blunted, restricted, flat or labile.

Thoughts

Thought process: Flow of ideas and quality of associations (racing, slowed, circumstantiality, blocking, perseveration, loosening, flight of ideas, neologisms, word salad, echolalia).

Thought content: Distortions, delusions, ideas of reference, depersonalization, derealization, preoccupations, obsessions, phobias, somatic concerns, suicidal or homicidal ideation.

Perception

Evaluate presence of illusions, hallucinations (sight, hearing, taste, touch, smell), depersonalization or derealization.

Cognitive Functions

  1. Sensorium: Level of consciousness.
  2. Orientation: Time, place, and person.
  3. Attention and concentration: Naming objects, repeating numbers, simple calculations (serial sevens).
  4. Memory: Immediate (recall), recent (hours to 2-3 days), and remote (past years).
  5. Intelligence: Use IQ.
  6. Judgment: Understanding harmful consequences, decision making in life threatening situations.
  7. Insight: Awareness of illness and its implication.

üìä Psychiatric Classification

We adopt the DSM-IV TR classification using a multiaxial approach.

Axis Description Examples
Axis I Clinical Disorders Schizophrenia, Mood disorders, Anxiety disorders, Substance related disorders, Delirium/Dementia.
Axis II Personality Disorders & Mental Retardation Paranoid, Antisocial, Borderline PD, Mental retardation.
Axis III General Medical Conditions Conditions relevant to the understanding or management of the mental disorder.
Axis IV Psychosocial & Environmental Problems Problems with support group, social environment, education, occupation, housing, economics, health care access, legal system.
Axis V Global Assessment of Functioning (GAF) Clinician's judgement of overall level of functioning (Scale 0-100).

Axis V: GAF Scale Codes

100-91

Superior functioning, no symptoms.

90-81

Absent or minimal symptoms, good functioning.

80-71

Transient symptoms expectable reactions to psychosocial stressors; no more than slight impairment.

70-61

Some mild symptoms or some difficulty in social, occupational or school functioning.

60-51

Moderate symptoms or moderate difficulty in social occupational or school functioning.

50-41

Serious symptoms or any serious impairment in social, occupational, or school functioning.

40-31

Some impairment in reality testing or communication or major impairment in several areas.

30-21

Behaviour considerably influenced by delusions or hallucinations or serious impairment in communication or judgement.

20-11

Some danger of hurting self or others or occasionally fails to maintain minimal personal hygiene.

10-1

Persistent danger of severely hurting self or others or persistent inability to maintain minimal personal hygiene.
