üìä Overview & Classification

Mood disorders are characterized by disturbance of mood, accompanied by full or partial manic or depressive syndrome, which is not due to any other physical or mental disorder.

Classification of Mood Disorders

  • Manic episode
  • Bipolar affective disorder
  • Depressive episode
  • Recurrent depressive disorder
  • Persistent mood disorder
02

‚ö° Manic Episode

Mania refers to a syndrome in which the central features are over-activity, mood change which may be towards elation or irritability, and self-important ideas. It is related to excessive levels of norepinephrine and dopamine and a deficiency in serotonin.

Stages of Mood Elevation

  • IEuphoria: Increased sense of well-being/happiness.
  • IIElation: Moderate elevation with increased psychomotor activity.
  • IIIExaltation: Intense elevation with delusions of grandeur.
  • IVEcstasy: Severe elevation, intense rapture/bliss (delirious/stuporous).

Speech & Thought

  • Flight of ideas: Racing thoughts, rapid shifts.
  • Pressure of speech: Forceful, loud, difficult to interrupt.
  • Delusions: Grandeur or persecution.
  • Distractibility.

Other Clinical Features

Increased sociability, impulsive/disinhibited behaviour, hypersexuality, poor judgment, high risk activities (spending sprees, reckless driving), flamboyant dress, decreased need for sleep (< 3 hours), decreased food intake, absent insight.

Nursing Diagnoses & Goals

  • High risk for injury/violence: Related to extreme hyperactivity and impulsivity.
    Goal: Patient will not injure self or others.
  • Altered nutrition (less than body requirements): Related to refusal to eat or inability to sit still.
    Goal: Patient will not exhibit signs of malnutrition.
  • Impaired social interaction: Related to egocentric/narcissistic behaviour.
    Goal: Patient will interact appropriately.
03

üòî Depressive Episode

Characterized by one or more depressive episodes lasting at least two weeks. Caused by negative cognitions and decreased neurotransmitters (norepinephrine, serotonin).

Key Symptoms

  • Depressed Mood: Pervasive and persistent sadness.
  • Cognitions: Hopelessness, Helplessness, Worthlessness, Unreasonable guilt.
  • Suicidal thoughts: Gloomy preoccupations, death as release.
  • Psychomotor: Retardation (slow thought/action) or Agitation.

Somatic (Melancholic) Features

  • Significant appetite/weight loss.
  • Early morning awakening (>2 hours early).
  • Diurnal variation (worse in morning).
  • Anhedonia (lack of pleasure).

  • Dysfunctional grieving: Goal: Verbalize normal grieving behaviors.
  • Self-esteem disturbance: Goal: Verbalize positive aspects of self.
  • Altered sleep/nutrition/self-care: Goals: Sleep adequately, improved nutrition, maintain hygiene.

‚ÜîÔ∏è Bipolar Mood Disorder

Characterized by recurrent episodes of mania and depression in the same patient at different times. Interspersed with periods of normal mood. Onset usually between 20-30 years.

Expansive, grandiose, hyperirritable mood Low self-esteem

Rapid speech, frequent topic changes Difficulty concentrating/thinking
Decreased need for sleep/food Anhedonia
Impulsivity, impaired judgment Suicidal ideation

Treatment

  • Mood Stabilizers: Lithium, Valproic acid, Carbamazepine.
  • Antidepressants: Used with caution (may trigger mania).
  • Antipsychotics: For psychotic symptoms or acute mania.

Course & Prognosis

An average manic episode lasts for 3-4 months, while a depressive episode lasts for 4-9 months.
