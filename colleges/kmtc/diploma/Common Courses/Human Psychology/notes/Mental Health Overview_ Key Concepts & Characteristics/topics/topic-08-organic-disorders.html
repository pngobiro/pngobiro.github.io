üîç Overview & Causes

Organic brain disorders are mental disorders occurring as a result of medical conditions which induce brain dysfunction and lead to the appearance of characteristic psychological symptoms.

Causes of Brain Dysfunction

Category Specific Causes
Infections (Cerebral) Malaria (falciparum), typhoid fever, syphilis, trypanosomiasis, bacterial/viral meningitis, toxoplasmosis, HIV/AIDS.
Infections (Systemic) Septicemia, typhoid fever, bronchopneumonia.
Hypoxia Carbon monoxide poisoning.
Metabolic Electrolyte imbalance, vitamin B deficiency, porphyria, liver/renal diseases, spontaneous hypoglycemia.
Endocrine Thyrotoxicosis, myxedema, Cushing's syndrome, diabetic ketoacidosis.
Structural Cerebral tumor (Primary/metastatic), Head injury.
Vascular Disease Sub-arachnoid hemorrhage, multi-infarct dementia.
Degenerative Alzheimer's disease.
Substance Use Alcohol, barbiturates, cannabis, hallucinogens.

üë¥ Dementia (Alzheimer's Disease)

Dementia refers to chronic progressive brain dysfunction that leads to impaired memory, personality changes, and intellectual deterioration but without impairment of consciousness.

Alzheimer's disease is a chronic neurodegenerative disease that usually starts slowly and gets worse over time. Types include Presenile (onset <65 years) and Senile (onset >65 years).

Stages of Dementia

Stage I: Early

Duration: 2-4 years

  • Forgetfulness
  • Declining interest in environment
  • Hesitancy in initiating actions
  • Poor performance at work

Stage II: Middle

Duration: 2-12 years

  • Progressive memory loss
  • Hesitates in response to questions
  • Difficulty following instructions
  • Irritable, anxious, social isolation
  • Neglects personal hygiene

Stage III: Final

Duration: Up to a year

  • Marked weight loss
  • Unable to communicate
  • Does not recognize family
  • Incontinence (urine/feces)
  • Loss of mobility
  • Death (usually aspiration pneumonia)

Clinical Features (Alzheimer's Type)

  • Memory impairment: Recent memory is prominently affected.
  • Cognitive impairment: Disorientation, poor judgment, difficulty in abstraction, decreased attention span.
  • Personality changes: Lack of interest, self-centered, withdrawn, decreased self-care.
  • Affective impairment: Labile mood, irritability, depression.
  • Neurological impairment: Aphasia, apraxia, agnosia, seizures.
üåô

Specific Syndromes

Sundowner Syndrome: Characterized by drowsiness, confusion, ataxia, and accidental falls occurring at night when external stimuli and orienting cues are diminished.

Catastrophic Reaction: Agitation and attempts to compensate for defects by using strategies to avoid demonstrating failure (e.g., changing the subject, cracking jokes).

ü©∫ Management of Dementia

Aim: Maintain the patient in the community.

Pharmacological Methods

  • Antipsychotics (Risperidone, Haloperidol): Decrease verbal/physical aggressiveness, alleviate hallucinations/delusions.
  • Benzodiazepines: For insomnia and anxiety.
  • Antidepressants: For depression.
  • Anticonvulsants: Control seizures.

Nursing Interventions

Daily Routine

Maintain fixed timetable (waking, toilet, meals) for security. Use reality orientation (newspapers, calendar) to decrease confusion. Additional care during evening (Sundowning).

Personal Hygiene

Assist with brushing, bathing, and keeping skin dry. Nail care. Note: If they have problems with locks, remove the lock on the bathroom door.

Nutrition & Weight

Balanced diet. Allow plenty of time. Explain the meal. Ensure food is neither too hot nor too cold.

Toilet Habits

Establish rigid routine (e.g., conditioned behavior like bowel movement after tea). Take to urinate at fixed intervals.

Safety / Accidents

Avoid tripping hazards. Wear fitting shoes. Persuade to stop driving. Do not allow patient to take medication alone.

Mood & Emotions

Control abrupt changes by keeping a calm environment. Do not question repeatedly or give too many choices. Use distraction (music, nostalgic topics).

Wandering

Ensure identification bracelet. Secure locks. Accompany patient outside. Patients often lose geographic orientation.

Sleep & Interpersonal

Maintain sleep patterns; avoid daytime napping. Clear, unhurried communication (Yes/No questions). Involve old friends.

üòµ Delirium

An acute organic mental disorder characterized by impairment of consciousness, disorientation, disturbances in perception, and restlessness.

Course: Abrupt onset. Brief duration (usually about a week).

Clinical Features

  • Clouding of Consciousness: Main symptom. GCS ‚â§ 8 (Eye opening, verbal, and motor responses impaired).
  • Perceptual: Illusions and hallucinations (mostly visual).
  • Cognitive: Impaired attention, abstract thinking, comprehension, immediate/recent memory.
  • Psychomotor: Hypo/hyperactivity, aimless groping/picking at bed clothes (flocculation), startle reaction.
  • Sleep-wake cycle: Insomnia, reversal of cycle, nocturnal worsening.
  • Emotional: Fear, anxiety, depression, irritability, euphoria.

Management of Delirium

Pharmacotherapy: Identify and correct cause immediately (e.g., Dextrose for hypoglycemia, O2 for hypoxia, Vitamin B1 for thiamine deficiency, IV fluids).

Nursing Interventions

  • Safe Environment: Restrict stimuli, calm/well-lit room. Constant attendant for reassurance. Safety precautions against terrifying hallucinations.
  • Alleviate Fear: Remove objects causing misinterpreted perception. Consistent caregivers.
  • Physical Needs: Reduce fever, maintain intake/output, skin/mouth care, monitor vitals. Observe for coma (extreme drowsiness).
  • Facilitate Orientation: Repeatedly explain location/time. Introduce people by name. Use calendars/clocks.

üß† Organic Amnestic Syndrome

Impairment or loss of recent memory with preservation of immediate recall, remote memory, and other cognitive functions. No disturbance of consciousness.
