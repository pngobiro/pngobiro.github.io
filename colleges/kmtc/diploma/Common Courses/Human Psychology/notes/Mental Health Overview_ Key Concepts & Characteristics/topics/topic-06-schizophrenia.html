Schizophrenia

üìñ Definition & Overview

This is a psychotic condition characterized by a disturbance in thinking, emotions, volitions and faculties in the presence of clear consciousness, which usually leads to social withdrawal.

‚ÑπÔ∏è

Clinical Overview

A disturbance that lasts for at least 6 months and includes at least 1 month of 2 or more active phase symptoms.

The symptoms include: Delusions, hallucinations, disorganised speech, grossly disorganised or catatonic behaviour, negative symptoms.

Etiology (Neurotransmitters)

  • There is a hyperactivity of dopaminergic systems.
  • Drugs that increase dopamine (e.g. Amphetamine and cocaine) aggravate or trigger schizophrenia.
  • Epinephrine activity is increased in schizophrenia, leading to increased sensitization.
  • There is decrease in Gamma Amino butyric Acid (GABA) activity resulting in hyperactivity of dopaminergic neurons.

ü©∫ Clinical Signs & Symptoms

1. Reality Distortion

This leads to delusions and auditory hallucinations.

2. Disorganization

This manifests as thought disorder, inappropriate affect and bizarre behavior. The formal thought disorder results in incoherent speech. In severe cases words maybe so mixed up (word salad) making it difficult to understand.

3. Psychomotor Poverty (Negative Symptoms)

These include: Avolition ‚Äì lack of initiative, decreased spontaneous movement, poverty of speech and blunted or flattened affect (main ones) others are; poor grooming, anhedonia and social withdrawal.

Diagnostic Criteria (DSM-IV)

A. Symptoms (1 month or more of at least two):

  • Delusions
  • Hallucinations
  • Disorganized speech
  • Grossly disorganized or catatonic behavior
  • Negative symptoms (decrease expression of emotions, poverty of speech, loss of interests and drive, tremendous inertia)

B. Social and Occupational Dysfunction: One or more areas affected (work, relationships, self-care).

C. Duration: Continuous symptoms and signs for greater or equal to 6 months. These 6 months must include at least one month of symptoms meeting criteria A.

üóÇÔ∏è Subtypes of Schizophrenia

‚ñ∂ Paranoid Schizophrenia

A type of schizo in which the following criteria are met:

  • Preoccupation with one or more delusions or frequent auditory hallucinations.
  • None of the following is dominant: disorganized speech, disorganized or catatonic behavior, or flat or inappropriate affect.
  • This is a disorder characterized by delusions of persecution or grandeur.
‚ñ∂ Disorganized (Hebephrenic) Schizophrenia

A type of schizo in which:

  • All of the following are prominent: Disorganized speech, Disorganized behavior, Flat or inappropriate affect.
  • The criteria are not met for catatonic type.
‚ñ∂ Catatonic Schizophrenia

Clinical picture dominated by at least two of the following:

  • Motoric immobility AEB catalepsy including waxy flexibility or stupor.
  • Excessive motor activity (apparently purposeless and not influenced by external stimuli).
  • Extreme negativism (motiveless resistance to instructions or rigid posture) or mutism.
  • Peculiarities of voluntary movement AEB posturing, stereotyped movements, prominent mannerisms or prominent grimacing.
  • Echolalia or echopraxia.
‚ñ∂ Undifferentiated Schizophrenia

A type in which symptoms that meet criteria A are present, but the criteria are not met for the paranoid, disorganized or catatonic type. This disorder is also known as simple types.

‚ñ∂ Residual Type

Criteria met:

  • Absence of prominent delusions, hallucinations, disorganized speech and grossly disorganized or catatonic behaviour.
  • There is continuing evidence of the disturbance as indicated by the presence of negative symptoms or two or more symptoms listed in criteria A for schizophrenia, present in an attenuated form (e.g. odd beliefs, unusual perceptual experiences).

Other Psychotic Disorders

  • Schizophreniform disorder: Symptomatic presentation equivalent to schizophrenia except duration of 1 to 6 months.
  • Schizoaffective disorder: Mood episode and active phase symptoms of schizophrenia occur together; preceded or followed by at least 2 weeks of delusions/hallucinations without prominent mood symptoms.
  • Delusional disorder: At least 1 month of non-bizarre delusions without other active-phase symptoms.
  • Brief psychotic disorder: Lasts more than 1 day and remits by 1 month.
  • Shared psychotic disorder: Disturbance in an individual influenced by someone else with an established delusion.

üíâ Management

  • Pharmacological: Give antipsychotics.
  • Physical therapies: ECT.
  • Psychological therapies: Group therapy, behaviour therapy, social skills training, cognitive therapy, family therapy.

Nursing Management

Schizophrenic patients in an acute episode are seldom able to make a significant contribution to their history. Data may be obtained from family members/records.

Assessment

  • Observe behaviour pattern, posturing, psychomotor disturbance, hygiene.
  • Identify the type of disturbance experienced.
  • Note affect and emotional tone (congruency).
  • Assess for theme/content of delusional thinking (risk for violence).
  • Assess speech patterns associated with delusions.
  • Assess ability to perform self-care (sleep, interaction).
  • Determine any suicidal intent or recent attempts.

Nursing Care Plan (Diagnosis & Goals)

Disturbed Thought Process

Related to: Inability to trust, panic anxiety.
Evidenced by: Delusional thinking, extreme suspiciousness.

Goal: Eliminate delusional thinking, demonstrate trust, decreased anxiety, improved reality orientation.

Ineffective Health Maintenance

Related to: Inability to trust, suspiciousness.
Evidenced by: Poor diet, difficulty sleeping.

Goal: Maintain adequate nutrition, hydration, elimination, sleep, and take medication.

Self-Care Deficit

Related to: Withdrawal, regression, panic anxiety, cognitive impairment.
Evidenced by: Difficulty in hygiene, dressing, eating, toileting.

Goal: Demonstrate increased interest in self-care, complete ADLs with min assistance, adequate hygiene.

Potential for Violence

Related to: Command hallucinations.
Evidenced by: Physical violence, destruction of objects, self-destructive behaviour.

Goal: Not injure others/self, verbalize anger/frustration, express decreased agitation.

Disturbed Sensory Perception

Related to: Panic anxiety, biochemical factors.
Evidenced by: Inappropriate responses, disordered thought, poor concentration.

Goal: Demonstrate decreased hallucinations, interact with others, verbalize plans to deal with hallucinations.

Social Isolation

Related to: Inability to trust, delusional thinking.
Evidenced by: Withdrawal, sad dull affect, preoccupation.

Goal: Voluntarily spend time with other patients and staff in group activities.

üìà Course and Prognosis

Complete remission is possible after an acute attack and the remission of symptoms, maintenance therapy should be continued for at least one year.

  • Negative symptoms: Presence indicates poorer prognosis.
  • Sex: Females tend to be better socially adjusted.
  • Marital status: Prognosis better in those who are married.
  • Onset: Prognosis better in acute onset with clear trigger vs. gradual onset.
  • Premorbid personality: Social withdrawal/schizoid traits predict poor outcome. Social contacts predict better prognosis.
  • Comorbid disorders: Substance use and structural brain changes associated with poor prognosis.
