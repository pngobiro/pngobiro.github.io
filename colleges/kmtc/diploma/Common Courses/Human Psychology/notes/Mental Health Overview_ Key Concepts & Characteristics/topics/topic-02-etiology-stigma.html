üîç Aetiological Factors of Mental Illness

Many factors are responsible for the causation of mental illness. These factors may predispose an individual to mental illness, precipitate or perpetuate the mental illness.

Predisposing Factors

Determine an individual's susceptibility.

  • Genetic make-up
  • Physical damage to CNS
  • Adverse psychosocial influence

Precipitating Factors

Events occurring shortly before onset that appear to induce it.

  • Physical stress
  • Psychological stress

Perpetuating Factors

Responsible for aggravating or prolonging existing diseases.

A. Biological Factors

1. Heredity

What one inherits is not the illness or its symptoms, but a predisposition to the illness, which is determined by the genes that we inherit directly.

2. Brain Damage

Any damage to the structure and functioning of the brain can give rise to mental illness due to:

  • Infection (neurosyphilis, encephalitis, HIV)
  • Injury (loss of brain tissue)
  • Intoxication (alcohol, barbiturates)
  • Vascular (poor blood supply, bleeding)
  • Alteration in brain function (glucose levels, hypoxia, electrolyte imbalance)
  • Brain tumors, Degenerative diseases (dementia)
  • Vitamin deficiency (B complex) and malnutrition
  • Endocrine disturbances (hypothyroidism, thyrotoxicosis)

3. Biochemical Factors

Disturbance in neurotransmitters in the brain is found to play an important role in the etiology of certain psychiatric disorders.

B. Physiological Changes

Mental disorders are more likely to occur at certain critical periods of life marked by physiological (endocrine) changes and psychological issues: puberty, menstruation, pregnancy, delivery, and puerperium.

C. Psychological Factors

  • Specific personality types (e.g., unsocial, reserved).
  • Strained interpersonal relationships, bereavement, loss of prestige/job.
  • Childhood insecurities (pathological parents, faulty attitudes, abnormal parent-child relationships, deprivation).
  • Social and recreational deprivations.
  • Marital problems (incompatibility, childlessness).
  • Sexual difficulties (improper education, unhealthy attitudes).

D. Social Factors

Poverty, unemployment, injustice, insecurity, immigration, urbanization, gambling, alcoholism, broken homes, and political upheavals.

üõë Attitudes, Stigma & Myths

Factors Influencing Attitudes

üåç Culture

Shapes thinking, behavior, and feelings. Determines features of insanity (who is labeled insane). Guidelines on treatment and identity of helper differ by culture.

üéì Education

An educated person has a better understanding of health and mental illness, thus making their attitude more positive.

ü©∫ Health Beliefs

Determine positive or negative attitudes based on how the patient explains the illness (germ theory vs. evil spirits vs. imbalance).

‚õ™ Religion

Belief systems influence reaction to illness (e.g., evil spirits). Religion often encourages empathy.

Stigma and Mental Disorders

Stigma: the feelings of disapproval that people have about particular illnesses or way of behaving.

Stigma related to mental illness is in three forms:

  1. The patient may be stigmatized by health care providers, relatives and society.
  2. The relatives also experience stigma from society (associated with curse/taboo).
  3. Self-stigma experienced by the patient.

Myths on Mental Illness

‚ùå

Common Myths vs. Facts

  1. 1 Myth: People with mental illness cannot work.
    Fact: They can work; work is vital for rehabilitation and self-esteem.
  2. 2 Myth: People with mental illness are violent.
    Fact: In general, they are no more dangerous than healthy individuals (unless substance abuse is involved). Violence is often directed at family members.
  3. 3 Myth: All people with mental illness are mentally retarded.
    Fact: They are entirely different conditions. Illness occurs at all levels of intelligence.
  4. 4 Myth: Jail is an appropriate place for people with mental illness.
    Fact: Jails lack psychiatric services; patients are subjected to "double punishment" and abuse.
  5. 5 Myth: People never recover from a psychotic illness.
    Fact: Courses vary. Some recover completely, others lead productive lives with symptoms.
  6. 6 Myth: Mental illness is contagious.
    Fact: It is not contagious. This fear leads to avoidance and isolation.
  7. 7 Myth: Mental illness is caused by evil spirits or witchcraft.
    Fact: It is not caused by curses, evil eyes, or demonic possession.
  8. 8 Myth: People with mental illness are not able to make decisions about their own treatment.
    Fact: Most are able and eager to participate, though decision-making may be difficult during acute onset/relapse.

Reducing Stigma and Discrimination

  • Increase use of treatment strategies that control symptoms while avoiding side effects.
  • Initiate community educational activities to change attitudes.
  • Include anti-stigma education in training of teachers and health care providers.
  • Improve psychoeducation of patients and families.
  • Involve patient and families in identifying discriminatory practices.
  • Develop medications that improve quality of life and minimize stigmatizing side effects.

‚öñÔ∏è Principles of Psychiatric Nursing

‚ñ∂ Respect for the Patient

Achieved by accepting the patient as they are. Take time to listen, provide privacy, minimize humiliating situations, and be honest about medicines, privileges, and length of stay.

‚ñ∂ Availability

The nurse must be constantly available to assist the patient to attain their basic needs and alleviate suffering.

‚ñ∂ Spontaneity

Avoid being overly formal. Be comfortable with yourself, flexible, and aware of therapeutic goals.

‚ñ∂ Acceptance

Even if behavior does not please the nurse, the patient should be accepted as they are, but taking care not to reinforce the behavior.

‚ñ∂ Sensitivity

Show genuine interest and concern. Be persistent and patient even if no observable improvement is made.

‚ñ∂ Accountability

Accountability is required more in a psychiatric setting than any other type of health care because patients are vulnerable due to distorted thinking.

‚ñ∂ Empathy

Process of putting yourself in another's shoes and remaining emotionally detached. Enables you to give the patient the feeling of being understood and cared about.

‚ñ∂ Self-understanding

Recognition and acceptance of your own behavior and how it affects your relationship with other people.

‚ñ∂ Permissiveness and Firmness

Accepting the patient does not mean allowing them to do whatever they like. The therapist is expected to set limits and be firm in implementing them.

‚ñ∂ Skill in Observation

Be alert and observant at all times of the patient's behavior, attitudes, and how they react to staff, relatives, and fellow patients.
