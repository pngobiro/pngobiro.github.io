Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions

40 Marks
1.
With regards to occupational health and safety, which of the following is a type of pneumoconiosis
a) Silicosis of lungs
b) carbonization of lungs
c) kalic pneumoniosis
d) silicon poisoning
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Silicosis of lungs

Pneumoconiosis is a group of lung diseases caused by breathing in certain kinds of dust particles. Silicosis (caused by silica dust) is a classic example. The other terms are not standard medical classifications.

2.
In regards to ergonomic hazards
a) not wearing PPEs is a risk
b) It involves risk of ankward movement
c) Poor lighting and ventilation is a risk factor
d) It is the same as physical hazard
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) It involves risk of ankward movement

Ergonomic hazards relate to the fit between the worker and the job. Awkward movements, repetitive strain, and poor posture are defining characteristics. Poor lighting is a physical hazard, and not wearing PPE is a behavioral safety issue.

3.
Examples of international notifiable diseases include the following, except?
a) Ebola
b) SARS
c) Yellow fever
d) Leprosy
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Leprosy

Under the International Health Regulations (IHR 2005), diseases requiring immediate notification include Smallpox, Polio (wild type), New subtype Influenza, and SARS. Other Public Health Emergencies of International Concern (PHEIC) like Ebola and Yellow Fever are also reportable. Leprosy is a chronic disease and not an immediate international emergency notification target.

4.
The most hygienic method of disposing body of a Covid-19 victim is
a) Sea burial
b) Earth burial
c) Cremation
d) Home burial
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Cremation

Cremation completely destroys the body and any potential pathogens, making it the most hygienic method for highly infectious diseases, although deep earth burial is also accepted under strict guidelines.

5.
Water washed disease include
a) Malaria, typhoid, amoeba
b) Dysentery, typhoid, amoeba
c) Filariasis, yellow fever, measles
d) Trachoma, schistosomiasis, amoeba
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Trachoma, schistosomiasis, amoeba

Correction on options: Standard water-washed diseases are Trachoma, Scabies, and Conjunctivitis (due to lack of water for washing). Option D is the "best" fit in this context because Trachoma is the classic example, though Schistosomiasis is water-based and Amoeba is water-borne. This is likely the intended answer due to Trachoma.

6.
Concerning water treatment, the next phase after aeration is
a) Filtration
b) Sedimentation
c) Flocculation
d) Chlorination
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Flocculation

The typical sequence is: Aeration → Coagulation/Flocculation → Sedimentation → Filtration → Chlorination.

7.
Concerning communicable disease transmission
a) Mosquitoes transmit yellow fever and filariasis
b) Sand flies transmit onchocerciasis and malaria
c) Ticks transmit dengue and relapsing fever
d) Fleas transmit relapsing and dengue fever
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Mosquitoes transmit yellow fever and filariasis

This is correct. Aedes transmits Yellow Fever and Culex/Anopheles transmit Filariasis. Sandflies transmit Leishmaniasis (not Malaria). Ticks transmit Relapsing Fever (but Dengue is mosquito). Fleas transmit Plague.

8.
About clinical waste from hospital
a) Black is for sharps and general wastes
b) Red is for nonhazardous wastes
c) Yellow is for infectious wastes
d) Safely box is for waste with blood stains
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Yellow is for infectious wastes

Standard color coding: Yellow = Infectious, Black = General/Non-hazardous, Red = Highly Infectious/Anatomical, Safety Box = Sharps.

9.
Human fascioliasis arises from consumption of vegetables contaminated by
a) Urine
b) Faeces
c) Larva
d) Eggs
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Larva

Specifically, the metacercariae (encysted larvae) on aquatic plants (like watercress). While feces are the ultimate source of eggs, the infective stage consumed on the vegetable is the larva.

10.
Cyst of echinococcus granulosus is usually found in uncooked vegetables contaminated by excreta of
a) Human being
b) Dog
c) Goat
d) Pigs
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Dog

The dog is the definitive host for Echinococcus granulosus (Hydatid disease). Humans ingest eggs passed in dog feces.

11.
Regarding elements of PHC
a) It was developed in Alma Atta conference in 1998 by head of states
b) They are ten in number where Kenya added four to make them 14
c) Education that is tertiary level and secondary is a key element
d) It helps in improving the living standards only for the community
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) They are ten in number where Kenya added four to make them 14

Alma Ata was in 1978 (not 1998). The original declaration had 8 elements. Kenya expanded these (adding mental health, dental health, etc.). This option reflects the specific Kenyan context taught in the curriculum.

12.
In the four tiers of health, primary health care involves
a) Health centers and sub county hospitals
b) Dispensary and health centers
c) County referral hospital and community level
d) National referral hospital
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Dispensary and health centers

In the Kenyan health system structure (KEPH), Level 2 (Dispensaries) and Level 3 (Health Centers) are the primary care facilities. Level 1 is Community.

13.
The vector involved in transmission of Trachoma
a) Pulex irritants
b) Musica domestica
c) Aedis egyptae

Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Musica domestica

Musca domestica (the housefly) acts as a mechanical vector carrying Chlamydia trachomatis from eye to eye. (Note: Spelling in option is "Musica", but refers to Musca).

14.
A high BOD (Biological Oxygen Demand) indicates
a) Water is clean
b) Absence of microbial action
c) High level of microbial pollution
d) Low level of microbial pollution
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) High level of microbial pollution

High BOD means bacteria are using up a lot of oxygen to decompose organic matter, indicating high pollution levels.

a) birth

17.
In regards to rabies
a) It is a water bone disease
b) Bacterial infection
c) Usually treatable
d) It's a zoonotic disease
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) It's a zoonotic disease

Rabies is a viral disease transmitted from animals (zoonosis). Once symptoms appear, it is almost always fatal (not "usually treatable" in the curative sense, though preventable via PEP).

18.
The presence of high coliform counts in water indicate
a) Contamination by human wastes
b) Decreased biological oxygen demand
c) Hydrocarbon contamination
d) Phosphorous contamination
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Contamination by human wastes

Coliforms (specifically E. coli) are indicator organisms found in the intestines of warm-blooded animals. Their presence indicates fecal contamination.

19.
Aluminum sulphate is usually added at which level of water treatment
a) Disinfection
b) Coagulation
c) Flocculation
d) Filtration
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Coagulation

Alum (Aluminum sulphate) is a coagulant added to destabilize particles so they can clump together (flocculate).

20.
The most effective way to prevent water washed diseases
a) Provision of sufficient amount of clean water
b) Provision of high quality water
c) Minimize wastage of water
d) All the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Provision of sufficient amount of clean water

Water-washed diseases (like scabies, trachoma) are caused by lack of water for hygiene. Quantity is more important than quality for this specific category.

21.
Among the following which one is an element of primary health care
a) Prevention
b) Impartiality
c) Immunization
d) Treatment
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Immunization

"Immunization against major infectious diseases" is one of the specific 8 essential elements of PHC defined at Alma Ata. (Prevention and Treatment are broad concepts, Impartiality is a principle, but Immunization is a specific element).

22.
Aedes egyptae is a causative agent of
a) Filariasis
b) Malaria
c) Yellow fever
d) Dengue fever
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Yellow fever (and d) Dengue fever)

Aedes aegypti transmits both Yellow Fever and Dengue. In exams, Yellow Fever is often the classic answer, but both C and D are biologically correct. Select C as the primary classic association in historical context.

23.
Signs of microbial spoilage of food
a) Sliminess
b) Bruises
c) Physical reaction
d) Enzymatic browning
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Sliminess

Sliminess, off-odors, and mold growth are signs of microbial action. Bruises are physical, and enzymatic browning is chemical.

24.
Products which contain hydrogen cyanide poisoning
a) Tomatoes which are brownish in colour
b) Spinach
c) Potatoes which are greenish in colour
d) Cassava
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Cassava

Cassava contains cyanogenic glycosides which release hydrogen cyanide if not properly processed. Green potatoes contain solanine.

25.
Which of the following diseases can be transmitted through direct contact?
a) Hepatitis B
b) Typhoid
c) Amoebiasis
d) Pneumonia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Hepatitis B

Hepatitis B is transmitted via blood and body fluids (sexual contact, direct contact with open sores). Typhoid and Amoebiasis are fecal-oral (indirect). Pneumonia is droplet.

Among the non-communicable diseases which one is the leading cause of mortality world wide
a) Cardiovascular disease

Cardiovascular diseases (Ischemic heart disease, stroke) are the leading global cause of death.

27.
Concerning cancer, which statement is false
a) Viruses can be a causative agent
b) Usually hereditary
c) Prostate cancer is common in males above 60 years
d) Cancer of the cervix can be treated with human papilloma vaccine
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Usually hereditary

Most cancers are not hereditary (only about 5-10% are). They are largely due to environmental and lifestyle factors. Also, note D: Vaccines prevent cancer, they don't usually treat established cancer (though therapeutic vaccines are researched, prevention is the standard role). But B is the most definitively "False" general statement.

28.
The following are sources of demographic data Except?
a) Population census
b) Hospital records
c) Demographic sample surveys
d) Level of economy
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Level of economy

"Level of economy" is an indicator or outcome, not a source of data like a Census, Survey, or Record.

29.
Which is not a factor responsible for change in population
a) Marriages
b) Migration
c) Death
d) Age groups
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Marriages (and d) Age groups)

The three components of population change are Births (Fertility), Deaths (Mortality), and Migration. Age groups are a structure, not a change factor. Marriages influence fertility but are not a direct component of the demographic balancing equation $P2 = P1 + (B - D) + (I - E)$. "Age groups" is a characteristic. "Marriages" is an intermediate determinant. D is also not a process of change. Usually, Marriages is accepted as "not a direct component" in this context.

30.
A factor which does not influence the fertility rate
a) Age of marriage
b) Birth interval
c) Religion
d) Climate
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Climate

Age of marriage, birth spacing (interval), and religion/culture are major determinants of fertility. Climate has negligible direct influence.

31.
The following are details contained in a death certificate except?
a) Particulars of the victim
b) Immediate cause of the death
c) Next of kin
d) Serial number
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Next of kin

While the informant's name is recorded, the standard Medical Certificate of Cause of Death focuses on the deceased's particulars, cause of death, and registration number. The "Next of kin" field is more common in police/admission forms, though usually the informant (often kin) is listed. In exam context, "Next of kin" details are often considered secondary to the medical cause and identification.

35.
Which is NOT a component of epidemiological triad
a) Host
b) Agent
c) Time
d) Reservoir
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Time (and d) Reservoir)

The classic triad is Host, Agent, and Environment. "Time" is part of the descriptive epidemiology triad (Time, Place, Person) but not the *disease causation* triad. "Reservoir" is part of the Chain of Infection. Usually, in exam contexts asking for the triad of causation, "Environment" is the missing key. Since neither C nor D are part of Agent-Host-Environment, D (Reservoir) is a component of the chain, while C (Time) is a variable. Wait - some models use Agent-Host-Environment. Time is the 4th dimension. The answer depends on which "triad" is meant. Causation: Host, Agent, Environment. So C and D are not in it. D is likely the intended answer as "Time" is fundamental in epidemiology generally, while "Reservoir" is specifically Chain of Infection.

36.
The following are agent characteristic for disease causation Except?
a) Infectivity
b) Virulence
c) Pathogenicity
d) Resistance

Correct Answer: d) Resistance

Infectivity, Virulence, and Pathogenicity are intrinsic properties of the Agent. Resistance is typically a characteristic of the Host (immunity).

Section 2: Short Answer Questions

40 Marks
1.
Define the following terms as used in epidemiology
a) Epidemic
b) Endemic
c) Holoendemic
d) Pandemic
e) Morbidity
Show Answer

Answer:

  • a) Epidemic: The occurrence of disease cases in excess of normal expectancy in a community or region.
  • b) Endemic: The constant presence or usual prevalence of a disease within a geographic area.
  • c) Holoendemic: A disease affecting essentially every individual in a population (high prevalence starting early in life).
  • d) Pandemic: An epidemic occurring over a very wide area, crossing international boundaries, and usually affecting a large number of people.
  • e) Morbidity: Any departure, subjective or objective, from a state of physiological or psychological well-being (illness/sickness).

a) Define sampling
b) List four methods of probability sampling
Show Answer

Answer:

a) Sampling: The process of selecting a subset (sample) from a larger population to estimate characteristics of the whole population.

b) Probability Sampling Methods:

  1. Simple Random Sampling
  2. Systematic Sampling
  3. Stratified Random Sampling
  4. Cluster Sampling
  5. Multi-stage Sampling
3.
Explain five elements of primary health care
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Health Education: Educating communities on prevailing health problems and prevention methods.
  2. Food Supply & Nutrition: Promoting proper nutrition and adequate food supply.
  3. Safe Water & Sanitation: Ensuring access to safe water and basic sanitation.
  4. Maternal & Child Health: Providing care for mothers and children, including Family Planning.
  5. Immunization: Vaccination against major infectious diseases.
  6. (Others: Prevention/Control of endemic diseases, Treatment of common diseases/injuries, Provision of essential drugs).
4.
Using examples list five classes of hazards in a work place
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Physical Hazards: Noise, heat, radiation, vibration.
  2. Chemical Hazards: Dusts (silica), fumes, gases, solvents.
  3. Biological Hazards: Bacteria, viruses (HIV, Hep B), fungi.
  4. Ergonomic Hazards: Poor posture, repetitive motion, heavy lifting.
  5. Psychosocial Hazards: Workplace stress, bullying, violence, burnout.
5.
With examples, differentiate between emerging and re-emerging diseases
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Emerging Diseases: Diseases that have appeared in a population for the first time, or that may have existed previously but are rapidly increasing in incidence or geographic range.
    Examples: COVID-19, Ebola, HIV/AIDS, SARS.
  • Re-emerging Diseases: Diseases that were previously controlled or suppressed but are now returning or increasing in frequency.
    Examples: Tuberculosis (MDR-TB), Malaria, Cholera.

Explain five causes and risk factors for drug and substance abuse in our society
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Peer Pressure: Influence from friends to fit in.
  2. Stress/Trauma: Coping mechanism for life difficulties.
  3. Family History/Genetics: Predisposition or exposure at home.

8.
In experimental study designs, list the key features of a clinical trial
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Randomization: Random assignment to study groups to eliminate bias.
  • Control Group: Use of a placebo or standard treatment for comparison.
  • Blinding: Masking participants/researchers to group assignment (Single/Double blind) to prevent bias.
  • Intervention/Manipulation: The researcher actively applies a treatment/exposure.
  • Prospective Follow-up: Following subjects forward in time to measure outcomes.

Section 3: Long Answer Question

20 Marks
1.
Explain three (3) methods in which infectious diseases can be prevented and controlled
Show Answer

Answer:

Infectious diseases are controlled by targeting the links in the chain of infection. Three main methods include:

1. Controlling the Reservoir/Source:

  • Diagnosis and Treatment: Early identification and treatment of infected cases (e.g., TB treatment) reduces the infectious load and transmission.
  • Isolation: Separating sick individuals from healthy ones (e.g., Cholera/Ebola isolation wards).
  • Quarantine: Restricting movement of exposed contacts.
  • Animal Reservoir Control: Vaccinating dogs (Rabies) or culling infected animals.

2. Interrupting the Route of Transmission:

  • Vector Control: Reducing vectors via insecticide spraying (IRS), using bed nets (ITNs) for malaria, or draining stagnant water.
  • Environmental Sanitation: Safe disposal of feces (latrines) to prevent fecal-oral diseases like Typhoid.
  • Water Safety: Chlorination and boiling of water to prevent Cholera.
  • Hygiene: Hand washing to break contact transmission.

3. Protecting the Susceptible Host:

  • Immunization: The most effective method. Active immunization (vaccines) provides long-term protection (e.g., Measles, Polio).
  • Chemoprophylaxis: Giving drugs to prevent infection (e.g., Malaria prophylaxis for travelers).
  • Better Nutrition: Improving immune status.
  • Personal Protective Equipment (PPE): Use of masks, gloves, and condoms to prevent entry of pathogens.
