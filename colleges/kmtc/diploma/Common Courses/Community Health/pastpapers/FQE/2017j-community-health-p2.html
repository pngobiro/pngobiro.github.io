Section A: Multiple Choice Questions

30 Marks
1.
Best definition of disaster preparedness is:
a) Identification and mitigation of various risks
b) Coordination and organization of governmental and non-governmental relief agencies
c) Development and implementation of an overall plan
d) Hiring and training emergency personnel
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Development and implementation of an overall plan

Explanation: Preparedness involves planning, training, and education events that occur prior to a disaster. While training personnel (d) is part of it, the overall plan encompasses all aspects.

2.
Regarding poliomyelitis:
a) Outbreaks would be greatly reduced if majority of individuals in a community attain herd immunity
b) The polio vaccine should be given to adults
c) Other vaccines can be administered together with the polio vaccine during outbreaks
d) Physiotherapy and occupational therapy are effective during the acute phase
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Outbreaks would be greatly reduced if majority of individuals in a community attain herd immunity

Explanation: Herd immunity blocks transmission. Physio is contraindicated in the acute phase (rest is required) to prevent muscle damage.

3.
On demography:
a) Wider perspective of the definition fully describes the fertility, mortality and migration only
b) Natural increase in population is the sum of birth, fertility and deaths only
c) Level of education, occupation, nationality and religious beliefs are critical in the wider definition
d) Involves only population parameters
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Level of education, occupation, nationality and religious beliefs are critical in the wider definition

Explanation: Demography is not just about numbers (formal demography) but also social demography which includes qualitative characteristics like education and religion.

4.
Road carnage is a national socio-economic problem in Kenya; which of the following risk factors that influences post-crash outcome:
a) Presence of alcohol and other drugs and difficulty in rescuing and extracting people from vehicles
b) Seat-belts and child restraints not used
c) Human tolerance factors and inappropriate or excessive speed
d) Roadside objects and non-crash-protective vehicles
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Presence of alcohol... and difficulty in rescuing... (specifically the rescue part)

Explanation: The question asks about post-crash outcome. Difficulty in rescue and extraction directly affects the survival and recovery (outcome) after the crash has occurred. Speed and alcohol (preventive factors) influence the crash itself or severity, but rescue is post-event.

5.
Advantages of implementing the occupational health and safety management system include:
a) Safe working environment with increased costs
b) Safe work place, motivation and reduced cost of doing business
c) Enhanced stakeholder's confidence with costly early retirement
d) High insurance premiums due to work relate accident and diseases
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Safe work place, motivation and reduced cost of doing business

Explanation: OHS systems reduce accidents, which reduces insurance premiums, legal costs, and lost man-hours, thereby reducing the cost of doing business while improving morale.

8.
In a stable situation:
a) Incidence = Prevalence + Duration
b) Incidence = Prevalence × Duration
c) Prevalence = Incidence + Duration
d) Prevalence = Incidence × Duration
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Prevalence = Incidence × Duration ($P = I \times D$)

Explanation: In a stable population (steady state), Prevalence is approximately equal to the Incidence rate multiplied by the average Duration of the disease.

9.
The following disease give lifelong immunity after an attack:
a) Diphtheria
b) Mumps
c) Tetanus
d) Typhoid
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Mumps

Explanation: Natural infection with Mumps usually confers lifelong immunity. Tetanus does not confer immunity (toxin mediated, requires vaccination). Typhoid and Diphtheria immunity wanes over time.

10.
The STOP principle of protecting workers from harmful substances stands for:
a) Substitution-Technical Measures-Organizational Measures-Personal protective measures
b) Substitution-Technical Measures-Oversight-Participation
c) Substitution-Training-Organizational Measures-Participation
d) Substitution-Training-Organizational Measures-Punishment
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Substitution-Technical Measures-Organizational Measures-Personal protective measures

Explanation: This is the hierarchy of controls: Substitute the danger, use Technical (Engineering) controls, then Organizational (Admin) controls, and finally PPE.

12.
A disease is endemic when it:
a) Is constantly present at low rates in a specified geographical area
b) Occurs in a frequency more than expected in a specified geographical area
c) Occurs in more than one geographical area
d) Occurs in more than one season
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Is constantly present at low rates in a specified geographical area

Explanation: Endemic refers to the habitual presence of a disease within a given geographic area. Option B defines an Epidemic.

14.
Which of the following is true about the use of antiretroviral treatment in HIV/AIDS treatment?
a) Incidence will decrease
b) Incidence will increase
c) Prevalence will decrease
d) Prevalence will increase
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Prevalence will increase

Explanation: ARVs prolong the life of patients living with HIV. Since they do not cure the disease but prevent death (reduce mortality), the duration ($D$) increases, which increases the Prevalence ($P = I \times D$), assuming incidence is constant.

16.
Water washed diseases include:
a) Cholera, typhoid and dysenteries
b) Malaria, cholera and filariasis
c) Skin infections, trachoma and schistosomiasis
d) Trachoma, malaria and typhoid
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Skin infections, trachoma (and scabies)

Explanation: Water-washed diseases occur due to a lack of water for hygiene (e.g., Trachoma, Scabies, Conjunctivitis). Cholera and Typhoid are water-borne (fecal-oral via water). Schistosomiasis is water-based.

17.
On child feeding:
a) Artificial feeding involves bottle feeding, partial; breastfeeding and infant formula
b) Bottle feeding cannot be part of exclusive breastfeeding
c) Full breast feeding involves exclusive, partial and predominant breast feeding
d) In exclusive breastfeeding, the infant receives only breast milk
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) In exclusive breastfeeding, the infant receives only breast milk

Explanation: EBF means no other liquids or solids, not even water, with the exception of oral rehydration solution, drops, or syrups consisting of vitamins, minerals supplements, or medicines.

20.
The most common cause of maternal death in Kenya is:
a) Haemorrhage
b) Obstructed labour
c) Sepsis/infections
d) Unsafe abortions
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Haemorrhage

Explanation: Postpartum Hemorrhage (PPH) remains the leading cause of maternal mortality in Kenya and globally.

21.
The vaccine most sensitive to heat is:
a) BCG
b) Measles
c) OPV
d) Tetanus
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) OPV

Explanation: Oral Polio Vaccine (OPV) is the most heat-sensitive vaccine and must be stored at -20°C in district stores and kept on ice during sessions.

22.
Clinical features of kwashiorkor include:
a) Oedema, dehydration, old man's face and microcytic anaemia
b) Oedema, hepatomegaly, monkey-like face and microcytic anaemia
c) Oedema, moon-face checks, brownish sparse hair and flaky paint dermatitis
d) Oedema, moon-face checks, muscle wasting and a ferocious appetite
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Oedema, moon-face checks, brownish sparse hair and flaky paint dermatitis

Explanation: Kwashiorkor (protein deficiency) presents with edema, moon face, hair changes, and dermatosis. "Old man's face" is typical of Marasmus. "Ferocious appetite" is typical of Marasmus (Kwashiorkor children are apathetic and anorexic).

Section B: Scenarios & True/False

30 Marks
1.
In an outbreak of cholera in a village of 2000 people, 20 cases have occurred and 5 died. The attack rate is:
a) 1%
b) 0.25%
c) 5%
d) 25%
e) 0.02%
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) 1%

Calculation: Attack Rate = (Number of new cases / Population at risk) × 100
Rate = (20 / 2000) × 100 = 0.01 × 100 = 1%

2.
The following are preventive measures against tuberculosis:
a) Health education on good nutrition (True)
b) BCG immunization of all persons (False - children at birth)
c) Routine immunization of pregnant mothers (False)
d) Immunization of all new-borns with BCG vaccines (True)
e) Education of traditional birth attendants on aseptic techniques (Irrelevant to TB - False)
Show Answer

Correct/True Options: d) Immunization of all new-borns with BCG vaccines

Explanation: BCG is given at birth. Good nutrition boosts immunity (True) but is general. Immunizing "all persons" or "pregnant mothers" with BCG is not standard protocol.

3.
Cross-sectional study:
a) Is cheap (True)
b) Is a snapshot study (True)
c) Is used for rare diseases (False - Case control is better)
d) Is used for Communicable diseases only (False)
e) Uses only one cohort (False - not a cohort study)
Show Answer

Correct/True Options: a, b

Explanation: Cross-sectional studies measure prevalence at a single point in time (snapshot). They are relatively cheap and quick. They are not suitable for rare diseases.

7.
The following are advantages of pit latrine:
a) It uses locally available material (True)
b) It can be used as manure (False - handling human waste is risky unless composted properly)
c) It Controls flies (True - if covered/VIP)
d) Can be constructed next to a water source (False - contaminates water)
e) It Has more light (False - usually dark)
Show Answer

Correct/True Options: a

Explanation: Pit latrines are low cost because they use local materials. They must be located away from water sources (at least 30m). They do not necessarily have more light; in fact, dark interiors help control flies (phototropism in VIP latrines).

8.
Concerning BCG vaccine:
a) given to all HIV positive children (True - if asymptomatic)
b) do not give it to HIV positive children (False)
c) a booster dose should be given at 6 month (False)
d) should be given to those who have clinical AIDS (False)
e) is contra-indicated to those who have clinical AIDS (True)
Show Answer

Correct/True Options: a, e

Explanation: WHO guidelines state BCG should be given to infants born to HIV+ mothers or who are HIV+ themselves, provided they do NOT have clinical symptoms of AIDS. It is a live vaccine and can cause disseminated disease in severely immunocompromised (clinical AIDS) children.
