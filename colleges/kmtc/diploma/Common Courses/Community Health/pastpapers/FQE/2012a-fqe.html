Part 1: Essay Questions

1.
Highlight the routes of exposure to occupational health hazards
Show Answer

Answer:

Routes of Exposure:

  • Topical or Subcutaneous tissues (Absorption): Through skin/mucous membranes. Common for fat-soluble substances or lipids.
    Examples: Aniline, hydrogen cyanide, phenols, mercury, benzene, organophosphates, organochlorides, organonitrogens.
  • Respiratory system via Inhalation: Entering lungs and respiratory tract.
    Examples: Dust, mist, fumes, gases, smoke, vapours.
  • Gastrointestinal Tract (GIT) via Ingestion: Swallowing contaminants (hand-to-mouth).
    Examples: Infections, accidental poisoning.
  • Bodily or Organ Impaction (Physical): Direct physical effect on body structures.
    Examples: Radiation, vibration, noise, electromagnetic waves.

Note: Exposure to hazards mainly affects body organs such as the liver, kidney, skin, nervous system, and lungs.

2.
Describe the impact of HIV/AIDS on the health systems in a developing country like Kenya
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Increased Demand on Weak Systems: An increased demand of patients on health systems that are already weak in human and financial resources, stretching them further.
  • Economic Impact on Families: Devastating impact by increasing medical and health expenditures among families, loss of savings, and increasing funeral expenses.
  • Undermining Development Goals: It undermines efforts in achieving international targets such as the Millennium Development Goals (MDGs) / SDGs.
  • Health Workforce Attrition: Lack of enough health professionals is compounded by the fact that the available health personnel are also getting infected and dying from the epidemic.
  • Psychological Impact on Staff: Adversely affects health workers' morale as more patients respond poorly to treatment and die.
  • Displacement of Other Services: Patients suffering from other curable diseases may fail to gain admission or care due to bed occupancy by HIV-related ailments, worsening general morbidity/mortality.
  • Resource Diversion: More health resources and workforce are diverted to HIV/AIDS treatment, exacerbating shortages for other health care needs.
3.
Describe the health effects of floods in Kenya
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Death and Injury: Immediate drowning and physical trauma to both people and animals.
  • Mental and Physical Stress: Anxiety, depression, loss of security, and domestic problems due to displacement and loss of property.
  • Water and Sanitation Problems: Contamination of water resources and sewerage systems leading to unsafe water.
  • Nutritional Problems: Lack of food as floods destroy food reserves and crops (food insecurity).
  • Disease Epidemics: Increased incidence of water-related/water-borne diseases like diarrhoea, dysentery, typhoid, and cholera due to contamination.
  • Vector-borne Diseases: Stagnant water increases breeding sites for mosquitoes (Malaria/Rift Valley Fever).
  • Social Conflict: Increased conflicts over scarce safe water and food resources.
4.
Describe the principles of prevention and control of Communicable Diseases
Show Answer

Answer:

The aim is to reduce mortality and morbidity. Measures must often be continued indefinitely to prevent resurgence. Ideally, eradication is the goal but rarely achieved (e.g., smallpox).

Main Principles (Breaking the Chain of Infection):

  1. Attacking the Source (Reservoir):
    • Early diagnosis and treatment of cases (e.g., TB).
    • Isolation of infectious cases.
    • Notification of disease.
    • Surveillance.
  2. Interrupting the Route of Transmission:
    • Environmental sanitation (latrines, waste disposal).
    • Vector control (mosquito nets, spraying).
    • Water treatment (chlorination, boiling).
    • Personal hygiene (hand washing).
    • Disinfection and sterilization.
  3. Protecting the Susceptible Host:
    • Immunization: Active (vaccines) and Passive.
    • Chemoprophylaxis (e.g., malaria prophylaxis).
    • Better nutrition to boost immunity.
    • Personal protection (PPEs).
