Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions

40 Marks
1.
Water borne diseases include;
a) Cholera, typhoid and dysentery
b) Malaria, Cholera, and Filariasis
c) Skin infections, trachoma and schistosomiasis
d) Trachoma, malaria and typhoid
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Cholera, typhoid and dysentery

Water-borne diseases are transmitted through the ingestion of contaminated water. Malaria and Filariasis are vector-borne, while Trachoma and skin infections are water-washed (due to lack of water for hygiene).

2.
Natural history of non-communicable diseases entails;
a) Progress over a long duration of time
b) Association with micro-organism
c) Ease of prevention at the community level
d) Rapid progression
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Progress over a long duration of time

NCDs (like diabetes, hypertension, cancer) are characterized by a chronic course, long latency period, and slow progression, unlike infectious diseases which often have rapid progression.

3.
The following are true about measles vaccine except;
a) It is a live attenuated vaccine
b) Can be destroyed by contact with antiseptic
c) Offers protection for life
d) Is always administered at 9 months of age only.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Is always administered at 9 months of age only.

This statement is false (the exception). While the first dose is given at 9 months, a second dose is given at 18 months in the KEPI schedule. Additionally, it can be given at 6 months during outbreaks.

4.
The following disease gives a lifelong immunity after an attack;
a) Diphtheria
b) Mumps
c) Tetanus
d) Typhoid
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Mumps

Viral diseases like Mumps, Measles, and Rubella typically confer lifelong immunity after natural infection. Tetanus, Diphtheria, and Typhoid do not confer lifelong immunity and require booster vaccinations/re-exposure.

5.
Herd immunity;
a) Is conferred when a high percentage of the population is vaccinated
b) Is conferred when a high percentage of the population has acquired passive immunity
c) Gives a high level of individual immunity thus is alternative of getting vaccinated
d) Offers protection against all other vaccine preventable diseases
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Is conferred when a high percentage of the population is vaccinated

Herd immunity protects the unvaccinated community members by breaking transmission chains when a critical threshold of the population is immune (usually via active immunization/infection).

6.
Which of the following is transmitted through fecal oral route?
a) Hookworm
b) Schistosomiasis
c) Ascariasis
d) Filariasis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Ascariasis

Ascaris lumbricoides eggs are ingested via contaminated food/water (fecal-oral). Hookworm and Schistosomiasis larvae penetrate the skin. Filariasis is vector-borne (mosquito).

7.
Controlling of rodents is done by;
a) Airing clothes
b) Use of shoes
c) Cage traps
d) Watering earthen floor
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Cage traps

Trapping (cage/spring traps) is a mechanical method of rodent control. Other methods include chemical (rodenticides) and environmental sanitation (removing food sources).

8.
Which of the following is NOT a pillar of Primary Health Care?
a) Information
b) Financing
c) Human resource
d) Training of traditional birth attendants
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Training of traditional birth attendants

While TBAs were involved in early PHC, "Training TBAs" is an activity, not a fundamental pillar. The modern pillars are Community Participation, Inter-sectoral Collaboration, Appropriate Technology, and Support Mechanisms (which include HR, Financing/Logistics, Information).

9.
The following are components of wellness. Which one is NOT?
a) Intellectual
b) Psychological
c) Spiritual
d) Physical
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Psychological

The standard dimensions of wellness are often listed as: Physical, Emotional, Intellectual, Social, Spiritual, Environmental, and Occupational. "Psychological" is usually covered under Emotional/Mental, making it the most likely intended "odd one out" in this specific listing, though it is closely related.

10.
Improved sanitation methods include;
a) Latrine without slab and open latrines
b) Open latrines and ventilated improved pit latrines
c) Bucket latrines and pit latrines without slab
d) Flush toilet to piped sewer system or septic tank
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Flush toilet to piped sewer system or septic tank

"Improved sanitation" is defined by the WHO/UNICEF JMP as facilities that hygienically separate human excreta from human contact. Flush toilets, septic tanks, and VIP latrines are improved. Open pits, bucket latrines, and latrines without slabs are "unimproved".

11.
The following are roles of community health workers except;
a) Defaulter tracing
b) Treatment of minor ailments
c) Referral of patients from community level to health centers
d) Performing surgical operations
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Performing surgical operations

CHWs perform basic tasks like health education, minor treatments (first aid), and referrals. Surgery requires specialized medical training and is outside their scope.

12.
Water treatment methods include the following except;
a) Chlorination
b) Pasteurization
c) Solar disinfection
d) Filtration
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Pasteurization

Pasteurization is primarily used for milk and other liquids to kill pathogens. While boiling (heat) is used for water, "Pasteurization" as a technical process is not a standard municipal or household water treatment method compared to Chlorination, SODIS, or Filtration.

13.
Aedes aegypti spreads all the following except;
a) Yellow fever
b) Filariasis
c) Dengue fever
d) Cholera
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Cholera

Cholera is a water-borne bacterial disease (Vibrio cholerae), not vector-borne. Aedes aegypti transmits viral diseases like Yellow Fever, Dengue, and Chikungunya. (Note: Filariasis is mainly Culex/Anopheles, but Cholera is the definitive non-vector disease here).

14.
The best type of disease prevention is;
a) Secondary prevention
b) Tertiary prevention
c) Attacking the source
d) Immunization
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Immunization

While "Primary Prevention" is the best *category*, Immunization is arguably the single most cost-effective and successful public health intervention (specific method) for preventing infectious diseases.

15.
Which of the following diseases is transmitted by rodents?
a) Ebola
b) Plaque
c) Dengue fever
d) Leishmaniasis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Plaque (Plague)

Plague (caused by Yersinia pestis) is transmitted by fleas that infest rodents (rats). Ebola is associated with bats/primates, Dengue with mosquitoes, and Leishmaniasis with sandflies.

16.
The severity of a pollutant is determined by;
a) Chemical nature
b) Point source
c) Naturally occurring contaminants
d) Secondary source
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Chemical nature

The severity (toxicity/harm) depends on the chemical nature, concentration, and persistence of the pollutant. The source (point/secondary) describes origin, not inherent severity.

17.
This is a practice in pollution control;
a) Waste minimization
b) Littering
c) Environmental laws
d) Environmental policies
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Waste minimization

Waste minimization (Reduce, Reuse, Recycle) is an active practice to control pollution. Laws and policies are frameworks/tools, while littering causes pollution.

18.
Septic tanks;
a) Is a small sewage treatment plant
b) Are adaptable to big estates and towns
c) Have the capacity to contaminate ground water
d) Is not fit for individual household
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Have the capacity to contaminate ground water

If not properly sited or maintained, septic tanks can leak pathogens and nitrates into groundwater tables. They are typically used for individual households (contradicting d) and not for large towns (contradicting b).

19.
Communicable diseases are Most common in childhood due to;
a) Nutritional supplementation
b) Culture and practice
c) Provision of health services
d) Poorly developed immunity
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Poorly developed immunity

Children have immature immune systems and have not yet been exposed to many pathogens to develop natural immunity, making them more susceptible to infections.

20.
Antenatal care entails;
a) Four visits
b) Three visits
c) Two visits
d) Five visits
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Four visits

Under the classic Focused Antenatal Care (FANC) model, a minimum of four visits is recommended. (Note: New 2016 WHO guidelines recommend 8 contacts, but "Four visits" is the standard answer for FANC questions).

21.
The landmark event for Primary Health Care was;
a) The Ottawa Charter of 1986
b) The Alma Mata Conference of 1978
c) Bangkok Conference of 2005
d) Adelaide Conference of 1988
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) The Alma Mata Conference of 1978

The International Conference on Primary Health Care, held in Alma-Ata in 1978, established the Declaration of Alma-Ata, identifying PHC as the key to attaining "Health for All".

22.
One of the following is not a group approach to health education;
a) Lecturers
b) Group discussion
c) Clinical consultation
d) Workshop and seminars
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Clinical consultation

Clinical consultation is an individual approach (doctor-patient), whereas lectures, discussions, and workshops involve groups of people.

23.
Below are concepts of occupational health and safety. Which one is NOT.
a) Recognition
b) Service delivery
c) Control
d) Engineering
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Service delivery

Core OHS concepts (industrial hygiene) include Anticipation, Recognition, Evaluation, and Control. Engineering is a type of control. "Service delivery" is a general health system term, not a specific OHS concept.

24.
The scientific study of human population is referred to as;
a) Census
b) Special survey
c) Civil registration
d) Demography
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Demography

Demography is the statistical study of human populations (structure, distribution, and trends). Census and registration are methods of data collection.

25.
The most effective ventilation system for a home is;
a) Ceiling fan
b) Open window
c) Mechanical system that exhaust air to the outside
d) Ventilation fans in the kitchen and bathroom
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Mechanical system that exhaust air to the outside

While natural ventilation (windows) is common, a mechanical exhaust system is the most controllable and effective way to remove stale air and pollutants consistently, especially in modern sealed homes.

26.
The following are factors to consider before selecting a child spacing method except;
a) Availability of the method
b) Socio-economic status of the couple
c) Number of children couple desire to have
d) Acceptability of the method to the couple
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Socio-economic status of the couple

While cost can be a factor, family planning in public health is often free or subsidized. Clinical eligibility, availability, effectiveness, and acceptability are primary medical/personal considerations. SES is less of a direct selection criteria compared to medical/personal fit.

27.
Regarding hospital waste management;
a) Clinical waste such used gloves are stored in yellow jerry cans
b) Clinical waste such as used needles are stored in black liner bags
c) General waste including food remains are stored in red bags
d) Sharps and clinical waste such as syringes are stored in safety boxes
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Sharps and clinical waste such as syringes are stored in safety boxes

Sharps (needles, blades) must go into puncture-proof safety boxes. Black bags are for general waste. Yellow bags are for infectious soft waste. Red bags are often for highly infectious/anatomical waste (depending on color coding system, but D is the definitively correct statement).

28.
The following are strategies to enhance community participation. Which one is NOT?
a) Create awareness
b) Understanding community structure
c) Planning
d) Involve donors only
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Involve donors only

Community participation requires involving the community members themselves, leaders, and stakeholders. Involving "donors only" excludes the community, which is the exact opposite of participation.

29.
A characteristic of good drinking water is;
a) Hard water
b) Moderate hard water
c) Soft water
d) Very hard water
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Soft water

In the context of standard water quality questions, soft water is often cited as preferable for domestic use (lathers well, no scale). However, moderately hard water is often tastier and healthier (minerals). Given the options, "Soft water" is usually the intended answer for "characteristics of good water" in this context vs "Hard".

30.
Ethical issues concerning research subjects;
a) Confidentiality and privacy
b) Fraud and anonymity
c) Plagiarism and academic freedom
d) Plagiarism and fraud
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Confidentiality and privacy

These are core ethical principles protecting human subjects. Fraud and Plagiarism are research misconduct issues, not ethical rights of the subject.

31.
Which of the following is a barrier method of birth control?
a) Diaphragm
b) IUCD
c) Withdrawal
d) Sterilization
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Diaphragm

The diaphragm creates a physical barrier to sperm. IUCD prevents implantation/fertilization. Withdrawal is a behavioral method. Sterilization is permanent/surgical.

32.
Drug and substance dependence will constitute the following occurring within one-year period;
a) Tolerance and withdrawal
b) Tolerance and persistent desire to control usage
c) An individual gives up significant social and occupational activities
d) Recurrent use and tolerance
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Tolerance and withdrawal

According to DSM/ICD criteria, physiologic dependence is characterized by Tolerance (needing more for same effect) and Withdrawal (symptoms upon cessation).

33.
John is weighing 85 kg and his height is 1.56 meters. How do you classify his BMI category?
a) Underweight
b) Normal weight
c) Overweight
d) Obese
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Obese

Calculation: $BMI = \frac{Weight (kg)}{Height (m)^2}$

$BMI = \frac{85}{1.56 \times 1.56} = \frac{85}{2.4336} \approx 34.9$

A BMI > 30 is classified as Obese.

34.
Sex ratio is determined by;
a) Number of females present
b) Fertility level
c) Number of births
d) Number of males and females present
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Number of males and females present

Sex ratio is defined as the number of males per 100 females (or vice versa) in a population. It depends on the count of both sexes.

35.
Occupational health hazards associated with the practice of clinical medicine include;
a) Sexually transmitted infections
b) Needle stick injury
c) Lung cancer
d) Chronic Bronchitis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Needle stick injury

Needle stick injuries (and consequent blood-borne infections like HIV/Hep B) are a specific and significant occupational hazard for clinical medical practitioners.

36.
Disadvantages of landfills may include;
a) It becomes unstable with decomposing
b) Buried chemicals leak into the soil
c) It is often a breeding ground for infectious diseases
d) Becomes a nuisance
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Buried chemicals leak into the soil

Leachate (liquid carrying dissolved chemicals) can leak into the soil and groundwater if the landfill is not properly lined, posing a severe environmental hazard. (Note: All options are potential disadvantages, but B is a major environmental concern).

37.
One of the following is NOT true about disease surveillance.
a) Is a collection of disease and death data
b) Information on birth certificate is important
c) Information on death certificate is included
d) Is a collection of environmental parameters influencing disease occurrence
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Is a collection of environmental parameters influencing disease occurrence

Disease surveillance primarily focuses on health data (morbidity/mortality) rather than general environmental parameters (like rainfall, temperature), although these may be correlated later. The core definition is "ongoing systematic collection, analysis, and interpretation of health data".

38.
Which of the following statements best describes Malnutrition?
a) Lack of enough food in the body
b) Lack of Vitamin D
c) Low amount of sugar levels in the body
d) Lack of sufficient nutrients in the body
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Lack of sufficient nutrients in the body

Malnutrition refers to deficiencies, excesses, or imbalances in a person's intake of energy and/or nutrients. "Lack of sufficient nutrients" covers undernutrition effectively.

39.
Chronic diseases are generally caused by the following except;
a) Immunization
b) Lifestyle
c) Environmental factor
d) Genetic factors
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Immunization

Immunization prevents infectious diseases. It does not cause chronic diseases. Lifestyle, Environment, and Genetics are the main determinants of NCDs.

40.
Qualitative Research;
a) Involves description
b) Relies on principles of verifiability
c) Scenarios are artificial
d) Main focus is on substantiation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Involves description

Qualitative research focuses on understanding concepts, thoughts, or experiences via description (words), whereas quantitative research focuses on statistics and numbers.

Section 2: Short Answer Questions

40 Marks
41.
The weight of babies in the MCH on a given day was found to be; 3, 4, 5, 4, 2, 4, 5 and 3.
a) Calculate the Mean weight
b) Calculate the Variance
Show Answer

Answer:

Data set: 3, 4, 5, 4, 2, 4, 5, 3 (n = 8)

a) Mean ($\bar{x}$) calculation:

Sum ($\Sigma x$) = $3 + 4 + 5 + 4 + 2 + 4 + 5 + 3 = 30$

Mean = $\frac{\Sigma x}{n} = \frac{30}{8} = 3.75$

b) Variance ($s^2$) calculation:

Formula: $s^2 = \frac{\Sigma(x - \bar{x})^2}{n-1}$

  • $(3 - 3.75)^2 = 0.5625$
  • $(4 - 3.75)^2 = 0.0625$
  • $(5 - 3.75)^2 = 1.5625$
  • $(4 - 3.75)^2 = 0.0625$
  • $(2 - 3.75)^2 = 3.0625$
  • $(4 - 3.75)^2 = 0.0625$
  • $(5 - 3.75)^2 = 1.5625$
  • $(3 - 3.75)^2 = 0.5625$

Sum of squares ($\Sigma(x - \bar{x})^2$) = $0.5625 + 0.0625 + 1.5625 + 0.0625 + 3.0625 + 0.0625 + 1.5625 + 0.5625 = 7.5$

Variance ($s^2$) = $\frac{7.5}{8-1} = \frac{7.5}{7} \approx 1.07$

(Note: If population variance is required: $7.5 / 8 = 0.9375$)

42.
Explain five (5) factors affecting quality implementation of Universal Health Care
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Health Financing: Inadequate funding or inefficient allocation of resources affects service availability.
  2. Human Resources for Health: Shortage, maldistribution, or lack of motivation of health workers.
  3. Infrastructure and Equipment: Lack of essential medical equipment and poor facility conditions.
  4. Supply Chain Management: Frequent stock-outs of essential medicines and commodities.
  5. Leadership and Governance: Corruption, poor management, and lack of political will.
43.
Explain at least five (5) methods of waste disposal.
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Incineration: Burning waste at high temperatures (suitable for infectious waste).
  2. Sanitary Landfill: Controlled burial of waste with soil cover to prevent environmental contamination.
  3. Composting: Biological decomposition of organic waste into manure.
  4. Recycling: Processing waste materials (glass, plastic) into new products.
  5. Controlled Tipping/Dumping: Depositing waste in prepared pits/areas (less safe than landfills).
44.
Using examples, describe the various methods for vector control.
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Chemical Control: Using insecticides e.g., Indoor Residual Spraying (IRS) for mosquitoes.
  • Biological Control: Using natural enemies e.g., Gambusia fish to eat mosquito larvae.
  • Environmental Management: Modifying the environment e.g., draining stagnant water to prevent mosquito breeding.
  • Personal Protection/Barriers: Using Insecticide Treated Nets (ITNs) or screening windows.
  • Genetic Control: Releasing sterile male insects to reduce reproduction rates.
45.
Mr. Peter has been working in a quarry for the last 20 years.
a) Which disease (condition) is he likely to suffer from in the long run?
b) What measures will you institute to protect Peter against occupational related diseases?
Show Answer

Answer:

a) Likely Condition: Silicosis (a form of pneumoconiosis caused by inhaling silica dust).

b) Protective Measures:

  • PPEs: Provide and enforce the use of dust masks/respirators.
  • Engineering Controls: Wet drilling to suppress dust.
  • Administrative Controls: Job rotation to reduce exposure time.
  • Medical Surveillance: Regular chest X-rays and lung function tests.
  • Health Education: Training on the risks of dust exposure.
46.
Obesity is a health risk in Kenya.
a) What four (4) factors contribute to obesity?
b) Outline three (3) complications of obesity.
Show Answer

Answer:

a) Contributing Factors:

  1. Unhealthy Diet: High intake of processed, sugary, and fatty foods.
  2. Physical Inactivity: Sedentary lifestyle.
  3. Genetics: Family history of obesity.
  4. Socio-economic factors: Urbanization and availability of fast food.

b) Complications:

  1. Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus.
  2. Hypertension (High Blood Pressure).
  3. Cardiovascular diseases (Heart disease, Stroke).
  4. Osteoarthritis (Joint problems).
47.
Outline five (5) methods of food preservation.
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Drying/Dehydration: Removing moisture (e.g., sun drying grains).
  2. Salting/Curing: Using salt to draw out moisture and inhibit bacteria (e.g., meat/fish).
  3. Freezing/Refrigeration: Low temperatures to slow bacterial growth.
  4. Smoking: Using smoke to preserve and flavor meat/fish.
  5. Canning: Sealing in sterile containers.
  6. Chemical preservatives: Adding substances like sodium benzoate.
48.
Alcoholism develops gradually over time to heavy drinkers. Explain any five (5) risk factors that can lead to alcoholism.
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Peer Pressure: Influence from friends and social circles.
  2. Stress/Psychological factors: Using alcohol to cope with anxiety, depression, or trauma.
  3. Genetics/Family History: Biological predisposition to addiction.
  4. Availability and Cost: Easy access to cheap alcohol.
  5. Early Age of Onset: Starting drinking at a young age increases addiction risk.

Section 3: Long Answer Question

20 Marks
49.
The country has been experiencing a number of disasters in the recent past.
a) Explain what you understand by the term disaster.
b) Classify disasters and give three (3) examples of each class
c) Outline the four (4) disaster management phases.
Show Answer

Answer:

a) Definition of Disaster:

A disaster is a sudden, calamitous event that seriously disrupts the functioning of a community or society and causes human, material, and economic or environmental losses that exceed the community's or society's ability to cope using its own resources.

b) Classification of Disasters:

Disasters are generally classified into two main categories:

  • 1. Natural Disasters: Caused by natural forces.
    • Floods
    • Earthquakes
    • Droughts
    • Landslides
  • 2. Man-made (Anthropogenic) Disasters: Caused by human activities.
    • Terrorist attacks / Bomb blasts
    • Road Traffic Accidents (Major crashes)
    • Fires (Arson or industrial)
    • Chemical spills / Industrial accidents

c) Disaster Management Phases:

  1. Mitigation: Measures taken to minimize the effects of a disaster before it occurs (e.g., building codes, zoning, vaccination).
  2. Preparedness: Planning how to respond (e.g., training, stockpiling supplies, early warning systems).
  3. Response: Immediate actions taken during or immediately after the disaster to save lives (e.g., search and rescue, first aid).
  4. Recovery: Actions taken to return the community to normal or better (e.g., reconstruction, rehabilitation).
