Maternal & Child Health (MCH) and Nutrition

Maternal and Child Health

Goal: Understand the concept of facility and community based maternal and child health care

Specific objectives:

  • Outline the goal and focus of MCH and family planning
  • Describe MCH and family planning services
  • Describe community base approach to MCH and FP

Introduction

Maternal health is the health of women during pregnancy, childbirth, and the postpartum period. It encompasses the health care dimensions of family planning, preconception, prenatal, and postnatal care in order to reduce maternal morbidity and mortality.

MCH Services

The services offered in MCH clinic are as follows:

  • Focused antenatal care.
  • Targeted post natal care.
  • Providing all family planning methods: short term, long term, permanent i.e. vasectomy & tubal ligation.
  • Screening of Breast and Cervical cancer in all women of reproductive ages.
  • Giving immunization i.e. BCG, Pentavalent, Polio, Measles.
  • Growth monitoring for children under the age of five.
  • Counseling and testing of antenatal mothers and partners on PMTCT - eMTC
  • Create awareness through Micro teaching on various topics i.e. Nutrition, Breast feeding, Weaning etc.

Family Planning and Contraceptive Methods

The choice of one method of contraception against the other depend on: individual medical condition, taste of an individual or partner. Type of family planning and contraceptives can largely be divided into four categories:

  • Natural Methods: Withdrawal, Safe Period (Rhythm), Breastfeeding Amenorrhea, Cervical Mucus, Basal Body Temperature.
  • Barrier Methods: Male and Female Condoms, Spermicides, Diaphragms, Cervical Caps.
  • Hormonal Methods: Emergency contraception (e-pills), Ordinary pills, Injectable contraceptives, Contraceptive Implants, Hormone-releasing intrauterine systems.
  • Voluntary Surgical Contraception: Tubal Ligation, Vasectomy.

Nutrition and Health

Goal: Increase understanding on nutrition in the context of community health

Specific objectives:

  • Describe nutrition and dietary requirements in our lives
  • Describe the implications of malnutrition in health and development

Nutrition is the intake of food, considered in relation to the body's dietary needs. Good nutrition is an adequate, well balanced diet combined with regular physical activity.

Malnutrition is the condition that develops when the body does not get the right amount of the vitamins, minerals, and other nutrients it needs to maintain healthy tissues and organ function.

Malnutrition

Individuals are malnourished, or suffer from under nutrition if their diet does not provide them with adequate calories and protein for maintenance and growth, or they cannot fully utilize the food they eat due to illness. Growth failure malnutrition can take three serious types:

  • Acute malnutrition or wasting arises out of sudden, drastic weight loss. It leads to three clinical malnutrition types:
    • Marasmus: this occurs when body fat and tissues degenerate at an alarming rate to compensate for the lack of nutrients.
    • Kwashiorkor: this is characterized by bilateral pitting oedema (fluid retention) in the legs and feet.
    • Marasmic-kwashiorkor: This is characterized by both severe wasting and oedema.
  • Chronic malnutrition or stunting happens over a long period of time and has more long-lasting consequences.
  • Micronutrient malnutrition implies a moderate to severe lack of Vitamins A, B, C and D, Calcium, Folate, Iodine, Iron, Zinc and Selenium.

Impact of Malnutrition on Health and Development

Malnutrition and starvation have devastating impact on children, adults and especially on pregnant women. They also have severe and far-reaching socio-economic impacts.

  • Effects on children - Mental retardation, stunted growth, poor immune system, micronutrient deficiency, GI tract infections, anemia and inevitably - death.
  • Effects on pregnant women - Besides the health of the child being poor, a pregnant woman might have difficult labour, postpartum hemorrhage and anemia.
  • General effects - Weak immunity, inactivity of muscles, apathy, depression, and kidney function impairment.
  • Long-Term Implications: The short-term implications of malnutrition eventually give way to long-term complications, such as growth and cognitive delays including attention deficit disorder, learning disabilities, and decreased IQ scores.

Field Practice Exercise

Group 1: Surveillance of food chain
Develop a food contamination monitoring questionnaire and use it to assess foodstuff in a local market.

Group 2: Nutritional epidemiology
Develop a food frequency assessment tool and use it to assess the food frequency among pupils attending a local primary school.

Group 3: Clinical nutrition and diabetes
Capture the number of diabetes cases in a nearby hospital for 5 months and assess nutritional therapy in 10 randomized patient notes.

Group 4: Nutrition education
Use a suitable communication strategy to sensitize 5 mothers with children having malnutrition related conditions in a pediatric ward.

Group 5: Food and nutrition policy
Visit a nearby hospital, identify nutrition policy documents and explain their content.

Group 6: Food industry and standard agency
Visit a supermarket, sample 10 different foodstuffs to identify the manufacturer, ingredients, and Kenya Bureau of Standards certification.

Group 7: Mass catering
Visit a local MTC rationing store, identify supplies, quantities, and describe the documentation process.
