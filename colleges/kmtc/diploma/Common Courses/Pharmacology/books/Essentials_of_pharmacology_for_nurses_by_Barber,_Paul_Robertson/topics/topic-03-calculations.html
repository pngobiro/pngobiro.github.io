Drug calculations and numeracy skills

üìÑ Clinical Skills ‚è±Ô∏è 25 min read
üéØ Learning Objectives

After studying this chapter you should be able to:

  • Understand the importance of numeracy skills in nursing.
  • Be aware of the International System of Units (SI) units of number and be able to convert from one unit to another.
  • Be able to perform basic mathematics tasks of addition, subtraction, multiplication and division.
  • Use basic mathematics to calculate simple drug dosages.
01

Introduction

An important part of the nurse's role is the need to ensure that medicines dosages are calculated and administered appropriately. That is why it is essential that the nurse has a good knowledge and understanding of basic mathematical and arithmetic skills in relation to drug calculation.

The aim of this chapter is to introduce the basic principles of numeracy and the specifics of calculating drug dosages. We will cover basic arithmetic principles and use worked examples to illustrate each area to help develop your knowledge and understanding. Where appropriate we will refer you to other texts or sources of information to augment your skills.

02

An introduction to number

It is important that you are familiar with the structure of numbers and how they are expressed in written form. Going back to basics and starting from simple principles will help you develop your knowledge as we move into the area of drug calculation.

Number format and the way numbers are written and expressed are important. We are all familiar with numbers but how many of us think about the written format and what it means to us when we perform calculations?

Number order

The order in which a digit is placed within a written number tells us the value of that digit. If we look at single digits we call these units and they contain the numbers 0 to 9. They form the basis of all the numbers we can make. Look at Box 3.1 to see how numbers are divided into places within an individual number.

üìä Box 3.1 Order of numbers
Hundreds of thousands Tens of thousands Thousands Hundreds Tens Units
$100000-999999$ $10000-99999$ $1000-9999$ $100-999$ $10-99$ $0-9$

So if we look at some random numbers we can work out the value of each digit within the number:

  • 17 contains the digits 1 (tens column) and 7 (units column).
  • 183 contains the digits 1 (hundreds column), 8 (tens column) and 3 (units column).
  • 4567 contains the digits 4 (thousands column), 5 (hundreds column), 6 (tens column) and 7 (units column).

The same principles can be applied to numbers that contain digits after the decimal point, as shown in Box 3.2.

üìä Box 3.2 Order of numbers after the decimal point

So again if we look at some numbers we can work out the value of each digit within the number:

  • 1.2 contains the digits 1 (units column) and 2 (tenths column).
  • 2.75 contains the digits 2 (units column), 7 (tenths column) and 5 (hundredths column).
  • 3.825 contains the digits 3 (units column), 8 (tenths column), 2 (hundredths column) and 5 (thousandths column).
03

Basic arithmetic skills

These basic skills form the foundation of all drug calculations and so are an important place for us to begin. The processes involved are addition, subtraction, multiplication and division.

üßÆ Box 3.3 Mathematical symbol and notation
Mathematical symbol(s) What it means When to use
+ Plus or add Calculations requiring addition
- Minus or subtract Calculations requiring subtraction
$\times$ or $*$ Multiply or times Calculations requiring multiplication
$\div$ or $/$ Divide Calculations requiring division
$=$ Equals All calculations

Addition

Addition is the process of taking two or more numbers and combining them to make a new number. It is a simple process but often used to work out how many tablets a patient needs to take at any one time.

Example: Jane is on a medication A for her heart and she takes 1 tablet in the morning. She is also on a medication B for her blood pressure, she takes 2 tablets for that in the morning. Finally, she is on a medication C for pain and takes 2 tablets for that in the morning.

$$ 1 + 2 + 2 = 5 $$

This means Jane takes five tablets every morning.

Subtraction

Subtraction is the process of taking one or more numbers from a starting number to make a new number. Again this is a simple process but often used to work out how long a bottle of medication may last.

Example: John has a 100 ml bottle of medication. He knows he need to take 10 ml per day and he has already had 4 days' worth from it, so has used 40 ml. He is going away for 7 days and knows he needs 70 ml to last him the whole time he is away. He needs to calculate how much is left in his current bottle.

$$ 100 - 40 = 60 \mathrm{ml} $$

That means John does not have enough medication and must obtain more.

Multiplication

Multiplication is the process of taking one or more numbers and combining them to make a new number called a product. As we often give drugs many 'times' a day we can use multiplication to calculate the total amount of medicine a person needs in a day.

Example: Francine needs her medication 4 times per day. Each time she takes it she needs to have 2 tablets. How many does she take in a day?

$$ 4 \times 2 = 8 \text{ tablets per day} $$

Division

This is the opposite of multiplication where we take one number and divide it by another to create a new number. If we have calculated a total dose a patient has to receive over a day in more than one dose, we can use division to find out how much each individual dose should be.

Example: Sam requires 100 mg of medication to be given today, but he must have the 100 mg split into 4 equal doses. How many mg for each dose?

$$ 100 / 4 = 25 \mathrm{mg} \text{ for each dose.} $$
04

Basic introduction to units and conversions

Before performing many drug calculations you will need to convert the numbers you use in the calculation so that they all appear in the same units. You should be familiar with the units in Box 3.4 as they are the most common in drug calculation.

‚öñÔ∏è Box 3.4 Equivalencies of weight
UNIT SYMBOL EQUIVALENT SYMBOL
1 kilogram kg 1000 grams g
1 gram g 1000 milligrams mg
1 milligram mg 1000 micrograms mcg or $\mu \mathrm{g}$
1 microgram mcg 1000 nanograms ng

To convert from a smaller unit (e.g. gram) to a larger unit (e.g. kilogram), divide by 1000:

$$ 5000 \mathrm{~g} \div 1000 = 5 \mathrm{~kg} $$

To convert from a larger unit (e.g. gram) to a smaller unit (e.g. milligram), multiply by 1000:

$$ 5 \mathrm{~g} \times 1000 = 5000 \mathrm{mg} $$
05

Basic formula

There is more than one way to do a drug calculation, but the simplest and most widely used method is the basic formula shown here:

$$ \frac{\mathrm{D} \times \mathrm{Q}}{\mathrm{H}} = \mathrm{X} $$

Where:

  • D = desired dose
  • H = strength available (Have)
  • Q = quantity or unit of measure (for tablets Q = 1, for liquids Q can vary)
  • X = dose to be determined/amount to be given

The steps for using the basic formula method are as follows:

  1. 1First, memorize the formula.
  2. 2Remember to convert all the units to same system and size.
  3. 3Place all of the unit information into the correct position within the basic formula.
  4. 4Calculate your answer.
  5. 5Label all answers (e.g. tablets, capsules, millilitres).
06

Solid dose calculations

Much of the drug calculations you will perform are solid dose calculations, as tablets and capsules are by far the largest area of prescribed medication.

Remember that, when using the basic formula equation for solid dose drugs, Q will always $= 1$.

Example 1

Scenario: The doctor orders a dose of 0.05 g [corrected from text 'mg' for mathematical accuracy] to be given orally. You have 25 mg tablets. How many tablets should you give?

Step 1: Convert units
$0.05 \text{ g} \times 1000 = 50 \text{ mg}$

Step 2: Apply formula
$$ D=50, H=25, Q=1 $$ $$ \frac{50}{25} \times 1 = 2 $$

Answer: 2 tablets

Example 2

Scenario: Your patient needs 375 mg of Drug B. 75 mg tablets are available. How many tablets will you give?

$$ D=375, H=75, Q=1 $$ $$ \frac{375}{75} \times 1 = 5 $$

Answer: 5 tablets

07

Liquid dose calculations

Many medications are available in liquid form, whether this be oral or injectable liquids. The principles are the same for each. Remember that, when using the basic formula equation for liquid dose drugs, Q will vary.

Example 1

Scenario: Your patient needs 125 mg of Drug A by subcutaneous (SC) injection. 50 mg in 1 ml of liquid for SC injection is available.

$$ D=125, H=50, Q=1 $$ $$ \frac{125}{50} \times 1 = 2.5 $$

Answer: 2.5 ml

Example 2

Scenario: Your patient needs 150 mg of Drug B orally. 125 mg in 5 ml of Syrup is available.

$$ D=150, H=125, Q=5 $$ $$ \frac{150}{125} \times 5 = 6 $$

Answer: 6 ml

08

Bolus doses and calculations by weight

Bolus doses and time

Sometimes we have to give bolus doses, or a one-off dose. We need to calculate this and very often these doses cannot be given in an instant, such as swallowing a tablet, and must be infused over a period of time.

Example

Scenario: Your patient requires a bolus dose of 2 g of medication. You have 500 mg in 5 ml solution for injection available. The drug can be given at a rate of 1 ml every minute.

Step 1: Unit conversion
$2 \times 1000 = 2000 \text{ mg}$

Step 2: Calculate volume
$$ \frac{2000}{500} \times 5 = 20 \text{ ml} $$

Step 3: Calculate time
$20 \text{ ml} \times 1 \text{ min/ml} = 20 \text{ minutes}$

Drug calculations by weight

Some medications require us to calculate the dose based on a patients weight. These drugs are prescribed on a mg per kg basis and therefore we must obtain the patients weight in kg in an accurate manner.

Example

Scenario: Your patient requires their medicine at a dose of $60 \mathrm{mg} / \mathrm{kg} /$ day. They weigh 12 kg. The dose must be given in 4 divided doses.

Step 1: Calculate daily total
$60 \times 12 = 720 \mathrm{mg} \text{ per day}$

Step 2: Divide for single dose
$720 / 4 = 180 \mathrm{mg} \text{ per dose}$

09

Case studies

Case Study 1

James Jones is 4 years old and has a fever. His doctor has prescribed paracetamol to help reduce his temperature and wants it given at a dose of 60 mg per kg per day, but it must be divided into 4 doses and not given all at once. James has been weighed on admission and he is 16 kg. Work out how much paracetamol he can have per day and how much he can have if this has to be divided into 4 doses.

Looking back on the chapter on numeracy and calculation, outline which mathematical processes you have had to use and which important areas of drug calculation you have performed. Reflect on the importance of getting this calculation correct.

Case Study 2

Helen Taylor is admitted for surgery and bolus dose of antibiotic before theatre. She is prescribed 300 mg of Ciprofloxacin by intravenous infusion over 60 minutes. The dose has to be prepared and you have a 200 ml bottle containing 2 mg per ml. How many ml from the bottle is required to give 300 mg and how much will be left in the bottle? If this dose is to be given as prescribed, what rate do you require to ensure that it is delivered over 60 minutes?
