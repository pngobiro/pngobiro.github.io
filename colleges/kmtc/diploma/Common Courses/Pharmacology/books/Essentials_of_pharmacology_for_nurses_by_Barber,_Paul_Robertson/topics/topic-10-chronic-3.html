Learning Objectives

This chapter covers four broad sections. After studying this chapter you should be able to:

  • Hypertension: Explain the mode of action of medicines used in treating raised blood pressure and relate these to physiology.
  • Parkinson's Disease: Describe the basic pathology and the mode of action of medicines used in managing this condition (e.g., Levodopa).
  • Epilepsy: Categorize seizures and explain the mechanisms of anticonvulsants (Sodium channel inhibition, GABA enhancement).
  • Comorbidities: Discuss the implications of polypharmacy and multiple chronic diseases.

Introduction

This chapter concludes the section on long-term conditions. While patients may present with isolated conditions such as epilepsy or hypertension, these often co-exist, particularly in the elderly (e.g., Parkinson's disease). This chapter also addresses the complexities of polypharmacy.

01

Hypertension

Hypertension (high blood pressure) is a "silent killer" often asymptomatic until routine screening. It increases the risk of stroke, myocardial infarction, and renal disease. It leads to ventricular hypertrophy (enlargement of the heart) and atherosclerosis (fatty plaques in vessels).

üí° Non-pharmacological Treatment

Before commencing medicines, weight reduction, salt restriction, alcohol moderation, smoking cessation, and moderate exercise are recommended.

Pharmacological Management

1. Thiazide Diuretics

Example: Bendroflumethiazide.

Action: Stops sodium reabsorption in the kidney, reducing blood volume and causing vasodilation.

Side-effects: Gout (increases uric acid), potassium depletion, hyperglycaemia, and potential interactions with lithium.

2. Beta-adrenoreceptor Antagonists (Beta Blockers)

Example: Atenolol (selective), Propranolol (non-selective).

Action: Reduces cardiac output, renin production, and stress hormone release.

‚ö†Ô∏è Important Note

Beta blockers inhibit sympathetic responses and may mask signs of hypoglycaemia in diabetic patients. They are generally contraindicated in asthma (especially non-selective types) and peripheral vascular disease (cold hands/feet).

3. ACE Inhibitors

Example: Captopril, Lisinopril, Ramipril (ending in '-pril').

Action: Prevents conversion of Angiotensin I to Angiotensin II (a powerful vasoconstrictor).

Side-effects: Dry cough (common), hypotension after first dose (take at night), renal failure in renal artery stenosis.

4. Calcium Antagonists

Example: Nifedipine, Amlodipine.

Action: Limits calcium entry into smooth muscle, causing vasodilation.

Side-effects: Flushing, headache, ankle swelling.

02

Parkinson's Disease

A progressive disorder caused by the degeneration of neurones in the basal nuclei, leading to a lack of dopamine. Symptoms (tremor, rigidity, bradykinesia) appear when dopamine levels fall below 20-40% of normal.

Levodopa

Mechanism: Dopamine cannot cross the Blood-Brain Barrier (BBB). Levodopa crosses the BBB and is converted to dopamine. It is combined with Carbidopa or Benserazide to prevent peripheral breakdown.

Side-effects:

  • Dyskinesia: Involuntary movements.
  • On-off effect: Sudden cessation of drug effect.
  • Psychosis: Delusions/hallucinations due to high dopamine.

Other Agents

Selegiline: Inhibits Monoamine Oxidase-B (enzyme that breaks down dopamine).

Dopamine Agonists: (e.g., Bromocriptine) Mimic dopamine action.

Anticholinergics: (e.g., Benzatropine) Restore balance between dopamine and acetylcholine. Used less frequently due to side effects (glaucoma, urinary retention).

03

Epilepsy

Epilepsy results from an imbalance of excitatory and inhibitory mechanisms. Seizures are classified as:

  • Partial: Localized (e.g., Jacksonian twitching, Temporal lobe complex seizures).
  • Generalized: Whole brain involved. Includes Absence (petit mal) and Tonic Clonic (grand mal).

Anticonvulsant Mechanisms

‚ñ∂ Inhibition of Sodium Channels

Drug: Phenytoin

Dampens high-frequency impulses. Has enzyme induction properties (speeds up metabolism of other drugs like anticoagulants). Requires blood monitoring (therapeutic range 40-100 micromoles/L).

Side-effects: Gum hyperplasia, ataxia, nystagmus, teratogenicity (cleft palate).

‚ñ∂ Inhibition of Calcium Channels

Drug: Ethosuximide

First-line treatment for absence seizures. Works on the thalamus.

‚ñ∂ Enhancement of GABA

Drug: Sodium Valproate

Prevents breakdown of GABA (inhibitory neurotransmitter). Relatively free of side effects but can cause hepatotoxicity and is a teratogen (spina bifida risk).

Drug: Vigabatrin

Irreversibly blocks GABA transaminase. Side effects include depression and visual field problems.

04

Multiple Comorbidities & Polypharmacy

Polypharmacy is generally accepted as taking four or more medications. It is prevalent in the elderly (40% of older adults at home). Risks include increased adverse drug reactions and interactions.

‚ö° Risk Factors

Common drug categories involved in adverse reactions include cardiovascular agents, antibiotics, diuretics, anticoagulants, hypoglycemics, and NSAIDs. Patients may also use OTC medicines or herbal remedies without informing professionals.

Case Studies

1. Joseph Olita (Asthma)

Scenario: 30-year-old man with uncontrolled asthma suspected of poor inhaler technique.

Discussion: Reinforce technique (cap off, shake, breathe out, seal lips, breathe in slow/deep while pressing, hold breath 10s). Explain Salbutamol is a bronchodilator (opener) and Steroids reduce inflammation (swelling). Mention rinsing mouth to prevent thrush.

2. Elizabeth Jones (Asthma)

Scenario: 11-year-old embarrassed to use inhalers at school.

Discussion: Explain the preventative nature of steroids‚Äîthey must be taken to stop the airways twitching. Salbutamol prevents severe attacks. Address self-esteem; perhaps use a different device if possible or private area.

3. Tom McBrayer (Hypertension)

Scenario: 55-year-old with BP 185/95 mmHg, starting Perindopril (ACE inhibitor).

Discussion: ACE inhibitors stop Angiotensin I converting to Angiotensin II (a vasoconstrictor). This opens vessels to lower BP. Warn about the "first dose hypotension" (take at night initially) and the potential dry cough.

4. Eric Vardey (Parkinson's)

Scenario: 55-year-old with worsening tremors/rigidity taking Co-careldopa.

Discussion: Levodopa converts to dopamine in the brain. Carbidopa prevents breakdown in the body. Side effects to watch for: Nausea, postural hypotension, and potential psychological changes or dyskinesia.

5. Younis Khan (Epilepsy/Diabetes)

Scenario: Type 1 Diabetic prescribed Phenytoin. Current meds: Insulin Glargine, Fluvastatin, Aspirin.

Discussion: Phenytoin induces liver enzymes. Check BNF. Insulin: safe. Aspirin: safe. Fluvastatin: Interaction risk (lipid regulators). Monitor closely.
