Pharmacokinetics: Timing & Availability

Onset of action:

Reefers to the time interval between administration and notation of the first therapeutic effect. It depends on

  1. 1The route of administration.
  2. 2The characteristics of the drug.
  3. 3The drug‚Äôs rate of absorption through various membranes.
  4. 4The formulation of the dose.
üìùN.B.

The onset of action is especially variable after oral administration depending on the presence of food in the stomach, the motility of the GI tract and other factors.

Peak of activity:-

When the drug reaches its maximum effect (coincides often with peak serum concentration). Many drugs cause this peak to surpass the optimally effective level but the concentration can fall rapidly below this level as a result of biotransformation and excretion. This drop occurs especially often when a short acting drug is given initially or intermittently.

Example: In treatment of diabetes, insulin with various lengths of action are mixed to keep insulin levels at a therapeutically effective level around the clock.

Duration:

is the period between the administration of the drug and the excretion of the entire dose of that drug or its metabolites.

Bioavailability:-

It measures the concentration of the pharmacologically active substance at the target site and / or in the serum.

It is the function of:

  1. 1The drug itself.
  2. 2The metabolism of the patient.
  3. 3The rate at which the drug is liberated from its dosage form or from storage in the body.

Example: many drugs bind to serum protein (albumin) from which they released gradually, others stored at specific site as bone (tetracycline).

Drug isn‚Äôt to be bioavilable if:

  1. 1Bound to protein or to any other substance that makes the drug permanently or temporarily inactive.
  2. 2Not released from its dosage form or site of administration.
  3. 3Partially or totally degraded.

Factors that influence drug dosage and action

It is important for the nurse to be oriented of the characteristic that modify cell conditions and therefore modify the activity of a drug. These characteristics include the following: -

1- Age:

  • ‚Ä¢Children and elderly persons are highly responsive to drug.
  • ‚Ä¢Infants often have immature hepatic and renal systems and therefore incomplete metabolic & excretory mechanisms.
‚ö†Ô∏èN.B.

Aged individuals may demonstrate different responses to drug therapy because of deterioration of hepatic and renal function which is often accompanied by concurrent disease process such as C.V.D. (cardiovascular disease).

2- Weight:

  • ‚Ä¢The greater the weight, the greater must be the dose.
  • ‚Ä¢However, body weight due to fat or edema fluid should not be taken into consideration.
  • ‚Ä¢For very lean and very obese individuals, drug dosage is frequently determined on the basis of drug/kg of body weight or body surface area.

3- Sex:

  • ‚Ä¢Females don‚Äôt always respond to the action of drug in the same manner as do men.
  • ‚Ä¢Women are usually smaller than men, which lead to high drug concentration if dosage is prescribed indifferently.
  • ‚Ä¢Female‚Äôs body is composed of higher % of adipose tissue than males, absorption rate of drug are slower in fatty tissue than in skeletal muscle, so the effect of drug will be more pronounced and prolonged.
üö´N.B.: During pregnancy, lactation, and menstruation, many drugs are stopped:
  1. 1Aspirin: not used during menstruation as it increase blood fluidity.
  2. 2Drugs excreted in milk aren‚Äôt given during lactation as penicillin.
  3. 3Uterine stimulant should be avoided during pregnancy as they may produce abortion such as prostaglandin.
  4. 4Drugs that might affect fetus and placenta should not be given during pregnancy.

4- Time of administration:

  • ‚Ä¢Drug is more rapidly absorbed when the GIT is free of food, while irritating drugs are more tolerated if there is food in stomach.
  • ‚Ä¢Body resistant to drug is generally greater in the early morning when the body is at its lowest point of physiologic functioning and conversely, the body is more sensitive to drugs effect during time of maximal activity.

5- Pathologic state :-

  • ‚Ä¢Diseases alter the functional activity and accordingly its response to drug. e.g. sever pain tends to increase patient‚Äôs requirement to opiates.
  • ‚Ä¢The presence of circulatory, hepatic and/ or renal dysfunction will interfere with the physiologic process of drug action.

6- Environmental Milieu:

Drugs affecting mood & behavior are particularly susceptible to the influence of the patient‚Äôs environment.

With such drugs one has to consider effects in light of 4 FACTORS:-

  1. 1The drug itself.
  2. 2The personality of the user.
  3. 3The environment of the user.
  4. 4The interaction of these 3 components.

5) Heat relaxes peripheral blood vessels while cold has the opposite effect.

7- Genetic factors.

Channels of Administration

1- The ORAL route: (P.O.)

- The first and most convenient method is the oral rout i.e. the patient usually swallows the drug, which then enters the gastrointestinal tract and absorbed from that area.

Advantages:-

  • ‚úìThis route accounts for 80% of all medications administered to patients.
  • ‚úìDoctors usually decide the amount and frequency and the patient administer the drug to himself.
  • ‚úìThe cost of medication and therapy is low.
  • ‚úìOral administration is painless and there is no necessity to practice sterile technique.

Disadvantages:

  • ‚úóInconsistent absorption from the gastrointestinal tract.
  • ‚úóBlood level may vary among different patients because of different GI characteristics e.g. acidity, gastric motility & intestinal mucosa.
  • ‚úóDrug irritation that causes nausea & vomiting.
  • ‚úóUnconscious patient & vomiting patients can‚Äôt be given the drug by this route.

2- Parenteral Route:

- The next most popular route of drug administration.

- The word parenteral indicates that the drug is administered by a route other than enteral route (GI).

- Common agreement has come to indicate the injectable route.

Advantages:

  • ‚úìDrugs that cannot be given through GIT, can be given by parenteraly.
  • ‚úìEliminate the unpredictability of absorption from GIT i.e. the amount of drug will be delivered to the patient from injection site.
  • ‚úìMedication can be given to an unconscious patient.

Disadvantages:

  • ‚úóNeed sterile technique.
  • ‚úóMore expensive than oral preparations.
  • ‚úóPatient usually not able to medicate himself especially when medication needs to be injected deep into a muscle.
  • ‚úóAccidental penetration of blood vessel, especially if medication is not suitable for I.V. administration (complication).

There are a number of techniques for administering drugs by injection, these are related to the target area of the injection:

A. Subcutaneous Injection:-

SC injection is administration of a drug into the subcutaneous tissues.

- Slowly drug absorption (advantage)

- Disadvantages:

  1. 1The most painful route.
  2. 2Slow rate of absorption.
  3. 3Not effective in emergency (poor blood supply).
‚ÑπÔ∏èN.B.

Drug absorption from SC injection can be reduced by:

  • Addition of vasoconstrictor e.g. epinephrine.
  • Application of cold packs (also causes vasoconstriction).

Heat causes vasodilatation which result in increased blood supply to the area and thus quicker drug absorption.

- This manipulation may be used to reduce the severity of toxicity from a SC injection.

A. Intramuscular Route: IM

  • ‚Ä¢Is the second most widely used route for injectable drugs.
  • ‚Ä¢Suitable for administration of both solutions and suspensions.
  • ‚Ä¢Drugs that are very water insoluble can be given IM in an oil base. (Remain for an extended period of time).
  • ‚Ä¢Has greater vascular network than SC, so drug absorption is more quickly than SC route.
  • ‚Ä¢Less painful (less nerve supply).

B. Intravenous Injection : I V

- Administering a quantity of medication through a needle into the circulation.

- 2 methods of intravenous injection are used:

  1. 1Administering the drug as a bolus.
  2. 2Continuous infusion of a drug, which is called IV drip.
Advantages:
  1. 1To achieve highly, accurate and quick blood level.
  2. 2A channel to administer drugs that irritate another sites e.g. chemotherapy.
  3. 3Less painful during drug administration.
Disadvantages:
  1. 1Accidental overdose, and there is no way to withdraw the drug from circulation once it is injected.
  2. 2Infiltrating the area around the vein i.e. needle slip out of the vein.
  3. 3Thrombophlebitis from repeated injections. To minimize this phenomena (but doesn‚Äôt exclude it), drug should be given I.V. drip.
  4. 4Inadvertent intra-arterial injection instead of a vein.
‚ö†Ô∏èN.B.:

IV solutions must be completely homogenous. No solution containing particulate matter, regardless of how fine the particles should ever be injected IV because of the danger of creating EMBLOI.

C. Intradermal (ID) and Intrathecal administration:-

  • -ID is a route of administration in which the drug is injected just below the epidermis.
  • -Injection is usually used for allergy test.
  • -Used for TB tests and for penicillin sensitivity test.
  • -Intrathecal route of administration involves inserting a needle between 2 vertebrae and injecting the drug into the CSF.
  • -Used frequently in OR when anesthesiologist desires to produce a spinal block and inject local anesthetic directly into the spinal cord. Used to administer Chemotherapy to prevent CNS metastases of some types of cancers.

D. Rectal, urethral and vaginal suppositories:

Another route of administration that is useful when:

  • -The oral route can‚Äôt be used.
  • -The physician desires not to use the injectable route or.
  • -When self-medication other than P.O. is desired, the rectal route of administration is used.

N.B.: rectal route can produce local effect or systemic effect.

Disadvantages:
  • -Erratic absorption from the rectal area result in an inaccurate predicted blood level of drug so the dosage must be adjusted.
  • -Rectal route is ineffective when the patient suffers from diarrhea.

E. Topical route:-

  • -Are intended for use at the site of administration (there are exceptions e.g. Nitroglycerin ointment that is rubbed into the skin and systemic effect is achieved.
  • -Two topical preparations are frequently confused with each other (ointment and creams).
1- Ointments:-
- Are usually made up of a petrolatum type of base.
- They are quite greasy, as they intended for prolonged contact with the skin.
- They don‚Äôt disappear or penetrate the skin, but remain on the surface.
2- Creams:-
- Made of viscous water-soluble chemicals, which usually disappear after being placed to the skin.
- Patient prefers cream to ointment, as creams don‚Äôt discolor clothing.
