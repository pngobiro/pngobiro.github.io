How do drugs work?

some drugs produce their effect, but it is possible to describe the way in which some of them act.

1- The receptor theory:

  • ‚û¢It is believed that the cells in certain tissues contain structures (called receptors). These receptors combine with substances, which are produced naturally in the body.
  • ‚û¢The drugs stimulate the receptors which will cause the cells to be stimulated.
  • ‚û¢The contraction of muscle fibers produced by acetylcholine is an example.
  • ‚û¢The drug is thought to fit into a receptor rather as a key fits into a lock. It will then stimulate the receptor and produce an effect similar to that of the naturally occurring substance or it may occupy (block) the receptor without producing any effect but preventing any naturally occurring stimulation to happen (the blocking of acetylcholine by atropine is a good example).

2- Antimetabolites:

  • ‚û¢These drugs closely resemble substances which are used by the cells for nutrition and when absorbed, the cells cannot use them and so fail to multiply.
  • ‚û¢The Sulfonamides, which are used to stop the multiplication of bacteria, are a good example.
  • ‚û¢They are very similar in structure to para-aminobenzoic acid and certain bacteria can‚Äôt distinguish between them, and absorb the sulfonamide so mulitiplication is stopped.

3- Enzyme Inhibitors:

  • ‚û¢Enzymes are substances that speed up many chemical processes within the body.
  • ‚û¢Some of these enzyme-activated processes are concerned with the transport of chemicals in and out of the cells. Certain drugs have the property of inhibiting their action and thus interfere with some of these processes.
  • ‚û¢Diuretics are a good example as normally salt and water is transported out of the renal tubule back into the body, but this action requires enzymes and if they are inhibited by a diuretic, salt and water are not reabsorbed and pass out of the kidney with a resulting diuresis.

4-Action on cell membranes:-

  • ‚û¢The function of nerves and muscles depends on ions passing across the membranes surrounding these cells.
  • ‚û¢Certain drugs interfere with movement of these ions and thus prevent nerve or muscle function as demonstrated by local anesthetics which block impulses passing up a sensory nerve.

5-Cytotoxic Effect:-

  • ‚û¢Drugs may be used to kill bacteria or malignant cells without undue damage to the patient‚Äôs cells. The way this is brought about varies between drugs.

These are just a few of the ways in which drugs may work. It is probable that all drug action depends on their interference with cell activity and when more is known about the processes within the cell, then more will be discovered about how do drugs work.

Side effects and drug toxicity

A. Drug Allergies:

  • ‚û¢Allergic responses to drugs occur in some patients and not in others.
  • ‚û¢It is an unwanted response to a drug resulting from previous exposure to that drug or one closely related to it. So drug allergy is happens only after a second or subsequent exposure to the drug.
  • ‚û¢
    Allergic reactions to drugs differ from drug toxicity in the following ways.
    1. The allergic reaction occurs in only a fraction of the population where as toxicity will occur in all individuals if the dose is high enough.
    2. The allergic response is unusual in that a small amount of unsafe drug causes a severe reaction.
    3. With allergy, the reaction is different from the usual pharmacological effect of the drug.
    4. For an allergic reaction to occur, the patient must have had a previous exposure to that drug or one closely related to it.

‚û¢There are 2 types of allergic responses:

1- Immediate reaction involving antigen and antibody resulting in the release of histamine.

  • In mild cases, the reaction is limited to urticaria (wheals and itching of the skin).
  • In severe cases (anaphylactic reaction), characterized by circulatory collapse or asphyxia due to swelling of the larynx & occlusion of the air way passages.
  • Example: many patients are allergic to penicillin.

2- Delayed reaction:

  • Occurring several days or even weeks after the drug has been administered.
  • Characterized by fever (drug fever), swelling of the joints, reaction may involves blood-forming organs and kidneys.
üöëTreatment of anaphylactic reaction

may include administration of:

  1. Epinephrine
  2. Oxygen
  3. Antihistamines
  4. Corticosteroids

B. Drug idiosyncrasies:

‚û¢Idiosyncratic reactions are defined as those reactions that occur in patients who have abnormal genes which cause this abnormal response to that drug. Reaction may be excessive and unusual.

‚û¢Example: Succinylcholine, a muscle relaxant drug, usually is broken down rapidly by enzymes in the plasma and liver so that the effects of the drug last for only a few minutes. In few patients, a normal dose of this drug produces profound muscle relaxation and suppression of respiration, which may last few hours. Those patients have a genetic defect that produces unusual enzymes & the drug is not broken down.

C. Drug Hypersensitivity:

‚û¢It occurs when the patient shows extreme sensitivity to an effect of the drug. The response is the usual pharmacological effect, however the effect is intense and exaggerated. A simple decrease in the dose may be sufficient to eliminate this type of reactions.

D. Drug toxicity:

‚û¢Excess dosage may be either accidental or intentional, results in an exaggerated response to this drug. It may be sever & may lead to respiratory depression. Cardio-vascular collapse and death may occur if the drug is not withdrawn and adequate treatment is started.

E. General side effects:

‚û¢Unpleasant or unwanted reaction to a drug is termed as side-effects.

Types of orders

‚û¢It is probably obvious, that outpatients are free to medicate themselves with any accessible medication. Many Over the Counter (OTC) drugs are available for the public without the need for a prescription.

‚û¢Once an individual is admitted to a clinical institution, usually neither the patient nor the nurse may legally administer any medication without a written order.

‚û¢Use of variant or non-standard abbreviations should be avoided because of the danger of misinterpretation.

1. Routine Order:

‚û¢The most common type of orders. It means that the drug, as ordered, is to be regularly administered until a formal discontinuation order is written, or until a specified termination date is reached.

‚û¢Automatic termination or stop may be explicit in agency policy; e.g. narcotic analgesics 3 days, Antibiotics 5 days, steroids 7 days ‚Ä¶‚Ä¶‚Ä¶

‚û¢The policies act as a stimulus to the prescriber to evaluate continued need for these drugs that require especial close attention.

2. Single Order:

‚û¢Drug is to be administered once at the time indicated.

‚û¢e.g. a preoperative medication (Atropine) 0.4 mg IM on call to OR.

3. Stat Order:

‚û¢This is a single order that a drug is to be administered immediately. Stat orders are written often for emergency when the client‚Äôs condition changes suddenly.

‚û¢Example: Give Aprisoline 10 mg IM stat.

4. prn Orders:

‚û¢These drugs are to be administered by the nurse as necessary within the order criteria specified by the doctor order. The decision of when to give the drug is left to the nurse‚Äôs judgment.

‚û¢Medications to reduce the perception of pain make up the bulk of prn orders.

‚û¢Keeping nursing assessment of the pain is required to carry out these prn orders appropriately.

5. Telephone order and verbal order:

‚û¢Usually given in emergency and should be written in the patient‚Äôs chart and assigned by the nurse who receive the order and to be signed by doctor as soon as possible.

‚û¢Example: 10:15 am, diclofine 50 mg IM stat, Phone order Dr. Ahmad Attallah to Nurse Sami Mahmoud. Then the nurse sings his/her name

General Guidelines for Handling Medications

  1. 1When preparing or giving medicines, concentrate your whole attention on what you are doing.
  2. 2Make certain that you have a written order for every medication you will administer.
  3. 3Read the label before taking, while using, and returning the drug to its place.
  4. 4All medicines should be labeled and the label should be clear.
  5. 5Never give medicine from unlabeled container.
  6. 6Never give a put a drug in a bottle that has a different label. For example, if you have kefex that you need to put it in a bottle, and you have empty bottle that was used to contain ampecillin, don‚Äôt put the keflex in that bottle.
  7. 7Measure quantities and calculate doses as ordered in proper way.
  8. 8Handling tablets, capsules and pills properly and DO‚ÄôNOT touch with fingers. Use the cap of the container to guide or from the container to the cup directly.
  9. 9Avoid wastage of medicine.
  10. 10Never administer medications prepared by another person; otherwise you will bear the responsibility of any mistake.
  11. 11Some preparations as insulin, vaccines & suppositories need to be kept in the refrigerator ‚Äï2-8¬∫ C‚Äñ.
  12. 12Stay with the patient until he takes his medicine.
  13. 13All mixtures should be made immediately before use.
  14. 14NEVER return unused drug to a stock bottle.
  15. 15Don‚Äôt add any drug to the blood; interactions may occur without visible changes.
  16. 16Don‚Äôt use any sterile article that gets unsterile by any way.
  17. 17Don‚Äôt use clear solutions which have become cloudy or have sediment.
  18. 18Don‚Äôt use a drug that is out date.
  19. 19Don‚Äôt use a drug that has changes its color.
  20. 20Don‚Äôt use a drug which arise doubt in mind.
  21. 21All medicines should be kept in cupboard, NARCOTICS in a locked one.
  22. 22Narcotics have a rule controlling their ordering, giving, and their registration.
  23. 23Many liquids should be diluted with water or other liquids. ‚ÄïThis is especially when medicine has a bad taste‚Äñ.
  24. 24Exceptions to this rule, cough medicines are not diluted, or the patient not allowed drinking water after taking cough syrup.
  25. 25Don‚Äôt leave a tray of medicines unattended. If you are in a patient‚Äôs room and must leave, take the tray with you.
  26. 26Never chart a medicine as having been given, until it has been administered.
  27. 27
    Follow up the five rights
    1. Right patient.
    2. Right drug.
    3. Right dose.
    4. Right route of administration.
    5. Right time.
