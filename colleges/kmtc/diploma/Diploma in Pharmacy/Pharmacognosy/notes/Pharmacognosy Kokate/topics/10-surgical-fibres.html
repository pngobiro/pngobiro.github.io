üìñ INTRODUCTION

Fibres are elongated, thick-walled cells with pointed ends, cell walls consisting of cellulose and/or lignin. In pharmacy, fibres are used for the preparation of surgical dressings, sutures, and ligatures.

Surgical Dressings are materials used for covering, protecting, and supporting wounds. They function to prevent infection, absorb discharge (exudate), and provide mechanical support to the healing tissue.

Sutures and Ligatures are sterile threads used in surgery.

  • Suture: A thread used for sewing the edges of a wound or incision together.
  • Ligature: A thread used for tying a blood vessel to stop bleeding.

üåø VEGETABLE FIBRES

COTTON (RAW & ABSORBENT)

Synonyms: Cotton wool, Purified cotton.

Biological Source:

Cotton consists of epidermal trichomes (hairs) of the seeds of cultivated species of Gossypium herbaceum and other species of Gossypium (Family: Malvaceae), freed from impurities, fats, and sterilized.

Preparation:

The cotton fruits (balls) are collected, and the trichomes are separated from seeds by a process called ginning. The raw cotton contains impurities like wax, fat, and coloring matter (making it non-absorbent). To prepare Absorbent Cotton:

  1. Raw cotton is boiled with dilute soda ash (sodium carbonate) solution under pressure for 10-15 hours to remove cuticle, wax, and fat.
  2. It is washed with water and treated with suitable bleaching agents (chlorinated lime or hydrogen peroxide).
  3. It is washed again, dried, and carded (combed) to form flat sheets.
  4. Finally, it is sterilized using gamma radiation.

Chemical Constituents:

Raw cotton contains 90% cellulose, 7-8% moisture, and small amounts of wax/fat. Absorbent cotton is almost pure cellulose (98-99%).

Chemical Tests:

  1. Ignition Test: Burns with a flame, gives very little odor/fumes, leaves no bead, and leaves a small white ash (Distinction from animal fibres).
  2. Cuoxam Test: Dissolves in ammoniacal copper oxide solution with ballooning effect.
  3. Iodine Test: Dried cotton moistened with N/50 iodine and 80% sulfuric acid gives a blue color.

JUTE

Synonyms: Gunny.

Biological Source:

It consists of phloem fibres from the stem of various species of Corchorus; C. capsularis Linn, C. olitorius Linn (Family: Tiliaceae).

Preparation:

The fibres are separated from the stem by a process called retting (microbial decomposition of pectin). The stalks are submerged in water for 10-20 days. The bark is then peeled off, and fibres are washed and dried.

Characteristics & Constituents:

Jute is a lignified fibre. It contains cellulose (53%), hemicellulose (20%), and lignin (10-11%).

Chemical Test:

Phloroglucinol Test: Treat the fibre with phloroglucinol and conc. HCl. It stains deep red (pink) due to the presence of lignin.

HEMP & FLAX

Hemp (Cannabis)

Source: Pericyclic fibres obtained from Cannabis sativa (Family: Cannabinaceae).
Uses: Making rope, canvas, and twine.

Flax (Linen)

Source: Pericyclic fibres from the stem of Linum usitatissimum (Family: Linaceae).
Characteristics: Non-lignified, cellulosic fibres with thick walls and narrow lumen. Used for making lint and surgical thread.

üêë ANIMAL FIBRES

SILK

Biological Source:

Fibres obtained from the cocoons spun by the larvae of Bombyx mori Linn. (Family: Bombycidae/Moraceae).

Preparation:

The cocoons are heated to 60-80¬∞C to kill the pupae inside (stifling). They are then placed in hot water to soften the gum (sericin). The loose ends of fibres from several cocoons are brought together and reeled into a single thread.

Chemical Constituents:

Silk consists chiefly of the protein Fibroin (the inner fibre core) and Sericin (the outer gummy coating). Hydrolysis of fibroin yields amino acids like glycine and alanine.

Chemical Tests:

  1. Soluble in warm conc. HCl and 66% H2SO4.
  2. Millon's Test: Gives a red color (indicates protein).
  3. Does not contain sulfur (Lead acetate test negative).

WOOL

Biological Source:

Wool consists of hairs from the fleece of sheep Ovis aries Linn. (Family: Bovidae).

Preparation:

The wool is removed from the sheep (shearing), washed with alkali to remove dirt and grease (wool fat/lanolin), bleached with sulfur dioxide, and dried.

Chemical Constituents:

Wool consists of the protein Keratin, which is rich in the sulfur-containing amino acid cystine.

Chemical Tests:

  1. Solubility: Insoluble in warm HCl (distinction from silk) but soluble in warm alkali.
  2. Lead Acetate Test: When treated with lead acetate in alkaline solution, it turns black due to the presence of sulfur (formation of lead sulfide).

üß™ REGENERATED & MINERAL FIBRES

REGENERATED FIBRES

These are produced from naturally occurring polymers (like cellulose or protein) that are dissolved and re-solidified (regenerated) into fibre form.

  • Viscose Rayon: Made from cellulose (wood pulp) treated with sodium hydroxide and carbon disulfide (Xanthate process). It is chemically identical to cotton (cellulose) but differs physically (solid cylinder vs. flattened tube).
  • Fibrolane: Regenerated protein fibre made from milk casein.
  • Ardil: Regenerated protein fibre made from groundnut (peanut) protein.
  • Alginate Fibres: Made from sodium alginate (seaweed). Used as absorbable haemostatic dressings (Calcium alginate).

MINERAL & SYNTHETIC FIBRES

  • Glass Fibres: Made by melting glass (silica) and drawing it into fine threads. Used for filtering corrosive liquids.
  • Carbon Fibres: Synthetic fibres composed mostly of carbon atoms. Known for high stiffness, high tensile strength, and low weight.
  • Asbestos: Natural mineral fibre (magnesium silicate). Used previously for filtering but now restricted due to toxicity.

üßµ SURGICAL CATGUT

Definition: Surgical catgut is a sterilized fibre or strand prepared from collagen of connective tissues (submucosal layer) of the small intestine of healthy sheep (Ovis aries).

Etymology

The name "Catgut" is derived from "Kitgut" (strings for a kit or fiddle), not from cats.

Preparation Process:

  1. Cleaning: The intestine is cleaned and split longitudinally into ribbons.
  2. Removal of Layers: The inner mucosal and outer muscular/serosal layers are scraped off, leaving only the submucosa.
  3. Spinning: Several ribbons are twisted together (spun) to form a strand.
  4. Drying & Polishing: The strands are dried under tension and polished to smooth the surface.
  5. Hardening (Optional): Treated with chromium salts (Chromic Catgut) to delay absorption in the body.
  6. Sterilization: Sterilized by heating in a tube with anhydrous fluid (toluene/xylene) or by gamma radiation. It cannot be boiled in water as collagen turns to gelatin.

Types:

Type Description Absorption Time
Plain Catgut Untreated, yellowish. Rapid (absorbed in days).
Chromic Catgut Treated with chromium salts. Slow (absorbed in weeks). Used for deep tissues.
