üå± Cultivation of Medicinal Plants

The crude drugs available in the market are obtained from two sources: wild (natural) sources and cultivated sources. While wild sources were historically the primary supply, modern pharmacognosy emphasizes cultivation to ensure quality and sustainability.

Advantages of Cultivation

  • Quality Control: Cultivation ensures the purity and potency of the drug as species purity can be maintained.
  • Better Yield: Through scientific methods (irrigation, fertilizers, pest control), the yield of active constituents can be improved.
  • Regular Supply: Dependence on wild sources often leads to scarcity; cultivation guarantees a consistent market supply.
  • Industrial Processing: Cultivation allows for mechanized harvesting and processing, which is essential for large-scale pharmaceutical industries.

Water is essential for plant physiology. Continuous rain can leach water-soluble substances from leaves and roots.

Soil provides mechanical support and essential nutrients.

Fertilizers: NPK (Nitrogen, Phosphorus, Potassium) and manures (Farmyard manure, Neem cake) enrich the soil to boost active constituents.

üçÇ Collection of Crude Drugs

The quantity of active principles in a plant varies throughout the year and with the stage of development. Therefore, the "Time of Collection" is the most crucial factor in pharmacognosy. Improper collection time can result in a drug with little or no therapeutic value.

üìÖ General Rules for Collection
Plant Part Optimal Collection Time Reasoning
Leaves Flowering stage (just before or during) Photosynthetic activity is highest; maximum content of active principles (e.g., Digitalis, Senna, Belladonna).
Flowers Prior to or at the time of pollination To ensure they are fully developed but not withered (e.g., Clove buds, Saffron).
Barks Spring or early Summer Cambium is active, making the bark easy to peel and detach from the wood (e.g., Cinchona, Cinnamon).
Roots / Rhizomes Autumn (Fall) or Winter Vegetative processes cease, and food materials are stored in the roots (e.g., Rauwolfia, Ginger, Turmeric).
Fruits Depends on fruit type Fully grown but unripe (Black Pepper) or fully ripe (Fennel, Coriander).
Seeds When fully mature To ensure the embryo is developed and constituents are fixed (e.g., Nux-vomica).

Special Case: Seasonal Variation

Rhubarb: In winter, it contains anthranols (reduced form). In summer, these oxidize to anthraquinones (active form). Therefore, Rhubarb must be collected in summer to be therapeutically effective.

‚òÄÔ∏è Harvesting and Drying

Harvesting Methods

Harvesting methods vary depending on the drug type and economic considerations:

  • Hand Picking: Used for delicate drugs like leaves (Digitalis) and flowers (Saffron, Cloves). Labor-intensive but ensures quality.
  • Uprooting: Used for roots and rhizomes (Rauwolfia). The plant is dug out manually or mechanically.
  • Mechanical Harvesting: Used for aerial parts like Peppermint (mowers) or seaweeds (forks).
  • Coppicing: Cutting the plant a few centimeters above ground to allow regeneration (Cinnamon).

Drying of Crude Drugs

Drying is the most important step in preservation. Fresh drugs contain 60-90% water, which facilitates enzymatic hydrolysis and microbial growth. Drying reduces moisture content to a safe level.

Objectives of Drying:

  1. To prevent enzymatic decomposition of active constituents.
  2. To prevent the growth of bacteria and fungi (moulds).
  3. To reduce weight and bulk for easier transport.
  4. To facilitate grinding and pulverization.

‚òÄÔ∏è Natural Drying

Sun Drying: Used for drugs stable to heat and sunlight (e.g., Gum Acacia, Seeds, Fruits).

Shade Drying: Essential for drugs containing volatile oils or natural colors that fade in sunlight (e.g., Peppermint, Senna, Digitalis, Clove).

‚öôÔ∏è Artificial Drying

Tray Dryers: Controlled temperature circulation.

Vacuum Dryers: Low temperature drying for heat-sensitive drugs (e.g., Tannic acid, Digitalis).

Spray Dryers: For highly sensitive extracts (e.g., Papaya latex, Pectin).

üì¶ Preparation for Market (Garbling & Packing)

Garbling (Dressing)

Garbling is the process of removing extraneous matter and adulterants from the collected drug. This ensures the drug meets pharmacopoeial standards.

  • Removal of sand/dirt: Roots and rhizomes are washed and brushed.
  • Removal of other plant parts: Excessive stems in Lobelia, stalks in Cloves, or rootlets in Rhizomes must be removed.
  • Magnetic Separation: Removal of iron pieces (e.g., in Castor seeds before crushing).

Packing

üèöÔ∏è Storage and Preservation

Proper storage is vital to maintain the therapeutic value of crude drugs. Poor storage can lead to deterioration due to:

  • Moisture: Activates enzymes and promotes fungal growth.
  • Heat: Accelerates chemical decomposition (e.g., loss of volatile oils).
  • Air/Oxygen: Causes oxidation of fats and resins.
  • Light: Causes fading of color and decomposition of vitamins (e.g., Cod liver oil).
  • Pests: Insects (beetles, moths), nematodes, and rodents can consume or contaminate the drug. Fumigation with methyl bromide or carbon disulphide is often used.
