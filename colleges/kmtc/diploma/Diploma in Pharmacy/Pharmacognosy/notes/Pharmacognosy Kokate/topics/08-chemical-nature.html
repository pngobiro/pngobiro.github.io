üå± INTRODUCTION TO METABOLITES

Plants produce chemical compounds through their metabolic activities. These compounds are broadly classified into two categories:

  1. Primary Metabolites: Compounds essential for the growth and maintenance of the plant (e.g., Carbohydrates, Proteins, Lipids, Nucleic acids).
  2. Secondary Metabolites: Compounds derived from primary metabolites that are not essential for basic growth but play a role in defense or interaction with the environment. These are the therapeutically active principles (e.g., Alkaloids, Glycosides, Tannins, Volatile Oils).

‚öóÔ∏è ALKALOIDS

Definition: Alkaloids are basic nitrogenous organic compounds of plant origin that possess a marked physiological action on man and animals. The nitrogen is usually contained in a heterocyclic ring.

Properties 
PHYSICAL PROPERTIES:
‚Ä¢ State: Usually crystalline solids (Quinine). Few are liquids (Nicotine, Coniine).
‚Ä¢ Color: Mostly colorless. Exceptions: Berberine (Yellow), Betanidin (Red).
‚Ä¢ Taste: Intensely bitter.
‚Ä¢ Solubility: Free bases are soluble in organic solvents (Chloroform, Ether).
              Salts are soluble in water.

CHEMICAL PROPERTIES:
‚Ä¢ Basic in nature (due to lone pair of electrons on Nitrogen).
‚Ä¢ Form precipitates with heavy metal iodides (General Alkaloidal Reagents).

Classification

Alkaloids are classified based on their chemical structure or biosynthetic origin:

  1. True Alkaloids: Derived from amino acids and have nitrogen in a heterocyclic ring. (e.g., Atropine, Morphine).
  2. Proto-alkaloids (Amino-alkaloids): Derived from amino acids but nitrogen is not in a heterocyclic ring. (e.g., Ephedrine, Colchicine).
  3. Pseudo-alkaloids: Not derived from amino acids but possess nitrogen in a heterocyclic ring. (e.g., Caffeine - Purine alkaloid).

üîó GLYCOSIDES

Definition: Glycosides are organic compounds from plants or animals which, on enzymatic or acid hydrolysis, yield one or more sugar moieties (Glycone) and a non-sugar moiety (Aglycone or Genin).

$$\text{Glycoside} \xrightarrow{\text{Hydrolysis (Acid/Enzyme)}} \text{Sugar (Glycone)} + \text{Non-Sugar (Aglycone)}$$

The Aglycone part is responsible for the therapeutic activity, while the Glycone part facilitates absorption and transport in the body.

Classification (Based on Aglycone)

Anthraquinone Glycosides

Purgative action. Borntrager's test positive.
Examples: Senna, Aloe, Rhubarb, Cascara.

Cardiac Glycosides

Act on heart muscles. Steroidal structure.
Examples: Digitalis, Squill, Strophanthus.

Saponin Glycosides

Form foam with water. Haemolyse RBCs.
Examples: Liquorice, Ginseng, Dioscorea.

Cyanogenetic Glycosides

Yield Hydrocyanic acid (HCN) on hydrolysis.
Examples: Bitter Almond, Wild Cherry.

Isolation: Stas-Otto Method

The general method for isolation of glycosides involves:

  1. Extraction with alcohol (to deactivate enzymes).
  2. Treatment with Lead Acetate (to precipitate tannins and impurities).
  3. Removal of excess lead using $H_2S$ gas or Sodium Phosphate.
  4. Fractional crystallization or chromatography to obtain pure glycoside.

üßà LIPIDS (FATS, OILS, WAXES)

Fixed Oils & Fats: These are esters of higher fatty acids with glycerin. They are called "Fixed" because they do not evaporate at ordinary temperature (unlike volatile oils) and leave a permanent greasy spot on paper.

  • Fats: Solid at room temperature (e.g., Cocoa butter, Lard).
  • Fixed Oils: Liquid at room temperature (e.g., Castor oil, Olive oil, Arachis oil).

Waxes: Esters of higher fatty acids with higher monohydric alcohols (not glycerin). Example: Beeswax (Myricyl palmitate).

üí°

Key Constants for Evaluation

  • Acid Value: Measures free fatty acids (Indicates rancidity).
  • Saponification Value: Measures average molecular weight of fatty acids.
  • Iodine Value: Measures degree of unsaturation (drying property).

üå¨Ô∏è VOLATILE OILS (ESSENTIAL OILS)

Definition: Volatile oils are odorous, volatile principles of plant and animal source. They evaporate at ordinary temperature and do not leave a permanent grease spot on paper. Chemically, they are mixtures of terpenes and their derivatives.

Difference: Volatile Oil vs. Fixed Oil

Property Volatile Oils Fixed Oils
Volatility Evaporate at room temp. Do not evaporate.
Spot Test No permanent spot. Permanent greasy spot.
Saponification Cannot be saponified. Can be saponified (form soaps).
Chemistry Terpenes Esters of Glycerin
Examples Clove oil, Peppermint oil Castor oil, Almond oil

üçÇ TANNINS

Definition: Complex organic, non-nitrogenous, polyphenolic substances with high molecular weight. They possess an astringent taste and have the property of precipitating proteins (used in leather tanning).

Classification

1. Hydrolysable Tannins

Hydrolysed by acids/enzymes to yield Gallic acid or Ellagic acid.

Test: $\text{FeCl}_3$ $\rightarrow$ Blue-Black Color.

Examples: Myrobalan, Bahera, Rhubarb.

2. Condensed Tannins

Resistant to hydrolysis. Related to flavonoids. Yield 'Phlobaphenes' (Red) on treatment with acids.

Test: $\text{FeCl}_3$ $\rightarrow$ Brownish-Green Color.

Examples: Catechu (Black & Pale), Cinchona, Tea.

Goldbeater's Skin Test: A specific test for tannins where ox-intestine membrane (Goldbeater's skin) treated with HCl and drug extract turns black with Ferrous Sulfate solution.

07

üå≤ RESINS & RESIN COMBINATIONS

Definition: Amorphous, solid or semi-solid substances produced in plants as metabolic end products. They are insoluble in water but soluble in alcohol and volatile oils.

Classification

  • Acid Resins: Contain resin acids (e.g., Colophony - Abietic acid).
  • Oleoresins: Homogeneous mixture of Resin + Volatile Oil (e.g., Ginger, Capsicum).
  • Gum-Resins: Mixture of Resin + Gum (e.g., Asafoetida).
  • Oleo-Gum-Resins: Mixture of Resin + Gum + Volatile Oil (e.g., Myrrh).
  • Balsams: Resinous mixtures containing cinnamic acid, benzoic acid, or their esters (e.g., Balsam of Tolu, Balsam of Peru, Benzoin).

üç¨ CARBOHYDRATES (GUMS & MUCILAGES)

Carbohydrates are polyhydroxy aldehydes or ketones. In pharmacognosy, gums and mucilages are the most important polysaccharides.

Difference: Gums vs. Mucilages

Gums Mucilages
Origin Pathological products (formed after injury to plant). Physiological products (normal metabolic products).
Solubility Readily dissolve in water. Form slimy masses with water.
Examples Acacia, Tragacanth. Agar, Isapgol (Psyllium), Senna.
