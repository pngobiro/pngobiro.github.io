üí° Anomalous Behavior of Lithium

In the case of Group 1 and Li the following differences can be noted: Lithium has properties similar to magnesium.

  • Lithium can exhibit a high degree of covalent character in its bonding. This covalent character is due to the high polarizing power of the $\mathrm{Li}^{+}$ ion associated with high charge density.
  • Lithium forms the normal oxide when burnt in oxygen, whereas other Group 1 elements form peroxides or superoxides.
  • Lithium is the only alkali metal to form a nitride, $\mathrm{Li}_{3} \mathrm{~N}$, when heated in nitrogen, and a carbide, $\mathrm{Li}_{2} \mathrm{C}_{2}$, when heated with graphite.
  • Some lithium salts such as the carbonate, phosphate, and fluoride have very low solubilities in water. Other lithium salts crystallize as hydrates or are hygroscopic.
  • Lithium forms many stable organometallic compounds.
  • Lithium nitrate decomposes directly to the oxide, whereas the other alkali metals initially form nitrites, $\mathrm{MNO}_{2}$.
  • Lithium hydride is stable to heating to $900^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$, whereas the other hydrides decompose on heating above $400^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$.

General Characteristics

  • They are metals, with electropositive character
  • Hydroxides are strong bases,
  • Forms salt with strong acids, not hydrolyzed
  • Carbonates stable to heat
  • Bicarbonate, obtainable in solid form
  • Nitrates fairly stable to heat, evolve oxygen on strong heating
  • Complexes are rarely formed.
  • They are electro positive and have low valency of 1 , they lose 1 electrons to form +1 ion
  • They are electropositive and have little tendency to share electrons and don't form covalent bonds except lithium.
  • They do not form complex ions, but ions are hydrated in aqueous solution and sometimes crystals,
  • Alkali metals ions are colorless and any color comes from the anion.
  • It is easier to obtain potassium salts in higher state of purity than those of sodium so frequent use of potassium salts in analytical work. For example, potassium permanganate potassium iodide

Periodic Changes down the group

Ionization energy decreases hence,

  • a Electro positive character increases
  • b Metal becomes more reactive

Ionic size increase hence,

  • Hydroxides become stronger bases.
  • Salt have decreasing tendency to be hydrated and are often more soluble in water
  • Carbonates become more stable to heat
  • polyhalides become more stable

Relative ion sizes: Elements on the left side of periodic table and are reactive and electropositive giving strong basic oxide

üßÇ Sodium (Na)

Occurrence- Sodium does not occur free and there are a few natural occurring compounds some are found in large quantities e.g. sodium chloride NaCl , sodium nitrate $\mathrm{NaNO}_{3}$, (Chilesalt petre), sodium sesquicarbonate $\mathrm{Na}_{2} \mathrm{CO}_{3} \cdot \mathrm{NaHCO}_{3} \cdot 2 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}$, sodium aluminum fluoride $\mathrm{Na}_{3} \mathrm{AlF}_{6}$.

Industrial preparation- Almost all of it is made by electrolysis of fused sodium chloride using down cell. The graphite anode is cylindrical and is surrounded by the steel gauze diaphragm and concentric cylindrical cathodes (also of steel). Electrolyte is a mixture of sodium chloride and calcium chloride. The latter is added to reduce the melting point of sodium chloride to approximately $500^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ (some calcium is therefore liberated with the sodium), the gap between anode and cathode is kept as small as possible to reduce resistance. The heat evolved by current maintains temperature of the cell. Chlorine is set free at the anode surface rises into the nickel core and is collected. Sodium liberated at the cathode is prevented by the diaphragm from passing into anode region. The molten sodium collects under the circular hood and rises up the pipe being assisted if necessary, by the paddle rod. The calcium being almost immiscible with sodium and much denser can readily separate from molten sodium. The graphite anode wears away and must be renewed from time to time.

Physical and Chemical Properties

  • Sodium is a soft silvery metal melting point $97.5^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$, boiling point $880^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$. Vaporizing to give a violet mono atomic gas.
  • It is readily molded and extruded into wire form (ductile). This is used to dry organic solvents such as ether and benzene with which it does not react chemically.
  • Sodium is rapidly tarnishes in moist air giving first film of the monoxide. This reacts with moisture to give the hydroxide with which atmospheric carbon dioxide is converted slowly to the carbonate. So, sodium must be kept in air tight vessels or under paraffin oil, not under water.
  • Sodium burns in air with a familiar bright yellow flame giving with insufficient air the monoxide andwith excess air or oxygen the peroxide
  • Sodium combines on heating with most elements eg with halogens it gives halides, with phosphorus it gives a phosphide with sulfur forms a sulfide, (with excess sulfur a polysulfide)
  • Heating sodium in a stream of hydrogen produces sodium hydride. If sodium is heated with the chloride or oxides of less electropositive metals, these are often reduced to the metal
    $\mathrm{AlCl}_{3}+3 \mathrm{Na} \longrightarrow 3 \mathrm{NaCl}+\mathrm{Al}$
  • Sodium floats on water but reacts with it immediately to give sodium hydroxide, which defaults and hydrogen
    $2 \mathrm{Na}+2 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O} \longrightarrow 2 \mathrm{Na}^{+}+2 \mathrm{OH}^{-}+\mathrm{H}_{2} \uparrow$
  • The reaction is strongly exothermic but the hydrogen is not ignited unless movement of the sodium over the surface is prevented. Sodium dissolves much more quietly in ethyl alcohol giving sodium ethoxide
    $2 \mathrm{Na}+2 \mathrm{C}_{2} \mathrm{H}_{5} \mathrm{OH} \longrightarrow 2 \mathrm{NaOC}_{2} \mathrm{H}_{5}+\mathrm{H}_{2} \uparrow$
  • This is a process of preparing hydrogen and is also a safe and useful way of getting rid of excess sodium from other reactions. Sodium dissolved in liquid ammonia to give a deep blue solution like ink in appearance (in liquid ammonia sodium ionizes as follows; $\mathrm{Na} \rightarrow \mathrm{Na}^{+}+\mathrm{e}^{-}$ the electrons produced associated in some way with ammonia to give a deep blue solution. Sodium in liquid ammonia is an excellent reducing agent and it reduces many metallic salts to metals.
  • If this solution is kept for some time, the color disappears and sodium amide or sodamide is formed, hydrogen being evolved.
    $2 \mathrm{Na}+2 \mathrm{NH}_{3} \longrightarrow 2 \mathrm{NaNH}_{2} \downarrow+\mathrm{H}_{2} \uparrow$

Sodium hydroxide, caustic soda (NaOH)- It is manufactured either by (a) Gossage (lime soda) process or b) electronically.

Gossage process- The reaction is between sodium carbonate solution and the suspension of slaked lime

Electrolysis- from aqueous sodium chloride, chlorine is equally important product. To obtain NaOH, anode and cathode must be either separated by a diaphragm to avoid mixing of products (diaphragm cell) or cathode may be of mercury, (Mercury cell) giving sodium amalgam.

NaOH solution attacks flesh hence common name caustic soda, (caustic- burning) Glass is very slowly dissolved to give sodium silicate. Concentrated solutions cannot therefore be kept in glass bottles, unless lined with paraffin wax.

üçå Potassium (K)

The chemistry is similar to sodium. It occurs in wood ash as a carbonate and may be extracted with water. The aqueous solution was evaporated in iron pots to yield potash $\left(\mathbf{K}_{\mathbf{2}} \mathbf{C O}_{\mathbf{3}}\right)$ hence the name of the element

Occurrence- it's to reactive to occur free, but it's compounds are widely distributed important deposits found around strassfurt Germany, mainly as sylvine KCl and Carnallite $\mathrm{KCl} \cdot \mathrm{Mg}_{2} \cdot 6 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}$.

Preparation -Pure potassium is made by heating KCl with calcium in vacuo

$2 \mathrm{KCl}+\mathrm{Ca} \rightleftharpoons \mathrm{CaCl}_{2}+2 \mathrm{~K} \uparrow$

Properties

  • The property resembles sodium in both physical and chemical properties
  • The metal is softer than sodium and exhibit slight radioactivity due to the presence of the isotope ${ }^{40}{ }_{19} \mathrm{~K}$ in very small amounts.
  • It is more reactive than sodium e.g. when it reacts with water heat is evolved causing ignition of hydrogen evolved
  • In excess oxygen potassium burns to give the superperoxide $\mathrm{KO}_{2}$ while for sodium it gives the peroxide

Compounds of Potassium

Potassium superoxide $\mathrm{KO}_{2}$ - Yellow solid composed of ions $\mathrm{K}^{+}$ and $[\mathrm{O}-\mathrm{O}]^{-}$ reacts with water involving oxygen and hydrogen peroxide. It is a strong oxidizing agent

$2 \mathrm{KO}_{2}+2 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O} \longrightarrow 2 \mathrm{KOH}+\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{2}+\mathrm{O}_{2} \uparrow$

Potassium hydroxide, caustic potash, KOH - it's stronger base and more hygroscopic than NaOH. Potassium carbonate is soluble in solution of concentrated KOH hence exposure of KOH to moist air does not produce a crust of carbonate

Potassium nitrate, nitre, saltpeter $\mathrm{KNO}_{3}$ This well-known compound is prepared by mixing hot saturated solution of potassium chloride and sodium nitrate

$\mathrm{K}^{+}+\mathrm{Cl}^{-}+\mathrm{Na}^{+}+\mathrm{NO}_{3}^{-} \longrightarrow \mathrm{NaCl} \downarrow+\mathrm{K}^{+}+\mathrm{NO}_{3}^{-}$

Sodium chloride is the least soluble of the four salts $\mathrm{KCl}, \mathrm{NaNO}_{3}, \mathrm{KNO}_{3}$ and NaCl and crystallizes out, the remaining solution is then cooled to ordinary temperature when potassium nitrate (which is much less soluble in cold than in hot water) separate.

Tests for Potassium

  • 1 Flame test; a lilac-coloured flame is produced.
  • 2 Sodium perchlorate, NaC1Oa, precipitates potassimm perchlorate, KClO. slowly as colourless crystals.
  • 3 Sodium tetraphenylboron, $\mathrm{NaB}\left(\mathrm{C}_{6} \mathrm{H}_{5}\right)_{4}$, gives an immediate white precipitate of the potassimm salt, $\mathrm{KB}\left(\mathrm{C}_{6} \mathrm{H}_{5}\right)_{4}$, in weakly acid solution. The test is quantitative; the precipitate can be filtered off, dried, and weighed to estimate potassium.

üè• Biological Importance & Medical Uses

Medical uses of the alkali metals

Lithium toxicity, also known as lithium overdose, is the condition of having too much lithium. Symptoms may include a tremor, increased reflexes, trouble walking, kidney problems, and an altered level of consciousness. Some symptoms may last for a year after levels return to normal. Complications may include serotonin syndrome. Lithium toxicity can occur due to excessive intake or decreased excretion. Excessive intake may be either a suicide attempt or accidental. Decreased excretion may occur as a result of dehydration such as from vomiting or diarrhea, a low sodium diet, or from kidney problems. The diagnosis is generally based on symptoms and supported by a lithium level of greater than $1.2 \mathrm{mEq} / \mathrm{L}$.

The biology of lithium and its medicinal application- Lithium salts are used in the prophylaxis and treatment of mania, and in the prophylaxis of bipolar disorder and recurrent depression. Lithium therapy is taken orally, usually as lithium carbonate $\left(\mathrm{Li} 2 \mathrm{CO}_{3}\right)$ or lithium citrate $\left[\mathrm{Li}_{3} \mathrm{C}_{3} \mathrm{H}_{5} \mathrm{O}(\mathrm{COO})_{3}\right]$, with a total dose of up to $30 \mathrm{mmol} /$ day. $\mathrm{Li}_{2} \mathrm{CO}_{3}$ is the preferred lithium salt used, as it causes the least irritation to the stomach. The treatment has to be closely monitored, and $\mathrm{Li}+$ blood concentrations are measured 12 h after administration to achieve a serum lithium concentration of $0.4-1 \mathrm{mmol} / \mathrm{l}$. The therapeutic index (concentration window from efficacy to toxicity) for $\mathrm{Li}+$ is very narrow, and plasma $\mathrm{Li}+$ concentrations above 2 mM require emergency treatment for poisoning

Drugs, diet and toxicity

Sodium chloride solutions are normally used when the patient is diagnosed with sodium depletion and dehydration. Treatment is mostly administered intravenously, but in chronic conditions (mild to moderate sodium loss) sodium chloride or sodium bicarbonate can be given orally. Oral rehydration therapies usually use a mixture of alkali metal-based salts such as $\mathrm{NaCl}, \mathrm{KCl}$ and their citrates.

‚ú® Sodium-Potassium Pump

Potassium ions are crucial for the functioning of neurons, by influencing the osmotic balance between the cells and the interstitial fluid. The concentration of K+ within and outside the cells is regulated by the so-called Na+/K+-ATPase pump. Under the use of ATP, three $\mathrm{Na}+$ ions are pumped outside the cell and two $\mathrm{K}+$ ions are actively transported into the cell. As a result, an electrochemical gradient over the cell membrane is created; the so-called resting potential is established.

Hypokalaemia is a potentially serious condition where the patient has low levels of $\mathrm{K}+$ in his/her blood plasma. Symptoms can include weakness of the muscles or ECG (electrocardiogram) abnormalities. Mostly, hypokalaemia can be a result of reduced K+ intake caused by GI disturbance, such as diarrhoea and vomiting, or increased excretion of $\mathrm{K}+$ caused by diuresis.

Hyperkalaemia- The therapeutic window for $\mathrm{K}+$ in the blood plasma is very small $(3.5-5.0 \mathrm{mmol})$, and especially hyperkalaemia, an increased level of K+ in the plasma, can lead to severe health problems. Potassium salts can cause nausea and vomiting and in extreme cases can lead to small bowel ulcerations. Acute severe hyperkalaemia is defined when the plasma potassium concentration exceeds $6.5 \mathrm{mmol} / \mathrm{l}$ or if ECG changes are seen. This can lead to cardiac arrest, which needs immediate treatment. Treatment options include the use of calcium gluconate intravenous injections, which minimises the effects of hyperkalaemia on the heart.

BIOLOGICAL IMPORTANCE OF CAESIUM

  • It is capable of replacing potassium in the body to some extent because of its chemical similarity. Ingestion of any Caesium compounds is therefore to be avoided.
  • Because of this similarity, the isotopes 134Cs and 137Cs (present in the biosphere in small amounts as a result of radiation leaks) are very toxic.
  • Rats fed Caesium in place of potassium in their diet die.
