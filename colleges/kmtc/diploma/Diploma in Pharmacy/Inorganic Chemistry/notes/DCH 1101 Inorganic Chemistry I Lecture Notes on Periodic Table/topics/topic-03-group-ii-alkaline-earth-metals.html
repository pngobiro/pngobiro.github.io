Group II, IIA: The Alkaline Earth Metals

üìÑ DCH 1101 Lecture Notes
01

üîç General Characteristics

  • They are metals, electropositive character.
  • Hydroxide, sparingly soluble in water and not very strong bases
  • Salts of strong acids, not usually hydrolyzed (some salts of beryllium and magnesium exempted)
  • Carbonates evolve carbon dioxide on heating
  • Bicarbonates unstable in the solid states,
  • Nitrates evolve nitrogen dioxide and oxygen on heating
  • Complexes formed by beryllium and magnesium less commonly by other elements.
  • Thus, whereas calcium, strontium, barium, and radium react readily with cold water, magnesium reacts only with hot water

Changes down the group

  • Ionic size increases hence: Hydroxides stronger bases salts less hydrated and usually more soluble carbonates more soluble.
  • Ionization energy decreases hence: Electropositive character increases metals more reactive
üí° Solubility Trend

Beryllium ion is the most soluble and the solubility decreases with increasing size so that Barium ion is the least watersoluble alkaline earth metal ion. Solubility in water is related to the ionic nature and size. Smaller ions have higher charge density and can be solvated by more water molecules.

Solubility of $\mathrm{Be}^{2+}$ > Solubility of $\mathrm{Mg}^{2+}$ > Solubility of $\mathrm{Ca}^{2+}$ > Solubility of $\mathrm{Sr}^{2+}$ > Solubility of $\mathrm{Ba}^{2+}$

Flame Colouration

In Alkaline Earth Metals, the energy needed for an electronic transition between the available energy levels falls in the visible spectrum region. So, on heating, except beryllium and magnesium produce a characteristic colour to the flame reflective of their emission or absorption spectrum.

Element Ca Sr Ba Ra
Color Orange-red / Brick Red Crimson Yellowish-green / Apple Green Deep red

‚öôÔ∏è Magnesium (Mg)

Occurrence - Magnesium is not found free, but its compounds are widely distributed and plentiful. Dolomite $\mathrm{MgCa}\left(\mathrm{CO}_{3}\right)_{2}$ constitutes the Dolomites and other mountains, carnallite $\mathrm{KCl} \cdot \mathrm{MgCl}_{2} \cdot 6 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}$ occur in deposits in Germany, other minerals are magnesite $\mathrm{MgCO}_{3}$ and asbestos $\mathrm{CaMg}_{3}(\mathrm{SiO} 3)_{4}$, magnesium halides are found in seawater and magnesium is an essential constituent of chlorophyll, the green coloring matter of plants.

Extraction

Magnesium is obtained commercially by the electrolysis of fused anhydrous chloride mixed with some sodium chloride to lower the melting point. The anhydrous chloride may therefore be made by heating crushed magnesite with coke and passing chlorine over it, the Magnesite is first decomposed to give the oxide which is then reduced.

$$\begin{aligned} \mathrm{MgCO}_{3} & \longrightarrow \mathrm{MgO}+\mathrm{CO}_{2} \uparrow \\ \mathrm{MgO}+\mathrm{C}+\mathrm{Cl}_{2} & \longrightarrow \mathrm{MgCl}_{2}+\mathrm{CO} \uparrow+33 \mathrm{kcal} \end{aligned}$$

Seawater Extraction: Magnesium is extracted from seawater in a large plant on the Gulf of Mexico. The first stage is to precipitate magnesium hydroxide by adding of lime

$\mathrm{MgCl}_{2}+\mathrm{Ca}(\mathrm{OH})_{2} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Mg}(\mathrm{OH})_{2} \downarrow+\mathrm{CaCl}_{2}$

Chemical Properties

  • It tarnishes, somewhat in moist air, a film of the oxide MgO being formed. On heating in air magnesium burns with a brilliant light forming both the oxide and the nitride $\mathrm{Mg}_{3} \mathrm{~N}_{2}$.
  • Magnesium will also burn in steam and in carbon dioxide:
    $\mathrm{Mg}+\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O} \longrightarrow \mathrm{MgO}+\mathrm{H}_{2} \uparrow$
    $2 \mathrm{Mg}+\mathrm{CO}_{2} \longrightarrow 2 \mathrm{MgO}+\mathrm{C} \downarrow$
  • Dry magnesium reacts to alkyl-halides to form covalent Grignard reagents much use in organic chemistry.

Compounds of Magnesium

‚ñ∂ View Magnesium Compounds (Oxide, Carbonate, Sulfate)

Magnesium hydride $\mathrm{MgH}_{2}$- the hydride is prepared by the action of hydrogen under pressure on heated magnesium powder, addition of a little iodine catalyzes the reaction

$\mathrm{Mg}+\mathrm{H}_{2} \longrightarrow \mathrm{MgH}_{2}$

Magnesium nitrite $\mathrm{Mg}_{2} \mathrm{~N}_{2}$ The nitride is formed when magnesium is heated to redness in nitrogen or ammonium gas

$3 \mathrm{Mg}+2 \mathrm{NH}_{3} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Mg}_{3} \mathrm{~N}_{2}+3 \mathrm{H}_{2} \uparrow$

Magnesium carbonate $\mathrm{MgCO}_{3}$ The carbonate occurs naturally as magnesite. It is fairly insoluble in water, but if carbon dioxide is bubbled through a suspension in water, the soluble bicarbonate is formed

$\mathrm{MgCO}_{3}+\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}+\mathrm{CO}_{2} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Mg}^{2+}+2 \mathrm{HCO}_{3}^{-}$

Magnesium sulfate $\mathrm{MgSO}_{4}$-The sulfate is prepared by the usual method for sulfates and crystallizes as $\mathrm{MgSO}_{4} \cdot 7 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}$ (Epsom salt) on heating, the hydrate water is first lost and then sulfur trioxide leaving finally the oxide.

Magnesium ammonium phosphate $\mathrm{MgNH}_{4} \mathrm{PO}_{4} \cdot \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}$-it's formed as a white crystalline precipitate when a solution of disodium hydrogen phosphate $\mathrm{Na}_{2} \mathrm{HPO}_{4}$ is added to a solution of magnesium salt containing ammonium chloride and ammonia

$6 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}+\mathrm{Mg}^{2+}+\mathrm{NH}_{4}^{+}+\mathrm{PO}_{4}^{3-} \longrightarrow \mathrm{MgNH}_{4} \mathrm{PO}_{4} \cdot 6 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O} \downarrow$

ü•õ Calcium (Ca)

Occurrence- Calcium is too reactive to occur naturally as the element but its compounds occur widely and abundantly. Calcium carbonate $\mathrm{CaCO}_{3}$ is found as marble, limestone, chalk, calcite, aragonite and Iceland spar.

Extraction- Metallic calcium is obtained by the electrolysis of a fused mixture of calcium chloride and calcium fluoride- the latter reduces the melting point below that of the metallic calcium.

Compounds of Calcium

Calcium oxide, quick lime CaO

The oxide is formed when calcium is burned in oxygen ($2 \mathrm{Ca}+\mathrm{O}_{2} \longrightarrow 2 \mathrm{CaO}$). On the large scale limestone calcium carbonate is heated in a lime kiln.

$\mathrm{CaCO}_{3} \rightleftharpoons \mathrm{CaO}+\mathrm{CO}_{2}$

On exposure to air it quickly absorbs moisture and carbon dioxide.

$\mathrm{CaO}+\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Ca}(\mathrm{OH})_{2}+15 \mathrm{kcal}$

Calcium hydroxide slaked lime $\mathrm{Ca}(\mathrm{OH})_{2}$

The hydroxide is made by the addition of a limited amount of water to quicklime and hence is alternatively called hydrated lime. It is sparingly soluble in water being less soluble in hot water than in cold. The solution is known as lime-water.

When carbon dioxide is passed through lime water, a precipitate of calcium carbonate is first formed

$\mathrm{Ca}(\mathrm{OH})_{2}+\mathrm{CO}_{2} \longrightarrow \mathrm{CaCO}_{3} \downarrow+\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}$

More carbon dioxide converts the soluble bicarbonate and so the precipitate dissolves (Temporary Hardness)

$\mathrm{CaCO}_{3}+\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}+\mathrm{CO}_{2} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Ca}\left(\mathrm{HCO}_{3}\right)_{2}$

Bleaching powder

With cold solid slaked lime, the substance bleaching powder is formed. It is a mixture of calcium hypochlorite and a basic calcium chloride.

$3 \mathrm{Ca}(\mathrm{OH})_{2}+2 \mathrm{Cl}_{2} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Ca}(\mathrm{ClO})_{2}+\mathrm{CaCl}_{2} \cdot \mathrm{Ca}(\mathrm{OH})_{2} \cdot \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}+\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}$

If a dilute acid is added, to bleaching powder, chlorine is liberated

$\mathrm{Ca}(\mathrm{ClO})_{2} \cdot \mathrm{Ca}(\mathrm{OH})_{2} \cdot \mathrm{CaCl}_{2}+6 \mathrm{HCl} \longrightarrow 3 \mathrm{CaCl}_{2}+4 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}+2 \mathrm{Cl}_{2} \uparrow$

Portland Cement

Portland cement is made by heating almost to fusion. Any mixture of substance containing calcium oxide ( $60-65 \%$ ), silica $\mathrm{SiO}_{2}(20-25 \%)$ and alumina $\mathrm{Al}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{2}(5-10 \%)$. Usually limestone and clay are used.

Calcium sulfate $\mathrm{CaSO}_{4}$ (Plaster of Paris)

The sulfate occurs naturally as anhydrite and as the dihydrate $\mathrm{CaSO}_{4} \cdot 2 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}$ (gypsum). When Gypsum is heated, it first loses water from the hemihydrate $\mathbf{2CaSO}_{\mathbf{4}} \cdot \mathbf{H}_{\mathbf{2}} \mathbf{O}$ (Plaster of Paris).

To release sulfur dioxide from anhydrite quantitatively the latter is heated with coke.

$\mathrm{CaSO}_{4}+2 \mathrm{C} \longrightarrow \mathrm{CaS}+2 \mathrm{CO}_{2} \uparrow$

Calcium carbide $\mathrm{CaC}_{2}$

The carbide is made by heating a mixture of calcium oxide and coke to a very high temperature.

$\mathrm{CaO}+3 \mathrm{C} \longrightarrow \mathrm{CaC}_{2}+\mathrm{CO} \uparrow$

If calcium carbide is heated in a stream of nitrogen, calcium cyanamide $\mathrm{CaCN}_{2}$ and is formed

üîã Strontium and Barium

These two elements are similar to one another in properties, and together they resemble calcium in many respects.

  • The metals are similar to calcium but more reactive e.g. finely divided barium inflames in air.
  • The oxides are similar to that of calcium but there is an increased tendency to unite directly with oxygen to give peroxides.
  • The hydroxides are stronger bases than calcium hydroxide and are more soluble in water.
  • The carbonates are more stable to heat than that of calcium. Barium carbonate can be heated in a blowpipe flame without any change.
  • The sulfates are less soluble in water than that of calcium and barium sulfate is readily precipitate when sulfate ions are added to a barium salt.

üè• Medical Uses & Biological Importance

Magnesium

Biological importance- $\mathbf{M g}^{2+}$ is an essential ion in the human body and is a crucial constituent in numerous enzymatic processes. Indeed, $\mathrm{Mg}^{2+}$ is essential to most living cells as a signaling molecule and is involved in nucleic acid biochemistry dealing with the manipulation of ATP (adenosine triphosphate), DNA, RNA and related processes. $\mathrm{Mg}^{2+}$ ions form the redox-active centre in chlorophyll.

Magnesium hydroxide $[\mathrm{Mg}(\mathrm{OH}) 2]$ is present in antacids because of its laxative properties and is also the main ingredient of the 'milk of magnesia'. The alkaline suspension neutralizes any excess stomach acid.

Calcium

Biological importance- A normal adult body contains $\sim 1000 \mathrm{~g}$ of calcium, of which around $99 \%$ are extracellular and most of which is stored in bones and teeth. The remaining $1 \%$ of $\mathrm{Ca}^{2+}$ can be found in the extracellular space, such as plasma, lymph and extracellular water.

Clinical application - A variety of calcium salts are used for clinical application, including calcium carbonate, calcium chloride, calcium phosphate, calcium lactate, calcium aspartate and calcium gluconate.

Barium Toxicity

‚ö†Ô∏è Health Hazards

Barium salts can be highly toxic even at low concentrations. Barium carbonate is highly toxic and can be used as rat poison as it readily dissolves in the stomach acid. However, Barium sulfate is the least toxic barium compound mainly because of its insolubility. Barium sulfate is used in a variety of applications ranging from white paint to X-ray contrast agent (Barium meal).

Beryllium Hazards

Beryllium and its compounds are extremely poisonous and therefore there is only a very limited potential for their clinical applications. Indeed, even the inhalation of beryllium or its compounds can lead to serious respiratory diseases such as the chronic beryllium disease.

Biological Importance of Radium

  • Radium chloride is used in medicine as a cancer treatment. Radium-223 isotope is currently under investigated to use as a medicine for cancer treatment.
  • Radioactive iodine which produces from radium and iodine is used as a treatment of thyrotoxicosis and some types of thyroid cancer that absorb iodine.
