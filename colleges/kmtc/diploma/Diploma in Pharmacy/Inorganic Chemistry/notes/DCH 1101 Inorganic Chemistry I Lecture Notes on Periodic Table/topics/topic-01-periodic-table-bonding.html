üìä The Periodic Table

The periodic table is an arrangement of the elements in order of increasing atomic number. Elements that exhibit similar chemistry appear in vertical columns called groups (numbered $1-18$ from left to right); the seven horizontal rows are called periods.

  • The CAS system used letters to differentiate main group (A) and transition (B) elements.
  • The modern IUPAC system uses Arabic numbers 1-18, simply numbering the columns of the periodic table from left to right. Many periodic tables include both Roman and Arabic numbers. The Arabic numbering system is the most widely accepted today

The periodic law states that "the properties of the elements are the periodic functions of their atomic numbers" i.e. if the elements are arranged in the increasing order of their atomic numbers, the recurrence of properties of the elements takes place at regular intervals.

The elements of s and p-block (total 8 columns) are collectively called main group elements. The elements placed in s-block and p-block except the elements of groups 18 (zero group) are also known as representative elements and zero group elements are called inert or noble gases. The elements of the groups 3 or (IIIB), 4 or (IVB), ‚Ä¶ . 7 or (VIIB), 8 to 10 or (VIII), 11 or (IB) and 12 or (IIB) (total ten vertical columns) have been placed in d-block (also called transition elements). There are 28 elements arranged separately in two horizontal rows at the bottom of the main body of the periodic table, each row containing 14 elements. These have been included in f-block also known as inner transition elements or rare earth metals. First row of the elements is called $\mathbf{4 f}$-block elements or lanthanides because they all are having almost similar properties and follow the element lanthanum. The second row is the $\mathbf{5 f}$-block elements or actinides as they fall after actinium and also have almost similar properties. The transition elements have been placed between s-and p-block elements and inner transition elements within transition elements.

In general, all the elements of the long form of periodic table have been divided into four categories:

(i) Representative elements: These are the main group elements including the groups $1,2,13 \ldots \ldots .17$ which have either ns 1 or ns 2 and $\mathrm{ns}^{2} \mathrm{px}(\mathrm{x}=1-5)$ outer shell electronic configuration where n is the highest value of principal quantum number. All these elements have been placed in subgroups A of the periodic table.

(ii) Noble gases: These also fall within main group elements and include group 18 (or zero) elements. These all have completely filled s- or s- and p-subshells of outer most or ultimate shell with highest n value, i.e. ns 2 or ns 2 p 6 (ns2 for $\mathrm{He})$. These have also been placed in subgroup A of periodic table.

(iii) Transition metals: These are the elements of groups 3 to 12 and in general have incomplete d -subshell of the penultimate shell and filled s-subshell of ultimate shell with highest n -value. But the elements of some of the groups also have incompletely filled s-subshell of the ultimate shell in addition to incompletely filled d-subshell of the penultimate shell. All these elements have been placed in subgroups B of the periodic table.

(iv) Inner transition elements: These elements are said to belong to group 3 or (IIIB) due to close similarity in their chemical properties with those placed in group 3. These elements generally have incompletely filled ( $\mathrm{n}-1$ ) d and ( $\mathrm{n}-2$ ) fsubshells. According to their electronic configuration and the type of subshell to which the last of differentiating electron enters in the atom of the elements, the elements in the modern periodic table have been classified into four blocks i.e. s, p, d and f-block elements.

s-Block elements

(i) s-Block elements: All these elements are metals and solid except H and He which are gases. These are the elements in which the last electron enters the s-subshell. The elements of group 1 (alkali metals, $\mathrm{Li}, \mathrm{Na}, \mathrm{K}, \mathrm{Rb}, \mathrm{Cs}, \mathrm{Fr}$ ) and group 2 (alkaline earth metals, viz., $\mathrm{Be}, \mathrm{Mg}, \mathrm{Ca}, \mathrm{Sr}, \mathrm{Ba}, \mathrm{Ra}$ ) belong to this block in addition to hydrogen (H) and helium (He). The general valence shell electronic configuration of these elements is $\mathrm{ns}^{\mathrm{x}}(\mathrm{x}=1$ or 2$)$ where n is the number of valence shell or number of period which the element belongs to.

p-Block elements

(ii) p-Block elements: These elements in which the differentiating electron enters the orbitals of p -subshell of the valence or outer most shell are called p-block elements. The elements of groups 13 to 18 (except He with configuration 1s2) belong to this block. The group number and the elements contained in them are shown here:

  • Group 13 : B, Al, Ga, In, Tl
  • Group 14 : C, Si, Ge, Sn, Pb
  • Group 15 : N, P, As, Sb, Bi
  • Group 16 : O, S, Se, Te, Po
  • Group 17 : F, Cl, Br, I, At
  • Group 18 : Ne, Ar, Kr, Xe, Rn

The general valence shell electronic configuration of these elements in $\mathrm{ns} 2 \mathrm{np}^{\mathrm{x}}(\mathrm{x}=1$ to 6$)$ and n is the number of shell. The elements of this block exist in all the three states of matter viz. solid, liquid and gas. Most of them are non- metals and the rest are metals and metalloids (or semimetals).

d-Block elements

(iii) d-Block elements: The elements in which the last electron enters the orbitals of (n-1) d subshell, (i.e. the subshell of penultimate shell) and d-orbitals are, thus, being progressively filled are known as d-block elements. These elements are also termed as transition elements and have been placed in the middle between s-block and p-block elements in the periodic table. Thus, these elements serve as a bridge or transition between highly electropositive s-block and highly electronegative p -block elements thereby being responsible for gradual change in the properties along a period. Hence the name, transition elements has been given to them. These include the elements of group 3 to group 12 (ten columns). These have further been categorised into 3d, 4d, 5d and 6d series depending on the shell number whose dorbitals are being filled. Each of these series contains ten elements as given below:

  • 3d series: scandium ( $\mathrm{Sc}, \mathrm{Z}=21$ ) to zinc ( $\mathrm{Zn}, \mathrm{Z}=30$ )
  • 4d series: yttrium ( $\mathrm{Y}, \mathrm{Z}=39$ ) to cadmium ( $\mathrm{Cd}, \mathrm{Z}=48$ )
  • 5d series: lanthanum ( $\mathrm{La}, \mathrm{Z}=57$ ), hafnium ( $\mathrm{Hf}, \mathrm{Z}=72$ ) to mercury ( $\mathrm{Hg}, \mathrm{Z}=80$ )
  • 6d series: actinium ( $\mathrm{Ac}, \mathrm{Z}=89$ ), rutherfordium ( $\mathrm{Rf}, \mathrm{Z}=104$ ) to copernicium ( $\mathrm{Cn}, \mathrm{Z}=112$ )

The general valence shell electronic configuration of these elements is $(\mathrm{n}-1) \mathrm{d}^{\mathrm{x}} \mathrm{ns} 1$ or2 $(\mathrm{x}=1$ to 10$)$. All these elements are metals and solid except Hg (a liquid).

f-Block elements

(iv) f-Block elements: In these elements, the last or differentiating electron, in general, enters the orbitals of (n-2) f subshell, i.e. the subshell of antipenultimate shell. All these elements are metals and solids. These elements are also called inner transition elements because they have incomplete f subshell as well as incomplete d -subshell and fall within d-block elements. These elements have been divided into two series of 14 elements each, as follows:

  • 4f-series elements (lanthanides): cerium ( $\mathrm{Ce}, \mathrm{Z}=58$ ) to lutetium ( $\mathrm{Lu}, \mathrm{Z}=71$ )
  • 5f- series elements (actinides): thorium (Th, $\mathrm{Z}=90$ ) to lawrencium ( $\mathrm{Lr}, \mathrm{Z}=103$ )

The general valence shell electronic configuration of these elements is ( $n-2$ ) f0-14 ( $n-1) d 0-2 n s^{2}$.

It has been noticed that for s and p -block elements, the outermost shell itself is the valence shell. For d -block elements, two subshells, $(\mathrm{n}-1) \mathrm{d}$ and n -s subshells taken together form the valence shell. For f -block elements, three subshells, ( n 2)f (n-1)d and n-s subshells jointly form the valence shell. This is due to nearly similar energy of these subshells. A valence shell is that shell of the atoms of the elements, from which the electrons are used for bond formation or chemical combination. For long form of the periodic table, it may be noted that the s-block elements (group 1and2) have been placed in the extreme left portion of the table and the p-block elements are kept in the extreme right portion. The middle portion includes d-block elements. At the bottom, f-block elements have been placed separately in two series/rows.

Elements of the periodic table are written in two letter abbreviations and the atomic number is written as a left superscript and the mass number as a right subscript

üìà Trends in the Periodic Table

Periodic trends are specific patterns that are present in the periodic table that illustrate different aspects of a certain element. They were discovered by the Russian chemist Dmitri Mendeleev in the year 1863. Major periodic trends include atomic radius, ionization energy, electron affinity, electronegativity, valency and metallic character. These trends exist because of the similar electronic configuration of the elements within their respective groups or periods and because of the periodic nature of the elements. These give a qualitative assessment of the properties of each element.

Across the period from left to right

  • Electrons fill the one ns and the three np orbitals in accordance with paulis exclusion principle and the hunds rule
  • As a result of increasing nuclear charge and electrons being added to orbitals of the same energy level atoms size generally decrease whereas first ionization energy and electronegativity generally increases
  • Ionization energy- energy to remove the electron with the highest energy
  • Electronegativity- ability to form covalent bonds
  • Metallic character decreases with increase in nuclear charge of the element across the period as elements change from metals to metalloids to non-metals
  • Reactivity is highest at the left and right ends of the period except for innert noble gases
  • Bonding between atoms of an element changes from metallic to covalent in networks to covalent in individual molecules to none between separate atoms. As expected physical properties change abruptly at the network/molecule boundary which occurs in the period between carbon and nitrogen
  • Bonding between each element and an active non-metal changes from ionic to polar covalent to covalent. Bonding between each element and an active metal changes from metallic to polar covalent to ionic
  • The acid base behavior of the common oxides in water changes from basic to amphoteric to acidic as the bond between the elements and oxygen becomes more covalent
  • Reducing strength decreases through the metal and oxidizing strength increases through the non-metals

Trends down the group

Group 1A: Alkali metal

They are given this name because of their alkali (basic) nature of their oxides

Properties down the group
  • All members have the oxidation number +1 and form $\mathrm{E}^{+}$ ions.
  • Atoms have the largest size and the lowest ionization energy and electronegativity in their period
  • Atomic and ionic size increases while ionization energy and electronegativity decreases
  • Metallic bonding is relatively weak because there is only one valence electron i.e. metals have low melting point and boiling point which decreases down the group
  • Large atomic size and low atomic mass results in low density that in alkali metals the rate of increase of mass is greater than rate of increase of volume, therefore the density increases down the group

Group 2A: Alkaline earth metals

  • All members have the oxidation number of +2 and form the compound with $\mathrm{E}^{2+}$ ions
  • Atomic and ionic sizes increase down the group but are smaller than for the alkali earth metals
  • The ionization energy and electronegativity decreases down the group
  • Metallic bonding involves two valence electrons
  • Melting and boiling point generally decrease and density increase down the group

Group 3A (3) Boron family)

  • All except thallium commonly display +3 oxidative state. The +1 state become more common down the group $\mathrm{Tl}^{+}$
  • Atomic size, ionization energy and electronegativity do not change as expected down the group because there are intervening transition and inner transition elements
  • Bonding changes from network covalent in boron to metallic in the rest of the group thus boron has a higher melting point than the others but there is no overall trend
  • Boiling point decreases while density increases down the group

Group 4A (4) (carbon family)

  • Down the group the number of oxidation state decreases and the lower ( +2 ) state becomes more common
  • Atomic size increases down the group while ionization energy and electronegativity generally decreases but deviations occur for Ge and Pb because of intervening transition and inner transition elements
  • Decreasing hardness and melting point are due to change in type of bonding within the solid e.g. covalent network in carbon and silicon and Geranium, metallic in Sn and Pb
  • Density increases down the group because of crystal packing

Group 5A Nitrogen family

  • The number of oxidation state decreases down the group and the lower $(+3)$ state becomes more common
  • The atomic size, ionization energy and electronegativity decrease down the group
  • Change from individual molecules to covalent network and to metals. Thus melting point increases and then decreases
  • Density increases down the group

Group 6A oxygen family

  • A lower oxidation state becomes more common down the group
  • Down the group atomic and ionic size increase and ionization energy and electronegativity decreases
  • Melting point increases through to tellurium and then decreases for polonium due to its metallic bonding
  • Density of the elements increases down the group

Group 7A Halogens

  • Elements lack one electron to complete the outer shell. The -1 oxidation state is the most common for all members
  • Down the group atomic and ionic size increases steadily as ionization energy and electronegativity decreases
  • Phase change temperatures exhibit smooth trends down the group the melting and boiling point increases as a result of stronger dispersion forces between havier molecules
  • The density of the elements as liquids increase steadily with molar mass

Group 8 noble gases

  • All the valence shells are filled and are said to be unreactive. Only Kr and Xe are known to form compounds
  • This group contains the smallest atom with highest ionization energy in their period. Down the group atomic size increases and ionization energy decrease steadily
  • Melting and boiling point of these gaseous elements are extremely low but increase down the group
  • Density increases steadily down the group

General properties of elements

(i). Size of atom and ion (Atomic radius)- The atomic radius is the distance from the atomic nucleus to the outermost electron orbital in an atom. The size of atom decreases from left to right across a period in the periodic table. This is because there is a high force of attraction between electrons and the nucleus. On descending the group the size of atoms increase due to increase of number of energy levels. A positive ion is smaller than the neutral atom because it looses the electron in the outer most shell and the more electrons are removed the smaller the ion because of the higher effective nuclear charge

When a negative ion is formed, one or more electrons are added to an atom, the effective nuclear charge is reduced and hence the electron cloud expands. Negative ions are bigger than the corresponding atoms.

(ii). Ionization energy- The ionization energy is the minimum amount of energy that an electron in a gaseous atom or ion has to absorb to come out of the influence of attracting force of the nucleus. It is also referred to as ionization potential. If energy is supplied to an atom, electrons may be promoted to a higher energy level. If sufficient energy is supplied the electron can be ionized giving positive ions. The energy required to remove the most loosely bound electron is the ionization energy.

Factors affecting ionization energy are size of the atom, charge on the nucleus, and screening effect of inner electron shells. In small atoms electrons are tightly held and in large ones they are loosely held

The first ionization energy is the amount of energy that is required to remove the first electron from a neutral atom. The energy needed to remove the second electron from the neutral atom is called the second ionization energy and so on. As one moves from left to right across a period in the modern periodic table, the ionization energy increases as the nuclear charge increases and the atomic size decreases. The decrease in the atomic size results in a more potent force of attraction between the electrons and the nucleus. However, suppose one moves down in a group. In that case, the ionization energy decreases as atomic size increases due to adding a valence shell, thereby diminishing the nucleus's attraction to electrons. The $2^{\text {nd }}$ ionization energy is always greater than the first and the third greater than the second and so on.

(iii). Electron affinity- The energy released when an electron is added to a neutral gaseous atom to form an anion is known as electron affinity. Trend-wise, as one progresses from left to right across a period, the electron affinity will increase as the nuclear charge increases and the atomic size decreases resulting in a more potent force of attraction of the nucleus and the added electron. However, suppose one moves down in a group. In that case, the electron affinity will decrease as atomic size increases due to adding a valence shell, thereby diminishing the nucleus's attraction to electrons. Although it may seem that fluorine should have the greatest electron affinity, its small size generates enough repulsion among the electrons, resulting in chlorine having the highest electron affinity in the halogen family. Usually only one electron is added to form uni-negative ion. This repels other electrons and energy is needed to add a second electron e.g. $\mathrm{O}^{2-}$

(iv). Electronegativity- The tendency of an atom in a molecule to attract the shared pair of electrons towards itself is known as electronegativity. It is a dimensionless property because it is only a tendency. The most commonly used scale to measure electronegativity was designed by Linus Pauling. The scale has been named the Pauling scale in his honour. According to this scale, fluorine is the most electronegative element, while cesium is the least electronegative element.

  • Trend-wise, as one moves from left to right across a period in the modern periodic table, the electronegativity increases as the nuclear charge increases and the atomic size decreases.
  • However, if one moves down in a group, the electronegativity decreases as atomic size increases due to the addition of a valence shell, thereby decreasing the atom's attraction to electrons. However, in group XIII (Boron Family), the electronegativity first decreases from boron to aluminium and then increases down the group.
  • It is due to the fact that the atomic size increases as we move down the group, but at the same time the effective nuclear charge increases due to poor shielding of the inner d and f electrons. As a result, the force of attraction of the nucleus for the electrons increases and hence the electronegativity increases from aluminium to thalium.
  • Small atoms attract electrons more than large ones while atoms with nearly filled shells will tend to have higher electronegativity. Covalent bonds are formed by elements with similar high electronegativity values while ionioc bonds are formed by elements with large electronegative differences

(v). Polarization (fajans rule)- For two ions $\mathrm{A}^{+}$and $\mathrm{B}^{-}$the positive ions attract the electrons on the negative ion and repel the nucleus thus polarizing or distorting the negative ion, if the polarization is small ionic bonds result if the degree of polarization is large electrons are drawn from the negative ion to the positive ion and appreciably covalent bonds result. Fajans rule states that covalent bonding is favoured by

  • Small positive ions
  • Large negative ions
  • Large charge on either ion

(vi). Valency- The valency of an element is the number of electrons that must be lost or gained by an atom to obtain a stable electron configuration. In simple terms, it is the measure of the combining capacity of an element to form chemical compounds. Electrons found in the outermost shell are generally known as valence electrons; the number of valence electrons determines the valency of an atom. Trend-wise,

  • while moving from left to right across a period, the number of valence electrons of elements increases and varies between 1 to 8 . But the valency of elements first increases from 1 to 4 , and then it decreases to zero as we reach the noble gases.
  • However, as we move down in a group, the number of valence electrons does not change. Hence, all the elements of a particular group have the same valency.
  • However, this periodic trend is sparsely followed for heavier elements, especially for the F block and the transition metals. These elements show variable valency as these elements have d-orbital as the penultimate orbital and sorbital as the outermost orbital. The energies of these ( $\mathrm{n}-1$ )d $\&$ ns orbitals are relatively close.

(vii). Metallic character- Metals are electropositive and have a tendency to loose electrons if supplied with energy. The stronger this tendency the more electropositive and the more metallic an element is. Electropositivity is really the opposite of electronegativity. Strong electropositive elements react with water and acid to form strong basic oxides and hydroxides and react with oxyacids to form stable salts such as carbonates, nitrates and sulphates. Weak electropositive elements are unaffected by water and are much less readily attacked by acid and form unstable carbonates.

Salts of strong electropositive metals have little tendency to hydrolyse and form oxysalts while those of weakly electropositive elements hydrolyse and may form oxysalts. Metallic properties generally increase down the groups, as decreasing attraction between the nuclei and outermost electrons causes these electrons to be more loosely bound and thus able to conduct heat and electricity. Across each period, from left to right, the increasing attraction between the nuclei and the outermost electrons causes the metallic character to decrease. In contrast, the nonmetallic character decreases down the groups and increases across the periods.

(viii). Effective nuclear charge- Since the positive nuclear charge is generally offset by the negative charge of the internal electrons in the electron shell inside the valence electrons, the nuclear charge that valence electrons feel is smaller than the integer charge, Ze for an atomic number Z. This reduction is expressed by the shielding constant $\sigma$, and the net nuclear charge is called the effective nuclear charge, Zeff e. Zeff $=Z-\sigma$ The effective nuclear charge varies with different electron orbitals and distances from the nucleus. The effective nuclear charge ( $Z$ effective or Zeff) is defined as the net positive charge pulling these electrons towards the nucleus. The stronger the pull on the outermost electrons (valence electrons) towards the nucleus, the higher the effective nuclear charge and decrease down the group due to shielding.

‚öõÔ∏è Hybridization of Orbitals

In chemistry, orbital hybridisation (or hybridization) is the concept of mixing atomic orbitals to form new hybrid orbitals (with different energies, shapes, etc., than the component atomic orbitals) suitable for the pairing of electrons to form chemical bonds in valence bond theory. Carbon $1 \mathrm{~S}^{2} 2 \mathrm{~S}^{2} 2 \mathrm{P}^{2}$ (ground state) have only two unpaired electron, yet the chemistry of carbon is characterized by its ability to form four electron pair bonds. Any theoretical explanation of this must also agree with the following facts:

  1. The four bonds are equal in strength.
  2. The four bonds are symmetrical directed in space, when linking carbon to four identical atoms as in the methane and carbon tetrachloride molecule

Four unpaired electrons may be obtained if sufficient energy is absorbed to uncouple one of the 2s-electrons and excite to the 2P level (excited state). This process is called promotion.

Should bond formation take place immediately after promotion four equal bonds would not be produced. To overcome this, the orbitals are now equalized, so that four new equivalent orbitals are produced, which then take part in bonding.

The symbol given to these four new orbitals indicate their origin. In this case, they are labeled $\mathrm{SP}^{3}$ hybrid orbitals and the equalization process is termed hybridization. The shape of an $\mathrm{SP}^{3}$ hybridized orbital is shown in figure and since they are four of these new orbitals, they are directed symmetrically in space, ie tetrahedral. $\mathbf{S p}^{3}$ Hybridization (Methane)

The stages in the bonding of hydrogen and carbon in the formation of methane can been outlined as follows (carbon $\mathrm{S}^{2} 2 \mathrm{~S}^{2} 2 \mathrm{P}^{2}$)

The carbon atom can use its two singly occupied p-type orbitals to form two covalent bonds with two hydrogen atoms, yielding the singlet methylene $\mathrm{CH}_{2}$, the simplest carbene. The carbon atom can also bond to four hydrogen atoms by an excitation (or promotion) of an electron from the doubly occupied 2s orbital to the empty 2p orbital, producing four singly occupied orbitals. In $\mathrm{CH}_{4}$, four $\mathrm{sp}^{3}$ hybrid orbitals are overlapped by hydrogen 1 s orbitals, yielding four $\underline{\sigma}$ (sigma) bonds (that is, four single covalent bonds) of equal length and strength.

Carbon when bonded by four single bonds shows at the tetrahedral orientation of these bonds. In diamond each carbon atom is bonded to four neighboring carbon atoms by the overlapping of $\mathrm{SP}^{3}$ orbitals. So forming a crystal which is in effect a giant molecule

Methane $\mathbf{C H}_{\mathbf{4}}$ : The hybridization of the carbon atom in methane is of $\mathrm{sp}^{3}$ type. A $\sigma$ bond is formed by the overlap of each $\mathrm{sp}^{3}$ orbital and the s orbital of H . Thus the shape of methane molecule is regular tetrahedron. Like methane, $\mathrm{CCl}_{4}$, $\mathrm{CHCl}_{3}, \mathrm{CH}_{2} \mathrm{Cl}_{2}, \mathrm{C}_{2} \mathrm{H}_{6}, \mathrm{C}_{2} \mathrm{H}_{5} \mathrm{OH}$, etc. In many other carbonic compounds where only single bonds are formed with carbon, the hybridization of carbon occurs in a similar manner also in silane $\mathrm{SiH}_{4}$

$\mathbf{S p}^{2}$ Hybridization (Ethene)

For example, ethene $\left(\mathrm{C}_{2} \mathrm{H}_{4}\right)$ has a double bond between the carbons. For this molecule, carbon $\mathrm{sp}^{2}$ hybridizes, because one $\pi(\mathrm{pi})$ bond is required for the double bond between the carbons and only three $\sigma$ bonds are formed per carbon atom. In $\mathrm{sp}^{2}$ hybridisation the 2 s orbital is mixed with only two of the three available 2 p orbitals, usually denoted $2 p_{x}$ and $2 p_{y}$. The third $2 p$ orbital ( $2 p_{z}$ ) remains unhybridised forming a total of three $s p^{2}$ orbitals with one remaining p orbital. In ethylene ( ethene) the two carbon atoms form $\mathrm{a} \sigma$ bond by overlapping one $\mathrm{sp}^{2}$ orbital from each carbon atom. The $\pi$ bond between the carbon atoms perpendicular to the molecular plane is formed by $2 \mathrm{p}-2 \mathrm{p}$ overlap. Each carbon atom forms covalent $\mathrm{C}-\mathrm{H}$ bonds with two hydrogens by $\mathrm{s}-\mathrm{sp}^{2}$ overlap, all with $120^{\circ}$ bond angles. The hydrogen-carbon bonds are all of equal strength and length, in agreement with experimental data.

The chemical bonding in compounds such as alkynes with triple bonds is explained by sp hybridization. How to Draw Lewis Structures

A Lewis electron dot structure describes the bonding atoms, the number of bonds in the molecule, and the lone pairs left in the bonding atoms. The steps that must be followed while drawing a Lewis structure are listed below

  1. First, the total number of valence electrons present in the molecule is calculated by adding the individual valencies of each atom.
  2. If the molecule is an anion, extra electrons (number of electrons added $=$ the magnitude of negative charge) are added to the Lewis dot structure.
  3. When cationic molecules are considered, electrons are subtracted from the total count in order to make up for the positive charge.
  4. The least electronegative atom is made the central atom of the molecule or ion.
  5. The atoms are now connected via single bonds.
  6. Now, the lone pairs of electrons are assigned to each atom belonging to the molecule. Commonly, the lone pairs are assigned to the most electronegative atoms first.
  7. Once the lone pairs are assigned, if every atom does not have an octet configuration, a double or triple bond must be drawn to satisfy the octet valency of each atom.
  8. If required, a lone pair can be converted into a bond pair in order to satisfy the octet rule for two atoms.

It is important to note that only the valence electrons are considered while drawing Lewis dot structures and the electrons that do not belong to the outermost shell are ignored.

United atom approach- For example, a simple homonuclear diatomic molecule, the two nuclei A and B coalesce. When atomic orbitals interact, the resulting molecular orbital can be of three types: bonding, antibonding, or nonbonding.

Bonding Molecular orbitals

  • Bonding interactions between atomic orbitals are constructive (in-phase) interactions.
  • Bonding Molecular orbitals are lower in energy than the atomic orbitals that combine to produce them.
  • Those levels or molecular orbitals corresponding to a decrease in energy as the atoms get closer together i.e. related to lines sloping downwards from right to left (namely, the $(\mathrm{K}) \mathrm{z} \delta, \mathrm{z} \delta, \mathrm{w} \pi \ldots . . \mathrm{etc}$. are called the bonding molecular orbitals.

Antibonding Molecular orbitals

  • Antibonding interactions between atomic orbitals are destructive (out-of-phase) interactions, with a nodal plane where the wave function of the antibonding orbital is zero between the two interacting atoms
  • Antibonding Molecular orbitals are higher in energy than the atomic orbitals that combine to produce them.
  • Those corresponding to an increase in energy as the inter nuclear distance decreases and so related to lines sloping upwards from right to left e.g. $(\mathrm{k}) \mathrm{y} \delta, \mathrm{y} \delta, \mathrm{v} \pi \ldots$. etc are called antibonding molecular orbitals

Nonbonding Molecular orbitals

  • Nonbonding Molecular orbitals are the result of no interaction between atomic orbitals because of lack of compatible symmetries.
  • Nonbonding Molecular orbitals will have the same energy as the atomic orbitals of one of the atoms in the molecule.

In the Lewis electron structures the number of electron pairs holding two atoms together was called the bond order. Bond length is approximately equal to the average distance between the nuclei of two bonded atoms. More will be the bond order more will be the pull between electrons of two atoms and less will be the bond length.
