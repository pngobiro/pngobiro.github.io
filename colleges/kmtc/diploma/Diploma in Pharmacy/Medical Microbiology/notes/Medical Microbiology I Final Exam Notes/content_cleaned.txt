<p>Microbiology is the study of microorganisms, too small to be seen with the naked eye.</p>
<p>It considers the microscopic forms of life and deals about their reproduction, physiology, and participation in the process of nature, helpful and harmful relationships with other living things, and significance in science and industry.</p>
<p>Medical microbiology: is a branch of microbiology which deals with the study of microbes, such as bacteria, viruses, fungi and parasites, which cause human illness and their role in the disease.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="history-of-microbiology">History of Microbiology<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#history-of-microbiology" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Mankind has always been affected by diseases which were originally believed to be visitations by the gods and meant to punish evil doers.</p>
<p>Hippocrates (460 BC -370 BC), father of medicine, observed that ill health resulted due to changes in air, winds, water, climate, food, nature of soil and habits of people.</p>
<p>Marcus Terentius Varro (116–27 BC) theorized that disease was caused by minute living creatures invisible to naked eye but which were carried in the air through the mouth and nose into the body.</p>
<p>Girolamo Fracastoro (1478–1553) proposed that the agents of communicable disease were living germs that could be transmitted by direct contact with humans and animals, and indirectly by objects; but no proof because of lacking experimental evidence.</p>
<p>Antony Van Leeuwenhoek (1632–1723), father of Microbiology, observed “animalcules” using simple microscope with one lens. He drew what hesaw. He discovered protozoa, blood cells and sperms. He was the first who properly described the different shapes of bacteria. Although Leeuwenhoek was not concerned about the origin of microorganism; many other scientists were searching for an explanation for spontaneous appearance of living things from decaying meat, stagnating ponds, fermenting grains and infected wounds.</p>
<p>On the basis of this observation, two major theories were formulated.</p>
<ol>
<li>Theory of Abiogenesis</li>
<li>Theory of Biogenesis</li>
</ol>
<p>Theory of Abiogenesis deals with the theory of spontaneous generation; stating that living things originated from non-living matter.</p>
<p>Aristotle (384–322 BC): The founder of the theory spontaneous generation. He observed spontaneous existence of fishes from dried ponds, when the pond was filled with rain.</p>
<p>Francesco Redi (1626–1697): He is the scientist who first tried to set an experiment to disprove spontaneous generation.</p>
<ul>
<li>He put the meat in a bottle and covered it with a gauze.</li>
<li>He observed that the flies laid eggs from which the maggots developed.</li>
<li>He proved maggots did not develop from meat but from fly’s eggs.</li>
</ul>
<p>Theory of Biogenesis states that life comes from pre-existing life.</p>
<p>Louis Pasteur (1822–1895) was the scientist who disproved the theory of abiogenesis. He designed a large curved flask (Pasteur goose neck flask) and placed a sterile growth broth medium. Air freely moved through the tube; but dust particles were trapped in the curved portion of flask. Microbial growth in the broth was not seen. Therefore Pasteur proved that micro-organisms entered to substrates through the air and micro-organisms did not evolve spontaneously.</p>

<p>The germ theory of disease</p>
<p>Is the currently accepted scientific theory for many diseases. It states that microorganisms known as pathogens or "germs" can lead to disease.</p>
<p>The complete establishment of the germ theory of disease depended on the work of a Germans scientist, Robert Koch (1843-1910).</p>
<p>Major achievements of Robert Koch</p>
<ol>
<li>Discovery and use of solid medium in bacteriology</li>
<li>Discovery of causative agents of tuberculosis and cholera.</li>
<li>Koch's phenomenon</li>
<li>Koch's postulates</li>
</ol>
<p>Koch's postulates: proof of germ theory of disease</p>
<p>A micro-organism can be accepted as a causative agent of an infectious disease only if the following conditions are satisfied.</p>
<ol>
<li>The micro-organism should be found in every case of the disease and under conditions which explain the pathological changes and clinical features.</li>
<li>It should be possible to isolate the causative agent in pure culture from the lesion.</li>
<li>When such pure culture is inoculated into appropriate laboratory animal, the lesion of the disease should be reproduced.</li>
<li>It should be possible to re-isolate the bacterium in pure culture from the lesion produced in the experimental animal.</li>
<li>Nowadays additional postulate is mentioned i.e. Specific antibody to the bacterium should be detectable in the serum during the course of the disease.</li>
</ol>
<p>It has not been possible to fulfil every one of Koch's postulates, but by adhering to them as closely as possible, serious errors have been prevented.</p>
<p>Exceptions to Koch's postulates</p>
<ol>
<li>Many healthy people carry pathogens but do not exhibit symptoms of the disease.</li>
<li>Some microbes are very difficult or impossible to grow in vitro (in the laboratory) in artificial media e.g. Treponema pallidum</li>
<li>Many species are species specific e.g. Brucella abortus cause abortion in animals but no report in humans.</li>
<li>Certain diseases develop only when an opportunistic pathogen invades immunocompromised host.</li>
</ol>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="taxonomic-classification-of-organisms">TAXONOMIC CLASSIFICATION OF ORGANISMS<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#taxonomic-classification-of-organisms" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>TAXONOMY is the science of organismal classification.</p>
<p>Classification is the assignment of organisms (species) into an organized scheme of naming. Ideally these schemes are based on evolutionary relationships (i.e. the more similar the name, the closer the evolutionary relationships).</p>
<p>TAXON: -A group or category of related organisms. Two key characteristics of taxa are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Members of lower level taxa (e.g. Species) are more similar to each other than are members of higher level taxa (e.g. Kingdom or domain).</li>
<li>Member of specific taxa are more similar to each other than any are to members of different specific taxa found at the same hierarchical level (e.g. Humans are more similar to apes, i.e., comparison between species, than either is similar to, for example, Escherichia coli). Thus once you know that two individuals are member of the same taxon, you can ascertain similarities between the two organisms.</li>
</ul>
<p>NOTE that taxa are dynamic, changing as our knowledge of organism and evolutionary relationships change</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="binomial-nomenclature">BINOMIAL NOMENCLATURE<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#binomial-nomenclature" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Organisms are named using binomial nomenclature (viruses are exceptions). Binomial nomenclature employs the names of the two level taxa, genus and species, to name aspecie. Binomial nomenclature includes:</p>
<p>i. Genus comes before species (e.g., Escherichia coli)
ii. Genus name is always capitalized (e.g., Escherichia)
iii. Species name is never capitalized (e.g., coli)
iv. Both names are always either italicized or underlined (e.g. Escherichia coli)
v. The genus name may be used alone, but not the species name (i.e. saying or writing "Escherichia" alone is legitimate while saying or writing "coli" is not)
vi. The genus name may be abbreviated and the species written in full; e.g. Staphylococcus aureus is written as S. aureus.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="pathogenecity-and-host-defences">PATHOGENECITY AND HOST DEFENCES<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#pathogenecity-and-host-defences" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>A pathogen is defined as an organism causing disease to its host. The severity of the disease symptoms is referred to as virulence. Pathogens are taxonomically widely diverse and comprise viruses and bacteria as well as unicellular and multicellular eukaryotes. Every living organism is affected by pathogens, including bacteria, which are targeted by specialized viruses called phages.</p>
<p>A pathogen may also be referred to as an infectious agent, or simply a germ.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="host-pathogen-interactions">Host-pathogen interactions<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#host-pathogen-interactions" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Host-pathogen interactions provide information that can help understand disease pathogenesis, the biology of one or many pathogens, as well as the biology of the host.</p>
<p>The interaction is referred to as symbiosis.</p>
<p>Symbiosis is a neutral, antagonistic or synergistic relationship between two dissimilar organisms (symbiotes, symbionts) living in close association with each other.</p>
<p>There are three forms of symbiotic relationships that can occur at an anatomical level.</p>
<ul>
<li>Mutualism (+/+): mutually beneficial relationship between two species: In mutualism, both the microorganism and the body work together. An example of this relationship would be cows and the bacteria in their rumen. Bacterial cellulase facilitates digestion in the animal, while the bacteria benefit from nutrients in the rumen.</li>
<li>Commensalism (+/0): relationship between two species in which one is benefited and the other is not affected, neither negatively nor positively: In commensalism, either the body or the microorganism benefits, while the other is not affected by the interaction. Examples of this include microorganisms that make up the normal flora that inhabit the eyes. These organisms thrive on secretions and dead cells, but do not affect the host.</li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li>Parasitism (+/-): relationship between two species in which one benefits (parasite) from the other (host); usually involves detriment to the host: In parasitism, one organism benefits at the expense of the other. For example, parasites use the gastrointestinal tract of a human or animal as an environment in which to reproduce.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="the-body-s-normal-flora">The Body's Normal Flora<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-body-s-normal-flora" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The body contains two types of normal flora:</p>
<ol>
<li>Resident flora (survive for extended periods), and</li>
<li>Transient flora (temporary).</li>
</ol>
<p>Normal flora help to provide defenses against invading pathogens by covering adherence sites, producing compounds toxic to other organisms, and preventing pathogens from consuming available nutrients. For disease to occur there must be a change in the body's environment, which, in turn, allows the pathogen to overcome the normal flora. This can occur through a change in the pH of the body or elimination of normal flora due to antibiotics.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="host-defenses">Host Defenses<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#host-defenses" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>A microorganism will not be able to invade unless it overcomes individual's host defenses. Specific host defenses may include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Skin and mucosal secretions</li>
<li>Non-specific local responses (e.g., pH)</li>
<li>Non-specific inflammatory responses</li>
<li>Specific immune responses (e.g., lymphocytes)</li>
</ul>
<p>The ability for a pathogen to overcome host defenses can be accomplished by two distinct components: a primary pathogen (causes disease in a healthy host) or opportunistic pathogen (causes disease if host is immunocompromised).</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="pathogen-defenses">Pathogen Defenses<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#pathogen-defenses" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Pathogens contain virulence factors that promote disease formation and provide the opportunity for a microbe to infect and cause disease. The greater the virulence, the more likely disease will occur. Such factors include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Ability of a pathogen to adhere to a host</li>
<li>Ability of a pathogen to colonize (overcome) a host</li>
<li>Ability of a pathogen to evade host defenses</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="mechanisms-of-pathogenesis">Mechanisms of Pathogenesis<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#mechanisms-of-pathogenesis" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Pathogenesis is the method by which a disease can develop. This can occur through foodborne intoxication where the causative agent produces toxins in the body (e.g., botulism). Another route is the colonization of an invading pathogen on the host surface, which allows the pathogen to increase in numbers and produce toxins that are damaging to the host's cells (e.g., Vibrio and Corynebacterium).</p>

<p>The relationship between a host and pathogen is dynamic. Production of disease occurs through a process of steps. The first five mechanisms make up a pathogen's invasiveness (i.e., ability to invade tissues).</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="transmission">Transmission<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#transmission" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>In order to begin infection and eventually cause disease, pathogens must find a transmission route. Transmission of an infectious agent can occur in many ways, but it is typically through exposed skin (e.g., a cut, abrasion, puncture, or wound) or mucous membranes (e.g., gastrointestinal tract, respiratory tract, or urogenital tract).</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="adherence">Adherence<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#adherence" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Once the pathogen has gained access to the body, it must have some means of attaching itself to the host's tissues. This attachment is called adherence and is a necessary step in pathogenicity. Microbes contain ligands, which are projections that attach host receptors or surface proteins.</p>
<p>Pathogens may have specific adherence mechanisms to attach to cells or tissue surfaces. Examples of this include:</p>
<ol>
<li>Tissue tropism (i.e., pathogens that prefer specific tissues over others),</li>
<li>Species specificity (i.e., pathogens that only infect certain species), and</li>
<li>Genetic specificity (i.e., surface mutations that occur so previous antibodies do not recognize the invading pathogen). If a microorganism cannot adhere to a host cell membrane, disease will not occur.</li>
</ol>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="invasion">Invasion<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#invasion" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>At this point, microbes begin to invade the host and produce a bacteremia (i.e., presence of bacteria in the bloodstream) or viremia (presence of a virus in the bloodstream). Microorganisms are exposed to many barriers after introduction into the host. Some bacteria are able to cause disease while remaining on the epithelial barriers, while many need to penetrate that barrier.</p>
<p>Once this barrier has been penetrated, these pathogens can multiply without competition.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="colonization">Colonization<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#colonization" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Colonization is the multiplication of pathogenic organisms where toxins are produced and the normal flora are overcome. During this stage, pathogens compete with normal flora for space and nutrients. Pathogens usually colonize host tissues that are in contact with the external environment. During colonization, the host begins to show signs of septicemia (i.e., blood infection where bacteria are reproducing). For infection to proceed an infectious dose should be determined. This is the minimal number of microbes necessary to establish infection. Certain pathogens are less contagious and therefore require larger numbers of pathogens to cause disease (i.e., 10-100 for Shigella and 1,000,000 for Salmonella).</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="mechanisms-of-pathogenesis-infectious-diseases-the-basics">Mechanisms of pathogenesis. Infectious Diseases: The Basics<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#mechanisms-of-pathogenesis-infectious-diseases-the-basics" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>An infectious disease is a clinically evident deviation from health. It occurs when there is a parasitic relationship between a host and a microorganism. Several different factors influence amicoorganism's relationship to its host and level of severity. These include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Pathogenicity: The ability to produce disease in a host organism.</li>
<li>Pathogenesis Is the origin and development of a disease and the chain of events leading to the disease.</li>
<li>Virulence: The degree of pathogenicity of a microorganism. Determinants of virulence for a pathogen include a pathogen's genetic, biochemical, or structural features. For example, one strain of influenza may only cause a fever and sore throat, while another may cause pneumonia or other serious respiratory condition.</li>
<li>Infectivity: The level at which a microorganism is able to infect or invade a host.</li>
<li>Transmissibility: The measure of a microorganism's ability to spread from one host to the next. This can include both distance and number of affected individuals.</li>
</ul>

<p>Infections can be either <strong>subclinical</strong> or <strong>inapparent</strong>. If clinical signs are seen, disease occurs. The incubation period is the time between infection and first appearance of signs or symptoms. This period depends on the dose level of microbes, microbe type, virulence, and host health.</p>
<p>The prodromal period of 1 to 2 days follows incubation in some diseases. This includes early, mild signs/symptoms such as, fatigue, muscle aches, and/or headache. Recovery occurs when signs/symptoms are subsiding but the host may remain more susceptible to secondary, opportunistic infections. During the convalescent period, the body returns to a pre-diseased state.</p>
<p><strong>Nosocomial infections</strong>: an infection acquired in a hospital setting that was not present in the host prior to admission, generally occurring within 72 hours of admission.</p>

<p>(a) Exotoxins are proteins produced inside pathogenic bacteria, most commonly gram-positive bacteria, as part of their growth and metabolism. The exotoxins are then secreted or released into the surrounding medium following lysis.</p>
<p><img src="assets/images/image-20251215-1b850c1d.jpeg" alt="img-4.jpeg"></p>
<p>(b) Endotoxins are the lipid portions of lipopolysaccharides (LPSs) that are part of the outer membrane of the cell wall of gram-negative bacteria (lipid A; see Figure 4.13c). The endotoxins are liberated when the bacteria die and the cell wall breaks apart.</p>
