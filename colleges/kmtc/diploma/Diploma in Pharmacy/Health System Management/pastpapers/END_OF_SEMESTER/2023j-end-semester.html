41.
Differentiate between monitoring and evaluation (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Timing: Monitoring is continuous and ongoing during implementation; Evaluation is periodic (e.g., mid-term, end-term, post-project).
  • Focus: Monitoring focuses on inputs, activities, and outputs (efficiency); Evaluation focuses on outcomes and impacts (effectiveness and relevance).
  • Purpose: Monitoring is for immediate corrective action and management; Evaluation is for learning, policy-making, and assessing overall success.
  • Scope: Monitoring is usually internal (conducted by project staff); Evaluation often involves external/independent assessors.
  • Questions: Monitoring asks "Are we doing things right?"; Evaluation asks "Are we doing the right things?"
42.
State any five steps involved in project management cycle (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Project Identification/Initiation: Defining the problem, conducting needs assessment, and approving the project concept.
  2. Project Planning/Design: Developing the project roadmap, WBS, schedule, budget, and resource plan.
  3. Project Implementation/Execution: Carrying out the planned activities and producing deliverables.
  4. Project Monitoring and Control: Tracking progress, managing risks, and ensuring the project stays on course.
  5. Project Closure/Evaluation: Handing over deliverables, releasing resources, and reviewing lessons learned.
43.
Describe the following concepts:
(i) Health information system
(ii) Health system development
Show Answer

Answer:

(i) Health Information System (HIS):
A system that integrates data collection, processing, reporting, and use of the information necessary for improving health service effectiveness and efficiency. It collects data from the health sector and other relevant sectors, analyses it and ensures its overall quality, relevance and timeliness, and converts data into information for health-related decision-making.

(ii) Health System Development:
The process of strengthening the health system to improve health outcomes. It involves designing and implementing changes to the six building blocks (service delivery, workforce, information, medical products, financing, governance) to ensure the system provides equitable, efficient, and sustainable services that improve the health status of the population.

44.
Outline any five (5) principles of a health systems.
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Equity: Ensuring fair distribution of health services and resources so that everyone has a fair opportunity to attain their full health potential.
  2. Accessibility: Services must be geographically, financially, and culturally accessible to the population.
  3. Efficiency: Maximizing the health benefits gained from available resources (value for money).
  4. Quality: Providing care that is safe, effective, patient-centered, timely, efficient, and equitable.
  5. Sustainability: The ability of the health system to maintain service coverage and quality over the long term.
  6. Participation: Involving communities and stakeholders in health decision-making.
45.
Explain any THREE building blocks of a health system. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer: (Any 3)

  1. Service Delivery: Good health services are those which deliver effective, safe, quality personal and non-personal health interventions to those who need them, when and where needed, with minimum waste of resources.
  2. Health Workforce: A well-performing health workforce is one which works in ways that are responsive, fair and efficient to achieve the best health outcomes possible, given available resources and circumstances.
  3. Health Information System: Ensuring the production, analysis, dissemination and use of reliable and timely information on health determinants, health system performance and health status.
  4. Medical Products, Vaccines and Technologies: Ensuring equitable access to essential medical products, vaccines and technologies of assured quality, safety, efficacy and cost-effectiveness.
  5. Health Financing: Raising adequate funds for health, in ways that ensure people can use needed services, and are protected from financial catastrophe or impoverishment associated with having to pay for them.
  6. Leadership and Governance: Ensuring strategic policy frameworks exist and are combined with effective oversight, coalition-building, the provision of appropriate regulations and incentives, attention to system-design, and accountability.
46.
Distinguish between: Health system strengthening and Health system performance (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

Health System Strengthening (HSS) refers to the strategies, investments, and activities aimed at improving the functions of the health system (the inputs and processes). It is the effort to fix or improve the six building blocks, such as training staff, building clinics, or improving supply chains.

Health System Performance refers to the actual results or outcomes achieved by the system. It is the measurement of how well the system is meeting its goals (e.g., coverage rates, reduced mortality, patient satisfaction). Performance is the "output/outcome" of the strengthening "inputs".

47.
Distinguish between 'imprest' and 'per diem'
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Imprest: A standing sum of money advanced to a person or department for making minor payments (petty cash) or for a specific activity. It operates on a replenishment basis: the holder must account for every shilling spent with receipts, and the spent amount is reimbursed to restore the float to its original level.
  • Per Diem (Daily Subsistence Allowance): A fixed daily allowance paid to an employee to cover living expenses (accommodation, meals, incidentals) when travelling on official duty. It is an entitlement based on the number of days away and usually does not require accountability (receipts) for the specific meals eaten, provided the travel itself is authorized and undertaken.
48.
Explain the role of a vote-book and voucher in financial management (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Vote-book: This is a commitment ledger used in public financial management. Its primary role is to control expenditure against the approved budget (the "vote"). Before any expense is incurred, it is entered in the vote book to ensure sufficient funds are available. It records the total budget, actual payments, and commitments (orders placed but not yet paid), thus preventing overspending.
  • Voucher: A payment voucher is an internal document used to authorize a payment. Its role is to provide a formal audit trail. It details the nature of the transaction, the amount, the payee, and the budget line to be charged. It must be approved by authorized signatories and attached to supporting documents (invoices, LPOs) to prove the validity of the payment.

49.

A) Discuss FIVE advantages of telemedicine in achieving healthcare for all (10 marks)

B) Describe the project risk management process (10 marks)

Show Answer

A) Advantages of Telemedicine in Achieving Healthcare for All:

  1. Overcoming Geographical Barriers: Telemedicine allows patients in rural, remote, or underserved areas to access specialist care without the need for long-distance travel. This is crucial for achieving equity in health access.
  2. Cost Effectiveness: It significantly reduces travel and accommodation costs for patients. For the health system, it can optimize the use of specialists (who can serve multiple remote sites from one hub) and reduce unnecessary hospital admissions through early intervention.
  3. Improved Timeliness of Care: Patients can receive consultations and diagnoses much faster than waiting for a physical appointment or travel. This leads to earlier treatment initiation and better health outcomes.
  4. Capacity Building and Training: Telemedicine platforms are used for tele-mentoring, where specialists in national referral hospitals guide and train primary care workers. This upskills the local workforce, improving the quality of care available at the grassroots level.
  5. Continuity of Care for Chronic Conditions: It facilitates easier follow-up for patients with chronic diseases (diabetes, hypertension) through remote monitoring. This improves adherence to treatment and reduces complications, supporting the goal of comprehensive care.

B) The Project Risk Management Process:

Risk management is the systematic process of identifying, analyzing, and responding to project risks. The steps include:

  1. Risk Management Planning: Deciding how to approach and conduct risk management activities for the project. This involves defining the methodology, roles, and responsibilities.
  2. Risk Identification: Determining which risks might affect the project and documenting their characteristics. This involves brainstorming, SWOT analysis, and reviewing past projects to list potential threats (e.g., budget cuts, staff turnover).
  3. Qualitative Risk Analysis: Prioritizing risks by assessing their probability of occurrence and their potential impact. This typically uses a Risk Matrix (High/Medium/Low) to focus attention on the most critical risks.
  4. Quantitative Risk Analysis: Numerically analyzing the effect of identified risks on overall project objectives (cost/time). This helps in determining the necessary contingency reserves.
  5. Risk Response Planning: Developing options and actions to enhance opportunities and reduce threats. Strategies include:
    • Avoid: Changing the plan to eliminate the risk entirely.
    • Mitigate: Reducing the probability or impact to an acceptable level.
    • Transfer: Shifting the risk to a third party (e.g., insurance, outsourcing).
    • Accept: Acknowledging the risk and establishing a contingency plan if it occurs.
  6. Risk Monitoring and Control: Tracking identified risks, monitoring residual risks, identifying new risks, executing risk response plans, and evaluating their effectiveness throughout the project life cycle.
