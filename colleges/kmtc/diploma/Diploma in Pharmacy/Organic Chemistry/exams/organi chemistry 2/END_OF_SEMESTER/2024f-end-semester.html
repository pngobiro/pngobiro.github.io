Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions

40 Marks
1.
Which reaction do Primary alcohols undergo to form alkenes?
a) Reduction
b) Hydrolysis
c) Oxidation
d) Elimination
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Elimination

Alcohols undergo dehydration (loss of water) in the presence of acid catalysts (like conc. $H_2SO_4$) to form alkenes. This is a type of elimination reaction.

  • Oxidation converts primary alcohols to aldehydes or carboxylic acids.
  • Reduction is not a typical reaction for alcohols (they are already reduced compared to carbonyls).
2.
Which alkyl halide has the highest reactivity for a particular alkyl group?
a) R-Br
b) R-I
c) R-Cl
d) R-F
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) R-I

The reactivity of alkyl halides depends on the bond strength of the Carbon-Halogen bond. The bond strength decreases as the size of the halogen increases.

Order of Reactivity: R-I > R-Br > R-Cl > R-F

The C-I bond is the weakest and breaks most easily, making alkyl iodides the most reactive in nucleophilic substitution reactions.

3.
Which of the following is not present in Grignard reagent?
a) -COOH group
b) Halogen
c) Magnesium
d) Methyl group
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) -COOH group

A Grignard reagent has the general formula R-Mg-X.

  • R = Alkyl or Aryl group (e.g., Methyl group).
  • Mg = Magnesium.
  • X = Halogen.

It does not contain a carboxylic acid (-COOH) group. In fact, Grignard reagents are destroyed by acidic protons found in -COOH groups.

c) $SO_2, Cl_2$

Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) $SOCl_2$

Thionyl chloride ($SOCl_2$) is the preferred reagent for converting carboxylic acids to acid chlorides.

Reaction: $C_6H_5COOH + SOCl_2 \rightarrow C_6H_5COCl + SO_2 + HCl$

Pyridine is a six-membered heterocyclic ring containing five carbon atoms and one Nitrogen atom. Its formula is $C_5H_5N$.

6.
Which of the following is a distinguishing test for ethers?
a) Lucas test
b) $FeCl_3$ test
c) Molisch test
d) Zeisel test
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Zeisel test

The Zeisel's method is used quantitatively to estimate alkoxy groups (ethers). It involves heating the ether with hydriodic acid (HI).

  • Lucas test: Distinguishes alcohols.
  • $FeCl_3$ test: Distinguishes phenols.
  • Molisch test: Distinguishes carbohydrates.
7.
Which of the following statement is not true about ethers?
a) The lower ethers are highly volatile and flammable
b) Lower ethers also act as anaesthetics
c) Ethers are not organic solvents
d) Simple ethers (such as diethyl ether) are tasteless
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Ethers are not organic solvents

This statement is false. Ethers (especially diethyl ether) are excellent and widely used organic solvents because they are relatively inert and can dissolve a wide range of organic compounds.

8.
Why is ether more volatile than an alcohol having the same molecular formula?
a) dipolar character of ethers
b) alcohols having resonance structures
c) inter-molecular hydrogen bonding in ethers
d) inter-molecular hydrogen bonding in alcohols
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) inter-molecular hydrogen bonding in alcohols

Alcohols contain an -OH group which allows them to form strong hydrogen bonds with each other, raising their boiling points. Ethers lack the -OH proton and cannot form hydrogen bonds with themselves, leading to much weaker intermolecular forces and higher volatility.

Diethyl ether has a characteristic sweet, pungent solvent odor.

10.
Carboxylic acids are more acidic than phenols and alcohols due to?
a) Intermolecular hydrogen bonding
b) Formation of dimers
c) Highly acidic hydrogen
d) Resonance stabilization of their conjugate base
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Resonance stabilization of their conjugate base

When a carboxylic acid loses a proton, it forms a carboxylate ion ($RCOO^-$). This ion is stabilized by resonance where the negative charge is delocalized over two equivalent electronegative oxygen atoms. Phenoxide ions are also stabilized by resonance but less effectively (charge on carbon). Alkoxide ions have no resonance stabilization.

11.
Which class of compounds shows H-bonding even more than in alcohols?
a) Phenols
b) Carboxylic acids
c) Ethers
d) Aldehydes
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Carboxylic acids

Carboxylic acids form stronger hydrogen bonds than alcohols. They often exist as dimers (pairs of molecules linked by two H-bonds), even in the vapor phase, leading to significantly higher boiling points than corresponding alcohols.

13.
Acetic acid is weak acid than sulphuric acid because which of the following reasons?
a) It decomposes on increasing temperature
b) It has less degree of ionisation
c) It has -COOH group

Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) It has less degree of ionisation

Acid strength is determined by the extent of dissociation in water. Sulphuric acid is a strong acid that ionizes completely. Acetic acid is a weak organic acid that only partially ionizes in solution (low degree of ionization).

18.
Which of the following compound has more boiling point than ketones?
a) Alcohol
b) Alkanes
c) Aldehyde
d) Ether
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) Alcohol

Alcohols form intermolecular hydrogen bonds, which are stronger than the dipole-dipole interactions found in ketones and aldehydes. Thus, alcohols have higher boiling points than ketones of comparable molecular weight.

19.
Which of the following is more volatile?
a) Carboxylic acid
b) Alcohol
c) Ketones
d) Aldehyde
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Aldehyde

Volatility is inversely related to boiling point. Aldehydes generally have lower boiling points than comparable ketones, alcohols, and acids due to weaker intermolecular forces (dipole-dipole vs H-bonding in acids/alcohols). Among aldehydes and ketones, aldehydes are often slightly more volatile.

20.
The product(s) obtained via oxymercuration ($HgSO_4 + H_2SO_4$) of 1-butyne would be what?
a) $CH_3CH_2COCH_3$
b) $CH_3CH_2CH_2CHO$
c) $CH_3CH_2CHO + HCHO$
d) $CH_3CH_2COOH + HCOOH$
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) $CH_3CH_2COCH_3$

Hydration of terminal alkynes (like 1-butyne) follows Markovnikov's rule. The -OH adds to the more substituted carbon, forming an enol which tautomerizes to a ketone. 1-butyne yields 2-butanone (ethyl methyl ketone).

Section 2: Short Answer Questions

40 Marks
41.
Define the following:
i. constitutional isomers
ii. stereoisomers
iii. enantiomers
iv. diastereomers
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Constitutional Isomers (Structural Isomers): Compounds that have the same molecular formula but differ in the connectivity or bonding sequence of their atoms (e.g., butane and isobutane).
  2. Stereoisomers: Compounds that have the same molecular formula and the same connectivity of atoms but differ in the 3D spatial arrangement of atoms in space.
  3. Enantiomers: A pair of stereoisomers that are non-superimposable mirror images of each other. They typically have chiral centers.
  4. Diastereomers: Stereoisomers that are not mirror images of each other. This includes cis-trans isomers and compounds with multiple chiral centers that are not enantiomers.
42.
Draw and name structures showing a primary, secondary and tertiary amine.
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Primary Amine ($1^\circ$): Nitrogen attached to 1 carbon.
    Example: Methylamine ($CH_3NH_2$)
    Structure: $H_3C - NH_2$
  • Secondary Amine ($2^\circ$): Nitrogen attached to 2 carbons.
    Example: Dimethylamine ($(CH_3)_2NH$)
    Structure: $H_3C - NH - CH_3$
  • Tertiary Amine ($3^\circ$): Nitrogen attached to 3 carbons.
    Example: Trimethylamine ($(CH_3)_3N$)
    Structure: N bonded to three $CH_3$ groups.
43.
Describe the aldol and claisen condensation.
Show Answer

Answer:

Aldol Condensation:

  • A reaction between two molecules of an aldehyde or ketone having at least one $\alpha$-hydrogen atom.
  • Catalyzed by dilute base (or acid).
  • Product: $\beta$-hydroxy aldehyde (aldol) or $\beta$-hydroxy ketone. Dehydration usually follows to form an $\alpha,\beta$-unsaturated carbonyl.
  • Example: $2 CH_3CHO \xrightarrow{OH^-} CH_3CH(OH)CH_2CHO$

Claisen Condensation:

  • A reaction between two ester molecules having $\alpha$-hydrogen atoms.
  • Catalyzed by a strong base (like alkoxide).
  • Product: $\beta$-keto ester.
  • Example: $2 CH_3COOC_2H_5 \xrightarrow{EtO^-} CH_3COCH_2COOC_2H_5 + C_2H_5OH$
44.
Draw the following heterocyclic compounds.
i. Furan
ii. Pyrrolidine
iii. Thiophene
iv. Pyrrole
v. Pyrazole
Show Answer

Answer:

All are 5-membered rings.

  • Furan: 5-membered aromatic ring with 1 Oxygen. (Double bonds at 2-3 and 4-5).
  • Pyrrolidine: 5-membered saturated ring with 1 Nitrogen (NH). No double bonds.
  • Thiophene: 5-membered aromatic ring with 1 Sulfur. (Double bonds at 2-3 and 4-5).
  • Pyrrole: 5-membered aromatic ring with 1 Nitrogen (NH). (Double bonds at 2-3 and 4-5).
  • Pyrazole: 5-membered aromatic ring with 2 Nitrogens adjacent to each other (1,2-diazole).

46.
List three properties of alcohols.
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. High Boiling Points: Due to intermolecular hydrogen bonding, they have higher boiling points than corresponding hydrocarbons or ethers.
  2. Solubility: Lower alcohols are soluble in water due to H-bonding with water molecules. Solubility decreases as the alkyl chain grows.
  3. Acidity: They are weakly acidic ($pK_a \approx 16-18$) and react with active metals (Na, K) to release Hydrogen gas.
  4. Combustibility: They burn with a blue flame to produce $CO_2$ and water.

49.
Distinguish between polycyclic and heterocyclic compounds. (2 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Polycyclic Compounds: Organic compounds containing two or more fused or bridged rings (e.g., Naphthalene, Anthracene). The rings can be carbocyclic (all carbon) or heterocyclic.
  • Heterocyclic Compounds: Cyclic compounds where at least one atom in the ring structure is a heteroatom (not Carbon), such as Nitrogen, Oxygen, or Sulfur (e.g., Pyridine, Furan). A compound can be both polycyclic and heterocyclic (e.g., Quinoline).

20 Marks
50.
a) Write five reactions in which ketones and aldehydes are involved in.
b) Outline five uses and the physical chemical properties of ketones and aldehydes. (20 Marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

a) Five Reactions of Aldehydes and Ketones:

  1. Nucleophilic Addition (HCN): React with HCN to form Cyanohydrins.
    $RCHO + HCN \rightarrow RCH(OH)CN$
  2. Oxidation:
    Aldehydes $\xrightarrow{[O]}$ Carboxylic Acids (Tollen's/Fehling's test).
    Ketones are resistant to mild oxidation.
  3. Reduction:
    To Alcohols: Using $NaBH_4$ or $LiAlH_4$ (Aldehydes $\rightarrow 1^\circ$, Ketones $\rightarrow 2^\circ$).
    To Hydrocarbons: Clemmensen Reduction ($Zn-Hg/HCl$) or Wolff-Kishner ($NH_2NH_2/KOH$).
  4. Aldol Condensation: Reaction of carbonyls with $\alpha$-hydrogens in base to form $\beta$-hydroxy carbonyls.
  5. Reaction with Grignard Reagents:
    Formaldehyde $\rightarrow 1^\circ$ Alcohol
    Other Aldehydes $\rightarrow 2^\circ$ Alcohol
    Ketones $\rightarrow 3^\circ$ Alcohol

b) Physical Chemical Properties & Uses:

Properties:

  1. Boiling Point: Higher than alkanes/ethers due to dipole-dipole interactions, but lower than alcohols (no H-bonding).
  2. Solubility: Lower members are soluble in water due to H-bonding with water. Solubility decreases with chain length.
  3. Odor: Lower aldehydes are pungent; higher ones and ketones often have pleasant/sweet smells.
  4. State: Methanal is gas; ethanal is volatile liquid; others are liquids/solids.

Uses:

  1. Formaldehyde: 40% solution (Formalin) used as biological preservative and disinfectant. Also for resins (Bakelite).
  2. Acetone (Propanone): Crucial industrial solvent (paints, nail polish remover) and raw material.
  3. Benzaldehyde: Flavoring agent (Almond essence), perfumes, and dye manufacture.
  4. Acetaldehyde: Production of acetic acid and mirrors.
  5. Solvents: General solvents in chromatography and organic synthesis.
