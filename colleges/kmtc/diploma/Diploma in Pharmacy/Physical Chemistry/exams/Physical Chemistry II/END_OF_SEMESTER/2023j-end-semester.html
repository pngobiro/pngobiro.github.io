1.
Cobalt-60 decays to nickel-60. What particle is emitted?
A) proton
B) positron
C) Neutron
D) alpha
E) electron
Show Answer

Correct Answer: E) electron

Explanation: Cobalt-60 undergoes beta-minus decay to become Nickel-60. In beta-minus decay, a neutron converts to a proton and an electron (beta particle). The emitted particle is an electron.

2.
Iodine-131 has a half-life of 8.1 days. If a patient drinks a sodium iodide (NaI) solution containing iodine-131 on a Tuesday, how many days will it take for the concentration of iodine-131 to drop to 5.0% of its initial concentration?
A) 19 days
B) 35 days
C) 0.81 day
D) 4.3 days
E) 8.1 days
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) 35 days

Calculation:

Formula: $N = N_0(0.5)^n$, where $n$ is number of half-lives.

$0.05 = (0.5)^n$

$\ln(0.05) = n \ln(0.5) \implies -2.996 = n(-0.693)$

$n \approx 4.32$ half-lives.

Time = $4.32 \times 8.1 \text{ days} \approx 35 \text{ days}$.

3.
How much phosphorus-32 was originally used if there is only 3.50 mg left in a sample after 288 h? (The half-life of phosphorus-32 is 14.3 days.)
A) 1.96 mg
B) 7.00 mg
C) 6.26 mg
D) 17.9 mg
E) 4.17 mg
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) 6.26 mg

Calculation:

Time elapsed = 288 hours = $288/24 = 12$ days.

Number of half-lives $n = 12 / 14.3 \approx 0.839$.

$N = N_0 (0.5)^n \implies 3.50 = N_0 (0.5)^{0.839}$

$3.50 = N_0 (0.559)$

$N_0 = 3.50 / 0.559 \approx 6.26 \text{ mg}$.

4.
Which one of the following statements is not correct?
A) Carbon-10 is unstable because it has too few neutrons.
B) All nuclides with Z > 83 decay into nuclides with smaller Z values.
C) Generally, the number of neutrons in a nuclide is equal to or less than the atomic number.
D) As the atomic number increases, the ratio of neutrons to protons in a nuclide increases.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Generally, the number of neutrons in a nuclide is equal to or less than the atomic number.

Explanation: This statement is incorrect for most stable isotopes. For light elements, N $\approx$ Z. For heavier elements, N > Z to overcome proton-proton repulsion. Therefore, neutrons are generally equal to or *greater* than the atomic number.

13.
Which of the following statements correctly describes the signs of q and w for the exothermic process $H_2O(g) \rightarrow H_2O(l)$ at 370 K and 1 atm?
A) q and w are both negative
B) q is positive and w is negative
C) q is negative and w is positive
D) q and w are both positive
E) q and w are both zero
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) q is negative and w is positive

Explanation:

  • q (Heat): Exothermic process releases heat, so $q < 0$ (negative).
  • w (Work): Gas converts to liquid, so volume decreases significantly. Work is done *on* the system by surroundings. $w = -P\Delta V$. Since $\Delta V$ is negative, $w$ is positive.

14.
$\mathrm{SF}_{4}(\mathrm{~g})+\mathrm{F}_{2}(\mathrm{~g}) \rightarrow \mathrm{SF}_{6}(\mathrm{~g})$. The reaction is spontaneous at 298 K but non-spontaneous at higher temperatures. Which is true at 298 K?
A) $\Delta G, \Delta H, \Delta S$ are all positive
B) $\Delta G, \Delta H, \Delta S$ are all negative
C) $\Delta G$ and $\Delta H$ negative, $\Delta S$ positive
D) $\Delta G$ and $\Delta S$ negative, $\Delta H$ positive
E) $\Delta G$ and $\Delta H$ positive, $\Delta S$ negative
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) $\Delta G, \Delta H, \Delta S$ are all negative

Explanation:

  • Spontaneous at 298K means $\Delta G < 0$.
  • Non-spontaneous at high T means the $-T\Delta S$ term becomes large and positive, overtaking $\Delta H$. This implies $\Delta S < 0$ (negative).
  • For spontaneous at low T and non-spontaneous at high T, $\Delta H$ must be negative (exothermic).

Calculate enthalpy change for $2\mathrm{H}_{2}(\mathrm{~g})+\mathrm{O}_{2}(\mathrm{~g}) \rightarrow 2\mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O}(\mathrm{g})$ using bond energies: H-H (436), O=O (499), O-H (464).

41.

(i) What is half-life of a chemical reaction? (1 mark)

(ii) If the mass of an antibiotic in a patient is 2.464 g, what mass of antibiotic will remain after 6.0 h, if the half-life is 2.0 h, and no further drug is taken? (4 marks)

Show Answer

Answer:

(i) Half-life: The time required for the concentration (or quantity) of a reactant to decrease to half of its initial value.

(ii) Calculation:

Total time $t = 6.0$ hours.

Half-life $t_{1/2} = 2.0$ hours.

Number of half-lives $n = 6.0 / 2.0 = 3$.

Remaining mass = Initial Mass $\times (1/2)^n$

$= 2.464 \times (1/2)^3 = 2.464 \times (1/8)$

$= 0.308 \text{ g}$.

42.

The activation energy for the gas phase decomposition of t-butyl propionate is 164 kJ. The rate constant at 528 K is $3.80 \times 10^{-4} \text{ s}^{-1}$. What will the rate constant be at 569 K? (5 marks)

Show Answer

Answer:

Use Arrhenius Equation: $\ln(\frac{k_2}{k_1}) = \frac{E_a}{R}(\frac{1}{T_1} - \frac{1}{T_2})$

Given:

  • $k_1 = 3.80 \times 10^{-4} \text{ s}^{-1}$
  • $T_1 = 528 \text{ K}$
  • $T_2 = 569 \text{ K}$
  • $E_a = 164000 \text{ J/mol}$
  • $R = 8.314 \text{ J/mol K}$

$\ln(\frac{k_2}{k_1}) = \frac{164000}{8.314}(\frac{1}{528} - \frac{1}{569})$

$\ln(\frac{k_2}{k_1}) = 19725.76 \times (0.0018939 - 0.0017575)$

$\ln(\frac{k_2}{k_1}) = 19725.76 \times 0.0001364 = 2.69$

$\frac{k_2}{k_1} = e^{2.69} = 14.73$

$k_2 = 14.73 \times (3.80 \times 10^{-4}) = \mathbf{5.60 \times 10^{-3} \text{ s}^{-1}}$

43.

Determine the free energy of the cell that has the reaction below (5 marks)

$Cu^{2+}(aq) + Fe(s) \longrightarrow Cu(s) + Fe^{2+}(aq)$

Given:

$Cu^{2+}(aq) + 2e \longrightarrow Cu \quad \epsilon^0 = 0.34 \text{ V}$

$Fe^{2+}(aq) + 2e \longrightarrow Fe \quad \epsilon^0 = -0.44 \text{ V}$

Show Answer

Answer:

1. Calculate Standard Cell Potential ($E^0_{cell}$):

Cathode (Reduction): $Cu^{2+} + 2e \to Cu$ ($E = 0.34 \text{ V}$)

Anode (Oxidation): $Fe \to Fe^{2+} + 2e$ ($E_{ox} = +0.44 \text{ V}$)

$E^0_{cell} = E_{cathode} - E_{anode(red)} = 0.34 - (-0.44) = \mathbf{0.78 \text{ V}}$

2. Calculate Free Energy ($\Delta G^0$):

Formula: $\Delta G^0 = -nFE^0_{cell}$

  • $n = 2$ (moles of electrons)
  • $F = 96500 \text{ C/mol}$
  • $E = 0.78 \text{ V}$

$\Delta G^0 = -2 \times 96500 \times 0.78 = -150,540 \text{ J/mol}$

$\mathbf{\Delta G^0 = -150.54 \text{ kJ/mol}}$

44.
Define (i) Internal energy (2 marks), (ii) Enthalpy of the system (2 marks), (iii) Enthalpy of reaction (1 mark)
Show Answer

Answer:

(i) Internal Energy ($U$): The sum of all kinetic and potential energies of the particles (atoms, molecules) making up a system.

(ii) Enthalpy of System ($H$): A thermodynamic property defined as the sum of the internal energy ($U$) plus the product of pressure and volume ($PV$). $H = U + PV$. It represents the total heat content.

(iii) Enthalpy of Reaction ($\Delta H_r$): The difference between the total enthalpy of the products and the total enthalpy of the reactants in a chemical reaction.

45.
Differentiate between heat and specific heat capacity giving their respective units (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Heat ($q$): A form of energy transfer between two bodies due to a temperature difference. It is an extensive property.
    Unit: Joules (J) or Calories (cal).
  • Specific Heat Capacity ($c$): The amount of heat energy required to raise the temperature of 1 gram of a substance by 1 degree Celsius (or 1 Kelvin). It is an intensive property.
    Unit: $J \cdot g^{-1} \cdot K^{-1}$ or $J/g^{\circ}C$.

47.

(i) Define Electrolysis (1 mark)

(ii) Differentiate between electrolytic and metallic conductors and explain how their resistance vary with temperature (4 marks)

Show Answer

Answer:

(i) Electrolysis: The process of decomposing an electrolyte in a molten state or aqueous solution by passing a direct electric current through it.

(ii) Differences:

  • Metallic Conductors: Conduct via free electrons. No chemical change occurs.
    Temp effect: Resistance increases with temperature (due to increased vibration of lattice ions hindering electron flow).
  • Electrolytic Conductors: Conduct via movement of ions. Chemical decomposition occurs.
    Temp effect: Resistance decreases with temperature (due to decreased viscosity and increased ion mobility).
48.

(i) What is radioactivity (1 mark)

(ii) Discuss radiation measurement using film badge and thermoluminiscence-dosimeter (4 marks)

Show Answer

Answer:

(i) Radioactivity: The spontaneous emission of radiation (particles or energy) from an unstable atomic nucleus to achieve stability.

(ii) Measurement:

  • Film Badge: Contains photographic film encased in a holder. When exposed to radiation, the film darkens (after developing). The degree of darkening indicates the amount of radiation exposure over time. It is cheap and provides a permanent record.
  • Thermoluminescent Dosimeter (TLD): Contains crystals (like LiF) that trap energy from radiation. When heated, these crystals release the trapped energy as light. The intensity of the emitted light is proportional to the radiation dose received. They are more accurate and reusable compared to film badges.

49.

A) State and explain 3 types of radioactive emissions

b) Outline the various methods of detection of radioactivity

c) Describe the applications of radioactivity

Show Answer

Answer:

(A) Types of Radioactive Emissions

1. Alpha Radiation ($\alpha$): Stream of helium nuclei (2 protons, 2 neutrons). Positively charged. Heavy mass. Low penetration (stopped by paper) but high ionization capability.

2. Beta Radiation ($\beta$): Stream of high-speed electrons. Negatively charged. Negligible mass. Moderate penetration (stopped by aluminum foil).

3. Gamma Radiation ($\gamma$): High-energy electromagnetic waves. Neutral charge. No mass. High penetration (requires lead or thick concrete to stop). Lowest ionization power.

(B) Methods of Detection

  • Geiger-Muller Counter: Detects radiation by ionization of gas (Argon) in a tube, creating an electric pulse that produces a click or count.
  • Scintillation Counter: Uses specific crystals (phosphors) that emit flashes of light (scintillations) when struck by radiation. These flashes are converted to electrical signals by a photomultiplier tube.
  • Photographic Film: Radiation darkens photographic plates/film (used in badges for personnel monitoring).
  • Cloud Chamber: Visualizes tracks of ionizing radiation in supersaturated vapor.

(C) Applications of Radioactivity

  • Medicine: Diagnosis (tracers like Technetium-99m) and Treatment (Radiotherapy for cancer using Cobalt-60).
  • Agriculture: Inducing mutations for better crops, tracing fertilizer uptake, sterilizing male insects for pest control.
  • Industry: Measuring thickness of materials (paper/metal sheets), detecting leaks in pipes, checking weld integrity (radiography).
  • Archaeology: Carbon-14 dating to determine the age of fossils and artifacts.
  • Energy: Nuclear power plants generate electricity via fission.
