Calculate the heat of combustion of ethane, in the reaction $\mathrm{C}_{2} \mathrm{H}_{6(g)}+3\frac{1}{2} \mathrm{O}_{2(g)} \rightarrow 2 \mathrm{CO}_{2(g)} + 3 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{(l)}$ where the heats of formation of ethane gas, carbon dioxide gas and water liquid are $-84.7$, $-393.5$ and $-285.8 \text{ kJ mol}^{-1}$ respectively.

20.
A first-order reaction is 50 percent complete in 30 minutes. Calculate the time taken for completion of 87.5 percent of the reaction.
a) 30 minutes
b) 60 minutes
c) 90 minutes
d) 120 minutes
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) 90 minutes

Calculation:

50% complete = 1 half-life = 30 mins.

87.5% complete means 12.5% remains.

100% $\to$ 50% (1st half-life) $\to$ 25% (2nd half-life) $\to$ 12.5% (3rd half-life).

Total time = $3 \times 30 \text{ mins} = 90 \text{ mins}$.

31.
Radioactive material decays by simultaneous emission of two particles with respective half-lives 1620 and 810 years. The time, in years, after which one-fourth of the material remains?
a) 1080
b) 2430
c) 3240
d) 4860
Show Answer

Correct Answer: a) 1080

Calculation:

For simultaneous decay, effective half-life ($t_{eff}$) is: $\frac{1}{t_{eff}} = \frac{1}{t_1} + \frac{1}{t_2}$

$\frac{1}{t_{eff}} = \frac{1}{1620} + \frac{1}{810} = \frac{1}{1620} + \frac{2}{1620} = \frac{3}{1620}$

$t_{eff} = \frac{1620}{3} = 540 \text{ years}$

One-fourth remaining means 25%, which occurs after 2 half-lives.

$Time = 2 \times 540 = 1080 \text{ years}$.

39.
Silver is electrodeposited on a metallic vessel of surface area $800 \text{ cm}^{2}$ by passing a current of 0.2 A for 3 hours. The thickness of silver deposited is _______
a) 0.1 mm
b) 0.02 mm
c) 0.05 mm
d) 0.2 mm
Show Answer

Answer: None of the options match the standard calculation exactly, but calculation is provided below.

Calculation:

1. Calculate Charge ($Q$): $Q = I \times t = 0.2 \text{ A} \times (3 \times 3600 \text{ s}) = 2160 \text{ C}$.

2. Calculate Mass ($m$): $Ag^+ + e^- \to Ag$. (96500 C deposits 107.9 g).
$m = \frac{2160}{96500} \times 107.9 = 2.415 \text{ g}$.

3. Calculate Volume ($V$): Density of Ag $\approx 10.5 \text{ g/cm}^3$.
$V = \frac{mass}{density} = \frac{2.415}{10.5} \approx 0.23 \text{ cm}^3$.

4. Calculate Thickness: $T = \frac{Volume}{Area} = \frac{0.23}{800} = 0.0002875 \text{ cm}$.

Convert to mm: $0.0002875 \text{ cm} \times 10 = 0.0029 \text{ mm}$.

Note: This result is far smaller than the options provided. If the area were $80 cm^2$, thickness would be ~0.029mm (closer to b).

41.

(a) Calculate the oxidation number of chromium in $\mathrm{Cr}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{7}^{2-}$. Given that the oxidation of O is -2 (2mks)

(b) Calculate the electromotive force of a cell consisting of copper and zinc electrodes immersed in the solutions of their respective ions (2 mks)

$\mathrm{Cu}^{2+}+2 \mathrm{e} \rightarrow \mathrm{Cu} \quad E^{0}=+0.34 \text{ volts}$

$\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}+2 \mathrm{e} \rightarrow \mathrm{Zn} \quad E^{0}=-0.76 \text{ volts}$

Show Answer

Answer:

(a) Oxidation Number Calculation:

Let oxidation state of Cr be $x$.

$2x + 7(-2) = -2$ (Total charge of ion)

$2x - 14 = -2$

$2x = +12$

$x = +6$. Oxidation number of Chromium is +6.

(b) EMF Calculation:

Formula: $E_{cell} = E_{cathode} - E_{anode}$ (Reduction Potentials)

Cathode (Higher potential/Reduction): Cu ($+0.34$ V)

Anode (Lower potential/Oxidation): Zn ($-0.76$ V)

$E_{cell} = 0.34 - (-0.76) = 0.34 + 0.76 = \mathbf{+1.10 \text{ V}}$

42.

(a) List four uses of an electrochemical series (4 mks)

(b) The half reactions for the reduction of $\mathrm{Ag}^{+}$ and $\mathrm{Zn}^{2+}$ are as follows:

$Ag^{+}+\mathrm{e} \rightarrow \mathrm{Ag} \quad E^{0}=+0.80 \text{ volts}$

$Zn^{2+}+2 \mathrm{e} \rightarrow \mathrm{Zn} \quad E^{0}=-0.76 \text{ volts}$

Predict whether silver will displace zinc from a solution of its salt (3 mks)

(c) Write briefly on the following 3 factors that affect the preferential discharge of ions during electrolysis:

  • i. Standard reduction potential (2 mks)
  • ii. Concentration (2 mks)
  • iii. Nature of electrodes (2 mks)

(d) Calculate the mass of silver that is formed when a current of 2.4 ampere is passed through a solution of silver nitrate for 15 minutes (atomic mass of silver = 108) (3 mks)

Show Answer

Answer:

(a) Uses of Electrochemical Series:

  • To compare the relative oxidizing and reducing powers of substances.
  • To calculate the Standard EMF of a cell ($E^0$).
  • To predict the feasibility/spontaneity of a redox reaction.
  • To predict whether a metal will displace hydrogen from an acid.

(b) Prediction:

For Silver (Ag) to displace Zinc (Zn), Ag must oxidize to Ag+ and Zn2+ must reduce to Zn.

$E_{cell} = E_{red}(Zn) - E_{red}(Ag) = -0.76 - 0.80 = -1.56V$

Since $E_{cell}$ is negative, the reaction is non-spontaneous. Silver will NOT displace Zinc. (Zinc is more reactive than Silver).

(c) Factors affecting discharge:

  • Standard Reduction Potential: Cations with higher (more positive) reduction potentials are discharged at the cathode in preference to those with lower potentials. Anions with lower oxidation potentials are discharged at the anode.
  • Concentration: If an ion is present in much higher concentration than another, it may be discharged preferentially even if its standard potential suggests otherwise.
  • Nature of Electrodes: Inert electrodes (Pt, Graphite) do not participate. Active electrodes (e.g., Cu anode) may participate in the reaction (oxidize) instead of an anion being discharged.

(d) Mass Calculation:

$Q = I \times t = 2.4 \text{ A} \times (15 \times 60) \text{ s} = 2160 \text{ Coulombs}$

Reaction: $Ag^+ + e^- \rightarrow Ag$. (1 mole e- deposits 1 mole Ag)

1 Faraday (96500 C) deposits 108g Ag.

Mass = $\frac{2160}{96500} \times 108 = \mathbf{2.417 \text{ g}}$

43.

(a) Define the following terms (4 mks): i. Endothermic reactions, ii. Exothermic reactions, iii. System, iv. Surrounding

(b) State the second law of thermodynamics (1 mk)

Show Answer

Answer:

(a) Definitions:

  • Endothermic reaction: A chemical reaction that absorbs heat energy from the surroundings ($\Delta H$ is positive).
  • Exothermic reaction: A chemical reaction that releases heat energy to the surroundings ($\Delta H$ is negative).
  • System: The specific part of the universe that is under study or observation (e.g., reactants in a beaker).
  • Surrounding: Everything else in the universe outside the system boundaries.

(b) Second Law of Thermodynamics:

The total entropy of an isolated system can never decrease over time; for a spontaneous process, the total entropy of the universe ($\Delta S_{total}$) increases.

44.

(a) Describe the following: a. Alpha emission (2 mks), b. Beta emission (2 mks)

(b) Differentiate between the energy of fission and the energy of fusion (4 mks)

(c) Find the cell potential of a galvanic cell based on the following reduction half-reactions at $2^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$:

$\mathrm{Cd}^{2+} + 2 \mathrm{e}^{-} \rightarrow \mathrm{Cd} \quad E^{0}=-0.403 \text{ V}$

$\mathrm{Pb}^{2+} + 2 \mathrm{e}^{-} \rightarrow \mathrm{Pb} \quad E^{0}=-0.126 \text{ V}$

Where $[\mathrm{Cd}^{2+}]=0.020 \mathrm{M}$ and $[\mathrm{Pb}^{2+}]=0.200 \mathrm{M}$ (4 mks)

Show Answer

Answer:

(a) Descriptions:

  • Alpha emission: Decay process where an unstable nucleus emits an alpha particle (Helium nucleus, $^4_2He$). The mass number decreases by 4 and atomic number by 2.
  • Beta emission: Decay process where a neutron turns into a proton and an electron. The electron (beta particle) is emitted. Mass number stays same, atomic number increases by 1.

(b) Fission vs Fusion Energy:

  • Fission: Splitting of a heavy nucleus into lighter nuclei. Releases huge energy per event, but less energy per unit mass compared to fusion. Used in current nuclear power plants.
  • Fusion: Combining light nuclei (like Hydrogen) to form a heavier nucleus. Releases much more energy per unit mass than fission. This is the energy source of the sun.

(c) Cell Potential Calculation:

1. Identify Cathode/Anode: Pb has higher potential ($-0.126 > -0.403$), so Pb is Cathode, Cd is Anode.

2. Calculate $E^0_{cell} = E_{cat} - E_{an} = -0.126 - (-0.403) = \mathbf{+0.277 \text{ V}}$

3. Use Nernst Equation (Assuming standard $25^{\circ}C$ as $2^{\circ}C$ is likely a typo, using 0.0591 coefficient):

Reaction: $Cd + Pb^{2+} \rightarrow Cd^{2+} + Pb$

$E = E^0 - \frac{0.0591}{n} \log Q$

$Q = \frac{[Cd^{2+}]}{[Pb^{2+}]} = \frac{0.020}{0.200} = 0.1$

$E = 0.277 - \frac{0.0591}{2} \log(0.1)$

$E = 0.277 - 0.02955 \times (-1)$

$E = 0.277 + 0.02955 = \mathbf{0.307 \text{ V}}$

45.
Distinguish between a solution, an emulsion and a gel (3 mks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Solution: A homogeneous mixture composed of a solute dissolved in a solvent at the molecular level (particle size < 1 nm). Single phase.
  • Emulsion: A colloidal dispersion of two immiscible liquids (e.g., oil and water), stabilized by an emulsifying agent. Liquid-in-liquid system.

46.
Discuss the types of radioactive emission giving five uses of radioactivity (20 mks)
Show Answer

Answer:

Part 1: Types of Radioactive Emission

1. Alpha Particles ($\alpha$):

  • Consist of 2 protons and 2 neutrons (Helium nucleus).
  • Charge: +2. Mass: 4 amu.
  • Properties: Low penetrating power (stopped by paper), high ionizing power.

2. Beta Particles ($\beta$):

  • High-energy, high-speed electrons emitted from the nucleus.
  • Charge: -1. Mass: Negligible.
  • Properties: Moderate penetrating power (stopped by aluminum foil), moderate ionizing power.

3. Gamma Rays ($\gamma$):

  • High-energy electromagnetic radiation (photons).
  • Charge: 0. Mass: 0.
  • Properties: Very high penetrating power (requires thick lead/concrete), low ionizing power. Often accompanies alpha or beta decay.

Part 2: Uses of Radioactivity (5 Uses)

  1. Medical Diagnosis (Tracers): Radioisotopes like Iodine-131 are used to diagnose thyroid disorders. Technetium-99m is used for bone and organ imaging.
  2. Medical Treatment (Radiotherapy): Cobalt-60 (gamma emitter) is used to target and kill cancer cells.
  3. Sterilization: Gamma rays are used to sterilize medical equipment (syringes, surgical tools) and food by killing bacteria without heat.
  4. Carbon Dating: Carbon-14 isotopes are used to determine the age of archaeological artifacts and fossils.
  5. Industrial Applications: Used to check for cracks in metal welds (gamma radiography) or to control thickness of paper/metal sheets (beta gauging).
