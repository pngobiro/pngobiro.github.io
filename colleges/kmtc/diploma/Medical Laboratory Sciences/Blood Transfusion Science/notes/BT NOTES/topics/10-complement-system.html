THE COMPLEMENT SYSTEM

üìÑ Pathways, Activation & Effects
01

üß¨ Definition and Roles

A term applied to a system of 25 to 30 serum and membrane proteins that act in a cascading manner similar to coagulation, fibrinolytic and kinin systems to produce numerous biologic effects. The participating proteins remain inactive until an event initiates the process, following which the product of one reaction becomes the catalyst for the next step.

Complement's major roles are

  • Promotion of acute inflammatory events
  • Alteration of surfaces so that phagocytosis is enhanced
  • Modification of cell membranes, leading to cell lyses
  • Making bacteria / viruses / fungi more susceptible to phagocytosis
  • Directly lysing some bacteria and foreign cells
  • Producing chemotactic substances
  • Increasing vascular permeability
  • Causing smooth muscle contraction promoting mast cell degranulation

Characteristics:

  • Very unstable
  • Destroyed by heat @ $56^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ for $30 \mathrm{~min}$ .
  • Inactivated by storage for long periods. $4^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ can maintain for $24-48$ hrs.
  • Always fresh samples are used to perform tests involving complements.
  • They cause lyses of cells both in vivo and in vitro in the presence of antigen antibody components
  • Found in normal fresh serum in active state
  • Destroyed or inhibited by most anticoagulants.
  • Plasma should not be used in place of serum in serological tests involving complements. (Anti-coagulants are anticomplementary)

‚ö° Complement Activation Pathways

CLASSICAL PATHWAY

Immune complexes consisting of IgM or IgG molecules when they combine with the antigen on the red cell surface activate classical pathway.
IgM antibodies are more potent because of the pentavalent complex. For the classical pathway of complement activation to occur, an immunoglobulin must react with target antigen.

Mechanism:
The first complement to be activated is C1
When $\mathrm{C}_{1}$ is activated it is broken down into three sub-units $\mathrm{C} 1_{\mathrm{q}}, \mathrm{C} 1_{\mathrm{r}}$, and C1s.
The subunits are activated in sequences $\mathrm{C} 1_{\mathrm{q}}, \mathrm{C} 1_{\mathrm{r}}, \mathrm{C} 1_{\mathrm{s}}$.
Calcium ions are very important.
When activated they form esterase activity (capability of breaking ester bonds.) = Starting of lysing.
$\mathrm{C}_{4}$ is the next complement to be activated.
Magnesium ions are required for this reaction.
Complement $\mathrm{C}_{4}$ activates complements $\mathrm{C}_{2}$.
$\mathrm{C}_{1} \mathrm{C}_{4}, \mathrm{C}_{2}$ are called $\mathrm{C}_{3}$ convertase.
$\mathrm{C}_{3}$ is the most abundant
C3 activates $\mathrm{C} 5 \longrightarrow \mathrm{C} 6 \longrightarrow \mathrm{C} 7$ which circulate together as single molecule (unit) and they activate $\mathrm{C}_{8} \mathrm{C}_{9}$ is activated and cell lyses occurs.

ALTERNATE PATHWAY

Also referred to as Properdin pathway
The alternate pathway allows complement activation in the absence of an antigen / antibody interaction.

Initiators:

  • dialysis membranes,
  • the cell wall (lipolysaccharides) of many bacteria, yeasts and viruses,
  • protein complexes, including those containing antibodies such as dextran;
  • some tumour cells.
  • Snake venom

Four proteins participate in the alternate pathway: factor B, factor D, Properdin (factor P ) and C 3 .

03

üéØ Physiologic Effects

OPSONIZATION

Neutrophils and macrophages phagocytose any particle that protrudes from the surface of a cell or micro-organism with no regard to the nature of the material. Phagocytosis is more intense if the particle adheres firmly to the membrane of the phagocytic cell. To achieve adherence, phagocytic cells have various receptor molecules such as the Fc receptors for certain immunoglobulins and receptors for C3b. the enhancement of phagocytosis resulting from antibody or complement coating of cells is called opsonization.

ANAPHYLATOXINS PROMOTE INFLAMMATION

Complement fragments C3a and C5a are anaphylatoxins and they play an important role in acute inflammation. These anaphylatoxins bind to the receptors on mast cells and basophils, causing them to release histamine and other biologic response modifiers that can be associated with anaphylaxis.

CYTOKINES

They are diverse group of intracellular signalling peptides and glycoproteins that have molecular weights ranging from 6,000 to 60,000 Daltons. Each cytokine is secreted by particular cell types in response to different stimuli, and has been implicated in a wide variety of important regulatory biologic functions such as: inflammation, tissue repair, cell activation, cell growth, fibrosis, and morphogenesis.
