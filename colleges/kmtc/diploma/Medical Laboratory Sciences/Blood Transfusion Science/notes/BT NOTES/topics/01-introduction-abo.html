üß¨ Blood Composition and Functions

Subject of science that concerns blood as far as antigens and antibodies are concerned.

Blood: - Is a living tissue composed of cellular elements suspended in a watery fluid called plasma.

Plasma: -its principal component is water ( $90 \%$ ). It also contains ( $10 \%$ ) carbohydrates, plasma proteins, inorganic constituents such as sodium, potassium, calcium chloride, carbonate, and bicarbonate; sugar; hormones; enzymes; fats; amino acids; and such waste products as urea and creatinine. All these substances occur in minute quantities.

Cellular elements:

  • Erythrocytes (R.B.C's).
  • Leucocytes (W.B.C's).
  • Thrombocytes (Platelets).

Functions:

  • Erythrocytes: - Transport oxygen from lungs to tissues. Carries oxygen combined with haemoglobin from the lungs to tissues and carbon dioxide from tissues to lungs.
  • Leucocytes: - Defence mechanism. Divided into two Granulocytes & Agranulocytes.
üí° Leucocyte Classification

Granulocytes contain granules in the cytoplasm. Agranulocytes don't have granules in the cytoplasm.

Granulocytes:

  • Neutrophils.
  • Basophils.
  • Eosinophils.

Agranulocytes

  • Lymphocyte, small/large.
  • Monocytes.

Specific Cell Functions

Pus is degenerated leucocytes that form acne or abscess.

üíß Plasma and Serum

PLASMA

Proteins in plasma include:

  • a) Albumin $4.5 \mathrm{~g} \%$
  • b) Globulin $2.5 \mathrm{~g} \%$
  • c) Fibrinogen $0.3 \mathrm{~g} \%$

Plasma proteins are albumin, the principal agent in maintaining the osmotic pressure of the blood and therefore in controlling its tendency to diffuse through the walls of blood vessels.
Other proteins, including fibrinogen and prothrombin, participate in clotting; (agglutinins), which cause the agglutination reaction between samples of blood of different types and the reaction known as anaphylaxis, a form of allergic shock; and globulins of many types, which include the antibodies that provide immunity against many diseases. Other significant plasma proteins function as carriers of such essential tissue nutrients as copper, iron, other metals, and a variety of hormones. Globulin: are antibodies (poly peptides)

  • Alpha globulin
  • Beta globulin
  • Gamma globulin.

Gamma globulins are mostly used in formation of most antibodies.
Plasma is considered to be anticomplementary. This is because the ions (e.g. $\mathrm{Mg}^{+}$ and $\mathrm{Ca}^{++}$) that are vital for activation of complement have been removed by anticoagulant.
$\mathrm{Mg}^{++}$ and $\mathrm{Ca}^{++}$ ions are responsible for activation of complement in serological test in blood transfusion.

SERUM:

Liquid potion of coagulated blood or clotted blood. Obtained through leaving blood in a bottle (bijou) with no anticoagulant.

Serum has two proteins:

  • Albumin
  • Globulin

There is no fibrinogen in serum because it has been used during clotting mechanism, by thrombokinase to form a fibrin.
Serum is used in serological tests in Transfusion Sciences because there is a complement which activates the ab - antigen reaction e.g. haemolysis.

FUNCTIONS OF BLOOD:

Nutrition and respiration of tissues located far from food and air supplies. Transportation of waste products from the tissues to the excretory organs e.g. kidneys, lungs (urea, uric acid, carbon-dioxide, xanthine, creatinine).
Chemical and thermo co-ordination of the body: - Normal body Temperature $-37^{\circ} \mathrm{c}$
PH - 7.3-7.4
Electrolytes maintain the balance of PH of blood.
Defence mechanism, against infections through actions of antibodies and Monocytes

üÖ∞Ô∏è The ABO Blood Group System

Reasons of Blood Transfusion:

  • Replace blood lost by haemorrhage, haemorrhage can be caused by haematemesis, trauma, and surgery.
  • To increase the amount of circulating Hb , especially with patients suffering from anaemia e.g. in Thalassaemia, Sickle Cell Disease, leukaemia e.t.c.

Discovery

In 1901, Karl Landsteiner discovered the ABO blood group system. He was working closely with Sturli Von Decastero who discovered AB blood group. Karl Landsteiner and Weiner discovered the Rhesus blood group - in 1940. Thus there are four blood groups A, B, AB, and O in the ABO blood group system.

Inheritance:

Three Allelomorphic genes determine it. Allele: - pair of chromosomes found on the same loci of gene.
The Allele A gene, B gene, O gene.
O gene is an amorph - cannot express itself when A, B, genes are present.
Genes are inherited from parents according to Mendelian law.
The antigens from the genes inherited

A antigen A gene
B antigen B gene.
An individual inherits either A, B, O gene from one parent or the other thus the offspring has a gene called genotype, individual for a particular trait. Thus if the chromosome inherited from the father carries gene A and that from the mother carries gene B , the offspring will be of AB genotype.
Different genotypes are $AA, AO, AB, BB, BO$, and $OO$.
It is difficult to know whether an individual is AA or AO genotype.

Phenotype:

Visible expression of genotype. There are four phenotypes in A, B, O blood groups.

Genes inherited can either be the same or they can be different.
Homozygous: Inherits similar genes, AA, BB, and OO .
Heterozygous: Inherits different genes, AO, BO, AB.

Antigens and antibodies cause reactions in blood. Genes are responsible for the production of the corresponding antigen thus A and B genes in the A, B, O, blood groups are the causes of the production of A and B antigens.
A and B antigens are the major antigens found in ABO blood group system. There are two antibodies found in ABO blood system ab A and B antibody. The antigens and antibodies are the ones that enable us to determine the blood group of an individual.

Discovered by Von-Dungein and Hisszfield- in 1911. There are various types of subgroups of A. There are two main agglutinogens $A_{1}$ & $A_{2}$. They divide A & AB into $A_{1}$, $A_{2}$, $A_{1}B$ and $A_{2}B$. The other subgroups of A are $A_{el}$, $A_{m}$ and $A_{x}$ The classification of these sub-groups is dependent on the amount of Aantigens on A cells.
Thus $A_{1}$ has the highest antigen antibody reaction (titre).

There are two methods of production

Anti $A_{1}$ occurs as an allo-antibody in the serum of $1-8\%$ of $A_{2}$ persons and $22-35\%$ of $A_{2}B$ persons. Anti $A_{1}$ can also sometimes be found in the sera of $A_{2}$ individuals or those of other weak subgroups of A . Anti $A_{1}$ can cause incompatibility in cross matches with $A_{1}$ or $A_{1}B$ red cells. Anti $A_{1}$ usually reacts better or only at temperatures well below 37 degrees and is considered clinically insignificant unless there is reactivity at $37^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$, only $A_{2}$ or O red cells should be used for transfusion.
In simple Adsorption studies, the Anti A of group B serum appears to contain separable anti A and anti $A_{1}$. Native group B serum agglutinates $A_{1}$ and $A_{2}$ red cell. If further tests are performed, however, the differences in A antigen expression between $A_{1}$ and $A_{2}$ red cells appear to be quantitative rather than qualitative.
A reliable Anti $A_{1}$ reagent from lectin of Dolichos biflorous is commercially available or may be prepared. The raw plant extract will react with both $A_{1}$ and $A_{2}$ red cells, but the appropriately diluted reagent preparation will not agglutinate $A_{2}$ cells and thus constitute an anti $A_{1}$.

Subgroups of B:

In group B there is no variation in Antigen strength comparable to that of A nor is group B divisible into two main subgroups as A is divided. However subgroups of B do occur and may very rarely be cause of anomalies in ABO grouping. Thus they have no clinical importance. Examples of subgroups of B are $B_{z}, B_{w}, B_{x}, B_{x}, B_{v}$.

Importance of ABO sub- groups

  • Can be used in cases of disputed paternity.
  • They can cause incompatibility thus transfusion reactions.
  • Can cause problems in grouping e.g. $A_{x}$ and $A_{0}$ can be grouped as O
  • Are used for identification of sub-groups. We normally use $A_{1}$.
  • Professional and academic purposes.
