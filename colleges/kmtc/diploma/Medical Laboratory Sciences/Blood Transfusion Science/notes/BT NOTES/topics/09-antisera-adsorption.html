01

üß™ Preparation of Antisera

Normally preparation of antiserum is done in specialized reference laboratories. There are four points to consider: i. Potency ii. Avidity iii. Specific iv. Free from Rouleaux

The antisera should be tested for the above four things it must contain a powerful antibody that reacts only with the red cells carrying the corresponding antigen.

CRITERIA FOR DETERMINING SERUMS SUITABILITY POTENCY

1. Titre
This is the strength of the antisera. It should have a titre of at least $1: 256$ 1.

Avidity
This is a measure of avidity, the speed at which antisera agglutinates red cells.
The avidity is determined by adding one drop of $10 \%$ saline suspension of washed red cells to an equal volume of antiserum to a microscope slide or a glass tile. Record the time it takes for the cells to agglutinate. Repeat this three times and then take an average. Should be less than 10 seconds

2. Specificity
The antisera should react c the corresponding antigen.

3. Rouleaux formation
The antisera should be free from fat since it gives antisera a turbid appearance suggesting presence of bacteria.

-If plasma is used to prepare antisera:
i. Recalcification
ii. Antibodiesorption of fibrinogen (Kaolin and Benfonile)

Steps involved in preparation of Antisera

  • Identifythesourceofantisera(sheep/horse/rabfidi)n. Preparation the source with...
  • Titrate to test the strength of antiserum by steps likely to be used routinely.
  • Select suitable antisera according to criteria laid down.
  • Confirm the specifically of antiserum by testing against a panel of std cells.
  • Removal of unwanted antibodies can be done in a. Adsorption with appropriate red cells.
  • Sterilize the antisera by Seitz filters
  • preservative is added / Sodium azide
  • Suitable dyes are added. > Anti A- blue - Trypan blue > Anti B yellow -Acriflavine orange > AntiD No colour is added
  • Dispense in suitable quantities in sterile containers.
  • Store at $4^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ if it is to be used immediately or at $-20^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ if not to be used immediately.
02

üß≤ ADSORPTION

This is the removal of an antibody from a serum by adsorption on to red cells that have the corresponding antigen determinants, hence an adsorbed serum is serum from which antibodies have been sorely removed.

Purpose of adsorption in blood transfusion:

  • Adsorption is used un separating antibodies in sera containing several antibodies
  • Used in the identification of antibodies
  • Used in confirmation of antibody specificity
  • Preparation of typing antisera
  • Used as a preliminary step in preparation of eluates. Elution / removal of antibodies adsorbed in the red cells
  • Confirmation of the presence of antigens on the red cells.

Adsorption procedures

Preparation of adsorbed anti $\mathbf{A}_{\mathbf{1}}$ serum
Reagents
$A_{2}$ cells, $A_{2} B$ cells $B$ serum and $O$ serum $A_{2}$ serum $A_{2} B$ serum.
Anti $A_{1}$ can be isolated from the blood group $B, O, A_{2}$ and $A_{2} B$.

Procedure
Select and wash $A_{2}$ or $A_{2} B$ cells in large volumes of saline for three times depending on what blood group you are using. Blood group B serum use A2 cells. Blood group O serum use $A_{2} B$ cells.
Select either blood group B serum group O serum from healthy donors / the blood is clotted to get the serum.
NB: suitable titre for A is $1: 512$.
Titration of anti A is performed, first to determine the titre.
- in a large test tube / universal bottle combine 2 mls of group B serum and 1 ml packed $A_{2}$ cells / for O serum 2 mls of O serum and 1 mls of packed $A_{2} B$ cells
- Incubate at $4^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ for 2 hours or overnight.
- Centrifuge the mixture in chilled centrifuge cups, remove the supernatant serum immediately and put it in a clean test tube labelled "ADSORBED ANTI A $_{1}$"
- Test for specificity of anti $A$, with $A_{1}$ cells $A_{2}$ cells $A_{2}$ or $A_{1} B$ cells $A_{2} B$ cells.

Principles:
Preparation of anti $A_{1}$ serum is based on the fact that $A_{2}$ cells and $A_{2} B$ cells both have antigenic sites for the anti A to attach itself. Thus living the anti $A_{1}$ in the supernatant which is not attached to the cells.

üåø LECTINS

Lectins are extracts from certain plants normally from the seeds of particular plants. These extracts are capable of causing agglutination of the red bld cells. The agglutination is not specific in most cases (can react c any antigens present). Not all the extracts, some show blood group specificity. It is important to note that the extracts are not antibodies but they have a similar effect as antibodies they are thus referred to as phytoagglutins. There are many types of phytoagglutins. But the two most commonly used are:
1. Dolichos biflorous (Indian cattle bean) house gram/grain. Its specificity shows a reaction similar to antibody $\mathrm{A}_{1}$-Aggregates $\mathrm{A}_{1}$ cells.
2. Ulex europaeus (common gorse) it has Anti H specificity can agglutinate O cells but not Bombay cells.

Preparation of Anti $\mathbf{A}_{\mathbf{1}}$ from Dolichos biflorous

  • Take the seeds and grid with pestle and mortal or madrine until they are in powder from -weigh about 7g of the powder D. biflorous / put in a testtube.
  • Add 10 mls of N. saline
  • Using a shaking machine mix the mixture
  • Centrifuge at 3000 rpm for 5 mins
  • Discard the sediment / by pipeting the supernatant
  • Refine the supernatant fluid by filtration using Whatman No 1 filter paper
  • Standardize the antiserum / perform titration
  • Titration titre 1:512
  • Check specificity using $\mathrm{A}_{1}$ cells
  • Check the avidity $\mathrm{A}_{1}$ - 10 seconds
  • Sterilize the antiserum / use filters ( Seitz)
