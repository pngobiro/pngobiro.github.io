üíâ Types of Immunization

Immunization is a process by which antibodies are produced in response to antigenic stimulus. It involves the immune mechanism. There are three types of immunization

Iso-immunization

It is the production of antibodies after the antigenic simulation of a member of the same species e.g. A person who is blood group A-negative is transfused with person with A-positive he's going to be stimulated and produces antibodies against D antigens. Antibodies involved in Iso-immunization are iso-agglutinins. They are also referred to as Allo-agglutinins.

Hetero-immunization

It is the production of antibodies after antigenic stimulation from different species like in Coombs reagent production. Hybrid-rabbits are injected with human serum (Immunoglobulins) and the rabbit produces anti human globulin (antibodies) otherwise called AHG or Coomb's reagent. Antibody involved in hetero immunization is hetero-agglutinin.

Auto Immunization

It is the production of antibodies against ones cells auto haemolytic reaction.
This is due to:
Malfunction of the thymus glands
Patients with auto haemolytic anaemia
They can cause problem during blood transfusion. The anti body which reacts with the individual's antigen is referred to as auto-antibody

üß™ Preparation of AHG (Coombs Reagent)

There are several animals used for preparation of A.H.G. The rabbit is the most commonly used. It can also be prepared from goats and sheep. Not all strains of rabbits are good for A.H.G preparation. Brown rabbit (Hybrid-type) are mostly used
This is achieved by use of purified human sera e.g. -Gamma globulins
-Beta globulin
Adjuvants are the compounds used to increase the production of A.H.G.
(saturated or $40\%$) - Freud's Adjuvant,
- Sodium alignate.

Human group O serum pooled from several donors is used. The rabbit is injected with purified gamma globulin or human Beta globulin in either one of the adjuvants. The rabbit is normally injected in the peritoneal area (cavity area). Afterward Calcium chloride ( $\mathrm{Cacl}_{2}$ ) is injected and the area is massaged. Massaging is done to prevent swelling. It also helps adjuvant mixed to flow properly.
The A.H.G can be prepared from separate colonies. Beta globulin or globulin can be used. One colony is injected with human Gamma globulin. The other colony is injected with human Beta globulin. It produces antibodies specific for B-globulin e.g. compliment-component (reacts with compliment component). The two are blended to come up with poly-specific reagent
-When a reagent is prepared separately it is known as mono-specific. If combined together they are referred to as poly-specific or broad-spectrum A.H.G. Broad spectrum A.H.G detects both immunoglobulins and complimentcomponents.

Principle behind A.H.G.

When the anti-sera is added to the sensitized cells (cells attached with antibodies) the rabbits anti-IgG molecules attach their antigen binding pieces to the F.C.P's (fragment crystallization pieces) of the coated cells resulting to agglutination.
In a similar manner the rabbit anti-compliment attaches to the complimentcomponent and since the compliment is attached to the red cell haemolysis occurs.
Anti human globulin doesn't react with antigen it just forms bridges for agglutination to the sensitized red blood cells.

üîç Coombs Tests

Any method using Coomb's (AHG) reagent is Coomb's test. They are of two major types.

  • Direct Coomb's test
  • Indirect Coomb's test

DIRECT COOMB'S TEST

It is a test used for detection of sensitized cells in vivo. Under normal circumstances there are no sensitized cells found in the body.

Sensitized red blood cells are fond in;

  • Children born with H.D.N.B (Haemolytic disease of the new born).
  • Patients with Haemolytic transfusion reactions (HTRs) Also
  • Auto immune haemolytic anaemia AIHA
  • Acquired haemolytic anaemia:

Procedure

  • Label test tube Direct Coomb's
  • Put two drops of test cells in a clean grouping tube $8 \mathrm{~mm} \times 75 \mathrm{~mm}$
  • Wash the test cells with large amounts of normal saline for about three times.
  • Discard the last wash of N. saline completely Add two drops of A.H.G.
  • Mix and centrifuge at 1000 rpm for 1 min ( 3000 for 20 seconds)
  • Examine for agglutination/haemolysis Record results:

If agglutination record Direct Coomb's test positive
If no agglutination record Direct Coomb's test -ve
Set positive and -ve control
In positive control we use coated (sensitized red cells). Prepared by diluting AntiD 1:20 with Normal-saline and making $5\%$ cell suspension using this solution.

INDIRECT COOMB'S TEST:

The test is used to detect the presence of sensitized cells in vitro. Therefore it is used to detect antibodies in serum using known antigens e.g. O-positive pooled cells or panel cells or reagent cells.

Significance

  • Cross -match to detect incompatibility
  • Detection and identification of irregular antibodies (immune antibodies)

Indirect Coomb's test has three stages

  • Sensitization
  • Washing cells to free from all globulin
  • Mixing washed cells with A.H.G

Procedure

  • Label test tube as I.C. (Indirect Coomb's)
  • Make a 4% cell suspension of O positive pooled cells
  • Add two drops of cell suspension into a test tube
  • Add 2 drop of patient's serum
  • Mix and incubate at $37^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ for $30-60$ minutes (sensitization)
  • Centrifuge at 1000 rpm for one minute
  • Examine for agglutination. Both macro and microscopically
  • Record results

If agglutination is present record Indirect Coomb's test= positive
If no agglutination is present record Indirect Coomb's Test =negative.

COMMENT
Positive test is due to presence of immune antibodies
Negative test = no immune antibodies in the test serum

üîé Antibody Screening & Identification

ANTI BODY SCREENING TEST:

Test done to detect irregular antibodies in vitro. (Immune antibodies) the test is done on women suspected to be immunized and would like to get pregnant, or patients who have undergone multiple transfusions and is suspected to be immunized and requires blood transfusion.
Conditions indicating presence of irregular antibodies where antibody screening is requested are;

  • Jaundice in new born
  • Incompatible cross match
  • blood transfusion reaction

Antibody screening test has six phases

  • $4^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$
  • RT
  • A $37^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$
  • C $37^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$
  • S $37^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$
  • Enzyme. (Apply Enzyme technique)

$4^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ PHASE; Detects cold antibodies which react at $4^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ Anti-I, Anti-I, antiM, N etc.
RT PHASE also detects cold antibodies Anti I and Anti $\mathrm{P}_{1}$ react strongly at low temp. $4^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$. Anti A anti B, (complete Anti -AB) react well at RT. Most of the immune antibodies are detected at $37^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$.

Technique used in screening test

Requirements / Reagents
- Test Serum
- $\mathrm{O}^{+}$ pooled cells, or panel cells or Selectogen I & II

Procedure

  • Label 6 test tubes- $4^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$, RT, S $37^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$, A $37^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$, C $37^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$, and Enzyme $37^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$
  • Add two drops of patients / donors serum to all test tubes
  • Add two drops of panel cells / O+ pooled cells/reagent cells to other except Enzyme tube.
  • Add two drops of enzyme treated cells to test-tube labelled enzyme
  • To A $37^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ add two drops of $20\%$ albumin & mix
  • Incubate the tubes at respective temperatures $4^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$, $37^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$, RT for $1 \mathrm{hr}$ . - this is to allow reaction to take place at optimum temp.
  • Wash the Coombs test tube in large volumes of normal saline X3, Add A.H.G (two drops)
  • Centrifuge the test tubes and look for agglutination.

PERFORM ANTIBODY IDENTIFICATION TEST:

This is to identify the particular antibody present in the serum / encountered during screening test. The identification of an antibody is based on the characteristic of the particular antibody. It is also based on the media at which it reacted and the temp e.g. C $37^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$. (Coombs @ $37^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$)
In identification panel cells are used e.g. panel cells in Rh system: They include

üî¢ Titration of Antibody

It's a test that determines the titre / strength relative amount of ab in serum against corresponding antigen. The titre of antibodies is acquired by making serial dilutions in a media that has been selected as the most suitable.

Method

  • Label ten test tubes -1 . $2,4,8,16,32,64,128,256,512$ Add at least three drops of test serum to first two test-tubes.
  • Serial dilution
  • Add normal saline from 2 onwards.
  • Mix contents in test tube two with aid of a Pasteur pipette -without causing any air bubbles
  • Transfer at least three drops of serum in test-tube 2 to test-tube labelled 4 and rinse the pipette thoroughly (about five (5) times
  • Mix the contents in 4 with a pipette and transfer three drops to test-tube labelled 8, continue with rinsing thoroughly, and transferring up to 512 Discard last three drops.
  • Add known cells of known A cells to the row of testing A cells / three drops or two drops in each.
  • Add B cells to the other row.
  • Incubate at R.T for $60 \mathrm{~min}$ .
  • Centrifuge and observe the results.

üì§ Elution

Elution frees antibody from sensitised red cells and recovers antibody in a usable form. OR: This is the removal of antibodies that has been antibodiesorbed onto the red cells, either in vivo or in vitro. The product of elution is known as eluates.

HEAT ELUTION METHOD: (Landsteiner and Muller)

Principle: Heat_elution uses an increase in temperature to dissociate antibodies from red cells. Mostly used for ABO HDNB.

Make sure the supernatant is removed completely.

  • To one volume of packed cells, use one volume of the suspending media (serum or 6% Bovine albumin).
  • Mix well and incubate @ $56^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ in a water bath for about $10-20$ mins agitating the mixture frequently during incubation.
  • Immediately transfer the tube to a pre-warm centrifuge bucket at $56^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ at a high speed. ( 3000 r.p.m.)
  • Remove the Hb tinted supernatant and place in another tube. This is the eluate

RUBINS ETHER METHOD:

  • Wash the cells 4-6 times in Normal saline.
  • Add an equal volume of N. saline and mix.
  • Add di-ethyl-ether reagent equal to the total volume of cells and N. saline.
  • Stopper the test tube and mix by inversion for $1 \mathrm{~min}$ .
  • The mixture is then incubated @ $37^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ for $30 \mathrm{mins}$ .
  • Centrifuge @ 3000 rpm (speed) for $5 \mathrm{mins}$ . - The tube will have three layers - The first layer has red cells stroma.
  • Second, layer Di-ethyl ether.
  • The bottom layer constitutes eluate.

Aspirate and discard the top two layers. OR by use of a long Pasteur pipette access the eluate and aspirate.
Transfer eluate to another tube and incubate @ $37^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ for $30 \mathrm{mins}$ to drive off residuals Di-ethylether. Or bubble air through at $37^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ for $15 \mathrm{mins}$.
