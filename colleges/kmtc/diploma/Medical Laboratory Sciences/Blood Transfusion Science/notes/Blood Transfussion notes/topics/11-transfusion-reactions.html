Transfusion Reactions

ü©∫ Investigation & Management
01

üìã Definition & Classification

Transfusion reaction is a term used to describe any unfavourable response by the patient following transfusion of blood or blood products.
They are classified into two

  • iIntravascular (in blood vessels)
  • iiExtravascular (liver , spleen, organs )
02

ü©∏ Intravascular Reactions

Intra vascular is more severe causing massive release of Hb it is usually caused by anti A and anti B, which are readily haemolytic in vitro. This severe transfusion reaction will follow transfusion with the wrong blood group e.g. a receiver of blood group A being transfused with blood group B the reaction can be fatal

Symptoms:

  • Fever
  • Chills
  • Burning sensations
  • Severe headaches
  • Severe lumbar pain
  • Constrictive chest pain
  • Low blood pressure
  • Increased pulse rate
  • Sense of fainting (increase)

Other symptoms - they follow initial symptoms

  • Jaundice
  • Haemoglobinuria

Factors influencing severity of the intravascular reactions:

  • Amount of blood transfused.
  • Rate of transfusion.
  • Antibodies present in the patient.
  • Baby's develop antibodies in about 6 months.
  • Antibodies of other blood grouped e.g. anti $\mathrm{Le}^{\mathrm{a}}$ anti $\mathrm{Tj}^{\mathrm{a}}$
03

ü©∫ Extravascular Reactions

It is less severe and occurs when incompatible blood has been transfused. The patient plasma does not have an antibody capable of activating compliments.

Symptoms involved are:

Mild fever
Chills it may occur after a whole pint has been given. Causes DHTR's (Delayed Haemolytic Transfusion Reactions). The mechanism of a cell removal involves neutrophils (engulfing macrophages engulfing the cells Antibody coated cells are phagocytosed and removed by the tissue bound macrophages. In most cases, there might not be haemolysis. Antibodies involved are such as Rhesus, Kell, Kidd and Duffy blood group systems. The reaction occur in spleen, liver and lymph nodes.

DIFFERENCES BETWEEN INTRA AND EXTRA VASCULAR REACTION

Intravascular. Extravascular.
Occurs in the veins. Occurs in the R.E.S. organs
Very severe Not very severe
Immediate red cell destruction releasing a lot of Hb to the plasma Slow rate of red cell destruction, no immediate increase of blood products
Immediate increase in Hb by-products e.g. bilirubin Bilirubin may not be present in DHTR's
Haemolysis is always present There might be no haemolysis

üå°Ô∏è Specific Reaction Types

3. FEBRILE REACTIONS:

Due to bacteria products / toxins.
It is brought about by pyogens -bacteria.
The pt feels unwell and has fever and chills temperature rises rapidly in the patient.
They are caused by:
-Anti leukocyte and antiplatelet antibodies
-Use of unclean tubes.

4. ALLERGIC REACTIONS:

Most common and they are the least dangerous reactions / due to tissue bound antibodies.
Signs: Urticaria rashes
Rx -administering cortisones

It is good to take the history of the patient before transfusion.

5. OTHER CAUSES OF AB/AG REACTION:

‚ö†Ô∏è Warning

N.B. Don't administer any drugs when transfusing.

Ensure you do not cause circulatory overload / should be avoided because it can cause cardiac arrest.
Expired blood or haemolysed blood (pt develops jaundice within 12--24 hours) plasma levels of $\mathrm{K}^{+}$ and $\mathrm{Na}^{+}$ ions in ACD -Acid citrate dextrose is distorted after 21days. It can keep for 21 days.
Adenocide -keeps for 35 days.
-gives energy to red blood cells.
Frozen blood should NEVER be given.
Wrong anticoagulant should be avoided.

6. ANAPHYLACTIC REACTION: (very rare)

Occurs to pts with 1gA deficiency. Thus the pt forms compliment binding e.g. IgG. The reaction is rare but when it occurs it has very severe symptoms e.g.
- Laryngeal oedema
- Chills
- Fever sweating

Avoid the problem by transfusing plasma free blood IgA deficient blood and also washed cells.

üîé Investigations

INVESTIGATIONS AFTER TRANSFUSION REACTION:

Specimen: Blood / pre sample -cells and serum
Post sample-clotted and un-clotted
Remains of donors pint-that was transfused
Urine sample (for spectroscopy & Iron stain)

PROCEDURE:

  • Forward and reverse grouping to rule out mis-grouping
  • Repeat crossmatch using patient's samples and donors samples-Rules out any antibody which would react.
  • Perform direct Coombs test to the post sample
    -detects antibodies coating the cells in vivo (sensitized cells)
  • AB screening test / detects presence of antigens performed on all samples of blood.
  • Antibody titration -to know the level of reacting antibodies.
  • On urine perform spectroscopy and stain for iron PPB on the deposit.
  • Hb-Will be low
  • Peripheral blood film
    Schistocytes are observed -fragments of blood cells
  • Bilirubin level -serum of post sample

Urine after centrifugation
-check for iron (stain)
-presence of bile pigments
On remains of pint:
-check for haemolysis.
-culture for bacteria.
After a month and if the patient develops jaundice, perform hepatitis B surface antigen test.
