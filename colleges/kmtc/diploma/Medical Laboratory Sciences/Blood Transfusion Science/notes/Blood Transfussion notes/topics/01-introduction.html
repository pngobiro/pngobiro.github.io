01

üìå Definition

Blood transfusion is the process of receiving blood products into ones circulation intravenously. Transfusions are used in a variety of conditions to replace lost components of blood. Early transfusion used whole blood but modern medical practice commonly uses only components of the blood such as red blood cells, white blood cells, platelets, plasma, clotting factors.

Globally 85million units of red blood cells are transfused in a given year.

Blood: - Is a living tissue composed of cellular elements suspended in a watery fluid called plasma.

Plasma: -its principal component is water ( $90 \%$ ). It also contains ( $10 \%$ ) carbohydrates, plasma proteins, inorganic constituents such as sodium, potassium, calcium chloride, carbonate, and bicarbonate; sugar; hormones; enzymes; fats; amino acids; and such waste products as urea and creatinine. All these substances occur in minute quantities.

‚öôÔ∏è Functions

Erythrocytes: - Transport oxygen from lungs to tissues. Carries oxygen combined with haemoglobin from the lungs to tissues and carbon dioxide from tissues to lungs.

Leucocytes: - Defence mechanism.
- Divided into two Granulocytes & Agranulocytes.

  • Granulocytes contain granules in the cytoplasm.
  • Agranulocytes don't have granules in the cytoplasm.

Granulocytes:

  • Neutrophils.
  • Basophils.
  • Eosinophils.

Agranulocytes

  • Lymphocyte, small/large.
  • Monocytes.
04

üõ°Ô∏è Specific Cell Functions

Neutrophils: Ingest foreign bacteria by phagocytosis.

Basophils: Produces Heparin an anticoagulant that helps in preventing the clotting of blood in blood in circulation. Also involved in hypersensitivity. Basophils bind IgE on their surface and are involved in anaphylactic, hypersensitivity, and inflammatory reactions. Basophils interact with eosinophils and macrophages in allergic reactions. When specific antigen react with IgE bound to basophils, degranulation of the cells occurs with the release of inflammatory substances including heparin, histamine, and platelet activating factor.

Eosinophils: Antigen antibody infection parasitic infection and allergies (Hypersensitivity) by producing Anti- histamine.
Eosinophils enter the blood from the bone marrow. They circulate for about 8 hours after which they enter the tissues. They are found mainly in the skin gastrointestinal tract and lungs where they are involved in hypersensitivity reactions e.g. asthma, hay fever, eczema. Eosinophils contain substances that are able to inactivate histamine and factors released during anaphylaxis.

Lymphocytes:

  • B-lymphocytes produce of antibodies.
  • T-helper T lymphocytes regulate the immune system by directing immune responses, either antibody mediated or cellular

Monocytes: Scavengers. Ingest bacteria in the second line of defence.

üí° Note

Pus is degenerated leucocytes that form acne or abscess.

Platelets: Plays a major role in coagulation mechanism.

üß™ Types of Blood Samples

  • Whole blood: Blood that has both cells and plasma intact or blood that is not clotted.
  • Plasma: Is the fluid potion from anti-coagulated blood. Plasma can be obtained by centrifuging whole blood, or by living the whole blood to settle.
  • Serum: The fluid part of clotted blood

PLASMA

Proteins in plasma include:

  1. Albumin 4.5g %
  2. Globulin 2.5g %
  3. Fibrinogen 0.3 g %

Plasma proteins are albumin, the principal agent in maintaining the osmotic pressure of the blood and therefore in controlling its tendency to diffuse through the walls of blood vessels.

Other proteins, including fibrinogen and prothrombin, participate in clotting; (agglutinins), which cause the agglutination reaction between samples of blood of different types and the reaction known as anaphylaxis, a form of allergic shock; and globulins of many types, which include the antibodies that provide immunity against many diseases. Other significant plasma proteins function as carriers of such essential tissue nutrients as copper, iron, other metals, and a variety of hormones.

Globulin: are antibodies (poly peptides)
- Alpha globulin
- Beta globulin
- Gamma globulin.

Gamma globulins are mostly used in formation of most antibodies.
Plasma is considered to be anti-complementary. This is because the ions (e.g. $\mathrm{Mg}^{++}$ and $\mathrm{Ca}^{++}$) that are vital for activation of complement have been removed by anticoagulant.
$\mathrm{Mg}^{++}$ and $\mathrm{Ca}^{++}$ ions are responsible for activation of complement in serological test in blood transfusion.
Fibrinogen: Used up in clotting of blood.

SERUM:

Liquid potion of coagulated blood or clotted blood. Obtained through leaving blood in a bottle (bijou) with no anti-coagulant.
Serum has two proteins:
- Albumin
- Globulin

There is no fibrinogen in serum because it has been used during clotting mechanism, by thrombokinase to form a fibrin.
Serum is used in serological tests in Transfusion Sciences because there is a complement which activates the ab-antigen reaction e.g. haemolysis.

üìã Functions of Blood

  • Nutrition and respiration of tissues located far from food and air supplies.
  • Transportation of waste products from the tissues to the excretory organs e.g. kidneys, lungs (urea, uric acid, carbon-dioxide, xanthine, creatinine).
  • Chemical and thermo co-ordination of the body: -
    Normal body Temperature - $37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$
    PH -7.3-7.4
    Electrolytes maintain the balance of PH of blood.
  • Defence mechanism, against infections through actions of antibodies and Monocytes
