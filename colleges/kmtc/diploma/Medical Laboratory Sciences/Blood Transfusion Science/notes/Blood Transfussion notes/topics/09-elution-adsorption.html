üî¢ TITRATION OF ANTIBODY

It's a test that determines the titre / strength relative amount of ab in serum against corresponding antigen. The titre of antibodies is acquired by making serial dilutions in a media that has been selected as the most suitable.

Factors that affects the antibody titration

Media e.g. when saline is used as a diluent, red cells should be suspended in saline /if high protein diluent is used, 20% albumin is also used to suspend. The characteristic of the ab determines the media to be used.

Dilutions used /not excess

When performing dilutions, separate graduated pipette should be used to avoid carry over of antibodies also rinse the pipette after each transfer. If dealing with high titred serum, as little as $0.0005 \mathrm{~ml}$ carried over would cause a change in entry.

Incubation temperature & time

Governed by the characteristic of the antibody.

Centrifugation

Might cause haemolysis (over centrifugation) or lack of agglutination. (Under centrifugation)

Red cells

Test red cells should be freshly prepared, preferably from the donors, homozygous for the antigenic determinant concerned /or from the most strongly reacting type of cells available. Avidity of cells should be known
After performing the test, results should be Read macroscopically.
Also Readings are taken from test tube labelled 512 to test tube labelled 1

Techniques

Purpose of titrating:

  • Test the potency of serum in ABO typing / high titred serum is the most potent -strong
  • To screen for blood give O-negative donor distinguish between dangerous universal donor and some universal donor / low titre is a save universal donor.
  • To check the strength of an offending antibody (anti-D) in HDNB,
  • In HTRs also to know the titre of the offending antibody.

Method

  1. Label ten test tubes $-1.2,4,8,16,32,64,128,256,512$
  2. Add at least three drops of test serum to first two test-tubes.
  3. Serial dilution
  4. Add normal saline from 2 onwards.
  5. Mix contents in test tube two with aid of a Pasteur pipette -without causing any air bubbles Transfer at least three drops of serum in test-tube 2 to test-tube labelled 4 and rinse the pipette thoroughly (about five (5) times
  6. Mix the contents in 4 with a pipette and transfer three drops to test-tube labelled 8, continue with rinsing thoroughly, and transferring up to 512
  7. Discard last three drops.
  8. Add known cells of known A cells to the row of testing A cells / three drops or two drops in each.
  9. Add B cells to the other row.
  10. Incubate at R.T for 60 min .
  11. Centrifuge and observe the results.

TITRATION OF ANTI-D (RH SYSTEM). Purpose.

Determine good antiserum to use in Rh typing.
Ante-natal clinic to predict occurrence of H.D.N.B by monitoring the immunization of an Rhnegative mother who has developed immune anti-D.
PROCEDURE
Similar to that of anti A and anti B except that 20% albumin is used and test tubes are incubated $37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ for 30-60 minutes.

Sources of error in anti D titration.

  • Poor technique for serial dilution / avoid air bubble in Pasteur pipettes. Rinsing of Pasteur pipettes between dilutions.
  • Using very old reagents-expired.
  • Failure to incubate long enough and at the required temperature.

üå°Ô∏è ELUTION

Elution frees antibody from sensitised red cells and recovers antibody in a usable form. OR: This is the removal of antibodies that has been antibodiesorbed onto the red cells, either in vivo or in vitro. The product of elution is known as eluates. In elution, we use AB serum as a diluent or 6% bovine albumen. If not available saline can be used but potency of antibodies is deteriorated. In cases of HDNB or HTR's, specific antibody (or antibodies) is usually detected in the eluate, which may or may not be detectable in the serum.

Application from elution in blood transfusion

  1. Elution techniques are used to demonstrate an identify the antibody on the red cells of umbilical cord blood, or infants blood, in case of H.D.N.B when mothers serum is not available. The eluate can be used from the baby's blood cells to perform compatibility test eluates is antibody delivered from elution.
  2. Used to demonstrate and identify antibodiesorbed antibodies on the red cells of pts with acquired haemolytic anaemia.
  3. Used to demonstrate and identify antibodies coating transfused red cells in suspected haemolytic transfusion reactions (HTR's).
  4. To separate and identify an antibody in a mixture of antibodies
  5. Confirmation of antibody specificity

Techniques:

There are three methods commonly used in elution

  1. Rubin's ether method.
  2. Heat elution Landsteiner and Muller method
  3. Weiner freeze and thaw method (Lui)
  4. Methylene chloride elution
  5. Glycine-HCL EDTA elution
  6. Cold acid elution.

HEAT ELUTION METHOD:

Landsteiner and Muller:

Principle: Heat_elution uses an increase in temperature to dissociate antibodies from red cells. Mostly used for ABO HDNB.

  • Wash the cells (clot) / sensitised six times.
  • Make sure the supernatant is removed completely.
  • To one volume of packed cells, use one volume of the suspending media (serum or 6% Bovine albumin).
  • Mix well and incubate @ $56^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ in a water bath for about 10 - 20 mins agitating the mixture frequently during incubation.
  • Immediately transfer the tube to a pre-warm centrifuge bucket at $56^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ at a high speed. (3000 r.p.m.)
  • Remove the Hb tinted supernatant and place in another tube. This is the eluate

RUBINS ETHER METHOD:

  • Wash the cells 4-6 times in Normal saline.
  • Add an equal volume of N. saline and mix.
  • Add di-ethyl-ether reagent equal to the total volume of cells and N. saline.
  • Stopper the test tube and mix by inversion for 1 min .
  • The mixture is then incubated @ $37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ for 30 mins .
  • Centrifuge @ 3000 rpm (speed) for 5 mins .
  • The tube will have three layers
    - The first layer has red cells stroma.
    - Second, layer Di-ethyl ether.
    - The bottom layer constitutes eluate.

Aspirate and discard the top two layers. OR by use of a long Pasteur pipette access the eluate and aspirate.
Transfer eluate to another tube and incubate @ $37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ for 30 mins to drive off residuals Diethylether. Or bubble air through at $37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ for 15 mins .

Principle

Di- ethyl ether lyses cells.
Disadvantages eluate is heavily stained with Hb that one might think lysing has not occurred.

LUI FREEZE-THAW ELUTION:

PRINCIPLE: As RBC's freeze, extracellular ice crystals form that attract water from their surroundings.
This increases the osmolarity of the remaining extracellular fluid, which then attracts water from the red cells. The red cells shrink, resulting in lysis. As the membranes are disrupted, antibody is dissociated. This method is used for investigation of ABO HDNB.

Procedure.

  1. DAT positive red cells washed six times with Normal Saline.
  2. Mix 0.5 mls of red cells to be tested with 3 drops of saline in a test tube.
  3. Cap the test tube, and then rotate the tube to coat the tube wall with cells.
  4. Place the tube in a freezer at ${ }^{-} 20$ to ${ }^{-} 70^{0}\mathrm{C}$ for 10 minutes.
  5. Remove the tube in a freezer and thaw quickly with warm tap water.
  6. Centrifuge for 2 minutes at $900 \times 1000 \times \mathrm{g}$
  7. Transfer supernatant (eluate) to a clean test tube and test in parallel with the supernatant saline from last wash.

üß≤ ADSORPTION

This is the removal of an antibody from a serum by adsorption on to red cells that have the corresponding antigen determinants, hence an adsorbed serum is serum from which antibodies have been sorely removed.

Purpose of adsorption in blood transfusion:

  1. Adsorption is used un separating antibodies in sera containing several antibodies
  2. Used in the identification of antibodies
  3. Used in confirmation of antibody specificity
  4. Preparation of typing antisera
  5. Used as a preliminary step in preparation of eluates. Elution / removal of antibodies adsorbed in the red cells
  6. Confirmation of the presence of antigens on the red cells.

üß¨ Advanced Adsorption Techniques

In a patient who has not been recently transfused, autologous adsorption is the best way to detect alloantibodies in the presence of warm-reactive autoantibodies. The adsorbed serum can be used in the routine antibody detection procedure.
Auto-adsorption generally requires some initial preparation of patient's red cells. At $37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$, invivo adsorption will have occurred and all antigen sites on the patient's own red cells may be blocked. It may be necessary, therefore, to remove autoantibody from the red cells to make sites available for adsorption.
A gentle heat elution at $56^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ for 3 to 5 minutes can dissociate some of the bound IgG. This can be followed by treatment of the autologous red cells with proteolytic enzymes to increase their capacity to adsorb autoantibody. Treatment of the RBC's with ZZAP, a mixture of papain or ficin and DTT accomplishes both of these actions in one step; the sulfhydryl component makes the IgG molecules more susceptible to the protease and dissociates the antibody molecules from the cell.

ZZAP treatment removes IgM and complement from autologous cells and uncovers antigen sites that can be used to bind free autoantibody in serum.

Multiple sequential auto-adsorptions with new aliquots of RBC's may be necessary if the serum contains high levels of autoantibody. Once autoantibody has been removed, the adsorbed serum is examined for alloantibody activity
Autologous adsorption is not recommended for patients who have been recently transfused, because they may have an admixture of transfused red cells that might adsorb alloantibody. RBC's normally live about 110 to 120 days. In patients with AIHA, autologous and transfused RBCs can be expected to have shortened survival. However, determining how long transfused RBCs remain in circulation in patients who need repeated transfusion is not feasible. It has been demonstrated that very small amounts ($<10\%$) of antigen-positive RBCs are capable of removing alloantibody reactivity in in-vitro studies; therefore. It is recommended to wait for three (3) months after transfusion before autologous adsorption are performed.
The patient's own red cells, a portion of which will be younger and less dense than transfused red cells can sometimes be separated by simple or gradient centrifugation for use in autoadsorption experiments. However, these techniques are highly variable and may require expensive equipment and/or considerable expertise in interpretation.
If patient is to be transfused, it can be advantageous to collect and save additional aliquots of pretransfusion cells, to be used for later adsorptions.

ALLOGENEIC ADSORPTION

The use of allogeneic red cells for adsorption may be helpful when the patient has been recently transfused or when insufficient autologous red cells are available. The goal is to remove autoantibody and leave the alloantibody in the adsorbed serum. The adsorbing cells must not have the antigens against which the alloantibodies react. Because alloantibody specificity is unknown, red cells of different phenotypes will usually be used to adsorb several aliquots of the patient's serum.
Given the number of potential alloantibodies, the task of selecting cells may appear formidable. However, the selected cells need only demonstrate those few alloantibodies of clinical significance likely to be present. These include the common Rh antigens ( D, C, E, c, and e ), K, $\mathrm{Fy}^{\mathrm{a}}$, $\mathrm{Jk}^{\mathrm{a}}$, and $\mathrm{Jk}^{\mathrm{b}}$ and S and s . Cell selection is made easier by the fact that some antigens can be destroyed by appropriate treatment (e.g. with enzymes) prior to use in adsorption procedures. Antibodies to high-incidence antigens cannot be excluded by allogeneic adsorption because the adsorbing cells will almost invariably express the antigen and adsorb the alloantibody along with autoantibody.
When the patient's Rh phenotype is not known, group O red cell samples of three different Rh phenotypes ( $\mathrm{R}_{1}\mathrm{R}_{1}, \mathrm{R}_{2}\mathrm{R}_{2}$, and rr ) should be selected. One should lack Jka and another Jkb. If treated with ZZAP, these cells would lack all antigens of the Kell system, M, N, S, Fya and $\mathrm{Fy}^{\mathrm{b}}$. If ZZAP is not available, cells treated only with proteolytic enzyme can be used, but at least one of the adsorbing cells must be K-negative because Kell system antigens will not be destroyed. Untreated cells may be used, but antibody may be more difficult to remove and the adsorbing cells must, at a minimum, include at least one negative for the S , s , Fya, $\mathrm{Fy}^{\mathrm{b}}$, and K antigens in addition to Rh and Kidd requirement above.
Each aliquot may need to be adsorbed two or three times. The fully adsorbed aliquots are tested against reagent red cells known either to carry lack or to carry common antigens of the Rh , MNS, Kidd, Kell and Duffy blood group systems. If an adsorbed aliquot is reactive, that aliquot (or an additional specimen similarly adsorbed) should be tested to identify the antibody. Adsorbing several aliquots with different red cell samples provides a battery of potentially informative specimens. For example, if the aliquot adsorbed with Jk(a-) red cells subsequently reacts only with Jk(a+) red cells, the presence of allo-anti-Jka can confidently be inferred.
The adsorbed serum can also be used for crossmatching; if necessary, adsorbed aliquots may be pooled for compatibility tests.
If the patients Rh and Kidd phenotypes are known or can be determined, adsorption can be performed with a single sample of allogeneic ZZAP-treated red cells of the same Rh and Kidd phenotypes as the patient.
Occasionally autoantibody will not be removed by three sequential adsorptions. Further adsorptions can be done, but multiple adsorptions have the potential to dilute the serum. If adsorbing cells do not appear to remove the antibody, the autoantibody may have an unusual specificity that does not react with the cells used for adsorption. For example, autoantibodies with Kell or LW specificity would not react with cells treated with ZZAP or proteolytic enzymes.

‚ñ∂ AUTOLOGOUS ADSORPTION PROCEDURE

PRINCIPLE

Warm-reactive autoantibodies in serum may mask the concomitant presence of clinically significant alloantibodies. Adsorption of the serum with autologous red cells can remove autoantibody from the serum, permitting detection of underlying alloantibodies. However, autologous red cells in the circulation are coated with autoantibody. Autologous adsorption of warm-reactive autoantibodies can be facilitated by dissociating autoantibody from the red cell membrane, thereby uncovering antigen sites that can bind free autoantibody to remove it from the serum. Some autoantibody can be dissociated by a gentle heat elution for 3 to 5 minutes at $56^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$. subsequent treatment of the cells with enzymes enhances the adsorption process by removing membrane structures that otherwise hinder the association between antigen and antibody. The most effective procedure involves the use of ZZAP reagent, a mixture of a proteolytic enzyme and sulfhydryl reagent. ZZAP removes immunoglobulins and complement from the red cells and enhances the adsorption process. Red cells from patients transfused within the last three months should not be used for auto-adsorption because transfused red cells present in circulation are likely to adsorb the alloantibodies that are being sought.

SPECIMEN

  1. 2 ml of serum to be adsorbed
  2. 2 ml of autologous red cells

REAGENTS

  1. 1% cysteine-activated papain or 1% ficin
  2. Phosphate buffered saline (PBS) at pH 7.3
  3. 0.2 M of DTT prepared by dissolving !gram of DTT in $32,4 \mathrm{~mL}$ of pH 7.3 PBS . Dispense into $3-\mathrm{mL}$ aliquots and store at $-18^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ or colder.

PROCEDURE

  1. Prepare ZZAP reagent by mixing 0.5 mL of 1% cysteine-activated papain with 2.5 mL of 0.2 M DTT and 2 mL of pH 7.3 PBS. Alternatively, use 1 mL of 1% ficin, 2.5 mL of 0.2 M DTT and 1.5 mL of pH 7.3 PBS. Check pH and adjust to between 6.0 and 6.5 if necessary.
  2. to each of the tubes containing 1 mL of packed red cells add 2 mL of ZZAP reagent. Mix and incubate at $37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ for 30 minutes with periodic mixing.
  3. wash the red cells in saline. Centrifuge the last wash for at least 5 minutes at 900 to 1000 xg . Use suction or a Pasteur pipette to remove as much of the supernatant as practically possible.
  4. add serum to an equal volume of ZZAP treated red cells, mix, and incubate at $37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ for approximately 30 to 45 minutes.
  5. centrifuge and carefully remove serum.
  6. steps 4 and 5 should be repeated once more using the once-adsorbed patient's serum and the second aliquot of ZZAP-treated cells.
  7. after the second adsorption, test the serum against a specimen of group O reagent cells. If reactivity persists repeat steps 4 and 5.

INTERPRETATION

Two adsorptions ordinarily remove sufficient autoantibody so that alloantibody reactivity, if present, is readily apparent. If twice auto-adsorbed serum reacts with defined specificity, as shown by testing against a small antibody identification panel, then the defined specificity of the antibody is probably an alloantibody (e.g. Ant-Kpb) that does not react with ZZAP-treated cells and thus will not be adsorbed by this procedure. To check this latter possibility, test the reactive autoadsorbed serum against reagent cells that have been pre-treated with ZZAP reagent.

NOTES

  1. ZZAP treatment destroys all Kell system antigens and all other antigens that are destroyed by proteases e.g. M, N, Fya, Fyb, and S. ZZAP reagent also denatures the antigens of the LW, Yt, Dombrock and Knops systems. If the autoantibody is suspected to have specificity in any of these latter blood groups, an alternative procedure is to perform auto-adsorption with untreated autologous red cells or autologous red cells treated only with 1% ficin or 1% cysteine-activated papain.
  2. there is no need to wash packed red cells before treatment with ZZAP
  3. cold autoantibodies reactive at room temperature can also be present in the serum of about 30% of patients with warm reactive autoantibodies. Removal of these cold antibodies can be facilitated by placing serum and cell mixture at $4^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ for about 15 minutes after incubation at $37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$.
  4. greater than four adsorptions increases the risk of diluting alloantibody reactivity.

OTHER ADSORPTION METHODS

  1. Differential warm adsorption using ZZAP-treated allogeneic red cells
  2. One cell sample ZZAP allogeneic adsorption
  3. Polyethylene glycol adsorption
