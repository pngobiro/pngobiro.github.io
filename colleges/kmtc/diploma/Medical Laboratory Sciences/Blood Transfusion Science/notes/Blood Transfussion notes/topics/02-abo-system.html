01

üìú Historical Context & Basics

In 1901, Karl Landsteiner discovered the ABO blood group system. He was working closely with Sturli Von Decastero who discovered AB blood group.
Karl Landsteiner and Weiner discovered the Rhesus blood group - in 1940.
Thus there are four blood groups A, B, AB, and O in the ABO blood group system.

02

üß¨ Inheritance

Three common alleles ( A, B and O ) are located on the ABO locus on chromosome 9.
Three Allelomorphic genes determine it.
Allele: - pair of chromosomes found on the same loci of gene.
The Allele A gene, B gene, O gene.
O gene is an amorph - cannot express itself when A, B, genes are present.
Genes are inherited from parents according to Mendelian law.
The antigens from the genes inherited

  • A antigen — A gene
  • B antigen — B gene.

An individual inherits either A, B, O gene from one parent or the other thus the offspring has a gene called genotype, individual for a particular trait.
Thus if the chromosome inherited from the father carries gene A and that from the mother carries gene B , the offspring will be of AB genotype.
Different genotypes are AA, AO, AB, BB, BO, and OO .
It is difficult to know whether an individual is AA or AO genotype.

Phenotype:

Visible expression of genotype. There are four phenotypes in A, B, O blood groups.

Genes inherited can either be the same or they can be different.

  • Homozygous: Inherits similar genes, AA, BB, and OO .
  • Heterozygous: Inherits different genes, AO, BO, AB.

Antigens and antibodies cause reactions in blood. Genes are responsible for the production of the corresponding antigen thus A and B genes in the A, B, O, blood groups are the causes of the production of A and B antigens.
A and B antigens are the major antigens found in ABO blood group system.
There are two antibodies found in ABO blood system ab A and B antibody.
The antigens and antibodies are the ones that enable us to determine the blood group of an individual.

üß™ Antigens & Antibodies

ANTIGEN (IMMUNOGENE).

It is a foreign substance which when introduced into the body of an individual who lacks it stimulates the production of antibodies and it can react to those specific antibodies produced.
E.g. If we introduce antigen A in a blood group B individual, it produces antibody A which can react with A antigen.
Antigens are made of poly-saccharide amino acid complex.

$$(\mathrm{C}_{6} \mathrm{H}_{10} \mathrm{O}_{5})-\mathrm{COOH}$$ $$\mathrm{NH}_{2}$$

The purpose of poly-saccharide is concerned with the specificity of the antigen.
The amino-acid is antigen.
Antigenicity is the strength/ability of the antigen to stimulate the production of the antibodies.
Specificity: the ability of antigen to react with a specific corresponding antibody e.g. Antigen A reacts with antibody A.
Antigen B reacts with antibody B .

ANTIBODY:

Proteinous in nature produced due to antigenic stimulant. They react specifically with corresponding antigen.

HAPTEN:

A substance that combines specifically with an antibody but does not stimulate the production of antibodies.
They are antigens with little antigenicity.
Thus it has specificity but does not have antigenicity.

A hapten is a small molecule that can elicit immune response only when attached to a large carrier such as a protein.

FACTORS AFFECTING ANTIGENICITY

  1. Chemical structure of the antigen
    For an antigen to be recognised by the body (host) it has to have a high molecular weight of at least 10,000 Daltons
  2. Route of entry
    Either through the mouth, intravenously, peritoneal (skin). The body is able to recognise the antibody depending on where it is introduced. If the route is normally intravenous the body cannot recognise if the route of entry is changed.
  3. Dosage
    The right quality and quantity. Low dosage does not impact immunological response.
  4. Susceptibility of the host
    The body of the host must recognise the material as foreign failure to which it will not stimulate the production of antibodies
  5. Frequency of exposure
    The more the exposure the body has for a particular disease or antigen, the more the immunity it develops. Secondary immunity in response to antigenic stimuli acts as a booster.

They divide A & AB into $\mathrm{A}_{1}, \mathrm{A}_{2}, \mathrm{A}_{1}\mathrm{B}$ and $\mathrm{A}_{2}\mathrm{B}$. The other subgroups of A are $\mathrm{A}_{\mathrm{el}}, \mathrm{A}_{\mathrm{m}}$ and $\mathrm{A}_{\mathrm{x}}$
The classification of these sub-groups is dependent on the amount of A -antigens on A cells. Thus $\mathrm{A}_{1}$ has the highest antigen antibody reaction (titre).

Anti $\mathrm{A}_{1}$ occurs as an allo-antibody in the serum of $1-8 \%$ of $\mathrm{A}_{2}$ persons and $22-35 \%$ of $\mathrm{A}_{2}\mathrm{B}$ persons. Anti $\mathrm{A}_{1}$ can also sometimes be found in the sera of $\mathrm{A}_{2}$ individuals or those of other weak subgroups of A . Anti $\mathrm{A}_{1}$ can cause incompatibility in cross matches with $\mathrm{A}_{1}$ or $\mathrm{A}_{1}\mathrm{B}$ red cells.

Anti $\mathrm{A}_{1}$ usually reacts better or only at temperatures well below 37 degrees and is considered clinically insignificant unless there is reactivity at $37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$, only $\mathrm{A}_{2}$ or O red cells should be used for transfusion.
In simple Adsorption studies, the Anti A of group B serum appears to contain separable anti A and anti $\mathrm{A}_{1}$. Native group B serum agglutinates $\mathrm{A}_{1}$ and $\mathrm{A}_{2}$ red cell. If further tests are performed, however, the differences in A antigen expression between $\mathrm{A}_{1}$ and $\mathrm{A}_{2}$ red cells appear to be quantitative rather than qualitative.
A reliable Anti $\mathrm{A}_{1}$ reagent from lectin of Dolichos biflorous is commercially available or may be prepared. The raw plant extract will react with both $\mathrm{A}_{1}$ and $\mathrm{A}_{2}$ red cells, but the appropriately diluted reagent preparation will not agglutinate $\mathrm{A}_{2}$ cells and thus constitute an anti $\mathrm{A}_{1}$.

Subgroups of B:

In group B there is no variation in Antigen strength comparable to that of A nor is group B divisible into two main subgroups as A is divided. However subgroups of B do occur and may very rarely be cause of anomalies in ABO grouping. Thus they have no clinical importance. Examples of subgroups of B are $\mathrm{B}_{\mathrm{z}}, \mathrm{B}_{\mathrm{w}}, \mathrm{B}_{\mathrm{x}}, \mathrm{B}_{\mathrm{x}}, \mathrm{B}_{\mathrm{v}}$.

Importance of ABO sub- groups

  • Can be used in cases of disputed paternity.
  • They can cause incompatibility thus transfusion reactions.
  • Can cause problems in grouping e.g. $\mathrm{A}_{\mathrm{x}}$ and $\mathrm{A}_{0}$ can be grouped as O
  • Are used for identification of sub-groups. We normally use $\mathrm{A}_{1}$.
  • Professional and academic purposes.
