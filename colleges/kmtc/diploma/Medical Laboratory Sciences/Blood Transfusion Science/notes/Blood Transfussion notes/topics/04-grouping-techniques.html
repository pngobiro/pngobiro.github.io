ü©∏ ABO Grouping Basics

ABO CELL GROUPING:

This is the detection of presence or absence of A antigen or B antigen on cells of RBC's. Thus we use known antibodies, Anti-A and Anti-B.
e.g.

Anti A Anti B Blood group
+ - A
- + B
- - O
+ + AB

ABO cell grouping is also known as forward grouping or forward typing / direct grouping direct typing

ABO serum grouping

This is detection of presence or absence of Anti A or Anti B using known antigens (cells) We use known A B and O cells e.g.

A cells B cells O cells Blood group
+ + - O
- - - AB
+ - - B
- + - A

-O cells are used as control - no reaction is expected.
-ABO serum grouping is also known as backward cell grouping or reverse cell typing.
In routine work both reverse and forward grouping methods are used. The reason being one confirms the other e.g.

üîÑ Combined Grouping (Forward & Reverse)
Forward Reverse
Anti A Anti B A cells B cells O cells Blood group
+ - - + - A
- + + - - B
+ + - - - AB
- - + + - O

üõ†Ô∏è Methods used in ABO Grouping

There are basically three methods

  • Tube method
  • Slide method
  • Tile method

Tube method is the most effective method and the best. It is the most commonly used.

TUBE METHOD

Label five test-tubes ( $8 \mathrm{~mm} \mathrm{x} 75 \mathrm{~mm}$ ) in a blood grouping rack Anti-A, Anti-B, A-cell, B-cells, Ocells

  1. Add one volume of $4 \%$ patient/donor cell suspension to tubes labelled anti-A and anti-B.
  2. Add one drop of $4 \%$ known cell suspension of known A, B and O cells in A, B and O (respective test tubes).
  3. Add one drop of the anti-serum anti-A & anti-B in respective test tubes Anti-A and Anti-B and mix
  4. Add one drop of donors or patient's serum tubes A , B and O cells
  5. Take the tubes and centrifuge at 1000 r.p.m. for a minute OR 3000 rpm for 20 seconds
  6. Examine the tubes for agglutination both macroscopically and microscopically.
  7. Chart your results (after examination).
Anti-A Anti-B A-cells B-cells O-cells Blood group
+ - - + - A
- + + - - B
- - + + - O
+ + - - - AB

-The method is mostly recommended because one can't miss agglutination;
-centrifugation enhances agglutination.

SLIDE METHOD

Take two slides and divide them into two. Label them anti A anti B, A cells and B cells.
Slide 1 slide 2

  1. Put one volume (drop) of anti-serum into respective positions (Anti-A and anti-B)
  2. Put 1 volume of cell suspension to the respective positions (A-cells and B-cells)
  3. Add one drop of donor's (patient's) cells in position marked anti-A and anti-B
  4. Add one drop of donors (patients) serum on slide 2 (A-cells, B-cells)
  5. Use an applicator stick and mix.
  6. Leave slides at room temperature for 2 minutes
  7. Examine for agglutination and chart the results

Disadvantages of slide method

  • False negative results can be obtained if mixing is not done properly.
  • False results can occur as a result of blood drying on the glass slide.
  • If glass is divided as above contents on one portion could mix with contents on the other portion.

Advantages

  • It is simple and quick
  • It is cheap

TILE METHOD

It employs the use of plates as a depression of surface.
The technique is the same as the slide method.

Advantages of tile method over slide method

  • It can be rotated so as to observe agglutination
  • The cells may adhere to the walls when mixing the contents giving a false positive reaction

‚ö†Ô∏è Problems (Anomalies) Encountered in Grouping

1. Rouleaux formation

This is pilling of cells on top of each other appearing as coins. This is not agglutination. Another name of Rouleaux is Pseudo-agglutination
Pseudo agglutination occurs by other means other than antigen-antibody reaction. It is chiefly associated with a shift of protein ratio in the serum
-Albumin/globulin ratio is altered:
This occurs in patients with e.g.

  • Kalaazar
  • Multiple myelomatosis where there are abnormal cells e.g. macroglobulins are present.
  • Patients administered with Dextran-40 (plasma expander)

There is more globulin than albumin in infections and chronic conditions.

üí° Differentiating between Rouleaux formation & Normal agglutination

Use washed cells or add a drop of normal saline and the agglutination will disperse.

2. Pan-agglutination

This is a spontaneous clumping of all red cells.
-The phenomenon occurs in two ways
Either red cells are altered upon by bacteria or certain viruses in such a manner they become agglutinated by all human serum said to be pan-agglutinationary.
Bacteria and viruses remove certain receptors e.g. T-receptors exposing T-antigenic sites. All human serum contains anti-T.
Example of micro-organisms Staphylococci, Clostridium welchi, Vibrio cholerae
A sample of serum may be contaminated with certain organisms e.g. Corynebacteria rendering it capable of agglutinating all samples of r.b.c's. In this case the serum is said to be panagglutinum serum.
The organisms act upon the patient's red blood cells in-vitro to expose H-antigens. In our blood there is agglutinogen- H and there will be a reaction between agglutinin- H and agglutinogen- H .

Solution/correcting the discrepancy
Cells should be washed at $37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ using warm Normal saline and the grouping done at $37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$

Auto agglutination

It is caused by auto agglutins. The patient's antibodies agglutinate his own r.b.c's. These autoantibodies can either be cold or warm e.g. cold anti I, anti M anti N, anti-I, anti-P e.g. warm anti e
These cases occur in Paroxysmal Nocturnal Haemoglobinuria (P.N.H.), passing bloody urine at night.
Other causes;
Viral pneumonia

Leukaemia
Syphilis (It has anti P specificity)

Solution/resolving the discrepancy
Wash the r.b.c in warm saline at $37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ to free them from auto agglutinins.

Poly-agglutination

A few individuals have been described whose red cells are agglutinated by a proportion of normal human serum. These cells are said to be poly agglutinable (agglutinated by many sera) as opposed to pan-agglutination (agglutinated by all sera)

‚úÖ Controls in ABO Grouping

The following are common controls used in ABO grouping.

Auto controls

Patient's serum pt's cells

Negative control

E.g. saline and cells
Use of O-cells in reverse grouping is considered a control. No agglutination is expected.

Positive control

Known sera and known cells
E.g. anti-A agglutinates group A red blood cells

Anti-B agglutinates group B red blood cells

Importance of controls

  • Confirm the blood group
  • Test potency of reagents used
  • Test accuracy of the test
  • Test efficiency of technician / technologist
  • Test specificity of test.
