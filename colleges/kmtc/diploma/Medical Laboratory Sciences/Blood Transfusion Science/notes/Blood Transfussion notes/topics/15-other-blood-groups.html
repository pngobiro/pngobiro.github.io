01

üìã Introduction & Objectives

These are Blood groups other than ABO and Rh. They are rare and are not routinely determined in routine serological studies e.g. blood bank. There major clinical significance is when they cause problems such as transfusion reaction and HDNB. They also help in disputed parentage and forensic medicine.

Purpose:

  • They cause H.D.N.B
  • Transfusion reaction
  • Immunization reactions
  • Problem in grouping
  • Problem in x - matching.
  • Helps to settle paternal disputes
  • In forensic medicine

  • Antigens and
  • Genotypes/phenotypes
  • Dosage effect
  • Secretor status
  • Serology
  • Clinical significance

üß¨ LUTHERAN

It was first in 1946 when an unknown antibody appeared in the blood of a pt that had received multiple transfusions. The ab was immune and the stimulating ag was present in one of the donors whose name was Lutheran. The antibodies involved in this blood group is such as:
antibodies Anti $\mathrm{Lu}^{\mathrm{a}}$, Anti $\mathrm{Lu}^{\mathrm{b}}$, Anti $\mathrm{Lu}^{\mathrm{ab}}$.

üìä Lutheran Genotypes and Phenotypes
GENOTYPES PHENOTYPES OCCURRENCE
$\mathrm{Lu}^{\mathrm{a}} \mathrm{Lu}^{\mathrm{b}}$ $\mathrm{Lu}(\mathrm{b}+) (\mathrm{a}+)$ $8\%$ (or $7.5\%$)
$\mathrm{Lu}^{\mathrm{a}} \mathrm{Lu}^{\mathrm{a}}$ $\mathrm{Lu}(\mathrm{a}+) (\mathrm{b}-)$ $0.2\%$ (or $0.15\%$)
$\mathrm{Lu}^{\mathrm{b}} \mathrm{Lu}^{\mathrm{b}}$ $\mathrm{Lu}(\mathrm{a}-) (\mathrm{b}+)$ $92\%$ (or $90\%$)

Lu-linkage.

Lutheran gene is independent of all other blood groups otherwise linkage exists between the secretor genes. Lutheran a ( $\mathrm{Lu}^{\mathrm{a}}$ ) is a weak antigen and possibly plays a minor role in transfusion reactions.
Anti-$\mathrm{Lu}^{\mathrm{a}}$ has not been found to cause HDNB.
Lutheran b ( $\mathrm{Lu}^{\mathrm{b}}$ ) is a weak antigen. It rarely forms Anti-$\mathrm{Lu}^{\mathrm{b}}$. Anti $\mathrm{Lu}^{\mathrm{b}}$ when formed can cause transfusion reactions.

Serology.

  • Anti-$\mathrm{Lu}^{\mathrm{a}}$ can be natural or immune antibody. It is best detected in saline at a temperature below $20^{0} \mathrm{C}$ (R.T) $\mathrm{opt}=15^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$. It rarely reacts at temps beyond $20^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$. It doesn't react in albumin, A.H.G. or enzyme
  • Anti-$\mathrm{Lu}^{\mathrm{b}}$ reacts strongly in saline @ $18-20^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$, rarely at $37^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$.
  • The strongest reaction is when using A.H.G. Both $\mathrm{Lu}^{\mathrm{a}}$ and $\mathrm{Lu}^{\mathrm{b}}$ are well developed at birth.
  • The Lu antigens are confined to the red cells. It is rare to find $\mathrm{Lu}^{\mathrm{a}}$ and $\mathrm{Lu}^{\mathrm{b}}$ in the body fluids. Neither Anti-$\mathrm{Lu}^{\mathrm{a}}$ nor Anti Lub has ever been produced in animals.
  • Lutheran antigens exhibit dosage effect.
  • Lutheran antigens also exhibit Mulberry like agglutination.

üî¥ KELL

Mouran and Race discovered this blood group system in 1946. These antibodies were discovered after they had caused H.D.N.B. they are represented as
Anti-Kell Anti-K and Anti $\Re$ (cellano)

GENOTYPES PHENOTYPES OCCURRENCE.
KK K+ve $0.2 \%$
K$\Re$ K+ve $10 \%$
$\Re$R K-ve $90 \%$

It is the third blood group of importance from ABO & Rhesus. The genotypes of Kell +ve are KK and Kell -ve are $\Re \mathrm{R}$. (Or cellano). Anti-K and Anti-$\Re$ (cellano) exists. They are immune antibodies though some natural antibodies have been encountered. No linkage exists between this blood group and others.
K is a strong antigen in man. Anti-K causes H.D.N.B. and transfusion reactions.
The antigenicity of anti cellano is not of importance but when present it can cause H.D.N.B. and transfusion reactions.

SEROLOGY.

  • Both antibodies fail to agglutinate cells suspended in saline. They are best detected by A.H.G. method.
  • Agglutination best occurs when the cells are suspended on the human serum.
  • Agglutination is inhibited by bovine albumin.
  • Immune anti-K best reacts at $37^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$.
  • Kell antibodies are labile so fresh specimens are required every time for reliable results.
  • Anti-K exhibits dosage effect (reacts more strongly in monozygous than heterozygous).
  • Both Kell and cellano antigens are well developed at birth.
  • Neither Kell nor Cellano has been produced in animals.
  • The Kell antigens are confined to the R.B.C's.

üíß LEWIS BLOOD GROUP SYSTEM

An unknown antibody believed to be naturally occurring was found in samples of blood from two different persons.
This antibody recognised an antigen present in about $22 \%$ of individuals in English population. The system was called Lewis after one of the two individuals after whom it was first found.
Lewis substances are rarely found in other body fluids. The only cases whereby they can be detected in red cells is when the concentration in body fluids or in serum is so high such that they are adsorbed onto the red cells. The system is related to the secretor status (A.B.H.) in such a way that all the $\mathrm{Le}^{\mathrm{a}+}$ ve individuals are always non-secretors of A.B.H. specific substances, but secretors of $\mathrm{Le}^{\mathrm{s}}$ substances.
Lewis system plays no part in paternity disputes. This is because both parents will have $\mathrm{Le}^{\mathrm{a}}+$ ve and still have babies who are $\mathrm{Le}^{\mathrm{a}}$-ve. $\mathrm{Le}^{\mathrm{a}}$ antigens are not inherited. They can be found in the plasma.

CHARACTERISTICS ANTI $\mathbf{Le}^{\mathbf{a}}$

It mostly occurs as a natural cold complete antibody. It reacts in saline and also in the albumin medium. It is a common cause of difficulties in compatibility tests. Anti-$\mathrm{Le}^{\mathrm{a}}$ has got lytic properties. It can lyse cells in the presence of complements and enzymes (Ficin, Papain). Anti-$\mathrm{Le}^{\mathrm{a}}$ precipitating and agglutinating sera has been prepared in animals. The reaction of Anti $\mathrm{Le}^{\mathrm{a}}$ appears like a necklace. Anti-$\mathrm{Le}^{\mathrm{a}}$ occurs more frequently in blood group A , B and AB more than in blood group O . it can be obtained by immunization of $\mathrm{Le}^{\mathrm{a}-\mathrm{b}-}$ phenotype individuals. (Lewis negative.). In adults the Ag is detectable by using fresh samples. $\mathrm{Le}^{\mathrm{a}}$ soluble substances are found in the ovarian cyst fluid. The frequency of $\mathrm{Le}^{\mathrm{a}}$ substance is highest in infant's upto 18 months. This is because $\mathrm{Le}^{\mathrm{a}}$ antigen can be detected in heterozygous state, $\mathrm{Le}^{\mathrm{a}+\mathrm{b}}$ in infants but in adults it has to be homozygous $\mathrm{Le}^{\mathrm{a}+\mathrm{a}+}$ ( $\mathrm{Le}^{\mathrm{a}} \mathrm{Le}^{\mathrm{a}}$ ). $\mathrm{Le}^{\mathrm{a}}$ substances can be detected in saliva in the inhibition state.

CLINICAL IMPORTANCE OF ANTI-$\mathbf{LE}^{\mathbf{a}}$ ANTIBODIES.

Many haemolytic transfusion reactions are due to Anti-$\mathrm{Le}^{\mathrm{a}}$ but it does not cause H.D.N.B. anti $\mathrm{Le}^{\mathrm{a}}$ are of IgM, 19s class and they are compliment binding. Anti-$\mathrm{Le}^{\mathrm{a}}$ lyses cells treated with enzyme e.g. Papain. Anti-$\mathrm{Le}^{\mathrm{a}}$ always has anti-$\mathrm{Le}^{\mathrm{b}}$ as an extra antibody. Anti-$\mathrm{Le}^{\mathrm{b}}$ develops more slowly and agglutinates more weakly in pregnancy more than anti-$\mathrm{Le}^{\mathrm{a}}$. This is because the plasma of a pregnant woman has substances that antibodiesorb the Lewis substances

üî¨ THE P BLOOD GROUP SYSTEM

Landsteiner and Levine discovered this blood group in 1927. They were doing research by immunising rabbits with human R.B.C's. They discovered antibodies, which they named anti-P, then later called anti-$\mathrm{P}_{1}$. $75 \%$ of the people were found to be $\mathrm{P}_{1}+$ ve and $25 \%$ were found to be $\mathrm{P}_{1}$-ve. Those who were found to be $\mathrm{P}_{1}$-ve were termed as $\mathrm{P}_{2}$.

GENOTYPES PHENOTYPES
$\mathrm{P}_{1}\mathrm{P}_{1}, \mathrm{P}_{1}\mathrm{P}_{2}$ $\mathrm{P}_{1}$
$\mathrm{P}_{2}\mathrm{P}_{2}, \mathrm{P}_{2}\mathrm{PP}$ $\mathrm{P}_{2}$

CHARACTERISTICS OF ANTI $\mathbf{P}_{\mathbf{1}}$.

  1. 1A naturally occurring antibody (very rare)
  2. 2It belongs to IgM class
  3. 3It is the antibody that most commonly causes problems in grouping and compatibility tests.
  4. 4It binds complement in enzyme treated cells.
  5. 5It is gotten from individuals of $\mathrm{P}_{2}$ (when immunized)
  6. 6It is also gotten from pigs, horses and rabbits.
  7. 7It is inhibited by Hydatid cyst fluid.
  8. 8It does not cause H.D.N.B. or transfusion reaction.
  9. 9It is detected at room temperature and $4^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ when blood grouping (cold agglutinin)
  10. 10It gives the best reaction with enzyme treated cells. It can also be detected by Indirect antiglobulin method. (ICT)

$\mathbf{Tj}^{\mathbf{a}}$

This is an antigen, which was later discovered by Levin, Bobbit Waller and Kuhmichel. The antigen is part of P bld grp system. Anti-$\mathrm{Tj}^{\mathrm{a}}$ reacts as anti-$\mathrm{P}_{1}+\mathrm{P}$. If Anti-$\mathrm{Tj}^{\mathrm{a}}$ is antibodiesorbed with $\mathrm{P}_{2}$ cells anti-$\mathrm{P}_{1}$ is left behind. This shows that $\mathrm{P}_{2}$ is specific for P . P antigens are not fully developed at birth. $\mathrm{P}_{1}$ does not cause H.D.N.B. but anti-$\mathrm{P}_{1}+\mathrm{P}$ (Anti $\mathrm{Tj}^{\mathrm{a}}$ ) causes H.D.N.B) and transfusion reactions.
In some cases it is known to cause early miscarriages. Anti-$\mathrm{Tj}^{\mathrm{a}}$ in the presence of a compliment lyses red cells. People with anti-$\mathrm{Tj}^{\mathrm{a}}$ are always $\mathrm{P}_{1}$-ve.

$\mathbf{P}^{\mathbf{k}}$ an extension of $\mathbf{P}$.

$\mathrm{P}^{\mathrm{k}}$ was discovered due to difficulties encountered in compatibility tests. Cells of the $\mathrm{P}^{\mathrm{k}}$ are agglutinated by both anti $\mathrm{P}_{1}$ and anti $\mathrm{P}_{1}+\mathrm{P}$. (Anti $\mathrm{Tj}^{\mathrm{a}}$ ). Anti-$\mathrm{Tj}^{\mathrm{a}}$ was found to haemolyse $\mathrm{P}^{\mathrm{k}}$ cells @ RT strongly.
Investigations showed that $\mathrm{P}^{\mathrm{k}}$ cells do not antibodiesorb Anti-$\mathrm{P}_{1}$ or anti-$\mathrm{P}_{1}+\mathrm{P}$ ( $\mathrm{Tj}^{\mathrm{a}}$ ). Anti $\mathrm{P}_{1}$ or anti-$\mathrm{Tj}^{\mathrm{a}}$ antibodiesorbed with $\mathrm{P}_{1}$ cells still agglutinates $\mathrm{P}^{\mathrm{k}}$ cells. This concludes that the P system apart from having and $\mathrm{P}_{1}+\mathrm{P}$ ( $\mathrm{TJ}^{\mathrm{a}}$ ) has $\mathrm{P}^{\mathrm{k}}$, which will react with patient's red cells.
Anti $\mathrm{P}^{\mathrm{k}}$ alone has not been found but can be prepared from anti-$\mathrm{P}_{1}+\mathrm{P}$ by antibodiesorption with $\mathrm{P}_{1}$ cells. It has not been isolated from anti $\mathrm{P}_{1}$ serum and this could be due to anti-$\mathrm{P}_{1}$ cross-reacting with $\mathrm{P}^{\mathrm{k}}$ antigen or possibly due to the $\mathrm{P}^{\mathrm{k}}$ antigen also having $\mathrm{P}_{1}$ antigens.

  • Disputed paternity cases (second from ABO in paternity cases).

üßí KIDD (Discovered in 1951):

Jk(a-b-) red cells, which lack Jk protein, are more resistant to lyses by 2M urea. Red cells of normal Jk phenotype swell and lyse rapidly in a solution of 2 M urea.
Antigens $\mathrm{Jk}^{\mathrm{a}} \mathrm{Jk}^{\mathrm{b}}$ Antibodies Anti $\mathrm{Jk}^{\mathrm{a}}$ Anti $\mathrm{Jk}^{\mathrm{b}}$.

Xtics of Anti $\mathbf{J k}^{\mathbf{a}}$

  • It is an immune ab belonging to IgG class and
  • Cause HDNB and delayed transfusion reaction DHTR's.
  • Detected by enzyme technique.
  • Detected by indirect antiglobulin technique and also in polyethylene glycol (PEG).

Xtic of $\mathbf{J k}^{\mathbf{b}}$

  • Very rare as compared to $\mathrm{Jk}^{\mathrm{a}}$
  • It is hard to obtain $\mathrm{Jk}^{\mathrm{b}}$ because they are impotent and unstable.

N.B. DHTR's occur when antibody develops so rapidly in an anamnestic response to antigens on transfused red cells that it destroys the still circulating red cells. In many cases, retesting the patients pretransfusion serum confirms that the antibody was, indeed, undetectable in the original tests. This highlights the importance of consulting previous records before selecting blood for transfusion. Patients whose antibody has previously been detected and identified can, by review of past records, be protected against repeated contact with the known immunising stimuli.

üë´ THE Xg system:

  • The sex linked blood group
  • Discovered by Mann in 1962. It is carried in the X -chromosome that makes it unique from other blood groups.

Antigens $\div \mathrm{Xg}^{\mathrm{a}}$
$A b \div \text { AntiXg }^{a}$
- Genes $\mathrm{Xg}^{\mathrm{a}}$ can be either inherited as heterozygous or homozygous.
- If a male is $\mathrm{Xg}^{\mathrm{a}}$ positive, all the daughters will be $\mathrm{Xg}^{\mathrm{a}}$ positive.
- A mother who is $\mathrm{Xg}^{\mathrm{a}}$ positive can either be homozygous or heterozygous.
- If all parents are $\mathrm{Xg}^{\mathrm{a}}$ (+ve) the daughters are $\mathrm{Xg}^{\mathrm{a}}$ positive and $1 / 2$ the sons will have $\mathrm{Xg}^{\mathrm{a}}$ negative.

Xtics of Anti $\mathbf{X g}^{\mathbf{a}}$

  • Shows dosage effect
  • AntiXg ${ }^{\mathrm{a}}$ is an uncommon antibody that usually reacts only in antiglobulin testing.
  • Found mostly in-ve men.
  • It has not been implicated in HDN or HTR's.
  • It may be usefull for tracing the transmission of genetic traits associated with the X chromosome, although linkage with the Xg locus has been demonstrated for few traits.
  • It is of IgG class.
  • Has a titre of 1:32
  • Papain and Ficin denatures the antigen.
  • Can be obtained from animals e.g. Gibbons "Long armed apes".
