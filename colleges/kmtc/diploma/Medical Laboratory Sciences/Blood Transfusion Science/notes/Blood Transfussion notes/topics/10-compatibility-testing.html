Compatibility Testing (Cross Match)

üè• Pre-Transfusion Testing
01

üìã Definition & Purpose

Compatibility test is a laboratory procedure to determine ahead of time that there is serology compatibility between a blood donor and the intended recipient. This test is one of the most important tests done in a transfusion service. Great care is necessary because of its importance, since an error in cross match may not be recognized until the recipient has received part or even the entire selected unit of blood with adverse and likely fatal reactions.

Purpose of performing cross-match:

  1. 1 To detect irregular antibodies in the recipient's serums that are directed against donor's cells. This is known as the major crossmatch
    To detect donor's antibodies directed against the recipients r.b.c's. This is minor x-match.
  2. 2 To detect errors in ABO grouping
  3. 3 To detect errors in labelling, recording or identifying donors or recipients
  4. 4 Assurance of maximum benefit for the recipient receiving the blood. This is done by
    (i) Preventing transfusion reaction by giving compatible blood to recipient
    (ii) Prevent allo-immunization
‚ÑπÔ∏è Limitations

Cross match does not:
- Ensure normal survival of donors r.b.c's
- Prevent iso-immunization of the pt
- Detect all the ABO grouping errors
- Detect all errors of identification

Transfusion is required in
- Surgical cases
- Anaemic patients; Thalassaemia, S.C.D.
- Haemorrhage / Accident
- H.D.N.B-exchange transfusion
- Maternity cases-elective surgery

üîÑ Types & Phases of X-Match

TYPES OF X-MATCH

Major X-match

To check whether serum has antibodies, which will react with donor's cells. Pts serum is used & donors cells.

Minor X-match

To check whether donor's serum has irregular antibodies which would react with pts cells hence causing transfusion ration. Donor's serum & pts cells are used.

Phases of X-match

There are four phases in X-match
(i) S.R.T
(ii) $\mathrm{S} 37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$
(iii) $\mathrm{A} 37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$
(iv) $\mathrm{C} 37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$
(v) Enzymes (not commonly used) because
(a) There is tendency to give false +ve due to auto-antibodies
(b) Extremely sensitive cold antibodies are detected e.g. anti P

Importance of the Phases

  • (i) R.T (saline)
    (i) To detect ABO incompatibility
    (ii) To detect incompatibility caused by other naturally occurring antibodies
    (iii) To detect incompatibility caused by the few immune antibodies that react in saline at R.T
  • (II) $\mathrm{S} 37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$
    (i) To detect saline reacting antibodies e.g. of Rh system-warm agglutinins (antibodies)
    (ii) To distinguish between warm and cold antibodies
  • (III) A $37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$
    - Detects most of Rh incomplete antibodies
  • (IV) $\mathrm{C} 37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$
    - Best techniques for the detection of most irregular antibodies
    - Detects almost all Rh antibodies
    - Detects most antibodies associated with haemolytic anaemia e.g. acquired haemolytic anaemia
    - The only means to detect antibodies found in rare blood groups particularly antibodies found in- Duffy, Kidd, Kell blood group system

üß™ METHODS

There are three methods of x-match
1. 2 tube method
2. 3 tube method
3. 4 tube method

4 TUBE METHOD:

Procedure

  • label test tubes S.R.T, S37, A37, C37
  • Make $4 \%$ cell suspension of donors cells and add two drops of cells to the four test tubes
  • Add two drops of pts serum to each tube
  • To A $37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ add two drops of $20 \%$ Albumin
  • Incubate the test tube to respective temperature for $\frac{1}{2} \mathrm{hr}-1 \mathrm{hr}$
  • Wash the Coombs cells with saline X3
  • Add 1 drop of AHG and mix to resuspend the cells
  • Centrifuge all test tubes and observe for agglutination record results
  • If negative, label blood/pint=COMPATIBLE and put your initials/sign

3 TUBE METHOD:

Procedure:

  • Label RT, S $37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ and A $37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$
  • Add two drops of $4 \%$ donors cell suspension to the three tubes
  • Add two drops of pts serum to the three tubes
  • Add two drops of bovine albumin $20 \%$ to t.t. labelled A $37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$
  • Incubate tubes at respective temps for $30-60$ minutes
  • Centrifuge the tubes and examine for agglutination (centrifugation 1000 r.p.m for 1 min )

Results
- If positive (agglutination) the blood is incompatible
- If negative, convert A $37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ to Coombs $37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$
- Wash with large volumes of N. saline /Three times
- Add 2 drops of AHG
- Centrifuge and examine for agglutination
- Table the results
- If negative, label blood/pint=COMPATIBLE and put your initials/sign

2TUBE METHOD:

  • label the tube as R.T and A $37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$
  • Add 2 drops of $4 \%$ donors cell suspension to the two tubes
  • add two drops of pts serum to both test tubes
  • in a $37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ add two drops $20 \%$ bovine albumin
  • Incubate at respective temps test tube at $30-60 \mathrm{mins}$
  • Centrifuge the t.t. and examine for agglutination

Results
- If positive the blood is incompatible
- If negative convert A $37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ to C $37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$
- Centrifuge and examine for agg
- Tabulate results

Commenting on match

-If all phases are ----- (minus, minus, minus) blood is compatible
If phase 1 is positive and other phases are negative, the blood can the transfused but if there is another unit that is not + ve in this phase it should be used. If with $37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ is weakly positive and all other phases are negative, this is due to weak antibodies Davy / Kidd / Kell that can cause delayed extravascular HTR's. To be on blood for transfusion that is positive

üö® EMERGENCY X-MATCH

In cases of emergency the patient's physician must weigh the risks of transfusing un-xmatched blood against the hazards of waiting for a compatibility test. If the physician/Doctor believes the urgency of situation warrants releasing of blood without x-match then it may justifiably be done. But the physician/Doctor must accept any responsibility of any incompatibility to the compatibility test ( Ab/Ag reaction) and should put it in writing.
In urgent situation:

  • Give the un x-matched blood to physician if he demands it. (He puts it in writing)
  • Make sure the physician signs the release of the blood.
  • Begin the routine compatibility testing procedures.
  • Compare the tests as soon as possible and if incompatibility is noticed at any stage of testing, notify the physician so that he stops the transfusion.

In cases of extreme emergencies blood group O-ve with low titre is given although transfusions of such blood is not without a risk.

PROCEDURE

  • It's almost the same although time is shortened $15-30 \mathrm{mins}$.
  • Four phases are in-corporated, if +ve at any step blood is incompatible warn the physician.

‚ö†Ô∏è ERRORS IN X-MATCHING:

False +ve reactions.

  1. iPoor quality of normal saline can cause false +ve reaction in x - matching.
  2. iiOver-centrifugation can cause false +ve reaction.
  3. iiiBlood with fibrin clot can cause false +ve reaction.
  4. ivDirty glassware.
  5. vUse of contaminated reagents.
  6. viSlight haemolysis that goes unnoticed.

False-ve reactions

  • Failure to add serum to tubes
  • Failure to incubate for right time with right reagents
  • Incomplete washing of A $37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$, due to traces of globulin that neutralizes AHG.

ü©∏ SELECTION OF BLOOD FOR TRANSFUSION:

i. Choice of ABO group

ABO is the most dangerous in causing incompatible blood transfusion. This is because over $90 \%$ of the population have Anti A or Anti B in their body, Thus the patient should receive blood of their own blood group. This minimizes possible serological reaction to the patient.
Emergencies due to clinical or shortage of certain blood in the bank it is not always possible to select homologous blood.

Group O recipients

- If possible even when in emergency blood group A should receive from blood group A. If circumstances do not allow blood group A individual can be transfused with blood group O. Blood group A recipients are more in danger (risk) more than blood group B individual if given blood group O . This is because antibody A found in blood group O is more potent than the antibody B in blood group O. Even when blood group O is selected from a bank from which even low titre has been performed it is not advisable to give more than one or two units if it's of high titre. If of low titre, amount required can be given.

Blood group B recipients

It is normally preferable to give the patient his own blood group. But group B individuals have a better chance of receiving blood group O than blood group A. AntiB in blood group O is not very potent.

Blood group AB recipients

Group AB individuals were regarded to be universal recipients. AB individuals lack Antibody A & B thus red cells of all donors are compatible with blood group AB individuals. Transfusion with blood group O should be avoided at all times. If AB donors are not available, either group A or group B blood can be used. Blood group A is likely to be more available and its anti B is slightly weaker than anti A in blood group B.
1. Choice / consideration of Rh type

Typing with anti D is a routine procedure for all recipients all D -ve individuals should receive D ve blood when the Rh -ve blood is in short supply the following have the following priority
1 pt that has Rh antibody
2. Rh -ve women not past child bearing age
3. Infant with HDNB

NB: it is very important that girls and women who are Rh -ve should never receive Rh +ve blood this is because of possible future effect on their pregnancy.
