‚öóÔ∏è Antigen Antibody Reactions

Reaction Type Antigen Antibody
i) Agglutination agglutinogen agglutinin
ii) Precipitation precipitinogen precipitin
iii) Haemolysis Haemolysinogen haemolysin

A complement is required for Haemolysis to occur.

1. AGGLUTINATION:

Is a visible antigen - antibody reaction causing R.B.C's to clump together. The antigen involved is known as agglutinogen. An example is Antigen A and Antigen B. The antibody involved in agglutination is agglutinin. E.g. Antibody A and B.
Agglutination takes place in two stages (phases).

Sensitization ($1^{\text{st}}$ stage)

It is not visible - invisible.
The antibody (agglutinin) attaches itself on the agglutinogen on the cell surface (R.B.C's), and thus the red cell that has taken up the agglutinin is said to be sensitised. There is no reaction. This process takes a very short time.

Actual agglutination ($2^{\text{nd}}$ stage) or clumping of R.B.C's takes place.

The red blood cells form agglutinates which are large enough to be seen by the naked eye. The cells change in shape and become distorted, and sometimes the R.B.C's membranes break to release haemoglobin.
Agglutination is the most important of the reactions (Antigen - Antibody).

2. HAEMOLYSIS:

It is a visible Antigen - Antibody reaction causing the lying of R.B.C's (lyse - break down) in the presence of a complement. The antibody involved in haemolysin and the antigen is haemolysinogen.
Complement is a group of enzymatic proteins found in normal fresh serum (not in plasma).
The complement is thermo labile - destroyed at $56^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ for 30 minutes.
There are three phases in haemolysis;

  • Sensitization: The haemolysin attaches itself on the surface of R.B.C's
  • $2^{\text{nd}}$ stage: Compliment fixation
    The complement fixes itself on the antigen/antibody complex.
  • Final stage ($3^{\text{rd}}$ stage). Haemolysis
    Haemolysis occurs and haemoglobin is liberated from the R.B.C's and can readily be observed staining the supernatant fluid.

3. PRECIPITATION:

The main difference:
In agglutination and haemolysis we use cellular antigen.
In precipitation the antigen and the antibody are soluble
Precipitinogen is the antigen involved in precipitation.
Precipitin is the antibody used in precipitation.
The soluble antibody and antigen react in the solution causing a precipitate or flocculation.

üß¨ Immunoglobulins (Antibodies)

They are made of amino-acids (proteins) that are regulated in sequence by the genes concerned in the reproduction of antibodies. The antibodies are produced in the gamma globulin. The immunoglobulins are classified into five classes.
IgG
IgM
IgA Antibodies
IgE
IgD

It is only the IgG which have sub-classes i.e. $\mathrm{IgG}_{1} \mathrm{IgG}_{2} \mathrm{IgG}_{3} \mathrm{IgG}_{4}$.

IgG - Immunoglobulin Gamma ($\gamma$)

It is composed of 4 chains that are made of amino - acids. Among the 4 - chains there are two identical heavy chains (H).
The heavy chains are the ones that are Gamma ($\gamma$).
There are also two identical light chains Kappa or Lambda.
The chains are joined together by a covalent di-sulphide bond.

IgG is a monomer.

IgM - Immunoglobulin Mu. ($\mu$)

It is made up of five structural units arranged in a circular manner and forms a crab lice shape. The two light chains are made of Kappa or Lambda and the heavy chain is made up of Mu (—Ü). (DIAGRAMME)

IgA - Immunoglobulin Alpha ($\alpha$)

Molecule is a dimmer (2)
The heavy chain is Alpha and the light chain is either Kappa or Lambda.

IgD: Immunoglobulin Delta ($\delta$)

Made up of two delta heavy chains and two light chains made up of Kappa or Lambda. It is catabolised and has a half life of only 3 -days. It was first encountered as a myeloma protein.

IgE Immunoglobulin Epsilon ($\varepsilon$)

Two heavy chains made up of epsilon and two identical light chains that can be lambda or kappa. After research it has been found IgE is increased in parasitic infection. It's also increased in allergic reactions. It's also increased in myelomas, liver disease and auto-immune diseases, and helminthiasis.

Immune antibodies;

  1. Heavy chain is Gamma
  2. Incomplete antibodies
  3. Are developed by known antigenic stimuli.
  4. Caused by secondary immune response
  5. This can be either during pregnancy or blood transfusion and immunization.
  6. The immune antibodies generally agglutinate R.B.C's in Bovine albumin media
  7. Incomplete antibodies causes sensitization in vivo/vitro
  8. They react best at $37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ and thus they are called warm agglutinins.
  9. They have a molecular weight of about 150,000 Dalton.
  10. They measure about 250 √É (Armstrong) in length.
  11. They belong to IgG class.
  12. They are able to cross through the placenta thus causing Haemolytic Disease of The New Born (H.D.N.B.).
  13. They have $4 \%$ carbohydrates.
  14. They have a sedimentation rate of 7s .
  15. Has a valency of 2
  16. They fix the complement and thus very haemolytic.
  17. The component of an immune globulin chain is a monomer, because it has one structure.
  18. The percentage of total globulin is $75 \%$ in serum.
  19. Immunological response of immune globulins is secondary.
  20. They are not completely neutralised by soluble blood group substances.
  21. It has a serum half life of 23 days.
  22. Serum concentration of 1,000 to $1,5000 \mathrm{mg} / \mathrm{dl}$
  23. Synthetic rate of $33 \mathrm{mg} / \mathrm{kg} / \mathrm{day}$
  24. Not present in epithelial secretion
  25. Distribution (% of total in intravascular space) is $45 \%$
  26. Fixes the compliment and highly haemolytic
  27. Not broken down by sulphyhydryl reagents e.g. 2-ME
  28. Can span a distance of 35 nanometers
  29. Neutralization test positive
  30. Microglobulins
  31. Causes extravascular transfusion reactions

Natural antibodies;

  1. Heavy chain is Mu
  2. Produced by non-pathogenic stimulant.
  3. Cause by primary immune response
  4. They are complete antibodies thus can react in saline media.
  5. They cause agglutination after immediate spin procedure
  6. They react better below $37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ i.e. at room temperature ( $18^{\circ}\mathrm{C}-25^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ ).
  7. They are cold agglutinins.
  8. They have a molecular weight of 900,000 - 1,000,000 Daltons
  9. They don't pass through the placental barrier (membrane).
  10. They measure about 1000 √É in length.
  11. They belong to the IgM class of antibodies.
  12. They have a sedimentation rate of 18s or 19s.
  13. They develop between 3-6 months.
  14. They are pentamer (polymer) in shape.
  15. Has a valency of 10
  16. Examples are Anti-A and Anti-B.
  17. The carbohydrates concentration is about $11.8 \%$.
  18. They are completely neutralized by soluble blood group substances (BGSS).
  19. The immunoglobulin has a half life of 5days.
  20. Synthetic rate is 6 to $7 \mathrm{mg} / \mathrm{kg} / \mathrm{day}$
  21. Fixes the compliment but not haemolytic
  22. Distribution (% of total in intravascular space) is 76%
  23. Not present in epithelial secretions
  24. Can span a distance of 14nanometers
  25. Macroglobulin
  26. Causes intravascular transfusion reactions
  27. Denatured by sulphydryl reagents
  28. HMwt

üéØ Zones of Antibody/Antigen Reaction

There are basically three zones of antigen-antibody reactions.

  1. Zones of antibody excess in which un-combined antibody is present i.e. antibody is more than antigen also referred to as PROZONE phenomenon.
    In an antigen antibody system, a negative reaction is obtained with low dilutions (concentrations) of the antibody, although a positive reaction is obtained with higher dilutions (low concentration) of the same antibodies. This presents problem (anomaly) in grouping and cross-matching because of false positive and false-ve reactions.
  2. Zone of antigen excess in which all antigen have combined with antibody and antigen are more than antibody(un-combined antigen) e.g. while preparing sensitized pooled O cells for use as positive control in Coomb's test.
  3. Zone of equivalence. Both antigen and antibody are present in equal amounts and we have equal antigen-antibody reaction and maximum compliment fixation.
