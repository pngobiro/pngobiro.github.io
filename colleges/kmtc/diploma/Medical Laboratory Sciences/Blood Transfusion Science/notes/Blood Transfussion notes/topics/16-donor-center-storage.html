üìã Planning and Setting of a Blood Donor Centre

Roles of donor centre;

  1. 1Donor recruitment
  2. 2Phlebotomy.
  3. 3Blood screening e.g. blood grouping
  4. 4Preparing of blood products e.g. Fresh Frozen Plasma
  5. 5Advisory centre or Research centre.

STAFF

  1. 1The centre should be headed by a qualified staff either a medical technologist or pathologist. The in charge serves as the administrator, public relation adviser and a research worker.
  2. 2Technical staff worker in the centre must have a thorough knowledge of blood donor recruitment. They should make sure that they perform phlebotomy to donors who are fit medically to avoid any undue legal implications.
  3. 3Only people in experience in vene-puncture should perform phlebotomy to donors using aseptic technique. It is important that donors have confidence in the staff.
  4. 4Clerical officers are also essential for record keeping and read other supporting officers for cleaning the place and equipment that does no require any skills.

THE BUILDING:

  1. 1The donor, area includes - space for reception (waiting)
  2. 2Counselling room)
  3. 3Phlebotomy area
  4. 4Recovering room.
  5. 5It should be located in an area that has a free and unobstructed flow of traffic.
  6. 6It should have good lighting and ventilation.
  7. 7All surfaces should be of materials that could be easily and thoroughly cleaned.
  8. 8The room for registration and waiting should be arranged to prevent observation of other hospital activities.
  9. 9A private waiting room is always desirable particularly if the centre is in the hospital premises since some people may be discouraged from donating blood after observing other hospital activities.
  10. 10Privacy is desirable for interview and examination of prospective donors. Donors may not give a reliable medical history or may fail to ask questions if other donors, visitors or other blood donor personnel can hear what they are saying. Physical examination cannot be done in open place.
  11. 11The donor bleeding room should be arranged to have the least disturbance either from visitors or non-essential personnel. If possible curtains should be used to separate one donor from the other.
  12. 12Air conditioning may be required during warm humid whether.
  13. 13A canteen may be just adjacent to the bleeding room for a drink and can be combined in an observation room.
  14. 14A separate and private room should be available for treatment of donors who have reactions during or after donation.
  15. 15The centre should have room or area for processing blood and storage purposes.
  16. 16The centre should always have blood taking sets or bags.

Its always important to take detailed information of the donor e.g. address, names, locality, where they work.

02

üì¢ Recruitment & Equipment

RECRUITMENT OF DONORS:

In order of the blood donor centre to be able to cope in blood supplies due to increased demand, a blood donor centre should formulate a programme for getting it's own donors. i.e. Voluntary Non remunerated Safe Blood Donors.
Where applicable relatives should be encouraged to donate blood for their patients.
Some of the methods of recruitment of donors is to lecture in schools, colleges and even factories and also through chief's baraza.
There are some people due to superstitious or religious belief shall the responsibility for donating blood. Great effort should be done to educate such a people.
Other mode of blood campaign is thro' posters mass media e.g. T.V, radio(most effective) and even local newspapers.

EQUIPMENT:

At no time should a blood donor centre lack blood-taking sets.

  • Pressure cuffs
  • Hb machines
  • Haematocrit machines
  • Clinical thermometer
  • Centrifuges
  • Water baths
  • Blood bank refrigerator(s)
  • Refrigerated centrifuges for preparation of blood components.
  • Hand magnification lens
  • Microscopes
  • Equipment for testing Aids, syphilis, Hepatitis B, Hepatitis C, Sterilising equipment

Glassware should be carefully washed and rinsed in detergent.
Refrigerators in which blood is stored must contain only blood or blood products , grouping seras or reagents that are necessary for blood bank.
Food should never be kept in refrigerators where blood is kept; even bacteriological specimen.
The refrigerator should have adequate circulatory of air preferably by means of a fan. Blood should never be kept near freezing compartment because of the risk of haemolysing the blood.
The interior of blood storing refrigerator must be immaculate, should have light preferably internal light system necessary in facilitating inspection of blood. Should have reliable thermometer and normal temperature range 2 $-6^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$. Temperature of each refrigerator must be recorded by automating recording thermometer and should be checked from time to time to ascertain its reliability.
The sensor of the thermometer recording system must be in water or $10 \%$ glycerol in a container similar to a blood container. This should be located in an ideal place in the refrigerator fluid is used because it changes like temperature of blood. Temperature records must be dated.
Alarm system in the storage refrigerator protects against accidental loses of blood due to failure of proper refrigerator, audible bell, visible light signals or both should be arranged to warn the changes outside the permissible limits of $2-6^{\circ} \mathrm{c}$.
Alarm system must be separate from electrical circuit that operates the refrigerator e.g. run by battery. It can be connected to a police station if the premise is not guarded at night.
Fridge for blood should have a standby generator that switches on automatically once there is a power failure.

03

ü©∏ Donor Selection & Phlebotomy

Receipt and Registration:

The prospective donor should be made to feel welcome relaxed and at ease.
Donor registration sheet (card) should be designed such that they give as much details regarding the donor. The information given will be of value in present or future references.

DONOR CRITERIA:

A prospective donor is only allowed to donate if she/he fulfils the following criteria.

  1. 1Age - 16 - 65 years
  2. 2Physical appearance: A donor should not have rushes on the body - or many scars especially on the arms.
  3. 3Lactating, pregnant women are not allowed to donate blood
  4. 4Period of last donation = Not less than 4 months for Female and 3 months for male.
  5. 5Anyone who has suffered from hepatitis and/or HIV are permanently deffered
  6. 6Anyone who is alcoholic or drug addicted should be permanently deferred.
  7. 7People with bleeding disorders.
  8. 8Hb level should be in the normal range.
    Female $\mathbf{1 2 . 5}-\mathbf{1 6 . 5} \mathrm{gm} / \mathbf{d l}$
    Male $\mathbf{1 3 . 5 - 1 8} \mathrm{g} / \mathbf{d l}$
  9. 9People suffering from syphilis, Aids, hypertension, Diabetes should not donate blood.
  10. 10Low risk group.
  11. 11Should NOT have taken a heavy meal prior to the procedure and also should at least have taken a meal in 12 hrs .

NB: Those transfused in blood must be treated for malaria.

PHLEBOTOMY:

The donor should be greeted cheerfully and should be assisted to the phlebotomy bed. The room should be a peaceful, pleasant and cool one.

PROCEDURE:

Always confirm the serial number of the pint and donors card sample tube and vice versa.

  1. 1Sterilise the vein puncture site, after it has been prepared nothing should be touched to this area except the needle.
  2. 2Select the best vein for the sterilised area and unless the veins of donors are visible, apply a tourniquet or blood pressure cuff to the donor upper arm and inflate to $50-60 \mathrm{mmHg}$ and instruct the donor to open and close the first.
  3. 3Immediately do the phlebotomy without delay.
  4. 4After phlebotomy, tape the needle and place sterile gauze over it. Donor must never be left unattended during donation. Should take approx. 9 minutes
  5. 5Mix blood and anticoagulant gently, frequently enough to prevent coagulation.
  6. 6If the blood is adequate, this can be determined by either weighing the pint or apply pressure, the pint should never be too hard or flappy.

After collection:

  • i= Heat seal the tube, apply a knot or put a clip. (metal clip with a hand sealer)
  • ii= Apply gentle pressure on the punctured site in the thumb using a sterile gauze.
  • iii= Tell the donor to raise his arm and elbow kept straight and to hold the gauze firmly on the punctured site in his other hand.
  • iv= Another seal should be made near the beg.
  • v= Recheck the blood pint number and the donors card again and refrigerate the unit immediately either in a special blood transportation box with enough ice packs (should not go $>10^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ during transportation) or in a refrigerator at $2-4^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$.

INSTRUCTION TO BLOOD DONORS AFTER PHLEBOTOMY:

  1. 1The donor should lie at least for 10 minutes.
  2. 2Drink more fluids than usual for the next few hours and have a good meal 24 hrs .
  3. 3In case of any bleeding from the puncture, should apply pressure with the finger to stop it.
  4. 4Should he/she feel faint or dizzy after leaving the bed, should sit down immediately and put head between knees or lie with head lower than the rest of the body until the dizziness stops.
  5. 5Donor should avoid strenuous activities for the next 24 -hrs e.g. playing football.
  6. 6Should not donate again for the next $3 / 4$ months.

üíä Blood Products & Inspection

BLOOD INSPECTION:

All blood units in storage refrigerator should be examined periodically during storage and immediately before issuing. A record of this inspection must be maintained and show date and description of any abnormal findings.
Blood should be rejected for transfusion if the colour or physical appearance is abnormal or any suspicion of contamination e.g. if the blood has a purplish colour or if a zone of haemolysis is observed in the plasma or if clots appear.
Other features are, purple, golden yellow or excessive greenish plasma. At times green colour develops in the plasma of blood stored in the dark. It fades on exposure to light, units of these nature are used for transfusion. Inadequate sealing or closure of bag renders that blood unsuitable for transfusion. In case a donor unit is rejected it should never be discarded in the form of intact unit in public waste collecting system i.e. for both health and public relationship reasons may cause; Risk of spreading infection; discourage donors.

CHECKLIST FOR SIGNS OF DETIRIORATION IN BLOOD AND PLASMA:

  • Any leaks at the pig-tails/heat seal /clip
  • Haemolysis in plasma/pink plasma
  • Haemolysis on the line between red cells and plasma
  • Clots in the plasma
  • Red cells -are they normal or purple or black?

BLOOD PRODUCTS:

‚ñ∂ 1. PACKED RED CELLS

These are cells from which virtually all plasma has been removed.

Preparation:

  1. iCentrifuge whole blood at about 2000rpm for 15-30 minutes.
  2. iiRemove the supernatant - plasma
  3. iiiWhat remains is packed cells.

Uses:
iv. For treating anaemic pts particularly those requiring many pints: For pts who had formed mild reactions to some none - red cell components of the blood.

‚ñ∂ 2. WASHED RED CELLS

Also called white cells poor blood.

Preparation:

  1. iCentrifuge whole blood
  2. iiRemove the supernatant - plasma
  3. iiiAdd sterile physiological saline to the remaining packed cells/resuspend the cells.
  4. ivCentrifuge at 2000 rpm for about 15 minutes.
  5. vRemove the supernatant.
  6. viRepeat washing for about 3 times and all this is done in sterile conditions.

Washing - is to remove all traces of plasma and some white cells and platelets.
Washed cells are used in cases of ;
(i) Paroxysmal Nocturnal Haemoglobinuria - PNH
(ii) Patients who have transfusion reactions due to antibodies against platelets, WBC & protein components.

‚ñ∂ 3. WHITE CELLS

Are prepared by continuous plasmapheresis. This involves cell separators, which will specifically remove white cells from whole blood of the donor, and the rest is returned to the donor. The best donors for white cells are pts who have Chronic Myeloid Leukaemia who are undergoing leucopheresis.
White cells can also be obtained from the buffy coat.
White cells are used to Rx pts with Aplastic anaemia & also patients with low white cell count. 20 - 30 pints for one patient. The procedure takes 2 - 3 hrs.

‚ñ∂ 4. PLATELETS RICH PLASMA

Platelet rich plasma is prepared by bleeding donor in double (satellite) bags in 1 or 2 bags. The blood is collected in the main bag and centrifuge at low speed about 1000 r.p.m. for $20-30$ minutes at R.T.

The reason of centrifuging at low speed is to avoid packing of platelets.
After centrifuging the platelet rich plasma is extracted to the satellite bag.
The pilot tube connecting the satellite bag is cut after clipping.
Platelet rich plasma must be used immediately or in 24 hrs and stored at R.T(btn 20-24oC) ambient temperature and mixed continuously. Kept at R.T in continuous shaking with a platelet mixer.

Uses:
To treat pts in thrombocytopenia, to stop spontaneous bleeding; in pts with platelet dysfunction; hypoplastic anaemia; or bone marrow failure due to replacement or to the effects of chemotherapy.

‚ñ∂ 5. PLATELET CONCENTRATE

Preparation is the same as platelet rich plasma but after obtaining the platelet rich plasma the pilot tube is not cut from the main bag immediately.
The satellite bag in the platelet rich plasma is centrifuged hard at approximately 3000 rpm for $10-20$ minutes.
Then the plasma is taken back to the main bag leaving about 20 ml of the plasma in the satellite bag for resuspending the packed platelets.

Uses:
In cases of thrombocytopaenia where plasma is not required in large quantity to avoid overloading/ pts in acute bleeding/those in purpura platelet deficiency has been established. In acute bleeding particularly where platelet deficiency has been established.

Purpura.
A condition where a pt bleeds through the skin due to platelet deficiency.

‚ñ∂ 6. FRESH FROZEN PLASMA (F.F.P.)

Is prepared from fresh blood less than 4 hrs old.
Is prepared by centrifuging whole blood a $2000-3000 \mathrm{rpm}$ for $20-30$ minutes and then plasma extracted to the satellite bag at Room temperature.
The two bags are then separated.
F.F.P is immediately stored @ $-20^{\circ} \mathrm{c}$ or less.

Uses:
Pts with burns
Pts in shock
Surgery (heart surgery)

‚ñ∂ 7. CRYOPRECIPITATE

Is prepared from fresh blood less than 4 hrs old.
Blood is collected in double bag and then centrifuged @ $4^{\circ} \mathrm{c}$ @ 1000 rpm for 10 - 15minutes.
Plasma is then extracted into the satellite bag.
The satellite bag is then immediately immersed completely in a mixture of dry ice and alcohol (methyl alcohol).
This mixture freezes the plasma immediately and attains a temperature of about $-70^{\circ} \mathrm{c}$.

At this stage the pilot tube is still attached to the main bag.
The main bag should be kept away from the mixture to avoid haemolysis.
The plasma is then thawed @ $4^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ overnight
When thawed, the bags are centrifuged hard at 2000 rpm for about $10-20$ minutes.
The clear supernatant - plasma - is extracted back to the main bag.
About $10-20 \mathrm{mls}$ of plasma is left in the satellite bag.
After thawing the plasma contain cotton - wool like precipitate.
This precipitate is rich in factor VIII and Fibrinogen and is what is known as cryoprecipitate and does not dissolve back in plasma.
After allowing almost all clear supernatant to the main bag and leaving $10-20 \mathrm{mls}$ in the satellite the main bag is separated from the satellite.
The cryoprecipitate in satellite bag is stored @ $-20^{\circ}$.

Uses:
Treatment of haemophilic patients.
vii. Cryoprecipitate is thawed before use contents from units are given intra - venously using syringes.

Anticoagulants;

These are anticoagulants used primarily for blood transfusion.
Include: ACD, CPD, CPDA, and Heparin.

1. A.C.D. - Acid - Citrate - Dextrose:

- Citric acid - monohydrate 80g
- Dextrose 25g
- Trisodium citrate - dihydrate 22g
- Water 1000 mls (1litre)

Purpose of the ingredients:
(a) Trisodium citrate: Prevents coagulation by binding calcium
(b) Citric Acid. Maintain the pt
(c) Dextrose Nutrient for cells/energy
(d) Water Solvent.

ACD:
When used in plastic bags, contains $60-75 \mathrm{mls}$ and should be mixed in 450 mls of whole blood.
Blood collected in ACD should be used in 21 days and should be stored @ $4^{\circ} \mathrm{c}$.
After 21 days $\mathrm{K}^{+}$ escapes from the cells into the solution through osmosis and combine in citrate to form K citrate that is toxic.
Has pH 5 (acid) which makes RBC's loose visibility faster Likewise Na+ escapes from anticoagulant to cell hence ion content of cells is altered.

2. C.P.D. (Citrate Phosphate Dextrose)

Trisodium citrate (dihydrate) -26-3g
Citric acid 3.27 g
Sodium dihydrogen phosphate (monohydrate) 2.22 g - for buffer pH .
Dextrose 25.5g
Water 1000 mls .

USES
63 mls of C.P.D is mixed in 450 mls of whole blood.
Blood to be used in 28-32 days
C.P.D is less acid than ACD and hence causes less haemolysis.
Loss of $\mathrm{K}^{+}$ from RBC is also less.
Cell viability in CPD is better than ACD.
CPD maintains the RBC's D.P.G - diphosphoglyceric acid.
Due to less acidity, an enzyme known as mutase is capable of producing D.P.G.
Acid pH enhances production of phosphatase that breaks down D.P.G

3. C.P.D.A

Trisodium citrate (dihydrate)
Citric acid
Na HPO4
Dextrose
Adenine
Water.

The addition of adenine to C.P.D raises the ATP level in stored blood.
It also increases the post - survival level of cells when blood is transfused.
It also maintains the D.P.G better than when C.P.D is used alone.
Adenine has no adverse clinical reactions
It is safe even for exchange transfusion in new - borns.
Blood stored in C.P.D.A can be used between $32-35$ days.
Available as CPDA I and II

4. HEPARIN:

Is the best anticoagulant but does not keep blood for long.
Blood collected in heparin must be used immediately within 24 hours.
A large quantity of heparin is needed to collect a pint of blood - Heparin is very expensive.

SAFE STORAGE OF BLOOD

Whole blood and red cells must always be stored at a temperature between ${ }^{+} 2^{0} \mathrm{C}$ and $6^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$.

The main reasons for giving a blood transfusion are to restore or help to maintain the body's oxygen carrying capacity and the volume of blood circulating around the body. If blood is not stored at between ${ }^{+} 2^{0} \mathrm{C}$ and ${ }^{+} 6^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$, its oxygen carrying ability is greatly reduced.
The anticoagulant/preservative solution in the blood bag contains nutrients for the blood during storage and stops the blood from clotting. The red cells can only carry and deliver oxygen if they remain viable: that is, if they retain the same properties as they have during their normal circulation in the body.
The most important substances in maintaining the viability of red cells are glucose and adenosine triphosphste(ATP). It is essential to maintain an equilibrium between ATP, 2,3 Diphosphoglycerate (2,3DPG), glucose and pH . One of the anticoagulant/preservatives most commonly used is citrate phosphate dextrose with adenine (CPDA-1). The dextrose and adenine help the red cells to maintain ATP during storage, and the citrate is the anticoagulant which stops the blood from clotting.
Another important reason for storing blood between ${ }^{+} 2^{0} \mathrm{C}$ and ${ }^{+} 6^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ is to keep the growth of any bacterial contamination in the unit of blood to a minimum. If blood is stored above ${ }^{+} 6^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$, bacteria that may have inadvertently entered the unit during collection may grow to such an extent that transfusion of the contaminated blood could be fatal.
The lower limit of ${ }^{+} 2^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ is also very important. This is because red cells are very sensitive to freezing. If they are allowed to freeze, the red red cells membranes rupture and haemoglobin is released; that is, the cells are haemolysed. The transfusion of haemolysed blood can also be fatal.

Changes that take place in stored blood.

  1. 1During storage there is fall in ATP.
  2. 2Depletion of ATP during storage have the following effects.
  3. 3The R.b.c changes their shape from disc shape to spherical.
  4. 4RBC's loose their membrane lipids, integrity of cell membrane is interfered with
  5. 5There is an increase in cellular rigidity rbc's loose the bi-concave shape
  6. 6The depletion of 2,3-DPG lowers cell/RBC's $\mathrm{O}_{2}$ affinity.
  7. 7Potassium leaves the red cells into plasma
  8. 8Sodium leaves plasma into red cells
  9. 9Formation of citrate toxicity
  10. 10Haemolysis is evident
  11. 11Bacterial contamination
