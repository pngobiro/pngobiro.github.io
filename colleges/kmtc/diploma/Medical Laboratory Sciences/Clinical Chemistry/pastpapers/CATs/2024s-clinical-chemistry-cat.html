Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions (20 Marks)

1.
What is the normal pH range of arterial blood?
a) 7.5 - 7.8
b) 7.35 - 7.45
c) 7.0 - 7.2
d) 6.8 - 7.0
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) 7.35 - 7.45

Explanation: The normal pH range for arterial blood is strictly regulated between 7.35 and 7.45. Values below 7.35 indicate acidosis, and values above 7.45 indicate alkalosis.

2.

b) Carbonic acid-bicarbonate buffer system
c) Calcium phosphate system
d) Chloride shift
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Carbonic acid-bicarbonate buffer system

Explanation: The carbonic acid-bicarbonate buffer system is the most important extracellular buffer system in the blood, helping to maintain pH homeostasis.

3.

Explanation: PaCO2 (Partial pressure of Carbon Dioxide) is directly regulated by alveolar ventilation. It is the respiratory component of acid-base balance.

Section 2: Short Answer Questions (20 Marks)

1.
What is the importance of HDL cholesterol in cardiovascular health? (5 marks)
Show Answer
  • Reverse Cholesterol Transport: HDL (High-Density Lipoprotein) helps transport cholesterol from peripheral tissues back to the liver for excretion, reducing the risk of plaque formation in arteries.
  • Anti-inflammatory Properties: HDL has anti-inflammatory effects, which can protect blood vessels from damage.
  • Antioxidant Effects: HDL prevents the oxidation of LDL (Low-Density Lipoprotein), which is a key step in the development of atherosclerosis.
  • Vasodilatory Effects: HDL promotes the production of nitric oxide, which helps dilate blood vessels and improve blood flow.
  • Cardioprotective Role: High levels of HDL are associated with a lower risk of cardiovascular diseases, such as heart attacks and strokes.

Show Answer

Process:

  • Sample Collection: Blood gas samples are typically collected from an arterial puncture (e.g., radial artery) or from an arterial line.
  • Measurement: Blood gas analyzers measure parameters such as pH, PaCO2, PaO2, HCO3-, and SaO2.

Importance:

  • Acid-Base Balance: Helps diagnose and manage acid-base imbalances (e.g., acidosis, alkalosis).
  • Guide Treatment: Assists in making decisions regarding oxygen therapy, mechanical ventilation, and other critical interventions.
  • Prognostic Indicator: Provides valuable information about the patient's clinical status and prognosis.

4.
A patient's lipid profile shows the following values: Total cholesterol (TC): 250 mg/dL, HDL-C: 50 mg/dL, Triglycerides (TG): 150 mg/dL. Using the Friedewald equation, calculate the patient's Low-Density Lipoprotein Cholesterol (LDL-C). Provide your answer in mg/dL. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Given Values:

  • Total cholesterol (TC) = 250 mg/dL
  • HDL-C = 50 mg/dL
  • Triglycerides (TG) = 150 mg/dL

Friedewald Equation:

$$ LDL-C = TC - HDL-C - (\frac{TG}{5}) $$

Calculation:

$$ LDL-C = 250 - 50 - (\frac{150}{5}) $$

$$ LDL-C = 250 - 50 - 30 $$

$$ LDL-C = 170 \mathrm{mg/dL} $$

Answer: The patient's LDL-C is 170 mg/dL.

Section 3: Long Essay Questions (20 Marks)

1.
Explain the significance of the Henderson-Hasselbalch equation in acid-base physiology. Describe how it is used to calculate blood pH and interpret acid-base imbalances in clinical practice. (20 Marks)
Show Answer

Significance:

  • pH Calculation: The Henderson-Hasselbalch equation is used to calculate the pH of blood based on the concentration of bicarbonate ($HCO_3^-$) and the partial pressure of carbon dioxide ($PaCO_2$).
  • Equation: $$ pH = pKa + \log \left( \frac{[HCO_3^-]}{PaCO_2 \times 0.03} \right) $$
  • Interpretation of Imbalances: Helps in understanding and interpreting acid-base imbalances such as metabolic acidosis, metabolic alkalosis, respiratory acidosis, and respiratory alkalosis.
  • Clinical Application: Guides the diagnosis and management of acid-base disorders in clinical practice.
  • Buffer Systems: Explains the role of bicarbonate and carbon dioxide in maintaining acid-base balance.

Calculation and Interpretation:

  • Example Calculation: Given $HCO_3^- = 24 \mathrm{mEq/L}$, $PaCO_2 = 40 \mathrm{mmHg}$, pKa for bicarbonate buffer system = 6.1.
  • pH Calculation: $$ pH = 6.1 + \log \left( \frac{24}{40 \times 0.03} \right) $$
  • $$ pH = 6.1 + \log \left( \frac{24}{1.2} \right) $$
  • $$ pH = 6.1 + \log(20) $$
  • $$ pH = 6.1 + 1.3 $$
  • $$ pH = 7.4 $$
  • Clinical Significance: pH of 7.4 indicates normal acid-base balance. Deviations from this value indicate acid-base disturbances that require clinical attention.
2.
Discuss the physiological mechanisms involved in maintaining acid-base balance in the human body. Include the roles of the respiratory system, renal system, and buffer systems in your explanation. (20 marks)
Show Answer

Buffer Systems:

  • Bicarbonate Buffer System: Primary extracellular buffer that neutralizes acids and bases.
  • Phosphate Buffer System: Important in the intracellular and renal buffering of hydrogen ions.
  • Protein Buffers: Hemoglobin and plasma proteins act as buffers by binding to hydrogen ions.

Respiratory System:

  • CO2 Regulation: The respiratory system maintains acid-base balance by regulating the exhalation of CO2.
  • Hyperventilation: Decreases PaCO2, leading to respiratory alkalosis.
  • Hypoventilation: Increases PaCO2, leading to respiratory acidosis.

Renal System:

  • Bicarbonate Reabsorption: The kidneys reabsorb bicarbonate to maintain buffer capacity.
  • Hydrogen Ion Excretion: Excretes hydrogen ions and generates new bicarbonate.
  • Ammonium Excretion: Ammonium ions are excreted to remove excess hydrogen ions.

Integrated Regulation: The respiratory and renal systems work together to compensate for acid-base imbalances, maintaining pH within the normal range.

3.
Discuss the metabolic functions of the liver. (20 marks)
Show Answer

Carbohydrate Metabolism:

  • Gluconeogenesis: Synthesis of glucose from non-carbohydrate sources.
  • Glycogenesis: Conversion of glucose to glycogen for storage.
  • Glycogenolysis: Breakdown of glycogen to release glucose.
  • Regulation of Blood Glucose Levels: Maintains blood glucose homeostasis.

Lipid Metabolism:

  • Synthesis of Lipoproteins: Production of VLDL, LDL, and HDL.
  • Triglyceride Storage: Stores and releases triglycerides as needed.
  • Cholesterol Synthesis and Excretion: Synthesizes and excretes cholesterol.
  • Beta-Oxidation: Breakdown of fatty acids for energy production.

Protein Metabolism:

  • Synthesis of Plasma Proteins: Produces albumin, clotting factors, and other plasma proteins.
  • Amino Acid Deamination: Removes amino groups from amino acids for energy production or excretion.
  • Urea Cycle: Converts toxic ammonia to urea for excretion.

Detoxification and Excretion:

  • Drug Metabolism: Detoxifies and metabolizes drugs and toxins.
  • Bilirubin Metabolism: Converts unconjugated bilirubin to conjugated bilirubin for excretion.
  • Excretion of Metabolic Waste: Removes metabolic waste products.

Vitamin and Mineral Storage:

  • Storage of Vitamins: Stores fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) and vitamin B12.
  • Iron Storage: Stores iron as ferritin.

Hormone Metabolism:

  • Synthesis and Degradation: Synthesizes and degrades hormones like insulin, glucagon, and sex hormones.
