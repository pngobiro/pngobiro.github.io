üß¨ Introduction to Enzymes

Enzymes are proteins produced by living cells. They are biocatalysts, i.e., they are able, in very small amounts, to hasten or modify a chemical reaction in the body without becoming involved in the reaction or affected by it. Enzymes are very useful tools in the diagnosis of diseases.

Enzymes are specific in their reaction. They act on one type of substrate and will take part in only one type of reaction or in a small range of reactions. The substrate is the substance on which the enzyme acts. For example, protease acts on protein, amylase on starch and urease on urea. Note that the names of enzymes usually end in the suffix -ase.

Different enzymes work in different ways:

  • Dehydrogenases: Catalyse oxidation-reduction process.
  • Amino transferases: Catalyse the transfer of functional amino groups from one compound to another.
  • Phosphatases: Break down a substrate by introducing water (hydrolytic).

Many enzymes work only in the presence of an activator (a simple inorganic salt or ion) or a co-enzyme (a complex organic compound).

Measurement of Enzymes

Enzymes are not measured directly because they are present in serum or plasma in very small amounts. They are therefore assayed by measuring either the amount of substrate reduced or used during the reaction or the amount of product formed during the reaction. Thus, it is in fact the enzyme activity that is measured.

Units of Enzyme Activity

In recent times, the units of enzyme activity have been, to some extent, standardised. The unit mostly used is the International Unit (IU).

üìù

Definition: International Unit (IU)

One unit of any enzyme is that amount, which under defined assay conditions, will catalyse the conversion of 1 micromole (Œºmol) of the substrate per minute. The results are expressed in International Unit (IU) per litre.

Many laboratories prefer to report enzyme activity in Katals. The Katal unit refers to the conversion of 1 mol of substrate per second.

‚öñÔ∏è Factors Affecting Enzyme Analysis

The factors that affect enzyme activity are many. Small errors in technique may result in wrong and unreliable results.

  1. 1. Temperature: Enzyme activity increases with temperature. Optimal activity of most enzymes occurs between 30¬∞C and 50¬∞C; above 60¬∞C, most enzymes are denatured. It is essential that incubation temperatures in test methods be strictly adhered to.
  2. 2. Time: Enzyme reactions proceed fairly rapidly and the substrate concentration falls with the time as the product concentration increases. Timing is therefore important as the substrate and product concentrations are changing all the time.
  3. 3. pH: The configuration of the active site of an enzyme must be in the correct ionic form for the best results. The activity of an enzyme decreases if the optimum pH is not maintained. A marked change in pH can result in the denaturation of an enzyme.
  4. 4. Co-factors: These are the non protein components of an enzyme system including co-enzymes and activators. It is necessary to control the amount of co-factors so as to avoid instability in enzyme activity.
  5. 5. Inhibitors: Many substances inhibit enzyme activity by altering the active sites (e.g., heavy metals, cyanide, some drugs). Ultraviolet light tends to inhibit enzyme activity while blue or red light tends to increase it. Samples should be kept away from direct light.
  6. 6. Substrates: The concentration of substrates determines the speed of enzyme reaction. The substrate concentration used in the test must be well defined. Michaeli‚Äôs constant is the molar concentration of the substrate that leads to a speed of reaction that is half the maximum speed.
  7. 7. Glassware: Contamination by traces of heavy metals or detergent can inhibit enzyme activity. Chemically clean glassware is essential.

Methods of Measurement

The early part of enzymatic reaction is linear but as time increases, the speed slows down because the equilibrium point is being approached.

Kinetic methods: Instruments measure the rate of product formation (or substrate utilisation) continuously.

Non-kinetic methods: Activity is manually measured at zero time and at regular intervals to determine the rate of enzyme reaction.

ü¶¥ Serum Alkaline Phosphatase (ALP)

Alkaline phosphatase is capable of transporting phosphate groups across cell membranes and is most active between pH 8 and 10. It is found in bone, liver, kidney and many other tissues. It is estimated to investigate diseases of the liver or bone, especially in cases of biliary obstruction, tumours, hydatid cyst and abscesses.

Principle

The enzyme alkaline phosphatase hydrolyses the substrate, disodium phenyl phosphate to release phenol. The quantity of phenol released under standardised conditions is measured by the absorbance of the red colour it assumes in alkaline solution. The phenol reacts with 4-aminophenazone in the presence of alkaline potassium ferricyanide to produce the red colour.

Reagents

  1. 1.Buffered substrate:
    • Disodium phenyl phosphate (0.01M)
    • Buffer: Sodium carbonate and sodium bicarbonate
    • Working buffered substrate (pH 10): Mix equal volumes of the above.
  2. 2.Sodium hydroxide 0.5 M
  3. 3.Sodium bicarbonate 0.5 M
  4. 4.4-aminophenazone 3.0 g/500 ml
  5. 5.Potassium ferricyanide 12.0 g/500 ml

Standards

Stock and working phenol standards are prepared and standardised using iodine titration.

Calculation

$$\text{KA units/100 ml} = \frac{\text{Ab (T)} - \text{Ab (B)}}{\text{Ab (S)} - \text{Ab (SB)}} \times \text{conc. std} \times \frac{100}{0.1}$$

where Ab is Absorbance, T is Test, B is Blank, S is Standard, SB is Standard Blank.

Note: 1 KA unit = 7.1 IU/litre

Normal Values

3‚Äì13 KA units/100 ml (21‚Äì92 IU/litre)

üß™ Serum Prostatic Acid Phosphatase

The two main sources of acid phosphatase are the prostate gland in men and the red cells. The enzyme is active at an acid pH between 4.8 and 6.0.

Principle

The prostatic acid phosphatase splits sodium thymolphthalein monophosphate at pH 6.0 to release free thymolphthalein. When an alkaline colour developer is added, the thymolphthalein forms a blue complex which is measured spectrophotometrically.

Method

  1. 1.Set up Test and Control tubes with buffered substrate.
  2. 2.Pre-incubate at 37¬∞C.
  3. 3.Add serum. Incubate for exactly 30 minutes.
  4. 4.Add colour developer.
  5. 5.Read absorbance at 590 nm.

Calibration Curve: A calibration curve should be prepared using diluted thymolphthalein standard to determine the enzyme activity in the test.

Clinical Significance

Elevated levels are seen in:

  • Carcinoma of the prostate
  • Acute myelocytic leukaemia
  • Metabolic bone diseases

‚ö° Lactate Dehydrogenase (LDH)

LDH is present in the cytoplasm of all tissue cells in the body. It catalyses the reversible conversion of lactic acid to pyruvic acid.

$$\text{Pyruvic acid} + \text{NADH}_2 \xrightleftharpoons{\text{LDH}} \text{Lactic acid} + \text{NAD}$$

Principle

At 340 nm wavelength (ultraviolet spectrophotometer), NAD has no absorbance whereas $\text{NADH}_2$ absorbs strongly. The rate of decrease in absorbance is measured.

Method

The reaction is kinetic. Absorbance is read at 340 nm at 1 minute intervals for 5 minutes after adding sodium pyruvate solution to the buffered serum/NADH mixture.

Calculation

\text{Units/ml} = \frac{\text{Average decrease in Abs/min}}{0.001} \times \frac{1}{0.1}

International units/litre = units/ml √ó 0.482

Normal Value: 200 ‚Äì 650 units/ml or 95‚Äì300 IU/L

üîÑ Aminotransferases (AST & ALT)

The two enzymes concerned with amino acid metabolism are Aspartate aminotransferase (AST/SGOT) and Alanine aminotransferase (ALT/SGPT).

AST (GOT) Principle

\text{Aspartate} + \text{Ketoglutarate} \leftrightarrow \text{Glutamate} + \text{Oxalacetate}

Oxaloacetate reacts with 2,4 dinitrophenylhydrazine to form a red-brown hydrazone in alkaline pH.

ALT (GPT) Principle

\text{Alanine} + \alpha\text{-ketoglutarate} \rightleftharpoons \text{Pyruvate} + \text{l-glutamate}

Pyruvate reacts with DNPH to form a red-brown hydrazone.

Method (Reitman and Frankel)

Both methods use a calibration curve prepared from a Pyruvate standard to determine enzyme activity in Units/L.

Interpretation

AST: High in acute hepatocellular damage, myocardial infarction.

ALT: Very high in liver necrosis and acute hepatitis.
