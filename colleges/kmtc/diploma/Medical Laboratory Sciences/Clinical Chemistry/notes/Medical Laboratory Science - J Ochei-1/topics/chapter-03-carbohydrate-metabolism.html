üîÑ Metabolism

Metabolism is a process made up of:

  1. 1. Catabolic reactions, in which complex substances are broken down to simpler forms with release of energy for cellular activity. e.g., hydrolysis of glycogen to glucose.
  2. 2. Anabolic reactions, in which absorbed nutrients are built up into complex compounds that the body needs for its activity. e.g. synthesis of protein from amino acids.

Metabolism is the general term that describes the different changes taking place in the body. Catabolic processes involve the production of heat and energy. The energy requirements of an individual depends on his/her age, size and metabolic rate. The metabolic rate is partly controlled by the thyroid gland hormones and is affected by temperature and a person's activity. What is referred to as Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR) is the heat and energy required to maintain cellular functions when the body is at rest.

üçû Metabolism of Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates are made up of carbon, hydrogen and oxygen. They act as source of body heat and energy. Carbohydrates are found in sugar, jam, cereals, fruits, potatoes, yams, vegetables and milk.

There are three major groups of carbohydrates. These are:

  1. 1. Monosaccharides These are simple sugars that can be absorbed directly from the intestine. Monosaccharides include the hexoses (which contain 6 carbon atoms) such as glucose, fructose and galactose; and the pentoses (which contain 5 carbon atoms) such as xylose, ribose and arabinose.
  2. 2. Disaccharides These are not directly absorbed from the intestine. On hydrolysis with specific enzymes, they yield two molecules of monosaccharides. This group includes sugars such as sucrose, lactose and maltose. Table 3.1 shows the specific enzymes acting on these sugars and the monosaccharide components.

  1. 3. Polysaccharides Many carbohydrates in nature exist in the form of polysaccharides. They are more complex and are not easily absorbed in the body. The most common polysaccharides are starch and glycogen which are hydrolysed by amylase to mainly maltose and glucose.

When carbohydrate which has been ingested is converted into glucose by various enzymes, and the glucose is absorbed from the small intestine, then the metabolism of carbohydrate in the body begins. The glucose is transported through the bloodstream to the liver where it is used or stored depending on the need of the body at that time.

There are three ways in which the glucose is either utilised or stored in the body:

1. Glycolysis

Glycolysis is the breakdown of glucose to pyruvic acid or lactic acid (or both) for the production of energy in the form of the compound adenosine triphosphate (ATP). It is used to control the level of glucose in the blood. Glucose is taken from the blood by the tissues and is converted into pyruvic acid by glycolysis. The heat and energy produced is used for cellular activity.

Glycolysis occurs through aerobic or anaerobic pathway.

Aerobic pathway
$$\text{Glucose} \rightarrow \text{Pyruvate} \rightarrow \text{Energy, Heat}$$ $$\text{CO}_2 + \text{H}_2\text{O}$$
Anaerobic pathway
$$\text{Glucose} \xrightarrow{\text{Glycolysis}} \text{Pyruvate} \xrightarrow{\text{Reduction}} \text{Lactate} + \text{Energy}$$

Anaerobic pathway occurs when the energy requirement exceeds the oxygen available. This process continues only for a limited period till enough oxygen is made available for the cellular activity.

2. Glycogenesis

The process of glycogen formation is called glycogenesis, while the term glycogenolysis describes the breakdown of glycogen. Glucose is converted to glycogen and stored in the liver and skeletal muscles when it is not needed for immediate use.

$$\text{Glucose} \xrightarrow{\text{Glycogenesis}} \text{Glycogen}$$

When glucose is required, the stored glycogen is converted back to glucose.

$$\text{Glucose} \xleftarrow{\text{Glycogenolysis}} \text{Glycogen}$$

A number of enzymes are involved in glycogenesis and glycogenolysis.

3. Conversion to fat

Excess glucose which is not immediately needed by the body and which cannot be stored as glycogen in the liver, is oxidised to fatty acids and is stored as fat in the tissues.

$$\text{Glucose} \xrightarrow{\text{Oxidation}} \text{Fatty acids} \longrightarrow \text{Fat}$$

Glucose, when needed by the body can be recovered from fat and protein. This process of re-conversion is known as gluconeogenesis.

03

üíâ Factors Determining Blood Glucose Level

  1. 1. Insulin Insulin is a hormone produced and secreted by the beta cells in the islets of Langerhans in the pancreas. It is the most important hormone that regulates the level of glucose in the blood, the rate of its absorption and its conversion to glycogen. Its secretion is stimulated by a rise in the blood glucose level and by some gastrointestinal hormones, some sugars and fatty acids. In the absence of insulin, the blood glucose level rises.
  2. 2. Glucagon Glucagon is a polypeptide hormone secreted by the alpha cells of the islets of Langerhans. It stimulates:
    • (i) Breakdown of stored glycogen
    • (ii) Liver gluconeogenesis
    • (iii) Hepatic lipolysis.
    Thus, when this hormone is secreted, the blood glucose and free fatty acid levels rise.
  3. 3. Adrenalin The hormone, adrenalin (epinephrine) is produced and secreted in the medulla of the adrenal gland. It increases glycogenolysis and inhibits the uptake of glucose in the tissues, thus resulting in rise in the blood glucose level.
  4. 4. Growth hormone Growth hormone (somatotrophic hormone) is synthesised in the anterior pituitary gland. It inhibits the uptake of glucose by tissues and increases liver glucose output. It also helps in gluconeogenesis of fat. In this way, it increases the overall blood glucose level.
  5. 5. Adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH) This hormone is produced by the anterior pituitary. Its action is similar to that of the growth hormone as it inhibits the action of insulin.
  6. 6. Corticosteroid hormones These hormones are also known as glucocorticoids and are secreted by the adrenal cortex. They inhibit the action of insulin thus blocking glucose utilisation by the tissues and promoting the use of protein instead of glucose for the production of heat and energy.
  7. 7. Thyroxine It is one of the hormones produced by the thyroid gland. It increases the absorption of glucose from the gastrointestinal tract and stimulates glycogenolysis. It promotes an increase in blood glucose level by degrading insulin.

Glucose Homeostasis in the Blood

The level of blood glucose is maintained within the fairly narrow limits by the homeostatic mechanisms described above. Following the intake of carbohydrates, all metabolic activities lead to lower blood glucose levels till it reaches the fasting level. The storage and distribution of glucose by the liver, and the use of glucose by tissues are determined by the above mentioned hormones. A simplified mechanism of glucose homeostasis is illustrated in Fig. 3.1.

When the fasting blood glucose level rises above 100 mg/dl (5.56 mmol/L), the condition is known as hyperglycaemia. A fasting blood glucose level which is less than 80 mg/dl (4.44 mmol/L), is known as hypoglycaemia. Glucose is normally filtered by the renal glomeruli and almost totally reabsorbed by the renal tubules. When, however, the blood glucose concentration rises above the range of 160 to 180 mg/dl (8.89 to 10.0 mmol/L), glucose appears in urine. This condition is characteristic of diabetes mellitus (which means "lots of urine tasting of honey"), and is referred to as glycosuria. A blood glucose level of 160 to 180 mg/dl is referred to as the renal threshold for glucose.

Blood Glucose and Blood Sugar

Glucose is the main carbohydrate in the blood, but the term "blood sugar" is generally used to include glucose, other sugars and reducing substances that may be present in the blood.

Some methods of estimation of glucose depend on the reducing properties of the sugar. These properties are shared by other sugars and reducing substances. Thus, these methods are not specific for glucose; and such estimations are expressed as Blood Sugar estimations. When the non-glucose reducing substances are removed as part of glucose determination, the resulting value is known as the true glucose value. Other methods like the oxidase-peroxidase method, confer a high degree of specificity on the measurement of glucose in blood and urine.

Glycolysis

When blood is shed, the cells do not die immediately. They continue to metabolise and use up glucose as a source of energy. Glucose, thus, disappears from whole blood on standing over a period of time. This is known as glycolysis. Glycolysis can be prevented with an enzyme inhibitor. The commonest inhibitor for this purpose is sodium fluoride which is usually used in conjunction with an anticoagulant potassium oxalate or sequestrene. Fluoride actually inhibits the enzyme enolase which is found in the metabolic pathway of glucose and has little effect on glucose oxidase and peroxidase enzymes. It also inhibits bacterial growth.

üß™ Estimation of Blood Glucose

Specimens

Blood glucose is usually measured on a protein-free filtrate of whole blood. This normally gives a value lower than that of plasma due to the presence of RBCs. However, most methods make use of plasma instead of serum or whole blood. Glucose estimation using plasma or whole blood requires the use of an anticoagulant. The fluoride-oxalate mixture used for this purpose is composed of 2 mg of sodium fluoride and 6 mg of potassium oxalate per ml of blood. More than 5 mg of sodium fluoride per ml of blood acts as an inhibitor for the enzymatic estimations.

Collection of specimen for measurements of blood glucose level should be done on the day and time requested. This is because collection times are usually related to food intake, insulin treatment, or both. It may be necessary sometimes to note the time of blood collection on the specimen container and on the patient's request form. Urgent glucose measurement becomes necessary in cases of severe hypoglycaemia or hyperglycaemia as both conditions can result in loss of consciousness (coma).

  • Fasting specimen refers to blood collected after a period of no food intake. For adults, fasting time is usually 10 to 16 hours; while for children, it is 6 hours.
  • Post-prandial specimen is blood collected usually 2 hours after a meal has been eaten.
  • Random specimen refers to blood sample collected at any time regardless of food consumption. Collection of blood samples for glucose tolerance test is described separately.

Four methods are described in this book for the estimation of sugar/glucose levels in the blood. They are: 1. Oxidase-peroxidase method; 2. Reduction of cupric to cuprous salt (Folin-Wu method); 3. Reflometric method, and 4. O-Toluidine method.

1. Oxidase-peroxidase Method

Principle

Glucose oxidase catalyses the oxidation of glucose to produce hydrogen peroxide and gluconic acid. The hydrogen peroxide, in the presence of enzyme peroxidase is broken down and the oxygen given off reacts with 4-aminophenazone and phenol to give a pink colour.

$$\text{Glucose} + \text{Oxygen} \xrightarrow{\text{Glucose oxidase}} \text{Hydrogen peroxide} + \text{Gluconic acid}$$ $$\text{Hydrogen peroxide} + \text{Aminophenazone} + \text{Phenol} \xrightarrow{\text{Peroxidase}} \text{Pink coloured complex}$$

Add 125 ml of 1.0 M HCl to bring the pH to 3.0. Add 1 g phenol and dilute to 1 litre with distilled water. This solution has very long shelf-life at 4¬∞C.
  2. (ii) Phosphate buffer, pH 7.0. Dissolve 8.52 g of disodium hydrogen phosphate and 5.44 g of potassium dihydrogen phosphate in 500 ml of distilled water. Adjust the pH and make up to 1 litre.
  3. (iii) Colour reagent 300 ml of phosphate buffer, add 5 ml of Permcozyme 952 DM (glucose oxidase and peroxidase), 0.3 g phenol, 0.3 g sodium azide and 0.1 g 4-aminophenazole. Mix and store at 4¬∞C. It is stable for at least 2 months.
  4. (iv) Standard glucose solution (10 mmol/L or 180 mg/dl): Dissolve 1.80 g of glucose in, and make up to, 1 litre with saturated (0.3 %) benzoic acid. Prepare the benzoic acid at least 24 hours before use.

2. Reduction of Cupric to Cuprous Salt (Folin-Wu Method)

This is one of the oldest methods for the estimation of blood sugar. However, it is still very much in use in countries where enzyme preparations are not easy to obtain.

Principle

The method is based on three stages:

  1. (i) Precipitation of blood proteins with copper tungstate.
  2. (ii) Reduction of cupric sulphate to cuprous oxide.
  3. (iii) Colorimetric measurement of the subsequent blue-green colour produced on the addition of molybdate reagent to the cuprous oxide.

Reagents

  1. (i) Sodium tungstate (10 %) Dissolve 10 g per 100 ml distilled water.
  2. (ii) Sulphuric acid, 2/3 N Add 2 ml of conc. sulphuric acid to about 50 ml of distilled water and dilute up to 100 ml with distilled water.
  3. (iii) Alkaline copper tartrate reagent Dissolve 40 g sodium carbonate and 7.5 g tartaric acid in about 400 ml of distilled water. Separately dissolve 4.5 g copper sulphate in about 100 ml of distilled water. Mix both the solutions together and make the volume up to 1 litre with distilled water. Filter before use.
  4. (iv) Phosphomolybdic acid reagent Dissolve 35 g molybdic acid and 5 g sodium tungstate in 200 ml of 10 % sodium hydroxide. Add it to 200 ml of distilled water in one litre beaker. Boil for 45 minutes to remove ammonia, cool and add slowly 125 ml of 89 % phosphoric acid (specific gravity 1.75). Make up the volume to 500 ml with distilled water.

3. Reflometric Method

This is a simple enzyme method in which oxidase-peroxidase enzyme and the colour reagent are impregnated on the reagent strips. The method uses a Reflocheck meter to register the amount of glucose in the blood.

Technique A drop of capillary blood is placed on the test strip which after the prescribed time interval is wiped and inserted into the instrument. The colour change is read in a reflectance meter on which the result in mg/dl is visualised.

There are many instruments based on the same principle which are used mainly by diabetic patients.

Advantages of Reflometric Methods

  1. (i) They are simple and reliable.
  2. (ii) They have excellent correlation with reference methods.
  3. (iii) They give good precision and accuracy.
  4. (iv) The meter is automatically calibrated by the bar code on each test strip.
  5. (v) They are battery or mains operated.
  6. (vi) They can measure values as high as 450 mg/dl.

4. O-Toluidine Method

This method makes use of the principle that glucose reacts specifically with O-toluidine, an aromatic amine, in glacial acetic acid at 100¬∞C to produce a blue-green colour. This colour can be measured colorimetrically. The method has the additional advantage that it can be performed directly on serum, plasma, cerebrospinal fluid or urine without deproteinization. The O-toluidine method is no longer widely used today because O-toluidine is believed to be a carcinogen.

üè• Diabetes Mellitus

When glycosuria is detected and hyperglycaemia is established, a chronic metabolic disorder known as diabetes mellitus is indicated. It is a condition which is characterised by disturbances in carbohydrate, lipid and protein metabolism. Hyperglycaemia and glycosuria indicate the major metabolic lesion in carbohydrate metabolism, accompanied by metabolic disturbances in protein (gluconeogenesis) and lipids (ketosis and hypercholesterolaemia).

The presence of hyperglycaemia and renal glycosuria with an osmotic diuresis (polyuria) eventually will lead to dehydration and its associated polydipsia (increased thirst). Hyperglycaemia is sustained by the processes of glycogenolysis and gluconeogenesis which enhance the production of more glucose.

Formation of ketone bodies

Acetoacetic acid, beta-hydroxybutyric acid and acetone are collectively known as ketone bodies or ketones. When any of these substances is detected in blood, the condition is referred to as ketoacidosis or ketonaemia; and when found in urine, it is called ketonuria.

When the intake of glucose is insufficient, as in starvation, or when glucose metabolism is impaired due to lack of insulin as in the case of untreated or uncontrolled diabetes, the body obtains its energy by breaking down fats to fatty acids. This increase in fat metabolism leads to the production of acetyl coenzyme A. The two carbon fragments in this enzyme condense to form the ketone bodies. Ketoacidosis is a potentially fatal complication of diabetes mellitus.

Types of Diabetes Mellitus

There are two types of the conditions known as primary diabetes:

  1. 1. Maturity-onset (adult type)
  2. 2. Growth onset (juvenile or child diabetes)

The course of diabetes mellitus runs in four stages:

  1. 1. Prediabetes ‚Äî The prediabetic stage is the period from birth until the first evidence of diabetes emerges.
  2. 2. Suspected diabetes ‚Äî When an individual displays abnormal glucose tolerance test (GTT) result, or shows symptoms of diabetes under stressful conditions such as obesity, pregnancy, trauma or infections, the condition is referred to as suspected diabetes.
  3. 3. Chemical or latent diabetes ‚Äî When a patient who is not under any stress and with no signs or symptoms of disease, displays an abnormal GTT result, or fasting hyperglycaemia, he/she is said to have chemical or latent diabetes.
  4. 4. Overt diabetes With overt diabetes, symptoms of polyuria, polydipsia and weight loss are often linked to fasting hyperglycaemia and glycosuria.

Modern Classification of Diabetes Mellitus

National Diabetes Data Group has proposed a classification for hyperglycaemic disorders which is outlined below:

1. Idiopathic diabetes mellitus

(a) Insulin dependent diabetes mellitus (IDDM Type I)

The individuals in this category produce very little insulin. The patient is acutely ill with severe hyperglycaemia at the time of diagnosis. Satisfactory control of blood sugar is obtained with insulin treatment. About 10 per cent of diabetes mellitus cases belong to this category.

(b) Non-insulin dependent diabetes mellitus (NIDDM)

This is the most common form of diabetes mellitus (80‚Äì90%). Most patients with this form of diabetes are relatively deficient in insulin and have mild hyperglycaemia. Obesity is commonly associated with NIDDM and decrease in obesity brings about reduction in hyperglycaemia in most cases. Some persons with NIDDM may be non-obese. A small percentage of NIDDM patients may require insulin injections or an oral hypoglycaemia agent to control hyperglycaemia.

  1. 2. Gestational diabetes mellitus (GDM) In these cases, glucose intolerance is discovered for the first time during pregnancy. Such a woman is at high risk for foetal morbidity and should be promptly treated.
  2. 3. Impaired glucose tolerance (IGT) Persons with IGT are borderline cases with slightly raised glucose levels without overt hyperglycaemia. Such cases may later develop diabetes mellitus or revert to normal levels. Generally, they are at high risk of development of cardiovascular disease.
  3. 4. Previous abnormality of glucose tolerance (Prev. AGT) Persons in this category have a history of diabetes but have reverted to normal levels. e.g. those with GDM or obese NIDDM.
  4. 5. Potential abnormality of glucose tolerance (Pot. AGT) Persons at a more than average risk of developing diabetes belong to this category. e.g., child of parents with NIDDM.
  5. 6. Glucose intolerance associated with other disorders Hyperglycaemia due to underlying disorders such as chronic pancreatitis, Cushing‚Äôs disease, and acromegaly are included in this category.

ü©∫ Screening Tests for Diabetes Mellitus

The screening tests for diabetes include urine and blood glucose examinations.

  1. 1. Urine glucose In determining the presence of glucose in urine, it should be remembered that ‚Äútrue glucose‚Äù must exceed 10 mmol/L (180 mg/dl) before glucose can pass into the urine (Renal threshold).

    The renal threshold is, however, elevated in diabetic kidney. Also, the renal threshold increases with age. In some elderly patients no glycosuria may be present even with serum glucose level as high as 11.1 mmol/L (200 mg/dl).

    Methods of screening for urine glucose are discussed later in the next chapter.

  2. 2. Fasting blood sugar It is preferable to use plasma for this test. Glucose levels in excess of 6.6 mmol/L (120 mg/dl) is suggestive of diabetes mellitus; and values between 6.0 to 6.6 mmol (110 to 120 mg/dl) are doubtful and should be confirmed by performing the glucose tolerance test. It is important to remember that conditions such as emotional hyperglycaemia resulting from secretion of epinephrine; general brain damage such as skull fractures, tumours, vascular accidents, encephalitis as well as carbon monoxide poisoning can provoke hyperglycaemia and glycosuria.
  3. 3. Two-hour post-prandial (pp) blood glucose

    Two hours after the patient has broken fast by being fed on adequate carbohydrate meal, a single blood sample is collected and analysed. Glucose level in excess of 10 mmol/L (180 mg/dl) is highly suggestive of diabetes mellitus and should be confirmed with a GTT. A single two-hour pp glucose level has the limitations due to

    • (i) Delayed peak level due to slow absorption
    • (ii) Early hyperglycaemia due to rapid absorption
    • (iii) Quick fall in concentration of blood glucose level due to the release of insulin.
    • (iv) Wrong specimen collection timing.
  4. 4. Glucose Tolerance Test (GTT)

    This is also usually referred to as oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT). Glucose tolerance test (GTT) measures the ability of the body to tolerate a measured dose of glucose. The rate of glucose absorption and response of insulin mainly determines the extent of glucose tolerance as shown by the changes in the level of blood glucose. When there is rise in blood glucose, insulin is released from the pancreas to lower the glucose level. Tolerance is reduced when insulin is absent or insufficient. A rise in blood glucose value usually occurs after having a meal containing carbohydrates. Therefore, the response to carbohydrate level depends on the previous carbohydrate diet and on the amount ingested. The effect of ingested carbohydrate is the basis of the glucose tolerance test. It simply detects abnormalities in the carbohydrate metabolism and in cases where glycosuria has been found.

    The oral glucose tolerance test is performed in order to screen

    • (i) Patients having glycosuria with no apparent symptoms of diabetes and with normal fasting and post-prandial blood glucose levels.
    • (ii) Patients showing symptoms of diabetes with no glycosuria and normal fasting level of blood glucose.
    • (iii) Persons with family history of diabetes but with no overt diabetes.
    • (iv) Patients having glycosuria in conjunction with pregnancy, thyrotoxicosis, liver disease or infection.
    • (v) Patients showing evidence of neuropathies and retinopathies of unconfirmed origin.

Preparing the Patient for GTT

  1. (i) Place the patient on a diet containing not less than 150 g of carbohydrate for about three days before the test.
  2. (ii) The patient should not eat anything after supper and no breakfast is given on the day of the test. No drink, except ordinary plain water should be given. For children, the time the food is denied is as follows:
    • (a) Infants less than 4 months = 4 hours fast
    • (b) Infants age 4 to 6 months = 6 hours fast
    • (c) Children aged 6 to 24 months = 8 hours fast.
  3. (iii) Patient should not be subjected to excessive exercise like walking long distance, excitement or fear, because all these can lead to a reduction in glucose tolerance.
  4. (iv) The test is preferably done in the morning and ideally, when the patient is not suffering from any other disease.

Glucose tolerance test method (WHO Reference method)

  1. (i) Collect a fasting sample for blood glucose and collect a urine sample from the patient.
  2. (ii) Give the patient 75 g glucose dissolved in 250 to 350 ml of water to drink. For children 1.75 g per kg body weight to a maximum of 75 g is given. Alternatively, a 105 ml of lemon flavoured glucose syrup called Hycal (Beecham research laboratories), diluted to 250 to 350 ml with water, can be given. This dose applies to adults only. For children, the pharmacy will supply the appropriate dose on request.
  3. (iii) After one hour, take a sample for blood glucose and also collect a urine sample.
  4. (iv) After 2 hours take a further sample for blood glucose and collect the final urine sample.
  5. (v) Estimate the blood glucose levels of each specimen, and test the urines for the presence of glucose.

Results

Normal response: The fasting blood glucose should be less than 5.5 mmol/L and the two hour level less than 7.0 mmol/L.

Diabetic response: The fasting levels of glucose should be greater than 7.0 mmol/L and the two hour level should be greater than 10.0 mmol/L.
