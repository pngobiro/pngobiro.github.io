üß¨ Anatomy and Physiology

The alimentary tract extends from the mouth to the anus, and comprises the mouth, pharynx, oesophagus, stomach, small intestine and large intestine including the rectum and anus. The tract is a convoluted tube in which food is ingested, digested, absorbed and excreted. Secretion from organs such as salivary glands, pancreas, liver and biliary tract are poured into the alimentary tract through various ducts.

The stomach contains three important types of cells:

  1. 1. Peptic cells: These produce the enzyme pepsin which digests proteins.
  2. 2. Parietal (Oxyntic) cells: These produce hydrochloric acid and intrinsic factor.
  3. 3. Goblet cells: These secrete mucus which protects stomach wall and lubricates the food.

Digestion of food takes place in the stomach; and gastric juice is secreted by the cells lining the walls of the stomach. The secretion is prompted by

  • (i) the hormone gastrin which is secreted by the gastric antrum mucosa when food is in the stomach;
  • (ii) sensory factors (sight, taste or smell of food); and
  • (iii) the presence of some by-products of digestion.

‚öóÔ∏è Examination of Gastric Juice

Examination of gastric juice involves four steps:

  1. I. Stimulation
  2. II. Collection
  3. III. Gross examination
  4. IV. Titration of acidity

I. Stimulated Gastric Juice

The gastric juice is stimulated by anything that enters the stomach, but some foods tend to stimulate the secretion of the juice more than the others. The following are well recognised gastric juice stimulants.

  1. One pint (568 ml) of oatmeal gruel.
  2. Tea and toasted bread.
  3. About 100 ml of 7% alcohol with methylene blue as indicator.

Direct stimulation method

Some stimulants directly stimulate the parietal cells in the stomach. Histamine acts directly on the parietal cells and stimulates the secretion of gastric acid. It however has unpleasant side effects. It is administered with anti-histamine.

Pentagastrin is a synthetic drug which resembles the active part of gastric hormone gastrin and mimics its action. It is used in the same way as histamine but in smaller amounts. It is expensive but more acceptable than histamine. Pentagastrin has virtually replaced histamine.

Augmented histamine test (AHT): A modification of the histamine test is the augmented histamine test. In this test, higher doses of histamine are given (about 0.04 mg per kg body weight). All parietal cells capable of acid secretion are stimulated by histamine but this test helps to assess the total functioning parietal cell mass. Even though the AHT and its analogous histology are well established definitive tests, the pentagastrin test has gradually superseded them due to the adverse side effects of AHT. The pentagastrin test and AHT are carried out in similar manner but 6 ¬µg per kg body weight of pentagastrin is administered intramuscularly.

II. Collection of Gastric Juice

The collection of gastric juice is usually a ward procedure.

  1. 1.The patient must be in the fasting state and free from sight or odour of food overnight.
  2. 2.All medications that can influence gastric secretion must be withheld for 24 hours.
  3. 3.The patient must be isolated from environmental situation evoking untoward psychological responses such as fear, anger or depression.
  4. 4.Pass a number 14 Ryle‚Äôs tube through the nose until it is lying on the pyloric antrum. The markings on the tube show how far the tube has been pushed down.
  5. 5.All the gastric juice is aspirated from the stomach. This specimen is the ‚Äúresting juice‚Äù.
  6. 6.In next 1 hour the juice is collected at 15 minutes interval and the sample is called ‚Äúbasal juice‚Äù.
  7. 7.The patient is injected intramuscularly with 6 ¬µg per kg body weight of pentagastrin. This gives maximal secretion.
  8. 8.The gastric juice is aspirated for four consecutive 15 minute periods and the samples are labelled 1, 2, 3, 4.

The laboratory should receive the following samples:

  • Resting juice
  • Basal juice
  • Post pentagastrin samples 1, 2, 3, 4.

IV. Titration of Acidity in Gastric Juice

Reagents

  1. NaOH, 0.02 N: Dilute 20.0 ml stock solution of 0.1 mol/l of NaOH to 100 ml with distilled water. This gives 0.02 N NaOH. Standardise. Prepared fresh each day.
  2. Indicator: Neutral red (0.1% in 70% alcohol) or Phenol red.

Method

  1. Spin the gastric juice.
  2. Transfer 1 ml of the clear gastric juice into a small flask, add 2‚Äì3 drops of the indicator.
  3. Titrate each sample with the standardised 0.02 N NaOH until a faint pink endpoint is reached.
  4. Note the titre and record the result.

Calculations

1.0 ml of 0.02 N NaOH is equivalent to 20 mmol/L.

\text{Titrable acidity (mmol/L)} = \frac{t \times 20}{v}

Where:
$t$ = titre of gastric juice
$v$ = volume of sample used for the test

Example: $t = 4.4$, $v = 1.0 \text{ ml}$.

\text{Titrable acidity} = \frac{4.4 \times 20}{1.0} = 88.0 \, \text{mmol/L}

Clinical Significance

  1. 1.Hyperchlorhydria (Hypersecretion): Indicative of peptic and duodenal ulcers (basal > 5 mmol/L, maximal 20‚Äì60 mmol/L). Zollinger-Ellison syndrome (basal > 10 mmol/L).
  2. 2.Hypochlorhydria/Achlorhydria (Hyposecretion): Associated with pernicious anaemia, gastric carcinoma and chronic gastritis.

Insulin (Hypoglycaemia) Test

Acid secretion is stimulated by hypoglycaemia induced by insulin administration due to stimulation of vagus nerve. The test is performed to determine the efficiency of vagotomy. A successful vagotomy show no rise in acidity after a sufficient dose of insulin.

ü©∏ Occult Blood in Faeces

Blood in faeces may be as a result of bleeding into the gastrointestinal tract. When the bleeding is chronic, the blood becomes non-recognisable in the faeces and it is referred to as occult (hidden) blood. Occult blood is investigated in order to diagnose iron deficiency anaemia, peptic ulceration or cancer of the gastrointestinal tract.

Tests for Occult Blood

The principle of chemical tests is based on the fact that haemoglobin and its derivatives react in a similar way to peroxidase enzymes‚Äîby catalysing the transfer of an oxygen atom from a peroxide to a chromogen such as benzidine, o-toluidine, guaiac or aminophenazone. Oxidation of the chromogen produces a blue, blue-green or pink colour.

‚ò¢Ô∏è

Safety Warning

Two chromogens, benzidine and o-toluidine which give satisfactory results, have been found to be carcinogenic and so their use is no longer justified.

1. Okokit tablet test

Supplied along with the kit are Diluent, Okokit tablet and Test papers.

Method:

  1. Prepare a thin smear of the faeces in the centre of the test paper.
  2. One okokit tablet is placed in the centre of the smear.
  3. Two or three drops of diluent are applied on the tablet. Two more drops added after 2 minutes.
  4. Read after 5 minutes.

Results: Negative (No colour), Positive (Blue colour).

2. Haemoccult method (Slide and tape test)

This method uses filter paper envelopes impregnated with guaiac resin.

Reports:

  • Trace: Faint blue-green in 1 minute
  • 1+: Light blue-slowly
  • 2+: Clear blue-rapidly
  • 3+: Deep blue-almost immediately

Benzidine Test

Produces green or blue colour in presence of blood. Note: Benzidine is a carcinogen and should be handled with care.

üí© Faecal Fat

Disorders of fat absorption cause excessive quantity of fat to be excreted in the faeces. This condition is referred to as steatorrhoea. Steatorrhoeal stools are usually bulky, pale in colour, float on water, are frothy and have an offensive odour.

Microscopic Examination

Excess fat in faeces may be in the form of neutral fat globules, fatty acid crystals or soapy flakes.

  • Neutral fat globules: Indicative of lipase deficiency due to pancreatic disease.
  • Fatty acid crystals/soaps: Suggestive of malabsorption (split fats not yet absorbed).

Chemical Estimation

Involves homogenising a 3-5 day collection, saponification with alcoholic KOH, acidification to release fatty acids, extraction into organic solvent, and titration. Note: Because of the messy and unsafe nature of the test, most laboratories no longer measure faecal fats.

üß© Pancreatic Function

The pancreas performs a dual role:

  1. Exocrine: Secretes pancreatic juice containing enzymes (Trypsin, Lipase, Diastase/Amylase) into the duodenum.
  2. Endocrine: Islets of Langerhans secrete Insulin ($\beta$-cells) and Glucagon ($\alpha$-cells).

Pancreatic Juice

Stimulated by secretin. Characteristics: Volume 450‚Äì800 ml/24h, Sp.gr 1.007, pH 7.0‚Äì8.3 (Alkaline).

Digestive Enzymes

  • Proteolytic: Trypsin, Chymotrypsin, Collagenase, Elastase.
  • Peptidases: Carboxypeptidase, Aminopeptidase.
  • Nucleases: Ribonuclease, Deoxyribonuclease.
  • Amylolytic: Amylase (hydrolyses starches).
  • Lipolytic: Lipase (hydrolyses fat), Lecithinase.

Acute Pancreatitis

Acute inflammation causes release of amylase into circulation. Serum amylase activity increases within hours. Values >5 times normal are suggestive. Urine amylase also rises.

üß™ Estimation of Serum/Plasma Amylase

Caraway Method

Principle: A small quantity of plasma or serum is incubated at $37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ for 7.5 minutes with a solution containing $0.4\mathrm{mg}$ of starch. The disappearance of blue colour that starch gives with iodine solution is the measure of the extent to which the starch has been hydrolysed by the amylase.

Reagents

  1. Buffered starch substrate (pH 7.0): Disodium hydrogen phosphate + potassium dihydrogen phosphate + 0.2g soluble starch.
  2. Stock iodine solution (0.1 N)
  3. Working iodine solution (0.01 N)

Method

Set up Test and Blank tubes. Incubate serum with buffered substrate for exactly 7.5 minutes. Add iodine solution. Read absorbance at 660 nm (red filter).

Calculation

\text{Serum amylase (Caraway units)} = \frac{\text{Ab Blank} - \text{Ab Test}}{\text{Ab Blank}} \times 400

Amylase International Units/Litre (IU/L) = Caraway units √ó 1.85

Normal values: 60‚Äì160 Caraway units (100‚Äì340 IU/L).

üß¨ Fibrocystic Disease (Cystic Fibrosis)

This is a familial Mendelian recessive disease characterised by abnormally high secretion of sodium and chloride by the various exocrine glands of the body.

Laboratory diagnosis

The principle is to demonstrate increased sodium and chloride in the sweat of patients.

Collection Methods:

  1. 1.Methacholine chloride: (Obsolete).
  2. 2.Pilocarpine iontophoresis: A current is passed between electrodes containing pilocarpine nitrate (positive) and sodium nitrate (negative) to stimulate sweat. Sweat is collected on filter paper and electrolytes are eluted and measured.
  3. 3.Wescar sweat collection system: Uses a heated cup to reduce evaporation errors.
