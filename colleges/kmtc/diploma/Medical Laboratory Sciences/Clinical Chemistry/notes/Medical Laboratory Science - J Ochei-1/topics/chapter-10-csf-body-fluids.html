üß† Cerebrospinal Fluid (CSF)

The investigation of clinical disorder of the central nervous system (CNS) is partly based on the examination of cerebrospinal fluid (CSF). The central nervous system consists of the brain, spinal cord and peripheral nerves.

The brain and the spinal cord are covered by three membranes collectively called the meninges:

  1. 1.Dura mater: An outer protective membrane.
  2. 2.Arachnoid mater: A middle membrane.
  3. 3.Pia mater: An inner membrane.

The arachnoid mater and the pia mater are separated from each other by the subarachnoid space which is filled with the cerebrospinal fluid.

Formation and Functions

CSF is formed in the choroid plexuses within the lateral ventricles. It flows through the ventricles and into the subarachnoid space to completely surround the brain and spinal cord.

Functions of CSF:

  • Provides a "fluid cushion" and acts as a shock absorber.
  • Carries nutrients and removes waste products.
  • Maintains constant pressure inside the head.
  • Keeps the brain and spinal cord moist.

üíâ Collection of CSF

A specimen of CSF is collected by a lumbar puncture, introducing a needle between the third and fourth lumbar vertebrae into the spinal sub-arachnoid space. The CSF is usually collected into 2 or 3 sterile containers, numbered according to the order of collection, each containing about 1‚Äì3ml.

Possible Complications

  1. Production of cerebellar pressure cone in patients with increased intracranial pressure.
  2. Paresis progression to paralysis in spinal cord tumors.
  3. Introduction of infection due to unsterile equipment or poor technique.
  4. After-puncture headache due to leakage of CSF.
  5. Infantile death due to asphyxiation from restraint.

Indications for Examination

  • Suspected meningitis (bacterial, viral, fungal, tubercular)
  • Subarachnoid haemorrhage
  • Encephalitis
  • Central nervous system syphilis
  • Spinal cord tumour or multiple sclerosis

üìä Composition of CSF

The CSF is mainly composed of water, dissolved oxygen and solids. The average volume is between 100 ml and 150 ml. It is similar to plasma but concentrations of sodium, chloride, magnesium and glutamine are higher, while glucose, potassium, calcium, cholesterol and protein are lower.

üî¨ Routine Laboratory Examination

1. Macroscopic (Gross) Examination

Appearance
Normal CSF is clear and colourless. Evaluate against distilled water.
Turbidity
Associated with pyogenic meningitis (Neisseria, Streptococcus, Haemophilus). Due to large numbers of pus cells or bacteria.
Xanthochromia
Associated with breakdown of haemoglobin (old hemorrhage), cerebral tumour, jaundice, or very high protein levels.
  • Subarachnoid bleeding xanthochromia appears in 2‚Äì12 hours.
  • Traumatic tap: First tube bloody, subsequent tubes clearer.
  • Pathological hemorrhage: All tubes equally bloody.
Clot
Indicates elevated protein/fibrinogen. Web-like clot ("spider web") typical of Tuberculous meningitis.

2. Cell Count

Normal: 0‚Äì5 lymphocytes/¬µl. No red cells.

  • Predominant Polymorphs: Pyogenic bacterial infection.
  • Predominant Mononuclear cells: Viral, tubercular, or fungal infection.

Calculation (Neubauer Chamber)

Dilute CSF 1:20 (optional if clear). Count cells in 4 large squares ($4 \text{ mm}^2$, depth $0.1 \text{ mm}$).

\text{White cell count} = \frac{N \times \text{Dilution Factor}}{\text{Area} \times \text{Depth}}

For neat sample (no dilution):

\text{Count} = \frac{N \times 10}{4} = N \times 2.5 / \text{cu.mm}

Where $N$ is the number of cells counted.

3. Chemical Examination

Proteins (Turbidimetric Methods)

Principle: Proteins precipitated by trichloroacetic acid (TCA) or sulphosalicylic acid. Turbidity measured colorimetrically.

TCA Method Calculation

\text{g CSF protein/L} = \frac{\text{Absorbance Test}}{\text{Absorbance Standard}} \times 0.5

(Based on 0.5 g/L standard).

Globulin Tests

  • Pandy's Test: Saturated phenol solution. Turbidity indicates increased globulin.
  • Nonne-Apelt's Test: Saturated ammonium sulphate. Ring precipitin test.

Glucose

Estimated same as blood glucose. Clinical Significance:

Tuberculous meningitisModerate reduction
Fungal meningitisMarked reduction

Note: Analysis must be prompt to prevent glycolysis.

ü¶µ Synovial Fluid

Found in large joints (knee, ankle, hip). Used to investigate arthritis and joint infections.

Viscosity (Mucin Clot Test)

Principle: Polymerisation of hyaluronidase. Add fluid to 5% acetic acid.

  • Good clot: Normal.
  • Poor/Friable clot: Inflammatory condition (Rheumatoid arthritis, septic arthritis).

Microscopy

  • Crystals:
    • Monosodium Urate (MSU): Needle/rod shaped. Indicates Gout.
    • Calcium Pyrophosphate Dihydrate (CPPD): Rhomboid/rod shaped. Indicates Pseudogout.
06

ü´Å Pleural Fluid

Fluid separating the lung pleurae. Increase is called Pleural Effusion.

Appearance

  • Bloody (Haemorrhagic): Malignancy, pulmonary infarction, trauma, tuberculosis.
  • Turbid/Cloudy: Septic inflammation (high white cell count).
  • Milky: Chylous effusion (lymphatic obstruction).

Biochemistry

  • Glucose: Low in bacterial infection, malignancy.
  • Protein: Differentiates exudates (high protein, inflammation) from transudates (low protein, congestive heart failure).
07

üíß Peritoneal Fluid

Fluid from the abdominal cavity. Examined in Ascites.

Biochemical Markers

  • Amylase: High in acute pancreatitis.
  • Low pH: Suggestive of perforated peptic ulcer.
  • White cells: High neutrophils indicate acute peritonitis; high lymphocytes suggest tuberculous peritonitis.

Urine Contamination

Accidental aspiration of the bladder can be ruled out by measuring urea and creatinine. High fluid levels compared to serum suggest urine aspiration.
