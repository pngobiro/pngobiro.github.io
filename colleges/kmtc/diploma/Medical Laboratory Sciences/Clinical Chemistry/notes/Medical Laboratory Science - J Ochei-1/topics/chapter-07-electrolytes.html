üîã Introduction to Electrolytes

Electrolytes are compounds which when dissolved or melted, will conduct electricity. They form positively charged ions (cations) and negatively charged ions (anions). Electrolytes help to maintain the stability of body fluids. A balanced proportion of cations and anions is essential for the maintenance of electrical neutrality in the body.

Major Cations (+)

  • Sodium ($\mathrm{Na^{+}}$)
  • Potassium ($\mathrm{K^{+}}$)
  • Magnesium ($\mathrm{Mg^{2+}}$)
  • Calcium ($\mathrm{Ca^{2+}}$)

Major Anions (-)

  • Chloride ($\mathrm{Cl^{-}}$)
  • Bicarbonate ($\mathrm{HCO_{3}^{-}}$)
  • Phosphate ($\mathrm{PO_{4}^{3-}}$)
  • Sulphate ($\mathrm{SO_{4}^{2-}}$)
02

üíß Water Balance

The total volume of fluid in a normal adult is roughly 42 litres, 60% of which is water while the total volume of fluid in a normal infant is about 2 litres, and 70% of which is water.

Fluid Compartments

  • Intracellular fluid: Fluid found inside the cells. It makes up about 28 litres in an adult. Main electrolytes: Potassium, Magnesium, Phosphate, Protein.
  • Extracellular fluid: Fluid found in spaces between cells.
    • Intravascular (Plasma): Fluid part of blood. High $\mathrm{Na^{+}}$, Low $\mathrm{K^{+}}$. High Chloride.
    • Interstitial/Other: Lymph, CSF, etc.

ü´Å Blood Gases and Acid-Base Balance

The pH of the extracellular fluid should be kept within the narrow limits of pH 7.37 to 7.45. Carbonic acid ($\mathrm{H_2CO_3}$) influences the acid-base balance because it is abundantly produced from metabolic activity. The equilibrium is maintained by the bicarbonate buffering system:

$$\mathrm{H}^{+} + \mathrm{HCO}_{3}^{-} \rightleftharpoons \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{CO}_{3} \rightleftharpoons \mathrm{H}_{2}\mathrm{O} + \mathrm{CO}_{2}$$

Henderson-Hasselbalch Equation

The relationship between pH, bicarbonate, and $\mathrm{PCO_2}$ is described by:

\mathrm{pH} = \mathrm{pK}_{1} + \log \frac{(\mathrm{HCO}_{3}^{-})}{0.0301 \times \mathrm{PCO}_{2}}

Where:

  • $\mathrm{pK}_{1} = 6.1$ for plasma
  • $0.0301$ is the $\mathrm{CO_2}$ solubility constant
  • $\mathrm{PCO_2}$ is expressed in mmHg

üî• Measurement of Sodium and Potassium

Flame Emission Spectrometry (Flame Photometry) is the most popular method for routine analysis. Other methods include Ion Selective Electrodes (ISE) and solid phase chemistry.

Flame Photometer Principle

Using compressed air, diluted serum or plasma is sprayed as fine droplets into a non-luminous gas flame. The flame becomes coloured by the characteristic emission of the metallic ions:

  • Sodium: Yellow flame
  • Potassium: Violet flame
  • Lithium (Internal Standard): Red flame

Light from the flame is measured at appropriate wavelengths by a photodetector. The use of an internal lithium standard compensates for fluctuations in flame conditions, atomization rate, and viscosity.

Sample Handling

A blood sample left to stand for more than 4 hours prior to plasma or serum separation is unsuitable for estimation of electrolytes due to leakage of electrolytes, especially potassium, from red cells into the plasma.

Normal Values

Serum Sodium: 135‚Äì146 mmol/L

Serum Potassium: 3.5‚Äì5.2 mmol/L

Clinical Significance

Hyponatraemia: Low sodium. Causes: Salt-losing nephritis, diarrhoea, vomiting, burns, diuretic drugs.

Hypernatraemia: High sodium. Causes: Dehydration, excessive sodium intake, Conn's syndrome.

Hypokalaemia: Low potassium. Causes: Chronic diarrhea, renal tubular acidosis, insulin therapy.

Hyperkalaemia: High potassium. Causes: Renal failure, ketoacidosis, hemolysis (artifact).

üßÇ Serum/Plasma Chloride

Mercuric Nitrate Method

Principle: This method involves titrating $\mathrm{Cl^{-}}$ ions with $\mathrm{Hg^{2+}}$ ions to form soluble but non-ionised mercuric chloride. The endpoint is reached when excess $\mathrm{Hg^{2+}}$ forms a complex with the indicator diphenylcarbazone producing a pale violet colour.

\frac{\text{Titration of test}}{\text{Titration of standard}} \times 100 = \text{mmol/litre of serum chloride}

Chloride Meter (Coulometry)

Principle: Silver ions are generated from a silver anode by a constant electric current. The silver ions react with chloride in the sample to form insoluble silver chloride. When all chloride is consumed, the appearance of free silver ions increases conductivity, shutting off the timer. The time elapsed is proportional to chloride concentration.

Anion Gap

The anion gap is a calculation used to detect unmeasured anions (like ketones, lactate, proteins).

(\text{Na}^+ + \text{K}^+) - (\text{Cl}^- + \text{HCO}_3^-) = \text{Anion Gap}

Normal range: 10-20 mmol/L (approx. 17 mmol/L). An anion gap > 17 mmol/L usually indicates metabolic acidosis (ketoacidosis, lactic acidosis, renal failure).

06

üß™ Bicarbonate Estimation

Principle: When serum is mixed with acid ($\mathrm{0.01N\ HCl}$), there is a loss of acidity due to the reaction with bicarbonate. This decrease in acidity is determined by back-titrating with alkali ($\mathrm{0.01N\ NaOH}$) using phenol red indicator.

\text{Serum Bicarbonate (mmol/L)} = (1 - \text{ml of NaOH}) \times 100

This titration method estimates "Alkali Reserve" or Total $\mathrm{CO_2}$.

Methylthymol Blue Method

Principle: Calcium ions produce a deep blue complex with methylthymol blue in alkaline solution. Magnesium interference is masked by adding 8-hydroxyquinoline.

Method

  1. 1.Mix serum with working reagent (dye + base).
  2. 2.Read absorbance at 612 nm (red filter).
\text{Calcium (mmol/L)} = \frac{\text{Ab Test}}{\text{Ab Std}} \times \text{Conc. of Std}

‚öõÔ∏è Inorganic Phosphorus

Principle: Phosphate reacts with ammonium molybdate in acid medium to form phosphomolybdic acid. This is reduced by a reducing agent (e.g., Metol) to form a blue complex (Molybdenum Blue) which is measured colorimetrically at 680 nm.

Serum should be separated rapidly. Hemolysis causes false elevation because red cells are rich in organic phosphates which may hydrolyze to inorganic phosphate.

Normal values: Adults: 0.8‚Äì1.5 mmol/L. Children: Higher (1.3‚Äì1.9 mmol/L) due to bone growth.
