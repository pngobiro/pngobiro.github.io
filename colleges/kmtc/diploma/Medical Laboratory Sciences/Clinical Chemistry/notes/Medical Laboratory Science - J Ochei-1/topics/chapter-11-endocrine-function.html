üß™ Hormones and Endocrine Glands

A hormone is a substance produced by specific tissue or gland within the body which conveys a message to regulate the function of another tissue or gland, the target tissue. The organs which produce hormones are called endocrine or ductless glands because the hormones produced by them are directly released into the circulating blood.

The major endocrine glands in the body are:

  • Hypothalamus
  • Pituitary
  • Adrenal
  • Thyroid
  • Testes (Men) / Ovaries (Women)
  • Pancreas (Islets of Langerhans)
02

üß† Hypothalamus and Pituitary Gland

Hypothalamus

The hypothalamus produces "releasing factors" (trophic hormones) that stimulate the anterior pituitary. It also synthesizes ADH and Oxytocin, which are stored in the posterior pituitary.

  • Growth hormone releasing factor (GHRF)
  • Prolactin releasing/inhibiting factors (PRF/PIF)
  • Corticotropic releasing factor (CRF)
  • Gonadotropin releasing factor (GnRH)
  • Thyrotropin releasing hormone (TRH)

Posterior Pituitary Hormones

  1. Antidiuretic Hormone (ADH): Enhances water reabsorption. Deficiency causes Diabetes Insipidus.
  2. Oxytocin: Milk ejection and uterine contractions.
03

‚ö° Adrenal Glands

The adrenal glands are situated above each kidney and consist of two parts: the Cortex and the Medulla.

Adrenal Cortex Hormones

Mineralocorticoids

Aldosterone. Regulates Na+ reabsorption and K+ excretion. Excess leads to hypertension; deficiency (Addison's) leads to Na+ depletion.

Glucocorticoids

Cortisol. Stimulates protein/fat breakdown and gluconeogenesis. Regulated by ACTH. Excess causes Cushing's syndrome.

Androgens

DHA and Androstenedione. Mildly androgenic. Hypersecretion causes hirsutism and virilism.

Adrenal Medulla Hormones

Produces Catecholamines (Epinephrine/Adrenalin and Norepinephrine/Noradrenalin). These regulate the "fight or flight" response, increasing heart rate, blood pressure, and blood glucose.

04

ü¶ã Thyroid and Parathyroid

Thyroid Hormones

  • Thyroxine (T4) and Triiodothyronine (T3): Regulate metabolic rate, growth, and development. Synthesized from tyrosine and iodine.
  • Calcitonin: Lowers plasma calcium.

Disorders

  • Hyperthyroidism (e.g., Grave's Disease): High metabolic rate, weight loss, tremor.
  • Hypothyroidism (e.g., Hashimoto's): Slow metabolism, lethargy, weight gain.
  • Goitre: Enlargement of the gland (often due to iodine deficiency).

Parathyroid Glands

Produce Parathyroid Hormone (PTH). PTH increases plasma calcium by releasing it from bones and increasing renal reabsorption, while decreasing plasma phosphate.

‚ôÄÔ∏è‚ôÇÔ∏è Reproductive Hormones

Male (Testes)

  • Testosterone: Produced by Leydig cells. Sexual differentiation and spermatogenesis.
  • Inhibitin: Produced by Sertoli cells. Regulates FSH.

Female (Ovaries)

  • Oestrogens: Produced by follicles. Secondary characteristics, menstruation.
  • Progesterone: Produced by Corpus Luteum. Maintains pregnancy.

Menstrual Cycle

Consists of the Follicular Phase (Oestrogen dominant, follicle growth) and the Luteal Phase (Progesterone dominant, post-ovulation). Ovulation is triggered by an LH surge.

Pregnancy

After implantation, the placenta produces Human Chorionic Gonadotrophin (HCG). HCG maintains the corpus luteum. Detection of HCG in urine/serum is the basis of pregnancy tests.

üî¨ Methods of Assay

Hormones are present in very low concentrations ($\mu g$ or $ng$ per litre). Sensitive methods like RIA (Radioimmunoassay), ELISA, and Fluorimetry are used.

Fluorimetric Assay for Cortisol

Principle: Steroids with an 11-hydroxyl group (like cortisol) fluoresce in concentrated sulphuric acid.

Reagents

  • Methylene dichloride (purified)
  • Fluorescence reagent: Ethanol + Conc. $H_2SO_4$ (30:70)
  • Cortisol standards (Stock 30 mmol/L, Working 3.0 mmol/L)

Method

  1. Extract plasma (2.0 ml) with methylene dichloride (15 ml).
  2. Centrifuge and remove plasma layer.
  3. Add extract to fluorescence reagent.
  4. Read fluorescence at 530 nm (excitation 470 nm) exactly at 12 minutes.

Calculation

\text{Plasma cortisol (nmol/L)} = \frac{\text{Reading of Test} - \text{Blank}}{\text{Reading of Std} - \text{Blank}} \times \frac{3.0 \times 1000}{2}

üìã Clinical Investigations

Dynamic tests (Stimulation and Suppression) are used when baseline levels are indecisive.

1. Hypopituitarism (Combined Stimulation Test)

Protocol: Inject Insulin (to induce hypoglycemia), TRH, and GnRH. Measure Glucose, Cortisol, GH, TSH, LH, and FSH at 0, 30, 60, 90, 120 mins.

Normal Response: Rise in Cortisol (>550 nmol/L), GH (>20 mU/L), TSH, LH, and FSH.

2. Growth Hormone Disorders

  • Deficiency: Insulin stimulation test. Failure of GH to rise indicates deficiency.
  • Excess (Acromegaly): Glucose suppression test. Normal response is suppression of GH to < 2 mU/L. Failure to suppress indicates acromegaly.

3. Cushing's Syndrome (Hypercortisolism)

  • Low dose Dexamethasone: Screening. Failure to suppress cortisol suggests Cushing's.
  • High dose Dexamethasone: Differentiates pituitary cause (Cushing's disease - usually suppresses) from adrenal/ectopic tumours (no suppression).

4. Adrenal Hypofunction (Addison's)

Short Synacthen (Tetracosactrin) Test: Inject ACTH analogue. Normal response is rise in cortisol by >200 nmol/L. Failure to rise confirms primary adrenal insufficiency.

5. Thyroid Function

TRH Test: Inject TRH. Measure TSH.

  • Exaggerated rise: Primary Hypothyroidism.
  • No rise: Hyperthyroidism.
  • Delayed rise: Hypothalamic dysfunction.

6. Infertility / Hirsutism

  • Hirsutism: Measure Testosterone, DHAS, LH, FSH.
  • Amenorrhoea: Pregnancy test first. Then LH/FSH/Prolactin/GnRH tests.

üî¨ Semen Analysis

Semen analysis determines the fertilizing capacity of spermatozoa. Semen consists of spermatozoa (5%) and fluid from seminal vesicles (60%), prostate (20%), and other glands.

Collection

Collect after 2-5 days abstinence. Analyze within 2 hours. Keep at 20-40¬∞C.

Macroscopic Examination

  • Liquefaction: Should occur within 20 mins.
  • Volume: 2‚Äì5 ml.
  • Appearance: Opaque, creamy-white.
  • Viscosity: Droplets should fall distinctly.

Microscopic Examination

  1. Motility: >60% motile with good forward progression.
  2. Count (Neubauer chamber): Dilute 1:20. Count 5 small squares (center).
    \text{Count/ml} = N \times 50,000 \times 20 = N \times 10^6
    Normal: 20‚Äì200 million/ml.
  3. Morphology: Stain with Papanicolaou. Normal sperm has oval head (3-5 $\mu m$), midpiece, and tail (50 $\mu m$). >20% abnormal forms is abnormal.

Post Coital Test

Performed 2-12 hours after intercourse at mid-cycle. Evaluates sperm survival in cervical mucus and mucus quality (spinnbarkeit, cellularity).
