üß¨ Introduction to Lipids

Lipids, commonly known as fats, are a heterogeneous group of organic substances which are insoluble in water and are soluble in a group of organic solvents (chloroform, ether, benzene, hexane). The major lipids in human plasma include cholesterol, triglycerides, phospholipids and non-esterified fatty acids (NEFA).

Cholesterol

An unsaturated steroid alcohol. About 70‚Äì80% in plasma is esterified with fatty acids. It functions as a metabolic precursor for bile acids and steroid hormones and is a structural component of cell membranes.

Triglycerides

Long chain fatty acids esterified to glycerol. They are the primary constituents used for long-term storage of energy. One molecule produces twice the energy of carbohydrates.

Phospholipids

Esters of glycerol with two fatty acyl groups and phosphatidic acid (e.g., lecithin, cephalins). Major components of plasma membranes providing an interface between lipids and water.

NEFA

Non-Esterified Fatty Acids. A small fraction of plasma lipids complexed with albumin, providing energy to cells via oxidation.

üöö Lipoproteins

Lipids are bound to specific proteins to form lipoproteins which provide solubility in the aqueous environment of the blood. Lipoproteins have a spherical shape with neutral lipids (triglycerides, cholesterol esters) at the center, surrounded by free cholesterol, phospholipids, and apolipoproteins.

Based on density (ultracentrifugation), they are classified as:

  • Chylomicrons: Lowest density, transport exogenous triglycerides.
  • VLDL: Very Low Density Lipoproteins.
  • IDL: Intermediate Density Lipoproteins.
  • LDL: Low Density Lipoproteins.
  • HDL: High Density Lipoproteins.

üîÑ Lipoprotein Metabolism

Exogenous Pathway (Dietary)

  1. 1.Dietary fats hydrolysed by pancreatic lipase in the intestine.
  2. 2.Absorbed and resynthesised into triglycerides/phospholipids.
  3. 3.Packaged into Chylomicrons which enter blood via thoracic duct (lymph).
  4. 4.Lipoprotein lipase in capillaries hydrolyses TG to fatty acids for tissue use.
  5. 5.Remnants cleared by liver.

Endogenous Pathway (Liver)

  1. 1.Liver packages TG and cholesterol into VLDL.
  2. 2.Lipoprotein lipase hydrolyses TG from VLDL forming IDL and then LDL.
  3. 3.LDL transports cholesterol to peripheral tissues.
  4. 4.HDL acts as reverse transport, moving cholesterol from tissues back to the liver.

üß™ Estimation of Total Cholesterol

‚ö†Ô∏è

Specimen

Serum or plasma should be collected after a 12-hour fast. Haemolysed or icteric serum should not be used.

Method 1: Ferric Chloride (Salkowski Reaction)

Principle: In the presence of excess acid (phosphoric acid) and ferric ions, cholesterol is oxidised to disulphonic acid which is reddish purple. Read at 560 nm.

Method 2: Acetic Anhydride (Liebermann-Burchard)

Principle: When dehydrated with acetic anhydride and oxidized with sulphuric acid, cholesterol forms a green complex read at 560 nm.

Method 3: Enzymatic Method (Cholesterol Oxidase)

Principle:

\text{Cholesterol esters} \xrightarrow{\text{Esterase}} \text{Cholesterol} + \text{Fatty Acids}
\text{Cholesterol} + O_2 \xrightarrow{\text{Cholesterol Oxidase}} \text{Cholest-4-en-3-one} + H_2O_2
H_2O_2 + \text{Phenol} + \text{4-Aminophenazone} \xrightarrow{\text{Peroxidase}} \text{Red Complex} + H_2O

The absorbance of the red complex is measured at 500 nm.

Calculation

\text{Cholesterol (mg/dl)} = \frac{\text{Abs Test} - \text{Abs Test Blank}}{\text{Abs Std} - \text{Abs Std Blank}} \times \text{Conc. of Std (e.g. 200)}

Unit Conversion

$\text{mmol/L} = \text{mg/dl} \times 0.0259$

Normal Range: 150‚Äì250 mg/dl (3.8‚Äì6.2 mmol/L)

‚öóÔ∏è Estimation of Triglycerides

Method 1: Chemical Extraction

Involves extraction with isopropanol, saponification with sodium methylate, oxidation of glycerol with sodium periodate to formaldehyde, and reaction with acetylacetone (Hantzsch reaction) to form a yellow compound measured at 420 nm.

Method 2: Enzymatic

Principle:

\text{TG} + H_2O \xrightarrow{\text{Lipase}} \text{Glycerol} + \text{Fatty Acids}
\text{Glycerol} + \text{ATP} \xrightarrow{\text{Glycerol Kinase}} \text{Glycerol-3-Phosphate} + \text{ADP}
\text{G-3-P} + O_2 \xrightarrow{\text{GPO}} \text{Dihydroxyacetone phosphate} + H_2O_2

The $H_2O_2$ is then measured using the peroxidase reaction (Trinder reaction) producing a coloured product.

Unit Conversion

$\text{mmol/L} = \text{mg/dl} \times 0.0113$

Normal Range: 80‚Äì150 mg/dl (0.9‚Äì1.7 mmol/L)

üìâ HDL and LDL Estimation

HDL Cholesterol

Principle: Chylomicrons, VLDL, and LDL are precipitated by phosphotungstic acid in the presence of magnesium ions. HDL remains in the supernatant. The cholesterol content of the supernatant is then determined by the enzymatic method.

Calculation

\text{HDL (mg/dl)} = \frac{\text{Abs Test}}{\text{Abs Std}} \times \text{Conc. Std} \times \text{Dilution Factor (e.g. 1.15)}

Normal HDL: 40‚Äì50 mg/dl (1.1‚Äì1.2 mmol/L). Higher levels reduce CHD risk.

LDL Cholesterol

LDL is often calculated using the Friedewald formula (valid only if TG < 4.5 mmol/L):

\text{LDL (mmol/L)} = \text{Total Chol} - (\text{HDL} + 0.46 \times \text{TG})

Note: If using mg/dl, the factor for TG is usually $\text{TG}/5$.

Visual observation of standing serum (18 hours at 4¬∞C) can provide clues:

  • Creamy top layer: Hyperchylomicronaemia.
  • Turbid throughout: High VLDL.
