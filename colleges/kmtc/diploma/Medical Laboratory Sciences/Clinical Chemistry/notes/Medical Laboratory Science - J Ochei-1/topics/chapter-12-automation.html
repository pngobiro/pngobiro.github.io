01

‚öôÔ∏è Overview of Automation

With the evolution of Clinical Chemistry, a wide range of tests has been developed to assist in the diagnosis of diseases and disorders and to monitor therapy. With the growing demand for investigations, it is becoming inevitable to replace manual operations by mechanical devices for fast results on a large number of specimens. Automation provides a means by which an increased work load can be processed rapidly and with reproducible results. The quality of results is controlled by running the samples of known values (standards) with every batch of tests.

An autoanalyser may be fully automated and can perform all the steps involved in a manual assay, or it may be semiautomated which needs a part of the procedure to be performed manually.

Components of a Completely Automated System:

  • (i) A device for sampling patient's specimen, standards, controls or blanks.
  • (ii) A mechanism to add a required amount of the reagents in proper sequence.
  • (iii) Incubation modules for the required temperature and time.
  • (iv) A monitoring or measuring device to quantitate the extent of reaction.
  • (v) A recording mechanism to provide the final result with a permanent record.
‚ÑπÔ∏è

Modern Systems

The new generation of autoanalysers include a microprocessor or a small computer which monitors the operations of the analyser.

Advantages

  1. 1.A large number of samples may be processed with minimal staff time.
  2. 2.Two or more assays may be performed simultaneously.
  3. 3.Precision is superior to that of the manual method.
  4. 4.Calculations may not be required.

Disadvantages

  1. 1.Impractical for small number of specimens.
  2. 2.Instrument failures do occur; back-up procedures must be available.
  3. 3.Needs additional training of the staff regarding working and maintenance.
  4. 4.Limitations to methodology that can be used.
  5. 5.Expensive to purchase and maintain.

üè≠ Types of Automated Analyser

There are four main types of autoanalysers which are used in a Clinical Chemistry laboratory for routine tests. Depending on their working principles, they can be grouped as follows:

03

üåä Continuous Flow Systems

Single Channel Continuous Flow Analyser

In this system, the specimens to be analysed pass sequentially through a single hydraulic line in which all the reactions and incubations take place in a continuous stream of fluid. All the samples and reagents pass along tubular pathways. The reagents flow continuously and the specimens are introduced at intervals. Each specimen segment is separated by air bubbles which are large enough to scrub the sides of the tube to prevent mixing from specimen to specimen.

Main Parts:

  1. 1. Sampler: Consists of a circular tray with separate cups for samples. A probe aspirates the samples serially. An appropriate wash solution is aspirated between two samples.
  2. 2. Proportionating pump: A peristaltic pump drives the fluid along the pump tubes. It acts like using different sizes of pipettes to add varying volumes of reagents. The air bubbles are also introduced by this pump.
  3. 3. Manifold: Contains submodules:
    • Dialyser: Removes protein molecules via a semi-permeable membrane.
    • Mixing coils: Multi-turn horizontal glass coils that mix fluids.
    • Heating bath: Thermostatically controlled oil bath for incubation ($37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ to $95^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$).
  4. 4. Colorimeter: Continuously measures the absorbance of the solution passing through the flow cell.
  5. 5. Recorder: Records the voltage changes from the photodetector as a graph or print-out.

Example: Autoanalyser II System

Multi-channel Continuous Flow Analyser

Large scale machines which analyse twenty or more parameters at the same time. A computer is used for the continuous monitoring of the chemical reactions and calibration. Example: Sequential Multiple Analyser with Computer (SMAC).

04

üß™ Discrete Analysers

A discrete analyser simulates manual pipetting, mixing, incubation and colorimetric reading. Each specimen is treated as a separate process in its own designated reaction vessel.

Types

  • Single-channel: Each specimen analysed for one constituent only.
  • Multi-channel: Selects more than one analysis to be performed (e.g., ASTRA analyser).
  • End-point: Absorbance measured at the end of the reaction.
  • Reaction rate: Measures rate of change of absorbance (faster results).

Working Principles

  1. Sampler: Aspirates sample and transfers it with diluents to a reaction tube.
  2. Reaction unit: May have an incubator for temperature regulation.
  3. Measuring unit: Measures chemical change via colorimetry, flame photometry, etc.
  4. Display unit: Digital display or print-out.

Complete Discretionary Ability: The operator programs the required tests for each sample. The computer determines the sequence for maximum speed. Examples: Abbott 200, Hitachi 705.

05

üé° Centrifugal Analysers

In this type of analysers, the reaction takes place in the rotor of a centrifuge.

Components

  1. 1.Rotor disc: Contains concentric rings of wells. Wells in one radial row are connected by capillary passages. Each row processes one specimen.
  2. 2.Photometer: Mounted near the periphery. Its light path is parallel to the axis of rotation (vertical).

Working Principle

The rotor disc is loaded with reagents and samples. When spun, reagents move outward due to centrifugal force, mixing with the sample in the peripheral cuvettes. The reaction proceeds and is read by the photometer as the cuvettes pass through the beam. A computer processes the signals. Example: ENI Ge MSAEC automatic fast analyser.

06

üéûÔ∏è Thin-Film / Dry Chemistry

These discrete analysers use dry reagents spread in extremely thin layers on a plastic slide to which serum specimen is added.

System Components:

  1. 1.Plastic chip: Contains layers of dry reagents. The serum provides the solvent.
  2. 2.Reflectance spectrophotometer: Measures the intensity of colour by determining the proportion of light reflected off the coloured area.

The analysers are completely discretionary. Chips are delivered from cartridges, incubated, and read. Example: Kodak Ektachem.

07

üîß Automatic Measuring Devices

Automatic Pipettes

Permit rapid, repetitive, accurate measurement and delivery of predefined volumes. Some have tip-ejector devices or variable digital settings (1 Œºl to 1000 Œºl).

Hand-operated mechanical pipette

Air is displaced by a thumb operated push button. The liquid does not enter the syringe mechanism, only the disposable tip, preventing contamination.

Automatic Dispensers

Useful for repetitive adding of multiple volumes of the same reagent. Available as syringes or bottle top devices.

Automatic Diluters

Used to prepare samples for analyses. Mostly dual piston type: one for sample, one for diluent.
