Puberty

During childhood, circulatory levels of the sex steroids and gonadotropins are low and similar in both sexes. Values for LH, FSH and gonadol steroids (testosterone, progesterone, estradiol,) rise gradually over several years before stabilizing in the adult range when full sexual maturity is reached.

  • In girls, precocious puberty is defined as the onset of pubertal development before the age 8.5 years and puberty is said to be delayed if there has been no development by the the age 12-14 years and if menarche (first menstrual cycle) has not occurred by age 16.5 years.
  • In boys, puberty is considered precocious if it occurs before the age of 10 years and delayed when the onset is later than age 16 years. Nb precocious puberty is more common in girls.

Male Reproductive System and Hormones

Sperm cell development starts at about the age of 11 years. This is influenced by the hormone testosterone. By the time the boy is 15 years, sufficient levels of testosterone exist so that the testis become capable of full spermatogenesis. Before the age of 15 years most of the sperm cells produced in the testis are incapable of fertilization. The production of fully mature sperm (spermatozoa) is triggered by FSH. LH influences the secretion of testosterone by the leyding cells of the testis.

Male Hormonal Reproductive Disorders (Infertility)

Infertility in male is rarely the presentation of primary and secondary hypogonadism, other clinical pictures eg impotence, azoospermia, oligospermia generally points to that diagnosis. Other feature is androgen deficiency.

(1) Impotence

This is the persistent inability to develop or maintain a penile erection sufficient for intercourse and ejaculation.

Causes of impotence are-:

  • low levels of testosterone.
  • hyperprolactinemia.
  • a number of systemic diseases that affect the pituitary/ gonadal axis eg CRF, Chronic liver cirrhosis and diabetes mellitus.
  • severe malnutrition, stress,
  • alcohol may have a direct effect on the testis impairing spermatogenesis.
  • Low and elevated levels of the following hormones can cause impotence eg cortisol, Thyroid hormones, and low levels of LH and FSH.

(2) Gynecomastia

This is the growth of glandular breast tissue in male. The growth may be bilateral or unilateral .NB unilateral growth has no specific significance.

  • gynecomastia may develop due to excessive use of alcohol, marijuana or certain medication.
  • gynecomastia may be associated with hyper- and hypo thyroidism.
  • Tumors that produce chorionic gonadotropin, estradiol producing tumors of the adrenals and testis and prolactin-producing pituitary adenomas all may cause gynecomastia.

When physical examination and history do not point to any specific disorder, measurement of HCG, ESTRADIOL and PROLACTIN are appropriate. The presence of glandular breast tissue in pubertal males is quite common and almost always resolves sportenously,

Female Reproductive System and Hormones

The Menstrual Cycle (MC)

Each month or so the inner wall of the uterus prepares for a possible pregnancy. Incase a pregnancy does not occur (as in the case throughout most months of a woman's fertile years ),the linning of the uterus must be released and a new one prepared. The breakdown of the endometrial wall and the resultant discharge of blood and endometrial tissue are known as menstruation (or menses)

The Cyclic timing of menstruation is governed by the hormones released from the pituitary and the ovaries. Girls generally have their first menstrual cycle, the onset of which is called menarche sometimes between 12-14 years of age. Body weight, nutrition, heredity, and overall health are factors related to menarche.

This cycle activity continuous until about age 45-55 years. The normal MC lasts for 28 days but very few women perfects 28 - day cycle. Most women fluctuates by a few days to a week around this 28 day pattern and some are extremely from this cycle. Day 1 of the cycle starts with the first day of bleeding or menstrual flow.

The normal cycle occurs in three phases:

  • FOLLICULAR PHASE---This is the phase after the menstrual flow and takes around 2 weeks up to the period of ovulation. During this phase the hormonal status are that Estradiol and progesterone are low, FSH and LH are elevated .
  • OVULATORY PHASE----This is the phase that comes at the mid cycle and last for 48 hours. Ovulation takes place in this phase and the released ovum travels in the fallopian tube for 48 hours awaiting fertilization. The hormonal status is that there is a rise in estradiol, LH and FSH. Progesterone is low.
  • LUTEUL PHASE----This is the last half of the MC. It comes between the ovulation periods up to the time of bleeding. The hormonal status is that there is increasing production of progesterone and estradiol and low production of LH and FSH.

Female Hormonal Reproductive Disorders (Infertility)

Irregular Menses

In a normal ovulatory menstrual cycle, bleeding usually occurs 14 to 15 days after the mid cycle. In infertile women, luteal phase may be shortened and menses occurs 5-10 days after mid cycle peak. The cycle may be normal but no ovulation occurs ie anovulatory.

(1) Amenorrhea

This is of two types ie primary and secondary.

  • Primary amenorrhea is a disorder whereby no menses has begun by the age of 16.5 years or the patient has never menstruated and this is encountered in delayed puberty.
    The cause of this are endocrine disturbances such as hirsutism, virilism (testicular ferminization)
  • Secondary amenorrhea is a disorder which occurs when previously established MC has stopped. This could be due to physiological factors eg pregnancy and menopause which are not abnormal, or stress and some contraceptive medication.
    Other causes are severe illness, excessive and rapid weight loss and obesity.
    Hyperprolactinemia is an important cause of secondary amenorrhea. High levels of prolactin inhibit normal release of GnRH and this affects the fertility hormone s release directly.

(2) Menopause

This is a condition whereby the ovulation and menstrual cycle stops. This occurs in women at the age of 45-55 years and above. If it occurs at the age less than 40 years it is regarded as immature. Menopause is a normal physiological process and not a disease condition. Other signs associated with menopause are:- hot flashes (temporary feeling of warmth caused by blood vessel dilation), vaginal wall dryness and breast changes. Ovarian function and hormonal production diminishes.

(3) Hirsutism

This is excess of facial and body hair in a woman. Could be caused by:- excess testosterone production which could be due to some medication or from a neoplasm in the ovaries or the adrenal glands.

(4) Virilism (Testicular Feminization)

This is the enlargement of the clitoris that resembles a penis (clitomegaly). Caused by excessive testosterone production. It is characterized by hair growth of male distribution, deepening of the voice and breast atrophy (no female breasts). Other causes are ovarian tumor, congenital adrenal hyperplasia.

Fertility Hormonal Profile

Fertility hormonal profile analysis plays an important role in the diagnosis and management of various pathological disorders that contribute to infertility in an individual. Infertility is defined as the absence of pregnancy following 12 months of unprotected intercourse. Infertility may be caused by ovulatory dysfunction, blocked fallopian tubes, male factor infertility or unexplained causes.

To investigate primary and secondary infertility that affect both male and female the following diagnostic parameters are carried out: testosterone (TESTO), prolactin (PRL), follicle stimulating hormone (FSH), luteinizing hormone (LH), estradiol 2 (EII) and progesterone (PRG)

‚ñ∂ Testosterone (TESTO)

Testosterone, a steroid hormone derived from cholesterol, occurs more abundantly in circulation among men, than women. In men, testosterone plays two key roles:

  • Anabolic effects include growth of muscle mass and strength, increased bone density and strength, and stimulation of linear growth and bone maturation
  • Androgenic effects include maturation of the sex organs, particularly the penis and the formation of the scrotum in the fetus, and after birth (usually at puberty) a deepening of the voice, growth of the beard and axillary hair.

In females testosterone increases the libido which is expressed by clitoral engorgement. The levels are increased at puberty and mid-cycle where it plays a great role during ovulation

‚ñ∂ Prolactin (PRL)

Prolactin is a protein hormone produced in the anterior pituitary by the pituitary mammotropic cells.Its secretion is controlled by an inhibitory influence from the hypothalamus that is thought to be dopamine. It stimulates the mammary glands to produce milk (lactation). Increased serum concentrations of prolactin during pregnancy cause enlargement of the mammary glands of the breasts and prepare the production of milk.

Prolactin provides the body with sexual gratification after sexual acts: The hormone counteracts the effect of dopamine, which is responsible for sexual arousal. This is thought to cause the sexual refractory period. Unusually high amounts are suspected to be responsible for impotence and loss of libido. Hyperprolactinemia is the most frequent abnormality of the anterior pituitary tumors. Clinical signs include inappropriate lactation, lack of menses, infertility in females, and impotence in males.

‚ñ∂ Follicle Stimulating Hormone (FSH)

Follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH) is a hormone found in humans and other animals. It is synthesized and secreted by gonadotrophs of the anterior pituitary gland. FSH regulates the development, growth, pubertal maturation, and reproductive processes of the body. FSH and Luteinizing hormone (LH) act synergistically in reproduction.

In males, FSH stimulates maturation of seminiferous tubules and spermatogenesis. In females, FSH initiates follicular growth, specifically affecting granulosa cells. With the concomitant rise in inhibin B, FSH levels then decline in the late follicular phase. This seems to be critical in selecting only the most advanced follicle to proceed to ovulation.

The most common reason for high serum FSH concentration is in a female who is undergoing or has recently undergone menopause. If high FSH levels occur during the reproductive years, it is abnormal. Conditions with high FSH levels include: premature menopause, poor ovarian reserve, gonadal dysgenesis (in female) castration and testicular failure (in male).

‚ñ∂ Luteinizing Hormone (LH)

Luteinizing hormone is a hormone produced by the anterior pituitary gland. The release of LH at the pituitary gland is controlled by pulses of gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH) from the hypothalamus. In females, an acute rise of LH called the LH surge triggers ovulation and development of the corpus luteum. In males, where LH had also been called interstitial cell-stimulating hormone (ICSH), it stimulates Leydig cell production of testosterone.

Persistently high LH levels are indicative of situations where the normal restricting feedback from the gonad is absent, leading to a pituitary production of both LH and FSH. While this is typical in the menopause, it is abnormal in the reproductive years.

‚ñ∂ Estradiol 2 (EII)

Estradiol is the predominant sex hormone present in females. It is also present in males, being produced as an active metabolic product of testosterone. It represents the major estrogen in humans. In the female, estradiol acts as a growth hormone for tissue of the reproductive organs, supporting the lining of the vagina, the cervical glands, the endometrium, and the lining of the fallopian tubes. The development of secondary sex characteristics in women is driven by estrogens, to be specific, estradiol.

The effect of estradiol (and estrogens) upon male reproduction is complex. Estradiol is produced in the Sertoli cells of the testes. There is evidence that estradiol functions to prevent apoptosis of male sperm cells.

‚ñ∂ Progesterone (PRG)

Progesterone also known as P4 (pregn-4-ene-3,20-dione) is a C-21 steroid hormone involved in the female menstrual cycle, pregnancy (supports gestation) and embryogenesis of humans and other species. Progesterone is produced in the ovaries (to be specific, after ovulation in the corpus luteum), the adrenal glands (near the kidney), and, during pregnancy, in the placenta.

Progesterone is sometimes called the "hormone of pregnancy", and it has many roles relating to the development of the fetus: Progesterone converts the endometrium to its secretory stage to prepare the uterus for implantation. At the same time progesterone affects the vaginal epithelium and cervical mucus, making it thick and impenetrable to sperm.

Analytical Methods for Hormones

The current analytical technique employed in the analysis of all the hormones use the principle of immunochemical reaction based on an antiboby/antigen reaction. The set up of the reagent could either have the the antigen to the hormone being determined and this will react with the specific antibody (being the hormone in the specimen) in the specimen.

Analytical Principle:

The assay principle combined an enzyme immunoassay sandwich method with a final fluorescent detection (enzyme linked fluorescent assay (ELFA))

Analytical method:

The SPR served as the solid phase as well as the pipetting device for the assay. Reagents for the assay were ready for use and pre-dispensed in the sealed reagent strips. All the assay steps were performed automatically by the machine. The reaction medium was cycled in and out of the SPR several times. The sample was taken and transferred into the well containing alkaline - phosphatase labeled anti- (T4, T3, FT4, FT3, TSH, LH, PRG, FSH, PRL,, EST, TESTO) immunoglobulins (conjugate). The sample/conjugate mixture was cycled in and out of SPR several times to increase reaction speed. The antigens binds with the antibodies coated on the SPR and to the conjugate forming a "sandwich". Unbound components were eliminated during the washing steps of the assay.

In the final detection step, the substrate (4-methyl-umbelliferyl phosphate) was cycled in and out of the SPR. The conjugate enzyme catalyzed the hydrolysis of the substrate into a fluorescent product (4-methylumbelliferone). The intensity of the fluorescence was directly proportional to the antigen present in the sample.At the end of the assay, results were automatically calculated by the machine in relation to the calibration curve stored in memory and then printed out.
