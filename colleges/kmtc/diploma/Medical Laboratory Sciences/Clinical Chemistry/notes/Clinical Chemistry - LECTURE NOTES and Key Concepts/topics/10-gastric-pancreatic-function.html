Introduction and Anatomy

The stomach, intestines and the pancrease functions are inter-related well coordinated and regulated with a final result of ensuring efficient digestion and absorption of nutrients that are essential for the maintenance and survival of body cells. These coordination and regulation of the organs activities are all dependent on hormones that stimulate or inhibit secretion of fluids containing hydrochloric acid (HCL), Bile acids, bicarbonates and digestive enzymes.

The stomach is the centre of food digestion. It is divided into three sections (zones):- (1) cardiac zone (2)the stomach body zone (3) pyloric zone.

Zonal activities

  • (1) Cardiac zones-----contains mucus secreting epithelial cells
  • (2) Body of stomach---has four different cells each performing specific duty (a)surface epithelial cells secrete mucus (b) parietal cells--- the main and possibly only source of HCL and intrinsic factors (c) chief or peptic cells---secretes considerable amount of pepsinogen (d) neck chief cells or mucus cells----secretes mucus and pepsinogen.
  • (3) Pyloric zone---(sub-divided into:-antrum, pyloric canal and the pyloric sphincter)-The cells in this zone secretes mucus, some pepsinogen and gastrin BUT NO HCL.

Digestive Process

Digestive process consists of three phases namely :-(1)Cephalic phase (2) gastric phase (3) intestinal phase.

  1. (1) Cephalic phase----initiated by the sight, smell and the taste of food. This will stimulate the cerebral cortex and subsequently the vagal nuclei which results with the secretion of pepsinogen, HCL and gastrin in readiness for the food entering the stomach.
  2. (2) gastric phase-----This phase is initiated by the distention caused by food entering the stomach. There is the release of HCL caused by the direct stimulation of the parietal cells by the vagus nerve. There is also the release of gastrin which stimulate the secretion of pepsinogen and pancreatic fluid rich in enzymes and the release of a number of gastrointestinal hormones(secretin, insulin, acetylcholine, somatostatin and pancreatin polypeptide). As a result of acidic environment, pepsinogen is rapidly converted to the active proteolytic enzyme pepsin.
  3. (3) intestinal phase--- This process begins when the partially degested food mixed with mucus (chime) enters the duodenum.This phase is accompanied with the release of the hormones secretin, gastrin and cholecystokinin.

Accessory Organs

PANCREASE---This organ lies across the posterior wall of the abdomen. It secretes the bicarbonate richpancreatic juice which contains the pancreatic digestive enzymes. Amylase is concerned with the breaking down od oligo- and disaccharides into monosaccharides which are then actively absorded into the blood stream. Trypsin, and carboxypeptidase from the pancrease and aminopeptidase from the small intestine are concerned with the break down of proteins into amino acids which are then absorded in the blood stream.

Enzymes of Gastrointestinal Tract

Pepsin and Pepsinogen

The chief and peptic cells, the mucus cells of the stomach produce pepsinogen. It is secreted into the stomach and forms part of the gastric fluid.Its production is stimulated by the vagus nerve and some GI hormones eg secretin, gastrin. Its secretion is inhibited by gastric inhibitory polypeptide.

Methods of analysis includes:- measurement of the enzymatic activity by hydrolysis of specific protein or peptide substrates followed by measurement of the amount of substrate remaining or product formed (2) radioimmunoassay test (3)diffusion of the enzyme through a turbid mixture of agarose and dye-linked protein with subsequent measurement of cleared zones as the dye is split off from the protein by proteolytic action.

Amylase

Alpha-Amylase is one of the diagnostic parameter performed in the investigation of pancreatic disorders. It belongs to a group of hydrolaes that split complex carbohydrates composed of alpha-D-glucose units linked through carbon atoms 1 and 4 located on adjacent glucose residues.

Diseases resulting in elevation of plasma alpha-amylase include: acute pancreatitis, parotitis, alcoholism, renal insufficiency and viral diseases such as hepatitis, AIDS and abdominal typhoid. In acute pancreatitis, amylase increases 5-6 hours aftervthe onset of symptoms and remains elevated for 2-5 days.The increase in amylase plasma activity does not reflect disease severity and conversely, extensive destruction of the pancreas may not cause a significant increase in the plasma concentration of pancreatic alpha-amylase

Laboratory Analysis of Amylase

Amylase reagent is used to measure the concentration of amylase by a kinetic colour method. In the reaction, 2-chloro-4-nitrophenyl-alpha-D-maltotrioside ($\text{CNPG}_{3}$) substrate reacted with amylase in the serum to release 2-chloro-4-nitrophenol (CNP) from the substrate which is directly proportional to the concentration of amylase in the sample.

$$ \text{CNPG}_3 + \text{H}_2\text{O} \xrightarrow{\text{amylase}} \text{CNP} + \text{maltotriose} $$

Lipase

Assays fro lipase in serum are used chiefly for the diagnosis of acute pancreatitis, in which levels change in a manner similar to those of amylase. The elevations are frequently more pronounced, more prolonged and more specific for the disease than are amylase elevations. For diagnostic purposes, lipase and amylase evaluations complement one another.

Trypsin

Recently developed immunochemical techniques have shown that elevation of serum trypsin is a highly sensitive and relatively specific indicator of pancreatic disease.

Gastric Function Tests

This can be achieved through the analysis of gastric residue, the determination of basal and stimulated secretion rates and the determinations of intrinsic factors and pepsinogen.

Gastric Residue Test

Gastric residue is defined as the entire contents of the stomach after a fast of approximately 12 hours. The specimen is obtained by aspiration through a gastric tube. A normal total volume is between 20 to 100 mls but usually below 50 mls . Increased volume could be due to delayed emptying, increased gastric secretion and admixture of material from the duodenum. The colour of a normal gastric residue is clear.

Free HCL Determination

The concentration of free HCL in a normal person is 0-40 mmol/l. Deermination of FREE HCL is often done through titration methods but measurement of pH of the gastric residue is the preferred method.

Analysis of Gastric Residue

Qualitative analysis:

  • preparation of the patient---PT should be in a fasting state (no food or liquids for 10-12 hrs). No smoking or any exercise the morning of or during the test
  • Gastric residue is collected by oral or nasal intubation under fluoroscopic guidance.
  • Screening test for free acid---the pH of aspirated gastric juice is measured using a pH meter OR use of Toepfer's reagent ( 0.5 g diethylaminoazobenzene/ 100 ml ) which gives red color at a pH less than 3 indicating free HCL and a yellow color when the pH is above 3 indicating absence of free HCLacid.

Quantitative analysis:

a known volume of gastric residue is titrated with NaOH 0.1MOL/L, to a pH of 3.5 by using a pH meter or until the toepfers reagent added to the gastric residue juice takes a salmon colour. The concentration of free hcl acid is then calculated as follows

$$ \text{Free HCL (mmol/l)} = \frac{\text{NaOH (ml)} \times 0.1\text{mol/l} \times 1000}{\text{gastric specimen titrated(ml)}} $$

REF RANGE(0-40 MMOL/L)

Indirect Test Pancreatic Function

These tests are based on the fact that proper pancreatic function is essential for normal intestinal absorption. Since the pancrease is the major source of amylase, lipase, and proteolytic enzymes, significant decrease in pancreatic function cause decreased digestion of starch, fats and protein and a parallel increase in the excretion of these food materials in stool. In pancreatic dysfunction

Total Faecal Fat Estimation

The quantification of faecal fat is the most definitive test for determining the presence of steatorrhea (increased presence of lipids but not for determining its cause.

Method 1 (gravimetric method)

PRINCIPLE---In the gravimetric method, a preweighed, emulsified stool specimen is acidified to decrease the ionization of fatty acids. The lipids are then extracted from stool specimen into an organic solvent, the solvent is evaporated, and the residue is quantitated by gravimetry

Method 2 (titrimetric method)

In titrimetric method, a weighed stool specimen is subjected to alkaline hydrolysis to convert esterified lipids to fatty acids and then acidified to facilitate the transfer of fatty acids into an organic solvent.The solvent is evaporated, and the residue is dissolved in ethanol and titrated with a standard solution of sodium hydroxide. T he results represent 80% of total faecal fat.

Specimen collection

The pt should be on a diet containing 50-150 g of fat per day for 3 days prior to the day of testing and during the 72 hrs stool collection period. The pt should not have had castor oil or other laxatives prior to the specimen collection. Stool specimen are conveniently collected in one-gallon paint can ( 4 litre). Contamination of the faeces with urine should be avoided.

Reference ranges: children <6 yrs (<2g/day), adults (2-6g/day). Values >7g/day indicates steatorrhea.

Gastric, Pancreatic and Intestinal Diseases

Peptic Ulcer Disease

This iis a common name given to chronic duodenal ulcer and chronic gastric ulcer. Although both of these diseases have many features in common they differ with respect to incidences, pathogenesis, medical history, outcome and management.

‚ñ∂ Chronic Duodenal Ulcer

This type of ulcer affects the duodenum or the pyloric channel of the stomach.

complications associated with this type of ulcer are :-

  • haemorrahage due to ulcer eroding a blood vessel.
  • perforation of the duodenal cap causing peritonitis
  • penetration of the ulcer into the surrounding organs such as pancrease, liver, colon, abdominal wall and even the lungs.
  • luminal obstruction caused by gradual contraction of the ulcer scar..

DIAGNOSIS---Is made primary by radiologic technique or endoscopy or the estimation of pepsinogen in the serum.

‚ñ∂ Chronic Benign Gastric Ulcer

Gastric ulcers occur anywhere in the stomach. Some patients with gastric ulcer have a coexistent duodenal ulcer. As with duodenal ulcer, gastric ulcer can cause haemorrhage and may perforate

DIAGNOSIS--- is through radiologic examination.

Zollinger-Ellison (Z-E) Syndrome

Gastrin is produced and stored mainly by special endocrine cells(G-CELLS) of the mucosa of the stomach and to a lesser extent G-cells of the duodenum and D-cells of the pancreatic islets.. After secretion the principal circulating type of gastrin is G-34( denoting the number of amino acids found in gastrin hormone.

Knowledge of blood gastrin levels is helpful in the diagnosis of Zollinger-ellison syndrome which is caused by GASTRINOMAS( duodinal and pancreatic endocrine tumors) that produce and secrete large quantities of gastrin. This syndrome consists of peptic ulcer, massive gastric hyper secretion, non-b-islets cells tumors of the pancrease. ---Other characteristics of Z-E syndrome includes:-hypergastrinaemia, diarrhea, steotorrhae ( losing of a lot of fats in stoll). Gastrin levels are usually elevated 2-2000 times that of the normal.

DIAGNOSIS-----A secretin challenge is the most useful provocative test for the diagnosis of Z-E syndrome.

Gastritis

this is the term used to denote mucosal inflamation. it can be classified as erosive , nonerosive or specific gastritis.

  • (A) Erosive Gastritis( also referred to as stress lesions or acute gastritis) occurs in five well defined clinical situations:- 1)following severe trauma, 2) following severe burns,3) in patients with intra-cranial disease 4) following craniotomy or tramatic head injuries, 5)in patients who chronically have to take drugs such as corticosteroids, aspirin or ethanol.
    Diagnosis----endoscopy is usually definitive technique
  • (b) Non-Erosive---(also referred to as chronic gastritis)-associated with gastric ulcer dse and gastric carcinoma. Occurs following gastrectomy or in elderly healthy persons.
  • (c) Specific Gastritis---this is very rare.

Other Syndromes

Gastric Cancer (Adenocarcinoma)

Arises fro the mucous cells of the gastric mucosa. Diagnosis is by biobsy. Pts with gastric cancer have fasting achlorhydria (high levels of HCL), volume of gastric residue may be increased and some cases of blood in the stomach may also be present.

Post Gastrectomy Syndrome

this occurs after the treatment of peptic u,lcer disease by gastric resection or denervation ( vagatomy) of the stomach.. pts always suffer some functional incapacity. Symptoms includes- dumping syndrome, diarrhea, weight loss, anaemia and bone dse. The syndrome is caused by decrease in absorption of vital nutrients.

Dumping Syndrome

Occurs because the stomach after gastrectomy empties more rapidly than normal. pts experiences abdominal pains ,diarrhae and cardiac palpitations30-60 min after eating. weight loss and some specific nutritional deficiencies are not uncommon following gastric surgery. Anaemia develops due to decreased iron, vit b12 and folate absorption. maldigestion will occur due to rapid gastric empting resulting with less gstric dispersion of solid foods and reduced digestion time.

Pancreatic Disorders

The pancrease is the organ concerned with the secretions of exocrine and endocrine substances. The endocrine function is the secretion of insulin and glucagons and the exocrine function is the production of amylase and alkaline fluid. . the diseases associated with the pancreas are acute pancreatitis, chronic pancreatitis and tumor of the pancreas.

  • 1) Acute Pancreatitis---this is the sudden enzymatic destruction of the pancrease tissues resulting with the escape of the active pancreatic enzymes into the glandular parenchyma,. This dse can resolve once the causative factor has been eliminated.. Alcohol abuse has been associated with this dse. Diagnosis is by the estimation of serum amylase or radiological examination.
  • 2) Chronic pancreatitis---this is the progressive damage of the pancrease even after the causative factor has been eliminated.. Ethanol abuse has been associated with this dse. Diagnosis is through radiological examination.
  • 3) Tumor of the pancrease--this includes carcinoma of the pancrease as a whole, adenocarcinoma, of thepancreatic ductal epithelium . Tumors includes_ ; insulinoma, glucagonoma, gastrinoma, stomatostatinoma. These tumors can also secrete adenocorticotropic hormone and calcitonin.
