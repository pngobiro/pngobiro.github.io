Introduction to Clinical Chemistry

This is a major discipline in the field of laboratory medicine. Clinical chemistry (also known as clinical biochemistry, chemical pathology, medical biochemistry or pure blood chemistry) is the area of pathology that is generally concerned with analysis of body fluids.

Historical Development

The discipline originated in the late 19th century with the use of simple chemical tests for various components of blood and urine. Subsequently other techniques were applied including the use and measurement of enzyme activities, spectrophotometry, electrophoresis and immunoassay. Most current laboratories are now highly automated and use assays that are closely monitored and quality controlled. All biochemical tests come under chemical pathology. These are performed on any kind of body fluid, but mostly on serum or plasma. Serum is the yellow watery part of blood that is left after blood has been allowed to clot and all blood cells have been removed. This is most easily done by centrifugation which packs the denser blood cells and platelets to the bottom of the centrifuge tube, leaving the liquid serum fraction resting above the packed cells. Plasma is essentially the same as serum, but is obtained by centrifuging the blood without clotting. Plasma therefore contains all of the clotting factors, including fibrinogen. A large medical laboratory will accept samples for up to about 700 different kinds of tests. Even the largest of laboratories rarely do all these tests themselves and some need to be referred to other labs.

This large array of tests can be further sub-categorised into sub-specialities of:

  • General or routine chemistry
  • Endocrinology - the study of hormones
  • Immunology - the study of the immune system and antibodies
  • Pharmacology or Toxicology - the study of drugs

Clinical Chemistry Goals

  1. diagnosis of pathological disorder using either qualitative and quantitative analysis using appropriate measurements and examinations
  2. Indicate the order in which such measurements and examinations should be requested
  3. confirmation of clinical impression,
  4. provide therapeutic guideline for the purpose of treatment.
  5. to carryout a comprehensive follow up on the disease management
  6. determination of disease prognosis
  7. provide necessary skills through training of the laboratory personnel
  8. Interpret and translate laboratory measurements and examinations.
  9. Recognize pitfalls, problems, and limitations of laboratory data,
  10. Understand pathophysiology or sequence of disease as reflected by clinical chemistry data.
  11. Appreciate and understand the importance of laboratory organization and management for efficient and cost - effective medical care delivery.
  12. Be part of the medical team that constitute the working structure of medical with the ultimate goal of solving medical problems.

Analytical Techniques

The function of the clinical chemistry laboratory in the delivery of quality health care services is to perform qualitative and quantitative analysis on body fluids such as blood, urine, cerebral spinal fluid as well as faces, tissues, calculi and other materials.

If the results have to be useful to the health provider in the diagnosis and management of various pathological disorders, the tests must be performed as accurately as possible. To achieve this goal, the instruments, reagents, analytical methods must be of good state. Qualified personnel and good infrastructure also plays an important role in achieving this goal of provision of quality laboratory results

There are two important techniques used in clinical chemistry laboratory. These techniques are:

  • QUALITATIVE ANALYSIS
  • QUANTITATIVE ANALYSIS

1. Qualitative Analysis

  • This is a technique of analysis which is based on detecting the presence or absence of an analyte in a sample/specimen.
  • The results of qualitative analysis are given as either POSITIVE (indicates presence of an analyte) or NEGATIVE (indicates absence of an analyte).
  • Dry chemistry which involves the use of reagent strips is widely used in this technique.
  • The constituent reagents used for a specific analyte are immobilized on the reagent strip and can be used directly by the health provider or indeed by bthe patients themselves.
  • Comparatively the reagents strips are expensive but easy to handle and usually gives results in a very short time. That is they are very fast.
  • Among the most commonly employed reagents strips are those designed to detect glucose in body fluids. The wafer thin plastic strips contain a narrow pad at one end, to which the sample is applied. The sample material diffuses inwards into the body of the pad which houses the appropriate diagnostic reagents.
  • The reagents strips are designed such that one of the products is a chromogen thus coloured.
  • Most systems are based on visual examination of any colour development on the pad after the sample has been applied. The intensity of the colour produced reflects the concentration of the metabolite in the sample applied.
  • These reagent strips are also widely used in routine examination of urine for the detection of the presence or absence of proteins, bilirubin, blood, glucose, ketones, nitrites etc.

2. Quantitative Analysis

  • This is the type of a technique which is based on determination of the actual concentration of an analyte in a sample.
  • The results are given as a concentration followed by the unit of measure eg x mmol/l, x mol/l, xg/l, xg/dl, xg%, x Œºiu/ml, x Œºmol/l etc.
  • The procedures used in quantitative analysis can either be wet or dry chemistry. Wet chemistry procedures involve reactions whereby the participating reactants reacts with each other in a certain ratio.
  • Currently there are other procedures in dry chemistry that give the actual concentration of an analyte under investigation.
  • The reagents used in wet chemistry procedures are comparatively less expensive.
  • Several instruments are involved in wet chemistry analysis eg simple calorimeters, spectrophotometers, automated chemistry analyzers, flame photometer.

  2. Reagent---Any procedure carried out in the laboratory must make use of the reagents/set of reagents.The reagents contains the molecules that react with the analyte in the specimen

  6. Request form---This is what a health provider use to inform the technician what is supposed to be tested in a client
  7. Technician---This is a qualified trained personell who performs the test in the laboratory using all the facilities provided in the laboratory.

Solutions Preparations and Dilutions

Solutions

  • Solutions are made of solutes and solvents.
  • The concentration of a solvent in a solution may be expressed in different ways. Traditionally, in clinical chemistry concentration has most often been expressed as mass of a solute per volume of solution ( ie mass concentration)
  • The system international (SI) recommends the use whenever possible of moles of solute per volume of solution (ie substance concentration), and the use of a litre as the reference volume.
  • Atomic weights of elements are always considered whenever such reagents solutions are being prepared.

Examples

(1) How many grams of NaCL are required to make 1 litre of 0.05 M NaCL solution?
(mwt: sodium = 23, chloride = 35.5)

$$23+35.5=58.5 \mathrm{gms}$$

58.5 g dissolved in a litre of distilled water = 1 molar solution of NaCL

$$58.5 \mathrm{G} = 1 \mathrm{M}$$ $$? = 0.05 \mathrm{M}$$

$$0.05 \mathrm{M} / 1 \mathrm{M} \times 58.5 \mathrm{G} = 2.925 \mathrm{G} \mathrm{NaCL}$$

(2) How many grams of NaCL are required to make 1500 ml of 2 M NaCL

$1 \mathrm{M} = 58.5 \mathrm{g}$ dissolved in $(1 \mathrm{L}) 1000 \mathrm{ml}$
$2 \mathrm{M} = 58.5 \mathrm{g} \times 2 = 117 \mathrm{g}$ dissolved in a liter $(1000 \mathrm{ml})$
$1500 \mathrm{ml} = 1.5 \mathrm{L}$
$117 \mathrm{g} = 1 \mathrm{L}$
$? = 1.5 \mathrm{L}$
$1.5 \mathrm{L} / 1 \mathrm{L} \times 117 \mathrm{g}$
$= 175.5 \mathrm{g}$ of NaCL

Dilutions

  • Dilutions involve adding more solvent to a given solution. For instance: A 1:10 dilution results when 1 volume of a given solution is diluted to a total volume of 10 with solvent. Generally it would be acceptable to add 9 volumes of a solvent to obtain a total volume of 10 . The concentration of the solute in the final solution then is 1/10 of its [ ] in the original solution
  • If two successive 1:10 dilutions are carried out, the [ ] of the final sol. is 1/10 x 1/10 = 1/100 times the [ ] of the original solution.
  • It is useful to carryout successive dilutions not only to avoid diluting very small volumes (which are difficult to measure accurately), but also to avoid diluting into large volumes ( which require large containers and excessive amount of solvent) eg 1;5000 ( 1 in 5000) dilution can be carried out by successive 1:50 and 1:100 dilutions ( 1 ml of the original solution diluted to 50 ml , then 1 ml of this solution diluted to 100 ml ), rather than a straight 1:5000 dilution, which would require a 5 litre container and nearly 5,000 ml (5litres) of solvent if 1 ml of the original solution were taken for this solution.

A Dilution Formula

Consider that a solution designated as A is diluted to give a solution B . If volume $V_{A}$ of solution A is diluted to give volume $V_{B}$ of solution B and the concentrations of solutions A and B are $C_{A}$ and $C_{B}$ respectively, then the following very useful relationship is true whenever the same volume and [ ] units are used for both.

$$V_{A} \times C_{A} = V_{B} \times C_{B}$$

This formula is derived from the fact that the amount of solute in a volume $V_{A}$ is the same as the amount of solute in volume $V_{B}$. The amount is equal to the [ ] times the volume in each case , that is :
Amount of solute in $V_{A} = V_{A} \times C_{A}$
Amount of solute in $V_{B} = V_{B} \times C_{B}$

Example

What would be the final concentration of a NaCL solution if 10 mls distilled water is added to 10 mls of 2 mg/ml NaCL solution

$$V_{A} \times C_{A} = V_{B} \times C_{B}$$ $$10 \mathrm{ml} \times 2 \mathrm{mg}/\mathrm{ml} = 20 \mathrm{ml} \times C_{B}$$ $$1 \mathrm{mg}/\mathrm{ml} = C_{B}$$

Instrumentation & Automation

Factors Considered in Choosing the Best Instrument

  • Selection of the instrument is governed by the type of the tests being carried out in a specific laboratory.
  • The choice of instrument is governed by the number of tests analyzed in a specific laboratory.
  • The reliability and safety of the instrument must be assured.
  • The laboratory should have enough space for the placement of the instrument.
  • The cost of the instrument should be considered and the laboratory should source for funds to purchase the instrument.
  • The climatic conditions of the country of origin and the country of use should be considered. This will take care of any variation that might be there that can affect the overall working of the instrument.
  • The warranty period of the instrument should be well understood and signed by the buyer and the seller.
  • Availability of spare parts must be assured. It has to be stated clearly whether they are locally available or have to be imported.
  • During the acquisition of the instrument the buyer should ensure that the following are provided by the seller : (a) parts manual (b) operators manual (c) service manual
  • Staff training on how to operate the instrument is important. This enables the staff to familiarize themselves with the instrument. The training can be organized locally or from the instruments country of origin.
  • Operation procedures and instruments maintenance (periodical-daily,weekly or monthly) must be emphazised during the training.

Automation in Clinical Chemistry/Reagent Kits

  • Automation in clinical chemistry is a representation of mechanization of conventional manual technics.
  • The first practical automation was introduced in the clinical laboratory in mid last century.
  • Factors that necessitated the introduction of automation were:-
    (a) Increase in number of patient in health institutions
    (b) Clinicians started ordering panel of tests in an effort of making conclusive diagnosis and this became increasingly difficult to handle using the manual techniques.
    (c) Invention of new analytical methods which replaced the older methods.
  • Several types of automated systems have been since mid last century. Although these systems may differ from each other in many ways, they all represent mechanization of conventional methods.
  • The operating details for each instrument is best found in manuals supplied by the manufacturers.

Discrete Auto Analyzer

  • The discrete auto-analyzer involves the treatment and measurement of samples individually.
  • This system is made up of four major compartments namely:- (a) reagent (b) specimen (c) reaction and (d) data manager.

Advantages and Disadvantages of Automation

(1) Advantages

  • (a) Large number of samples can be analysed within a very short time.
  • (b) Two or more analytical methods may be performed simultaneously.
  • (c) Precision and accuracy is very superior to manual methods (techniques).
  • (d) Cost of reagents is minimal compared to manual methods.
  • (e) Very little amount of sample is required for analysis.
  • (f) It is very fast (improve turnaround time) therefore saves the time the health provider uses to manage a patient.
  • (g) Automation also helps relieve stress on technologists brought about by increased workload
  • (h) The manual handling of samples that may be infectious constitutes a hazard to laboratory staff, hence robotic system improves safety for laboratory staff by eliminating the exposure to pathogens
  • (i) boost productivity of the laboratory which could not be achieved through manual method.

(2) Disadvantages

  • (a) The automatic nature of the machine discourages technicians from making observations regarding potential problems.
  • (b) These systems are only economical in busy laboratories. Small laboratories will always require the use of manual or semi automated methods of analyzing samples.
  • (c) These systems contribute to unemployment in developing countries
  • (d) The systems require placement in big rooms ie occupies huge spaces in the laboratory.
  • (e) The systems are very expensive to purchase and maintain.

Reagent Kits

There are many methods available commercially as kits. A kit is a package which contains either some or all the reagents and components necessary to carry out an analytical procedure. Some of these reagent kits are well designed and reliable while others are not and should be discarded.

A good reagent kit should have the following characteristics (information)

  1. The name of the reagent which corresponds to the intended analysis.
  2. An insert which should have the following information:-
    (a) Clinical significance of the analyte to be investigated using the reagent kit
    (b) Test principle
    (c) The specimen/s of choice to be used ( either whole blood, serum, plasma,urine,csf etc)
    (b) Methodology
    (c) Instructions on the reagent reconstitution if necessary
    (d) Linearity range of the test ie the lowest and the highest value that can be achieved during analysis.
    (e)Reference range of the analyte for the population used during the development of the kit
    (f) Statement regarding accuracy and precision to be expected
    (g) Description of any formula to be and a sample calculation eg OD TEST/OD STD x [STD]
    (h) Should have unit conversion factors (if indicated)
    (i) A performance evaluation from a reputable laboratory
    (j) The expected number of tests which can be achieved with the kit
  3. The date of the manufacturing of the kit.
  4. The expiring date of the kit
  5. The production batch number
  6. Good and user friendly packaging
  7. The storage conditions
  8. The exact volume of the contained reagents

Specimen Collection, Handling and Processing

Health providers use laboratory test results to diagonize pathological disorders, to evaluate its severity, to monitor its progress or response to treatment and to screen for disease in the healthy individuals.

Many factors besides disease affect the composition of the body fluids, these factors are pre-analytical based whilest those that affect the quality of result produced by a laboratory may be analytical or post analytical based.

The variability of test results due to biological factors is often greater than the analytical factors and the control of these biological factors should begin with proper preparation of the client/ patient prior to specimen collection.

Pre-Analytical Errors

NB/ These errors are known to occur before the specimen reaches the analytical area. They include:-

  • (a) Specimen from the wrong patient
  • (b) Incorrectly labeled bottles
  • (c) Blood taken from a vein close to intravenous infusion
  • (d) Prolonged venous occlusion of the upper arm
  • (e) Transferring blood from one tube to another, eg, EDTA to heparin to make up for a shortfall.
  • (f) Haemolysis of blood specimens
  • (g) Delay in a blood specimen reaching the laboratory
  • (h) In function tests a common source of error is the failure to empty the bladder completely or an incomplete or inaccurately timed collection of urine or faeces.
  • (i) Some drugs may interfere with certain analysis. If this is suspected the laboratory should be informed immediately.
  • (j) Failure of the requesting person to give a brief history about the patients illness.

Analytical Errors

NB/ These errors are known to be contributed by the laboratory personnel. They include:-

  • (a) Use of wrong analytical procedure
  • (b) Failure to calibrate the test
  • (c) failure to run a QC specimen
  • (d) Use of wrong specimen
  • (e) Use of a faulty equipment
  • (f) Failure to observe the incubation temperature where appropriate
  • (g) Wrong calculation of test results . Use of inappropriate formula
  • (h) Use of incorrect volumes for the sample and reagents.

Post-Analytical Errors

NB/ These errors can originate from the laboratory or other sections of the health facility. They include:

  • (a) Transposition (interchanging) of results ie giving the patient the wrong results.
  • (b) Use of the incorrect units of measure
  • (c) Errors can arise when results are given by telephone. Results should always be read back to check them.
  • (d) Delay to dispatch results to the intended destination on time.
  • (e) Incorrect interpretation of laboratory results due to the use of wrong reference values.

Specimen Collection: Blood

Blood for analysis may be collected from veins, arteries and capillaries. Veinuos blood is the most commonly and usually the specimen of choice and is collected by a method referred to as venipunture. In young children, skin puncture is frequently used to obtain what is predominantly capillary blood. Arterial blood is collected for blood gases analysis.

An evacuated blood tube, needle and a syringe are usually used for collection of blood specimens. Before bleeding , the phlebotomist should verify the patients identity, the patient should be seated or supine if seating is not feasible and should be in that position 20 min before the specimen is drawn. Standardization of the patients position minimizes differences in [ ] of blood constituents caused by postural variations in blood volume( hemoconcentrations).

The skin is cleaned with 70% isopropanol and allowed to dry, a tourniquet is applied (10-15) cm above the intended site of puncture so as to obstruct the return of blood to the heart band in the process distend the vein.

Items for the collection of blood are-(1) vacuatainer tube (2) tube holder (3) a needle for use with multiple tubes intended for same patient..

There are two classes of vacuatainer tubes ie those with a serum separating material and those without. Various anticoagulants and preservatives may be added to these tubes. The stopper colour denotes the type of the additives eg

The release of intracellular components into the serum is prevented by the barrier for several hours or some cases for several days.

Before venipunture the phlebotomist selects the appropriate tubes and needles (gauges 19-22) and they must be sterile and sharp.

VENOUS OCCLUTION ----- It is rarely necessary to leave the tourniquet in place for more than 1 min . When the flow of blood in the large vein is obstructed by a tourniquet, the filtration pressure across the capillary wall is increased. This increase causes fluids and compounds with molecular weight less than 5000 mwt to pass through the capillary wall. Slight changes might occur if left for 1 min but marked changes have been observed after 3 min . EG protein will increase by 4.9 g/l, ast(9.3), total choleterol(5.1), bili(8.4), iron(6.7) and potassium (< 6.2). The composition of blood drawn first will be the most representative of the circulating blood.

Stress associated with blood collection can have affects in patients at any age. Cortisol and growth hormone concentration in plasma may increase. Particularly in young children who are frightened , struggling and held in physical restraint, collection may cause adrenal stimulation leading to an increase in plasma glucose concentration increase or increase in activities of enzymes that originate in the skeletal muscles.

Vigorous sunction on a syringe during collection or forceful transfer from the syringe to the receiving vessel may cause haemolysis of blood.

(B) Skin Puncture

This is done when only a small amount of blood is required. However this procedure is time consuming and the risk of infection is higher than that of venipunture since the finger tip is more difficult to sterilize than the anticubital fossa. . Care should be taken when prick to avoid reaching the bone. The depth should be less than 2.5 cm. . The finger should be held in such way that gravity assists the collection of blood on the finger tip . Massage of the finger to stimulate blood flow should be avoided. .For an infant less than 1 hear old , the lateral and medial plantar surface of the foot should be used for skin puncture. For the collection of blood on filter paper for neonatal screening, the skin is cleaned and punctured. Then the filter paper is gently touched against a very large drop of blood and allowed to soak and fill the marked area.

(C) Arterial Puncture

This procedure requires considerable skills and it is usually performed by the clinician, or specially trained nurse technician. The preferred site puncture are:- radial artery at at the wrist, brachial artery in the elbow, and femoral artery in the groin.

Anti Coagulant and Preservatives for Blood

  • If whole blood or plasma is required for analysis, an anticoagulant must be added to the specimen during collection time.
  • Whole blood is rarely required for clinical chemistry tests, only for blood gases, lactate and glycosylated haemoglobin. Serum from coagulated blood is the choice for many analytes
  • The use of plasma is usually required for emergency cases since the blood has to wait for 15-30 min for completion of coagulation before centrifugation.
  • The other advantage is that plasma yield from a given volume of whole blood is always greater than the yield of serum.
  • The only disadvantage is the formation of fibrous clots or fragments during plasma storage and subsequent risk of clogging the sample probe of autoanalyzer.
  • HEPARIN-----is the most widely used anticoagulant for clinical chemical tests and causes least interferences . It acts by preventing the conversion of prothrombin to thrombin and thus the formation of fibrous fibrinogen. Types of heparin anticoagulant are ammonium and lithium heparin .
  • ETHYLENEDIAMINE TETRA-ACETIC ACID (EDTA)---- This is a chelating agent and in concentration of 1-2 mg/ml of blood. It is used for haematological examination since it preserves cellular components of blood. It is used as disodium or dipotassium salt and the later is more soluble. It prevents coagulation by binding calcium , which is essential for the clotting process.. In clinical chemistry is used to collect blood for GLYCOSYLATED HAEMOGLOBIN (HBA1C) analysis.
  • SODIUM FLUORIDE--- This is the best preservative of blood glucose, together with an anticoagulant such as potassium, sodium, ammonium or lithium oxalate, . It also acts as a weak anticoagulant. It exerts its preservative action by inhibiting the enzyme system involved in glycolysis. It is also an inhibitor of urease and many other enzymes. Glucose in blood specimen without antiglycolytic agent will decrease as much as 10 mg/dl (0.56 mmol/l) per hour at 25 ¬∞C. The decrease is even more in infants due to their increased metabolic activities of rbcs and in leukemia pts due to their high metabolic activity of the leucocytes. Sodium iodoacetate at a concentration of 2 mg/ ml of blood is an effective antiglycolytic agent and a substitute for sodium fluoride . It has no effect on urease and can be used when urea and glucose are requested in the same specimen.. It inhibits only creatine kinase and has no significant effect on the other parameters.

Haemolysis

This is the breakdown of rbcs. Serum or plasma shows haemolysis when the [hb] exceeds 20 mg/dl. Slight haemolysis has little effect on most of the values. Severe haemolysis causes a slight dilutional effect on the constituent present at a lower concentration in the rbcs than in the plasma. However a marked effect may be obtained on those constituents that are present at a higher concentration in the erythrocytes than in the plasma. Eg potassium, alp, ldh, mg, aldolase and INpo4 . IF an electrophoresis is done on an heamolized specimen, an additional band due to hb appears = fibrinogen band..

Causes of Haemolysis

  • use of wet blood containers.
  • Excess preservative
  • Pushing through the neddle when transferring it from the syringes into blood container.
  • Spending a lot of time during bleeding..
  • Shaking the blood container vigorously.
  • Separating the blood immediately after bleeding..

Urine Collection

  • The type of urine specimen to be collected is dictated by the tests to be performed.
  • Generally urine for quantitative analysis must be collected over an interval of time eg 1,4 , or 24 hours.
  • Untimed or random urine specimen are suitable for only very few biochemical tests. A clean, early morning specimen is generally the most concentrated and this is prepared for routine examination.eg testing for unusual compounds such as beta chorionic gonadotropin.
  • A clean timed urine specimen is the one obtained at specific times of the day or during certain phases of micturition. A double voided specimen is the urine excreted during a timed period following a complete emptying of the bladder . It is used to assess glucose excretion during a glucose tolerance test procedure. Its collection must be timed in relation to the ingestion of glucose.
  • When specimen must be collected over a specified period of time, the patient must adhere closely to the instructions with regard to diet or medication , to avoid interference of ingested compounds with analytical procedures.
  • Appropriate information regarding the collection including warnings with respect to handling of the specimen, should appear on the bottle label.
  • Urine cannot be collected at the same time for two or more tests for which different procedures are required.
  • The removal of aliquots is not permissible even when the volume removed is measured and corrected for , since the excretion rate of most compounds varies throughout the day and the effect of the removal of some specimen on the total concentration cannot be corrected.

Urine Preservatives

Preservatives of urine specimens have different roles but are usually added to:-

  • (i) reduce bacterial action or chemical decomposition.
  • (ii) solubilize constituents that might otherwise precipitate out of solution.
  • (iii) decrease atmospheric oxidationof unstable cpds.

Some specimens should not have any preservative added bcoz of the possibility of interference with analytical methods.
-NB/ one of the most satisfactory form of urine preservation is refrigeration during collection. Other preservatives are :-
(i) concentrated HCL ACID (10 ML) = 6 mol/L
(ii) sulfamic acid
(iii) thymol
(iv) chloroform
(v) toluene----- this is the only organic solvent that is still in use as a preservative.
- When timed collection is complete the specimen must be delivered to the laboratory without delay where the volume is measured using a graduated cylinder.

Other Specimen Types

Faeces (Stool)

  • Small portions of faeces are frequently analyzed to detect the presence of occult blood (hidden), which is recognized as one of the most effective clues to the presence of a bleeding ulcer or malignant dse in the gastro-intestinal tract. The patient is instructed to abstain from eating proteinous foods three days prior to the collection of the specimen.
  • 72 hour faeces specimen is collected for the estimation of faecal fat ( to diagonize malabsorption disorders ).

Cerebral Spinal Fluid (CSF)

  • Obtained from the puncture of the lumber region
  • Lumber puncture are done by the physicians.
  • it is requested where there is suspicion of a cerebro-vascular accident, meningitis, demyelinating dse, or menengial involvement in malignant dse.
  • The csf specimen is collected in fluoride bottles for the analysis of micro protein and glucose.

Synovial Fluid

  • Obtained from the joint to aid in the characterization of arthritis and differentiation of non- inflammatory effusions from inflammatory effusions. Normally only a small amount is present in any joint , but the fluid is increased in the presence of inflammatory conditions.. The technique of removing the fluid from the joints is referred to as arthrocentesis which is done by a physician.. The fluid is examined for proteins and glucose.
  • Amniocentesis is the procedure for removing amniotic fluid for prenatal diagnosis of congenital disorders, to assess fetal maturity or detect intrauterine infection.

Pleural/ Ascitic/Pericardial Fluid Collection

  • Pleural, pericardial and peritoneal cavities normally contain a small amount of fluid that lubricates the opposing parietal and visceral membrane surfaces.
  • Inflammation or infections affecting the cavities causes the fluids to accumulate.
  • The tests analyzed are proteins and glucose.
  • The removal of the specimen is done by the physician and the procedure is referred to as paracentesis but when specially applied to pleural cavity is called thoracentesis and for pericardial cavity is referred to as cardiocentesis.

Handling, Storage and Transportation

Handling of Specimens

A quality result will depend on a quality specimen which has been well collected, preserved, transported and well identified. The information on the label should have the following:-
(i) pt name,
(ii) location
(iii) identity number
(iv) date and time of collection
(v) type specimen.

Separation and Storage

  • Serum or plasma should be separated from the cells as soon as possible and certainly within 2 hours from the collection time. If this is not possible the specimen should be kept at room temperature rather than at 4¬∞C to avoid haemolysis.
  • Separated serum or plasma can be placed at 4¬∞C for later analysis. If analysis is to be carried out in two weeks time, the specimens can be stored at -20¬∞C. or at -70¬∞C if it is to be analyzed in future.
  • Specimens are supposed to be centrifuged with the stoppers (caps) in place to reduce evaporation and to prevent aerosolization of infectious particles.

Transportation of Specimen to Reference Laboratory

  • It should be assumed that transport from a referring lab. To a referral lab. may take as long as 72 hrs . Referring lab. should retain some specimen under optimal conditions for retesting should an unanticipated problem arise during shipment.
  • for transportation of frozen or refrigerated specimen, a Styrofoam container should be used . Dry ice is the most convenient refrigerant material for maintaining specimen frozen temperature as low as -70¬∞C can be achieved. The amount of dry ice required depends on the size of container, insulation efficiency and time for freezing.

Criteria Used for Accepting or Rejecting Specimen

  1. All specimens must be brought to the laboratory in the correct container.
  2. The must be accompanied with a duly filled request form. The following information must be given:-
    (i) patient/ client name -- identification
    (ii) type of specimen
    (iii) location of patient/ client (address)
    (iv) time and date of collection.
    (v) signature and name of the person ordering the tests
    (vi) urgency of the tests
    (vii) the request / report form should be clean ie not soiled (if soiled reject the specimen.
    (viii) specimen which is grossly haemolyzed should be rejected.

Biochemical Reference Values

Introduction

  • The interpretation of any clinical laboratory analytical test report involves an important concept in comparing the patient's results to the test's reference range. A health provider compares the given laboratory data with the given reference range of the particular analyte which then aid in making a diagnosis or in managing a pathological disorder.
  • In any qualitative analytical reporting the patient's test result is either indicated as positive or negative.
  • For the quantitative analytical reporting the patient's result's is followed by a reference range. The term reference range is preferred over normal range because the reference population can be clearly defined. Rather than implying that the test results are being compared with ill-defined concept of "normal", the reference range means the results are being considered in the most relevant context.
  • When test results from different populations are examined it is quickly discovered that what is normal for one group is not necessarily normal for another group. For example, pregnancy changes many aspects of the body's chemistry so pregnant women have their own set of reference ranges.

Establishment and Types of Reference Range

  • According to IFCC (International Federation Of Clinical Chemists), reference ranges are constructed from 95% of a healthy population.
  • There are two types of reference ranges namely: the group (population) and subjects based reference ranges.
  • In group (population) based, the health provider uses these (reference ranges) by estimating the probability that the client from whom the given laboratory report was obtained belongs to a group of healthy subjects from whom the reference ranges were obtained.
  • If the reference values are from the same client when he/she was in a defined state of health, the health provider compares the present value with the past values to estimate whether the client is still in the same state of health eg managing a diabetic patient.

Methods of Establishment:

Reference ranges can be constructed using two methods ie direct and indirect. Direct method involves the use of data obtained from a healthy population accessible to hospital, such as blood donors. A lot of work has been done in the production of reference ranges using direct methods. The indirect method involves the use of data obtained from patients' records. This data is used with the assumption that the majority of values come from subjects whose values are compatible with those of healthy subjects (in the sense that their values have not been altered by disease)). eg a client admitted for elective surgery.

Factors Affecting Reference Ranges

In the management of reference ranges it is very important to consider the affect the following factors have on reference ranges. These factors are:-

  • Age,
  • sex,
  • race,
  • geographical location,
  • instruments,
  • analytical methods,
  • sample type
  • diet

Reference ranges of some biochemical parameter differ between male and female. The cause of this is due to different physiological activities.

  • A woman has comparatively lower uric acid levels than a man due to the fact that uric acid clearance by the kidney is more in a woman than a man.

RACE

EXAMPLES

Quality Assurance in Clinical Chemistry

  • All laboratory staff must be conscious of how the quality of their work affects medical diagnosis and treatment of patients.
  • The staff should ensure that the product of the laboratory ie tests, are of good quality and beneficial to the clients (patients) and health providers.
  • Erroneous laboratory data (results) can lead to unnecessary medical treatment, prolonged hospital stay and missed or wrong diagnosis.
  • In order to avoid all these, it is important therefore to introduce a system which will control the quality of data (results) produced in the laboratory.
  • The system put in place is referred to as quality assurance system .
  • Quality assurance system is a broad spectrum of plans, policies and procedures that together provide an administrative structure for laboratory efforts to achieve quality goals.
  • A quality assurance program involves virtually everything and everybody in the laboratory. An error in any step during acquisition, processing and analysis of specimens and the reporting of a laboratory test result can invalidate the quality of the analysis and cause the laboratory to fall short of its quality goals.

There are several essential elements of a good quality assurance program. These are:-commitment, facilities and resources, technical competence, quality assurance procedures, problem solving mechanism.

  1. COMMITMENT
    Dedication to quality service must be central. Quality must be a major consideration in all management decisions because any single decision may compromise other plans and practice for attaining quality goals.
  2. FACILITIES AND RESOURCES,
    laboratories must have adequate space , equipments , materials, supplies, staff and supervisory personnel and budgetary resources. The facilities and resources are the basis upon which quality services can be developed and maintained.
  3. TECHNICAL COMPETENCE
    High quality personnel are essential for high quality services. The educational background and experience of all personnel are important as is the capability for providing in-services training to develop and maintain skills and knowledge.
  4. QUALITY ASSURANCE PROCEDURES
    This can be divided into three groups:-
    (a) Control of pre-analytical variables;- such as test request, patient preparation and identification, specimen acquisition and transport, specimen processing and distribution, preparation of work list and record maintainenance.
    (b) control of analytical variables:- This includes analytical methodology, standardization/calibration procedures
    (c) monitoring of analytical quality by the use of statistical methods and QC charts.
  5. PROBLEM SOLVING MECHANISM
    This involves the link between the identification of a problem and the implementation of a solution to solve that problem. In this case of problem solving, teams must be established

Activities Involved in a Good and Complete Quality Control Program

These interrelated activities includes:- assessment, maintenance and improvements

(A) ASSESSMENT

For an operational laboratory, the quality data being produced must be assessed and evaluated. Two terms that are important in this regard are ACCURACY and PRECISION

ACCURACY

  • This refers to how close a measurement is to the true value. This true value is also referred to as the actual, correct, most expected, the target value or the right value.
  • The closer the measured value is to the true value, the greater is the accuracy.

PRECISION

  • This refers to how close together are multiple measurements of the same sample. The question here is whether the same result will be obtained when the test is repeated.
  • Precision can also refer to reproducibility or the agreement between replicate measurements. This means that the results of repeated analysis on the same sample are all very close to each other .
  • It is worth to note that demonstrated precision is not a guarantee of accuracy

MAINTENANCE AND IMPROVEMENT

  • To ensure quality data from the laboratory, accuracy and precision must always be maintained. This is achieved by the use of quality control materials. The quality control material must always be analyzed before any analysis of specimens is commenced.
  • Improvement of a good quality control program is achieved by taking the necessary remedy whenever anything that can compromise quality is detected.
  • the laboratory must have quality reagents, good working equipments and skilled personnel in order to maintain and improve on the quality of test results produced by the clinical laboratory.

Internal QC Program (IQC)

DEFINITION

  • This can be defined as the set of procedures undertaken in a laboratory for the continual assessment of work carried out within the laboratory and the evaluation of the results of tests to decide whether the latter are reliable enough to be released to the requesting health provider.

CHARACTERISTICS

  • (a)-must be done always when an analytical test is being carried out.
  • (b)- must be done before the patients specimens are analysed
  • (c)-when many specimens are set for analysis, it can be included within the run.
  • (d) -has a lower and upper value ie has a given range which represents mean +/_2 SD
  • (e)- must be treated the same way as test sample during the analysis.

External Quality Control (EQC) Program

DEFINITION

  • This is a retrospective comparison of performance with a pre-set criteria or with other laboratories. The aim of EQC is to assess the degree of harmonizationof results between clinical laboratories_

CHARACTERISTICS

  • (a) This is organized by an external agency that may be nationally or internationally based.
  • (b)Lyophilized material is suitable for this purpose
  • (c) the EQC material can be of two forms ie blind or known .Blind material (blind QC serum) means that the QC ranges are not given but are determined by the participating laboratories .It is upon the results given by these laboratories that their performance is determined by the external agent. On the other hand the known qc is similar to the internal QC but the results have to be sent back to the external agent for statistical analysis and interpretation .
  • (d) -the EQC materials are analyzed periodically eg once in every 3 months
  • (e) -all participating laboratories are expected to perform all the requested tests and send the results back to the organizing centre(agent)

STANDARDS FOR HANDLING PROFICIENCY TESTING SPECIMENS ARE AS FOLLOWS:

  • PT samples must be tested with the laboratory's regular patient load.
  • PT samples must be tested the same number of times that patients' samples are tested routinely.
  • Laboratories participating in PT programs must not engage in interlaboratory comparison of PT sample results.
  • Laboratories may not send PT samples to another laboratory for analysis.
  • Laboratories must document all steps of processing for PT samples.
  • PT is required for only the primary method used for testing of analytes in patients' samples during the period covered by the PT event.

In return for their participation, the laboratory will receive the following information:

  • results for each analyte sample;
  • mean result for each analyte;
  • standard deviation of results by the comparative method;
  • number of laboratories using the same method;
  • standard deviation index (SDI); and
  • lower and upper limits of acceptability of results.

PT results that are between the lower and upper limits of acceptability are considered satisfactory.

External quality control serves several purposes, including:

  • evaluating the internal quality control program;
  • detecting errors in a lab's methods, including technical errors; and
  • providing a comparison of testing methods, which is useful in selecting new methods.
