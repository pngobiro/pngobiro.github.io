Body Fluids and Electrolytes Balance

The main fluid in the body is water. Total body water is 60% of body weight whilst the other 40% is made up of fats, protein, carbohydrates and minerals.

The distribution of the body water is as follows:-

  • Intracellular fluid which makes up 40% or ( 2/3 ) of the body water.
  • The extracellular fluid makes up the remainder 20% or (1/3) of the body water and consists of: Interstitial fluid - ( fluid between the cells),-Plasma and lymph -(the intra-vascular fluid, - Cerebrospinal fluid ( found in ventricles of brain and surrounding the brain and spinal cord), - G.I. tract fluids (gastric, pancreatic and intestinal juices), - Synovial fluid (found inside synovial joint capsules), - Eye and ear fluids ( aqueous and vitreous humours, perilymph and endolymph.), - Pleural, pericardial and peritoneal fluids and Glomerular filtrate.

Water moves from one compartment to another due to osmosis. Osmosis is chiefly influenced by dissolved solutes in fluids. Selectively permeable membranes separate body fluids into distinct compartments. Plasma membranes of individual cells separate ICF from ECF and blood vessel walls separate blood plasma from interstitial fluid.

The major components of these fluids include water and solutes. The solute is mostly comprised of electrolytes: inorganic compounds that dissociate into ions. Electrolytes include cations (positively charged atoms - sodium, potassium, calcium, magnesium, carbonate) and anions (negatively charged atoms - chloride, sulphide, phosphate, bicarbonate).

Sources of Body Fluids

  1. Preformed water represents about 2,300 ml/day of our daily intake. This is the water found in our food and drink.
  2. Metabolic water is produced through the catabolic breakdown of nutrients occurring during cellular respiration, This amounts to about 200 ml/day .
  3. Combining preformed and metabolic water gives us a total daily intake of 2,500 ml.
02

Fluid Balance Regulation

Fluid Balance - When each compartment of the body contains the appropriate concentration of water and electrolytes, the body is said to be in fluid balance. Normally, there is a balance achieved between our total daily intake and output of water.

Regulation of Fluid Gain

The body regulates water intake via the thirst reflex which stimulates us to drink. When water loss is greater than water gain the body reaches a state of dehydration, and dehydration stimulates the thirst reflex in three ways:

  1. the level of saliva drops resulting in a dry mucosa in the mouth and pharynx;
  2. there is an increase in blood osmotic pressure which stimulates osmoreceptors in the hypothalamus;
  3. there is a drop in blood volume, which leads to the renin/angiotensinII pathway stimulating the thirst centre in the hypothalamus.

All three mechanisms stimulate the thirst centre in the hypothalamus resulting in the sensation of thirst and causing us to drink to increase fluid volume. Drinking inhibits the thirst centre by stretching the stomach and intestines and reducing the osmotic pressure of the blood.

Regulation of Fluid Loss

If fluid loss regulation is considered as the maintenance of the volume of fluid already present in the body, then three hormones play a key role: Antidiuretic Hormone (ADH), Aldosterone, and Atrial Natriuretic Peptide (ANP).

  • ADH: A drop in body fluid results in an increase in blood tonicity and a decrease in blood volume which in turn causes the release of renin in the kidneys and stimulation of osmoreceptors in the hypothalamus. The hypothalamus causes ADH to be released from the posterior pituitary gland. ADH targets the kidneys, and sudoriferous glands reducing fluid loss through each, and it also causes arterioles to constrict.
  • Renin/Angiotensin/Aldosterone: Renin is released by the juxtaglomerular cells in the kidneys, it acts on angiotensinogen a plasma protein produced in the liver to form angiotensin I. Angiotensin I is converted in the lungs to the active hormone angiotensin II by the imaginatively named angiotensin converting enzyme (ACE). Angiotensin II causes: vasoconstriction of the arterioles; stimulation of the release of aldosterone by the adrenal cortex; stimulation of the thirst centre in the hypothalamus; and stimulation of the release of more ADH. Aldosterone increases the retention of sodium and chloride ions as well as water by the kidneys.
  • ANP: On the other hand an increase in body fluid results in an increase in blood volume. This causes stretching of the right atrium, which stimulates the release of ANP a hormone that increases the loss of fluid in the urine. At the same time the level of ADH and renin is decreased under negative feedback.

Fluid Imbalances

(1) Fluid Volume Deficit (FVD)

Deficiency in both the amount of water and electrolytes in the ECF where water and electrolyte proportions remain near normal (commonly known as hypovolemia). Occurs as a result of: -(A) abnormal losses through the skin, gastrointestinal tract, or kidney (B) decreased intake of fluid (C) bleeding (D)a shift of fluid into a third space ie the shift of fluid from the intravascular space into an area where it is not readily accessible as ECF, e.g.: sequestered in the bowel, in the interstitial spaces as edema, in inflamed tissue, in potential spaces such as the peritoneal or pleural cavities . Nb/ the patient with a shift of fluid into a third space may not manifest signs/symptoms of fluid volume deficit

(2) Fluid Volume Excess (FVE)

Excessive retention of water and sodium in similar proportions to normal ECF (commonly known as hypervolemia). Occurs as a result of: (A) excessive intake of sodium chloride (B) administering sodium-containing infusions too rapidly, particularly to patients with impaired regulatory mechanisms (C) disease processes that alter regulatory mechanisms, such as congestive heart failure, renal failure, cirrhosis of the liver, and Cushing's syndrome.

(3) Dehydration

Dehydration is a deficit of body water that results when the output of water exceeds intake. Dehydration stimulates the thirst mechanism, instigating water consumption. Sweating and the output of urine both decrease. If water intake continues to fall short of water loss, dehydration worsens.

Causes of dehydration may include: vomiting, diarrhea, diuretics, excessive heat, excessive sweating, fever, decreased water intake.

Overhydration

Overhydration is an excess of body water that results when water intake exceeds output. Drinking large amounts of water does not typically lead to overhydration if the kidneys, heart, and pituitary gland are functioning properly. Excessive body water causes electrolytes in the blood, including sodium, to become overly diluted.

03

Electrolytes

The role of electrolytes is manifold.There are almost no metabolic processes that are not dependent on or affected by electrolytes. Among functions of electrolytes are:-

  • (a) maintenance of osmotic pressure and water distribution in various body fluid compartments.
  • (b) maintenance of proper body ph
  • (c) regulation of proper heart and other muscle functions
  • (d) involvement in oxidation-reduction (electron transfer) reactions
  • (e) participation in catalysis as cofactors for enzymes

Sodium (Na+)

Chief electrolyte in the ECF representing 90% of the total cations per liter of plasma water. Thus sodium ions are responsible for almost half the osmolality of the plasma.

Functions: (a) regulating ECF volume and distribution, (b) maintaining blood volume, (c) transmitting nerve impulses and contracting muscles

DETERMINATION OF SODIUM IN BODY FLUIDS: Is estimated through flame photometry, ion selective electrodes(ISE) or colorometric method.

Reference range for serum sodium as quoted in literature = 136-148 mmol/l.

Potassium (K+)

This is the chief electrolyte in the intracellular fluid. In tissue cell its average concentration is 150 mmol/l. In the erythrocytes the concentration is 105 mmol/l, approximately 23 times that of the plasma..High intracellular concentration are maintained because potassium difuses only slowlyoutward through the cell membrane whereas the sodium- potassium -ATP pump continually transports potassiuminto the cell.

Functions: (a) maintaining ICF osmolality, (b) transmitting nerve and other chemical impulses, (c) regulating cardiac impulse transmission and muscle contraction, (d) skeletal and smooth muscle function, (e) regulating acid-base balance

DETERMINATION OF POTASSIUM IN BODY FLUIDS: Is estimated through, ion selective electrodes(ISE) or colorometric method.

Reference range for serum potassium as quoted in literature = 3.5-5.2 mmol/l.

Chloride (Cl-)

This is the major extracellular anion and represents the largest fraction of the plasma total anion concentration of approximately 154 mmol/l. It is present in the blood, interstitial fluid and in minute amount in the ICF.

Functions: (a)hydrochloric acid (HCL) production, (b)regulating ECF balance and vascular volume, (c) regulating acid-base balance, (d)buffer in oxygen-carbon dioxide exchange in red blood cells (RBCs)

DETERMINATION OF CHLORIDE IN SERUM:, ion selective electrodes(ISE) or colorometric method.

Reference range for serum chloride as quoted in literature = 95-105 mmol/l.

Bicarbonate (HCO3-)

Chief chemical base buffer within the body present in both the ECF and ICF. Average daily requirement is not known. Unlike other electrolytes that must be consumed in the diet, adequate amounts of bicarbonate are produced through metabolic processes to meet the body's needs

Functions: (a)chief chemical base buffer involved in acid-base balance, (b)essential component of the carbonic acid-bicarbonate buffering system

DETERMINATION OF bicarbonate IN SERUM: colorometric method.

Reference range for serum bicarbonate as quoted in literature = 23-29 MMOL/L

Fluid and Electrolyte Movement

There are four ways in which fluid and electrolytes move in the body:-osmosis,diffusion, active transport and filtration.

(1) OSMOSIS

This is the movement of a solvent across a selectively permeable cell membrane from an area of higher concentration of solutes to an area of lower concentration of solutes until equilibrium is established. Water is the primary solvent that holds solutes (substances) in solution in the body. The concentration of these solutes in the body fluids is osmolality, whichis determined by the total solute concentrationwithin a a fluid compartment. Osmolality is measured as parts of solute per kilogram of water and is reported as milliosmols per lkg (mOsmol/L). The osmolality of plasma is 275-295 mOsmol/L. The greatest determinant of osmolality within a fluid compartment is sodium concentration.

(2) DIFFUSION

This is the movement of solutes across a selectively permeable cell membrane from an area of higher concentration of solutes to an area of lower concentration of solutes until equilibrium is established . "Coasting downhill"--- diffusion is affected by:

  • (A) the size of the solutes:-. Larger solutes move less quickly and, consequently, have a lower rate of diffusion WHILST smaller solutes move more quickly and, consequently, have a higher rate of diffusion
  • (B) the concentration of the solutes :-solutes move from an area of higher concentration of solutes to an area of lower concentration of solutes
  • (C) the temperature of the solutes:- increases in temperature increase the rate of motion of solutes and, consequently, leads to a higher rate of diffusion WHILST decreases in temperature decrease the rate of motion of solutes and, consequently, leads to a lower rate of diffusion

(3) ACTIVE TRANSPORT

Movement of solutes across a selectively permeable cell membrane, usually against a pressure gradient and with the expenditure of metabolic energy(ATP), from an area of higher concentration of solutes to an area of lower concentration of solutes until equilibrium is established . "Pumping uphill"

(4) FILTRATION

Movement of solutes and solvent across a permeable cell membrane from an area of higher concentration of solutes to an area of lower concentration of solutes until equilibrium is established. This is influenced by two pressures ie colloid osmotic, (or oncotic pressure) and the hydrostatic pressure. Oncotic pressure is that pressure that prevents water from moving out from the intravascular to extravascular compartments to maintain vascular volume whilst hydrostatic pressure is that pressure that moves water outward from the intravascular to extravascular compartments

Electrolyte Imbalances

Electrolyte imbalance ----This occurs when the levels of the body electrolytes are either elevated (increased) or reduced (decreased) in the ECF.

‚ñ∂ Sodium Imbalances

(1) HYPONATREMIA ---sodium deficit or (reduced levels of sodium) in the ECF, or serum sodium level less than 135 mmol/l (reference range lower limit).
*Signs/symptoms :- anorexia, nausea and vomiting , lethargy, confusion , muscle cramps muscular twitching, seizures and coma

(2) HYPERNATREMIA --sodium excess (or elevated levels of sodium) in the ECF, or serum sodium level greater than 145 mmol/l (reference range upper limit).
*Signs/symptoms :-thirst , elevated body temperature, tongue dry and swollen , sticky mucus membranes AND in severe hypernatremia: the symptom/signs are:-disorientation ,hallucinations , lethargy when undisturbed , irritable and hyperactive , focal or grand mal seizures

‚ñ∂ Potassium Imbalances

(2)HYPOKALEMIA ----potassium deficit (reduced )in the ECF, or serum potassium level less than 3.5 mmol/l (reference range lower limit).
* Signs/symptoms :-fatigue, anorexia, nausea, and vomiting, muscle weakness, decreased bowel motility, cardiac arrythmias, increased sensitivity to digitalis, polyuria, nocturia, dilute urine, postural hypotension, ECG changes, paresthesias or tender muscles

(3) HYPERKALEMIA ---potassium excess (elevateLevels) in the ECF, or serum potassium level greater than 5.2 mmol/L.
*Signs/symptoms ----vague muscle weakness, cardiac arrythmias , paresthesias of the face, tongue, feet, and hands, flaccid muscle paralysis, GIT symptoms such as nausea, intermittent intestinal colic, or diarrhea may occur

(4) HYPOCHLOREMIA AND HYPERCHLOREMIA are very rare conditions since most of the foodsconsumed has cholide and the body is able to control any deficiency or excesses of this ion successively.

* Signs/symptoms -neuromuscular irritability , increased reflexes , coarse tremors , convulsions cardiac manifestations tachyarrythmias, increases susceptibility for digitalis toxicity, mental changes, disorientation, mood changes

(8) HYPERMAGNESEMIA--- magnesium excess in the ECF, or serum magnesium level greater than 1.1 MMOL/L .

(9)HYPOPHOSPHATEMIA --phosphate deficit in the ECF, or serum phosphate level less than 0.83 MMOL/L.

Blood Gases and Acid/Base Balance

Metabolic processes of the body results with the production of large amounts of carbonic acids ($\text{H}_2\text{CO}_3$), sulfuric, phosphoric and other acids. These products of metabolism are transported to the excretory organs (lungs and kidney) via the ECF and the blood with minimal change in the plasma PH. This is accomplished by the combined functions of the buffer systems of the blood and the mechanisms of respiratory and renal systems.

The gases commonly measured are $\text{O}_2$ and $\text{CO}_2$. Although small amounts of these gases exist as dissolved gases in the blood , they are for the most part , chemically combined with other substances. $\text{CO}_2$ exist chiefly as $\mathbf{HCO}_{3}^{-}$ while most of $\mathbf{O}_2$ is bound to haemoglobin to form oxyhaemoglobin. The PH of blood is dependent on the relative amounts of $\mathbf{HCO}_{3}^{-}$ and carbonic acid ($\mathbf{H}_2\mathbf{CO}_{3}$) which are present in the blood.

Respiration

There are two types of respiration ie EXTERNAL AND INTERNAL RESPIRATION.

  • EXTERNAL RESPIRATION---This is the term given for the exchange of oxygen and carbon dioxide in the lungs between alveolar air and blood
  • INTERNAL $\text{O}_2$ and $\text{CO}_2$ RESPIRATION the oxygen in the blood is exchanged with the carbon dioxide in the cells.

$\text{O}_2$ and $\text{CO}_2$ in the Blood

Carbon dioxide is transported in the blood in several forms and the three most abundant and important are :
a) bicarbonate ---in plasma and rbcs
b) carbamino haemoglobin-----in rbcs
c) dissolved carbondioxide ($\text{dCO}_2$) ---in fluid of rbcs and plasma

In plasma, a small portion of dissolved cardon dioxide reacts with plasma water to form carbonic acid and bicardonate

$$ \text{CO}_2 + \text{H}_2\text{O} \leftrightarrow \text{CD} \leftrightarrow \text{H}_2\text{CO}_3 \leftrightarrow \text{H}^+ + \text{HCO}_3^- $$

(CD = carbonate dehydratase)

Nb/ (1) the kidney plays an important role in maintaining the circulating $\text{HCO}_{3}^{-}$ and eliminating the circulating H+ from circulation
(2) The CD (CARBONATE DEHYDRATASE) is present in high concentration in rbcs and renal tubular cells therefore the organs concerned with acid/base balance are rbcs and tubular cells.

The Relationship Between pH, $\text{HCO}_3^-$ and $\text{H}_2\text{CO}_3$

This relationship is explained by the HENDERSON-HASSELBALCH equation.

$$ \text{pH} = \text{pK} + \log \frac{[\text{HCO}_3^-]}{[\text{H}_2\text{CO}_3]} $$

Nb/ The pK of $\text{H}_2\text{CO}_3$ at 37¬∞c is 6.1 thus:

$$ \text{pH} = 6.1 + \log \frac{[\text{HCO}_3^-]}{[\text{H}_2\text{CO}_3]} $$

Normally the $\text{HCO}_3^-$ in blood is approximately 26 mmol/l while the $[\text{H}_2\text{CO}_3]$ is approximately 1.3 mmol/l. . The blood PH can be calculated by inserting these values into the equation

$$ \text{pH} = 6.1 + \log \frac{26}{1.3} $$

Acid/Base Disorders

Acid / base balances disorders can be classified into four groups ie METABOLIC ACIDOSIS and ALKALOSIS and RESPIRATORY ACIDOSIS and ALKALOSIS.

Metabolic Acidosis (Primary Bicarbonate Deficit)

Caused by -: (a) production of organic acids that exceeds the rate of elimination, (b) reduced excretion of acids in renal failure, (c) excessive loss of bicarbonates (base) due to excessive loss of duodenal fluids as in diarrhaea.
Laboratory finding-----plasma bicarbonate, total carbondioxide and partial pressure of carbondioxide and PH are all low.

Metabolic Alkalosis (Primary Bicarbonate Excess)

Caused by :- (a) excess administration of alkali eg $\text{NaHCO}_3$, Citrate (as in transfused blood) or some anti acids. (b) excess loss of HCL from the stomach after prolonged vomiting and after gastric suction. (c) renal bicarbonate retention.
Laboratory finding------plasma bicarbonate, dissolved carbon dioxide and partial pressure of carbondioxide , and PH are all high.

Respiratory Acidosis (Primary Dissolved Carbonic Dioxide Excess)

Any condition that decreases elimination of carbondioxide through the lungs results in an increase of partial pressure of carbondioxide( hypercapnia) and respiratory acidosis.
Laboratory findings:- low PH, High $[\text{H}_2\text{CO}_3]$

Respiratory Alkalosis (Primary Dissolved Carbonic Dioxide Deficit)

A decrease in partial pressure of carbon dioxide ( hypocapnia) caused by an increase in the rate of or depth of respiration or both. Excess elimination of carbon dioxide which causes an increase in $[\text{HCO}_3^-]$ ratio and an increase in PH.
Lab findings- PH high, $[\text{HCO}_3^-]$ is high,

Laboratory Investigation

Arterial Blood Gas (ABG)

An arterial blood gas (ABG) is a blood test that is performed using blood from an artery. The test is used to determine the pH of the blood, the partial pressure of carbon dioxide and oxygen, and the bicarbonate level.

TEST PRINCIPLES: $\text{pH}$, $\text{pCO}_2$, and $\text{pO}_2$ are measured directly from the specimen by using electrodes. The bicarbonate ($\text{cHCO}_3^-(\text{P})_\text{c}$), total $\text{CO}_2$ ($\text{ctCO}_2(\text{P})_\text{c}$) and arterial base excess ($\text{ABE}_\text{c}$) are calculated.

SPECIMEN TYPE: Heparinized Arterial blood is the specimen of choice. Capillary blood collected in heparinized tubes can also be used especially when dealing with neonates. Specimens must be placed on wet ice analyzed within 10 minutes of collection if held at room temperature or within one hour if kept at 4-8¬∞C.

Anion Gap

This is a calculation used to detect altered concentration of ions other than ( sodium, potassium, chloride and bicarbonates.

Therefore the term anion gap refers to the differences in charge concentration between unmeasured anions (UA-) and unmeasured cations(UC+). The anion gap can be calculated using the follwing formula where "A" represent the anion gap

$$ "A" = (\text{Na}^+ + \text{K}^+) - (\text{Cl}^- + \text{HCO}_3^-) $$

REFERENCE RANGE IN LITERATURE OF ANION GAP: (15-20) mmol/L
