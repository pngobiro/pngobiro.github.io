Lipids (Fats) Disorders

Lipids are insoluble (does not dissolve in water but are soluble (dissolves) in alcohol and other solvents. When diatary fats are digested and absorbed into the small intestine, they eventually re-form into triglycerides, which are then packaged into lipoproteins.

Dietary fats, including cholesterol are absorbed from the small intestine and transported into the liver by lipoproteins called CHYLOMICRONS. Chylomicrons are large droplets of lipids with a thin shell of phospholipids, cholesterol, and protein . Once chylomicrons enter the bloodstream , an enzyme called lipoprotein lipase breaks down the tryglycerides into fatty acids and glycerol.After 12-14 hours fast, chylomicrons are absent from the bloodstream. Thus, individuals who are having a lipid profile done should fast overnight to ensure that chylomicrons have been cleared.

Lipoprotein Types

  • The liver removes the chylomicrons fragments and the cholesterol is repackaged for transport in the blood in very low density lipoproteins (VLDL) , which eventually turn into low density lipoprotein(LDL).
  • LDL- cholesterol(LDL-C) ---the bad cholesterol---consist mainly of cholesterol . Most LDL-C particles are absorbed from the bloodstream by receptor cells in the liver. Cholesterol is then transported throughout the cells. Diets high in saturated fats decrease the uptake of LDL particles by the liver. LDL particles are also removed from the blood stream by scavenger cells or macrophages, which are white blood cells that bury themselves in the blood vessels such as arteries. Scavanger cells prevent cholesterol from re-entering the bloodstream, but they deposits the cholesterol in the inner walls of the blood vessels, eventually leading to the development of plaque.
  • High density lipoprotein -cholesterol (HDL-C), are a separate group of lipoproteins that contain more protein and less cholesterol than LDL. HDL-C is also called -good cholesterol--. HDL-C is produced primarily in the liver and intestine, and it travels in the bloodstream, picks up cholesterol, and gives the cholesterol to other lipopteins for transport back to the liver.

HDL-C and LDL-C are the major types of lipids that circulate in the bloodstream. Therefore, a person"s total cholesterol is mostly determined by the amount of the HDL-C and LDL-C in a measured sample of blood.( For example a total cholesterol level of 5.6 mmol/l could be represented by an HDL-C of 3 mmol/l and an LDL-C of 2.6 mmol/l or HDL-C of 2.6 mmol/l and an LDL-C of 3 mmol/l..

From a clinical laboratory perspective, the levels of lipids is measured by carrying out a lipid profile estimation which takes into account total cholesterol (TC), HDL-C, LDL-C, the triglycerides and the ratio of TC/HDL-C. a health provider may order a lipid profile as part of annual exam or if there is specific concern about cardiovascular disease (CVD), especially coronary artery disease (CAD) also referred to as coronary heart disease(CHD)

Lipids and Cardiovascular Disease

The lipids disorders forms part of the cardiovascular diseases which are coronary heart disease, hypertension, stroke, congenital heart disease and rheumatic heart disease. A person may have one of these diseases or combination of forms at the same time. Each form exists in varying degrees of severity. All forms are capable of causing secondary damage to other body organs and body systems. Among the above mentioned major CVD, coronary heart disease or coronary artery disease is the one directly related to the lipids disorder.

Coronary Heart Disease (CHD) or Coronary Artery Disease (CAD)

This form CVD involves damage to the vessels that supply blood to the heart muscles. The bulk of this blood is supplied by the coronary arteries. Any damage to these important vessels can cause a reduction of blood (and its vital oxygen and nutrients) to specific areas of heart muscles. The ultimate result of inadequate blood supply is a heart attack.

Atherosclerosis

The principal cause for the development of coronary heart disease is atherosclerosis. Atherosclerosis produces a narrowing of the coronary arteries. This narrowing stems from the long term buildup of fatty deposits, called plaque, on the inner walls of the arteries. This buildup reduces the blood supply to specific portions of the heart. Some arteries of the heart can become so blocked (occluded) that all blood supply is stopped. Heart muscle begins to die when deprived of oxygen and nutrients. This damage is known as myocardial infarction (in lay terms, this event is called a heart attack).

Lipids and Atherosclerosis

After much scientific studies, it has been determined that high levels of LDL-C, are a significant cause of atherosclerosis. This makes sense because LDL-C carry the greatest percentage of cholesterol in the bloodstream. LDL-C are more likely to deposit excess cholesterol into the artery walls. This contribute to plaque formation.For this reason, LDL-C, is often referred to as -THE BAD CHOLESTEROL---. High levels of LDL-C are partially related to inheritance, but they are clearly associated with smoking, poor dietary patterns, obesity and lack of exercise.

On the other hand, high levels of HDL-C are related to decrease in the development of atherosclerosis. HDL-Care thought to transport cholesterol out of the bloodstream . Thus HDL-C, have been called the----GOOD CHOLESTEROL---. Certain lifestyle alteration such as quitting smoking, reducing obesity, increase physical activities, and decreasing dietary fats, help in increasing the levels of HDL-C.

Reducing serum total cholesterol levels is a significant step in reducing the risk of death from coronary heart desease.

Angina Pectoris

When coronary arteries becomes narrowed, chest pain, or angina pectoris is often felt. This pain results from a reduction supply of oxygen to heart muscles . Usually angina pectoris is felt when the patient becomes stressed or exercises too strenuously. Angina pectoris reportedly can range from a feeling of mild indigestion to a severe viselike pressure in the chest. The pain may extend from the centre of the chest to the arm and even up to the jaw. Generally the more severe the blockage , the more pain is felt.

Cardiac Function and Cardiac Test Profile

Introduction

Over the past thirty years, the ability to diagnose heart disease has improved dramatically, largely because of the evolution of new, increasingly sophisticatedcardiac-testing techniques that include electrocardiography, exercise stress testing, radioisotope studies, echocardiography, and cardiac catheterization.Despite these technological advances, the initialdiagnosis of heart disease is still supported by twolow-technology, low-cost cornerstones: the medical history and physical examination. When carefullyperformed and properly interpreted, the history andthe physical will yield an accurate diagnosis in many, if not the majority of cases. Signs and symptoms such as chest pain, shortness of breath, and an abnormal pulse, coupled with detailed cardiopulmonary examinationand a careful history that may reveal major risk factors, have proved over and over their value in establishing a diagnosis.

After an initial presumptive diagnosis is made based on the findings of the history and physical, cardiac testing can be used to establish the diagnosis and determine the functional capability of the patient, the severity of the disease, and the category of risk into which the individual falls. With varying levels of detail and precision, diagnostic tests can establish or confirm the presence of blockages in the coronary arteries, the degree of blockages, damage to the heart muscle, enlargement of the heart chambers, congenital heart defects, abnormalities of the heart valves, and electrical disturbances that interfere with the rhythm of the heartbeat.

Thus, a major value of cardiac testing is its ability to increase the precision of the diagnosis, enabling today's physician to prescribe the treatment with the greatest likelihood of success for each individual patient. In many situations, judiciously ordered tests may also be used to uncover a cardiac abnormality even when there are no signs or symptoms. The choice of tests and the order in which they are used is guided by the findings of the history and physical and by the physician's clinical judgment.

In general, the diagnosis of heart disease progresses in a stepwise fashion from the simplest, least invasive, least expensive, and least risky method. As more information about a patient's condition is accumulated, appropriate decisions can be made regarding the use of more sophisticated and more invasive diagnostic procedures.

Normal Cardiovascular Function

The cardiovascular or circulatory system uses a muscular pump to send a complex fluid on a continuous trip within a closed system of tubes. This pump is the heart, the fluid is the blood and the closed system of tubes is the network of blood vessels.

Cardiovascular Disease Risk Factors

The following are compounding risk factors that are associated with cardiovascular diseases. These risk factors includes those that cannot be changed, those that can be changed and those that are thought to be contributing to heart disease.

‚ñ∂ Risk Factors That Cannot Be Changed
  • (a) Increasing age----Heart diseases tend to develop gradually over the course of one's life. Most of the consequences of heart disease are evident in old ages eg approximately 80% of people who die from heart attack are 65 years and above.
  • (b) Male gender----Men are prone to heart disease than women. It is thought that women are some what more protected from heart disease than men because of their natural production of the hormone estrogen during their fertile years.
  • (c) Heredity----Children born in families with history of heart disease are said to have a genetic predisposition to develop heart disease as they grow and develop throughout their lives
‚ñ∂ Risk Factors That Can Be Changed

There are four cardiovascular risk factors that that are influenced, in large part by our lifestyle choices.

  • (a) Cigarette or tobacco consumption
    Smokers have a heart attack risk that is over twice that of nonsmokers. Smoking cigarettes is the major risk factor associated with sudden cardiac death. Cigarettes and tobacco smoke also adversely affects nonsmokers who are exposed to environmental tobacco smoke. Due to this risk, indoor smoking in public areas and business settings are increasing tremendously in most countries.
  • (b) Physical inactivity
    Lack of exercise is a significant risk factor for heart disease. Regular exercise helps to strengthen the heart muscle, maintain healthy blood vessels, and improve the ability of the vascular system to transfer blood and oxygen to all parts of the body. Physical activities also helps to lower the overall blood cholesterol, encourages weight loss and retention of lean muscle mass and allows people to moderate stress in their lives.
  • (c) high blood lipid levels
    The higher the blood cholesterol the higher the risk of heart disease. Specifically the high levels of low density lipoprotein cholesterol and triglycerides
  • (d) high blood pressure
    High blood pressure can seriously damage a person's heart and blood vessels. High blood pressure causes the heart to work much harder, eventually causing the heart to enlarge and weaken. It increases the chances of stroke, heart attack, congestive heart failure and kidney disease.
‚ñ∂ Contributing Risk Factors
  • (a) diabetes
    Diabetes mellitus is a debilitating chronic disease that has a significant effect on the human body. In addition to increasing the risk of developing kidney disease, blindness, and nerve damage, DM increases the likelihood of developing heart and blood vessel disease. The cardiovascular damage is thought to occur when DM begins to alter normal cholesterol and blood fat levels.
  • (b) Obesity
    Even without any other risk factor, odese people are more likely than nonobese people to develop heart disease and stroke. Obesity places considerable strain on the heart, and tends to influence both blood pressure and blood cholesterol levels.It also triggers diabetes in predisposed people.
  • (c) Individual response to stress
    Unresolved stress over a long period may be a contributing factor to the development of heart disease. People who are unable to cope with stressful life experiences are more likely to develop negative dependence behaviors eg smoking, underactivity, poor dietary practice 0 which can lead to cardiovascular problems.

Forms of Cardiovascular Disease

  • CORONARY HEART DISEASE (coronary artery disease)
    Involves damage to the vessels that supply blood to the heart.The bulk of this blood is supplied by the coronary arteries. Any damage to these important vessels will cause a reduction of blood (and its vital oxygen and nutrients) to specific areas of the heart muscle. The ultimate result of inadequate blood supply is a heart attack.
    Atherosclerosis is the principle cause of the development of coronary heart disease. It produces the narrowing of the coronary arteries which is due to long term buildup of fatty deposits called plaque on the inner walls of the arteries.
  • Hypertension
    This is the consistent elevation of blood pressure. People with hypertension are often not aware that they have it. Hypertension is not thought as a curable disease, rather, it is controllable disease.
  • Stroke
    Results due to blood vessel damage in the brain perhaps due to the blockage of a cerebral artery. Blood clot that forms within the artery (thrombus) or a clot that travels from another part of the body to the brain (embolus). The resultants accidents ie cerebral thrombosis or cerebral embolism causes between 70% to 80% of all strokes.
  • Congenital heart disease
    This disease is present at birth. It is caused by valve damage, holes in the septum, blood vessel transposition and underdevelopment of the left side of the heart. Rubella has been known to cause this disease.
  • Rheumatic heart disease
    This is the final stage in a series of complications started by streptococcal infections of the throat.

Cardiac Function Tests (Cardiac Profile)

To investigate the above mention pathological complications of the cardiac system, the following parameters which makes cardiac profile are analyzed in the clinical laboratory:

  1. Creatine kinase or creatine phosphokinase(CK or CPK)
  2. Creatine kinase isoenzymes ie CK BB (CK-1), CK MB (CK-2) and CK MM (CK-3)
  3. Lactate dehydrogenase (LDH)
  4. Aspartate aminotransferase (AST)
  5. Hydroxybutyrate dehydrogenase (HBDH)
  6. Myoglobin (MYG)
  7. Troponin

Most routine laboratory are able to carry out analytes CPK,LDH, and AST. HBDH is analyzed in place of LDH. Creatine isoenzymes CK BB, CK MB and CK MM , MYOGLOBIN and TROPONIN are done as special chemistry.

Creatine Kinase (CK or CPK) and its Isoenzymes

CK activity is greatest in striated muscles, brain and heart tissue. CK is a dimer composed of two sub units ie B or Brain and M or muscle which are the product of two distinct genes. From these two sub units, three pairs of iso enzymes (isoforms) do exists ie CK BB, CK MB and CK MM. All these three isoenzymes are found in the cell cytosol .

  • The CK BB isoform is predorminantly found in the brain,
  • CK MB isoform predorminantly found in the heart tissue
  • CK MM predorminantly found in the skeletal muscles.

Clinical Significance

  • Investigated in the diagnosis of diseases of the skeletal muscles (muscular dystrophy) especially in the Duchenne type
  • Investigated in the diagnosis of diseases of the heart. After myocardial infarction, CK levels in blood are invariably elevated.Also after a heart surgery the CK levels are elevated.
  • Diseases of the central nervous system will increase the activity of CK especially in cerebral ischemia, acute cerebralvascular disease and head injury.
  • Disease of thyroid------Serum CK activity demonstrate an inverse relationship with thyroid activity. About 60% of hypothyroid subjects usually have elevated CK levels. By contrast, in hyperthyroidism subjects, the serum CK activity tends to be at the low end of the reference ranges.

Myoglobin Estimation

Myoglobin is a heme protein which reversibly binds oxygen, thus favouring its distribution in the muscle cells. Myoglobin is stored in the cytoplasm and this accounts for its rapid release from damaged muscle cells. The rapid increase of myoglobin concentration in blood makes it a useful, early maker of acute myocardial infarction. Since myoglobin is present in both cardiac and skeletal muscles, serum levels may increase in conditions relating not only to the heart (infarctus, heart surgery) but to the muscles (strenuous exercise, muscular dystrophy)

LAB INVESTIGATION: Uses immunological technique ie ELFA

SPECIMEN OF CHOICE: serum, heparinized plasma

Reference range in literature: (10-46 ¬µg/l).

Lactate Dehydrogenase (LDH)

LDH activity is present in all cells of the body and it is invariably found in the cytoplasm of the cell.LDH is a hydrogen transfer enzyme that catalyzes the oxidation of l-lactate to pyruvate with the mediation of NAD+ as hydrogen acceptor. The reaction is reversible and the equilibrium strongly favors the reverse reaction, namely the reduction of pyruvate to lactate.

Troponin

The troponins are a family of regulartory proteins found in skeletal and heart muscle (cardiac) fibers. There are three different types: troponin C (TnC), troponin T (TnT), and troponin I (TnI). Together, these three proteins regulate muscular contraction. The main difference is that the TnC subunit of troponin in skeletal muscle has four calcium ion binding sites, whereas (TnT), and (TnI) in cardiac muscle have only three calcium binding sites.

The other difference is that, TnC is predominantly found in the skeletal muscles where it exacts its regulatory function whereas Cardiac-specific troponins I and T (cTnI and cTnT) are troponins that are found only in the heart. Cardiac-specific troponins differ in their molecular weights in that cTnI has a molecular weight of 23.9 kD whereas cTnT has a molecular weight of 39.7 kD .

All these types of troponin are normally present in very small to undetectable quantities in the blood.

  • When there is damage to heart muscle cells, cardiac-specific troponins I and T are released into circulation. The more damage there is, the greater their concentration in the blood. The troponin test measures the amount of cardiac-specific troponin I or T in the blood and is used to help determine if an individual has suffered a heart attack (myocardial infarction).
  • When a person has a heart attack, levels of cardiac-specific troponins I and T can become elevated in the blood within 3 or 4 hours after injury and may remain elevated for 4-9 days (cTnI) OR up to 14 days (cTnT)

Troponin tests (I and T) are primarily ordered to evaluate people who have chest pain to see if they have had a heart attack or other damage to their heart. Either a cardiac-specific troponin I or troponin T test can be performed; usually a laboratory will offer one test or the other. Troponin tests are sometimes ordered along with other cardiac biomarkers, such as AST,LDH,CPK, CK-MB, CK-MM or myoglobin. However, troponins are the preferred tests for a suspected heart attack because they are more specific for heart injury than other tests (which may become elevated in skeletal muscle injury) and remain elevated for a longer period of time.

TnT and Tnl circulation levels are not affected by damage to other muscles, so injections, accidents, and drugs that can damage muscle do not affect cardiac troponin levels. Troponin may rise following strenuous exercise, although in the absence of signs and symptoms of heart disease, it is usually of no medical significance..Increased cardiac troponin concentrations should not be used by themselves to diagnose or rule out a heart attack. A physical exam, clinical history, and ECG are also important as is whether the troponin levels from a series of tests are stably elevated or show a rise and/or fall over several hours. Very rarely, people who have a heart attack will have normal troponin concentrations, and some people with increased troponin concentrations have no apparent heart injury.

Troponin (TnC, Tnl and TnT) levels may also be elevated with acute or chronic conditions such as myocarditis (heart inflammation), congestive heart failure, severe infections, kidney disease, and certain chronic inflammatory conditions of muscles and skin.
