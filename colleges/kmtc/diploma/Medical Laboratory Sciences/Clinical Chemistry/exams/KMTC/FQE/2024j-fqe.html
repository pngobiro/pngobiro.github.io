Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions (40 Marks)

1.
Polyuria is pathologically caused by:
A. Obstruction of low urinary tract
B. Over secretion of insulin
C. Under production of antidiuretic hormone
D. Intake of large volumes of water
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Under production of antidiuretic hormone

Explanation: Polyuria (excessive urine production) is a hallmark symptom of Diabetes Insipidus, which is caused by a deficiency in Antidiuretic Hormone (ADH) or vasopressin (Central Diabetes Insipidus) or the kidney's inability to respond to it (Nephrogenic Diabetes Insipidus). ADH is responsible for water reabsorption in the collecting ducts; without it, large volumes of dilute urine are excreted. While intake of large volumes of water (psychogenic polydipsia) causes polyuria, it is physiological, not pathological in the context of the primary defect.

2.
Which of the following disorders is associated with decreased specific gravity (SG)?
A. Diabetes insipidus
B. Diabetes mellitus
C. Severe dehydration
D. Nephritis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Diabetes insipidus

Explanation: Diabetes insipidus is characterized by the excretion of large volumes of dilute urine, resulting in a low specific gravity (often < 1.005). This is due to the lack of ADH effect, preventing water reabsorption. In contrast, Diabetes mellitus typically presents with high specific gravity due to glycosuria (glucose in urine). Dehydration leads to concentrated urine with high specific gravity.

3.
Glomerulonephritis is usually characterized by:
A. Nitrites in urine
B. Proteins in urine
C. Increased GFR
D. Ketones in urine
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Proteins in urine

Explanation: Glomerulonephritis involves inflammation of the glomeruli, leading to increased permeability of the glomerular filtration barrier. This allows proteins (proteinuria) and red blood cells (hematuria) to leak into the urine. Nitrites are associated with UTIs, and ketones with DKA or starvation. GFR is typically decreased, not increased, in glomerulonephritis.

4.
$1.4 \mathrm{mMol} / \mathrm{L}$ of serum urea obtained from pregnant mother is interpreted as:
A. Normal level
B. Decreased level
C. Increased level
D. Borderline levels
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Decreased level

Explanation: Normal serum urea ranges are approximately 2.5 - 7.1 mmol/L (though this can vary slightly by lab). In pregnancy, GFR increases significantly (by up to 50%), leading to increased clearance of urea and creatinine. Additionally, there is an anabolic state with positive nitrogen balance for fetal growth. Therefore, serum urea levels are typically lower in pregnancy. A value of 1.4 mmol/L is below the normal non-pregnant reference range, reflecting this physiological change.

5.
An endogenous substance preferred for study of glomerular filtrations rate is:
A. Inulin
B. Uric acid
C. Creatinine
D. Urea
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Creatinine

Explanation: Creatinine is the preferred endogenous marker for GFR estimation because it is produced at a relatively constant rate from muscle metabolism, is freely filtered by the glomerulus, and is not significantly reabsorbed (though a small amount is secreted). Inulin is the gold standard but is an exogenous substance requiring infusion. Urea is reabsorbed and affected by protein intake/catabolism, making it less reliable.

6.
Synthetic functions of the liver may be monitored by:
A. Prothrombin time test
B. Estimating bilirubin levels
C. Detection of bile salts in urine
D. Performing a lipid profile
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Prothrombin time test

Explanation: The liver synthesizes most coagulation factors (except Factor VIII). Prothrombin Time (PT) measures the extrinsic and common pathways of coagulation and is a sensitive indicator of the liver's synthetic function. Albumin is another marker, but PT prolongs earlier in acute liver failure due to the short half-life of clotting factors (e.g., Factor VII). Bilirubin measures excretory function.

7.
Which of the following cardiac markers is affected by muscle injury?
A. Creatine kinase
B. Troponin I
C. Troponin T
D. Aspartate transaminase
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Creatine kinase

Explanation: Creatine Kinase (CK) is found in skeletal muscle, heart muscle, and brain. Total CK is significantly elevated in skeletal muscle injury (rhabdomyolysis, trauma, injection). While CK-MB is more specific to the heart, total CK is not. Troponin I and T are highly specific for cardiac muscle damage and are not typically elevated in skeletal muscle injury alone (though newer high-sensitivity assays are very sensitive). AST is also found in liver and muscle but CK is the classic "muscle enzyme".

8.
Identify an exocrine pancreatic secretion:
A. Pepsin
B. Insulin
C. Glucagon
D. Trypsin
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Trypsin

Explanation: The pancreas has both endocrine and exocrine functions. The exocrine pancreas secretes digestive enzymes like Trypsin (as trypsinogen), Amylase, and Lipase into the duodenum. Insulin and Glucagon are endocrine hormones secreted by the Islets of Langerhans into the blood. Pepsin is secreted by the stomach.

9.
Positive occult blood test is suggestive of:
A. Duodenal ulcers
B. Obstructive jaundice
C. Malabsorption
D. Hemorrhoids
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Duodenal ulcers

Explanation: A Fecal Occult Blood Test (FOBT) detects hidden (occult) blood in the stool, which indicates bleeding somewhere in the gastrointestinal tract. Duodenal ulcers are a common cause of upper GI bleeding that can result in a positive FOBT. Hemorrhoids typically cause frank (visible) bright red blood. Obstructive jaundice causes pale stools, not bloody ones. Malabsorption causes steatorrhea.

10.
The mechanism effectively employed by the kidney to maintain the blood pH is:
A. Excretion of hydrogen ions and net generation of bicarbonate ions
B. Excretion of bicarbonate ions and net generation of hydrogen ions
C. Rapid excretion of $\mathrm{NH}_{3}$
D. Controlling the levels of $\mathrm{PCO}_{2}$
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Excretion of hydrogen ions and net generation of bicarbonate ions

Explanation: The kidneys regulate acid-base balance by excreting fixed acids (H+) and reabsorbing or generating new bicarbonate (HCO3-). This replenishes the bicarbonate buffer system. PCO2 is regulated by the lungs (respiratory compensation). While NH3 excretion (as NH4+) is part of the process of H+ excretion, the overall effective mechanism is described by option A.

11.
Metabolic acidosis is primarily marked by the deficit of:
A. $\mathrm{PCO}_{2}$
B. $\mathrm{HCO}_{3}{ }^{-}$
C. $\mathrm{PO}_{2}$
D. $\mathrm{H}^{+}$
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. $\mathrm{HCO}_{3}{ }^{-}$

Explanation: Metabolic acidosis is defined by a primary decrease in plasma Bicarbonate ($\mathrm{HCO}_{3}{ }^{-}$). This can occur due to loss of bicarbonate (e.g., diarrhea) or buffering of excess non-volatile acids (e.g., DKA, lactic acidosis). The body compensates by hyperventilating to lower PCO2, but the primary defect is the bicarbonate deficit.

12.
Bronchopneumonia is a common cause of:
A. Acute respiratory acidosis
B. Respiratory alkalosis
C. Metabolic alkalosis
D. Chronic respiratory acidosis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Acute respiratory acidosis

Explanation: Bronchopneumonia impairs gas exchange and ventilation, leading to the retention of CO2 (hypercapnia). An acute rise in PCO2 lowers the pH, resulting in Acute Respiratory Acidosis. Chronic respiratory acidosis is seen in long-standing conditions like COPD. Respiratory alkalosis is caused by hyperventilation (blowing off too much CO2).

13.
Fasting prior to blood sample collection for lipid profile is necessary to prevent:
A. Elevated levels of LDL
B. Elevated levels of HDL
C. Elevated levels of TG
D. Elevated levels of total cholesterol
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Elevated levels of TG

Explanation: Triglycerides (TG) are significantly affected by recent food intake, as chylomicrons (rich in TGs) enter the circulation after a meal. This causes lipemia and falsely elevates TG measurements. Total cholesterol and HDL are less affected by non-fasting states, but LDL (often calculated using the Friedewald formula which uses TG) would be inaccurate if TGs are elevated.

14.
$5.1 \mathrm{mMol} / \mathrm{L}$ of plasma cholesterol may be interpreted as:
A. Normal levels
B. Low levels
C. Increased levels
D. Borderline levels
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Normal levels

Explanation: The desirable level for total cholesterol is typically < 5.2 mmol/L (< 200 mg/dL). A value of 5.1 mmol/L falls within the normal or desirable range. Levels between 5.2 and 6.2 mmol/L are often considered borderline high, and> 6.2 mmol/L is high.

15.
Hypersecretion of Growth Hormone (GH) during adulthood may lead to:
A. Dwarfism
B. Gigantisms
C. Acromegaly
D. Cretinism
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Acromegaly

Explanation: Hypersecretion of GH after the closure of epiphyseal plates (adulthood) causes Acromegaly, characterized by the enlargement of bones in the hands, feet, and face, and thickening of soft tissues. Gigantism occurs if GH hypersecretion happens before epiphyseal closure (childhood), leading to excessive linear growth. Dwarfism is GH deficiency. Cretinism is congenital hypothyroidism.

16.
Hyperthyroidism is usually characterized by:
A. Low TSH and high $\mathrm{T}_{3}$
B. High TSH and low $\mathrm{T}_{3}$
C. Normal TSH and low $\mathrm{T}_{3}$
D. Low $\mathrm{T}_{3}$ and low $\mathrm{T}_{4}$
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Low TSH and high $\mathrm{T}_{3}$

Explanation: In primary hyperthyroidism (e.g., Graves' disease), the thyroid gland produces excess T3 and T4. These high levels exert negative feedback on the pituitary gland, suppressing TSH secretion. Therefore, the classic profile is High T3/T4 and Low (suppressed) TSH.

17.
Identify the most suitable marker of liver carcinoma:
A. Bilirubin
B. $\alpha$-feto proteins
C. PSA
D. CA-9
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. $\alpha$-feto proteins

Explanation: Alpha-fetoprotein (AFP) is the tumor marker of choice for Hepatocellular Carcinoma (HCC) or liver cancer. It is normally produced by the fetal liver but is elevated in adults with HCC. PSA is for prostate cancer. Bilirubin is a liver function test, not a specific tumor marker.

18.
Calcium ions in the plasma are regulated by:
A. Prolactin
B. Parathyroid hormone
C. Aldosterone
D. Antidiuretic hormone
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Parathyroid hormone

Explanation: Parathyroid Hormone (PTH) is the primary regulator of calcium homeostasis. When blood calcium drops, PTH is secreted, which increases calcium levels by mobilizing it from bone, increasing renal reabsorption, and stimulating Vitamin D activation (which increases gut absorption). Calcitonin acts to lower calcium levels.

20.
Malabsorption of lipids in the intestinal mucosa causes the deficiency of the following vitamins:
A. Riboflavin
B. Tocopherol
C. Niacin
D. Ascorbic acid
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Tocopherol

Explanation: Lipid malabsorption leads to deficiency of fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K). Tocopherol is Vitamin E, which is fat-soluble. Riboflavin (B2), Niacin (B3), and Ascorbic acid (C) are water-soluble vitamins and are less affected by lipid malabsorption.

21.
The mediators of the systematic effects of inflammation that are associated with chemotactic and stimulate adhesion molecules include:
A. Interleukin I and tumour necrosis factor (TNF)
B. C5a and leukotrienes B-4
C. C 3 b
D. Bradykinin
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Interleukin I and tumour necrosis factor (TNF)

Explanation: IL-1 and TNF are the major cytokines that mediate the systemic effects of inflammation (fever, acute phase protein synthesis) and local effects like endothelial activation, which includes stimulating the expression of adhesion molecules (selectins, ICAM-1, VCAM-1) and production of chemokines. C5a and LTB4 are potent chemotactic agents but IL-1 and TNF are the primary regulators of adhesion molecule expression.

22.
The reduction of silver solution with aid of a reducing agent is:
A. Argentaffin reaction
B. Chromaffin reaction
C. Argyrophil reaction
D. Metachromatic reaction
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Argyrophil reaction

Explanation: In the Argyrophil reaction, tissue components bind silver ions but cannot reduce them to visible metallic silver on their own; they require an external reducing agent (like hydroquinone) to do so. In contrast, the Argentaffin reaction involves tissue components (like melanin or enterochromaffin cells) that can both bind and reduce silver without an external reducer.

23.
Which of the following reactions is used to demonstrate polysaccharides?
A. Congo red
B. PAS reaction
C. Feulgen reaction
D. Prussian reaction
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. PAS reaction

Explanation: The Periodic Acid-Schiff (PAS) reaction is the standard method for demonstrating polysaccharides (like glycogen), neutral mucosubstances, and basement membranes. Periodic acid oxidizes glycols to aldehydes, which then react with Schiff's reagent to give a magenta color. Congo red is for amyloid, Feulgen for DNA, and Prussian blue for iron.

26.
The recommended impregnation technique for bone tissue is:
A. Double impregnation
B. Peterfyi's impregnation
C. Freeze substitution method
D. Vacuum impregnation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Double impregnation

Explanation: Bone is a hard, dense tissue that is difficult to infiltrate with paraffin wax. Double impregnation (usually using celloidin/nitrocellulose first, then paraffin) or prolonged infiltration is often recommended to ensure complete support and prevent crumbling during sectioning. Vacuum impregnation (D) is also very useful and commonly used to speed up the process and remove air bubbles, but double embedding is a classic technique for hard tissues.

27.
Low viscosity Nitrocellulose embedded tissues are stored in:
A. 70% alcohol
B. Absolute alcohol
C. Dry atmosphere
D. Water
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. 70% alcohol

Explanation: Nitrocellulose (celloidin) blocks must be kept moist because if they dry out, they shrink and become extremely hard and impossible to cut. 28.
When using a cytobrush for sample collection from the cervix, it should only be rotated to:
A. 90 degrees
B. 180 degrees
C. 270 degrees
D. 360 degrees
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. 360 degrees

Explanation: When using a cytobrush (endocervical brush), it is inserted into the cervical canal and rotated 90 to 180 degrees (some guidelines say up to 360 degrees, but usually one full turn is sufficient and safe) to collect cells. The type of necrosis associated with tuberculosis is:

C. Liquefactive

Explanation: Caseous necrosis ("cheese-like") is the hallmark of Tuberculosis. It is a combination of coagulative and liquefactive necrosis, appearing as soft, friable, white-grey debris. Coagulative necrosis is typical of ischemia (infarcts). Liquefactive is seen in brain infarcts and abscesses.

C. Haematin
D. Lipofuscin
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Lipofuscin

Explanation: Lipofuscin is known as the "wear-and-tear" pigment. 41.
Calculate the creatinine clearance given urine volume 1.1 liters/ 24 hours; plasma creatinine $1.2 \mathrm{mg} / \mathrm{dl}$ urine creatinine $90 \mathrm{mg} / \mathrm{dl}$. Comment on the result.
Show Answer

Formula:

$$ C_{Cr} = \frac{U \times V}{P} $$

Where:

  • $U$ = Urine Creatinine Concentration ($90 \mathrm{mg/dl}$)
  • $V$ = Urine Volume per minute ($\mathrm{ml/min}$)
  • $P$ = Plasma Creatinine Concentration ($1.2 \mathrm{mg/dl}$)

Step 1: Calculate V (Urine Volume in ml/min)

Total Volume = $1.1 \mathrm{L} = 1100 \mathrm{ml}$

Time = 24 hours = $24 \times 60 = 1440 \mathrm{minutes}$

$$ V = \frac{1100}{1440} = 0.764 \mathrm{ml/min} $$

Step 2: Calculate Clearance

$$ C_{Cr} = \frac{90 \times 0.764}{1.2} $$

$$ C_{Cr} = \frac{68.76}{1.2} = 57.3 \mathrm{ml/min} $$

Comment:

The calculated Creatinine Clearance is $57.3 \mathrm{ml/min}$.

Interpretation: The normal range for creatinine clearance is approximately 90-120 ml/min (varies slightly by age and sex). A value of $57.3 \mathrm{ml/min}$ indicates significantly decreased glomerular filtration rate (GFR), suggesting moderate renal impairment (Stage 3 Chronic Kidney Disease range, if chronic). This patient requires further evaluation for kidney function.

43.
State the role of liver in blood glucose regulation.
Show Answer

The liver plays a central role in maintaining blood glucose homeostasis ("Glucostat" function) through the following mechanisms:

  1. Glycogenesis: In the fed state (high insulin), the liver takes up glucose and converts it into glycogen for storage.
  2. Glycogenolysis: In the fasting state (high glucagon), the liver breaks down stored glycogen into glucose and releases it into the bloodstream to maintain levels.
  3. Gluconeogenesis: During prolonged fasting, the liver synthesizes new glucose from non-carbohydrate precursors (lactate, glycerol, amino acids).
  4. Glycolysis: The liver breaks down glucose for its own energy needs.
44.
Explain the laboratory precautions necessary for electrolyte analysis.
Show Answer

Accurate electrolyte analysis (Na+, K+, Cl-, HCO3-) requires strict adherence to pre-analytical precautions:

  • Prevent Hemolysis: This is critical for Potassium ($K^+$). RBCs have high intracellular $K^+$. Hemolysis releases it, causing falsely elevated serum $K^+$ (pseudohyperkalemia). Use gentle venipuncture and avoid vigorous mixing.
  • Prompt Separation: Serum/plasma should be separated from cells within 2-3 hours. Prolonged contact allows leakage of $K^+$ from cells and consumption of glucose (which shifts $K^+$ out).
  • Avoid EDTA/Oxalate Contamination: Do not pour blood from EDTA tubes (contains $K^+$) into plain tubes. This causes massive hyperkalemia and hypocalcemia.
  • Anaerobic Collection (for HCO3-): Keep tubes capped to prevent loss of $\mathrm{CO}_2$, which would lower measured bicarbonate/TCO2.
  • Avoid Prolonged Tourniquet Use: Can cause hemoconcentration and local acidosis, affecting $K^+$ and $Ca^{2+}$.
45.
Describe five (5) cardiac markers.
Show Answer
  1. Troponins (TnI and TnT): The "gold standard" biomarkers. Highly specific to cardiac muscle. They rise 3-4 hours after MI, peak at 24-48 hours, and remain elevated for 7-14 days.
  2. Creatine Kinase-MB (CK-MB): An isoenzyme of CK found mainly in the heart. Rises 4-6 hours after MI, peaks at 24 hours, and returns to normal in 2-3 days. Useful for detecting re-infarction.
  3. Myoglobin: An oxygen-binding protein. It is the earliest marker to rise (1-2 hours) but is non-specific (also in skeletal muscle). High negative predictive value.
  4. BNP / NT-proBNP: B-type Natriuretic Peptide. Released by ventricles in response to stretch/volume overload. Marker for Heart Failure, not ischemia.
  5. LDH (Lactate Dehydrogenase): Specifically LDH-1 isoenzyme ("flipped pattern" where LDH1 > LDH2). Rises late (24-48 hours) and stays elevated for a week. Largely replaced by Troponins.
46.
Using chemical equations, illustrate the mode of action of haemoglobin buffer and transport of oxygen to the tissues.
Show Answer

1. Oxygen Transport (at Lungs):

Haemoglobin (HHb) binds oxygen and releases protons (Bohr effect reversed).

$$ \mathrm{HHb} + \mathrm{O}_2 \rightarrow \mathrm{HbO}_2^{-} + \mathrm{H}^{+} $$

2. Buffering Action (at Tissues):

$\mathrm{CO}_2$ from tissues diffuses into RBCs and combines with water (catalyzed by Carbonic Anhydrase, C.A.) to form Carbonic Acid, which dissociates:

$$ \mathrm{CO}_2 + \mathrm{H}_2\mathrm{O} \xrightarrow{\text{C.A.}} \mathrm{H}_2\mathrm{CO}_3 \rightleftharpoons \mathrm{H}^{+} + \mathrm{HCO}_3^{-} $$

The $\mathrm{H}^{+}$ ions generated would lower pH, but Deoxyhaemoglobin ($\mathrm{Hb}^{-}$) is a better base than Oxyhaemoglobin. It buffers the $\mathrm{H}^{+}$:

$$ \mathrm{HbO}_2^{-} \rightarrow \mathrm{Hb}^{-} + \mathrm{O}_2 \quad (\text{Oxygen release to tissues}) $$

$$ \mathrm{Hb}^{-} + \mathrm{H}^{+} \rightarrow \mathrm{HHb} \quad (\text{Buffering of proton}) $$

Summary: Haemoglobin buffers the $\mathrm{H}^{+}$ produced from $\mathrm{CO}_2$ transport, preventing acidosis, while simultaneously facilitating $\mathrm{O}_2$ release (Bohr Effect).

47.
Illustrate the advantages of newer technology in tissue processing
Show Answer

Newer technologies (e.g., Microwave tissue processing, Automated vacuum processors) offer significant advantages over traditional manual methods:

  • Speed/Turnaround Time: Drastically reduced processing times (e.g., same-day diagnosis) allowing for faster patient management. Microwave energy accelerates diffusion.
  • Standardization: Automated processors ensure consistent timing, agitation, and temperature, reducing human error and inter-batch variability.
  • Reagent Safety: Closed systems reduce exposure to hazardous fumes (xylene, formalin). Newer methods often use safer, xylene-free substitutes (e.g., isopropanol).
  • Quality: Improved infiltration (especially with vacuum) leads to better sectioning quality and staining characteristics.
  • Cost-effectiveness: Reduced reagent consumption and labor costs in the long run.

Describe the Bethesda reporting system for cervical squamous epithelial cell abnormalities

The Bethesda System (TBS) classifies cervical cytology (Pap smear) squamous cell abnormalities into the following categories:

  • ASC (Atypical Squamous Cells):
    • ASC-US: Of Undetermined Significance (suggestive but not diagnostic of SIL).
    • ASC-H: Cannot exclude HSIL (higher risk).
  • LSIL (Low-Grade Squamous Intraepithelial Lesion): Associated with transient HPV infection. Includes mild dysplasia (CIN 1) and HPV effect (koilocytosis).
  • HSIL (High-Grade Squamous Intraepithelial Lesion): Associated with persistent high-risk HPV. Includes moderate and severe dysplasia (CIN 2, CIN 3) and Carcinoma in situ. High risk of progression to cancer.
  • Squamous Cell Carcinoma: Invasive malignancy.

Differentiation Between Benign and Malignant Neoplasms

Feature Benign Neoplasm Malignant Neoplasm
A. Gross Features
1. Capsule Usually encapsulated (fibrous capsule). Non-encapsulated (poorly defined margins).
2. Mode of Growth Expansile (pushes surrounding tissue). Invasive and infiltrative (invades surrounding tissue).
3. Rate of Growth Generally slow. Generally rapid.
4. Cut Surface Homogeneous, no hemorrhage or necrosis. Heterogeneous, often with areas of hemorrhage, necrosis, and ulceration.
5. Mobility Often mobile (not fixed to underlying structures). Often fixed to surrounding structures.
B. Microscopic Features
1. Differentiation Well-differentiated: Cells closely resemble the tissue of origin. Poorly differentiated (Anaplasia): Cells lose resemblance to tissue of origin.
2. Nuclear-to-Cytoplasmic (N:C) Ratio Normal (low, e.g., 1:4 or 1:6). Increased (high, e.g., 1:1). Nucleus occupies most of the cell.
3. Nuclear Features Normochromatic, regular shape, small nucleoli. Hyperchromatic (dark staining), Pleomorphic (variation in size/shape), prominent/multiple nucleoli.
4. Mitotic Figures Few, typical (normal bipolar spindles). Numerous, often atypical (tripolar, quadripolar spindles).
5. Polarity Maintained (organized orientation). Loss of polarity (disorganized arrangement).
6. Metastasis Absent. Never metastasize. Present. Capable of spreading to distant sites (lymphatic, hematogenous). *The definitive sign of malignancy.*
