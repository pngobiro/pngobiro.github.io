Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions (20 Marks)

1.
The medical discipline which is applied in criminal and civil cases activities is called?
A. Morbid anatomy
B. Forensic
C. Histopathology
D. Criminology
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Forensic

Explanation: Forensic medicine/science involves the application of medical and scientific knowledge to legal problems, encompassing both criminal and civil law.

2.
Eye witness in a criminal case are example of?
A. Circumstantial evidence
B. Circumstantial and direct evidence
C. Direct and circumstantial evidence
D. Direct evidence
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Direct evidence

Explanation: Direct evidence supports the truth of an assertion directly—i.e., without need for any additional evidence or inference. Eyewitness testimony is the classic example of direct evidence (e.g., "I saw him shoot the victim").

3.
The seizure of the human body total activities is referred to as:
A. Coma
B. Unconsciousness
C. Deep sleep
D. Death
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Death

Explanation: Death is the permanent cessation (seizure) of all vital functions (heart, lungs, brain).

4.
Rigidity of the body after death is referred to as:
A. Livor mortis
B. Algor mortis
C. Rigor mortis
D. None of the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Rigor mortis

Explanation: Rigor mortis (stiffness of death) is the chemical change in muscles causing limbs to become stiff and difficult to move/manipulate.

5.
The study of suffering is the literal meaning of the word:
A. Histology
B. Pathology
C. Cytology
D. Biopsy
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Pathology

Explanation: Pathology is derived from Greek pathos (suffering/disease) and logos (study).

6.
"Open to public discussion" is a synonym of:
A. Autopsy
B. Investigation
C. Forensic
D. Histopathology
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Forensic

Explanation: The term "Forensic" comes from the Latin forensis, meaning "of the forum," which was the public area in ancient Rome for debates and legal proceedings.

7.

A. Histopathology
B. Autolysis
C. Autopsy
D. Histology

Correct Answer: C. Autopsy

Explanation: An autopsy (post-mortem examination) is the surgical procedure performed on a corpse to determine the cause and manner of death.

Cooling of the body to that of the surrounding after death is called:
A. Rigor mortis
B. Algor mortis
C. Livor mortis
D. Lividity
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Algor mortis

9.
Forensic science can be used to investigate the following, except:
A. Murder
B. Assault
C. Rape
D. Malaria patient death in hospital
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Malaria patient death in hospital

Explanation: A death from malaria in a hospital is generally considered a Natural death (disease-related) and does not typically require a forensic investigation unless medical negligence is suspected. Murder, Assault, and Rape are criminal matters requiring forensics.

10.
What is the first step to be taken at the crime scene to protect the crime scene layout?
A. Secure the scene
B. Separating the witnesses
C. Scanning the scene
D. Sketching the scene

Correct Answer: A. Secure the scene

Explanation: The first priority of the first responder is to secure the scene (cordon it off) to prevent contamination, loss, or movement of evidence.

12.
Biological evidence is:
A. Spent cartridge
B. Sharp knife
C. Hair
D. Clothes
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Hair

Explanation: Hair is biological material (tissue/keratin) that may contain DNA (root). Cartridges, knives, and clothes are physical evidence (though clothes may *carry* biological stains).

13.
A manner of death in which a person takes her/his own life is called:
A. Homicidal
B. Suicidal
C. Accidental
D. Natural
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Suicidal

Explanation: Suicide is the intentional taking of one's own life.

14.
When a person dies in the hand of a specialist e.g. Doctor, the manner of death is called:
A. Accidental
B. Suicidal
C. Homicidal
D. Natural
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Accidental (or undetermined pending inquiry)

Explanation: Deaths during medical procedures (Therapeutic Misadventure) are often classified as Accidental if caused by a mishap or error, unless there is evidence of gross negligence (Homicide) or the underlying disease caused it (Natural). In the context of exam options, "Accidental" is the standard classification for procedural deaths.

15.
The humane way of disposing off dead body is:
A. Incineration
B. Burying
C. Cremation
D. None of the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Burying (or C. Cremation)

Explanation: Both burying and cremation are respectful, humane methods. However, "Incineration" typically refers to waste. Between Burying and Cremation, burial is often cited as the traditional humane method in many contexts, but this is culturally dependent. In exam keys, Burying is often the expected answer for "humane/respectful" disposal.

16.
The artery that is used during embalming is called:
A. Renal
B. Aorta
C. Vein
D. All of the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Aorta (Contextual Best Fit)

Explanation: Standard embalming uses the Carotid or Femoral arteries. The Aorta is the main artery of the body and is used in infant embalming or specialized cases. Options A (Renal) and C (Vein) are incorrect for *injection* (veins are for drainage). Thus, Aorta is the only plausible artery listed for distribution.

17.
The chemical used to carry out embalming is called:
A. Formaldehyde
B. Alcohol
C. Liquid paraffin
D. All the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Formaldehyde

Explanation: Formaldehyde (Formalin) is the primary preservative and fixative agent in modern embalming fluids.

18.
Breakdown of blood cell after death is called?
A. Rigor
B. Lividity
C. Lysis
D. Autolysis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Lysis (Hemolysis)

Explanation: The breakdown of blood cells specifically is hemolysis (lysis). This release of hemoglobin stains the vessel walls and tissues, contributing to Livor Mortis (Lividity). Autolysis is the general self-digestion of all cells.

19.
A person who is suspected to have committed a crime is called:
A. Complainant
B. Criminal
C. Offender
D. Accused
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Accused (or Suspect)

Explanation: A person suspected is a "Suspect". Once formally charged, they are the "Accused". A "Criminal" or "Offender" implies guilt has been proven. "Accused" is the appropriate legal term for someone facing charges in the justice system.

20.
The science that help us to determine poison and drugs effect in the body of human being, is called:
A. Anthropology
B. Histopathology
C. Toxicology
D. Criminology
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Toxicology

Explanation: Forensic toxicology is the study of the presence of drugs and poisons in body fluids and tissues.

Section 2: Short Answer Questions (40 Marks)

21.
Outline five (5) principles of forensic science (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Law of Individuality: Every object/person is unique; no two things are exactly alike.
  2. Locard's Exchange Principle: "Every contact leaves a trace."
  3. Law of Progressive Change: Everything changes with time (e.g., aging, decomposition).
  4. Principle of Comparison: Only likes can be compared (e.g., bullet to bullet, not bullet to knife).
  5. Principle of Analysis: The analysis can be no better than the sample analyzed.
  6. Law of Probability: Used to determine the likelihood of an event or identification (e.g., DNA match statistics).
22.
Outline five (5) types of death (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Somatic Death: Death of the organism as a whole (cessation of heart, lungs, brain).
  2. Cellular (Molecular) Death: Death of individual cells/tissues, occurring some time after somatic death.
  3. Brain Death: Irreversible cessation of all functions of the entire brain, including the brain stem.
  4. Clinical Death: Cessation of blood circulation and breathing (reversible if treated immediately).
  5. Legal Death: Recognition by the law that a person has died (usually brain or somatic death).
23.
Distinguish between direct and circumstantial evidence (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Direct Evidence: Evidence that directly proves a fact without requiring any inference or presumption.
    Example: An eyewitness testimony ("I saw him stab the victim") or a video recording of the crime.
  • Circumstantial (Indirect) Evidence: Evidence that implies a fact but does not directly prove it; it requires the judge/jury to make an inference.
    Example: A fingerprint at the scene, DNA, or owning the murder weapon (suggests presence/means, but not the act itself).

  2. Tissues: Hair, Skin scrapings, Nails.

25.
Outline five (5) types of wounds (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Abrasions: Scrapes or grazes on the skin surface (blunt force).
  2. Contusions (Bruises): Bleeding beneath the skin without breaking the surface (blunt force).
  3. Lacerations: Tearing or splitting of tissue due to crushing/stretching (blunt force).
  4. Incised Wounds: Cuts longer than they are deep (sharp force).
  5. Stab Wounds: Penetrating wounds deeper than they are long (sharp force).
  6. Gunshot Wounds: Caused by projectiles.
26.
Outline five (5) importance of preserving the body after death (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Forensic Investigation: To allow for autopsy to determine cause and manner of death without decomposition artifacts.
  2. Identification: To keep facial features recognizable for family or authorities.
  3. Evidence Preservation: To protect biological clues (toxicology, DNA) from degradation.
  4. Public Health: To prevent the spread of infectious diseases or bad odors.
  5. Social/Cultural Rites: To allow time for funeral arrangements and viewing by relatives.
27.
Give five (5) examples of circumstantial evidence (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Fingerprints found at the crime scene.
  2. DNA matching the suspect found on the victim.
  3. Shoe prints or tire tracks linking suspect to the location.
  4. Cell phone records placing suspect near the scene (Ping data).
  5. Motive or Threats: Evidence of prior arguments or financial gain.
  6. Possession of Weapon: Suspect owns the same type of gun used in the crime.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions (40 Marks)

29.
Time and Manner of Death

(a) State and briefly explain four (4) ways of estimating time of death (16 marks)

Show Answer
  1. Algor Mortis (Body Cooling):

    The body cools at a predictable rate (approx. 1.5°F or 0.8°C per hour) until it reaches ambient temperature. By measuring liver or rectal temperature and applying the Glaister equation, time since death can be estimated.

  2. Rigor Mortis (Stiffening):

    Muscles stiffen due to ATP depletion. It starts 2-4 hours after death, peaks at 12 hours, and disappears by 36-48 hours. The degree of stiffness helps bracket the time.

  3. Livor Mortis (Hypostasis):

    The pooling of blood in lower body parts. It becomes visible in 30 mins to 2 hours and becomes "fixed" (non-blanching) after 8-12 hours. This indicates if the body was moved and helps estimate time.

  4. Stomach Contents (Digestion):

    The extent of digestion can indicate time. A light meal takes ~2 hours to leave the stomach; a heavy meal takes ~4-6 hours. If the time of the last meal is known, this provides a timeline.

  5. Forensic Entomology (Insects):

    (Alternative point) Flies lay eggs on a corpse immediately. The developmental stage of maggots (instars) provides a precise minimum time since death.

(b) Give four (4) manners of death (4 marks)

Show Answer
  1. Natural: Due to disease or aging.
  2. Accidental: Unintentional injury.
  3. Suicidal: Intentional self-harm.
  4. Homicidal: Caused by another person.
  5. Undetermined: (Bonus) When cause cannot be established.
30.
Discuss Locard’s Exchange Principle (20 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

Definition: Locard's Exchange Principle is the foundational concept of forensic science, formulated by Dr. Edmond Locard. It states: "Every contact leaves a trace." Meaning, whenever two objects come into contact, there is a transfer of material between them.

Key Concepts:

  1. Transfer of Evidence:
    • The perpetrator will leave something at the scene (e.g., hair, fingerprints, DNA, shoe prints).
    • The perpetrator will take something from the scene (e.g., victim's blood, carpet fibers, soil on shoes).
  2. Trace Evidence:

    The principle specifically highlights the importance of "trace evidence"—microscopic materials like pollen, glass fragments, or fibers—which are easily transferred but often overlooked by criminals.

  3. Linkage Triangle:

    The principle connects the Suspect, the Victim, and the Crime Scene.

Application in Investigation:

  • Crime Scene Search: Investigators meticulously search for foreign materials not native to the environment.
  • Suspect Examination: Clothing and body of suspects are examined for evidence from the scene (e.g., checking a hit-and-run car for victim's paint/blood).
  • Exclusion/Inclusion: Absence of transfer can sometimes exclude a suspect, while presence strongly implies contact.

Limitations:

  • Evidence Loss: Traces can degrade or be lost over time (Law of Progressive Change).
  • Secondary Transfer: Evidence can be moved from person A to B to C, falsely implicating B.

Conclusion: It validates the entire field of trace evidence analysis, asserting that the silent witness of physical evidence cannot lie, unlike human witnesses.
