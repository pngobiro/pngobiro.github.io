Section A: Multiple Choice Questions (10 Marks)

1.
The medical discipline which is applied in criminal and civil cases activities is called?
A) Morbid anatomy
B) Forensic
C) Histopathology
D) Criminology
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Forensic

Explanation: Forensic medicine (or forensic science) is the application of medical/scientific knowledge to the administration of law (both criminal and civil).

2.
Eye witness in a criminal case are example of?
A) Circumstantial evidence
B) Circumstantial and direct evidence
C) Direct and circumstantial evidence
D) Direct evidence
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) Direct evidence

Explanation: Direct evidence supports the truth of an assertion directly (e.g., "I saw him do it") without need for inference. Circumstantial evidence requires an inference to connect it to a conclusion.

3.
The seizure of the human body total activities is referred to as?
A) Coma
B) Unconsciousness
C) Deep sleep
D) Death
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) Death

Explanation: Death is defined as the irreversible cessation (seizure) of all vital functions of the body.

4.
Rigidity of the body after death is referred to?
A) Livor mortis
B) Algor mortis
C) Rigor mortis
D) None of the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Rigor mortis

Explanation: Rigor mortis is the post-mortem stiffening of the body's muscles due to chemical changes (lack of ATP).

5.
The study of suffering is the literal meaning of the word?
A) Histology
B) Pathology
C) Autopsy
D) Autolysis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Pathology

Explanation: Pathology comes from the Greek pathos (suffering) and logos (study).

6.
"Open to public discussion" is a synonym of?
A) Autopsy
B) Investigation
C) Forensic
D) Histopathology
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Forensic

Explanation: Forensic comes from the Latin forensis, meaning "of the forum" (public place of debate). It implies knowledge applied in a public/legal setting.

7.
The procedure carried on the dead bodies like in the case of shakahola, to know the exact course of death is called?
A) Histopathology
B) Autolysis
C) Autopsy
D) Histology
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C) Autopsy

Explanation: An autopsy is a post-mortem examination to determine the cause and manner of death.

8.
Cooling of the body to that of the surrounding after death is called?
A) Rigor mortis
B) Algor mortis
C) Livor mortis
D) Lividity
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B) Algor mortis

Explanation: Algor mortis refers to the cooling of the body temperature to ambient temperature after death.

9.
Forensic science can be used to investigate the following, EXCEPT?
A) Murder
B) Assault
C) Rape
D) Malaria patient death in hospital
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D) Malaria patient death in hospital

Explanation: A death from a known disease like malaria in a hospital is usually a "Natural" death and does not require forensic investigation, unlike criminal cases (Murder, Assault, Rape).

10.
What is the first step to be taken at the crime scene to protect the crime scene layout?
A) Secure the scene
B) Separating the witnesses
C) Scanning the scene
D) Sketching the scene
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A) Secure the scene

Explanation: The first responder's priority is to cordon off the area (secure it) to prevent contamination, loss, or destruction of evidence.

Section B: Short Answer Questions (20 Marks)

11.
Outline five (5) principles of forensic science (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Locard's Exchange Principle: "Every contact leaves a trace."
  2. Law of Individuality: Every object is unique.
  3. Law of Progressive Change: Everything changes with time (e.g. decomposition).
  4. Principle of Comparison: Only likes can be compared.
  5. Principle of Analysis: The analysis is only as good as the sample.
  6. Law of Probability: Likelihood of an event.
12.
Outline five (5) types of death (2.5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Somatic Death: Death of the entire organism (tripod of life: heart, lungs, brain).
  2. Cellular (Molecular) Death: Death of individual cells/tissues.
  3. Brain Death: Irreversible cessation of brain function.
  4. Clinical Death: Cessation of circulation and breathing (potentially reversible).
  5. Legal Death: Official pronouncement by a doctor/court.
13.
Give five (5) forensic samples (2.5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Blood (wet or dried stains).
  2. Semen (swabs or stains).
  3. Saliva (buccal swabs, envelopes).
  4. Hair (roots).
  5. Fingerprints.
  6. Urine (toxicology).
14.
Outline five (5) types of wounds (2.5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Abrasions: Scrapes/grazes.
  2. Contusions: Bruises.
  3. Lacerations: Tears from blunt force.
  4. Incised wounds: Cuts (sharp force, longer than deep).
  5. Stab wounds: Punctures (sharp force, deeper than long).
  6. Gunshot wounds: From firearms.
15.
Outline five (5) importance of preserving the body after death (2.5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. To facilitate forensic investigation/autopsy.
  2. To allow for identification by relatives.
  3. To prevent spread of disease (public health).
  4. To allow for proper cultural/religious rites and burial.
  5. To delay decomposition and bad odors.
16.
Give five (5) examples of circumstantial evidence (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Fingerprints at the crime scene.
  2. DNA matches.
  3. Shoe prints or tire marks.
  4. Motive (financial, emotional).
  5. Opportunity (no alibi).
  6. Possession of weapon used in crime.

Section C: Long Answer Question (10 Marks)

17.
State and briefly explain FIVE (5) ways of estimating time of death (10 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Algor Mortis (Body Cooling):

    The body cools from normal temperature (37°C) to ambient temperature at a predictable rate (approx. 0.8-1°C per hour). Measuring core temperature (rectal/liver) helps estimate time passed.

  2. Rigor Mortis (Stiffness):

    Muscles stiffen due to ATP loss. It typically starts 2-4 hours post-mortem, peaks at 12 hours, and passes off by 36-48 hours. The degree of stiffness helps bracket the time.

  3. Livor Mortis (Hypostasis/Lividity):

    Pooling of blood in lower body parts. It appears within 2 hours and becomes fixed after 8-12 hours. Fixed vs. blanchable lividity helps determine timeframe.

  4. Stomach Contents (Gastric Emptying):

    The state of digestion indicates time since last meal. A light meal takes ~2 hours to digest; a heavy meal takes ~4-6 hours. If time of meal is known, time of death can be inferred.

  5. Forensic Entomology (Insect Activity):

    Flies (e.g., blowflies) lay eggs on the body shortly after death. The developmental stage of the maggots (instar 1, 2, 3) gives a precise minimum time since death (Post-Mortem Interval).

  6. Vitreous Potassium Levels:

    Potassium concentration in the eye fluid increases linearly after death, providing a reliable clock for the first few days.
