Section 2: Short Answer Questions (40 Marks)

1.
Describe 5 signs of death (5mks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Cessation of circulation and respiration (no pulse or breathing).
  • Changes in the eye (e.g., sinking, corneal clouding).
  • Coldness of the body (Algor mortis).
  • Stiffening of voluntary muscles (Rigor mortis).
  • Discoloration of the skin (Livor mortis/Hypostasis).
2.
Describe somatic death (5mks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Defined as the death of the organism as a whole.
  • It involves the complete and irreversible loss of ability to function.
  • It is characterized by the failure of the three organs essential for maintenance of life: the Heart (circulation), Lungs (respiration), and Brain (CNS function).
3.
Explain the expert test to determine death (5mks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Stethoscope: Used to detect audible sounds of respiration and heartbeats. Absence suggests death.
  • Ophthalmoscope: Used to examine the retina of the eye. If there is no blood flow (segmentation of vessels) and no pupillary response, the victim is dead.
  • Injection of Dye: Fluorescein dye injected into circulation. If there is no flow (skin does not turn yellow/green), it indicates cessation of circulation and hence death.
4.
State why it is important to dispose the human body after death (5mks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Avoidance of spread of infectious diseases.
  • Avoidance of contamination of food and water sources.
  • Avoidance of causes of offensive smell due to decomposition.
  • Avoidance of psychological trauma to the next of kin and the community.
  • Avoidance of creating a breeding place for flies and vermin.
5.
Give (5) five reasons why everyone should be familiar with death investigations (5mks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Legal: A death may result from someone breaking the law (e.g., murder), requiring justice.
  • Public health safety: Some deaths result from bad environmental conditions (e.g., carbon monoxide, epidemics) that need correction.
  • Institutional concerns: Deaths in hospitals or prisons may involve negligence that needs investigation.
  • Medical care quality: Some conditions/treatments are not known prior to death; investigation improves future care.
  • Personal reasons: Anyone may someday be directly affected by a death and its subsequent investigation.
6.
Describe the human body organs which are associated with in maintenance cooperate action of life (5mks)
Show Answer

Answer:

The maintenance of life depends on the cooperative action of:

  • Tissue cells: The basic units of life.
  • Tissues: Groups of cells performing functions.
  • Organs: Specifically the "Tripod of Life":
    • Heart: Circulates blood.
    • Lungs: Oxygenates blood.
    • Brain: Controls vital functions.
  • Systems: Organ systems working together.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions (40 Marks)

1.
Describe (5) five factors affecting body resolution (decomposition) after death (10mks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Physical condition of the body: Obese bodies decompose faster than lean ones due to heat retention and moisture.
  • Time lapse: The duration between death and burial directly affects the extent of decomposition.
  • Presence or absence of clothing: Clothing can speed up decomposition by retaining heat, or slow it down by protecting from insects.
  • Depth of burial and type of soil: Deep burial in clay soil slows decomposition; shallow burial allows access to air and scavengers.
  • Access of air: In shallow graves, oxygen access hastens aerobic decomposition and insect activity.
2.
State (5) five methods of human disposal after death (10mks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  • Earth burial: Interring the body in the ground.
  • Exposure and abandonment: Leaving the body in the open (historical/cultural practice).
  • In vaults: Placing the body in a sealed chamber.
  • In tombs: Entombment in structures above ground.
  • At sea: Disposal in the ocean.
  • (Additional modern methods: Cremation).
3.
Explain the methods of embalming (20mks)
Show Answer

Answer:

Embalming is the chemical treatment of the dead human body to reduce the presence and growth of microorganisms, retard organic decomposition, and restore an acceptable physical appearance. The four main methods are:

  1. Arterial Embalming:
    • Involves the injection of preservative chemicals (arterial fluid) into the blood vessels (arteries), usually via the right common carotid or femoral artery.
    • Blood is simultaneously drained from the veins (e.g., jugular vein).
    • This is the primary method for preserving the entire body.
  2. Cavity Embalming:
    • Involves the direct treatment of the contents of the body cavities (thoracic, abdominal, and pelvic).
    • A trocar (hollow needle) is used to aspirate gas and fluids from the hollow organs.
    • Strong, undiluted cavity fluid is then injected to saturate the organs.
  3. Hypodermic Embalming:
    • Involves the injection of chemicals directly into the tissues using a syringe and needle.
    • This is used for areas that did not receive enough fluid during arterial embalming (e.g., fingertips, nose) or for localized treatment.
  4. Surface Embalming:
    • Involves the application of preservative chemicals directly to the surface of the skin or tissues.
    • Used for surface lesions, ulcers, or areas of skin slip.
    • Can be done using gels, sprays, or cotton packs soaked in cavity fluid.
