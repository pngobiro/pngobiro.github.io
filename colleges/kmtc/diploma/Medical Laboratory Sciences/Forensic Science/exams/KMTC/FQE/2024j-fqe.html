Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions (20 Marks)

1.
The medical discipline which deals with criminal activities is referred to as:
A. Histopathology
B. Morbid anatomy
C. Forensic
D. Human rights department
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Forensic

Explanation: Forensic medicine (or forensic science) is the application of medical and scientific knowledge to legal questions, particularly in the investigation of crime.

2.
The seizure of the human body total activities is referred to as:
A. Coma
B. Unconsciousness
C. Deep sleep
D. Death
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Death

Explanation: Death is defined as the permanent cessation (seizure) of all vital functions of the body, including total brain function, respiration, and circulation.

3.
The investigator of the causes of human death is known as:
A. CID
B. Pathologist
C. CIA
D. Physician
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Pathologist

Explanation: A forensic pathologist is a medical doctor who specializes in investigating the cause, manner, and mechanism of death, usually by performing autopsies.

4.
The specimen removed from a dead body is known as:
A. Waste matter
B. A biopsy
C. A scrap
D. Autopsy
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Autopsy

Explanation: While "Autopsy" technically refers to the examination itself (Post-Mortem Examination), in the context of this curriculum, it is contrasted with "Biopsy" (tissue from a living person). The intended answer is D to distinguish it from living tissue sampling.

5.
A death may result from someone breaking the law such as:
A. An assault
B. Drowning
C. Hunger
D. Negligence
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. An assault

Explanation: An assault is a criminal act of violence. If it leads to death, it is a homicide, which involves breaking the law. Drowning (B) and Hunger (C) can be accidental or natural. Negligence (D) is also a legal breach but "Assault" is the most direct active criminal act listed.

6.
An example of a death type includes:
A. Cellular
B. Suicide
C. Drowning
D. Poisoning
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Suicide

Explanation: "Suicide" is a Manner of Death (Type). Drowning (C) and Poisoning (D) are Causes of Death. Cellular (A) is a level/stage of death (Cellular death vs. Somatic death).

Recall: The 5 Manners of Death are Homicide, Suicide, Accidental, Natural, and Undetermined (NASH-U).

7.
One of the body organs which is essential for maintenance of life include:
A. Skin
B. Heart
C. Stomach
D. Spleen
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Heart

Explanation: The "Tripod of Life" consists of the Heart (Circulation), Lungs (Respiration), and Brain (CNS Function). Cessation of any of these leads to somatic death.

8.
Depletion of oxygen to the lungs results to a condition known as:
A. Cyanosis
B. Comatose
C. Asphyxia
D. Pyrexia
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Asphyxia

Explanation: Asphyxia is the condition caused by the deprivation of oxygen to the body, often leading to unconsciousness or death. Cyanosis is a sign (blue color) resulting from it.

9.
After death the human body becomes cold this is refered to as:
A. Non vital
B. Ischemia
C. Apnea
D. Algor mortis
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Algor mortis

Explanation: Algor mortis (Latin: algor—coldness; mortis—of death) is the reduction in body temperature following death until the ambient temperature is matched.

10.
Tests of death includes:
A. Postmortem
B. Small test
C. Expert test
D. Colour test
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Expert test

Explanation: "Expert tests" refer to medical examinations performed by a doctor (like stethoscope auscultation, ECG, Magnus test for circulation) to confirm death, as opposed to layperson observations.

11.
Modes of somatic death include:
A. Infection
B. Syncope
C. Suicide
D. Poisoning
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Syncope

Explanation: The modes of somatic death correspond to the failure of the "Tripod of Life":
1. Syncope: Failure of circulation (Heart).
2. Asphyxia: Failure of respiration (Lungs).
3. Coma: Failure of brain function.

12.
The person who is responsible for deaths occurring out of hospital is known as:
A. Oncologist
B. CID
C. Coroner
D. Detective
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Coroner

Explanation: A coroner is a government official empowered to conduct or order an inquest into the manner or cause of death, especially if the death occurred outside a hospital or under suspicious circumstances.

13.
The preferred valuable clues about the cause of death investigation is the:
A. Autopsy
B. Disease
C. Witness
D. Scene
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Scene

Explanation: The crime scene is the primary source of physical evidence. While an autopsy gives the medical cause, the scene provides the context (manner) and physical clues crucial for the investigation.

14.
The word autopsy is derived from Greek which means:
A. Individual
B. Death
C. Search
D. One's self
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. One's self

Explanation: Autopsy comes from the Greek word autopsia, meaning "to see with one's own eyes" (autos = self, opsis = eye).

15.
Public health death investigations are performed by:
A. Public health agencies
B. Hospital administration
C. Non - aliens
D. Govt.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Public health agencies

Explanation: Investigations into deaths that affect public safety (epidemics, food poisoning) are typically led by public health agencies (like the Ministry of Health) to prevent further loss of life.

16.
Medicolegal death investigations are done by:
A. Provision of state law
B. Request from a lawyer
C. Religious believe
D. Owners will
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Provision of state law

Explanation: Medicolegal investigations (autopsies) are mandated by law (e.g., the Coroners Act or Criminal Procedure Code) for sudden, suspicious, or unnatural deaths. They do not require family consent ("Owners will").

17.
The word pathology literally means:
A. Study of human rights
B. Accredited study
C. Study of suffering
D. Open to public demand
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Study of suffering

Explanation: Pathology comes from Greek pathos (suffering/disease) and logos (study of).

18.
Forensic means:
A. Hidden
B. Court records
C. Approval research
D. Open to public discussion
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Open to public discussion

Explanation: The word "Forensic" comes from the Latin forensis, meaning "of or before the forum." The forum was a public place in Rome where legal cases were debated. Thus, it relates to public discussion or debate in a legal setting.

19.
Disposal of the dead is guided by the government law of Kenya cap………
A. 1999
B. 62
C. 242
D. 141
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. 242

Explanation: Cap 242 is the Public Health Act of the Laws of Kenya, which contains provisions regarding sanitation, infectious diseases, and the disposal of bodies.

20.
The human body cells which are prone to die immediately after death are the:
A. Muscle cells
B. Bone marrow cells
C. Nerve cells
D. Blood cells
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Nerve cells

Explanation: Nerve cells (neurons) in the brain are the most sensitive to oxygen deprivation and begin to die within 3–7 minutes of cardiac arrest. Other cells like muscle and bone marrow can survive for hours.

Section 2: Short Answer Questions (40 Marks)

21.
Outline duties of forensic laboratory technologist. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Evidence Analysis: Examining physical evidence (blood, hair, fibers) using scientific techniques.
  2. Report Writing: documenting findings and methods clearly for legal use.
  3. Court Testimony: Appearing as an expert witness to explain technical findings to a judge or jury.
  4. Maintenance of Equipment: Ensuring all lab instruments are calibrated and functioning correctly.
  5. Chain of Custody: Maintaining strict records of who handles evidence to ensure its integrity.
  6. Sample Collection: Assisting in the proper collection and preservation of biological samples.
22.
State the role of police in crime scene. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Securing the Scene: Cordoning off the area to prevent contamination or loss of evidence.
  2. Safety: Ensuring the scene is safe for investigators and medical personnel.
  3. Separating Witnesses: Keeping witnesses apart to prevent cross-contamination of their accounts.
  4. Log Keeping: Recording the names of everyone entering and exiting the scene.
  5. Initial Documentation: Taking notes on the condition of the scene upon arrival.
23.
Explain the roles of forensic science experts in forensic investigation. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

Forensic experts specialize in specific fields to aid investigations:

  1. Pathologists: Determine cause and time of death via autopsy.
  2. Toxicologists: Analyze bodily fluids for drugs, poisons, and alcohol.
  3. Odontologists: Identify victims via dental records and analyze bite marks.
  4. Anthropologists: Identify skeletal remains (age, sex, ancestry).
  5. Entomologists: Estimate time of death based on insect activity on the body.
  6. Serologists: Analyze blood and other body fluids for DNA profiling.
24.
Briefly explain the classification of manner of death. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

The manner of death explains how the death arose, classified into the "NASH-U" categories:

  1. Natural: Death caused exclusively by disease or old age (e.g., heart attack).
  2. Accidental: Death caused by unexpected, unintended injury (e.g., car crash, drowning).
  3. Suicidal: Death resulting from intentional self-harm.
  4. Homicidal: Death caused by the act of another person (e.g., murder).
  5. Undetermined: When the information points to more than one manner or there is insufficient evidence to conclude.
25.
Outline what happens during early postmortem changes. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Algor Mortis: Cooling of the body to ambient temperature.
  2. Livor Mortis (Hypostasis): Settling of blood in lower parts of the body causing purplish discoloration.
  3. Rigor Mortis: Stiffening of muscles due to chemical changes (ATP depletion).
  4. Eye Changes: Corneal clouding (opacity) and loss of intraocular pressure (eyeballs become flaccid).
  5. Primary Flaccidity: Immediate relaxation of all muscles right after death.
26.
Explain embalming and state three goals of embalming. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Definition: Embalming is the chemical treatment of the dead human body to reduce the presence and growth of microorganisms, to temporarily inhibit decomposition, and to restore an acceptable physical appearance.

Goals:

  1. Preservation: To delay decomposition and allow time for funeral arrangements.
  2. Sanitation: To destroy disease-causing pathogens and protect public health.
  3. Restoration (Presentation): To restore a lifelike appearance for viewing by the family.
27.
Explain blood pattern analysis. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

Bloodstain Pattern Analysis (BPA) is the examination of the shapes, locations, and distribution patterns of bloodstains to interpret the events that created them. It helps determine:

  • Type of Weapon: (e.g., blunt force vs. sharp force).
  • Number of Blows: Based on cast-off patterns.
  • Position of Victim/Assailant: Using convergence and triangulation.
  • Movement: Trails of blood drops (passive stains).
  • Velocity of Impact: Low (drips), Medium (beating), or High (gunshot) velocity spatter.
28.
Outline five current trends being investigated in forensic science. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Rapid DNA Analysis: Technology allowing DNA profiling in under 90 minutes at the crime scene.
  2. Digital Forensics: Investigation of cybercrimes, cloud data, and mobile device evidence.
  3. Forensic Palynology: Use of pollen grains to link suspects to specific locations.
  4. Microbial Forensics: Analyzing the "necrobiome" (bacteria on a corpse) to estimate time of death more accurately.
  5. Virtopsy (Virtual Autopsy): Using CT and MRI scans to examine bodies without invasive cutting.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions (40 Marks)

29.
Describe the embalming fluids. (20 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

Embalming fluids are chemical solutions used to preserve, sanitize, and restore dead bodies. They are typically classified by their function and composition.

1. Main Components:

  • Preservatives (Fixatives): Stop decomposition by cross-linking proteins.
    Examples: Formaldehyde (Formalin), Glutaraldehyde, Phenol.
  • Germicides (Disinfectants): Kill bacteria and pathogens.
    Examples: Quaternary ammonium compounds, Glutaraldehyde.
  • Modifying Agents:
    • Buffers: Maintain pH balance (e.g., Borax).
    • Humectants: Retain moisture to prevent dehydration (e.g., Glycerine, Lanolin).
    • Anticoagulants: Dissolve blood clots (e.g., Sodium Citrate).
    • Surfactants: Reduce surface tension for better penetration (e.g., Sulfonates).
  • Dyes (Coloring Agents): Restore natural skin color.
    Examples: Eosine (red), Erythrosine.
  • Perfumes (Deodorants): Mask odors.
    Examples: Oil of cloves, Sassafras.
  • Vehicles (Diluents): The solvent for the chemicals.
    Examples: Water, Alcohol.

2. Types of Embalming Fluids:

  • Arterial Fluid: Concentrated fluid injected into the arterial system (e.g., Carotid artery). It contains strong preservatives and dyes.
  • Cavity Fluid: Very strong fixative injected directly into the body cavities (thoracic/abdominal) to preserve organs. Contains high formaldehyde content and no dyes.
  • Supplemental Fluids: Used to aid the arterial fluid (e.g., Pre-injection fluids to clear clots).
  • Accessory Chemicals: Used on the surface (e.g., Gels, hardening compounds for autopsied bodies).
30.
Demonstrate the specimens used for forensic investigations. (20 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

Forensic investigations rely on various biological and physical specimens to link suspects to crimes, identify victims, or determine causes of death.

1. Biological Specimens:

  • Blood: Used for DNA profiling, blood group analysis, and toxicology.
  • Saliva: Collected from bite marks, envelopes, or cigarette butts for DNA.
  • Semen: Critical in sexual assault cases for identifying perpetrators via DNA.
  • Hair: Can provide mitochondrial DNA and indicate drug use history over time.
  • Urine: Primary sample for drug testing and toxicology screens.
  • Vaginal/Anal Swabs: Collected in rape kits to recover assailant's DNA.
  • Stomach Contents: Analyzed to determine time of last meal and presence of poisons/pills.
  • Vitreous Humor (Eye Fluid): Stable fluid used to test for potassium levels (time of death) and alcohol/drugs.

2. Physical/Trace Evidence:

  • Fingerprints: Unique ridge patterns found on surfaces; key for identification.
  • Footprints/Shoe Prints: Indicate movement, number of people, and shoe type.
  • Bullets/Casings: Ballistic evidence linked to specific firearms.
  • Clothing: Examined for tears, stains, gunshot residue, or fibers.
  • Nail Scrapings: Debris under fingernails may contain skin/blood of the attacker (defense wounds).
  • Insects (Entomology): Maggots and beetles collected to estimate Post-Mortem Interval (PMI).

Collection Protocol: All specimens must be collected with sterile tools, packaged in paper (not plastic, to prevent mold) for biologicals, sealed with evidence tape, and accompanied by a strict Chain of Custody form.
