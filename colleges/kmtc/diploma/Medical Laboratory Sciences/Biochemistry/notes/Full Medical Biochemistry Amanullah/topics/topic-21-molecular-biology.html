üå± Mendelian Genetics and Inheritance

Biological inheritance is the process by which an offspring acquires characteristics from its parent. The description of a mode of biological inheritance involves:

  • Number of loci: Monogenetic (one), Oligogenetic (few), Polygenetic (many).
  • Chromosomes: Autosomal (non-sex), Gonosomal (sex chromosomes - X or Y), Mitochondrial.
  • Genotype-Phenotype Correlation: Dominant, Intermediate (Codominant), Recessive.

Mendel's Laws

  1. 1 Law of Segregation (First Law):
    • Alternative versions of genes (alleles) account for variations.
    • For each characteristic, an organism inherits two alleles (one from each parent).
    • The two alleles segregate during gamete production (meiosis), so each gamete carries only one allele.
  2. 2 Law of Independent Assortment (Second Law):

    The inheritance pattern of one trait will not affect the inheritance pattern of another (provided genes are not linked). Occurs during Metaphase I of meiosis.
üìä

Punnett Square

Used to predict genotypes. For a cross between two heterozygous parents (Pp x Pp):

Pp
PPP (Dominant)Pp (Dominant)
pPp (Dominant)pp (Recessive)

Phenotypic ratio = 3:1.

Types of Dominance

  • Simple/Complete Dominance: Dominant allele completely masks recessive allele (e.g., Purple vs White flowers).
  • Incomplete Dominance: Heterozygote has an intermediate phenotype (e.g., Red x White = Pink flowers). Ratio 1:2:1.
  • Codominance: Both alleles are fully expressed (e.g., ABO blood groups - Type AB). Ratio 1:2:1.

üîÑ Central Dogma of Molecular Biology

The flow of genetic information in a cell:

\text{DNA} \xrightarrow{\text{Replication}} \text{DNA} \xrightarrow{\text{Transcription}} \text{RNA} \xrightarrow{\text{Translation}} \text{Protein}

Gene: A chromosomal segment that codes for a single polypeptide chain or RNA molecule.

Genome: All the genes and intergenic DNA of an organism. Human genome $\approx 100,000$ genes (historical estimate; current estimate is ~20,000-25,000).

Gene Structure

  • Introns: Non-coding sequences (removed during splicing).
  • Exons: Coding sequences (expressed).

üß¨ DNA Replication

DNA replication is semi-conservative (one parental strand + one new strand). Requires a "Replisome".

Steps of Replication

  1. 1 Unwinding: Helicase unwinds the double helix at the origin (oriC). SSB proteins keep strands apart. Topoisomerase (Gyrase) relieves supercoiling tension.
  2. 2 Initiation: Primase synthesizes a short RNA primer.
  3. 3 Elongation: DNA Polymerase III adds nucleotides in $5' \rightarrow 3'$ direction.
    • Leading Strand: Synthesized continuously.
    • Lagging Strand: Synthesized discontinuously as Okazaki fragments.
  4. 4 Termination: DNA Polymerase I removes RNA primers and fills gaps. DNA Ligase joins fragments.
04

üìù Transcription (RNA Synthesis)

Synthesis of RNA from DNA template by DNA-dependent RNA Polymerase. Direction: $5' \rightarrow 3'$.

Process

  1. Initiation: RNA polymerase binds to Promoter region (TATA box). DNA unwinds.
  2. Elongation: Ribonucleotides (ATP, UTP, GTP, CTP) are added complementary to the template strand.
  3. Termination: Enzyme reaches a termination signal (hairpin loop or Rho protein dependent). RNA is released.

Post-Transcriptional Modification (Eukaryotes)

Primary transcript (hnRNA) is processed to mature mRNA:

  • Capping: Addition of 7-methylguanosine cap at $5'$ end.
  • Tailing: Addition of Poly-A tail at $3'$ end.
  • Splicing: Removal of Introns and joining of Exons. (Self-splicing or Spliceosome mediated).
üö´

Inhibitors of Transcription

  • Rifampicin: Inhibits bacterial RNA polymerase (TB treatment).
  • Actinomycin D: Intercalates DNA.
  • $\alpha$-Amanitin: Toxin from mushroom, inhibits eukaryotic RNA Pol II.

üè≠ Translation (Protein Synthesis)

The Genetic Code

Triplet code (Codon) on mRNA specifies amino acids.

  • Universal: Same in all organisms.
  • Degenerate: Multiple codons for one amino acid (e.g., Leu has 6).
  • Start Codon: AUG (Methionine).
  • Stop Codons: UAA, UAG, UGA.

Mechanism of Translation

  1. 1 Activation: Amino acids attached to specific tRNA by Aminoacyl-tRNA synthetase (requires ATP).
  2. 2 Initiation: Small ribosomal subunit binds mRNA. Met-tRNA binds AUG. Large subunit joins to form 70S/80S complex. (Sites: P-site, A-site).
  3. 3 Elongation:
    • Binding of next Aminoacyl-tRNA to A-site.
    • Peptide bond formation (Peptidyl transferase).
    • Translocation (Ribosome moves 1 codon).
  4. 4 Termination: Stop codon reaches A-site. Release factors hydrolyze peptide. Complex dissociates.
üíä

Antibiotic Inhibitors

  • Tetracycline: Blocks A-site.
  • Chloramphenicol: Inhibits peptidyl transferase (Prokaryotes).
  • Streptomycin: Causes misreading of code.
  • Puromycin: Causes premature termination.

üéõÔ∏è Regulation of Gene Expression

Constitutive Genes: Always expressed (Housekeeping genes).
Inducible Genes: Expressed only when needed.

The Lac Operon (Prokaryotes)

Operon for lactose metabolism in E. coli. Consists of:

  • Structural Genes: Z ( $\beta$-galactosidase), Y (Permease), A (Transacetylase).
  • Operator (O): Binds repressor.
  • Promoter (P): Binds RNA polymerase.
  • Regulator (I): Produces Repressor protein.

In absence of Lactose

Repressor protein binds to Operator. RNA Polymerase blocked. Gene OFF.

In presence of Lactose

Lactose (Inducer) binds Repressor. Repressor inactivates and releases Operator. RNA Polymerase transcribes genes. Gene ON.

Also regulated by cAMP-CAP complex (Glucose effect).

‚ö†Ô∏è Mutations and DNA Repair

Mutation: Change in nucleotide sequence.

Types of Mutations

  • Point Mutation: Single base change.
    • Transition: Purine $\leftrightarrow$ Purine (A $\leftrightarrow$ G).
    • Transversion: Purine $\leftrightarrow$ Pyrimidine.
  • Missense: Codes for different amino acid (e.g., Sickle Cell).
  • Nonsense: Codes for Stop codon (Premature termination).
  • Silent: No change in amino acid.
  • Frameshift: Insertion/Deletion alters reading frame.

DNA Repair Mechanisms

  1. Base Excision Repair (BER): Removes damaged bases (e.g., deaminated C $\rightarrow$ U).
  2. Nucleotide Excision Repair (NER): Removes bulky lesions like Thymine dimers (caused by UV). Defect causes Xeroderma Pigmentosum.
  3. Mismatch Repair (MMR): Corrects replication errors.
