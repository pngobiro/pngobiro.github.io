üå°Ô∏è Measurement of Calorific Value

Calorie: The unit of energy equal to the amount of heat required to raise the temperature of one gram of water by one degree Celsius (from $14.5^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ to $15.5^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$). In nutrition, Kilocalories (Kcal) are used.

Methods of Determination

  1. 1 Direct Method (Bomb Calorimeter): Measures heat produced when a sample is completely burnt in oxygen under pressure.
  2. 2 Indirect Method (Oxycalorimeter): Measures the volume of oxygen required to burn the food sample. Based on the principle that 1 liter of $O_2$ utilized releases approx 4.8 Kcal.
Calorific Values of Foodstuffs
Food Stuff Potential Energy (Bomb Calorimeter) Physiological Energy (In Body)
Carbohydrates 4.1 Kcal/gm 4.0 Kcal/gm
Fats 9.4 Kcal/gm 9.0 Kcal/gm
Proteins 5.6 Kcal/gm 4.0 Kcal/gm

*Note: Physiological value for protein is less because nitrogen is eliminated as urea, not completely oxidized.

02

üèÉ Measurement of Energy Expenditure

Metabolic Rate: Total energy expenditure per unit time.

Measurement Techniques

  • Direct Calorimetry (Atwater Calorimeter): A chamber where a person lives/works, measuring total heat output.
  • Indirect Calorimetry (Benedict-Roth Spirometer): Closed-circuit apparatus. Measures oxygen consumption. Used for BMR estimation.
  • Douglas Bag Method: Collects expired air. Measures both $O_2$ consumption and $CO_2$ production. Useful for measuring energy expenditure during exercise.

Factors Increasing RQ

  • Hyperventilation
  • Metabolic acidosis
  • Overfeeding (Lipogenesis)
  • Exercise

Factors Decreasing RQ

  • Hypoventilation
  • Starvation (Ketosis)
  • Diabetes Mellitus (Ketoacidosis)
  • Gluconeogenesis

üõå Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR)

Definition: The energy released when the subject is at complete mental and physical rest (awake, reclining, post-absorptive state 12-14 hours after meal). It represents the minimum energy to maintain vital functions (heart rate, respiration, etc.).

Normal Value: ~1750-1900 Kcal/day (Adult Male). Around 1000 Kcal/m¬≤ body surface area/day.

Calculation of Surface Area

$$S = 0.007184 \times W^{0.425} \times H^{0.725}$$

Where $S$ = Surface area ($m^2$), $W$ = Weight ($kg$), $H$ = Height ($cm$).

Factors Influencing BMR

  1. 1 Surface Area: Directly proportional.
  2. 2 Age: Inversely proportional. Highest in children. Decreases with age.
  3. 3 Gender: Men have higher BMR than women (more muscle mass).
  4. 4 Temperature: Increases by ~7% for every 0.5¬∞C rise in body temperature (Fever).
  5. 5 Hormones: Thyroxine and Epinephrine increase BMR. (Hyperthyroidism: +20 to +80%; Hypothyroidism: -20 to -40%).
  6. 6 Climate: Higher in cold climates.
  7. 7 Starvation: BMR decreases (adaptive mechanism).
05

üçΩÔ∏è Specific Dynamic Action (SDA)

Definition: The extra heat production over and above the basal metabolic rate following ingestion of food. Also called "Thermic Effect of Food".

Food Nutrient Increase in BMR (SDA)
Proteins 30%
Carbohydrates 6%
Fats 4%
Mixed Diet ~10-15%

Cause: Energy required for digestion, absorption, transport, metabolism, and storage of nutrients (especially deamination of amino acids in liver).

06

ü•ó Principles of Nutrition

Balanced Diet: A diet containing all food constituents in proper proportion to meet energy and nutritional requirements. Calorie distribution:

  • Carbohydrates: 50-70%
  • Fats: 20-30%
  • Proteins: 10-15%

Basic Food Groups

Milk Group

Milk, cheese, dairy. Source of Calcium, Protein.

Meat Group

Meat, fish, poultry, eggs, pulses. Source of Protein.

Vegetable/Fruit Group

Green/yellow vegetables, citrus. Source of Vitamins, Minerals, Fiber.

Cereal Group

Whole grains. Source of Carbohydrates, B-vitamins.

Calorie Calculation Example

Total Calorie Requirement = BMR + Activity + SDA

Example for moderate worker (Medical Student):
Basal Energy (~24 hrs): 1525 Kcal
Activity (8 hrs moderate + 8 hrs sedentary): 1120 Kcal
SDA (10% of basal): 152.5 Kcal
Total: ~2800 Kcal/day
