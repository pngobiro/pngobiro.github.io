üçΩÔ∏è Digestion and Absorption

Dietary proteins are broken down into amino acids by proteolytic enzymes in the gastrointestinal tract.

Proteolytic Enzymes

Site Enzyme Specificity / Action
Stomach Pepsin Endopeptidase. Acts on aromatic AA bonds. Active at acidic pH.
Pancreas Trypsin Acts on basic AA bonds (Arg, Lys). Activates other zymogens.
Chymotrypsin Acts on aromatic AA bonds (Phe, Tyr, Trp).
Elastase Acts on small neutral aliphatic AA.
Carboxypeptidase Exopeptidase. Cleaves from C-terminal end.
Small Intestine Aminopeptidase Exopeptidase. Cleaves from N-terminal end.
Dipeptidase Hydrolyzes dipeptides into free amino acids.

Absorption of Amino Acids

Amino acids are absorbed by active transport mechanisms involving carrier proteins and $\mathrm{Na}^+$ ions (similar to glucose transport). The Meister cycle (Gamma-glutamyl cycle) involving glutathione is one mechanism proposed for amino acid transport.

Protein Turnover

Body proteins are continuously degraded and resynthesized. The rate of turnover varies (e.g., enzymes have short half-lives, collagen has a long half-life). Ubiquitin-proteasome pathway degrades intracellular proteins.

02

‚öóÔ∏è General Reactions of Amino Acids

1. Transamination

Reversible transfer of an amino group from an amino acid to a keto acid. Catalyzed by Transaminases (Aminotransferases) requiring Pyridoxal Phosphate (PLP).

$$\text{Amino Acid}_1 + \alpha\text{-Keto Acid}_2 \rightleftharpoons \alpha\text{-Keto Acid}_1 + \text{Amino Acid}_2$$

Common Reaction: Amino groups are channeled to $\alpha$-Ketoglutarate to form Glutamate.

2. Deamination

Removal of the amino group as ammonia ($\mathrm{NH}_3$).

(a) Oxidative Deamination

Occurs mainly in liver and kidney. Key enzyme: Glutamate Dehydrogenase.

$$\text{Glutamate} + \mathrm{NAD(P)^+} + \mathrm{H_2O} \rightleftharpoons \alpha\text{-Ketoglutarate} + \mathrm{NH_4^+} + \mathrm{NAD(P)H}$$

This enzyme is allosterically regulated (inhibited by ATP/GTP, activated by ADP/GDP).

(b) Non-oxidative Deamination

Catalyzed by Dehydrases (e.g., Serine dehydratase) acting on hydroxy amino acids.

3. Decarboxylation

Removal of carboxyl group as $\mathrm{CO}_2$ to form biogenic amines. Requires PLP.

  • Histidine $\rightarrow$ Histamine
  • Glutamate $\rightarrow$ GABA
  • Tryptophan $\rightarrow$ Serotonin
  • Tyrosine $\rightarrow$ Tyramine / Dopamine

4. Ammonia Transport

Ammonia is toxic to the CNS. It is transported in non-toxic forms:

  • Glutamine: Transport form from brain and tissues (Glutamate + $\mathrm{NH_3} \rightarrow$ Glutamine).
  • Alanine: Transport form from muscle (Glucose-Alanine Cycle).
03

üîÑ Urea Cycle (Krebs-Henseleit Cycle)

Detoxification of ammonia into urea. Occurs in the Liver. Spans mitochondria (first 2 steps) and cytosol (last 3 steps).

Steps of Urea Cycle

  1. 1 Formation of Carbamoyl Phosphate: Ammonia + $\mathrm{CO}_2$ + 2 ATP $\rightarrow$ Carbamoyl phosphate. Enzyme: CPS-I (Mitochondria). Rate-limiting step. Activator: N-Acetyl Glutamate (NAG).
  2. 2 Formation of Citrulline: Carbamoyl phosphate + Ornithine $\rightarrow$ Citrulline. Enzyme: OTC. Citrulline moves to cytosol.
  3. 3 Formation of Argininosuccinate: Citrulline + Aspartate + ATP $\rightarrow$ Argininosuccinate. Enzyme: Argininosuccinate synthase.
  4. 4 Cleavage to Arginine: Argininosuccinate $\rightarrow$ Arginine + Fumarate. Enzyme: Argininosuccinase. (Fumarate links to TCA cycle).
  5. 5 Formation of Urea: Arginine $\rightarrow$ Urea + Ornithine. Enzyme: Arginase. Ornithine re-enters mitochondria.
$$\mathrm{NH}_4^+ + \mathrm{CO}_2 + \text{Aspartate} + 3\mathrm{ATP} \rightarrow \text{Urea} + \text{Fumarate} + 2\mathrm{ADP} + 2\mathrm{P}_i + \mathrm{AMP} + \mathrm{PP}_i$$

Metabolic Disorders of Urea Cycle

  • Hyperammonemia Type I: Defect in CPS-I.
  • Hyperammonemia Type II: Defect in OTC (X-linked).
  • Citrullinemia: Defect in Argininosuccinate synthase.
  • Argininosuccinic aciduria: Defect in Argininosuccinase.
  • Hyperargininemia: Defect in Arginase.

Symptoms: Ammonia intoxication (tremors, slurring of speech, vomiting, coma, mental retardation).

04

üß¨ Metabolism of Specific Amino Acids

1. Glycine

Non-essential, glucogenic. Used for synthesis of:

  • Heme (with Succinyl-CoA)
  • Creatine (with Arginine and SAM)
  • Purines (C4, C5, N7)
  • Glutathione
  • Bile salts (Glycocholic acid)

Disorders: Primary Hyperoxaluria (excess oxalate stones).

2. Methionine and Cysteine (Sulfur containing)

Methionine: Essential. Precursor of SAM (S-Adenosyl Methionine), the universal methyl donor.

Cysteine: Formed from Methionine. Used for Glutathione and Taurine synthesis.

Transmethylation

Transfer of methyl group from SAM to acceptors (e.g., Norepinephrine $\rightarrow$ Epinephrine).

ü©∫

Disorders

  • Homocystinuria: Defect in Cystathionine synthase. High homocysteine, thrombosis, lens dislocation, skeletal abnormalities.
  • Cystinuria: Defect in renal transport of Cystine, Lysine, Arginine, Ornithine (COLA). Cystine stones in kidney.

3. Phenylalanine and Tyrosine

Phenylalanine: Essential. Converted to Tyrosine by Phenylalanine Hydroxylase.

Tyrosine: Precursor for:

  • Catecholamines: Dopamine, Norepinephrine, Epinephrine.
  • Thyroid Hormones: T3, T4.
  • Melanin: Skin pigment (via Tyrosinase).

Disorders of Aromatic Amino Acids

Disease Enzyme Defect Symptoms
Phenylketonuria (PKU) Phenylalanine Hydroxylase Mental retardation, mousy odor of urine (phenylketones), hypopigmentation.
Alkaptonuria Homogentisate Oxidase Black urine upon standing, Ochronosis (black pigment in cartilage/joints), arthritis.
Albinism Tyrosinase Absence of melanin (white hair, pink eyes, skin cancer risk).
Tyrosinemia Fumarylacetoacetate hydrolase (Type I) Liver failure, cabbage-like odor.

4. Tryptophan

Essential, both glucogenic and ketogenic. Precursor for:

  • Serotonin (5-HT): Neurotransmitter. Metabolized to 5-HIAA.
  • Melatonin: Pineal hormone.
  • Niacin (Vitamin B3): 60 mg Trp $\rightarrow$ 1 mg Niacin.

Hartnup's Disease: Defect in intestinal/renal transport of neutral amino acids (Tryp). Symptoms similar to Pellagra (due to niacin deficiency).

5. Branched Chain Amino Acids (Valine, Leucine, Isoleucine)

Metabolized via oxidative decarboxylation by Branched Chain $\alpha$-Keto Acid Dehydrogenase complex.

Maple Syrup Urine Disease (MSUD): Defect in this enzyme complex. Urine smells like burnt sugar/maple syrup. Severe neurological damage.
