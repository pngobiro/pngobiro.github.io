üëÅÔ∏è Hemoglobin Degradation and Jaundice

Jaundice (Icterus) is caused due to accumulation of bile pigments (Bilirubin) in the blood ($>2 \text{ mg/100ml}$), leading to yellow discoloration of tissues.

Degradation of Hemoglobin

RBCs have a life span of 120 days. They are degraded in the reticuloendothelial system (Spleen, Liver).

\text{Hemoglobin} \xrightarrow{\text{Heme oxygenase}} \text{Biliverdin} + \text{Iron} + \text{Globin}
\text{Biliverdin} \xrightarrow{\text{Biliverdin reductase}} \text{Bilirubin (Unconjugated)}

Unconjugated bilirubin is transported to the liver bound to Albumin. In the liver, it is conjugated with UDP-Glucuronic acid to form water-soluble Bilirubin Diglucuronide, which is excreted in bile.

Type Cause Van Den Bergh Test Urine Findings
Hemolytic (Pre-hepatic) Excessive hemolysis Indirect +ve Increased Urobilinogen
Hepatic (Infective) Liver damage (Viral, Toxic) Biphasic Bilirubin +, Urobilinogen +

Congenital Hyperbilirubinemias

  • Crigler-Najjar Syndrome: Deficiency of UDP-glucuronyl transferase. Severe unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia.
  • Gilbert's Syndrome: Defect in bilirubin uptake by liver. Mild unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia.
  • Dubin-Johnson Syndrome: Defect in excretion of conjugated bilirubin.

üß™ Liver Function and Detoxification

Liver Function Tests (LFTs)

Used to assess the synthetic, excretory, and metabolic functions of the liver.

Test Significance
Serum Bilirubin Total and Direct fractions to classify jaundice.
Serum Enzymes ALT (SGPT): Specific for liver damage.
AST (SGOT): Liver and heart damage.
ALP: Elevated in obstructive jaundice.
GGT: Marker for alcoholic liver disease.
Serum Albumin Synthesized by liver. Decreased in chronic liver disease (Cirrhosis). Reversal of A/G ratio.
Prothrombin Time Increased in liver damage (deficiency of clotting factors).

Detoxification

The conversion of toxic substances into non-toxic, excretable forms (biotransformation). Occurs in two phases:

Phase I (Modification)

  • Oxidation: Alcohols to acids.
  • Reduction: Nitro compounds to amines.
  • Hydrolysis: Drugs like aspirin.

Phase II (Conjugation)

Foreign substances are conjugated to become water-soluble.

  • Glucuronic Acid: Bilirubin, Benzoic acid, Steroids.
  • Glycine: Benzoic acid $\rightarrow$ Hippuric acid.
  • Active Sulfate (PAPS): Phenols $\rightarrow$ Ethereal sulfates.
  • Glutathione: Electrophilic xenobiotics.

üß¨ Renal Function Tests

Used to assess the functional capacity of the kidneys (glomerular filtration, tubular reabsorption, secretion).

Clearance Tests

Clearance is the volume of plasma completely cleared of a substance per minute.

C = \frac{U \times V}{B}

Where $U$ = Urine concentration, $V$ = Urine volume/min, $B$ = Plasma concentration.

  • Inulin Clearance: Gold standard for GFR ($120 \text{ ml/min}$). Inulin is freely filtered, neither reabsorbed nor secreted.
  • Creatinine Clearance: Most commonly used clinical measure of GFR. Normal: $90-120 \text{ ml/min}$.
  • Urea Clearance: Less than GFR due to reabsorption. Standard clearance ($54 \text{ ml/min}$) vs Maximum clearance ($75 \text{ ml/min}$).

Non-Protein Nitrogenous (NPN) Substances

Substance Normal Blood Level Diagnostic Significance
Blood Urea 15-40 mg/dl Increased in Renal failure (Uremia).
Pre-renal: Dehydration.
Post-renal: Obstruction (Stones).
Serum Creatinine 0.6-1.2 mg/dl More sensitive indicator of renal function than urea. Increased in renal failure.
Uric Acid 3-7 mg/dl Increased in Gout and renal failure.

üß™ Urine Analysis

Physical Examination: Volume (1500 ml/day), pH (Acidic ~6.0), Specific Gravity (1.015-1.025).

Pathological Constituents

Condition Constituent Causes
Glycosuria Glucose Diabetes Mellitus (Renal threshold > 180 mg/dl).
Proteinuria Protein (Albumin) Nephrotic syndrome, Glomerulonephritis.
Ketonuria Ketone Bodies Starvation, Uncontrolled Diabetes (Ketoacidosis).
Hematuria Blood (RBCs) Renal stones, Infection, Trauma.
Bile Salts/Pigments Bilirubin/Salts Obstructive Jaundice.

Renal Calculi (Kidney Stones)

Solid concretions of dissolved minerals in urine.

  • Calcium Oxalate: Most common. Seen in hypercalciuria/oxaluria.
  • Uric Acid: In acidic urine, Gout.
  • Struvite (Triple Phosphate): Magnesium ammonium phosphate. "Staghorn" calculi. Associated with infection.
  • Cystine: Rare, in Cystinuria.
05

üçΩÔ∏è Gastric Function Tests

Assesses gastric secretion (HCl, Pepsin). Used for Peptic Ulcer (Hyperacidity) or Pernicious Anemia/Gastric Cancer (Achlorhydria).

Tubeless Gastric Analysis (Diagnex Blue)

Uses a cation exchange resin coupled with a dye (Azure A). In the presence of free HCl in the stomach, the dye is displaced, absorbed, and excreted in urine. The intensity of blue color in urine is proportional to free acidity.

Stimulation Tests

Use agents like Pentagastrin or Histamine to stimulate acid secretion. Basal Acid Output (BAO) and Maximal Acid Output (MAO) are measured.

Atherosclerosis

Deposition of lipids (Cholesterol esters) in arterial walls leading to hardening. High LDL ("Bad cholesterol") and low HDL ("Good cholesterol") are major risk factors.

üéóÔ∏è Cancer and Tumor Markers

Tumor markers are substances found in blood/urine produced by tumor cells or the body in response to cancer. Used for screening, diagnosis, and monitoring.

  • PSA (Prostate Specific Antigen): Prostate Cancer.
  • AFP (Alpha-Fetoprotein): Liver Cancer (Hepatocellular carcinoma), Testicular cancer.
  • CEA (Carcinoembryonic Antigen): Colorectal cancer, GI cancers.
  • hCG (Human Chorionic Gonadotropin): Choriocarcinoma, Testicular cancer.
  • CA-125: Ovarian cancer.
  • Bence Jones Protein: Multiple Myeloma (Light chains in urine).
