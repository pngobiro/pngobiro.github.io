01

üß¨ Introduction to Hormones

Hormones are organic substances required in minute quantities, produced by ductless glands directly into the blood, and act on specific target tissues to regulate physiological activities.

Classification of Hormones

  • Peptide Hormones: Insulin, Glucagon, Pituitary hormones.
  • Steroid Hormones: Corticosteroids, Sex hormones (derived from cholesterol).
  • Catecholamines: Epinephrine, Norepinephrine (derived from Tyrosine).
  • Thyroid Hormones: $T_3, T_4$ (derived from Tyrosine).

Mechanism of Action

Cell Surface Receptors (Peptide Hormones)

Bind to membrane receptors and act via Second Messengers (e.g., cAMP, $IP_3$, DAG, $Ca^{2+}$).

Example: Epinephrine activates Adenylate cyclase $\rightarrow$ cAMP $\rightarrow$ Protein Kinase A.

Intracellular Receptors (Steroid/Thyroid)

Lipid-soluble hormones enter the cell, bind to cytoplasmic or nuclear receptors, and act on DNA (Gene Expression) to stimulate protein synthesis.

02

üß† Hypothalamus & Pituitary Gland

Hypothalamus: Produces Releasing Factors (e.g., GHRH, TRH) that control the Anterior Pituitary. Also produces Oxytocin and ADH, stored in the Posterior Pituitary.

Anterior Pituitary (Adenohypophysis)

Produces Tropic Hormones (stimulate other glands) and direct acting hormones.

1. Growth Hormone (GH)

Protein hormone (191 AA). Stimulates protein synthesis, lipolysis, and growth of somatic tissues (cartilage/bone). Hyperglycemic effect.

ü©∫

Disorders of GH

  • Gigantism: Excess in childhood (before epiphyseal closure).
  • Acromegaly: Excess in adulthood (thickened bones, coarse features).
  • Dwarfism: Deficiency in childhood.

2. Prolactin (LTH)

Stimulates development of mammary tissue and milk production (Lactogenic). Excessive production causes Galactorrhea and Amenorrhea.

Posterior Pituitary (Neurohypophysis)

Stores hormones produced by the hypothalamus.

1. Oxytocin

Stimulates uterine contractions (labour) and milk ejection (let-down reflex).

2. Vasopressin (ADH - Anti-Diuretic Hormone)

Increases water reabsorption in distal tubules of kidney. Causes vasoconstriction (BP increase).

üíß

Diabetes Insipidus

Caused by deficiency of ADH. Characterized by Polyuria (excess dilute urine) and Polydipsia (thirst).

03

ü¶ã Thyroid Gland

Produces Thyroxine ($T_4$) and Triiodothyronine ($T_3$). Synthesized from Tyrosine and Iodine on Thyroglobulin protein.

Functions

  • Increases Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR) and heat production (Calorigenic action).
  • Essential for physical and mental growth.
  • Increases carbohydrate absorption and metabolism.

Pathophysiology

Condition Cause Clinical Features
Hypothyroidism (Cretinism) Deficiency in children Mental retardation, dwarfism, thick tongue.
Hypothyroidism (Myxedema) Deficiency in adults Dry skin, intolerance to cold, low BMR, sluggishness.
Hyperthyroidism (Grave's Disease) Excess (LATS autoantibody) High BMR, weight loss, tremors, Exophthalmos (bulging eyes).
Goiter Iodine deficiency Enlargement of thyroid gland.
04

ü¶¥ Calcium Regulation

Parathormone (PTH)

Secreted by Parathyroid glands. Increases serum calcium by:

  1. Mobilizing calcium from bone (Resorption).
  2. Increasing renal reabsorption of calcium.
  3. Stimulating formation of Calcitriol (active Vitamin D) to increase intestinal absorption.

Hypoparathyroidism: Leads to Tetany (neuromuscular irritability, muscle spasms) due to hypocalcemia.

Calcitonin

Secreted by Parafollicular cells of Thyroid. Decreases serum calcium (Antagonist to PTH). Promotes deposition of calcium in bone.

05

üç¨ Pancreas (Islets of Langerhans)

1. Insulin ($\beta$-cells)

Peptide hormone (51 AA, A & B chains linked by disulfide bonds). Hypoglycemic hormone.

  • Promotes glucose uptake by tissues (Muscle, Adipose).
  • Stimulates Glycogenesis and Lipogenesis.
  • Inhibits Gluconeogenesis and Lipolysis.

2. Glucagon ($\alpha$-cells)

Hyperglycemic hormone. Secreted in response to hypoglycemia.

  • Stimulates Glycogenolysis (Liver) via cAMP.
  • Stimulates Gluconeogenesis.
üîÑ

Regulation

Insulin and Glucagon work reciprocally to maintain blood glucose homeostasis. Insulin is the only hormone that lowers blood glucose; Glucagon, Epinephrine, Cortisol, and GH increase it.

06

‚ö° Adrenal Gland

Adrenal Medulla

Secretes Catecholamines (Epinephrine, Norepinephrine). "Fight or Flight" hormones.

  • Increase heart rate and blood pressure.
  • Increase blood glucose (Glycogenolysis).
  • Increase lipolysis.

Adrenal Cortex

Secretes Steroid Hormones (Corticosteroids).

1. Glucocorticoids (Cortisol)

  • Promote Gluconeogenesis (Hyperglycemic).
  • Anti-inflammatory and immunosuppressive action.
  • Response to stress.

2. Mineralocorticoids (Aldosterone)

  • Regulate water and electrolyte balance.
  • Increase $Na^+$ and water reabsorption in kidneys (retention).
  • Increase $K^+$ excretion.
  • Regulated by Renin-Angiotensin System.
Condition Defect Symptoms
Cushing's Syndrome Excess Cortisol Moon face, buffalo hump, hyperglycemia, hypertension.
Addison's Disease Adrenal Insufficiency Hypoglycemia, hypotension, $Na^+$ loss, hyperpigmentation.
07

‚öß Gonadal Hormones

Testosterone (Testis)

Anabolic steroid. Promotes spermatogenesis, male secondary sexual characters, and protein anabolism.

Estrogens (Ovary)

Promote development of female secondary sexual characters, regulate menstrual cycle. Lipogenic effect.

Progesterone (Corpus Luteum)

Prepares uterus for implantation (pregnancy maintenance). Thermogenic action.

Key Points

  • ‚úì

    Insulin is the only hypoglycemic hormone; all others (Glucagon, Epi, GH, Cortisol) are counter-regulatory (hyperglycemic).

  • ‚úì

    cAMP is the common second messenger for many peptide hormones (Glucagon, Epinephrine).

  • ‚úì

    Steroid hormones act via nuclear receptors to regulate gene expression.

  • ‚úì

    Diabetes Insipidus is due to ADH deficiency; Diabetes Mellitus is due to Insulin deficiency/resistance.
