üçΩÔ∏è Digestion and Absorption

Nucleic acids are consumed in large quantities but are not utilized directly by the body. They are digested, catabolized, and excreted. Human beings are prototrophic for nucleic acids (can synthesize them endogenously).

Digestion Process

  • 1 Stomach/Intestine: Protein part of nucleoprotein is removed by proteolytic enzymes.
  • 2 Nucleases: Ribonuclease (RNase) and Deoxyribonuclease (DNase) from pancreatic juice convert nucleic acids to mononucleotides.
  • 3 Nucleotidases: Intestinal phosphatases hydrolyze nucleotides to nucleosides.
  • 4 Nucleosidases: Liberate free purine/pyrimidine bases and ribose/deoxyribose sugars.

Absorption: Nucleosides are absorbed by intestinal mucosa into portal blood, transported to the liver, and then to systemic circulation.

02

‚¨áÔ∏è Catabolism of Nucleosides

Purine Catabolism

The end product of purine metabolism in humans (primates) is Uric Acid. In lower animals, uric acid is further converted to allantoin by the enzyme uricase (absent in humans).

Uric Acid Metabolism

Miscible Pool: Total uric acid in body fluids. Males: $3.4 - 7.0 \text{ mg/dl}$; Females: $2.4 - 5.7 \text{ mg/dl}$.

Excretion: Mainly by kidneys ($250-750 \text{ mg/day}$). Some excreted in bile ($1/5^{th}$), where intestinal flora convert it to $\mathrm{CO}_2$ and $\mathrm{NH}_3$.

üë£

Gout

Hyperuricemia ($>7 \text{ mg/dl}$) leads to precipitation of sodium urate crystals in soft tissues, joints, and tendons (TOPHI), causing inflammation and arthritis.

  • Primary Gout: Genetic defect (e.g., PRPP synthetase overactivity).
  • Secondary Gout: Due to excess catabolism (e.g., Leukemia) or decreased excretion (Renal failure).
  • Treatment: Allopurinol (competitively inhibits Xanthine Oxidase).

Secondary Hyperuricemia causes: Old age, Febrile diseases, Hypoxia, Trauma, Kidney dysfunction, High non-vegetarian diets.

03

‚¨áÔ∏è Catabolism of Pyrimidines

Pyrimidine nucleosides are catabolized in the liver. The ring is opened and degraded to highly soluble products.

  • Cytosine/Uracil: $\rightarrow$ $\beta$-Alanine, $\mathrm{CO}_2$, $\mathrm{NH}_3$.
  • Thymine: $\rightarrow$ $\beta$-Aminoisobutyrate, $\mathrm{CO}_2$, $\mathrm{NH}_3$.

$\beta$-Alanine is converted to Acetyl-CoA (via Malonyl-CoA). $\beta$-Aminoisobutyrate is converted to Succinyl-CoA. Ammonia is converted to Urea.

04

üèóÔ∏è Biosynthesis of Purine Nucleotides

De Novo Synthesis

Purine ring is built on the Ribose-5-phosphate sugar. The pathway requires:

  • N-1: Aspartate
  • N-3, N-9: Glutamine (Amide N)
  • C-2, C-8: Formate (from THF)
  • C-6: Respiratory $\mathrm{CO}_2$
  • C-4, C-5, N-7: Glycine

Key Intermediate: Inosine Monophosphate (IMP). IMP serves as the precursor for both AMP (Adenylate) and GMP (Guanylate).

Regulation

Regulated by feedback inhibition at the committed step (PRPP Glutamyl Amidotransferase) by IMP, AMP, and GMP.

Salvage Pathway

Recycling of free purine bases (Adenine, Guanine, Hypoxanthine) to nucleotides. Important in brain and leukocytes. Two enzymes:

  1. 1 Adenine Phosphoribosyl Transferase (APRT): Adenine + PRPP $\rightarrow$ AMP + PPi
  2. 2 Hypoxanthine Guanine Phosphoribosyl Transferase (HGPRT):
    • Guanine + PRPP $\rightarrow$ GMP + PPi
    • Hypoxanthine + PRPP $\rightarrow$ IMP + PPi
üß¨

Lesch-Nyhan Syndrome

Genetic disorder (X-linked) caused by deficiency of HGPRT. Purines are not salvaged and are degraded to uric acid (hyperuricemia). Symptoms: Mental retardation, self-mutilation (biting lips/fingers), gout.

05

üèóÔ∏è Biosynthesis of Pyrimidine Nucleotides

The pyrimidine ring is synthesized first and then attached to Ribose-phosphate. Sources of atoms: Glutamine (N3), $\mathrm{CO}_2$ (C2), Aspartate (N1, C4, C5, C6).

Key Steps

  1. 1 Formation of Carbamoyl Phosphate: Catalyzed by cytosolic Carbamoyl Phosphate Synthetase II (CPS-II). (Distinct from mitochondrial CPS-I of Urea cycle).
  2. 2 Formation of Orotic Acid: Carbamoyl phosphate + Aspartate $\rightarrow$ Orotate.
  3. 3 Formation of Nucleotide: Orotate + PRPP $\rightarrow$ OMP (Orotidylate).
  4. 4 Decarboxylation: OMP $\rightarrow$ UMP (Uridylate).

UMP is the precursor for CTP and TMP.

Regulation

CPS-II is inhibited by UTP (feedback inhibition) and activated by PRPP. Aspartate transcarbamoylase is inhibited by CTP.

ü©∫

Orotic Aciduria

Metabolic disorder due to deficiency of Orotate phosphoribosyl transferase or OMP decarboxylase. Causes accumulation of orotic acid (crystals in urine), growth retardation, and megaloblastic anemia (refractory to B12/Folate). Treatment: Oral Uridine/Cytidine (bypass the block).

06

üß¨ Biosynthesis of Deoxyribonucleotides

Deoxyribonucleotides are formed from ribonucleotides by reduction of the ribose sugar at the 2' position.

$$\text{NDP} + \text{Thioredoxin (reduced)} \xrightarrow{\text{Ribonucleotide Reductase}} \text{dNDP} + \text{Thioredoxin (oxidized)}$$

Key Enzyme: Ribonucleotide Reductase.

Hydrogen Donor: Thioredoxin (a protein with sulfhydryl groups), which is regenerated by Thioredoxin Reductase and NADPH.

Thymidylate Synthesis: dUMP is methylated to dTMP by Thymidylate Synthase. The methyl donor is $N^5, N^{10}$-Methylene THF.

$$\text{dUMP} + \text{Methylene-THF} \rightarrow \text{dTMP} + \text{DHF}$$

This step is a target for chemotherapy drugs like 5-Fluorouracil and Methotrexate.
