01

üçΩÔ∏è Digestion and Absorption

The dietary carbohydrates comprise of the polysaccharides viz. starch and glycogen, the disaccharides-lactose, maltose, sucrose and the monosaccharides like glucose, fructose etc. The complex poly and disaccharides are converted into simple monosaccharides which are absorbed by the body.

Digestion Process

  • Mouth: Salivary amylase (ptyalin) acts on cooked starch to release maltose. Optimum pH 6.9. Requires $\mathrm{Ca^{++}}$ and $\mathrm{Cl^{-}}$.
  • Stomach: No carbohydrate digestion occurs (amylase deactivated by HCl).
  • Intestine: Pancreatic amylase (amylopsin) acts on starch/glycogen. Acts on $\alpha-1 \rightarrow 4$ glycosidic linkages. Branching points ($\alpha-1 \rightarrow 6$) form isomaltose, digested by isomaltase.
    Disaccharidases: Maltase, Lactase, Sucrase (Invertase).

Absorption of Carbohydrates

The monosaccharides are absorbed in the small intestine by three mechanisms:

  1. 1 Simple diffusion: Downhill movement from high to low concentration.
  2. 2 Active transport: Glucose and Galactose. Binds to a carrier protein along with $2\mathrm{Na^+}$ ions (SGLT). Energy comes from $\mathrm{Na^+}-\mathrm{K^+}$ ATPase pump.
  3. 3 Facilitated transport: Fructose is transported by a carrier protein which does not require energy.
ü•õ

Lactose Intolerance

A genetic defect where the enzyme lactase is deficient or absent. Lactose is not digested, leading to bacterial fermentation, diarrhea, and vomiting.

‚ö° Glycolysis (Embden-Meyerhof Pathway)

Glycolysis is the breakdown (lysis) of glucose to pyruvic acid under aerobic conditions and to lactic acid under anaerobic conditions. It occurs in the cytosol.

Bioenergetics of Glycolysis

Aerobic Glycolysis (Net Gain: 8 ATP)

Step Reaction ATP Change
Hexokinase Glucose $\rightarrow$ G-6-P -1 ATP
Phosphofructokinase F-6-P $\rightarrow$ F-1,6-BP -1 ATP
G3P Dehydrogenase 2 NADH produced ($2 \times 3$) +6 ATP
Phosphoglycerate Kinase 2 ATP produced +2 ATP
Pyruvate Kinase 2 ATP produced +2 ATP
Net Gain 8 ATP

Anaerobic Glycolysis (Net Gain: 2 ATP)

Occurs in RBCs and exercising muscle. NADH is not oxidized via ETC but used to reduce pyruvate to lactate. No production of 6 ATPs from NADH.

Net Gain: $4 \text{ (produced)} - 2 \text{ (utilized)} = \mathbf{2 \text{ ATP}}$.

üí°

Rapaport-Luebering Cycle

In RBCs, a bypass route forms 2,3-Bisphosphoglycerate (2,3-BPG) instead of 1,3-BPG. This reduces ATP production but supplies 2,3-BPG which aids in unloading oxygen from hemoglobin to tissues.

03

üîÄ Fate of Pyruvate

Under aerobic conditions, pyruvate enters the mitochondria and is converted to Acetyl-CoA by the Pyruvate Dehydrogenase (PDH) Complex.

PDH Complex requires 5 Coenzymes:

  1. 1 TPP (Thiamine)
  2. 2 Lipoic Acid
  3. 3 Coenzyme-A
  4. 4 FAD
  5. 5 NAD$^+$

This reaction produces 1 NADH per pyruvate (total 2 NADH per glucose = 6 ATP).

04

üîÑ Citric Acid Cycle (Krebs Cycle)

Occurs in the mitochondrial matrix. Acetyl-CoA is oxidized to $\mathrm{CO}_2$ and $\mathrm{H}_2\mathrm{O}$.

Energetics of TCA Cycle (Per Acetyl-CoA)

Enzyme Product ATP Yield
Isocitrate dehydrogenase NADH 3
$\alpha$-Ketoglutarate dehydrogenase NADH 3
Succinate thiokinase GTP (substrate level) 1
Succinate dehydrogenase FADH$_2$ 2
Malate dehydrogenase NADH 3
Total per Acetyl-CoA 12 ATP

Total ATP per Glucose: 12 $\times$ 2 = 24 ATP from TCA cycle.

Total ATP from Complete Oxidation of Glucose

Glycolysis 8 ATP
Pyruvate $\rightarrow$ Acetyl-CoA ($2 \times$ NADH) 6 ATP
TCA Cycle ($2 \times$ Acetyl-CoA) 24 ATP
Grand Total 38 ATP

*Note: Net gain is 36 ATP if the glycerol phosphate shuttle is used instead of the malate-aspartate shuttle.

Amphibolic Nature & Anaplerosis

TCA cycle is amphibolic (both catabolic and anabolic). Anaplerosis is the replenishment of depleted intermediates (e.g., Pyruvate Carboxylase replenishing Oxaloacetate).

05

üîã Glycogen Metabolism

Glycogen is the storage form of glucose in liver and muscle.

Glycogenesis (Synthesis)

  • Key Enzyme: Glycogen Synthase (adds glucose via $\alpha-1 \rightarrow 4$ linkage).
  • Branching Enzyme: Creates $\alpha-1 \rightarrow 6$ linkages.
  • Activated Glucose: UDP-Glucose.

Glycogenolysis (Breakdown)

  • Key Enzyme: Glycogen Phosphorylase (cleaves $\alpha-1 \rightarrow 4$ bonds, releasing Glucose-1-Phosphate).
  • Debranching Enzyme: Removes branches.
  • Limit Dextrin: The product remaining if phosphorylase acts alone without debranching enzyme.

06

üîÑ Gluconeogenesis

Formation of glucose from non-carbohydrate sources (Lactate, Amino acids, Glycerol). Occurs mainly in Liver and Kidney.

Key Enzymes (Bypass irreversible glycolytic steps)

  1. 1 Pyruvate carboxylase (Pyruvate $\rightarrow$ Oxaloacetate).
  2. 2 Phosphoenolpyruvate carboxykinase (Oxaloacetate $\rightarrow$ PEP).
  3. 3 Fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase.
  4. 4 Glucose-6-phosphatase.

Cori's Cycle (Lactic Acid Cycle)

Conversion of muscle glycogen to liver glycogen through blood lactate and back to muscle glycogen through blood glucose. Important for removing lactate from muscle.

07

üß™ HMP Shunt (Pentose Phosphate Pathway)

Alternative pathway for glucose oxidation. Does not generate ATP.

Significance

  1. Produces NADPH for reductive biosynthesis (fatty acids, steroids) and antioxidant defense (glutathione).
  2. Produces Ribose-5-phosphate for nucleic acid synthesis.

Clinical Significance

G6PD Deficiency: Deficiency of Glucose-6-Phosphate Dehydrogenase leads to hemolytic anemia due to lack of NADPH to keep glutathione reduced (protects RBCs from oxidative stress).

08

ü©∏ Blood Glucose Regulation & Diabetes

Normal Fasting Blood Glucose: 60-90 mg/dl.
Post-prandial: 100-140 mg/dl.
Renal Threshold: 180 mg/dl (threshold for glycosuria).

Hormonal Regulation

  • Insulin: Hypoglycemic hormone. Promotes glucose uptake, glycogenesis, lipogenesis.
  • Glucagon: Hyperglycemic. Promotes glycogenolysis, gluconeogenesis.
  • Epinephrine/Cortisol/GH: Insulin antagonists (Hyperglycemic).

Diabetes Mellitus

Characterized by hyperglycemia and glycosuria. Symptoms: Polyuria, Polydipsia, Polyphagia.

Diagnosis: Glucose Tolerance Test (GTT).

HbA1c (Glycosylated Hemoglobin): Reflects average blood glucose over past 4 months. Normal < 7%.
