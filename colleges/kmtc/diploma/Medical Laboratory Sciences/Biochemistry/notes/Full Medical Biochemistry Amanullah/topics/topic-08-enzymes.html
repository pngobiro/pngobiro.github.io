üß¨ Introduction to Enzymes

Enzymes are proteinaceous (and even nucleic acids) biocatalyst which alter (generally enhance) the rate of a reaction.

Free energy of activation and effect of catalysis

A chemical reaction like substrate to product, will take place when a certain number of substrate molecules at any instant, possess enough energy to attain an activated condition called the "transition state" in which the probability of making or breaking a chemical bond to form the product is very high. "Free energy of activation" is the amount of energy required to bring all the molecules in one gram mole of a substrate at a given temperature to the transition state.

In presence of a catalyst, the substrate combines with it to produce a transient state having a lower energy of activation than that of the substrate alone. This accelerates the reaction. Once the product is formed, the enzyme (catalyst) is free to combine with another molecule of the substrate and repeat the process. Though there is a change in the free energy of activation in presence of an enzyme, the overall free energy change of the reaction remains the same whether the reaction is catalyzed by an enzyme or not.

üìä Classification of Enzymes

1. Classification based upon the reaction catalyzed

Enzymes are broadly divided into six groups based on the type of reaction catalyzed.

Class Function Example
(a) Oxido-reductases Enzymes which bring about oxidation and reduction reactions. Lactate Dehydrogenase
Glutamate Dehydrogenase
(b) Transferases Catalyze transfer of groups from one substrate to another, other than hydrogen. Alanine Aminotransferase (ALT)
Aspartate Aminotransferase (AST)
(c) Hydrolases Catalyze the breakage of bonds with addition of water (hydrolysis). Pepsin, Trypsin, Amylase, Maltase
(d) Lyases Catalyze the breakage of a compound into two substances by mechanism other than addition of water. The resulting product always has a double bond. Aldolase
(e) Isomerases Catalyze the interconversion of optical and geometric isomers. Triose phosphate isomerase
(f) Ligases Catalyze union of two compounds. This is always an energy requiring process (active process). Pyruvate carboxylase

2. Other Classifications

Based upon presence/absence at a given time:

  • Inducible enzymes: Those enzymes that are synthesized by the cell whenever they are required. Synthesis usually requires an inducer. Ex. Invertase, HMG-CoA reductase, $\beta$-galactosidase.
  • Constitutive enzymes: Enzymes which are constantly present in normal amounts in the body, irrespective of inducers. Ex. Enzymes of glycolysis.

Based upon regulation:

  • Regulatory enzymes: Action is regulated depending upon the status of the cell. Action is increased or decreased by a modulator at an "allosteric site". Ex. Phosphofructokinase (PFK).
  • Non-regulatory enzymes: Action is not regulated. Ex. Succinate dehydrogenase.

Based upon place of action:

  • Intracellular enzymes: Produced by the cell and act inside the same cell. Ex. Enzymes of glycolysis.
  • Extracellular enzymes: Produced by a cell but act outside that cell independent of it. Ex. Digestive enzymes.

Based upon clinical importance:

  • Functional plasma enzymes: Present in plasma in high concentration and are functional there. Ex. Serum lipase, blood clotting enzymes.
  • Non-functional plasma enzymes: Present in plasma in negligible concentration and have no function there. Diagnostic importance.

Enzyme Commission (EC) Number

Enzymes are named in 4 digits by the enzyme nomenclature commission:
1st digit: Main classification
2nd digit: Sub-classification
3rd digit: Sub-sub classification
4th digit: Particular enzyme
Ex. 2.7.3.2 is creatine kinase.

‚öôÔ∏è Mechanism of Enzyme Action

An enzyme (or protein) should be in its native conformation to be biologically active. The three dimensional conformation of enzymes have a particular site where the substrate binds and is acted upon, this site is called the active site. The active site is earmarked into two specific areas (1) binding site and (2) catalytic site.

Michaelis and Menten established the theory of combination of enzyme with substrate to form the enzyme-substrate complex.

$$\mathrm{E}\left[\mathrm{Enzyme}\right] + \mathrm{S}\left[\mathrm{Substrate}\right] \rightarrow \mathrm{ES}\left[\mathrm{Enzyme-Substrate complex}\right] \rightarrow \mathrm{E} + \mathrm{Product}$$

Mechanisms of Acceleration

  1. 1 Proximity and orientation: The enzyme binds to the substrate so that the susceptible bond is in close proximity to the catalytic group and precisely oriented.
  2. 2 Strain and distortion or induced fit model: Binding of the substrate induces a conformational change in the enzyme which strains the active site and distorts the substrate, destabilizing it to form the product.
  3. 3 General acid-base catalysis: Amino acids at the active site act as proton donors or acceptors.
  4. 4 Covalent catalysis: Formation of unstable, covalently joined enzyme-substrate complexes.

üìâ Factors Affecting Enzyme Action

1. Effect of Temperature

The rate of enzyme reaction increases slowly with increase of temperature till it reaches a maximum (optimum temperature), then decreases due to denaturation. Majority of enzymes in human body have optimum temperatures between 37¬∞C and 40¬∞C. Vant Hoff's coefficient: For every 10¬∞C rise in temperature the enzyme activity increases 2 fold till the optimum temperature.

2. Effect of pH

Velocity increases till optimum pH and then decreases. Optimum pH for most enzymes is 7.4. Exceptions: Pepsin (1.5), Acid phosphatase (4.5), Alkaline phosphatase (9.8).

3. Effect of Substrate Concentration

Rate increases steadily till the enzyme is saturated (Vmax). Plotting velocity versus substrate concentration gives a hyperbolic curve.

üìê

Michaelis-Menten Kinetics

$$V = \frac {V _ {\max} [ S ]}{K _ {m} + [ S ]}$$

Where:
$V$ = Velocity
$V_{max}$ = Maximum velocity
$[S]$ = Substrate concentration
$K_m$ = Michaelis-Menten constant

Definition: $K_m$ is defined as the substrate concentration at which the velocity of the enzyme catalyzed reaction is half the maximum velocity ($V_{max}/2$).

High $K_m$ = Weak binding (low affinity).
Low $K_m$ = Strong binding (high affinity).

Lineweaver-Burk Plot

To overcome limitations of the Michaelis-Menten equation (which gives a hyperbolic curve), it is inverted to give a straight line plot:

$$\frac{1}{V} = \frac{K_m}{V_{\mathrm{max}}}\times \frac{1}{[S]} +\frac{1}{V_{\mathrm{max}}}$$

This is similar to $y = ax + b$, where $y = 1/V$, $x = 1/S$, slope $a = K_m/V_{max}$, and intercept $b = 1/V_{max}$.

Other Factors

  • Enzyme Concentration: Rate increases steadily with enzyme concentration (linear curve) if substrate is excess.
  • Product Concentration: Excess product decreases reaction rate due to feedback inhibition.
  • Light: Blue light enhances salivary amylase; U.V. light decreases velocity.
  • Ions: Presence/absence of ions enhances or reduces activity (e.g. $Mg^{2+}$ for Kinases).

üéØ Specificity and Coenzymes

Enzyme Specificity

  1. 1 Reaction specificity: Specific for the type of reaction catalyzed (e.g. amino acid oxidase vs decarboxylase).
  2. 2 Substrate specificity:
    • (a) Absolute specificity: Acts on one specific substrate only (e.g. Urease).
    • (b) Relative specificity: Acts on one particular bond.
    • (c) Group specificity: Acts on specific groups (e.g. Trypsin on basic amino acids, Pepsin on aromatic amino acids).
    • (d) Stereo specificity: Acts on one particular stereo isomer (e.g. Succinate dehydrogenase for fumarate, Cellulase for $\beta$ linkage).

Coenzymes

Non protein, heat stable, low molecular weight dialyzable organic compounds required for the action of enzymes. Generally vitamins act as coenzymes.

$$\text{Apoenzyme (protein)} + \text{Co-enzyme (non-protein)} \rightarrow \text{Holoenzyme (active)}$$

One unit of enzyme activity: Amount of enzyme that converts 1.0 $\mu$M of substrate per minute at 25¬∞C.

Enzyme turnover number: Number of substrate molecules transformed per minute by a single enzyme (Carbonic anhydrase = 36,000,000).

Zymogen: Inactive form of an enzyme (e.g., Pepsinogen).

Ribozyme: RNA molecules that catalyze reactions.

üõë Enzyme Inhibition

1. Irreversible Inhibition

Activity inhibited by covalent binding at active site. Cannot be dissociated.

  • Aldolase by iodoacetate.
  • Digestive enzymes by DFP (nerve gas).
  • Hexokinase/Urease by PCMB.

2. Reversible Inhibition

(a) Competitive Inhibition

Competition between substrate and inhibitor for the active site due to structural similarity. Clinically important as most drugs act this way.

Enzyme Substrate Competitive Inhibitor Clinical Significance
Succinate dehydrogenase Succinic acid Malonic acid -
Folic acid synthase (bacteria) PABA Sulphonamides Antibiotic
Alcohol dehydrogenase Methanol Ethanol Methanol poisoning treatment
Xanthine oxidase Hypoxanthine Allopurinol Gout treatment
Carbonic anhydrase - Acetazolamide Glaucoma treatment

(b) Non-competitive Inhibition (Allosteric)

Inhibitor binds to a site other than the active site (allosteric site). No competition with substrate. Shown by regulatory enzymes.

Regulatory enzymes Non-regulatory enzymes
Two sites (active & modulator) One active site
Sigmoid curve Hyperbolic curve
Non competitive inhibition Competitive inhibition
$V_{max}$ varies $V_{max}$ remains same
$K_m$ remains same $K_m$ varies

Reversible Covalent Modification

Activity regulated by cyclic inter-conversion (modified vs unmodified) via converting enzymes, often by phosphorylation/de-phosphorylation (e.g. Glycogen phosphorylase).

üè• Diagnostic Importance of Enzymes

Functional plasma enzymes: Present in plasma in high amounts and are functional there (e.g. clotting enzymes). Decrease indicates organ dysfunction.

Non-functional plasma enzymes: Present in trace amounts. Increase indicates tissue damage.

Enzyme Increased in
Lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) Myocardial infarction, myopathy, leukemias, hepatitis, carcinomatitis
AST (SGOT) Myocardial infarction
ALT (SGPT) Liver disorders
Creatine phosphokinase (CPK) Myocardial infarction, myopathy
Alkaline phosphatase Bone disorders, obstructive jaundice, hyperparathyroidism
Acid phosphatase Prostrate carcinoma
Amylase & Lipase Pancreatitis
Gamma glutamyl transpeptidase Alcoholism (Liver damage)

Diagnosis of Myocardial Infarction (MI)

  1. 1 Lactate dehydrogenase (LDH): Exists in 5 isoenzyme forms (tetramers of H and M chains).
    • $LDH_1 (H_4)$: Heart (Increases in MI)
    • $LDH_2 (H_3M)$: RBC
    • $LDH_3 (H_2M_2)$: Brain/Lungs
    • $LDH_4 (HM_3)$: Kidney
    • $LDH_5 (M_4)$: Liver/Skeletal Muscle
    Peak levels of $LDH$ (specifically $LDH_1$) maintain for 6 days after attack.
  2. 2 Creatine phosphokinase (CPK): Marker enzyme for MI. Increases first. Has 3 isoenzymes (Dimers of B & M):
    • MB: Cardiac muscle (Indicative of heart attack)
    • BB: Brain
    • MM: Skeletal muscle
  3. 3 Transaminases: AST (SGOT) increases 4-5 days following an attack.
