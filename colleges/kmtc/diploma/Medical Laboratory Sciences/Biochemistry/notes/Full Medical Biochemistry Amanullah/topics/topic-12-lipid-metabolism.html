üçΩÔ∏è Digestion and Absorption

Lipids are digested primarily in the intestine, although lingual and gastric lipases play a minor role, especially in infants. Bile salts emulsify fats, increasing the surface area for pancreatic lipase action.

Digestion Process

  • Mouth & Stomach: Minor digestion by lingual/gastric lipase (acts on short-chain fatty acids in milk).
  • Intestine: Major site. Bile salts emulsify fats. Pancreatic lipase (with co-lipase) hydrolyzes TAGs into 2-monoacylglycerols and free fatty acids.
  • Absorption: Micelles form and enter intestinal cells. Inside enterocytes, TAGs are re-synthesized and packaged into Chylomicrons for transport via lymph.

Clinical Correlate: Steatorrhea

Excretion of large amounts of fat in feces due to malabsorption (e.g., bile obstruction or pancreatic insufficiency).

üî• Oxidation of Fatty Acids

Beta-Oxidation (Knoop's Oxidation)

Oxidation of fatty acids at the $\beta$-carbon atom to remove two-carbon units as Acetyl-CoA.

1. Activation of Fatty Acids

Occurs in the cytosol. Fatty acids are converted to Fatty Acyl-CoA by Thiokinase (Acyl-CoA Synthetase). Requires 2 ATP equivalents.

2. Transport into Mitochondria (Carnitine Shuttle)

Fatty Acyl-CoA cannot cross the inner mitochondrial membrane. It is transported via Carnitine.

  • CAT-I: Forms Acyl-Carnitine in cytosol.
  • Translocase: Transports Acyl-Carnitine into matrix.
  • CAT-II: Regenerates Acyl-CoA in matrix.

3. Beta-Oxidation Proper

Repeated cycle of 4 reactions:

  1. 1 Oxidation (Dehydrogenation): Forms double bond ($\alpha-\beta$ unsaturated). Produces FADH$_2$. Enzyme: Acyl-CoA dehydrogenase.
  2. 2 Hydration: Adds water across double bond. Forms $\beta$-hydroxy acyl-CoA. Enzyme: Enoyl-CoA hydratase.
  3. 3 Oxidation (Dehydrogenation): Converts hydroxyl to keto group. Produces NADH. Enzyme: $\beta$-hydroxy acyl-CoA dehydrogenase.
  4. 4 Thiolysis (Cleavage): Cleaves $\beta$-keto ester. Releases Acetyl-CoA and Acyl-CoA (2 carbons shorter). Enzyme: Thiolase.

Other Types of Oxidation

  • $\alpha$-Oxidation: Removal of one carbon at a time. Important for branched-chain fatty acids (e.g., Phytanic acid) in brain tissue. Defect causes Refsum's disease.
  • $\omega$-Oxidation: Oxidation at the omega (methyl) end. Occurs in liver SER. Minor pathway, increases in defective $\beta$-oxidation.

üß™ Ketone Bodies

Ketone bodies: Acetone, Acetoacetate, $\beta$-Hydroxybutyrate.

Ketogenesis: Occurs in Liver mitochondria. Acetyl-CoA is the precursor.

$$\text{2 Acetyl-CoA} \rightarrow \text{Acetoacetyl-CoA} \rightarrow \text{HMG-CoA} \rightarrow \text{Acetoacetate}$$

Utilization (Ketolysis): Occurs in extrahepatic tissues (Heart, Muscle, Brain). Liver cannot utilize ketone bodies due to lack of the enzyme Thiophorase (Succinyl-CoA:Acetoacetate CoA transferase).

ü©∏

Ketoacidosis (Ketosis)

Increased accumulation of ketone bodies in blood (Ketonemia) and excretion in urine (Ketonuria).
Causes: Starvation, Diabetes Mellitus (Uncontrolled).
Effects: Metabolic acidosis, dehydration, fruity odor of breath (Acetone), coma.

üèóÔ∏è Biosynthesis of Fatty Acids (Lipogenesis)

Occurs in the cytosol of liver, adipose tissue, etc. Source of carbon is Acetyl-CoA. Source of reducing equivalents is NADPH (from HMP Shunt).

1. Formation of Malonyl-CoA

Rate-limiting step catalyzed by Acetyl-CoA Carboxylase. Requires Biotin and ATP.

$$\text{Acetyl-CoA} + \text{CO}_2 + \text{ATP} \rightarrow \text{Malonyl-CoA} + \text{ADP} + \text{Pi}$$

2. Fatty Acid Synthase (FAS) Complex

Multienzyme complex (Dimer). Contains ACP (Acyl Carrier Protein) with phosphopantetheine arm.

Cycle of reactions: Condensation $\rightarrow$ Reduction $\rightarrow$ Dehydration $\rightarrow$ Reduction.

This cycle repeats to elongate the chain by 2 carbons each time until Palmitate (16C) is formed.

05

ü•ö Biosynthesis of Cholesterol

Occurs mainly in the liver. Precursor: Acetyl-CoA.

Key Steps

  1. 1 Formation of HMG-CoA: 3 Acetyl-CoA condense.
  2. 2 Formation of Mevalonate: HMG-CoA Reductase catalyzes this step. This is the rate-limiting enzyme of cholesterol synthesis. Inhibited by Statins.
  3. 3 Formation of Isoprenoid units: Decarboxylation to 5-carbon units.
  4. 4 Formation of Squalene: Condensation of isoprenoids (30C).
  5. 5 Cyclization: Squalene cyclizes to Lanosterol $\rightarrow$ Cholesterol (27C).

Regulation

HMG-CoA Reductase is inhibited by Cholesterol (Feedback inhibition) and Glucagon. It is activated by Insulin.

Lipids are transported in blood as lipoproteins. Structure: Hydrophobic core (TAG, Cholesterol esters) + Hydrophilic shell (Phospholipids, Free cholesterol, Apoproteins).

Fatty Liver

Abnormal accumulation of fat (TAG) in the liver.

  • Causes: Alcoholism, Diabetes, Starvation, Toxins, Lipotropic deficiency.
  • Lipotropic Factors: Substances required for normal mobilization of fat from liver (e.g., Choline, Methionine, Betaine, Inositol, Vitamin B12, Folic Acid). They help in synthesis of phospholipids/lipoproteins.

Atherosclerosis

Deposition of lipids (cholesterol) in arterial walls leading to hardening and narrowing. Risk factors: High LDL, Low HDL, Smoking, Hypertension, Diabetes.
