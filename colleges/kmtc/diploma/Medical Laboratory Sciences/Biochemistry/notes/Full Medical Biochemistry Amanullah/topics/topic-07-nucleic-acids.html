üìú Introduction and Chemistry

The continuity of species since ages is being maintained without any amendments. It is due to the genetic material present in stable form in a cell. This genetic material is unique and specific for a given species.

The genetic material is chemically termed as DNA, i.e., Deoxyribonucleic acid. DNA is a linear polymer of nucleotide bases arranged in a unique sequence specific for individuals of different species. The message contained in the DNA encodes the instructions for forming all other cellular components.

üí°

Key Definition

Any change in the message contained in the genetic material is called mutation and if expressed from generations to generations it results in evolution of species.

Chemistry: Nucleic acids are acidic compounds found in the cell nucleus, cytoplasm, mitochondria, and chloroplast. They are high molecular weight nitrogenous organic compounds. Nucleic acids are defined as polynucleotides, i.e., chain-like polymers of unto thousands of nucleotide units. Each nucleotide is a molecular complex of nucleoside and phosphoric acid.

02

üèóÔ∏è Nucleosides and Nucleotides

Nucleoside

A nucleoside is composed of two components‚Äî(a) nitrogenous base and (b) five-carbon sugar (pentose).

1. Nitrogenous bases

The nitrogenous bases are derivatives of two parent heterocyclic compounds, i.e., purine and pyrimidine.

2. Pentose Sugars

The pentose sugar present in the nucleic acids is ribose in RNA and deoxyribose in DNA.

A nucleoside is formed by the linkage of the nitrogenous base to the -OH group at the first carbon of the pentose sugar through an N-glycosyl linkage. Purine bases are linked through N-9 and pyrimidine bases through N-1.

  • Adenine + Pentose = Adenosine
  • Guanine + Pentose = Guanosine
  • Cytosine + Pentose = Cytidine
  • Thymine + Pentose = Thymidine
  • Uracil + Pentose = Uridine

Nucleotide

A nucleotide is formed by esterification of phosphoric acid to the -OH group present at the fifth (5th) position of the pentose sugar in a nucleoside.

To avoid confusion, members of the pentose sugar are numbered as $1', 2', 3', 4'$ and $5'$ (read as one prime, etc.).

üìù

Examples of Nucleotides

  • Adenine + Ribose + Phosphoric acid ‚Üí Adenylate (in RNA)
  • Adenine + Deoxyribose + Phosphoric acid ‚Üí Deoxyadenylate (in DNA)
  • Cytosine + Ribose + Phosphoric acid ‚Üí Cytidylate (in RNA)
  • Thymidine + Deoxyribose + Phosphoric acid ‚Üí Deoxythymidylate (in DNA)
03

üß¨ Deoxyribonucleic Acid (DNA)

Polynucleotide Structure

A polynucleotide is formed by linkage of nucleotides through phosphodiester bonds formed between the hydroxyl group at $3'$ carbon of the sugar of one nucleotide and the phosphate at the $5'$ carbon of the other nucleotide. This creates a $5'$ end (free phosphate) and a $3'$ end (free hydroxyl). The sequence is represented in the $5' \rightarrow 3'$ direction:

$5' - A - A - T - C - T - G - G - C - A - C - T - 3'$

Double Helical Structure of DNA (Watson and Crick Model)

Proposed by Watson and Crick in 1953.

  1. 1DNA is made of two helical chains coiled around the same axis to form a right-handed double helix.
  2. 2The two chains are anti-parallel; the $5'$ end of one strand is adjacent to the $3'$ end of the other.
  3. 3The distance between each turn is $3.6 \text{ nm}$ (formerly $3.4 \text{ nm}$).
  4. 4There are 10.5 nucleotides per turn.
  5. 5The structure creates major and minor grooves.
  6. 6Hydrophilic sugar-phosphate backbones are on the outside; hydrophobic bases are on the inside.
  7. 7Base pairing is specific: Adenine pairs with Thymine ($A=T$, 2 hydrogen bonds) and Guanine pairs with Cytosine ($G \equiv C$, 3 hydrogen bonds).
  8. 8The two strands are complementary; the number of 'A' bases equals 'T' bases, and 'G' equals 'C'.

Functions of DNA

  • Stores complete genetic information for proteins and RNA.
  • Source of information for synthesis of all cellular body proteins and enzymes.
  • Determines activities throughout life cycle (growth, maturity, senescence).
  • Determines individuality and identity.
  • Duplicates (replicates) to transfer genetic material to daughter cells.

üì¶ DNA Packaging and Physical Properties

Packaging of DNA

The total contour length of DNA in a single human cell is about 2 metres, yet it fits into a nucleus 5-10 nm in diameter. This is achieved by supercoiling and association with proteins.

Eukaryotic Packaging

The helical DNA is bound tightly around beads of basic proteins called histones to form nucleosomes.

  • Nucleosome: Contains eight histone molecules (two each of $\mathrm{H}_2\mathrm{A}, \mathrm{H}_2\mathrm{B}, \mathrm{H}_3, \mathrm{H}_4$) winding 146 base pairs of DNA.
  • Spacer DNA: 54 base pairs between nucleosomes, associated with histone $\mathrm{H}_1$.
  • 30 nm fibre: Nucleosomes organized close together (100-fold compaction).
  • Rosette: Six loops of 30 nm fibre bound by scaffold proteins.
  • Chromatid: Formed by 30 rosettes forming a coil, and 10 such coils forming a chromatid.

Physical Properties of DNA

(a) Denaturation (Melting)

When DNA is subjected to heat ($>80-90^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$) or extreme pH, the double helix unfolds due to disruption of hydrogen bonds. The temperature at which 50% of DNA is denatured is the Melting Temperature ($T_M$). Higher $G \equiv C$ content leads to higher $T_M$. When slowly cooled, strands anneal (rewind).

(b) Buoyant Density

Measured by centrifugation in Cesium Chloride (CsCl). Dependent on $G \equiv C$ content; higher G-C content results in higher buoyant density.

üß¨ Ribonucleic Acid (RNA)

RNA is a polyribonucleotide containing ribose sugar and Uracil (U) instead of Thymine (T). It is generally single-stranded.

Types of RNA

1. Messenger RNA (mRNA)

Carries genetic message from DNA to ribosome. Synthesized in the nucleus. In prokaryotes, it can be monocistronic (coding for one polypeptide) or polycistronic (coding for multiple). In eukaryotes, it is mostly monocistronic.

2. Transfer RNA (tRNA)

An adapter molecule that reads mRNA information and transfers specific amino acids to the growing polypeptide chain. Structure is clover-leaf shaped.

  • Amino acyl arm: Carries specific amino acid at 3' end (CCA sequence).
  • Anticodon arm: Contains 3 nucleotide bases complementary to mRNA codon.
  • DHU arm: Contains dihydrouridine.
  • T$\Psi$C arm: Contains ribothymidine and pseudouridine.

3. Ribosomal RNA (rRNA)

Associated with proteins to form ribosomes, the site of protein synthesis. Prokaryotic ribosomes are 70S (30S + 50S subunits) containing 16S, 23S, 5S rRNAs. Eukaryotic ribosomes are 80S (40S + 60S subunits) containing 18S, 28S, 5.8S, 5S rRNAs.
