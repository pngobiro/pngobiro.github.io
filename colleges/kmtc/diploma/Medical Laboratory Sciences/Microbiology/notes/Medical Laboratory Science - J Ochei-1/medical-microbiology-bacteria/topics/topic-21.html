üîç Overview

The term 'Spirochaete' describes a family of bacteria, Spirochaetaceae, whose members are long, flexible, motile, and twisted spirally round a long axis. They vary in size ($5-20 \mu\mathrm{m}$ long), move by bending and rotating, and divide by transverse fission. They are not easily stained by ordinary methods.

ü¶† Genus Treponema

Type species: Treponema pallidum. Small, slender, flexible anaerobic organisms with 6‚Äì12 evenly sized coils. Visible only with dark field microscopy.

Syphilis

Caused by T. pallidum subspecies pallidum.

Stages of Sexually Transmitted Syphilis

  1. 1 Primary Syphilis: Hard Chancre (painless, indurated ulcer) appears 2‚Äì4 weeks after infection. Highly infectious. Heals spontaneously.
  2. 2 Secondary Syphilis: 2‚Äì8 weeks after primary. Skin rashes, mucous patches, generalized lymphadenopathy, condylomata lata. Highly infectious.
  3. 3 Latent Syphilis: Asymptomatic phase. Early latent is infectious to fetus; Late latent is generally non-infectious.
  4. 4 Tertiary (Late) Syphilis: >3 years later. Gummas (granulomas), Cardiovascular syphilis, Neurosyphilis (General Paralysis of the Insane - GPI). Non-infectious.
üë∂

Congenital Syphilis

Transplacental transmission. Can cause fetal death, abortion, or "Saddle nose", Hutchinson's teeth, and other deformities in live-born infants.

Other Treponemal Infections

  • Yaws (Framboesia): T. pallidum subsp. pertenue. Non-venereal, skin/bone lesions.
  • Pinta: T. carateum. Skin depigmentation.
  • Bejel (Endemic Syphilis): T. pallidum subsp. endemicum.
03

üî¨ Laboratory Diagnosis of Syphilis

Microscopy

Dark Ground Microscopy: Definitive for primary (chancre fluid) and secondary stages.

  1. Clean lesion with saline.
  2. Gently scrape/squeeze to get serous exudate (avoid blood).
  3. Examine immediately wet film. Look for slender, rotating, flexing spirochaetes.

Serological Tests (STS)

1. Non-Treponemal Tests (Reagin)

Detect non-specific antibodies (Reagin) against cardiolipin.

  • VDRL (Venereal Disease Research Lab)
  • RPR (Rapid Plasma Reagin)

Pros: Screening, monitoring treatment (titres fall after cure).

Cons: Biological False Positives (BFP) in malaria, leprosy, pregnancy, SLE.

2. Treponemal Tests (Specific)

Detect specific antibodies against T. pallidum.

  • TPHA (T. pallidum Haemagglutination)
  • FTA-ABS (Fluorescent Treponemal Antibody - Absorbed)

Pros: Confirmatory, very specific.

Cons: Remain positive for life (cannot monitor treatment).

üêï Genus Leptospira

Thin, coiled spirochaetes ($6-20 \mu\mathrm{m}$) with one or both ends hooked. Motile (spinning/undulating). Cultivable.

Pathogenicity

Leptospirosis (Weil's Disease): Zoonosis. Reservoirs: Rats (L. icterohaemorrhagiae), Dogs (L. canicola), Pigs.
Transmission: Urine of infected animals contaminating water/soil entering through skin/mucosa.
Symptoms: Fever, jaundice, haemorrhage, renal failure (Weil's disease), meningitis.

Laboratory Diagnosis

  1. 1 Specimens: Blood (1st week), Urine (2nd week onwards).
  2. 2 Microscopy: Dark ground microscopy of centrifuged urine or plasma. Look for rotating/spinning hooked spirochaetes.
  3. 3 Culture: Korthof's or Fletcher's medium (contains rabbit serum). Incubate at 30¬∞C for weeks.
  4. 4 Serology:
    • Screening: Macroscopic slide agglutination (killed antigen).
    • Confirmatory: Microscopic Agglutination Test (MAT) using live antigens. Titre > 1:300 significant.

Treatment: Penicillin (high dose) or Tetracycline.
