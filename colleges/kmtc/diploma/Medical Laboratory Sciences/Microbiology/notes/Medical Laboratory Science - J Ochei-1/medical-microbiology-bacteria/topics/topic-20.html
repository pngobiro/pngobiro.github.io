ü¶† Genus Clostridium

The genus consists of Gram positive, anaerobic spore forming rods. A few strains are aerotolerant. The shape and the position of the spores within the bacterial cell aid in the identification of the species.

  • Characteristics: Clostridia are motile (except C. perfringens and C. tetani type 6). They are Gram positive pleomorphic rods about $3-8 \times 0.10 \ \mu\mathrm{m}$ with rounded ends. Some species are haemolytic and some may show swarming. They produce powerful exotoxins.
  • Habitat: Normally present in soil, dust, water and in the intestinal tract of humans and animals.

Classification

A. Gas Gangrene Group

  • Established pathogens: C. perfringens, C. septicum, C. oedematiens (C.novyi).
  • Less pathogenic: C. histolyticum, C. fallax, C. bifermentans, C. sporogenes.

B. Tetanus

C. tetani

C. Food Poisoning Group

  • Gastro-enteritis: C. perfringens (Type A).
  • Botulism: C. botulinum.

D. Pseudomembranous Colitis

C. difficile

02

ü§¢ Pathogenicity

1. C. perfringens (C. welchii)

Produces alpha toxin (lecithinase C) and other toxins (collagenase, proteinase, hyaluronidase, DNase).

  • Type A: Causes gas gangrene (myonecrosis), food poisoning, puerperal infection and septicaemia. Food poisoning is caused by non-haemolytic strains whose spores resist boiling; symptoms appear 8‚Äì12 hours after ingestion.
  • Type C: Causes acute jejunitis (pigbel), a severe intestinal disease (necrotizing enteritis) associated with eating infected pigmeat.

2. C. tetani

Produces a powerful neurotoxin (tetanospasmin). Causes Tetanus (lockjaw), characterised by tonic muscular spasms. Infection enters through puncture wounds (e.g., rusty nail, septic abortion, unsterile instruments).

3. C. botulinum

Produces a potent neurotoxin in food under anaerobic conditions (e.g., canned food). Ingestion causes Botulism, a neuroparalytic disease. Symptoms (blurred vision, difficulty swallowing/breathing) appear 12‚Äì26 hours after ingestion. Types A, B, and E cause human botulism.

Safety Warning

Specimens meant for C. botulinum should carry the biohazard label and should be handled with extra caution.

4. C. difficile

Causes Pseudomembranous colitis (antibiotic-associated diarrhoea). Occurs when broad-spectrum antibiotics eliminate normal flora, allowing C. difficile to overgrow and produce toxins.

üî¨ Laboratory Diagnosis

Microscopy

C. perfringens

Stout Gram positive bacilli with straight, parallel sides and rounded ends ($4-6 \mu\mathrm{m} \times 1 \mu\mathrm{m}$). Usually no spores seen in clinical specimens (spores are central/subterminal). Often capsulated.

C. tetani

Long, slender Gram positive rods with round, terminal spores giving the classical "drum stick" appearance. Motile.

Culture

Media used: Gentamycin blood agar, Plain blood agar, Cooked meat broth (RCM), Litmus milk, Lactose-egg-yolk-milk agar. Incubate anaerobically at 35‚Äì37¬∞C.

  • C. perfringens: Produces double zone haemolysis on blood agar (inner complete, outer partial). In Litmus milk, produces "stormy clot" (acid clot broken by gas). In RCM, turns meat pink (saccharolytic).
  • C. tetani: Produces a thin spreading film (swarming) on blood agar. In RCM, turns meat black (proteolytic).
  • C. septicum: Beta-haemolytic, irregular colonies.
  • C. botulinum: Large irregular colonies. Lipase positive (pearly layer on egg-yolk agar).
  • C. difficile: Non-haemolytic. Characteristic "horse manure" odour.

Nagler Reaction

Detects Lecithinase C (alpha toxin) produced by C. perfringens. The enzyme breaks down lecithin in egg yolk producing opacity.

Procedure

  1. 1Coat one half of an egg-yolk agar plate with C. perfringens antitoxin.
  2. 2Streak the test organism across both halves.
  3. 3Incubate anaerobically.
  4. 4Positive Result: Opacity around colonies on the side without antitoxin. No opacity on the side with antitoxin (neutralisation).

Reverse CAMP Test

Specific for C. perfringens. It produces a "bow-tie" or "bullet-shaped" zone of enhanced haemolysis when streaked towards Group B Streptococcus on blood agar.

ü¶† Genus Propionibacterium

Type species: P. acnes.

Characteristics: Anaerobic (aerotolerant) Gram positive diphtheroid-like rods. Non-motile. Catalase positive, Indole positive.

Pathogenicity: Normal skin flora. Associated with Acne. Can cause opportunistic infections (endocarditis, prosthetic joint infection).
