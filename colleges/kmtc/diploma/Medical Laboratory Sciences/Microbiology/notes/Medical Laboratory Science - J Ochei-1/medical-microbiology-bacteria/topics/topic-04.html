üìñ Definitions

Sterilisation
Sterilisation may be defined as any process by means of which all forms of microbial life (bacteria, spores, fungi and viruses) are completely destroyed. The word ‚Äòsterile‚Äô denotes the complete freedom from, or absence of, all living microbes and other forms of life.
Disinfection
This is any process, chemical or physical, whereby harmful microbial agents are destroyed. Disinfection essentially destroys infectious micro-organisms but not necessarily their spores. Disinfection is applied on inanimate objects only. The common disinfecting agents (disinfectants) are chemicals, boiling water, flowing steam and ultraviolet radiation. Disinfectants are bactericidal.
Antiseptic
Antiseptics are substances which, when used on micro-organisms, will render them inactive by inhibiting their multiplication. The effectiveness of the antiseptic depends on the character of preparation or the method of application. Antiseptics are bacteriostatic and they can be applied on living tissues.
Germicide
This is any substance that will kill germs or microbes. Chemical agents are employed to kill pathogens but not necessarily their resistant spores; and they are used in solution form on living tissues as well as on inanimate objects. Other commonly used terms with similar definitions are bactericide, fungicide, viricide and sporicide. Most disinfectants are germicides.
Bacteriostat
This is a chemical agent that can inhibit the growth or multiplication of bacteria. Bacteriostatic action is reversible.

Basic Principle

An important aim of all sterilisation and disinfection strategy is to prevent the spread of bacteria. In certain cases, all microbes are regarded as potential pathogens and therefore it is essential to eliminate all forms of microbial life. All microbiological techniques require appropriate and adequate sterilisation.

‚öôÔ∏è Methods of Sterilisation

The various methods of sterilisation are designed to kill resistant spores. The vegetative cells can be destroyed with relative ease.

Physical Methods: Heat

Heat is the most rapid, and best method of sterilisation. It is the method of choice if the material to be sterilised is stable enough to withstand the required temperature necessary to kill the microbes. The time needed for sterilisation depends on the initial number of organisms present and also on the temperature used. Higher the temperature, shorter the time needed. Heat kills bacteria by coagulation of their proteins. Different types of bacteria show differences in heat susceptibility. Spores need high temperatures while vegetative bacteria can be destroyed at lower temperatures.

Dry Heat

This is the preferred method for sterilising glasswares, e.g., glass syringes and materials such as oils and powder which cannot be penetrated by water or steam. Dry heat is applied in the following ways:

  1. Flaming: The articles are passed through the Bunsen flame. The articles need not become red hot. It is used for scalpels, mouths of culture tubes, glass slides and points of forceps. It is of limited immediate use as only the surfaces touched by the flame are sterilised.
  2. Red heat: Inoculating wire loops and needles are heated in the Bunsen flame until red hot. This is also of limited immediate use.
  3. Incineration: Micro-organisms exposed to open flames are burnt and killed. This is ideal for the carcasses of infected animals.
  4. Hot air oven: This is an oven that has a double walled chamber with a stout door, and it is electrically heated and thermostatically controlled. Sterilisation times of three hours at 140 ¬∞C, one hour at 160 ¬∞C and 30 minutes at 180 ¬∞C are commonly employed. The hot air oven is ideal for all dry glassware, non-absorbent cotton wool, paraffin oil, and powder. It is not useful for culture media, liquids, rubber connections, glass to metal fitting and fabrics, e.g., masks, towels, or gowns.

Moist Heat

  1. Water at 60‚Äì65¬∞C: At 60‚Äì65¬∞C, water will kill most vegetative organisms. It does not fully sterilise but drastically reduces the number of pathogenic bacteria. This low temperature of water is useful mainly in the pasteurisation of milk and in the preparation of some heat killed bacterial vaccines.
  2. Water at 100¬∞C (Boiling): Boiling water at 100¬∞C is frequently used in an emergency in the wards, to sterilise syringes, scissors and forceps. Most vegetative bacteria will die in 5‚Äì10 minutes when immersed in boiling water, but some spores will survive at this temperature for several hours.
  3. Steam sterilisation: Steam sterilisation is the most effective technique of moist heat sterilisation.
    • Steam at 100¬∞C: Usually achieved in the Koch steamer. Used for carbohydrate media (Tyndallisation). The materials are exposed to free steam at 100¬∞C for 30 minutes on each of three successive days.
    • Low temperature steam at 80¬∞C: Used to sterilise such articles that cannot withstand the normal heat sterilisation procedures (e.g., blankets, mattresses). Special autoclave operating at reduced pressure is used. Formalin vapour is introduced to enhance sterilisation.
    • High temperature steam (Autoclave): Steam above 100¬∞C is the most efficient means of sterilisation. The boiling point of water is raised by increasing the pressure inside the autoclave. The usual sterilisation temperature is 121¬∞C at 15 lbs per square inch (psi) pressure for 15 minutes.

‚úÖ Testing Autoclave Efficiency

There are several methods of testing whether or not material is adequately sterilised.

  1. Brown's steriliser control tubes: Contain an indicator liquid that changes from red to green if the correct temperature/time combination is employed.
  2. Biological indicator: Filter paper strips impregnated with spores of Bacillus stearothermophilus. After autoclaving, the strip is cultured at 55¬∞C. No growth indicates proper sterilisation.
  3. Autoclave tapes: Tapes like the Bowie-Dick autoclave tape change colour uniformly when sterilisation is adequate.
  4. Thermocouples: Used to monitor temperature within the contents.

Sterilisation Failure

Failures may be due to:

  • Incomplete removal of air (air pockets reduce temperature).
  • Incorrect methods of packaging.
  • Careless loading.
  • Failure to time correctly (holding time).
  • Materials impervious to steam (e.g., oils).
  • Faulty equipment.

Inspissation

This is the method of coagulating and further sterilising culture media that were initially prepared aseptically. The media should contain a coagulable protein such as serum or egg (e.g., Loeffler‚Äôs medium or Lowenstein Jensen‚Äôs medium). The temperature is controlled between $75^{\circ} - 80^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$. Inspissation is carried out for two hours on each of three consecutive days.

‚ò¢Ô∏è Radiation and Filtration

Radiation

Non-ionising Radiation:

  • Infra-red radiation: Kills by oxidation as a result of heat generated. Sterilises at $190^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ for 10 minutes.
  • Ultraviolet light: Absorbed by nucleic acids (240‚Äì280 nm), altering DNA structure. Used to sterilise surfaces and air.

Ionising Radiation: Penetrates non-living and living organisms readily. Also called 'cold sterilisation'. Used for heat-sensitive medical supplies (plastic syringes, drugs). Cathode rays and gamma rays (Cobalt 60) are used. Bacillus subtilis spores are used for control.

Filtration

Used to sterilise heat-sensitive fluids (toxins, enzymes, drugs, serum, sugars) and air.

  1. Candle-type Filters: Made from diatomaceous earth (Berkefeld and Mandler).
  2. Porcelain Filters: Made of unglazed porcelain (Chamberland Doulton).
  3. Sintered Glass Filter: Finely ground glass fused at the bottom.
  4. Seitz Filter: Asbestos disk or pad supported in a metal mount.
  5. Hemmings Filter: Small asbestos pad between two bijou bottles.
  6. Membrane Filters: Made of cellulose esters. Most widely used due to versatility and accurate pore grading. Can also be used for bacterial counts.
  7. Air Filter: HEPA (High Efficiency Particle Arrester) filters used for safety cabinets and laminar flow cabinets.

üß™ Chemical Sterilisation

Mode of Action

  1. Interfering with the enzymatic system.
  2. Coagulation of proteins.
  3. Disruption of cell membrane.
  4. Oxidative damage.

Major Groups of Disinfectants

Alcohols

Denature proteins. 70% concentration has optimum activity as skin antiseptic.

Aldehydes

Formaldehyde: Highly bactericidal (formalin). Irritating and toxic.
Glutaraldehyde: 2% buffered solution (Cidex). Effective against spores. Used for endoscopes.

Phenolic Compounds

Used at 2‚Äì3%. Effective against spores. Unpleasant odour. Examples: 'Sudol', 'Hycolin'.

Chlorxylenols

Example: Dettol. Non-irritating. Active against Gram positive/negative but not tubercle bacilli/spores.

Halogens

Hypochlorites: (Milton, chloros). Active against viruses. Inactivated by organic matter.
Iodophores: Iodine detergent complexes (Betadine). Non-irritant.
Iodine: Alcoholic tincture.

Quaternary Ammonium Compounds

Cetrimide, benzalkonium chloride. Detergent properties. Active against Gram positive. Antagonised by soaps.

Gaseous Sterilisation

  • Ethylene Oxide: Gas at room temperature. Highly inflammable. Effective against all bacteria/spores. Toxic. Used for heat-labile items (catheters, heart-lung machines).
  • Formaldehyde: Used as $40\%$ formalin gas. Irritant. Used for fumigating rooms.

üìä Laboratory Evaluation of Disinfectants

The In-Use Test (Kelsey Maurer's method) is simple and easy to perform.

  1. 1 Take 1 ml disinfectant solution from the jar.
  2. 2 Add to 9 ml diluent (appropriate inactivator e.g., 0.5% 'Tween 80').
  3. 3 Place 10 drops on each of two nutrient agar plates.
  4. 4 Incubate one plate at 37¬∞C for 3 days and the other at room temperature for 7 days.

Result: Growth of five or more colonies indicates poor bactericidal activity.
