ü¶† Aerobic Gram Negative Cocci

This group includes the genera Neisseria and Branhamella in the family of Neisseriaceae.

Group Characteristics

These are Gram negative cocci which occur in pairs (diplococci) with flattened or concave opposing sides. They are strict aerobes, non-spore forming, catalase positive, oxidase positive and attack carbohydrates oxidatively. They are non-motile and generally fastidious in their growth requirements.

Genus Neisseria

The genus consists of two medically important species: N. meningitidis (meningococcus) and N. gonorrhoeae (gonococcus). Other species are mainly commensals in the upper respiratory tract.

02

üß† Neisseria meningitidis

Pathogenicity

The organism enters through the nasopharynx into the blood stream and meninges. It causes:

  1. 1 Cerebrospinal meningitis: Serious pyogenic infection, common in tropical countries. Epidemics occur in overcrowded conditions.
  2. 2 Meningococcal bacteraemia (meningococcaemia): Can lead to Waterhouse-Friderichsen syndrome (adrenal destruction, shock, coma) with typical petechial rashes.
  3. 3 Occasionally chronic arthritis and endocarditis.

Laboratory Diagnosis

Specimens: CSF, blood culture, pus from skin lesions.

Microscopy: Gram negative intracellular and extracellular diplococci in CSF sediment.

Culture: Grows best on enriched media (Chocolate agar) in 5‚Äì10% $CO_2$ at 36¬∞C. Selective media include Modified New York City (MNYC) medium and Modified Thayer‚ÄìMartin (MTM) medium.

Identification:

  • Oxidase: Strongly positive.
  • Carbohydrate Utilisation: Ferments glucose and maltose; no action on lactose or sucrose.

Serology: Classified into serogroups A, B, C, X, Y, Z, W135. Groups A, B, and C are most pathogenic. Latex agglutination can detect antigen in CSF.

03

‚öß Neisseria gonorrhoeae

Pathogenicity

Causes Gonorrhoea, a sexually transmitted disease.

  • Men: Acute urethritis, dysuria, purulent discharge. Complications: Prostatitis, epididymitis, sterility.
  • Women: Often asymptomatic (60%). Cervicitis, urethritis. Complications: Salpingitis, Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID), sterility.
  • Infants: Gonococcal ophthalmia neonatorum (acute conjunctivitis) acquired during birth.
  • Other sites: Rectal infection, pharyngitis, septic arthritis.

Laboratory Diagnosis

Specimens: Urethral, cervical, rectal swabs. Prostatic fluid. Eye swabs.

Microscopy: Gram negative intracellular diplococci within pus cells. Presumptively diagnostic in men; must be confirmed by culture in women.

Culture: Use selective media (MTM or MNYC) incubated in moist $CO_2$ at 35‚Äì37¬∞C. Colonies are small, convex, colourless.

Identification:

  • Oxidase: Positive.
  • Carbohydrate Utilisation: Ferments glucose ONLY. No maltose, lactose, sucrose.
  • Superoxol Test: Strong bubbling with 30% $H_2O_2$ (differentiates from N. meningitidis).

PPNG (Penicillinase Producing N. gonorrhoeae)

Many strains now produce beta-lactamase (penicillinase), rendering them resistant to penicillin. All isolates should be tested for beta-lactamase production (e.g., acidometric method).

ü¶† Other Gram Negative Cocci

Branhamella catarrhalis (Moraxella catarrhalis)

Characteristics: Gram negative diplococci. Oxidase positive. Does NOT ferment carbohydrates. DNAse positive.

Pathogenicity: Commensal of URT. Opportunistic pathogen causing lower respiratory tract infections (bronchitis, pneumonia) especially in compromised lungs.

Note: Some strains produce beta-lactamase and are resistant to penicillin.

Anaerobic Gram Negative Cocci (Veillonella)

Type species: Veillonella parvula.

Characteristics: Small Gram negative cocci in pairs/clusters. Strict anaerobe. Catalase negative. Non-haemolytic.

Habitat: Commensal of mouth, GIT, and vagina. Rare cause of infection.
