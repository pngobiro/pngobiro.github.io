üéØ Core Principles

Diagnostic bacteriology entails two major functions:

  1. 1 The isolation and identification of disease causing agents
  2. 2 The rational choice of antimicrobial drugs based on antimicrobial susceptibility tests.

Koch‚Äôs Postulates

To label a microorganism as the cause of an infection, it should satisfy Koch‚Äôs postulates which briefly state that:

  1. The organism must be found in every case of the disease.
  2. The organism must not be found in a healthy person.
  3. It must be isolated and grown in pure culture.
  4. Inoculation into an animal should reproduce the disease and the organism should be isolated from the animal.
  5. Infection should produce circulating antibody specific for the infecting organism.

Tests specifically designed for the diagnosis of infectious diseases must be able to:

  • demonstrate an infectious agent in the specimen obtained from the patient.
  • demonstrate a four-fold rise in antibody titre when an acute and convalescent serum samples are tested.
  • demonstrate a reasonable cell-mediated response to antigen associated with some infectious agents.

Communication Between Clinician and Laboratory

It is of great mutual benefit for both the physician and the laboratory to establish an open line of two-way communication.

üõ°Ô∏è Infection and Safety

Infections are usually acquired by inhalation or ingestion of harmful microorganisms. Microorganisms which are released in the form of aerosols contribute greatly to infection by inhalation.

Classification of Pathogenic Organisms

Pathogenic organisms are classified according to the risk of infection they pose to health workers.

Group 1

An organism that is most unlikely to cause disease in man. For example, common moulds and food spoilage bacteria.

Group 2

An organism that may cause disease in man and which may be hazardous to laboratory worker but not likely to spread within the community. Exposure does not usually produce infection and there is effective prophylaxis and treatment available. For example, common pathogenic bacteria such as staphylococci, polioviruses, Toxoplasma.

Group 3

An organism which may cause disease in man and pose a serious hazard to the laboratory worker. There may also be a risk of spread within the community. Effective prophylaxis and treatment are usually available. Examples in this group include Brucella, Salmonella typhi, Mycobacterium tuberculosis, arboviruses and Histoplasma.

Group 4

An organism which causes severe disease in man and is a serious hazard to the laboratory worker. There is also a risk of spread within the community. There is no effective prophylaxis and treatment. This group consists of viruses such as Ebola, Lassa, Marburg.

Safety Cabinets

Specimens should be processed in the appropriate safety cabinet depending on the group of organisms suspected.

  • Class I cabinet: This is an open-front exhaust safety cabinet. Potentially infectious aerosols are contained within the cabinet and filtered from the exhaust air by a high efficiency particulate absorption (HEPA) filter (Fig. 8.6). This cabinet is used for groups 1, 2 and 3 organisms.
  • Class II cabinet: Designed to control airborne contamination of the material inside the cabinet and reduce exposure of the operator. Used for containment of group 2 and 3 organisms. It has a recirculating flow of filtered air over the work area (Fig. 8.7).
  • Class III cabinet: This cabinet is the only one that offers protection against group 4 organisms. It is a totally enclosed exhaust safety cabinet with gloves mechanically attached (Fig. 8.8).

Safety Rules

  1. 1Wear gloves and protective clothing when processing clinical specimens or handling cultures.
  2. 2Do not lay a culture tube on the bench; always put it in the rack.
  3. 3Process samples or culture which involve the risk of aerosols in the safety cabinet.
  4. 4Always discard finished cultures into the appropriate discard containers which are sterilised later.
  5. 5Do not smoke, eat or drink in the laboratory.
  6. 6Do not lick gummed labels.
  7. 7Handle all instruments and materials with care.
  8. 8Always wash hands with soap and water after handling cultures and specimens and before leaving the laboratory.

üìñ Clinical Definitions & Pathogenesis

Infection: Invasion and multiplication of microorganisms in or on a host with or without a disease process.

Infectious disease: An infection causing structural and functional harm to the host with signs and symptoms.

Opportunistic Infectious disease: An infection caused by microorganisms that are normally harmless to the host.

Nosocomial infection: A hospital-acquired infection.

Pathogenicity: Ability of a micro-organism to cause disease without any specification of conditions.

Virulence: The extent to which an organism can cause damage. The degree of pathogenicity is dependent on three major conditions: invasiveness, production of extracellular enzymes and toxigenicity.

üî¨ Specimen Collection and Handling

Basic Concepts

  1. 1The clinical specimen should be taken from the actual infection site.
  2. 2Optimal times for specimen collection should be established.
  3. 3The quantity of specimen must be adequate.
  4. 4Proper and sterile collection devices and specimen containers must be used.
  5. 5Prompt delivery of specimens to the laboratory is important.
  6. 6Obtain all the necessary specimens prior to the administration of antimicrobial drugs.
  7. 7The clinical specimen container must be properly labelled.

Postal Specimens

Must be sent by letter post only, clearly labelled 'Bacteriological Specimen', in a sealed container packed in a wooden or metal box with absorbent material.

Reception of Specimens

Rejection criteria:

  • badly leaking samples
  • when specimen and request form do not match
  • specimen collected in a wrong container
