üìò Antimicrobial Therapy

Antimicrobial therapy aims at treating infections with a drug to which the causal microorganism is sensitive and which has a selective toxicity only for the infecting organism without harming the host cells.

Definitions

  • Antibiotic: Antimicrobial agent derived from a microorganism (e.g., Penicillin).
  • Chemotherapeutic drug: Chemical compound (natural or synthetic) used to treat disease.
  • Bacteriostatic: Arrests bacterial multiplication (e.g., Chloramphenicol).
  • Bactericidal: Kills the organism (e.g., Penicillin, Aminoglycosides).

Drug Combinations

Combinations are indicated for:

  • Synergy (e.g., Cotrimoxazole).
  • Preventing resistance (e.g., Tuberculosis).
  • Treating mixed infections.
02

‚öôÔ∏è Mode of Action

There are five main ways in which antimicrobial drugs act on bacteria:

Mechanism Action Examples
Inhibition of cell wall synthesis Prevents cross-linking of peptidoglycan. Penicillins, Cephalosporins, Vancomycin
Damage to cell membrane Loss of semipermeability, leakage of contents. Polymixins, Amphotericin B
Inhibition of protein synthesis Affects translation (ribosomes). Aminoglycosides, Tetracyclines, Chloramphenicol, Erythromycin
Inhibition of nucleic acid synthesis Inhibits DNA replication or RNA synthesis. Quinolones (DNA), Rifampicin (RNA)
Inhibition of folic acid synthesis Interferes with metabolic pathways (PABA analogs). Sulphonamides, Trimethoprim
03

üíä Drugs of Clinical Importance

1. Penicillins

Contains a $\beta$-lactam ring. Inhibits cell wall synthesis. Resistance: Due to $\beta$-lactamase (Penicillinase) enzyme which breaks the ring.

  • Penicillin G (Benzyl penicillin): Effective against Streptococci, Clostridia, non-penicillinase Staph/Neisseria.
  • Ampicillin/Amoxycillin: Broad spectrum. Active against Gram negatives (E. coli, Proteus) but sensitive to penicillinase.
  • Cloxacillins (Methicillin, Cloxacillin): Resistant to $\beta$-lactamase. Used for penicillin-resistant Staphylococci.
  • Augmentin: Amoxycillin + Clavulanic acid ($\beta$-lactamase inhibitor).

2. Cephalosporins

Chemically similar to penicillins but more resistant to $\beta$-lactamase. Broad spectrum.

3. Aminoglycosides

Bactericidal inhibitors of protein synthesis. Effective against Gram negative bacteria (Gentamicin, Amikacin). Toxicity: Nephrotoxic and Ototoxic.

4. Other Antibiotics

  • Tetracyclines: Broad spectrum (Chlamydia, Rickettsia, Mycoplasma). Bacteriostatic.
  • Chloramphenicol: Broad spectrum. Toxic to bone marrow (aplastic anaemia). Reserved for typhoid/meningitis.
  • Erythromycin: Macrolide. Useful for penicillin-allergic patients. Active against Legionella, Campylobacter.
  • Metronidazole: Effective against anaerobes and protozoa.
  • Rifampicin: Anti-tuberculosis. Inhibits RNA polymerase.
04

üõë Bacterial Resistance

Types of Resistance

Natural: Innate property (e.g., Mycoplasma resistant to penicillin due to lack of cell wall).

Acquired:

  • Mutation: Chromosomal change (e.g., M. tuberculosis).
  • Gene Transfer: Usually plasmid-mediated (R-factors) via conjugation or transduction.

Mechanisms of Resistance

Mechanism Example
Permeability Alteration of cell membrane preventing drug entry (Ps. aeruginosa).
Modification of Target Altered penicillin binding proteins (MRSA). Altered ribosomes (Erythromycin).
Inactivation Production of enzymes like $\beta$-lactamase (Penicillinase) or Aminoglycoside modifying enzymes.
05

üß™ Susceptibility Testing Methods

I. Dilution Tests

Used to determine:

  • MIC (Minimum Inhibitory Concentration): Lowest concentration showing no visible growth.
  • MBC (Minimum Bactericidal Concentration): Lowest concentration that kills 99.9% of bacteria.

II. Diffusion Tests

Kirby-Bauer Method

Standardized single disc diffusion method. Uses Mueller-Hinton agar. Zone sizes are compared to NCCLS tables.

Stoke's Method

Test and Control strains are tested on the same plate.
Test organism is inoculated in the center, Control organism at top/bottom (or periphery). Discs placed between them.

Interpretation (Stoke's):

  • Sensitive: Test zone $\ge$ Control zone (or not >3mm smaller).
  • Resistant: No zone or zone >3mm smaller than control.
06

‚öñÔ∏è Factors Affecting Diffusion Tests

  1. 1Inoculum Density: Must be 0.5 McFarland standard ($10^8$ organisms/ml). Too heavy = smaller zones.
  2. 2Medium Depth: Standard 4mm. Thicker agar = smaller zones.
  3. 3pH: Should be 7.2-7.4. Acid pH reduces activity of aminoglycosides/erythromycin.
  4. 4Cations: Ca++ and Mg++ levels affect Pseudomonas sensitivity to aminoglycosides.
  5. 5Incubation: 35-37¬∞C in ambient air (CO2 affects pH). MRSA requires 30¬∞C.

üîç Special Resistance Considerations

Beta-Lactamase Production

Important in Staph. aureus, H. influenzae, N. gonorrhoeae (PPNG).
Detection: Acidometric method (Penicillin + Phenol red/Bromocresol purple). Yellow colour = Positive.

Methicillin Resistant Staph. aureus (MRSA)

Hetero-resistance (only small fraction of population expresses resistance).
Detection: Incubate at 30¬∞C. Add 4% NaCl to agar. Look for "feathering" edge of zone.

üìä Antibiotic Assays

Used to monitor toxic drugs (e.g., Gentamicin, Vancomycin) or ensure therapeutic levels.

Serum Bactericidal Test (Schlichter Test)

Determines the highest dilution of patient's serum (containing antibiotic) that kills 99.9% of the infecting organism. Uses patient's own isolate.
Adequate therapy: Bactericidal titre of 1:8 or 1:16.
