üß´ Genus Bacteroides

This genus consists of numerous species which have now been classified into two groups:

  • Bacteroides fragilis group (6 species)
  • Bacteroides melaninogenicus group (4 species) (Renamed Prevotella melaninogenicus)

Characteristics: The organisms in the genus are small Gram negative, non-sporing and generally non-motile rods. They are obligate anaerobes.

Habitat: Most of the Bacteroides species live as normal flora in the mouth, intestinal tract, urethra, and female genital tract.

Pathogenicity

Infection with Bacteroides are usually endogenous from their resident sites in the body, and very often they are mixed with other organisms, both obligate and facultative anaerobes.

Bacteroides species are associated with the following infections:

  1. 1Genitourinary infections including puerperal sepsis.
  2. 2Post-operative abdominal wound infections and peritonitis.
  3. 3Abscesses of the brain, lung, and maxillo-facial region.
  4. 4Ulcerative gingivitis is caused by B. melaninogenicus.

Laboratory Diagnosis

Specimen Transport

Specimens for anaerobic cultivation (Pus or swabs) should be sent to the laboratory without delay in a transport medium e.g. Stuart's transport medium.

Microscopy: Stained smear shows small or pleomorphic Gram negative bacilli.

Culture: Bacteroides species grow well in cooked meat broth. Growth on solid media is enhanced in an anaerobic environment enriched with $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$.

Selective Media: Gentamicin, neomycin or kanamycin blood agar. Schaedler's blood agar is also highly recommended.

  • B. fragilis: Produces 1‚Äì3 mm, entire, smooth, shiny pale grey colonies after 48 hours incubation at $37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$.
  • B. melaninogenicus: Produces brown to black haemolytic colonies in about 3‚Äì7 days. The colonies are smooth, circular and opaque. Colonies will fluoresce pink-red under UV light.

Rapid Detection

For quick presumptive detection of anaerobes such as Bacteroides species, a disc of metronidazole (5 Œºg) is placed on the primary culture plate. Most anaerobes are sensitive to metronidazole.

Identification and Antibiotic Sensitivity

They are usually reported simply as Bacteroides species. Identification is based on cultural, microscopical and antimicrobial sensitivity. They are distinguished from Fusobacterium based on antibiotic activity (Table 19.1).

Antibiotic Sensitivity: B. fragilis is uniformly resistant to penicillin, vancomycin and aminoglycosides. B. melaninogenicus is resistant to kanamycin. All Bacteroides species are sensitive to metronidazole, and generally to tetracycline and chloramphenicol.

üèπ Genus Fusobacterium

Type species: F. nucleatum. Another important species is F. necrophorum.

The fusobacteria are large Gram negative rods that are wide at the centre and with tapering pointed ends (fusiform), some are pleomorphic. They are obligate anaerobes, non-motile and non-sporing.

Habitat: Normal flora of the mouth, gastrointestinal tract and female genital tract.

Pathogenicity

These organisms tend to occur in mixed infections.

  • F. nucleatum: Abdominal wound, genital infections and lung abscess.
  • F. necrophorum: Frequently associated with Borrelia vincenti spirochaetes in ulcerative lesions of the mouth and throat (Vincent's angina).

Laboratory Diagnosis

Microscopy:

  • F. nucleatum: Slender spindle-shaped Gram negative rods about $5-10 \times 1 \mu\mathrm{m}$ with tapering ends.
  • F. necrophorum: Pleomorphic, irregularly stained. 1‚Äì10 √ó 0.5 Œºm; long filamentous forms frequently seen.

Culture: Incubate anaerobically at 37¬∞C for 48 hours. Use Schaedler's blood agar.

  • F. nucleatum: Irregular, grey, non-haemolytic colonies (1‚Äì2 mm).
  • F. necrophorum: Circular, grey, often beta-haemolytic colonies.

Antibiotic Sensitivity: Unlike Bacteroides, fusobacteria are sensitive to Kanamycin. They are usually sensitive to penicillin and always sensitive to metronidazole.

ü¶† Genus Leptotrichia

Type species: L. buccalis.

These organisms are found in close association with fusobacteria. They are characteristically seen in Gram stained smears from clinical specimens as long Gram negative rods with tapering ends, measuring up to $15 \times 1.5 \mu\mathrm{m}$.

Pathogenicity: L. buccalis mixed with F. necrophorum, Bacteroides melaninogenicus and Borrelia vincenti is associated with Vincent's angina and mouth ulcers.

Culture: On blood agar after 48 hours anaerobic incubation at 37¬∞C, Leptotrichia species produce irregular and striated colonies about 2‚Äì3 mm in diameter.

Antibiotic Sensitivity: The organism is sensitive to metronidazole, and is gentian violet tolerant.
