üß¨ Genus Mycobacterium

Mycobacteria are slender, straight or slightly curved, non-motile rods; are aerobic, non-capsulated and non-sporing. They do not stain easily but once stained, they resist decolourisation with dilute mineral acids. They are therefore referred to as acid fast bacilli (AFB). They comprise obligate pathogens, opportunistic pathogens and saprophytes.

Type species: M. tuberculosis (tubercle bacilli).

Key Terms

  • M. tuberculosis complex: Describes M. tuberculosis, M. africanum and M. bovis collectively.
  • Environmental Mycobacteria: Previously called 'atypical' or anonymous mycobacteria; saprophytic species isolated from environmental sources.

üìä Runyon's Classification

Cultural differentiation of mycobacteria is based on:

  1. 1Rate of growth: Most grow slowly (6-8 weeks). Rapid growers (e.g., M. fortuitum) appear in 2-5 days.
  2. 2Temperature of growth: Optimum 35-37¬∞C. M. ulcerans and M. marinum prefer 30-32¬∞C.
  3. 3Pigment production: Response to light.
Group Characteristics Examples
I. Photochromogens Produce pigment only when exposed to light. M. kansasii, M. marinum
II. Scotochromogens Produce pigment in both dark and light (yellow-orange). M. scrofulaceum, M. szulgai
III. Non-chromogens No pigment produced. M. tuberculosis, M. bovis, M. avium
IV. Rapid Growers Grow in 2-7 days. Usually non-pigmented. M. fortuitum, M. chelonei, M. smegmatis

üß´ Culture Media

  • Lowenstein-Jensen (LJ) glycerol medium: Standard medium for human tubercle bacilli. Contains malachite green (inhibitor) and glycerol (enhancer for M. tuberculosis). Colonies are rough, tough, buff.
  • Lowenstein-Jensen (LJ) pyruvate medium: Used for M. bovis (glycerol inhibits M. bovis).
  • Dorset egg medium: Simple egg medium for laboratory strains.
  • Middlebrook 7H10/7H9: Agar/Broth media. Require OADC enrichment.

ü¶† Mycobacterium tuberculosis

Pathogenicity

Pulmonary tuberculosis: Infection via inhalation. Primary complex forms in lungs/lymph nodes. Can progress to productive lesions with caseation (cheese-like necrosis) and cavitation.

Non-pulmonary tuberculosis:

  • Renal: Sterile pyuria (pus cells but negative routine culture).
  • Miliary: Disseminated spread via blood to liver, spleen, etc.
  • Meningitis: Serious complication in children.

Laboratory Diagnosis

‚ò£Ô∏è

Safety Warning

All procedures involving M. tuberculosis must be carried out in a safety cabinet located in a special TB room.

Specimens

Sputum (most common), pleural effusion, CSF, urine, gastric lavage.

Microscopy

  1. Ziehl-Neelsen (ZN) Stain: Acid-fast bacilli (AFB) appear red against a blue/green background. Cell wall contains mycolic acid which resists decolourisation by acid-alcohol.
  2. Fluorescent Microscopy: Stained with auramine-phenol. Bacilli fluoresce bright yellow. Useful for screening.

Concentration Methods

Sputum is homogenised and decontaminated (to kill commensals) before culture. Common method: Petroff's method using 4% NaOH.

Culture and Identification

Inoculate LJ medium. Incubate at 37¬∞C for up to 8 weeks. M. tuberculosis colonies are rough, tough, and buff (non-pigmented).
Confirmation: Niacin test positive.

Tuberculin Skin Test (Mantoux)

Intradermal injection of PPD (Purified Protein Derivative). Positive reaction (induration $\ge$ 10mm) indicates hypersensitivity (past or present infection/vaccination).

ü©π Mycobacterium leprae

Causative agent of Leprosy (Hansen's disease). Affects skin and peripheral nerves. Cannot be grown on artificial media.

Clinical Types

Feature Tuberculoid (TT) Lepromatous (LL)
Immunity (CMI) Good Absent
Lesions Few, defined, hypopigmented Many, nodules, diffuse
Bacilli in smears Scanty/Negative Numerous (Globi)
Lepromin Test Positive Negative

Laboratory Diagnosis

Skin Smears

Collection: Make a small cut (5mm long, 2-3mm deep) in a skin fold (ear lobe, lesion). Scrape the sides of the cut to obtain tissue pulp (not blood). Make a smear.

Microscopy

Stain with ZN (use 5% $H_2SO_4$ for decolourisation as M. leprae is less acid-fast). Look for acid-fast rods, often in clumps called globi.

Indices

  • Bacterial Index (BI): Logarithmic scale of bacterial load.
  • Morphological Index (MI): Percentage of viable (solid staining) bacilli. Used to monitor treatment response.
