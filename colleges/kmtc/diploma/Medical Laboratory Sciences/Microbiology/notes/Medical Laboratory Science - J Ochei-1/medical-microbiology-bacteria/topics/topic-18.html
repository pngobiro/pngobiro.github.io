ü¶† Genus Corynebacterium

This genus consists of species that are Gram positive straight or curved rods, that may be club shaped at one pole and may contain volutin granules (intracytoplasmic polyphosphate bodies). They are non-motile, non-acid fast, non-sporing and may stain irregularly and so appear beaded or barred.

Habitat: These organisms are normally found in soil, plants and animals. Non-pathogenic strains form part of the resident flora of the skin, upper respiratory tract, urinary tract and conjunctiva.

Type species: C. diphtheriae (with three biovars: gravis, intermedius, and mitis).

Corynebacterium diphtheriae

C. diphtheriae causes nasal, nasopharyngeal and tonsilar diphtheria. Young children are most vulnerable.

Pathogenesis: Infection is by inhalation of droplets or contact. Virulent strains produce specific exotoxins which destroy epithelium, resulting in a grey pseudomembrane (fibrin, bacteria, leucocytes). Diphtheria is a toxaemic infection. The pseudomembrane can obstruct the larynx causing asphyxia.

Complications

  • Acute circulatory failure (peripheral or cardiac).
  • Septic conditions (pneumonia, otitis media).
  • Postdiphtheric paralysis.

In Vivo Tests

Intradermal Test: Inject bacterial suspension intradermally into two guinea pigs, one protected with antitoxin. Virulent strains cause a necrotic patch in the unprotected animal.

Schick Test: A skin test to demonstrate circulating diphtheria antitoxin in humans (susceptibility test).

üî¨ Laboratory Diagnosis of Diphtheria

Specimens: Throat, nasal, or nasopharyngeal swabs.

Microscopy

Gram Stain: Pleomorphic Gram positive rods with club-shaped ends, arranged in palisades or "Chinese lettering".

Albert's Stain: Demonstrates volutin (metachromatic) granules. Organisms stain green; granules stain dark blue/black.

Culture

  • Hoyle's Tellurite Medium: Selective. Reduces tellurite producing grey to black colonies.
  • Loeffler's Serum Slope: Enhances production of volutin granules. Rapid growth (4-6 hours).
  • Blood Agar: To exclude haemolytic streptococci.

üè∑Ô∏è Other Corynebacteria

  • C. ulcerans: Pathogen of animals. Can cause diphtheria-like lesions. Produces toxin partially identical to diphtheria toxin. Liquefies gelatin and hydrolyses urea.
  • C. haemolyticum: Occasional human pathogen (pharyngitis, skin ulcers). Wide zone of haemolysis on human blood.
  • C. pseudotuberculosis (C. ovis): Causes caseous lymphadenitis in sheep. Toxin different from diphtheria.
  • JK Group (C. jeikeium): Opportunistic nosocomial pathogen (leukaemia, cardiac surgery). Resistant to many antibiotics.
  • C. hofmanii: Commensal. Urease positive. Inert carbohydrates.
  • C. xerosis: Commensal of conjunctiva. Ferments sucrose.

üå°Ô∏è Genus Listeria

Listeria monocytogenes

Small Gram positive coccobacillus. Aerobic/Facultative anaerobe. Motile at room temperature ($20-25^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$) but not at $37^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$.

Pathogenicity: Causes Listeriosis. Meningitis and septicaemia in neonates (intrauterine infection), pregnant women, and immunocompromised. Associated with contaminated food (vegetables, milk).

Laboratory Diagnosis

Specimens: CSF, blood.

Microscopy: Gram positive diphtheroid-like bacilli. CSF shows lymphocytosis.

Motility Test: Characteristic tumbling motility at $25^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$.

Culture: Grows on blood agar ($2-40^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$). Small colonies with narrow zone of beta-haemolysis. Cold enrichment at $4^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ used for isolation from contaminated specimens.

Identification: Catalase +, Bile aesculin +, CAMP test + (block type haemolysis).

Erysipelothrix rhusiopathiae

Gram positive, small curved bacillus. Non-motile. Microaerophilic.

Pathogenicity: Causes Erysipeloid (painful swollen erythematous area) in man (butchers, fish workers). Causes swine erysipelas in pigs.

Identification: H‚ÇÇS positive in TSI (blackening). Catalase negative. Alpha-haemolytic.

ü•õ Genus Lactobacillus

Gram positive, non-motile, non-sporing rods. Microaerophilic/Anaerobic.

Habitat: Normal vaginal flora (Doderlein's bacilli) producing lactic acid. Also in mouth and gut.

Culture: Grow best in acid pH (Tomato juice agar).

üåæ Genus Bacillus

Large aerobic spore-bearing Gram positive rods.

Bacillus anthracis

Pathogen of herbivores. Causes Anthrax in humans.

  • Cutaneous Anthrax: Malignant pustule (black eschar).
  • Pulmonary Anthrax: Wool sorter's disease (haemorrhagic mediastinitis).
  • Enteric Anthrax: Severe gastroenteritis.
‚ò£Ô∏è

Safety Warning

All procedures involving B. anthracis should be carried out in a safety cabinet. Specimens must carry biohazard labels.

Laboratory Diagnosis

Microscopy: Large Gram positive rods ($5-8 \times 1.5 \mu\mathrm{m}$) with square ends ("Bamboo stick"). Non-motile. No spores in clinical specimen.

McFadyean's Reaction: Blood film stained with polychrome methylene blue shows purple capsule around blue bacilli.

Culture: Non-haemolytic, "Medusa head" colonies (wavy edges) on blood agar. "Inverted fir tree" growth in gelatin stab.

Bacillus cereus

Causes food poisoning (vomiting/diarrhoea) associated with contaminated rice. Motile. Beta-haemolytic on blood agar.
