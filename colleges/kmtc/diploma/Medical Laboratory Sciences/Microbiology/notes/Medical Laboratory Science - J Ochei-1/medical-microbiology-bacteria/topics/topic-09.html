üëÉ Upper Respiratory Tract (URT)

Throat

'Sore throat' describes most infections of the throat, majority of which are caused by viruses.

Pathogens

  • Conventional: Beta-haemolytic streptococcus group A (Streptococcus pyogenes).
  • Casual: Beta-haemolytic streptococci (groups B,C,G), Haemophilus influenzae, Staphylococcus aureus, Candida species, Vincent's organisms, Corynebacterium diphtheriae, Neisseria gonorrhoeae, Neisseria meningitidis.

Nasopharynx (Pernasal)

Used for diagnosis of whooping cough (Pertussis) and meningococcal carriage.

  • Specimen: Nasopharyngeal aspirate or Pernasal swab (West's swab).
  • Culture: Charcoal agar or Bordet-Gengou medium for Bordetella pertussis.

Nasal Specimens

Anterior nasal swabs are usually done to detect carriers of Staphylococcus aureus.

  • Culture: Blood agar, Mannitol Salt Agar.
  • Note: Perform methicillin sensitivity test (MRSA).

ü´Å Lower Respiratory Tract

Sputum

Sputum specimens should be expectorated from the lower respiratory tract and must be distinct from saliva. Presence of >25 PMNs per low power field indicates infection.

Table 9.4 LRT Diseases and Agents
Disease Agents

Procedure

  1. 1Macroscopic: Note appearance (purulent, mucopurulent, salivary, blood-stained).
  2. 2Homogenisation: Add Sputasol or digestant to liquefy mucus.
  3. 3Microscopy: Gram stain (pus cells, bacteria). ZN stain if TB suspected.
  4. 4Culture: Blood agar (aerobic & anaerobic), Chocolate agar ($CO_2$), MacConkey/CLED.

üëÅÔ∏è Eye and Ear Infections

Eye Swabs

Categories: Conjunctivitis, Ophthalmia neonatorum, penetrating injury.

  • Pathogens: S. aureus, H. influenzae, N. gonorrhoeae, Strep. pneumoniae, Chlamydia trachomatis, Ps. aeruginosa.
  • Note: Process immediately to minimise antibacterial activity of ocular enzymes.

Ear Swabs

Otitis Media: Strep. pneumoniae, H. influenzae, S. pyogenes, S. aureus.

External Ear: Ps. aeruginosa, S. aureus, Aspergillus (Otomycosis).

Chronic Infection: Bacteroides species, Proteus species.

üß† Cerebrospinal Fluid (CSF)

Examination of CSF is a medical emergency. Normal CSF is sterile, clear, and colourless.

Pathogens

Classical: N. meningitidis, H. influenzae, Strep. pneumoniae.

Neonates: Group B Strep, E. coli, Listeria monocytogenes.

Laboratory Procedure

  1. 1 Appearance: Clear, turbid (pyogenic), xanthochromic (old haemorrhage), bloody (trauma), cobweb clot (TB).
  2. 2 Cell Count: Using Fuchs-Rosenthal or Neubauer chamber.
  3. 3 Gram Stain: From centrifuged deposit.
  4. 4 Culture: Chocolate agar ($CO_2$), Blood agar.
  5. 5 Antigen Test: Latex agglutination for rapid detection of specific pathogens.

07

üß™ Sterile Body Fluids (Effusions)

Exudate: Inflammatory origin (High protein, cells, clots).
Transudate: Non-inflammatory (Low protein, few cells, no clot).

Synovial Fluid (Joints)

  • Infection: S. aureus, N. gonorrhoeae, H. influenzae.
  • Crystal induced: Gout (Urate crystals), Pseudogout (Pyrophosphate).
  • Mucin clot test: Differentiates infective (poor clot) from non-infective (good clot) arthritis.

Pleural/Pericardial/Ascitic Fluid

Collect in citrate to prevent clotting for cell count, and plain tube for observation of clotting. Culture aerobically and anaerobically.

Specimen: Mid-Stream Urine (MSU). Transport immediately or use boric acid preservative / refrigeration.

Quantitative Culture

Used to distinguish infection from contamination. A standard loop (0.001 ml or 0.003 ml) or Dip-slide is used.
