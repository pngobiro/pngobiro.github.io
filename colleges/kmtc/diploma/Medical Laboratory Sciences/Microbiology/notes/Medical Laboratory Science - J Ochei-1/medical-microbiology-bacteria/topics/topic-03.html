Microscopic Examination of Bacteria

üìö Chapter 3
‚è±Ô∏è 20 min read

Microscopic examination of stained or unstained preparation of bacteria is usually one of the essential steps in the long process of isolating and identifying bacteria. Microscopy of stained smears helps to differentiate cellular constituents and render them more visible. Staining also helps to classify organisms by placing them in their separate groups, based on the staining reaction of the organism.

01

‚ûø Wire Loops

The microbiological wire loops are used to make smears. They are made of platinum or nichrome wires. The platinum wire used is actually a mixture of platinum (90%) and iridium (10%). Platinum on its own is too soft to be of any use. The nichrome wire is cheaper and more flexible. Disposable plastic loops are also available. Wire loops are made in various sizes of between 1.5 mm and 3 mm in diameter with the handle or holder about 6‚Äì8 cm long (Fig. 3.1a).

The wire loops may be purchased commercially though making a wire loop is not very difficult (Fig. 3.1b):

  1. Wind the wire once round a rod of appropriate diameter.
  2. With a pair of scissors, cut one arm of the wire wound around the rod, forming a loop.
  3. Bend back the loop with a pair of forceps to centre it.
  4. Insert into a metal wire loop holder.
02

üíß Making a Wet Preparation for Microscopy

  1. On a clean microscope slide, place a drop of the material to be examined, e.g., urine, or mix a small portion of the specimen such as vaginal secretion in a drop of saline.
  2. Carefully place a cover-slip over the preparation, making sure that there are no air bubbles trapped under the cover-slip.
  3. Examine immediately. If there is going to be a delay in examining the preparation, it is recommended to seal the edges of the cover-slip with nail varnish or petroleum jelly.
03

üß™ Hanging Drop Preparation

The hanging drop is a microscopic technique used to determine the motility of bacteria when suspended in fluid. True motility is when bacteria actively move from one position to another in a haphazard manner. It should not be confused with Brownian movement which is the vibration caused by molecular bombardment, or the one directional movement caused by convectional currents.

  1. Make a ring of plasticine or vaseline about 2 cm in diameter on a clean slide.
  2. Place a loopful of culture in the centre of a clean 22 mm square cover-slip.
  3. Carefully press the ring of plasticine on to the cover-slip with the drop of culture in the centre of the ring and not touching the slide.
  4. With a quick movement invert the slide so that the cover-slip is uppermost (Fig. 3.2).
  5. Examine under the microscope, first using the low power objective (16 mm) to focus on the edge of the 'drop', then observe motility with high-power (4 mm) objective.
  6. At the end of the examination, discard the whole preparation into a jar of disinfectant and allow to soak to kill the microorganisms.
üìù

Note

  1. A 'well-slide' with a depression in the centre can be used in place of the slide with a ring of plasticine. Remember to seal the cover-slip with vaseline or nail varnish.
  2. With experience, one can use the ordinary wet preparation to determine motility microscopically.
04

ü¶† Preparation of Smears

Stained preparations are needed to examine micro-organisms microscopically in order to study their morphology and observe their cellular constituents. Smears (or tissue sections) are made and stained by any one of the recognised staining methods. Smears can be made from liquid or solid cultures or from clinical specimens.

Smears from Solid Media

  1. Sterilise the wire loop in bunsen flame.
  2. Place one drop of sterile saline on a clean slide with the sterilised loop.
  3. Re-sterilise the loop.
  4. With the wire loop, pick a small portion of bacterial growth and emulsify it in the drop of saline and spread to give a thin homogeneous film or smear on the slide (Fig. 3.3). Sterilise the loop.
  5. Allow the smear to dry in air, fix and stain.

Smears from Liquid Media

  1. Sterilise wire loop in the bunsen flame.
  2. Using aseptic technique, remove a loopful of the culture.
  3. Place the culture on a clean slide and spread it with the loop to give a fairly thick film of culture. Sterilise the loop.
  4. Allow the film to dry, fix and stain.
‚ö†Ô∏è

Important Notes

  1. Always allow the film to dry on its own in air without heating before fixing it. This is to prevent the smear from being washed off during staining.
  2. From solid media the smear should be thin and from liquid media, it should be thick.

Smears From Clinical Specimens

Smears are made from original clinical specimens directly on the slide if it is a swab or with a loop if it is fluid.

Fixation of Bacterial Smears

The smear having been made and allowed to dry, is fixed by quickly passing it two or three times over a bunsen flame to a temperature of about 55 to 60 ¬∞C. This is to coagulate the bacterial proteins which makes them adhere to the slide. Fixation should be such that the bacterial morphology remains intact, preventing the smear from being washed off during staining. Overheating will lead to distortion of morphology.

05

üñåÔ∏è Staining of Bacteria

Dyes from which stains are made, are either natural or synthetic products. Most of the dyes used for bacterial smears are synthetic. The natural dyes are mostly used in histopathology. The synthetic dyes are all derivatives of benzene and are usually referred to as aniline dyes.

Stains are mostly salts, comprising a base and an acid. They are classified into acid, basic and neutral stains. Basic stains are those in which the colouring substances are contained in the basic radical with the acid radical being colourless, while those stains in which the colouring substances are in the acidic radical and base components being colourless, are Acid stains. An aqueous mixture of some acid and basic dyes results in Neutral stains, with the colouring substances contained in both acid and base components.

The theory of staining reaction is not fully understood but it is generally believed to be a combination of chemical and physical reactions. The bacterial cell which is rich in nucleic acid, has affinity for basic stains and so is stained by the basic stain. The acid stain is useful for basic components and as a background stain to give a good contrast.

Mordant Some organisms (or tissues) do not take up the stain except in the presence of a mordant‚Äîa substance that forms a link between the organism and the stain to bring about a staining reaction. The mordant forms a 'lake' with the dye which is capable of attaching itself firmly to the organism. A popular mordant in bacteriology is the Lugol's iodine. The mordant should not be confused with an accentuator which is a substance that greatly increases the staining power and selectivity of a stain. It does not combine or form a lake with the dye.

Examination of stained smears All stained smears are examined with oil immersion objective (‚â• 100).

Types of Staining

  • Indirect staining This is when an organism is stained only in the presence of a mordant, e.g., Gram's stain.
  • Direct staining This is a simple one-step staining procedure in which the presence and morphology of bacteria are demonstrated. Some of the stains used for this technique are also used as counterstains in some other staining procedures, e.g., methylene blue.
  • Negative staining This is when the organism remains unstained against a stained background. This is one of the few methods where acid stains such as nigrosin, are used.
  • Metachromatic staining In this staining method the organism or part of the organism is stained a different shade of colour from that of the stain.

Commonly Used Staining Methods

In Bacteriology, staining methods are divided into three categories:

  1. Simple stains This makes use of the direct staining method.
  2. Differential stains This staining method divides bacteria into two groups
  3. Special stains These are specialised staining methods to demonstrate certain bacterial components., e.g., spore.

üåà Differential Staining Methods

1. Gram's Stain

In 1884, Christian Gram, a Danish bacteriologist, described this staining method which is the most important stain in routine bacteriology. It divides bacteria into two groups: the Gram positive and Gram negative bacteria.

The Gram positive organism must have an intact cell wall. A damaged cell wall inevitably results in Gram negative reaction. This shows the importance of cell wall in Gram stain reaction. The Gram's stain reaction is based on the ability of the organism to resist decolourisation with acetone, alcohol or aniline oil after the initial staining with one of the rosaniline basic dyes and then treating with a mordant. The rosaniline dyes commonly used are crystal violet, methyl violet and gentian violet. Iodine is the mordant used.

Grams staining method involves four steps:

  1. Primary staining A primary stain such as crystal violet stains all the bacteria in the smear.
  2. Mordanting Application of a mordant such as Lugol's iodine results in the formation of a complex with the primary stain.
  3. Decolourisation Some bacteria resist removal of the dye-iodine complex with the decolourising agent and retain the primary stain. These are called Gram positive bacteria. On the other hand, Gram negative bacteria get decolourised with the decolourising agent.
  4. Counterstaining A counterstain, contrasting in colour with the primary stain, is used to stain Gram negative bacteria which are decolourised in the third step.

Gram positive bacteria In a Gram stained smear, Gram positive bacteria which retain the primary stain, appear violet or deep purple in colour. The Gram positive reaction is due to the presence of tecnoic acid in the cell wall. The tecnoic acid forms a mesh which prevents the primary stain-mordant complex from being washed away by the decolouring agent.

Gram negative bacteria These appear red. Gram negative bacteria have a high lipid content which dissolves in the decolourising agent. This allows the primary stain-mordant complex to be washed off. The red counterstain makes the decolourised Gram negative bacteria visible in a contrast colour.

Solutions

  1. Crystal Violet 0.5‚Äì1 % in distilled water.
  2. Lugol's iodine
    • Iodine 10 g
    • Potassium iodide 20 g
    • Distilled water 1000 ml
    • Dissolve the potassium iodide in about 50 ml of the distilled water, add iodine.

  3. Wash with tap water.
  4. Apply Lugol's iodine and leave for one minute.
  5. Wash with tap water.
  6. Decolourise with acetone or alcohol until no more colour appears to ooze out of the smear (about two seconds for acetone and 1‚Äì2 minutes for alcohol).
  7. Wash immediately with tap water.
  8. Counterstain with neutral red or safranin or dilute carbol fuchsin for one minute.
  9. Wash with tap water.
  10. Blot dry with a blotting or filter paper, and dry.
  11. Examine microscopically.

Technical Notes

  1. The timings for the different staining reagents are not too critical.
  2. In step three, some workers prefer to replace the crystal violet with the iodine instead of first washing in tap water.
  3. Dilute carbol fuchsin is the best counterstain for Haemophilus species.
  4. Sandiford's stain is a useful counterstain for intra-cellular Gram negative diplococci from CSF (Cerebrospinal fluid), urethral or cervical swabs.

2. Acid-Fast Stain

Acid-fast staining is another example of a differential stain used in Bacteriology. It divides bacteria into two groups, acid fast-and non acid fast. Members of the genus Mycobacterium are acid-fast in nature. Like the Gram reaction, the acid-fastness is also cell wall dependant. Mycobacteria have a high lipid content, especially mycolic acid, in their cell wall. The ordinary aniline dye solutions cannot penetrate the mycobacterial cell wall. Strong staining solutions containing phenol preferably with application of heat, are used for staining them. Once stained, they resist decolourisation with mineral acid. Therefore they are called acid-fast bacilli (AFB). The degree of acid-fastness varies with species. Mycobacterium tuberculosis can resist decolourisation with 20% sulphuric acid, or 3% hydrochloric acid in 95% ethanol. Hence they are also called acid-alcohol-fast (AAFB). M. leprae resist 5% sulphuric acid while Nocardia species are acid-fast only with 1% sulphuric acid. Acid-fast staining is performed in 3 steps:

  1. Primary staining A strong primary stain containing phenol is applied, usually with heat, to penetrate the cell wall. Basic fuchsin is commonly used.
  2. Decolourisation An appropriate decolourising agent, e.g., 20% sulphuric acid applied to the smear decolourises non acid-fast bacteria and other cells. The acid fast bacteria resist decolourisation and retain the colour of the primary stain.
  3. Counterstaining Decolourised non acid-fast bacteria are counterstained in a contrast colour (blue or green) to differentiate them from the acid-fast bacteria.

Ziehl-Neelsen (ZN) Method

Solutions
  1. Strong carbol fuchsin
    • Powdered basic fuchsin 5 g
    • Phenol 25 g
    • 95% alcohol 50 ml
    • Distilled water 50 ml
    • Dissolve the fuchsin in the solution of phenol with little water in a boiling water bath. Add the alcohol and mix. Add the rest of the water.
  2. 20% Sulphuric acid (H‚ÇÇSO‚ÇÑ) or 3% acid alcohol (i.e 3% hydrochloric acid in 95% alcohol).
  3. 0.5% Methylene blue or 0.5% malachite green.
Procedure
  1. Make and fix smear by heat.
  2. Flood the slide with the carbol fuchsin solution and heat gently until steam rises. Do not allow the stain to boil. Stain for 5-10 minutes and reheat. Leave for another 10 minutes.
  3. Wash with tap water.
  4. Decolourise with 20% sulphuric acid or 3% acid alcohol for about five minutes (with 20% H‚ÇÇSO‚ÇÑ, until the film becomes yellowish).
  5. Wash well in water.
  6. Counterstain with 0.5% methylene blue or malachite green for 30 seconds.
  7. Wash well with tap water.
  8. Blot, dry and examine under oil immersion lens.
Results

Acid-alcohol fast bacteria: red
Background and other bacteria: blue or green.

  2. For Nocardia species, decolourise with 1 % sulphuric acid.
  3. It is important not to allow the stain to evaporate or dry up on the slide.

üî¨ Special Staining Methods

Staining of Metachromatic Granules

Certain bacteria possess granules which are seen in the cytoplasm. The presence of these granules help in the identification of such bacteria, e.g., Corynebacterium species. Special stains such as Albert's, Neisser's and Pugh's stains are used to demonstrate these granules. Two of these staining methods are described below, though the simple Loeffler's alkaline methylene blue stain already described can be used for the same purpose.

Albert's Stain

Solutions
  1. Albert I
    • Toluidine blue: 0.015 g
    • Malachite green: 0.2 g
    • 95 % ethyl alcohol: 2 ml
    • Glacial acetic acid: 1 ml
    • Distilled water: 100 ml
  2. Albert II (Albert's iodine)
    • Iodine: 2 g
    • Potassium iodide: 3 g
    • Distilled water: 300 ml

Note: Lugol's iodine may be used instead of Albert's iodine.

Procedure
  1. Make a smear from an 18‚Äì24 hours Loeffler's serum culture of the organism.
  2. Fix with gentle heat.
  3. Stain with Albert's I solution for 3‚Äì5 minutes.
  4. Wash with water and blot carefully.
  5. Stain with Albert's II solution for one minute.
  6. Wash with water, blot and dry.
Results

Granules: bluish black.
Organisms: green.

Neisser's Stain (Modified)

Solutions
  1. Neisser's methylene blue (A)
    • Methylene blue, 2 % aqueous 10 ml
    • Ethyl alcohol 4 ml
    • Glacial acetic acid 10 ml
    • Distilled water 180 ml.
  2. Neisser's crystal violet (B)
    • Crystal violet 0.33 g
    • Ethyl alcohol 3.3 ml
    • Distilled water 100 ml
  3. Bismarck Brown (C)
    • Bismarck brown 1 g
    • Distilled water 500 ml
    • Heat water to 60¬∞C, add the dye to dissolve.
Procedure
  1. Make a smear from an 18‚Äì24 hours Loeffler's serum culture of the organism.
  2. Fix with gentle heat.
  3. Stain with two parts of solution A and 1 part of solution B for 15‚Äì20 seconds.
  4. Rinse rapidly in water.
  5. Counterstain with solution C for 3 seconds.
  6. Wash rapidly in water, blot and dry.

Organisms: Yellowish brown.

Staining of Capsules

Capsules are not stained by ordinary stains due to their low affinity for stains. They are demonstrated by either negative staining or one of the direct staining methods such as the Hiss method.

Nigrosin (or India Ink) Negative Staining

This simple method demonstrates capsules in wet preparation.

Procedure
  1. Place a loopful of 10 % nigrosin (or India ink) on a clean microscope slide.
  2. Emulsify a small portion of solid bacterial culture in the drop of nigrosin or mix in a loopful of liquid culture with the nigrosin.
  3. Place a clean cover-slip on the mixture. Press gently but firmly down to remove excess fluid, leaving a thin black film. Allow to settle for five minutes and then examine using the oil immersion objective. Alternatively, seal the edges of the cover-slip with paraffin wax or any other suitable medium and then examine.
Results

Bacteria are refractile surrounded by a clear zone against a dark grey background. Non-capsulated bacteria do not show this clear zone.

Note: Some workers prefer to emulsify a small portion of the culture first in saline before mixing in a loopful of nigrosin.

Nigrosin (or India Ink)-6 % Dextrose Method

Solutions
  1. 6 % Dextrose
  2. 10 % Nigrosin (or Indian Ink)
  3. Absolute alcohol or Leishman stain.
  4. 1 % methylene blue or 0.5 % crystal violet.
Procedure
  1. Place a loopful of the culture towards one end of a clean slide.
  2. Mix a loopful of 6 % dextrose with the culture.
  3. Add a loopful of 10 % nigrosin (or India ink) to the emulsion and mix.
  4. With the edge of another slide make a thin film as when preparing blood film. Dry by waving in air.
  5. Fix the film by flooding the slide with absolute alcohol (or with Leishman stain) for 1/2‚Äì1 minute and drain.
  6. Stain with 1% methylene blue (or 0.5% crystal violet) for 1 minute.
  7. Wash with water and stand to dry.
Results

Capsule: Unstained zone around the bacteria which are stained blue (or violet) against a bluish-grey background.

Note: The 6% dextrose makes the capsule appear larger.

Hiss Method

Solutions
  1. Hiss capsule stain solution
    • 10% alcoholic solution of basic fuchsin
    • Distilled water 95 ml
  2. 20% aqueous solution of copper sulphate.
Procedure
  1. Make smear and fix by heat.
  2. Stain with hot Hiss capsule stain (heated to 60¬∞C just before use) or flood the slide with the stain and heat until steam rises, leave for 15‚Äì30 seconds.
  3. Wash two or three times with the copper sulphate solution.
  4. Blot and dry.
Results

Capsules: pale brown or violet
Bacteria: red brown or deep violet.

Staining of Spores

The spore wall is resistant to stains but spores can be demonstrated with the application of heat to the preparation.

Malachite Green Stain for Spores

Solutions
  1. 5% Malachite green in distilled water.
  2. 0.5% safranin in distilled water.
Procedure
  1. Prepare a thin smear and allow to dry.
  2. Fix with minimum amount of heat.
  3. Flood the slide with the 5% malachite green solution. Heat until steam rises and leave for five minutes.
  4. Wash well with water.
  5. Counterstain with 0.5% safranin for 1/2 minute.
  6. Wash with water, blot and dry.
Results

Spores: green
Vegetative bacteria: red

Fuchsin-Nigrosin Spore Stain

Solutions
  1. Strong carbol fuchsin (Ziehl-Neelsen).
  2. 1% Nigrosin
  3. 5% sodium sulphite
  4. 10% Nigrosin
Procedure
  1. Make a thin smear and allow to dry
  2. Fix with heat
  3. Flood slide with the carbol fuchsin, apply heat until steam rises. Leave for five minutes
  4. Wash with water
  5. Treat with 5% sodium sulphite for 10 to 30 seconds, or 1% nigrosin for 5-10 minutes
  6. Wash with water, blot carefully and dry
  7. Place a small drop of 10% nigrosin at one end of the slide and spread evenly over the stained smear with the edge of another slide
  8. Allow to dry and examine
Results

Spores: bright red
Organisms: unstained against a dark grey background

Staining of Bacterial Cell Wall

The composition of cell wall differs from bacteria to bacteria, therefore there are variations in the length of time for the stain and cell wall to interact. The staining method uses tannic acid as mordant and Congo red as a selective decolouriser while crystal violet stains the cell wall, leaving the cytoplasm unstained.

Solutions
  1. 5% Tannic acid.
  2. 0.5% Aqueous crystal violet.
  3. 0.5% Congo red.
Procedure
  1. Make a thin smear and allow to dry. Do not fix.
  2. Place the slide in tannic acid for 30-60 minutes.
  3. Wash in tap water.
  4. Flood the slide with 0.5% crystal violet for two minutes.
  5. Wash with water.
  6. Flood the slide with 0.5% Congo red for 2-3 minutes.
  7. Blot, dry and examine.
Results

Outer cell wall and cross walls: Mauve

Staining Methods for Flagella

Young cultures are used to demonstrate flagella. Chemically clean slides must be used and the smear made with proper care. All the methods are of silver impregnation techniques.

Fontana's Silver Method for Flagella

Solutions
  1. 1% Osmic acid
  2. Fontana's mordant
    • 10% Tannic acid 10 ml
    • Saturated potassium alum 10 ml
    • 10% Ferric chloride 1 ml
    • Mix and stand for three minutes before use. The solution does not keep.
  3. Fontana's silver solution
    • Dissolve 5 g silver nitrate in 100 ml distilled water.
    • Dilute 5 ml of concentrated ammonia with 45 ml distilled water.
    • Add 35 ml of the diluted ammonia solution to 90 ml of silver nitrate.
    • Continue to add drop by drop until the precipitate which is formed is redissolved.
    • Continue to add more until a faint opalescence is seen.
Procedure
  1. Place one drop of 1% osmic acid towards one end of a very clean slide.
  2. With a loop, gently transfer one drop of the water of condensation of an 18 hour old agar slope culture to the drop of Osmic acid and mix gently. Allow the drop to run the whole length of the slide.
  3. Allow to dry in air.
  4. Add Fontana's mordant for three minutes.
  5. Wash in distilled water.
  6. Heat Fontana's silver solution in a test tube and flood the slide. Leave for two minutes.
  7. Wash in distilled water.
  8. Dry in air and mount immediately.
Results

Organisms: black
Flagella: light brown

Loeffler's silver method for flagella

Solutions
  1. Loeffler's flagella mordant
    • 20% aqueous Tannic acid 100 ml
    • Ferrous sulphite crystals 2 g
    • 10% alcoholic solution basic fuchsin 10 ml
    • Distilled water 4 ml
  2. Loeffler's flagella stain
    • 10% alcoholic solution of basic fuchsin 20 ml
    • 3% Aniline water 80 ml
Procedure
  1. Make a thin smear and fix with gentle heat.
  2. Flood slide with Loeffler's flagella mordant for five minutes.
  3. Wash with distilled water.
  4. Stain with heated Loeffler's flagella stain for three minutes.
  5. Wash with distilled water, dry and mount.

Bacteria: red
Flagella: pink.

Note: The smear for the staining of flagella should be prepared very gently to avoid breaking of flagella.

Staining Method for Spirochaetes

The usual and easier way of examining spirochaetes is with the darkfield microscope. They do not stain well with aniline dyes and because the spirochaete spirals are so thin, they are not easily seen by most other staining methods. However, they have the capacity to reduce silver nitrate to metallic silver, thus the demonstration of spirochaetes in smears can be achieved by the silver impregnation method.

Fontana's Method for Spirochaetes

Solutions
  1. Fontana's fixative
    • Glacial acetic acid 1 ml
    • Formalin 2 ml
    • Distilled water 100 ml
  2. Absolute alcohol
  3. Fontana's mordant: refer to Fontana's silver method for flagella
  4. Fontana's silver solution: refer to Fontana's silver method for flagella
Procedure
  1. Make a thin smear
  2. Treat smear with Fontana's fixative three times for 30 seconds each.
  3. Wash off the fixative with absolute alcohol and cover with absolute alcohol for three minutes.
  4. Drain off the alcohol and carefully burn off the remainder until the film is dry.
  5. Apply Fontana's mordant, heat until steam rises and leave for 30 seconds.
  6. Wash well in distilled water and dry the slide.
  7. Apply the Fontana's silver solution, heat until steam rises and leave for about 1/2 minute (the film turns brown in colour).
  8. Wash in distilled water, dry and mount in neutral synthetic medium.
Results

Spirochaetes: brownish black
Background: brownish yellow
