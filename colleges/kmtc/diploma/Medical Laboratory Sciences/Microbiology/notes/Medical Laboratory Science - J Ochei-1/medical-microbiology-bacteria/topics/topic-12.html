ü¶† Aerobic Gram Positive Cocci

This group of organisms belong to two main families: Micrococcaceae and Streptococcaceae.

Family Micrococcaceae

Characteristics: Members of this family are Gram positive cocci in regular or irregular clusters, aerobic or facultative anaerobic, non-motile, non-spore forming and non-capsulated. There are two medically important genera: Micrococcus and Staphylococcus.

Genus Micrococcus

Micrococci are Gram positive cocci of irregular sizes in irregular clusters or tetrads. Generally, they are of little medical importance.

  • Normal habitat: Micrococci are found in soil, water as well as on the skin.
  • Characteristics: They grow more slowly than the staphylococci. They are strict aerobes, and so do not ferment glucose anaerobically. They are novobiocin resistant.
  • Pathogenicity: They are saprophytes and commensals. They are known to cause a form of urinary tract infection in young women.
02

üçá Genus Staphylococcus

Type Species: Staph. aureus

Staph. aureus is the most medically important member in terms of pathogenicity of the group. Two other less important members are Staph. epidermis and Staph. saprophyticus.

Staphylococcus aureus

Normal habitat: Staphylococci are commonly present as parasites of man and they form part of bacterial flora of the skin, upper respiratory tract and intestinal tract. Staph. aureus is carried in the nose of 20‚Äì40% of healthy individuals.

Characteristics: They are Gram positive cocci in clusters, pairs or singles. They grow readily on a variety of media and ferment many carbohydrates without gas. Colonies may be white, grey or golden yellow. The species name aureus is derived from the Latin word, 'aureus' meaning golden. They are halophilic (salt loving).

Pathogenicity

Staph. aureus causes

  1. 1Abscesses, boils and impetigo: It is a common cause of secondary infection of ulcers, burns, wound, skin disorders and insect bites.
  2. 2Conjunctivitis, especially in neonates.
  3. 3Septicaemia, endocarditis and osteomyelitis
  4. 4Pneumonia and empyema
  5. 5Cross infections in hospitals
  6. 6Food poisoning due to the production of enterotoxin B.

Toxins and Enzymes Produced by Staph. aureus

The pathogenicity of Staph. aureus depends on its ability to multiply and spread widely in tissues, and on the production of many extracellular substances‚Äîtoxins and enzymes. These substances include:

  1. Exotoxin: A thermolabile mixture that contains several haemolysins (lytic toxins) that destroy red blood cells and platelets.
  2. Leucocidin: A soluble substance that kills exposed white blood cells. It is heatlabile.
  3. Enterotoxin: A soluble material that is produced by about 30 to 50% of strains of Staph. aureus. It is a protein that can withstand boiling for about 30 minutes. Staph. aureus grows and forms the toxin in food stuff and causes vomiting and diarrhoea when such food is ingested. The toxin acts on the central nervous system (in the vomit centre) and not, like other enterotoxins, on the intestinal epithelium. There are four distinct antigen groups (A-D) of these enterotoxins.
  4. Coagulase: Staph. aureus produces an enzyme, coagulase, which clots fibrinogen in plasma to form fibrin. The demonstration of coagulase is the best single test to identify a pathogenic Staph. aureus.
  5. Deoxyribonuclease (DNAse): This enzyme is capable of destroying deoxyribomucleic acid (DNA).
  6. Hyaluronidase: This is a spreading factor that aids the Staph. aureus to invade the tissue.
  7. Staphylokinase: This causes fibrinolysis.
  8. Beta-lactamase: This is an enzyme that inactivates antibiotics such as penicillin resulting in penicillin resistance.
  9. Exfoliatin: This substance causes skin to peel off in scalded skin syndrome.

Staphylococcus epidermidis

Staph. epidermidis is generally non-pathogenic.

  • Normal habitat: Staph. epidermidis is a normal flora of the skin. It may also be found in the mouth and in dust.
  • Characteristics: Like Staph. aureus, it grows readily on a variety of media. The colonies are usually whiter than those of Staph. aureus. For this reason, it was previously referred to as Staph. albus (from the Latin word ‚Äúalbus‚Äù meaning white).
  • Pathogenicity: Staph. epidermidis is commonly encountered as a contaminant or commensal in specimens from skin lesions, wounds, burns, throat, sputum and faeces. However, in some special circumstances, they are regarded pathogenic when linked to peritonitis in patients on peritoneal dialysis; chronic septicaemia or endocarditis in patients having heart surgery or fitted with artificial heart valve; bacteraemia following infection of indwelling catheters, shunts and annulae.

Staphylococcus saprophyticus

This species is widely distributed in the environment. It is usually associated with cystitis, pyelonephritis and acute urethritis in young women.

üî¨ Laboratory Diagnosis of Staphylococcal Infections

Specimens: Specimens include skin swab, pus, sputum or tracheal aspirate, blood culture, cerebero-spinal fluid. When food-poisoning is suspected, faeces, vomitus and remains of food are also submitted. Anterior nasal swabs are collected to detect Staph. aureus carriers.

Stained smears: Gram positive cocci of uniform size about 1¬µm in diameter, and in clusters, pairs or singly. (Fig 12.2).

Culture: Staphylococci grow well on most routine bacteriological media aerobically in CO‚ÇÇ enriched atmosphere and less well anaerobically. The optimum temperature of growth is 37¬∞C but can grow in a range of 10‚Äì42¬∞C. The colonies on most media are round, smooth, raised and measuring about 1‚Äì2 mm in diameter.

Staph. aureus colonies are golden yellow to creamy-white; some strains are beta-haemolytic on blood agar. Staph. epidermidis (Staph.albus) colonies are white. Staph. saprophyticus colonies are white or yellow.

Selective media for Staph. aureus

  1. Manintol salt agar: Staph. aureus produces yellow colonies.
  2. 10% salt cooked meat medium.
  3. Lithium chloride and tellurite (Ludlam's) medium. Staph. aureus reduces potassium tellurite in the medium to produce black colonies.

These media are used to recover Staph. aureus from contaminated specimens.

Identification media for Staph. aureus

  1. Deoxyribonucleic acid (DNAse) agar.
  2. Phenolphthalein phosphate agar.

Bacteriophage typing of Staph. aureus: Phage typing of Staph. aureus is done to investigate cross infections in hospitals and staphylococcal food poisoning. Strains are generally distinguished by their patterns of susceptibility to lysis by any of the universally accepted set of 23 standard typing phages. The phage type of the staphylococcal strain is reported as the combined designations of the phages which strongly lyse it. Phage typing is usually carried out by a central reference laboratory.

Antibiotic sensitivity: Many strains of Staph. aureus produce the enzyme penicillinase (a beta lactamase) which inactivates penicillin and renders them penicillin-resistant. For such strains, chemotherapy is effected with methicilin, cloxacillin, flucloxacillin and the cephalosporins. Ordinarily Staph. aureus is sensitive to erythromycin, lincomycin, penicillins, vancomycin, fucidin, clindamycin, rifampicin, cephalosporins, ciprofloxacin.

üîó Streptococci

Family Streptococcaceae

Characteristics: Streptococci are Gram positive cocci arranged in pairs, short or long chains. They are facultative anaerobes, growth being enhanced in microaerophilic condition. These are non motile, non sporing and catalase negative. Some species produce haemolysis.

üí°

Note

Catalase test is useful for differentiating streptococci from staphylococci, which are catalase positive.

Classification of Streptococci

Streptococci can be classified into obligate anaerobes and the facultative anaerobes. The traditional classification depends on the type of haemolysis produced on blood agar media. Three types of haemolysis are recognised:

  1. 1 Alpha (Œ±) haemolytic streptococci produce partial haemolysis which is indicated by green discolouration of the medium. They show small zone of partial haemolysis around the colonies about 1‚Äì2 mm wide. The discolouration is due to reduced haemoglobin.
  2. 2 Beta (Œ≤) haemolytic streptococci produce well defined, clear zone of complete haemolysis (about 2‚Äì4 mm wide) around the colonies. Haemolysis is enhanced under anaerobic incubation.
  3. 3 Gamma (Œ≥) or non haemolytic streptococci produce no change in the medium.

Lancefield groups of Streptococci: In 1933, Lancefield classified the haemolytic streptococci serologically into groups based on the nature of specific carbohydrate (C carbohydrate) antigen on the bacterial cell wall. This antigen can be identified using group specific antiserum. The carbohydrate identification enables a strain to be placed in one of the 20 Lancefield groups designated A‚ÄìV excluding I and J.

üî• Strep. pyogenes (Group A)

Pathogenicity: Strep. pyogenes causes pyogenic infections with a tendency to spread locally, along lymphatics and through the blood stream.

  • Respiratory infections: Acute sore throat (tonsillitis and pharyngitis), peritonsilar abscess (quinsy), and 'Scarlet' fever.
  • Ear infections: Otitis media and mastoiditis.
  • Skin infections: Wound infections, cellulitis, burns, erysipelas and impetigo.
  • Puerperal sepsis (genital infections).
  • Septicaemia.
  • Endocarditis.
  • Abscesses of organs such as brain, lung, liver and kidney.
  • Non-suppurative complications: Acute rheumatic fever and acute glomerulonephritis.

Enzymes and Toxins

  1. 1Haemolysins: Haemolysin O (oxygen labile, antigenic) and Streptolysin S (oxygen stable, non-antigenic).
  2. 2Streptokinase (Fibrinolysin): Causes lysis of fibrin.
  3. 3Hyaluronidase: Spreading factor.
  4. 4Deoxyribonuclease (streptodornase): Causes hydrolysis of DNA.
  5. 5NADase: Kills leucocytes.
  6. 6Erythrogenic (Dick) toxin: Causes erythematous skin rashes in scarlet fever.

Dick test

Used to test susceptibility to scarlet fever.

  1. Inject toxin intradermally into one forearm.
  2. Inject heat inactivated toxin into the other forearm as a control.
  3. Positive result: Erythematous flush appearing within 8‚Äì12 hours on the first arm and fading after 24 hours.

üè∑Ô∏è Other Streptococcal Groups

Strep. agalactiae (Group B)

Causes bovine mastitis in cattle. In humans:

  • Neonates: septicaemia, pneumonia, and meningitis.
  • Septic abortion and puerperal sepsis.
  • Otitis media, arthritis and urinary tract infection in children.

Group C

Causes pharyngitis, impetigo, wound infections, puerperal sepsis, and urinary tract diseases.

Group D Streptococci (Enterococci)

Includes enterococci (Strep. faecalis, Strep. facium, etc.) and non-enterococci (Strep. bovis, Strep. equinus). Causes urinary tract infections, endocarditis, ear infections, suppurative abdominal lesions, wound infections.

Viridans Streptococci

Alpha haemolytic. Includes Strep. salivarius, Strep. mutans, Strep. sanguis, Strep. mitis and Strep. milleri. Normal flora of throat and mouth. Capable of causing sub-acute bacterial endocarditis (SBE).

üß™ Laboratory Diagnosis of Streptococcal Infections

Specimens: Throat swab, pus, blood, ear swab, wound swab, CSF (neonates), urine.

Stained smears: Gram positive cocci in short chains, in pairs or singly. Long chains in fluid media.

Identification Tests

  • Group A Streptococcus: Beta-haemolytic on blood agar. Sensitive to bacitracin (0.05 units/disc). Selective medium: Crystal violet blood agar.
  • Group B Streptococcus (S. agalactiae): Beta-haemolytic. CAMP test positive (arrow-head haemolysis with S. aureus). Resistant to bacitracin. Selective medium: Gentamicin blood agar.
  • Group D Streptococci: Bile-aesculin test positive. Growth in 6.5% NaCl (enterococci only). Growth at 45¬∞C.
  • Viridans Streptococci: Alpha haemolytic. Optochin resistant.

Lancefield Grouping

Serological classification based on C-carbohydrate antigen using specific antisera. Methods include acid extraction, formamide method, or commercial latex agglutination kits (e.g., Streptex).

Serological Tests

Used to diagnose complications of post Group A streptococcal infections.

  • Anti-streptolysin O (ASO): Useful for rheumatic fever diagnosis.
  • Anti-DNAse B: Useful for post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis.

Antibiotic Sensitivity

Penicillin and erythromycin are active against Strep. pyogenes and Strep. agalactiae. Enterococci are sensitive to ampicillin but resistant to penicillin.

üåë Pneumococci (Streptococcus pneumoniae)

Characteristics: Capsulated, lancet-shaped Gram positive diplococci. Facultatively anaerobic. Alpha haemolytic. Catalase and oxidase negative.

Pathogenicity: Lobar pneumonia, bronchopneumonia, septicaemia, meningitis, endocarditis, otitis media, sinusitis, conjunctivitis, peritonitis, arthritis.

Laboratory Diagnosis

Specimens: Blood, sputum, CSF.

Microscopy: Gram positive, lancet-shaped diplococci. Numerous PMNs.

Quellung Reaction: Capsule swelling test with specific antiserum.

Culture: Alpha haemolytic colonies on blood agar. "Draughtsman" appearance (umbonated) after 48 hours.

üåë Anaerobic Gram Positive Cocci

This group includes the genera Peptococcus and Peptostreptococcus.

  • Genus Peptococcus: Strict anaerobe, gram positive coccus in small clusters or singly. Catalase negative.
  • Genus Peptostreptococcus: Gram positive cocci in chains.

Habitat: Commensals on skin, mouth, GIT, and female genital tract.

Pathogenicity: Mixed infections with Bacteroides in intra-abdominal infections, brain abscesses, puerperal sepsis, bone/joint infections.

Laboratory Diagnosis: Gram positive cocci. Slow growing, non-haemolytic colonies. Unpleasant smell due to gas production.

Identification: Peptococci are resistant to novobiocin; Peptostreptococci are sensitive. Peptostreptococci inhibited by SPS.
