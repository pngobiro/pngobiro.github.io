üß™ Introduction & Composition

To study and identify micro-organisms, they have to be cultivated and isolated in pure culture. Most bacteria can be cultured in artificial media if the media meet the nutritional and physical growth requirements of the bacteria.

Categories of Media

Chemically Defined (Simple)

Exact concentration of each ingredient is known. Usually highly purified inorganic salts and simple organic compounds. Used to determine specific growth requirements. Not for routine use.

Chemically Undefined (Complex)

Prepared using natural products (meat extract, vegetable infusions). Exact concentration unknown. Used routinely because they are easy to prepare, cheap, and support many bacteria.

Composition of Culture Media

  • Water: Main solvent. Must be deionised or distilled.
  • Peptone: Water soluble product of protein hydrolysis. Source of nitrogen (animal protein) and carbohydrates (soya bean). A good peptone must be neutral, light in colour, hygroscopic, and contain tryptophan.
  • Meat Extracts: Source of amino acids and essential vitamins (e.g., Lab Lemco).
  • Yeast Extract: Stimulant for bacterial growth (e.g., in TCBS medium).
  • Mineral Salts: Sulphates, phosphates, magnesium, potassium, iron, calcium for enzyme activity. NaCl maintains isotonicity.
  • Carbohydrates: Sources of carbon and energy. Also used for differentiation (e.g., lactose in MacConkey).

üìã Types of Media

Liquid vs. Solid Media

Liquid Media (Broth): Bacteria move freely. Growth shown by turbidity. Used for biochemical testing, blood culture, motility. Disadvantage: purity cannot be guaranteed.

Solid Media: Bacteria form visible colonies. Used for identification. Solidified using Agar or Gelatin.

Properties of Agar

  • Gels at 1% concentration
  • Melts at 98¬∞C
  • Sets on cooling at 42¬∞C
  • Bacteriologically inert

Classification by Function

Type Description Examples
Basal Media Simple media supporting non-fastidious organisms. Nutrient broth, Infusion broth, Digest broth.
Enriched Media Enriched with blood, serum, or extracts for fastidious bacteria. Blood agar, Chocolate agar, Loeffler's serum.
Selective Media Solid media containing inhibitors to suppress unwanted bacteria. Tellurite (Diphtheria), DCA (Salmonella/Shigella).
Enrichment Media Liquid media favoring specific pathogens. Selenite F broth (Salmonella).
Differential Media Contains indicators to distinguish organisms. MacConkey (lactose fermentation), Blood agar (haemolysis).
Auxanographic Media Deficient in nutrients; used to identify growth factor requirements. Testing for X and V factors for H. influenzae.
Transport Media Preserves delicate organisms during transit. Amies, Stuart's.

‚öôÔ∏è Preparation of Culture Media

Most laboratories use dehydrated or ready-made media for ease, standardization, and cost-effectiveness. Preparation requires strict adherence to instructions.

Stages in Preparation

  1. Weighing and dissolving: Use bacteriological quality materials and distilled/deionised water. Dehydrated media are hygroscopic; close bottles tightly. Avoid overheating.
  2. Adjustment of pH: Routine media pH is 6.8‚Äì7.4. Adjust with 0.1 mol/L NaOH or HCl.
  3. Sterilisation: Autoclave usually at 121¬∞C (15 psi) for 15 mins or 115¬∞C (10 psi). Add heat-sensitive ingredients (blood, serum) after cooling to 50¬∞C.
  4. Dispensing: Perform in a drought-free "pouring room". Pour 15‚Äì20 ml per petri dish. Flame surface to remove bubbles. Store at 2‚Äì4¬∞C.

Common Faults

  • Poor growth: Overheating, repeated remelting, incorrect pH.
  • Soft agar: Too much water, acidic pH hydrolysis, over-sterilisation.
  • Darkening: Caramelisation of sugars, oversterilisation.
  • Precipitation: Phosphates not removed, prolonged heating.

General Media Control

Sterility Test: Incubate media at 37¬∞C overnight. Check for visible growth. Contamination can be surface (aerosols, unsterile blood) or sub-surface (unsterile dish, inadequate autoclaving).

Performance Test (Quality Control): Tests the ability of media to support growth, differentiate, or inhibit specific organisms.

üî¨ Culture Methods

Aseptic technique is mandatory to prevent contamination of cultures/specimens and the work area.

Plate Culture Methods

Drying plates: Surface must be dried to isolate single colonies (10 mins at 45¬∞C).

Inoculation (Plating out): Apply inoculum to area 1, sterilise loop, streak to area 2, and so on. Do not cross previously inoculated areas. Do not touch sides.

Tube Culture Methods

  • Slope Cultures: Streak inoculum down the centre and then zig-zag. Used for maintenance and biochemical tests.
  • Deep Cultures (Shake cultures): Melted, cooled to 45¬∞C, inoculated, mixed, and solidified. Good for anaerobes.
  • Stab Cultures: Inoculate with straight wire through the centre (e.g., Motility Indole Urea medium).
  • Roll Tubes: Medium rolled on tube walls. Obsolete method for viable count.
  • Liquid Media: Use loop, straight wire or pipette. For subculture, rub loop against inner side below liquid level.
