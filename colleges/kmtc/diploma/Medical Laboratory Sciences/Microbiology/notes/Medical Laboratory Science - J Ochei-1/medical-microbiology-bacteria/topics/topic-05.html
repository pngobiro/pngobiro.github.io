Nutrition, Growth and Metabolism of Bacteria

üìö Chapter 5
‚è±Ô∏è 15 min read
01

üå± Overview of Growth

The major biological function of the microbial cells is growth. Growth is the orderly increase in mass and number of all the components of the cell. All living organisms require nutrients for their growth as well as for cellular maintenance and multiplication. The nutrients provide the energy required by the microbial cell to synthesise new cellular components and reproduce.

Bacterial cell consists chiefly of proteins, chromosomes, enzymes, membranes, cell wall and flagella. These components are made up of large (macro) molecules. The main function of the bacterial metabolism is the synthesis of macromolecules. These macromolecules are derived from their respective subunits: proteins from amino acid; nucleic acids from nucleotides; polysaccharides from simple sugars and lipids from glycerol, alcohols and fatty acids.

Bacteria differ greatly in their needs of nutrients. Basically, they obtain their nutrients from their environment. Bacteria (and fungi) are called osmotrophs because they obtain their food in solutions that diffuse through their plasma membranes. They cannot however, transport macromolecules such as proteins or polysaccharides through their membranes. These macromolecules are therefore first digested outside the cell by exoenzymes that are excreted by the cell to the external environment.

02

ü•ò Nutritional Requirements

  1. Water All metabolic functions of a bacterial cell are performed in aqueous environments. Bacterial cultivation and growth requires water, and any loss of water from the cell disrupts cellular functions.
  2. Energy source Bacterial cellular functions such as synthesis of new components, concentration of nutrients in the cell, or cell mobility require energy.
    • Phototrophs: Obtain energy from sunlight (e.g., cyanobacteria produce $O_2$).
    • Chemotrophs: Obtain energy from the oxidation of chemical compounds (e.g., glucose).
  3. Carbon requirements Carbon atoms are essential in the nutritional requirements of bacteria.
    • Autotrophs: Derive carbon from atmospheric $CO_2$ (mainly free-living).
    • Heterotrophs: Depend on organic molecules for carbon source (includes most human pathogens).
  4. Nitrogen requirements Nitrogen is necessary for the synthesis of essential amino sugars, nucleotides and vitamins. Most bacteria satisfy their nitrogen requirements from inorganic salts of nitrate or ammonium.
  5. Growth factor requirements Some bacteria require organic compounds they cannot synthesise (growth factors):
    • Amino acids (from degraded proteins)
    • Purines and pyrimidines (for DNA/RNA)
    • Bacterial vitamins (for co-enzymes)
  6. Oxygen requirements Oxygen is required by most bacteria to generate energy for respiration.
  7. Trace elements Minerals required in small amounts for enzyme activity (Potassium, Cobalt, Magnesium, Manganese, Calcium, Iron).

üî¢ Counting Bacterial Populations

Direct Microscopic Count

Petroff-Hausser counting chamber: This consists of a platform with a grid. The suspension is drawn into the chamber by capillary action. The bacteria in the central area (25 large squares) are counted and multiplied by 50,000 to give bacteria per ml. Ideally, dilute sample so one square contains about 10 organisms. This methods counts both dead and living bacteria.

Electronic counters: Devices like the Coulter counter record changes in conductivity as cells pass through a slit.

Viable Counts

Based on the assumption that each cell can multiply to form a distinct colony. Common methods include:

  • Spread plate method: Spread 0.1‚Äì0.5 ml of diluted suspension over dry solid medium.
  • Pour plate method: Mix known volume with cooled molten agar and pour into petri dish.
  • Miles and Mizra method: A surface drop method using measured drops on divided plate segments.

Turbidimetric Measurements

Based on the principle that the number of particles in suspension is inversely proportional to light transmitted. Optical density (OD) is measured using a photometer and converted to bacterial numbers using a calibration curve.

üå°Ô∏è Factors Affecting Growth

Temperature

  • Mesophiles: Grow between $20-50^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$. Most pathogens belong to this group.
  • Psychrophiles: Grow between $0^{\circ}\mathrm{C}-20^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$.
  • Thermophiles: Grow between $50^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ and $100^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$.

pH

Most bacteria reproduce at a pH range of 6‚Äì8 (Neutrophiles). Acidophiles grow in acid pH, Alkalophiles grow in alkaline pH.

Gaseous Requirements

Obligatory Aerobes Require free oxygen (e.g., Pseudomonas aeruginosa).
Obligatory Anaerobes Grow only in absence of oxygen (e.g., Clostridium tetani).
Facultative Anaerobes Multiply in presence or absence of oxygen (e.g., Staphylococcus aureus).
Microaerophilic Require low oxygen tension (e.g., Campylobacter spp.).
Carboxyphilic Require raised $CO_2$ levels (e.g., Neisseria gonorrhoeae).
05

üå™Ô∏è Principle of Anaerobiasis

Obligatory anaerobes lack cytochrome enzymes, catalase and peroxidase. In the presence of free $O_2$, many bacteria produce hydrogen peroxide ($H_2O_2$). Aerobic bacteria possess catalase which breaks down $H_2O_2$:

$$2 \mathrm{H}_2\mathrm{O}_2 \longrightarrow 2 \mathrm{H}_2\mathrm{O} + \mathrm{O}_2$$

Since anaerobes lack catalase, they are destroyed by $H_2O_2$. Their growth depends on the oxidation-reduction (redox) potential (Eh) of the environment. Low Eh allows anaerobes to grow. Anaerobiasis can be achieved using an anaerobic jar system.

‚ûó Bacterial Division & Growth Cycle

Bacteria multiply by binary fission. Mitotic cell division occurs in five steps: Interphase, Prophase, Metaphase, Anaphase, and Telophase (Fig 5.12).

The increase in bacterial number follows a geometric progression: $1 \rightarrow 2^1 \rightarrow 2^2 \rightarrow \ldots \rightarrow 2^n$, where $n$ is the number of generations.

‚è±Ô∏è

Generation Time

The length of time it takes for a single bacterial cell to divide or for the population to double. E.g., E. coli is ~12.5 minutes, M. tuberculosis is 13-15 hours.

Bacterial Growth Curve

There are four phases in a bacterial growth cycle (Fig 5.14):

  1. 1Lag phase: Adjustment to new environment; metabolic activity without division.
  2. 2Log (Exponential) phase: Multiplication at constant maximum rate.
  3. 3Stationary phase: Balance between death and multiplication due to crowding/nutrient exhaustion.
  4. 4Decline phase: Death rate exceeds multiplication.
07

‚ö° Bacterial Metabolism

Metabolism involves two parts:

  • Anabolism (biosynthesis): Construction of new cell material (requires energy).
  • Catabolism (dissimilation): Breakdown of nutrients into simpler molecules (releases energy).

Metabolism of nutrients occurs in four stages: breakdown of large molecules (hydrolysis), transport into cell, partial oxidation (producing ATP), and complete oxidation.

Fermentation vs. Respiration

Fermentation is an oxygen-free process where organic electron acceptors are reduced. Various end products are produced.

Respiration involves an electron transport system. If oxygen is the final acceptor, it is aerobic respiration. If other acceptors (nitrates, sulphates) are used, it is anaerobic respiration.
