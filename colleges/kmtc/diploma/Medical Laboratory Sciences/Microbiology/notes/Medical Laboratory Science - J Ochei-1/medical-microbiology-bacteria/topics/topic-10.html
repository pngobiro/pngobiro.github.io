Bacterial Identification Methods

üìö Chapter 10
‚è±Ô∏è 20 min read

Bacterial identification methods are tests done to speciate, differentiate or identify bacteria. Most of these tests are based on the biochemical reactions and enzymatic activity of bacteria. These tests should be performed on pure cultures isolated from single colonies to ensure validity.

üí°

Controls

It is good laboratory practice to include positive and negative controls along with the tests to confirm that media and reagents are working correctly.

01

‚ö° Key Enzymatic Tests

Catalase Test

Used to differentiate staphylococci (positive) from streptococci (negative).

Principle: The enzyme catalase breaks down hydrogen peroxide into water and oxygen.

$$2 \mathrm{H}_2\mathrm{O}_2 \longrightarrow 2 \mathrm{H}_2\mathrm{O} + \mathrm{O}_2$$

Method: Add a drop of 3% $H_2O_2$ to a colony on a slide or dip a colony into the solution. Immediate gas bubbling indicates a positive result.

‚ö†Ô∏è

Precautions

  • Do not use a nichrome wire loop (causes false positives).
  • Avoid picking up blood agar (RBCs contain catalase).
  • Use fresh cultures (24h).

Coagulase Test

Used to differentiate Staphylococcus aureus (positive) from other staphylococci.

Bound Coagulase (Slide Test)

Converts fibrinogen directly to fibrin on the cell surface. Detects "clumping factor".

Method: Mix colony with plasma on a slide. Immediate clumping within 5-10 seconds = Positive.

Free Coagulase (Tube Test)

Activates coagulase reacting factor (CRF) in plasma to convert fibrinogen to fibrin.

Method: Mix broth culture with plasma in a tube. Incubate at 37¬∞C. Clot formation within 1-4 hours = Positive.

Oxidase Test

Used to identify Pseudomonas, Neisseria, Vibrio (positive) vs Enterobacteria (negative).

Principle: Cytochrome oxidase oxidizes a redox dye (tetramethyl-p-phenylenediamine dihydrochloride) to a deep purple colour.

Result: Deep purple colour within 10 seconds indicates a positive result.

Urease Test

Used to identify Proteus species and H. pylori.

Principle: Urease hydrolyses urea to ammonia and $CO_2$, raising the pH and turning the indicator (phenol red) pink.

$$\mathrm{CO(NH_2)_2} + \mathrm{H_2O} \xrightarrow{\text{Urease}} 2\mathrm{NH_3} + \mathrm{CO_2}$$

Controls: Proteus vulgaris (+), E. coli (-).

üç¨ Carbohydrate Metabolic Tests

Oxidation-Fermentation (O-F) Test

Differentiates oxidative (aerobic) bacteria (e.g., Ps. aeruginosa) from fermentative (anaerobic) bacteria (e.g., E. coli).

Open Tube Sealed Tube (Oil) Interpretation
Yellow (Acid) Green/Blue (No change) Oxidative (e.g., Pseudomonas)
Yellow (Acid) Yellow (Acid) Fermentative (e.g., E. coli)
Green/Blue Green/Blue Asaccharolytic

IMViC Tests

A set of tests for differentiating Enterobacteriaceae.

Indole Test
Detects breakdown of tryptophan to indole. Positive: Red ring with Kovac's reagent. (E. coli +, Klebsiella -)
Methyl Red (MR)
Detects stable acid production from glucose fermentation. Positive: Red colour (pH < 4.5). (E. coli +, Enterobacter -)
Voges-Proskauer (VP)
Detects production of acetoin from glucose. Positive: Pink colour with KOH and alpha-naphthol. (Enterobacter +, E. coli -)
Citrate Utilisation
Detects ability to use citrate as sole carbon source. Positive: Blue colour on Simmon's Citrate agar. (Klebsiella +, E. coli -)

ONPG Test

Detects $\beta$-galactosidase enzyme. Differentiates late lactose fermenters from non-lactose fermenters.

Positive: Yellow colour. (E. coli +, Proteus -)

03

üîç Specific Identification Tests

Bile Solubility Test

Differentiates Strep. pneumoniae (soluble) from viridans streptococci (insoluble). Pneumococci autolyse in the presence of bile salts (sodium deoxycholate), clearing the turbidity.

CAMP Test

Identifies Group B Streptococci (S. agalactiae). They produce a factor that enhances the $\beta$-haemolysis of Staph. aureus, forming an arrow-head zone of haemolysis.

Aesculin Hydrolysis

Differentiates Group D streptococci (Enterococcus). Organism hydrolyses aesculin to aesculetin, which reacts with ferric salts to produce blackening of the medium.

Phenylalanine Deaminase (PPA) Test

Differentiates Proteus group (Positive). Phenylalanine is deaminated to phenylpyruvic acid, which turns green with ferric chloride.

04

üß© Miscellaneous Tests

Hydrogen Sulphide ($H_2S$)

Detects $H_2S$ production (blackening) using lead acetate paper or iron-containing media (TSI, KIA). Positive: Proteus vulgaris, Salmonella.

Nitrate Reduction

Detects reduction of nitrate to nitrite. Red colour with sulphanilic acid and alpha-naphthylamine.

DNAse Test

Detects DNA hydrolysis. Clear zone around colony on DNA agar after HCl addition. Positive: Staph. aureus.

Lecithinase (Nagler)

Detects lecithinase production on egg-yolk agar. Opacity around colonies inhibited by antitoxin. Specific for Clostridium perfringens.

05

üöÄ Modern Identification Systems

Modern laboratories use miniaturised multi-test systems to perform biochemical identification rapidly and accurately.

  • API System: Plastic strips with cupules containing dehydrated substrates. A suspension of the organism is added. Results generate a numerical profile code.
  • Enterotube: A compartmentalised tube for identifying Enterobacteriaceae.
  • Molecular Methods: PCR, LCR, and genetic probes are the newest tools for rapid and specific identification.
