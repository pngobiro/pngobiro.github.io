Methods of Cultivation of Viruses

01

üß™ Overview

Viruses are obligate intracellular parasites and cannot grow on inanimate media. They need living cells for replication, which can be provided by three different methods:

  1. Animal inoculation.
  2. Inoculation of embryonated eggs.
  3. Inoculation of organs, tissue fragments or cell monolayers.
02

üêÅ Animal Inoculation

In the beginning of this century, inoculation of human volunteers was the only known method of cultivation of viruses and understanding viral disease. In the past few decades, however, animal inoculation has been employed for virus isolation. The laboratory animals used include monkeys, rabbits, guinea pigs, rats, hamsters and mice. The choice of animals and route of inoculation (intracerebral, intraperitoneal, subcutaneous, intradermal or intraocular) depends on the type of virus to be isolated. Handling of animals and inoculation into various routes requires special experience. In addition to virus isolation, animal inoculation can also be used to observe pathogenesis, immune response, epidemiology and oncogenesis. Growth of a virus in the inoculated animal may be indicated by visible lesions, disease or death. Sometimes serial passage into animals may be required to obtain visible evidence of viral growth.

Methods of animal inoculation are described in the section 'Small Laboratory Animals'.

03

ü•ö Inoculation of Embryonated Eggs

An embryonated egg provides an aseptic environment containing various types of tissues. A large number of viruses can be grown in the different areas of the embryonated egg (Fig. 2.1). The inoculation of eggs can be performed with relatively simple equipment.

For inoculation, an 8‚Äì11 days old hen‚Äôs egg, preferably with a white shell, is used. Duck eggs can be used in some cases. After inoculation, virus replication takes 2‚Äì7 days. The contents of the egg are harvested and inspected for evidence of virus growth. Most viruses either produce morphological changes at the site of inoculation or kill the embryo; or produce haemagglutinins as evidence of growth. Egg inoculation is also useful for cultivation of chlamydiae and rickettsiae.

Steps for Cultivation of Viruses in Egg

  1. Candling Candling is the inspection of an egg over a lamp. A special candling lamp may be used. Alternatively, the egg may be held over a strong incandescent lamp. Candling the egg after 3‚Äì5 days of hatching shows whether the egg is fertile or not. Discard the infertile eggs which show a dead embryo. Candle the egg again on the tenth or eleventh day. Mark the air space with a pencil. Select a suitable site for inoculation depending on the type of virus.
  2. Drilling the egg shell The egg shell is disinfected by wiping with dilute alcoholic antiseptic solution. Cut a small hole at the selected site using a sterile motor driven flexible shaft or a dental handpiece. Care must be taken not to damage the egg membrane.
  3. Inoculation The inoculum is injected through the hole on the shell onto the desired site, using a fine needle. Inject slowly to avoid spillage of the inoculum. Seal the opening of inoculation with a sealing mixture prepared by two parts of molten paraffin (55¬∞C) and one part of petroleum jelly. Incubate the egg at 36¬∞C.

Routes of inoculation

The egg can be inoculated by four routes:

‚ñ∂ (a) Intra-amniotic inoculation

Drill a hole just above the amniotic cavity, located by candling. Gently insert the needle until the amniotic sac moves. Thrust the needle through the amniotic membrane and inject the fluid slowly. Use a small inoculum (0.1 ml). Seal the opening immediately. This route of inoculation is useful for influenza, parainfluenza and mumps viruses.

‚ñ∂ (b) Intra-allantoic inoculation

Select a site above the allantoic cavity by candling. Follow the same technique as for the intra-amniotic inoculation. Inoculate into the allantoic cavity. Seal the opening. This is the simplest method of inoculation with a relatively large yield and is suitable for the preparation of vaccines. Influenza and paramyxoviruses grow well in the allantoic cavity.

‚ñ∂ (c) Yolk-sac inoculation

Determine the position of the embryo by candling. Drill a hole in the shell at the centre of the air space at the blunt end. Insert a long needle and deposit the inoculum just below the centre of the egg. To ensure correct position, pull back the plunger until the yolk-sac is pulled up with it. Seal the opening of inoculation. Yolk-sac inoculation is also useful for cultivation of fastidious groups of bacteria such as Chlamydia and Rickettsia species which do not grow on inanimate media.

‚ñ∂ (d) Chorioallantoic membrane (CAM) inoculation

Mark a small triangle at a site where there are no major blood vessels. This can be located by candling. Cut the shell along the triangle to expose the CAM. Prepare a new air space at the site of inoculation by reducing the original air sac. Cut a small hole at the blunt end above the air sac. Place the egg horizontally. Apply a gentle suction with a rubber teat. A new air space develops at the top of the horizontal egg at the site of the triangular cut. Fill a pipette with about 1 ml inoculum and inoculate through the gap in the CAM and the shell. Rock the egg gently to disperse the inoculum evenly over the membrane. Seal both the openings by replacing the cut triangles with the sealing mixture.

Harvesting the fluids

To harvest the virus infected fluids and other structures, the shell must be opened with great care to avoid unwanted dissemination of the virus. Use of safety cabinets is recommended. Never use a drill to open infected eggs because it will create aerosols.

After the desired incubation period, open the air sac portion at the blunt end which is already marked during candling. Use forceps or a pair of scissors for cutting. Collect the contents of the egg in a sterile petri-dish. Aspirate the fluid from the inoculated area with a syringe. For harvesting CAM, after removing the contents of the egg, pull out the CAM gently with tweezers. Wash in saline 2‚Äì3 times. Inspect it for the presence of lesions or pocks. Figure 2.2 shows the pocks produced by a pox virus. Test the harvested fluids by direct method or other methods for the detection and identification of the virus. These methods are discussed later in the 'Diagnosis of viral infections'.

üî¨ Inoculation of Organs, Tissue Fragments or Cell Monolayers

The first application of tissue culture in virology was by Steinhardt et al., in 1913. They used it for the maintenance of vaccinia virus in the fragments of rabbit cornea. The major obstacle in the development of tissue culture was contamination by bacteria. It was overcome when antibiotics became available. After this, major progress was achieved by Enders and others in 1949 by growing polio virus in the tissues of non-neural origin. The virus was till then considered to be a strictly neurotropic virus. Since then, a large number of human viruses have been grown and maintained in tissue cultures.

Types of Tissue Cultures

There are three types of tissue cultures:

  • (i) Organ culture Organ cultures are essentially cultured tissue pieces in which the architecture and physiology of the tissue is retained. Such small bits of organs can be maintained in vitro for a few days. Organ cultures are necessary for the growth of some fastidious viruses, e.g. ferret trachea can be used for the isolation of some rhinoviruses and coronaviruses.
  • (ii) Explant culture This technique is particularly important for the isolation of viruses in the latent stage. The fragments of the tissue are placed in a test tube or a petri dish in a drop of plasma or fibrin clot. These 'explants' are then covered with growth medium. Adenoid tissue explant cultures were used for the isolation of adenoviruses.
  • (iii) Cell culture This is the most widely used technique for growing viruses. By the action of proteolytic enzymes such as trypsin, a tissue is dissociated into its component cells. After washing, cells are suspended in growth medium containing essential amino acids, vitamins, salts, glucose and a buffer. Serum such as foetal calf or newborn bovine serum is added as supplement. Antibiotics are also added to prevent bacterial contamination. A change in pH is indicated by phenol red incorporated in the medium. The cell suspension in this growth medium is dispensed in flasks or tubes. The cells adhere to the surface of the flasks. When incubated under appropriate conditions, they divide and redivide to form a confluent sheet of cells in a single layer. This is known as monolayer. It is achieved by a mechanism known as contact inhibition. This occurs when cells come in contact with surrounding cells which inhibits further multiplication. Formation of a monolayer to cover the surface of the culture vessel usually takes three to seven days depending on the type of cells used.

Types of Cell Culture

Three types of cell cultures are used in virology depending on their origin, chromosomal characters and the number of generations they can be maintained.

Primary cell cultures

These are prepared from an organ which is minced into small pieces, treated with an enzyme and then used for the preparation of a monolayer. They are capable of only limited growth in culture. Only a few serial passages can be made from such primary cell cultures (Fig. 2.3).

The advantage of using a primary cell culture is that, if sufficient animal organs are available, a large number of cultures can be prepared. The disadvantage is that primary cell cultures can contain latent viruses in the donor animal. The commonly used primary cell cultures are monkey kidney, human embryonic kidney, human aninion and chick embryo cell cultures. Primary cell cultures are useful for isolation of viruses such as enteroviruses, myxo- and paramyxoviruses. They are used for large scale production of vaccines.

Diploid cell cultures (semicontinuous cell cultures or cell strains)

These cultures mostly consist of a single type of cells which may undergo 30‚Äì50 passages before ‚Äòsenescence‚Äô or death of the culture. The human embryonic lung or kidney fibroblasts make excellent diploid cell strains, e.g. MRC5, W138 are fibroblast strains.

Diploid cultures are maintained by creating a pool of frozen cells stored in liquid nitrogen. Serial passages of primary cell culture are made and large portions of the initial 8‚Äì10 passages are stored in liquid nitrogen. Routine cultures may be started later, for example, from the fifteenth passage and the same line can be used up to about the thirtieth passage. When the diploid cell strains start dying, the subcultures can be restarted from the frozen portions (Fig. 2.4). In this way, the cells from the same source can be used for many years giving constant results.

Diploid cell strains are used for selective isolation of some viruses, and for preparation of vaccines. These include herpes simplex, cytomegalovirus, varicellazoster and rhinovirus.

Continuous cell-lines

As the name suggests, these cells may be serially subcultured indefinitely (Fig. 2.5). The continuous cell lines have a malignant character, and the number of chromosomes is different from that of the original host. The cells are described as heteroploid. These cells have a very fast growth rate and contact inhibition is absent.

The continuous cell lines commonly used in the laboratory are HeLa (human cervical cancer), HEp2 (human epithelial), BHK21 (baby hamster kidney), RK13 (rabbit kidney) and Vero (African green monkey kidney). HeLa, HEp2 and Vero cells are used for the cultivation of poliovirus, coxsackie virus, adenovirus and herpes simplex virus. RK13 and BHK21 can be valuable for the isolation and propagation of rubella virus. However, the continuous cell lines cannot be used for the preparation of vaccines because vaccines grown in cancer cells are not considered to be safe.

Tissue Culture Media

Cells need culture media for their growth. A large number of tissue culture media have been devised for the growth of cells in culture. Some of them are made for special purposes. Others are made for general use. These media contain various concentrations of amino acids, vitamins, growth factors and inorganic salts. Carbohydrates such as glucose, fructose or galactose are added with glutamine as a source of carbon for metabolism of cells. Such media are also available commercially. For example, MEM (minimum essential medium) by Eagle.

Conditions for Growth and Maintenance of Cell Cultures

Conditions required for cell cultures vary depending upon the type of cells used. Essential vitamins and amino acids are required by all the cell lines. Most components are well preserved at 4¬∞C except for L-glutamine, which should be added freshly at the time of use.

MEM is supplemented with about 10% serum to enhance initial growth. After the monolayer reaches confluence, the serum concentration may be reduced to 0.5% for maintenance of the monolayer. The serum used must be free from infectious agents such as mycoplasma and from antibodies to viruses. Foetal calf serum is most commonly used. Some cell lines need higher concentration of nutrients than others. MEM is the commonest culture medium for cell lines. It contains the following:

  • Base: Balanced salt solution.
  • L-amino acids: Arginine, histidine, lysine, tryptophane, phenylalanine, theonine, leucine, valine, isoleucine, methionine, tyrosine and cystine. L-glutamine is added just before use.
  • Vitamins: Nicotinamide, pyridoxal, thiamine, pantothenic acid, choline, i-inositol, riboflavin, biotin and folic acid.
  • Antibiotics: Penicillin (100u/ml), Streptomycin (200Œºg/ml) and Gentamicin (50Œºg/ml) are freshly added just before use.
  • Foetal calf serum: 10% for growth medium, 0.5% for maintenance medium. Add just before use.

Storage

The serum, antibiotics and l-glutamine need to be stored at -20¬∞C, others are stable at 4¬∞C.

The optimum temperature for cell growth is 35‚Äì37¬∞C, but may be lowered to 33¬∞C for the isolation of viruses such as rhinoviruses or myxoviruses. The incubation should be in the atmosphere of 5‚Äì10% carbon dioxide. The pH of the medium should be 7.2 to 7.4. Phenol red is often added to the medium to indicate changes in pH.

Contamination of Cell Cultures

Cell cultures may be contaminated with cells from another cell-line, bacteria especially mycoplasmas, viruses or fungi. Sources of contamination can be the environment, improper sterilisation of media and other reagents, or poor aseptic techniques.

Cell contamination of cell cultures

A mixed monolayer may result when cell cultures are contaminated with cells from another cell-line. This contamination can be detected by microscopic examination of the cell layer. Cell contamination may be avoided by taking the following precautions:

  1. One should never work with more than one cell strain at a time in a safety cabinet.
  2. Periodic cell identity checks should be done.
  3. Sterile pipettes should be used only once.
  4. Media and reagents should be dispensed and kept frozen in aliquots sufficient only for one task.

Microbial contamination of cell cultures

Accidental microbial contamination can be minimized by taking precautions:

  1. Media, serum and other solutions should be thoroughly tested for sterility before use.
  2. The use of laminar flow cabinets will minimize contamination from air.
  3. Uncontaminated frozen cell-stocks should be always available for replacement.
  4. Contaminated cultures should be immediately discarded and replaced with new stock cultures.

Note

Treatment of contaminated cultures may be attempted only if the contaminant is isolated and identified; and sensitivity test results are obtained. Usually the contamination is considerable by the time it is detected. Generally it is time consuming and impossible to eliminate. However, identification of the contaminant may help in indicating the source of contamination and further precautions can then be taken accordingly.

Materials for Cell Culture Techniques

  • Glassware Borosilicate glassware which can withstand strong solvents and autoclaving is suitable for cell cultures. While cleaning, residual cell fragments must be removed from the used cell culture tubes. This is easily achieved if the cell culture tubes are immersed in hypochlorite solution before autoclaving. This is followed by mechanical brushing and several rinses in tap water and two rinses in purified water.
  • Culture containers Glass and disposable polystyrene plastic containers are routinely used for cell cultures. Large surfaces of cells are produced in a pyrex glass Roux flask. Glass flasks can be readily cleaned and offer good support for most cell lines. Small disposable tissue culture flasks made of polystyrene (Fig. 2.6) are fit for propagating small stocks of viruses. Large volumes of cells can be obtained by growing monolayer cultures in cylindrical roller bottles. These bottles, filled with dispersed cells, are placed horizontally in roller drums and slowly rotated. The cells adhere to the inner surface of the bottles and multiply to form monolayers.
  • Purified water A supply of highly purified water is mandatory for all the solutions coming into contact with the cells. The use of de-ionized single or double distilled water is acceptable.
  • Membrane filters Cellulose membrane filters are used for the sterilization of culture media and other solutions. The pore sizes should vary from 8 ¬µm to 0.025 ¬µm.
  • Laminar flow cabinets The laminar flow cabinets minimize the risk of contaminating the cell cultures with airborne microbes. These cabinets have high efficiency particulate air (HEPA) filters which sterilize the air flowing through the cabinet. Such cabinets protect the operator from potential pathogens and minimize the number of organisms in the working area.
  • Incubators A temperature range of 35‚Äì37¬∞C is optimum for the growth of cell cultures. Humidified incubators with 5% CO‚ÇÇ in air are ideal to make up for the loss of water and CO‚ÇÇ from cultures during incubation.
  • Microscopes Direct viewing of cells cultured in plastic or glass bottles is possible with an inverted microscope at a magnification of 40X.

Technique

  4. Warm about 50 ml of 0.25% (w/v) trypsin solution to 37¬∞C. Transfer the minced tissue to it.
  5. Stir it moderately on a magnetic stirrer at 37¬∞C for 2¬Ω hours. During this period, stop the stirrer at one hour interval, allow the non-dispersed tissue to settle, and collect the supernatant fluid in a separate container. Add fresh, prewarmed trypsin solution and repeat the process.

  7. Pool all the cells collected above. Wash the cells twice in PBS. Dilute the suspension in the growth medium to contain 2 √ó 10‚Åµ cells/ml. Dispense into suitable culture vessels.

After the monolayer in a culture vessel has become confluent, and before the cell death begins, the continuous and semi-continuous cell lines should be subcultured into fresh culture vessels.

Trypsinisation technique

Before trypsinizing any cell-line, ensure that it is healthy and confluent.

  1. Pour off the culture medium aseptically from the culture vessel containing cells.
  2. Wash the cell layer twice with PBS (Solution A) to remove traces of medium.
  3. Add sufficient trypsin-EDTA solution so that it covers the monolayer. Leave for 30‚Äì60 seconds at room temperature. The cell sheet appears opaque. At this stage, the cells appear rounded, but not detached, under the inverted microscope.
  4. Remove the excess of trypsin-EDTA solution and place the cells at 36¬∞C for 2‚Äì3 minutes or until the cell sheet is detached from the surface of the culture vessel.

Freezing Cells for Storage

  1. Trypsinize subconfluent, actively growing cells.
  2. Resuspend the cells in the growth medium and dilute to a desired concentration of 2‚Äì8 √ó 10‚Å∂ viable cells/ml.
  3. Add glycerol to a final concentration of 10% (v/v).
  4. Label sterile ampoules or disposable bottles with the name of the cell line and the number of subculture (passage) (Fig. 2.4 and 2.5). Dispense the cell suspension in aliquots of 1‚Äì2 ml. Place the ampoules in a thick walled polystyrene box. Seal the box and place it in a deep freezer at ‚Äì70¬∞C for about 2‚Äì18 hours.
  5. At ‚Äì70¬∞C, the cell container may be placed in the vapour phase of liquid nitrogen for a long time storage. The cells should not be allowed to thaw before placing them into the liquid nitrogen from the freezer.

Revival of Frozen Cells

  1. Remove the ampoule or bijoux bottle from the liquid nitrogen only after it has been in the liquid nitrogen for at least 18 hours. Immediately thaw the contents by agitation at 37¬∞C in a water bath.
  2. Open the cell suspension container aseptically and put two drops of the suspension in nutrient broth to check for sterility. Incubate at 37¬∞C for 24 hours.
  3. Add the remaining suspension to 15 ml of a suitable growth medium.
  4. Remove 2 ml of the mixture of cells and growth medium and place it in a tube or petri dish containing a coverslip for mycoplasma screening.
  5. Put the remainder in a small (25 cm¬≤) culture flask.
  6. Incubate overnight and note the percentage of viable cells. Decant the growth medium in the flask and replace it with fresh growth medium. Continue incubation until the cell layer is confluent.
  7. Check the sterility tube and mycoplasma culture. Record the number of cells recovered from the frozen cells.

Detection of Mycoplasma in Cell Culture Using Hoechst Stain Kit

Principle This is a DNA fluorochrome staining technique used for the detection of contamination of cell cultures. Cytoplasmic DNA of contaminants such as mycoplasma fluoresces prominently when viewed under fluorescent microscope. DNA fluorochromes such as DAPI or quinacrine mustard can be used.

Reagents

1. Carnoy's fluid

Glacial acetic acid 10 ml
Methanol 30 ml

2. DNA fluorochrome stain

  • Hoechst stain No. 33258 500 ¬µg
  • Merthiolate 100 mg
  • PBS 1000 ml

Working solution: Dilute the stain 1:10 in PBS.

3. Mounting medium

  • Citric acid 1.0507 g
  • Disodium hydrogen phosphate 1.4196 g
  • Glycerol 50 ml
  • Distilled water 50 ml

Method

  1. Remove 2 ml of the cell suspension in growth medium to be checked for mycoplasma and place it in a petri dish containing a coverslip.
  2. Incubate at 36¬∞C till the cell layer is semi confluent on the coverslip.
  3. Add an equal volume of Carnoy's fixative and leave for two minutes.
  4. Decant or aspirate the fluid and replace Carnoy's fixative for five minutes.
  5. Repeat step 4.
  6. Air-dry the coverslip for 30 minutes.
  7. Add 1.0 ml of the working solution of the stain and leave for 10‚Äì30 minutes at room temperature.
  8. Wash the coverslip three times in distilled water.
  9. Allow the coverslip to air-dry.
  10. Mount in the mountant and examine under fluorescent microscope.

Results

Nuclei of the cells in the monolayer exhibit fluorescence under the microscope. Mycoplasma, if present appear as tiny fluorescent dots around the nucleus either singly or in groups. They have a uniform size ranging from 0.1‚Äì0.3 ¬µm in diameter and may be pleomorphic, ranging from spherical to filamentous forms.
