Subcutaneous Mycoses

üìö Mycetoma, Chromoblastomycosis, and Sporotrichosis

01

ü©∫ Overview

Subcutaneous mycoses are infections that involve the skin and subcutaneous tissue. Generally, there is no dissemination to other organs of the body. However, there are exceptions to the rule; Sporothrix schenckii may affect the lungs, meninges, joints, and other organs. Fungi causing subcutaneous mycoses grow in soil or on decaying vegetation. It is a very diverse group of fungi which belong to several unrelated fungal genera. Man and animals act as accidental hosts. The fungal spores are introduced in the cutaneous or subcutaneous tissue through a trauma such as a thorn prick or contamination of an open wound by soil.

üí°

Note

Being a very diverse group, only the fungi most commonly isolated from subcutaneous mycoses are discussed here.

ü¶† Mycetoma (Madurmycosis or Madura Foot)

Mycetoma was first reported from Madura, a town in Southern India, and therefore, is commonly known as Madurmycosis or Madura foot. It is a chronic, slowly progressive, granulomatous infection of the subcutaneous tissue, usually of the foot, and rarely, of the other parts of the body.

Causative Agents

Mycetoma may be caused by a number of actinomycetes and filamentous fungi. A similar condition, botryomycosis is caused by pyogenic bacteria such as Staphylococcus aureus. Identification of the causative agent of mycetoma is therefore, extremely important for treatment.

Fungi commonly involved in mycetoma are Madurella mycetomatis, M. grisea, Phialophora jeanselmei, Leptosphaeria senegalensis, Petriellidium (Allescheria) boydii, Acremonium species. Mycetoma is also caused by actinomycetes such as: Actinomyces madurae, A. pelletieri, Streptomyces somaliensis, Nocardia brasiliensis.

Clinical Disease

After the entry of the organism into the tissue, the disease usually begins as a small subcutaneous swelling of the foot. It enlarges, burrows into the deeper tissues and finally tracks to the skin surface as multiple sinuses discharging viscid, seropurulent fluid containing granules. The granules are microcolonies of the causative agent. Macroscopic and microscopic examination of the granules is of diagnostic value.

Laboratory Diagnosis

Specimen: Exudate from the mycetoma, or tissue.

1. Macroscopic examination

Examine the specimen for the presence of granules. The colour, size and consistency of the granules are helpful in the identification of the causative agent (Table 5.4).

2. Microscopic examination

Direct KOH mount: Crush the granules in a drop of 10% KOH between two slides and examine microscopically. Observe the presence of branching, intertwining hyphae. If they are 1Œºm or less in diameter, they belong to the group of actinomycetes and the infection is called actinomycetoma. If the hyphae are 4 to 8 Œºm in diameter, the causative agent is a filamentous fungus, and the infection is called eumycetoma.

If it is an actinomycetoma, prepare a smear, air dry and stain by Gram's method (refer to Bacteriology section) and confirm the morphology of Gram positive filamentous bacteria. Identify by bacteriological methods.

Inoculate the specimen on Sabourand's agar, with and without cycloheximide and chloramphenicol; and brain heart infusion agar. Incubate at 22‚Äì26¬∞C and examine after 2‚Äì3 days for four weeks.

If there is no growth discard the cultures as negative after four weeks. Identify the fungi isolated on the basis of colonial morphology, microscopic morphology and growth rate. Use other tests if necessary.

üåã Chromoblastomycosis

Causative agents: The disease is widely distributed in the tropical and subtropical areas of the world. Chromoblastomycosis is caused by a group of geophilic dematiaceous (dark coloured) fungi. Three genera are known to cause the disease: Cladosporium, e.g. Cladosporium carrionii, Phialophora, e.g. Phialophora verrucosa, and Fonsecaea, e.g. Fonsecaea pedrosoi.

Clinical disease: Chromoblastomycosis occurs among agricultural workers who fail to wear protective clothing and footware. The spores of the geophilic fungi inhabiting the soil enter the subcutaneous tissue through thorn-pricks or splinter puncture wounds. The infection develops as a papule at the site of inoculation and form warty cutaneous nodules, resembling the florets of a cauliflower. The disease is usually confined to the subcutaneous tissues of the arms and legs, but may also involve the head, face, neck and other body surfaces. Occasionally, these fungi may cause brain abscess.

Laboratory Diagnosis

Specimen: Tissue from the lesion.

(i) Direct microscopy

KOH Mount: Prepare a KOH mount. Examine microscopically for sclerotic bodies (Fig. 5.3) which appear as round or irregular, dark brown, yeast like bodies with septa. They are about 5 to 20 Œºm in size.

Tissue section stained by H&E: (refer to Histopathology section). Presence of sclerotic bodies in the section indicates chromoblastomycosis.

(ii) Culture

Homogenize the tissue and inoculate Sabouraud's agar, with and without cycloheximide and chloromphenicol, and yeast extract phosphate medium if Histoplasma capsulatum is suspected.

Incubate the cultures at 30¬∞C, examining every 2‚Äì3 days, for up to four weeks.

Identify the fungi isolated on the basis of colonial and microscopic morphology and other tests.

Colonial morphology

Causative agents of chromoblastomycosis produce darkly pigmented, slightly folded and heaped-up colonies with a greyish-velvetty surface, and showing a black pigment on the reverse.

Microscopic morphology

Cladosporium species

Branching long chains of elliptical dematiaceous conidia (2‚Äì3 Œºm √ó 4‚Äì5 Œºm), arising from an erect, tall, branching conidiophore are characteristic of some common species of Cladosporium, e.g. Cladosporium carrionii.

Phialophora species

It shows tube-like or cup-shaped phialides from which conidia arise, each having a distinct, small, collar-like projection (collarette). Conidia occur in clusters near the tip of the phialide, e.g. Phialophora verrucosa.

Fonsecaea species

Primary conidia give rise to secondary conidia (sympodial arrangement). This gives an appearance of short chains of primary and secondary conidia around an erect sympodial conidiophore. Cladosporium type of sporulation may also occur showing phialides with collarettes.

üåë Phaeohyphomycosis

Causative agents: There are about 70 dematiaceous fungi which are known to cause phaeohyphomycosis. The most common ones are the species of the genera Exophiala, Bipolaris, Alterneria, Phialophora and Cuvularia.

Clinical disease: The causative agents of phaeohyphomycosis are associated with soil, wood and decaying plants. The infection begins when the fungus is implanted subcutaneously. It may then develop into firm nodules or a cyst, draining abscesses or ulcers. Sometimes, lungs may be affected due to inhalation of spores.

Laboratory Diagnosis

Specimen: Exudate or aspirate from a cyst, or tissue.

Techniques

  • Direct KOH mount: A direct KOH mount of the specimen shows short or elongated, usually dark coloured, hyphal elements, or distorted and swollen yeast-like cells; or a mixture of both. Most causative agents are dematiaceous fungi.
  • Culture: Most routine laboratories are not equipped for the culture and identification of the causative agent of phaecohyphomycosis. The specimens are usually sent to a reference laboratory for processing.

üåπ Sporotrichosis

Causative agent: Sporotrichosis is a subcutaneous infection caused by the dimorphic fungus Sporothrix schenckii. It is a chronic infection having a world-wide distribution. The natural habitat of the fungus is the living or dead vegetation. The infection is an occupational hazard for farmers, gardeners and miners.

Clinical disease: Humans acquire the infection through trauma such as thorn pricks or other minor injuries, usually on the hand, arm or leg. The infection begins as a small, non-healing ulcer. It develops later as nodular lesions of the skin and subcutaneous tissue. The fungus spreads from the primary site through lymphatics, but usually, does not extend beyond the regional lymph nodes. The subcutaneous nodules eventually break down and ulcerate.

Very rarely, the infection may disseminate to lungs and other organs.

Laboratory Diagnosis

Specimens: Exudate from open draining lesions or from unopened subcutaneous nodules, or tissue.

Techniques

(i) Direct microscopy
KOH mount: Sporothrix schenckii appears sparsely as isolated yeast cells, 3‚Äì5 Œºm in diameter, which are usually round in shape and sometimes cigar shaped. The yeast cells do not show budding. Occasionally the fungus shows ‚Äòasteroid bodies‚Äô which are composed of a central fungus cell with eosinophilic material radiating from it.

Staining Sensitivity

The detection of the yeast forms of S. schenckii in the tissue or exudate is very difficult because they are present in small numbers. Periodic-acid Schiff (PAS) or Gomori's methanamine silver (GMS) stains are more sensitive than the KOH mount.

(ii) Culture

  • At 30¬∞C: Being a dimorphic fungus, Sporothrix schenckii grows as a filamentous mould on media such as Sabouraud's agar at 30¬∞C. It grows rapidly within 3-5 days. The colonies are usually small, moist, white to cream coloured initially, they gradually turn dark brown, wrinkled and membranous. Microscopically, the lactophenol cotton blue preparation shows delicate hyphae, which are 1‚Äì2 Œºm in thickness, branching, and septate. They bear single celled conidia, 2‚Äì5 Œºm in diameter. The conidia are arranged sympodially, appearing as flower-like clusters at the tips of single conidiophores (Fig. 5.5). The conidia are often dark brown in colour.
  • At 37¬∞C: On enriched media such as brain heart infusion blood agar (BHIBA), at 37¬∞C, the colonies of S. schenckii appear as soft, and creamy. Microscopically, spherical, oval or cigar-shaped budding cells are seen. The conversion from mould to yeast form occurs within one to five days.

üëÉ Rhinosporidiosis

Causative agent: The agent of rhinosporidiosis is called Rhinosporidium seeberi. The disease is more prevalent in India and Sri Lanka, but is also reported from USA.

Clinical disease: Rhinosporidiosis is a chronic, granulomatous infection of the mucous membranes of the face, mouth or eye, with the development of friable polyps.

Laboratory Diagnosis

Specimen: Tissue

Technique

  • (i) Direct microscopy: Histological examination of the tissue section stained by haematoxylin and eosin (H&E) method (refer to Histopathology section) reveals a lesion composed of a large number of spherules, embedded in a stroma of connective tissue. The spherules vary in size from 10Œºm to 200-300 Œºm. When mature, the spherule contains a large number of endospores, each about the size of a red blood cell (7Œºm). The endospores are released from the spherule through a pore in the wall.
  • (ii) Culture: Rhinosporidium seeberi does not grow on inanimate laboratory media. It has been cultured in mammalian epithelial cell tissue culture.
