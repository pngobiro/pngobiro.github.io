üß¥ Surface Infections

Fungi that cause surface infections live exclusively on the dead layers of the skin and its appendages. They have no contact with the living tissue and therefore, induce no immune response. The infections produced are asymptomatic. They produce aesthetically displeasing lesions showing hypopigmentation or hyperpigmentation of the skin; or nodules on the hair shaft away from the skin.

1. Tinea Versicolor or Pityriasis Versicolor

  • Causative agent: Malassezia furfur
  • Clinical disease: Tinea versicolor is a mild to chronic infection of the outer dead layer of the skin, the stratum corneum. It is usually asymptomatic. The lesions are discrete or confluent scaly areas which appear discoloured or depigmented. They occur on the smooth surfaces of the body such as the chest, abdomen, back and upper limbs.

Laboratory diagnosis

Specimen: Skin scrapings from the lesion.

Techniques:

  • Direct KOH mount: The fungus appears as clusters of bottle-shaped yeast cells with short, branched hyphae.
  • Culture: This yeast does not grow on Sabouraud's agar but Sabouraud's agar covered with olive oil can be used for its isolation because of its lipophilic nature. Direct KOH mount in association with clinical symptoms establishes the diagnosis.
üí°

Note

M. furfur has been found to cause disseminated infections in infants and children who are on lipid replacement therapy. Blood and skin culture shows the typical bottle shaped yeast cells without hyphae.

2. Tinea Nigra

  • Causative agent: Exophiala werneckii (formerly, Cladosporium werneckii).
  • Clinical disease: Tinea nigra is a localised infection, particularly of the hands and soles of the feet. It produces brownish or black coloured patches.

Laboratory diagnosis

Specimen: Skin scrapings

Techniques:

  • Direct KOH mount: The KOH mount of skin scrapings shows brownish, branched budding cells and septate hyphae.
  • Culture: Exophiala werneckii is a dematiaceous fungus, appearing as black, shiny, yeast-like colonies on Sabouraud's agar, and later becoming filamentous and velvety-grey. Microscopically, it shows olive coloured budding yeast cells which later may show one or two celled conidia arising from hyphae.

3. Piedra

Causative agents: Two varieties of piedra have been recognized; Black piedra is caused by Piedraia hortae and white piedra is caused by Trichosporon beigelii.

Clinical disease: Piedra is a fungal infection of the hair, mainly of the scalp, characterised by the appearance of firm, irregular nodules along the hair shaft. The nodules are composed of fungal elements cemented together on the hair. The nodule produced by Piedraia hortae is black in colour and is more common than the white nodule produced by Trichosporon beigelii.

Laboratory diagnosis

Specimen: Nodule on the hair shaft.

Technique:

  • Direct KOH mount: Mount the nodule in 10% KOH, place a coverslip and apply slight pressure to crush the nodule. Observe microscopically. Black nodule shows oval asci approximately 20-50 ¬µm √ó 5 ¬µm in size, containing 2 to 8 aseptate ascospores of Piedraia hortae. White nodule, formed by a yeast-like fungus Trichosporon beigelii, shows hyaline hyphae 2-4 ¬µm in diameter, and arthroconidia. The hyphae are bound together by a cement-like substance.
  • Culture: Both the causative agents grow on Sabouraud's agar, though P. hortae is inhibited by cycloheximide.

üíÖ Cutaneous Infections (Dermatophytoses)

Dermatomycoses are fungal infections that involve only superficial keratinized tissues of the body: skin, hair and nails. These cutaneous mycoses are the most common fungal infections of man and are popularly called tinea or ringworm. Cutaneous infections caused by Candida albicans are also included in this group.

Causative Agents

Causative agents of dermatomycoses are collectively called dermatophytoses. About 40 species of dermatophytes are known to cause infections in man and animals. They belong to three genera:

  1. Trichophyton: Capable of invading the hair, skin and nails.
  2. Microsporum: Involves the hair and skin.
  3. Epidermophyton: Involves the skin and nails.

Clinical Disease

Dermatophytes grow only in the keratinised layers of the skin and its appendages. They utilise keratin as a source of nitrogen and are usually incapable of penetrating into the deeper, living tissue.

The lesions caused by dermatophytes are referred to as tinea. In gross appearance, the lesion shows an outer ring of an active, progressive infection with a central healing region. The body sites affected include scalp and hair (tinea capitis), beard (tinea barbae), body (tinea corporis), foot (tinea pedis) and nail (tinea unguinum).

The ringworm infections have a world-wide distribution. The infections can be acquired from:

  • Man to man (anthropophilic): e.g. Trichophyton rubrum, Microsporum audouinii or Epidermophyton floccosum.
  • Animal to man (zoophilic): e.g. Microsporum canis, Trichophyton verrucosum.
  • Soil to man (geophilic): e.g. Microsporum gypseum, Trichophyton ajelloi.

üî¨ Laboratory Diagnosis of Dermatophytoses

Laboratory diagnosis of dermatophytoses is made by microscopic and cultural examination of the specimen such as skin, nail or hair. The identification of the isolate depends on the colonial morphology and microscopic appearance of the isolated fungus.

Specimens: Skin scrapings, nail scrapings or fragments, hair.

Techniques

Direct KOH mount

In the KOH mount of a properly digested skin scraping or nail fragments, the fungus appears as hyaline septate hyphae of uniform width (3-15 Œºm) which may be associated with chains of arthroconidia.

The KOH mount of infected hairs may show two types of infections. The infection may be ectothrix i.e. the arthroconidia appear on the periphery of the hair shaft, or endothrix, when the arthroconidia are seen within the hair shaft. Trichophyton schoenleinii can cause endothrix infection but does not break up into arthroconidia. This type of endothrix infection is called favus.

ü¶† Common Dermatophyte Genera

Genus Trichophyton

Microscopically, the genus Trichophyton is characterised by smooth, club-shaped macroconidia borne singly at the terminal ends of hyphae or on short conidiophores. The macroconidia are thin walled with 8-10 septa, range in size from 4 √ó 8 to 8 √ó 15 Œºm and are usually few in number. The microconidia predominate. They are 2-4 Œºm in size and may be spherical, clavate (club shaped) or pyriform (teardrop shaped).

Trichophyton rubrum is a slow growing fungus and produces white to reddish colony with a cottony or velvety surface. The characteristic cherry-red colour, which gives the fungus its name 'rubrum' (Latin word for red), appears after 2-3 weeks on the reverse side of the colony.
Microscopically, macroconidia are not very commonly seen. When present, they are pencil shaped, thin walled with 3-8 septa. Microconidia are generally abundant, borne laterally on hyphae and appear as teardrops. T. rubrum is a very common cause of skin, nail and hair infections.

T. mentagrophytes appears in two distinct varieties: granular or fluffy. The granular colonies sporulate more freely with numerous small, spherical microconidia in grape-like clusters. Cigar shaped macroconidia may be present. Spiral hyphae are seen in some isolates.
T. mentagrophytes perforates hair and produces urease within 2‚Äì3 days of incubation. These features are used to differentiate it from T. rubrum, because T. mentagrophytes also is frequently isolated from skin, hair and nail infections.

T. schoenleinii causes a severe type of infection of the scalp called favus. It produces cup-shaped crusts with considerable scarring of the scalp, sometimes leading to alopecia (loss of hair). Microscopically, the hair infection is typical. It shows arthroconidia at the base of the hair follicle and branching hyphae (favus) throughout the length of the hair shaft.
Longitudinal tunnels or empty spaces in the hair shaft indicate disintegrated hyphae. Short lateral and terminal branches, which become knobbly and club-shaped at the terminal ends, are described as antler hyphae or favic chandeliers and are characteristic. Chlamydospores are generally numerous.

T. violaceum causes infection of the scalp and the body. The hair infection is endothrix showing chains of arthroconidia in the hair shaft. In the laboratory it grows very slowly. Macroconidia and microconidia are rarely seen. Distorted hyphae and chlamydospores are a common feature.

T. tonsurans is the commonest cause of tinea capitis affecting the scalp and hair. It produces an endothrix infection. The microconidia vary greatly in shape and size. 'Balloon form' microconidia and chlamydospores are seen in old cultures.

T. verrucosum is a zoophilic fungus. The infections are acquired by man from cattle. It mainly affects the beard, neck, wrist and back of hands. Chlamydospores in chains and branching hyphae may be the only characteristic structures observed microscopically.

Genus Microsporum

The presence of large (10‚Äì30 Œºm √ó 40‚Äì120 Œºm), spindle-shaped, thick, rough-walled (upto 4 Œºm) macroconidia, containing 4 to 15 septa, is characteristic of most species of the genus Microsporum. Microconidia are generally scanty. When present they are small (3‚Äì7 Œºm), club-shaped (clavate) and are borne laterally, either directly or with short conidiophores, on the hyphae.

M. canis is a zoophilic fungus. Humans acquire infections through contact with infected animals, particularly cats and dogs. Infections of the body (tinea corporis) and scalp (tinea capitis) are most common. The hair infection is of the ectothrix type. M. canis grows rapidly on laboratory media. Abundant, large (15 Œºm √ó 60‚Äì120 Œºm), thick walled, spindle shaped, multi-celled (4‚Äì8) macroconidia with curved ends are characteristic of the species. Microconidia are usually few in number.

M. audouinii is one of the most common causes of tinea capitis especially among school children. It is an anthropophilic fungus. Transmission of the disease is from man to man, and through fomites such as caps, combs and upholstery etc. The hair infection is of the ectothrix type. M. audouinii grows more slowly than other species of Microsporum and does not usually sporulate in culture. Microscopically, chlamydospores, and thickened, distorted (pectinate) hyphae are seen. Characteristic macroconidia of the genus are generally absent.

M. gypseum is a geophilic fungus, free-living in soil. It occasionally causes human infections. The hair infection is of the ectothrix type, the arthroconidia being much larger in size than others. M. gypseum grows rapidly on laboratory media. Abundant, large, multi-celled (4‚Äì9) macroconidia are seen. They differ from those of M. canis in shape. Although spindle shaped, macroconidia of M. gypseum are more rounded at the distal end than those of M. canis.

Genus Epidermophyton

This genus includes only one species, E. floccosum. It can cause infections of the skin and nails, and is a common cause of tinea cruris (groins) and tinea pedis. It is seen as fine branching hyphae in KOH mounts of skin or nail scrapings.

E. floccosum grows slowly on laboratory media. Microscopically, a large number of macroconidia, borne singly or in clusters of two or three on a conidiophore is seen. They are thin walled, club-shaped and may contain one to five cells. Microconidia are always absent. Chlamydospores are usually numerous.
