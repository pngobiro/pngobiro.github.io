üçÑ Identification of Filamentous Fungi (Moulds)

Filamentous fungi isolated on laboratory media from the clinical specimens are identified on the basis of:

1. The Growth Rate

The time required for a fungus to produce visible growth on a culture medium can be described as its growth rate. Observation of the growth rate of a culture is an important information for its identification, especially when used in combination with other features.

For example, colonies of Zygomycetes such as Mucor or Rhizopus appear within 24 hours and those of other hyaline and dematiaceous saprobes appear in 1-5 days. On the other hand, the growth rate of dimorphic fungi is generally slow, and requires 1-4 weeks for the appearance of visible colonies. Growth rate of some fungi is variable, and may depend upon the size of the inoculum, type of media and temperature of incubation.

2. Colonial Morphology

Visual examination of a fungal colony gives important data regarding the physical appearance such as:

  • (i) Colour: The colour may be white, grey, cream, buff, pale orange, brown, deep purple or green.
  • (ii) Texture: The texture may be powdery, cottony, velvety, silky or waxy.
  • (iii) Colonial topography: The fungi can form flat, heaped, grooved, folded or wrinkled colonies.
  • (iv) Difussible pigments: The pigments produced by fungi can be seen on the reverse side of the dish as buff, pale orange, deep yellow, grey, brown or deep red colours.

The observation of these features, supplemented with the growth rate and microscopic appearance, help in the identification of fungi. Although it may be possible to recognise certain species recovered repeatedly in the laboratory based on their colonial morphology, it is not a very reliable criterion for identification. The colonial morphology varies greatly with the type of media used and the temperature of incubation, especially in the case of dimorphic fungi. For example, Histoplasma capsulatum appears as a white to tan mould on brain heart infusion agar (BHIA), but may appear yeast-like when grown on the same medium enriched with blood.

3. Microscopic Morphology

The microscopic morphological features of most fungi are stable and exhibit minimal variation. The definitive identification is based on the morphology of the spores and hyphae.

(i) Spores

The morphology of spores depends on:

  • (a) Type of spores: e.g. sporangiospores, chlamydospores, microconidia, macroconidia, etc. (Fig. 1.1).
  • (b) Characteristic shape: e.g. spindle-shaped macroconidia of Microsporum species
  • (c) Arrangement of spores on the hyphae: The spores may appear singly, in chains, clusters, terminal, flower-like, etc.

(ii) Hyphae

The important features of hyphae which help in the identification are:

  • (a) Size or diameter of hyphae: Diameter of 1-2 ¬µm may suggest dimorphic fungi or actinomycetes. Dermatophytes have hyphae ranging from 3-15 ¬µm Rhizopus or Mucor hyphae may be large, ribbon-like, about 25 ¬µm in diameter.
  • (b) Shape of hyphae: The hyphae may show characteristic shapes such as spiral, pectinate, or favic chandeliers (Fig. 5.2).

Dimorphic Fungi

Dimorphic fungi have the ability to grow at 25¬∞C on routine mycological culture media as moulds. When these fungi are in tissue or on special media incubated at 37¬∞C, they grow either as yeasts or as spherules containing endospores. This ability to grow in two different vegetative forms helps in distinguishing dimorphic fungi from other species. Special techniques, such as exoantigen tests, are also available for confirmation of the identity of a dimorphic fungus.

Additional Identification Tests

  1. (i) Hair penetration test: Some fungal isolates have the ability to penetrate hair in vitro. This test is useful in distinguishing atypical isolates of dermatophytes, especially Trichophyton rubrum from Trichophyton mentagrophytes.
  2. (ii) Nutritional studies: These tests are used to determine the ability of a fungus to utilize a specific nutrient.
  3. (iii) Absence of proteolytic activity: This test determines the pathogenicity of certain fungi. It is done by growing the isolates on media containing gelatine or serum.
  4. (iv) Rice grain test: Inability of some dermatophytes such as Microsporum audouinii to grow on rice grains is used to differentiate them from other species of the genus, especially Microsporum canis.
  5. (v) Temperature studies: Ability of some moulds to grow at higher temperatures is used to differentiate them from other closely related species.
  6. (vi) Urea hydrolysis: This test is used to differentiate between Trichophyton mentagrophytes which is urease positive and T. rubrum which is urease negative.

Note: These tests are used when the growth rate, colonial morphology and microscopic morphology does not lead to a confirmed identification of a fungus.

üìã Identification Procedures

I. Microscopic Examination

1. Wet mount or tease mount

This is the most common technique for microscopic examination of fungi.

Reagents

(i) Lactophenol cotton blue

  • Phenol, concentrated 20.0 ml
  • Lactic acid 20.0 ml
  • Glycerol 40.0 ml
  • Cotton blue 0.05 g
  • Distilled water 20.0 ml.

Dissolve cotton blue in distilled water and add phenol, lactic acid and glycerol.
(ii) 95% ethanol

Technique

  • Place a drop of 95% ethanol on a microscope slide.
  • Using a sterile inoculating needle or fine forceps, gently remove a small portion of fungal growth from midway between the colony centre and the edge. Take care that both the aerial and substrate (vegetative) mycelium is included in the specimen.
  • With two dissecting needles, tease the fungus gently so that it is thinly spread out in the mounting medium.
  • When most of the ethanol has evaporated, add a drop of lactophenol cotton blue and cover with a cover glass.
  • Examine the fungus microscopically.

(ii) In a tease mount, conidia or spores are detached from their position on the fungus. Therefore, identification is more difficult with a tease mount than with a slide culture technique or tape technique.

(ii) Ethanol acts as a wetting agent which prevents the formation of air bubbles around the structure to be studied.

2. Tape technique

This procedure permits the observation of conidia or spores still attached, as well as other structures in their natural position.

  • Place one drop of lactophenol cotton blue on a clean slide.
  • Attach one end of small piece of transparent tape to a round wooden applicator stick and then lightly touch the colony with the sticky surface of the tape.
  • Loosen the tape and lay it with the surface containing the fungus face down into the mounting medium on the slide.
  • Detach the tape from the wooden applicator stick.
  • Examine the mount directly with a microscope.

3. Slide culture technique

Slide culture technique is used for the identification of moulds which are difficult to identify with tease mounts. Media such as cornmeal agar, potato dextrose agar and cereal agar are preferred for this technique because they enhance spore development. However, Sabouraud's agar can be used if these media are not available.

Step I: Slide culture set up
  • Place one piece of filter paper in a petri dish so that it just covers the bottom.
  • Place a glass rod that has been bent into a 'V' or 'A' shape on top of the filter paper.
  • Place a clean glass slide and a cover glass on the bent rod.
  • Autoclave this set up at 15 psi for 15 minutes.
üí°

Note

The same set up can be prepared by assembling the sterile components (filter paper, glass rod, slide, coverslip and petri dish) aseptically.

Step II: Preparation of the slide culture
  • From a plate of the appropriate medium, cut the agar with a sterile scalpel into squares which are 1 cm on each side.
  • Aseptically transfer one agar block using a sterile scalpel to the microscope slide in the slide culture set-up.
  • With an inoculating wire, transfer a small amount of the fungus from the edge of the colony to each of the four sides of the agar block.
  • Aseptically place the coverglass on top of the agar block.
  • Carefully add 1-2 ml of sterile distilled water to the bottom of the slide culture onto the filter paper. This is necessary to maintain a humid atmosphere in the petri dish, in order to prevent the agar block from drying.
  • Incubate for about two weeks in the dark at room temperature.
Step III: Examination of the slide culture
  • Remove the slide from the set-up.
  • Place a small drop of lactophenol cotton blue on another slide.
  • With a pair of forceps, carefully remove the cover glass from the agar block and quickly pass it through a flame once or twice to heat-fix the fungus and the conidia in place.
  • Place the coverslip on top of the lactophenol cotton blue, the fungus facing downwards.
  • Examine microscopically.
  • If there are too many air bubbles, warm the preparation gently over a flame.
üí°

Note

It is recommended that the slide culture should be prepared in duplicate so that in case of insufficient sporulation in the first mount, the second one can be examined after further incubation.

1. In Vitro Hair Penetration Test

The ability of some dermatophytes to infect hair is used as an identification test. The hair infection can be external (ectothrix) or internal (endothrix). When hyphae are seen without spores the infection is known as favus (Fig. 3.1).

Fig. 3.1 Ectothrix, endothrix and favus hair infection

Technique

  • Place several fragments of sterile hairs in a sterile petri dish. Blond hair shows the results more clearly.
  • Add approximately 25 ml of sterile distilled water and 1 ml of 1% sterile yeast extract, to the petri dish.
  • With a sterile needle, transfer a small amount of fungal growth to the water in the petri dish.
  • Inoculate known isolates of Trichophyton mentagrophytes and T. rubrum as positive and negative control respectively at the same time in the same way in separate petri dishes.
  • Incubate at 25¬∞C and examine weekly for up to four weeks by placing a few fragments of hairs on a microscope slide in a drop of lactophenol cotton blue.
  • T. mentagrophytes usually gives a positive result within two weeks. However, the test should not be read as negative until four weeks.

2. In Vitro Conversion of Dimorphic Moulds

Dimorphic moulds, except Coccidioides immitis, grow as yeast in tissue and on special media at 37¬∞C. Total conversion from mould to yeast form usually requires rapid, continuous subculturing. However, appearance of a few yeast forms in the first subculture is sufficient for identification.

Coccidioides immitis produces spherules and endospores at 37¬∞C when grown on special media in 5-10% carbon dioxide atmosphere.

  • Incubate one tube at 25¬∞C and the other tube at 37¬∞C. If Coccidioides immitis is suspected, incubate at 40¬∞C.
  • If Sporothrix schenckii or Coccidioides immitis is suspected, incubate the second tube in 5-10% CO‚ÇÇ, either using a candle jar or Gas Pak for CO‚ÇÇ (refer to Bacteriology).
  • Incubate for 3-4 weeks, examining after every 2-3 days. Examine the yeast-like creamy colonies microscopically for the presence of typical yeast cells.

B. Technique for yeast mould conversion

Dimorphic fungi in yeast form will form typical mould colonies when the yeast or spherule form is subcultured on Sabouraud‚Äôs agar and incubated at 25¬∞C.

3. Exoantigen Test for Identification of Dimorphic Moulds

Exoantigen test is useful in identification of dimorphic fungi when their differentiation on the basis of colonial and microscopic morphology is difficult.

The test can also be used for some other fungi such as Aspergillus species.

These fungi produce water soluble specific antigens which can be reacted with their homologous antibodies in a double diffusion agar test system.

A commercial test kit is available for the exoantigen test. Detailed instructions for the test are available with the kit.

üß´ Identification of Yeasts

Yeasts are unicellular fungi which occur as spherical or ellipsoidal cells. On culture, they form smooth, creamy colonies resembling those of bacteria. Based on their ability or inability to reproduce by sexual means, yeasts have been traditionally divided into two groups. The true yeasts are those fungi that reproduce sexually, developing ascospores or basidiospores, when the appropriate conditions are met. In contrast to the true yeast, yeast-like fungi or the imperfect yeasts, reproduce only by asexual means, that is, by budding. Yeast-like fungi grow partly as yeasts, and partly as elongated cells resembling hyphae, called psudohyphae. An example of true yeast is Cryptococcus neoformans and that of yeast-like fungus is Candida albicans.

Identification of yeast is based on the following criteria:

1. Cultural Characteristics

Yeasts pathogenic to man appear as pasty, opaque and usually white, off-white or beige coloured colonies resembling bacterial colonies. They have a sweet smell resembling that of a ripe apple. Depending upon the medium used, it is occasionally possible to distinguish between species based on their colonial morphology. However, apart from differentiating them from filamentous moulds, colonial morphology is not very useful in identification.

2. Microscopic Morphology

Microscopic morphology of yeast cells can be studied in various ways depending on the type of microscopic feature to be studied.

(i) Temporary direct mount (wet preparation)

Direct mounts are used to study the size and shape of yeast cells, and to observe the presence and absence of other structures such as psuedohyphae or chlamydospores. Temporary direct mount is prepared in distilled water because yeasts tend to settle much faster in water than in mounting media such as lactophenol cotton blue (LPCB).

Technique

  • Mix a small portion of the yeast colony with a small drop of distilled water on a clean slide.
  • Place a clean cover glass over it and observe immediately under the microscope.
  • Note morphological characteristics of the yeast cells.
üí°

Note

A temporary direct mount may also be prepared by using LPCB. Allow the cells to settle for five minutes before examining them microscopically.

(ii) Permanent direct mount

  • Prepare a smear from the fungal culture.
  • Heat fix the smear by gently passing through a bunsen flame 2-3 times.
  • Place a small drop of LPCB on the fixed smear and cover with a cover glass.
  • Seal the edges of the cover glass either with DPX or with finger nail polish.
  • Examine microscopically.
  • Figure 3.2 shows budding yeast cells.

(iii) Germ tube test

A 'germ tube' is a filamentous extension from a yeast cell, about half the width and three to four times the length of the cell (Fig. 3.3). About 90% of yeasts isolated from clinical material are Candida albicans; and the germ tube test provides a simple and reliable procedure for its tentative identification.

Technique

  • Suspend a small portion of an 18-72 hours old isolated colony of the yeast in 0.5 ml of rabbit, horse or human serum in a test tube.
  • Repeat the same procedure with a known culture of Candida albicans (Positive control) and Candida tropicalis (Negative control).
  • Incubate all the tubes at 37¬∞C for 2-3 hours.
  • Place a drop of the yeast suspension on a glass slide and cover with a cover glass. Read the controls first and then the 'test' for the presence or absence of germ tubes. Only C. albicans produces germtubes within three hours.
üí°

Note

If no germ tubes are observed within three hours, other methods such as Dalamau plate technique and biochemical tests should be performed for further identification.

(iv) Dalamau plate technique

The microscopic morphology of various yeasts on cornmeal agar containing 1% Tween 80 can help to a great extent in their identification. Different genera and species of yeasts produce hyphae, pseudohyphae, chlamydospores, arthroconidia and blastoconidia, while some retain the original yeast form, when grown on this medium at room temperature for 1-2 days. This method of identification is known as Dalamau plate technique.

Begin the examination by scanning the area under the cover glass. If no filamentous structures are observed on the surface, carefully focus on the submerged growth that tracks along the incisions in the agar. Further examination of the hyphae should be made to distinguish between the true hyphae and pseudohyphae. A true hypha or true mycelium has cells of constant width with square ends whereas a pseudomycelium is composed of cells of varying width with rounded ends (Fig. 3.4).

When a true mycelium is observed, individual hyphae should be checked for the formation of arthroconidia and blastoconidia. If present, it identifies the fungus as Trichosporon species.

üß™ Biochemical Tests

There are various biochemical tests used for the identification of yeasts up to the species level. (Depending on their morphological and cultural characteristics, only those tests which are critical for identification and confirmatory in nature should be performed). The commonly used tests are described below.

(i) Assimilation tests

Assimilation is the utilization of a carbon source in the presence of oxygen. For example, assimilation (aerobic oxidation) of a sugar such as glucose results in the formation of carbon dioxide and water.

$$ \mathrm{C_6H_{12}O_6} + 6\mathrm{O_2} \rightarrow 6\mathrm{CO_2} + 6\mathrm{H_2O} $$

A positive assimilation reaction is usually indicated by the presence of growth. Several methods are available for performing the test. The method using carbohydrate impregnated disks is convenient and commonly used in clinical laboratories. Characteristic carbohydrate utilization profiles are used to distinguish one species from another, but not usually for differentiating genera.

Requirements

  1. A carbohydrate-free basal medium such as Yeast Nitrogen Base (YNB) agar.
  2. Carbohydrate impregnated filter paper discs: Soak sterile filter-paper disks, 10 mm in diameter, in 1% carbohydrate solution and dry before use.

Technique

Prepare a suspension of the yeast to be identified in saline or distilled water to a density equivalent to McFarland Standard No. 4.

  • Cover the surface of the YNB agar with the yeast cell suspension. Aspirate the excess of suspension using a sterile pasteur pipette and allow the agar surface to dry for 5 minutes.
  • Using sterile forceps, place the discs of selected carbohydrates onto the surface of the agar, about 30 mm apart from each other.
  • Incubate the plate at 30¬∞C for 24-48 hours.
  • Observe for the presence of growth around each disc which indicates that the particular carbohydrate has been assimilated by the yeast.
  • Interpret the results to identify the yeast as shown in Table 3.2.

Alcoholic fermentation is the process by which a carbohydrate is fermented anaerobically by yeasts to produce ethanol and carbon dioxide. This fermentation is indicated by the production of gas.

(iii) Urea hydrolysis

A number of yeasts produce the enzyme urease which hydrolyses urea to form ammonia and carbon dioxide. This test is useful for distinguishing between the medically important yeasts of genera Cryptococcus and Rhodotorula (both urease positive) from those of genus Torulopsis, (urease negative). The test can also be used to identify species within the genera Candida and Trichosporon. (Table 3.2).

Requirements

Christensen's urea agar (refer to Bacteriology section).

Technique

  • Inoculate the yeast on the Christensen's urea agar slant.
  • Incubate the slant at 30¬∞C for five days.
  • Pink or red colour of the agar indicates a positive test.

Commercial Yeast Identification Kits

Several packaged kits have been introduced by commercial companies. Most of the methods are rapid and results are available within 72 hours. These systems provide an identification based on a very broad database of several yeast biotypes. Examples of such systems are API-20C yeast system, API yeast-Ident system, Uni-yeast Tek system etc.
