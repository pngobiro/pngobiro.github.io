Opportunistic Mycoses

üìö Infections in Immunocompromised Hosts

01

üè• Overview

A group of saprophytic fungi, which are ubiquitous in nature and are common laboratory contaminants, can cause infections almost exclusively in immunocompromised patients. These opportunistic fungi can cause disease in patients who are compromised due to some underlying disease process, such as diabetes mellitus, leukaemia, lymphoma, or an impaired immune system or immunosuppressive therapy. Although harmless to normal healthy individuals, opportunistic fungi can produce serious and even fatal infections in debilitated persons.

Out of a large number of fungi which can cause opportunistic infections, only those infections which are frequently encountered will be discussed. They are:

  1. Aspergillosis
  2. Zygomycosis
  3. Cryptococcosis
  4. Candidiasis (Candidosis, Moniliasis)
02

üçÇ Aspergillosis

Causative agents: Aspergillosis can be caused by several species of Aspergillus. The commonest are Aspergillus niger, A. fumigatus and A. flavus.

Clinical disease: Aspergillus species are widespread in the environment, living mainly in soil and living plants. The spores are easily dispersed in the air and the majority of infections are acquired by inhalation of airborne spores.

Aspergillosis includes various disease conditions such as toxicosis (due to aflatoxin produced by A. flavus); allergic reactions; localized infections of the skin, external ear (otomycosis), nasal sinuses and the orbit of the eye (keratitis); pulmonary infection; and disseminated multi-organ disease. Pulmonary aspergillosis may occur in the form of an allergy, aspergilloma or invasive tissue disease.

Aspergilloma is the colonization by the fungus of the pre-existing cavities in the lung due to previous infections such as tuberculosis. In invasive aspergillosis, the mycelia extend into the tissue and subsequently may disseminate to other parts of the body through the blood stream.

Laboratory diagnosis

Specimens

The specimens suitable for culture include:

  1. (i) Sputum
  2. (ii) Bronchial aspirate
  3. (iii) Swabs or tissue biopsies from localized infections

Techniques

(i) Direct microscopy

KOH Mount: On direct microscopic examination, species of Aspergillus shows dichotomous branching (at 45¬∞) of hyaline, septate hyphae 3‚Äì4 Œºm in width (Fig. 5.10). The walls are parallel and refractile. However, on the basis of morphology, it is not possible to distinguish them from other septate fungi.

Tissue sections: These are stained with standard histological techniques such as H and E staining. The section shows poorly stained septate hyphae in a necrotic, pyogenic lesion.

(ii) Culture

Inoculate the specimen on Sabouraud's agar without cycloheximide. Aspergillus is sensitive to cycloheximide. A medium enriched with blood may also be inoculated.

Aspergillus grows as a mould both at 30¬∞C and 37¬∞C rapidly within two to four days. Identification is based on both gross and microscopic morphology of the isolate, and pigment production. All species of Aspergillus produce erect unbranched conidiophores with swollen apices or vesicles. Depending on the species, a portion or the entire surface of the vesicle is covered by flask shaped phialides or sterigmata. Conidia are formed in chains from the tips of the phialides (Fig. 5.11a). The hyphae are septate.

Recently, about 200 species of the genus Aspergillus have been divided into 18 groups. A definitive identification to the species level should be requested from a reference laboratory. The most common groups are described below.

Aspergillus Fumigatus Group

The growth appears as a small white colony, becoming green or slate-grey and powdery with age. Microscopically, the phialides cover only upper half of the surface of the dome-shaped vesicle. Conidia are usually round and slightly rough, 2‚Äì3 Œºm in diameter. A. fumigatus is among a few species which are capable of growing at 45¬∞C.

Aspergillus Flavus Group

A. flavus grows within one to five days, producing a yellow-green colony with a rough granular texture. Conidiophores are rough-walled, ending in a rounded vesicle. The phialides cover the entire surface of the vesicle and give rise to short chains of conidia.

Aspergillus Niger Group

The growth of A. niger appears within two to six days, beginning as a white filamentous growth which soon develops a black, dotted surface as conidia are produced. Microscopically, the long conidiophores terminate in spherical vesicles. The phialides cover the entire surface of the vesicle. The conidia are thick-walled, very rough and darkly pigmented showing carbon-black conidia.

Note

Occasionally, non-sporulating isolates may be obtained from pulmonary lesions.

(iii) Exoantigen test: It is useful for identifying non-sporulating isolates of Aspergillus.

üï∏Ô∏è Zygomycosis

Causative agents: Zygomycosis is an opportunistic fungal infection caused by several genera within the class Zygomycetes. These moulds are characterized by broad, ribbon-like, non-septate hyphae. The spores are produced within a sac-like fruiting structure, a sporangium, which is formed at the tip of a supporting structure, a sporangiophore. Columnella is the protrusion of the sporangiophore into the sporangium. The spores are produced internally within the sporangium and are called sporangiospores. After maturation, the sporangium ruptures to release the spores into the environment. The sporangiophores are connected to each other by hyphae called stolons. The stolons, usually, remain in contact with the substrate by root-like structures, rhizoids. The three most common zygomycetes isolated in a clinical laboratory are species of Rhizopus, Mucor and Abisidia.

Clinical disease: Like many other opportunistic pathogens, the zygomycetes are abundant in the environment, and are transient residents of the human body. Throughout the world, the zygomycetes are known to cause infections in patients with predisposing conditions such as diabetes mellitus, malignancies and immunodeficiencies. The disease is acute and rapidly fatal, most often when associated with Rhizopus species. The most common form of the disease is the rhinocerebral form where the nasal mucosa, palate, sinuses, face, orbit and brain are involved. The other common forms are pulmonary and intestinal; and may disseminate to any organ of the body. Zygomycetes have a remarkable capacity to invade blood vessels. Small arteries can be filled with hyphae, which penetrate into the adjacent tissues and cut off the blood supply to the organ, leading to thrombosis and necrosis.

Cutaneous and subcutaneous zygomycosis is occasionally seen in burn cases and in post-operative wounds.

Laboratory diagnosis

Specimens

  1. (i) Scrapings of the mucosal linings or necrotic discharge from the nose, sinuses, facial lesions or orbit of the eye.
  2. (ii) Sputum, bronchial aspiration.
  3. (iii) Biopsies of necrotic tissues from the edge of the lesions.

Techniques

(i) Direct microscopy

KOH mount: The KOH mount of the specimen shows aseptate hyphae. The branching is not common. If present it is usually at 90¬∞.

Urgent Action Required

Report a positive finding as quickly as possible because zygomycosis can be rapidly fatal.

Tissue biopsies should be stained with standard histopathological techniques, preferably using special stains such as periodic acid Schiff (PAS) or Gomori's methanamine silver (GMS) stains.

(ii) Culture

Inoculate the specimens on Sabouraud's agar without cycloheximide and chloramphenicol because zygomycetes are sensitive to them. Incubate the cultures at 30¬∞C for two to four days, examining daily.

Zygomycete colonies are visible within 48‚Äì72 hours, and show characteristic morphological features. They produce abundant aerial mycelium with fluffy, white, grey or brown hyphal growth that rapidly covers the entire agar surface. It is not possible to distinguish between the genera on the basis of colony morphology. Microscopically, identification to the genus level is adequate. Microscopic features of the three important genera are described as follows.

Mucor species

The sporangiophores are often branched. The sporangia are spherical. The distinctive feature of Mucor species is that the rhizoids are absent.

Rhizopus species

The sporangiophores are long and unbranched. The rhizoids and the sporangiophores arise at the same point where the stolons touch the substrate. The sporangia are spherical.

Abisidia Species

The rhizoids originate between the two sporangiophores. The sporangia are pear-shaped. The sporangiophores are branched and occur in groups of three to five.

üê¶ Cryptococcosis

Causative agents: Cryptococcosis is almost always caused by Cryptococcus neoformans. However, other species of the genus Cryptococcus are also occasionally associated with the disease.

Members of the genus Cryptococcus are true yeasts, which are unicellular fungi that reproduce asexually by budding. Cryptococcus neoformans exists as a saprophyte in nature. It is most often found associated with pigeon faeces. A distinctive feature of C. neoformans is that it produces a prominent polysaccharide capsule.

Clinical disease: Cryptococcosis is acquired by inhalation of yeast through aerosols from the soil contaminated with bird excreta. The primary focus of infection is the lungs and it is often asymptomatic or may lead to mild pneumonia. As there is no calcification, healed pulmonary lesions may not be seen radiologically. Extra pulmonary disease is frequently observed in immunocompromised patients, particularly those with AIDS, Hodgkin's disease or leukaemia. Disseminated cryptococcosis most commonly involves the central nervous system causing meningitis, but can also cause endocarditis, hepatitis, renal infection and pleural effusion.

Antigenically, the polysaccharide capsule of C. neoformans can be differentiated into four serotypes, A, B, C, and D; A and D being common in temperate climate, while B and C prevail in tropical areas.

Laboratory diagnosis

Specimens

  1. (i) Sputum or bronchial aspirate
  2. (ii) CSF
  3. (iii) Blood
  4. (iv) Urine
  5. (v) Tissue biopsies

Techniques

(i) Direct Microscopy

KOH Mount: A KOH mount of the specimen shows spherical, thick walled yeast cells, with single or multiple budding, varying in size from 2 to 15 Œºm.

India Ink Preparation: Negative staining with India ink shows a wide, refractile, polysaccharide capsule (Fig. 2.2). However, some strains of C. neoformans may not exhibit a capsule.

(ii) Culture

Inoculate the specimen on Sabouraud's agar without cycloheximide and on blood agar. Incubate at 30¬∞C for one week. It is recommended that for specimens such as CSF or urine, a 0.45 Œºm pore size membrane filter or centrifugation should be used for concentration of the specimen.

Colonies of C. neoformans appear within one to five days as smooth white to tan colonies which may become mucoid. The polysaccharide capsule may not be produced on culture media.

Microscopic examination of the colonies may provide a tentative identification of C. neoformans appearing as spherical yeast cells showing a wide variation in size. For final identification, biochemical tests may be performed as shown in Table 3.2.

ü•õ Candidiasis (Candidosis, Moniliasis)

Causative agents: Candidiasis is caused by yeast-like fungi belonging to the genus Candida. C. albicans is the most common causative agent followed by C. tropicalis and Torulopsis glabrata. Candida species form part of the normal flora of the skin, mucous membranes and intestinal tract.

Clinical disease: Candidiasis is an opportunistic endogenous infection. Depending on the immunocompetence of the host, candidiasis can range from trivial or mild infection of skin or mucosa to a very severe systemic disease (Table 5.5). The extent of system involvement appears to be directly related to the severity of the underlying disorder such as diabetes mellitus, leukaemia, lymphoma or T-cell deficiencies.

Cutaneous candidiasis may involve the skin folds between the fingers (intertriginous) or surrounding the nails (paronychial), groin, perineum, axillae and inframammary folds where the skin is macerated by perspiration. They appear as scaly, moist lesions with sharply demarcated borders.

Mucosal candidiasis most commonly observed is in the valvovaginal area characterised by a whitish discharge, especially during pregnancy; and oral thrush, in neonates, the elderly and debilitated patients. Oral thrush appears as creamy white patches on the oral mucosa and the tongue.

Systemic candidiasis may involve intestines presenting as a diarrhoea not responding to treatment; pulmonary infection, endocarditis, meningitis, fungaemia and disseminated infection.

üí°

Note

Cutaneous and mucosal infections may occur in a normal host whereas systemic infections are most commonly seen in immunocompromised patients.

Laboratory diagnosis

Specimens: The specimens include swab or scrapings from the cutaneous or mucosal lesions; sputum, CSF, blood, urine, faeces, etc. depending on the body site involved.

Techniques

(i) Direct Microscopy

Wet mount in saline: Candida appears as oval, budding yeast cells (2‚Äì4 Œºm in diameter) with or without pseudohyphae and hyphae (Fig. 3.2). Appearance of mycelial forms in fresh specimens indicates colonisation and tissue invasion by the yeasts.

Gram stain: The Gram stain of the smear prepared from the specimen shows Gram positive yeast cells, pseudohyphae and hyphae.

(ii) Culture

Inoculate the specimens on Sabouraud's agar and incubate at 30¬∞C for 24‚Äì48 hours.

Colonies of Candida species appear creamy white, smooth with a yeasty odour.

Candida albicans can be identified by the germ-tube test (Fig.3.3) and by the formation of chlamydospores on cornmeal agar (Fig. 3.5). The final identification of the species is made by biochemical tests as shown in Table 3.2.
