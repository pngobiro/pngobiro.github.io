üìñ Basic Definitions

Atom & Molecule

Atom: The smallest particle of an element. Contains protons (+), neutrons (neutral), and electrons (-).
Molecule: The smallest particle of an element or compound that can exist independently (e.g., NaCl).

Weights & Valency

Atomic Weight: Sum of protons and neutrons.
Molecular Weight: Sum of all atomic weights in a molecule.
Valence: Number of hydrogen atoms which will combine with or replace one atom of the substance.

‚öñÔ∏è

Equivalent Weight Calculation

The equivalent weight is the weight that combines with, liberates or replaces one atom of hydrogen.

$$ \text{Equivalent Weight} = \frac{\text{Molecular Weight}}{\text{Valence}} $$
Electrolyte
A compound which when dissolved in water conducts electric current via dissociation into ions (cations + and anions -).
Oxidation
Loss of electrons (addition of oxygen or removal of hydrogen).
Reduction
Gain of electrons (removal of oxygen or addition of hydrogen).

Hydrogen Ion Concentration and pH

pH (puissance d‚Äôhydrogen) is defined as the logarithm to the base 10 of the reciprocal of the hydrogen ion concentration.

$$ \mathrm{pH} = \log \frac{1}{[\mathrm{H}^+]} = -\log [\mathrm{H}^+] $$
üíß

pH of Water

Pure water contains $1 \times 10^{-7} \mathrm{g}$ of hydrogen ions per litre at $25^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$.

$$ \mathrm{pH} = -\log (10^{-7}) = 7 \quad (\text{Neutral}) $$

Buffer Solutions

A buffer solution resists changes in pH on the addition of small quantities of acid or alkali. They are made of a weak acid and a salt of a strong base (or vice versa).

  • Acetate buffer: Acetic acid and sodium acetate
  • Phosphate buffer: $KH_2PO_4$ and $Na_2HPO_4$
  • Barbitone buffer: Sodium diethyl barbiturate and HCl

ü•õ Solutions and Dilutions

Concentration Expressions

  • w/v (Weight per volume): g/L or mg/ml.
  • v/v (Volume per volume): ml/100ml.
  • Molar Solution (M or mol/L): Contains 1 mole (molecular weight in grams) of solute in 1 litre of solution.
  • Normal Solution (N): Contains 1 equivalent weight of solute in 1 litre.
üßÆ

Dilution Formula

To make a weaker solution from a stronger one:

$$ \text{Volume Required} = \frac{R \times V}{O} $$

Where:
R = Required concentration
V = Total Volume required
O = Original concentration

Example: Prepare 500 ml of 9% solution from 30% solution.

$$ \frac{9 \times 500}{30} = 150 \text{ ml} $$

Take 150 ml of 30% solution and make up to 500 ml with water.

Serial Dilutions

A series of dilutions (e.g., doubling dilutions 1:2, 1:4, 1:8, 1:16) often used in serology. 1 volume of specimen is transferred sequentially through tubes containing equal volumes of diluent.

üìè Standard Solutions

Primary Standard: A highly pure, stable substance used as a reference (e.g., Oxalic acid, Anhydrous Sodium Carbonate).
Secondary Standard: Solution whose concentration is determined by titration against a primary standard (e.g., NaOH, HCl).

Titration Calculation

Formula for neutralisation:

$$ N_1 V_1 = N_2 V_2 $$

If Normality obtained is different from required, calculate Dilution Factor (D.F.):

$$ D.F. = \frac{\text{Normality Required}}{\text{Normality Obtained}} $$

üìê SI Units & Conversion

The International System of Units (SI) is the standard. Key units include Mole (amount), Kilogram (mass), and Meter (length).

üîÑ

Unit Conversion Formulas

Convert mg/100ml to mmol/L:

$$ \text{mmol/L} = \frac{\text{mg/100ml} \times 10}{\text{Molecular Weight}} $$

Example (Glucose, MW=180):

$$ 90 \text{ mg/100ml} = \frac{90 \times 10}{180} = 5.0 \text{ mmol/L} $$

üìä Laboratory Statistics

Central Tendency

  • Mean ($\bar{x}$): The arithmetic average. $$ \bar{x} = \frac{\sum x}{n} $$
  • Median: The middle value when data is arranged in order.
  • Mode: The most frequently occurring value.

Measures of Dispersion

Standard Deviation (SD)

Describes the scatter of data around the mean. In a Gaussian distribution, 95% of values fall within $\pm 2SD$.

$$ SD = \sqrt{\frac{\sum (x - \bar{x})^2}{n - 1}} $$

Coefficient of Variation (CV)

Expresses SD as a percentage of the mean, useful for comparing precision.

$$ CV (\%) = \frac{SD}{\bar{x}} \times 100 $$

Analytical Errors

Random Error

Affects precision (reproducibility). Caused by chance variations in temperature, technique, etc. Detected by SD.

Systematic Error

Affects accuracy (trueness). Caused by bad standards, reagents, or calibration. Causes a shift in the mean.
