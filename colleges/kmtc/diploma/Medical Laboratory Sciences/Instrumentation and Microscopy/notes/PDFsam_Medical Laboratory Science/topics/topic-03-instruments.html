üß™ Glassware and Plasticware

Most of the routine laboratory wares used to be of glass, but recent advances made in the use of plastic resins to manufacture a wide range of plasticwares have led to a gradual replacement of glasswares with durable plasticwares. The plasticware used in the laboratory should be of high quality.

Glassware

  • Re-usable and autoclavable.
  • Heavier and more costly.
  • Easily broken.
  • Must be chemically clean for clinical chemistry and bacteriologically clean/sterile for bacteriology.

Plasticware

  • Cheaper and safer to use.
  • Often disposable, cutting down cleaning costs.
  • Encouraged for use with infectious agents (Hepatitis, HIV).

Cleaning Procedures

It is important never to allow dirty glassware to dry-out. Once dried out on the surface, it is not easy to remove the dirt.

üßº

Cleaning Methods

  • New Glassware: Often slightly alkaline; soak for several hours in dilute (approx. 1%) hydrochloric acid or nitric acid solution, then wash and rinse in distilled water.
  • Contaminated Glassware: Decontaminate before washing by presoaking in 5% hypochlorite solution or by boiling/autoclaving.
  • Plasticware: Soaked in very dilute disinfectants; some are non-autoclavable.

‚öñÔ∏è Balances and Centrifuges

Equipments for Weighing

Weighing equipments are essential for the preparation of reagents. There are three types of balances:

  1. Analytical balance: Takes a maximum load of about $200\mathrm{g}$ with a sensitivity of $1\mathrm{mg}$.
  2. Electronic balance: Correlates weight with electromagnetic force. Offers accuracy, speed, and ease of operation.
  3. Direct read-out/top-loading balance: Easy and quick to use; compensates for the weight of the weighing container.

Centrifuge

The centrifuge is used to rapidly sediment particles such as cells suspended in a fluid. The relative centrifugal force (RCF) is calculated from the rpm and the radius (r):

$$ \mathrm{RCF} \, (\mathrm{g}) = 1.12 \times 10^{-5} \times r \, (\text{in cm}) \times (\mathrm{rpm})^{2} $$

Types of Rotors:

Fixed angle rotor

Cups are held in a fixed angle. Sedimentation is more rapid, and there is less chance of sediment disturbance. Particles deposit on the sides of the tube.

Swing-out rotor

Cups occupy a vertical position at rest but swing out to horizontal during revolution. Particles sediment at the bottom of the tube.

Models of Centrifuges:

  • Hand centrifuge: Manual rotation, low speeds.
  • Battery operated: Ideal for areas without electricity.
  • Electric bench centrifuge: Most popular, various sizes/speeds.
  • Microhaematocrit centrifuge: For PCV measurement (10,000‚Äì15,000 rpm).
  • Ultracentrifuge: Refrigerated, high speed ($-15^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ to $-25^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$).
  • Cytocentrifuge: For transferring cells to a glass slide (cytospin).
‚ö†Ô∏è

Care of Centrifuge

  • Balance the centrifuge (buckets/tubes opposite each other must be same weight).
  • Leave fluid level at least 2 cms below the rim.
  • Cap tubes with potentially infectious material.
  • Do not stop by hand while rotating.
  • Use non-corrosive disinfectant for cleaning.

Incubator and Water Bath

Incubator: Required for incubation of microbiological cultures (5¬∞C to 80¬∞C).

Water Bath: Thermostatically controlled (20¬∞C to 100¬∞C). Used for incubation of liquids/tests.

üìù

Water Bath Care

Maintain level with chemically pure water (avoid tap water to prevent salt deposits). Check temp with thermometer. Ensure substance is below water surface.

Water Stills and Deionisers

Chemically pure water is required for laboratory tests. Two main methods:

Distillation

Boiling water and condensing steam. Product should be clear, colorless, odorless, tasteless. Can be pyrogen free. Test quality with Silver Nitrate (for chlorides) and Barium Chloride (for sulphates).

Deionisation

Passing tap water through anion and cation exchange resins. Cations exchanged for $H^+$, Anions for $OH^-$. Product has low electrical conductivity but is not pyrogen free.

üî¨ The Microscope

The microscope is one of the most expensive and delicate instruments used in a clinical laboratory. The common light microscope is also referred to as a bright field microscope.

Key Principles

Refractive Index ($n$)

The measure of refraction. $n = \frac{\text{Velocity in vacuum}}{\text{Velocity in medium}}$. Air = 1.0, Water = 1.3, Glass = 1.5.

Numerical Aperture (NA)

$$ NA = n \sin u $$ where $n$ is refractive index and $u$ is half the angle of the light cone entering the objective.

üìê

Resolving Power

The ability to reveal closely adjacent points as separate and distinct. The limit of useful magnification.

$$ \text{Resolving Power} (r) = \frac{0.61 \times \lambda}{\text{NA}} $$

For green light ($540\mathrm{nm}$) and NA 1.4, resolution is about $0.2\mu\mathrm{m}$.

Microscope Components

  1. Objectives:
    Objective Numerical Aperture Working Distance Code
    Low power (10X) Approx. 0.25 16mm Yellow ring
    High dry (40X) Approx. 0.65 4mm Blue ring
    Oil Immersion (100X) Approx. 1.3 2mm White ring
  2. Eyepieces (Oculars): Huygenian (common), Rhamsden, Compensating (for apochromatic objectives), Wide-field.
  3. Condenser: Focuses light on specimen. Abbe condenser is common. Includes Iris Diaphragm to regulate light cone.
üí°

Kohler Illumination

Provides uniform illumination which just fills the field of view. Essential for maximum performance.

  1. Focus on specimen with 10X.
  2. Close iris diaphragm; move condenser to focus the image of the iris.
  3. Center the circle of light.
  4. Open iris until light fills the field.

üî≠ Types of Microscopy

Type Principle/Feature Applications
Bright-field Direct light, stained specimens. Gross morphology of bacteria, tissues.
Dark-field Special condenser blocks central light; object reflects peripheral light. Treponema pallidum (Syphilis), Leptospira.
Fluorescence UV light source, fluorochromes, exciter/barrier filters. Immunofluorescence, AFB (Auramine), Trichomonas.
Phase-contrast Converts refractive index differences into amplitude (brightness) differences. Unstained living cells, urine sediments, platelets.
Electron Electron beam instead of light. High resolution (0.3-0.5 nm). Viruses, ultrastructure of cells.

Micrometry

Micrometry is the measurement of microscopic objects using a calibrated eyepiece micrometer. The unit is the micron ($\mu$m).

üî¢

Calculation Example

If the distance measured on the stage micrometer is 0.4 mm and this covers 25 divisions on the eyepiece scale:

$$ 1 \text{ division} = \frac{0.4}{25} = 0.016 \, \text{mm} $$

$$ 0.016 \times 1000 = 16 \, \mu\text{m} $$

If an object covers 2 divisions: $16 \times 2 = 32 \, \mu\text{m}$.
