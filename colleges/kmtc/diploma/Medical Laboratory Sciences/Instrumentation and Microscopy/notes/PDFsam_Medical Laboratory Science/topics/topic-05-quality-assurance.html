üéØ Overview of QA System

Quality assurance system (QAS) can be defined as all the systematic actions necessary to provide adequate confidence that laboratory services will satisfy given medical needs for patient care. It encompasses factors in three different areas:

1. Preanalytical

Factors occurring outside the laboratory: patient preparation, collection, handling and storage of specimens.

2. Analytical

Factors controlled within the laboratory by an efficient quality control system to minimize errors.

3. Postanalytical

Recording and transmitting patient data to the physician for correct interpretation.

02

üè• Preanalytical Factors

Before a sample is taken, the patient should be positively identified. The laboratory should provide a procedure manual containing information regarding each test. Patient preparation may include instructions about nutritional status, alcohol, drugs, smoking, exercise and sleep.

Specimen Collection and Handling

  • Collection: Adhere strictly to guidelines (anticoagulant type, timing). Label with name, ID, date, and time.
  • Transportation: Deliver in a timely manner. Maintain appropriate temperature.
  • Receiving: Properly log in computer or register with a reference number.
03

‚öôÔ∏è Analytical Factors

Analytical system checks ensure accurate results. This involves Internal and External Quality Control.

Internal Quality Control Checklist

  1. Maintenance of equipment: Daily/monthly schedules, temperature logs for incubators/fridges, calibration of balances/pipettes.
  2. Reagents and Standards: Use highly purified chemicals. Date new reagents. Run parallel tests with old lots.
  3. Control Material: Should resemble real specimens, be stable, and cover clinically important ranges. Never use the same material as calibrator and control.

Method Selection & Evaluation

Selection depends on managerial factors (cost, personnel) and technical factors (sensitivity, specificity, linear range).

Sensitivity

Ability to give a true positive result when disease is present.

$$ \text{Sensitivity}(\%) = \frac{TP}{(TP + FN)} \times 100 $$

TP = True Positive, FN = False Negative

Specificity

Ability to give a true negative result in patients without the disease.

$$ \text{Specificity}(\%) = \frac{TN}{(TN + FP)} \times 100 $$

TN = True Negative, FP = False Positive

Types of Analytical Errors

üìâ

Imprecision vs Inaccuracy

  • Imprecision (Random Error): Dispersion of repeated measurements about the mean. Due to chance (technique, temperature).
  • Inaccuracy (Systematic Error): Difference between measured value and true value. Due to bad standards, reagents, or instrumentation.

üìä Application of Quality Control

Control values are compared with statistical limits on a control chart (Levey-Jennings). The y-axis shows concentration ($\bar{x} \pm 3SD$) and the x-axis shows days.

üö´

Control Rules (Westgard)

  • 1-2s (Warning): One observation exceeds Mean ¬± 2SD.
  • 1-3s (Rejection): One observation exceeds Mean ¬± 3SD.
  • 2-2s (Rejection): Two consecutive observations on same side exceed ¬± 2SD.
  • R-4s (Rejection): Difference between two observations in one run exceeds 4SD.
  • 4-1s (Rejection): Four consecutive observations on same side exceed ¬± 1SD.
  • 10x (Rejection): Ten consecutive observations on same side of mean.
05

üåç External Quality Control

Also known as proficiency surveys. Laboratories receive unknown samples, analyze them, and return results for comparison with other laboratories or reference labs.

Performance is often evaluated using the Standard Deviation Index (SDI):

$$ \text{SDI} = \frac{\text{Lab Result} - \text{Group Mean}}{\text{Standard Deviation of the Group}} $$
üìè

Interpretation

  • > ¬± 2 SDI: Requires investigative action.
  • > ¬± 3 SDI: Unacceptable result.
