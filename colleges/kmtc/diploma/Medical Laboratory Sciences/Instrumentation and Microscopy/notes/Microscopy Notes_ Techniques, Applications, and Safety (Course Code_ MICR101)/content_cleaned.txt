<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="microscopy">MICROSCOPY<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#microscopy" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Microscopy is the technique of using a microscope to view objects and areas of objects that cannot be seen with the naked eye. It is essential in medical diagnostics, research, and quality control in healthcare settings.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="2-purpose-and-application-in-medical-engineering">2. Purpose and Application in Medical Engineering<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#2-purpose-and-application-in-medical-engineering" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>To examine cells, tissues, and microorganisms (e.g., in pathology labs).</li>
<li>To aid in diagnosing infections, cancers, and blood disorders.</li>
<li>To assess material surfaces in medical equipment (e.g., wear or contamination).</li>
<li>To support research in drug development and biomedical sciences</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="3-types-of-microscopes">3. Types of Microscopes<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#3-types-of-microscopes" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<table><thead><tr><th>Type</th><th>Description</th><th>Application</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>Light Microscope</td><td>Uses visible light to magnify samples.</td><td>Viewing stained tissue, bacteria, and blood smears.</td></tr><tr><td>Electron Microscope (EM)</td><td>Uses electron beams for very high magnification.</td><td>Imaging viruses, cell ultrastructure.</td></tr><tr><td>Fluorescence Microscope</td><td>Uses UV light and fluorescent dyes.</td><td>Detecting specific proteins or cells using tags.</td></tr><tr><td>Digital Microscope</td><td>Captures and displays images on a screen.</td><td>Teaching, image documentation, telepathology.</td></tr></tbody></table>

Principle of Operation<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#5-principle-of-operation" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>The microscope magnifies small objects by:</p>
<ul>
<li>Passing light or electrons through or around the specimen.</li>
<li>Using lenses (or magnetic fields in EM) to enlarge the image.</li>
<li>The image is projected to the eye or camera for observation or analysis.</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="7-maintenance-procedures">7. Maintenance Procedures<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#7-maintenance-procedures" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Clean lenses with lens paper and approved cleaning solutions.</li>
<li>Store microscope covered and in dry place to prevent dust and fungi.</li>
<li>Handle with care; avoid touching optical parts with fingers.</li>
<li>Regularly inspect and calibrate for accurate magnification and focus.</li>
<li>Lubricate mechanical parts as recommended.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="8-safety-precautions">8. Safety Precautions<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#8-safety-precautions" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Always carry the microscope with both hands (one on the arm, one under the base).</li>
<li>Never use alcohol on plastic parts (may cause damage).</li>
<li>Ensure the power is off when changing the bulb.</li>
<li>Avoid looking directly into the light source.</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="1-use-of-a-light-microscope">1. Use of a Light Microscope<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#1-use-of-a-light-microscope" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>To magnify small objects that cannot be seen clearly with the naked eye, such as cells, tissues, bacteria, and microorganisms.</li>
<li>To observe the structure and details of biological specimens in medical labs, research, and diagnostics.</li>
<li>To assist in identifying pathogens in clinical samples (e.g., blood smears, urine sediments).</li>
<li>To support quality control in medical equipment by examining surface details or contaminants.</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="2-function-of-a-light-microscope">2. Function of a Light Microscope<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#2-function-of-a-light-microscope" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>The light microscope illuminates the specimen with visible light, which passes through or reflects off the sample.</li>
<li>It uses a system of lenses (ocular and objective lenses) to magnify the image of the specimen.</li>
<li>The objective lens collects light from the specimen and creates a magnified real image.</li>
<li>The eyepiece lens further magnifies this real image to produce a virtual image that the observer sees.</li>
<li>It enables the visualization of small details such as cell walls, nuclei, bacteria shape, and arrangement.</li>
<li>It also provides contrast and resolution needed to distinguish fine structures using techniques like staining and adjusting light intensity.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>NB</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Use: To view and analyze microscopic biological and material samples.</li>
<li>Function: To magnify and clarify the image of small objects by using light and lenses.</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="principle-of-operation-of-a-light-microscope">Principle of Operation of a Light Microscope<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#principle-of-operation-of-a-light-microscope" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The light microscope works on the basic principle of magnification of small objects using visible light and a system of lenses.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="how-it-works">How it works:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#how-it-works" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ol>
<li>
<p>Illumination:
A light source (either a built-in bulb or reflected light using a mirror) illuminates the specimen placed on the stage.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Light Transmission:
The light passes through the condenser lens, which focuses the light onto the specimen to improve clarity and brightness.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Specimen Interaction:
The light passes through (or reflects off) the specimen. Depending on the specimen’s transparency, thickness, and staining, some light is absorbed or scattered.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Magnification by Objective Lens:
The objective lens, positioned just above the specimen, collects the transmitted light and creates a magnified real image of the specimen inside the microscope tube.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Further Magnification by Eyepiece Lens:
The eyepiece lens (ocular lens) magnifies this real image again to produce a larger virtual image that the observer sees.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Focusing:
The coarse and fine focus knobs adjust the distance between the lenses and specimen to make the image sharp and clear.</p>
</li>
</ol>
<p>Summary:</p>
<ul>
<li>The light microscope uses visible light to illuminate the specimen.</li>
<li>Lenses bend (refract) the light rays to magnify the image.</li>
<li>The image formation involves creating a real magnified image by the objective lens, which is then enlarged into a virtual image by the eyepiece.</li>
<li>The observer sees this enlarged virtual image, allowing examination of tiny details not visible to the naked eye.</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="principle-of-operation-of-an-electron-microscope">Principle of Operation of an Electron Microscope<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#principle-of-operation-of-an-electron-microscope" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>An electron microscope operates on the principle of using a beam of accelerated electrons instead of visible light to illuminate the specimen, enabling much higher magnification and resolution.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="how-it-works">How it works:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#how-it-works" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Downloaded by performance cordination (<a href="mailto:performance.cordination@gmail.com" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">performance.cordination@gmail.com</a>)</p>
<ol>
<li>
<p>Electron Generation:
Electrons are produced by an electron gun (usually a heated tungsten filament or field emission source).</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Electron Beam Focusing:
The electron beam is focused and controlled by electromagnetic lenses (condenser lenses) to form a narrow, intense beam aimed at the specimen.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Specimen Interaction:</p>
</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>In a Transmission Electron Microscope (TEM), electrons pass through a very thin specimen. Differences in electron density create contrast, forming an image of the internal structure.</li>
<li>In a Scanning Electron Microscope (SEM), the electron beam scans the specimen surface, and secondary or backscattered electrons emitted from the specimen are detected to create a 3D surface image.</li>
</ul>
<ol start="4">
<li>
<p>Magnification and Imaging:
Electromagnetic objective and projector lenses magnify the electrons’ interaction pattern. The resulting image is projected onto a fluorescent screen, photographic film, or digital detector for viewing or analysis.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Vacuum Environment:
The whole system operates under vacuum to prevent electrons from scattering by air molecules, which would degrade image quality.</p>
</li>
</ol>
<p>Summary:</p>
<ul>
<li>Uses electron beam instead of light for imaging.</li>
<li>Electrons are focused by electromagnetic lenses.</li>
<li>Interaction of electrons with specimen creates image (either transmitted or surface).</li>
<li>Operates in a vacuum to maintain electron beam integrity.</li>
<li>Produces images with much higher resolution than light microscopes.</li>
</ul>
