DNA Polymorphisms and Human Identification

üìö Techniques in the Clinical Lab
‚è±Ô∏è 60 min read

The human genome consists of 2.9 billion nucleotide base pairs. Despite the high degree of sequence identity between individuals, polymorphisms‚ÄîDNA sequences that differ in a small percentage of the population‚Äîprovide a unique genetic fingerprint for every individual (except identical twins). These polymorphisms are the basis for human identification, parentage testing, and genetic mapping.

01

üîÄ Types of Polymorphisms

Polymorphisms can range from single base pair changes to large repeated sequences. The major types used in the laboratory include:

Mobile Elements: Large blocks of repeated sequences can be inverted, deleted, or duplicated. LINES (Long Interspersed Nucleotide Sequences) and SINES (Short Interspersed Nucleotide Sequences, e.g., Alu elements) are mobile elements that make up a significant portion of the human genome.

02

‚úÇÔ∏è RFLP Typing

RFLPs are observed as differences in the sizes and number of fragments generated by restriction enzyme digestion of DNA. These differences arise from mutations that create or destroy restriction sites or from insertions/deletions between sites.

RFLP analysis by Southern blot was the first method used for DNA fingerprinting. It allows for the detection of unique banding patterns for human identification.

Parentage Testing

In parentage testing, the alleles (fragment sizes) of the child are compared to those of the mother and alleged father. The child inherits one allele from each parent. Any allele in the child not present in the mother must come from the biological father. If the alleged father does not have that allele, he is excluded.

03

üß¨ STR Typing by PCR

Short Tandem Repeats (STRs) are small repetitive sequences (1-7 bp repeat units). Because they are small, they can be efficiently amplified by PCR, even from degraded DNA. This makes them ideal for forensic analysis.

CODIS and Allelic Ladders

The FBI established the Combined DNA Index System (CODIS), which uses 13 core STR loci for human identification. STR analysis uses allelic ladders (standards containing all common alleles) to precisely identify the alleles in a sample.

Capillary Electrophoresis and Multiplexing

Modern STR analysis uses capillary electrophoresis with fluorescently labeled primers. Different fluorescent dyes allow loci with overlapping size ranges to be analyzed simultaneously in a single capillary (Multiplexing).

Gender Identification

The amelogenin locus is used for gender identification. The gene is present on both X and Y chromosomes, but the X allele is 6 bp shorter than the Y allele. Females (XX) show a single peak; males (XY) show two peaks.

04

üìä Statistical Analysis

To determine the significance of a match, the frequency of the profile in the population is calculated. Using the Product Rule, the frequencies of each independent locus genotype are multiplied to give the overall profile frequency.

$$OF = F_1 \times F_2 \times F_3 \times \dots \times F_n$$

Paternity Index (PI): A ratio indicating how much more likely it is that the child's allele came from the alleged father than from a random man. The Combined Paternity Index (CPI) is the product of the PIs for all tested loci.

üßÆ

Probability of Paternity

The probability of paternity is calculated using the CPI and prior odds (usually assumed to be 0.5):

$$Probability = \frac{CPI \times 0.5}{(CPI \times 0.5) + (1 - 0.5)} \times 100$$
05

‚ôÇÔ∏è Y-STR Analysis

Y-STRs are found only on the Y chromosome and are inherited as a block (haplotype) from father to son. They do not recombine. This makes them useful for:

  • Lineage Studies: Tracing paternal ancestry.
  • Forensics: Analyzing mixtures of male and female DNA (e.g., sexual assault cases), where the female component overwhelms the male component. Y-STR primers amplify only the male DNA.

Because Y-STRs are linked, the product rule cannot be used. Haplotype frequencies are determined by counting observations in a database.

06

üè• Engraftment Testing

After a bone marrow transplant, DNA typing is used to monitor chimerism‚Äîthe mix of donor and recipient cells. Short Tandem Repeats (STRs) are used to distinguish donor and recipient cells.

Informative Loci: Loci where the donor and recipient have different alleles. These are identified pre-transplant.

Chimerism Analysis: Post-transplant samples (blood or marrow) are tested to quantify the percentage of donor cells.

  • Full Chimerism: Only donor alleles are present.
  • Mixed Chimerism: A mixture of donor and recipient alleles.
  • Graft Failure: Only recipient alleles are present.
‚ö†Ô∏è

Calculation of % Recipient DNA

Using areas under the peaks (from capillary electrophoresis):

$$\% \text{Recipient} = \frac{R}{R + D} \times 100$$

Where $R$ is the area of recipient-specific peaks and $D$ is the area of donor-specific peaks.

07

üî¨ Quality Assurance

STR typing can verify the identity of tissue specimens in pathology. If a tissue block is suspected to be mislabeled or contain "floaters" (contaminating tissue from another block), DNA profiles from the tissue can be compared to a reference sample (e.g., blood) from the patient.

08

üìç Single Nucleotide Polymorphisms (SNPs)

SNPs are single base differences that occur approximately every 1000 bases in the human genome. They are the most abundant form of genetic variation.

Haplotypes: Blocks of SNPs on the same chromosome are inherited together as a unit (linkage disequilibrium). A haplotype can be identified by a few representative SNPs, called Tag SNPs.

HapMap Project: An international effort to map these blocks of haplotypes across the human genome to facilitate the discovery of disease-associated genes.

09

‚ö° Mitochondrial DNA Polymorphisms

Mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA) is maternally inherited. All maternal relatives share the same mtDNA sequence. It is useful for:

  • Forensics: Analyzing samples with degraded nuclear DNA (hair shafts, bones, teeth) because cells contain thousands of copies of mtDNA.
  • Identification: When reference samples are available only from distant maternal relatives.

The Hypervariable Regions (HV1 and HV2) in the non-coding control region (D-loop) are sequenced to determine the mtDNA profile. This profile is compared to the Anderson Reference Sequence.

Key Points

  • ‚úì

    Polymorphisms are variations in DNA sequence shared by a percentage of the population; they are the basis for human identification.

  • ‚úì

    STR typing is the current standard for forensics and paternity testing due to high discrimination power and PCR sensitivity.

  • ‚úì

    CODIS is the FBI's database of DNA profiles from convicted offenders and crime scenes, based on 13 core STR loci.

  • ‚úì

    Y-STRs are used for male lineage and mixed sample analysis; mtDNA is used for maternal lineage and degraded samples.

  • ‚úì

    Engraftment monitoring uses STRs to quantify the percentage of donor and recipient cells in bone marrow transplant patients.
