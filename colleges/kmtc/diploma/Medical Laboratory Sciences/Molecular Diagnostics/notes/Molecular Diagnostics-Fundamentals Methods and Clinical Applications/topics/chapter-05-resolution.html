Resolution and Detection of Nucleic Acids

üìö Common Techniques in Molecular Biology
‚è±Ô∏è 40 min read

Resolution and detection of nucleic acids are done in several ways. Gel and capillary electrophoresis are the most practical and frequently used methods. DNA can also be spotted and detected using specific hybridization probes. This chapter focuses on the principles and applications of electrophoretic separation.

01

‚ö° Electrophoresis

Electrophoresis is the movement of molecules by an electric current. This can occur in solution, but it is practically done in a matrix to limit migration and contain the migrating material. Each phosphate group on a DNA polymer is ionized, making DNA a negatively charged molecule. Under an electric current, DNA will migrate toward the positive pole (anode).

When DNA is applied to a macromolecular cage such as agarose or polyacrylamide, its migration under the pull of the current is impeded, depending on the size of the DNA and the spaces in the gel. Because each nucleotide has one negative charge, the charge-to-mass ratio of molecules of different sizes will remain constant. DNA fragments will therefore migrate at speeds inversely related to their size. Electrophoresis can be performed in tubes, slab gels, or capillaries.

üí°

Advanced Concept: Denaturing Conditions

Double-stranded DNA and RNA are analyzed by native gel electrophoresis. Unpaired, or denatured, DNA and RNA must be analyzed in conditions that prevent the hydrogen bonding between complementary sequences. These conditions are maintained through a combination of formamide mixed with the sample, urea mixed with the gel, and/or heat-denaturing electrophoresis.

02

üß™ Gel Systems

Gel matrices provide resistance to the movement of molecules under the force of the electric current. They prevent diffusion and reduce convection currents so that the separated molecules form a defined group, or "band."

Agarose Gels

Agarose is a polysaccharide polymer extracted from seaweed. It is a linear polymer of agarobiose. The concentration of the agarose dictates the size of the spaces in the gel and will, therefore, be determined by the size of DNA to be resolved.

Pulsed Field Gel Electrophoresis

Very large (50,000‚Äì250,000 bp) pieces of DNA cannot be resolved efficiently by simple agarose electrophoresis. For genomic-sized DNA molecules, pulses of current applied to the gel in alternating dimensions enhance migration. This process is called pulsed field gel electrophoresis (PFGE).

PFGE instruments are designed to apply current in alternating directions at specific times (called the switch interval). Using PFGE, the large fragments are resolved not only by sifting through the spaces in the polymer but also by their reorientation and the time necessary to realign themselves to move in a second dimension.

Polyacrylamide Gels

Very small DNA fragments and single-stranded DNA are best resolved on polyacrylamide gels in polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (PAGE). Acrylamide, in combination with the cross-linker methylene bisacrylamide, polymerizes into a gel that has consistent resolution characteristics. The main advantage of polyacrylamide over agarose is the higher resolution capability for small fragments. A variation of 1 base pair in a 1-kb molecule (0.1% difference) can be detected.

Capillary Electrophoresis

In capillary electrophoresis, the analyte is resolved in a thin glass (fused silica) capillary. The fused silica has a negative charge along the walls of the capillary. This establishes an electro-osmotic flow when a current is introduced along the length of the capillary. Under the force of the current, small and negatively charged molecules migrate faster than large and positively charged molecules.

Nucleic acids do not separate well in solution. As the size or length of a nucleic acid increases (retarding migration), so does its negative charge (speeding migration), effectively confounding the charge/mass resolution. Introduction of a polymer inside the capillary restores resolution by retarding migration according to size more than charge.

03

‚öñÔ∏è Buffer Systems

The purpose of a buffer system is to carry the current and protect the samples during electrophoresis. A buffer is a solution of a weak acid and its conjugate base. The pH of a buffered solution remains constant as the buffer molecules take up or release protons given off or absorbed by other solutes.

üßÆ

Henderson-Hasselbach Equation

The amount the pH of a buffer will differ from the $pK_a$ is expressed as:

$$pH = pK_a + \log \frac{[\text{basic form}]}{[\text{acidic form}]}$$

A buffer should be chosen that has a $pK_a$ within a half point of the desired pH, which is about 8.0 for nucleic acids.

Common Buffers

  • TBE (Tris Borate EDTA): Has a greater buffering capacity. Not recommended for some post-electrophoretic isolation procedures. Prone to precipitation.
  • TAE (Tris Acetate EDTA): DNA migrates twice as fast in TAE than in TBE in a constant current. Ion species are more easily exhausted during extended or high-voltage electrophoresis.
  • TPE (Tris Phosphate EDTA): Used less frequently.

Buffer Additives

Buffer additives are often used to modify sample molecules in ways that affect their migration. Denaturing agents, such as formamide or urea, break hydrogen bonds between complementary strands or within the same strand of DNA or RNA. Methylmercuric hydroxide (MMH), formaldehyde, or glyoxal are used as denaturants for RNA electrophoresis.

04

üîå Equipment & Loading

Gel electrophoresis can be done in one of two conformations, horizontal or vertical. In general, agarose gels are run horizontally, and polyacrylamide gels are run vertically.

Combs: Combs are used to create wells in the gel for sample loading. Vertical gels may use specialized combs, such as shark's-tooth combs, often used for sequencing gels.

Gel Loading

Prior to loading the sample, tracking dye and a density agent are added. The density agent (Ficoll, sucrose, or glycerol) ensures the sample sinks into the well. Tracking dyes allow monitoring of the run progress. Commonly used dyes include Bromophenol Blue and Xylene Cyanol.

Nucleic Acid-Specific Dyes

Ethidium Bromide (EtBr): An intercalating agent that stacks between nitrogen bases in double-stranded nucleic acid. It emits orange light (590 nm) under UV excitation (300 nm). EtBr is carcinogenic and requires careful handling.

SyBr Green: A dye that binds to the minor groove of the double helix. It is 25‚Äì100 times more sensitive than EtBr and less toxic. It is the preferred dye for real-time PCR methods.

Silver Stain

Silver staining is a sensitive system originally developed for protein visualization. Interaction of silver ions with acidic or nucleophilic groups results in crystallization or deposition of metallic silver. It avoids the hazards of intercalators but is more complex to perform. It is useful for detection of limiting amounts of product.

Key Points

  • ‚úì

    Electrophoresis separates molecules based on charge and size using a matrix to impede migration.

  • ‚úì

    Agarose is used for larger DNA fragments, while polyacrylamide is used for smaller fragments requiring higher resolution (1 bp).

  • ‚úì

    Pulsed Field Gel Electrophoresis (PFGE) allows separation of very large DNA molecules (megabases).

  • ‚úì

    Capillary electrophoresis offers higher sensitivity, speed, and automation capabilities compared to slab gels.

  • ‚úì

    Buffer systems (TBE, TAE) maintain pH and conduct current; denaturants like urea or formamide are used for single-stranded separation.
