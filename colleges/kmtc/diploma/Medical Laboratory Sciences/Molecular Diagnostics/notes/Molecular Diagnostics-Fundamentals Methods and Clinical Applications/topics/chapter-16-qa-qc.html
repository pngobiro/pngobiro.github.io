Quality Assurance and Quality Control in the Molecular Laboratory

üìö Techniques in the Clinical Lab
‚è±Ô∏è 35 min read

Quality Assurance (QA) and Quality Control (QC) are critical components of the molecular diagnostics laboratory. Because molecular methods are highly sensitive, they are susceptible to contamination and inhibition, which can lead to false-positive or false-negative results. A comprehensive quality management system ensures the accuracy, reliability, and timeliness of test results.

01

üß™ Specimen Handling

Proper specimen collection and handling are the first steps in ensuring quality results. Nucleic acids, particularly RNA, are labile and can be degraded by nucleases present in the environment or the specimen itself.

Collection Tubes

The choice of anticoagulant is vital for PCR-based tests.

Storage and Transport

DNA is relatively stable. Whole blood for DNA analysis can be stored at $4^{\circ}\text{C}$ for up to 72 hours. Long-term storage of extracted DNA should be at $-20^{\circ}\text{C}$ or $-70^{\circ}\text{C}$ in TE buffer.

RNA is extremely labile. Specimens for RNA analysis (e.g., HIV viral load, gene expression) should be processed immediately (within 2‚Äì4 hours) or collected in specialized stabilizing tubes (e.g., PAXgene). Plasma must be separated from cells quickly to prevent leakage of cellular RNA. Extracted RNA is stored at $-70^{\circ}\text{C}$ or lower.

Precautions

To prevent contamination with RNases, a separate RNase-free area should be designated. Use RNase-free plasticware, DEPC-treated water, and wear gloves at all times.

02

‚öôÔ∏è Test Performance & Controls

Controls are essential to verify the performance of the assay and interpret patient results.

Types of Controls

  • Positive Control: A sample known to contain the target sequence. Verifies that the enzyme, primers, and thermal cycling conditions worked properly.
  • Negative Control (No Template Control - NTC): Contains all reagents but no DNA/RNA. Checks for contamination.
  • Sensitivity Control: A low-copy number positive control (near the Limit of Detection) to ensure the assay can detect low levels of target.
  • Amplification Control (Internal Control): A second target (e.g., housekeeping gene) amplified in the same tube. It distinguishes true negatives from false negatives caused by inhibition.

Assay Validation

Before a new test is implemented, it must be validated. Key parameters include:

  • Accuracy: Agreement with a reference method or gold standard.
  • Precision: Reproducibility of results (intra-run and inter-run).
  • Analytic Sensitivity (Limit of Detection): The lowest amount of target that can be consistently detected.
  • Analytic Specificity: Ability to detect only the target without cross-reactivity to other organisms or sequences.
  • Linearity: For quantitative tests, the range over which the result is proportional to the concentration.
03

üîß Instrument Maintenance

Proper function of equipment is vital for assay reproducibility. A schedule of maintenance and calibration must be documented.

Thermal Cyclers

The accuracy of heating and cooling rates (ramp speeds) and well temperatures affects PCR efficiency. Temperature verification systems (thermocouple probes) are used to check well accuracy ($\pm 0.5^{\circ}\text{C}$) and uniformity across the block.

Pipettes

Pipettes must be calibrated (gravimetrically or photometrically) at least every 6 months to ensure accurate volume delivery. Poor pipetting causes variation in quantification and reagent formulation.

Other Equipment:

  • Centrifuges: Check RPM with a tachometer; check timers.
  • Spectrophotometers: Check wavelength accuracy and linearity filters.
  • Refrigerators/Freezers: Continuous temperature monitoring with alarm systems.

04

üß™ Reagents & Safety

Reagent QC

All reagents (primers, probes, enzymes, buffers) must be tested before use in patient testing. New lots of reagents are tested in parallel with old lots using positive and negative controls to ensure consistent performance. Expiration dates must be strictly observed.

Primers and Probes: Oligonucleotides are susceptible to degradation by repeated freeze-thaw cycles. They should be aliquoted into single-use volumes.

Chemical Safety

Hazardous Chemicals

Ethidium Bromide (EtBr): A potent mutagen used to stain DNA. It intercalates into double-stranded DNA. Alternatives like SYBR Green are less toxic but still bind DNA. Waste must be handled according to specific hazardous waste regulations.

Phenol/Chloroform: Organic solvents used for extraction. Phenol causes severe burns; chloroform is a carcinogen. Use in a chemical fume hood.

Polyacrylamide: Unpolymerized acrylamide is a neurotoxin. Polymerized gels are safer but may contain residual monomers.

05

üìÑ Documentation & Reporting

Accurate documentation is required for regulatory compliance (CLIA, CAP).

Test Reports

Molecular reports should include:

  • Methodology: Description of the test method (e.g., "Real-time PCR", "Sanger Sequencing").
  • Locus/Mutation Tested: Specific gene and mutation (using standard nomenclature, e.g., Factor V R506Q).
  • Result: "Detected", "Not Detected", or quantitative value (copies/mL).
  • Interpretation: Clinical significance of the result (e.g., "Heterozygous for Factor V Leiden mutation; associated with increased risk of thrombosis").
  • Limitations: Limits of detection, specific mutations not detected by the assay.

Proficiency Testing

Laboratories must participate in external proficiency testing (PT) programs (e.g., CAP surveys) for all analytes. Blind samples are received, tested, and reported; results are graded against peer laboratories.
