Molecular Oncology

üìö Techniques in the Clinical Lab
‚è±Ô∏è 45 min read

Molecular diagnostics has revolutionized the diagnosis, prognosis, and treatment of cancer. By analyzing the genetic alterations within tumors, clinicians can classify neoplasms more accurately, select targeted therapies, and monitor disease progression. This chapter explores the molecular basis of cancer and the specific molecular targets used in the clinical laboratory.

01

üìÇ Classification of Neoplasms

Neoplasms are classified based on their tissue of origin and their potential for invasion.

  • Carcinomas: Derived from epithelial tissue (e.g., breast, lung, colon).
  • Sarcomas: Derived from connective tissue (e.g., bone, cartilage, muscle).
  • Hematopoietic Neoplasms: Derived from blood-forming tissues (e.g., leukemias, lymphomas).
02

üß¨ Molecular Basis of Cancer

Cancer is a genetic disease caused by the accumulation of mutations in genes that control cell growth and division. These mutations typically affect two classes of genes:

Oncogenes

Mutated versions of normal genes (proto-oncogenes) that promote cell growth. Mutations are typically gain-of-function and dominant. Examples: KRAS, HER2, EGFR.

Tumor Suppressor Genes

Genes that normally inhibit cell growth or repair DNA. Mutations are typically loss-of-function and recessive (require loss of both alleles). Examples: TP53, RB1, BRCA1.

03

üéØ Analytical Targets of Molecular Testing

Gene and Chromosomal Mutations in Solid Tumors

Specific mutations in solid tumors serve as diagnostic markers and targets for therapy.

  • HER2/neu (ERBB2): Amplification in breast cancer predicts response to Trastuzumab (Herceptin). Detection by FISH or IHC.
  • EGFR: Mutations in the Epidermal Growth Factor Receptor gene in lung cancer predict response to tyrosine kinase inhibitors (TKIs).
  • KRAS: Mutations in colorectal cancer indicate resistance to anti-EGFR therapies (e.g., Cetuximab).
  • BRAF: The V600E mutation in melanoma is a target for specific inhibitors (Vemurafenib).

Microsatellite Instability (MSI)

Microsatellites are short repetitive DNA sequences. In cells with defective Mismatch Repair (MMR) systems, these sequences change length (instability). MSI is a hallmark of Hereditary Non-Polyposis Colorectal Cancer (HNPCC or Lynch Syndrome).

Detection: PCR amplification of specific microsatellite markers (Bethesda panel). Instability is seen as extra peaks or shifts in fragment size on capillary electrophoresis.

Loss of Heterozygosity (LOH)

LOH refers to the loss of one allele of a gene, often a tumor suppressor gene. This can occur via deletion or mitotic recombination. LOH analysis compares normal tissue DNA to tumor DNA using polymorphic markers (STRs or SNPs) flanking the gene of interest.

04

ü©∏ Gene Rearrangements in Leukemia and Lymphoma

V(D)J Recombination and Clonality

Normal B and T lymphocytes undergo rearrangement of their antigen receptor genes (Immunoglobulin heavy/light chains and T-cell receptors) to generate diversity. This process involves the random joining of Variable (V), Diversity (D), and Joining (J) segments.

Clonality Detection: In a malignancy (leukemia/lymphoma), all cells originate from a single clone and therefore share the same specific gene rearrangement. In a reactive (benign) condition, cells are polyclonal (many different rearrangements). PCR or Southern blot can detect the presence of a dominant clonal population.

üí°

Methodology

PCR using consensus primers flanking the V-J or V-D-J junction amplifies the rearranged region. A clonal sample produces a sharp single band (or peak). A polyclonal sample produces a smear (or broad distribution) because many different sized fragments are amplified.

  • Karyotyping: Visualizes the entire chromosome set but has lower resolution.
