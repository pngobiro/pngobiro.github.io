Nucleic Acid Amplification

üìö Common Techniques in Molecular Biology
‚è±Ô∏è 60 min read

The ability to amplify a specific DNA sequence opened the possibility to analyze at the nucleotide level virtually any piece of DNA in nature. The first specific amplification method was the polymerase chain reaction (PCR). Other methods have been developed based on modifications of PCR. These methods can be divided into three groups based on what is amplified: the target nucleic acid itself, a probe specific for the target, or the signal used to detect the target.

01

üéØ Target Amplification

Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR)

Target amplification involves making millions of copies of a target sequence so it can be detected in vitro. PCR, conceived by Kary Mullis in 1983, essentially duplicates the in vivo replication of DNA in a test tube.

üìú

Historical Highlight

Kary Mullis conceived PCR while driving in California. He realized that by using two oligonucleotides, DNA polymerase, and nucleoside triphosphates, he could double a DNA target repeatedly. "I confirmed that two to the tenth power was about a thousand... and that two to the thirtieth power was around a billion... Once I had cycled this reaction thirty times I would be able to [copy] the sequence of a sample with an immense signal and almost no background."

Basic Procedure

Reaction Components

Primers: Single-stranded DNA fragments (20‚Äì30 bases) that determine the specificity of the PCR. They must flank the region to be amplified. Forward primers bind to the antisense strand, and reverse primers bind to the sense strand.

DNA Polymerase: Taq polymerase, isolated from the thermophile Thermus aquaticus, is heat-stable and survives the denaturation steps. It extends primers in the $5' \rightarrow 3'$ direction.

Buffer: Contains magnesium ($Mg^{2+}$), which is critical for enzyme activity and primer annealing. Too little $Mg^{2+}$ lowers yield; too much increases nonspecific products.

Controls and Contamination

Contamination is a major concern. "Amplicon carryover" from previous reactions can cause false positives. Controls are essential:

  • Positive Control: Ensures reagents and cycler are working.
  • Negative Control (No Template): Checks for contamination.
  • Amplification Control (Internal): Detects inhibitors in the sample (avoids false negatives).

Contamination Control:

  • Physical separation of Pre-PCR and Post-PCR areas.
  • dUTP-UNG System: Use dUTP instead of dTTP in the mix. Treat with Uracil-N-Glycosylase (UNG) before cycling to degrade any uracil-containing DNA (contaminants from previous runs).
  • Hot-Start PCR: Prevents mispriming at room temperature by sequestering the polymerase (e.g., via antibody or wax barrier) until the first heat denaturation step.
02

üß¨ PCR Modifications

Multiplex PCR

Simultaneous amplification of multiple targets using multiple primer pairs in a single reaction. Used for pathogen typing and forensic identification.

Reverse Transcriptase PCR (RT-PCR)

Amplifies RNA targets (e.g., HIV, HCV). Reverse transcriptase (RT) converts RNA to cDNA, which is then amplified by PCR. Can be one-step (RT and PCR in same tube) or two-step.

Nested PCR

Increases sensitivity and specificity. Two rounds of PCR are performed. The second pair of primers binds inside the region amplified by the first pair.

Real-Time (Quantitative) PCR (qPCR)

Monitors the accumulation of PCR products as they are made (in real time), allowing for accurate quantitation of the starting template. Analysis is done during the exponential phase.

Threshold Cycle ($C_T$): The cycle number at which the fluorescent signal crosses a set threshold. $C_T$ is inversely proportional to the amount of starting template.

Detection Systems for qPCR

1. SYBR Green: A dye that binds specificially to double-stranded DNA. It fluoresces when bound. Non-specific (binds any dsDNA).

2. TaqMan (Hydrolysis Probes): A probe with a $5'$ reporter dye and $3'$ quencher. When intact, the quencher stops the reporter from fluorescing. During extension, the $5' \rightarrow 3'$ exonuclease activity of Taq digests the probe, releasing the reporter.

3. Molecular Beacons: Hairpin probes with a reporter and quencher. When bound to the target, the hairpin opens, separating R and Q, causing fluorescence.

4. FRET Probes: Two probes bind adjacent to each other. Energy transfers from a donor fluorophore on one probe to an acceptor on the other, causing fluorescence.

03

üîÑ Transcription-Based Amplification

Systems like NASBA (Nucleic Acid Sequence-Based Amplification) and TMA (Transcription-Mediated Amplification) amplify RNA targets isothermally (at one temperature). They use Reverse Transcriptase to make a cDNA copy with a promoter sequence, and T7 RNA Polymerase to transcribe millions of RNA copies from that cDNA.

04

üîç Probe Amplification

These methods amplify the probe molecule itself rather than the target.

Ligase Chain Reaction (LCR): Uses two pairs of probes that cover the target sequence. If they match perfectly, Ligase joins them. The ligated probes become templates for more probes.

Strand Displacement Amplification (SDA): An isothermal process using a restriction enzyme and a DNA polymerase. The polymerase displaces the downstream strand while extending a nick created by the restriction enzyme.

Q$\beta$ Replicase: Uses an RNA-dependent RNA polymerase from bacteriophage Q$\beta$ to replicate a specific probe sequence bound to the target.

05

üì° Signal Amplification

These methods increase the signal strength without increasing the number of target molecules. Useful for quantification.

Branched DNA (bDNA): Uses a series of hybridization probes (capture, extender, and amplifier probes). The "Christmas tree" structure of the amplifier probe binds many alkaline phosphatase molecules, generating a strong signal.

Hybrid Capture: RNA probes bind to DNA targets. The RNA:DNA hybrids are captured by antibodies and detected with chemiluminescence.

Cycling Probe: Uses a DNA-RNA-DNA probe. When bound to a DNA target, RNase H cuts the RNA part, releasing the fragments and allowing new probe to bind.

Key Points

  • ‚úì

    PCR amplifies specific DNA sequences using denaturation, annealing, and extension cycles with a thermostable polymerase.

  • ‚úì

    Real-time PCR (qPCR) quantifies target DNA by detecting fluorescence during the exponential phase of amplification.

  • ‚úì

    Transcription-based methods (NASBA, TMA) amplify RNA isothermally using reverse transcriptase and RNA polymerase.

  • ‚úì

    Signal amplification methods (bDNA, Hybrid Capture) amplify the detection signal rather than the target sequence, making them excellent for quantification.

  • ‚úì

    Contamination control (physical separation, dUTP-UNG) is critical in amplification laboratories to prevent false positives.
