Gene Mutations

üìö Common Techniques in Molecular Biology
‚è±Ô∏è 55 min read

Gene mutations include deletions, insertions, inversions, translocations, and other changes that can affect one base pair to hundreds or thousands of base pairs. Alterations of a single or a few base pairs, or point mutations, will have a range of effects on protein sequence and phenotype. Detection of these mutations requires specialized molecular methods, as they are often too small to be seen by karyotyping.

01

üîÑ Types of Gene Mutations

Because the genetic code is redundant, DNA sequence changes do not necessarily change the amino acid sequence.

üìä Table 9.1 Types of Point Mutations
Type Description Example Effect
Silent Nucleotide change does not alter amino acid. No phenotype change.
Conservative Amino acid changes, but to one with similar biochemical properties. Function may not be significantly affected (e.g., Leu $\to$ Val).
Nonconservative Amino acid changes to one with different properties. Biochemical nature changes (e.g., Pro $\to$ Gln).
Nonsense Nucleotide substitution produces a stop codon. Premature termination of protein.
Frameshift Insertion/deletion of non-triplet nucleotides. Shifts triplet reading frame; often leads to premature stop.
02

üß¨ Hybridization-Based Methods

Single-Strand Conformation Polymorphism (SSCP)

SSCP is based on the preference of single-stranded DNA to fold into a specific three-dimensional shape (conformer) determined by its sequence. A single base difference can cause the conformer to fold differently, altering its migration speed in a non-denaturing polyacrylamide gel.

Denaturing Gradient Gel Electrophoresis (DGGE)

DGGE exploits differences in denaturation (melting) between normal and mutant DNA helices. Double-stranded DNA fragments are separated on a polyacrylamide gel containing a gradient of denaturants (urea and formamide). As the fragment moves into higher denaturant concentration, it begins to melt. A single nucleotide difference changes the melting behavior, shifting the band position.

Variations:

  • CDGE: Constant Denaturant Gel Electrophoresis (constant concentration).
  • TTGE: Temporal Temperature Gradient Gel Electrophoresis (temperature increases over time).

Allele-Specific Oligomer Hybridization (ASO)

ASO uses short probes (oligomers) that bind only to perfectly matched sequences under stringent conditions. A probe for the normal sequence will not bind to a mutant sequence, and vice versa. This is often performed as a dot blot or reverse dot blot.

Melt Curve Analysis (MCA)

Used post-PCR with fluorescent dyes (like SYBR Green). The temperature is raised, and the point at which the DNA denatures ($T_m$) is monitored. Sequence differences result in different $T_m$ peaks. High Resolution Melt (HRM) analysis offers greater sensitivity.

Array Technology

High-density oligonucleotide arrays use thousands of probes to screen for mutations or single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs). Bead arrays use color-coded beads with specific probes for high-throughput analysis.

03

üß™ Polymerization-Based Methods

Sequence-Specific PCR (SSP-PCR)

Primers are designed such that the $3^{\prime}$ end matches the specific allele (normal or mutant). Extension occurs only if the $3^{\prime}$ end is perfectly matched. Also known as Amplification Refractory Mutation System (ARMS).

Allelic Discrimination

Uses fluorescently labeled probes (e.g., TaqMan) specific for each allele in a real-time PCR reaction. Different fluorophores (e.g., FAM and VIC) indicate which allele is present.

Protein Truncation Test (PTT)

Detects nonsense or frameshift mutations that cause premature stop codons. The gene region is amplified, transcribed, and translated in vitro. The resulting protein is analyzed by gel electrophoresis; smaller proteins indicate a truncation mutation.

04

‚úÇÔ∏è Cleavage Methods

PCR-RFLP

Amplifies a region containing a potential mutation that alters a restriction enzyme site. Digestion of the amplicon reveals the mutation by changing the band pattern.

Invader Assay

Uses a proprietary enzyme (Cleavase) that recognizes a specific structure formed by the overlap of two probes (Invader and Signal) on the target. Cleavage releases a flap that triggers a secondary reaction producing fluorescence. It does not require PCR amplification.

05

üìù Gene Mutation Nomenclature

Standard nomenclature describes mutations consistently. Prefixes indicate the reference sequence: g. (genomic), c. (coding DNA), p. (protein), r. (RNA).

üí°

DNA Nomenclature

Position +1: The A of the ATG initiation codon.

  • Substitution: $76A>T$ (A at position 76 changed to T)
  • Deletion: $76delA$ or $76\_78delACT$
  • Insertion: $76\_77insG$
  • Intron: $IVS2+1G>A$ (First base of intron 2 changed G to A)
üß¨

Protein Nomenclature

Uses single or three-letter amino acid codes.

  • Substitution: $R506Q$ (Arginine at 506 changed to Glutamine)
  • Nonsense: $W32X$ (Tryptophan at 32 changed to Stop)

Key Points

  • ‚úì

    Mutations range from single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) to large structural changes.

  • ‚úì

    Hybridization methods (SSCP, DGGE, ASO) exploit differences in binding or folding stability caused by sequence changes.

  • ‚úì

    Sequencing methods (SSP-PCR, Pyrosequencing, Dye Terminator) directly determine the identity of the nucleotide.

  • ‚úì

    Cleavage methods (RFLP, Invader) use enzymes that recognize specific structures or sequences altered by mutations.

  • ‚úì

    Standardized nomenclature is essential for accurately reporting and communicating genetic variations.
