DNA Sequencing

üìö Common Techniques in Molecular Biology
‚è±Ô∏è 50 min read

Direct determination of the order, or sequence, of nucleotides in a DNA polymer is the most specific and direct method for identifying genetic lesions (mutations) or polymorphisms. Molecular techniques, from Southern blot to mutation detection methods, often rely on knowing the nucleotide sequence of the targeted areas. Without this knowledge, results from many methods would be difficult to interpret. DNA sequencing is the most definitive molecular method to identify genetic lesions.

01

üß™ Direct Sequencing

Manual Sequencing

Two types of sequencing methods have been used most extensively: the Maxam-Gilbert method and the Sanger method.

Chemical (Maxam-Gilbert) Sequencing

Developed in the late 1970s, Maxam-Gilbert sequencing requires a single- or double-stranded DNA fragment labeled at one end (usually with ${}^{32}\text{P}$). The fragment is chemically treated to break the DNA chain at specific bases (G, G+A, T+C, C). The resulting fragments are separated on a denaturing polyacrylamide gel.

Although efficient for short runs, Maxam-Gilbert sequencing uses hazardous chemicals (hydrazine, piperidine) and is not practical for high-throughput applications. It has largely been replaced by the dideoxy chain termination method.

Dideoxy (Sanger) Sequencing

Dideoxy chain termination sequencing is a modification of the DNA replication process. A short, synthetic single-stranded DNA primer complementary to sequences just $5^{\prime}$ to the region of interest is used to prime the reaction.

The key to this method is the use of dideoxynucleotides (ddNTPs). Unlike normal deoxynucleotides (dNTPs) which have a $3^{\prime}$ hydroxyl group necessary for chain extension, ddNTPs lack this hydroxyl group. Incorporation of a ddNTP into the growing DNA chain prevents the formation of a phosphodiester bond with the next nucleotide, thus terminating the chain.

In manual Sanger sequencing, four separate reactions are set up. Each contains the template, primer, DNA polymerase, and all four dNTPs. A different ddNTP (ddATP, ddCTP, ddGTP, or ddTTP) is added to each of the four tubes. The ratio of ddNTP to dNTP is critical; it must be low enough to allow some extension but high enough to generate a population of fragments terminated at every possible position.

The reaction products are separated on a denaturing polyacrylamide gel. The "sequencing ladder" is read from the bottom (smallest fragments, $5^{\prime}$ end) to the top (largest fragments, $3^{\prime}$ end).

02

üíª Automated Fluorescent Sequencing

Automated sequencing uses fluorescent dyes to label the DNA fragments, allowing all four reactions to be run in a single lane (or capillary) and read by a laser detector. The output is an electropherogram, where peaks of different colors represent the four nucleotides.

Approaches

There are two main chemistries for automated sequencing:

  1. Dye Primer Sequencing: The primer is labeled with four different fluorescent dyes (one for each reaction tube). Four separate reactions are performed, then pooled and run together.
  2. Dye Terminator Sequencing: The ddNTPs themselves are labeled with different fluorescent dyes. All four reactions can be performed in a single tube. This is the most common method today.

Fragments migrate through a gel or capillary. Capillary electrophoresis is faster and provides better resolution. The instrument reads the fluorescence as fragments pass the detector.

Sequence Quality

The quality of the electropherogram depends on the template quality and the cleanup of the reaction. "Dye blobs" (excess dye terminators) can obscure the sequence. A clean baseline with distinct, non-overlapping peaks indicates a good sequence.

üî• Pyrosequencing

Pyrosequencing determines DNA sequence without making a sequencing ladder. It relies on the generation of light (luminescence) when nucleotides are added to a growing strand of DNA. It is useful for short-read sequencing and SNP detection.

Mechanism:

  1. 1 Nucleotides (dNTPs) are added sequentially. If complementary, polymerase adds the base, releasing pyrophosphate (PPi).
  2. 2 Sulfurylase converts PPi to ATP.
  3. 3 Luciferase uses ATP to convert luciferin to oxyluciferin, emitting light.
  4. 4 Apyrase degrades excess nucleotides before the next addition.
$$(\text{DNA})_n + \text{dNTP} \xrightarrow{\text{Polymerase}} (\text{DNA})_{n+1} + \text{PPi}$$ $$\text{PPi} + \text{APS} \xrightarrow{\text{Sulfurylase}} \text{ATP} + \text{Sulfate}$$ $$\text{Luciferin} + \text{ATP} \xrightarrow{\text{Luciferase}} \text{Oxyluciferin} + \text{Light}$$
04

üß™ Bisulfite DNA Sequencing

Bisulfite sequencing is used to detect methylated nucleotides. Methylation of cytosine residues (CpG islands) is an important epigenetic regulation mechanism. In this method, DNA is treated with bisulfite, which deaminates cytosine, converting it to uracil. However, 5-methyl cytosine is resistant to this modification and remains as cytosine.

After PCR amplification (where uracils are converted to thymines), the sequence is compared to the original. Unmethylated cytosines appear as thymines, while methylated cytosines remain as cytosines.

üìù

Example

Original: ...GTCMeAGCMeTATCTATCMeGTGCA...

Bisulfite Treated: ...GTCMeAGCMeTATUTATCMeGTGUA...

After PCR/Sequencing: Methylated C stays C; Unmethylated C becomes T.

üåç The Human Genome Project

The Human Genome Project (HGP) was a massive international effort to sequence the entire human genome. It officially began in 1990 and a rough draft was completed in 2000. Two competing approaches were used:

  • Hierarchical Shotgun Sequencing (NIH): Start with known regions (BAC clones), map them, and then sequence small fragments. Systematic and orderly.
  • Whole-Genome Shotgun Sequencing (Celera): Shred the entire genome into small fragments, sequence them randomly, and use powerful computers to assemble the overlaps. Faster but computationally demanding.

Key Findings:

  • The human genome contains ~2.9 billion base pairs.
  • There are only ~20,000‚Äì25,000 protein-coding genes (much fewer than expected).
  • Less than 2% of the genome codes for protein.
  • Over 50% of the genome consists of repeated sequences.
