Chromosomal Structure and Mutations

üìö Common Techniques in Molecular Biology
‚è±Ô∏è 45 min read

The human genome consists of 2.9 billion nucleotide base pairs of DNA organized into 23 chromosomes. Genotypic analysis is performed to confirm or predict phenotype. In the laboratory, some changes in chromosome structure and changes in chromosome number can be observed microscopically. This chapter covers the structure of chromosomes and methods for their analysis, including karyotyping and Fluorescence In Situ Hybridization (FISH).

üí°

Key Concepts

  • Genome Mutation: A change in the number of chromosomes (aneuploidy).
  • Chromosomal Mutation: A change in the structure of a chromosome (translocation, deletion, etc.).
  • Gene Mutation: A change in the DNA sequence of a gene.
01

üß∂ Chromosomal Structure

Compaction and Histones

A eukaryotic chromosome is a double helix of DNA. This DNA must be compacted to fit into the cell nucleus. There is an 8000-fold compaction of an extended DNA double helix to make a metaphase chromosome.

Winding of DNA onto histones is the first step. Approximately 160‚Äì180 bp of DNA is wrapped around a set of 8 histone proteins (2 each of H2a, H2b, H3 and H4) to form a nucleosome. Nucleosomes form a "bead-on-a-string" structure (10-nm fiber). This is further coiled around histone H1 into a 30-nm fiber (solenoid), which is eventually compacted into the 1400-nm fibers seen in metaphase chromosomes.

Chromosomal Morphology

Mitotic chromosomes are distinguished by their relative size and centromere placement. The centromere is the site of attachment to the spindle apparatus via the kinetochore. Chromosomes are classified based on centromere position:

  • Metacentric: Arms of equal size.
  • Submetacentric: Arms of unequal size (long arm = $q$, short arm = $p$).
  • Acrocentric: Centromere near one end; very short $p$ arms.
  • Telocentric: Centromere at the very end (not found in humans).

üî¨ Visualizing Chromosomes

Cytological stains allow identification of individual chromosomes and detection of abnormalities through banding patterns.

üß¨ Genome and Chromosomal Mutations

Karyotyping

A karyotype is the complete set of chromosomes in a cell, arranged by size. It is used to detect genome mutations (aneuploidy) and large structural mutations.

Aneuploidy is a change in chromosome number (e.g., trisomy). Euploid refers to the normal complement (46 chromosomes). A normal karyotype is designated 46,XX (female) or 46,XY (male).

Structural Abnormalities

Structural mutations include translocations, deletions, inversions, and insertions.

  • Translocation (t): Exchange of material between chromosomes.
    • Reciprocal: Exchange between two chromosomes (balanced if no genetic material lost).
    • Robertsonian: Fusion of two acrocentric chromosomes at the centromere.
  • Deletion (del): Loss of chromosomal material.
  • Inversion (inv): Reorientation of a segment within a chromosome.
    • Pericentric: Includes the centromere.
    • Paracentric: Within one arm.
  • Isochromosome (iso): Mirror image chromosome consisting of two long arms or two short arms.
04

üî¶ Fluorescence In Situ Hybridization (FISH)

FISH uses fluorescently labeled probes to detect specific DNA sequences in cells (in situ). It can be performed on interphase nuclei (no culture required) or metaphase chromosomes.

Interphase FISH

Useful for detecting aneuploidy, deletions, and translocations in non-dividing cells.

  • Enumeration Probes (CEP): Target centromeric alpha satellite sequences to count chromosomes.
  • Dual Color Probes: Used to detect translocations. Fusion of two signals (e.g., red and green making yellow) indicates a translocation.
  • Break-apart Probes: Probes flank a breakpoint. Separation of signals indicates a rearrangement.

Metaphase FISH

Includes Chromosome Painting (probes cover the entire chromosome) and Spectral Karyotyping (SKY) (uses combinations of 5 fluors to color every chromosome differently). Useful for detecting complex rearrangements.

Comparative Genome Hybridization (CGH)

Used to detect intrachromosomal amplifications or deletions. Test DNA and reference DNA are labeled with different colors (e.g., green and red) and hybridized to normal metaphase chromosomes. The ratio of fluorescence intensities indicates regions of gain or loss.

Key Points

  • ‚úì
