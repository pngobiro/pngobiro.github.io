Analysis and Characterization of Nucleic Acids and Proteins

üìö Common Techniques in Molecular Biology
‚è±Ô∏è 50 min read

Clinical and forensic analyses require characterization of specific genes or genomic regions at the molecular level. Procedures performed in the clinical molecular laboratory are aimed at specific targets in genomic DNA. This requires visualization or detection of a specific gene or region of DNA in the background of all other genes.

There are several ways to find a particular region of DNA from within an isolated DNA sample. The initial method for molecular analysis of specific DNA sites within a complex background was the Southern blot. Modifications of the Southern blot are applied to analysis of RNA and protein in order to study gene expression and regulation.

01

‚úÇÔ∏è Restriction Enzyme Mapping

Because of their sequence-specific activity, restriction endonucleases provide a convenient tool for molecular characterization of DNA. Restriction sites will occur naturally in DNA. Restriction site mapping, determining where in the DNA sequence a particular restriction enzyme recognition site is located, was initially developed using small circular bacterial plasmids.

To make a restriction map, DNA is exposed to several restriction enzymes separately and then in particular combinations. The sizes of the fragments, as determined by comparison with known molecular-weight standards, indicate the distance between these sites or from the site to the end of the fragment.

Mapping of a circular plasmid is slightly different, as there are no free ends. Cutting the plasmid with one enzyme will yield one fragment the size of the plasmid, unless there are multiple sites. Double digests allow ordering of the sites.

The pattern of fragments produced by restriction enzyme digestion can be used to identify that DNA. The resulting differences in the size or number of restriction fragments due to inherited sequence differences are called restriction fragment length polymorphisms (RFLPs). RFLPs can be used for the clinical analysis of structural changes in chromosomes associated with disease.

Southern Blots

In the Southern blot, DNA is isolated and cut with restriction enzymes. The fragments are separated by gel electrophoresis, depurinated and denatured, and then transferred to a solid support such as nitrocellulose. Finally, the DNA fragments are exposed to a labeled probe that is specific in sequence to the region of interest.

Depurination and Denaturation

Before blotting, double-stranded DNA fragments must be denatured into single strands. Large fragments (>500 bp) are more efficiently denatured if they are depurinated first by soaking the gel in HCl solution, which removes purine bases.

Blotting (Transfer)

The denatured DNA is transferred to a solid substrate, usually nitrocellulose or nylon membranes. Several methods exist:

  • Capillary Transfer: Uses capillary action to draw buffer through the gel, carrying DNA onto the membrane (Fig. 6-6).
  • Electrophoretic Transfer: Uses electric current to move DNA from gel to membrane (Fig. 6-7).
  • Vacuum Transfer: Uses suction to move DNA (Fig. 6-8).

Northern Blots

The Northern blot is designed to investigate RNA structure and quantity. RNA is isolated, separated by denaturing gel electrophoresis (to prevent secondary structures), and transferred to a membrane. It is used to analyze gene expression (transcription).

Western Blots

Western blots analyze protein. Proteins are separated by SDS-PAGE or isoelectric focusing, transferred to a membrane (nitrocellulose or PVDF), and probed with specific antibodies.

03

üîç Probes

The probe is a single-stranded fragment of nucleic acid (or antibody for Westerns) used to identify a specific sequence or target. Probes can be DNA, RNA, or modified nucleic acids like Peptide Nucleic Acids (PNA) or Locked Nucleic Acids (LNA).

Probe Labeling

Probes must be labeled to be detected. Common labels include radioactive isotopes ($^{32}\text{P}$) or nonradioactive tags like biotin or digoxygenin.

04

üå°Ô∏è Hybridization Conditions & Stringency

Stringency is the combination of conditions in which the target is exposed to the probe. High stringency demands perfect complementarity; low stringency allows some mismatch. Stringency is affected by temperature, salt concentration, and formamide concentration.

The ideal hybridization temperature is estimated from the melting temperature ($T_m$), where half the sequence is double-stranded.

üßÆ

Tm Calculation Formula

$$T_m = 81.5^{\circ}\text{C} + 16.6 \log M + 0.41 (\%G + \%C) - 0.61 (\% \text{formamide}) - (600/n)$$

Where:

  • M = sodium concentration (mol/L)
  • n = number of base pairs

For short probes (14-20 bases):

$$T_m = 4^{\circ}\text{C} \times (\text{G/C pairs}) + 2^{\circ}\text{C} \times (\text{A/T pairs})$$

The $C_o t$ value expresses sequence complexity. $C_o t$ is the initial DNA concentration ($C_o$) times the time required to reanneal ($t$). Complex sequences require more time to reassociate.

05

üëÅÔ∏è Detection Systems

Detection methods visualize where the probe has bound. Radioactive ${}^{32}\text{P}$ probes are detected by autoradiography.

Nonradioactive systems use enzymes (HRP, Alkaline Phosphatase) conjugated to antibodies (anti-digoxygenin) or streptavidin (for biotin) to generate signals from substrates (Fig. 6-16).

Chemiluminescent detection uses substrates like dioxetane that emit light when processed by the enzyme (Fig. 6-17). Chromogenic detection uses substrates like NBT/BCIP that produce a colored precipitate (Fig. 6-18).

06

üìä Interpretation of Results

Southern blot bands allow comparison of restriction patterns (Fig. 6-19). Northern/Western blots allow quantification of expression relative to controls (Fig. 6-20).

07

‚ñ¶ Array-Based Hybridization

Dot/Slot Blots

Target DNA/RNA is deposited directly on the membrane without resolution by size. Used for high-throughput screening (Fig. 6-21).

Microarrays

Miniaturized arrays on glass slides (chips) allow screening of thousands of targets simultaneously. Targets can be deposited by automated arrayers or synthesized directly on the chip (photolithography).

Microelectronic Arrays: Use electrodes to concentrate samples at specific spots (Fig. 6-25).

Applications: Expression arrays measure transcript levels. Comparative Genomic Hybridization (CGH) arrays detect deletions and amplifications in the genome (Fig. 6-27).

08

üß™ Solution Hybridization

RNase Protection (S1 Analysis): Target RNA is hybridized to a labeled probe in solution. Unhybridized single-stranded nucleic acids are digested by S1 nuclease. The remaining protected double-stranded hybrid is analyzed by electrophoresis (Fig. 6-28).

Gel Mobility Shift Assay: Analyzes protein-DNA interactions. Binding of a protein to a labeled DNA fragment slows its migration in a gel (shift) (Fig. 6-29).
