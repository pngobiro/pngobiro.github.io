Detection and Identification of Microorganisms

üìö Techniques in the Clinical Lab
‚è±Ô∏è 65 min read

Molecular-based methods have revolutionized clinical microbiology. The genome of the microorganism is the analyte, replacing traditional phenotypic traits like colonial morphology or biochemical reactions. These methods are particularly valuable for organisms that are difficult to culture (M. tuberculosis), hazardous (Coccidioides), or lack reliable serological tests (early HIV/HCV).

Primary methods include PCR, real-time PCR (qPCR), reverse transcriptase PCR (RT-PCR), and sequencing. Molecular epidemiology uses techniques like Pulsed-Field Gel Electrophoresis (PFGE) to trace outbreaks.

üß™ Specimen & Sample Prep

Specimen Collection

Proper collection is critical. Swabs with plastic shafts (Dacron/calcium alginate) are recommended over wooden shafts, which can inhibit PCR. Special transport media are often required.

Sample Preparation

Key steps in preparation include:

  • Lysis: Mycobacteria and fungi require rigorous lysis (e.g., bead beating) due to thick cell walls.
  • Concentration: Centrifugation of fluids (CSF, urine) increases sensitivity.
  • Inhibitor Removal: Hemoglobin (blood), acidic polysaccharides (sputum), and nitrate/crystals (urine) inhibit DNA polymerase and must be removed.

Quality Control

Molecular testing requires rigorous QC to prevent false positives (contamination) and false negatives (inhibition).

Amplification Controls

Amplification Control (Internal Control): A second target added to the reaction (e.g., a housekeeping gene or extrinsic DNA). If the target is negative, the internal control must be positive. If the internal control is also negative, the reaction is invalid due to inhibition or failure.

ü´Å Respiratory Tract Pathogens

Mycobacterium tuberculosis (MTB)

Traditional culture takes weeks. Molecular assays provide results in hours. Target: 16S rRNA or IS6110.

  • Tests: AMPLICOR MTB (PCR), MTD (Transcription-Mediated Amplification).
  • Sensitivity: Very high for smear-positive specimens; lower for smear-negative.

Bordetella pertussis

Cause of whooping cough. Culture is difficult (Regan-Lowe media). PCR is the method of choice.

  • Targets: IS481 (highly sensitive but found in B. holmesii) and IS1001 (specific for B. parapertussis).

üß¨ Urogenital Tract Pathogens

Neisseria gonorrhoeae (NG) & Chlamydia trachomatis (CT)

Molecular testing is the standard of care due to high sensitivity and the ability to use urine or vaginal swabs (non-invasive).

  • Methods: PCR (Roche Amplicor), SDA (BD ProbeTec), TMA (Aptima).
  • Targets: Cryptic plasmid (CT), Opa gene/16S rRNA (NG).
  • Note: Not recommended for child abuse cases (culture is required for legal confirmation due to specificity issues).

Treponema pallidum (Syphilis)

Cannot be cultured in vitro. Diagnosis relies on serology (RPR, VDRL, FTA-ABS). PCR (targeting polA) is useful for primary chancres (ulcers) or CSF (neurosyphilis) where serology might be equivocal.

üåç Molecular Epidemiology

Used to determine the relatedness of isolates (e.g., in a hospital outbreak). Are patient A and patient B infected with the same strain?

Pulsed-Field Gel Electrophoresis (PFGE)

Considered the "gold standard" for typing many bacteria. Genomic DNA is digested with a rare-cutting restriction enzyme. Large fragments are separated by switching the direction of the electric field.

Interpretation (Tenover Criteria):

  • Indistinguishable: Same band pattern. (Same strain).
  • Closely Related: 2-3 band difference (1 genetic event).
  • Possibly Related: 4-6 band difference (2 genetic events).
  • Different: ‚â•7 band differences (‚â•3 genetic events). Unrelated.

Other Typing Methods

Repetitive Elements (rep-PCR): Amplifies DNA between repetitive sequences (ERIC, REP) found in bacteria. Faster than PFGE but sometimes less discriminatory.

Multilocus Sequence Typing (MLST): Sequences 6-7 housekeeping genes. Allelic profiles are compared via database (pubmlst.org). Highly reproducible and portable data.

06

ü¶† Viruses

Viral Load Testing

Quantitation of virus (copies/mL) is crucial for monitoring HIV and HCV therapy.

Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV)

Viral Load: Measures HIV RNA in plasma. Used to determine when to start therapy and to monitor response. Effective therapy should result in a significant drop (‚â• 0.5 log or 3-fold) in viral load.

Genotyping: Detects mutations in Reverse Transcriptase (RT) and Protease genes that confer resistance to antiretroviral drugs. Used when drug failure is suspected.

üí°

Primary vs. Secondary Mutations

Primary Mutations: Specific to the drug, appear first, reduce susceptibility.

Secondary Mutations: Compensatory mutations that help the virus replicate in the presence of the drug or fix fitness costs of primary mutations.

Hepatitis C Virus (HCV)

Qualitative: Confirms active infection in antibody-positive patients.

Quantitative: Baseline viral load helps predict response to therapy (interferon/ribavirin).

Genotyping: HCV has 6 genotypes. Genotypes 2 and 3 respond better to therapy than Genotype 1. Genotyping is essential for determining treatment duration (24 vs 48 weeks).

Case Studies

Case 12.1: Norovirus Outbreak

Guests at a hotel suffered nausea and vomiting. RT-PCR for Norovirus was performed on stool and food.

Conclusion: The guests have Norovirus. The source was the salad lettuce (Lane 5 match). The employees (Lanes 6-8) were negative, suggesting the contamination came from the food distributor, not the kitchen staff.

Case 12.2: MRSA Outbreak

Skin infections in high school students were screened for MRSA. PFGE was performed.

Conclusion: Isolates 1, 2, 4, and 5 represent a single clone/outbreak strain. Isolate 3 is unrelated.
