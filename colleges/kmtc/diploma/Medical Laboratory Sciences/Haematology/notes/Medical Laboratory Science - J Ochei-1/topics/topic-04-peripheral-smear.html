üß™ Preparation of Blood Smear

Microscopic examination of the peripheral blood is performed by preparing, staining and examining a thin film of blood. It provides valuable information on the status of blood cells and presence of parasitic elements. The blood film is the only permanent record of haematological investigations.

Capillary blood is ideal for preparing a blood smear. A drop of blood is transferred to a slide or touch the slide to the site of fingerprick during blood flow. If venous blood is used, EDTA is the anticoagulant of choice because it retains the morphological features of blood cells and also prevents clumping of platelets.

Characteristics of an Ideal Blood Film

  • It should be smooth and not interrupted by ridges and holes.
  • It should cover 1/2 to 3/4th of the area of the slide.
  • It should be thickest at the origin or the 'head' (thick end), and gradually thin out forming a 'tail' (feather edge) without a defined border at the end.
üìù

Technique

  1. Place a small drop of EDTA anticoagulated blood, about 2 mm in diameter, about 1 cm from one end of the slide. If blood is collected from a finger-prick, wipe away the first drop and place the second drop of blood on the slide by touching it with the slide.
  2. Place the slide on a flat surface. Hold it down firmly at the opposite end with the thumb and the forefinger.
  3. Quickly, place the spreader just in front of the drop of blood at 45¬∞ angle. Draw it back slightly to touch the drop of blood and to allow the blood to spread along the contact line. Push the spreader forward smoothly and rapidly, maintaining the contact between the slide and the spreader.

üé® Staining the Blood Smear

After preparation, the blood smear should be stained as soon as possible, otherwise it should be fixed by dipping in methanol for 1‚Äì2 seconds.

Romanowsky Stains

Affinity of a cell or its constituents for a particular stain depends on its chemical nature and the pH of the medium. The Romanowsky stains contain methylene blue (a basic dye), eosin (an acidic dye) and polychrome methylene blue or methylene azure.

  • Acidic components: Nuclear DNA and cytoplasmic RNA are stained bluish purple (basophilic) with polychrome methylene blue.
  • Basic components: Haemoglobin and granules in eosinophils are stained orange to pink (acidophilic) with eosin.

1. Leishman Stain

Uses methylene blue, sodium carbonate, eosin, and methyl alcohol. Staining involves covering the smear with stain for 1-2 minutes, then diluting with buffer (pH 6.8) for 10 minutes.

2. Giemsa Stain

Uses Azur II eosin, Azur II, glycerol, and methyl alcohol. Requires fixation in methyl alcohol first. The slide is flooded with diluted stain (1:9 with buffer) for 10-15 minutes.

üî¨ Microscopic Examination

Low Power (10x) Examination

The purpose is to evaluate the quality of the blood film and estimate roughly the red cell and white cell counts. A good quality blood smear should show evenly distributed red and white blood cells.

Rough estimation: In an area where the cells are evenly spread in a single layer, 5 white blood cells in a low power field are roughly equal to 1000 red cells/cu.mm ($1 \times 10^9$ cells/L).

Oil Immersion (100x) Examination

It should include:

  1. The differential count of leucocytes and detection of abnormalities.
  2. Morphological alterations in red blood cells.
  3. Evaluation of platelet count and morphology.
  4. Detection of blood parasites.

‚ö†Ô∏è Leucocyte Abnormalities

Quantitative Changes

  • Neutrophil leucocytosis (Neutrophilia): Increase in neutrophils. Seen in acute infections, toxic conditions, haemorrhage. "Shift to the left" indicates presence of immature cells.
  • Neutropenia: Reduction in neutrophils. Seen in aplastic anaemia, viral/bacterial infections, drug treatment.
  • Eosinophilia: Allergic reactions, parasitic infestations, asthma.
  • Basophilia: Chronic myeloid leukaemia, haemolytic anaemia.
  • Lymphocytosis: Acute viral infections (infectious mononucleosis, mumps), chronic infections (TB).
  • Lymphopenia: Drug treatment, AIDS.
  • Monocytosis: Infectious mononucleosis, malaria.

Leukaemias

Leukaemia is characterised by uncontrolled and abnormal formation of cells of one or more of the blood cell series.

Acute Myeloid Leukaemia (AML)

Marked leucocytosis (20,000‚Äì100,000/cu.mm). 30‚Äì90% blast cells. Auer bodies may be present. Severe anaemia and thrombocytopenia.

Chronic Myeloid Leukaemia (CML)

Very high counts (up to 800,000/cu.mm). All stages of myeloid maturation seen. Increased basophils/eosinophils. Platelet count usually normal.

Acute Lymphocytic Leukaemia (ALL)

High white cell count. Large number of lymphoblasts (70‚Äì90%). Common in children. Normocytic anaemia.

Chronic Lymphocytic Leukaemia (CLL)

Increase in mature small lymphocytes. Smudge/basket cells are frequently seen.

üî¥ Morphological Variations in Erythrocytes

Alterations in the morphology of erythrocytes are associated with many diseases, most significantly anaemia.

1. Variation in Colour

  • Normochromic: Normal staining.
  • Hypochromic: Pale, increased central pallor (Iron deficiency).
  • Polychromatic: Blue-orange colour, residual RNA (Reticulocytes).

2. Variation in Size (Anisocytosis)

  • Normocytic: Normal size.
  • Macrocytosis: MCV > 100 fL (Megaloblastic anaemia, liver disease).
  • Microcytosis: MCV < 80 fL (Iron deficiency, Thalassemia).

3. Variation in Shape (Poikilocytosis)

Cell Type Description Probable Causes
Spherocytes Spherical, dark, no central pallor. Hereditary spherocytosis, immune haemolytic anaemia.
Elliptocytes Oval or egg-shaped. Hereditary elliptocytosis, megaloblastic anaemia.
Target cells Central area of Hb, target appearance. Liver disease, thalassaemia, post-splenectomy.
Sickle cells Crescent/holly leaf shape. Sickle cell anaemia (HbS).
Schistocytes Fragmented cells. Mechanical damage (heart valves), burns, DIC.
Burr cells Irregular spine-like processes. Uraemia, renal insufficiency.

4. Red Cell Inclusions

  • Basophilic stippling: Fine blue granules (RNA). Seen in Lead poisoning, Thalassemia.
  • Howell-Jolly bodies: DNA remnants. Seen post-splenectomy, megaloblastic anaemia.
  • Heinz bodies: Denatured Hb (supravital stain). Seen in G6PD deficiency.
  • Pappenheimer bodies: Iron granules (Siderocytes). Seen post-splenectomy.
  • Cabot's rings: Threadlike rings. Seen in megaloblastic anaemia.
