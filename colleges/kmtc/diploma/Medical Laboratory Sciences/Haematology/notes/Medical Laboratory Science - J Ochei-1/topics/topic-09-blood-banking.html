üë®‚Äç‚öïÔ∏è Selection of a Donor

Therapeutic administration of blood and blood products is indicated in many diseases and other health problems. The technique of replacing a patient's whole blood or its components is known as blood transfusion. The procedures involved in collecting, storing, processing and distributing the blood of potential donors is called blood banking.

Two basic principles must be kept in mind when the donor is selected:

  1. The blood collection procedure should not be harmful to the donor.
  2. The donor's blood should not be harmful to the recipient.

Pre-transfusion Tests on Donor's Blood

Haemoglobin Concentration

It is best to disqualify persons with haemoglobin of less than $12\mathrm{g}/100\mathrm{ml}$. This rule may be relaxed to permit individuals with $10\mathrm{g}/100\mathrm{ml}$ haemoglobin to donate blood in emergencies.

Infectious Disease Screening

  • Parasites: Malaria, microfilaria, trypanosomes.
  • Syphilis: Screening by VDRL or RPR. (Note: T. pallidum cannot survive for more than 48‚Äì72 hours at 4¬∞C).
  • Hepatitis B: Screening for HBsAg.
  • HIV: Screening by ELISA.

ü©∏ Collection and Storage

Blood for transfusion must be collected and handled under strictly sterile conditions. Plastic blood collection bags are used which contain an anticoagulant. Generally, a combination of citrate and dextrose is used. Citrate binds with calcium and prevents activation of the coagulation cascade. Dextrose provides energy for the red blood cells.

Anticoagulants

  • Anticoagulant Citrate Dextrose (ACD): Older solution. 21 days storage.
  • Citrate Phosphate Dextrose (CPD): Higher pH, better isotonicity, improved red cell function. 21 days storage.
  • Citrate Phosphate Dextrose Adenine-1 (CPDA-1): Adenine helps maintain high ATP levels. Storage up to 35 days.

The blood must be stored in a refrigerator with a constant temperature of 1‚Äì6¬∞C. An alarm should sound if the temperature exceeds the limit. The stored blood should be inspected daily for colour, turbidity, haemolysis and clot formation.

üî¨ Compatibility Testing

The donor's blood should be compatible with recipient's blood and should give maximum benefit to the recipient. Testing involves ABO and Rh typing of both donor and recipient, followed by crossmatching.

Crossmatching

Crossmatching is divided into two parts:

Major Crossmatch

Tests Donor's red cells with Recipient's serum. Detects unexpected antibodies in recipient's serum that will destroy donor's red cells.

Minor Crossmatch

Tests Recipient's red cells with Donor's serum. Detects antibodies in donor plasma.

Methods of Crossmatching

‚ñ∂ Method I: Saline Method (Slide & Tube)

A. Slide method

  1. Divide slide into Major (Donor cells + Recipient serum) and Minor (Recipient cells + Donor serum).
  2. Mix and stand at room temperature for 5-10 minutes.
  3. Examine for agglutination.

B. Tube method

  1. Prepare Major and Minor tubes as above.
  2. Allow to stand at room temperature for 5 mins, centrifuge and read.
  3. If no agglutination, incubate at 37¬∞C for 60 mins (detects warm antibodies).
‚ñ∂ Method 2: Albumin Tube Method

This technique can detect some incompatibilities due to incomplete immune antibodies such as Rh-antibodies which may not be detected by the saline method.

  1. Incubate tubes at 37¬∞C for 60 minutes.
  2. Add 22% bovine albumin down the side of the tube.
  3. Incubate further for 30 minutes and read.
‚ñ∂ Method 3: Antihuman Globulin (Coombs) Crossmatch

This test is very sensitive for the detection of incomplete, immune antibodies such as Rh antibodies, and antibodies of Duffy, Kidd and Kell systems.

  1. Incubate Major and Minor mixtures at 37¬∞C for 60 minutes.
  2. Wash cells three times in saline.
  3. Add AHG serum, centrifuge and examine for agglutination.

Unexpected Antibody Screening

This is done by testing recipient's serum with a series of group O red cells coated with antigens to some clinically significant antibodies (e.g., C, c, D, E, e, Kell, Duffy, Kidd).

üì¶ Blood Components and Derivatives

Indiscriminate use of whole blood is not justified when most patients can be effectively treated with one or more of the components. Blood components are prepared by physical separation (centrifugation), while derivatives are prepared by complex fractionation of pooled plasma.

Blood component or Derivative Use
Whole blood Acute blood loss where both red cells and volume are desired: rarely indicated
Red blood cells (Packed) To increase red cell mass (e.g. therapy for anaemia); use with colloids or crystalloids in active bleeding.
Leukocyte-reduced RBCs To avoid febrile and allergic reactions from leukocytes or plasma proteins.
Deglyceralised blood To extend storage of red cells with rare blood types and autologous transfusion.
Platelets Functional or quantitative platelet defects.
Granulocytes Rare; for septic, severely granulocytopenic patients.
Fresh frozen plasma (FFP) In bleeding patients with multiple coagulation defects; also for factor V or XI deficiency.
Cryoprecipitate For treatment of von Willebrand's disease, factor XIII deficiency or hypofibrinogenemia.
Factor VIII concentrate For haemophilia A.
Albumin/plasma protein fraction For volume expansion and colloid replacement.
Rh immune globulin To prevent HDN in Rh-negative women exposed to Rh-positive red cells.

‚ö†Ô∏è Transfusion Reactions

The term transfusion reaction means an obviously adverse response to transfusion. These can be haemolytic, febrile, allergic, or due to circulatory overload.

Haemolytic Reactions

Caused by in vivo combination of antibody with antigen on red cells. Major incompatibility (Antibody in recipient, Antigen on donor) is most severe. Anti-A/Anti-B cause rapid intravascular haemolysis. Rh antibodies usually cause extravascular haemolysis (delayed).

Febrile Reactions

Manifested as chills and fever. Often caused by recipient antibodies against donor leukocyte antigens or plasma proteins.

Allergic Reactions

Urticaria, facial oedema, asthma. Caused by passive transfer of donor allergens.

Circulatory Overload

Tightness in chest, dry cough, pulmonary oedema due to increased plasma volume.

Investigation of Suspected Reaction

üìù

Immediate Steps

The most important immediate step is to check the clerical records that the patient has received the blood intended for him.

‚ñ∂ Laboratory Investigation Schedule (Table 9.4)

A. All Reported Reactions

  1. Collect Pre-transfusion and Post-transfusion blood.
  2. Check ID and records.
  3. Repeat ABO/Rh typing.
  4. Perform Direct Antiglobulin Test (DAT) on post-transfusion sample.
  5. Examine for visible haemolysis.

B. If Evidence of Haemolysis/Incompatibility

  1. Collect donor pilot samples, bag blood, and post-transfusion urine.
  2. Repeat Crossmatch (Major and Minor).
  3. Repeat Antibody Screen.
  4. Biochemical/Microbiological: Serum haptoglobin, bilirubin, urea; Urine haemoglobin; Culture of donor bag.
