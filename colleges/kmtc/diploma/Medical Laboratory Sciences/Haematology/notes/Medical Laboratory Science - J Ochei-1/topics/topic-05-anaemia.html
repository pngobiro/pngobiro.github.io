üìã Definition and Classification

Anaemia is not a specific disease. It is a condition in which oxygen carrying capacity of blood is reduced. Anaemia is always a sign of an underlying disease, and never a diagnosis. A red cell count of less than 4.0 million/cu.mm ($\mu$l) in males and 3.5 million/cu.mm ($\mu$l) in females, or haemoglobin content of less than 10.8 g per 100 ml (108g/L) in males and 9.5 g/100 ml (95 g/L) in females is an indication of anaemia.

Anaemia may be caused due to the following factors:

  • Increased haemolysis.
  • Diminished erythropoiesis.
  • Blood loss.

Morphological Classification

Morphological classification of anaemia depends on the examination of blood which includes the determination of haemoglobin, enumeration of red cells, red cell indices and the examination of the peripheral blood smear.

Etiological Classification

‚ñ∂ I. Anaemia Resulting from Excessive Destruction of Red Cells
  1. Due to intracellular defects: Hereditary defects of red cell membrane (e.g. spherocytosis, ovalocytosis); reduced globin chain synthesis (e.g. thalassaemia); and abnormal globin chain synthesis (e.g. sickle cell anaemia). Acquired defects (e.g. paroxysmal nocturnal haemoglobinuria).
  2. Due to extracellular abnormalities:
    • Immune mechanism (e.g. autoimmune haemolytic anaemia).
    • Non-immune mechanism (e.g. malaria, bacterial toxins, toxic chemicals).
‚ñ∂ II. Decreased Production of Haemoglobin/Erythrocyte Mass
  1. Anaemia resulting from specific deficiency: Vitamin B$_{12}$, Folic acid, or Iron deficiency.
  2. Anaemia resulting from endocrine abnormality: e.g. thyroid, pituitary, adrenal glands.
  3. Anaemia resulting from inadequate marrow function: Mechanical interference, relative marrow failure (infection, renal disease), or bone-marrow failure (aplasia).
‚ñ∂ III. Anaemia Resulting from Blood Loss

Acute haemorrhage (accidental wounds, duodenal ulcer) or chronic haemorrhage.

üî© Iron Deficiency and Related Anaemias

The commonest cause of anaemia is iron deficiency. In this type of anaemia, the red cells appear microcytic and hypochromic, and red cell indices (MCV, MCH, MCHC) are lowered.

Iron Distribution: About 66% of body iron is present in haemoglobin molecules. Excess iron is stored in the RE cells as hemosiderin and ferritin.

Clinical symptoms: Brittle, ridged or spoon nails (Koilonychia); Dysphagia; Unusual dietary cravings (pica).

‚ö†Ô∏è

Laboratory Findings in Iron Deficiency Anaemia

  1. Decrease in the concentration of haemoglobin, RBC count, and packed cell volume. MCV, MCH, MCHC are lowered.
  2. Red cell morphology in peripheral blood shows microcytosis and hypochromia, target cells may be present.
  3. Decrease in the reticulocyte count.
  4. Examination of bone marrow indicates erythroid hyperplasia with delay in haemoglobinisation of normoblasts.

Sideroblastic Anaemia

A sideroblast is a normal erythroblast containing 2 to 4 randomly distributed iron granules in the cytoplasm. In sideroblastic anaemia, abnormal erythroblasts containing numerous iron granules arranged in a ring around the nucleus are seen (Fig. 5.1). The cause is probably a defect in the synthesis of haem.

Other Hypochromic Anaemias

  • Lead Poisoning: Lead inhibits synthesis of both haem and globin. Peripheral blood smear shows hypochromic red cells and punctate basophilia (basophilic stippling).
  • Anaemia Due to Chronic Disorders: Seen in chronic inflammatory diseases or malignancies. Features include decreased release of iron from RE macrophages and reduced red cell life span.
03

üíä Megaloblastic Anaemia

This group of anaemias is a result of defective DNA synthesis leading to delayed maturation of the nucleus of erythroblasts as compared to the maturation of their cytoplasm. The defect is most commonly due to deficiency of vitamin B$_{12}$ or folic acid.

Pernicious Anaemia

A type of megaloblastic anaemia caused due to malabsorption of vitamin B$_{12}$ from the gastric mucosa. Causes include absence of intrinsic factor (IF) or autoimmune antibodies against parietal cells or IF.

Clinical Features

Insidious onset, mild jaundice, glossitis (beefy, red, sore tongue), loss of weight, and sometimes purpura/pigmentation.

üî¨

Laboratory Findings

  1. Decrease in haemoglobin, RBC count and PCV.
  2. MCV and MCH are increased, MCHC is normal.
  3. Peripheral blood smear shows: Macrocytosis, Howell-Jolly bodies, Cabot rings, and Hypersegmented neutrophils.
  4. Decreased white cell count, platelet count and reticulocyte count.
  5. Mild increase in unconjugated serum bilirubin level.
  6. Bone marrow examination reveals erythroid hyperplasia with a large number of megaloblasts.

üß™ Haemolytic Anaemias

Haemolytic anaemias result from increased destruction of blood cells. In severe cases, red cells survive only for a few days.

General features: Pallor, mild fluctuating jaundice, splenomegaly.

Haemolytic Anaemias Due to Membrane Defects

  1. Hereditary Spherocytosis: Defect in spectrin protein. Cells lose biconcave shape and become spherical.
    Findings: Reticulocytosis, spherocytes in smear, increased osmotic fragility, increased autohaemolysis (corrected with glucose), negative Coombs' test.
  2. Hereditary Elliptocytosis: Similar to spherocytosis but smear shows elliptocytes. Mild form.

Haemolytic Anaemias Due to Defective Metabolism

  1. Glucose-6-Phosphate Dehydrogenase (G6PD) Deficiency: Susceptible to oxidant stresses (drugs like anti-malarials, sulphonamides, fava beans). Sex-linked inheritance.
    Findings: Deficient enzyme, fragmented cells, blister cells, Heinz bodies.
  2. Pyruvate Kinase (PK) Deficiency: Reduced ATP formation causing rigid cells.
    Findings: Positive autohaemolysis test (not corrected by glucose), poikilocytes.

üß¨ Haemolytic Anaemias Due to Hb Abnormalities

Sickle-Cell Anaemia

Abnormal haemoglobin S (HbS) is formed by substitution of valine for glutamic acid in position 6 of the beta chain. HbS is insoluble and oxidises under reduced oxygen tension, distorting cells into sickle shapes.

  • Sickle cell disease (homozygous): Severe haemolytic anaemia, painful crises. Positive sickling test. Electrophoresis shows HbS and variable HbF, no HbA.
  • Sickle cell trait (heterozygous): Benign condition. Electrophoresis shows both HbS and HbA.

Thalassaemias

Reduced capacity to produce one type of globin chain.

  • Beta-thalassaemia major (Homozygous): Severe anaemia 3‚Äì6 months after birth. Enlarged liver/spleen. Iron overload. Lab findings: Severe microcytic hypochromic anaemia, target cells, increased reticulocytes, electrophoresis shows mostly HbF.

üè• Other Anaemias

Autoimmune Haemolytic Anaemias (AIHA)

Occur due to production of antibodies against patient's own red cells.

  • Warm AIHA: Antibodies react at 37¬∞C (IgG). Positive Direct Coombs' test. Spherocytosis.
  • Cold AIHA: Antibodies active at 4¬∞C (IgM). Agglutination in cold weather.
  • Iso-immune: Transfusion reactions or Haemolytic disease of newborn.
  • Drug-induced: Antibodies against drugs (e.g. penicillin).

Rare Types

  • Paroxysmal Nocturnal Haemoglobinuria (PNH): Acquired defect making cells susceptible to complement lysis. Positive Ham's test.
  • Paroxysmal Cold Haemoglobinuria (PCH): Biphasic Donath-Landsteiner antibody. Lysis at 37¬∞C after cold exposure.

Aplastic Anaemia

Results from aplasia of bone marrow causing pancytopenia. Can be primary (idiopathic) or secondary (radiation, chemicals, drugs, viral hepatitis).

Laboratory findings: Normocytic/macrocytic normochromic red cells, low reticulocyte count, leucopenia, thrombocytopenia, and hypoplastic bone marrow (fatty degeneration).
