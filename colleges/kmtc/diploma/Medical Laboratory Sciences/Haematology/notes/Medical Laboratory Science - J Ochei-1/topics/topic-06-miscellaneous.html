üî¥ Reticulocyte Count

Reticulocytes, the young red cells that have lost their nuclei but not the cytoplasmic RNA, appear in the peripheral blood as polychromatic red cells when stained with a Romanowsky stain. However, reticulocytes are demonstrated with a supra-vital stain, when the RNA appears as a network of strands or granules.

Staining Solution for Supra-vital staining

Brilliant cresyl blue or New methylene blue is used. Sodium citrate in the staining solution prevents coagulation when capillary specimen is used, and sodium chloride provides an isotonicity to preserve the red cells.

üìù

Technique

  1. Mix two drops of blood and two drops of the staining solution in a test tube.
  2. Allow to stand at room temperature for 15-20 minutes or at 37¬∞C for 10 minutes.
  3. Gently tap the tube to remix the contents.
  4. With a drop of the mixture, prepare a blood film in the same manner as for peripheral smear.
  5. Allow the film to dry completely.
  6. Examine under the oil-immersion objective (X 100). The reticular material (RNA) is precipitated by the dye and is stained blue-black. Mature red cells appear pale green (Fig. 6.1).

Calculation

$$\% \text{Reticulocytes} = \frac{\text{Number of reticulocytes counted}}{\text{Number of red cells + reticulocytes counted}} \times 100$$

Miller-eyepiece Method for Counting

Alternatively, a special eyepiece, called Miller eyepiece can be used (Fig 6.2). The small square occupies one-ninth of the large square. Count the red cells in the small square and reticulocytes in the whole area of the large square. Count 10‚Äì20 fields showing an even distribution of cells.

$$\% \text{Reticulocytes} = \frac{\text{Number of reticulocytes}}{\text{Number of reticulocytes + (Red cells} \times 9)} \times 100$$

Normal range: Adults 0.2‚Äì2 %; New born babies 2.5‚Äì6 %.

üé® Special Staining Techniques

Staining of Heinz Bodies

Heinz bodies are denatured molecules of haemoglobin. They can be stained supravitally with methyl violet or crystal violet. They are seen as intense purple granules, 1‚Äì3 microns in size, situated close to the periphery of the red cells. Heinz bodies are absent in a normal blood specimen. They may appear after splenectomy, in hyposplenism or in haemolytic anaemias e.g. due to G6PD deficiency.

Staining of Siderocytes (Demonstration of Pappenheimer Bodies)

Siderocytes are red cells containing Pappenheimer bodies. Pappenheimer bodies are small, dense bodies of free iron, which can be demonstrated by a positive 'Prussian blue reaction'. The bodies stain deep blue-black.

They are rarely seen in a normal blood specimen. They are found in disorders associated with impaired haemoglobin synthesis (e.g. thalassaemia), in lead poisoning, in some haemolytic conditions, or after splenectomy.

Peroxidase Staining

This staining reaction is used to differentiate between the cells of lymphoid series and those of myeloid and monocyte series. The granules in both the latter series of cells contain peroxidase which liberates oxygen from hydrogen peroxide and oxidises benzidine. A blue colour indicates a positive reaction.

A positive peroxidase reaction is indicated by the presence of blue-back granules in the cytoplasm. Early granulocyte precursors show a weak, localised reaction, becoming stronger and more generalised with increasing cell maturity. Monocytes may be negative, but sometimes show a weak positive reaction. Normal lymphoid and erythroid cells are invariably negative.

Periodic-Acid Schiff (PAS) Reaction

This reaction depends on the liberation of carbohydrate radicals from compounds, such as glycogen; and their oxidisation to aldehydes by the Schiff reagent. A positive reaction is indicated by a bright red colour.

  • Neutrophils: React most strongly showing large amounts of magenta coloured granules.
  • Lymphocytes: Show a few fine or coarse PAS positive granules.
  • Normoblasts: Cytoplasm does not normally stain. Positivity seen in erythroleukaemia, thalassaemia, iron deficiency anaemia.
  • Lymphoblasts: Positive granules are enormous in acute lymphocytic leukaemia.

Neutrophil (Leucocyte) Alkaline Phosphatase (NAP/LAP)

Leucocyte alkaline phosphatase (LAP) activity reflects intracellular metabolic activity. The enzyme is located in cytoplasmic granules. This enzyme activity is greatly increased in leucocytosis; and is mainly associated with bacterial infections. It is particularly useful in differentiating leukaemias from leukaemoid reactions.

Alkaline phosphatase activity is indicated by the presence of bright blue granules in the cytoplasm of mature neutrophils. Count at least 100, preferably 200, neutrophils. Each cell is rated 0-4 on the basis of intensity of the stain.

ü¶¥ Examination of Bone Marrow

Bone marrow examination is useful in the assessment of composition, cellularity and maturation of haemopoietic cells.

Site of Aspiration: The sternum and the iliac crest are the most common sites. In infants and young children the medial aspect of the upper end of the tibia may be used.

The normal ratio of myeloid/erythroid cells ranges from 2.5:1 to 5:1.

ü©∏ Investigation of Red Cell Disorders

Test for Sickle Cells

If whole blood is exposed to reduced oxygen tension with the help of a reducing agent (2% sodium metabisulphite), sickling can be demonstrated. In sickle cell trait, the red cells lose their characteristic morphology and some cells may appear crescent-shaped.

Osmotic Fragility Test

This test determines the resistance of red cells to haemolysis in various concentrations of hypotonic saline solutions (Fig. 6.3). The ability of erythrocytes to absorb water without lysis depends on the ratio of volume to surface area of the cells.

üßÆ

Calculation

$$\text{Percentage haemolysis} = \frac{\text{Absorbance of test}}{\text{Absorbance of 100 \% lysis type}} \times 100$$

Interpretation: Osmotic fragility is increased (resistance reduced) in hereditary spherocytosis. It is reduced (resistance increased) in thalassaemia, iron deficiency anaemia, and liver disease.

Autohaemolysis Test

When fresh normal blood is incubated for 24 to 48 hours, some lysis occurs. In hereditary spherocytosis, autohaemolysis is positive (>2.5%) and is corrected with glucose. In pyruvate kinase deficiency, it is positive but is not corrected with glucose.

HbF Estimation (Alkali Denaturation)

HbF is more resistant to denaturation by alkali than HbA. The test involves alkali denaturation followed by precipitation of denatured HbA. The unaltered HbF in the filtrate is measured.

Normal range: 0.5 to 1.7 % of total haemoglobin. Increased in Beta-thalassaemia major.

Haemoglobin Electrophoresis

At an alkaline pH of 8.4 to 8.6, haemoglobin is negatively charged and migrates towards the anode. Structural variations cause different migration rates, allowing detection of abnormal haemoglobins (HbS, HbC, etc.) using cellulose acetate medium.

G-6-PD Screening Test

Uses a dye reduction test (Nitro blue tetrazolium). Normal G6PD activity reduces the dye to a dark brown colour. No color change indicates deficiency.

Ham's Test (Acidified Serum Test)

Diagnostic for Paroxysmal Nocturnal Haemoglobinuria (PNH). PNH red cells are lysed by complement in acidified serum.

Donath-Landsteiner Antibody Test

Diagnostic for Paroxysmal Cold Haemoglobinuria (PCH). It detects a biphasic IgG autoantibody that binds at 0¬∞C and causes lysis at 37¬∞C in the presence of complement.

Spectroscopic Examination

Used to detect abnormal haemoglobin pigments. Absorption bands appear at specific wavelengths.

  • Methaemoglobin: Bands at 630 nm (red region). Disappears with cyanide.
  • Sulphhaemoglobin: Band at 618 nm. Does not disappear with cyanide.
  • Carboxyhaemoglobin: Similar to oxyhaemoglobin but bands do not change with reducing agents.

üîé Other Investigations

Demonstration of LE Cells

An LE cell is a neutrophil which contains a basophilic, opaque, homogeneous mass in its cytoplasm (ingested nuclear material). It is formed when the LE factor (IgG antibody to nucleoprotein) reacts with nuclear protein. It is associated with Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE).

Latex Agglutination Test for LE Factor

Uses latex particles coated with deoxyribonucleoprotein (DNP). Agglutination indicates the presence of LE factor.
