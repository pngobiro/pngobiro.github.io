‚öôÔ∏è Physiology of Haemostasis

Hemostasis is the arrest of blood flow and control of haemorrhage from an injured blood vessel. It is the process by which the blood is retained within the vascular system. Haemostasis is initiated when a blood vessel is injured. The mechanism of haemostasis is very complex and involves platelets, the coagulation factors and fibrinolytic system.

Mechanism of Haemostasis

Haemostasis involves a series of delicately balanced physical and biochemical changes following an injury to a blood vessel (Fig. 7.1(a)).

The entire mechanism of haemostasis can be divided into three parts:

  1. Extravascular effects: Physical effects include the attempts of the tissue surrounding the injury site to mechanically close the break. Biochemical effects involve substances released from tissues.
  2. Vascular effects:
    • Vasoconstriction: Spontaneous response prolonged by serotonin.
    • Platelet plug: Platelets adhere to exposed collagen, release ADP, and aggregate to form a haemostatic plug.
  3. Intravascular effects: Complex sequence of reactions involving activation of intrinsic and extrinsic pathways leading to fibrin clot formation.

Function of Platelets

  • Form a platelet plug: Physical obstruction to blood loss.
  • Help in activation of coagulation factors: Produce Platelet Factor 3 (PF3) and contain factors II, V, VIII, IX, XII, XIII.
  • Maintain endothelial lining: Plug gaps in endothelial lining.

Properties of Factors

  • Fibrinogen Group (I, V, VIII, XIII): Consumed during coagulation (absent in serum). Increase during inflammation/pregnancy. V and VIII are labile in stored plasma.
  • Prothrombin Group (II, VII, IX, X): Vitamin K dependent. Require Ca++. Not consumed (except II). Stable in stored plasma.
  • Contact Group (XI, XII, PK): Stable, not consumed.

Pathways

The coagulation process involves two pathways leading to a common pathway (Fig. 7.2).

  1. Intrinsic Pathway: Initiated by contact with foreign surface (collagen/glass). Involves XII, XI, IX, VIII.
  2. Extrinsic Pathway: Initiated by tissue damage releasing tissue thromboplastin. Involves factor VII.
  3. Common Pathway: Both pathways activate Factor X. Xa + V + Ca++ + PF3 convert Prothrombin (II) to Thrombin. Thrombin converts Fibrinogen (I) to Fibrin.

Fibrinolysis

The physiological process of removing unwanted, insoluble deposits (fibrin clots) is called fibrinolysis. Plasminogen is converted to plasmin, which digests fibrin.

$$ \text{Plasminogen} \xrightarrow{\text{Activators}} \text{Plasmin} $$ $$ \text{Fibrin polymer} \xrightarrow{\text{Plasmin}} \text{soluble fragments} $$

‚ö†Ô∏è Disorders of Coagulation

Inherited Disorders

  1. Haemophilia A (Classical): Factor VIII:C deficiency. Sex-linked (males affected). Lab: Prolonged PTT, Normal PT/BT/Platelets.
  2. Haemophilia B (Christmas Disease): Factor IX deficiency. Sex-linked. Clinically similar to Haemophilia A.
  3. von Willebrand Syndrome: Deficiency of vWF (carrier for VIII:C and aids platelet adhesion). Bleeding from mucous membranes. Lab: Prolonged BT and PTT.

Acquired Disorders

  1. Vitamin K Deficiency: Affects factors II, VII, IX, X. Causes: Malabsorption, liver disease, coumarin therapy.
  2. Heparin Therapy: Inhibits IIa, Xa, IXa, XIa, XIIa. Monitored by APTT.
  3. Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC): Widespread activation of coagulation, consumption of factors/platelets. Lab: Prolonged PT/PTT, Low platelets, Schistocytes.

Platelet Disorders

Inherited

  • Glanzmann's thrombasthenia: Aggregation defect. Normal count.
  • Bernard-Soulier syndrome: Adhesion defect (large platelets).

Quantitative

  • Thrombocytopenia: Decreased production or increased destruction.
  • Thrombocytosis: Reactive or myeloproliferative.

üî¨ Tests for Coagulation Function

Bleeding Time

Measures platelet function and capillary integrity.

  • Ivy Method: Forearm, cuff at 40mmHg. Normal: 2‚Äì6 min.
  • Duke Method: Earlobe/finger. Normal: 0‚Äì6 min.

Clotting Time (Lee-White)

Qualitative measurement of intrinsic pathway. Normal: 5‚Äì10 min.

Clot Retraction

Indicates platelet function. Normal: 58‚Äì97%.

$$\text{Clot retraction (\%)} = \frac{\text{Volume of serum expressed from 10 ml blood} \times 10}{100 - \text{Haematocrit \%}} \times 100$$

One-Stage Prothrombin Time (PT)

Measures extrinsic pathway (VII, X, V, II, I). Uses Tissue Thromboplastin + Calcium.

$$\text{Prothrombin ratio} = \frac{\text{Patient's clotting time}}{\text{Control clotting time}}$$

Prothrombin Consumption Index (PCI)

Measures intrinsic factor activity/platelet function.

$$\text{PCI} = \frac{\text{Plasma clotting time}}{\text{Serum clotting time}} \times 100$$

Normal range: <30%.

Activated Partial Thromboplastin Time (APTT)

Measures intrinsic pathway. Uses Kaolin/Phospholipid + Calcium. Normal: 35‚Äì43 seconds. Prolonged in Haemophilia.

Factor Assays

Uses factor-deficient substrate plasma to determine specific factor levels (e.g., Factor VIII assay for Haemophilia A).
