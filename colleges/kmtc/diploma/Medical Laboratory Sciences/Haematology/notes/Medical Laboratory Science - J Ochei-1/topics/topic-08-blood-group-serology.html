üß¨ Blood Group Genetics

Each species of animals, including humans, has certain inherited antigens on the surface of the red cells which are unique for that species. These are known as isoantigens. Similarly, certain antigens, the alloantigens are common to some, but not all, members of that particular species. Blood group serology involves the detection of these antigens and their antibodies.

All the antigenic substances present on the red cells of an individual are inherited. The unit of inheritance is a gene and each antigen is controlled by a gene for that antigen.

Key Genetic Terms

  • Allele or allelomorph: Two or more genes responsible for the same characteristic occupying the same position on the chromosomes.
  • Homozygous: A person who has inherited same allelic genes from both parents e.g. having both blue eyed colour genes.
  • Heterozygous: A person who has inherited different allelic genes for a trait e.g. having a blue and brown- eye colour genes.
  • Dominant gene: Certain alleles are stronger than other alleles. A dominant gene expresses itself in both homozygous and heterozygous states.
  • Recessive gene: The recessive genes are expressed only when their dominant allele is absent e.g. only in the homozygous state.
  • Co-dominant gene: In a co-dominant gene, various alleles for the same character are equally dominant e.g. alleles for a particular blood group system.
  • Genotype: The genetic make up of an individual or a cell.
  • Phenotype: The observable characteristics of an individual or a cell. e.g. an individual of group BO genotype would be expressed as group B because O gene is recessive.

Blood Group Antibodies

Specific blood group antibodies develop in response to the antigenic stimulation by a particular blood group antigen.

Alloantibodies

Antibodies present in some members of a species, but not all. They can be naturally occurring (e.g., Anti-A in group B) or immune antibodies resulting from immunisation through transfusion or pregnancy (e.g., Rh antibodies).

Autoantibodies

Antibodies induced by an antigen in the same individual. Divided into Warm (37¬∞C, IgG) and Cold (4¬∞C, IgM) types.

üÖ∞Ô∏è ABO Blood Group System

The ABO system is the most important blood group system in blood transfusion and organ transplantation. It consists of four blood groups or phenotypes: A, B, AB and O.

AB AB AB None

Laws of Inheritance

  1. It is not possible that an offspring can possess antigen A, B or both unless it is inherited from one or both parents.
  2. A parent of blood group AB cannot produce an offspring of group O. Similarly, a parent of blood group O can not produce AB offspring.

H Substance and Bombay Group

The H gene produces H substance, a precursor for A and B antigens. Group O individuals have H antigen. The rare Bombay group (Oh) individuals are homozygous for the silent allele (hh) and lack the H antigen. Thus, they cannot form A or B antigens even if they possess the genes. They type as group O but have Anti-A, Anti-B, and Anti-H in their serum.

Landsteiner's Rule

Individuals whose red cells lack A, B or both antigens have the respective antibody in their sera.

03

üß¨ Rhesus (Rh) Grouping System

The Rh blood group system involves six related blood group factors C, D, E, c, d and e. The factor D is the most immunogenic. If the blood sample shows the presence of Rh (D) antigen, the individual is termed as Rh Positive (85% of population); if it is absent, as Rh negative.

The D$^{u}$ Antigen (Weak D)

Some D antigens react weakly with anti-D sera. This incomplete D antigen is termed as D$^{u}$. It does not usually react with complete anti-D, but reacts with incomplete anti-D antisera in the antiglobulin test. Donors with D$^{u}$ are considered Rh positive, but recipients are considered Rh negative.

Rh Antibodies

  • Complete (Saline-active): IgM type. Can agglutinate red cells in saline.
  • Incomplete (Albumin-active): IgG type. Cannot agglutinate in saline due to zeta potential. Require high protein medium or antihuman globulin to demonstrate reaction.

üß™ Blood Grouping Methods

ABO Grouping

Cell grouping: Detecting antigens on red cells using known antisera (Anti-A, Anti-B).
Serum grouping: Detecting antibodies in serum using known red cells (A, B, O cells).

Method 1: The Tile Method

Performed on a white tile. One drop of antiserum is mixed with one drop of 10% cell suspension. Read for agglutination.

Rh Typing Methods

  • Saline Method: Uses complete Anti-D. Can be done on slide or in tube.
  • Albumin Displacement: Uses incomplete Anti-D and 22% bovine albumin. Albumin reduces zeta potential allowing IgG to agglutinate cells.
  • Enzyme Technique: Uses proteolytic enzymes (papain, ficin) to treat cells, removing surface charge and enhancing agglutination.

üîç Antihuman Globulin (Coombs') Tests

Antiglobulin techniques are used for the detection of incomplete univalent antibodies (IgG) which cannot be detected by saline methods. The Coombs' reagent (Antihuman globulin - AHG) acts as a bridge between antibody-coated red cells, causing agglutination.

Direct Antiglobulin Test (DAT)

Detects in vivo sensitisation of red cells. Used in HDN, autoimmune haemolytic anaemia, and transfusion reactions.

Technique: Wash patient's cells ‚Üí Add AHG ‚Üí Centrifuge ‚Üí Read.

Indirect Antiglobulin Test (IAT)

Detects in vitro sensitisation (antibodies in serum). Used for antibody screening, crossmatching, and cell typing.

Technique: Incubate serum + cells ‚Üí Wash ‚Üí Add AHG ‚Üí Centrifuge ‚Üí Read.

06

üë∂ Haemolytic Disease of the New-born (HDN)

HDN, or erythroblastosis foetalis, occurs when a child inherits an antigen from the father which is absent in the mother (e.g., Rh D). The mother produces IgG antibodies against the foetal antigen, which cross the placenta and destroy foetal red cells.

Clinical Significance

Most common in Rh-D negative mothers with Rh-D positive foetus. First child usually unaffected. Subsequent pregnancies at risk. Prevention: Rh immune globulin (RMG) given to mother within 72 hours of delivery.

üåê Other Blood Group Systems

Lewis (Le)

Antigens adsorbed onto red cells from plasma. Antibodies (Anti-Le$^a$, Anti-Le$^b$) are IgM and can cause transfusion reactions but rarely HDN.

I System

Adult cells have 'I', cord cells have 'i'. Anti-I is a common cold autoantibody. Anti-i is associated with infectious mononucleosis.

P System

Antigens P1, P2. Anti-P1 is common cold antibody. Anti-P associated with PCH.

MNSs

MN and Ss antigens. Anti-M/N are usually natural IgM. Anti-S/s are immune IgG.

Kell (K)

Strongly immunogenic (next to D). Anti-K is IgG, causes severe reactions and HDN.

Duffy (Fy)

Fy$^a$, Fy$^b$. Antibodies are IgG. Fy(a-b-) phenotype common in West Africans (malaria resistance).

Others: Kidd (Jk), LW, Lutheran (Lu), Xg (Sex-linked).
