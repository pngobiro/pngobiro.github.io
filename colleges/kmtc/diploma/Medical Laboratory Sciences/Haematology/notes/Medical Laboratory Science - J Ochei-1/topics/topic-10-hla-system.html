üîç Overview of the HLA System

Most of the blood group antigens belong exclusively to red cells except the ABO antigens which are present on almost all body cells. However, a single highly complex antigenic system called HLA is responsible for most of the antigens possessed by tissue cells, leucocytes and platelets. This HLA system, originally called Human leucocyte Locus A, is now known as Human Leucocyte Antigen system.

The HLA system is controlled by a supergene called Major Histocompatibility Complex (MHC) which includes several closely linked loci on the human chromosome 6. The antigens controlled by alleles on these loci are found on all nucleated cells in the body, and also on some non-nucleated cells and in some body fluids.

The antigens of the HLA system are proteins which are involved in various aspects of immunological recognition and reaction. In addition, these antigens play a major role in organ transplantation and in blood transfusion.

02

üß¨ Proteins of the HLA System

The MHC genes encode proteins of the HLA system. These genes have six main subregions: HLA-A, HLA-B, HLA-C, HLA-DR, HLA-DQ and HLA-DP. Each subregion encodes at least one glycoprotein.

On the basis of structure, genetic origin and function, these proteins of the MHC genes are grouped into three classes: Class I, Class II and Class III (Fig. 10.1).

Class I Molecules

There are three Class I molecules in the human MHC: HLA-A, HLA-B and HLA-C. Each Class I molecule is made up of two polypeptide chains of unequal size (Fig. 10.2).

  • Heavy chain ($\alpha$-chain): A glycoprotein which spans across the cell membrane with its amino-terminus outside the cell. Encoded on chromosome 6. Highly polymorphic due to variable region at amino-terminus.
  • $\beta$-microglobulin: The smaller chain associated with the extracellular region of the heavy chain. Encoded on chromosome 15. Necessary for cell surface expression.

Function of Class I Molecules

Class I molecules are expressed on almost all nucleated cells and platelets. They play a major role in recognizing endogenous antigens (e.g., viral antigens, tumour antigens). Cytotoxic T lymphocytes recognize the antigen only in conjunction with the Class I molecule. This interaction triggers cellular and humoral immune responses, including rejection of transplants (alloantigens) or reactions to self-antigens (autoimmune disease).

Class II Molecules

The Class II molecules include the proteins of the HLA-DR, HLA-DQ and HLA-DP regions. They are glycoproteins consisting of two chains, an $\alpha$-chain and a $\beta$-chain (Fig. 10.3). Both chains are encoded within the MHC on chromosome 6 and extend beyond the cell membrane. They are highly polymorphic.

Function: Expressed on cells of the immune system (not on resting T lymphocytes). They bind to soluble and particulate exogenous antigens and present them to cytotoxic T lymphocytes.

Class III Molecules

This is a heterogeneous group ('non-I non-II') including:

  • Complement components: C2, C4 (C4A, C4B) and Factor B (BF). Involved in inflammation, opsonisation, and cytotoxicity.
  • 21-hydroxylase (21OH): Enzyme for cortisol synthesis.
  • Tumour necrosis factor (TNF): Cytotoxic for tumour cells, regulates cellular growth.

üë™ Inheritance of MHC

The inheritance of MHC genes follows Mendel's laws. The MHC loci are closely linked, so the haplotype (single strand of chromosome with MHC loci) is inherited from each parent. The two alleles are co-dominant.

üí°

Genotype Combinations

If father's haplotypes are ab and mother's are cd, the four possible offspring genotypes are: ac, ad, bc, bd.

Therefore, if there are five children in a family, at least two siblings will possess identical HLA.

üî¨ Techniques for HLA Typing

I. Lymphocyte Microcytotoxicity Assay

Principle: Living lymphocytes are exposed to antisera and complement. If the specific antigen is present, the antibody binds, complement is fixed, and the cell dies. Dead cells are detected by vital stains (trypan blue or eosin) which penetrate only dead cells.

Requirements

  • Lymphocytes: Separated using Ficoll-Hypaque density gradient. B lymphocytes (enriched via nylon-wool column) are needed for HLA-DQ/DR typing.
  • Typing Sera: Obtained from multiparous women or multitransfused patients.
  • Complement: Usually rabbit serum.
üìù

Technique Summary

  1. Mix lymphocyte suspension + Antiserum. Incubate.
  2. Add Complement. Incubate.
  3. Add Vital Dye.
  4. Read microscopically (% dead cells). >85% dead = Strong Positive.

II. Mixed Lymphocyte Culture (MLC) Technique

Principle: T lymphocytes recognise antigens in vitro and respond by cell division (proliferation). The degree of DNA replication is measured using radioactive thymidine ($^3$H-thymidine). This measures differences in Class II molecules.

üß™

Process

Donor lymphocytes (Stimulator cells) are treated with Mitomycin-C to prevent replication. They are mixed with Recipient lymphocytes (Responder cells). After 5-6 days incubation, radioactive thymidine is added. High radioactivity indicates high proliferation (incompatibility). Low radioactivity indicates compatibility.

üè• Role of HLA in Transplantation

Survival of a graft depends on overcoming the immunological rejection caused by recognition of foreign alloantigens. This can be achieved by:

1. Immunosuppressive Therapy

Drugs such as prednisone, azathioprine, and cyclosporin A suppress the immune response.

2. Histocompatibility Matching

Selecting a donor with HLA optimally matching the recipient. Grafts between HLA identical siblings have the highest survival rates.

  • Kidney Transplantation: Best results with identical AB and DR antigens. C antigen has little significance. In living donors, MLC compatibility is determined.
  • Bone Marrow Transplantation: Requires HLA-identical or related donors (usually siblings) compatible by both cytotoxicity and MLC techniques.
  • Heart and Liver Transplants: Antibody screening test is the major decisive factor. If negative, transplant usually proceeds without full compatibility testing due to urgency.
