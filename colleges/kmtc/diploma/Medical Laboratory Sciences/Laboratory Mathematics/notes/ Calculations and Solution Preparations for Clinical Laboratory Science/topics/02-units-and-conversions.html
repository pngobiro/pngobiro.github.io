    Units of Measurement & Conversions

    01

    SI Units

    For the units of measure, it is a must that you memorize several numbers, units, and conversion factors, especially if a physician is dealing with laboratory results or interpreting a clinical trial.

    For the SI units, we have the base quantity, selected derived, and selected acceptance non-SI.

    The SI unit is founded on seven base units for seven base quantities and they are assumed to be mutually independent. So in short, a base quantity is an original unit of measure.

    üìäSI Base Units
    Base QuantityNameSymbol
    LengthMeterm
    MassKilogramkg
    TimeSeconds
    Electric currentAmpereA
    Thermodynamic temperatureKelvinK
    Amount of substanceMolemol
    Luminous intensityCandelacd

    Selected Derived Quantity

    A selected derived is a derived quantity and is defined based on a combination of base quantities and has a derived unit that is the exponent, product or quotient of these base units.

    Example, let's take the Frequency. The name is Hertz and the symbol is Hz, but the base unit for frequency is $s^{-1}$.

    Selected Acceptance Non-SI

    We also have the selected acceptance non-SI such as the minute, hour, day, liter in volume, and then the Angstrom.

    üí°Did You Know?

    The SI unit is also known as the International System of Units or sometimes they are referred to us as the System International. Actually, the SI unit is the modern form of the metric system and it's the world's most widely used system of measurement.

    It was in 1966 in which your IFCC (International Federation of Clinical Chemistry and Laboratory Medicine) recommended the use of selected or favored SI units for clinical laboratory.

    03

    Conversion Factors

    This table is very important for us Medical Technologists and also to the doctors who are interpreting laboratory results.

    Usually laboratory tests are performed as a tool for diagnosing diseases and we medtechs are helping the doctors to diagnose those diseases by having accurate and precise laboratory results.

    However, a common issue is the reporting units for laboratory test because some of the laboratories use SI unit while others use conventional unit.

    So in order to have a clear distinction between the units and the interpretation of results, conversion factors are generated to identify and then interpret the values in either conventional or SI units.

    The other name for your Conventional Unit is the Customary Unit or Gravimetric Unit.

    ‚ú®Conversion Rules

    Conventional to SI: MULTIPLY (by the conversion factor)

    SI to Conventional: DIVIDE (by the conversion factor)

    Example: Glucose. The conversion factor for glucose is 0.0555. So that means if your result is in conventional unit and if you want it to be reflected in SI unit, you just have to multiply it.

    But if you have an SI unit and you want to convert it to conventional unit, what you are going to do is you just have to divide the SI unit with the conversion factor.

    Temperature Conversions

    For temperature conversions, we have the following formula:

    Formulas
    • To convert Celsius to Fahrenheit:
      $$^\circ F = \frac{9}{5}(^\circ C) + 32$$
    • To convert Fahrenheit to Celsius:
      $$^\circ C = \frac{5}{9}(^\circ F - 32)$$
    • To convert Celsius to Kelvin:
      $$K = ^\circ C + 273$$
