    Ratios, Dilutions, and Proportions

    01

    Proportionally Equivalent Solutions

    This deals with the calculations of two solutions whose concentrations do not change.

    We have examples such as weight per volume (w/v), weight over weight (w/w), and volume per volume (v/v). The most common is weight per volume (w/v).

    General Formula
    $$\frac{w_1}{v_1} = \frac{w_2}{v_2}$$
    02

    Dilution Equations

    On the other hand, when we make dilution, the volume of the original solution is less than the volume of the diluted solution. Because when we make dilutions, we add diluents.

    üí°Key Concept

    The amount of the solute remains the same in this dilution, but there is a change in concentration.

    Formula
    $$V_1 \times C_1 = V_2 \times C_2$$
    03

    Ratio vs. Dilution

    Ratio

    Ratios can be used to express part-per-part or part-per-whole, and are written with a colon between figures.

    Example: 9 parts blood to 1 part sodium citrate anticoagulant.

    • Ratio of Blood to Anticoagulant = 9:1
    • Ratio of Anticoagulant to Blood = 1:9

    A ratio indicates how many times one number contains another.

    Dilution

    A dilution can be expressed as a fraction of specimen volume divided by the sum of specimen volume and diluent.

    Formula
    $$\text{Dilution} = \frac{\text{solute}}{\text{total volume of solution}}$$
    $$\text{Solution} = \text{solute} + \text{solvent (diluent)}$$

    Dilution Factor

    Our dilution factor is the reciprocal of the dilution.

    04

    Sample Problems

    1. Proportionally Equivalent

    Problem: If a 5.0 dL solution contains 4.5 mg of bilirubin, how much bilirubin would there be in a 2.0 dL aliquot of the solution?

    ‚ñ∂Show Solution

    Formula: $\frac{w_1}{v_1} = \frac{w_2}{v_2}$

    Given:

    • $w_1 = 4.5 \text{ mg}$
    • $v_1 = 5.0 \text{ dL}$
    • $v_2 = 2.0 \text{ dL}$
    • $w_2 = ?$

    Calculation:

    $$w_2 = \frac{w_1 \times v_2}{v_1} = \frac{4.5 \text{ mg} \times 2.0 \text{ dL}}{5.0 \text{ dL}}$$
    $$w_2 = 1.8 \text{ mg}$$

    Answer: 1.8 mg of bilirubin

    2. Total Carbon Dioxide

    Problem: The total carbon dioxide concentration of a serum sample is 25 mEq/L. How many milliequivalents of $CO_2$ are contained in the 10-mL collection tube?

    ‚ñ∂Show Solution

    Formula: $\frac{n_1}{v_1} = \frac{n_2}{v_2}$

    Given:

    • $n_1 = 25 \text{ mEq}$
    • $v_1 = 1 \text{ L} = 1000 \text{ mL}$
    • $v_2 = 10 \text{ mL}$
    • $n_2 = ?$

    Calculation:

    $$n_2 = \frac{25 \text{ mEq} \times 10 \text{ mL}}{1000 \text{ mL}}$$
    $$n_2 = 0.25 \text{ mEq}$$

    Answer: 0.25 mEq

    3. Dilution Equation

    Problem: What volume of a 0.5 M glucose solution can be made from 100 mL of a 3 M stock solution?

    ‚ñ∂Show Solution

    Formula: $V_1 \times C_1 = V_2 \times C_2$

    Given:

    • $V_1 = 100 \text{ mL}$
    • $C_1 = 3 \text{ M}$
    • $C_2 = 0.5 \text{ M}$
    • $V_2 = ?$

    Calculation:

    $$V_2 = \frac{V_1 \times C_1}{C_2} = \frac{100 \text{ mL} \times 3 \text{ M}}{0.5 \text{ M}}$$
    $$V_2 = 600 \text{ mL}$$

    Answer: 600 mL

    4. Original Concentration

    Problem: A 3.5 L solution was diluted with 1.5 L of water to create a 0.5 M solution with a total volume of 5.0 L. What was the concentration of the original solution?

    ‚ñ∂Show Solution

    Formula: $V_1 \times C_1 = V_2 \times C_2$

    Given:

    • $V_1 = 3.5 \text{ L}$
    • $V_2 = 5.0 \text{ L}$
    • $C_2 = 0.5 \text{ M}$
    • $C_1 = ?$

    Calculation:

    $$C_1 = \frac{V_2 \times C_2}{V_1} = \frac{5.0 \text{ L} \times 0.5 \text{ M}}{3.5 \text{ L}}$$
    $$C_1 = 0.7 \text{ M}$$

    Answer: 0.7 M

    6. Serial Dilution (The Tube F Problem)

    Problem: 6 tubes were prepared and labelled A to F. Tube A contains 10 mL of pure/undiluted penicillin. 1 mL of penicillin from tube A is transferred to tube B with 9 mL of diluent awaiting and mixed. 1 mL of the mixture from tube B was transferred to tube C with 9 mL of diluent. Same procedure was done until tube F. What is the final dilution of tube F?

    ‚ñ∂Show Solution

    This is a Serial Dilution where the volumes used are constant.

    Step 1: Determine the dilution of the first transfer (Tube B)

    • Solute (from Tube A) = 1 mL
    • Diluent = 9 mL
    • Total Volume = 1 mL + 9 mL = 10 mL
    • Dilution = $\frac{1}{10}$

    Step 2: Calculate subsequent dilutions

    Since the same procedure (1 mL transfer to 9 mL diluent) is repeated for each tube, the dilution factor for each step is $\frac{1}{10}$. We multiply the previous tube's dilution by the new dilution.

    TubeCalculationFinal Dilution
    APure / UndilutedNeat
    B$1 \times \frac{1}{10}$1/10
    C$\frac{1}{10} \times \frac{1}{10}$1/100
    D$\frac{1}{100} \times \frac{1}{10}$1/1,000
    E$\frac{1}{1000} \times \frac{1}{10}$1/10,000
    F$\frac{1}{10000} \times \frac{1}{10}$1/100,000

    Answer: The final dilution of tube F is 1/100,000.
