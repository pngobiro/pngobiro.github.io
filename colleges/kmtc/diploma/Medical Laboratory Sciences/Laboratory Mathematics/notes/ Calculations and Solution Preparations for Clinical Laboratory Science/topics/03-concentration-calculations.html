    Units of Concentration and Solution Preparations

    We have different ways to express the concentrations of our solutions such as molarity, molality, and normality.

    01

    Molarity (M)

    Molarity is the number of moles (mol) of solute per liter of solution.

    ‚ö†Ô∏èImportant Note

    Take note of this one. We are dealing with the solution when we are talking about molarity. So solution would mean solute + solvent.

    Formula
    $$M = \frac{\text{mol}}{\text{L}}$$

    The formula for getting the number of moles is:

    $$\text{moles} = \frac{\text{grams of solute}}{\text{MW of solute}}$$
    02

    Molality (m)

    Molality is the number of moles (mol) of solute per kilogram of solvent.

    So this time, in molality, we are only dealing with the solvent. It is expressed in small letter m.

    Formula
    $$m = \frac{\text{mol}}{\text{kg}}$$
    03

    Normality (N)

    Normality is a term of concentration based on the ability of some substances to release hydrogen or hydroxide ion in a solution. It is expressed as Equivalents per liter.

    Formula
    $$N = \frac{\text{Eq}}{\text{L}}$$

    Shortcut Formula

    If we know already our molarity value, we can solve now for our normality.

    $$N = M \times \text{Valence}$$

    We can also transpose the formula:

    $$M = \frac{N}{\text{Valence}}$$

    Understanding Valence

    Valence is the number of replaceable hydrogen ions (if acid) or hydroxide ions (if base). For salts (mixture of cation and anion), the valence refers to the number of replaceable anions.

    üß™Valence Examples
    SubstanceTypeValence
    $HCl$Acid1 (1 Hydrogen)
    $H_2SO_4$Acid2 (2 Hydrogens)
    $NaOH$Base1 (1 Hydroxide)
    $Ca(OH)_2$Base2 (2 Hydroxides)
    $NaCl$Salt1 (1 Chloride anion)

    Calculating Equivalents

    Before we solve for the actual normality (if molarity is not given), we need to get the Gram Equivalent Weight (GEW) and then the Equivalents.

    Formulas
    $$\text{Gram eq. wt.} = \frac{\text{MW of solute}}{\text{valence}}$$
    $$\text{Equivalents} = \frac{\text{grams of solute}}{\text{Gram eq. wt.}}$$
    04

    Sample Problems

    1. Molarity Calculation

    Problem: What is the molarity of a solution containing 23.4 grams of potassium chloride dissolved in 500 mL of water? (MW of KCl = 74.5 g/mol)

    ‚ñ∂Show Solution

    Given:

    • Grams of solute: 23.4 g
    • Volume of solution: 500 mL = 0.5 L
    • MW of KCl: 74.5 g/mol

    Step 1: Calculate moles

    $$\text{moles} = \frac{23.4 \text{ g}}{74.5 \text{ g/mol}} = 0.31 \text{ mol}$$

    Step 2: Calculate Molarity

    $$M = \frac{0.31 \text{ mol}}{0.5 \text{ L}} = 0.63 \text{ M}$$

    Answer: 0.63 M or 0.63 moles per liter

    2. Molality Calculation

    Problem: What is the molality of a solution containing 5.0 moles of glucose and 4.0 kg of water?

    ‚ñ∂Show Solution

    Given:

    • Moles: 5.0 moles
    • Weight of solvent: 4.0 kg

    Calculation:

    $$m = \frac{5.0 \text{ moles}}{4.0 \text{ kg}} = 1.2 \text{ molal}$$

    Answer: 1.2 molal or 1.2 moles per kg

    3. Normality Calculation

    Problem: What is the normality of a 1-L solution containing 46.3 grams of $Ca(OH)_2$? (MW of $Ca(OH)_2$ = 74.1 g/mol)

    ‚ñ∂Show Solution

    Given:

    • Grams of solute: 46.3 g
    • Volume: 1 L
    • MW: 74.1 g/mol
    • Valence ($Ca(OH)_2$): 2 (two hydroxide ions)

    Step 1: Calculate Gram Equivalent Weight (GEW)

    $$\text{GEW} = \frac{74.1 \text{ g/mol}}{2} = 37.05 \text{ g}$$

    Step 2: Calculate Equivalents

    $$\text{Eq} = \frac{46.3 \text{ g}}{37.05 \text{ g}} = 1.25 \text{ Eq}$$

    Step 3: Calculate Normality

    $$N = \frac{1.25 \text{ Eq}}{1 \text{ L}} = 1.25 \text{ N}$$

    Answer: 1.25 N

    ‚ú®Shortcut Check

    If we wanted to find Molarity from this:

    $$M = \frac{N}{\text{Valence}} = \frac{1.25}{2} = 0.625 \text{ M}$$
