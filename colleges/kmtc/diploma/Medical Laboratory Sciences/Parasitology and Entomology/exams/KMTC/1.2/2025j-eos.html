Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions (20 Marks)

1.
During microscopic examination of urine sediment, motile trophozoite was identified whose motility can be described as jerky. What is the possible parasite?
A. Trichomonas hominis
B. Trichomonas Vaginalis
C. Trichomonas foetus
D. Trichomonas tenax
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Trichomonas Vaginalis

Explanation: Trichomonas vaginalis is the only pathogenic Trichomonad found in the urogenital tract. It exhibits a characteristic jerky, twitching, or non-directional motility in wet preparations. T. hominis is intestinal, T. tenax is oral, and T. foetus is found in cattle.

2.
Stool examination revealed some remains of flagella in the cystic stage, which parasite is associated with this morphological characteristic?
A. Balantidium coli
B. Dientamoeba fragilis
C. Giardia lamblia
D. Chilomastix mensineli
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Chilomastix mensineli

Explanation: Cysts of Chilomastix mesnili (likely typo for mesnili) are lemon-shaped and characteristically contain a cytostome and the remains of flagella (often described as a "shepherd's crook" appearance) visible inside the cyst.

3.
A patient was attacked by a parasite along the lower limbs causing inflammatory reaction. What is the appropriate terminology on the effect of the parasite to the host?
A. Autoinfection
B. Retro infection
C. Infestation
D. Infection
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Infestation

Explanation: The term "infestation" is typically used to describe the presence of ectoparasites (parasites living on the surface of the host) or arthropods burrowing in the skin (like chiggers or jiggers on lower limbs), causing local inflammation. "Infection" generally implies endoparasites invading body tissues.

4.
Which of the following parasite has intracellular location in the host?
A. Trypanosoma brucei rhodesiense
B. Toxoplasma gondii
C. Entamoeba histolytica
D. Balantidium coli
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Toxoplasma gondii

Explanation: Toxoplasma gondii is an obligate intracellular parasite. Trypanosoma brucei is extracellular (in blood/tissue fluid), Entamoeba and Balantidium are typically extracellular or tissue-invasive but not obligate intracellular dwellers in the same sense.

5.
Which protozoan parasites is bi-nucleated in its trophozoite stage?
A. Trichomonas foetus
B. Endolimax nana
C. Giardia lamblia
D. Chilomastix mensinelli
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Giardia lamblia

Explanation: The trophozoite of Giardia lamblia is pear-shaped and characteristically possesses two nuclei (appearing like "eyes" on a face). The other listed parasites have a single nucleus in their trophozoite stage.

6.
A disease like malaria that constantly infect individual in a certain geographical area is known as:
A. Epidemic
B. Pandemic

Explanation: An endemic disease is one that is constantly present in a specific population or geographical area.

7.
Laboratory findings in a stool specimen revealed quandrinucleated cyst. This is a characteristic feature of a mature cyst of:
A. Giardia lamblia
B. Entamoeba histolytica
C. Entamoeba coli
D. Iodamoeba bustichilii
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Entamoeba histolytica

Explanation: While Giardia lamblia cysts also have 4 nuclei, the term "quadrinucleated cyst" in a diagnostic context is classically associated with the mature infective cyst of Entamoeba histolytica. Entamoeba coli cysts have 8 nuclei (octonucleated), and Iodamoeba has 1 nucleus.

8.
Which of the following parasites has the mouth as their natural habitat?
A. Dientamoeba fragilis
B. Chilomastix mensineli
C. Trichomonas tenax
D. Iodamoeba bustichilli
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Trichomonas tenax

Explanation: Trichomonas tenax (and Entamoeba gingivalis) inhabits the oral cavity (mouth), specifically in tartar and gum crevices. The others are intestinal.

9.
Which Non-nuclear DNA is present in protozoan parasites?
A. Karyosome
B. Basal body
C. Chromatid body
D. Kinetoplast
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Kinetoplast

Explanation: The kinetoplast is a network of circular DNA (kDNA) inside a large mitochondrion that contains many copies of the mitochondrial genome. It is a distinct feature of Kinetoplastids like Trypanosoma and Leishmania.

10.
When examining wet preparation for protozoan parasites, what is the motility of trichomonas hominis?
A. Lashing
B. Wobbling
C. Falling leaf
D. Rotational
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Wobbling

Explanation: Trichomonas hominis motility is often described as nervous, jerky, or wobbling. "Falling leaf" is specific to Giardia. "Rotational" (boring) is often Balantidium. "Lashing" is more typical of sperm or some larvae.

11.
What is the direct effect of the parasite to their host after invasion?
A. Deleterious effect of toxic substance
B. Immunological reaction
C. Mechanical injury
D. Deprivation of nutrients, fluids and metabolites
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Mechanical injury

Explanation: While parasites cause many effects, mechanical injury (e.g., blockage, pressure, tissue destruction by migration) is a primary "direct" physical effect of the parasite itself invading tissues. Nutrient deprivation is also a direct metabolic effect, but mechanical injury fits the "invasion" context well (e.g., hookworm attachment, Ascaris migration).

12.
Which of the following factor is the most determinant of parasite effect to human?
A. Genetic constitution and mutation
B. Intensity and frequency of infections
C. Site of localization
D. Presence of co-existing disease
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Intensity and frequency of infections

Explanation: Parasite load (intensity) is often the most significant factor determining whether an infection remains asymptomatic or becomes a clinical disease (e.g., in hookworm or malaria).

13.
Which parasite has an intracellular development stage in human?
A. Entamoeba Histolytica
B. Giardia lamblia
C. African trypanosomes
D. Leishmania species
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Leishmania species

Explanation: Leishmania parasites exist as intracellular amastigotes within macrophages in the human host. Entamoeba, Giardia, and African trypanosomes (in blood/lymph/CSF) are extracellular.

14.
What species of amoeba habituating small intestines tract has griding motility?
A. Isospora belli
B. Entamoeba coli
C. Iodamoeba bustichilii
D. Entamoeba hartmanni
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Isospora belli

Explanation: This question contains terminology issues. Isospora belli is the only parasite listed here that inhabits the small intestine. It is a coccidian (Sporozoa), not an amoeba, and its sporozoites can exhibit gliding motility. The other options are amoebae but inhabit the large intestine.

15.
When parasitic protozoa parasitize other protozoan it is known as?
A. Parasitism
B. Hyper parasitism
C. Mutualism
D. Commensalism
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Hyper parasitism

Explanation: Hyperparasitism is the condition where a parasite itself is infected by another parasite.

16.
What is the function of contractile vacuole in protozoa?
A. Assist in reproduction
B. Create sites of food digestion
C. Contain specific enzymes that perform various functions
D. Maintain osmotic balance by continuous water expulsion
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Maintain osmotic balance by continuous water expulsion

Explanation: Contractile vacuoles accumulate excess water entering the cell (due to osmosis in freshwater environments) and pump it out to prevent the cell from bursting (osmoregulation).

17.
The optimal pH range for maximum metabolic activity of intestinal protozoa is?
A. 2-3
B. 12-14
C. 6-8
D. 4-6
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. 6-8

Explanation: Intestinal protozoa generally thrive in the neutral to slightly alkaline environment of the intestines (pH 6-8). The stomach is too acidic (pH 2-3), and pH 12-14 is too basic.

18.
Which of the feature can be used to classify protozoa?
A. Shape.
B. Locomotory organelle
C. Number of nuclei.
D. Size.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Locomotory organelle

Explanation: The traditional classification of Phylum Protozoa is primarily based on their organs of locomotion: Sarcodina (pseudopodia), Mastigophora (flagella), Ciliophora (cilia), and Sporozoa (no locomotory organs in adult stage).

20.
On examination of a stool specimen, a trophozoite of the parasite was noted to have a clearly outlined glycogen mass that stains a deep mahogany brown with iodine. What would be the parasite?
A. Iodamoeba butschlii
B. Endolimax nana
C. Entamoeba coli
D. Entamoeba hartmanni
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Iodamoeba butschlii

Explanation: The cyst (and sometimes pre-cyst/trophozoite) of Iodamoeba butschlii is characterized by a large, compact glycogen vacuole that stains intensely (mahogany brown) with iodine. The name "Iodamoeba" refers to this affinity for iodine.

21.
Draw diagram of trophozoites Balantidium coli (5 marks)
Show Answer

Key Features for Drawing:

  • Shape: Large, ovoid or egg-shaped body.
  • Cilia: Covered in short, fine cilia.
  • Nuclei: Two distinct nuclei – a large, kidney-bean shaped Macronucleus and a small, spherical Micronucleus (often nestled in the curve of the macronucleus).
  • Cytostome: Funnel-like mouth part at the anterior end.
  • Cytopyge: Anal pore at the posterior end.
  • Vacuoles: Food vacuoles and contractile vacuoles (usually two) in the cytoplasm.

24.
State five (5) general features of Phylum protozoa (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Unicellular: They are single-celled eukaryotic organisms.
  2. Microscopic: Most are microscopic in size.
  3. Locomotion: Most possess locomotory organelles (flagella, cilia, pseudopodia).
  4. Nutrition: Can be heterotrophic (holozoic, saprophytic, parasitic) or rarely autotrophic.
  5. Reproduction: Reproduce asexually (binary fission, schizogony) and some sexually (conjugation, syngamy).
  6. Life Cycle: Many have a cyst stage (infective/resistant) and a trophozoite stage (vegetative/feeding).
25.
Describe enterotest (string test) in examination of intestinal protozoan Parasites (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Principle: A method to sample duodenal contents to detect parasites like Giardia lamblia or Strongyloides larvae without invasive intubation.
  2. Procedure: A gelatin capsule containing a coiled nylon string is swallowed by the patient. One end of the string is taped to the patient's cheek.
  3. Dissolution: The capsule dissolves in the stomach, and the string uncoils and passes into the duodenum.
  4. Retrieval: After about 4 hours (or overnight), the string is withdrawn.
  5. Examination: The bile-stained mucus adhering to the distal end of the string is squeezed out and examined microscopically (wet mount) for trophozoites.
26.
Outline the life cycle of Sarcocystis hominis (5marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Definitive Host (Human): Ingests raw or undercooked beef containing sarcocysts.
  2. Excystation: Cyst wall is digested in the small intestine, releasing bradyzoites.
  3. Gametogony: Bradyzoites invade intestinal epithelium and differentiate into male and female gametes. Fertilization occurs to form oocysts.
  4. Sporulation: Oocysts sporulate within the host intestine (rare for coccidia, but typical here).
  5. Excretion: Infective sporocysts (or thin-walled oocysts) are passed in human feces.
  6. Intermediate Host (Cattle): Ingests sporocysts from contaminated environment. Sporozoites infect muscle tissue forming sarcocysts.

28.
Explain five (5) control and preventive measures of intestinal protozoan parasites (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Sanitation: Proper disposal of human feces (use of latrines/toilets) to prevent soil and water contamination.
  2. Water Treatment: Boiling or filtering drinking water to remove cysts (chlorination alone is often ineffective against Giardia/Cryptosporidium cysts).
  3. Food Hygiene: Washing raw fruits and vegetables with clean water; proper cooking of food to kill cysts.
  4. Personal Hygiene: Hand washing with soap and water before eating and after using the toilet.
  5. Health Education: Educating the community on transmission routes and hygiene practices.
  6. Screening/Treatment: Identifying and treating carriers (e.g., food handlers) to prevent spread.

SECTION 3: LONG ANSWER QUESTIONS (40 MARKS)

29.
A patient was suspected to be suffering from amoebiasis in a health facility. On examination of the stool sample no cyst or trophozoites were seen. Clinician insisted that the condition is a complication of amoebiasis. Discuss the complications of the disease noting the possible specimen which can be used to confirm diagnosis (20 marks)
Show Answer

Model Answer:

1. Introduction:
Amoebiasis, caused by Entamoeba histolytica, typically affects the intestine (dysentery) but can spread via the bloodstream to cause extra-intestinal complications.

2. Complications of Amoebiasis:

  • Amoebic Liver Abscess (ALA): The most common extra-intestinal complication. Trophozoites reach the liver via the portal vein. Features include fever, right upper quadrant pain, and hepatomegaly.
  • Pulmonary Amoebiasis: Usually results from the direct rupture of a liver abscess through the diaphragm into the lung. Causes cough, chest pain, and chocolate-colored sputum.
  • Cerebral Amoebiasis (Brain Abscess): A rare but fatal complication where parasites reach the brain via systemic circulation.
  • Amoebic Peritonitis: Rupture of a liver abscess or intestinal perforation into the peritoneal cavity.
  • Amoeboma: A granulomatous mass in the colon wall that can mimic carcinoma.
  • Cutaneous Amoebiasis: Ulceration of the skin around the anus or a draining sinus from a liver abscess.

3. Diagnostic Specimens & Methods for Complications:

  • Liver Aspirate (Pus): For Amoebic Liver Abscess. The aspirate is described as "anchovy paste" or chocolate-colored. Microscopy may reveal trophozoites (often in the last portion of the aspirate from the abscess wall).
  • Sputum: In pulmonary amoebiasis, sputum may contain trophozoites.
  • Serum (Serology): Since stool is often negative in extra-intestinal cases, serological tests (ELISA, IHA) to detect antibodies against *E. histolytica* are crucial.
  • Biopsy Tissue: From rectal ulcers (amoeboma) or skin ulcers (cutaneous) for histopathology.
  • Imaging (Radiology): Ultrasound or CT scan is essential to visualize liver abscesses, though not a specimen per se.
30.
You are on duty in the reagent preparation room and you are requested to prepare 5 litres of Normal saline. You are provided with 60 Gms of sodium chloride analytical grade, 12 litres of distilled water, weighing balance, filter paper, 10 litre reagent bottle, measuring cylinder, conical flask to hold two litres.
  1. Describe how you would prepare the required volume (10 marks)
  2. Determine what amount of sodium chloride and distilled water will remain (4 marks)
  3. Explain how you would confirm the concentration of the prepared solution (3 marks)
  4. State its uses in parasitology laboratory (3 marks)
Show Answer

Model Answer:

i. Preparation of 5 Litres of Normal Saline (0.85% w/v):

  • Calculation: Normal saline in parasitology is typically 0.85% NaCl. $$0.85\% = 0.85g \text{ in } 100ml$$ $$8.5g \text{ in } 1000ml (1 Litre)$$ $$\text{For } 5 \text{ Litres: } 8.5g \times 5 = 42.5g$$
  • Procedure:
    1. Weigh exactly 42.5g of Sodium Chloride using the weighing balance and filter paper.
    2. Measure about 1-2 litres of distilled water using the measuring cylinder and transfer it into the 10L reagent bottle (or initially into a large flask to dissolve).
    3. Add the weighed salt to the water and mix/swirl until completely dissolved.
    4. Add the remaining distilled water to make the total volume up to exactly 5 Litres (using the measuring cylinder).
    5. Label the bottle clearly: "Normal Saline 0.85%", Date of preparation, and Batch number.

ii. Remaining Amounts:

  • Sodium Chloride: Initial: 60g. Used: 42.5g. $$\text{Remaining} = 60 - 42.5 = \mathbf{17.5g}$$ *(Note: If 0.9% Medical Saline is used: $0.9 \times 10 \times 5 = 45g$. Remaining = 15g)*
  • Distilled Water: Initial: 12L. Used: 5L. $$\text{Remaining} = 12 - 5 = \mathbf{7 \text{ Litres}}$$

iii. Confirmation of Concentration:

  • Titration: Titrate a sample against a standard Silver Nitrate ($AgNO_3$) solution using potassium chromate indicator to determine chloride content.
  • Refractometer/Conductivity Meter: Use a calibrated instrument to measure salinity/conductivity.

iv. Uses in Parasitology Laboratory:

  • Direct Wet Mount: Used to prepare physiological saline wet mounts of stool to observe motility of trophozoites (e.g., *Giardia*, *Entamoeba*).
  • Washing: Used to wash stool samples during concentration techniques (e.g., sedimentation).
  • Preservation: Maintains osmotic balance of parasites, preventing distortion or rupture during observation.
