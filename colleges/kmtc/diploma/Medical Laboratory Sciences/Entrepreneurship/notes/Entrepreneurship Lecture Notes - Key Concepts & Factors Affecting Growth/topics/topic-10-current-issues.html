‚öñÔ∏è Business Ethics

Ethics are principles that play a significant role in guiding the conduct of managers, entrepreneurs, and employees. Business ethics is concerned with the right and wrong in the practice of entrepreneurs.

üìú Doctrines

Primarily religion & politics that have existed since the beginning of history (e.g., Mosaic Ten Commandments, Athenian democracy, Bill of Rights).

ü§ù Values & Norms

Related to religious tendencies, customs, and traditions that vary from society to society.

üìã Codes & Standards

Formulated by particular professional groups and business organizations.

Important Aspects of Business Ethics

  • Competition: Regarded as healthy when carried on within acceptable limits. Unfair aspects include predatory pricing, artificial scarcity, and bribery.
  • Pricing: Fair prices are necessary. Overpricing puts customers away; underpricing hurts competitors.
  • Customer/Supply relations: Treat with fairness, consideration, honesty, and courtesy.
  • Employee treatment: Reasonable hours, pleasant conditions, listening to complaints, no discrimination.
  • Product promotions: No misrepresentation, correct packaging, fair methods.
‚ñ∂ Management Ethics & Code of Conduct

Organization Ethics: Moral principles or policies set by the organization (Code of Ethics). It guides behavior and deals with legal issues, government regulations, and social pressures.

Individual Ethics: Personal attributes like honesty and willingness to perform, influenced by background.

Social Ethics: Environmental awareness, public relations, fair competition.

Government Obligations: Payment of taxes, making annual returns (NHIF, NSSF), insurance.

Ethical Principles to Guide Behaviour:

  • Honesty: Truthful, sincere, forthright.
  • Integrity: Principled, honorable, upright.
  • Promise keeping: Trustworthy, fulfilling commitments.
  • Fidelity: Faithful and loyal to stakeholders.
  • Fairness: Justice and equal treatment.
  • Confidentiality: Respecting privacy.

ü§ù Social Responsibility

Refers to the roles undertaken by business organizations on the surrounding environment. It is the liability the organization undertakes to account for conduct that affects communities or society at large.

The Stakeholders

üë• Internal

Employees & Management: Interested in continuity of employment, growth, personal esteem, and individual goals.

üîó Connected

Shareholders: ROI, ethical performance.
Bankers: Financial performance, risk.
Customers: Quality goods, fair prices.

üåç External

Government: Compliance, tax revenue, employment.
Local Authorities: Economic activity, environment.
Competitors: Fair competition.

Social Concerns of an Entrepreneur

  • Protection of the environment: Pollution control, preventive measures, economical use of raw materials.
  • Participation in community work: Welfare programmes, supporting education, health, and housing.
  • Gender issues: Merit-based employment, empowerment programmes, funding against gender-based violence.
  • Provision of quality goods and services: Safe products, correct standards, attending to complaints.

üíª Technology Issues & E-Everything

ICT (Information and Communication Technology): The new science of collecting, storing, processing and transmitting information. Business needs accessible information that is accurate, up-to-date and sufficient.

Benefits of ICT to Business

  • Improved accuracy internally and externally.
  • Comprehensive customer services.
  • Faster processing and prompt responses.
  • Information for management and tighter financial control.
  • New sources of information for product design/marketing.
  • Reduced costs due to greater productivity.
üåê

E-Business

Use of the internet and other networks to support electronic commerce, enterprise communication, and collaboration. It empowers small enterprises to access new markets, improve customization, lower costs, and compete globally.

‚ñ∂ E-Government & E-Procurement

E-Government

Application of advanced ICT to deliver government services. Features include:

  • Smart: Simple, Moral, Accountable, Responsive, Transparent.
  • Customer-driven: Needs drive structures.
  • Available 24x7.
  • Accessible from anywhere.

E-Procurement

Business-to-business purchase and sale of supplies and services over the internet. Implementation issues include competition, supplier perceptions, and control.

üåè Globalization Trends

Globalization puts the customer at the center of business. Entrepreneurs are moving to different countries and starting new ventures (Joint ventures, mergers, acquisitions).

üöÄ Advantages

  • Large markets beyond home borders.
  • Greater motivation in new opportunities.
  • Improvements in technologies/quality.
  • Extending product life cycle.
  • Earning foreign exchange.

üõë Restraining Factors

  • Government controls: Tariffs, quotas, foreign exchange controls.
  • Non-monitoring barriers: Bureaucracy, legal insecurity.
  • Social and Cultural barriers.
  • Entrepreneurial culture: Need for wide vision.

üìä Social, Economic & Consumer Trends

üé≠ Social Cultural Trends

Consists of class structure, social mobility, social organization, customs, and taboos. Culture defines what is appropriate to eat, wear, etc. Social status (income, education) influences consumption.

üí∏ Economic Trends

Factors promoting/hindering entrepreneurship:
Capital: Funds/equipment available.
Land: Natural resources.
Labour: Physical/mental effort (quantity & quality).
Entrepreneurship: Combining factors.

Consumer Trends

Factors influencing consumer behaviour:

Internal Influences External Influences
Needs & Motives: Deprivation leading to action. Culture: Learned behavior shared by society.
Perception: How one views the world. Social Class: Ranking based on income/education.
Attitudes: Tendency to respond favorably/unfavorably. Family: Roles in the buying process.
Personality: Enduring traits affecting behavior. Purchasing Power: Ability to buy based on income/prices.

üèîÔ∏è Challenges & Management

Challenges Posed by Emerging Trends

  • Challenge in changing the type of business activity.
  • Difficult to attract additional capital (preference for large enterprises).
  • Challenge of sustaining and maintaining businesses.
  • Human resource management challenges.
  • Marketing challenges (lifeline of the firm).
  • Developing an entrepreneurial culture amidst inhibiting cultural activities.
üí°

Management Strategies

  • Identify opportunities and evaluate business ideas thoroughly.
  • Avoid excessive optimism; prepare marketing plans and cash projections.
  • Ensure a proper business plan to attract capital.
  • Effective measures to develop and motivate employees.
  • Update technology processes and products.
  • Consider factors affecting consumer consumption in marketing strategy.
