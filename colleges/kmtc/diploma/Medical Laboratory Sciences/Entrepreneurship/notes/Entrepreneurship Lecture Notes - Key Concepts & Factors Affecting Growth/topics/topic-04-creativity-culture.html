üé® Creativity and Innovation

‚ú® Creativity

Creativity refers to the process of devising a new idea, or thing that results in the improved efficiency or effectiveness of a system.

üöÄ Innovation

Innovation means improving an existing idea or thing. Commercializing of an idea or turning idea into an opportunity. Innovation builds on creativity when something new, tangible and value-creating is developed from the ideas.

‚ÑπÔ∏è

Impact

Innovation turns new concepts into realities, creating wealth and power. Innovation makes long-standing arrangements obsolete and frees resources to be employed elsewhere leading to greater economic efficiency (e.g., computerization).

‚öôÔ∏è Dynamics of Innovation

Reasons for Opposing Innovation

  • The entrepreneur stands to have a practical concern that unforeseen innovation may cause a disaster. e.g. side effects. e.g. of a drug.
  • Fear of losing profits in the event innovation does not translate to the expectations.
  • Where the entrepreneur held a monopoly position in the market, there is fear of losing authority and control.
  • Fear of upsetting the moral and social value of demand for the product.
  • Desire to preserve the existing market confidence.
  • Fear of upsetting tradition in production management and market scope.
  • Fear of opening a loophole to competition hence loss of business grip.

Reasons for Innovation

  • Innovation is essential for the entrepreneur in solving the inefficiency problems.
  • As a means of cost reduction and imposing a significant social and market grip.
  • Profit improvements are looked at from the innovation point of view through new technology in management and production.
  • To encounter competition by already established businesses.
  • To facilitate the opening up of new markets both locally and internationally.
  • To facilitate the diversification of product risks and losses. To protect current position of monopolies.

Requirement for Innovation

  1. Economic demand: People engage in innovation out of belief that the economic return will be greater than its costs.
  2. Surplus capital: Provides the necessary time and startup costs for implementing a new idea.
  3. Ability to assemble and invest capital.
  4. Mobile capital which is stable: Capital cannot serve unless it can move to potential innovator unless it can move to allow the various types of wealth to be created e.g. title deeds ‚Äì stability is provided by a rule of law.
  5. Availability of growth-fostering social institutions: Which facilitate the speed of technological advancement.
  6. Ability and willingness to think and act creatively (Entrepreneurs): I.e. the philosophical and psychological requirements.
  7. Geographical and other circumstantial causes such as ethical issues: Societies in which innovation is seen as sinful or people are punished or are shunned to think differently than others are unlikely to experience innovation.
  8. The size of the firm: Large firms have the advantage of introducing innovation since they can afford it. They tend to attract more talented employees to advice on new ideas.

üåç The Entrepreneurship Culture

üìò

Culture Definition

Culture is defined as a set of values, perceptions of wants and behavior learned by a member of a society from family and other institutions.

Hosted defines culture as a collective programming of the mind which distinguishes the member of one group or category of people from another.

Entrepreneurial Culture: Refers to the way of embracing the concept of finding new opportunities in business and gathering the necessary resources to fill the opportunity. In other words, entrepreneurship is a way of people embracing life by participating in activities that enable them to create new business enterprises.

Importance of Entrepreneurship Culture

  • Enhances economic growth and building of social capital.
  • Enhances job creation. Acts as a primary source of innovation.
  • Helps in the devolution of government power for policy implementation.
  • Directly influenced development in tech. H/R capital formation etc.

üå± Cultural Habits & Factors

Habits Promoting Entrepreneurial Development

  1. Money orientation: Money oriented people know the value of money and have the intention of making it. The money oriented people use the need of money as a motivating factor.
  2. Future orientation: A society that has foresight to know about the future business environment is likely to have more entrepreneurs. They are likely to visualize key changes that are likely to create opportunity.
  3. Time-consciousness: Knowledge that time exists and its importance. Utilization of time and the correct timing of the market conditions.
  4. Trust and honesty: Through trust, consumer demand is gained on the products and services available. Entrepreneurs should reciprocate this by ensuring honesty by providing the expected standards.
  5. Hard work: Willingness to work hard distinguishes between success and unsuccessful persons.

Factors Inhibiting Entrepreneurial Development

  1. Religion: religious beliefs may deter entrepreneurial investments in items such as nightclubs and pubs.
  2. Language: establishing businesses in areas where language barriers may allow poor communication or fear of invasion.
  3. Personal relationship: Married people may avoid getting involved in business activities so someone's time is spared for the family.
  4. Attitude towards innovation: Especially in cultures which oppose innovation due to fear of change.
  5. Networks: poor networking and ability to meet people limit new opportunities, new knowledge, and new information.
  6. Technology: lack of technical skills and knowledge may slow growth and dev. of entrepreneurial.
