üß≠ Planning for the Business Venture

Planning is a predetermine cause of action. It is a statement outlining an organizational mission and future direction, short and long term performance, targets and strategies.

Planning involves:

  • Predicting into the future by defining the enterprise mission statement.
  • Determining the organizational goals and objectives.
  • Formulating strategies towards achieving and objectives.
  • Assigning of responsibilities and functions.
  • Allocating resources.
  • Monitoring and evaluation.
  • Taking corrective action or redesigning the original.
üèóÔ∏è

Components of Planning Function

  1. Objective: Is the measurable, verifiable, specific and attainable target. Gives focus and direction to a business mission statement.
  2. Strategies: Are a scheme & methods which an entrepreneur hopes to deploy in order to move the enterprises from its present position to arrive at its targeted goals.
  3. Policies: Are overall guides to action which must be followed consistently for the achievement of organization goals.
  4. Standards: Are a planning function that permit an entrepreneur to use values as forms (acceptable standards) for evaluation of performance.
  5. Budgets: Are quantitative expressions of future cause of action. They usually show cash flow of an organization.
  6. Procedures: Help an entrepreneur to chart the sequence or related tasks to be performed.
  7. Methods: Reveal the manner of performing specific tasks.
  8. Programme: Are a set of activities undertaken to accomplish objectives.

üìò The Business Plan Concept

Definition: A business plan is a document that convincingly demonstrates that your business idea can sell enough of its products and services so as to make satisfactory profit and attract able to potential financiers. In other words, a business plan is a road map you can follow to start and manage a successful business.

Why Prepare a Business Plan?

  • To avoid silly mistakes.
  • It defines and focuses business objectives and goals.
  • As a tool for fundraising, marketing, monitoring, evaluation, staffing.
  • To be realistic on our intentions.
  • To clearly communicate your vision/ideas to others within and outside.

üõ†Ô∏è Business Plan as a Tool

  • Objective and goal creating tool
  • Management tool
  • Training tool
  • Promotion tool
  • Fundraising tool/capital
  • Staffing tool
  • Monitoring and evaluating tool

‚úÖ What is a "good" Plan?

  • A good plan should be dynamic document available for reference.
  • It should clearly communicate visions and ideas.
  • Should show the evidence of understanding of target customers.
  • Appealing to the potential financier.

üìë Components of a Business Plan

Business plans include details under the following main sections:

  1. Executive Summary: This should be done last. It includes: type of venture, products/service, uniqueness, opportunity, business status, legal form, location, target market, % share of market, competitor strength/weakness, strategy, management staff, time frame, money needed, type of financing, strength of the business, and future plans.
  2. Business Description: Includes objectives, vision, mission statement, specific objectives (SMART), type/form of business venture, date of commencement, physical location, postal address, telephone contact, brief history, and experiences of the owners.
  3. Marketing Plan:
    • Description of the target market (customer segment)
    • Description of products/services
    • Prices of products/services
    • Distribution of products/services
    • Promotion of productions/services
  4. Competitor Analysis: Internal analysis (strength and weakness), external analysis (opportunities and threats), and environmental analysis (political, social, economic, regulatory factors).
  5. Management and Organization: Key management staff (positions, responsibilities, qualification), other staff, and Human resources practices (recruitment, motivation, training, reward, appraisal).
  6. Business Operation: Product/service development design, Description of premises, ownership status, renovations, machinery/tools required, implementation (procurement, repair), legal requirements, monthly overhead expenses, and professional/support services.
  7. Financial Plan: Pre-operational costs, working capital, projected monthly cash flow statement, projected annual cash flow statement, projected proforma income statement, and projected balance sheet.
  8. Appendices: Brochures, maps, photos, leases, certificates, list of assets/equipment, licenses, research results.

üßê Evaluation and Presentation

How Potential Lenders and Investors Evaluate the Plan

Potential readers or evaluators should reflect on the strengths of management and personnel, the product or service and the available resources.

üè¶ Lenders (Banks)

Primarily interested in the ability of the new venture to pay back the debt together with the interest within a designated period of time. Lenders focus on the 4Cs of credit:

  • Cash flow: Ability to meet debt and interest.
  • Collateral: Tangible assets secured.
  • Character: Entrepreneur's credit history.
  • Contribution of equity: Personal equity invested.

üíº Investors

Place more emphasis on the entrepreneur's character than lenders. Investors want to make sure that the entrepreneur is compliant and willing to accept this involvement. They also demand a high rate of returns and will therefore focus on the market and financial projections.

Presenting the Business Plan

It is often necessary for an entrepreneur to orally present the business plan before an audience of potential investors. The entrepreneurs are expected to sell their business concept in this short period ‚Äì try and persuade potential investors that this is a good investment. The focus is why this is a good opportunity.

Market Information Needs

An upside-down pyramid approach to gathering information:

  • General Environmental Trends: Household income, population shifts, employment trends.
  • National Industry Trends: Total sales, commercial trends.
  • Local Market Trends: Local economic trends and assessment.
  • Local Competition: Strengths & weaknesses.
  • Market Positioning: Clarify product/service and objectives.
