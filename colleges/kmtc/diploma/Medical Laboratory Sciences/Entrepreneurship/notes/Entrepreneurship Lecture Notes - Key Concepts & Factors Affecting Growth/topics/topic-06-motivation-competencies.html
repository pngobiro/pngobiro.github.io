üî• Entrepreneurial Motivation

Entrepreneurial motivation are those factors and forces or events that energizes an individual, his desires and the need to stop into and sustain a business venture. Motivation is that which causes you to behave in the way you behave i.e. the why of behavior.

üß† Internal Motivation Factors

Refer to those personal traits and desires that induce a person to become an entrepreneur:

  • Employment Creation Need.
  • Need for independence or self-Reliance (internal locus of control).
  • Need for Power: Need to exercise power over others.
  • Need for Recognition: Strive to get recognition by peers, family and society.
  • Need for Security: Strive to be free from anxiety about survival.
  • Self-actualization need: The need to accomplish what you are capable of achieving.

üåç External Motivation Factors

Refer to those factors provided by others, especially legal authorities and society, creating an enabling environment:

  • Infrastructure (power, water, communication).
  • Credit Facilities (Government agencies, Banks, NGOs).
  • Information Support (Market research).
  • Pricing Policy (Incentives).
  • Tax Policy.
  • Legal Control (Minimized bureaucracy).
  • Political Climate (Conducive environment).
  • Technical/Training Assistance.

Demotivating Factors

  • Lack of proper information
  • Fear of competition / Fear of taking risk
  • Inhibiting government laws
  • Lack of adequate start-up capital
  • Lack of knowledge and skills
  • Wrong attitudes towards employment

ü™ú Maslow's Need Theory

Theory of human needs is identified with the psychologist Abraham Maslow. This theory is based on three specific assumptions:

  1. The human beings are never satisfied. Their wants are determined by what they have.
  2. A satisfied need does not cause behavior. Once people satisfy their need for safety, they are motivated by yet unsatisfied needs.
  3. Human needs are arranged in hierarchy of importance.

Hierarchy of Needs

  • Physiological Needs: The need for Food, Clothing, Shelter. They are required for survival.
  • Safety or security needs: The need to be free from physical danger and fear of loss of job.
  • Belongingness Need (Affiliation): Since we are social beings, we need to belong, to be accepted by others like family, work groups, mentors.
  • Esteem needs: Produces such satisfaction as Power, Prestige, Status, self-confidence.
  • Self-Actualization Needs: Means making the most of what we have to maximize our potential. Aspire for a standard of excellence.

Leadership is the ability to inspire, influence, persuade others to give maximum effort and cooperation willingly and voluntarily towards the attainment of the unplanned goals.

Sources of Influence in Leadership

ü§î Decision Making

This is selection from among alternatives of a course of action. It is a process of choosing from among various alternatives.

Step of the Decision Making Process

  1. Identify the major problem (e.g., low productivity).
  2. Determine major causes of the problem (e.g., low salaries, poor working conditions).
  3. Determine potential solutions ‚Äî how to solve.
  4. Evaluate potential solutions.
  5. Select the best solution.
  6. Implement the solution.
  7. Verify the solution.

Methods of Decision Making

  • Rule of Thumb: Based on existing established procedures e.g. based on laws, customs.
  • Committee Approach/Teamwork: Using a selected number of people to make a decision.
  • Critical Path Analysis: Making decision stage by stage until the final decision is reached.
  • Brainstorming: All members of a group develop ideas for a solution.
‚ñ∂ Group Participation: Pros & Cons

Positive Aspects

  • The sum total of the groups knowledge is greater.
  • Develops a much wider range of alternatives.
  • Increases the acceptability of the decision.
  • Group members better understand why a decision was made.

Negative Aspects

  • It takes more time.
  • One individual may dominate and control the group.
  • Social pressures to conform may inhibit members.
  • Competition can become overly intense.
  • Tendency to accept the first potentially positive alternative.
  • The decision is often a compromise that is likely to be unsatisfactory.

‚è±Ô∏è Time Management

Time is a measure of life. It is a unique resource, shared equally, cannot be stored, cannot be replaced once it is lost. Time is an entrepreneur's scarcest resource.

‚ö†Ô∏è

Time Wasters

  • Failure to delegate duties.
  • Poor mailing process.
  • Unnecessary interruptions by friends or relatives.
  • Poorly conducted meetings.
  • Poor scheduling of events.
  • Excessive paperwork.
  • Indecisiveness.

How to Manage Time

  • Draw-up an action plan ‚Äî i.e. "things to do list".
  • Set out priorities based on what is urgent and important.
  • Schedule your time realistically.
  • Delegate as much as you can.
  • Attempt to perform as much/many tasks as possible during the early part of the day. "Use 20% of your time to accomplish 50% of task".

üîÑ Managing Change

Change is the need to make or become different, to replace or improve, to reform or reorganize to fit to the current situation which results in higher productivity or performance. It is a systematic planned effort to improve effectiveness.

Why People Resist Change

  1. Fear of the unknown.
  2. Misinformation or no communication.
  3. Threat to status quo.
  4. Threat to power base.
  5. Mistrust of change agents.
  6. Fear of possible failure (social or economic).
  7. No perceived tangible benefits.

Process of Change Implementation

  • Precise definition of the operational changes needed.
  • Define how the new working methods will affect particular people and groups.
  • Identification of attitudes currently held by employees.
  • Outline statement of attitudes necessary to enable people to adapt successfully.
  • Implementation of measures designed to change existing attitudes.

üèÜ Coping with Competition

Competition is the act to vie for customers or market.

  • Direct competitors: Services / Products which are similar to the same already in the market.
  • Indirect competitor: Offering different kinds of goods/services to the same market/customers.
üìä

Analysis (SWOT)

Analyze yourself and your competitors on the basis of:

  • Market: Market share and advantages.
  • Product: Quality, special services, possibilities for improvement.
  • Production: Technology, raw materials, skills, location.
  • Finance: Financial resources.
  • Government Policy: Opportunities or threats from policies.

Types of Competition

  • Pure/Perfect competition: Characterized by situation homogeneous (similar).
  • Oligopolistic competition.
  • Monopolistic competition.
