üí° Generating Business Ideas

Identifying a Need

A need can be an opportunity and indeed a consumer buys to satisfy need. Therefore identifying an unidentified or unserved need is a sure way of generating business ideas.

Brain Storming

This is a process of detaching analysis of an idea from the actual ideas. The idea may or may not be related to a given product. In brainstorming even silly and stupid ideas may be generated.

Skills, Hobbies or Interests

Business ideas can be generated through personal interests and hobbies or copying or improving somebody's ideas (skills).

Spotting a Market Niche

Entrepreneurs usually look for gaps in the growing markets, identifying market sections which are not being utilized.

Additional Methods

  • Listening to what People say: These are people who simply say or speak their needs.
  • Attribute listening: This method of generating business ideas is based on changing the way one looks at something in order to find a new use for it. It attempts to answer the question - what do we do with this product.
  • Gaining from Waste: What would appear waste can be used - say recycles to create a new opportunity.
  • Others: Look to see and listen to hear, research, importing an idea, daydreaming, Spin off from employment.

üîç Screening and Evaluation

The screening process is a systematic evaluation of ideas in order to select the best idea which would suit one. The screening process must be done carefully, objectively, soberly and without any emotions.

üìä

The Screening Process (SWOT Analysis)

"Strengths" and "Weaknesses" should focus on the internal environment (management, product/service), whilst "Opportunities" and "Threats" should focus on the external environment (economy, market and competitors).

  • Strengths: E.g. Distinctive competence, Adequate finances, Access to economies of scale, Good innovation ability, proven management.
  • Weaknesses: E.g. Lack of key skill, Internal operations problems, Low morale, Poor track records, Weak internal image.
  • Opportunities: E.g. Potential customers, Potential goodwill, A favorable social environment.
  • Threats: Strong competitions, Adverse government policies, Political instability, mismanaged economy, Unfavorable legislation.

Areas of Analysis

  1. Personal Evaluation: Objective for going to business, personal interests, degree of commitment.
  2. Market valuations: Consumer demand analysis, product price and placements, no. of competitors in markets.
  3. Availability of raw materials: Adequacy, reliability, price.
  4. Providing technology: Appropriateness, affordability.
  5. Analysis of skills available.
  6. Analysis of the government policies.

Characteristics of a Good Business Idea

  • Easy to manage and involve minimal risk.
  • Does not require excessive capital investments.
  • Offers a good return on capital.
  • The idea has scope for growth, expansion and diversification.
  • Comparative with owner's goal and interest.
  • Not against expectation of the society.
  • Has a short gestation period.
  • Easy to exit when necessary.

üéØ Business Opportunity & Feasibility

A business opportunity may be defined as an attractive project idea which an entrepreneur accepts for investment on the basis of what is known about the possible success for the project. A business opportunity can be distinguished from a mere idea through two ingredients: A good market scope and An attractive return on investment (profit).

Qualities of a Good Business Opportunity

  • Demand ‚Äì there should exist a good market scope.
  • Return on investment ‚Äì the business should be sufficiently profitable.
  • Availability of raw materials.
  • Enough skilled people.

Pre-feasibility Study (Market Research)

The main objective of a feasibility study is to determine whether the investment opportunity is promising enough and to confirm that the project is viable from the marketing, manufacturing and other points of view.

Purposes:

  • To identify any aspect of the project that is critical or crucial enough to call for in depth analysis.
  • To acquire comprehensive technical, economic and commercial data for the final investment decision.
  • To enable an in-depth study of aspects such as market potential, technical requirements, managerial ability, financial projections, and risk evaluation.
  • To establish the final outcome of whether or not to proceed with the business.

ü•ö Business Incubation

Business incubation is the process of nurturing small and start-up initiatives or businesses to relative maturity to become self-sustaining business, healthy and wealth-generating entities.

‚ö†Ô∏è

Failure Rate

The failure rate of any start-up business stands at 90% globally. Statistics show that the success rate for incubated businesses in initiatives is very high (over 80%) are bound to succeed.

The Incubation Process

The business incubation programmes are designed to accelerate success for future pre-neural companies through an awareness of support resources and services. Entrepreneurs who may wish to enter a business incubation program must apply for admission. Acceptance criteria vary from program to program but in general only those with feasible businesses and workable businesses plan are admitted.

Benefits of Incubation

  • Creating jobs and wealth.
  • Fosters a community's Entrepreneurial climate - Technology commercialization.
  • Diversification of Local Resource.
  • Acceleration of local development.
  • Facilitation of Business creation and growth.
  • Encouraging entrepreneurship especially women.
  • Revitalization of the community as a whole.
  • Growth of Private Sector Investment.
  • Increased Tax Revenue.
  • Equitable Development.

üîí Protection of Business Ideas

Most entrepreneurs will not be inventors, but all entrepreneurs are concerned with protecting their business ideas, especially when those ideas are related to unusual production or unique designs. Understanding the "patent law", trademarks, and copyrights is vital.

üìú Patents

A patent is a grant of property right by the government to an inventor. Anything that is patentable must be new and useful (must have some demonstrated function). Patents are exclusive property rights that can be sold, transferred, or used as collateral.

‚Ñ¢Ô∏è Trademarks

Include any word, name, symbol or distinguishing device or any combination thereof adopted and used by a manufacturer or merchant to identify his goods and distinguish them from those manufactured or sold by others.

¬©Ô∏è Copyrights

Establish ownership and protection for creative ideas pertaining to intellectual property (authors, composers, artists). Protected for the life of the originator plus a further 50 years.

Trade Secrets

Are proprietary information used in the course of business to gain an advantage in manufacturing or commercialization of products or services (e.g., formulas, patterns, list of customers, chemical compounds). Every organization must keep their secrets because modern communications systems contain so much information which if not guarded, the business may collapse.

Licensing

Licensing may be defined as an agreement between two parties, where one party has proprietary rights over some information, process or technology protected by a patent, trademark or copyright. This arrangement specified in a contract requires the licence to pay royalty or some other specified sum to the holder of the proprietary rights.

Product Safety and Liability

It is very important for the entrepreneur to assess whether any product that is to be marketed in the new venture is subject to any regulations under the consumer product. Any products introduced by entrepreneurs must obtain clearance from the Kenya bureau of standards under the consumer's protection Act.
