üë§ Unincorporated Business: Sole Proprietorship

Unincorporated businesses do not have separate entity (existence from that of their owners). According to law, such organizations are one and same in the existence of the owner. They do not have separate rights and obligations from those of their owners. They include Sole proprietors and Partnership.

Sole Proprietorship

Sole means single while proprietorship refers to the owner of a business owned by one person who takes responsibility on risks of the business. He either enjoys the profits or suffers the losses of the business alone.

Formation & Management

  • Simple and easy to form; usually only a license from the government is required.
  • If the business name differs from the owner's, it must be registered.
  • The owner is the manager, makes all decisions, and may employ others or use family labor.

Liability

  • Unlimited Liability: The owner's liability is not restricted to capital contributed but extends to include personal property.
  • If the business cannot pay debts, personal assets can be sold to clear them.
üí∞

Sources of Capital

Personal savings, borrowing from friends/relatives, banks, credit suppliers, government institutions (KIE, ICDC), NGOs, hire purchase funds, and retained profits.

ü§ù Partnership

According to the Partnership Act, a partnership is a relationship which subsists between persons carrying on a business in common with a view of making profits. It is an extension of sole proprietorship, combining financial and managerial skills.

Formation

A partnership may come into existence: Orally, by action of persons concerned, by a simple written agreement, or by a Partnership Deed.

‚ö†Ô∏è

Illegal Partnerships

A partnership is deemed illegal if:

  • Formed for an illegal purpose (e.g., theft).
  • It contains more than 20 members (except for professional firms).
  • The name does not disclose true names of partners and is not registered.

Types of Partners

General Partners

Real partners who are active and liable for debts.

Limited Partners

Liability limited to capital contributed. Do not participate in management.

Active Partner

Takes active part in running business; may be paid a salary.

Dormant/Sleeping Partner

Contributes capital, shares profits/liabilities, but no management role.

Nominal Partner

No capital contribution, allows name use. Not fully liable unless presented as general partner.

Minor Partner

Under 18. Shares profits not losses. Liability limited to capital.

The Partnership Deed (Articles of Partnership)

A written agreement safeguarding the interest of each partner. Contents include:

  • Nature of business and capital contributions.
  • Profit/loss sharing ratios.
  • Interest on capital and drawings.
  • Powers/duties of partners.
  • Procedures for dissolution, admission, or retirement.

Dissolution: Can occur due to expiry of time, completion of venture, mutual consent, death/bankruptcy of a partner, or court order (insanity, misconduct, losses).

üè¢ Incorporated Business (Companies)

Incorporated businesses (Joint Stock Companies) are legally viewed as separate and distinct units from the owners. They are governed by the Companies Act (Cap 486 in Kenya). The act of registering is known as incorporation.

üìú

Features of a Company

  • Legal Personality: Separate identity from owners; can own property, sue/be sued.
  • Perpetual Succession: Independent life regardless of member changes.
  • Limited Liability: Owners' liability restricted to investment (shares/guarantee).
  • Transferability: Shares can be transferred (freely for public, restricted for private).

Formation Documents

  1. Memorandum of Association: Defines relationship with outsiders. Mandatory.
    • Name clause (must end with "Limited")
    • Object clause (objectives)
    • Situation clause (location)
    • Liability clause
    • Capital clause
  2. Articles of Association: Internal rules and regulations (rights of members, meetings, directors). Optional (can adopt standard Table A).

üèõÔ∏è Public Corporations & Parastatals

These are enterprises where the government has stakes. If wholly owned by the government, it is a parastatal. Formed by specific Acts of Parliament.

Examples in Kenya: Kenya Pipeline, Kenya Airways, KCB, KPLC, Marketing Boards (Coffee Board, KTDA).

Features

  • Service motive (essential services) over profit.
  • Created by Act of Parliament.
  • Subsidized by government.
  • Board appointed by government.
  • Separate legal entity with limited liability.

Pros & Cons

Pros: Easy capital (gov funding), improves welfare (affordable services), limited liability.

Cons: Political influence, inefficiency/monopoly behavior, conflicting objectives, bureaucratic decision making, often loss-making.

üöú Co-operative Societies

A voluntary association of persons who come together to promote their social-economic interest. Minimum 10 members. Governed by by-laws.

Principles of Cooperatives

  • Open and Voluntary Membership.
  • Democratic Administration (One person, one vote).
  • Service to members (primary purpose).
  • Distribution of surplus based on participation.
  • Limited interest on capital.
  • Education to members.

Types of Cooperatives

  1. Producer Cooperatives: Collect, process, market, and distribute members' produce (e.g., agricultural societies). Functions: better prices, transport, storage, grading, credit, education.
  2. Consumer Cooperatives: Purchase goods in bulk and sell to members at fair prices.
  3. Savings and Credit Co-operative Societies (SACCOs): Members save money to get loans (usually 3x savings) at low interest. Very popular among employees due to check-off system and ease of access.

Structure

  • Primary Cooperatives: Grassroots level.
  • Secondary Cooperatives (Unions): Regional grouping of primary co-ops.
  • National Unions: Umbrella bodies (e.g., KPCU, KUSCCO).
  • Apex Cooperatives: Overall body (e.g., Kenya National Federation of Cooperatives).
