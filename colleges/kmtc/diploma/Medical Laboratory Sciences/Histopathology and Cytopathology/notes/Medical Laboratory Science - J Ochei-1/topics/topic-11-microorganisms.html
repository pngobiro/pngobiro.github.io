üß´ Staining of Bacteria

Bacteria are microscopic unicellular organisms and they are ubiquitous. A simple classification of bacteria may be based on their morphology‚Äîspherical (coccus), rod shaped (bacillus) or spiral (spirillum), or by their staining characteristics. Histological methods are not the best for the identification of bacteria. Bacterial culture is by far superior and more specific. Therefore where possible, a swab from the tissue or unfixed piece of tissue should be sent to the Bacteriology department for culture and identification. It is however still important for the histotechnologist to be able to give a simple general identification of bacteria when seen in the tissue. The most commonly used staining methods in the histopathology laboratory for demonstrating bacteria include the Grams stain or its modifications, acid-fast techniques and certain silver methods.

Gram's Staining Method

This staining method is named after the bacteriologist, Christian Gram (1884). It is the most classical and most informative routine stain for the demonstration of bacteria in tissue sections. It classifies bacteria into two groups: Gram Positive bacteria which retain the primary stain after treatment with alcohol or acetone, and Gram Negative bacteria which are 'decolourised' of the primary colour by alcohol or acetone. It is a regressive staining method. The bacteria are first stained with a strong basic aniline dye, then treated with iodine solution and differentiated (decolourised) with alcohol or acetone for a brief period of time. The Gram positive bacteria retain the stain while the Gram negative bacteria which lose the stain are counterstained with dilute carbol fuchsin, safranin or neutral red.

üí°

Principle

Basically, the method depends on the differential solubility of the aniline stain in alcohol or acetone when the stain is adsorbed by the bacteria. The iodine which acts as a mordant, forms a dye lake complex that colours both the Gram positive and Gram negative cells blue-black. The decolourising agent (alcohol or acetone) extracts the stain from the Gram negative bacteria more readily than from the Gram positive bacteria. The action of the decolourising agent is relative rather than an absolute one.

Brown-Brenn Modified Gram's Stain

This modification of the Gram's stain is ideal for sections. It gives good colour contrasts.

Procedure

  1. De-wax and hydrate sections.
  2. Stain with solution 1 (Hucker's ammonium oxalate crystal violet) for 1 minute.
  3. Rinse in water. Treat with solution 2 (Gram's Iodine) for 1‚Äì2 minutes.
  4. Rinse in water and blot with filter paper without leaving it completely dry.
  5. Decolourise with solution 3 (Ether-acetone) until no more blue colour runs off.
  6. Stain with solution 4 (Alcoholic basic fuchsin) for 1 minute.
  7. Wash in water. Blot gently but not completely dry.
  8. Dip in acetone for 10‚Äì15 seconds.
  9. Treat briefly with picric acid-acetone to decolourise the background tissue until it becomes brownish-yellow (15‚Äì30 seconds).
  10. Pass quickly through two changes of acetone-xylene mixture.
  11. Pass through two changes of xylene. Mount in a synthetic mounting medium.

Gram-Weigert Method for Bacteria

In this method, counterstaining is performed before the Gram‚Äôs stain, and decolourisation is slowly carried out with aniline-xylene.

Procedure

  1. De-wax and hydrate sections. Stain nuclei for 5 minutes in Lithium carmine.
  2. Treat with acid-alcohol for a few seconds. Wash well in tap water.
  3. Stain with Hucker‚Äôs crystal violet for 2‚Äì3 minutes. Blot gently.
  4. Treat with Gram-Weigert iodine for 1/2 minute. Blot gently.
  5. Decolourise with equal parts mixture of aniline xylene. Blot and pour on fresh aniline-xylene several times until no more purple colour washes off.
  6. Blot and rinse in 3 or 4 changes of xylene to remove the aniline. Mount in synthetic medium.

Result: Gram positive bacteria are blue black, Gram negative bacteria and nuclei are red. This modification also demonstrates fibrin (blue-black).

üî¥ Acid-Fast Staining

A certain group of bacteria is acid fast i.e. they resist decolourisation with acid when stained with a strong stain. These acid fast bacteria belong to the Genus Mycobacterium, including Mycobacterium tuberculosis and Mycobacterium leprae. This property of acid and/or acid-alcohol fastness is due to the high lipid content of the bacterial cell wall.

Ziehl Neelsen‚Äôs method for acid-fast bacteria in tissue

The method depends on the resistance of tubercle bacilli to acid or acid-alcohol after staining with hot carbol fuchsin.

Procedure

  1. Bring section to water.
  2. Flood the slide with carbol fuchsin. Heat from underneath until steam rises. Reheat at three intervals of five minutes each (or stain in a jar at 37¬∞C for 1 hour).
  3. Wash well in tap water.
  4. Decolourise with 1% acid-alcohol until a faint pink colour is retained.
  5. Wash in water.
  6. Counterstain with the 1% methylene blue or 0.5% malachite green for 20‚Äì30 seconds.
  7. Wash in water. Dehydrate, clear and mount.

Fite-Faraco-Wade Stain

This method is used for Mycobacterium leprae, which is less acid-fast than tubercle bacilli. Paraffin wax is removed with a mixture of paraffin oil and gasoline/turpentine/xylene to protect the waxy capsule.

Procedure: Deparaffinise in oil mixture. Stain with carbol fuchsin. Decolourise in 1% acid alcohol (faint pink). Counterstain with methylene blue.
Result: Acid fast bacilli are red, background is blue.

üåÄ Staining Methods for Spirochaetes

Most of the methods in histopathology for demonstrating spirochaetes are based on silver impregnation techniques.

Hage-Fontana Silver Method

This is a silver impregnation method which is very good for the detection of spirochaetes in smears.

  1. Fix smear in Ruge‚Äôs fluid. Wash.
  2. Flood with mordant (Phenol/Tannic acid) and warm. Wash.
  3. Flood with ammoniacal silver nitrate. Warm.
  4. Rinse, blot and examine.

Result: Spirochaetes are black, background is yellow.

Levaditi‚Äôs Silver Impregnation Method

Impregnates spirochaetes in tissue blocks before sectioning. Requires 3-5 days impregnation in silver nitrate, followed by reduction in hydroquinone.

Warthin-Starry Method

Acts on sections. Based on the fact that spirochaetes are argyrophilic. Uses a developer containing hydroquinone to reduce adsorbed silver.

Result: Spirochaetes are black, background is pale yellow to light brown.

The ordinary routine haematoxylin and eosin stain most fungi sufficiently enough for them to be recognized, but special stains are often required for identification.

Grocott's Modification of Gomori's Methenamine Silver Method

This method depends on the reduction of silver nitrate by the aldehyde groups of the fungal cell wall components, which have been oxidised by chromic acid.

Procedure

  1. Oxidise in 5% chromic acid for 1 hour.
  2. Wash in tap water. Rinse in 1% sodium bisulphite to remove excess chromic acid.
  3. Wash in tap water. Rinse in distilled water.
  4. Place in methenamine silver nitrate working solution at 58 ¬∞C for 45‚Äì60 minutes. Check microscopically (fungi should be dark brown).
  5. Rinse in distilled water. Tone in 0.1% gold chloride.
  6. Treat with 2% sodium thiosulphate. Wash.
  7. Counterstain with light green solution. Dehydrate, clear and mount.

Result: Fungi are sharply outlined in black. Mucin is grey. Background is pale green.

ü¶† Methods for Viral Inclusion Bodies

Viral inclusion bodies can be stained by various methods. They may be found in the cytoplasm (e.g., Negri bodies of rabies) or in the nucleus.

Lendrum's Phloxine Tartrazine Method

This method is based on the affinity of certain structures including viral bodies for phloxine. These structures retain the red stain when treated with a yellow tartrazine solution.

Procedure

  1. Stain with Mayer‚Äôs haematoxylin. Blue in tap water.
  2. Stain with phloxine solution (Phloxine + calcium chloride) for 30 minutes.
  3. Differentiate with tartrazine solution (Tartrazine in Cellosolve) until inclusion bodies are bright red and background yellow.

Result: Inclusion bodies are bright red. Nuclei are blue. Background is yellow.

Schleifstein‚Äôs Method for Negri Bodies

Designed for Negri bodies of rabies. Uses Basic fuchsin and Methylene blue.

Result: Negri bodies are deep magenta. Cytoplasm is bluish violet.

üêõ Protozoa and Other Parasites

Gomori Trichrome Staining Technique

Recommended for Entamoeba histolytica in faeces and tissue.

  1. Fix in Schaudinn's fixative.
  2. Treat with trichrome stain for 10 minutes.
  3. Rinse in acidified ethanol.

Result: Cytoplasm of amoebic trophozoites is blue green with purple tinge. Nuclear chromatin is purple to red.

Iron-haematoxylin Staining Technique

Standard method for parasitologists. Shows fine details of amoebae. Uses Iron-haematoxylin solution.

Result: Nuclear structures and inclusions are black.

Kirkpatrick's Carmalum Staining of Whole Worms

Used to demonstrate worms and their various organs. The worm is stained in carmalum for at least 12 hours, differentiated in acid-alcohol, dehydrated and cleared in beechwood creosote.

Result: Worm is transparent red.
