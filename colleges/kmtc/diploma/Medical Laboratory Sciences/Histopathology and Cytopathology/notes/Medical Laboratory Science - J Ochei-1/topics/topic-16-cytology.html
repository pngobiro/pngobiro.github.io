01

üìò Introduction

Cytology is the study of free cells from different organs of the body. These cells may have been shed by the body itself; aspirated by tube or needle; or scraped or washed from tissue surfaces. Therefore, effusions, secretions, aspirates and scrapings are all used in diagnostic cytology. Exfoliative cytology is the study of cells which are shed spontaneously from epithelial surfaces of the body. This spontaneous shedding is a function of normal epithelium. The epithelial surfaces undergo constant growth and so they continue to shed worn out cells which are replaced by new ones. However, malignant tumour cells exfoliate more readily than those from healthy tissues. The detection of malignant cells in clinical specimens under microscopic examination is the most important role of diagnostic cytology. In addition, valuable information may be obtained about infections and infestations, reproductive defects and hormonal status. Clinical specimens are obtained with little or no discomfort to the patient.

Safety Precaution

All clinical specimens sent to the Cytology laboratory should be regarded as potentially pathogenic. Therefore, all safety precautions applicable to Microbiology laboratory should also be applied here. Safety cabinets are a must for processing sputum. Disinfectant jars should be placed at strategic points on the work bench and the staff should wear protective clothing.

Microscopic Evaluation

In exfoliative cytology, the microscopic evaluation is based upon observation and characteristics of the basic cells. Since a majority of the cells are in the form of single cast off cells lying in a fluid medium, there is no distortion to their shapes. This is unlike histopathology where the arrangement of cell aggregates is the basis of diagnosis of malignancy. Most of the information from the study of discrete cells is based on the shape or size of the nuclei. Cytoplasm of the cells may only assist in identifying the cell type.

Some of the characteristic nuclear abnormalities associated with carcinomas are:

  1. Nuclear enlargement with no increase in the overall cell size, showing a decreased cytoplasm/nucleus ratio.
  2. Irregularity of nuclear outline with variation in size and shape.
  3. Hyperchromasia. This is as a result of increased amounts of deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA); the nuclei of malignant cells often stain more intensely with basic dyes.
  4. Multinucleation due to abnormal cell division.
  5. Uneven distribution and variation in size of chromatin particles.
  6. Increase in size and number of nucleoli.
02

üß™ Fixation

To achieve a meaningful result and interpretation, smears should be prepared from fresh specimens and fixed while still wet. The nature of the specimen, the age of cell population, method of collection and fixation will all affect morphology of the cells. Most importantly, a prompt and proper fixation is essential.

A good cytological fixative should penetrate rapidly with no distortion to the cell morphology.

üí°

Transport

If the smears are to be sent by post to a distant laboratory, aerosol fixative sprays give an effective form of fixation. These aerosol sprays consist usually of an alcohol and wax‚Äîthe alcohol fixes the smear and the wax sets to provide a water-soluble protective film. Some laboratories coat pre-fixed smears with glycerine or water soluble wax.

Common Fixatives

  • Alcohol-ether: Equal parts of diethyl ether and 95% alcohol.
  • 95% alcohol: With or without 3% glacial acetic acid.
  • Tertiary butyl alcohol mixture: Seven parts of tertiary butyl alcohol and three parts of 95% alcohol.
  • Schaudinn‚Äôs solution: Saturated aqueous mercuric chloride (66 ml) + Absolute ethyl alcohol (33 ml) + Glacial acetic acid (0.3 ml).

Fixation time in any of these fixatives is at least 15 minutes and maximum 7 days.

03

üìë Subdivisions of Cytology

For practical purposes, cytology is subdivided as follows:

  1. Gynaecological Cytology (Gynae-cytology): This deals with the cytology of the vagina, cervix and endometrium.
  2. Non-gynaecological cytology: This is the cytology of cells suspended in body fluids.
  3. Fine needle aspiration cytology (FNAC): The study of cells aspirated from organs and structures of the body.
04

üë© Gynaecological Cytology

There are three most important applications of gynae-cytology:

  • The detection of malignancy‚Äîmainly cancer of cervix.
  • Assessment of hormonal activity.
  • Identification of vaginal infection and inflammatory conditions.

Specimen Collection and Preparation of Smears

1. Cervical smears

Cervical smears are made from material collected with the help of a speculum (a metal or plastic device) which is inserted into the vagina and allows the uterine cervix to be readily visible. A specialised spatula known as the Ayre spatula or cervical spatula is used for collection. The collection is made at the junction of the columnar epithelium by visualising the cervix, the spatula is inserted via the speculum into the cervical os and rotated through 360 degrees. The material collected is quickly smeared over a pre-labelled microscope slide and fixed immediately.

2. Aspiration from the posterior fornix

With the aid of a speculum, cellular material is collected from the posterior fornix, using a disposable plastic pipette with a suction bulb. Following aspiration, smears are prepared and fixed immediately.

3. Vaginal smears

Vaginal smears are valuable for the assessment of hormonal function. Cellular material is collected by scraping the upper third of the lateral wall of the vagina with a wooden spatula. The cells are evenly and thinly smeared over a clean pre-labelled microscope slide and fixed.

4. Endocervical smears

This is used mainly for follow up cases where a surgical treatment has been used after a cone biopsy has been taken for assessment of dysplasia and malignancy or as a curative procedure. A cotton tip swab is inserted into the endocervix and rotated gently to cover a wide area of the endocervix. The material collected is smeared on a clean pre-labelled microscope slide and fixed.

5. Endometrial aspiration

This procedure has to be performed under strict aseptic conditions so as not to introduce infection into the patient. A cannula is inserted into the uterine cavity and the cellular material is aspirated using a syringe. Thin smears are made on clean pre-labelled slides and fixed.

üé® Papanicoloau Staining Method

The most commonly used stain in the gynae-cytology is the Papanicoloau stain. It is a stain specially suitable for cervical smears. It gives sharp nuclear staining and good differential colouring of acidophilic and basophilic cells. It makes the cytoplasm transparent. This staining method can be performed either manually when there are only a few slides to deal with, or by using automatic staining machine when a large number of slides is to be stained.

Solutions

  1. Harris haematoxylin (see Chapter 7)
  2. Orange G solution (OG 6): 0.5% Orange G in 95% alcohol (100 ml) + Phosphotungstic acid (0.015 g).
  3. Eosin Azure 50 (E.A. 50 or E A 36): 0.5% light green SF (45 ml) + 0.5% Bismark brown Y (10 ml) + 0.5% Eosin (45 ml) + Phosphotungstic acid (0.2 g) + Lithium carbonate (1 drop).

Procedure for manual staining

  1. Remove smears from fixative. Rinse in descending grades of alcohol (80, 70 and 50%) and water for 10 seconds each.
  2. Stain in Harris haematoxylin for 2 minutes. Rinse in water for 1‚Äì2 minutes.
  3. Differentiate in 1% acid alcohol until only the nuclei retain the stain. Wash and blue in tap water for 3‚Äì5 minutes.
  4. Transfer to 70% alcohol for a few seconds. Transfer to 95% alcohol for a few seconds.
  5. Stain in OG 6 for 2 minutes. Rinse in 2 changes of 95% alcohol.
  6. Stain in EA 50 for 2‚Äì4 minutes. Rinse in 2 changes of 95% alcohol.
  7. Dehydrate in absolute alcohol, clear in xylene and mount in neutral synthetic medium.

üìä Hormone Assessment

Hormonal activity can be evaluated on the basis of microscopic examination of Papanicoloau stained vaginal smears.

Karyopyknotic Index (KPI)

Superficial cornified squamous epithelial cells show condensed, deeply stained, structureless (pyknotic) nuclei, with pink to red stained cytoplasm. The calculation of KPI is done by counting a total of 200 squamous cells. The cornified squamous cells are expressed as a percentage of the total number of squamous cells.

Maturation Index (MI)

Based on cell maturation determined by the morphology and staining reactions of the non-cornified squamous epithelial cells (superficial intermediate and parabasal cells). High oestrogenic activity is indicated by a preponderance of superficial intermediate cells while low activity is indicated by a predominance of parabasal cells.

Shorr's Staining Method

This method uses a single differential staining solution. It gives less cytoplasmic transparency and poorer nuclear definition than the Papanicoloau. It is not very useful in the diagnosis of malignant cells.

Method: Stain in Shorr stain for 1‚Äì2 minutes. Rinse in 70% alcohol. Dehydrate and clear.
Result: Nuclei: Red. Superficial cornified cells: Bright orange-red. Non-cornified cells: Green-blue.

07

üß¨ Sex Chromatin (Barr bodies)

Sex chromatin is present in about 30% of cells from the female whereas cells from the male do not have the sex chromatin. The sex chromatin appears as a darkly staining dot in the nucleus (attached to the nuclear membrane). Specimens are conveniently obtained by scrapings of the buccal mucosa.

Cresyl Fast Violet Acetate Method

This is a rapid, simple and effective method for demonstrating sex chromatin. It requires no differentiation.

Procedure: Fix wet smear in 95% alcohol (3 min). Stain with cresyl fast violet acetate solution (5 min). Rinse in tap water. Dehydrate, clear and mount.
Result: Sex chromatin is deeply stained. Cytoplasm is faintly stained.

08

üî¨ Non-Gynaecological Cytology

This aspect of cytology involves the study of cells suspended in body fluids.

Sputum

Valuable for respiratory tract disorders. Sputum must be processed in a biological safety cabinet. Purulent or blood stained particles are selected from the sputum with a microbiological wire loop and used to make thin smears. Bronchial washings are usually centrifuged and smears made from the sediment.

Pleural & Ascitic Fluid

Serous fluids that normally lubricate the walls of pleural and peritoneal cavities. Specimens are aspirated, centrifuged or cytospun, and thin smears made.

Gastric Brushing/Lavage

Useful for squamous cell carcinoma of the oesophagus or adenocarcinoma of the stomach. Material is collected by brush or lavage.

Urine

Valuable in the diagnosis of urinary tract tumours. Early morning specimens are preferred. Fixation can be difficult due to low protein content; using albuminized slides or celloidin coating helps.

Staining Methods

Papanicoloau stain
Equally as useful as in Gynae-cytology.
Romanowsky staining methods
Following fixation in ether-alcohol, smears may be stained by Giemsa or Leishman stains. Useful for cells in fluids and effusions.
Methylene blue
Rapid and simple method useful for screening fresh specimens, especially sputum. Add one drop of stain to specimen, cover and examine. Nuclei stain shades of blue. Not permanent.
Acridine Orange Fluorescence Method
Based on the principle that acridine orange emits visible light when excited by UV light. At pH 6.0, DNA fluoresces green and RNA orange-red. Malignant cells have high RNA content and are readily seen. Positive cases are confirmed by Papanicoloau stain.
Haematoxylin and Eosin
Can be used for smears. Gives good result but lacks the cytoplasmic transparency of Papanicoloau.

üíâ Fine Needle Aspiration Cytology (FNAC)

The fine needle aspiration technique has almost completely superseded the more traumatic method of cone biopsy for collecting material from lesions in organs that do not normally exfoliate cells.

For aspiration of specimens, the FNAC basically requires a sterile syringe and needle, the length of the needle depending on the location of the organ to be sampled. The needle thickness is usually in the range of 0.5‚Äì0.9 mm. A special handle can be attached to the syringe to allow single hand grip.

üìù

Procedure

  1. Clean skin with antiseptic. Immobilise the mass.
  2. Insert needle into the mass without retracting piston.
  3. Retract piston to aspirate cells. Move needle if necessary.
  4. Release the piston before withdrawing the needle. This prevents material being drawn into the barrel.
  5. Express specimen onto slide, spread, fix immediately.
  6. Stain with Papanicoloau or H&E.

FNAC is very valuable for preliminary diagnosis of carcinomas as well as inflammatory conditions. It is rapid and fairly inexpensive. FNAC is particularly useful in dealing with suspected masses or lesions on the skin, lymph node, breast, thyroid, liver, kidney, lung and bone.
