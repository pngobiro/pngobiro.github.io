üìò Demonstration of Lipids

Lipid is a generic term when used to describe any fatty or fat-like substance that occurs naturally and is capable of being utilised by animals and plants. It is soluble in ether, benzene, xylene, toluene and alcohols (fat solvents), but generally insoluble in water. It is therefore not easy to demonstrate in routine paraffin sections as they are dissolved during clearing. Therefore, in routine sections, for example H & E stained sections, fat appears as hollow spaces in tissues.

There are several classes of lipids:

Simple Lipids

These are esters of fatty acids and alcohols, e.g.,

  1. Neutral fats ‚Äî triple esters of fatty acids and glycerol (triglycerides).
  2. Ester waxes ‚Äî esters of fatty acids and higher alcohols.

Compound Lipids

These consist of fatty acids, alcohol (glycerol), in addition to nitrogenous base and other compounds, e.g.,

  1. Phospholipids ‚Äî These have phosphoric acid radical.
  2. Derived lipids ‚Äî These are formed from simple or compound lipids by hydrolysis, e.g., Fatty acids derived from triglycerides, and cholesterol derived from ester waxes.

üî¨ Fixation and Stains

Fixation of Lipids

For general preservation of lipids, 10% formal saline is adequate. The addition of 1% calcium chloride and 1% cobalt chloride or cobalt nitrate to 10% formal saline gives a better result. Further treatment of the tissue with potassium dichromate (post chromatin) or with osmium tetroxide will render the fats insoluble in, and non extractable by, fat solvents. The use of carbowax embedding technique excludes the use of fat solvents and provides an excellent means of studying fat in thin sections. However, for one reason or the other in most laboratories, fats and fat embolism are still demonstrated by means of frozen section of fixed or unfixed tissues.

Fat Stains

The most common stains used to demonstrate fats are oil soluble dyes. This group of dyes is more soluble in the fat than in alcohol. They are used in saturated form so that their uptake by the fats is made easy. This group of dyes includes the orange-red dye Sudan III, Sudan IV, oil red O and Sudan black B. Sudan black B is the most sensitive of them. Free floating sections are to be preferred for staining because with mounted sections, particularly cryostat sections of fresh tissue, staining of fat is not usually complete as the staining tends to be superficial.

General solvents for these dyes are 70% alcohol, equal parts 70% alcohol and acetone, isopropyl alcohol and propylene glycol. Ideally there should be no removal of tissue lipids with 70% alcohol and the ethanol-acetone mixture. There is minimal removal of lipids with isopropyl alcohol and no removal with propylene glycol.

üî¥ Oil Red O Method (Little)

This method is ideal for neutral fats.

Solutions

  1. Stock solution: Saturated solution of oil red O (300 mg/dl) in 99% isopropanol.
  2. Working solution: Mix six parts of stock oil red O with four parts of distilled water. Allow to stand for 10 minutes and then filter. This solution should be used within 2‚Äì4 hours. Keep in air-tight staining vessels to prevent precipitation of the dye as a result of evaporation.
  3. Mayer's or Ehrlich's or Harris's haematoxylin.
  4. 0.05% lithium or sodium carbonate.

Procedure

  1. Rinse sections well in distilled water.
  2. Stain in oil red O for 10 minutes.
  3. Rinse in tap water. On being placed in water the sections unfold nicely.
  4. Counterstain in Mayer's haemalum for 2‚Äì3 minutes or Harris's haematoxylin for 1 minute.
  5. Rinse in tap water.
  6. Blue in 0.5% lithium carbonate.
  7. Rinse well in several changes of distilled water.
  8. Float section onto a clean slide.
  9. Drain the slide but do not allow to dry.
  10. Mount in glycerine jelly or any good aqueous mounting medium.

‚ö´ Osmium Tetroxide Method

Osmium tetroxide (osmic acid) is one of the oldest and traditional fat stains. The mechanism is not quite fully understood. Osmium tetroxide is normally soluble in fats and forms a black reduction complex with them. This complex (ester) so formed, will, as a result of a further chemical bonding or reaction, form another complex that is no longer soluble in the usual fat solvents. But when mounted in synthetic resins, the osmium will, due to oxidation, become decolourised with time. The method does not give a permanent preparation.

Procedure

  1. Place frozen section in distilled water.
  2. Transfer section to, and allow to remain in, the 1% osmium tetroxide for 24 hours.
  3. Wash in several changes of water for 12‚Äì16 hours.
  4. Dehydrate in 70% (2 changes), 90% (3 changes) and absolute (2 changes) of alcohol.
  5. Wash well in distilled water.
  6. Mount in glycerol gelatin.
  7. Dehydrate, clear and mount in a synthetic medium.

Results

Fatblack
Backgroundyellow to brown

Use osmium tetroxide in a fume cupboard because of its toxic vapour.

‚ú® Fluorescence Microscopy

This special form of microscopy can be employed to demonstrate lipids. Primary fluorescence is exhibited by the oxidation products of lipids referred to as lipofuchsins. Solutions containing phosphine 3R or 3,4 benzpyrene (fluorochromes) exhibit secondary fluorescence when the fluorochromes are dissolved in the lipids. The phosphine method is more permanent though the benzpyrene method is more sensitive. Both are useful for formalin frozen sections.

Procedure

  1. Wash sections in distilled water.
  2. Stain in 0.1% aqueous phosphine 3R for three minutes.
  3. Rinse rapidly in distilled water and mount in 90% glycerine.
  4. Examine with ultraviolet blue exciter filter and a colourless barrier filter under fluorescence microscope.

Cholesterolno fluorescence

üß¨ Schiff‚Äôs Reaction for Unsaturated Lipids

This method demonstrates glyco-lipids (cerebrosides and gangliosides) and all other unsaturated lipids that contain groups which yield aldehydes on oxidation by periodic acid. There are several methods in this group, some use performic acid while others use peracetic acid or a combination of the two to oxidise the sections.

Performic Acid - Schiff Reaction

This method can be used for paraffin or frozen sections of formal-saline fixed tissue.

Solutions

  1. Performic acid: Add 31 ml of 30 % hydrogen peroxide and 0.22 ml of concentrated sulphuric acid to 8 ml of 90% formic acid. This gives a 4.7% concentration of performic acid in 2 hours. This acid is unstable.
  2. Schiff‚Äôs Reagent (Chapter 8)

Procedure

  1. Bring section to water.
  2. Oxidise with performic acid for 2‚Äì5 minutes.
  3. Treat with Schiff‚Äôs reagent for 30 minutes.
  4. Wash in warm running water for 10 minutes.
  5. Mount in aqueous mounting medium such as glycerol gelatin.

Result: Unsaturated lipids stain magenta-red.

üîµ Nile Blue Method

This method demonstrates neutral and acidic lipids. It involves the staining of formal-calcium fixed tissue with a Nile blue sulphate solution. The staining reaction is as follows: Neutral lipids stain red with Nile red solution component (of the Nile blue). Acidic lipids also have a tendency to stain red. However, these acidic lipids are capable of forming salts with the free base present in the staining solution. This salt so formed is lipid-soluble and dark blue in colour. This dark blue colour masks the red colour with which the acidic lipids stain. Many fatty acids are stained blue with Nile blue sulphate.

Procedure

  1. Rinse frozen section in distilled water.
  2. Stain with 1% aqueous Nile blue sulphate solution.
  3. Rinse quickly in distilled water.
  4. Differentiate in 1% acetic acid for 30 seconds or until of a uniform colour. This step helps to remove dye bound to monolipid structures.
  5. Rinse in distilled water and mount in glycerol gelatin.

Results

Neutral lipids (triglycerides, cholesterol esters, steroids)red to pink
Acidic lipids (fatty acids, phospholipids)blue
Nucleiblue
Cytoplasmpale blue

üß™ Baker‚Äôs Acid Haematein Method

In order to prevent the dissolution of phospholipids, tissues are fixed in formol-calcium. The tissues are then chromated, and the phospholipids and certain other non-lipid acidic substances form chelate complexes. After the dichromate treatment, sections are stained with acid haematein to form dye lakes of bluish brown colour with the bound chromium. Differentiation with borax ferricyanide solution removes most of the dye attached to the non lipid substances.

Baker‚Äôs Pyridine Extraction Method

Prior to staining of phospholipids by the Baker‚Äôs acid haematein method, an extraction of lipid has to be carried out. The extracted tissue serves as a negative control during the staining procedure.

Procedure: Fix in Dilute Bouin's Fluid. Wash in 70% ethanol, then 50% alcohol. Extract with pyridine (RT for 1 hr, then $60^{\circ}\text{C}$ for 24 hrs). Wash. Proceed to staining.

Staining Method

  1. Fix small pieces of tissue in the formol calcium solution for between 6 and 18 hours.
  2. Transfer into dichromate-calcium solution at room temperature for 18 hours.
  3. Transfer into fresh dichromate-calcium solution at $60^{\circ}\text{C}$ for 24 hours.
  4. Wash in tap water for six hours, rinse in distilled water.
  5. Cut frozen sections at 10 $\mu\text{m}$.
  6. Treat with dichromate-calcium solution at $60^{\circ}\text{C}$ for 1 hour.
  7. Wash in running water for three minutes and then rinse in distilled water.
  8. Stain in acid haematein at $37^{\circ}\text{C}$ for five hours.
  9. Rinse in distilled water.
  10. Differentiate in borax-ferricyanide solution at $37^{\circ}\text{C}$ for 18 hours.
  11. Wash in tap water for 10 minutes. Rinse in distilled water. Mount in aqueous medium.

Result: Phospholipids and Nucleoproteins stain dark blue. Cytoplasm is pale yellow.

üßä Methods for Demonstrating Cholesterol

Polarised Light Method

Cholesterol usually exhibits birefringence when viewed with polarised light. While the birefringence seen in discrete chronic lesion may be attributed to the presence of cholesterol, other lipids, certain proteins, amyloid or even formalin artefacts may also show birefringence. The use of polarised light therefore may only indicate the presence or absence of cholesterol. It may however, be useful when performed in conjunction with extraction procedures.

Schultz‚Äôs Method for Cholesterol and Its Esters

This method is based on the oxidation of cholesterol to oxycholesterol. This oxidation may be accomplished with ferric ammonium salt. A colour reaction develops when acid mixture is added to the section.

Procedure

  1. Rinse the formalin or formol-calcium fixed frozen section in distilled water.
  2. Place the section in the iron alum at $37^{\circ}\text{C}$ for 3 days.
  3. Rinse in water, mount on a clean slide and blot gently with fine filter paper, or drain off excess fluid.
  4. Add some drops of the acid mixture (conc. sulphuric acid + glacial acetic acid), carefully apply a coverslip. Examine.

Result: Rapid appearance of blue-green colour that reaches maximum intensity within a few minutes. Within 30 minutes, changes to greenish-brown.

Digitonin Reaction for Non-esterified Cholesterol

Digitonin, a naturally occurring glucoside and a precipitating agent will form crystalline complexes with non-esterified cholesterol.

Method: Immerse frozen section in 0.5% digitonin for several hours. Rinse in 50% alcohol. Counterstain with Sudan IV or oil red O. Mount.

Result: Examination with polarised microscope shows rosettes of digitonin-cholesterol complexes which are birefringent. In the counterstained slide, the cholesterol compound remains doubly refractile and does not stain, the cholesterol esters are stained and lose their birefringence.
