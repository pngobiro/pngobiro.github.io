Staining Reactions of Carbohydrates

üìö Carbohydrates & Mucins - Chapter 8
‚è±Ô∏è 35 min read
01

üìò Overview

The most important groups of carbohydrates in histology are the polysaccharides and the mucins.

Polysaccharides (Glycogens)

The only polysaccharides widely distributed in animal tissues are the glycogens; starch and cellulose are present in plants. Glycogens are composed of chains of glucose units; they are labile and are converted to sugars soon after tissue death due to the action of glycogenolytic enzymes. They are normally stored in the liver, heart and striated muscles.

Mucins

The mucins form a large group of different compounds. They are normally found as mixture of compounds. The term mucin was used to describe tissues or secretions from glands which are stainable with mucin carmine when precipitated by alcohol and acetic acid. Mucins are composed of hexosamines.

It is now a common practice to include mucins within the mucoid carbohydrates:

  • Mucopolysaccharide: a complex consisting of polysaccharides and proteins with the carbohydrate predominating.
  • Mucoprotein: a polysaccharide-protein complex with the protein predominating.
  • Mucolipid: a polysaccharide-fat complex.
02

üß™ Periodic Acid Schiff (PAS) Reaction

The most important staining method for carbohydrates is based on the periodic acid Schiff (PAS) reaction. This reaction is a true histochemical method. It has its roots in the original Feulgen reaction during which hydrolysis of carbohydrates with hydrochloric acid liberates aldehydes which restore the colour of Schiff reagent.

üí°

Principle

Periodic acid produces aldehydes by oxidation of carbohydrates. These aldehydes restore the colour of the Schiff reagent (leucofuchsin). Basic fuchsin is a compound comprising three dyes: rosaniline, pararosaniline and magenta II. The decolourised fuchsin (leucofuchsin) is colourless because its chromophoric bond (i.e. its quinonoid structure) has been broken. This bond and the colour can be restored by aldehydes.

The PAS reaction is histochemically specific but not diagnostic since many other substances or components of tissues give positive reaction. Some other substances that are PAS positive include cartilaginous matrix, the granules of megakaryocytes, phospholipids, and colloid of thyroid gland.

Preparation of Schiff Reagent

‚ñ∂ 1. Itikanen and Ogura Method
  1. Dissolve 1g basic fuchsin in $200\text{ml}$ boiling distilled water.
  2. Cool and filter.
  3. Bubble sulphur dioxide $(\text{SO}_2)$ gas slowly through the solution through a fitted glass filter with occasional shaking.
  4. Turn off the gas when the solution becomes clear transparent red.
  5. Stopper the flask and allow to stand overnight in the dark at room temperature.
  6. If the solution is pale straw coloured or colourless next morning, it is ready for use. If it is still faintly red in colour, decolorize it by adding 1g of activated charcoal, shake and filter. If still red, discard it.
  7. Filter into a stock brown bottle and keep in the refrigerator.
‚ñ∂ 2. De Tomasi-Coleman Method
  1. Dissolve 1g basic fuchsin in $200\text{ml}$ of boiling distilled water and shake for five minutes.
  2. Cool to $50^{\circ}\text{C}$ and filter.
  3. To the warm filtrate, add $20\text{ml}$ of $1\text{M}$ HCl (98.3 ml HCL, Sp.gr. 1.16, make up to $1000\text{ml}$ with distilled water or $83.5\text{ml}$ conc. HCl Sp. gr. 1.19 made up to $1000\text{ml}$ with distilled water).
  4. Cool to $25^{\circ}\text{C}$ and add 1 g anhydrous sodium or potassium metabisulphite $(\text{Na}_2\text{S}_2\text{O}_5$ or $\text{K}_2\text{S}_2\text{O}_5)$.
  5. Allow to stand in the dark for about 15 hours. The solution changes to orange or straw colour.
  6. Add 2 g activated charcoal, shake and filter into a brown stock bottle.
  7. Store in the refrigerator.
‚ñ∂ 3. Barger and Delamoter Method
  1. Dissolve 1g basic fuschsin in 400 ml boiling distilled water.
  2. Cool to 50¬∞C and filter.
  3. Add 1 ml thionyl chloride ($\text{SOCl}_2$) to the filtrate; stopper, shake and then let stand for 12 hours in the dark.
  4. Add 2 g activated charcoal, shake for 1 minute and filter into a brown stock bottle.
  5. Store in the refrigerator.

Thionyl reacts with water to form sulphur dioxide:

$$\text{SOCl}_2 + \text{H}_2\text{O} \longrightarrow \text{SO}_2 + 2\text{HCl}$$

Testing Reactivity

Reactivity is tested by adding a few drops of the Schiff's reagent to about 4 ml of formalin on a watchglass; a good reagent will cause a rapid development of reddish purple colour while a delayed development of bluish purple suggests that the reagent is fading.

PAS Staining Procedure

  1. Dewax and rehydrate paraffin sections. Remove mercuric pigment if necessary.
  2. Oxidise for five minutes in 1% aqueous periodic acid.
  3. Wash in running water for five minutes and rinse in distilled water.
  4. Place in Schiff's reagent for fifteen minutes (10 minutes for frozen section).
  5. Rinse for two minutes in each of three changes of freshly prepared sulphite rinse (10% sodium metabisulphite 9.0 ml + 1% HCl 7.5 ml + Distilled water 150.0 ml).
  6. Wash in running water for ten minutes.
  7. Counterstain with Harris haematoxylin for 1‚Äì3 minutes, differentiate in 1% acid alcohol, wash in tap water and blue in running water. If searching for fungi, counterstain with 0.1% light green in 0.1% acetic acid for 10‚Äì15 seconds.
  8. Dehydrate, clear and mount.

Results

PAS positive substances purple red (magenta)
Nuclei blue

üç¨ Glycogen Demonstration

Glycogen is a polymer of glucose. Due to its ready solubility in water it is important to fix the tissue immediately and to use thin slices of tissue. Neutral 10% formal alcohol and 10% formal saline are the fixatives of choice. Glycogens are usually present in a mixture with proteins which helps protection from loss. However, alcohol fixatives are theoretically better but have disadvantages like poor penetration and streaming.

PAS reaction in conjunction with a diastase control is used by most laboratories to demonstrate glycogen. However, due to the shortcoming of the PAS reaction, the older empirical method of Best‚Äôs carmine is still considered useful.

Diastase Digestion

Diastase which is normally present in the saliva, is used to remove glycogen from sections. Malt diastase is commercially available. Digestion makes the PAS reaction a specific test for glycogen.

PAS Reaction for Glycogen (Procedure)

Two sections of the material are prepared; one as a control:

  1. Bring down to water.
  2. Treat one section with human saliva or with 0.1% malt diastase in distilled water, freshly prepared, for 30 minutes at 37 ¬∞C.
  3. Wash in running water for five minutes. The other section is left in distilled water for similar time.
  4. Both sections are then stained by the PAS method.

Result: PAS positive material that is present in the untreated section and absent in the digested section may be assumed to be glycogen.

Best's Carmine Method

This empirical method has affinity for glycogen and is specific when controlled by diastase digestion. Paraffin sections must be covered with a thin film of celloidin before staining to prevent loss.

Preparation of Stain

Stock: Carmine 2g, Potassium carbonate 1g, Potassium chloride 5g, Distilled water 60ml. Boil for 5 mins (froths), cool, add 20ml conc. ammonia.

Working: Stock solution 2 parts, Ammonia 2 parts, Absolute methyl alcohol 3 parts.

Differentiator: Absolute methyl alcohol 40ml, Absolute ethyl alcohol 80ml, Distilled water 100ml.

Procedure

  1. Dewax and coat with 1% celloidin. Harden in 80% alcohol.
  2. Stain with Ehrlich's haematoxylin. Differentiate.
  3. Stain in Working Best's Carmine (closed jar) for 10-20 mins.
  4. Differentiate in Best's differentiator (1-5 mins).
  5. Dehydrate (celloidin dissolves), clear, mount.

Results

Glycogenbright red granules
Nucleiblue

Langhan's Method

Uses Lugol's iodine. Glycogen stains mahogany brown, tissues yellow. Non-permanent.

üíß Mucins

Mucins are mucopolysaccharides or mucoproteins with varying degrees of acidification. Common methods include PAS (for neutral mucins) and Alcian blue (for acid mucins).

PAS Reaction for Mucin

Remove glycogen from the section by diastase digestion. The staining method is same as for glycogen.

Southgate's Mucicarmine Method

Useful for epithelial mucins. Uses carmine, aluminium hydroxide and aluminium chloride boiled in 50% alcohol.

Staining procedure

  1. Bring section to water.
  2. Stain nuclei with haematoxylin; differentiate and blue.
  3. Stain for 35‚Äì45 minutes in the mucicarmine solution.
  4. Rinse, dehydrate, clear and mount.

Result: Epithelial mucin is red, nuclei are blue.

üîµ Acid Polysaccharides & Mucoproteins

Acid polysaccharides and acid mucoprotein are usually PAS negative. They are demonstrated by Alcian blue, Hale‚Äôs colloidal iron and metachromatic stains.

Alcian Blue Methods

Based on copper phthalocyanin basic dye. At pH 2.5 it stains acid mucosubstances.

‚öóÔ∏è

Staining Solution

Alcian blue 0.5g, Glacial acetic acid 3ml, Distilled water 100ml. Adjust pH to 2.5.

Method: Stain for 10 mins, rinse, wash, counterstain (red), dehydrate and mount.
Result: Acid mucopolysaccharides are blue, nuclei are red.

Metachromatic Staining Methods

Metachromatic dyes (e.g., Toluidine blue, Thionin) stain acid mucosubstances in a color different from the dye solution (e.g., red/purple instead of blue). Usually non-permanent.

Toluidine Blue Method

Uses 0.25g Toluidine blue in 100ml Michael's buffer pH 4.5. Stain for 10 seconds. Mount in glycerine jelly.
Result: Metachromatic substances are red/purple, nuclei blue.

Schmorl's Thionin Method

Uses saturated thionin. Sections treated with mercuric chloride first. Stained for 15-25 mins.
Result: Metachromatic substances red, nuclei blue.

Uranyl Nitrate Method

Uses 1% Toluidine blue followed by 0.2% Uranyl nitrate differentiation. Allows permanent mounting.
Result: Acid mucopolysaccharides are red/purple.
