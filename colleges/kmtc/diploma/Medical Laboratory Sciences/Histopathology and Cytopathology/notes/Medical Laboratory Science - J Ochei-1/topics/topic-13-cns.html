The Central Nervous System (CNS)

01

üìò Introduction

The anatomy of the central nervous system (CNS) is such that structurally, the whole system is divided into nerve cells (neurones) and their processes on one hand and the neuroglia (supporting cells) on the other. Extracellular components are present in the peripheral nerves and the meninges (protective layer of the brain and the spinal cord). There are millions of nerve cells which are interconnected in a most complex but orderly manner.

Structure Components
Neurone Cell body, Axon, Dendrites, Neurofibrils, Nissl substance, Myelin sheath
Neuroglia Astrocytes, Oligodendroglia, Microglia, Ependyma

There is no one stain or combination of stains, metallic impregnation or histochemical method which can demonstrate all the components of the CNS in a single preparation. For this reason, there are several and various methods for the demonstration of CNS components. A generated message is sent out by the nerve cell along its efferent fibre, the axon. The same axon receives the messages from other cells by means of short branched processes known as dendrites. The conducting fibres (axons) are 'insulated' or protected from each other. The 'insulation' is brought about by the action of a white fatty substance called the myelin, which surrounds each of the fibres. Parts of the brain and most peripheral nerves are myelinated. The abundance of myelin gives the surrounding areas and structures a whitish appearance, hence the name 'white matter'. Parts of the brain where the nerve cells are bound together have a grey colour and so they are called grey matter.

The nerve cells are supported by the neuroglia which form the specialised connective tissue of the CNS. Neuroglia, structurally consists of four types of cells‚Äîastrocytes, oligodendroglia, microglia and ependymal cells. A neurone is a complete cell consisting of several cytoplasmic structures including Golgi apparatus, mitochondria, inclusion bodies and Nissl substance (Nissl bodies, chromophil substances or trigoid substances), and the nucleus.

The responses of CNS to diseases or injury take place in the neurones and in the axons. The Nissl granules are lost and the nucleus moves from its central location to the periphery of the cytoplasm which begins to shrink. A myelinated fibre begins to degenerate and eventually is lost.

üî™ Preparation of Sections of CNS

The CNS is greatly prone to autolytic changes and so prompt fixation is essential. The fixation of CNS tissues has already been described under fixations, but it is worth remembering that 10% buffered formal saline is the best general purpose fixative for the CNS.

Paraffin Sections

The CNS tissues are dense and quite rich in lipids. They are therefore not easily penetrated by paraffin wax. It is recommended to leave large blocks in the paraffin wax bath for 24 hours. Distortion of the tissue can be reduced by using the celloidin-paraffin medium. Frozen sections of the CNS tissues are necessary in order to effect proper study of certain parts of the CNS.

üü£ Nissl Substance Staining

The form, size and distribution of Nissl substance vary in different types of neurones. Because of their rich content of ribonucleic acid (RNA), they are well stained by basic aniline dyes, e.g., thionin, azure A and Cresyl echt violet. The loss of Nissl substance during neuronal injury or disease is called ‚Äòchromatolysis‚Äô, and it is a valuable means of microscopically assessing the physiological status of the nerve cells. In the same way, the reappearance of the granules is indication of recovery of functions and conduction of the neurone.

Thionin Stain for Nissl Substance in Paraffin Section

Solutions

  1. Thionin stain
    • Thionin 0.5 g
    • Distilled water 100 ml
    • Acetic acid 2 drops
  2. Differentiating solution
    • Lute alcohol and 1,4-dioxane. Equal parts of thionin

Procedure

  1. Take section through xylene, dioxane and distilled water.
  2. Stain in thionin solution for five minutes.
  3. Differentiate until practically colourless in the differentiating solution.
  4. Pass through two changes of dioxane.
  5. Rinse in two changes of xylene.
  6. Mount in synthetic resin medium.

Results

Nissl substancebright blue
Backgroundcolourless

  2. Never place stained sections in water or alcohol before or after differentiation as both will remove the stain.

Cresyl Echt Violet for Nissl Substance in Paraffin Section

Solutions

  1. Cresyl echt violet: 0.5% aqueous solution of cresyl echt violet ripened for 24‚Äì48 hours. Filter before use.
  2. Balsam xylene solution: Equal parts of Canada balsam and xylene.

Procedure

  1. Bring section to distilled water.
  2. Stain in cresyl echt violet solution for five minutes.
  3. Rinse in two changes of distilled water.
  4. Rinse in 95% alcohol for 30 seconds.
  5. Rinse in absolute alcohol for 30 seconds.
  6. Rinse in xylene for one minute.
  7. Rinse in balsam xylene mixture for two minutes.
  8. Rinse in two changes of absolute alcohol for 10‚Äì30 seconds.
  9. Wash in 3‚Äì4 changes of xylene.
  10. Mount in synthetic resin medium.

Results: Nissl substance is blue.

Note: Steps 7‚Äì9 may have to be repeated several times.

04

‚ö° Neurones, Axons & Neurofibrils

Axons and dendrites are the two processes that originate from the cell body of the neurones. Dendrites, which are usually short and have many branches, lack myelin sheaths. They contain both Nissl substance and neurofibrils. Axons carry nerve impulses over a long distance. They terminate either on the dendrites or cell body of other neurones, thus forming a synapse, or end on special sites associated with an effector organ such as muscles or glands. The axon contains neurofibrils but no Nissl substance.

Neurofibrils are present in the cytoplasm of the cell body, axon and dendrites. Silver impregnation methods are utilised to demonstrate nerve fibres and neurofibrils, though most of these methods are capricious. They are invaluable for the study of CNS because of the clarity of images produced by them. They give a thick black impregnation of nerve cells and their processes.

Golgi-Cox Potassium Dichromate-Mercuric Chloride Method

This is a modification of the original Golgi method of 1878. The method is based on the treatment of pieces of central nervous system with potassium dichromate, followed by impregnation with silver nitrate.

Fixation

Golgi - Cox solution: 5% potassium dichromate (10 vols), 5% mercuric chloride (10 vols), 5% potassium chromate (8 vols), Distilled water (15‚Äì20 vols). Use at $37^{\circ}\text{C}$.

Place the tissue on a layer of glass wool in 20 times its volume of fixative solution. Change the fixative after 24 and 48 hours incubation. Then seal the container and keep in the dark at $37^{\circ}\text{C}$ for about 1‚Äì2 months.

Preparation of sections

After sufficient impregnation, soak the block in 20% solution of neutral dextrin for 1‚Äì3 days. Embed in celloidin. With the freezing microtome, cut section at 50 $\mu\text{m}$‚Äì100 $\mu\text{m}$.

Staining method

  1. Place the sections in 5% sodium carbonate for 1 hour in order to blacken the impregnation.
  2. Wash well in distilled water.
  3. Dehydrate in graded alcohols.
  4. Clear in creosote for a few minutes.
  5. Transfer to turpentine. Mount in synthetic resin.

Results: Nerve cells, Axon, Dendrites, Neuroglia are all black.

Bielschowsky's Method for Axons and Neurofibrils

Formalin fixed tissue block (3‚Äì6 weeks in formalin) are cut into thin slices of tissue and washed in running tap water for 24 hours, followed by several changes of distilled water.

Results

Neurofibrils, axons and dendritesblack
Backgroundgreyish

Holme's Method for Axons

This method is a modification of the Bodian copper Protargol method, and it gives more consistent and reliable results on formalin-fixed tissues. Protargol, a silver proteinate in the Bordian stain, never reaches the optimal impregnation. Holmes developed a buffered impregnating solution so that the specificity of the stain is determined during impregnation by the effect of the solution pH and the concentration of silver ions.

Results: Axons and neurofibrils are blue black, background is greyish.

üï∏Ô∏è Neuroglia Demonstration

Neuroglial cells provide the internal support for the central nervous system. They form the connective tissue of the CNS, and could be referred to as the ‚ÄòNerve glue‚Äô. Four types of cells make up the glia‚Äîastrocytes, oligodendroglia, microglia and ependymal cells.

Astrocytes

Stellate cells in two forms: protoplasmic (grey matter) and fibrous (white matter). They provide support and take part in metabolic exchanges. Involved in gliosis.

Oligodendroglia

Small cells involved in the formation and maintenance of myelin sheath. Found in both grey and white matter.

Microglia

Mesodermal in origin. Fixed ‚Äòscavenger‚Äô cells found throughout the brain and spinal cord.

Ependymal cells

Epithelial cells occurring along the ventricles and spinal canal. Form a selective barrier between CSF and nervous tissue.

Toluidine Blue for Nerve Cells and Glia

Fixation in alcohol gives the best result but formalin serves well too.

Procedure

  1. Pass the paraffin section through xylene, absolute alcohol and 95% alcohol.
  2. Place slides in alcoholic colophonium solution for 3‚Äì5 minutes.
  3. Rinse in two changes of 95% alcohol for three minutes each.
  4. Stain with toluidine blue for 30 seconds.
  5. Differentiate in aniline-alcohol until the background is clear.
  6. Rinse in oil of cajput until clear. Clear in two changes of xylene. Mount in synthetic resin.

Results

Nucleuspale blue
Nissl substancedark blue
Astrocytespale blue
Oligodendrogliavery dark blue

Cajal's Gold Chloride-Sublimate Method for Astrocytes

This is a gold impregnation method that selectively stains astrocytes, and it is performed on frozen sections. Fixation is in Cajal‚Äôs formalin-ammonium bromide mixture (FAB).

Result: Astrocytes are opaque purplish-black.

Phosphotungstic Acid-Haematoxylin (PTAH) (Mallory's Modification)

This method which was described for connective tissue, stains glial fibres more satisfactorily when slightly modified. For glial fibres, decolourisation with sodium thiosulphate is missed. This method which is particularly useful for the demonstration of hyperplasia and hypertrophy of fibrous astrocytes, can be used with paraffin or cellulose nitrate sections.

Results: Astrocytes, Nuclei, Fibrin, Myelin are blue. Collagen is rose or brick red.

üéóÔ∏è Myelin

Myelin is the white fatty material that forms an ‚Äòinsulation‚Äô for the nerve fibres. The myelin is formed by the Schwann cells in the peripheral nervous system and oligodendroglia in the central nervous system. It consists of a mixture of lipids and proteins.

Weil‚Äôs Haematoxylin Method for Myelin

This is a regressive staining method in which the sections are initially overstained and then differentiated in two stages. Formalin fixed tissue with or without a chrome salt mordanting is used.

Results

Myelin sheathblue to blue black
Red blood cellsvery black
Nucleidark grey

Osmium Tetroxide Method

This simple method is very useful for the demonstration of normal and degenerating myelin. The use of osmium tetroxide is however, hazardous and costly. Fresh or formalin fixed tissue may be used.

Results: Healthy myelin is black or dark grey. Degenerating myelin shows black granular deposits.

Pal-Weigert Method for Myelin

The staining mixture (haematoxylin and lithium carbonate) forms a lake with the tissue components. Potassium permanganate is used for the first differentiation and also aids in confining the action of the dye lake on the myelin sheath and red blood cells. Pal‚Äôs bleach is used for the second and final differentiation. Oxalic acid removes excess permanganate from the section.

Results: Myelin sheaths are dark blue. Other structures are unstained.

Luxol Fast Blue Method for Myelin

Luxol fast blue is a dye of the sulphonated copper phthalocyanine type that is alcohol soluble. It has affinity for phospholipid of myelin. Ten per cent formal-saline is fixative of choice. Paraffin, cellulose nitrate or frozen sections may be used.

Results

Myelindeep purplish blue
Erythrocytesdeep purplish blue
Nucleipurplish-blue (or red if counterstained)
Nissl substancepurplish-blue (or red if counterstained)

Luxol fast blue-oil red O

This method demonstrates fat as well as myelin. Ten per cent neutral buffered formalin is the fixative of choice.

Results: Fat is red, Myelin sheath is blue.
