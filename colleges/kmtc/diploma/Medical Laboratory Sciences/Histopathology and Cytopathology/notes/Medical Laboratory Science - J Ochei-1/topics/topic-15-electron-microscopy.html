üìò Introduction

There are several techniques and methods required and employed for transmission electron microscopy (TEM) by the electron microscope (EM) histotechnologist. Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) is not a fast diagnostic procedure since it takes several days for results as opposed to frozen sections which takes an hour or less, or general routine histology that produces results within 24‚Äì48 hours. It nevertheless offers information and answers to questions in long term diseases involving the kidneys, lungs and tumours among others. In most cases, such information complements light microscopy, histochemistry and histoimmunology.

üß™ Fixation

The most important step in preparing material for electron microscopy is the fixation. It is very essential to fix surgical tissue within 15 minutes of it being removed from the patient‚Äôs body. This is to prevent cellular changes due to autolysis and to obtain a correct picture of the cells in the tissue.

Tissue Preparation

Tissues taken for fixation should be minced in about 1 ml volume of fixative in order to ensure proper infiltration and fixation of all portions of the tissue especially with the slow penetrating fixatives.

Commonly Used Fixatives

Zamboni‚Äôs fixative

This is a mixture of picric acid and paraformaldehyde that is buffered with phosphate. It penetrates fairly rapidly, preserves optimally and is stable at room temperature for up to one year. This fixative allows post-osmication, and allows the fixed material to be kept at room temperature.

Paraformaldehyde-glutaraldehyde

This mixture is also sometimes called formaldehyde glutaldehyde (F-G) because of the dissociation of paraformaldehyde to formaldehyde. It penetrates rapidly with little shrinkage. It is important to make it fresh because it tends to become too acidic and it should be kept in the refrigerator. Microtubules are well preserved but lipids are extracted.

Glutaraldehyde, 2-3%

This is a slow acting fixative. The big advantage in its favour is its excellent preservation of tissues. It must be prepared fresh and regularly checked for acidity as the pH decreases with time. Ideally stock solutions in 8% or 25% concentration are prepared and sealed in vials which are kept in the refrigerator until ready to use.

Osmium tetroxide

This is used as 1% in phosphate buffered saline (PBS), pH 7.4. It is stable for only few days in the refrigerator when diluted. It must be used with caution and in the fume cabinet because it is a very caustic substance.

Dehydration

Dehydration of tissues for EM is carried out starting with a lower grade of alcohol (40%). The concentrations and number of grades chosen depends upon the type of tissue.

Clearing

The clearing agents in EM are referred to as transitional fluids and consist of media miscible with ethanol and embedding medium. Examples are xylene, toluene and epoxypropane (propylene oxide).

Embedding

Thin sections (50‚Äì90 nm) are used in TEM in order for the electrons to penetrate and allow photons to reach the fluorescent viewing screen of the EM. For routine purposes, the epoxy resins are employed. These resins are relatively easy to handle and are fairly easily available. Paraffin is much too soft to permit ultra thin sections to be cut.

The most commonly used types of epoxy resins are those with an Araldite base and those with an Epon 812 base. Araldite, a resin that is relatively stable under electron bombardments, is soluble in acetone and ethanol. Araldite formulas for embedding must be prepared with great care so as to obtain the correct consistency. Epon resin is also stable in the face of electron bombardment.

04

üî™ Sectioning for Electron Microscope

Thick and thin sections are cut with an ultramicrotome. The thick sections are cut at between 0.5 and 2 mm and they are used to study general picture of the tissue and to locate area of special interest, such as a glomerulus in a kidney biopsy. These sections are cut with glass knives.

Thin sections are usually cut at between 50 and 90 nm and appear grey in reflected light colours. These sections are cut with diamond knives if available, otherwise fresh or new glass knives may also be used. Some laboratories prefer to make their own glass knives instead of purchasing from knife making companies.

Materials Used for Cutting Thick and Thin Sections

  1. Microscope slides for thick sections: Cleaned slides with frosted ends, with a ringed area in the centre made with wax pencil when slides are warmed.
  2. Applicator sticks with whittled tips: These are used to clean knife edge after soaking them in distilled water.
  3. Hair brushes: These are used for orienting thick and thin sections for pick up and removal of unwanted sections. It is best to use eyelashes or straight hair.
  4. Rose lens paper: This lens paper is used for wiping debris from trough surfaces and drying grids.
  5. 2 ml disposable syringe with needle: This is for filling and adjusting floatation fluid level in the knife trough.
  6. Floatation medium: The medium is distilled water or acetone in a small container. The 2 ml syringe and needle is used to withdraw the medium to fill the knife trough.
05

üé® Staining

Staining of Thick Sections

A variety of stains has been used to stain thick sections in electron microscopy. The most effective of these stains are the basic dyes. Toluidine blue and Trypan blue in 1% sodium borate have proved very useful. The toluidine blue is metachromatic in reaction and accentuates cytoplasmic differentiation. Equally important is the fact that it is simple and easy to make up and also gives reproducible results. Trypan blue is used as 2% in 1% sodium borate. It is similar to toluidine blue in action and procedures.

Staining of Thin Sections

Uranyl and lead stains are the more widely employed stains for thin sections. An important factor common to both stains is the possession of electron dense metal which is essential for proper contrast in cellular components for photomicrography.

Uranyl acetate

A nuclear stain that specifically binds to nuclear proteins. It is used in saturated concentration in distilled water. It has a tendency to break down or dissociate so that the stock solution should not be kept in the refrigerator beyond one month. It is also soluble in alcohol. Many workers prefer to use 25% to 50% concentration in ethyl or methyl alcohol. The alcoholic solution penetrates faster and requires a shorter staining time of about 2 to 5 minutes.

Lead citrate

A cytoplasmic stain. It intensifies the detail of cellular membrane and inclusions.
