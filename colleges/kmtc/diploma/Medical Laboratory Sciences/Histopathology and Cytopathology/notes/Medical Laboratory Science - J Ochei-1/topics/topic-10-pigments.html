01

üìò Classification of Pigments

Pigments are substances found in living matter. They absorb visible light. They differ in their origin, composition and significance. Pigments are classified into:

  1. Artifacts
  2. Endogenous pigments
  3. Exogenous pigments
02

‚ö†Ô∏è Artifacts

These are pigments that result from methods used during processing of the tissues.

Formalin pigment

This is a dark brown pigment formed by the action of acidic formaldehyde on blood. The pigment is also known as acid formaldehyde haematein. It is removed by treating sections with alcoholic picric acid.

Mercuric pigments

These are grey black granular deposits resulting from the use of mercury containing fixatives. They are removed by treating sections with alcoholic iodine, followed by sodium thiosulphate.

Chrome deposits

These brownish black granular deposits result when tissues fixed in chrome fixatives are treated with alcohol. The chrome-fixed tissues must be washed in running tap water for 12 to 18 hours in order to remove excess chromate from the tissue before dehydration in alcohol.

Stain precipitates

These are highly coloured, amorphous and granular precipitates seen in stained sections. They result from improper staining procedures. They are common if saturated solution of stain is allowed to evaporate or dry up on the section.

03

ü©∏ Haemoglobin

Haemoglobin is the most widely encountered haematogenous pigment in the tissue. Despite its presence in large amounts in all tissues, it is difficult to stain. It is poorly preserved following fixation in such fixatives as 'Susa'; it is well stained by acid dyes after fixation in formalin.

Dunn Thompson Method for Haemoglobin

This staining method gives very reliable and quick results. It is a modified van Gieson staining method.

üìù

Method

  1. Bring section to water.
  2. Stain in matured aqueous solution of alum haematoxylin for 15 minutes.
  3. Wash in tap water.
  4. Mordant for 1 minute in aqueous solution of 4% ferric ammonium sulphate (iron alum).
  5. Stain in the aqueous alum haematoxylin solution for 10 minutes.
  6. Rinse rapidly in tap water.
  7. Stain in van Gieson solution.
  8. Transfer directly to 95% alcohol and differentiate for 1‚Äì3 minutes.
  9. Dehydrate, clear and mount.

Results

Red blood corpusclesgreenish-black
Haemoglobin casts or ingested haemoglobinlight shades of green

Benzidine Method for Haemoglobin

This staining reaction is based on the action of the enzyme peroxidase in the transfer of oxygen from hydrogen peroxide, oxidising benzidine to a blue dye.

Safety Warning

Benzidine is carcinogenic; substitutes such as 3-amino-9 ethylcarbazole may be used.

Method: Stain in benzidine/nitroprusside solution (10 mins) then in peroxidase reagent (20-30 mins).
Result: Erythrocytes are black, other tissues light grey.

04

üî© Haemosiderins (Iron)

Haemosiderin consists of ferric hydroxide ($\text{Fe(OH)}_3$) bound to protein, ferritin. It appears in about 2 or 3 days after the release of haemoglobin. It is a granular, yellowish brown pigment; soluble in acids but not in alkalis. Buffered neutral formalin should be used for fixation.

Perls‚Äô Prussian Blue Reaction

The principle of this reaction is based on the combination of ferric (inorganic) iron with potassium ferrocyanide in acid solution to form ferric ferrocyanide (Prussian blue).

$$4\text{FeCl}_3 + 3\text{K}_4\text{Fe(CN)}_6 \longrightarrow \text{Fe}_4[\text{Fe(CN)}_6]_3 + 12\text{KCl}$$

[Ferric chloride] + [Potassium ferrocyanide] $\longrightarrow$ [Ferric ferrocyanide (Prussian blue)] + [Potassium Chloride]

Procedure

  1. Bring section to tap water. Rinse in distilled water.
  2. Stain with a mixture of equal volumes of 2% potassium ferrocyanide and 2% hydrochloric acid in distilled water for 20-30 minutes.
  3. Wash in distilled water.
  4. Counterstain with 0.5% aqueous eosin or 1% neutral red.
  5. Dehydrate, clear and mount.

Results

Haemosiderin and ferric saltsblue
Cell nucleipink to red

Gomori's Modification

Place section in freshly made 10% aqueous potassium ferrocyanide for 5-10 minutes. Then add 10% solution of HCl in an amount equal to half the volume of the ferrocyanide solution used. Mix and leave for 20 minutes. This gives sharper staining because ferrocyanide penetrates before acid releases the iron.

Turnbull‚Äôs Blue Reaction for Ferrous Iron

This staining reaction is based on the formation of a blue precipitate (Turnbull‚Äôs blue) by the reaction between ferrous salts and potassium ferricyanide.

$$3\text{FeCl}_2 + 2\text{K}_3\text{Fe(CN)}_6 \longrightarrow \text{Fe}_3[\text{Fe(CN)}_6]_2 + 6\text{KCl}$$

Result: Ferrous salts stain blue.

05

üü° Bile Pigments

Bile pigments are iron-free. They are soluble in tissues and do not give primary fluorescence.

Gmelin‚Äôs Method

Based on progressive oxidation of bilirubin by nitric acid. Smear nitric acid over section under coverslip. Yellowish-brown pigment changes to green, then blue and purple.

Stein‚Äôs Iodine Test

Based on oxidation of pigments to green biliverdin by iodine.

  1. Stain in mixture of Lugol‚Äôs iodine and tincture of iodine overnight.
  2. Decolourise with sodium thiosulphate.
  3. Counterstain with neutral red.

Result: Bilirubin is green.

06

üåë Melanin

Melanin is a granular black or yellowish-brown pigment formed from tyrosine by tyrosinase. It is insoluble in organic solvents but soluble in 1M sodium hydroxide. It reduces ammoniacal silver nitrate to metallic silver (argentaffin reaction).

Masson-Fontana Ammoniacal Silver Reaction

This method is the argentaffin reaction which is based on the ability of tyrosine derivatives to reduce silver solutions to metallic silver.

Procedure

  1. Treat with Gram‚Äôs iodine solution.
  2. Place in silver solution in the dark for 18‚Äì24 hours.
  3. Tone in 0.1% gold chloride.
  4. Fix in sodium thiosulphate.
  5. Counterstain with safranin or neutral red.

Result: Melanin and Argentaffin cell granules stain black.

Bleaching Methods

Bleaching helps distinguish melanin from other pigments (like lipofuscin which resists bleaching longer).

  • 40% peracetic acid: Bleaches melanin in 2‚Äì14 hours.
  • Chlorate method: Potassium chlorate + HCl. 24 hours.
  • Peroxide method: 10% hydrogen peroxide. 24‚Äì36 hours.
  • Permanganate method: Potassium permanganate followed by oxalic acid.

üïØÔ∏è Lipofuscin & Other Pigments

Lipofuscin

Lipofuscin pigments are produced by oxidation of lipids. They reduce silver and ferricyanide solutions, are Sudan black positive, and PAS positive.

Schmorl‚Äôs Method for Lipofuscin

Based on reduction of ferricyanide to ferrocyanide (Turnbull's blue).
Result: Lipofuscins stain dark blue.

Exogenous Pigments

Carbon

Found in lungs (anthracosis). Yellowish brown particles insoluble in all solvents, resists bleaching.

Iron ore

Found in lungs of miners. May give positive Prussian blue reaction.

Copper

Seen in Wilson‚Äôs disease. Demonstrated by rubeanic acid method (forms copper rubeanate).

Asbestos

Long thin crystalline needles. Gold-yellow unstained. Positive Prussian blue reaction.

Calcium salts can be demonstrated by Alizarin red S or von Kossa methods.

von Kossa‚Äôs Method

A metal substitution technique where silver replaces calcium in salts (carbonate/phosphate) and is reduced to metallic silver by light.

Procedure

  1. Immerse in 5% silver nitrate for 5‚Äì10 minutes.
  2. Expose to bright sunlight or UV light (10-20 mins) or strong electric bulb (60 mins).
  3. Treat with sodium thiosulphate to remove unreacted silver.
  4. Counterstain with safranin O.
