01

üìò Introduction

When histology and biochemistry come together, the science is known as Histochemistry. This branch of science aims at identifying chemical substances and determine their significance at the cellular level. Enzyme histochemistry is therefore a sub-division of this field that specifically deals with enzymes.

Enzymes are catalysts that speed up chemical reactions that occur in living cells. In addition to accelerating reactions, enzymes are highly specific in their activity. They act on specific substrates‚Äîthe active reagents that must be present for enzyme activity to occur. In ordinary histological demonstration, there is a direct interaction between the tissue constituents in the section and a dye or reagent to produce visible colouration of the tissue. With enzyme histochemistry, the final opaque or coloured product is derived from the substrate rather than the tissue. Enzymes are very unstable and almost all of them disappear from the tissues quickly and progressively after removal of tissue from the body and after cellular death. Many of them are simply dissolved in the cell cytoplasm (soluble enzymes) and others are tightly attached to certain cell organelles such as mitochondria and lysosomes. Special precautions are necessary to preserve them.

Factors Affecting Enzyme Demonstration

The factors that influence enzyme demonstration should well be borne in mind in order to successfully identify the enzymes and determine their significance. The following factors affect enzyme demonstration:

  1. Optimal temperature: Optimal temperature is one at which maximal enzyme activity is achieved. Most enzymes require incubation at temperatures between $25^{\circ}\text{C}$ and $37^{\circ}\text{C}$.
  2. Optimal pH: Maximal enzyme activity will occur at an optimal pH level. In majority of cases, this pH level is close to the neutral on the pH scale. The optimal pH may vary according to the substrate used.
  3. Concentration: The speed of enzyme reaction is greatly influenced by the amount (concentration) of the enzyme, the substrate, and of the reaction product.
  4. Inhibitors: Enzyme activity is affected by the presence of inhibitors. Some inhibitors are specific when they are capable of affecting only particular enzymes; or non specific when they do act on nearly all the enzymes.
  5. Activators: These are substances that further enhance enzyme activity.

üî¨ Specimen Preparation

Histochemical methods for enzymes have played a great role in the identification and evaluation of enzyme activity. Many specimen preparations have been tried and used over the years until the arrival of refrigerated microtome (cryostat). The important aim always is that tissue must be prepared in such a way as to preserve and retain the enzymes as much as possible in their original sites. Enzymes should not deteriorate or diffuse during fixation and the final colour reaction should be sharp and precise. Fresh frozen (cryostat) sections have almost completely superseded sections of freeze-dried tissue, tissue that has been fixed in conventional fixatives and sectioned on carbon dioxide freezing microtome; or fixed tissues embedded in paraffin.

Fresh Tissue Preparations

The fresh unfixed tissue may be frozen by the cryostat freezer plate method, or the liquid nitrogen immersion method or by the pre-chilled isopentane method which is particularly suitable for muscle tissue. Within 3 or 4 minutes of obtaining fresh tissue, cryostat sections can be prepared and introduced into the substrate solution thus bypassing the complicated and prolonged processing. This method of tissue preparation for enzyme demonstration is fast and easy to handle.

Fixed Tissue Preparations

Some soluble enzymes survive fixation to the extent that sufficient amounts of the enzyme is retained for demonstration. Fixed tissues tend to show just few diffusion artefacts. Ice-cold acetone is the fixative of choice, alternatively ice-cold alcohol or formalin or ice-cold formal-calcium can be used. Paraffin embedding must be brief at $56^{\circ}\text{C}$ in the vacuum oven. It is also possible to cut frozen sections following complete fixation especially with ice-cold formalin. Also, cryostat sections can be post-fixed for 10‚Äì30 minutes in any of the aforementioned ice-cold reagents. This method is particularly useful for such enzymes as the phosphatase and lipases.

Controls

False negative or false positive reactions can easily occur due to certain factors associated with the general procedures for enzyme demonstration.

False-negative reactions may be caused by:

  • Insufficient amount of enzyme.
  • Non-optimal incubation conditions.
  • Substrate degeneration.
  • Solubility or diffusion of either enzyme or the final product of the enzyme activity.

False-positive reactions may be due to:

  • Non-enzymatic splitting of the substrate.
  • Diffusion of the enzyme or the final product of enzyme activity away from the initial positive site.
  • Non-specific affinity of tissue components for reagents used in the procedure.
  • Confusion of natural pigments with coloured end product of the reaction.

A known positive and a known negative control must be treated in conjunction with the sections under investigation. Negative controls are usually prepared by omission of an essential step (substrate), specific inhibitors, or non-specific destruction (boiling water, strong acids).

03

‚öóÔ∏è Reaction Process

Most of the enzyme histochemical methods are based on the formation of a primary reaction product (PRP) as a result of the action of the enzyme upon the substrate. Following this, is the production of a coloured or opaque final reaction product (FRP) from the combination of PRP with a second reagent.

Azo-dye method

For example the blackening of the PRP by chemical reaction with a diazonium salt to form a diazo-dye. The enzyme activity frees the naphthol moiety on the substrate (containing naphthol compounds), which combines with the diazonium salt. When the production of the PRP and its transformation as a coloured FRP take place at the same time, it is called 'simultaneous coupling'. In a reaction where the two reactions occur one after the other, it is 'post incubation coupling'.

Indoxyl substrates

Some procedures employ indoxyl substrates. The enzyme activity results in the release of indoxyl radicals which are then rapidly oxidised to insoluble indigo blue final products.

Metal salt capture

There are other methods that use metal salts when one of the products of enzyme activity is an acid. The released acid is 'captured' by a metal ion in the incubation medium and so precipitated. Additional reagents are used up and ultimately an insoluble coloured salt is formed at the site of enzyme activity.

Redox reaction

The redox (oxidation‚Äìreduction) reaction methods involve the removal of hydrogen from one substance (oxidation) and transferring it to another (reduction). A good number of oxidative enzymes are demonstrated by the methods that depend on the transfer of hydrogen from the substrate to a tetrazolium salt. The tetrazolium salt is then reduced to a water insoluble purple formazan pigment at the site of enzyme activity.

üß™ Alkaline Phosphatase

The alkaline phosphatase hydrolyses at alkaline pH while the acid phosphotase does it at acid pH. Alkaline phosphatase is widely distributed in tissues. It is usually activated by certain ions such as magnesium, manganese, zinc and cobalt. It is inhibited by cysteine, cyanides and arsenates. The optimum pH for activity is 9.27 but the range is 9.0‚Äì9.6.

Azo-dye Coupling Method

The substrate used in this method is a solution of alpha naphthyl phosphate. The alpha naphthol moiety is released by the enzyme and is coupled with the diazonium salt to form an insoluble coloured final reaction product.

Solutions

  1. Dissolve 10‚Äì20 mg of sodium alpha-naphthyl phosphate in 20 ml of TRIS buffer at pH 9.1. Add 20 mg of Fast-Red TR or Fast Black B and stir well. This should be prepared fresh.
  2. Mayer‚Äôs haemalum.

Procedure

  1. Filter the freshly prepared substrate-diazonium salt on the slides. Leave for 15‚Äì60 minutes.
  2. Wash in running water for 2‚Äì3 minutes.
  3. Counterstain in Mayer‚Äôs haemalum for 1‚Äì2 minutes.
  4. Wash and blue nuclei in running water for 20‚Äì30 minutes.
  5. Mount in glycerine jelly.

Result: Site of enzyme activity is brown (Fast-Red) or black (Fast Black B).

Indoxyl Method

When sections are incubated in a buffer containing either iodoindoxyl phosphate or bromoindoxyl phosphate as substrate, orthophosphate and indoxyl derivative are released. The indoxyl derivative is oxidised to insoluble indigo blue to locate the site of enzyme activity.

Result: Sites of enzyme activity show fine precipitate of indigo blue.

Calcium Phosphate Method

Phosphate ions which are released from the substrate glycerophosphate are precipitated as calcium phosphate. The calcium is then substituted for cobalt and seen as opaque cobalt sulphide. The site of enzyme activity is brownish-black.

05

üß™ Acid Phosphatase

Acid phosphatase hydrolyses orthophosphate at an acid pH. The optimum pH for the enzyme activity is 5.0. It is inhibited by fluoride but not activated by magnesium.

Indoxyl Method

When sections are incubated in a buffered medium with 5-iodoindoxyl phosphate or 5-bromoindoxyl phosphate as substrate, the activity of the enzyme at acid pH releases orthophosphate and indoxyl derivatives. By the enhancing oxidising action of potassium ferricyanide and potassium ferrocyanide solution, the indoxyl derivatives are changed to insoluble indigo blue precipitates.

Procedure

  1. Mix 0.00192 g substrate with sodium chloride, distilled water, Citrate buffer (pH 5.4), Potassium ferricyanide, and Potassium ferrocyanide. Pour onto sections.
  2. Incubate at $37^{\circ}\text{C}$ for 60 minutes.
  3. Rinse in distilled water. Counterstain with 0.5% safranin O. Rinse. Mount.

Result: Sites of enzyme activity show fine precipitate of indigo blue.

Coupling Azo-dye Method

The enzyme hydrolyses the substrate, alpha naphthyl phosphate, to liberate alpha naphthol which couples with a diazonium salt to form an insoluble coloured azo-dye.

Result: Sites of acid phosphatase activity are reddish brown.

06

‚ö° Esterases & Peptidases

Esterases

The enzymes known as esterases hydrolyse esters of carboxylic acid. They are sub-classified into aliesterases (non-specific) and azolesterases (cholinesterase and acetylcholinesterase).

Thiocholine Method for Cholinesterase

The cholinesterases will hydrolyse acetylcholine and the liberated thiocholine is precipitated as its cupric salt.

  1. Incubate sections in the incubating medium (containing Acetylthiocholine iodide) at $37^{\circ}\text{C}$ for 20‚Äì40 minutes.
  2. Rinse in sodium sulphate.
  3. Place in dilute ammonium sulphide for two minutes. Rinse. Mount.

Result: Sites of enzyme activity are dark brown (cupric sulphide).

Indoxyl Method for Esterases

The substrate is indoxyl acetate, the esterases hydrolyse the substrate to release indoxyl. Additional oxidisers facilitate the transformation of the diffusable product to insoluble indigo blue.

Peptidases

These enzymes, also referred to as aminopeptidases, hydrolyse peptide bonds. They release $\beta$-naphthylamine from the substrate and this reaction product couples with a diazo-compound to form an insoluble coloured final reaction product.

Demonstration of Leucine Aminopeptidase (LAP)

The incubating medium consists of the substrate L-leucyl-$\beta$-naphthylamide and a diazonium salt, Fast Blue B.

Result: Sites of leucine aminopeptidase activity are purplish blue.

Safety Warning

07

üî• Oxidative Enzymes

The oxidative enzymes include the dehydrogenases, diaphorases, oxidases and peroxidases.

Succinate Dehydrogenase

This is an anaerobic dehydrogenase which catalyses the reversible oxidation of succinic acid to fumaric acid. In the presence of a tetrazolium salt (Nitro BT), the transfer of hydrogen reduces the tetrazolium salt to water-insoluble coloured formazan.

Result: Sites of enzyme activity show blue purple deposits of formazan pigment.

NADPH Diaphorase

Flavoprotein enzymes capable of transferring hydrogen from reduced nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate (NADPH) to dyes. Tetrazolium salt is a ready hydrogen acceptor.

Result: Site of enzyme activity is purplish.

Cytochrome Oxidase ('Nadi' reaction)

Cytochrome oxidase catalyses the oxidative reaction between alpha-naphthol and dimethyl-para-phenylenediamine to form indophenol blue.

Result: Sites of cytochrome enzyme activity are bluish violet.

DOPA-oxidase (Tyrosinase)

The enzyme is demonstrated by the action of Di-hydroxyphenylalanine (DOPA), which converts tyrosine into DOPA which is eventually converted to melanin.

Result: DOPA-oxidase (tyrosinase) site is dark brown granules.

Peroxidase (Benzidine Method)

Peroxidases catalyse the removal of oxygen from hydrogen peroxide to a suitable acceptor and release free oxygen. Benzidine is oxidised to blue dye by the free oxygen.

Result: Peroxidase granules are dark blue.

Safety Warning

Benzidine is carcinogenic and should be handled with care.
