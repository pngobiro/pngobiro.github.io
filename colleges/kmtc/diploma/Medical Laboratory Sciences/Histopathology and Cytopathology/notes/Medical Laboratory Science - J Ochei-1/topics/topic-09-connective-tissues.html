üìò Collagen and Reticulin Overview

Collagen consists of coarse extracellular framework (or scaffolding). The collagen fibres are the coarse connective tissue fibres. The fibres stain red with van Gieson's stain; and yellow or brown with silver impregnation.

Reticulin comprises a fibrillary extracellular scaffolding found throughout the tissues of the body. The fibrils are delicate and stain black on impregnation with silver and appear as unstained or a faint pink colour with van Gieson's stain. Collagen and reticulin are so similar that some workers believe that they are same, and that reticulin precedes the collagen.

ü•à Silver Impregnation Methods

There are many methods of silver impregnation of reticulin. They are based on the local reduction and selective precipitation of silver by the aldehyde groups of the carbohydrates of reticulin. Some of these methods depend on silver oxide or hydroxide in ammoniacal solution. Many of these are prepared by producing a precipitate from silver nitrate with sodium, potassium, or ammonium hydroxide or with lithium or sodium carbonate.

Technical Precautions

Silver impregnation methods require very scrupulous techniques in order to produce reliable consistent results. All glasswares used for the stain and in the staining technique should be thoroughly washed in 10% nitric acid and then rinsed in several changes of distilled water. Plastic forceps should be used for manipulating slides during staining. This is to prevent the non-specific precipitation of silver on the slide if metallic forceps are used. In order to prevent deterioration and precipitation of silver, dust must be avoided. Splashing or the use of glass rods without careful washing between solutions should also be avoided.

Modified Gomori's Method for Reticulin Fibres

This is the most reliable method for demonstrating reticulin fibres and for the differentiation of reticulin and collagen fibres.

Preparation of Silver Solution

  1. Add 5 ml of 10% potassium hydroxide to 20 ml of 10% silver nitrate. A precipitate is formed. Mark the fluid level on the flask.
  2. Pour off the supernatant.
  3. Wash the precipitated silver with distilled water twice until the water is clear.
  4. Fill up to the marked level with fresh distilled water. (This gives a clearer solution.)
  5. Add 28% ammonia water drop by drop while continuously shaking until all the precipitate is dissolved.
  6. Carefully add 10% silver nitrate solution, drop by drop until the precipitate that form disappears on shaking.
  7. Add an equal volume of distilled water to the solution.
  8. Store in a well stoppered dark bottle. It may be used for 2 days.

Procedure

  1. Bring paraffin section to water.
  2. Oxidise with 0.5 % aqueous potassium permanganate for two minutes.
  3. Rinse in distilled or tap water for two minutes.
  4. Place in two per cent aqueous potassium metabisulphite until colourless (about one minute).
  5. Wash in distilled or tap water for 2‚Äì5 minutes.
  6. Place in two per cent aqueous ferric ammonium sulphate for one minute to sensitise.
  7. Wash in distilled or tap water for 1‚Äì2 minutes and then for 30 seconds in each of two changes of distilled water.
  8. Place in the ammoniacal silver solution to impregnate for one minute.
  9. Rinse in distilled water for 20 seconds.
  10. Reduce in 20% formalin (10 ml formaldehyde plus 40 ml distilled water) for three minutes.
  11. Wash in distilled water for three minutes.
  12. Tone in 0.2 % gold chloride solution for 10 minutes.
  13. Rinse in distilled water for two minutes.
  14. Place in 3% aqueous potassium metabisulphite for one minute to reduce toning. Rinse in distilled water.
  15. Fix in 2.5 % aqueous sodium thiosulphate (hypo) for 1‚Äì2 minute.
  16. Stain with Cole‚Äôs haematoxylin for five minutes.
  17. Wash in running tap water for five minutes.
  18. Dehydrate, clear and mount.

üß¨ Staining of Collagen

Certain acid aniline dyes such as aniline blue, acid fuchsin, methyl blue and indigo carmine are very useful for the demonstration of collagen and reticulin fibres. They stain selectively with the acid aniline dyes in fairly strong acid solutions. Picric acid is widely employed because in addition to providing acidity and acting as a counterstain for muscle and cell cytoplasm, it forms with the dyes a complex which seems to have a great affinity for collagen. The basis of stains such as van Gieson (Picrofuchsin) is formed on the foregoing reactions.

Weigert‚Äôs Iron-Haematoxylin and van Gieson Stain

This is a widely used stain for differentiating muscle fibres and connective tissue.

Solution Preparation

1. Haematoxylin (solution A): Haematoxylin 1 g, Ethyl alcohol 95% 100 ml.

2. Iron chloride (solution B): Iron chloride (ferric) 29 % aqueous solution 4 ml, Hydrochloric acid 1 ml, Distilled water 95 ml.

3. Working solution: Mix equal volumes of Solution A and B just before use. Use within 2 days.

4. van Gieson‚Äôs Stain: Saturated aqueous picric acid 100 ml, 1% acid fuchsin 10 ml.

Results

  • Cell nuclei: black
  • Collagen: red
  • Muscle fibres: yellow
  • Cell cytoplasm: yellow
  • Red blood cells, fibrin: yellow

Procedure

  1. Bring sections to water.
  2. Stain with Weigert‚Äôs iron haematoxylin for 20 minutes.
  3. Wash in tap water.
  4. Differentiate in 1% acid alcohol, blue in tap water (control the degree of differentiation microscopically until the nuclei are just overstained).
  5. Counterstain with van Gieson‚Äôs stain for 3‚Äì5 minutes.
  6. Rinse rapidly in distilled water or blot lightly. Do not use tap water at this stage.
  7. Dehydrate rapidly in 95% alcohol and ethanol, clear and mount in acid balsam or DPX.

Rapid one Step Trichrome Stain (Gomori 1950)

This is a simple trichrome stain that demonstrates collagen fibres well.

Reagents

Solution 1: Cole‚Äôs, Harris or Mayer‚Äôs haematoxylin.
Solution 2: Chromotrope 2 R (0.6 g), Fast green FCF (0.3 g), Phosphotungstic acid (0.6 g), Glacial acetic acid (1.0 ml), Distilled water (100.0 ml).

Method

  1. Bring section to water.
  2. Stain with solution 1 for 5 minutes.
  3. Wash well in tap water. Rinse in distilled water.
  4. Stain in solution 2 for 15‚Äì20 minutes.
  5. Rinse well in 0.2 % acetic acid.
  6. Blot, dehydrate in absolute alcohol, clear and mount in synthetic resin medium.

üß∂ Elastic Fibres

Elastic tissue is present in many animal tissues such as skin, ligaments, blood vessels, lung and especially in the wall of aorta. It occurs as fine, sometimes branching fibres like in the dermis, or in laminated sheets as in blood vessels. It is subject to alteration, e.g., the wrinkled skin of old age is largely due to atrophy of elastic fibres.

Elastic fibres are very insoluble in organic and inorganic solvents, unlike the collagen fibres that are swollen and soluble in 2% acetic acid. They are resistant to most enzymes except elastase of the pancreas, and also resistant to autolytic changes. Elastic tissue is autofluorescent and gives greenish yellow (blue if unstained) fluorescence in ultra violet light. Elastic fibres are stained by both acid and basic dyes. Many methods are available for their selective staining but most of the staining methods are empirical.

Gomori‚Äôs Aldehyde-Fuchsin Method

Reagent: Basic fuchsin 0.5 g, 70% ethanol 100.0 ml, Paraldehyde (USP) 1.0 ml, Concentrated HCl 1.0 ml. Shake well and leave at room temperature for 24‚Äì48 hours until deep purple.

Procedure

  1. Bring section to water. Treat with Lugol‚Äôs iodine for 30 minutes. Bleach in 5% sodium thiosulphate.
  2. Wash in running tap water for 3 minutes. Rinse in 90% ethanol.
  3. Stain in aldehyde-fuchsin for 5‚Äì10 minutes. Rinse in 90% ethanol.
  4. Counterstain with light green. Dehydrate, clear and mount.

Result: Elastic fibres are deep purple.

Verhoeff‚Äôs Elastic Fibre Stain

Verhoeff‚Äôs solution: To 30 ml of 5% alcoholic haematoxylin, add 12 ml of 10% aqueous ferric chloride ($\mathrm{FeCl}_3$); mix and then add 12 ml of Lugol's iodine and mix.

Procedure

  1. De-wax and hydrate paraffin section.
  2. Stain in Verhoeff's solution until the section is black (about 20‚Äì45 minutes).
  3. Differentiate in 2% aqueous $\mathrm{FeCl}_3$, controlling microscopically until only elastic fibres and cell nuclei are black.
  4. Rinse in water and then transfer to 95% alcohol to remove iodine. Wash in water.
  5. Counterstain with van Gieson stain for 1‚Äì2 minutes. Dehydrate, clear and mount.

Result: Elastic fibres are black. Nuclei are blue to black. Collagen is red.

Orcein and Methylene Blue Method

Demonstrates fine and delicate elastic fibres. Uses synthetic orcein in acidified alcohol.

Result: Elastic fibres are light to deep brown. Nuclei are blue.

Resorcin-Fuchsin Stain

Very useful for the demonstration of elastic fibres and good for photography.

Result: Elastic fibres are dark blue to jet black.
