Module 5: Tissue Reception, Labeling and Fixation

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Describe the procedure for receiving and labeling tissue specimens.
  • Explain the importance of proper specimen identification and documentation.
  • Understand the use of tissue processing baskets and curretting baskets.
  • Define fixation and explain its purpose in tissue processing.

Tissue Reception and Labeling

  1. As soon as the specimen is received in the lab, check for correct labeling/identification. Check for: name, age, hospital/lab registration number, diagnosis, nature of tissue/description.
  2. All these details must match with those on the requisition form, which must also include a brief history and previous investigations/reports.
  3. A fool-proof system must be used, and the label must remain with the appropriate specimen throughout the procedures. Use printed graphite pencil, typewritten stenciled or Indian ink written labels. Do not use ordinary ink.
  4. Check if the specimen has been put in a proper fixative. The fixative should be 20 times the volume of the specimen. Add fixative if it is not in sufficient amounts.
  5. Make entries in the biopsy register/ or autopsy register and give the specimen an accession/specimen number. This number should be written on the requisition form as well, as well as the container. This number will accompany the specimen throughout the processes.
  6. The numbers should run concurrently with the year. Autopsy last two figures of the year are given in roman numbers e.g., .1 specimen in 1991 would be written 1/XC1.
  7. Details of clinical biopsies should be entered including the number of pieces taken.

4. Transferance of Labelled Tissues

To ensure that labels remain with the correct specimen, use tissue processing baskets.

They are small metallic/plastic containers inside of which pieces together with the appropriate label may be clipped.

The containers are perforated and may be transferred from reagent to reagent and are designed for use with ATP.

Curretting Baskets

These are tissue baskets used during the processing of tiny tissue specimens, e.g., renal biopsy, endometrial curettings, etc.

The tissue fragments are wrapped in filter papers and put into the tissue curettings together with their labels.

Fixation/Preservation of Tissue

📚 Fixation is the use of chemical reagents, alteration in environmental conditions or other means during processing to prevent or retard biological or physical deterioration of a specimen.

In order to prevent postmortem changes, fixatives are used.

💡 Pro Tip

Accurate labeling and proper fixation are crucial for maintaining the integrity of the specimen and ensuring accurate diagnosis.

✍️ Practice Exercise

1. List the information that should be checked upon receiving a tissue specimen.

2. What is the purpose of using tissue processing baskets?

3. Define fixation and explain why it is important.

View Solution

1. Name, age, hospital/lab registration number, diagnosis, nature of tissue/description.

2. To ensure that labels remain with the correct specimen during processing.

3. Fixation is the use of chemical reagents or other means to prevent or retard biological or physical deterioration of a specimen. It is important to preserve the tissue's structure and prevent postmortem changes.

📚 Key Takeaways

  • Proper specimen reception and labeling are essential for accurate identification and tracking.
  • Tissue processing baskets and curretting baskets help maintain the association between labels and specimens.
  • Fixation is a critical step in tissue processing that prevents deterioration and preserves tissue morphology.
