Module 7: Action of Fixatives and Factors Affecting Fixation

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Describe the three main methods of stabilizing proteins during fixation.
  • Differentiate between additive and non-additive fixatives.
  • Distinguish between coagulant and non-coagulant fixatives.
  • Identify the factors that affect fixation, including temperature, size of tissue, volume ratio, time, choice of fixative, penetration, tissue storage, pH, and osmolality.
  • Explain how each factor influences the fixation process.

Action of Fixatives

Methods of Stabilizing Proteins

  1. Heat (physical method): this method is becoming more used in histology labs with the introduction of microwave fixation.
  2. Desiccation (physical method): rarely used in histology. Touch preps for Giemsa stains are probably the most common used for this method.
  3. Chemical method: this is considered the primary method of fixation. These chemical reagents may be classified as additive and non-additive or coagulant and non-coagulant.
    • Additive - they chemically link or bind to the tissue and change it.
    • Non-additive - include organic compounds such as acetone and alcohols, which act on the tissue without chemically combining with the tissue.

Coagulant and Non-coagulant

Coagulation - will allow the solutions to penetrate into the interior of the tissue very easily

Examples of coagulant fixatives

  • Alcohol, Zinc salts.
  • Mercuric chloride
  • Picric acid
  • Chromium trioxide
  • Trichloroacetic acid

Non-coagulant - they act by creating a gel barrier that makes solutions more difficult to penetrate to the interior of the tissue.

Examples of non-coagulant fixatives

  • Formaldehyde
  • Glutaraldehyde
  • Osmium tetroxide
  • Potassium dichromate
  • Acetic acid

Factors Affecting Fixation

1. Temperature

Temperature affects the morphology of the tissue. Normally tissue fixation is done under room temp. for electron microscopy and some histochemical procedures, fixation is done under 0-4°C. The use of chemical reactions at higher temps to penetrate tissue, including those involved in fixation of tissue will increase the rate of penetration of the fixative to the specimen, however, an increase in temp will also increase the rate of autolysis an diffusion of cellular components.

2. Size of Tissue

In order to achieve satisfactory fixation, tissue blocks should be small or thin. Large specimens should be opened and washed of contents or sliced thinly before placement in fixative.

For routine processing, the recommended thickness of the specimens should be no more than 3mm thick.

3. Volume Ratio

Should be at least 15-20 times greater than the tissue volume.

4. Time

Fix 2mm thick tissue blocks in buffered formalin for 4- 8 hours possibly followed by a period in formol sublimate. Large specimens and viscera are cut into 5 mm slices or viscera are emptied and pinned out on a board, before fixing overnight in buffered formalin. This allows easier handling, examination and dissection, particularly sampling of lymph nodes.

In-case of electron microscopy, diced tissues are fixed for 3 hours in glutaraldehyde before placing in a holding buffer such as sodium cacodylate.

Prolonged fixation in aldehydes can cause shrinkage and hardening of tissue and severe inhibition of enzyme activity.

Prolonged fixation with oxidizing fixatives can degrade tissues by oxidative cleavage of proteins and loss of peptides.

Fix tissues immediately after surgery, autopsies should be performed immediately after death.

5. Choice of Fixative

For immunofluorescence studies, fix tissues in a transport media. For enzyme studies, use frozen sections. For diagnosis of gout, use absolute alcohol.

For electron microscopy, the choice is glutaraldehyde. Post fixation (a mordanting process) will help with the enhancement of the staining by allowing a better penetration of solution to the tissue structures to be observed. Eg Masson Trichrome.

6. Penetration

Fixatives penetrate the tissue at different rates. These rates can be affected by heat. The tissue is fixed starting at the periphery of the tissue and working inwards toward the center of the tissue.

7. Tissue Storage

Storing wet tissue is very important because often the tissue is needed for further studies.

Tissues fixed in neutral buffered formalin are usually safe to use for further studies since they can remain in this fixative indefinitely, but this isn't true for other fixatives.

Non-fixed tissue may remain in 70% methyl alcohol/ methanol.

8. pH

The hydrogen ion concentration varies between fixatives, but in general, the ph should be kept in the physiological range, between pH 4-9. If formalin is allowed to fall to a lower Ph than this, it can produce formalin pigments.

In EM, pH is critical. The pH for the ultra-structural preservation of great specimen the fixation should be buffered between 7.2- 7.4.

9. Osmolality

The addition of a buffer to the fixation solution may alter the osmotic pressure exerted by the solution. Hypertonic solutions give rise to cell shrinkage whereas isotonic and hypotonic fixatives result in cell swelling and poor fixation.

With EM, the best results are obtained using slightly hypertonic solutions (osmotic solutions being 340mOsm) adjusted using sucrose.

💡 Pro Tip

Careful consideration of the factors affecting fixation is essential for obtaining high-quality histological preparations.

✍️ Practice Exercise

1. Explain the difference between additive and non-additive fixatives, and provide one example of each.

2. List five factors that can affect the fixation process and briefly explain how each factor influences fixation.

View Solution

1. Additive fixatives chemically link to the tissue (e.g., formaldehyde), while non-additive fixatives act on the tissue without chemically combining (e.g., alcohol).

2. Five factors affecting fixation: Temperature (higher temp increases penetration but also autolysis), Size of tissue (smaller is better), Volume ratio (fixative should be 15-20 times greater), Time (immediate fixation is crucial), Choice of fixative (depends on the study), Penetration (varies with fixative and heat), Tissue storage (proper storage is needed), pH (should be physiological), Osmolality (should be slightly hypertonic).

📚 Key Takeaways

  • Fixatives stabilize proteins through heat, desiccation, or chemical methods.
  • Chemical fixatives can be additive or non-additive, as well as coagulant or non-coagulant.
  • Numerous factors influence the fixation process, including temperature, tissue size, volume ratio, time, choice of fixative, penetration, tissue storage, pH, and osmolality.
  • Proper control of these factors is essential for optimal tissue preservation and subsequent analysis.
