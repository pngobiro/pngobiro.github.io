Unit 2: Sampling Methods & Sample Size

üéØ 3.3.17.2 Non-probability Sampling Methods

(Selection is non-random; not every member has a chance of being selected. Prone to bias, limits generalizability).

  • ‚úì Convenience (Accidental/Haphazard) Sampling: Selecting participants who are readily available and convenient.
    • Advantages: Easy, inexpensive, quick.
    • Disadvantages: Highly prone to bias, sample may not be representative.
  • ‚úì Quota Sampling: Identifying strata and determining the proportion of participants needed from each stratum, then selecting participants conveniently until quotas are filled. (Non-random version of stratified sampling).
    • Advantages: Ensures inclusion of subgroups, relatively easy.
    • Disadvantages: Selection within strata is non-random and prone to bias.
  • ‚úì Purposive (Judgmental) Sampling: Selecting participants based on the researcher's judgment about who will be most informative for the study's purpose. Often used in qualitative research.
    • Advantages: Can select information-rich cases, useful for specific expertise or experience.
    • Disadvantages: Highly subjective, prone to researcher bias.
  • ‚úì Snowball (Network/Chain Referral) Sampling: Initial participants are asked to refer other potential participants who meet the study criteria. Useful for hard-to-reach populations.
    • Advantages: Useful for hidden or rare populations.
    • Disadvantages: Sample may not be representative, potential for bias as participants likely know each other.

üî¢ 3.3.18 Sample Size Determination

  • ‚úì Refers to the number of participants or units included in the study.
  • ‚úì Crucial for ensuring the study has enough statistical power to detect meaningful effects or relationships.
  • ‚úì Factors influencing sample size:
    • Research Design: Different designs require different sample sizes (e.g., experimental often need smaller samples than surveys).
    • Desired Level of Significance (Alpha, $\alpha$): The probability of making a Type I error (rejecting a true null hypothesis). Commonly set at 0.05. Lower alpha requires larger sample size.
    • Desired Power (1 - Beta, $1-\beta$): The probability of correctly rejecting a false null hypothesis (avoiding Type II error). Commonly set at 0.80 (80%). Higher power requires larger sample size.
    • Effect Size: The magnitude of the difference or relationship being investigated. Smaller expected effect sizes require larger samples to detect.
    • Population Variability: Higher variability (standard deviation) in the population requires larger sample size.
    • Precision Required (Margin of Error): How close the sample estimate needs to be to the true population value. Smaller margin of error requires larger sample size.
    • Population Size (for finite populations): If the sample size is a large proportion of the population (>5%), a correction factor may be applied, slightly reducing the required sample size.
    • Analysis Plan: More complex analyses or subgroup analyses may require larger samples.
    • Attrition Rate: Anticipated dropout rate; the initial sample size should be inflated to account for this.
    • Resources: Time, budget, and personnel constraints often limit the feasible sample size.
  • ‚úì Methods for calculating sample size:
    • Formulas: Specific formulas exist for different types of data (proportions, means) and research designs. A common formula for proportions is: $n = Z^2 P(1-P) / d^2$, where Z is the Z-score for the desired confidence level, P is the estimated proportion, and d is the desired margin of error.
    • Power Analysis Software: Statistical software (e.g., G*Power, R, SAS, SPSS) can perform power analyses to determine the required sample size based on the factors above.
    • Tables: Published tables provide estimated sample sizes for common scenarios.
    • Rules of Thumb: General guidelines exist for certain types of research (e.g., minimum number per group in experiments), but these are less precise.
  • ‚úì It's essential to justify the chosen sample size in the research proposal.
