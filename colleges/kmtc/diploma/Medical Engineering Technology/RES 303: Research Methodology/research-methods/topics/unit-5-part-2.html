Unit 5: Report Structure (cont.) & Utilization

üìë 5.4.2 Main body

(Usually organized into chapters or sections, numbered with Arabic numerals)

  • ‚úì Chapter 1: Introduction
    • Background of the study
    • Statement of the problem
    • Purpose/Aim of the study
    • Research objectives and/or questions/hypotheses
    • Significance/Justification of the study
    • Scope and Delimitations
    • Definition of key terms (Conceptual and Operational)
    • Conceptual/Theoretical framework (if applicable)
    • Organization of the report
  • ‚úì Chapter 2: Literature Review
    • Systematic review of existing knowledge relevant to the topic.
    • Organized thematically or chronologically.
    • Identifies gaps the current study aims to fill.
    • Provides context for the study.
  • ‚úì Chapter 3: Methodology
    • Detailed description of how the study was conducted.
    • Research design
    • Study setting/site
    • Study population and sampling method (including inclusion/exclusion criteria, sample size calculation)
    • Data collection instruments (description, development, validity, reliability)
    • Data collection procedures
    • Data analysis plan (statistical methods used)
    • Ethical considerations (IRB approval, consent process, confidentiality)
  • ‚úì Chapter 4: Results/Findings
    • Presentation of the data analysis results.
    • Objective description of findings using text, tables, and figures.
    • Organized logically, often following the order of research questions or objectives.
    • No interpretation or discussion in this section.
  • ‚úì Chapter 5: Discussion
    • Interpretation of the results.
    • Summary of key findings.
    • Comparison of findings with previous research (literature review).
    • Explanation of unexpected results.
    • Discussion of the study's strengths and limitations.
    • Implications of the findings (for practice, policy, theory).
  • ‚úì Chapter 6: Summary, Conclusions, and Recommendations
    • Brief summary of the study (problem, methods, key findings).
    • Conclusions drawn directly from the findings, addressing the research questions/objectives.
    • Recommendations for action (practice, policy) and/or future research based on the study's outcomes and limitations.

üìö 5.4.3 References & 5.4.4 Appendices

5.4.3 References

  • ‚úì List of all sources cited within the report.
  • ‚úì Must follow a consistent citation style (e.g., APA, Harvard, Vancouver).
  • ‚úì Includes books, journal articles, reports, websites, etc.
  • ‚úì Allows readers to locate the original sources.

5.4.4 Appendices

  • ‚úì Contains supplementary material not essential for understanding the main text but provides additional detail.
  • ‚úì Examples:
    • Data collection instruments (questionnaires, interview guides).
    • Informed consent forms.
    • Detailed statistical output.
    • Letters of permission.
    • IRB approval letter.
    • Participant information sheets.
  • ‚úì Each appendix should be labeled (e.g., Appendix A, Appendix B) and referenced in the main text where relevant.

üöÄ 5.5 Utilization of research findings

  • ‚úì The process of applying research results to improve practice, inform policy, or guide further inquiry (Research Utilization or Knowledge Translation).
  • ‚úì Bridges the gap between research and practice.
  • ‚úì Can involve different levels:
    • Conceptual Utilization: Changing thinking or understanding of an issue based on research.
    • Instrumental Utilization: Direct application of findings to change specific practices or policies.
    • Symbolic Utilization: Using research to legitimize or support a predetermined position.
  • ‚úì Strategies to promote utilization:
    • Effective dissemination tailored to different audiences (see 5.3).
    • Involving stakeholders (practitioners, policymakers, community members) throughout the research process.
    • Developing clear and actionable recommendations.
    • Creating user-friendly summaries and tools based on findings.
    • Collaborating with organizations to implement changes.
  • ‚úì Barriers to utilization: Lack of awareness, lack of relevance, conflicting evidence, resistance to change, resource constraints.
