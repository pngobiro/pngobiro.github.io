❓ Identifying Research Problems

Steps

  1. Identify the broad area of interest
    • ✓ The area should be relevant to the profession and goals of the researcher e.g. HIV/AIDS, TB, malaria, FP, breastfeeding
  2. Identify a specific problem within broad area e.g. in HIV/AIDS
    • ✓ Prevention of new infections
    • ✓ Circumcision
    • ✓ Needle prick injuries to nurses
    • ✓ Breastfeeding or not of infected
    • ✓ Management (HAART/ARV Rx)
    • ✓ Compliance
    • ✓ HAART in mother to child transmission
    • ✓ Health workers handling
    • ✓ Perceptions

3.3.2 What is a Researchable problem?

It should be an important problem i.e.

  1. Lead to findings that have wide spread implication to a particular area
  2. Challenge some commonly held altruism
  3. Reviews the inadequacies of existing laws, policies, theories, views e.t.c.
  4. Cover a reasonable scope i.e. not too narrow or general

3.3.4 Sources of research problems

  • ✓ Personal experience
  • ✓ Literature sources
  • ✓ Past research
  • ✓ Testing theories
  • ✓ Consultation with experts
  • ✓ Social and political issues
  • ✓ Programme evaluation

3.3.5 Criteria for selecting a research problem

  • ✓ Significance of the problem
  • ✓ Researchability of the problem
  • ✓ Feasibility of the problem
  • ✓ Interest to the researcher
  • ✓ Ethical consideration
  • ✓ Time
  • ✓ Cost
  • ✓ Administrative support
  • ✓ Peer support

3.3.6 Stating the research problem

  • ✓ Should be stated clearly and unambiguously
  • ✓ Should express a relationship between two or more variables
  • ✓ Should be stated in question form
  • ✓ Should imply possibilities of empirical testing
  • ✓ Should specify the nature of the population being studied

3.3.7 Purpose of the study

It describes why the study has been designed. The purpose reflects the intent of the investigations and use of the knowledge derived

3.3.8 Research objectives

  • ✓ Are statements of what the researcher intends to do
  • ✓ Should be stated clearly in measurable terms
  • ✓ Should guide the researcher in the research process
  • ✓ Should be derived from the research problem
  • ✓ Should be achievable within the research period
  • ✓ Should be realistic
  • ✓ Should be phrased using action verbs e.g. to determine, to establish, to find out, to compare

3.3.9 Research questions

  • ✓ Are questions that the researcher would like to be answered by undertaking the study
  • ✓ Should be derived from the research objectives
  • ✓ Should be stated clearly in question form
  • ✓ Should be researchable

3.3.10 Research hypothesis

  • ✓ Is a statement of the expectations of the researcher concerning the relationship of the variables under study
  • ✓ It is a tentative answer to a research question
  • ✓ It is derived from the research problem or literature review
  • ✓ It is stated in declarative form
  • ✓ It should be testable
  • ✓ It should state the relationship between two or more variables
  • ✓ It should be stated clearly and unambiguously

Types of hypothesis

  • ✓ Null hypothesis ($H_0$): States that there is no relationship between the variables under study. It is stated in negative form. It is the hypothesis that is subjected to statistical testing.
  • ✓ Alternative hypothesis ($H_1$ or $H_a$): States that there is a relationship between the variables under study. It is stated in positive form. It is accepted when the null hypothesis is rejected.
  • ✓ Directional hypothesis: States the expected direction of the relationship between variables. Uses terms like 'increase', 'decrease', 'less than', 'more than'.
  • ✓ Non-directional hypothesis: States that a relationship exists but does not specify the direction. Uses terms like 'related to', 'associated with', 'difference between'.
  • ✓ Simple hypothesis: Predicts the relationship between one independent variable and one dependent variable.
  • ✓ Complex hypothesis: Predicts the relationship between two or more independent variables and/or two or more dependent variables.
  • ✓ Research hypothesis: The statement created by researchers when they speculate upon the outcome of a research or experiment.
  • ✓ Statistical hypothesis: A statement about a population parameter.

Purpose of hypothesis

  • ✓ Guides the research process
  • ✓ Provides direction for the collection, analysis and interpretation of data
  • ✓ Provides a framework for reporting the conclusions of the study
  • ✓ Facilitates the extension of knowledge in an area
  • ✓ Enables the researcher to formulate theories
