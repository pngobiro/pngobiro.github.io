Ethical considerations are paramount in health research involving human participants (and animals) to ensure their rights, dignity, and well-being are protected.

üìú 4.2 Basic ethical principles

Based on foundational reports like the Belmont Report, key principles include:

  • ‚úì Respect for Persons (Autonomy):
    • Treating individuals as autonomous agents capable of making their own decisions.
    • Protecting those with diminished autonomy (e.g., children, prisoners, individuals with cognitive impairments).
    • Requires obtaining informed consent.
  • ‚úì Beneficence:
    • Maximizing potential benefits for participants and society.
    • Minimizing potential risks and harms.
    • Requires a careful risk/benefit assessment.
  • ‚úì Non-maleficence:
    • "Do no harm." Avoiding causing unnecessary harm or injury to participants.
  • ‚úì Justice:
    • Fair distribution of the burdens and benefits of research.
    • Ensuring participant selection is equitable and not based on vulnerability or convenience alone.
    • Avoiding exploitation of participants.
  • ‚úì Fidelity and Responsibility: Establishing trust with participants and upholding professional standards.
  • ‚úì Integrity: Promoting accuracy, honesty, and truthfulness in research.

‚úçÔ∏è 4.3 Informed consent

  • ‚úì A process, not just a form, ensuring potential participants understand the research and voluntarily agree to participate.
  • ‚úì Essential elements to disclose:
    • Purpose of the research.
    • Procedures involved and duration.
    • Potential risks and benefits.
    • Alternative treatments/procedures (if applicable).
    • Extent of confidentiality.
    • Compensation (if any).
    • Contact information for questions.
    • Statement that participation is voluntary and can be withdrawn at any time without penalty.
  • ‚úì Must be obtained before participation begins.
  • ‚úì Language must be understandable to the participant.
  • ‚úì Special considerations for vulnerable populations (e.g., parental permission and child assent, legally authorized representative for cognitively impaired).
  • ‚úì Documentation usually involves a signed consent form, but waivers may be possible under specific circumstances (approved by an ethics committee).

üîí 4.4 Confidentiality and anonymity

  • ‚úì Protecting participants' private information.
  • ‚úì Anonymity: No identifying information is collected, making it impossible to link data back to the individual. (Often difficult to achieve).
  • ‚úì Confidentiality: Identifying information is collected but kept private and separate from the data. Researchers know who participated but promise not to disclose identities.
  • ‚úì Methods to ensure confidentiality:
    • Using codes instead of names.
    • Storing identifying information securely and separately from data.
    • Limiting access to identifiable data.
    • Reporting data in aggregate form.
    • Obtaining Certificates of Confidentiality (in some jurisdictions) to protect data from legal subpoena.

üõ°Ô∏è 4.5 Vulnerable groups

  • ‚úì Populations who may be at increased risk of harm or exploitation in research due to compromised ability to provide informed consent or protect their own interests.
  • ‚úì Examples: Children, prisoners, pregnant women, fetuses, individuals with cognitive impairments, economically or educationally disadvantaged individuals, terminally ill patients.
  • ‚úì Require additional safeguards and justification for inclusion in research.
  • ‚úì Consent procedures must be adapted (e.g., parental permission, assent from minors, legally authorized representatives).
  • ‚úì Research must offer potential direct benefit or be minimal risk with potential societal benefit.

üêæ 4.6 Research involving animals

  • ‚úì Requires ethical justification and adherence to guidelines promoting humane treatment.
  • ‚úì Principles of the "3 Rs":
    • Replacement: Using non-animal methods whenever possible.
    • Reduction: Using the minimum number of animals necessary to obtain valid results.
    • Refinement: Minimizing pain, suffering, and distress, and enhancing animal welfare.
  • ‚úì Requires approval from an Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee (IACUC) or equivalent body.

üö´ 4.7 Scientific misconduct

  • ‚úì Violation of ethical principles and standards in research.
  • ‚úì Includes:
    • Fabrication: Making up data or results.
    • Falsification: Manipulating research materials, equipment, or processes, or changing/omitting data such that the research is not accurately represented.
    • Plagiarism: Appropriating another person's ideas, processes, results, or words without giving appropriate credit.
    • Other issues: Conflicts of interest, authorship disputes, inadequate data management.
  • ‚úì Undermines the integrity of science and public trust.
  • ‚úì Institutions have procedures for investigating and addressing allegations of misconduct.

üßë‚Äç‚öñÔ∏è 4.8 Role of ethical review committees

  • ‚úì Institutional Review Boards (IRBs) or Research Ethics Committees (RECs) are responsible for reviewing research involving human participants to ensure ethical conduct.
  • ‚úì Mandate: Protect the rights and welfare of research participants.
  • ‚úì Functions:
    • Review research protocols before initiation.
    • Assess risks and potential benefits.
    • Evaluate the adequacy of the informed consent process and documents.
    • Ensure equitable participant selection.
    • Monitor ongoing research.
    • Require modifications or disapprove research that does not meet ethical standards.
  • ‚úì Composition: Diverse membership including scientists, non-scientists, and community members unaffiliated with the institution.
  • ‚úì Researchers must obtain approval from the relevant ethics committee before starting data collection involving human participants.
