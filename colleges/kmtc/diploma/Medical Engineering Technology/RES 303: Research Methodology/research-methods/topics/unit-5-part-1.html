ü§î 5.2 Interpreting research findings

  • ‚úì Going beyond the statistical results to understand their meaning and implications.
  • ‚úì Involves:
    • Relating findings back to the research questions and objectives.
    • Considering statistical significance (p-values) vs. clinical/practical significance (magnitude of effect).
    • Comparing findings with existing literature (agreement or contradiction).
    • Considering limitations of the study (e.g., sample size, design flaws, bias) and their potential impact on results.
    • Acknowledging alternative explanations for the findings.
    • Drawing conclusions based on the evidence.
    • Identifying implications for practice, policy, or future research.

üó£Ô∏è 5.3 Communicating research findings (Dissemination)

  • ‚úì Sharing the results of the research with relevant audiences.
  • ‚úì Purpose: To inform practice, influence policy, contribute to knowledge, and allow for scrutiny and replication.
  • ‚úì Target Audiences:
    • Academic/Scientific community (other researchers).
    • Practitioners (clinicians, health workers).
    • Policy makers and administrators.
    • Participants and the community involved.
    • General public.
    • Funding agencies.
  • ‚úì Methods of Dissemination:
    • Formal Research Report/Thesis/Dissertation.
    • Journal Articles: Peer-reviewed publications in academic journals (most common for scientific audience).
    • Conference Presentations: Oral presentations or posters at scientific meetings.
    • Workshops and Seminars: Interactive sessions for practitioners or community members.
    • Policy Briefs: Concise summaries for policymakers.
    • Training Manuals: Incorporating findings into educational materials.
    • Community Meetings: Presenting results back to the community where research was conducted.
    • Media (Press releases, articles, interviews): For reaching the general public.
    • Websites and Social Media.
  • ‚úì Tailor the communication method and language to the specific audience.

‚úçÔ∏è 5.4 Research report writing

  • ‚úì The formal, written documentation of the entire research process and its findings.
  • ‚úì Purpose: To communicate the study clearly, accurately, and comprehensively so others can understand, evaluate, and potentially replicate it.
  • ‚úì Structure often follows a standard format (though variations exist):
    • Preliminaries
    • Main Body (Chapters/Sections)
    • References
    • Appendices
  • ‚úì Style: Formal, objective, clear, concise, precise language. Use past tense for methods and results.

5.4.1 Preliminaries

(Pages before the main text, usually numbered with Roman numerals)

  • ‚úì Title Page: Includes title of the study, author's name, institutional affiliation, degree requirement (if applicable), date.
  • ‚úì Declaration: Statement by the author that the work is original and has not been submitted elsewhere.
  • ‚úì Dedication (Optional): Personal dedication.
  • ‚úì Acknowledgements: Thanking individuals and organizations who supported the research (e.g., supervisors, funders, participants).
  • ‚úì Abstract: Brief summary (typically 150-300 words) covering the background, objectives, methods, key findings, and conclusions. Crucial for quickly understanding the study.
  • ‚úì Table of Contents: Lists chapters/sections and their page numbers.
  • ‚úì List of Tables (if applicable): Lists table numbers, titles, and page numbers.
  • ‚úì List of Figures (if applicable): Lists figure numbers, titles, and page numbers.
  • ‚úì List of Abbreviations/Acronyms (if applicable): Defines abbreviations used in the report.
