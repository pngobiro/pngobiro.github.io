3.3.11 Variables

  • ‚úì Is a measurable characteristic that assumes different values among subjects
  • ‚úì Types of variables
    • Independent variable: The variable that is manipulated or changed by the researcher to observe its effect on the dependent variable.
    • Dependent variable: The variable that is measured to see the effect of the independent variable.
    • Extraneous variable: Any variable other than the independent variable that could potentially influence the dependent variable.
    • Confounding variable: An extraneous variable that is related to both the independent and dependent variables, potentially distorting the observed relationship.
    • Intervening variable: A variable that explains the relationship between the independent and dependent variables.
    • Categorical variable: Variables that can be put into categories (e.g., gender, marital status).
      • Nominal: Categories with no inherent order (e.g., blood type).
      • Ordinal: Categories with a meaningful order but unequal intervals (e.g., pain scale: mild, moderate, severe).
    • Continuous variable: Variables that can take on any value within a range (e.g., height, weight, temperature).
      • Interval: Equal intervals between values but no true zero (e.g., Celsius temperature).
      • Ratio: Equal intervals and a true zero point (e.g., height, age).

3.3.12 Conceptual and operational definitions

  • ‚úì Conceptual definition: Defines a concept in abstract terms (dictionary definition).
  • ‚úì Operational definition: Defines a concept by specifying how it will be measured or manipulated in the study.

3.3.13 Conceptual framework

  • ‚úì A structure of concepts or theories used to guide the research.
  • ‚úì Provides a map for the study, showing relationships between variables.

3.3.14 Literature review

  • ‚úì A comprehensive summary of previous research on a topic.
  • ‚úì Purpose:
    • Identify gaps in current knowledge.
    • Avoid duplication of effort.
    • Provide theoretical background.
    • Identify relevant variables and methodologies.
    • Help refine the research question.
  • ‚úì Sources: Journals, books, conference proceedings, theses, reports.
  • ‚úì Steps:
    • Identify keywords.
    • Search relevant databases.
    • Select relevant articles.
    • Critically evaluate the literature.
    • Synthesize the findings.
    • Organize and write the review.

üë• Population and Sampling

3.3.17 Population and sampling

  • ‚úì Population: The entire group of individuals or objects that the researcher is interested in studying.
  • ‚úì Target Population: The specific group to whom the researcher wants to generalize the results.
  • ‚úì Accessible Population: The portion of the target population that is accessible to the researcher.
  • ‚úì Sample: A subset of the population selected to participate in the study.
  • ‚úì Sampling: The process of selecting a sample from a population.
  • ‚úì Sampling Frame: A list of all individuals or units in the accessible population from which the sample will be drawn.
  • ‚úì Representativeness: The extent to which the sample accurately reflects the characteristics of the population.
  • ‚úì Sampling Bias: Occurs when the sample is not representative of the population.

Types of Sampling Methods

3.3.17.1 Probability Sampling Methods

(Each member of the population has a known, non-zero chance of being selected)

  • ‚úì Simple Random Sampling: Each member has an equal chance of being selected. (e.g., drawing names from a hat, using a random number generator).
    • Advantages: Simple, ensures high representativeness if done correctly.
    • Disadvantages: Need a complete list of the population (sampling frame), can be time-consuming for large populations.
  • ‚úì Systematic Random Sampling: Selecting every $k^{th}$ member from a list after a random start. (Calculate $k$ = Population size / Sample size).
    • Advantages: Easier than simple random sampling if a list exists.
    • Disadvantages: Potential for bias if there is a hidden pattern (periodicity) in the list that matches the sampling interval.
  • ‚úì Stratified Random Sampling: Dividing the population into subgroups (strata) based on relevant characteristics (e.g., age, gender) and then taking a random sample from each stratum. Can be proportional (sample size from each stratum reflects population proportion) or disproportional.
    • Advantages: Ensures representation of key subgroups, allows for comparisons between strata, potentially more precise than simple random sampling.
    • Disadvantages: Requires knowledge of population characteristics for stratification, can be complex to implement.
  • ‚úì Cluster (Area) Sampling: Dividing the population into clusters (often geographic areas), randomly selecting clusters, and then including all members within the selected clusters (one-stage) or taking a random sample within selected clusters (two-stage/multi-stage).
    • Advantages: Practical and cost-effective for large, geographically dispersed populations where a complete sampling frame is unavailable.
    • Disadvantages: Less precise than simple or stratified random sampling (higher sampling error), requires careful cluster definition.
  • ‚úì Multi-stage Sampling: A complex form of cluster sampling involving multiple stages of selection (e.g., randomly select regions, then districts, then villages, then households).
