üìà 3.4 Data analysis

  • ‚úì The process of inspecting, cleaning, transforming, and modeling data to discover useful information, draw conclusions, and support decision-making.
  • ‚úì Purpose: To organize, summarize, and interpret the collected data to answer the research questions or test hypotheses.
  • ‚úì Steps:
    • Data cleaning and preparation (checking for errors, handling missing data).
    • Choosing appropriate statistical techniques based on research questions, variable types, and data distribution.
    • Performing the analysis using statistical software (e.g., SPSS, R, SAS, Excel).
    • Interpreting the results.
  • ‚úì Two main types: Descriptive and Inferential statistics.

3.4.1 Descriptive statistics

  • ‚úì Used to summarize and describe the main features of a dataset.
  • ‚úì Does not allow conclusions beyond the data analyzed or regarding hypotheses.
  • ‚úì Common measures:
    • Measures of Frequency:
      • Counts, Percentages, Frequencies.
      • Frequency Distributions (Tables showing how often each value occurs).
    • Measures of Central Tendency: (Describe the center of the data)
      • Mean (Average): Sum of values divided by the number of values. Sensitive to outliers. Suitable for interval/ratio data.
      • Median (Middle value): The value that divides the dataset in half when ordered. Less sensitive to outliers. Suitable for ordinal/interval/ratio data.
      • Mode (Most frequent value): The value that occurs most often. Suitable for all data types, especially nominal.
    • Measures of Dispersion (Variability): (Describe the spread of the data)
      • Range (Highest value - Lowest value): Simple but affected by outliers.
      • Variance: Average of squared differences from the mean.
      • Standard Deviation: Square root of the variance. Most common measure of spread for interval/ratio data. Indicates average distance from the mean.
      • Interquartile Range (IQR): Range of the middle 50% of the data (Q3 - Q1). Less affected by outliers.
    • Measures of Position:
      • Percentiles, Quartiles (divide data into 100 or 4 equal parts).

3.4.2 Inferential statistics

  • ‚úì Used to make inferences or generalizations about a larger population based on data from a sample.
  • ‚úì Involves hypothesis testing and estimating population parameters.
  • ‚úì Key concepts:
    • Hypothesis Testing: Formal procedure to decide whether to reject or fail to reject a null hypothesis ($H_0$) based on sample evidence.
    • P-value: The probability of observing the sample results (or more extreme results) if the null hypothesis were true. A small p-value (typically $p < 0.05$) suggests evidence against the null hypothesis.
    • Confidence Intervals: A range of values estimated from the sample data that is likely to contain the true population parameter (e.g., 95% CI).
  • ‚úì Common techniques:
    Common Inferential Statistical Tests
    Purpose Test Variable Types
    Compare means between two independent groups Independent samples t-test 1 Continuous (DV), 1 Categorical (2 groups) (IV)
    Compare means between two related groups (e.g., pre/post) Paired samples t-test 1 Continuous (DV), 1 Within-subjects factor (2 levels) (IV)
    Compare means between three or more independent groups Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) 1 Continuous (DV), 1 Categorical (>2 groups) (IV)
    Assess relationship between two categorical variables Chi-Square ($\chi^2$) Test 2 Categorical
    Assess relationship between two continuous variables Pearson Correlation (r) 2 Continuous
    Predict a continuous outcome from one or more predictors Linear Regression 1 Continuous (DV), Continuous or Categorical (IVs)
    Predict a categorical outcome from one or more predictors Logistic Regression 1 Categorical (DV), Continuous or Categorical (IVs)

üñºÔ∏è 3.5 Data presentation

  • ‚úì Communicating data and analysis results clearly and effectively.
  • ‚úì Methods:
    • Tables: Good for presenting precise numerical data, comparing values across categories. Should have clear titles, labels, and footnotes if needed.
      • Frequency tables
      • Cross-tabulations
      • Summary statistics tables
    • Graphs/Charts: Good for visualizing patterns, trends, and relationships. Choice depends on the type of data.
      • Bar Chart: Compares frequencies or means across categories (nominal/ordinal IV). Bars are separated.
      • Pie Chart: Shows proportions of a whole (for categorical data). Best for few categories.
      • Histogram: Shows frequency distribution of continuous data. Bars touch each other.
      • Frequency Polygon: Line graph connecting midpoints of histogram bars. Shows shape of distribution.
      • Line Graph: Shows trends over time or relationship between two continuous variables.
      • Scatter Plot: Shows relationship between two continuous variables. Each point represents an individual case.
      • Box Plot (Box-and-Whisker Plot): Shows distribution, central tendency (median), and variability (IQR, range) of continuous data, useful for comparing groups.
      • Lorenz Curve: Graphical representation of income or wealth distribution.
    • Textual Description: Summarizing key findings and statistical results in narrative form within the report.
  • ‚úì Principles: Accuracy, Clarity, Conciseness, Visual Appeal. Avoid misleading presentations. Label axes and provide legends.
