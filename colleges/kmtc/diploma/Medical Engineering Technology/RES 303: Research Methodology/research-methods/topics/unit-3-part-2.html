Unit 3: Records, Instrument Development, Validity & Reliability

üìÇ 3.1.4 Use of available data/records

  • ‚úì Involves analyzing existing data collected for other purposes (secondary data analysis).
  • ‚úì Sources: Hospital records, government statistics, previous research data, public databases.
  • ‚úì Advantages:
    • Cost-effective and time-saving.
    • Allows analysis of large datasets.
    • Useful for studying trends over time or rare conditions.
  • ‚úì Disadvantages:
    • Data may not be suitable for the specific research question (missing variables, different definitions).
    • Data quality and accuracy may be unknown or variable.
    • May require permission or have restricted access.
    • Ethical considerations regarding privacy and consent.

üîß 3.2 Development of data collection instruments

  • ‚úì The process of creating tools (e.g., questionnaires, interview guides, observation checklists) to measure the variables of interest.
  • ‚úì Steps:
    • Define the concepts to be measured clearly (operational definitions).
    • Review existing instruments (adapt or adopt if suitable).
    • Draft the instrument items/questions.
    • Determine the format and layout.
    • Review by experts for content validity.
    • Pre-test (pilot test) with a small sample similar to the target population.
    • Revise based on pre-test feedback.
    • Establish reliability and validity (see below).
  • ‚úì Considerations: Clarity, relevance, brevity, sensitivity, cultural appropriateness, respondent burden.

‚úÖ 3.3 Validity and reliability of instruments

  • ‚úì Crucial aspects of measurement quality.
  • ‚úì Validity: Does the instrument measure what it is intended to measure? (Accuracy)
    • Content Validity: Extent to which the instrument covers all relevant aspects of the concept being measured. Assessed by expert review.
    • Face Validity: Extent to which the instrument appears to measure the concept "on the face of it". Assessed by experts or potential respondents. (Weakest form).
    • Criterion-related Validity: How well the instrument's scores correlate with an external criterion (a gold standard or future outcome).
      • Concurrent Validity: Correlation with a criterion measured at the same time.
      • Predictive Validity: Correlation with a criterion measured in the future.
    • Construct Validity: Extent to which the instrument measures the underlying theoretical construct it's supposed to measure. Assessed through various methods like:
      • Convergent Validity: High correlation with other measures of the same construct.
      • Discriminant (Divergent) Validity: Low correlation with measures of different constructs.
      • Factor Analysis: Statistical technique to identify underlying dimensions.
      • Known-groups technique: Comparing scores between groups known to differ on the construct.
  • ‚úì Reliability: Does the instrument consistently produce the same results under the same conditions? (Consistency, Stability)
    • Test-Retest Reliability: Consistency of scores over time when the instrument is administered to the same individuals on two separate occasions. Assessed by correlation (e.g., Pearson's r).
    • Inter-Rater (Inter-Observer) Reliability: Consistency of scores when two or more raters/observers use the instrument to score the same thing/person independently. Assessed by correlation or agreement statistics (e.g., Cohen's Kappa).
    • Internal Consistency Reliability: Consistency among the items within the instrument (i.e., do items measuring the same construct correlate with each other?). Assessed by statistics like:
      • Cronbach's Alpha (most common for scales with multiple items).
      • Split-Half Reliability: Correlating scores from two halves of the instrument.
      • Kuder-Richardson (KR-20, KR-21): For instruments with dichotomous (Yes/No) items.
    • Parallel Forms (Equivalent Forms) Reliability: Consistency between scores from two different but equivalent versions of the instrument administered to the same individuals. Assessed by correlation.
  • ‚úì An instrument must be reliable to be valid, but a reliable instrument is not necessarily valid (e.g., a scale could consistently measure the wrong weight).
