üìù 3.1 Data collection methods

  • ‚úì Refers to the techniques used to gather information relevant to the research questions.
  • ‚úì Choice depends on the research design, variables, population, and resources.
  • ‚úì Common methods include:
    • Questionnaires
    • Interviews
    • Observation
    • Use of available data/records

3.1.1 Questionnaires

  • ‚úì A set of written questions used to collect data from respondents.
  • ‚úì Can be self-administered (mail, online) or researcher-administered.
  • ‚úì Types of questions:
    • Closed-ended: Provide predefined response options (e.g., Yes/No, multiple choice, rating scales like Likert). Easier to analyze.
    • Open-ended: Allow respondents to answer in their own words. Provide rich qualitative data but harder to analyze.
  • ‚úì Advantages:
    • Cost-effective for large samples.
    • Can ensure anonymity.
    • Respondents can answer at their convenience (self-administered).
    • Standardized questions reduce interviewer bias.
  • ‚úì Disadvantages:
    • Low response rates (especially mail surveys).
    • Cannot probe for deeper answers or clarify questions.
    • Potential for misinterpretation of questions.
    • Not suitable for complex topics or respondents with low literacy.
  • ‚úì Considerations for design: Clear wording, logical flow, avoid leading questions, pre-testing.

3.1.2 Interviews

  • ‚úì A method of data collection involving verbal interaction between the researcher (interviewer) and the participant (interviewee).
  • ‚úì Types:
    • Structured: Uses a fixed set of closed-ended questions (similar to a verbally administered questionnaire). Ensures consistency.
    • Semi-structured: Uses a guide with key questions and themes, but allows flexibility to probe and explore responses.
    • Unstructured (In-depth): Explores topics broadly with open-ended questions, allowing the conversation to flow naturally. Common in qualitative research.
    • Focus Group Interviews: Interviewing a small group of participants together to explore their collective views and interactions.
    • Key Informant Interviews: Interviewing individuals with specific knowledge or expertise on a topic.
    • Exit Interviews: Conducted as people leave a service or program to gather feedback.
  • ‚úì Advantages:
    • Allows for clarification and probing.
    • Can collect in-depth, nuanced information.
    • Suitable for complex topics and diverse populations (including low literacy).
    • Higher response rates usually than questionnaires.
    • Can observe non-verbal cues.
  • ‚úì Disadvantages:
    • Time-consuming and expensive.
    • Potential for interviewer bias (affecting responses).
    • Requires skilled interviewers.
    • Data analysis can be complex (especially for unstructured interviews).
    • Lack of anonymity may affect responses on sensitive topics.

3.1.3 Observation

  • ‚úì Systematically watching and recording behaviors, events, or characteristics in their natural setting.
  • ‚úì Types:
    • Participant Observation: The researcher becomes part of the group being observed.
    • Non-participant Observation: The researcher observes from the outside without participating.
    • Structured Observation: Using predefined categories and checklists to record specific behaviors.
    • Unstructured Observation: Recording observations descriptively without predefined categories.
    • Covert Observation: Observing without the participants' knowledge (raises ethical concerns).
    • Overt Observation: Observing with the participants' knowledge.
  • ‚úì Advantages:
    • Provides direct information about behavior in natural settings.
    • Can capture context and non-verbal cues.
    • Useful when participants cannot or will not report their own behavior accurately.
  • ‚úì Disadvantages:
    • Potential for observer bias (interpreting behavior subjectively).
    • Hawthorne effect (participants changing behavior because they know they are being watched).
    • Time-consuming.
    • Cannot observe attitudes, beliefs, or past events.
    • Ethical concerns (especially with covert observation).
  • ‚úì Requires clear definitions of behaviors to be observed and systematic recording methods (e.g., field notes, checklists, rating scales, video recording).
