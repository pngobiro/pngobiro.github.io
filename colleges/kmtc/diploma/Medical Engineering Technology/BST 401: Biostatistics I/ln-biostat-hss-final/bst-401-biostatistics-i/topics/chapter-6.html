Sampling methods

  1. 1.Define sampling and understand the rationale for sampling

üìñ 6.2 Introduction

In the previous chapters, we have seen how to organize, present and summarize data. However, the data we have might be collected from the entire population or from a part of the population (sample).

When data are collected from the entire population, it is called a census.

When data are collected from a part of the population, it is called a sample survey.

Sampling is the process of selecting a part of the population (sample) to represent the whole population.

Rationale for Sampling:

  • ‚Ä¢Reduced Cost: Collecting data from a sample is less expensive than a census.
  • ‚Ä¢Greater Speed: Data can be collected and processed more quickly from a sample.
  • ‚Ä¢Greater Scope: Allows for more detailed information to be collected on each unit.
  • ‚Ä¢Greater Accuracy: Better quality control and training of personnel are possible with smaller-scale operations, potentially leading to more accurate data than a census.
  • ‚Ä¢Necessity: Sometimes testing involves destroying the item (e.g., testing the lifespan of light bulbs), making sampling the only option.
  • ‚Ä¢Feasibility: When the population is infinite or very large, a census is impossible.

üó£Ô∏è 6.3 Common terms used in sampling

  • ‚Ä¢Population (or Universe): The entire group of individuals or items that the study is interested in.
  • ‚Ä¢Target Population: The specific population about which inferences are to be made.
  • ‚Ä¢Study Population: The population from which the sample is actually drawn. Ideally, it should be the same as the target population.
  • ‚Ä¢Sampling Unit: The element or group of elements on which observations are made (e.g., individuals, households, villages).
  • ‚Ä¢Sampling Frame: A list of all sampling units in the study population. It is needed for probability sampling methods. (e.g., list of all households in a kebele).
  • ‚Ä¢Sample: A subset of the population selected for study.
  • ‚Ä¢Parameter: A descriptive measure computed from the data of a population (e.g., population mean $\mu$, population proportion P).
  • ‚Ä¢Statistic: A descriptive measure computed from the data of a sample (e.g., sample mean $\bar{x}$, sample proportion p). Statistics are used to estimate parameters.

üõ†Ô∏è 6.4 Sampling methods

Sampling methods are broadly classified into two types:

  1. 1.Probability (Random) Sampling: Methods where every unit in the population has a known, non-zero chance of being selected. Allows for statistical inference about the population based on the sample.
  2. 2.Non-Probability (Non-Random) Sampling: Methods where the selection of units is based on subjective judgment, convenience, or other non-random criteria. The probability of selection is unknown. Does not allow for generalization to the population with statistical confidence.

Probability Sampling Methods

  • a)Simple Random Sampling (SRS): Every possible sample of a given size has an equal chance of being selected. Requires a complete sampling frame. Selection can be done using lottery method or random number tables/generators.
    Advantages: Simple concept, allows easy calculation of sampling error.
    Disadvantages: Requires a complete sampling frame, can be inefficient if population is large or dispersed, may not ensure representation of subgroups.
  • b)Systematic Random Sampling: Selects units at regular intervals from the sampling frame after a random start. Calculate sampling interval $k = N/n$ (N=population size, n=sample size). Randomly select a starting point between 1 and k. Select every k-th unit thereafter.
    Advantages: Easier to implement than SRS, often provides a representative sample if the list is randomly ordered.
    Disadvantages: Requires a sampling frame, potential for bias if there is periodicity in the list that matches the sampling interval.
  • c)Stratified Random Sampling: Divides the population into mutually exclusive subgroups (strata) based on relevant characteristics (e.g., age, sex, region). Then, a simple random or systematic sample is drawn from each stratum. Can be proportionate (sample size in stratum proportional to population size in stratum) or disproportionate.
    Advantages: Ensures representation of key subgroups, can provide more precise estimates for each stratum and overall population compared to SRS of the same size.
    Disadvantages: Requires knowledge of stratum characteristics for all units in the frame, more complex to implement and analyze.
  • d)Cluster (Area) Sampling: Divides the population into clusters (often geographic areas like villages or enumeration areas). A random sample of clusters is selected. All units within the selected clusters are included in the sample (one-stage cluster sampling).
    Advantages: Does not require a complete list of all individual units (only clusters), reduces travel costs if clusters are geographic areas.
    Disadvantages: Less precise than SRS or stratified sampling of the same size (units within a cluster tend to be similar), requires larger sample size to achieve same precision, complex analysis.
  • e)Multi-Stage Sampling: Involves sampling in two or more stages. Example: First stage - randomly select regions; Second stage - randomly select kebeles within selected regions; Third stage - randomly select households within selected kebeles. Combines different methods (e.g., cluster sampling followed by SRS).
    Advantages: Flexible, practical for large and dispersed populations, reduces costs.
    Disadvantages: More complex design and analysis, potentially less precise than simpler methods.

Non-Probability Sampling Methods

(Used mainly in qualitative research or exploratory studies where generalization is not the primary goal).

  • ‚Ä¢Convenience Sampling: Selecting units that are easily accessible (e.g., interviewing patients visiting a clinic). Prone to bias.
  • ‚Ä¢Judgment (Purposive) Sampling: Selecting units based on the researcher's judgment about which units are most representative or informative.
  • ‚Ä¢Quota Sampling: Selecting units to meet predefined quotas for certain characteristics (e.g., age, sex) to reflect the population structure, but selection within quotas is non-random (often convenience).
  • ‚Ä¢Snowball Sampling: Used for hard-to-reach populations. Initial participants are asked to refer other potential participants.

‚ö†Ô∏è 6.5 Errors in Sampling

Errors in sample surveys can be broadly classified into two types:

  1. 1.Sampling Error (Random Error): The difference between a sample statistic and the true population parameter that occurs due to chance variation in selecting the sample. It is inherent in sampling. Can be reduced by increasing sample size. Can be estimated in probability sampling.
  2. 2.Non-Sampling Error (Systematic Error or Bias): Errors that occur during data collection, processing, or analysis, regardless of whether a sample or census is used. Cannot be reduced by increasing sample size. Difficult to estimate. Sources include:
    • Coverage Errors: Due to inadequate sampling frame (omissions, duplications).
    • Non-response Errors: When selected units do not participate or provide data.
    • Measurement Errors: Inaccurate responses due to poorly worded questions, interviewer bias, respondent bias (e.g., recall bias, social desirability bias), faulty instruments.
    • Processing Errors: Mistakes in coding, data entry, or analysis.

Minimizing non-sampling errors through careful planning, questionnaire design, interviewer training, supervision, and data quality checks is crucial for survey accuracy.
