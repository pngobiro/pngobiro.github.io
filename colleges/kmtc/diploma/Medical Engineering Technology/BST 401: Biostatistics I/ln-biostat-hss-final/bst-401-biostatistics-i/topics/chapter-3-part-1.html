CHAPTER THREE
Summarizing data

üéØ 3.1 Learning Objectives

At the end of this chapter, the student is expected to be able to:

  1. 1.Describe the purposes of measures of central tendency and dispersion
  2. 2.Calculate the different measures of central tendency and dispersion
  3. 3.Identify the properties and limitations of the different measures of central tendency and dispersion
  4. 4.Understand the criterion for the selection of a measure of central tendency and dispersion

üìñ 3.2 Introduction

The frequency distribution gives a general picture of the data. However, sometimes we need a more precise description, especially when comparing two or more sets of data.

Statistical measures can be used to summarize the main characteristics of the data. These measures are broadly divided into two categories:

  1. 1.Measures of Central Tendency (Averages): These measures indicate the center or typical value of the data set. Common measures include the mean, median, and mode.
  2. 2.Measures of Variation (Dispersion): These measures indicate the spread or variability of the data around the center. Common measures include the range, variance, standard deviation, and coefficient of variation.

These summary measures are also known as descriptive statistics.

When calculating descriptive statistics, we distinguish between:

  • ‚Ä¢Parameters: Summary measures calculated for a population.
  • ‚Ä¢Statistics: Summary measures calculated for a sample.

We often use sample statistics to estimate population parameters.

üéØ 3.3 Measures of Central Tendency

A measure of central tendency is a single value that attempts to describe a set of data by identifying the central position within that set of data. As such, measures of central tendency are sometimes called measures of central location. They are also classed as summary statistics.

The Mean

The mean is the most commonly used measure of central tendency. It is calculated by summing all the observations and dividing by the total number of observations.

Mean for Ungrouped Data

Let $x_1, x_2, ..., x_n$ be the n observations in a sample.

The sample mean, denoted by $\bar{x}$ (read as "x-bar"), is calculated as:

$$ \bar{x} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i}{n} = \frac{x_1 + x_2 + ... + x_n}{n} $$

Let $X_1, X_2, ..., X_N$ be the N observations in a population.

The population mean, denoted by $\mu$ (Greek letter mu), is calculated as:

$$ \mu = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N} X_i}{N} = \frac{X_1 + X_2 + ... + X_N}{N} $$

Example: Calculate the mean weight of the first 5 students from the data in the previous chapter's example (68, 62, 75, 60, 58 kg).

$$ \bar{x} = \frac{68 + 62 + 75 + 60 + 58}{5} = \frac{323}{5} = 64.6 \text{ kg} $$

Mean for Grouped Data

When data are grouped into a frequency distribution, we approximate the mean using the class midpoints.

Let $m_1, m_2, ..., m_k$ be the midpoints of the k classes, and let $f_1, f_2, ..., f_k$ be the corresponding class frequencies.

The mean for grouped data is calculated as:

$$ \bar{x} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{k} f_i m_i}{\sum_{i=1}^{k} f_i} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{k} f_i m_i}{n} $$

where $n = \sum f_i$ is the total number of observations.

Properties of the Mean:

  • ‚Ä¢Uniqueness: For a given set of data there is one and only one mean.
  • ‚Ä¢Simplicity: The mean is easily understood and easy to compute.
  • ‚Ä¢Uses all data: The mean takes into account every value in the data set.
  • ‚Ä¢Affected by extreme values: The mean is sensitive to outliers (very large or very small values), which can pull the mean towards them.
  • ‚Ä¢The sum of the deviations of the values from their mean is zero: $\sum (x_i - \bar{x}) = 0$.

The Median

The median is the middle value in an ordered data set. It divides the data into two equal halves: 50% of the observations are below the median, and 50% are above.

Median for Ungrouped Data

Steps:

  1. 1.Arrange the data in ascending (or descending) order.
  2. 2.Determine the position of the median: $(n+1)/2$.
  3. 3. If n is odd, the median is the value at the $(n+1)/2$ position.
    If n is even, the median is the average of the values at the $n/2$ and $(n/2)+1$ positions.

Example 1 (n is odd): Find the median for the data: 2, 5, 8, 3, 6.

  1. 1.Ordered data: 2, 3, 5, 6, 8
  2. 2.Position: (5+1)/2 = 3rd position
  3. 3.Median = 5

Example 2 (n is even): Find the median for the data: 10, 4, 7, 12, 6, 9.

  1. 1.Ordered data: 4, 6, 7, 9, 10, 12
  2. 2.Position: (6+1)/2 = 3.5th position (between 3rd and 4th)
  3. 3.Median = Average of 3rd (7) and 4th (9) values = (7+9)/2 = 8

Median for Grouped Data

The median for grouped data is found using the following formula:

$$ \text{Median} = L_m + \left( \frac{n/2 - cf_{m-1}}{f_m} \right) w $$

Where:

  • ‚Ä¢$L_m$ = Lower class boundary of the median class (the class containing the n/2-th observation).
  • ‚Ä¢$n$ = Total number of observations.
  • ‚Ä¢$cf_{m-1}$ = Cumulative frequency of the class preceding the median class.
  • ‚Ä¢$f_m$ = Frequency of the median class.
  • ‚Ä¢$w$ = Width of the median class.

Example: Calculate the median weight using the grouped frequency distribution from Table 2.3.

  1. 1.Find the position of the median: n/2 = 40/2 = 20th observation.
  2. 2.Identify the median class: Look at the cumulative frequency (cf) column in Table 2.3. The 20th observation falls in the class 67-72 (cf goes from 14 to 23).
  3. 3.Determine the values for the formula:
    • $L_m$ = Lower boundary of 67-72 class = 66.5
    • $n/2 = 20$
    • $cf_{m-1}$ = Cumulative frequency of the preceding class (61-66) = 14
    • $f_m$ = Frequency of the median class (67-72) = 9
    • $w$ = Class width = 6
  4. 4.Calculate the median:
$$ \text{Median} = 66.5 + \left( \frac{20 - 14}{9} \right) 6 = 66.5 + \left( \frac{6}{9} \right) 6 = 66.5 + 4 = 70.5 \text{ kg} $$
