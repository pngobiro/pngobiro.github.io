üìñ 7.2 Introduction

Statistical inference is the process of drawing conclusions about a population based on information obtained from a sample drawn from that population.

There are two main branches of statistical inference:

  1. 1.Estimation: Estimating the value of an unknown population parameter (e.g., population mean $\mu$, population proportion P) using sample data.
  2. 2.Hypothesis Testing: Testing a claim or hypothesis about a population parameter using sample data (Covered in Chapter 8).

This chapter focuses on estimation.

üìç 7.3 Point estimation

A point estimate is a single numerical value calculated from sample data that serves as the best guess for the value of an unknown population parameter.

  • ‚Ä¢The sample mean ($\bar{x}$) is the point estimate for the population mean ($\mu$).
  • ‚Ä¢The sample proportion ($p$) is the point estimate for the population proportion ($P$).
  • ‚Ä¢The sample standard deviation ($s$) is the point estimate for the population standard deviation ($\sigma$).

Properties of Good Point Estimators:

  • 1.Unbiasedness: The expected value (average over all possible samples) of the estimator equals the true population parameter. $E(\bar{x}) = \mu$. $E(p) = P$. ($s^2$ is an unbiased estimator of $\sigma^2$, which is why we use $n-1$ in the denominator).
  • 2.Efficiency: Among unbiased estimators, the one with the smallest variance (smallest spread in its sampling distribution) is considered the most efficient. $\bar{x}$ is the most efficient estimator of $\mu$.
  • 3.Consistency: As the sample size ($n$) increases, the estimator gets closer and closer to the true population parameter.

While point estimates provide a single best guess, they don't convey the uncertainty associated with the estimate. Interval estimation addresses this.

üìä 7.4 Sampling distribution of means

Imagine taking many random samples of the same size ($n$) from a population and calculating the mean ($\bar{x}$) for each sample. The distribution of these sample means is called the sampling distribution of the mean.

Key Properties:

  • 1.Mean of the Sampling Distribution: The mean of all possible sample means ($\mu_{\bar{x}}$) is equal to the population mean ($\mu$). $E(\bar{x}) = \mu_{\bar{x}} = \mu$. (This confirms $\bar{x}$ is an unbiased estimator of $\mu$).
  • 2.Standard Deviation of the Sampling Distribution (Standard Error of the Mean, SEM): The standard deviation of all possible sample means ($\sigma_{\bar{x}}$) is related to the population standard deviation ($\sigma$) and the sample size ($n$):
    $$ \sigma_{\bar{x}} = \text{SEM} = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} $$
    (If sampling is from a finite population without replacement and $n/N > 0.05$, a finite population correction factor is applied: $\sigma_{\bar{x}} = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \sqrt{\frac{N-n}{N-1}}$).
    The standard error measures the variability of sample means around the population mean. It decreases as the sample size ($n$) increases.
  • 3.Shape of the Sampling Distribution (Central Limit Theorem - CLT):
    • If the original population is normally distributed, the sampling distribution of $\bar{x}$ will also be normally distributed, regardless of the sample size $n$.
    • Central Limit Theorem (CLT): If the original population is *not* normally distributed, the sampling distribution of $\bar{x}$ will become approximately normal as the sample size $n$ increases. Generally, if $n \ge 30$, the sampling distribution of $\bar{x}$ can be considered approximately normal, regardless of the shape of the original population distribution.

The Central Limit Theorem is extremely important because it allows us to use the normal distribution for inference about the population mean even when the population itself is not normal, provided the sample size is large enough.

üìè 7.5 Interval estimation (large samples)

An interval estimate provides a range of plausible values for an unknown population parameter, based on sample data. This range is called a confidence interval (CI).

A confidence interval is associated with a confidence level (e.g., 90%, 95%, 99%), which represents the probability that the interval estimation procedure will produce an interval containing the true population parameter if the process were repeated many times.

The general form of a confidence interval is:

$$ \text{Point Estimate} \pm (\text{Critical Value}) \times (\text{Standard Error of the Estimate}) $$

Confidence Interval for the Population Mean ($\mu$) (Large Samples, $\sigma$ Known or $n \ge 30$)

When the population standard deviation ($\sigma$) is known, or when the sample size is large ($n \ge 30$, allowing us to approximate $\sigma$ with the sample standard deviation $s$ and invoke the CLT), the confidence interval for $\mu$ is based on the Z-distribution.

The formula for a $(1-\alpha) \times 100\%$ confidence interval for $\mu$ is:

$$ \bar{x} \pm Z_{\alpha/2} \left( \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \right) $$

Or, if $\sigma$ is unknown but $n \ge 30$:

$$ \bar{x} \pm Z_{\alpha/2} \left( \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} \right) $$

Where:

  • ‚Ä¢$\bar{x}$ is the sample mean.
  • ‚Ä¢$s$ is the sample standard deviation (used if $\sigma$ is unknown and $n \ge 30$).
  • ‚Ä¢$n$ is the sample size.
  • ‚Ä¢$Z_{\alpha/2}$ is the critical value from the standard normal distribution corresponding to the desired confidence level $(1-\alpha)$. It leaves an area of $\alpha/2$ in each tail of the Z-distribution.

Common Confidence Levels and $Z_{\alpha/2}$ values:

  • ‚Ä¢90% Confidence Level: $\alpha = 0.10$, $\alpha/2 = 0.05$, $Z_{0.05} = 1.645$
  • ‚Ä¢95% Confidence Level: $\alpha = 0.05$, $\alpha/2 = 0.025$, $Z_{0.025} = 1.96$
  • ‚Ä¢99% Confidence Level: $\alpha = 0.01$, $\alpha/2 = 0.005$, $Z_{0.005} = 2.576$

The term $Z_{\alpha/2} \left( \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} \right)$ is called the margin of error (ME).

Confidence Interval = Point Estimate $\pm$ Margin of Error.

Example: A sample of 100 students has a mean weight $\bar{x} = 70$ kg with a sample standard deviation $s = 10$ kg. Construct a 95% confidence interval for the true mean weight of all students.

Here, $n=100$ (large sample), $\bar{x}=70$, $s=10$.

For 95% confidence, $Z_{\alpha/2} = 1.96$.

Standard Error (SEM) = $s / \sqrt{n} = 10 / \sqrt{100} = 10 / 10 = 1$.

Margin of Error (ME) = $Z_{\alpha/2} \times SEM = 1.96 \times 1 = 1.96$.

Confidence Interval = $\bar{x} \pm ME = 70 \pm 1.96$.

The 95% CI is $(70 - 1.96, 70 + 1.96) = (68.04, 71.96)$ kg.

Interpretation: We are 95% confident that the true mean weight of all students lies between 68.04 kg and 71.96 kg.
