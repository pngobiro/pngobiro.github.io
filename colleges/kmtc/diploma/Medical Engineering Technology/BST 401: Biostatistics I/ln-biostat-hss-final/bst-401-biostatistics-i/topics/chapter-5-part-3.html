Common Probability Distributions (Continued)

2. The Poisson Distribution

Used for discrete random variables representing the number of events occurring in a fixed interval of time or space, when these events occur independently and with a known average rate.

Examples: Number of phone calls received per hour, number of bacteria colonies per plate, number of radioactive decays per minute.

The probability of observing exactly $x$ events in an interval is given by the Poisson Probability Formula:

$$ P(X=x) = \frac{e^{-\lambda} \lambda^x}{x!} \quad \text{for } x = 0, 1, 2, ... $$

Where:

  • ‚Ä¢$X$ is the number of events.
  • ‚Ä¢$\lambda$ (lambda) is the average number of events per interval (the rate parameter).
  • ‚Ä¢$e$ is the base of the natural logarithm ($e \approx 2.71828$).
  • ‚Ä¢$x!$ is x factorial.

Mean and Variance of Poisson Distribution:

  • ‚Ä¢Mean: $E(X) = \mu = \lambda$
  • ‚Ä¢Variance: $Var(X) = \sigma^2 = \lambda$

(Note: For the Poisson distribution, the mean and variance are equal).

Example: A hospital emergency room receives patients at an average rate of 3 per hour ($\lambda=3$). What is the probability that exactly 2 patients arrive in the next hour ($x=2$)?

$$ P(X=2) = \frac{e^{-3} 3^2}{2!} = \frac{(0.0498) \times 9}{2 \times 1} = \frac{0.4482}{2} = 0.2241 $$

So, there is about a 22.41% probability that exactly 2 patients will arrive in the next hour.

The Poisson distribution can approximate the Binomial distribution when $n$ is large and $p$ is small (typically $n \ge 20$ and $p \le 0.05$), using $\lambda = np$.

3. The Normal (Gaussian) Distribution

The most important distribution for continuous random variables in statistics.

Many natural phenomena follow a normal distribution (e.g., height, weight, blood pressure, measurement errors).

It is described by its mean ($\mu$) and standard deviation ($\sigma$).

The probability density function (PDF) is:

$$ f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma \sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{x-\mu}{\sigma} \right)^2} $$

Properties of the Normal Distribution:

  • ‚Ä¢Bell-shaped and symmetric about the mean ($\mu$).
  • ‚Ä¢Mean = Median = Mode.
  • ‚Ä¢The curve extends indefinitely in both directions but approaches the horizontal axis asymptotically.
  • ‚Ä¢The total area under the curve is 1 (or 100%).
  • ‚Ä¢The shape is determined by $\mu$ and $\sigma$. Changing $\mu$ shifts the curve left or right. Changing $\sigma$ changes the spread (smaller $\sigma$ = taller, narrower curve; larger $\sigma$ = shorter, wider curve).
  • ‚Ä¢Empirical Rule applies (approx. 68% within $\pm 1\sigma$, 95% within $\pm 2\sigma$, 99.7% within $\pm 3\sigma$).

The Standard Normal Distribution (Z-distribution)

A special case of the normal distribution with mean $\mu=0$ and standard deviation $\sigma=1$. Denoted as N(0, 1).

Any normal random variable X with mean $\mu$ and standard deviation $\sigma$ can be transformed into a standard normal variable Z using the Z-score formula:

$$ Z = \frac{X - \mu}{\sigma} $$

The Z-score represents the number of standard deviations a particular value X is away from the mean $\mu$.

Probabilities for the standard normal distribution are found using standard normal tables (like Table A2 in the appendix) or statistical software. The table typically gives the area under the curve to the left of a given Z-score, $P(Z \le z)$.

Example: Assume adult male heights are normally distributed with $\mu = 175$ cm and $\sigma = 7$ cm. What is the probability that a randomly selected adult male is shorter than 182 cm?

First, find the Z-score for X = 182 cm:

$$ Z = \frac{182 - 175}{7} = \frac{7}{7} = 1.00 $$

We need to find $P(X < 182)$, which is equivalent to $P(Z < 1.00)$.

Using a standard normal table (or software), look up the area to the left of Z = 1.00. This area is approximately 0.8413.

So, $P(X < 182) = P(Z < 1.00) \approx 0.8413$. There is about an 84.13% probability that a randomly selected adult male is shorter than 182 cm.

Example: What is the probability that a male's height is between 168 cm and 189 cm?

Find Z-scores for X=168 and X=189:

$$ Z_1 = \frac{168 - 175}{7} = \frac{-7}{7} = -1.00 $$ $$ Z_2 = \frac{189 - 175}{7} = \frac{14}{7} = 2.00 $$

We need $P(168 < X < 189) = P(-1.00 < Z < 2.00)$.

This is calculated as $P(Z < 2.00) - P(Z < -1.00)$.

From the table: $P(Z < 2.00) \approx 0.9772$ and $P(Z < -1.00) \approx 0.1587$.

$$ P(-1.00 < Z < 2.00) = 0.9772 - 0.1587 = 0.8185 $$

So, there is about an 81.85% probability that a male's height is between 168 cm and 189 cm.
