Chi-Square Test of Independence

Tests whether two categorical variables, measured on the same subjects, are associated or independent in the population.

Data are presented in a contingency table (cross-tabulation).

Hypotheses:

$H_0$: The two variables are independent (no association).

$H_1$: The two variables are dependent (associated).

Expected Frequencies (E): Under the assumption of independence ($H_0$), the expected frequency for a cell in row $i$ and column $j$ is calculated as:

$$ E_{ij} = \frac{(\text{Row } i \text{ Total}) \times (\text{Column } j \text{ Total})}{\text{Grand Total}} $$

Test Statistic: $\chi^2_{calc} = \sum \frac{(O_{ij} - E_{ij})^2}{E_{ij}}$, summed over all cells in the table.

Degrees of Freedom: $df = (\text{Number of Rows} - 1) \times (\text{Number of Columns} - 1)$.

Decision Rule: Reject $H_0$ if $\chi^2_{calc} > \chi^2_{\alpha, df}$ or if p-value $\le \alpha$.

Assumption: Most expected frequencies ($E_{ij}$) should be $\ge 5$. No expected frequency should be < 1. (Some sources say no more than 20% of cells should have $E < 5$).

Example: Test if there is an association between smoking status and hypertension status using the following observed data at $\alpha=0.05$.

Table 8.2 Observed Frequencies: Smoking and Hypertension
HypertensiveNot HypertensiveRow Total
Smoker30 (O)70 (O)100
Non-Smoker20 (O)180 (O)200
Column Total50250300 (Grand Total)

Calculate Expected Frequencies:

  • ‚Ä¢E(Smoker, Hyper) = (100 * 50) / 300 = 16.67
  • ‚Ä¢E(Smoker, Not Hyper) = (100 * 250) / 300 = 83.33
  • ‚Ä¢E(Non-Smoker, Hyper) = (200 * 50) / 300 = 33.33
  • ‚Ä¢E(Non-Smoker, Not Hyper) = (200 * 250) / 300 = 166.67

(All $E_{ij} \ge 5$).

Calculate $\chi^2$ statistic:

$$ \chi^2_{calc} = \frac{(30-16.67)^2}{16.67} + \frac{(70-83.33)^2}{83.33} + \frac{(20-33.33)^2}{33.33} + \frac{(180-166.67)^2}{166.67} $$ $$ = \frac{(13.33)^2}{16.67} + \frac{(-13.33)^2}{83.33} + \frac{(-13.33)^2}{33.33} + \frac{(13.33)^2}{166.67} $$ $$ = \frac{177.69}{16.67} + \frac{177.69}{83.33} + \frac{177.69}{33.33} + \frac{177.69}{166.67} $$ $$ \approx 10.66 + 2.13 + 5.33 + 1.07 \approx 19.19 $$

$df = (2-1) \times (2-1) = 1 \times 1 = 1$.

Critical value $\chi^2_{0.05, 1}$ from table is 3.841.

Decision: Since $\chi^2_{calc} = 19.19 > 3.841$, reject $H_0$.

Conclusion: There is a significant association between smoking status and hypertension status at $\alpha=0.05$.

Chi-Square Test of Homogeneity

Tests if the distribution of a categorical variable is the same across two or more different populations (groups).

The calculation procedure and test statistic are identical to the test of independence. The difference lies in the study design and interpretation:

  • ‚Ä¢Independence: One sample drawn, two variables measured on each subject. Question: Are the variables associated?
  • ‚Ä¢Homogeneity: Two or more samples drawn from different populations/groups. One variable measured on each subject. Question: Is the distribution of the variable the same across the groups?

Hypotheses:

$H_0$: The proportions for the variable are the same across all populations/groups.

$H_1$: The proportions for the variable differ across at least two populations/groups.

Fisher's Exact Test

An alternative test for association in 2x2 contingency tables, especially used when the assumptions for the Chi-square test are not met (i.e., when expected frequencies are small, typically < 5).

It calculates the exact probability of observing the actual cell frequencies, or more extreme frequencies, given the fixed row and column totals, assuming the null hypothesis of independence is true.

The calculation involves factorials and is often performed using statistical software.

If the calculated exact probability (p-value) is less than or equal to $\alpha$, $H_0$ is rejected.
