The Variance (Continued)

Variance for Grouped Data

The sample variance for grouped data is calculated using class midpoints:

$$ s^2 = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{k} f_i (m_i - \bar{x})^2}{n-1} $$

Computational formula:

$$ s^2 = \frac{\sum f_i m_i^2 - \frac{(\sum f_i m_i)^2}{n}}{n-1} $$

Example: Calculate the sample variance using the grouped frequency distribution from Table 2.3. We know $\bar{x} = 71.75$ and $n=40$.

Properties of Variance:

  • •Always non-negative ($s^2 \ge 0$).
  • •Uses all data values.
  • •Sensitive to outliers (since deviations are squared).
  • •The unit of variance is the square of the unit of the original data (e.g., kg²), making interpretation difficult.

The Standard Deviation

The standard deviation is the most commonly used measure of variation.

It is the square root of the variance.

It measures the typical or average deviation of observations from the mean.

The sample standard deviation, denoted by $s$, is:

$$ s = \sqrt{s^2} = \sqrt{\frac{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2}{n-1}} $$

The population standard deviation, denoted by $\sigma$, is:

$$ \sigma = \sqrt{\sigma^2} = \sqrt{\frac{\sum (X_i - \mu)^2}{N}} $$

Example (Ungrouped): For the first 5 weights, $s^2 = 47.8$.

$$ s = \sqrt{47.8} \approx 6.91 \text{ kg} $$

Example (Grouped): For the 40 weights, $s^2 \approx 108.35$.

$$ s = \sqrt{108.35} \approx 10.41 \text{ kg} $$

Properties of Standard Deviation:

  • •Always non-negative ($s \ge 0$).
  • •Has the same unit as the original data, making it easier to interpret than variance.
  • •Uses all data values.
  • •Sensitive to outliers.
  • •For normally distributed data (bell-shaped curve):
    • Approximately 68% of data falls within $\bar{x} \pm 1s$.
    • Approximately 95% of data falls within $\bar{x} \pm 2s$.
    • Approximately 99.7% of data falls within $\bar{x} \pm 3s$. (Empirical Rule)

The Coefficient of Variation (CV)

The coefficient of variation expresses the standard deviation as a percentage of the mean.

It is a unitless measure of relative variability, useful for comparing the dispersion of data sets with different units or different means.

$$ CV = \left( \frac{s}{\bar{x}} \right) \times 100\% $$

For population data:

$$ CV = \left( \frac{\sigma}{\mu} \right) \times 100\% $$

Example: For the 40 student weights, $\bar{x} = 71.75$ kg and $s \approx 10.41$ kg.

$$ CV = \left( \frac{10.41}{71.75} \right) \times 100\% \approx 14.51\% $$

This means the standard deviation is about 14.5% of the mean weight.

A higher CV indicates greater relative variability.
