üìñ 8.2 Introduction

Hypothesis testing is a procedure based on sample evidence and probability theory used to determine whether a statement (hypothesis) about a population parameter is reasonable and should not be rejected, or is unreasonable and should be rejected.

It helps us make decisions or draw conclusions about a population based on sample results.

Examples of hypotheses we might test:

  • ‚Ä¢Is the average blood pressure of patients taking a new drug lower than the average blood pressure of patients taking a standard drug?
  • ‚Ä¢Is the proportion of smokers in a community different from the national average?
  • ‚Ä¢Does a new health education program increase the rate of vaccination coverage?

General Steps in Hypothesis Testing:

  1. 1.State the null hypothesis ($H_0$) and the alternative hypothesis ($H_1$ or $H_a$).
  2. 2.Choose the level of significance ($\alpha$).
  3. 3.Select the appropriate test statistic (e.g., Z, t, Chi-square).
  4. 4.Formulate the decision rule (determine the critical region based on $\alpha$ and the test statistic distribution).
  5. 5.Collect data and compute the value of the test statistic from the sample data.
  6. 6.Make a decision: Reject $H_0$ if the test statistic falls in the critical region; otherwise, do not reject $H_0$.
  7. 7.Interpret the results in the context of the problem.

(Alternatively, steps 4 and 6 can be replaced by calculating the p-value and comparing it to $\alpha$).

‚ùì 8.3 The Null and Alternative Hypotheses

Null Hypothesis ($H_0$):

  • ‚Ä¢A statement about the population parameter that is assumed to be true until evidence suggests otherwise.
  • ‚Ä¢Usually represents the status quo, no effect, or no difference.
  • ‚Ä¢Always contains an equality sign (=, $\le$, or $\ge$).
  • ‚Ä¢The hypothesis that we attempt to reject based on sample evidence.

Alternative Hypothesis ($H_1$ or $H_a$):

  • ‚Ä¢A statement that contradicts the null hypothesis.
  • ‚Ä¢Represents what the researcher is trying to find evidence for (e.g., an effect, a difference).
  • ‚Ä¢Never contains an equality sign; uses $\ne$, <, or >.
  • ‚Ä¢If $H_0$ is rejected, we conclude there is evidence in favor of $H_1$.

$H_0$ and $H_1$ are mutually exclusive and exhaustive (one of them must be true).

Types of Tests based on $H_1$:

  • 1.Two-tailed test: Tests for a difference in either direction. $H_1$ uses $\ne$. (e.g., $H_0: \mu = 100$, $H_1: \mu \ne 100$).
  • 2.One-tailed test (Left-tailed): Tests for a decrease. $H_1$ uses <. (e.g., $H_0: \mu \ge 100$, $H_1: \mu < 100$).
  • 3.One-tailed test (Right-tailed): Tests for an increase. $H_1$ uses >. (e.g., $H_0: \mu \le 100$, $H_1: \mu > 100$).

Example: A researcher wants to test if a new drug lowers blood pressure compared to a standard drug where the mean reduction is 10 mmHg.

$H_0: \mu \ge 10$ (The new drug is not better; reduction is 10 or more)

$H_1: \mu < 10$ (The new drug is better; reduction is less than 10)

This is a left-tailed test.

Example: Testing if the proportion of smokers in a town ($P$) is different from the national proportion of 0.20.

$H_0: P = 0.20$

$H_1: P \ne 0.20$

This is a two-tailed test.

‚öñÔ∏è 8.4 Level of significance

When making a decision based on sample data, there is always a risk of making an error.

(Confidence = $1-\alpha$)		Type I Error
(Probability = $\alpha$)

(Probability = $\beta$)		Correct Decision
(Power = $1-\beta$)
  • ‚Ä¢Type I Error: Rejecting the null hypothesis ($H_0$) when it is actually true. (False positive). The probability of committing a Type I error is denoted by $\alpha$.
  • ‚Ä¢Type II Error: Failing to reject the null hypothesis ($H_0$) when it is actually false. (False negative). The probability of committing a Type II error is denoted by $\beta$.
  • ‚Ä¢Level of Significance ($\alpha$): The maximum acceptable probability of making a Type I error. It is set by the researcher before the test (commonly $\alpha = 0.05$ or $\alpha = 0.01$). It defines the critical region for rejecting $H_0$.
  • ‚Ä¢Power of the Test ($1-\beta$): The probability of correctly rejecting a false null hypothesis. It is the ability of the test to detect a true effect or difference.

There is a trade-off between $\alpha$ and $\beta$. Decreasing $\alpha$ (making it harder to reject $H_0$) generally increases $\beta$ (making it harder to detect a true effect), and vice versa, for a fixed sample size.

The choice of $\alpha$ depends on the consequences of making each type of error. If a Type I error is more serious (e.g., approving an ineffective drug), a smaller $\alpha$ (like 0.01) is chosen. If a Type II error is more serious (e.g., failing to detect a beneficial drug), a larger $\alpha$ (like 0.05 or 0.10) might be acceptable to increase power.

üß™ 8.5 Tests of significance on means and proportions (large samples)

When the sample size is large ($n \ge 30$) or the population standard deviation ($\sigma$) is known, we use the Z-test statistic based on the standard normal distribution.

Hypothesis Test for a Single Population Mean ($\mu$) (Large Sample)

Assumptions: Sample is random, $n \ge 30$ or population is normal and $\sigma$ is known.

Hypotheses:

  • 1.Two-tailed: $H_0: \mu = \mu_0$ vs $H_1: \mu \ne \mu_0$
  • 2.Left-tailed: $H_0: \mu \ge \mu_0$ vs $H_1: \mu < \mu_0$
  • 3.Right-tailed: $H_0: \mu \le \mu_0$ vs $H_1: \mu > \mu_0$

(Where $\mu_0$ is the hypothesized value of the population mean).

Test Statistic (Z-test):

$$ Z_{calc} = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}} \quad (\text{if } \sigma \text{ known}) $$

Or

$$ Z_{calc} = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{s / \sqrt{n}} \quad (\text{if } \sigma \text{ unknown, } n \ge 30) $$

Decision Rule (Critical Value Approach):

  • 1.Two-tailed: Reject $H_0$ if $|Z_{calc}| > Z_{\alpha/2}$.
  • 2.Left-tailed: Reject $H_0$ if $Z_{calc} < -Z_{\alpha}$.
  • 3.Right-tailed: Reject $H_0$ if $Z_{calc} > Z_{\alpha}$.

(Where $Z_{\alpha/2}$ and $Z_{\alpha}$ are critical values from the standard normal table corresponding to the chosen $\alpha$).

Decision Rule (P-value Approach):

The p-value is the probability of observing a test statistic as extreme as, or more extreme than, the one calculated from the sample, assuming the null hypothesis ($H_0$) is true.

  • 1.Two-tailed: p-value = $2 \times P(Z > |Z_{calc}|)$.
  • 2.Left-tailed: p-value = $P(Z < Z_{calc})$.
  • 3.Right-tailed: p-value = $P(Z > Z_{calc})$.

Decision: Reject $H_0$ if p-value $\le \alpha$. Do not reject $H_0$ if p-value $> \alpha$.

Example: A researcher believes the average recovery time for a certain illness is more than 10 days. A random sample of 50 patients ($n=50$) has a mean recovery time of $\bar{x} = 10.8$ days with a standard deviation of $s = 3$ days. Test the researcher's belief at $\alpha = 0.05$.

Step 1: Hypotheses

$H_0: \mu \le 10$ (Average recovery time is not more than 10 days)

$H_1: \mu > 10$ (Average recovery time is more than 10 days) (Right-tailed test)

Step 2: Level of Significance

$\alpha = 0.05$

Step 3: Test Statistic

Since $n=50 \ge 30$, use Z-test. $\sigma$ is unknown, use $s$.

$$ Z_{calc} = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{s / \sqrt{n}} = \frac{10.8 - 10}{3 / \sqrt{50}} = \frac{0.8}{3 / 7.071} = \frac{0.8}{0.424} \approx 1.886 $$

Step 4: Decision Rule (Critical Value Approach)

For a right-tailed test with $\alpha = 0.05$, the critical value is $Z_{\alpha} = Z_{0.05} = 1.645$.

Reject $H_0$ if $Z_{calc} > 1.645$.

Step 5: Decision

Since $Z_{calc} = 1.886$ is greater than the critical value $1.645$, we reject $H_0$.

Step 6: Interpretation

There is sufficient evidence at the 0.05 significance level to conclude that the average recovery time for this illness is more than 10 days.

Decision Rule (P-value Approach for the example):

For a right-tailed test, p-value = $P(Z > Z_{calc}) = P(Z > 1.886)$.

Using a Z-table or software, $P(Z < 1.886) \approx 0.9704$.

p-value = $1 - P(Z < 1.886) = 1 - 0.9704 = 0.0296$.

Decision: Since p-value (0.0296) $\le \alpha (0.05)$, we reject $H_0$. (Same conclusion).
