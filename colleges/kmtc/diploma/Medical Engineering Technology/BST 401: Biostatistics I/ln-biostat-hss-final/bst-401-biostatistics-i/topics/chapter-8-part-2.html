Hypothesis Test for a Single Population Proportion ($P$) (Large Sample)

Assumptions: Sample is random, sample size is large ($np_0 \ge 5$ and $nq_0 \ge 5$, where $p_0$ is the hypothesized proportion and $q_0 = 1-p_0$).

Hypotheses:

  • 1.Two-tailed: $H_0: P = P_0$ vs $H_1: P \ne P_0$
  • 2.Left-tailed: $H_0: P \ge P_0$ vs $H_1: P < P_0$
  • 3.Right-tailed: $H_0: P \le P_0$ vs $H_1: P > P_0$

(Where $P_0$ is the hypothesized value of the population proportion).

Test Statistic (Z-test):

$$ Z_{calc} = \frac{p - P_0}{\sqrt{P_0(1-P_0)/n}} $$

Where $p = x/n$ is the sample proportion.

Decision Rule: Same as for the mean (using Z critical values or p-value approach).

Example: It is claimed that 30% ($P_0=0.30$) of adults in a region are smokers. In a random sample of 200 adults ($n=200$), 70 are found to be smokers ($x=70$). Test the claim at $\alpha = 0.05$.

Step 1: Hypotheses

$H_0: P = 0.30$ (The proportion of smokers is 30%)

$H_1: P \ne 0.30$ (The proportion of smokers is different from 30%) (Two-tailed test)

Step 2: Level of Significance

$\alpha = 0.05$

Step 3: Check Assumptions & Calculate Test Statistic

Check large sample: $np_0 = 200(0.30) = 60 \ge 5$. $nq_0 = 200(1-0.30) = 140 \ge 5$. Condition met.

Sample proportion $p = x/n = 70/200 = 0.35$.

$$ Z_{calc} = \frac{0.35 - 0.30}{\sqrt{0.30(1-0.30)/200}} = \frac{0.05}{\sqrt{0.30(0.70)/200}} = \frac{0.05}{\sqrt{0.21/200}} = \frac{0.05}{\sqrt{0.00105}} \approx \frac{0.05}{0.0324} \approx 1.543 $$

Step 4: Decision Rule (Critical Value Approach)

For a two-tailed test with $\alpha = 0.05$, the critical values are $Z_{\alpha/2} = \pm Z_{0.025} = \pm 1.96$.

Reject $H_0$ if $|Z_{calc}| > 1.96$.

Step 5: Decision

Since $|Z_{calc}| = 1.543$ is not greater than $1.96$, we do not reject $H_0$.

Step 6: Interpretation

There is not sufficient evidence at the 0.05 significance level to conclude that the proportion of smokers in this region is different from 30%.

Decision Rule (P-value Approach for the example):

For a two-tailed test, p-value = $2 \times P(Z > |Z_{calc}|) = 2 \times P(Z > 1.543)$.

Using a Z-table, $P(Z < 1.543) \approx 0.9386$.

p-value = $2 \times (1 - 0.9386) = 2 \times 0.0614 = 0.1228$.

Decision: Since p-value (0.1228) $> \alpha (0.05)$, we do not reject $H_0$. (Same conclusion).

Hypothesis Test for Comparing Two Population Means ($\mu_1 - \mu_2$) (Large Independent Samples)

Used to compare the means of two independent groups when both sample sizes are large ($n_1 \ge 30$ and $n_2 \ge 30$).

Hypotheses:

  • 1.Two-tailed: $H_0: \mu_1 = \mu_2$ (or $\mu_1 - \mu_2 = 0$) vs $H_1: \mu_1 \ne \mu_2$ (or $\mu_1 - \mu_2 \ne 0$)
  • 2.Left-tailed: $H_0: \mu_1 \ge \mu_2$ vs $H_1: \mu_1 < \mu_2$
  • 3.Right-tailed: $H_0: \mu_1 \le \mu_2$ vs $H_1: \mu_1 > \mu_2$

Test Statistic (Z-test):

$$ Z_{calc} = \frac{(\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2) - (\mu_1 - \mu_2)_0}{\sqrt{\frac{\sigma_1^2}{n_1} + \frac{\sigma_2^2}{n_2}}} $$

Where $(\mu_1 - \mu_2)_0$ is the hypothesized difference (usually 0 under $H_0$). If population variances $\sigma_1^2$ and $\sigma_2^2$ are unknown, use sample variances $s_1^2$ and $s_2^2$ since samples are large.

$$ Z_{calc} = \frac{(\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2)}{\sqrt{\frac{s_1^2}{n_1} + \frac{s_2^2}{n_2}}} \quad (\text{when testing } H_0: \mu_1 = \mu_2) $$

Decision Rule: Use Z critical values or p-value approach as before.

Example: Compare the mean recovery times for two groups. Group 1 (New Drug): $n_1=50, \bar{x}_1=10.8, s_1=3$. Group 2 (Standard Drug): $n_2=60, \bar{x}_2=12.0, s_2=3.5$. Test if the new drug has a shorter mean recovery time at $\alpha=0.05$.

$H_0: \mu_1 \ge \mu_2$

$H_1: \mu_1 < \mu_2$ (Left-tailed)

$$ Z_{calc} = \frac{(10.8 - 12.0)}{\sqrt{\frac{3^2}{50} + \frac{3.5^2}{60}}} = \frac{-1.2}{\sqrt{\frac{9}{50} + \frac{12.25}{60}}} = \frac{-1.2}{\sqrt{0.18 + 0.204}} = \frac{-1.2}{\sqrt{0.384}} \approx \frac{-1.2}{0.620} \approx -1.935 $$

Critical value for left-tailed test at $\alpha=0.05$ is $-Z_{0.05} = -1.645$.

Since $Z_{calc} = -1.935 < -1.645$, reject $H_0$.

Conclusion: There is sufficient evidence at $\alpha=0.05$ that the new drug has a shorter mean recovery time.

Hypothesis Test for Comparing Two Population Proportions ($P_1 - P_2$) (Large Independent Samples)

Used to compare proportions from two independent groups when sample sizes are large ($n_1p_1, n_1q_1, n_2p_2, n_2q_2$ all $\ge 5$).

Hypotheses:

  • 1.Two-tailed: $H_0: P_1 = P_2$ vs $H_1: P_1 \ne P_2$
  • 2.Left-tailed: $H_0: P_1 \ge P_2$ vs $H_1: P_1 < P_2$
  • 3.Right-tailed: $H_0: P_1 \le P_2$ vs $H_1: P_1 > P_2$

Test Statistic (Z-test):

First, calculate the pooled sample proportion ($\bar{p}$) under the assumption $H_0: P_1 = P_2$:

$$ \bar{p} = \frac{x_1 + x_2}{n_1 + n_2} $$

Then calculate the test statistic:

$$ Z_{calc} = \frac{(p_1 - p_2)}{\sqrt{\bar{p}(1-\bar{p}) \left( \frac{1}{n_1} + \frac{1}{n_2} \right)}} $$

Where $p_1 = x_1/n_1$ and $p_2 = x_2/n_2$.

Decision Rule: Use Z critical values or p-value approach.

Example: Test if the proportion of hypertensive individuals differs between males and females. Sample 1 (Males): $n_1=200, x_1=60 \implies p_1=0.30$. Sample 2 (Females): $n_2=150, x_2=30 \implies p_2=0.20$. Use $\alpha=0.05$.

$H_0: P_1 = P_2$

$H_1: P_1 \ne P_2$ (Two-tailed)

Pooled proportion: $\bar{p} = (60+30)/(200+150) = 90/350 \approx 0.257$.

$$ Z_{calc} = \frac{(0.30 - 0.20)}{\sqrt{0.257(1-0.257) \left( \frac{1}{200} + \frac{1}{150} \right)}} = \frac{0.10}{\sqrt{0.257(0.743) \left( 0.005 + 0.00667 \right)}} $$ $$ = \frac{0.10}{\sqrt{0.191(0.01167)}} = \frac{0.10}{\sqrt{0.002229}} \approx \frac{0.10}{0.0472} \approx 2.119 $$

Critical values for two-tailed test at $\alpha=0.05$ are $\pm 1.96$.

Since $|Z_{calc}| = 2.119 > 1.96$, reject $H_0$.

Conclusion: There is sufficient evidence at $\alpha=0.05$ that the proportion of hypertensive individuals differs between males and females.

‚û°Ô∏è 8.6 One tailed tests

As seen in the examples above, one-tailed tests are used when the alternative hypothesis specifies a direction (less than < or greater than >).

The critical region is located entirely in one tail of the distribution.

  • ‚Ä¢Left-tailed test ($H_1$ uses <): Reject $H_0$ if the test statistic is less than the negative critical value (e.g., $Z_{calc} < -Z_{\alpha}$ or $t_{calc} < -t_{\alpha, df}$). The p-value is the area to the left of the calculated test statistic.
  • ‚Ä¢Right-tailed test ($H_1$ uses >): Reject $H_0$ if the test statistic is greater than the positive critical value (e.g., $Z_{calc} > Z_{\alpha}$ or $t_{calc} > t_{\alpha, df}$). The p-value is the area to the right of the calculated test statistic.

The choice between a one-tailed and two-tailed test depends on the research question before collecting data. If there is a specific directional interest (e.g., proving something is *better*, *shorter*, *higher*), a one-tailed test is appropriate. If the interest is simply whether there is *any difference*, a two-tailed test is used.

üî¨ 8.7 Comparing the means of small samples

When comparing the means of two groups with small sample sizes ($n_1 < 30$ or $n_2 < 30$) and unknown population standard deviations, we use the t-test, assuming the populations are approximately normally distributed.

There are two main cases depending on whether the samples are independent or paired.

Independent Samples t-test

Used when the two samples are selected independently from two populations.

Additional Assumption: We often need to decide whether to assume the population variances are equal ($\sigma_1^2 = \sigma_2^2$) or unequal ($\sigma_1^2 \ne \sigma_2^2$). A preliminary test (like Levene's test or F-test for variances) can be used, but often the unequal variances version (Welch's t-test) is preferred as it is more robust.

Case 1: Assuming Equal Population Variances

First, calculate the pooled sample variance ($s_p^2$), which is a weighted average of the two sample variances:

$$ s_p^2 = \frac{(n_1-1)s_1^2 + (n_2-1)s_2^2}{n_1 + n_2 - 2} $$

The test statistic is:

$$ t_{calc} = \frac{(\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2) - (\mu_1 - \mu_2)_0}{\sqrt{s_p^2 \left( \frac{1}{n_1} + \frac{1}{n_2} \right)}} $$

The degrees of freedom for the t-distribution are $df = n_1 + n_2 - 2$.

Case 2: Assuming Unequal Population Variances (Welch's t-test)

The test statistic is:

$$ t_{calc} = \frac{(\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2) - (\mu_1 - \mu_2)_0}{\sqrt{\frac{s_1^2}{n_1} + \frac{s_2^2}{n_2}}} $$

The degrees of freedom are calculated using the Welch-Satterthwaite equation (complex formula, often calculated by software):

$$ df \approx \frac{\left( \frac{s_1^2}{n_1} + \frac{s_2^2}{n_2} \right)^2}{\frac{\left( \frac{s_1^2}{n_1} \right)^2}{n_1-1} + \frac{\left( \frac{s_2^2}{n_2} \right)^2}{n_2-1}} $$

(The calculated df is often not an integer and may be rounded down).

Decision Rule: Compare $t_{calc}$ to the critical value $t_{\alpha/2, df}$ (two-tailed) or $t_{\alpha, df}$ (one-tailed) from the t-distribution table using the appropriate degrees of freedom, or use the p-value approach.

Example (Assuming Equal Variances): A sample of 10 male students ($n_1=10$) has a mean height $\bar{x}_1=175$ cm, $s_1=6$ cm. A sample of 12 female students ($n_2=12$) has $\bar{x}_2=165$ cm, $s_2=5$ cm. Test if there is a significant difference in mean height between males and females at $\alpha=0.05$, assuming equal variances and normal distributions.

$H_0: \mu_1 = \mu_2$

$H_1: \mu_1 \ne \mu_2$ (Two-tailed)

Pooled variance:

$$ s_p^2 = \frac{(10-1)6^2 + (12-1)5^2}{10 + 12 - 2} = \frac{9(36) + 11(25)}{20} = \frac{324 + 275}{20} = \frac{599}{20} = 29.95 $$

Test statistic:

$$ t_{calc} = \frac{(175 - 165) - 0}{\sqrt{29.95 \left( \frac{1}{10} + \frac{1}{12} \right)}} = \frac{10}{\sqrt{29.95 (0.1 + 0.0833)}} = \frac{10}{\sqrt{29.95 (0.1833)}} = \frac{10}{\sqrt{5.489}} \approx \frac{10}{2.343} \approx 4.268 $$

Degrees of freedom $df = 10 + 12 - 2 = 20$.

Critical value $t_{\alpha/2, df} = t_{0.025, 20}$ from t-table is 2.086.

Decision: Since $|t_{calc}| = 4.268 > 2.086$, reject $H_0$.

Conclusion: There is a significant difference in mean height between male and female students at $\alpha=0.05$.
