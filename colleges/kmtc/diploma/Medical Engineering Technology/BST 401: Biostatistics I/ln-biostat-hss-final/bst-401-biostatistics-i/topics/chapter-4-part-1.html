üìñ 4.2 Introduction

Demography: is a science that studies human population with respect to size, distribution, composition, and the components of change such as fertility, mortality and migration and the social and economic factors related to them.

  • ‚Ä¢Population size: is the number of persons in the population at a certain time.
  • ‚Ä¢Population distribution: is the arrangement of the population in space at a given time i.e. geographically or among various types of residential areas (e.g. Urban, Rural).
  • ‚Ä¢Population composition (Structure): is the distribution of a population among its sex and age groupings.
  • ‚Ä¢Components of change: are factors influencing population size, namely births, deaths and migration.

Demography has immense importance in the health field.

  • -It helps to describe the state of health of a community.
  • -It helps for comparison of health status of different communities.
  • -It helps in planning, implementing and evaluating health services.
  • -It helps in determining the felt needs of a community.
  • -It helps in identifying health problems of a community.

üìö 4.3 Sources of demographic data

The primary sources of demographic data are:

  1. 1.Census
  2. 2.Vital registration system
  3. 3.Sample surveys

1. Census

A census is defined as the total process of collecting, compiling and publishing demographic, economic and social data pertaining, at a specified time or times, to all persons in a country or delimited territory.

Features of a census:

  • ‚Ä¢Universality: It should cover the entire population or territory.
  • ‚Ä¢Simultaneity: Data collection should refer to a specific point in time.
  • ‚Ä¢Periodicity: Censuses should be taken at regular intervals (usually every 10 years).
  • ‚Ä¢Individual units: Data should be collected for each individual.

Types of Census:

  • a)De facto census: Counts individuals where they are found on the census day.
  • b)De jure census: Counts individuals at their usual place of residence.

Ethiopia conducted its first census in 1984 and the second in 1994.

Information collected in a census typically includes:

  • -Relationship to head of household
  • -Age, Sex, Marital status
  • -Place of birth, Place of usual residence
  • -Religion, Ethnicity, Language
  • -Educational attainment, Literacy status
  • -Economic activity status
  • -Number of children ever born and surviving (for women)

Uses of Census Data:

  • -Provides baseline data for population projections and planning.
  • -Denominator for calculating vital rates.
  • -Sampling frame for surveys.
  • -Resource allocation.

Limitations of Census:

  • -Expensive and time-consuming.
  • -Errors in coverage (undercount or overcount) and content.
  • -Provides data only at infrequent intervals.

2. Vital Registration System (VRS)

A vital registration system involves the continuous and compulsory recording of vital events (births, deaths, marriages, divorces) by government agencies.

It is the best source of data on births and deaths if the system is complete and accurate.

In many developing countries, including Ethiopia, the VRS is often incomplete or non-existent, especially in rural areas.

Uses of VRS Data:

  • -Provides data for calculating fertility and mortality rates.
  • -Monitors population change between censuses.
  • -Legal purposes (birth/death certificates).

Limitations of VRS:

  • -Incompleteness of registration.
  • -Errors in reported information (e.g., age, cause of death).
  • -Requires significant infrastructure and resources.

3. Sample Surveys

Sample surveys collect data from a representative sample of the population.

They are often used to supplement census and VRS data, especially in countries where these systems are weak.

Surveys can collect more detailed information on specific topics (e.g., health, fertility, migration) than censuses.

Examples in Ethiopia: Demographic and Health Surveys (DHS), Welfare Monitoring Surveys (WMS).

Uses of Survey Data:

  • -Estimate demographic parameters when census/VRS data are unavailable or unreliable.
  • -Study relationships between demographic and socioeconomic variables.
  • -Evaluate health programs.

Limitations of Surveys:

  • -Sampling errors (results are estimates with margins of error).
  • -Non-sampling errors (e.g., response errors, interviewer bias).
  • -Can be expensive, though less so than a census.
  • -May suffer from recall bias for past events.

üìà 4.4 Stages in demographic transition

The demographic transition model describes the historical shift in birth and death rates from high to low levels in a population.

It typically involves four or five stages:

  • Stage 1: High Stationary - High birth rates and high death rates. Population growth is slow and fluctuating. (Characteristic of pre-industrial societies).
  • Stage 2: Early Expanding - Death rates begin to fall due to improvements in health and sanitation, while birth rates remain high. Population growth accelerates rapidly. (Many developing countries entered this stage in the 20th century).
  • Stage 3: Late Expanding - Birth rates start to decline due to factors like increased education, urbanization, access to contraception, and changing social norms. Population growth continues but slows down.
  • Stage 4: Low Stationary - Both birth rates and death rates are low. Population growth is low or stable. (Characteristic of most developed countries).
  • Stage 5: Declining? - Some demographers propose a fifth stage where birth rates fall below death rates, leading to population decline. (Observed in some European countries and Japan).

The pace and timing of the transition vary between countries.

Understanding a country's stage in the demographic transition is crucial for planning health services, education, and economic development.
