Sample Size for Estimating Population Proportion ($P$)

The margin of error (ME) for estimating $P$ (large sample) is $ME = Z_{\alpha/2} \sqrt{P(1-P)/n}$.

Solving for $n$, we get the formula for the required sample size:

$$ n = \frac{Z_{\alpha/2}^2 P(1-P)}{ME^2} $$

Where:

  • ‚Ä¢$n$ = required sample size (always round up).
  • ‚Ä¢$Z_{\alpha/2}$ = Z-value for the desired confidence level.
  • ‚Ä¢$P$ = expected population proportion.
  • ‚Ä¢$ME$ = desired margin of error (maximum acceptable difference between $p$ and $P$, expressed as a proportion).

Estimating $P$:** Since the population proportion ($P$) is the value we want to estimate, it is unknown. We need an estimate for the calculation:

  • -Use results from previous similar studies or a pilot study.
  • -Use an educated guess.
  • -Most conservative approach: Use $P = 0.5$. This maximizes the product $P(1-P)$ (which equals 0.25) and thus yields the largest possible required sample size, ensuring the desired margin of error is met regardless of the true proportion.

Example: We want to estimate the proportion of children who are fully immunized in a district. We desire 95% confidence and a margin of error of $\pm 5\%$ (ME = 0.05). We have no prior estimate for the proportion.

Here, Confidence = 95% $\implies Z_{\alpha/2} = 1.96$.

$ME = 0.05$.

Since $P$ is unknown, use the conservative estimate $P = 0.5$.

$$ n = \frac{(1.96)^2 (0.5)(1-0.5)}{(0.05)^2} = \frac{(3.8416) (0.25)}{0.0025} = \frac{0.9604}{0.0025} = 384.16 $$

Always round up: $n = 385$.

We need a sample size of at least 385 children.

Example (with prior estimate): If a previous study suggested the immunization proportion was around 80% ($P=0.8$), what sample size is needed with ME=0.05 and 95% confidence?

$$ n = \frac{(1.96)^2 (0.8)(1-0.8)}{(0.05)^2} = \frac{(3.8416) (0.8)(0.2)}{0.0025} = \frac{0.614656}{0.0025} = 245.86 $$

Round up: $n = 246$.

Using a prior estimate (if reliable) can reduce the required sample size compared to the conservative $P=0.5$ approach.

  1. 1.Explain the difference between a point estimate and an interval estimate. Which one provides more information about the population parameter?
  2. 2.What is the Central Limit Theorem, and why is it important for constructing confidence intervals for the mean?
  3. 3.A random sample of 64 medical students ($n=64$) had a mean score of 85 on a standardized test, with a sample standard deviation of 8 ($s=8$). Construct a 99% confidence interval for the true mean score of all medical students.
  4. 4.In a survey of 400 randomly selected adults ($n=400$), 120 reported experiencing back pain in the last month ($x=120$). Construct a 90% confidence interval for the true proportion of adults experiencing back pain.
  5. 5.A sample of 15 diabetic patients ($n=15$) had their fasting blood sugar levels measured. The sample mean was $\bar{x} = 140$ mg/dL and the sample standard deviation was $s = 12$ mg/dL. Assuming blood sugar levels are normally distributed, construct a 95% confidence interval for the true mean fasting blood sugar level in this patient population.
  6. 6.A health researcher wants to estimate the average number of hours children watch television per week. They want to be 95% confident that their estimate is within $\pm 1$ hour of the true mean. Based on previous studies, the standard deviation is estimated to be 3 hours. What sample size is required?
  7. 7.A public health official wants to estimate the proportion of adults in a town who have received a flu vaccine. They want the estimate to be within $\pm 4\%$ (ME=0.04) of the true proportion with 95% confidence.
    1. What sample size is needed if no prior estimate of the proportion is available?
    2. What sample size is needed if it is believed that the proportion is around 30%?
