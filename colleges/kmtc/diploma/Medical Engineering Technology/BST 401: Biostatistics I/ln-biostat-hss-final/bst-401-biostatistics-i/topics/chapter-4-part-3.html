üå± 4.8 Population growth and Projection

Population change is determined by the balance of births, deaths, and migration.

Balancing Equation:

$$ P_t = P_0 + (B - D) + (I - E) $$

Where:

  • ‚Ä¢$P_t$ = Population at time t
  • ‚Ä¢$P_0$ = Population at time 0
  • ‚Ä¢$B$ = Births between time 0 and t
  • ‚Ä¢$D$ = Deaths between time 0 and t
  • ‚Ä¢$I$ = Immigrants between time 0 and t
  • ‚Ä¢$E$ = Emigrants between time 0 and t

$(B - D)$ is called Natural Increase.

$(I - E)$ is called Net Migration.

Rate of Natural Increase (RNI)

$$ RNI = CBR - CDR $$

(Usually expressed per 1000 population).

Population Growth Rate (GR)

Includes the effect of migration.

$$ GR = RNI + \text{Net Migration Rate} $$

(Net Migration Rate = (I - E) / Mid-year population * 1000)

Population Projection

Population projection is the estimation of future population size, distribution, and composition based on assumptions about future trends in fertility, mortality, and migration.

Methods:

  • a)Mathematical Methods: Use formulas based on past growth trends.
    • Arithmetic Projection: Assumes constant absolute increase per year. $P_t = P_0 + (b \times t)$
    • Geometric Projection: Assumes constant rate of growth per year. $P_t = P_0 (1 + r)^t$
    • Exponential Projection: Assumes continuous compounding of growth. $P_t = P_0 e^{rt}$
    (Where r is the annual growth rate, t is the number of years).
  • b)Component Method: Projects future population by applying assumed future rates of fertility, mortality, and migration to the current age-sex structure. More complex but generally more accurate, especially for long-term projections.

Population Doubling Time: The approximate time it takes for a population to double, assuming a constant growth rate.

$$ \text{Doubling Time} \approx \frac{70}{\text{Growth Rate (in percent)}} $$

Example: If the growth rate is 2.5% per year, Doubling Time ‚âà 70 / 2.5 = 28 years.

üè• 4.9 Health services statistics

Health services statistics provide information on the resources, utilization, and outcomes of health services.

They are crucial for managing and evaluating health programs and facilities.

Common indicators relate to hospital activities:

Hospital Beds

  • ‚Ä¢Bed Complement: Total number of beds available for inpatient use.
  • ‚Ä¢Bed Count: Number of beds actually occupied at a specific time (e.g., midnight census).
  • ‚Ä¢Bed Days: Sum of days each bed was occupied over a period (equivalent to total inpatient days).

Hospital Utilization Rates

  • ‚Ä¢Admission Rate: (Number of admissions / Population) * k
  • ‚Ä¢Average Length of Stay (ALOS): Total inpatient days / Number of discharges (including deaths)
  • ‚Ä¢Bed Occupancy Rate (BOR): (Actual inpatient days / Potential inpatient days) * 100
    (Potential inpatient days = Bed complement * Number of days in period)
  • ‚Ä¢Bed Turnover Rate (BTR): Number of discharges (including deaths) / Average bed complement
  • ‚Ä¢Turnover Interval (TI): Average number of days a bed remains empty between patients.
    TI = (Potential days - Actual days) / Number of discharges

Hospital Outcome Rates

  • ‚Ä¢Gross Death Rate: (Total hospital deaths / Total discharges including deaths) * 100
  • ‚Ä¢Net Death Rate (Institutional Death Rate): (Deaths occurring >48 hours after admission / Discharges including deaths >48 hours after admission) * 100
  • ‚Ä¢Postoperative Death Rate: (Deaths within 10 days of surgery / Total patients operated) * 100
  • ‚Ä¢Maternal Mortality Rate (Hospital): (Maternal deaths in hospital / Total obstetric discharges including deaths) * 100,000
  • ‚Ä¢Infant Death Rate (Hospital): (Infant deaths in hospital / Total infant discharges including deaths) * 1000
  • ‚Ä¢Anesthesia Death Rate: (Deaths due to anesthesia / Total patients given anesthesia) * 100,000
  • ‚Ä¢Autopsy Rate: (Number of autopsies performed / Total deaths) * 100
