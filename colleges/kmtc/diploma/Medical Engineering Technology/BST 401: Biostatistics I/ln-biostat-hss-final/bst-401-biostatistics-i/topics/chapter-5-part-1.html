üìñ 5.2 Introduction

The concept of probability is fundamental to statistical inference. It allows us to quantify uncertainty and make decisions based on data.

Definitions:

  • ‚Ä¢Experiment: Any process that generates well-defined outcomes. (e.g., tossing a coin, measuring blood pressure).
  • ‚Ä¢Sample Space (S): The set of all possible outcomes of an experiment. (e.g., S = {Head, Tail} for a coin toss).
  • ‚Ä¢Event (E): A subset of the sample space; a collection of one or more outcomes. (e.g., E = {Head}).
  • ‚Ä¢Probability of an Event P(E): A numerical measure of the likelihood that the event will occur. It ranges from 0 (impossible event) to 1 (certain event).

Approaches to Probability:

  1. 1.Classical (or Priori) Probability: Assumes all outcomes in the sample space are equally likely.
    $$ P(E) = \frac{\text{Number of outcomes favorable to E}}{\text{Total number of possible outcomes}} $$
    Example: Probability of getting a Head in a fair coin toss = 1/2.
  2. 2.Relative Frequency (or Empirical/Posteriori) Probability: Based on observed data from repeating an experiment many times.
    $$ P(E) = \frac{\text{Number of times E occurred}}{\text{Total number of trials}} $$
    Example: If a drug cures 80 out of 100 patients, the empirical probability of cure is 80/100 = 0.8.
  3. 3.Subjective Probability: Based on personal belief, experience, or judgment. Used when classical or empirical approaches are not feasible. Example: Probability of a specific candidate winning an election.

Basic Properties of Probability:

  • 1.The probability of any event E is between 0 and 1, inclusive: $0 \le P(E) \le 1$.
  • 2.The sum of the probabilities of all possible mutually exclusive outcomes in the sample space is 1: $\sum P(O_i) = 1$, where $O_i$ are the individual outcomes.
  • 3.The probability of the complement of an event E (denoted $E'$ or $E^c$, meaning E does not occur) is: $P(E') = 1 - P(E)$.

‚ûï 5.3 Mutually exclusive events and the additive law

Two events A and B are mutually exclusive (or disjoint) if they cannot occur at the same time. If A occurs, B cannot, and vice versa. Their intersection is empty: $P(A \cap B) = 0$.

Example: When rolling a single die, the events 'getting a 2' and 'getting a 5' are mutually exclusive.

Additive Law of Probability:

The probability that either event A or event B (or both) occurs is given by:

$$ P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B) $$

Where:

  • ‚Ä¢$P(A \cup B)$ is the probability of A or B or both (Union).
  • ‚Ä¢$P(A \cap B)$ is the probability of both A and B occurring (Intersection).

Special Case (Mutually Exclusive Events): If A and B are mutually exclusive, then $P(A \cap B) = 0$, and the additive law simplifies to:

$$ P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) $$

Example 1 (Mutually Exclusive): Probability of drawing a King or a Queen from a standard deck of 52 cards.

$P(King) = 4/52$, $P(Queen) = 4/52$. These are mutually exclusive.

$$ P(King \cup Queen) = P(King) + P(Queen) = \frac{4}{52} + \frac{4}{52} = \frac{8}{52} = \frac{2}{13} $$

Example 2 (Not Mutually Exclusive): Probability of drawing a King or a Heart from a standard deck.

$P(King) = 4/52$, $P(Heart) = 13/52$.

The event 'King and Heart' is the King of Hearts. $P(King \cap Heart) = 1/52$.

$$ P(King \cup Heart) = P(King) + P(Heart) - P(King \cap Heart) $$ $$ = \frac{4}{52} + \frac{13}{52} - \frac{1}{52} = \frac{16}{52} = \frac{4}{13} $$

‚úñÔ∏è 5.4 Conditional Probability and the multiplicative law

Conditional Probability: The probability of event A occurring given that event B has already occurred. It is denoted by $P(A|B)$.

$$ P(A|B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}, \quad \text{provided } P(B) > 0 $$

Similarly,

$$ P(B|A) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(A)}, \quad \text{provided } P(A) > 0 $$

Independent Events: Two events A and B are independent if the occurrence of one does not affect the probability of the occurrence of the other.

If A and B are independent, then:

  • ‚Ä¢$P(A|B) = P(A)$
  • ‚Ä¢$P(B|A) = P(B)$

Events that are not independent are called dependent.

Multiplicative Law of Probability:

The probability that both event A and event B occur is given by rearranging the conditional probability formula:

$$ P(A \cap B) = P(A|B) P(B) $$

Or equivalently:

$$ P(A \cap B) = P(B|A) P(A) $$

Special Case (Independent Events): If A and B are independent, then $P(A|B) = P(A)$ and $P(B|A) = P(B)$, so the multiplicative law simplifies to:

$$ P(A \cap B) = P(A) P(B) $$

Example 1 (Independent): Probability of getting Heads on two consecutive fair coin tosses.

Let A = Head on 1st toss, B = Head on 2nd toss. $P(A) = 1/2$, $P(B) = 1/2$. Events are independent.

$$ P(A \cap B) = P(A) P(B) = \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{4} $$

Example 2 (Dependent): Drawing two cards from a deck without replacement. What is the probability of drawing two Kings?

Let A = 1st card is King, B = 2nd card is King.

$P(A) = 4/52$.

Given the first was a King, there are 3 Kings left in 51 cards. So, $P(B|A) = 3/51$.

$$ P(A \cap B) = P(B|A) P(A) = \left( \frac{3}{51} \right) \left( \frac{4}{52} \right) = \frac{12}{2652} = \frac{1}{221} $$

Example 3 (Using Conditional Probability Definition): In a group of 100 patients, 40 have disease D, 30 have symptom S, and 20 have both D and S.

$P(D) = 40/100 = 0.4$

$P(S) = 30/100 = 0.3$

$P(D \cap S) = 20/100 = 0.2$

What is the probability a patient has symptom S, given they have disease D?

$$ P(S|D) = \frac{P(D \cap S)}{P(D)} = \frac{0.2}{0.4} = 0.5 $$

Are disease D and symptom S independent?

Check if $P(S|D) = P(S)$. Here, $0.5 \ne 0.3$. So, they are dependent.

Alternatively, check if $P(D \cap S) = P(D)P(S)$. Here, $0.2 \ne (0.4)(0.3) = 0.12$. So, they are dependent.
