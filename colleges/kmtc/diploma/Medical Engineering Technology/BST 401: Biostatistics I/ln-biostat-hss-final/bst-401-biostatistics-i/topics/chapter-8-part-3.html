üî¨ 8.7 Comparing the means of small samples (Continued)

Paired (Dependent) Samples t-test

Used when the two samples are related or matched (e.g., measurements on the same subject before and after an intervention, measurements on matched pairs like twins).

The test focuses on the differences between the paired observations.

Steps:

  1. 1.Calculate the difference ($d_i$) for each pair: $d_i = x_{1i} - x_{2i}$ (or $x_{after} - x_{before}$).
  2. 2.Calculate the mean of the differences ($\bar{d}$).
  3. 3.Calculate the standard deviation of the differences ($s_d$).
  4. 4.Perform a one-sample t-test on the differences, testing if the mean difference ($\mu_d$) is significantly different from 0 (or some other hypothesized value).

Hypotheses:

  • 1.Two-tailed: $H_0: \mu_d = 0$ vs $H_1: \mu_d \ne 0$
  • 2.Left-tailed: $H_0: \mu_d \ge 0$ vs $H_1: \mu_d < 0$
  • 3.Right-tailed: $H_0: \mu_d \le 0$ vs $H_1: \mu_d > 0$

Test Statistic (Paired t-test):

$$ t_{calc} = \frac{\bar{d} - \mu_{d0}}{s_d / \sqrt{n}} $$

Where $\mu_{d0}$ is the hypothesized mean difference (usually 0), $n$ is the number of pairs, and $s_d$ is the standard deviation of the differences.

Degrees of Freedom: $df = n-1$.

Decision Rule: Compare $t_{calc}$ to the critical value $t_{\alpha/2, df}$ or $t_{\alpha, df}$ from the t-distribution table, or use the p-value approach.

Example: Systolic blood pressure (SBP) was measured for 8 patients before and after taking a new medication. Test if the medication significantly reduces SBP at $\alpha=0.05$. Assume differences are normally distributed.

$H_0: \mu_d \le 0$

$H_1: \mu_d > 0$ (Right-tailed)

Test Statistic:

$$ t_{calc} = \frac{\bar{d} - 0}{s_d / \sqrt{n}} = \frac{7 - 0}{2.070 / \sqrt{8}} = \frac{7}{2.070 / 2.828} = \frac{7}{0.732} \approx 9.56 $$

Decision Rule: $df = n-1 = 8-1 = 7$. For a right-tailed test at $\alpha=0.05$, the critical value is $t_{\alpha, df} = t_{0.05, 7} = 1.895$. Reject $H_0$ if $t_{calc} > 1.895$.

Decision: Since $t_{calc} = 9.56 > 1.895$, reject $H_0$.

Conclusion: There is significant evidence at $\alpha=0.05$ that the medication reduces systolic blood pressure.

ü§î 8.8 Confidence interval or P-value?

Both confidence intervals (Chapter 7) and hypothesis tests (p-values) are methods of statistical inference, and they are closely related.

  • ‚Ä¢A $(1-\alpha) \times 100\%$ confidence interval contains the range of plausible values for the population parameter. If the hypothesized value under $H_0$ (e.g., $\mu_0$ or $P_0$) falls *outside* this interval, it is equivalent to rejecting $H_0$ at the $\alpha$ level of significance in a two-tailed test. If the hypothesized value falls *inside* the interval, it is equivalent to not rejecting $H_0$.
  • ‚Ä¢The p-value provides the exact probability of observing the sample result (or more extreme) if $H_0$ were true. It gives a measure of the strength of evidence against $H_0$. Smaller p-values indicate stronger evidence against $H_0$.

Advantages of Confidence Intervals:

  • -Provide a range of plausible values for the parameter, giving information about the magnitude and precision of the estimate.
  • -Easier to interpret in terms of practical significance.

Advantages of P-values:

  • -Provide the exact level of significance achieved by the data.
  • -Allow readers to make their own conclusions based on their preferred $\alpha$ level.

Often, it is best practice to report both the confidence interval and the p-value.

üìä 8.9 Test of significance using the Chi-square and Fisher's exact tests

The Chi-square ($\chi^2$) test is used for analyzing categorical data. It compares observed frequencies in categories with expected frequencies under a null hypothesis.

The general formula for the Chi-square test statistic is:

$$ \chi^2_{calc} = \sum \frac{(O - E)^2}{E} $$

Where:

  • ‚Ä¢$O$ = Observed frequency in a category.
  • ‚Ä¢$E$ = Expected frequency in that category under $H_0$.
  • ‚Ä¢The sum is taken over all categories.

The calculated $\chi^2$ value is compared to a critical value from the Chi-square distribution with appropriate degrees of freedom ($df$). The Chi-square distribution is skewed to the right and depends on $df$.

Common Applications:

  • 1.Goodness-of-Fit Test: Tests if the observed frequencies of a single categorical variable match the expected frequencies from a hypothesized distribution.
  • 2.Test of Independence: Tests if two categorical variables are associated or independent in a population, using data from a contingency table.
  • 3.Test of Homogeneity: Tests if the proportion distribution for one categorical variable is the same across different populations (groups defined by a second categorical variable). Also uses contingency table data.

Chi-Square Goodness-of-Fit Test

Tests how well the observed frequency distribution of a single categorical variable fits an expected distribution.

Hypotheses:

$H_0$: The observed frequencies follow the specified distribution.

$H_1$: The observed frequencies do not follow the specified distribution.

Expected Frequencies (E): Calculated based on the hypothesized proportions ($P_i$) and the total sample size ($n$): $E_i = n \times P_i$.

Test Statistic: $\chi^2_{calc} = \sum \frac{(O_i - E_i)^2}{E_i}$

Degrees of Freedom: $df = k - 1$, where $k$ is the number of categories.

Decision Rule: Reject $H_0$ if $\chi^2_{calc} > \chi^2_{\alpha, df}$ (critical value from Chi-square table) or if p-value $\le \alpha$. (Chi-square tests are typically right-tailed).

Assumption: Expected frequency in each category should be at least 5 ($E_i \ge 5$). If not, categories may need to be combined.

Example: A standard die is rolled 120 times. The observed frequencies for each face are: 1 (18 times), 2 (22), 3 (15), 4 (25), 5 (19), 6 (21). Test if the die is fair at $\alpha=0.05$.

$H_0$: The die is fair ($P_1=P_2=...=P_6 = 1/6$).

$H_1$: The die is not fair.

Expected frequency for each face: $E = n \times P = 120 \times (1/6) = 20$. (All $E_i \ge 5$).
