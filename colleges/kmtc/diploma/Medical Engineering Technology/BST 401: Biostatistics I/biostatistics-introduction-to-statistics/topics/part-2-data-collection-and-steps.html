Data Collection Methods and Statistical Process

üìä Data Collection Methods

Primary data is data that you collect yourself using such methods as: direct observation, surveys and interviews.

1. Questionnaires

Questionnaires are a popular means of collecting data, but are difficult to design and often require many rewrites before an acceptable questionnaire is produced.

Advantages:

  • ‚Ä¢Can be used as a method in its own right or as a basis for interviewing or a telephone survey.
  • ‚Ä¢Can be posted, e-mailed or faxed.
  • ‚Ä¢Can cover a large number of people or organizations.
  • ‚Ä¢Wide geographic coverage.
  • ‚Ä¢Relatively cheap.
  • ‚Ä¢No prior arrangements are needed.
  • ‚Ä¢Avoids embarrassment on the part of the respondent.
  • ‚Ä¢Respondent can consider responses.
  • ‚Ä¢No interviewer bias

Disadvantages:

  • ‚Ä¢Design problems.
  • ‚Ä¢Questions have to be relatively simple.
  • ‚Ä¢Historically low response rate (although inducements may help).
  • ‚Ä¢Time delay whilst waiting for responses to be returned.
  • ‚Ä¢Require a return deadline.
  • ‚Ä¢Several reminders may be required.
  • ‚Ä¢Assumes no literacy problems.
  • ‚Ä¢No control over who completes it.
  • ‚Ä¢Not possible to give assistance if required.
  • ‚Ä¢Problems with incomplete questionnaires.
  • ‚Ä¢Replies not spontaneous and independent of each other.
  • ‚Ä¢Respondent can read all questions beforehand and then decide whether to complete or not. For example, perhaps because it is too long, too complex, uninteresting, or too personal

2. Interviews

Interviewing is a technique that is primarily used to gain an understanding of the underlying reasons and motivations for people's attitudes, preferences or behavior. Interviews can be undertaken on a personal one-to-one basis or in a group. They can be conducted at work, at home, in the street or in a shopping centre, or some other agreed location.

Advantages:

  • ‚Ä¢Serious approach by respondent resulting in accurate information. Good response rate.
  • ‚Ä¢Completed and immediate.
  • ‚Ä¢Possible in-depth questions
  • ‚Ä¢Interviewer in control and can give help if there is a problem.
  • ‚Ä¢Can investigate motives and feelings.
  • ‚Ä¢Can use recording equipment.
  • ‚Ä¢Characteristics of respondent assessed - tone of voice, facial expression, hesitation, etc.
  • ‚Ä¢Can use props.
  • ‚Ä¢One interviewer used, uniformity of approach. Used to pilot other methods.

Disadvantages:

  • ‚Ä¢Need to set up interviews.
  • ‚Ä¢Time consuming.
  • ‚Ä¢Geographic limitations.
  • ‚Ä¢Can be expensive.
  • ‚Ä¢Normally need a set of questions.
  • ‚Ä¢Respondent bias - tendency to please or impress, create false personal image, or end interview quickly.
  • ‚Ä¢Embarrassment possible if personal questions.
  • ‚Ä¢Transcription and analysis can present problems - subjectivity.
  • ‚Ä¢If many interviewers, training required

Observation

In the method of data collection called observation, the researcher observes participants in natural and/or structured environments.

It is important to collect observational data because what people say is not always what they do!

Observation can be carried out in two types of environments:

  1. Laboratory observation (which is done in a lab set up by the researcher).
  2. Naturalistic observation (which is done in real-world settings).

Advantages

  • ‚Ä¢Allows one to directly see what people do without having to rely on what they say they do.
  • ‚Ä¢Provides first-hand experience, especially if the observer participates in activities.
  • ‚Ä¢Can provide relatively objective measurement of behavior (especially for standardized observations).
  • ‚Ä¢Observer can determine what does not occur.
  • ‚Ä¢Observer may see things that escape the awareness of people in the setting.
  • ‚Ä¢Excellent way to discover what is occurring in a setting.
  • ‚Ä¢Helps in understanding importance of contextual factors.
  • ‚Ä¢Can be used with participants with weak verbal skills.
  • ‚Ä¢May provide information on things people would otherwise be unwilling to talk about.
  • ‚Ä¢Observer may move beyond selective perceptions of people in the setting.
  • ‚Ä¢Good for description.
  • ‚Ä¢Provides moderate degree of realism (when done outside of the laboratory).

Disadvantages

  • ‚Ä¢Reasons for observed behaviour may be unclear.
  • ‚Ä¢Reactive effects may occur when respondents know they are being observed (e.g., people being observed may behave in atypical ways).
  • ‚Ä¢Investigator effects (e.g., personal biases and selective perception of observers)
  • ‚Ä¢Observer may "go native" (i.e., over-identifying with the group being studied).
  • ‚Ä¢Sampling of observed people and settings may be limited.
  • ‚Ä¢Cannot observe large or dispersed populations.
  • ‚Ä¢Some settings and content of interest cannot be observed.
  • ‚Ä¢Collection of unimportant material may be moderately high.
  • ‚Ä¢More expensive to conduct than questionnaires and tests.
  • ‚Ä¢Data analysis can be time consuming.

üìÇ Secondary/Existing Data

Secondary data (i.e., data originally used for a different purpose) are contrasted with primary data (i.e., original data collected for the new research study).

The most commonly used secondary data are documents, physical data, and archived research data.

Documents

There are two main kinds of documents.

  1. Personal documents (i.e., things written or recorded for private purposes), Letters, diaries, family pictures.
  2. Official documents (i.e., things written or recorded for public or private organizations). Newspapers, annual reports, yearbooks, minutes.

Physical Data

Physical data (are any material thing created or left by humans that might provide information about a phenomenon of interest to a researcher).

Archived Research Data

Archived research data (i.e., research data collected by other researchers for other purposes, and these data are save often in tape form or cd form so that others might later use the data).

Advantages documents and physical data

  • ‚Ä¢Can provide insight into what people think and what they do.
  • ‚Ä¢Unobtrusive, making reactive and investigator effects very unlikely.
  • ‚Ä¢Can be collected for time periods occurring in the past (e.g., historical data).
  • ‚Ä¢Provides useful background and historical data on people, groups, and organizations.
  • ‚Ä¢Useful for corroboration.
  • ‚Ä¢Grounded in local setting.
  • ‚Ä¢Useful for exploration.

Advantages of archived research data

  • ‚Ä¢Archived research data are available on a wide variety of topics.
  • ‚Ä¢Inexpensive.
  • ‚Ä¢Often are reliable and valid (high measurement validity).
  • ‚Ä¢Can study trends. - Ease of data analysis.
  • ‚Ä¢Often based on high quality or large probability samples.

Disadvantages of documents and physical data

  • ‚Ä¢May be incomplete.
  • ‚Ä¢May be representative only of one perspective.
  • ‚Ä¢Access to some types of content is limited.
  • ‚Ä¢May not provide insight into participants' personal thinking for physical data.
  • ‚Ä¢May not apply to general populations.

Disadvantages of archived research data

  • ‚Ä¢May not be available for the population of interest to you.
  • ‚Ä¢May not be available for the research questions of interest to you.
  • ‚Ä¢Data may be dated.
  • ‚Ä¢Open-ended or qualitative data usually not available.
  • ‚Ä¢Many of the most important findings have already been mined from the data.

üìù Steps in a statistical enquiry.

1. Problem statement:

State the problem clearly. Suppose the problem concerns wages in a factory or teachers. Is it wages earned or wage rates? Must the statistics concern all employees or separate grade?

2. Sample Selection

A sample is a group of items selected from the population for study, i.e. identify a group of objects drawn from a population of size N where $\mathrm{n}>\mathrm{N}$. Population refers to the aggregate of all individual items, people, objects, etc about which information is required. That is a set of all possible observations or measurements that are under consideration. Individual items in the population are called elementary limits.

Population can be classified into;

  • ‚Ä¢Finite population - Those, which contain precise and definite number of elementary units or items, e.g. population of University of somalia students in 2018.
  • ‚Ä¢Infinite Population - one, which contains an infinity large number of elementary units, e.g. population of ocean fish. For infinite population, complete information cannot be obtained and therefore any study for such a population must be based on the study of a sample. Note that If it's not possible to have a complete coverage (population) of information being sought, then one needs to determine the size of the sample and the method of sampling. This depends on
  • >Kind of information wanted
  • >Cost
  • >The degree of accuracy required

Reasons why one would study a sample and not the entire population:

  • >Population may be infinitely large and may not be possible to study at all, or if population is finite it might be too large for all items to be examined
  • >Financial and time constraints: Study of entire population may not be feasible.
  • >Entire population might not be accessible, e.g. in time scores analysis, only recent data may be available

3. Drafting the Questionnaire

Questions should be drafted to give the exact information wanted. A pilot survey is useful to enable a satisfactory questionnaire to be obtained.

4. Collection of Data

By use of enumerators, ask questions and fill in the questionnaire

  • >Secondary - already published
  • >Primary - raw from field for specific purpose

5. Editing the Schedules

Questionnaires require to be checked sometimes coded and calculations made before tabulation can be done.

6. Organization of Data

For example, counting items, summing values, categorizing values, etc.

7. Analysis and Interpretation of information

This requires sound knowledge of statistical methods and secondary sound knowledge of the subject for which statistical evidence has been obtained.

8. Presentation

May be in form of tables, charts, and graphs.

9. Writing of the report

  • >Gives the results of investigation
  • >Makes recommendations where necessary

N/B: For data collection, it can either be primary or secondary. Data can be collected from published sources (secondary) designing and experiment, conducting a survey.
