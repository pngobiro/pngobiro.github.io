Chapter 3: Muscular and Nervous Tissues

üí™ Muscular Tissues

Muscular tissues are highly specialized tissues for the production of tension which results in the generation of force that allows movement.

Properties of Muscular Tissue

  • Contractile - can shorten and generate pulling force
  • Excitable - contract in response to stimulation
  • Connected to bones - cause movement when contracted

Types of Muscular Tissue

1. Skeletal Muscles

  • Attached to bones
  • Comprise 40% of body mass
  • Generate heat during contraction
  • Involved in thermal homeostasis
  • Responsible for shivering response to low temperature

2. Cardiac Muscles

  • Form contractile walls of the heart
  • Contract on intrinsic rhythms
  • No external stimulation needed
  • Under involuntary control

3. Smooth Muscles

  • Responsible for involuntary movements in internal organs
  • Found in digestive system
  • Present in urinary system
  • Located in reproductive system
  • Found in airways and arteries

üß† Nervous Tissue

Nervous Tissue is an excitable tissue capable of sending and receiving electrochemical signals that provide the body with information.

Main Cell Types

  1. The neuron
  2. The neuroglia

The Neuron

Neurons have a characteristic morphology with three main parts:

  • Cell body - contains cytoplasm, organelles, and nucleus
  • Dendrites - thin extensions branching from cell body
  • Axon - long tail wrapped in myelin insulation

About Myelin

Myelin is a fatty white substance that:

  • Surrounds the axon of nerve cells
  • Forms an electrically insulating layer
  • Increases speed of impulse propagation

The Neuroglia

Also known as glial cells, neuroglia are non-neuronal cells that support neurons and play key roles in:

  • Formation of myelin
  • Nutrition provision for neurons
  • General structural support
  • Protection through pathogen destruction
  • Blood-brain barrier formation
  • Dead neuron cleanup
