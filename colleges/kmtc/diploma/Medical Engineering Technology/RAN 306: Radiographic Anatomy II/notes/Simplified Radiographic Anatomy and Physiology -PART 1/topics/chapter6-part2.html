Chapter 6: The Vertebral Column - Part 2

ü¶¥Thoracic Vertebrae

The main function of the thoracic spine is to hold the rib cage and protect the heart and lungs. There are twelve thoracic vertebrae numbered from T1 to T12. The range of motion in the thoracic spine is limited. Thoracic vertebrae articulate with the ribs on the superior and inferior costal facets found on the bodies of thoracic vertebrae.

ü¶¥Lumbar Vertebrae

The main function of the lumbar spine is to bear the weight of the body. The five lumbar vertebrae are numbered from L1 to L5. These vertebrae are much larger in size to absorb the stress of lifting and carrying heavy objects.

ü¶¥Sacrum and Coccyx

The main function of the sacrum is the connection between the spine and the hip bone. Sacrum is composed of five fused vertebrae. The union of the sacrum with the ilium forms the pelvic girdle.

The coccyx or tailbone is composed of four fused bones; they provide attachment for muscles and ligaments of the pelvic floor.

üí†Intervertebral Discs

Intervertebral discs are fibrocartilaginous cushions located between adjacent bony vertebrae in the spine they keep them from rubbing together and form fibrocartilaginous joint, to allow slight movement of the vertebrae. Intervertebral discs are composed of two layers:

  1. The annulus fibrosus is an outer fibrous ring.
  2. The nucleus pulposus is an inner gel-like center.

The fibers of the annulus fibrosus pull the vertebral bodies together against the elastic resistance of the gel-filled nucleus pulposus which act like a ball-bearing during movement, allowing the vertebral bodies to roll over the incompressible gel. The gel-filled nucleus is composed mostly of fluid. This fluid absorbed in the night during lying down and is pushed out during the day due to upright movement.

üîóJoints and Ligaments of the Vertebral Column

The vertebrae articulate with each other via joints between their articular facets and joints between their bodies and surrounding cartilages. There are also ligaments that strengthen and stabilize these joints; they resist hyperflexion and hyperextension of the vertebral column and hold the articulating parts that form the vertebral joints together.

Joints of the vertebral column:

  1. Intervertebral joints: There are two types of intervertebral joints between each adjacent vertebra:
    • Intervertebral fibrocartilaginous joint between the vertebral bodies and their adjacent intervertebral discs.
    • Intervertebral synovial joint between the facets of adjoining vertebral arches.
  2. Craniovertebral joints: there are two craniovertebral joints:
    • Atlantooccipital joint between the superior articular surfaces of the atlas and the occipital condyles ("yes"-nodding).
    • Atlantoaxial joint composed of two lateral joints between the inferior facets of atlas and superior facets of axis, and one median joint between the dens of the axis and anterior arch of the atlas ("no"-movement).
  3. Costovertebral joints: between the head of the ribs and adjacent vertebral bodies

Ligaments of the vertebral column:

  1. Ligamentum Flavum: are paired ligaments run between adjacent laminae of the vertebral bodies from the second cervical vertebra down to the sacrum. They mainly connect the laminae of each adjacent vertebrae and fuses them with the capsule of the facet joint serving as a covering band over the spinal canal.
  2. Anterior and posterior longitudinal ligaments run in bands down the anterior and posterior surfaces of the vertebral bodies from the skull to the sacrum. They help to stabilize the vertebral column.
  3. The interspinous ligament runs between the spinous processes.
  4. The supraspinous ligament connects the tips of the spinous processes and forms the strong nuchal ligament that runs posterior to the cervical spine.
