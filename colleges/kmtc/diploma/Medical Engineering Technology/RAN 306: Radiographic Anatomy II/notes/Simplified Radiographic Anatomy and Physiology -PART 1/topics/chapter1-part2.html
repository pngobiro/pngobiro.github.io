üîÑ Computed Tomography Scan (CT scan)

It refers to computerized x-ray imaging; it is the application of computer to process a group of X-ray images taken from different angles to produce precisely detailed cross-sectional images of the internal organs of specific part of human body.

CT Scanner Components and Operation

CT scanner is a ring-shaped machine uses a motorized x-ray tube that rotates around a circular gantry. During a CT scan imaging, the patient lies on a motorized platform that moves slowly through the gantry while the x-ray tube rotates around the patient and produce narrow fan shaped beams of x-rays through the patient. As the x-rays pass through the patient body they are picked up by special array of digital x-ray detectors, which are located directly opposite the x-ray tube, and transmit these attenuated rays to a computer.

Basic Principle of CT Scan

CT scan is based on the principle that the internal structure of the examined body part can be reconstructed from multiple projections, to build up cross-sectional slices through measurement of the linear attenuation coefficient.

The Linear Attenuation Coefficient ($\mu$)

The linear attenuation coefficient describes the fraction of a beam of x-rays that is absorbed or scattered per unit thickness of the medium.

The image of the section irradiated by x-ray is reconstructed from a large number of measurements of attenuation coefficient. It gathers together all the data coming from the elementary volumes of material through the detectors.

CT Image Composition

Because CT scan image is basically a digital image, it consists of a square matrix of elements (pixel), each of which represents a volume element (voxel) of the tissue of the examined part of the patient. It is typically composed of 512 rows, each of 512 pixels, which means that a square matrix of $512 \times 512=262144$ pixels (one for each voxel).

The Hounsfield Scale

Named for the Nobel Prize-winning inventor of CT scan, the British physicist Godfrey N. Hounsfield, The Hounsfield unit (HU) scale transforms the linear attenuation coefficient measurement where:

  • ‚Ä¢Distilled water at standard pressure and temperature (STP) is defined as zero Hounsfield units (HU)
  • ‚Ä¢Air at (STP) is defined as -1000 HU
  • ‚Ä¢Dense materials such as bone approach +1000 HU

CT Windows

Although there are more than 2,000 different Hounsfield values, the monitor can display only 256 shades of gray, and the human eye cannot appreciate more than 40. To overcome these inherent limitations, a gray scale is used in image display.

üß≤ Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)

It is the medical imaging that applies a magnetic field and radio wave pulses to build up images of the internal anatomical structure and give information about the physiological processes of the human body.

MRI does not involve the use of ionizing radiation; it is mainly based on the distribution of hydrogen nuclei within different types of body tissue to produces extremely detailed picture of the body soft tissue.

MRI Scanner Components

  • 1.Large magnet that produces a very strong magnetic field
  • 2.Radiofrequency coils which produce and receive RF signals
  • 3.Gradient coils for creating variable fields (x, y, z)
  • 4.Patient Table that slides into the MRI machine
  • 5.Computer System for image processing and analysis

Basic Concept of MRI Imaging

In normal circumstances, hydrogen protons of the human body spin on their own axis in a manner very similar to the planet earth, with a north-south pole. In this respect hydrogen protons behave like small bar magnets distributed in the body with their axes randomly aligned.

MRI Process Steps

  1. 1. The body is placed in a uniform magnetic field (0.5 to 1.5 Tesla), causing hydrogen protons to align and create a magnetic vector.
  2. 2. A radio-frequency (RF) pulse is applied perpendicular to the magnetic field, causing protons to tilt perpendicular to the field direction.
  3. 3. When the RF pulse stops, the nuclei realign themselves and emit their own RF signal (free-induction decay response).

MRI Image Types

  • ‚Ä¢ T1 weighted MR Image: Displays tissue contrast based on longitudinal relaxation time. Fats appear bright, fluids appear dark.
  • ‚Ä¢ T2 weighted MR Image: Based on transverse relaxation time. Both fats and fluids appear bright, but fluids are brighter.
  • ‚Ä¢ Proton density weighted MR image: Based on proton density of tissue. Fluids appear white but darker than in T2, fats appear bright but darker than in T1.
