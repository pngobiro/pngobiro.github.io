Chapter 9: Upper Limb Bones

Upper Limb Divisions and Bones

Upper limb is composed of three anatomical divisions:

  1. Arm: extends between the shoulder and elbow joints
  2. Forearm: extends between the elbow and wrist joints
  3. Hand: located distal to the wrist joint

Each upper limb is composed of 30 bones as follows:

  • One bone in the arm known as the Humerus
  • Forearm composed of two bones: Radius and Ulna
  • Hand composed of 27 bones:
    • 8 Carpals
    • 5 Metacarpals
    • 14 Phalanges

The Humerus

The humerus is the bone of the arm, extending between the scapula proximally and the radius and ulna distally. The proximal end of the humerus consists of:

  • Head
  • Anatomical neck
  • Surgical neck
  • Greater and lesser tubercles

The head is a rounded, smooth area that faces medially and articulates with the glenoid cavity of the scapula to form the shoulder joint (Glenohumeral joint). The anatomical neck is a smooth groove that surrounds the articular margin of the smooth area of the head.

The Greater tubercle is an expanded bony area located on the lateral side of the proximal humerus. The Lesser tubercle is located on the anterior aspect of the proximal humerus. There is a narrow groove passing between the greater and lesser tubercles known as The intertubercular groove or (bicipital groove) which provides passage for the biceps brachii muscle tendon.

The surgical neck is located at the expanded region at the base of the proximal end of the humerus; it represents the connection between the head and the shaft of the humerus, and is a common site of fractures.

The shaft of the humerus is almost triangular in cut section with 3 surfaces:

  • The anteromedial surface
  • The anterolateral surface (separated from anteromedial by a line drawn as a continuation of the crest of the greater tubercle)
  • The posterior surface (located between the medial and lateral borders)

There is a roughened, V-shaped area at the lateral side in the midshaft of the humerus known as the deltoid tuberosity; it is the site of attachment for the deltoid muscle. There is a shallow oblique spiral groove found on the posterior surface of the shaft of the humerus known as the radial sulcus; it is the groove for the passage of the radial nerve and deep vessels.

The distal end of the humerus features:

  • Medial and lateral epicondyles (with the medial being larger)
  • Lateral and medial supracondylar ridges
  • The trochlea (articulates with the ulna)
  • The capitulum (articulates with the radius)

Radius and Ulna

Radius and ulna are the bones of the forearm. They extend from the elbow proximally to the wrist distally.

The Radius

It is the lateral of the two bones of the forearm; it extends from the lateral side of the elbow to the thumb side of the wrist and runs parallel to the ulna. Features include:

  • The head: a disc-shaped structure that articulates with the capitulum of the humerus
  • The neck: a narrow region immediately distal to the radial head
  • Radial tuberosity: an oval-shaped, bony protuberance for muscular attachment
  • Interosseous border: for attachment of interosseous membrane
  • Distal end: articulates with carpal bones and has the styloid process

The Ulna

It is the medial bone of the forearm, extending parallel to the radius. Notable features include:

  • Trochlear notch: wrench-shaped area that articulates with the trochlea of the humerus
  • Coronoid process: forms the lower margin of the trochlear notch
  • Olecranon process: forms the upper margin of the trochlear notch
  • Ulnar tuberosity: attachment area for brachialis muscle
  • Interosseous border: for membrane attachment
  • Head and styloid process at the distal end

The Carpal Bones

The carpal bones are a series of eight small bones located at the wrist and base of the hand. They are arranged in two rows, a proximal row and a distal row. Each row is composed of four carpal bones attached to each other by ligaments to form one unit.

Proximal Row Bones

  1. Scaphoid: boat-shaped, largest carpal bone on radial side
  2. Lunate: crescent-shaped, most frequently dislocated
  3. Triquetrum: pyramidal shaped with three corners
  4. Pisiform: pea-shaped, smallest carpal bone

Distal Row Bones

  1. Trapezium: four-sided with multiple articulation surfaces
  2. Trapezoid: wedge-shaped, smallest in distal row
  3. Capitate: head-shaped, largest carpal bone
  4. Hamate: wedge-shaped with hook-like process

Metacarpal Bones

Metacarpal bones lie between the carpal bones of the wrist and the bones of the fingers and thumb. Each metacarpal bone is composed of a base, shaft, and head. They articulate proximally with the distal row of carpal bones and distally with the proximal phalanges.

Metacarpal bones are numbered in a lateral to medial direction and correspond to the fingers:

  1. First Metacarpal proximal to the Thumb
  2. Second Metacarpal proximal to the Index Finger
  3. Third Metacarpal proximal to the Middle Finger
  4. Fourth Metacarpal proximal to the Ring Finger
  5. Fifth Metacarpal proximal to the Little Finger

Upper limb Phalanges

Upper limb phalanges are the bones of fingers and thumb, totaling 14 bones:

  • The thumb has two phalanges (proximal and distal)
  • The other digits have three phalanges (proximal, middle, and distal)

The phalanges articulate with each other through interphalangeal joints, while the proximal phalanges articulate with the metatarsal bones through metatarsophalangeal joints. The bone of each phalanx consists of:

  • Head at the distal end
  • Shaft
  • Base at the proximal end
