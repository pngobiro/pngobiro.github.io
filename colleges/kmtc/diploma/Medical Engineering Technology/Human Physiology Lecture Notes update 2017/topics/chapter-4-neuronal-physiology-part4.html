Chapter 4: Neuronal Physiology (Final Part)

üß† Neurons

Three Basic Parts:

  1. Cell Body
    • Houses nucleus and organelles
    • Receives signals from other cells
    • Contains receptors for chemical messengers
  2. Dendrites
    • Projections from cell body
    • Increase surface area for receiving signals
  3. Axon
    • Single elongated projection
    • Conducts action potentials away from cell body
    • May have collaterals (side branches)
    • Axon hillock generates action potentials
    • Ends in axon terminals releasing chemical messengers
    • Length can vary from less than a mm to more than a meter

‚è±Ô∏è Refractory Period

Purpose and Types:

  • Ensures action potentials propagated in only one direction
  • Types:
    1. Absolute Refractory Period
      • No amount of stimulation will induce another AP
      • Time between $\mathrm{Na}^{+}$ gates opening and returning to "ready" state
    2. Relative Refractory Period
      • Needs stronger than usual stimulation
      • Only some $\mathrm{Na}^{+}$ gates ready
      • Some $\mathrm{K}^{+}$ gates still open
  • Duration: Lasts for one to a few milliseconds

üéØ All-or-None Law

  • A membrane responds with a maximal AP or it doesn't respond at all
  • Stimulus must reach threshold to initiate AP
  • Nervous system differentiates stimulus strength by frequency of APs
    • Stronger stimuli ‚Üí more APs/second
