Goal: Understand the concept of facility and community based maternal and child health care

  • ‚Ä¢Describe MCH and family planning services
  • ‚Ä¢Describe community base approach to MCT and FP

Introduction

  • ‚Ä¢Maternal health is the health of women during pregnancy, childbirth, and the postpartum period.
  • ‚Ä¢It encompasses the health care dimensions of family planning, preconception, prenatal, and postnatal care in order to reduce maternal morbidity and mortality.
  • ‚Ä¢Preconception care can include:
  • ‚Ä¢Education,
  • ‚Ä¢Health promotion,
  • ‚Ä¢Screening
  • ‚Ä¢and other interventions among women of reproductive age to reduce risk factors that might affect future pregnancies.
  • ‚Ä¢Postnatal care issues include:
  • ‚Ä¢Recovery from childbirth,
  • ‚Ä¢Concerns about newborn care,
  • ‚Ä¢Nutrition,
  • ‚Ä¢Breastfeeding,
  • ‚Ä¢Family planning
  • ‚Ä¢The goal of prenatal care is to:
  • ‚Ä¢Detect any potential complications of pregnancy early,
  • ‚Ä¢Prevent them if possible,
  • ‚Ä¢Direct the woman to appropriate specialist medical services as appropriate.
  • ‚Ä¢Primary areas of focus in MCH include:
  • ‚Ä¢Increasing healthy birth outcomes;
  • ‚Ä¢Promoting and assuring comprehensive primary care for children, including children with special health care needs;
  • ‚Ä¢Promoting healthy lifestyles among school-age youth, ages 6-21, including children with special health care needs;
  • ‚Ä¢Assuring access to safe, healthy child care up to age six, including children with special health care needs;
  • ‚Ä¢Preconception, pregnancy testing and planning.
  • ‚Ä¢In Africa, one out of 210 mothers dies during pregnancy or delivery.
  • ‚Ä¢Most maternal deaths are due to causes directly related to:
  • ‚Ä¢Pregnancy
  • ‚Ä¢Childbirth,
  • ‚Ä¢Unsafe abortion
  • ‚Ä¢Obstetric complications such as:
  • ‚Ä¢Severe bleeding
  • ‚Ä¢Infection
  • ‚Ä¢Hypertensive disorders
  • ‚Ä¢Obstructed labor
  • ‚Ä¢Other indirect cause like:
  • ‚Ä¢Relatively low rate of institutional deliveries,
  • ‚Ä¢Due to transport problems
  • ‚Ä¢Lack of infrastructure,
  • ‚Ä¢Due to cultural prejudices and resistance against giving birth outside the family circle
  • ‚Ä¢The fifth Millennium Development Goal (MDG) aims to reduce the maternal mortality ratio by $75\%$ between 1990 and 2015.
  • ‚Ä¢In Kenya, maternal mortality remains high at 488 maternal deaths per 100,000 live births.
  • ‚Ä¢The MCH/FP clinic is founded on quality and afford-able services that advocate for safe motherhood, proper child development, and a healthy family.

‚öïÔ∏èMCH Services

The services offered in MCH clinic are as follows:

  • ‚Ä¢Focused antenatal care.
  • ‚Ä¢Targeted post natal care.
  • ‚Ä¢Providing all family planning methods: short term, long term, permanent i.e. vasectomy & tubal ligation.
  • ‚Ä¢Screening of Breast and Cervical cancer in all women of reproductive ages.
  • ‚Ä¢Giving immunization i.e. BCG, Pentavalent, Polio, Measles.
  • ‚Ä¢Growth monitoring for children under the age of five.
  • ‚Ä¢Counseling and testing of antenatal mothers and partners on PMTCT - eMTC
  • ‚Ä¢Create awareness through Micro teaching on various topics i.e. Nutrition, Breast feeding, Weaning etc.

üíâImmunization

  • ‚Ä¢The basic principle of immunization is to administer into a healthy person a vaccine that will prevent that person from getting a certain disease.
  • ‚Ä¢Vaccines may be of:
  • ‚Ä¢Live attenuated: (e.g. Rubella, OPV, Measles and BCG),
  • ‚Ä¢Inactivated or killed (e.g. Hib, IPV)
  • ‚Ä¢Micro-organisms
  • ‚Ä¢Detoxified toxins (e.g. Tetanus)
  • ‚Ä¢Generally, several vaccines can be given at the same time. This is important since you do not know when you will see the child again.
  • ‚Ä¢BCG, OPV, DPT-HeB-Hib and Measles vaccines can be given simultaneously if the child is of the appropriate age and has not received the early immunizations.
  • ‚Ä¢A critically ill child needing hospital admission must be given the appropriate vaccines upon recovery
  • ‚Ä¢Remember:
  • ‚Ä¢That a slight fever and/or other minor illness should not prevent you from immunizing a child
  • ‚Ä¢To inform mothers/child-caretakers about possible side effects of each of the given vaccines
  • ‚Ä¢To record all vaccinations on tally sheets and on the Child-Health immunization cards and instruct the mothers always to bring the cards along with them when taking children to a health facility
  • ‚Ä¢To instruct mothers to return the child for the next immunization date as indicated on the card
  • ‚Ä¢That vaccines are easily destroyed by heat and rendered ineffective
  • ‚Ä¢To handle the disposal of used sharp syringes appropriately
  • ‚Ä¢To ensure appropriate cold storage of the vaccines and follow the recommended cold- chain instructions for each of the vaccines carefully.

Facts about the common vaccine-preventable diseases

Whooping cough (Pertussis):

  • ‚Ä¢A communicable disease.
  • ‚Ä¢Spread by droplets.
  • ‚Ä¢Symptoms include:
  • ‚Ä¢Severe cough followed by a whoop and vomiting, Leads to malnutrition, Can cause death, Severe in under 1 year old.

Diphtheria:

  • ‚Ä¢An infectious disease.
  • ‚Ä¢Spread by droplets.
  • ‚Ä¢Symptoms include: Difficulty in breathing, Swallowing, Enlarged neck.
  • ‚Ä¢Very severe when it occurs.

Tetanus: [see tetanus]:

  • ‚Ä¢A clinical syndrome involving primarily the central nervous system and resulting from the tetanus toxins.
  • ‚Ä¢Enters through open wounds, cuts and umbilical stump.
  • ‚Ä¢Symptoms include: Stiffness, Locked jaw, Inability to suckle and muscle spasms.
  • ‚Ä¢Has a very high mortality (>50%).
  • ‚Ä¢Immunizing pregnant mothers ensures protection of her new born baby.

Measles (Rubeola):

  • ‚Ä¢Highly infectious killer disease.
  • ‚Ä¢Symptoms include: Rash, Fever, Cough, Red eyes;
  • ‚Ä¢Is associated with: blindness, malnutrition, deafness, pneumonia and death.

Poliomyelitis (Infantile Paralysis):

  • ‚Ä¢An acute communicable disease.
  • ‚Ä¢Spread by droplets and oro-faecal contamination.
  • ‚Ä¢Symptoms include:
  • ‚Ä¢Pain and flaccid paralysis in limbs, fever, vomiting; can lead to permanent deformity, and can cause death.

Tuberculosis:

  • ‚Ä¢A communicable disease.
  • ‚Ä¢Spread by droplet.
  • ‚Ä¢Symptoms include: fever, wasting, deep chesty cough, Night sweats, May have lymphadenopathy.
  • ‚Ä¢Leads to lowered resistance to other diseases and may be fatal.

Hepatitis B:

  • ‚Ä¢Is a highly infectious disease.
  • ‚Ä¢Transmitted mainly by:
  • ‚Ä¢Parenteral route,
  • ‚Ä¢From person to person
  • ‚Ä¢By close contact
  • ‚Ä¢Through exchange of body fluids such as:
  • ‚Ä¢Saliva, Secretions from open wounds, Blood, Vaginal secretions and semen.
  • ‚Ä¢Transmission between children is common, since they are often more infectious than adults.
  • ‚Ä¢Transmission from carrier mothers can occur in up to $80\%$ of babies during the perinatal period, - Infection may be transmitted either:
  • ‚Ä¢Vertically (transplacentally from mother to unborn baby)
  • ‚Ä¢Horizontally by close contact.

HIV/AIDS INFECTION AND IMMUNIZATION

Asymptomatic children infected with HIV should receive all standard Kenya Expanded Programme on Immunization (KEPI) vaccines. Non-immunized children with symptomatic HIV infection should receive all standard vaccines except BCG.

Side-effects and adverse reactions to vaccinations

Range from mild to severe for various vaccines.

BCG Vaccine

These include injection abscess, regional or wide-spread lymphadenitis, osteomyelitis and disseminated BCG infection. These should be treated with anti-tuberculosis drugs [see 12.8. TB]

Oral Polio Vaccine

Adverse reactions rarely occur.

Measles Vaccine

Adverse reactions include fever, mild rash and rarely convulsions, encephalitis and sub-acute schlerosing pan-encephalitis (SSPE).

DPT (Diphtheria, Pertussis, Tetanus)

Most adverse reactions are attributed to the pertussis component. Minor reactions include, pain at the site of injection and fever. Major reactions are persistent crying, high pitched cry, excessive somnolescence, convulsions, encephalopathy and coma.

Recombinant DNA Hepatitis B Vaccine

Side effects include pain, fever and swelling at the site of injection.
