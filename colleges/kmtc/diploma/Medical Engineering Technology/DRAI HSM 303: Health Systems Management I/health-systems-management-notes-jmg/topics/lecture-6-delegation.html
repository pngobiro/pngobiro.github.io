LECTURE 6: DELEGATION & PROBLEM SOLVING

ü§ù DELEGATION. DEF:

  • ‚Ä¢Passing down of authority from a superior to a subordinate.
  • ‚Ä¢Process of entrusting authority and responsibility from a manager or a supervisor to a subordinate.
  • ‚Ä¢The one to whom authority is delegated becomes responsible to the superior for doing the job, but the superior remains responsible for getting the job done.
  • ‚Ä¢The best way for a manager to effectively concentrate on their responsibilities is by unloading as much of their work as possible.
  • ‚Ä¢Delegation can take place at the individual or organizational level.

üìä Delegation Levels

1. Organizational level.

  • ‚Ä¢The structure of the organization as depicted in the organization chart is itself a reserve of delegation.
  • ‚Ä¢It relates to the position of decision-making in the organization.
  • ‚Ä¢It involves passing down of authority and responsibility to various levels throughout the organization with consideration of centralization, decentralization, provincial levels, district levels, etc., to be established on the basis of tasks or elements of function.

2. Individual level.

  • ‚Ä¢It will involve the various activities that have to be undertaken.
  • ‚Ä¢Must be distributed among individual members of the workforce by entrusting authority and responsibility to others.
  • ‚Ä¢Delegation is founded on the concept of:
    • -Authority.
    • -Responsibility.
    • -Accountability.

Authority.

  • ‚Ä¢The right to take action or make decisions that the manager would otherwise have done.
  • ‚Ä¢Legitimizes the exercise of power within the structures and rules of the organization.
  • ‚Ä¢Enables the subordinate to issue valued instructions for others to follow.

Responsibility.

  • ‚Ä¢Work assigned to a position.
  • ‚Ä¢Involves an obligation by the subordinate to perform certain duties or make certain decisions and having to accept the possible reprimand from the manager or the superior for unsatisfactory performance.

Accountability.

  • ‚Ä¢Any means of ensuring that the person who is supposed to do a task actually performs it and does so correctly.
  • ‚Ä¢It requires certain conditions to be established, i.e.,
    • -Must be thoroughly and clearly understood.
    • -The person must be qualified and capable of fulfilling the obligation.
    • -Sufficient authority to accomplish the task must be delegated.
  • ‚Ä¢You cannot delegate authority without responsibility and vice versa.

‚úÖ EFFECTIVE DELEGATION PROCESS.

  1. 1.Grant appropriate authority
    - Responsibility should not be less than the authority delegated.
    - Enough authority should always be delegated to achieve desired results.
  2. 2.Consider the capabilities of the subordinate.
    - Authority should be delegated to those who are competent and willing to accept delegation.
    - Consider the job to be done, knowledge, experience, intelligence, and limitations of the person(s).
  3. 3.Make sure the authority is clearly stated.
    - Should be clearly defined not only to the subordinate but to all the others.
    - Everyone should know who is in charge and where authority resides.
  4. 4.Modify the authority whenever necessary.
    - Maintain a flexible attitude about what kind of and how much authority to delegate.
    - Because the environment of business is dynamic, authority relationships may need to be altered from time to time.
    - Authority is revocable-it can be modified by increasing it, decreasing it, or withdrawing it altogether.
  5. 5.Follow unity of command and chain of command.
    - There should be clear reporting arrangements.
    - Each individual reports to one supervisor.
    - Follow the chain of command.
  6. 6.Develop willingness to delegate.
    - No organization can function without delegation.
    - Some obstacles are psychological, e.g., lack of confidence in the staff, fear of losing control, etc.
    - The manager should be willing to let go.
  7. 7.Create a supportive climate.
    - Moral and material support should be available, i.e., adequate resources (staff, equipment, expenses, time).
    - Advice and encouragement.
  8. 8.Develop an effective communication system.
    - Flow of information between superiors and subordinates.
    - Enables superiors to give clear instructions and subordinates to seek clarification and guidance.
  9. 9.Establish an effective control system.
    - To ensure authority delegated is properly used.
    - Set the standards of performance.
    - Evaluate performance.
  10. 10.Give incentives.
    - Financial and non-financial incentives-reward for successful assumption of authority.
  11. 11.Define the results expected.
    - Avoid ambiguous instructions.
    - Clearly define expectations.

üëç RULES OF THUMB OF DELEGATING

Rules for the manager

  1. 1.Know the employees' strengths and weaknesses.
  2. 2.State tasks clearly-what is to be done, but not how to do it.
  3. 3.Delegate a little at a time-break up large projects.
  4. 4.Ask for feedback-to ensure the employee understands.
  5. 5.Grant enough authority to do the job.
  6. 6.Let go and trust the employee to do the job.
  7. 7.Provide support only after the employee has had a chance to work out problems.
  8. 8.Do not expect the job to be done as you would do it.
  9. 9.Evaluate-Is it acceptable or perfect?
  10. 10.Provide rewards consistent with the result and level of responsibility.

Rules for the subordinate.

  1. 1.Recognize assignment and opportunities.
  2. 2.Be realistic about what you are able to do.
  3. 3.Ask for clarification.
  4. 4.Determine the resources available before accepting the assignment.
  5. 5.Report progress periodically.
  6. 6.Request support or advice if needed.
  7. 7.Be creative in solving problems.
  8. 8.Use feedback.

‚öñÔ∏è Advantages & Barriers

Advantages of delegation.

  1. 1.Extra time for supervisors or managers.
  2. 2.Improves morale and productivity.
  3. 3.A means of training to assess a person's likely performance at a higher level of authority and responsibility.
  4. 4.Recognition for supervisor and subordinate.
  5. 5.Decrease in stress.
  6. 6.Planning the future, rather than organizing the present.

Barriers to effective delegation.

  • ‚Ä¢Managers' perception of subordinate and vice versa.

Manager may fear that the subordinate is not capable or may do too good a job and show the manager in bad light.

Supervisors' reasons for avoiding delegation.

  1. a)Lack of trust.
  2. b)Loss of control.
  3. c)Shared recognition, i.e., "someone else may get the credit I deserve."
  4. d)Fear for his or her position. This document is available on
  5. e)Lack of time.
  6. f)Lack of incentives.

Subordinates' reasons for avoiding delegation.

  1. a)They find it easier to ask the boss to make decisions.
  2. b)Fear of criticism.
  3. c)Believe they lack the necessary information and resources to do the job.
  4. d)Subordinate may already have too much to do.
  5. e)Lack of confidence.
  6. f)Lack of incentives.

‚öôÔ∏è Factors affecting delegation.

  1. 1.Nature of the task:
    - Is it appropriate for the employee?
  2. 2.Organizational culture:
    - Does the employee know organizational goals and policies?
    - Do they encourage delegation?
  3. 3.Experience of the employee:
    - How familiar is the employee with the job?
  4. 4.Supervisors' ability:
    - Capability of selecting and motivating employees.
  5. 5.Employees' knowledge of the job.
  6. 6.Willingness of the employee.

ü™ú Stages in systematic approach to delegation.

  1. 1.Clarification of policy and procedures.
    - Establish and define policies and procedures to provide a framework for the exercise of authority and the acceptance of responsibility.
    - Managers must be clear.
    - Chain of command with effective communication and coordination between various levels of authority within the organizational structure.
  2. 2.Agreement on terms of reference.
    - Identify and discuss areas of work the subordinate is to be responsible for.
    - Subordinate should understand reasons for delegation, the nature of work, and limitations imposed.
    - This involves the acceptance theory:
    "Although the authority gives people power to act officially within the scope of their delegation, this power becomes somewhat meaningless unless those affected accept it and respond to it. In most cases, when delegation is made, a subordinate is left free to choose a response within a certain range of behavior. But even then an employee still has the choice of doing it or not doing it and taking the consequences. Therefore, the subordinates control the response to authority. Managers cannot afford to overlook the human part when they use authority."
  3. 3.Briefing, guidance, and training.
    - Once delegation is accepted, give guidance, briefing, and training.
    - Advice on where and to whom to seek help or advice.
  4. 4.Review and monitoring.
    - Set time limits for delegation.
    - Give target dates for the completion of tasks.
    - Set the performance standards.
  5. 5.Freedom of action.
    - Leave the subordinate alone to get on with the job.

üß© PROBLEM SOLVING

  • ‚Ä¢A scientific process provides a theoretical framework utilized in attaining solutions to simple or complex problems.
  • ‚Ä¢It is a systematic procedure with a moderate systematic progression.
  • ‚Ä¢You need to gather information about the problem and then identify and state the problem.
  • ‚Ä¢Then start discussing the possible solutions to implement.
  • ‚Ä¢The problem-solving process can be used by anybody to solve day-to-day problems. It requires experience in application.
  • ‚Ä¢The process has 8 phases:
    1. 1.Define the problem.
    2. 2.Gather the information.
    3. 3.Analyze the information.
    4. 4.Develop the solutions.
    5. 5.Consider the consequences.
    6. 6.Make the decisions.
    7. 7.Implement decisions.
    8. 8.Evaluate the solution.
