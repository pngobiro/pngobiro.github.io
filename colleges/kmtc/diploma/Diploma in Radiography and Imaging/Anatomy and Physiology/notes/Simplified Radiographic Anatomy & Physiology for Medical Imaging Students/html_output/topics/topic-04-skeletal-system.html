The skeletal system is the strong internal framework of the human body around which other body structures are built (Figure 4.1).

Components of skeletal system:

The skeletal system is mainly composed of bones, cartilage, joints, tendons and ligaments.

Functions of the skeletal system:

The skeletal system performs the following functions for the human body:

  1. It supports the body.
  2. It facilitates body movement.
  3. It protects the internal organs.
  4. It produces red blood cells.
  5. It stores and releases minerals and fats.

Divisions of skeletal system:

The Skeletal System is mainly subdivided into two main subdivisions:

  1. The axial skeleton.
  2. The appendicular skeleton.

ü¶¥ The Axial Skeleton

The axial skeleton forms the central axis of the body. It includes the bones of the following parts (Figure 4.2):

  1. The Skull bones.
  2. The Ossicles of the middle ear.
  3. The Hyoid bone.
  4. The Thoracic cage.
  5. The Sternum.
  6. The Vertebral Column.

Functions of the Axial Skeleton:

it has two main functions:

  1. It supports and protects soft tissue organs, such as the brain and the lung.
  2. It creates a surface for the attachment of muscles for movement.

The Axial Skeleton consists of 80 bones:

  • The Skull; formed of 22 bones (Figure 4.4):
    - One Frontal bone
    - Two Parietal bones
    - Two Temporal bones
    - One Occipital bone
    - One Ethmoid bone
    - One Sphenoid bone
    - One Mandible
    - Two Maxillae (singular: maxilla)
    - One Vomer
    - Two Palatine bones
    - Two Nasal bones
    - Two Zygomatic bones
    - Two Nasal conchae (singular: concha)
    - Two Lacrimal bones
  • One Hyoid bone.
  • The middle ear contains 6 ossicles ( 3 on each side) (Figure 4.5):
    - Malleus
    - Incus
    - Stapes
  • The vertebral column consists of 26 bones; 24 vertebrae, 1 sacrum and 1 coccyx (Figure 4.6).
  • The thoracic cage consists of 25 bones; 12 pairs of ribs, and one sternum (Figure 4.7).

üí™ The Appendicular Skeleton

The appendicular skeleton forms the peripheral part of the skeletal system. It includes the bones of the following parts (Figure 4.3):

  1. The upper limb bones.
  2. The shoulder girdle that attaches the upper limb to the axial skeleton.
  3. The lower limb bones.
  4. The pelvic girdle that attaches the lower limb to the axial skeleton.

Function of the Appendicular Skeleton: The main function of the appendicular skeleton is locomotion beside grasping and manipulation of objects.

The Appendicular Skeleton consists of 126 bones (Figure 4.8):

  • The Upper limb consist of 60 bones:
    - Two Humerus
    - Two Radius
    - Two Ulna
    - Sixteen Carpal bones
    - Ten Metacarpal bones
    - Twenty Eight Phalanges
  • The Shoulder (Pectoral) Girdle consist of 4 bones:
    - Two Clavicles
    - Two Scapulae
  • The Lower limb consist of 60 bones:
    - Two Femur
    - Two Patella
    - Two Tibia
    - Two Fibula
    - Fourteen Tarsal bones
    - Ten Metatarsal bones
    - Twenty Eight Phalanges
  • The Pelvic Girdle consists of 2 hip bones: each hip bone is composed of ilium, ischium and pubic bone.

üß© Components of Skeletal System

The skeletal system is composed of:

  1. Bones: form the rigid part of the skeletal system, they support and protect the body and its organs.
  2. Cartilage: are of intermediate firmness to provide flexible support for certain structures in the human body.
  3. Joints: are the areas where two or more bones meet, they are mobile, allowing the bones to move.
  4. Tendons: are bands of fibrous connective tissue that connects muscles to bones.
  5. Ligaments: are band of connective tissue that connects bones to another bones.
  6. Fascia: are sheets of connective tissue join muscles together, they stabilize, enclose, and separate muscles.

ü¶¥ 1. Bones

Bones are the hard and dense connective tissues that form most of the skeleton. They represent the structural and supportive part of the human body.

Types of Bone:

  1. Long Bones.
  2. Short Bones.
  3. Flat Bones.
  4. Irregular Bones.
  5. Sesamoid Bones.

Long Bones: they are cylindrical in shape, being longer than they are wide. Long bones are found in the arms (humerus, ulna, radius) and legs (femur, tibia, fibula), as well as in the fingers (metacarpals, phalanges) and toes (metatarsals, phalanges). Long bones function as levers; they move due to muscular contraction (Figure.4.9 & 4.10).

Short Bones: they are cube-like in shape, being approximately equal in length, width, and thickness. The only short bones in the human skeleton are in the carpals of the wrists and the tarsals of the ankles. Short bones provide stability and support as well as some limited motion (Figures.4.11 & 4.12).

Flat Bones: they are flat, thin and mostly curved bones such as the skull, flat bones also include scapula, sternum and ribs. Flat bones serve as points of attachment for muscles and often protect internal organs (Figures.4.13 & 4.14).

Irregular Bones: these bones are complex in shapes, like the vertebrae and many facial bones, particularly the ones containing sinuses. Irregular bones protect internal components such vertebrae which support the spinal cord and protect it from compressive force (Figures 4.15 & 4.16).

Sesamoid Bones: as the name suggests, they have a shape of a sesame seed, they are small and rounded, sesamoid bones are found in tendons associated with the feet, hands, and knees; they protect tendons by helping them overcome compressive forces, such as the patella in the knee joint (Figures 4.17 & 4.18).

Anatomical parts of long bone:

A long bone is composed of three parts:

  1. The diaphysis: represents the medial part of the bone.
  2. The epiphysis: represents the distal end of the bone.
  3. The metaphysis: represents the portion between the diaphysis and the epiphysis (Figure.4.19).
  1. The diaphysis: is the tubular shaft that runs between the proximal and distal ends of the bone. It contains:
    1) The medullary cavity: it is the hollow region in the diaphysis.
    2) The compact bone: it is the wall of the diaphysis, mainly composed of dense and hard bone.
    3) The Periosteum: it is the tough fibrous layer that envelopes the bone.
    4) The Endosteum: it is the inner layer that lines the medullary cavity (Figure.4.20).
  1. The epiphysis: is the wider section at each end of the bone, it is composed of:
    1) Spongy bone: fills the inner part of epiphysis.
    2) Compact bone: form the outer layer of epiphyses.
    3) Articular cartilage: covers the articular surface of epiphysis (Figure 4.21).
  1. The Metaphysis: is the narrow portion of a long bone between the epiphysis and the diaphysis. It contains the growth plate, for growth during childhood; it is rich in blood supply which makes it prone to infection in childhood leading to osteomyelitis (Figure 4.22).

Structural components of the Bone:

basically any bone is composed of the following four components:

  1. Periosteum: is a dense fibrous layer that covers the outer surface of all bones, except at the joints of long bones. It contains nerve fibers that cause pain when the tissue is damaged. It also contains many blood vessels, which penetrate the bone to supply the bone cells. The periosteum represents the layer where ligaments and tendons attach to the bone.
  2. Compact (Cortical) bone: Also known as (Cortical) bone. It is the strongest and densest part of the bone; it is found under the periosteum and forms the hard outer shell around spongy bone in the diaphysis of long bones, where it provides support and protection. Its main function is to provide support and protection. It is the primary component of the long bones of the arm and leg and other bones, where its greater strength and rigidity are needed, it contains many tiny passages for blood vessels and nerves and houses the cells that repair and maintain bones. Compact bone represents the main component of the shaft of long bones covered with the periosteum, where it provides strength and rigidity for long bones (Figure 4.23).
  3. Spongy (Cancellous) Bone: also known as the (Cancellous) bone. It is made up of spongy, porous, bone tissue that is filled with red bone marrow. It is very important for producing blood cells. It is found in the ends of long bones and in the bones of the pelvis, ribs, vertebrae, and skull (Figure 4.23).
  4. Endosteum: is a thin vascular membrane of connective tissue that lines the inner surface of the bony tissue that forms the medullary cavity of long bones.
  5. The medullary cavity: is the central cavity of bone shafts (Diaphysis) it contains:
    1- Red bone marrow: where new blood cells are produced.
    2- Yellow bone marrow: where adipose tissue is stored.

Bone Development:

At 3 month fetal life an early skeleton-like framework composed of cartilage and connective tissue membrane starts to develop. As the fetus matures, the cartilage and connective tissue change into bone. The formation of bones is called (Ossification), it occurs in different ways in flat and long bones.
Ossification: is the process of new bone formation by laying down new bone material by cells called osteoblasts.
There are two processes resulting in the formation of normal, healthy bone tissue: -

  1. Intramembranous ossification is the process of bone development from fibrous membranes. It is involved in the formation of the flat bones.
  2. Endochondral ossification is the process of bone development from hyaline cartilage. It is involved in the formation of long bones.

Intramembranous Ossification:

Intramembranous ossification is one of the two essential processes of bone formation during fetal development it involves replacement of thin membrane of connective tissue with new bone, it is mainly involved in the formation of the flat bones of the skull, the mandible, and the clavicles. Intramembranous ossification is also an essential process during the natural healing of bone fractures and the rudimentary formation of the bones of the head.

Steps of Intramembranous ossification:

  1. Development of the Ossification Center: a group of mesenchymal cells enlarge and become rounded to differentiate into osteoblasts, these osteoblasts cluster together to and secrete an extracellular matrix known as osteoid to form an ossification center (Figure 4.26).
  1. Calcification of osteoid: clustered osteoblasts secrete calcium and other minerals into the spaces between the thin connective tissue membranes and around the framework of collagen, leading to the calcification of the osteoid. Trapped osteoblasts within the calcifying osteoid become known as osteocytes (Figure 4.27).
  1. New bone formation and Periosteum Development: The non-mineralized portion of the osteoid forms the spongy bone around blood vessels, the superficial part of spongy bone remodels into a thin layer of compact bone, the matrix differentiates into red bone marrow. Mesenchymal cells at periphery condense to form periosteum (Figure 4.28 & 4.29).

Endochondral Ossification:

Endochondral ossification is the second essential processes of bone formation during fetal development. It involves the replacement of hyaline cartilage with bony tissue. It occurs in most of the bones of the skeleton. It is essential during the growth of the length of long bones, and the natural healing of bone fractures.

Steps of Endochondral ossification:

  1. Development of the Fetal Cartilage Model: Endochondral ossification begins when mesenchyme cells aggregate and differentiate into Chondrocytes which secrete a matrix to form hyaline cartilage model, this cartilage model will be surrounded by a layer of dense irregular connective tissue known as perichondrium (Figure 4.30).
  1. Periosteum and calcified cartilage formation: The perichondrium of the diaphysis begins to calcify and becomes the periosteum, internally, chondrocytes in the diaphysis begin to accumulate glycogen, undergo hypertrophy and produce alkaline phosphatase and the surrounding matrix begins to calcify leading to calcified cartilage formation(Figure 4.31).
  1. Primary ossification center formation: Blood vessels and osteoblasts from periosteum begin to invade the calcified cartilage leading to primary ossification center formation in the diaphysis; Osteoblasts begin to lay down bone matrix, leading to cancellous bone formation.
  2. Secondary Ossification Center formation: Chondrocytes in epiphyses undergo hypertrophy, the cartilage matrix compressed, calcifies, and dies, which open up spaces between spicules of calcified cartilage, blood vessels enter spaces in epiphyses and secondary ossification centers develop, a medullary cavity begins to form in the center of the diaphysis. (Figure 4.32).
  1. Epiphyseal plate articular cartilage formation: The original cartilage model becomes almost completely ossified. Unossified cartilage becomes the epiphyseal plate and the articular cartilage.
  2. Mature bone formation: Epiphyseal plates gradually ossify and become the epiphyseal lines, all the cartilage become bone except the articular cartilage (Figure 4.33).

üß¨ 2. Cartilage

Cartilage is an elastic connective tissue, composed of closely packed collagenous fibers in a rubbery gelatinous ground substance known as chondrin. Cartilaginous material is of intermediate firmness, it is neither as hard as bones nor as soft as tendons to provide flexible support for certain structures in the human body.

Types of cartilage:

  1. Hyaline Cartilage: It is the most common type of cartilage; its main function is to resist compressive stress, support and reinforcement. This type of cartilage is found in the following regions:
    - The articular surface of bones.
    - The anterior portion of the ribs (Figure 4.34).
    - The nose.
    - The supporting rings of the trachea.
    - Some parts of the skull.
    - In the embryo, bone begins as hyaline cartilage and later ossifies.
  1. Elastic Cartilage: It has a ground substance containing rich network of yellow elastic fibers and collagenous fibers leading to its elasticity, elastic cartilage is found in the following regions:
    - In the framework of the outer ear (Figure 4.35)
    - In the epiglottis.
    - In the Eustachian tube.
  1. Fibrocartilage: It has a ground substance containing large amount of strong collagen fibers leading the toughness and flexibility of fibrocartilage. This type of cartilage absorbs shock and reduces friction between joints. It is found in structures that withstand tension and pressure such as:
    - The symphysis pubis
    - The sternoclavicular joint
    - The temporomandibular joint
    - The intervertebral disc (Figure 4.36)

Characteristic features of cartilage:

  • Cartilage has no blood or lymphatics supply, its nutrition diffuses through the matrix. This explains the slowness of healing process in cartilages.
  • Cartilage has no nerve supply and therefore it is insensitive.
  • Hyaline and elastic cartilage is surrounded by a fibrous layer known as the perichondrium provides blood supply to the cartilage and plays a major role in regeneration of the cartilage after injury.
  • There is no perichondrium around articular cartilage thus its regeneration is poor after injury.

‚öôÔ∏è 3. Joints

Joints are the location where bones come together. It represents the connection between bones in the body to allow for movement, flexibility, stability and protection.

Classifications of joints:

There are two types of joints classification:
1- Structural classification.
2- Functional classification.

Structural classification: based on the way the bones connect to each other, according to their structure, joints are classified into:

  1. Synovial joints.
  2. Fibrous joints.
  3. Cartilaginous joints.
  4. Facet joints.

Functional classification: based on the degree of joint movement they classified into three main types:

  1. Synarthrosis: with limited degree of mobility such as fibrous joints
  2. Amphiarthrosis: with small amount of mobility such as cartilaginous joints.
  3. Diarthrosis: freely-movable joint such as synovial joints.

1. Synovial joints:

They are also known as Diarthrosis, these are the most common type of joints in the body, the bones of this joint are not directly joined to each other, they have a synovial cavity and are united by a dense irregular connective tissue that forms an articular capsule that is normally associated with accessory ligaments.
Structural Features of Synovial: Synovial joints are characterized by the presence of an articular capsule formed by a fibrous connective tissue structure, the walls of the capsule form a joint cavity, filled with a synovial fluid. The bones of the joint articulate with each other within the joint cavity. The entire articulating surface of each bone is covered by a thin layer of hyaline cartilage known as the articular cartilage (Figure 4.37).

Friction between the bones at synovial joint is prevented by two factors:
1) The presence of the articular cartilage that covers the entire articulating surface of each bone to facilitate the smooth gliding of articular surfaces during joint movement.
2) The presence of the synovial fluid which lubricates the joint and allows for ease of movement.

Types of Synovial joints: Synovial joints are subdivided according to the shapes of the articulating surfaces into six types:

  1. Pivot joint
  2. Hinge joint
  3. Condyloid joint
  4. Saddle joint
  5. Plane joint
  6. Ball-and socket joint

1) Pivot joint: it is composed of a rounded portion of a bone, enclosed within a ring formed partially by the articulation with another bone and partially by a ligament, such as atlantoaxial joint (Figures 4.38 & 4.39).

2) Hinge joint: In a hinge joint, the convex end of one bone articulates with the concave end of the adjoining bone, such as elbow joint (Figure 4.40 & 4.41).

3) Condyloid joint: Also known as ellipsoid joint, there is shallow depression at the end of one bone articulates with a rounded structure from an adjacent bone or bones, such as the articulation between the radius and carpal bones at wrist joint (Figure 4.42 & 4.43) .

4) Saddle joint: At this type of joint, both of the articulating surfaces have a saddle shape, which is concave in one direction and convex in the other, such as trapeziometacarpal joint (Figure 4.44 & 4.45).

5) Plain joint: Also known as gliding joint, the articulating surfaces of the bones are flat or slightly curved and of approximately the same size, which allows the bones to slide against each other, such as the tarsal bones (Figure 4.46 & 4.47).

6) Ball-and socket joint: At this joint, the rounded head of one bone (the ball) fits into the concave articulation (the socket) of the adjacent bone, such as the hip joint, the ball-and socket joint with the greatest range of motion (Figures 4.48 & 4.49).

2. Fibrous joints:

These joints are connected by dense fibrous connective tissue and have no joint cavity; this is why these joints do not move.
There are three types of fibrous joints:
1) Sutures: the joints that join the skull bones (Figure 4.50).

2) Syndesmosis: the joints between two bone bound by long collagen fibers, such as the tibia and fibula and between radius and ulna (Figure 4.51).

3) Gomphosis: the joint between roots of teeth and the bony socket in the jaw (Figure 4.52).

3. Cartilaginous joints:

They are mainly joined by cartilage. There are two types of cartilaginous joints:
1) Synchondrosis: this joint is mainly composed two bones joined together by hyaline cartilage, such as the first sternocostal joint, where the first rib is anchored to the manubrium by hyaline cartilage (Figure 4.53).

2) Symphysis: these are secondary cartilaginous joints, because they are composed of hyaline cartilage covering fibrocartilage connecting the articular surfaces of the involved bones. It is very strong because it contains bundles of thick collagen fibers, thus giving it a much greater ability to resist pulling and bending forces when compared with hyaline cartilage, such as symphysis pubis (Figure 4.54).

4. Facet joints:

These are set of joints between two articular processes of two adjacent vertebrae. Each vertebra has two sets of facet joints. One pair faces upward and one pair faces downward (Figures 4.55 & 4.56).

üîó 4. Tendons

Tendon is a fibrous connective tissue attaches muscle to bone to transmit the mechanical force of muscle contraction to the bones and joints. The tendon is firmly connected to muscle fibers at one end and to the bone at its other end. Tendons are remarkably strong, having one of the highest tensile strengths found among soft tissues. Their great strength is necessary for withstanding the stresses generated by muscular contraction (Figure 4.57 & 4.58).

üéóÔ∏è 5. Ligaments

Ligament is a tough fibrous connective tissue that connects between two bones, it serves to support and hold bones together in proper articulation at the joints. Some ligaments form a capsular sac around joints to enclose the articulating ends of bones and the synovial membrane. Other ligaments fasten around or across bone ends in bands, permitting varying degrees of movement, or act as tie pieces between bones to restrict any inappropriate movement (Figure 4.59 & 4.60).

üï∏Ô∏è 6. Fascia

Fascia is a sheet of fibrous connective tissue that wraps all internal body organs and muscles to provide support for them and to hold their parts together. Fasciae are similar to ligaments and tendons as they are fibrous connective tissue, they differ in that ligaments join one bone to another bone, tendons join muscle to bone, and fasciae join muscles to muscles and join other internal structures.
Types of fascia:

  1. Superficial Fascia
  2. Deep Fascia
  3. Visceral Fascia

Superficial fascia: lies directly beneath the skin, it connects the skin to the underlying internal structures (Figure 4.62).

Deep fascia: lies deep inside human body, it is arranged in sheets surrounding individual muscles and groups of vessels and nerves, forming an investing fascia (Figure 4.63).

Visceral fascia: suspends the organs within their cavities and wraps them in layers of connective tissue (Figure 4.64).

Retinaculum: A retinaculum (plural retinacula) is a thickened band of deep fascia located around groups of tendons from different muscles, it hold these tendons in place to stabilize them (Figure 4.65). Retinacula are mainly found in the wrist and ankle joints, wrist retinacula envelope the flexor and extensor tendons of the hand, and ankle retinacula envelope the peroneal, the flexor, superior extensor, and inferior extensor tendons of the foot.
