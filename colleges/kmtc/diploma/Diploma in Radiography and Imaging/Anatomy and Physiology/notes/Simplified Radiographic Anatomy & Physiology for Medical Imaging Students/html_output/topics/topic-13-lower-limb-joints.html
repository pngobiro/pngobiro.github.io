ü¶¥ The Hip Joint

The hip joint is a ball and socket synovial joint, formed by the articulation between the acetabulum of the hip bone and the head of the femur.
It represents a connection between the lower limb and the pelvic girdle, thus it is designed for weight-bearing and stability rather than of movement.
The acetabulum is a cup shaped socket located on the inferolateral aspect of the hip bone, it is deepened by a fibrocartilaginous collar known as the acetabular labrum. The head of femur is hemispherical structure with smooth surface and covered with articular cartilage; it completely fits into the cavity of the acetabulum (Figure 13.1).

An articular cartilage surrounds the articular surfaces of the head of the femur and the acetabulum; it is much stronger at weight bearing areas. There is strong, fibrous capsule attached to the edge of the acetabulum proximally, and on the distal part it is attached to the intertrochanteric line anteriorly and to the neck of the femur posteriorly.
There is group of inner and outer ligaments attached to the hip joint to increase its stability; the inner ligaments are the head of femur ligament and transverse acetabular ligament, while the outer ligaments are Iliofemoral, Pubofemoral, and Ischiofemoral ligaments. Although these ligaments are strong and tough, they are loose enough to accommodate the high range of hip joint movement (Figure 13.1).

ü¶µ The knee joint

The knee joint is a synovial hinge joint, formed by articulations between the patella, femur and tibia. It mainly allows for flexion and extension as well as small degree of medial and lateral rotation. Knee joint is composed of two articulations, the tibiofemoral articulation, connecting the distal femur to the proximal tibia, and the patellofemoral articulation, connecting the anterior portion of distal femur to the patella, knee joint is an unstable joint, thus it requires a great deal of support from tendons and muscles (Figure 13.2).

Menisci:

The term "meniscus" is a Greek word means "crescent", menisci of the knee joint are two crescent-shaped pads of fibrocartilaginous tissue lie between femur and tibia; they serve to disperse body weight and reduce friction during movement.,.
The medial and lateral menisci sit between the tibiofemoral articulations, where they mainly articulate with the tibia. The thicker parts of menisci face the outer edges and they wedge into the centre of the tibiofemoral articulation to allow even distribution of pressure on the tibia. The medial meniscus is more C -shaped while the lateral is more circular. The medial meniscus is fixed to the medial collateral ligament and the joint capsule while the lateral meniscus does not have any extra attachments (Figure 13.3), rendering it fairly mobile. The transverse ligament (meniscomeniscal ligament) connects the anterior convex margin of the lateral meniscus to the anterior end of the medial meniscus; it holds the menisci in place during knee extension. The meniscofemoral ligaments attach the posterior horn of the lateral meniscus to the lateral aspect of the medial femoral condyle

Ligaments:

There are three major ligaments in the knee joint which are:

  1. Patellar ligament
  2. Collateral ligaments
  3. Cruciate Ligaments

Patellar ligament: is the continuation of the quadriceps femoris tendon distal to the patella. It attaches to the tibial tuberosity.
Collateral ligaments: are two strap-like ligaments:
Medial collateral ligament
Lateral collateral ligament
They play major role in stabilizing the hinge motion of the knee joint by preventing excessive medial or lateral movement.

Medial collateral ligament: is a wide and flat ligament, located on the medial side of the knee joint, it is attached proximally to the medial epicondyle of the femur, and attached distally to the medial condyle of the tibia.
Lateral collateral ligament: is located on the lateral side of the knee joint, it is thinner and rounder than the medial collateral ligament, it is attached proximally to the lateral epicondyle of the femur, and attached distally to the lateral surface of the fibular head.
Cruciate Ligaments: 'cruciate' is a latin term means "cross", cruciate ligaments are two ligaments, anterior and posterior, they cross each other as they extend from the femur proximally and the tibia distally.
Anterior cruciate ligament: it is attached proximally to the intercondylar fossa of the femur and distally to the anterior intercondylar region of the tibia where it blends with the medial meniscus. It acts to prevents anterior dislocation of the tibia onto the femur.
Posterior cruciate ligament: it is attached proximally to the anteromedial femoral condyle and distally to the posterior intercondylar region of the tibia. It acts to prevent posterior dislocation of the tibia onto the femur (Figure 13.3 & 13.4).

Knee joint bursae: there are multiple bursae surrounding the knee joint, they facilitate movement and reduce friction where tendons or muscles pass over bony prominences. They can be either communicating or non-communicating with the knee joint itself. There are four bursae anterior to the knee joint:

  1. Suprapatellar bursa: located between the femur and quadriceps femoris muscle.
  2. Subcutaneous Prepatellar bursa: between the skin and patella
  3. Subcutaneous Infrapatellar bursa: between the skin and tibial tuberosity
  4. Deep Infrapatellar bursa: between patella ligament and upper tibia (Figure 13.5).

ü¶¥ The Tibiofibular Joints

The proximal and distal tibiofibular joints refer to two articulations between the tibia and fibula of the leg. These two joints play a major role in stability and weight-bearing rather than movement (Figures 13.6 & 13.7).

The Proximal Tibiofibular Joint:

The proximal tibiofibular joint is a plane type synovial joint formed by articulation between the head of the fibula and the lateral condyle of the tibia.
The articular surfaces of the proximal tibiofibular joint are lined with hyaline cartilage and contained within a joint capsule.
The joint capsule receives additional support from:

  • Anterior and posterior superior tibiofibular ligaments which span between the fibular head and lateral tibial condyle.
  • Lateral collateral ligament of the knee joint.
  • Biceps femoris - provides reinforcement as it inserts onto the fibular head (Figure 13.7).

The Distal Tibiofibular joint: also known as (tibiofibular syndesmosis) is fibrous joint formed by articulation between the fibular notch of the distal tibia and the fibula. As it is a fibrous joint, the distal tibiofibular joint does not have a joint capsule, it is supported by:

  • Interosseous membrane which spans the whole length of the tibia and fibula.
  • Anterior and posterior inferior tibiofibular ligaments.
  • Inferior transverse tibiofibular ligament (Figures 13.8 & 13.9).

ü¶∂ The Ankle Joint

The ankle joint is a hinge type synovial joint formed by articulation between three bones; the bones of the leg (tibia and fibula) and the foot bone (talus). The tibia and fibula are strongly bound together by tibiofibular ligaments, and they form a bracket shaped socket known as a mortise into which the dome of the talus fits.
The medial malleolus of the tibia extends inferiorly to articulate with the talus on its medial aspect, and the lateral malleolus of the fibula articulate with the talus on its lateral aspect laterally.

The ankle joint Ligaments: There are two main groups of ligaments; medial and lateral groups, which originate the medial and lateral malleoli respectively, they mainly stabilize the ankle joint, and act as a guide to direct the motion of the ankle joint by attaching the lateral malleolus to the bones below the ankle joint, and they are also responsible for resistance against over-inversion and over-eversion stress of the foot.
Medial Ligament: also known as the deltoid ligament, it consists of four ligaments, which fan out from the medial malleolus and attached to the talus, calcaneus and navicular bones. Their primary action is to resist over-eversion stress of the foot (Figure 13.11).

Lateral Ligament: it originates from the lateral malleolus and is consists of three distinct ligaments (Figure 13.12):
Anterior Talofibular: it spans between the lateral malleolus and lateral aspect of the talus. Posterior Talofibular: it spans between the lateral malleolus and the posterior aspect of the talus.
Calcaneofibular: it spans between the lateral malleolus and the calcaneus.
The primary action of the lateral ligament is to resist over-inversion stress of the foot.

‚öôÔ∏è The Subtalar joint

The subtalar joint (also known as the talocalcaneal joint) is a plane synovial joint formed by the articulation between the inferior surface of the body of the talus and the superior surface of the calcaneus, it is enclosed by a joint capsule, which is strengthened externally by a fibrous layer and supported by the following ligaments:

  1. Posterior talocalcaneal ligament.
  2. Medial talocalcaneal ligament.
  3. Lateral talocalcaneal ligament.
  4. Interosseous talocalcaneal ligament.

ü¶¥ Joints of the Foot

Talocalcaneonavicular joint

This joint is composed of two articulations: the anterior articulation of the subtalar joint and the articulation between the talus and the navicular. They are stabilized by a fibrous capsule and by the talonavicular and plantar calcaneonavicular ligaments. The talonavicular ligament connects the dorsal aspect of the talar neck with the navicular bone and is covered by extensor tendons. The plantar calcaneonavicular ligament, also known as the spring ligament, runs from the anterior aspect of the sustentaculum tali to the plantar surface of the navicular bone.

Calcaneocuboid joint

The calcaneocuboid joint is a saddle (biaxial) joint, and is formed by the distal surface of the calcaneus and the proximal aspect of the cuboid. This joint is stabilized by three ligaments:

  1. The bifurcate ligament is a Y -shaped band, attaches proximally to the anterior aspect of the calcaneus and divides distally into two parts: the calcaneocuboid part attaches to the dorsomedial surface of the cuboid bone and the calcaneonavicular part attaches to the dorsolateral aspect of the navicular bone.
  2. The longest ligament also known as the long plantar ligament. It runs from the plantar surface of the calcaneus bone to the tuberosity located on the plantar aspect of the cuboid bone. More superficial fibers continue on to attach to the bases of the second to fourth metatarsals.
  3. The plantar calcaneocuboid ligament extends from the anterior tubercle of the calcaneus to the plantar aspect of the cuboid. It is located deep to the long plantar ligament and is separated from it by areolar tissue.

Naviculocuneiform joint

The naviculocuneiform joint is a compound joint and consists of articulations between the navicular and the three cuneiform bones. The bones of this joint are connected by two ligaments: The dorsal and plantar ligaments connect the navicular bone with each cuneiform. The medial dorsal ligament continues as a capsule around the medial aspect of the joint.

Cuboideonavicular joint

The cuboideonavicular joint is a syndesmosis that connects the cuboid and navicular bones. This joint is stabilized by dorsal, plantar and interosseous ligaments.

Intercuneiform and cuneocuboid joints

The intercuneiform and cuneocuboid joints are synovial joints involving the cuneiform and cuboid bones. The bones are connected together by dorsal, plantar and interosseous ligaments. Both the dorsal and plantar ligaments consist of three transverse bands, which run between the cuneiform bones and between the lateral cuneiform and the cuboid bone. The interosseous ligaments connect non-articular surfaces of the bones.

Tarsometatarsal joints

The tarsometatarsal joints connect the metatarsals with some of the tarsal bones of the foot. The first metatarsal articulates with the medial cuneiform, the second with the intermediate cuneiform and the third metatarsal articulates with the lateral cuneiform. The lateral cuneiform also articulates with the fourth metatarsal and the cuboid bone forms articulations with both the fourth and fifth metatarsals. There are three bands of ligaments involved in stabilizing these joints:

  1. The dorsal tarsometatarsal ligaments connect the metatarsal bones to the cuboid and cuneiform bones.
  2. The plantar tarsometatarsal ligaments consist of both longitudinal and oblique bands.
  3. The interosseous cuneometatarsal ligaments this is the strongest of the three ligaments, it runs from the second metatarsal to the lateral aspect of the medial cuneiform (Figure 13.9).

Intermetatarsal joints

The intermetatarsal joints are articulations formed between the metatarsal bones and are stabilized by intermetatarsal interosseous ligaments. These ligaments run between the lateral four metatarsal bones. Dorsal and plantar intermetatarsal ligaments are also involved in the stabilization of this joint.

Metatarsophalangeal joints

The metatarsophalangeal joints are ellipsoid joints, which consist of articulations between the heads of the metatarsals and the bases of the proximal phalanges. On the plantar surface of the first metatarsal head, there are two longitudinal grooves separated by a ridge, the crista. These two grooves articulate with the two sesamoid bones within the joint capsule. The bones of the metatarsophalangeal joints are stabilized by the following ligaments:

  1. The collateral ligaments: run from the dorsal tubercles of the metatarsal heads before widening and attaching to the bases of the proximal phalanges.
  2. The Plantar ligaments: lie between the collateral ligaments, are attached firmly to the phalangeal bases and loosely to the metatarsal heads. They blend with both the deep transverse metatarsal and collateral ligaments.
  3. The Deep transverse metatarsal ligaments: they are four flat bands, unite the plantar ligaments and lie between the interossei and the lumbricals (Figure 13.10).

Interphalangeal joints

These hinge joints are formed between the trochlear surface of the phalangeal heads and the curved surface of the bases of the corresponding phalanges. They are stabilized by an articular capsule and two collateral ligaments.
