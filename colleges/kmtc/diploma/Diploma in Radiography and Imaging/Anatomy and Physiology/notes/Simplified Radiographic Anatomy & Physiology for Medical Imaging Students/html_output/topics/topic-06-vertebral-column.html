ü¶¥ The Vertebral Column

The vertebral column (also known as the backbone) is the part of the axial skeleton that extends from the base of the skull down to the pelvis, it consists of sequence of 33 separate bony vertebrae, 24 of them are joined by cartilaginous intervertebral discs inbetween known as presacral vertebrae, and 9 are fused in two regions: sacral 5 and coccygeal 4. The vertebral column houses the spinal cord in a cavity known as the spinal canal.

Regions of vertebral Column:

Vertebral column is divided into five regions (Figure 6.1):

  1. Cervical region at the neck, it is composed of 7 vertebrae.
  2. Thoracic region at the mid-back, it is composed of 12 vertebrae.
  3. Lumbar region at the lower-back, it is composed of 5 vertebrae.
  4. Sacral region at the upper pelvis, it is composed of 5 vertebrae.
  5. Coccygeal region at the lower pelvis, it is composed of 5 vertebrae.

Curvatures of the Vertebral Column:

Normal vertebral column does not form a straight line, but instead has four curvatures (Figure 6.2).

  1. The cervical curve.
  2. The thoracic curve.
  3. The lumbar curve.
  4. The sacrococcygeal curve.

These curvatures increase the strength and flexibility of the vertebral column, beside they increase the ability of the vertebral column to absorb shock.

Function of the vertebral column:

Protection: The major function of the vertebral column is protection of the spinal cord.
Support: It carries the weight of the body above the pelvis.
Central Axis: it forms the central axis of the body, and provides attachment for the muscles of pectoral and pelvic girdles.
Movement: it controls movement and transmits body weight during walking and standing.

üß± Anatomical structure of vertebra

Although the anatomical structure of vertebrae varies in size and shape within the different regions of the vertebral column, all vertebrae follow general similarity in their structural pattern.
The basic anatomical structure of a typical vertebra is composed of two main parts (Figure 6.4):

  1. Vertebral body anteriorly.
  2. Vertebral arch posteriorly.

Vertebral Body:

The vertebral body forms the anterior part of vertebra and it is the weight-bearing part, this is why lower vertebral bodies are larger than the upper to better support the increased weight.

Vertebral Arch:

The vertebral arch forms the lateral and posterior aspect of each vertebrae, it is formed by seven processes (Figure 6.4):

  1. Single spinous process centered posteriorly.
  2. Two transverse processes extend laterally and posteriorly from the vertebral body.
  3. Two pedicles connect the vertebral body to the transverse processes.
  4. Two laminae connect the transverse and spinous processes.

On each side of vertebrae there are two articular facets:

  1. Two superior articular facets facing superiorly and backward.
  2. Two inferior articular facets facing inferiorly and forward.

Each inferior facet articulates with the superior facet of the lower vertebra forming facet joint between each two vertebra (Figures 6.5 & 6.6).

Vertebral canal: The vertebral body and the vertebral arch surround an opening known as the vertebral foramen; it provides passage of the spinal cord and its meninges. Vertebral foramina extend from the first cervical vertebra down to the fifth lumber vertebra forming a tunnel known as the vertebral canal or spinal canal.

03

ü¶í Cervical Vertebrae

The cervical region is composed of seven cervical vertebrae numbered from C1 to C7; their main function is to support the weight of the head, which weighs between 10 and 13 pounds. In addition, they allow for the great range of head motion and neck flexibility.
Atlas and Axis: The uppermost two cervical vertebrae are specialized vertebrae; the first cervical vertebra C1 is known as the Atlas (Figure 6.7). It is ring-shaped vertebra lacks a body and a spinous process; and there is no intervertebral disc below or above it. Atlas is directly connected to skull through the articulation of the superior articular facets of the atlas with the occipital condyles of the skull at the Atlanto-occipital joint, this joint allows for the "yes" motion of the head.

The second cervical vertebra (C2) is known as the Axis (Figure 6.8). This vertebra is pegshaped composed of a vertebral body, pedicles, laminae, transverse processes, and a unique projection called the (Dens) or (Odontoid process), the anterior arch of the Atlas articulates and pivots around this projection at the atlantoaxial joint allowing for the side-to-side or "no" motion of the head (Figure 6.9).

The five lower cervical vertebrae from C3 down to C7 are similar to each other; each has a vertebral body with a concave superior surface and convex inferior surface. They have facets of superior and inferior articular processes that articulate in oblique planes that slope downward laterally and posteriorly. Only cervical vertebrae have a transverse foramen in each of their transverse processes (Figure 6.11).

04

ü´Å Thoracic Vertebrae

Thoracic Vertebrae: the main function of the thoracic spine is to hold the rib cage and protect the heart and lungs. There are twelve thoracic vertebrae are numbered from T1 to T12. The range of motion in the thoracic spine is limited. Thoracic vertebrae articulate with the ribs on the superior and inferior costal facets found on the bodies of thoracic vertebrae (Figure 6.12 & 13).

üèãÔ∏è Lumbar vertebrae

Lumber vertebrae: The main function of the lumbar spine is to bear the weight of the body. The five lumbar vertebrae are numbered from L1 to L5. These vertebrae are much larger in size to absorb the stress of lifting and carrying heavy objects (Figure 6.14).

06

üõ°Ô∏è Sacrum and Coccyx

Sacrum and Coccyx: The main function of the sacrum is the connection of between the spine and the hip bone. Sacrum is composed of five fused vertebrae (Fig 6.15). The union of the sacrum with the ilium forms the pelvic girdle.
The coccyx or tailbone is composed of four fused bones; they provide attachment for muscles and ligaments of the pelvic floor (Fig 6.15 & 16).

07

üç© Intervertebral discs

Intervertebral discs: intervertebral discs are fibrocartilaginous cushions located between adjacent bony vertebrae in the spine they keep them from rubbing together and form fibrocartilaginous joint, to allow slight movement of the vertebrae. Intervertebral discs are composed of two layers;

  1. The annulus fibrosus is an outer fibrous ring.
  2. The nucleus pulposus is an inner gel-like center.

The fibers of the annulus fibrosus pull the vertebral bodies together against the elastic resistance of the gel-filled nucleus pulposus which act like a ball-bearing during movement, allowing the vertebral bodies to roll over the incompressible gel. The gelfilled nucleus is composed mostly of fluid. This fluid absorbed in the night during lying down and is pushed out during the day due to upright movement (Figures 6.17, 6.18 & 6.19).

‚öôÔ∏è Joints and Ligaments

The vertebrae articulate with each other via joints between their articular facets and joints between their bodies and surrounding cartilages, there are also ligaments that strengthen and stabilize these joints; they resist hyperflexion and hyperextension of the vertebral column and hold the articulating parts that form the vertebral joints together.

Joints of the vertebral column:

  1. Intervertebral joints: There are two types of intervertebral joints between each adjacent vertebra;
    1- Intervertebral fibrocartilaginous joint between the vertebral bodies and their adjacent intervertebral discs.
    2- Intervertebral synovial joint between the facets of adjoining vertebral arches (Figures $6.5 & 6.6$ ).
  2. Craniovertebral joints: there are two craniovertebral joints;
    1- Atlantooccipital joint between the superior articular surfaces of the atlas and the occipital condyles ("yes"-nodding).
    2- Atlantoaxial joint composed of two lateral joints between the inferior facets of atlas and superior facets of axis, and one median joint between the dens of the axis and anterior arch of the atlas ("no"-movement).
  3. Costovertebral joints: between the head of the ribs and adjacent vertebral bodies

Ligaments of the vertebral column:

  1. Ligamentum Flavum: are paired ligaments run between adjacent laminae of the vertebral bodies from the second cervical vertebra down to the sacrum, they mainly connect the laminae of each adjacent vertebrae and fuses them with the capsule of the facet joint serving as a covering band over the spinal canal. Ligamentum flavum means "yellow ligament," and is so known because it has a yellow coloring due to the large amount of a springy type of collagen known as elastin, which elastin pulls the ligament out of the canal when the spine is extended (Figures 6.22, 6.23 & 6.24).
  2. Anterior and posterior longitudinal ligaments run in bands down the anterior and posterior surfaces of the vertebral bodies from the skull to the sacrum. They help to stabilize the vertebral column (Figure 6.23).
  3. The interspinous ligament runs between the spinous processes (Figure 6.23).
  4. The supraspinous ligament connects the tips of the spinous processes and forms the strong nuchal ligament that runs posterior to the cervical spine (Figure 6.23).
