üíÄ The Skull

The Skull is the skeletal framework of the head; it is the bony structure that forms the protective cavity for the brain and supports the face. It is comprised of many bones which are joined by fibrous joints known as sutures (Figure 5.1).

Skull divisions:

The skull can be divided into two divisions (Figure 5.2):

  1. Cranium: also known as the skullcap, it is the upper part of the skull represents the brain case, it surrounds the brain and protects its components, and it mainly houses the meninges, cerebral vasculature and the structures of the middle and inner ear.
  2. Facial bones: they underlie the facial structures and supports the soft tissues of the face, enclose the eyeballs, form the nasal and oral cavities, and support the teeth of the upper and lower jaws.
02

ü¶¥ Cranium of the Skull

Anatomically, the cranium is subdivided into a roof known as the calvarium and a base. The cranium of the skull is composed of several bones joined together by sutures, the base of the cranium has several openings known as foramina, through which blood vessels and nerves enter and leave the cavity of the cranium.

The cranial bones:

There are 8 cranial bones (Figure 5.3):

  • The Frontal bone (1)
  • The Parietal bones (2)
  • The Temporal bones (2)
  • The Sphenoid bone (1)
  • The Ethmoid bone (1)
  • The Occipital bone (1)

The Frontal Bone:

The frontal bone is located at the front of the skull and corresponds to the region known as the forehead. The frontal bone consists of two portions:

  1. Vertical portion, also known as the squama, corresponding with the region of the forehead.
  2. Horizontal portion, also known as the orbital portion, it forms the roofs of the orbital and nasal cavities (Figure 5.4).

The Parietal Bones:

The two parietal bones are roughly square shaped, found on both sides of the skull they are connected and make up the roof and part of sides of the skull (Figure 5.5).

The Occipital Bone:

The occipital bone located at the rear of the cranium. This bone protects the brain and supports the back of the head (Figure 5.6).
The occipital bone contains the foramen magnum through which passes the spinal cord, the foramen magnum allows the cranial cavity to communicate with the vertebral column (Figure 5.7).

The Temporal Bones:

The temporal bones are situated on the sides and bases of the skull. The functions of the temporal bones are protection of the brain and support of the face (Figure 5.8).
Each temporal bone consists of five parts:

  1. The Squamous part.
  2. The Tympanic part.
  3. The Styloid process.
  4. The Petrous part.
  5. The Mastoid part.

Parts of Temporal Bone:

  1. The Squamous part: It is the largest part of the temporal bone, located superiorly. It is flat and plate-like, with its outer surface convex in shape.
  2. The Tympanic part: It the part that surrounds the external ear, it lies inferior to the squamous part, and anterior to the mastoid part.
  3. The Mastoid part: It is an inferior bony projection, lies just behind the external ear. It is a site of attachment for many muscles.
  4. The Petrous part: It is pyramidal shaped, and lies superior to the mastoid part, it contains the inner ear.
  5. The Styloid process: It is located immediately underneath the tympanic part; it acts as an attachment point for muscles and ligaments. (Figure 5.9)

The Sphenoid Bone:

The sphenoid bone is 'butterfly-shaped bone located at the base of the skull (Figure 5.10). It is one of several bones that form the eye socket (orbit), located posterior to the eye socket and anterior to the temporal bone.

Components of sphenoid bone: The sphenoid bone consists of a body, two greater wings and lesser wings, and two pterygoid processes; each pterygoid process is composed of medial and lateral plates (Figure 5.11).

The Ethmoid bone:

The Ethmoid bone is located in the midline of the anterior part of the skull between the two orbits contributing in the formation of the medial wall of the orbits, and on the upper part of the nasal cavity forming its roof and lateral walls, and the upper portion of the nasal septum (Figure 5.12). The ethmoid bone contributes in the formation of the floor of the anterior cranial fossa.

Components of Ethmoid Bone:

  1. Crista galli: an upward bony projection located at the midline of the superior surface of the ethmoid bone.
  2. Cribriform plate: a flattened horizontal areas located to either side of the crista galli with numerous small openings, at the superior surface of the ethmoid bone.
  3. Perpendicular plate: a thin, vertical lamina descends from the under surface of the cribriform plate, forming part of the nasal septum (Figure 5.13).
  4. Superior nasal concha: a curled shelf like bony structure protrudes into the superior part of the nasal cavity.
  5. Middle nasal concha: a curled shelf like bony structure protrudes into the middle part of the nasal cavity (Figure 5.13).
03

üß∂ Sutures

Sutures: Also known as syndesmosis, they are rigid, immovable fibrous joints connecting the skull bones. In the skull of growing fetus sutures are wide and filled with membranous gap known as fontanelles. There are four major sutures (Figure 5.15):

  1. Sagittal Suture: the joint between the two parietal bones.
  2. Coronal Suture: the joint between the frontal bone and the parietal bones.
  3. Squamous Suture: the joint between the parietal and temporal bones.
  4. Lambdoid Suture: the joint between the parietal bones and the occipital bone.

üèõÔ∏è The Cranial Base

The Cranial base: it is also known as the base of the skull; it forms the floor of the cranium and it acts as a bony diaphragm that separates the cranial cavity and the brain from the facial structures.
Bones of the cranial base: The cranial base is composed of the following bones from anterior to posterior as follows (Figure 5.16).

  1. Frontal bone
  2. Ethmoid bone
  3. Sphenoid bone
  4. Parietal bone
  5. Temporal bone
  6. Occipital bone

Regions of the cranial base: The cranial base is divided by the sphenoidal ridge anteriorly and the petrous ridge posteriorly into three regions or fossae (Figure 5.17):

  1. Anterior cranial fossa
  2. Middle cranial fossa
  3. Posterior cranial fossa

Foramina of the cranial base: The following is a list of them and their contents (Figures 5.18 & 5.19):

  1. Foramen Caecum: emissary vein to superior sagittal sinus.
  2. Foramina of Cribriform Plate: olfactory nerve bundles.
  3. Optic Canal: optic nerve [II], ophthalmic artery.
  4. Superior Orbital fissure: oculomotor nerve [III] trochlear nerve [IV] lacrimal, frontal and nasociliary branches of ophthalmic nerve [VI] abducens nerve [VI] superior ophthalmic vein foramen rotundum: maxillary nerve [V2].
  5. The carotid canal: the internal carotid artery.
  6. Foramen Rotundum: The maxillary branch (V2) of the trigeminal nerve (CN V).
  7. Foramen Ovale: mandibular nerve [V3] accessory meningeal artery lesser petrosal nerve (occasionally)
  8. Foramen Spinosum: middle meningeal artery and vein meningeal branch of mandibular nerve.
  9. Foramen Lacerum: internal carotid artery internal carotid nerve plexus canal of lesser petrosal nerve canal of greater petrosal nerve
  10. Internal Acoustic Canal: facial nerve [VII] vestibulocochlear nerve [VIII] labyrinthine artery.
  11. Jugular Foramen: inferior petrosal sinus, glossopharyngeal nerve [IX], Vagus nerve $[\mathrm{X}]$, accessory nerve $[\mathrm{XI}]$ sigmoid sinus posterior meningeal artery internal jugular vein.
  12. Hypoglossal Canal: hypoglossal nerve [XII]
  13. Foramen Magnum: medulla oblongata vertebral arteries meningeal branches of vertebral arteries spinal roots of accessory nerves.

üò∂ The Facial Bones

The facial bones underlie the facial structures and supports the soft tissues of the face, enclose the eyeballs, form the nasal and oral cavities, and support the teeth of the upper and lower jaws.
There are 14 facial bones comprising the facial skeleton: (Figure 5.20)

  • Maxilla (2)
  • Palatine bones (2)
  • Zygomatic bones (2)
  • Nasal bones (2)
  • Inferior nasal concha (2)
  • Lacrimal bones (2)
  • Vomer (1)
  • Mandible (1)

Maxilla:

it is pair of pyramidal shaped bones fused to form the upper jaw. Maxilla is composed of body and four projections (Figure 5.21):

  • Frontal process
  • Zygomatic process
  • Alveolar process
  • Palatine process

Processes of Maxilla:

the maxilla has three processes (Figure 5.22):

  1. The Frontal process.
  2. The Zygomatic process.
  3. The Alveolar process.
  4. The Palatine process.

The Frontal process: is a strong plate, projects upward, medially and backward, by the side of the nose, forming part of its lateral boundary.
The Zygomatic process: is a rough triangular eminence, which protrudes laterally creating a non-smooth articular surface with the zygomatic bone.
The Alveolar process: is the thickened ridge of bone that contains the tooth sockets (dental alveoli) on the jaw bones that hold teeth; it represents the upper jaw

The Palatine process of maxilla: it is a thick, horizontal process of the maxilla. It forms the anterior three quarters of the hard palate (at the roof of the mouth), and the posterior one quarter of the hard palate is formed by the horizontal plate of the palatine bone (Figure 5.23).

Palatine Bone:

it is one of a pair of bones forms the posterior portion of hard palate (the roof of the oral cavity) and is directly inferior to the nasal cavity (Figure 5.24).

Zygomatic Bones:

The two zygomatic bones are located in the upper and lateral parts of the face forming the lateral wall of the orbit. The zygomatic bone, also known as the cheek bone, supports the region of the face known as the cheek, protects organs in this area. The short temporal process of the zygomatic bone projects posteriorly, where it forms the anterior portion of the zygomatic arc (Figure 5.25).

Nasal Bones:

consists of two bones located near the middle of the face, they come together to form the nasal bridge of the nose. These bones vary in size from individual to individual. The main function of nasal bones is the support of the nose (Figure 5.26).

Nasal concha:

plural conchae, it is a long, narrow, curled shelf of bone that protrudes into the nasal cavity, there are three nasal conchae, superior, middle and inferior.
The superior and middle nasal conchae form part of the ethmoid bone, while the inferior nasal concha is a bony structure by itself Nasal conchae divide the nasal airway into 4 groove-like air passages, and are responsible for forcing inhaled air to flow in a steady, regular pattern around the largest possible surface area of nasal mucosa (Figure 5.27).

Lacrimal Bone:

The lacrimal bone (lacrima is Latin word means tear) is the smallest bone of the skull. It is paired and sits inside the bony orbit taking up a small area of the most anterior part of the medial wall (Figure 5.28). The lacrimal bone has two surfaces, a lateral (orbital) and medial (nasal) one.

Vomer:

The Vomer is a singular bone that runs vertically within the nasal cavity, separating the left and right sides. The Vomer forms the posterior inferior aspect of the nasal septum posteroinferior to the perpendicular plate of the ethmoid bone (Figure 5.29).

The Mandible:

This bone forms the lower jaw of the human skull; its main function is support of the lower part of the face and holding the bottom half of the teeth in place. The mandible is essential for movement of the mouth (Figure 5.30).

The mandible consists of following parts (Figure 5.31):

  1. Body.
  2. Ramus.
  3. Angle.

The body of the mandible: it is horseshoe shaped part of the mandible, lies anteriorly in horizontal direction; it is connected to the two rami posteriorly at the angle of the mandible.
The Ramus of the mandible: There are two mandibular rami, which project perpendicularly upwards from the angle of the mandible. Each ramus contains the following bony landmarks.

  1. Coronoid process.
  2. Head.
  3. Neck.

The Angle of the mandible: it is located at the junction between the posterior part of the body and the lower part of the ramus of the mandible.
