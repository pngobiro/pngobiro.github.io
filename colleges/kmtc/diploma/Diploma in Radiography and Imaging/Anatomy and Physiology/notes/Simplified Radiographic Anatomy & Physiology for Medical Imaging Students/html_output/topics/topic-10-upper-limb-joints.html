01

ü¶¥ The Shoulder Joint

Shoulder joint, also known as Glenohumeral joint, it is the multiaxial ball-and-socket synovial joint formed by the articulation of the head of the humerus with the glenoid cavity of the scapula, which is deepened and slightly enlarged by the glenoid labrum, a fibrocartilage ring attaching to the outside margin of the glenoid fossa (Figure 10.1).

The capsule of the shoulder joint:

As a synovial joint, the shoulder joint has a capsule surrounds the joint cavity and encloses synovial fluid that lubricates the articular surfaces of the joint, it is attached medially to the outer margin of the glenoid fossa and laterally to the anatomical neck of the humerus, just distal to the head of the humerus. This capsule is loose inferiorly, and lies in folds during full adduction of the arm, these folds allow for abduction of the arm, during which they become taught (Figure 10.2).

Bursae of the shoulder joint:

The bursa is a fluid filled sac provides cushioning between joint structures to protect the joint capsule from wear. The glenohumeral joint has a number of bursae that protect the joint capsule from excessive wear from overlying structures. The most common of which is the subacromial bursa which lies between the acromion process of the scapula and the supraspinatus muscle. Subscapular bursa protects the joint capsule and the muscle tendon of the supraspinatus muscle from wear from the acromion process during arm abduction. Subscapular bursa lies between the tendon of subscapularis muscle and the scapula. It reduces wear and tear on the subscapularis tendon during shoulder joint movement (Figure 10.3).

02

üí™ Elbow joint

The elbow is the joint connecting the upper arm to the forearm. It is classed as a hingetype synovial joint. It is the articulation point of the humerus and the radius and the ulna.
The elbow joint consists of two separate articulations:

  1. Humeroulnar joint between the trochlea on the medial aspect of the distal end of the humerus and the trochlear notch on the proximal ulna.
  2. Humeroradial joint between the capitulum on the lateral aspect of the distal end of the humerus with the head of the radius.

These two joints give the elbow its characteristic hinge like properties. The rounded surfaces of the capitulum of the humerus rotate against the concave surfaces of the head of the radius, and the wrench shape of the trochlear notch of the ulna moves around the trochlea of the humerus (Figure 10.6).

The capsule of the elbow joint:

The elbow joint has a strong and fibrous capsule encloses and strengthens the joint. This capsule is thickened medially and laterally to form collateral ligaments, which stabilize the flexing and extending motion of the arm (Figure 10.7).

The bursa of the elbow joint:

There are many bursae in the elbow, but only a few have clinical importance:

  1. Intratendinous bursa: leis within the tendon of the triceps brachii muscle.
  2. Subtendinous bursa: reduces friction between the olecranon and the tendon of the triceps brachii muscle during extension and flexion of the arm.
  3. Olecranon bursa: lies between the olecranon and the overlying connective tissue.
03

ü¶¥ Radioulnar Joints

Proximal Radioulnar Joint

The proximal radioulnar joint is pivot-joint formed by an articulation between the head of the radius and the radial notch of the ulna; it lies immediately distal to the elbow joint, and is enclosed with in the same articular capsule.
The radial head is held in place by the Annular Radial Ligament, which forms a 'collar' around the joint. The annular radial ligament is lined with a synovial membrane, reducing friction during movement. Movement is produced by the head of the radius rotating within the annular ligament. There are two movements possible at this joint; pronation and supination (Figure 10.10).

Distal Radioulnar Joint

The distal radioulnar joint is pivot-joint formed by an articulation between the head of the ulna and the ulnar notch on the distal end of the radius, just proximally to the wrist joint. The distal radioulnar joint is strengthened by anterior and posterior ligaments and a fibrocartilaginous ligament known as the articular disk, they hold the radius and ulna together during joint movement and separate the distal radioulnar joint from the wrist joint (Figure 10.10).

üñêÔ∏è Wrist Joint

The wrist is the joint connecting the forearm and the hand, it is a synovial joint formed by articulation between the distal end of the radius and the articular disk proximally and scaphoid, lunate and triquetrum distally the ulna is not part of the wrist joint).
The radius articulates with the scaphoid laterally and the lunate medially (Figure 10.11), on the medial side of the wrist joint there is a cartilaginous structure known as the triangular fibrocartilage complex (TFCC), it extends from the ulna to insert into the lunate and triquetrum via the ulnolunate and ulnotriquetral ligaments respectively (Figure 10.12). The triangular fibrocartilage complex (TFCC) is a major contributor to the stability of the wrist joint.

05

üëÜ Hand Joints

Metacarpophalangeal joint

Metacarpophalangeal joints are condyloid type synovial joints between the head of metacarpal and the base of proximal phalanx in each of the 5 digits. Each joint is enclosed by a fibrous capsule which attaches to the margins of the articular surfaces and is lined by a synovial membrane. There are two collateral ligaments and one palmar ligament associated with each metacarpophalangeal joint.

Interphalangeal joints

Interphalangeal joints are hinge joints between the phalanges, there are two Interphalangeal joints in each digit except the thumb which has only one interphalangeal joint. The two joints are: Proximal Interphalangeal Joints between the proximal phalanx and intermediate phalanx in each of the 2nd to 5th digits and Distal Interphalangeal Joints between the intermediate phalanx and distal phalanx in each of the 2nd to 5th digits (Figure 10.13).
