üó£Ô∏è ANATOMICAL TERMINOLOGY

Terminology is a group of technical terms used in a particular field or profession. The purpose of terminology in the medical field:

  1. Facilitate communication.
  2. Increase precision.
  3. Reduce confusion.
  4. Reduce medical errors.

Anatomical terminology is a form of scientific words used by anatomists and medical professionals.
Anatomical terminology is composed of:

  1. Anatomical Position
  2. Regional Terms
  3. Directional Terms
  4. Body Planes
  5. Body Cavities and Serous Membranes

üßç Anatomical Position

Anatomical Position: For precision, there is standard way to view the human body anatomically. It is that of the human body standing upright position and facing forward, with straight, parallel feet and at shoulder width with toes directed forward, the upper limbs are held out to each side, and the palms facing forward with extended fingers and thumb points anteriorly (Figure 2.1).

ü©∫ Abdominal Regions

Abdominal Regions: Due to complexity of abdominal components, it is divided into either nine regions or four quadrants; these divisions are used by medical professionals to describe the location of internal organs.

The four abdominal quadrants:

  • Two right and left upper quadrants
  • Two right and left lower quadrants (Figure 2.8).

Contents of Abdominal Quadrants

Each abdominal quadrant holds its own organs as follows:

Right upper quadrant:

  1. The right lobe of the liver
  2. The gallbladder
  3. The pylorus of the stomach
  4. The entire duodenum
  5. The head of the pancreas
  6. The right kidney and the right suprarenal gland
  7. The distal ascending colon
  8. The hepatic flexure of the colon
  9. The right half of the transverse colon

Right Lower Quadrant:

  1. The majority of the ileum
  2. The cecum and vermiform appendix
  3. The proximal ascending colon
  4. The proximal right ureter

Left upper quadrant:

  1. The left lobe of the liver
  2. The spleen
  3. The stomach
  4. The jejunum
  5. The proximal ileum
  6. The body and the tail of the pancreas
  7. The left kidney and the left suprarenal gland
  8. The left half of the transverse colon
  9. The splenic flexure of the colon
  10. The superior part of the descending colon

Left lower quadrant:

  1. The distal descending colon
  2. The sigmoid colon
  3. The left ureter

The nine abdominal regions:

The nine regions are two hypochondrial, two lumbar, two inguinal, one epigastric, one umbilical and one hypogastric.

  1. Hypochondriac regions: are the two upper right and left regions below the cartilage of the ribs that extend over the abdomen.
  2. Epigastric region: is located above the stomach.
  3. Lumbar regions: are the two middle right and left regions near the waist.
  4. Umbilical region: is located on middle of distance between xiphoid process and pubic symphysis.
  5. Hypogastric region: is located below the stomach
  6. Iliac regions: are located on either side of the hypogastric regions, and below the lumbar regions. (Figure 2.9).

‚¨ÖÔ∏è Directional Terms

Directional Terms: These terms are essential for describing the relative locations of different body structures:

  1. Anterior (or ventral): describes the front or direction toward the front of the body.
  2. Posterior (or dorsal): describes the back or direction toward the back of the body.
  3. Superior (or cranial): describes a position above or higher than another part of the body
  4. Inferior (or caudal): describes a position below or lower than another part of the body.
  5. Lateral: describes the side or direction toward the side of the body.
  6. Medial: describes the middle or direction toward the middle of the body.
  7. Proximal: describes a position in a limb that is nearer to the point of attachment or the trunk of the body.
  8. Distal: describes a position in a limb that is farther from the point of attachment or the trunk of the body.
  9. Superficial: describes a position closer to the surface of the body.
  10. Deep: describes a position farther from the surface of the body (Figure 2.10).

üìê Anatomical Planes

Anatomical Planes: a plane is an imaginary two-dimensional surface that passes through the body (Figure 2.11). There are three planes commonly referred to in anatomy and medicine:

  1. The sagittal plane.
  2. The coronal plane.
  3. The transverse plane.

The Sagittal Plane: is the plane that divides the body or an organ vertically into right and left sides, it is known as midsagittal plane when it divides the body into two equal right and left sides, and its known parasagittal plane when it divides the body into unequal right and left sides (Figures 2.12 & 2.13).

The Coronal Plane: is the plane that divides the body or an organ into an anterior (front) portion and a posterior (rear) portion. The coronal plane is often referred to as frontal plane (Figures 2.14 & 2.15).

The Transverse plane: also known as axial plane, the transverse plane is the plane that divides the body or organ horizontally into upper and lower portions. Transverse planes produce images referred to as cross sections (Figure 2.16 & 2.17).

üï≥Ô∏è Body Cavities:

The body maintains its internal organization by means of membranes, sheaths, and other structures that separate the body into two main compartments:

  1. The dorsal (posterior) cavity.
  2. The ventral (anterior) cavity.

These cavities contain and protect delicate internal organs, and allow for significant changes in the size and shape of the organs as they perform their functions. The lungs, heart, stomach, and intestines, for example, can expand and contract without distorting other tissues or disrupting the activity of nearby organs.
The dorsal (posterior) cavity: this cavity is subdivided into two main cavities:

  1. The cranial cavity houses the brain.
  2. The spinal cavity (vertebral cavity) encloses the spinal cord.

The cranial and spinal cavities are continuous. The brain and spinal cord are protected by the bones of the skull and vertebral column and by cerebrospinal fluid.
The anterior (ventral) cavity: it has two main subdivisions:

  1. The thoracic cavity: is the superior subdivision of the anterior cavity
  2. The abdominopelvic cavity: is the inferior subdivision of the anterior cavity.

The thoracic cavity is enclosed by the rib cage and contains the lungs and the heart, which is located in the mediastinum. The diaphragm forms the floor of the thoracic cavity and separates it from the inferior abdominopelvic cavity.
The abdominopelvic cavity: is the largest cavity in the body, it is divided into two part the abdominal part houses the digestive organs and the pelvic part houses the organs of reproduction (Figures 2.18 & 2.19).
