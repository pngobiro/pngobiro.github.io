ü¶¥ The Pelvic Girdle

The pelvic girdle is formed by the two hip bones; it connects the bones of the lower limb to the axial skeleton. The bones are firmly attached to the axial skeleton via the sacrum of the vertebral column; they also converge anteriorly to attach to each other. The pelvic girdle is part of the bony pelvis which is composed of the two hip bones, the sacrum and the coccyx (Figure 11.1). The bones of the pelvis are strongly attached to each other to form a strong immobile, weight-bearing structure providing a stable foundation for the upper body as the pelvis transfers the body weight from the vertebral column, through the pelvic girdle into the lower limbs.

02

ü¶¥ The Hip Bone

The right and left hip bones (also known as the innominate bones) are two irregularly shaped bones that form the main component of the pelvic girdle, each hip bone is comprised of the three parts;

  1. The ilium.
  2. The pubis.
  3. The ischium.

There is a cup shaped socket located at the middle region between ilium, pubis and ischium known as the acetabulum (a Latin ward means "vinegar cup"), where the head of the femur articulates to form the hip joint, hip bone has two more articulations beside the hip joint; the right and left hip bones articulate with each other anteriorly to form the symphysis pubis, the two hip bones articulate with the sacrum posteriorly to form the sacroiliac joint (Figures 11.2 & 11.3).

The Ilium:

The ilium is the widest, largest and the superior of the three parts of the hip bone. The body of the ilium forms the superior part of the acetabulum (acetabular roof). Immediately above the acetabulum, the ilium expands to form the wing.
The ilium has two surfaces provide muscular attachment. The inner surface has a concave shape produces the iliac fossa. The external surface has a convex shape.
The ilium has a superior thickened margin known as the iliac crest; it extends anteriorly from a bony prominence known as the anterior superior iliac spine (ASIS) to a posterior bony prominence known as the posterior superior iliac spine (PSIS). There is an indentation on the posteroinferior aspect of the ilium known as the greater sciatic notch; it lies between the posterior inferior iliac spine (above), and the ischial spine (below).

The Pubis:

The Pubis: is the most anterior portion of the hip bone. It consists of:

  1. A body.
  2. A superior ramus.
  3. An inferior ramus (ramus means branch.)

The Pubic body: is located medially, it articulates with the opposite pubic body at the pubic symphysis. Its superior aspect is marked by a rounded thickening known as the pubic crest, which extends laterally as the pubic tubercle.
The Superior pubic ramus: extends laterally from the body to form part of the acetabulum.
The Inferior pubic ramus: projects towards the ischium. Together, the superior and inferior rami enclose part of the Obturator foramen, through which the obturator nerve, artery and vein pass through to reach the lower limb.

The Ischium:

The Ischium forms the posteroinferior part of the hip bone; it is composed of

  1. Ischial body.
  2. Ischial ramus.
  3. Ischial tuberosity.
  4. Ischial spine.

The ischial ramus combines with the inferior pubic ramus to enclose part of the obturator foramen.
The ischial tuberosity is located on the posteroinferior aspect of the ischium, when sitting; the body weight falls on right and left ischial tuberosities.
The ischial spine is a posteromedial projection located near the junction of the superior ramus and body.
