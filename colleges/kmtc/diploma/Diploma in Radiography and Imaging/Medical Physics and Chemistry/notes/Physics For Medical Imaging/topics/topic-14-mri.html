üåÄ The Spinning Proton

Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) exploits the magnetic properties of certain nuclei, primarily the hydrogen nucleus (proton), which is abundant in water and fat. Protons possess "spin," creating a small magnetic moment.

The Larmor Equation

In a strong external magnetic field ($B_0$), protons precess around the field lines at a specific frequency called the Larmor frequency ($f$):

$$f = \gamma B_0$$

Where $\gamma$ is the gyromagnetic ratio (42.6 MHz/Tesla for hydrogen). At 1.5 T, the frequency is approximately 63.9 MHz.

10.2

‚è±Ô∏è The MR Signal and Relaxation

When an RF pulse is applied at the Larmor frequency, protons absorb energy (resonance). After the pulse, they return to equilibrium, emitting a signal. This recovery is described by two relaxation times:

T1 Relaxation (Longitudinal)

The time taken for the longitudinal magnetization to recover. Also known as spin-lattice relaxation. T1 is longer in fluids (CSF) and shorter in fat.

T2 Relaxation (Transverse)

The time taken for the transverse magnetization to decay due to dephasing. Also known as spin-spin relaxation. T2 is always shorter than or equal to T1.

Spin-Echo Sequence: Uses a 90¬∞ pulse followed by a 180¬∞ rephasing pulse to produce an "echo" signal, minimizing the effects of magnetic field inhomogeneities ($T2^*$).

10.4

üìç Spatial Encoding

To create an image, the signal must be localized in three dimensions using magnetic field gradients:

  • 1 Slice Selection: A gradient is applied during the RF pulse so only a specific plane reaches resonance.
  • 2 Phase Encoding: A brief gradient alters the phase of protons across one axis of the slice.
  • 3 Frequency Encoding: Applied during signal readout to map positions along the final axis.

MRI does not use ionizing radiation, but involves three distinct safety concerns:

Safety Risks

  • Static Field ($B_0$): The Projectile Effect‚Äîferromagnetic objects can become lethal missiles. Contraindicated for patients with pacemakers or certain metallic implants.
  • Gradients: Rapidly switching fields can induce peripheral nerve stimulation or acoustic noise exceeding 100 dB.
  • Radiofrequency (RF): Causes tissue heating (Specific Absorption Rate - SAR). Metals in contact with skin can cause burns.
  • Quenching: The sudden loss of superconductivity can release large volumes of cryogenic helium gas, posing asphyxiation risks.
