2.1

üß¨ Interaction with Tissue

When ionizing radiations interact with living tissue, the absorption of radiation energy causes damage. Chemical changes are produced virtually immediately, followed by molecular damage (seconds to minutes) and eventually evident biological damage (hours to decades).

üîÑ

Indirect Damage Mechanism

Since tissue is 70‚Äì90% water, indirect damage is most common. Irradiation of water produces reactive hydroxyl free radicals:

$$\mathrm{H_2O} + \text{radiation} \rightarrow \mathrm{H_2O^+} + \mathrm{e^-}$$ $$\mathrm{H_2O^+} \rightarrow \mathrm{H^+} + \mathrm{OH}$$

The hydroxyl free radical (OH) is a powerful oxidizing agent that starts damaging chemical chain reactions in organic solute molecules like DNA.

2.2

üìê Radiation Protection Quantities

To account for radiation type and tissue sensitivity, two specific protection quantities are used, both measured in Sieverts (Sv):

Equivalent Dose

Derived from absorbed dose multiplied by a radiation weighting factor ($w_R$). It accounts for the Relative Biological Effectiveness (RBE) of different radiations.

  • X-rays, Gamma rays, Electrons: $w_R = 1$
  • Alpha particles: $w_R = 20$

Effective Dose

A quantity that incorporates tissue weighting factors ($w_T$) to account for varying radiosensitivities of different body organs. It is the primary index for comparing risk across different radiological procedures.

‚ò£Ô∏è Deterministic vs. Stochastic Effects

Deterministic (Non-Stochastic)

Characterized by a threshold dose. Below this, the effect does not occur. Once exceeded, severity increases with dose. Examples: Cataracts (5 Gy threshold), skin erythema.

Stochastic (Statistical)

No threshold dose. The probability of occurrence (not severity) increases with dose. Principal examples are cancer induction and hereditary effects.

2.4

‚öñÔ∏è UK Legislation and Dose Limits

Protection is governed by three main principles: Justification (benefit > risk), Optimization (ALARP), and Dose Limitation (legal limits).

‚ñ∂ IRR 1999: Staff and Public Protection

The Ionising Radiations Regulations 1999 focus on employers and employees. Key requirements include the appointment of a Radiation Protection Adviser (RPA) and Radiation Protection Supervisor (RPS).

Designated Areas:

  • Controlled Area: Special procedures required; dose likely to exceed 6 mSv/year.
  • Supervised Area: Conditions kept under review; dose likely to exceed 1 mSv/year.
‚ñ∂ IRMER 2000: Patient Protection

The Ionising Radiation (Medical Exposure) Regulations 2000 establish roles: Referrer, Practitioner (justifies exposure), and Operator (carries out practical aspects).
