5.1

üî¢ Digital Imaging

Radiological images in conventional systems are analog images. In digital radiography, the video signal or detector output is converted to digital form, allowing the image to be enhanced using a computer before display.

Digitization Process

The signal is applied to an analog-digital converter (ADC) or digitizer. This samples the signal at regular intervals (e.g., 512 times along each of 512 scan lines). The voltage at each data point is expressed as a 10-bit binary number (levels 0 to 1023).

The Image Matrix

The image is divided into a matrix of pixels. A 512 x 512 matrix with each pixel roughly 0.5 mm allows a resolution of 1 mm. Each memory location stores a 10-bit value representing brightness (grey level).

üíæ

Storage Calculation

A $512 \times 512$ matrix 10 bits deep requires:
$10 \times 512 \times 512$ bits = 320 kilobytes.

Increasing the matrix to $1024 \times 1024$ improves resolution by a factor of 2 but quadruples storage requirements.

üé® Image Processing and Manipulation

Windowing

Also called 'grey level mapping'. The digitized image contains 1024 intensity levels, but the human eye can only distinguish about 32 shades of grey. Windowing optimizes contrast by selecting a specific range of values for display.

  • Window Width: The range of CT/digital numbers displayed over the full grey scale.
  • Window Level: The central value (mid-point) of the window width.

Spatial Filtering

Low-pass filtering reduces noise but impairs resolution. High-pass filtering (Edge enhancement) emphasizes boundaries but increases noise.

Frame Averaging

Adding and averaging successive frames reduces random noise. SNR improves by the square root of the number of frames.

Background Subtraction

Subtracting a fixed number from each pixel to reduce the effect of scatter and veiling glare, thus increasing contrast.

üìÅ Storage and Recording

Digital data is stored in different tiers based on access speed and capacity:

  • RAM: Rapid access (microseconds), limited capacity.
  • Hard Disks: Medium-term storage, thousands of images.
  • Optical Disks / Archiving: Long-term storage (gigabytes/terabytes), lower cost but slower access.
