1.6.1

üß™ Absorbed Dose and Kerma

The absorbed dose is the energy deposited per unit mass of the stated material (in joules per kilogram). The SI unit of absorbed dose is the gray (Gy):

$$1 \text{ Gy} = 1 \text{ J kg}^{-1}$$

Absorbed dose rate is measured in grays per second. Before 1980, the international unit was the rad ($1 \text{ Gy} = 100 \text{ rad}$).

üí°

Kerma

Kerma is an acronym for kinetic energy released to matter. It is the energy transferred per unit mass of irradiated material, from photons to electrons at the specified position. For diagnostic energies, kerma and absorbed dose are effectively equal and can be used interchangeably.

1.6.3

üîå Measurement: The Ionization Chamber

Air kerma is commonly determined by measuring the amount of ionization produced by the photon beam in air. The instrument used is known as an ionization chamber.

Each X-ray photon absorbed in the wall liberates a secondary electron ($\mathrm{e}^{-}$), which produces ion pairs along its track. For each ion pair, approximately 34 eV of energy is deposited. A polarizing voltage (100‚Äì300 V) separates the ions before they can recombine, and the resulting current is measured by an electrometer.

Air-Equivalent Wall

The chamber wall must be air-equivalent, meaning its effective atomic number matches that of air ($Z \approx 7.6$), usually achieved with plastic coated with graphite. The wall must be thick enough to exclude electrons produced outside the chamber but not so thick as to attenuate the radiation being measured.

üìä Exposure and Dose-Area Product

Exposure

An obsolete quantity still found in older literature, exposure describes the quantity of ionization produced per unit mass of air. Its SI unit is coulombs per kilogram ($\mathrm{Ckg}^{-1}$). The legacy unit is the roentgen (R), where $1 \text{ R} \approx 10 \text{ mGy}$.

$$\text{Absorbed dose to air (Gy)} = 34 \times \text{Exposure (C kg}^{-1}\text{)}$$

Dose-Area Product (DAP)

Dose-area product meters are used for patient dose assessment. Mounted on the X-ray tube collimator, they use a parallel-plate ionization chamber larger than the beam size. The signal is proportional to both dose and area, measured in $\text{Gy cm}^2$.
