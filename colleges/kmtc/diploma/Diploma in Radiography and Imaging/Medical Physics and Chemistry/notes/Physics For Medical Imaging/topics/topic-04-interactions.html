üîÑ The Three Fates of a Photon

Figure 1.10 illustrates the three possible fates of the individual photons when a beam of X- or gamma rays travels through matter:

  • Transmitted: pass through unaffected, as primary or direct radiation.
  • Absorbed: transferring to the matter all of their energy (the photon disappearing completely).
  • Scattered: diverted in a new direction, with or without loss of energy, and so may leave the material as scattered or secondary radiation.
üé≤

Stochastic Process

X-ray absorption and scattering processes are stochastic processes governed by the statistical laws of chance. It is impossible to predict which individual photon will be transmitted, but the fraction of the total beam is precise due to the large number of photons.

1.4.1

üìâ Attenuation and HVL

For a narrow, monoenergetic beam, the fundamental law of X-ray attenuation states that equal thicknesses of an absorber transmit equal fractions (percentages) of the radiation entering them. This is an exponential law described by the equation:

$$I = I_{0}e^{-\mu d}$$

Where $I_0$ is the incident intensity, $\mu$ is the linear attenuation coefficient, and $d$ is the thickness.

Half-Value Layer (HVL)

The Half-Value Layer (HVL) is the thickness of material required to reduce the intensity of a narrow beam of radiation to one-half of its original value. It is a measure of the penetrating power (quality) of the beam.

$$\mu = \frac{0.693}{\mathrm{HVL}}$$

Linear Attenuation Coefficient

The HVL decreases (and $\mu$ increases) as:

  • The density of the material increases.
  • The atomic number (Z) of the material increases.
  • The photon energy of the radiation decreases.
1.4.2

üí• Compton Effect (Inelastic Scatter)

The photon bounces off a loosely bound or "free" electron, which recoils. The photon is scattered (diverted in a new direction) with reduced energy. This process is partial absorption.

Key Facts:

  • The probability ($\sigma$) is proportional to physical density and electron density.
  • It is independent of the atomic number ($Z$).
  • The greater the angle of scatter, the greater the loss of photon energy.
1.4.3

üåë Photoelectric Effect

A photon collides with a bound inner-shell (e.g. K-shell) electron. The photon is totally absorbed and the electron is ejected as a photoelectron.

$$KE_{\text{photoelectron}} = \text{Photon energy} - E_{\text{binding}}$$

This process is characterized by the subsequent emission of characteristic radiation as outer-shell electrons fall in to fill the vacancy.

üí°

Probability Factors

The probability ($\tau$) of the Photoelectric Effect is:

  • Proportional to $\mathbf{Z^3}$ (increases markedly with high atomic number).
  • Inversely proportional to $\mathbf{E^3}$ (decreases markedly as photon energy increases).
1.4.4

‚öñÔ∏è Relative Importance

The dominance of each effect depends on the photon energy and the atomic number of the material. The crossover point (where both effects are equal) occurs at:

  • 30 keV for air, water, and soft tissue.
  • 50 keV for bone and aluminium.
  • 300 keV for iodine and barium contrast.
