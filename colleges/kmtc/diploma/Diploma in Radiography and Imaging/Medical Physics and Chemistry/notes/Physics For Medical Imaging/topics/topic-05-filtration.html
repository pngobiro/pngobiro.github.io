üõ°Ô∏è Filtration

When a radiograph is taken, the lower-energy photons in the X-ray beam are mainly absorbed by and deposit dose in the patient. Only a small fraction, if any, reaches the film and contributes to the image.

The object of filtration is to remove a large proportion of the lower-energy photons before they reach the skin. This reduces the dose received by the patient while hardly affecting the radiation reaching the film, and so the resulting image.

Inherent Filtration

Photons are filtered within the target itself, the window of the tube housing, the insulating oil, and the glass insert. Typically 0.5‚Äì1.0 mm Al equivalent.

Added Filtration

A uniform flat sheet of metal (usually aluminium) interposed between the tube and patient. Typically 1.5 mm Al to reach a total of 2.5 mm Al.

Total Filtration is the sum of added and inherent filtration. For general diagnostic radiology, it should be at least 2.5 mm Al equivalent.

02

üß™ Choice of Filter Material

The atomic number should be sufficiently high to make photoelectric absorption predominate, but not so high that the useful spectrum lies on the low-energy side of the absorption edge.

  • Aluminium (Z = 13): Suitable for most diagnostic X-ray beams.
  • Copper (Z = 29): More efficient for higher kV; requires an aluminium "backing filter" to absorb copper's characteristic 9 keV X-rays.
  • Molybdenum / Rhodium: Used in mammography for their specific K-edges (20 keV and 23 keV).
üìà

Effects of Increasing Filtration

  • Spectrum shrinks and moves to the right (beam hardening).
  • Minimum and effective photon energies increase.
  • Maximum photon energy remains unchanged.
  • Total output (area of spectrum) is reduced.

üìè Radiation Quantity and Quality

Radiation Quantity (Intensity)

Intensity or air kerma rate is the amount of radiation. It follows these proportionalities:

$$\text{Dose rate} \propto \frac{kV^2 \times mA}{F^2}$$

The total air kerma (Dose) is also proportional to exposure time:

$$\text{Dose} \propto \frac{kV^2 \times mAs}{F^2}$$

Radiation Quality (Penetrating Power)

Specified as the HVL of the beam in a stated material (usually Aluminium) or by the effective energy. The HVL and effective energy:

  • Increase as applied kV increases.
  • Increase as filtration increases.
  • Are unaffected by mA, mAs, or distance from the target.

A 'hard' beam is produced by high kV and thick filters; a 'soft' beam by low kV and thin filters.
