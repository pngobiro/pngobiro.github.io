üß™ The X-Ray Tube

X-rays are produced when fast-moving electrons are suddenly stopped by impact on a metal target. The kinetic energy of the electrons is converted into X-rays (no more than 1%) and into heat ($\ge 99\%$).

An X-ray tube consists of two electrodes sealed into an evacuated glass envelope:

  • A negative electrode (cathode) that incorporates a fine tungsten coil or filament.
  • A positive electrode (anode) that incorporates a smooth flat metal target, usually of tungsten.

Thermionic Emission

The filament is heated by passing an electrical current through it to a temperature at which it is white hot (incandescent). In this state, it emits electrons by the process of thermionic emission. At such high temperatures ($\sim 2200^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$), atomic and electronic motion enables free electrons to leave the surface.

üéØ Processes in the Target

Interaction with the K-shell: Characteristic Radiation

An electron from the filament collides with an electron in the K-shell of a target atom. If the energy of the bombarding electron is greater than the binding energy of the shell, the K-electron is ejected. The resulting 'hole' is filled by an electron falling from an outer shell (e.g., L or M shell).

This process results in the emission of a single X-ray photon of energy equal to the difference in binding energies ($E_{\mathrm{K}} - E_{\mathrm{L}}$). These photons have discrete energies, forming a line spectrum.

Interaction with the Nucleus: Bremsstrahlung

When an electron passes near the nucleus of a tungsten atom, the positive charge of the nucleus deflects and slows it down. The kinetic energy lost is emitted directly as a photon of Bremsstrahlung ("braking radiation").

Bremsstrahlung produces a continuous spectrum of energies up to a maximum equal to the tube kilovoltage ($kVp$). Except in mammography, 80% or more of the X-rays emitted are Bremsstrahlung.

üìä The X-Ray Spectrum

The X-ray spectrum plots the relative number of photons against their energy in keV. The area of the spectrum represents the total output.

Controlling the Spectrum

  • Increasing kV: Shifts spectrum up and to the right; increases maximum/effective energy and total photon count.
  • Increasing mA: Increases the output of both Bremsstrahlung and characteristic radiation proportionately without changing the shape.
