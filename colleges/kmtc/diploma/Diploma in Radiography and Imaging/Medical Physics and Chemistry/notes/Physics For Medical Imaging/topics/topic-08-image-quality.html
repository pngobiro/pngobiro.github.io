3.1

üë§ Attenuation of X-Rays by the Patient

In conventional projection radiography, a fairly uniform beam of X-radiation falls on the patient and is differentially absorbed by the tissues of the body. The X-ray beam emerging from the patient carries a pattern of intensity dependent on the thickness and composition of organs.

The imaging system requires a minimum dose to produce a satisfactory image, known as the exit dose. The entrance dose (skin proximal to the tube) must be much higher due to attenuation:

  • PA Chest: 10 times greater than exit dose.
  • AP Abdomen/Skull: 100 times greater.
  • Lateral Pelvis: 1000 times greater.
üìè

Effect of FFD on Patient Dose

Increasing the focus-film distance (FFD) reduces the dose to the patient. At a longer FFD, the beam area is larger, spreading the photons over a larger surface area of skin, thereby producing a lower skin dose.

3.2

üåì Subject Contrast

Subject contrast ($C$) depends on the thickness ($t$) of a structure and the difference in linear attenuation coefficients ($\mu_1 - \mu_2$) of the tissues involved:

$$C \propto (\mu_1 - \mu_2)t$$
  • Contrast between bone and muscle is large but decreases as tube kV increases due to reduced photoelectric absorption.
  • Contrast between fat and muscle is small and remains relatively constant as kV increases.
  • Contrast between air and tissue is mainly due to the large difference in density.
3.3

üå´Ô∏è Effect of Scattered Radiation

Primary radiation ($P$) carries information; scattered radiation ($S$) obscures it. The ratio $S/P$ can be 4:1 for a PA chest and 9:1 for a lateral pelvis. Scattered radiation acts like a veil, reducing contrast by a factor of $(1 + S/P)$.

üõ†Ô∏è

Measures to Control Scatter

  1. Collimation: Reduces the volume of scattering tissue.
  2. Compression: Moves tissues laterally, reducing volume.
  3. Lower kV: Produces less forward scatter.
  4. Secondary Radiation Grids: Absorb oblique scattered rays.
  5. Air Gap Technique: Moving the film 30cm from the patient causes scatter to miss the detector.
3.4

üèÅ Secondary Radiation Grids

A grid consists of thin lead strips ($0.07\text{ mm}$) separated by radiolucent interspace material. It allows primary rays to pass while absorbing oblique scattered rays.

$$\text{Grid Ratio} = \frac{\text{depth of channel}}{\text{width of channel}}$$

Typical ratios are 8:1; higher ratios (12:1, 16:1) are more efficient at absorbing scatter but require higher patient dose (Bucky factor).

3.6

üëì Unsharpness and Blurring

Three types of unsharpness limit resolution:

  • Geometrical Blurring ($B_g$): Caused by the finite size of the focal spot ($f$). $B_g = \frac{fh}{F}$ where $h$ is object-film distance and $F$ is FFD.
  • Movement Blurring ($B_m$): Caused by patient or organ motion during exposure. $B_m \approx vt$ (velocity $\times$ time).
  • Absorption Unsharpness: Caused by gradual absorption at the edges of rounded or tapered structures.
$$B_{total} = \sqrt{B_g^2 + B_m^2 + B_s^2}$$

Minimum total blurring occurs when individual blurring components are approximately equal.
