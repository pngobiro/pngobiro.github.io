6.1

üî¶ The Image Intensifier (II)

Fluoroscopy provides real-time X-ray imaging. The Image Intensifier is a vacuum tube used to increase the brightness of the image while maintaining its proportionality with X-ray beam intensity.

Key Components

  • Input Phosphor (CsI): Absorbs ~60% of X-ray energy and converts it into light. Cesium Iodide is used for its high Z (53/55) and needle-like crystal structure that reduces light diffusion.
  • Photocathode: A photoemissive metal that emits photoelectrons in numbers proportional to screen brightness.
  • Electrostatic Lenses: Focus the electron beam and minify the image onto the output phosphor.
  • Output Phosphor: Silver-activated zinc-cadmium sulfide converts the electron pattern back into light.
02

‚ö° Brightness Gain

The total brightness gain is typically 5,000‚Äì10,000 and arises from two sources:

$$\text{Brightness Gain} = \text{Minification Gain} \times \text{Flux Gain}$$

Minification Gain

Results from the reduction in image size from the large input phosphor to the small output phosphor. For a 1-inch output screen, it is the square of the input screen diameter.

$$\text{Gain}_m = \left(\frac{d_{input}}{d_{output}}\right)^2$$

Flux Gain

The increase in light photons due to the acceleration of electrons by the high-voltage potential (25-35 kV). Each electron produces ~50 light photons at the output screen.

03

üì∫ TV Systems and Scanning

The output image is typically viewed via a closed-circuit TV system. A Vidicon or Plumbicon camera scans the target and converts the image into an electrical video signal.

Interlaced Scanning

To avoid flicker, the electron beam scans the 525 lines in two passes (fields) 60 times per second. Even-numbered lines are scanned first, followed by odd-numbered lines. This creates 30 full frames per second.

‚öñÔ∏è Quality and Control

Quantum Mottle (Noise)

Due to statistical fluctuations in X-ray photons absorbed. Fluoroscopy is "dose-limited"; increasing the mA improves the SNR but increases patient dose.

Distortion and Vignetting

Pincushion distortion occurs at the periphery. Vignetting causes the center of the image to be brighter than the edges.

‚öôÔ∏è

Automatic Brightness Control (ABC)

A feedback system maintains constant brightness when moving between high and low attenuation areas. Mechanisms include:

  • kVp Variability: Fast response, broad range.
  • mA Variability: Simple but slow response (filament heating delay).
  • Pulse Width Variability: Used with grid-controlled tubes for rapid switching.
