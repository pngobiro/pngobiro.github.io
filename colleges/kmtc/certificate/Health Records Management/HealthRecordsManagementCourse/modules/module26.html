ðŸ“š Introduction to Electronic Health Records

Introduction to Electronic Health Records (EHRs) marks a significant shift in healthcare from traditional paper-based systems to digital management of patient information. EHRs are now integral to healthcare delivery worldwide. Key aspects of this introduction include:

INTRODUCTION TO ELECTRONIC HEALTH RECORDS

Global Trend Towards Computerization:

  • Worldwide Adoption: Any countries now have a number of computerized applications as part of a Health Information System (HIS) within the health care facility. Globally, there is a widespread trend towards adopting computerized systems in healthcare, with EHRs as a central component.
  • Health Information System (HIS) Integration: EHRs are typically part of a broader Health Information System (HIS), which encompasses various computerized applications designed to manage different aspects of healthcare operations.

Aim of Health Care Authorities:

  • Automated Patient Information Service: The aim of health care authorities around the world is for the development of an automated patient information service that will increase the efficient retrieval of information for patient care, statistics, research and teaching. The overarching goal is to develop and implement EHR systems that automate patient information management to enhance:
    • Efficient Information Retrieval: Quick and easy access to patient data for clinical and administrative purposes.
    • Patient Care: Improving the quality, safety, and effectiveness of patient care delivery.
    • Statistics and Reporting: Streamlining data collection and reporting for healthcare statistics and performance monitoring.
    • Research and Education: Facilitating medical research and providing robust educational resources for healthcare professionals.
  • Integration of Data Collection and Use: Health Information Systems are designed to integrate data collection, processing, reporting, and the use of information necessary for improving the effectiveness and efficiency of the health service through better management at all levels of health care (WHO, 2000). HIS, including EHRs, are designed to integrate all aspects of health information management, from data capture to application, aiming to improve:
    • Effectiveness of Health Services: Enhancing the quality and impact of healthcare services.
    • Efficiency of Health Services: Optimizing resource utilization and streamlining healthcare operations.
    • Better Management at All Levels: Improving decision-making and management across all levels of healthcare administration and clinical practice.

Important Consideration for EHR Implementation:

  • Prerequisite of Organized Manual Procedures: An important point to remember, however, is that the use of a fully computerized system may improve the effectiveness and efficiency of a Medical Record Department, but ONLY where the basic manual procedures are already in place organized. The success of EHR implementation is contingent upon having well-established and efficient manual systems in place first. A solid foundation of organized manual processes is crucial because:
    • EHRs Digitize Existing Processes: EHR systems are designed to digitize and enhance existing workflows. If manual processes are disorganized or inefficient, simply digitizing them will not solve underlying problems and may even amplify inefficiencies.
    • Process Optimization Before Automation: It is essential to streamline and optimize manual processes before automating them. This ensures that the EHR system is built upon a solid operational framework, maximizing its benefits and avoiding the digitization of flawed workflows.

In summary, the transition to EHRs represents a global move towards leveraging technology to enhance healthcare. However, the success of EHR implementation crucially depends on the presence of well-organized and efficient manual systems as a foundation.

EHR implementation is not just a technical upgrade but a significant organizational change that impacts workflows, roles, and culture across the healthcare facility.
  • Transformative Impact: Recognize that EHR implementation is a transformative project that will fundamentally alter how healthcare is delivered and managed within the institution.

Change Management Imperative:

  • Effective Change Management: and managing that change effectively and efficiently will be crucial to a successful outcome. Successful EHR adoption hinges on effective change management. This involves:
    • Planning for Change: Developing a structured change management plan that addresses potential resistance, communication strategies, and user training.
    • Effective Implementation: Ensuring that the implementation process is well-managed, with clear timelines, responsibilities, and support mechanisms.
    • Efficient Transition: Striving for a smooth transition from manual to electronic systems, minimizing disruptions to patient care and clinical workflows.

Strategies for EHR Preparation involve addressing key critical factors to ensure successful implementation. These include strategies for patient identification, documentation standards, and provider signatures:

Strategies should include the identification of critical factors to success.

  1. a) Patient Identification:
    • Essential Step: As mentioned many times, an essential step to be taken when preparing for the introduction of an EHR is to ensure that all patients are uniquely identified at all times. Accurate and unique patient identification is foundational for an EHR system.
    • Unique Patient Identifier (UPI): Some form of Unique Patient Identifier is essential to provide the linking mechanism that underpins the EHR. A UPI is crucial for linking all patient data within the EHR system, ensuring data integrity and accuracy.
    • National Patient Identification Numbers: In many countries, national patient identification numbers are already being used. If this is the case in your country, you are already on the first rung of the ladder to implementing an EHR. Utilizing national patient IDs, where available, greatly simplifies EHR implementation by providing a standardized, pre-existing identifier.
    • Addressing Lack of National ID: If it is not possibleto have a national identification number, the current system used for patient identification should be used and adapted if required but if there are problems with patient identification that need to be solved before moving forward. If a national ID is not available, healthcare facilities must:
      • Adapt Existing Systems: Enhance and adapt current patient identification systems within the facility.
      • Solve Identification Issues First: Address and resolve any existing issues with patient identification accuracy and uniqueness before EHR implementation proceeds.
    • Patientsâ€™ Master Index (PMI): As previously mentioned, identifying information should be stored ina Patientsâ€™ Master Index (PMI) and would include demographic information such as:. A robust PMI is essential for managing patient identities within the EHR. Key demographic data to include in the PMI are:
      • a unique patient identification number
      • medical record/hospital number
      • date of birth
      • sex
      • address
      • And other specific demographic information.
    • Automation of PMI: Currently, many institutions have already automated their PMI. If this is not the case, an electronic patientsâ€™ master index system is essential when considering implementing an electronic health record. Automation of the PMI is highly recommended. An electronic PMI system improves:
      • Efficiency: Speeds up patient lookup and verification processes.
      • Accuracy: Reduces manual errors in patient identification.
      • Integration: Facilitates seamless integration with other EHR components.
  2. b) Documentation Standards for Information Exchange:
    • Need for Standards: Just as there are a set of standards for manual medical records and medical record services, there also need to be standards in electronic health records systems. Standardized documentation is crucial for interoperability, data exchange, and consistent data quality in EHRs.
    • Steering Committee Role: The Steering Committee needs to ensure that standards are in place to address definitions of data to be exchanged, the timing of the exchange, the management and integration of data to support patient care, and the evaluation of healthcare services. The Steering Committee plays a vital role in:
      • Defining Data Standards: Establishing standards for data definitions, formats, and terminologies used within the EHR.
      • Exchange Protocols: Setting protocols for data exchange between different systems and departments.
      • Data Integration: Planning for the management and integration of data to support comprehensive patient care.
      • Quality Evaluation: Ensuring standards support the evaluation of healthcare services and outcomes.
    • Accredited Standard-Developing Organizations: There are several accredited standard-developing organizations operating in the international healthcare industry, the most well-known being Health Level 7. This organization and the standards they develop are known as HL7. These standards are developed to provide a structure that defines data and data elements and specifies how data is coded. Consider adopting standards developed by recognized organizations like Health Level Seven International (HL7). HL7 standards:
      • Provide Structure: Offer frameworks for defining data and data elements in health records.
      • Specify Data Coding: Standardize how data is coded and represented for interoperability.
      • Promote Interoperability: Enable disparate healthcare applications to exchange key sets of data effectively.
    • HL7 Specifications and Benefits: Specifications developed by HL7 include the widely used messaging standard that enables disparate healthcare applications to exchange key sets of clinical and administrative data. Such standards have been developed specifically to create flexible, cost-effective approaches, standards, guidelines, methodologies and related services for interoperability between healthcare information system(HL7 2005). Theuseofinternationallyacceptedstandardsinindividualapplications will improve the integration of the application with other applications in the system. Using HL7 and similar international standards offers benefits such as:
      • Flexible and Cost-Effective Approaches: Promote efficient and scalable EHR implementations.
      • Guidelines and Methodologies: Provide established guidelines, methodologies, and services for interoperability.
      • Improved Integration: Enhance the integration of the EHR with other healthcare applications within and outside the institution.
    • Decision on Standards Organization: A decision needs to be made as to which, if any, standards organization the institution/government will use. In many commercial EHR systems standards are already imbedded within the program. Decide which standards organization to follow, if any. Many commercial EHR systems already incorporate industry standards, simplifying compliance.
  3. c) Incorporating Provider Signatures:
    • Signature Requirement: In an electronic health record, as in manual records, treatment and medication orders must be signed to ensure accountability and legal validity.
    • Authenticity Importance: The authenticity of an electronic signature is extremely important particularly when the record is used for documentary evidence in legal cases. Verifying the authenticity of provider signatures is crucial for legal and evidentiary purposes.
    • Electronic System Authenticity Features: In an electronic system, authenticity is often easier with documents automatically stamped with date, time, and user identification. Some systems use the providerâ€™s password as verification of their signature. This is accomplished by requesting that the password be entered a second time for verification. EHR systems employ various methods to ensure signature authenticity:
      • Automatic Stamping: Automatically stamping documents with date, time, and user ID.
      • Password Verification: Using password re-entry as a form of signature verification.
    • Digital Signatures and Cryptography: Some countries do not see this as sufficient and plan to use a digital signature created cryptographically. Cryptography keeps data secret through mathematical or logical functions that transform intelligible data into seemingly unintelligible data and back again so as to authenticate the user and provide non-repudiation. That is, it is a process that enables positive identification of the sender of a computer message so that the sender cannot deny sending the message. For advanced security, consider using cryptographic digital signatures. Cryptography offers:
      • Enhanced Security: Superior security through data encryption and authentication protocols.
      • Non-Repudiation: Ensures that providers cannot deny their signatures, providing strong legal validity.
