ðŸ“š Contents of the Unit Health Record: 1. The Folder

The folder itself is the foundational component of a unit health record. Selecting the right folder is crucial for durability, organization, and ease of use. Key considerations when choosing a folder include:

  • Strength of Manila: The folder should be made of tough manila to withstand frequent handling and long-term storage. Manila folders are preferred for their durability and resistance to wear and tear.
  • Method of Fastening Documents: The folder must have an efficient mechanism to securely fasten documents inside. Common methods include:
    • Gusset and Fold: Folders with a gusset (an expandable section) and single or double fold inside to accommodate a growing volume of documents.
    • Threaded Prongs: Metal or plastic prongs threaded through the folder to hold loose sheets securely. These prongs should be robust and easy to use for adding or removing documents.
  • Clarity of Numbering on the Outside Cover: The unit number on the folder's cover must be clearly and legibly printed for easy identification and retrieval. Numbering should be durable and not easily fade or rub off.
  • Cost of the Folder: While quality is paramount, cost-effectiveness is also a consideration, especially for large healthcare facilities. The folder should be affordable while meeting the required standards of durability and functionality.

A well-chosen folder ensures that the health record is protected, organized, and easily accessible, contributing to efficient health information management.

ðŸ“š Contents of the Unit Health Record: 2. Identification Sheet

The front sheet of identification is the first page within the unit health record folder and serves as a summary of key patient information. Its purpose and content include:

  • Area for Social Details: This sheet is designated for transcribing all the patient's social details collected during the registration process. The information is typically derived from a pre-registration form.
  • Inside Front Cover Usage: The inside front cover of the folder is often used for this identification sheet to make it immediately visible upon opening the record.
  • Key Information Included: The identification sheet typically contains:
    • Patient's full name
    • Date of birth
    • Unit number
    • Address
    • Contact information
    • Emergency contact details
    • Insurance information
  • Space for Updates: It is essential to provide space on this sheet to record changes over time, such as:
    • Changes of Address: Allowing for multiple address entries to keep the record current.
    • Change of Doctor: Recording any changes in the primary care physician or consultant overseeing the patient's care.
  • Consultant Listing Section: Often, a section is included to list the consultants whose clinics the patient has attended or the wards they have been admitted to. This provides a quick overview of the patient's healthcare providers within the facility.
  • Visibility and Accuracy: The information on the identification sheet should be readily visible and consistently accurate. This ensures that doctors and staff can quickly grasp essential patient details without sifting through documents.
  • Letter Addressing Information: Accurate details on this sheet ensure that any correspondence, such as letters to the doctor or patient, is correctly addressed and delivered.

The identification sheet is a critical reference point within the health record, ensuring quick access to essential patient information and facilitating efficient communication and record management.

ðŸ“š Contents of the Unit Health Record: 3. Clinical History Sheet

The clinical history sheet is a core component of the health record, used to document the patient's medical history, encounters, and clinical progress. Two main types of clinical history sheets are commonly used:

  • Individual Consultant History Sheets:
    • Consultant-Specific Sheets: Each consultant uses their own history sheets, on which only they will write. This approach is discipline-specific, allowing specialists to document findings and treatments relevant to their area of expertise.
    • Multiple Sheets in Record: A patient attending multiple clinics (e.g., three different clinics) will have separate history sheets for each, although these are typically filed together, often next to each other within the unit record.
    • Potential Disadvantages: This method can lead to fragmented records if not carefully managed, with information spread across multiple sheets, potentially making it harder to get a holistic view of the patient's overall condition.
  • Continuous History Sheet:
    • Single Sheet for All Entries: A continuous history sheet is designed for all doctors to write on as they see the patient. This creates a chronological record of all clinical encounters in one place.
    • Chronological Statement: This method achieves a chronological statement about the patientâ€™s health journey, which is considered advantageous for tracking the progression of illness and treatments over time.
    • Identification of Clinics: When using continuous history sheets, it is important to clearly identify which clinic or service each entry pertains to. This can be done by:
      • Coloured Rubber Date/Clinic Stamps: Using different coloured stamps to indicate the date and clinic for each entry, making it easy to differentiate between visits and specialties at a glance.

The choice between individual consultant history sheets and continuous history sheets depends on the healthcare facility's preferences, workflow, and the need for integrated versus specialty-focused documentation.

ðŸ“š Contents of the Unit Health Record: 4. Continuation Sheet to 10. Consent Forms

  1. Continuation sheet:
    • Used when a patient's hospital episode is brief and can be documented on a single history sheet, though this is not common.
    • Similar to history sheets but without consultant or clinic-specific identification, allowing any consultant to use them.
  2. Prescription chart:
    • Provides a formal space for doctors to prescribe drugs for both in and out-patients.
    • The form must be returned from pharmacy to the case folder so that all medications are tracked.
  3. Surgical operation sheet:
    • Specialized forms for recording surgical operations, including sections for:
      • Names of all surgeons and anaesthetists involved.
      • Details of the procedure.
      • Special remarks about drainage, blood loss, and recovery treatment.
  4. Anaesthetic record:
    • Detailed forms for recording anaesthetic techniques, which have become increasingly sophisticated.
    • Contains substantial detail filled in by the anaesthetist during and immediately after the operation.
  5. Temperature, pulse and respiration (TPR) chart:
    • Two common versions:
      • b.d. chart for twice-daily routine observations.
      • Separate chart for 4-hourly recordings for seriously ill patients.
    • Urine and stool details are typically recorded at the foot of the TPR chart.
    • Often printed on coloured paper or in coloured ink to be easily noticeable.
  6. Report mount sheets:
    • Used for filing reports smaller than standard form sizes, mounted to keep them organized.
    • Vary in design and gumming methods, including options with gummed edges or strips of self-adhesive gum protected with greased paper.
    • Reports are filed overlapping slightly to keep dates visible, allowing many reports on one sheet.
  7. Consent Forms:
    • Written consent must be obtained for all operative procedures (technically assaults on the patient).
    • Consent is also required for sterilization procedures and post-mortem examinations.
    • Forms must be signed by the patient, or a relative or parent (if the patient is a child under 16).

ðŸ“š Contents of the Unit Health Record: 11. Report Forms to 13. In-Patient Summary

  1. Report Forms:
    • Include all investigation reports: laboratory (Haematology, biochemistry, bacteriology, histology), cardiology, and radiology.
    • These reports, often on separate sheets, must be fastened into patient notes, causing bulk and storage issues.
    • Some labs issue cumulative reports to consolidate information, but old reports must be removed to reduce bulk, which is often not done.
    • Computerized laboratory reports are an evolving solution but storage of even digital reports remains a consideration within EHR systems.
  2. Clinical photographs:
    • Taken when a patient has an unusual or interesting condition, or to record progress, especially in plastic surgery.
    • May be kept centrally in a Medical Photograph Department but are also frequently filed in the case folder.
    • Can be mounted on cards or kept in special plastic folders with pockets for slides or smaller photos.
  3. In-Patient Summary:
    • Dictated by a registrar or consultant after a patient is discharged, summarizing the hospital stay.
    • Includes salient points of the patientâ€™s progress, operations, treatments, and condition at discharge.
    • Often used as the hospitalâ€™s full report to the general practitioner and is the document used for diagnostic coding.

ðŸ“š Contents of the Unit Health Record: 14. Correspondences

  1. Correspondences:
    • Copies or originals of all communications related to the patient's care, including:
      • Letters from the general practitioner to the hospital doctor.
      • Responses from the hospital back to the general practitioner.
    • Include copies of the discharge note, traditionally written by a junior hospital doctor on the day of discharge.
    • These notes inform the general practitioner about the patient's hospital stay until the full discharge summary is available.

These documents, along with hundreds more depending on the hospital type and services, form the comprehensive case notes within a unit health record. Familiarity with these forms is essential for health records professionals.

ðŸ“š Introduction to Types of Health Records

Health records can be broadly categorized into different types based on the setting and purpose of their use. Understanding these types helps in managing and utilizing health information effectively. The main categories include:

  • Case records: Primarily for patients admitted to wards or those continuously attending consultant clinics.
  • Outpatient records: Used in Out-Patient Departments, such as Casualty, Ante-Natal, and Immunization clinics.
  • Diagnostic records: Notes and reports from Radiography, Pathology, Electrocardiography, and other investigations.

The following modules will further explore these types of health records and their specific values and uses in healthcare.

ðŸ“š Key Takeaways

  • A unit health record folder must be durable, made of tough manila, and have a secure fastening mechanism.
  • The front identification sheet contains key patient demographics and contact details, serving as a quick reference.
  • Clinical history sheets can be consultant-specific or continuous, each offering different advantages for documenting patient encounters.
  • Unit health records include various forms like prescription charts, operation sheets, anaesthetic records, TPR charts, report mount sheets, and consent forms, each serving a specific documentation purpose.
  • Report forms, clinical photographs, and in-patient summaries are also crucial components, along with correspondences that ensure communication continuity between healthcare providers.
  • Health records are categorized into case records, outpatient records, and diagnostic records, based on their primary use and patient setting.
