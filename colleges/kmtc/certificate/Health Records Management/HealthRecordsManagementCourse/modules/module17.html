ðŸ“š Types of Waiting Lists: (ii) Decentralized Waiting List

A Decentralized Waiting List contrasts with the centralized system by distributing waiting list management across different departments or clinics. Key features, advantages, and disadvantages include:

(ii) Decentralized waiting list: The decentralized waiting list will be maintained in several places, possibly each consultantâ€™s secretary, or an individual wards or individual departments. Decentralization disperses waiting list management to various clinical areas.

Advantages of decentralised waiting list:

  1. Consultant Convenience:
    • No Need to Visit Central Office: Consultants need not go to the central office to select their patients from the waiting list. Decentralization allows consultants to manage their waiting lists directly from their clinics or offices, saving time and improving convenience.
  2. Personalized Patient Knowledge:
    • Secretary-Patient Familiarity: The secretaries get familiar with the patients and can call them by name. Clinic-based staff develop a personal relationship with patients on their waiting list, enhancing communication and patient experience.

Decentralized systems are particularly beneficial in fostering personalization and consultant autonomy in managing patient schedules.

Disadvantages of decentralised waiting list:

  1. Resource Duplication and Inefficiency:
    • Staff Deployment: Several staff will be deployed in the maintenance of the waiting list in different areas, leading to potential duplication of effort.
    • Cost Inefficiency: It will be very expensive since each department will need to use its own equipment. Duplication of equipment and resources across multiple locations increases operational costs.
  2. Lack of Central Oversight and Coordination:
    • Decentralized Data: Data is spread across different locations, making it harder to get a comprehensive overview of hospital-wide waiting lists.
    • Coordination Challenges: A clerk will have to walk to the central records department to check for admissions and discharges from the daily returns. Checking for admissions and discharges becomes more complex and time-consuming as it requires manual coordination between decentralized units and central records.

Decentralized waiting lists, while offering personalization benefits, can lead to inefficiencies, higher costs, and challenges in maintaining a unified overview of waiting list data.

ðŸ“š Creation of the Waiting List Record

The Creation of the Waiting List Record is the initial step in adding a patient to the waiting list. This process is typically initiated at outpatient clinics, though patients may also be added from other sources. Common methods for creating waiting list records include:

Most of the waiting list records are initiated at the out-patient clinics. Some other patients may come from another hospital and be put in the waiting list for another different hospital, where there are more beds or facilities. There are four ways in which information can be conveyed to the waiting list:-

  1. (a) Card:
    • Dedicated Card per Patient: The card is created for every patient who is to be put on the waiting list. A physical card is generated for each patient added to the list.
    • Filing as Part of Waiting List: This card will be filed and form part of the waiting list, serving as the primary record for each patient on the list.
  2. (b) Nurse or Doctor List:
    • List Submission: The nurse or doctor may send a list of patients to be included in the waiting list to the records department for action. Healthcare providers submit lists of patients who require admission or day case treatment.
    • Action by Records Department: Upon receiving the list, the records department processes and officially adds these patients to the waiting list.
  3. (c) Letter from Consultant:
    • Consultant Referral Letter: A consultant in one hospital may wish to include his patientâ€™s name in his waiting list, in another hospital for his name to be included in that waiting list. Consultants may refer patients to other facilities and initiate waiting list entries via formal letters.
    • Letter Content: In this letter he will in clued diagnosis, and priority for admission. The referral letter typically includes essential details such as the patient's diagnosis and the urgency or priority of admission.
    • Case Folder Creation: A case folder will then be created for this patient. Upon receiving a consultant's letter, a preliminary case folder may be initiated for the patient, even before admission.
    • Information in Record: The information to be included in this record are: the patientâ€™s name, address, title, telephone number, holiday dates, diagnosis, operation to be carried out, duration of stay in the hospital, name of the surgeon, or consultant. The record will contain comprehensive patient details, medical information, and planned treatment specifics to facilitate waiting list management and subsequent care.

These methods ensure that patient information is accurately captured and formally entered into the waiting list system, regardless of the referral source.

ðŸ“š Method of Filing Waiting Lists

Effective Method of Filing is crucial for managing waiting lists, which often contain dozens to hundreds of names. A well-organized filing system ensures that the waiting list is easy to use, update, and access. A typical waiting list filing system needs two main components:

  1. Alphabetical Index:
    • Alphabetical Order: Alphabetical index of the names of all patients on the waiting list. This index is primarily organized alphabetically by patient name for easy lookup.
    • Card-Based System: Typically, this index is maintained using cards, with one card per patient, filed alphabetically.
    • Non-Removal Until Admission: These cards should not be removed until the patient is admitted. Cards remain in the alphabetical index until the patient is admitted, at which point they are removed to reflect their admission status.
  2. Consultants List:
    • Chronological and Ward-Based Lists: Consultants list will make provision for date, time, and ward. These lists are often organized chronologically by date and time of expected admission and further categorized by ward or consultant.
    • Priority Indication: These lists will have priorities indicated â€“ routine, soon, and urgent. Priority codes or indicators are included to differentiate between routine, soon-to-be-admitted, and urgent cases, helping in bed allocation and scheduling.
    • Dual File System Purpose: These two files should be able to answer an enquiry from a patient and consultant. The combination of an alphabetical index and consultant-specific lists ensures that the waiting list system can efficiently respond to queries from both patients and healthcare providers, providing necessary information on waiting times and admission statuses.

This dual-file approach ensures that the waiting list is both alphabetically accessible for general patient lookups and chronologically/departmentally organized for effective operational management and consultant use.

The Filing Equipment for waiting lists should be chosen based on the size of the list, space availability, and the need for accessibility and ease of updating. Common types of filing equipment include:

  1. (a) Visible edge card filed in trays:
    • Manila Flaps with Rigid Bar: When this is used to maintain a waiting list may be in the form of manila flaps, with a rigid bar at the top. Visible edge card systems often use manila flaps with a rigid top bar for organized filing.
    • Transparent Plastic Pocket: and a transparent plastic pocket about half an inch deep at the lower edge of each flap. Each flap has a transparent pocket at the bottom edge for inserting cards.
    • Card Insertion: A card is inserted into this pocket and the flap is held in a metal tray in such a way that all the information in the plastics is visible. Cards are inserted into these pockets and then hung in metal trays in a way that the information on the card, especially at the visible edge, remains viewable without pulling out the card.
    • Visible Information: Date is put on the list, diagnosis, operation and admission priority is put to allow the consultant to select his patients. Key information such as date of listing, diagnosis, planned operation, and admission priority are placed at the visible edge of the card, enabling quick scanning and consultant selection of patients based on urgency and medical needs.
  2. (b) Strip index:
    • Limited Space: A strip index is limited in space so that no space is left for change of address. Strip indexes are compact but offer limited space for detailed information or updates.
    • Suitability for Short Lists: It can only be used if the list is not too long, making it suitable for smaller waiting lists.
    • Previously Mentioned Equipment: It has already been mentioned as one of the equipment used in the maintenance of the patient master index card, indicating its versatility for different types of indices.
  3. (c) Diaries:
    • Consultant Diaries: Each consultant could have a diary for his patients put on the waiting list according to dates. Diaries can be used to organize waiting lists by date, potentially managed by individual consultants.
    • Risk of Oversight: Its danger is that a patient can easily be missed or overlooked on date which passed. Diaries pose a risk of oversight if not carefully managed, as patients might be missed if dates are overlooked or entries are not systematically reviewed.
  4. (d) Colour coding:
    • Priority Indication: Colour coding could be used to indicate soon, urgent and routine case. Different coloured cards or markers can be used to visually categorize cases by priority (routine, soon, urgent), aiding in quick identification and prioritization of admissions.

The choice of filing equipment should align with the volume of the waiting list, the level of detail needed, and the workflow preferences of the healthcare facility.

ðŸ“š Procedures for Admitting Patients from Waiting Lists

Procedures for admitting patients from waiting lists are systematic steps to ensure that patients are admitted in a fair and organized manner, based on clinical priority and waiting list order. Typical procedures include:

  1. 1. Patient Selection by Consultants:
    • Consultant Review: The patient is selected from the waiting list y the consultants. Consultants regularly review the waiting list to select patients for admission based on medical priority and available slots.
  2. 2. Patient Notification:
    • Clerk Communication: The clerk writes to the patient or telephones him inviting the patient to the hospital. Once a patient is selected, the clerk contacts the patient to inform them about the admission and provide necessary instructions.
  3. 3. Record Retrieval and Documentation:
    • File Retrieval: The records are got out from the files and sent to the documentation office. Patient health records are retrieved from storage and sent to the documentation office for pre-admission review and preparation.
  4. 4. Waiting List Card to Admission Office:
    • Card Transfer: The waiting list card is sent to the admission office to alert them of the impending admission.
    • Admission Office Checks: so that the patient is expected on the day he comes in. the admission office checks the record before the patients comes. When he comes, his details are checked and confirmed by the admission office. The admission office uses the waiting list card to prepare for the patient's arrival, verifies patient details, and confirms admission arrangements.
  5. 5. Patient Admission to Ward:
    • Formal Admission: Patient is admitted and sent to the ward after completing admission procedures in the admission office.
  6. 6. Removal from Waiting List Post-Discharge:
    • List Update: When the patient has been discharged, his name is removed from the waiting list. Discharged patients are promptly removed from the waiting list to keep it current and accurate.
  7. 7. Regular Waiting List Checks:
    • Periodic Checks: Certain checks are made on the waiting list to remove the names of the patient who have died to remove their names from the waiting list. Regular checks are conducted to identify and remove records of patients who are no longer waiting for admission due to death or other reasons, ensuring the list remains accurate.

7. Statistics:

  • Regular Returns for Analysis: Regular returns are compiled from the waiting list for hospital activity analysis. Data from the waiting list is used to generate reports and statistics for hospital activity analysis, capacity planning, and performance monitoring.
  • Application to Day Case Treatment: The procedures described above also apply to patients who come for day case treatment, ensuring consistent management of all scheduled treatments.
  • Special Letters for Day Cases: Special letters are sent to these patients because of preparations needed if general anaesthesia is to be given. Day case patients, especially those requiring general anaesthesia, receive specific instructions and preparations via special letters, ensuring they are adequately prepared for their procedures.

Following these procedures ensures a systematic and equitable approach to admitting patients from waiting lists, optimizing resource utilization and patient flow.
