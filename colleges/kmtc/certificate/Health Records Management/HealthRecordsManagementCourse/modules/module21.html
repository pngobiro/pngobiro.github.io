ðŸ“š Tracing Systems for Health Records

Tracing is a critical function in health records management, essential for tracking the location of health records when they are removed from their permanent filing location. Effective tracing systems ensure that records can be quickly retrieved and are not lost or misplaced. There are three systems of tracing most commonly used in a records library:

  1. (a) The common tracer card
  2. (b) Library tracer system
  3. (c) Personal Tracer Card

Each of these systems provides a method for tracking records, with varying levels of complexity and suitability depending on the healthcare setting and resources.

ðŸ“š Tracing Systems: (a) The Common Tracer Card

(a) The common tracer card system is widely used due to its simplicity and effectiveness in tracking health records that are temporarily removed from the file. Key aspects of this system include:

(a) The common tracer card:

Components and Usage:

  • Tracer Card Supply: Enough supply of tracer cards is kept in the library, ensuring they are always available when needed.
  • Replacement for Removed Record: When a record is removed from the filing area, it is replaced by a tracer card. The tracer card acts as a placeholder in the file, indicating that the original record has been taken out.
  • Card Removal upon Record Return: When the record is returned to the file, the tracer card is removed and the notes filed back. Upon return, the tracer card is taken out, and the original record is refiled in its place.
  • Card Cancellation and Reuse: The tracer card is cancelled and reused for another set of records-hence the adjective â€œcommonâ€™. Tracer cards are designed to be reusable. Once a record is returned, the tracer card is canceled (often by marking it as used) and then made available for tracking another record. This reusability makes it economical.
  • Strong Card Material: The common tracer card should be made of strong card as it may be used up to eighty times. Durability is important as these cards are handled repeatedly. Strong card material ensures they can withstand frequent use over an extended period.

Information to be Recorded on the Tracer Card:

The information to be recorded on the tracer card should be as follows:

  • Date: Date when the record was taken out.
  • Destination (ward or clinic): Location where the record has been taken (e.g., specific ward or clinic).
  • Hospital Number: Patientâ€™s unique hospital or unit number for record identification.
  • Patientâ€™s full names: Patientâ€™s complete name for clear identification.
  • Reasons for extraction: Brief reason why the record was pulled (e.g., clinic appointment, doctorâ€™s review, research).
  • Borrowerâ€™s signature: Signature of the person who took out the record, ensuring accountability.

Date Specificity:

  • Date of Clinic vs. Extraction Date: Some special mention should be made about the date. Where the notes are being extracted for an outpatient clinic, the date recorded on the tracer card should be the date of the clinic not the date of extraction. It is crucial to record the date of the clinic appointment, not just the date the record was pulled, especially for outpatient clinic preparations.
  • Admission Date for Inpatients: When the patient is being admitted, the date of admission should be included on the tracer card, if the record is pulled for admission purposes.
  • Extraction Date for Other Purposes: If the notes are for research or for a letter to be written, the date should be the date of extraction. For non-clinical uses like research or administrative tasks, the date of extraction is recorded.
  • Size for Visibility: The tracer card should be one quarter inch larger that the notes so that it can guide the filing clerk of where the notes should be inserted. The tracer card is slightly larger than the file it replaces, making it easily visible in the file and serving as a guide for refiling the record correctly.

ðŸ“š Tracing Systems: (b) Library Tracer System

(b) Library tracer system is another method for tracking health records, drawing inspiration from library book tracking systems. It is particularly suited for smaller health record libraries. Key aspects include:

(b) Library tracer system:

Components and Usage:

  • Pocket Inside Record Cover: Each case record has a small pocket inside the cover in which is held a small card. A pocket is integrated into each record folder to hold a tracer card permanently associated with that record.
  • Card with Patient and Hospital Details: a small card with the patients name and the hospital number at the top, and a series of lines below on which borrower and date may be entered. The card contains basic patient identification details and lines to log borrowing information.
  • Principle from Public Libraries: very much on the principle still being used by public libraries, mirroring how libraries track books that are checked out.
  • Card Marking and Filing on Withdrawal: When the notes are withdrawn from the file the card is marked with date and name of the borrower and filed in a small tracer card index. When a record is removed, the tracer card is taken out of the pocket, marked with borrower details and date, and then filed in a separate tracer card index, acting as a temporary record of the file's location.
  • Card Extraction and Entry Cancellation on Return: When notes are returned to file, the card is extracted from this index, the entry cancelled and the card filed into the pocket in the folder which is itself then re-filed. Upon return of the record, the corresponding tracer card is retrieved from the tracer card index, the borrowing entry is cancelled or marked as returned, and the card is placed back into the pocket of the record before refiling the record itself.

Disadvantages of Library Tracer System:

  1. Disadvantages:
  2. 1. Small Library Suitability:
    • Limited Scale Application: This system is recommended only for a small library. The library tracer system is best suited for smaller healthcare facilities or departments due to its manual nature and the need to manage a separate card index.
  3. 2. No Physical Placeholder:
    • Absence of File Placeholder: There is no object left in place of the file to show where the record is in the main filing system. Unlike the common tracer card system, the library tracer system does not leave a placeholder in the file location, which can make it less visually obvious that a record is missing from the shelf.
  4. 3. Misfiling Risk:
    • Increased Error Potential: Misfiling is likely to occur. The need to manage a separate tracer card index and then refile cards into pockets increases the chances of errors in tracking and refiling, potentially leading to misfiled or misplaced tracer cards and records.

Advantage of Library Tracer System:

  1. Advantage:
  2. Facts Always Right:
    • Permanent Card Association: Because the card is created permanently when the folder is created, the facts are always right. A key advantage is that the tracer card is permanently linked to the record folder, ensuring that basic patient identification details on the tracer card are always accurate and consistent, as they are created at the same time as the folder itself.

The library tracer system is a simple, low-tech solution that works best for smaller operations where the risk of misfiling can be carefully managed, and the benefit of permanently associated tracer cards is valued.

ðŸ“š Tracing Systems: (c) Personal Tracer Card

(c) Personal Tracer Card system combines features of both the common and library tracer card systems, aiming to enhance record tracking and reduce misfiling. Key aspects include:

(c) Personal Tracer Card:

Components and Usage:

  • Card Creation at Folder Creation: This card is created the same time as the case folder and identified with the patientâ€™s name and hospital number. Like the library tracer card, the personal tracer card is created at the same time as the patient's case folder, ensuring permanent association and accurate initial data. It includes patient name and hospital number for direct record linkage.
  • Card Stored Inside Folder: It is filed inside the folder and is only taken out of file when the notes are taken out of the file. The card is normally kept inside the patient's folder and is only removed when the entire folder is taken out of the filing system.
  • Same Details as Common Tracer Card: The same details as the common tracer card are entered onto it when a record is borrowed. When a record is borrowed, the same information as on a common tracer card (date, borrower, destination, etc.) is recorded on the personal tracer card.

Advantages of Personal Tracer Card:

  1. Advantages:
  2. 1. Clue to Record Destination:
    • Destination Tracking: When the record cannot be traced, the details of the previous destination may give a clue as to the destination of the record. If a record is misplaced, the details on the personal tracer card, especially the last recorded destination, can provide valuable clues for locating it.
  3. 2. Reduced Transposition Errors:
    • Permanent Number on Card: No transposition of numbers since the number is created permanently on the card. Because the unit number is pre-printed or permanently written on the card at the time of creation, the risk of transposition errors (incorrectly copying the number) during borrowing and refiling is minimized.

The personal tracer card system offers enhanced tracking and reduced error potential, combining the benefits of permanent association with detailed borrowing information.

ðŸ“š Importance of Tracer Systems

The importance of the tracer systems cannot be overstressed in health records management. These systems are not merely procedural steps but are fundamental to maintaining efficient and reliable access to patient information. Key aspects of their importance include:

  • Preventing Record Loss and Misplacement: Tracer systems are crucial for minimizing the loss or misplacement of health records, which can have serious implications for patient care and legal compliance.
  • Ensuring Accountability: By documenting who has borrowed a record and when, tracer systems ensure accountability and help track down records quickly if needed urgently.
  • Supporting Timely Record Retrieval: Effective tracer systems ensure that health records can be retrieved promptly when needed for patient appointments, medical reviews, or other purposes, minimizing delays and improving workflow.
  • Maintaining Data Integrity: By keeping track of record movements, tracer systems help maintain the integrity of the health records system, ensuring that records are not only locatable but also properly managed and returned to their correct place.
  • Patient Care and Safety: Ultimately, the effectiveness of tracer systems directly impacts patient care. It is no excuse that notes cannot be found because the tracer is out of date. This will not help the patient. The inability to locate patient records can lead to delays in diagnosis, treatment errors, and compromised patient safety.
  • Achieving Instant Availability: Like any other procedure, the tracer must help to achieve instant availability of the patientsâ€™ notes when they are needed. The goal of any tracer system is to ensure that patient records are available instantly when healthcare providers need them, supporting timely and informed patient care decisions.

In conclusion, tracer systems are indispensable tools in health records management, directly contributing to the quality, safety, and efficiency of healthcare delivery.

ðŸ“š Key Takeaways

  • Tracing is essential for tracking health records removed from filing, ensuring they are locatable and not lost.
  • The common tracer card system uses reusable cards to replace removed records, recording borrower details, destination, and date for tracking.
  • Advantages of common tracer cards include reusability and ease of advance preparation for clinics.
  • Library tracer systems use permanently pocketed cards in each folder, marked upon withdrawal and filed in a separate index, best for small libraries but prone to misfiling.
  • Personal tracer cards combine features by being permanently associated with a folder but removed and marked when borrowed, providing clues for record location and reducing transposition errors.
  • The importance of tracer systems cannot be overstated as they ensure record availability, accountability, and support timely patient care and safety.
