üìö Editing and Auditing of the Health Record

Editing and Auditing of the Health Record are crucial post-discharge processes to ensure completeness, accuracy, and compliance. These steps are essential for maintaining high-quality health information. Key aspects include:

  • Auditing of the Record:
    • Arrangement of Forms: Auditing of the record is the arrangement of all the forms inside the unit folder in a prescribed manner. This involves checking and organizing the health record to ensure all documents are present and correctly ordered.
    • Hospital-Specific Manner: This can be decided by the hospital concerned, allowing facilities to tailor the arrangement to their specific needs and standards.
    • Chronological or Specialty Order: It can either be done chronologically or in order of speciality. Records can be organized by date of events or by medical specialty (e.g., cardiology reports together, surgical notes together).
    • Ward Clerk Responsibility: This can be done by the ward clerks if they are available, leveraging ward-level staff familiarity with patient cases to streamline initial record preparation.
  • Post-Editing Procedures:
    • Return to Health Records Department: After the record has been edited, it should be returned to the health records department for further processing and quality checks.
    • Cross-Checking with Bed Returns: In the department, the records should be cross checked with the daily bed returns to ensure that all records have been received in the records department. This step verifies that all discharged patient records are accounted for and returned for processing.
    • Coding and Indexing: After this, the records will be coded and then indexed ready for filing back to the library. Coding involves assigning standardized codes for diagnoses and procedures, while indexing prepares the records for efficient storage and retrieval.
    • Information Completeness Check: The health records and information officer should complete all the information that is supposed to be contained in it is there. A final review to ensure all necessary information and documentation are present and complete before final filing.
    • Discharge Procedure Completion: The discharge procedure cannot be complete until the record returns back to the file, emphasizing that proper record processing is an integral part of the discharge process.
  • Follow-up Appointments:
    • Return Appointment Scheduling: The patient is then given a return appointment to a consultative clinic and this is recorded on his attendance card, ensuring continuity of care for follow-up needs.
    • Record Availability for Clinic: When the patient comes back to the clinic, his record should be available in the clinic, highlighting the importance of efficient record processing and retrieval systems.

These editing and auditing steps ensure that health records are not only complete and accurate but also organized and readily accessible for future clinical, administrative, and legal needs.

üìö The Medical Audit

The Medical Audit is a systematic review of health records to evaluate the quality of medical care. It is a critical component of quality assurance in healthcare. Key aspects of medical audit include:

  • Definition of Medical Audit: The Medical Audit has been defined as an ‚Äúobjective method for applying a yardstick to the quality of professional performance.‚Äù It is a structured process to measure and improve healthcare quality.
  • Purpose of Medical Audit:
    • Evaluating Quality of Care: It is the method of evaluating quality of medical care given to a patient, ensuring that care meets established standards.
    • Tool for Improvement: It is a tool to enable the administrator and the medical staff to uncover inefficient service and to point away to an improvement of standards in the health facility- a tool of management. Medical audits are used to identify areas for service improvement and enhance the overall quality of care.
    • Management Responsibility: Medical audit is recognized as a management tool for continuous quality improvement and maintaining high standards of care.
  • Focus on Patient Outcomes: It is important to evaluate the care rendered to the patients in terms of lives saved, avoidable deaths, and patients rehabilitated back to society. Medical audit assesses the impact of healthcare interventions on patient outcomes and societal health.
  • Conducted by Health Records Committee: This evaluation is carried out by health records committee, emphasizing that quality assessment is a structured, committee-led activity within healthcare facilities.

Medical audits are vital for maintaining and enhancing the quality of healthcare services, ensuring accountability, and promoting best practices in patient care.

üìö Accident and Emergency Records Procedure: Register and Clinical Record

Accident and Emergency (A&E) Records Procedure differs from routine health records due to the urgent and immediate nature of care provided in emergency settings. Specific procedures are in place for managing A&E records, including record types and handling.

Records to be kept:

  1. Register:
    • Maintenance of Register: Firstly a register must be maintained at the Accident and Emergency department. This register is a primary record of all patients who present at the A&E.
    • Information Included in Register: The register should include comprehensive details for each patient:
      • Name of patient
      • Address
      • Age
      • Doctor referring him (if applicable)
      • Time of arrival
      • Brief description of injury or complaint
      • Brief details of treatment provided
      • Mode of disposal (e.g., discharged, admitted, referred)
      • Particulars of the person who brought the patient to A&E (and their details if police officer)
    • Register Format: The register may be in loose leaf form or in bound volumes, depending on facility preference and volume.
    • Statistics Compilation: From the register statistics of attendance will be compiled, providing data for workload analysis, resource planning, and epidemiological tracking of emergency cases.
  2. Clinical record:

    Clinical records in A&E are designed for quick and efficient documentation of immediate care. Types of clinical records used include:

    • (i) Single card:
      • Card Specifications: Single card measuring 8‚Äù x5‚Äù or 6‚Äù x 4‚Äù, compact for rapid documentation.
      • Content Details: On which the identification details are recorded, along with:
        • For R.T.A (Road traffic accident) patient: time and place of accident must be given
        • Space left for the clinician to write on: brief clinical notes and treatment details
    • (ii) Two part card:
      • Carbonized Card: A card with carbonized part made from N.C.R material, allowing for a duplicate copy to be created instantly.
      • Envelope Format: The card is made up in an envelope form so that x- ray reports and any other correspondence may be filled in it, providing a convenient pocket for additional documents.
    • (iii) Thick paper envelope:
      • Envelope Size: Thick paper envelope four and an eighth by five and three quarters inches (half the size of A4 paper).
      • Dual Purpose: The envelope will serve as a card and a pocket for reports, combining documentation space with storage for reports in a single form.

These specialized A&E records are designed for rapid documentation and efficient management of emergency patient information, balancing detail with the urgency of the care environment.

üìö Accident and Emergency Records Procedure: Appointments, Disposal and Legal Requirements

  1. Appointments:
    • Return Appointments Uncommon: Most patients attending this department rarely come back for return appointment, as A&E typically handles acute and emergency conditions.
    • Managing Return Appointments: For those patients that need return appointments, this can be carried out in two ways:
      • (i) Appointment register:
        • Receptionist Management: Appointment register may be retained by the receptionist.
        • Card Documentation: and the appointment written on the patient‚Äôs attendance card for those few requiring follow-up.
      • (ii) Pre-printed appointment cards:
        • Pre-printed Cards for Each Day: Pre-printed appointment cards for each day and time may be given to the patient for scheduled returns.
        • Color-Coded Cards: A different colour of the card could be kept for each day of the week and when a patient arrives seeking an appointment a card is simply pulled out and given to him, aiding in quick identification and distribution of appointment slots.
  2. Disposal of patients:

    Disposal refers to the outcome or next step for patients after their A&E visit. Disposal categories include:

    • (a) Treated to finality and discharged: Patients who receive complete treatment in A&E and are discharged home.
    • (b) Treated initially and referred back to the nearest health centre: Patients who receive initial emergency care and are then referred back to a local health center for ongoing management.
    • (c) Referred to an out-patient consultant clinic: Patients needing specialist follow-up are referred to outpatient clinics within the hospital.
    • (d) Referred to a consultant clinic in another hospital: Patients requiring specialized care not available at the current facility are referred to other hospitals.
    • (e) Admitted to the wards for further treatment: Patients requiring continued in-patient care are admitted to appropriate hospital wards. Full documentation for admission will be carried out before the patient goes to the ward.
    • (f) Transferred to another hospital for further treatment: Patients needing specialized services are transferred to other facilities.
    • (g) In the case of patient who has been brought dead (B.I.D) the doctor just goes to certify the death and the body is conveyed straight to the mortuary: Procedures for patients brought in dead involve certification of death and transfer to the mortuary.
  3. 4. Legal requirements:
    • Legal Compliance: It is important to note that the same legal requirements that are applicable to other health records as far as retention is concerned still apply in accident and emergency records. A&E records are subject to the same legal standards as other health records.
    • Retention Period: They may be retained for a minimum of six years after the last attendance, aligning with general health record retention policies.
    • Filing System: The records may be filed numerically or alphabetically depending on the number of records created annually, allowing flexibility in filing based on volume and retrieval needs.
    • Annual File Start: At the beginning of the year, 1st January, a new file is started, indicating an annual cycle for record management, likely for statistical and organizational purposes.
    • Security and Confidentiality: The records should be kept o\in lock because most of these records are usually required in court and as much details as possible should be recorded. Due to their potential legal importance, A&E records must be securely stored and confidentiality maintained.
    • Road Traffic Accident Claims: Most patients who have been involved in road traffic accidents need some claims in future. Therefore statistics for these patients may be sent to finance department so as to issue the necessary claims. Data on RTA patients is often needed for insurance and legal claims, necessitating specific data handling and reporting to finance departments.

üìö Key Takeaways

  • Editing and auditing health records post-discharge involve arranging forms, cross-checking with bed returns, coding, indexing, and ensuring completeness.
  • Medical audit is an objective method to evaluate and improve the quality of professional performance and patient care.
  • A&E records include a register for all patients and clinical records on single or two-part cards or thick paper envelopes for rapid documentation.
  • Appointments for A&E patients are rare but managed via registers or pre-printed cards for follow-ups; disposal categories range from discharge to admission or transfer, including cases of patients brought in dead.
  • Legal requirements for A&E records are similar to other health records, including minimum six-year retention, secure storage, and specific handling for potential court use and RTA claims.
