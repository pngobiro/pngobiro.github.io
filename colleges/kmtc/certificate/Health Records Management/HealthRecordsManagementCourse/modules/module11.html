üìö Equipment Used for Filing Master Index Cards

Various types of equipment are used for efficiently filing and managing Master Index Cards. The choice of equipment depends on factors like the volume of cards, space availability, and budget. Common equipment includes:

  • Cards: The fundamental component of the Master Index. These are typically made of durable card stock to withstand frequent handling.
  • Strip Index: A system using thin wooden or metal strips with paper inserts, mounted on panels.
  • Elevator Files: Automated or manual systems that bring files to an accessible height.
  • Cartwheel: Files attached to a rotating wheel for easy access.
  • Carousel Type Files: Rotary files housed in open metal boxes on a central spindle.
  • Guide Cards: Used to divide sections within the filing system for easier navigation.

Each type of equipment offers different advantages in terms of capacity, accessibility, and space utilization, catering to various needs of health records departments.

üìö Cards and Drawer Filing

Cards are the basic record document in most master indexes. They must meet certain standards to be effective:

  • Basic Record Document: Cards serve as the primary medium for recording index information. Each card typically represents a patient and contains essential details for identification and record retrieval.
  • Quality for Endurance: Generally, a card must be of good quality for endurance. This means they should be made from card stock that is:
    • Durable: Able to withstand frequent handling without tearing or bending.
    • Long-lasting: Resistant to wear and tear over extended periods of use and storage.
    • Suitable for Writing: Surface should be appropriate for writing or printing information clearly and legibly.

Drawer filing is a common method for organizing these cards:

  • "Blind" Filing: Cards may be filled ‚Äúblind‚Äù in drawers, meaning they are filed one behind the other in an upright position within drawer cabinets.
  • Upright Position: Cards stand vertically in drawers, allowing for easy flipping through to locate specific cards.
  • Drawer Cabinets: Typically stored in specialized drawer cabinets designed for index cards, which may include features like dividers and compressor plates to keep cards organized and upright.

Drawer filing is a straightforward and space-efficient method, particularly suitable for manual card-based index systems.

üìö Strip Index and Elevator Files

Strip Index offers an alternative to traditional card filing, especially where space is a concern:

  • Components: A strip index, as its name suggests, consists of:
    • Thin Strips: Series of thin wooden or metal strips.
    • Paper Coating: Strips are coated with paper on which the details required for the master index are written or printed.
    • Metal Panels: Strips are filed on metal panels.
  • Panel Design: The metal panels are designed with:
    • Folded Edges: A fold of metal down each edge which holds the strips in place, preventing them from sliding out.
    • Revolving Spindle: Panels are hung on a central revolving spindle, allowing them to fan out like leaves on an open book when accessed.
  • Mounting Options: Strip indexes can be:
    • Wall Mounted: Affixed to walls to save floor space.
    • Table or Desk Mounted: Placed on desks or tables for easy access.
  • Suitability: Recommended for specific applications:
    • Waiting List: Well-suited for managing waiting lists due to its compact and easily updatable nature.
    • Not Ideal for Master Index: Less recommended for the main Patient Master Index due to potential limitations in expansion and handling very large volumes of data.

Elevator Files are advanced, often automated systems designed for high-volume card filing:

  • Tray-Based Filing: In an elevator file, cards are filed in trays that are:
    • Foot-Long Trays: Typically about a foot long to hold a substantial number of cards.
    • Multiple Trays: Several trays are stacked vertically within the machine.
  • Machine Structure: The elevator file machine resembles:
    • Large Metal Cabinet: A cabinet that can be up to five feet high and ten feet wide, providing significant storage capacity.
    • Top Opening: An opening at the top of the cabinet where shelves with trays of cards are visible and accessible.
  • Capacity: Large elevator files can hold a substantial number of index cards:
    • High Capacity: The largest elevator files can hold up to 900,000 index cards, suitable for very large healthcare facilities.
  • Operation: Elevator files can be electrically driven or manually controlled:
    • Electrically Driven: Large files are typically electrically driven. To retrieve a card, the filing clerk:
      • Presses a Button: Presses a button indicating the desired shelf or section.
      • Rotation for Access: The cards rotate until the correct one arrives at hand level at the top opening.
    • Manually Controlled: Smaller elevator files are manually controlled, often using a hand crank to move trays.
  • Advantages:
    • Speedy Access: Provides quick access to all cards.
    • Clerk Comfort: Allows the clerk to remain comfortably seated while accessing records.
  • Disadvantages:
    • Expensive: Elevator files are costly to purchase and maintain.
    • Affordability: May not be affordable for all health facilities, particularly smaller or resource-constrained ones.

Elevator files are ideal for facilities needing high-density, rapid access to a large volume of index cards, despite their higher cost.

üìö Cartwheel and Carousel Type Files

Cartwheel Type Files offer a unique rotary filing solution:

  • Design: In this type, index cards are:
    • Attached to a Wheel: Attached to a large upright central wheel.
    • Rotational Access: The wheel is rotated until the required section of cards comes to the top for access.
  • Suitability:
    • Not for Large Hospitals: Probably not suitable for very large hospitals due to limitations in simultaneous access and capacity.
    • Best for Smaller Volumes: More appropriate for smaller clinics or departments with moderate card volumes.

Carousel Type Files, also rotary, provide a different approach to carousel filing:

  • Design: In this type, index cards are:
    • Housed in Boxes: Cards are housed in open metal boxes.
    • Central Spindle Attachment: Boxes are attached to a central upright spindle.
    • Swinging Boxes: Boxes can be swung out for use, allowing access to cards, and then folded back towards the spindle when finished.
  • Accessibility:
    • Multiple Access Points: Because boxes are attached around the stem, several clerks can have access at once, improving efficiency in busy environments.
  • Space Saving:
    • Compact Design: Equipment is space saving, especially considering the number of cards that can be housed in a relatively small footprint.

Both cartwheel and carousel files offer rotary access, but carousel files are generally more advantageous for larger teams needing simultaneous access and space efficiency.

üìö Guide Cards and Back Stops

Guide Cards and Back Stops are essential accessories for drawer, cartwheel, and carousel filing systems (excluding strip index) to enhance organization and ease of use:

  • Guide Cards:
    • Function: The function of guide cards is to subdivide the file into reasonable sections.
    • Purpose of Subdivision: Subdivision is crucial to:
      • Reduce Search Time: Prevent excessive time spent hunting through hundreds of cards to find a specific one.
      • Improve Efficiency: Make filing and finding cards quicker and more efficient.
    • Design Features: Whatever type of guide card is chosen, certain features are important:
      • Clear, Bold Lettering: Lettering should be clear and bold to aid in quick identification of sections.
      • Aids Filing and Finding: Well-designed guide cards significantly aid both the filing and finding of cards.
  • Back Stops:
    • Function: Back stops are pieces of metal or sturdy material used to:
      • Support Cards: Stop cards in a drawer that is not full from sliding down or falling over.
    • Placement and Stability:
      • Drawer Back: Placed at the back of a drawer to support cards.
      • Firm and Adjustable: Should be firm enough to stay in place even when the drawer is closed roughly, but also mobile enough to be readily adjustable as the number of cards expands or contracts.

Guide cards and back stops are simple yet crucial tools that significantly improve the organization and usability of card-based filing systems.

üìö Filing Techniques Used in Filing Master Index Cards

Effective filing of Master Index Cards requires specific techniques to ensure accuracy, speed, and ease of retrieval. The main techniques include sorting, filing, and checking:

  1. (a) Sorting:
    • Most Important Step: Sorting is the most important step in filing. It involves putting the cards into good order before being filed.
    • Purpose: Sorting prepares the cards for systematic filing, making the actual filing process more efficient and accurate.
    • Sorting Methods: Two methods are particularly appropriate for sorting Master Index Cards:
      • Use of Sort-File:
        • Series of Flaps: A sort-file consists of a series of flaps.
        • Alphabetical Letters: Each flap bears a letter of the alphabet.
        • Solid Base Arrangement: Flaps are arranged in alphabetical order in a solid base.
        • Functionality: Cards are initially sorted into these alphabetical flaps before final filing.
      • Pigeon Holes:
        • Lettered Holes: Pigeon holes are used, with each hole lettered for initial sorting.
        • Initial Sorting: Index cards are sorted into these lettered pigeon holes as a preliminary step before being filed in drawers or other equipment.
  2. (b) Filing:
    • Requires Concentration: Filing index cards requires a lot of concentration to maintain accuracy and avoid misfiling.
    • Checking Surrounding Files: To ensure accuracy, it is essential to:
      • Check Front and Behind: When filing a card, the files in front and behind in the drawer should be checked to ensure correct alphabetical sequence.
    • Allocate to Experienced Clerk: This task should be allocated to an experienced clerk who is detail-oriented and understands the importance of accuracy in filing.
  3. (c) Checking:
    • Mistakes Inevitable: It is obvious that mistakes do occur when filing, even with careful sorting and filing.
    • Constant Checking Required: Therefore, constant checking of filed cards is required to catch and correct errors.
    • Reference to Index: After cards have been filed and checked:
      • Limited Removal: They should only be removed when the address needs to be changed or for essential updates.
      • Prevent Overpacking: Cards should never be so tightly packed that checking and filing become difficult.
    • Alphabetical Order and Staff Responsibility: Key points to ensure effective checking and filing:
      • Strict Alphabetical Order: Master Index Cards should be filed strictly alphabetically.
      • Single Staff Member: Only one member of staff should work on the cards at any one time to maintain consistency and prevent misfiling due to multiple handling.

By adhering to these filing techniques‚Äîsorting, careful filing, and consistent checking‚Äîhealth records departments can maintain a Master Index that is accurate, reliable, and easy to use.

üìö Diagnostic Index and Operations Index

Besides the Patient Master Index, two other critical indices are the Diagnostic Index and the Operations Index, which serve specific purposes in health information management.

  1. Diagnostic Index:
    • Purpose: The diagnostic index is designed to provide access for study purposes to the clinical data contained in hospital records.
    • User Group: Primarily used by health personnel wishing to conduct studies on specific diseases.
    • Information Retrieval: Researchers can use the diagnostic index to:
      • Obtain Case Folder Numbers: Find the case folder numbers of patients who have been admitted or attended outpatient departments with a particular disease of interest.
    • Disease-Specific Information: The disease index provides:
      • Number of Relevant Records: The count of case records related to a specific disease.
      • Minimal Patient Data: May also include minimal data such as patient‚Äôs age, sex, and outcome of the episode of treatment to provide context for the cases.
  2. Operations Index:
    • Purpose: Similar to the diagnostic index but focused on surgical and medical procedures. It is possible for the Health Records and Information Officer to maintain an operations index besides the diagnostic index.
    • Coding of Procedures: Operative procedures need to be coded, just as diagnoses are. The code used is typically from a classification of surgical operations (e.g., ICP-9-CM, CPT).
    • Equipment for Maintenance: The equipment used to maintain the operations index is similar to that used for the disease index, including:
      • Vertical Index Cards
      • Visible Edge Cards
      • Computers (for electronic indices)
    • Information Recorded: The information recorded on the operations index includes:
      • Case record number
      • Patient sex and age
      • Surgeon's name
      • Outcome of the episode
      • Date of discharge

Both Diagnostic and Operations Indices are vital for specialized data retrieval and analysis, supporting research, quality improvement, and administrative reporting within healthcare facilities.

üìö Key Takeaways

  • Various equipment types, including cards, strip indexes, elevator files, cartwheel, and carousel files, are used for filing Master Index Cards, each with unique benefits.
  • Drawer filing involves filing cards "blind" and upright in drawers, a simple and space-efficient method for card indexes.
  • Strip indexes are compact, suitable for waiting lists, while elevator files are high-capacity, automated systems for large volumes.
  • Guide cards subdivide files for easier navigation, and back stops support cards in partially filled drawers, enhancing organization.
  • Effective filing techniques include sorting (alphabetical sort-files or pigeon holes), careful filing by experienced clerks, and constant checking to maintain accuracy.
  • Diagnostic and Operations Indices provide study access to clinical data, using similar equipment as PMI but indexing diseases and procedures for specialized retrieval and analysis.
