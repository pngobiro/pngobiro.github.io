Developing and Designing Health Records Forms

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Describe the basic principles of effective form design for health records.
  • Identify and explain the purpose of various medical forms used in health records departments.
  • Outline the procedures for pre-testing medical forms before implementation.
  • Explain different methods of reproduction and costing considerations for medical forms.
  • Describe control procedures for managing and maintaining medical forms.

📚 Basic Principles of Form Design

Basic Principles of Form Design are essential for creating effective, user-friendly, and efficient medical forms. Before designing any form, a health records professional should consider several key questions to ensure the form meets its intended purpose. These questions serve as a checklist to guide the design process:

Basic Principles of Form Design

Pre-Design Questions:

Before one designs a form he should ask himself some questions as:-

  • (a)Is the form necessary?:
    • Justification of Need: Question the necessity of the form itself. Is there a genuine need for this new form? Does it duplicate existing forms or information capture methods? Ensuring necessity prevents redundancy and unnecessary paperwork.
  • (b) Is it the best form for its purpose?:
    • Purpose-Driven Design: Evaluate if the proposed form is the most effective way to collect the required information for its intended use. Consider alternative formats or methods that might be more efficient or user-friendly.
  • (c)Is it easy to use?:
    • User-Friendliness: Assess the ease of use for those who will be filling out and processing the form. A form should be intuitive, straightforward, and require minimal training to use effectively.
  • (d) Are the instructions clear and unambiguous?:
    • Clarity of Instructions: Ensure that all instructions on the form are clear, concise, and unambiguous. Instructions should be easy to understand for all users, minimizing errors and confusion.
  • (e)Is there any unnecessary duplication with another form?:
    • Avoid Redundancy: Check for potential duplication with existing forms. Eliminate any unnecessary overlap in data collection to streamline processes and reduce paperwork.
  • (f) How can be improved?:
    • Continuous Improvement Mindset: Consider potential improvements to the form design. Seek feedback from users and stakeholders to identify areas for enhancement in terms of clarity, efficiency, and effectiveness.
  • (g) Is it economical in terms of paper, printing and equipment required for its use?:
    • Cost-Effectiveness: Evaluate the economic aspects of the form, including paper usage, printing costs, and any specialized equipment required for its processing or storage. Aim for a design that is cost-effective without compromising functionality.
  • (h) Can it be combined with any other form or forms, making it a multipurpose document?:
    • Multipurpose Potential: Explore opportunities to combine the form with other forms to create a multipurpose document. Consolidating forms can reduce the number of forms in use, simplify data collection, and improve efficiency.

Basic Principles of Form Design:

There are basic principles of form design that should be in one’s mind before he designs a form. These are:-

  1. (1) Simplicity:
    • Keep it Simple: Design forms to be as simple and straightforward as possible. Avoid unnecessary complexity, jargon, or overly detailed sections that can confuse users or complicate data entry. Simplicity enhances usability and reduces errors.
  2. (2) Uniformity of size and style:
    • Standardization: Maintain uniformity in size and style across all forms within the health records system. Consistent design elements, layout, and font styles create a cohesive and professional look.
    • Standard Location for Basic Information: in particular the “standard’ location of basic information on the form is important. Ensure that basic identification information (e.g., patient name, unit number, date of birth) is consistently located in the same area on all forms. This standardization aids in quick recognition and data retrieval.
  3. (3) Clear instructions on the form:
    • Provide Guidance: Include clear and concise instructions directly on the form. Instructions should guide users on how to properly complete each section, clarify any potentially confusing items, and ensure accurate data collection.
  4. (4) Information requested should be minimized:
    • Essential Data Only: Information requested should be minimized to only what is absolutely necessary. Avoid collecting redundant or non-essential data that adds to form length and complexity without providing significant value.
    • Pre-printing and Choice Questions: Information requested should be minimized, by pre-printing and multiple choice questions and answers. Use techniques like:
      • Pre-printing: Pre-fill sections with fixed information where possible to reduce manual entry.
      • Multiple Choice Questions: Use check boxes, radio buttons, or drop-down lists for structured data entry, simplifying completion and ensuring data consistency.
  5. (5) Layout should be eye catching:
    • Visually Appealing Design: Layout should be eye catching (drawing attention) to special areas of interest) and easy to read. Form layout should be designed to be visually appealing and easy to navigate. Use design elements to:
      • Drawing Attention: Highlight important sections or fields using visual cues like bolding, boxes, or color-coding to draw the user’s eye to critical information.
      • Easy to Read: Ensure forms are easy to read with clear fonts, sufficient spacing, and logical flow to guide users through the form efficiently.

By adhering to these basic principles, health records professionals can design forms that are not only functional and efficient but also contribute to data accuracy, user satisfaction, and overall effectiveness of health information management.

📚 Various Medical Forms Used in Health Records Departments: Case Folder to Letter of Referral

Various medical forms used in health records departments are designed to capture specific types of information necessary for patient care, administrative processes, and legal documentation. Understanding the purpose of each form is crucial for effective health records management. Common medical forms include:

Various medical forms used in health records departments are:-

  1. Case folder:
    • Purpose: To serve as the primary container for all documents related to a patient’s health record.
    • Information on Folder: The case folder itself typically has pre-printed information on its cover for quick identification:
      • Should have the name of the hospital: Clearly identifies the healthcare facility.
      • the patient’s name: Patient’s full name for easy identification.
      • the hospital number: Patient’s unique hospital or unit number.
      • and the information “highly confidential” enclosed printed on top of the folder: A confidentiality notice to emphasize the sensitive nature of the contents and ensure privacy.
  2. In-Patient summary sheet:
    • Purpose: To provide a concise summary of a patient’s hospital stay.
    • Content: Contains complete clinical summary of the individual’s stay and treatment given during the stay. This includes:
      • Admission and discharge dates
      • Principal and secondary diagnoses
      • Procedures and treatments performed
      • Significant findings and progress notes
      • Condition at discharge
      • Follow-up recommendations
  3. Letter of referral:
    • Purpose: To formally refer a patient to another healthcare provider or institution for specialized care or consultation.
    • Usage: This is a letter given to a patient by a doctor referring to a hospital or health institution for further management. It facilitates coordinated care between different healthcare settings.
    • Content: Typically includes:
      • Patient demographics and contact information
      • Referring doctor’s details and contact information
      • Reason for referral and clinical summary
      • Specific requests or questions for the receiving provider

📚 Various Medical Forms Used in Health Records Departments: Prescription Sheet to Nursing Record

  1. Prescription sheet:
    • Purpose: To document all medications prescribed to a patient during their care.
    • Usage: This is used for recording treatment and drugs given by the medical staff. It serves as a legal and clinical record of prescriptions.
    • Content: Includes details such as:
      • Medication name and dosage
      • Route of administration
      • Frequency and timing
      • Start and end dates
      • Prescribing doctor’s signature
  2. Mount sheet:
    • Purpose: To provide a standardized format for mounting and filing various reports that are smaller than standard paper sizes.
    • Usage: This is for mounting x- rays, laboratory and other reports. It should be A4 size and made of heavy material. Mount sheets are used to neatly organize and preserve reports such as:
      • X-ray films (or copies)
      • Laboratory results
      • Other diagnostic reports
    • Specifications: Typically A4 size and made of heavy material to support the mounted items and ensure durability.
  3. History and continuation sheet:
    • Purpose: To document the patient’s medical history, progress notes, and ongoing treatment details.
    • Usage: The physician continues to write the history of the patient’s illness, the continued treatment on these sheets. They are used for:
      • Initial history taking
      • Progress notes during hospital stay or outpatient visits
      • Updates on treatment plans and patient responses
  4. Operation and anaesthetic record:
    • Purpose: To document details of surgical procedures and anaesthesia administration.
    • Usage: There could be special operation forms used for different operation purposes. The name of the surgeon and the anaesthetist should be recorded on this form. These records are critical for:
      • Surgical planning and documentation
      • Anaesthesia administration and monitoring
      • Post-operative care and review
    • Content: Includes information such as:
      • Type of operation
      • Surgical team members (surgeon, assistants, nurses)
      • Anaesthesia type and agents
      • Pre-operative and post-operative assessments
      • Intraoperative events and findings
  5. Temperature, pulse, respiration and blood pressure charts:
    • Purpose: To record vital signs monitoring over time.
    • Usage and Disposal: These may be destroyed after the patient’s discharge; they are transitory records unless one is marked for retention by the Medical officer of Health. Fluid and Diabetic charts. These once could also be destroyed unless otherwise stated. TPR and BP charts are used for:
      • Routine monitoring of vital signs during hospital stay
      • Tracking patient’s physiological status
      • Guiding immediate care decisions
    • Retention Policy: Typically considered transitory and may be destroyed post-discharge unless marked for permanent retention for specific medical or legal reasons, particularly fluid balance and diabetic charts which may have long-term significance.
  6. Nursing Record:
    • Purpose: To document nursing care, observations, and patient status from a nursing perspective.
    • Usage: Should be included in the unit record to provide a holistic view of patient care. Nursing records are essential for:
      • Documenting daily nursing care activities
      • Recording patient responses to treatment
      • Noting patient condition changes and nursing interventions
      • Ensuring continuity of nursing care

📚 Various Medical Forms Used in Health Records Departments: Notification of Discharge to Medical Social Workers' Report

  1. Notification of discharge and discharge letter:
    • Purpose: To officially document and communicate patient discharge information.
    • Components: Involves two parts:
      • Notification of Discharge: Internal hospital notification of a patient’s discharge for administrative and record-keeping purposes.
      • Discharge Letter: A letter of discharge from a health institution should be given to him and his doctor notified as well. A formal letter provided to the patient and their general practitioner, summarizing the hospital stay and outlining follow-up care.
    • Content of Discharge Letter: In this letter the drugs prescribed to him should be recorded, along with:
      • Summary of diagnosis and treatment
      • Medications prescribed at discharge
      • Follow-up appointment details
      • Advice on post-discharge care and activities
  2. Report forms:
    • Purpose: To standardize the format for various medical reports for consistent filing and review.
    • Usage: The report forms should be gummed onto the mount sheets for organization within the patient record. Report forms include:
      • Laboratory reports
      • Radiology reports
      • Special investigation reports
    • Mounting Requirement: Gumming reports onto mount sheets ensures they are neatly presented and securely filed within the health record.
  3. Post-Mortem reports:
    • Purpose: To document the findings of a post-mortem examination.
    • Final Document Status: This should be the final document and should be filed in the unit file. The post-mortem report is considered the concluding document in cases where a post-mortem is performed.
    • File Location: It is permanently filed in the patient’s unit file as part of their complete medical record.
  4. Medical social workers’ report:
    • Purpose: To document social and psychosocial assessments and interventions provided by medical social workers.
    • Usage: This should be inserted in the case folder to provide a comprehensive view of patient care, including social and emotional aspects.
    • Content: Typically includes:
      • Patient’s social history and circumstances
      • Psychosocial assessments and evaluations
      • Intervention plans and actions
      • Follow-up and recommendations

These various medical forms collectively ensure that all pertinent aspects of patient care, from initial encounter to discharge and beyond, are thoroughly and systematically documented within the health records system.

📚 Pre-testing of Medical Forms

Pre-testing of medical forms is a crucial step before the widespread implementation of any new or revised form. Pre-testing helps identify potential issues, improve form usability, and ensure that the form effectively captures the intended information. The process involves:

Pre-testing of medical forms:

User Testing and Feedback:

  • Testing Before Use: Before the form is used, it may be tested by the users to see whether it has been appropriately been designed for the work it is going to be used for. Pre-testing involves pilot testing the form with a representative group of end-users—clinicians, nurses, administrative staff—who will regularly use the form.
  • User Feedback Collection: Gather feedback from users on various aspects of the form, such as:
    • Clarity of Instructions: Are the instructions easy to understand?
    • Ease of Use: Is the form straightforward to fill out?
    • Completeness of Information Capture: Does the form effectively capture all necessary data?
    • Layout and Design: Is the form layout logical and easy to navigate?
    • Any Areas of Confusion or Difficulty: Identify sections that users find confusing or challenging to complete.

Purpose of Pre-testing:

  • Identify Design Flaws: Pre-testing helps uncover any design flaws or usability issues before the form is fully implemented.
  • Improve Form Design: Feedback from pre-testing is used to refine and improve the form design, making it more user-friendly and efficient.
  • Ensure Data Quality: By improving form design based on user input, pre-testing contributes to better data quality and accuracy in health records.

Pre-testing is an essential quality control step in form design, ensuring that medical forms are practical, effective, and meet the needs of both users and the healthcare system.

📚 Reproduction and Costing of Medical Forms

Reproduction and costing of medical forms are important practical considerations in health records management. Choosing the right reproduction method and managing costs effectively are essential for efficient operations. Key aspects include:

Reproduction and costing of medical forms:

Document Reproduction Methods:

  • "Document reproduction" vs. "photocopying": Very often “document reproduction’ is interpreted as “photocopying’. This is only one method of document reproduction. Document reproduction encompasses various methods beyond just photocopying.
  • Photocopying:
    • Common Method: Photocopying is a widely used method for reproducing documents, especially for on-demand, small to medium volume reproduction.
    • Manufacturing Advances: Manufacturing are now supplying new models of photocopying machines at ever increasing rates, with continuous improvements in speed, quality, and cost-effectiveness of photocopying technology.
    • Cost Consideration: When a machine is hired photocopying becomes more expensive than when it is bought. The cost-effectiveness of photocopying depends on whether machines are purchased outright or hired. Hiring can become more expensive over time due to ongoing rental fees.
  • Spirit and wax stencil reproduction:
    • Versatile Method: This is a very special yet versatile method of reproduction, offering capabilities beyond simple copying.
    • Medium Description: The actual medium of reproduction is a sheet of paper with fine clay-like coating on the reverse side. This sheet is placed over the sheet of hectograph paper, the clay-like coating on the first sheet actually in contact with the carbon surface of the second sheet. These sheets are typed with the information or handwritten. Spirit and wax stencil methods involve creating a master copy on special paper that is then used to produce multiple copies. This method is particularly suited for:
      • Multi-part forms
      • Forms with carbonless copy requirements
    • Master Sheet Copies: From the master sheet copies are made clamping to a revolving drum moistened by a spirit solvent. As the machine and the dampened carbon impression on the master sheet is reproduced in mirror form on each sheet. From a well prepared master sheet 300 copies can be produced. Mechanical documentation uses this method also. Using a spirit solvent and a mechanical process, copies are produced in mirror image from the master sheet. A well-prepared master sheet can yield around 300 copies, making it efficient for medium-volume reproduction. This method is historically significant in mechanical documentation systems.
  • Other means of document reproduction:
    • Offset Litho and Typeset Plates: Other means of document reproduction are offset litho and typeset plates, representing more advanced printing technologies.
    • Offset Litho: Offset litho depends on reproduction by either metal or paper (plate). The plate is placed on the rotary press and copies are made. Offset lithography uses a plate (metal or paper) to transfer an image to a rubber "blanket", which then transfers the image to paper. It is suitable for high-volume, high-quality printing.
    • Typeset Duplication: Typeset duplication equipment is the same as most professional printers still in use. It is expensive to install. Typeset duplication involves using typesetting equipment similar to professional printing presses, offering very high quality but at a significant setup and operational cost.

Considerations for Equipment Purchase:

Before equipment for document reproduction is bought the following points should be considered:-

  • (a)What kind of documents need to be reproduced?:
    • Document Type: Consider the types of documents to be reproduced—forms, reports, letters, etc. Different reproduction methods are better suited for different document types based on quality and volume requirements.
  • (b) Is the material likely to be done single or double sided?:
    • Printing Needs: Determine if documents are typically single-sided or double-sided. Duplex printing capability can significantly reduce paper consumption and costs for double-sided documents.
  • (c)How many copies are required?:
    • Volume Requirements: Estimate the typical volume of copies needed for different forms and reports. Volume requirements will influence the choice between methods suitable for low, medium, or high volume reproduction.
  • (d) At what speed are copies required?:
    • Speed and Turnaround Time: Assess the required speed and turnaround time for document reproduction. High-volume, time-sensitive environments may require faster, more automated systems.
  • (e)What quality of copy is required?:
    • Quality Standards: Determine the necessary copy quality. For medical records, legibility and durability are crucial, while for internal drafts, lower quality may be acceptable.
  • (f) What will be the capital and the running cost-(important to consider):
    • Cost Analysis: What will be the capital and the running cost-(important to consider). Conduct a thorough cost analysis, considering both the initial capital investment (purchase price, installation) and ongoing running costs (paper, ink/toner, maintenance, electricity). Cost-benefit analysis is crucial in selecting the most economical and sustainable reproduction method.

Careful consideration of these factors ensures that the chosen reproduction method is both efficient and cost-effective for the health records department's needs.

📚 Control Procedures for Medical Forms

Control Procedures for Medical Forms are vital for managing forms effectively, preventing wastage, and ensuring that only current and approved forms are in use. These procedures cover various aspects from form usage to obsolescence. Key control measures include:

CONTROL PROCEDURES FOR MEDICAL FORMS

  • Strict Control of Forms and Records Routine: There should be strict control of all forms and records routine to prevent wasteful duplication in inefficient procedures. Strict control mechanisms are necessary to manage form usage and prevent inefficiencies.
  • Preventing Wasteful Duplication: Control procedures aim to:
    • Minimize Unnecessary Forms: Prevent the proliferation of redundant or unnecessary forms.
    • Improve Efficiency: Streamline processes by ensuring forms are used efficiently and effectively.
  • Management of Obsolete and Redundant Forms: Obsolete and redundant forms should be removed from circulation and unsatisfactory forms amended where necessary. Regular review and management of forms involves:
    • Removal of Obsolete Forms: Systematically removing outdated forms from use to prevent confusion and errors.
    • Amending Unsatisfactory Forms: Revising and updating forms that are found to be unclear, inefficient, or not meeting user needs based on feedback and usage reviews.
  • Preventing Duplication of Forms: There should not be duplication of any forms within the health records system. Strict control ensures that each form serves a unique and necessary purpose, avoiding redundancy and streamlining documentation processes.
  • Management of Ad Hoc Investigation Forms: Forms used for ad hoc investigations should be withdrawn as soon as investigation is over. Forms designed for temporary or specific investigations should be controlled and withdrawn from use once the investigation is completed to prevent misuse or accumulation of outdated forms.
  • Maintenance of a Forms List: The records officer should maintain a list of all the forms with a similar function to be kept together. This will give the records officer an opportunity periodically to examine, combine, simplify or eliminate the forms if necessary. A centralized list or inventory of all forms, categorized by function, should be maintained by the records officer. This list enables:
    • Periodic Review: Regular examination of forms to assess their ongoing necessity and effectiveness.
    • Form Combination: Opportunities to combine similar forms into multipurpose documents, reducing the total number of forms.
    • Simplification: Streamlining and simplifying forms based on user feedback and efficiency analysis.
    • Form Elimination: Identifying and eliminating forms that are no longer needed or are redundant, further streamlining the forms management system.

Implementing these control procedures ensures that medical forms are managed systematically, efficiently, and effectively, contributing to better data quality and streamlined healthcare operations.

📚 Key Takeaways

  • Basic principles of form design include simplicity, uniformity, clear instructions, minimized information requests, and eye-catching layout for effective use.
  • Various medical forms are essential in health records, including case folders, in-patient summary sheets, letters of referral, prescription sheets, mount sheets, history and continuation sheets, operation and anaesthetic records, TPR charts, nursing records, discharge notifications, report forms, post-mortem reports, and medical social workers' reports.
  • Pre-testing medical forms with users before implementation is crucial to identify and correct design flaws, ensuring usability and data accuracy.
  • Reproduction methods for medical forms range from photocopying and spirit/wax stencil to offset litho and typeset plates, with cost and quality implications for each method.
  • Control procedures for medical forms involve strict management to prevent duplication, remove obsolete forms, and maintain a centralized list for periodic review and improvement, ensuring efficient and effective form usage.
