Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions (20 Marks)

1.
Which one of the following sugar is a monosaccharide?
A. Galactose
B. Maltose
C. Sucrose
D. Lactose
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Galactose

Explanation: Monosaccharides are the simplest forms of sugar and the most basic units of carbohydrates. The three common monosaccharides are Glucose, Fructose, and Galactose.

Why other options are incorrect:

  • B) Maltose: A disaccharide made of two glucose molecules.
  • C) Sucrose: A disaccharide made of glucose and fructose.
  • D) Lactose: A disaccharide made of glucose and galactose.
2.
__________ is an impairment of health resulting from a deficiency of excess or imbalance of nutrients
A. Nutrition
B. Malnutrition
C. Diet
D. Nutritional status
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Malnutrition

Explanation: Malnutrition refers to deficiencies, excesses, or imbalances in a person's intake of energy and/or nutrients. It covers two broad groups of conditions: undernutrition (stunting, wasting, underweight) and overweight/obesity.

3.
Which one of the following is not a basic principle of nutrition?
A. Should be inadequacy
B. Should be nutrient dense
C. Should be of variety
D. Should of natural color
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Should be inadequacy

Explanation: The principles of diet planning include Adequacy (not inadequacy), Balance, Calorie control, Nutrient Density, Moderation, and Variety (ABCDMV). A diet should provide adequate nutrients, not be inadequate.

4.
The digestion of carbohydrate starts in the mouth through an enzyme known as
A. Lingual lipases
B. Amylases
C. Peptidases
D. Insulin
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Amylases

Explanation: Salivary amylase (ptyalin) initiates the breakdown of starch (carbohydrates) into maltose in the mouth. Lipases digest fats, peptidases digest proteins, and insulin is a hormone, not a digestive enzyme.

5.
Which one of the following is a conjugated protein?
A. Albumins
B. Globulins
C. Lipoproteins
D. Keratin
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Lipoproteins

Explanation: Conjugated proteins are simple proteins combined with a non-protein component (prosthetic group). Lipoproteins are composed of lipids and proteins. Albumin and Globulins are simple proteins; Keratin is a fibrous structural protein.

6.
One of the non-essential amino acids is
A. Methionine
B. Alanine
C. Valine
D. Lysine
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Alanine

Explanation: Alanine is a non-essential amino acid, meaning the body can synthesize it. Methionine, Valine, and Lysine are essential amino acids that must be obtained from the diet.

7.
Which of the following statements best describes what is complete proteins?
A. The one that contains most of the essential amino acids and with a few non-essentials
B. Contains all the essential amino acids in relatively different proportions as the human body requires
C. Those that may contain all the non-essential amino acids in good composition
D. Contains all the essential amino acids in the proportions needed by the body
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Contains all the essential amino acids in the proportions needed by the body

Explanation: Complete proteins (high-quality proteins) contain all nine essential amino acids in sufficient quantities to meet the body's needs. Animal proteins (meat, eggs, dairy) are typically complete.

8.
The storage form of lipids in the body is in
A. Triglyceride's
B. Micelles
C. Lipoproteins
D. Emulsifiers
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Triglyceride's

Explanation: Triglycerides are the main constituents of body fat in humans and other animals. They are the form in which fat is stored in adipose tissue.

9.
Lipids serve as a carrier of fat soluble vitamins which are
A. A, B1, C and D
B. E, B2, K and B12
C. A, D, E, K
D. D, C, K and E

Correct Answer: C. A, D, E, K

Explanation: The fat-soluble vitamins are Vitamin A, Vitamin D, Vitamin E, and Vitamin K. Vitamins B and C are water-soluble.

10.
__________ is the water contained within cells and accounts for two thirds of total body water
A. Intracellular
B. Extracellular
C. Intercellular
D. Metabolic
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Intracellular

Explanation: Intracellular fluid (ICF) is the fluid inside the cells, making up about two-thirds of the body's total water. Extracellular fluid (ECF) makes up the remaining third.

11.

A. Dissolve most of the substances

Correct Answer: A. Dissolve most of the substances

Explanation: Water is called the "universal solvent" because it dissolves more substances than any other liquid, which is critical for transporting nutrients and waste in the body.

13.
Which one of the following forms of vitamin A supports reproduction and is the major transport and storage form of the vitamin?

14.
Pellagra is caused mainly by a deficiency of dietary
A. Ascorbic acid
B. Cobalt
C. Niacin

Explanation: Pellagra is a disease caused by a lack of the vitamin Niacin (Vitamin B3). Symptoms include the 4 Ds: Diarrhea, Dermatitis, Dementia, and Death.

15.
The process of bone formation is called
A. Buffering
B. Ossification
C. Osteomalacia

16.
Which one of the following is the function of magnesium in the body?
A. Essential part of DNA and RNA which are essential for cell reproduction
B. Calcification of bones and teeth
C. Regulation of acids-base balance
D. Conduction of nerve impulses and for normal muscular contraction
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Conduction of nerve impulses and for normal muscular contraction

Explanation: Magnesium plays a critical role in nerve transmission and neuromuscular conduction. It also acts as a cofactor in hundreds of enzymatic reactions, including protein synthesis.

17.
__________ is a form of iron provided from plant sources and elemental components of animal tissues
A. Heme
B. Non-heme
C. Dietary
D. Sulphate
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Non-heme

Explanation: Dietary iron comes in two forms: Heme iron (found in animal flesh) and Non-heme iron (found in plant foods and also in animal tissues). Non-heme iron is less easily absorbed by the body.

18.
__________ are nutrients that must be obtained from food because the body cannot synthesize them in sufficient amounts
A. Essential nutrients
B. Non-essential nutrients
C. Micro-nutrients
D. Normal nutrition
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Essential nutrients

Explanation: Essential nutrients cannot be made by the body (or not in sufficient quantity) and must therefore be provided through the diet.

19.

A. Liquid oil
B. Electrolyte
C. Colostrum
D. Conductor
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Electrolyte

Explanation: Electrolytes (sodium, potassium, chloride, etc.) dissolve in water to form ions and conduct electricity, essential for nerve and muscle function.

20.
Which one of the following arise due to the antagonism of insulin hormone during pregnancy?
A. Morning sickness
B. Anemia
C. Gestational diabetes
D. Edema
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Gestational diabetes

Explanation: During pregnancy, placental hormones can cause insulin resistance (antagonize insulin), leading to elevated blood sugar levels known as gestational diabetes mellitus (GDM).

Section 2: Short Answer Questions (40 Marks)

21.
Highlight five consequences of iron deficiency anemia (5 marks)
Show Answer

Complete Answer:

  1. Fatigue and Weakness: Reduced oxygen-carrying capacity leads to physical exhaustion.
  2. Impaired Cognitive Function: Poor concentration, memory issues, and learning difficulties in children.
  3. Reduced Immunity: Increased susceptibility to infections.
  4. Developmental Delays: In infants and children, it can lead to delayed psychomotor development.
  5. Pregnancy Complications: Increased risk of preterm delivery, low birth weight, and maternal mortality.
22.
State five functions of iodine in the health of a person (5 marks)
Show Answer

Complete Answer:

  1. Synthesis of Thyroid Hormones: Essential component of thyroxine (T4) and triiodothyronine (T3).
  2. Regulation of Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR): Controls the rate at which the body uses energy.
  3. Physical Growth: Promotes normal growth and skeletal development.
  4. Brain Development: Critical for CNS development in fetus and infants (prevents cretinism).
  5. Prevents Goiter: Maintains normal thyroid gland size and function.
23.
Outline five functions of water in the body (5 marks)
Show Answer

Complete Answer:

  1. Transport: Carries nutrients and oxygen to cells and removes waste products.
  2. Temperature Regulation: Dissipates heat through sweating.
  3. Lubrication: Lubricates joints and moistens tissues (eyes, mouth).
  4. Solvent: Dissolves minerals, vitamins, and other nutrients for absorption.
  5. Structural Component: Maintains cell shape and turgidity.
24.
State five roles of fats in the body (5 marks)
Show Answer

Complete Answer:

  1. Energy Storage: Provides a concentrated source of energy (9 kcal/g).
  2. Insulation: Subcutaneous fat preserves body heat.
  3. Protection: Cushions vital organs (kidneys, heart) from shock.
  4. Cell Structure: Phospholipids are essential for cell membranes.
  5. Transport: Carries fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K).
25.
Outline five functions of proteins to the health of an individual (5 marks)
Show Answer

Complete Answer:

  1. Growth and Maintenance: Builds and repairs tissues (muscles, skin, organs).
  2. Enzymatic Function: All enzymes are proteins that catalyze metabolic reactions.
  3. Hormonal Regulation: Many hormones (e.g., insulin) are proteins.
  4. Immunity: Antibodies (immunoglobulins) are proteins that fight infection.
  5. Fluid Balance: Plasma proteins (albumin) help maintain oncotic pressure to prevent edema.

28.
Outline five causes of marasmus-kwashiorkor in a community (5 marks)
Show Answer

Complete Answer:

  1. Poverty: Inability to afford adequate food (both calories and protein).
  2. Ignorance/Lack of Education: Poor knowledge of weaning practices and balanced diet.
  3. Food Insecurity: Drought, famine, or seasonal shortages affecting food availability.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions (40 Marks)

29.
Discuss nutritional assessment methods using the ABCD method (20 marks)
Show Answer

Complete Answer:

The ABCD method is a comprehensive approach to nutritional assessment involving four components:

1. Anthropometric Measurements (A)

Involves measuring physical dimensions and body composition.

  • Weight & Height: Used to calculate BMI (Body Mass Index) to classify underweight, normal, or overweight status.
  • MUAC (Mid-Upper Arm Circumference): Used for rapid screening of malnutrition in children (Red < 11.5cm indicates severe wasting).
  • Head Circumference: Monitors brain growth in infants.
  • Skinfold Thickness: Measures subcutaneous fat reserves.

2. Biochemical Assessment (B)

Involves laboratory tests of body fluids (blood, urine) to detect sub-clinical deficiencies.

  • Hemoglobin (Hb): Detecting anemia.
  • Serum Albumin: Assessing protein status over the long term.
  • Serum Retinol: Assessing Vitamin A status.
  • Urinary Iodine: Assessing iodine levels.

3. Clinical Assessment (C)

Physical examination to identify clinical signs of malnutrition.

  • Hair: Discoloration, sparseness (sign of Kwashiorkor).
  • Eyes: Bitot's spots, night blindness (Vitamin A deficiency).
  • Skin: Dermatitis, pallor (Anemia/Pellagra).
  • Extremities: Edema (fluid retention), muscle wasting.

4. Dietary Assessment (D)

Assessing food intake and dietary habits.

  • 24-Hour Recall: Listing all foods consumed in the last 24 hours.
  • Food Frequency Questionnaire (FFQ): How often specific foods are eaten.
  • Diet History: Long-term eating patterns and preferences.
  • Food Diary: Prospective recording of intake.
30.
Malnutrition is a common condition among children arising from poor nutrition.

a. Discuss various types of malnutrition (10 marks)

Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Protein-Energy Malnutrition (PEM):
    • Marasmus: Severe deficiency of calories and protein. Characterized by extreme wasting ("old man face"), loss of muscle/fat, but no edema.
    • Kwashiorkor: Severe protein deficiency with adequate calories. Characterized by edema (moon face), pot belly, skin lesions, and hair changes.
    • Marasmic-Kwashiorkor: Mixed form showing wasting and edema.
  2. Micronutrient Deficiencies ("Hidden Hunger"):
    • Iron Deficiency Anemia: Lack of iron.
    • Vitamin A Deficiency (VAD): Leading cause of preventable blindness.
    • Iodine Deficiency Disorders (IDD): Causes goiter and cretinism.
  3. Overnutrition:
    • Obesity/Overweight: Excessive fat accumulation due to energy imbalance, increasing risk of NCDs later in life.

b. Discuss factors that contribute to malnutrition (10 marks)

Show Answer

Answer:

According to the UNICEF Conceptual Framework of Malnutrition:

  1. Immediate Causes:
    • Inadequate Dietary Intake: Not eating enough food or lack of variety.
    • Disease: Infections like diarrhea, malaria, and measles impair nutrient absorption and increase needs.
  2. Underlying Causes:
    • Household Food Insecurity: Lack of access to food due to poverty or production failure.
    • Inadequate Care Practices: Poor breastfeeding, weaning practices, and hygiene.
    • Unhealthy Household Environment: Lack of safe water, sanitation, and health services.
  3. Basic Causes:
    • Socio-economic Factors: Poverty, lack of education (especially maternal).
    • Political/Structural: Poor resource distribution, war, and political instability.
