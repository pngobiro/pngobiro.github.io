Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions (40 Marks)

1.
MRSA is a common hospital acquired infection. The most frequently occurring pathogen identified with this disease is:
A. Escherichia coli.
B. Proteus.
C. Pseudomonas aeruginosa.
D. Staphylococcus aureus.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Staphylococcus aureus.

Explanation: MRSA stands for Methicillin-Resistant Staphylococcus Aureus. It is a strain of staph bacteria that has become resistant to the antibiotics commonly used to treat ordinary staph infections.

2.
A common bacterial cause of diarrhea that has been linked to the ingestion of undercooked beef is:
A. Escherichia coli.
B. Campylobacter.
C. Salmonella.
D. Shigella.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Escherichia coli.

Explanation: E. coli O157:H7 is specifically associated with undercooked ground beef (hamburgers). Salmonella is often linked to poultry/eggs; Campylobacter to poultry; Shigella to fecal-oral human transmission.

3.
The most serious complication of an open fracture is:
A. Infection
B. Muscle atrophy caused by loss of supporting bone structure.
C. Necrosis of adjacent soft tissue caused by blood loss.
D. Nerve damage.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Infection

Explanation: In open fractures, the bone is exposed to the environment, making osteomyelitis (bone infection) the most serious risk, potentially leading to delayed union, non-union, or amputation.

4.
Shock, as an immediate complication of fractures, is usually classified as:
A. Cardiogenic.
B. Hypovolemic.
C. Neurogenic.
D. Septicemic
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Hypovolemic.

Explanation: Fractures, especially of long bones (femur) or the pelvis, can cause significant internal hemorrhage, leading to hypovolemic shock. (Neurogenic shock is possible with spinal injuries, but hypovolemic is the general risk for fractures).

5.
When lifting objects, patients with low back pain should be encouraged to maximize the use of which of the following muscles:
A. Gastrocnemius.
B. Latissimus dorsi.
C. Quadriceps.
D. Rectus abdominis.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Quadriceps.

Explanation: Proper lifting technique involves bending the knees and using the strong leg muscles (quadriceps and gluteals) rather than the back muscles to lift the load.

6.
The sternum, a bone that is a site for hematopoiesis, is classified as a:
A. Flat bone.
B. Irregular bone.
C. Long bone.
D. Short bone
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Flat bone.

Explanation: The sternum is a flat bone. Flat bones contain red bone marrow and are significant sites for hematopoiesis (blood cell formation) in adults.

7.
Spinal cord injury can be classified according to the area of spinal cord damage. Motor deficits in the upper rather than the lower extremities, usually caused by edema in the cervical area, are classified as:
A. Anterior cord syndrome.
B. Brown-séquard syndrome.
C. Central cord syndrome.
D. Peripheral syndrome.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Central cord syndrome.

Explanation: Central Cord Syndrome is characterized by motor impairment that is greater in the upper limbs than in the lower limbs, often seen in hyperextension injuries in the elderly.

8.
Respiratory difficulty and paralysis of all four extremities occur with spinal cord injury:
A. Above C2.
B. At C6.
C. At C7.
D. Around C8.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Above C2. (High Cervical)

Explanation: Injuries above C4 (especially C1-C2) affect the phrenic nerve (C3-C5), causing respiratory paralysis and quadriplegia. Lower cervical injuries (C6-C8) may spare some diaphragm function.

9.
The Glasgow Coma Scale is used to determine the level of consciousness. A score considered indicative of a coma is:
A. 1.
B. 3.
C. 5.
D. 7.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. 7. (Generally 8 or less)

Explanation: A GCS score of 8 or less is generally defined as a coma. The lowest possible score is 3 (deep coma). Since the question asks for "a score indicative of a coma" and lists 7, this is the correct cutoff point contextually (though 3 and 5 are also coma, 8 is the threshold, making 7 a definitive coma score). Note: Score of 1 is impossible.

10.
For a lumbar puncture, the nurse should assist the patient to flex his or her head and thighs while lying on the side so that the needle can be inserted between the:
A. Fourth and fifth cervical vertebrae.
B. Fifth and sixth thoracic vertebrae.
C. Third and fourth lumbar vertebrae.
D. First and second sacral vertebrae.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Third and fourth lumbar vertebrae.

Explanation: The spinal cord typically ends at L1-L2 in adults. To avoid damaging the cord, lumbar punctures are performed in the subarachnoid space between L3-L4 or L4-L5.

11.
Fluid shifts during the first week of the acute phase of a burn injury that cause massive cell destruction result in:
A. Hypernatremia.
B. Hypokalemia.
C. Hyperkalemia.
D. Hypercalcemia.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Hyperkalemia.

Explanation: Massive cell destruction releases intracellular potassium into the extracellular space, causing Hyperkalemia in the acute/emergent phase.

12.
A full-thickness burn is
A. Classified by the appearance of blisters.
B. Identified by the destruction of the dermis and epidermis.
C. Not associated with edema formation.
D. Usually very painful because of exposed nerve endings.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Identified by the destruction of the dermis and epidermis.

Explanation: Full-thickness (3rd degree) burns involve destruction of the entire epidermis and dermis, extending into subcutaneous tissue. They are often painless (nerve endings destroyed) and do not typically blister (blisters are 2nd degree).

13.
The characteristic lesion of psoriasis is a:
A. red, raised patch covered with silver scales.
B. cluster of pustules.
C. group of raised vesicles.
D. pattern of bullae that rupture and form a scaly crust.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. red, raised patch covered with silver scales.

Explanation: The hallmark of plaque psoriasis is well-demarcated, erythematous (red) plaques covered with silvery-white scales.

14.
The most common preventable cause of valvular heart disease is:-
A. Marfan's syndrome
B. Calcium deposits and thrombus formation
C. Beta – hemolytic streptococcal infection
D. Congenital disease acquired intra uterine
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Beta – hemolytic streptococcal infection

Explanation: Rheumatic Heart Disease, caused by untreated Group A Beta-hemolytic streptococcal infection (Rheumatic Fever), is the leading preventable cause of valvular damage.

15.
The causes of secondary hypertension include:-
A. Renal artery stenosis, Cushing's syndrome, pheochromocytoma
B. Kidney failure, high sodium intake, renal artery stenosis
C. Fluid volume deficit, high sodium intake, increase in blood pressure
D. Tachycardia, high sodium intake, blood pressure
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Renal artery stenosis, Cushing's syndrome, pheochromocytoma

Explanation: Secondary hypertension has an identifiable underlying cause. Renal artery stenosis, Cushing's syndrome (endocrine), and Pheochromocytoma (adrenal tumor) are classic causes. "High sodium intake" is a risk factor for primary/essential hypertension.

16.
The primary reason for administering morphine to a patient with myocardial infarction is to:-
A. Sedate the client
B. Decrease the client's pain
C. Decrease the client's anxiety
D. Decrease oxygen demand on the client's heart
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Decrease oxygen demand on the client's heart

Explanation: While morphine relieves pain (B) and anxiety (C), the physiological goal in MI is to reduce cardiac workload and oxygen consumption (by decreasing preload/afterload and sympathetic drive), thus limiting ischemic damage.

17.
Allergic contact dermatitis is as a result of which type of hypersensitivity reaction?
A. Type I immediate hypersensitivity.
B. Type II hypersensitivity.
C. Type III hypersensitivity.
D. Type IV delayed hypersensitivity.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Type IV delayed hypersensitivity.

Explanation: Allergic contact dermatitis (e.g., poison ivy, nickel) is a cell-mediated (T-cell) delayed reaction, characteristic of Type IV.

18.
Which description best explains iatrogenic infections
A. Enterococci normally found in faecal matter are transferred to skin and cause wound infection
B. A TB carrier acquires HIV virus and develops active TB
C. A school girl takes contaminated water and develops cholera
D. An elderly woman develops an infection in the gums after tooth extraction
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. An elderly woman develops an infection in the gums after tooth extraction

Explanation: Iatrogenic infections are those caused by medical treatment or procedures (e.g., tooth extraction). Option A is endogenous; C is community-acquired.

19.
The sounds auscultated during taking of BP are referred to as
A. Percussion tones
B. Tympanic sounds
C. Korotkoff sounds
D. Mur mur sounds
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Korotkoff sounds

Explanation: Korotkoff sounds are the sounds heard over the artery when measuring blood pressure.

20.
One of the following is NOT a negative feedback mechanism, which one:
A. Control of blood pressure
B. Control of body temperature
C. Control of blood glucose
D. Control of blood clotting
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Control of blood clotting

Explanation: Blood clotting (coagulation cascade) is a classic example of Positive Feedback, where the reaction accelerates until the clot is formed. Homeostatic mechanisms like BP, Temperature, and Glucose control rely on Negative Feedback.

21.
The principle of doing good for another is terned as:-
A. beneficence
B. Autonomy
C. Justice
D. Veracity
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. beneficence

Explanation: Beneficence is the ethical principle of acting in the best interest of the patient (doing good).

22.
Pernicious anaemia is associated with
A. Gastrectomy, autoimmune destruction of gastric parietal cells
B. Vit B6 deficiency, folic acid excess
C. Excess intake of niacin, deficiency of folic acid
D. partial gastrectomy, vit B2 and B6 deficiency
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Gastrectomy, autoimmune destruction of gastric parietal cells

Explanation: Pernicious anemia causes Vitamin B12 deficiency due to lack of Intrinsic Factor, which is produced by parietal cells. This can result from autoimmune destruction or surgical removal of the stomach (gastrectomy).

23.
One of the following is characterised by the presence of reed stern berg cells
A. Burkitts lymphoma
B. Non hodgkins lymphoma
C. Hodgkins lymphoma
D. B cell lymphoma
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Hodgkins lymphoma

Explanation: The presence of Reed-Sternberg cells (large, multi-nucleated cells) is the diagnostic hallmark of Hodgkin's Lymphoma.

24.
The type of haemophilia resulting from deficient factor ix is
A. Haemophilia A
B. Haemophilia B
C. Von willebrand disease
D. Haemophillia C
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Haemophilia B

Explanation:
- Hemophilia A = Factor VIII deficiency.
- Hemophilia B (Christmas Disease) = Factor IX deficiency.
- Hemophilia C = Factor XI deficiency.

25.
A nurse applies a cold towel to a patient's neck to reduce body heat, this process encourages heat loss by:
A. Conduction.
B. Convection.
C. Evaporation.
D. Radiation
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Conduction.

Explanation: Conduction is the transfer of heat through direct contact (towel touching skin). Evaporation also occurs as the water dries, but the direct application of a cold object is conduction.

26.
The major manifestation of nephrotic syndrome is:
A. Hematuria.
B. Hyperalbuminemia.
C. Edema.
D. Anemia.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Edema.

Explanation: Nephrotic syndrome is characterized by massive proteinuria, hypoalbuminemia, and consequent generalized edema (anasarca). Hematuria is typical of Nephritic syndrome.

27.
An example of exocrine glands are the:
A. Adrenals.
B. Ovaries.
C. Parathyroids.
D. Sweat glands.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Sweat glands.

Explanation: Exocrine glands secrete into ducts (sweat, salivary). Endocrine glands (Adrenals, Ovaries, Parathyroids) secrete hormones directly into the blood.

28.
The nurse knows that the anterior pituitary gland is responsible for secreting all of the following except the:
A. Adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH).
B. Antidiuretic hormone (ADH).
C. Follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH).
D. Thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH)
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Antidiuretic hormone (ADH).

Explanation: ADH (Vasopressin) and Oxytocin are produced in the Hypothalamus and stored/secreted by the Posterior Pituitary.

29.
A hiatal hernia involves:
A. An extension of the esophagus through an opening in the diaphragm.
B. An involution of the esophagus, which causes a severe stricture.
C. A protrusion of the upper stomach into the lower portion of the thorax.
D. A twisting of the duodenum through an opening in the diaphragm.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. A protrusion of the upper stomach into the lower portion of the thorax.

Explanation: A hiatal hernia occurs when part of the stomach pushes up through the diaphragm muscle (hiatus) into the chest cavity.

30.
Intervention for a person who has swallowed strong acid includes all of the following except:
A. Administering an irritant that will stimulate vomiting.
B. Aspirating secretions from the pharynx if respirations are affected.
C. Neutralizing the chemical.
D. Washing the esophagus with large volumes of water.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. Administering an irritant that will stimulate vomiting.

Explanation: Inducing vomiting (Option A) is contraindicated in corrosive poisoning (acids/alkalis) because bringing the corrosive substance back up causes further damage to the esophagus and risk of aspiration.

31.
The most common symptom that patients with gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD) mention is:
A. Dyspepsia.
B. Odynophagia.
C. Pyrosis.
D. Regurgitation.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Pyrosis.

Explanation: Pyrosis (Heartburn) is the hallmark and most common symptom of GERD.

32.
Two diseases common to the etiology of COPD are:
A. Asthma and atelectasis.
B. Chronic bronchitis and emphysema.
C. Pneumonia and pleurisy.
D. Tuberculosis and pleural effusions.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: B. Chronic bronchitis and emphysema.

Explanation: COPD is an umbrella term that primarily includes Chronic Bronchitis and Emphysema.

33.
There are four stages to general anesthesia. An unconscious patient with normal pulse and respirations is considered to be in the stage known as:
A. Induction.
B. Excitement.
C. Surgical anesthesia.
D. Medullary depression
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Surgical anesthesia. (Stage III)

Explanation:
- Stage I (Induction): Conscious to unconscious.
- Stage II (Excitement): Unconscious, irregular pulse/resp, struggling.
- Stage III (Surgical): Unconscious, regular/normal pulse and respiration, relaxed muscles.
- Stage IV (Medullary depression): Overdose, respiratory/cardiac failure.

34.
An example of a negative feedback process is, increased:
A. Aldosterone secretion in burn trauma, resulting in excess sodium retention.
B. Cardiac output in hemorrhage, resulting in increased blood loss.
C. Secretion of antidiuretic hormone (adh) in congestive heart failure, causing increased fluid retention.
D. Secretion of thyroid-stimulating factor (tsf), which stops when circulating thyroxin levels reach normal.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. Secretion of thyroid-stimulating factor (tsf), which stops when circulating thyroxin levels reach normal.

Explanation: Negative feedback works to maintain equilibrium. When thyroxin levels rise, they inhibit (stop) the secretion of TSH to prevent levels from getting too high.

35.
A recommended position for a patient in acute pulmonary edema is:
A. prone, to encourage maximum rest, thereby decreasing respiratory and cardiac rates.
B. semi-Fowler's, to facilitate breathing and promote pooling of blood in the sacral area.
C. Trendelenburg, to drain the upper airways of congestion.
D. upright with the legs down, to decrease venous return
Show Answer

Correct Answer: D. upright with the legs down, to decrease venous return

Explanation: Sitting upright (High Fowler's) with legs dangling helps pool blood in the extremities (decreasing venous return/preload) and eases breathing by lowering the diaphragm.

39.
The blood gas sequence that indicates a need for mechanical ventilation.
A. Decreasing PO2, decreasing PCO2, normal pH.
B. Increasing PO2, decreasing PCO2, increasing pH.
C. Decreasing PO2, increasing PCO2, decreasing pH.
D. Increasing PO2, decreasing PCO2, decreasing pH.
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. Decreasing PO2, increasing PCO2, decreasing pH.

Explanation: This pattern describes Respiratory Acidosis with Hypoxemia (Respiratory Failure). Low O2, high CO2 (retention), and low pH (acidosis) indicate the patient cannot ventilate effectively, requiring mechanical support.

40.
During assessment, the presence of a "barrel chest," in a patient is caused by:
A. A compensatory expansion of the bronchial airway.
B. A decrease in intrapleural pressure.
C. "Air trapping" in the lungs.
D. A progressive increase in vital capacity
Show Answer

Correct Answer: C. "Air trapping" in the lungs.

Explanation: Barrel chest (increased AP diameter) is classic in Emphysema/COPD due to hyperinflation and air trapping in the alveoli.

Section 2: Short Answer Questions (40 Marks)

41.
Outline five discharge messages you will give a patient after being admitted with iron deficiency anaemia. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Dietary Advice: Encourage intake of iron-rich foods (red meat, liver, spinach, beans).
  2. Vitamin C Intake: Advise taking iron supplements or foods with Vitamin C (citrus fruits) to enhance absorption.
  3. Medication Adherence: Finish the full course of iron supplements even if feeling better.
  4. Side Effects: Inform that stools may turn black (normal) and to take supplements with food if gastric upset occurs.
  5. Follow-up: Return for Hb check as scheduled to monitor progress.
42.
Outline the four classical features of Parkinson disease. (4 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Tremor: Resting tremor ("pill-rolling").
  2. Rigidity: Muscle stiffness (cogwheel rigidity).
  3. Bradykinesia: Slowness of movement.
  4. Postural Instability: Impaired balance and coordination.
43.
Describe five functions of the code of ethics in nursing. (5 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Inform the Public: It informs the public about the minimum standards of the profession.
  2. Professional Regulation: It provides a sign of the profession's commitment to the public it serves.
  3. Guidance: It outlines major ethical considerations and guidelines for professional practice.
  4. Discipline: It serves as a guide for self-regulation and peer review (discipline of errant members).
  5. Protection: It protects the nurse, the patient, and the employer by defining boundaries.
44.
Outline four types of surgery based on the purpose and give one example of each. (4 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Diagnostic: To confirm diagnosis (e.g., Biopsy, Exploratory laparotomy).
  2. Curative/Ablative: To remove diseased part (e.g., Appendectomy).
  3. Palliative: To relieve symptoms without curing (e.g., Colostomy for tumor obstruction).
  4. Reconstructive/Cosmetic: To restore function or appearance (e.g., Cleft lip repair, Skin graft).
45.
Discuss THREE (3) methods of determining the extent of burns by percentage total body surface area (%TBSA) (6 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Rule of Nines (Wallace): Body divided into multiples of 9 (Head 9%, Arms 9% each, Legs 18% each, Trunk 36%, Perineum 1%). Quick for adults.
  2. Lund and Browder Chart: More accurate method that accounts for age-related changes in body proportions (e.g., larger head in children).
  3. Palm Method (Rule of Palms): The patient's palm (including fingers) represents approximately 1% of TBSA. Useful for small or scattered burns.
46.
Explain three (3) causes of pleural effusion (6 marks)
Show Answer
  1. Congestive Heart Failure (Transudative): Increased hydrostatic pressure in pulmonary capillaries forces fluid into the pleural space.
  2. Infections (Exudative): Pneumonia or Tuberculosis causes inflammation and increased capillary permeability (Parapneumonic effusion).
  3. Malignancy (Exudative): Lung or breast cancer blocking lymphatic drainage or secreting fluid.
  4. (Other valid: Hypoalbuminemia/Liver failure causing reduced oncotic pressure).
47.
Discuss Quinine under the following headings:
Show Answer
  1. a) Class (1 mark): Antimalarial (Cinchona alkaloid).
  2. b) Indications (1 mark): Severe/Complicated Malaria (usually P. falciparum), or when first-line (Artemether) is unavailable/failed.
  3. c) Mode of action (2 marks): Inhibits parasite reproduction/DNA synthesis (Blood schizonticide).
  4. d) Side effects (2 marks): Cinchonism (tinnitus, hearing loss, headache), Hypoglycemia, Hypotension (if rapid IV).
  5. e) Describe iv quinine administration in adults (4 marks): Administer a loading dose (20mg/kg) diluted in 5% Dextrose, infused slowly over 4 hours. Follow with maintenance dose (10mg/kg) every 8 hours. Monitor blood glucose (risk of hypoglycemia) and cardiac rhythm. Never give IV bolus.

Section 3: Long Answer Question (20 Marks)

48.
Mr. K is admitted in the high dependency with a history of massive bleeding following an RTA.

a) Define shock (1 mark)

Show Answer

Shock is a life-threatening condition characterized by inadequate tissue perfusion and oxygenation, leading to cellular hypoxia and metabolic dysfunction.

b) State the 4 types of shock (2 marks)

Show Answer
  1. Hypovolemic Shock
  2. Cardiogenic Shock
  3. Distributive Shock (includes Septic, Neurogenic, Anaphylactic)
  4. Obstructive Shock

c) Describe the three stages of shock (6 marks)

Show Answer
  1. Compensated (Non-progressive): Body mechanisms (SNS, RAAS) maintain BP and perfusion. Signs: Tachycardia, cool skin, normal BP.
  2. Decompensated (Progressive): Mechanisms fail. Hypoperfusion worsens. Anaerobic metabolism begins (Lactic acidosis). Signs: Hypotension, oliguria, confusion.
  3. Irreversible (Refractory): Severe cell/tissue damage and organ failure occur. Shock becomes unresponsive to therapy. Death is imminent.

d) Explain the specific nursing management of Mr. K in the ward to discharge (11 marks)

Show Answer

Management of Hypovolemic Shock:

  1. Airway & Breathing: Maintain patent airway, administer oxygen (high flow) to maximize saturation.
  2. Circulation/Fluid Resuscitation: Insert large-bore IV cannulas (14/16G). Administer crystalloids (NS/RL) rapidly and prepare for blood transfusion (cross-match done).
  3. Positioning: Elevate legs (modified Trendelenburg) to improve venous return. Keep patient warm.
  4. Monitoring: Continuous monitoring of Vitals (BP, Pulse, RR, SpO2). Insert urinary catheter to monitor hourly urine output (indicator of renal perfusion). Monitor GCS.
  5. Stop Bleeding: Apply pressure to external wounds. Prepare for surgical intervention if internal bleeding.
  6. Medication: Administer analgesics for pain, antibiotics if open wounds (as prescribed).
  7. Laboratory: Monitor Hb/Hct, Electrolytes, Clotting profile.
  8. Diet: Keep NPO initially, then gradual reintroduction as stabilized.
  9. Rehabilitation/Discharge: Physiotherapy once stable. Health education on wound care, nutrition (iron/protein), and follow-up.
