üìö Evolution of the ICD

The ICD has undergone several revisions to improve its accuracy and usability. Here‚Äôs a summary of key milestones:

  • Sixth Revision: The international health congress in 1946 entrusted the interim commission of the WHO with reviewing the existing classification systems which resulted in the WHO endorsing the 6th revision in 1948.
  • Seventh Revision: In 1955, the 7th revision was held in Paris, focusing on essential changes and amendments of errors and inconsistencies.
  • Eighth Revision: In 1965, the 8th revision in Geneva, was more radical than the 7th but retained the basic structure of classification. This revision began to classify by etiology rather than manifestation where possible.
  • Ninth Revision: In 1975, discussions started on whether there should be a change rather than an update of the existing system which resulted in the decision that there should be changes.
  • Tenth Revision: In 1989, the tenth revision was created. This revision of the ICD expanded the system to include ambulatory care services and capture risk factors in primary care and include emergent diseases.

üìö Basic Structure and Principles of Disease Classification

Disease classification in the ICD is based on a hierarchical structure designed to group diseases based on common characteristics. Key structural elements include:

  • Categories: Diseases are grouped into broad categories such as ‚ÄúCommunicable Diseases‚Äù or ‚ÄúNeoplasms‚Äù.
  • Chapters: There are 22 chapters used to divide the ICD into broad classes of disease.
  • Blocks: Chapters are subdivided into homogenous ‚Äúblocks‚Äù of three-character categories.
  • Three-Character Categories: Some categories are for single conditions, while others are for common groups of diseases.
  • Four-Character Sub-categories: These are used to add specificity to the three-character categories.

Regardless of the purpose of the classification, whether practical or epidemiological, statistical data on diseases should be grouped as follows:

  • Epidemic diseases (communicable diseases)
  • Constitutional or general diseases that affect the whole body
  • Local diseases arranged by site
  • Developmental diseases
  • Injuries
  • External causes

The principles of classification are based on a common structure which has subdivisions of morbid entities that are assigned based on consensus criteria. These include:

  • Cause of the disease or condition (etiology)
  • Part of the body affected (anatomical site)
  • Circumstance of onset
  • Nature of pathological change in the tissue (morphology)
  • Resulting functional abnormalities or outcome (manifestation)
  • Quality of information available on the health report

üìö Volumes of the ICD

The ICD is organized into three volumes:

  • Volume 1: Tabular List, contains the main classifications
  • Volume 2: Instructional Manual, provides guidance to users of the ICD
  • Volume 3: Alphabetical Index to the classification

üìö Dual Classification

Dual classification is an important concept in ICD coding. It is often needed when a patient has multiple conditions ‚Äì a generalized underlying disease and a localized manifestation of that disease.

Dual classification provides insight into cases and helps support hospital systems, such as tracking food, drugs, and other non-pharmaceutical supplies.

üí° Pro Tip

Understanding the hierarchical structure and basic principles will help you navigate the ICD effectively. Remember, each component of the structure contributes to the system's accuracy and precision.

üìö Key Takeaways

  • The ICD has evolved through multiple revisions, each refining its classification system.
  • Disease classification is hierarchical, moving from general categories to specific subcategories.
  • The ICD has three main volumes each serving different purposes.
  • Dual classification addresses complex cases with multiple conditions by coding underlying causes and specific manifestations.
