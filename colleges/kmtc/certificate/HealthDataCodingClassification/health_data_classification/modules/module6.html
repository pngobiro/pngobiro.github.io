ðŸ“š Structure of ICD Volumes

Each of the three volumes of the ICD serves a unique purpose:

  • Volume I (Tabular List): Referred to as the "alphatabular," this volume begins with an introduction covering the practical developments of the ICD. The code numbers are arranged according to alphabetical prefix.
  • Volume II (Instruction Manual): This volume serves as a guide for users and provides guidelines and explanations. It is divided into six sections: instruction, descriptions of the ICSD, and related health problems, how to use the ICD, rules and guidelines for mortality and morbidity coding, statistical presentation, and the historical development of the ICD.
  • Volume III (Alphabetical Index): Contains an alphabetical list of diseases, injuries, external causes of injuries, drugs, and chemicals. It includes a section that provides details on how to use the index, the terms used in the index, and how to utilize the code numbers.

ðŸ“š Common Abbreviations

Several abbreviations are used within the ICD to save space and ensure consistency:

  • NEC: Not elsewhere classified. This is used to imply unspecified or unqualified conditions.
  • NOS: Not otherwise specified. This abbreviation is used to indicate conditions with no further clarification.

An optional additional code, often for the manifestation of that disease, is marked with an asterisk (*).

This system addresses the need to link underlying causes with specific manifestations of a condition. This method is used for the compilation of statistics relating to a particular area of medicine.

ðŸ“š Use of Parentheses, Brackets, Colons, Semicolons

Various punctuation marks have specific functions in the ICD coding process:

  • Parentheses ( ): Used to enclose supplementary words that do not affect the code assignment, and also used to enclose the code to which an exclusion term refers.
  • Square Brackets [ ]: Used to enclose synonyms, alternative words, or explanatory phrases, as well as to refer to previous notes or sub-divisions within the index.
  • Colons (:): Used in listings of inclusion and exclusion terms to indicate that the words that precede it are not a complete term for assignment to that rubric.
  • Semicolons (;): Used in listings of inclusion and exclusion terms to indicate that the words that precede it nor the words after it are not complete terms.
  • Point Dash: Used in 4th character sub category codes to replace a dash e.g T39.4.

ðŸ“š Other Conventions

  • "See" and "See also": Directs the coder to another term or location in the index for more information. "See" is a mandatory instruction and "See also" is a recommendation.
  • Exclusion Terms: Indicate that certain conditions are classified elsewhere and that coders should look for them in another category.
  • Non-Essential Modifiers: A lead term or sub term that may be listed in parenthesis. These may or may not be present in the diagnosis and do not change the code assigned.
  • Essential Modifiers: Are listed under the lead term and have a hyphen in front of them. They describe different types of disease or condition and different sites.

ðŸ“š Structure of Volume III

Volume III has a specific structure:

It contains lead terms which are positioned to the left of the column. These lead terms can have other words that modify or qualify the term.

  • Section 1. Includes the modifiers and qualifiers that are usually sites, or circumstances that affect coding.
  • Section 2: Includes different types of accident or occurrences and vehicles involved.
  • Modifiers that do not affect coding appear in parenthesis after the condition.

  • Volume III (The Alphabetical Index) lists lead terms with their qualifiers.
