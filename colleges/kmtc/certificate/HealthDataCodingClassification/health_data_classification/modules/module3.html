üìö ICD Chapters

The ICD classification is divided into 22 chapters. The first character of the ICD code is a letter, and each letter is associated with a particular chapter, except for the letter ‚ÄúD‚Äù which is used in both Chapter II, Neoplasms, and Chapter III, Diseases of the Blood and Blood-forming Organs and certain disorders involving the immune mechanism. The letter ‚ÄúH‚Äù is used in both Chapter VII, Diseases of the Eye and adnexa and Chapter VIII, Diseases of the Ear and mastoid process. Each chapter has its own content.

üìö Blocks and Categories

The chapters are subdivided into homogenous blocks of three character categories.

The blocks are based on the following:

  • Topography - anatomical site
  • Aetiology - causative factor
  • Morphology - histology, form, structure, malignant, benign
  • Function

These blocks are internally organised from A-Z.

The last character in the category is purely numerical, and it denotes the ICD chapter in which the disease belongs.

üìö Three-Character Categories

Three-character categories are used for single conditions or for groups of conditions with common characteristics. Some categories are selected due to the frequency, severity, or susceptibility to public health intervention of the conditions included. There is also provision for ‚Äúother‚Äù and ‚Äúunspecified‚Äù conditions.

üìö Four-Character Sub-categories

Three-character categories are subdivided by means of a fourth, numerical character after a decimal point, allowing for up to ten subcategories. Where a three-character category is not subdivided, it is recommended that the letter "X" be used to fill the fourth position so that the codes are of a standard length for data processing.

The fourth-character subcategories are used to identify different sites or varieties of disease or for individual diseases. The fourth character ‚Äú.8‚Äù is generally used for ‚Äúother‚Äù conditions belonging to the three character category, and ‚Äú.9‚Äù is usually used to convey the same meaning as the three-character category title without adding any additional information.

üìö Purpose of Disease Classification

Disease classification serves several important purposes. It:

  • Establishes a standardized health data system.
  • Provides a base for internal research activities.
  • Serves as a tool for teaching health professionals.
  • Supports health service management daily and geographically.
  • Provides a base for short term and long term health service planning.

üí° Pro Tip

When looking at an ICD code, pay close attention to the letters and numbers as each is significant in determining which category or subcategory it belongs to.

üìö Key Takeaways

  • The ICD is divided into 22 chapters.
  • Chapters are subdivided into blocks and categories.
  • Three-character categories are for single or groups of conditions, while four-character subcategories add more detail.
  • Disease classification is necessary for standardizing health data, aiding in research, teaching, and health service management.
