üìä Classification of Complications

Complications of plaster cast can be divided in systemic, which affects whole body or local which affects limb where plaster has been applied.

Local complications of plaster can be further classified as immediate and delayed.

üß¨

Systemic Complications

  • The most serious is deep venous thrombosis (DVT) leading to pulmonary embolism. Pain in the calf is an important sign needing medical advice.
  • Immobilization in trunk plasters or plaster beds may also produce nausea, abdominal muscle cramps, retention of urine and abdominal distention.

Good nursing and diet with regular exercises will help ensure that the initial period of extensive immobilization is achieved without complications.

02

‚ö° Immediate Local Complications

Swelling of the Part

A plaster produces constricting effect on the limb and most of it is well tolerated but a moderate constriction will produce compression of the veins, damming the blood, and causing swelling, discomfort or pain, and a blue color in the skin and under the nails.

Temporary remedies such as elevation of the limb and exercising the digits may be tried, but, if persistent, the constriction must be relieved. The cast can be split and eased or bivalved, taking care not to damage the skin.

Impaired Arterial Supply

üö®

Critical Warning

A pale skin which is cool and without a palpable pulse indicates that the arterial supply is disrupted. This is a serious complication. Medical advice must be sought immediately.

  • Splitting the cast may relieve the arterial compression but sometimes surgery may be necessary.
  • Incomplete arterial occlusion may present with pain or aching with loss of power. If in doubt ask for medical advice.

Pain

Pain has many causes. This may be due to tissue damage at injury or reduction, swelling within the cast, muscle spasm, pressure on blood vessels or nerves, skin irritation or sores. Although diagnosis may be difficult, persistent pain or intermittent acute pain should not be ignored. Medical advice must be sought.

üï∞Ô∏è Delayed Local Complications

Plaster Sores

The most common cause of sores is pressure of the plaster on the skin due to poor cast application. The patient may report burning, itching or stabbing pain. Children may have disturbed sleep and elevated temperature.

Signs of Plaster Sore

  • Heat and swelling of the digits.
  • Increased warmth over a localized area of the cast.
  • Localized odour.
  • Visible pus or staining of the cast.

Reasons for Development

  • Poor technique with inadequate padding, ridge inside cast, or failure to trim ends.
  • Local cast breakdown with skin irritation.
  • Foreign bodies (toys, coins, beads).
  • Scratching with implements.
  • Plaster soakage.
  • Cut edges irritating skin.
üí°

Clinical Note

Development of plaster sore is very painful. It is a constantly nagging pain that does not leave the patient. The patient is often able to pinpoint the sore area. If patient complains of unrelenting pain or digging sensation the part should be examined. It should not be ignored at any cost otherwise the results could be disastrous consequences.

Loss of Position

  • Because swelling occurs with most fractures especially after reduction, the technician puts padding under the cast to protect the skin. This padding gets compressed. After 48 hours when the oedema is subsiding, the cast may be too loose to hold the bone ends in position against undesirable muscle action.
  • Such displacement may be sudden and cause pain or gradual displacement being first noticed on the next x-ray. This complication may seriously delay sound healing and may produce permanent deformity.
  • Medical advice must be sought if the position is suspect.

Nerve Damage

  • Loss of power, tingling and numbness distal to the cast are signs of impaired nerve function. The cause may be direct compression by bone ends or plaster pressure, indirect compression of oedematous tissue or tourniquet effect, or reduced blood flow.
  • Routine testing of power and sensation will detect any defect quickly. Corrective action includes relieving cast pressure, supporting and protecting paralyzed parts, and physiotherapy to help restore normal function of muscle and joints.

Avoiding Complications

Complications of plaster cast can be reduced by taking all precautions of application of cast, keep a vigilant eye and making sure that patient is well instructed about care of cast.

  • Application of the plaster cast should be done by a skilled person in proper manner.
  • Patient, as a routine should always be called for follow up examination next day. Strict elevation of the limb should be instructed.
  • Patient should report on pain that is not relieved, swelling, bluishness or pallor of distal part.
  • Patient should be carefully examined in the follow up for probable complications of plaster cast.

Detailed Management Actions

1

Loss of Position (Fractures)

Swelling is the main feature of any fracture. A cast can be loose when the swelling has subsided leading to loose plaster. If left like that there will be movement of fragments. Solve that ‚Äì change P.O.P if swelling subsides.

2

Plaster Sores

Recognition: Heat, swelling, staining, odour (pronounced odour/visible pus).
Action: Encourage patient to pinpoint area. Window the part and inspect underlying skin. Cut by electric plaster cutter/saw. Dress the sore.

3

Loss of Power

Inability to extend fingers/toes. Can be due to pressure on superficial nerve, tourniquet pressure, or impaired circulation. Management: Split the cast to relieve tension.

4

Impairment of Circulation

Coldness, blueness, swelling. Management: Elevate part affected. Ensure digital exercise. If problems persist/discomfort, split P.O.P and elevate.

5

General Constitutional Complications

Stiffness (inactivity), Muscle wasting, Venous thrombosis (pulmonary infarction). Advise on exercise to maintain general circulation.

üõë Dangers & Recognition

Pressure

Skin: Cast pressure on bony prominences (e.g., acromion process, olecranon, radial styloid, iliac crests, head of fibula, ankle malleoli) compresses skin/subcutaneous tissues causing Localized Ischemia. Prolonged ischemia results in tissue necrosis and pressure sores.

Nerve: A superficial peripheral nerve may be compressed. Common peroneal nerve compression leads to anesthesia, paralysis, "foot drop", and loss of sensation on dorsolateral aspect of foot.

Constriction

Caused by circumferentially rigid pressure. If venous return is diminished, distal extremity becomes swollen/engorged. If arterial supply is stopped, limb becomes ischemic (gangrene risk).

Constriction from within: Most frequent cause is swelling of the limb after cast application. Blood soaked and clotted dressings under Plaster cast may also bind the limb tightly.

Recognition of Constriction: The 5 P's

Diagnosis begins with suspicion and is confirmed by subjective and objective findings.

1

Pain

Symptom of developing circulatory embarrassment. Pain is burning or cramping, not localized.

2

Paresthesia

Numbness of exposed fingers/toes. Prickly, tingly, or burning sensation.

3

Paralysis

Inability to move fingers/toes. Late finding in ischemic limb. May be due to pain or nerve injury.

4

Pulseless

Gentle pressure on nail bed causes blanching. Rapidity of return indicates circulation. Pulseless limb may still show capillary refill.

5

Pallor

Pale and cool digits (Poikilothermia). Compare with opposite side. Hypesthesia/anesthesia are ominous signs.

‚úÖ

Correction of Constriction

The result of prolonged circulating insufficiency may be amputation or irreversible tissue damage (Volkmann's ischaemic contracture). Constriction must immediately be relieved by removal of cast and division of all padding/dressing down to the skin. A cast may be bivalved and spread.

Summary: There is first suspicion, then observation and recognition, and finally prompt action to relieve compression.
