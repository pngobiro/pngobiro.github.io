Definition

Waste management refers to the many methods and processes of dealing with waste at every stage from generation and collection through to final disposal.

  • Waste can be categorized as liquid waste or solid waste depending on its physical state.
  • It can also be categorized as hazardous or non-hazardous.

Hazardous Wastes

Hazardous wastes are defined as wastes that have one or more of the following properties:

Non-hazardous Wastes

Non-hazardous wastes include all other types of waste.

Biodegradable wastes: Those that can be broken down (decomposed) into their constituent elements by bacteria and other microorganisms. Example: Human and animal wastes, food waste, paper, and agricultural wastes.

Non-biodegradable wastes: Wastes that cannot be easily broken down by biological processes.

üíß Liquid Waste

Liquid waste includes human waste, runoff (storm water or flood water), sullage, industrial wastewater and other forms of wastewater from different sources.

Human Waste

Mainly composed of faeces and urine, which together are known as excreta. All human body waste is classed as liquid waste.

Sewage

The mixture of human waste with wastewater (sometimes known as blackwater).

Runoff

Simply rainwater that collects on the ground and runs off into channels, ditches and rivers.

Sullage (Greywater)

Water that has been used for washing in bathrooms and kitchens; it does not include human waste.

Note: The decomposing bacteria found in pit latrines and septic tanks do not require oxygen and are called anaerobic bacteria. The digested waste matter is called sludge.

Liquid Waste Management Requirements

Basic requirements for a human waste (excreta) disposal method:

  • Surface water must not be contaminated.
  • There should be no contamination of groundwater that may, in turn, contaminate springs or wells.
  • Excreta should not be accessible to flies or other animals.
  • There should be no handling of excreta; where this is unavoidable, it should be kept to a minimum.
  • There should be no odours or unsightly conditions.
  • The method used should be simple and inexpensive in construction and operation.
  • The method should last for at least five years to be cost-effective.

Factors to Consider When Selecting Facilities

  • Topography and geological formations
  • Abundance or scarcity of water
  • Cultural acceptability
  • Affordability
  • Safety to users
  • Accessibility
  • Availability of resources and infrastructure
  • The presence of human skills
  • Energy source and pit emptying requirements

üóëÔ∏è Solid Waste

Solid waste is defined as any waste that is dry in form and is discarded by people as unwanted.

Classifications

Organic Solid Waste

Wastes that are generally biodegradable and decompose in the process of which emits offensive and irritating smell when left unattended.

Inorganic Solid Waste

Solid matter that does not decompose at any rate. This category of waste matter may be combustible depending on the type of the nature of the material they constitute.

Classes of Solid Waste

  1. Domestic wastes: e.g. Food wastes, Paper, cardboard, plastics, textiles, leather, yard wastes, wood, glass, metals, ashes, special wastes (e.g. Bulky items, consumer electronics, white goods, batteries, oil tires).
  2. Industrial wastes: Wastes from the manufacturing sector, example Housekeeping wastes, packaging, food wastes, hazardous wastes, ashes, and special wastes.
  3. Wastes from commercial: Stores, hotels, restaurants, markets, office buildings, etc. Paper, cardboard, plastics, wood, food wastes, glass, metals, special wastes, hazardous wastes.
  4. Institutional wastes: from Schools, hospitals, prisons, government centers.
  5. Construction and demolition: Example wastes from New construction sites, road repair, renovation sites, demolition of buildings Wood, Steel, concrete, dirt, etc.
  6. Street wastes: wastes from the streets and all activities there. Example cigarette ends, wrappers, fruits peels and bus tickets.
  7. Industrial process wastes: scrap materials, off-specification products, slag tailings.
  8. Agriculture wastes: from farms, gardens and ranches. Includes tree branches.
  9. Special wastes: from hospitals etc. example human tissues, amputated organs, bandages, reagents, expired drugs etc.

Risks and Problems Associated with Solid Wastes

  • It can be best media for the growth of microorganisms
  • Attraction of arthropods such as common housefly, mosquito, etc
  • Attraction of rodents and other animals e.g. rats mice dogs cats
  • Open dump can contaminate water sources
  • Can contaminate food supply and cause food borne disease
  • Hospital and pathological wastes are potential disease carrying waste products
  • It can create fire accident
  • It can create nuisance: Bad odor, smoke, dust Aesthetical problem
  • Discomfort: sneezing, coughing

‚öôÔ∏è Waste Management System

Why We Need to Manage Solid Wastes

  1. Prevent and control diseases.
  2. Eliminate nuisance.
  3. Prevent mechanical injury for example from broken bottles, syringes and tins.
  4. Prevent fires.
  5. Prevent pollution.
  6. Avoid wastage of resources.
  7. Creation of space.
  8. Prevent blockage of drains.

Definition & Elements

Solid waste management: Refers to the discipline associated with the control of generation, storage, collection, transfer and transport, processing, and disposal of solid wastes in a manner that is in accordance with the best principles of public health, economics, engineering, conservations, and that is also responsive to public attitudes.

Integrated Solid Waste Management (ISWM)

  • ISWM means considering not only the appropriate disposal of solid waste but integrating this with other management options such as minimising waste production, recycling, composting and other waste recovery options.
  • The advantages of ISWM are that it considers all options and aims to manage waste in ways that are most effective in protecting human health and the environment.
  • ISWM can also have many economic and social benefits for your community.

Solid Waste Disposal Methods

The major aim of refuse disposal is to change the physical characteristics of refuse and render it free from odor, less attractive to vectors and diseases.

  1. Crude dumping
  2. Hog feeding
  3. Incineration
  4. Sanitary landfill/controlled tipping
  5. Composting
  6. Dumping into water bodies

Major Sources

  • Hospitals
  • Veterinary clinics
  • Private clinics
  • Laboratories and research centres
  • Beauty parlors
  • Pharmaceuticals
  • Blood banks and blood collection services
  • Specialised health-care establishments: Nursing homes, disabled persons‚Äô institutions

International Categorization

  1. Low risk waste (Communal waste): Solid waste which does not contain high risk wastes, example bottles, office paper, boxes and packaging materials.
  2. Highly infectious wastes
  3. Infectious waste: Example blood and blood products, body fluids, items contaminated by blood, serum, plasma, cultures, bandages and surgical dressings.
  4. Anatomical wastes: Human tissues, body parts, fetuses, placenta, organs and tissues infected with human pathogens.
  5. Sharps: Needles and syringes, scalpel blades, suture needles, razors, infusion sets contaminated broken glass.
  6. Pharmaceuticals: Expired medications, discarded live vaccines.
  7. Radioactive: radiating residues produced as a result of radio therapy and diagnosis.
  8. Pressurized containers: Spray cans from morgues, cylinders containing gases, aerosols which when wrongly incinerated could explode.
  9. Wastes with high content of heavy metals: Example batteries, broken thermometers.

Key Steps in Management

  1. Generation/ waste minimization
  2. Segregation
  3. Containment
  4. Holding area/ storage
  5. Transportation
  6. Pre-treatment/destruction
  7. Disposal
