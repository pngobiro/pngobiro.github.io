Vector(s)

Arthropods or other invertebrates which transmit infection by inoculation into or through the skin or mucous membrane by biting or by deposit of infective materials on the skin or on food or other objects.

Rodents

Comprise a great number of mammals, ranging in size from the rats and mice to as large as the Porcupines and which belong to the order rodentia.

Biological Vectors

Vectors that transmit disease pathogens after the multiplication or development of the pathogen in the insect gut or muscle (e.g. malarial mosquito).

ü™∞

Mechanical Vectors

Vectors that transmit pathogenic microorganisms without undertaking any developmental change (e.g. common house fly).

üß™

Insecticides

Insecticides: Substances or an agent that kill insects or other arthropods.

Insecticide formulation: The addition of substances (solvent or diluents) which enable a given chemical insecticide to be used to greatest advantage.

Ways of Vector Borne Disease Transmission

01
Mechanical Disease Transmission

A type of disease transmission in which the vector is no more than a carrier that transmit pathogens without any change either on the number or form of disease pathogens.

02
Biological Disease Transmission

In this type of disease transmission certain developmental pattern exists either in the vector or host or in both cases.

03
Transovarian (Hereditary) Transmission

A type of disease transmission where by the causative agent is transmitted to the immature stage (usually to egg) from the adult insects / other arthropods who carry disease pathogens and when the infected egg complete its developmental stage, it become infective or can transmit the disease to man and other animals.

House Fly

Breeding Places

Houseflies can breed practically in any moist, decaying or fermenting human, animal and plant wastes. Favourite breeding media include:

  1. Human excreta: whenever exposed in open latrines, seepage pits, septic tanks or in open field.
  2. Animal wastes: horse manure, cow-dung, etc. whenever moist and decomposing.
  3. Garbage: whenever moist and exposed.
  4. Practically in any moist, rotting organic matter.

Public Health Importance

  • The housefly has been known to be the most dangerous contaminant of food, and transmitter of food borne and other diseases.
  • The housefly is known as a mechanical transmitter of disease agents, which means that it carries through its body parts disease agents (pathogens) from sources of infections to foods, food contact surfaces, to human eyes, and other exposed body parts.

Prevention and Control

  • Sanitation
  • Exclusion e.g. Make sure screens are tight fitting and without holes. Keep doors closed, making sure there are no openings at the top or bottom.
  • Sprays
  • Baits
  • Electronic traps

Fleas

Public Health Importance

  1. Flea Nuisance: The most widespread complaint concerns annoyance caused by bites, leading to discomfort and irritation.
  2. Plague: Caused by Yersinia pestis; primarily a disease of wild animals (especially rodents), transmitted to people.
  3. Flea-Borne Endemic Typhus
üõ°Ô∏è

Control Measures

Insecticide application (organophosphate or carbamate like 2% diazinon, 2% fenthion, 5% Malathion, 3-5% carbaryl). Cat and dogs coat can be sprayed with 2-5% malathion, 2-5% carbaryl or 1% permethrin.

Lice

There are two species parasitizing humans: Pediculus humanus (head & body lice) and Phthirus pubis (crab louse).

‚ö†Ô∏è Pediculosis: Presence of lice on a person.
üö® Diseases: Louse-borne epidemic Typhus, Trench fever, Louse-borne Relapsing fever.

Control Measures

  • Good personal hygiene: Regular bathing, Washing and ironing of clothes.
  • Disinfestations of clothes by boiling or steaming.
  • Sunshine exposure of clothing.
  • Hair hygiene.
  • Better housing and facilities.
  • Delousing with insecticide dusts (10% DDT dust).
  • Using pediculicides and malathion.

üêÄ Rodents

Types of Rodent

Three types of rodent are commonly associated with public health problems:

  1. Norway rats (Rattus norvegicus): Also known as the brown rat or sewer rat.
  2. Roof rats (Rattus rattus): Also known as the black or grey rat, more numerous in rural areas. They live in roofs, eat mainly grains. Smaller than Norway rats with longer tails and ears.
  3. Mice: Smaller in size than rats and generally prefer cereals to eat.

Public Health Importance

  • Disease transmission: Rats are natural hosts of fleas that may carry bubonic plague and murine typhus or endemic typhus from an infected rat to a human.
  • Food damage
  • Material damage

Basic Methods of Managing and Controlling Vectors

  • Basic sanitation: Targets elimination or reduction of environment that facilitates breeding and harborage.
  • Physical measures: Methods that stop vectors from getting into close contact with humans, and methods used to kill vectors.
  • Use of chemicals: Chemical insecticides for destruction of adults and larvae (DDT, malathion, pyrethrums).
  • Biological methods: Advanced methods preventing successful reproduction (e.g. sterilization of males).
  • Integrated approach: Integrated vector management includes a combination of two or more methods. Often more effective than single method.
