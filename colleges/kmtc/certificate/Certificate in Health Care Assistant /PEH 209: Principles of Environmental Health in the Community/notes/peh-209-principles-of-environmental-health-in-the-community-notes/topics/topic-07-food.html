Food is a major determinant of health, nutritional status and productivity of the population. It is, therefore, essential that the food we consume is wholesome and safe. Unsafe food can lead to a large number of food-borne diseases.

Definitions

üßº

Food Hygiene

Measures and conditions put in place to ensure food safety and quality throughout the entire food chain. Also: the action taken to ensure that food is handled, stored, prepared and served in such a way, and under such conditions, as to prevent as far as possible - the contamination of food.

üõ°Ô∏è

Food Safety

The assurance that food will not cause harm to the consumer when it is prepared and/or eaten according to its intended use.

‚ö†Ô∏è

Adulteration

When the normal content of the food has been intentionally changed by adding something to it that is not essential; for example, diluting milk with water and selling it as whole milk. Adulterated food could be unsafe for a number of reasons.

ü¶†

Contamination

The undesired presence of harmful microorganisms or substances in food. Food can be contaminated by unhygienic practices in storage, handling and preparation, and may compromise food safety and palatability.

Objectives of Food Hygiene

Food hygiene is primarily aimed at ensuring the safety, wholesomeness and soundness of food at all stages of production, preparation, marketing and distribution.

Objectives of food quality control services include:

  • Prevent food losses during harvesting, processing, storage, transportation and handling.
  • Protect consumers against being offered food that is harmful to health, not fit for human consumption, adulterated or presented in a deceptive manner.
  • Improve nutrition of the population.
  • Stimulate foreign exchange earning through export of foods that comply with acceptable standards.
  • Prevent importation of substandard foods.

‚ò£Ô∏è Hazards and Contamination

Types of Hazards in Food

Hazard is the relative probability that harm or injury will result when substance is not used in a prescribed manner and quantity.

üß±

Physical Hazard

Any physical material not normally found in food, which causes illness or injury and includes wood, stones, parts of pests, hair etc.

üß™

Chemical Hazards

Chemicals or deleterious substances which may be intentionally or un-intentionally added to foods. Includes pesticides, chemical residues, toxic metals, polychlorinated biphenyls, preservatives, food colours and other additives.

ü¶†

Biological Hazards

Living organisms and include microbiological organisms. Those micro-organisms which are associated with food and cause diseases are termed food-borne pathogens.

Sources & Routes of Contamination

Sources may be classified under: Food handlers, Equipment, Chemicals, Pests/parasites, and Water.

Routes of Microbial Contamination

  • Air and dust: Microorganisms are found everywhere in our environment. Many types can be found in air and dust, and can contaminate food at any time during food preparation or when food is left uncovered.
  • Soil, water and plants: Many microorganisms present in soil and water can contaminate foods. Microorganisms also grow on plants. Soil is a particularly rich source of Clostridium bacteria. Water may be contaminated by faeces. Plants may be contaminated by faeces if untreated sewage is used as fertiliser.
  • Gastrointestinal tract: The intestines of all humans and animals are full of microorganisms. Bacterial pathogens such as Salmonella, Campylobacter and Escherichia coli (strain O157:H7) are common examples. Contamination by faecal material is the major cause of food poisoning events.
  • Animals: Meat and poultry carcasses can be contaminated during slaughter by contact with small amounts of intestinal contents. Animal hides are a primary source of E.coli O157:H7 and Salmonella species.
  • Animal feeds: A source of microorganisms, especially Salmonella, which can contaminate poultry and other farm animals.
  • Food handlers: The presence of microorganisms on the hands and outer garments of food handlers reflects the standard of hygiene. Microorganisms transmitted to foods by food handlers may come from hides, soil, water, dust, GI tracts and other environmental sources.
  • Food utensils: Cutting boards, knives, spoons, bowls etc. used in food preparation may become contaminated during processing and preparation.
  • Cross-contamination: The transfer of harmful microorganisms between food items and food contact surfaces. Prepared food, utensils and surfaces may become contaminated by raw food products.
  • Unsafe temperature: If bacteria have warm, moist conditions and nutrients, one bacterium can reproduce (divide) every half an hour, producing 17 million bacteria in 12 hours.
  • Poor personal hygiene: Pathogens are transferred to the food from faecal matter present on the hands of food handlers.

üçÑ Food Spoilage

Definition: Spoiled food may be defined as food that has been damaged or injured so as to make it undesirable for human use.

Food Spoilage Agents

  • Microorganisms (Bacteria, Viruses, Fungi - Yeasts and moulds)
  • Enzymes
  • Chemical Reactions
  • Physical Reactions
  • Insects, rodents, and birds

Types of Food Spoilage

1. Microbial Spoilage

Primary causative agents are bacteria, yeasts and moulds. Change in taste, smell, colour, texture and appearance are the main features.

  • Odour: "Off odours" (like rotten eggs) produced when bacteria break down protein (Putrefaction).
  • Sliminess: Due to long chains of bacteria such as streptococcus.
  • Discoloration: Due to coloured spores (e.g., black pin mould on bread, blue/green mould on citrus).
  • Souring: When bacteria produce acids (e.g., milk sours from lactic acid).
  • Gas production: Gaseous by-products affecting food (spongy meat, swelling cans).
2. Enzymic and Chemical Spoilage (Autolysis)
  • Enzymatic browning: Initiated by polyphenol oxidase. Results in brown discoloration in fruits/vegetables (apples, potatoes, avocados). Requires enzyme, substrate (phenolic compounds) and oxygen.
  • Non-enzymatic browning: Reaction between reducing sugars and amino groups producing malanoidines (dark-brown substance). Example: browning of heated milk.
3. Insects, Rodents and Birds

Infect food with saliva/excreta, destroy grains (weevils), eat germ (rodents), lower harvest (birds).

4. Physical Injuries

Crushing/breaking, bruises, injuries from preservation methods (freezer burn, cold shortening).

Factors Affecting Spoilage Rate

  • Nature of food (nutrients)
  • Initial microbial load
  • Moisture content
  • PH of food
  • Temperature of air
  • Composition of air
  • Humidity
  • Exposure to sunlight

ü§¢ Food Borne Diseases

A disease caused by agents that enter the body through the ingestion of contaminated food. These agents may be infectious agents or toxic substances.

Classification

Food Borne Infections

Diseases whose etiologic agents are viable pathogenic organisms ingested with foods and that can establish infection.

Food Poisonings/ Intoxications

Diseases arising from the ingestion of toxins released by microorganisms, intoxications from poisonous plants or toxic animal tissues, or chemical poisons.

Sources of Food Poisoning

  1. Chemicals: e.g. cadmium from utensils, sodium fluoride accidentally added.
  2. Plants: e.g. young fava beans, poisonous mushrooms.
  3. Animals: e.g. ocean mussels/crabs containing poisonous alkali.
  4. Microorganisms and their toxins: The most common sources.

üõ°Ô∏è Prevention & Control

The Ten Main Reasons for Food Poisoning

  1. Inadequate cooling/refrigeration, food left at room temperature.
  2. Too long between preparation and consumption.
  3. Inadequate reheating.
  4. Inadequate cooking.
  5. Cross-contamination from raw to high risk/ready to eat foods.
  6. Infected food handlers.
  7. Inadequate hot holding temperatures.
  8. Inadequate hand washing.
  9. Contaminated raw foods and ingredients.
  10. Improper cleaning of equipment and utensils.

The Food Poisoning Chain

Breaking the Chain

01
Protecting food from contamination

Inspecting food, washing fruit/veg, separating raw/cooked, minimal handling, covering food, excluding pests, checking equipment, personal hygiene, protective clothing, correct cleaning, removing refuse.

02
Preventing bacteria from multiplying

Keeping high risk foods out of danger zone (below 4¬∞C or above 70¬∞C), minimizing time in danger zone during preparation, preservatives (salt/sugar), packing methods, keeping dried foods dry.

03
Destroying bacteria within food

Adequate cooking (min internal temp 80¬∞C), heat processing (pasteurization, sterilization, canning). Time + Temperature is key.

Five Keys to Safe Food

üßº

Keep Clean

Wash hands, sanitize surfaces, protect from pests, keep preparation areas and rubbish areas clean.

ü•©

Separate Raw & Cooked

Separate meats/poultry/seafood. Use separate equipment. Prevent juice spills. Store in covered containers.

üî•

Cook Thoroughly

Ensure thorough cooking (meat, poultry, eggs, seafood). Juices should be clear, not pink.

üå°Ô∏è

Store at Safe Temps

Don't leave cooked food at room temp >2 hours. Refrigerate promptly. Keep hot food >60¬∞C. Don't store too long. Don't reheat > once. Don't refreeze.

üíß

Safe Water & Materials

Use safe/treated water. Select fresh foods. Wash fruits/veg. Cut away damaged parts. Check expiry dates.

üîé Outbreak Investigation & Preservation

Objectives of Outbreak Investigation

  • Identify the causative agent responsible for the outbreak
  • Identify the food items, handlers, etc. responsible for the outbreak
  • Identify and trace the location of the source of the outbreak
  • Determine the conditions and mechanisms that led to the contamination (e.g. sanitary survey)
  • Limit the impacts and arrest the progression of the outbreak
  • Use information to prevent subsequent outbreaks

Steps in Investigation

  1. Verify the existence of an outbreak (Compare current cases with past; consider seasonal variations).
  2. Verify the diagnosis (Review clinical and laboratory findings).
  3. Describe the outbreak with respect to time, place and person.
  4. Prepare an epidemic curve.
  5. Calculate food-specific attack rates.
  6. Formulate and test hypotheses (consult higher levels).
  7. Search for additional cases.
  8. Analyse the data.
  9. Make a decision on the hypotheses tested.
  10. Intervene and follow-up.
  11. Report the investigation.
  12. Inform the public on control and prevention.

Common Methods of Food Preservation

Principles of Food Preservation include:

1. Prevention or delay of microbial decomposition

  • By keeping microbes out (asepsis).
  • By removal of microbes (e.g. filtration).
  • By reducing the rate of microbial growth (e.g. by low temperature, drying, anaerobic conditions and chemical inhibitors).
  • By killing microbes (e.g. by heat or radiation).

2. Prevention or delay of self-decomposition

  • By inactivation of food enzymes (e.g. blanching).
  • By prevention of chemical reactions (e.g. by using antioxidants).
