✓ It means the prevention of human contact with wastes, for hygienic purposes.
✓ It also means promoting health through the prevention of human contact with the
hazards associated with the lack of healthy food, clean water and healthful housing, the
control of vectors (living organisms that transmit diseases), and a clean environment. It
focuses on management of waste produced by human activities  |   |
|  <strong>types of sanitation relating to particular situations</strong>
• Basic sanitation: refers to the management of human faeces at the household level. It
means access to a toilet or latrine.
• Onsite sanitation: the collection and treatment of waste at the place where it is
deposited.
• Food sanitation: refers to the hygienic measures for ensuring food safety. Food hygiene
is similar to food sanitation.
• Housing sanitation: refers to safeguarding the home environment (the dwelling and its
immediate environment).
• <strong>Environmental sanitation</strong>: the control of environmental factors that form links in
disease transmission. This category includes solid waste management, water and
wastewater treatment, industrial waste treatment and noise and pollution control.
• <strong>Ecological sanitation</strong>: the concept of recycling the nutrients from human and animal
wastes to the environment.  |   |

○ Environmental risk factors are the environmental conditions and practices that
facilitate the carrying of infectious agents into our bodies.
○ Infectious agents are pathogenic (disease-causing) bacteria, viruses, fungi, protozoa
and parasites.
• To cause a disease, they must be introduced into our bodies in sufficient quantities.

○ Drinking water, which can be contaminated by human faecal matter that contains

<p>✓ Pollution prevention: It focuses on stopping pollution being produced in the first place, or reducing any waste generation at the source.
✓ Pollution control: those measures taken to control pollution and wastes after they have been generated or produced.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="principles-of-pollution-prevention">Principles of pollution prevention<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#principles-of-pollution-prevention" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>✓ Principle of waste optimisation: The motto in this principle is ‘Do not produce any waste; if this is not possible, reduce or minimise waste generation as much as possible’. There are three ‘Rs’ that are applied in waste optimisation: Reduce, Reuse and Recover.
✓ Polluter pays principle: This principle identifies the people or organisations who generate or produce waste or pollution as those who are accountable for any human or ecological damage. They are responsible for paying the costs of any damage. The principle is an economic tool to enforce accountability and responsibility. Strict standards for pollutant discharge permissions and enforcing heavy taxation on products or waste handling are ways of making the polluter pay.
✓ Principle of ‘Cradle to Grave’: This principle applies to the production of any object or to any activity by an individual or institution and all the pollution that object or activity might cause throughout its lifecycle; that is, from its ‘cradle’ to its ‘grave’.
✓ Precautionary principle: For any activity, there is an obligation not to cause harm even when someone is uncertain about the effect of the activity on humans and the environment. Under this principle, you take precautions to avoid environmental damage, even if you are not certain that damage will result.
✓ Principle of the right to know: The public has the right to information about pollution from a particular process.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="diseases-associated-with-water">Diseases associated with water<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#diseases-associated-with-water" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>

<li>Waterborne diseases are those caused by ingestion of water that is contaminated by human or animal excrement and contains pathogenic microorganisms. Transmission occurs by drinking contaminated water. Waterborne diseases include most of the enteric and diarrhoeal diseases caused by bacteria and viruses, including cholera, typhoid and bacillary dysentery. They also include diseases caused by protozoa (single-celled microorganisms) such as giardiasis, amoebic dysentery and cryptosporidiosis.</li>

<p>washing. They include scabies, trachoma, typhus, and other flea, lice and tick-borne diseases.</p>

<p>Water-based diseases are caused by parasites that spend part of their lifecycle in water. For example, schistosomiasis and dracunculiasis are both water-based diseases caused by helminths (parasitic worms). Schistosomiasis (also known as bilharzia) is caused by a worm that spends part of its lifecycle in the body of a particular species of water snail. Dracunculiasis or guinea worm is transmitted by drinking water that is contaminated with copepods (very small crustaceans) that contain the larvae of the worm.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="water-treatment">Water treatment<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#water-treatment" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>The purpose of water treatment is to reduce or remove all contaminants that are present in the water and to improve water quality so that it is completely safe to drink.</li>
<li>The types of water treatment processes depend on the characteristics of the raw water (untreated water direct from its source) and required water quality standards.</li>
<li>Suspended solids, bacteria, algae, viruses, fungi, minerals such as iron and manganese, and fertilisers are among the substances that are removed during water treatment. (Suspended solids are tiny particles of solid material that are carried along or suspended in the water.)</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="hot-water">Hot water<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#hot-water" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Hot water let out by power plants and industries that use large volumes of water to cool the plant result in rise in temperature of the local water bodies.</li>
<li>Thermal pollution occurs when industry returns the heated water to a water source.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="organic-chemicals">organic chemicals,<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#organic-chemicals" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Which include oil, gasoline, plastics, pesticides, cleaning solvents, detergent and many other chemicals.</li>
<li>These are harmful to aquatic life and human health.</li>
<li>They get into the water directly from industrial activity either</li>
<li>From improper handling of the chemicals in industries and more often from improper and illegal disposal of chemical wastes.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="control-of-water-pollution">Control of water pollution<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#control-of-water-pollution" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>The control of pollution should ideally take place at the point of generation, or, in other words, it should be prevented at source.</li>
<li>The control of excess nutrients is an important issue both from a public health perspective and to keep natural waters free from eutrophication. Farmers may need guidance on good agricultural practices that will help reduce water pollution from agriculture.</li>
<li>☑ Pollution prevention is best achieved by ensuring that each potential point source is properly sited, designed, constructed and managed;</li>
<li>Sources of pollution should be sited as far from water sources as possible (at least 15 m away) and below any water sources on the site.</li>
<li>☑ Appropriate use of excreta disposal, solid waste disposal and animal waste disposal will help prevent contamination of both surface and groundwater.</li>
</ul>

<p>Hazardous wastes are defined as wastes that have one or more of the following properties.</p>
<p>☑ Corrosive (substances that cause damage on contact, e.g. acids).
☑ Ignitable (materials that can catch fire easily like benzene).
☑ Toxic (materials that can be poisonous to humans when inhaled or ingested, or come in contact with skin or mucous membranes).
☑ Reactive (substances that can yield a harmful chemical if they react with other substances).</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="housing">Housing<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#housing" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>is the physical structure that man uses for shelter and the environs of that structure including all necessary services, facilities, equipment, and devices needed or desired for the physical, mental, and social wellbeing of the family and individual.</p>
<ul>
<li>The goal of housing is preserving one’s health. This is the purpose of a physical building and its immediate environment.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Standard Housing:</strong>- is a house that is properly planned and constructed, comfortable, safe to live in and fulfill the basic housing necessities ( constructed in proper site, provided with safe and adequate water supply, safe and proper waste disposal , adequate light, space, well ventilated, etc.</p>
<p><strong>Substandard Housing:</strong>- is a house, which is poorly sited, planned and constructed, inadequate floor space for the family (overcrowded), poorly maintained and does not in general comply</p>
<p>with the more important sanitary facilities.</p>
<p><strong>Slum:</strong>- is an area in which substandard housing predominates and frequently accompanied with overcrowding.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="definitions">Definitions<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#definitions" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Rodents:- comprise a great number of mammals, ranging in size from the rats and mice to as large as the Porcupines and which belong to the order rodentia.</p>
<p>Vector(s):- refers arthropods or other invertebrates which transmit infection by inoculation into or through the skin or mucous membrane by biting or by deposit of infective materials on the skin or on food or other objects.</p>
<p>Biological vectors- are vectors that transmit disease pathogens after the multiplication or development of the pathogen in the insect gut or muscle. E.g. malarial mosquito</p>
<p>Insecticides: are substances or an agent that kill insects or other arthropods.</p>
<p>Insecticide formulation:- is the addition of substances ( solvent or diluents ) which enable a given chemical insecticide to be used to greatest advantage.</p>
<p>Mechanical vectors- are vectors that transmit pathogenic microorganisms without undertaking any developmental change. E.g. common house fly</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="ways-of-vector-borne-disease-transmission">Ways of Vector Borne Disease Transmission:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#ways-of-vector-borne-disease-transmission" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ol>
<li>Mechanical disease transmission:- is a type of disease transmission in which the vector is no more than a carrier that transmit pathogens without any change either on the number or form of disease pathogens.</li>
<li>Biological disease transmission: In this type of disease transmission certain developmental pattern exists either in the vector or host or in both cases.</li>
<li>Transovarian (Hereditary) disease transmission:- is a type of disease transmission where by the causative agent is transmitted to the immature stage ( usually to egg ) from the adult insects / other arthropods who carry disease pathogens and when the infected egg complete its developmental stage, it become infective or can transmit the disease to man and other animals.</li>
</ol>

<p><strong>Definitions</strong></p>
<p><strong>CLTS</strong> refers to Community-Led Total Sanitation. This is an integrated approach to achieving and sustaining open defecation free (ODF) status.</p>
<p>CLTS entails the facilitation of the community’s analysis of their sanitation profile, their practices of defecation and the consequences, leading to collective action to become ODF</p>
<p><strong>OD</strong> means open defecation – defecating in the open and leaving the stuff exposed.</p>
<p><strong>ODF</strong> means open defecation free, that is, when no faeces are openly exposed to the air.</p>
<p><strong>Natural Leaders (NLs):</strong> are activists and enthusiasts (CLTS champions) who emerge and take the lead during CLTS processes. Men, women, youths and children can all be NLs. Some NLs become community consultants. They trigger and provide encouragement and support to communities other than their own. NLs are active through the processes of construction; innovation; monitoring; developing and implementing community norms and rules;</p>
<p><strong>When CLTS Approach can be used:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>In areas where OD is a common practice.</li>
<li>In communities which have not been previously reached by other hygiene and sanitation interventions.</li>
<li>In communities which have not previously received hardware subsidies for hygiene and sanitation.</li>
<li>Rural communities. However the approach is currently being adjusted to suit urban and peri-urban settings.</li>
</ul>

<p>Welcome to the last topic of this module where we will get to understand that food is a major determinant of health, nutritional status and productivity of the population. It is, therefore, essential that the food we consume is wholesome and safe. Unsafe food can lead to a large number of food-borne diseases.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="definitions">Definitions<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#definitions" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>☑ Food Hygiene- refers to Measures and conditions put in place to ensure food safety and quality throughout the entire food chain</li>
<li>☑ Food hygiene is the action taken to ensure that food is handled, stored, prepared and served in such a way, and under such conditions, as to prevent as far as possible - the contamination of food.”</li>
<li>☑ Food safety is the assurance that food will not cause harm to the consumer when it is prepared and/or eaten according to its intended use</li>
<li>☑ Adulteration is when the normal content of the food has been intentionally changed by adding something to it that is not essential; for example, diluting milk with water and selling it as whole milk. Adulterated food could be unsafe for a number of reasons.</li>
<li>☑ Contamination is the undesired presence of harmful microorganisms or substances in food. Food can be contaminated by unhygienic practices in storage, handling and preparation, and may compromise food safety and palatability.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="objectives-of-food-hygiene">Objectives of Food Hygiene<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#objectives-of-food-hygiene" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Food hygiene is primarily aimed at ensuring the safety, wholesomeness and soundness of food at all stages of production, preparation, marketing and distribution.</li>
</ul>
<p>42</p>
<ul>
<li>In order to achieve the above aim, food quality control services are put in place. Objectives of such services include.</li>
<li>Prevent food losses during harvesting, processing, storage, transportation and handling.</li>
<li>Protect consumers against being offered food that is harmful to health, not fit for human consumption, adulterated or presented in a deceptive manner.</li>
<li>Improve nutrition of the population</li>
<li>Stimulate foreign exchange earning through export of foods that comply with acceptable standards.</li>
<li>Prevent importation of substandard foods</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="types-of-hazards-in-food">Types of Hazards in food<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#types-of-hazards-in-food" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>Hazard is the relative probability that harm or injury will result when substance is not used in a prescribed manner and quantity.</p>
<p>Hazards can be physical, chemical and biological causing harmful / adverse effects on the health of consumers.</p>
<ol>
<li>Physical hazard is any physical material not normally found in food, which causes illness or injury and includes wood, stones, parts of pests, hair etc</li>
<li>Chemical hazards are chemicals or deleterious substances which may be intentionally or un-intentionally added to foods. This category of hazards includes pesticides, chemical residues, toxic metals, polychlorinated biphenyls, preservatives, food colours and other additives</li>
<li>Biological hazards are living organisms and include microbiological organisms. Those micro-organisms which are associated with food and cause diseases are termed food-borne pathogens.</li>
</ol>
<p>There are two types of food-borne diseases from microbial pathogens—infections and poisoning.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="routes-of-microbial-contamination-of-food">Routes of microbial contamination of food<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#routes-of-microbial-contamination-of-food" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<li>Bacteria are a major source of microbial contamination of food, i.e. the undesired presence in food of harmful microorganisms or the harmful substances they produce.</li>
<li>Viruses, parasites and fungi are also able to contaminate food and cause foodborne illnesses in humans.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="air-and-dust">Air and dust<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#air-and-dust" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Microorganisms are found everywhere in our environment.</li>
<li>Many types can be found in air and dust, and can contaminate food at any time during food preparation or when food is left uncovered.</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="soil-water-and-plants">Soil, water and plants<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#soil-water-and-plants" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Many microorganisms present in soil and water can contaminate foods.</li>
<li>Microorganisms also grow on plants and can contaminate food if care is not taken to remove them by washing or inactivate them by cooking.</li>
<li>Soil is a particularly rich source of <em>Clostridium</em> bacteria. Water may be contaminated by faeces. Plants may also be contaminated by faeces if untreated sewage has been used as a fertiliser.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="gastrointestinal-tract">Gastrointestinal tract<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#gastrointestinal-tract" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>The intestines of all humans and animals are full of microorganisms, some of which are beneficial but others are pathogenic.</li>
<li>Bacterial pathogens such as <em>Salmonella</em>, <em>Campylobacter</em> and <em>Escherichia coli</em> (strain O157:H7) are common examples.</li>
<li>Contamination of foods by faecal material is the major cause of food poisoning events.</li>
<li>This includes indirect contamination, for example from people’s hands if they prepare food without washing their hands after visiting the latrine/toilet.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="animals">Animals<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#animals" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Many foodborne microorganisms are present in healthy animals raised for food, usually in their intestines, hides, feathers, etc. Meat and poultry carcasses can be contaminated during slaughter by contact with small amounts of intestinal contents.</li>
<li>Animal hides are a primary source of <em>E.coli</em> O157:H7 and <em>Salmonella</em> species, both of which cause sickness and diarrhoea.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="animal-feeds">Animal feeds<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#animal-feeds" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<li>Animal feeds are a source of microorganisms, especially <em>Salmonella</em>, which can contaminate poultry and other farm animals.</li>
<li>The organisms in dry animal feed spread throughout the local environment and may get on to animal hides, hair and feathers, as well as on people who handle the feeds.</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="cross-contamination">Cross-contamination<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#cross-contamination" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>☑ Cross-contamination of food is the transfer of harmful microorganisms between food items and food contact surfaces.
☑ Prepared food, utensils and surfaces may become contaminated by raw food products and microorganisms.
☑ These can be transferred from one food to another by using the same knife, cutting board or other utensil without washing it between uses.
☑ A food that is fully cooked can become re-contaminated if it touches raw foods or contaminated surfaces or utensils that contain pathogens.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="unsafe-temperature">Unsafe temperature<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#unsafe-temperature" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>☑ An unsafe temperature for food storage is a major factor in food contamination.
☑ Many microorganisms need to multiply to a very large number before enough are present in food to cause disease in someone who eats it. However, if bacteria can have warm, moist conditions and an ample supply of nutrients, one bacterium can reproduce by dividing (on average) every half an hour and can produce 17 million bacteria in 12 hours! So, if you leave lightly contaminated food out overnight, it will be highly contaminated and infectious by the next day.</p>

☑ The most important contaminants of food are the microorganisms excreted with faeces from the intestinal tract of humans.
☑ These pathogens are transferred to the food from faecal matter present on the hands.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="types-of-food-spoilage">Types of food spoilage<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#types-of-food-spoilage" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p><strong>Definition:</strong> Spoiled food may be defined as food that has been damaged or injured so as to make it undesirable for human use.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="1-microbial-spoilage">1) Microbial Spoilage<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#1-microbial-spoilage" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>Microbial spoilage arises from the normal function of these organisms in the total ecology of all living organisms. In their normal metabolic processes, microorganisms destroy plant and animal organic matter by converting it into inorganic compounds. These primary activities of Microorganisms on foods bring about the phenomenon called spoilage.</p>
<p>Change in taste, smell, colour, texture and appearance are the main features of microbial spoilage.</p>
<p>The primary causative agents of microbial spoilage are the bacteria, yeasts and moulds.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="signs-of-microbial-food-spoilage">Signs of Microbial Food Spoilage<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#signs-of-microbial-food-spoilage" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>☑ Odour: “off odours” are smells (sometimes like rotten eggs) that are produced when bacteria break down the protein in foods, (usually fatty foods). This process is called <em>putrefaction</em>.</li>
<li>☑ Sliminess: Food becomes slimy as the bacterial population grows. The sliminess is due to long chains of bacteria such as streptococcus.</li>
<li>☑ Discoloration: Foods may be discoloured by microbial growth. The coloration is due to coloured spores that give the food a distinctive colour, e.g. black pin mould on bread, or blue and green mould on citrus fruit and cheese.</li>
<li>☑ Souring: Foods go sour when certain bacteria produce acids. A common example is when milk sours from the production of lactic acid.</li>
<li>☑ Gas production: Bacteria and yeasts often produce gaseous by-products that can affect food e.g. meat becoming spongy, swelling of canned food or a popping sound on open</li>
</ul>

<ol start="2">
<li>Enzymic and Chemical Spoilage</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Also referred to as autolysis. Autolysis is self-digestion emanating from factors within the food tissues themselves. These factors are mainly enzymes and chemical compounds. Examples of such spoilage are:</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Enzymatic browning - it is initiated by the enzyme polyphenol oxidase (polyphenolase). The action of this enzyme results in slightly brown discoloration accompanied by undesirable changes in flavor and texture in fruits and vegetables e.g. apples, potatoes, avocados, beetroot etc.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>In order for the browning to occur three components must be present - the enzyme, the substrate (phenolic compounds) and oxygen</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Non-enzymatic browning – Brought about by chemicals. It is found in many foods other than fruits, and results from a reaction between reducing sugars and compounds containing amino groups, which produce a dark-brown substance called malanoidines.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>An example of non-enzymic browning is the browning of heated milk.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<ol start="3">
<li>Insects, rodents and birds</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>Insects and rodents may infect food by infecting organisms with their saliva or deposit them with their excreta.</li>
<li>They also greatly destroy the quality of grains e.g. weevils burrowing in maize grains</li>
<li>Rodents lower the nutritive value of maize by eating the germ.</li>
<li>Birds can significantly lower the amount of harvest of some cereals by feeding on them while still in the field.</li>
</ul>
<ol start="4">
<li>Physical Injuries</li>
</ol>
<p>These can be in form of:</p>
<ul>
<li>Crushing/breaking due to excessive mechanical forces</li>
<li>Bruises arising from poor handling methods</li>
<li>Injuries from preservation methods e.g. "freezer burn" and "cold shortening" resulting from freezing.</li>
</ul>
<p>Factors affecting the rate of food spoilage</p>
<ul>
<li>The nature of the food itself - in providing nutrients for microbial growth</li>
<li>The initial microbial load in food</li>
<li>Moisture content of food</li>
<li>PH of the food</li>
<li>Temperature of air in storage environment.</li>
<li>Composition of air in storage environment.</li>
<li>Humidity of the storage environment.</li>
<li>Exposure to sunlight</li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li>The desired stage of development or maturity of the food.</li>
<li>Freedom from pollution at any stage in the production and subsequent handling of the food.</li>
<li>Freedom from objectionable, chemical and physical changes resulting from action of food enzymes; activity of microbes, insects, rodents, invasion of parasites; and damage from pressure, freezing, heating, drying, and the like.</li>
<li>Freedom from micro-organisms and parasites causing food borne illnesses</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="food-borne-diseases">Food Borne Diseases<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#food-borne-diseases" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="sources-of-food-poisoning">Sources of food poisoning<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#sources-of-food-poisoning" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>a) Chemicals - e.g. cadmium from utensils, sodium fluoride that may be accidentally added to food.
b) Plants - poisoning may result from consumption of plants or their products e.g. young fava beans, poisonous mushrooms etc.
c) Animals - e.g. consumption of ocean mussels and crabs during certain seasons of the year when they may contain a poisonous alkali.
d) Microorganisms and their toxins - these are by far the most common sources of foodborne diseases and the learner is therefore most likely to encounter these kind of outbreaks in his or her practice. It is therefore necessary to deal with this type of illnesses in greater detail.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="high-risk-foods">High-risk foods<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#high-risk-foods" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>They are ready to eat foods that under unfavorable conditions support the multiplication of harmful bacteria and intended for consumption without treatment which would destroy such organisms.</p>

<p>High risk foods are most likely to be involved in cases of food poisoning.</p>
<p>They are usually high in protein and moisture, requiring strict temperature control and protection from contamination</p>
<p>They include:</p>
<ul>
<li>☑ All cooked meat and poultry</li>
<li>☑ Milk cream, custards, dairy products and ice cream</li>
<li>☑ Cooked eggs, especially those made with raw eggs and not thoroughly cooked, for example mayonnaise</li>
<li>☑ Sea food for example cooked prawns</li>
<li>☑ Cooked rice (not high in protein)</li>
</ul>
<p>Low-risk foods</p>
<p>They are rarely implicated in food poisoning and may be stored, suitably packaged, at ambient temperatures.</p>
<p>They do not support multiplication of food poisoning bacteria.</p>
<p>Some examples include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Preserved food such as jam;</li>
<li>Dried foods or food with little moisture, such as flour, rice bread or biscuits. But once liquid has been added to powered food, such as milk, the food becomes high risk;</li>
<li>Acid foods such as vinegar or products stored in vinegar;</li>
<li>Fermented products;</li>
<li>Foods with high fat/sugar content, such as chocolate; and</li>
<li>Canned foods, whilst unopened.</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="protecting-food-from-contamination-by">Protecting food from contamination by -<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#protecting-food-from-contamination-by" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<li>Separating raw and high risk/ready to eat foods at all stages of preparation, storage, display and distribution.</li>

<p>The same equipment, utensils and working surfaces must not be used to handle raw and high risk/ready to eat foods.</p>

<li>Only handling food when unavoidable.

<li>Keeping food covered as much as possible.</li>
<li>Preventing insects, animals and birds from entering food rooms.</li>
<li>Not using unsuitable, defective, or dirty equipment.</li>
<li>Using good personal hygiene practices - always.</li>
<li>Not coughing or sneezing over or around food.</li>
<li>Not handling the food contact surfaces of crockery, cutlery or utensils.</li>
<li>All food handlers wearing suitable protective clothing.</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="common-methods-of-food-preservation">COMMON METHODS OF FOOD PRESERVATION<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#common-methods-of-food-preservation" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Principles of Food Preservation include the following:</p>
<p>i) Prevention or delay of microbial decomposition.</p>
<ul>
<li>☑ By keeping microbes out (asepsis).</li>
<li>☑ By removal of microbes (e.g. filtration).</li>
<li>☑ By reducing the rate of microbial growth (e.g. by low temperature, drying, anaerobic conditions and chemical inhibitors).</li>
<li>☑ By killing microbes (e.g. by heat or radiation).</li>
</ul>
<p>ii) Prevention or delay of self-decomposition of food</p>
<ul>
<li>☑ By inactivation of food enzymes (e.g. blanching).</li>
<li>☑ By prevention of chemical reactions (e.g. by using antioxidants).</li>
</ul>
