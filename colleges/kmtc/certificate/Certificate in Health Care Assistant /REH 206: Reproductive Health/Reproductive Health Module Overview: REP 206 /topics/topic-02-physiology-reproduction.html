üìÖ The Menstrual Cycle

Each month during the years between puberty and menopause, a woman's body goes through a number of changes to get it ready for a possible pregnancy. This series of hormone-driven events is called the menstrual cycle.

During each menstrual cycle, an egg develops and is released from the ovaries. The lining of the uterus builds up. If a pregnancy doesn't happen, the uterine lining sheds during a menstrual period. Then the cycle starts again.

Phases Overview

A woman's menstrual cycle is divided into four phases based on a 28-day cycle:

  1. Menstrual phase (Day 1 to 5)
  2. Follicular phase (Day 1 to 13)
  3. Ovulation phase (Day 14)
  4. Luteal phase (Day 15 to 28)

1. Menstrual Phase (Day 1-5)

Menstrual phase begins on the first day of menstruation when blood starts to come out of the vagina and lasts till the 5th day of the menstrual cycle. The following events occur during this phase:

  • The uterus sheds its inner lining of soft tissue and blood vessels which exits the body from the vagina in the form of menstrual fluid.
  • Blood loss of 10 ml to 80 ml is considered normal.
  • You may experience abdominal cramps. These cramps are caused by the contraction of the uterine and the abdominal muscles to expel the menstrual fluid.

2. Follicular Phase (Day 1-13)

This phase also begins on the first day of menstruation, but it lasts till the 13th day of the menstrual cycle. The following events occur during this phase:

  • The pituitary gland secretes a hormone that stimulates the egg cells in the ovaries to grow.
  • One of these egg cells begins to mature in a sac-like-structure called follicle. It takes 13 days for the egg cell to reach maturity.
  • While the egg cell matures, its follicle secretes a hormone that stimulates the uterus to develop a lining of blood vessels and soft tissue called endometrium.

3. Ovulation Phase (Day 14)

On the 14th day of the cycle, the pituitary gland secretes a hormone that causes the ovary to release the matured egg cell. The released egg cell is swept into the fallopian tube by the cilia of the fimbriae. Fimbriae are finger like projections located at the end of the fallopian tube close to the ovaries and cilia are slender hair like projections on each Fimbria.

4. Luteal Phase (Day 15-28)

This phase begins on the 15th day and lasts till the end of the cycle. The following events occur during this phase:

  • The egg cell released during the ovulation phase stays in the fallopian tube for 24 hours.
  • If a sperm cell does not impregnate the egg cell within that time, the egg cell disintegrates.
  • The hormone that causes the uterus to retain its endometrium gets used up by the end of the menstrual cycle. This causes the menstrual phase of the next cycle to begin.

üëµ Menopause

Menopause is the end of a woman's menstrual cycles. The term can describe any of the changes you go through just before or after you stop having your period, marking the end of your reproductive years.

Causes

Women are born with all of their eggs, which are stored in their ovaries. Their ovaries also make the hormones estrogen and progesterone, which control their period (menstruation) and the release of eggs (ovulation). Menopause happens when the ovaries no longer release an egg every month and menstruation stops.

Menopause is a regular part of aging when it happens after the age of 40. But some women can go through menopause early.

Premature Menopause

If it happens before age 40, for any reason, it's called premature menopause. Causes include:

  • Premature ovarian failure: When ovaries prematurely stop releasing eggs for unknown reasons.
  • Induced menopause: Result of surgery (hysterectomy with ovary removal) or damage from chemotherapy/radiation.

Symptoms

First Signs

Most women nearing menopause will have hot flashes, sudden feelings of warmth that spread over the upper body, often with blushing and sweating.

  • Uneven or missed periods
  • Vaginal dryness
  • Sore breasts
  • Needing to pee more often
  • Trouble sleeping
  • Emotional changes
  • Dry skin, eyes, or mouth

Later Symptoms

  • Fatigue
  • Depression
  • Crankiness
  • Racing heart
  • Headaches
  • Joint and muscle aches and pains
  • Weight gain
  • Hair loss
  • Changes in libido (sex drive)

Stages of Menopause

  • Perimenopause: Usually begins several years before menopause, when ovaries slowly make less estrogen. Lasts until menopause. In the last 1 to 2 years, estrogen levels fall faster.
  • Menopause: This is when it's been a year since you had a period. Your ovaries have stopped releasing eggs and making most of their estrogen.
  • Post menopause: The years after menopause. Menopausal symptoms such as hot flashes usually ease. But health risks related to the loss of estrogen increase.

Diagnosis & Treatment

Diagnosis

You can keep track of your periods and chart them as they become uneven. Your doctor might also test your blood for levels of:

  • Follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH): usually goes up as you near menopause.
  • Estradiol: tells your doctor how much estrogen your ovaries are making.
  • Thyroid hormones: shows problems with thyroid gland which can mimic menopause symptoms.
  • Anti-Mullerian hormone (AMH): helps learn about the reserve of eggs in ovaries.

Treatment Options

  • Hormone replacement therapy (HRT): Medications to replace hormones not being made. Helps with hot flashes and bone strength but has risks.
  • Topical hormone therapy: Estrogen cream, insert, or gel for vaginal dryness.
  • Nonhormone medications: Certain depression drugs, nerve drugs, or blood pressure drugs can ease hot flashes.
  • Medications for osteoporosis: Medicines or vitamin D supplements to keep bones strong.

Lifestyle Changes

  • Drink cold water, sit/sleep near a fan, dress in layers for hot flashes.
  • Use moisturizers/lubricants for dryness.
  • Exercise regularly.
  • Strengthen pelvic floor muscles (Kegels).
  • Don't smoke; limit alcohol.
  • Yoga, tai chi, acupuncture may help.

Complications

The loss of estrogen linked with menopause is tied to a number of health problems that become more common as women age:

  • Bone loss (osteoporosis)
  • Heart disease
  • Bladder and bowels that don't work like they should
  • Higher risk of Alzheimer's disease
  • More wrinkles, poor muscle power and tone
  • Weaker vision (cataracts, macular degeneration)

Note: You can still get a sexually transmitted disease after menopause. Use safer sex practices.
