üè• Maternal Health Overview

Maternal health refers to a woman's health and well-being before, during, and after pregnancy and encompasses aspects of physical, mental, emotional, and social health. The World Health Organization defines maternal health as: ‚Äúthe health of women during pregnancy, childbirth, and the postnatal period‚Äù.

Objectives of MCH Program

  • To reduce maternal morbidity and mortality due to pregnancy and child birth.
  • To reduce morbidity and mortality due to unsafe abortion.
  • To reduce perinatal and neonatal morbidity and mortality.
  • To reduce the levels of unwanted pregnancies.
  • To reduce the incidence and prevalence of STIs and HIV infection.
  • To reduce female genital mutilation and domestic violence.

Maternal Morbidity & Mortality

For every woman who dies, another 30 suffer long-term injuries and illness due to pregnancy and childbirth-related complications (e.g., fistula, infertility). Maternal morbidity is any symptom or condition resulting from or made worse by pregnancy.

Direct Causes

  • Severe bleeding (Hemorrhage): Antepartum, postpartum, abortion, ectopic.
  • Infections (Sepsis): Often after childbirth or unsafe abortion.
  • Hypertension: Pre-eclampsia and eclampsia.
  • Obstructed labour: Complications from delivery.
  • Unsafe abortion: Common cause of death.

Indirect Causes

  • The 3 Delays: Delay in seeking care, reaching care, and receiving care.
  • Poverty: Lack of funds for transport or services.
  • Accessibility: Distance to health facilities.
  • Cultural practices: Traditional beliefs affecting care seeking.
  • Diseases: Malaria, Anemia, HIV/AIDS.

Specific Health Problems

‚ñ∂ Hemorrhage (Bleeding)

Can occur during pregnancy (Antepartum), delivery (Intrapartum), or after birth (Postpartum).
Antepartum Hemorrhage: Placenta praevia, Placenta abruption.
Postpartum Hemorrhage (PPH): Atonic uterus, retained placenta, trauma, coagulopathy. Risk factors include multiparity, multiple pregnancy, anemia, and prolonged labour.

‚ñ∂ Puerperal Sepsis (Infection)

Occurs following long/complicated deliveries or unsafe abortion. Risk factors include home delivery, untrained assistance, multiple vaginal examinations, and prolonged rupture of membranes.

Prevention: Clean delivery practices, prophylactic antibiotics for prolonged rupture of membranes, and prompt evacuation of retained placental fragments.

‚ñ∂ Malaria & Anemia

Malaria during pregnancy increases the risk of severe anemia, spontaneous abortion, premature labour, stillbirth, and low birth weight. Anemic women have less tolerance to blood loss and higher risk of heart failure.

ü§∞ Antenatal Care (ANC)

ANC is the care given to expectant mothers to ensure safe pregnancy and a healthy baby. It helps identify high-risk pregnancies and provides preventive care.

Activities During Visits

Visit Type Key Activities
First Visit
  • Diagnose pregnancy & History taking
  • Physical & Lab Exams (Hb, VDRL/Syphilis)
  • Tetanus Toxoid (TT) Injection
  • Treat anemia & syphilis if present
Subsequent Visits
  • Measure Blood Pressure
  • Measure Symphysis Fundal Height
  • Follow-up TT doses
  • Monitor fetal growth & movement

Risk Factors Identifiable in ANC

  • Age under 18 or above 35
  • Primigravida (first pregnancy) or Grand multiparity (6+ pregnancies)
  • Previous C-section, vacuum, or forceps delivery
  • Previous perinatal death, stillbirth, or hemorrhage
  • Multiple pregnancy (Twins)
  • Medical conditions: Pre-eclampsia, Diabetes, Cardiac/Renal disease

Health Education Topics

Diet/Nutrition, Rest, Personal Hygiene, Danger signs, Birth preparedness, Breastfeeding, Family Planning, and avoidance of harmful substances (alcohol, tobacco).

üë∂ Infant Health & Immunization

Newborn Care

Immediate Care: Airway maintenance, cord clamping/cutting, thermal regulation (warmth), eye care, early breastfeeding initiation, weighing, and identification.

Daily Routine Care: Goal is to maintain homeostasis. Includes observation, hygiene (cord/skin/eye), breastfeeding support, and immunization.

Expanded Program on Immunization (EPI)

Immunization protects against specific diseases by administration of a vaccine (living modified, killed, or toxoid). It reduces susceptibility by producing antibodies.

Objectives of EPI

  • Reduce morbidity/mortality from six major diseases: Diphtheria, Pertussis, Tetanus, Measles, Polio, Tuberculosis.
  • Promote national self-reliance in delivery.

Delivery Strategies

1. Fixed Facilities

Permanent health institutions. Cost-effective but requires parents to travel.

2. Outreach/Mobile

Reaches remote areas. Staff travel to communities. More expensive due to logistics.

3. Campaigns

Intensive mass immunization (e.g., Polio days) to stop epidemics quickly.

Herd Immunity

When a large proportion of a community is immune (approx. 75%), the spread of disease is halted, protecting those who are not immune. Discontinuing vaccination risks epidemics.
