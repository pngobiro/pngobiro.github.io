ü©∫ Reproductive Tract Infections (RTIs)

Reproductive tract infections are infections of the genital tract. There are three main types, which often overlap:

1. Sexually Transmitted (STIs)

Passed through sexual activity. Examples: Chlamydia, Gonorrhea, HIV, Syphilis.

2. Endogenous

Overgrowth of normal organisms. Examples: Candidiasis (Yeast), Bacterial Vaginosis.

3. Iatrogenic

Introduced during medical procedures (abortion, IUD insertion, childbirth) due to poor infection prevention.

Principal Causes

‚ñ∂ Bacterial Infections
  • Gonorrhea: Caused by Neisseria gonorrhoeae. Can lead to strictures in men and infertility/PID in women.
  • Syphilis: Caused by Treponema pallidum. Has primary (chancre), secondary (rash), and tertiary (gummas/neurosyphilis) stages. Congenital transmission causes stillbirth.
  • Chlamydia: Causes inflammation of cervix/urethra. Major cause of preventable blindness (trachoma) and infertility.
‚ñ∂ Viral & Fungal Infections
  • Genital Herpes: Caused by HSV-2. Painful ulcers, recurrent.
  • HIV/AIDS & Hepatitis B: Viral, systemic effects.
  • Candidiasis (Thrush): Caused by Candida albicans overgrowth. Precipitated by antibiotics, pregnancy, diabetes.
  • Trichomonas vaginalis: Protozoal infection causing offensive discharge.

üìã Classification of STIs

Syndromic Classification

Syndrome Associated Conditions
Genital Ulcer Syphilis, Genital Herpes, Chancroid, Granuloma Inguinale
Urethral Discharge Gonorrhea, Chlamydia
Vaginal Discharge Candidiasis, Trichomoniasis, Bacterial Vaginosis
Lower Abdominal Pain Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID)
Scrotal Swelling Epididymitis

Prognostic Classification

  • Curable (Mostly Bacterial/Protozoal): Gonorrhea, Syphilis, Chlamydia, Trichomoniasis.
  • Incurable (Viral): HIV/AIDS, Herpes, Hepatitis B, HPV (Genital Warts).

üíä Management & Control

Syndromic Approach

Uses flowcharts to guide diagnosis and treatment based on signs and symptoms (syndromes) rather than laboratory tests.

Advantages

  • Prompt treatment at first contact (no lab delay).
  • Cost-effective (no expensive lab equipment).
  • Treats multiple possible causes simultaneously.
  • Standardized care usable by primary health workers.

Disadvantages

  • Over-treatment (patients receive drugs they may not need).
  • Less specific diagnosis.

Control Strategies

  • Prevention: Abstinence, Safer sex practices (condoms, reducing partners).
  • Early Treatment: Encouraging early seeking of care for patients and partners.
  • Screening: Asymptomatic patients (e.g., syphilis in pregnancy).
  • Vaccination: Hepatitis B, HPV.
  • Congenital Care: Prevention of congenital syphilis and neonatal conjunctivitis.

Public Health Significance

STIs are a major public health concern because:

  • HIV Link: Untreated STIs (especially ulcers) increase HIV transmission risk.
  • Complications: Can lead to Infertility (PID), Ectopic Pregnancy, Cervical Cancer, and adverse pregnancy outcomes (Stillbirth).
  • Economic Burden: High cost of treatment and loss of productivity.
