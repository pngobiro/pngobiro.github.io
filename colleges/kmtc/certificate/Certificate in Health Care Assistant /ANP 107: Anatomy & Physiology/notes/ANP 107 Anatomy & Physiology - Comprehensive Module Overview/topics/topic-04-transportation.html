ü©∏ The Blood

Blood is a viscous connective tissue composed of cells moving freely in a liquid portion called plasma. An average adult male (70 kg) has about 5-6 liters of blood (8% of body weight).

Functions of Blood

üöö

1. Transportation

  • Oxygen and carbon dioxide.
  • Nutrients.
  • Cellular metabolic waste and excess water.
  • Hormones from origin sites to target organs.

2. Regulation

  • pH Balance: Buffers help keep body fluid pH at about 7.4.
  • Fluid Balance: Proteins maintain osmotic pressure.
  • Temperature: Transports heat from muscles to other body parts.

3. Protection

  • Defenders against pathogens (WBCs).
  • Immunity factors (Antibodies).
  • Protection against blood loss (Clotting factors).

‚ù§Ô∏è The Heart

A muscular double pump located in the mediastinum.

Structure of Heart Wall

  1. 1 Endocardium: Thin, smooth inner lining (squamous epithelium-like). Forms valves.
  2. 2 Myocardium: Thickest layer, cardiac muscle.
  3. 3 Epicardium: Thin outermost layer, continuous with the pericardium (fibrous sac enclosing the heart).

Chambers and Partition

Divided into two sides by the Septum (Interatrial and Interventricular).

Right Side (Deoxygenated) Left Side (Oxygenated)
Right Atrium: Receives venous blood from body tissues. Left Atrium: Receives oxygenated blood from lungs.
Right Ventricle: Pumps blood to lungs (Pulmonary circulation). Left Ventricle: Thickest wall; Pumps blood to body (Systemic circulation).

Heart Valves

Valves ensure one-way blood flow.

Atrioventricular (AV) Valves (Entrance)

  • Tricuspid Valve (Right): Three cusps. Prevents backflow to right atrium.
  • Mitral/Bicuspid Valve (Left): Two heavy cusps. Prevents backflow to left atrium. Anchored by chordae tendineae.

Semilunar Valves (Exit)

  • Pulmonic Valve: Between right ventricle and pulmonary artery.
  • Aortic Valve: Between left ventricle and aorta.

Blood Supply (Coronary Circulation)

Myocardium is supplied by Right and Left Coronary Arteries (first branches of aorta). Blood drains into cardiac veins and the coronary sinus.

Conduction System

Specialized tissue for electrical impulse initiation and conduction.

  1. 1 Sinoatrial (SA) Node: "Pacemaker" in upper right atrium. Initiates heartbeat.
  2. 2 Atrioventricular (AV) Node: Delays impulse to allow atrial contraction.
  3. 3 Bundle of His (AV Bundle): Travels down interventricular septum.
  4. 4 Purkinje Fibers: Network stimulating ventricular contraction.

üõ£Ô∏è Blood Vessels

Functional Classification

Arteries:
Carry blood away from heart. Smallest are arterioles.
Veins:
Return blood to the heart. Smallest are venules.
Capillaries:
Connect arterioles and venules; site of exchange.

Structure

Arteries and veins have three layers (tunics):

  1. 1 Tunica Externa: Outer layer.
  2. 2 Tunica Media: Middle smooth muscle layer. Thicker in arteries to resist pressure and control blood pressure.
  3. 3 Tunica Interna (Intima): Inner endothelial lining.

Key Differences: Arteries have thicker walls. Veins have valves to prevent backflow. Capillaries have only one layer (tunica interna) to facilitate exchange.

05

üõ°Ô∏è Lymphatic System

Closely associated with the circulatory system. Drains tissue fluid (lymph) and fights infection.

Components

  • Lymphatic Capillaries: Simple squamous epithelium; drain waste from interstitial spaces.
  • Lymphatic Vessels: Three layers, have valves.
  • Lymphatic Ducts:
    • Right Lymphatic Duct: Drains upper right body ‚Üí Right Subclavian Vein.
    • Thoracic Duct: Drains rest of body ‚Üí Left Subclavian Vein.
  • Lymphoid Tissues:
    • Lymph Nodes: Filtration of lymph.
    • Tonsils: Filtration of tissue fluids.
    • Thymus: T-lymphocyte processing, thymosin secretion.
    • Spleen: Blood filtration, destruction of old RBCs.
