üìò Introduction & Objectives

Behavior depends on rapid information travel and processing. The nervous system is the body's communication network, handling information just as the circulatory system handles blood. The basic components of the nervous system are living cells called neurons and glia.

Objectives

  6. Understand the divisions of the nervous system
  7. Describe the normal structure and function of special senses

üî¨ Anatomy of the Nervous System

Neurons and Glia

Glia are cells that provide structure and insulation for neurons: neural "glue." Neurons are cells that receive, integrate, and transmit information.

Name Function
Astrocytes Support neurons, help maintain K+ level, contribute to the blood-brain barrier (BBB)
Oligodendrocytes Produce the myelin sheath to electrically insulate neurons of the CNS, provide support
Microglia Capable of movement and phagocytosis of pathogens and damaged tissue
Ependymal cells Line the ventricles of the brain; many have cilia; involved in circulation of CSF

Structure of a Neuron

A "typical" neuron consists of:

  • Soma: Cell body.
  • Dendrites: Feeler-like structures specialized to receive information.
  • Axon: Long, thin fiber that transmits signals away from the soma.
  • Myelin Sheath: Insulating material covering axons to speed up transmission.
  • Terminal Buttons: Small knobs at the end of an axon that secrete neurotransmitters.

‚ö° Action Potential & Synapse

Action Potential

When a neuron is stimulated, channels in the cell membrane open, allowing positive ions to flow into the electronegative inside. This shift in electrical charge traveling along the axon is an action potential.

Absolute refractory period: The minimum length of time after an action potential during which another action potential cannot begin.

Synaptic Transmission

The synapse is the point at which the nerve impulse passes from one neuron to another. Neurons do not touch; they are separated by the synaptic cleft.

‚ÑπÔ∏è

Mechanism

  1. 1Action potential reaches terminal button.
  2. 2Synaptic vesicles release neurotransmitters into the synaptic cleft.
  3. 3Neurotransmitters diffuse across the gap.
  4. 4Neurotransmitters bind to receptor sites on the postsynaptic neuron.

Postsynaptic potentials can be:

  • Excitatory: Makes the neuron more likely to fire.
  • Inhibitory: Makes the neuron less likely to fire.

3.6.1 Central Nervous System (CNS)

Consists of the Brain and Spinal Cord.

A. The Brain

  • Cerebrum: Largest part, divided into right and left hemispheres connected by corpus callosum. Divided into lobes: Frontal, Parietal, Temporal, Occipital.
  • Diencephalon: Contains Thalamus (relay station) and Hypothalamus (controls homeostasis, linked to pituitary).
  • Brain Stem:
    • Midbrain: Relay station for ascending/descending fibers.
    • Pons: Bridge between cerebellum hemispheres and higher brain levels.
    • Medulla Oblongata: Continuous with spinal cord; vital centers.
    • Reticular Formation: Core of brain stem involved in arousal.
  • Cerebellum: Ovoid shape, posterior to pons. Essential for coordination, posture, and balance.

B. The Meninges

Three layers of tissue membranes covering the brain and spinal cord:

  1. Dura Mater: Outer layer, dense fibrous tissue.
  2. Arachnoid Mater: Delicate serous membrane, separated from dura by subdural space.
  3. Pia Mater: Innermost layer, adheres to brain surface, contains blood vessels.

C. The Spinal Cord

Elongated cylindrical part of CNS, extends from medulla oblongata to L1 vertebra. Composed of grey matter in the center surrounded by white matter.

Reflex Arc: Pathway that nerve impulses travel when a reflex is elicited. 5 essential parts:

  1. 1Receptors
  2. 2Sensory neurons
  3. 3Central nervous system (synapse)
  4. 4Motor neurons
  5. 5Effector

Hearing (The Ear)

‚ñ∂ Structure of the Ear
  • Outer Ear: Auricle, external acoustic meatus, tympanic membrane (eardrum).
  • Middle Ear: Air-filled cavity containing ossicles (Malleus, Incus, Stapes). Connected to pharynx.
  • Inner Ear: Bony and membranous labyrinth. Contains Cochlea (hearing) and Vestibule/Semicircular canals (balance).

Sight (The Eye)

Three layers of tissue:

  1. Outer Fibrous Layer: Sclera (white of eye) and Cornea (transparent anterior).
  2. Middle Vascular Layer (Uveal Tract): Choroid (pigmented, vascular), Ciliary body (controls lens shape), Iris (colored part, controls pupil).
  3. Inner Nervous Layer: Retina (contains rods and cones).

Interior: Lens, Aqueous humor (anterior), Vitreous humor (posterior).

Smell and Taste

  • Smell (Olfaction): Chemoreceptors in roof of nasal cavity. Linked with taste.
  • Taste (Gustation): Taste buds on tongue papillae. Receptors for dissolved chemicals.
