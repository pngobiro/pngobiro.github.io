The Role of the HCA in Administering Immunisations and the B12 Injection

üìö Clinical Skills
‚è±Ô∏è 30 min read
üìù Quiz Available

Administering injections such as influenza and pneumococcal pneumonia vaccines is something that HCAs have been doing successfully now for several years. However, it wasn't that long ago that I attended a nursing conference when the first discussions about this task being delegated to HCAs were occurring, and there was uproar! Many nurses thought that in the absence of regulation and recognised HCA training, it was a step too far and was putting patient safety on the line. Now, of course, HCAs are routinely and safely administering flu, pneumococcal and shingles vaccines, and some HCAs are giving vitamin B12, vitamin D, heparin and insulin injections, as well as human papilloma virus vaccines.

What you do will depend very much on the area you work in and your local policy, but should also be dependent on you having the appropriate training and documentation (e.g. patient-specific directions) in place. You must also be up to date with resuscitation training and anaphylaxis management.

01

üìú Standards and Training

Immunisation programmes are one of the most successful public health measures to prevent disease, but it is essential to maintain public confidence in the programme to ensure its continued success. When confidence is dented, the uptake of vaccines goes down, with resulting peaks in disease levels. We must make sure that when people are given an injection, they can have confidence in the vaccination and in the person administering it.

National Minimum Standards (PHE 2015b)

  • HCSW must have completed relevant training and been assessed as competent.
  • The practitioner who delegates the role must be on a relevant professional register.
  • Any HCSW who immunises should receive specific training (min 2 days) and annual updates.
  • HCSWs should have access to The Green Book.
  • HCSWs must have an identified supervisor who is a registered practitioner.

ü¶† What is Vaccination?

Types of Immunity

ü§∞

Passive Immunity

Transfer of antibodies (e.g., via placenta, breast milk, or immunoglobulin injection). Provides short-term protection. No 'immunological memory'.

üõ°Ô∏è

Active Immunity

Long-lasting immunity where the body's immune system produces antibodies in response to a disease or vaccine. Creates memory cells for future protection.

Types of Vaccine

  • Live attenuated vaccines: Weakened organisms. Cannot cause the disease but may cause effects in immunocompromised people (e.g., nasal flu, shingles).
  • Inactivated/killed vaccines: Organism completely disabled (e.g., injectable flu).
  • Subunit vaccines: Segments of the pathogen (e.g., pneumococcal).

‚öñÔ∏è Legal Issues & PSDs

HCAs should only administer injectable medication to people over the age of 16, and can also administer intranasal vaccines to children aged between 2 and 18.

The Patient Specific Direction (PSD)

While registered nurses can work under Patient Group Directions (PGDs), HCAs must work under a Patient Specific Direction (PSD).

üìù

Patient Specific Direction (PSD)

A written instruction, signed by a doctor, dentist or non-medical prescriber, for medicine to be supplied and/or administered to a named patient after the prescriber has assessed the patient on an individual basis.

A PSD is NOT:

  • The same as a PGD.
  • A verbal instruction.
  • A generic instruction for any patient who fits criteria.
üí°

PSD Requirements

The PSD should include the surgery details, prescriber's name, HCA's name, and the name, dose, route, and frequency of the injection.

05

ü§ß Influenza

Influenza is an acute viral infection. There are three types (A, B, C). A causes epidemics. The virus changes (antigenic drift/shift), requiring annual vaccine updates.

ü§í Box 19.7 Symptoms of Flu
  • Sudden onset of fever
  • Headache
  • Myalgia (aching muscles)
  • Extreme fatigue
  • Dry cough
  • Nasal congestion

Recovery usually takes seven days or longer.

The Flu Vaccine

  • Injectable inactivated vaccine: For adults. Given IM (deltoid). Safe for immunocompromised.
  • Live Attenuated Influenza Vaccine (LAIV): Nasal spray for children (2-18). Live vaccine. Contraindicated in severe immunodeficiency, severe asthma, pregnancy.
06

üíâ Pneumococcal & Shingles

Pneumococcal Disease

Caused by Streptococcus pneumoniae. Can cause pneumonia, meningitis, bacteraemia. The vaccine (PPV23) is usually a single dose for adults 65+ and at-risk groups.

Shingles (Herpes Zoster)

Reactivation of the chickenpox virus. Causes a painful blistering rash in a dermatome pattern. Complication: Post-herpetic neuralgia (PHN).

07

ü©∏ B12 Deficiency

Vitamin B12 is essential for red blood cell production and nervous system health. Deficiency causes anaemia and neurological symptoms.

Causes of Deficiency

  • Pernicious anaemia (autoimmune)
  • Inflammatory bowel disease
  • Vegan diet
  • Long-term Metformin use

Injection

Hydroxocobalamin is given IM. Usually a loading dose followed by maintenance every 12 weeks.

08

üìã Giving an Injection: The Procedure

Preparation

  • Check patient identity and PSD.
  • Obtain informed consent.
  • Check for contraindications (allergies, illness, pregnancy).
  • Prepare equipment and check vaccine (expiry, cold chain).
01
Intramuscular (IM) Injection

1. Locate deltoid muscle (2 fingers below acromion).
2. Stretch the skin.
3. Insert needle at 90-degree angle.
4. Inject steadily (1ml/sec).
5. Remove needle, do not rub.

02
Subcutaneous (SC) Injection

1. Locate upper outer arm.
2. Bunch the skin.
3. Insert needle at 45-degree angle.
4. Inject steadily.
5. Remove needle.

03
Intranasal (LAIV)

1. Patient sitting comfortably.
2. Place tip just inside nostril.
3. Depress plunger with a rapid motion (fine mist).
4. Repeat in other nostril.
5. Patient should breathe normally, not sniff.
