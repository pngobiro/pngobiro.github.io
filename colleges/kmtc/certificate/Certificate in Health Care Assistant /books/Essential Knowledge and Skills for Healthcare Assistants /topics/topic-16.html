Examining the Feet of People with Diabetes

üìö Advanced Clinical Skills
‚è±Ô∏è 25 min read
üìù Quiz Available

Examining people's feet may not sound like a very pleasant job, but for people who have diabetes it is vital that their feet are checked regularly. This can be a very suitable role for the HCA or AP who has completed appropriate training. The examination may be performed by the HCA in conjunction with other tests such as BMI, BP and relevant blood tests, and the patient can then be seen in a follow-up diabetic clinic with the appropriately trained nurse.

As with everything else you do, it is always useful to have a clear understanding of why the problems might occur and what you are looking for when you perform the task. This chapter will explain some of the underlying reasons for problems in the feet of diabetic patients and will identify how you can check for these to determine the patient's risk of ulceration.

01

üß¨ An Overview of Diabetes

Diabetes mellitus is the medical term used to encompass Type 1 and Type 2 diabetes. The word Diabetes is Greek in origin, and means siphon (to pass through), referring to the glucose passing into the blood and urine. Mellitus is Latin in origin, and means honeyed or sweet. In medieval times, diabetes would be diagnosed by tasting the patient's urine to see if it was sweet. Fortunately, we have moved on a little in our testing techniques!

There are an estimated 4.6 million people with diabetes in the UK. This figure includes 1 million people who are yet to be diagnosed and are unaware that they have it (Diabetes UK 2017).

People without diabetes have a pancreas that produces a hormone called insulin. We need glucose in its simplest form to provide our cells with energy so that they can work efficiently. When we ingest glucose or more complex carbohydrates that are broken down into glucose, the insulin acts like a key to facilitate the entry of glucose through the cell wall and into the cell.

üíâ

Type 1 Diabetes

Autoimmune disease where the body destroys insulin-producing cells. Onset usually in younger people. Requires lifelong insulin. Accounts for ~10% of cases.

üíä

Type 2 Diabetes

Insufficient insulin production or insulin resistance. Associated with obesity, age, and lifestyle. Can be treated with diet, tablets, and/or insulin.

Obesity is the single biggest risk factor for diabetes, accounting for 80-85 per cent of the overall risk of developing type 2 diabetes. The good news is that this type of diabetes can be reversed in some patients who are able to lose weight.

ü¶∂ How Does Diabetes Cause Foot Problems?

Diabetic foot disease is still the most common cause of hospital admission in diabetic patients.

üîç Click to zoom

This is because of two main factors: peripheral neuropathy and peripheral vascular disease.

Peripheral Neuropathy

About half of patients with diabetes are likely to have some degree of peripheral neuropathy (Boulton 2005). This is nerve damage that is caused by increased glucose circulating in the blood. The glucose injures the walls of the arteries and prevents essential nutrients from reaching the nerves. Nerves carry messages of sensation and pain. If the nerves are damaged, patients may experience symptoms such as numbness, tingling, burning or stabbing pain, extreme sensitivity to touch, skin, hair or nail changes, and a lack of coordination. The patient may lose sensation in the feet and may not know if they have an injury.

‚ö†Ô∏è

Loss of Protective Sensation (LOPS)

This loss of protective sensation (LOPS) is the single most important risk factor in the development of a foot ulcer.

Severe neuropathy can also cause a rare deformity called Charcot foot. The bones in the foot become weak and can fracture, and if the patient continues to walk on these weakened or fractured bones, the foot will change shape and will develop the characteristic 'rocker bottom'. Sometimes there may be a deformity on the side of the foot as well (see Figure 16.2). The protrusion forms a point of increased pressure, and can ulcerate and become infected easily.

üîç Click to zoom

Figure 16.2 Charcot foot

Suspect acute Charcot arthropathy if the foot is abnormally red, warm, swollen or deformed. If Charcot foot is suspected, it should be treated as an active risk. Early diagnosis and management are important to avoid rapid progressive deterioration (Tidy 2016b).

Peripheral Vascular Disease

Diabetes is strongly associated with atherosclerosis, which is the narrowing of the arteries due to fatty deposits (atheroma). Associated risk factors include hypertension, smoking and hyperlipidaemia. Blood flow is reduced, and skin with a poor blood supply is more prone to damage and does not heal well. It is estimated that 10 per cent of people with diabetes will have a diabetic foot ulcer at some point in their lives (NICE 2015).

üõ°Ô∏è What Can We Do About It?

The NICE guidance (NICE 2015) recommends that adults who have diabetes should have their risk of developing a foot problem assessed on diagnosis, and then at least annually, when any foot problems arise and on admission to hospital.

Diabetes UK has coined the phrase 'Fast Track for a Foot Attack' to emphasise the seriousness of new or increasing symptoms, and to promote the concept of rapid access for treatment by a specialist multidisciplinary team to promote faster healing and fewer amputations.

All nurses and HCAs who are involved in the management of patients with diabetes should be able to perform a diabetic foot check and should receive training for this from a recognised organisation or qualified podiatrist.

üìã Checking the Feet: The Procedure

You can download the National Occupational Standard HWB63 from the NHS Knowledge and Skills Framework to use as a checklist for your competencies (Skills for Health 2011b).

Aims

  • üéØDetect risk factors for ulceration
  • üéØClassify the risk as low, increased or high
  • üéØRefer appropriately
  • üéØFeed back to the nurse or doctor

Step-by-Step Procedure

  1. 1 Confirm the identity of the patient.
  2. 2 Obtain informed verbal consent.
  3. 3 Prior to the patient removing their footwear, have a good look at it. Does it fit well? Is it enclosed? Does it support the foot well? Is it 'breathable'?
  4. 4 Check the history: Ask: Have you ever had a foot ulcer? Have you seen any cuts or blisters that you didn't feel? Do you have any discomfort or altered sensation? Do you have any problems looking after your feet?
  5. 5 The patient should be seated comfortably on an examination couch and legs should be bare from the knees down. Any socks, stockings or bandages must be removed.
  6. 6 Wash your hands.
  7. 7 Inspection:
    • Inspect the shape of the foot. Is there any evidence of deformity or a Charcot foot?
    • Inspect the skin on all areas of the lower legs and feet. Check behind the heels, between toes. Note colour, texture, callus, hair growth. Check for varicose veins or hemosiderosis (brown staining).
    • Note any bunions or calluses.
    • Check skin temperature using the back of your hand.
  8. 8 Vascular Assessment:
    • Check capillary filling time (press big toe, release, should return to pink in < 5 seconds).
    • Feel for the dorsalis pedis pulse (top of foot) and posterior tibial pulse (behind inside ankle).

üö¶ Classifying Risk (NICE 2015)

Risk Definition Action
Low Risk No risk factors present except callus alone (e.g. no loss of sensation, no signs of peripheral vascular disease). Annual foot assessments. Emphasise importance of foot care.
Moderate Risk One risk factor present (e.g. loss of sensation OR signs of peripheral vascular disease). Refer to podiatrist (seen within 6-8 weeks). Review in 3-6 months.
High Risk Previous ulceration/amputation, renal replacement therapy, OR more than one risk factor present. Seek advice from diabetic nurse/GP. Refer to podiatrist (1-3 months). Review every 1-2 months.
Active Presence of active ulceration, spreading infection, critical ischaemia, gangrene, acute Charcot foot. Seek immediate advice. Refer within one working day to multidisciplinary foot care service.

üè° Aftercare Advice

You can type out this advice and make it into a leaflet for your patient or use a leaflet from Diabetes UK:

  • üë£Look for and report any changes.
  • üë£Never try to deal with corns, calluses or verrucae yourself.
  • üë£Use moisturiser regularly (but not between the toes).
  • üë£Always cut nails straight across and never down at the sides.
  • üë£Wash feet daily and dry carefully.
  • üë£Never walk barefoot.
  • üë£Always wear socks with shoes (and sandals!).
  • üë£Shoes should fit well and be wide enough to reduce the risk of rubbing.
  • üë£Always feel inside shoes before putting them on.
  • üë£Check bath temperature before stepping in.
  • üë£Avoid the use of hot water bottles or electric blankets.
  • üë£Never sit too close to a fire.
