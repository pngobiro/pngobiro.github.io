The Skin and the Healing Process: Basic Wound Care

üìö Advanced Clinical Skills
‚è±Ô∏è 25 min read
üìù Quiz Available

As with all other clinical procedures, before you can embark on basic wound care, you must attend a recognised training course, have written evidence of your competence and have a clear protocol in place with care plans that have been drawn up by the registered nurse for each patient. A care plan does not need to be a long, complicated document, but can be a simple set of instructions on the computer notes, identifying things such as the duration of treatment, the type of dressing to be used and when to refer to the nurse for advice.

You should have knowledge of the skin and how it heals, and you must understand the principles of the aseptic non-touch technique (ANTT), which is discussed later in this chapter.

üß¨ Skin Anatomy

Skin is the largest organ of the body ‚Äî about two square metres in volume, weighing about six pounds. It basically keeps the outside out and the inside in! There are three layers in skin, and each layer has a specific function.

üõ°Ô∏è

Epidermis

The outermost layer that protects the body from the elements. It prevents the entry of harmful microorganisms and can dispose of harmful pathogens. It prevents loss of fluid and contains melanin cells (color/UV protection).

ü©∏

Dermis

The layer under the epidermis containing nerve endings, blood vessels, oil glands, sweat glands, collagen, and elastin. Regulates temperature and produces Vitamin D.

üßà

Subcutaneous Fat

Loose connective tissue largely made up of fat cells (adipocytes). Stores energy, keeps the body warm, acts as a shock absorber, and houses hair follicles.

üîÑ The Physiology of Wound Healing

It is worth stopping to think sometimes about everything that is going on in your body without you even realising. When you cut a finger or if you sustain a much larger wound, the body sets to work immediately to clean and heal the wound. It is quite amazing how efficient the body can be at looking after us, even when we may not be very good at it ourselves!

Haemostasis (meaning the stopping of blood flow) is the body's natural response to injury. The damaged blood vessels temporarily constrict to stop the bleeding. Activation of the clotting mechanism then occurs, resulting in the release of chemicals called clotting factors. Factor I, called fibrinogen, is converted to solid strands of protein called fibrin. The platelets become sticky and clump around the injured tissue. These are then reinforced by the strands of fibrin, forming platelet plugs, sealing the injury and initiating healing.

01
Inflammatory Phase (0-5 days)

Begins the healing response. There may be heat, redness, swelling and pain. White blood cells collect to remove dead tissue, foreign material and pathogens. Yellow slough may appear (normal at this stage).

02
Proliferation/Granulation Phase (3-14 days)

Tiny new blood vessels form in the dermis. Healing 'granulating' tissue should appear bright red, 'beefy' and moist. New skin cells multiply and migrate (epithelialisation). Requires a moist wound bed.

03
Maturation Phase (Day 7 - 12 months)

Progressive reduction in blood supply to the scar. Collagen fibres become bigger and reoriented to improve strength. The scar becomes paler and should eventually flatten and soften.

Types of Healing

  • Primary healing (First intention): The wound closes by the coming together of the wound edges (e.g. sutures or clips).
  • Secondary healing: Occurs where there has been tissue loss. The wound heals by the process of epithelialisation and wound contraction. No surgical intervention.

ü¶† Infection and Swabbing

Clinical signs of infection

Wound infection occurs when there are sufficient pathogens present in the wound so that they cause damage to the body tissue, and ultimately provoke a host reaction (Garner 2016). Wound swabbing should only be carried out when there are clinical signs of infection.

Taking a wound swab: the procedure

The decision on whether to perform a swab should be made by the registered nurse or doctor, but the task may be performed by the HCA.

  1. 1Explain the procedure to the patient and obtain informed consent.
  2. 2Decontaminate your hands and put on gloves.
  3. 3Irrigate the wound to remove slough, necrotic tissue or dried exudate using tap water or sterile saline. The wound must be moistened if it appears dry as this will improve the 'catch' of the swab.
  4. 4Move the swab across the wound bed in a zigzag motion at the same time as rotating the swab between the fingers, covering as large an area of the wound as possible, working away from the area that is cleanest. Include material from the wound bed and wound margin (Cooper 2010).
  5. 5Immediately place the swab back into the container with the transport medium.
  6. 6Remove your gloves and decontaminate your hands.
  7. 7Label the sample correctly and complete the necessary documentation.
  8. 8Record in the patient notes.
  9. 9Ensure transport of the swab to the laboratory as soon as possible (ideally within four hours).

ü©π Choosing the Wound Dressing

Each patient should have a clear care plan drawn up by the registered nurse. The choice of dressing should always be guided by the local wound care formulary. Dressings should be prescribed for individual patients and are single-use only. They should not be resealed and used again.

Box 17.2 Characteristics of the ideal wound dressing

  • Comfortable and acceptable for the patient.
  • Keep pain to a minimum.
  • Maintain a moist environment.
  • Remove excess exudate.
  • Allow the exchange of gases (oxygen/carbon dioxide).
  • Maintain the correct temperature.
  • Provide protection from further trauma.
  • Prevent the entry of microorganisms.
  • Leave no particles in the wound.
  • Be easily removed without causing trauma.
  • Be non-allergenic and non-toxic.

üõ°Ô∏è Reducing Infection Risk

Most healthcare-acquired infections are entirely preventable. The main infection risk to the patient is the health worker ‚Äì that's you! The level of risk can be minimised by using an effective aseptic technique during any invasive procedure, such as when performing a wound dressing. The aseptic non-touch technique, referred to as ANTT, is a framework for aseptic practice.

Asepsis is achieved by 'key part' and 'key site' protection:

  • Key parts: things such as the tip of a syringe or the wound side of a dressing.
  • Key sites: things such as open wounds and surgical incisions or any portal of entry on a patient.

Standard ANTT is suitable for the type of dressings most HCAs will be performing. The aseptic field is an important component of this to ensure a controlled safe working space. Use non-sterilised gloves if you can perform the procedure without touching key parts or key sites directly, and use sterilised gloves if you are likely to touch key parts or sites.

üè• Wound Dressing: The Procedure

You can download the National Occupational Standard SFHCHS12 Undertake Treatments and Dressing Related to the Care of Lesions and Wounds to use as a checklist for your competencies (Skills for Health 2011b).

Remember

  • Always use a clean trolley.
  • Wash your hands before, after and at any point during the procedure if they become contaminated. Put on a disposable plastic apron.
  • Use sterile equipment, fluids and dressing materials.
  • Do not do wound dressings if you have any hand infections, boils, a sore throat or a runny nose.
  • Never attempt complex dressings when you have not had appropriate training.
  1. 1Confirm the patient's identity and obtain informed consent before proceeding.
  2. 2Check the care plan and patient notes.
  3. 3Prepare the patient so that they are comfortable. Offer a chaperone.
  4. 4Put on gloves to remove the old dressing.
  5. 5Remove gloves. Decontaminate your hands.
  6. 6Note the condition of the wound. Seek advice if problems.
  7. 7Prepare the clean trolley. Open the sterile field by touching corners only.
  8. 8Drop sterile items onto the field. Place non-sterile items on the lower level.
  9. 9Decontaminate your hands. Put on gloves.
  10. 10Clean the wound if necessary by gently irrigating with warm tap water or sterile saline. Do not routinely clean wounds ‚Äì only if contaminated. Never use gauze/cotton wool ON the wound, only around it.
  11. 11Apply the dressing(s) according to instructions, without touching the side that will be in contact with the wound.
  12. 12Apply adhesive tape if necessary.
  13. 13Dispose of waste in clinical waste bin.
  14. 14Remove gloves and decontaminate your hands.
  15. 15Make sure the patient has a new appointment.
  16. 16Document your actions and observations.

‚úÇÔ∏è Removing Wound Closures

You can download the National Occupational Standard SFHCHS14 Remove Wound Closure Materials from Individuals to use as a checklist for your competencies (Skills for Health 2011b).

Steps 1-9 as above (preparation). Then proceed as follows:

  1. 1The nurse should assess the wound prior to suture removal.
  2. 2Remove alternate sutures to begin with in case the wound has not healed well.
  3. 3Remove the suture by pulling the knot up with forceps, and then cutting underneath the knot closest to the skin and pulling the suture through.
  4. 4If there are any problems, always check with the registered nurse before proceeding.
  5. 5Dispose of the sutures and sharps appropriately.
  6. 6Apply a dry dressing if required.
  7. 7Remove gloves and decontaminate your hands.
  8. 8Advise the patient to come back if they have any problems.
  9. 9Document your actions and observations.
