üìñ Definition of Terms & Concepts

üëÅÔ∏è

Supervision

The process of directing and supporting staff so that they may effectively perform their duties. A management function that is planned and carried out to guide, support and assist staff in carrying out assigned tasks.

Refers to an act by which any person inspects the work of other people, i.e., whether they are working properly or not. Also refers to the direct & immediate guidance & control of subordinates in the performance of their tasks.

It is direction, guidance & control of working force with a view to see:

  • (i) that they are working according to plan &,
  • (ii) are observing & adhering to time schedules.
ü§ù

Facilitative Supervision

A process that promotes quality at all health system levels by strengthening relationships, focusing on identification and resolution of problems and helping optimize the allocation of resources.

A process of guiding, monitoring & coaching workers to promote compliance with standards of practice & assure the delivery of quality care services.

Also known as supportive supervision. Supervisory process permits supervisors & supervisees the opportunity to work as a team to meet common goals and objectives.

üëî

Supervisors

Those having authority to exercise independent judgment in hiring, discharging, disciplining, rewarding & taking other actions of a similar nature with respect to employees.

Supervision is concerned with three main functions of management:

  1. A Directing
  2. B Guiding
  3. C Controlling

Facilitative Supervision (F.S) is a best practice for CHW supervision in international settings.

Principles of General Supervision

  • Right intensity: Lesser the weight of supervision higher the output. So a supervisor must be given optimum work.
  • Personal interest: Supervisors should take personal interest in the welfare of the workers under them. Consultations: A good supervisor must consult people under them. They can afford opportunities for free and frank discussion of issues and problems.
  • Loyalty: They must exhibit their loyalty to the subordinates by safeguarding their interests. They should not offend the dignity of workers.
  • Informal assessment: Informal appraisal of the performance of the subordinates and pointing out their shortcomings with a view to improve.
  • Broader interest: Supervisors are expected to take interest in management functions like planning, organizing, directing and controlling.
  • Work climate: They can create better work climate for successful achievements. Any strain in work climate will spoil the whole organization.

Difference between Traditional and Facilitative Supervision

Facilitative Supervision Traditional Supervision
It is objective It is superficial
Focuses on helping staff solve problems It is often punitive, fault finding and critical
Focus on processes rather than individual Focus on individual rather than processes
Assist staff in improving for the future Emphasizes the past rather than the future
Continuous and builds on past gains It is not continuous
Considerate and protects self esteem It is inconsiderate, harsh, sarcastic and has threats
Timely, occurs as soon as possible to correct Not timely/random

Why is Facilitative Supervision different?

  • Focuses on helping staff solve problems through using quality-improvement tools ‚Äì Traditional Supervision does not.
  • Focuses on processes rather than on individuals - Traditional Supervision does not.
  • Assists staff in planning for future quality-improvement goals - Traditional Supervision does not.
  • Is continuous and builds on past gains while setting higher quality-improvement goals - Traditional Supervision is not.

Traditional Supervision Characteristics

A superficial/Shallow process that only looks at the face value of the problem & not the root cause ‚Äì no set standards.

It is often punitive, fault-finding does not offer solutions.

Focuses on individuals rather than processes.

Does not endeavor to strengthen systems and processes that may cause disconnect between current outputs of health care to the expected standards.

Emphasizes the past rather than the future.

It's not continuous but it's rather intermittent.

Usually creates resentment, suspicion, and disintegration between team members rather than focusing on strengthening team work for quality service delivery.

üåü Qualities & Functions

Qualities of a Good Facilitative Supervisor

  1. 1 Ability to inspire, establish trust and promote team work
  2. 2 Good communication skills (listening and constructive feedback)
  3. 3 Desire to empower others and provide growth opportunities
  4. 4 Ability to work in teams
  5. 5 Expertise in specific technical knowledge
  6. 6 Flexibility
  7. 7 Openness to new ideas
  8. 8 Ability to train or convey information to others
  9. 9 Empathy

Additional Qualities:

  • Good listener
  • More knowledgeable in technical issues than the community health service providers (It is ok for the supervisor not to know everything, but they can enquire)
  • Resourceful (problem-solving skills) e.g. being able to make things happen when confronted by obstacles.
  • Supportive
  • Respectful
  • Acts as a role model
  • Has good mentorship and coaching skills

Functions of Supervision

üéì

Educative (Formative)

Professional development of the supervisee.

ü§ù

Supportive (Restorative)

Welfare of the supervisee.

üìÇ

Administrative (Normative)

Quality assurance, managing supplies, scheduling appointments, filing information, answering phone calls etc.

Facilitative Supervision Also Includes...

  • Collaborative reviews
  • Observations
  • Monitoring - identifies problems and opportunities
  • Coordination - support community health service providers in service delivery
  • Constructive feedback - emphasizing building confidence and self-esteem
  • Active participation
  • Problem-solving
  • Training and education

Emphasis & Benefits

Emphasis: Mentoring, Joint problem solving, 2-way communication between supervisor and those being supervised.

Benefits of Facilitative Supervision:

  • Fewer routine, low-level problems to solve
  • Less supervision work with less technical assistance since workers know how to do it
  • Gaining reputation as a leader, an effective supervisor, & an enabler
  • Always welcome at sites - you help staff solve their problems, rather than criticize them for their faults
  • Satisfaction of working as a team member, watching staff learn and grow and watching quality improve
  • More fulfilling jobs as staff's motivation & commitment increase
  • Staff will openly share problems

üìã Tasks & Challenges

Supervision Tasks

  • Monitoring: the professional & ethical aspects of supervision
  • Support: deals with personal issues as they emerge from work
  • Teaching: e.g., modeling & other methods such as role playing (recedes as experience is gained)
  • Evaluation: seasonally assessing the performance of the staff.
  • Consultation: to know what's happening with clients.
  • Administration: Help solve logistical challenges
    • Conduct spot-checks to ensure CHVs/CHOs are working as per established standards
    • Accompany individual CHVs on household visits to assess performance.
    • Improve cooperation by acting as a link between CHVs and the tier one facilities
    • Provide mentorship to CHVs
    • Discuss performance and provide constructive feedback on one-on-one with the CHVs
    • Ensure quality assurance in service delivery
    • Compile reports

Key Supervisory Tasks

Motivate & Provide feedback

  • Resolve performance problems
  • Blend employee goals with work requirements
  • Improve communications and keep employees informed
  • Responsibility for employee training and skills development

Professional Issues

  • Competency: provide services for which you are qualified by training and experience
  • Minimum amount of supervisor-supervisee contact: for example, no of hours per week, total number of hours
  • Number of supervisees allowed
  • Format of supervision: for example, individual vs. group, face-to-face vs. phone or e-mail, etc.
  • Documentation of supervisory activities

Ethical Issues

  • Autonomy: freedom of choice and responsibility of ones behaviour
  • Beneficence: Client/supervisee welfare, doing what is right
  • Non-maleficence: do not harm staff
  • Justice: treat all staff equally
  • Confidentiality: information obtained should not be shared out
  • Moral and legal standards: e.g., Fidelity (loyalty, faithfulness, promise-keeping)
  • Public statement: ensure that supervisees represent themselves correctly to the public
  • Professional relationships: facilitate further professional development for the supervisees

üö∂ Supervisory Visits

An activity conducted by supervisors to supervisees to check the progress of work discuss way forward and solve any challenges or conflicts that may exist.

Methods used during supervisory visits:

  • Observation
  • Dialogue/discussion
  • Review of records
  • Exit interviews
  • Community interviews

Good supervision involves interaction, is supportive, not punitive!

Frequency of Supervisory Visits

  • Frequently enough to provide needed support
  • Monthly to health facilities and community level
  • Quarterly to sub-counties
  • Every six months to Regional/Provincial level

Preparing for a supervisory visit

  • Review past performance at the CU
  • Find out about follow-up actions
  • Collect appropriate checklists and recording forms
  • Prepare to inform staff at the CU of any updates, plans
  • Collect supplies, equipment, and/or materials that can be delivered
  • Collect materials to take so that you will be prepared for problem solving
  • Confirm logistic arrangements

Steps to conduct a supervisory visit

01

Private interview with the person in-charge

02

A review and assessment of current practices, procedures, logistics (walk about)

03

A technical assessment of the performance of the health workers and the health services

04

A discussion of perceptions of services and activities with caregivers

05

Feedback of results

06

An opportunity for health workers to ask questions

07

Problem solving

08

Plan for next steps

09

Action after the visit

Techniques for giving feedback

  • Comments task oriented
  • Feedback should be prompt
  • Motivating
  • Action-oriented
  • Constructive

How to conduct a problem-solving discussion

  • Identify the likely cause (causes) of the problem
  • Responsibility of the task clearly assigned?
  • CHV/CHOs lack necessary knowledge and skills?
  • CHV/CHOs lack motivation?
  • Are there obstacles (time, authority, funds, supplies, etc.)
  • Define one or more feasible solutions
