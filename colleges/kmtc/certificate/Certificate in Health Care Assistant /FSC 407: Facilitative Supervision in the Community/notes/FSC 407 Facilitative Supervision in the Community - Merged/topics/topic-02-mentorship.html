Mentoring

A process for the informal transmission of knowledge, social capital and the psychosocial support perceived by the recipient as relevant to work, career or professional development.

It entails informal communication, usually face to face during sustained period of time, between a person who is perceived to have less.

A process of deploying experienced individuals to provide guidance and advice that will help to develop the careers of mentees allocated to them.

Mentoring is the transfer and exchange of information between a mentor and a mentee as they develop a long-term relationship.

üë§

Mentee

A person who takes charge of their own development and sees real value in learning from others (Goodman, 2004).

üîó Characteristics & Relationships

Characteristics of Mentorship

  • Takes place outside a line manager‚Äîemployee relationship
  • Is career focused or focuses on professional development that maybe outside the mentee‚Äôs area of work
  • Relationship is personal and crosses job boundaries
  • Relationship may last for a specific period of time
  • Dynamic - growth & knowledge transfer
  • Reciprocal relationship
  • Takes place within the work environment
  • Mentor is the wise career incumbent
  • Mentor is focused on helping the mentee (Less experienced) develop his or her potential
  • Mentor is not the direct supervisor of the mentee, but a third party such as a manager in a different dept
  • The mentee is developed in some specified capacity (in other words there must be a clear purpose and focus for development through mentoring)
  • Mentoring is not about being sponsored for promotion

Mentoring Relationship

A relationship between two or more people, (whether formalized or not) with a specific purpose.

The relationship is reciprocal & both parties benefit, albeit in different ways.

The mentor-mentee relationship is dynamic with different stages or phases.

Each mentor-mentee relationship is unique.

This relationship transcends duty & obligation & often involves coaching, networking, sponsoring & career counselling. (Meyer, M 2006).

Types of Mentoring Relationships

  • 1 Traditional One-on-one Mentoring ‚Äì A mentee & mentor are matched, either through a program or on their own. Mentee-mentor partners participate in a mentoring relationship with structure and timeframe of their making or as established by a formal mentoring program.
  • 2 Distance Mentoring ‚Äì the two parties (or group) are in different locations. Sometimes called ‚Äúvirtual/E‚Äù mentoring.
  • 3 Group Mentoring ‚Äì A single mentor is matched with cohort of mentees. Initial program structure is provided while allowing mentor to direct progress, pace and activities.

One on one mentoring

This method matches one mentor with one mentee. It allows both of them to develop a personal relationship and provides individual support for the mentee.

üë•

Group mentoring

It requires that a mentor works with 4-6 mentees at one time. The group meets once or twice a month to discuss various topics.

üìö

Resource based mentoring

The mentee initiates the process by asking one of the volunteers for assistance. Mentors add their names to a list of available mentors from which a mentee can choose from. This model has limited support within the organization and may result in mismatched mentor -mentee pairing.

üéì

Training based mentoring

This method is directly tied to a training program. A mentor is assigned to a mentee to help develop the specific skills taught in the program. Training based mentoring is limited because it focuses on the subject at hand and doesn‚Äôt help the mentee develop a broader skill set.

üëî

Executive mentoring

This top down model may be the most effective way to create a mentoring culture and cultivate skills and knowledge throughout an organization. It is also an effective succession planning tool because it prevents the knowledge brain drain that would otherwise take place when senior management retires.

Questions for Mentees

  • What do you want out of a mentoring relationship?
  • What goals have you set for yourself? (Long term? Short term? Where do you want to focus now?)
  • How do you learn best?
  • How do you like to be challenged?
  • How frequently do you want to meet?
  • What happens if one of us needs to cancel? If one of us does not show up for an appointment?
  • What norms do we have? Rules?
  • Etc...?

Mentoring Plan

Develop a mentoring plan based on your questions and the discussion about how you will work together.

Plan can include:

  • Setting professional goals
  • How you will achieve the goals
  • Role of the mentor in your career development
  • What you expect from mentor

Schedule Meetings When:

  • You Can Listen Actively
  • Pay Attention: Look at the person you are talking to and wipe all other thoughts out of your mind
  • Show That You're Listening: Nod, smile, ‚Äòuh-huh‚Äô, etc...

üìà Stages/Phases of Mentoring

01
Phase 1: Connection (Acquainted)

The mentor and mentee become acquainted and informally clarify their common interests, shared values, and goals given a high priority. In this stage, there may be lack of communication or difficulty in communicating. Mentees may be reluctant to trust mentors. The relationship may be like this from one to six meetings.

Core Activities: Rapport building. Exploration of interests, motivations, needs, concerns & expectations. The mentor should have a personal dev‚Äôment discussion with the mentee.

Mentee Expectations in Phase I

  • Coaching: skills gap and uncertainty need to be addressed.
  • Feedback: since they are there to learn and grow
  • Role modelling: new to them.
  • Acceptance & Confirmation: they need to develop new skills
  • Challenges: anxious of assignments & other learning opportunities. Mentors will have to normalize these feelings and reassure them of success! So predict initial failure as a sign of courage, tenacity and a journey towards excellence!
02
Phase 2: Relationship-building & Implementation

At this stage, the mentor and mentee communicate initial expectations and agree upon some common procedures and expectations as a starting point. In the less likely event that the two individuals may not be compatible, the pair is able to part on a friendly basis. There is more listening, sharing and confiding in each other. Values will be compared and personal concerns will be expressed.

Focuses on deepening the relationship. There is increased interaction & level of self-disclosure. Definite shift on the continuum from dependence to interdependence. Greater levels of commitment to the mentoring space.

Mentee Expectations in Phase II

  • Guidance & Support: normalizing fears and feelings and provide an accepting and safe space.
  • Instructions: helps reduce fears, anxieties and frustrations.
  • Feedback: communication that empowers the mentee.
  • Encouragement: Speak to the mentee in the language of possibility.
03
Phase 3: Assessment/Core Stage

The mentor and the mentee begin to accomplish the actual purpose of the mentoring. Gradually needs become fulfilled, objectives are met and natural growth takes place. New challenges are presented and achieved. It is the stage of acceptance but is also a stage of change, where a mentee is more likely to exercise self-discipline.

Determines whether the objectives of the relationship have been met as well as the level of competence & growth have been reached.

Critical questions: To what extent have the goals been met? Is the mentee competent? What have the mentors and mentees learnt? What unforeseen outcomes have occurred? How has the mentorship affected the organization? What obstacles were encountered?

04
Phase 4: Separation (Closure)

The mentor and the mentee close their mentoring association and redefine their relationship. Follow up is conducted.

Mentee requires less guidance from the mentor due to an increase in levels of confidence and competence. Mentor play a less directive role in the mentee‚Äôs ability to make decisions. Mentee initiates actions to further their growth & dev‚Äôment. Termination is evident and opportunities are leveraged to publicize achievements in the mentoring rships.

üé≠ Roles & Challenges

üßë‚Äçüè´

The Role of the Mentor

  • To provide career development behaviors such as coaching
  • To provide challenging assignments
  • To foster the mentee‚Äôs visibility.
  • To provide psychosocial support such as counseling, support and role modelling

NB: The role of the mentor is not merely to provide guidance & advice on how to accomplish certain tasks skillfully.

üë®‚Äçüéì

Role of the Mentee

  • To assume responsibility for their own growth and to see feedback from the mentor as an opportunity to grow and improve their skills.

‚òê For mentoring to work the mentor co-creates a learning partnership with a mentee.

Mentees/coachees need to:

  • Understand the programme objectives/purpose and process
  • Be self motivated
  • Be able to articulate expectations and own objectives
  • Meet commitments
  • Accept feedback and act on it
  • Listen
  • Be self-aware
  • Be receptive to and able to benefit from legitimate challenges
  • Not to be a passive receiver but an active partner in the process
  • To be ambitious with aspirations to go further
  • To be realistically ambitious about their expectations of the programme
  • Strong interpersonal skills
  • Prepared to take responsibility for their own actions
  • Able to approach the relationship with respect, good humor and openness
  • High belief in their ability to influence events in their favor
  • Have the ability to reflect
  • Be open
  • Willing to engage in meaningful feedback
  • Be trustworthy
