Introduction to Non-Communicable Diseases (NCDs)

01

üìñ Definition and Overview

Non-communicable diseases (NCDs) are chronic conditions that do not result from acute infectious processes and are generally not transmissible from person to person. They typically develop gradually over time, often without initial symptoms, but eventually cause death, dysfunction, or impairment in quality of life.

üìä

Global Impact

Globally, NCDs are the leading causes of death, accounting for approximately 71% of all deaths annually, which amounts to about 41 million deaths each year.

The major NCDs include cardiovascular diseases, cancers, diabetes, and chronic respiratory diseases. They pose a significant health challenge, especially in low- and middle-income countries, where nearly 80% of NCD deaths occur, emphasizing the importance of prevention, early detection, and management.

02

üóÇÔ∏è Classification of NCDs

NCDs are classified into several major categories based on their nature and affected systems:

  • Cardiovascular diseases (CVDs): Disorders of the heart and blood vessels, including heart attacks and strokes.
  • Cancers: Uncontrolled growth and spread of abnormal cells, affecting various organs.
  • Chronic respiratory diseases: Conditions like COPD and asthma affecting the lungs and airways.
  • Diabetes mellitus: Disorders affecting blood sugar regulation, primarily type 1, type 2, and gestational diabetes.
  • Nutritional diseases: Conditions such as scurvy, rickets, goiter, beriberi, pellagra, and dental caries caused by nutritional deficiencies.
  • Congenital problems: Birth defects like congenital heart diseases, spinal bifida, and Down's syndrome.
  • Mental illnesses: Disorders like psychosis, neurosis, anxiety, depression, and puerperal psychosis.
  • Accidents: Road traffic, industrial, and home accidents.
  • Degenerative diseases: Conditions such as arthritis.

Note

This broad classification underscores the diversity of NCDs and the need for targeted prevention and control strategies for each category.

03

üìà Epidemiology

NCDs are responsible for approximately 71% of all global deaths, translating to 41 million deaths annually. Notably:

  • Over 15 million people die prematurely (between ages 30-69) from NCDs each year.
  • 85% of these premature deaths occur in low- and middle-income countries.
  • In Africa, NCDs caused about 2.8 million deaths in 2008, with a rising trend projected to make NCDs the leading cause of death by 2030.
  • More than 62% of deaths in individuals over 45 years are attributable to NCDs.

The epidemiological data highlight the urgent need for effective prevention, early detection, and management programs, especially in resource-limited settings.

04

‚ö†Ô∏è Risk Factors

Risk factors for NCDs are broadly categorized into behavioral and metabolic/physiological factors:

Behavioral Risk Factors

  • Tobacco use and exposure: Smoking causes about 71% of lung cancers, 42% of chronic respiratory diseases, and nearly 10% of cardiovascular diseases. The prevalence of smoking is increasing among youth, including young girls, especially in developing countries, driven by aggressive marketing by tobacco companies.
  • Insufficient physical activity: About 3.2 million deaths annually are linked to physical inactivity. Regular physical activity reduces risks of cardiovascular diseases, diabetes, certain cancers, and depression.
  • Harmful use of alcohol: Responsible for approximately 2.3 million deaths per year, alcohol consumption is linked to cancers, liver cirrhosis, and cardiovascular problems.
  • Unhealthy diet: High salt intake, saturated fats, trans fats, and low fruit and vegetable consumption contribute to high blood pressure, obesity, and cardiovascular risk.

Metabolic/Physiological Risk Factors

  • Raised blood pressure: Causes an estimated 7.5 million deaths annually and is a major risk factor for cardiovascular disease.
  • Overweight and obesity: Responsible for at least 2.8 million deaths each year; increasing BMI elevates the risk of heart disease, stroke, diabetes, and certain cancers.
  • Raised cholesterol: Contributes to 2.6 million deaths annually, increasing the risk of heart disease and stroke.
  • Cancer-associated infections: Infections like human papillomavirus (HPV), Hepatitis B and C, and Helicobacter pylori are responsible for a significant proportion of cancers globally. These infections are largely preventable through vaccination and hygiene measures.
05

üìâ Impact and Effects of NCDs

NCDs impose a heavy burden on individuals, families, and health systems:

  • Financial hardship: Long-term treatment costs lead to poverty, especially affecting low-income households.
  • Healthcare costs: Extended and expensive treatments, hospitalizations, and management of complications increase economic strain.
  • Family and community impact: Illness can lead to children dropping out of school, reduced household income, and decreased productivity.
  • Quality of life: Chronic pain, disability, and mental health issues diminish overall well-being.
  • Poverty link: Socioeconomic status influences the risk and outcomes of NCDs, with poorer populations experiencing higher morbidity and mortality due to barriers in accessing care.
