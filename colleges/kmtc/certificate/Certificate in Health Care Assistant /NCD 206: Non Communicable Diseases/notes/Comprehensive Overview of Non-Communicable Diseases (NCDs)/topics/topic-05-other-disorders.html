üß† Part 1: Epilepsy

01

Definition and Types

Epilepsy is a neurological disorder characterized by recurrent, unprovoked seizures caused by abnormal electrical activity in the brain. It affects around 50 million people worldwide.

Types of Seizures

  • Generalized seizures: Affect the entire brain; include tonic‚Äìclonic, absence, myoclonic, atonic, and clonic seizures.
  • Focal (partial) seizures: Affect only part of the brain; may be aware or unaware during episodes.
02

Symptoms and Causes

Signs and Symptoms

  • Loss of consciousness or awareness
  • Convulsions or muscle jerks
  • Sensory disturbances (taste, smell, vision)
  • Repetitive movements
  • Aura or warning signs before seizures

Causes

  • Brain injury or trauma
  • Brain infections (e.g., meningitis, HIV)
  • Stroke
  • Brain tumors or cysts
  • Genetic predisposition
  • High fever (especially in children)
03

Myths and Management

üö´

Common Myths and Misconceptions

  • Myth: People will swallow their tongue (Incorrect).
  • Fact: Restraining a person during a seizure is dangerous.
  • Myth: Epilepsy is contagious or linked to mental illness.
  • Myth: People with epilepsy are disabled or incapable of working.

Management Strategies

  • Long-term use of antiepileptic medications
  • Protecting from head injuries
  • Avoiding alcohol and substances
  • Managing stress and sleep
  • Avoiding flashing lights and triggers

Overview and Epidemiology

Sickle cell anemia is an inherited blood disorder where abnormal hemoglobin causes red blood cells to become sickle-shaped, impairing oxygen transport and causing blockages in blood flow.

üåç

Epidemiology

Prevalent in Sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, Middle East, and the Mediterranean. In the US, approximately 100,000 people are affected. It is the most common monogenic disorder globally.

05

Clinical Details

Signs and Symptoms

  • Anemia and fatigue
  • Pain crises in various body parts
  • Swelling of hands and feet
  • Frequent infections
  • Delayed growth

Causes and Types

Caused by an inherited mutation in the gene responsible for hemoglobin production.

  • Sickle cell anemia: Homozygous form (two defective genes) with symptoms.
  • Sickle cell trait: Heterozygous (one defective gene), usually asymptomatic but carrier.
