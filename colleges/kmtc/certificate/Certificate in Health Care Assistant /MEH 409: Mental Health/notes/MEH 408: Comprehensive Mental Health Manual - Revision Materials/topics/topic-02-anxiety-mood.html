üòü Anxiety Disorders Overview

Anxiety disorders involve frequently experiencing intense, excessive, and persistent worry and fear about everyday situations.

Types of Anxiety Disorders

  • Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD)
  • Phobic Disorders
  • Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD)
  • Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)

Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD)

Characterized by severe, ongoing worry that interferes with daily activities. Individuals experience persistent and excessive worry about various things (disaster, money, health, family) that is difficult to control.

‚ñ∂ Common Symptoms
  • Persistent worrying out of proportion to events
  • Overthinking plans/solutions for worst-case outcomes
  • Difficulty handling uncertainty
  • Indecisiveness and fear of wrong decisions
  • Inability to relax, feeling restless or keyed up
  • Difficulty concentrating ("mind goes blank")
‚ñ∂ Physical Signs
  • Fatigue and trouble sleeping
  • Muscle tension/aches
  • Trembling, twitching
  • Sweating, nausea, diarrhea
  • Irritability
‚ñ∂ Symptoms in Children/Teens
  • Excessive worry about performance (school/sports)
  • Perfectionism and redoing tasks
  • Lack of confidence; striving for approval
  • Frequent stomachaches or physical complaints
  • Avoidance of school or social situations
üöë

When to Seek Medical Attention

  • Worrying interferes with work, relationships, or other parts of life.
  • Feeling depressed, irritable, or having trouble with alcohol/drugs.
  • Having suicidal thoughts or behaviors ‚Äî seek treatment immediately.

üï∑Ô∏è Phobic Disorders

A phobia is an excessive and irrational fear reaction. Persons experience a deep sense of dread or panic when encountering the source of fear.

Types of Phobias

Phobia Name Description
Agoraphobia Fear of places/situations one cannot escape (e.g., crowds, being outside alone).
Social Phobia Extreme worry about social situations; fear of interaction causing self-isolation.
Glossophobia Performance anxiety; fear of speaking in front of an audience.
Acrophobia Fear of heights (vertigo, dizziness).
Claustrophobia Fear of enclosed or tight spaces.
Hemophobia Fear of blood or injury.

*Others include Arachnophobia (spiders), Cynophobia (dogs), and Nyctophobia (darkness).

Management of Phobias

  1. Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT): Involves exposure to the fear source in a controlled setting to decondition reactions.
  2. Medication: Antidepressants and anti-anxiety medications to calm emotional/physical reactions.
  3. Coping Techniques: Relaxation, facing the fear, challenging thoughts, and the "AWARE" technique (Acknowledge, Wait, Action, Repeat, End).

üòî Depression

A mood disorder causing a persistent feeling of sadness and loss of interest. It affects feelings, thinking, and behavior. It is not the same as sadness/grief from life experiences.

Signs & Symptoms

‚ù§Ô∏è Emotional

  • Continuous low mood
  • Loss of interest/pleasure
  • Hopelessness
  • Helplessness
  • Worthlessness

üß† Psychological

  • Guilt/negative attitude
  • Loss of concentration
  • Thoughts of death/suicide
  • Tearfulness
  • Sensitivity to criticism

üí™ Physical

  • Slowing down or agitation
  • Tiredness/lack of energy
  • Sleep problems
  • Appetite changes
  • Unkempt appearance

Types of Depression

‚ñ∂ Postpartum Depression

Moderate to severe depression in a woman after giving birth (most common within first 3 months).

Management: Seek medical help immediately. Ask for support, don't hide feelings, rest when baby sleeps, join support groups.

‚ñ∂ Major Depressive Disorder (MDD)

Episodes of sad mood or anhedonia lasting longer than two weeks, significantly disrupting function. Causes include genetics, biological factors, and environmental stress.

‚ñ∂ Persistent Depressive Disorder

Chronic (ongoing) depression. Symptoms may be less severe than MDD but last longer.

‚ñ∂ Premenstrual Dysphoric Disorder (PMDD)

Severe depression/physical symptoms that usually resolve with the onset of menstruation. Symptoms include marked irritability, anger, and mood changes.

Psychosocial Interventions

  • üì¢

    Psychoeducation: Explain that depression is common, not a sign of weakness/laziness, and that negative thoughts are symptoms of the illness.

  • ü§ù

    Reduce Stress & Support: Reactivate social networks, join support groups, and identify stressors.

  • üèÉ

    Promote Functioning: Encourage regular sleep, physical activity, eating regularly, and participating in community activities.
