üìñ Core Concepts & Definitions

What is Mental Health?

‚ÑπÔ∏è

WHO Definition

It is a state of well-being in which the individual realizes his/her potentials/abilities, can cope with normal stresses of life, can work productively and fruitfully and is able to make a contribution to his/her community.

Mental health does not mean just the absence of mental illness but a sense of well-being felt. Mental health and physical health are interrelated and interdependent.

Key Definitions

Health
A state of complete physical, mental and social well-being and not merely the absence of disease or infirmity.
Mental illness/disorder
Maladaptive responses to stressors from the internal or external environment, evidenced by thoughts, feelings, and behaviors that are incongruent with the local and cultural norms, and interfere with the individual‚Äôs social, occupational, and/or physical functioning (Townsend, 2009).

üï∞Ô∏è Brief History of Mental Health

Ancient & Primitive Beliefs

Mental disturbances were often viewed through a supernatural lens:

  • Dispossessed Soul: Wellness achieved only if the soul returned.
  • Evil Spirits/Magic: "Care" involved exorcism, beatings, starvation, or torture.
  • Sin/Taboo: Mental illness seen as punishment for sin; led to burning at the stake.

Greek Era (400 BC)

Hippocrates began the movement away from supernatural beliefs. He associated insanity with an irregularity in the interaction of the four body fluids (humors). Treatment involved inducing vomiting and diarrhea.

The Middle Ages (AD 500 - 1500)

European communities reverted to associating mental illness with witchcraft. The "Ship of Fools" concept emerged‚Äîsending the mentally ill out to sea.

In Islamic countries, "Special Units" for the mentally ill were established within general hospitals, considered the first Asylums.

Colonial America & 18th/19th Century

Benjamin Rush (Father of American Psychiatry) initiated humanistic treatment but still used harsh methods like blood-letting. Dorothea Dix lobbied for state hospitals to provide humanistic therapeutic care.

Kenyan Context

  • 1949: "Mental Treatment Act" enacted based on British law.
  • 1989: Current Mental Health Act became operational. It provides for voluntary treatment and created a regulatory body.

üîç Aetiological Factors

The "3 Ps" of Causation

  1. Predisposing Factors: Determine the likelihood of getting a mental illness (e.g., adverse experiences in early life).
  2. Precipitating Factors: Events that take place shortly before the onset of a disorder (e.g., drug abuse, loss of job).
  3. Perpetuating Factors: Factors that prolong the course of the disease after onset (e.g., secondary demoralization, social withdrawal).

Classification of Factors

‚ñ∂ Biological Factors
  • Genetic: Inheritance of faulty genes/chromosomal abnormalities (e.g., Down syndrome).
  • Physical Illness: Leading to loss of mental capacity (e.g., acute malaria).
  • Brain Defects/Injury: Trauma or structural defects.
  • Others: Prenatal damage, nutrition, substance abuse.
‚ñ∂ Psychological Factors
  • Severe psychological trauma in childhood (emotional, physical, sexual abuse).
  • Early loss of a parent.
  • Neglect.
  • Poor ability to relate to others.
‚ñ∂ Socio-Cultural Factors
  • Low socio-economic class.
  • Unemployment.
  • Broken homes.
  • Violence.
  • Social roles (e.g., soldier developing guilt).

üåç Determinants of Attitudes

Several factors influence community attitudes towards mental health:

  • Culture: Shapes thinking and behavior. Determines who is labeled "insane" and the nature of treatment/helper identity.
  • Education: Higher levels of education generally lead to better understanding and more positive attitudes.
  • Health Beliefs: Depends on whether the individual believes in germ theory, evil spirits, or imbalances.
  • Religion: Often dictates reactions (e.g., belief in evil spirits). However, religion usually encourages empathy.
