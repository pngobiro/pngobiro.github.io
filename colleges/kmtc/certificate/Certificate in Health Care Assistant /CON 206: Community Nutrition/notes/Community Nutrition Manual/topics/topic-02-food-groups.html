Carbohydrates are the sugars, starches, and dietary fiber that occur in plant foods and dairy products. Carbohydrates are mainly found in plant foods. They also occur in dairy products in the form of a milk sugar called lactose.

Carbohydrates contain carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen atoms. Oxygen and hydrogen are present in the same proportion as in water.

Two basic compounds make up carbohydrates: Aldehydes, which are double-bonded carbon and oxygen atoms, plus a hydrogen atom, and ketones, which are double-bonded carbon and oxygen atoms, plus two additional carbon atoms.

Classification of Carbohydrates

Carbohydrate can be monosaccharides, disaccharides, or polysaccharides.

Monosaccharides

Single units of sugar.

  • Glucose: The body‚Äôs main source of energy. It is found in ripe fruits and some vegetables e.g onions, beetroot.
  • Galactose: Most readily available in milk and dairy products.
  • Fructose: Mostly occurs in fruits and vegetables.
2Ô∏è‚É£

Disaccharides

Two sugar molecules joined together.

  • Lactose: Found in milk, made up of glucose and galactose.
  • Sucrose: Table sugar, made up of glucose and fructose.
  • Maltose: Formed by two units of glucose. Sometimes called ‚Äòmalt sugar‚Äô and is found in cereals such as barley where it is formed during germination.

During digestion, disaccharides are broken down to glucose and other monosaccharides before being absorbed into the blood stream.

üîó

Polysaccharides

Chains of many sugars (hundreds or thousands).

  • Glycogen: Stores energy in the liver and muscles.
  • Starches: Abundant in potatoes, rice, and wheat.
  • Cellulose: One of the main structural components of plants.
  • Dextrin: Formed when foods containing starch e.g bread are baked or toasted. Forms part of the crust.
  • Pectin: Complex polysaccharide formed by some plants (plums, apple). Responsible for setting jam.

Effects of Heat on Carbohydrates

Sugar

  • Dry heat: Sugar melts then caramelizes and finally burns leaving a black residue.
  • Wet heat: Sugar first dissolves then becomes a syrup which caramelizes and finally burns when the water has evaporated.

Starch

  • Dry heat: Starch changes to dextrin.
  • Wet heat: Starch grains first soften, then absorb water and swell causing some to rupture. The starch then dissolves to form a paste.
üí°

Sources of Starch

  • Wholegrain cereals e.g wheat, rice, oats, e.g whole meal bread, wholegrain pasta
  • Fruits e.g skin of apples, plums
  • Vegetables e.g potato skins, green leafy vegetables

Protein Basics

Proteins are vital for growth, repair and maintenance of the body. Protein can also be used to provide the body with energy once it has been used for its main functions of growth and repair.

Proteins contains these elements; oxygen, carbon, hydrogen, nitrogen and sometimes sulphur and phosphorus. The protein molecules are made up of small units joined together like links in a chain. These units are called amino-acids.

ü•©

High Biologic Value

Contains all essential amino-acids (Complete proteins).

Sources: Meat, cheese, fish, milk, eggs, Soya beans (plant exception).

üåæ

Low Biologic Value

Lacks one or more indispensable amino acids (Incomplete proteins).

Sources: Cereals (wheat, rice, oats), Pulses (peas, beans, lentils), Nuts, Vegetables, Gelatine (animal exception).

‚ÑπÔ∏è

Protein Complementation

Low biologic value protein foods are not inferior to high biologic value protein foods. When a combination of low biologic value foods is eaten e.g lentil soup with bread, then indispensable amino-acids which are limited in one are provided by the other. In this way, proteins complement each other to provide a sufficient supply of indispensable amino-acids.

Protein Requirements & Functions

Everyone needs some protein in their daily diet when they have stopped growing but at certain times of our lives these needs increase. For example:

  • Babies and children require a lot of proteins as they grow rapidly
  • Adolescents require proteins for their rapid spurt of growth
  • Pregnant women require more than normal to cater for the growing baby
  • Nursing mothers requires more than normal milk production during breast-feeding (lactation)

Functions of Proteins

  • Growth and repair of body cells and tissues
  • Synthesis of enzymes, plasma proteins, antibodies (immunoglobulins) and some hormones
  • Provision of energy (secondary function)

ü•ë Fats and Oils

Fats and oils have the same basic chemical structure but the physical appearance differ in normal room temperature:

  • Fats: Solid at room temperature
  • Oils: Liquid at room temperature

Fats are composed of the elements: carbon, oxygen, hydrogen. These molecules make up molecules of glycerol and fatty acids which combine to form fat molecules known as triglycerol (sometimes called a triglyceride).

Types of Fats

ü´í

Monounsaturated Fats

Have one double bond. Found in olive, peanut, canola oils, avocados, almonds, hazelnuts, pumpkin seeds.

üåª

Polyunsaturated Fats

Have more than one double bond. Found in sunflower, corn, soybean, flaxseed oils, walnuts.

üêü

Omega-3 Fats

Body cannot make these. Found in fish, flax seeds, walnuts, canola or soybean oil.

Functions of Fats

  • Provides a convenient and concentrated source of energy
  • Surrounds and protects vital organs e.g kidney, glands
  • Forms insulating layer (adipose tissue) beneath the skin
  • Forms part of the structure of cell membranes
  • Provides a source of fat-soluble vitamins A,D,E, and K
  • Provides texture and flavour in food
  • Provides a feeling of fullness (satiety)

üíä Micronutrients: Vitamins

Fat-Soluble Vitamins

‚ñ∂ Vitamin A (Retinol)

Roles: Regulation of gene expression, promotes development of immune cells (Natural Killer Cells), antioxidant, promotes good vision (rhodopsin), promotes healthy skin, growth and maintenance of bones and teeth.

Sources:

  • Retinol (Animal): Milk, cheese, eggs (yolk), butter, oily fish, Liver, kidney.
  • Beta-carotene (Plant): Carrots, spinach, apricots, parsley, cabbage, tomatoes, prunes.
‚ö†Ô∏è

Warning

Too much vitamin A is poisonous. Pregnant women are advised not to eat liver due to high retinol which could lead to defects in unborn baby.

‚ñ∂ Vitamin D (Cholecalciferol)

Functions: Required for proper formation of bones and teeth. Helps promote absorption of calcium and phosphorus.

Sources: Liver, Fish-liver oils, Oily fish (herring, sardine), Sunlight (converts dehydro-cholesterol to cholecalciferol).

Deficiency: Rickets in children, Osteomalacia in adults, Osteoporosis.

Toxicity: Loss of calcium from bone, loss of appetite, nausea, vomiting, psychological depression.

‚ñ∂ Vitamin E (Tocopherol)

Roles: Antioxidant, prevents damage to lungs/RBCs/WBCs, necessary for normal nerve development, acts as blood thinner.

Sources: Vegetable oils, salad dressings, whole grain cereals, green leafy vegetables, nuts, seeds, peanut butter, wheat germ.

Deficiency: Liver disease, premature babies with fragile RBCs, loss of muscle coordination/vision/immune functions.

Toxicity: Increases effects of anticoagulants.

‚ñ∂ Vitamin K

Functions: Promotes synthesis of blood clotting proteins, acts in bone formation. Produced by bacteria in large intestines.

Sources: Turnip greens, cauliflower, spinach, liver, broccoli, kales, cabbage.

Deficiency: Rare but seen in infants or after prolonged antibiotics.

Toxicity: Rupture of RBCs, jaundice.

Major Minerals

ü¶¥

Calcium

Most abundant. 99% stored in bones. Essential for bone/tooth strength, blood clotting, nerve function. Deficiency: Rickets/Osteomalacia.

üß™

Phosphorus

Aids bone/tooth formation, metabolism. Deficiency causes rickets, osteomalacia, slow blood clotting.

‚ö°

Magnesium

Regulates body fluids, activates enzymes, ATP formation.

üçå

Potassium

Muscle contraction, nerve impulses. Deficiency: Hypokalemia (fluid imbalance).

üßÇ

Sodium

Muscle contraction, nerve impulses, fluid balance. Excess leads to hypertension, edema.

Trace Minerals

  • Zinc: Wound healing, taste/smell sensitivity, protein synthesis. Deficiency: Dwarfism, impaired wound healing.
  • Iron: Hemoglobin formation. Deficiency: Anemia. Excess: Hemosiderosis (liver damage).
  • Iodine: Thyroid hormone formation. Deficiency: Goiter, cretinism.
  • Fluoride: Bone/tooth mineralization, protects against caries. Excess: Fluorosis (mottled teeth).
  • Copper: Hemoglobin formation, iron absorption.
  • Selenium: Antioxidant. Deficiency: Heart disease risk.

üíä Supplements & Healthy Eating

Food Supplements

A food supplement is a preparation that is intended to supply a nutrient that is missing from a diet. It is intended to be taken by mouth as a pill, capsule, tablet or liquid.

Principles of Supplementation

  1. Read carefully the labels.
  2. Professionally determined individual needs govern specific supplement usage.
  3. All nutrient work together to promote good health.
  4. Food is the best source of nutrients.
‚ò£Ô∏è

Supplement Toxicity

Taking large amounts of vitamins can lead to hypervitaminosis.

  • Too much vit D can harm your kidneys
  • Vit A overdose causes nausea, vomiting, dizziness, blurry vision
  • Excess calcium settles in arteries causing obstruction

Guidelines on Healthy Eating

A healthy diet helps to protect against malnutrition in all its forms, as well as non-communicable diseases (NCDs).

üçé

Fruits & Veg

At least 400g (5 portions) per day.

üßÇ

Salt

Less than 5g per day (iodized).

üç¨

Sugars

Limit free sugars to less than 10% of energy.

üçî

Fats

Total fat < 30%. Saturated < 10%. Trans-fats < 1%.

üç≥ Food Preparation

Cooking Methods

01
Grilling

Cooking food over direct heat (open flame or grill pan). Example: Nyama choma.

02
Steaming

Food placed in a steamer over hot liquid. Good way to retain nutrients (water-soluble vitamins).

03
Searing

Browning of food using small amounts of fat over high heat to caramelize the outside.

04
Boiling

Cooking in water at 100¬∞C. Most water-soluble vitamins are destroyed.

05
Sauteing

Cooking in very little oil or fat until tender.

06
Poaching

Submerged in water between 71¬∞C and 82¬∞C. Example: cooking githeri.

07
Broiling

Source of heat comes from the top (ovens).

Importance of Food Processing

  • It helps to preserve food and prevent it from deterioration e.g milk.
  • Many food are inedible unless processed in some manner e.g coffee, cocoa, etc.
  • It helps to remove toxins occurring naturally in foods e.g the hydrogen cyanide removed by fermenting cassava.
  • To make the food taste better.
  • To make food stay longer e.g drying cassava, plantain, etc.
