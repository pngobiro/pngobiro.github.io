üìñ What is Malnutrition?

Malnutrition means poor nutrition. This can be divided into:

‚¨áÔ∏è

Under nutrition

When one does not eat enough of one or more nutrients that the body requires, then the body does not have enough nutrients to produce energy, grow and repair the tissues and protect itself from diseases. This results in people becoming underweight. They may become too thin (Wasted) or too short (Stunted).

‚¨ÜÔ∏è

Over nutrition

When one takes diets that provide excess energy and lack physical exercise. This results in over nutrition and may lead to nutrition related diseases that develop over a long period of time e.g. heart disease, and diabetes.

üî¨

Micronutrient deficiencies

This occurs when the body lacks one or more micronutrients.

ü©∫ Clinical Signs of Malnutrition

1. Marasmus

Marasmus is a severe form of protein-energy malnutrition that results when a person does not consume enough protein and calories. It leads to wasting, or the loss of body fat and muscle.

ü¶¥

Appearance

Extremely emaciated (thin), no fat on body, ribs are visible. "Old man's appearance".

üß¨

Skin & Hair

Thin, flaccid skin hanging in loose folds (especially buttocks). Hair becomes sparse, dry, brittle, yellow/reddish.

üíß

State

Frequent dehydration. Alert and irritable. Usually has appetite.

‚ñ∂ Complications & Causes

Complications: Bradycardia (Slow heart rate beat), Hypotension (Low blood pressure), Hypothermia (Low body temperature). Diarrhea, measles, or respiratory infections can be fatal.

Causes: Not having enough nutrition, consuming wrong nutrients, health conditions affecting absorption. In places where food is scarce, extended breastfeeding (>6 months) without solid food can increase risk.

2. Kwashiorkor

Kwashiorkor is another severe form of protein-energy malnutrition where the main deficiency is protein.

üéà

Oedema

Severe swelling beginning in legs/feet, generalized to hands/arms ("moon face"). Masks muscle loss.

üßí

Appearance

Distended/bulging abdomen. Changes in hair color to yellow/orange. Skin lesions (cracked, peeling).

üò¥

Behavior

Apathetic and lethargic (inactive). Irritable when handled. Poor appetite.

üö®

Critical Note

Kwashiorkor needs immediate diagnosis and treatment, as it can quickly become life-threatening. Liver problems can occur with kwashiorkor, but they are rare with marasmus.

Nutrition and Infection

Infection and malnutrition have always been intricately linked. Malnutrition is the primary cause of immunodeficiency worldwide. Five infectious diseases account for more than one-half of all deaths in children aged <5 years, most of whom are undernourished.

Malnutrition and nutritional alterations are common complications of human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) infection.

Dangers of Malnutrition

  • Reduced muscle and tissue mass.
  • Decreased mobility and stamina due to muscle wasting.
  • Breathing difficulties and increased risk of chest infection/respiratory failure.
  • Wounds take longer to heal and illnesses take longer to recover from.
  • Slower immune response increasing risk of infection.
  • Difficulty staying warm (risk of hypothermia).
  • Poor libido and fertility problems.

üìè Nutritional Assessment (ABCD)

üÖ∞Ô∏è

Anthropometry

Measuring human body (Length, height, weight, head circumference).

üÖ±Ô∏è

Biochemical

Checking levels of nutrients in blood, urine or stools.

¬©Ô∏è

Clinical

Checking visible signs (edema, emaciation, hair loss) and medical history.

üá©

Dietary

Assessing food and fluid intake quantity and quality.

Anthropometric Measurements

01
Length

Children under 2 years. Using wooden measuring board (lying down). Readings are 1-2 cm greater than height.

02
Height

Children >2 years and adults. Standing position (Frankfurt position). Use stadiometer.

03
Weight

Salter Scale (spring balance) for <2 years. Beam balance or digital scale for others. Nearest 0.1 kg.

04
Head Circumference (HC)

Along supra orbital ridge and occipital prominence. Useful for children <2 years (brain growth).

Mid Upper Arm Circumference (MUAC)

Circumference of the left upper arm measured at mid-point. Independent of height. Proxy for muscle/fat reserves.

Use MUAC for: Pregnant/postpartum women, children 6-59 months, adolescents, and adults who cannot stand.

üõ°Ô∏è Management & Prevention

  • Prenatal care: Weight checks, anemia/BP checks, micronutrient supplementation, tetanus immunization, counseling.
  • Women's health: Family planning, improving status/resource allocation.
  • Breastfeeding: Promoting exclusive (6 mo) and continued breastfeeding.
  • Complementary feeding: Counseling on timing, type, energy density, frequency.
  • Growth monitoring: Weighing at birth and monthly.
  • Micronutrient supplementation: Vitamin A (mothers & infants), Iron/Folic Acid, Iodine.
  • Micronutrient fortification: Central programs, iodized salt testing.

üë• Community Health Role

Building Partnership

Effective partnership is essential for community-based solutions. The causes of malnutrition are multi-sectoral (Planning, Agriculture, Industry, Economy, Education, etc.). Action is needed nationally, locally and within families.

Role of Community Health Assistants (CHAs)

  • Provide IEC on nutrition services available at health facility and community levels
  • Screen, identify and make referrals for malnutrition
  • Conduct growth monitoring for under-fives
  • Follow-up and defaulter tracing for clients with malnutrition
  • Referrals for micronutrient supplementation
  • Promote, protect and support exclusive breastfeeding for the first six months of life and sustained breastfeeding for the first two years and beyond within the community
  • Carry out health promotion by providing information, education and communication (IEC) about healthy diet for people in all stages of the life cycle, particularly among vulnerable populations
  • Promote use of improved home-based recipes and preparation methods for locally available foods, including home fortification
  • Liaise and collaborate with other sectors to address food and nutrition security at household level
  • Maternal nutrition, screening, advice, referral for further management
  • Distribute iron and folic supplements
  • Distribute de-wormers
