üìñ Definitions & Concepts

This unit covers the fundamental concepts of child immunization and school health services in Kenya, including the Expanded Programme on Immunization (EPI), Vitamin A supplementation, and deworming strategies.

Cold-chain

The cold chain is defined as a system of ensuring that vaccines are maintained at the required low temperatures from the point of production until it reaches the consumer. It refers to all the equipment, processes and mechanisms used to store and transport vaccines from the producer to the user (including vaccine carriers, cold-boxes, refrigerators, freezers and cold rooms) by air, road and over water bodies.

üõ°Ô∏è

Immunization

The process by which an individual‚Äôs immune system becomes fortified against an agent (known as the immunogen). When this system is exposed to molecules that are foreign to the body, called non-self, it will orchestrate an immune response, and it will also develop the ability to quickly respond to a subsequent encounter because of immunological memory.

üíâ

Vaccination

The administration of antigenic material (a vaccine) to stimulate an individual‚Äôs immune system to develop adaptive immunity to a pathogen. Vaccines can prevent or ameliorate infectious disease.

üå°Ô∏è

Vaccine Vial Monitor (VVM)

The VVM is a heat-sensitive label attached to vaccine vials which gradually and irreversibly changes colour, from light to dark, as the vaccine is exposed to heat. It warns the health worker as to when a vial of a vaccine should be discarded because the vaccine is likely to have been degraded by exposure to heat. VVMs measure cumulative heat exposure.

Fully Immunized Status

  • A fully immunized child is one who has received all the prescribed antigens and at least one Vitamin A dose under the national immunization schedule before the first birthday.
  • A fully immunized person (other than an infant) refers to an individual who has:
    • received all the prescribed doses for a particular antigen or,
    • is beyond the ‚Äòwindow period of efficacy‚Äô of an antigen - where only one dose is required (e.g. yellow fever vaccine)

üè• Kenya Expanded Programme on Immunization (KEPI)

The Ministry of Health established The Kenya Expanded Programme on Immunization (KEPI) in 1980 with the main aim of providing immunization against six killer diseases of childhood, namely tuberculosis, polio, diphtheria, whooping cough, tetanus and measles to all children in the country before their first birthday, and tetanus toxoid vaccination to all pregnant women.

EPI was established in June, 1980 by the Kenya government to oversee and monitor vaccinations to all children in Kenya against five common diseases at that time. To improve outcomes, a number of vaccines have been added since then.

Principles of Kenya Expanded Programme on Immunization

üí°

üóìÔ∏è Kenya Expanded Programme Immunization Schedule

üíâ

BCG (Bacillus Calmette‚ÄìGu√©rin vaccine)

  • Schedule: Given at birth
  • Administration: Given by injection into the skin.
  • Purpose: It helps to build immunity against Tuberculosis (TB).
üíß

Oral Polio Vaccine (OPV)

  • Schedule: Given at birth, 6th Week, 10th Week and 14th Week
  • Purpose: Used to prevent poliomyelitis (polio). Poliomyelitis, often called polio or infantile paralysis, is an infectious disease caused by the poliovirus. In about 0.5 percent of cases there is muscle weakness resulting in an inability to move.
  • Efficacy: Three doses of live-attenuated OPV produce protective antibodies to all three poliovirus types in more than 95% of recipients.
  • Administration: OPV is given orally (by mouth).
üõ°Ô∏è

DPT-HepB-Hib (Pentavalent Vaccine)

A vaccine that contains five antigens: Diphtheria, Pertussis, Tetanus, Hepatitis B, and Haemophilus influenzae type b.

  • Schedule: Given at 6th week, 10th Week and 14th Week
  • Diseases Prevented:
    • Diphtheria: Serious bacterial infection affecting mucous membranes of throat and nose. Caused by Corynebacterium diphtheriae.
    • Pertussis (Whooping cough): Highly contagious respiratory disease caused by Bordetella pertussis. Known for uncontrollable, violent coughing.
    • Tetanus: Bacterial disease affecting the nervous system, leading to painful muscle contractions (lockjaw). Caused by Clostridium tetani.
    • Hepatitis B: Infectious disease caused by hepatitis B virus (HBV) affecting the liver.
    • Haemophilus influenzae type b (Hib): Bacteria responsible for severe pneumonia, meningitis and other invasive diseases almost exclusively in children aged less than 5 years.
ü´Å

Pneumococcal vaccine (PCV 10)

  • Schedule: Given at 6th Week, 10th Week and 14th Week
  • Purpose: Vaccines against the bacteria Streptococcus pneumoniae. Their use can prevent some cases of pneumonia, meningitis, and sepsis.
üå°Ô∏è

Measles Vaccine

  • Schedule: Given at 9 months
  • Disease Info: Measles is a highly contagious infectious disease caused by the measles virus. Symptoms usually develop 10‚Äì12 days after exposure and last 7‚Äì10 days. Initial symptoms include fever (>40 ¬∞C), cough, runny nose, and inflamed eyes. A red, flat rash usually starts on the face 3-5 days after symptoms begin.
ü¶ü

Yellow Fever Vaccine

  • Schedule: Given at 9 months
  • Disease Info: Yellow fever is a viral infection that occurs in Africa and South America. Symptoms include fever, chills, loss of appetite, nausea, muscle pains particularly in the back, and headaches.
üè•

Tetanus vaccine (TT)

  • Target Groups: Pregnant women, WCBA (Women of Child Bearing Age), and School aged children 7 to 14 years.
  • Purpose: Inactive vaccine used to prevent tetanus. During childhood five doses are recommended, with a sixth given during adolescence.
  • Disease Info: Tetanus often begins with mild spasms in the jaw muscles (lockjaw or trismus). Spasms can affect facial muscles (risus sardonicus), chest, neck, back, abdominal muscles, and buttocks. Back muscle spasms often cause arching (opisthotonos). Can lead to breathing problems.

üìú Ministry of Health Guidelines & Obligations

Obligations of the Ministry of Health on Vaccination Services

‚úì

The Ministry of Health shall provide all routine vaccination services free of charge except those required for foreign travel, which shall be offered at a cost.

‚úì

Vitamin A supplementation when provided as part of the Child Health Services shall also be provided free of charge.

‚úì

The main strategy for delivery of immunization services shall be through health facilities (fixed outlets), during working days of the week (i.e. Monday to Friday).

‚úì

Facilities that operate maternity (delivery) units must provide BCG and Birth OPV vaccination to newborns seven days a week.

‚úì

All districts and counties should achieve and maintain a minimum coverage of 80% of fully immunized children, based on the principle of ‚Äúthe full protection of any child is based on the collective protection of all children‚Äù.

‚úì

The Ministry of Health shall promote the uptake of vaccination services through the community strategy.

‚úì

All vaccines administered should be to the correct target groups, according to the prescribed schedule, through the prescribed route of administration, at the correct dosage and using the recommended injection device.

Guiding Principles on Immunization Services

  • The Ministry of Health will ensure that there is sustained demand for all available vaccines to all eligible Kenyans.
  • All vaccines for human use in Kenya must meet quality requirements as determined by the Pharmacy and Poisons Board and must be duly approved for use within the country by the Pharmacy and Poisons Board.
  • All vaccines for human use must be certified as safe under normal circumstances of use. All known and unknown adverse effects of specific brands should be well articulated.
  • Where the safety profile of a particular vaccine or immunological cannot be guaranteed but the risk of the disease is serious, then the vaccine/immunological should be administered after obtaining consent from the client.
  • All injectable vaccines must only be administered by duly registered clinicians.
  • All injectable vaccines are to be administered using non re-useable injection devices.
  • All vaccines for human use must be stored in specialized medical refrigerators as prescribed by the World Health Organization. The specifications for these refrigerators can be obtained from the Division of Vaccines.
  • All unused doses of a liquid multi-dose vial vaccine without a preservative must be discarded 6 hours after opening of the vial - e.g. multi-dose vials of liquid Pneumococcal Conjugate Vaccines.

üíä Vitamin A Supplementation

Vitamin A, though not a vaccine was integrated into the infant immunization schedule in the 1980s due to its immune boosting effects and the obviously optimal opportunity for administration.

Vitamin A deficiency is a cause of preventable blindness in Kenya and therefore all efforts must be made to strengthen the supplementation of Vitamin A for all infants.

A key strategy for increasing Vitamin A supplementation coverage to all infants and young children is Regular Vitamin A supplementation of infants and young children up to their 5th year of life.

üí°

Remember

A child should have gotten at least two doses of Vitamin A before the first birthday. Vitamin A is an essential vitamin needed for growth and development, cell recognition, vision, immune function, and reproduction. Natural sources include eggs, milk, fruits (e.g. carrots), and dark green leafy vegetables.

ü™± Deworming

Deworming is the process of expelling intestinal worms or parasitic worms from the body by administering an anthelmintic medicine/drug. In more simple terms, it is a medicated process to kill worms.

Why is Deworming Important?

Parasitic worms can lead to malnutrition. Deworming tablets allows people to absorb the critical nutrients needed to be and stay healthy.

Parasitic worms and their larvae are generally found in contaminated food and water in poor communities or areas where cleaning does not frequently happen.

Those who walk without wearing any socks or shoes in high-risk areas are most likely to contract worms.

Symptoms of Intestinal Worms

ü§¢

Digestive Issues

Abdominal pain, Diarrhea, nausea, or vomiting, Gas/bloating, Blood in the stool.

üîã

Physical Condition

Fatigue, Tiredness, weakness or hunger due to worms, Weight loss, Dehydration.

üöΩ

Other Signs

Redness or rash on the buttocks, Urinating frequently, Abdominal pain or tenderness.

Prevention of Worms

One of the more important aspects of prevention is basic sanitation. For example, people should always wash their hands both before and after using the toilet to avoid possible exposure. Washing the hands before cooking or handling food is also essential.

Many intestinal worms enter the body through the food that a person eats. As a result, it is essential to follow some safe food and general cleanliness practices:

‚ùì Frequently Asked Questions about Deworming
  1. What is the Importance of Deworming in Adults?

    Deworming is advised at least once a year. Adults need deworming because there is a mild chance of parasites getting in our body orally either due to poor hygiene or outside food.

  2. How Frequently Should I Deworm My Child?

    We recommend at least once every six months.

  3. Who's Supposed to Deworm?

    Everyone who is at risk of getting worm infestation is supposed to deworm. More particularly, it's needed for toddlers and small children. Also, grown-ups with poor body or food hygiene due to poor living conditions or unclean habits, people handling pets or raw meat and persons who walk barefoot ought to deworm.

  4. Can Babies Get Worms?

    Yes, everyone is susceptible to intestinal worms, regardless of their age.

  5. Is There a Particular Age for Deworming?

    Kids above the age of 2 can be dewormed.
