Specific Objectives

By the end of this session, the CHV should be able to:

  • Identify children with diarrhoea for less than 14 days or fever for less than 7 days in a malaria area who can be treated at home
  • Determine if the child with cough has fast breathing (a sign of pneumonia).
  • Identify chest in-drawing as a danger sign (a sign of severe pneumonia).
  • Identify children with other danger signs: cough for 14 days or more, diarrhoea with blood in stool, fever for 7 days or more, not able to drink or feed, vomiting everything, convulsions and unusually sleepy or unconscious.
  • Identify children with danger signs for malnutrition‚Äîred on MUAC strap and swelling of both feet.
  • Use the sick child recording form

What is iCCM?

  • Is a strategy which delivers treatment interventions closer to where caregivers and sick children with access challenges are
  • Evidence supporting iCCM has established that well trained and supervised Community Health Volunteers (CHVs) can effectively assess, classify and treat or refer sick children

Cut-off Points for Fast Breathing

  • In a child age 2 months up to 12 months, fast breathing is 50 breaths or more per minute.
  • In a child age 12 months up to 5 years, fast breathing is 40 breaths or more per minute.

Note: Chest in drawing occurs when a child breathes in and the upper chest wall goes up, while the lower chest wall goes in. The cause of chest in drawing is severe pneumonia.

Conducting RDT for Malaria

  • Rapid diagnostic tests (RDTs) most often use a dipstick or cassette format, and provide results in about 20 minutes.
  • A blood specimen collected from the patient is applied to the sample pad on the test card along with certain reagents.
  • After 15 to 20 minutes (depending on the test), the presence of specific bands in the test card window indicates whether the patient is infected with P. falciparum or one of the other 3 species of human malaria.

Rapid diagnostic tests (RDTs) offer a useful alternative to microscopy in situations where reliable microscopic diagnosis is not available‚Äîor is not available right away.

Nutrition Assessment

Nutrition assessment includes taking anthropometric measurements and collecting information about a client‚Äôs medical history, clinical and biochemical characteristics, dietary practices, current treatment, and food security situation.

Importance of nutrition assessment:

  • To identify people at risk of malnutrition for early intervention or referral before they become malnourished
  • To identify malnourished clients for treatment
  • To track child growth
  • To identify medical complications that affect the body‚Äôs ability to digest food and utilize nutrients
  • To inform nutrition education and counseling

Mid-Upper Arm Circumference (MUAC)

MUAC is the circumference of the left upper arm measured at the mid-point between the tip of the shoulder and the tip of the elbow. It is a proxy measure of nutrient reserves in muscle and fat.

Interpretation of MUAC Indicators

  • Red (Severe Acute Malnutrition - SAM): Less than 115mm (11.5cm). Refer immediately for treatment.
  • Yellow (Moderate Acute Malnutrition - MAM): Between 115mm (11.5cm) and 125mm (12.5cm). Refer for supplementation.
  • Green (Well Nourished): Over 125mm (12.5cm).

How to Take MUAC

01

Remove any clothing covering the child's left arm.

02

03

When the tape is in the correct position on the arm with correct tension, read measurement to the nearest 0.1cm (step 10).

04

Immediately record the measurement and refer if necessary.

Checking for Edema

  • Apply moderate thumb pressure just above the ankle or the tops of the feet for about three seconds on both feet at the same time.
  • If the pressing causes an indentation that persists for some time after the release of the pressure, then there is oedema.
  • Nutritional oedema is characterized by pitting on both feet.

üè• Referral and Case Management

Referral

Referral care represents an important step in the management of the sick child. Primary health care providers are trained to recognize children with severe conditions who require urgent hospital management. These situations are considered medical emergencies at that level of the health system.

Pre-referral Treatment

The health provider is trained to administer any pre-referral, urgent treatment as needed, to reduce the delay in initiating treatment at the referral site. Many deaths in severe cases occur in fact on the way to the hospital or during the first 24 hours of hospitalization.

The provider also plays an important role in advising and supporting the sick child's caretakers, to explain the need for taking the child to a hospital without delay and overcoming existing cultural barriers.

Case Management Process

01
Assessment

Assess a child by checking first for general danger signs, asking questions about common conditions, examining the child, and checking nutrition and immunization status.

02
Classification

Classify a child‚Äôs illnesses using a colour-coded classification system (Pink: urgent referral, Yellow: specific medical treatment, Green: home management).

03
Identify Treatment

Identify specific treatments. Give urgent pre-referral treatment if needed. Develop an integrated treatment plan if treating at home. Give immunizations if needed.

04
Follow-up Care

When a child is brought back to the health post, give follow-up care and reassess for new problems.

üì¶ Medicines and Supplies Management

Drug Supply Management (DSM) is concerned with practical ways in which high quality essential drugs are available, affordable and used rationally.

Importance of DSM:

  • National drug policy provides a sound foundation for managing drug supply
  • Wise drug selection underlies all other improvements
  • Effective management saves money and improve performance
  • Rational drug use requires more than drug information
  • Systematic assessment and monitoring are essential

üö∞ WASH (Water, Sanitation and Hygiene)

WASH covers three main areas: Water Safety, Sanitation, and Hygiene.

Key Community Health Practices:

  1. Understanding the sources of water in the community
  2. Understanding sources of water contamination
  3. Protection of water sources
  4. Understanding the water treatment and practice methods
  5. Safe water storage
  6. Hand washing with soap, tippy taps, leaky tin practices
  7. Proper use of pit latrines and waste disposal
  8. Vector control for vector borne diseases

Roles and responsibilities of CHVs in WASH:

  1. To carry out health promotion activities including demonstration on all practices mentioned above in their communities e.g. Community led total sanitation (CLTS)
  2. To identify water sanitation and hygiene related diseases and to negotiate improved practices
  3. To identify root causes of the water and sanitation concerns in the community and their solutions
