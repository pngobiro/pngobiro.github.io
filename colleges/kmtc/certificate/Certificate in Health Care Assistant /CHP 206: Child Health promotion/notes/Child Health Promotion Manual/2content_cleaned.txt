<p>The cold chain is defined as a system of ensuring that vaccines are maintained at the required low temperatures from the point of production until it reaches the consumer. It refers to all the equipment, processes and mechanisms used to store and transport vaccines from the producer to the user (including vaccine carriers, cold-boxes, refrigerators, freezers and cold rooms) by air, road and over water bodies.</p>
<p><strong>Immunization, or Immunisation</strong>, is the process by which an individual’s immune system becomes fortified against an agent (known as the immunogen).</p>
<p>When this system is exposed to molecules that are foreign to the body, called non-self, it will orchestrate an immune response, and it will also develop the ability to quickly respond to a subsequent encounter because of immunological memory</p>
<p><strong>Vaccination</strong> is the administration of antigenic material (a vaccine) to stimulate an individual’s immune system to develop adaptive immunity to a pathogen. Vaccines can prevent or ameliorate infectious disease.</p>
<p>Immunization is a proven tool for controlling and eliminating life-threatening infectious diseases and is estimated to avert between 2 and 3 million deaths each year according to WHO</p>
<p><strong>Vaccine Vial Monitor</strong>: The VVM is a heat-sensitive label attached to vaccine vials which gradually and irreversibly changes colour, from light to dark, as the vaccine is exposed to heat. It warns the health worker as to when a vial of a vaccine should be discarded because the vaccine is likely to have been degraded by exposure to heat. VVMs measure cumulative heat exposure</p>
<p><strong>A fully immunized child</strong> is one who has received all the prescribed antigens and at least one Vitamin A dose under the national immunization schedule before the first birthday.</p>
<p><strong>A fully immunized person</strong> (other than an infant) refers to an individual who has;</p>
<ul>
<li>received all the prescribed doses for a particular antigen or,</li>
<li>is beyond the ‘window period of efficacy’ of an antigen - where only one dose is required (e.g. yellow fever vaccine)</li>
</ul>
<p>The Ministry of Health established The Kenya Expanded Programme on Immunization (KEPI) in 1980 with the main aim of providing immunization against six killer diseases of childhood, namely <strong>tuberculosis</strong>, <strong>polio</strong>, <strong>diphtheria</strong>, <strong>whooping cough</strong>, <strong>tetanus and measles</strong> to all children in the country before their first birthday, and <strong>tetanus toxoid</strong> vaccination to all pregnant women.</p>
<p>KEPI was part of the global Expanded Programmes on Immunization (EPIs), whose main goal was to control killer vaccine-preventable diseases of childhood. Prior to 1980 vaccination services had been provided on an ad-hoc basis mainly through primary schools and the larger health institutions and facilities.</p>

<p>KENYA’S IMMUNISATION SCHEDULE-KENYA EXPANDED PROGRAMME ON IMMUNISATION SCHEDULE</p>
<ul>
<li>BCG (Bacillus Calmette–Guérin vaccine)</li>
</ul>
<p>Given at birth</p>
<p>The vaccine is given by injection into the skin. It helps to build immunity against Tuberculosis(TB)</p>
<ul>
<li>Oral Polio Vaccine (OPV)</li>
</ul>
<p>Given at birth, 6th Week, 10th Week and 14th Week</p>
<p>Polio vaccines are vaccines used to prevent poliomyelitis (polio).</p>
<p>Poliomyelitis, often called polio or infantile paralysis, is an infectious disease caused by the poliovirus. In about 0.5 percent of cases there is muscle weakness resulting in an inability to move.</p>
<p>Three doses of live-attenuated OPV produce protective antibodies to all three poliovirus types in more than 95% of recipients. OPV is given orally (by mouth)</p>
<ul>
<li>DPT-HepB-Hib(Diphtheria, Pertussis, Tetanus, and Hepatitis B and Haemophilus influenzae type b)</li>
</ul>
<p>Given at 6th week, 10th Week and 14th Week</p>
<p>Pentavalent Vaccine is a vaccine that contains five antigens (diphtheria, pertussis, tetanus, and hepatitis B and Haemophilus influenzae type b)</p>
<p>Diphtheria is a serious bacterial infection that affects the mucous membranes of the throat and nose.</p>
<p>Symptoms include sore throat, fever, swollen lymph nodes and weakness. A type of bacteria called Corynebacterium diphtheriae causes diphtheria</p>
<p>Pertussis, also known as whooping cough, is a highly contagious respiratory disease. It is caused by the bacterium Bordetella pertussis. Pertussis is known for uncontrollable, violent coughing which often makes it hard to breathe</p>
<p>Tetanus is a serious bacterial disease that affects your nervous system, leading to painful muscle contractions, particularly of your jaw and neck muscles. Spasms may be so severe that bone fractures may occur.</p>
<p>Tetanus is caused by an infection with the bacterium Clostridium tetani.</p>
<p>Hepatitis B is an infectious disease caused by the hepatitis B virus (HBV) that affects the liver. Haemophilus influenzae type b (Hib) Haemophilus influenza type b (Hib) is a bacteria responsible for severe pneumonia, meningitis and other invasive diseases almost exclusively in children aged less than 5 years. It is transmitted through the respiratory tract from infected to susceptible individuals.</p>
<ul>
<li>Pneumococcal vaccine (PCV 10)</li>
</ul>
<p>Given at 6th Week, 10th Week and 14th Week</p>
<p>Pneumococcal vaccines are vaccines against the bacteria Streptococcus pneumoniae. Their use can prevent some cases of pneumonia, meningitis, and sepsis.</p>
<ul>
<li>Measles Vaccine</li>
</ul>
<p>Given at 9 months</p>
<p>Measles vaccine is a vaccine that prevents measles. Measles is a highly contagious infectious disease caused by the measles virus. Symptoms usually develop 10–12 days after exposure to an infected person and last 7–10 days. Initial symptoms typically include fever, often greater than 40 °C (104.0 °F), cough, runny nose, and inflamed eyes.</p>
<p>A red, flat rash which usually starts on the face and then spreads to the rest of the body typically begins three to five days after the start of symptoms.</p>
<ul>
<li>Yellow Fever Vaccine</li>
</ul>
<p>Given at 9 months</p>
<p>Yellow fever vaccine is a vaccine that protects against yellow fever. Yellow fever is a viral infection that occurs in Africa and South America. In most cases, symptoms include fever, chills, loss of appetite, nausea, muscle pains particularly in the back, and headaches.</p>
<ul>
<li>Tetanus vaccine (TT)</li>
</ul>
<p>Given at Pregnant women, WCBA and School aged children 7 to 14 years</p>
<p>Tetanus vaccine, also known as tetanus toxoid (TT), is an inactive vaccine used to prevent tetanus. During childhood five doses are recommended, with a sixth given during adolescence. Tetanus often begins with mild spasms in the jaw muscles—also known as lockjaw or trismus. The spasms can also affect the facial muscles resulting in an appearance called risus sardonicus. Chest, neck, back, abdominal muscles, and buttocks may be affected. Back muscle spasms often cause arching, called opisthotonos. Sometimes the spasms affect muscles that help with breathing, which can lead to breathing problems.</p>

<p>In more simple terms, it is a medicated process to kill worms.</p>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="background-of-national-school-health-policy">Background of National School Health Policy;<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#background-of-national-school-health-policy" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>The National School Health Policy was launched in 2009. Its aim was to address eight thematic areas namely: values and life skills; gender issues; child right &amp; responsibilities; nutrition; special needs, disabilities and rehabilitation; WASH; disease prevention and control; school infrastructure &amp; environmental Safety.</p>
<p>The policy sought to address education and health needs of all basic education learners including those with special needs and disabilities. It provided the objectives and strategies to address these needs. The policy further aimed at identifying and mainstreaming key health interventions for improved school health and education.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="rationale-of-the-school-health-policy">Rationale of the School Health Policy<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#rationale-of-the-school-health-policy" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<p>This policy is guided by the Kenya's Vision 2030 which acknowledges that improved health and more so to all learners is a critical driver to the achievement of this vision.</p>
<p>The Constitution of Kenya, 2010 guarantees all learners the right to basic, compulsory and quality education; the highest attainable standard of health, clean and healthy learning environment, accessibility to reasonable standards of sanitation, free from hunger, to have adequate food of acceptable quality, clean and safe water in adequate quantities</p>
<p>The school provides an organized structure that is conducive for the provision of health, nutrition services as well as a key avenue for disease prevention and control. It can either promote health or accelerate the spread of ill health.</p>
<p>14</p>
<p>15</p>
<p>Schools are ideal settings to implement health programmes because they are;</p>
<ol>
<li>An efficient and effective channel to reach many people for introducing health promotion practices through Behavior Change Communication (BCC);</li>
<li>Provide interventions in a variety of ways (learning experiences, linkages to services, supportive environment);</li>
<li>Learners are admitted at early stages of their development when lifelong behaviors, values, skills and attitudes are being formed;</li>
<li>Improved health enhances cognitive development, concentration, participation and retention of learners in school.</li>
<li>It also reduces absenteeism, increases enrolment and improves academic performance</li>
</ol>
<p>The Comprehensive School Health Programme (CSHP) meets greater proportion of health and psychosocial needs of learners in and out of school.</p>
<p>The programme leads to efficient resource utilization resulting in greater impact. The components of a CSHP include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Values and life skills</li>
<li>Gender, Growth and Development</li>
<li>Child Rights and Responsibilities</li>
<li>Water, Sanitation and Hygiene</li>
<li>Nutrition</li>
<li>Disease prevention and control</li>
<li>Special needs, disabilities and rehabilitation</li>
<li>School infrastructure and environmental health safe guards</li>
<li>Cross cutting issues</li>
</ul>
<p>Guiding Principles of the School Health Policy</p>
<p>The National and County governments in collaboration with stakeholders shall ensure that each level supports the other for proper implementation of the school health programme in all schools.</p>
<p>The guiding principles include:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Access to Health and Nutritional Services: Every child has a right to quality health and nutrition services. School establishments shall act as a tool towards upholding these rights in partnership with the communities in which they live and learn.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Access to Water, Sanitation &amp; Hygiene: Every child has a right to safe and clean drinking water and adequate sanitation. Provision of safe and clean water and sanitation shall be complemented by appropriate hygiene promotion and education.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Access to Education: Every child has a right to basic, compulsory and quality education. Access to education will continue to be provided for school age learners and youth including the vulnerable groups</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Equality and Non-discrimination: Every child shall have equal rights, opportunities and responsibilities without any discrimination. They shall be protected from all forms of neglect and abuse on the basis of sex, gender, ethnicity, race, family and social status, religion, locality, political affiliation, disability, HIV status or illness among others.</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Access to Information: Every child shall have access to relevant and factual health information, knowledge and skills that are appropriate for their age, gender, culture, language, context, and disability.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Equity: Learning institutions shall adopt School Health Programmes to respond to the needs of a Privacy and Confidentiality: Every child has the right to privacy and confidentiality regarding their health. A child's health status and medical condition shall not be disclosed to others without the consent of the child (or the consent of the child's legal guardian acting in the best interest of the child). A child's medical information may be accessed by authorized health personnel, parents and teachers in order to provide medical advice or treatment or to prevent the spread of infectious diseases.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Safety in schools: All schools shall provide safe and accessible physical environment. They shall be responsible for minimizing the risk of physical injury and disease transmission by ensuring that adequate safety measures are put in place. In addition, all schools shall provide safe psychosocial environment. There shall be no tolerance to child abuse, sexual abuse and other forms of juvenile exploitation.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Gender Responsiveness and Transformative Approaches: Planning and implementation of School Health Programmes shall be sensitive to different needs of boys and girls.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Partnerships: A multi-sectoral approach for effective collaboration of all stakeholders (state and non-state) among relevant sectors shall be developed and fostered at all stages of planning and implementation of the School Health Programme.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Accessibility to school physical facilities: School infrastructure shall be accessible to all school age learners, and youth including those with special needs and disabilities.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Child Participation: Learners shall be involved in the planning, designing and implementation of the school health programme.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>NUTRITION:</p>
<p>Nutrition refers to provision of food to the human body for growth, development and maintenance of life. Nutrients are needed in the right amounts to provide materials for growth and repair of body tissues, energy for physical activity and basic body functions; including</p>
<p>16</p>
<p>breathing, body temperature, immunity and blood circulation. Proper nutrition increases a child’s attention span, learning capacity and ability to fully engage in education experiences and therefore reach their full potential in life</p>
<p>Schools provide an ideal setting to promote good nutrition to all learners including those with special needs (including pregnant adolescent girls) and disabilities. This includes offering nutrition services, nutrition education and healthy food environment and ensuring community involvement and participation to promote nutrition.</p>
<p>Nutrition services relates to interventions such as regular assessment, monitoring of nutritional status, de-worming and micronutrient supplementation to school going children.</p>
<p>Good nutrition practices in schools and integration of nutrition interventions can impact the community since children are good change agents.</p>

<li>Show the Site: The chest or stomach.</li>
<li>Look at the chest movements: up and down.</li>

<p>To count the breaths in one minute:</p>
<ul>
<li>Use a watch with a second hand (or a digital watch, or a timer). Put the watch in a place where you can see the watch and the child’s breathing.</li>
<li>Look for breathing movement anywhere on the child’s chest or stomach.</li>
<li>Start counting the child’s breaths when the child is calm.</li>
<li>Repeat the count if you have difficulty.</li>
<li>If the child moves or starts to cry, wait until the child is calm. Then start again.</li>
<li>After you count the breaths, record the number of breaths per minute in the space provided on the Sick child recording Form. Decide if the child has fast breathing.</li>

<p>Conducting RDT for Malaria:</p>
<ul>
<li>Rapid diagnostic tests (RDTs) most often use a dipstick or cassette format, and provide results in about 20 minutes.</li>
<li>A blood specimen collected from the patient is applied to the sample pad on the test card along with certain reagents.</li>
<li>After 15 to 20 minutes (depending on the test), the presence of specific bands in the test card window indicates whether the patient is infected with <em>P. falciparum</em> or one of the other 3 species of human malaria.</li>

<p>NUTRITION ASSESSMENTS, SCREENING AND REFERRALS</p>
<p>Nutrition assessment</p>
<ul>
<li>Nutrition assessment includes taking anthropometric measurements and collecting information about a client’s medical history, clinical and biochemical characteristics, dietary practices, current treatment, and food security situation.</li>
</ul>
<p>Importance of nutrition assessment</p>
<ul>
<li>To identify people at risk of malnutrition for early intervention or referral before they become malnourished</li>
<li>To identify malnourished clients for treatment—malnourished people who are not treated early have longer hospital stays, slower recovery from infection and complications, and higher morbidity and mortality</li>
<li>To track child growth</li>
<li>To identify medical complications that affect the body’s ability to digest food and utilize nutrients</li>
<li>To inform nutrition education and counseling</li>
</ul>
<p>Assessing nutritional status</p>
<ul>
<li>There are various methods but here we concentrate on MUAC</li>
<li>MUAC is the circumference of the left upper arm measured at the mid-point between the tip of the shoulder and the tip of the elbow, using a measuring or MUAC tape.</li>
<li>MUAC measurements in millimeters (mm) are more accurate than measurements in cm. MUAC is a proxy measure of nutrient reserves in muscle and fat that are unaffected by pregnancy and independent of height. Use MUAC to measure all pregnant women and women up to 6 months postpartum.</li>
<li>MUAC is also an appropriate alternative for measuring children (instead of WHZ), adolescents (instead of BMI-for-age), and non-pregnant/postpartum adults whose weight and height cannot be measured (e.g., if they cannot stand or no equipment is available). MUAC is not currently recommended for infants under 6 months and should not be used to assess nutritional status in people with edema.</li>
<li>Well-nourished and healthy children and adolescents grow at a healthy growth rate</li>
<li>For children and adolescents 0 – 17 years, Growth should be monitored through weighing and taking of age and height.</li>
<li>For children 0-59 months, they should be weighed monthly the weight plotted on a growth chart in the mother and child booklet</li>
<li>The nutrition status of adults should be assessed using Body mass index assessment</li>
</ul>
<p>24</p>
<ul>
<li>Older children and adolescents 5-9 years, adults and older persons should have their height and weight monitored at regular intervals to ensure healthy weight is maintained</li>
<li>Unhealthy weight – overweight and obesity is a risk factor for non-communicable diseases.</li>
<li>Community health volunteers should ensure all members of the community have frequent assessment of their nutrition status through referral to wellness clinics</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="why-muac">Why MUAC<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#why-muac" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>It is simple and cheap. It can be used by service providers at different contact points without greatly increasing their workload and it can be effectively used by community-based people for active case finding.</li>
<li>It is more sensitive. MUAC is a better indicator of mortality risk associated with malnutrition than Weight-for-Height. It is therefore a better measure to identify children most in need of treatment.</li>
<li>It is less prone to mistakes. Comparative studies have shown that MUAC is subject to fewer errors than Weight-for-Height (Myatt et al, 2006).</li>
<li>It increases the link with the beneficiary community. MUAC screening allows service providers from peripheral health units and from the community to refer children with</li>
</ul>
<p>acute malnutrition to therapeutic or supplementary feeding programs. The MUAC colour coding is easy to understand for the child's care-taker.</p>

<p>Referral care represents an important step in the management of the sick child.</p>
<p>One of the key aspects of the IMCI training is that the primary health care provider is trained to recognize children with severe conditions who require urgent hospital management. These situations are considered medical emergencies at that level of the health system.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="pre-referral-treatment">Pre-referral treatment<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#pre-referral-treatment" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>The health provider is therefore trained to administer any pre-referral, urgent treatment as needed, to reduce the delay in initiating treatment at the referral site caused by the long time that the referral process may take. Many deaths in severe cases occur in fact on the way to the hospital or during the first 24 hours of hospitalization.</p>
<p>The provider also plays an important role in advising and supporting the sick child's caretakers, to explain the need for taking the child to a hospital without delay and overcoming existing cultural barriers.</p>
<p>IMNCI is an integrated approach to child health that focuses on the wellbeing of the whole child. IMNCI aims to reduce death, illness and disability, and to promote improved growth and development among children under five years of age. IMNCI includes both preventive and</p>
<p>curative elements that are implemented by families and communities as well as by health facilities. In summary, the IMNCI strategy includes three main components:</p>
<ol>
<li>Improving case management skills of healthcare staff.</li>
<li>Improving the health systems.</li>
<li>Improving family and community health practices.</li>
</ol>

<li>Effective management saves money and improve performance</li>
<li>Rational drug use requires more than drug information</li>
<li>Systematic assessment and monitoring are essential</li>
</ul>
<p>Challenges of Medicines and Supplies management:</p>
<ul>
<li>Achieving financial sustainability</li>
<li>Improving efficiency in public drug supply</li>
<li>Changing the perceptions and behaviors of providers, patients and the public</li>
<li>Reorienting the role of government</li>
<li>Regulating safety, efficacy and quality</li>
</ul>
<p>Essential medicine concept and its principles:</p>
<p>Concept:</p>
<ul>
<li>A limited range of carefully selected medicines leads to better health care, better drug management and lower costs.</li>
</ul>
<p>Principles:</p>
<ul>
<li>The vast majority of health problems can be treated with a small, carefully selected number of drugs.</li>
<li>Most practitioners routinely use fewer than 200 drugs.</li>
<li>Supply activities can be carried out most efficiently for a limited number of pharmaceutical products.</li>
<li>Patients can be better informed when confronted with limited drugs.</li>
</ul>
