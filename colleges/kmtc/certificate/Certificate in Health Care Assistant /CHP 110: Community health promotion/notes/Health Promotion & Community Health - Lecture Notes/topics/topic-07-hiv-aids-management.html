HIV & AIDS Vulnerable Groups

AIDS is caused by HIV, a virus that can be passed from person to person through sexual fluids, blood and breast milk.

Worldwide, the majority of HIV infections are transmitted through sex between men and women, and half of all adults living with HIV are women. Certain groups of people have been particularly affected and these include people who inject drugs, sex workers and men who have sex with men.

HIV particularly affects adolescents and young people, who accounted for 39 percent of all new infections and 15 percent of all people living with HIV in 2012. AIDS-related deaths among young adults have an especially damaging impact on their families and communities: skills are lost, workforces shrink and children are orphaned.

In some countries in sub-Saharan Africa, life expectancies have fallen below 40 years, whereas they would have been above 60 without the epidemic. Roughly 17.8 million children have lost at least one parent to AIDS-related illnesses.

Stigma and discrimination particularly affects these vulnerable groups. People known to be living with HIV are often shunned or abused by community members, employers even healthcare workers. As well as causing much personal suffering, this sort of prejudice discourages people from seeking HIV testing, treatment and care, undermining efforts to tackle the epidemic.

HIV/AIDS Counseling

The two main areas of HIV counseling are;

  1. Educating the infected person so that they will understand the nature of their problems, explanation of the available treatment options; outlining ways to prevent others from being infected by them.
  2. Support through anxieties and other physical need meets by people in the cause of their illness.

Appropriate counseling intervention include:

  • Initial attendance at a clinic or medical centre where the at risk person will have the HIV testing procedures explained.
  • Personal counseling of the HIV-positive person and the partner family where this is possible and applicable
  • Available treatment regimes
  • Instruction is safer sexual practices
  • Explanation of the development and effect of opportunistic infection and their delay /treatment
  • Counseling the family of bereaved

HIV Strains: Types, Groups and Subtypes

HIV is a highly variable virus which mutates very readily. This means there are many different strains of HIV, even within the body of a single infected person.

Types

HIV-1: Predominant virus worldwide.

HIV-2: Less easily transmitted, longer period between infection and illness. Concentrated in West Africa.

HIV-1 Groups

Group M: "Major" group, >90% of infections. Has at least 9 subtypes (A, B, C, D, F, G, H, J, K).

Group O: "Outlier" group, west-central Africa.

Group N: Rare, Cameroon.

Group P: Rare, related to gorilla SIV.

üí°

Implications

Subtype B: Dominant in Europe, Americas, Japan, Australia.

Subtype C: Predominant in Southern/East Africa, India, Nepal (world's worst epidemics).

Treatment: Most drugs designed for Subtype B but work on others. HIV-2 has natural resistance to NNRTI drugs.

ü§í Opportunistic Infections (OIs)

In our bodies, we carry many germs ‚Äì bacteria, protozoa, fungi, and viruses. When our immune system is working, it controls these germs. But when the immune system is weakened by HIV disease or by some medications, these germs can get out of control and cause health problems. Infections that take advantage of weakness in the immune defenses are called "opportunistic".

Cancers Common to HIV/AIDS

  • Kaposi's sarcoma: Tumor of blood vessel walls. Pink/red/purple lesions on skin and mouth. Can affect internal organs.
  • Lymphomas: Cancer originating in white blood cells. Painless swelling of lymph nodes.

Other Complications

  • Wasting syndrome: Loss of at least 10% body weight, diarrhea, weakness, fever.
  • Neurological complications: Confusion, forgetfulness, depression, AIDS dementia complex.
  • Kidney disease: HIV-associated nephropathy (HIVAN). Higher risk in blacks.
