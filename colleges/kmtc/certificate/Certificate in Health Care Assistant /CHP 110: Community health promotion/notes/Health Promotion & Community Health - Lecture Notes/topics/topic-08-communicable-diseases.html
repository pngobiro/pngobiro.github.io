Key Definitions

An illness due to specific infections agent or its toxic product which arise through transmission of the agent or its toxin form a reservoir susceptible host either directly, indirectly as an infected animals or person or indirectly through the agency of intermediate plant or animals host vector or in animate environment.

Introduction

Most of the common diseases in Africa are environmental diseases and are due to infection by living organisms which are viruses, bacteria, Chlamydia, ricketsiae, fungi, protozoa, metazoan or Helminthes. These are called communicable diseases because they spread from person to person or from animals to people. Together with malnutrition they are today the major cause of illness in Africa. The communicable diseases occur at all ages but are most serious in childhood due to an increased intensity of exposure and a poorly developed immunity. These diseases are to a great extent preventable.

In countries where they have been largely eliminated, other conditions such as degenerative and malignant diseases have taken place. This process is known as the Epidemiological transition.

Emerging Infectious Diseases

Infections that have increased in incidence during the last couple of decades and whose incidence is predicted to increase in the near future have appeared. They are called "emerging infectious diseases". The reasons behind these emerging infections include:

Importance in Africa

Communicable diseases are very important in Africa because:-

  • Many of them are very common
  • Some of them are very serious and cause death and disability
  • Some come as widespread outbreaks, i.e. epidemics
  • Many of them are fairly preventable by fairly simple means
  • Many are particularly serious (and more common) in infants and children.

üóÇÔ∏è Classification of Diseases

I. Broad Classification

  • Metabolic disorders: Chemical and physical processes within the body-disordered biochemistry in the body i.e. cholesterol imbalance e.g. diabetes
  • Congenital diseases: Mental or physical abnormalities that are present at, and usually before birth. Examples: cataract, cleft palate, cretinism, down's syndrome, congenital heart disease, hemophilia, joints disorders, spinal bifida.
  • Hereditary diseases: Transmission of mental and physical characteristics from parents to their children. Examples: hemophilia, sickle cell anemia.
  • Allergy diseases: Body reacts with unusual sensitivity to a certain substance(s). Examples: hay fever, asthma.
  • Traumatic diseases: Medical term for an injury, could be physical or psychological e.g. shock, Accident.
  • Nutritional/deficiency diseases: In adequate, lack of essential nutrients e.g. Obesity, marasmus, kwashiorkor, nutritional anemia etc.
  • Occupational diseases: Diseases associated with one's occupation e.g. silicosis, asbestosis, Lung fibrosis etc.
  • Toxic diseases: Due to contact with toxic substances e.g. poisoning, snake venom, etc.
  • Degenerative diseases: Wear and tear of the body tissues e.g. arthritis, arteriosclerosis, rheumatism, etc.
  • Addictive diseases: Due to desire/urge to use some substances e.g. alcoholism, smoking etc.
  • Mental disorders: Psychiatric disorders reflecting a deviation from normal thought and behavior patterns e.g. psychosis, schizophrenia.
  • Neoplastic disorders: Abnormal new growth commonly referred to as tumors e.g. cancers.
  • Psychosocial disorders: Mental illness caused or influenced by life experiences as well as maladjusted cognitive and behavioral processes e.g. stress.
  • Communicable diseases: Diseases spread from one person to another or from animal to person.

II. Classification of Communicable Diseases

Based on how the disease is transmitted:

Epidemiological Triad

Different diseases are common in different places and at different times. To understand why this is so, we need to consider the living organism of disease (agent), the people they infect (host) and the surrounding in which they live (environment).

üîç Click to zoom

The Epidemiological Triad: Agent, Host, Environment

The agents need a suitable environment in which to grow and multiply and thus be able to spread and infect another host. If they are not successful in doing this they die out.

When the balance between the agent, the host and the environment is fairly constant, we tend to see approximately the same number of cases of the disease every month. When this happens the disease is said to be ENDEMIC.

When the balance is shifted in favour of the agent (organism), e.g. when many non-immune children have been born in an area since the last measles epidemic, a large number of cases of measles may occur in a short time. This is called an EPIDEMIC.

In some parts of the country, some disease outbreaks occur only occasionally without a regular pattern. Such diseases are said to be SPORADIC in their occurrence.

Contributing Factors

Predisposing factors

Create a state of susceptibility, so that the host becomes vulnerable to the agent (e.g. age, sex, previous illness).

Enabling factors

Assist in the development of (or in recovery from) the disease (e.g. housing conditions, socio-economic status).

Precipitating factors

Associated with immediate exposure to the disease agent or onset of disease (e.g. drinking contaminated water).

Reinforcing factors

Aggravate an already existing disease (e.g. malnutrition, repeated exposures).

Risk factor

A condition, quality or attribute, the presence of which increases the chances of an individual to have, develop or be adversely affected by a disease process.

The Host and Infection Stages

A person who is invaded by a disease causing micro-organism is called a HOST. An infection occurs when this microorganism begins to reproduce (multiply) and grow. When an organism infects a person, there are three possible stages to consider:

01
Incubation Period

Is the time between infection and appearance of signs and symptoms of an illness. During the incubation period the host does not realize that she/he has an infection until several days later when detectable signs and symptoms of the illness occur.

02
Clinical Infection

This is the period when the host develops detectable signs and symptoms of an illness.

03
Sub-Clinical Infection

At this stage, infection does not produce clear signs and symptoms. Individuals who suffer from this stage are also likely to infect others as in the case of HIV infection which leads to AIDS after a long period. They are therefore known as CARRIERS.

üõë Disease Control and Prevention

Principles of Control

Communicable diseases can be controlled and eradicated from the community. The aim of control is to tip the balance against the agent. The control and eradication can be done by:

a. Attacking the Source

  • Treatment of cases: Treating the infected person or animal with appropriate antibiotics. Treating carriers and subclinical cases.
  • Mass treatment: Treating specific groups of persons who are at high risks of being infected (chemoprophylaxis).
  • Isolation: Isolating those persons who are infected with highly infectious diseases such as Ebola, Marburg fever, Lassa fever.
  • Reservoir control: Treating sick animals, immunizing animals, killing vectors/animals.
  • Notification & reports: Essential means of keeping a watch (Surveillance).

b. Interrupting the Transmission Route

  • Personal hygiene & behavioral changes
  • Environmental sanitation
  • Water and sanitation
  • Vector control
  • Good and adequate housing
  • Food handling
  • Sterilization of medical equipments

c. Protecting the Host

Specific measures:

  • Immunization (increases host resistance).
  • Better Nutrition (malnourished children get infections more easily).
  • Chemoprophylaxis (drugs that protect the host).
  • Personal protection (PPDs, barrier methods).

General measures: Use of bed nets, wearing shoes, adequate housing, improved nutrition, health education.

Application Levels

Individual & Village

Immunizations, personal hygiene, food hygiene, bed nets, abstaining from casual sex, protecting water supply, pit latrines, controlling vectors.

Dispensary & Health Centre

Increase immunization coverage, participate in vector control, emphasize water protection, inspect food markets, encourage sanitation, promote health education, notify diseases.

District, Regional & National

Vector control schemes, mass communication campaigns, mass treatment, research, emergency epidemiology teams, manpower training.

ü¶ü Case Study: Malaria

Definition & Etiology

Def: An acute infection of the blood with plasmodium parasite characterized by chills fever, pyrexia, nausea and anorexis.

Aetiological agent: A protozoan of genus plasmodium. 4 species:

  • P. falciparum - 12 days
  • P. vivax - 14 days
  • P. ovale - 14 days
  • P. malariae - 30 days

Occurrence: Hypo endemic (low transmission) or hyper endemic.

Distribution: Equatorial & humid tropical region.

Source: Infected female anopheline mosquito.

Pathogenicity (Two Phases)

  1. Sexual Form (Schizogony): Mosquito picks gametocytes. Fertilization forms zygote -> cyst -> sporozoite -> salivary gland.
  2. Asexual Form (Sporogony): Sporozoites introduced by bite -> liver -> RBC -> burst -> gametocytes.
