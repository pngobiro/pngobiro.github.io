üî• Fundamentals of Motivation

Motivation

The driving force or impetus behind behavior, thoughts, and actions.

Key Concepts

  • Motives: Needs or desires that energize and direct behavior toward a goal.
    • Inborn: Acquired naturally (food, water).
    • Acquired: Developed through interaction with environment (social approval).
  • Homeostasis: Tendency of the body to maintain a balanced internal state (e.g., body temperature). Disturbance creates a drive.
  • Drive: State of tension brought about by an underlying need.
    • Primary drives: Unlearned, biological (hunger, thirst).
    • Secondary drives: Acquired (need for recognition, achievement).
  • Incentive: An external stimulus that motivates behavior (e.g., money, fame).

Primary Characteristics

1. Activation

The initiation of motivated behavior. Taking the first steps.

2. Persistence

The faithful and continued effort put forth to achieve a goal.

3. Intensity

The focused energy and attention applied to achieve a goal.

ü§ù Social Motives

Motives acquired through social or cultural experience. Classified broadly into:

‚ñ∂ Affiliative Motives (Love & Belonging)

Desire to be with others (gregariousness), intimacy, and social interaction.

Role in Learning:

  • Character development (learning socially acceptable behavior).
  • Moral learning (seeking commendation).
  • Language development.
  • Enhancing aspiration (working better with liked teachers).
‚ñ∂ Prestige Motives (Esteem & Achievement)

Aimed at enhancing the degree to which an individual is admired, respected, or envied.

Role in Learning:

  • Encourages competitive spirit.
  • Improving quality of work through competition.

üî∫ Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs

Abraham Maslow (1970) suggested humans have a hierarchy of needs. Lower level needs (survival/safety) must be met before striving for higher level needs.

üîç Click to zoom

Fig 7.2: Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs.

  1. Physiological Needs (Level 1): Biological, unlearned. Food, water, oxygen, relief from pain.
  2. Safety Needs (Level 2): Security in physical/social environments. Saving money, job security, peace.
  3. Love & Belonging (Level 3): Gregariousness, giving/receiving affection, identification with others.
  4. Esteem Needs (Level 4): Success, competence, recognition, prestige.
  5. Self-Actualization (Level 5): Becoming the best one can be. Creativity, spiritual cleanliness, community service.

üòä Emotions

Emotion

A motivated state marked by physiological arousal (heartbeat), expressive behavior (smile), and mental experience (feeling).

Components of Emotion

  • Cognitive Appraisal: Evaluation of events/objects.
  • Bodily Symptoms: Physiological component (arousal).
  • Action Tendencies: Motivational component (preparation for response).
  • Expression: Facial/vocal communication.
  • Feelings: Subjective experience.

üß† Theories of Emotion

Physiological & Cognitive Theories

James-Lange Theory

"We feel sad because we cry."

Emotions occur after the body reacts. Physiological response comes first, then the awareness of it is the emotion.

Cannon-Bard Theory

Simultaneous Occurrence

The thalamus sends signals to the brain (feeling) and the nervous system (arousal) at the same time. Bodily changes and emotional experience happen together.

Schachter-Singer (Two-Factor)

Arousal + Label

Emotion = Physiological Arousal + Cognitive Labeling. We interpret external cues to label the arousal (e.g., heart pounding + lion = fear).

Lazarus (Cognitive Appraisal)

Thought Before Emotion

Cognitive appraisal is a precondition. We evaluate events (values, goals, beliefs) which determines the emotion.
