üìñ Definition & Components

Intelligence is the ability to benefit from experiences and therefore modify future behaviours to accomplish new tasks successfully. It involves:

  • Adaptive quality: Used flexibly to respond to situations.
  • Learning ability: Acquiring new information quickly.
  • Use of prior knowledge: Analyzing new situations.
  • Culture-specific: What is intelligent behavior in one culture is not necessarily so in another.
üß©

Components of Intelligence

  1. Ability to reason / think abstractly
  2. Ability to learn from experience
  3. Ability to adapt to the environment
  4. Ability to solve problems
  5. Understand and learn complex material
  6. Mental quickness

üìê Measurement of Intelligence (IQ)

To allow for comparison of test scores among persons, L. Terman devised the concept of Intelligence Quotient (IQ).

$$ \text{IQ} = \frac{\text{MA}}{\text{CA}} \times 100 $$
  • MA (Mental Age): Level of mental development determined by standardized tests.
  • CA (Chronological Age): Age from birth.
üßÆ

Calculation Example

If a child has a CA = 3 and MA = 4:

$$ \text{IQ} = \frac{4}{3} \times 100 \approx 133 $$

This child is capable of doing work of an older child (4 years) and is deemed very bright.

üìâ Mental Retardation

Children with mental retardation show developmental delays in most aspects of their academic and social functioning. They exhibit below-average intelligence and deficits in adaptive behaviour.

Causes of Mental Retardation

‚ñ∂ Biological & Genetic Factors
  • Inherited deficiencies/Chromosomal deficiencies (e.g., Down syndrome).
  • Inbreeding (marrying relatives who share genes).
  • RH factor incompatibility between parents.
  • Age of mother (past 40 years).
‚ñ∂ Prenatal & Birth Factors
  • Birth injuries leading to brain damage.
  • Prolonged labour leading to oxygen deprivation (anoxia).
  • Ingestion of toxic substances by mother (alcohol, drugs, lead).
  • Malnutrition (especially lack of proteins).
  • Diseases (Syphilis, German measles).
  • Radiation (X-rays).
‚ñ∂ Environmental Factors
  • Parental neglect or impoverished/unstimulating home life.
  • Iodine deficiency (leads to cretinism).

üèóÔ∏è Structure of Intelligence

  • Abstract Intelligence: Ability to deal with formulas and symbols (e.g., statistics, calculations).
  • Concrete Intelligence: Ability to deal with objects mechanically (e.g., plumbers, mechanics) without necessarily understanding underlying reasons.
  • Social Intelligence: Ability to deal with social situations and people appropriately and effectively.
  • Emotional Intelligence: Ability to identify, assess or control emotions of oneself and others.
  • Practical Intelligence: Knowing what to do in situations and doing it (implementers).

üí≠ Theoretical Perspectives

1. Charles Spearman

Proposed the Two-Factor Theory:

  • g-factor (General): Generalized ability contributing to all intellectual activities.
  • s-factor (Specific): Specialized abilities for particular tasks.

2. Cattell & Horn

Distinguished two aspects:

  • Fluid Intelligence: Inherited, biological, problem-solving in novel situations. Peaks in early 20s.
  • Crystallized Intelligence: Learned knowledge, schooling, cultural influence. Increases throughout adulthood.

3. Sternberg's Triarchic Model

  • Analytical: Academic problem solving.
  • Creative: Adusting to new tasks, insight.
  • Practical: Finding solutions to personal problems, street smarts.

4. Thurstone's Multifactor Theory

Proposed 7 primary mental abilities including: Number ability, Word fluency, Verbal factor, Memory, Reasoning, Spatial relations, and Perceptual speed.

üìä Tests & Determinants

Intelligence Tests

  • Weschler Intelligence Scale for Children (WISC): For ages 6-16; verbal and object manipulation.
  • Stanford-Binet Intelligence Scale: Ages 2-Adult; verbal reasoning, quantitative, abstract/visual reasoning.
  • Universal Nonverbal Intelligence Test (UNIT): Ages 5-17; gestures and pantomime instructions; ideal for hearing impairment or language barriers.
‚ö†Ô∏è

Problems with Tests

Tests are often not culture-fair. Performance is affected by home background, motivation, and anxiety. They may overlook personality aspects like interest and creativity.

Determinants: Heredity vs. Environment

üß¨ Heredity (Nature)

Evidence includes:

  • Twin Studies: High correlation (0.86) between identical twins reared together.
  • Adoption Studies: IQ scores correlate more with biological parents.
  • Neurological Efficiency: Speed of processing is genetically controlled.

üå± Environment (Nurture)

Evidence includes:

  • Separated Twins: Correlations drop when identical twins are reared apart.
  • Stimulating Home: Interaction and reading materials improve IQ.
  • Nutrition: Malnutrition limits neurological development.
