üìñ What is Psychology?

The word psychology comes from the Greek words:

  • Psyche: meaning "mind, soul, or spirit"
  • Logos: meaning "knowledge or study"
üìå

Formal Definition

Psychology is the scientific study of behavior and mental processes of humans and other animals.

Key Components

Empirical Evidence

Psychology relies on empirical evidence and scientific methods to avoid mere opinions or intuition. It uses logic to reason about possible causes and tests resulting ideas.

Aspect Description Examples
Behavior Outward (Overt) observable acts Walking, talking, facial expressions
Mental Processes Private (Covert) internal activities Thinking, dreaming, remembering, feeling

üéØ The Goals of Psychology

  1. Describe Behavior

    Answering psychological questions requires a careful description of behavior based on detailed records of observation.

  2. Understand (Explain) Behavior

    Stating the causes of behavior and determining how they led to the described behavior. Example: Understanding "bystander apathy" through the concept of "diffusion of responsibility."

  3. Predict Behavior

    The ability to forecast or foretell behavior accurately based on past observations (e.g., predicting that having many potential helpers reduces the likelihood of any single person helping).

  4. Control or Influence

    Altering conditions that affect behavior. If you help a student overcome exam anxiety or improve learning methods, you have exerted control. Note: Must be used wisely and humanely.

üèõÔ∏è Historical Schools of Thought

1. Structuralism

Founder: Wilhelm Wundt (1832‚Äì1920), the "Father of Scientific Psychology."

  • Established the first psychology lab in 1879 in Leipzig, Germany.
  • Goal: Analyze the structure of conscious mind by breaking it down into basic elements (sensations, feelings, images).
  • Method: Introspection.
‚ö†Ô∏è

Limitations of Structuralism

Introspection was subjective and unreliable; results could not be objectively repeated. Analyzing an experience often altered the experience itself.

2. Functionalism

Proponent: William James (1842‚Äì1910).

  • Influenced by Charles Darwin‚Äôs theory of natural selection.
  • Goal: Understand how the mind helps us adapt to the world (function) rather than just its structure.
  • Expanded psychology to include animal observation and practical applications in education.

3. Gestalt Psychology

Proponents: Max Wertheimer, Wolfgang Kohler, Kurt Koffka.

  • Core Idea: "The whole is greater than the sum of its parts."
  • Opposed analyzing events into pieces; studied thinking, learning, and perception as whole units (e.g., perceiving a melody, not just individual notes).

üî≠ Major Perspectives

üß† Psychoanalytic

Sigmund Freud: Emphasizes the unconscious mind, early childhood experiences, and primitive drives (sexual/aggressive). Uses free association to uncover hidden conflicts.

üêï Behavioral

Watson, Skinner, Pavlov: Focuses purely on observable behavior and learning through conditioning (Stimulus-Response). Rejects the study of internal mental states.

üå± Humanistic

Rogers, Maslow: Emphasizes free will, personal growth, and the innate drive toward self-actualization. Views people as essentially good.

üí≠ Cognitive

Piaget, Simon: Focuses on mental processes: thinking, memory, decision-making, and language. Views the mind as an information processor.

üß¨ Biological

Understands behavior through the brain, nervous system, genetics, and body chemistry. Links mental processes to physical structures.

üêí Evolutionary

Darwinian influence: Explains behavior patterns (e.g., mate selection, aggression) as adaptations that evolved to ensure survival and reproduction.

üåø Branches of Psychology

Psychology has diversified into many specialized fields:

  • Clinical & Counseling: Diagnosis and treatment of psychological problems.
  • Developmental: Human growth and development across the lifespan (Child, Adolescent, Life-span).
  • Social Psychology: Impact of social environments, attitudes, conformity, and group behavior.
  • Industrial/Organizational: Improving workplace behavior, morale, and productivity.
  • Educational/School: Learning methods, curriculum improvement, and student support.
  • Forensic: Psychology applied to the legal and criminal justice system.
  • Health Psychology: Relationship between psychological factors and physical health.
