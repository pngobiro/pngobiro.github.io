‚öñÔ∏è The Determinants of Human Development

Although genes play a role in human behaviour, they alone do not determine who we are. Genes exist within the context of a complex environment and are necessary for an organism to exist.

The interaction of nature and nurture, genes and environment, influences every aspect of mind and behaviour. Neither factor operates alone.

Nurture (Environment)

Refers to all the surrounding conditions and influences that affect the development of living things.

  • Food we eat and air we breathe
  • Physical and social contexts (towns, cities)
  • Relationships with parents, peers, teachers

Nature (Genes)

Describes an organism‚Äôs biological inheritance.

  • Innate biological structures
  • Hereditary transmission
  • Genetic makeup (DNA)

üî¨ Biological Foundations

Fertilization Process

An individual starts to form when male and female reproductive cells (gametes) combine to form a single cell called a zygote. This is facilitated by meiosis (gamete formation halving chromosomes from 46 to 23). Mitosis takes place after the zygote has been formed.

üß¨

Chromosomes & Sex Determination

  • Total: 46 chromosomes (23 pairs).
  • Autosomes: 22 pairs containing most genetic information.
  • Sex Chromosomes: The 23rd pair determines sex.
    • XX = Female
    • XY = Male (Sex determination is influenced by the male gametes).

Genetic Principles

Each chromosome contains thousands of biochemical messengers called genes (DNA segments).

  • Dominant-Recessive Principle: A dominant gene exerts its effects overriding the recessive gene. A recessive gene exerts influence only if both genes of a pair are recessive.
  • Genotype: A person's genetic heritage; the actual genetic material.
  • Phenotype: How the genotype is expressed in observable characteristics (physical traits like height/eye color, and psychological traits like intelligence/personality).

1. Sex Chromosome Abnormalities

‚ñº Monosomy: Turner‚Äôs Syndrome (XO)

Disorder in females where an X chromosome is missing (XO instead of XX) or partially deleted.

Characteristics: Short stature, webbed neck, may be infertile, poorly developed secondary sex features, sexual dysfunction in adulthood, broad chest with widely spaced nipples, perceptual and cognitive deficiencies.

‚ñ∂ Trisomy: Klinefelter‚Äôs Syndrome (XXY)

Males have an extra X chromosome (XXY instead of XY).

Characteristics: Underdeveloped testes, enlarged breasts, reduced body hair, possible infertility, poor muscular/motor development, speech/language disorders, taller than average.

‚ñ∂ Trisomy: XYY (Supermale Syndrome)

Sex chromosomal disorder affecting males with an extra Y chromosome.

Characteristics: Heavy beards, unusual tallness, mild mental retardation (language), violent antisocial personality.

‚ñ∂ Trisomy: XXX (Super Female Syndrome)

Females with an extra X chromosome.

Characteristics: Delay in speech/language, lack of coordination, poor academic performance, taller than average, timid personality. Sexual development is usually normal.

2. Autosomal Abnormalities

  • Autosomal Monosomy: Has not been observed in humans because the embryo cannot proceed beyond early developmental stages.
  • Down Syndrome (Trisomy 21): Caused by a deviation on the 21st pair (individual may have 47 chromosomes).
    Characteristics: Small hands, large tongue, severe mental retardation, heart defects, flattened facial features, short stature, poor motor coordination. Probability increases with mother's age.
  • Trisomy D and E: Extra chromosome to pairs 13 and 15.
    Characteristics: Deficient forebrain development, small or absent eyes, cardiac/kidney defects.
