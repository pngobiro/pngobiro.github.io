üìñ Defining Learning

Behavioral View

An enduring change in behavior potentiality, which occurs as a result of reinforced practice. Focuses on observable behaviors.

Cognitive View

The mental activity by means of which knowledge, skills, habits, attitudes and ideas are acquired, retained and utilized, resulting in the progressive adaptation and modification of behavior.

What is NOT Learning?

  • Maturation/Organic deterioration: Changes due to growth or aging.
  • Temporary States: Fatigue, pain, or sensory adaptation.
  • Chemical Influence: Changes caused by alcohol or drugs.
02

üêÅ Research in Learning

Psychologists use animals (dogs, rats, cats, pigeons, chimpanzees) for research to discover conditions that facilitate learning.

Why use animals?

  • Humans and animals learn in much the same way (generalizability).
  • Animal experiences and genetics can be precisely controlled.
  • Animals have shorter lifespans, allowing study of generations.
  • Ethical concerns prevent subjecting humans to certain laboratory conditions.

üìú Principles of Classical Conditioning

  1. Reinforcement: The presentation of a pleasant event (food) after the stimulus (tone).
  2. Extinction: The gradual weakening and disappearance of a learned response when reinforcement is withdrawn (bell rung without food).
  3. Spontaneous Recovery: The reappearance of a learned response after extinction and a rest period, without new reinforcement.
  4. Generalization: Responding to stimuli similar to the original stimulus (e.g., salivating to different tones).
  5. Discrimination: Learning to pick out differences in stimuli and responding only to a specific one (reinforced for one tone but not others).

Reinforcement Contingencies

  • Positive Reinforcement: Increasing behavior by presenting a rewarding stimulus (e.g., food, praise, grades).
  • Negative Reinforcement: Increasing behavior by removing an unpleasant stimulus (e.g., doing homework to stop a parent's nagging).
  • Punishment: Decreasing undesirable behavior by presenting aversive stimuli (e.g., spanking) or removing a reward.
‚≠ê

Types of Reinforcers

Primary: Inherently reinforcing, satisfy biological needs (food, water, pain avoidance).

Secondary: Learned/acquired needs (money, grades, praise, attention).

06

‚è∞ Schedules of Reinforcement

Continuous Reinforcement: Every instance of desired behavior is reinforced.

Partial Reinforcement: Reinforcing only part of the time.

Schedule Description Example
Fixed Ratio (FR) Reinforcement after a fixed number of responses. Praising a student after every 4th correct answer.
Variable Ratio (VR) Reinforcement after an unpredictable number of responses. Gambling; reinforcing the 10th, then 5th, then 14th response.
Fixed Interval (FI) Reinforcement after a set time interval. Weekly test; Monthly salary.
Variable Interval (VI) Reinforcement after variable time intervals. Surprise quizzes. Highly resistant to extinction.
