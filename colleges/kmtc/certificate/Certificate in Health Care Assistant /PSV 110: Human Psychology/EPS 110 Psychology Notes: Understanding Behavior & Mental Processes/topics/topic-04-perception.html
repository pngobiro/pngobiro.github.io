üìñ Definitions & Concepts

Sensation

The process by which the senses gather information about the environment. It involves transduction: the process that converts physical energy into neural messages (electro-chemical in nature).

Perception

Perception is a creation of the brain; it is based on input extracted from sensory organs. It involves selection, organization, and interpretation of sensory information.

üîó

Relationship

  • Sensation is the process of inputting information through the senses.
  • Perception is the process through which organization and understanding of what has been sensed occurs.

üìâ Sensory Thresholds

Absolute Threshold

The smallest amount of stimulus needed in order to notice that the stimulus is present at all.

Sense Threshold Example
Light A candle light 30 miles away on a dark, clear night.
Smell A drop of perfume diffused throughout a three-room apartment.
Hearing The ticking of a watch from 6 meters (20 feet) in quiet conditions.
Taste One tea spoon of sugar in two gallons of water.
Touch The wing of a fly falling on your cheek from a height of 1 cm.

Difference Threshold (Just Noticeable Difference - jnd)

The smallest change in stimulation that you can detect (e.g., how much stronger must a sound become before you notice it has grown louder?).

Sensory Adaptation

The diminishing sensitivity to unchanging stimuli. Examples: Eyes adjusting to a dark theater, adapting to the smell of fresh paint, or ignoring continuous noise. Adaptation allows our senses to be keenly attuned to environmental conditions without becoming overloaded.

üß© Perceptual Organization (Gestalt Principles)

We use structures to categorize various stimuli, resulting in meaningful perceptions.

1. Figure and Ground

Organizing the perceptual field into stimuli that stand out (figure) and those that are left over (ground/background). Example: Words on a paper (figure) vs. the paper itself (ground).

2. Perceptual Grouping Principles

  • Proximity: Things near one another seem to belong together.
    Example: XXX XXX is seen as two groups.
  • Similarity: Things that look alike tend to be grouped together.
  • Continuity: We tend to perceive smooth continuous patterns rather than discontinuous ones.
  • Closure: People group elements to create a sense of completeness; filling in gaps (e.g., seeing a circle even if a small section is missing).
  • Simplicity (Good Form): People tend to group elements that combine to form a good, simple figure.

‚öì Perceptual Constancies

The tendency to perceive objects as relatively stable and unchanging despite changing sensory information.

Shape Constancy

Perceiving familiar objects as having a constant shape regardless of viewing angle. (e.g., a door remains rectangular even when opening).

Size Constancy

Perceiving an object as having a constant size despite distance. (e.g., a car seen from afar is still perceived as big enough to carry people).

Brightness Constancy

Perceiving objects as having consistent brightness even when illumination varies. (e.g., white paper looks white indoors or outdoors).

Color Constancy

Perceiving familiar objects as having consistent color even when illumination changes. (e.g., a yellow dress looks yellow day or night).

üß† Factors Influencing Perception

‚ñ∂ 1. Characteristics of the Stimulus

Objects that are vivid, colorful, or patterned are easier to perceive and draw more attention.

‚ñ∂ 2. Culture

Demands of different cultures lead to emphasis on certain senses. E.g., Deregowski's study found Malawians perceived colored photos better than black and white ones due to their colorful culture.

‚ñ∂ 3. Context

The setting in which perception occurs affects interpretation. E.g., The symbol "13" can be seen as a "B" depending on context (A 13 C vs 12 13 14).

‚ñ∂ 4. Selective Attention

Tendency to focus on specific stimuli and ignore others based on interests and needs (e.g., a naturalist vs. a picnicker in a park).

‚ñ∂ 5. Motivation

Desires and needs shape perception. Hungry people are more likely to perceive vague images as food.

‚ñ∂ 6. Past Experience / Perceptual Set

Tendency to perceive the environment in determined ways based on experience. It influences attitudes and values.

07

üîÆ Extrasensory Perception (ESP)

Perception that occurs without the use of any known sensory process (Paranormal).

  • Telepathy: Mind-to-mind communication; thought transference.
  • Clairvoyance: Perceiving remote events not in sight (e.g., sensing a sealed number).
  • Precognition: Perceiving future events before they happen.
  • Psychokinesis: Mind-over-matter; moving objects without touching them.
  • Teleportation: Ability to relocate oneself to places of one's wish.
