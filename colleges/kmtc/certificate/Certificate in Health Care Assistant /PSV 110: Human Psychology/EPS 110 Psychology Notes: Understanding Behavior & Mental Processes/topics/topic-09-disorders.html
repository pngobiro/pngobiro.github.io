üìú Defining Abnormality

Psychological disorders are mental processes and/or behavior patterns that cause emotional distress and/or substantial impairment in functioning.

Historical Perspective

  • Ancient Times: Attributed to the devil. Cured by trephination (chiseling a hole in the skull to release spirits).
  • Medieval Europe: Demonological model; witch hunts.
  • Hippocrates (5th Century BC): Viewed mental illness as a disease rooted in the brain (early biological perspective).

DSM-IV Definition

A clinically significant behavioral or psychological syndrome associated with present distress (pain), disability (impairment in functioning), or significantly increased risk of suffering death, pain, disability, or loss of freedom.

üß¨ Explaining Abnormality (Etiology)

Biological/Medical Model

Caused by physiological malfunctions (nervous system, endocrine glands, heredity, chemical deficiency). Treated via physical examination and medication.

Psychoanalytic Model

Symbolic expressions of unconscious conflicts, often traced to childhood traumas. Resolution requires becoming aware of these hidden sources.

Behavioral Model

Abnormal behavior is a product of learning (response to stimuli). Maladaptive behaviors like phobias are learned and can be unlearned.

Cognitive Model

Root cause is ineffective or inaccurate thinking (cognitions). Faulty beliefs lead to abnormal behavior.

Sociocultural Perspective

Product of broad social factors: poverty, unemployment, inequality. Includes culture-bound syndromes (e.g., Taijin Kyofusho in Japan).

Family Systems Model

The individual is a symptom of unhealthy family dynamics and poor communication. The locus of disorder resides in the family, not the individual.

The Diathesis-Stress Model

A biological predisposition (diathesis) must combine with a stressful circumstance before the predisposition shows up as a behavior.

üò∞ Neurosis

Mild mental disorders causing distress and impairment but without loss of contact with reality. Neurotics are generally unhappy individuals with inadequate coping mechanisms.

‚ñ∂ 1. Anxiety Neurosis

General feeling of worry and apprehension (free-floating anxiety). Symptoms: Tenseness, sweating, rapid heartbeat, sleeping difficulties.

‚ñ∂ 2. Phobias

‚ñ∂ 3. Hysteria (Conversion Reaction)

Appearance of physical symptoms with no organic origin (e.g., temporary paralysis, blindness, uncontrolled laughing) caused by severe stress.

‚ñ∂ 4. Obsessive-Compulsive Reactions

Obsession: Persistent, irrational thoughts (e.g., "Did I lock the door?").

Compulsion: Ritualistic behavior performed to relieve the obsession (e.g., checking the door 10 times).

‚ñ∂ 5. Hypochondriasis

Excessive concern with one's health. Interpreting minor sensations (headache) as serious illness (tumor).

‚ñ∂ 6. Dissociative Reactions

Sudden repression of entire episodes of life.

  • Amnesia: Loss of memory (identity or traumatic event).
  • Fugue: "To flee"; wandering off and adopting a new identity with no memory of the past.
  • Multiple Personality: Developing two or more distinct personalities that may not be aware of each other.

üåÄ Psychosis

Severe mental illness where the individual loses touch with reality. Often requires institutional care.

Key Symptoms

Hallucinations

Sensory perceptions in the absence of external stimuli (imaginary sensations, hearing voices).

Delusions

Strong false beliefs held despite contrary evidence.

  • Grandeur: Belief one is famous/powerful.
  • Persecution: Belief enemies are out to harm.
  • Reference: Misinterpreting chance occurrences as personal.

Types of Psychosis

1. Manic-Depressive Reactions (Bipolar)

Extreme mood swings from strong excitement (mania) to extreme sadness (depression).

2. Schizophrenia ("Split Mind")

Characterized by being "out of touch" with reality.

Type Characteristics
Paranoid Delusions of grandeur/persecution, hallucinations. May look normal otherwise.
Disorganized Silliness, laughter, grotesque mannerisms, bizarre behavior.
Catatonic Complete stillness/stupor or great agitation. Maintaining unusual postures for long periods.
Undifferentiated Symptoms do not fit neatly into one category.
