‚öóÔ∏è Science vs. Common Sense

Psychologists gather information by thinking critically about, and scientifically studying the mind/mental processes and behaviour. This entails the use of scientific research (objective, systematic, and testable).

Psychology differs from common sense in various ways:

Scientific Psychology Common Sense
Scientific inquiry based on thorough research. Information gathered from everyday informal observations and experiences.
Rigorously tested and verified judgments. Does not use analytical methods crucial to decision making.
Relies on empirical evidence and logic. Allows belief in metaphysical causal agents (ghosts, angels).
Requires precision, accuracy, and lack of bias. Notions tend to be vague and ambiguous.
Scrutinizes findings; demands objective data. Little effort to verify ideas or detect errors ("Armchair speculation").

üë£ Steps in Scientific Investigation

  1. a) Conceptualizing/Specifying a Problem

    This is the question one wants to answer, or the puzzle one wants to solve. Theories can be used in conceptualizing a problem.

  2. b) Formulating a Testable Hypothesis

    A hypothesis is a tentative idea that might explain a set of observations, or specific assumptions/predictions that can be tested.

    üí°

    Types of Hypotheses

    • Alternative Hypothesis (\(H_1\)): States there are significant differences or relationships between variables.
      Directional: Shows direction of effect (e.g., "positive relationship").
      Non-directional: Does not give direction (e.g., "there are differences").
    • Null Hypothesis (\(H_0\)): States there is no effect/difference/relationship. Any difference is due to chance.
  3. c) Operational Definition of Terms

    Establishes precisely what is meant by each variable in the context of the study by specifying the observational measurements that define them.

  4. d) Select Research Method & Design

    Choosing strategies (experiments, case studies, surveys, etc.) based on the nature of the question. Includes planning for participants (subjects).

  5. e) Collect Data

    Using techniques like observation, questionnaires, interviews, psychological tests, or physiological recordings.

  6. f) Analyze Data

    Using statistics to decide whether hypotheses have been supported.

  7. g) Draw Conclusions

    Based on statistical analysis.

  8. h) Report Findings

    Sharing findings with the public and scientific community via journals or meetings.

üìä Research Methods

1. Experimental Research

A method in which an investigator manipulates a variable under carefully controlled conditions and observes whether changes occur in a second variable. It allows psychologists to determine behavior‚Äôs causes.

  • üîë

    Independent Variable (IV): The variable manipulated by the experimenter.

  • üîë

    Dependent Variable (DV): The measured result (behavior) that depends on the IV.

  • üîë

    Groups: Experimental Group receives treatment; Control Group does not.

  • üîë

    Random Assignment: Subjects assigned on a chance basis so each has an equal chance of being in any group.

Advantages Disadvantages
Establishes cause and effect relationships (causality). Some variables cannot be manipulated (e.g., age, gender).
Allows for precise control of variables. Ethical issues may prevent manipulation (e.g., withholding treatment).
Artificiality of laboratory settings may affect normal performance.

2. Observation

Recording ongoing behavior with as little interaction as possible.

  • Naturalistic Observation: Observing in real-life settings without altering behavior.
  • Laboratory Observation: Observing in a controlled environment.
‚ñ∂ View Advantages & Disadvantages

Advantages

  • Observed in natural settings.
  • Crucial for determining function in everyday life.
  • Researcher does not impose on the subject.

Disadvantages

  • Does not explain why (cause-effect).
  • Observer Bias: Tendency to interpret data to fit expectations.
  • Observer Effect: Presence of observer can alter behavior.
  • No information on internal psychological events (thoughts/beliefs).

3. Case Study

An in-depth study of an individual, group, or institution. Frequently used for new phenomena or rare events. Methods include interviews, direct observation, and records examination.

Pros: Offers useful in-depth information; flexible exploration; useful for exploring behavior.
Cons: Time-consuming; costly; subject drop-out; low generalizability; highly subjective (investigators see what they expect).

4. Correlational Studies

Seeks to determine whether a relationship exists between variables.

  • Positive Correlation: Variables increase or decrease together (e.g., TV violence and aggression).
  • Negative Correlation: As one increases, the other decreases (e.g., schooling and dementia risk).
‚ö†Ô∏è

Limitation

Correlation enables prediction but does not imply causation. Cause and effect cannot be determined.

5. Surveys

Using questionnaires or interviews to gather information about specific aspects of participants' behavior.

‚ñ∂ View Advantages & Disadvantages

Advantages

  • Inexpensive way to collect lots of data quickly.
  • Easy to collect data on attitudes, opinions, beliefs.
  • Good for behaviors difficult to observe directly.

Disadvantages

  • Depends on self-report (intentional deception possible).
  • Limited by capability to report accurately.

6. Clinical Method

Involves a researcher and a subject at a time, entailing observation and careful questioning.

  • Advantage: Questions can be individualized; detailed observation.
  • Disadvantage: Conclusions depend on interviewer's ability; results may require verification.
