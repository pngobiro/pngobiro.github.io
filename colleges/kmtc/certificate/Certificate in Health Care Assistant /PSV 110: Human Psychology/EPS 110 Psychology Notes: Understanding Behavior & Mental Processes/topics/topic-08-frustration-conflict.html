üõë Understanding Frustration

Frustration

A negative emotional state that occurs when people are prevented from reaching desired goals. It is experienced whenever the pursuit of a goal is thwarted or when we are not able to satisfy a motive.

Sources of Frustration

  • Environmental Obstacles:
    Physical: Earthquakes, floods, traffic jams, rain at a picnic.
    Social: Strict rules, poor working conditions, prejudice.
  • Personal Obstacles: Limitations or inadequacies preventing satisfaction (e.g., lack of skill, wealth, attractiveness, physical disability).
  • Conflicts: Inability to decide between opposing motives.

üîÄ Types of Conflicts

Conflict occurs when two or more incompatible motivations or behavioral impulses compete for expression.

1. Approach ‚Äì Approach

Definition: Choosing between two equally attractive goals.

Key: Least stressful; resolved by choosing one.

2. Approach ‚Äì Avoidance

Definition: A single goal has both attractive and unattractive aspects.

Example: Wanting a promotion (pay raise) but fearing the increased responsibility.

3. Avoidance ‚Äì Avoidance

Definition: Choosing between two unpleasant alternatives ("Between a rock and a hard place").

Example: Enduring a toothache vs. going to the dentist.

4. Double Approach ‚Äì Avoidance

Definition: Choosing between two alternatives, each having both positive and negative qualities.

Example: Job A (Good pay, boring work) vs. Job B (Low pay, interesting work).

üõ°Ô∏è Defense Mechanisms

People react to frustration in various ways, such as withdrawal, aggression, or apathy. Often, they use defense mechanisms‚Äîunconscious strategies to reduce anxiety and maintain self-esteem.

Short-Term Mechanisms

‚ñ∂ Restlessness & Tension

Whimpering, sighing, complaining, trembling, pacing.

‚ñ∂ Destructiveness (Aggression)

Direct: Attacking the source of frustration.

Indirect: Scapegoating (blaming innocent) or Displaced aggression.

‚ñ∂ Apathy

Indifference, inactivity, "don't care" attitude (common in prisoners).

‚ñ∂ Regression

Returning to primitive/childish modes of behavior (e.g., tantrums, bedwetting in older children).

Long-Term Mechanisms

Mechanism Description Example
Rationalization Giving logical excuses instead of real reasons. "I didn't want that job anyway."
Projection Attributing own unacceptable traits to others. A cheating partner accuses spouse of cheating.
Reaction Formation Expressing the opposite of disturbing unconscious impulses. Being overly polite to someone you hate.
Repression Involuntarily blocking unpleasant memories. Forgetting a traumatic accident.
Denial Refusing to accept reality. Refusing to believe a diagnosis.
Displacement Shifting hostility to a safer target. Yelling at spouse after boss yells at you.
Sublimation Rechanneling unacceptable impulses into socially approved activities. Channeling aggression into sports.
Compensation Making up for failure in one area by excelling in another. A poor student becoming a great athlete.
