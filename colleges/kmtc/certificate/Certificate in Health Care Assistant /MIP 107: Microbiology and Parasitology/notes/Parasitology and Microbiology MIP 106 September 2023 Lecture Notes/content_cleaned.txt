<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="definition-of-terms-in-parasitology">DEFINITION OF TERMS IN PARASITOLOGY<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#definition-of-terms-in-parasitology" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Parasitology- Science that deals with organisms that live temporarily or permanently on/within other living organisms for the purpose of procuring food and the relationship of this organisms to their hosts</li>
<li>Endo parasite- live inside body e.g. Tenia saginata</li>
<li>Permanent Parasite- spends major part</li>
<li>Temporary parasite- only make contact to the host e.g. bed bugs</li>
<li>Facultative parasites- Live either paristic or free lives e.g. hookworms</li>
<li>Obligate- In capable of living independently.</li>
<li>Auto Heteroxenous parasites- A definitive host of one generation becomes the intermediate host of another generation.</li>
<li>Pathogenic Parasites- Parasite that causes injury to host e.g. mechanical, traumatic or toxic</li>
<li>Parasite- Organism which lives in another organism and derives its food from another host and gives nothing in turn.</li>
<li>Definitive Host – Host in which parasite reaches maturity e.g. man in case of taenia saginata</li>
<li>Intermediate host- Host where parasite undergoes preliminary development .g taenia saginata in bovine</li>
<li>Reservoir- Source of Infestation</li>
<li>Cyst- Larvae stage of parasite, no development goes on in this stage.</li>
<li>Encystment- Process of forming a cyst</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="host-parasite-association">HOST-PARASITE ASSOCIATION<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#host-parasite-association" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Parasites and hosts interact in the following ways;</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Parasitism</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>An association where one organism is harmed while the other benefits. E.g. Ascaris lumbricoides residing in the intestinal tract of man to feed on the food in the intestines resulting to infections</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Downloaded by Patrick Ngobiro (<a href="mailto:kenyaewakili@gmail.com" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">kenyaewakili@gmail.com</a>)</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Mutualism</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>An association where both parasite and host benefits from the relationship</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Whereby organisms benefit each other but one can live an independent life (independency)</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Commensalism</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>An association where a parasite benefits from the relationship without causing any harm to the host</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Whereby an organism lives in the host, confers no benefit nor harm except where the host’s immunity impairs then the it can become an opportunist</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>An association between two organisms in which one benefits and the other derives neither benefit nor harm.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Symbiosis</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>relationship where the parasite and the host benefit each other and cannot live independent of each other (dependency)</p>
</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="introduction">Introduction<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#introduction" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>The classification of parasites is controversial as there is no universally accepted system.</li>
<li>The most acceptable taxonomic classification of human parasites includes:</li>
<li>Endoparasites and Ectoparasites.</li>
<li>Endoparasites are sub-classified into Helminthic parasites and Protozoan parasites.</li>
</ul>
<p>Helminthic parasites are either flat worms (Trematodes), segmented ribbon like worms (Cestodes) or cylindrical worms (Nematodes).</p>

<p>PREVENTION AND CONTROL MEASURES OF PARASITES</p>
<ul>
<li>Use of chemicals for spraying especially the ectoparasites</li>
<li>Proper cooking of beef and other vegetables</li>
<li>Proper washing of fruits and vegetables before consumption</li>
<li>Regular deworming of animals and man</li>
<li>Proper waste management (excreta and other wastes)</li>
<li>Treating or boiling water before drinking</li>
<li>Proper meat inspection</li>
<li>Proper hygiene</li>
<li>Wearing of shoes /gumboots while walking in water or swampy water infested with worms</li>
<li>Chemotherapy-preventive chemotherapy for school-going children such as deworming</li>
<li>Health education and promotion on proper hygiene</li>
<li>Improve access to clean water and sanitation</li>
<li>Avoid swimming/bathing in water infested with worms</li>
<li>Drainage of stagnant water to prevent breeding of mosquito larvae</li>
<li>Use of bed nets to prevent mosquito bites</li>
<li>Washing hands regularly especially after handling raw food and fecal matter</li>
<li>Periodic examination of food handlers to prevent cross-contamination of food</li>
<li>Get rid of intermediate host</li>
<li>Zero grazing/rotational grazing</li>
<li>Proper storage of food to avoid mechanical damage</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="introduction">Introduction<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#introduction" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Life cycles of parasites may be simple or complex. Parasites that are characterized by a simple or direct life cycle have only one host and are described as <strong>monoxenous</strong> (e.g. life cycle of <em>Ascaris lumbricoides</em>).</li>
</ul>
<p>Downloaded by Patrick Ngobiro (<a href="mailto:kenyaewakili@gmail.com" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">kenyaewakili@gmail.com</a>)</p>
<p>The parasite generally spends most of its life in or on the host, and may reproduce within the host. Because offspring must be transmitted to other hosts, however, the parasite or its progeny must have some way of leaving the host, surviving in the external environment for some period, and locating and infecting a new host.</p>
<p>Parasites with simple life cycles have both parasitic and free living life stages. The proportion of the total life cycle spent in each stage varies according to the parasite.</p>
<ul>
<li>Parasites with more complex life cycles involving multiple hosts are described as having indirect or heteroxenous life cycles (e.g. life cycle of Fasciola spp).</li>
<li>The primary or definitive host of a heteroxenous species is the one in which adult parasites live and reproduce sexually.</li>
<li>The secondary or intermediate host (IH) is the host where immature life stages of the parasite live and reproduce asexually.</li>
</ul>
<p>In many cases, the parasite passes through critical developmental stages in the IH. The latter may also aid in transmitting parasites to their final host. Rat flea, for example, is the IH for mammalian parasites such as the tapeworm; Hymenolepis diminuta.</p>
<ul>
<li>Some parasites are transmitted directly from one host to another, often by insects, described as vectors.</li>
</ul>
<p>One particularly effective vector for vertebrate parasites is the mosquito, which plays a role in transmission of numerous parasites including heartworm, the viruses that cause yellow fever and encephalitis, and Plasmodium, the protozoan that causes malaria.</p>

<p>☐ Fillarial form larvae penetrate through the skin of man. They travel via circulatory system to reach the heart. From heart to lungs and ascend through trachea where they are swallowed and go through digestive system then develop into adults in the small intestines. After mating the gravid (ripe) females lay eggs (between 24,000- 30,000 a day). Ripe eggs come out together with feaces and hatch into filarial form larvae. If the filarial form larvae penetrate the skin of man, cycle continues.</p>

<ul>
<li>After circulating in blood circulation for about 20-30 minutes, they enter carenchymal cells of the liver- begin to multiply (This stage is referred to as erythrocytic stage)</li>
<li>In 10 days multiply small forms called merozoites break out of the liver cells and are released into the circulatory system and seek to enter erythrocytes</li>
<li>In the erythrocytes (intra- erythrocytic stage) a series of development takes place and form a ring form known as trophozoite and depending on space they enlarge and divide into segments known as schizonts. Individual schizonts are known as merozoites</li>
<li>When mature schizonts rapture from erythrocytes the merozoites are released into the circulation and seek to enter uninfected erythrocytes.</li>
<li>The period of release of merozoites into circulation accounts for fever (mostly in 2 days) plasmodium vivax (tartian). (3 days) plasmodium falciparum (quartan)</li>
<li>After multiple erythrocytic cycles, some merozoites transform themselves into sexual macrogametocytes (female) and others into microgametocytes (males)</li>
<li>If a mosquito bites a human being with malaria gametocytes are ingested with blood meal</li>
<li>In the mosquito stomach microgametocytes develop flagella. Microgametocytes break free and penetrate macrogametocytes and produce a zygote (fertilized)</li>
<li>Zygote enter mosquito wall, develop into ookinetes which develop into oocyst and sporozites eventually break out of body cavity and migrate to the salivary glands. When the mosquito bites uninfected man, the sporozoites are squeezed from salivary glands into circulatory system of man and the cycle continues.</li>
</ul>

<li>Disease- Druncontiasis/Druncuncillosis</li>

<li>Definitive host- Man- fur bearing animals e.g. cats, wolves, dogs e.t.c</li>

<p>Man → Drink infested water (with Cyclops) → Larvae is released → Larvae penetrate wall of small intestines → Migrate to CTs through blood and lymphatic system circulation → Mating takes place in deep tissues → Males disappear → Ripe or gravid females migrate to the subcutaneous layer that come into contact with water e.g. legs, arms → When ready they discharge microfilarial into the water and Cyclops ingest the larvae and the cycle continues</p>
