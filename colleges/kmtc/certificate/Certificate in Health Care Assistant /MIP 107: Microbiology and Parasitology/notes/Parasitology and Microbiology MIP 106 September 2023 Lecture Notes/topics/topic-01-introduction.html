ü§ù Host-Parasite Association

Parasites and hosts interact in the following ways:

  • Parasitism
    An association where one organism is harmed while the other benefits. E.g. Ascaris lumbricoides residing in the intestinal tract of man to feed on the food in the intestines resulting to infections
  • Mutualism
    An association where both parasite and host benefits from the relationship. Whereby organisms benefit each other but one can live an independent life (independency).
  • Commensalism
    An association where a parasite benefits from the relationship without causing any harm to the host. Whereby an organism lives in the host, confers no benefit nor harm except where the host‚Äôs immunity impairs then the it can become an opportunist. An association between two organisms in which one benefits and the other derives neither benefit nor harm.
  • Symbiosis
    relationship where the parasite and the host benefit each other and cannot live independent of each other (dependency)

üìë Parasites of Public Health Importance

Introduction

  • The classification of parasites is controversial as there is no universally accepted system.
  • The most acceptable taxonomic classification of human parasites includes:
    • Endoparasites and Ectoparasites.
  • Endoparasites are sub-classified into Helminthic parasites and Protozoan parasites.

Helminthic parasites are either flat worms (Trematodes), segmented ribbon like worms (Cestodes) or cylindrical worms (Nematodes).

Classification of parasites

Parasites occur in two distinct forms:

  • Single-celled protozoa
  • Multicellular metozoa helminths

ü™± Helminths (Worms)

Helminths cause much disease and are the largest of human parasites, also known as soil transmitted worms.

Helminths of medical importance are divided into three zoological classes, namely:

  • Cestodes (tapeworms)
  • Nematodes (round worms)
  • Trematodes (flukes)

Morphology of the Helminths

üìè

CESTODES

  • They have a segmented tape-like shape
  • Head end has suckers with hooks
  • Have no alimentary canal
  • Lack the body cavity
  • The sexes are not separated (monoecious)
Examples:
  • Taenia saginata (Cow tapeworm)
  • Taenia solium (Pig tapeworm)
  • Diphylobotrium latum (Fish tapeworm)
  • Echinococcus granulosus (Dog tapeworm)

NEMATODES

  • They have an elongated cylindrical shape
  • Head end have no suckers and hooks(lack)
  • Have a complete alimentary canal(present and complete)
  • Have a body cavity
  • The sexes are separated(diecious)
Examples:
  • Ascaris lumbricoides (roundworms)
  • Enterobius vermicularis (pin worm)
  • Trichuris trichuria ((whip worm)
  • Ancylostoma duodenale (hook worm)

ü§í Effects of Parasites to the Host

  • Cause trauma by damaging tissues and organs where they are lodged
  • Causes anemia- they deplete blood causing normocytic anemia
  • Compete with the host for food nutrients resulting to malnutrition of the affected host
  • Some causes indigestion e.g. Taenia saginata they cause indigestion in the cow
  • They cause blockage of vessels and organs e.g. fish tapeworm in the fish intestines
  • Immunosuppression- bilharzia, malaria etc. leads to certain degree of immune suppression which renders the infected host to other diseases
  • Anaphylactic shock- maybe induced by sudden release of larger amounts of parasites into the blood stream
  • Allergic reactions- in the case of River blindness, the presence of filarial worms under the skin leads to depigmentation due to allergic reactions
  • Mechanical damage- in the cases of Ascaris infections, the presence of the worms in the intestines may lead to intestinal damage
  • Physical damage- caused by irritation of the skin and tissues by the ecto and endoparasites
  • Cause/source of nuisance- e.g. mosquito noise disrupt the sleeping patterns of the people
  • Super infections- in the case of (muco) cutaneous leishmaniasis, ulceration may lead to super infections with the bacteria
  • Production of toxic compounds- it's thought that African trypanosome when it's in the central nervous system, it produces aromatic amino acid analogues which influence the brain functioning
  • Reduces the host population size through death
  • Cause tumors for the case of Cysticercus bovis in the cow
  • Leads to severe loss of weight in both animals and man (hosts)
  • Can cause medical complications e.g. fits (when the ova of Echinococcus granulosus is lodged in the dog brain)
  • Cause psychological discomforts e.g. crawling of the tapeworms
  • Reduces productivity of the host

üîÑ Parasites' Life Cycle

Introduction

  • Life cycles of parasites may be simple or complex. Parasites that are characterized by a simple or direct life cycle have only one host and are described as monoxenous (e.g. life cycle of Ascaris lumbricoides).

The parasite generally spends most of its life in or on the host, and may reproduce within the host. Because offspring must be transmitted to other hosts, however, the parasite or its progeny must have some way of leaving the host, surviving in the external environment for some period, and locating and infecting a new host.

Parasites with simple life cycles have both parasitic and free living life stages. The proportion of the total life cycle spent in each stage varies according to the parasite.

  • Parasites with more complex life cycles involving multiple hosts are described as having indirect or heteroxenous life cycles (e.g. life cycle of Fasciola spp).
  • The primary or definitive host of a heteroxenous species is the one in which adult parasites live and reproduce sexually.
  • The secondary or intermediate host (IH) is the host where immature life stages of the parasite live and reproduce asexually.

In many cases, the parasite passes through critical developmental stages in the IH. The latter may also aid in transmitting parasites to their final host. Rat flea, for example, is the IH for mammalian parasites such as the tapeworm; Hymenolepis diminuta.

  • Some parasites are transmitted directly from one host to another, often by insects, described as vectors.

One particularly effective vector for vertebrate parasites is the mosquito, which plays a role in transmission of numerous parasites including heartworm, the viruses that cause yellow fever and encephalitis, and Plasmodium, the protozoan that causes malaria.
