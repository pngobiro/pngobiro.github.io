üß∂ Trichuris Trichiura (Whip Worm)

  • Disease: Trichiuriasis
  • Distribution: cosmopolitan but common in East Africa.
  • Definitive host: man
  • Intermediate host: Nil
  • How man is infected: Ingestion of un-hatched larvae

Life Cycle

01

Adults are found in the small intestines of man. Mating takes place here. Gravid females lay eggs about 2000 per day.

02

Adult bury the head in the wall of small intestines in a whip like form. Eggs come out together with feaces.

03

Man swallows eggs or un hatched larvae. They pass through the mouth, oesophagus to stomach where eggs are dissolved larvae emerge and go to small intestines where they mature to adults. The cycle continues.

Effects in Man

  • During the process of burying whip like head in the mucous membrane of small intestine there is no apparent damage.
  • Areas of local inflammation
  • Depending on the dose/number they may cause Appendicitis

  • Diagnosis: Ova in feaces
  • Treatment: Use antihelminths

ü™± Strongyloides Stercolaris

  • Facultative parasite
  • Causes strogyloidosis
  • Distribution: cosmopolitan
  • Definitive host: man
  • Intermediate host: nill
  • How man is infected: penetration of skin by filarial larvae

Life Cycle

Fillarial form larvae penetrates skin of man. It enters blood circulatory system to reach lungs. In lungs they ascend through trachea where they are swallowed. They go to stomach- intestines and develop into adults. Eggs are laid and come out with feaces. The eggs hatch into filarial form larvae. (Rhabidth form larvae)

üå°Ô∏è

Symptoms

  • Irritation at the point of penetration
  • During migration; ill feeling, fever, urticaria (change of color at the point of penetration), difficult breathing, mild broncho pneumonia
  • When they develop into adults; diarrhoea
üè•

Effects in Man

  1. Raised blotches (red)
  2. Inflammation of the respiratory system
  3. Pneumonia
üî¨

Diagnosis

Ova in feaces/ Rabiditi form

Prevention/ control

  1. Safe disposal of feaces
  2. Protect the skin (wearing shoes)
  3. Carry out treatment of the sick
  4. Create awareness on methods of prevention and control

ü™ù Ankylostomes (Hook Worm)

Disease: Ankylostosomias (hook worm disease)

Distribution: Tropical areas, America

Type Definitive Host
Ankylostoma Americanus Man
Ankylostoma Duodenale Man
Ankylostoma Caninum Cats / dogs
Ankylostoma Brazilianze Cats / dogs
Intermediate host: Nill

Life Cycle

Fillarial form larvae penetrate through the skin of man. They travel via circulatory system to reach the heart. From heart to lungs and ascend through trachea where they are swallowed and go through digestive system then develop into adults in the small intestines. After mating the gravid (ripe) females lay eggs (between 24,000- 30,000 a day). Ripe eggs come out together with feaces and hatch into filarial form larvae. If the filarial form larvae penetrate the skin of man, cycle continues.

üîç

Symptoms

  • Larvae migrans or creeping eruption is visible on the skin (produces intense irritation)
  • Ground itch on point of entry (redness, irritation may be ulcerative. If the point attracts pus forming bacteria (likely to form abscess)
  • During migration- cough, sneezing, pneumonia, fever
  • Adult stage- cause abdominal pains, general weakness

  1. Visible damage of the subcutaneous layer of the skin
  2. Supprative sores at point of entry or between toes
  3. Potential haemorrhages in the lungs and possible pneumonia.

ü¶ü Plasmodium (Malaria)

  • Causes: malaria
  • Distribution: cosmopolitan
  • Disease: Malaria

Types of Plasmodium

  1. Plasmodium Vivax
  2. Plasmodium fulciparum
  3. Plasmodium Ovale
  4. Plasmodium Malariae

N/B- P. vivax and p. falciparum are more common causes of malaria than are p. ovale and p. malriae.

Phases of Life Cycle

üß¨

Sexual Phase

Known as sporogony; take place in intestinal tract of the mosquito

üîÑ

Asexual Phase

Known as schizogony; occurs in human host.

Detailed Life Cycle

01
Infection

Sporozite - are infective form of plasmodium found in salivary glands of female anophline mosquitoes. Saliva containing sporozites is infected to man through a mosquito bite. (blood stream through proboscis)

02
Liver Stage

After circulating in blood circulation for about 20-30 minutes, they enter carenchymal cells of the liver- begin to multiply (This stage is referred to as erythrocytic stage). In 10 days multiply small forms called merozoites break out of the liver cells and are released into the circulatory system and seek to enter erythrocytes

03
Erythrocytic Stage

In the erythrocytes (intra- erythrocytic stage) a series of development takes place and form a ring form known as trophozoite and depending on space they enlarge and divide into segments known as schizonts. Individual schizonts are known as merozoites. When mature schizonts rapture from erythrocytes the merozoites are released into the circulation and seek to enter uninfected erythrocytes.

04
Fever Cycle

The period of release of merozoites into circulation accounts for fever (mostly in 2 days) plasmodium vivax (tartian). (3 days) plasmodium falciparum (quartan)

05
Sexual Development

After multiple erythrocytic cycles, some merozoites transform themselves into sexual macrogametocytes (female) and others into microgametocytes (males). If a mosquito bites a human being with malaria gametocytes are ingested with blood meal

06
In Mosquito

In the mosquito stomach microgametocytes develop flagella. Microgametocytes break free and penetrate macrogametocytes and produce a zygote (fertilized). Zygote enter mosquito wall, develop into ookinetes which develop into oocyst and sporozites eventually break out of body cavity and migrate to the salivary glands. When the mosquito bites uninfected man, the sporozoites are squeezed from salivary glands into circulatory system of man and the cycle continues.
