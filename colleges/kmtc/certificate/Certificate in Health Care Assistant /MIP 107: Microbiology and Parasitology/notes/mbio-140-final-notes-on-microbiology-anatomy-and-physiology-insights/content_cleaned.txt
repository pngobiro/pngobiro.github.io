<p>Microorganisms are single-celled microscopic organisms and viruses, which are microscopic but not cellular.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="what-is-microbiology-all-about">What is microbiology all about?<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#what-is-microbiology-all-about" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Microbiology is about cells and how they work, especially the bacteria, a large group of cells of enormous basic and practical importance (Figure 1.1).</li>
</ul>
<p><img src="assets/images/image-20260115-bafcfe70.jpeg" alt="img-3.jpeg"></p>

<ul>
<li>Microbiology is about diversity and evolution, about how different kinds of microorganisms arose and why.</li>
<li>It is about what microorganisms do in the world at large, in soils and waters, in the human body, and in animals and plants.</li>
<li>One way or another, microorganisms affect all other life forms on Earth, and thus we may think of microbiology as the foundation of the biological sciences.</li>
<li>Microorganisms differ from the cells of macroorganisms. The cells of macroorganisms such as plants and animals are unable to live alone in nature and exist only as parts of multicellular structures, such as the organ systems of animals or the leaves of leafy plants.</li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li>By contrast, most microorganisms can carry out their life processes of growth, energy generation, and reproduction independently of other cells.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="1-introduction-to-microbiology">1. INTRODUCTION TO MICROBIOLOGY<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#1-introduction-to-microbiology" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="1-1-microbiology">1.1 Microbiology<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#1-1-microbiology" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<p>The science of microbiology revolves around two themes:</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h4 level="4" id="1-understanding-basic-life-processes-and">(1) Understanding basic life processes, and<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#1-understanding-basic-life-processes-and" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h4></div>
<ul>
<li>As a basic biological science, microbiology uses and develops tools for probing the fundamental processes of life.</li>
<li>Scientists have been able to gain a sophisticated understanding of the chemical and physical basis of life from studies of microorganisms because microbial cells share many characteristics with cells of multicellular organisms; indeed, all cells have much in common.</li>
<li>Moreover, microbial cells can grow to extremely high densities in laboratory culture, making them readily amenable to biochemical and genetic study.</li>
<li>These features make microorganisms excellent models for understanding cellular processes in multicellular organisms, including humans</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h4 level="4" id="2-applying-our-understanding-of-microbiology-for-the-benefit-of-humankind">(2) Applying our understanding of microbiology for the benefit of humankind.<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#2-applying-our-understanding-of-microbiology-for-the-benefit-of-humankind" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h4></div>
<p>As an applied biological science, microbiology deals with many important practical problems in medicine, agriculture, and industry. For example:</p>
<ul>
<li>Most animal and plant diseases are caused by microorganisms.</li>
<li>Microorganisms play major roles as agents of soil fertility and in supporting domestic animal production.</li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li>Many large-scale industrial processes, such as the production of antibiotics and human proteins, rely heavily on microorganisms. Thus both the detrimental and the beneficial aspects of microorganisms affect the everyday lives of humans.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="the-importance-of-microorganisms">The Importance of Microorganisms<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-importance-of-microorganisms" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>In this book we will see that microorganisms play central roles in both human activities and the web of life on Earth.</li>
<li>Although microorganisms are the smallest forms of life, collectively they constitute the largest mass of living material on Earth and carry out many chemical processes necessary for other organisms.</li>
<li>In the absence of microorganisms, other life forms would never have arisen and could not now be sustained. Indeed, the very oxygen we breathe is the result of past microbial activity (Figure 1.b).</li>
<li>Moreover, we will see how humans, plants, and animals are intimately tied to microbial activities for the recycling of key nutrients and for degrading organic matter.</li>
<li>No other life forms are as important as microorganisms for the support and maintenance of life on Earth.</li>
<li>Microorganisms existed on Earth for billions of years before plants and animals appeared, and we will see in later chapters that the diversity of microbial life far exceeds that of the plants and animals.</li>
<li>This huge diversity accounts for some of the spectacular properties of microorganisms. For example, we will see how microorganisms can live in places unsuitable for other organisms and how the diverse physiological capacities of microorganisms rank them as Earth's premier chemists.</li>
</ul>

PATHWAYS OF DISCOVERY IN MICROBIOLOGY<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#1-2-pathways-of-discovery-in-microbiology" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<p>Like any science, microbiology owes much to its past. Although able to claim early roots, the science of microbiology didn't really develop until the nineteenth century. Since that time, the field has exploded and spawned several new but related fields. We retrace these pathways of discovery now.</p>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h1 level="1" id="the-historical-roots-of-microbiology-hooke-van-leeuwenhoek-and-cohn">The Historical Roots of Microbiology: Hooke, van Leeuwenhoek, and Cohn<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-historical-roots-of-microbiology-hooke-van-leeuwenhoek-and-cohn" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h1></div>
<ul>
<li>Although the existence of creatures too small to be seen with the naked eye had long been suspected, their discovery was linked to the invention of the microscope.</li>
<li>Robert Hooke (1635–1703), an English mathematician and natural historian, was also an excellent microscopist. In his famous book Micrographia (1665), the first book devoted to microscopic observations, Hooke illustrated, among many other things, the fruiting structures of molds (Figure 1.9). This was the first known description of microorganisms.</li>
<li>The first person to see bacteria was the Dutch draper and amateur microscope builder Antoni van Leeuwenhoek (1632–1723).</li>
</ul>

<p>In 1684, van Leeuwenhoek, who was aware of the work of Hooke, used extremely simple microscopes of his own construction (Figure 1.10) to examine the microbial content of a variety of natural substances</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Van Leeuwenhoek's microscopes were crude by today's standards, but by careful manipulation and focusing he was able to see bacteria, microorganisms considerably smaller than molds. He discovered bacteria in 1676 while studying pepper–water infusions.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>He reported his observations in a series of letters to the prestigious Royal Society of London, which published them in 1684 in English translation. Drawings of some of van Leeuwenhoek's “wee animalcules,” as he referred to them, are shown in Figure 1.10b.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>As years went by, van Leeuwenhoek's observations were confirmed by others, but progress in understanding the nature and importance of these tiny organisms remained slow for nearly the next 150 years.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Only in the nineteenth century did improved microscopes become widely distributed, and about this time the extent and nature of microbial life forms became more apparent.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>In the mid- to late nineteenth century major advances were made in the new science of microbiology, primarily because of the attention that was given to two major questions that pervaded biology and medicine at the time: (1) does spontaneous generation occur and (2) what is the nature of infectious disease.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Answers to these penetrating questions emerged from the work of two giants in the fledgling field of microbiology: the French chemist Louis Pasteur and the German physician Robert Koch. But before we explore their work, let us briefly consider the groundbreaking work of a German botanist, Ferdinand Cohn, a contemporary of Pasteur and Koch and the founder of the field we now call bacteriology</p>
</li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li>Ferdinand Cohn (1828-1898) was born in Poland. He was trained as a botanist and became an excellent microscopist.</li>
<li>His interests in microscopy naturally led him to the study of unicellular plants—the algae—and later to photosynthetic bacteria.</li>
<li>Cohn believed that all bacteria, even those lacking photosynthetic pigments, were members of the plant kingdom, and his microscopic studies gradually drifted away from plants and algae to bacteria, including the large sulfur bacterium Beggiatoa (Figure 1.11).</li>
<li>Cohn was particularly interested in heat resistance in bacteria, which led him to discover the important group of bacteria that form endospores.</li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li>The mid- to late nineteenth century saw the science of microbiology blossom.</li>
<li>The concept of spontaneous generation was crushed and the science of pure culture microbiology emerged.</li>
<li>Several scientific giants emerged in this era, and the first was the Frenchman Louis Pasteur (1822–1895), a contemporary of Cohn. Pasteur was trained as a chemist</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="fermentations">Fermentations<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#fermentations" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>At the invitation of a local industrialist who was having problems making alcohol by the fermentation of beets, Pasteur began a detailed study of the mechanism of the alcoholic fermentation, at that time thought to be a strictly chemical process.</li>
<li>The yeast cells in the fermenting broth were thought to be a complex chemical substance and a result, rather than a catalyst, of the fermentation.</li>
<li>Microscopic observations and other simple but rigorous experiments convinced Pasteur that the alcoholic fermentation was catalyzed by living yeast cells. Indeed, in Pasteur’s own words: “. . . fermentation is associated with the life and structural integrity of the cells and not with their death and decay.”</li>
<li>From this foundation, Pasteur began a series of classic experiments on spontaneous generation, experiments that are forever linked to his name and to the science of microbiology.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="spontaneous-generation">Spontaneous Generation:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#spontaneous-generation" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>The concept of spontaneous generation had existed since biblical times.</li>
<li>The basic idea of spontaneous generation can easily be understood. For example, if food is allowed to stand for some time, it putrefies.</li>
<li>When the putrefied material is examined microscopically, it is found to be teeming with bacteria and perhaps even higher organisms such as maggots and worms.</li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li>
<p>Where do these organisms that are not apparent in the fresh food come from? Some people said they developed from seeds or germs that entered the food from air. Others said they arose spontaneously from nonliving materials, that is, spontaneous generation. Who was right?</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Keen insight was necessary to solve this controversy, and this was exactly the kind of problem that appealed to Louis Pasteur.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Pasteur was a powerful opponent of spontaneous generation. Following his discoveries about fermentation.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Pasteur showed that microorganisms closely resembling those observed in putrefying materials could be found in air.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Pasteur concluded that the organisms found in putrefying materials originated from microorganisms present in the air and on the surfaces of the containers that held the materials.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>He postulated that cells are constantly being deposited on all objects and that they grow when conditions are favorable. Furthermore, Pasteur reasoned that if food were treated in such a way as to destroy all living organisms contaminating it, that is, if it were rendered sterile and then protected from further contamination, it should not putrefy.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Pasteur used heat to eliminate contaminants. Other workers had shown that when a nutrient solution was sealed in a glass flask and heated to boiling for several minutes, it did not support microbial growth (of course, only if endospores were not present; see discussion of Cohn). Killing all the bacteria or other microorganisms in or on objects is a process we now call sterilization.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Proponents of spontaneous generation criticized such experiments by declaring that "fresh air" was necessary for the phenomenon to occur. Boiling, so they claimed, in</p>

<ul>
<li>In 1864 Pasteur countered this objection simply and brilliantly by constructing a swan-necked flask, now called a Pasteur flask (Figure 1.13).</li>
<li>In such a flask nutrient solutions could be heated to boiling and sterilized. However, after the flask was cooled, air was allowed to reenter, but bends in the neck (the "swan neck" design) prevented particulate matter (containing microorganisms) from entering the main body of the flask and causing putrefaction.</li>
</ul>

In those days, a bite from a rabid animal was akin to a death sentence.</li>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="microbiology">Microbiology:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#microbiology" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="388d2847">The Germ Theory of Disease and Koch’s Postulates:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#388d2847" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>In his early work Koch studied anthrax, a disease of cattle and occasionally of humans.</li>
<li>Anthrax is caused by an endospore-forming bacterium called <em>Bacillus anthracis</em>.</li>
<li>By careful microscopy and by using special stains, Koch established that the bacteria were always present in the blood of an animal that was succumbing to the disease.</li>
<li>However, Koch reasoned that mere association of the bacterium with the disease was not proof that it actually caused the disease. Instead, the bacterium might be a result of the disease. How could cause and effect be linked? With anthrax Koch sensed an opportunity to study cause and effect experimentally, and his results formed the standard by which infectious diseases have been studied ever since.</li>
<li>Koch used mice as experimental animals. Using all of the proper controls, Koch demonstrated that when a small amount of blood from a diseased mouse was injected into a healthy mouse, the latter quickly developed anthrax.</li>
<li>He took blood from this second animal, injected it into another, and again obtained the characteristic disease symptoms.</li>
<li>However, Koch carried this experiment a critically important step further. He discovered that the anthrax bacteria could be grown in nutrient fluids outside the animal body and</li>
</ul>
<p>Downloaded by Patrick Ngobiro (<a href="mailto:pngobiro@gmail.com" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">pngobiro@gmail.com</a>)</p>
<p>that even after many transfers in laboratory culture, the bacteria still caused the disease when inoculated into a healthy animal.</p>
<ul>
<li>On the basis of these and related experiments carried out in his seminal work on the causative agent of tuberculosis, Koch formulated a set of rigorous criteria, now known as <strong>Koch’s postulates</strong>, for definitively linking a specific microorganism to a specific disease:</li>
</ul>
<ol>
<li>The disease-causing organism must always be present in animals suffering from the disease and should not be present in healthy animals.</li>
<li>The organism must be cultivated in a pure culture away from the animal body</li>
<li>The isolated organism must cause the disease when inoculated into a healthy susceptible animal</li>
<li>The organism must be reisolated from these experimental animals and cultured again in the laboratory, after which it should still be the same as the original organism</li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>Koch’s postulates are summarized in Figure 1.15. Koch’s postulates were a monumental step forward in the study of infectious diseases.</li>
<li>The postulates not only offered a means for linking the cause and effect of an infectious disease, but also stressed the importance of laboratory culture of the putative infectious agent. With these postulates as a guide, Koch, his students, and those that followed them discovered the causative agents of most of the important infectious diseases of humans and other animals.</li>
<li>These discoveries led to the development of successful treatments for the prevention and cure of many of these diseases, thereby greatly improving the scientific basis of clinical medicine and human health and welfare (Figure 1.8)</li>
</ul>

<p>Culture: Increase in the population of cells</p>
<p>Generation time: The time cell takes to divide (double) is called</p>
<p>Reproduction: Binary Fission</p>
<ul>
<li>Division exactly in half</li>
<li>Most common means of bacterial reproduction</li>
<li>Forming two equal size progeny</li>
<li>Genetically identical offspring</li>

<p>2.4. Bacterial Culture Growth</p>
<p>Growth of culture goes through four phases with time:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Lag phase:</strong></li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>Organisms are adapting to the environment</li>
<li>Synthesizing DNA, ribosomes and enzymes in order to breakdown nutrients, and to be used for growth</li>
<li>Little or no division</li>
</ul>
<ol start="2">
<li><strong>Log or Logarithmic phase</strong></li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>Division is at a constant rate</li>
</ul>
<ol start="3">
<li><strong>Stationary phase:</strong></li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>Dying and dividing organisms are at an equilibrium</li>
<li>Death is due to reduced nutrients, pH changes, toxic waste and reduced oxygen</li>
<li>Cells are smaller and have fewer ribosomes</li>
</ul>
<ol start="4">
<li><strong>Death or Decline phase</strong></li>
</ol>
<ul>
<li>The population is dying in a geometric fashion so there are more deaths than new cells</li>
<li>Deaths are due to severe reduced nutrients, pH changes, toxic waste and reduced oxygen</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="3-1-main-characteristics-of-fungi">3.1. Main Characteristics of Fungi:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#3-1-main-characteristics-of-fungi" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Eukaryotic cells</li>
<li>Non-photosynthetic (heterotrophic)</li>
<li>Most fungi are multicellular</li>
<li>Most fungi are microscopic molds or yeasts</li>
<li>Fungal cell wall:</li>
<li>The cell walls of Fungi are made of chitin (polysaccharides)</li>
<li>Some fungi have cross walls, or septa, which divide the filaments into compartments having a single nucleus.</li>
<li>Filaments of fungi are called hyphae.</li>
<li>The mycelium is a mat of hyphae visible to the unaided eye (e.g. bread mold)</li>
<li>Some hyphae may divided by cross sections called septa</li>
<li>Some cells lack septa and are multi-nucleated, or coenocytic (have many nuclei)</li>
<li>The Primary Structures of a Fungi:</li>
<li>Spores (haploid reproductive cell)</li>
<li>Hypha (a single filament)</li>
<li>Mycelium (a mass of hyphae)</li>
<li>Fruiting Body (reproductive and dispersion)</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="6-1-viral-characteristics">6.1. Viral Characteristics<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#6-1-viral-characteristics" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Virus" is from the Greek meaning for "poison" and was initially described by Edward Jenner in 1798</li>
<li>Virus is a package of genetic information protected by a protein shell for delivery into a host cell to be expressed and replicated</li>
<li>Nucleic acid (DNA or RNA)</li>
<li>Lack of nuclear membrane and external cell wall</li>
<li>They have very small genomes, produce limited numbers of proteins and do not possess many intracellular systems ie they are parasites &gt; intracellular replication</li>
<li>Viruses – three basic forms:</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="1-complex">1. <ul>
<li>Poxviruses: No capsid DNA surrounded by core membrane</li>
<li>Bacteriophages: Complex capsid head and tail structures</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="viral-structure">Viral structure:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#viral-structure" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<ul>
<li>Capsomeres are structural subunits containing several proteins</li>
<li>Capsomeres aggregate to produce the viral capsid.</li>
<li>The viral capsid associates with the viral nucleic acid to produce a nucleocapsid</li>
<li>Nucleocapsids are usually arranged in one of three ways</li>
<li>Lipid envelopes are derived from cellular membranes</li>

<li>Obligate intracellular parasites</li>

<li>Acellular</li>
<li>No metabolic enzymes</li>
<li>No machinery for protein production</li>
<li>Have instructions for their own reproduction, but not outside of host cell</li>

<p>6.4. Viral Host Range:</p>
<ol>
<li>Tropisms: viral tissue specificities</li>
<li>Restricted: Human liver cells – hepatitis B</li>
<li>Intermediate: Intestinal and nerve cells of primates - poliovirus</li>
<li>Broad: Various cells of all mammals – rabies</li>
</ol>
<p>How do you acquire these viral infections?</p>
<ul>
<li>Direct personal contact: Herpes viruses, HIV, Influenza</li>
<li>Airborne spread: Chicken pox</li>
<li>Parenteral: HIV, Hepatitis B and C, cytomegalovirus (CMV)</li>
<li>Fomites: Enteroviruses and other sturdy drying resistant viruses</li>
<li>Vectors: West Nile</li>
<li>Vertical transmission: HIV, Herpes simplex, cytomegalovirus, rubella (German measles)</li>
<li>Enteral (foodborne): Hepatitis A, gastroenteritis viruses</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="7-1-metabolism">7.1. Metabolism:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#7-1-metabolism" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>The sum of the chemical reactions in an organism</li>
<li>A metabolic pathway is a sequence of enzymatically catalyzed chemical reactions in a cell.</li>
<li>Metabolic pathways are determined by enzymes.</li>
<li>Enzymes are encoded by genes.</li>
</ul>
<ol>
<li>
<p><strong>Catabolism</strong>: The energy-releasing processes. Catabolism provides the building blocks and energy for anabolism</p>

<li>
<p><strong>Anabolism</strong>: The energy-using processes</p>
</li>
</ol>

<ul>
<li>The collision theory states that chemical reactions can occur when atoms, ions, and molecules collide.</li>
<li>Activation energy is needed to disrupt electronic configurations.</li>
<li>Reaction rate is the frequency of collisions with enough energy to bring about a reaction.</li>
</ul>

<li>Most, but not all, microorganisms are <strong>benign</strong>; a few <strong>contribute</strong> directly to our <strong>health</strong>, and even <strong>fewer</strong> pose direct <strong>threats</strong> to <strong>health</strong>.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h3 level="3" id="colonization-by-microorganism">Colonization by Microorganism:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#colonization-by-microorganism" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h3></div>
<ul>
<li>Mammals in <strong>utero</strong> develop in a <strong>sterile environment</strong> and have no exposure to microorganisms.</li>
<li>Starting with the <strong>birth process</strong>, colonization, growth of a microorganism after it has gained access to host tissues, begins as animals are <strong>exposed to microorganisms</strong>.</li>
<li>The <strong>skin surfaces</strong> are readily <strong>colonized</strong> by many species.</li>
<li>Likewise, the <strong>oral cavity</strong> and <strong>gastrointestinal tract</strong> acquire microorganisms through feeding and exposure to the mother's body, which, along with other environmental sources, initiates colonization of the skin, oral cavity, upper respiratory tract, and gastrointestinal tract.</li>
<li>Different populations of microorganisms colonize individuals in different localities and at different times.</li>
<li>Genetic factors also play a role. Thus, the <strong>normal microbial flora</strong> is highly dependent on the <strong>conditions</strong> to which an individual is exposed.</li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li>The normal flora is highly diverse in each individual and may differ significantly between individuals, even in a given population</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="pathogen">Pathogen:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#pathogen" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>A host is an organism that harbors a parasite, another organism that lives on or in the host and causes damage.</li>
<li>Microbial parasites are called pathogens.</li>
<li>The outcome of a host–parasite relationship depends on pathogenicity, the ability of a parasite to inflict damage on the host.</li>
<li>Pathogenicity differs considerably among potential pathogens, as does the resistance or susceptibility of the host to the pathogen.</li>
<li>An opportunistic pathogen causes disease only in the absence of normal host resistance. Pathogenicity varies markedly for individual pathogens. The quantitative measure of pathogenicity is called virulence.</li>
<li>Neither the virulence of the pathogen nor the relative resistance of the host is a constant factor.</li>
<li>The host–parasite interaction is a dynamic relationship between the two organisms, influenced by changing conditions in the pathogen, the host, and the environment.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="infection-and-disease">Infection and Disease:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#infection-and-disease" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Infection refers to any situation in which a microorganism is established and growing in a host, whether or not the host is harmed.</li>
<li>Disease is damage or injury to the host that impairs host function.</li>
<li>Infection is not synonymous with disease because growth of a microorganism on a host does not always cause host damage.</li>
<li>Thus, species of the normal microbial flora have infected the host, but seldom cause disease.</li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li>However, the normal flora sometimes cause disease if <strong>host resistance</strong> is compromised, as happens in diseases such as cancer and acquired immune deficiency syndrome (AIDS).</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="f316c364">Host–Parasite Interactions<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#f316c364" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Animal hosts provide <strong>favorable environments</strong> for the growth of many microorganisms.</li>
<li>They are rich in the organic nutrients and growth factors required by chemoorganotrophs, and provide conditions of controlled pH, osmotic pressure, and temperature.</li>
<li>However, the animal body is <strong>not a uniform</strong> environment.</li>
<li>Each region or <strong>organ</strong> differs <strong>chemically</strong> and <strong>physically</strong> from others and thus provides a selective environment where the growth of certain microorganisms is favored.</li>
<li>For example, the skin, respiratory tract, and gastrointestinal tract provide selective chemical and physical environments that support the growth of a highly diverse microflora.</li>
<li><strong>Animals</strong> also possess <strong>defense mechanisms</strong> that collectively <strong>prevent</strong> or <strong>inhibit</strong> microbial invasion and growth.</li>
<li>The microorganisms that successfully colonize the host have <strong>circumvented</strong> these <strong>defense mechanisms</strong>.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="the-infection-process">The Infection Process:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#the-infection-process" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Infections frequently begin at sites in the animal's <strong>mucous membranes</strong>.</li>
<li><strong>Mucous membranes</strong> consist of single or multiple layers of epithelial cells, tightly packed cells that interface with the external environment.</li>
<li>They are found throughout the body, lining the urogenital, respiratory, and gastrointestinal tracts.</li>
<li>Mucous membranes are frequently coated with a protective layer of viscous soluble glycoproteins called <strong>mucus</strong>.</li>
</ul>

<ul>
<li>Microorganisms that contact host tissues at mucous membranes may associate loosely with the mucosal surface and are usually swept away by physical processes.</li>
<li>Microorganisms may also adhere more strongly to the epithelial surface as a result of specific cell–cell recognition between pathogen and host.</li>
<li>Tissue infection may follow, <strong>breaching</strong> the mucosal barrier and allowing the microorganism to invade deeper into submucosal tissues.</li>
</ul>

<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="11-2-introduction-to-immunology">11.2. Introduction to immunology<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#11-2-introduction-to-immunology" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Immunity: is a biological term that describes a state of having sufficient biological defenses to avoid infection, disease, or other unwanted biological invasion.</li>
<li>Immunity involves both specific and non-specific components.</li>
<li>The non-specific components act either as barriers or as eliminators of wide range of pathogens irrespective of antigenic specificity.</li>
<li>Other components of the immune system adapt themselves to each new disease encountered and are able to generate pathogen-specific immunity.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="1-innate-immunity">1. Innate immunity:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#1-innate-immunity" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Innate immunity or nonspecific, immunity is the natural resistance with which a person is born.</li>
<li>It provides resistance through several physical, chemical, and cellular approaches.</li>
<li>Microbes first encounter the epithelial layers, physical barriers that line our skin and mucous membranes.</li>
<li>Subsequent general defenses include secreted chemical signals (cytokines), antimicrobial substances, fever, and phagocytic activity associated with the inflammatory response.</li>
<li>The phagocytes express cell surface receptors that can bind and respond to common molecular patterns expressed on the surface of invading microbes. Through these approaches, innate immunity can prevent the colonization, entry, and spread of microbes.</li>
</ul>
<div data-testid="stHeadingWithActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-1p9ibxm e1icttdg2"><h2 level="2" id="2-adaptive-immunity">2. Adaptive immunity:<span data-testid="stHeaderActionElements" class="st-emotion-cache-gi0tri e1icttdg3"><a href="#2-adaptive-immunity" class="st-emotion-cache-yinll1 e1icttdg1"><svg xmlns="http://www.w3.org/2000/svg" width="16" height="16" viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"><path d="M15 7h3a5 5 0 0 1 5 5 5 5 0 0 1-5 5h-3m-6 0H6a5 5 0 0 1-5-5 5 5 0 0 1 5-5h3"></path><line x1="8" y1="12" x2="16" y2="12"></line></svg></a></span></h2></div>
<ul>
<li>Adaptive immunity is often sub-divided into two major types depending on how the immunity was introduced:
&gt; Naturally acquired immunity occurs through contact with a disease causing agent, when the contact was not deliberate</li>
</ul>

<p>&gt; Artificially acquired immunity develops only through deliberate actions such as vaccination.</p>
<ul>
<li>Both naturally and artificially acquired immunity can be further subdivided depending on whether immunity is induced in the host or passively transferred from a immune host.</li>
</ul>
<p>&gt; Passive immunity is acquired through transfer of antibodies or activated T-cells from an immune host, and is short lived — usually lasting only a few months</p>
