üèÉ Disaster Response

Disaster response is the sum total of actions taken by people and institutions in the face of disaster. These actions commence with the warning of an oncoming threatening event or with the event itself if it occurs without warning.

The aim of emergency response is to provide immediate assistance to maintain life, improve health, and to support the morale of the affected population. Such assistance may range from providing specific but limited aid, such as assisting refugees with transportation, temporary shelter, and food, to establishing semi-permanent settlement in camps and other locations. It also may involve initial repairs to damaged infrastructure.

Aims of Disaster Response

01

Survival & Health

To ensure the survival of the maximum possible number of victims, keeping them in the best possible health in the circumstances.

02

Re-establish Services

To re-establish self-sufficiency and essential services as quickly as possible for all population groups, with special attention to those whose needs are greatest: the most vulnerable and underprivileged.

03

Repair Infrastructure

To repair or replace damaged infrastructure and regenerate viable economic activities. To do this in a manner that contributes to long term development goals and reduces vulnerability to any future recurrence of potentially damaging hazards.

04

Civil Protection

In situations of civil or international conflict, the aim is to protect and assist the civilian population, in close collaboration with the International Committee of the Red Cross (ICRC) and in compliance with international conventions.

05

Durable Solutions

In cases involving population displacements (due to any type of disaster) the aim is to find durable solutions as quickly as possible, while ensuring protection and assistance as necessary in the meantime.

üìã Disaster Response Activities

The following are typical activities of emergency response:

01
Warning

Information concerning the nature of the danger and imminent disaster threats. Must be rapidly disseminated to government officials, institutions and the population at large.

02
Evacuation and Migration

Relocation of a population from zones at risk to a safer location. Requires timely warning systems, clear escape routes, and established policy.

03
Search and Rescue (SAR)

Identifying the location of disaster victims that may be trapped or isolated and bringing them to safety and medical attention.

04
Post-disaster Assessment

Providing a clear, concise picture of the post-disaster situation to identify relief needs and develop strategies for recovery.

05
Response and Relief

Provision on a humanitarian basis of material aid and emergency medical care necessary to save and preserve human lives.

06
Logistics and Supply

Handling the procurement or receipt, storage, and dispatch of relief supplies for distribution to disaster victims.

07
Communication and Information Management

Essential equipment for information flow (radios, telephones) and the protocol of knowing who communicates what information to whom.

08
Survivor Response and Coping

Taking into account existing social coping mechanisms and new special needs for social services to help adjust to trauma.

09
Security

Protection of human rights and safety of displaced populations and refugees, typically handled by civil defence or police.

10
Emergency Operations Management

Policies and procedures for management requirements established well in advance of the disaster.

11
Rehabilitation

Actions to enable basic services to resume functioning, assist victims' self-help efforts, and facilitate the revival of economic activities.

12
Reconstruction

Permanent construction or replacement of severely damaged physical structures, full restoration of all services, and revitalization of the economy.

üì± Response Methods & Approaches

Modern and Traditional Responses

The responses to disasters may employ a mix of approaches from traditional to modern. The scope of disasters has influenced responses in the following ways:

ü§ù

Humanitarian

Aid to relieve pain and suffering.

üí∏

Remittance

People residing outside the disaster area channel sums of money or goods over long distances to those affected.

üì¶

Relief Assistance

Food, medication, tents, and other essential items.

üåê

Networking

Contacting organizations and establishing connections.

üôã

Volunteerism

Internal and external groups volunteering help. E.g., Community Emergency Response Team (CERT).

üìú

Mutual Aid Agreements

Pre-drawn up agreements to provide resources.

Modern Methods of Disaster Response

New technologies can be very useful and powerful tools in disaster response:

  • Cell phones: Short messages can be sent to recipients warning of imminent threat.
  • Spatial information: Use of satellite imagery, geographic information systems (GIS), remote sensing, and global positioning systems (GPS).
  • Social media and social networking: Text messaging (Twitter) and social networking (Facebook) used as channels of communication. Applications include:
    • Use blogs to rapidly publicize need for assistance.
    • Create geo-tagged photo groups to document damage.
    • Publicize volunteers willing to share recovery-relevant expertise.
    • Use map-based mashups to display relevant local information.
    • Immediately share "lessons learned".

üèóÔ∏è Disaster Recovery

As the emergency is brought under control, the affected population is capable of undertaking a growing number of activities aimed at restoring their lives and the infrastructure that supports them. Ideally, there should be a smooth transition from recovery to on-going development.

‚è±Ô∏è

Short-term Recovery

Immediate and tends to overlap with response. Restoring interrupted utility services, clearing roads, fixing/demolishing buildings, providing food/shelter. May last for weeks.

üìÖ

Long-term Recovery

May continue for a number of months or years. Includes complete redevelopment of damaged areas. Goal is to return to a state better than before (build back better).

The Recovery Plan

The primary purpose of the plan is to spell out the major steps for managing successful recovery. The plan should have at least the following seven steps:

  1. 1
    Gathering basic information
  2. 2
    Organizing recovery
  3. 3
    Mobilizing resources for recovery
  4. 4
    Administering recovery
  5. 5
    Regulating recovery
  6. 6
    Coordinating recovery activities
  7. 7
    Evaluating recovery
üì∏

Documentation Steps

Documenting the effects of the disaster is the best way to request outside aid. Five simple steps:

  1. Take pictures of damages and repairs.
  2. Take notes on damages and repairs (or dictate into tape recorder).
  3. Clip and file newspaper reports and stories.
  4. Record all expenditures carefully and keep all receipts and invoices.
  5. Make sure anyone acting on behalf of the jurisdiction does the same.

üè• Emergency Health Services

During the first few hours/days, priority is treating casualties. Demand for curative care is highest during the acute emergency stage. Thereafter, priority shifts to preventive measures.

Managing Mass Casualty Incidents (MCI)

MCI is any event producing a large number of victims such that the normal capacity of local health services is disrupted. Priorities include:

  • Priority for transportation to hospital based on referrals of priority needs.
  • Priorities for care in the field identified by visible color-coded tags.

Triage

Sorting and prioritizing patients for medical attention according to the degree of injury or illness and expectations for survival. Triage is carried out to reduce the burden on health facilities.

Transportation of Casualties

üöë

Evacuation vehicles assigned for medical purposes must be used exclusively for the latter.

üìã

Report departure time, number/condition of casualties, destination, and route.

üõå

Transport should allow emergency/stabilization measures to continue.

üöó

Driving needs to be smooth and safe to avoid further trauma.

ü¶† Communicable Diseases

Common communicable diseases in disaster situations include:

ü§ß

Contact Transmitted

Acute Respiratory Infections (ARI) like common cold, influenza, bronchitis, diphtheria, pneumonia. Spread through personal contact or overcrowding.

ü¶ü

Vector Transmitted

Malaria, yellow fever, dengue, leptospirosis. Prominent when balance of nature is disturbed.

üíß

Fecal-Oral / Water

Cholera, typhoid fever, diarrhea diseases, leptospirosis. Transmitted through contaminated water/food or poor hygiene.

üí®

Airborne

Tuberculosis, measles, meningococcal meningitis, whooping cough. Transmitted through breathing contaminated air.

‚öïÔ∏è

Sexually Transmitted

HIV/AIDS, gonorrhea, syphilis, Chlamydia. Transmitted through sexual contact.
