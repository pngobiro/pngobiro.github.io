üõ°Ô∏è Phase I: Mitigation

Disaster management is an enormous task. They are not confined to any particular location, neither do they disappear as quickly as they appear. Therefore, it is imperative that there is proper management to optimize efficiency of planning and response. Due to limited resources, collaborative efforts at the governmental, private and community levels are necessary. This level of collaboration requires a coordinated and organized effort to mitigate against, prepare for, respond to, and recover from emergencies and their effects in the shortest possible time.

Key Definitions

üìâ

Mitigation

Reducing or minimizing an impact of a hazard or disaster. It refers to all actions taken before a disaster to reduce its impacts, including preparedness and long-term risk reduction measures.

üìä

Risk Management

Consists of identifying threats (hazards likely to occur), determining their probability of occurrence, estimating what the impact of the threat might be to the community at risk, determining measures that can reduce the risk, and taking action to reduce the threat.

üèòÔ∏è

Vulnerability

A condition wherein human settlements, buildings, agriculture, or human health are exposed to a disaster by virtue of their construction or proximity to hazardous terrain.

Categories of Mitigation Activities

Mitigation activities fall broadly into two categories:

üèóÔ∏è

1. Structural Mitigation

Construction projects which reduce economic and social impacts.

üìú

2. Non-structural Activities

Policies and practices which raise awareness of hazards or encourage developments to reduce the impact of disasters.

Mitigation includes reviewing building codes; vulnerability analysis updates; zoning and land-use management and planning; reviewing of building use regulations and safety codes; and implementing preventative health measures. (World Development Report, 1998)

üõ†Ô∏è Mitigation Strategies

The primary focus of disaster management is to prevent disasters wherever possible or to mitigate those which are inevitable. Four sets of tools that could be used to prevent or mitigate disasters include:

  • i
    Hazard management and vulnerability reduction (Applies exclusively to natural disasters)
  • ii
    Economic diversification (Applies exclusively to natural disasters)
  • iii
    Political intervention and commitment
  • iv
    Public awareness

Specific Measures

üåæ

Adjusting Development

For instance, varieties of crops that are more wind, flood or drought resistant can often be introduced in areas prone to floods, drought and cyclones.

üíπ

Economic Diversification

In areas where the principal or sole source of the income may be threatened, attempts should be made to diversify the economy. Extremely important where economies are dependent on a single cash crop.

üè≠

Disaster Resistant Activities

Developing enterprises less vulnerable to hazards. Example: Situating warehouses in flood plains may be more appropriate than manufacturing plants. Coconut palms could be more suitable than other fruit trees in cyclone-prone coastal areas.

Infrastructure Planning Factors

For most infrastructure projects, natural hazard mitigation should be addressed during the conceptual development of the project. The preliminary design should take into consideration:

  • Situating the facility to avoid flooding, soil erosion, exposure to high winds and unstable soils, and to minimize exposure to storm surge and high waves.
  • Designing the shape of the buildings and structural systems to minimize effects of high winds and earthquake effects, tornados.
  • Using construction materials that are corrosion resistant and of appropriate durability and strength.

üéí Phase II: Preparedness

The goal of emergency preparedness programmes is to achieve a satisfactory level of readiness to respond to any emergency situation through programmes that strengthen the technical and managerial capacity of governments, organizations, and community.

These measures can be described as logistical readiness to deal with disasters and can be enhanced by having response mechanisms and procedures, rehearsals, developing long-term and short-term strategies, public education and building early warning systems.

Preparedness Measures

During the preparedness phase, governments, organizations, and individuals develop plans to save lives, minimize disaster damage, and enhance disaster response operations. Measures include:

  1. 1
    Preparedness plans
  2. 2
    Emergency exercises/training
  3. 3
    Warning systems
  4. 4
    Emergency communications systems
  5. 5
    Evacuations plans and training
  6. 6
    Resource inventories
  7. 7
    Emergency personnel/contact lists
  8. 8
    Mutual aid agreements
  9. 9
    Public information/education

üìâ Disaster Risk Reduction (DRR)

Disaster Risk Reduction (DRR) forms the pillar of disaster preparedness, that is, it forms the action plan to be implemented before, during and after disasters.

The IFRCRCS defines risk reduction as physical measures to reduce the vulnerability and exposure of infrastructure to natural hazards as well and to provide coping and adaptive infrastructure in case of a disaster event.

Factors Increasing Human Vulnerability

  • Rapid urbanization
  • Population growth
  • Lack of knowledge about how to effectively resist the effects of disasters
  • Poverty (perhaps at the root of what makes most people vulnerable)

Examples of DRR Measures

üåä

Flood Mitigation

Proper planning to mitigate flooding in flood prone areas and alternate infrastructure for the provision of food and potable water.

üè†

Shelters

Provision of raised flood shelters as those constructed in Bangladesh.

üö∞

Water Supply

Improvement of water supply systems in rural areas to provide sufficient potable water supply during floods or droughts.

üë©

Women's Role

Enhance community-based disaster preparedness by focusing more on the roles of women.

üì°

Communications

Improve wireless communication that is robust and integrated with both electronic and manual system.

üåΩ

Agriculture

Train farmers to diversify food crops as a strategy to survive in the event of disaster.

üìò The Emergency Operation Plan (EOP)

At the national level, an Emergency Operation Plan (EOP) needs to be established to set out the scope of activities required for community preparedness and response. It must declare what the community can realistically do.

Structure of the EOP

The EOP is specific in its layout as follows:

  1. i
    Statement of Purpose: This is what the Plan seeks to achieve for citizens.
  2. ii
    Situation and assumptions: Statements of the emergency events, actual and potential, warning methods, and peculiar situations.
  3. iii
    Organization and assignment of responsibilities: How the jurisdiction will assign emergency functions.
  4. iv
    Concept of operations: Describes roles and relationships of government agencies and private sector.
  5. v
    Administration and logistics: Management of resources, support requirements, and availability of services.
  6. vi
    Plan development and maintenance: Activities to keep the plan current and reflect changes.
  7. vii
    Authorities and references: Statutes, executive orders, regulations, and formal agreements.
  8. viii
    Definition of terms: Common understanding of terms used in communication, directing and control.

Gender in Planning

It is high time that women take an active role when designing mitigating plans and activities so that appropriate gender issues are mainstreamed. Experts agree that involving women broadens the range of ideas proposed for and incorporated into disaster planning initiatives and results in plans that are more disaster-resilient.

Gender mainstreaming in disaster preparedness, relief and construction include:

  • Mapping of existing forms and sources of gender discrimination in each context.
  • Involving community based women's organizations of marginalized groups in preparedness, relief and reconstruction planning.
  • Pressing for accountability in implementation.

Besides, by offering children the opportunism to participate more fully in disaster situations, we cease to be interpreters of their needs and thoughts, and instead begin to accompany them in the design of actions and adequate strategies that strengthen their capacity to reflect, contribute, and lead their own development processes.
