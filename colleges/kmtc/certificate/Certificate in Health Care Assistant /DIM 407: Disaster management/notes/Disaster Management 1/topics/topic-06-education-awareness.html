üèòÔ∏è Community-Based Initiatives

Every country is at the risk of exposure to some type of disaster, whether natural or man-made. In order for each country to prepare for any kind of disaster, it must inform its citizens about the different types of disasters. The local residents must also be aware of how they can effectively participate in preparing for a disaster, mitigating potential impacts of a disaster and the recovery process after a disaster.

Public awareness in disaster management is a process of educating and empowering the population through sharing knowledge and information about the various types of disasters and their potential risks as widely as possible so that people act appropriately when a disaster happens.

Key Terminologies

üì¢

Public Awareness

The process of transmitting information to the general population to increase their levels of consciousness about disaster risks so they can prepare appropriately to cope with a disaster.

ü§ù

Community-based Approach

A method of education and public awareness in disaster management in which community members are involved in the planning and implementation of the awareness programmes.

üó∫Ô∏è

Hazard Map

A map which shows areas that are vulnerable to particular hazards such as earthquakes, cyclones, flooding, volcanic activity.

Rationale for a Community-Based Approach

Members of a community are the immediate victims of adverse effects of a disaster. They have the best knowledge about their local surrounding in terms of the most disaster-prone areas, the demography of their community and their social and traditional organisation.

It is important that they have the capacity to cope with the impacts of a disaster and are involved in the development of disaster management activities right from the initial planning stages. Community participation can also make them more confident in their capabilities to act in the event of a disaster leading to a self-reliant community.

‚öôÔ∏è Implementation Actions

To oversee and monitor progress, a Community Disaster Management Organization (CDMO) is needed. Key tasks include:

01
Tasking

Setting up appropriate committees (risk communication, health, evacuation, etc.). Assigning roles: Leadership, Management, Administrative, Technical, Financial, Social mobilization.

02
Capacity Building

Imperative that responsible individuals have technical capability. Can be done before or during implementation.

03
Mobilising Resources

Mobilize external partners (government, NGOs, business) to meet needs. Includes human, physical/material, natural and financial resources.

04
Monitoring

Participatory monitoring to track progress on activities, time frames, budgets, indicators. Involves data collection, review meetings, and reporting.

Pros and Cons of Community-Based Approach

Advantages
  • Ownership and Sustainability: Involves people, giving sense of ownership.
  • Addresses Immediate Needs: Targeted specifically at particular community needs.
  • User Friendly: Information presented in familiar language/format.
  • Provides Knowledge/Skills: Empowers people for first 72 hours.
Disadvantages
  • Fear: Reluctance to expose vulnerabilities (fear of losing investors/tourists).
  • Lack of Resources: Improvisation may impact quality.
  • Misleading Information: If not carried out properly, can lead to chaos.
  • Lack of Proper Training: Interpreters/trainers may lack skills.
  • Gender Bias: Tendency to exclude women/youth in creation of tools.

üè´ School-Based Programmes

School-based programmes focus on disaster prevention strategies. Development begins with determining possible disasters in the school area. Schools should document property and assess structures.

Key Components

‚ôø

Special Needs

Identify particular needs of physically/mentally challenged students. Communicate with transport for mobility impairments.

üö®

Drills

Ideally quarterly, minimum each term/semester. Teach appropriate procedures, test plans, identify strengths/weaknesses.

üëÆ

Law Enforcement

Involved in crisis plan development, drills, and review. Core is safety and welfare of students.

üéí

Supplies

Food, water, flashlights, first aid kits, blankets, radio. Keys to access supplies.

üéí

Individual Comfort Kits

Some schools ask students to bring student-assembled "comfort kits" including:

  • A little food
  • Some water
  • Space blanket or large plastic trash bag
  • Non-toxic chemical emergency light stick
  • Letter or photograph from home

üé≠ Culture and Awareness

Culture is the way of life of a people (language, beliefs, behaviour, norms, values). It can be a useful resource or a barrier. Local people know best about their community.

Roles of Different People

  • Indigenous community leaders: Know community well, offer valuable opinions, assist in promotion.
  • Religious leaders: Respected members, community listens to them.
  • Teachers: Transmit information to students who pass it to families.
  • Extended families: Hierarchical structure with different responsibilities.

Important Cultural Considerations

üó£Ô∏è

Language

Use mother tongue. Avoid taboo words. Ensure accurate translations.

üëó

Attire

Agents must dress appropriately/modestly to be accepted.

ü•Å

Medium

Use traditional signals like drums or conch shells.

üåø

Indigenous Knowledge Systems

  • Preventive knowledge: Traditional strategies (e.g., not clearing logs to prevent erosion).
  • Survival resources: Knowledge of edible fruits, nuts, plants, and water sources.
  • Traditional healing: Herbal medicine useful when outside help is delayed.
  • Indigenous sites: Knowledge of sacred sites needing protection.

üì∫ Media and Disaster Management

The media forges a direct link between the public and emergency organizations. It educates, warns, gathers information, alerts officials, and facilitates discussions.

üìª

Electronic Media

Radio: Most popular/widespread. Affordable. Reaches remote areas. Satellite radio works when towers damaged.
Television: Powerful visual impact. Realistic combination of picture and sound.

üì∞

Print Media

Newspaper: Oldest means. Widely circulated.
Magazines/Journals: Target specific audiences (builders, academics).

Impact of Media

Positive Effects
  • First to define event as official disaster.
  • Provides instantaneous, trusted information.
  • Disseminates public safety info (roadways, water safety).
  • Communicates with outside world.
  • Increases lobbying and international donations.
Negative Effects
  • Exaggeration creating unnecessary panic.
  • Inaccurate portrayal (e.g., looting).
  • Manipulation by politicians.
  • Biased coverage for sensationalism.
  • Convergence creating congestion in affected area.
