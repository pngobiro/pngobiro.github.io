üíß Introduction to Fecal-Oral Diseases

Diseases caused by fecal contamination are transmitted through the fecal-oral route, where pathogens in feces from an infected person or animal are introduced into the oral cavity of another person. This typically occurs through contaminated water, food, or poor hygiene practices.

These diseases are particularly common in areas with inadequate sanitation, poor water quality, and limited access to clean water and proper hygiene facilities.

‚ö†Ô∏è Public Health Impact

Fecal-oral diseases are a leading cause of morbidity and mortality, especially among children in developing countries. They contribute significantly to the global burden of diarrheal diseases.

02

ü¶† Cholera

Causative Agent

Vibrio cholerae serogroups O1 and O139

Transmission

  • Ingestion of contaminated water or food
  • Often associated with poor sanitation and overcrowding
  • Can cause epidemics and pandemics

Clinical Features

  • Profuse watery diarrhea: "Rice-water stools"
  • Severe dehydration: Can lead to shock and death if untreated
  • Vomiting: Often accompanies diarrhea
  • Leg cramps: Due to electrolyte loss

Management

  • Rehydration: Oral rehydration solution (ORS) or IV fluids for severe cases
  • Antibiotics: Doxycycline, azithromycin, or ciprofloxacin (reduce duration and severity)
  • Zinc supplementation: Especially important for children
03

ü¶† Giardiasis

Causative Agent

Giardia lamblia (also known as G. intestinalis or G. duodenalis)

Transmission

  • Ingestion of cysts from contaminated water or food
  • Person-to-person transmission (fecal-oral)
  • Common in areas with poor water treatment

Clinical Features

  • Chronic diarrhea: Often greasy, foul-smelling stools
  • Abdominal cramps and bloating
  • Weight loss and malnutrition
  • Fatigue and weakness

Management

  • Metronidazole: Drug of choice (250mg TDS for 5-7 days)
  • Tinidazole: Single dose alternative
  • Albendazole: Alternative treatment
04

ü¶† Amoebiasis

Causative Agent

Entamoeba histolytica

Transmission

  • Ingestion of cysts from contaminated water or food
  • Person-to-person transmission
  • Sexual transmission (anal-oral contact)

Clinical Features

  • Intestinal amoebiasis: Diarrhea (often bloody), abdominal pain, fever
  • Amoebic liver abscess: Right upper quadrant pain, fever, hepatomegaly
  • Asymptomatic carriage: Many infected individuals show no symptoms

Management

  • Metronidazole: 750mg TDS for 5-10 days (tissue amoebicide)
  • Tinidazole: 2g daily for 3 days
  • Paromomycin or Diloxanide furoate: Luminal amoebicide to eliminate cysts
05

ü¶† Bacillary Dysentery (Shigellosis)

Causative Agent

Shigella species (S. dysenteriae, S. flexneri, S. boydii, S. sonnei)

Transmission

  • Fecal-oral route
  • Contaminated food and water
  • Direct person-to-person contact
  • Flies can act as mechanical vectors

Clinical Features

  • Sudden onset: Abdominal cramps, fever, nausea
  • Diarrhea: Frequent small stools with blood and mucus
  • Tenesmus: Painful straining during defecation
  • Dehydration: Can be severe

Management

  • Fluid replacement: ORS or IV fluids
  • Antibiotics: Ciprofloxacin, azithromycin, or ceftriaxone (resistance patterns vary)
  • Zinc: For children under 5 years
06

ü¶† Other Fecal-Oral Diseases

Poliomyelitis

  • Causative agent: Poliovirus (enterovirus)
  • Transmission: Fecal-oral route, rarely respiratory droplets
  • Clinical features: Asymptomatic in most cases; paralytic poliomyelitis causes flaccid paralysis
  • Prevention: Oral polio vaccine (OPV) and inactivated polio vaccine (IPV)

Enteric Fevers (Typhoid and Paratyphoid)

  • Causative agents: Salmonella Typhi (typhoid) and S. Paratyphi (paratyphoid)
  • Transmission: Contaminated food and water, chronic carriers
  • Clinical features: Sustained fever, headache, abdominal pain, rose spots, constipation or diarrhea
  • Management: Ciprofloxacin, ceftriaxone, or azithromycin

Food Poisoning

  • Causative agents: Staphylococcus aureus, Bacillus cereus, Clostridium perfringens, Salmonella, E. coli
  • Transmission: Improperly stored or cooked food
  • Clinical features: Acute onset vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal cramps (usually self-limiting)
  • Management: Supportive care, fluid replacement, antibiotics in severe cases
07

üõ°Ô∏è Prevention and Control

Prevention of fecal-oral diseases requires a comprehensive approach addressing water, sanitation, and hygiene (WASH):

  • Safe water supply: Ensure access to clean, treated drinking water
  • Proper sanitation: Use of improved sanitation facilities (latrines, toilets)
  • Hand hygiene: Regular handwashing with soap, especially after defecation and before eating
  • Food safety: Proper food handling, cooking, and storage
  • Vaccination: Where available (e.g., typhoid, polio, rotavirus)
  • Health education: Community awareness about hygiene practices
  • Carrier identification and treatment: Especially for typhoid
üí° WHO Recommendations

The WHO emphasizes the importance of integrated WASH interventions as the most effective strategy for preventing fecal-oral diseases. This includes access to safe water, adequate sanitation, and promotion of good hygiene practices.
