üêõ Introduction to Helminthic Diseases

Helminthic diseases are caused by parasitic worms (helminths) that infect humans. These include nematodes (roundworms), cestodes (tapeworms), and trematodes (flukes). Helminthic infections are particularly common in tropical and subtropical regions with poor sanitation.

These infections can cause significant morbidity, including malnutrition, anemia, impaired physical and cognitive development, and organ damage.

üìå Global Burden

Soil-transmitted helminths infect approximately 1.5 billion people worldwide, with the highest prevalence in children living in poverty. They contribute significantly to the global burden of neglected tropical diseases.

üêõ Ascariasis

Causative Agent

Ascaris lumbricoides (large roundworm)

Transmission

  • Ingestion of eggs from contaminated soil, food, or water
  • Common in areas with poor sanitation and use of human feces as fertilizer

Clinical Features

  • Larval migration: Cough, wheezing, fever (L√∂ffler's syndrome)
  • Intestinal phase: Abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea
  • Complications: Intestinal obstruction, biliary obstruction, pancreatitis
  • Malnutrition: Especially in children with heavy infections

Management

  • Albendazole: 400mg single dose (drug of choice)
  • Mebendazole: 500mg single dose or 100mg BID for 3 days
  • Surgical intervention: For intestinal obstruction or biliary complications
03

üêõ Enterobiasis (Pinworm)

Causative Agent

Enterobius vermicularis (pinworm or threadworm)

Transmission

  • Ingestion of eggs from contaminated hands, clothing, or bedding
  • Autoinfection (scratching perianal area)
  • Common in children and institutional settings

Clinical Features

  • Perianal itching: Especially at night (most common symptom)
  • Restlessness and sleep disturbance
  • Vaginal itching: In girls (worms may migrate)
  • Secondary bacterial infection: From scratching

Management

  • Mebendazole: 100mg single dose, repeat in 2 weeks
  • Albendazole: 400mg single dose, repeat in 2 weeks
  • Pyrantel pamoate: 11mg/kg single dose
  • Hygiene measures: Daily bathing, change of underwear, handwashing, cleaning of bedding
04

üêõ Trichuriasis (Whipworm)

Causative Agent

Trichuris trichiura (whipworm)

Transmission

  • Ingestion of eggs from contaminated soil, food, or water
  • Associated with poor sanitation and warm, humid climates

Clinical Features

  • Light infections: Often asymptomatic
  • Heavy infections: Abdominal pain, diarrhea, rectal prolapse
  • Growth retardation: In children
  • Anemia: Due to chronic blood loss

Management

  • Mebendazole: 100mg BID for 3 days
  • Albendazole: 400mg daily for 3 days
05

üêõ Taeniasis (Tapeworm Infection)

Causative Agents

  • Taenia saginata (beef tapeworm)
  • Taenia solium (pork tapeworm) - can cause cysticercosis

Transmission

  • Ingestion of undercooked or raw infected meat
  • T. saginata: From beef
  • T. solium: From pork

Clinical Features

  • Often asymptomatic
  • Abdominal discomfort: Nausea, pain, bloating
  • Weight loss: In heavy infections
  • Passage of proglottids: Visible in stool

Cysticercosis (T. solium)

  • Caused by ingestion of T. solium eggs (autoinfection or contaminated food)
  • Larvae form cysts in various tissues (brain, muscle, eye)
  • Neurocysticercosis can cause seizures, headaches, neurological deficits

Management

  • Praziquantel: 5-10mg/kg single dose (drug of choice)
  • Albendazole: Alternative treatment
  • Neurocysticercosis: Requires specialist management (antiparasitic drugs, steroids, anticonvulsants, possible surgery)
