üîÑ Understanding Disease Transmission

Understanding how communicable diseases are transmitted is crucial for implementing effective prevention and control strategies. The mode of transmission refers to the mechanism by which an infectious agent is transferred from a reservoir or source to a susceptible host.

Transmission can occur through various pathways, which are generally classified into two main categories: direct transmission and indirect transmission.

üìå Chain of Infection

Disease transmission involves a chain of six links: infectious agent, reservoir, portal of exit, mode of transmission, portal of entry, and susceptible host. Breaking any link in this chain can prevent disease transmission.

02

ü§ù Direct Transmission

Direct transmission occurs when there is immediate transfer of the infectious agent from a reservoir or source to a susceptible host through direct contact or droplet spread.

Types of Direct Transmission

  1. Direct Contact: Physical contact between an infected person and a susceptible person. This can include touching, kissing, sexual contact, or contact with open wounds. Examples: scabies, HIV through sexual contact, skin infections.
  2. Droplet Infection: Spread through respiratory droplets produced when an infected person coughs, sneezes, or talks. These droplets travel short distances (usually less than 1 meter) and land on the mucous membranes of another person. Examples: influenza, COVID-19, meningococcal meningitis.
  3. Contact with Soil: Direct contact with contaminated soil can transmit certain pathogens. Examples: tetanus (Clostridium tetani), hookworm larvae penetrating the skin.
  4. Inoculation into Skin or Mucosa: Introduction of infectious agents through breaks in the skin or mucous membranes, often via contaminated needles, instruments, or animal bites. Examples: hepatitis B and C through needle sticks, rabies through animal bites.
  5. Trans-placental (Vertical) Transmission: Transmission from mother to fetus across the placenta during pregnancy. Examples: congenital rubella, HIV, syphilis, toxoplasmosis.
03

üîÑ Indirect Transmission

Indirect transmission occurs when there is transfer of an infectious agent from a reservoir to a host by means of an inanimate object or vector.

Types of Indirect Transmission

  1. Vehicle-borne Transmission: Transmission through contaminated inanimate materials (vehicles) such as water, food, blood, or other biological products. Examples: cholera through contaminated water, food poisoning through contaminated food, hepatitis B through blood transfusion.
  2. Vector-borne Transmission: Transmission through living intermediaries (vectors) such as arthropods (insects, ticks, mites) that carry the infectious agent from one host to another. Vectors can be:
    • Mechanical vectors: Simply carry pathogens on their body parts (e.g., houseflies transmitting bacteria from feces to food)
    • Biological vectors: Pathogens multiply or develop within the vector before being transmitted (e.g., mosquitoes in malaria, ticks in Lyme disease)
  3. Fomite-borne Transmission: Transmission through contaminated inanimate objects (fomites) such as doorknobs, bed linens, surgical instruments, toys, or other objects. Examples: MRSA through contaminated hospital equipment, cold viruses through contaminated surfaces.
  4. Unclean Hands and Fingers: A specific type of fomite transmission where hands serve as vehicles for transferring pathogens from contaminated surfaces to mucous membranes or food. This is a major route for gastrointestinal and respiratory infections.
04

üë§ The Susceptible Host

A susceptible host is an individual who lacks immunity or resistance to a particular infectious agent and is therefore at risk of developing disease if exposed.

Factors Affecting Host Susceptibility

  • Immune status: Previous exposure or vaccination can provide immunity
  • Age: Very young and elderly are often more susceptible
  • Nutritional status: Malnutrition increases susceptibility
  • Chronic diseases: Conditions like diabetes, HIV/AIDS increase risk
  • Medications: Immunosuppressive drugs increase susceptibility

Stages of Successful Parasitism

For an infectious agent to successfully infect a host, four stages are required:

  1. Portal of entry: The pathogen must enter the host through a specific route (respiratory tract, gastrointestinal tract, skin, etc.)
  2. Site of election inside the body: The pathogen must reach and establish itself in the appropriate tissue or organ
  3. Portal of exit: The pathogen must have a way to leave the host to infect others
  4. Survival in external environment: The pathogen must survive long enough outside the host to reach a new susceptible host

üìä Disease Severity Measures

Virulence

The degree of pathogenicity; the disease-evoking power of a microorganism in a given host. Numerically expressed as the ratio of the number of cases of overt infection to the total number infected, as determined by immunoassay. When death is the only criterion of severity, this is the case fatality rate.

Case Fatality Rate

The proportion of infected individuals who die of the infection. This is a function of the severity of the infection and is heavily influenced by how many mild cases are not diagnosed. It is calculated as:

Case Fatality Rate = (Number of deaths from disease / Number of cases of disease) √ó 100

Incubation Period

Time from exposure to development of disease. In other words, the time interval between invasion by an infectious agent and the appearance of the first sign or symptom of the disease in question. This period varies depending on the pathogen and can range from hours to years.

Latent Period

The period between exposure and the onset of infectiousness (this may be shorter or longer than the incubation period). During the latent period, a person is infected but not yet capable of transmitting the infection to others.

üí° Important Distinction

Incubation period = time from exposure to symptoms
Latent period = time from exposure to becoming infectious
