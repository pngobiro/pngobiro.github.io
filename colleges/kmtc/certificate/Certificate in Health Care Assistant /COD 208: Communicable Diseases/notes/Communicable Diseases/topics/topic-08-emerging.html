üÜï Understanding Emerging and Re-emerging Diseases

Emerging infectious diseases are those whose incidence in humans has increased in the past two decades or threatens to increase in the near future. Re-emerging diseases are those that have reappeared after a significant decline in incidence.

These diseases pose significant public health challenges due to their unpredictable nature, potential for rapid spread, and often limited understanding of their transmission and treatment.

Factors Contributing to Emergence

  • Human demographics and behavior: Population growth, urbanization, international travel
  • Environmental changes: Deforestation, climate change, ecosystem disruption
  • Microbial adaptation: Antibiotic resistance, genetic mutations
  • Breakdown in public health measures: Inadequate vaccination, sanitation, and surveillance
‚ö†Ô∏è Global Impact

Emerging diseases can rapidly become global threats due to modern travel and trade. The COVID-19 pandemic demonstrated how quickly a novel pathogen can spread worldwide, causing significant morbidity, mortality, and socioeconomic disruption.

ü¶† Ebola Virus Disease

Causative Agent

Ebola virus (family Filoviridae)

Transmission

  • Direct contact with blood, secretions, organs, or other bodily fluids of infected people
  • Contact with contaminated surfaces and materials
  • Handling or consumption of wild animal meat (bushmeat)
  • Sexual transmission from survivors through semen

Clinical Features

  • Incubation period: 2-21 days (average 8-10 days)
  • Early symptoms: Fever, fatigue, muscle pain, headache, sore throat
  • Progression: Vomiting, diarrhea, rash, impaired kidney and liver function, internal and external bleeding
  • Case fatality rate: Average 50%, varies from 25% to 90% in past outbreaks

Management

  • Supportive care: Fluid and electrolyte management, maintaining blood pressure, treating secondary infections
  • Monoclonal antibodies: Inmazeb, Ebanga (approved treatments)
  • Infection prevention: Strict isolation, PPE, safe burial practices
  • Vaccine: rVSV-ZEBOV vaccine for prevention during outbreaks

ü¶ü Chikungunya

Causative Agent

Chikungunya virus (alphavirus)

Transmission

  • Primarily through bite of infected Aedes aegypti and Aedes albopictus mosquitoes
  • These mosquitoes bite mainly during daylight hours

Clinical Features

  • Incubation period: 3-7 days
  • Sudden onset fever: Often >39¬∞C
  • Severe joint pain: Typically in hands and feet (name means "that which bends up")
  • Other symptoms: Headache, muscle pain, joint swelling, rash
  • Chronic arthritis: Can persist for months or years in some patients

Management

  • Supportive care: Rest, fluids, antipyretics for fever
  • Pain management: NSAIDs, analgesics for joint pain
  • Vector control: Eliminate mosquito breeding sites, use repellents

ü¶ü Zika Virus Disease

Causative Agent

Zika virus (flavivirus)

Transmission

  • Primarily through bite of infected Aedes mosquitoes
  • Mother to child during pregnancy (congenital Zika syndrome)
  • Sexual transmission
  • Blood transfusion

Clinical Features

  • Most infections: Asymptomatic or mild
  • Symptoms: Fever, rash, headache, joint pain, conjunctivitis (red eyes)
  • Duration: Usually 2-7 days
  • Complications: Guillain-Barr√© syndrome, microcephaly and other congenital abnormalities

Management

  • Supportive care: Rest, fluids, antipyretics, analgesics
  • Avoid NSAIDs: Until dengue is ruled out (risk of bleeding)
  • Pregnant women: Special monitoring and counseling
  • Prevention: Mosquito control, safe sex practices, avoid travel to endemic areas during pregnancy

ü¶† Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome (SARS)

Causative Agent

SARS-associated coronavirus (SARS-CoV)

Transmission

  • Respiratory droplets from infected persons
  • Direct contact with contaminated surfaces
  • Aerosol transmission in certain settings
  • Zoonotic origin (likely from bats via civet cats)

Clinical Features

  • Incubation period: 2-7 days
  • Early symptoms: High fever, headache, body aches, malaise
  • Respiratory symptoms: Dry cough, dyspnea, pneumonia
  • Progression: Can lead to respiratory failure and death

Management

  • Isolation: Strict infection control measures
  • Supportive care: Oxygen, ventilatory support if needed
  • Antiviral therapy: Various agents tried (no proven specific treatment)
üìå Historical Note

The 2002-2003 SARS outbreak resulted in over 8,000 cases and 774 deaths worldwide. The outbreak was contained through public health measures, and no cases have been reported since 2004.
