üêæ Introduction to Zoonotic Diseases

Zoonotic diseases are infections that are transmissible under natural conditions from vertebrate animals to humans. These diseases can be caused by bacteria, viruses, parasites, or fungi and represent a significant public health challenge worldwide.

Approximately 60% of known infectious diseases and up to 75% of new or emerging infectious diseases in humans are zoonotic in origin.

üìå Transmission Routes

Zoonotic diseases can be transmitted through direct contact with animals, indirect contact (through contaminated environments), vector-borne transmission, or consumption of contaminated food products.

02

üêï Rabies

Causative Agent

Rabies virus (Lyssavirus)

Transmission

  • Bite or scratch from infected animal (dogs, cats, bats, foxes, etc.)
  • Saliva entering broken skin or mucous membranes

Clinical Features

  • Incubation period: Usually 1-3 months, but can range from days to years
  • Prodromal phase: Fever, headache, malaise, pain or paresthesia at bite site
  • Neurologic phase: Agitation, confusion, hallucinations, hydrophobia (fear of water), aerophobia (fear of air drafts)
  • Coma and death: Once symptoms appear, rabies is almost invariably fatal

Management

  • Post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP): Must be started immediately after exposure
  • Wound care: Thorough washing with soap and water for at least 15 minutes
  • Rabies immunoglobulin (RIG): Infiltrated around wound (for category III exposures)
  • Rabies vaccine: 4 doses on days 0, 3, 7, and 14
‚ö†Ô∏è Critical

Rabies is 100% fatal once clinical symptoms appear. Immediate post-exposure prophylaxis is life-saving. Never delay seeking medical attention after a potential rabies exposure.

03

ü¶† Tetanus

Causative Agent

Clostridium tetani (anaerobic spore-forming bacterium)

Transmission

  • Introduction of spores into wounds or breaks in skin
  • Common in contaminated soil, dust, or animal feces

Clinical Features

  • Incubation period: 3-21 days (average 10 days)
  • Generalized tetanus: Lockjaw (trismus), risus sardonicus, muscle spasms, opisthotonos
  • Autonomic dysfunction: Hypertension, tachycardia, arrhythmias
  • Respiratory failure: Leading cause of death

Management

  • Wound debridement: Remove necrotic tissue and foreign material
  • Human tetanus immunoglobulin (HTIG): Neutralizes circulating toxin
  • Metronidazole or penicillin: To prevent further toxin production
  • Muscle relaxants and sedatives: Diazepam, baclofen for spasms
  • Supportive care: Airway management, ventilation if needed
04

ü¶† Anthrax

Causative Agent

Bacillus anthracis (spore-forming bacterium)

Transmission

  • Contact with infected animals or contaminated animal products
  • Inhalation of spores (pulmonary anthrax)
  • Ingestion of contaminated meat (gastrointestinal anthrax)

Clinical Forms

  • Cutaneous anthrax: Painless papule that develops into vesicle, then eschar (black scab) with edema
  • Inhalation anthrax: Fever, malaise, respiratory distress, shock (high mortality)
  • Gastrointestinal anthrax: Abdominal pain, vomiting, bloody diarrhea, sepsis

Management

  • Antibiotics: Ciprofloxacin, doxycycline, or penicillin (depending on severity and form)
  • Supportive care: Fluid management, vasopressors if needed
  • Post-exposure prophylaxis: For exposed individuals
