Basic Definitions and Concepts

01

üìñ Core Definitions

Understanding the fundamental terminology is essential for studying communicable diseases and epidemiology.

Infection

Infection is the entry and development or multiplication of an infectious agent in the body of man or animals. An infection does not always cause illness. A person can be infected without showing clinical signs or symptoms.

Contamination

The presence of an infectious agent on a body surface, on or in clothes, beddings, surgical instruments or dressings, or other articles or substances including water and food. Contamination does not necessarily mean infection has occurred.

Infestation

It is the lodgment, development and reproduction of arthropods on the surface of the body or in the clothing, e.g. lice, itch mite. This term could also be used to describe the invasion of the gut by parasitic worms, e.g. Ascariasis.

Contagious Disease

A disease that is transmitted through contact. Examples include scabies, trachoma, sexually transmitted diseases (STDs) and leprosy.

02

üîó Host, Vector, and Reservoir

Host

A person or an animal that affords subsistence or lodgement to an infectious agent under natural conditions. An animal or an organism, or human being that harbours an infectious agent or disease.

Vector

An insect or any living carrier that transports an infectious agent from an infected individual or its wastes to a susceptible individual or its food or immediate surroundings. Vectors are crucial in the transmission of many diseases like malaria, dengue, and filariasis.

Reservoir

Any person, animal, arthropod, plant, soil, or substance, or a combination of these, in which an infectious agent normally lives and multiplies, on which it depends primarily for survival, and where it reproduces itself in such a manner that it can be transmitted to a susceptible host. It is the natural habitat of the infectious agent.

üìå Key Point

The reservoir is where the infectious agent lives and multiplies naturally, while the source is the immediate origin from which infection is transmitted to the host.

03

üè† Types of Reservoirs

Reservoirs can be classified into three main categories:

  1. Human reservoir: Humans themselves serve as reservoirs for many infectious diseases (e.g., measles, HIV, tuberculosis). This includes cases, carriers, and those with subclinical infections.
  2. Animal reservoir: Animals serve as reservoirs for zoonotic diseases (e.g., rabies in dogs, plague in rodents, avian influenza in birds). These diseases can be transmitted from animals to humans.
  3. Non-living reservoir: Inanimate objects or substances can serve as reservoirs (e.g., soil for tetanus and Clostridium botulinum, water for Vibrio cholerae, contaminated food for various pathogens).
04

üë§ Cases and Carriers

Case

A case is defined as "a person in the population or study group identified as having the particular disease, health disorder, or condition under investigation." Cases can be clinical (showing symptoms) or subclinical (asymptomatic).

Carriers

An infected person or animal that harbors a specific infectious agent in the absence of discernible (visible) clinical disease and serves as a potential source of infection to others. Carriers play a crucial role in disease transmission as they can spread infection unknowingly.

‚ö†Ô∏è Important

Carriers are particularly dangerous in disease control because they appear healthy and continue their normal activities while spreading the infection. Examples include typhoid carriers and chronic hepatitis B carriers.

üìä Disease Frequency Measures

Incidence

The number of new cases in a given time period expressed as percent infected per year (cumulative incidence) or number per person time of observation (incidence density). Incidence measures the rate at which new cases occur in a population.

Prevalence

The number of cases at a given time expressed as a percent at a given time. Prevalence is a product of incidence √ó duration of disease. It is of little interest if an infectious disease is of short duration (i.e., measles), but may be of interest if an infectious disease is of long duration (i.e., chronic hepatitis B).

üí° Remember

Incidence = New cases (measures risk/rate of new infections)
Prevalence = Total cases (measures burden of disease at a point in time)

06

üåç Epidemiological Patterns

Epidemic

The unusual occurrence in a community of disease, specific health related behavior, or other health related events clearly in excess of expected occurrence. An epidemic indicates a sudden increase in cases above the expected level.

Endemic

It refers to the constant presence of a disease or infectious agent within a given geographic area or population group. It is the usual or expected frequency of disease within a population. Endemic diseases are always present in a community at a relatively stable level.

Pandemic

An epidemic usually affecting a large proportion of the population, occurring over a wide geographic area such as a section of a nation, the entire nation, a continent or the world. Examples include influenza pandemics and COVID-19.

Exotic Disease

Diseases which are imported into a country in which they do not otherwise occur. For example, rabies in the UK is considered an exotic disease as it is not endemic there.

Sporadic

The word sporadic means "scattered about." The cases occur irregularly, haphazardly from time to time, and generally infrequently. The cases are few and separated widely in time and place that they show no or little connection with each other, nor a recognizable common source of infection. Examples include polio, meningococcal meningitis, and tetanus.

07

üêæ Animal-Related Terms

Zoonosis

An infection that is transmissible under natural conditions from vertebrate animals to man. Examples include rabies, plague, bovine tuberculosis, and avian influenza.

Epizootic

An outbreak (epidemic) of disease in an animal population. For example, Rift Valley fever outbreaks in animals.

Enzootic

An endemic occurring in animals. For example, bovine TB is enzootic in certain cattle populations.

08

üè• Special Infection Types

Nosocomial Infections

Hospital-acquired infections originating in a patient while in a hospital or another health care facility. It has to be a new disorder unrelated to the patient's primary condition. Examples include infection of surgical wounds, hepatitis B, and urinary tract infections acquired in healthcare settings.

Opportunistic Infections

Infection by organisms that take the opportunity provided by a defect in host defense (e.g., immunity) to infect the host and thus cause disease. Opportunistic infections are very common in AIDS patients. Organisms include Herpes simplex, cytomegalovirus, and Pneumocystis jirovecii.

09

‚úÖ Disease Control Concepts

Eradication

Termination of all transmission of infection by the extermination of the infectious agent through surveillance and containment. Eradication is an absolute process, an "all or none" phenomenon, restricted to termination of infection from the whole world. Smallpox is the only human disease that has been eradicated.

Elimination

Sometimes used to describe eradication of a disease from a large geographic region. Diseases which are amenable to elimination in the meantime include polio, measles, and diphtheria. While not yet eradicated globally, these diseases have been eliminated from many regions.

üìå Distinction

Eradication = Global elimination (no cases anywhere in the world)
Elimination = Regional elimination (no cases in a specific geographic area)
