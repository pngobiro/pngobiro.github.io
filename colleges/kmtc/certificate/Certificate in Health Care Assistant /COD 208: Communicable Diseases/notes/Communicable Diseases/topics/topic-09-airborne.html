üí® Introduction to Airborne Diseases

Airborne diseases are caused by pathogenic microorganisms that are transmitted through the air. These pathogens can be spread through droplets, dust, or aerosolized particles and can remain suspended in the air for extended periods.

Airborne transmission is particularly concerning because it can occur over long distances and through indirect contact with contaminated surfaces or air. Effective control requires proper ventilation, air filtration, and respiratory protection.

üìå Transmission Routes

Airborne diseases spread through droplet transmission (large droplets that settle quickly) and aerosol transmission (smaller particles that remain airborne longer). Understanding these differences is crucial for implementing appropriate infection control measures.

02

ü¶† Influenza (The Flu)

Causative Agent

Influenza viruses (types A, B, C, and D)

Transmission

  • Respiratory droplets from coughing, sneezing, or talking
  • Direct contact with contaminated surfaces
  • Aerosol transmission in certain circumstances

Clinical Features

  • Incubation period: 1-4 days
  • Sudden onset: Fever, chills, headache, myalgia, fatigue
  • Respiratory symptoms: Cough, sore throat, runny nose, nasal congestion
  • Gastrointestinal symptoms: More common in children
  • Complications: Pneumonia, bronchitis, sinus infections, exacerbation of chronic conditions

Management

  • Supportive care: Rest, fluids, antipyretics, analgesics
  • Antivirals: Oseltamivir (Tamiflu), zanamivir, baloxavir marboxil (most effective when started within 48 hours)
  • Antibiotics: Only for bacterial complications

Prevention

  • Annual vaccination: Recommended for everyone 6 months and older
  • Hand hygiene: Regular handwashing with soap
  • Respiratory etiquette: Cover coughs and sneezes
  • Stay home when sick: Prevent spread to others

ü¶† Mumps

Causative Agent

Mumps virus (paramyxovirus)

Transmission

  • Respiratory droplets and saliva
  • Direct contact with infected saliva
  • Contaminated surfaces (fomites)

Clinical Features

  • Incubation period: 12-25 days
  • Prodromal symptoms: Fever, headache, malaise, myalgia, anorexia
  • Parotitis: Painful swelling of one or both parotid glands (most characteristic sign)
  • Complications: Orchitis (testicular inflammation), oophoritis, meningitis, encephalitis, pancreatitis, deafness

Management

  • Supportive care: Rest, fluids, soft foods, cold or warm compresses for swollen glands
  • Analgesics and antipyretics: For pain and fever
  • Isolation: For 5 days after parotitis onset

Prevention

  • MMR vaccine: Two doses of measles-mumps-rubella vaccine
  • First dose: 12-15 months
  • Second dose: 4-6 years
04

ü¶† Rubella (German Measles)

Causative Agent

Rubella virus (togavirus)

Transmission

  • Respiratory droplets
  • Direct contact with respiratory secretions
  • Congenital transmission from mother to fetus

Clinical Features

  • Incubation period: 14-21 days
  • Mild illness in children: Low-grade fever, malaise, lymphadenopathy
  • Rash: Pink maculopapular rash starting on face and spreading to trunk and extremities
  • Forchheimer's sign: Petechial lesions on soft palate
  • Adults: May have arthralgia and arthritis

Congenital Rubella Syndrome (CRS)

  • Most severe when infection occurs in first trimester
  • Manifestations: Deafness, cataracts, congenital heart defects, intellectual disability, growth retardation

Management

  • Supportive care: Rest, fluids, antipyretics
  • Isolation: For 7 days after rash onset
  • Pregnant women: IgG and IgM testing if exposed

Prevention

  • MMR vaccine: Two doses
  • Rubella immunity screening: For women of childbearing age
