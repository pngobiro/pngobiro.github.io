Vector-Borne Diseases: Malaria

01

ü¶ü Definition and Overview

Malaria is an acute febrile illness caused by protozoa called Plasmodium. It is one of the most significant vector-borne diseases globally, causing substantial morbidity and mortality, particularly in tropical and subtropical regions.

Key Characteristics

  • Characteristic feature: Intermittent fever which alternates with chills
  • Causative organism: Main cause is Plasmodium falciparum. Other species include P. malariae, P. vivax, and P. ovale
  • Vector: Female Anopheles mosquito, which requires humid climate, warm temperature, and suitable breeding places
02

ü¶ü Anopheles Species

There are three main Anopheles species in Africa that transmit malaria:

  1. Anopheles gambiae: The most important vector in Africa. Feeds in temporary water bodies such as pools
  2. Anopheles funestus: Breeds in permanent vegetation such as swamps and rice fields
  3. Anopheles melas: Adapted to slightly salty water (brackish water)
üìå Important Note

Development of mosquitoes from eggs to larvae to adult is temperature dependent. Warmer temperatures accelerate development, while cooler temperatures slow it down.

03

‚ö†Ô∏è Risk Groups

The people at highest risk from malaria include:

  • Children under 5 years of age
  • Travelers from non-malaria areas
  • People with sickle cell disease
  • Women in their first pregnancy (malaria thrives in placenta)
  • Splenectomised patients (those who have had their spleen removed)
04

üîÑ Transmission and Life Cycle

Malaria is transmitted by the female Anopheles mosquito, which requires human blood for the development of its eggs. The parasite has two cycles of development: a sexual cycle in the mosquito and an asexual cycle in human beings.

In Human Beings (Asexual Cycle)

  1. Sporozoites: Injected into host via mosquito proboscis. They stay in circulation for 30 minutes then migrate to the liver
  2. Hepatic (liver) stage: Takes 9-14 days. Sporozoites mature into hepatic schizonts, which multiply into merozoites released into general circulation
  3. Hypnozoites: Occurs only in P. ovale and P. vivax. Can mature unpredictably and cause recurrent infections up to 18 months after primary infection
  4. Erythrocytic (blood) stage: Merozoites invade red blood cells, develop into trophozoites and blood schizonts, which rupture releasing more merozoites
  5. Gametocytes: Some merozoites develop into sexual forms (male and female gametocytes)

In Mosquitoes (Sexual Reproduction)

  1. Sexual reproduction: Gametocytes develop into gametes, fuse in stomach to form zygote, which develops into ookinete
  2. Oocyst: Ookinete migrates through stomach wall to form oocyst, which matures and releases sporozoites
  3. Sporozoites: Migrate into salivary gland ready for delivery with mosquito's next meal
05

üè• Clinical Picture

Sporozoites and liver stages do not cause clinical symptoms. Symptoms develop when invaded red blood cells rupture to release new merozoites and vasoactive peptides, which activate the immune system.

Signs and Symptoms

Symptoms

  • Fever and chills
  • General malaise
  • Joint pains
  • Backache
  • Nausea, vomiting, and diarrhoea
  • Headache and dizziness

Signs

  • High temperature
  • Rigors (sudden feeling of cold with temperature rise)
  • Splenomegaly (enlarged spleen)
  • Jaundice
  • Convulsions
  • Anemia

Stages of Malarial Fever

A typical attack has three stages:

  1. Cold stage: Temperature rises, patient shivers (1-2 hours). Infected RBCs burst releasing merozoites
  2. Hot stage: High temperature, dry hot skin, severe headache, nausea and vomiting (3-4 hours)
  3. Sweating stage: Temperature falls rapidly, patient sweats profusely (2-4 hours). Patient is relieved but exhausted
06

üî¨ Diagnosis

Clinical Diagnosis

Based on patient's symptoms and physical findings. However, symptoms are often not specific and are also found in other diseases like flu and viral infections.

Microscopic Diagnosis

Malarial parasites can be identified by examining a drop of the patient's blood under a microscope, spread out as a "blood smear" on a microscope slide. This is the gold standard for malaria diagnosis.

üìå Important

Parasitological diagnosis (microscopic examination or rapid diagnostic tests) is recommended for all suspected malaria cases to confirm diagnosis and guide appropriate treatment.

07

üíä Management

Uncomplicated Malaria

  • First-line treatment: Artemisin-based combination therapy (ACT) - artemether (20mg) and lumefantrine (120mg). 6-dose regimen over 3 days
  • Supportive treatment: Paracetamol or ibuprofen for hyperpyrexia
  • Hydration and nutrition: Small quantities of fluids and food at infrequent intervals until normal appetite returns
  • Counselling: All patients should be counselled on importance of completing the full dose

Severe/Complicated Malaria

Malaria is considered complicated if there is slide-confirmed P. falciparum with any of:

  • Hyperparasitemia > 5%
  • Severe anemia < 5g/dl
  • Hypoglycemia < 2.2 mmol/litre
  • Cerebral dysfunction (confusion to coma)
  • Convulsions
  • Concurrent infections (pneumonia, septicemia)
  • Major organ failure (renal, hepatic, heart)
  • Pulmonary edema

Treatment of Severe Malaria

  • Treatment of choice: Parenteral quinine (IV infusion, NOT bolus injection)
  • Alternative (if quinine unavailable): Artesunate (IM), Artemether (IM), or Rectal artesunate

08

üõ°Ô∏è Prevention and Control

Effective malaria prevention and control requires a multi-faceted approach:

  • Environmental management: Clearing bushes around homes, draining stagnant water and ditches
  • Vector control: Use of insecticide-treated mosquito nets (ITNs) and indoor residual spraying
  • Chemoprophylaxis: For travelers and those living in endemic areas
  • Case finding and treatment: Prompt diagnosis and treatment of cases
  • Intermittent preventive therapy: Sulfadoxine-pyrimethamine (SP) recommended for all pregnant women
üí° Key Strategy

The WHO recommends a comprehensive approach combining vector control, chemoprevention, prompt diagnosis, and effective treatment for sustainable malaria control and elimination.
