Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions (40 Marks)

Note: Questions 8 through 21 are missing from the source document provided. The sequence jumps from Q7 to Q22 due to missing pages in the source PDF.

1.
D'Espine classified diseases according to their
a) System
b) Severity
c) Nature
d) Topography
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Nature

Explanation: Marc d'Espine (Geneva) classified diseases according to their nature (e.g., gouty, herpetic), while William Farr (London) classified them by anatomical site.

2.
In ICD 10, dagger and asterisk stand for
a) NEC and NOC
b) Round and square brackets
c) Etiology and manifestations
d) Dual classification
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Dual classification (and c)

Explanation: The system is called "Dual classification". The symbols represent etiology (dagger) and manifestation (asterisk).

3.
External causes of morbidity and mortality means
a) The disease has been caused by natural factors
b) The disease has been caused by external factor
c) The code number should be found in section one of the alphabetical index
d) The disease has been classified in vital statistics
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) The disease has been caused by external factor

Explanation: This refers to environmental events, accidents, and circumstances causing injury.

4.
ICPM has how many categories
a) 21 categories
b) 17 categories
c) 2 categories
d) 9 categories
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) 9 categories

Explanation: The International Classification of Procedures in Medicine (ICPM) published in 1978 had 9 chapters/categories.

5.
Which of the following is not a section of the alphabetical index
a) Alphabetical index to disease and nature of injury
b) Glossary for chapter 5 of ICD 10
c) External cause of injury
d) Table of drugs and chemicals
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) Glossary for chapter 5 of ICD 10

Explanation: The Alphabetical Index (Volume 3) contains three sections (I, II, and III). The glossary is found in the notes of Chapter V in Volume 1 (or as a separate booklet), not in the Index volume.

6.
Which of the following convention is used for referring to previous notes:-
a) Square brackets
b) Parenthesis
c) Brace
d) Colon
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Colon

Explanation: A colon is used in inclusion and exclusion lists when the words that precede it are not complete terms; it effectively refers the user to the list of modifiers below.

7.
-----------------implies unspecified, unqualified
a) NOC
b) Brace
c) Parenthesis
d) NEC
Show Answer

Correct Answer: [NOS] (Option A "NOC" is likely a typo for NOS)

Explanation: NOS (Not Otherwise Specified) implies "unspecified" or "unqualified". NEC (Not Elsewhere Classified) implies "other specified".

22.
Which of the following can not be used as a lead term
a) Name of disease
b) Condition
c) Site
d) All of the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Site

Explanation: In the Alphabetical Index, you typically search by the pathological condition or disease name (Lead term), not the anatomical site. The site is usually a secondary modifier.

23.
Who was the first medical statistician
a) William Cullen
b) William Far
c) John Grant
d) Francois Bossier de Lacrose
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) William Far

Explanation: William Farr (often spelled Far in these papers) is widely regarded as the first medical statistician and a founder of modern epidemiology.

24.
The first character of the ICD code is a letter and each letter is associated with a particular chapter EXCEPT
a) T and R
b) D and H
c) E and F
d) K and L
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) D and H

Explanation: - D is shared between Chapter II (Neoplasms) and Chapter III (Blood). - H is shared between Chapter VII (Eye) and Chapter VIII (Ear). - R, T, E, F, K, L are uniquely assigned to single chapters in standard ICD-10.

25.
In ICD 10 which chapter uses glossary description in addition to inclusion and exclusion terms
a) Chapter II
b) Chapter XIX
c) Chapter XX
d) Chapter V
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Chapter V

Explanation: Chapter V (Mental and behavioural disorders) includes a glossary of definitions because mental disorders can be subjective and vary in description.

a) Surgical procedures

c) Procedures for medical diagnosis

3.
Basic principles of disease classification (6 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Classification should be based on a consistent axis (e.g., etiology or anatomy).
  2. Categories should be mutually exclusive.
  3. The system must be exhaustive (cover all conditions).
  4. Allow for statistical aggregation.
  5. Flexibility for future expansion.
  6. Suitability for the specific purpose (e.g., mortality vs morbidity).
4.
Explain the importance and purpose of death registration and certification (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  • Legal: Provides proof of death for burial, inheritance, insurance, and re-marriage.
  • Statistical: Source of mortality data to identify leading causes of death.
  • Public Health: Surveillance of disease outbreaks and health trends.
  • Demographic: Calculation of population statistics and life expectancy.
5.
List six patients particulars to be indexed (6 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Name
  2. IP/OP Number
  3. Age/DOB
  4. Sex
  5. Diagnosis
  6. Operation performed
  7. Result of treatment
  8. Date of Admission/Discharge
6.
Discuss five importance of coded medical data (10 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Data Retrieval: Makes finding records for research and analysis efficient.
  2. Standardization: Allows comparison of data between different hospitals, regions, and countries.
  3. Reimbursement: Essential for billing insurance and costing health services (DRGs).
  4. Resource Allocation: Helps in planning hospital resources based on disease burden.
  5. Epidemiology: Critical for monitoring disease trends and public health planning.
7.
What is nomenclature classification? (2 Marks)
Show Answer

A nomenclature is a systematic listing of proper names for diseases, operations, and procedures (e.g., SNOMED), intended to provide a unique name for every distinct condition, unlike a classification which groups them.

Section 3: Short Answer Questions (20 Marks)

1.
Explain with practical example procedures involved in translation of disease into code numbers (20 Marks)
Show Answer

Steps for Coding a Diagnosis:

  1. Analyze the diagnosis: Read the medical record (discharge summary) to identify the main condition.
    Example: Acute Appendicitis.
  2. Identify the Lead Term: Find the key word (the pathological condition).
    Example: "Appendicitis".
  3. Locate in Alphabetical Index (Vol 3): Look up the lead term.
    Example: Search "Appendicitis".
  4. Check for Modifiers: Look for essential modifiers (sub-terms) indented below the lead term.
    Example: Look for "Acute".
  5. Follow Cross-References: If a "see" or "see also" note is present, follow it.
  6. Select Tentative Code: Note the code provided in the index.
    Example: K35.8.
  7. Verify in Tabular List (Vol 1): Locate the code in the main book to ensure it is correct.
    Example: Go to Chapter XI, Block K35.
  8. Check Conventions: Read inclusion/exclusion notes, and checking for 4th/5th character requirements.
    Example: Check exclusions under K35.
  9. Assign Final Code: Assign the verified code.
    Example: K35.8 Acute appendicitis, other and unspecified.
