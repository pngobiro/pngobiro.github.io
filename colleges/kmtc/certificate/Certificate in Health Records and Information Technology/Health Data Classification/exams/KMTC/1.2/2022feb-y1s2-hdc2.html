19.
Upon request in 1853 ................... Presented his work in 1855 in which he grouped diseases in one way that is according to their nature?
A. William Farr
B. Marc De Espine
C. John Grant
D. John Snow
Show Answer

Correct Answer: A. William Farr

Explanation: William Farr and Marc d'Espine were tasked by the first International Statistical Congress in 1853 to prepare a uniform nomenclature of causes of death. In 1855, Farr submitted a classification based on five groups (Epidemic diseases, Constitutional (general) diseases, Local diseases arranged by anatomical site, Developmental diseases, and diseases that are the direct result of violence/Injuries).

A. 3
B. 10
C. 6
D. 9

Correct Answer: A. 3

Section 2: Short Answer Questions (30 Marks)

1.
State any 5 requirements for indexing of health conditions. (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Accuracy: Codes assigned must accurately reflect the diagnosis.
  2. Completeness: All relevant conditions must be indexed.
  3. Consistency: The same condition should always be assigned the same code.
  4. Timeliness: Indexing should be done promptly after discharge.
  5. Retrievability: The system must allow for easy retrieval of records.
  6. Standardization: Use of standard classification systems (e.g., ICD-10).
2.
List any 4 principles of classification as per the family of classification (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer: (Referring to the WHO Family of International Classifications - WHO-FIC)

  1. Reference Classifications: Main classifications covering main health parameters (e.g., ICD for mortality/morbidity, ICF for functioning).
  2. Derived Classifications: Based on reference classifications but specialized (e.g., ICD-O for Oncology).
  3. Related Classifications: Included in the family but not derived from ICD (e.g., ICPC for Primary Care, ATC for drugs).
  4. Interoperability: Ability to work together within health information systems.
3.
List the 5 proposals of William Farr in disease classification (5marks)
Show Answer

Answer: William Farr proposed classifying diseases into five groups based on how they affect the population:

  1. Epidemic diseases: Diseases that attack numbers of people at the same time.
  2. Constitutional (General) diseases: Diseases affecting the general state of the body (e.g., cancer, gout).
  3. Local diseases: Arranged according to the anatomical site or organ system involved.
  4. Developmental diseases: Disorders of growth and development.
  5. Violence/Injuries: Diseases that are the direct result of violence or accidents.
4.
List any 5 data security measurers (5 marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Physical security: Locking record rooms and cabinets, restricting access to server rooms.
  2. Authentication: Using strong passwords, biometric scans, or smart cards for user login.
  3. Access controls: Assigning role-based access rights (only authorized personnel see specific data).
  4. Data Encryption: Encrypting data both in transit and at rest to prevent unauthorized reading.
  5. Audit trails: Tracking who accessed what record and when.
  6. Backups: Regular data backups to ensure recovery in case of loss.
  7. Antivirus/Firewalls: Protecting digital systems from malware and cyber attacks.
5.
Enumerate any 5 conventions found in disease classification(5marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Parentheses ( ): Used to enclose supplementary words (modifiers) that do not affect the code assignment.
  2. Square brackets [ ]: Used to enclose synonyms, alternative wordings, or explanatory phrases.
  3. Colon : Used in inclusion and exclusion notes when the words that precede it are not complete terms; requires a modifier from the list below it.
  4. Brace }: Used to group a list of terms that are modified by a common term.
  5. NOS: "Not Otherwise Specified" - implies unspecified.
  6. NEC: "Not Elsewhere Classified" - implies the condition is specific but no specific code exists.
  7. Dagger (†) and Asterisk (*): Used for dual classification (etiology and manifestation).
6.
List any 5 characteristics of I.C.P.M (5marks)
Show Answer

Answer: (International Classification of Procedures in Medicine)

  1. Structure: It is organized into chapters based on medical specialties or body systems (e.g., Surgery, Radiology).
  2. Code Format: Uses a numeric or alphanumeric coding structure (often 4 digits).
  3. Purpose: Designed to classify surgical, diagnostic, and preventive procedures.
  4. Historical Context: Published by WHO in 1978 for trial purposes (predecessor to current procedure coding systems).
  5. Chapters: Includes chapters for Procedures for Medical Diagnosis, Laboratory Procedures, Radiology, Preventive Procedures, Surgical Operations, etc.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions (20 Marks)

1.
Briefly discuss the concept of non-diagnostic related classifications (10 marks)
Show Answer

Answer Structure:

Non-diagnostic related classifications refer to systems used to classify health-related data other than the disease diagnosis itself. These are essential for a comprehensive view of patient care and health administration.

Key Concepts to Discuss:

  • Procedural Classifications: Classifying interventions, surgeries, and treatments (e.g., ICHI, ICPM). This allows for analysis of what was done to the patient, not just what they suffered from.
  • Functioning and Disability: Classifying the impact of disease on a person's life (e.g., ICF - International Classification of Functioning, Disability and Health). This moves beyond the "medical model" to a "biopsychosocial model".
  • Reasons for Encounter: Classifying why a patient sought care, which might not result in a specific diagnosis (e.g., ICPC - International Classification of Primary Care). Important for primary care settings where symptoms are often the recorded data.
  • Pharmaceuticals: Classifying drugs and medications (e.g., ATC - Anatomical Therapeutic Chemical Classification). Essential for analyzing drug utilization and costs.
  • Nursing Interventions: Classifying nursing care (e.g., NIC/NOC).

Importance: These classifications enable reimbursement (DRGs), resource allocation, quality of care assessment, and policy making by providing a fuller picture of health service delivery.

2.
Discuss any 5 importance of disease classification (10 Marks)
Show Answer

Answer:

  1. Statistical Analysis: It allows for the aggregation of data to produce health statistics (morbidity and mortality rates) which are comparable across different regions and time periods.
  2. Epidemiological Surveillance: Essential for monitoring disease outbreaks, trends, and patterns. It helps identifying the incidence and prevalence of diseases to plan public health interventions.
  3. Resource Allocation & Planning: Helps governments and hospitals determine where resources (money, staff, equipment) are needed most based on the disease burden of the population.
  4. Reimbursement and Billing: Forms the basis of payment systems (like DRGs - Diagnosis Related Groups). Insurance companies and national health funds use codes to process claims and pay providers.
  5. Clinical Research: Enables researchers to retrieve records of patients with specific conditions for studies on treatment effectiveness, outcomes, and disease progression.
  6. Standardization of Communication: Provides a common language for health professionals globally, reducing ambiguity in describing clinical conditions.
