Section 1: Multiple Choice Questions (40 Marks)

1.
D'Espine classified diseases according to their
a) System
b) Severity
c) Nature
d) Topography
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Nature

Explanation: Marc d'Espine classified diseases according to their nature (e.g., gouty, herpetic, haematic), whereas William Farr classified them based on anatomical site.

2.
In ICD 10, dagger and asterisk stand for
a) NEC and NOC
b) Round and square brackets
c) Etiology and manifestations
d) Dual classification
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) Dual classification (or c, as it describes the system)

Explanation: The system is called "Dual Classification". The dagger represents the etiology (underlying disease) and the asterisk represents the manifestation.

3.
External causes of morbidity and mortality means
a) The disease has been caused by natural factors
b) The disease has been caused by external factor
c) The code number should be found in section one of the alphabetical index
d) The disease has been classified in vital statistics
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) The disease has been caused by external factor

Explanation: This refers to environmental events, circumstances, and conditions as the cause of injury, poisoning, and other adverse effects.

4.

a) 21 categories
b) 17 categories
c) 2 categories
d) 9 categories

a) Square brackets

d) Colon

22.
Which of the following can not be used as a lead term
a) Name of disease
b) Condition
c) Site
d) All of the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: c) Site

Explanation: The lead term is usually the name of the disease, condition, or pathological state (e.g., "Fracture", "Pneumonia"). The anatomical site is usually a modifier (sub-term), not the lead term.

23.
Who was the first medical statistician
a) William Cullen
b) William Far
c) John Grant
d) Francois Bossier de Lacrose
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) William Far

Explanation: William Farr is often considered the first medical statistician and the father of modern vital statistics.

24.
The first character of the ICD code is a letter and each letter is associated with a particular chapter EXCEPT
a) T and R
b) D and H
c) E and F
d) K and L
Show Answer

Correct Answer: b) D and H

Explanation: - D is shared between Chap II (Neoplasms D00-D48) and Chap III (Blood D50-D89). - H is shared between Chap VII (Eye H00-H59) and Chap VIII (Ear H60-H95). - E, F, K, L, R, T are exclusively associated with single chapters (IV, V, XI, XII, XVIII, XIX respectively).

25.

d) Chapter V

Explanation: Chapter V (Mental and behavioural disorders) includes a glossary of definitions for the clinical disorders to ensure uniformity in coding, as mental disorders can be subjective.

33.
Disease can be classified in the following ways EXCEPT
a) Aetiology
b) Morphology
c) Resulting functional abnormality
d) All the above
Show Answer

Correct Answer: d) All the above

Explanation: This is a trick question structure. Since A, B, and C are ALL valid ways to classify disease (SNOMED axes), "All the above" usually implies they are all correct. The "Except" asks which is NOT a way. If they are ALL ways, then none is the exception. However, in MCQs like this, if D is "All the above", it often means "All the above are valid classification methods", making D the answer to "How can they be classified", but the question is "EXCEPT". It's likely D is intended to mean "None of these are exceptions".

3.
Basic principles of disease classification (6 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Classification should be based on a single principle (e.g., etiology, anatomy) or a consistent combination.
  2. Categories should be mutually exclusive (a disease fits in only one place).
  3. The system must be exhaustive (cover all known conditions).
  4. It should allow for statistical aggregation (grouping similar conditions).
  5. It must be flexible to accommodate new knowledge (update capability).
  6. It should serve specific purposes (e.g., mortality stats, morbidity stats).
4.
Explain the importance and purpose of death registration and certification (5 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Legal Requirement: Provides official proof of death for burial and estate settlement.
  2. Health Statistics: Identifies leading causes of death to prioritize health programs.
  3. Epidemiology: Tracks disease outbreaks and mortality trends.
  4. Civil Rights: Protects rights of survivors (inheritance, insurance).
  5. Demography: Used to calculate population growth and life expectancy.
5.
List six patients particulars to be indexed (6 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Patient Name
  2. Registration/IP Number
  3. Age/Date of Birth
  4. Sex
  5. Address/Residence
  6. Diagnosis (Primary and Secondary)
  7. Operation/Procedure performed
  8. Result (Discharged, Dead, Transferred)
6.
Discuss five importance of coded medical data (10 Marks)
Show Answer
  1. Standardization: Enables comparison of health data across different regions, countries, and time periods.
  2. Reimbursement: Facilitates billing and payment systems (like NHIF claims) by translating services into billable codes.
  3. Resource Allocation: Helps hospital management plan resources (beds, staff, drugs) based on the disease burden identified through codes.
  4. Research: Simplifies the retrieval of records for clinical studies and epidemiological research.
  5. Planning & Policy: Assists governments in policy-making and monitoring progress towards health goals (e.g., SDGs) by providing accurate morbidity/mortality stats.
7.
What is nomenclature classification? (2 Marks)
Show Answer

Nomenclature is a systematic listing of proper names for diseases, conditions, and procedures (e.g., SNOMED). Unlike classification which groups similar diseases for statistics, nomenclature provides a unique, specific name and code for every distinct clinical concept, intended for detailed clinical recording.

Section 3: Short Answer Questions (20 Marks)

1.
Explain with practical example procedures involved in translation of disease into code numbers (20 Marks)
Show Answer

Procedure for Coding (Step-by-Step):

  1. Identify the Diagnosis: Read the medical record (discharge summary) to determine the main condition treated.
    Example: Acute Appendicitis with Peritonitis.
  2. Identify the Lead Term: Determine the key word to search in the index. Usually the pathological condition.
    Example: "Appendicitis".
  3. Locate in Volume 3 (Index): Look up the lead term in the Alphabetical Index.
    Example: Look for "Appendicitis".
  4. Check Modifiers: Look for sub-terms (essential modifiers) under the lead term that match the diagnosis.
    Example: Under "Appendicitis", look for "acute" and "with peritonitis".
  5. Note the Tentative Code: Read the code provided in the index.
    Example: Index might point to K35.2.
  6. Verify in Volume 1 (Tabular List): Go to the alphanumeric code in the Tabular List to verify it.
    Example: Go to Chapter XI, Block K35-K38, Category K35.
  7. Check Conventions/Notes: Read any inclusion or exclusion notes, or instructions like "Use additional code".
    Example: Check for exclusions under K35.
  8. Assign Final Code: Select the most specific code (4th character) based on the tabular verification.
    Example: Assign K35.2 Acute appendicitis with generalized peritonitis.
