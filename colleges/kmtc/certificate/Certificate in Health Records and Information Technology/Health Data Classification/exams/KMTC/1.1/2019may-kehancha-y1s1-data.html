Section One

Q1.
Give two (2) reasons why it is important to identify cause of death. (2mks)
Show Answer
  1. Public Health Planning: To identify the leading causes of mortality in a population, which helps in resource allocation and designing prevention programs.
  2. Legal Purposes: To provide official proof of the cause of death for legal matters such as inheritance, insurance claims, and investigation of crimes (in case of unnatural deaths).
Q2.
In medical certificate, under the cause of death, the certifier does not include symptoms and mode of dying. Why? (5mks)
Show Answer

Explanation:

  • Specific Etiology: Public health is interested in the underlying cause—the disease or injury that initiated the train of morbid events leading to death. Modes of dying (e.g., cardiac arrest, respiratory failure) are merely the final mechanisms common to many deaths and do not identify the root problem.
  • Prevention: You cannot prevent "cardiac arrest" in the general sense, but you can prevent "Ischaemic Heart Disease" or "Trauma". Including specific diseases allows for targeted preventive interventions.
  • Statistical Value: Modes of dying are statistically useless for epidemiological analysis because they do not reflect the burden of disease in the community.
Q3.
List the chapters that use more than one letter in the first position of their codes. (4mks)
Show Answer

In ICD-10, the following chapters use codes spanning multiple letters:

  1. Chapter I: Certain infectious and parasitic diseases (Codes A00–B99).
  2. Chapter II: Neoplasms (Codes C00–D48).
  3. Chapter XIX: Injury, poisoning and certain other consequences of external causes (Codes S00–T98).
  4. Chapter XX: External causes of morbidity and mortality (Codes V01–Y98).
Q4.
Within each block, some of the 3-character categories are for single condition selected. Why? (10mks)
Show Answer

Rationale for Single Condition Categories:

  • Frequency/Prevalence: Certain conditions occur very frequently in the population (e.g., Cholera, Essential Hypertension) and warrant their own specific category for easy tracking and statistical significance.
  • Public Health Importance: Diseases with high public health priority, such as specific infectious diseases (e.g., Measles, Malaria), are assigned unique categories to ensure detailed surveillance.
  • Severity/Impact: Conditions with significant severity or resource consumption may need to be isolated from grouped "other" conditions to monitor their specific burden.
  • Distinct Etiology: A condition may have a very distinct and singular cause that separates it clearly from other related conditions in the block, requiring a dedicated code.
  • Standardization: To maintain consistency with international reporting standards where specific diseases are monitored globally.
Q5.
Highlight basic structures of ICD. (7mks)
Show Answer

The ICD (specifically ICD-10) is structured as follows:

  1. Volumes: It consists of three volumes:
    • Volume 1: Tabular List (The codes).
    • Volume 2: Instruction Manual (Rules and guidelines).
    • Volume 3: Alphabetical Index.
  2. Chapters: The classification is divided into chapters (I to XXII), usually denoting body systems or special groups.
  3. Blocks: Chapters are subdivided into blocks of three-character categories (e.g., A00–A09).
  4. Categories (Rubrics): Three-character codes (e.g., A00) representing a single disease or group of diseases.
  5. Sub-categories: Four-character codes (e.g., A00.0) providing more specificity.
  6. Codes: Alphanumeric structure (Letter followed by numbers).
Q6.
The fifth and subsequent character levels are usually sub-classifications along a different axis from the fourth character. State these two (2) chapters. (5mks)
Show Answer

Chapters using 5th Character for a Different Axis:

  1. Chapter XIII: Diseases of the Musculoskeletal System and Connective Tissue.
    Explanation: The 4th character identifies the specific condition, while the 5th character (subclassification) identifies the anatomical site (e.g., 0 for multiple sites, 1 for shoulder, 7 for ankle).
  2. Chapter XIX: Injury, Poisoning and Certain Other Consequences of External Causes.
    Explanation: Often uses 5th characters to indicate the type of injury (e.g., open vs. closed fracture) or specificity of the site, distinct from the 4th character category.
  3. (Alternative): Chapter XX (External Causes) sometimes uses 4th/5th characters for Place of Occurrence or Activity, which is a different axis from the cause itself.
