üìö Prefixes in Medical Terms

A prefix is a word element attached to the beginning of a word or word root. Prefixes usually indicate a number, time, position, direction, or negation. Not all medical terms have a prefix. Adding or changing a prefix changes the meaning of the word. Common prefixes include:

üìö Common Body Structures

It is important to be familiar with common body structures. A variety of elements are used to form the names of these anatomical structures.

Here are some common cellular combining forms:

  • cyt/o: cell
  • hist/o: tissue
  • kary/o: nucleus
  • nucle/o: nucleus

Here are some combining forms related to position and direction:

  • anter/o: anterior, front
  • caud/o: tail
  • crani/o: cranium (skull)
  • dist/o: far, farthest
  • dors/o: back (of body)
  • infer/o: lower, below
  • later/o: side, to one side
  • medi/o: middle
  • poster/o: back (of body), behind, posterior
  • proxim/o: near, nearest
  • ventr/o: belly, belly side

Combining forms related to regions of the body include:

  • abdomin/o: abdomen
  • cervic/o: neck, cervix uteri (neck of uterus)

üìö Directional Terms

Directional terms are used to show the position of a structure in relation to another structure. Here are some key directional terms:

  • Abduction: Movement away from the midline.
  • Adduction: Movement toward the midline.
  • Medial: Pertaining to the midline of the body.
  • Lateral: Pertaining to a side.
  • Superior: Toward the head or upper portion of a structure
  • Inferior: Away from the head, toward the tail or lower part of a structure.
  • Proximal: Nearer to the center (trunk of the body) or to the point of attachment to the body.
  • Distal: Further from the center (trunk of the body) or from the point of attachment to the body.
  • Anterior (ventral): Front of the body.
  • Posterior (dorsal): Back of the body.
  • Parietal: Pertaining to the outer wall of the body cavity
  • Visceral: Pertaining to the viscera, or internal organs.
  • Prone: Lying on the abdomen, face down.
  • Supine: Lying horizontally on the back, face up.
  • Inversion: Turning inward or inside out.
  • Eversion: Turning outward.
  • Palmar: Pertaining to the palm of the hand.
  • Plantar: Pertaining to the sole of the foot.
  • Superficial: Toward the surface of the body (external).
  • Deep: Away from the surface of the body (internal)

üí° Pro Tip

When studying medical terminology, it is important to have a good understanding of general anatomical structures, as well as directional terms. By understanding these basic elements you can more easily interpret complex medical terminology.
