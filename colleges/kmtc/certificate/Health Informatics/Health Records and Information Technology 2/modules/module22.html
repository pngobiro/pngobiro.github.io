Module 22: GENERATING INDICATORS (Examples and Key Indicators)

  • Understand examples for applying Significance and Validity criteria for indicators.
  • Understand examples for applying Reliability and Feasibility criteria for indicators.
  • Identify Key Indicators for Outpatient, Inpatient services, Maternity child health / Family Planning, Nutrition, Immunization, and Communicable disease control.

üìö GENERATING INDICATORS (continued)

üìö 1. SIGNIFICANCE: - is the indicator significant, useful and interesting? (continued)

  1. Sample indicator: percentage of children attending under five who are male or female

    This indicator may provide some interesting information to the health workers but to be routinely collecting and reporting this piece of information will not provide significant or useful information. This is basically because the sex of a child under five years if age does not affect the type of services provided to that child

  2. Sample indicator: percentage of children attending under five clinics who are under weight for ages.

    This information is of great significance since it helps determine one of the basic purposes of the nutritious surveillance program. If this indicator is routinely observed, the nutritional status of children can be monitored. As an indicator it is significant, useful and interesting.

üìö 2. VALIDITY - Can this indicator be correctly quantified? (continued)

  1. Sample indicator: percentage of women attending antenatal clinic who are in poor health.

    It would be very difficult to define or measure ‚Äúpoor health‚Äù so this indicator must be judged to lack validity

  2. Sample indicator: percentage of pregnant women in the population who attend antenatal clinic during the month.

    This can be validly measured. ANC routinely reports the number of women attending and the number of pregnant women expected to be in the population during any given month can be determined from the census data. The indicator would be a valid measure of the coverage of ANC services. It can be correctly quantified on routine basis.

üìö 3. RELIABILITY - Can the indicator be quantified repeatedly? (continued)

  1. Sample indicator: percentage of women bringing children to <5 clinics who believe that immunization prevent disease.

    This would be difficult to measure especially on routine basis. It would also be difficult to measure and quantify believe.

  2. Sample indicator: dropout rate for the month for children <1 year of age comparing DPT -3 to DPT -1 immunization.

    This indicator could be reliably measured and calculated on a routine basis, the immunization on records and reports routinely contain the information required to determine this rate.

üìö 4. FEASIBILITY - Can the indicator be collected with the available resources? (continued)

  1. Sample indicator: monthly fluctuation in the infant mortality

    This would be extremely difficult indicator to measure on a routine basis. The only infant deaths that can be accurately be measured are those that occur in institutions. The only feasible method of determining this rate is through more complex statistical extrapolation calculated on annual basis.

  2. Sample indicator: seasonal variation in outpatient attendance at health facilities.

    It would be feasible to measure this indicator by examining the monthly reports of health facilities on a monthly basis. Shifts in monthly outpatient attendants can be easily recognized from such information.

üìö Key Indicator for each major program area of health system

Key Indicator for each major program area of health system must be developed to aid in the analysis process. Some indicators for each major area are listed below;

  1. Outpatient services
    • Incidence of top five diseases in catchment area ‚Äì for general population and for population < 5 years
    • Attendance of outpatient at health facilities (utilization)
  2. Inpatient services
    • Bed occupancy rates
    • % of expected deliveries in catchment area - taking place in maternity clinic
    • Average length of inpatient stay
    • Death rate of inpatient admissions
  3. Maternity child health / Family Planning
    • % of pregnant women in the catchment area who attend antenatal clinic during the month
    • % of newly pregnant women in catchment area who register for antenatal clinic during the month
    • % of post-partum women in the catchment area who attend postnatal clinic during the month
    • % of women aged 15-19 in the catchment area who are active family planning acceptors
    • Proportional % of active family planning acceptors who utilize the various family planning methods offered
  4. Nutrition
    • % of children <5 years of age attending CWC who are underweight for age (<80%)
    • % of children <5 years of age attending CWC who are severely underweight for age (<60%)
    • % of children <5 years of age attending CWC with growth failure
    • % of children <5 years of age in the catchment area who attend CWC during the month
  5. Immunization
    • % of children <1 year of age in the catchment area who are fully immunized
  6. Communicable disease control
    • Incidence of newly cases of specific communicable diseases
    • Deaths caused by specific communicable disease

üìö Key Takeaways

  • Significance of an indicator is judged by its relevance and utility in providing meaningful health insights, exemplified by comparing sex-disaggregated vs. weight-for-age indicators for children under five.
  • Validity of an indicator is assessed by its measurability and accurate quantification, illustrated by contrasting "poor health" vs. antenatal clinic attendance rates for pregnant women.
  • Reliability of an indicator is determined by its ability to be consistently and repeatedly quantified, demonstrated by comparing beliefs about immunization vs. immunization dropout rates.
  • Feasibility of an indicator relates to the practicality of data collection with available resources, exemplified by the challenges in measuring monthly infant mortality vs. seasonal outpatient attendance variations.
  • Key indicators are identified across major health program areas including Outpatient services, Inpatient services, Maternity and Child Health/Family Planning, Nutrition, Immunization, and Communicable Disease Control, each tailored to monitor specific program objectives and outcomes.
