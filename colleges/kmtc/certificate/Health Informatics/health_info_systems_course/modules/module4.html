üìö Defining Health Sector Indicators

Health sector indicators are statistical values used to measure progress and assess the quality of health services or health status. They are used to:

  • Provide insight into the quality of health services.
  • Measure changes over time.
  • Document change in specific conditions.

Indicators are intimately related to the activities and serve to define the magnitude of a problem. Developing indicators is a key step in health information analysis and this is done by reviewing the objectives of the various health programs.

Indicators should be clearly defined, measurable, and relevant to the objectives of a health program.

üìö Uses of Indicators

Health sector indicators are used for several purposes, including:

  • Setting performance targets and assessing progress towards achieving them.
  • Identifying problems through early warning systems to allow corrective actions.
  • Indicating whether in-depth evaluation or review is needed.
  • Benchmarking comparisons between different organizational units.
  • Converting raw data into useful information and enabling comparisons.
  • Describing situations and measuring changes over time.
  • Providing information on a broad range of conditions.
  • Providing a yardstick for comparing teams doing similar work.
  • Enabling managers to compare program implementation against work plans.

üìö Types of Indicators

Indicators can be classified into different domains:

  • Input indicators: Measure the resources invested in a program (e.g. drugs, staff).
  • Process indicators: Measure activities performed according to standards (e.g. procedure for treatment).
  • Output indicators: Measure the amount of activities completed or services delivered (e.g., number of children vaccinated).
  • Outcome/Impact indicators: Measure long-term effects of the program (e.g., impact on morbidity and mortality).

They can also be classified as:

  • Morbidity and Mortality indicators
  • Morbidity indicators.

Indicators can be measured as counts, proportions, rates or ratios.

üìö Qualities of a Good Indicator

A good indicator should have the following qualities:

  • Significance: Should represent important, actionable, and interesting information.
  • Validity: Should measure what it is intended to measure.
  • Reliability: Should provide consistent results when measured repeatedly.
  • Feasibility: Should be collectable with available resources.
  • Precise Definition: Should be defined using clear and specific terms.
  • Timeliness: Should be measured at appropriate time intervals according to availability of data.
  • Comparability: Should be comparable across different populations.
  • Relevance for National and Program Monitoring: Should measure progress towards set goals.
  • Ease of generation and Measurement: Should be a result of normal service and surveillance.
  • Ethical: Should not conflict with accepted ethical values.

üìö Coverage and Performance

Analyzing data in HIS involves calculating coverage to understand how many people are reached by a service. This includes:

  • Calculating coverage using appropriate numerators and denominators (number of cases in clinic x 100/total number of cases in catchment population).
  • Estimating target populations for various services.
  • Measuring the number of visits per new attendant for antenatal care.
  • Considering new cases to understand morbidity patterns in facilities.

Data is analyzed to identify appropriate denominators and numerators and also to monitor the operational efficiency of health facilities and impact of activities in the health sector.

üìö Data Quality

Data quality refers to the degree of excellence exhibited by data, making it appropriate for specific use. It involves:

  • Completeness, validity, consistency, timeliness, and accuracy.
  • Data Quality Assessment (DQA) ‚Äì Assessing the system that produces the data and improving it.
  • Routine Data Quality Assessment (RDQA) ‚Äì Regular verification of data quality and action plans for improvement.
  • Quality Assurance ‚Äì Ensuring compliance with established standards.
  • Quality Improvement (QI) ‚Äì Improving health care by monitoring and correcting problems.
  • Quality Control ‚Äì Monitoring and maintaining data reliability, accuracy, and completeness.

Dimensions/attributes of Data Quality:

  • Accuracy: Data that measures what it is intended to measure.
  • Reliability: Data generated based on procedures that do not change.
  • Timeliness: Data that is produced on time adhering to schedules.
  • Completeness: Capture of all required data.
  • Confidentiality: Assurance that client data is maintained according to ethical standards.
  • Precision: All variables for data analysis are captured with sufficient detail.
  • Integrity: The system is protected from deliberate bias.

Threats to data quality come from:Inappropriate data collection instruments, poor recording and reporting and errors in data processing

üìö Threats to Data Quality

Threats to data quality include:

  • Inappropriate data collection instruments and procedures: Imprecise definitions of terms and lack of standardization. The use of forms not designed for a specific setting. Poor layout and quality of printed forms.
  • Failure to report data: Data transmission does not occur due to logistical challenges, staff absence, or high opportunity costs of recording data.
  • Unintentional recording of inaccurate data: Errors introduced due to lack of skills or supporting equipment.
  • Intentional reporting of false errors: Reporting inaccurate data to avoid repercussions and in some instances to avoid litigation.
  • Errors in processing data: Inaccuracies during the transfer and aggregation of data during manual processing and data entry.

Key points in processing data include: editing, coding, data entry and tabulation

üìö Principles of Tally Sheets and Summary Forms

Tally sheets are working sheets on which data is recorded to facilitate ease of count during data summarization. Summary forms are used for reporting and are completed at specified periods using the tally sheets, registers or other source documents.

General guidelines for using these forms include:

  • Ensuring that all identification particulars are filled in before completing the particular form.
  • Reporting specific ages within the appropriate age classification.
  • Filling appropriate data in forms where data is disaggregated by sex.
  • Separating new or first visits from revisits.
  • Aggregating totals where applicable.
  • Maintaining accuracy to avoid errors and transpositions.
  • Ensuring completeness (no blanks or dashes) and practicing zero reporting.
  • Filling in the name of the person preparing the report, date and a signature.
  • Submitting the completed reports to the District Health Information System (DHIS) before the 5th of the following month.
  • Districts must make summaries promptly once all reports have been received and maintain a checklist of reports and facilities to ensure timeliness and completeness

Tally sheets are marked using forward slashes (killing one zero at ago for example 0) and are usually made immediately a clinician is through with a client before attending to the next one.

üìö Key Takeaways

  • Health sector indicators are statistical values that provide insight into the quality of health services and health status.
  • Indicators can be categorized into Input, Process, Output and Outcome/Impact.
  • A good indicator must be significant, valid, reliable, feasible, precise, timely, comparable and ethical.
  • Data quality refers to completeness, validity, consistency, timeliness and accuracy.
  • Data quality is affected by problems in data collection, processing and reporting.
  • Tally sheets are working documents that guide summary forms, which are used for reporting.
