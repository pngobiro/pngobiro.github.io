üìö Sources of Health Information

Health information can be gathered from different sources. These include:

  1. Outpatient Records: Provide data on preventive, curative, health promotion, and rehabilitative services from vertical programs.
  2. Inpatient Records: Give information on curative services for admitted patients.
  3. Notification of Infectious Diseases: Provides important indicators for tracking communicable diseases.
  4. Registration of Persons: Vital for tracking births and deaths.
  5. Surveys: Periodic data collection methods using sample populations (formal and informal).
  6. Community-Based Health Care Projects: Community driven initiatives that stimulate individuals to determine their own health needs.

These sources are used for planning, managing and monitoring of services at various levels of the health system

üìö Uses of Health Information from Community Based Health Care Projects

Data obtained from Community Based Health Care Projects is used in several ways that include:

  • To monitor the nutritional status of children using height and weight information.
  • To identify and correct environmental hazards.
  • To identify and screen persons who need treatment.
  • To diagnose and treat individual health problems.
  • To maximize program effectiveness, efficiency and to compare methods of health care delivery.
  • To identify problems and audit the use of program funds.
  • To predict disease outbreaks using surveillance data.
  • To help donor agencies identify priority areas for funding.
  • To monitor progress towards health goals.
  • For research, manpower training and program evaluation purposes.

üìö Common Terminologies

Understanding various terminologies is crucial for accurate data collection and analysis:

  • Baseline: Information gathered at the beginning of a study which is used as a point of comparison.
  • Population: The total number of people inhabiting a specific area.
  • Parent population: The original population from which samples are drawn for analysis.
  • Population proportion: The percentage of the population that has specific characteristics.
  • Population pyramid: A graphic presentation of the age and sex structure of a population.
  • Population at risk: A group with a higher likelihood of developing a specific condition.
  • Population size: A measure of actual counting of a total population.
  • Target population: The specific group from which an unbiased sample is taken.
  • Catchment area: The geographical area served by a particular facility.
  • Eligible population: Number of persons targeted for a specific indicator.
  • Input: The resources needed to achieve the results.
  • Process: Activities done to achieve desired results
  • Output: The immediate results of activities.
  • Outcome: Short term change in service use.
  • Impact: Long term change among a population.

üìö National Eligible Population and Proportions

National eligible populations and proportions guide the planning and implementation of health programs:

Examples include proportions of the population that are:

  • Under 1 year
  • Under 5 years
  • 6-11 months
  • 12-59 months
  • 5-14 years
  • Women of reproductive age (15-49 years)
  • The elderly (60 years and above)
  • Expected deliveries
  • Surviving infants
  • Emergency obstetric complications

Note: Specific proportions vary based on district and year of population projections.

üìö Primary and Secondary Data

There are different types of data:

  • Primary Data: Data collected firsthand by the investigator, tailored for a specific objective (e.g. census data). This is considered the most reliable type of data but it is expensive and difficult to conduct and it has short term values.
  • Secondary Data: Data that has been previously collected by someone else and is being used in a new investigation (e.g. KNBS data used by another researcher). This is a less expensive and time saving method with long term value but it depends on the quality of the original data.

üìö Data Collection Tools

Data collection tools are categorized into:

  • Registers: These are primary data collection tools categorized into longitudinal and cross-sectional registers. Longitudinal registers capture multiple events of the same patient, while cross-sectional registers capture one-time events.
  • Tally Sheets: Working sheets to facilitate ease of count at the time of making summaries.
  • Reporting tools: Used to compile the information gathered for reporting and analysis.

üìö Data Collection

Data collection is the process of gathering raw facts or figures. This process forms the basis for further analysis. Public health decision making relies on the availability of sound data.

Before data collection, the following questions must be answered:

  • What is the purpose of the collection?
  • What is the scope of the data to be collected?
  • What is the source of the data (primary or secondary)?
  • What is the method of collection?
  • What is the required degree of accuracy?
