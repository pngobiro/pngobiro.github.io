Module 6: Food Drugs and Chemical Substances Act (CAP 254)

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Understand the regulations concerning the sale and handling of food under the Act.
  • Explain the provisions related to the sale and handling of drugs.
  • Identify the offenses related to food and drug standards and labeling.

Food

1. Any person who sells any food that-

(a) Has in or upon it any poisonous or harmful substance; or

(b) Is unwholesome or unfit for human consumption; or

(c) consists in whole or in part of any filthy, putrid, disgusting, rotten, decomposed or diseased substance or foreign matter; or

(d) Is adulterated, shall be guilty of an offence.

2. Deception

Any person who labels, packages, treats, processes, sells or advertises any food in contravention of any regulations made under this Act, or in a manner that is false, misleading or deceptive as regards its character, nature, value, substance, quality, composition, merit or safety, shall be guilty of an offence.

3. Standards of foods

Where a standard has been prescribed for any food, any person who labels, packages, sells or advertises any food which does not comply with that standard, in such a manner that it is likely to be mistaken for food of the prescribed standard, shall be guilty of an offence.

4. Prohibition against sale of food not of nature, substance or quality demanded

Any person who sells to the prejudice of the purchaser any food which is not of the nature, or is not of the substance, or is not of the quality, of the article demanded by the purchaser shall be guilty of an offence.

5. Preparation of food under insanitary conditions

Any person who sells, prepares, packages, conveys, stores or displays for sale any food under insanitary conditions shall be guilty of an offence.

💡 Pro Tip

Pay close attention to the definitions of offenses related to food safety and labeling.

Drugs

1. Any person who sells any drug that—

(a) Is adulterated; or

(b) Consists in whole or in part of any filthy, putrid, disgusting, rotten, decomposed or diseased substance or foreign matter, shall be guilty of an offence.

2. Deception

Any person who labels, packages, treats, processes, sells or advertises any drug in contravention of any regulations made under this Act, or in a manner that is false, misleading or deceptive as regards its character, constitution, value, potency, quality, composition, merit or safety, shall be guilty of an offence.

3. Standards of drugs

(1) Where a standard has been prescribed for a drug, any person who labels, packages, sells or advertises any substance in such a manner that it is likely to be mistaken for that drug shall be guilty of an offence unless the substance is the drug in question and complies with the prescribed standard.

(2) Where a standard has not been prescribed for a drug but a standard for the drug is contained in any of the publications specified in the Schedule, any person who labels, packages, sells or advertises any other substance or article in such a manner that it is likely to be mistaken for such drug shall be guilty of an offence.

(3) Any person who labels, packages, sells or advertises any drug for which no standard has been prescribed, or for which no standard is contained in any of the publications specified in the Schedule, shall be guilty of an offence unless such drug—

(a) Is in accordance with the professed standard under which it is labelled, packaged, sold or advertised; and

(b) Does not resemble, in a manner likely to deceive, any drug for which a standard has been prescribed or which is contained in any of the publications specified in the Schedule.

4. Prohibition against sale of drugs not of nature, substance or quality demanded

Any person who sells to the prejudice of the purchaser any drug which is not of the nature, or is not of the substance, or is not of the quality, of the article demanded by the purchaser shall be guilty of an offence.

5. Preparation of drug under insanitary conditions

Any person who sells, prepares, preserves, packages, stores or conveys for sale any drug under insanitary conditions shall be guilty of an offence.

💡 Pro Tip

Understand the specific requirements for drug standards and the implications of non-compliance.

✍️ Practice Exercise

1. Describe the conditions under which the sale of food constitutes an offense under the Act.

2. Explain the concept of "deception" in the context of labeling and selling food and drugs.

3. What are the requirements for selling a drug for which no standard has been prescribed?

4. Discuss the prohibition against selling food or drugs that do not meet the nature, substance, or quality demanded by the purchaser.

View Solution

1. The sale of food constitutes an offense if the food contains any poisonous or harmful substance, is unwholesome or unfit for consumption, consists of any filthy or decomposed substance, or is adulterated.

2. "Deception" involves labeling, packaging, treating, processing, selling, or advertising food or drugs in a manner that is false, misleading, or deceptive regarding their character, value, quality, composition, merit, or safety.

3. For a drug with no prescribed standard, it must be in accordance with the professed standard under which it is sold and must not resemble any drug with a prescribed standard in a manner likely to deceive.

4. It is prohibited to sell food or drugs that do not meet the nature, substance, or quality demanded by the purchaser, ensuring that consumers receive what they expect and pay for.

📚 Key Takeaways

  • The Act prohibits the sale of food that is harmful, unwholesome, or adulterated.
  • Deception in labeling and advertising food or drugs is an offense.
  • Drugs must meet prescribed standards or, in the absence of such standards, adhere to the professed standard and not deceive consumers.
  • Selling food or drugs under insanitary conditions is prohibited.

  1. Selling food with a harmful substance.
  2. Selling food that is slightly past its best-before date.
  3. Selling adulterated food.
  4. Selling food under insanitary conditions.

2. What constitutes deception in the context of food and drug labeling?

  1. Using a foreign language on the label.
  2. Labeling in a manner that is false or misleading about its quality or safety.
  3. Using colorful packaging.
  4. Not listing all minor ingredients.

3. True or False: A drug that does not meet a prescribed standard can still be sold legally.

  1. True
  2. False

4. What is required when selling a drug for which no standard is prescribed?

  1. It must be sold at a lower price.
  2. It must adhere to the professed standard and not be deceptive.
  3. It must be labeled "experimental."
  4. It cannot be sold.
View Solution

1. b) Selling food that is slightly past its best-before date.

2. b) Labeling in a manner that is false or misleading about its quality or safety.

3. a) True

4. b) It must adhere to the professed standard and not be deceptive.
