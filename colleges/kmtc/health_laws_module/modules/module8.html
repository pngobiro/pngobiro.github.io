Module 8: Criminal Law

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Define what constitutes a crime.
  • Understand the goals of the criminal justice system.
  • Explain the general rules of criminal responsibility, including ignorance of the law, bona fide claim of right, intention, motive, and mistake of fact.
  • Discuss the concepts of insanity and intoxication as defenses in criminal law.

Introduction to Criminal Law

Criminal law involves prosecution by the government of a person for an act considered to be a crime.

A crime is an act or omission of an act in violation of a public law forbidding or commanding it.

Crime includes both felonies (more serious offences e.g. murder or rape) or misdemeanor (less serious offences).An act is considered to be a crime if it had been seriously established by statute (a formal written enactment of legislative authority that governs a city,city or country) or common law to be crime.

Purpose / goal of the criminal justice system

  1. Discouraging or deter people from committing crime;
  2. Protect the society from dangerous and harmful people;
  3. Punish people who have committed crime;
  4. Rehabilitate and reform people who have committed crime.

💡 Pro Tip

Understand the distinction between felonies and misdemeanors, and the overall goals of the criminal justice system.

General Rules as to Criminal Responsibility

Ignorance of law

  • Ignorance of the law does not afford any excuse for any act or omission which would otherwise constitute an offence.

Bona fide claim of right

  • A person is not criminally responsible in respect of an offence relating to property, if the act done was done in the exercise of an honest claim of right and without intention to defraud.

Intention and motive

  • A person is not criminally responsible for an act which occurs independently of the exercise of his will, / by accident.
  • The motive, by which a person is induced to do an act, is immaterial with regard to criminal responsibility.

Mistake of fact

  • A person who does an act under an honest and reasonable but mistaken belief in the existence of any state of things is not criminally responsible.

Presumption of sanity

  • Every person is presumed to be of sound mind and to have been of sound mind at any time which comes in question, until the contrary is proved.

Insanity

  • A person is not criminally responsible for an act/ omission if at the time of doing the act/ making the omission he is:
    • through any disease affecting his mind
    • incapable of understanding what he is doing
  • A person may be criminally responsible for an act/ omission although his mind is affected by disease, if such disease does not in fact produce upon his mind one or more of the effects mentioned above.

Intoxication

It includes a state produced by narcotics or drugs.

  • Intoxication shall not constitute a defense to any criminal charge.
  • Intoxication shall be a defence to any criminal charge if by reason the person charged complained that he did not know that such act/ omission was wrong or did not know what he was doing .It could be due the following reasons:

(a) The state of intoxication was caused without his consent by the malicious or negligent act of another person; or

(b) The person charged was by reason of intoxication insane temporarily at the time of such act/omission.

Immature age

  • A person under the age of eight years is not criminally responsible for any act or omission.
  • A person under the age of twelve years is not criminally responsible for an act or omission, unless it is proved that at the time of doing the act /omission he had capacity to know that he ought not to do the act / omission.

Judicial officers

  • A judicial officer is not criminally responsible for anything done/ omitted in the exercise of his judicial functions, although the act done is in excess of his judicial authority.

💡 Pro Tip

Pay close attention to the rules of criminal responsibility, as they determine liability in criminal cases.

✍️ Practice Exercise

1. Define what constitutes a crime and explain the difference between a felony and a misdemeanor.

2. Discuss the four main goals of the criminal justice system.

3. Explain how ignorance of the law, bona fide claim of right, intention, motive, and mistake of fact affect criminal responsibility.

4. How does the law treat insanity and intoxication as defenses in criminal cases?

View Solution

1. A crime is an act or omission that violates a public law, either forbidding or commanding it. Felonies are more serious offenses, such as murder or rape, while misdemeanors are less serious offenses.

2. The four main goals are: (a) discouraging or deterring people from committing crimes; (b) protecting society from dangerous individuals; (c) punishing those who commit crimes; and (d) rehabilitating and reforming offenders.

3. Ignorance of the law is not an excuse. A bona fide claim of right can negate criminal responsibility in property offenses. Intention relates to the wilfulness of an act, while motive is generally immaterial. Mistake of fact can negate responsibility if the belief was honest and reasonable.

4. Insanity can be a defense if the person was incapable of understanding their actions due to a mental disease. Intoxication is generally not a defense unless it was involuntary or resulted in temporary insanity.

📚 Key Takeaways

  • A crime is an act or omission that violates public law, with felonies being more serious than misdemeanors.
  • The criminal justice system aims to deter crime, protect society, punish offenders, and rehabilitate them.
  • Ignorance of the law is not a defense, while a bona fide claim of right, intention, motive, and mistake of fact can affect criminal responsibility.
  • Insanity and, in limited circumstances, intoxication can be defenses to criminal charges.
