Module 7: Law of Torts (Continued)

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Define defamation and differentiate between libel and slander.
  • Identify the defenses to defamation.
  • Explain the concept of trespass and its various forms.
  • Describe the defenses and remedies for trespass to persons, land, and goods.

Defamation

This is a false statement about a person to his discredit which exposes him to hatred ridicule or causing to be stunned or avoided by right thinking members of the society generally.

A defamatory statement is libel if made in permanent form e.g. writing, printing or in a picture

It is slander if its merely spoken or a gesture.

Defences

  • Justification if the statement is true
  • Consent of the plaintiff to publication can be a defense.

Remedies

  • Damages
  • Injunction

💡 Pro Tip

Understand the distinction between libel and slander, as it affects the legal approach to defamation cases.

Trespass

There are 3 types of trespass; Trespass to land, Trespass to person, Trespass to goods

Trespass is actionable without proof of any damage ones it is established that it has been committed the principle injuria since Damnun distinguishes between negligence and trespass. Negligence is only actionable upon proof of damage

Trespass to persons

This may take the form of an assault battery or false imprisonment.

Assault is an attempt or threat to violence to another without actuality touching him in such circumstances that the victim reasonably believes in himself to be in danger of violence.

Battery: is the actual carrying out of a threat. The mere fact of touching or pushing another person against his will can amount to a battery and there is no need to show that an injury or damage has resulted.

An act can only amount to a battery if it is intentional and voluntary, thus a person who suffers injuries in the process of scrambling for a school bus or taxi or a matatu will find it difficult to maintain an action for battery against anyone.

False imprisonment: There is said to be a false imprisonment where a person is totally deprive of his freedom of his freedom without lawful justification, whether physically or otherwise e.g. looking up a person in a room whose only exit is the locked door

False imprisonment may be committed even without the plaintiff knowledge e.g. by locking him up in his bedroom while he is asleep and then re-opening the door before he has awaken .on being informed of this facts the plaintiff may sue the person who did the locking and re-opening of his bedroom .

The length of time during which a false imprisonment lasts is immaterial but his a relevant factor in gauging the extent of the defendant liability in damages.

Defences

Parental authorities:

A parent has a right to reasonable discipline of his children .This means that where a parent beats his children or looking him up in a room for sometime by way of reasonable discipline, he cannot be sued for battery or false imprisonment .similarly if a parent gets a knife and threatens that he will out of his child's mouth unless the child steps abusing grownups an action can be brought against him for assault.

When a child is at school all his parents right of ordinary control over him are delegated to the schools authority (or teachers) and are exercised by the school authorities.

Reasonable chastise by the school authority e.g. reasonable punishment by teachers is not actionable

Trespass to land

This means without lawful justification;

  • entering or
  • remaining on the land in the possession of complainant or
  • Depositing any material object there on .There need not be any prove of damage.

It is committed where the plaintiff possession of land is wrongly interfered with it is the fact of possession rather than ownership that is important i.e. The plaintiff may be anyone in possession of land whether it is the owner or the tenant.

Trespass ab initio

This is where a person whose original entry was lawful may yet become a trespass by his subsequent misconduct.

Main defenses actionable of trespass of land

  1. Prescription of possession: land acquired by possession is also said to have been acquired by prescription. The new owner may plead title by prescription as a defense to an action brought by the previous owner to recover the land .a defendant may also plead prescription as by proving a right of common grazing or a right of way over the plaintiff land.
  2. Act of necessity : the necessity may be pleaded as a defense to an action of trespass to land e.g. entry to put out fire for public safety
  3. Statutory authority :is where the authority is conferred by law e.g. by a court order can also be a defense
  4. Entry by license: an entry authorized by the plaintiff is not actionable in trespass unless the authority or license given is abused.

REMEDIES IN RESPECT TO TRESSPASS TO LAND

  1. Damages: the plaintiff may recover compensation from the defendant; the extent depends on the effect of the defendants act on the value of land in question.
  2. Ejection: a person is entitled to use reasonable force to defend his properties thus where a person wrongfully enters or remains on another's land may be effected using reasonable force. An ejection may also be based on a court order /eviction order.
  3. Action for recovery of land: the plaintiff may bring an action to recover his land from the defendant where there has been a wrongful dispossession .it is common for such an action to be coupled with damages and ejection remedies.
  4. Injunction: an injunction may be obtained to prevent trespass or threatened trespass.

Trespass to goods

Trespass to good may occur by wrongful interference with goods in another's possession or by forcefully interfering with them while in his possession. It involves direct physical interference as e.g. where a person removes the wheels of a car parked in a street.

Detinue- it is wrongful detaining the goods of another and denying it right to possession of it. The tort of detinue also consists of the wrongful retention of anothers goods but there is no denial of his right to possess it e.g. Where the defendance can say ‘I cannot return your shoe because I have lost it '.He is liable in detinue to compensate the complainant.

Conversion – this means willful interference with goods in a manner inconsistence with the rights of another whereby the person is deprived off the use or possession of the good e.g. Eating food belonging to another person, selling goods belonging to another.

Defences

Not many defenses are available to a defendant in an action brought in respect of a wrong to goods. However where the defendant has a right to posses the goods he has a good defences. Other defenses include statutory /judicial authority.

Remedies

If the plaintiff succeeds he can recover either the goods or their value. He can claim the full value of goods and damages for any inconvenience suffered by him. The court may also order return of goods if the award of damages is not an adequate remedy.

Medical abandonment

This generally means a situation where a physician-patient relationship is ended without giving the patient reasonable notice at time where there is necessity for continuing care.To prove medical abandonment the patient must establish the existence of a physician-patient relationship and that this abandonment is the proximate cause of the injury of which the patient is suing.

Negligence

Negligence is a breach of duties caused by the omission to do something which a reasonable man guided upon those consideration which ordinarily regulate the conduct of human affairs would do or doing something which a prudent a reasonable man will not do

In order to maintain an action for negligence the complainant must prove the following

  • The defendant owed him a duty of care
  • Prove that there has been a breach of that legal duty
  • The complainant has suffered injury to his person or property.

Contributory negligence – sometimes both parties are to be blamed and each one claims the other with negligence e.g. in a traffic accident where two vehicles collided and both drivers are partly to blame. In such cases total damage is proved and then court shares is proportionality between the two according to responsibility.

Co-operate negligence - Recognizes that health care organization such as hospital owes a duty directly to a patient with regard to care and treatment. This duty cannot be delegated and because it is not centered on the physician hospital relationship; It is separate.

Failure to warn Sometimes referred to failure to protect. It is also a negligence theory e.g. A physician failing to protect an innocent 3rd part from a dangerous patient.

Doctrine of res ispa loquitur

Here, 'the facts speak for themselves'. The general rule of law is that the complainant must prove that the defendant has been negligent. In this cases however the burden of prove moves to the defendant who must show that in fact he was not negligent e.g. swabs left in a patient after operation

Vicarious liability

In certain circumstances a man is liable not only for his acts but for the acts of others.

This arises in particularly when the relationships of master-servant exist. Thus a master is always liable for wrongful acts done by his servant under his specific orders; he is also liable for the wrongful act of his servants committed in the course of their employment or within the scope of their authority.

Nuisance

There are two types of nuisance

Public nuisance is some unlawful act or mission endangering or interfering the lives, comfort, property or common rights of the public e.g. obstruction of a highway.A public nuisance is a crime and has normal remedies as in criminal proceedings

Private nuisance is the unlawful interfering with another use of ;enjoyment of land or some right over or connection with land. Such interference might be caused by loud noises, smells, smoke, clump or vibration or machinery.Not every annoyance is actionable and there must a certain amount of give and take between neighbors.

✍️ Practice Exercise

1. Define defamation and explain the difference between libel and slander.

2. What are the main defenses to a claim of defamation?

3. Explain the three types of trespass and provide an example of each.

4. Describe the defenses available in an action for trespass to land.

5. What remedies are available for trespass to land?

6. Explain the concepts of "detinue" and "conversion" in relation to trespass to goods.

7. Discuss the concept of medical abandonment and the elements a patient must prove to establish a claim.

8. What must a complainant prove to maintain an action for negligence?

9. Explain the concepts of contributory negligence, corporate negligence, and failure to warn.

10. Discuss the doctrine of "res ipsa loquitur" and its application in negligence cases.

11. What is vicarious liability, and when does it arise?

12. Differentiate between public and private nuisance.

View Solution

1. Defamation is a false statement that harms a person's reputation. Libel is written or printed defamation, while slander is spoken defamation.

2. The main defenses to defamation are justification (truth) and consent of the plaintiff to the publication.

3. The three types of trespass are: (a) Trespass to persons (e.g., assault, battery, false imprisonment); (b) Trespass to land (e.g., unauthorized entry); and (c) Trespass to goods (e.g., wrongfully taking someone's possessions).

4. Defenses for trespass to land include prescription of possession, act of necessity, statutory authority, and entry by license.

5. Remedies for trespass to land include damages, ejection, action for recovery of land, and injunction.

6. "Detinue" is the wrongful detention of another's goods, while "conversion" is the willful interference with goods in a manner inconsistent with the owner's rights.

7. Medical abandonment occurs when a physician-patient relationship is terminated without reasonable notice when there is a need for continuing care. The patient must prove the existence of the relationship and that the abandonment caused the injury.

8. To maintain an action for negligence, the complainant must prove that the defendant owed them a duty of care, breached that duty, and caused injury to the complainant or their property.

9. Contributory negligence involves both parties being at fault, corporate negligence recognizes a healthcare organization's direct duty to a patient, and failure to warn involves not protecting someone from a foreseeable harm.

10. "Res ipsa loquitur" means "the facts speak for themselves." It shifts the burden of proof to the defendant in negligence cases where the circumstances suggest negligence.

11. Vicarious liability is when someone is held responsible for the actions of another, such as an employer for an employee's actions within the scope of their employment.

12. Public nuisance affects the general public, while private nuisance interferes with an individual's use or enjoyment of their land.

📚 Key Takeaways

  • Defamation involves making false statements that harm someone's reputation, with libel referring to written and slander to spoken defamation.
  • Trespass can be to persons, land, or goods, each with specific defenses and remedies.
  • Medical abandonment involves the termination of a physician-patient relationship without proper notice.
  • Negligence requires proving a duty of care, breach of that duty, and resulting injury.
  • Vicarious liability holds one person responsible for the actions of another, often in employer-employee relationships.
  • Nuisance can be public or private, involving interference with public or individual rights, respectively.
