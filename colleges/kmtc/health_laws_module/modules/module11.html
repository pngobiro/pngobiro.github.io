Module 11: Sanitation, Housing, Cemeteries and Other Acts

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Describe the rules regarding nuisances under the Public Health Act.
  • Outline the regulations for the storage of food stuffs
  • Describe the provisions for cemeteries under the Public Health Act.
  • Summarize the key provisions of the Food, Drugs & Chemical substances act (CAP 254)

📚 Part IX: Sanitation and Housing - Nuisances

No person shall cause a nuisance or shall suffer to exist on any land or premises owned or occupied by him or of which he is in charge any nuisance or other condition liable to be injurious or dangerous to health.

It is the duty of every local authority to take measures to maintain its district in a clean and sanitary condition, and to take action against any person causing a nuisance. Local authorities must also take measures to prevent danger to health from unsuitable dwellings.

The Act lists a variety of conditions that are considered a nuisance. These include:

  • Any vessel, and any railway carriage or other conveyance, in such a state or condition as to be injurious or dangerous to health;
  • Any dwelling or premises which is so dirty as to be injurious or dangerous to health.
  • Any street, road, stream, pool, ditch or gutter that is so foul as to be offensive or injurious to health;
  • Any well or other source of water supply that is polluted;
  • Any stable, cow-shed or other building or premises for animals that is offensive or injurious to health;
  • Any accumulation or deposit of refuse which is offensive or injurious to health.
  • Any premises that is so dilapidated or overcrowded, or lacking proper sanitation.
  • Any occupied dwelling for which a wholesome water supply is not available.
  • Any factory or trade premises not kept clean, or not properly ventilated.
  • Any area of land kept in a state that is offensive or likely to cause disease.
  • Any cemetery, burial-place or place of sepulture so situated or crowded as to be offensive or dangerous.
  • Any act, omission or thing which is, or may be, dangerous to life, or Injurious to health

📚 Part X – Protection of Foodstuffs

All warehouses, godowns or buildings used for the storage of foodstuffs shall be constructed in a way that renders them rat-proof. Where any warehouse is in a state of disrepair or does not afford sufficient protection against rat invasion, the medical officer may require the owner to effect repairs.

No person shall reside or sleep in any kitchen or room in which foodstuffs are prepared or stored for sale. If a kitchen or store is being used improperly, the medical officer of health may serve notice to have the improper use addressed.

📚 Part XIII – Cemeteries

It is lawful for the Minister to select and appoint and to notify in the Gazette sufficient and proper places to be the sites of and to be used as cemeteries. It is obligatory where cemeteries exist to bury the dead in such cemeteries in conformity with the provisions of rules made by any local authority. Any person who is guilty of a breach of any such rule shall be guilty of an offence.

All cemeteries being used as such and those authorised by the Minister shall be deemed authorised cemeteries.

It is not lawful to exhume any body interred in an authorised cemetery without a permit. Permits will only be granted to the legal representative or next of kin of the person. The Minister may prescribe precautions as a condition of the permit, and any person who neglects these precautions is guilty of an offence.

The Minister can also remove remains from a grave for public works or purposes. In these cases, notice is required before remains are removed. The government shall make arrangements for reinterment of any remains removed, and meet all costs associated.

The Minister may also close any cemetery or burial ground from a time specified. It is an offense to bury any remains in a closed cemetery.

📚 Food Drugs and Chemical Substances Act (CAP 254)

This Act outlines the rules regarding Food, Drugs and Chemical Substances.

Food: It is an offense to sell food that is poisonous, unfit for consumption, or adulterated. Any person who labels, packages, treats, sells or advertises food falsely is also guilty of an offense. It is an offense to sell food that is not of the quality or substance demanded, or to prepare food under insanitary conditions.

Drugs: It is an offense to sell adulterated drugs, and to label, package or sell or advertise a drug in a false manner. A person is also guilty of an offense if they sell drugs that are not of the nature, substance or quality demanded, or prepare drugs under insanitary conditions.

Chemical Substances: It is an offence to sell adulterated chemical substances, and to label package, or sell chemical substances in a false manner. A person is guilty of an offense if they dispose of chemicals in a manner that may be dangerous to the health of others, or sell a chemical substance that may cause injury if used according to instructions. It is also an offense to prepare chemical substances under insanitary conditions.

An authorised officer may take out proceedings for an offence under this Act. Penalties for a first offence are a fine or imprisonment or both. For subsequent offenses the fines and imprisonment terms are increased.

💡 Pro Tip

This act provides wide ranging regulations designed to ensure food, drugs and chemicals are safe for consumers.

📚 Key Takeaways

  • The Public Health Act outlines various conditions that are defined as a nuisance.
  • Warehouses for storing food must be rat proof, and the act also regulates living or sleeping in kitchens or food stores
  • The act specifies rules for burials and exhumations.
  • The Food, Drugs & Chemical Substances Act protects consumers from unsafe products
