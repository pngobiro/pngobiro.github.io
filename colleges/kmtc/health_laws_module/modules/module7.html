Module 7: Law of Torts

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Define what constitutes a tort.
  • Differentiate between a tort and a crime.
  • Understand the different types of damages that can be awarded in tort cases.
  • Explain the concepts of "damage" and "liability" in tort law.

Introduction to Law of Torts

This chapter introduces the reverse of criminal wrongs which are civil wrongs. A tort is a dispute between two persons as opposed to a person and the state. This chapter thus defines what actions constitute torts and what remedies are available.

Key Definitions

  • Tort: - It is a civil wrong other than breach of contract which gives rise to an action at Common Law for unliquidated damages or other relief
  • Negligence: - omission to do something which a reasonable man guided upon those regulations which ordinarily regulate the conduct of human affairs would do or doing something which a reasonable and prudent man would not have done.
  • Occupier: - a person who has a sufficient degree of control over premises to put him under a duty of care towards those lawfully upon his premises.
  • Bailor:-A person who owns goods and takes them for bailment
  • Bailee:-A person who takes possession of goods on bailment
  • Bailment: It is a transaction under which goods are delivered by one party (the bailer) to another (the bailee) on terms, which normally require the bailee to hold the goods and ultimately to redeliver them to the bailor or in accordance with his directions.
  • Tortfeasor:-A person who commits a tort.

What is a tort?

A tort is a civil wrong other than a breach of contract whose remedy is a common law action for damages or other relief. However, not every wrong is a tort. A single action may give rise to a tort and a crime.

The law of tort protects various personal and proprietary interests.

Tortious liability arises from the breach of a duty primarily fixed by law; this duty is towards persons generally and its breach is redressable by an action for unliquidated damages.

Tort and Contract distinguished

Tort Contract
The duty is fixed by law The duty is fixed by the parties
The duty is owed to persons generally The duty is owed to the parties to the contract
The remedies are few (restricted) The remedies are far much wider.

Tort and crime

Tort is a civil wrong of which the person committing the tort may have to pay compensatory or punitive damages to the party who suffered some type of injury as a result of the tort

A crime: is a criminal wrong doing prohibited by the government for which the person committing the crime may be sentenced to jail or to be fined.

Torts are brought to court by the injured party while the government initiates for the prosecution for a crime.

Damages

Damages may be of three types

  • Nominal damages
  • Actual damages
  • Punitive damages

Nominal damages are awarded for a right in which minimal injury can be proved .It constitutes a small amount of money.

Actual damage/ compensatory damages are awarded to “make the complainant fall /restore him to his position before injury. This damage compensate for actual loss. Sometimes it may include of past and future medical expense and future loss of income.

Punitive damages /exemplary damages are awarded above and beyond actual damages. This damage are awarded when theirs is prove of outrageous malicious or intentional conduct.

Damage and Liabilities

The law distinguishes between two concepts: damnum which means damage suffered and Injuria which means injury having legal consequences.

Damnum sine injuria

The mere fact that a person has suffered damage does not entitle him to maintain an action in torts. Before an action has succeeded the harm suffered must be caused by an act which is a violation of right which the law vests in the injured party. Damage suffered in the absence of violation of legal right is known as damnum sine injuria.

Injuria sine damnum

Whenever there is an invasion of legal right the person in whom the right is vested may bring an action and recover damage although he has suffered no actual harm.

Intentional and Non-Intentional Torts

The motive with which an act is done is general irrelevant in determining whether that act is tortuous or not. The good motive will not excuse a wrongful act and a bad motive / malice will not make an otherwise lawful act unlawful.

Intentional torts

✍️ Practice Exercise

1. Define the term "tort" and explain how it differs from a breach of contract.

2. Describe the key differences between a tort and a crime.

3. Explain the three types of damages that can be awarded in tort cases.

4. Discuss the concepts of "damnum sine injuria" and "injuria sine damnum."

View Solution

1. A tort is a civil wrong, other than a breach of contract, that results in injury or harm to another, for which the injured party may seek compensation. Unlike a breach of contract, which involves a violation of an agreement between parties, a tort involves a breach of a duty imposed by law.

2. A tort is a civil wrong where the injured party seeks compensation, while a crime is a wrong against the state, prosecuted by the government, with penalties like fines or imprisonment. Torts focus on compensating the victim, while crimes focus on punishing the offender.

3. The three types of damages are: (a) Nominal damages, awarded when a legal right is violated but minimal injury is proved; (b) Actual or compensatory damages, awarded to compensate for actual losses; and (c) Punitive or exemplary damages, awarded to punish outrageous conduct and deter similar behavior.

4. "Damnum sine injuria" refers to damage suffered without the violation of a legal right, which is not actionable in tort. "Injuria sine damnum" refers to the violation of a legal right without actual damage, which is actionable.

📚 Key Takeaways

  • A tort is a civil wrong that results in injury or harm to another, for which compensation may be sought.
  • Torts differ from crimes in their purpose, initiation, and the remedies sought.
  • Damages in tort cases can be nominal, actual, or punitive, depending on the nature of the injury and the conduct of the tortfeasor.
  • The concepts of "damnum sine injuria" and "injuria sine damnum" are crucial for understanding liability in tort law.
