Module 8: Criminal Law (Continued)

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Understand the concept of compulsion as a defense in criminal law.
  • Explain who are considered principal offenders.
  • Describe the liability of corporations for criminal offenses.
  • Identify the various types of punishments that can be inflicted by a court.

Compulsion

  • A person is not criminally responsible for an offence if it is committed by two or more offenders, if the person is compelled by instant threats of the offender to kill/ harm him if he refuses;

- Threats of future injury do not excuse any offence.

Compulsion by husband

  • A married woman is not free from criminal responsibility for doing/ omitting an act merely because it happens in the presence of her husband;
  • It can be a good defence if proved that the offence was committed under the coercion of the husband.

💡 Pro Tip

Understand that compulsion can be a defense, but it has specific requirements.

Parties to Offenders

Principal offenders

Any person who procures another to do or omit to do any act is guilty of an offence of the same kind, and is liable to the same punishment

  • When an offence is committed, each of the following persons is deemed to have taken part in committing the offence hence guilty of an offence :

(a) every person who actually does the act / omission which constitutes the offence;

(b) every person who does / omits any act for the purpose of enabling or aiding another person to commit the offence;

(c) any person who counsels any other person to commit the offence;

Offences by corporations,

Where an offence is committed by any company, every person concerned in management of its affairs shall be guilty of that offence and liable to be punished unless he proves that he was not aware that the offence was being committed, or that he took all reasonable steps to prevent its commission.

💡 Pro Tip

Recognize that aiding, abetting, or counseling an offense can make someone equally liable as the principal offender.

Punishments

The following punishments may be inflicted by a court -

(a) Death;

(b) Imprisonment

(c) Detention under the Detention Camps Act;

(e) Fine;

(f) Payment of compensation;

(g) Any other punishment provided by any other Act.

💡 Pro Tip

Familiarize yourself with the range of punishments available under the law.

✍️ Practice Exercise

1. Explain the defense of compulsion and its limitations in criminal law.

2. Discuss the liability of a married woman for offenses committed in the presence of her husband.

3. Identify who are considered principal offenders under the law.

4. How are corporations held liable for criminal offenses, and what defense is available to individuals within the corporation?

5. List the different types of punishments that a court can impose.

View Solution

1. Compulsion is a defense where a person is forced to commit an offense due to immediate threats of death or serious harm. However, threats of future injury do not constitute a valid defense.

2. A married woman is generally not exempt from criminal responsibility for acts done in her husband's presence, unless she can prove she was coerced by him.

3. Principal offenders include anyone who actually commits the act, aids or enables another to commit it, or counsels another to commit it.

4. Corporations are liable through the actions of individuals involved in their management. Those individuals can defend themselves by proving they were unaware of the offense or took reasonable steps to prevent it.

5. Punishments include death, imprisonment, detention under the Detention Camps Act, fines, payment of compensation, and any other punishment provided by law.

📚 Key Takeaways

  • Compulsion can be a defense in criminal law under specific circumstances involving immediate threats.
  • Principal offenders include those who commit, aid, or counsel the commission of an offense.
  • Corporations can be held liable for offenses, with certain defenses available to individuals within the corporation.
  • Courts can impose various punishments, including death, imprisonment, fines, and compensation.
