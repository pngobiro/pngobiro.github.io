Lesson 4

Methods and Approaches of Teaching Social Studies in ECE

Objectives

By the end of the lesson, students should be able to:

  • Describe five methods of teaching social studies in ECE.
  • Discuss the benefits of each method.
  • Describe the steps to follow when planning for an ECE observational trip.

Introduction

Various approaches are used in teaching social studies in ECE. They include:

  • Observational trips
  • Modeling
  • Demonstration
  • Role play
  • Storytelling

Observational Trips

Observational trips allow young children to explore and observe their environment. These trips provide concrete experiences or first-hand information that cannot be found in pictures, books, or printed materials. They help children use multiple senses, relate theory to reality, and make concepts meaningful.

Types of Observational Trips

1. Theme Walk

During a theme walk, the teacher gives students guided information on what to observe in the school compound. This can include observing buildings, the staffroom, library, and offices. The teacher asks guiding questions to focus on specific learning objectives, listens to conversations to clarify wrong concepts, and answers questions.

Teachers can also plan neighborhood trips to explore the surroundings, familiarize students with their environment, and apply concepts. Safety is emphasized, such as walking carefully to avoid hazards and not provoking insects. After the trip, the teacher will display collected materials and organize follow-up activities, such as:

  • Drawing and coloring collected objects
  • Grouping objects by shape, size, or color
  • Engaging in songs or reciting poems about the trip
  • Displaying drawings and artwork in a designated area to build self-esteem

2. Specific and Purposeful Observational Trips

This type of trip has specific objectives. For example, a trip to a river might have objectives like:

  • Naming the river
  • Identifying sources of water
  • Discussing uses of the water
  • Exploring inappropriate uses of the water/river

Specific questions should be prepared to meet these objectives, and follow-up activities, like drawing and coloring the river, should be included.

3. Major Field Trips

Major field trips address several learning objectives and may include visits to museums, zoos, animal orphanages, musical festivals, theaters, and airports. These trips often involve multiple classes and meet the needs of different age groups.

Conducting a Major Trip

Steps to conducting a major trip include:

  • Planning the trip
  • Undertaking the trip
  • Organizing follow-up activities

Planning the Trip

Planning a trip involves several considerations:

  • Decision-making: Choose an appropriate location based on objectives and age suitability.
  • Selecting the location: Ensure the location is safe, interesting, and close enough to avoid fatigue.
  • Pre-visit: The teacher should pre-visit the site to meet employees, understand rules, and plan logistics.
  • Preparation for children and parents: Inform parents, secure commitments, and provide details of the trip.
  • Assigning duties: Define roles for supervision, first aid, snack distribution, etc.

Taking the Trip

On the day of the trip, assemble participants, conduct a roll call, and distribute name tags. Remind students of rules, answer questions, and engage them with thought-provoking questions to guide learning. Simplify the guide’s information for children, and thank helpers at the end of the visit.

Follow-Up Activities

Organize follow-up activities, such as:

  • Drawing, painting, and coloring
  • News telling, reciting poems, and singing songs
  • Modeling and role-playing

Modeling

Role modeling enables children to acquire new behaviors by observing others, aligning with humanistic and social learning theories. Albert Bandura’s social learning theory states that learning occurs through observation.

Factors Influencing Observational Learning

  • Relationship between the role model and learners
  • Relevance of the modeled behavior
  • Ability of learners to imitate the model
  • Motivation of learners

The teacher should model desired behaviors, such as consistent thoughts, positive attitude, warmth, and respect for learners.

Role Play

Role play involves students imitating real-life situations. This method fosters skills like communication, social competence, and understanding societal roles. Teachers should guide students, correct errors carefully to maintain self-esteem, and provide activities that reinforce the role play experience.

Demonstration

Demonstration is a practical approach to teaching. The teacher or a student shows a process or operation to illustrate how something works.

Ways to Ensure Successful Demonstrations

  • Conduct a trial run of the demonstration
  • Ensure all students have a clear view
  • Engage students in the demonstration
  • Follow a logical sequence of steps

Advantages of Demonstration

  • Cost-effective in terms of time and resources
  • Provides accurate results
  • Allows for real-time clarification

Disadvantages of Demonstration

  • Students may be passive
  • Not all students may participate

Storytelling Method

In storytelling, the teacher engages students by narrating stories to teach concepts. This can be made more child-centered by using questions, discussions, dramatizations, or visual aids.

Suitable Topics for Storytelling

  • Domestic and wild animals
  • Roles of family members
  • Cultural ceremonies
  • Eating habits

Exploration and Discovery

Exploration involves children observing and interacting with their surroundings, while discovery involves learning through finding new information. These methods build confidence and encourage holistic development.

Teacher’s Role in Learning through Discovery

  • Setting specific, measurable objectives
  • Encouraging interest in learning
  • Providing safe environments for exploration
  • Helping children develop skills such as concentration and observation
