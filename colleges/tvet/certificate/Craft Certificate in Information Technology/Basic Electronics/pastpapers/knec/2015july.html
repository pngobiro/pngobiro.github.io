Question 1 (4 marks)

Outline four advantages of electricity as a source of power in the society.

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

  • Versatility: Electricity can be easily converted into various forms of energy like light (lighting), heat (heating, cooking), mechanical energy (motors), and sound (audio devices).
  • Cleanliness at Point of Use: Unlike burning fossil fuels directly, using electricity does not produce smoke, soot, or fumes at the location where it's used, leading to cleaner indoor and urban environments.
  • Ease of Transmission: Electricity can be efficiently transported over long distances through power lines (the grid), allowing power generation facilities to be located far from consumption centers.
  • Controllability: Electrical power flow can be easily switched on/off and controlled precisely using switches, circuits, and electronic devices, enabling automation and sophisticated applications.

Question 2 (4 marks)

With the aid of a sketch, explain the alternating current ( AC ) through a pure inductor.

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

When an AC voltage $V(t) = V_m \sin(\omega t)$ is applied across a pure inductor (resistance R=0), the inductor opposes the change in current flow by inducing a back EMF. According to Faraday's law and Lenz's law, the voltage across the inductor is given by $V_L = L \frac{di}{dt}$.

Therefore, $L \frac{di}{dt} = V_m \sin(\omega t)$.

To find the current $i(t)$, we integrate: $di = \frac{V_m}{L} \sin(\omega t) dt$.

$i(t) = \int \frac{V_m}{L} \sin(\omega t) dt = \frac{V_m}{L} (-\frac{\cos(\omega t)}{\omega}) = -\frac{V_m}{\omega L} \cos(\omega t)$.

Since $-\cos(\theta) = \sin(\theta - 90^\circ)$, we can write:

$i(t) = \frac{V_m}{\omega L} \sin(\omega t - 90^\circ)$.

The term $\omega L$ is the inductive reactance ($X_L$). So, $i(t) = \frac{V_m}{X_L} \sin(\omega t - 90^\circ) = I_m \sin(\omega t - 90^\circ)$.

Explanation: This shows that the current ($i$) through a pure inductor lags the voltage ($V$) across it by 90 degrees (or $\pi/2$ radians). When the voltage is at its peak, the rate of change of current is maximum, but the current itself is zero. When the voltage is zero, the rate of change of current is zero, and the current is at its peak (positive or negative).

Question 3 (4 marks)

Explain two uses of special bit patterns in binary coded decimal.

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

In standard 8421 BCD, only 10 out of the 16 possible 4-bit patterns (0000 to 1001) are used to represent decimal digits 0-9. The remaining six patterns (1010 to 1111) are invalid BCD codes. Special bit patterns can refer to these invalid codes or specific valid codes used for signalling purposes.

  1. Error Detection/Signalling: The six invalid BCD patterns (1010, 1011, 1100, 1101, 1110, 1111) can be used to signal errors during transmission or processing. If one of these patterns is detected where a valid BCD digit is expected, it indicates a fault. Some systems might also use specific valid patterns (like 1001 for 9) or invalid patterns for special signals like end-of-record or sign indication (+/-), although this is less common in pure BCD.
  2. Representing Signed Numbers: While not standard BCD itself, special patterns outside the 0-9 range (often using the invalid codes like 1100 for '+' and 1101 for '-') can be assigned in specific BCD implementations (like Packed BCD) to represent the sign of a number, typically in the least significant nibble.

Question 6 (4 marks)

A conductor wire of length 36 m has a resistivity of $6 \times 10^{-6} \Omega \mathrm{~m}$ and resistance of $9 \Omega$. Determine the:

(i) cross sectional area of the wire; (2 marks)

(ii) conductivity of the wire. (2 marks)

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

Given: Length $L = 36 \, m$, Resistivity $\rho = 6 \times 10^{-6} \, \Omega \cdot m$, Resistance $R = 9 \, \Omega$.

(i) Cross-sectional Area (A):

The formula relating resistance, resistivity, length, and area is $R = \rho \frac{L}{A}$.

Rearranging to solve for A: $A = \rho \frac{L}{R}$.

$A = (6 \times 10^{-6} \, \Omega \cdot m) \times \frac{36 \, m}{9 \, \Omega}$

$A = (6 \times 10^{-6}) \times 4 \, m^2$

$A = 24 \times 10^{-6} \, m^2$ (or $2.4 \times 10^{-5} \, m^2$)

(ii) Conductivity ($\sigma$):

Conductivity is the reciprocal of resistivity: $\sigma = \frac{1}{\rho}$.

$\sigma = \frac{1}{6 \times 10^{-6} \, \Omega \cdot m}$

$\sigma = \frac{1}{6} \times 10^{6} \, S/m$

$\sigma \approx 0.1667 \times 10^{6} \, S/m = 166,667 \, S/m$ (Siemens per meter)

Question 7 (4 marks)

Explain two methods used to encode Binary Coded Decimal (BCD) numbers.

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

Two common methods used to encode decimal digits into BCD:

  1. 8421 Code (Natural BCD): This is the most common method. Each decimal digit (0-9) is represented by its 4-bit weighted binary equivalent. The weights of the bits are 8, 4, 2, and 1, corresponding to the powers of 2 ($2^3, 2^2, 2^1, 2^0$). For example, the decimal digit 7 is encoded as 0111 ($0 \times 8 + 1 \times 4 + 1 \times 2 + 1 \times 1 = 7$).
  2. Excess-3 Code: This is an unweighted BCD code where each decimal digit is represented by its 4-bit binary equivalent plus 3 (binary 0011). For example, the decimal digit 7 is encoded as $7+3=10$, which is $1010$ in binary. An advantage is that the 9's complement of an Excess-3 digit can be found by simply inverting its bits, which simplifies subtraction logic in some older systems.

Question 8 (4 marks)

Outline four applications of diodes in electronics.

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

Four applications of diodes in electronics:

  • Rectification: Converting alternating current (AC) to direct current (DC). Diodes allow current to flow in only one direction, forming the basis of half-wave and full-wave rectifiers used in power supplies.
  • Voltage Regulation (Zener Diodes): Zener diodes are used to maintain a constant voltage across a load, protecting circuits from voltage fluctuations. They operate in the reverse breakdown region.
  • Signal Demodulation (AM Radio): Diodes can be used as envelope detectors to extract the original audio signal from an amplitude-modulated (AM) radio wave.
  • Circuit Protection: Diodes can protect circuits from reverse voltage (reverse polarity protection) or voltage spikes (flyback diodes in inductive circuits, transient voltage suppression diodes).
  • Logic Gates: While less common now with integrated circuits, diodes can be used to implement simple OR and AND logic functions.
  • Light Emission (LEDs): Light Emitting Diodes (LEDs) are specialized diodes that emit light when forward biased, used for indicators, displays, and lighting.

Question 11 (15 marks)

(a) (i) Explain two disadvantages of DRAM as used in computers. (4 marks)

(ii) Differentiate between hard disk and optical disc as used in computers. (4 marks)

(b) (i) Determine the excess-3 equivalent of the number $1658_{10}$. (3 marks)

(ii) A potential difference of 5 V is connected to a uniform resistance wire of length 0.8 m and cross sectional area of $8 \times 10^{-8} \mathrm{~m}^{2}$ with 0.06 A of current flowing through the wire. Determine resistivity of the wire. (4 marks)

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

(a) (i) Disadvantages of DRAM (Dynamic RAM):

  • Requires Refreshing: DRAM stores data in capacitors which leak charge over time. Therefore, it needs to be constantly refreshed (read and rewritten) every few milliseconds to retain data, consuming extra power and time.
  • Slower Access Times: Compared to SRAM (Static RAM), DRAM generally has slower read and write access times due to the need for refreshing and the more complex addressing circuitry.

(a) (ii) Hard Disk vs Optical Disc:

  • Hard Disk Drive (HDD): Stores data magnetically on rotating platters coated with magnetic material. Uses read/write heads that move across the platters. Offers high capacity and relatively low cost per gigabyte. It's a mechanical device, making it susceptible to physical shock and slower than solid-state storage. Typically used as primary internal storage.
  • Optical Disc (e.g., CD, DVD, Blu-ray): Stores data as pits and lands on a plastic disc, read by a laser. Data is accessed optically. Generally offers lower capacity than HDDs (though Blu-ray capacity is significant). Can be read-only (CD-ROM), recordable (CD-R), or rewritable (CD-RW). More portable and less susceptible to magnetic fields, but prone to scratches. Often used for software distribution, media playback, and backups/archiving.

(b) (i) Excess-3 equivalent of $1658_{10}$:

Convert each decimal digit to its 4-bit binary equivalent and add 3 (0011):

  • $1_{10} \rightarrow 0001_2$. Excess-3: $0001 + 0011 = 0100$
  • $6_{10} \rightarrow 0110_2$. Excess-3: $0110 + 0011 = 1001$
  • $5_{10} \rightarrow 0101_2$. Excess-3: $0101 + 0011 = 1000$
  • $8_{10} \rightarrow 1000_2$. Excess-3: $1000 + 0011 = 1011$

The Excess-3 equivalent is $0100 \, 1001 \, 1000 \, 1011$.

(b) (ii) Resistivity calculation:

Given: $V = 5 \, V$, $L = 0.8 \, m$, $A = 8 \times 10^{-8} \, m^2$, $I = 0.06 \, A$.

  1. Find Resistance (R) using Ohm's Law: $R = V / I = 5 \, V / 0.06 \, A \approx 83.33 \, \Omega$.
  2. Use the resistivity formula: $R = \rho \frac{L}{A}$.
  3. Rearrange to solve for $\rho$: $\rho = R \frac{A}{L}$.
  4. Calculate: $\rho = (83.33 \, \Omega) \times \frac{8 \times 10^{-8} \, m^2}{0.8 \, m} = 83.33 \times 10 \times 10^{-8} \, \Omega \cdot m$
  5. $\rho = 833.3 \times 10^{-8} \, \Omega \cdot m = 8.333 \times 10^{-6} \, \Omega \cdot m$.

The resistivity of the wire is approximately $8.33 \times 10^{-6} \, \Omega \cdot m$.

(a) (i) Lattice defects affecting low-temperature resistivity:

  • Impurities: Foreign atoms within the crystal lattice disrupt the periodic potential and scatter charge carriers (electrons), increasing resistivity. Even small concentrations can have a significant effect at low temperatures where thermal scattering is reduced.
  • Vacancies: Missing atoms from their regular lattice sites create localized disruptions that scatter charge carriers.
  • Dislocations: Imperfections in the arrangement of atoms along a line (line defects) within the crystal structure act as scattering centers.
  • Grain Boundaries: In polycrystalline materials, the boundaries between different crystal grains disrupt the lattice and impede carrier motion.

  • Positive Charge: Located in the nucleus, carried by protons. The number of protons determines the element's atomic number.

(a) (i) Coping with challenges of emerging trends in electronics:

  • Continuous Learning & Education: Engineers and technicians need to constantly update their skills and knowledge through training, workshops, and self-study to keep up with new technologies, materials, and design methodologies.
  • Investment in R&D and Advanced Tools: Companies need to invest in research and development to innovate and stay competitive. This includes acquiring advanced simulation software, testing equipment, and manufacturing capabilities required for complex, miniaturized components.
  • Standardization and Collaboration: Developing industry standards for new technologies helps ensure interoperability and reduces design complexity. Collaboration between companies, research institutions, and standards bodies accelerates progress.

(a) (ii) Silicon vs Germanium Semiconductors:

  • Band Gap Energy: Silicon has a larger band gap energy (~1.1 eV) compared to Germanium (~0.7 eV). This means Silicon requires more energy to excite electrons into the conduction band.
  • Temperature Stability: Silicon is more stable at higher temperatures. Germanium's higher leakage current increases significantly with temperature, limiting its operating range.
  • Operating Voltage: Silicon diodes and transistors typically have a higher forward voltage drop (~0.7V) compared to Germanium (~0.3V).
  • Prevalence: Silicon is far more abundant, cheaper to process, and forms a stable oxide (Silicon Dioxide, SiO2) which is an excellent insulator crucial for manufacturing integrated circuits. Germanium is less common and more expensive.
