Question 1 (4 marks)

Outline four advantages of an alkaline battery.

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

Four advantages of an alkaline battery include:

  • Higher Energy Density: They store more energy than zinc-carbon batteries of the same size, leading to longer operational life.
  • Longer Shelf Life: Alkaline batteries retain their charge for several years when stored, significantly longer than standard zinc-carbon batteries.
  • Better Low-Temperature Performance: They perform more reliably in colder conditions compared to other primary battery types.
  • Reduced Leakage Risk: Improved sealing and chemistry make them less prone to leaking corrosive electrolytes, especially when fully discharged.

Question 3 (4 marks)

With the aid of sketches, use NAND gate in each case to represent the expressions:

(i) $\bar{A} \cdot \bar{B}=\bar{C}$

(ii) $V \cdot \bar{W}=\bar{X}$

(Sketches to be added in answer section if possible, or described)

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

Using only NAND gates to represent the expressions:

(i) $\bar{A} \cdot \bar{B}=\bar{C}$

This expression is equivalent to $C = \overline{\bar{A} \cdot \bar{B}} = A + B$ (De Morgan's Law). This represents an OR gate.

To implement an OR gate using NAND gates:

  1. Invert A using a NAND gate (connect A to both inputs): Output is $\bar{A}$.
  2. Invert B using a NAND gate (connect B to both inputs): Output is $\bar{B}$.
  3. Feed $\bar{A}$ and $\bar{B}$ into a third NAND gate: Output is $\overline{\bar{A} \cdot \bar{B}} = A + B = C$.
A ---+ Gate1 ---+
     | (NAND)   |
     +----------+ Gate3 --- C (Output = A+B)
B ---+ Gate2 ---+ (NAND)
     | (NAND)
     +----------+

(ii) $V \cdot \bar{W}=\bar{X}$

This expression is equivalent to $X = \overline{V \cdot \bar{W}}$. This is already in a form directly implementable with NAND gates if we have $\bar{W}$.

  1. Invert W using a NAND gate (connect W to both inputs): Output is $\bar{W}$.
  2. Feed V and $\bar{W}$ into a second NAND gate: Output is $\overline{V \cdot \bar{W}} = X$.
W ---+ Gate1 ---+
     | (NAND)   |
     +----------+ Gate2 --- X (Output = NAND(V, NOT W))
V --------------+ (NAND)

Question 4 (4 marks)

A motor gives an output power of 40 kW and operates with an efficiency of 80%. When the constant input voltage to the motor is 400 V, determine the constant current supply.

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

Given:

  • Output Power ($P_{out}$) = 40 kW = 40,000 W
  • Efficiency ($\eta$) = 80% = 0.80
  • Input Voltage ($V_{in}$) = 400 V

Efficiency is defined as the ratio of output power to input power:

$$ \eta = \frac{P_{out}}{P_{in}} $$

First, calculate the input power ($P_{in}$):

$$ P_{in} = \frac{P_{out}}{\eta} = \frac{40000 \text{ W}}{0.80} = 50000 \text{ W} $$

Input power is also given by the product of input voltage and input current ($I_{in}$):

$$ P_{in} = V_{in} \times I_{in} $$

Now, solve for the input current ($I_{in}$):

$$ I_{in} = \frac{P_{in}}{V_{in}} = \frac{50000 \text{ W}}{400 \text{ V}} = 125 \text{ A} $$

Therefore, the constant current supply is 125 A. (4 marks)

Question 5 (4 marks)

With the aid of a sketch, describe a pure germanium atom.

(Sketch to be added in answer section if possible, or described)

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

Description of a Pure Germanium (Ge) Atom:

A pure germanium atom is a semiconductor element found in Group 14 of the periodic table. Its atomic structure is key to its electrical properties:

  • Atomic Number: Germanium has an atomic number of 32, meaning its nucleus contains 32 protons. A neutral atom also has 32 electrons orbiting the nucleus.
  • Electron Shells: These 32 electrons are arranged in specific energy levels or shells. The configuration is typically 2 electrons in the first shell, 8 in the second, 18 in the third, and 4 in the outermost (valence) shell.
  • Valence Electrons: The crucial feature is the 4 electrons in the valence shell. These electrons are relatively loosely bound and are involved in chemical bonding and electrical conduction.
  • Covalent Bonding: In a pure germanium crystal, each atom forms covalent bonds with four neighboring atoms. Each atom shares one of its valence electrons with each neighbor, effectively filling its valence shell to a stable 8 electrons (octet rule).
  • Semiconductor Nature: At absolute zero temperature, these valence electrons are tightly bound, and pure germanium acts as an insulator. However, at room temperature, thermal energy is sufficient to break some covalent bonds, freeing electrons to move and conduct electricity, leaving behind "holes" (vacancies) that can also contribute to current flow. This ability to conduct electricity under certain conditions defines it as a semiconductor.

Question 6 (4 marks)

Explain two applications of nibbles in computers.

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

A nibble (or nybble) is a unit of digital information equal to 4 bits, which is half of a standard 8-bit byte.

Two applications of nibbles in computers include:

  1. Representing Hexadecimal Digits: A single hexadecimal digit (0-9, A-F) can represent values from 0 to 15. Since $2^4 = 16$, exactly 4 bits (one nibble) are required to represent one hexadecimal digit. This makes conversions between binary and hexadecimal straightforward, as each nibble in a binary number corresponds directly to one hex digit. This is useful in low-level programming, memory addressing, and data representation. For example, the byte `1110 0101` can be easily read as `E5` in hexadecimal by considering each nibble separately.
  2. Binary-Coded Decimal (BCD) Representation: BCD is a system where each decimal digit (0-9) is encoded using a fixed number of bits, typically 4 (a nibble). For example, the decimal number 97 would be represented in BCD as `1001 0111`, where `1001` is the nibble for 9 and `0111` is the nibble for 7. While less space-efficient than pure binary, BCD simplifies arithmetic operations involving decimal numbers, particularly in financial and commercial applications where exact decimal representation and rounding are critical. Early computers and calculators often used BCD extensively.

Question 8 (4 marks)

Figure 1 represent a DC series circuit. Determine the current flow, given $R_1 (10 \Omega)$, $R_2 (20 \Omega)$, $R_3 (30 \Omega)$ and a voltage of 120 V.

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

The circuit shown in Figure 1 is a DC series circuit containing three resistors ($R_1, R_2, R_3$) and a voltage source ($V$).

Given values:

  • $R_1 = 10 \, \Omega$
  • $R_2 = 20 \, \Omega$
  • $R_3 = 30 \, \Omega$
  • $V = 120 \, \text{V}$

In a series circuit, the total resistance ($R_T$) is the sum of the individual resistances:

$$ R_T = R_1 + R_2 + R_3 $$ $$ R_T = 10 \, \Omega + 20 \, \Omega + 30 \, \Omega = 60 \, \Omega $$

According to Ohm's Law, the total current ($I$) flowing in the circuit is the total voltage divided by the total resistance:

$$ I = \frac{V}{R_T} $$ $$ I = \frac{120 \, \text{V}}{60 \, \Omega} = 2 \, \text{A} $$

Therefore, the current flow in the series circuit is 2 A. (4 marks)

Question 10 (4 marks)

Explain two types of binary complements.

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

Binary complements are primarily used to represent negative numbers in binary systems and simplify subtraction operations.

Two main types of binary complements are:

  1. 1's Complement (One's Complement):
    • Definition: The 1's complement of a binary number is obtained by inverting all the bits in the number. Each 0 becomes a 1, and each 1 becomes a 0.
    • Example: The 1's complement of the 8-bit binary number `01101001` is `10010110`.
    • Usage: It was used in some older computers for signed number representation and arithmetic. Subtraction can be performed by adding the 1's complement of the subtrahend to the minuend (with an end-around carry adjustment).
    • Drawback: It has two representations for zero: `00000000` (+0) and `11111111` (-0), which can complicate arithmetic logic.
  2. 2's Complement (Two's Complement):
    • Definition: The 2's complement of a binary number is obtained by first finding the 1's complement and then adding 1 to the result. Alternatively, starting from the rightmost bit, find the first '1', keep that bit and all subsequent zeros to its right unchanged, and invert all bits to the left of that first '1'.
    • Example: To find the 2's complement of `01101001`:
      1. 1's Complement: `10010110`
      2. Add 1: `10010110 + 1 = 10010111`
      So, the 2's complement is `10010111`.
    • Usage: This is the most common method used in modern computers to represent signed integers and perform arithmetic. Subtraction is simplified to addition using the 2's complement of the subtrahend, without needing special adjustments like the end-around carry.
    • Advantage: It has only one representation for zero (`00000000`) and simplifies the hardware logic for arithmetic operations.

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

(a) (i) Two Reasons for Using DRAM as Primary Memory: (4 marks)

  • High Density: DRAM cells are structurally simple (typically one transistor and one capacitor per bit), allowing a very large number of memory cells to be packed into a small physical area on an IC chip. This leads to high-capacity memory modules at a reasonable cost.
  • Relatively Low Cost per Bit: Compared to Static RAM (SRAM), which is faster but uses more complex cells (typically 4-6 transistors per bit), DRAM offers a significantly lower cost per bit of storage, making it economically feasible for the large amounts of RAM required by modern computers.
  • (Note: While DRAM is slower than SRAM, its density and cost advantages make it the preferred choice for main system memory, while faster SRAM is used for CPU caches.)

(a) (ii) Two Advantages of ROM Storage Media for Firmware:

  • Non-Volatility: ROM (Read-Only Memory) retains its stored information even when power is removed. This is essential for firmware (like the BIOS/UEFI in a computer), which needs to be available immediately when the device is turned on to initialize hardware and boot the system.
  • Data Security/Integrity: Since ROM is typically read-only or difficult to write to after manufacturing (depending on the specific type like PROM, EPROM, EEPROM, Flash), the firmware stored on it is protected from accidental or malicious modification, ensuring the device's fundamental operating instructions remain intact.

Using the distributive law $A(B+C) = AB + AC$, let $A = (X+Y)$. This doesn't directly apply easily.

Using the distributive law $(A+B)(C+D) = AC + AD + BC + BD$ or specifically $(A+B)(A+C) = A + BC$:

$$ (X+Y)(X+Z) = X \cdot X + X \cdot Z + Y \cdot X + Y \cdot Z $$

Using Boolean algebra identities:

  • $X \cdot X = X$ (Idempotent Law)
