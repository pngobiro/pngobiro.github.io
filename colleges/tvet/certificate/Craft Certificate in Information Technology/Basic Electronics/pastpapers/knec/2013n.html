Question 1 (4 marks)

Define the following terms as used in electricity:

(i) volt; (2 marks)

(ii) watt. (2 marks)

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

(i) Volt (V): The volt is the unit of electric potential difference or electromotive force (EMF). It represents the potential energy difference per unit charge between two points in an electric field. One volt is defined as the potential difference across a conductor when a current of one ampere dissipates one watt of power.

(ii) Watt (W): The watt is the unit of power in the International System of Units (SI). It measures the rate at which energy is transferred or converted. One watt is defined as one joule per second (1 J/s). In electrical terms, it's the power dissipated when one ampere of current flows through a potential difference of one volt (P = IV).

Question 7 (4 marks)

Outline four characteristics of integrated circuits.

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

Four characteristics of integrated circuits (ICs):

  • Miniaturization: ICs contain thousands or millions of transistors, diodes, resistors, and capacitors on a tiny semiconductor chip, leading to significantly smaller electronic devices.
  • Low Power Consumption: Compared to discrete component circuits, ICs generally consume less power due to the small size and proximity of components.
  • Increased Reliability: Fewer external connections and the integrated nature of components reduce the chances of connection failures, leading to higher reliability.
  • Cost-Effectiveness: Mass production techniques make the cost per component within an IC very low, making complex circuits more affordable.
  • Higher Operating Speed: Shorter interconnections between components within an IC reduce signal propagation delays, allowing for faster operation compared to circuits built with discrete components.

Question 9 (4 marks)

Ruth intends to write a term paper on types of bonds between atoms. Outline four types of bonds she is likely to mention.

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

Four types of bonds between atoms likely to be mentioned:

  • Ionic Bond: Formed by the electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions, typically formed when one atom (usually a metal) transfers electrons to another atom (usually a non-metal).
  • Covalent Bond: Formed when two atoms share one or more pairs of electrons to achieve stable electron configurations. Common between non-metal atoms.
  • Metallic Bond: Formed between metal atoms, characterized by a "sea" of delocalized electrons shared among a lattice of positive metal ions. This allows for electrical conductivity.
  • Van der Waals Forces: Weaker intermolecular forces arising from temporary fluctuations in electron distribution (London dispersion forces) or permanent dipoles (dipole-dipole interactions). Hydrogen bonds are a special type of dipole-dipole interaction.

Question 10 (4 marks)

Distinguish between $O R$ and $A N D$ logic gates.

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

OR Gate:

  • Symbol: Typically a curved input side and a pointed output side.
  • Function: The output is HIGH (1) if any of its inputs are HIGH (1). The output is LOW (0) only when all inputs are LOW (0).
  • Boolean Expression: $Q = A + B$ (for a 2-input gate)

AND Gate:

  • Symbol: Typically a flat input side and a rounded output side.
  • Function: The output is HIGH (1) only when all of its inputs are HIGH (1). The output is LOW (0) if any input is LOW (0).
  • Boolean Expression: $Q = A \cdot B$ (for a 2-input gate)

(a) (i) Explain two advantages of using flash memory. (4 marks)

(ii) Differentiate between $P R O M$ and $E P R O M$ as used in computers. (4 marks)

(b) (i) Determine the excess-3 equivalent $756_{8}$. (3 marks)

(ii) With the aid of a block diagram, describe an open d.c. circuit showing the flow of current. (4 marks)

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

(a) (i) Advantages of Flash Memory:

  • Non-volatility: Retains data even when power is turned off, unlike RAM.
  • Durability/Shock Resistance: Being solid-state (no moving parts), it's more resistant to physical shock and vibration than hard disk drives.
  • Speed: Offers faster read access times than traditional hard drives, although write speeds can vary.
  • Low Power Consumption: Generally consumes less power than hard disk drives.

(a) (ii) PROM vs EPROM:

  • PROM (Programmable Read-Only Memory): Can be programmed (written to) exactly once by the user after manufacturing using special equipment (PROM programmer). Once programmed, the data is permanent and cannot be changed.
  • EPROM (Erasable Programmable Read-Only Memory): Can be programmed like PROM, but it can also be erased and reprogrammed multiple times. Erasure is typically done by exposing the chip's window to strong ultraviolet (UV) light.

(b) (i) Excess-3 equivalent of $756_8$:

    • $4_{10} \rightarrow 0100_2$. Excess-3: $0100 + 0011 = 0111$

The Excess-3 equivalent is $0111 \, 1100 \, 0111$.

Question 12 (15 marks)

(a) (i) Outline three factors that could affect the resistivity of metal conductors. (3 marks)

(ii) Explain two uses of the reverse bias of a diode. (4 marks)

(b) (i) Using BCD, evaluate $456+234$. (4 marks)

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

(a) (i) Factors affecting resistivity of metal conductors:

  • Temperature: Resistivity generally increases with increasing temperature for most metals due to increased atomic vibrations hindering electron flow.
  • Type of Material: Different metals have inherently different atomic structures and electron configurations, leading to different resistivities (e.g., copper has lower resistivity than iron).
  • Impurities and Crystal Defects: Imperfections in the crystal lattice structure scatter electrons, increasing resistivity.

(a) (ii) Uses of reverse bias of a diode:

  • Blocking Current: In its basic function, a reverse-biased diode acts as an open switch, preventing significant current flow (except for a small leakage current) in one direction. This is used in rectification.
  • Voltage Regulation (Zener Diode): Zener diodes are designed to operate in the reverse breakdown region at a specific voltage (Zener voltage). They maintain a nearly constant voltage across their terminals despite changes in current, making them useful for voltage regulation.
  • Variable Capacitance (Varactor Diode): The depletion region width of a reverse-biased diode changes with the applied voltage. This change in width alters the diode's capacitance, allowing it to be used as a voltage-controlled capacitor (varactor) in tuning circuits (e.g., radio receivers).

Question 14 (15 marks)

(a) (i) Outline three challenges of emerging trends in the basic electronics. (3 marks)

(ii) Differentiate between intrinsic and extrinsic semiconductors. (4 marks)

(b) (i) Using laws of Boolean algebra, evaluate the function. $\sum \mathrm{m}(0,2,4,6,8,10,12,14)$ (5 marks)

(ii) Simplify the Boolean expression $\overline{\overline{\overline{A B}}+\overline{\overline{B C}}+\overline{A B C}}$. (3 marks)

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

(a) (i) Challenges of emerging trends in basic electronics:

  • Complexity: Increasing integration and functionality make design, testing, and debugging more complex.
  • Power Dissipation/Heat Management: As components get smaller and denser, managing the heat generated becomes a significant challenge.
  • Miniaturization Limits: Reaching physical limits governed by quantum mechanics makes further size reduction increasingly difficult and expensive.
  • Cost of Manufacturing: While ICs are cheap per component, the initial cost of designing and setting up fabrication plants (fabs) for cutting-edge technology is extremely high.
  • Environmental Concerns: Manufacturing processes involve hazardous materials, and electronic waste (e-waste) disposal is a growing environmental problem.

(a) (ii) Intrinsic vs Extrinsic Semiconductors:

  • Intrinsic Semiconductor: A semiconductor material in its pure form, without any significant doping impurities (e.g., pure Silicon or Germanium). Its conductivity is determined by thermally generated electron-hole pairs and is relatively low.
  • Extrinsic Semiconductor: A semiconductor material that has been intentionally doped with impurity atoms to increase its conductivity. Doping creates either an excess of free electrons (n-type, doped with pentavalent atoms like Phosphorus) or an excess of holes (p-type, doped with trivalent atoms like Boron).

(b) (i) Evaluate the function $\sum \mathrm{m}(0,2,4,6,8,10,12,14)$ using Boolean algebra:

Assuming a 4-variable function (A, B, C, D), the minterms correspond to inputs where the function output is 1:

Minterm | A B C D
-----------------
0       | 0 0 0 0
2       | 0 0 1 0
4       | 0 1 0 0
6       | 0 1 1 0
8       | 1 0 0 0
10      | 1 0 1 0
12      | 1 1 0 0
14      | 1 1 1 0

Observing the table, the output is 1 whenever D=0, regardless of the values of A, B, and C.

Therefore, the simplified Boolean function is $F(A, B, C, D) = \overline{D}$

(b) (ii) Simplify the Boolean expression $\overline{\overline{\overline{A B}}+\overline{\overline{B C}}+\overline{A B C}}$:

Apply DeMorgan's theorem repeatedly:

  1. $\overline{\overline{A B}} = AB$
  2. $\overline{\overline{B C}} = BC$
  3. The expression becomes: $\overline{AB + BC + \overline{ABC}}$
  4. Apply DeMorgan's to the outer bar: $\overline{(AB)} \cdot \overline{(BC)} \cdot \overline{(\overline{ABC})}$
  5. Simplify the last term: $\overline{(\overline{ABC})} = ABC$
  6. The expression is now: $\overline{(AB)} \cdot \overline{(BC)} \cdot (ABC)$
  7. Apply DeMorgan's again: $(\overline{A} + \overline{B}) \cdot (\overline{B} + \overline{C}) \cdot (ABC)$
  8. Expand the first two terms: $(\overline{A}\overline{B} + \overline{A}\overline{C} + \overline{B}\overline{B} + \overline{B}\overline{C})$ Since $\overline{B}\overline{B} = \overline{B}$, this simplifies to: $(\overline{A}\overline{B} + \overline{A}\overline{C} + \overline{B} + \overline{B}\overline{C})$ Using absorption law ($\overline{B} + \overline{A}\overline{B} = \overline{B}$ and $\overline{B} + \overline{B}\overline{C} = \overline{B}$): $(\overline{A}\overline{C} + \overline{B})$
  9. Multiply by ABC: $(\overline{A}\overline{C} + \overline{B}) \cdot (ABC)$
  10. Distribute: $(\overline{A}\overline{C} \cdot ABC) + (\overline{B} \cdot ABC)$
  11. Simplify using $X \cdot \overline{X} = 0$: $(0) + (0) = 0$

The simplified Boolean expression is $0$.
