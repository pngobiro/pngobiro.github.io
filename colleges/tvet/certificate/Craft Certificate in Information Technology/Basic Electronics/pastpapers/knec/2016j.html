Question 1 (4 marks)

Define each of the following terms as used in international systems:

(i) inertia mass;

(ii) gravitational mass;

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

(i) Inertia Mass: A measure of an object's resistance to acceleration when a force is applied. It quantifies how much force is needed to change the object's state of motion (start moving, stop moving, or change direction). It's the mass used in Newton's second law ($F=ma$).

(ii) Gravitational Mass: A measure of the strength of the gravitational interaction between an object and other objects. It determines the gravitational force experienced by the object in a gravitational field (like near the Earth, $W=mg$) and the gravitational field it produces. According to the equivalence principle, inertial mass and gravitational mass are experimentally found to be equivalent.

Question 3 (4 marks)

Explain two applications of BCD in computers.

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

Binary Coded Decimal (BCD) represents each decimal digit (0-9) with a fixed 4-bit binary code (usually the standard 8421 code). Two key applications include:

  1. Financial and Commercial Systems: BCD is often preferred in calculators, point-of-sale systems, and financial applications where precise decimal representation and rounding are critical. Pure binary representation can sometimes introduce small rounding errors when converting between decimal and binary fractions, which is unacceptable in financial calculations. BCD avoids these specific conversion errors as each decimal digit is handled individually.
  2. Digital Displays and Interfacing: BCD is convenient for driving numeric displays like 7-segment displays. Each 4-bit BCD code can be easily decoded to activate the correct segments to display the corresponding decimal digit (0-9). This simplifies the interface hardware compared to converting a pure binary number to the multiple decimal digits needed for a display.

Question 6 (4 marks)

A conductor wire of length 24 m has a resistance of $16 \Omega$ and conductivity of $3.2 \times 10 \Omega^{-1} \mathrm{~m}^{-1}$. Determine the:

(i) resistivity of the wire in $\Omega \cdot m$;

(ii) cross-sectional area of the wire.

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

Given: Length $L = 24 \, m$, Resistance $R = 16 \, \Omega$, Conductivity $\sigma = 3.2 \times 10 \, \Omega^{-1} m^{-1} = 32 \, S/m$.

(i) Resistivity ($\rho$):

Resistivity is the reciprocal of conductivity: $\rho = \frac{1}{\sigma}$.

$\rho = \frac{1}{32 \, S/m} = 0.03125 \, \Omega \cdot m$.

(ii) Cross-sectional Area (A):

Using the formula $R = \rho \frac{L}{A}$.

Rearranging for A: $A = \rho \frac{L}{R}$.

$A = (0.03125 \, \Omega \cdot m) \times \frac{24 \, m}{16 \, \Omega}$

$A = 0.03125 \times \frac{24}{16} \, m^2 = 0.03125 \times 1.5 \, m^2$

$A = 0.046875 \, m^2$.

Question 7 (4 marks)

Explain two methods used to encode a BCD number.

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

Two common methods used to encode decimal digits into BCD:

  1. 8421 Code (Natural BCD): This is the most common, weighted BCD code. Each decimal digit (0-9) is represented by its direct 4-bit binary equivalent. The weights of the bits are 8, 4, 2, and 1. For example, decimal 5 is $0101_2$ ($0*8 + 1*4 + 0*2 + 1*1 = 5$).
  2. Excess-3 Code: This is an unweighted, self-complementing BCD code. Each decimal digit is represented by its 4-bit 8421 code plus 3 (binary 0011). For example, decimal 5 is represented as $5+3=8$, which is $1000_2$ in Excess-3. Its self-complementing property (inverting bits gives the 9's complement) simplifies some arithmetic circuit designs.

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

Evaluate $10011001_{2}-10101010_{2}$ using 2's complement:

  1. Find the 2's complement of the subtrahend ($10101010_2$):
    • 1's complement: $01010101_2$
    • Add 1: $01010101 + 1 = 01010110_2$
  2. Add the 2's complement to the minuend ($10011001_2$): 
       10011001
+  01010110
-----------
  11101111
  3. Since there is no end carry (the result does not exceed 8 bits), the result is negative and is in 2's complement form ($11101111_2$).
  4. To find the magnitude, take the 2's complement of the result ($11101111_2$):
    • 1's complement: $00010000_2$
    • Add 1: $00010000 + 1 = 00010001_2$

The magnitude is $00010001_2 = 16 + 1 = 17_{10}$.

Therefore, the result is $-00010001_2$ (or $-17_{10}$).

(Check: $10011001_2 = 153_{10}$. $10101010_2 = 170_{10}$. $153 - 170 = -17$.)

Question 9 (4 marks)

The reverse-bias in a silicon p-n junction is significant to the evolution of computers. Explain two uses of this reverse-bias in computers.

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

The reverse-bias condition of a P-N junction (where voltage is applied opposing the normal forward current flow) is crucial for several semiconductor device functions used in computers:

  1. Capacitance in Varactor Diodes: The width of the depletion region at a reverse-biased P-N junction changes with the applied reverse voltage. This effect makes the junction act like a voltage-controlled capacitor (varactor diode). Varactors are used in voltage-controlled oscillators (VCOs), frequency multipliers, and tuning circuits found in communication systems often interfaced with computers.
  2. Field-Effect Transistors (FETs): In Junction FETs (JFETs), the gate-channel junction is typically reverse-biased. The reverse voltage applied to the gate controls the width of the depletion region, which in turn modulates the conductivity of the channel between the source and drain, allowing the FET to act as a switch or amplifier – fundamental operations in computer logic and memory circuits. MOSFETs, while structurally different, also rely on electric fields created by gate voltages, analogous to controlling depletion regions.
  3. Photodiodes: When operated in reverse bias (photoconductive mode), the small reverse leakage current is proportional to the amount of light striking the junction. This allows photodiodes to be used as light sensors in various computer peripherals and systems (e.g., optical mice, barcode scanners, ambient light sensors).

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

(a) (i) Disadvantages of CD-ROM:

  • Read-Only: Standard CD-ROMs cannot be written to or erased by the user, making them unsuitable for data that needs frequent updates.
  • Limited Capacity: Compared to modern storage like DVDs, Blu-rays, HDDs, SSDs, and USB drives, CD-ROMs have a relatively small storage capacity (typically around 700MB).
  • Susceptibility to Damage: The data surface is prone to scratches, fingerprints, and warping, which can render the disc unreadable.
  • Slower Access Speed: Access times and data transfer rates are significantly slower than hard drives or solid-state drives.

(a) (ii) Main Memory vs. Cache Memory:

  • Main Memory (RAM - usually DRAM): The primary workspace for the computer where the operating system, running applications, and data currently in use are stored for fast access by the CPU. It's volatile (data is lost when power is off), larger in capacity than cache, but slower than cache.
  • Cache Memory (usually SRAM): A smaller, faster type of volatile memory located closer to the CPU (often integrated into the CPU chip itself). It stores frequently accessed data and instructions from main memory to reduce the average time it takes for the CPU to access memory. It acts as a high-speed buffer between the CPU and main memory.

Question 14 (15 marks)

(a) (i) List four types of ferromagnetic core inductors. (4 marks)

(ii) Differentiate between holes and electrons in semiconductor materials. (4 marks)

(b) (i) Using laws of Boolean algebra, evaluate. (3 marks)

$$A B \bar{C}+A B C+A \bar{B} \bar{C}+\overline{A B} \bar{C}$$

(ii) Figure 1 shows a simple electric circuit with four resistors of resistance $R_{1}(16 \Omega), R_{2}(8 \Omega), R_{3}(2 \Omega)$, and $R_{4}(6 \Omega)$ and voltage of 80 V . Determine the supply current I. (4 marks)

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

(a) (i) Types of Ferromagnetic Core Inductors:

  • Laminated Iron Core: Used primarily for low-frequency applications (like power supplies and audio transformers). The core is made of thin, insulated iron sheets (laminations) to reduce eddy currents.
  • Ferrite Core: Made from ceramic ferromagnetic materials (ferrites). They have high permeability and high electrical resistance, making them suitable for high-frequency applications (like switch-mode power supplies, RF chokes) as they minimize eddy current losses. Common shapes include toroidal (donut), E-core, and pot core.
  • Powdered Iron Core: Made from fine iron particles mixed with a binder material. They offer good stability and lower core losses than laminated iron at intermediate frequencies. Often used in RF circuits and power inductors.
  • Toroidal Core: A donut-shaped core (can be ferrite or powdered iron). The magnetic field is largely contained within the core, reducing electromagnetic interference (EMI). Efficient and widely used.

(a) (ii) Holes vs. Electrons in Semiconductors:

  • Electrons: Negatively charged fundamental particles. In semiconductors, they can exist as valence electrons (bound within covalent bonds) or free electrons (in the conduction band, able to move and conduct current). In N-type semiconductors, they are the majority charge carriers.
  • Holes: The absence of an electron in a covalent bond within the semiconductor crystal lattice. A hole acts as a positive charge carrier because a nearby valence electron can move into the hole, effectively causing the hole to move in the opposite direction. In P-type semiconductors, holes are the majority charge carriers.

(b) (i) Boolean Algebra Evaluation: $A B \bar{C}+A B C+A \bar{B} \bar{C}+\overline{A B} \bar{C}$

Let $F = A B \bar{C}+A B C+A \bar{B} \bar{C}+\overline{A B} \bar{C}$. Assuming $\overline{AB}\bar{C}$ means $(\overline{A} + \overline{B})\bar{C}$ based on standard notation.

  1. Factor common terms: $F = AB(\bar{C} + C) + A\bar{B}\bar{C} + (\bar{A}+\bar{B})\bar{C}$
  2. Simplify $AB(\bar{C} + C) = AB(1) = AB$. $F = AB + A\bar{B}\bar{C} + \bar{A}\bar{C} + \bar{B}\bar{C}$
  3. Factor $\bar{C}$: $F = AB + \bar{C}(A\bar{B} + \bar{A} + \bar{B})$
  4. Simplify inside parenthesis: $A\bar{B} + \bar{A} + \bar{B} = \bar{A} + \bar{B}(A+1) = \bar{A} + \bar{B}(1) = \bar{A} + \bar{B}$ $F = AB + \bar{C}(\bar{A} + \bar{B})$
  5. Distribute: $F = AB + \bar{A}\bar{C} + \bar{B}\bar{C}$

Simplified Result: $F = AB + \bar{A}\bar{C} + \bar{B}\bar{C}$

(b) (ii) Circuit Analysis (Figure 1):

Resistors $R_2$ and $R_3$ are in parallel. Their equivalent resistance $R_{23}$ is:

$\frac{1}{R_{23}} = \frac{1}{R_2} + \frac{1}{R_3} = \frac{1}{8 \Omega} + \frac{1}{2 \Omega} = \frac{1 + 4}{8 \Omega} = \frac{5}{8 \Omega}$

$R_{23} = \frac{8}{5} \Omega = 1.6 \, \Omega$.

This combination $R_{23}$ is in series with $R_1$ and $R_4$. The total resistance $R_{total}$ is:

$R_{total} = R_1 + R_{23} + R_4 = 16 \, \Omega + 1.6 \, \Omega + 6 \, \Omega = 23.6 \, \Omega$.

The supply current $I$ is given by Ohm's Law: $I = V / R_{total}$.

$I = \frac{80 \, V}{23.6 \, \Omega} \approx 3.3898 \, A$.
