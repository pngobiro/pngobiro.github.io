Question 1 (2 marks)

Define the unit of measure for each of the following electrical quantities:

(i) electromotive force;

(ii) current.

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

(i) Electromotive force (EMF): The unit of measure is the Volt (V). It represents the energy provided by a source (like a battery or generator) per unit charge passing through it.

(ii) Current: The unit of measure is the Ampere (A). It represents the rate of flow of electric charge. One ampere is defined as one coulomb of charge passing a point per second (1 A = 1 C/s).

Question 3 (4 marks)

(a) Explain one limitation of excess-3 code as used in computers. (2 marks)

(b) Convert $24,900 \mathrm{~m} \Omega$ to ohms. (2 marks)

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

(a) Limitation of Excess-3 Code:

One limitation is that standard binary arithmetic operations (addition, subtraction) cannot be directly applied to Excess-3 coded numbers without requiring corrections. After performing binary addition on two Excess-3 numbers, a correction factor (adding or subtracting 3, depending on carry) must be applied to each nibble to get the correct Excess-3 result. This adds complexity to arithmetic logic units (ALUs) designed to handle it.

(b) Convert $24,900 \mathrm{~m} \Omega$ to ohms:

$1 \, \Omega = 1000 \, \mathrm{m}\Omega$

Therefore, $1 \, \mathrm{m}\Omega = 0.001 \, \Omega$

$24,900 \, \mathrm{m}\Omega = 24,900 \times 0.001 \, \Omega = 24.9 \, \Omega$

Question 7 (4 marks)

Explain two methods of BCD subtraction.

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

Two methods of BCD subtraction:

  1. 9's Complement Method:
    • Find the 9's complement of each digit in the subtrahend BCD number (subtract each digit from 9).
    • Add the 9's complement of the subtrahend to the minuend using BCD addition rules (add 6 if a nibble exceeds 9).
    • If there is an end-around carry from the most significant digit, add it to the least significant digit (end-around carry).
    • If there is no end-around carry, the result is negative, and the true magnitude is found by taking the 9's complement of the result.
  2. 10's Complement Method:
    • Find the 10's complement of the subtrahend BCD number (find the 9's complement and add 1 to the least significant digit).
    • Add the 10's complement of the subtrahend to the minuend using BCD addition rules.
    • If there is an end-around carry from the most significant digit, discard it. The remaining result is the positive difference.
    • If there is no end-around carry, the result is negative, and the true magnitude is found by taking the 10's complement of the result.

Question 11 (15 marks)

(a) (i) Explain two risks of using secondary storage media in computers. (4 marks)

(ii) Differentiate between optical memory and magnetic tapes as used in computers. (4 marks)

(b) (i) Determine the excess-3 equivalent of the hexadecimal number 7AD. (3 marks)

(ii) A potential difference of 10 V is connected to a uniform resistance wire of length 3 meters and a cross sectional area of $0.09 \mathrm{~m}^{2}$ with 0.01 A of current flowing in the wire. Determine the resistivity of the material. (4 marks)

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

(a) (i) Risks of using secondary storage media:

  • Data Loss/Corruption: Media can fail due to physical damage (scratches, drops), magnetic degradation (tapes, HDDs), electrical issues, or wear and tear over time, leading to data loss.
  • Security Vulnerabilities: Lost or stolen media can lead to unauthorized access to sensitive data. Malware can also infect media and spread to other systems.
  • Obsolescence: Storage formats and reader devices become obsolete, making it difficult or impossible to access data stored on older media (e.g., floppy disks).

(a) (ii) Optical Memory vs Magnetic Tapes:

  • Optical Memory (e.g., CD, DVD, Blu-ray): Stores data as pits and lands on a reflective surface, read by a laser. Offers random access (faster retrieval of specific data). Generally more durable for short-term handling but susceptible to scratches. Lower capacity per unit compared to modern tapes.
  • Magnetic Tapes: Stores data magnetically on a long strip of plastic film. Offers sequential access (must read through preceding data to reach a specific point), making it slower for retrieving specific files but suitable for backups. Higher storage capacity per unit and lower cost per gigabyte, often used for archival purposes. Susceptible to magnetic fields and physical wear.

(b) (i) Excess-3 equivalent of 7AD16:

  1. Convert Hex to Decimal: $7AD_{16} = (7 \times 16^2) + (10 \times 16^1) + (13 \times 16^0) = (7 \times 256) + (10 \times 16) + (13 \times 1) = 1792 + 160 + 13 = 1965_{10}$
  2. Convert each decimal digit to its 4-bit binary equivalent and add 3 (0011):
    • $1_{10} \rightarrow 0001_2$. Excess-3: $0001 + 0011 = 0100$
    • $9_{10} \rightarrow 1001_2$. Excess-3: $1001 + 0011 = 1100$
    • $6_{10} \rightarrow 0110_2$. Excess-3: $0110 + 0011 = 1001$
    • $5_{10} \rightarrow 0101_2$. Excess-3: $0101 + 0011 = 1000$

The Excess-3 equivalent is $0100 \, 1100 \, 1001 \, 1000$.

(b) (ii) Resistivity calculation:

Given: $V = 10 \, V$, $L = 3 \, m$, $A = 0.09 \, m^2$, $I = 0.01 \, A$.

  1. Find Resistance (R) using Ohm's Law: $R = V / I = 10 \, V / 0.01 \, A = 1000 \, \Omega$.
  2. Use the resistivity formula: $R = \rho \frac{L}{A}$, where $\rho$ is resistivity.
  3. Rearrange to solve for $\rho$: $\rho = R \frac{A}{L}$.
  4. Calculate: $\rho = (1000 \, \Omega) \times \frac{0.09 \, m^2}{3 \, m} = 1000 \times 0.03 \, \Omega \cdot m = 30 \, \Omega \cdot m$.

The resistivity of the material is $30 \, \Omega \cdot m$.

(a) (i) Conductivity and Resistivity:

  • I. Metal: High conductivity, Low resistivity. Metals have many free electrons that move easily under an electric field.
  • II. Glass: Very Low conductivity, Very High resistivity. Glass is an insulator with very few free charge carriers.
  • III. Semiconductor: Moderate conductivity, Moderate resistivity (between conductors and insulators). Conductivity can be significantly changed by doping or temperature.

(a) (i) Outline three opportunities created by emerging trends of electronics components. (3 marks)

(ii) Differentiate between forward bias and reverse bias junction diodes. (4 marks)

(b) (i) Using laws of Boolean algebra, evaluate the function: $\sum \mathrm{m}(1,3,5,7,9,11,13,15)$. (5 marks)

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

(a) (i) Opportunities created by emerging trends in electronics components:

  • Increased Functionality & Performance: Smaller, faster, and more power-efficient components enable more powerful devices (smartphones, IoT, AI hardware).
  • New Applications: Trends like flexible electronics, MEMS, and advanced sensors open doors for wearable technology, advanced medical devices, and sophisticated industrial automation.
  • Miniaturization & Portability: Continued shrinking of components leads to smaller and more portable consumer electronics and embedded systems.
  • Cost Reduction (in some areas): Mass production and integration can lower the cost of complex functionalities over time.

(a) (ii) Forward Bias vs Reverse Bias Junction Diodes:

  • Forward Bias: Occurs when the positive terminal of a voltage source is connected to the P-type material and the negative terminal to the N-type material. This reduces the potential barrier across the PN junction, allowing majority carriers (holes from P, electrons from N) to cross easily, resulting in significant current flow. The diode acts like a closed switch with a small voltage drop (typically ~0.7V for silicon).
  • Reverse Bias: Occurs when the negative terminal of a voltage source is connected to the P-type material and the positive terminal to the N-type material. This increases the potential barrier across the junction, preventing majority carriers from crossing. Only a very small leakage current due to minority carriers flows. The diode acts like an open switch.

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

(a) (i) Characteristics of an electron:

  • Negative Charge: Electrons carry a fundamental unit of negative electric charge (-1.602 x 10-19 Coulombs).
  • Small Mass: Electrons have a very small mass (approximately 9.109 x 10-31 kilograms), significantly less than protons or neutrons.
  • Orbit Nucleus: In the Bohr model, electrons orbit the atomic nucleus in specific energy levels or shells. In quantum mechanics, they exist in probability clouds (orbitals) around the nucleus.
  • Fundamental Particle: Electrons are elementary particles belonging to the lepton family.

(a) (ii) Circuit Analysis (Figure 4): Resistors R1, R2, R3 are in parallel.

I. Total Resistance ($R_T$):

$\frac{1}{R_T} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \frac{1}{R_3} = \frac{1}{50 \Omega} + \frac{1}{40 \Omega} + \frac{1}{60 \Omega}$

Common denominator is 600:

$\frac{1}{R_T} = \frac{12}{600 \Omega} + \frac{15}{600 \Omega} + \frac{10}{600 \Omega} = \frac{12+15+10}{600 \Omega} = \frac{37}{600 \Omega}$

$R_T = \frac{600}{37} \Omega \approx 16.22 \, \Omega$

II. Current across the resistors:

In a parallel circuit, the voltage across each resistor is the same as the source voltage, $V = 450 \, V$.

Current through $R_1$: $I_1 = V / R_1 = 450 \, V / 50 \, \Omega = 9 \, A$.

Current through $R_2$: $I_2 = V / R_2 = 450 \, V / 40 \, \Omega = 11.25 \, A$.

Current through $R_3$: $I_3 = V / R_3 = 450 \, V / 60 \, \Omega = 7.5 \, A$.

(Total current $I_T = V / R_T = 450 \, V / (600/37) \, \Omega = (450 \times 37) / 600 \, A = 27.75 \, A$. Check: $9 + 11.25 + 7.5 = 27.75 \, A$.)
