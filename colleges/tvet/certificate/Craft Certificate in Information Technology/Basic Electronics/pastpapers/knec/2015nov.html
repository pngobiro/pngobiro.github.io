Question 1 (4 marks)

Outline four sources of energy that could be used in the society.

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

Four sources of energy used in society:

  • Fossil Fuels: Coal, oil (petroleum), and natural gas. Formed from ancient organic matter, they are burned to release energy, primarily for electricity generation, transportation, and heating. (Non-renewable)
  • Nuclear Energy: Energy released from nuclear reactions (fission or fusion). Nuclear fission in power plants heats water to create steam, driving turbines for electricity generation. (Non-renewable fuel source, but large energy output)
  • Renewable Energy Sources:
    • Solar Energy: Energy from the sun, captured using photovoltaic panels (for electricity) or solar thermal systems (for heating).
    • Wind Energy: Kinetic energy of wind captured by wind turbines to generate electricity.
    • Hydroelectric Power: Energy from moving water (rivers, dams) used to spin turbines and generate electricity.
    • Geothermal Energy: Heat from the Earth's interior used for heating or electricity generation.
    • Biomass Energy: Energy from organic matter (wood, crops, waste) burned directly or converted into biofuels.
  • Geothermal Energy: Heat from within the Earth, accessed via drilling, used for direct heating or electricity generation through steam turbines.

Question 3 (4 marks)

Explain two disadvantages of BCD in comparison to binary number system.

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

Two disadvantages of BCD compared to pure binary:

  1. Inefficient Storage/Representation: BCD uses 4 bits to represent each decimal digit (0-9). This means numbers require more bits than their pure binary equivalent. For example, the decimal number 99 requires two 4-bit BCD groups (1001 1001 = 8 bits), whereas its binary equivalent (1100011) only needs 7 bits. This leads to less efficient use of memory and storage space.
  2. Complex Arithmetic Operations: Standard binary arithmetic logic units (ALUs) cannot directly perform arithmetic on BCD numbers. BCD addition and subtraction require extra correction steps (like adding 6 when a nibble sum exceeds 9) to ensure the result remains a valid BCD representation. This makes the arithmetic circuitry more complex and potentially slower than pure binary arithmetic.

Question 6 (4 marks)

A conductor wire of length 24 m has a resistivity of $8 \times 10^{-4} \Omega \mathrm{~m}$ and cross sectional area of $1.6 \times 10^{-2} \mathrm{~m}^{2}$. Determine the:

(i) resistance of the wire in $\Omega$; (2 marks)

(ii) conductivity of the wire. (2 marks)

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

Given: Length $L = 24 \, m$, Resistivity $\rho = 8 \times 10^{-4} \, \Omega \cdot m$, Area $A = 1.6 \times 10^{-2} \, m^2$.

(i) Resistance (R):

Using the formula $R = \rho \frac{L}{A}$.

$R = (8 \times 10^{-4} \, \Omega \cdot m) \times \frac{24 \, m}{1.6 \times 10^{-2} \, m^2}$

$R = \frac{8 \times 24}{1.6} \times \frac{10^{-4}}{10^{-2}} \, \Omega$

$R = \frac{192}{1.6} \times 10^{-2} \, \Omega$

$R = 120 \times 10^{-2} \, \Omega = 1.2 \, \Omega$

(ii) Conductivity ($\sigma$):

Conductivity is the reciprocal of resistivity: $\sigma = \frac{1}{\rho}$.

$\sigma = \frac{1}{8 \times 10^{-4} \, \Omega \cdot m}$

$\sigma = \frac{1}{8} \times 10^{4} \, S/m$

$\sigma = 0.125 \times 10^{4} \, S/m = 1250 \, S/m$ (Siemens per meter)

Question 7 (4 marks)

Explain two standard sign values for packed BCD used in computers.

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

Packed BCD represents two decimal digits within a single 8-bit byte. In signed packed BCD formats (like those used in IBM mainframes or COBOL), the last nibble (4 bits) is typically used to indicate the sign, while the preceding nibbles represent the magnitude digits.

Two standard sign values commonly used are:

  1. Positive Sign: The hexadecimal value 'C' (binary 1100) is often used as the standard representation for a positive sign in the trailing sign nibble. For example, +123 might be stored as `12 3C` in hex (0001 0010 0011 1100 in binary). Other values like A, E, F might also represent positive in some conventions.
  2. Negative Sign: The hexadecimal value 'D' (binary 1101) is often used as the standard representation for a negative sign in the trailing sign nibble. For example, -123 might be stored as `12 3D` in hex (0001 0010 0011 1101 in binary). The value B might also represent negative in some conventions.

(Note: Unsigned packed BCD typically uses 'F' (1111) in the last nibble, or omits the sign nibble entirely if the number of digits is odd).

Question 9 (4 marks)

Bipolar transistors have distinct regions of operation. Outline four of these regions defined by junction biases.

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

Four regions of operation for a Bipolar Junction Transistor (BJT), defined by the biasing of the Base-Emitter (BE) and Base-Collector (BC) junctions:

  1. Cut-off Region:
    • BE Junction: Reverse-biased
    • BC Junction: Reverse-biased
    The transistor acts like an open switch. Ideally, no collector current flows ($I_C \approx 0$).
  2. Active Region (or Forward-Active):
    • BE Junction: Forward-biased
    • BC Junction: Reverse-biased
    The transistor acts as an amplifier. Collector current ($I_C$) is proportional to the base current ($I_B$), i.e., $I_C = \beta I_B$, where $\beta$ is the current gain.
  3. Saturation Region:
    • BE Junction: Forward-biased
    • BC Junction: Forward-biased
    The transistor acts like a closed switch. Collector current reaches its maximum value, limited primarily by the external circuit, and is no longer controlled by the base current. The voltage between collector and emitter ($V_{CE}$) is very small (typically ~0.2V).
  4. Reverse-Active Region:
    • BE Junction: Reverse-biased
    • BC Junction: Forward-biased
    This is essentially operating the transistor backward (swapping emitter and collector roles). The current gain ($\beta_R$) in this mode is usually much lower than in the forward-active region. This mode is rarely used intentionally in standard circuit design.

Question 11 (15 marks)

(a) (i) Explain two disadvantages of flash memory as used in computers. (4 marks)

(ii) Differentiate between asynchronous and synchronous as a character of Static RAM. (4 marks)

(b) (i) Determine the BCD equivalent of $1100111110101001_{2}$. (3 marks)

(ii) A potential difference of 10 V is connected to a uniform resistance wire of length 0.4 m and cross sectional area of $16 \times 10^{-8} \mathrm{~m}^{2}$ with resistivity of $3.2 \times 10^{-6} \Omega \mathrm{~m}$. Determine of the current flowing through the wire. (4 marks)

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

(a) (i) Disadvantages of Flash Memory:

  • Limited Write Endurance: Flash memory cells can only withstand a finite number of write/erase cycles (typically thousands to millions depending on the type). Repeated writing eventually wears out the cells, making them unreliable.
  • Write Speed: While read speeds are generally fast, writing data to flash memory (especially erasing blocks before writing) can be significantly slower than reading or compared to DRAM write speeds.
  • Cost per Bit (Historically): While prices have dropped significantly, flash memory has historically been more expensive per bit compared to traditional magnetic storage like HDDs, although it's cheaper than DRAM or SRAM.

(a) (ii) Asynchronous vs Synchronous SRAM:

  • Asynchronous SRAM: Memory access (read/write operations) is not tied to a system clock signal. Access begins as soon as the address and control signals (like Chip Enable, Write Enable) are valid. Access times depend directly on the propagation delays of the memory chip itself. Simpler interface but potentially slower integration in high-speed systems.
  • Synchronous SRAM (SSRAM): Memory access is synchronized with an external clock signal. Address, data, and control signals are latched based on clock edges. This allows for higher speeds and pipelining of operations, making it suitable for cache memory in modern processors where timing is critical. The interface is more complex as it requires clock management.

(b) (i) BCD equivalent of $1100111110101001_{2}$:

To convert a binary number to BCD, first convert the binary number to decimal:

$1100111110101001_2 = 1 \cdot 2^{15} + 1 \cdot 2^{14} + 0 \cdot 2^{13} + ... + 1 \cdot 2^0$

$= 32768 + 16384 + 0 + 0 + 8192 + 4096 + 2048 + 1024 + 0 + 512 + 0 + 128 + 0 + 0 + 0 + 1$

$= 65153_{10}$

Now convert each decimal digit to its 4-bit BCD representation:

  • $6 \rightarrow 0110$
  • $5 \rightarrow 0101$
  • $1 \rightarrow 0001$
  • $5 \rightarrow 0101$
  • $3 \rightarrow 0011$

Result: $0110 \, 0101 \, 0001 \, 0101 \, 0011_{BCD}$

(b) (ii) Current calculation:

Given: $V = 10 \, V$, $L = 0.4 \, m$, $A = 16 \times 10^{-8} \, m^2$, $\rho = 3.2 \times 10^{-6} \, \Omega \cdot m$.

  1. Calculate Resistance (R): $R = \rho \frac{L}{A}$
  2. $R = (3.2 \times 10^{-6} \, \Omega \cdot m) \times \frac{0.4 \, m}{16 \times 10^{-8} \, m^2}$
  3. $R = \frac{3.2 \times 0.4}{16} \times \frac{10^{-6}}{10^{-8}} \, \Omega$
  4. $R = \frac{1.28}{16} \times 10^{2} \, \Omega$
  5. $R = 0.08 \times 100 \, \Omega = 8 \, \Omega$
  6. Calculate Current (I) using Ohm's Law: $I = V / R$
  7. $I = 10 \, V / 8 \, \Omega = 1.25 \, A$

The current flowing through the wire is $1.25 \, A$.

(ii) Differentiate between N-type and P-type semiconductor materials. (4 marks)

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

(a) (i) Applications of Diodes (other than logic gates):

  • Rectification: Converting AC voltage to DC voltage in power supplies.
  • Voltage Regulation: Zener diodes are used to maintain a constant voltage across a load.
  • Signal Demodulation: Extracting information (like audio) from radio signals (AM demodulation).
  • Overvoltage Protection: Clamping voltage spikes to protect sensitive circuits (TVS diodes).
  • Light Emission: Light Emitting Diodes (LEDs) for indicators and lighting.
  • Light Detection: Photodiodes used in light sensors, solar cells.

(a) (ii) N-type vs P-type Semiconductors:

  • N-type Semiconductor: Created by doping an intrinsic semiconductor (like Silicon or Germanium) with pentavalent impurities (atoms with 5 valence electrons, e.g., Phosphorus, Arsenic). These impurities donate extra free electrons, making electrons the majority charge carriers and holes the minority charge carriers. The material remains electrically neutral overall, but conductivity is primarily due to electron movement.
  • P-type Semiconductor: Created by doping an intrinsic semiconductor with trivalent impurities (atoms with 3 valence electrons, e.g., Boron, Gallium). These impurities create "holes" (absence of an electron in the covalent bond structure). Holes act as positive charge carriers and are the majority carriers, while electrons are the minority carriers. The material remains electrically neutral overall, but conductivity is primarily due to hole movement.

(b) (i) Boolean Algebra Simplification:

$F = C(B + A) + AB\bar{C}$ (Since $X + \bar{X}Y = X + Y \implies B + A\bar{B} = B + A$)

$F = BC + AC + AB\bar{C}$

$F = \bar{A}BC + A\bar{B}C + AB\bar{C} + ABC + ABC + ABC$ (Add ABC twice, since X+X=X)

$F = (\bar{A}BC + ABC) + (A\bar{B}C + ABC) + (AB\bar{C} + ABC)$ (Group terms)

$F = BC(\bar{A} + A) + AC(\bar{B} + B) + AB(\bar{C} + C)$ (Factor)

$F = BC(1) + AC(1) + AB(1)$ (Complement law)

$F = BC + AC + AB$ (Consensus Theorem form)

Question 15 (15 marks)

(a) (i) Outline three uses of extrinsic semiconductors. (3 marks)

(ii) Using the K-map, simplify the function $\sum \mathbf{m}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{2}, \mathbf{8}, \mathbf{1 0})$. (5 marks)

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

(a) (i) Uses of Extrinsic Semiconductors:

  • Transistors (BJTs, FETs): The fundamental building blocks of modern electronics, used for amplification and switching. They rely on junctions between P-type and N-type materials.
  • Diodes: Used for rectification, voltage regulation, light emission (LEDs), light detection (photodiodes), etc. Formed by a P-N junction.
  • Integrated Circuits (ICs): Complex circuits containing millions or billions of transistors, diodes, resistors, and capacitors fabricated on a single semiconductor chip.
  • Solar Cells: Convert light energy directly into electrical energy using the photovoltaic effect at a P-N junction.

(a) (ii) K-Map Simplification for $F(A,B,C,D) = \sum m(0, 2, 8, 10)$:

  • m(0) = 0000 ($\bar{A}\bar{B}\bar{C}\bar{D}$)
  • m(2) = 0010 ($\bar{A}\bar{B}C\bar{D}$)

  • m(10) = 1010 ($A\bar{B}C\bar{D}$)

      CD\AB | 00 | 01 | 11 | 10 |

       00   | 1  | 0  | 0  | 1  |  <- m0, m8
       01   | 0  | 0  | 0  | 0  |
       11   | 0  | 0  | 0  | 0  |
       10   | 1  | 0  | 0  | 1  |  <- m2, m10
     -------|----|----|----|----|

(b) (i) Memory Management Bugs:

  • Memory Leaks: Occur when a program allocates memory but fails to release it when it's no longer needed. Over time, this consumes available memory, potentially crashing the program or system. (Common in languages with manual memory management like C/C++).
  • Dangling Pointers / Use After Free: Occurs when a program tries to access memory through a pointer after that memory has been freed or deallocated. This can lead to unpredictable behavior, data corruption, or crashes.
  • Buffer Overflows/Overruns: Happen when a program writes data beyond the allocated boundary of a buffer (array or memory block). This can overwrite adjacent memory, corrupting data, altering program execution flow, or creating security vulnerabilities.
  • Double Free: Attempting to free the same block of memory more than once. This can corrupt the memory manager's internal data structures, leading to crashes or unpredictable behavior.

(b) (ii) Truth Table for Room Lights:

Let M, N, O represent the state of the windows (assume 1 = High/Open/Different Position, 0 = Low/Closed/Same Position for simplicity, though the wording is ambiguous). Let L be the light entering (1 = Light Enters, 0 = No Light).

Conditions for Light (L=1):

  1. M, N, O are in different positions. This implies exactly one or exactly two are HIGH (1). (M XOR N XOR O might be intended, but "different positions" is vague. Let's interpret as "not all same"). This means (M=0, N=0, O=1), (M=0, N=1, O=0), (M=1, N=0, O=0), (M=0, N=1, O=1), (M=1, N=0, O=1), (M=1, N=1, O=0). Basically, any combination except 000 and 111.
  2. M and O are in the same position (M=O) AND N is high (N=1). This covers (M=0, N=1, O=0) and (M=1, N=1, O=1).

Combining these conditions:

  • Condition 1 covers all rows except 000 and 111.
  • Condition 2 covers rows 010 and 111.

The union of these conditions means Light (L=1) occurs for all combinations EXCEPT when M, N, O are all 0 (M=0, N=0, O=0).
