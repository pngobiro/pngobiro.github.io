Question 1 (4 marks)

Outline four use of computer cache memory.

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

Four uses of computer cache memory:

  • Reducing Latency: Cache stores frequently accessed data and instructions closer to the CPU than main memory (RAM). This significantly reduces the time the CPU has to wait for data, speeding up processing.
  • Improving Throughput: By providing faster access to data, cache allows the CPU to execute instructions more quickly and continuously, increasing the overall number of operations performed per unit of time.
  • Bridging Speed Gaps: Cache acts as a high-speed buffer between the very fast CPU and the relatively slower main memory, mitigating the performance bottleneck caused by this speed difference.
  • Prefetching: Cache controllers often predict data or instructions the CPU might need next and prefetch them from main memory into the cache, further reducing potential wait times.

Question 2 (4 marks)

Explain each of the following terms as used in Electronics:

(i) charge;

(ii) joule.

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

(i) Charge: A fundamental property of matter that experiences a force when placed in an electromagnetic field. Electric charge can be positive (like protons) or negative (like electrons). It is quantized, meaning it exists in discrete units (multiples of the elementary charge, e). The unit of charge is the Coulomb (C).

(ii) Joule (J): The SI unit of energy, work, or quantity of heat. One joule is defined as the work done when a force of one newton displaces an object by one meter in the direction of the force. In electrical terms, one joule is the energy dissipated as heat when an electric current of one ampere passes through a resistance of one ohm for one second, or the work required to move an electric charge of one coulomb through an electrical potential difference of one volt.

Question 4 (4 marks)

With the aid of a sketch, outline a purely capacitive circuit.

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

A purely capacitive circuit consists of an AC voltage source connected directly across a capacitor.

      +-------||-------+
      |        C        |
     ~                  |
     Vs                 |
     ~                  |
      |                 |
      +-----------------+
      Current I(t) leads Voltage Vc(t) by 90 degrees

Sketch: AC voltage source (Vs) connected across a capacitor (C).

In this circuit, the capacitor continuously charges and discharges as the AC voltage changes. The current flow is maximum when the rate of change of voltage is maximum (as voltage crosses zero) and zero when the voltage is at its peak (rate of change is zero). This results in the current leading the voltage across the capacitor by 90 degrees.

Question 6 (4 marks)

Explain two advantages of using Excess-3 code?

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

Excess-3 code is a self-complementing BCD code.

  1. Self-Complementing Property: The 9's complement of a decimal digit represented in Excess-3 can be obtained by simply inverting all the bits (0s become 1s and 1s become 0s). This simplifies the hardware required for subtraction in BCD arithmetic circuits, as subtraction can be performed by adding the complement.
  2. No All-Zero or All-One Codes Used: The 4-bit patterns for decimal digits 0-9 in Excess-3 (0011 to 1100) do not include 0000 or 1111. This can be useful for error detection, as the occurrence of 0000 or 1111 might indicate a fault condition.

Question 7 (4 marks)

Explain two circumstances under which fixed resistors are most applicable in the construction of electrical components.

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

Fixed resistors are most applicable when:

  1. A Specific, Unchanging Resistance is Required: In circuits where a precise and stable resistance value is needed for biasing transistors, setting time constants (with capacitors), dividing voltage, or limiting current, and this value does not need to be adjusted during operation or for calibration.
  2. Cost and Simplicity are Factors: Fixed resistors are generally simpler and cheaper than variable resistors (potentiometers/rheostats). For mass-produced circuits or designs where adjustability is unnecessary, fixed resistors are the standard choice for providing specific resistance values.

Question 8 (4 marks)

Define each of the following terms as used in Electronics:

(i) cycle;

(ii) peak.

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

(i) Cycle: In the context of periodic waveforms (like AC voltage or current), a cycle is one complete repetition of the wave's pattern. For a sine wave, this includes one positive half-cycle and one negative half-cycle. The time taken to complete one cycle is the period (T), and the number of cycles completed per second is the frequency (f).

(ii) Peak: The peak value (or amplitude) of a waveform is its maximum instantaneous value, measured from the zero reference level, during a cycle. For a symmetrical AC waveform like a sine wave, there is a positive peak value and a negative peak value, which typically have the same magnitude.

In a purely inductive AC circuit, the current (I) lags the voltage (V) by 90 degrees. A sketch would show two sine waves: one for voltage (starting at 0, rising to a peak at 90°, zero at 180°, negative peak at 270°, zero at 360°) and one for current (starting at its negative peak at 0°, zero at 90°, positive peak at 180°, zero at 270°, negative peak at 360°).

(a) (ii) Circuit Calculations:

Given: Time $t = 1 \text{ hour} = 3600 \text{ s}$, Voltage $V = 20 \text{ V}$, Power $P = 10 \text{ W}$, Conductivity $\sigma = 1.8 \times 10^{-2} \text{ S/m}$.

I. Resistivity ($\rho$):

Resistivity is the reciprocal of conductivity.

$$ \rho = \frac{1}{\sigma} = \frac{1}{1.8 \times 10^{-2} \text{ S/m}} \approx 55.56 \, \Omega \cdot m $$

First find the current (I) using $P = VI$.

$$ I = \frac{P}{V} = \frac{10 \text{ W}}{20 \text{ V}} = 0.5 \text{ A} $$

Charge (Q) is current multiplied by time ($Q = It$).

$$ Q = (0.5 \text{ A}) \times (3600 \text{ s}) = 1800 \text{ Coulombs (C)} $$

(a) (i) List four colours used in a resistor colour scheme.

(ii) Evaluate each of the following hexadecinal number operations your assigner in binary equivalent:

II. $F 1-D 0$

(b) (i) Write the Boolean expression for the logic circuit in Figure 4.

(ii) Explain two circumstances that would necessitate the use of an electronic voltameter in electric circuits. (4 marks)

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

(a) (i) Four colours in resistor colour scheme:

Common colours include: Black (0), Brown (1), Red (2), Orange (3), Yellow (4), Green (5), Blue (6), Violet (7), Grey (8), White (9). Gold (±5%) and Silver (±10%) are used for tolerance.

Four examples: Red, Green, Blue, Gold.

(a) (ii) Hexadecimal operations in binary:

I. $294_{16} + 73_{16}$

  1. Convert to binary: $294_{16} = 0010 \, 1001 \, 0100_2$ $73_{16} = 0000 \, 0111 \, 0011_2$ (Pad to match length)
  2. Add binary numbers: 
       0010 1001 0100
+  0000 0111 0011
-------------------
   0011 0000 0111

Result in binary: $\mathbf{001100000111_2}$ (or $307_{16}$)

II. $F1_{16} - D0_{16}$

  1. Convert to binary: $F1_{16} = 1111 \, 0001_2$ $D0_{16} = 1101 \, 0000_2$
  2. Subtract binary numbers (or add 2's complement): 
       1111 0001
-  1101 0000
-------------------
   0010 0001

Result in binary: $\mathbf{00100001_2}$ (or $21_{16}$)

(b) (i) Boolean expression for Figure 4:

Inputs: A, B, C

  • Gate 1 (NAND): Output = $\overline{A \cdot B}$
  • Gate 2 (NAND): Output = $\overline{B \cdot C}$
  • Gate 3 (NAND): Output = $\overline{(\overline{A \cdot B}) \cdot (\overline{B \cdot C})}$

Using De Morgan's: $X = \overline{\overline{AB}} + \overline{\overline{BC}} = AB + BC$

Simplified Expression: $X = B(A+C)$

(b) (ii) Circumstances necessitating electronic voltmeter use:

  1. Measuring Voltage in High Impedance Circuits: Traditional analog voltmeters (like D'Arsonval movements) draw some current from the circuit to operate, which can significantly alter the voltage being measured in high impedance circuits (the "loading effect"). Electronic voltmeters (especially FET or MOSFET input types) have extremely high input impedance, drawing negligible current, thus providing a much more accurate voltage reading without disturbing the circuit.
  2. Measuring Small Voltages or Requiring High Sensitivity/Accuracy: Electronic voltmeters can incorporate amplifiers, allowing them to measure very small voltages (millivolts or microvolts) with high sensitivity and accuracy, often beyond the capability of simple analog meters. Digital electronic voltmeters also provide precise numerical readouts, eliminating parallax errors associated with reading analog scales.

(ii) Differentiate between laminated core transformer and autotransformer.

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

(a) (i) Disadvantages of Holographic Memory:

  • Cost and Complexity: The technology (lasers, modulators, detectors, materials) is currently expensive and complex compared to established storage methods.
  • Material Stability/Sensitivity: Recording materials can be sensitive to environmental factors, potentially leading to data degradation over time. Developing stable, rewritable media is challenging.

(a) (ii) Laminated Core Transformer vs. Autotransformer:

  • Laminated Core Transformer: Has two or more electrically isolated windings (primary and secondary) wound on a shared laminated iron core. It transfers energy via mutual induction, providing electrical isolation between input and output. Used for stepping voltage up/down and isolation.
  • Autotransformer: Has only one winding, with a portion of it common to both the primary and secondary circuits. Voltage transformation is achieved by tapping the winding at different points. It does *not* provide electrical isolation between input and output. Often smaller, lighter, and cheaper than an equivalent two-winding transformer for smaller voltage adjustments, but lacks isolation.

(b) (i) Octal equivalent of Gray code 11:

The question likely means Gray code $11_G$.

  1. Convert Gray to Binary:
    • MSB Binary = MSB Gray = 1.
    • Next Binary bit = Previous Binary bit XOR Current Gray bit = $1 \oplus 1 = 0$.
    Binary equivalent: $10_2$.
  2. Convert Binary to Octal: $10_2 = 2_{10} = 2_8$.

Result: $2_8$
