Question 1 (4 marks)

Define each of the following terms used in basic electronics:

(i) magnitude;

(ii) transformer;

(iii) load;

(iv) electron.

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

Basic electronics terms defined:

  1. Magnitude: A measure of the size, amplitude, or absolute value of a quantity, regardless of its sign. In electronics, it commonly refers to the size of voltage, current, or impedance. For example, a -5V signal has a magnitude of 5 volts.
  2. Transformer: A passive electrical device that transfers electrical energy between two or more circuits through electromagnetic induction. It consists of two or more coils of wire wound around a common core, allowing voltage and current transformation while maintaining power (ideally). Used for voltage conversion, isolation, and impedance matching.
  3. Load: Any device or circuit component that consumes electrical power from a source. The load converts electrical energy into another form (heat, light, motion, etc.). Examples include resistors, motors, LEDs, and speakers. The load's characteristics determine the current drawn from the source.
  4. Electron: A fundamental subatomic particle carrying a negative electric charge (1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹ coulombs). In electronics, electrons are the primary charge carriers in conductors and n-type semiconductors, their movement constitutes electric current. They occupy shells around an atom's nucleus and the outermost (valence) electrons participate in electrical conduction and bonding.

Question 2 (4 marks)

Explain each of the following terms as used in basic electronics:

(i) error codes;

(ii) NOT gate.

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

(i) Error Codes:

Error codes are standardized numerical or alphanumeric identifiers used in electronics and digital systems to indicate specific types of faults, malfunctions, or error conditions. They serve several purposes:

  • Help in troubleshooting by providing specific information about what went wrong
  • Enable systematic error detection and handling in digital systems
  • Allow for standardized error reporting across different devices or systems
  • Facilitate maintenance and repair by pointing technicians to specific issues

(ii) NOT Gate:

A NOT gate (or inverter) is a fundamental logic gate that performs logical negation. Its characteristics include:

  • Has a single input and a single output
  • Output is always the opposite (complement) of the input
  • When input is 1 (HIGH), output is 0 (LOW)
  • When input is 0 (LOW), output is 1 (HIGH)

Question 5 (4 marks)

Explain two characteristics of Read Only Memory (ROM).

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

Two key characteristics of Read Only Memory (ROM) are:

  1. Non-volatility: ROM retains its stored information even when the power supply is turned off. This makes it suitable for storing firmware, bootloaders, and other essential system software that needs to be available immediately upon power-up.
  2. Read-Mostly Operation: As the name suggests, data in traditional ROM is written during manufacturing and cannot be easily altered afterward. While modern variants like EEPROM and Flash allow reprogramming, the primary mode of operation is reading data, and writing is either impossible or significantly slower/more complex than reading.

Question 6 (4 marks)

Outline four disadvantages of semiconductor components.

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

Four disadvantages of semiconductor components include:

  • Temperature Sensitivity: The electrical characteristics (like conductivity, leakage current) of semiconductors can change significantly with temperature variations, potentially affecting circuit performance and stability. They often require heat sinks or cooling systems.
  • Susceptibility to Electrostatic Discharge (ESD): Semiconductor devices, especially MOSFETs, can be easily damaged or destroyed by static electricity during handling or installation if proper precautions are not taken.
  • Lower Power Handling (compared to some alternatives): While power semiconductors exist, individual components generally handle less power and voltage compared to older technologies like vacuum tubes or electromechanical relays in certain high-power applications.
  • Sensitivity to Radiation: Semiconductor performance can degrade or fail when exposed to certain types of radiation (like in space or nuclear environments), requiring special shielding or hardening.

Question 9 (4 marks)

Explain each of the following terms as used in number systems:

(i) logical shift;

(ii) bitmask.

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

(i) Logical Shift:

A logical shift is a bitwise operation that moves all bits in a binary number to the left or right by a specified number of positions. In a logical shift:

  • Bits shifted out from one end are discarded.
  • Vacated positions at the other end are always filled with zeros (0).
  • It treats the number as an unsigned sequence of bits, ignoring any potential sign bit interpretation.
  • Logical left shift by 'n' is equivalent to multiplication by $2^n$.
  • Logical right shift by 'n' is equivalent to integer division by $2^n$.

(ii) Bitmask:

A bitmask (or mask) is a binary pattern (a sequence of bits) used to manipulate or query specific bits within another binary number (the target). This is achieved using bitwise operations:

  • AND (&): Used to clear (set to 0) specific bits or to check if specific bits are set. (e.g., `target & mask`).
  • OR (|): Used to set (set to 1) specific bits. (e.g., `target | mask`).
  • XOR (^): Used to toggle (flip) specific bits. (e.g., `target ^ mask`).

Masks allow for efficient selection, setting, clearing, or toggling of individual bits or groups of bits within a larger data value.

Question 10 (4 marks)

With the aid of a diagram, describe the n-type semiconductors.

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

N-type Semiconductors:

An n-type semiconductor is created by doping an intrinsic semiconductor (like pure Silicon (Si) or Germanium (Ge), which have 4 valence electrons) with a small amount of pentavalent impurity atoms (atoms with 5 valence electrons, e.g., Phosphorus (P), Arsenic (As), Antimony (Sb)).

When a pentavalent atom replaces an intrinsic atom in the crystal lattice, four of its valence electrons form covalent bonds with the neighboring Si/Ge atoms. The fifth valence electron is loosely bound and requires very little energy to break free and become a mobile charge carrier (a free electron) in the conduction band.

These added impurity atoms are called "donor" atoms because they donate free electrons.

In an n-type semiconductor:

  • The number of free electrons significantly increases compared to the intrinsic state.
  • Electrons become the majority charge carriers.
  • Holes (created by thermal generation) are the minority charge carriers.
  • The material remains electrically neutral overall, but conduction is primarily due to the movement of negative electrons.

(a) (i) Four types of resistors:

  1. Fixed Resistors: Have a constant resistance value determined during manufacturing. Examples: Carbon composition, carbon film, metal film, wire-wound resistors.
  2. Variable Resistors (Potentiometers/Rheostats): Resistance can be adjusted manually. Potentiometers typically have three terminals and are used as voltage dividers. Rheostats usually have two terminals and control current.
  3. Thermistors: Resistance changes significantly with temperature (NTC - Negative Temperature Coefficient, PTC - Positive Temperature Coefficient). Used in temperature sensing/control.
  4. Photoresistors (Light Dependent Resistors - LDRs): Resistance changes with the intensity of incident light. Used in light-sensing circuits.

(a) (ii) Two disadvantages of DRAM (Dynamic RAM):

  1. Volatility: DRAM requires constant power and periodic refreshing (rewriting the stored charge) to maintain its data. If power is lost or refreshing stops, the stored information disappears.
  2. Slower Speed compared to SRAM: The need for refreshing and the architecture of DRAM cells (capacitor and transistor) make its access times slower than Static RAM (SRAM), which uses flip-flops and doesn't require refreshing.

Question 14 (15 marks)

(a) (i) Outline three disadvantages of using floppy disks.

(ii) Figure 2 is a closed circuit with three capacitors C1 (40F), C2 (20F), C3 (10F) and voltage of 50 V. Use it to answer the question that follows.

Determine voltage across each capacitor.

Answer

Disclaimer: Answers are AI-generated and may contain errors. Verify independently.

(a) (i) Three disadvantages of using floppy disks:

  1. Low Storage Capacity: Compared to modern storage media (USB drives, hard drives, cloud storage), floppy disks hold very small amounts of data (typically 1.44 MB).
  2. Slow Data Transfer Speed: Reading from and writing to floppy disks is significantly slower than other storage methods.
  3. Lack of Reliability / Durability: The magnetic media is susceptible to damage from magnetic fields, dust, physical handling, and degradation over time, leading to data loss. The drives themselves were also prone to mechanical failure.

(a) (ii) Voltage across capacitors in series (Figure 2):

Capacitors C1 (40F), C2 (20F), C3 (10F) are in series. Total voltage V = 50V.

  1. Calculate the total capacitance ($C_T$): $$ \frac{1}{C_T} = \frac{1}{C_1} + \frac{1}{C_2} + \frac{1}{C_3} = \frac{1}{40} + \frac{1}{20} + \frac{1}{10} $$ $$ \frac{1}{C_T} = \frac{1 + 2 + 4}{40} = \frac{7}{40} $$ $$ C_T = \frac{40}{7} \, F $$
  2. Calculate the total charge (Q) stored in the series circuit (charge is the same on each capacitor in series): $$ Q = C_T \times V = \frac{40}{7} \times 50 = \frac{2000}{7} \, C $$
  3. Calculate the voltage across each capacitor using $V = Q/C$: $$ V_1 = \frac{Q}{C_1} = \frac{2000/7}{40} = \frac{2000}{7 \times 40} = \frac{50}{7} \, V \approx 7.14 \, V $$ $$ V_2 = \frac{Q}{C_2} = \frac{2000/7}{20} = \frac{2000}{7 \times 20} = \frac{100}{7} \, V \approx 14.29 \, V $$ $$ V_3 = \frac{Q}{C_3} = \frac{2000/7}{10} = \frac{2000}{7 \times 10} = \frac{200}{7} \, V \approx 28.57 \, V $$
  4. Check: $V_1 + V_2 + V_3 = \frac{50}{7} + \frac{100}{7} + \frac{200}{7} = \frac{350}{7} = 50 \, V$. (Matches total voltage)

(b) (i) Three factors affecting resistance of a wire conductor:

  1. Length (L): Resistance is directly proportional to the length of the conductor. Longer wire = higher resistance. ($R \propto L$)
  2. Cross-Sectional Area (A): Resistance is inversely proportional to the cross-sectional area of the conductor. Thicker wire (larger area) = lower resistance. ($R \propto 1/A$)
  3. Material (Resistivity, $\rho$): Different materials have different inherent abilities to resist current flow. This property is called resistivity. Materials like copper have low resistivity (good conductors), while materials like nichrome have higher resistivity. ($R \propto \rho$)
  4. Temperature: For most conductors, resistance increases as temperature increases.

Formula: $R = \frac{\rho L}{A}$
