Public Corporations (State Corporations)

Introduction

These are organizations formed by and/or controlled by the government (the government has a controlling interest). This means that the government owns more than 50% shares in the corporation. Where the government has full ownership, the organization is known as a parastatal.

Public corporations are formed to:

  • Perform specific functions on behalf of the government
  • Provide essential services in the public interest
  • Handle services requiring heavy initial capital investment

Examples

  • Kenya Railways Corporation - railway transport
  • Telkom Kenya - telecommunication services
  • Postal Corporation of Kenya
  • Industrial and Commercial Development Corporation (ICDC)
  • Kenya Airways - air transport services

Characteristics

  • Formed by government under existing laws (Act of Parliament)
  • Initial capital provided by government
  • Jointly owned by government and private investors
  • Managed by government-appointed board of directors
  • Have separate legal entity
  • Have limited liability
  • Some operate with profit motive, others without

Formation

The Act of Parliament outlines:

  • Proposed name of the corporation
  • Aims and objectives
  • Goods or services to be produced
  • Location (Area of operation)
  • Appointment of top executives
  • Powers of the Board of Directors
  • Ministry under which it will operate

Management

  • Managed by board of directors appointed by president or relevant minister
  • Chairman and board implement aims and objectives
  • Chairman reports to government through relevant minister
  • Managing director serves as chief executive officer

Sources of Capital

  • Government provided initial capital
  • Sale of shares (for joint ownership)
  • Loans from financial institutions
  • Retained profits
  • Hire purchase arrangements

Advantages

  • Ready availability of initial capital
  • Can provide services at lower prices
  • Benefits from large scale production
  • Monopoly benefits for some corporations
  • Government support during financial difficulties
  • Limited liability protection
  • Access to research and development funding
  • Helps remove foreign economic domination
  • Can afford qualified personnel

Disadvantages

  • Management by political appointees who may lack expertise
  • Government bailouts may lead to higher taxation
  • Monopoly can lead to inefficiency
  • Political interference affects efficiency
  • Slow decision-making process
  • Lack of close supervision due to size
  • Risk of public funds embezzlement
  • May operate in uneconomical areas
  • Subject to diseconomies of scale

Dissolution

Public corporations can only be dissolved by the government due to:

  • Persistent loss making
  • Bankruptcy
  • Change in the act of parliament
  • Privatization
  • Mismanagement
