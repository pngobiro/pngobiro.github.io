Forms of Business Ownership: Co-operatives

Introduction to Co-operatives

A co-operative society is a form of business organization that is owned by and run for the economic welfare of its members. It is a body of persons who have joined together to do collectively what they were previously doing individually for mutual benefit.

Historical Example

In Kenya, the co-operative movement was started by white settlers in 1908 to market their agricultural produce. They knew that they could sell their produce better if they were as a group and not alone.

Principles of Co-operatives

1. Open and Voluntary Membership

Membership is open and voluntary to any person who has attained the age of 18 years. No one should be denied membership due to social, political, tribal or religious differences. A member is also free to leave the society at will.

2. Democratic Administration

The principle is one man one vote. Each member has only one vote irrespective of the number of shares held or how much they buy or sell to the society.

3. Dividend or Repayment

Any profit/surplus made at the end of every financial year should be distributed to the members in relation to their contribution. Part of the profit may be retained to strengthen the financial position.

4. Limited Interest on Share Capital

Little or no interest is paid on share capital contributed to encourage production and savings rather than financial investment habits.

5. Promotion of Education

Co-operative societies should educate their members and staff through seminars, study tours, and open days to enhance quality of decisions.

6. Co-operation with Other Co-operatives

Co-operatives must learn from each other's experience since they have a lot in common. Their co-operation should be extended to local, national and international levels.

Types of Co-operatives

A. By Nature of Activities

1. Producer Co-operatives

An association of producers who have come together to improve production and marketing of their products.

Functions:
  • Obtaining better prices for members' products
  • Providing better storage facilities
  • Providing reliable transport means
  • Providing loans to members
  • Providing grading, packing and processing services
  • Providing farm inputs on credit
  • Educating members on better farming methods

2. Consumer Co-operatives

Formed by consumers to buy goods wholesale and sell to members at existing market prices.

Advantages:
  • Sell quality goods at fair prices
  • Eliminate middlemen
  • Provide credit facilities
  • Pay interest on capital
  • Convenient location for members

3. Savings and Credit Co-operatives (SACCOs)

Formed by employed persons who save part of their monthly salary through check-off system. Members can borrow money for personal projects at lower interest rates than banks.

B. By Level of Operations

1. Primary Co-operatives

Composed of individuals who are either producers, consumers or savers. Operate at village or district levels.

2. Secondary Co-operatives

Usually referred to as unions, composed of primary co-operative societies as members. Found at district or national levels.

Advantages of Co-operatives

  • Collective ownership serves members' interests effectively
  • Limited liability protection
  • Free and voluntary membership
  • Profit sharing through dividends
  • Improved living standards through increased income
  • Access to credit facilities and loans
  • Democratic management
