Business Life Cycle and Challenges

Business Life Cycle

Business life cycle refers to the phases that a business passes from the time the idea is formed in the entrepreneur's mind to the time business rolls and expands or even declines. Many businesses go through six stages in their life, while others may go through five stages.

Stages of Business Life Cycle

1. Idea Generation Stage

This is the preliminary stage where the entrepreneur:

  • Does groundwork to assess venture viability
  • Selects the most viable business idea
  • Decides on business mission, scope and direction
  • Carries out due diligence
  • May incur expenses for legal and professional services

2. Start-up Stage

  • Preparation of formal business plan
  • Business registration
  • Sourcing capital
  • Recruiting staff
  • Product launch and distribution setup

3. Growth Stage

  • Increased sales and profits
  • Wider market coverage
  • Growing number of employees
  • Variety of products/services
  • Increased competition
  • Need for additional expenditure

4. Stabilization Stage

  • Sales and profits stagnate
  • Intensified competition
  • Market saturation by similar products
  • Consumer indifference to products
  • Focus on corporate governance

5. Innovation Stage

Organizations that fail to innovate at stabilization stage are likely to decline. Innovation attempts include:

  • Change of management
  • Re-package products/services
  • Change technology
  • New distribution methods
  • Different advertising and promotion strategies

6. Decline Stage

This stage is not in the normal plan of business. Experiences include:

  • Drastic fall in sales and profits
  • Consumer indifference to products
  • Inability to meet bills/debts
  • Key management staff leaving

Challenges Faced When Starting a Small Business

  • Poor infrastructure facilities including power
  • Deficiency in managerial and technical skills
  • Financial challenges/shortages
  • Gender-based challenges in male-dominated societies
  • Lack of proper planning
  • Government limitations favoring larger enterprises
  • Environmental changes (economic, political, social, technical)
  • Legal requirements and licensing
  • Lack of necessary entrepreneurial skills
  • High level of competition
