Forms of Business Ownership: Sole Proprietorship

Introduction to Sole Proprietorship

This is a business enterprise owned by one person who is called a sole trader or a sole proprietor. It is the most common form of business unit and usually found in retail trade e.g. in small shops, kiosks, agriculture etc. and for direct services e.g. cobblers, saloons etc.

Characteristics/Features

  • The business is owned by one person
  • The capital is contributed by the owner and is usually small
  • The owner enjoys all the profits alone and also suffers the losses alone
  • The owner is personally responsible for the management of the business
  • Sometimes assisted by members of family or a few employees
  • The sole proprietor has unlimited liability
  • There are very few legal requirements to start the business unit
  • Sole proprietorship is flexible - easy to change location or nature of business

Formation and Capital Sources

Formation

The formation of a sole proprietorship is very simple. Few legal formalities are required i.e. to start a sole proprietorship, one need only to raise the capital required and then apply for a trading license to operate the business. A small fee is paid and the trade license issued.

Sources of Capital

  • Owner's savings (main source)
  • Borrowing from friends, banks and other money lending institutions
  • Inheritance
  • Getting goods on credit
  • Getting goods on hire purchase
  • Leasing or renting out one's properties
  • Donations from friends and relatives
  • Ploughing back profit

Advantages

  1. Small capital requirement - anybody who can spare small amounts of money can start one
  2. Few formal/legal procedures required to set up
  3. Fast decision making and implementation - no consultation needed
  4. Close and personal contact with customers - helps in understanding customer needs
  5. Better assessment of customer credit-worthiness due to personal relationships
  6. Accountable only to oneself
  7. Ability to keep business secrets
  8. Enjoys all profits alone
  9. Flexible in changing nature or location of business

Disadvantages

  1. Unlimited liability - personal property at risk for business debts
  2. Insufficient capital for expansion
  3. Owner is overworked with no time for recreation
  4. Lack of continuity - business affected by owner's sickness or death
  5. Cannot benefit from economies of large scale
  6. Lack of specialization may lead to poor performance
  7. Cannot attract and retain highly qualified personnel

Dissolution

Dissolution refers to the termination of the legal life of a business. The following circumstances may lead to the dissolution of a sole proprietorship:

  • Death or insanity of the owner
  • Transfer of the business to another person
  • Bankruptcy of the owner
  • Voluntary decision to dissolve due to continued losses
  • Passing of a law which renders activities illegal
  • Expiry of the intended operation period
