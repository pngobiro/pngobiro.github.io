SECTION A: MATERIALS, PROCESSES AND WORKSHOP TECHNOLOGY

Answer THREE questions from this section.

Question 1

(a) Explain one way each of the following can contribute to accidents in the workshop:

(i) carelessness;

(ii) ignorance;

(iii) unsuitable clothings.

(b) (i) Explain the term 'workshop layout'.

(ii) List four factors in workshop layout.

(c) Outline the first aid procedure for treatment of burns and scalds.

Answer

This question deals with causes of workshop accidents, principles of workshop layout, and first aid for burns.

(a) Contributions to Accidents:

  • (i) Carelessness: Rushing work, not paying attention to the task, taking shortcuts, failing to use safety guards, or general lack of focus can lead to mistakes like mishandling tools, incorrect machine operation, or ignoring hazards, directly causing injury or damage. For example, not looking where one is walking can lead to tripping over an obstacle.
  • (ii) Ignorance: Lack of knowledge about the correct operating procedures for machinery, the properties of materials being handled, or the specific hazards involved in a process can lead to unintentional unsafe actions. For example, attempting to operate a complex machine without proper training might lead to incorrect setup and an accident.
  • (iii) Unsuitable Clothing: Wearing loose clothing (baggy sleeves, ties), jewelry (rings, necklaces), or having long, untied hair can lead to entanglement in rotating machinery parts (like lathes, drills), pulling the person into the machine and causing severe injury. Inappropriate footwear (sandals, high heels) can lead to slips, trips, or foot injuries.

(b) Workshop Layout:

  • (i) Workshop Layout Explained: The physical arrangement and positioning of machinery, equipment, workstations, storage areas, aisles, offices, and service facilities within a workshop space. The goal is to optimize efficiency, safety, workflow, material handling, and supervision while considering space utilization and flexibility.
  • (ii) Factors in Workshop Layout (Four): (Repeated from Nov 2017 Q1a)
    1. Workflow/Process Sequence: Logical arrangement according to production steps.
    2. Space Requirements: Adequate space for operation, maintenance, storage, movement.
    3. Safety and Health: Hazard minimization, clear exits, lighting, ventilation.
    4. Material Handling: Efficient movement of materials and components.
    5. Flexibility/Future Expansion: Adaptability to changes.
    6. Utilities Access: Power, air, water requirements.
    7. Supervision and Communication: Visibility for supervisors, ease of communication.
    8. Employee Comfort/Welfare: Rest areas, washrooms, ergonomics.

(c) First Aid for Burns and Scalds:

(Repeated from Nov 2021 Q1c)

  1. Safety First: Ensure the cause of the burn is removed/neutralized (stop the burning).
  2. Cool Immediately: Apply cool (not ice-cold) running water for 10-20 minutes.
  3. Remove Constrictions: Gently remove tight clothing, rings, watches before swelling occurs (unless stuck to the burn).
  4. Cover Loosely: Use a sterile, non-fluffy dressing (e.g., gauze, cling film).
  5. Keep Warm: Prevent hypothermia by keeping the casualty warm (blanket), but don't put blankets on the burn.
  6. Seek Medical Help: Get professional medical advice for anything more than a minor superficial burn, or for burns in sensitive areas, electrical/chemical burns, large burns, or burns in infants/elderly.

Understanding how carelessness, ignorance, and improper attire cause accidents is key to prevention. Effective workshop layout enhances safety and efficiency. Prompt and correct first aid for burns minimizes damage.

Question 2

(a) List four physical properties of engineering materials.

(b) Define each of the following mechanical properties of engineering materials:

(i) toughness;

(ii) elasticity;

(iii) hardness.

(c) With aid of a diagram, describe the production of pig iron.

Answer

This question covers physical and mechanical properties of materials and the production of pig iron in a blast furnace.

(a) Physical Properties (Four):

(Repeated from July 2020 Q2a)

  • Density
  • Melting Point / Boiling Point
  • Thermal Conductivity
  • Electrical Conductivity / Resistivity
  • Coefficient of Thermal Expansion
  • Specific Heat Capacity
  • Colour / Lustre
  • Magnetic Properties

(b) Mechanical Properties Definitions:

  • (i) Toughness: (Repeated from Nov 2018 Q2a, July 2014 Q2a, July 2015 Q1c) A material's ability to absorb energy and deform plastically without fracturing, particularly under impact loading. It is a measure of resistance to fracture.
  • (ii) Elasticity: The ability of a material to deform under an applied load (stress) and return completely to its original shape and size once the load is removed. If deformation persists after load removal, it's plastic deformation.
  • (iii) Hardness: (Repeated from Nov 2016 Q1a, July 2015 Q1c, July 2014 Q2a) A material's resistance to localized surface deformation, such as scratching, abrasion, or indentation.

(c) Production of Pig Iron (Blast Furnace):

(Repeated from July 2020 Q2b, Nov 2018 Q2d, July 2017 Q4c)

Pig iron is the intermediate product from smelting iron ore in a blast furnace. It has a high carbon content (around 4%).

Description:

  1. Charge: Iron ore (e.g., Fe$_2$O$_3$), Coke (C), Limestone (CaCO$_3$) loaded at the top.
  2. Blast: Hot air blown in at the bottom (tuyeres).
  3. Reactions:
    • Coke burns: $C + O_2 \rightarrow CO_2$; $CO_2 + C \rightarrow 2CO$ (Heat + Reducing Agent)
    • Ore Reduction: $Fe_2O_3 \xrightarrow{CO} Fe_3O_4 \xrightarrow{CO} FeO \xrightarrow{CO/C} Fe$ (Molten Iron)
    • Flux Action: $CaCO_3 \rightarrow CaO + CO_2$; $CaO + SiO_2 \rightarrow CaSiO_3$ (Molten Slag)
  4. Collection: Molten pig iron and lighter molten slag collect in the hearth and are tapped separately.
  5. Gases: Waste gases exit at the top.

Defines key physical and mechanical properties, and describes the blast furnace process used to produce pig iron from iron ore.

Question 3

(a) (i) Explain the term 'datum' in reference to marking out.

(ii) Illustrate the marking out of a plate work piece using a scribing block and an angle plate.

(b) List three parts of a vernier caliper.

(c) Illustrate the following methods of filing in reference to hand tools:

(i) cross filing;

(ii) straight filing;

(iii) draw filing.

(d) (i) List three types of engineer's hammers.

(ii) State one safety precaution in use of engineer's hammers.

Answer

This question covers marking out principles and tools, vernier caliper parts, filing techniques, and types/safety of hammers.

(a) Datum and Marking Out Illustration:

  • (i) Datum Explained: (Repeated from July 2020 Q3a) A datum is a theoretically exact reference point, line, plane, or surface from which measurements are made and dimensions are established during marking out or dimensioning. It acts as a stable origin to ensure accuracy and consistency.
  • (ii) Illustration (Scribing Block & Angle Plate):

    Requires a sketch showing the setup on a surface plate.

    • A surface plate provides the flat reference base (datum plane).
    • An angle plate (L-shaped) is placed on the surface plate.
    • The plate workpiece is clamped securely to the vertical face of the angle plate, ensuring one edge rests firmly on the surface plate (this edge becomes a datum edge).
    • A scribing block (or surface gauge / vernier height gauge) with its scriber point set to the desired height rests on the surface plate.
    • The scribing block is slid along the surface plate, allowing its scriber point to mark a precise horizontal line (parallel to the surface plate datum) onto the face of the workpiece held by the angle plate.

(b) Parts of a Vernier Caliper (Three):

(Repeated from Nov 2021 Q2a)

  • Fixed Jaw
  • Moving Jaw
  • Main Scale (on body)
  • Vernier Scale (on sliding jaw)
  • Internal Jaws
  • Depth Rod
  • Locking Screw / Thumb Clamp

(c) Filing Methods Illustrated:

Requires sketches showing file movement relative to workpiece.

  • (i) Cross Filing: Sketch showing the file pushed diagonally across the work surface (e.g., 45Â°), then on the next pass, diagonally in the opposite direction, creating a criss-cross pattern. Used for rapid removal and flattening.
  • (ii) Straight Filing: Sketch showing the file pushed straight forward along the length (or width) of the work surface. Often follows cross filing.
  • (iii) Draw Filing: (Repeated from Nov 2016 Q1c) Sketch showing the file held perpendicular to the direction of movement, grasped at both ends, and pushed/pulled along the work length. Used for fine finishing and straightening edges.

(d) Engineer's Hammers and Safety:

  • (i) Types of Engineer's Hammers (Three):
    1. Ball Pein Hammer: Has one flat face (for general striking) and one rounded (ball) end (for riveting, shaping metal). Most common type.
    2. Cross Pein Hammer: Has one flat face and one wedge-shaped pein oriented perpendicular (crosswise) to the handle axis. Used for starting nails, shaping metal grooves.
    3. Straight Pein Hammer: Has one flat face and one wedge-shaped pein oriented parallel to the handle axis. Used for shaping and riveting in restricted areas.
    4. Club Hammer (Lump Hammer): Short handle, heavy double-faced head. Used for heavy blows, often with chisels or stakes.
    5. Soft-Faced Hammer/Mallet: Heads made of wood, plastic, rubber, copper, lead etc. Used to strike materials without causing damage.
  • (ii) Safety Precaution with Hammers (One):
    • Ensure the hammer head is securely fixed to the handle (check wedge).
    • Wear safety glasses to protect eyes from flying debris or chips.
    • Keep the striking face clean and free from oil/grease.
    • Ensure a firm grip on the handle.
    • Strike the object squarely; avoid glancing blows.
    • Do not use a hammer with a damaged handle (cracked, split) or a damaged head (chipped, mushroomed).

Covers marking out concepts/tools, vernier parts, filing methods, and types/safe use of hammers.

Question 4

(a) (i) Distinguish between permanent and temporary joints in mechanical joining of metals, giving one example in each case.

(ii) List four tools and equipment used in soft soldering.

(b) Sketch two surfaces or work produced by a shaping machine.

(c) Explain the following parts of a drilling machine:

(i) spindle;

(ii) base;

(iii) head.

Answer

This question covers types of joints, soldering tools, shaping machine applications, and drilling machine components.

(a) Joints and Soldering Tools:

  • (i) Permanent vs. Temporary Joints Distinction:
    • Permanent Joints: Designed not to be disassembled without destroying the fastener or the components themselves. They create a fixed connection.
      Example: Welded joints, Riveted joints, Brazed/Soldered joints (considered permanent in most structural contexts), Adhesive joints (strong structural adhesives).
    • Temporary Joints (Demountable): Designed to allow for easy disassembly and reassembly without damaging the components or fasteners.
      Example: Bolted joints (using nuts and bolts), Screwed joints, Joints using pins and cotters, Clamped joints.

    Distinction is based on the intent and ease of disassembly without damage.

  • (ii) Tools and Equipment for Soft Soldering (Four):
    1. Soldering Iron / Soldering Gun: Heat source to melt the solder and heat the joint.
    2. Solder Wire: Filler metal alloy (typically tin-based, lead-free or tin-lead) often with a flux core.
    3. Flux: Chemical agent (paste, liquid, or core in solder wire) to clean surfaces and aid wetting.
    4. Cleaning Tools: Wire brush, abrasive pad, solvent for cleaning components before soldering.
    5. Holding Devices: Vice, clamps, 'helping hands' to hold components steady during soldering.
    6. Tip Cleaner / Wet Sponge: For cleaning the soldering iron tip during use.
    7. Safety Equipment: Safety glasses, fume extractor (optional but recommended).

(b) Surfaces Produced by Shaping Machine (Sketches):

A shaping machine uses a reciprocating single-point tool to produce flat or profiled surfaces. Requires sketches of typical workpieces.

  • Sketch 1: Flat Horizontal Surface: Show a rectangular block workpiece held in the shaper vice. Indicate the cutting tool moving back and forth across the top surface, removing material to make it flat. Show the cross-feed direction of the table.
  • Sketch 2: Vertical Surface / Shoulder: Show the workpiece positioned so the tool cuts a vertical face or creates a square shoulder (step). The tool head might be swiveled slightly for clearance.
  • Sketch 3: Slot / Groove: Show the tool cutting a rectangular groove (e.g., a keyway) along the top surface of the workpiece.
  • Sketch 4: Dovetail Slide: Show the tool head swiveled and the tool ground to shape to cut an angled dovetail groove or slide.

(c) Drilling Machine Parts Explained:

  • (i) Spindle: (Repeated from Nov 2021 Q2d) The rotating shaft that holds the drill chuck (or directly mounts tapered shank drills). It transmits rotation from the motor and provides the axial feed movement for the cutting tool.
  • (ii) Base: (Repeated from Nov 2015 Q4b) The heavy, bottom-most part of the drill press that provides stability and support for the entire machine. It usually has slots or holes for mounting the machine to a bench or floor.
  • (iii) Head: The upper part of the drill press, mounted on top of the column. It houses the motor, drive mechanism (belts/pulleys or gears), spindle assembly, feed mechanism, and controls (switch, speed change levers).

Distinguishes between joint types, lists soldering essentials, illustrates shaping machine capabilities, and explains the function of key drilling machine components.

Question 5 (20 marks)

(a) (i) Construct a rectangle whose diagonal is 85 mm long and one side 35 mm long.

(ii) Construct a square side 100 mm. Within the square, construct a regular octagon. (12 marks)

(b) Construct a triangle with a perimeter of 115 mm whose sides are in the ratio of 2:4:5. (8 marks)

Answer

This question involves geometric constructions: a rectangle from diagonal and side, an octagon within a square, and a triangle from perimeter and side ratio.

(a) Rectangle and Octagon Constructions:

  • (i) Rectangle Construction (Diagonal=85mm, Side=35mm):
    1. Draw line segment AB = 35 mm (one side).
    2. Construct a perpendicular to AB at point B.
    3. Set compass radius to 85 mm (diagonal length). Place point at A and draw an arc intersecting the perpendicular line constructed in step 2. Label the intersection point C. (AC is the diagonal).
    4. Set compass radius to AB (35 mm). Place point at C and draw an arc.
    5. Set compass radius to BC (length found by measurement or Pythagoras: $BC = \sqrt{AC^2 - AB^2} = \sqrt{85^2 - 35^2} = \sqrt{7225 - 1225} = \sqrt{6000} \approx 77.46$ mm). Place point at A and draw an arc intersecting the arc from step 4. Label this intersection point D.
    6. Join C-D and A-D to complete the rectangle ABCD.
  • (ii) Octagon in Square (Side=100mm): (Repeated from July 2015 Q7c)
    1. Draw the square ABCD with side 100 mm.
    2. Draw diagonals AC and BD intersecting at center O.
    3. Set compass radius to AO (half diagonal = $100\sqrt{2}/2 \approx 70.7$ mm).
    4. Place compass point at each corner (A, B, C, D) and draw arcs cutting the adjacent sides of the square. Each side will be cut at two points.
    5. Connect the eight intersection points consecutively with straight lines to form the regular octagon inscribed within the square.

(b) Triangle Construction (Perimeter=115mm, Ratio 2:4:5):

  1. Calculate Side Lengths:
    • Total ratio parts = 2 + 4 + 5 = 11.
    • Length per part = Perimeter / Total parts = 115 mm / 11 $\approx$ 10.45 mm.
    • Side a = 2 parts * 10.45 mm/part $\approx$ 20.9 mm
    • Side b = 4 parts * 10.45 mm/part $\approx$ 41.8 mm
    • Side c = 5 parts * 10.45 mm/part $\approx$ 52.3 mm
    • (Check: 20.9 + 41.8 + 52.3 = 115 mm. Check inequality: 20.9+41.8 > 52.3 (62.7>52.3), ok).

    Alternatively, a construction method avoids calculation.

  2. Construction Method (Using Perimeter Line):
    1. Draw a line segment XY = 115 mm (the perimeter).
    2. Divide XY into the ratio 2:4:5. Mark points P and Q such that XP:PQ:QY = 2:4:5. (Use geometric division of a line). XP = 2/11 * 115 $\approx$ 20.9mm, PQ = 4/11 * 115 $\approx$ 41.8mm, QY = 5/11 * 115 $\approx$ 52.3mm.
    3. Set compass radius to XP. Place point at P, draw an arc above XY.
    4. Set compass radius to QY. Place point at Q, draw another arc intersecting the first arc at point R.
    5. Join P-R and Q-R. Triangle PQR has sides PR=XP, QR=QY, PQ. Wait, this doesn't use PQ as a side. This method is wrong.
  3. Construction Method 2 (Standard Triangle):
    1. Draw base side c $\approx$ 52.3 mm (Label endpoints P, Q).
    2. Set compass radius to side b $\approx$ 41.8 mm. Place point at P, draw an arc.
    3. Set compass radius to side a $\approx$ 20.9 mm. Place point at Q, draw an arc intersecting the first arc at R.
    4. Join P-R and Q-R to form the required triangle.

Requires accurate geometric construction techniques for rectangles, octagons within squares, and triangles given perimeter and side ratios.

Question 6 (20 marks)

Figure 1 shows a front elevation and plan of a block drawn in first angle projection. Construct its isometric view taking part marked C as the lowest point.

Answer

This question requires constructing an isometric view from given first angle orthographic projections (Front Elevation and Plan, Figure 1), ensuring corner C is the lowest point.

Understanding First Angle Projection:

  • Plan (Top View) is BELOW the Front Elevation.
  • Front Elevation shows height and width.
  • Plan shows width and depth.

Analysis of Figure 1 (Block):

  • Identify the Front Elevation and the Plan view below it.
  • Determine the object's shape: A base block with a complex upper structure, possibly including angled faces, steps, or cutouts.
  • Locate corner C on the views (likely a front-bottom corner).
  • Extract Width, Height, and Depth dimensions from the views.

Isometric Construction Steps:

  1. Orient Axes: Set up isometric axes originating from corner C as the lowest point. Determine Width, Depth, Height directions based on the orthographic views relative to C.
  2. Bounding Box (Optional): Draw a light isometric box using overall dimensions.
  3. Construct Base/Features: Start drawing from C, laying out the base footprint and building upwards. Use dimensions from the Plan (for width/depth) and Front Elevation (for height). Transfer all features like steps, angles, curves accurately onto the isometric planes.
  4. Circles/Curves: Draw any circular features as ellipses on the appropriate isometric planes.
  5. Refine: Complete the outline, darken visible lines, erase construction lines. Omit hidden lines.

Requires accurate interpretation of First Angle orthographic views and careful construction of the isometric projection with the specified orientation (C lowest).

Question 7 (20 marks)

Figure 2 shows a plan and elevation of a solid square section with a square hole running through the centre. Using the figure, draw the following types of sections:

(a) full section,

(b) half section,

(c) part (local) section;

(d) revolved section,

(e) removed section.

Answer

This question requires illustrating various types of sectional views based on the given orthographic views of a square block with a central square hole (Figure 2).

Analysis of Figure 2: Shows a simple square prism with a concentric square hole passing through it.

Types of Sections Explained and Illustrated:

  1. (a) Full Section:
    • Explanation: Assumes a cutting plane passes fully through the object (typically along a centre line). The view shows the object as if the portion in front of the cutting plane were removed. Cut surfaces are hatched. Hidden details behind the cut plane are usually omitted.
    • Illustration: Draw the elevation (or plan). Indicate a cutting plane line passing fully through the center (e.g., A-A). Draw the sectional view (e.g., Section A-A). Show the outer square boundary, the inner square hole boundary, and hatch the solid material between the outer and inner squares.
  2. (b) Half Section:
    • Explanation: Used primarily for symmetrical objects. Two cutting planes at right angles pass halfway through the object (meeting at the axis of symmetry). One quarter of the object is imagined removed. The view shows half as an external view and half as a section view, separated by a centre line.
    • Illustration: Draw the elevation. Show half (e.g., right side) as a normal external view (including hidden lines for the hole if needed). Show the other half (e.g., left side) as a section view (hatching the cut material). Use a centre line to divide the two halves.
  3. (c) Part (Local/Broken-Out) Section:
    • Explanation: Used to show internal detail in a small specific area without drawing a full or half section. A freehand break line encloses the area of interest, and the material within the break line is shown sectioned (hatched).
    • Illustration: Draw the normal elevation. Draw a freehand break line around a portion where the hole is. Hatch the solid material within the break line to reveal the edge of the hole locally.
  4. (d) Revolved Section:
    • Explanation: Shows the cross-sectional shape of an elongated feature (like an arm, rib, or spoke) directly on the main view. The cross-section is imagined to be revolved 90 degrees and drawn in place, often superimposed on the feature with a break around it.
    • Illustration: Draw the elevation (or plan). Imagine cutting through the square block perpendicular to its axis. Revolve this square cross-section (with the square hole) by 90 degrees and draw it directly on the view, perhaps centered on the axis line. Use thin lines for the section outline.
  5. (e) Removed Section:
    • Explanation: Similar to a revolved section in showing cross-sectional shape, but the section view is drawn *off* the main view, usually nearby and connected by a centre line or designated by cutting plane lines and labels (e.g., Section B-B). Allows for clearer viewing and dimensioning of the section.
    • Illustration: Draw the elevation (or plan). Indicate a cutting plane line (e.g., B-B) where the section is taken. Draw the cross-sectional shape (square with square hole) separately nearby, labeling it "SECTION B-B". Hatch the solid material.

Requires understanding and ability to draw various standard conventions for representing internal features using different types of sectional views.

Question 8 (20 marks)

Figure 3 shows a pictorial view of an object. Draw the following using third angle orthographic projection:

(a) a front elevation in the direction of arrow A;

(b) an end elevation;

(c) a plan.

Insert at least six necessary dimensions.

Answer

This question requires creating standard orthographic views (Front Elevation, End Elevation, Plan) in Third Angle Projection from the provided pictorial view (Figure 3), including dimensions and potentially the projection symbol.

Understanding Third Angle Projection:

  • Plan (Top View) is ABOVE the Front Elevation.
  • End Elevation (assume Right End View if not specified) is drawn to the RIGHT of the Front Elevation.

Analysis of Figure 3:

  • The object has a base, an upright section with a large hole/bore, and possibly angled surfaces or ribs.
  • Arrow A indicates the direction for the Front Elevation.
  • Dimensions need to be extracted from the pictorial view.

Required Views:

  • (a) Front Elevation (View A): Shows the view looking along arrow A. Will display height and width, including the profile of the upright section and the hole.
  • (b) End Elevation (Right): Drawn to the RIGHT of the Front Elevation. Shows height and depth, including the profile of the base and upright section from the side. Hidden details (like the hole) shown dashed.
  • (c) Plan (Top View): Drawn ABOVE the Front Elevation. Shows width and depth, including the shape of the base and the top view of the upright section and hole.

Procedure:

  1. Set up drawing sheet for Third Angle Projection.
  2. Draw the Front Elevation (A) accurately, transferring dimensions. Include hidden lines.
  3. Project lines vertically upwards from the Front Elevation for the Plan. Draw the Plan view above, aligning widths and using depth dimensions. Show top features and hidden lines.
  4. Project lines horizontally to the right from the Front Elevation for the Right End Elevation. Draw the End Elevation to the right, aligning heights and using depth dimensions (consistent with Plan). Show hidden lines.
  5. Add the Third Angle projection symbol.
  6. Add at least six necessary dimensions across the views.

Requires accurate orthographic drafting in Third Angle Projection, interpreting the pictorial view, showing hidden lines correctly, and applying standard dimensioning.
