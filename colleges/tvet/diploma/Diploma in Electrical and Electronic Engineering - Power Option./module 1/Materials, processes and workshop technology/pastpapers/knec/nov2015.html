Question 1 (20 marks)

(a) State three protective measures to be observed with regard to workshop clothing when using electrical machines. (3 marks)

(b) (i) Explain four classes of fire.

(ii) State the type of fire extinguisher for each class of fire in b(i). (8 marks)

(c) Explain how the following factors contribute to accidents in the workshop:

(i) human;

(ii) environment. (4 marks)

(d) Outline the procedure for administering mouth to mouth resuscitation to an unconscious electric shock victim. (5 marks)

Answer

This question covers workshop safety regarding clothing, fire classification and extinguishing, causes of accidents, and first aid for electric shock.

(a) Protective Measures for Clothing with Electrical Machines:

  • Avoid Loose Clothing: Do not wear baggy sleeves, ties, scarves, or unbuttoned shirts that could get caught in rotating parts of the machine (e.g., chucks, spindles, belts).
  • Secure Long Hair and Remove Jewelry: Tie back long hair securely. Remove rings, watches, necklaces, and bracelets that could conduct electricity or get entangled in machinery.
  • Wear Appropriate Footwear: Wear sturdy, closed-toe shoes, preferably with non-slip soles and electrical insulation properties (safety boots often recommended). Avoid sandals or open shoes.
  • Use Provided PPE: Wear any specific PPE required for the machine, like safety glasses or face shields, which although not clothing, are part of personal protection. Ensure overalls or aprons are properly fitted.

(b) Fire Classes and Extinguishers:

  • (i) Four Classes of Fire Explained:
    • Class A: Fires involving ordinary combustible solids like wood, paper, cloth, rubber, and many plastics. They typically leave ash.
    • Class B: Fires involving flammable liquids (like petrol, oil, paint, grease) or liquefiable solids.
    • Class C: Fires involving flammable gases (like propane, butane, natural gas).
    • Class F (or K in US): Fires involving cooking oils and fats in cooking appliances. (Note: Older systems might not include Class F/K explicitly).
    • Electrical Fires: Not a separate class, but indicates the *presence* of live electrical equipment. Extinguishers suitable for electrical fires must be non-conductive.
  • (ii) Extinguisher Types for Classes:
    • Class A: Water, Foam, Dry Powder (ABC), Wet Chemical.
    • Class B: Foam, Carbon Dioxide (CO2), Dry Powder (ABC, BC).
    • Class C: Dry Powder (ABC, BC). (Important: Isolate gas supply first if possible).
    • Class F/K: Wet Chemical.
    • Electrical Fires: Carbon Dioxide (CO2), Dry Powder (ABC, BC). **NEVER use Water or standard Foam.**

(c) Factors Contributing to Accidents:

  • (i) Human Factors:
    • Carelessness/Lack of Concentration: Daydreaming, rushing, taking shortcuts, not paying attention to the task.
    • Lack of Training/Skill/Knowledge: Improper use of tools or machines, unawareness of hazards.
    • Fatigue/Illness/Stress: Reduced alertness, poor judgment, slower reaction times.
    • Poor Attitude/Horseplay: Disregard for safety rules, deliberate risk-taking, distracting others.
    • Influence of Drugs/Alcohol: Impaired judgment and coordination.
  • (ii) Environment Factors:
    • Poor Housekeeping: Cluttered walkways, spills on floors, improperly stored materials leading to trips, slips, falls, or fire hazards.
    • Inadequate Lighting: Difficulty seeing hazards or performing tasks accurately.
    • Poor Ventilation: Build-up of harmful fumes, dust, or vapors.
    • Excessive Noise: Can be distracting, mask warning sounds, and cause long-term hearing damage.
    • Unsafe Equipment/Machinery: Lack of guards, faulty wiring, poorly maintained tools.
    • Extreme Temperatures: Can cause fatigue or discomfort, affecting concentration.

(d) Mouth-to-Mouth Resuscitation Procedure:

(Repeated from Q3a(ii) of 2010 paper)

Modern guidelines prioritize chest compressions (CPR) and calling emergency services. This describes the traditional method as likely expected by the question.

  1. Safety First: Ensure the area and victim are safe from the electrical source (turn off power or remove victim using non-conductive object).
  2. Check Response & Breathing: Check if the victim is conscious and breathing normally. Call for emergency help.
  3. Position Victim: Lay the victim flat on their back on a firm surface.
  4. Open Airway: Use the head-tilt/chin-lift maneuver.
  5. Give Breaths:
    • Pinch the victim's nose.
    • Take a normal breath and seal your mouth over the victim's mouth.
    • Blow steadily for about 1 second, watching for chest rise.
    • Allow the chest to fall.
    • Give a second breath.
  6. Continue: If the victim has a pulse but isn't breathing, continue breaths (approx. 1 breath every 5-6 seconds) until help arrives or breathing resumes. (If no pulse, CPR combining chest compressions and breaths is required).

Workshop safety involves appropriate clothing, understanding fire types and extinguishers, recognizing human and environmental accident causes, and knowing basic first aid like resuscitation.

Question 2 (20 marks)

(a) Explain the following properties of engineering materials:

(i) ductility;

(ii) conductivity.

(b) Describe the following finishing and decorative processes for engineering materials:

(i) electroplating;

(ii) enamelling.

(c) Outline the procedure for bending a metal rod in forging.

(d) Draw a labelled diagram of an anvil used in forging.

Answer

This question covers material properties, finishing processes, and basic forging operations and tools.

(a) Material Properties Explained:

  • (i) Ductility: The ability of a material to deform plastically under tensile stress without fracturing. It is often characterized by the material's ability to be stretched or drawn into a thin wire. Materials like copper and mild steel are highly ductile.
  • (ii) Conductivity: The ability of a material to conduct energy. This usually refers to:
    • Electrical Conductivity: The measure of how easily electric current flows through a material (reciprocal of resistivity). Metals like silver, copper, and aluminium are good electrical conductors.
    • Thermal Conductivity: The measure of how easily heat energy flows through a material. Metals are generally good thermal conductors, while materials like plastic, wood, and ceramics are thermal insulators.
    (The context usually implies electrical conductivity unless otherwise specified).

(b) Finishing and Decorative Processes:

  • (i) Electroplating: (Repeated from Q2d of July 2014 & Q3b(i) of 2010) A process using electrolysis to deposit a thin layer of one metal (e.g., chromium, nickel, zinc, gold) onto the surface of another object (usually metallic) to improve appearance, corrosion resistance, hardness, or conductivity. The object acts as the cathode, an anode (often of the plating metal) is used, and both are immersed in an electrolyte solution containing ions of the plating metal while a DC current is passed.
  • (ii) Enamelling (Vitreous Enamelling): A process where a glassy coating (enamel frit - powdered glass mixed with pigments and fluxes) is applied to a metal surface (typically steel, cast iron, aluminium, or copper) and fused to it by firing at high temperatures (typically 750-850°C). This creates a hard, smooth, durable, corrosion-resistant, and often decorative surface. Used for cookware, appliances, signage, jewelry, and architectural panels.

(c) Procedure for Bending a Metal Rod in Forging:

Forging involves shaping metal, usually heated, using compressive forces (hammering, pressing).

  1. Heating: Heat the section of the metal rod to be bent to the appropriate forging temperature (e.g., bright red/orange heat for steel) in a forge or furnace. This makes the metal plastic and easier to deform.
  2. Positioning: Place the heated rod securely on an anvil or a suitable bending jig/tool. Position it so the force can be applied effectively to create the desired bend.
  3. Applying Force: Use a hammer (e.g., sledgehammer or hand hammer depending on rod size) or a bending tool/press to apply force to the rod. For a simple bend over the anvil's edge (horn or heel), strike the overhanging portion downwards. For sharper bends or specific shapes, specialized bending forks or jigs might be used.
  4. Shaping: Apply controlled blows or pressure, gradually forming the bend. Reheat the rod as necessary if it cools too much before the bend is complete. Check the angle or shape against a template or drawing.
  5. Finishing/Cooling: Once the desired bend is achieved, the rod can be finished (e.g., smoothed, straightened) if needed and then allowed to cool (air cooling or quenching, depending on the material and desired properties).

(d) Labelled Diagram of an Anvil:

Requires a sketch showing the main parts of a blacksmith's anvil.

  • Labels should include:
    • Face: The main flat, hardened top working surface.
    • Horn (or Bick): The conical tapered end used for shaping curves, rings, and scrolls.
    • Heel: The flat, often unhardened, square end opposite the horn.
    • Step: A small step down between the face and the heel, sometimes used for cutting.
    • Hardie Hole: A square hole in the heel used to hold various anvil tools (hardies) like cutting or shaping stakes.
    • Pritchel Hole: A smaller round hole near the Hardie hole, used for punching holes in metal (allows the punch to pass through).
    • Body/Waist: The main mass of the anvil below the face.
    • Feet/Base: Provides stability, often with holes for bolting down.

The answer defines ductility and conductivity, describes electroplating and enamelling, outlines the forging process for bending, and requires a labelled sketch of an anvil.

Question 3 (20 marks)

(a) (i) State two applications of mechanical fasteners.

(ii) Explain four differences between soldering and brazing.

(b) Draw part of a vernier scale to show a reading of 25.44 mm.

(c) (i) Draw a labelled diagram of a flat file.

(ii) State four ways of taking care of files.

Answer

This question covers mechanical fasteners, compares soldering and brazing, and deals with vernier scale reading and the use/care of files.

(a) Fasteners and Joining Methods:

  • (i) Applications of Mechanical Fasteners (Any Two): Mechanical fasteners (like bolts, nuts, screws, rivets, pins, clips) are used to:
    • Assemble components together in machines, structures, and products (e.g., bolting engine parts, screwing furniture together).
    • Allow for disassembly and reassembly of parts for maintenance or repair.
    • Join dissimilar materials where welding or adhesives might be unsuitable.
    • Provide temporary or permanent joints in construction and manufacturing.
  • (ii) Differences Between Soldering and Brazing (Four Differences):
    1. Temperature: Soldering uses filler metals (solders) that melt below 450°C, while brazing uses filler metals (brazing alloys) that melt above 450°C (but still below the melting point of the base metals).
    2. Filler Metal Composition: Solders are typically tin-lead, tin-silver, tin-copper alloys. Brazing alloys are often copper-based (like brass - copper-zinc), silver-based, or aluminium-based alloys.
    3. Joint Strength: Brazed joints are generally much stronger than soldered joints due to the higher melting point alloys and better diffusion into the base metal.
    4. Capillary Action: Both rely on capillary action, but the mechanisms and joint clearances can differ. Brazing typically requires tighter clearances for optimal capillary flow and strength compared to general soldering.
    5. Heat Source: Soldering often uses soldering irons, torches, or ovens. Brazing requires higher temperatures, typically using torches (oxy-fuel), furnaces, or induction heating.
    6. Applications: Soldering is common for electronics, plumbing (copper pipes with specific solders), and sheet metal seams. Brazing is used for stronger joints in plumbing, refrigeration, tool manufacturing (carbide tips), and structural components.

(b) Vernier Scale Reading (25.44 mm):

Assumes a standard metric vernier caliper with a least count of 0.02 mm (common type).

  • Main Scale Reading: Find the largest whole millimeter mark on the main scale that is visible to the left of the vernier scale's zero mark. In this case, it will be 25 mm.
  • Vernier Scale Reading: Look along the vernier scale to find the division line that perfectly aligns with any line on the main scale. For a reading ending in .44 mm on a 0.02 mm least count vernier, the aligning line will be the 22nd division on the vernier scale (since $22 \times 0.02 \, \text{mm} = 0.44 \, \text{mm}$).
  • Total Reading: Add the main scale reading and the vernier scale reading: $25 \, \text{mm} + 0.44 \, \text{mm} = 25.44 \, \text{mm}$.
  • Drawing: The sketch should show the main scale with the 25 mm mark visible just before the vernier zero. The vernier scale should be positioned such that its 22nd division line aligns perfectly with a main scale line (e.g., the 25 + 22*0.02 = 25.44 aligns with main scale 25+22=47 approx? No, vernier 22 aligns with some main scale line). The vernier scale usually has markings 0, 1, 2... up to 10, representing 0.00, 0.10, 0.20 mm etc., with subdivisions for 0.02mm. The alignment should be at the 22nd mark after 0.

(c) Flat File Diagram and Care:

  • (i) Labelled Diagram of a Flat File: (Repeated from Q7a(iii) July 2015 & Q8a(iv) July 2014) Sketch should show: Tang (pointed end for handle), Heel (shoulder where body starts), Body/Face (main cutting surface, flat rectangular profile), Edge (may also have teeth), Point/Tip (end opposite tang). Labels for Length, Cut (e.g., double-cut pattern), and Grade (e.g., Bastard, Second Cut, Smooth) could be included conceptually.
  • (ii) Care of Files (Four Ways):
    1. Use a File Card/Brush: Clean the file frequently during use with a file card (wire brush) to remove pinned metal chips (pinning) that clog the teeth and reduce cutting efficiency.
    2. Store Properly: Do not throw files together in a drawer or toolbox where teeth can damage each other. Store them separately in racks, cloth rolls, or plastic sleeves.
    3. Use Correctly: Apply pressure only on the forward stroke. Avoid dragging the file backward under pressure. Do not use a file as a hammer or pry bar. Use the right file type and cut for the job.
    4. Keep Dry and Rust-Free: Avoid exposing files to moisture. Lightly oiling after use can help prevent rust, especially in humid environments (ensure oil is removed before next use if necessary). Fit a proper handle securely onto the tang before use for safety and control.

This question tests understanding of fasteners, soldering vs. brazing, reading vernier scales, and the proper use and maintenance of files.

Question 4 (20 marks)

(a) (i) List two types of materials used in sheet metal work.

(ii) Explain the procedure for fabricating an open box using a tinplate.

(b) Figure 1 shows a diagram of a bench drilling machine. Name the parts labelled A to H.

(c) Sketch the following lathe tool shapes:

(i) round nose roughing tool;

(ii) parting-off tool.

Answer

This question covers sheet metal materials and fabrication, identification of drilling machine parts, and sketching lathe tool shapes.

(a) Sheet Metal Work:

  • (i) Materials Used in Sheet Metal Work (Any Two):
    • Mild Steel (often galvanized - coated with zinc - or uncoated 'black iron').
    • Tinplate (Mild steel sheet coated with a thin layer of tin).
    • Aluminium and its alloys.
    • Copper.
    • Brass (Copper-Zinc alloy).
    • Stainless Steel.
    • Zinc sheet.
  • (ii) Procedure for Fabricating an Open Box (Tinplate):
    1. Design and Development: Determine the required dimensions of the box (length, width, height). Draw the development (flat pattern or net) of the box on paper, including the base and the four sides, plus allowances (flaps/tabs) for joining the corners (e.g., folded seams, soldered joints) and potentially for hemming the top edges.
    2. Marking Out: Transfer the development pattern accurately onto the tinplate sheet using a scriber, engineer's square, and rule. Ensure marking blue is used if necessary for visibility. Mark fold lines clearly (often with dotted lines or light scratches).
    3. Cutting: Carefully cut out the marked pattern using tin snips or bench shears. Notch the corners where the flaps are located.
    4. Folding: Fold up the four sides along the marked fold lines using a folding bar or bending brake to form right angles with the base. Fold the corner joining flaps/tabs.
    5. Joining Corners: Secure the corners using the chosen method. If using folded seams, interlock the flaps and hammer them flat. If soldering, clean the flaps, apply flux, clamp or hold the corner square, and apply solder using a soldering iron. Repeat for all four corners.
    6. Edge Finishing (Optional): Hem the top edges (fold them over once or twice) for safety and stiffness if required by the design.
    7. Cleaning: Clean the finished box, removing any flux residue, burrs, or marking lines.

(b) Bench Drilling Machine Parts (Fig. 1 Labels):

Based on a typical bench drill press diagram:

  • A: Motor (Provides power)
  • B: Belt Guard / Pulley Cover (Encloses belts and pulleys)
  • C: Depth Stop (Adjustable stop to control drilling depth)
  • D: Chuck (Holds the drill bit)
  • E: Table (Supports the workpiece, often adjustable height and angle)
  • F: Column / Pillar (Main vertical support)
  • G: Base (Heavy bottom part for stability, mounts to bench)
  • H: Feed Lever / Handwheel (Used to lower/raise the spindle/chuck)

Confirmation requires viewing the actual Figure 1 from the exam paper.

(c) Lathe Tool Shape Sketches:

Requires sketches showing the cutting edge geometry from the top or side view.

  • (i) Round Nose Roughing Tool:
    Sketch: Tool bit (typically High-Speed Steel or carbide insert) showing a relatively large nose radius at the cutting corner. Side cutting edge angle and end cutting edge angle are designed for heavy material removal (roughing). Top rake and side rake angles visible in side/end views.
  • (ii) Parting-Off Tool (Cutoff Tool):
    Sketch: A narrow, blade-like tool designed to cut radially into the workpiece to sever (part off) a finished section. The cutting edge is at the front end, often straight across or slightly angled ('pip' reducing). Side clearance angles are crucial to prevent binding in the narrow groove.

This question tests practical knowledge of sheet metal work basics, identification of standard machine tool components, and recognition of common lathe tool geometries.

Question 5 (20 marks)

Figure 2 shows an isometric drawing of an anchor stop. Draw, in third angle projection, the following views:

(a) a plan;

(b) an end elevation;

(c) a front elevation in the direction of arrow A.

Insert six major dimensions.

Answer

This question requires creating standard orthographic views (Plan, Front Elevation, End Elevation) in Third Angle Projection from the provided isometric view (Figure 2). Six major dimensions must be included.

Understanding Third Angle Projection:

  • Object imagined in the third quadrant.
  • Plan (Top View) is drawn ABOVE the Front Elevation.
  • End Elevation from the Right is drawn to the RIGHT of the Front Elevation.
  • End Elevation from the Left is drawn to the LEFT of the Front Elevation.

Analysis of Figure 2 (Anchor Stop):

  • A base plate, likely rectangular or square.
  • A central raised feature, possibly cylindrical or stepped.
  • Angled webs or ribs supporting the central feature from the base.
  • Holes or slots might be present in the base or central feature.
  • Arrow A indicates the direction for the Front Elevation.
  • Dimensions need to be inferred or scaled from the isometric view if not explicitly given (assuming it's drawn to scale or proportions are clear).

Required Views:

  • (c) Front Elevation (View A): Shows the view looking directly at the face indicated by arrow A. Will show height and width.
  • (a) Plan (Top View): Drawn ABOVE the Front Elevation. Shows the width and depth.
  • (b) End Elevation: Assuming a Right End Elevation (standard if not specified). Drawn to the RIGHT of the Front Elevation. Shows the depth and height.

Procedure:

  1. Set up the drawing sheet for Third Angle Projection.
  2. Draw the Front Elevation (View A) based on the isometric view. Show hidden details with dashed lines.
  3. Project lines vertically upwards from the Front Elevation to establish the width limits for the Plan view. Draw the Plan view above the Front Elevation, showing features as seen from the top and using depth dimensions from the isometric.
  4. Project lines horizontally to the right from the Front Elevation to establish height limits for the Right End Elevation. Draw the Right End Elevation to the right of the Front Elevation, using depth dimensions (same as Plan widths) and aligning heights. Show hidden details.
  5. Add the Third Angle projection symbol.
  6. Add six major dimensions (e.g., overall width, height, depth; key feature sizes/locations) across the views.

Requires accurate orthographic drafting in Third Angle Projection, correctly interpreting the isometric view, showing hidden lines, and applying standard dimensioning.

Question 6 (20 marks)

(a) Draw free hand sketches of the following hand tools:

(i) long nose pliers;

(ii) side cutter pliers;

(iii) bent end scriber;

(iv) combination pliers;

(v) dot-punch.

(b) Figure 3 shows two views of an object. Draw an oblique cabinet pictorial view taking corner N as the lowest point. (10 marks)

Answer

This question combines freehand sketching of various hand tools with the construction of an oblique pictorial view from orthographic projections.

(a) Free Hand Sketches of Hand Tools (10 marks):

Requires reasonably recognizable freehand sketches of each tool.

  • (i) Long Nose Pliers (Needle Nose Pliers): Show handles, pivot, and long, tapering jaws (often with cutting edge near the pivot).
  • (ii) Side Cutter Pliers (Diagonal Cutters): Show handles, pivot, and short, angled cutting jaws designed for cutting wire close to a surface.
  • (iii) Bent End Scriber: Show handle/shaft and a sharp point, with the tip bent at an angle (often 90°) for reaching into confined spaces or marking specific features. May have a straight point at the other end.
  • (iv) Combination Pliers: Show handles, pivot, flat gripping jaws (often serrated), curved gripping section for round objects, and wire cutting blades near the pivot.
  • (v) Dot Punch: Show shaft/body (often knurled for grip) and a sharp conical point (typically 60° included angle) at the end, designed to be struck with a hammer to make small indentations on layout lines. Distinct from a centre punch (90° point).

(b) Oblique Cabinet Pictorial View (10 marks):

Requires constructing an oblique view from the given orthographic views (Figure 3), using Cabinet projection rules, with corner N lowest.

  • Orthographic Views Analysis (Assuming 1st or 3rd Angle): Determine the Front Elevation and Plan/End Elevation from the layout in Figure 3. Extract Width, Height, and Depth dimensions. Identify corner N.
  • Oblique Projection Basics:
    • The front face (usually based on the Front Elevation) is drawn true shape (undistorted).
    • Receding lines representing depth are drawn parallel to each other at an angle (commonly 30°, 45°, or 60°) to the horizontal.
    • Cabinet Projection Specific Rule: Receding lines are drawn at half their true depth dimension to reduce distortion.
  • Construction Steps:
    1. Orient the object so corner N is the lowest point, likely the front-bottom corner in the oblique view.
    2. Draw the Front Elevation (true shape) using the Width and Height dimensions. Position corner N at the bottom.
    3. From each corner of the front face, draw receding lines backwards at the chosen angle (e.g., 45°).
    4. Measure HALF the true depth along these receding lines.
    5. Connect the ends of these receding lines to form the back face(s) of the object, drawing lines parallel to the corresponding front face lines.
    6. Add any internal features, steps, or cutouts, projecting them onto the front face (true shape) and receding back at half depth.
    7. Darken visible lines. Hidden lines are usually omitted in oblique views.

Part (a) tests sketching ability and tool recognition. Part (b) requires interpreting orthographic views and applying the specific rules of oblique cabinet projection to create a pictorial representation.

Question 7 (20 marks)

Figure 4 shows an elevation of a truncated cylinder. Redraw full size the elevation and complete the following:

(a) plan;

(b) true shape;

(c) end elevation;

(d) surface development.

Answer

This question involves the orthographic projection, true shape determination, and surface development of a truncated cylinder based on its given elevation.

Procedure:

  1. Redraw Elevation: Accurately copy the given Front Elevation from Figure 4, noting the cylinder diameter, height, and the position/angle of the cutting plane.
  2. (a) Construct Plan View:
    • Project the diameter vertically downwards from the Elevation to draw the circular base in the Plan view, centred below the Elevation.
    • The cutting plane intersects the cylinder, forming an ellipse in the Plan view.
    • Divide the base circle (Plan) into equal segments (e.g., 12). Project these points up to the base line in the Elevation.
    • Draw vertical generator lines from these base points up to the cutting plane line in the Elevation.
    • Mark the points where these generators intersect the cutting plane.
    • Project these intersection points vertically downwards to the corresponding radial lines (or positions) in the Plan view.
    • Draw a smooth elliptical curve through these projected points in the Plan.
  3. (c) Construct End Elevation: (Assuming Right End Elevation drawn to the right of Front Elevation in Third Angle, or Left End Elevation drawn to the right in First Angle. Standard practice should be followed).
    • Project heights horizontally from the Front Elevation.
    • Project the width (diameter) from the Plan (using 45-degree projection or measurement).
    • Draw the rectangular outline representing the cylinder in the End Elevation.
    • Project the intersection points from the cutting plane in both the Front Elevation (for height) and Plan (for depth/width) to plot the elliptical curve representing the cut face in the End Elevation.
  4. (b) Construct True Shape of Cut Surface:
    • Draw projection lines perpendicular to the cutting plane line in the Front Elevation from the key intersection points previously marked.
    • Establish a datum line (X1-Y1) parallel to the cutting plane line.
    • Measure the perpendicular widths of the cut ellipse in the Plan view from its centre line.
    • Transfer these widths onto the corresponding projection lines from the Elevation, measuring outwards from the datum line X1-Y1.
    • Draw a smooth elliptical curve through the plotted points to obtain the true shape.
  5. (d) Construct Surface Development:
    • The development of the cylinder's lateral surface is a rectangle.
    • The width of the rectangle is the circumference of the cylinder base ($\pi \times D$).
    • The height varies along the width. Draw the baseline of the rectangle equal to the circumference. Divide this baseline into the same number of equal segments (e.g., 12) as the Plan base circle.
    • At each division point on the baseline, draw a vertical line.
    • Transfer the true height of each generator line (from the base up to the cutting plane intersection) from the Front Elevation onto the corresponding vertical line in the development rectangle.
    • Draw a smooth curve connecting the tops of these height lines.
    • The shape enclosed by the baseline, the two vertical side lines (heights of the shortest/longest generators), and the top curve is the development of the truncated cylinder's lateral surface. (Optionally, add the base circle and true shape ellipse if full development is needed, but usually just the lateral surface is required).

Requires accurate geometric construction for orthographic views, auxiliary projection for true shape, and parallel line development for the cylinder surface.

Question 8 (20 marks)

(a) Use the rectangular method to draw an ellipse with major and minor axes as 130 mm and 100 mm respectively.

(b) Using a pair of compasses and a ruler only, construct a regular pentagon with sides 40 mm.

Answer

This question requires the construction of an ellipse using a specific method and the construction of a regular pentagon given its side length.

(a) Ellipse Construction (Rectangular Method):

Also known as the Oblong Method or Trammel Method (though trammel usually refers to a different tool-based method).

  1. Draw the major axis (130 mm) and minor axis (100 mm) perpendicular to each other, bisecting at the center O. Label ends of major axis A, A' and minor axis B, B'.
  2. Construct a rectangle with sides 130 mm and 100 mm, centered at O, with sides parallel to the axes.
  3. Divide the semi-major axis (e.g., OA = 65 mm) into a number of equal parts (e.g., 5 parts: points 1, 2, 3, 4 from O).
  4. Divide the corresponding half-side of the rectangle parallel to the minor axis (e.g., the segment from A to the top-right corner = 50 mm) into the same number of equal parts (5 parts: points 1', 2', 3', 4' from the corner towards A).
  5. Draw lines from one end of the minor axis (e.g., B) to points 1, 2, 3, 4 on the semi-major axis OA.
  6. Draw lines from the other end of the minor axis (B') *through* points 1', 2', 3', 4' on the rectangle side, extending them to intersect the corresponding lines drawn in the previous step (Line B-1 intersects Line B'-through-1', etc.).
  7. The points of intersection lie on the ellipse.
  8. Repeat for the other three quadrants.
  9. Draw a smooth curve through the plotted points and the ends of the axes (A, A', B, B') to complete the ellipse.

(b) Regular Pentagon Construction (Side = 40 mm):

Using only compass and ruler (straightedge).

  1. Draw Base: Draw a line segment AB = 40 mm. This will be one side of the pentagon.
  2. Construct Perpendicular Bisector of AB: Find the midpoint M of AB. Construct a perpendicular line passing through M.
  3. Construct Length Related to Golden Ratio (Method 1):
    • Extend line AB to the right from B.
    • Construct a perpendicular to AB at point B.
    • Mark point P on this perpendicular such that BP = AB = 40 mm.
    • Join A to P.
    • Set compass radius to BP (40 mm). Place point at B and draw an arc intersecting AP at Q.
    • Set compass radius to AQ. Place point at A and draw an arc above AB. Place point at B and draw another arc with the same radius (AQ) intersecting the first arc at point D. (D is the vertex opposite side AB).
  4. Locate Other Vertices:
    • Set compass radius to AB (40 mm).
    • Place compass point at A and draw an arc intersecting the arc drawn from B (to find D) at point E. E is a vertex.
    • Place compass point at B and draw an arc intersecting the arc drawn from A (to find D) at point C. C is a vertex.
    • Alternatively, after finding D: Place compass point at D, radius 40mm, draw arcs. Place compass point at A, radius 40mm, intersect arc from D to find E. Place compass point at B, radius 40mm, intersect arc from D to find C.
  5. Join Vertices: Connect points A-B-C-D-E-A with straight lines to form the regular pentagon.

There are several valid compass-and-ruler methods; another common one involves constructing a circle related to the side length.

Requires accurate execution of geometric construction techniques: the rectangular method for ellipse generation and a standard compass-and-ruler method for constructing a regular pentagon from a given side length.
