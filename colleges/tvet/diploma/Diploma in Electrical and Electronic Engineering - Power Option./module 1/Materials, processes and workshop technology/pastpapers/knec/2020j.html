Question 1

(a) (i) State three conditions necessary to keep a fire burning.

(ii) Table 1 shows classes of fires. Complete the table by stating one source of fire and two extinguishing agents for each class.

(b) Outline the procedure for mouth-to-mouth resuscitation.

(c) Highlight five actions to be taken in case of a fire outbreak.

Answer

This question covers fire safety (fire triangle, classification, response) and first aid (resuscitation).

(a) Fire Conditions and Classification:

  • (i) Three Conditions for Fire (Fire Triangle/Tetrahedron):
    1. Fuel: Combustible material (solid, liquid, or gas).
    2. Oxygen: Sufficient oxygen from the air or an oxidizing agent to sustain combustion.
    3. Heat (Ignition Source): Sufficient heat energy to raise the fuel to its ignition temperature.
    4. (Often a fourth element, the Uninhibited Chemical Chain Reaction, is added, forming the fire tetrahedron, especially relevant for extinguishment).
  • (ii) Table Completion (Classes A & B):
    ClassSource of fire (Example)Extinguishing agents (Two Examples)
    1AWood, paper, cloth, plasticsWater, Foam, ABC Dry Powder, Wet Chemical
    2BPetrol, oil, paint, grease, solventsFoam, Carbon Dioxide (CO2), ABC/BC Dry Powder

    Note: Water should generally NOT be used on Class B fires (can spread the fire) unless applied as a fine mist/fog under specific conditions. CO2 and Dry Powder are generally preferred for liquids.

(b) Mouth-to-Mouth Resuscitation Procedure:

(Repeated from Nov 2018 Q1d, Nov 2015 Q1d, July 2016 Q1b, 2010 Q3a)

Modern guidelines prioritize chest compressions (CPR) and calling emergency services. This describes the traditional method.

  1. Safety First: Ensure scene safety (e.g., no ongoing electrical hazard).
  2. Check Response & Breathing: Tap and shout. Check for normal breathing (look, listen, feel <10 sec). Call for emergency help.
  3. Position Victim: Lay flat on back on firm surface.
  4. Open Airway: Head-tilt/chin-lift maneuver.
  5. Give Breaths: Pinch nose. Seal mouth over victim's mouth. Give 2 effective breaths (1 sec each, watch chest rise).
  6. Continue (If pulse present, no breathing): Give 1 breath every 5-6 seconds. (If no pulse, start CPR).

(c) Actions in Case of Fire Outbreak (Five Actions - R.A.C.E. principle often used):

  1. R - Rescue/Remove: Rescue anyone in immediate danger from the fire, if it can be done safely without endangering yourself.
  2. A - Alarm/Alert: Activate the nearest fire alarm system. Shout "FIRE!" to alert others. Call the fire department/emergency services.
  3. C - Confine/Contain: Close doors and windows (do not lock them) as you leave the fire area to slow the spread of smoke and fire.
  4. E - Extinguish / Evacuate:
    • Extinguish: ONLY if the fire is small (e.g., waste bin size), you know how to use the correct type of extinguisher, you have a clear escape route, and you feel confident and safe doing so. If the fire doesn't go out quickly, leave immediately.
    • Evacuate: If unable or unsafe to extinguish, evacuate the building immediately via the nearest safe exit route. Proceed calmly to the designated assembly point. Do not use elevators.
  5. Follow Instructions: Listen to instructions from fire wardens or emergency personnel. Do not re-enter the building until declared safe.

Understanding the conditions for fire, how to classify fires for appropriate extinguisher use, basic life support, and emergency fire response procedures are essential safety knowledge.

Question 2

(a) (i) List six physical properties of engineering materials.

(ii) State three factors that affect the properties of engineering materials.

(b) With the aid of a labeled sketch, describe the production of iron from iron ore, using the blast furnace.

(c) List four properties of aluminium that makes it popular in engineering applications.

Answer

This question covers physical properties of materials, factors affecting them, the blast furnace process for iron, and key properties of aluminium.

(a) Material Properties:

  • (i) Physical Properties (Any Six): Physical properties describe characteristics that can be observed or measured without changing the chemical identity of the substance.
    • Density (Mass per unit volume)
    • Melting Point / Boiling Point
    • Thermal Conductivity (Rate of heat transfer)
    • Electrical Conductivity / Resistivity
    • Coefficient of Thermal Expansion (How much size changes with temperature)
    • Specific Heat Capacity (Heat required to raise temperature)
    • Colour
    • Lustre (How light reflects)
    • Magnetic Properties (Ferromagnetic, Paramagnetic, Diamagnetic)
    • Optical Properties (Transparency, Opacity, Refractive Index)
    • Acoustic Properties (Sound transmission/absorption)

    Note: Sometimes mechanical properties like Strength, Hardness, Ductility, Malleability, Elasticity are grouped broadly under physical properties, but often distinguished as mechanical. The question asks for *physical* properties, so the list above is more strictly correct.

  • (ii) Factors Affecting Material Properties (Three):
    1. Composition and Microstructure: The types of atoms/molecules present, how they are bonded, the type and size of crystal grains (for metals), presence of phases, defects, or impurities significantly influence properties. Alloying is a key example.
    2. Temperature: Most properties (strength, conductivity, ductility, expansion) change significantly with temperature.
    3. Processing History (Manufacturing Method): How a material was formed (casting, forging, rolling), heat treatments applied (annealing, quenching, tempering), or amount of cold work affects its structure and thus its properties.
    4. External Environment: Factors like pressure, humidity, radiation, or corrosive atmospheres can affect properties over time.
    5. Loading Conditions: Rate of loading (static vs. impact), type of stress (tension, compression, cyclic) affects measured strength or fatigue life.

(b) Iron Production (Blast Furnace):

(Repeated from Nov 2018 Q2d, July 2017 Q4c)

Iron is extracted from iron ore (mainly oxides like hematite Fe$_2$O$_3$) in a blast furnace.

Description:

  1. Charge Input: Iron ore, Coke (carbon), and Limestone (CaCO$_3$, flux) are fed into the top.
  2. Hot Air Blast: Hot air blown in at the bottom (tuyeres).
  3. Combustion/CO Production: Coke burns ($C+O_2 \rightarrow CO_2$), then $CO_2+C \rightarrow 2CO$. Provides heat and reducing agent (CO).
  4. Reduction: Iron oxides are reduced by CO (and direct C at high temps) in stages as the charge descends: $Fe_2O_3 \rightarrow Fe_3O_4 \rightarrow FeO \rightarrow Fe$.
  5. Flux Action: Limestone decomposes ($CaCO_3 \rightarrow CaO+CO_2$). CaO reacts with silica impurities to form molten slag ($CaO+SiO_2 \rightarrow CaSiO_3$).
  6. Collection: Molten iron (pig iron) and lighter molten slag collect at the bottom (hearth) and are tapped off separately.

Labels: Charge Hopper, Throat, Shaft, Reduction Zone, Tuyeres, Hearth, Slag Notch, Iron Notch, Waste Gas Outlet, Hot Air Blast Inlet.

(c) Popular Properties of Aluminium (Four):

(Repeated from Nov 2017 Q2c & July 2017 Q4a)

  • Low Density / Lightweight: Excellent strength-to-weight ratio, crucial for transport applications (aerospace, automotive).
  • Excellent Corrosion Resistance: Forms a tough, thin, adherent layer of aluminium oxide immediately upon exposure to air, protecting the underlying metal from further corrosion in many environments.
  • Good Electrical and Thermal Conductivity: Widely used in electrical conductors (power lines) and heat exchangers/cookware.
  • High Ductility and Malleability: Easily formed into various shapes by processes like extrusion, rolling, drawing (e.g., wires, foil, complex profiles).
  • Good Appearance and Reflectivity: Can be finished to various appearances, reflects heat and light well.
  • Non-toxic: Suitable for food packaging and cookware.
  • Recyclability: Can be recycled repeatedly using significantly less energy than primary production.

Covers definition of physical properties, factors influencing them, the blast furnace process, and key advantages making aluminium a popular engineering material.

Question 3

(a) (i) State three objectives of marking out;

(ii) Explain the term datum with reference to marking out.

(b) Illustrate the use of a vernier caliper in taking the following measurements:

(i) internal diameter:

(ii) depth of a blind hole.

(c) (i) State four factors that determine the choice of a rivet.

(ii) Outline the procedure for riveting a lap joint.

Answer

This question deals with the principles and practices of marking out, using vernier calipers, and riveting.

(a) Marking Out Objectives and Datum:

  • (i) Objectives of Marking Out (Three): (Similar to July 2016 Q2b)
    1. To transfer dimensions and shapes accurately from a drawing onto the workpiece surface.
    2. To provide clear guidelines for subsequent machining or fabrication operations (cutting, drilling, bending).
    3. To ensure the finished part conforms to the required size and shape specifications.
    4. To help position the workpiece correctly for machining.
    5. To minimize material wastage by accurate layout.
  • (ii) Datum Explained: In marking out and dimensioning, a datum is a theoretically exact reference point, line, plane, or surface from which measurements are taken and dimensions are established. It serves as the origin or base for locating other features accurately and consistently. Using datums ensures that measurement errors are not cumulative and that features are correctly positioned relative to each other or to a key reference surface. Examples: A machined flat edge, a centre line, the corner of a block.

(b) Illustrating Vernier Caliper Use:

Requires sketches showing the caliper interacting with the workpiece.

  • (i) Internal Diameter:
    Illustration: Sketch showing the *internal jaws* (the smaller pair on top) of the vernier caliper inserted inside the hole whose diameter is being measured. The jaws should be opened until they lightly touch the inner surfaces of the hole. The reading is then taken from the main and vernier scales.
  • (ii) Depth of a Blind Hole:
    Illustration: Sketch showing the end face of the main body of the vernier caliper resting squarely on the surface around the blind hole. The *depth rod* (which extends from the end of the moving jaw slide) is lowered into the hole until it touches the bottom. The reading is taken from the main and vernier scales.

(c) Riveting Choice and Procedure:

  • (i) Factors Determining Choice of Rivet (Four):
    1. Materials Being Joined: Rivet material should be compatible (similar electrochemical potential to avoid galvanic corrosion) and strong enough for the base materials.
    2. Strength Requirements: The required shear and tensile strength of the joint dictates the rivet material (e.g., steel, aluminium alloy), diameter, and number of rivets.
    3. Type of Loading: Whether the joint is primarily in shear or tension influences rivet selection and joint design.
    4. Head Style: Choice depends on application requirements (e.g., snap head for general use, countersunk for flush finish, pan head for higher strength).
    5. Accessibility: Whether both sides of the joint are accessible (for solid rivets) or only one side (requiring blind rivets like pop rivets).
    6. Corrosion Environment: Rivet material must resist corrosion in the service environment.
    7. Cost and Appearance.
  • (ii) Procedure for Riveting a Lap Joint: A lap joint involves overlapping the two plates to be joined.
    1. Preparation: Overlap the plates by the required amount. Mark the positions for the rivet holes. Drill or punch holes through both plates simultaneously or separately but ensuring alignment. Deburr the holes.
    2. Rivet Insertion: Insert the correct type and size of rivet through the aligned holes.
    3. Support: Place the pre-formed head of the rivet on a suitable support (dolly or rivet set).
    4. Upsetting: Form the second head on the tail end of the rivet using appropriate tools (hammer and snap for solid rivets, riveting gun for specific types, pop rivet tool for blind rivets). Ensure the rivet swells to fill the hole and clamps the plates tightly together.
    5. Inspection: Check the formed head for correct shape and size, and ensure the joint is tight.

Covers the purpose of marking out and datums, the use of vernier calipers for different measurements, factors in selecting rivets, and the basic procedure for creating a riveted lap joint.

Question 4

(a) (i) List four components of the oxy-acetylene welding equipment.

(ii) Define arc welding.

(b) (i) List four types of drilling machines.

(ii) State three safety precautions to be observed when drilling.

(c) Illustrate the following types of lathe operations:

(i) parting-off:

(ii) parallel turning.

Answer

This question covers welding equipment and definition, types of drilling machines and safety, and specific lathe operations.

(a) Welding Equipment and Definition:

  • (i) Components of Oxy-Acetylene Welding Equipment (Four):
    1. Gas Cylinders (One for Oxygen, one for Acetylene).
    2. Pressure Regulators (One for each cylinder, to reduce pressure).
    3. Hoses (Twin hoses, color-coded, for gas transport).
    4. Welding Torch (Mixes gases and holds the nozzle/tip).
    5. Torch Tip/Nozzle (Determines flame size).
    6. Spark Lighter/Igniter (To light the torch safely).
    7. Flashback Arrestors and Non-Return Valves (Safety devices, often fitted at regulator and torch ends).
    8. Trolley (Optional, for cylinder transport).
  • (ii) Arc Welding Definition: (Repeated from July 2014 Q4c) A welding process that uses the intense heat generated by an electric arc established between an electrode (consumable or non-consumable) and the workpiece (base metal) to melt the materials at the joining point, allowing them to fuse together upon cooling.

(b) Drilling Machines and Safety:

  • (i) Types of Drilling Machines (Four): (Repeated from July 2015 Q4a)
    1. Bench Drilling Machine (Drill Press).
    2. Pillar Drilling Machine.
    3. Radial Arm Drilling Machine.
    4. Portable Electric Hand Drill.
    5. Multi-spindle Drilling Machine.
    6. Sensitive Drilling Machine.
    7. CNC Drilling Machine.
  • (ii) Safety Precautions When Drilling (Three):
    1. Secure Workpiece: Always clamp the workpiece securely to the drill table using vices, clamps, or fixtures. Never hold small workpieces by hand.
    2. Wear Eye Protection: Always wear safety glasses or a face shield to protect eyes from flying chips (swarf).
    3. Clothing and Hair: Avoid loose clothing, gloves, ties, or jewelry that could get caught in the rotating spindle or drill bit. Tie back long hair.
    4. Correct Speed and Feed: Use the appropriate drilling speed and feed rate for the material being drilled and the drill bit size.
    5. Remove Chuck Key: Always remove the chuck key immediately after tightening or loosening the chuck.
    6. Chip Removal: Stop the machine before clearing chips from the hole or table; use a brush, not hands.
    7. Keep Hands Clear: Keep hands away from the rotating spindle and cutting tool during operation.

(c) Illustrating Lathe Operations:

Requires sketches showing the tool, workpiece, and direction of motion.

  • (i) Parting-Off (Cutoff):
    Illustration: Sketch showing the workpiece rotating in the chuck. A narrow parting-off tool is fed radially (perpendicular to the axis of rotation) into the workpiece, cutting a groove that eventually severs the part.
  • (ii) Parallel Turning (Plain Turning):
    Illustration: Sketch showing the workpiece rotating in the chuck (possibly supported by tailstock). A turning tool (e.g., roughing or finishing) is fed parallel to the axis of rotation along the length of the workpiece, removing material from the outside diameter to create a cylinder of uniform diameter.

Covers essential welding equipment, drilling machine types and safety practices, and illustrates two fundamental lathe operations.

Question 5

Figure 1 shows a pictorial view of a towing hook. Draw full size, the following views in first angle projection:

(a) a sectional front elevation along cutting plane 'A-A';

(b) an end elevation in the direction of arrow B;

(c) the plan.

Answer

This question requires drawing orthographic views (including a section) of a towing hook in First Angle Projection, based on the provided pictorial view (Figure 1).

Understanding First Angle Projection and Sectioning:

  • First Angle: Plan below Elevation, Left End View to the Right, Right End View to the Left.
  • Cutting Plane A-A is defined on the pictorial view for the Front Elevation.
  • Arrow B indicates the direction for the End Elevation (Right End View).
  • Plan is the view from above.

Analysis of Figure 1 (Towing Hook):

  • A substantial hook shape.
  • A base or mounting flange, likely with holes for attachment.
  • The cutting plane A-A appears to pass vertically through the center of the hook and mounting feature.

Required Views:

  • (a) Sectional Front Elevation A-A: The view seen after cutting along A-A. Shows the internal profile and solid material cut by the plane (which should be hatched). Features behind the plane shown if visible.
  • (b) End Elevation (View B): View from the right (Arrow B). Drawn to the LEFT of the Front Elevation. Shows the profile of the hook and mounting flange from the side. Hidden details shown dashed.
  • (c) Plan (Top View): Drawn BELOW the Front Elevation. Shows the shape of the hook and flange as seen from above. Hidden details shown dashed.

Procedure:

  1. Set up drawing sheet for First Angle Projection.
  2. Interpret dimensions and shapes from Figure 1.
  3. Draw the Sectional Front Elevation A-A, applying hatching correctly to cut surfaces.
  4. Project heights horizontally leftwards for the End Elevation (B). Draw the End Elevation using depth dimensions and aligning heights.
  5. Project widths vertically downwards from the Sectional Front Elevation for the Plan. Draw the Plan below, using depth dimensions (consistent with View B) and aligning widths.
  6. Add the First Angle projection symbol.
  7. Dimensioning is not explicitly asked for, but may be good practice to add key ones if time permits.

Requires accurate orthographic drafting in First Angle Projection, correct application of sectioning conventions, and interpretation of a potentially complex 3D shape from a pictorial view.

Question 6

Figure 2, shows front and end elevation of an object. Draw the isometric view of the object with corner 'X' as the lowest point.

Answer

This question requires constructing an isometric view from the given orthographic views (Front and End Elevation, Figure 2), assuming a standard projection (likely First Angle if not specified, based on layout, but should confirm convention if possible), with corner X as the lowest point.

Analysis of Views (Figure 2):

  • Identify Front Elevation and End Elevation. Determine Width, Height (from Front/End Elev) and Depth (from End Elev).
  • Understand the object's shape: Appears to be a base with an upright section containing slots or cutouts, and possibly angled features.
  • Locate corner X on the views.

Isometric Construction Steps:

  1. Orient Axes: Set up isometric axes (vertical, 30° left, 30° right) starting from corner X as the origin (lowest point). Assign Width, Depth, Height directions based on the orthographic views relative to X.
  2. Bounding Box (Optional): Draw a light isometric box using overall dimensions.
  3. Construct Features: Build the object within the isometric axes starting from X. Transfer shapes and dimensions from the Front Elevation (for Width/Height features) and End Elevation (for Depth/Height features) onto the corresponding isometric planes.
  4. Curves/Holes: Draw circles as ellipses on isometric planes.
  5. Refine: Connect points, darken visible lines, erase construction lines. Omit hidden lines.

Requires accurate interpretation of the orthographic views and careful construction of the isometric projection, oriented correctly with corner X lowest.

Question 7

Figure 3 shows an elevation of a truncated cylinder. Draw the given view and construct the following:

(a) end elevation in the direction of arrow 'A'

(b) the plan;

(c) development of the truncated cylinder.

Answer

This question involves constructing orthographic views (Plan, End Elevation) and the surface development for a truncated cylinder, given its front elevation.

Procedure:

  1. Draw Given Elevation: Accurately copy the Front Elevation from Figure 3, showing the cylinder diameter, height, and the oblique cutting plane.
  2. (b) Construct Plan View:
    • Project the diameter downwards to draw the circular base in the Plan, centered below the Elevation.
    • Divide the base circle (Plan) into 12 equal segments (0-11). Project these points up to the base line in the Elevation.
    • Draw vertical element lines from these base points up the cylinder surface in the Elevation, intersecting the cutting plane. Mark intersection points (0'-11').
    • Project points 0'-11' vertically down to the corresponding radial lines (0-11) in the Plan.
    • Draw a smooth ellipse through these points in the Plan, representing the top cut surface.
  3. (a) Construct End Elevation (View A): (Assuming View A is Left End View, drawn Right in First Angle or Left in Third Angle).
    • Project heights horizontally from the Front Elevation.
    • Project widths (depths) from the Plan using 45° projection or measurement. The width is the cylinder diameter.
    • Draw the rectangular outline of the cylinder in the End Elevation.
    • Project the intersection points 0'-11' from both Front Elevation (heights) and Plan (depths) to plot the elliptical curve of the cut surface in the End Elevation.
  4. (c) Construct Development:
    • Draw a baseline equal to the cylinder circumference ($\pi \times D$). Divide into 12 equal segments.
    • Draw vertical lines (element lines) at each division point.
    • Transfer the true height of each generator (from base to cut plane) from the Front Elevation to the corresponding element line on the development.
    • Draw a smooth curve connecting the top points. The resulting shape is the development of the lateral surface.

Requires accurate orthographic projection, plotting elliptical curves resulting from oblique sections, and applying parallel line development for the cylinder surface.

Question 8

(a) A triangle has sides 70 mm, 90 mm and 40 mm long. Draw the triangle and construct the following:

(i) the inscribed circle;

(ii) the circumscribed circle;

(iii) the smallest escribed circle.

(b) Make free hand sketches of the following:

(i) ball pein hammer:

(ii) angle plate;

(iii) bench vice.

Answer

This question involves geometric constructions related to a triangle (incircle, circumcircle, excircle) and freehand sketching of workshop equipment.

(a) Triangle Constructions:

Given sides: a=40 mm, b=70 mm, c=90 mm (Check inequality: 40+70 > 90, ok).

  1. Draw Triangle: Draw base c=90mm (PQ). Arc radius 70mm from P. Arc radius 40mm from Q. Intersection is R. Join PR, QR.
  2. (i) Inscribed Circle (Incircle):
    • Bisect two internal angles (e.g., P and Q).
    • The intersection of the bisectors is the Incenter (I).
    • Construct a perpendicular from I to any side (e.g., PQ). The length is the inradius (r).
    • Draw circle center I, radius r.
  3. (ii) Circumscribed Circle (Circumcircle):
    • Construct perpendicular bisectors of two sides (e.g., PQ and PR).
    • The intersection of the bisectors is the Circumcenter (O).
    • The distance from O to any vertex (OP, OQ, or OR) is the circumradius (R).
    • Draw circle center O, radius R.
  4. (iii) Smallest Escribed Circle (Excircle):
    • An escribed circle is tangent to one side and the extensions of the other two. There are three escribed circles. The smallest one is generally tangent to the *longest* side (opposite the largest angle). In this case, tangent to side c=90mm (PQ).
    • Bisect the *external* angles at P and Q.
    • The intersection of these external angle bisectors is the Excenter ($I_c$).
    • Construct a perpendicular from $I_c$ to side PQ. The length is the exradius ($r_c$).
    • Draw circle center $I_c$, radius $r_c$.

(b) Free Hand Sketches:

Requires reasonably recognizable sketches.

  • (i) Ball Pein Hammer: (Repeated) Show handle, head with flat face and round pein.
  • (ii) Angle Plate: Show two flat plates joined accurately at 90°, usually cast iron, with slots or holes for clamping workpieces or clamping the plate itself to a machine table or surface plate. Used to hold work square during marking out or machining.
  • (iii) Bench Vice: Show fixed jaw, movable jaw, screw mechanism, handle, anvil area (optional), base for mounting to bench. Used for securely holding workpieces during filing, sawing, etc.

Combines accurate geometric construction skills for triangle properties (incircle, circumcircle, excircle) with the ability to sketch common workshop tools and equipment.
