SECTION A: MATERIALS, PROCESSES AND WORKSHOP TECHNOLOGY

Answer any THREE questions from this section.

Question 1

(a) (i) State two:

(I) functions of a soldering bit;

(II) factors that determine the size of a soldering bit.

(ii) Outline two requirements of a good soldering flux.

(b) (i) Explain the oxy-acetylene welding process.

(ii) Draw and label the neutral flame used in oxy acetylene welding.

(c) Distinguish between 'forging' and 'casting' with reference to metalwork.

(d) Sketch the following locking devices in their final positions:

(i) the stud;

(ii) set bolt.

Answer

This question covers soldering, oxy-acetylene welding, metal forming processes (forging vs. casting), and mechanical fasteners.

(a) Soldering Bit and Flux:

  • (i) (I) Functions of a Soldering Bit (Tip):
    1. Heat Transfer: Conducts heat efficiently from the soldering iron's heating element to the joint (workpiece and solder).
    2. Solder Application/Manipulation: Holds a small amount of molten solder (via wetting/'tinning') and allows the user to transfer and flow the solder precisely into the joint.
    3. Melting Solder: Provides the localized heat needed to melt the solder wire.
  • (i) (II) Factors Determining Soldering Bit Size/Shape:
    1. Size/Mass of the Joint: Larger joints require larger, more massive bits to store and transfer sufficient heat quickly without excessive cooling (thermal mass). Smaller bits are used for delicate electronic components.
    2. Accessibility of the Joint: The shape of the bit (e.g., chisel, conical, bevelled, bent) is chosen based on the access needed to reach the joint area effectively.
    3. Type of Work: Different shapes are suited for different tasks (e.g., chisel for wire/tag connections, conical for through-hole components, hoof for drag soldering).
    4. Power Rating of the Iron: The bit size must be compatible with the heating capacity of the soldering iron.
  • (ii) Requirements of a Good Soldering Flux: (Repeated from July 2016 Q3a)
    1. Chemically removes oxides from base metals at soldering temperature.
    2. Prevents re-oxidation of surfaces during heating.
    3. Promotes wetting/flow of molten solder.
    4. Residue should be non-corrosive or easily removable.
    5. Activation temperature compatible with solder.

(b) Oxy-Acetylene Welding:

  • (i) Process Explanation: (Repeated from July 2014 Q4c) A gas welding process using a high-temperature flame produced by burning acetylene fuel gas with pure oxygen. The heat melts the edges of the base metals. Usually, a filler rod of similar metal is melted into the joint pool to add material and form the weld bead upon solidification. The flame characteristics (neutral, oxidizing, carburizing) can be adjusted by varying the gas mixture ratio.
  • (ii) Neutral Flame Sketch:

    Requires a sketch of the flame shape.

    Labels: Torch Tip, Inner Cone (bright white, well-defined, rounded end), Outer Envelope (larger, less distinct, bluish/orange).

(c) Forging vs. Casting Distinction:

  • Forging: (Repeated from July 2017 Q1c) A process where metal is shaped by applying compressive forces (hammering/pressing), usually when the metal is heated to a plastic state (hot forging). The metal remains solid throughout. Results in a refined grain structure and improved mechanical properties (strength, toughness).
  • Casting (Foundry Work): (Repeated from July 2017 Q1c) A process where molten metal is poured into a mold cavity having the desired shape and allowed to cool and solidify. The resulting part (casting) takes the shape of the mold. Suitable for complex shapes but generally has different mechanical properties compared to forged parts due to solidification structure.
  • Distinction: Forging shapes solid metal using force (often heated); Casting shapes liquid metal by pouring it into a mold to solidify.

(d) Sketches of Locking Devices:

Requires sketches showing the fasteners in their assembled/locked position.

  • (i) The Stud: (Repeated from July 2015 Q3b) Sketch should show the stud (threaded at both ends) screwed into a tapped hole in one component (Part 1). The second component (Part 2) is placed over the protruding end, and a nut (and possibly washer) is tightened onto the stud to clamp Part 2 to Part 1.
  • (ii) Set Bolt (Setscrew): A set bolt (more commonly called a setscrew) is typically fully threaded and often headless (e.g., grub screw) or has a socket head. It's used to secure one part relative to another, often preventing rotation (like locking a pulley onto a shaft). It does this by screwing through a tapped hole in the outer part until its point presses firmly against (or into a small depression in) the inner part.
    Sketching a common application: Show a collar or hub (Outer Part) on a shaft (Inner Part). Sketch the setscrew passing through a tapped hole in the hub and its point pressing against the shaft surface.

Covers soldering basics, oxy-acetylene welding flame types, differentiating forging and casting, and illustrating common mechanical fasteners in assembly.

Question 2

(a) Explain the following properties of engineering materials:

(i) toughness:

(ii) conductivity.

(b) Distinguish between cast iron and wrought iron.

(c) State two:

(i) properties of copper;

(ii) applications of rubber.

(d) Draw a labelled diagram showing the extraction of iron from its ore.

Answer

This question covers material properties, distinguishes between types of iron, lists properties/applications of copper and rubber, and asks for a diagram of iron extraction.

(a) Material Properties Explained:

  • (i) Toughness: (Repeated from July 2014 Q2a & July 2015 Q1c) A material's ability to absorb energy and deform plastically without fracturing, especially under impact or shock loading. It represents resistance to fracture initiation and propagation.
  • (ii) Conductivity: (Repeated from Nov 2015 Q2a) The ability of a material to conduct energy, usually referring to either electrical conductivity (ease of electric current flow) or thermal conductivity (ease of heat energy flow).

(b) Cast Iron vs. Wrought Iron Distinction:

  • Cast Iron: An alloy of iron with a high carbon content (typically 2% - 4%) and significant silicon content (1% - 3%), along with other impurities.
    • Properties: Hard, brittle, relatively weak in tension, excellent compressive strength, good wear resistance, excellent castability (low melting point, fluid when molten), good damping capacity.
    • Structure: Carbon exists primarily as graphite flakes (grey cast iron) or iron carbide/cementite (white cast iron).
    • Production: Produced by re-melting pig iron (from blast furnace) with scrap iron/steel in a cupola furnace and casting into molds.
  • Wrought Iron: A form of commercial iron with very low carbon content (typically < 0.08%) and containing fibrous slag inclusions (iron silicate).
    • Properties: Softer, more ductile, and tougher than cast iron; good tensile strength; easily welded and forged; resistant to corrosion and fatigue.
    • Structure: Consists mainly of ferrite (pure iron) with elongated slag fibers interspersed within it.
    • Production (Historical): Produced by refining pig iron in a puddling furnace, removing most carbon and incorporating slag. Largely obsolete now, replaced by mild steel.
  • Distinction: Key differences are carbon content (high in cast iron, very low in wrought iron), presence of slag fibers (wrought iron), and resulting properties (cast iron is hard/brittle/castable, wrought iron is soft/ductile/forgeable).

(c) Properties of Copper and Applications of Rubber:

  • (i) Properties of Copper (Two):
    • Excellent Electrical Conductivity (second only to silver).
    • Excellent Thermal Conductivity.
    • Good Corrosion Resistance.
    • Highly Ductile and Malleable (easily drawn into wires and formed).
    • Readily joined by soldering and brazing.
    • Distinctive reddish colour.
    • Antimicrobial properties.
  • (ii) Applications of Rubber (Two): (See also Nov 2017 Q2c)
    • Vehicle Tires and Inner Tubes.
    • Seals, Gaskets, O-rings (for sealing fluids/gases).
    • Electrical Insulation (wire/cable covering, gloves, mats).
    • Hoses (for water, air, chemicals).
    • Belts (conveyor belts, drive belts).
    • Vibration Dampers / Shock Absorbers (engine mounts, suspension parts).
    • Footwear (soles, boots).

(d) Extraction of Iron (Blast Furnace Diagram):

(Repeated from July 2017 Q4c)

Requires a labelled diagram of a blast furnace showing the input charge, hot air blast, different zones (reduction, heat generation), and output products (molten pig iron, slag, waste gases).

Labels: Charge Hopper, Throat, Shaft, Reduction Zone, Tuyeres, Hearth, Slag Notch, Iron Notch, Waste Gas Outlet, Hot Air Blast Inlet.

Covers material properties definitions, comparison of iron types, specific properties/uses of copper and rubber, and the blast furnace iron extraction process.

Question 3

(a) Explain the difference between linear scale and non-linear scale.

(b) (i) State the functions of a divider.

(ii) Draw a labelled diagram of a spring divider.

(c) (i) Draw a micrometer screw gauge, reading 8.90 mm.

(ii) Outline the procedure of reading the 8.90 mm on the scale in c(i).

(d) State three safety precautions observed in the care of scribers.

Answer

This question focuses on measurement scales, dividers, micrometer usage, and tool safety (scribers).

(a) Linear vs. Non-linear Scale:

  • Linear Scale: A scale where the divisions are spaced equally along its length, representing equal increments of the measured quantity. The relationship between the scale reading and the quantity is directly proportional. Examples: Standard ruler (mm/cm/inches), main scale of vernier calipers/micrometers, linear scale on an ammeter measuring current directly.
  • Non-linear Scale: A scale where the divisions are not spaced equally. The distance between marks changes along the scale, representing unequal increments for equal changes in reading, or vice-versa. This occurs when the relationship between the measured quantity and the indicator's movement is non-linear. Examples: Ohmmeter scale on an analog multimeter (often cramped at high resistance end), logarithmic scales (like pH scale or decibel scale represented linearly), some pressure gauges.

Difference: Equal distances on a linear scale represent equal changes in value; on a non-linear scale, equal distances represent unequal changes in value (or vice-versa).

(b) Dividers:

  • (i) Functions of a Divider: (Repeated from Nov 2017 Q5c(ii))
    1. Scribing circles and arcs on a workpiece during marking out.
    2. Transferring measurements accurately from a ruler or scale to a workpiece.
    3. Stepping off equal distances along a line or curve (dividing a line into equal parts).
    4. Comparing dimensions between parts or against a standard.
  • (ii) Labelled Diagram of a Spring Divider:

    Sketch should show two pointed legs joined at the top by a pivot, a bow spring that tends to push the legs apart, and a threaded rod with a knurled adjusting nut used to precisely set and hold the distance between the points.

    Labels: Legs, Points, Pivot, Bow Spring, Adjusting Nut, Threaded Rod.

(c) Micrometer Reading:

  • (i) Draw Micrometer Reading 8.90 mm:
    • Main Scale Reading: The thimble edge must be past the 8 mm mark and the 0.5 mm mark after it. Visible main scale = 8.5 mm.
    • Thimble Scale Reading: Remaining amount = $8.90 - 8.5 = 0.40 \, \text{mm}$. The thimble division aligning with the datum line is '40'.
    • Sketch: Show sleeve with 8 mm and 8.5 mm marks visible. Show thimble positioned past 8.5 mm mark, with the '40' mark on its scale aligned with the sleeve datum line.
  • (ii) Procedure for Reading 8.90 mm:
    1. Read the largest whole millimeter mark exposed on the sleeve to the left of the thimble edge (8 mm).
    2. Check if the half-millimeter mark after the whole millimeter mark is visible (Yes, the 0.5 mm mark after 8 mm is visible). Add 0.50 mm to the reading (Total so far = 8.50 mm).
    3. Read the division number on the thimble scale that aligns perfectly with the horizontal datum line on the sleeve (This is the '40' mark).
    4. Multiply this thimble division number by the least count (usually 0.01 mm): $40 \times 0.01 \, \text{mm} = 0.40 \, \text{mm}$.
    5. Add the main scale reading (including the half-mm) and the thimble scale reading: $8.50 \, \text{mm} + 0.40 \, \text{mm} = 8.90 \, \text{mm}$.

(d) Safety Precautions for Care of Scribers (Three):

  • Protect the Point: Store the scriber with its point protected (e.g., by inserting it into cork, wood, or a protective cap, or retracting if applicable) to prevent damage to the sharp tip and to avoid accidental injury.
  • Handle with Care: Avoid dropping the scriber, as this can easily break or dull the hardened point.
  • Keep Separate from Other Tools: Do not store scribers loose with other tools where the point could be damaged or could damage other tools.
  • Use for Intended Purpose Only: Do not use a scriber as a punch, pry bar, or for any purpose other than marking lines.

Defines scale types, describes the function and parts of dividers, explains how to read a micrometer for a specific value, and lists safety precautions for handling sharp scribers.

Question 4

(a) (i) List three hazards that may be found in a workshop.

(ii) With regard to safety, explain the dressing code used in the workshop.

(b) Explain the following lathe machine operations:

(i) knurling;

(ii) facing.

(c) Draw a labelled diagram of a bench drilling machine.

Answer

This question covers general workshop hazards, appropriate dress code, specific lathe operations, and identification of drilling machine components.

(a) Workshop Hazards and Dress Code:

  • (i) Workshop Hazards (Any Three):
    • Mechanical Hazards: Moving/rotating machinery parts (entanglement, impact, crushing), sharp tools, falling objects.
    • Electrical Hazards: Electric shock, burns, fires from faulty wiring or equipment.
    • Fire Hazards: Flammable materials (solvents, oils), sparks from grinding/welding, overloaded circuits.
    • Chemical Hazards: Exposure to solvents, acids, cleaning agents, fumes, dust (skin contact, inhalation).
    • Slips, Trips, and Falls: Wet floors, clutter, trailing cables, uneven surfaces.
    • Noise Hazards: High noise levels from machinery causing hearing damage.
    • Manual Handling Hazards: Lifting heavy objects improperly causing strains/sprains.
    • Radiation Hazards: UV light from welding, laser hazards.
  • (ii) Workshop Dressing Code Explained: The dressing code aims to minimize risks associated with clothing and personal items interacting dangerously with the workshop environment, especially machinery. Key aspects include:
    • Appropriate Clothing: Wear sturdy, close-fitting clothing (e.g., overalls, workshop coats). Avoid loose sleeves, baggy trousers, or anything that could easily get caught in moving parts.
    • Footwear: Wear strong, fully enclosed shoes, preferably safety boots with steel toe caps and non-slip soles. No sandals, open-toed shoes, or high heels.
    • Hair and Jewelry: Tie back long hair securely. Remove or secure necklaces, bracelets, rings, and watches, as they can get caught in machinery or conduct electricity.
    • Personal Protective Equipment (PPE): Wear mandatory PPE such as safety glasses/goggles at all times. Use additional PPE like gloves, ear defenders, respirators, or face shields as required by the specific task or machine.

(b) Lathe Machine Operations Explained:

  • (i) Knurling: (Repeated from July 2016 Q4c) A forming process (not cutting) used to create a regular, patterned texture (diamond or straight lines) on a cylindrical surface. A knurling tool with hardened, patterned wheels is pressed against the rotating workpiece, displacing metal to form the raised pattern, typically to improve grip.
  • (ii) Facing: (Repeated from July 2016 Q4c) A machining operation to create a flat surface on the end of a workpiece, perpendicular to its axis of rotation. The cutting tool is fed radially across the end face while the workpiece rotates in the chuck.

(c) Labelled Diagram of Bench Drilling Machine:

(Repeated from Nov 2015 Q4b) Sketch should show:

  • Labels: Motor, Belt Guard/Pulley Cover, Spindle, Chuck, Feed Lever/Handwheel, Depth Stop, Table, Column/Pillar, Base, Power Switch.

Covers awareness of workshop hazards, appropriate attire for safety, understanding common lathe operations (knurling, facing), and identifying parts of a bench drill press.

Question 5

Figure 1 shows a cast-iron web bracket. Draw in first angle projection:

(a) sectional front elevation along X-X in direction of arrow A;

(b) end elevation in the direction of arrow B.

Insert six major dimensions.

Answer

This question requires drawing two orthographic views (one sectioned) in First Angle Projection based on the provided pictorial view (Figure 1), including dimensions.

Understanding First Angle Projection and Sectioning:

  • First Angle: Plan below Elevation, Left End View to the Right, Right End View to the Left.
  • Sectioning: Cutting plane X-X is indicated for the Front Elevation (View A). Cut surfaces are hatched.

Analysis of Figure 1 (Web Bracket):

  • A main body with a base, possibly with mounting holes.
  • An upright section, likely with a bore or hole.
  • Webs providing support between the base and upright sections.
  • Cutting plane X-X passes vertically through the object.
  • Arrow A indicates the viewing direction for the Front Elevation (showing the section).
  • Arrow B indicates the viewing direction for the End Elevation (likely Right End View).

Required Views:

  • (a) Sectional Front Elevation (View A along X-X): View looking along A after cutting on X-X. Shows internal features revealed by the cut (like the bore, web thickness at cut). Cut solid material (cast iron) is hatched. Features behind the cut plane are shown if visible. Webs are often not hatched even if cut longitudinally by convention, unless specifically required.
  • (b) End Elevation (View B): View looking along B (Right End View). Drawn to the LEFT of the Front Elevation. Shows the height and depth profile of the bracket, including the base, upright section, and web profile. Hidden details shown dashed.

Procedure:

  1. Set up drawing sheet for First Angle Projection.
  2. Determine the path of cutting plane X-X.
  3. Draw the Sectional Front Elevation A-A. Hatch cut surfaces (except possibly webs). Show features behind the plane.
  4. Project heights horizontally to the left for the End Elevation (B). Draw the End Elevation using depth dimensions from the pictorial view, aligning heights. Show hidden lines.
  5. Add the First Angle projection symbol.
  6. Add six major dimensions across the two views.

Requires accurate orthographic drafting in First Angle Projection, correctly applying sectioning conventions for cast iron (including web convention if applicable), and adding standard dimensioning.

Question 6

Figure 2 shows the elevation of a truncated cone. Redraw the given elevation and complete the following:

(a) plan;

(b) end elevation from the direction of arrow E;

(d) true shape.

Answer

This question involves orthographic projection and finding the true shape of the cut section for a truncated cone, given its elevation.

Assuming part (d) in the question should be (c) and refers to the true shape of the cut surface.

Procedure:

  1. Redraw Elevation: Accurately copy the given Front Elevation from Figure 2, noting the base diameter, height, and the oblique cutting plane. Find or project the apex of the original cone.
  2. (a) Construct Plan View:
    • Project the base diameter vertically downwards to draw the circular base in the Plan, centered below the Elevation.
    • The oblique cutting plane creates an elliptical shape in the Plan.
    • Divide the base circle (Plan) into 12 equal segments. Project these points up to the base line in the Elevation.
    • Draw generator lines from the cone's apex through these base points in the Elevation, intersecting the cutting plane. Mark these intersection points.
    • Project these intersection points vertically down to the corresponding radial lines in the Plan.
    • Draw a smooth ellipse through these points in the Plan.
  3. (b) Construct End Elevation (View E): (Assume Left End View, drawn to the right in First Angle projection).
    • Project heights horizontally from the Front Elevation.
    • Project widths from the Plan (using 45-degree projection or measurement).
    • Draw the triangular outline of the cone in the End Elevation.
    • Project the points on the cutting plane from both the Elevation (height) and Plan (width/depth) to plot the elliptical cut shape in the End Elevation. Draw a smooth curve.
  4. (c) Construct True Shape of Cut Surface:
    • Draw projection lines perpendicular to the cutting plane line in the Elevation from the key intersection points.
    • Establish a datum line parallel to the cutting plane line.
    • Measure the perpendicular widths of the cut ellipse in the Plan view from its center line.
    • Transfer these widths onto the corresponding projection lines in the true shape view, measuring perpendicularly from the datum line.
    • Draw a smooth ellipse connecting the plotted points.

Requires accurate orthographic projection, including constructing elliptical curves representing oblique sections, and auxiliary projection techniques to find the true shape of the cut.

Question 7

Figure 3 shows two views of an object drawn in third angle projection. Draw an isometric view of the object taking corner P as the lowest.

Answer

This question requires constructing an isometric view from given third angle orthographic projections (Figure 3), ensuring corner P is the lowest point.

Understanding Third Angle Projection:

  • Plan (Top View) is ABOVE the Front Elevation.
  • Front Elevation shows height and width.
  • Plan shows width and depth.

Analysis of Figure 3:

  • The object appears to be a block with features like steps, slots, or possibly angled surfaces.
  • Corner P is identified, likely a front-bottom corner based on typical layout.
  • Dimensions need to be extracted from the Front Elevation and Plan views.

Isometric Construction Steps:

  1. Orient Axes: Set up isometric axes (vertical, 30° left, 30° right) originating from corner P as the lowest point. Determine Width, Depth, Height directions relative to P based on the views.
  2. Overall Dimensions: Find the object's maximum Width, Height, and Depth from the views.
  3. Construct Base/Framework: Start drawing from P, laying out the main features along the isometric axes using dimensions from the Plan (for width/depth) and Front Elevation (for height). An isometric bounding box can be helpful.
  4. Transfer Details: Carefully transfer all steps, slots, curves, and other features shown in the orthographic views onto the isometric framework. Remember circles become ellipses. Draw lines parallel to the isometric axes.
  5. Refine and Darken: Complete the outline, ensuring all lines connect correctly. Darken visible object lines and erase construction lines. Omit hidden lines unless needed for clarity.

Requires accurate interpretation of Third Angle orthographic views and careful construction of the isometric projection with the specified orientation (P lowest).

Question 8

(a) Construct an internal tangent to two unequal circles of diameters 40 mm and 60 mm with their centres 90 mm apart.

(b) Make free hand sketches of the following engineering hand tools:

(i) Ball pein hammer:

(ii) Flat screw driver:

(iii) Centre punch;

(iv) Combination pliers.

(c) Construct the locus of a point P on the circumference of a circle 35 mm diameter as it rolls for one revolution without slipping.

Answer

This question involves geometric constructions (internal tangent, locus/cycloid) and freehand sketching of tools.

(a) Internal Tangent Construction:

To construct a common internal tangent (transverse tangent) to two circles.

Given: Circle 1: Diameter D1 = 40 mm (Radius R1 = 20 mm), Center O1. Circle 2: Diameter D2 = 60 mm (Radius R2 = 30 mm), Center O2. Distance O1O2 = 90 mm.

  1. Draw the line segment O1O2 = 90 mm. Draw Circle 1 (R1=20) centered at O1 and Circle 2 (R2=30) centered at O2.
  2. Find the midpoint M of the line segment O1O2.
  3. Draw a semicircle with center M and diameter O1O2.
  4. Construct an auxiliary circle centered at O1 with radius R_aux = R1 + R2 = 20 + 30 = 50 mm.
  5. Find the intersection point(s), let one be P, where this auxiliary circle (R=50) intersects the semicircle drawn in step 3.
  6. Draw the line segment O1P and extend it to intersect the original Circle 1 at point T1 (this is a tangent point).
  7. Draw a line through O2 parallel to O1P. This line will intersect Circle 2 at point T2 (the second tangent point) such that O2T2 is on the *opposite* side of O1O2 compared to O1T1 (for internal tangent).
  8. The line connecting T1 and T2 is the required common internal tangent. Repeat for the other intersection of the auxiliary circle and semicircle if both tangents are needed.

(b) Free Hand Sketches of Hand Tools:

Requires reasonably recognizable freehand sketches.

  • (i) Ball Pein Hammer: (Repeated from Nov 2016 Q8a) Show handle, head with flat face and round pein.
  • (ii) Flat Screw Driver: (Repeated from Nov 2016 Q8a) Show handle, shaft, flat tip.
  • (iii) Centre Punch: Show shaft/body (often knurled), conical point (90° included angle typically), striking head.
  • (iv) Combination Pliers: (Repeated from Nov 2015 Q6a) Show handles, pivot, flat/curved gripping jaws, wire cutter.

(c) Locus Construction (Cycloid):

(Similar to July 2017 Q5a)

Construct the path of point P on the circumference of a circle (diameter 35 mm, radius R=17.5 mm) as it rolls along a straight line for one revolution.

  1. Draw baseline. Draw circle tangent to baseline (P at bottom).
  2. Mark distance = Circumference = $\pi D = \pi \times 35 \approx 110 \, \text{mm}$ along baseline.
  3. Divide circle and baseline distance into 12 equal parts.
  4. Plot positions of P relative to the circle's center as it rolls through each 1/12th revolution (30° rotation).
  5. Draw smooth curve (cycloid) through the plotted points.

Combines geometric construction skills for tangents and loci with basic freehand sketching ability for common hand tools.
