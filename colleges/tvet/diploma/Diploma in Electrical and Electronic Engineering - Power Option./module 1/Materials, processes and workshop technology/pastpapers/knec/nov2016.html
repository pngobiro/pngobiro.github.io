Question 1

(a) Explain the following properties of engineering materials:

(i) hardness;

(ii) malleability.

(b) (i) State two reasons why 'finishing' is done on an engineering product.

(ii) Distinguish between lacquering and enamelling finishing processes.

(c) Describe the two techniques of filing.

(d) Draw a labelled diagram showing the extraction of aluminium by electrolysis.

Answer

This question covers material properties, surface finishing, filing techniques, and aluminium extraction.

(a) Material Properties Explained:

  • (i) Hardness: (Repeated from July 2015 Q1c & July 2014 Q2a) A material's resistance to scratching, abrasion, indentation, or penetration by another harder material. It relates to wear resistance.
  • (ii) Malleability: (Repeated from July 2016 Q1c) The ability of a material to deform plastically under compressive stress without fracturing. It is commonly characterized by the ability to be hammered or rolled into thin sheets.

(b) Finishing Processes:

  • (i) Reasons for Finishing (Two):
    1. Protection: To protect the underlying material from corrosion, wear, abrasion, or environmental degradation (e.g., painting, plating, anodizing).
    2. Appearance/Aesthetics: To improve the visual appeal of the product (e.g., polishing, painting, texturing, decorative plating).
    3. Improved Functionality: To provide specific surface properties like reduced friction (lubricious coatings), increased hardness (case hardening), improved electrical insulation, or better adhesion.
    4. Cleaning/Hygiene: To provide a smooth, easily cleanable surface (e.g., enamel on cookware, stainless steel polish).
  • (ii) Lacquering vs. Enamelling Distinction:
    • Lacquering: Applying a thin layer of lacquer (a clear or coloured coating, typically solvent-based, made from resins like nitrocellulose or acrylic) that dries by solvent evaporation to form a hard, protective film. It's generally applied at room temperature or slightly elevated temperatures and provides moderate protection and aesthetic enhancement, often used on wood or metal.
    • Enamelling (Vitreous Enamelling): Applying a layer of powdered glass frit (mixed with pigments) to a metal surface and firing it at high temperatures (750-850°C). The glass melts, fuses to the metal, and solidifies upon cooling to form a hard, durable, glassy, corrosion-resistant coating. Provides much higher durability and heat resistance than lacquer. (See also Nov 2015 Q2b).

    Distinction: Key differences are the coating material (resin vs. glass), application temperature (low vs. high temperature firing), and resulting properties (lacquer is less durable/heat resistant than enamel).

(c) Techniques of Filing (Assuming Cross and Draw Filing):

  • Cross Filing: The file is pushed diagonally across the workpiece surface (e.g., at roughly 45° to the edge). On the next pass, the direction is changed to be diagonal in the opposite direction, creating a cross-hatched pattern. This method is used for rapid material removal and achieving a reasonably flat surface.
  • Draw Filing: The file is held with both hands (one on the handle, one on the file tip) perpendicular to the direction of motion. It is then pushed and pulled along the length of the workpiece surface without lifting. This technique produces a finer finish than cross filing and is used for final smoothing, straightening edges, and removing marks left by cross filing. It removes less material per stroke.

(d) Extraction of Aluminium by Electrolysis (Labelled Diagram):

(Repeated from July 2015 Q1d)

The process is the Hall-Héroult process.

  1. Purified Alumina (Al$_2$O$_3$) is dissolved in molten Cryolite (Na$_3$AlF$_6$) in a large electrolysis cell.
  2. The cell has a carbon lining acting as the cathode (-).
  3. Carbon blocks suspended in the melt act as anodes (+).
  4. High current passes through the melt (~1000°C).
  5. Aluminium ions (Al$^{3+}$) are reduced at the cathode to molten aluminium.
  6. Oxide ions (O$^{2-}$) are oxidized at the anode, reacting with the carbon to form CO$_2$.

Labels: Steel Shell, Carbon Lining (Cathode), Molten Aluminium, Molten Electrolyte (Cryolite + Alumina), Carbon Anodes, Busbar Connection, Tapping Hole (optional).

The question assesses understanding of material properties, the purpose and types of finishing, basic hand tool techniques (filing), and the industrial electrolytic process for aluminium production.

Question 2

(a) State two:

(i) (I) reasons for the formulation of IEE regulations;

(II) conditions upon which a person can get an electric shock.

(ii) List four obligations on an employer at a work place as stipulated in the Safety and Health Act.

(b) Outline two reasons for using a cutting fluid with high speed tools.

(c) Describe the functions of the following lathe machine parts:

(i) bed;

(ii) headstock.

(d) Sketch the following 'left hand' lathe machine cutting tools:

(i) facing;

(ii) roughing;

(iii) finishing.

Answer

This question covers electrical safety regulations, occupational safety laws, machining fluids, lathe components, and lathe tool geometry.

(a) Electrical Safety and Occupational Health:

  • (i) (I) Reasons for IEE Regulations (BS 7671 Wiring Regulations):
    1. Safety: To provide requirements for electrical installations to protect persons, property, and livestock from dangers like electric shock, fire, burns, and explosions arising from the use of electricity.
    2. Functionality: To ensure electrical installations function correctly for their intended purpose and operate reliably.
    3. Standardization: To provide a common standard for design, erection, inspection, testing, and maintenance of electrical installations, promoting consistency and quality.
  • (i) (II) Conditions for Electric Shock:
    1. A person must come into contact with a conductive part that is live at a hazardous voltage (relative to earth or another conductive part).
    2. The person must simultaneously be in contact with earth or another conductor at a different potential, allowing current to flow through their body.
    3. The magnitude and duration of the current flowing through the body must be sufficient to cause harm (muscle contraction, breathing difficulty, burns, fibrillation).
    4. The body's resistance (influenced by skin condition - wet/dry, contact area) must be low enough to allow significant current flow at the given voltage.
  • (ii) Employer Obligations under Safety and Health Act (e.g., OSHA 2007 Kenya - Four Obligations):
    1. Provide and maintain a workplace that is safe and without risks to health.
    2. Provide and maintain safe plant and systems of work.
    3. Make arrangements for ensuring safety and absence of risks in connection with the use, handling, storage, and transport of articles and substances.
    4. Provide necessary information, instruction, training, and supervision to ensure the safety and health of employees.
    5. Provide and maintain adequate welfare facilities for employees.
    6. Conduct risk assessments and implement necessary preventive/protective measures.
    7. Establish a safety and health committee where required.
    8. Report accidents, dangerous occurrences, and occupational diseases to the relevant authority.

(b) Reasons for Using Cutting Fluid with High Speed Tools (Two):

  • Cooling: High-speed cutting generates significant heat at the tool-workpiece interface. Cutting fluid absorbs and carries away this heat, preventing the tool from overheating (which can cause softening, rapid wear, or failure) and preventing thermal damage to the workpiece.
  • Lubrication: Reduces friction between the cutting tool face and the chip, and between the tool flank and the newly machined surface. This reduces cutting forces, lowers heat generation, improves surface finish, and reduces tool wear (especially built-up edge formation).
  • Chip Removal: Helps flush away chips from the cutting zone, preventing them from interfering with the cutting action or damaging the surface finish.

(c) Lathe Machine Part Functions:

  • (i) Bed: The main foundation and structural base of the lathe. It provides a rigid platform and accurately machined guideways (ways) upon which the carriage, tailstock, and other components slide. Its rigidity and accuracy are crucial for precision machining.
  • (ii) Headstock: Located at one end of the bed (usually the left), it houses the main spindle, the drive mechanism (motor, gears, belts) to rotate the spindle, and often the gear train for selecting spindle speeds and controlling feeds for threading. The spindle holds the workpiece via a chuck or faceplate.

(d) Sketches of 'Left Hand' Lathe Cutting Tools:

"Left Hand" typically means the tool cuts when fed from right to left (towards the headstock). Sketches should show the tool bit shape, particularly the cutting edge angles.

  • (i) Facing Tool (Left Hand): Designed to cut on the end face of the workpiece. The main cutting edge is angled to allow the tool to feed radially outwards from the center when facing. A left-hand facing tool would typically have its main cutting edge angled such that it cuts effectively as it moves towards the operator when facing the end near the tailstock. Often has a sharp point or small nose radius.
  • (ii) Roughing Tool (Left Hand): Designed for rapid removal of material along the diameter (turning). Often has a larger nose radius than a finishing tool for strength and may have chipbreaker geometry. The side cutting edge angle is oriented to lead into the cut when feeding leftwards.
  • (iii) Finishing Tool (Left Hand): Designed to produce a smooth surface finish and accurate final dimension. Typically has a smaller side cutting edge angle and often a larger nose radius than a pointed roughing tool (but smaller than a round nose rougher) to smooth the surface. Cuts are light.

Covers important aspects of electrical and workplace safety legislation, the function of cutting fluids, descriptions of essential lathe components, and recognition of different lathe tool geometries.

Question 3

(a) Define the term 'tolerance' with reference to workshop measurements.

(b) State three:

(i) categories of fits;

(ii) functions of a steel rule.

(c) Draw a labelled diagram of a vernier callipers.

(d) Outline:

(i) two factors considered when selecting a hacksaw blade;

(ii) three safety precautions observed when cutting a work piece using a hacksaw blade.

Answer

This question deals with measurement concepts (tolerance, fits), basic measuring tools (steel rule, vernier callipers), and hand tool usage (hacksaw).

(a) Tolerance Definition:

(Repeated from July 2016 Q2c) Tolerance is the total permissible variation allowed in the size or dimension of a manufactured part. It is the difference between the maximum and minimum permissible limits of size (upper limit - lower limit). Tolerance defines the acceptable range of inaccuracy for a dimension.

(b) Fits and Steel Rule Functions:

  • (i) Categories of Fits (Three): Fit refers to the degree of tightness or looseness between two mating parts (e.g., a shaft and a hole) resulting from the difference in their sizes before assembly. Based on the relationship between the tolerance zones of the hole and shaft:
    1. Clearance Fit: Always results in a clearance (gap) between the mating parts when assembled (largest shaft is smaller than the smallest hole). Allows relative motion (e.g., sliding, rotation).
    2. Interference Fit (Press Fit): Always results in an interference (overlap) between the mating parts before assembly (smallest shaft is larger than the largest hole). Requires force or temperature difference to assemble, resulting in a tight, fixed joint.
    3. Transition Fit: May result in either a clearance or an interference depending on the actual sizes of the mating parts within their tolerance zones. Used where accurate location is needed but assembly/disassembly without excessive force is desired.
  • (ii) Functions of a Steel Rule (Three):
    1. Measuring lengths and dimensions directly with moderate accuracy (typically to 0.5mm or 1/64 inch).
    2. Setting or transferring measurements to other tools like dividers or calipers.
    3. Providing a straight edge for drawing or scribing straight lines.
    4. Checking surfaces for flatness over short distances (using the edge).

(c) Labelled Diagram of Vernier Callipers:

(Repeated from July 2014 Q3d) Sketch should show: Fixed Jaw, Moving Jaw, Internal Jaws, Main Scale, Vernier Scale, Depth Rod, Thumb Screw/Locking Screw.

(d) Hacksaw Blade Selection and Safety:

  • (i) Factors for Selecting Hacksaw Blade (Two):
    1. Material Being Cut: Harder materials require blades made of harder material (e.g., High-Speed Steel - HSS, Bi-Metal) and often finer teeth. Softer materials can use less expensive blades (e.g., High Carbon Steel) and coarser teeth.
    2. Thickness/Cross-Section of Material: The number of teeth per inch (TPI) should be chosen so that at least two or three teeth are in contact with the workpiece thickness at any time. Use finer TPI (e.g., 24-32 TPI) for thin sections (sheet metal, tubing) and coarser TPI (e.g., 14-18 TPI) for thicker sections (solid bars).
    3. Type of Frame: Ensure the blade length matches the hacksaw frame.
    4. Desired Finish/Cutting Speed: Coarser teeth cut faster but leave a rougher finish; finer teeth cut slower but leave a smoother finish.
  • (ii) Safety Precautions When Using Hacksaw (Three):
    1. Ensure the workpiece is securely clamped in a vice to prevent movement during cutting.
    2. Mount the blade correctly with teeth pointing forward (away from the handle) and ensure proper tension (firm but not overtightened, check frame instructions).
    3. Use steady, controlled strokes, applying pressure only on the forward stroke and using most of the blade length. Avoid excessive force or speed.
    4. Keep hands and fingers clear of the blade and the workpiece edges during operation.
    5. Wear safety glasses to protect eyes from flying chips.
    6. If a blade breaks, stop immediately and replace it carefully, ensuring the replacement is correctly tensioned.

Focuses on measurement principles (tolerance, fits), the function and depiction of measuring tools (rule, vernier), and the practical aspects of selecting and safely using a hacksaw.

Question 4

(a) Describe the following metal joining processes:

(i) riveting;

(ii) bolting.

(b) Distinguish between 'soft soldering' and 'brazing'.

(c) Sketch the following self secured joints used in sheet metal:

(i) 'paned down' bottom joint;

(ii) slip joint;

(iii) grooved seam.

(d) (i) List two machines used in sheet metal fabrication.

(ii) Outline four safety precautions observed when sharpening cutting tools on a grinding wheel.

Answer

This question covers metal joining methods (mechanical and thermal), sheet metal seams, fabrication machines, and grinding safety.

(a) Metal Joining Processes Described:

  • (i) Riveting: A method of creating a permanent mechanical joint between two or more pieces of material (usually metal plates) using rivets. Holes are drilled or punched through the overlapping parts. A rivet (a metal pin with a pre-formed head on one end) is inserted through the holes. The plain end (tail) of the rivet is then deformed (upset) using a hammer or a riveting gun to form a second head, securely clamping the parts together.
  • (ii) Bolting: A method of creating a non-permanent (demountable) mechanical joint using bolts and nuts. A bolt (a threaded fastener with a head) is passed through clearance holes in the parts to be joined. A nut (an internally threaded fastener) is then screwed onto the protruding thread of the bolt. Tightening the nut clamps the parts together. Washers are often used under the bolt head and/or nut.

(b) Soft Soldering vs. Brazing Distinction:

(Repeated from Nov 2015 Q3a)

Key distinctions:

  • Temperature: Soft soldering uses filler metals melting below 450°C; Brazing uses filler metals melting above 450°C (but below base metal melting point).
  • Strength: Brazed joints are significantly stronger than soft soldered joints.
  • Filler Metals: Soft solders are typically tin-based alloys; Brazing alloys are typically copper, silver, or aluminium based.
  • Applications: Soft soldering for electronics, light sheet metal, some plumbing; Brazing for stronger structural joints, plumbing, tool tipping.

(c) Sketches of Self-Secured Sheet Metal Joints:

Requires freehand sketches showing the cross-section or formation of the joints.

  • (i) 'Paned Down' Bottom Joint: (Repeated from July 2016 Q3d) Used for bottoms of containers. Body edge flanged inwards, base edge folded up over the flange, then hammered flat against the body.
  • (ii) Slip Joint (Not a standard self-secured seam, maybe means a simple overlap or specific type?): If referring to a simple ducting slip joint, one pipe end is slightly smaller (crimped) to slide inside the larger end of the next section, often secured with screws or tape, not truly self-secured by folding. If referring to a type of folded seam used for adjustability or sliding, needs clarification. Assuming a simple folded edge connection: Two edges folded, one slips into the other's fold.
  • (iii) Grooved Seam (Lock Seam): (Repeated from July 2016 Q3d) Edges are folded, hooked together, and flattened/grooved for a strong, interlocking seam used on flat sheets or cylinder bodies.

The term "slip joint" in sheet metal can have different meanings depending on context (e.g., plumbing P-trap slip nuts, adjustable duct fittings). A basic folded slip connection is sketched above.

(d) Sheet Metal Machines and Grinding Safety:

  • (i) Machines Used in Sheet Metal Fabrication (Two):
    • Guillotine (Shear) - For straight cuts.
    • Bending Brake (Folder) - For straight bends.
    • Slip Rolls (Bending Rolls) - For forming curves/cylinders.
    • Punch Press - For punching holes or shapes.
    • Notching Machine - For cutting notches in edges/corners.
    • Seaming Machine - For closing seams automatically.
  • (ii) Safety Precautions for Grinding Wheel Sharpening (Four):
    1. Eye Protection: ALWAYS wear safety glasses or a full face shield to protect against flying sparks, abrasive particles, and fragments from the wheel or tool.
    2. Wheel Condition Check: Before starting, visually inspect the grinding wheel for cracks or damage. Perform a 'ring test' (tap gently with a non-metallic handle) on larger wheels – a good wheel sounds clear, a cracked one sounds dull. Ensure the wheel is rated for the grinder's speed.
    3. Tool Rest Adjustment: Adjust the tool rest so it is close to the wheel (typically 1-3 mm gap) and slightly below the wheel centerline. This prevents the tool from being jammed between the rest and the wheel. Ensure the rest is securely tightened.
    4. Proper Grinding Technique: Hold the tool firmly against the tool rest. Apply gradual pressure. Keep the tool moving across the face of the wheel to ensure even wear and prevent overheating of the tool tip. Cool the tool frequently in water (if appropriate for the tool steel) to prevent loss of temper.
    5. Wheel Guards: Ensure all guards are securely in place and properly adjusted. Stand to one side of the wheel when starting the grinder, not directly in line.
    6. Avoid Side Grinding: Do not grind on the side of a standard bench grinding wheel unless it is specifically designed for side grinding.

Covers mechanical joining (rivets, bolts), thermal joining distinctions (soldering/brazing), sheet metal seam types, fabrication machinery, and crucial safety practices for using grinding wheels.

Question 5

Figure 1 shows a pictorial view of an object. Draw, full size in first angle projection, the following views:

(i) front elevation in the direction of arrow A;

(ii) end elevation in the direction of arrow B;

(iii) plan in the direction of arrow P.

Insert six major dimensions.

Answer

This question requires creating standard orthographic views (Front Elevation, End Elevation, Plan) in First Angle Projection from the provided pictorial view (Figure 1). Six major dimensions must be included.

Understanding First Angle Projection:

  • Object imagined in the first quadrant.
  • Plan (View P, Top View) is drawn BELOW the Front Elevation (View A).
  • End Elevation from the Left (View B) is drawn to the RIGHT of the Front Elevation (View A).

Analysis of Figure 1:

  • A base block with angled/sloped top surfaces.
  • A central raised section with a hole or bore through it.
  • Possible steps or grooves on the sides or base.
  • Dimensions need to be taken from the pictorial view.

Required Views:

  • (i) Front Elevation (View A): Shows the view looking along arrow A. Will show overall width and height profile, including the raised central section and the angled surfaces meeting it.
  • (ii) End Elevation (View B): Shows the view looking along arrow B (Left End View). Drawn to the RIGHT of the Front Elevation. Will show overall depth and height, the profile of the end including the slope, and the central bore (likely as hidden detail).
  • (iii) Plan (View P): Drawn BELOW the Front Elevation. Shows the view looking down along arrow P. Will show overall width and depth, the top surface features including the projection of the angled surfaces and the circular bore.

Procedure:

  1. Set up drawing sheet for First Angle Projection.
  2. Draw the Front Elevation (A) based on the pictorial view, accurately transferring width and height dimensions. Show hidden details (like the bore).
  3. Project lines vertically downwards from the Front Elevation for the Plan (P). Draw the Plan view below, using depth dimensions from the pictorial and aligning widths. Show the top features and hidden lines.
  4. Project lines horizontally to the right from the Front Elevation for the End Elevation (B). Draw the End Elevation to the right, using depth dimensions (same as Plan widths) and aligning heights. Show hidden lines.
  5. Add the First Angle projection symbol.
  6. Add six major dimensions (overall width, height, depth; location/size of bore, angles, or step locations) following standard dimensioning practices.

Requires accurate orthographic drafting in First Angle Projection, interpreting the pictorial view, correctly representing features including angled surfaces and holes, showing hidden details, and adding appropriate dimensioning.

Question 6

Figure 2 shows the intersection of a square prism and a square base pyramid. Re-draw the given views and draw the following:

(i) line of intersection;

(ii) complete plan;

(iii) end elevation;

(iv) development of the pyramid.

Answer

This question involves determining the lines of intersection between two penetrating solids (a square prism and a square pyramid) and constructing related views and the development of one solid.

Procedure:

  1. Redraw Given Views: Accurately copy the Front Elevation and Plan views provided in Figure 2, showing the relative positions, dimensions, and orientation of the prism and pyramid.
  2. (i) Line of Intersection:
    • This involves finding where the surfaces (faces and edges) of the prism pierce the surfaces (faces) of the pyramid.
    • Use cutting planes or element lines. For plane-sided objects like these:
    • Identify where edges of the prism intersect the faces of the pyramid in one view (e.g., Plan).
    • Project these points onto the corresponding edges/faces in the other view (Elevation).
    • Identify where edges of the pyramid intersect the faces of the prism in one view.
    • Project these points onto the corresponding edges/faces in the other view.
    • Connect the obtained intersection points with straight lines (since flat surfaces intersect in straight lines) in both views to define the lines of intersection. Pay attention to visibility (solid vs. hidden lines).
  3. (ii) Complete Plan: The initial Plan view might be incomplete. Ensure the lines of intersection found in step (i) are correctly projected and drawn onto the Plan view, showing which parts of the prism and pyramid are visible or hidden from the top.
  4. (iii) End Elevation: (Assuming Right End View drawn to right in Third Angle, or Left End View drawn to right in First Angle).
    • Project heights horizontally from the Front Elevation.
    • Project depths from the Plan (using 45-degree projection or measurement).
    • Draw the end view outlines of both the prism and the pyramid.
    • Project the points defining the lines of intersection from both the Front Elevation (heights) and Plan (depths) to accurately plot the intersection lines in the End Elevation. Show visible and hidden lines correctly.
  5. (iv) Development of the Pyramid:
    • This requires finding the true lengths of the pyramid's slant edges and base edges.
    • Use radial line development. Find the true length of a slant edge (e.g., by rotating an edge in the plan until it's parallel to the view line for the elevation, then project up to find true length in elevation, or use Pythagoras if height and base diagonal/2 are known).
    • Using the true slant edge length as radius, draw an arc from an apex point O'.
    • Step off the base side lengths (true lengths) along this arc using compasses. Since it's a square base, step off four equal lengths.
    • Connect the endpoints on the arc back to the apex O' to form the four triangular faces of the pyramid.
    • Determine the true shape of the intersection lines on each pyramid face (where the prism cuts through). This involves finding the true distances from the apex along the slant edges (or element lines on the faces) to the intersection points.
    • Transfer these true distances onto the corresponding lines in the development.
    • Connect these points on the development to show the shape of the hole or cut needed to accommodate the intersecting prism.
    • Add the square base attached to one of the base edges in the development if full development is required.

Requires proficiency in orthographic projection, methods for finding lines of intersection between geometric solids, and radial line development techniques for pyramids.

Question 7

(a) Figure 3 shows a link AB pin-jointed at end A. End B is constrained to slide along the path XY. Draw the locus of the midpoint M for one revolution of the crank OA. (15 marks)

(b) Construct a hexagon, using a pair of compasses and a ruler only, in a circle of diameter 80 mm. (5 marks)

Answer

This question involves constructing the locus of a point on a mechanism and constructing a regular hexagon within a given circle.

(a) Locus Construction (Slider-Crank Variation):

This mechanism is a variation of the slider-crank mechanism. Crank OA rotates about fixed point O. Link AB connects the crank pin A to a slider B, which moves along the straight path XY passing through O.

  1. Redraw the Mechanism: Draw the fixed point O, the path XY passing through O, the crank OA in its initial position (as shown or choose one, e.g., horizontal right), and the link AB connecting A to a point B on XY. Mark the midpoint M of AB. Note the lengths OA and AB from the figure.
  2. Divide Crank Rotation: Divide the circular path of point A (circle centered at O with radius OA) into a number of equal divisions, representing equal time intervals for one revolution (e.g., 12 divisions, 30° each). Label these points A0, A1, A2,... A12=A0.
  3. Plot Slider Positions: For each position of A (A0, A1, A2,...), determine the corresponding position of B on the line XY. To do this, set the compass radius to the length of the link AB. Place the compass point at the current position of A (e.g., A1) and draw an arc intersecting the line XY. This intersection point is the corresponding position of B (B1). Repeat for all positions A0 to A11.
  4. Plot Midpoint Positions: For each pair of corresponding points (A0, B0), (A1, B1), (A2, B2), etc., find the midpoint M. This can be done by construction (bisecting the line segment A_i B_i) or measurement. Label these midpoints M0, M1, M2,... M11.
  5. Draw the Locus: Draw a smooth curve connecting the plotted midpoint positions M0, M1, M2,... M11 back to M0. This curve is the required locus of the midpoint M for one full revolution of the crank OA. The locus will likely be an oval or elliptical shape.

(b) Hexagon in Circle Construction:

Constructing a regular hexagon inside a circle of diameter 80 mm (radius R = 40 mm) using only compass and ruler.

  1. Draw Circle: Draw the circle with center O and radius R = 40 mm.
  2. Mark Starting Point: Choose any point P on the circumference as the first vertex.
  3. Step off Radius: Set the compass radius to the radius of the circle (R = 40 mm). The side length of a regular hexagon inscribed in a circle is equal to the circle's radius.
  4. Mark Vertices: Place the compass point at P and draw an arc intersecting the circle. This is the second vertex (Q). Move the compass point to Q and draw another arc intersecting the circle (R). Continue this process around the circle (S, T, U). The sixth arc should intersect back at the starting point P.
  5. Connect Vertices: Join the consecutive vertices P-Q-R-S-T-U-P with straight lines using the ruler to form the regular hexagon inscribed in the circle.

Part (a) requires careful step-by-step plotting of points based on the mechanism's movement. Part (b) uses the fundamental property that the side length of a regular inscribed hexagon equals the radius of the circumscribing circle.

Question 8

(a) Sketch the following hand tools:

(i) ball pein hammer;

(ii) flat screw driver.

(b) Figure 4 shows two views of a solid drawn in $3^{\text {rd }}$ angle projection. Draw an isometric view of the object taking corner P as the lowest point.

Answer

This question involves sketching basic hand tools and constructing an isometric view from third angle orthographic projections.

(a) Hand Tool Sketches:

Requires freehand sketches.

  • (i) Ball Pein Hammer: (Repeated from July 2015 Q7a) Show handle, head with a flat striking face and a rounded (ball) pein end, wedge.
  • (ii) Flat Screw Driver: (Repeated from July 2014 Q8a) Show handle, shaft (shank), and flat, wedge-shaped tip.

(b) Isometric View from Third Angle Projections:

Requires constructing an isometric view from the given Front Elevation and Plan (Figure 4, Third Angle layout: Plan Above Front Elevation), making corner P the lowest point.

Understanding Views (Third Angle):

  • Plan (Top View) is ABOVE the Front Elevation.
  • Front Elevation shows height and width.
  • Plan shows width and depth.

Analysis of Figure 4:

  • The object appears to be a base block with a stepped or multi-level structure on top, possibly including slots or cutouts.
  • Corner P is identified on the views.

Isometric Construction Steps:

  1. Orient Axes: Set up isometric axes (vertical, 30° left, 30° right) with corner P as the origin (lowest point). Determine which directions correspond to Width, Depth, and Height based on the Front Elevation and Plan views relative to corner P.
  2. Overall Dimensions: Extract overall Width, Height, Depth from the orthographic views.
  3. Construct Base: Draw the base footprint starting from P along the Width and Depth isometric axes, based on the Plan view.
  4. Build Height: Project heights vertically from the base features using dimensions from the Front Elevation.
  5. Transfer Features: Map all steps, slots, and surfaces from the Front Elevation and Plan onto the isometric drawing, drawing lines parallel to the isometric axes.
  6. Connect & Refine: Complete the object outline by connecting points. Darken visible edges and erase construction lines. Omit hidden lines unless necessary.

Part (a) tests basic sketching skills. Part (b) requires accurate interpretation of Third Angle orthographic views and construction of an isometric projection with a specified orientation.
