Question 1 (20 marks)

(a) List three safety practices to be observed in the workshop in regard to each of the following:

(i) hand tools;

(ii) tidiness. (6 marks)

(b) (i) List four electric hazards that may occur in the workshop.

(ii) State three classes of fires and their respective sources. (6 marks)

(c) (i) State three causes of burns that may occur in the workshop.

(ii) Outline the procedure for treatment of burns and scalds. (8 marks)

Answer

This question covers workshop safety practices related to tools, tidiness, electrical hazards, fire classification, and first aid for burns.

(a) Safety Practices:

  • (i) Hand Tools Safety Practices (Three):
    1. Use the Right Tool for the Job: Avoid using tools for purposes they were not designed for (e.g., screwdriver as a chisel).
    2. Inspect Tools Before Use: Check for damage (e.g., cracked handles, mushroomed chisel heads, dull blades, loose parts). Do not use damaged tools.
    3. Handle and Carry Safely: Carry sharp tools with points/edges facing down. Pass tools handle-first to others. Never throw tools.
    4. Use Correctly: Cut away from your body. Ensure a firm grip. Use appropriate PPE (gloves, eye protection).
    5. Store Properly: Return tools to their designated storage (tool racks, boxes) after use. Protect sharp edges.
  • (ii) Tidiness Safety Practices (Three):
    1. Keep Walkways Clear: Ensure floors, aisles, and exits are free from obstructions (tools, materials, cables, waste).
    2. Clean Up Spills Immediately: Promptly clean up oil, grease, water, or other spills to prevent slips.
    3. Store Materials Properly: Stack materials securely and avoid overloading shelves. Keep flammable materials in designated areas.
    4. Dispose of Waste Regularly: Empty waste bins frequently. Dispose of oily rags and hazardous waste in appropriate containers.
    5. Maintain Order: Return tools and equipment to their storage locations after use. Keep workbenches tidy.

(b) Electrical Hazards and Fire Classes:

  • (i) Electric Hazards in Workshop (Four):
    1. Electric Shock (contact with live parts).
    2. Electrical Burns (current passage or arc flash).
    3. Fire (from overheating, short circuits, sparks igniting flammables).
    4. Explosion (electrical spark igniting flammable atmosphere).
    5. Faulty Equipment/Wiring (damaged insulation, improper grounding).
    6. Static Electricity Discharge (can damage sensitive electronics or ignite flammables).
    7. Overloading Circuits.
  • (ii) Fire Classes and Sources (Three): (Similar to July 2020 Q1a)
    1. Class A: Source - Ordinary combustibles (wood, paper, cloth, plastics).
    2. Class B: Source - Flammable liquids (petrol, oil, paint, grease).
    3. Class C: Source - Flammable gases (propane, butane, acetylene).
    4. (Class D: Source - Combustible metals like magnesium, sodium).
    5. (Class F/K: Source - Cooking oils/fats).

(c) Burns and Scalds:

  • (i) Causes of Burns/Scalds in Workshop (Three):
    1. Thermal Burns: Contact with hot surfaces (machines, engines, welded parts), hot liquids/steam (scalds), open flames (welding, forge), friction.
    2. Electrical Burns: Passage of electric current through the body, or from arc flash heat.
    3. Chemical Burns: Contact with corrosive substances (acids, alkalis, solvents).
    4. Radiation Burns: Overexposure to UV radiation (welding arcs without protection), intense infrared, or ionizing radiation.
  • (ii) First Aid Procedure for Burns and Scalds (Thermal):
    1. Ensure Safety: Make sure the source of the burn is removed or neutralized (stop the burning process).
    2. Cool the Burn Immediately: Hold the affected area under cool (not ice cold) running water for at least 10-20 minutes, or until the pain subsides. This helps reduce tissue damage and pain.
    3. Remove Constrictions: Gently remove any rings, watches, belts, or tight clothing from the burned area before it starts to swell. Do not remove clothing stuck to the burn.
    4. Cover the Burn: Cover the burn loosely with a sterile, non-fluffy dressing (like sterile gauze or cling film/plastic wrap - do not wrap tightly). This protects against infection and reduces pain by keeping air off the surface.
    5. Keep Warm: Keep the casualty warm with a blanket, as cooling a large burn area can lead to hypothermia, but do not place blankets directly on the burn itself.
    6. Seek Medical Attention: Seek professional medical help for all but very minor superficial burns. Seek immediate help for large burns, deep burns, electrical/chemical burns, burns on face/hands/feet/genitals, or if the casualty is an infant/elderly person or shows signs of shock.
    7. Do NOT: Do not apply ointments, creams, butter, or lotions. Do not burst blisters. Do not use adhesive dressings directly on the burn.

    Treatment for chemical burns involves flushing with copious amounts of water (after brushing off dry chemicals). Electrical burns always require immediate medical evaluation.

Covers fundamental workshop safety rules for tools and tidiness, common electrical hazards, fire classification, causes of burns, and standard first aid treatment for thermal burns.

Question 2 (20 marks)

(a) State two parts of each of the following tools:

(i) engineer's steel wire;

(ii) odd leg caliper;

(iii) divider.

(b) Illustrate the use of a vernier caliper in carrying out each of the following:

(i) internal measurement;

(ii) external measurement;

(iii) depth measurement;

(iv) stepped measurement.

(c) (i) Describe a micrometer.

(ii) List two care and maintenance measures for the micrometer.

Answer

This question focuses on identifying parts of common measuring/marking tools and describing the use and care of precision instruments like vernier calipers and micrometers.

(a) Tool Parts:

Part (a)(i) "engineer's steel wire" seems incorrect. Assuming it meant "engineer's steel rule" or perhaps "wire gauge". Answering for "steel rule".

  • (i) Engineer's Steel Rule (Two Parts):
    1. Body/Blade: The main flat strip of steel containing the markings.
    2. Graduations/Markings: The engraved or etched lines indicating units of measurement (e.g., mm, cm, inches).
    3. Edge(s): The straight edges used for measuring against or drawing lines.
    4. End(s): The starting/finishing points of the scale.
  • (ii) Odd Leg Caliper (Jenny/Hermaphrodite) (Two Parts): (See July 2021 Q1b)
    1. Legs (Two): One straight leg with a sharp point, one leg bent inwards at the tip.
    2. Pivot/Joint: Connects the two legs.
    3. Adjusting Mechanism (Optional): Spring and nut for fine adjustment (on spring joint type).
    4. Point: The sharp tip on one leg.
    5. Bent Leg/Heel: The inwardly bent tip on the other leg.
  • (iii) Divider (Two Parts): (See Nov 2018 Q3b)
    1. Legs (Two): Both legs have sharp points.
    2. Pivot/Joint: Connects the two legs.
    3. Adjusting Mechanism (Spring Type): Bow spring and adjusting nut.
    4. Points: The sharp tips on both legs.

(b) Illustrating Vernier Caliper Use:

Requires sketches showing the caliper parts interacting with the workpiece feature being measured.

  • (i) Internal Measurement: Sketch showing the *internal jaws* inserted inside a hole, opened to touch the inner surfaces.
  • (ii) External Measurement: Sketch showing the *external jaws* closed around the outside of an object (e.g., diameter of a rod, thickness of a block).
  • (iii) Depth Measurement: Sketch showing the end of the caliper body resting on the top surface, with the *depth rod* extended into a blind hole until it touches the bottom.
  • (iv) Stepped Measurement: Sketch showing the step between two parallel surfaces being measured. The end face of the fixed jaw rests on the lower step, while the step on the end of the moving jaw slide rests on the upper step. The depth rod is not typically used for this; it relies on the main external jaws and the step machined onto the sliding jaw end.

(Sketches similar to July 2020 Q3b are needed for (i) and (iii)).

(c) Micrometer Description and Care:

  • (i) Describe a Micrometer: A precision measuring instrument used for making accurate measurements of small distances or thicknesses, typically external dimensions (though internal and depth versions exist). It works on the principle of a precisely machined screw thread. Turning a calibrated thimble advances a spindle towards a fixed anvil. The measurement is read from a combination of a main scale on the sleeve (barrel) and a vernier/circular scale on the thimble, allowing readings typically to 0.01 mm or 0.001 inch accuracy. A ratchet stop is usually included to ensure consistent measuring pressure.
  • (ii) Care and Maintenance Measures for Micrometer (Two):
    1. Cleanliness: Keep the micrometer clean, especially the measuring faces (anvil and spindle). Wipe them clean before and after use.
    2. Check Zero Setting: Regularly check the zero reading by closing the measuring faces carefully (using the ratchet). Adjust if necessary using the C-spanner provided.
    3. Proper Handling: Avoid dropping or mishandling. Do not overtighten using the thimble; always use the ratchet stop for final closure to ensure consistent pressure.
    4. Storage: Store the micrometer in its protective case when not in use, preferably with the measuring faces slightly apart. Keep it away from moisture and excessive temperature changes.
    5. Lubrication: Occasionally apply a drop of light, high-quality oil to the spindle thread if required by manufacturer instructions.

Tests identification of tool parts, understanding the different measurement capabilities of a vernier caliper, and describing the function and proper care of a micrometer.

Question 3 (20 marks)

(a) (i) List four arc welding tools and equipment.

(ii) State four safety precautions to be taken when carrying out manual metal arc welding.

(b) Illustrate the following arc welding defects:

(i) unequal leg length;

(ii) under cut;

(iii) spatter;

(iv) over reinforcement.

(c) Explain two differences between alternating current (AC) and direct current (DC).

Answer

This question focuses on arc welding equipment, safety, common defects, and the basic difference between AC and DC.

(a) Arc Welding Equipment and Safety:

  • (i) Arc Welding Tools and Equipment (Four - for MMAW/Stick assumed):
    1. Welding Power Source (AC/DC Welder).
    2. Electrode Holder (Stinger).
    3. Work Clamp (Earth Clamp).
    4. Welding Cables (Electrode and Work leads).
    5. Electrodes (Flux-coated consumables).
    6. Chipping Hammer (for slag removal).
    7. Wire Brush (for cleaning).
    8. Welding Helmet/Shield (with appropriate filter lens).
    9. Protective Clothing (Leather gloves, apron, jacket, safety boots).
  • (ii) Safety Precautions for Manual Metal Arc Welding (MMAW/Stick) (Four):
    1. Eye and Skin Protection: ALWAYS use a welding helmet with the correct shade filter lens to protect eyes from intense UV/IR radiation and visible light. Wear non-flammable protective clothing, including leather gloves, apron/jacket, and full trousers/boots to protect skin from arc rays, sparks, and spatter.
    2. Ventilation: Ensure adequate ventilation (natural or mechanical extraction) to remove hazardous fumes and gases produced during welding, especially when working in confined spaces or with certain materials/electrodes. Use respirators if needed.
    3. Electrical Safety: Inspect cables, electrode holder, and work clamp for damage before use. Ensure good electrical connections. Avoid working in wet/damp conditions. Keep cables tidy to prevent tripping hazards. Be aware of the risk of electric shock.
    4. Fire Prevention: Keep the work area clear of flammable materials (wood, paper, rags, liquids, gases). Have a suitable fire extinguisher readily available. Be aware of sparks and spatter igniting materials nearby.
    5. Slag Removal: Chip slag carefully after the weld has cooled slightly, pointing the chipping hammer away from yourself and others, and always wear eye protection as slag chips can fly off forcefully.

(b) Illustrations of Arc Welding Defects:

Requires sketches showing the weld bead profile relative to the base metal.

  • (i) Unequal Leg Length (Fillet Weld):
    Illustration: Sketch of a fillet weld cross-section where the distance from the joint root to the weld toe on one plate (leg length) is significantly different from the leg length on the other plate.
  • (ii) Undercut: (Repeated from July 2021 Q3b & July 2019 Q2c)
    Illustration: Sketch showing a groove melted into the base metal alongside the weld toe, which hasn't been filled by weld metal.
  • (iii) Spatter:
    Illustration: Sketch showing the main weld bead with numerous small droplets of solidified metal scattered on the surface of the base metal nearby.
  • (iv) Over Reinforcement (Excessive Reinforcement):
    Illustration: Sketch showing the weld metal protruding excessively above the surface of the base metal. While some reinforcement is needed, too much can cause stress concentrations at the weld toes.

(c) Differences Between AC and DC (Two):

  1. Direction of Flow:
    • DC (Direct Current): Electric charge (current) flows consistently in one direction only.
    • AC (Alternating Current): Electric charge flow periodically reverses direction, oscillating back and forth (typically 50 or 60 times per second - Hz).
  2. Source:
    • DC: Typically produced by batteries, solar cells, DC generators (dynamos), or rectified AC (using diodes in power supplies).
    • AC: Typically produced by AC generators (alternators) in power stations; it's the standard form of electricity delivered via the grid.
  3. Voltage Transformation:
    • AC: Voltage can be easily stepped up or down using transformers, making efficient long-distance transmission possible.
    • DC: Voltage transformation is more complex and less efficient, often requiring electronic converters.
  4. Applications:
    • DC: Electronics (computers, phones require DC internally), batteries, electroplating, some welding, DC motors.
    • AC: Mains power distribution, lighting, heating, AC motors (common in industry/appliances), most household power.

Covers MMAW equipment/safety, visual identification of common weld imperfections, and the fundamental differences between AC and DC electricity.

Question 4 (20 marks)

(a) List four principal parts of a lathe machine.

(b) With the aid of sketches, explain the following lathe operations:

(i) step turning;

(ii) facing.

(c) State four safety measures associated with drilling machines.

Answer

This question covers the main components of a lathe, specific lathe operations, and safety measures for drilling machines.

(a) Principal Parts of a Lathe Machine (Four):

  1. Bed: The foundation providing rigid support and accurately machined ways for other parts.
  2. Headstock: Contains the main spindle, drive mechanism (motor, gears/belts), and holds the workpiece (via chuck/faceplate).
  3. Tailstock: Slides on the bed opposite the headstock; supports the end of long workpieces or holds tools for axial operations (drilling, reaming).
  4. Carriage Assembly: Moves along the bed ways and consists of:
    • Saddle: Slides along the bed ways.
    • Cross-slide: Moves perpendicular to the lathe axis (across the bed) mounted on the saddle, controls radial tool movement/diameter.
    • Compound Rest (Top Slide): Mounted on the cross-slide, can be swiveled to any angle for turning tapers or specific angles, carries the tool post.
    • Apron: Attached to the front of the saddle, contains gears and controls for engaging automatic feeds (longitudinal/cross) and thread cutting via the leadscrew/feed rod.
  5. Tool Post: Mounted on the compound rest, securely holds the cutting tool.
  6. Leadscrew and Feed Rod: Transmit motion from the headstock gear train to the carriage for automatic feeds and thread cutting.

(b) Lathe Operations Explained with Sketches:

  • (i) Step Turning:
    • Explanation: Creating multiple cylindrical sections of different diameters on a single workpiece. This involves turning one diameter for a specific length, then changing the tool position (or using a different tool) to turn another section to a different diameter for another specific length, creating a 'step' between the diameters.
    • Sketch: Show a workpiece rotating. Show a turning tool cutting along the diameter for a certain length, then withdrawn and repositioned (or a different tool used) to cut a smaller/larger diameter adjacent to the first, creating a distinct step. Indicate tool feed direction (axial) and workpiece rotation.
  • (ii) Facing: (Repeated from July 2019 Q4b & July 2016 Q4c)
    • Explanation: Machining the end face of a workpiece flat and perpendicular to the axis of rotation. The tool feeds radially across the end.
    • Sketch: Show workpiece rotating in chuck. Show facing tool moving radially across the end face (e.g., center outwards or vice-versa). Indicate tool feed (radial) and workpiece rotation.

(c) Safety Measures Associated with Drilling Machines (Four):

(Repeated from July 2020 Q4b)

  1. Secure Workpiece: Clamp work firmly to the table; never hold small pieces by hand.
  2. Eye Protection: Wear safety glasses/face shield.
  3. No Loose Items: Avoid loose clothing, gloves, jewelry; tie back long hair.
  4. Remove Chuck Key: Always remove key immediately after use.
  5. Correct Speed/Feed: Use appropriate settings for material and drill size.
  6. Keep Hands Clear: Stay away from rotating spindle and tool.
  7. Use Guards: Ensure any guards (e.g., chuck guard) are in place.
  8. Stop Before Adjusting/Cleaning: Turn off power before making adjustments or clearing chips (use a brush).

Covers identification of main lathe parts, explanation of step turning and facing operations, and crucial safety rules for operating drilling machines.

Question 5 (20 marks)

Figure 1 shows an isometric view of an object. Draw the following for the object using first angle projection:

(a) front elevation in the direction of arrow X;

(b) side elevation in relation to (a);

(c) plan view.

Include six major dimensions and the projection symbol.

Answer

This question requires creating standard orthographic views (Front Elevation, Side Elevation, Plan) in First Angle Projection from the provided isometric view (Figure 1), including dimensions and the projection symbol.

Understanding First Angle Projection:

  • Plan (Top View) is BELOW the Front Elevation.
  • Side Elevation (View from Left) is drawn to the RIGHT of the Front Elevation.

Analysis of Figure 1:

  • A block with a sloping top surface and a central cut-out or slot feature.
  • Possibly steps or other details on the sides or bottom.
  • Arrow X indicates the direction for the Front Elevation.

Required Views:

  • (a) Front Elevation (View X): Shows the view looking along arrow X. Will display height and width profile, including the end view of the slope and the central slot.
  • (b) Side Elevation (From Left): Drawn to the RIGHT of the Front Elevation. Shows height and depth profile, including the sloping top surface and the depth of the slot.
  • (c) Plan View (From Above): Drawn BELOW the Front Elevation. Shows width and depth profile, including the top edges, the slot, and how the sloping surface projects.

Procedure:

  1. Set up drawing sheet for First Angle Projection.
  2. Draw the Front Elevation (X), transferring dimensions accurately. Show hidden details.
  3. Project lines vertically downwards for the Plan. Draw the Plan below, aligning widths and using depth dimensions. Show hidden details.
  4. Project lines horizontally to the right from the Front Elevation for the Left Side Elevation. Draw the Side Elevation to the right, aligning heights and using depth dimensions (same as Plan widths). Show hidden details.
  5. Add the First Angle projection symbol.
  6. Add six major dimensions across the views.

Requires accurate orthographic drafting in First Angle Projection, correctly interpreting the isometric view including slopes and slots, showing hidden lines, and adding dimensions and the symbol.

Question 6 (20 marks)

Figure 2 shows two views of a cast iron hinge block. Make an oblique drawing of this object with face A being front view.

Answer

This question requires constructing an oblique pictorial view from the given orthographic views (Figure 2), with a specified face (A) oriented as the front view. The type of oblique (Cavalier or Cabinet) is not specified; Cavalier (full depth) is often assumed if not stated, but Cabinet (half depth) is also common.

Analysis of Views (Figure 2):

  • Identify the Front Elevation (containing Face A) and the other view (likely Plan or End Elevation).
  • Determine the shape of the hinge block: Likely a block with hinge knuckles (cylindrical sections with holes for a pin) and possibly mounting holes or flanges.
  • Extract overall Width, Height, and Depth dimensions.

Oblique Drawing Procedure (Assuming Cavalier - Full Depth):

  1. Orientation: Position the drawing axes so that Face A is drawn true shape in the frontal plane. Receding axes go back at an angle (e.g., 45°).
  2. Draw Front Face (A): Draw Face A true size and shape using Width and Height dimensions from the orthographic views.
  3. Draw Receding Lines: From corners and key points on the front face, draw parallel receding lines at the chosen angle (e.g., 45°).
  4. Mark Full Depth: Measure the object's true Depth along these receding lines. Mark these points.
  5. Draw Back Features: Connect the ends of the receding lines to form the back edges and surfaces, drawing lines parallel to the corresponding front face lines.
  6. Add Details: Construct cylindrical features (hinge knuckles), holes, etc., remembering that circles on the front face are true circles, while circles on receding or angled faces become ellipses (though often simplified to circles in basic oblique drawings).
  7. Refine: Darken visible lines. Omit hidden lines typically.

If Cabinet projection was intended, Step 4 would involve marking HALF the true depth along receding lines.

Requires interpreting the orthographic views and applying the rules of oblique projection (likely Cavalier unless specified otherwise) to create a pictorial view with the correct front face orientation.

Question 7 (20 marks)

Figure 3 shows an incomplete front elevation of two unequal cylinders intersecting at $30^{\circ}$. Copy the elevation and construct the following:

(i) complete front elevation showing the curve of intersection.

(ii) complete plan;

(iii) surface development of cylinder B.

Answer

This question involves determining the curve of intersection between two intersecting cylinders of unequal diameter meeting at an angle, completing the orthographic views, and developing the surface of one cylinder.

Procedure:

  1. Copy Elevation: Accurately redraw the given incomplete Front Elevation from Figure 3, showing the main cylinder (A?), the intersecting cylinder (B) at 30°, and their diameters.
  2. (ii) Construct Complete Plan:
    • Draw the Plan view (assuming below Elevation for First Angle, or above for Third Angle).
    • Project the main cylinder (A) down as a circle.
    • Project the intersecting cylinder (B) down. In the Plan view, it will appear as an ellipse since it's viewed at an angle relative to its axis (unless its axis is vertical). The width of this ellipse will be the diameter of cylinder B, and its length will depend on the projection. The centerline of cylinder B in the plan will be aligned under its centerline in the elevation.
    • The curve of intersection in the Plan will be determined by projecting points from the Elevation (see next step).
  3. (i) Construct Complete Front Elevation (Curve of Intersection):
    • Use element lines. Divide the profile (circle) of the smaller cylinder (B) in an auxiliary view (or its projected elliptical shape in the plan) into equal segments (e.g., 12).
    • Draw element lines parallel to cylinder B's axis in both Elevation and Plan views.
    • Find where these element lines pierce the surface of the main cylinder (A). This is easiest seen in the Plan view where cylinder A is a circle. Mark where element lines on cylinder B cross the circle of cylinder A.
    • Project these piercing points from the Plan view vertically up to intersect the corresponding element lines on cylinder B in the Front Elevation.
    • Plot the series of intersection points found in the Front Elevation. Connect these points with a smooth curve, paying attention to visibility (some parts of the curve might be hidden behind cylinder B). This completes the elevation. Project these points back to the Plan to complete the intersection curve there.
  4. (iii) Surface Development of Cylinder B:
    • Draw a baseline equal to the circumference of cylinder B ($\pi \times D_B$). Divide this baseline into the same number of equal segments (e.g., 12) used for the element lines.
    • Draw element lines perpendicular to the baseline at each division point.
    • Measure the true length of each element line on cylinder B from a reference end (e.g., the free end) up to the point where it intersects cylinder A. These lengths can be taken directly from the Front Elevation if cylinder B's axis is parallel to the viewing plane, otherwise true lengths need to be determined.
    • Plot these varying true lengths on the corresponding element lines in the development layout.
    • Draw a smooth curve connecting the endpoints. The resulting shape is the development of cylinder B's lateral surface, shaped to fit against cylinder A.

Requires proficiency in orthographic projection, accurately determining curves of intersection between cylindrical surfaces using element lines or cutting planes, and applying parallel line development techniques.

Question 8 (20 marks)

(a) (i) Construct a regular heptagon within a circle of radius 40 mm.

(ii) Construct an isosceles triangle ABC whose included angle A is $67 \frac{1}{2}^{\circ}$, and AB = AC = 52 mm. Circumscribe the triangle.

(b) Make free hand sketches of the following engineering tools:

(i) vee block;

(ii) bench vice;

(iii) angle plate.

Answer

This question involves geometric constructions (heptagon, isosceles triangle, circumcircle) and freehand sketching of workshop equipment.

(a) Geometric Constructions:

  • (i) Regular Heptagon in Circle (Radius 40 mm): Constructing an exact regular heptagon (7 sides) using only compass and straightedge is impossible. However, accurate approximations exist. A common method:
    1. Draw the circle (Radius R=40 mm) and a diameter.
    2. Construct a perpendicular bisector to the diameter (a vertical diameter).
    3. Set compass radius to R (40mm). Place point at one end of the horizontal diameter, draw arc intersecting the circle above and below.
    4. Connect these two intersection points with a chord. The distance from the center to the midpoint of this chord is R * sqrt(3)/2. Alternatively, half the side length of the inscribed hexagon is R/2 = 20mm? No, this isn't right.
    5. **Approximate Method:** Draw circle R=40. Draw radius OA. Construct perpendicular bisector of OA, meeting circle at P. Length AP is a good approximation of the heptagon side length. Step off length AP around the circle 7 times. Connect vertices.
    6. **Another Approximation:** Draw vertical radius OC. Set compass to R=40. From bottom end of vertical diameter (D), draw arc cutting circle at E, F. Line EF intersects OC at G. Length EG (or FG) is approx side length. Step off EG around circle.

    Since exact construction is impossible, an accurate approximation method should be used and potentially noted.

  • (ii) Isosceles Triangle and Circumcircle:
    1. Construct Angle A ($67.5^{\circ}$): This angle is half of 135°, or $45° + 22.5°$. Construct a 90° angle. Bisect it to get 45°. Bisect the 45° angle again to get 22.5°. Add the 45° and 22.5° sections together. Alternatively, construct 90°, bisect (45°), bisect remaining angle between 45° and 90° (gives 67.5°).
    2. Construct Triangle: Draw vertex A with angle 67.5°. Mark off AB = 52 mm along one arm and AC = 52 mm along the other arm. Join B to C to complete the isosceles triangle.
    3. Circumscribe Circle: Construct the perpendicular bisectors of any two sides (e.g., AB and AC). The point where they intersect is the Circumcenter (O). Set compass radius to OA (or OB or OC, they are equal). Draw the circle centered at O passing through A, B, and C.

(b) Free Hand Sketches of Engineering Tools:

Requires reasonably recognizable sketches.

  • (i) Vee Block (V-Block): (Repeated from July 2015 Q4b) Show block with 90° V-groove(s). May show clamp. Used for holding round stock.
  • (ii) Bench Vice: (Repeated from July 2020 Q8b) Show fixed jaw, movable jaw, screw, handle, slide, base mounting. Used for holding work.
  • (iii) Angle Plate: (Repeated from July 2020 Q8b) Show two plates at 90°, usually cast iron, with slots/holes. Used for holding work square on surface plate or machine table.

Tests geometric construction skills (approximate heptagon, specific triangle, circumcircle) and the ability to sketch common workshop holding/fixturing equipment.
