SECTION A: MATERIALS, PROCESSES AND WORKSHOP TECHNOLOGY

Answer THREE questions from this section.

Question 1 (20 marks)

(a) (i) List four factors considered in the workshop layout;

(ii) State four objectives of workshop layout. (8 marks)

(b) Outline the procedure for performing mouth to mouth resuscitation. (6 marks)

(c) State two safety precautions with regard to each of the following:

(i) Machinery;

(ii) Electricity;

(iii) Hand tools. (6 marks)

Answer

This question covers workshop layout principles, first aid (resuscitation), and safety precautions for machinery, electricity, and hand tools.

(a) Workshop Layout:

  • (i) Factors Considered in Workshop Layout (Four): (Repeated from Nov 2022 Q1b & Nov 2017 Q1a)
    1. Workflow/Process Sequence: Logical flow of materials and operations.
    2. Space Requirements: Adequate space for machines, operation, maintenance, storage, movement.
    3. Safety and Health: Hazard minimization, clear exits, lighting, ventilation, noise control.
    4. Material Handling: Efficient methods and routes for moving materials.
    5. Flexibility/Future Expansion: Adaptability for changes.
    6. Utilities Access: Power, compressed air, water, etc.
    7. Supervision and Communication: Ease of oversight and interaction.
    8. Employee Comfort/Welfare: Ergonomics, rest areas, facilities.
  • (ii) Objectives of Workshop Layout (Four):
    1. Maximize Efficiency: Minimize material travel distance, reduce handling time, streamline workflow.
    2. Ensure Safety: Reduce accident risks by providing adequate space, clear aisles, safe machine positioning, and easy access to emergency equipment/exits.
    3. Optimize Space Utilization: Make effective use of the available floor space.
    4. Facilitate Supervision and Control: Allow for easy monitoring of operations and personnel.
    5. Promote Quality: Reduce chances of damage to materials/products during handling and processing.
    6. Provide Flexibility: Allow for adjustments due to changes in product design, process, or volume.
    7. Improve Employee Morale: Provide a safe, comfortable, and organized working environment.

(b) Mouth-to-Mouth Resuscitation Procedure:

(Repeated frequently, e.g., July 2021 Q4b, July 2020 Q1b, etc.)

Modern guidelines prioritize chest compressions (CPR) and calling emergency services. This describes the traditional method.

  1. Safety First: Ensure scene is safe (e.g., no electrical hazard).
  2. Check Response & Breathing: Check consciousness & normal breathing (<10 sec). Call emergency services.
  3. Position Victim: Flat on back, firm surface.
  4. Open Airway: Head-tilt/chin-lift.
  5. Give Breaths: Pinch nose, seal mouth, give 2 effective breaths (1 sec each, watch chest rise).
  6. Continue (If pulse present, no breathing): 1 breath every 5-6 seconds. (Start CPR if no pulse).

(c) Safety Precautions (Two for each):

  • (i) Machinery:
    1. Ensure all guards are in place and functioning before operation.
    2. Operate only machines you are trained and authorized to use; know the emergency stop location.
    3. Avoid loose clothing/jewelry/long hair; use appropriate PPE (e.g., safety glasses).
    4. Disconnect power before maintenance, adjustment, or clearing jams.
  • (ii) Electricity:
    1. Assume all circuits are live unless proven otherwise; use proper isolation procedures (LOTO).
    2. Inspect cords and equipment for damage before use; do not use damaged equipment.
    3. Use appropriate PPE (insulated tools, gloves, mats where necessary).
    4. Avoid working in wet conditions; ensure proper earthing/grounding and use RCDs where applicable.
    5. Do not overload circuits or use incorrect fuses.
  • (iii) Hand Tools: (Repeated from Nov 2021 Q1a)
    1. Use the correct tool for the specific job.
    2. Inspect tools for damage (cracked handles, mushroomed heads, dull edges) before use; do not use damaged tools.
    3. Keep cutting tools sharp and handle with points/edges away from the body.
    4. Store tools properly and safely when not in use.
    5. Wear appropriate PPE (e.g., safety glasses when chipping/cutting, gloves when handling sharp items).

Covers key aspects of workshop planning (layout factors/objectives), emergency first aid (resuscitation), and fundamental safety rules for common workshop hazards.

Question 2 (20 marks)

(a) (i) List three types of drilling machines.

(ii) Illustrate "reaming" and "boring" operations with regard to drilling.

(b) Illustrate the following lathe operations:

(i) plain turning;

(ii) undercutting.

(c) (i) List three parts of a shaping machine;

(ii) Sketch two surfaces that can be produced by a shaper.

Answer

This question covers drilling machines and operations, lathe operations, and shaping machine parts and capabilities.

(a) Drilling Machines and Operations:

  • (i) Types of Drilling Machines (Three): (Repeated from July 2020 Q4b & July 2015 Q4a)
    1. Bench Drilling Machine (Drill Press).
    2. Pillar Drilling Machine.
    3. Radial Arm Drilling Machine.
    4. Portable Electric Hand Drill.
    5. Multi-spindle Drilling Machine.
  • (ii) Illustrate Reaming and Boring: Requires sketches showing the tool action.
    • Reaming Illustration: Sketch showing a multi-fluted reamer tool entering a previously drilled hole. Indicate slow rotation and steady feed. Emphasize that it slightly enlarges the hole and improves finish/roundness.
    • Boring Illustration: Sketch showing a single-point cutting tool held in a boring bar, entering an existing hole. Indicate rotation (either tool or workpiece, depending on machine) and feed (axial or radial depending on operation). Emphasize that it enlarges the hole accurately and can correct concentricity.

(b) Lathe Operations Illustrated:

  • (i) Plain Turning (Parallel Turning): (Repeated from July 2020 Q4c)
    Illustration: Sketch showing workpiece rotating, turning tool feeding parallel to the axis of rotation, removing material from the outside diameter to create a uniform cylinder.
  • (ii) Undercutting (Grooving / Necking):
    Illustration: Sketch showing workpiece rotating. A specially shaped tool (often narrow or ground to a specific profile) is fed radially into the workpiece to cut a groove or recess of a specific width and depth. Often done near a shoulder to provide clearance for mating parts or grinding wheels.

(c) Shaping Machine Parts and Surfaces:

  • (i) Parts of a Shaping Machine (Three):
    1. Base: Foundation providing stability.
    2. Column: Main vertical structure housing drive mechanism and supporting other parts.
    3. Ram: Reciprocating arm carrying the tool head.
    4. Tool Head: Mounted on the ram, holds the tool post and often includes a clapper box (lifts tool on return stroke) and down-feed mechanism.
    5. Table: Supports the workpiece (usually in a vice); can be moved horizontally (cross-feed) and vertically.
    6. Cross Rail: Supports the table and allows its cross movement.
    7. Drive Mechanism: Motor, gears, quick-return mechanism (e.g., crank and slotted link).
  • (ii) Surfaces Produced by Shaper (Sketches): (Repeated from Nov 2022 Q4b)
    1. Flat Horizontal Surface: Sketch block with top surface machined flat.
    2. Flat Vertical Surface (Shoulder): Sketch block with a vertical face machined.
    3. Slot/Groove: Sketch block with a rectangular groove machined along its length.
    4. Dovetail/Angled Surface: Sketch block with an angled surface or dovetail slot machined (requires swiveling tool head).

Covers types of drilling machines, hole finishing operations (reaming/boring), basic lathe operations (turning/undercutting), and shaping machine components and capabilities.

Question 3 (20 marks)

(a) State two safety precautions with regard to each of the following tools:

(i) Chisels;

(ii) Files;

(iii) Hacksaws.

(b) Explain the term 'datum' in reference to marking out.

(c) (i) Define the following terms used in measurement:

(I) Limits;

(II) Fit;

(III) Tolerance.

(ii) Explain two purposes of marking out.

(d) Sketch:

(i) a Try Square;

(ii) Odd-leg caliper being used to mark the centre of the end of a round bar.

Answer

This question covers hand tool safety, marking out principles (datum, purpose), measurement terminology (limits, fit, tolerance), and sketching specific tools.

(a) Tool Safety Precautions (Two for each):

  • (i) Chisels (Cold Chisels):
    1. Wear safety glasses/goggles to protect eyes from flying chips.
    2. Ensure the cutting edge is sharp and correctly ground for the material.
    3. Discard or redress chisels with mushroomed striking heads (can splinter).
    4. Use the correct size hammer and strike squarely on the chisel head.
    5. Hold the chisel firmly but not rigidly; use knuckle guards if available.
  • (ii) Files: (Repeated from Nov 2021 Q2b & others)
    1. Always use a file with a securely fitted handle.
    2. Apply pressure only on the forward (cutting) stroke.
    3. Keep the file clean using a file card/brush.
    4. Store files separately to prevent teeth damage.
    5. Do not use as a hammer or pry bar.
  • (iii) Hacksaws: (Repeated from Nov 2021 Q3b & Nov 2016 Q3d)
    1. Ensure blade is correct TPI for the material, mounted with teeth forward, and properly tensioned.
    2. Secure workpiece firmly in a vice.
    3. Use steady strokes, applying pressure forward only, using most of the blade length.
    4. Wear safety glasses.
    5. Keep hands clear of the blade.

(b) Datum Explained:

(Repeated from Nov 2022 Q3a & July 2020 Q3a) A datum is a theoretically exact reference point, line, plane, or surface from which measurements are made and dimensions are established during marking out or technical drawing. It serves as a common origin to ensure accuracy and consistency in locating features.

(c) Measurement Terms and Marking Out Purpose:

  • (i) Definitions:
    • (I) Limits: (Repeated from July 2014 Q3a) The maximum and minimum permissible sizes for a specific dimension.
    • (II) Fit: (Repeated from Nov 2016 Q3b) The degree of tightness or looseness between two mating parts resulting from the difference between their sizes before assembly (categorized as Clearance, Interference, or Transition).
    • (III) Tolerance: (Repeated from Nov 2016 Q3a & others) The total permissible variation in a dimension; the difference between the upper and lower limits of size.
  • (ii) Purposes of Marking Out (Two): (Repeated from July 2021 Q1b & July 2016 Q2b)
    1. Provide accurate guidelines for machining/fabrication (cutting, drilling, etc.).
    2. Ensure the final part conforms to specified dimensions/shape.
    3. Minimize material waste.
    4. Allow verification of layout before cutting.

(d) Tool Sketches:

  • (i) Try Square Sketch: (Repeated from July 2021 Q1b) Sketch the L-shape with thicker stock and thinner blade at 90°.
  • (ii) Odd-leg Caliper Use Sketch: Sketch the end view of a round bar. Show the odd-leg caliper with its bent leg placed against the outside circumference and the pointed leg scribing a short arc near the center. Repeat this from 3 or 4 points around the circumference. The intersection area of the arcs indicates the approximate center of the round bar.

Covers safety for specific hand tools, the concept of datums, definition of key measurement terms, the purpose of marking out, and sketching/application of Try Square and Odd-leg Caliper.

Question 4 (20 marks)

(a) Define the following properties of materials giving one example in each case:

(i) Stiffness;

(ii) Ductility;

(iii) Tensile strength;

(iv) Semiconductor.

(b) (i) Define soft soldering as a joining method.

(ii) List four sheet metal operations.

(iii) Outline the procedure for soft-soldering.

(c) (i) State two arc-welding equipment.

(ii) List one mechanical joining process.

Answer

This question covers material properties definitions, soft soldering, sheet metal operations, arc welding equipment, and mechanical joining.

(a) Material Properties Definitions and Examples:

  • (i) Stiffness (Rigidity): A material's resistance to elastic deformation under an applied load. Quantified by the Modulus of Elasticity (Young's Modulus). A stiff material deforms very little under load.
    Example: Steel is stiffer than Aluminium; Ceramics are generally very stiff.
  • (ii) Ductility: (Repeated from Nov 2018 Q2a & others) A material's ability to deform plastically under tensile stress without fracturing; ability to be drawn into a wire.
    Example: Copper, Mild Steel, Gold, Aluminium.
  • (iii) Tensile Strength (Ultimate Tensile Strength - UTS): The maximum stress a material can withstand while being stretched or pulled (tension) before necking (local reduction in cross-section) begins, which leads to fracture. It represents the material's maximum resistance to breaking under tension.
    Example: High-tensile steels have high UTS; Lead has low UTS.
  • (iv) Semiconductor: (Repeated from July 2017 Q4a) A material with electrical conductivity intermediate between that of a conductor and an insulator. Its conductivity can be controlled by temperature or doping with impurities.
    Example: Silicon (Si), Germanium (Ge), Gallium Arsenide (GaAs).

(b) Soft Soldering and Sheet Metal Operations:

  • (i) Soft Soldering Definition: (Repeated from July 2019 Q3b) A joining process using a filler metal (solder) with a melting point *below* 450°C to join base metals without melting them. Bonding occurs through wetting and capillary action.
  • (ii) Sheet Metal Operations (Four):
    1. Cutting/Shearing: Using snips, shears, or guillotines.
    2. Bending/Folding: Using brakes, folders, or stakes and mallets.
    3. Forming/Shaping: Using hammers, mallets, stakes, rollers (e.g., rolling cylinders).
    4. Joining: Using seams (grooved, knocked-up), riveting, soldering, welding.
    5. Punching/Piercing: Creating holes.
    6. Drawing/Stretching: Forming cup shapes or complex contours.
    7. Hemming/Wiring Edges: Finishing edges for safety/stiffness.
  • (iii) Procedure for Soft Soldering:
    1. Cleaning: Thoroughly clean the surfaces to be joined (mechanically with abrasive, chemically if needed) to remove dirt, grease, and oxides.
    2. Flux Application: Apply appropriate flux to the cleaned surfaces.
    3. Assembly & Support: Assemble the parts in the correct position and hold them steady (using clamps, vice, hands-free tool).
    4. Heating: Heat the *joint* (the base metals) with the soldering iron until it reaches the melting temperature of the solder. Heat both parts evenly.
    5. Applying Solder: Touch the solder wire to the heated joint (not directly to the iron tip). The heat of the joint should melt the solder, allowing it to flow via capillary action into the gap. Apply only enough solder to fill the joint.
    6. Cooling: Remove the iron and allow the joint to cool undisturbed until the solder solidifies completely.
    7. Cleaning (Post-solder): Clean off any corrosive flux residue using an appropriate solvent or method.

(c) Welding Equipment and Mechanical Joining:

  • (i) Arc Welding Equipment (Two): (Repeated from Nov 2021 Q3a)
    1. Welding Power Source (AC/DC).
    2. Electrode Holder (for Stick/MMAW) or Welding Gun (for MIG/TIG).
    3. Work Clamp (Earth Clamp).
    4. Welding Cables.
    5. Welding Helmet/Shield.
    6. Electrodes/Filler Wire.
  • (ii) Mechanical Joining Process (One):
    1. Bolting (Nuts and Bolts).
    2. Riveting.
    3. Screwing (using screws).
    4. Using Pins/Cotters.
    5. Seaming (Sheet metal).
    6. Clamping.

Defines key material properties, explains soft soldering, lists sheet metal operations, identifies welding equipment, and names a mechanical joining method.

Question 5 (20 marks)

Figure 1 shows a pictorial view of an object. Draw the following views in first angle orthographic projection:

(a) front elevation in the direction of arrow X;

(b) end elevation;

(c) plan.

Insert six major dimensions.

Answer

This question requires creating standard orthographic views (Front Elevation, End Elevation, Plan) in First Angle Projection from the provided pictorial view (Figure 1), including dimensions.

Understanding First Angle Projection:

  • Plan (Top View) is BELOW the Front Elevation.
  • End Elevation (assume Left End View if not specified by arrow) is drawn to the RIGHT of the Front Elevation.

Analysis of Figure 1:

  • A base plate with a raised block on top.
  • The raised block has an angled/sloped face and potentially cutouts or holes (e.g., a through slot is visible).
  • Arrow X indicates the direction for the Front Elevation.

Required Views:

  • (a) Front Elevation (View X): Shows the view looking along arrow X. Displays height and width, including the profile of the base and the raised block with its angled face and slot.
  • (b) End Elevation (From Left): Drawn to the RIGHT of the Front Elevation. Shows height and depth, including the end profile of the base and raised block, and the slot (likely as hidden detail).
  • (c) Plan (From Above): Drawn BELOW the Front Elevation. Shows width and depth, including the top surfaces, the projection of the angled face, and the slot.

Procedure:

  1. Set up drawing sheet for First Angle Projection.
  2. Draw the Front Elevation (X) accurately, transferring dimensions. Show hidden details (e.g., slot).
  3. Project lines vertically downwards for the Plan. Draw the Plan below, aligning widths and using depth dimensions. Show hidden details.
  4. Project lines horizontally to the right from the Front Elevation for the Left End Elevation. Draw the End Elevation to the right, aligning heights and using depth dimensions. Show hidden details.
  5. Add the First Angle projection symbol.
  6. Add six major dimensions across the views.

Requires accurate orthographic drafting in First Angle Projection, interpreting the pictorial view including angled features and slots, showing hidden lines, and applying standard dimensioning.

Question 6 (20 marks)

Figure 2 shows two views of a cylinder intersecting a square prism at right angle. Draw the following:

(a) the given views.

(b) curve of intersection;

(c) development of the cylinder.

Answer

This question involves drawing the intersection between a cylinder and a square prism, and developing the surface of the cylinder.

Procedure:

  1. (a) Draw Given Views: Accurately copy the provided Front Elevation and Plan (or End Elevation) views from Figure 2, showing the cylinder intersecting the square prism at right angles. Establish the projection method used (assume First or Third Angle).
  2. (b) Construct Curve of Intersection:
    • The intersection curve appears where the cylindrical surface meets the flat faces of the square prism.
    • Use element lines on the cylinder. Divide the cylinder's circular profile (in the end view or plan) into equal segments (e.g., 12).
    • Draw these element lines along the cylinder's surface in both the Front Elevation and the other given view (Plan/End).
    • In the view where the prism's faces are shown edge-on (likely the Plan or End view), find where each element line on the cylinder intersects the lines representing the prism faces.
    • Project these intersection points onto the corresponding element lines in the Front Elevation.
    • Connect the plotted points in the Front Elevation with smooth curves. Parts of the curve will be visible, parts hidden (dashed). The curve on the front face of the prism will differ from the curve on the side face.
    • Project these points to the other view (Plan/End) to complete the intersection curve there as well.
  3. (c) Construct Development of Cylinder:
    • Draw a baseline equal to the cylinder circumference ($\pi \times D_{cylinder}$). Divide into the same number of equal segments (e.g., 12) used for element lines.
    • Draw element lines perpendicular to the baseline at each division point.
    • Measure the true length of each element line on the cylinder's surface from a reference end up to the point where it intersects the prism faces. These lengths are taken from the Front Elevation (assuming it shows true lengths along the cylinder axis).
    • Plot these varying lengths on the corresponding element lines in the development layout. Note that lengths will change abruptly where the element moves from intersecting one prism face to intersecting the next.
    • Connect the endpoints with lines representing the intersection with the flat prism faces (these will likely be sections of curves or straight lines depending on perspective, but true shape on development needs care). The resulting shape is the development of the cylinder's lateral surface, shaped to fit onto the square prism.

Requires skill in orthographic projection, accurately finding and drawing the curves of intersection between a cylinder and prism faces, and developing the cylinder surface using parallel line development.

Question 7 (20 marks)

(a) (i) Draw a triangle whose perimeter is 100 mm and the ratio of the sides is 3:4:5 respectively. Circumscribe the triangle.

(ii) Construct a regular heptagon, sides 33 mm.

(b) Plot the locus of a point P on the circumference of a wheel 70 mm diameter which rolls without slipping along a straight path for one complete revolution.

Answer

This question involves geometric constructions: a triangle from perimeter/ratio and its circumcircle, a regular heptagon from side length, and a cycloid locus.

(a) Triangle and Heptagon Constructions:

  • (i) Triangle (Perimeter=100mm, Ratio 3:4:5) and Circumcircle:
    1. Calculate Sides: Total parts = 3+4+5 = 12. Length/part = 100mm/12 = 8.33mm. Sides: a=3*8.33=25mm, b=4*8.33=33.3mm, c=5*8.33=41.7mm. (Check: 25+33.3=58.3>41.7 ok). This is approximately a 3-4-5 right-angled triangle scaled down.
    2. Construct Triangle: Draw base c=41.7mm (PQ). Arc radius b=33.3mm from P. Arc radius a=25mm from Q. Intersection is R. Join PR, QR.
    3. Circumscribe Circle: (Repeated from July 2021 Q8a) Construct perpendicular bisectors of two sides (e.g., PQ, PR). Intersection is Circumcenter O. Radius R=OP (or OQ, OR). Draw circle center O, radius R.
  • (ii) Regular Heptagon (Side = 33 mm): Constructing a regular heptagon from side length using only compass/ruler is complex and usually approximate.
    1. Find Interior Angle: Angle = $(n-2) \times 180 / n = (7-2) \times 180 / 7 = 5 \times 180 / 7 \approx 128.57^{\circ}$.
    2. Approximate Construction:
      • Draw base AB = 33 mm.
      • At A and B, construct lines making approx 128.6° outwards (or 180-128.6 = 51.4° inwards). This is difficult accurately with compass/ruler.
      • **Alternative (Using center):** Finding the center is hard. An approximation: Draw AB=33. Construct perpendicular bisector. The angle subtended by the side at the center is 360/7 $\approx$ 51.4°. Constructing this accurately is the challenge.
      • **Common Method:** Draw side AB=33. Construct perpendicular at B. Mark BP=AB. Draw line AP. Construct perpendicular bisector of AB, meeting AP at Q. Use Q as center, radius QA (or QB) draw circle. Step off AB=33 around circle? (This is often inexact). Need a reliable approximation method.

    Specify the approximation method used if possible, as exact construction is not feasible with only compass and straightedge.

(b) Locus Construction (Cycloid, Dia=70mm):

(Repeated from Nov 2018 Q8c)

Construct the path of point P on the circumference of a circle (diameter 70 mm, radius R=35 mm) rolling along a straight line for one revolution.

  1. Setup: Draw baseline. Draw circle (R=35 mm) tangent to baseline (P at bottom).
  2. Rolling Distance: Mark distance = Circumference = $\pi D = \pi \times 70 \approx 220 \, \text{mm}$ along baseline.
  3. Divide: Divide circle and baseline distance into 12 equal parts. Mark center positions C0, C1,... C12.
  4. Plot Points: For each center position Ci, locate the corresponding point Pi by considering a rotation of $i \times 30^{\circ}$ around Ci relative to P0.
  5. Draw Locus: Draw a smooth curve (cycloid) connecting P0, P1,... P12.

Combines geometric constructions for triangles (from perimeter/ratio), circumcircles, approximate polygons (heptagon), and the generation of a cycloid locus.

Question 8 (20 marks)

Figure 3 shows orthographic views of a machined block drawn in third angle projection. Draw, full size, oblique view of the block.

Answer

This question requires constructing an oblique pictorial view from the given third angle orthographic projections (Figure 3). The type of oblique (Cavalier or Cabinet) and the viewing angle for receding lines are not specified.

Understanding Third Angle Projection: Plan is ABOVE Front Elevation, Right End View is to the RIGHT.

Analysis of Views (Figure 3):

  • Identify the Front Elevation and Plan view.
  • Determine the object's shape: Appears to be a block with a stepped profile, possibly a central raised section or groove, and angled/sloped surfaces.
  • Extract overall Width, Height, and Depth dimensions.

Oblique Drawing Procedure (Assuming Cavalier - Full Depth, 45° Angle):

  1. Orientation: Choose the Front Elevation as the front face (drawn true shape).
  2. Draw Front Face: Draw the Front Elevation profile accurately using Width and Height dimensions.
  3. Draw Receding Lines: From each corner/key point on the front face, draw parallel receding lines going back at 45° to the horizontal.
  4. Mark Full Depth: Measure the object's true Depth (from the Plan view) along these receding lines. Mark these points.
  5. Draw Back Features: Connect the ends of the receding lines to form the back edges/surfaces, drawing lines parallel to the corresponding front face lines.
  6. Add Details: Construct any steps, slopes, or other features shown in the views, projecting them onto the front face and then receding back at full depth. Circles on the front face are true circles; circles on receding faces become ellipses (often simplified).
  7. Refine: Darken visible lines. Omit hidden lines.

If Cabinet projection is used, receding lines are drawn at HALF the true depth. If another angle (e.g., 30° or 60°) is preferred, use that consistently for receding lines.

Requires interpreting the third angle orthographic views and applying the rules of oblique projection (specifying type used, e.g., Cavalier 45°) to create a clear pictorial representation of the machined block.
