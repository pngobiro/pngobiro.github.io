Question 1

(a) Distinguish between a centre punch and a dot punch.

(b) With the aid of sketches, describe each of the following marking out tools:

(i) try square;

(ii) odd leg caliper.

(c) (i) Explain how the accuracy of 0.02 mm is obtained from the vernier caliper.

(ii) State four precautions in using the vernier caliper.

Answer

This question covers marking out tools (punches, square, caliper) and the principle/use of vernier calipers.

(a) Centre Punch vs. Dot Punch Distinction:

  • Centre Punch: Has a conical point ground to a steeper angle, typically 90 degrees. Its primary use is to create a larger, wider indentation to accurately locate the centre of a hole before drilling, providing a guide for the drill bit to start without wandering.
  • Dot Punch: Has a conical point ground to a sharper angle, typically 60 degrees. It is used to make smaller, sharper indentations, often to permanently mark scribed layout lines, making them more visible, or to mark points along a line without creating an indentation too large for subsequent accurate work or drilling guidance.

Key Difference: Point angle (90° for centre punch vs. 60° for dot punch) and primary application (drill starting vs. marking lines).

(b) Marking Out Tools Descriptions with Sketches:

  • (i) Try Square (Engineer's Square):
    • Description: An L-shaped precision tool consisting of a thicker 'stock' and a thinner 'blade', fixed accurately at 90 degrees to each other.
    • Sketch: Show the L-shape with the distinct stock and blade, clearly indicating the 90-degree angle.
    • Use: Checking surfaces for flatness and squareness (90°); marking lines perpendicular to a reference edge.
  • (ii) Odd Leg Caliper (Jenny Caliper / Hermaphrodite Caliper):
    • Description: A caliper with two legs joined by a pivot (similar to dividers). One leg has a sharp point (like a divider leg), while the other leg is bent inwards at the tip, often with a small step or heel on the inside of the bend.
    • Sketch: Show the two legs, pivot, one sharp point, and one bent leg with an inward-facing edge/step.
    • Use: Scribing lines parallel to an edge (the bent leg runs along the edge while the sharp point scribes the line); finding the centre of round or square bars (by scribing arcs from the edges).

(c) Vernier Caliper Accuracy and Precautions:

Corrected label from (b) to (c) based on context.

  • (i) Obtaining 0.02 mm Accuracy: This accuracy (least count) is achieved through the principle of the vernier scale. A standard metric vernier scale for 0.02 mm accuracy typically has 50 divisions on the sliding vernier scale that correspond exactly in length to 49 divisions on the main scale (where main scale divisions are 1 mm apart).
    The difference in length between one main scale division (1 mm) and one vernier scale division (49/50 mm = 0.98 mm) is $1 - 0.98 = 0.02 \, \text{mm}$.
    Therefore, when the vernier scale slides, each of its divisions represents a movement of 0.02 mm relative to the main scale markings, allowing measurements to be read to this precision by identifying which vernier division aligns perfectly with a main scale division.
    (Alternatively, sometimes 25 vernier divisions span 24 mm, giving a least count of 1mm - (24/25)mm = 1/25mm = 0.04mm? No, common is 50 div over 49mm for 0.02mm, or 25 div over 24.5mm? Check common designs. Another common 0.02mm design: Main scale has 0.5mm subdivisions. Vernier has 25 divisions spanning 24.5mm (49 half-mm marks). Vernier div = 24.5/25 = 0.49mm. Smallest main div = 0.5mm. Least Count = 0.5 - 0.49 = 0.01mm? No, something is wrong. Let's stick to the 50 div over 49 mm explanation)
    Revisiting: A common 0.02mm vernier has 50 divisions on the vernier scale spanning 49 mm on the main scale. Least Count = (Value of 1 Main Scale Division) - (Value of 1 Vernier Scale Division) = 1 mm - (49/50) mm = 1/50 mm = 0.02 mm. The reading is taken by adding the main scale reading (mm before vernier zero) to the value of the aligning vernier mark (aligning division number x 0.02 mm).
  • (ii) Precautions in Using Vernier Caliper (Four):
    1. Cleanliness: Ensure the workpiece and the caliper jaws/measuring surfaces are clean before taking a measurement to avoid errors.
    2. Check Zero Setting: Close the jaws fully and check if the zero mark on the vernier scale aligns perfectly with the zero mark on the main scale. If not, note the zero error or adjust if possible.
    3. Correct Application of Jaws: Apply the jaws squarely to the surfaces being measured. Use the appropriate jaws (external, internal) for the dimension. Apply gentle, consistent pressure; avoid forcing the jaws.
    4. Avoid Parallax Error: View the scales directly from above (perpendicularly) when reading the main and vernier scales to avoid misinterpreting the alignment.
    5. Handle with Care: Avoid dropping or knocking the instrument. Store it carefully in its protective case when not in use. Do not use it as a hammer or scribe.
    6. Locking Screw: Use the locking screw gently to hold the reading while removing the caliper from the workpiece, but re-check the reading after locking as it can sometimes shift slightly.

Distinguishes between centre and dot punches, describes common marking tools, explains the principle behind vernier accuracy, and lists precautions for using vernier calipers.

Question 2

(a) List four metals used in sheet metal work.

(b) Explain each of the following sheet metal tools and state their respective uses.

(i) mallets;

(ii) stakes;

(iii) pair of snips;

(iv) trammel points.

(c) Illustrate the following sheet metal seams

(i) double grooved;

(ii) paned down;

(iii) knocked-up.

Answer

This question covers materials, tools, and joining techniques used in sheet metal work.

(a) Metals Used in Sheet Metal Work (Four):

(Repeated from July 2016 Q4a & July 2020 Q4a)

  • Mild Steel (including Galvanized Iron/Steel - GI, Tinplate)
  • Aluminium
  • Copper
  • Stainless Steel
  • Brass
  • Zinc

(b) Sheet Metal Tools Explained:

  • (i) Mallets:
    • Explanation: Hammers with heads made of softer materials like wood, plastic, rawhide, or rubber.
    • Uses: Used for shaping and forming sheet metal without marring or stretching the surface excessively, as a steel hammer would. Used for bending flanges, closing seams, and general forming operations, often in conjunction with stakes.
  • (ii) Stakes:
    • Explanation: Specially shaped anvils, typically made of cast iron or steel, designed to fit into a bench socket or vice. They come in various shapes (hatchet, beakhorn, creasing, blowhorn, etc.).
    • Uses: Provide a shaped surface (anvil) over which sheet metal can be formed, bent, seamed, or planished using a mallet or hammer. Each stake shape facilitates specific forming operations (e.g., forming curves, sharp bends, wiring edges, closing seams).
  • (iii) Pair of Snips (Tin Snips):
    • Explanation: Hand shears designed specifically for cutting sheet metal. They operate like heavy-duty scissors. Different types exist for straight cuts, curves (left/right cutting - aviation snips), and heavier gauge metal (bench shears).
    • Uses: Cutting sheet metal along marked lines, trimming edges, cutting out patterns.
  • (iv) Trammel Points (Beam Compass):
    • Explanation: A marking out tool consisting of two adjustable points (trammel heads) mounted on a long beam (wood or metal). One point is typically a sharp scriber, the other can be a scriber or pencil holder. The distance between the points can be set accurately along the beam.
    • Uses: Scribing large circles or arcs beyond the capacity of standard dividers; transferring large measurements accurately during layout on large sheets.

(c) Illustrations of Sheet Metal Seams:

Requires sketches showing the joint formation/cross-section.

  • (i) Double Grooved Seam: A variation of the grooved seam providing extra strength or a flush internal/external finish depending on construction. It involves folding both edges twice, hooking them, and then grooving. It's essentially two interlocked U-shaped folds.
  • (ii) Paned Down Joint: (Repeated from July 2016 Q3d & Nov 2016 Q4c) Used for bottoms. Body edge flanged inwards, base edge folded up over the flange, hammered flat ("paned") against the body.
  • (iii) Knocked-Up Joint: (Repeated from July 2016 Q3d) Also for bottoms, often stronger. Body edge flanged outwards, base edge flanged upwards. Base fits inside body flange. Both flanges hammered vertically ("knocked up") against body wall.

Identifies common sheet metals, explains the function of key sheet metal hand tools, and illustrates common methods for forming seams or joints.

Question 3

(a) (i) State two tools and equipment necessary when cleaning a weld bead.

(ii) Illustrate the manual metal arc welding set-up.

(b) State two causes of each of the following weld defects

(i) overlap;

(ii) undercut;

(iii) poor weld bead appearance.

(c) List four arc welding positions.

Answer

This question focuses on arc welding, specifically cleaning, setup illustration, common defects and their causes, and welding positions.

(a) Weld Cleaning and MMAW Setup:

  • (i) Tools/Equipment for Cleaning Weld Bead (Two):
    1. Wire Brush (Hand or Power): Used to remove loose slag, spatter, and surface discoloration after welding (especially important between passes in multi-pass welds).
    2. Chipping Hammer: A hammer with a pointed end (pick) and a chisel-like end, used specifically to chip away the hardened slag covering from Shielded Metal Arc Welding (SMAW/Stick) or Flux-Cored Arc Welding (FCAW) beads.
    3. Grinder (Angle Grinder): Used for heavier cleaning, removing spatter, smoothing rough beads, or preparing edges (though care must be taken not to remove weld reinforcement).
    4. Needle Scaler (less common): Pneumatic tool with multiple needles for removing slag/rust.
  • (ii) Manual Metal Arc Welding (MMAW/Stick) Setup Illustration:

    Sketch should show:

    • Power Source: AC or DC welding machine/transformer/rectifier.
    • Electrode Holder (Stinger): Connected via cable to one terminal of the power source, holds the flux-coated electrode.
    • Electrode (Stick): Flux-coated consumable electrode held in the holder.
    • Workpiece: The metal being welded.
    • Work Clamp (Earth Clamp): Connected via cable to the other terminal of the power source, clamped securely to the workpiece or welding table.
    • Welding Cables: Heavy-duty insulated cables connecting holder and clamp to the power source.
    • Arc: Show the electric arc between the electrode tip and the workpiece.
    • Shielding Gas/Slag: Indicate the gas shield and molten slag produced by the burning flux coating, protecting the molten weld pool.

(b) Weld Defects and Causes (Two Causes Each):

  • (i) Overlap: Weld metal flows onto the base metal surface without fusing to it.
    • Cause 1: Incorrect electrode angle (too shallow, pointing away from the joint root).
    • Cause 2: Too slow travel speed, allowing excessive weld metal buildup.
    • Cause 3: Insufficient current (not enough heat to fuse base metal edge).
  • (ii) Undercut: (Repeated from July 2019 Q2c) A groove melted into the base metal adjacent to the weld toe or root, left unfilled by weld metal.
    • Cause 1: Excessive welding current or voltage.
    • Cause 2: Incorrect electrode angle (too much heat directed at edges).
    • Cause 3: Too fast travel speed (doesn't allow filler metal to fill the melted groove).
    • Cause 4: Too long an arc length.
  • (iii) Poor Weld Bead Appearance: Irregular shape, uneven ripples, inconsistent width.
    • Cause 1: Inconsistent travel speed or arc length by the welder.
    • Cause 2: Incorrect welding parameters (current, voltage, polarity).
    • Cause 3: Incorrect electrode manipulation/weave technique.
    • Cause 4: Unstable arc conditions.

(c) Arc Welding Positions (Four):

(Repeated from July 2015 Q3c)

  1. Flat (1G/1F): Welding on the top side of a horizontal surface.
  2. Horizontal (2G/2F): Welding on a horizontal seam, either on a vertical plate or the underside of a horizontal plate.
  3. Vertical (3G/3F): Welding on a vertical seam (can be uphill or downhill).
  4. Overhead (4G/4F): Welding from the underside of a horizontal surface.

Focuses on the practical aspects of welding: cleaning tools, MMAW setup, common visual defects and their origins, and standard welding positions.

Question 4

(a) List five first aid materials.

(b) Outline the procedure for mouth-to-mouth artificial respiration.

(c) (i) Outline four safety precautions against fire in the workshop.

(ii) Explain four safety measures against electric shock.

Answer

This question focuses entirely on safety: first aid materials, resuscitation, fire prevention, and electrical shock prevention.

(a) First Aid Materials (Five):

(Repeated from July 2017 Q1a(ii))

  • Sterile Dressings / Gauze Pads
  • Bandages (Roller, Triangular)
  • Adhesive Tape / Plasters
  • Antiseptic Wipes / Solution
  • Disposable Gloves
  • Scissors / Tweezers
  • Eye Wash / Eye Pads
  • Safety Pins
  • Burn Dressings (specific types)
  • First Aid Guidance Leaflet

(b) Mouth-to-Mouth Respiration Procedure:

(Repeated from Q1b July 2020, Nov 2018 Q1d, etc.)

Modern guidelines prioritize chest compressions (CPR) and calling emergency services. This describes the traditional method.

  1. Safety First: Ensure scene safety.
  2. Check Response & Breathing: Check consciousness and normal breathing. Call for help.
  3. Position Victim: Flat on back, firm surface.
  4. Open Airway: Head-tilt/chin-lift.
  5. Give Breaths: Pinch nose, seal mouth, give 2 effective breaths (1 sec each, watch chest rise).
  6. Continue (If pulse, no breathing): 1 breath every 5-6 seconds. (Start CPR if no pulse).

(c) Fire and Electrical Safety:

  • (i) Safety Precautions Against Fire (Four):
    1. Good Housekeeping: Keep work areas clean, remove combustible waste (oily rags, wood shavings, paper) regularly. Store materials properly.
    2. Safe Storage of Flammables: Store flammable liquids and gases in approved containers and designated, well-ventilated areas away from ignition sources.
    3. Control Ignition Sources: Prevent sparks/flames near flammable materials (e.g., during welding/grinding). Ensure electrical equipment is properly maintained to prevent overheating/sparks. No smoking in designated areas.
    4. Maintain Firefighting Equipment: Ensure fire extinguishers, alarms, and sprinkler systems (if present) are accessible, regularly inspected, maintained, and appropriate for the hazards present. Ensure staff know how to use them.
    5. Clear Evacuation Routes: Keep fire exits and escape routes clear and unobstructed at all times. Conduct regular fire drills.
  • (ii) Safety Measures Against Electric Shock (Four Explained):
    1. Earthing (Grounding): Connecting the metal casings of equipment to earth. If a fault makes the casing live, current flows to earth, tripping a protective device (fuse/breaker) and disconnecting power. (See July 2014 Q1b).
    2. Insulation: Using materials with high electrical resistance (insulators like rubber, plastic) to cover live conductors (wires) or enclose equipment (casings), preventing accidental contact.
    3. Double Insulation: Providing two layers of insulation (basic + supplementary) on appliances, eliminating the need for earthing by ensuring the outer casing cannot become live even if basic insulation fails. (See July 2014 Q1b).
    4. Fuses and Circuit Breakers (Protective Devices): Devices designed to automatically interrupt the current flow if it exceeds a safe level (due to overload or short circuit), preventing overheating, fire, and reducing the duration of potential shock currents.
    5. Residual Current Devices (RCDs/RCCBs): Detect small leakage currents flowing to earth (e.g., through a person) and trip very quickly, disconnecting the supply before a fatal shock can occur. Offer much higher protection against shock than fuses/breakers alone.
    6. Reduced/Low Voltage Systems: Using lower operating voltages (e.g., 110V centre-tapped, 24V) in hazardous environments significantly reduces the potential severity of an electric shock. (See July 2014 Q1b).
    7. Isolation: Using isolating switches or procedures (like Lockout/Tagout - LOTO) to completely disconnect equipment from the power supply before maintenance or repair work begins.

Emphasizes crucial safety knowledge including first aid kit contents, resuscitation techniques, fire prevention strategies, and methods used to protect against electric shock hazards.

Question 5

Figure 1, shows an isometric view of a V-block.

Draw the following using third angle orthographic projection:

(i) front elevation in the direction of arrow X;

(ii) side elevation;

(iii) a plan.

Include six major dimensions and a projection symbol.

Answer

This question requires creating standard orthographic views (Front Elevation, Side Elevation, Plan) in Third Angle Projection from the provided isometric view of a V-block (Figure 1), including dimensions and the projection symbol.

Understanding Third Angle Projection:

  • Object imagined in the third quadrant.
  • Plan (Top View) is drawn ABOVE the Front Elevation.
  • Side Elevation from the Right is drawn to the RIGHT of the Front Elevation.
  • Side Elevation from the Left is drawn to the LEFT of the Front Elevation.

Analysis of Figure 1 (V-Block):

  • A rectangular block with a precise V-shaped groove running along its length on the top surface.
  • May have additional features like grooves on the sides or ends, or clamping slots (not clearly visible in thumbnail, assume standard V-block features).
  • Arrow X indicates the direction for the Front Elevation.
  • Dimensions need to be extracted from the isometric view.

Required Views:

  • (i) Front Elevation (View X): Shows the view looking along arrow X. Will display the height and width profile, including the end view of the V-groove.
  • (ii) Side Elevation: Assuming Right Side Elevation if not specified. Drawn to the RIGHT of the Front Elevation. Shows the length and height profile, including the side view of the block (likely rectangular).
  • (iii) Plan (Top View): Drawn ABOVE the Front Elevation. Shows the length and width profile, including the top surface with the V-groove visible along its length.

Procedure:

  1. Set up drawing sheet for Third Angle Projection.
  2. Draw the Front Elevation (X) accurately, showing the V-groove profile.
  3. Project lines vertically upwards from the Front Elevation for the Plan. Draw the Plan view above, using length dimensions from the isometric and aligning widths. Show the V-groove running along the top.
  4. Project lines horizontally to the right from the Front Elevation for the Right Side Elevation. Draw the Side Elevation to the right, using length dimensions (consistent with Plan view) and aligning heights. Show the rectangular side profile.
  5. Add the Third Angle projection symbol.
  6. Add six major dimensions (overall length, width, height; V-groove angle/depth/width; position if relevant) across the views.

Requires accurate orthographic drafting in Third Angle Projection, interpretation of the isometric view, and correct dimensioning and use of the projection symbol.

Question 6

Figure 2, shows two views of a solid drawn in first angle projection.

Make an isometric projection of the object with corner P as the lowest point. Include six major dimensions.

Answer

This question requires constructing an isometric view from given first angle orthographic projections (Figure 2), ensuring corner P is the lowest point, and including dimensions.

Understanding First Angle Projection:

  • Plan (Top View) is BELOW the Front Elevation.
  • Front Elevation shows height and width.
  • Plan shows width and depth.

Analysis of Figure 2:

  • Identify the Front Elevation and Plan views based on the First Angle layout.
  • Determine the object's shape: Appears to be a stepped block or base with an upright section, possibly containing holes or slots.
  • Locate corner P on the views (likely a front-bottom corner).
  • Extract Width, Height, and Depth dimensions from the views.

Isometric Construction Steps:

  1. Orient Axes: Set up isometric axes originating from corner P as the lowest point. Determine Width, Depth, Height directions based on the orthographic views relative to P.
  2. Bounding Box (Optional): Draw a light isometric box using overall dimensions.
  3. Construct Base/Features: Start drawing from P, laying out the features along the isometric axes using dimensions from the Plan (for width/depth) and Front Elevation (for height). Build up the object step-by-step.
  4. Circles/Curves: Draw circles as ellipses on the appropriate isometric planes.
  5. Refine Lines: Complete the object outline, darken visible lines, erase construction lines. Omit hidden lines.
  6. Dimensioning: Add six major dimensions directly onto the isometric view using standard isometric dimensioning techniques (dimension lines/text aligned with isometric planes).

Requires accurate interpretation of First Angle orthographic views and careful construction of the isometric projection with the specified orientation (P lowest) and dimensions.

Question 7

Figure 3, shows an elevation and plan of a right square pyramid cut obliquely at $30^{\circ}$.

Copy the elevation and draw the following:

(i) end elevation in the direction of arrow A;

(ii) complete truncated plan;

(iii) true shape of the cut surface;

(iv) development of the frustum.

Answer

This question involves completing orthographic views, finding the true shape of an oblique section, and developing the surface of a truncated square pyramid.

Procedure:

  1. Copy Elevation & Plan: Accurately redraw the given Front Elevation and Plan from Figure 3. Note the base size, height, apex position, and the 30° cutting plane in the Elevation. Label base vertices (e.g., 1, 2, 3, 4) in the Plan and apex O. Project base vertices to Elevation.
  2. (ii) Complete Truncated Plan:
    • Project the points where the cutting plane intersects the slant edges (O-1, O-2, O-3, O-4) in the Elevation vertically down onto the corresponding slant edges in the Plan view. Label these intersection points (e.g., 1', 2', 3', 4').
    • Connect points 1', 2', 3', 4' in the Plan with straight lines. This polygon represents the cut surface as seen from above.
    • Determine visibility: The base square (1-2-3-4) and the cut polygon (1'-2'-3'-4') are visible. The parts of the slant edges between the base and the cut are also visible. Use solid lines for visible edges and dashed for hidden (if any, unlikely in plan).
  3. (i) Construct End Elevation (View A): (Assume Left End View drawn Right in First Angle, or Right End View drawn Right in Third Angle).
    • Project heights horizontally from the Front Elevation (base, cut points 1'-4', apex O).
    • Project depths from the Plan view (using 45° line or measurement).
    • Construct the end view of the base, the apex, and the slant edges.
    • Project the cut points 1'-4' from both Elevation (heights) and Plan (depths) to locate their positions in the End Elevation.
    • Connect these points in the End Elevation to show the cut shape. Determine visibility (solid/dashed lines).
  4. (iii) Construct True Shape of Cut Surface:
    • Draw projection lines perpendicular to the cutting plane line (at 30°) in the Elevation, extending from points 1', 2', 3', 4'.
    • Establish a datum line parallel to the cutting plane line.
    • Measure the perpendicular distances (widths) of points 1', 2', 3', 4' from a reference center line in the Plan view.
    • Transfer these widths onto the corresponding projection lines in the true shape view, measuring perpendicularly from the datum line.
    • Connect the plotted points (1''-4'') with straight lines. This polygon is the true shape.
  5. (iv) Construct Development of Frustum:
    • Find the true length (L) of a slant edge (e.g., O-1). If not shown directly in elevation, rotate O1 in the plan until parallel to the viewing plane, project up to elevation base, join to O to get true length.
    • Using true length L as radius, draw an arc from a development apex O'.
    • Step off the base side lengths (true lengths, likely from Plan) along this arc (four times). Label points 1, 2, 3, 4, 1 on the arc.
    • Draw radial lines from O' to these base points.
    • Find the true lengths from the apex O to the cut points 1', 2', 3', 4' (e.g., O-1', O-2', etc.). These might require projection similar to finding true slant edge length.
    • Measure these true lengths O-1', O-2', etc., along the corresponding radial lines O'-1, O'-2, etc., in the development, starting from O'. Label points 1', 2', 3', 4'.
    • Connect points 1', 2', 3', 4' with straight lines. Connect 1-1', 2-2', 3-3', 4-4'.
    • The shape bounded by 1-2-3-4-1 (base curve/lines) and 1'-2'-3'-4'-1' (top cut curve/lines) and the side lines 1-1', etc., is the development of the frustum's lateral surface. Optionally add the base square and true shape cut polygon attached.

Requires proficiency in orthographic projection, auxiliary views for true shape determination, and radial line development techniques for pyramidal surfaces.

Question 8

(a) Construct the common external tangent between two circles with centres 120 mm apart and radii 40 mm and 20 mm respectively.

(b) Figure 4, shows a point P which moves so that the sum of the distances from P to two fixed points M and N, 95 mm apart is constant and equal to 120 mm.

(i) Plot the path of the point P around the two points M and N;

(ii) Name the curve and the two fixed points.

Answer

This question involves geometric constructions: a common external tangent to two circles and plotting an ellipse using its definition.

(a) Common External Tangent Construction:

Given: Circle 1: R1 = 40 mm, Center O1. Circle 2: R2 = 20 mm, Center O2. Distance O1O2 = 120 mm.

  1. Draw line segment O1O2 = 120 mm. Draw Circle 1 (R1=40) at O1 and Circle 2 (R2=20) at O2.
  2. Construct an auxiliary circle centered at O1 with radius R_aux = R1 - R2 = 40 - 20 = 20 mm.
  3. Find the midpoint M of O1O2. Draw a semicircle with diameter O1O2 (centered at M).
  4. Find the intersection point(s), P, where the auxiliary circle (R=20) intersects the semicircle.
  5. Draw the line O1P and extend it to intersect the original Circle 1 at point T1. This is a tangent point.
  6. Draw a line through O2 parallel to O1P. This line intersects Circle 2 at point T2, on the *same* side of O1O2 as T1. This is the second tangent point.
  7. The line connecting T1 and T2 is the required common external tangent. Construct the second tangent similarly if needed using the other intersection point of the auxiliary circle and semicircle.

(b) Ellipse Construction (Definition Method):

Given: Fixed points M and N, distance MN = 95 mm. Sum of distances from P to M and N is constant = 120 mm (PM + PN = 120 mm).

  • (ii) Name Curve and Points:
    • The curve defined by the set of all points P such that the sum of the distances from P to two fixed points (M and N) is constant, is an Ellipse.
    • The two fixed points M and N are called the Foci (singular: Focus) of the ellipse.
  • (i) Plotting the Path (Ellipse): This can be done using several methods based on the definition:
    • String Method (Conceptual): Fix pins at M and N. Take a loop of string of length (MN + 120) = (95 + 120) = 215 mm. Place the loop around the pins and pull it taut with a pencil point P. Move the pencil while keeping the string taut; it will trace the ellipse.
    • Intersecting Arcs Method (Construction):
      1. Draw line segment MN = 95 mm.
      2. Choose a radius $r_1$ (e.g., 30 mm). Calculate $r_2 = 120 - r_1 = 120 - 30 = 90$ mm.
      3. Set compass radius to $r_1$. Place point at M, draw arcs above and below MN.
      4. Set compass radius to $r_2$. Place point at N, draw arcs intersecting the arcs from step 3. These intersection points lie on the ellipse.
      5. Repeat with different pairs of radii $r_1$ and $r_2$ such that $r_1 + r_2 = 120$ mm and $r_1 > (120-95)/2 = 12.5$ mm and $r_1 < 120 - 12.5 = 107.5$ mm (approximately). Choose enough points to define the curve. (e.g., r1=40, r2=80; r1=50, r2=70; r1=60, r2=60 [gives points on major axis]; r1=70, r2=50, etc.).
      6. Also plot the ends of the major axis: the distance from center to end is half the constant sum = 120/2 = 60 mm. The center O is the midpoint of MN. Ends are 60mm from O along the line MN extended.
      7. Draw a smooth curve connecting all the plotted points to form the ellipse.

Requires accurate geometric constructions for common tangents between circles and for plotting an ellipse using the locus definition (sum of distances to foci is constant).
