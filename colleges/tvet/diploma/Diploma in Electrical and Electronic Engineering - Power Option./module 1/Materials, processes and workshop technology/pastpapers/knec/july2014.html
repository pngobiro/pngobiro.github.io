Question 1

(a) List any three:

(i) types of electrical hazards;

(ii) effects of exposure to hazardous substances.

(b) Explain how the following are used to minimize electrical hazards:

(i) double insulation;

(ii) earthing;

(iii) safe voltages.

(c) With regard to the Factory's Act, state any two safety precautions for each of the following:

(i) machinery;

(ii) first aid;

(iii) fire;

(iv) movement at the place of work.

Answer

This question focuses on electrical safety, chemical safety, and general workshop safety regulations as per the Factory's Act.

(a) Hazards and Effects:

  • (i) Types of Electrical Hazards (Any Three):
    • Electric Shock (contact with live parts).
    • Electrical Burns (from current passing through the body or arc flash).
    • Fires (caused by overheating wires, short circuits, faulty equipment).
    • Explosions (ignition of flammable atmospheres by sparks).
    • Arc Flash/Blast (sudden release of energy due to an arc fault).
    • Static Electricity hazards.
  • (ii) Effects of Exposure to Hazardous Substances (Any Three):
    • Skin irritation, burns, or dermatitis (e.g., from solvents, acids).
    • Respiratory problems, asthma, or lung damage (e.g., from dust, fumes, vapors).
    • Poisoning (systemic effects) if ingested, inhaled, or absorbed (e.g., lead, mercury, certain solvents).
    • Eye irritation or damage (e.g., from splashes, fumes).
    • Allergic reactions or sensitization.
    • Long-term effects like cancer or organ damage (carcinogens, toxins).
    • Asphyxiation (displacement of oxygen by gases like CO2, nitrogen).

(b) Minimizing Electrical Hazards:

  • (i) Double Insulation: This safety measure, often found on portable appliances (indicated by a square within a square symbol), uses two layers of insulation – basic insulation and supplementary insulation. This eliminates the need for an earth connection, as even if the basic insulation fails, the supplementary layer prevents the outer casing from becoming live.
  • (ii) Earthing (Grounding): Connecting the metal casings of electrical equipment to the earth via a low-resistance conductor (earth wire). If a fault causes a live wire to touch the casing, a large current flows to earth, tripping a protective device (fuse or circuit breaker) and disconnecting the power supply, thus preventing electric shock from touching the faulty appliance.
  • (iii) Safe Voltages (Reduced Low Voltage Systems): Using voltages significantly lower than standard mains voltage (e.g., 110V centre-tapped to earth, providing 55V to earth, or even lower voltages like 12V or 24V) in hazardous environments (like construction sites, damp locations) or for certain portable tools. This reduces the potential severity of an electric shock if contact occurs.

(c) Factory's Act Safety Precautions (Two for each):

Note: The specific wording might vary based on the exact version/jurisdiction of the Act (e.g., Factories Act Cap 514 in Kenya, replaced/updated by OSHA 2007). General principles include:

  • (i) Machinery:
    1. All dangerous parts of machinery must be securely fenced/guarded unless they are safe by position or construction.
    2. Machinery must only be operated by trained and authorized personnel. Stop controls must be easily accessible.
    3. Regular maintenance and inspection of machinery must be carried out.
  • (ii) First Aid:
    1. A readily accessible first-aid box or cupboard containing prescribed items must be provided and maintained for every 150 workers (or part thereof).
    2. A responsible person, trained in first aid, must be in charge of the first-aid box, especially if more than 50 people are employed.
    3. Clear notices indicating the location of the first-aid box and the person in charge should be posted.
  • (iii) Fire:
    1. Adequate and suitable means for extinguishing fire (e.g., extinguishers, fire hoses, buckets) must be provided and maintained, readily available for use.
    2. Effective means of escape (e.g., clear passageways, unlocked doors opening outwards, fire exits) must be provided and maintained, clearly marked.
    3. Workers must be familiarized with the means of escape and fire-fighting procedures (fire drills).
  • (iv) Movement at the Place of Work (Safe Access/Egress):
    1. Floors, steps, passages, and gangways must be of sound construction, properly maintained, and kept free from obstruction and slippery substances.
    2. Adequate lighting must be provided in all areas where people work or pass.
    3. Openings in floors must be securely fenced or covered. Handrails should be provided for stairs.

Understanding and implementing safety measures related to electrical hazards, hazardous substances, and general workplace regulations like the Factory's Act is crucial for preventing accidents and ensuring worker well-being.

Question 2

(a) Define the following terms with reference to engineering materials:

(i) hardness;

(ii) corrosion resistance;

(iii) toughness.

(b) State:

(i) the composition of a ferrous metal;

(ii) any two examples of non-ferrous metals.

(c) Describe the following processes used in finishes and decorations:

(i) engineering;

(ii) etching.

(d) Outline the process of 'electroplating'.

Answer

This question covers definitions of material properties, classification of metals, and descriptions of finishing processes.

(a) Material Properties Definitions:

  • (i) Hardness: A material's resistance to scratching, abrasion, indentation, or penetration by another harder material. It relates to the ability to resist surface wear.
  • (ii) Corrosion Resistance: A material's ability to withstand degradation or deterioration due to chemical or electrochemical reactions with its environment (e.g., oxidation/rusting from air and moisture, attack by acids).
  • (iii) Toughness: A material's ability to absorb energy and deform plastically without fracturing. It represents resistance to fracture when subjected to impact or shock loading. A tough material can withstand sudden loads.

(b) Metal Classification:

  • (i) Composition of a Ferrous Metal: A ferrous metal is an alloy whose primary component is iron (Fe). Common examples include steel (iron + carbon) and cast iron (iron + carbon + silicon). They often contain other alloying elements to modify properties. Most ferrous metals are magnetic.
  • (ii) Examples of Non-Ferrous Metals (Any Two): Metals that do not contain iron as their primary component.
    • Copper (Cu)
    • Aluminum (Al)
    • Zinc (Zn)
    • Lead (Pb)
    • Tin (Sn)
    • Nickel (Ni)
    • Magnesium (Mg)
    • Titanium (Ti)
    • Precious metals (Gold-Au, Silver-Ag, Platinum-Pt)

(c) Finishing and Decorative Processes:

  • (i) Engineering (Assuming Engraving or Engine Turning):
    Engraving: The process of cutting grooves or patterns into a hard surface, usually metal, using a sharp tool (graver) or specialized machinery (laser, CNC). It's used for decoration, marking identification, or creating printing plates.
    Engine Turning (Guilloché): A precision mechanical engraving technique that creates intricate, repetitive patterns (like intersecting circles or waves) on metal surfaces, often used on watch dials, jewelry, or high-quality metalwork for decorative effect. It uses a specialized lathe called a rose engine or straight line engine.

    The term "engineering" alone as a finishing process is ambiguous. Engraving or Engine Turning are plausible interpretations in this context.

  • (ii) Etching: A process using strong acid or mordant (etching agent) to cut into the unprotected parts of a metal surface to create a design or pattern in intaglio (incised). The process involves:
    1. Applying a protective, acid-resistant coating (resist) to the metal surface.
    2. Removing the resist from the areas intended to be etched, exposing the metal.
    3. Immersing the metal in the etching solution (acid bath), which selectively dissolves the exposed metal.
    4. Removing the metal from the bath and cleaning off the remaining resist. Used for decoration, creating printing plates (printmaking), or manufacturing printed circuit boards (PCBs).

(d) Outline of Electroplating Process:

(Repeated from Q3b(i) of 2010 paper)

Electroplating uses electric current to deposit a thin layer of one metal onto another object (the substrate) primarily for protection, appearance, or improved surface properties.

  1. Preparation: The object to be plated (substrate) is thoroughly cleaned to remove dirt, grease, and oxides. This is crucial for good adhesion.
  2. Setup: An electrolytic cell is set up. The cleaned object acts as the cathode (negative electrode). The anode (positive electrode) is often made of the metal being plated (e.g., copper anode for copper plating). Both electrodes are immersed in an electrolyte solution containing ions of the plating metal (e.g., copper sulfate solution for copper plating).
  3. Electrolysis: A DC power source is connected. Current flows, causing positive metal ions from the electrolyte to migrate to the cathode (the object).
  4. Deposition: At the cathode, the metal ions gain electrons (reduction) and are deposited as a thin, solid metal layer onto the object's surface. (e.g., Cu$^{2+}$ + 2e$^-$ $\rightarrow$ Cu(s)).
  5. Anode Reaction: If the anode is made of the plating metal, it dissolves (oxidizes), replenishing the metal ions in the electrolyte. (e.g., Cu(s) $\rightarrow$ Cu$^{2+}$ + 2e$^-$). If an inert anode is used, the electrolyte needs periodic replenishment.
  6. Completion: The process continues until the desired coating thickness is achieved. The object is then removed, rinsed, and dried.

The answer defines key material properties, classifies metals, and describes common surface finishing techniques including engraving/etching and the electrolytic process of electroplating.

Question 3

(a) Define the following terms with reference to workshop measurements:

(i) limits;

(ii) tolerance.

(b) Explain the need for marking out a workpiece and state any two tools used.

(c) Describe the following techniques used to mark out a workpiece:

(i) scribing;

(ii) centre punching.

(d) Draw and label the following instruments:

(i) vernier callipers;

(ii) vernier height gauge.

Answer

This question focuses on terminology and practices related to precision measurement and marking out in a workshop setting.

(a) Measurement Terms Definitions:

  • (i) Limits: The maximum and minimum permissible sizes (dimensions) for a specific feature of a workpiece. They define the acceptable range within which the actual dimension must fall for the part to be functional and interchangeable. For example, a shaft diameter might be specified as 25.00 mm +0.02/-0.01, meaning the upper limit is 25.02 mm and the lower limit is 24.99 mm.
  • (ii) Tolerance: The total permissible variation in a dimension. It is the difference between the upper limit and the lower limit of size. In the example above, the tolerance is (25.02 - 24.99) = 0.03 mm. Tolerance represents the allowable inaccuracy in manufacturing.

(b) Need for Marking Out and Tools:

  • Need for Marking Out: Marking out (or layout) is the process of transferring dimensions and shapes from a drawing onto a workpiece before machining or fabrication. It is necessary to:
    • Guide the machinist or fabricator during cutting, drilling, filing, or shaping operations.
    • Ensure the final component meets the required dimensions and specifications accurately.
    • Minimize material wastage by ensuring operations are performed in the correct locations.
    • Serve as a visual check before and during manufacturing.
  • Marking Out Tools (Any Two):
    • Scriber: Used to scratch fine lines onto metal surfaces.
    • Engineer's Try Square: Used to mark lines perpendicular to an edge and check for squareness.
    • Centre Punch / Dot Punch: Used to make small indentations to mark centres for drilling or to make layout lines more permanent.
    • Dividers: Used to scribe circles and arcs, or transfer measurements.
    • Odd-leg Calipers (Jenny Calipers): Used to scribe lines parallel to an edge or find the centre of round bars.
    • Surface Plate: A flat, stable reference surface on which marking out is performed.
    • Angle Plate: Used to hold workpieces square to the surface plate.
    • Vee Block: Used to hold cylindrical workpieces securely for marking or drilling.
    • Vernier Height Gauge / Scribing Block: Used with a surface plate to scribe accurate horizontal lines at specific heights.
    • Marking Blue / Layout Dye: Applied to the surface to make scribed lines more visible.

(c) Marking Out Techniques:

  • (i) Scribing: The technique of using a sharp-pointed tool, called a scriber, to scratch thin, precise lines onto the surface of a workpiece (often coated with marking blue). These lines indicate where material should be cut, bent, or drilled. The scriber is typically guided along a straight edge (like a rule or square) or using instruments like dividers or a height gauge.
  • (ii) Centre Punching: The technique of using a centre punch (which has a conical point, typically 90°) and a hammer to create a small, conical indentation at a specific point on the workpiece surface. This indentation serves primarily as a starting point to accurately guide a drill bit and prevent it from wandering when starting a hole. A dot punch (typically 60° point) is used similarly but creates smaller indentations to make scribed lines more permanent or mark intersections without creating too large a hole for drilling.

(d) Instrument Drawings:

These require actual technical drawings.

  • (i) Vernier Callipers: Sketch should show:
    • Fixed Jaw and Moving Jaw (for external measurements).
    • Internal Jaws (smaller, for internal measurements).
    • Main Scale (usually in mm).
    • Vernier Scale (sliding scale for precision reading).
    • Depth Rod (extends from the end for depth measurements).
    • Thumb Screw/Locking Screw.
  • (ii) Vernier Height Gauge: Sketch should show:
    • Heavy Base (to rest on surface plate).
    • Vertical Beam/Column with Main Scale.
    • Sliding Jaw/Head assembly carrying the Vernier Scale and a Scriber (or indicator).
    • Fine Adjustment Mechanism.
    • Locking Screws.
    • Scriber tip offset from the base.

Precise manufacturing requires understanding limits and tolerance. Marking out provides essential guides for fabrication, using tools like scribers and punches. Vernier instruments like callipers and height gauges allow for accurate measurement and marking.

Answer

This question covers sheet metal gauges, calculations, drilling machine components, welding methods, and lathe chuck types.

(a) Sheet Metal Gauge and Calculation:

  • (i) Completing Table 1: Standard Wire Gauge (SWG) is a system where lower gauge numbers represent thicker sheets. Common values (approximate) are:
    Standard Wire Gauge (SWG)Thickness (mm)
    103.2
    122.6
    142.0
    161.6
    181.2

    Note: Exact SWG values can vary slightly depending on the standard reference.

  • (ii) Cylinder Construction Length:

    Sheet metal SWG = 19 (Thickness = 1.0 mm, though thickness isn't needed for this calculation).

    Cylinder Outside Diameter (OD) = 150 mm.

    The question asks for the length of the *square* sheet metal required. This is highly ambiguous. It most likely means the length of the *rectangular* sheet metal piece needed to form the cylinder's curved surface (i.e., the circumference).

    Assuming the OD refers to the finished cylinder:

    The length required for the curved surface is the circumference based on the mean diameter or accounting for bending allowance. A simpler approach often used is to calculate the circumference based on the neutral axis, roughly the middle of the thickness.

    Mean Diameter = OD - Thickness = 150 mm - 1.0 mm = 149 mm.

    Circumference = $\pi \times \text{Mean Diameter} = \pi \times 149 \approx 468.1 \, \text{mm}$.

    If we ignore the thickness effect and use the OD: Circumference = $\pi \times OD = \pi \times 150 \approx 471.2 \, \text{mm}$.

    This circumference value would be the required length of the rectangular sheet metal piece (plus any overlap allowance for a seam, which isn't mentioned).

    If the question genuinely means a *square* sheet, it's unanswerable without knowing the cylinder's height or how the square is used. Assuming it asks for the length dimension of the rectangle needed for the cylinder body (circumference), the length required is approximately 468-471 mm, depending on the calculation method (plus seam allowance).

(b) Drilling Machine Parts Functions:

  • (i) Pillar (or Column): The main vertical support structure of the drill press. It holds the head (containing the motor and spindle assembly) and the worktable, providing rigidity and alignment for the drilling operation.
  • (ii) Spindle: The rotating shaft that holds the drill chuck or directly mounts the drill bit. It transmits the rotational motion from the motor (via belts and pulleys or gears) to the cutting tool and also allows for vertical feed movement into the workpiece.
  • (iii) Handwheel (or Feed Lever): The lever or wheel operated by the user to manually control the vertical downward movement (feed) of the rotating spindle and drill bit into the workpiece. Releasing it allows a spring mechanism to retract the spindle.

(c) Metal Joining Methods Descriptions:

  • (i) Oxy-acetylene Welding: A gas welding process that uses the combustion of oxygen and acetylene gases to produce a very high-temperature flame (approx. 3200°C). This flame melts the edges of the base metals to be joined. Often, a filler rod of similar composition is melted into the joint pool to add material and ensure a strong bond as the molten metal solidifies.
  • (ii) Arc Welding: A group of welding processes that use an electric arc formed between an electrode and the base metal to melt the metals at the joining point. The intense heat of the arc creates a molten weld pool. Examples include:
    • Shielded Metal Arc Welding (SMAW or Stick): Uses a consumable electrode coated in flux.
    • Gas Metal Arc Welding (GMAW or MIG): Uses a continuously fed wire electrode and a shielding gas.
    • Gas Tungsten Arc Welding (GTAW or TIG): Uses a non-consumable tungsten electrode, often a separate filler rod, and a shielding gas.

(d) Lathe Chucks Distinction:

  • Three-Jaw Self Centering Scroll Chuck:
    • Has three jaws that move simultaneously (radially inwards or outwards) when a single key (chuck key) is turned.
    • Automatically centers cylindrical or hexagonal workpieces quickly and with reasonable accuracy.
    • Cannot easily hold square, rectangular, or irregular shapes concentrically.
    • Less gripping force compared to a four-jaw chuck for the same size.
  • Four-Jaw Independent Chuck:
    • Has four jaws, each of which can be moved independently of the others using separate adjusting screws (keys).
    • Can hold square, rectangular, irregular, or cylindrical workpieces.
    • Allows for precise centering of workpieces (using a dial indicator) or intentionally offsetting the workpiece for eccentric turning.
    • Requires more time and skill to set up and center the workpiece accurately.
    • Generally provides a stronger grip than a three-jaw chuck.

This question tests knowledge of sheet metal standards, drilling machine components, common welding processes, and workpiece holding devices used on lathes.

Question 5

Figure 1 shows a bracket. Draw full size in $1^{st}$ angle projection the following views:

(a) front elevation in the direction of arrow F ;

(b) end elevation in the direction of arrow E ;

(c) plan in the direction of arrow P.

Insert six major dimensions.

Answer

This question requires creating standard orthographic views (Front Elevation, End Elevation, Plan) in First Angle Projection based on the provided pictorial view (Figure 1). Six major dimensions must be included.

Understanding First Angle Projection:

  • Object in the first quadrant.
  • Plan (Top View) is BELOW the Front Elevation.
  • End Elevation from the LEFT (Arrow E) is drawn to the RIGHT of the Front Elevation.

Analysis of Figure 1 (Bracket):

The pictorial view shows:

  • A base plate with curved ends and mounting slots/holes.
  • Two vertical upright supports extending from the base.
  • A horizontal top plate connecting the uprights, possibly with features like holes or bosses (details need careful observation from the drawing).
  • Ribs or webs might be present for support (check angles).
  • Dimensions are indicated on the pictorial view.

Required Views:

  • (a) Front Elevation (View F): Shows the main length and height profile. The shape of the base, the uprights, the top plate, and any features viewed directly from the front (Arrow F). Hidden details behind visible features are shown dashed.
  • (b) End Elevation (View E): Shows the width/depth and height profile. View from the Left (Arrow E), drawn to the right of the Front Elevation. Shows the profile of the base end, the thickness/profile of the uprights, the top plate, and any side features.
  • (c) Plan (View P): Shows the length and width/depth profile from above. Drawn below the Front Elevation. Shows the shape of the base plate including curved ends and slots/holes, the top edges of the uprights, the shape of the top plate, and any features on the top surface.

Procedure:

  1. Set up the drawing sheet.
  2. Draw the Front Elevation (View F), accurately transferring dimensions. Include hidden lines.
  3. Project lines horizontally to the right for the End Elevation (View E). Draw View E, aligning features and using dimensions for width/depth.
  4. Project lines vertically downwards from the Front Elevation for the Plan (View P). Draw View P, aligning features and using dimensions for width/depth (which correspond to the dimensions in View E).
  5. Add the First Angle projection symbol.
  6. Add six important dimensions (overall length, height, width; key feature locations or sizes) following standard dimensioning practice.

Requires accurate orthographic drafting in First Angle Projection, interpreting the pictorial view, showing hidden details, and adding correct dimensioning.

Question 6

Figure 2 shows two views of a solid drawn in $1^{\text {st }}$ angle projection. Draw an isometric view of the object making corner N the lowest.

Answer

This question requires constructing an isometric view from given orthographic views (Front Elevation and Plan, assumed based on 1st Angle layout), ensuring a specific corner (N) is the lowest point in the isometric drawing.

Understanding the Views (First Angle):

  • The top view (Plan) is below the Front Elevation.
  • The Front Elevation shows height and width.
  • The Plan shows width and depth.

Analysis of Figure 2:

  • The object appears to be a block with multiple levels or steps.
  • The Front Elevation shows a profile with different heights.
  • The Plan shows the top view shape, indicating widths and depths, possibly cutouts or angled surfaces.
  • Corner N is identified on the Plan view.

Isometric Construction Steps:

  1. Orient the Axes: Determine the orientation of the isometric axes (typically vertical, 30° left, 30° right) so that corner N (identified from the Plan and Front Elevation) becomes the lowest point of the drawing. This means the Width, Depth, and Height axes will originate from point N.
  2. Overall Dimensions: Extract the overall Width, Depth, and Height from the Front Elevation and Plan views.
  3. Bounding Box (Optional but helpful): Draw a light isometric box representing these overall dimensions, starting from corner N.
  4. Transfer Features from Plan: Project the shape seen in the Plan view onto the base plane of the isometric drawing (the plane defined by the Width and Depth axes starting at N). Transfer key points and lines along the isometric axes.
  5. Transfer Features from Elevation: Project heights from the Front Elevation vertically upwards along the Height axis from the corresponding points established on the base plane.
  6. Connect Points: Connect the transferred points with lines parallel to the isometric axes to define the edges and surfaces of the object. Pay attention to visible and hidden lines (though isometric views often omit hidden lines unless specifically requested or necessary for clarity).
  7. Refine and Darken: Check the proportions and connections. Darken the visible object lines and erase construction lines.

Requires accurate interpretation of the First Angle orthographic views and careful construction of the isometric projection, ensuring the correct orientation with corner N as the origin/lowest point.

Question 7

Figure 3 shows a truncated cone. Draw the given view and add the following:

(i) the plan;

(ii) true shape;

(iii) the development of the cone.

Answer

This question involves working with the geometry of a truncated cone, requiring the construction of the plan view, the true shape of the cut surface, and the surface development of the remaining frustum.

Procedure:

  1. Redraw the Elevation: Accurately copy the given Front Elevation of the truncated cone from Figure 3, including the base diameter, height (or apex position if shown/implied), and the cutting plane angle/position.
  2. (i) Construct the Plan View:
    • Project the base diameter vertically downwards from the Elevation to draw the circular base in the Plan view.
    • The cutting plane intersects the cone, forming an ellipse in the Plan view (since the cut is oblique).
    • Divide the base circle in the Plan into a number of equal parts (e.g., 12). Project these points up to the base line in the Elevation.
    • Draw generator lines from the apex (or extended apex if only frustum is shown) through these base points in the Elevation, extending up to the cutting plane.
    • Mark the points where these generators intersect the cutting plane in the Elevation.
    • Project these intersection points vertically downwards to the corresponding radial lines (drawn from the centre to the division points on the base circle) in the Plan view.
    • Draw a smooth curve through these projected points in the Plan to represent the elliptical cut surface.
  3. (ii) Construct the True Shape of the Cut Surface:
    • Draw projection lines perpendicular to the cutting plane line in the Elevation from the intersection points found earlier.
    • Establish a datum line (X1-Y1) parallel to the cutting plane line in the Elevation.
    • Measure the perpendicular distances (widths) of the points on the elliptical cut surface in the Plan view from a centre line (parallel to the projection lines).
    • Transfer these widths onto the corresponding projection lines drawn from the Elevation, measuring outwards from the datum line X1-Y1.
    • Draw a smooth curve through these plotted points to obtain the true shape (which will be an ellipse).
  4. (iii) Construct the Development of the Truncated Cone (Frustum):
    • Determine the true slant height (L) of the original (untruncated) cone. This is the length from the apex to the base along the cone surface, visible in the Elevation if the generator is parallel to the viewing plane, or needs calculation/projection otherwise. Let the apex be O, base radius R.
    • Using the true slant height L as the radius, draw a large arc from a center point O'.
    • Calculate the angle of the sector required for the full cone development: $\alpha = (R/L) \times 360^{\circ}$.
    • Mark out this sector angle $\alpha$ on the large arc. The arc length of this sector should equal the base circumference ($2\pi R$). Divide this arc into the same number of equal parts (e.g., 12) as the Plan base circle. Draw radial lines from O' to these division points.
    • Transfer the true lengths of the generators from the apex (O) to the cutting plane intersections in the Elevation onto the corresponding radial lines in the development, measuring from O'. (True lengths might need to be found by rotation if not directly visible).
    • Draw a smooth curve connecting these points on the radial lines in the development.
    • The area between the large base arc and this smooth curve represents the development of the lateral surface of the truncated cone (frustum).

Requires accurate geometric construction techniques for projecting views, finding true shapes of oblique sections, and developing the surface of a truncated cone using radial line development.

Question 8

(a) Draw free hand sketches of the following hand tools:

(i) hacksaw;

(ii) side cutter;

(iii) flat screw driver;

(iv) flat file;

(v) tape measure.

(b) Draw a regular hexagon given that the length across the flats is 70 mm .

Answer

This question involves sketching common hand tools and constructing a regular hexagon given the distance across the flats.

(a) Free Hand Sketches of Hand Tools:

Requires reasonably recognizable freehand sketches of each tool, showing key features.

  • (i) Hacksaw: Should show the frame (adjustable or fixed), handle, blade (with teeth direction indicated), and tensioning mechanism (wing nut or lever).
  • (ii) Side Cutter (Diagonal Pliers): Should show the two handles, pivot point, and the cutting jaws oriented diagonally to the handles.
  • (iii) Flat Screw Driver: Should show the handle (plastic or wood), shaft (shank), and the flat, wedge-shaped tip.
  • (iv) Flat File: Should show the tang (for the handle), body (rectangular cross-section, tapering slightly), teeth pattern (e.g., single or double cut), and point (tip). Often shown with a separate handle fitted onto the tang.
  • (v) Tape Measure: Should show the coiled tape (metal or fabric) within a casing, the hook at the end of the tape, measurement markings (cm/mm or inches), and possibly a locking mechanism and belt clip.

(b) Construction of Regular Hexagon (Across Flats = 70 mm):

The distance "across the flats" (A/F) is the perpendicular distance between two opposite parallel sides of the hexagon.

Method 1 (Using Circles):

  1. Draw two parallel horizontal lines 70 mm apart. These represent two opposite flats of the hexagon.
  2. Draw a perpendicular bisector (centre line) to these two lines.
  3. The distance from the centre to a flat is half the A/F distance, so 70 mm / 2 = 35 mm.
  4. The side length (s) of a regular hexagon is related to the distance across flats (A/F) by $A/F = s \sqrt{3}$. So, $s = (A/F) / \sqrt{3} = 70 / \sqrt{3} \approx 40.4 \, \text{mm}$.
  5. The distance across corners (A/C) is $A/C = 2s = 2 \times (70 / \sqrt{3}) \approx 80.8 \, \text{mm}$. The radius of the circumscribing circle is $R = s = 70 / \sqrt{3}$. The radius of the inscribing circle (tangent to flats) is $r = (A/F)/2 = 35 \, \text{mm}$.
  6. Draw the inscribed circle with radius 35 mm centered on the intersection of the centre lines drawn in step 2.
  7. Using a 60° set square (or compasses), draw tangent lines to this circle at 60° intervals, starting from the top or bottom flat line intersection. These tangent lines will form the hexagon. Draw tangents at 0°, 60°, 120°, 180°, 240°, 300° relative to the vertical center line, ensuring they touch the circle. The intersection points of these tangents form the vertices.

Method 2 (Direct Construction with 60°):

  1. Draw two parallel horizontal lines 70 mm apart.
  2. Draw a vertical centre line intersecting these lines at points A and B.
  3. Using a 60°/30° set square, draw lines from A at 60° to the horizontal (i.e., 30° to the vertical centre line) outwards in both directions.
  4. Similarly, draw lines from B at 60° to the horizontal outwards in both directions.
  5. The intersection of the lines from A with the bottom horizontal line, and the lines from B with the top horizontal line, define four vertices.
  6. The remaining two vertices lie on the horizontal centre line. Alternatively, the length of the side where the 60° line meets the parallel line can be used to mark other sides. The intersection points of the 60° lines form the hexagon outline. The points on the parallel lines are the midpoints of the sides.
  7. Connect the appropriate intersection points to form the hexagon. The sides should be parallel to the 60° construction lines or horizontal.

Requires freehand sketching skills for part (a) and accurate geometric construction using drawing instruments for part (b), applying the properties of a regular hexagon.
