Question 1

(a) State:

(i) the purpose of the Factory's Act (Electricity special regulation) in relation to industrial safety;

(ii) four factors to be considered in designing workshop layout.

(b) Describe the procedure followed in rescuing a victim who is in contact with live electrical conductors and is in a state of electric shock.

(c) Explain where the following types of fire extinguishers are used and state their colours:

(i) foam;

(ii) vaporizing liquid.

Answer

This question addresses electrical safety regulations, workshop layout, electric shock rescue, and fire extinguisher types.

(a) Factory's Act and Workshop Layout:

  • (i) Purpose of Factory's Act (Electricity Special Regulation): The primary purpose is to ensure the safety of persons working with or near electrical equipment in industrial settings (factories, workshops, etc.). It sets minimum standards for the installation, maintenance, operation, and use of electrical systems and apparatus to prevent dangers such as electric shock, burns, fire, and explosion arising from electricity.
  • (ii) Factors in Designing Workshop Layout (Four Factors):
    1. Workflow/Process Sequence: Arrange machines and workstations logically to match the sequence of operations, minimizing material movement and backtracking.
    2. Space Requirements: Allow adequate space around machines for safe operation, maintenance access, material handling, and operator movement. Consider space for storage of raw materials, work-in-progress, and finished goods.
    3. Safety and Health: Position machines to minimize hazards (e.g., sparks away from flammable materials), ensure clear walkways and emergency exits, provide adequate lighting and ventilation, and consider noise levels.
    4. Material Handling: Plan efficient routes and methods for moving materials (e.g., use of trolleys, conveyors, cranes), minimizing distances and obstructions.
    5. Flexibility/Future Expansion: Consider potential changes in production or future expansion when planning the layout.
    6. Utilities Access: Ensure convenient access to power supplies, compressed air, water, etc., for machines and workstations.

(b) Rescuing an Electric Shock Victim in Contact with Live Conductor:

Priority is rescuer safety FIRST. Do NOT touch the victim directly.

  1. Break Electrical Contact Safely:
    • Immediately switch off the power supply at the source (main switch, breaker, socket) if it can be done quickly and safely.
    • If the power cannot be switched off immediately, use a dry, non-conductive object (e.g., wooden broom handle, dry plank of wood, rubber mat, dry rope) to push or pull the victim away from the live conductor, OR push/pull the conductor away from the victim. Stand on a dry insulating surface if possible (e.g., rubber mat, dry wood).
  2. Assess the Victim (After ensuring safety): Once clear of the electrical source, check for responsiveness (tap and shout) and normal breathing (look, listen, feel).
  3. Call for Emergency Help: Shout for help. Call emergency medical services immediately (or have someone else call).
  4. Provide First Aid:
    • If unresponsive and not breathing normally, start CPR (Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation) immediately if trained.
    • If breathing normally but unresponsive, place the victim in the recovery position.
    • Treat any visible burns with cool running water (after ensuring they are no longer in shock) and cover loosely with a sterile dressing.
    • Keep the victim warm and monitor their condition until professional help arrives.

(c) Fire Extinguisher Types, Uses, and Colours:

  • (i) Foam Extinguisher:
    • Use: Primarily used for Class A fires (solids like wood, paper) and Class B fires (flammable liquids like petrol, oil, paint). The foam forms a blanket, smothering the fire and cooling the fuel. Not suitable for live electrical fires (foam is water-based and conductive).
    • Colour (Traditional UK/EU): Cream. (Modern EN3 Standard: Red body with Cream band).
  • (ii) Vaporizing Liquid Extinguisher (Halon/Halon Replacement):
    • Use: Historically, Halon extinguishers were very effective on Class B, C, and electrical fires as the gas interfered with the chemical reaction of fire and left no residue. However, due to their ozone-depleting properties, Halon production is banned under the Montreal Protocol. Modern replacements (e.g., Halotron, FM-200, Novec 1230 - often called 'Clean Agents') are used for similar applications, especially protecting sensitive electronic equipment (computer rooms, aircraft) where residue is undesirable. They work by chemical inhibition or cooling.
    • Colour (Traditional Halon - UK): Emerald Green. Clean Agent replacements often have a Green band on a Red body (EN3) or specific manufacturer colours.

Electrical safety involves regulations and safe rescue procedures. Workshop layout impacts efficiency and safety. Knowing fire extinguisher types and their appropriate use is crucial.

Question 2

(a) Outline:

(i) one application in modern engineering where plastic has replaced metal parts and state any two of their advantages;

(ii) three factors to be considered when selecting a type of polish for a particular finish.

(b) Explain the following properties of metals:

(i) malleability;

(ii) ductility.

(c) Describe the characteristics of the following engineering materials and state their applications:

(i) tin;

(ii) aluminium;

(iii) rubber;

(iv) mild steel.

Answer

This question covers material substitution (plastic for metal), polishing, metal properties, and characteristics/applications of specific materials.

(a) Plastics, Polishing:

  • (i) Plastic Replacing Metal Application & Advantages:
    • Application Example: Car Bumpers and Body Panels. Traditionally made of steel, many modern car bumpers, fenders, and trim pieces are made from engineering plastics (like Polypropylene, ABS, Polycarbonate blends).
    • Advantages (Any Two):
      • Weight Reduction: Plastics are significantly lighter than steel, contributing to improved fuel efficiency and vehicle handling.
      • Corrosion Resistance: Plastics do not rust or corrode like steel, improving longevity and appearance, especially in harsh weather conditions.
      • Design Flexibility & Cost: Plastics can be easily molded into complex aerodynamic shapes, integrating features like grilles or light housings, often at a lower manufacturing cost (via injection molding) compared to stamping and assembling metal parts.
      • Impact Absorption: Engineered plastics can be designed to absorb impact energy effectively in low-speed collisions, often returning to their original shape.
    Other examples: Plumbing pipes (PVC/PEX replacing copper/lead/steel), appliance housings, electrical enclosures, gears in light-duty applications.
  • (ii) Factors in Selecting Polish (Three):
    1. Material Being Polished: Different materials (metals, plastics, wood, paint) require polishes with appropriate abrasives and chemical compositions to avoid damage (e.g., harsh abrasive on soft plastic vs. metal polish).
    2. Desired Level of Finish: Polishes range from coarse cutting compounds (removing heavy scratches) to medium polishing compounds (removing lighter scratches, restoring shine) to fine finishing polishes (producing a high gloss, mirror-like finish). The initial condition and desired final appearance dictate the choice.
    3. Type of Defect to Remove: Specific polishes are formulated to remove particular defects like oxidation, swirl marks, light scratches, or water spots.
    4. Application Method: Some polishes are designed for hand application, while others are optimized for use with machine polishers (rotary or dual-action).
    5. Environmental/Safety Considerations: Availability of water-based vs. solvent-based, low-VOC options.

(b) Metal Properties Explained:

  • (i) Malleability: (Repeated from July 2016 Q1c) The ability of a material to deform plastically under compressive stress without fracture. Characterized by the ability to be hammered or rolled into thin sheets.
  • (ii) Ductility: (Repeated from July 2016 Q1c) The ability of a material to deform plastically under tensile stress without fracture. Characterized by the ability to be drawn into a thin wire.

(c) Material Characteristics and Applications:

  • (i) Tin (Sn):
    • Characteristics: Soft, silvery-white, highly malleable and ductile metal; excellent corrosion resistance (especially against water and weak acids); low melting point; non-toxic.
    • Applications: Coating for steel (tinplate) used in food cans; ingredient in solders (lead-free and traditional); component in alloys like bronze (copper-tin) and pewter (tin-antimony-copper); bearings (babbitt metal).
  • (ii) Aluminium (Al):
    • Characteristics: (Repeated from July 2017 Q4a) Lightweight (low density); good electrical and thermal conductivity; excellent corrosion resistance (forms passive oxide layer); highly ductile and malleable; non-magnetic; high reflectivity; good strength-to-weight ratio (especially alloys).
    • Applications: Aerospace components; automotive parts (engine blocks, wheels, body panels); electrical conductors (overhead power lines); construction (window frames, roofing, cladding); packaging (cans, foil); cookware.
  • (iii) Rubber (Natural or Synthetic):
    • Characteristics: Elastomer (can undergo large elastic deformation and return to original shape); high elasticity; good electrical insulator; waterproof; resistant to abrasion (depending on type); can be vulcanized to improve strength and durability. Properties vary widely between natural rubber and different synthetic types (e.g., SBR, Neoprene, Silicone).
    • Applications: Vehicle tires; seals, gaskets, O-rings; electrical insulation (wire coverings, gloves); hoses; belts; shock absorbers/vibration dampers; footwear soles; waterproof clothing.
  • (iv) Mild Steel (Low Carbon Steel):
    • Characteristics: Alloy of iron with low carbon content (typically 0.05-0.25%); relatively soft but tough; high tensile strength; ductile and malleable; easily welded and machined; poor corrosion resistance (rusts easily); magnetic. Most common and least expensive type of steel.
    • Applications: Structural sections (beams, columns); car bodies; reinforcing bars (rebar) for concrete; machinery parts; fencing; nuts, bolts, screws; pipelines; general fabrication.

Addresses the trend of using plastics, factors in polishing, definitions of malleability/ductility, and the key features and uses of tin, aluminium, rubber, and mild steel.

Question 3

(a) (i) Draw a labelled diagram of a metric micrometer screw gauge.

(ii) Make a sketch of a(i) above showing a scale reading of 10.67 mm.

(b) Explain the safety procedures and care when using and handling the following hand tools:

(i) hacksaw;

(ii) files.

Answer

This question focuses on the micrometer screw gauge (diagram, reading) and the safe use and care of hacksaws and files.

(a) Micrometer Screw Gauge:

  • (i) Labelled Diagram: (Repeated from July 2016 Q2d & Nov 2015 Q2b) Sketch should show: Frame, Anvil, Spindle, Sleeve (Barrel) with Main Scale, Thimble with Circular/Vernier Scale, Ratchet Stop, Locking Lever/Nut.
  • (ii) Sketch Showing 10.67 mm Reading:
    • Main Scale Reading: The edge of the thimble must have passed the 10 mm mark and the next half-millimeter mark (10.5 mm) on the sleeve. Visible main scale reading = 10.5 mm.
    • Thimble Scale Reading: The remaining amount is $10.67 - 10.5 = 0.17 \, \text{mm}$. The thimble division that aligns with the sleeve's datum line must be '17' (since each division is 0.01 mm).
    • Drawing: Sketch the sleeve showing the 10 mm mark and the 0.5 mm mark after it. Draw the thimble positioned so its edge is past the 10.5 mm mark, and the '17' mark on its scale aligns with the datum line on the sleeve.

(b) Safety Procedures and Care for Hand Tools:

  • (i) Hacksaw:
    • Safety/Use: Secure workpiece firmly. Select correct blade TPI and mount with teeth forward, tensioned properly. Use steady, full strokes applying pressure forward only. Keep hands clear. Wear safety glasses.
    • Care: Keep blades clean and lightly oiled when stored (if prone to rust). Store frame carefully to avoid bending. Replace worn or broken blades promptly. Check handle security.
  • (ii) Files:
    • Safety/Use: Always use a file with a secure handle fitted to the tang. Apply pressure only on the forward stroke. Select the right file type and cut for the material and task. Keep hands away from sharp edges of the workpiece. Wear safety glasses.
    • Care: (Repeated from July 2017 Q2b & Nov 2015 Q3c) Store files separately (not touching each other). Clean frequently with a file card/brush during use. Keep dry to prevent rust. Do not use as hammers or pry bars.

Requires understanding the components and reading of a micrometer, and the safe, effective use and proper care of common hand tools like hacksaws and files.

Question 4

(a) With the aid of a diagram, explain the procedure of joining two pieces of metal using a snap rivet.

(b) Draw a labelled diagram of a screw thread and state the difference between a pitch and the lead.

(c) Distinguish between:

(i) soft soldering and brazing;

(ii) gas welding and arc welding.

Answer

This question covers riveting, screw thread terminology, and distinctions between different thermal joining processes.

(a) Joining with a Snap Head Rivet:

A snap head rivet is a common type with a rounded head. Riveting joins parts permanently.

Procedure:

  1. Preparation: Align the two pieces of metal to be joined. Drill or punch matching holes through both pieces, slightly larger than the rivet shank diameter. Deburr the holes.
  2. Insertion: Insert the snap head rivet through the aligned holes so the pre-formed snap head rests against one side.
  3. Support: Place the pre-formed head onto a rivet set (a tool with a matching concave depression) or a solid surface (dolly) to support it.
  4. Upsetting (Forming Second Head): Use a hammer and another tool called a snap (which also has a concave depression matching the desired finished head shape) on the tail end of the rivet. Strike the snap tool firmly with the hammer. This action causes the tail end of the rivet shank to deform, shorten, and expand radially to fill the hole tightly, while forming a second rounded (snap) head that matches the first one. Initial blows might use the flat face of the hammer or a different tool to start forming the head before finishing with the snap tool.
  5. Result: The two formed heads clamp the metal pieces tightly together.

(b) Screw Thread Diagram and Terminology:

  • Labelled Diagram: Sketch should show a helical thread profile on a cylinder (external thread) or inside a hole (internal thread). Labels should include:
    • Major Diameter: Largest diameter (crest-to-crest for external, root-to-root for internal).
    • Minor Diameter (Root Diameter): Smallest diameter (root-to-root for external, crest-to-crest for internal).
    • Pitch Diameter (Effective Diameter): An imaginary diameter between major and minor diameters where the thread and groove widths are equal.
    • Crest: Top surface of the thread.
    • Root: Bottom surface between adjacent threads.
    • Flank: Surface connecting crest and root.
    • Pitch (P): Axial distance between corresponding points on adjacent threads (e.g., crest to crest), measured parallel to the axis.
    • Lead (L): Axial distance the screw advances in one complete revolution (360°).
    • Thread Angle: Included angle between flanks.
    • Helix Angle: Angle the thread makes with a plane perpendicular to the axis.
  • Difference between Pitch and Lead:
    • Pitch (P): The distance between adjacent thread forms measured parallel to the thread axis.
    • Lead (L): The distance the threaded part moves axially in one complete revolution relative to its mating part.
    • For a single-start thread (most common), the lead is equal to the pitch (L = P).
    • For a multi-start thread (e.g., double-start, triple-start), the lead is equal to the pitch multiplied by the number of starts (L = nP, where n is the number of starts). Multi-start threads provide faster axial movement per revolution.

(c) Distinctions Between Joining Processes:

  • (i) Soft Soldering vs. Brazing: (Repeated from Nov 2015 Q3a & Nov 2016 Q4b)
    • Temperature: Soldering < 450°C; Brazing > 450°C.
    • Strength: Brazing stronger than soldering.
    • Filler Metals: Solder (Tin-based); Brazing Alloys (Copper/Silver/Aluminium-based).
  • (ii) Gas Welding vs. Arc Welding:
    • Heat Source: Gas welding (typically Oxy-acetylene) uses heat from the combustion of fuel gas with oxygen. Arc welding uses heat generated by an electric arc struck between an electrode and the workpiece.
    • Temperature: Arc welding generally produces higher temperatures concentrated in a smaller area compared to gas welding flames (though oxy-acetylene flame is very hot, arc temperatures are higher).
    • Equipment: Gas welding requires gas cylinders, regulators, hoses, and a torch. Arc welding requires a power source (AC/DC welder), electrode holder/gun, earth clamp, and cables (plus shielding gas/flux depending on process).
    • Application/Control: Gas welding often offers finer heat control, suitable for thin materials or brazing/soldering. Arc welding processes are generally faster and more suitable for thicker materials and structural work. Different arc processes (Stick, MIG, TIG) offer varying levels of control and suitability for different materials.

Covers the practical steps of riveting, fundamental screw thread terminology, and key differences between related joining processes like soldering/brazing and gas/arc welding.

Question 5

(a) Draw a labelled diagram of a twist drill and state the functions of the following parts:

(i) flute;

(ii) land.

(b) Draw the following lathe machine tools shapes and state the function of each:

(i) round nose rougher;

(ii) knife tool;

(iii) form tool.

(c) (i) Describe the following tools and state the function of each:

I. steel rule;

II. scriber;

III. engineer's square.

(ii) Sketch a pair of dividers and state its two functions.

Answer

This question focuses on drilling tools, lathe tools, and basic measuring/marking out tools.

(a) Twist Drill Diagram and Parts Functions:

  • Labelled Diagram: (Repeated from July 2016 Q4d) Sketch should show Shank, Body, Flutes, Land, Point (Cutting Lips, Chisel Edge, Point Angle, Lip Clearance Angle), Web.
  • Functions:
    • (i) Flute: The helical groove(s) running up the drill body. Functions:
      1. Allow chips (swarf) produced during cutting to escape from the hole.
      2. Allow cutting fluid (if used) to reach the cutting edges at the point.
      3. Help form the cutting lips at the point.
    • (ii) Land: The narrow raised surface between the flutes, running along the helix. Functions:
      1. Supports the drill bit within the hole, keeping it aligned.
      2. Provides clearance behind the cutting edge (due to body clearance/back taper) to reduce friction against the hole wall.

(b) Lathe Tool Shapes and Functions:

Requires sketches showing tool geometry.

  • (i) Round Nose Rougher:
    • Shape Sketch: Tool bit with a distinct, relatively large radius at the cutting corner (nose). Robust geometry.
    • Function: Used for heavy cuts (roughing) to remove large amounts of material quickly during turning or facing operations. The large nose radius provides strength and helps distribute cutting forces.
  • (ii) Knife Tool:
    • Shape Sketch: Tool bit with a pointed nose formed by straight cutting edges meeting at a sharp angle (like a knife edge). Can have very small or no nose radius. Often has significant end relief.
    • Function: Primarily used for operations requiring a sharp point or narrow cut, such as turning into sharp corners (square shoulders), grooving, cutting V-threads (if ground to correct angle), or chamfering. Can also be used for light turning or facing.
  • (iii) Form Tool:
    • Shape Sketch: Tool bit ground to a specific profile or contour that matches the shape required on the workpiece. The cutting edge has the inverse shape of the desired form.
    • Function: Used to produce specific shapes or profiles directly onto the workpiece in one plunge cut (feeding radially) or sometimes a form turning cut (feeding axially). Examples include creating grooves of specific shapes, rounded corners (radii), or complex decorative profiles.

(c) Tool Descriptions and Functions:

  • (i) Descriptions and Functions:
    • I. Steel Rule: A flat strip of steel (or sometimes other material) marked with graduated scales (e.g., mm, cm, inches). Function: Used for making linear measurements with moderate accuracy and as a straight edge for marking or checking. (See also Nov 2016 Q3b).
    • II. Scriber: A hand tool with a hardened, sharp point. Function: Used to scratch fine lines on metal or other materials during marking out to indicate cutting lines, centres, etc. (See also July 2016 Q2a).
    • III. Engineer's Square: An L-shaped precision tool with two arms (stock and blade) set at exactly 90°. Function: Used for marking lines perpendicular to an edge and for checking surfaces or components for squareness (90°). (See also July 2016 Q2a).
  • (ii) Dividers Sketch and Functions:
    • Sketch: Show two pointed legs joined by a pivot at the top, often with a spring and adjusting screw mechanism for setting the distance between the points.
    • Functions (Two):
      1. Scribing circles and arcs onto a workpiece.
      2. Transferring measurements from a rule to a workpiece, or comparing dimensions.
      3. Stepping off equal distances along a line or curve.

Tests knowledge of twist drill features, common lathe tool shapes and their uses, and the description and function of basic measuring and marking tools.

Question 6

Figure 1 shows a bracket. Draw in first angle projection the following:

(i) front elevation in the direction of arrow A;

(ii) end elevation in the direction of arrow B;

(iii) plan in the direction of arrow C.

Insert six major dimensions.

Answer

This question requires creating standard orthographic views (Front Elevation, End Elevation, Plan) in First Angle Projection from the provided pictorial view of a bracket (Figure 1). Six major dimensions must be included.

Understanding First Angle Projection:

  • Object imagined in the first quadrant.
  • Plan (View C, Top View) is drawn BELOW the Front Elevation (View A).
  • End Elevation from the Right (View B) is drawn to the LEFT of the Front Elevation (View A).

Analysis of Figure 1 (Bracket):

  • A base plate, possibly rectangular, with mounting features (holes/slots).
  • An upright section, possibly cylindrical or prismatic, maybe offset or central.
  • Supporting ribs or webs connecting the base and upright section.
  • Dimensions need to be inferred or scaled from the pictorial view.

Required Views:

  • (i) Front Elevation (View A): Shows the view looking along arrow A. Will display the primary height and width/length profile, including the base, upright, and ribs as seen from this direction. Hidden features shown dashed.
  • (ii) End Elevation (View B): Shows the view looking along arrow B (Right End View). Drawn to the LEFT of the Front Elevation. Will display the height and depth/width profile, including the end shape of the base, the profile of the upright section, and rib thickness. Hidden features shown dashed.
  • (iii) Plan (View C): Shows the view looking down along arrow C (Top View). Drawn BELOW the Front Elevation. Will display the overall length/width and depth profile, including the shape of the base, mounting holes, top view of the upright section, and top edges of ribs. Hidden features shown dashed.

Procedure:

  1. Set up drawing sheet for First Angle Projection.
  2. Draw the Front Elevation (A) accurately based on the pictorial view.
  3. Project lines horizontally to the left for the End Elevation (B). Draw view B using depth/width dimensions and aligning heights.
  4. Project lines vertically downwards from the Front Elevation (A) for the Plan (C). Draw view C below, using depth/width dimensions (consistent with view B) and aligning lengths/widths.
  5. Add the First Angle projection symbol.
  6. Add six major dimensions (overall length, width, height; key feature locations/sizes) following standard dimensioning practices.

Requires accurate orthographic drafting in First Angle Projection, interpreting the pictorial view, correctly representing all features including hidden lines, and applying standard dimensioning.

Question 7

(a) Construct a pentagon given that the diameter of circle is 40 mm.

(b) Figure 2 shows the front elevation of truncated hexagonal base pyramid. Copy the front elevation and complete the :

(i) plan;

(ii) true shape;

(iii) auxiliary view at BB'.

Answer

This question involves geometric constructions: inscribing a pentagon in a circle, and performing orthographic and auxiliary projections for a truncated hexagonal pyramid.

(a) Pentagon Construction in Circle (Diameter 40 mm):

Construct a regular pentagon inscribed within a circle of diameter 40 mm (Radius R = 20 mm).

  1. Draw the circle with center O and radius 20 mm.
  2. Draw a horizontal diameter AOB and a perpendicular vertical radius OC.
  3. Find the midpoint M of radius OB.
  4. Set compass radius to MC. Place compass point at M and draw an arc intersecting diameter AOB at point D (between O and A).
  5. The length CD is the side length of the inscribed regular pentagon.
  6. Set compass radius to CD. Starting from point C (top vertex), step off this distance five times around the circumference of the circle to mark the five vertices of the pentagon.
  7. Connect the consecutive vertices with straight lines to form the regular pentagon.

(b) Truncated Hexagonal Pyramid Projections:

  1. Copy Elevation: Accurately redraw the given Front Elevation from Figure 2, showing the hexagonal base (as a line), the apex (or height), the slant edges, and the cutting plane line.
  2. (i) Complete Plan View:
    • Project the base width(s) vertically downwards from the Elevation. Construct the regular hexagonal base in the Plan view below the Elevation, correctly oriented (e.g., flat or corner facing front based on Elevation). Label vertices 1-6.
    • Project the apex vertically down to the center of the hexagon in the Plan. Draw the slant edges (lines from the center/apex to vertices 1-6) in the Plan.
    • Project the points where the cutting plane intersects the slant edges in the Elevation vertically down onto the corresponding slant edges in the Plan view (1'-6').
    • Connect points 1'-6' in the Plan to show the shape of the cut surface as seen from above. Show hidden lines appropriately.
  3. (ii) True Shape of Cut Surface:
    • Project lines perpendicularly from the cutting plane line in the Elevation, using intersection points 1'-6'.
    • Establish a datum line parallel to the cutting plane line.
    • Measure the perpendicular distances (widths) of points 1'-6' from a reference line (e.g., center line) in the Plan view.
    • Transfer these widths onto the corresponding projection lines in the true shape view, measuring from the datum line.
    • Connect the plotted points 1''-6'' to obtain the true shape of the hexagonal cut surface.
  4. (iii) Auxiliary View at BB':
    • The line BB' indicates the direction/plane for an auxiliary view. Typically, this implies viewing the object perpendicularly to line BB'.
    • Draw projection lines perpendicular to BB' from key points on the Elevation (base vertices, apex, cut points 1'-6').
    • Establish a datum line (X1-Y1) parallel to BB'.
    • Measure distances of the key points from a reference plane in the original views (e.g., distances from the center line in the Plan view, representing depth).
    • Transfer these distances onto the corresponding projection lines in the auxiliary view, measuring perpendicularly from the datum line X1-Y1.
    • Connect the plotted points to form the auxiliary view of the truncated pyramid as seen looking along BB'.

Requires accurate geometric construction for the pentagon, and proficiency in orthographic and auxiliary projection techniques to determine the plan, true shape, and auxiliary view of the truncated pyramid.

Question 8

(a) Figure 3 shows two views of an object. Draw the isometric view of this casting making corner N as the lowest point. Insert six dimensions.

(b) Sketch the following workshop tools:

(i) wood chisel;

(ii) combination pliers.

Answer

This question involves constructing an isometric view from orthographic projections and sketching common workshop tools.

(a) Isometric View Construction:

Requires constructing an isometric view from the given orthographic views (Figure 3, assume Third Angle: Plan Above Front Elevation), ensuring corner N is the lowest point. Six dimensions must be added to the isometric view.

Analysis of Views (Figure 3):

  • Identify the Front Elevation and Plan views.
  • Determine the object's shape: Looks like a base plate with an upright section containing curves and possibly a hole or slot.
  • Locate corner N on both views.
  • Extract overall Width, Height, and Depth dimensions.

Isometric Construction Steps:

  1. Orient Axes: Set up isometric axes originating from corner N as the lowest point. Determine the directions corresponding to Width, Depth, and Height based on the orthographic views and the position of N.
  2. Construct Basic Shape: Draw the main block or bounding box corresponding to the overall dimensions starting from N.
  3. Transfer Features: Carefully transfer all features (curves, holes, steps, ribs) from the Front Elevation and Plan onto the isometric framework. Use the ellipse construction method for circles/curves on isometric planes. Measure distances along the isometric axes.
  4. Refine Lines: Complete the object outline, darkening visible lines and erasing construction lines. Omit hidden lines.
  5. Dimensioning: Add six major dimensions directly onto the isometric view using standard isometric dimensioning techniques (dimension lines and text aligned with isometric planes). Choose important overall dimensions and key feature sizes/locations.

(b) Workshop Tool Sketches:

Requires freehand sketches.

  • (i) Wood Chisel: Show the handle (wood or plastic, often with a metal ferrule), the steel blade (shank), and the flat, beveled cutting edge at the end. Specify type if known (e.g., bevel edge, firmer).
  • (ii) Combination Pliers: (Repeated from Nov 2015 Q6a) Show handles, pivot, flat serrated gripping jaws, curved gripping section, and wire cutting blades near the pivot.

Part (a) requires proficiency in creating dimensioned isometric drawings from third angle orthographic views. Part (b) tests the ability to sketch common hand tools.
