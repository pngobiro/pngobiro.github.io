SECTION A: MATERIALS, PROCESSES AND WORKSHOP TECHNOLOGY

Answer THREE questions from this section.

Question 1

(a) List four safety measures observed in a mechanical workshop.

(b) Explain the following metal finishing processes:

(i) enameling;

(ii) electroplating.

(c) (i) State four properties of engineering materials.

(ii) Distinguish between non-metals and alloys, giving one example of each.

(d) Sketch part of a micrometer to show a reading of 5.54 mm.

Answer

This question covers general workshop safety, metal finishing, material properties and classification, and micrometer reading.

(a) Workshop Safety Measures (Four):

  • Wear appropriate Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) such as safety glasses, sturdy footwear, gloves when needed.
  • Maintain good housekeeping: keep floors clear, tools stored properly, clean up spills immediately.
  • Use machine guards correctly and ensure they are in place before operating machinery.
  • Operate machines only after proper training and authorization.
  • Avoid loose clothing, jewelry, or untied long hair near moving machinery.
  • Report faulty equipment or unsafe conditions immediately.
  • Know the location and use of emergency stops and first aid facilities.
  • Use tools only for their intended purpose.

(b) Metal Finishing Processes:

  • (i) Enameling (Vitreous Enamelling): (Repeated from Nov 2015 Q2b) A process where a layer of powdered glass frit is applied to a metal surface (usually steel or cast iron) and fused to it by firing at high temperatures (750-850°C). This creates a hard, smooth, durable, corrosion-resistant, and often decorative glassy coating.
  • (ii) Electroplating: (Repeated from Nov 2018 Q1a, Nov 2015 Q2b, etc.) A process using electrolysis to deposit a thin layer of one metal onto another object. The object is the cathode, an anode (often of the plating metal) is used, and both are immersed in an electrolyte solution containing ions of the plating metal while a DC current is passed. Used for corrosion protection, appearance, or improving surface properties.

(c) Material Properties and Classification:

  • (i) Properties of Engineering Materials (Any Four):
    • Mechanical Properties: Strength (Tensile, Compressive, Shear), Hardness, Toughness, Ductility, Malleability, Elasticity, Stiffness, Fatigue Resistance, Creep Resistance.
    • Physical Properties: Density, Melting Point, Thermal Conductivity, Electrical Conductivity/Resistivity, Coefficient of Thermal Expansion, Magnetic Properties.
    • Chemical Properties: Corrosion Resistance, Reactivity.
  • (ii) Non-metals vs. Alloys Distinction:
    • Non-metals: Chemical elements that generally lack metallic properties (like luster, high conductivity, malleability). They are often insulators or semiconductors, can be gases, liquids, or brittle solids at room temperature.
      Example: Carbon (in diamond/graphite form), Sulfur, Phosphorus, Silicon, Oxygen, Rubber, Plastic, Wood, Glass.
    • Alloys: A mixture composed primarily of a metal and combined with one or more other elements (which can be metals or non-metals). Alloys are created to enhance properties like strength, hardness, or corrosion resistance compared to the pure base metal.
      Example: Steel (Iron + Carbon), Brass (Copper + Zinc), Bronze (Copper + Tin), Stainless Steel (Iron + Chromium + Nickel + Carbon), Duralumin (Aluminium + Copper + Magnesium + Manganese).

    Distinction: Non-metals are elements lacking metallic characteristics. Alloys are mixtures based on a metal, designed to achieve specific properties.

(d) Micrometer Reading Sketch (5.54 mm):

  • Main Scale Reading: Thimble edge past 5 mm mark and past the 0.5 mm mark after it. Visible main scale = 5.5 mm.
  • Thimble Scale Reading: Remaining amount = $5.54 - 5.5 = 0.04 \, \text{mm}$. The thimble division aligning with the datum line is '4'.
  • Sketch: Show sleeve with 5 mm and 5.5 mm marks visible. Show thimble positioned past 5.5 mm mark, with the '4' mark on its scale aligned with the sleeve datum line.

Covers fundamental workshop safety, common finishing processes, material properties/classification, and interpreting micrometer scales.

Question 2

(a) With the aid of sketches, describe the following workshop tools and state one application of each:

(i) scriber;

(ii) centre punch.

(b) With the aid of a sketch, explain the following sheet metal joints:

(i) folded seam;

(ii) grooved seam.

(c) Name four weld defects and one cause for each defect.

Answer

This question covers marking out tools, sheet metal joints, and common welding defects.

(a) Workshop Tools Descriptions:

  • (i) Scriber:
    • Description: A hand tool with a slender body and a sharp, hardened point (or points, sometimes one straight and one bent). Used for marking out.
    • Sketch: Show a pencil-like tool with a sharp point.
    • Application: Scribing fine, accurate lines onto metal or other workpiece surfaces as guidelines for cutting, drilling, or bending.
  • (ii) Centre Punch:
    • Description: A steel punch with a knurled body for grip and a conical point, typically ground to a 90° included angle. It is struck with a hammer.
    • Sketch: Show the body, striking head, and sharp conical (90°) point.
    • Application: Creating a conical indentation in a workpiece to accurately locate the centre of a hole before drilling, preventing the drill bit from wandering.

(b) Sheet Metal Joints Explained:

  • (i) Folded Seam (Single or Double Hem/Fold): A simple seam where the edges of one or both sheets are folded over (often onto themselves - single hem, or folded twice - double hem or 'safe edge'). A simple folded seam might involve folding the edge of one sheet and slipping it over the straight edge of another, or hooking two single folds together. Often used for stiffening edges or simple joining where strength is not paramount.
  • (ii) Grooved Seam (Lock Seam): (Repeated from Nov 2016 Q4c & July 2016 Q3d) A stronger, interlocking seam used for joining flat sheets or longitudinal seams on cylinders. Edges are folded (one up, one down), hooked together, and then flattened/locked using a hand groover tool or machine.

(c) Weld Defects and Causes (Four):

Weld DefectOne Possible Cause
Porosity (Gas Pores/Pockets) Contamination (moisture, grease, rust) on base metal or filler; inadequate shielding gas coverage (MIG/TIG); damp electrodes (Stick).
Undercut (Groove melted into base metal beside the weld) Excessive current or voltage; incorrect electrode angle; excessive travel speed.
Lack of Fusion / Penetration (Weld metal doesn't fuse properly with base metal or penetrate the joint root) Insufficient heat input (low current/voltage); excessive travel speed; incorrect joint preparation/gap; wrong electrode size/type.
Cracking (Cracks in weld metal or heat-affected zone) High residual stresses (rapid cooling, restraint); hydrogen embrittlement (moisture); incorrect filler metal selection; base metal sensitivity.
Slag Inclusions (Trapped slag within the weld metal - Stick/Flux-cored) Incomplete slag removal between passes; incorrect electrode manipulation; poor joint design trapping slag.
Spatter (Small droplets of molten metal expelled from the arc) Incorrect settings (current/voltage/polarity); long arc length; poor shielding gas (MIG); damp electrodes (Stick).

Describes basic marking tools, common sheet metal joining methods, and identifies several common welding defects along with their potential causes.

Question 3

(a) Sketch the following mechanical fasteners and state their application:

(i) lock nut;

(ii) pan head screw.

(b) (i) Define the term 'soldering'.

(ii) Outline four differences between soldering and brazing.

(c) Describe the following parts of a shaping machine and state one function for each:

(i) column;

(ii) ram.

Answer

This question covers specific fasteners, soldering/brazing comparison, and shaping machine components.

(a) Mechanical Fasteners Sketch and Application:

  • (i) Lock Nut:
    • Sketch: Various types exist. Common examples:
      • Nylon Insert Lock Nut (Nyloc): A nut with a nylon ring insert at the top that deforms around the bolt thread, creating friction. Sketch a hex nut with a visible nylon collar at the top.
      • Prevailing Torque Lock Nut (e.g., Stover, Elliptical Offset): Nut body is slightly deformed (e.g., ovalized thread) to create friction with the bolt thread. Sketch a hex nut, perhaps indicating slight distortion.
      • Serrated Flange Nut: Nut with an integrated serrated washer face that digs into the mating surface. Sketch a hex nut with a wide, toothed flange at the base.
      • Castle Nut / Slotted Nut: Nut with slots cut into the top, used with a cotter pin passing through a hole in the bolt. Sketch a hex nut with slots on top.
    • Application: Used to prevent unintentional loosening of bolted joints due to vibration or changing loads. Provides resistance to rotation after initial tightening.
  • (ii) Pan Head Screw:
    • Sketch: Show a threaded screw shank with a specific head shape: a low-profile, cylindrical head with a flat top surface and slightly curved or flat sides. Often has a Phillips or slotted drive recess.
    • Application: A general-purpose screw head commonly used for fastening sheet metal, plastics, and components where a low-profile head is desired and countersinking is not practical. Provides a good bearing surface. Often used with machine threads (into tapped holes) or self-tapping threads.

(b) Soldering and Brazing:

  • (i) Soldering Definition: (Repeated from July 2015 Q3a) A process for joining metals using a filler metal (solder) that melts below 450°C and below the melting point of the base metals. Heat is applied to the joint, solder melts and flows in via capillary action, bonding the parts upon cooling.
  • (ii) Differences Between Soldering and Brazing (Four): (Repeated from Nov 2017 Q4c & Nov 2015 Q3a)
    1. Temperature: Soldering < 450°C; Brazing > 450°C.
    2. Filler Metal: Solder (Tin-based); Brazing Alloys (Copper/Silver/Aluminium-based).
    3. Strength: Brazed joints are significantly stronger.
    4. Capillary Action/Clearances: Both use it, but brazing often requires tighter clearances.
    5. Heat Source: Irons/low temp torches for soldering; Torches/furnaces for brazing.

(c) Shaping Machine Parts Description and Function:

  • (i) Column:
    • Description: The main vertical supporting structure of the shaper, typically a hollow box-like casting. It houses the drive mechanism (motor, gears, crank mechanism) and provides guideways on its front face for the cross rail (which supports the table).
    • Function: Provides the main structural rigidity for the machine, supports the table assembly, and houses the driving components.
  • (ii) Ram:
    • Description: A reciprocating arm mounted on guideways on top of the column. It carries the tool head (clapper box and tool post) at its front end.
    • Function: Provides the linear reciprocating motion (cutting stroke forward, return stroke backward) that moves the cutting tool across the workpiece. Its stroke length and position relative to the work are adjustable.

Covers specific fasteners, compares soldering and brazing, and describes the function of major components of a shaping machine.

Question 4

(a) Explain the function of the following lathe machine components:

(i) tailstock;

(ii) headstock.

(b) Explain the following lathe machine operations:

(i) turning;

(ii) drilling.

(c) List two types of grinding machines commonly used in a workshop.

(d) Figure 1 shows a bench drilling machine. Name and state the functions of the parts labelled A - E.

Answer

This question focuses on lathe components and operations, grinding machine types, and identification/function of drilling machine parts.

(a) Lathe Machine Components Functions:

  • (i) Tailstock: Located on the lathe bed ways opposite the headstock, it can slide along the bed and be locked in position.
    • Function: Primarily used to support the free end of long workpieces using a centre (live or dead centre) fitted into its barrel/quill. It can also hold tools like drill bits, reamers, or taps mounted in a drill chuck or directly in its Morse taper spindle for performing operations along the workpiece axis (drilling, reaming, tapping). The tailstock quill can be fed manually using a handwheel. Can be offset for turning slight tapers.
  • (ii) Headstock: (Repeated from Nov 2016 Q2c) Located at one end of the bed (usually left), it contains the main spindle which rotates the workpiece.
    • Function: Houses the drive mechanism (motor, gears, belts) to provide various spindle speeds. The spindle holds the workpiece securely using devices like chucks, collets, or faceplates. It provides the power and rotation for the machining operations.

(b) Lathe Machine Operations Explained:

  • (i) Turning: The fundamental lathe operation of removing material from the outside diameter of a rotating workpiece to reduce its diameter, produce cylindrical or tapered shapes, or create specific profiles. A cutting tool is fed parallel to the axis of rotation (longitudinal feed).
  • (ii) Drilling: Creating a hole along the axis of rotation of a workpiece mounted in the lathe chuck. A drill bit is held stationary in the tailstock chuck or spindle and fed axially into the rotating workpiece using the tailstock handwheel.

(c) Types of Grinding Machines (Two Common):

  • Bench Grinder: A common workshop machine with abrasive wheels mounted on a motor spindle, used for sharpening tools (chisels, drill bits), deburring, or general shaping/smoothing of metal parts held by hand against a tool rest.
  • Surface Grinder: A precision machine tool used to produce very flat surfaces with a fine finish. The workpiece is held on a reciprocating or rotating table (often magnetic), and a rotating abrasive wheel grinds the surface.
  • Cylindrical Grinder: Used to grind external or internal cylindrical surfaces and shoulders accurately. The workpiece rotates between centres or in a chuck while the rotating grinding wheel traverses along it or plunges in.
  • Pedestal Grinder: Similar to a bench grinder but mounted on a floor stand (pedestal).

(d) Bench Drilling Machine Parts (Fig. 1 Labels A-E):

Identifying parts A-E based on the diagram in Figure 1:

  • A: Chuck Key Hole/Chuck: Assuming A points to the chuck (holds the drill bit) or the key hole used to tighten it. Function: Securely holds the drill bit.
  • B: Spindle/Quill: The rotating shaft that holds the chuck and moves vertically. Function: Rotates the cutting tool and provides feed motion.
  • C: Feed Lever Handle(s): Used to manually lower and raise the spindle/quill. Function: Control the drilling feed rate.
  • D: Table: Supports the workpiece. Function: Provide a stable platform for the workpiece, often adjustable in height.
  • E: Column/Pillar: The main vertical support structure. Function: Supports the head and table, provides rigidity.

Exact identification depends on precisely where the label lines point on the actual Figure 1.

Covers the function of key lathe parts (headstock, tailstock), basic lathe operations (turning, drilling), common types of grinders, and identifying parts of a bench drill press.

Question 5

Figure 2 shows a machined block. Draw in third angle projection, showing hidden details, the following views:

(a) the plan in the direction of arrow P;

(b) front elevation in the direction of arrow F;

(c) end elevation in the direction of arrow E.

Insert six dimensions.

Answer

This question requires creating standard orthographic views (Plan, Front Elevation, End Elevation) in Third Angle Projection from the provided pictorial view (Figure 2), including hidden details and dimensions.

Understanding Third Angle Projection:

  • Object imagined in the third quadrant.
  • Plan (View P, Top View) is drawn ABOVE the Front Elevation (View F).
  • End Elevation from the Right (View E) is drawn to the RIGHT of the Front Elevation (View F).

Analysis of Figure 2 (Machined Block):

  • A block with complex features, including steps, slots, angled surfaces, and possibly holes or curves.
  • Arrow F indicates the Front Elevation view direction.
  • Arrow P indicates the Plan view direction (from above).
  • Arrow E indicates the End Elevation direction (from the Right).
  • Dimensions need to be extracted from the pictorial view.

Required Views:

  • (b) Front Elevation (View F): Shows the view looking along arrow F. Displays height and width, including profiles of steps, slots, and projections of angled surfaces. Hidden details must be shown with dashed lines.
  • (a) Plan (View P): Drawn ABOVE the Front Elevation. Shows the view looking down along arrow P. Displays width and depth, including top surface shapes, slots, holes, and edges of angled surfaces. Hidden details below the top surface shown dashed.
  • (c) End Elevation (View E): Shows the view looking along arrow E (Right End View). Drawn to the RIGHT of the Front Elevation. Displays depth and height, including end profiles of steps, slots, and angled surfaces. Hidden details shown dashed.

Procedure:

  1. Set up drawing sheet for Third Angle Projection.
  2. Draw the Front Elevation (F) accurately, transferring dimensions from the pictorial. Include all necessary hidden lines.
  3. Project lines vertically upwards from the Front Elevation for the Plan (P). Draw the Plan view above, using depth dimensions from the pictorial and aligning widths. Show top features and hidden lines.
  4. Project lines horizontally to the right from the Front Elevation for the End Elevation (E). Draw the End Elevation to the right, using depth dimensions (consistent with Plan view) and aligning heights. Show hidden lines.
  5. Add the Third Angle projection symbol.
  6. Add six major dimensions across the three views, following standard dimensioning practices.

Requires accurate orthographic drafting in Third Angle Projection, careful interpretation of the pictorial view to identify all features and hidden details, and correct application of dimensioning standards.

Question 6 (20 marks)

Figure 3 shows parts of a C-clamp. Draw an assembly of the C-clamp and include a parts list.

Answer

This question requires creating an assembly drawing of a C-clamp based on the individual part drawings provided in Figure 3, including a parts list.

Analysis of Parts (Figure 3):

  • Identify the main parts: C-shaped frame, threaded screw/spindle, swivel pad (at the end of the screw), handle (sliding T-bar or fixed).
  • Understand how the parts fit together: The screw threads into a tapped hole in the frame. The handle fits through a hole in the screw's head. The swivel pad attaches to the end of the screw, allowing it to align with the workpiece.

Assembly Drawing Procedure:

  1. Choose View(s): Decide on the most appropriate view(s) to show the assembled clamp clearly. Often a single main view (e.g., front elevation showing the clamp open or partially closed) is sufficient, possibly with a section view if internal details need clarification (though not explicitly asked for).
  2. Draw Main Component: Start by drawing the C-frame in the chosen orientation.
  3. Add Screw: Draw the threaded screw engaged in the tapped hole of the frame. Show it partially screwed in.
  4. Add Handle: Draw the handle inserted through the head of the screw.
  5. Add Swivel Pad: Draw the swivel pad attached to the end of the screw.
  6. Alignment: Ensure all parts are drawn in their correct assembled positions relative to each other.
  7. Sectioning (Optional): If internal details are complex, a section view through the screw/frame might be useful, but usually not required for a basic assembly drawing unless requested.
  8. Balloons/Item Numbers: Add balloon callouts (circles with numbers inside, connected by leader lines) pointing to each distinct part in the assembly view.
  9. Overall Dimensions (Optional): Sometimes key assembly dimensions (like maximum opening) are added, but often dimensions are left off assembly drawings, referring back to part drawings. (Instructions don't ask for dimensions here).

Parts List (Bill of Materials - BOM):

Create a table, typically placed near the bottom right or top right of the drawing sheet, containing the following columns:

Item No.Part NameMaterialQty.Remarks (Optional)
1FrameCast Iron / Malleable Iron / Steel1
2Screw / SpindleSteel1E.g., ACME Thread
3HandleSteel1E.g., Sliding T-Bar
4Swivel Pad AssemblySteel1May include Pad & Retainer

Match the 'Item No.' in the parts list to the balloon numbers on the assembly drawing.

Requires interpreting individual part drawings, visualizing how they assemble, creating a clear assembly drawing showing the parts in position, and generating an accurate, corresponding parts list (BOM).

Question 7

Figure 4 shows two incomplete views of a cylinder meeting a square pyramid at right angles. Copy the given views and complete the:

(a) elevation and the plan;

(b) line of intersection;

(c) end elevation.

Answer

This question involves determining the lines of intersection between a cylinder and a square pyramid intersecting at right angles, and completing the associated orthographic views.

Procedure:

  1. Copy Given Views: Accurately redraw the incomplete Front Elevation and Plan views provided in Figure 4, showing the relative positions and dimensions of the cylinder and pyramid. Establish the projection type used (assume First or Third Angle based on typical conventions if not stated).
  2. (b) Determine Line of Intersection: This is the core task. Since a curved surface (cylinder) intersects flat surfaces (pyramid faces), the intersection lines will be curves.
    • Method: Use Cutting Planes or Element Lines.
      • Cutting Planes Parallel to Cylinder Base: Imagine horizontal cutting planes passing through the intersection zone. Each plane cuts a circle on the cylinder and a square (or smaller square towards apex) on the pyramid. Find the intersection points of these circles and squares in the Plan view. Project these points up to the corresponding cutting plane level in the Elevation.
      • Element Lines on Cylinder: Divide the cylinder profile (Plan view circle) into equal segments (e.g., 12). Draw element lines along the cylinder surface parallel to its axis in both Plan and Elevation. Find where each element line pierces the flat faces of the pyramid (this might require finding the intersection of a line with a plane in 3D space, projectable in the views). Plot these piercing points in both views.
    • Plotting: Systematically find multiple intersection points using the chosen method. Connect these points with smooth curves in both the Elevation and Plan views. Pay close attention to visibility (which part of the curve is in front/behind or above/below other surfaces).
  3. (a) Complete Elevation and Plan: Once the intersection curves are plotted, complete the views by showing which lines are visible and which are hidden. Draw the full outlines of both solids, stopping lines where they meet the intersection curve or become hidden. Use solid lines for visible edges and dashed lines for hidden edges.
  4. (c) Complete End Elevation: (Assuming Right End View drawn to the right in Third Angle, or Left End View drawn to the right in First Angle).
    • Project heights horizontally from the completed Front Elevation.
    • Project depths from the completed Plan view (using 45-degree projection or measurement).
    • Draw the end view outlines of both the cylinder and the pyramid.
    • Project the points defining the lines of intersection from both the Front Elevation (heights) and Plan (depths) to accurately plot the intersection curves in the End Elevation. Determine visibility and draw solid/hidden lines accordingly.

Requires advanced skills in orthographic projection and descriptive geometry to accurately determine and represent the complex curves formed by the intersection of a cylinder and a pyramid.

Question 8

(a) Make free hand sketches of the following engineering hand tools:

(i) tin snips;

(ii) cold chisel;

(iii) flat screw driver;

(iv) engineers square;

(v) electric soldering iron.

(b) Figure 5 shows a point P on a circle of diameter 40 mm. Draw the locus of point P as it rotates $360^{\circ}$ along a flat surface AB without slipping.

Answer

This question involves freehand sketching of various hand tools and constructing the locus of a point on a rolling circle (a cycloid).

(a) Free Hand Sketches of Hand Tools:

Requires reasonably recognizable freehand sketches showing key features.

  • (i) Tin Snips: Show two handles connected at a pivot to two short, strong cutting blades designed for cutting sheet metal. Various patterns exist (straight cut, left cut, right cut).
  • (ii) Cold Chisel: (Repeated from July 2015 Q7a) Show striking head, shaft, and flat, wedge-shaped cutting edge.
  • (iii) Flat Screw Driver: (Repeated from Nov 2018 Q8a & others) Show handle, shaft, flat tip.
  • (iv) Engineers Square: (Repeated from July 2016 Q2a) Show stock and blade joined accurately at 90°.
  • (v) Electric Soldering Iron: Show handle, power cord, heating element housing, and replaceable soldering bit/tip at the end.

(b) Locus Construction (Cycloid):

(Repeated from July 2017 Q5a)

Construct the path of point P on the circumference of a circle (diameter 40 mm, radius R=20 mm) rolling along straight line AB for one revolution.

  1. Setup: Draw line AB. Draw the circle (R=20 mm) tangent to AB at P's starting position (P0).
  2. Rolling Distance: Mark distance = Circumference = $\pi D = \pi \times 40 \approx 125.7 \, \text{mm}$ along AB.
  3. Divide: Divide circle and baseline distance into 12 equal parts. Mark center positions C0, C1,... C12.
  4. Plot Points: For each center position Ci, locate the corresponding point Pi by considering a rotation of $i \times 30^{\circ}$ around Ci relative to P0.
  5. Draw Locus: Draw a smooth curve (cycloid) connecting P0, P1,... P12.

Part (a) tests sketching ability for a range of tools. Part (b) requires the accurate geometric construction of a cycloid curve.
