Question 1

(a) (i) Outline the procedure for extinguishing flames that have engulfed a person.

(ii) List three contents of a first aid kit.

(b) Describe the following metal finishes and decorative processes:

(i) pickling;

(ii) annealing.

(c) Explain the following metal forming processes:

(i) forging;

(ii) foundry work.

(d) Draw a vernier scale to show a reading of 10.02 cm.

Answer

This question covers first aid, metal treatment processes (finishing, heat treatment, forming), and vernier scale reading.

(a) First Aid:

  • (i) Extinguishing Flames on a Person: The primary goal is to smother the flames quickly. The procedure is often remembered by "Stop, Drop, and Roll":
    1. Stop: Tell the person to stop immediately, not to run (running fans the flames).
    2. Drop: Help the person drop to the ground quickly.
    3. Roll: Instruct or help the person to roll over and over on the ground to smother the flames. If available, cover the person (except the face/airway) with a heavy blanket, rug, or fire blanket to help extinguish the flames.
    4. Once flames are out, cool burns with cool running water and seek immediate medical attention.
  • (ii) Contents of a First Aid Kit (Any Three): Standard contents often include:
    • Sterile Dressings (various sizes)
    • Bandages (roller, triangular)
    • Adhesive Tape
    • Antiseptic Wipes or Solution
    • Sterile Eye Pads / Eye Wash
    • Disposable Gloves
    • Scissors / Tweezers
    • Safety Pins
    • First Aid Guidance Leaflet
    • (Painkillers like paracetamol or ibuprofen might be included, but policies vary).

(b) Metal Finishes and Processes:

  • (i) Pickling: A surface treatment process used to remove impurities, such as scale (oxides formed during hot working), rust, or stains, from the surface of metals, typically steel. It involves immersing the metal in an acid solution (the pickling liquor), commonly sulfuric acid or hydrochloric acid. This chemical cleaning prepares the surface for subsequent processes like coating, plating, or painting.
  • (ii) Annealing: A heat treatment process used to alter the physical and sometimes chemical properties of a material to increase its ductility, reduce its hardness, and make it more workable (easier to shape or machine). It involves heating the material to a specific temperature (above its recrystallization temperature), holding it at that temperature for a sufficient time, and then cooling it slowly (often within the furnace). This process relieves internal stresses, refines the grain structure, and improves uniformity.

(c) Metal Forming Processes:

  • (i) Forging: A manufacturing process involving the shaping of metal using localized compressive forces delivered by hammering (manual or power hammers) or pressing. The metal is often heated (hot forging) to make it more plastic and easier to shape, although cold forging is also used. Forging refines the grain structure of the metal, generally leading to improved strength and toughness compared to casting or machining. Used to make parts like crankshafts, connecting rods, hand tools, and bolts.
  • (ii) Foundry Work (Casting): A manufacturing process where molten metal is poured into a mold cavity having the desired shape. The molten metal cools and solidifies within the mold, taking its shape. The solidified part (the casting) is then removed from the mold. Common casting methods include sand casting, die casting, and investment casting. It is used to create complex shapes that might be difficult or costly to produce by other methods.

(d) Vernier Scale Reading (10.02 cm = 100.2 mm):

Requires drawing the scales to show this reading. Assume a standard metric vernier caliper with 0.02 mm least count.

  • Convert reading: $10.02 \, \text{cm} = 100.20 \, \text{mm}$.
  • Main Scale Reading: The largest whole millimeter mark visible to the left of the vernier zero will be 100 mm.
  • Vernier Scale Reading: We need to read the remaining 0.20 mm. Find the vernier scale division line that aligns perfectly with a main scale line. Since the least count is 0.02 mm, the aligning line will be $0.20 \, \text{mm} / 0.02 \, \text{mm/division} = 10$. The 10th division line on the vernier scale aligns with a main scale line.
  • Drawing: Sketch the main scale showing the 100 mm mark (e.g., '10' if marked in cm) visible just before the vernier zero. Draw the vernier scale positioned so its 10th division aligns exactly with a line on the main scale.

Covers practical first aid, metal treatment and forming definitions, and the interpretation and representation of vernier scale readings.

Question 2

(a) List three:

(i) marking out tools;

(ii) cutting tools.

(b) (i) Draw a labelled diagram of a flat file.

(ii) State four precautions in handling and storage of files.

(c) Explain the following methods of joining metals:

(i) riveting;

(ii) bolting.

Answer

This question focuses on workshop tools (marking out, cutting, filing) and common mechanical joining methods.

(a) Tool Lists:

  • (i) Marking Out Tools (Any Three):
    • Scriber
    • Engineer's Try Square
    • Centre Punch / Dot Punch
    • Dividers
    • Odd-leg Calipers (Jenny Calipers)
    • Surface Plate / Marking Table
    • Angle Plate
    • Vee Block
    • Vernier Height Gauge / Scribing Block
    • Marking Blue / Layout Dye
    • Straight Edge / Rule
  • (ii) Cutting Tools (Any Three - Hand tools assumed unless specified):
    • Hacksaw
    • Chisels (Cold Chisel, Diamond Point Chisel)
    • Files (although primarily shaping, they cut material)
    • Snips / Shears (for sheet metal)
    • Hand Reamers (for sizing holes)
    • Taps (for cutting internal threads)
    • Dies (for cutting external threads)
    • Drill Bits (used in drilling machines or hand drills)

(b) Flat File Diagram and Precautions:

  • (i) Labelled Diagram of a Flat File: (Repeated from Nov 2015 Q3c & others) Sketch should show: Tang, Heel, Body/Face, Edge, Point/Tip. Labels for Length, Cut, Grade are also relevant.
  • (ii) Precautions in Handling and Storage of Files (Four): (Similar to Nov 2015 Q3c(ii))
    1. Always Use a Handle: Never use a file without a securely fitted handle, as the sharp tang can cause serious hand injury.
    2. Store Separately: Do not store files loose together where their teeth can damage each other. Use racks, rolls, or individual sleeves.
    3. Keep Clean: Use a file card or wire brush frequently during use to remove pinned chips, which impair cutting and can scratch the work.
    4. Apply Forward Pressure Only: Files cut only on the forward stroke; lift slightly or relieve pressure on the return stroke to avoid dulling the teeth.
    5. Keep Dry: Protect files from moisture to prevent rusting.
    6. Use for Intended Purpose: Do not use a file as a hammer, pry bar, or scraper.

(c) Metal Joining Methods Explained:

  • (i) Riveting: (Repeated from Nov 2016 Q4a) A permanent mechanical joining method using rivets (metal pins with a head). Rivets are inserted into aligned holes in overlapping parts, and the tail end is deformed (upset) to form a second head, clamping the parts tightly.
  • (ii) Bolting: (Repeated from Nov 2016 Q4a) A non-permanent (demountable) mechanical joining method using bolts (headed, threaded fasteners) and nuts (internally threaded fasteners). The bolt passes through holes, and the nut is tightened onto the thread to clamp the parts.

Covers identification of common workshop tools, specifics of file usage and care, and descriptions of fundamental mechanical joining techniques.

Answer

This question explores hole machining operations (reaming, boring), lathe tool shapes, identification of grinding machine parts, and grinding safety.

(a) Drilling Machine Operations Explained:

  • (i) Reaming: An operation used to slightly enlarge a previously drilled hole to a very precise diameter and improve its surface finish and roundness. A reamer (a multi-fluted cutting tool) is rotated slowly and fed into the hole, removing only a small amount of material.
  • (ii) Boring: An operation used to enlarge an existing hole (drilled or cast) to a precise diameter, ensuring it is concentric with the axis of rotation. Unlike reaming which follows the existing hole path, boring uses a single-point cutting tool mounted on a boring bar, allowing the hole to be straightened and accurately sized, even if the initial hole was slightly off-center or irregular. Can be done on a drill press (less common), lathe, or milling machine.

(b) Illustrations of Left Hand Lathe Tools:

(Repeated from Nov 2016 Q2d). "Left Hand" cuts when fed right to left.

  • (i) Facing Tool (Left Hand): Sketch should show tool angled to cut efficiently on the end face as it moves radially.
  • (ii) Roughing Tool (Left Hand): Sketch shows robust geometry, often larger nose radius, angled for material removal along the diameter feeding leftwards.
  • (iii) Finishing Tool (Left Hand): Sketch shows geometry designed for light cuts and smooth finish, specific nose radius and cutting angles for finishing passes feeding leftwards.

(d) Safety Precautions When Using Grinding Machine:

(Similar to Nov 2016 Q4d(ii))

  • Wear Eye Protection: Always wear safety glasses or a face shield.
  • Check Wheel Condition: Inspect for cracks/damage before use (ring test if applicable). Ensure speed rating matches grinder.
  • Ensure Guards are in Place: Wheel guards and eye shields must be correctly positioned and secure.
  • Adjust Tool Rest Correctly: Keep the gap between the rest and wheel small (1-3 mm) and the rest just below the centerline.
  • Hold Workpiece Securely: Use the tool rest for support; do not grind freehand unless experienced and necessary for the task. Apply gradual pressure.
  • Cool Workpiece: Prevent overheating by cooling the workpiece frequently in water (if appropriate).
  • Stand Aside on Startup: Stand to one side, not directly in front, when switching the grinder on.
  • No Side Grinding: Do not use the side of the wheel unless it's designed for it.

This question assesses knowledge of specific machining operations, lathe tool types, and the components and safe operation of a common workshop machine, the bench grinder.

Question 4

(a) State:

(i) two properties of alluminium.

(ii) three classifications of electrical materials.

(b) (i) Explain the term 'corrosion'.

(ii) List three factors that determine the corrosion of metals.

(c) With the aid of a labelled diagram, describe the extraction of iron from the ore.

Answer

This question deals with material properties (aluminium), classification of electrical materials, corrosion, and the extraction of iron.

(a) Aluminium Properties and Electrical Material Classification:

  • (i) Properties of Aluminium (Any Two):
    • Low Density / Lightweight
    • Good Electrical Conductivity (approx. 60% of copper)
    • Good Thermal Conductivity
    • Excellent Corrosion Resistance (due to formation of a passive oxide layer)
    • High Reflectivity (of heat and light)
    • Ductile and Malleable (easily formed)
    • Non-magnetic
    • Good Strength-to-Weight Ratio (especially alloys)
    • Readily Recyclable
  • (ii) Classifications of Electrical Materials (Three): Based on their ability to conduct electricity:
    1. Conductors: Materials that allow electric current to flow easily due to the presence of free electrons. They have low electrical resistivity. Examples: Copper, Aluminium, Silver, Gold, Steel.
    2. Insulators (Dielectrics): Materials that strongly resist the flow of electric current because their electrons are tightly bound. They have high electrical resistivity. Examples: Rubber, Plastic (PVC, Polyethylene), Glass, Ceramics, Dry Air, Mica, Porcelain.
    3. Semiconductors: Materials with electrical conductivity intermediate between conductors and insulators. Their conductivity can be significantly altered by temperature, light, or the addition of impurities (doping). Examples: Silicon (Si), Germanium (Ge), Gallium Arsenide (GaAs).
    4. (Sometimes Magnetic Materials are considered a separate electrical classification due to their interaction with magnetic fields, important in transformers, motors etc. Examples: Iron, Nickel, Cobalt, Ferrites).

(b) Corrosion:

  • (i) Corrosion Explained: The gradual destruction or degradation of a material, usually a metal, by chemical or electrochemical reaction with its environment. Common examples include the rusting of iron/steel (oxidation in the presence of oxygen and water) and the tarnishing of silver. It often results in the formation of oxides, sulfides, or other compounds on the metal surface.
  • (ii) Factors Determining Corrosion Rate (Any Three):
    • Nature of the Metal: Some metals are inherently more reactive or noble than others (e.g., gold is very resistant, sodium is very reactive). Position in the electrochemical series matters.
    • Presence of Electrolyte: Corrosion, especially electrochemical corrosion, requires an electrolyte (like moisture containing dissolved salts or pollutants) to allow ion movement. Purity of water affects conductivity.
    • Presence of Oxygen (or other Oxidizing Agents): Oxygen is often required for the cathodic reaction in aqueous corrosion (like rusting).
    • Temperature: Generally, higher temperatures increase the rate of chemical reactions, including corrosion.
    • Acidity/Alkalinity (pH): The pH of the environment can significantly affect the stability of passive films and the rate of corrosion reactions.
    • Flow Rate/Velocity (for fluids): High flow rates can accelerate erosion-corrosion by removing protective layers.
    • Presence of Impurities/Alloying Elements: Impurities in the metal or specific alloying elements can form local galvanic cells, accelerating corrosion, or form protective passive layers (like chromium in stainless steel).
    • Mechanical Stress: Stressed areas can be more susceptible to certain types of corrosion (stress corrosion cracking).

(c) Extraction of Iron (Blast Furnace):

Iron is extracted from its ore (commonly Hematite - Fe$_2$O$_3$, or Magnetite - Fe$_3$O$_4$) in a blast furnace.

Process Description:

  1. Charge Input: Iron ore, Coke (carbon source and fuel), and Limestone (CaCO$_3$ - flux) are loaded into the top of the tall, shaft-like furnace in alternating layers.
  2. Hot Air Blast: Preheated air (around 1000-1200°C) is blown into the bottom of the furnace through nozzles called tuyeres.
  3. Combustion: The coke burns in the hot air, producing carbon dioxide and intense heat: $C + O_2 \rightarrow CO_2 + \text{Heat}$.
  4. CO Production: At the high temperatures in the lower furnace, carbon dioxide reacts with more hot coke to produce carbon monoxide, the main reducing agent: $CO_2 + C \rightarrow 2CO$.
  5. Reduction of Iron Ore: Carbon monoxide moves up the furnace and reduces the iron oxides in the ore to iron metal. This occurs in stages at different temperatures:
    • Upper part (lower temp): $3Fe_2O_3 + CO \rightarrow 2Fe_3O_4 + CO_2$
    • Middle part: $Fe_3O_4 + CO \rightarrow 3FeO + CO_2$
    • Lower part (higher temp): $FeO + CO \rightarrow Fe + CO_2$
    • Direct reduction by carbon also occurs at very high temperatures: $FeO + C \rightarrow Fe + CO$.
  6. Flux Action: Limestone decomposes in the heat ($CaCO_3 \rightarrow CaO + CO_2$). The calcium oxide (CaO), a basic oxide, reacts with acidic impurities in the ore (like silica, SiO$_2$) to form molten calcium silicate, known as slag: $CaO + SiO_2 \rightarrow CaSiO_3(l)$.
  7. Collection: Molten iron (called 'pig iron', containing about 4% carbon) and the lighter molten slag collect at the bottom (hearth) of the furnace in separate layers. They are periodically tapped off through separate tap holes.
  8. Waste Gases: Hot waste gases (containing N$_2$ from air, CO, CO$_2$) exit from the top of the furnace and are often used to preheat the incoming air blast.

Defines key properties of aluminium, classifies electrical materials, explains corrosion and its influencing factors, and describes the blast furnace process for iron extraction.

Question 5

(a) Figure 2 shows a circular wheel of 40 mm diameter with point P attached to its periphery. The wheel rolls without slipping along a straight line while remaining on the same plane. Plot the path of point P for a full revolution of the wheel. (10 marks)

(b) Inscribe two circles of radii 20 mm and 15 mm within a third circle of diameter 80 mm and all the three circles to touch each other. (6 marks)

(c) Construct a regular pentagon given the length of one side as 50 mm, using the compass method. (4 marks)

Answer

This question involves geometric constructions: plotting a cycloid locus, constructing tangent circles, and constructing a regular pentagon.

(a) Locus of Point P (Cycloid Construction):

The path traced by a point on the circumference of a circle as it rolls without slipping along a straight line is called a cycloid.

  1. Setup: Draw the straight line path (baseline). Draw the rolling circle (diameter 40 mm, radius R=20 mm) tangent to the baseline at the starting position of point P.
  2. Distance for One Revolution: The distance the circle's center travels along the baseline for one full revolution is equal to the circle's circumference: $C = \pi D = \pi \times 40 \approx 125.7 \, \text{mm}$. Mark this distance along the baseline from the start point.
  3. Divide Circle and Path: Divide the rolling circle into a number of equal segments (e.g., 12 segments, 30° each). Label the points on the circumference P0 (start), P1, P2,... P12=P0. Divide the baseline distance (Circumference C) into the same number of equal segments. Mark the positions of the circle's center C0, C1, C2,... C12 as it rolls along these segments. The height of the center remains constant (Radius R) above the baseline.
  4. Plot Points: For each position of the center (C1, C2, etc.), the original point P will have rotated by a corresponding angle (30°, 60°, etc.) relative to its starting position (usually bottom or top).
    • When the center is at C1 (1/12th revolution), point P has moved from the bottom to the position corresponding to 30° rotation. Locate P1 relative to C1 (radius R away from C1 at the 30° rotated position).
    • When the center is at C2 (2/12th revolution), P has rotated 60°. Locate P2 relative to C2.
    • Continue this for all 12 positions. For position Ci, locate point Pi by considering a rotation of $i \times 30^{\circ}$ around the center Ci, relative to the initial position of P.
  5. Draw Locus: Draw a smooth curve connecting the plotted points P0, P1, P2,... P12. This curve is the cycloid. It consists of a series of arches.

(b) Inscribing Tangent Circles:

This problem is ambiguous. "Inscribe" usually means inside, and "touch each other" usually implies external tangency between the inscribed circles, and internal tangency with the outer circle. Assuming this interpretation:

Given: Outer circle diameter 80 mm (Radius R = 40 mm). Inscribed circles radii r1 = 20 mm, r2 = 15 mm. Find positions where circles C1 (radius r1) and C2 (radius r2) are inside C (radius R), C1 and C2 touch each other externally, and both C1 and C2 touch C internally.

  1. Draw the outer circle C with center O and radius R=40 mm.
  2. The center of C1 (O1) must be R - r1 = 40 - 20 = 20 mm away from O. O1 lies on a circle centered at O with radius 20 mm.
  3. The center of C2 (O2) must be R - r2 = 40 - 15 = 25 mm away from O. O2 lies on a circle centered at O with radius 25 mm.
  4. Since C1 and C2 touch externally, the distance between their centers O1 and O2 must be r1 + r2 = 20 + 15 = 35 mm.
  5. To find the positions:
    • Choose a point O1 on the circle of radius 20 mm (centered at O).
    • Set compass radius to r1 + r2 = 35 mm. Place compass point at O1 and draw an arc.
    • This arc must intersect the circle of radius 25 mm (centered at O). The intersection point(s) are the possible locations for O2.
    • Geometric Check: Do these circles intersect? We have a triangle OO1O2 with sides OO1=20, OO2=25, O1O2=35. Check triangle inequality: 20+25 > 35 (45>35), 20+35 > 25 (55>25), 25+35 > 20 (60>20). Yes, a triangle can be formed, so intersection exists.
  6. Construct the intersection: Draw circle radius 20 (for O1). Draw circle radius 25 (for O2). Pick a point O1 on the radius 20 circle. Draw an arc radius 35 centered at O1. Find intersection O2 with the radius 25 circle.
  7. Draw the three circles: Outer circle (R=40), Circle C1 (center O1, radius 20), Circle C2 (center O2, radius 15). They should touch as required.

(c) Regular Pentagon Construction (Side = 50 mm):

(Repeated from Nov 2015 Q8b, but side length 50mm here)

  1. Draw Base: Draw line segment AB = 50 mm.
  2. Construct related lengths (Method 1 variation):
    • Construct perpendicular bisector of AB (midpoint M).
    • Construct perpendicular at B. Mark BP = AB = 50 mm.
    • Draw line AP.
    • Set compass to BM (25mm). Place point at P, draw arc intersecting AP at Q.
    • Set compass to AQ. Place point at A, draw arc above AB. Place point at B, same radius, draw arc intersecting first arc at D (top vertex).
  3. Locate Other Vertices:
    • Set compass to AB (50mm).
    • From A, draw arc intersecting arc from D (found above or drawn now) to find E.
    • From B, draw arc intersecting arc from D to find C.
  4. Join Vertices: Connect A-B-C-D-E-A.

Requires geometric construction skills: plotting a cycloid using divisions and rotation, solving a Apollonius-type problem for tangent circles, and constructing a regular pentagon using compass and straightedge.

Question 6

Figure 3 shows a pictorial view of a bracket. Draw full size in first angle projection the following views:

(a) Sectional front elevation A-A;

(b) An end elevation in the direction of arrow E;

(c) A sectional plan on B-B.

Include six major dimensions.

Answer

This question requires the creation of orthographic views (including sections) in First Angle Projection based on the provided pictorial view of a bracket (Figure 3). Dimensions and sectioning conventions must be applied correctly.

Understanding First Angle Projection and Sectioning:

  • First Angle: Plan below Elevation, Left End View to the Right, Right End View to the Left.
  • Sectioning: Shows internal features as if the object were cut along a specified cutting plane. Cut surfaces are hatched. Hidden lines behind the cutting plane are often omitted in the section view itself.
  • Cutting Plane Lines: Indicated on the view from which the section is taken (e.g., cutting plane A-A marked on Plan or End View to produce Sectional Elevation A-A). Arrows show the direction of viewing.

Analysis of Figure 3 (Bracket):

  • A main body with a base, possibly slotted or with holes.
  • Upright features, potentially including ribs, bosses, holes.
  • Cutting plane lines A-A and B-B indicate where the sections are taken. A-A seems to be a vertical cut through the main part for the Front Elevation. B-B seems to be a horizontal cut for the Plan view.

Required Views:

  • (a) Sectional Front Elevation A-A: Imagine the object cut along plane A-A (vertical). Draw the view looking in the direction of the arrows for A-A. Show internal features exposed by the cut. Hatch the areas where the cutting plane passes through solid material. Include visible features behind the cutting plane.
  • (b) End Elevation (View E): A standard orthographic view looking in the direction of arrow E (assume Left End View, drawn to the RIGHT of the sectional front elevation). Show all external features and necessary hidden details (dashed lines).
  • (c) Sectional Plan on B-B: Imagine the object cut horizontally along plane B-B. Remove the top portion and view the remainder from above. Draw this view BELOW the sectional front elevation. Hatch the areas where the cutting plane B-B passes through solid material. Show visible features below the cutting plane.

Procedure:

  1. Set up drawing sheet for First Angle Projection.
  2. Determine the paths of cutting planes A-A and B-B relative to the object's features from Figure 3.
  3. Draw the Sectional Front Elevation A-A. Identify solid material cut by A-A and apply hatching (e.g., 45° lines). Show visible features behind the plane.
  4. Project lines horizontally rightwards from the Sectional Elevation for the End Elevation (E). Draw the End Elevation, showing external features and hidden lines as seen from direction E.
  5. Project lines vertically downwards from the Sectional Elevation for the Sectional Plan B-B. Determine features visible below the cutting plane B-B and hatch the solid areas cut by B-B.
  6. Add the First Angle projection symbol.
  7. Add six major dimensions across the views following standard practices.

Requires accurate orthographic drafting in First Angle Projection, correct application of sectioning conventions (hatching, hidden lines), interpretation of cutting planes from a pictorial view, and standard dimensioning.

Question 7

Figure 4 shows the front elevation of a truncated cylinder. Copy the given view and draw the:

(a) plan;

(b) true shape at cutting plane X-X;

(c) surface development of the cylinder.

Answer

This question requires constructing the plan view, true shape of the cut section, and the surface development of a truncated cylinder, given its front elevation.

Procedure:

  1. Copy Elevation: Accurately redraw the given Front Elevation from Figure 4, noting the cylinder diameter, height, and the angle and position of the cutting plane X-X.
  2. (a) Construct Plan View:
    • Project the diameter downwards from the Elevation to draw the circular base in the Plan, centered below the Elevation.
    • The cutting plane X-X is oblique. The plan view of this cut will be an ellipse.
    • Divide the base circle (Plan) into 12 equal segments. Project these points (0-11) up to the base line in the Elevation.
    • Draw vertical element/generator lines from these base points up the cylinder surface in the Elevation until they intersect the cutting plane line X-X. Mark these intersection points (0'-11').
    • Project these intersection points (0'-11') vertically downwards to intersect the corresponding radial lines (0-11) in the Plan view.
    • Draw a smooth ellipse through these plotted points in the Plan.
  3. (b) Construct True Shape at Cutting Plane X-X:
    • Draw projection lines perpendicular to the cutting plane line X-X in the Elevation, extending outwards from the intersection points (0'-11').
    • Establish a datum line (e.g., parallel to X-X) for the true shape view.
    • Measure the perpendicular widths of the ellipse in the Plan view corresponding to each element line (e.g., width across points 1 and 11, 2 and 10, etc., relative to the center line).
    • Transfer these widths onto the corresponding projection lines in the true shape view, measuring perpendicularly from the datum line.
    • Draw a smooth ellipse connecting these plotted points. This is the true shape of the cut surface.
  4. (c) Construct Surface Development:
    • Draw a baseline equal to the circumference of the cylinder ($\pi \times D$).
    • Divide this baseline into the same number of equal segments (12) as the plan base circle. Label these 0, 1, 2,... 11, 0.
    • At each division point on the baseline, draw a vertical line perpendicular to it.
    • The height of each vertical line corresponds to the true length of the generator at that position on the cylinder, measured from the base up to the cutting plane X-X in the Front Elevation.
    • Transfer these heights accurately from the Front Elevation to the corresponding vertical lines (element lines) on the development layout.
    • Draw a smooth curve connecting the top points of these vertical lines.
    • The resulting shape (rectangle with one straight edge - the base, and one curved edge - the top cut) is the development of the lateral surface of the truncated cylinder.

Requires accurate application of orthographic projection, auxiliary views for true shape determination, and parallel line development techniques for cylindrical surfaces.

Question 8

Figure 5 shows two views of a bracket drawn in third angle projection. Draw full size the bracket in isometric projection taking corner X as the lowest point.

Answer

This question requires constructing an isometric view from given orthographic views (Front Elevation and Plan, Figure 5, assumed Third Angle layout: Plan Above Front Elevation), ensuring corner X is the lowest point.

Understanding Third Angle Projection:

  • Plan (Top View) is ABOVE the Front Elevation.
  • Front Elevation shows height and width.
  • Plan shows width and depth.

Analysis of Figure 5 (Bracket):

  • The object appears to be a base with an upright section, possibly including ribs, holes, or curved features.
  • Front Elevation shows the profile including height and width dimensions.
  • Plan shows the top view including width and depth dimensions, and shapes like curves or holes.
  • Corner X is identified on the views, likely a bottom-front corner.

Isometric Construction Steps:

  1. Orient Axes: Set up isometric axes (vertical, 30° left, 30° right) originating from corner X, which will be the lowest point in the drawing. Determine the directions for Width, Depth, and Height based on the Front and Plan views relative to X.
  2. Overall Dimensions: Extract overall Width, Height, and Depth from the orthographic views.
  3. Construct Base: Starting from X, draw the base footprint along the isometric Width and Depth axes according to the Plan view.
  4. Build Vertically: Use heights from the Front Elevation to construct the vertical features upwards from the base plane.
  5. Transfer Features: Map all details (curves, holes, ribs, steps) from both the Front Elevation and Plan view onto the isometric framework. Remember circles in orthographic views become ellipses on isometric planes. Draw lines parallel to the isometric axes.
  6. Connect & Refine: Complete the object outline. Darken visible edges. Erase construction lines. Omit hidden lines unless necessary for clarity.

Requires accurate interpretation of Third Angle orthographic views and careful construction of the isometric projection, paying attention to the specified lowest point (X) and correctly representing all features in 3D.
