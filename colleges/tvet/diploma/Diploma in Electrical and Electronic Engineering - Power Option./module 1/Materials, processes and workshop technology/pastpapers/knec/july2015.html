Question 1

(a) State two safety precautions to be observed when using electrical machines.

(b) Table 1, shows types of fire extinguishers. Complete the table indicating the colour of cylinder and class of fire each type extinguishes.

TypeColourClass/fire

(c) Distinguish between the following properties of engineering materials:

(i) hardness;

(ii) toughness.

(d) (i) Name two copper alloys used in engineering work.

(ii) With the aid of a labelled diagram, explain the extraction of aluminium from its ore.

Answer

This question covers workshop safety, fire safety, material properties, and metal extraction processes.

(a) Safety Precautions with Electrical Machines:

  • Ensure machine guards are in place and functioning correctly before operation.
  • Never operate machinery you are not trained or authorized to use.
  • Wear appropriate Personal Protective Equipment (PPE), such as safety glasses; avoid loose clothing, jewelry, or untied long hair that could get caught.
  • Disconnect the machine from the power supply before performing maintenance, adjustments, or cleaning.
  • Know the location and operation of the machine's emergency stop button.
  • Maintain a clean and clear work area around the machine, free from obstructions or spills.

(b) Fire Extinguisher Table Completion:

Note: Modern European standards (EN3) mandate all extinguisher bodies be Signal Red, with a large colour-coded band indicating the type (Red for Water, Black for CO2, Blue for Powder, Cream for Foam).

(c) Material Properties Distinction:

  • (i) Hardness: A material's resistance to surface indentation, scratching, or abrasion. It measures the ability to resist localized plastic deformation on the surface.
  • (ii) Toughness: A material's ability to absorb energy and deform plastically before fracturing. It combines strength and ductility and indicates resistance to fracture under impact or shock loading.

Distinction: Hardness relates to surface resistance, while toughness relates to the ability to absorb energy before breaking (often related to impact resistance).

(d) Copper Alloys and Aluminium Extraction:

  • (i) Copper Alloys (Any Two):
    • Brass (Copper + Zinc)
    • Bronze (Copper + Tin, sometimes Aluminium, Silicon, or Nickel)
    • Cupronickel (Copper + Nickel)
    • Beryllium Copper (Copper + Beryllium)
  • (ii) Extraction of Aluminium (Hall-Héroult Process):

    Aluminium is primarily extracted from its ore, Bauxite (hydrated aluminium oxide, Al$_2$O$_3 \cdot n$H$_2$O), via electrolysis.

    1. Purification (Bayer Process): Bauxite is first purified to obtain pure aluminium oxide (Alumina, Al$_2$O$_3$). Bauxite is treated with hot concentrated sodium hydroxide solution, which dissolves the alumina but not impurities like iron oxides. The solution is filtered, cooled, and seeded to precipitate pure aluminium hydroxide, which is then heated (calcined) to produce pure alumina (Al$_2$O$_3$).
    2. Electrolysis (Hall-Héroult): Pure alumina has a very high melting point (~2050°C). To lower the melting point for electrolysis, alumina is dissolved in molten Cryolite (Na$_3$AlF$_6$) at around 950-1000°C. This electrolysis occurs in a large steel cell lined with carbon (acting as the cathode) and having large carbon blocks suspended in the molten mixture (acting as anodes).
    3. Reactions: A high electric current is passed through the molten mixture.
      • At the Cathode (-): Aluminium ions gain electrons and are reduced to molten aluminium metal, which collects at the bottom of the cell. $Al^{3+} + 3e^- \rightarrow Al(l)$
      • At the Anode (+): Oxide ions lose electrons (oxidize). They react with the carbon anode to form carbon dioxide (and some carbon monoxide). $2O^{2-} \rightarrow O_2 + 4e^-$; followed by $C(s) + O_2(g) \rightarrow CO_2(g)$. The carbon anodes are gradually consumed and need periodic replacement.
    4. Collection: Molten aluminium is tapped off periodically from the bottom of the cell.

The question assesses knowledge of basic workshop safety, fire classification, material property definitions, common alloys, and the industrial process for aluminium production.

Question 2

(a) Name four measuring tools used in engineering for accurate and precision work.

(b) Draw a labelled diagram of a micrometer screw gauge and show on its scale a reading of 2.96 mm.

(c) (i) Sketch a hacksaw and show four main parts.

(ii) State four precautions taken while using the hacksaw (c(i)).

Answer

This question focuses on precision measuring tools and the use and care of a common cutting tool, the hacksaw.

(a) Accurate and Precision Measuring Tools (Any Four):

  • Micrometer Screw Gauge (External, Internal, Depth)
  • Vernier Callipers
  • Vernier Height Gauge
  • Dial Indicator (Dial Gauge)
  • Slip Gauges (Gauge Blocks)
  • Sine Bar (for precise angle measurement, used with slip gauges)
  • Profile Projector (Optical Comparator)
  • Coordinate Measuring Machine (CMM)

(b) Micrometer Screw Gauge Diagram and Reading:

Requires a labelled sketch of a standard external micrometer screw gauge and showing a specific reading.

  • Labels should include: Frame, Anvil, Spindle, Sleeve (Barrel), Thimble, Ratchet Stop, Locking Lever/Nut.
  • Showing 2.96 mm Reading:
    • Sleeve Main Scale: The main scale on the sleeve is typically marked in millimetres (upper line) and half-millimetres (lower line). To show 2.96 mm, the edge of the thimble must have passed the '2' mm mark and the next half-millimetre mark (2.5 mm). So, the visible main scale reading is 2.5 mm.
    • Thimble Scale: The thimble scale is typically divided into 50 divisions, where each division represents 0.01 mm. To get the remaining 0.46 mm (since 2.96 - 2.5 = 0.46), the thimble must be rotated so that the 46th division (marked '46') aligns with the main datum line on the sleeve.

(c) Hacksaw Sketch and Precautions:

  • (i) Hacksaw Sketch: A freehand sketch is needed.
    Four Main Parts:
    1. Frame (Usually adjustable or fixed length, C-shaped or rectangular)
    2. Handle (Pistol grip or straight)
    3. Blade (Thin metal strip with teeth)
    4. Tensioning Mechanism (Wing nut, screw, or lever to tighten the blade)
  • (ii) Hacksaw Precautions:
    • Select the correct blade (teeth per inch - TPI) for the material being cut (e.g., finer teeth for thin/hard materials, coarser teeth for thick/soft materials).
    • Ensure the blade is mounted correctly with teeth pointing forward (away from the handle) and tensioned properly (firm but not overtight).
    • Secure the workpiece firmly in a vice before starting to cut.
    • Start the cut carefully, possibly using a thumb guide or a small file notch, applying light pressure.
    • Use long, steady strokes utilizing most of the blade length, applying pressure only on the forward (cutting) stroke and relieving pressure on the return stroke.
    • Avoid excessive speed or pressure, which can overheat or break the blade. Use cutting fluid where appropriate.
    • Keep hands away from the blade during operation.

Accurate measurement relies on tools like micrometers. Safe and effective cutting with a hacksaw requires correct setup, blade selection, and usage technique.

Question 3

(a) (i) Define the term soldering.

(ii) State two functions of flux in the soldering process.

(b) Sketch the following mechanical fasteners:

(i) bolt and nut;

(ii) stud.

(c) State three:

(i) advantages of welding;

(ii) welding positions.

(d) Sketch an oxy-acetylene gas hose.

Answer

This question covers joining techniques (soldering, welding), mechanical fasteners, and welding specifics.

(a) Soldering and Flux:

  • (i) Soldering Definition: A process of joining two or more metal items by melting and flowing a filler metal (solder) into the joint. The solder has a lower melting point than the base metals being joined. The base metals are heated but not melted. Soldering is typically used for electrical connections or creating seals, using solders that melt below 450°C (soft soldering).
  • (ii) Functions of Flux (Two Functions):
    1. Cleaning: Removes oxides and other surface contaminants from the base metals, allowing the molten solder to 'wet' and bond effectively with the surfaces.
    2. Protection: Forms a protective barrier over the heated joint area, preventing re-oxidation of the surfaces during the soldering process.
    3. Improving Flow: Reduces the surface tension of the molten solder, helping it flow easily into the joint by capillary action.

(b) Sketches of Mechanical Fasteners:

Requires freehand sketches.

  • (i) Bolt and Nut: Sketch should show a bolt (threaded shaft with a head, e.g., hexagonal head) passing through clearance holes in parts to be joined, and a nut (internally threaded fastener) screwed onto the bolt's thread to clamp the parts together. A washer might also be shown under the nut or bolt head.
  • (ii) Stud: Sketch should show a headless fastener threaded at both ends. One end is screwed into a tapped hole in one component, and the other component is placed over the protruding threaded end, secured with a nut (and possibly washer).

(c) Welding Advantages and Positions:

  • (i) Advantages of Welding (Any Three):
    • Provides strong, permanent joints, often as strong as or stronger than the base metal.
    • Can create leak-proof joints required for pressure vessels or fluid containment.
    • Generally lighter than bolted or riveted joints for equivalent strength (no need for overlapping plates or fasteners).
    • Versatile process applicable to a wide range of materials, thicknesses, and complex shapes.
    • Can be automated for high production rates.
    • Allows for design flexibility.
  • (ii) Welding Positions (Any Three): Standard positions define the orientation of the workpiece and weld axis relative to the horizontal plane:
    • Flat Position (1G/1F): Welding performed from the upper side of the joint; the face of the weld is approximately horizontal. (Easiest position).
    • Horizontal Position (2G/2F): For a groove weld (G), the weld axis is horizontal. For a fillet weld (F), the weld axis is horizontal on a vertical surface or the underside of a horizontal surface.
    • Vertical Position (3G/3F): The weld axis is predominantly vertical. Welding can be performed upwards (uphill) or downwards (downhill).
    • Overhead Position (4G/4F): Welding performed from the underside of the joint. (Most difficult position).
    • (Pipe welding positions add R for rotated, e.g., 5G, 6G, 6GR).

(d) Sketch of Oxy-Acetylene Gas Hose:

Sketch should typically show a twin hose arrangement.

  • Two parallel hoses, often molded together (siamese hose) or separate but bundled.
  • One hose for Oxygen (typically green or blue, with right-hand thread fittings).
  • One hose for Acetylene (typically red or maroon, with left-hand thread fittings and possibly a grooved nut).
  • Should show the flexible hose material (reinforced rubber or synthetic).
  • May show crimped or clamped fittings at the ends for connection to regulators and the torch.

Soldering and welding are key joining processes with distinct characteristics. Mechanical fasteners like bolts and studs provide removable joints. Welding offers strong permanent joints but requires consideration of position. Understanding equipment like gas hoses is important for safety.

Question 4

(a) Name three:

(i) sheet metal tools ;

(ii) types of drilling machines.

(b) Sketch the following tools and state two applications of each:

(i) V-Block;

(ii) Engineer's square.

(c) Sketch a quick return mechanism of a shaping machine.

Answer

This question covers tools and machines used in metalwork, including sheet metal work, drilling, inspection/layout tools, and shaping machine mechanisms.

(a) Lists of Tools and Machines:

  • (i) Sheet Metal Tools (Any Three):
    • Snips / Shears (Straight, Combination, Aviation) - For cutting.
    • Mallets (Wood, Plastic, Rawhide) - For forming without marking.
    • Stakes (Various shapes: Hatchet, Bick Iron, Beakhorn) - Anvils for forming shapes.
    • Folding Bars / Bending Brake - For making straight bends.
    • Hammers (Setting, Raising, Planishing) - Specialized shaping hammers.
    • Scribers / Dividers - For marking out.
    • Hand Groovers / Seamers - For making seams.
    • Riveting Tools.
    • Slip Rolls / Bending Rolls - For forming curves and cylinders.
  • (ii) Types of Drilling Machines (Any Three):
    • Bench Drilling Machine (Drill Press) - Common workshop type.
    • Pillar Drilling Machine - Larger floor-standing version of bench drill.
    • Radial Arm Drilling Machine - Head can move along an arm for drilling large workpieces.
    • Portable Electric Hand Drill - Handheld drill.
    • Multi-spindle Drilling Machine - Has multiple spindles for simultaneous drilling.
    • Sensitive Drilling Machine - For small holes, provides good operator feel.

(b) Tool Sketches and Applications:

Requires freehand sketches.

  • (i) V-Block:
    Sketch: A block, typically metal, with a precise 90° V-shaped groove along its top surface (often one on the side too). May show clamps used with it.
    Applications (Two):
    1. Securely holding cylindrical workpieces (rods, pipes) for marking out operations (e.g., finding centers, marking lines along the length).
    2. Holding cylindrical workpieces accurately and stably for machining operations like drilling or milling on the centreline or at specific angles.
    3. Used in pairs for supporting long shafts during inspection or layout.
  • (ii) Engineer's Square (Try Square):
    Sketch: Two arms (stock and blade) joined accurately at 90°. The stock is usually thicker than the blade.
    Applications (Two):
    1. Checking surfaces for flatness and squareness (90° angles) relative to each other.
    2. Marking lines perpendicular (at 90°) to a reference edge on a workpiece during layout.
    3. Guiding tools like scribers or pencils for accurate marking.
    4. Setting up workpieces square in machines or vices.

(c) Quick Return Mechanism Sketch (Shaping Machine):

Shaping machines use a mechanism to make the forward (cutting) stroke slower and the return (idle) stroke faster, saving time. Common types include Crank and Slotted Link mechanism or Whitworth mechanism.

Sketch (Crank and Slotted Link):

  • Show a rotating crank (driven by the main gear).
  • A pin on the crank engages with a slot in an oscillating link (rocker arm).
  • The oscillating link pivots at its lower end.
  • The upper end of the oscillating link is connected via another link to the ram (which carries the cutting tool).
  • As the crank rotates uniformly, the geometry causes the oscillating link (and thus the ram) to move faster during one part of the cycle (return stroke) than the other (cutting stroke).

The question assesses familiarity with common workshop tools for sheet metal and drilling, inspection/layout tools like V-blocks and squares, and the operating principle of a key mechanism in shaping machines.

Question 5

Figure 1 shows a pictorial view of a block bearing. Draw full size the following views in first angle projection:

(a) front elevation in the direction of arrow A;

(b) end elevation in the direction of arrow B;

(c) plan in the direction of arrow C.

Insert any six major dimensions.

Answer

This question requires creating standard orthographic views (Front Elevation, End Elevation, Plan) in First Angle Projection from the provided pictorial view (Figure 1, Block Bearing). Six major dimensions must be included.

Understanding First Angle Projection:

  • Object imagined in the first quadrant.
  • Plan (View C, Top View) is drawn BELOW the Front Elevation (View A).
  • End Elevation from the Right (View B) is drawn to the LEFT of the Front Elevation (View A).

Analysis of Figure 1 (Block Bearing):

  • A rectangular base with mounting holes.
  • A semi-cylindrical bearing housing raised on the base.
  • Supporting webs or ribs connecting the bearing housing to the base.
  • Dimensions are provided on the pictorial view.

Required Views:

  • (a) Front Elevation (View A): Looking along Arrow A. Shows the length and height profile of the bearing block. The shape of the base, the profile of the bearing housing and webs. Hidden details (like the bore of the bearing, mounting holes if not fully visible) shown dashed.
  • (b) End Elevation (View B): Looking along Arrow B (Right side view). Drawn to the LEFT of the Front Elevation. Shows the width and height profile. The end shape of the base, the semi-circular bearing housing profile, thickness of webs.
  • (c) Plan (View C): Looking along Arrow C (Top view). Drawn BELOW the Front Elevation. Shows the length and width. Top shape of the base including mounting holes, top view of the bearing housing (rectangular edges and curve), top edges of the webs.

Procedure:

  1. Set up drawing sheet.
  2. Draw Front Elevation (A) accurately using given dimensions. Show hidden lines.
  3. Project lines horizontally to the left for End Elevation (B). Draw view B, using width dimensions and aligning heights.
  4. Project lines vertically down from Front Elevation (A) for Plan (C). Draw view C, using width dimensions (from B) and aligning lengths.
  5. Add the First Angle projection symbol.
  6. Add six major dimensions (overall length, width, height; bearing bore diameter; mounting hole size/position etc.).

Requires accurate orthographic drafting in First Angle Projection, interpreting the pictorial view of the block bearing, showing hidden details, and adding correct dimensioning.

Question 6

Figure 2 shows an elevation of a truncated cone. Redraw the given elevation and complete the following:

(a) plan;

(b) end elevation;

(c) true shape;

(d) surface development of the frustum.

Answer

This question involves working with the geometry of a truncated cone shown in elevation. It requires constructing auxiliary orthographic views (plan, end elevation), the true shape of the cut surface, and the surface development of the remaining frustum.

Procedure:

  1. Redraw Elevation: Accurately copy the given Front Elevation from Figure 2, noting the base diameter, height (or apex position), and the cutting plane.
  2. (a) Construct Plan View:
    • Project the base diameter downwards to draw the circular base in the Plan. Centre it below the Elevation's centre line.
    • The cutting plane is shown as a line in the Elevation. To find its shape in the Plan (an ellipse), divide the base circle (Plan) into equal segments (e.g., 12). Project these points up to the base line in the Elevation.
    • Draw generator lines from the cone's apex (or extended apex) through these base points in the Elevation. Mark where these generators intersect the cutting plane line.
    • Project these intersection points vertically down to intersect the corresponding radial lines in the Plan.
    • Draw a smooth elliptical curve through these points in the Plan.
  3. (b) Construct End Elevation: (Assuming a view from the right, drawn to the left in First Angle, or view from left drawn to right).
    • Project heights horizontally from the Elevation.
    • Project widths (diameters at various heights) from the Plan, typically using a 45° projection line or by direct measurement.
    • Draw the outline of the cone in the End Elevation (triangular profile).
    • Project the points on the cutting plane from both the Elevation (for height) and Plan (for width/depth) to plot the elliptical cut shape in the End Elevation. Draw a smooth curve.
  4. (c) Construct True Shape of Cut Surface:
    • Draw projection lines perpendicular to the cutting plane line in the Elevation.
    • Establish a datum line (X1-Y1) parallel to the cutting plane line.
    • Measure the perpendicular widths of the cut ellipse in the Plan view (relative to its centre line).
    • Transfer these widths onto the corresponding projection lines from the Elevation, measuring from the datum line X1-Y1.
    • Draw a smooth curve (ellipse) through the plotted points.
  5. (d) Construct Surface Development of Frustum:
    • Find the true slant height (L) of the original cone (from apex to base edge).
    • Draw an arc with radius L. Determine the sector angle $\alpha = (R_{base}/L) \times 360^{\circ}$. Mark out this sector and divide its arc into the same number of segments (e.g., 12) as the Plan base. Draw radial lines from the development apex (O').
    • Determine the true lengths from the apex to each point on the cutting plane intersection (these may need projection if not directly on edge generators in elevation).
    • Measure these true lengths along the corresponding radial lines in the development, starting from O'.
    • Draw a smooth curve connecting these points on the radial lines.
    • The area between the base arc (radius L) and the top curve represents the developed lateral surface of the frustum.

Requires accurate application of orthographic projection, auxiliary views for true shape, and radial line development techniques for the conical surface.

Question 7

(a) Make free hand sketches of the following hand tools:

(i) electric hand drill;

(ii) cold chisel;

(iii) flat file;

(iv) ball pein hammer;

(v) wall punch.

(b) Draw a triangle ABC where AB=60mm, AC=60mm, BC=55mm. Inscribe a circle along side BC.

(c) Draw a regular octagon in a square of side 80 mm.

Answer

This question involves freehand sketching of tools and geometric constructions: an escribed circle for a triangle and an octagon within a square.

(a) Free Hand Sketches of Hand Tools:

Requires reasonably recognizable freehand sketches of each tool.

  • (i) Electric Hand Drill: Show the body/casing, handle with trigger switch, chuck at the front (to hold drill bit), power cord (or battery pack for cordless).
  • (ii) Cold Chisel: Show the striking head (often chamfered), shaft (hexagonal or round), and the cutting edge (flat, wedge-shaped, hardened).
  • (iii) Flat File: Show tang (for handle), body (rectangular cross-section), teeth pattern, and point. (Similar to Q8a(iv) in July 2014).
  • (iv) Ball Pein Hammer: Show handle, head with flat striking face at one end and a rounded (ball) pein at the other end, wedge securing head to handle.
  • (v) Wall Punch (Likely Masonry Chisel or Star Drill):
    If Masonry Chisel: Similar to cold chisel but often broader, possibly with a hand guard.
    If Star Drill: A hardened steel tool with a cross-shaped (+ or *) cutting end, designed to be struck with a hammer to drill holes in masonry by pulverizing the material. Sketch should show the star-shaped tip and the striking head.

(b) Triangle and Escribed Circle Construction:

The phrasing "Inscribe a circle along side BC" is unusual. Standard terms are "inscribed circle" (inside, tangent to all sides) or "escribed circle" (outside, tangent to one side and extensions of the other two). Given the context, "escribed circle tangent to BC" is the most likely intent.

  1. Construct Triangle ABC:
    • Draw base BC = 55 mm.
    • Set compass radius to 60 mm. Place point at B, draw an arc.
    • Set compass radius to 60 mm. Place point at C, draw another arc intersecting the first arc at point A.
    • Join A to B and A to C. (This forms an isosceles triangle).
  2. Construct Escribed Circle Tangent to BC:
    • Extend sides AB and AC outwards beyond B and C.
    • Bisect the *external* angles at B and C. (To bisect external angle at B: place compass at B, draw arc intersecting BC and extended AB. From these points, draw arcs outside the triangle that intersect. Draw line from B through this intersection).
    • The point where the two external angle bisectors intersect is the center (Excenter, $I_a$) of the escribed circle tangent to BC.
    • Construct a perpendicular from the excenter $I_a$ to the side BC (or its extension). The length of this perpendicular is the radius ($r_a$) of the escribed circle.
    • Place compass point at $I_a$, set radius to $r_a$, and draw the circle. It should be tangent to side BC and the extensions of sides AB and AC.

(c) Regular Octagon in a Square (Side 80 mm):

  1. Draw the square ABCD with side length 80 mm.
  2. Draw the diagonals AC and BD, intersecting at the center O.
  3. Place the compass point at each corner of the square (A, B, C, D) and set the compass radius equal to half the diagonal length (AO = BO = CO = DO).
  4. Draw arcs from each corner intersecting the adjacent sides of the square. (Arc from A intersects AB and AD; arc from B intersects BA and BC, etc.).
  5. These intersection points (two on each side) are the vertices of the regular octagon.
  6. Connect these eight intersection points consecutively with straight lines to form the regular octagon inside the square.

Requires freehand sketching ability and accurate geometric construction techniques using compass and straightedge based on the properties of triangles, circles, and regular polygons.

Question 8

Figure 3 shows two views of an object drawn in third angle projection.

Draw an isometric view of the object taking corner X as the lowest point.

Answer

This question requires constructing an isometric view from given orthographic views (Front Elevation and Top Plan, assumed based on Third Angle layout), ensuring a specific corner (X) is the lowest point in the isometric drawing.

Understanding Third Angle Projection:

  • Object imagined in the third quadrant (behind and below projection planes).
  • Plan (Top View) is ABOVE the Front Elevation.
  • End Elevation from the Right is drawn to the RIGHT of the Front Elevation.
  • End Elevation from the Left is drawn to the LEFT of the Front Elevation.

Analysis of Figure 3:

  • Figure 3 shows a Front Elevation and a view above it (Plan).
  • The object appears to be a base block with features cut out or added.
  • Front Elevation shows height and width, including steps or slots.
  • Plan shows width and depth, indicating the shape from above, including any angled surfaces or cutouts.
  • Corner X is identified, likely on the front, bottom edge based on the view layout.

Isometric Construction Steps:

  1. Orient Axes: Position the isometric axes (vertical, 30° left, 30° right) such that corner X, identified from the Front Elevation and Plan, is the origin/lowest point.
  2. Overall Dimensions: Extract the overall Width, Depth, and Height from the given views.
  3. Base Construction: Start drawing from corner X, laying out the base shape according to the Plan view dimensions along the Width and Depth isometric axes.
  4. Build Vertically: Use heights from the Front Elevation to build up the features from the base plane along the vertical isometric axis.
  5. Transfer Features: Carefully transfer shapes, steps, slots, and any angled surfaces from the Front Elevation and Plan onto the corresponding isometric planes. Ensure lines are drawn parallel to the main isometric axes unless they represent non-isometric lines (like angled surface edges).
  6. Connect Points: Join the constructed points to form the complete object outline.
  7. Refine: Check all connections and proportions. Darken visible object lines. Hidden lines are usually omitted in isometric views unless essential for clarity.

Requires accurate interpretation of the Third Angle orthographic views and careful construction of the isometric projection, ensuring the correct orientation with corner X as the origin/lowest point and accurately representing all features shown in the views.
