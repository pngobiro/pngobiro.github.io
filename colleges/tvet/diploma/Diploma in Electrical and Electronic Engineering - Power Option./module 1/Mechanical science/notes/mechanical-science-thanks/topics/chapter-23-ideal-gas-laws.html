Mechanical science - Ideal gas laws

23.1 Introduction

The relationships that exist between pressure, volume and temperature in a gas are given in a set of laws called the gas laws.

23.2 Boyle's law

Boyle's law states: the volume $V$ of a fixed mass of gas is inversely proportional to its absolute pressure $p$ at constant temperature. i.e. $p \propto \frac{1}{V}$ or $p=\frac{k}{V}$ or $\boldsymbol{p}\boldsymbol{V}=\boldsymbol{k}$ at constant temperature, where $p=$ absolute pressure in Pascal's $(\mathrm{Pa})$, $V=$ volume in $\mathrm{m}^{3}$, and $k=\mathrm{a}$ constant.

Changes that occur at constant temperature are called isothermal changes. When a fixed mass of gas at constant temperature changes from pressure $p_{1}$ and volume $V_{1}$ to pressure $p_{2}$ and volume $V_{2}$ then: $$ p_{1}V_{1}=p_{2}V_{2} $$

Problem 1. A gas occupies a volume of $0.10\mathrm{~m}^{3}$ at a pressure of 1.8 MPa . Determine (a) the pressure if the volume is changed to $0.06\mathrm{~m}^{3}$ at constant temperature, and (b) the volume if the pressure is changed to 2.4 MPa at constant temperature.

(a) Since the change occurs at constant temperature (i.e. an isothermal change), Boyle's law applies, i.e. $p_{1}V_{1}=p_{2}V_{2}$, where $p_{1}=1.8\mathrm{~MPa}, V_{1}=0.10\mathrm{~m}^{3}$ and $V_{2}=0.06\mathrm{~m}^{3}$. Hence $(1.8)(0.10)=p_{2}(0.06)$ from which, $$ \text {pressure} p_{2}=\frac{1.8 \times 0.10}{0.06}=\mathbf{3}\mathbf{~ MPa} $$

(b) $p_{1}V_{1}=p_{2}V_{2}$ where $p_{1}=1.8\mathrm{~MPa}$, $V_{1}=0.10\mathrm{~m}^{3}$ and $p_{2}=2.4\mathrm{~MPa}$. Hence $\quad (1.8)(0.10)=(2.4)V_{2}$ from which $$ \begin{aligned} \text {volume}\boldsymbol{V}_{\mathbf{2}} &=\frac{1.8 \times 0.10}{2.4} \\ &=\mathbf{0.075}\mathbf{~ m}^{\mathbf{3}}\end{aligned} $$

Problem 2. In an isothermal process, a mass of gas has its volume reduced from $3200\mathrm{~mm}^{3}$ to $2000\mathrm{~mm}^{3}$. If the initial pressure of the gas is 110 kPa , determine the final pressure.

Since the process is isothermal, it takes place at constant temperature and hence Boyle's law applies, i.e. $p_{1}V_{1}=p_{2}V_{2}$, where $p_{1}=110\mathrm{~kPa}$, $V_{1}=3200\mathrm{~mm}^{3}$ and $\mathrm{V}_{2}=2000\mathrm{~mm}^{3}$. Hence $\quad (110)(3200)=p_{2}(2000)$, from which, $$ \text {final pressure}, \begin{aligned} p_{2} &=\frac{110 \times 3200}{2000} \\ &=\mathbf{176}\mathbf{~ kPa}\end{aligned} $$

Problem 3. Some gas occupies a volume of $1.5\mathrm{~m}^{3}$ in a cylinder at a pressure of 250 kPa . A piston, sliding in the cylinder, compresses the gas isothermally until the volume is $0.5\mathrm{~m}^{3}$. If the area of the piston is $300\mathrm{~cm}^{2}$, calculate the force on the piston when the gas is compressed.

An isothermal process means constant temperature and thus Boyle's law applies, i.e. $p_{1}V_{1}=p_{2}V_{2}$, where $V_{1}=1.5\mathrm{~m}^{3}, V_{2}=0.5\mathrm{~m}^{3}$ and $p_{1}=250\mathrm{~kPa}$. Hence, $\quad (250)(1.5)=p_{2}(0.5)$, from which, $$ \text {pressure}, p_{2}=\frac{250 \times 1.5}{0.5}=750\mathrm{~kPa} $$ Pressure $=\frac{\text {force}}{\text {area}}$, from which, force $=$ pressure $\times$ area. Hence, force on the piston $$ =(750 \times 10^{3}\mathrm{~Pa})(300 \times 10^{-4}\mathrm{~m}^{2})=\mathbf{22.5}\mathbf{~ kN} $$

23.3 Charles' law

Charles' law states: for a given mass of gas at constant pressure, the volume $V$ is directly proportional to its thermodynamic temperature $T$, i.e. $V \propto T$ or $V=kT$ or $\frac{\boldsymbol{V}}{\boldsymbol{T}}=\boldsymbol{k}$ at constant pressure, where $T=$ thermodynamic temperature in Kelvin (K).

A process that takes place at constant pressure is called an isobaric process. The relationship between the Celsius scale of temperature and the thermodynamic or absolute scale is given by: $$ \quad \text {kelvin}=\text {degrees Celsius}+273 $$ i.e. $\mathbf{K}=\mathbf{^{\circ}}\mathbf{C}+\mathbf{273}$ or $\mathbf{^{\circ}}\mathbf{C}=\mathbf{K}-\mathbf{273}$ (as stated in Chapter 19). If a given mass of gas at a constant pressure occupies a volume $V_{1}$ at a temperature $T_{1}$ and a volume $V_{2}$ at temperature $T_{2}$, then $$ \frac{V_{1}}{T_{1}}=\frac{V_{2}}{T_{2}} $$

Problem 4. A gas occupies a volume of 1.2 litres at $20^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$. Determine the volume it occupies at $130^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ if the pressure is kept constant.

Since the change occurs at constant pressure (i.e. an isobaric process), Charles' law applies, i.e. $\quad \frac{V_{1}}{T_{1}}=\frac{V_{2}}{T_{2}}$ where $V_{1}=1.21, T_{1}=20^{\circ}\mathrm{C}=(20+273)\mathrm{~K}=293\mathrm{~K}$ and $T_{2}=(130+273)\mathrm{~K}=403\mathrm{~K}$. Hence, $\quad \frac{1.2}{293}=\frac{V_{2}}{403}$ from which, volume at $\mathbf{130}{ }^{\circ}\mathbf{C}$, $\boldsymbol{V}_{\mathbf{2}}=\frac{(1.2)(403)}{293}=1.65$ litres

Problem 5. Gas at a temperature of $150^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ has its volume reduced by one-third in an isobaric process. Calculate the final temperature of the gas.

Since the process is isobaric it takes place at constant pressure and hence Charles' law applies, i.e. $\quad \frac{V_{1}}{T_{1}}=\frac{V_{2}}{T_{2}}$ where $T_{1}=(150+273)\mathrm{~K}=423\mathrm{~K}$ and $V_{2}=\frac{2}{3}\mathrm{V}_{1}$. Hence $$ \frac{V_{1}}{423}=\frac{\frac{2}{3}V_{1}}{T_{2}} $$ from which, final temperature, $\boldsymbol{T}_{\mathbf{2}}=\frac{2}{3}(423)=\mathbf{282}\mathbf{~ K}$ or $(282-273)^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ i.e. $\mathbf{9}{ }^{\circ}\mathbf{C}$

23.4 The pressure law

The pressure law states: the pressure p of a fixed mass of gas is directly proportional to its thermodynamic temperature $T$ at constant volume. i.e. $\quad p \propto T$ or $p=kT$ or $\frac{\boldsymbol{p}}{\boldsymbol{T}}=\boldsymbol{k}$ When a fixed mass of gas at constant volume changes from pressure $p_{1}$ and temperature $T_{1}$, to pressure $p_{2}$ and temperature $T_{2}$ then: $$ \frac{p_{1}}{T_{1}}=\frac{p_{2}}{T_{2}} $$

Problem 6. Gas initially at a temperature of $17^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ and pressure 150 kPa is heated at constant volume until its temperature is $124^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$. Determine the final pressure of the gas, assuming no loss of gas.

Since the gas is at constant volume, the pressure law applies, i.e. $\frac{p_{1}}{T_{1}}=\frac{p_{2}}{T_{2}}$ where $T_{1}=(17+273)\mathrm{~K}=290\mathrm{~K}, T_{2}=(124+273)\mathrm{~K}=397\mathrm{~K}$ and $p_{1}=150\mathrm{~kPa}$. Hence, $\quad \frac{150}{290}=\frac{p_{2}}{397}$ from which, final pressure, $$ p_{2}=\frac{(150)(397)}{290}=205.3\mathrm{~kPa} $$

23.5 Dalton's law of partial pressure

Dalton's law of partial pressure states:

the total pressure of a mixture of gases occupying a given volume is equal to the sum of the pressures of each gas, considered separately, at constant temperature.

The pressure of each constituent gas when occupying a fixed volume alone is known as the partial pressure of that gas.

An ideal gas is one that completely obeys the gas laws given in Sections 23.2 to 23.5. In practice no gas is an ideal gas, although air is very close to being one. For calculation purposes the difference between an ideal and an actual gas is very small.

Problem 7. A gas $R$ in a container exerts a pressure of 200 kPa at a temperature of $18^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$. Gas $Q$ is added to the container and the pressure increases to 320 kPa at the same temperature. Determine the pressure that gas $Q$ alone exerts at the same temperature.

Initial pressure $p_{R}=200\mathrm{~kPa}$, and the pressure of gases $R$ and $Q$ together, $p=p_{R}+p_{Q}=320\mathrm{~kPa}$. By Dalton's law of partial pressure, the pressure of gas $Q$ alone is $p_{Q}=p-p_{R}=320-200=\mathbf{120}\mathbf{~ kPa}$

23.6 Characteristic gas equation

Frequently, when a gas is undergoing some change, the pressure, temperature and volume all vary simultaneously. Provided there is no change in the mass of a gas, the above gas laws can be combined, giving $$ \frac{p_{1}V_{1}}{T_{1}}=\frac{p_{2}V_{2}}{T_{2}}=\mathbf{k}\quad\text {where}\mathrm{k}\text {is a constant.} $$

For an ideal gas, constant $k=mR$, where $m$ is the mass of the gas in kg, and $R$ is the characteristic gas constant, i.e. $\quad \frac{pV}{T}=mR$ or $\quad \boldsymbol{p}\boldsymbol{V}=\boldsymbol{m}\boldsymbol{R}\boldsymbol{T}$ This is called the characteristic gas equation. In this equation, $p=$ absolute pressure in Pascal's, $V=$ volume in $\mathrm{m}^{3}, m=$ mass in kg , $R=$ characteristic gas constant in $\mathrm{J}/(\mathrm{kg} \mathrm{~K})$, and $T=$ thermodynamic temperature in Kelvin.

Some typical values of the characteristic gas constant $R$ include: air, $287\mathrm{~J}/(\mathrm{kg} \mathrm{~K})$, hydrogen $4160\mathrm{~J}/(\mathrm{kg} \mathrm{~K})$, oxygen $260\mathrm{~J}/(\mathrm{kg} \mathrm{~K})$ and carbon dioxide $184\mathrm{~J}/(\mathrm{kg} \mathrm{~K})$.

Standard temperature and pressure (i.e. STP) refers to a temperature of $0^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$, i.e. 273 K , and normal atmospheric pressure of 101.325 kPa .

23.7 Worked problems on the characteristic gas equation

Problem 8. A gas occupies a volume of $2.0\mathrm{~m}^{3}$ when at a pressure of 100 kPa and a temperature of $120^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$. Determine the volume of the gas at $15^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ if the pressure is increased to 250 kPa .

Using the combined gas law: $$ \frac{p_{1}V_{1}}{T_{1}}=\frac{p_{2}V_{2}}{T_{2}} $$ where $V_{1}=2.0\mathrm{~m}^{3}, p_{1}=100\mathrm{~kPa}, p_{2}=250\mathrm{~kPa}$, $T_{1}=(120+273)\mathrm{~K}=393\mathrm{~K}$ and $T_{2}=(15+273)\mathrm{~K}=288\mathrm{~K}$, gives: $$ \frac{(100)(2.0)}{393}=\frac{(250)V_{2}}{288} $$ from which, volume at $\mathbf{15}^{\boldsymbol{\circ}}\mathbf{C}$, $$ V_{2}=\frac{(100)(2.0)(288)}{(393)(250)}=\mathbf{0.586}\mathbf{~ m}^{\mathbf{3}} $$

Problem 9. $20000\mathrm{~mm}^{3}$ of air initially at a pressure of 600 kPa and temperature $180^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ is expanded to a volume of $70000\mathrm{~mm}^{3}$ at a pressure of 120 kPa . Determine the final temperature of the air, assuming no losses during the process.

Using the combined gas law: $$ \frac{p_{1}V_{1}}{T_{1}}=\frac{p_{2}V_{2}}{T_{2}} $$ where $\mathrm{V}_{1}=20000\mathrm{~mm}^{3}, V_{2}=70000\mathrm{~mm}^{3}$, $p_{1}=600\mathrm{~kPa}, p_{2}=120\mathrm{~kPa}$, and $T_{1}=(180+273)\mathrm{~K}=453\mathrm{~K}$, gives: $$ \frac{(600)(20000)}{453}=\frac{(120)(70000)}{T_{2}} $$ from which, final temperature, $\boldsymbol{T}_{\mathbf{2}}=\frac{(120)(70000)(453)}{(600)(20000)}=\mathbf{317}\mathbf{~ K}$ or $\mathbf{44}{ }^{\circ}\mathbf{C}$

Problem 10. Some air at a temperature of $40^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ and pressure 4 bar occupies a volume of $0.05\mathrm{~m}^{3}$. Determine the mass of the air assuming the characteristic gas constant for air to be $287\mathrm{~J}/(\mathrm{kg} \mathrm{~K})$.

From above, $pV=mRT$, where $p=4\text { bar}=4 \times 10^{5}\mathrm{~Pa}$ (since $1\text { bar}=10^{5}\mathrm{~Pa}$ - see Chapter 21), $V=0.05\mathrm{~m}^{3}, T=(40+273)\mathrm{~K}=313\mathrm{~K}$, and $\mathrm{R}=287\mathrm{~J}/(\mathrm{kg} \mathrm{~K})$. Hence $\quad (4 \times 10^{5})(0.05)=m(287)(313)$ from which, mass of air, $\boldsymbol{m}=\frac{(4 \times 10^{5})(0.05)}{(287)(313)}=\mathbf{0.223}\mathbf{~ kg}$ or $\mathbf{223}\mathbf{~ g}$

Problem 11. A cylinder of helium has a volume of $600\mathrm{~cm}^{3}$. The cylinder contains 200 g of helium at a temperature of $25^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$. Determine the pressure of the helium if the characteristic gas constant for helium is $2080\mathrm{~J}/(\mathrm{kg} \mathrm{~K})$.

From the characteristic gas equation, $pV=mRT$, where $V=600\mathrm{~cm}^{3}=600 \times 10^{-6}\mathrm{~m}^{3}$, $m=200\mathrm{~g}=0.2\mathrm{~kg}, T=(25+273)\mathrm{~K}=298\mathrm{~K}$ and $R=2080\mathrm{~J}/(\mathrm{kg} \mathrm{~K})$. Hence $\quad (p)(600 \times 10^{-6})=(0.2)(2080)(298)$ from which, pressure, $\boldsymbol{p}\quad =\frac{(0.2)(2080)(298)}{(600 \times 10^{-6})}$ $$ \begin{aligned} &=206613333\mathrm{~Pa} \\ &=\mathbf{206.6}\mathbf{~ MPa}\end{aligned} $$

Problem 12. A spherical vessel has a diameter of 1.2 m and contains oxygen at a pressure of 2 bar and a temperature of $-20^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$. Determine the mass of oxygen in the vessel. Take the characteristic gas constant for oxygen to be $0.260\mathrm{~kJ}/(\mathrm{kg} \mathrm{~K})$.

From the characteristic gas equation, $pV=mRT$ where $\quad V=$ volume of spherical vessel $$ =\frac{4}{3}\pi r^{3}=\frac{4}{3}\pi\left(\frac{1.2}{2}\right)^{3}=0.905\mathrm{~m}^{3}, $$ $p=2\text { bar}=2 \times 10^{5}\mathrm{~Pa}$, $T=(-20+273)\mathrm{~K}=253\mathrm{~K}$ and $\quad R=0.260\mathrm{~kJ}/(\mathrm{kg} \mathrm{~K})=260\mathrm{~J}/(\mathrm{kg} \mathrm{~K})$. Hence $\quad (2 \times 10^{5})(0.905)=m(260)(253)$ from which, mass of oxygen, $$ \begin{aligned} \boldsymbol{m} &=\frac{(2 \times 10^{5})(0.905)}{(260)(253)} \\ &=\mathbf{2.75}\mathbf{~ kg}\end{aligned} $$

Problem 13. Determine the characteristic gas constant of a gas which has a specific volume of $0.5\mathrm{~m}^{3}/ \mathrm{kg}$ at a temperature of $20^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ and pressure 150 kPa .

From the characteristic gas equation, $pV=mRT$ from which, $$ R=\frac{pV}{mT} $$ where $$ \begin{aligned} p &=150 \times 10^{3}\mathrm{~Pa}, \\ T &=(20+273)\mathrm{~K} \\ &=293\mathrm{~K}\end{aligned} $$ and specific volume, $\quad V/m=0.5\mathrm{~m}^{3}/ \mathrm{kg}$. Hence the characteristic gas constant, $$ \begin{aligned} R=\left(\frac{p}{T}\right)\left(\frac{V}{m}\right) &=\left(\frac{150 \times 10^{3}}{293}\right) \\ &=\mathbf{256}\mathbf{~ J}/(\mathbf{kgK})\end{aligned} $$

23.8 Further worked problems on the characteristic gas equation

Problem 14. A vessel has a volume of $0.80\mathrm{~m}^{3}$ and contains a mixture of helium and hydrogen at a pressure of 450 kPa and a temperature of $17^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$. If the mass of helium present is 0.40 kg determine (a) the partial pressure of each gas, and (b) the mass of hydrogen present. Assume the characteristic gas constant for helium to be $2080\mathrm{~J}/(\mathrm{kg} \mathrm{~K})$ and for hydrogen $4160\mathrm{~J}/(\mathrm{kg} \mathrm{~K})$.

(a) $\quad V=0.80\mathrm{~m}^{3}, p=450\mathrm{~kPa}$, $T=(17+273)\mathrm{~K}=290\mathrm{~K}, m_{\mathrm{He}}=0.40\mathrm{~kg}$, $R_{\mathrm{He}}=2080\mathrm{~J}/(\mathrm{kg} \mathrm{~K})$. If $p_{\mathrm{He}}$ is the partial pressure of the helium, then using the characteristic gas equation, $p_{\mathrm{He}}V=m_{\mathrm{He}}R_{\mathrm{He}}T$ gives: $$ (p_{\mathrm{He}})(0.80)=(0.40)(2080)(290) $$ from which, the partial pressure of the helium, $$ \begin{aligned} \boldsymbol{p}_{\mathbf{H}\mathbf{e}} &=\frac{(0.40)(2080)(290)}{(0.80)} \\ &=\mathbf{301.6}\mathbf{~ kPa}\end{aligned} $$ By Dalton's law of partial pressure the total pressure $p$ is given by the sum of the partial pressures, i.e. $p=p_{\mathrm{H}}+p_{\mathrm{He}}$, from which, the partial pressure of the hydrogen, $$ \begin{aligned} \boldsymbol{p}_{\mathbf{H}}=p-p_{\mathrm{He}} &=450-301.6 \\ &=\mathbf{148.4}\mathbf{~ kPa}\end{aligned} $$

(b) From the characteristic gas equation, $\begin{aligned} p_{\mathrm{H}}V &=m_{\mathrm{H}}R_{\mathrm{H}}T. \\ \text {Hence}\quad (148.4 \times 10^{3})(0.8) &=m_{\mathrm{H}}(4160)(290)\end{aligned}$ from which, mass of hydrogen, $$ \begin{aligned} \boldsymbol{m}_{\mathbf{H}} &=\frac{(148.4 \times 10^{3})(0.8)}{(4160)(290)} \\ &=\mathbf{0.098}\mathbf{~ kg}\text {or}\mathbf{98}\mathbf{~ g}\end{aligned} $$

Problem 15. A compressed air cylinder has a volume of $1.2\mathrm{~m}^{3}$ and contains air at a pressure of 1 MPa and a temperature of $25^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$. Air is released from the cylinder until the pressure falls to 300 kPa and the temperature is $15^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$. Determine (a) the mass of air released from the container, and (b) the volume it would occupy at STP. Assume the characteristic gas constant for air to be $287\mathrm{~J}/(\mathrm{kg} \mathrm{~K})$.

$$ \begin{aligned} V_{1} &=1.2\mathrm{~m}^{3}\left(=V_{2}\right), p_{1}=1\mathrm{~MPa}=10^{6}\mathrm{~Pa}, \\ T_{1} &=(25+273)\mathrm{~K}=298\mathrm{~K}, \\ T_{2} &=(15+273)\mathrm{~K}=288\mathrm{~K}, \\ p_{2} &=300\mathrm{~kPa}=300 \times 10^{3}\mathrm{~Pa} \\ R &=287\mathrm{~J}/(\mathrm{kg} \mathrm{~K})\end{aligned} $$

(a) Using the characteristic gas equation, $p_{1}V_{1}=m_{1}RT_{1}$, to find the initial mass of air in the cylinder gives: $$ (10^{6})(1.2)=m_{1}(287)(298) $$ from which, mass $m_{1}=\frac{(10^{6})(1.2)}{(287)(298)}$ $$=14.03\mathrm{~kg}$$ Similarly, using $p_{2}V_{2}=m_{2}RT_{2}$ to find the final mass of air in the cylinder gives: $$ (300 \times 10^{3})(1.2)=m_{2}(287)(288) $$ from which, mass $m_{2}=\frac{(300 \times 10^{3})(1.2)}{(287)(288)}$ $$=4.36\mathrm{~kg}$$ Mass of air released from cylinder $$ =m_{1}-m_{2}=14.03-4.36=\mathbf{9.67}\mathbf{~ kg} $$

(b) At STP, $T=273\mathrm{~K}$ and $p=101.325\mathrm{~kPa}$. Using the characteristic gas equation $$ \begin{aligned} pV &=mRT \\ \text {volume}, V &=\frac{mRT}{p} \\ &=\frac{(9.67)(287)(273)}{101325} \\ &=\mathbf{7.48}\mathbf{~ m}^{\mathbf{3}}\end{aligned} $$

Problem 16. A vessel $X$ contains gas at a pressure of 750 kPa at a temperature of $27^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$. It is connected via a valve to vessel $Y$ that is filled with a similar gas at a pressure of 1.2 MPa and a temperature of $27^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$. The volume of vessel $X$ is $2.0\mathrm{~m}^{3}$ and that of vessel $Y$ is $3.0\mathrm{~m}^{3}$. Determine the final pressure at $27^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ when the valve is opened and the gases are allowed to mix. Assume $R$ for the gas to be $300\mathrm{~J}/(\mathrm{kg} \mathrm{~K})$.

For vessel $X$ :

$$ \begin{aligned} p_{X} &=750 \times 10^{3}\mathrm{~Pa}, T_{X}=(27+273)\mathrm{~K}=300\mathrm{~K}, \\ V_{X} &=2.0\mathrm{~m}^{3}\text {and} R=300\mathrm{~J}/(\mathrm{kg} \mathrm{~K})\end{aligned} $$ From the characteristic gas equation, $$ p_{X}V_{X}=m_{X}RT_{X} $$ Hence $\quad (750 \times 10^{3})(2.0)=m_{X}(300)(300)$ from which, mass of gas in vessel $X$, $$ m_{X}=\frac{(750 \times 10^{3})(2.0)}{(300)(300)}=16.67\mathrm{~kg} $$

For vessel $Y$ :

$$ \begin{aligned} p_{Y} &=1.2 \times 10^{6}\mathrm{~Pa}, T_{Y}=(27+273)\mathrm{~K}=300\mathrm{~K}, \\ V_{Y} &=3.0\mathrm{~m}^{3}\text {and} R=300\mathrm{~J}/(\mathrm{kg} \mathrm{~K})\end{aligned} $$ From the characteristic gas equation, Hence $\quad (1.2 \times 10^{6})(3.0)=m_{Y}(300)(300)$ from which, mass of gas in vessel $Y$, $$ m_{Y}=\frac{(1.2 \times 10^{6})(3.0)}{(300)(300)}=40\mathrm{~kg} $$ When the valve is opened, mass of mixture, $$ \begin{aligned} m &=m_{X}+m_{Y} \\ &=16.67+40=56.67\mathrm{~kg}.\end{aligned} $$ Total volume, $V=V_{X}+V_{Y}=2.0+3.0=5.0\mathrm{~m}^{3}$, $R=300\mathrm{~J}/(\mathrm{kg} \mathrm{~K}), T=300\mathrm{~K}$. From the characteristic gas equation, $$ \begin{aligned} pV &=mRT \\ p(5.0) &=(56.67)(300)(300)\end{aligned} $$ from which, final pressure, $$ p=\frac{(56.67)(300)(300)}{5.0}=1.02\mathrm{~MPa} $$
