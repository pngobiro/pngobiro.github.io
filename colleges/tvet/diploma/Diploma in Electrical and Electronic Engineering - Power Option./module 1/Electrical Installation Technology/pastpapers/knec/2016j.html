Question 1

(a) Explain the following cable jointing methods:

(i) pot and ladle;

(ii) clamping.

(b) State three IEE regulations requirement regarding joints and terminations.

(c) Outline the procedure for carrying out polarity test with circuit alive on a single phase installation.

(d) State three:

(i) factors that affect the choice of a wiring system;

(ii) advantages of trunking system over conduit system.

Answer

This answer explains cable jointing methods, outlines IEE requirements for joints, describes the live polarity test procedure, and lists factors for choosing wiring systems along with advantages of trunking.

(a) Cable Jointing Methods

(i) Pot and Ladle (Soldering): This method involves joining conductors (usually copper) by soldering them together to ensure a strong, low-resistance electrical connection.

  • Procedure: Conductors are prepared (stripped, cleaned, often twisted like a married joint), flux is applied, and molten solder (typically lead-tin alloy) is poured over the joint using a ladle, heated in a melting pot. Alternatively, a heavy-duty soldering iron/torch is used to heat the joint and melt solder into it.
  • Application: Historically used for power cable joints, especially larger ones, and still used in some specific applications requiring high reliability and current capacity. Less common now due to the advent of reliable mechanical connectors and health/safety concerns with molten lead.

(ii) Clamping (Mechanical Connectors): This method uses mechanical force to join conductors together within a conductive fitting.

  • Procedure: Conductors are stripped and inserted into a connector block or sleeve (e.g., screw terminals, compression lugs, crimp connectors). Screws are tightened, or a crimping tool applies pressure to deform the connector barrel around the conductor, creating a secure mechanical and electrical connection.
  • Application: Widely used for terminating cables into equipment (switches, sockets, MCBs), joining cables in junction boxes (terminal blocks), and for connecting larger cables using lugs or specialised connectors (e.g., line taps). It's the most common method in modern installations due to convenience, speed, and elimination of heat/fumes.

(b) IEE Regulations Requirements Regarding Joints and Terminations (BS 7671 Principles)

  1. Accessibility and Enclosure: Every joint and termination must, except for specific exemptions (like buried joints, soldered joints under specific conditions), be enclosed in suitable accessories (e.g., junction boxes, equipment enclosures) and remain accessible for inspection, testing, and maintenance throughout the lifetime of the installation. (Ref: Regulation 526.3, 526.5).
  2. Mechanical Strength and Electrical Continuity: Joints and terminations must provide durable electrical continuity and adequate mechanical strength and protection. They must withstand the thermal and mechanical stresses expected in service without deterioration. (Ref: Regulation 526.1, 526.2).
  3. Prevention of Damage: Connections must be made such that conductors are not damaged (e.g., nicked strands during stripping, excessive force during clamping). Appropriate tools and methods must be used. The method of connection must be suitable for the conductor material and number/shape of wires. (Ref: Regulation 526.2).

(c) Procedure for Live Polarity Test (Single Phase Installation)

WARNING: Live testing carries significant risks and should only be performed by competent persons using appropriate safety procedures and equipment (GS38 compliant test leads, PPE). Dead testing is always preferred. This test verifies that single-pole devices are in the phase conductor.

Instrument: Approved Voltage Indicator (AVI) or Test Lamp conforming to relevant safety standards (e.g., HSE GS38).

Procedure (Example: Testing a lighting switch):

  1. Safety: Ensure AVI is working correctly (check on known live source before and after test). Wear appropriate PPE.
  2. Access Terminals: Carefully expose the terminals of the device being tested (e.g., remove switch plate).
  3. Test Between Line and Earth: Connect one probe of the AVI to the incoming Line terminal feeding the switch and the other probe to a known good Earth point (e.g., the circuit protective conductor terminal). The AVI should indicate full mains voltage.
  4. Test Between Switched Line and Earth (Switch ON): With the switch in the ON position, connect one probe to the outgoing Switched Line terminal and the other probe to Earth. The AVI should indicate full mains voltage.
  5. Test Between Switched Line and Earth (Switch OFF): Turn the switch to the OFF position. Connect one probe to the outgoing Switched Line terminal and the other probe to Earth. The AVI should *NOT* indicate voltage (or indicate a very low residual voltage).
  6. Test Between Neutral and Earth: Connect one probe to the Neutral terminal (if present at the switch, e.g., for smart switches) and the other to Earth. The AVI should indicate zero or near-zero voltage.

Verification: If voltage is present on the switched line terminal only when the switch is ON and absent when OFF, and the neutral (if present) shows no significant voltage to earth, polarity is likely correct for that device. Similar tests are performed at sockets (L-E, N-E) and lampholders (Centre contact-E, Outer contact-E).

(d) Wiring Systems: Choice Factors and Trunking Advantages

(i) Factors Affecting Choice of Wiring System:

  • Type of Building/Environment: Domestic, industrial, hazardous area, damp conditions, presence of corrosive substances, risk of mechanical damage.
  • Cost and Budget: Material and labour costs vary significantly between systems (e.g., PVC surface vs. steel conduit).
  • Appearance/Aesthetics: Concealed systems (conduit) vs. surface systems (trunking, surface conduit, clipping).
  • Flexibility and Future Modifications: Ease of adding or altering circuits (trunking is generally easier than concealed conduit).
  • Durability and Lifespan Required: Expected lifetime and resistance to environmental factors.
  • Regulations and Standards: Compliance with BS 7671 and specific site requirements.

(ii) Advantages of Trunking System over Conduit System:

  • Ease of Access and Modification: Removable lids allow easy access to lay additional cables or modify existing circuits without needing to draw cables through long, potentially complex conduit runs.
  • Capacity for Multiple Circuits: Trunking can generally accommodate a larger number of cables and circuits within a single run compared to standard conduit sizes, simplifying complex distribution paths.
  • Reduced Installation Time (Potentially): For routes requiring numerous cables, installing one run of trunking can be quicker than installing multiple parallel conduit runs, especially considering bending and joining time for conduit.
  • Less Cable Stress: Cables are laid into trunking rather than pulled, reducing the risk of stress or damage to cable insulation during installation compared to pulling through conduit bends.

Proper jointing is essential for electrical integrity. IEE regulations ensure safety and reliability of connections. Polarity testing verifies correct switching, while the choice of wiring system depends on balancing factors like cost, protection, and aesthetics, with trunking offering advantages in accessibility and capacity over conduit in many situations.

Question 2

(a) (i) State two reasons of earthing an electrical installation.

(ii) Define the following in relation to earthing and protection:

(I) earth lead;

(II) cartridge fuse;

(III) circuit protective conductor.

(b) With aid of a labelled diagram, explain the following methods of earthing an electrical installation:

(i) direct earthing;

(ii) protective multiple earthing.

(c) (i) State three IEE regulations requirement regarding bell-transformers.

(ii) With aid of a circuit diagram, explain the working principle of a "closed circuit" burglar alarm having one sensing point.

Answer

This answer explains the reasons for earthing, defines related terms, describes direct (TT) and PME (TNC-S) earthing systems, lists requirements for bell transformers, and explains a closed-circuit burglar alarm.

(a) Reasons for Earthing and Definitions

(i) Two Reasons for Earthing:

  • Protection Against Electric Shock (Safety): To provide a path for fault current to flow safely to earth if a live conductor accidentally touches exposed metalwork (e.g., appliance casing). This large fault current should operate a protective device (fuse, MCB, RCD) quickly, disconnecting the supply and preventing the metalwork from remaining at a dangerous voltage relative to earth.
  • Voltage Stabilization and Reference: To anchor the electrical system's potential close to the earth's potential, preventing excessive voltages due to lightning, system faults, or static discharge, and providing a stable reference for correct operation of equipment.

(ii) Definitions:

  • (I) Earth Lead (Earthing Conductor): The conductor connecting the installation's Main Earthing Terminal (MET) to the means of earthing (e.g., the earth electrode in a TT system, or the supplier's earth terminal in TN systems).
  • (II) Cartridge Fuse: A type of fuse where the fuse element is enclosed within a sealed insulating tube (cartridge), usually made of ceramic or glass, often filled with arc-quenching material like sand. Provides more reliable and safer operation, especially at high fault currents, compared to rewireable fuses.
  • (III) Circuit Protective Conductor (CPC): A conductor used for protection against electric shock, connecting exposed-conductive-parts of equipment (e.g., metal casings) back to the Main Earthing Terminal (MET). In T&E cable, this is the bare conductor.

(b) Methods of Earthing

(i) Direct Earthing (TT System):

In this system, the consumer provides their *own* independent connection to the general mass of earth via an earth electrode (rod, plate, etc.). The supplier earths their transformer neutral separately at the substation. There is no earth connection provided by the supplier to the consumer.

(ii) Protective Multiple Earthing (PME / TNC-S System):

The supplier combines the neutral and protective functions in a single conductor (PEN - Protective Earth Neutral) in their distribution network. This PEN conductor is earthed at multiple points along its route. At the consumer's intake, the supplier provides an earthing terminal connected to this incoming PEN conductor. Inside the installation, the Neutral and Earth are kept separate (Separate).

(c) Bell Transformers and Closed Circuit Alarm

(i) IEE Regulations Requirements Regarding Bell Transformers (SELV/PELV Principles):

Bell transformers typically provide Separated Extra Low Voltage (SELV) or Protective Extra Low Voltage (PELV) for safety.

  • Separation: The transformer must provide at least simple separation (and often reinforced insulation for SELV) between the primary (mains voltage) winding and the secondary (extra-low voltage) winding. (Ref: Regulation Group 414).
  • Voltage Limits: The secondary output voltage must not exceed the AC or DC limits for ELV (typically 50V AC or 120V DC ripple-free). Bell circuits usually operate well below this (e.g., 6V, 8V, 12V AC).
  • Source Identification: The transformer should be clearly marked with its input/output voltages and power rating.
  • Protection (Primary Side): The primary side of the transformer must be protected against overcurrent by a suitable fuse or circuit breaker in the mains circuit. (Ref: Regulation 530.3).

(ii) Closed Circuit Burglar Alarm (One Sensor):

Earthing provides essential protection in electrical systems, with different methods like TT and TNC-S used globally. SELV/PELV circuits, like those for bells powered by transformers, enhance safety. Closed-circuit alarms offer basic tamper detection compared to open-circuit designs.

Question 3

(a) Explain the following with reference to safety:

(i) electric shock;

(ii) protective clothing.

(b) Describe the Holger Nelson Method carried out on an electric shock victim.

(c) (i) Explain how each of the following can cause accidents:

I. using defective tools;

II. improvising tools.

(ii) List three types of tools and their application in the field of electrical and electronics.

Answer

This answer defines electric shock and protective clothing in a safety context, describes the (now largely historical) Holger Nielsen resuscitation method, explains accident causes related to tool misuse, and lists common electrical/electronic tools.

(a) Safety Definitions

(i) Electric Shock: A physiological reaction or injury caused by electric current passing through the human body. It occurs when a person becomes part of an electrical circuit. The effects can range from a mild tingling sensation to severe muscle contractions, respiratory arrest, cardiac fibrillation, burns, and death, depending on the current magnitude, path, duration, and voltage.

(ii) Protective Clothing (in electrical context): Specialized clothing and Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) designed to protect workers from electrical hazards. This can include:

  • Insulating Gloves/Footwear: Made from rubber or other insulating materials to prevent current flow through the hands and feet. Rated for specific voltage levels.
  • Arc Flash Clothing: Fire-resistant (FR) clothing designed to withstand the intense heat and pressure generated during an electrical arc flash event. Rated in calories per square centimeter (cal/cm²).
  • Eye Protection: Safety glasses, goggles, or face shields to protect against flying debris, chemical splashes, or arc flash radiation.
  • Head Protection: Hard hats, sometimes with specific electrical ratings.
The purpose is to minimize the risk and severity of injury from electric shock, arc flash burns, and other related hazards.

(b) Holger Nielsen Method (Artificial Respiration)

Note: The Holger Nielsen method is a historical manual artificial respiration technique largely superseded by modern CPR (Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation) which includes chest compressions and rescue breaths. While historically significant, it is **not** the recommended first aid procedure today for someone who isn't breathing.

The method involved positioning the victim prone (face down) and manually compressing their back to force air out, then lifting their arms to expand the chest and draw air in.

Procedure Description (Historical):

  1. Positioning: Place the victim face down, head turned to one side, arms bent with hands placed under the head. Ensure the airway is clear.
  2. Expiration Phase (Pressure): Kneel at the victim's head. Place your hands flat on the victim's back over the lower part of the shoulder blades. Rock forward, keeping elbows straight, applying steady, moderate pressure downwards to compress the chest and force air out. (Count "One, Two").
  3. Inspiration Phase (Release and Lift): Rock backward, sliding your hands down to grasp the victim's arms just above the elbows. Lift and pull the arms upward and towards you, expanding the victim's chest to encourage air intake. (Count "Three").
  4. Return: Lower the victim's arms back to the starting position. (Count "Four").
  5. Repeat Cycle: Repeat the cycle rhythmically (approximately 12-15 times per minute) until the victim recovers spontaneous breathing or qualified help arrives.

Modern CPR is significantly more effective as it circulates oxygenated blood via chest compressions, which manual respiration methods alone do not achieve effectively.

(c) Tool Misuse and Examples

(i) How Tool Misuse Causes Accidents:

  • I. Using Defective Tools: Tools with defects like cracked handles (screwdriver, hammer), mushroomed heads (chisels, punches), worn insulation (pliers, screwdrivers), dull cutting edges (knives, side cutters), or damaged mechanisms can lead to accidents. Examples: A screwdriver tip slipping due to wear can cause hand injuries. A cracked hammer handle can break, causing the head to fly off. Worn insulation can lead to electric shock.
  • II. Improvising Tools: Using a tool for a purpose it was not designed for often leads to accidents because the tool cannot perform the task safely or effectively. Examples: Using pliers as a hammer can damage the pliers and cause slips. Using a screwdriver as a pry bar or chisel can break the tip or handle, causing injury. Using a knife instead of wire strippers can damage the conductor or insulation, or lead to cuts.

(ii) Three Types of Tools and Applications (Electrical/Electronics):

  1. Tool Type: Multimeter (Digital or Analog)
    • Application: Measuring various electrical quantities such as AC/DC Voltage, AC/DC Current, Resistance, and often Continuity, Capacitance, Frequency, Diode testing. Essential for troubleshooting, testing, and verifying circuit parameters.
  2. Tool Type: Wire Strippers
    • Application: Removing insulation from the ends of electrical wires cleanly and without damaging the underlying conductor strands. Various types exist for different wire gauges and insulation types.
  3. Tool Type: Soldering Iron
    • Application: Used to melt solder to create permanent electrical connections between components on printed circuit boards (PCBs), join wires, or terminate wires to connectors. Requires solder, flux, and often a stand and cleaning sponge.
  4. Tool Type: Combination Pliers
    • Application: Versatile tool for gripping flat or round objects, bending wire, cutting soft wire, and sometimes crimping.
  5. Tool Type: Side Cutters (Diagonal Cutters)
    • Application: Cutting wires, component leads, and small plastic parts cleanly.

Understanding electrical hazards, using appropriate protective clothing, and applying correct first aid (like modern CPR) are vital for safety. Proper tool selection, maintenance, and usage are crucial to prevent workshop accidents.

(ii) Three IEE Regulations Requirements Regarding Final Circuits (BS 7671 Principles):

  1. Overcurrent Protection: Every final circuit must be protected against overload current and fault current (short circuit) by an appropriate protective device (fuse or circuit breaker) located at the origin of the circuit (typically in the consumer unit). Kenya utilizes diverse energy sources feeding a complex grid system. The consumer intake provides the interface and primary control/protection for an installation. Question 5

(a) (i) State the quantity measured by the following instruments:

(I) Ohmeter;

(II) Wattmeter.

(ii) With aid of circuit diagrams, show the two ways an ammeter and voltmeter are connected to measure power of a circuit feeding a single phase load.

(b) Write in full the meaning of the following abbreviation of different cables:

(i) PVC SWA;

(ii) MIMs;

(iii) PILCSWA.

(c) (i) Explain how the following factors affect cable rating:

(I) ambient temperature;

(II) type of protective device.

(ii) A 10.5 kW cooker is connected to 250 V supply. The ambient temperature correction factor is 0.89 . If the protective device used is a re-wirable fuse with a correction factor of 0.725 , determine the current rating of the cable to be used.

Answer

This answer identifies quantities measured by specific instruments, illustrates power measurement using voltmeter-ammeter methods, explains cable abbreviations, discusses cable rating factors, and calculates the required cable rating for a cooker circuit.

(a) Measuring Instruments and Power Measurement

(i) Quantities Measured:

  • (I) Ohmmeter: Measures Electrical Resistance (Unit: Ohms, Ω).
  • (II) Wattmeter: Measures Electrical Power (Unit: Watts, W).

(ii) Power Measurement using Ammeter and Voltmeter:

Power (P) in a DC circuit or a purely resistive AC circuit is P = V * I. This can be measured using separate voltmeter and ammeter connections. There are two common connection methods, differing in whether the voltmeter measures the voltage across the load only or across the load plus the ammeter.

(b) Cable Abbreviations

  • (i) PVC SWA: Polyvinyl Chloride / Steel Wire Armoured. A cable with PVC insulation around the conductors, PVC bedding over the assembled cores, Steel Wire Armour for mechanical protection, and an overall PVC sheath.
  • (ii) MIMS: Mineral Insulated Metal Sheathed (often MICC - Mineral Insulated Copper Clad). Conductors are embedded in highly compressed magnesium oxide powder (mineral insulation) inside a solid metal sheath (usually copper).
  • (iii) PILCSWA: Paper Insulated / Lead Covered / Steel Wire Armoured. A cable with oil-impregnated paper insulation around conductors, covered by a lead sheath for moisture protection, followed by bedding, Steel Wire Armour, and an outer serving.

(c) Cable Rating Factors and Calculation

(i) Factors Affecting Cable Rating:

  • (I) Ambient Temperature (Ca): Higher ambient temperatures reduce the cable's ability to dissipate heat, thus lowering its safe current carrying capacity. A correction factor less than 1 is applied if the ambient temperature is higher than the standard reference temperature for the cable type/installation method.
  • (II) Type of Protective Device (Correction Factor - Cf): Specifically relevant for semi-enclosed rewireable fuses (BS 3036). These fuses operate slower under overload conditions than cartridge fuses or MCBs. To ensure the cable isn't damaged before the fuse blows, the required *tabulated* current rating of the cable (It) must be higher than the design current (Ib) divided by the fuse correction factor (typically 0.725). For MCBs and other fuse types (BS 88, BS 1361), this factor is effectively 1.0. So, It ≥ Ib / Cf.

(ii) Cable Current Rating Calculation:

  1. Calculate Design Current (Ib): Power (P) = 10.5 kW = 10500 W Voltage (V) = 250 V Design Current (Ib) = P / V = 10500 W / 250 V = 42 A (Note: Cooker diversity is often applied, but not mentioned here, so we use full load current.)
  2. Apply Correction Factors: Ambient Temperature Factor (Ca) = 0.89 Protective Device Factor (Cf for rewireable fuse) = 0.725 (Assume no other factors like grouping Cg or thermal insulation Ci apply, i.e., Cg=1, Ci=1) Overall Correction Factor (C) = Ca * Cf * Cg * Ci = 0.89 * 0.725 * 1 * 1 ≈ 0.645
  3. Determine Minimum Required Tabulated Cable Rating (It): The cable selected must have a tabulated current rating (It) such that: It ≥ Ib / (Overall Correction Factor C) It ≥ 42 A / 0.645 It ≥ 65.1 A

Conclusion: The cable selected must have a tabulated current rating (under the relevant installation method in BS 7671 appendices) of at least 65.1 Amperes. This would typically necessitate a 10mm² or potentially 16mm² cable, depending on the specific installation method chosen (e.g., clipped direct, in conduit, etc.) - refer to BS 7671 Appendix 4 tables.

Ohmmeters and wattmeters measure resistance and power respectively. Power can be indirectly measured using voltmeter/ammeter methods. Cable abbreviations denote construction materials. Cable ratings must be adjusted for ambient temperature and the type of protective device, particularly rewireable fuses, to ensure safety.

Answer

This answer illustrates series and parallel battery connections, lists sizing conditions for inverters and charge controllers, and outlines maintenance procedures for lights, switches, and PV modules.

(a) Battery Connections

(i) Connection Diagrams (Assuming identical V Volt, C Ah batteries):

(ii) Quantity Enhanced:

  • (I) Parallel Connection: Enhances (increases) the total Ampere-hour (Ah) capacity (energy storage). The voltage remains the same as a single battery. Total Capacity = C1 + C2 + C3.
  • (II) Series Connection: Enhances (increases) the total Voltage (V). The Ampere-hour capacity remains the same as that of a single battery (limited by the lowest capacity battery in the string). Total Voltage = V1 + V2 + V3.

(b) Sizing Conditions for Components

(i) Inverter:

  • Continuous Power Rating (Watts/VA): Must be sufficient to power the sum of all AC loads that might operate simultaneously under normal conditions.
  • Surge Power Rating (Watts/VA): Must be high enough to handle the brief high inrush currents required to start certain appliances (e.g., motors in fridges, pumps).
  • Input DC Voltage: Must match the nominal voltage of the battery bank (e.g., 12V, 24V, 48V).
  • Output AC Voltage and Frequency: Must match the requirements of the AC appliances (e.g., 230V, 50Hz or 120V, 60Hz).
  • Waveform Type: Modified Sine Wave (MSW - cheaper, suitable for simple loads) or Pure Sine Wave (PSW - better quality, required for sensitive electronics, motors). PSW is generally recommended.
  • Efficiency: Higher efficiency means less energy wasted during DC to AC conversion.

(ii) Solar Charge Controller:

  • Maximum PV Input Voltage (Voc): Controller's maximum open-circuit voltage rating must be higher than the maximum possible Voc of the PV array string(s), considering lowest expected temperatures (Voc increases as temperature drops).
  • Maximum PV Input Current (Isc): Controller's maximum short-circuit current rating must be sufficient to handle the total short-circuit current from the PV array string(s), plus a safety factor (e.g., 25%).
  • Nominal System Voltage: Must match the nominal voltage of the battery bank (e.g., 12V, 24V, 48V). Some controllers auto-detect or are configurable.
  • Maximum Charging Current (Output Current): Must be appropriate for the battery bank size and type (typically within the battery manufacturer's recommended charge rate limits, e.g., C/10 to C/5).
  • Technology Type (PWM vs. MPPT): Pulse Width Modulation (PWM) controllers are simpler and cheaper, suitable for smaller systems where array voltage closely matches battery voltage. Maximum Power Point Tracking (MPPT) controllers are more efficient (especially with higher voltage arrays or in cooler conditions) as they optimize the array's operating point, but are more expensive.
  • Load Control / LVD Rating (if applicable): If the controller manages DC loads, its load current rating and Low Voltage Disconnect settings must be suitable for the connected DC appliances.

(c) Maintenance Procedures

(i) Lights and Switches:

  • Cleaning: Periodically wipe light fittings (lamps/bulbs and fixtures) and switch plates with a dry or slightly damp cloth to remove dust and dirt, which can reduce light output or make switches look unsightly. Ensure power is off if using a damp cloth near electrical parts.
  • Functionality Check: Regularly operate switches to ensure they function correctly and smoothly. Listen for any unusual sounds (crackling).
  • Tightness Check: Periodically check that switches and light fittings are securely fixed to the wall/ceiling. Check terminal screw tightness inside switches/fittings if accessible and safe to do so (power isolated).
  • Lamp Replacement: Replace flickering or failed lamps/bulbs promptly with the correct type and wattage.

(ii) PV Module:

  • Cleaning: Inspect module surfaces for dirt, dust, leaves, bird droppings. Clean as necessary (typically 1-2 times per year or more in dusty areas) using water and a soft brush/cloth, preferably during cooler parts of the day. (See Q6(b)(iii) 2014j for more detail).
  • Visual Inspection: Check for any physical damage to the glass, frame, backsheet, or junction box (cracks, discoloration, delamination, water ingress).
  • Connections Check: Inspect module wiring and connections at the junction box for tightness, corrosion, or damage. Ensure connectors (e.g., MC4) are securely latched.
  • Mounting Structure Check: Verify that the mounting frame/rack is secure, bolts are tight, and there is no excessive corrosion or damage.
  • Shading Analysis: Periodically check if any new obstructions (e.g., tree growth) are causing shading on the modules during key sunlight hours.

Correctly connecting batteries in series or parallel achieves desired voltage or capacity. Sizing inverters and charge controllers requires matching their ratings to the system's voltage, current, and power demands. Routine maintenance of all components, including loads and PV modules, ensures system reliability and longevity.

Answer

This answer provides a block diagram for a hybrid AC/DC PV system, explains the functions of common solar installation accessories, and details the operation of a solar cell with a diagram.

(a) Block Diagram of AC/DC PV Solar System

(b) Function of Accessories in Solar Installation

(i) Socket Outlets: Provide a connection point for plugging in electrical appliances (loads). In solar systems, these can be DC sockets (specifically designed for the system's DC voltage, e.g., 12V cigarette lighter type or specialized DC outlets) for powering DC appliances directly, or standard AC sockets (e.g., 13A UK type) powered via an inverter for running conventional AC appliances.

(ii) Ceiling Roses: A wiring accessory typically mounted on the ceiling, providing a termination point for the fixed wiring of a lighting circuit and a connection point for the flexible cord feeding a pendant light fitting (luminaire). They facilitate safe connection and disconnection of the light fitting and often incorporate terminals for looping the circuit wiring (e.g., 3-plate or 4-plate designs).

(iii) Consumer Control Unit (or Consumer Unit / Distribution Board / Fuse Box): A central enclosure containing protective devices (fuses or Miniature Circuit Breakers - MCBs) for the various final circuits (lights, sockets, etc.) within the installation. It usually also contains a main switch for isolating the entire set of circuits it controls. In a solar system, separate DC and AC consumer units might be used for the respective parts of the installation, providing overcurrent protection for the different DC and AC load circuits.

(c) Operation of a Solar Cell

A solar cell converts sunlight directly into DC electricity using the photovoltaic effect within a semiconductor P-N junction.

Hybrid AC/DC PV systems provide flexibility in powering different types of appliances. Standard wiring accessories like sockets and ceiling roses are used, adapted for DC or AC as needed, alongside control units for protection. The fundamental energy conversion occurs within the solar cell via the photovoltaic effect at the P-N junction.

Question 8

(a) (i) State the basic energy resource for all types of solar systems.

(ii) Define the following angles with respect to available energy reaching the earth's surface:

(I) angle of incidence;

(II) altitude angle.

(b) (i) Explain the purpose of a solar collector.

(ii) List five types of solar collectors used in solar systems.

(c) Explain how solar energy is used in the following areas:

(i) crop drying;

(ii) cooking;

(iii) water heating;

(iv) space heating;

(v) green houses.

Answer

This answer identifies the basic solar resource, defines key solar angles, explains the purpose and types of solar collectors, and describes various applications of solar energy.

(a) Basic Resource and Solar Angles

(i) Basic Energy Resource: The basic energy resource for all types of solar energy systems is Solar Radiation (or sunlight), the electromagnetic energy emitted by the Sun.

(ii) Solar Angle Definitions:

  • (I) Angle of Incidence (θ): The angle between the sun's rays striking a surface and the line perpendicular (normal) to that surface. A smaller angle of incidence (closer to 0°, meaning the sun is perpendicular to the surface) results in higher energy intensity received per unit area of the surface.
  • (II) Altitude Angle (α): The vertical angle between the sun's rays and the horizontal plane (the horizon). It measures how high the sun is in the sky. The altitude angle varies throughout the day (lowest at sunrise/sunset, highest at solar noon) and throughout the year (higher in summer, lower in winter).

(b) Solar Collectors

(i) Purpose of a Solar Collector: To intercept solar radiation and convert it into useful thermal energy (heat) by transferring the absorbed energy to a working fluid (like water, air, or antifreeze solution), or, in the case of PV panels (which are also technically solar collectors), to convert it directly into electricity.

(ii) Five Types of Solar Collectors:

  1. Flat Plate Collectors (Glazed): Most common for domestic water and space heating, using a dark absorber plate under glass.
  2. Evacuated Tube Collectors: Use vacuum insulation between glass tubes to achieve higher efficiencies, especially in cold climates or for higher temperature needs (e.g., industrial process heat, absorption cooling).
  3. Unglazed Collectors: Simple dark absorber plates without glazing, typically used for low-temperature applications like swimming pool heating.
  4. Concentrating Collectors (e.g., Parabolic Troughs, Parabolic Dishes, Power Towers): Use mirrors or lenses to focus sunlight onto a smaller receiver area, achieving much higher temperatures suitable for industrial heat or electricity generation via thermal cycles.
  5. Photovoltaic (PV) Modules/Panels: Collect solar radiation and convert it directly into DC electricity (considered a collector in the broad sense of intercepting solar energy).
  6. Solar Air Heaters: Collectors designed specifically to heat air, often using a dark absorber plate with air flowing over or through it, used for space heating or crop drying.

(c) Applications of Solar Energy

(i) Crop Drying: Solar energy is used to heat air, which is then passed over or through crops (grains, fruits, vegetables) to remove moisture, aiding preservation. Simple solar dryers often consist of a dark enclosure (solar collector box) where air is heated and then rises through trays containing the crops.

(ii) Cooking: Solar cookers use various designs (box cookers, parabolic cookers, panel cookers) to capture sunlight and concentrate its heat for cooking food. Box cookers trap heat like a greenhouse, while parabolic cookers focus sunlight intensely onto a cooking pot.

(iii) Water Heating: Solar water heaters (using flat plate or evacuated tube collectors) absorb solar energy to heat water directly or via a heat transfer fluid, storing the hot water in an insulated tank for domestic (showers, taps) or commercial use.

(iv) Space Heating: Solar energy can be used for space heating through passive design (orienting buildings, using large windows, thermal mass) or active systems. Active systems use solar air heaters or solar water collectors to heat air or liquid, which is then circulated through the building via ducts or radiators.

(v) Greenhouses: Greenhouses utilize solar energy passively. The transparent glass or plastic covering allows sunlight (shortwave radiation) to enter and heat the interior surfaces (plants, soil, structure). These surfaces re-radiate heat as longer-wave infrared radiation, which is trapped by the covering (greenhouse effect), maintaining a warmer temperature inside than outside, conducive to plant growth, especially in cooler climates or seasons.

Sunlight is the fundamental resource for solar energy. Understanding solar angles helps optimize collection. Various collector types are designed for different applications, from low-temperature crop drying and water heating to high-temperature cooking and power generation, while greenhouses demonstrate passive solar energy utilization.
