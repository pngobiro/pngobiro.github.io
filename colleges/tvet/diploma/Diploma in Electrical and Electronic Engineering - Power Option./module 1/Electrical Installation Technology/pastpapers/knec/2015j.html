Answer

This answer details advantages of the electrical grid, identifies power production authorities, explains hydroelectric power generation, and illustrates DC three-wire and AC two-phase three-wire distribution systems.

(a) Grid System Advantages and Power Authorities

(i) Advantages of the Grid System (Interconnected Power System):

  • Increased Reliability: Failure of one generating station does not cause a total supply interruption as power can be fed from other interconnected stations.
  • Reduced Reserve Capacity Requirement: Each station doesn't need its own full backup (spinning reserve); reserve capacity can be shared across the grid, reducing overall investment costs.
  • Economic Operation: More efficient power stations can be run continuously at base load, while less efficient ones are used only for peak loads, leading to lower overall fuel consumption and operating costs.
  • Flexibility in Plant Location: Generating stations can be located at the most economical sites (e.g., near fuel sources or water for hydro) rather than needing to be close to load centers.
  • Load Diversity Benefit: Peak demands in different areas often occur at different times; interconnection allows sharing of generation capacity to meet these varying peaks more efficiently.

(ii) Authorities Involved in Power Production (Examples, specific to Kenya):

  • Kenya Electricity Generating Company (KenGen): The primary state-owned corporation responsible for generating the majority of electricity in Kenya through various sources like hydro, geothermal, thermal, and wind.
  • Independent Power Producers (IPPs): Private companies licensed to generate electricity and sell it to the national grid operator (e.g., Lake Turkana Wind Power, Orpower 4 Inc., Rabai Power).
  • Energy and Petroleum Regulatory Authority (EPRA): The government agency responsible for regulating the entire energy sector, including licensing power producers, setting tariffs, and ensuring compliance with standards.

(b) Hydroelectric Power Station Operation

Hydroelectric power stations utilize the potential energy of water stored at a height to generate electricity.

Question 2

(a) (i) Complete the following cable abbreviations and state an application for each.

(I) P.C.P

(II) MIMS

(ii) Outline the procedure for making a married joint.

(b) (i) Describe the 'vertical rising mains' trunking wiring system.

(ii) Outline the procedure of laying an underground cable.

(c) Explain the 'horizontal cabling' system structure.

Answer

This answer explains cable abbreviations, details the married joint procedure, describes vertical rising main trunking and underground cable laying, and explains horizontal cabling structure.

(a) Cable Abbreviations and Married Joint

(i) Cable Abbreviations:

  • (I) P.C.P: Polychloroprene (often referred to by the trade name Neoprene).
    • Application: Used as a sheathing material for flexible cables (e.g., H07RN-F type cables). It offers good resistance to abrasion, oils, chemicals, weathering, and flame, making it suitable for tough environments like workshops, industrial sites, trailing leads for portable equipment, and outdoor use.
  • (II) MIMS: Mineral Insulated Metal Sheathed (often MICC - Mineral Insulated Copper Clad).
    • Application: Used in applications requiring high fire resistance, mechanical strength, and durability. Common in fire alarm circuits, emergency lighting, hazardous areas, high-temperature locations, and where protection against moisture and corrosion is critical.

(ii) Procedure for Making a Married Joint (Joining two multi-strand cables):

A married joint creates a strong, low-resistance connection between two stranded conductors.

  1. Prepare Cables: Strip the insulation from the ends of both cables to be joined (e.g., 5-7 cm).
  2. Fan & Clean Strands: Fan out the strands of each conductor end like a brush. Clean the strands thoroughly (e.g., with fine emery cloth or wire brush) to remove any oxide layer.
  3. Interlace Strands ("Marriage"): Push the fanned ends of the two conductors together so the strands interlace ("marry") completely.
  4. Twist Strands: Tightly twist the interlaced strands together in one direction (e.g., clockwise) around the central core of the joint, ensuring all strands are incorporated smoothly.
  5. Wrap Loose Ends: Take a few strands from each side (before the main twist) and wrap them tightly in opposite directions over the main twisted section to secure it. Trim any excess sharp ends.
  6. Solder (Essential): Apply flux and solder the entire joint thoroughly using a suitable soldering iron or blowtorch and solder. Ensure solder flows fully into the strands, creating a solid electrical and mechanical bond. Wipe off excess flux residue.
  7. Insulate: Once cool, insulate the joint properly using layers of appropriate insulation tape (e.g., PVC electrical tape) or heat-shrink sleeving, ensuring the insulation overlaps the original cable insulation on both sides to provide full electrical protection.

(b) Vertical Rising Mains and Underground Cable Laying

(i) Vertical Rising Mains Trunking System:

This is a method for distributing electrical power vertically through multi-storey buildings, typically from a main switchroom in the basement or ground floor to distribution boards on each upper floor. It uses purpose-designed metal or PVC trunking sections installed vertically, often within dedicated service risers or shafts. Tap-off units, containing fuses or circuit breakers, can be securely fitted at intervals along the trunking to supply the distribution boards on each floor without needing to cut the main busbars or cables running within the trunking.

(ii) Procedure for Laying Underground Cable (Direct Burial - simplified):

  1. Planning & Route Survey: Determine the cable route, avoiding other services where possible. Obtain necessary permissions and check utility plans ('dial before you dig').
  2. Excavation: Dig a trench along the planned route to the required depth as specified by regulations (e.g., typically 450mm-750mm or more depending on location and voltage). The trench bottom should be level and free from sharp stones.
  3. Bedding: Lay a bedding layer of fine soil or sand (e.g., 50-75mm deep) at the bottom of the trench to protect the cable.
  4. Cable Laying: Carefully unroll the cable (suitable for direct burial, e.g., SWA) into the trench, avoiding kinks or excessive tension. Do not drag it over sharp objects. Leave some slack ('snaking') to allow for ground movement.
  5. Cable Covering: Place another layer of sand or fine soil (e.g., 75mm) over the cable.
  6. Protection & Warning: Lay protective cable covers (e.g., concrete tiles, plastic covers) or marker tape above the sand layer to provide mechanical protection and warn future excavators.
  7. Backfilling: Carefully backfill the trench with the excavated soil, removing large stones or sharp objects first. Compact the soil in layers.
  8. Marking: Install permanent cable markers at the surface indicating the route, especially at changes in direction or termination points.
  9. Testing: Perform insulation resistance and continuity tests before energizing the cable.

(c) Horizontal Cabling System Structure (Structured Cabling)

In structured cabling (typically for data/telecoms, but principles apply), the horizontal cabling refers to the portion of the cabling infrastructure that runs from the telecommunications room (TR) or horizontal cross-connect (HC) on a floor to the individual work area outlets (WAOs) or sockets where end-user equipment connects. It includes:

  • Horizontal Cables: The actual cables (e.g., Cat 5e, Cat 6, Cat 6A copper cables, or fiber optic cables) running horizontally above ceilings, below floors, or in conduits/trunking.
  • Termination Hardware: Patch panels located in the TR/HC where the horizontal cables are terminated.
  • Work Area Outlets (WAOs): The sockets or faceplates in the user's work area where the horizontal cables terminate and devices are plugged in.
  • Patch Cords/Fly Leads (Optional): Sometimes considered part of the horizontal subsystem, these connect the patch panel to network switches in the TR, and the WAO to the end-user device.

This subsystem is typically installed in a star topology, with each WAO connected back to the central point (TR/HC) on that floor via its own dedicated horizontal cable. It does not include backbone cabling (vertical runs between floors/buildings) or equipment cables.

Understanding cable types, jointing techniques, and different installation methods like rising mains, underground laying, and structured cabling components is essential for various electrical and data installations.

(a) (i) Define 'fire alarm system'.

(ii) Explain the purpose of a fire alarm system.

(b) (i) With aid of a circuit diagram, explain the operation of a closed intruder alarm system;

(ii) State three advantages of the system in (b)(i).

(c) Draw a labelled diagram of a trembler bell.

Answer

This answer defines and explains the purpose of fire alarm systems, describes the operation and advantages of closed-circuit intruder alarms, and provides a diagram of a trembler bell.

(a) Fire Alarm System Definition and Purpose

(i) Definition: A fire alarm system is a set of interconnected electronic and/or electromechanical devices designed to detect the presence of fire (through smoke, heat, flames, or carbon monoxide) and alert building occupants and emergency services, enabling timely evacuation and response.

(ii) Purpose: The primary purpose of a fire alarm system is life safety – to provide early warning of a fire, allowing occupants sufficient time to escape safely. Secondary purposes include:

  • Alerting emergency services (fire brigade) automatically or manually.
  • Initiating other fire safety functions (e.g., closing fire doors, shutting down ventilation systems, activating suppression systems).
  • Minimizing property damage by enabling a faster response.
  • Meeting building codes and regulatory requirements.

(b) Closed Intruder Alarm System

(i) Operation and Diagram:

A closed-circuit intruder alarm system operates by maintaining a continuous low-voltage electrical circuit through a series of detection sensors (e.g., door/window contacts, pressure mats). An interruption (break) in this circuit triggers the alarm.

(ii) Advantages of Closed-Circuit System:

  • Fail-Safe (Tamper Detection): If the wiring is cut or a sensor is tampered with in a way that breaks the circuit, the alarm will trigger, making it more secure against deliberate bypass attempts compared to simple open-circuit systems.
  • Supervision: The continuous current flow allows the system to monitor the integrity of the wiring loop. A break due to a fault (not just intrusion) will also be detected (often indicated differently at the panel).
  • Reliability: Generally considered more reliable as the normal state involves current flow, making faults like broken wires easily detectable.

(c) Trembler Bell Diagram

Alarm systems are crucial for safety and security. Fire alarms focus on early fire detection for evacuation, while intruder alarms detect unauthorized entry. Closed-circuit intruder alarms offer enhanced security through tamper detection. The trembler bell is a classic electromechanical sounder relying on an electromagnetic interrupter.

(c) Polarity Test on a Dead Circuit

This test verifies that single-pole switches/fuses are in the line conductor and checks socket/lampholder polarity before the installation is energized.

Instrument: Low-resistance ohmmeter or continuity tester.

Procedure (Example for a lighting circuit with one switch):

  1. Safety: Ensure the circuit and entire installation are safely isolated (dead) from the supply. Confirm isolation with a voltage tester. Remove the lamp from the holder.
  2. At Consumer Unit: Temporarily link the incoming Line (Phase) conductor for that circuit MCB to the installation's Main Earthing Terminal (MET).

Continuity should *not* exist here (this should be the neutral).

(d) Simmerstat vs. Thermostat

  • Simmerstat: An energy regulator, typically used on electric cooker hotplates or grills. It does *not* sense temperature directly. Instead, it controls the *proportion* of time that the heating element is switched ON over a cycle. Turning the knob varies this ON/OFF ratio (duty cycle), thus controlling the average power delivered and the heat output. It cycles continuously based on the setting, regardless of the actual temperature reached (unless it has a separate overheat cutout).
  • Thermostat: A temperature-sensitive controller. It measures the actual temperature of a substance (air, water, surface) and switches a heating or cooling element ON or OFF to maintain the temperature at or near a desired setpoint. It operates based on feedback from the measured temperature, opening the circuit when the set temperature is reached and closing it when the temperature falls below a certain differential. Examples include oven thermostats, room thermostats, and immersion heater thermostats.

Accurate measurement is key in electrical work. Consumer units provide central control and protection. Polarity testing is a critical safety check. Simmerstats regulate power input by time-proportioning, while thermostats regulate by sensing and reacting to actual temperature.

(a) Define the following terms as used in electrical circuit protection:

(i) fusing factor;

(ii) fusing current.

(b) (i) State four advantages of circuit breakers over fuses.

(ii) Draw a labelled diagram of a high breaking capacity fuse.

(c) With aid of a labelled diagram, show how an earth loop impedance test is carried out.

Answer

This answer defines fuse characteristics, lists advantages of circuit breakers, illustrates a High Breaking Capacity (HBC) fuse, and explains the Earth Fault Loop Impedance test procedure with a diagram.

(a) Fuse Terminology Definitions

(i) Fusing Factor: The ratio of the minimum current required to melt the fuse element (fusing current) to the fuse's rated current (the current it can carry continuously). It is calculated as: Fusing Factor = Minimum Fusing Current / Current Rating. This factor is always greater than 1 and indicates how much overload the fuse wire can withstand before blowing; a lower factor generally means faster operation for small overloads.

(ii) Fusing Current: The minimum value of current that will cause the fuse element to melt and permanently break the circuit under specified conditions. This is significantly higher than the fuse's normal current rating.

(b) Circuit Breakers vs. Fuses and HBC Fuse Diagram

(i) Four Advantages of Circuit Breakers (MCBs/MCCBs) over Fuses:

  • Resettable: Circuit breakers can be easily reset after tripping due to an overload or fault, unlike fuses which must be replaced, saving time and replacement costs.
  • More Precise Operation: Circuit breakers typically offer more accurately defined tripping characteristics (time/current curves) compared to fuses, allowing for better coordination and discrimination.
  • Convenience & Safety: Easier and safer to operate (reset) than replacing a fuse, especially for non-technical users. They often provide a clear visual indication of their tripped status.
  • Combined Functionality (e.g., RCBOs): Some circuit breakers (like RCBOs) combine overcurrent (overload and short circuit) protection with residual current (earth fault) protection in a single device.
  • Multi-Pole Switching: Circuit breakers are readily available in multi-pole versions (DP, TP, TPN) that ensure all relevant conductors (phases and potentially neutral) are disconnected simultaneously.

(c) Earth Fault Loop Impedance (Zs) Test

This test measures the total impedance of the path that fault current would take from the line conductor back to the source transformer's neutral via the installation's earthing system (CPC, MET, earth electrode/supplier's earth) and the transformer winding, under earth fault conditions. It's crucial for verifying that the impedance is low enough to allow sufficient fault current to flow to operate the protective device (MCB/fuse) within the required disconnection time.

Instrument: Earth Fault Loop Impedance Tester.

Procedure (Live Test - carried out with supply ON, extreme caution required):

  1. Safety: Ensure the tester is functioning correctly and test leads are in good condition. Understand the risks of live testing.
  2. Connection: Connect the tester's three leads (typically Line, Neutral, Earth) to the furthest point of the circuit under test (e.g., a socket outlet). Connect L to Line terminal, N to Neutral terminal, E to Earth terminal. For lighting circuits, specific adapters might be needed, or the test performed at the switch or ceiling rose.
  3. Test Execution: Initiate the test according to the instrument's instructions. The tester briefly applies a test current between Line and Earth and measures the impedance (Zs) of the complete loop.
  4. Reading & Verification: Record the measured Zs value. Compare this value against the maximum permissible Zs value for the specific type and rating of the protective device (MCB or fuse) protecting that circuit, as specified in BS 7671 tables (e.g., Table 41.3). The measured Zs must be *lower* than the maximum permissible value to ensure adequate fault protection.

Fuse characteristics define their protective behavior. Circuit breakers offer advantages in convenience and precision. The Earth Fault Loop Impedance test is a critical safety check verifying the effectiveness of the earthing system in enabling rapid disconnection under fault conditions.

Question 6

(a) Define the following solar energy terminologies:

(i) radiation;

(ii) solar constant.

(b) Explain the following solar energy harvesting devices and state one application of each:

(i) simple reflector;

(ii) parabolic dishes.

(c) Explain the reason for tracking and tilting of solar devices.

(d) With the aid of a labelled diagram, describe the constructional and working principle of flat plate collector in solar water heating.

Answer

This answer defines solar radiation terms, explains reflector and parabolic dish collectors, discusses the purpose of tracking and tilting, and details the construction and operation of flat plate collectors for water heating.

(a) Solar Energy Terminologies

(i) Radiation (Solar Radiation): The energy emitted by the sun in the form of electromagnetic waves, spanning various wavelengths including visible light, infrared (heat), and ultraviolet. When discussing solar energy, it specifically refers to this energy reaching the Earth's atmosphere or surface.

(ii) Solar Constant: The average amount of solar electromagnetic radiation energy received per unit area per unit time on a surface perpendicular to the sun's rays, measured outside the Earth's atmosphere at the mean Earth-Sun distance. Its value is approximately 1361 Watts per square meter (W/m²).

(b) Solar Energy Harvesting Devices

(i) Simple Reflector: A basic flat or slightly curved reflective surface (like a mirror or polished metal sheet) used to redirect sunlight onto a target area, increasing the amount of solar energy received by that target.

  • Application: Used in solar cookers (e.g., box cookers with reflector flaps), passive solar building design (reflecting light into windows), or sometimes to augment the light reaching flat plate collectors or PV panels.

(ii) Parabolic Dishes: A concentrating solar collector using a large, dish-shaped mirror with a parabolic profile. It reflects parallel incoming sunlight rays to a single focal point where a receiver is placed.

  • Application: Achieving very high temperatures at the receiver, suitable for generating electricity via Stirling engines placed at the focus (Dish-Stirling systems), or heating fluids for industrial processes or large-scale solar thermal power generation.

(c) Reason for Tracking and Tilting Solar Devices

Solar collectors (PV panels or thermal collectors) capture the maximum amount of energy when the sunlight strikes their surface perpendicularly (at a 90° angle).

  • Tilting: Adjusting the angle of the collector relative to the horizontal ground. This is done primarily to account for the seasonal variation in the sun's altitude (height in the sky). Optimal tilt angle generally depends on latitude and whether energy collection needs to be maximized year-round or biased towards winter or summer. Fixed tilt is common, but adjustable tilt allows seasonal optimization.
  • Tracking: Physically moving the collector throughout the day to follow the sun's apparent path across the sky from east to west. This ensures the surface remains as close to perpendicular to the sun's rays as possible, significantly increasing the total daily energy captured (by up to 25-40% or more compared to fixed systems, especially for concentrating collectors). Tracking can be single-axis (following East-West movement) or dual-axis (following both East-West and North-South/altitude changes).

The purpose of both tilting and tracking is to maximize the solar irradiance incident on the collector surface, thereby maximizing the energy harvested.

(d) Flat Plate Collector (Solar Water Heating)

Solar energy terminology helps quantify the resource. Different collectors are suited for different temperature requirements, with flat plates common for domestic hot water. Optimizing orientation through tilting and tracking maximizes energy capture.

Question 7

(a) Define the following in relation to solar installation:

(i) array;

(ii) appliance.

(b) (i) State two factors that determine the output of a solar cell.

(ii) Distinguish between monocrystalline and polycrystalline solar cells.

(c) Explain the:

(i) low voltage disconnect in a charge controller;

(ii) difference between a voltage converter and an voltage inverter.

Answer

This answer defines solar array and appliance, lists factors affecting solar cell output, distinguishes between monocrystalline and polycrystalline cells, and explains Low Voltage Disconnect, voltage converters, and inverters.

(a) Definitions in Solar Installation

(i) Array (Solar Array or PV Array): A complete power-generating unit consisting of multiple interconnected solar modules (panels) mounted together on a supporting structure. The modules are wired in series and/or parallel to achieve the desired system voltage and current output.

(ii) Appliance (Electrical Appliance/Load): Any device within the solar installation that consumes electrical energy supplied by the system to perform its intended function. Examples include lights, radios, televisions, refrigerators, phone chargers, pumps, etc. Loads can be DC (powered directly from the battery/controller) or AC (powered via an inverter).

(b) Solar Cell Output Factors and Types

(i) Two Factors Determining Solar Cell Output:

  • Solar Irradiance (Light Intensity): The amount of sunlight falling on the cell's surface. Higher irradiance generates more electron-hole pairs, leading to a higher current output (power is proportional to current and voltage).
  • Cell Temperature: The operating temperature of the cell. Higher temperatures decrease the cell's voltage output significantly, which generally leads to a lower overall power output, even though current slightly increases.
  • (Other factors include the spectrum of light, angle of incidence, and the cell's intrinsic properties like material and efficiency).

(ii) Monocrystalline vs. Polycrystalline Solar Cells:

  • Monocrystalline Cells: Made from a single, high-purity silicon crystal ingot, sliced into wafers. They typically have a uniform dark color (black or dark blue) and often have chamfered (cut) corners due to the cylindrical shape of the ingot.
    • Advantages: Highest efficiency (typically 15-22%+), slightly better performance in low light conditions, potentially longer lifespan, require less space for the same power output.
    • Disadvantages: More expensive to produce, slightly more affected by high temperatures compared to polycrystalline (though still good), manufacturing process is more energy-intensive and produces more waste silicon.
  • Polycrystalline Cells (or Multicrystalline): Made from silicon fragments melted together and cast into square blocks before being sliced into wafers. They have a characteristic "mosaic" or "marbled" blue appearance due to the multiple crystal boundaries within the cell.
    • Advantages: Less expensive to produce than monocrystalline, manufacturing process is simpler and less wasteful.
    • Disadvantages: Lower efficiency than monocrystalline (typically 13-18%+), require slightly more space for the same power output, slightly lower performance in low light.

(c) Charge Controller Feature and Power Electronics

(i) Low Voltage Disconnect (LVD) in a Charge Controller:

The LVD is a protective feature built into most solar charge controllers. Its purpose is to automatically disconnect the electrical loads (appliances) from the battery when the battery voltage drops below a predetermined safe minimum level. This prevents excessive discharge of the battery, which can cause permanent damage (sulfation, reduced capacity) and significantly shorten its lifespan, especially for lead-acid types. Once the battery is sufficiently recharged by the solar panels, the LVD feature typically reconnects the load automatically.

(ii) Difference Between Voltage Converter and Voltage Inverter:

  • Voltage Converter (DC-DC Converter): An electronic device that converts Direct Current (DC) voltage from one level to another DC voltage level. For example, it might step down a higher DC voltage (e.g., from a 24V battery) to a lower DC voltage (e.g., 12V or 5V) to power specific DC appliances, or step up a lower DC voltage to a higher one. It does *not* change the type of current (it remains DC).
  • Voltage Inverter (DC-AC Inverter): An electronic device that converts Direct Current (DC) voltage (typically from batteries or solar panels) into Alternating Current (AC) voltage (usually at the standard mains frequency and voltage, e.g., 230V/50Hz or 120V/60Hz). This allows standard AC appliances to be powered by a DC source like a solar PV system.

Understanding the components and terminology of a solar system, including array structure, cell types, and the functions of devices like charge controllers (with LVD), converters, and inverters, is crucial for proper system design and operation.

Answer

This answer lists common solar installation tests, battery handling precautions, methods for measuring battery state of charge, completes a troubleshooting table, and calculates energy requirements for a domestic solar system.

(a) Solar Installation Tests and Battery Precautions

(i) Two Tests Carried Out in a Solar Installation:

  • Continuity of Protective Conductors: Verifies that all equipment frames, mounting structures, and metallic components are properly bonded together and connected to the main earth terminal with low resistance, ensuring a safe path for fault current. (Tested with a low-resistance ohmmeter).
  • PV Array Insulation Resistance Test: Measures the resistance between the current-carrying conductors (positive and negative DC lines) and earth (e.g., the array frame). A high resistance value (typically megaohms) indicates good insulation, preventing leakage currents and shock hazards. (Tested with an insulation resistance tester at appropriate DC voltage).
  • Polarity Check: Verifies that positive conductors are connected to positive terminals and negative conductors to negative terminals throughout the DC side of the system (modules, controller, battery, loads).
  • Open Circuit Voltage (Voc) and Short Circuit Current (Isc) Tests: Measures the Voc and Isc of individual modules or strings under prevailing sunlight conditions and compares them to manufacturer specifications (adjusted for conditions) to check for module/string health and performance.

(ii) Two Precautions When Handling Lead-Acid Batteries:

  • Wear Personal Protective Equipment (PPE): Always wear appropriate eye protection (goggles/face shield) and acid-resistant gloves to protect against corrosive electrolyte splashes.
  • Avoid Sparks and Flames: Keep sparks, open flames, smoking materials, and non-insulated tools away from batteries, especially during charging, as they release flammable hydrogen gas which can cause explosions. Ensure good ventilation.
  • Lift Correctly: Batteries are heavy. Use proper lifting techniques or lifting aids to avoid back injury.
  • Handle Acid Carefully (Flooded types): Use appropriate containers and funnels if handling electrolyte. Have neutralizing agent (baking soda) ready for spills.

(b) Methods of Measuring Battery State of Charge (SoC)

  1. Voltage Measurement: Measuring the battery's terminal voltage *at rest* (disconnected from charge/discharge for several hours) provides an estimate of SoC. The relationship between voltage and SoC depends on battery type (flooded, AGM, Gel) and temperature. Higher voltage generally indicates higher SoC (e.g., ~12.7V+ for a fully charged 12V lead-acid battery). This method is less accurate than specific gravity, especially under load or immediately after charging/discharging.
  2. Specific Gravity Measurement (Flooded Lead-Acid Only): Using a hydrometer to measure the specific gravity of the electrolyte in each cell offers the most accurate indication of SoC for flooded batteries. Higher specific gravity (e.g., 1.265-1.280, temperature corrected) corresponds to a higher concentration of sulfuric acid and thus a higher SoC. It also helps identify variations between cells.
  3. Coulomb Counting (Battery Monitor): Electronic battery monitors track the Ampere-hours (Ah) flowing into and out of the battery. By knowing the battery's total capacity and tracking net charge/discharge, they calculate and display the SoC percentage. This requires initial calibration and can drift over time if not periodically reset based on a full charge indication.

(d) Domestic Solar System Energy Calculation

(i) Daily energy requirement for each lamp/appliance:

Energy (Wh) = Number x Rating (W) x Hours (h)

  • Indoor lights: 4 x 6W x 3h = 72 Wh
  • Outdoor lights: 1 x 8W x 1h = 8 Wh
  • 14" colour T.V: 1 x 70W x 3h = 210 Wh
  • Radio: 1 x 10W x 3h = 30 Wh
  • Phone charger: 1 x 5W x 1h = 5 Wh

(ii) Total daily energy requirement for the whole system:

Sum of individual energy requirements = 72 + 8 + 210 + 30 + 5 = 325 Wh/day.

(iii) System daily ampere hours (Ah), assuming no losses:

Ampere-hours (Ah) = Total Energy (Wh) / System Voltage (V)

Ah = 325 Wh / 12 V = 27.08 Ah/day

Note: Real-world sizing must account for system losses (wiring, controller, inverter if used), battery efficiency, desired autonomy (days without sun), and depth of discharge limits, leading to significantly larger battery and array requirements than this simple calculation suggests.

Thorough testing, safe handling practices, and understanding how to measure battery state of charge are vital for solar installation maintenance. Troubleshooting requires systematically checking components and connections. Basic energy calculations form the starting point for system sizing.
