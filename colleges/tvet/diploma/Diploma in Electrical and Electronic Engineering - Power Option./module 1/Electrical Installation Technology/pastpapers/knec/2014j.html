Question 1

(a) Define the following terms with respect to electrical installation work.

(i) equipotential bonding;

(ii) earth electrode.

(b) With the aid of labelled circuit diagrams, explain the following methods of earthing

(i) TNC-S

(ii) TT

(c) Draw the sequence of switch gear control arrangement at the consumers intake point.

Answer

This answer defines key earthing terms, explains TNC-S and TT earthing systems with diagrams, and illustrates the typical consumer intake control sequence.

(a) Definitions

(i) Equipotential Bonding: The electrical connection of various exposed-conductive-parts (metalwork of appliances, equipment casings) and extraneous-conductive-parts (metal water pipes, gas pipes, structural steelwork) within an installation to minimize the voltage differences that could appear between them during an earth fault or due to external electrical influences. This reduces the risk of electric shock by ensuring these parts are at substantially the same potential, usually earth potential.

(ii) Earth Electrode: A conductor or group of conductors (e.g., rod, plate, tape) buried in the ground in intimate contact with the general mass of earth. Its purpose is to provide a connection between the installation's main earthing terminal and the earth itself, facilitating the flow of earth fault current and establishing an earth reference potential.

(b) Earthing Systems Explained

(i) TNC-S (Terra Neutral Combined - Separate): Also known as Protective Multiple Earthing (PME). In this system, the supply neutral conductor also functions as the protective earth conductor (PEN - Protective Earth Neutral) within the supplier's network. At or near the consumer's intake, the PEN conductor is split into separate Neutral (N) and Protective Earth (PE) terminals, which are then kept separate throughout the consumer's installation. The supplier provides the consumer with an earth terminal connected to the incoming PEN conductor.

(ii) TT (Terra Terra): In this system, the supply transformer has its neutral point connected directly to earth at the source (first T). The consumer's installation is earthed independently via its own earth electrode (e.g., rod), which is separate from the supplier's earthing arrangement (second T). There is no protective earth conductor provided by the supplier.

Proper earthing and bonding are fundamental safety requirements in electrical installations. The specific earthing system (e.g., TNC-S, TT) dictates how this is achieved. The consumer intake equipment provides control, metering, and protection for the installation.

Question 2

(a) State three IEE Regulations regarding the rising main bus-bar systems.

(b) It is a requirement that before carrying out calculations for cable size using current edition of the IEE regulations, the assessment of general characteristics of the installation must be taken into consideration. Outline the considerations under the following headings:

(i) purpose supplies and structure;

(ii) external influences;

(iii) compatibility.

(c) Illustrate the following connection circuits:

(i) joint box method;

(ii) three plate ceiling rose method.

Answer

This answer covers IEE regulation requirements for rising main busbar systems, outlines the assessment of general installation characteristics as per regulations, and illustrates common lighting circuit connection methods.

(a) IEE Regulations Regarding Rising Main Bus-bar Systems (BS 7671 Requirements - General Principles)

Rising main busbar systems distribute power vertically through multi-storey buildings. Key requirements include:

  1. Accessibility and Support: Busbar trunking must be adequately supported throughout its length to prevent sagging and stress on joints. It should be installed in accessible positions for inspection, testing, and maintenance, but protected from unauthorized interference and mechanical damage. (Ref: Regulation Group 522).
  2. Fire Safety: Where busbar systems pass through fire compartment walls or floors, fire barriers must be installed within or around the trunking system to maintain the fire integrity of the compartment for the required period. Materials used should comply with relevant fire safety standards. (Ref: Regulation Group 527).
  3. Earthing and Bonding: The metal enclosure of the busbar trunking system must be effectively earthed and serve as a Circuit Protective Conductor (CPC), or a separate internal/external CPC must be provided. Equipotential bonding connections must be made where necessary, particularly at the origin and where the system enters different building zones or connects to distribution boards. (Ref: Regulations 543, 411.3).
  4. Voltage Drop: The design must ensure that the voltage drop along the busbar system under normal load conditions does not exceed the limits specified in the regulations (typically 3% for lighting and 5% for other uses from the origin of the installation). (Ref: Regulation 525, Appendix 4).
  5. Tap-off Units: Tap-off units used to connect distribution boards or final circuits must provide appropriate overcurrent protection, be securely fixed, and ensure correct phase connection. They should be rated for the load they serve and compatible with the busbar system. (Ref: Regulations related to distribution circuits).

(b) Assessment of General Characteristics (BS 7671 - Chapter 31)

Before detailed design and calculation, the following characteristics must be assessed:

(i) Purpose, Supplies, and Structure:

  • Purpose: Determine the intended use of the installation (e.g., domestic, commercial, industrial, special location) and the type of activities it will support.
  • Supplies: Identify the characteristics of the electricity supply - voltage, frequency, number of phases, prospective fault current (Ipf), type of earthing system provided by the supplier (e.g., TNC-S, TN-S, TT), and the nominal current/power requirements. Consider any standby or alternative supplies.
  • Structure: Understand the building's layout, construction materials, division into circuits, location of main switchgear, distribution boards, and major loads. Consider the need for different zones or areas requiring specific electrical considerations.

(ii) External Influences:

Assess the environmental conditions the installation will be exposed to, classified using the 'AD', 'AE', 'AF', etc., codes from BS 7671 Appendix 5. This includes:

  • AD (Water): Presence of water or high humidity (e.g., bathrooms, outdoors). Requires appropriate IP rating for equipment.
  • AE (Foreign Bodies): Presence of solid foreign bodies like dust or small objects. Requires appropriate IP rating.
  • AF (Corrosive/Polluting Substances): Presence of chemicals, salt spray, etc. Requires corrosion-resistant materials.
  • AG (Impact): Risk of mechanical impact. Requires robust equipment or mechanical protection.
  • AH (Vibration): Presence of vibration. Requires secure fixings and potentially specialized equipment.
  • AK/AL (Flora/Fauna): Risk from plant growth or animals (e.g., rodents).
  • AM (Electromagnetic Interference): Presence of EMI sources or sensitivity of equipment.
  • AN (Solar Radiation/UV): Exposure to sunlight. Requires UV-resistant materials.
  • AP (Seismic Effects): Risk of earthquakes.
  • AQ (Lightning): Risk of lightning strikes. May require surge protection.
  • BC (Capability of Persons): Whether users are skilled, instructed, or ordinary persons (affects safety measures).
  • BD (Contact with Earth Potential): Likelihood of persons being in contact with earth potential.

(iii) Compatibility:

Ensure that electrical equipment selected will not have harmful effects on other equipment or the supply network, and will not be adversely affected by them. Considerations include:

  • Electromagnetic Compatibility (EMC): Preventing conducted or radiated interference between devices.
  • Harmonic Currents: Effects of non-linear loads (e.g., electronics, VSDs) on the supply waveform.
  • Inrush Currents: High initial currents drawn by some equipment (e.g., motors, transformers).
  • Starting Currents: Ensuring the supply can handle motor starting currents without excessive voltage dip.
  • DC Components: Potential for DC currents in AC systems due to faulty equipment, affecting RCD operation.

(c) Connection Circuit Illustrations

(i) Joint Box Method (Lighting Circuit):

This method uses dedicated junction boxes to make connections between the supply cable, switch drops, and lamp outlets.

(ii) Three Plate Ceiling Rose Method (Lighting Circuit):

This method utilizes a ceiling rose with three main terminal plates (plus an Earth terminal) to make connections directly at the lamp position. It facilitates the loop-in wiring style.

Adherence to IEE Regulations for systems like rising mains and a thorough assessment of installation characteristics are vital for safe and functional electrical design. Common wiring methods like the joint box and three-plate rose provide different ways to achieve circuit connections.

Question 4

(a) State the reasons for carrying out tests in a completed electrical installation.

(b) With the aid of labelled single line diagram, explain the operation of the trembler bell.

(c) Describe the following wiring systems:

(i) conduit wiring system;

(ii) trunking wiring system.

Answer

This answer explains the necessity of testing completed electrical installations, details the operation of a trembler bell, and describes conduit and trunking wiring systems.

(a) Reasons for Testing Completed Electrical Installations

Testing (inspection and testing) is crucial before putting an installation into service for several reasons:

  • Safety Verification: To ensure the installation is safe for users and protects against electric shock, fire hazards, and burns. This includes verifying earthing, bonding, insulation, polarity, and correct operation of protective devices.
  • Compliance with Regulations: To confirm that the installation meets the requirements of the relevant wiring regulations (e.g., BS 7671 in the UK). This is often a legal requirement.
  • Correct Functionality: To verify that the installation operates as intended according to the design specifications – that circuits work, switches control the correct loads, etc.
  • Establishing Baseline Data: Test results provide a baseline record of the installation's condition, which is valuable for future periodic inspection and testing to detect deterioration.
  • Certification: To enable the issuing of an Electrical Installation Certificate (EIC) or Minor Works Certificate, formally declaring the installation's safety and compliance.

(b) Trembler Bell Operation

A trembler bell is an electromechanical device that produces a continuous ringing sound as long as power is applied. It operates using an electromagnet and an interrupter mechanism.

(c) Wiring Systems Description

(i) Conduit Wiring System:

This system uses tubes (conduits), typically made of PVC or galvanized steel, to enclose and protect insulated electrical cables (usually single-core PVC). The conduits are installed either concealed within the building structure (walls, floors, ceilings) or surface-mounted. Various fittings like bends, elbows, tees, junction boxes, and couplings are used to create a continuous protective pathway. Cables are drawn into the conduit after the conduit system is fully installed.

  • Advantages: Provides excellent mechanical protection for cables, allows for easy rewiring or addition of cables (if sized correctly), offers a neat appearance when concealed, suitable for harsh environments (especially steel conduit).
  • Disadvantages: Can be more labour-intensive and costly to install than surface clipping or trunking, requires skill in bending and joining, condensation can be an issue if not installed correctly.

(ii) Trunking Wiring System:

This system uses rectangular or square-sectioned ducts (trunking), usually made of PVC or galvanized steel, with removable lids. Trunking is typically surface-mounted on walls or ceilings and provides a pathway for multiple cables and circuits. Cables are laid inside the trunking, and the lid is secured afterwards. Various fittings (bends, tees, reducers, outlets) are available.

  • Advantages: Allows easy access for installing, modifying, or adding cables, suitable for accommodating many circuits in one run, provides good mechanical protection, relatively quick to install compared to conduit for complex runs.
  • Disadvantages: Can be visually intrusive if surface-mounted, may require more space than conduit, potentially lower IP rating than well-sealed conduit unless specific IP-rated trunking is used.

Testing ensures electrical installations are safe and compliant. The trembler bell is a classic electromechanical sounder. Conduit and trunking offer robust and versatile methods for cable management and protection in various environments.

Question 5

(a) Define the following terms with respect to solar system installations.

(i) photo voltaic system;

(ii) surge capacity;

(iii) transfer switch.

(b) Explain the reason for connecting solar cells in series.

(c) With the aid of labelled diagrams, explain the earthing arrangement for the solar array.

Answer

This answer defines key solar PV system terms, explains series connection of cells, and describes the earthing requirements for solar arrays.

(a) Solar System Terminology

(i) Photovoltaic (PV) System: An electrical system designed to capture sunlight and convert it directly into electricity using the photovoltaic effect. A complete PV system typically includes solar panels (modules/array), mounting structures, wiring, safety devices (fuses/breakers), often a charge controller, batteries (for off-grid systems), and possibly an inverter (to convert DC to AC).

(ii) Surge Capacity (Inverter): Refers to the ability of an inverter (particularly in off-grid systems) to deliver a higher power output than its continuous rating for a short period (typically seconds). This is important for starting loads with high inrush currents, such as motors in refrigerators, pumps, or power tools.

(iii) Transfer Switch: An electrical switch used in systems with multiple power sources (e.g., grid power and a backup generator, or grid power and an off-grid solar system with battery backup). Its function is to safely switch the load connection from one power source to another, preventing the two sources from being connected simultaneously (which could be dangerous or damage equipment). They can be manual (MTS) or automatic (ATS).

(b) Reason for Connecting Solar Cells in Series

Individual solar cells produce a relatively low voltage (typically around 0.5-0.6 volts under load). To achieve a higher, more usable voltage suitable for charging batteries (e.g., 12V, 24V) or feeding into an inverter, multiple cells are connected in series within a solar module.

  • Voltage Addition: When electrical components are connected in series, their voltages add up while the current remains the same (limited by the lowest current-producing cell).
  • Achieving Usable Voltage: By connecting, for example, 36 cells in series (36 x ~0.5V ≈ 18V open circuit), a module can produce sufficient voltage to effectively charge a 12V battery system, accounting for operating temperatures and voltage drops.

Similarly, modules themselves are often connected in series (forming strings) within an array to achieve even higher DC voltages required by grid-tied inverters or larger battery banks.

(c) Earthing Arrangement for Solar Array

Earthing (grounding) a solar array is crucial for safety, protecting against electric shock and potential lightning damage. There are typically two aspects to consider: equipment earthing and system earthing (depending on the system type).

  1. Equipment Earthing (Grounding): This involves bonding all metallic non-current-carrying parts of the array to the installation's main earthing system.
    • Purpose: To ensure that if a live conductor accidentally touches the metal frame or mounting structure, the fault current has a safe path to ground, causing the protective device (fuse/MCB/RCD) to operate and disconnect the supply, preventing the metalwork from becoming dangerously live.
    • Method: A continuous Circuit Protective Conductor (CPC), typically a copper wire of appropriate size, is run and connected securely to the metal frames of each module and the mounting racks/rails. This CPC is then connected back to the main earthing terminal of the installation. Specialised bonding washers or clips are often used to ensure good electrical contact with anodized aluminium frames.
  2. System Earthing (Grounding - Optional/Specific Cases): In some PV systems (particularly older designs or certain configurations), one of the DC current-carrying conductors (positive or negative) might be intentionally connected to earth.
    • Purpose: Can help stabilise system voltage relative to ground and may be required for certain types of inverters (e.g., some transformerless inverters require functional earthing) or specific safety standards.
    • Method: A connection is made from the designated DC conductor (e.g., negative) to the earthing system, often via a specific DC grounding electrode conductor and potentially through a fuse or breaker for protection.
    • Note: Many modern systems, especially transformerless grid-tied systems, are designed to be "floating" or ungrounded on the DC side, relying solely on equipment earthing and other protective measures like RCDs and insulation monitoring. System earthing choices depend heavily on inverter type, local regulations, and system design.

Defining terms clearly, understanding cell connections, and implementing correct earthing practices are fundamental aspects of safe and effective solar PV system design and installation.

Question 6

(a) State the application of the following tools and materials used in the maintenance of solar electric systems:

(i) hydrometer;

(ii) voltmeter;

(iii) petroleum Jelly:

(iv) distilled water.

(b) Outline how the following maintenance checks are carried out in batteries and modules.

(i) cleaning of batteries;

(ii) checking and topping up the electrolyte;

(iii) inspecting for dust and cleaning the modules.

(c) In the installation of smaller solar array systems it is recommended that a 2.5 mm² cable be used throughout the installation without compromising the quality of supply. State the conditions of installation necessary for this recommendation to hold true.

Answer

This answer describes the application of common tools and materials in solar system maintenance, outlines maintenance procedures for batteries and modules, and specifies conditions under which 2.5 mm² cable might be suitable throughout a small system.

(a) Applications of Maintenance Tools and Materials

(i) Hydrometer: Used to measure the specific gravity (density relative to water) of the electrolyte in each cell of a *flooded* lead-acid battery. Specific gravity provides an accurate indication of the battery's state of charge and can help identify weak or failing cells.

(ii) Voltmeter: Used to measure the voltage across battery terminals (to estimate state of charge or check for open circuits), the voltage output of solar modules (under load or open circuit), and voltages at various points in the system (charge controller, inverter) for troubleshooting.

(iii) Petroleum Jelly: Applied as a thin coating to cleaned battery terminals and cable clamps *after* they have been securely reconnected. It helps to prevent corrosion by sealing the metal surfaces from air and moisture, ensuring a good, low-resistance electrical connection.

(iv) Distilled Water: Used *exclusively* for topping up the electrolyte level in *flooded* lead-acid batteries. Tap water or mineral water contains impurities that can contaminate the electrolyte and damage the battery plates, reducing performance and lifespan. Distilled or deionised water avoids this contamination.

(b) Battery and Module Maintenance Checks

(i) Cleaning of Batteries:

  1. Ensure the charging source is disconnected or switched off.
  2. Wear appropriate PPE (gloves, eye protection).
  3. Visually inspect the battery casing and terminals for dirt, dust, acid residue, or corrosion.
  4. Clean the battery top and casing with a cloth dampened with a solution of baking soda and water to neutralize any spilled acid, followed by a clean damp cloth, then dry thoroughly. Avoid getting cleaning solution into the battery vents.
  5. If terminals are corroded, disconnect them (Negative first), clean with a wire brush or terminal cleaner, clean the cable clamps similarly, reconnect securely (Positive first), and apply petroleum jelly.

(ii) Checking and Topping Up Electrolyte (Flooded Batteries Only):

  1. Ensure the battery is fully charged if possible, or at least has been resting for a while.
  2. Carefully remove the vent caps or manifold cover (wear PPE).
  3. Visually inspect the electrolyte level in each cell. It should cover the top of the internal plates, typically up to the indicator ring or mark inside the cell opening.
  4. If the level is low, add only distilled or deionized water slowly using a funnel or dedicated filler until the correct level is reached. Do NOT overfill, as the electrolyte volume expands during charging and due to temperature changes.
  5. Replace vent caps securely.

(iii) Inspecting for Dust and Cleaning Modules:

  1. Visually inspect the surface of the solar modules for accumulation of dust, dirt, pollen, bird droppings, or leaves.
  2. If significant soiling is present (which reduces output), cleaning is recommended, preferably during cooler parts of the day (early morning, late evening) to avoid thermal stress on the glass.
  3. Use plain water (or rainwater if available) and a soft brush, cloth, or squeegee. Avoid abrasive cleaners or harsh chemicals.
  4. For stubborn dirt, a mild, non-abrasive detergent (like car wash soap) can be used, followed by thorough rinsing with clean water to prevent residue buildup.
  5. Work safely, especially if accessing modules on a roof. Consider using long-handled tools or professional cleaning services if access is difficult.

(c) Conditions for Using 2.5 mm² Cable Throughout Small Systems

Using a single cable size like 2.5 mm² PVC Twin & Earth (or equivalent DC solar cable) throughout a *small* solar installation might be acceptable under specific, carefully assessed conditions:

  1. Low System Current: The maximum continuous current flowing in *any* part of the circuit (module output, battery charging/discharging, load current) must be well within the rated current carrying capacity of the 2.5 mm² cable, considering all derating factors (temperature, grouping, installation method).
  2. Short Cable Runs: The lengths of all cable runs (module-to-controller, controller-to-battery, battery-to-loads, controller-to-loads) must be short enough to ensure that the voltage drop remains within acceptable limits (typically recommended below 3-5% for critical parts of the circuit) at the maximum operating current.
  3. Appropriate Overcurrent Protection: The protective devices (fuses or circuit breakers) used must have a rating suitable for protecting the 2.5 mm² cable under fault conditions and be lower than the derated current carrying capacity of the cable.
  4. Low Power System: The overall power output of the array and the power demand of the loads must be relatively low, consistent with the current and voltage drop limitations mentioned above. This typically applies to very small lighting systems or single-module setups.

While convenient, using a single cable size is often not optimal. Proper cable sizing calculations based on current, length, voltage drop, and derating factors are always recommended for safety and efficiency, especially for the main array-to-controller and controller-to-battery connections, which often carry the highest currents.

Regular maintenance using the correct tools and procedures is essential for solar system longevity. While simplifying cabling with a single size is tempting in small systems, it must be carefully evaluated against current, voltage drop, and protection requirements.

Question 7

(a) State any two safety measures necessary when installing solar systems in order to avoid shock risks arising from electricity.

(b) Describe the care necessary when handling batteries with respect to:

(i) battery acid;

(ii) location of batteries.

(c) Draw a schematic diagram of two photo voltaic modules supplying the following loads:

(i) 1-television set using dc system;

(ii) 2 -fluorescent luminaries using ac system.

Answer

This answer details safety measures for avoiding shock during solar installation, battery handling precautions concerning acid and location, and provides a schematic for a hybrid AC/DC solar system.

(a) Safety Measures to Avoid Shock Risks During Installation

  1. De-energize Circuits / Isolate Sources: Whenever possible, work on de-energized circuits. Isolate DC circuits using appropriate DC switches or breakers. Isolate AC circuits at the main switch or relevant breaker. Use lock-out/tag-out procedures. Cover exposed live terminals or connectors if they cannot be de-energized. Remember that PV modules generate voltage whenever exposed to light, even if disconnected from loads or batteries – handle module wiring with care.
  2. Use Insulated Tools and Personal Protective Equipment (PPE): Use tools specifically rated for electrical work with insulated handles (e.g., screwdrivers, pliers). Wear appropriate PPE, including insulating gloves (rated for the voltages involved), eye protection (safety glasses/goggles), and appropriate footwear. Avoid wearing metallic jewelry.
  3. Safe Work Practices: Avoid working in wet conditions. Use safe access equipment (ladders, scaffolding) when working at height. Test circuits for voltage before touching using a suitable voltage tester. Maintain awareness of surroundings and potential hazards. Work methodically and avoid rushing.

(b) Care When Handling Batteries

(i) Battery Acid (Electrolyte - primarily for Flooded Lead-Acid):

  • Corrosive Nature: Battery acid (sulfuric acid solution) is highly corrosive and can cause severe burns to skin and eyes, and damage clothing and materials.
  • PPE: Always wear acid-resistant gloves (e.g., neoprene or nitrile), eye protection (goggles or face shield), and protective clothing when handling electrolyte or working on batteries where spills might occur.
  • Neutralization: Keep a neutralizing agent like baking soda (sodium bicarbonate) readily available to neutralize any spills immediately. Flush affected skin or eyes with copious amounts of clean water for at least 15 minutes and seek medical attention.
  • Handling: Avoid splashing. Use appropriate funnels and containers when handling electrolyte or distilled water for topping up.

(ii) Location of Batteries:

  • Ventilation: Lead-acid batteries (especially flooded types) release flammable hydrogen gas during charging. The battery storage area must be well-ventilated to prevent gas accumulation, which could lead to an explosion risk. Avoid installing in sealed enclosures without specific ventilation.
  • Temperature: Battery performance and lifespan are affected by temperature. Avoid locations with extreme heat or cold. A cool, dry location is generally preferred. High temperatures accelerate degradation and self-discharge.
  • Accessibility: Locate batteries where they are accessible for regular inspection, maintenance (cleaning, watering for flooded types), and eventual replacement, but protected from tampering or accidental damage.
  • Protection & Containment: Install batteries in a sturdy, non-conductive, acid-resistant battery box or enclosure, especially if located in living areas or areas accessible to children/pets. Ensure adequate containment for potential spills (for flooded types).
  • Spark Prevention: Keep sparks, flames, and smoking materials away from the battery area due to the risk of hydrogen gas ignition. Use non-sparking tools if possible when working on terminals.

Question 8

(a) Describe the "Photo Electric Effect" of solar arrays.

(b) State four factors that affect the changes in the output of solar cell modules.

(c) With the aid of labelled graphical sketch of the current-voltage characteristic curve (I-V curve) of a typical solar cell module, describe the various changes in the curve at particular points.

Answer

This answer clarifies the relevant effect for solar arrays (photovoltaic), lists factors affecting module output, and explains the features of a typical PV module I-V curve with a graphical sketch.

(a) Relevant Effect for Solar Arrays (Photovoltaic Effect)

The question likely intends to ask about the **Photovoltaic Effect**, which is the principle solar arrays (composed of solar cells) operate on, rather than the Photoelectric Effect.

The Photovoltaic Effect is the generation of a voltage and current across a P-N junction (the basis of a solar cell) when it is exposed to electromagnetic radiation, particularly sunlight. When photons strike the semiconductor material, they excite electrons, creating electron-hole pairs. The internal electric field of the P-N junction separates these charges, creating a potential difference (voltage). If an external circuit is connected, this voltage drives a direct current (DC). This direct conversion of light energy into electrical energy is how solar arrays function.

(The Photoelectric Effect, strictly speaking, is the emission of electrons *from* a material surface when illuminated, which isn't the primary mechanism in a PV cell, although related phenomena occur).

(b) Factors Affecting Solar Cell Module Output Changes

Four key factors influencing the electrical output (power, voltage, current) of a solar module are:

  1. Solar Irradiance (Sunlight Intensity): The amount of solar power hitting the module surface (W/m²). Module current output is almost directly proportional to irradiance. Higher intensity means higher current and thus higher power.
  2. Cell Temperature: As the temperature of the solar cells within the module increases, the output voltage decreases significantly, while the current increases slightly. The overall effect is a reduction in maximum power output (typically 0.3% to 0.5% drop in power per °C rise above 25°C).
  3. Shading: Even partial shading of cells can drastically reduce the output of the entire module or string due to the series connection of cells. Bypass diodes within the module help mitigate this but don't eliminate the loss entirely.
  4. Load Connected / Operating Point: The actual power delivered depends on the electrical load connected. A PV module delivers maximum power only when operating at a specific point on its I-V curve (the Maximum Power Point - MPP). If the load forces operation away from the MPP, the delivered power will be lower. MPPT charge controllers are designed to keep the module operating at its MPP.
  5. (Other factors include angle of incidence, spectrum of light, module degradation over time, and soiling).

(c) Solar Module I-V Characteristic Curve

The I-V curve shows the relationship between the current (I) and voltage (V) output of a PV module under specific conditions of irradiance and temperature.

Solar arrays operate based on the photovoltaic effect. Their output is sensitive to environmental factors like sunlight intensity and temperature. The I-V curve graphically represents the module's operating characteristics and highlights the crucial Maximum Power Point for optimal energy harvesting.
