Question 1

(a) Name two:

(i) types of fire extinguishers and the type of fire they are used on;

(ii) components of a first aid box.

(b) Explain three main causes of electric fires.

(c) Sketch the following electrical joints.

(i) tee joint;

(ii) married joint.

(d) Describe the following methods used in joining electrical conductors.

(i) soldering;

(ii) crimping.

Answer

This answer identifies fire extinguisher types, lists first aid components, explains causes of electrical fires, sketches common joints, and describes soldering and crimping methods.

(a) Fire Extinguishers and First Aid Components

(i) Two Types of Fire Extinguishers and Use:

  • Carbon Dioxide (CO₂): Used primarily for Class B fires (flammable liquids like petrol, oil, paint) and Class E fires (electrical equipment). It works by displacing oxygen and cooling. Identified by a black colour band.
  • Dry Powder (ABC): Used for Class A (wood, paper, textiles), Class B, and Class C (flammable gases) fires. Also safe for use on electrical fires (Class E), though can leave a residue. Works by chemical inhibition of combustion. Identified by a blue colour band.
  • Water: Used primarily for Class A fires (solid combustibles). Works by cooling. **Never** use on electrical fires or flammable liquid fires. Identified by a red colour band (or solid red cylinder).
  • Foam: Used for Class A and Class B fires. Works by cooling and forming a barrier to exclude oxygen. Identified by a cream colour band. Not typically recommended for live electrical fires.

The question asked for two.

(ii) Two Components of a First Aid Box:

  • Sterile Dressings (various sizes) / Adhesive Plasters (various sizes)
  • Bandages (e.g., triangular, crepe roller)
  • Safety Pins
  • Disposable Gloves
  • Antiseptic Wipes / Cleansing Wipes
  • Scissors / Tweezers
  • Eye Wash / Eye Pad Dressings
  • First Aid Guidance Leaflet

The question asked for two.

(b) Three Main Causes of Electric Fires

  1. Overloading Circuits/Cables: Drawing more current through a circuit or cable than it is rated to handle causes overheating of the conductors and insulation. Prolonged overheating can degrade insulation and ignite nearby combustible materials. This often occurs when too many high-power appliances are plugged into one circuit or extension lead.
  2. Faulty Wiring/Connections: Loose connections (at terminals, sockets, switches), damaged insulation (due to age, rodents, impact), or incorrectly sized wiring can create high resistance points or short circuits. High resistance generates localized heat, while short circuits cause very high currents, both capable of starting fires.
  3. Defective Appliances/Equipment: Internal faults within electrical appliances (e.g., malfunctioning thermostats, damaged cords, faulty components) can lead to overheating, short circuits, or sparks, which can ignite the appliance itself or surrounding materials.

(c) Sketches of Electrical Joints

(i) Tee Joint: Used to connect a branch conductor to a main through conductor without cutting the main conductor.

(ii) Married Joint: Used to join the ends of two stranded conductors to form a continuous conductor.

(d) Methods of Joining Electrical Conductors

(i) Soldering: This process involves melting a filler metal alloy (solder, usually tin-lead or lead-free tin alloys) with a low melting point to join two or more metal conductors. The solder flows into the space between the conductors (aided by flux to clean surfaces and promote flow) and solidifies upon cooling, creating a permanent, low-resistance electrical bond and providing mechanical strength. Heat is applied using a soldering iron, torch, or solder pot.

(ii) Crimping: This is a mechanical method using a specialized tool (crimping tool) and connector (crimp terminal, lug, or splice). The stripped conductor is inserted into the barrel of the connector, and the tool applies precise, high pressure to deform the barrel and conductor together, forming a secure, cold-welded connection. Different die sets in the tool are used for different connector and wire sizes to ensure a correct, gas-tight crimp that provides good electrical conductivity and mechanical strength without heat.

Understanding fire safety, first aid basics, common causes of electrical hazards, and correct methods for joining conductors are fundamental aspects of electrical installation work.

Question 2

(a) State two:

(i) IEE regulations requirement regarding ceiling roses;

(ii) advantages of wiring socket outlets in ring.

(b) Outline four installation tests carried out in a completed installation.

(c) With aid of a labelled diagram, describe the sequence of control equipment at consumer's intake point.

(d) Explain two factors considered when siting a hydro-electric power station.

Answer

This answer outlines regulatory requirements for ceiling roses, advantages of ring circuits, lists key installation tests, describes the consumer intake sequence, and explains factors for siting hydro stations.

(a) Ceiling Roses and Ring Circuits

(i) Two IEE Regulations Requirements Regarding Ceiling Roses (BS 7671 Principles):

  • Terminal Capacity & Identification: Terminals within a ceiling rose must be suitable for connecting the number and size of conductors required (e.g., for looping). Terminals should be arranged or marked to ensure correct polarity is maintained, particularly distinguishing the switched line from the permanent line and neutral. (Ref: Regulation Group 526).
  • Cable Support & Enclosure: The ceiling rose must provide adequate support for the luminaire flexible cord and enclose all live terminations, preventing accidental contact. It should be suitable for the installation environment. (Ref: Regulation 526.6, Chapter 55).
  • Weight Limits: Standard ceiling roses have limitations on the weight of the luminaire they can safely support (typically around 5kg unless specifically designed for heavier loads). Heavier fittings require independent support. (Ref: Regulation 559.6).

The question asked for two.

(ii) Two Advantages of Wiring Socket Outlets in Ring (Ring Final Circuits):

  • Reduced Cable Size/Cost: Because the load is fed from both directions around the ring, the current in each leg of the ring cable is roughly halved compared to a radial circuit supplying the same total load. This allows a smaller cable size (typically 2.5mm²) protected by a larger protective device (typically 32A) to be used compared to what would be needed for a 32A radial, saving on copper cost.
  • Increased Reliability/Continuity: If there is a break in the ring cable conductor (but not a short circuit), the sockets on the ring will generally continue to function, being fed from the unbroken direction (though the circuit effectively becomes two radials and overload protection might be compromised).
  • Flexibility: Allows a large number of sockets to be conveniently installed over a wide area (up to 100m² floor area typically) on a single circuit.

The question asked for two.

(b) Four Installation Tests (Completed Installation - Dead & Live Tests)

Common tests sequence includes:

  1. Continuity of Protective Conductors (Dead Test): Verifies that all Circuit Protective Conductors (CPCs), main and supplementary bonding conductors have low resistance and are electrically continuous back to the Main Earthing Terminal (MET). Ensures the safety path exists.
  2. Continuity of Ring Final Circuit Conductors (Dead Test): Confirms that the phase, neutral, and protective conductors of a ring circuit are continuous loops with no breaks or cross-connections.
  3. Insulation Resistance Test (Dead Test): Measures the resistance between live conductors (L-N) and between live conductors and Earth (L-E, N-E). High resistance values (Megaohms) confirm that the insulation is intact and there are no short circuits or leakage paths.
  4. Polarity Test (Dead or Live Test): Checks that single-pole switches/protective devices are in the phase conductor, and checks correct connections within accessories like sockets and lampholders.
  5. Earth Fault Loop Impedance (Zs) Test (Live Test): Measures the impedance of the entire earth fault path to ensure it's low enough for the protective device to operate within the required time under fault conditions.
  6. Prospective Fault Current (PFC/PSC) Test (Live Test): Measures the maximum current likely to flow under short circuit (L-N) or earth fault (L-E) conditions. Ensures protective devices have adequate breaking capacity.
  7. RCD Test (Live Test): Verifies that any Residual Current Devices operate correctly within specified time limits when subjected to test currents (e.g., at 1x and 5x their rated residual current IΔn).

The question asked for four.

(c) Sequence of Control Equipment at Consumer's Intake Point

(d) Two Factors Considered When Siting a Hydro-electric Power Station

  • Water Availability and Head: Requires a reliable, consistent, and sufficient flow rate of water (from a river or large catchment area). Crucially, requires a significant difference in height (Head) between the water source (reservoir level) and the turbine level, as potential energy (proportional to head) is converted to electricity. Higher head generally means more power generation potential for a given flow rate.
  • Geological Stability and Topography: The site needs stable ground conditions suitable for constructing a large dam, powerhouse, and associated structures. The natural topography should ideally lend itself to creating a reservoir with minimal dam construction (e.g., a narrow gorge downstream of a wider valley). Needs to be geologically sound to withstand the pressure of the stored water and potential seismic activity.
  • Environmental and Social Impact: Consideration of the impact on the existing ecosystem (fish migration, habitats), land use (submergence of land for the reservoir), potential displacement of communities, and effects on downstream water flow and sediment transport.
  • Accessibility and Proximity to Grid/Load: Reasonable access is needed for construction and maintenance. While power can be transmitted long distances, proximity to the existing high-voltage grid reduces transmission line costs and losses. Proximity to load centers can also be a factor, though less critical than for other types of power plants.

The question asked for two.

Adherence to regulations for wiring accessories, understanding circuit configurations like rings, performing thorough installation tests, and considering geographical factors for power station siting are all important aspects of electrical installation and power systems.

Question 3

(a) Outline four precautions to be observed when charging lead acid batteries.

(b) With aid of a labelled diagram, describe the constant voltage charging method of charging a lead acid battery.

(c) (i) State four IEE regulations requirement regarding bell transformers.

(ii) Draw a wiring diagram of a bell controlled by a relay circuit.

Answer

This answer outlines precautions for charging lead-acid batteries, describes constant voltage charging, lists regulatory requirements for bell transformers, and provides a wiring diagram for a relay-controlled bell.

(a) Precautions When Charging Lead Acid Batteries

  1. Ensure Good Ventilation: Charge batteries in a well-ventilated area. Lead-acid batteries release flammable hydrogen gas during charging, which can accumulate and create an explosion hazard if confined.
  2. No Smoking, Sparks, or Open Flames: Keep all sources of ignition away from the charging area due to the presence of hydrogen gas.
  3. Wear Personal Protective Equipment (PPE): Use eye protection (goggles/face shield) and acid-resistant gloves to protect against potential splashes of corrosive sulfuric acid electrolyte.
  4. Check Connections and Polarity: Ensure the charger leads are connected correctly to the battery terminals (Positive to Positive, Negative to Negative). Reverse polarity can damage the battery and charger. Ensure connections are clean and tight.
  5. Monitor Charging Process: Avoid excessive overcharging. Monitor battery temperature (should not get excessively hot) and check electrolyte levels (for flooded types) before and after charging, topping up with distilled water *after* charging if necessary (as levels rise during charging). Use appropriate charger settings for the battery type and size.

The question asked for four.

(b) Constant Voltage Charging Method

In this method, the charger maintains a constant, specific voltage across the battery terminals. The charging current is initially high when the battery is discharged and gradually tapers off as the battery's internal voltage rises and approaches the charger's set voltage.

(c) Bell Transformers and Relay Control

(i) Four IEE Regulations Requirements Regarding Bell Transformers (SELV/PELV):

(Similar to Q2(c)(i) from 2016j paper)

  1. Source Requirement: Must be a safety isolating transformer complying with relevant standards (e.g., BS EN 61558-2-6), providing at least simple separation (often reinforced for SELV) between primary and secondary windings. (Ref: Regulation 414.3).
  2. Voltage Limitation: The nominal secondary voltage must not exceed the ELV limits (50V AC or 120V ripple-free DC). (Ref: Regulation 414.1).
  3. Circuit Separation (SELV): For SELV circuits, the secondary winding and the entire low-voltage circuit must be electrically separated from earth and from other circuits (including PELV and other SELV circuits). (Ref: Regulation 414.4.1).
  4. Protection Against Direct Contact (SELV/PELV): Although the voltage is low, basic protection (insulation or barriers/enclosures) is still generally required for ELV circuits unless the voltage is below 25V AC / 60V DC and used in dry conditions where contact is unlikely. (Ref: Regulation 414.4.5).
  5. Earthing (PELV): For PELV (Protective Extra Low Voltage), the secondary circuit *is* earthed. (Ref: Regulation 411.7). Bell circuits are more often SELV.

The question asked for four.

(ii) Wiring Diagram of Bell Controlled by a Relay Circuit:

This allows a low-voltage/low-current switch (like a push button) to control a higher-power or mains-voltage bell, or allows multiple switches to control one bell easily.

Safe battery charging requires attention to ventilation, ignition sources, and connections. Constant voltage charging controls voltage while current tapers. Bell transformers provide safe low voltages, and relays allow low-power circuits to control higher-power devices like bells.

Question 4

(a) Explain how the following factors influence the choice of a wiring system:

(i) type of building;

(ii) flexibility;

(iii) appearance.

(b) State two advantages of using

(i) PVC conduits over steel conduits;

(ii) trunking over conduits.

(c) With aid of a labelled diagram, describe the catenary wiring system.

(d) Outline four safety precautions observed when installing base conductor wiring systems.

Answer

This answer explains how building type, flexibility, and appearance influence wiring system choice, lists advantages of PVC conduit and trunking, describes catenary wiring, and outlines safety precautions for bare conductor systems (interpreting 'base' as 'bare').

(a) Factors Influencing Choice of Wiring System

(i) Type of Building: The building's function and construction materials heavily influence the choice.

  • Domestic: Often uses PVC Twin & Earth clipped direct or in PVC conduit/trunking for cost-effectiveness and ease of installation. Concealed wiring is common for aesthetics.
  • Industrial: Requires robust systems offering high mechanical protection, like steel conduit, steel trunking, or armoured cables, due to harsher environments and heavier machinery.
  • Commercial (Offices): May use trunking (perimeter, dado), underfloor systems, or conduit for flexibility and data/power integration. Aesthetics are important.
  • Agricultural/Damp Areas: Require systems with high IP ratings and corrosion resistance (e.g., PVC conduit, specific glands).

(ii) Flexibility: Refers to the ease with which the installation can be altered, extended, or circuits added later.

  • High Flexibility: Systems like surface trunking or accessible conduit allow easy addition or rerouting of cables. Underfloor or raised floor systems offer high flexibility for office layouts.
  • Low Flexibility: Systems like cables embedded directly in plaster or concealed conduit within solid walls are difficult and disruptive to modify.
The anticipated future needs of the building occupants determine the required level of flexibility.

(iii) Appearance (Aesthetics): How the wiring system looks after installation is important, especially in domestic, commercial, and public buildings.

  • Concealed Systems: Embedding conduits in walls/floors or running cables in ceiling voids provides the best appearance but is more costly and less flexible.
  • Surface Systems: Systems like PVC trunking, surface conduit, or clipped cables are more visible. While functional, careful planning and neat installation are needed to achieve an acceptable appearance. Dado trunking or skirting trunking can integrate wiring neatly at low levels.

(b) Advantages of PVC Conduits and Trunking

(i) Two Advantages of PVC Conduits over Steel Conduits:

  • Corrosion Resistance: PVC is resistant to rust and many chemicals, making it suitable for damp or corrosive environments where steel would degrade unless specially treated/coated.
  • Lighter Weight & Easier Installation: PVC is significantly lighter than steel, making it easier to handle, transport, and support. It is also generally easier to cut and join (using solvent cement) than threading or coupling steel conduit.
  • Cost: PVC conduit and fittings are typically less expensive than their galvanized steel counterparts.
  • Insulating Properties: PVC is an electrical insulator, eliminating the need for supplementary bonding across joints for earth continuity (though a separate CPC must always be run inside).

The question asked for two.

(ii) Two Advantages of Trunking over Conduits:

  • Ease of Access and Wiring: Cables are laid into trunking rather than pulled through, which is often quicker and easier, especially for multiple cables or complex routes. The removable lid provides easy access for inspection, modification, or adding cables later.
  • Higher Cable Capacity: Trunking generally offers more space and can accommodate a larger number and size of cables within a single run compared to standard conduit sizes, simplifying cable management for areas with high circuit density.

(See also Q1(d)(ii) for other advantages).

(c) Catenary Wiring System

This is a wiring system used for overhead distribution of cables, typically across large open areas like industrial workshops, hangars, or outdoor spaces. It uses a strong, tensioned support wire (the catenary wire), usually galvanized steel, stretched between structural supports (walls, stanchions).

(d) Safety Precautions for Bare Conductor Wiring Systems

(Assuming 'base' refers to 'bare' conductor systems, like overhead lines or busbar systems without full insulation/enclosure accessible to unqualified persons - these are rare inside buildings accessible to the public due to inherent dangers).

  1. Restricted Access & Warnings: Ensure the bare conductors are installed only in locations completely inaccessible to untrained/unauthorized persons (e.g., dedicated high-voltage switchyards, locked electrical rooms, sufficient height for overhead lines). Clear warning signs indicating electrical danger must be prominently displayed.
  2. Adequate Clearances: Maintain mandated safety clearances between bare conductors and surrounding structures (buildings, trees, ground), other services, and potential human activity (e.g., vehicle movement below overhead lines) to prevent accidental contact or flashover.
  3. Secure Mounting and Insulation: Use appropriate, high-quality insulators (e.g., porcelain, glass, polymer) to securely support the bare conductors and prevent current leakage to the support structures (poles, towers, busbar supports). Ensure supports are mechanically sound.
  4. Isolation Procedures: Implement strict lock-out/tag-out procedures and safe systems of work, including voltage testing, before any maintenance is performed on or near bare conductor systems. Ensure reliable means of isolation are available and clearly identified.
  5. Earthing: Ensure associated metal structures (poles, towers, enclosures) are effectively earthed to provide protection under fault conditions or due to induced voltages.

If 'base conductor' meant busbar trunking (which has internal conductors, often bare but enclosed), then precautions relate more to ensuring the enclosure is intact, correctly earthed, tap-off points are secure, and access covers are only removed by authorized personnel after isolation.

Choosing a wiring system involves balancing practical needs like protection and flexibility with cost and aesthetics. PVC conduit offers corrosion resistance over steel, while trunking provides better access than conduit. Catenary systems efficiently span large open areas. Bare conductor systems require stringent safety measures focused on preventing contact through restricted access and maintaining safe clearances.

Question 5

(a) Distinguish between class P and class Q type of fuses.

(b) (i) Draw a labelled diagram of a cartridge fuse.

Answer

This answer distinguishes between Class P and Q fuses, illustrates a cartridge fuse with colour codes, outlines the purpose of earthing and exceptions, and diagrams the components of an earthing system.

(a) Distinction Between Class P and Class Q Fuses (BS 88)

Class P and Class Q refer to categories within fuse-links primarily designed for protecting power semiconductors (like diodes, thyristors) which have very low thermal withstand capacity. They are characterized by extremely fast operation under fault conditions.

  • Class P (Protection): Offers specified performance characteristics for both overload and short-circuit conditions. They provide full-range protection, meaning they can interrupt any current from their minimum fusing current up to their rated breaking capacity.
  • Class Q (Quick / Semiconductor): Generally refers to fuses optimized primarily for extremely fast short-circuit protection of semiconductors, often designated as type aR or gR/gS within IEC/BS standards.
    • Type aR (Partial Range): Designed for short-circuit protection only. They must be protected against overloads by another device (like a circuit breaker or a gG fuse).
    • Type gR/gS (Full Range): Provide both overload and extremely fast short-circuit protection for semiconductors.

Distinction Summary: The key distinction lies in the range of protection. Class P implies full-range (overload and short-circuit) protection with specific performance characteristics suitable for certain applications, while Class Q often relates specifically to the extremely fast short-circuit protection needed for semiconductors (either partial range 'aR' or full range 'gR/gS'). Usage depends on the specific requirements of the circuit and the device being protected.

(c) Purpose of Earthing and Exceptions

(i) Purpose for Earthing: Primarily for safety, to protect against electric shock by providing a low-impedance path for fault current to flow to earth if exposed metalwork becomes live. This ensures rapid operation of protective devices (fuses, MCBs, RCDs) to disconnect the supply. It also helps stabilize system voltage and provides protection against lightning and static discharge.

(ii) Three Parts That Need Not Be Earthed (Exemptions in BS 7671):

  • Short isolated lengths of metal conduit or trunking used merely for mechanical protection of cables, not exceeding a certain length (e.g., 2 meters, check current regs) and unlikely to come into contact with live parts or other earthed metalwork.
  • Small metallic accessories like screws, labels, or nameplates that are isolated and unlikely to become live or be touched simultaneously with live parts or earthed metal.
  • Exposed-conductive-parts of equipment supplied by a SELV (Separated Extra Low Voltage) source, as the voltage is considered too low to present a shock hazard under normal and fault conditions (provided the SELV system integrity is maintained).
  • Double-insulated or Class II equipment (marked with a square within a square symbol). These appliances are designed with both basic and supplementary insulation, such that no single fault can cause accessible metal parts (if any) to become live. They do not require an earth connection.

Earthing is a fundamental safety measure, though specific small or double-insulated parts may be exempt. A complete earthing system involves multiple interconnected components providing a path to earth.

Question 6

(a) Define the following terms as used in solar systems

(i) radiance;

(ii) solar constant

(b) With aid of a labelled diagram, explain how solar energy is harvested using a parabolic dish for thermal use.

(c) Name four measuring instruments used in solar photo-voltaic (PV) systems.

(d) Draw a solar PV module current-voltage (I-V) curve and on it label and explain the following parts

(i) Isc

(ii) Pm

(iii) Voc

Answer

This answer defines solar radiance and constant, explains parabolic dish operation, lists PV measurement instruments, and illustrates/explains a PV module I-V curve.

(a) Solar Terminology Definitions

(i) Radiance (Solar Irradiance): The amount of solar power received per unit area on a surface, typically measured in Watts per square meter (W/m²). It represents the intensity of sunlight at a particular moment and location. It varies with time of day, season, weather conditions, and atmospheric clarity.

(ii) Solar Constant: The average solar irradiance (power per unit area) received on a surface perpendicular to the sun's rays outside the Earth's atmosphere at the mean Earth-Sun distance. Its accepted value is approximately 1361 W/m². This value is relatively constant, though it has minor variations.

(b) Parabolic Dish Solar Harvesting (Thermal Use)

A parabolic dish uses a large, reflective dish with a parabolic shape to concentrate incoming parallel sunlight rays onto a receiver located at the dish's focal point.

(c) Four Measuring Instruments Used in Solar PV Systems

  1. Multimeter (Digital): Essential for measuring DC Voltage (Voc, Vmp, battery voltage, system voltage), DC Current (Isc, Imp, charging/load current - often requires clamp attachment for higher currents), and Resistance/Continuity (for wiring checks).
  2. Insulation Resistance Tester (Megohmmeter): Used to measure the insulation resistance between DC conductors and earth, verifying the integrity of module and wiring insulation (crucial safety test).
  3. Clamp Meter (AC/DC): Allows measurement of current (AC or DC) by clamping around a conductor without breaking the circuit. Useful for measuring array string currents, battery charge/discharge currents, and AC load currents from an inverter.
  4. Solar Irradiance Meter (Pyranometer): Measures the intensity of solar radiation (W/m²) currently falling on a surface. Used to assess available solar resource and compare actual PV system output to expected output under measured conditions.
  5. Thermometer / Temperature Probe: Used to measure ambient temperature and, more importantly, PV module/cell temperature, as temperature significantly affects module voltage and power output. Often integrated with irradiance meters or PV analysers.

The question asked for four.

(d) Solar PV Module I-V Curve

Solar radiance and the solar constant quantify solar energy. Parabolic dishes concentrate this energy for high-temperature thermal applications. Various instruments are used to test and commission PV systems. The I-V curve is fundamental to understanding a PV module's electrical characteristics, including its maximum power point (Pm), short circuit current (Isc), and open circuit voltage (Voc).

Question 7

(a) Explain the following as used in solar systems

(i) photo-voltaic effect;

(ii) tracking.

(b) Describe the following types of PV module solar cell constructions

(i) mono-crystalline;

(ii) poly-crystalline

(c) (i) With aid of diagrams, explain how stratification occurs in batteries.

(ii) State two ways of minimizing the effect in (c) (i).

(d) With aid of a labelled diagram, explain the operation of a shunt type charge controller.

Answer

This answer explains the photovoltaic effect and solar tracking, describes mono- and polycrystalline cells, discusses battery stratification and mitigation, and details the operation of a shunt charge controller.

(a) Solar System Terminology

(i) Photovoltaic (PV) Effect: The phenomenon where light energy (photons) striking a suitable semiconductor material (like silicon in a solar cell's P-N junction) creates electron-hole pairs, which are then separated by the material's internal electric field, generating a voltage difference and enabling direct current flow when an external circuit is connected. It's the direct conversion of light into electricity.

(ii) Tracking (Solar Tracking): A system used to orient solar collectors (PV panels or thermal collectors) towards the sun as its position changes throughout the day (and sometimes seasonally). Trackers move the collectors to keep them aimed directly at the sun, maximizing the amount of incident solar radiation and thus increasing the energy harvested compared to fixed-mount systems. Trackers can be single-axis (following East-to-West movement) or dual-axis (following both East-to-West and altitude changes).

(b) PV Module Solar Cell Constructions

(i) Mono-crystalline: Cells made from wafers sliced from a single, continuous, high-purity silicon crystal ingot grown in a laboratory. They have a uniform crystal structure, resulting in a smooth, even dark color (often black or very dark blue) and typically the highest efficiency among common silicon cell types. Modules often have cells with cut/chamfered corners due to the cylindrical shape of the original ingot.

(ii) Poly-crystalline (or Multi-crystalline): Cells made from wafers sliced from blocks of silicon that were formed by melting multiple silicon fragments together and allowing them to solidify. The resulting wafer contains many individual silicon crystals (grains). This gives the cells a characteristic "flaked" or "marbled" appearance with visible grain boundaries, usually in varying shades of blue. They are generally less efficient but also less expensive to produce than monocrystalline cells.

(c) Battery Stratification

(i) Explanation and Diagram:

Stratification occurs primarily in *flooded* lead-acid batteries when they are regularly undercharged or only subjected to shallow charge/discharge cycles. The electrolyte (sulfuric acid solution) is a mixture of water and sulfuric acid. During discharge, acid is consumed and water is produced near the plates. During charge, acid is produced. Sulfuric acid is denser than water. Without sufficient mixing (caused by gassing during a full charge), the heavier acid tends to settle at the bottom of the battery cell, while the lighter water concentration increases towards the top.

(ii) Two Ways of Minimizing Stratification:

  1. Regular Full Charging / Equalization Charge: Ensuring the battery regularly receives a full charge, reaching the gassing stage, promotes mixing of the electrolyte through the bubbling action. Periodically performing a controlled overcharge (Equalization charge, as recommended by the manufacturer) specifically aims to mix the electrolyte and balance cell voltages, counteracting stratification and sulfation.
  2. Using AGM or Gel Batteries: Absorbed Glass Mat (AGM) and Gel VRLA (Valve Regulated Lead Acid) batteries immobilize the electrolyte (AGM holds it in fiberglass mats, Gel suspends it in a silica gel). This physical immobilization prevents the significant settling of acid, making stratification much less of an issue compared to flooded batteries.
  3. Pulse Charging / Mixing Systems: Some advanced charging systems or external devices introduce electrical pulses or physical mixing (e.g., air bubbling) specifically designed to stir the electrolyte and prevent stratification.

The question asked for two.

(d) Shunt Type Charge Controller Operation

A shunt charge controller regulates battery charging by diverting (shunting) excess current from the PV array away from the battery once the battery reaches its target regulation voltage. It essentially short-circuits or dissipates the excess PV power.

The photovoltaic effect is key to solar electricity. Tracking increases energy yield. Cell construction affects efficiency and cost. Battery stratification impacts flooded lead-acid performance and requires specific maintenance like equalization. Shunt controllers regulate charging by diverting excess PV power.

Question 8

(a) State three maintenance and servicing procedures carried out on the following PV solar systems to ensure their longevity.

(i) Solar battery

(ii) PV module

(b) Outline three:

(i) tools and materials required when servicing and maintaining solar PV batteries.

(ii) types of information required during trouble shooting and maintenance of solar systems.

(c) Describe the following accessories used in PV solar systems.

(i) Switches;

(ii) Socket outlets;

(iii) Circuit breakers.

(d) A 12 V DC domestic PV solar electric system has three 10 W lamps and 30 W black and white television. Determine the:

(i) size of fuse required;

(ii) ampere hour consumed if the daily use is 3 hours.

(Assume system losses are 15%)

Answer

This answer details maintenance procedures for batteries and modules, lists required tools/materials and information for servicing, describes common PV system accessories, and calculates fuse size and energy consumption for a small DC system.

(a) Maintenance Procedures for Longevity

(i) Solar Battery (Lead-Acid):

  1. Regular Cleaning: Keep battery tops and terminals clean from dust, dirt, and corrosion to prevent self-discharge paths and ensure good connections. Neutralize any spilled acid with baking soda solution.
  2. Check & Maintain Electrolyte Levels (Flooded types): Regularly check fluid levels and top up *only* with distilled/deionized water after charging to keep plates covered. Avoid overfilling.
  3. Ensure Full Recharge & Equalization: Prevent chronic undercharging which leads to sulfation. Ensure the system allows for regular full charges. Perform periodic equalization charges (controlled overcharge, per manufacturer guidance) for flooded batteries to reverse mild sulfation and balance cells.
  4. Check Connections: Periodically ensure all terminal connections are tight and clean. Apply petroleum jelly after cleaning/tightening to prevent corrosion.

The question asked for three.

(ii) PV Module:

  1. Regular Cleaning: Keep the module glass surface clean from dust, dirt, bird droppings, etc., as soiling significantly reduces output. Clean with water and a soft brush/cloth.
  2. Visual Inspection: Regularly check for physical damage like cracked glass, frame damage, backsheet degradation, water ingress into junction box, or signs of overheating (discoloration).
  3. Check Mounting Integrity: Ensure the module is securely fastened to the mounting structure and that the structure itself is sound and free from excessive corrosion or damage.
  4. Inspect Wiring & Connections: Check that wiring from the junction box is secure, undamaged, and connectors (e.g., MC4) are properly mated and sealed.

The question asked for three.

(b) Servicing Requirements

(i) Three Tools and Materials for Battery Servicing:

  • Tools: Insulated Spanners/Wrenches (for terminals), Wire Brush/Terminal Cleaner, Voltmeter (Multimeter), Hydrometer (for flooded), Funnel (for watering).
  • Materials: Distilled/Deionized Water, Baking Soda (for neutralizing spills), Petroleum Jelly/Anti-corrosion Grease, Clean Cloths.
  • PPE: Acid-resistant Gloves, Eye Protection (Goggles/Face shield).

The question asked for three combining tools/materials.

(ii) Three Types of Information Required for Troubleshooting/Maintenance:

  • System Documentation: Wiring diagrams (schematics), component manuals (for modules, controller, inverter, battery), installation records (commissioning data, test results).
  • Component Specifications: Datasheets showing ratings like module Voc/Isc/Pmax, battery capacity/voltage/charge limits, controller settings, inverter ratings.
  • Performance History / Monitoring Data: Records of past energy production, battery voltage cycles, charge controller logs (if available), previous maintenance actions, and details of the fault observed.
  • Environmental Conditions: Current and recent weather data (sunlight levels, temperature) can help diagnose performance issues.

The question asked for three.

(c) Description of PV System Accessories

(i) Switches (DC Isolators): Used to safely disconnect parts of the DC circuit for maintenance or isolation. Common locations include between the PV array and charge controller, between the controller and battery, and sometimes between the battery and DC loads or inverter. They must be specifically rated for DC voltage and current, as DC arcs are harder to extinguish than AC arcs.

(ii) Socket Outlets: Provide connection points for DC or AC loads. DC outlets must be rated for the system's DC voltage and current and are often different types (e.g., cigarette lighter style, Anderson plugs, specific DC plugs) to prevent accidental connection of AC devices. AC outlets are standard mains sockets powered via an inverter.

(iii) Circuit Breakers (DC): Overcurrent protective devices used instead of or alongside fuses in DC circuits (array, battery, load lines). They interrupt the circuit automatically if current exceeds a set limit due to overload or short circuit. Like DC switches, they must be specifically rated for DC operation at the system voltage, as interrupting DC faults is more demanding than AC.

(d) System Calculation

System Voltage = 12 V DC

Loads: 3 x 10 W lamps + 1 x 30 W TV

Total Load Power (Pload) = (3 * 10 W) + 30 W = 30 W + 30 W = 60 W

(i) Size of Fuse Required:

First, find the total operating current (Iload):

Iload = Pload / V = 60 W / 12 V = 5 A

Fuses protect the wiring and handle potential surges or faults. A fuse rating should be higher than the normal operating current but low enough to protect the cable. A standard practice is to size the fuse somewhat above the operating current, considering potential small surges but ensuring it blows before cable damage under fault. Common fuse ratings are 3A, 5A, 7A, 10A, 13A etc. for smaller DC circuits.

Since the operating current is exactly 5A, a 5A fuse might blow unnecessarily on startup or minor fluctuations. The next standard size up would typically be suitable.

Required Fuse Size: Typically 7 A or 10 A fuse would be appropriate, depending on availability and wiring size used (must protect the wire). Let's select 7 A as a reasonable choice above 5A.

Note: Cabling must be adequately sized for the fuse chosen (e.g., 1.5mm² cable is typically fine for 10A at 12V over short distances).

(ii) Ampere Hour Consumed (Daily Use = 3 hours):

First, calculate the total daily energy consumed by the loads (Energyload):

Energyload = Pload * Time = 60 W * 3 hours = 180 Wh/day

Now, consider the system losses (15%). This means the energy drawn *from the battery* (Energybatt) must be higher to account for losses in wiring, controller (if load connected via controller), etc.

Energybatt = Energyload / (1 - Loss Fraction)

Energybatt = 180 Wh / (1 - 0.15) = 180 Wh / 0.85 ≈ 211.8 Wh/day

Finally, convert this energy drawn from the battery to Ampere-hours (Ahconsumed):

Ahconsumed = Energybatt / V = 211.8 Wh / 12 V ≈ 17.65 Ah/day

Ampere hours consumed ≈ 17.7 Ah/day.

Regular maintenance is key to PV system longevity. Proper tools, documentation, and understanding of components like switches and breakers are necessary for servicing. Basic calculations allow determination of fuse sizes and daily energy consumption, forming the basis for system design.
