Answer

This answer covers reasons for polarity testing, instruments/readings, voltmeter/ammeter connections, closed-circuit alarms, and battery charging precautions/methods.

(a) Polarity Test Reasons, Instruments, and Readings

(i) Three Reasons for Verification of Polarity Test:**

  1. Safety During Operation/Maintenance: To ensure that single-pole switches, fuses, and circuit breakers are connected only in the line (phase) conductor. This guarantees that operating the switch/device isolates the appliance or circuit from the live supply, preventing shock risk during tasks like changing a lamp.
  2. Correct Device Operation: To ensure that protective devices (fuses, MCBs) operate correctly by interrupting the flow of current in the phase conductor under fault conditions.
  3. Appliance Safety & Function: To ensure correct connections within accessories, such as verifying the centre contact of Edison Screw (ES) lampholders is connected to the phase conductor and the outer screw to neutral, reducing the risk of accidental contact with live parts when inserting/removing a lamp. Also ensures correct operation of appliances sensitive to polarity.

(ii) Instruments Used and Expected Readings:**

  • Instrument(s):
    • For **Dead Testing:** Low-resistance ohmmeter or Continuity Tester.
    • For **Live Testing:** Approved Voltage Indicator (AVI) or Test Lamp conforming to safety standards (e.g., GS38).
  • Expected Readings (Examples using Dead Test Method - see Q4(c) 2015j for procedure):
    • **Continuity Test (Low Ohms):** Expected between the Line conductor at the origin (linked to Earth temporarily) and the Line terminal(s) of switches, the centre contact of ES lampholders, or the Line terminal of socket outlets.
    • **No Continuity (High Resistance / Open Circuit):** Expected between the Line conductor at the origin (linked to Earth) and any Neutral terminal or the outer contact of an ES lampholder.
  • Expected Readings (Examples using Live Test Method - see Q1(c) 2016j for procedure):**
    • **Full Voltage:** Expected between Line and Earth, and between Switched Line (when ON) and Earth.
    • **Zero/Near Zero Voltage:** Expected between Neutral and Earth, and between Switched Line (when OFF) and Earth.

Answer

This answer covers pros/cons of nuclear power, diagrams a hydro plant, outlines consumer intake regulations, distinguishes ring/radial circuits, and provides a loop-in wiring diagram for the house layout.

(a) Nuclear Power Plant Advantages and Disadvantages

Two Advantages:

  • Low Greenhouse Gas Emissions: Nuclear fission does not directly produce COâ‚‚ or other greenhouse gases during operation, making it a low-carbon source of electricity helpful in combating climate change.
  • High Power Density / Land Use Efficiency: Nuclear power plants generate a very large amount of energy from a small amount of fuel (uranium) and typically require less land area per unit of energy produced compared to some renewables like solar farms or large hydro reservoirs.
  • Reliable Base Load Power: Nuclear plants can operate continuously for long periods (1-2 years between refueling) at high capacity factors, providing a reliable source of base-load electricity independent of weather conditions.

Two Disadvantages:

  • Radioactive Waste Management: The process produces long-lasting, highly radioactive waste (spent fuel) that requires complex, secure, and very long-term storage and disposal solutions, posing environmental and security challenges.
  • High Capital Costs and Construction Time: Nuclear power plants are extremely expensive to build and take many years (often over a decade) to construct due to complex engineering and stringent safety requirements.
  • Safety Concerns / Accident Risk: Although rare, severe accidents (like Chernobyl, Fukushima) can have catastrophic and long-lasting environmental and health consequences. Public perception regarding safety remains a significant issue.
  • Nuclear Proliferation Risk: The technology and materials involved in nuclear power can potentially be diverted for military purposes, raising concerns about the proliferation of nuclear weapons.

(ii) Distinction Between Ring and Radial Final Circuits (for Sockets):**

  • Radial Circuit: The circuit cable runs directly from the protective device (MCB/fuse) in the consumer unit to the first socket outlet, then potentially "daisy-chains" to subsequent sockets in a line, ending at the last socket. The cable does *not* return to the consumer unit. Current flows only one way along the cable.
  • Ring Circuit (Ring Final Circuit): The circuit cable starts from the protective device, runs sequentially through all the connected socket outlets, and then returns back to *the same* protective device terminals in the consumer unit, forming a closed loop or ring. Current can flow in both directions around the ring to supply the connected loads.

Key Difference: Radial circuits have one path from the consumer unit; Ring circuits have two parallel paths forming a loop back to the consumer unit.

(ii) Explain the effect of heat on the following materials;

(I) pure metals;

(II) alloys;

(III) carbon and electrolytes.

(b) Explain the following methods of joining electrical conductors:

(i) soldering bit;

(ii) pot and laddle.

(c) Figure 2 shows the layout of steel conduit installations:

(i) Identify the parts labelled A, B , C, D and E.

(ii) Outline the procedure of installation the steel conduit system in Figure 2.

Answer

This answer defines ambient temperature, explains heat effects on materials, describes soldering methods, identifies conduit fittings, and outlines steel conduit installation procedure.

(a) Ambient Temperature and Heat Effects

(i) Ambient Temperature Definition:**

The temperature of the surrounding medium (usually air, but could be water or soil) in the immediate vicinity of an electrical component or cable, before the component itself generates any heat due to its operation. It's the baseline temperature influencing heat dissipation.

(ii) Effect of Heat on Materials:**

  • (I) Pure Metals (e.g., Copper, Aluminium): Generally, increasing temperature increases the electrical resistance of pure metals due to increased thermal vibration of the atoms hindering electron flow (positive temperature coefficient of resistance). Heat also causes thermal expansion and can eventually lead to softening or melting at high enough temperatures.
  • (II) Alloys: The effect of heat on resistance varies depending on the specific alloy. Some alloys (like Manganin, Constantan) are designed to have a very low temperature coefficient of resistance, making them suitable for precision resistors. Other alloys behave similarly to pure metals (resistance increases with temperature). Thermal expansion occurs, and melting points depend on the alloy composition.
  • (III) Carbon and Electrolytes: These generally exhibit a *negative* temperature coefficient of resistance, meaning their electrical resistance *decreases* as temperature increases (within certain limits). For carbon (like in motor brushes), higher temperature increases charge carrier availability. For electrolytes (like in batteries), higher temperature increases ion mobility, reducing resistance. However, excessive heat can cause degradation, boiling (electrolytes), or unwanted chemical reactions.

(b) Methods of Joining Electrical Conductors (Soldering)

(i) Soldering Bit (Soldering Iron):**

Using a hand-held tool (soldering iron) with an electrically heated metal tip (the bit). The hot bit is applied to the cleaned and fluxed conductors to be joined, heating them up. Solder wire (cored with flux or used with separate flux) is then touched to the heated joint (not the iron tip directly), melting and flowing into the joint via capillary action. The iron is removed, and the solder solidifies, creating the bond. Suitable for smaller wires and electronic components.

(ii) Pot and Ladle:**

(See Answer to Q1(a)(i) in 2016j paper). A method for larger cables where solder is melted in a container (pot) heated externally (e.g., by gas). The prepared, fluxed joint is positioned, and molten solder is poured over it using a ladle until the joint is fully filled/tinned. Requires careful handling of molten metal.

(c) Steel Conduit Installation (Figure 2)

(i) Identification of Labelled Parts:**

  • A: Conduit Box (likely Terminal Box or Through Box)
  • B: Normal Bend (90 Degree)
  • C: Conduit Pipe (Steel)
  • D: Coupler (Joining two lengths of conduit)
  • E: Saddle (Spacer Bar type likely) - For fixing conduit to surface.

(ii) Outline of Installation Procedure (Steel Conduit):**

  1. Planning & Marking: Mark the intended route of the conduit run on the surface (wall/ceiling), indicating positions of boxes, bends, and fixing points (saddles).
  2. Cutting Conduit: Measure and cut required straight lengths of steel conduit using a hacksaw (fine-toothed blade) in a pipe vice. Ensure cuts are square.
  3. Reaming & Cleaning: Remove sharp internal burrs from cut ends using a reamer or round file to prevent cable damage during pulling. Clean ends.
  4. Threading (if using threaded fittings): Secure conduit in pipe vice. Use appropriate die in a stock (with cutting fluid) to cut standard electrical threads on the conduit ends where connections to boxes or threaded fittings are needed.
  5. Bending (if not using pre-formed bends): Use a conduit bending machine or bending springs (for lighter gauge) to form bends as required, avoiding kinks or flattening the conduit.
  6. Assembling Run: Fit boxes, bends, couplers, and straight lengths together. For threaded systems, screw components tightly. For plain/lug-grip systems, ensure conduit is fully inserted and grub screws tightened (ensure earth continuity).
  7. Fixing to Surface: Secure the assembled conduit run to the surface using saddles at appropriate intervals (e.g., within 300mm of boxes/bends and typically every 1-1.5m on straight runs), ensuring the run is straight and properly aligned. Use appropriate screws/fixings for the surface material.
  8. Fitting Bushes & Locknuts: Where conduit enters boxes, fit appropriate male bushes and secure firmly with lock nuts inside the box to ensure mechanical security and earth continuity.
  9. Drawing In Cables: Once the conduit system is complete and secure, draw in the required electrical cables using draw wire or tape if necessary.

Ambient temperature is the surrounding temperature. Heat increases resistance in metals but decreases it in carbon/electrolytes. Soldering uses molten filler metal for joints. Steel conduit installation involves cutting, threading/bending, assembling fittings, fixing, and ensuring earth continuity before drawing cables.

Answer

This answer outlines workshop safety rules, causes of electrical fires, defines first aid and lists kit components, describes burn treatment, and lists workshop equipment with associated safety measures.

(a) Workshop Safety Rules and Fire Causes

(i) Three Safety Rules in an Electrical Workshop:**

  1. Assume All Circuits Are Live Until Proven Dead: Never touch exposed conductors or terminals without first safely isolating the circuit and verifying it is de-energized using approved testing procedures and equipment.
  2. Use Appropriate PPE: Always wear required Personal Protective Equipment, such as safety glasses, appropriate gloves (insulating if needed), and safety footwear. Avoid loose clothing or metallic jewelry.
  3. Maintain Good Housekeeping: Keep work areas clean, tidy, and free from obstructions, spills, and trailing cables. Store tools and materials properly.
  4. Use Tools Correctly: Use the right tool for the job, ensure tools (especially insulated ones) are in good condition, and report/replace defective tools immediately.
  5. Know Emergency Procedures: Be familiar with the location and use of emergency stop buttons, fire extinguishers, first aid kits, and emergency exit routes. Know how to report accidents or hazards.

The question asked for three.

(ii) Three Causes of Electrical Fires:**

(See Answer to Q4(a)(i) in 2020j paper).

  1. Overloading circuits/cables.
  2. Faulty/loose electrical connections.
  3. Short circuits or earth faults.
  4. Damaged wiring insulation.
  5. Defective electrical appliances/equipment.

The question asked for three.

(b) First Aid Definition and Kit Components

(i) Explanation of 'First Aid':**

First aid is the immediate and temporary assistance given to a person who is injured or suddenly becomes ill, using available skills and resources, until professional medical help arrives or the person recovers. Its main aims are to preserve life, prevent the condition from worsening, and promote recovery.

(ii) Three Components of a First Aid Kit:**

(See Answer to Q1(a)(ii) in 2016n paper).

  1. Sterile Dressings / Adhesive Plasters
  2. Bandages (Various types)
  3. Antiseptic Wipes
  4. Disposable Gloves
  5. Scissors / Tweezers
  6. Safety Pins
  7. Guidance Leaflet

The question asked for three.

(c) Procedure for Treating Burns or Scalds

(See Answer to Q2(a)(ii) in 2021j paper for detailed steps).

Key Steps Outline:

  1. Safety: Ensure scene/casualty safe, stop burning process.
  2. Cooling: Cool burn immediately with cool running water for at least 20 minutes.
  3. Remove Constrictions: Remove tight items (rings, watches) before swelling occurs (unless stuck).
  4. Covering: Cover loosely with sterile dressing or clean plastic film.
  5. Warmth: Keep casualty warm (avoiding burned area).
  6. Medical Attention: Seek professional help for anything more than minor superficial burns.

Applying creams/ointments/jellies is generally NOT recommended as initial first aid.

(d) Workshop Equipment and Safety Measures

(i) Two Electric Powered Equipment Used in the Workshop:**

  1. Bench Drill / Pillar Drill: Used for accurately drilling holes in materials like metal, wood, or plastic.
  2. Bench Grinder: Used for sharpening tools, shaping metal, or smoothing rough edges, typically with abrasive grinding wheels.
  3. Soldering Station: Used for electronic assembly and repair.
  4. Oscilloscope / Power Supply Units: Used for testing and powering electronic circuits.
  5. Portable Electric Drill / Screwdriver: Handheld tools for drilling/driving screws.

The question asked for two.

(ii) Three Safety Measures When Using Equipment (General - applicable to drills/grinders):**

  1. Eye Protection: Always wear safety glasses or goggles to protect eyes from flying debris (metal filings, wood chips). A face shield may be needed for grinding.
  2. Secure Workpiece: Firmly clamp the material being drilled or ground to prevent it from spinning or being thrown by the tool. Never hold small workpieces by hand close to the operating area.
  3. Guards and Adjustments: Ensure all safety guards (e.g., wheel guards on grinders, chuck guards on drills) are in place and correctly adjusted. Make adjustments only when the machine is switched off and stationary.
  4. Correct Use & Training: Ensure you are trained in the safe operation of the specific equipment. Use the correct speed, feed rate, drill bit type, or grinding wheel grade for the material being worked.
  5. Clothing & Hair: Avoid loose clothing, ties, or jewelry that could get caught in rotating parts. Tie back long hair.

The question asked for three.

Workshop safety relies on following rules regarding electrical work, tool use, and housekeeping. Electrical fires have common causes like overloading and faults. First aid provides immediate care, with burn treatment focusing on cooling. Operating powered equipment requires specific safety measures like using guards, PPE, and securing the workpiece.

Answer

This answer lists factors affecting module output, defines battery terms, distinguishes inverters/converters, explains solar cell operation, and names cell construction types.

(a) Four Factors Determining Solar Module Output

(See Answer to Q8(b) in 2014j paper or Q6(a)(ii) in 2020j paper).

  1. Solar Irradiance (Sunlight Intensity): Higher intensity = higher current = higher power.
  2. Cell Temperature: Higher temperature = lower voltage = lower power.
  3. Shading: Even partial shading significantly reduces output.
  4. Load / Operating Point: Maximum power delivered only at the MPP (Maximum Power Point).
  5. (Others: Angle of incidence, spectral content of light, dirt/soiling, module age/degradation).

The question asked for four.

(b) Solar Battery Terminology

(i) State of Charge (SoC):**

The level of electrical energy currently stored in a battery relative to its maximum capacity, usually expressed as a percentage (%). 100% SoC means the battery is fully charged; 0% SoC means it is fully discharged (though typically batteries should not be discharged below a certain level, e.g., 20-50% SoC, to preserve lifespan).

(ii) Self-Discharge:**

The gradual loss of stored charge (energy) within a battery over time due to internal chemical reactions, even when the battery is not connected to any load. The rate varies depending on battery chemistry, temperature (higher temp = higher rate), and age.

(c) Distinction Between DC-AC Inverter and DC-DC Converter

  • DC-AC Inverter: Converts Direct Current (DC) electricity (typically from a battery or PV array) into Alternating Current (AC) electricity (usually at standard mains voltage and frequency, e.g., 230V/50Hz). Used to power standard AC appliances from a DC source.
  • DC-DC Converter: Converts Direct Current (DC) electricity from one voltage level to another DC voltage level. It can either step-up (boost) the voltage to a higher level or step-down (buck) the voltage to a lower level. Used to match the voltage of a DC source to the requirement of a specific DC load or another part of the system.

(d) Solar Cell Operation and Construction Types

(i) Operation of a Solar Cell (with Diagram):**

(See Answer to Q7(c) in 2016j paper or Q7(b)(i) 2020j paper for diagram and detailed explanation of the photovoltaic effect).

Summary: Photons create electron-hole pairs; P-N junction field separates charges; voltage develops; connecting load allows DC current flow.

(ii) Two Types of Solar Cell Constructions (Based on Silicon):**

  1. Monocrystalline Silicon: Made from single-crystal silicon; highest efficiency, uniform dark appearance, often cut corners.
  2. Polycrystalline (Multicrystalline) Silicon: Made from multiple silicon crystals solidified together; lower efficiency than mono, characteristic blue "flaked" appearance, generally lower cost.
  3. Thin-Film Cells (e.g., Amorphous Silicon, CdTe, CIGS): Made by depositing very thin layers of photovoltaic material onto a substrate (like glass or flexible material). Generally lower efficiency but potentially lower cost, lighter weight, flexible form factors, better high-temperature performance in some cases.

The question asked for two. Mono- and Poly-crystalline are the most common crystalline silicon types.

PV module output is affected by sunlight, temperature, and shading. State of Charge indicates stored energy in a battery, while self-discharge is gradual loss. Inverters change DC to AC; converters change DC voltage levels. Solar cells work via the photovoltaic effect, with common types being monocrystalline and polycrystalline silicon.

  2. Environmental Benefits: Reduces deforestation (less firewood needed), lowers indoor air pollution (compared to burning biomass indoors), and produces no greenhouse gas emissions during use.
  3. Safety: No open flames, reducing fire hazards and risk of burns compared to traditional cooking methods.
  4. Convenience (in sunny conditions): Food can be left to cook slowly ("solar oven") without constant attention, freeing up time.

The question asked for two.

Solar radiance is instantaneous power, while PSH represents daily energy equivalent. Solar energy offers numerous environmental and economic benefits. Parabolic dishes concentrate sunlight for high temperatures. Solar box cookers provide a simple, fuel-free method for cooking using trapped solar heat.

Question 8 (20 marks)

(a) State four maintenance procedures for a solar battery.

(b) (i) Outline three IEE regulation requirements that govern solar batteries installation.

(ii) State three reasons for a lighting system failure to operate when connected to a newly installed P.V. solar system.

(c) Explain the following terms as used in P.V solar sizing:

(i) total daily system energy requirement;

(ii) voltage drop.

(d) A one roomed house has the following:

  • one 8 watt fluorescent lamp used 4 hours daily;
  • one 60 watt refrigerator used 24 hours daily;
  • one 40 watt coloured TV used for 3 hours daily.

Determine the:

(i) total daily load demand;

(ii) number of P.V module required if the P.V module generation factor is 3.4 .

(N.B. Neglect system losses.)

Answer

This answer details battery maintenance, outlines regulatory points for battery installation, lists reasons for lighting failure in new PV systems, explains sizing terms, and calculates load demand and required module number.

(a) Four Maintenance Procedures for a Solar Battery (Lead-Acid)

(See Answer to Q8(a)(i) in 2016n or Q5(b)(i) 2021j for details)

  1. Clean terminals and connections (check tightness, apply grease).
  2. Check electrolyte level and top up with distilled water (flooded types).
  3. Check State of Charge (voltage/specific gravity).
  4. Clean battery case/top.
  5. Ensure adequate ventilation.
  6. Perform periodic equalization charge (flooded types).

The question asked for four.

(b) Battery Installation Regulations and Lighting Failure Reasons

(i) Three IEE Regulation Requirements Governing Solar Batteries Installation (BS 7671 / Relevant Standards Principles):**

  1. Location & Ventilation: Batteries, especially lead-acid types releasing hydrogen gas, must be installed in a suitable location with adequate ventilation to prevent gas accumulation. They should be protected from mechanical damage, temperature extremes, and unauthorized access. (Ref: Regulation Group 551, Section 522).
  2. Isolation: Suitable means (e.g., DC isolator or fuse) must be provided to safely disconnect the battery bank from the rest of the installation for maintenance or emergencies. (Ref: Regulation Group 537).
  3. Overcurrent Protection: The battery circuit must be protected against overcurrent (particularly short circuits) by appropriately rated DC fuses or circuit breakers located close to the battery terminals. (Ref: Regulation 433, 434).
  4. Conductor Sizing: Cables connecting the battery must be adequately sized to handle the maximum charge/discharge currents without overheating and considering voltage drop. (Ref: Chapter 52).

The question asked for three.

(ii) Three Reasons for Lighting System Failure in Newly Installed PV System:**

  1. Incorrect Wiring/Connections: Reversed polarity (+/- swapped), loose connections at the lights, switch, controller load terminals, or battery; open circuit (broken wire).
  2. Battery Issue: Battery not charged (insufficient initial charge, problem with PV charging), battery voltage too low (below controller's LVD threshold or lamp operating voltage), faulty battery connection.
  3. Faulty Component: Defective lamp(s), faulty light switch, faulty charge controller (especially load output), blown fuse or tripped breaker in the lighting circuit.
  4. Incorrect Configuration: Charge controller load output disabled in settings, mismatch between lamp voltage rating and system voltage.

The question asked for three.

(c) PV Solar Sizing Terms Explanation

(i) Total Daily System Energy Requirement:**

(See Answer to Q8(c)(i) in 2019j paper). The total daily energy (Wh) the solar source (PV array/battery) must provide to power the loads PLUS compensate for all system energy losses (wiring, controller, battery, inverter etc.).

(ii) Voltage Drop:**

The reduction in electrical voltage that occurs along the length of a conductor as current flows through it, due to the conductor's resistance (Vdrop = I * Rcable). In PV systems (especially low voltage DC), minimizing voltage drop by using appropriately sized cables for the current and distance is crucial to avoid significant power loss (Ploss = IÂ² * Rcable) and ensure components operate within their required voltage range.

(d) Load Demand and PV Module Calculation

Loads:

  • Lamp: 1 x 8W x 4h = 32 Wh/day
  • Refrigerator: 1 x 60W x 24h = 1440 Wh/day
  • TV: 1 x 40W x 3h = 120 Wh/day

(i) Total Daily Load Demand (Energy):**

Total Load Energy = 32 Wh + 1440 Wh + 120 Wh = 1592 Wh/day

(ii) Number of PV Modules Required:**

Given: PV Module Generation Factor = 3.4 (This likely represents the daily Wh output per Wp of panel rating, effectively incorporating average Peak Sun Hours and some derating factors, but *not* system losses as defined in the problem N.B.).

System losses are neglected as per N.B., so the required generation equals the load demand = 1592 Wh/day.

Required PV Array Watt-peak (Wp) = Total Daily Load Demand (Wh/day) / Generation Factor (Wh/day/Wp)

Required Wp = 1592 Wh/day / 3.4 Wh/day/Wp

Solar battery maintenance involves cleaning, watering (if applicable), and checking connections/charge. Installation must adhere to safety regulations regarding location, isolation, and protection.
