Question 1

(a) Explain:

(i) The term wiring system.

(ii) Two factors taken into consideration when selecting a particular wiring system.

(b) List two types of wiring accessories and for each case, state its function.

(c) (i) Explain the importance of safety regulations in electrical workshops.

(ii) Outline four safety measures employed in electrical workshops to minimize accidents.

(d) Explain four first aid procedures when rescuing a person in contact with a live wire at a construction site.

Answer

This answer explains wiring systems, selection factors, accessories, workshop safety importance and measures, and first aid procedures for electric shock.

(a) Wiring System Explanation

(i) Wiring System: A wiring system refers to the complete assembly of electrical cables, their containment (e.g., conduits, trunking, trays, or methods like direct clipping), associated fittings (e.g., bends, boxes, couplings), and termination accessories (e.g., switches, sockets, ceiling roses) used to distribute electrical power from the consumer unit or distribution board to various points of use (lights, appliances, machinery) within a building or structure.

(ii) Two Factors Considered When Selecting a Wiring System:

  • Type of Environment / External Influences: The conditions where the wiring will be installed significantly affect the choice. Factors like presence of moisture (dampness), dust, corrosive substances, risk of mechanical impact, ambient temperature, and whether it's indoors or outdoors dictate the required level of protection (IP rating), durability, and material suitability (e.g., PVC conduit for damp areas, steel conduit for high impact risk).
  • Cost (Economic Considerations): The budget available for the installation influences the choice of materials and complexity. Some systems (e.g., surface-clipped PVC T&E) are relatively inexpensive, while others (e.g., concealed steel conduit or MIMS cable) are significantly more costly in terms of both materials and labour.
  • Other factors include: Durability required, appearance/aesthetics, flexibility for future alterations, safety regulations, type of building construction.

The question asked for two.

(b) Wiring Accessories and Functions

  1. Accessory Type: Switch (e.g., Light Switch)
    • Function: To safely make or break an electrical circuit, allowing control (turning ON or OFF) of a connected load, typically luminaires or appliances. Single-pole switches interrupt only the line conductor.
  2. Accessory Type: Socket Outlet (e.g., 13A Socket)
    • Function: To provide a safe and standardized connection point for plugging in portable appliances, supplying them with electrical power from the fixed wiring installation.
  3. Accessory Type: Junction Box
    • Function: An enclosure used to contain and protect electrical connections (joints) where cables are joined together or where circuits branch off.
  4. Accessory Type: Ceiling Rose
    • Function: Provides a termination point for a lighting circuit's fixed wiring in the ceiling and a connection point for a flexible cord supplying a pendant light fitting.

The question asked for two.

(c) Workshop Safety Regulations and Measures

(i) Importance of Safety Regulations: Safety regulations (like those derived from the Health and Safety at Work Act, Electricity at Work Regulations, and standards like BS 7671) are crucial in electrical workshops to:

  • Prevent accidents and injuries (electric shock, burns, falls, cuts).
  • Protect personnel (students, staff) and property from electrical hazards and fire risks.
  • Ensure a safe working environment compliant with legal requirements.
  • Promote safe working practices and awareness of potential dangers.
  • Minimize equipment damage and operational downtime due to accidents.

(ii) Four Safety Measures Employed in Electrical Workshops:

  1. Use of Personal Protective Equipment (PPE): Mandatory use of appropriate PPE such as safety glasses/goggles, insulating gloves (when necessary), safety footwear, and sometimes FR clothing.
  2. Proper Tool Usage and Maintenance: Ensuring only tools in good condition are used, using the right tool for the job, using insulated tools where appropriate, and regular inspection/maintenance of tools and equipment.
  3. Safe Isolation Procedures: Implementing strict procedures for isolating electrical circuits before working on them (lock-out/tag-out), and verifying circuits are dead using approved voltage indicators.
  4. Good Housekeeping: Keeping workbenches and floor areas clean, tidy, and free from obstructions, spills, or trailing cables to prevent slips, trips, and falls. Proper storage of tools and materials.
  5. Emergency Preparedness: Ensuring availability and knowledge of first aid equipment, fire extinguishers (appropriate types), emergency shut-off buttons, and clear emergency procedures.

The question asked for four.

(d) First Aid Procedures for Electric Shock Rescue (Construction Site)

Priority 1: Ensure Safety of Rescuer and Bystanders. Do NOT touch the victim directly if they are still in contact with the live source.

  1. Break Electrical Contact Safely:
    • Immediately attempt to switch off the power supply at the source (isolator, breaker) if it can be done safely and quickly.
    • If the power cannot be switched off, use a dry, non-conductive object (dry wood, rope, rubber mat, insulating pole) to push or pull the victim free from the live source, or to move the source away from the victim. Stand on a dry insulating surface yourself if possible. Avoid using metal or wet objects.
  2. Check Response and Call for Help: Once the victim is clear of the electrical source, check for responsiveness (shout and gently shake). If unresponsive, shout for help immediately and instruct someone specific to call emergency services (providing location details).
  3. Check Breathing: Open the victim's airway (head tilt, chin lift) and check for normal breathing (look, listen, feel) for no more than 10 seconds.
  4. Perform CPR (If Trained and Necessary): If the victim is unresponsive and not breathing normally, commence Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation (CPR) immediately, starting with chest compressions. Continue CPR until professional help arrives, the victim starts breathing normally, or you are exhausted.
  5. Treat Burns and Other Injuries: Once breathing is established (or while awaiting help if CPR not needed), check for and treat any visible burns (cool with running water if appropriate and cover with sterile dressing) or other injuries sustained during the shock or fall. Keep the victim warm and monitor their condition.

The question asked for four procedures.

A wiring system encompasses the entire electrical distribution path. Its selection depends on factors like environment and cost. Accessories provide connection and control points. Safety regulations and measures are paramount in workshops, and knowing correct first aid for electric shock is vital.

(b) Hydro vs. Nuclear Power and Hydro Siting

(i) Three Advantages of Hydro Station over Nuclear Power Station:

  • Renewable Energy Source: Hydro power utilizes the water cycle, which is a renewable resource, whereas nuclear power relies on finite uranium resources.
  • No Radioactive Waste: Hydro power generation does not produce hazardous long-lasting radioactive waste, which is a major environmental and disposal challenge for nuclear power.
  • Lower Operating Costs (Typically): Once built, hydro stations have very low running costs as the fuel (water) is free, unlike nuclear stations which require ongoing fuel procurement and complex waste management. Maintenance costs can also be lower for hydro.
  • Fast Response/Flexibility: Hydro stations can often start up and change output levels relatively quickly, making them suitable for meeting peak demand and providing grid stability services.
  • Multiple Uses (Reservoir): The reservoir created by a hydro dam can often provide other benefits like flood control, irrigation, recreation, and water supply.

(ii) Three Factors Considered When Siting a Hydro Power Station:

  • Water Availability & Head: Requires a reliable and sufficient water flow, and a significant vertical drop (Head) between the water source/reservoir and the turbines to maximize potential energy.
  • Geology & Topography: Needs stable ground for dam and powerhouse construction. Ideal topography involves a narrow gorge suitable for damming, opening into a larger valley upstream suitable for a reservoir.
  • Environmental & Social Impact: Assessment of land submergence, impact on ecosystems (fish, wildlife), displacement of communities, effects on downstream flow, and sediment management.
  • Other factors: Construction costs, accessibility, proximity to grid connection.

(d) Three Topologies of Structured Cables

(Referring typically to network cabling)

  1. Star Topology: The most common topology in modern structured cabling (especially for horizontal cabling). All cables run from individual work area outlets directly back to a central hub or switch (located in a telecommunications room or floor distributor). Advantages include ease of management, fault isolation (a fault on one cable only affects one outlet), and scalability. Disadvantage is the requirement for a large amount of cable.
  2. Bus Topology: An older topology where all devices are connected sequentially along a single main cable (the bus). Data travels along the bus, and devices tap into it. Advantages include simplicity and less cable usage initially. Disadvantages include difficulty in troubleshooting (a break affects the entire segment), performance degradation with many devices, and limited scalability. Rarely used for modern LANs but principle seen in some industrial control systems.
  3. Ring Topology: Devices are connected in a closed loop or ring. Data travels around the ring, typically in one direction. Advantages include orderly data transmission (using tokens). Disadvantages include susceptibility to failure (a single break can bring down the ring unless counter-rotating rings are used - FDDI), and difficulty adding/removing devices without disrupting the ring. Used in some legacy systems (Token Ring) and backbone networks (SONET/SDH).
  4. Mesh Topology: Devices are interconnected with multiple links, providing high redundancy and fault tolerance. Full mesh connects every device to every other device; partial mesh connects only some devices redundantly. Used mainly in backbone networks and WANs where reliability is critical, but expensive due to the number of connections required.
  5. Tree/Hierarchical Topology: A combination of star and bus topologies, creating a hierarchical structure. Central hubs (stars) might be connected via a main backbone cable (bus). Common in large networks.

The question asked for three. Star, Bus, and Ring are common examples taught.

Different distribution systems cater to various load types, with AC 3-phase 4-wire being prevalent. Structured cabling typically employs a star topology for horizontal runs due to its manageability and fault isolation benefits.

Question 3

(a) State three:

(i) Classifications of analog instruments.

(ii) Advantages of permanent magnet moving coil instruments over moving iron.

(b) A moving coil instrument has an element of resistance of 10 Ω and gives full-scale deflection with a potential difference of 100 mV . Determine the value of resistance to be placed in series with the element to give full scale deflection when connected across a potential difference of 120 V d.c.

(c) (i) Draw six socket outlets connected in ring. Include a spur.

(ii) State three IEE regulations requirement in (c) (i).

Answer

This answer classifies analog instruments, lists advantages of PMMC over MI types, calculates a voltmeter multiplier resistor, diagrams a ring final circuit with a spur, and states related regulatory requirements.

(a) Analog Instruments Classification and Comparison

(i) Three Classifications of Analog Instruments:

  • Based on Principle of Operation: e.g., Moving Coil (PMMC), Moving Iron (Attraction/Repulsion), Electrodynamic (Dynamometer), Induction Type, Hot Wire, Electrostatic, Rectifier Type.
  • Based on Quantity Measured: e.g., Ammeter (Current), Voltmeter (Voltage), Ohmmeter (Resistance), Wattmeter (Power), Frequency Meter, Power Factor Meter.
  • Based on Type of Display/Indication: e.g., Indicating Instruments (pointer and scale), Recording Instruments (chart recorder), Integrating Instruments (energy meter).
  • Based on Accuracy Class: Instruments are graded according to their percentage accuracy (e.g., Class 0.5, Class 1.0, Class 1.5, Class 2.5).

The question asked for three classifications.

(ii) Three Advantages of Permanent Magnet Moving Coil (PMMC) Instruments over Moving Iron (MI):

  • Uniform Scale: PMMC instruments typically have a linear scale because the deflecting torque is directly proportional to the current (Td ∝ I), making readings easier and more accurate across the range. MI instruments usually have non-linear, cramped scales, especially at the lower end (Td ∝ I²).
  • Higher Sensitivity / Lower Power Consumption: PMMC movements require very little current/power to operate due to the strong permanent magnet field, making them highly sensitive and suitable for measuring small currents/voltages with minimal circuit loading. MI instruments generally require more operating power.
  • Effective Damping: Eddy current damping in the aluminium former on which the coil is wound is very effective in PMMC instruments, leading to quick settling of the pointer with minimal oscillation. Damping in MI instruments (usually air friction) is often less effective.
  • Freedom from Hysteresis Error: PMMC instruments are free from hysteresis errors associated with iron parts used in MI instruments.

Main disadvantage of PMMC: Only responds accurately to DC unless a rectifier is used.

The question asked for three advantages.

(b) Voltmeter Multiplier Calculation

The instrument movement has:

  • Internal resistance (Rm) = 10 Ω
  • Full-scale deflection voltage (Vm) = 100 mV = 0.1 V

First, calculate the full-scale deflection current (Im) of the movement using Ohm's Law:

Im = Vm / Rm = 0.1 V / 10 Ω = 0.01 A (or 10 mA)

We want to convert this movement into a voltmeter that reads 120 V at full scale (V_fullscale = 120 V). To do this, we need to add a multiplier resistor (Rs) in series with the movement.

The total resistance (Rt) required for the voltmeter to read 120 V when 0.01 A flows is:

Rt = V_fullscale / Im = 120 V / 0.01 A = 12000 Ω

The total resistance Rt is the sum of the movement resistance (Rm) and the series multiplier resistance (Rs):

Rt = Rm + Rs

Therefore, the required multiplier resistance (Rs) is:

Rs = Rt - Rm = 12000 Ω - 10 Ω = 11990 Ω

A resistance of 11990 Ω (or 11.99 kΩ) must be placed in series with the movement.

(i) Diagram: Six Sockets in Ring with Spur:

(ii) Three IEE Regulations Requirements for Ring Final Circuits (BS 7671 Principles):

  1. Floor Area Served: A ring final circuit should not serve a floor area exceeding 100 m². (Ref: Appendix 15 guidance).
  2. Cable Size and Protection: Standard ring circuits commonly use 2.5 mm² PVC Twin & Earth cable protected by a 30A or 32A protective device (MCB or fuse). (Specific calculations may allow variations).
  3. Number of Spurs: The number of fused spurs connected to the ring should not exceed the total number of socket outlets and fixed appliances connected directly to the ring. An unfused spur should supply only one single or one twin socket outlet (or one fixed appliance) and should originate from a socket on the ring or via a junction box connected to the ring using the same cable size. (Ref: Appendix 15 guidance).
  4. Continuity Testing: Specific tests must be carried out upon completion to verify the continuity of all conductors (phase, neutral, CPC) around the entire ring. (Ref: Guidance Note 3 / Part 6).

Analog instruments are classified in various ways, with PMMC types offering advantages like linear scales and high sensitivity for DC measurements. Voltmeter ranges are extended using series multiplier resistors. Question 4

(a) Explain reasons for earthing electrical installations.

(b) (i) Outline four methods used to achieve the earthing of an installation.

(ii) List six types of earth electrodes.

(c) With the aid of a labelled diagram, explain the operation of a single phase current operated earth leakage circuit - breaker.

(d) State three requirements of a good electrical protection system.

Answer

This answer explains reasons for earthing, methods of achieving it, types of electrodes, the operation of an RCD (current-operated ELCB), and requirements for effective electrical protection.

(a) Reasons for Earthing Electrical Installations

Earthing (or grounding) is a fundamental safety measure in electrical installations primarily for:

  • Protection Against Electric Shock: To provide a low-impedance path for fault current to flow to earth if exposed-conductive-parts (like metal casings) become live due to an insulation failure. This large fault current operates a protective device (fuse, MCB, RCD) quickly, disconnecting the supply and preventing the metalwork from remaining at a dangerous potential relative to earth potential that a person might touch.
  • Voltage Stabilization: To provide a stable reference potential (close to zero volts or earth potential) for the electrical system, helping to prevent excessive voltages appearing on the system due to phenomena like lightning strikes, switching surges, or faults on higher voltage systems.
  • Equipment Protection: In some cases, earthing helps protect sensitive electronic equipment from electrical noise and interference.

(b) Earthing Methods and Electrodes

(i) Four Methods Used to Achieve Earthing (Earthing Systems):

These refer to the overall system arrangement connecting the installation to earth:

  1. TN-S (Terra Neutral - Separated): Supplier provides separate Neutral and Protective Earth conductors from the transformer to the consumer's premises. Consumer connects MET to the supplier's PE terminal.
  2. TN-C-S (Terra Neutral - Combined Separated) / PME: Supplier combines Neutral and Protective Earth functions in one conductor (PEN) in the network, earthing it multiple times. At the consumer intake, the PEN is split into separate N and PE terminals provided by the supplier. Consumer connects MET to the supplier's PE terminal.
  3. TT (Terra Terra): Supplier earths their transformer neutral. Consumer provides their *own* independent earth electrode connection for their installation's MET, separate from the supplier's earth.
  4. IT (Isolé Terra): The supply system has no intentional connection to earth at the source (or is earthed through a high impedance). The installation's exposed-conductive-parts are earthed independently or collectively. Used in special applications requiring high supply continuity (e.g., medical facilities, some industrial processes).

The question asked for four methods.

(ii) Six Types of Earth Electrodes:

These are the physical conductors making contact with the ground:

  1. Earth Rods: Copper-clad steel or solid copper rods driven vertically into the ground. Most common type. Can be coupled for greater depth.
  2. Earth Plates: Solid metal plates (copper or galvanized steel) buried vertically or horizontally.
  3. Earth Tapes/Strips: Copper or galvanized steel tapes buried horizontally in trenches.
  4. Foundation Earth Electrodes: Conductors embedded within the concrete foundations of a building during construction.
  5. Underground Structural Metalwork: Utilizing existing metallic structures like pipelines (if permitted and suitable) or well casings as electrodes (requires careful assessment).
  6. Wire Electrodes / Earth Mats: Grids or arrangements of buried bare conductors forming an earth mat, often used for substations or where low resistance is difficult to achieve with rods/plates.

The question asked for six types.

(c) Operation of Current Operated ELCB (RCD)

A Residual Current Device (RCD), formerly often called a current-operated ELCB, detects small imbalances between the current flowing in the Line and Neutral conductors, indicating leakage to earth.

(d) Three Requirements of a Good Electrical Protection System

  1. Selectivity (Discrimination): The system should isolate only the faulty part of the installation, leaving healthy circuits energized. Protective devices should coordinate so that the device nearest the fault operates first.
  2. Sensitivity: The protective devices must be sensitive enough to detect the smallest fault current that could cause danger (e.g., operate reliably on a high impedance earth fault for RCDs, or detect sustained small overloads for MCBs/fuses) within the required time.
  3. Speed of Operation: Protection must operate quickly enough to prevent injury (electric shock) or damage to equipment and minimize fire risk. Disconnection times for electric shock protection are specified in regulations based on system type and voltage.
  4. Reliability: Protective devices must operate correctly when called upon throughout their service life. They should be robust and require minimal maintenance.
  5. Stability: The protection system should remain stable and not operate unnecessarily (nuisance tripping) under normal operating conditions, including transient events like motor starting or switching surges.

The question asked for three.

Earthing is vital for electrical safety, achieved through various system types and electrodes. RCDs provide sensitive earth fault detection. Effective protection systems must be selective, sensitive, fast, reliable, and stable.

Question 5

(a) (i) Explain the term 'fire alarm system'.

(ii) Draw a labelled schematic diagram of a closed circuit alarm system.

(b) Outline four tasks involved in maintaining batteries and give a reason for each.

(c) With aid of a circuit diagrams describe how earth electrode resistance test is carried out on a completed installation.

Answer

This answer defines fire alarm systems, diagrams a closed-circuit alarm, outlines battery maintenance tasks, and describes the earth electrode resistance test.

(a) Fire Alarm System and Closed Circuit Diagram

(i) Fire Alarm System Explanation: A fire alarm system is a network of devices working together to detect signs of fire (smoke, heat, flame) and alert occupants and emergency services. Key components typically include detectors (smoke, heat), manual call points (break glass units), a control panel (which processes signals and manages the system), sounders/strobes (bells, sirens, visual alarms), and often an automatic connection to the fire brigade.

(ii) Closed Circuit Alarm System Diagram: (This usually refers to Intruder Alarms, as requested in Q3 2015j and Q2 2016j. Assuming the same intent here). A closed-circuit system maintains a continuous loop through normally closed sensors. Breaking the loop triggers the alarm.

(b) Four Battery Maintenance Tasks and Reasons

  1. Task: Check Electrolyte Level (Flooded Batteries)
    • Reason: To ensure the battery plates are fully submerged in electrolyte for proper operation and to prevent plate damage/drying out. Low levels reduce capacity and lifespan.
  2. Task: Clean Battery Terminals and Connections
    • Reason: To remove corrosion (sulfation/oxidation) which creates high resistance, hindering current flow during charging/discharging and potentially causing overheating at the terminal. Ensures a reliable, low-resistance connection.
  3. Task: Check Terminal Tightness
    • Reason: Loose terminals cause high resistance, voltage drops, inefficient charging/discharging, potential overheating, and intermittent operation. Ensures secure electrical connection.
  4. Task: Check Battery Voltage / Specific Gravity
    • Reason: To assess the battery's state of charge and general health. Consistently low voltage or specific gravity, or significant variation between cells (SG), indicates potential problems like undercharging, sulfation, or cell failure.
  5. Task: Clean Battery Case/Top
    • Reason: To remove dirt, dust, and spilled electrolyte which can create conductive paths across the battery top, leading to increased self-discharge.

The question asked for four.

(c) Earth Electrode Resistance Test

This test measures the resistance to the general mass of earth offered by the installation's earth electrode system. The most common method is the 'Fall of Potential' method using a dedicated 3- or 4-terminal Earth Tester.

Instrument: Earth Resistance Tester (3- or 4-pole).

Procedure (Fall of Potential - 3 Pole Method):

  1. Disconnect Electrode: Temporarily disconnect the installation's earth electrode (E) from the Main Earthing Terminal (MET) to measure only the electrode's resistance.
  2. Place Test Spikes: Drive two auxiliary test spikes into the ground in a straight line away from the earth electrode (E).
    • Current Spike (C): Driven far away (e.g., 20-30 meters or more, ideally 5-10 times the electrode depth/length).
    • Potential Spike (P): Driven between E and C, typically at about 62% of the distance from E to C.
  3. Connect Tester: Connect the tester terminals:
    • E terminal to the disconnected earth electrode under test.
    • P terminal to the Potential spike.
    • C terminal to the Current spike.
  4. Execute Test: Operate the earth tester according to its instructions. It injects an AC current between E and C and measures the potential difference between E and P. Using Ohm's Law (R = V/I), it calculates and displays the earth electrode resistance (RE).
  5. Verify Reading: Take additional readings by moving the potential spike (P) slightly closer to E and slightly further away (e.g., 10% of E-C distance each way). If the resistance readings remain relatively stable, the initial measurement is likely accurate. If they vary significantly, the current spike (C) may need to be moved further away.
  6. Reconnect Electrode: After testing, securely reconnect the earth electrode to the MET.

Fire alarms provide critical life safety warnings. Closed-circuit intruder alarms offer basic security with tamper indication. Regular battery maintenance is crucial for reliability. The earth electrode resistance test verifies the effectiveness of the installation's connection to the general mass of earth, essential for safety device operation.

(c) (i) Draw a labelled P.V. solar module I-V curve.

Answer

This answer lists solar energy applications and advantages, explains solar cell operation, details the PV I-V curve, and outlines solar harvesting methods.

(a) Solar Applications and Advantages

(i) Four Applications of Solar Energy:

  1. Electricity Generation (Photovoltaics - PV): Generating DC electricity using solar panels for homes (grid-tied or off-grid), businesses, utility-scale power plants, street lighting, water pumping, powering remote equipment (telecoms, sensors).
  2. Water Heating (Solar Thermal): Using solar collectors (flat plate or evacuated tube) to heat water for domestic use (showers, taps), swimming pools, or industrial processes.
  3. Space Heating/Cooling (Solar Thermal/Passive): Using solar air heaters or water collectors for active space heating, or passive solar design principles (building orientation, thermal mass, shading) to heat or help cool buildings. Solar thermal energy can also drive absorption chillers for cooling.
  4. Cooking (Solar Cookers): Using devices like box cookers or parabolic cookers to concentrate sunlight for cooking food, especially in areas with limited access to traditional fuels.
  5. Crop Drying: Using solar air heaters or simple solar dryers to remove moisture from agricultural produce for preservation.
  6. Desalination: Using solar thermal energy to evaporate water and condense fresh water, or PV to power reverse osmosis plants.

The question asked for four.

(ii) Two Advantages of Solar Electricity over Other Sources:

  • Renewable & Abundant Resource: Sunlight is a virtually inexhaustible energy source available globally, unlike finite fossil fuels (coal, oil, gas) or uranium.
  • Environmentally Clean: Solar PV electricity generation produces no direct greenhouse gas emissions, air or water pollutants, or noise during operation, contributing to cleaner air and mitigating climate change.
  • Low Operating Costs & Maintenance: No ongoing fuel costs after installation. PV panels are highly reliable with long lifespans (25+ years) and require minimal maintenance (mainly cleaning).
  • Scalability & Decentralization: PV systems can be installed in various sizes, from small portable units to large power plants, and can generate power close to the point of use, reducing transmission losses and increasing energy independence.

The question asked for two.

(b) How a Solar Cell Works

(See Answer to Q7(c) in 2016j paper for diagram and detailed explanation of the photovoltaic effect).

In summary: Photons from sunlight strike the P-N junction in the semiconductor (silicon). If photons have sufficient energy, they create electron-hole pairs. The junction's electric field separates these charges (electrons to N-side, holes to P-side), creating a voltage. Connecting an external circuit allows current (flow of electrons) to be drawn from the cell.

(c) PV Module I-V Curve

(i) Labelled I-V Curve Diagram:

(ii) Explanation of Three Points on the Curve:

  1. Short Circuit Current (Isc): The point on the curve where Voltage (V) = 0. This represents the maximum current the module can deliver under the given irradiance conditions when its terminals are short-circuited. It is highly dependent on light intensity.
  2. Open Circuit Voltage (Voc): The point on the curve where Current (I) = 0. This represents the maximum voltage the module can produce under the given conditions when no load is connected (open circuit). It is primarily dependent on cell temperature (decreasing as temperature rises).
  3. Maximum Power Point (MPP): The point on the "knee" of the curve where the product of Voltage (Vmp) and Current (Imp) is greatest (Pmax = Vmp × Imp). This is the operating point where the module delivers the most power and operates most efficiently under the specific irradiance and temperature conditions.

(d) Four Methods of Solar Harvesting

  1. Photovoltaics (PV): Direct conversion of sunlight into DC electricity using semiconductor solar cells arranged in modules/panels.
  2. Solar Thermal (Water/Air Heating): Absorbing solar radiation to directly heat a fluid (water, glycol, or air) using collectors like flat plates, evacuated tubes, or solar air heaters, typically for lower temperature applications.
  3. Concentrating Solar Power (CSP): Using mirrors or lenses (e.g., parabolic troughs, dishes, power towers) to concentrate sunlight onto a small receiver, generating high temperatures to produce steam or heat a working fluid for electricity generation via conventional turbines or engines.
  4. Passive Solar Design: Designing buildings to utilize solar energy naturally for heating, cooling, and lighting without active mechanical systems. Techniques include building orientation, window placement/sizing, thermal mass, shading, and natural ventilation.
  5. Solar Chemistry / Fuels: Using solar energy to drive chemical reactions, for example, producing hydrogen fuel through photoelectrolysis of water (still largely developmental).

The question asked for four.

Solar energy has diverse applications from electricity generation to heating and cooking. PV systems offer clean, renewable power. Understanding cell operation and the I-V curve is key to PV system design. Various methods exist to harvest solar energy, from direct PV conversion to thermal collection and passive building design.

Question 7

This answer lists selection features for domestic PV modules, outlines the main parts of a solar electric system with their functions, and completes a troubleshooting table.

(a) Four Features Considered When Selecting a Domestic PV Module

  1. Power Rating (Watts - Wp): The nominal power output under Standard Test Conditions (STC). This determines how much energy the panel can produce and influences the total number of panels needed to meet energy requirements.
  2. Physical Size and Weight: Important for determining if the panels will fit in the available roof space and whether the roof structure can support the weight.
  3. Efficiency (%): Higher efficiency panels produce more power per unit area, useful for limited roof space, but often come at a higher cost per watt.
  4. Warranty (Product and Performance): A good product warranty (covering materials and workmanship, e.g., 10-15 years) and a long performance warranty (guaranteeing a certain percentage of power output after 25 years) indicate manufacturer confidence and provide investment protection.
  5. Cost (€/$/£ per Watt): Comparing the cost relative to the power output helps determine the economic value.
  6. Temperature Coefficient: Indicates how much power output decreases as module temperature increases. Important in hot climates. A lower (less negative) percentage per °C is better.
  7. Manufacturer Reputation and Reliability: Choosing established manufacturers with proven track records and good customer support can be beneficial for long-term reliability and warranty claims.

The question asked for four.

(b) Five Parts of a Solar Electric System and Functions

(Typical for an off-grid or hybrid system)

  1. PV Array (Modules/Panels):
    • Function: Captures sunlight and converts it directly into DC electricity using the photovoltaic effect. This is the primary energy generation component.
  2. Charge Controller:
    • Function: Regulates the flow of electricity from the PV array to the battery bank. It prevents battery overcharging, often prevents deep discharging (Low Voltage Disconnect - LVD), and may optimize power transfer (MPPT). Essential for battery health and system efficiency.
  3. Battery Bank (Storage):
    • Function: Stores the DC electricity generated by the PV array for use when there is insufficient sunlight (e.g., at night, during cloudy weather). Provides energy storage and helps stabilize system voltage.
  4. Inverter (DC to AC):
    • Function: Converts the DC electricity stored in the batteries (or directly from the array/controller in some systems) into standard AC electricity (e.g., 230V/50Hz) suitable for powering conventional AC appliances. (Not present in purely DC systems).
  5. Loads (Appliances):
    • Function: The end-use devices that consume the electricity generated by the system (e.g., lights, TV, fridge, pumps). Can be DC loads (powered directly) or AC loads (powered via the inverter).
  6. Other essential parts include: Mounting Structure (to hold panels), Wiring/Cabling, Fuses/Circuit Breakers (Protection), Isolators/Switches (Safety/Control), Monitoring System (Optional).

Answer

This answer defines insolation and solar incident angle, calculates system energy requirements, lists maintenance tools, determines lamp power, and outlines cable joint types relevant to solar installations.

(a) Solar Term Definitions

(i) Insolation (Incident Solar Radiation): The amount of solar radiation energy received on a given surface area during a given time period. It is often expressed in kilowatt-hours per square meter per day (kWh/m²/day) or per year, representing the total solar energy available for harvesting over time. It differs from irradiance, which is instantaneous power per area (W/m²).

(ii) Solar Incident Angle (Angle of Incidence): The angle between the sun's rays striking a surface and the line perpendicular (normal) to that surface. A zero-degree incident angle means the sun's rays are directly perpendicular to the surface, maximizing energy intensity. A larger angle means the sunlight is spread over a larger area, reducing intensity.

(b) Energy Calculation and Maintenance Tools

(i) Total Daily System Energy Requirement:

First, calculate the daily energy consumption (Wh) for each appliance:

  • Fluorescent lamp: 14 W * 4 h = 56 Wh
  • Colour TV: 80 W * 3 h = 240 Wh
  • Lamps (assuming total 10W): 10 W * 8 h = 80 Wh

Total Daily Load Energy = 56 Wh + 240 Wh + 80 Wh = 376 Wh/day

Now, account for 15% energy losses (0.15 loss fraction). The energy drawn from the source (battery/system) must be higher.

Total Daily System Energy Requirement = Total Load Energy / (1 - Loss Fraction)

System Energy = 376 Wh / (1 - 0.15) = 376 Wh / 0.85

System Energy ≈ 442.35 Wh/day

(ii) Four Useful Tools for Solar System Maintenance:

  1. Multimeter: For measuring DC/AC voltage, current (with clamp), resistance, continuity.
  2. Insulated Hand Tools: Screwdrivers, spanners, wrenches, pliers, wire strippers/cutters with insulation rated for electrical work.
  3. Torque Wrench/Driver: For tightening electrical connections (terminals, bolts on mounting structures) to the correct manufacturer-specified torque.
  4. Cleaning Supplies: Soft brush, squeegee, cloths, water supply (hose or sprayer), mild detergent (optional) for cleaning PV modules.
  5. Battery Maintenance Tools: Hydrometer (flooded), distilled water filler, terminal cleaner brush, petroleum jelly, PPE (gloves, goggles).
  6. Safety Equipment: Voltage tester (proving dead), lock-out/tag-out kit, appropriate PPE.

Given:

  • Voltage (V) = 12 V
  • Current (I) = 5 A

Power (P) is calculated using the formula P = V * I

P = 12 V * 5 A = 60 W

The power of the lamp is 60 Watts.

(d) Four Types of Cable Joints Used in Solar Installations

While permanent joints in main DC runs are often discouraged in favour of continuous runs or specialized connectors, common methods for connections/terminations include:

  1. MC4 Connectors (or similar PV connectors): These are industry-standard, weatherproof, locking connectors used for interconnecting PV modules and connecting strings to combiner boxes or home run cables. They provide a reliable, low-resistance, plug-and-play connection designed specifically for PV applications. (Strictly a connector, but the primary 'joining' method for modules).
  2. Crimp Lugs/Terminals: Used to terminate larger DC cables (e.g., from combiner box to controller, controller to battery, battery to inverter) onto terminal studs or busbars. A calibrated crimping tool ensures a secure mechanical and electrical connection.
  3. Screw Terminals (in Enclosures): Found inside combiner boxes, charge controllers, DC distribution boxes, and inverters. Stripped cable ends are inserted and clamped securely under a screw or pressure plate. Requires correct tightening torque.
  4. Insulation Displacement Connectors (IDCs): Sometimes used in specific low-power DC applications or within certain pre-fabricated components, where a blade cuts through the insulation to make contact with the conductor. Less common for main power wiring.
  5. Waterproof Junction Boxes with Terminal Blocks: For outdoor connections or joining cables where connectors aren't suitable, weatherproof junction boxes fitted with appropriate DIN rail terminals or connector blocks provide a secure and protected environment for making connections using screw or spring cage terminals.
  6. Note: Traditional soldered joints like married or tee joints are generally avoided for field connections in modern PV installations due to potential reliability issues outdoors unless properly environmentally sealed, and preference for mechanical methods.

Insolation represents available solar energy over time. Accurate energy calculations are needed for system sizing. Proper tools facilitate safe and effective maintenance. Basic electrical formulas determine power (P=VI). Reliable connections in solar installations often utilize specialized connectors, crimps, and screw terminals within appropriate enclosures.
